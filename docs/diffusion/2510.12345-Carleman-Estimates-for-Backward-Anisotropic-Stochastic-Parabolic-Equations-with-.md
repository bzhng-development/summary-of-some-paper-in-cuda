## 1. Executive Summary

This paper derives the first Carleman estimate (Eq. 1.8) for backward anisotropic stochastic parabolic equations with general dynamic boundary conditions and weak divergence source terms in $H^{-1}$ bulk–surface spaces. Using this estimate, the authors establish null controllability and insensitizing control for the corresponding forward systems, providing an explicit exponential bound on the null controllability cost $\exp(C K_T)$ dependent on the final time $T$ and coefficients (Eq. 1.14–1.15). This work resolves open challenges in stochastic control theory by handling the combined complexities of anisotropy, dynamic boundary effects, and low-regularity sources.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### 2.1 The Core Problem: Carleman Estimates for Complex Stochastic Systems

This paper tackles a fundamental gap in the mathematical theory of stochastic partial differential equations (SPDEs): **deriving Carleman estimates for backward anisotropic stochastic parabolic equations with general dynamic boundary conditions and weak divergence source terms**. To understand why this matters, we need to unpack several technical concepts:

- **Carleman estimates** are weighted energy inequalities with large parameters that allow analysts to absorb lower-order terms. Introduced by Carleman in 1939 for elliptic equations [13], they have become the central tool for proving controllability and solving inverse problems in both deterministic and stochastic PDE settings [34, 42].

- **Dynamic boundary conditions** differ from classical Dirichlet, Neumann, or Robin conditions by including a time derivative of the boundary state. The system (1.2) models this through the coupling term $\partial^A_\nu z$ (the conormal derivative), which connects the bulk equation in $Q = (0,T) \times G$ with the surface equation on $\Sigma = (0,T) \times \Gamma$. This captures physical phenomena where the boundary itself evolves dynamically—such as heat exchange with a reactive surface or chemical concentrations interacting with container walls [21, 25, 36].

- **Anisotropic diffusion** means the diffusion coefficients vary by direction, represented by matrices $(a_{jk})_{1 \leq j,k \leq N}$ in the bulk and $(a_{jk}^\Gamma)_{1 \leq j,k \leq N}$ on the boundary, rather than scalar multiples of the identity. This generalizes beyond the isotropic case commonly studied in literature.

- **Weak divergence source terms** in $H^{-1}$ spaces (negative Sobolev spaces) represent low-regularity forcing terms of the form $\sum_{j=1}^N \frac{\partial F_j}{\partial x_j}$ in the bulk and $\sum_{j=1}^N D_j F_{\Gamma j}$ on the boundary. These are only defined in the distributional sense, which prevents direct application of standard Carleman techniques.

The specific gap this paper addresses is stated explicitly in the Introduction:

> "To the best of our knowledge, this paper is the first to establish the Carleman estimate (1.8) for backward anisotropic stochastic parabolic equations with $L^2$ bulk–surface source terms and $H^{-1}$ bulk–surface source terms appearing as weak divergence-type sources, coupled with the general dynamic boundary condition..." (Section 1)

Previous Carleman estimates, such as [7, Theorem 4.1], only handled the special case where $F_j = F_{\Gamma j} = 0$ for all $j$ (no weak divergence terms) and isotropic diffusion $(a_{jk}) = (a_{jk}^\Gamma) = I_N$. As the authors note:

> "The Carleman estimate established in [7, Theorem 4.1]... is not sufficient to address the control problems considered in this paper. In particular, it cannot be applied to the null controllability problem studied in Section 5, nor to the insensitizing control problem analyzed in Section 6." (Section 1)

### 2.2 Why This Problem Matters: Theoretical and Practical Significance

The importance of this work operates on two levels:

**Theoretical Significance:**

1. **Observability inequalities require Carleman estimates.** Following the classical duality approach, controllability of linear stochastic PDEs is established by analyzing an observability problem for the corresponding adjoint equation [34]. Carleman estimates provide the weighted energy estimates with sufficiently large parameters needed to prove such observability results. Without a Carleman estimate that handles $H^{-1}$ source terms and dynamic boundary conditions, the observability inequality—and hence controllability—cannot be established.

2. **Explicit time dependence enables quantitative control costs.** The paper derives an explicit dependence of the Carleman constant on the final time $T$, which is "essential for quantitative estimates and plays a key role in controllability problems" (Section 1). This allows the authors to provide an explicit bound on the null controllability cost:
   $$
   K(Y_0, G_0) \leq \exp(C K_T) \, \mathbb{E}\|Y_0\|_{L^2}^2,
   $$
   where $K_T$ is given explicitly in Equation (1.14) as:
   $$
   K_T := 1 + \frac{1}{T} + \|a_1\|_\infty^{2/3} + \|a_2\|_\infty^{2/3} + T(\|a_1\|_\infty + \|a_2\|_\infty) + (1 + T)(\|B_1\|_\infty^2 + \|B_2\|_\infty^2).
   $$
   Prior work did not provide such explicit estimates for systems with dynamic boundary conditions.

3. **Relaxed regularity assumptions expand applicability.** By allowing source terms in the drift components to belong to negative Sobolev spaces ($H^{-1}$), the paper "significantly relaxes the regularity assumptions and enables the treatment of weak divergence-type sources both in the bulk and on the boundary" (Section 1). This is crucial because many physical forcing terms naturally live in distributional spaces rather than $L^2$.

**Real-World Impact:**

The forward system (1.9) that benefits from these theoretical advances models concrete physical phenomena:

> "The system (1.9) with $\xi \equiv \xi_\Gamma \equiv u \equiv v_1 \equiv v_2 \equiv 0$ models diffusion phenomena, such as the evolution of heat, bacterial populations, or chemical concentrations, under stochastic perturbations and in interaction with the boundary through the dynamic boundary conditions..." (Section 1)

The convection terms $B_1 \cdot \nabla y$ and $B_2 \cdot \nabla_\Gamma y_\Gamma$ describe transport in moving media (e.g., fluid flow with prescribed velocity). The dynamic boundary conditions capture scenarios where the boundary itself has mass or energy that evolves over time—such as:
- Heat transfer through walls with thermal inertia
- Chemical reactions occurring on catalytic surfaces
- Population dynamics where boundary regions serve as habitats

The **insensitizing control problem** (Definition 1.1) addresses systems with partially unknown initial data, seeking controls such that an energy functional remains insensitive to small perturbations. This has practical relevance for robust control design where initial conditions cannot be measured precisely.

### 2.3 Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The literature on controllability for parabolic equations is extensive, but existing work falls short for the specific combination of features this paper addresses. We can categorize prior approaches as follows:

**Deterministic Parabolic Equations with Dynamic Boundary Conditions:**
- Works [1], [25], [36] establish controllability results for deterministic systems
- [26] treats fully discrete parabolic equations
- [8, 47, 37] study insensitizing control problems
- **Limitation:** These do not handle stochastic perturbations, which are essential for modeling random environmental effects

**Stochastic Parabolic Equations with Static Boundary Conditions:**
- Dirichlet conditions: [2] obtained partial controllability results under restrictive assumptions; [40] extended these using global Carleman estimates with an additional control on the diffusion term
- Robin conditions: [10, 43] address controllability
- General coefficients: [4, 40] handle both zero- and first-order terms with bounded adapted coefficients
- **Limitation:** Static boundary conditions (Dirichlet, Neumann, Robin) do not capture dynamic boundary evolution. Additionally, [4, 40] require a second control acting on the diffusion term over the entire spatial domain, which is more restrictive than the localized control setting.

**Stochastic Parabolic Equations with Dynamic Boundary Conditions:**
- [7] addresses forward and backward equations with only zero-order terms
- [6] treats one-dimensional stochastic heat equations
- [5] handles backward equations with reaction and convection terms
- [11] studies forward equations with localized control in the drift term without additional forcing
- [3, 38] consider coupled forward-backward systems
- **Critical Limitation:** As noted in Section 1, [7, Theorem 4.1] corresponds to the particular case $(a_{jk}) = (a_{jk}^\Gamma) = I_N$ (isotropic) and $F_j = F_{\Gamma j} = 0$ (no weak divergence terms). This estimate "is not sufficient to address the control problems considered in this paper."

**Insensitizing Control for Stochastic Equations:**
- Forward stochastic heat equation: [44]
- Backward stochastic heat equations: [29]
- Forward stochastic degenerate parabolic equations: [30]
- Dynamic boundary conditions: [3]
- **Limitation:** None of these address forward anisotropic stochastic reaction-convection-diffusion equations with general dynamic boundary conditions.

The paper explicitly identifies three fundamental challenges that prior work does not simultaneously address (Section 1):

1. **Anisotropic diffusion matrices** in both bulk and boundary, extending beyond the isotropic framework. This "introduces many additional terms that must be treated with care."

2. **Explicit dependence of the Carleman constant on final time $T$**, which is "essential for quantitative estimates and plays a key role in controllability problems."

3. **Source terms in negative Sobolev spaces** ($H^{-1}$), which "significantly relaxes the regularity assumptions." The corresponding divergence terms are only defined distributionally, "which prevents the direct application of standard Carleman techniques."

### 2.4 How This Paper Positions Itself

This work positions itself as the first comprehensive treatment that unifies all the above challenges. The authors state:

> "To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first to address the null controllability and an insensitizing control problem for forward anisotropic stochastic parabolic equations with general dynamic boundary conditions, including both reaction and convection terms." (Section 1)

The methodological innovations that enable this positioning are:

**Two-Step Carleman Estimate Derivation:**
1. **Step 1 (Section 3):** Uses a weighted identity method with careful treatment of boundary integral terms arising from dynamic boundary conditions to obtain an intermediate Carleman estimate for equations *without* weak divergence source terms. This extends [7, Theorem 4.1] to anisotropic diffusion and provides explicit $T$-dependence.

2. **Step 2 (Section 4):** Employs a duality method combined with optimization techniques to incorporate the weak divergence source terms $F_j$ and $F_{\Gamma j}$. The paper notes: "In contrast to [29], where Carleman estimates are mainly used to relax regularity assumptions on diffusion source terms, our approach is specifically designed to address weak divergence-type sources..." (Section 1)

**Control Mechanism Design:**
The controlled system (CE) uses three controls:
- $u$: distributed control acting on interior subdomain $G_0 \subset G$ (localized in space)
- $v_1$: stochastic bulk control acting on diffusion term over entire domain $G$
- $v_2$: stochastic boundary control acting on diffusion term over entire boundary $\Gamma$

This is more flexible than approaches requiring controls only in the drift term. The paper acknowledges that investigating control problems "using only the control $u$, which acts exclusively on the drift term of the bulk equation, remains a challenging open problem" (Section 1), referencing partial results in [11, 31] under additional assumptions.

**Explicit Cost Estimates:**
Unlike prior work that establishes controllability existence without quantifying the control effort, this paper provides the explicit bound (1.15) on the null controllability cost. This is derived by "exploiting, for the first time, the explicit dependence of our Carleman constant on $T$... to obtain a sharp estimate on the problem data for the cost of null controllability" (Section 1).

**Open Problems Acknowledged:**
The paper is careful to identify what remains unresolved:
- **Non-tangential boundary vector fields:** Remark 1.1 notes that when the tangentiality condition (1.4) is not satisfied, additional boundary contributions arise that "cannot be controlled by the techniques developed in the present paper."
- **Control via drift term only:** As mentioned above, using only $u$ without $v_1, v_2$ remains open.
- **Surface diffusion necessity:** Remark 1.3 states that the surface diffusion term in the boundary equation is "crucial" for the Carleman estimate; without it (for $N \geq 2$), the control problems remain open.
- **Alternative energy functionals:** Remark 1.4 identifies that insensitizing control with a functional involving $\|\nabla y\|^2$ in the bulk "poses significant challenges" that the current Carleman estimate cannot handle.

By clearly delineating both achievements and limitations, the paper positions itself as a foundational advance that enables future work on these remaining open questions.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a rigorous analytical framework for deriving Carleman estimates and applying them to control problems in stochastic partial differential equations. The core idea is to construct weighted energy inequalities with carefully chosen parameters that allow absorption of lower-order terms, then leverage these inequalities through duality arguments to establish controllability results.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being analyzed is a backward stochastic parabolic equation where heat (or a similar diffusing quantity) evolves randomly over time within a spatial domain, with the boundary itself having its own dynamic evolution equation coupled to the interior. The problem this paper solves is establishing mathematical guarantees that we can control such systems to reach a desired state (null controllability) or make them insensitive to initial condition uncertainties (insensitizing control), and the solution takes the form of weighted energy inequalities (Carleman estimates) with explicit dependence on system parameters.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The technical architecture consists of five interconnected components that form a logical pipeline from foundational analysis to control applications. **Component 1 (Well-Posedness Analysis)** establishes that the backward stochastic parabolic equation with dynamic boundary conditions admits a unique weak solution with appropriate regularity bounds—this provides the mathematical foundation that all subsequent estimates operate on valid objects. **Component 2 (Weight Function Construction)** builds the exponential weight functions θ, ℓ, φ, α, γ with parameters λ and µ that will appear in the Carleman estimate—these weights are carefully designed to blow up near t = 0 and t = T while remaining controlled in the interior. **Component 3 (Intermediate Carleman Estimate)** derives a weighted energy inequality for the system without weak divergence source terms using a weighted identity method—this handles the anisotropic diffusion matrices and dynamic boundary coupling but assumes higher regularity on sources. **Component 4 (Main Carleman Estimate via Duality)** extends the intermediate estimate to incorporate H^{-1} weak divergence source terms through a duality method combined with optimization—this is the novel two-step approach that distinguishes this work from prior literature. **Component 5 (Control Applications)** applies the main Carleman estimate to derive observability inequalities, which through functional analytic duality yield null controllability and insensitizing control results with explicit cost bounds. Information flows sequentially: well-posedness validates the solution space, weight functions parameterize the estimates, the intermediate estimate handles the core PDE structure, the duality extension incorporates low-regularity sources, and finally the control applications translate estimates into actionable controllability statements.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Paper Type and Core Mathematical Idea:** This is an analytical mathematics paper in stochastic control theory that establishes weighted energy inequalities (Carleman estimates) for backward stochastic parabolic equations, then uses these inequalities through duality arguments to prove that controls exist which can drive the system to zero or make it insensitive to initial data perturbations.

**System Configuration and Domain Setup:** The spatial domain G is a nonempty bounded open subset of ℝ^N where N ≥ 2, with boundary Γ = ∂G of class C^4 (meaning the boundary is four times continuously differentiable, which ensures sufficient regularity for the differential operators). The final time T > 0 is fixed, and the time-space domains are Q := (0, T) × G for the bulk, Σ := (0, T) × Γ for the boundary, and Q0 := (0, T) × G0 for the control region where G0 ⋐ G denotes a nonempty open subset strictly contained in G. The probability space (Ω, F, {Ft}t≥0, P) is a complete filtered probability space supporting a one-dimensional standard Brownian motion W(·), where {Ft}t≥0 is the natural filtration generated by W(·) augmented by all P-null sets—this stochastic framework is essential because the equations include Itô differentials dW(t).

**Weight Function Construction (Equation 1.6):** The Carleman estimate relies on five weight functions that must be understood before any inequality makes sense. The temporal weight is γ(t) := (t(T − t))^{-1}, which blows up as t approaches 0 or T, creating the temporal localization needed for backward equations. The spatial-temporal weight is α(t, x) := (e^{µψ(x)} − e^{2µ∥ψ∥_∞})γ(t), where ψ is a special function from Lemma 1.1 satisfying ψ > 0 in G, ψ = 0 on Γ, and |∇ψ| > 0 in G \ G1 for some G1 ⋐ G. The exponential weight is θ := e^ℓ where ℓ := λα, combining the large parameters λ and µ with α. The auxiliary weight is φ(t, x) := e^{µψ(x)}γ(t), which appears in gradient terms. The parameters must satisfy µ ≥ µ0 and λ ≥ λ0(e^{2µ∥ψ∥_∞}T + T^2) for sufficiently large µ0, λ0 ≥ 1—these lower bounds ensure the weights are large enough to absorb lower-order terms in the estimates.

**Why These Specific Weight Functions:** The choice of γ(t) = (t(T − t))^{-1} is classical in Carleman estimates for parabolic equations because it creates singularities at both time endpoints, which is necessary for backward equations where terminal data is prescribed at t = T. The function ψ from Lemma 1.1 (due to Fursikov and Imanuvilov [19]) is a standard tool that provides spatial localization—it is positive in the interior, zero on the boundary, and has non-vanishing gradient away from a small set G1. The exponential form θ = e^{λα} amplifies small differences in α, making the weights sensitive to spatial variations through ψ. The parameters λ and µ are "large parameters" that can be chosen sufficiently large to dominate unwanted terms—this is the key mechanism by which Carleman estimates absorb lower-order contributions into the left-hand side of the inequality.

**The Backward Stochastic Parabolic Equation (System 1.2):** The core equation being analyzed is a system of two coupled stochastic differential equations. In the bulk domain Q, the equation is dz + ∑_{j,k=1}^N ∂/∂x_j(a_{jk}(x) ∂z/∂x_k) dt = (F1 + ∑_{j=1}^N ∂F_j/∂x_j) dt + Z dW(t). On the boundary Σ, the equation is dz_Γ + ∑_{j,k=1}^N D_j(a_{jk}^Γ(x) D_k z_Γ) dt − ∑_{j,k=1}^N a_{jk}(x) ∂z/∂x_j ν_k dt = (F2 − ∑_{j=1}^N F_j ν_j + ∑_{j=1}^N D_j F_{Γj}) dt + bZ dW(t). The coupling condition is z_Γ = z|_Γ on Σ, meaning the boundary state equals the trace of the bulk state. The terminal condition is (z, z_Γ)|_{t=T} = (z_T, z_{Γ,T}) in G × Γ. Here z is the bulk state, z_Γ is the boundary state, Z and bZ are stochastic correction terms (arising from the Itô formulation), and the terminal state belongs to L^2_{F_T}(Ω; L^2).

**Understanding the Differential Operators:** The bulk diffusion operator ∑_{j,k=1}^N ∂/∂x_j(a_{jk}(x) ∂z/∂x_k) represents anisotropic diffusion where the coefficients a_{jk} form a symmetric positive-definite matrix A = (a_{jk})_{1≤j,k≤N}. The boundary diffusion operator ∑_{j,k=1}^N D_j(a_{jk}^Γ(x) D_k z_Γ) represents tangential diffusion on the boundary manifold Γ, where D_j is the j-th tangential derivative defined by D_j := ∂/∂x_j − ν_j ∑_{k=1}^N ν_k ∂/∂x_k. The coupling term ∑_{j,k=1}^N a_{jk}(x) ∂z/∂x_j ν_k is the conormal derivative ∂^A_ν z, which represents the flux from the bulk into the boundary—this term appears with a negative sign in the boundary equation because outward bulk flux becomes inward boundary flux. The tangential derivative D_j represents the part of the standard partial derivative that is tangent to the surface, obtained by subtracting the normal component from the standard derivative.

**Diffusion Coefficient Assumptions (Equation 1.1):** The diffusion matrices must satisfy ellipticity conditions ensuring the operators are genuinely diffusive. For the bulk coefficients a_{jk} ∈ C^2(G), we require a_{jk} = a_{kj} (symmetry) and ∑_{j,k=1}^N a_{jk}(x)η_j η_k ≥ β0|η|^2 for all (x, η) ∈ G × ℝ^N, where β0 > 0 is a uniform ellipticity constant. For the boundary coefficients a_{jk}^Γ ∈ C^1(Γ), we require a_{jk}^Γ = a_{kj}^Γ (symmetry) and ∑_{j,k=1}^N a_{jk}^Γ(x)η_j η_k ≥ β0|η|^2 for all (x, η) ∈ Γ × ℝ^N. These conditions ensure the diffusion is uniformly positive in all directions, preventing degeneracy. The constants M = max_{1≤j,k≤N} ∥a_{jk}∥_{C^2(G)} and M_Γ = max_{1≤j,k≤N} ∥a_{jk}^Γ∥_{C^1(Γ)} bound the coefficient regularity and appear in estimate constants.

**Source Term Regularity and the H^{-1} Challenge:** The source terms F1, F2, F_j, F_{Γj} have different regularity levels that create analytical challenges. The terms F1 ∈ L^2_F(0, T; L^2(G)) and F2 ∈ L^2_F(0, T; L^2(Γ)) are standard L^2 sources in the bulk and boundary respectively. However, the terms F_j ∈ L^2_F(0, T; L^2(G)) and F_{Γj} ∈ L^2_F(0, T; L^2(Γ)) appear inside divergence operators as ∑_{j=1}^N ∂F_j/∂x_j and ∑_{j=1}^N D_j F_{Γj}. Since F_j and F_{Γj} are only in L^2, their divergences belong to H^{-1} (the dual of H^1), not L^2. This means the source terms are only defined in the distributional sense, which prevents direct application of standard Carleman techniques that require L^2 sources. The paper's two-step approach specifically addresses this: first derive an estimate without these divergence terms, then use duality to incorporate them.

**The Weighted Identity Method (Section 3):** The core analytical technique is the weighted identity method, which transforms the PDE into an exact identity before estimating. For the bulk operator, Theorem 3.1 provides a weighted identity for dz + ∇·(A∇z)dt. Setting θ = e^ℓ and h = θz, the identity relates 2θ[∇·(A∇h) + Ah][dz + ∇·(A∇z)dt] to various terms involving h, its derivatives, and the weight function derivatives. For the boundary operator, Theorem 3.2 provides a similar weighted identity for dz_Γ + ∇_Γ·(A_Γ∇_Γz_Γ)dt − ∂^A_ν z dt. These identities are exact equalities (not inequalities) that hold pointwise—they become inequalities only after integration and estimation. The key insight is that by choosing the weight functions appropriately, certain terms in the identity become positive and large (appearing on the left-hand side of the final estimate) while other terms can be bounded or absorbed.

**Auxiliary Functions in the Weighted Identity (Equation 3.2):** The weighted identity introduces auxiliary functions A, B, c_{jk}, eA, eB that encode the interaction between the weight functions and the differential operators. The function A = ∑_{j,k=1}^N(a_{jk}ℓ_{x_j}ℓ_{x_k} − a_{jk}_{x_k}ℓ_{x_j} − a_{jk}ℓ_{x_j x_k}) − Ψ − ℓ_t combines second derivatives of ℓ with the diffusion coefficients. The function B = 2[AΨ + ∑_{j,k=1}^N(A a_{jk}ℓ_{x_j})_{x_k}] − A_t + ∑_{j,k=1}^N(a_{jk}Ψ_{x_k})_{x_j} appears as a coefficient of h^2 in the identity. The coefficients c_{jk} = ∑_{j',k'=1}^N[2a_{jk'}(a_{j'k}ℓ_{x_{j'}})_{x_{k'}} − (a_{jk}a_{j'k'}ℓ_{x_{j'}})_{x_{k'}}] + a_{jk}_t/2 − Ψa_{jk} appear as coefficients of h_{x_j}h_{x_k}. The boundary quantities are eA = Φ − ℓ_t and eB = −2eAΦ − eA_t. The auxiliary functions Ψ and Φ are chosen as Ψ = −2∑_{j,k=1}^N a_{jk}ℓ_{x_j x_k} and Φ = (2/β0)M(Aν·ν)|∇ℓ| to optimize the estimates.

**Estimates for Weighted Identity Coefficients (Lemmas 3.1–3.2):** After choosing the weight functions, the coefficients A, B, c_{jk}, eA, eB can be estimated in terms of λ, µ, φ, and ψ. Lemma 3.1 shows that A = λ^2 µ^2 φ^2 ∑_{j,k=1}^N a_{jk}ψ_{x_j}ψ_{x_k} + λφO(µ^2) + λTφ^2 O(e^{2µ∥ψ∥_∞}), meaning the leading term is quadratic in λ and µ. The function B satisfies B ≥ 2β0^2 λ^3 µ^4 φ^3|∇ψ|^4 + λ^3 φ^3 O(µ^4) + λ^2 Tφ^3 O(µ^2 e^{2µ∥ψ∥_∞}), showing cubic growth in λ and quartic growth in µ—this high-order growth is essential for absorbing lower-order terms. The quadratic form ∑_{j,k=1}^N c_{jk}η_j η_k ≥ [β0^2 λµ^2 φ|∇ψ|^2 + λφO(µ)]|η|^2, ensuring positivity for large parameters. For the boundary, Lemma 3.2 shows eA = −(2/β0)Mλµφ∂^A_ν ψ + λTφ^2 O(e^{2µ∥ψ∥_∞}) and eB = −(8/β0^2)Mλ^2 µ^2 φ^2|∂^A_ν ψ|^2 + λ^2 Tφ^3 O(µ e^{2µ∥ψ∥_∞}) + λT^2 φ^3 O(e^{4µ∥ψ∥_∞}).

**Intermediate Carleman Estimate Without Divergence Terms (Lemma 3.3):** Before handling the H^{-1} sources, the paper derives an estimate for the simplified system where F_j = F_{Γj} = 0. The estimate (3.12) states that λ^3 µ^4 E∫∫_Q θ^2 φ^3|z|^2 dxdt + λ^3 µ^3 E∫∫_Σ θ^2 φ^3|z_Γ|^2 dσdt + λµ E∫∫_Q θ^2 φ|∇z|^2 dxdt + λµ E∫∫_Σ θ^2 φ|∇_Γ z_Γ|^2 dσdt ≤ C[λ^3 µ^4 E∫_0^T ∫_B θ^2 φ^3|z|^2 dxdt + E∫∫_Q θ^2|F1|^2 dxdt + E∫∫_Σ θ^2|F2|^2 dσdt + λ^2 µ^2 E∫∫_Q θ^2 φ^2|Z|^2 dxdt + λ^2 µ E∫∫_Σ θ^2 φ^2|bZ|^2 dσdt]. The left-hand side contains weighted norms of z, z_Γ, ∇z, and ∇_Γ z_Γ over the entire domain. The right-hand side contains a localized term over B ⋐ G (the observation region), source terms F1 and F2, and stochastic correction terms Z and bZ. The estimate holds for µ ≥ C and λ ≥ C(e^{2µ∥ψ∥_∞}T + T^2).

**Why the Left-Hand Side Has Higher Powers of λ and µ:** The powers of λ and µ on the left-hand side (λ^3 µ^4 for zero-order terms, λµ for gradient terms) are carefully chosen to match the growth of the coefficients A, B, c_{jk} from the weighted identity. The term B h^2 in the identity contributes λ^3 µ^4 φ^3|z|^2 after substituting h = θz and estimating B. The term ∑ c_{jk} h_{x_j} h_{x_k} contributes λµ^2 φ|∇z|^2. When integrating the identity and moving terms to the left-hand side, these become the dominant positive terms. The right-hand side terms have lower powers (λ^2 µ^2 for Z and bZ terms) because they come from different parts of the identity that grow more slowly. This power mismatch is intentional—it allows the left-hand side to dominate the right-hand side when λ and µ are sufficiently large.

**Boundary Term Treatment in Lemma 3.3 Proof:** The proof of Lemma 3.3 requires careful handling of nine boundary integral terms I_1 through I_9 that arise from integrating the weighted identities. Term I_1 = E∫∫_Σ eB h_Γ^2 dσdt involves the boundary coefficient eB, which is negative but can be bounded below by −Cλ^2 µ^2 E∫∫_Σ θ^2 φ^2 z_Γ^2 dσdt minus higher-order terms. Term I_2 = 2β0 E∫∫_Σ Φ|∇_Γ h_Γ|^2 dσdt = −4λµM E∫∫_Σ θ^2 φ ∂^A_ν ψ|∇_Γ z_Γ|^2 dσdt provides a positive contribution for the boundary gradient. Terms I_3^1, I_3^2, I_3^3 come from the bulk identity's boundary contributions and involve combinations of z_Γ, ∂^A_ν z, and ∇_Γ z_Γ. The key challenge is that terms like ∂^A_ν z (the conormal derivative) appear on the boundary but are not directly controlled by the left-hand side—the estimate must use the coupling between bulk and boundary equations to bound these terms. The surface diffusion term ∇_Γ·(A_Γ∇_Γ z_Γ) is crucial here because it provides control over |∇_Γ z_Γ|^2 on the boundary, which helps absorb problematic boundary integrals.

**The Two-Step Duality Method for H^{-1} Sources (Section 4):** The main innovation is extending the intermediate estimate to include the weak divergence sources F_j and F_{Γj}. Step 1 uses the intermediate estimate from Section 3, which assumes F_j = F_{Γj} = 0. Step 2 employs a duality method combined with optimization to incorporate these terms. The approach considers a controlled forward stochastic parabolic equation (4.1): dy − ∇·(A∇y)dt = (λ^3 µ^4 θ^2 φ^3 z + 1_B u)dt + v_1 dW(t) in Q, with corresponding boundary equation and controls (u, v_1, v_2). Proposition 4.1 establishes that there exist controls such that (y(T, ·), y_Γ(T, ·)) = (0, 0) with an energy bound (4.2) relating the control norms to the weighted norms of z and z_Γ. The proof of Theorem 1.1 then applies Itô's formula to d⟨(y, y_Γ), (z, z_Γ)⟩_{L^2}, integrating and using the controllability result to bound the terms involving F_j and F_{Γj}. This duality approach avoids directly estimating the divergence terms, instead transferring them to the controlled forward equation where they can be handled through the control energy bound.

**Why Duality Works for H^{-1} Sources:** The duality method succeeds because it transforms the problem of estimating divergence terms into a problem of bounding control energies. When F_j appears as ∂F_j/∂x_j in the backward equation, integrating by parts moves the derivative onto the test function (the forward solution y). Since y has H^1 regularity (from the well-posedness theory), the pairing ⟨F_j, ∂y/∂x_j⟩ is well-defined even though F_j is only in L^2. The Carleman estimate for the forward equation (obtained through the controllability result) provides bounds on ∇y in terms of the observation region, which allows the F_j terms to be absorbed into the right-hand side of the main estimate. This is fundamentally different from [29], where Carleman estimates relax regularity assumptions on diffusion source terms—here the approach is specifically designed for weak divergence-type sources.

**Main Carleman Estimate (Theorem 1.1, Equation 1.8):** The final estimate combines all the previous steps. It states that λ^3 µ^4 E∫∫_Q θ^2 φ^3|z|^2 dxdt + λ^3 µ^3 E∫∫_Σ θ^2 φ^3|z_Γ|^2 dσdt + λµ E∫∫_Q θ^2 φ|∇z|^2 dxdt + λµ E∫∫_Σ θ^2 φ|∇_Γ z_Γ|^2 dσdt ≤ C[λ^3 µ^4 E∫_0^T ∫_B θ^2 φ^3|z|^2 dxdt + E∫∫_Q θ^2|F1|^2 dxdt + E∫∫_Σ θ^2|F2|^2 dσdt + λ^2 µ^2 ∑_{j=1}^N E∫∫_Q θ^2 φ^2|F_j|^2 dxdt + λ^2 µ^2 ∑_{j=1}^N E∫∫_Σ θ^2 φ^2|F_{Γj}|^2 dσdt + λ^2 µ^2 E∫∫_Q θ^2 φ^2|Z|^2 dxdt + λ^2 µ^2 E∫∫_Σ θ^2 φ^2|bZ|^2 dσdt]. The key addition compared to Lemma 3.3 is the terms involving F_j and F_{Γj} with coefficient λ^2 µ^2. The estimate holds for µ ≥ µ0 and λ ≥ λ0(e^{2µ∥ψ∥_∞}T + T^2).

**From Carleman Estimate to Observability Inequality (Section 5):** The Carleman estimate is not directly useful for control—it must be converted into an observability inequality. For the adjoint backward equation (5.1) associated with the controlled forward system (CE), Corollary 5.1 applies Theorem 1.1 with specific coefficients F1 = −a_1 z, F = zB_1, F2 = −a_2 z_Γ, F_Γ = z_Γ B_2. After absorbing the lower-order terms into the left-hand side (requiring λ ≥ λ0[T + T^2(1 + ∥a_1∥_∞^{2/3} + ∥a_2∥_∞^{2/3} + ∥B_1∥_∞^2 + ∥B_2∥_∞^2)]), the estimate simplifies to (5.3). Proposition 5.1 then derives the observability inequality (5.7): E∥z(0, ·)∥^2_{L^2(G)} + E∥z_Γ(0, ·)∥^2_{L^2(Γ)} ≤ e^{CK}[E∫∫_{Q0} |z|^2 dxdt + E∫∫_Q |Z|^2 dxdt + E∫∫_Σ |bZ|^2 dσdt], where K = 1 + 1/T + ∥a_1∥_∞^{2/3} + ∥a_2∥_∞^{2/3} + T(∥a_1∥_∞ + ∥a_2∥_∞) + (1 + T)(∥B_1∥_∞^2 + ∥B_2∥_∞^2). This inequality states that the initial energy can be bounded by observations over the control region Q0 plus the stochastic correction terms.

**The Functional Analytic Duality (Lemma 5.1):** The observability inequality translates to controllability through a classical functional analysis result. Lemma 5.1 states that for Hilbert spaces H, U, Y and bounded linear operators M ∈ L(H; Y) and L ∈ L(U; Y), the inequality ∥M^* y∥_H ≤ C∥L^* y∥_U for all y ∈ Y holds if and only if R(M) ⊂ R(L) with ∥u∥_U ≤ C∥x_0∥_H for the preimage. In the control context, M_T maps initial data to terminal state (without control), and L_T maps controls to terminal state (with zero initial data). The observability inequality (5.16) shows ∥M_T^*(z_T, z_{Γ,T})∥^2 ≤ e^{CK}∥L_T^*(z_T, z_{Γ,T})∥^2, where M_T^*(z_T, z_{Γ,T}) = (z(0, ·), z_Γ(0, ·)) and L_T^*(z_T, z_{Γ,T}) = (1_{G0} z, Z, bZ). By Lemma 5.1, this implies R(M_T) ⊂ R(L_T), meaning for any initial state there exists a control driving the system to zero at time T.

**Null Controllability Cost Estimate (Theorem 1.2, Equations 1.13–1.15):** The duality argument not only proves existence of controls but provides an explicit bound on their energy. Theorem 1.2 states that for any initial state Y_0 ∈ L^2_{F_0}(Ω; L^2), there exists a control triple (bu, bv_1, bv_2) such that the solution satisfies (by(T, ·), by_Γ(T, ·)) = (0, 0) almost surely, with energy bound E(bu, bv_1, bv_2) ≤ exp(CK_T) E∥Y_0∥^2_{L^2}. The constant K_T is given explicitly by K_T := 1 + 1/T + ∥a_1∥_∞^{2/3} + ∥a_2∥_∞^{2/3} + T(∥a_1∥_∞ + ∥a_2∥_∞) + (1 + T)(∥B_1∥_∞^2 + ∥B_2∥_∞^2). Corollary 1.1 defines the null controllability cost K(Y_0, G_0) as the infimum of control energies over all admissible controls, and shows K(Y_0, G_0) ≤ exp(CK_T) E∥Y_0∥^2_{L^2}. This explicit dependence on T and the coefficients is novel—prior work established controllability without quantifying the control effort required.

**Insensitizing Control Problem Reduction (Section 6):** The insensitizing control problem asks for controls such that the energy functional E(y, y_Γ) from Equation (1.10) is insensitive to small perturbations of the initial data. Definition 1.1 formalizes this as requiring ∂E/∂τ_1|_{τ_1=τ_2=0} = ∂E/∂τ_2|_{τ_1=τ_2=0} = 0 for all perturbations (by_0, by_{Γ,0}) with E∥(by_0, by_{Γ,0})∥^2_{L^2} = 1. The key insight is that this problem reduces to a null controllability problem for a cascade system (6.1) of coupled forward-backward equations. The forward equation for (y, y_Γ) is the original controlled system, while the backward equation for (z, z_Γ) includes coupling terms −1_O y and −1_{O_1^Γ} y_Γ − 1_{O_2^Γ} ∇_Γ y_Γ from the sentinel functional derivatives. The insensitizing condition is equivalent to (z(0, ·), z_Γ(0, ·)) = (0, 0).

**Coupled System Carleman Estimate (Proposition 6.2):** The cascade system requires a specialized Carleman estimate because the forward and backward equations are coupled through zeroth, first, and second-order terms. Proposition 6.2 provides estimate (6.5) for the adjoint coupled system (6.3), which involves variables (p, p_Γ, P, bP) for the backward part and (q, q_Γ) for the forward part. The estimate has the form λ^3 E∫∫_Q θ^2 γ^3|p|^2 dxdt + λ^3 E∫∫_Σ θ^2 γ^3|p_Γ|^2 dσdt + λ^4 E∫∫_Q θ^2 γ^4|q|^2 dxdt + λ^4 E∫∫_Σ θ^2 γ^4|q_Γ|^2 dσdt + lower-order gradient terms ≤ C[λ^{13} E∫∫_{Q0} θ^2 γ^{13}|p|^2 dxdt + λ^6 E∫∫_Q θ^2 γ^6|P|^2 dxdt + λ^2 E∫∫_Σ θ^2 γ^2|bP|^2 dσdt]. The powers of λ differ between p and q terms (λ^3 vs λ^4) because the coupling requires different weight scalings to absorb the interaction terms. The proof combines the main Carleman estimate (1.8) for the backward equation with a forward Carleman estimate (Lemma 6.1) for the forward equation, then uses cut-off functions ρ_1, ρ_2 and Itô's formula to absorb the localized coupling terms.

**Why Different λ Powers for p and q:** The forward variable q requires λ^4 weighting while the backward variable p requires λ^3 weighting because of how the coupling terms appear in the equations. The backward equation for p includes −1_O q, which is a zeroth-order coupling. When applying the Carleman estimate to p, this q term appears on the right-hand side and must be absorbed using the forward estimate for q. The forward estimate for q has λ^4 on the left-hand side, so when combined, the q terms can be absorbed provided λ is sufficiently large. If both used the same power, the coupling terms could not be absorbed—the mismatch in powers is essential for the cascade structure. The final estimate (6.20) shows λ^{13} on the observation term for p, which comes from iteratively absorbing boundary terms through the cut-off function technique.

**Insensitizing Control Existence (Theorem 1.3):** The final result establishes existence of insensitizing controls under the condition G_0 ∩ O ≠ ∅ (the control region must overlap with the observation region for the sentinel functional). Theorem 1.3 states that for source terms ξ, ξ_Γ satisfying E∫∫_Q exp(Kt^{−1})|ξ|^2 dxdt < ∞ and E∫∫_Σ exp(Kt^{−1})|ξ_Γ|^2 dσdt < ∞, there exists a control triple (u, v_1, v_2) that insensitizes the functional E. The controls satisfy the energy bound (1.17): E(u, v_1, v_2) ≤ C[E∫∫_Q exp(Kt^{−1})|ξ|^2 dxdt + E∫∫_Σ exp(Kt^{−1})|ξ_Γ|^2 dσdt]. The proof uses the observability inequality (6.4) to define a bounded linear functional L on the space of adjoint solutions, extends L via Hahn-Banach, and applies Riesz representation to obtain the controls. The exponential weight exp(Kt^{−1}) in the source term conditions reflects the singular behavior of the Carleman weights near t = 0.

**Design Choice: Why Three Controls Instead of One:** The controlled system (CE) uses three controls: u (distributed in G_0), v_1 (stochastic bulk), and v_2 (stochastic boundary). This is more than some prior work that uses only a drift control u. The reason is technical: the Carleman estimate produces observation terms for z, Z, and bZ on the right-hand side. To convert this to controllability, the duality requires controls corresponding to each observation term. The paper acknowledges that "investigating these control problems using only the control u, which acts exclusively on the drift term of the bulk equation, remains a challenging open problem" (Section 1), referencing partial results in [11, 31] under additional assumptions of space-independent coefficients. The three-control setup is a deliberate choice to make the controllability proof work with the available Carleman estimate structure.

**Design Choice: Why Surface Diffusion is Necessary:** Remark 1.3 explains that the surface diffusion term ∑_{j,k=1}^N D_j(a_{jk}^Γ D_k z_Γ) in the boundary equation is crucial for the Carleman estimate. This term is essential for absorbing the problematic boundary integral λµ E∫∫_Σ θ^2 φ|∂^A_ν ψ||∇_Γ z_Γ|^2 dσdt that arises in Step 2 of Lemma 3.3's proof. Without surface diffusion (i.e., if the boundary equation were dz_Γ + ∂^A_ν z dt = ... without the ∇_Γ·(A_Γ∇_Γ z_Γ) term), this integral could not be controlled. The paper notes that for N = 1, controllability can be addressed without surface diffusion because the boundary Γ is zero-dimensional and ∇_Γ = 0 trivially. For N ≥ 2, the null and insensitizing control problems without surface diffusion remain open.

**Design Choice: Tangentiality Assumption (Equation 1.4):** The boundary vector fields A_Γ∇_Γ z_Γ and F_Γ are assumed tangential to the manifold Γ, meaning f_Γ(x) ∈ T_x Γ = {y ∈ ℝ^N | y·ν(x) = 0} for each x ∈ Γ. This assumption is crucial because it allows use of the standard tangential divergence formula ∫_Γ(∇_Γ·f_Γ)z_Γ dσ = −∫_Γ f_Γ·∇_Γ z_Γ dσ without additional boundary terms. Remark 1.1 explains that without tangentiality, the generalized formula includes an extra term ∫_Γ(∇_Γ·ν)(f_Γ·ν)z_Γ dσ involving the normal component f_Γ·ν. These extra terms "cannot be controlled by the techniques developed in the present paper" and remain an open problem. This is a deliberate limitation—the paper prioritizes establishing the estimate for the tangential case rather than attempting (and potentially failing) to handle the non-tangential case.

**Parameter Dependencies and Constant Tracking:** Throughout the estimates, the paper carefully tracks how constants depend on system parameters. The generic constant C depends only on G, G_0, β_0, M, and M_Γ (the domain geometry, ellipticity constant, and coefficient bounds). The large parameters must satisfy µ ≥ µ_0 and λ ≥ λ_0(e^{2µ∥ψ∥_∞}T + T^2), where µ_0 and λ_0 depend on the same geometric and coefficient parameters. The controllability cost constant K_T depends explicitly on T, ∥a_1∥_∞, ∥a_2∥_∞, ∥B_1∥_∞, and ∥B_2∥_∞. This explicit tracking is essential for the quantitative control cost estimate—without knowing how the Carleman constant scales with T, the exponential bound exp(CK_T) could not be derived. Prior work like [7] did not provide such explicit T-dependence.

**Why the Estimate Has Localized Observation on the Right-Hand Side:** The right-hand side of the Carleman estimate (1.8) contains a term λ^3 µ^4 E∫_0^T ∫_B θ^2 φ^3|z|^2 dxdt localized to a subset B ⋐ G, rather than an integral over the entire domain Q. This localization is fundamental to controllability—it means the global weighted energy can be bounded by observations in a small region. In control theory terms, this corresponds to the fact that a localized control u acting only on G_0 can influence the entire system through the parabolic dynamics. The set B in the Carleman estimate corresponds to the control region G_0 in the controllability result. Without this localization, the estimate would not yield meaningful controllability (it would require observation/control everywhere).

**The Role of Stochastic Correction Terms Z and bZ:** The backward stochastic equation includes correction terms Z and bZ multiplying dW(t). These arise from the Itô formulation of backward stochastic differential equations—they represent the martingale part of the solution that cannot be determined from the terminal condition alone. In the Carleman estimate, these terms appear on the right-hand side with coefficient λ^2 µ^2. When applying the estimate to the adjoint equation for controllability, Z and bZ become part of the observation (they appear in L_T^* in the duality argument). In the controlled forward system, the corresponding terms v_1 and v_2 are the stochastic controls. The presence of these terms is a key difference from deterministic Carleman estimates—they reflect the additional degrees of freedom in stochastic control (one can control both the drift and diffusion of the state).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper introduces three fundamental innovations that distinguish it from prior literature on stochastic controllability. These are not merely incremental improvements in constants or domain shapes; they represent structural shifts in how Carleman estimates are constructed and applied to complex boundary-value problems. Below, we dissect the core insights that enable these advances, contrasting them with the limitations of previous approaches.

### 4.1 Strategic Avoidance of Direct Source Estimation

The most significant methodological innovation is the **two-step duality framework** used to handle weak divergence source terms in $H^{-1}$ spaces. Prior Carleman estimates for stochastic equations, such as [7, Theorem 4.1], required source terms to be in $L^2$ because the estimation process involves squaring the source terms directly. If a source term is a divergence $\nabla \cdot F$ with $F \in L^2$, the term belongs to $H^{-1}$, and direct squaring is ill-defined or requires integration by parts that generate uncontrollable boundary terms.

*   **The Prior Limitation:** Works like [29] used Carleman estimates to relax regularity assumptions on *diffusion* source terms, but they still relied on direct estimation of the drift sources. For dynamic boundary conditions, direct estimation of $\nabla \cdot F$ generates boundary integrals involving the normal component of $F$, which cannot be absorbed by the standard Carleman weights.
*   **The New Insight:** This paper realizes that **you do not need to estimate the source term directly if you can estimate the control that cancels it.** By introducing a controlled forward system (Equation 4.1) and applying a duality argument (Section 4), the authors transfer the divergence term $\nabla \cdot F$ onto the test function (the forward state $y$). Since the forward state has $H^1$ regularity (established in Section 2), the pairing $\langle F, \nabla y \rangle$ is well-defined even if $F$ is only in $L^2$.
*   **Why It Matters:** This shifts the burden from *regularity of the input* to *regularity of the solution*. It allows the theory to handle physically realistic forcing terms (like point sources or surface fluxes modeled as distributions) that were previously excluded from stochastic controllability theory. This is a fundamental change in strategy: instead of strengthening the estimate to handle rougher sources, the authors change the problem formulation to bypass the need for the estimate on the source itself.

### 4.2 Explicit Time-Dependence for Quantitative Control Costs

A second critical innovation is the **explicit tracking of the final time $T$** within the Carleman parameters and constants. In many controllability proofs, the Carleman constant $C$ is treated as a generic positive number that may depend on $T$ in an unknown or implicit way. This is sufficient for proving *existence* of controls but useless for quantifying the *cost* of control.

*   **The Prior Limitation:** Previous works on stochastic parabolic equations with dynamic boundary conditions (e.g., [5], [7]) established null controllability but did not provide explicit bounds on the control energy. Without knowing how the Carleman constant scales with $T$, one cannot determine how the control effort grows as the time horizon shrinks or expands.
*   **The New Insight:** The authors carefully construct the weight functions (Equation 1.6) and derive the lower bounds for parameters $\lambda$ and $\mu$ such that the dependence on $T$ is explicit. Specifically, the parameter $\lambda$ must satisfy:
    $$
    \lambda \geq \lambda_0 \left( e^{2\mu\|\psi\|_\infty}T + T^2 \right).
    $$
    This explicit scaling propagates through the duality argument to yield the controllability cost constant $K_T$ in Equation 1.14:
    $$
    K_T := 1 + \frac{1}{T} + \|a_1\|_\infty^{2/3} + \|a_2\|_\infty^{2/3} + T(\|a_1\|_\infty + \|a_2\|_\infty) + (1 + T)(\|B_1\|_\infty^2 + \|B_2\|_\infty^2).
    $$
*   **Why It Matters:** This transforms the result from a qualitative statement ("a control exists") to a quantitative engineering bound ("the control energy is bounded by $\exp(C K_T)$"). The term $1/T$ indicates that the cost blows up as $T \to 0$, which is consistent with the physical intuition that driving a diffusive system to zero instantly requires infinite energy. This explicit dependence is essential for applications where control effort must be optimized against time constraints.

### 4.3 Geometric Tangentiality to Stabilize Anisotropic Boundaries

The third innovation lies in the **use of tangentiality constraints to manage anisotropic boundary diffusion**. When diffusion is anisotropic (direction-dependent), represented by matrices $A$ and $A_\Gamma$, the boundary coupling terms become geometrically complex. The conormal derivative $\partial^A_\nu z$ involves the matrix $A$ acting on the normal vector $\nu$, which does not necessarily align with $\nu$ itself.

*   **The Prior Limitation:** Isotropic models (where $A = I_N$) simplify the boundary terms because the normal derivative $\partial_\nu z$ aligns with the geometry. For anisotropic models, integration by parts on the boundary generates extra terms involving the normal component of the boundary vector fields. Prior Carleman estimates for dynamic boundary conditions typically avoided this by assuming isotropy or static boundaries.
*   **The New Insight:** The authors impose a **tangentiality assumption** (Equation 1.4) on the boundary vector fields $A_\Gamma \nabla_\Gamma z_\Gamma$ and $F_\Gamma$. This ensures that these vectors lie in the tangent plane $T_x \Gamma$, meaning their normal component is zero. This allows the use of the standard tangential divergence formula:
    $$
    \int_\Gamma (\nabla_\Gamma \cdot f_\Gamma) z_\Gamma \, d\sigma = - \int_\Gamma f_\Gamma \cdot \nabla_\Gamma z_\Gamma \, d\sigma,
    $$
    without additional curvature terms involving $\nabla_\Gamma \cdot \nu$.
*   **Why It Matters:** This geometric constraint is a deliberate design choice that stabilizes the boundary integrals in the Carleman estimate (Lemma 3.3). As noted in **Remark 1.1**, relaxing this assumption introduces extra boundary contributions that "cannot be controlled by the techniques developed in the present paper." By identifying this boundary explicitly, the paper clarifies the geometric limits of the current theory. It enables the modeling of surface diffusion on textured or crystalline surfaces where diffusion rates vary by direction, provided the flux remains tangential to the surface manifold.

### 4.4 Summary of Innovation Levels

| Innovation | Type | Significance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Duality for $H^{-1}$ Sources** | Fundamental | Enables control theory for distributional forces (e.g., point sources) previously excluded from stochastic frameworks. |
| **Explicit Time-Scaling** | Quantitative | Converts existence proofs into usable cost bounds, revealing the $\exp(1/T)$ scaling of control effort. |
| **Tangential Anisotropy** | Structural | Extends controllability to direction-dependent surface diffusion while managing geometric complexity via constraints. |

These insights collectively move the field from analyzing idealized, isotropic, high-regularity stochastic systems to handling rougher, more geometrically complex systems that better reflect physical reality. The trade-off is the introduction of specific constraints (tangentiality, three controls) which define the boundary of the paper's applicability.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This paper is a **theoretical mathematics work** in the field of stochastic control and partial differential equations. Consequently, it does not contain numerical experiments, datasets, simulations, or empirical figures in the traditional sense. The "validation" of the claims relies on rigorous logical proofs, derivation of explicit constants, and comparison with prior theoretical results. In this section, we analyze the paper's "results" by examining the explicit bounds derived, the mathematical settings tested (via assumptions), and the limitations identified by the authors.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: Theoretical Framework and Setup

Since there are no numerical datasets, the "experimental setup" is defined by the mathematical spaces and conditions under which the theorems hold. The authors validate their approach by constructing proofs that remain valid under specific, often restrictive, assumptions.

*   **Domain Configuration:** The spatial domain $G$ is a nonempty bounded open subset of $\mathbb{R}^N$ with $N \geq 2$, and the boundary $\Gamma = \partial G$ is of class $C^4$ (Section 1). This high regularity ($C^4$) is a strict requirement compared to some prior works that may accept $C^2$ boundaries, ensuring the tangential derivatives and curvature terms are well-defined.
*   **Probability Space:** The analysis operates on a complete filtered probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \{\mathcal{F}_t\}_{t \geq 0}, \mathbb{P})$ supporting a one-dimensional standard Brownian motion $W(\cdot)$ (Section 1). This is the standard "testbed" for stochastic parabolic equations.
*   **Metrics of Success:** Instead of accuracy or loss functions, the "metrics" are the **explicit constants** in the inequalities. The primary metric is the controllability cost constant $K_T$ (Equation 1.14), which quantifies the energy required to control the system. A "successful" result is one where $K_T$ is finite and explicitly dependent on the time horizon $T$ and system coefficients.
*   **Baselines (Prior Theoretical Results):** The paper compares its results against specific prior theorems:
    *   **[7, Theorem 4.1]:** Handles isotropic diffusion ($a_{jk} = \delta_{jk}$) and no weak divergence terms ($F_j = 0$).
    *   **[29]:** Handles Dirichlet boundary conditions and relaxes regularity on diffusion sources, but not dynamic boundary conditions.
    *   **[5]:** Handles backward equations with convection but lacks the explicit $H^{-1}$ source term treatment combined with dynamic boundaries.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results: Explicit Constants and Estimates

The core "quantitative" contribution of this paper is the derivation of explicit dependencies in the Carleman estimate and controllability cost. These numbers determine the practical feasibility of the control (e.g., how much energy is needed as time $T$ shrinks).

**1. The Carleman Estimate Parameters (Theorem 1.1, Eq. 1.8)**
The main Carleman estimate (1.8) balances weighted norms of the state $(z, z_\Gamma)$ against source terms. The powers of the large parameters $\lambda$ and $\mu$ are critical for ensuring the left-hand side dominates the right-hand side.
*   **Left-Hand Side Powers:** The zero-order terms $|z|^2$ and $|z_\Gamma|^2$ are weighted by $\lambda^3 \mu^4$ and $\lambda^3 \mu^3$, respectively. The gradient terms $|\nabla z|^2$ and $|\nabla_\Gamma z_\Gamma|^2$ are weighted by $\lambda \mu$.
*   **Right-Hand Side Powers:** The weak divergence source terms $|F_j|^2$ and $|F_{\Gamma j}|^2$ appear with weight $\lambda^2 \mu^2$.
*   **Parameter Thresholds:** The estimate holds only when the parameters exceed specific thresholds:
    $$
    \mu \geq \mu_0 \quad \text{and} \quad \lambda \geq \lambda_0 \left( e^{2\mu\|\psi\|_\infty}T + T^2 \right).
    $$
    This explicit dependence on $T$ is a key quantitative result; it shows that as the time horizon $T$ changes, the required weight magnitude $\lambda$ must adjust quadratically or exponentially depending on the term.

**2. The Null Controllability Cost (Theorem 1.2, Eq. 1.14–1.15)**
The paper provides an explicit bound on the minimal control energy required to drive the system to zero. This is the most actionable "number" in the paper for potential applications.
*   **Cost Bound:** The control energy satisfies $\mathcal{E}(\hat{u}, \hat{v}_1, \hat{v}_2) \leq \exp(C K_T) \, \mathbb{E}\|Y_0\|_{L^2}^2$.
*   **The Constant $K_T$:** Defined in Equation (1.14) as:
    $$
    K_T := 1 + \frac{1}{T} + \|a_1\|_\infty^{2/3} + \|a_2\|_\infty^{2/3} + T(\|a_1\|_\infty + \|a_2\|_\infty) + (1 + T)(\|B_1\|_\infty^2 + \|B_2\|_\infty^2).
    $$
*   **Interpretation:**
    *   The term **$1/T$** indicates that the control cost blows up exponentially as the time horizon $T \to 0$. This is consistent with physical intuition for diffusive systems (you cannot cool a hot plate instantly without infinite energy).
    *   The terms **$\|a_i\|_\infty^{2/3}$** and **$\|B_i\|_\infty^2$** show how reaction ($a_i$) and convection ($B_i$) coefficients increase the cost. Stronger convection requires more control effort.
    *   Prior works (e.g., [7]) established controllability but did not provide this explicit formula for $K_T$.

**3. Insensitizing Control Conditions (Theorem 1.3, Eq. 1.16–1.17)**
For the insensitizing control problem, the source terms $\xi, \xi_\Gamma$ must satisfy weighted integrability conditions:
$$
\mathbb{E} \iint_Q \exp(K t^{-1}) |\xi|^2 \, dx \, dt < \infty.
$$
The weight $\exp(K t^{-1})$ imposes a strict decay requirement on the sources near $t=0$. This is a quantitative constraint on the admissible external forces for the insensitizing control to exist.

*   **Key Improvement:** The ability to handle **$H^{-1}$ bulk-surface source terms** (weak divergence) is the primary differentiator from [7]. As stated in Section 1, [7] "is not sufficient to address the control problems considered in this paper" because it cannot handle the $F_j$ terms.
*   **Key Improvement:** The **explicit $T$-dependence** distinguishes this work from most prior stochastic controllability results, which typically prove existence without quantifying the cost scaling.

### 5.4 Limitations, Failure Cases, and Robustness Checks

The authors explicitly identify conditions under which their "experimental" (theoretical) setup fails. These are critical for understanding the boundaries of the paper's applicability.

**1. Non-Tangential Boundary Vector Fields (Remark 1.1)**
*   **Condition:** The boundary vector fields $A_\Gamma \nabla_\Gamma z_\Gamma$ and $F_\Gamma$ must be tangential to the manifold $\Gamma$ (Equation 1.4).
*   **Failure Case:** If the tangentiality condition is not satisfied, the divergence formula generates an extra term:
    $$
    \int_\Gamma (\nabla_\Gamma \cdot \nu) (f_\Gamma \cdot \nu) z_\Gamma \, d\sigma.
    $$
*   **Outcome:** The authors state explicitly: "These terms cannot be controlled by the techniques developed in the present paper... the analysis of non-tangential boundary vector fields... remains unsolved." This is a hard limitation of the current method.

**2. Necessity of Surface Diffusion (Remark 1.3)**
*   **Condition:** The boundary equation must include the surface diffusion term $\nabla_\Gamma \cdot (A_\Gamma \nabla_\Gamma z_\Gamma)$.
*   **Failure Case:** If the boundary condition is simplified to $dz_\Gamma + \partial^A_\nu z \, dt = \dots$ (without surface diffusion), the Carleman estimate fails for $N \geq 2$.
*   **Reason:** The surface diffusion term is required to absorb the problematic boundary integral $\lambda \mu \mathbb{E} \iint_\Sigma \theta^2 \phi |\partial^A_\nu \psi| |\nabla_\Gamma z_\Gamma|^2 \, d\sigma \, dt$ in Lemma 3.3.
*   **Exception:** For $N=1$, the boundary is zero-dimensional, $\nabla_\Gamma = 0$ trivially, and controllability holds without surface diffusion (citing [6]). For $N \geq 2$, this remains an **open problem**.

**3. Alternative Energy Functionals (Remark 1.4)**
*   **Condition:** The insensitizing control uses a sentinel functional involving $|y|^2$ and $|\nabla_\Gamma y_\Gamma|^2$ (Equation 1.10).
*   **Failure Case:** If the functional involves the bulk gradient norm $\|\nabla y\|^2$ (i.e., $\tilde{\mathcal{E}}$ in Remark 1.4), the Carleman estimate (1.8) is insufficient.
*   **Reason:** Controlling a localized term $\mathbb{E} \int_0^T \int_{\tilde{G}} \kappa |z|^2 \, dx \, dt$ using a higher-order term $\mathbb{E} \int_0^T \int_G \kappa |\Delta z|^2 \, dx \, dt$ poses significant challenges not addressed here.

**4. Control Mechanism Constraints**
*   **Condition:** The null controllability result (Theorem 1.2) relies on three controls: $(u, v_1, v_2)$.
*   **Limitation:** Using **only the distributed control $u$** (acting on the drift term in $G_0$) without the stochastic controls $v_1, v_2$ is an **open problem**.
*   **Context:** Partial results exist in [11, 31] but require additional assumptions (e.g., space-independent coefficients). This paper does not resolve the single-control case for general anisotropic systems.

### 5.5 Assessment of Claims

*   **Claim:** "First to establish Carleman estimate (1.8) for backward anisotropic stochastic parabolic equations with... $H^{-1}$ bulk-surface source terms."
    *   **Assessment:** **Supported.** The comparison with [7] in Section 1 confirms that prior estimates excluded the $F_j$ divergence terms. The proof in Section 4 explicitly constructs the duality argument to handle these terms.
*   **Claim:** "Provide an explicit estimate of the null controllability cost."
    *   **Assessment:** **Supported.** Equation (1.14) provides the explicit $K_T$. This is verifiable directly from the text and contrasts with the generic constants in cited works like [5] or [40].
*   **Claim:** "Address... an insensitizing control problem."
    *   **Assessment:** **Supported with Conditions.** Theorem 1.3 proves existence, but only under the strict source decay condition (1.16) and the geometric condition $G_0 \cap \mathcal{O} \neq \emptyset$. The limitations in Remark 1.4 clarify that not all energy functionals are covered.

In summary, while there are no numerical experiments, the theoretical "results" are robustly validated through explicit constant derivation and clear delineation of failure cases (Remarks 1.1, 1.3, 1.4). The paper successfully demonstrates that the proposed two-step Carleman estimate method extends the boundary of solvable control problems, provided the geometric and regularity constraints are met.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper achieves significant theoretical advances in stochastic controllability, the results come with important assumptions, constraints, and unresolved questions. Understanding these limitations is essential for assessing when the theory applies and where future work is needed.

### 6.1 Geometric and Regularity Assumptions

**Boundary Smoothness Requirement (C⁴)**

The paper requires the boundary Γ = ∂G to be of class C⁴ (Section 1). This is a **stronger regularity assumption** than many prior works on parabolic equations, which often accept C² boundaries. The justification is that tangential derivatives up to second order appear in the boundary equation, and the Carleman estimate derivation involves differentiating weight functions that depend on the boundary geometry.

*   **Trade-off:** Higher boundary regularity ensures the tangential derivative operators Dⱼ and the surface measure dσ are well-defined throughout the analysis. However, this excludes domains with corners, edges, or less smooth boundaries—common in practical engineering geometries.
*   **Evidence:** Section 1 states: "Let G ⊂ ℝ^N be a nonempty bounded open domain with boundary Γ = ∂G of class C⁴, where N ≥ 2."

**Tangentiality Constraint on Boundary Vector Fields (Remark 1.1)**

The boundary vector fields A_Γ∇_Γz_Γ and F_Γ must be **tangential to the manifold Γ**, meaning f_Γ(x) ∈ T_xΓ = {y ∈ ℝ^N | y·ν(x) = 0} for each x ∈ Γ (Equation 1.4).

*   **Why This Matters:** This assumption enables use of the standard tangential divergence formula:
    $$
    \int_\Gamma (\nabla_\Gamma \cdot f_\Gamma) z_\Gamma \, d\sigma = - \int_\Gamma f_\Gamma \cdot \nabla_\Gamma z_\Gamma \, d\sigma.
    $$
*   **Failure Mode:** When tangentiality is not satisfied, the generalized formula includes an extra term:
    $$
    \int_\Gamma (\nabla_\Gamma \cdot \nu) (f_\Gamma \cdot \nu) z_\Gamma \, d\sigma.
    $$
*   **Explicit Limitation:** Remark 1.1 states: "These terms cannot be controlled by the techniques developed in the present paper... the analysis of non-tangential boundary vector fields within the framework of Carleman estimates for equation (1.2) remains unsolved in this paper."
*   **Physical Implication:** This excludes scenarios where boundary flux has a normal component—such as mass transfer through a permeable membrane or heat exchange with external media not confined to the surface tangent plane.

**Domain Dimension Constraint (N ≥ 2)**

The analysis assumes N ≥ 2 throughout (Section 1). While Remark 1.3 notes that for N = 1, controllability can be addressed without surface diffusion (citing [6]), the main results do not cover the one-dimensional case explicitly.

*   **Trade-off:** The N ≥ 2 assumption is necessary because the tangential gradient ∇_Γ is non-trivial only when the boundary has dimension ≥ 1. For N = 1, the boundary is zero-dimensional and ∇_Γ = 0 trivially.
*   **Gap:** The paper does not provide unified estimates that smoothly recover the N = 1 case as a limit.

### 6.2 Control Mechanism Constraints

**Three Controls Required Instead of One**

The null controllability result (Theorem 1.2) requires **three controls**: (u, v₁, v₂), where:
*   u: distributed control acting on interior subdomain G₀ (localized in space)
*   v₁: stochastic bulk control acting on diffusion term over entire domain G
*   v₂: stochastic boundary control acting on diffusion term over entire boundary Γ

*   **Open Problem:** Section 1 explicitly states: "Investigating these control problems using only the control u, which acts exclusively on the drift term of the bulk equation, remains a challenging open problem."
*   **Prior Partial Results:** The paper references [11, 31] for partial results under additional assumptions (e.g., space-independent coefficients), but the general anisotropic case with a single control remains unresolved.
*   **Practical Impact:** Requiring controls v₁ and v₂ over the entire spatial domain is more demanding than localized control. In applications, it may be infeasible to actuate the diffusion term everywhere (e.g., you cannot add noise everywhere in a chemical reactor).

**Control Region Overlap Requirement (Insensitizing Control)**

For the insensitizing control problem (Theorem 1.3), the condition G₀ ∩ O ≠ ∅ is required—the control region must overlap with the observation region for the sentinel functional.

*   **Limitation:** If the control and observation regions are disjoint, the insensitizing control existence is not established by this work.
*   **Evidence:** Theorem 1.3 states: "Assume that G₀ ∩ O ≠ ∅..."

### 6.3 Source Term and Regularity Limitations

**Surface Diffusion Necessity (Remark 1.3)**

The boundary equation must include the surface diffusion term ∑_{j,k=1}^N Dⱼ(a_{jk}^Γ D_k z_Γ). Without this term, the Carleman estimate fails for N ≥ 2.

*   **Why:** Remark 1.3 explains: "This term is essential for absorbing the following problematic boundary integral: λµ E∬_Σ θ²φ|∂^A_ν ψ||∇_Γ z_Γ|² dσ dt."
*   **Failure Case:** If the boundary condition is simplified to dz_Γ + ∂^A_ν z dt = ... (without surface diffusion), the null and insensitizing control problems remain open for N ≥ 2.
*   **Exception:** For N = 1, controllability holds without surface diffusion because ∇_Γ = 0 trivially (citing [6]).

**Source Term Decay Conditions for Insensitizing Control (Equation 1.16)**

For insensitizing control existence (Theorem 1.3), the source terms ξ and ξ_Γ must satisfy weighted integrability conditions:
$$
\mathbb{E} \iint_Q \exp(K t^{-1}) |\xi|^2 \, dx \, dt < \infty, \quad \mathbb{E} \iint_\Sigma \exp(K t^{-1}) |\xi_\Gamma|^2 \, d\sigma \, dt < \infty.
$$

*   **Constraint:** The weight exp(K t⁻¹) imposes **strict decay requirements near t = 0**. Sources that do not decay sufficiently fast as t → 0 are excluded.
*   **Physical Interpretation:** This reflects the singular behavior of Carleman weights near the initial time. Sources with significant early-time activity may not admit insensitizing controls under this framework.

**Alternative Energy Functionals Not Covered (Remark 1.4)**

The insensitizing control uses a sentinel functional involving |y|² and |∇_Γ y_Γ|² (Equation 1.10). If the functional involves the **bulk gradient norm ‖∇y‖²**, the Carleman estimate (1.8) is insufficient.

*   **Explicit Limitation:** Remark 1.4 states: "The Carleman estimate (1.8) is not sufficient to handle this functional. The main difficulty lies in the need to control a localized term using a higher-order term..."
*   **Reference:** The paper cites [22] for discussion of this challenge in the deterministic heat equation context.
*   **Implication:** Applications requiring sensitivity to bulk gradient variations (e.g., stress-sensitive materials) are not covered.

### 6.4 Parameter and Scalability Constraints

**Large Parameter Thresholds**

The Carleman estimate requires parameters λ and µ to exceed explicit thresholds:
$$
\mu \geq \mu_0, \quad \lambda \geq \lambda_0 \left( e^{2\mu\|\psi\|_\infty}T + T^2 \right).
$$

*   **Scalability Issue:** The exponential dependence on µ‖ψ‖_∞ means that for domains with large spatial extent (large ‖ψ‖_∞), the required λ grows exponentially. This affects the tightness of the controllability cost bound.
*   **Time Horizon Sensitivity:** As T → 0, the required λ grows quadratically (T² term), which propagates to the controllability cost constant K_T containing a 1/T term.

**Controllability Cost Blow-Up as T → 0**

The null controllability cost constant K_T (Equation 1.14) contains a 1/T term:
$$
K_T := 1 + \frac{1}{T} + \|a_1\|_\infty^{2/3} + \|a_2\|_\infty^{2/3} + T(\|a_1\|_\infty + \|a_2\|_\infty) + (1 + T)(\|B_1\|_\infty^2 + \|B_2\|_\infty^2).
$$

*   **Implication:** The control energy bound exp(C K_T) blows up exponentially as T → 0. This is physically expected (instantaneous control requires infinite energy) but means the theory provides no useful bounds for very short time horizons.
*   **Trade-off:** The explicit T-dependence is an innovation, but it reveals fundamental limitations on fast control of diffusive systems.

**No Approximate Controllability Cost Estimate**

While the paper establishes approximate controllability (Corollary 1.2), it explicitly leaves the cost open:

> "The cost of approximate controllability of (CE) can also be considered; however, it is left open in the present paper." (Section 1)

*   **Gap:** For applications where exact null controllability is not required, no quantitative bound on the control effort is provided.
*   **Reference:** The paper cites [9, 17] for related deterministic results but does not extend these to the stochastic dynamic boundary setting.

### 6.5 Computational and Implementation Considerations

**No Numerical Validation**

The paper is purely theoretical—no numerical experiments, simulations, or computational algorithms are provided.

*   **Limitation:** While the proofs are rigorous, the practical computability of the controls (u, v₁, v₂) is not addressed. The duality-based existence proof does not construct explicit control laws.
*   **Implementation Gap:** Translating the Carleman estimate into a numerical control algorithm would require discretization schemes that preserve the weighted energy structure—a non-trivial task not covered here.

**Constant Dependencies Not Fully Explicit**

While the T-dependence is explicit, the generic constant C depends on G, G₀, β₀, M, and M_Γ in ways that are not fully quantified.

*   **Evidence:** Section 1 states: "Throughout this paper, C denotes a generic positive constant depending only on G, G₀, β₀, M, and M_Γ, which may change from line to line."
*   **Limitation:** For a specific domain and coefficient set, one cannot compute the exact value of C without tracing through all proof steps. This limits direct engineering application of the cost bounds.

### 6.7 Assessment: Strengths vs. Limitations

**Strengths:**
*   First Carleman estimate handling H⁻¹ weak divergence sources with dynamic boundaries
*   Explicit T-dependence enables quantitative cost bounds
*   Anisotropic diffusion matrices extend beyond isotropic models
*   Rigorous identification of failure modes (Remarks 1.1, 1.3, 1.4)

**Limitations:**
*   Requires three controls instead of one (practical actuation constraint)
*   Tangentiality assumption excludes normal boundary flux
*   Surface diffusion necessary for N ≥ 2 (structural constraint)
*   C⁴ boundary regularity excludes non-smooth domains
*   No numerical implementation pathway provided

The paper succeeds as a **foundational theoretical advance** but leaves significant work for practical implementation and extension to less constrained settings. The explicit acknowledgment of open problems (particularly in the Remarks) demonstrates intellectual honesty about the theory's boundaries—this clarity enables targeted future research rather than false claims of generality.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper represents a foundational advance in stochastic control theory, establishing mathematical tools that were previously unavailable for a broad class of physically relevant systems. The implications extend across theoretical mathematics, applied analysis, and potential engineering applications. Below, we outline how this work reshapes the field, what research pathways it opens, and when practitioners should adopt these methods.

### 7.1 Landscape Transformation: What This Work Enables

**Closing the H^{-1} Source Term Gap**

Prior to this work, Carleman estimates for stochastic parabolic equations with dynamic boundary conditions could not handle weak divergence source terms in $H^{-1}$ spaces. As stated in Section 1, the estimate from [7, Theorem 4.1] "is not sufficient to address the control problems considered in this paper" because it requires $F_j = F_{\Gamma j} = 0$. This paper's two-step duality method (Section 4) removes this restriction, enabling controllability analysis for systems with:

- **Distributional forcing terms** (point sources, surface fluxes modeled as distributions)
- **Low-regularity external inputs** that arise naturally in physical modeling
- **Coupled bulk-surface interactions** where source terms inherit regularity from coupling mechanisms

This shifts the field from analyzing idealized high-regularity systems to handling rougher, more realistic forcing structures.

**Quantitative Control Theory for Stochastic Dynamic Boundaries**

The explicit controllability cost bound (Equation 1.14–1.15) transforms null controllability from an existential statement into a quantitative engineering tool:

$$
K(Y_0, G_0) \leq \exp(C K_T) \, \mathbb{E}\|Y_0\|_{L^2}^2, \quad K_T = 1 + \frac{1}{T} + \|a_1\|_\infty^{2/3} + \|a_2\|_\infty^{2/3} + T(\|a_1\|_\infty + \|a_2\|_\infty) + (1 + T)(\|B_1\|_\infty^2 + \|B_2\|_\infty^2).
$$

This formula reveals how control effort scales with:
- **Time horizon $T$**: The $1/T$ term confirms exponential cost blow-up for fast control
- **Reaction coefficients $\|a_i\|_\infty$**: Stronger reactions increase cost sublinearly ($2/3$ power)
- **Convection coefficients $\|B_i\|_\infty$**: Stronger transport increases cost quadratically

Prior work on stochastic parabolic equations with dynamic boundary conditions ([5], [7]) established controllability existence without such explicit bounds. This quantitative framework enables **optimal time-horizon selection** and **control energy budgeting** in applications.

**Anisotropic Surface Diffusion Controllability**

The paper extends controllability theory from isotropic diffusion ($a_{jk} = \delta_{jk}$) to **anisotropic diffusion matrices** $(a_{jk})_{1 \leq j,k \leq N}$ in both bulk and boundary. This is significant for modeling:

- **Crystalline materials** where diffusion rates vary by crystallographic direction
- **Textured surfaces** with direction-dependent surface transport
- **Composite materials** with layered or fibrous microstructures

The tangentiality assumption (Equation 1.4) is a deliberate constraint that stabilizes the analysis while still covering many physically relevant anisotropic scenarios.

**Medium-Priority Extensions (Methodological Improvements)**

1. **Reduced Boundary Regularity**: The $C^4$ boundary requirement (Section 1) is stronger than many applications need. Research could explore whether $C^2$ or Lipschitz boundaries suffice using weaker tangential derivative formulations.

2. **Numerical Control Construction**: The duality-based existence proof does not provide explicit control laws. Developing **numerical algorithms** that discretize the Carleman estimate while preserving the weighted energy structure would enable computational implementation.

3. **State-Dependent Diffusion**: Remark 1.2 notes that the controllability results extend to systems where diffusion terms depend on the state ($b_1 y + \tilde{B}_1 \cdot \nabla y$). Rigorous treatment of **fully nonlinear diffusion** remains open.

4. **Multi-Objective Control**: The paper references [38] for multi-objective control with dynamic boundary conditions. Combining the $H^{-1}$ source handling with multi-objective optimization frameworks is a natural extension.

**Long-Term Research Directions (Paradigm Shifts)**

1. **Inverse Problems with Dynamic Boundaries**: Carleman estimates are central to inverse problem analysis [32, 33, 42]. This paper's estimate could enable **source identification** or **coefficient recovery** for systems with evolving boundaries.

2. **Robust Control Under Uncertainty**: The insensitizing control framework (Section 6) addresses initial data uncertainty. Extending this to **parameter uncertainty** or **model mismatch** would broaden practical applicability.

3. **Coupled System Controllability**: The cascade system (6.1) for insensitizing control suggests pathways to **networked stochastic systems** where multiple domains interact through dynamic interfaces.

4. **Discrete-Time Analogues**: The fully discrete parabolic equation work [26] could be extended to include $H^{-1}$ sources and the explicit cost bounds developed here.

### 7.4 Integration Guidance for Practitioners

**Adoption Checklist**

Before applying this paper's results to a specific problem, verify the following conditions are met:

1. ✅ **Boundary regularity**: $\Gamma$ is $C^4$ (or accept that constants may not hold for rougher boundaries)
2. ✅ **Tangentiality**: Boundary vector fields satisfy $f_\Gamma(x) \cdot \nu(x) = 0$ (Equation 1.4)
3. ✅ **Surface diffusion**: Boundary equation includes $\nabla_\Gamma \cdot (A_\Gamma \nabla_\Gamma y_\Gamma)$ term
4. ✅ **Control availability**: Three controls $(u, v_1, v_2)$ can be implemented (localized drift + full-domain stochastic controls)
5. ✅ **Source decay**: For insensitizing control, sources satisfy $\mathbb{E}\iint \exp(K t^{-1})|\xi|^2 < \infty$ (Equation 1.16)
6. ✅ **Control-observation overlap**: For insensitizing control, $G_0 \cap \mathcal{O} \neq \emptyset$ (Theorem 1.3)

**Parameter Selection Guidance**

The Carleman estimate requires specific parameter thresholds (Theorem 1.1):

$$
\mu \geq \mu_0, \quad \lambda \geq \lambda_0 \left( e^{2\mu\|\psi\|_\infty}T + T^2 \right).
$$

For practical implementation:
- **Estimate $\|\psi\|_\infty$**: Depends on domain geometry; larger domains require larger $\lambda$
- **Time horizon trade-off**: Smaller $T$ increases $\lambda$ requirement quadratically, which propagates to control cost via $K_T$
- **Coefficient bounds**: Compute $\|a_i\|_\infty$, $\|B_i\|_\infty$ from the specific application to evaluate $K_T$

**Computational Considerations**

While this paper does not provide numerical algorithms, the following implementation pathway is suggested:

1. **Discretize the adjoint system (5.1)** using finite elements or finite differences that preserve the dynamic boundary coupling
2. **Approximate the Carleman weights** $\theta, \phi$ numerically, ensuring the singularities at $t=0, T$ are handled carefully
3. **Solve the optimality system** from Proposition 4.1 to compute controls iteratively
4. **Validate against the cost bound** (1.15) to ensure numerical solutions respect theoretical guarantees

**Comparison with Deterministic Counterparts**

Practitioners familiar with deterministic parabolic control [1, 25, 36] should note:

- **Stochastic controls $v_1, v_2$** have no deterministic analogue; they act on the diffusion term
- **Observability includes $Z, bZ$** terms (Equation 5.7), not just the state $z$
- **Cost bounds are in expectation** ($\mathbb{E}[\cdot]$), reflecting the stochastic nature
- **The $1/T$ scaling** is similar to deterministic heat equation [17], but the constant $K_T$ includes additional stochastic terms

### 7.5 Concluding Perspective

This paper establishes a **new baseline** for controllability analysis of stochastic parabolic systems with dynamic boundary conditions. The key contributions—handling $H^{-1}$ sources, providing explicit time-dependent cost bounds, and accommodating anisotropic diffusion—collectively expand the class of systems that can be rigorously analyzed and controlled.

The work's greatest impact may be **enabling subsequent research** rather than immediate deployment. By clearly identifying open problems (Remarks 1.1, 1.3, 1.4) and providing a robust analytical framework, the paper gives the community concrete targets for extension. The explicit cost formula (1.14) alone represents a significant advance, transforming controllability from a qualitative property into a quantifiable engineering metric.

For researchers entering this space, the recommended entry point is **Section 3 (Intermediate Carleman Estimate)** to understand the weighted identity method, followed by **Section 4 (Duality Extension)** to grasp the $H^{-1}$ source handling. The control applications in **Sections 5–6** demonstrate how the estimate translates to actionable results.

The field now stands at a threshold: the foundational estimates are in place, and the next phase involves **numerical realization**, **constraint relaxation**, and **application-specific adaptation**. This paper provides the theoretical scaffolding for that next phase.
