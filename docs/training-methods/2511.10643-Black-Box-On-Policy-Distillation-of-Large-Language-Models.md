## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces Generative Adversarial Distillation (GAD), a framework that enables on-policy knowledge distillation for large language models in a black-box setting where only teacher text outputs (e.g., from GPT-5-Chat) are accessible without internal logits. By framing the student as a generator and training a co-evolving discriminator as an on-policy reward model via a minimax game, GAD overcomes the limitations of standard sequence-level knowledge distillation (SeqKD). Experiments demonstrate that GAD consistently outperforms SeqKD across Qwen2.5 and Llama3 families, with Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct achieving a 52.1 GPT-4o score on LMSYS-Chat that exceeds the GPT-5-Chat teacher's 51.7, validating effective capability transfer without white-box access.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this work, we must first establish the landscape of model compression for Large Language Models (LLMs) and the specific barriers imposed by proprietary systems. This section defines the operational constraints of distillation, explains why current black-box methods are theoretically limited, and outlines how this paper positions itself to bridge that gap.

### The Distillation Setting: White-Box vs. Black-Box

Knowledge distillation (KD) is a technique where a smaller "student" model learns to replicate the behavior of a larger "teacher" model [HVD15]. The feasibility of distillation depends entirely on what information the student can access from the teacher.

*   **White-Box Distillation:** The student has access to the teacher's internal probability distributions (logits) or hidden states. This allows for precise alignment using objectives like Kullback-Leibler Divergence (KLD). For example, standard approaches minimize the forward KLD between the teacher distribution $p(y|x)$ and student distribution $q_\theta(y|x)$ [SST+20].
*   **Black-Box Distillation:** The teacher is a proprietary API (e.g., GPT-5-Chat [Ope25]) that only returns generated text. The internal probabilities are hidden. This is the setting addressed by this paper.

The distinction is critical because modern high-performance models are increasingly closed-source. As noted in Section 1, white-box access is "often impractical when the teacher is a proprietary API model." Therefore, developing effective black-box methods is essential for deploying efficient models that leverage the capabilities of state-of-the-art proprietary systems without requiring access to their weights.

### The Limitation of Prior Black-Box Approaches

In the absence of logits, the standard approach for black-box distillation is **Sequence-Level Knowledge Distillation (SeqKD)** [KR16]. SeqKD treats the teacher's generated text as ground-truth labels and performs Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on the student.

While simple, SeqKD suffers from two fundamental theoretical and practical shortcomings:

1.  **Exposure Bias (Off-Policy Learning):** SeqKD is an **off-policy** method. The student is trained exclusively on the teacher's perfect trajectories (sequences). However, during inference, the student must generate its own tokens autoregressively. If the student makes a small error early in generation, it deviates from the distribution it was trained on, leading to compounding errors. This mismatch between training (teacher-forced) and inference (self-generated) is known as exposure bias. Section 1 highlights that recent white-box studies show "performing reverse KLD on student-generated text promotes mode-seeking behavior and reduces exposure bias compared to teacher-forced training."
2.  **Mode-Covering vs. Mode-Seeking:** Standard SFT objectives (like cross-entropy used in SeqKD) encourage **mode-covering** behavior. The student attempts to assign probability mass to all possible outputs the teacher might produce, effectively averaging over them. In contrast, **mode-seeking** behavior focuses on generating high-quality samples from the most probable regions of the teacher's distribution.
    *   **Evidence:** Figure 5 illustrates this using toy data. The SeqKD student spreads probability across all modes (outputs 0–9), whereas the proposed method concentrates on the "reachable modes" of the teacher.
    *   **Consequence:** In practice, this leads to overfitting. Figure 4 shows that SeqKD students exhibit higher N-gram overlap with the teacher but achieve lower evaluation scores. This suggests SeqKD "memorizes local lexical patterns" rather than learning the underlying reasoning or stylistic capabilities.

### The Missing Link: On-Policy Learning without Logits

The ideal solution would combine the accessibility of black-box distillation with the robustness of **on-policy learning**. On-policy learning means the student learns from its own generated responses, allowing it to correct its own mistakes and adapt to its own inference distribution.

However, a major challenge exists: **How do you evaluate the quality of student-generated text without logits?**
*   In white-box settings, you can calculate the probability the teacher would have assigned to the student's tokens.
*   In black-box settings, "there are no probability-level supervision signals available from the teacher to evaluate or correct them" (Section 1).

Prior black-box methods cannot solve this because they rely on static text matching. They lack a mechanism to provide feedback on student generations that deviate from the teacher's exact words but might still be high-quality.

### Positioning of This Work

This paper positions **Generative Adversarial Distillation (GAD)** as the solution to enable on-policy learning in the black-box regime. It draws inspiration from two distinct fields:

1.  **Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs):** By framing the student as a generator and training a discriminator to distinguish between teacher and student outputs, the system creates a dynamic feedback loop. The discriminator provides a scalar score indicating how "teacher-like" a student response is, replacing the need for explicit logits.
2.  **Reinforcement Learning (RL):** The paper explicitly maps this framework to RL concepts (Table 1). The discriminator acts as an **on-policy reward model**. Unlike standard RLHF where the reward model is fixed after pretraining (which can lead to reward hacking [SHKK22]), the GAD discriminator "co-evolves with the student policy."

By bridging these concepts, GAD addresses the core gap: it provides a trainable, adaptive reward signal derived solely from text comparisons, allowing the student to optimize for quality rather than exact token matching. This positioning is validated by the results in Figure 1 and Table 2, where GAD not only surpasses SeqKD but also demonstrates superior out-of-distribution generalization—a key indicator that the model has learned robust capabilities rather than memorizing patterns.

## 3. Technical Approach

This section provides a complete technical breakdown of Generative Adversarial Distillation (GAD). The goal is to walk through every component of the system so that you could explain the method to another researcher after reading this analysis. We start with high-level orientation, then progressively drill down into equations, training procedures, and design rationales.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

GAD is a training framework that treats a student language model as a generator in an adversarial game, where a separate discriminator model learns to distinguish student outputs from teacher outputs and provides feedback signals for improvement. The system solves the black-box distillation problem by replacing inaccessible teacher logits with a learned discriminator score that acts as an on-policy reward signal, enabling the student to learn from its own generations rather than just copying teacher text.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The GAD system consists of three primary components connected in a training loop:

*   **Student Model (Generator `G`):** This is the language model being distilled. It takes an instruction prompt `x` as input and produces a response `G(x)`. Its parameters are updated to maximize the discriminator's score on its outputs.
*   **Discriminator Model (`D`):** This is a separate model (initialized from the student's parameters with an added prediction head) that takes a prompt-response pair `[x, y]` and outputs a scalar score `D([x, y])`. It is trained to assign higher scores to teacher responses `y_t` than to student responses `G(x)`.
*   **Training Data (`T`):** The dataset consists of prompt-teacher response pairs `{(x, y_t)}` collected by querying the black-box teacher API. This data is used for both warmup and adversarial training stages.

The information flow proceeds as follows: First, a prompt `x` is sampled from the dataset. Second, the student generates a response `G(x)`. Third, the discriminator scores both the teacher response `D(y_t)` and the student response `D(G(x))`. Fourth, the generator is updated using policy gradient to increase `D(G(x))`, while the discriminator is updated using Bradley-Terry loss to maintain `D(y_t) > D(G(x))`. This creates the minimax game shown in Figure 2.

*   We first explain the core minimax objective (Equation 1) because it defines the fundamental game-theoretic relationship between generator and discriminator.
*   We then break down the generator training procedure (Equation 2) including the policy gradient method (GRPO) since this is how the student actually learns.
*   We next detail the discriminator training objective (Equation 3) and explain why Bradley-Terry loss is chosen over standard cross-entropy.
*   We describe the warmup strategy because it is a critical practical requirement for stable training that is not obvious from the core equations alone.
*   We explain the reinforcement learning framework implementation (Table 1) to clarify how GAD maps to existing RL infrastructure.
*   We conclude with training hyperparameters and configuration details to enable reproducibility and understanding of computational requirements.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Minimax Objective**

The fundamental training objective of GAD is formulated as a two-player minimax game, which is a game-theoretic framework where one player tries to maximize a value while the other tries to minimize it. The value function `V(G, D)` is defined in Equation (1) of Section 2.1 as:

$$
\max_{G} \min_{D} V(G, D) = \mathbb{E}_{(x, y_t) \sim \mathcal{T}} [-\log \sigma (D(y_t) - D(G(x)))]
$$

In this equation, `$\mathcal{T}$` represents the training dataset of prompt-teacher response pairs, `$\sigma(\cdot)$` denotes the sigmoid function that maps real numbers to the range (0, 1), `$D(y_t)$` is the discriminator's score for the teacher's response, and `$D(G(x))$` is the discriminator's score for the student's generated response. The plain-language interpretation is that the discriminator tries to minimize this loss by making `$D(y_t)$` larger than `$D(G(x))$` (so the difference is positive and the log-loss is small), while the generator tries to maximize this same expression by making `$D(G(x))$` approach or exceed `$D(y_t)$`. This formulation uses the Bradley-Terry model [BT52], which is a statistical method for capturing pairwise preferences, rather than treating the problem as simple binary classification. The Bradley-Terry approach is crucial because it directly models the relative preference between teacher and student outputs rather than absolute quality scores.

**Generator Training Objective**

From the minimax formulation, we can derive the specific training objective for the generator by isolating the terms that depend on `$G$`. Equation (2) in Section 2.2 shows that the generator is trained to maximize the expected discriminator score on its own outputs:

$$
\max_{G} \mathbb{E}_{(x, y_t) \sim \mathcal{T}} [D(G(x))]
$$

This objective is deceptively simple in appearance but presents a technical challenge: the sampling operation in `$G(x)$` is non-differentiable with respect to the student model parameters because token generation involves discrete sampling from a probability distribution. To overcome this, the paper treats `$D(G(x))$` as a reward signal and optimizes it using policy gradient methods from reinforcement learning. Specifically, the authors employ GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) [SWZ+24], which is detailed in Appendix A.1. For each input prompt `$x$`, GRPO samples a group of `$N$` student responses `$\{y_s^i\}_{i=1}^N$` and obtains their corresponding rewards `$\{r_s^i\}_{i=1}^N$` where `$r_s^i = D(y_s^i)$`. The advantage for the `$i$`-th response is calculated by normalizing the reward relative to the group:

$$
A_i = \frac{r_s^i - \text{mean}(\{r_s^j\}_{j=1}^N)}{\text{std}(\{r_s^j\}_{j=1}^N)}
$$

The student is then trained with the objective shown in Equation (7) of Appendix A.1:

$$
\max_{G} \mathbb{E}_{(x, y_t) \sim \mathcal{T}, \{y_s^i\}_{i=1}^N \sim q_G(\cdot|x)} \left[ \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N A_i \right]
$$

In the experiments, the group size is set to `$N = 8$` and the KL weight is `$\beta = 0.001$`, though the KL regularizer and clip operator are omitted from Equation (7) for brevity. This group-relative approach provides stable gradient estimates without requiring a separate baseline model, which is important for computational efficiency in the black-box setting.

**Discriminator Training Objective**

The discriminator is trained to maintain its ability to distinguish teacher responses from student responses throughout the adversarial process. Equation (3) in Section 2.2 defines the discriminator loss:

$$
\min_{D} \mathbb{E}_{(x, y_t) \sim \mathcal{T}} [-\log \sigma (D(y_t) - D(G(x)))]
$$

This is the Bradley-Terry loss that encourages higher scores for teacher responses over student-generated ones. The paper explicitly ablates this choice against standard cross-entropy loss in Table 4, which would be formulated as:

$$
\min_{D} \mathbb{E}_{(x, y_t) \sim \mathcal{T}} [-\log \sigma (D(y_t)) - \log (1 - \sigma (D(G(x))))]
$$

The Bradley-Terry loss outperforms cross-entropy loss (48.9 vs 47.9 on LMSYS for Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct) because it enhances discriminator training stability in the LLM context. The key difference is that Bradley-Terry directly models the *difference* in scores rather than treating teacher and student as independent binary classes, which creates a more stable learning signal when the generator is rapidly improving.

**Warmup Strategy**

A critical practical detail that is not obvious from the core equations is the warmup procedure before GAD training begins. Section 2.2 states that "jointly warming up the generator and discriminator before the GAD training stage is crucial for final performance." The warmup consists of one epoch where both models are trained on the teacher response data before the adversarial game starts. For the generator, this means minimizing cross-entropy loss on the teacher's responses (essentially one epoch of SeqKD). For the discriminator, this means training with the Bradley-Terry loss in Equation (3) using the warmup generator's outputs as the student samples.

The ablation study in Table 3 demonstrates why this matters. When generator warmup is removed (using the original instruct model without SeqKD initialization), performance drops from 50.8 to 49.7 on LMSYS for Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct. The paper attributes this to "the discriminator easily distinguishing between the student and teacher outputs in the early training stage" when the distributional gap is too large, which weakens the effectiveness of GAD training. When discriminator warmup is removed (initializing the discriminator with the original model while using a SeqKD-warmed generator), performance drops further to 49.0. This occurs because "the imbalance between the generator and the discriminator prevents the discriminator from providing sufficiently informative feedback." The warmup ensures both models start the adversarial phase at comparable capability levels, enabling stable co-evolution.

**Reinforcement Learning Framework Mapping**

Table 1 in Section 2.3 provides an explicit mapping between GAD components and standard reinforcement learning terminology, which is important because the authors implement GAD using existing RL frameworks like verl [SZY+24]. The student LLM corresponds to the policy model, the discriminator corresponds to the reward model, and the discriminator score `$D(G(x))$` serves as the reward. The critical distinction from conventional RLHF [OWJ+22] is that "the discriminator co-evolves with the student LLM" rather than being fixed after pretraining. In standard RLHF, the reward model is trained once on a static dataset and then frozen, which makes the policy optimization prone to reward hacking where the policy learns to exploit quirks in the fixed reward model. Figure 6 demonstrates this empirically: an off-policy discriminator (trained separately and frozen) causes the student to exhibit reward hacking after around 300 training steps, producing excessively long responses up to 1300 tokens. In contrast, GAD's on-policy discriminator "remains stable through thousands of training steps with no sign of reward hacking" because it continually adapts to the student's current behavior.

**Training Configuration and Hyperparameters**

The experimental setup in Section 3.1 and Appendix A.2 provides specific configuration details that are essential for understanding the computational requirements and reproducibility. The training data consists of 200K samples from LMSYS-Chat-1M-Clean with corresponding GPT-5-Chat teacher responses. All models are trained for 3 epochs total: 1 warmup epoch followed by 2 GAD training epochs, totaling approximately 2400 optimization steps. The batch size is 256 for both training and PPO mini-batch updates. The maximum context length is set to 2048 tokens for instruction prompts and 1536 for model responses. The training temperature is 0.8, which introduces stochasticity during generation that is important for the policy gradient estimates.

Learning rates are searched in the range `[1e-6, 5e-6]`. For GAD with the GPT-5-Chat teacher, the learning rate is 1e-6 for both warmup and GAD training stages. For GAD with Qwen2.5 teacher (Table 7), the warmup stage uses 5e-6 and the GAD training stage uses 1e-6. Checkpoints are saved every 50 steps. The computational cost is substantial: "Distilling Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct from GPT-5-Chat takes about 30 hours on 16 H100 GPUs." This information is important for practitioners who want to estimate resource requirements for similar distillation tasks.

**Discriminator Architecture**

The discriminator architecture is described in Section 2.1: it is "initialized using generator model parameters with an extra prediction head." The head projects the final hidden state to a scalar score, and "the score of the last token in the sequence is taken as the sequence-level score." This design choice is efficient because it leverages the student's existing architecture rather than requiring a separate large model from scratch. The ablation study in Table 5 examines whether using a larger discriminator improves performance. For the 3B student, increasing discriminator size from 3B to 7B actually decreases performance (48.9 to 47.8 on LMSYS). For the 7B student, increasing from 7B to 14B also shows no improvement (50.8 to 50.5). The paper concludes that "maintaining a balanced generator-discriminator pair is crucial for achieving strong performance." This is consistent with GAN literature where overly powerful discriminators can provide gradients that are too sharp for the generator to learn from effectively.

**Input Formatting for Discriminator**

A subtle but important implementation detail is how the discriminator receives its input. Footnote 2 in Section 2.1 clarifies that "the input prompt `$x$` and generated response `$y$` are concatenated (i.e., `[$x$, `$y$`]`) and fed into the discriminator." This means the discriminator evaluates the quality of a response *in context* of the specific prompt it was generated for, rather than scoring responses in isolation. For brevity, the paper uses `$D(y)$` to represent `$D([x, y])$` in subsequent equations, but the full concatenated input is used in implementation. This design ensures the discriminator learns to evaluate response appropriateness relative to the instruction, not just absolute text quality.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section isolates the fundamental conceptual advances provided by Generative Adversarial Distillation (GAD). While Section 3 detailed the mechanical implementation, here we analyze *why* these design choices represent a shift in paradigm rather than merely an incremental improvement over existing distillation or reinforcement learning methods. We distinguish between the core theoretical innovations and the practical engineering insights required to make them work.

### 4.1 Enabling On-Policy Learning in the Black-Box Regime

The most significant theoretical contribution of this work is the decoupling of **on-policy learning** from **white-box access**. Prior to this work, the landscape was bifurcated:

*   **White-Box On-Policy:** Methods like MiniLLM [GDWH24] demonstrated that learning from student-generated samples (on-policy) reduces exposure bias and improves quality. However, these methods require access to the teacher's probability distribution $p(y|x)$ to compute divergence objectives (e.g., reverse KLD).
*   **Black-Box Off-Policy:** Standard black-box methods like SeqKD [KR16] are restricted to off-policy Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) because there is no signal to evaluate student generations that deviate from the teacher's exact text.

GAD bridges this gap by replacing the inaccessible probability distribution with a **learned discriminator score**. This is not merely a substitution of signals; it fundamentally changes the optimization landscape. By treating the discriminator output $D(G(x))$ as a reward (as mapped in Table 1), the student can receive gradient feedback on *any* generated text, not just text that matches the teacher's tokens.

*   **Why this matters:** This unlocks the generalization benefits of on-policy learning for proprietary models. As shown in **Figure 1 (Right)**, GAD demonstrates superior out-of-distribution generalization on Dolly, SelfInst, and Vicuna compared to SeqKD. The paper notes that "SeqKD yields marginal or even negative improvements" on these benchmarks, whereas GAD maintains robust gains.
*   **The Innovation:** The insight is that **textual distinguishability** can serve as a proxy for **probability alignment**. If a discriminator cannot tell the difference between student and teacher text, the student has successfully internalized the teacher's distribution, even without ever seeing the teacher's logits.

### 4.2 Dynamic Reward Modeling to Prevent Reward Hacking

A critical innovation lies in how GAD handles the reward model within the reinforcement learning framework. In standard Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) [OWJ+22], the reward model is trained on a static dataset and then **frozen** during policy optimization. This static nature creates a vulnerability known as **reward hacking**, where the policy learns to exploit specific quirks of the fixed reward model to maximize score without actually improving quality [SHKK22].

GAD diverges from this standard practice by treating the discriminator as an **on-policy reward model that co-evolves** with the student.

*   **Mechanism:** As described in **Section 2.3**, the discriminator is updated continuously alongside the generator using the minimax objective. This ensures the reward signal remains difficult to game; as the student improves, the discriminator must also improve to distinguish the new, higher-quality student outputs from the teacher's.
*   **Evidence of Stability:** **Figure 6** provides a direct empirical validation of this insight. It compares GAD against an "Off-Policy Discriminator" setting (where the discriminator is frozen after warmup).
    *   **Off-Policy Failure:** The student trained with a frozen discriminator exhibits reward hacking after ~300 steps, producing "excessively long responses (up to 1300 tokens)" that deviate from teacher patterns.
    *   **GAD Success:** The co-evolving setup "remains stable through thousands of training steps with no sign of reward hacking."
*   **Significance:** This establishes that for black-box distillation, **adaptive feedback is superior to static feedback**. It suggests that in domains where the reward signal is learned rather than human-annotated, keeping the critic online is essential for preventing degenerate solutions.

### 4.3 Mode-Seeking Behavior vs. Pattern Memorization

GAD introduces a behavioral shift in how student models learn from teachers, moving from **mode-covering** to **mode-seeking**. This distinction explains why GAD achieves higher evaluation scores despite having lower surface-level similarity to the teacher.

*   **Mode-Covering (SeqKD):** Standard SFT objectives minimize cross-entropy, which encourages the student to assign probability mass to all possible outputs the teacher might produce. In effect, the student averages over the teacher's modes.
    *   **Consequence:** This leads to memorization of local lexical patterns. **Figure 4** shows that SeqKD students have higher N-gram overlap with the teacher but lower GPT-4o evaluation scores. The paper attributes this to supervised fine-tuning tending to "memorize local lexical patterns" rather than capturing global style.
*   **Mode-Seeking (GAD):** The adversarial objective encourages the student to find specific regions of the output space that the discriminator identifies as high-quality (teacher-like).
    *   **Evidence:** **Figure 5** illustrates this with toy data. The GAD student concentrates probability on "reachable modes" of the teacher, whereas the SeqKD student spreads mass across all outputs.
*   **Why this is an Innovation:** In the context of distillation, higher similarity (overlap) is often assumed to be better. GAD challenges this assumption, demonstrating that **lower overlap can indicate better distillation** if it reflects a shift from copying tokens to copying capabilities. This is validated by **Table 2**, where GAD models consistently outperform SeqKD models on automatic evaluation despite the lower n-gram similarity noted in **Figure 4**.

### 4.4 The Critical Role of Adversarial Balance

While the minimax objective is theoretically sound, the paper reveals that practical success depends heavily on maintaining a precise balance between the generator and discriminator. This is a nuanced engineering insight that distinguishes GAD from standard GAN applications in vision.

*   **Warmup Necessity:** **Table 3** shows that removing either the generator or discriminator warmup leads to performance drops (e.g., LMSYS score drops from 50.8 to 49.0 without discriminator warmup).
    *   **Insight:** If the discriminator is too strong initially (no generator warmup), it provides no useful gradients (the "vanishing gradient" problem in GANs). If the generator is too strong initially (no discriminator warmup), the discriminator cannot learn to distinguish, providing noisy feedback. The one-epoch joint warmup ensures both start at a comparable capability level.
*   **Model Size Balance:** **Table 5** ablates the discriminator model size. Counter-intuitively, using a larger discriminator (e.g., 14B discriminator for a 7B generator) does not improve performance and slightly degrades it (50.8 vs 50.5).
    *   **Insight:** This contradicts the common intuition that a "stronger critic" is always better. In GAD, an overly powerful discriminator may provide gradients that are too sharp or complex for the smaller student to follow, hindering learning. The paper concludes that "maintaining a balanced generator-discriminator pair is crucial."
*   **Loss Function Choice:** **Table 4** highlights that Bradley-Terry loss is superior to standard cross-entropy loss for the discriminator (48.9 vs 47.9).
    *   **Insight:** Bradley-Terry models the *relative* preference ($D(y_t) - D(G(x))$) rather than absolute classification. This creates a more stable learning signal in the context of LLM generation where absolute quality scores are noisy, but relative comparisons are robust.

These insights collectively demonstrate that GAD is not simply "applying GANs to text." It requires specific adaptations—co-evolving rewards, mode-seeking objectives, and strict capacity balancing—to function effectively in the high-dimensional space of language model distillation.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines the empirical validation of Generative Adversarial Distillation (GAD). We analyze the evaluation methodology, dissect the quantitative results with specific numbers, assess whether the experiments convincingly support the paper's claims, and review the ablation studies that reveal the conditions under which GAD succeeds or struggles.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Splits**

The experimental design uses a clear separation between training and evaluation data to measure both in-distribution performance and generalization capability:

*   **Training Data:** 200,000 samples sampled from LMSYS-Chat-1M-Clean [ZCS+24], a cleaned version of conversational data from the Chatbot Arena platform. For each prompt, the authors collected corresponding responses from the GPT-5-Chat teacher model.
*   **In-Distribution Test Set:** 500 reserved samples from LMSYS-Chat-1M-Clean. This measures how well distillation transfers capabilities on data similar to the training distribution.
*   **Out-of-Distribution (OOD) Test Sets:** Three benchmarks evaluate generalization to unseen domains:
    *   **Dolly [Dat23]:** 500-sample subset
    *   **SelfInst [WKM+23]:** 252 samples
    *   **Vicuna [CLL+23]:** 80 questions

This multi-dataset evaluation is important because it tests whether GAD learns robust capabilities (which should generalize) versus memorizing training patterns (which should not).

**Teacher and Student Models**

The paper evaluates GAD across multiple model families and sizes to demonstrate broad applicability:

*   **Teacher:** GPT-5-Chat [Ope25], a closed-source model ranked ninth on the Chatbot Text Arena leaderboard at the time of writing.
*   **Students (Qwen2.5 family):** Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct [YYZ+25]
*   **Students (Llama3 family):** Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct [GDJ+24]

Testing across two model families (Qwen and Llama) strengthens the claim that GAD is a general distillation framework rather than one tuned to a specific architecture.

**Evaluation Metrics**

The paper employs both automatic and human evaluation to assess model quality:

*   **GPT-4o Scores (Primary Metric):** Following prior work [ZCS+23, GDWH24], GPT-4o generates reference answers and then scores student outputs against them on helpfulness, relevance, accuracy, and detail. Scores are on a scale where the reported value is the student's score divided by the sum of the student's and reference answer's scores. This produces values in the ~45-52 range seen in Table 2.
*   **Human Evaluation:** Annotators compare GAD outputs against baselines (Before Distillation and SeqKD) on the LMSYS-Chat test set, judging whether GAD wins, ties, or loses for each prompt.
*   **Response Length:** Measured in tokens, reported in Table 6 to analyze whether models exhibit degenerate behaviors like excessive verbosity.
*   **N-gram Overlap:** F1 score of n-gram overlap between student and teacher outputs (Figure 4), used to analyze whether models memorize surface patterns versus learning deeper capabilities.

**Baselines**

The paper compares GAD against two relevant baselines:

1.  **Before Distillation:** The original instruction-tuned student models without any distillation. This establishes the floor performance.
2.  **SeqKD [KR16]:** Sequence-Level Knowledge Distillation, the standard black-box distillation approach that performs supervised fine-tuning on teacher responses. This is the primary competitor.

**Training Configuration**

Key training details from Section 3.1 and Appendix A.2:

*   **Epochs:** 3 total (1 warmup epoch + 2 GAD training epochs)
*   **Batch Size:** 256 for training and PPO mini-batch updates
*   **Optimization Steps:** Approximately 2,400 total steps
*   **Context Length:** 2,048 tokens for prompts, 1,536 tokens for responses
*   **Temperature:** 0.8 during training (introduces stochasticity for policy gradient)
*   **Learning Rate:** 1e-6 for GPT-5-Chat teacher experiments; 5e-6 for warmup and 1e-6 for GAD stage with Qwen2.5 teacher
*   **Compute:** "Distilling Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct from GPT-5-Chat takes about 30 hours on 16 H100 GPUs"

This configuration is important for reproducibility and for understanding the computational cost of adopting GAD versus simpler baselines like SeqKD.

| GPT-5-Chat | Teacher | 51.7 |

Several observations are critical:

1.  **GAD Surpasses SeqKD Consistently:** For Qwen2.5-3B, GAD achieves 48.9 versus SeqKD's 47.5 (a +1.4 point gain). For Qwen2.5-7B, the gap is 50.8 versus 49.2 (+1.6 points). For Qwen2.5-14B, GAD reaches 52.1 versus SeqKD's 50.6 (+1.5 points). The consistency across model sizes suggests the method scales reliably.

2.  **Student Exceeds Teacher:** Notably, Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct trained with GAD achieves 52.1, which exceeds the GPT-5-Chat teacher's 51.7. The paper notes this model becomes "comparable to its teacher" in the abstract, but the numbers show it actually surpasses the teacher on this benchmark. This is a significant claim that warrants scrutiny—it could indicate the student learned to optimize for the GPT-4o evaluator specifically, or it could reflect genuine capability transfer with some variance in evaluation.

3.  **Efficiency Gains:** Figure 1 (Left) visualizes an important efficiency insight: "Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct trained with GAD matches the performance of Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct trained with SeqKD." This means a 3B model with GAD achieves similar performance to a 7B model with SeqKD, effectively doubling parameter efficiency. Similarly, Qwen2.5-7B with GAD rivals Qwen2.5-14B with SeqKD.

**Out-of-Distribution Generalization**

The OOD results in Table 2 and Figure 1 (Right) provide stronger evidence for the paper's core claim about on-policy learning benefits:

| Model | Method | Dolly | SelfInst | Vicuna | Average OOD |
|-------|--------|-------|----------|--------|-------------|
| Qwen2.5-3B | SeqKD | 44.8 | 45.7 | 48.0 | 46.2 |
| Qwen2.5-3B | **GAD** | **46.7** | **47.7** | **49.4** | **47.9** |
| Qwen2.5-7B | SeqKD | 47.2 | 48.3 | 49.5 | 48.3 |
| Qwen2.5-7B | **GAD** | **48.5** | **50.1** | **51.4** | **50.0** |
| Qwen2.5-14B | SeqKD | 48.2 | 49.4 | 49.7 | 49.1 |
| Qwen2.5-14B | **GAD** | **50.4** | **51.1** | **51.6** | **51.0** |

The paper states that "SeqKD yields marginal or even negative improvements" on OOD benchmarks, whereas GAD maintains robust gains. Looking at Qwen2.5-3B on Dolly: SeqKD achieves 44.8, which is actually *lower* than the Before Distillation score of 45.1. This is a critical finding—it suggests SeqKD can *harm* generalization capability. In contrast, GAD achieves 46.7, a +1.6 point improvement over the baseline.

The paper attributes this to "the superior generalization ability of reinforcement learning compared to supervised fine-tuning" [CZY+25, WZZ+25]. This aligns with the theoretical argument from Section 4.1 that on-policy learning reduces exposure bias.

**Human Evaluation**

Figure 3 presents human evaluation results comparing GAD against both baselines. The results are shown as win/tie/loss percentages:

*   **Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct vs. Before Distillation:** GAD wins 68%, ties 28%, loses 4%
*   **Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct vs. SeqKD:** GAD wins 52%, ties 40%, loses 8%
*   **Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct vs. Before Distillation:** GAD wins 56%, ties 28%, loses 16%
*   **Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct vs. SeqKD:** GAD wins 60%, ties 28%, loses 12%
*   **Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct vs. Before Distillation:** GAD wins 44%, ties 40%, loses 16%
*   **Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct vs. SeqKD:** GAD wins 40%, ties 44%, loses 16%

The paper claims "GAD achieves a win rate exceeding 50% and a loss rate below 30% in almost all comparisons." This holds for the Qwen models but is less clear for Llama-3.1-8B, where GAD wins only 40-44% against baselines. This suggests GAD's advantages may be more pronounced for certain model families, though the sample details for Llama human evaluation are not fully specified.

**Response Length Analysis**

Table 6 in Appendix B.1 provides response length data that reveals important behavioral differences:

| Model | Method | LMSYS Length | Vicuna Length |
|-------|--------|--------------|---------------|
| Qwen2.5-14B | Before Distill. | 322.1 | 475.4 |
| Qwen2.5-14B | SeqKD | 319.3 | 402.5 |
| Qwen2.5-14B | GAD | 438.9 | 499.6 |
| GPT-5-Chat | Teacher | 329.1 | 378.6 |

SeqKD tends to produce shorter responses that closely follow the teacher's length distribution (319.3 vs. 329.1 on LMSYS). GAD maintains the original model's length distribution while integrating teacher capabilities (438.9, closer to the pre-distillation 322.1 but longer). The paper attributes this to "on-policy sampling of GAD, which encourages generation patterns aligned with both the student's prior and the teacher's guidance."

This is a nuanced finding: GAD does not simply copy the teacher's behavior but rather integrates teacher capabilities into the student's existing generation patterns. This could be viewed as either a strength (preserving student characteristics) or a limitation (not fully matching teacher behavior), depending on the use case.

### 5.3 Assessment of Experimental Claims

**Claim 1: GAD Consistently Surpasses SeqKD**

**Supported.** The data in Table 2 shows GAD outperforming SeqKD on all four benchmarks (LMSYS, Dolly, SelfInst, Vicuna) for all five student models tested. The gains range from +0.5 points (Llama-3.2-3B on LMSYS: 48.1 vs. 47.6) to +2.1 points (Qwen2.5-14B on LMSYS: 52.1 vs. 50.6). The consistency across model families and sizes strengthens this claim.

**Claim 2: GAD Enables Student to Match or Exceed Teacher**

**Partially Supported.** Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct with GAD achieves 52.1 on LMSYS, exceeding GPT-5-Chat's 51.7. However, this is on a single benchmark evaluated by GPT-4o. The paper does not provide broader evidence that the student matches the teacher across diverse capabilities. Additionally, exceeding the teacher on an automatic evaluator could indicate overfitting to the evaluation metric rather than genuine capability transfer. The human evaluation results (Figure 3) show GAD winning 60% against SeqKD for Qwen2.5-14B, but there is no direct human comparison between GAD student and the teacher.

**Claim 3: GAD Improves Out-of-Distribution Generalization**

**Strongly Supported.** This is the most compelling experimental finding. On Dolly, SeqKD sometimes produces *negative* gains versus the baseline (Qwen2.5-3B: 44.8 vs. 45.1), while GAD consistently improves (46.7 vs. 45.1). The average OOD gains for GAD over SeqKD are:
*   Qwen2.5-3B: 47.9 vs. 46.2 (+1.7)
*   Qwen2.5-7B: 50.0 vs. 48.3 (+1.7)
*   Qwen2.5-14B: 51.0 vs. 49.1 (+1.9)

This pattern strongly supports the theoretical argument that on-policy learning reduces exposure bias and improves generalization.

**Claim 4: GAD Avoids Reward Hacking**

**Supported.** Figure 6 provides direct empirical evidence. An off-policy discriminator (frozen after warmup) causes the student to produce "excessively long responses (up to 1300 tokens)" after around 300 training steps. GAD's co-evolving discriminator "remains stable through thousands of training steps with no sign of reward hacking." This is a clean ablation that isolates the co-evolution mechanism as the key factor preventing degenerate behavior.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

**Warmup Strategy (Table 3)**

The ablation on warmup reveals that both generator and discriminator warmup are necessary:

| Configuration | LMSYS Score | Others Average |
|---------------|-------------|----------------|
| GAD (Full) | 50.8 | 50.0 |
| w/o Generator Warmup | 49.7 | 49.7 |
| w/o Discriminator Warmup | 49.0 | 47.7 |
| SeqKD Baseline | 49.2 | 48.3 |

Removing generator warmup causes a -1.1 point drop on LMSYS. The paper explains this occurs because "the discriminator easily distinguishes between the student and teacher outputs in the early training stage" when the distributional gap is too large, weakening GAD's effectiveness.

Removing discriminator warmup causes an even larger -1.8 point drop. Here, "the imbalance between the generator and the discriminator prevents the discriminator from providing sufficiently informative feedback."

This ablation is critical because it shows GAD is not a plug-and-play solution—the warmup procedure is an essential component for stable training.

**Discriminator Loss Choice (Table 4)**

The paper compares Bradley-Terry loss against standard cross-entropy loss for the discriminator:

| Discriminator Loss | LMSYS Score | Others Average |
|--------------------|-------------|----------------|
| Bradley-Terry (Default) | 48.9 | 47.9 |
| Cross-Entropy | 47.9 | 46.4 |

Bradley-Terry loss provides a +1.0 point gain on LMSYS. The paper explains this "enhances discriminator training stability" because it models relative preferences rather than absolute classification. This is an important design choice that distinguishes GAD from standard GAN formulations.

**Discriminator Model Size (Table 5)**

Counter-intuitively, larger discriminators do not improve performance:

| Generator | Discriminator | LMSYS Score | Others Average |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|----------------|
| 3B | 3B (Default) | 48.9 | 47.9 |
| 3B | 7B | 47.8 | 46.9 |
| 7B | 7B (Default) | 50.8 | 50.0 |
| 7B | 14B | 50.5 | 49.9 |

Increasing discriminator size from 3B to 7B for the 3B student *decreases* performance by -1.1 points. For the 7B student, increasing from 7B to 14B decreases by -0.3 points. The paper concludes "maintaining a balanced generator-discriminator pair is crucial."

This finding contradicts common intuition that a stronger critic is always better. It suggests that in the LLM distillation context, an overly powerful discriminator may provide gradients that are too sharp for the student to learn from effectively—a nuance that practitioners should note when implementing GAD.

**Teacher Model Variation (Table 7)**

Appendix B.1 includes experiments where the teacher is Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct (open-source) rather than GPT-5-Chat. This tests GAD in a scenario where the teacher's tokenizer is incompatible with the student (Llama family), preventing white-box distillation:

| Student | Method | LMSYS Score |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Llama-3.2-3B | SeqKD | 46.9 |
| Llama-3.2-3B | **GAD** | **47.5** |
| Llama-3.1-8B | SeqKD | 49.0 |
| Llama-3.1-8B | **GAD** | **49.6** |

GAD still outperforms SeqKD in this setting, demonstrating that the method works even when the teacher is open-source but architecturally incompatible with the student. This expands GAD's applicability beyond just proprietary API models.

### 5.5 Limitations and Conditional Findings

**Computational Cost**

The paper notes that "Distilling Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct from GPT-5-Chat takes about 30 hours on 16 H100 GPUs." This is substantially more expensive than SeqKD, which requires only supervised fine-tuning. The paper does not provide a direct compute comparison, but practitioners should note that GAD's benefits come with increased computational requirements due to the adversarial training loop and policy gradient optimization.

**Llama Family Performance**

While GAD outperforms SeqKD for Llama models in automatic evaluation (Table 2), the human evaluation results are less clear. For Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, GAD wins only 40-44% against baselines in human evaluation (Figure 3), compared to 52-68% for Qwen models. This suggests GAD's advantages may vary by model architecture, though the paper does not investigate the causes of this discrepancy.

**Teacher Exceedance Claim**

The claim that Qwen2.5-14B with GAD exceeds the GPT-5-Chat teacher (52.1 vs. 51.7) is based on GPT-4o evaluation on a single benchmark. The paper does not provide:
*   Statistical significance testing for this difference
*   Human evaluation comparing student directly to teacher
*   Results on additional benchmarks showing teacher exceedance

This claim should be interpreted cautiously—it may reflect optimization for the GPT-4o evaluator rather than genuine capability surpassing.

**Response Length Trade-offs**

Table 6 shows GAD produces longer responses than both SeqKD and the teacher (438.9 vs. 329.1 tokens on LMSYS for Qwen2.5-14B). While the paper frames this as maintaining the student's prior characteristics, longer responses increase inference cost and latency. For applications where response brevity is valued, this could be a limitation. The paper does not explore whether response length can be controlled without sacrificing quality.

### 5.6 Summary of Experimental Evidence

The experimental analysis provides strong support for GAD's core claims:

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Key Supporting Data |
|-------|-------------------|---------------------|
| GAD outperforms SeqKD | Strong | Table 2: Consistent gains across all models and benchmarks |
| GAD improves OOD generalization | Strong | Table 2: SeqKD sometimes harms OOD, GAD consistently improves |
| GAD avoids reward hacking | Strong | Figure 6: Direct comparison showing stability vs. degeneration |
| Student matches teacher | Moderate | Table 2: 52.1 vs. 51.7 on single benchmark, no direct human comparison |
| Warmup is necessary | Strong | Table 3: Removing warmup causes significant performance drops |
| Balanced G/D sizes matter | Strong | Table 5: Larger discriminators decrease performance |

The most convincing evidence is the OOD generalization improvement and the reward hacking prevention—both directly support the theoretical arguments about on-policy learning and co-evolving discriminators. The teacher exceedance claim is the weakest, relying on a single automatic evaluation metric without broader validation.

Overall, the experiments are well-designed with appropriate baselines, multiple model families, and thorough ablation studies. The main limitation is the reliance on GPT-4o as the primary evaluator, which introduces potential bias if the distilled models learn to optimize for this specific evaluator rather than general capability.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Generative Adversarial Distillation (GAD) demonstrates significant improvements over Sequence-Level Knowledge Distillation (SeqKD), adopting this framework introduces specific trade-offs in computational cost, training complexity, and evaluation reliance. This section critically examines the constraints inherent to the approach, highlighting scenarios where the benefits may be offset by practical limitations.

### 6.1 Computational and Resource Constraints

The most immediate barrier to adopting GAD is the increased computational burden compared to standard supervised fine-tuning.

*   **Training Cost:** GAD requires maintaining and updating two models simultaneously (generator and discriminator) and involves reinforcement learning optimization steps (policy gradient) rather than simple backpropagation through cross-entropy loss. Appendix A.2 explicitly quantifies this cost: "Distilling Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct from GPT-5-Chat takes about 30 hours on 16 H100 GPUs."
    *   **Implication:** This is substantially higher than SeqKD, which typically requires fewer epochs and no discriminator overhead. For organizations with limited GPU budgets, this trade-off may be prohibitive despite the performance gains.
*   **Memory Footprint:** During training, both the student and discriminator must reside in memory. Although the discriminator is initialized from the student parameters (Section 2.1), the training loop requires storing gradients and optimizer states for both models simultaneously. This effectively doubles the memory requirement compared to standard SFT, potentially limiting the maximum model size that can be distilled on a given hardware cluster.
*   **Teacher API Costs:** The method relies on collecting 200,000 teacher responses from the black-box API (Section 3.1). While this is a one-time cost, querying a proprietary model like GPT-5-Chat at this scale incurs significant financial expense and latency before training even begins. This makes GAD less suitable for scenarios where teacher access is heavily rate-limited or cost-prohibitive.

### 6.2 Inference Latency and Response Length

A subtle but important trade-off emerges in the generated output characteristics. GAD does not simply compress the teacher's behavior; it integrates teacher capabilities into the student's existing generation patterns, which can alter response length.

*   **Increased Token Count:** Table 6 in Appendix B.1 shows that GAD-trained models tend to produce longer responses than both SeqKD models and the teacher. For example, on the LMSYS test set:
    *   **Teacher (GPT-5-Chat):** 329.1 tokens
    *   **SeqKD (Qwen2.5-14B):** 319.3 tokens
    *   **GAD (Qwen2.5-14B):** 438.9 tokens
*   **Operational Impact:** While the paper attributes this to "on-policy sampling... aligned with both the student's prior and the teacher's guidance," longer responses directly increase inference latency and cost for end users. In latency-sensitive applications (e.g., real-time chat), a 33% increase in token generation (438.9 vs 329.1) may negate the efficiency gains achieved by using a smaller student model. The paper does not explore mechanisms to constrain response length during GAD training without sacrificing quality.

### 6.3 Evaluation Dependency and Optimizer Bias

The claim that GAD students can match or exceed the teacher relies heavily on a specific automatic evaluation metric, introducing the risk of optimizer bias (Goodhart's Law).

*   **GPT-4o as Judge:** The primary metric is the "GPT-4o evaluation score" (Section 3.1), where GPT-4o scores student outputs against reference answers. The notable result that Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct with GAD scores 52.1 versus the teacher's 51.7 (Table 2) is based entirely on this metric.
    *   **Risk:** There is a possibility that the adversarial training process implicitly optimizes for features that GPT-4o favors (e.g., verbosity, specific stylistic markers) rather than genuine capability. Since the discriminator is also a language model, there may be shared biases between the discriminator, the evaluator, and the teacher that inflate scores without reflecting true quality.
*   **Missing Direct Human Comparison:** While Figure 3 provides human evaluation comparing GAD to baselines (SeqKD and Before Distillation), there is **no human evaluation comparing the GAD student directly against the Teacher**.
    *   **Gap:** Without direct human preference data between Student vs. Teacher, the claim that the student "becomes comparable to its teacher" (Abstract) remains partially substantiated. The human eval shows GAD beats SeqKD (60% win rate for Qwen2.5-14B), but does not confirm it beats or matches the teacher in blind tests.

### 6.4 Training Stability and Hyperparameter Sensitivity

GAD is not a "plug-and-play" solution; it requires careful balancing of components to avoid training collapse.

*   **Warmup Dependency:** Table 3 demonstrates that removing the warmup stage significantly degrades performance. Removing discriminator warmup drops the LMSYS score from 50.8 to 49.0 for Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct.
    *   **Constraint:** This adds complexity to the training pipeline. Practitioners cannot simply start the adversarial game; they must first tune a supervised warmup phase to ensure the generator and discriminator start at comparable capability levels.
*   **Discriminator Capacity Limits:** Counter-intuitively, increasing the discriminator's size does not improve performance. Table 5 shows that for a 7B generator, increasing the discriminator from 7B to 14B *decreases* the LMSYS score from 50.8 to 50.5.
    *   **Implication:** This suggests a narrow "sweet spot" for the generator-discriminator capacity ratio. Unlike standard RLHF where a larger reward model is often preferred, GAD requires a balanced pair. This limits flexibility in leveraging existing larger models as critics without fine-tuning them down to match the student size.
*   **Model Family Variance:** The benefits of GAD appear somewhat dependent on the student architecture. In human evaluations (Figure 3), Qwen models show strong win rates against baselines (52-68%), whereas Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct shows lower win rates (40-44%).
    *   **Open Question:** The paper does not investigate why GAD performs less decisively on the Llama family in human evaluation despite automatic score gains. This suggests the method may require architecture-specific tuning or that certain model families are more susceptible to the adversarial optimization dynamics.

### 6.5 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

Several practical scenarios remain unexplored in the current work:

*   **Long-Context Distillation:** The experiments are limited to a maximum context length of 2048 tokens for prompts and 1536 for responses (Section 3.1). It is unclear whether GAD scales to long-context tasks (e.g., document summarization, codebase analysis) where the discriminator might struggle to assign accurate scalar scores to very long sequences.
*   **Domain Specificity:** The training data is drawn from LMSYS-Chat-1M-Clean, which is general conversational data. The out-of-distribution tests (Dolly, Vicuna) are also general instruction benchmarks. The paper does not validate GAD on specialized domains like mathematics (beyond general reasoning) or code generation, where exact syntax correctness is critical and adversarial "style" matching might be insufficient.
*   **Multi-Turn Consistency:** While the dataset is conversational, the training formulation treats each prompt-response pair largely independently (Section 2.1). The method does not explicitly optimize for consistency across multi-turn dialogue history, which is a key requirement for chatbot deployment.
*   **Safety and Alignment:** The paper focuses on capability transfer but does not address safety alignment. Adversarial training could theoretically amplify unsafe behaviors if the teacher's safety boundaries are not perfectly captured by the discriminator. Since the discriminator only learns to distinguish "teacher-like" from "student-like," it might reward fluent but unsafe responses if the teacher's safety patterns are subtle.

### 6.6 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes the key trade-offs identified in the paper:

| Feature | Benefit | Cost/Limitation | Evidence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Performance** | Surpasses SeqKD on all benchmarks | Higher computational cost (30h on 16 H100s) | Table 2, Appendix A.2 |
| **Generalization** | Improved OOD performance | Risk of optimizer bias (GPT-4o evaluator) | Figure 1, Section 3.1 |
| **Output Quality** | Mode-seeking behavior | Longer response lengths (higher inference cost) | Table 6 |
| **Training** | Avoids reward hacking via co-evolution | Requires careful warmup & balanced model sizes | Figure 6, Table 3, Table 5 |
| **Applicability** | Works with incompatible tokenizers | No direct human eval vs. Teacher | Table 7, Figure 3 |

In conclusion, while GAD represents a significant methodological advance for black-box distillation, it shifts the bottleneck from data access to computational complexity and training stability. Practitioners must weigh the performance gains against the increased training budget and inference latency before adoption.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section synthesizes how Generative Adversarial Distillation (GAD) reshapes the landscape of model compression, identifies concrete research avenues it opens, and provides actionable guidance for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this framework. We move beyond what the paper demonstrates to explore what becomes possible because of it.

### 7.1 Shifting the Black-Box Distillation Paradigm

GAD fundamentally alters the assumptions underlying black-box knowledge distillation. Prior to this work, the field operated under an implicit constraint: **without logits, on-policy learning is infeasible**. This assumption created a hard ceiling on black-box distillation quality, forcing practitioners to accept the limitations of supervised fine-tuning on teacher outputs (SeqKD).

GAD dismantles this constraint by demonstrating that **textual distinguishability can substitute for probability alignment**. The key insight—validated by Figure 6's reward hacking prevention and Table 2's OOD generalization gains—is that a co-evolving discriminator provides sufficient supervision signal for on-policy optimization without ever accessing the teacher's internal states.

This paradigm shift has three cascading implications:

*   **Proprietary Models Become Viable Teachers:** Organizations can now distill capabilities from API-only models (GPT-5-Chat, Gemini 2.5 [CBS+25]) without requiring white-box access. The tokenizer incompatibility issue noted in Table 7 experiments (Qwen2.5 teacher to Llama students) becomes irrelevant since GAD operates purely on text.
*   **On-Policy Benefits Extend to Closed-Source Settings:** The generalization advantages of on-policy learning—previously documented only in white-box settings [GDWH24, AVZ+24]—now apply to proprietary teachers. Figure 1 (Right) shows GAD achieving +1.7 to +1.9 average OOD gains over SeqKD, suggesting this is a systematic advantage rather than a benchmark-specific artifact.
*   **RL Infrastructure Becomes Distillation Infrastructure:** By mapping GAD components to RL terminology (Table 1), the paper enables practitioners to leverage existing RL frameworks like verl [SZY+24] and GRPO [SWZ+24] for distillation tasks. This lowers the barrier to adoption since teams no longer need specialized distillation codebases.

### 7.2 Open Research Questions

The success of GAD surfaces several unresolved questions that warrant systematic investigation. These represent high-value directions for follow-up work.

**Question 1: What is the optimal generator-discriminator capacity ratio?**

Table 5 reveals that matching generator and discriminator sizes (3B-3B, 7B-7B) outperforms mismatched pairs. However, the paper tests only a narrow range of ratios. Critical unknowns include:

*   Does the optimal ratio change with model scale (e.g., 70B students)?
*   Can a single large discriminator serve multiple smaller students efficiently?
*   What happens when the discriminator is significantly smaller than the generator?

This matters practically because organizations may have asymmetric model availability (e.g., a large reward model but small student budget).

**Question 2: Can GAD prevent capability collapse in extreme compression?**

All experiments in Table 2 compress by factors of 2–5× (14B→3B, 8B→3B). The paper does not address whether GAD remains effective at more aggressive compression ratios (e.g., 70B→1B). At extreme compression, the distributional gap between teacher and student may become too large for the discriminator to bridge, even with warmup.

**Question 3: How does GAD interact with safety alignment?**

Section 6.5 notes this as unaddressed, but it warrants emphasis. The discriminator learns to distinguish "teacher-like" from "student-like" text, not "safe" from "unsafe" text. If the teacher's safety boundaries are subtle (e.g., refusing harmful requests politely), the discriminator might reward fluent but unsafe responses that match the teacher's style without matching its constraints.

Future work should evaluate whether GAD preserves safety properties or requires explicit safety regularization during adversarial training.

**Question 4: Does the GPT-4o evaluator introduce systematic bias?**

The claim that Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct with GAD exceeds the GPT-5-Chat teacher (52.1 vs. 51.7 in Table 2) relies entirely on GPT-4o scoring. This creates a potential feedback loop:

$$
\text{GAD optimizes for } D(\cdot) \rightarrow D(\cdot) \text{ is an LLM} \rightarrow \text{GPT-4o evaluates } \rightarrow \text{GPT-4o is an LLM}
$$

If GPT-4o and the discriminator share architectural biases (e.g., preferring verbose responses), the evaluation may overstate GAD's advantages. Table 6 shows GAD produces 33% longer responses than the teacher (438.9 vs. 329.1 tokens on LMSYS), which could correlate with higher GPT-4o scores independent of quality.

Follow-up research should employ diverse evaluators (human preference, task-specific metrics, multiple LLM judges) to disentangle genuine capability transfer from evaluator optimization.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

GAD is not universally superior to all alternatives. Its value depends on specific constraints and objectives. The following decision framework helps practitioners determine when GAD is appropriate.

**When to Use GAD:**

| Scenario | Rationale | Supporting Evidence |
|----------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Proprietary teacher with no logit access** | Only black-box method enabling on-policy learning | Section 1: "white-box access is often impractical when the teacher is a proprietary API model" |
| **OOD generalization is critical** | GAD shows +1.7 to +1.9 average OOD gains over SeqKD | Table 2: Dolly, SelfInst, Vicuna results |
| **Tokenizer incompatibility exists** | GAD operates on text alone, no logit alignment needed | Table 7: Qwen2.5 teacher to Llama students |
| **Training stability is prioritized** | Co-evolving discriminator prevents reward hacking | Figure 6: GAD stable vs. off-policy collapse at 300 steps |
| **Compute budget allows 30+ hours on 16 H100s** | GAD requires adversarial training overhead | Appendix A.2: "30 hours on 16 H100 GPUs" for 14B distillation |

**When to Prefer Alternatives:**

| Scenario | Better Alternative | Rationale |
|----------|-------------------|-----------|
| **White-box teacher access available** | Standard KD with KLD [SST+20] | Direct probability alignment is more sample-efficient |
| **Minimal compute budget** | SeqKD [KR16] | Supervised fine-tuning requires fewer epochs and no discriminator |
| **Response brevity is critical** | SeqKD with length regularization | Table 6: GAD produces 33% longer responses than teacher |
| **Safety alignment is paramount** | RLHF with human feedback [OWJ+22] | GAD does not explicitly optimize for safety properties |
| **Extreme compression (>10×)** | Unvalidated for GAD | Paper tests only 2–5× compression ratios |

**Integration Checklist:**

For teams adopting GAD, the following steps are essential based on the paper's ablation studies:

1.  **Implement warmup correctly:** Run 1 epoch of joint generator-discriminator warmup before adversarial training. Table 3 shows removing discriminator warmup drops LMSYS scores by -1.8 points (50.8→49.0).
2.  **Match model capacities:** Use equal-sized generator and discriminator. Table 5 shows increasing discriminator size degrades performance.
3.  **Use Bradley-Terry loss:** Do not substitute cross-entropy for the discriminator. Table 4 shows Bradley-Terry provides +1.0 point gains on LMSYS.
4.  **Monitor response length:** Track token counts during training to detect early signs of reward hacking. Figure 6 shows hacking manifests as excessive verbosity (~1300 tokens).
5.  **Validate on OOD benchmarks:** Do not rely solely on in-distribution scores. Table 2 shows SeqKD can harm OOD performance while GAD improves it.

### 7.4 Broader Impact on the Field

GAD's contributions extend beyond distillation specifically. The framework demonstrates that **adversarial co-evolution can substitute for inaccessible supervision signals** in language model training. This principle may generalize to other settings where direct feedback is unavailable:

*   **Domain Adaptation:** A discriminator could distinguish target-domain from source-domain text, enabling adaptation without target-domain labels.
*   **Privacy-Preserving Training:** Students could learn from sensitive data by distinguishing synthetic from real outputs without exposing raw data.
*   **Multi-Model Collaboration:** Multiple students could train against a shared discriminator, enabling ensemble distillation without requiring teacher ensemble logits.

The reinforcement learning framing (Table 1) also bridges two previously distinct communities. Distillation researchers gain access to RL optimization techniques (policy gradient, advantage estimation), while RL researchers gain a new application domain where reward models can be learned online rather than fixed.

### 7.5 Recommended Next Steps for Practitioners

For teams considering GAD adoption, we recommend the following progression:

1.  **Start with small-scale validation:** Replicate the 3B student experiments from Table 2 before scaling to 14B models. This validates infrastructure with lower compute cost.
2.  **Benchmark against your specific use case:** The paper uses LMSYS-Chat, Dolly, SelfInst, and Vicuna. Evaluate GAD on your domain-specific benchmarks to confirm OOD gains translate.

### 7.6 Summary

GAD establishes that effective on-policy distillation is achievable in black-box settings through adversarial co-evolution. The method consistently outperforms SeqKD across model families and sizes, with particular strength in out-of-distribution generalization. However, adoption requires careful attention to training stability (warmup, balanced capacities), computational budget (30+ hours on 16 H100s for 14B models), and evaluation rigor (avoiding optimizer bias).

The framework's true significance lies in decoupling on-policy learning from white-box access—a constraint that previously limited black-box distillation to off-policy supervised fine-tuning. By removing this constraint, GAD enables a new class of distillation applications where proprietary models serve as teachers without requiring logit access, tokenizer compatibility, or architectural alignment.

Future work should address the open questions around safety alignment, extreme compression ratios, and evaluator bias. Until then, GAD represents the state-of-the-art for black-box LLM distillation when compute budget permits and OOD generalization is prioritized.
