## 1. Executive Summary

This paper challenges the prevailing belief that Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) enables Large Language Models (LLMs) to acquire fundamentally new reasoning abilities, demonstrating instead that current RLVR methods merely improve sampling efficiency within the existing capability boundaries of the base model. Through extensive evaluation across mathematics (e.g., AIME24, MATH500), code generation (LiveCodeBench), and visual reasoning benchmarks using the `pass@k` metric, the authors show that while RLVR-trained models (such as those based on Qwen2.5 and LLaMA-3.1) outperform base models at low sample counts ($k=1$), base models consistently surpass them at higher $k$ values (e.g., $k=128$ or $256$), indicating a narrower reasoning coverage post-training. The study concludes that RLVR does not expand the set of solvable problems—often reducing it—and that all reasoning paths exploited by RLVR models are already present in the base model's distribution, whereas distillation from stronger teachers remains the only method observed to genuinely expand reasoning capacity.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Distinguishing Efficiency from Capacity Expansion

The central question driving this research is whether **Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR)** genuinely expands the reasoning *capacity* of Large Language Models (LLMs) or merely optimizes their *sampling efficiency*. 

In the current landscape of AI development, RLVR has emerged as a dominant paradigm for post-training, credited with enabling breakthroughs in complex domains like mathematics (e.g., AIME, Olympiad problems) and code generation. The prevailing hypothesis, inspired by successes in traditional reinforcement learning (such as AlphaGo Zero mastering Go through self-play), is that RLVR allows LLMs to autonomously discover novel reasoning strategies—patterns of thought like self-reflection, backtracking, or iterative refinement—that were not present in the pretrained base model. This implies a qualitative leap in capability: the model learns to solve problems it previously could not.

However, this paper identifies a critical gap in our understanding: **we lack rigorous metrics to distinguish between a model that has learned *new* skills and one that has simply become better at retrieving *existing* skills.** 

Most standard evaluations rely on greedy decoding (generating the single most likely token sequence) or low-sample averages ($k=1$). These metrics measure "average-case" performance. If an RLVR-trained model outputs the correct answer more frequently on its first try, it appears superior. But this observation is ambiguous. It could mean:
1.  **Capacity Expansion:** The model has learned a new reasoning path to solve a problem it previously failed.
2.  **Efficiency Gain:** The model already knew how to solve the problem (the path existed in its base distribution) but was unlikely to sample it; RLVR has simply increased the probability of that specific path.

To resolve this ambiguity, the authors argue we must look beyond average performance and probe the **reasoning capability boundary**—the absolute set of problems a model *can* solve given sufficient attempts.

### Why This Distinction Matters

Resolving this ambiguity is not merely an academic exercise; it has profound implications for the future trajectory of AI development:

*   **Strategic Direction for Research:** If RLVR primarily improves efficiency, then pouring massive computational resources into scaling RL training may yield diminishing returns once the model converges on the optimal sampling distribution of its base prior. Conversely, if RLVR truly expands capacity, then scaling RL is a viable path to superhuman reasoning. The paper's findings suggest the former, urging a pivot toward methods that genuinely expand the prior (like distillation) or improve exploration mechanisms.
*   **Theoretical Understanding of Learning:** Traditional RL in bounded environments (like games) is known to discover strategies surpassing human knowledge. If RLVR fails to do this for LLMs, it highlights a fundamental difference between learning in discrete, low-dimensional action spaces (games) and the vast, high-dimensional space of natural language. Understanding *why* RLVR struggles to explore new reasoning patterns in LLMs is crucial for developing the next generation of algorithms.
*   **Resource Allocation:** Training RLVR models is computationally expensive. If the primary benefit is sampling efficiency, alternative approaches like **distillation** (copying reasoning patterns from a stronger teacher) might offer a more cost-effective route to capability gains, as the paper suggests distillation *does* expand the reasoning boundary while RLVR does not.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches

Before this work, the evaluation of RLVR suffered from several key limitations that obscured the true nature of its improvements:

1.  **Reliance on Pass@1 Metrics:** Previous studies largely focused on `pass@1` accuracy (the probability the first generated answer is correct). While useful for deployment scenarios where only one answer is shown to a user, `pass@1` is a poor proxy for *potential* capability. A model might fail a difficult math problem 99% of the time but succeed 1% of the time; `pass@1` records this as a failure, masking the fact that the model *possesses* the solution within its parameter space.
2.  **Lack of Baseline Comparison at Scale:** Few works systematically compared the **coverage** of solvable problems between base and RLVR models using high sample counts. Without measuring `pass@k` at large $k$ (e.g., $k=128, 256, 1024$), it is impossible to determine if the "tail" of the distribution (the hard problems) has improved or shrunk.
3.  **Assumption of Emergence:** There was a widespread assumption that complex behaviors like "Chain-of-Thought" (CoT) reflection observed in RLVR models (e.g., DeepSeek-R1) were *emergent* properties learned during RL. This paper challenges that assumption by demonstrating that these patterns often pre-exist in the base model, merely suppressed by lower probability.
4.  **Narrow Scope of Analysis:** Prior analyses that did hint at limitations (e.g., Dang et al., 2025) were often restricted to small models (e.g., 0.5B parameters) or single benchmarks (e.g., GSM8K), leaving open the question of whether these phenomena scale to frontier models and diverse tasks like coding or visual reasoning.

### Positioning of This Work

This paper positions itself as a **systematic stress-test** of the RLVR paradigm. Unlike prior work that celebrates RLVR's gains in `pass@1`, this study adopts a skeptical lens, asking: *"What has the model actually lost or gained in its total solution space?"*

The authors differentiate their approach through three key methodological choices:
*   **Adoption of `pass@k` as the Primary Metric:** By evaluating models at large $k$ values, the study measures the **union** of all solvable problems rather than the **mode**. This reveals the true "reasoning boundary."
*   **Comprehensive Scope:** The analysis spans multiple model families (Qwen2.5, LLaMA-3.1, Mistral), sizes (7B to 32B+), and domains (Math, Code, Visual Reasoning), ensuring the findings are robust and not artifacts of a specific architecture or task.
*   **Mechanistic Analysis:** Beyond performance curves, the paper employs **perplexity analysis** and **accuracy distribution histograms** to trace the origin of RLVR solutions. By showing that the perplexity of RLVR-generated paths is low under the *base* model, the authors provide evidence that these paths were already "known" to the base model, effectively proving that RLVR is reshaping the distribution rather than creating new knowledge.

In essence, this work shifts the narrative from "RLVR makes models smarter" to "RLVR makes models more consistent, but potentially narrower," challenging the community to rethink how we achieve genuine reasoning breakthroughs.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a systematic empirical investigation rather than a new algorithmic proposal, utilizing the `pass@k` metric as a diagnostic tool to map the "reasoning boundary" of Large Language Models (LLMs) before and after Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) training. The core idea is to treat the base model's capability at high sample counts ($k \to \infty$) as an upper bound of potential knowledge, and then measure whether RLVR training expands this boundary or merely redistributes probability mass within it.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system under study is not a new AI architecture, but a rigorous evaluation framework that compares pretrained "base" LLMs against their "RLVR-trained" counterparts across mathematics, coding, and visual reasoning tasks. The solution shape involves generating hundreds of diverse responses per problem to construct coverage curves (`pass@k`), revealing that while RLVR makes correct answers more likely to appear early (efficiency), it often shrinks the total set of solvable problems (capacity) compared to the original base model.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The experimental pipeline consists of four primary components operating in a sequential flow:
1.  **Model Pairs Selection:** The study selects specific pairs of models where one is a pretrained base (e.g., `Qwen2.5-7B-Base`) and the other is the same model fine-tuned via RLVR (e.g., `SimpleRLZoo-Qwen2.5-7B`), ensuring a controlled comparison of the RL effect.
2.  **High-Volume Sampling Engine:** For each benchmark problem, the system generates a large batch of $n$ responses (typically $n=128$ to $1024$) from both models using stochastic decoding (temperature $T=0.6$, top-p $0.95$), creating a diverse set of potential reasoning paths.
3.  **Verifier & Metric Calculator:** A deterministic verifier checks each response against ground truth (exact match for math, unit tests for code) to compute the `pass@k` metric, which estimates the probability that *at least one* of $k$ samples is correct.
4.  **Distributional Analysis Module:** Beyond simple accuracy, the system computes perplexity scores and accuracy histograms to determine if the reasoning paths found by the RLVR model were already probable under the base model's distribution.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Defining the Core Metric (`pass@k`):** We first explain the mathematical formulation of `pass@k` and why it is superior to standard `pass@1` for measuring reasoning boundaries.
*   **Experimental Configuration:** We detail the specific models, benchmarks, and hyperparameters (temperature, token limits) used to ensure a fair comparison between base and RLVR systems.
*   **The Verification Protocol:** We describe how correctness is determined across different domains (math vs. code) and the steps taken to prevent "guessing" from inflating scores.
*   **Diagnostic Mechanisms:** We explain the secondary analyses—perplexity measurement and accuracy distribution mapping—used to prove that RLVR paths originate from the base model's prior.
*   **Comparative Baselines:** We outline how distillation and different RL algorithms are integrated into the study to isolate the specific effects of RLVR.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Metric: The `pass@k` Estimator**
The study relies on the `pass@k` metric to quantify the reasoning boundary, defined as the probability that a model generates at least one correct solution within $k$ independent sampling attempts.
*   Unlike `pass@1`, which measures the likelihood of the single most probable output being correct, `pass@k` measures the *coverage* of the model's output distribution, effectively asking "Does the model know the answer anywhere in its probability space?"
*   To compute this efficiently without generating infinite samples, the authors use an unbiased, low-variance estimator derived from code generation literature.
*   For a given problem $x_i$, the system generates $n$ total samples (where $n \ge k$) and counts $c_i$ as the number of correct solutions among those $n$ samples.
*   The estimated `pass@k` for the entire dataset is the average over all problems of the probability that a random subset of size $k$ contains at least one correct answer, calculated as:
    $$ \text{pass@k} := \mathbb{E}_{x_i \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ 1 - \frac{\binom{n - c_i}{k}}{\binom{n}{k}} \right] $$
*   In this equation, $\binom{n}{k}$ represents the total number of ways to choose $k$ samples from $n$, and $\binom{n - c_i}{k}$ represents the number of ways to choose $k$ samples that are *all incorrect*.
*   By subtracting the probability of "all incorrect" from 1, the formula yields the probability of "at least one correct."
*   The authors set $n$ to the maximum $k$ value in their curves (typically $128$, $256$, or $1024$ depending on the benchmark difficulty) to ensure the estimator remains valid across the entire range of $k$.

**Model Selection and Training Configurations**
To isolate the effect of RLVR, the study strictly compares models that differ only by the application of reinforcement learning, avoiding confounding variables like different base architectures.
*   **Mathematics Domain:** The primary experiments use the `Qwen2.5` family (7B, 14B, 32B base variants) and `LLaMA-3.1-8B`.
*   The RLVR counterparts are sourced from `SimpleRLZoo`, which applies the GRPO algorithm in a "Zero-RL" setting (no supervised fine-tuning) on GSM8K and MATH training sets.
*   Additional strong RLVR models like `Oat-Zero-7B` and `DAPO-32B` are included to test if advanced algorithms change the fundamental findings.
*   **Code Generation Domain:** The study evaluates `CodeR1-Zero-Qwen2.5-7B` (trained on 12K LeetCode/TACO samples) and `DeepCoder-14B` (distilled from DeepSeek-R1 then RL-trained).
*   **Visual Reasoning Domain:** The `EasyR1` framework is used to train `Qwen2.5-VL-7B` on the Geometry3K dataset, testing multimodal reasoning boundaries.
*   **Hyperparameters for Sampling:** To ensure fair comparison, both base and RLVR models are sampled with a temperature of $0.6$ and a top-p value of $0.95$.
*   The maximum generation length is set to $16,384$ tokens to allow for long Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning without premature truncation.
*   Crucially, the evaluation uses **zero-shot prompts** for base models, deliberately avoiding few-shot examples that might artificially boost performance, whereas RLVR models are evaluated with the same prompts used during their training.

**Verification Protocols and Anti-Guessing Measures**
A critical technical challenge in using `pass@k` for math is distinguishing between genuine reasoning and lucky guessing, especially as $k$ increases.
*   **Math Verification:** For mathematical benchmarks, a deterministic verifier checks if the final numerical answer matches the ground truth exactly.
*   To address the "guessing" risk, the authors perform a manual inspection of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) traces for the hardest problems (those with average accuracy $&lt;5\%$).
*   This manual check confirms that when the base model solves a hard problem at high $k$, it typically employs valid, reflective reasoning steps rather than random number generation.
*   **Code Verification:** For coding tasks, the verification is inherently robust because a solution must pass all predefined unit tests to be marked correct, making accidental success statistically negligible.
*   **Visual Verification:** In visual reasoning, multiple-choice options are removed to force open-ended generation, and correctness is verified against ground truth solutions, with manual checks performed on low-accuracy subsets.

**Diagnostic Mechanism 1: Accuracy Distribution Analysis**
To understand *how* the performance changes, the authors analyze the histogram of per-problem accuracies rather than just the aggregate mean.
*   The system bins problems based on their solved rate (e.g., $0.0$, $(0.0, 0.1]$, ..., $1.0$) for both base and RLVR models.
*   This reveals a specific shift: RLVR training increases the frequency of problems with accuracy near $1.0$ (easy problems become trivial) but often increases the frequency of problems with accuracy $0.0$ (hard problems become unsolvable).
*   This "hollowing out" of the middle distribution explains why `pass@1` improves (more easy problems) while `pass@k` at large $k$ decreases (fewer hard problems remain in the solvable set).

**Diagnostic Mechanism 2: Perplexity Tracing**
The study employs perplexity analysis to determine if RLVR creates *new* reasoning paths or simply amplifies existing ones.
*   Perplexity ($PPL$) measures how "surprised" a model is by a specific sequence of tokens; lower perplexity indicates the sequence is highly probable under the model's distribution.
*   The authors calculate the perplexity of RLVR-generated correct responses ($Y_{RL}$) using the **base model** as the reference, denoted as $PPL_{Base}(Y_{RL} | x)$.
*   If RLVR discovered entirely new reasoning strategies, $PPL_{Base}(Y_{RL} | x)$ should be high (the base model would find these paths unlikely or nonsensical).
*   However, the experiments show that $PPL_{Base}(Y_{RL} | x)$ is consistently low and matches the lower end of the base model's own output distribution ($PPL_{Base}(Y_{Base} | x)$).
*   This mathematical evidence confirms that the "correct" paths found by the RLVR model were already present and relatively probable in the base model, implying RLVR acts as a filter rather than a creator.

**Comparative Baselines: Distillation and Algorithm Variants**
To contextualize the limitations of RLVR, the study includes two critical comparative dimensions.
*   **Distillation Baseline:** The authors evaluate `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B`, a model trained by mimicking the outputs of a stronger teacher model.
*   Unlike RLVR, the `pass@k` curves for distilled models lie strictly *above* the base model even at large $k$, proving that distillation *can* expand the reasoning boundary by importing new patterns from the teacher.
*   **Algorithm Variants:** To ensure the findings are not specific to one RL algorithm, the authors re-implement and test six popular algorithms: PPO, GRPO, Reinforce++, RLOO, ReMax, and DAPO using the `VeRL` framework.
*   All algorithms are trained with a learning rate of $10^{-6}$, a prompt batch size of $256$, and $8$ rollouts per prompt, with the KL-divergence penalty removed to allow maximum policy deviation.
*   The results show that while specific algorithms (like RLOO) achieve slightly better sampling efficiency, none of them overcome the fundamental ceiling imposed by the base model's prior knowledge.

**Sampling Efficiency Gap ($\Delta SE$)**
Finally, the paper introduces a derived metric to quantify the sub-optimality of current RLVR methods.
*   The Sampling Efficiency Gap is defined as $\Delta SE = \text{pass@}k_{base} - \text{pass@}1_{RL}$, where $k=256$ serves as a proxy for the base model's maximum potential.
*   This metric captures the difference between what the base model *could* do with enough tries and what the RLVR model does on its first try.
*   A large $\Delta SE$ indicates that the RLVR model has failed to fully capitalize on the base model's latent capabilities, remaining far from the theoretical optimum of converting all latent knowledge into immediate performance.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This study delivers four fundamental insights that reshape our understanding of Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR). Rather than offering incremental performance tweaks, these findings challenge the core theoretical assumptions about how LLMs learn reasoning skills.

### 4.1 The "Efficiency-Capacity Trade-off": RLVR Improves Sampling but Narrows Reasoning Boundaries

**The Insight:**
The most striking discovery is that **RLVR training induces a trade-off between sampling efficiency and reasoning coverage**. While RLVR models significantly outperform base models at low sample counts ($k=1$), they consistently underperform base models at high sample counts ($k \ge 128$). This phenomenon, observed across all tested model families (Qwen, LLaMA, Mistral) and domains (Math, Code, Vision), implies that RLVR does not expand the set of solvable problems; instead, it often *shrinks* it.

**Why This Differs from Prior Work:**
Previous literature has largely celebrated RLVR for its gains in `pass@1` accuracy, interpreting these gains as evidence of enhanced reasoning capability. Studies like DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025) highlighted the emergence of complex behaviors (e.g., self-reflection) as proof of new learning. This paper flips that narrative by showing that:
*   **Base models possess a wider "long tail" of capabilities:** They can solve a broader variety of hard problems if given enough attempts, even if they rarely do so on the first try.
*   **RLVR prunes the solution space:** By optimizing for the most reliable paths, RLVR inadvertently suppresses diverse, non-standard reasoning strategies that the base model used to access difficult solutions. As shown in **Figure 1 (Left)**, the RLVR model's search tree becomes narrower, losing branches that contained valid solutions for certain problem types (Problem B).

**Significance:**
This finding fundamentally alters the strategic view of RLVR. It suggests that current RLVR methods are **not a path to superhuman reasoning via self-discovery**, but rather a mechanism for **distilling and concentrating existing knowledge**. The implication is critical for scaling laws: simply adding more RL compute may hit a hard ceiling defined by the base model's pre-training, necessitating new approaches (like better pre-training or distillation) to genuinely expand capabilities.

### 4.2 The "Prior Bound" Hypothesis: RLVR Paths Pre-Exist in the Base Model Distribution

**The Insight:**
Through rigorous **perplexity analysis**, the authors provide empirical proof that **reasoning paths generated by RLVR models are not novel creations but are already present in the base model's output distribution**. Specifically, the perplexity of RLVR-generated correct responses ($Y_{RL}$) when evaluated by the base model ($PPL_{Base}(Y_{RL})$) is consistently low, matching the perplexity of the base model's own best samples (**Figure 6**).

**Why This Differs from Prior Work:**
The prevailing hypothesis in the field has been that RLVR enables "exploration" akin to AlphaGo Zero, discovering strategies unreachable by supervised learning or pre-training. This paper debunks that analogy for LLMs.
*   **No New Strategies:** Unlike game-playing agents that find moves humans never considered, RLVR-trained LLMs merely up-weight reasoning traces that were already probable (though perhaps less dominant) in the base model.
*   **Mechanism of Action:** The RL process acts as a **probability shaper**, sharpening the distribution around high-reward paths while suppressing low-probability (but potentially correct) outliers. This is confirmed by the accuracy histograms in **Figure 5**, which show a mass shift toward accuracy $1.0$ and $0.0$, with a depletion of the middle ground where diverse solving strategies reside.

**Significance:**
This insight establishes a **theoretical upper bound** for RLVR performance: an RLVR model cannot solve problems that its base model cannot solve given sufficient sampling. It reframes RLVR from a "learning" algorithm to a "selection" algorithm. This explains why "Zero-RL" approaches often struggle on tasks where the base model lacks any prior intuition—the RL signal cannot guide exploration in a void of zero probability.

### 4.3 Distillation vs. RLVR: A Fundamental Dichotomy in Capability Expansion

**The Insight:**
The study draws a sharp distinction between **RLVR** and **Distillation**, demonstrating that they operate via fundamentally different mechanisms. While RLVR is bounded by the base model's prior, **distillation from a stronger teacher genuinely expands the reasoning boundary**. As illustrated in **Figure 7**, the `pass@k` curve for a distilled model (e.g., `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B`) lies strictly above the base model's curve across all $k$, indicating the acquisition of new reasoning patterns.

**Why This Differs from Prior Work:**
Prior works often conflated the benefits of RL and distillation, or used them in tandem without disentangling their effects. For instance, many "RL-enhanced" models are first distilled from a stronger teacher before undergoing RL. This paper isolates the variables to show:
*   **Distillation = Capacity Expansion:** It imports new "software" (reasoning patterns) from the teacher into the student.
*   **RLVR = Efficiency Optimization:** It optimizes the execution of existing "software."

**Significance:**
This finding has immediate practical implications for model development pipelines. If the goal is to solve harder problems (expand the frontier), **distillation is currently superior to RLVR**. RLVR should be viewed as a final polishing step to make the model more reliable and cost-effective (by reducing the need for multiple samples), rather than a primary engine for capability growth. This suggests that the success of models like DeepSeek-R1 may be attributed more to the quality of the teacher data used in distillation than to the RL phase itself.

### 4.4 The Sampling Efficiency Gap ($\Delta SE$): Quantifying Sub-Optimality in Current RL Algorithms

**The Insight:**
The authors introduce the **Sampling Efficiency Gap ($\Delta SE$)** as a novel metric to quantify how far current RLVR methods fall short of the theoretical optimum. Defined as the difference between the base model's `pass@k` (at large $k$, e.g., 256) and the RLVR model's `pass@1`, $\Delta SE$ measures the unused potential of the base model. The study reveals that **all tested RL algorithms (PPO, GRPO, DAPO, etc.) exhibit a large, persistent $\Delta SE$ (>40 points)**, indicating they are far from optimally converting the base model's latent knowledge into immediate performance.

**Why This Differs from Prior Work:**
Most RL research focuses on maximizing absolute scores (`pass@1`) without referencing the base model's potential as a baseline. By introducing $\Delta SE$, this paper shifts the evaluation paradigm from "How good is the RL model?" to "How well did the RL algorithm exploit the base model?"
*   **Algorithm Agnosticism:** The results in **Figure 8** show that different algorithms yield surprisingly similar $\Delta SE$ values, suggesting that the limitation is not specific to PPO or GRPO but is inherent to the current RLVR paradigm itself.
*   **Training Dynamics:** The gap widens as training progresses (**Figure 1, Right**), showing that prolonged RL training further degrades coverage while marginally improving efficiency.

**Significance:**
This metric provides a clear target for future algorithmic innovation. The large $\Delta SE$ indicates a massive opportunity for improvement: if an RL algorithm could achieve the base model's `pass@256` performance at `pass@1`, it would represent a orders-of-magnitude leap in efficiency. The fact that current methods fail to do so highlights the need for **better exploration strategies**, **fine-grained process rewards**, or **multi-turn agentic frameworks** that can navigate the vast action space of language more effectively than simple outcome-based reinforcement.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section details the rigorous empirical evaluation conducted to test the hypothesis that RLVR fails to expand reasoning capacity beyond the base model's prior. The analysis spans three distinct domains—mathematics, code generation, and visual reasoning—utilizing a unified evaluation protocol designed to expose the "reasoning boundary" rather than just average performance.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

**Datasets and Benchmarks**
To ensure the findings are robust across difficulty levels and domains, the study employs a comprehensive suite of benchmarks:
*   **Mathematics:** The evaluation covers a spectrum from grade-school to Olympiad-level difficulty, including `GSM8K`, `MATH500`, `Minerva`, `Olympiad`, `AIME24`, and `AMC23`. These datasets require multi-step Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning and exact numerical answers.
*   **Code Generation:** To verify findings in a domain where "guessing" is statistically impossible, the study uses `LiveCodeBench` (v5, Aug 2024–Jan 2025), `HumanEval+`, and `MBPP+`. Correctness is determined by passing all unit tests.
*   **Visual Reasoning:** Multimodal capabilities are tested on `MathVista-TestMini` and `MathVision-TestMini`, focusing on geometry and visual math problems where models must interpret images alongside text.

**Model Families and Baselines**
The core experimental design relies on controlled comparisons between **Base Models** and their **RLVR-trained counterparts**.
*   **Primary Pairs:** The study focuses on the `Qwen2.5` family (7B, 14B, 32B) and `LLaMA-3.1-8B`. The RLVR variants are sourced from `SimpleRLZoo` (using GRPO in a Zero-RL setting) and other strong open-source releases like `Oat-Zero-7B` and `DAPO-32B`.
*   **Algorithm Comparison:** To rule out algorithm-specific artifacts, the authors re-implement six popular RL algorithms—`PPO`, `GRPO`, `Reinforce++`, `RLOO`, `ReMax`, and `DAPO`—using the `VeRL` framework under identical hyperparameters (learning rate $10^{-6}$, batch size 256, 8 rollouts).
*   **Distillation Baseline:** A critical baseline is `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B`, which allows the authors to contrast RLVR against a method known to import new capabilities.

**Metrics and Sampling Protocol**
The study departs from standard `pass@1` evaluation to adopt the **`pass@k`** metric as the primary measure of reasoning capacity.
*   **Sampling Strategy:** For every problem, the system generates $n$ independent responses (where $n$ ranges from 128 to 1024 depending on benchmark difficulty) using stochastic decoding (Temperature $T=0.6$, Top-p $0.95$).
*   **Metric Definition:** `pass@k` is calculated as the probability that *at least one* of $k$ samples is correct. A high `pass@k` at large $k$ (e.g., $k=256$) indicates that the solution exists somewhere in the model's distribution, even if it is rare.
*   **Anti-Guessing Verification:** For math problems, the authors manually inspect CoT traces for the hardest questions (average accuracy $&lt;5\%$) to ensure correct answers are derived from valid reasoning rather than lucky guesses. For code, the unit test verifier inherently prevents false positives.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results: The Efficiency-Capacity Trade-off

The experimental results consistently reveal a counter-intuitive phenomenon: while RLVR improves immediate accuracy, it degrades the model's ability to solve difficult problems given multiple attempts.

**Mathematical Reasoning: The Crossover Effect**
As illustrated in **Figure 2**, the `pass@k` curves for base and RLVR models exhibit a distinct "crossover" pattern across all math benchmarks.
*   **Low $k$ (Efficiency):** At $k=1$, RLVR models significantly outperform base models. For instance, on `MATH500`, the `Qwen2.5-32B` RLVR model achieves a higher `pass@1` than its base counterpart, confirming that RLVR successfully concentrates probability mass on correct answers.
*   **High $k$ (Capacity):** As $k$ increases, the base model's curve rises more steeply and eventually surpasses the RLVR model. On the `Minerva` benchmark with the 32B model, the base model outperforms the RLVR model by approximately **9%** at $k=128$. This implies the base model can solve ~9% more problems if allowed sufficient sampling attempts, whereas the RLVR model has "forgotten" or suppressed the diverse paths needed to solve them.
*   **Strong RLVR Models:** This trend holds even for state-of-the-art models. **Figure 11** shows that `Oat-Zero-7B` and `DAPO-32B`, despite their strong `pass@1` performance on `AIME24`, are eventually overtaken by their respective base models at high $k$.

**Code Generation and Visual Reasoning**
The phenomenon is not limited to mathematics.
*   **Code:** In **Figure 3** and **Figure 4 (Left)**, RLVR models like `CodeR1-Zero` show superior `pass@1` on `LiveCodeBench` but fall behind the base `Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct` as $k$ approaches 128. Since code correctness is binary and hard to guess, this confirms that the base model possesses a wider variety of valid algorithmic approaches that the RLVR process prunes away.
*   **Visual:** **Figure 4 (Right)** demonstrates the same crossover on `MathVista` and `MathVision`, indicating that multimodal reasoning boundaries are also narrowed by current RLVR training.

**Accuracy Distribution: The "Hollowing Out" Effect**
To understand the mechanism behind the crossover, the authors analyze the histogram of per-problem accuracies (**Figure 5**).
*   RLVR training shifts the distribution towards the extremes: it increases the frequency of problems with accuracy **1.0** (easy problems become trivial) and problems with accuracy **0.0** (hard problems become unsolvable).
*   Crucially, the frequency of problems with intermediate accuracy (e.g., 0.1–0.5) decreases. This suggests that RLVR eliminates the "long tail" of diverse, low-probability reasoning strategies that the base model used to occasionally solve hard problems.
*   **Table 2** quantifies this coverage loss on `AIME24` and `MATH500`. On `AIME24`, there are **13.3%** of problems solvable by the base model but *not* by the RLVR model, whereas only **0.0%** are solvable by the RLVR model but not the base. This asymmetry proves that the RLVR solvable set is nearly a strict subset of the base model's solvable set.

### 5.3 Mechanistic Evidence: Perplexity and the Prior Bound

The paper provides direct evidence that RLVR does not create new reasoning paths but merely selects existing ones.

**Perplexity Analysis**
Using the metric $PPL_{Base}(Y_{RL} | x)$, the authors measure how "surprising" RLVR-generated solutions are to the base model.
*   **Figure 6** shows that the perplexity distribution of RLVR correct responses ($Y_{RL}$) under the base model closely matches the lower end (most probable) of the base model's own output distribution ($Y_{Base}$).
*   If RLVR were discovering novel strategies, $PPL_{Base}(Y_{RL})$ would be high. Instead, the low perplexity confirms that **every reasoning path exploited by the RLVR model was already present and relatively probable in the base model**.
*   Furthermore, **Section C.4** notes that $PPL_{Base}(Y_{RL})$ decreases as RL training progresses, indicating the policy is increasingly confined to the high-density regions of the base prior.

**Distillation vs. RLVR**
The distinction between capacity expansion and efficiency optimization is starkly visible when comparing RLVR to distillation.
*   **Figure 7** plots the `pass@k` curves for `Qwen2.5-Math-7B` (Base), its RLVR version, and `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B`.
*   While the RLVR curve crosses below the base curve at high $k$, the **Distilled model's curve lies strictly above the base model** across all $k$.
*   This proves that distillation successfully imports new reasoning patterns (expanding the boundary), whereas RLVR fails to do so, remaining bounded by the base model's initial knowledge.

### 5.4 Algorithmic Robustness and Ablation Studies

To ensure these findings are not artifacts of a specific algorithm or hyperparameter choice, the authors conduct extensive ablations.

**Comparison of RL Algorithms**
**Figure 8 (Top)** and **Table 3** compare six different RL algorithms (PPO, GRPO, RLOO, etc.) on `Omni-MATH` and `MATH500`.
*   **Consistent Sub-Optimality:** All algorithms exhibit a large **Sampling Efficiency Gap ($\Delta SE$)**, defined as the difference between the base model's `pass@256` and the RL model's `pass@1`.
*   The $\Delta SE$ values range from **42.6** (RLOO) to **43.9** (GRPO) on the in-domain test set, remaining consistently above 40 points. This indicates that *no* current algorithm effectively converts the base model's latent potential into immediate performance.
*   While `DAPO` achieves slightly higher `pass@1`, it requires **3–6× more samples** per batch and suffers a significant drop in `pass@256`, reinforcing the efficiency-capacity trade-off.

**Training Dynamics and Hyperparameters**
*   **Training Steps:** **Figure 1 (Right)** and **Table 4** show that as training progresses (from step 150 to 450), `pass@1` improves (e.g., from 26.1 to 42.5 on `Omni-MATH-Train`), but `pass@256` *decreases* (from 66.3 to 64.3). This confirms that prolonged RL training actively narrows the reasoning boundary.
*   **Rollout Count ($n$):** Increasing the number of rollouts per prompt from 8 to 32 (**Figure 16**) yields a slight improvement in `pass@k` at higher $k$, but the RLVR model still fails to surpass the base model's coverage.
*   **KL Divergence:** Adding a KL penalty (**Figure 16**) preserves `pass@1` but drastically reduces `pass@128`, suggesting that constraining the policy further accelerates the loss of diverse reasoning paths.
*   **Entropy Matching:** In **Figure 18**, the authors increase the temperature of the RLVR model to match the output entropy of the base model. Even with matched diversity, the RLVR model underperforms the base model at high $k$, proving that reduced entropy is a symptom, not the sole cause, of the capacity reduction.

**Frontier Model Validation**
To address concerns about model scale, the study evaluates `Magistral-Medium` (a near-frontier model trained with pure RL) against `Mistral-Medium-3` (**Figure 9**).
*   The results mirror those of smaller models: `Magistral` gains ~7–8 points on `pass@1` for `AIME24/25` but offers negligible improvement at $k=1024$. This confirms that the "Prior Bound" limitation persists even at the frontier of current AI capabilities.

### 5.5 Assessment of Experimental Validity

The experiments convincingly support the paper's central claims through a combination of broad empirical coverage and deep mechanistic analysis.
*   **Strengths:** The use of `pass@k` at large $k$ is a methodological breakthrough that exposes limitations hidden by standard `pass@1` metrics. The consistency of results across math, code, and vision, and across multiple model families, rules out domain-specific or architecture-specific anomalies. The perplexity analysis provides a rigorous mathematical proof that RLVR paths are not novel.
*   **Limitations:** The study is constrained by the availability of open-weight base models. For proprietary models like `OpenAI-o1` or `Claude-3.5`, the base model is inaccessible, preventing a direct `pass@k` comparison. However, the results on `Magistral-Medium` suggest the trend likely holds. Additionally, the "guessing" check, while thorough for a subset of problems, relies on manual inspection which does not scale to the entire dataset, though the code results (immune to guessing) corroborate the math findings.

In summary, the experimental analysis demonstrates that current RLVR methods act as a **filter** that sharpens the base model's distribution at the cost of diversity. While this yields immediate performance gains (`pass@1`), it fundamentally fails to expand the model's reasoning horizon, leaving the vast potential of the base model's "long tail" untapped.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this study provides a rigorous stress-test of current RLVR paradigms, its conclusions are bounded by specific experimental constraints, methodological assumptions, and the rapidly evolving nature of the field. A critical understanding of these limitations is essential for correctly interpreting the "Prior Bound" hypothesis and avoiding over-generalization.

### 6.1 The "Open-Weight" Constraint and Proprietary Black Boxes

The most significant limitation of this work is its reliance on **publicly available base models**. To rigorously test whether RLVR expands reasoning capacity, one must compare an RL-trained model against its *exact* pretrained base counterpart.
*   **Inaccessibility of Frontier Models:** For state-of-the-art proprietary models like **OpenAI-o1** or **Claude-3.5**, the base pretrained weights are not public. Consequently, the authors cannot compute the `pass@k` curve for the base version of o1 to see if the RL-trained o1 surpasses it at high $k$.
*   **Proxy Limitations:** The study attempts to mitigate this by evaluating **Magistral-Medium** (a near-frontier pure-RL model) against **Mistral-Medium-3** (**Figure 9**). While the results mirror those of smaller models (gains at $k=1$, stagnation at $k=1024$), Magistral is not a perfect proxy for o1. It remains an open question whether massive-scale proprietary RL training pipelines, potentially utilizing distinct data curation or exploration strategies not present in open-source efforts, might overcome the "Prior Bound" observed here.
*   **Implication:** The conclusion that "RLVR rarely elicits novel reasoning" is strictly proven only for the *current generation of open-source RLVR methods*. It is a strong empirical finding but not a theoretical impossibility for future, potentially proprietary, systems.

### 6.2 Methodological Assumptions in `pass@k` and Verification

The core metric of the paper, `pass@k`, relies on assumptions that, while carefully managed, introduce potential noise into the boundary estimation.
*   **The "Guessing" Problem in Mathematics:** In code generation, a solution passing all unit tests is a robust signal of correctness. In mathematics, however, a model can generate a flawed Chain-of-Thought (CoT) that accidentally arrives at the correct numerical answer.
    *   **Mitigation:** The authors address this by manually inspecting CoTs for the hardest problems (accuracy $&lt;5\%$) on GSM8K and AIME24 (**Section 3.1**, **Section C.2**). They found that valid CoTs accounted for the vast majority of correct answers (e.g., 24 out of 25 for the base model on GSM8K hard problems).
    *   **Residual Risk:** This manual verification was performed on a *subset* of problems due to resource constraints. It is possible that at extremely high $k$ (e.g., $k > 1024$), the "long tail" of the base model's distribution includes a non-negligible fraction of lucky guesses, which could artificially inflate the base model's apparent reasoning boundary. However, the consistency of results with code benchmarks (where guessing is negligible) suggests this effect is likely minor.
*   **Sampling Feasibility:** The study assumes that if a solution exists in the model's distribution, it can be found within a computationally feasible number of samples ($k \le 1024$).
    *   **Theoretical Edge Case:** Mathematically, as $k \to \infty$, even uniform random sampling over the token vocabulary would eventually produce a correct solution. The paper defines the "reasoning boundary" pragmatically within realistic compute budgets. If an RLVR model shifts a correct path's probability from $10^{-10}$ to $10^{-2}$, it effectively "creates" the capability for any practical user, even if the path technically existed in the base prior. The paper acknowledges this but argues that for *fundamental* capacity expansion, the path should not rely on such extreme rarity in the base model.

### 6.3 Scope of Tasks and Reasoning Types

The evaluation focuses on domains with **verifiable, binary rewards** (Math, Code, Visual Math). This design choice is necessary for RLVR but limits the generalizability of the findings to other reasoning contexts.
*   **Single-Turn vs. Multi-Turn:** The experiments evaluate **single-turn** reasoning (prompt $\to$ response). The paper explicitly notes in **Section 5** that current RLVR lacks the ability to engage in multi-turn agent interactions, tool use, or iterative refinement based on environmental feedback.
    *   **Missing Scenario:** It is possible that RLVR could discover novel reasoning strategies in a **multi-turn agentic setting** where the model can actively query tools, correct its own errors, or explore a solution space dynamically. The "Prior Bound" observed here might be a limitation of the *single-turn* formulation rather than RLVR itself.
*   **Subjective Domains:** The study does not address domains without ground-truth verifiers, such as creative writing, strategic planning in open-ended environments, or scientific hypothesis generation. In these areas, where rewards are dense but noisy (provided by a reward model rather than a verifier), the dynamics of exploration vs. exploitation might differ significantly.

### 6.4 Computational and Scalability Constraints

The experimental design imposes hard limits on the scale of training and evaluation, which affects the robustness of the ablation studies.
*   **Rollout Count ($n$):** In **Section 4.4** and **Figure 16**, the authors investigate increasing the number of rollouts per prompt from 8 to 32. They observe a slight improvement in `pass@k` but note that they could only train for **220 steps** (vs. 450 for the baseline) due to **resource constraints**.
    *   **Open Question:** It remains unknown whether scaling the rollout count further (e.g., $n=128$) combined with full convergence training would allow RLVR to better preserve the base model's diversity. The current result suggests a trend, but the incomplete training leaves a gap in confirming whether "brute-force" exploration during training can break the efficiency-capacity trade-off.
*   **Model Size Ceiling:** While the study includes models up to 32B parameters and evaluates a frontier-tier API model (Magistral), it does not cover the largest dense models (e.g., 405B+) due to the prohibitive cost of generating $1024$ samples per problem for thousands of problems. The "Prior Bound" hypothesis assumes that the relationship between base and RLVR scales linearly, but emergent behaviors at extreme scales could theoretically alter this dynamic.

### 6.5 The "Moving Target" of RL Research

Finally, the paper acknowledges that **RL for LLMs is a rapidly evolving field**. The algorithms tested (PPO, GRPO, DAPO, etc.) represent the *current* state-of-the-art but may not represent the *final* solution.
*   **Algorithmic Immaturity:** The large **Sampling Efficiency Gap ($\Delta SE > 40$)** observed across all algorithms (**Figure 8**) indicates that current methods are sub-optimal. The failure of current RLVR to expand capacity may be a failure of *specific algorithms* (which lack effective exploration mechanisms in vast action spaces) rather than a fundamental flaw in the RL paradigm itself.
*   **Future Paradigms:** The authors explicitly suggest in **Section 5** that future breakthroughs may require **process rewards** (guiding intermediate steps), **curriculum learning**, or **high-level abstraction exploration** (e.g., AlphaEvolve). This study serves as a baseline for *today's* methods; it does not preclude the possibility that a fundamentally different RL architecture could overcome the limitations identified here.

In summary, while the evidence strongly supports the claim that *current* RLVR methods act as efficiency filters bounded by the base prior, these conclusions are contingent on the availability of open weights, the feasibility of sampling, and the maturity of existing RL algorithms. The paper successfully identifies a critical bottleneck in the current trajectory of LLM post-training, but it leaves the door open for future innovations to potentially transcend these bounds.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This study fundamentally recalibrates the community's understanding of Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR). By demonstrating that current RLVR methods act as **probability shapers** rather than **capability expanders**, the paper shifts the narrative from "RL enables self-improvement" to "RL optimizes existing priors." This distinction has profound consequences for how researchers design training pipelines, how practitioners deploy models, and where the field should direct its next wave of innovation.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Research Landscape: From Scaling RL to Expanding Priors

The most immediate impact of this work is the challenge it poses to the **"Scaling RL" hypothesis**. Previously, the prevailing belief was that increasing compute for RL training (more steps, larger batches, better algorithms) would continuously unlock new reasoning abilities, akin to how AlphaGo Zero surpassed human knowledge through self-play. This paper provides empirical evidence that for LLMs, **scaling RL alone hits a hard ceiling defined by the base model's pre-training distribution**.

*   **The End of "Zero-RL" as a Silver Bullet:** The finding that RLVR paths pre-exist in the base model (Section 4.2) suggests that "Zero-RL" approaches (training directly on base models without supervised fine-tuning) are inherently limited. If the base model assigns near-zero probability to a correct reasoning path, outcome-based RL signals cannot guide the model to discover it. This implies that future gains in reasoning will not come from better RL optimizers (e.g., tweaking PPO vs. GRPO) but from **better base priors**.
*   **Re-evaluating Distillation:** The study elevates **distillation** from a secondary technique to a primary engine for capability growth. Since distillation was shown to genuinely expand the reasoning boundary (Figure 7), while RLVR only improves efficiency, the optimal post-training pipeline may invert current practices:
    1.  **Phase 1 (Expansion):** Use distillation from a stronger teacher (or synthetic data generation from frontier models) to import *new* reasoning patterns into the student.
    2.  **Phase 2 (Optimization):** Apply RLVR *after* distillation to sharpen the distribution, reduce sampling variance, and improve `pass@1` reliability.
    *   *Current Practice:* Often applies RL directly to base models or uses weak distillation.
    *   *Recommended Practice:* Prioritize high-quality trajectory data (distillation) to expand the solution space, then use RL to consolidate those gains.

### 7.2 Strategic Roadmap: Four Pillars for Next-Generation RLVR

To break the "Prior Bound" identified in this work, future research must move beyond simple outcome-based reinforcement. The authors propose four concrete directions to enable genuine exploration in the vast language action space:

#### 1. High-Level Abstraction Exploration
Current RLVR operates at the **token level**, attempting to explore a space of $V^T$ (vocabulary size to the power of sequence length). This is computationally intractable for discovering novel strategies.
*   **Proposed Shift:** Move exploration to a **program-level or sketch-level abstraction**. Instead of sampling tokens, agents could sample high-level reasoning structures (e.g., "decompose problem," "write python script," "verify intermediate step") and then fill in the details.
*   **Reference:** Approaches like **AlphaEvolve** (Novikov et al., 2025), which evolve code structures rather than tokens, exemplify this direction. This reduces the effective action space, making it feasible to discover out-of-prior reasoning patterns.

#### 2. Process Reward Models (PRMs) over Outcome Rewards
The binary nature of verifiable rewards ($0$ or $1$ based on the final answer) provides a sparse signal that fails to guide exploration when the base model rarely succeeds.
*   **Proposed Shift:** Integrate **Process Reward Models** that provide dense, fine-grained feedback on intermediate reasoning steps.
*   **Mechanism:** If a model takes a correct logical step but fails at the end, a PRM assigns partial credit. This keeps promising but incomplete reasoning paths alive during training, allowing the model to "learn from near misses" rather than discarding them as failures. This effectively smooths the reward landscape, facilitating gradient-based exploration into regions the base model rarely visits.

#### 3. Curriculum Learning and Data Scaling
The "hollowing out" effect (Section 5.2) suggests that RLVR struggles when the success rate is too low (sparse rewards).
*   **Proposed Shift:** Implement **hierarchical curricula** that start with sub-problems where the base model has non-zero success rates.
*   **Mechanism:** By mastering easier variants of a problem first, the model acquires meta-skills (e.g., specific algebraic manipulations or coding patterns) that increase the probability of solving harder parent tasks. This creates a "scaffold" for exploration, gradually lifting the model from zero-shot failure to competence. This requires a deliberate, large-scale data pipeline that maps dependencies between easy and hard problems, rather than static datasets.

#### 4. Multi-Turn Agentic RL
Current RLVR is confined to **single-turn** generation. Real-world reasoning (e.g., IMO proofs, scientific discovery) often involves iterative refinement, tool use, and error correction.
*   **Proposed Shift:** Transition to **Multi-Turn Agentic RL** where the "action" includes using search engines, executing code, or critiquing previous outputs.
*   **Mechanism:** In this paradigm, the model generates *experience* rather than just text. By interacting with an environment (e.g., a Python interpreter or a theorem prover), the model can receive feedback that is impossible to derive from static text priors. This aligns with the "Era of Experience" (Silver & Sutton, 2025), where agents learn novel strategies through interaction rather than mere distributional shaping.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Deployment Guidelines

For practitioners and engineers deploying LLMs today, this study offers clear guidance on when and how to use RLVR versus alternative methods.

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Maximizing `pass@1` Accuracy** | **RLVR** | If the application requires a single, reliable answer (e.g., chatbots, coding assistants), RLVR is superior. It concentrates probability mass on correct paths, significantly boosting greedy decoding performance. |
| **Solving Novel/Hard Problems** | **Distillation** | If the goal is to solve problems the base model currently cannot (e.g., new math Olympiad types), distillation from a stronger teacher is the only proven method to expand the reasoning boundary. |
| **Resource-Constrained Inference** | **RLVR** | RLVR models achieve higher accuracy with fewer samples. This reduces inference costs (fewer tokens generated per query) compared to running a base model with high $k$ sampling. |
| **High-Stakes Reasoning (e.g., Science)** | **Base Model + High $k$** | Surprisingly, for offline batch processing where latency is less critical, sampling many times from a **base model** may yield a higher *total* success rate than an RLVR model, as the base model retains a wider diversity of solution paths. |
| **Domain Adaptation** | **Hybrid (Distill $\to$ RL)** | For adapting to a new domain (e.g., legal reasoning), first distill from a domain expert model to import knowledge, then apply RLVR to optimize reliability. Avoid pure RLVR on base models for domains where the prior is weak. |

### 7.4 Integration Guidance: A New Post-Training Paradigm

Based on these findings, we propose a revised standard workflow for developing reasoning-centric LLMs:

1.  **Baseline Assessment:** Before applying RL, evaluate the base model's `pass@k` curve. If `pass@k` at large $k$ is near zero, **do not apply RLVR**. The base prior is insufficient, and RL will fail to converge or will overfit to noise.
2.  **Prior Expansion (Distillation):** If the base model lacks capability, curate a dataset of high-quality reasoning traces (CoT) from a stronger teacher model. Fine-tune the base model on this data to shift its distribution toward valid reasoning patterns.
3.  **Efficiency Optimization (RLVR):** Once the model has a non-trivial success rate (via distillation or pre-training), apply RLVR (e.g., GRPO/PPO) to maximize `pass@1`.
    *   *Constraint:* Monitor `pass@k` at high $k$ during RL training. If coverage drops significantly while `pass@1` rises, consider early stopping or reducing the KL penalty to preserve diversity.
4.  **Inference Strategy:** For critical applications, do not rely solely on the RLVR model's greedy output. Consider **hybrid inference**: use the RLVR model for the first few samples (high efficiency) and fall back to the base model (or a higher temperature setting) for additional samples if the initial attempts fail, leveraging the base model's broader coverage.

### 7.5 Conclusion: A Call for Genuine Exploration

This paper serves as a critical checkpoint for the field. It confirms that while RLVR is a powerful tool for **refining** intelligence, it is not yet a mechanism for **creating** it. The "Prior Bound" is not a law of physics but a limitation of current algorithms that rely on outcome rewards in a vast, discrete action space.

Unlocking the next tier of AI reasoning requires moving beyond "sampling what we already know" to "exploring what we don't." Whether through high-level abstraction, process rewards, or agentic interaction, the path forward demands methods that can genuinely traverse the space of logical possibilities, rather than merely pruning the branches of the existing tree. The era of simple RLVR optimization is maturing; the era of exploratory, agentic reasoning has just begun.
