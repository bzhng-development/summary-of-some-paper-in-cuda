## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the lack of systematic investigation into multimodal positional encoding for Vision-Language Models (VLMs) by proposing Multi-Head RoPE (MHRoPE) and MRoPE-Interleave (MRoPE-I), two plug-and-play variants that optimize position design and frequency allocation. Built on the Qwen2.5 7B backbone, these methods consistently outperform existing approaches across 20+ benchmarks, achieving significant gains such as +5.28% on ChartQA and +3.27% on RefCOCOval compared to vanilla RoPE. By establishing guidelines for positional coherence and full frequency utilization, the work resolves critical failure modes like modalities confusion and impaired transfer learning, enabling robust general and fine-grained multimodal understanding without architectural changes.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contributions of this work, we must first establish why positional encoding is a critical bottleneck in Vision-Language Models (VLMs) and why existing solutions remain insufficient. This section breaks down the specific technical gap, the real-world implications of failing to address it, the landscape of prior approaches, and how this paper positions itself to resolve these issues systematically.

### The Specific Problem: Multimodal Positional Encoding is Under-Investigated
Large Language Models (LLMs) rely on positional encodings to inform the self-attention mechanism about the order of tokens. Without this, the model is "permutation-invariant," meaning it treats the sequence "The cat sat" identically to "sat cat The." While Rotary Positional Embedding (`RoPE`) has become the standard for text-only LLMs (e.g., Llama, Qwen), extending this to VLMs introduces significant complexity.

The core problem addressed in this paper is the **lack of systematic investigation into multimodal positional encoding** (Abstract). VLMs must handle heterogeneous inputs:
*   **1D Text:** Sequential tokens.
*   **2D/3D Visual Inputs:** Images (height, width) and Videos (time, height, width).

Current research has not established a unified standard for how to encode these diverse structures. Instead, the field suffers from a **fragmented landscape** where methods are highly specialized—some optimized exclusively for images, others for video, and others for generation (Introduction). This fragmentation means there is no robust, versatile strategy that supports core unified capabilities like simultaneous image/video understanding and fine-grained visual grounding.

### Why This Problem Matters
The design of positional encoding is not merely a theoretical detail; it directly dictates the model's capability in critical downstream tasks. The paper highlights three key areas where poor encoding design leads to tangible failures:

*   **Spatial Reasoning and Visual Grounding:** If the positional encoding discards the native geometry of an image (e.g., by flattening it into a 1D sequence), the model loses the ability to understand spatial relationships. This leads to significant degradation in tasks requiring visual grounding, such as identifying specific objects based on coordinates (Section 2.2.1).
*   **Long-Range Video Modeling:** Video understanding requires tracking objects over time. If the temporal axis is encoded using only high-frequency components, the model suffers from rapid attention decay, making it impossible to connect events separated by long durations (Section 2.3.2).
*   **Transfer Learning from Pre-trained LLMs:** Most VLMs are adapted from pre-trained LLMs. If the positional encoding for text tokens in the VLM differs from the base LLM, it compromises the **preservation of textual priors**. This breaks compatibility and hinders the effective transfer of knowledge, such as language reasoning capabilities, from the base model (Section 2.4).

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
To motivate their solution, the authors categorize existing methods into two main design families: **1D Sequential** and **Multi-Dimensional**. Each suffers from specific mechanistic flaws, summarized in Table 1 and visualized in Figure 1.

#### 1. 1D Sequential Designs (e.g., Vanilla RoPE, V2PE)
These methods treat all inputs—text and vision—as a single flattened sequence.
*   **Mechanism:** Position indices are assigned incrementally ($m_i = m_{i-1} + s_{mod}$). For Vanilla RoPE, the step size $s=1$ for all modalities.
*   **Shortcoming:** This approach **discards the native visual geometry**. An image is inherently 2D, but flattening it forces the model to learn spatial relationships purely from sequence distance, which is inefficient. Additionally, position indices can grow exceedingly large in long sequences (e.g., long videos), negatively affecting extrapolation performance (Section 2.2.1, Figure 1a).
*   **Attempted Fix:** V2PE introduces dynamic position scaling for visual tokens to slow index growth, but it still ignores the 3D structure of visual content (Section 2.2.1).

#### 2. Multi-Dimensional Designs (e.g., MRoPE, VideoRoPE, CircleRoPE)
These methods extend `RoPE` to multiple axes (time $t$, height $h$, width $w$) by partitioning embedding channels.
*   **MRoPE (Qwen2-VL):** Uses a tuple $m_i = (m^t_i, m^h_i, m^w_i)$.
    *   **Flaw:** It allocates temporal information entirely to **high-frequency channels**. This bias harms long-range video modeling because high frequencies decay quickly over distance (Introduction, Section 2.3.2, Figure 3).
    *   **Flaw:** It exhibits a visual **"attention sink,"** where attention concentrates disproportionately on the top-left corner of images, analogous to the start-token bias in LLMs (Section 2.2.3, Figure 2).
*   **VideoRoPE / HoPE:** Attempt to fix MRoPE's temporal bias by using a "diagonal layout."
    *   **Flaw:** This introduces **positional ambiguity**. The spatial coordinates of visual tokens can overlap with the index range of generated text tokens. The paper identifies this as **"modalities confusion in generation,"** which manifests as endless text repetition (e.g., "1111...") because the model confuses its own text output with visual input (Section 2.2.2, Figure 1c, Section 3.2).
*   **CircleRoPE:** Arranges image tokens in a circular layout orthogonal to text.
    *   **Flaw:** It lacks a temporal axis, collapsing all video frames onto a single ring, which introduces severe temporal ambiguity. It also creates a large interval between modalities, impeding cross-modal interaction (Section 2.2.2, Figure 1d).
*   **IL-RoPE / Omni-RoPE:** Modify text encoding to aid editing.
    *   **Flaw:** They reset spatial dimensions for text tokens to zero, which **breaks compatibility with text-only RoPE**. This prevents effective knowledge transfer from the base LLM (Section 2.4, Figure 1e).

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself not merely as another proposal of a new encoding variant, but as a **systematic decomposition and analysis** of the multimodal `RoPE` design space. Unlike prior work that focuses on specialized fixes (e.g., only for video or only for generation), this work aims to develop a **holistic positional encoding strategy** (Introduction).

The authors distinguish their approach through three key strategic choices:
1.  **Systematic Analysis:** Instead of proposing a method first, they decompose `RoPE` into two core components: **position design** (how coordinates are assigned) and **frequency allocation** (how rotary frequencies are distributed across dimensions). This allows them to isolate failure modes like "modalities confusion" or "restricted frequency allocation" (Abstract, Section 1).
2.  **Design Guidelines:** Based on their analysis, they distill three guidelines: **positional coherence** (unambiguous coordinates), **full frequency utilization** (all axes access the full frequency spectrum), and **preservation of textual priors** (text RoPE must match the base LLM) (Abstract, Section 1).
3.  **Plug-and-Play Solutions:** They propose **Multi-Head RoPE (MHRoPE)** and **MRoPE-Interleave (MRoPE-I)**. These are designed to be simple variants that require **no architectural changes** to the underlying model. They build upon the MRoPE foundation but correct its frequency allocation and position design flaws (e.g., by introducing `spatial-reset` to mitigate attention sinks) without sacrificing compatibility with pre-trained LLMs (Section 2.5, Table 1).

By addressing the root causes of failure in prior methods—specifically the trade-off between structural preservation and frequency resolution—this work seeks to enable robust performance across diverse benchmarks, from document understanding to long-video reasoning, within a single unified framework.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
This system is a modified Vision-Language Model (VLM) that upgrades how the model understands the order and geometry of text and images using a specialized mathematical rotation technique. It solves the problem of conflicting spatial and temporal signals by assigning clear 3D coordinates to visual tokens and ensuring every part of the model sees the full range of positional details without breaking the pre-trained language knowledge.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The architecture builds upon a standard VLM pipeline but inserts two critical modification points before the self-attention mechanism. First, the **Position ID Generator** assigns a 3-dimensional coordinate tuple $(t, h, w)$ to every token, applying a `spatial-reset` mechanism to ensure each image or video clip starts with a clean spatial origin. Second, the **Frequency Allocator** distributes the rotational frequencies across either attention heads (in `MHRoPE`) or interleaved channels (in `MRoPE-I`), ensuring no single dimension monopolizes the high or low frequencies. These modified positional encodings are then applied to the Query and Key vectors via rotary rotation before they enter the standard self-attention calculation, allowing the model to compute attention scores based on precise relative 3D distances rather than flattened sequence indices.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Position Design Mechanics:** We first explain how the 3D coordinate tuples are constructed and how `spatial-reset` decouples motion from position, as this is the foundation for all subsequent calculations.
*   **Frequency Allocation Strategies:** We then detail the two proposed methods (`MHRoPE` and `MRoPE-I`) for distributing frequency spectra, which resolves the information bottleneck found in prior chunked designs.
*   **Text Compatibility Preservation:** We clarify how the system maintains identical encoding for text tokens to ensure seamless knowledge transfer from the base LLM.
*   **Training and Configuration:** We conclude with the specific hyperparameters and training setup used to validate these designs, ensuring reproducibility of the reported gains.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown
This paper is an analysis-driven design study that proposes two plug-and-play variants of Rotary Positional Embedding (RoPE) optimized for multimodal inputs. The core idea is to decompose positional encoding into independent position design and frequency allocation components, optimizing each to satisfy three guidelines: positional coherence, full frequency utilization, and preservation of textual priors.

**System Pipeline and Data Flow**
The processing pipeline begins when raw text and visual inputs are tokenized into a unified sequence. First, the **Position ID Generator** assigns a multi-dimensional position identifier $m_i$ to each token $i$, distinguishing between temporal ($t$), vertical ($h$), and horizontal ($w$) axes for visual content while maintaining standard 1D indices for text. Second, the **Frequency Allocator** maps the rotary frequencies $\theta_i$ to the model's embedding dimensions or attention heads based on the chosen variant (`MHRoPE` or `MRoPE-I`). Third, the **RoPE Rotation Module** applies these frequencies to the Query ($q$) and Key ($k$) vectors using a rotational matrix $R_m$, transforming them into $R_m q$ and $R_m k$. Finally, the self-attention mechanism computes scores using these rotated vectors, where the dot product inherently captures the relative positional distance without requiring explicit additive embeddings.

**Position Design: 3D Coordinates and Spatial-Reset**
The foundation of this approach is a multi-dimensional position design that preserves the native geometry of visual content. Unlike 1D sequential designs that flatten images into a line, this system represents a token's position as a tuple $m_i = (m^t_i, m^h_i, m^w_i)$, corresponding to temporal, vertical, and horizontal axes respectively. A critical innovation here is the `spatial-reset` mechanism, which resets the spatial coordinates $(h, w)$ for each distinct visual content block rather than allowing them to accumulate continuously across the entire sequence. In standard MRoPE, the temporal position of the next token is calculated by jumping past the maximum coordinate of the current block, defined as $m^t_{\text{next}} = \max(m^t_{\text{prev}}, m^h_{\text{prev}}, m^w_{\text{prev}}) + 1$ (Section 2.2.2). However, without `spatial-reset`, the absolute positions for an object moving across frames become entangled. For example, an object at $(h_1, w_1)$ at time $t_1$ and $(h_2, w_2)$ at time $t_2$ would have relative positions where spatial and temporal differences are mixed:
$$
m_{\text{rel}} = (t_2 - t_1, (t_2 - t_1) + (h_2 - h_1), (t_2 - t_1) + (w_2 - w_1))
$$
This entanglement, shown in Equation 3 of Section 2.2.3, makes it difficult for the model to distinguish pure motion from static spatial layout. The proposed `spatial-reset` decouples these dimensions by defining positions as $m_1 = (t_1, h_1, w_1)$ and $m_2 = (t_2, h_2, w_2)$, yielding a clean relative vector:
$$
m_{\text{rel}} = (t_2 - t_1, h_2 - h_1, w_2 - w_1)
$$
This disentangled representation, shown in Equation 4, provides a cleaner inductive bias for learning motion. Furthermore, `spatial-reset` aligns the visual content with the LLM's inherent bias for small position IDs (often called an "attention sink"), ensuring the model focuses effectively on the beginning of visual sequences (Section 2.2.3, Figure 2).

**Frequency Allocation: Solving the Spectrum Bottleneck**
The second major component addresses how rotary frequencies are distributed across the model's capacity. In standard RoPE, frequencies $\theta_i$ follow a geometric sequence $\theta_i = \text{base}^{-2i/d}$, creating a spectrum from high frequencies (short-range dependencies) to low frequencies (long-range dependencies). Prior multimodal methods like MRoPE partitioned the feature dimensions $d$ into contiguous chunks for $t, h, w$, forcing the temporal axis to rely solely on high-frequency channels. This design choice causes rapid attention decay over time, harming long-video understanding (Section 2.3.2, Figure 3). The proposed methods ensure every positional axis accesses the **full frequency spectrum**.

*   **Multi-Head RoPE (MHRoPE):** This variant partitions the positional encoding task among different attention heads rather than splitting channels within a single head. By dedicating distinct heads to different positional axes, each axis retains access to the full frequency spectrum available within its assigned heads. This approach avoids the loss of frequency resolution inherent to channel-splitting methods and offers better scalability as the number of positional axes grows (Section 2.3.3). For Group Query Attention (GQA), the partition is applied on KV heads and repeated on corresponding query heads (Footnote 1, Section 2.3.3).
*   **MRoPE-Interleave (MRoPE-I):** This variant employs a fine-grained, round-robin distribution of channels to the temporal, vertical, and horizontal axes. Instead of contiguous blocks, channels are interleaved so that each axis is encoded using frequencies from across the entire spectrum (high to low). This design enables robust multi-scale modeling for each axis and maintains compatibility with extrapolation algorithms like NTK-aware and YaRN, which function by rescaling the frequency spectrum (Section 2.3.3, Appendix D.3). Empirical ablation in Appendix D.5.2 identifies the balanced allocation ratio of $t:h:w = 24:20:20$ as optimal, noting that increasing the temporal proportion reduces high-frequency capacity for spatial dimensions and degrades grounding ability.

**Compatibility with Text-Only RoPE**
A crucial design constraint is ensuring the VLM remains compatible with the pre-trained LLM's positional encoding to preserve textual priors. The system maintains strict identity between the text token encoding in the VLM and the vanilla RoPE used in the base LLM. Experiments showed that deviating from this, such as resetting spatial dimensions for text tokens to zero (as in IL-RoPE) or scaling the rotary base for spatial axes, led to significant performance drops (Section 2.4, Table 3). Therefore, the rotary base is fixed at $1,000,000$ for all axes, and text tokens follow the standard 1D RoPE formulation without modification. This ensures that the linguistic reasoning capabilities learned during pre-training are not disrupted by the multimodal adaptation (Section 2.4).

**Training Configuration and Hyperparameters**
The experimental validation relies on a controlled setup to isolate the effects of the positional encoding changes. All models use the QwenViT and connector from Qwen2.5VL, initializing the VLM backbone with the Qwen2.5 7B LLM (Section 3.1). During training, the ViT is frozen to fix visual representations, while the connector and LLM backbone are unfrozen. The training process adopts a batch size of 128 and a cosine-decayed learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-5}$. The training context length is set to 32K tokens, and the rotary base is set to $1,000,000$. Each experiment consumes approximately 512 Nvidia A100 GPU hours. The dataset comprises approximately 2M high-quality supervised fine-tuning (SFT) samples covering image captioning, OCR, visual reasoning, grounding, document comprehension, and long video understanding. This rigorous standardization ensures that the reported performance gains, such as the +5.28% improvement on ChartQA (Table 2), are attributable to the positional encoding design rather than data or scale differences.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper's contribution extends beyond proposing two new variants (`MHRoPE` and `MRoPE-I`); it establishes a diagnostic framework for understanding *why* multimodal positional encodings fail. The following insights distill the fundamental shifts in design philosophy required to build robust Vision-Language Models (VLMs), distinguishing between incremental tweaks and structural innovations.

### 4.1 Strategic Alignment with Attention Sinks
Prior work often treats the "attention sink" phenomenon—where models disproportionately attend to initial tokens—as a bug to be mitigated. This paper innovates by treating it as a feature to be aligned.

*   **Mechanism of Alignment:** As detailed in the **Technical Approach**, the `spatial-reset` mechanism ensures that every new image or video clip begins with spatial coordinates near zero. This is not merely for coordinate normalization; it strategically places visual tokens into the position range where the pre-trained LLM naturally focuses its attention.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The impact of this alignment is quantifiable. In ablation studies measuring average attention scores on visual content, models equipped with `spatial-reset` show significantly higher attention allocation in deeper layers. For instance, in `MRoPE-I`, attention on visual content in Layer 28 rises from **11.69%** without reset to **23.23%** with reset (Appendix D.5.1, Table 5).
*   **Significance:** This insight shifts the design goal from "preventing sinks" to "positioning content where sinks exist." It explains why simply preserving 3D structure (without reset) yields smaller gains than combining structure with reset (Table 3, **+3D structure** vs. **+3D + spatial-reset**).

### 4.2 Frequency Spectrum Democracy vs. Partitioning
A critical theoretical contribution is the identification of **frequency resolution loss** as a bottleneck in prior multi-dimensional designs.

*   **The Partitioning Flaw:** Standard `MRoPE` partitions the embedding dimension $d$ into contiguous chunks for time, height, and width. This forces each axis to operate on a restricted subset of the frequency spectrum $\theta_i$. For example, the temporal axis in `MRoPE` is confined to high-frequency channels, inducing rapid attention decay over time (Section 2.3.2, Figure 3).
*   **The Innovation:** Both `MHRoPE` and `MRoPE-I` enforce **full frequency utilization**.
    *   `MRoPE-I` achieves this via interleaved channel allocation, ensuring every axis accesses both high frequencies (for fine-grained detail) and low frequencies (for long-range dependency).
    *   `MHRoPE` achieves this via head-level partitioning, which preserves frequency resolution within each head.
*   **Performance Impact:** This design choice directly correlates with performance on tasks requiring specific frequency ranges. Long-video understanding (requiring low-frequency temporal encoding) improves on benchmarks like `LVBench` (+1.61% over vanilla RoPE), while visual grounding (requiring high-frequency spatial encoding) improves on `RefCOCOval` (+3.27%) (Table 2).
*   **Scalability Insight:** The paper notes that channel partitioning becomes untenable as positional axes grow (e.g., adding more dimensions). `MHRoPE`'s head-based approach is highlighted as a more scalable architecture for future models with complex positional needs (Section 2.3.3).

### 4.3 The Fragility of Textual Priors
While many works assume minor modifications to text encoding are harmless, this paper provides empirical evidence that **textual prior preservation** is a hard constraint for effective transfer learning.

*   **The Constraint:** The positional encoding for text tokens in the VLM must remain identical to the vanilla `RoPE` used in the base LLM. This includes maintaining the rotary base (set to **1,000,000** in experiments) and avoiding spatial resets for text tokens.
*   **Evidence of Failure:** Deviations lead to immediate performance collapses.
    *   **Text Spatial-Reset:** Resetting spatial dimensions for text tokens (as in `IL-RoPE`) causes performance to drop from **65.69** to **58.27** on Image benchmarks (Table 3).
    *   **Base Scaling:** Attempting to optimize the rotary base for spatial axes (e.g., reducing it to 10,000) results in consistent performance drops, indicating that the pre-trained LLM's frequency expectations are rigid (Section 3.3.1).
*   **Significance:** This insight warns against "over-optimizing" the text branch of a VLM. The cost of breaking compatibility with the pre-trained LLM's positional space outweighs the theoretical benefits of modality-specific tuning.

### 4.4 Diagnosis of "Modalities Confusion"
The paper identifies and diagnoses a specific, catastrophic failure mode termed **"modalities confusion in generation,"** which had been previously observed but not systematically explained.

*   **The Symptom:** Models generate endless, repetitive text (e.g., "1111...") during inference.
*   **The Root Cause:** This behavior is traced directly to **positional overlap** in designs like `VideoRoPE` or `HoPE` that use diagonal layouts. When the spatial coordinates of visual tokens overlap with the index range assigned to generated text tokens, the model confuses its own output with visual input (Section 3.3.1).
*   **Validation:** Ablation studies confirm this causality. Implementing a diagonal layout reproduces the failure, causing severe degradation on document-centric benchmarks like `DocVQA` (dropping from **82.94** to **60.13**) and `InfoVQA` (dropping from **58.85** to **37.42**) (Table 3).
*   **Significance:** This insight establishes **positional coherence** (unambiguous coordinates) as a non-negotiable requirement. It demonstrates that positional encoding errors do not just reduce accuracy; they can fundamentally break the generation loop by corrupting the model's distinction between input modalities and autoregressive output.

### 4.5 Unified Decay Profiles
A subtle but important theoretical advance is the normalization of attention decay rates across all positional axes.

*   **Prior Asymmetry:** In `MRoPE`, the height and width axes exhibit different long-range decay rates because they are assigned distinct, non-overlapping frequency ranges. This asymmetry impairs the model's ability to learn consistent spatial relationships (Section 2.3.2).
*   **Proposed Symmetry:** By ensuring full frequency utilization, `MHRoPE` and `MRoPE-I` yield a **unified decay profile** for time, height, and width (Figure 4b).
*   **Implication:** This uniformity simplifies the learning landscape. The model does not need to learn different "distance metrics" for temporal vs. spatial relationships, as the mathematical properties of the positional encoding remain consistent across all dimensions. This contributes to the method's robustness across diverse tasks without task-specific tuning.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their proposed methods. We will walk through the evaluation methodology, analyze the quantitative results with specific numbers, assess whether the experiments support the paper's claims, and examine the ablation studies that reveal why certain design choices succeed or fail. Understanding this experimental evidence is crucial for evaluating whether `MHRoPE` and `MRoPE-I` represent genuine improvements or merely incremental tweaks.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The authors employ a rigorous experimental design intended to isolate the effects of positional encoding changes while controlling for confounding variables. This section details the datasets, baselines, and training configuration.

**Model Architecture and Training Configuration**

All experiments use a consistent backbone to ensure fair comparison:
- **LLM Backbone:** Qwen2.5 7B, initialized with pre-trained weights
- **Visual Encoder:** QwenViT from Qwen2.5VL
- **Connector:** Same architecture as Qwen2.5VL
- **Training Strategy:** The ViT is frozen to fix visual representations, while the connector and LLM backbone are unfrozen. This isolates the effects of RoPE modifications while adhering to standard VLM adaptation paradigms (Section 3.1).

**Hyperparameters (Section 3.1):**
- **Batch Size:** 128
- **Learning Rate:** $1 \times 10^{-5}$ with cosine decay
- **Context Length:** 32K tokens during training
- **Rotary Base:** 1,000,000 (kept constant across all axes)
- **Compute:** Approximately 512 Nvidia A100 GPU hours per experiment
- **Training Data:** ~2M supervised fine-tuning (SFT) samples covering image captioning, OCR, visual reasoning, visual grounding, document comprehension, and long video understanding

Critically, **all experiments share identical training data, model architecture, and hyperparameters**, with the sole difference being the choice of multimodal RoPE. This controlled setup ensures that performance differences can be attributed to positional encoding design rather than data or scale variations.

**Evaluation Benchmarks (Section 3.1)**

The authors evaluate across more than 20 benchmarks spanning three task categories:

| Category | Benchmarks | What It Tests |
|----------|------------|---------------|
| **Image Understanding** | MMMU, MMBench, MMStar, OCRBench, AI2D, RealWorldQA, DocVQA, TextVQA, InfoVQA, ChartQA | General visual reasoning, OCR, document/chart comprehension |
| **Video Understanding** | MVBench, STAR, VideoMME, LVBench, MLVU, Charades-STA | Temporal reasoning, long-range video comprehension, action localization |
| **Visual Grounding** | RefCOCO series (val, testA, testB, +val, +testA, +testB, gval, gtest) | Fine-grained spatial localization, object referencing |

This diverse benchmark suite is designed to test the three core guidelines: positional coherence (grounding tasks), full frequency utilization (video tasks requiring long-range dependencies), and preservation of textual priors (document/OCR tasks requiring language reasoning).

**Baseline Methods (Table 1, Table 2)**

The paper compares against seven prior methods representing different design philosophies:
- **Vanilla RoPE** (Su et al., 2024): 1D sequential, no 3D structure
- **V2PE** (Ge et al., 2024): 1D sequential with dynamic position scaling
- **MRoPE** (Wang et al., 2024a): Multi-dimensional with chunked frequency allocation
- **VideoRoPE** (Wei et al., 2025): Diagonal layout, temporal axis in low frequencies
- **HoPE** (Li et al., 2025): Similar to VideoRoPE with hybrid design
- **CircleRoPE** (Wang et al., 2025): Circular layout for images
- **IL-RoPE** (Liao et al., 2025) / **Omni-RoPE** (Wu et al., 2025): Text spatial-reset variants

The proposed methods (`MHRoPE` and `MRoPE-I`) are compared directly against all baselines on identical benchmarks.

**MRoPE-I achieves the best overall balance**, with top performance on Image and Grounding tasks. **MHRoPE slightly edges out MRoPE-I on Video tasks** (52.58 vs 52.36), which aligns with the paper's note that MHRoPE's head-level partitioning may offer scalability advantages for complex temporal modeling (Appendix D.1).

### 5.3 Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

The experimental evidence strongly supports the paper's three core claims, though with some important nuances.

**Claim 1: Positional Coherence Prevents Modalities Confusion**

**Supported.** The ablation study in **Table 3** provides direct evidence:
- **Diagonal Layout** (VideoRoPE/HoPE style): DocVQA drops from 82.94 to **60.13** (-22.81 points), InfoVQA drops from 58.85 to **37.42** (-21.43 points)
- **Qualitative Failure:** The paper reports "repetitive, nonsensical text generation (e.g., '1111...')" when diagonal layout is applied, even at inference time only (Section 3.3.1)

This catastrophic degradation confirms that positional overlap between visual and text tokens causes the model to confuse its own generated text with visual input, validating the "modalities confusion" diagnosis.

**Claim 2: Full Frequency Utilization Improves Multi-Scale Modeling**

**Supported.** The frequency allocation ablation in **Table 4** shows:

| Allocation Type | Image | Video | Grounding | Overall |
|-----------------|-------|-------|-----------|---------|
| VideoRoPE-like | 65.33 | 52.11 | 72.50 | 63.31 |
| IL-RoPE-like | 65.26 | 51.15 | 72.80 | 63.07 |
| Multi-Head | 66.40 | 52.58 | 74.92 | 64.63 |
| **Interleave** | **66.65** | 52.36 | **75.85** | **64.95** |

Both full-spectrum methods (Multi-Head and Interleave) outperform partial-spectrum methods (VideoRoPE-like and IL-RoPE-like) by **~1.6-1.9 overall points**. This confirms that restricting frequency ranges for specific axes impairs performance.

**Claim 3: Preservation of Textual Priors is Critical**

**Strongly Supported.** The ablation study shows severe degradation when text RoPE compatibility is broken:
- **Text Spatial-Reset** (IL-RoPE/Omni-RoPE style): Image performance drops from 65.69 to **58.27** (-7.42 points)
- **Scaling Rotary Base** (reducing from 1,000,000 to 10,000 for spatial axes): Image performance drops to **60.15** (-5.54 points)

These results demonstrate that even well-intentioned deviations from the base LLM's RoPE formulation break compatibility and impair knowledge transfer (Section 3.3.1).

### 5.5 Robustness Checks and Failure Cases

**Long-Context Video Extrapolation (Appendix D.4, Figure 5)**

The authors test extrapolation to context lengths beyond the training window (32K → 64K, 128K, 256K):

- **Vanilla RoPE:** Shows sharp performance drop at 128K/256K due to "excessively fast-growing position IDs"
- **MHRoPE and MRoPE-I:** Maintain stable performance across all context lengths
- **VideoRoPE and HoPE:** Exhibit "slightly better extrapolation ability in long video scenario" due to low-frequency temporal allocation, but sacrifice image/grounding performance

This reveals an important **trade-off**: methods optimized for long-video extrapolation (VideoRoPE, HoPE) underperform on image and grounding tasks. MRoPE-I achieves the best balance, maintaining robust extrapolation while preserving multi-task performance.

**MHRoPE vs MRoPE-I Trade-offs (Appendix D.1)**

The authors explicitly compare their two proposed methods:

| Aspect | MHRoPE | MRoPE-I |
|--------|--------|---------|
| **Overall Performance** | Slightly lower | Slightly higher |
| **Video Performance** | 52.58 | 52.36 |
| **Grounding Performance** | 74.92 | 75.85 |
| **Implementation Complexity** | Higher (tensor parallelism issues) | Lower |
| **Scalability** | Better for many positional axes | Standard |
| **Extrapolation Compatibility** | Standard | Compatible with NTK-aware, YaRN |

The authors **recommend MRoPE-I over MHRoPE** for most use cases due to its "consistent (albeit slight) performance advantage and greater implementation simplicity" (Appendix D.1). However, MHRoPE's head-based design is noted as "potentially more scalable for future models that may need to accommodate a larger number of positional axes."

**Document-Centric Failure Cases**

The paper identifies specific failure modes for prior methods:

1. **VideoRoPE/HoPE on DocVQA:** Performance drops from 82.94 (Vanilla) to **60.13** (-22.81 points). The root cause is positional overlap causing modalities confusion.

2. **CircleRoPE on Video Tasks:** Collapses video frames onto a single ring, introducing "severe temporal ambiguity" (Section 2.2.2). VideoMME score: 57.70 vs MRoPE-I's 58.96.

3. **IL-RoPE/Omni-RoPE on Image Tasks:** Text spatial-reset breaks LLM compatibility, dropping Image performance from 65.69 to **58.27** (-7.42 points).

These failure cases are not merely performance degradations—they represent **fundamental capability losses** (e.g., endless text repetition, inability to distinguish modalities). This strengthens the paper's argument that positional encoding design is a critical, non-negotiable component of VLM architecture.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

**Strengths:**

1. **Controlled Experimental Design:** All methods trained with identical data, architecture, and hyperparameters. This isolates RoPE effects from confounding variables.

2. **Comprehensive Benchmark Suite:** 20+ benchmarks across three task categories test different aspects of the design guidelines (coherence, frequency utilization, textual priors).

3. **Mechanistic Ablation Studies:** The position design and frequency allocation ablations directly test the paper's theoretical claims, not just end-to-end performance.

4. **Failure Mode Diagnosis:** The paper doesn't just report numbers—it explains *why* methods fail (e.g., modalities confusion causing text repetition, attention sink misalignment reducing visual focus).

**Limitations:**

1. **Single Backbone:** All experiments use Qwen2.5 7B. Results may not generalize to other LLM architectures or scales (e.g., 72B models, non-Qwen backbones).

2. **Limited Extrapolation Analysis:** While Appendix D.4 tests context extrapolation, the analysis focuses primarily on LVBench. Broader extrapolation benchmarks would strengthen claims about long-context robustness.

3. **Compute-Intensive Validation:** Each experiment requires ~512 A100 GPU hours, limiting the number of ablation configurations tested. Some design choices (e.g., alternative frequency allocation ratios beyond those in Table 6) remain under-explored.

4. **No Generation Quality Metrics:** The paper focuses on understanding benchmarks. For generation tasks (e.g., image captioning, video description), qualitative evaluation or human preference scores would complement the quantitative results.

**Overall Assessment:**

The experimental evidence **convincingly supports the paper's core claims**. The ablation studies provide causal evidence linking design choices to performance outcomes, and the failure mode analysis demonstrates that positional encoding errors can cause catastrophic capability losses, not just accuracy degradation. The recommendation of MRoPE-I as the preferred method is well-justified by the balanced performance across all task categories and the implementation simplicity advantage over MHRoPE.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

No technical solution is without constraints. This section critically examines the assumptions underlying `MHRoPE` and `MRoPE-I`, identifies scenarios where the approach may underperform, and highlights open questions that remain after this work. Understanding these limitations is essential for practitioners deciding whether to adopt these methods and for researchers identifying future directions.

### 6.1 Core Assumptions and Their Implications

The proposed methods rely on several foundational assumptions that shape their applicability:

**Assumption 1: Text RoPE Must Remain Identical to Base LLM**

The paper treats **preservation of textual priors** as a non-negotiable constraint. All experiments maintain the vanilla RoPE formulation for text tokens, including the rotary base of **1,000,000** (Section 3.1). The ablation study in Table 3 shows that deviating from this—such as applying `spatial-reset` to text tokens or scaling the rotary base for spatial axes—causes severe performance drops (Image benchmarks: 65.69 → 58.27 for text spatial-reset).

*   **Implication:** This assumption limits the ability to optimize text encoding specifically for multimodal tasks. While the paper demonstrates this is necessary for knowledge transfer, it means the VLM cannot adapt its text positional encoding to better suit vision-language interactions. Future work may need to explore whether *gradual* adaptation of text RoPE during extended fine-tuning could recover some of this lost optimization potential.

**Assumption 2: 3D Structure Is Always Beneficial for Visual Content**

The position design assumes that preserving the native 3D structure (time, height, width) of visual content is universally advantageous. The ablation study shows **+3D structure** improves grounding performance (73.48 → 74.40) but provides minimal gains on overall image benchmarks (65.69 → 65.87) without `spatial-reset` (Table 3).

*   **Implication:** For tasks that do not require fine-grained spatial reasoning (e.g., image captioning, general VQA), the complexity of 3D position design may offer diminishing returns. The paper does not ablate by task type to determine when 3D structure is truly necessary versus when a simpler 1D design suffices.

**Assumption 3: Frozen ViT Is Sufficient for Isolating RoPE Effects**

All experiments freeze the QwenViT visual encoder to "isolate the effects of our proposed RoPE modifications" (Section 3.1). While this is methodologically sound for attribution, it assumes the visual representations are already optimal.

*   **Implication:** If the ViT itself benefits from joint training with the modified RoPE, the reported gains may underestimate the full potential of `MHRoPE` and `MRoPE-I`. Conversely, if the frozen ViT produces representations misaligned with the new positional encoding, the gains may be capped. The paper does not explore end-to-end training with an unfrozen ViT.

### 6.2 Scenarios and Edge Cases Not Addressed

Several important problem settings remain unexplored in this work:

**Extreme Long-Video Understanding**

While Appendix D.4 tests extrapolation up to 256K context length, the results reveal a notable weakness:

> "Overall, methods such as VideoRoPE and HoPE, which allocate most low-frequency channels to the temporal axis, exhibit slightly better extrapolation ability in long video scenario." (Appendix D.4)

*   **Evidence:** On LVBench at extended contexts, VideoRoPE and HoPE show marginally better stability than MRoPE-I, though MRoPE-I still outperforms Vanilla RoPE.
*   **Gap:** For applications requiring *extreme* long-video understanding (e.g., hour-long surveillance footage, full-length movie analysis), `MRoPE-I` may not be optimal. The balanced frequency allocation that benefits multi-task performance comes at a cost to specialized long-range temporal modeling.

**Multimodal Generation Tasks**

The evaluation focuses exclusively on **understanding benchmarks** (MMMU, ChartQA, RefCOCO, etc.). The paper does not report results on generation tasks such as:

*   Image captioning quality (beyond OCR-heavy benchmarks like DocVQA)
*   Video description fluency
*   Interleaved image-text generation

*   **Gap:** The "modalities confusion in generation" failure mode is diagnosed (Section 3.3.1), but the paper does not quantify how often this occurs in practice or provide generation-specific metrics (e.g., human preference scores, repetition rates). The claim that `spatial-reset` prevents endless text repetition is supported qualitatively but lacks systematic measurement.

**Alternative LLM Backbones and Scales**

All experiments use **Qwen2.5 7B** as the backbone (Section 3.1). The paper does not test:

*   Larger scales (e.g., 72B models) where attention patterns may differ
*   Alternative architectures (e.g., Llama, Mistral) with different RoPE implementations
*   Models using Group Query Attention (GQA) vs. standard multi-head attention

*   **Gap:** While Footnote 1 in Section 2.3.3 notes that MHRoPE partitions on KV heads for GQA, no ablation compares GQA vs. standard attention performance. The scalability claims for MHRoPE ("more scalable architecture for future models") remain theoretical without empirical validation at larger scales.

**Non-Rectangular Visual Inputs**

The position design assumes visual content can be represented as regular 3D tensors (time × height × width). The paper does not address:

*   Irregularly shaped images (e.g., panoramic, fisheye)
*   Point cloud or 3D mesh inputs
*   Graph-structured visual data

*   **Gap:** As VLMs expand to handle more diverse visual modalities, the 3D tuple position design may require extension. MHRoPE's head-based approach is noted as "more scalable" for additional axes (Section 2.3.3), but this remains untested.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

**Training Compute Requirements**

Each experiment consumes approximately **512 Nvidia A100 GPU hours** (Section 3.1). This high compute cost has practical implications:

*   **Limited Ablation Scope:** The paper acknowledges that some design choices remain "under-exploration" due to compute constraints. For example, Appendix D.5.2 tests only three frequency allocation ratios (24:20:20, 32:16:16, 48:8:8). Finer-grained search or task-specific ratios were not explored.
*   **Reproducibility Barrier:** The compute requirement may limit independent verification, especially for researchers without access to large GPU clusters.

**MHRoPE Implementation Complexity**

Appendix D.1 explicitly recommends `MRoPE-I` over `MHRoPE` for most use cases, citing implementation challenges:

> "From an engineering perspective, MRoPE-I is also simpler, avoiding the complexities that MHRoPE introduces with distributed training paradigms like tensor parallelism." (Appendix D.1)

*   **Trade-off:** MHRoPE's head-level partitioning complicates tensor parallelism, a common optimization for large-scale training. This may make MHRoPE impractical for training models beyond the 7B scale tested in this work.
*   **Performance Cost:** MHRoPE shows a "minor performance deficit" compared to MRoPE-I (e.g., Grounding: 74.92 vs 75.85), which the authors attribute to "head-level information partitioning, which prevents the integration of different positional axes within the self-attention mechanism" (Appendix D.1).

**Frequency Allocation Ratio Sensitivity**

Appendix D.5.2 reveals that the interleaved design is sensitive to the allocation ratio:

| Ratio (t:h:w) | Overall Score |
|---------------|---------------|
| 24:20:20 | 64.95 |
| 32:16:16 | 63.29 |
| 48:8:8 | 63.03 |

*   **Implication:** A suboptimal ratio costs ~1.7 overall points. This suggests practitioners cannot arbitrarily choose allocation ratios—they must either replicate the 24:20:20 balance or conduct expensive tuning. The paper does not provide guidance on how to determine optimal ratios for different task mixes.

### 6.4 Remaining Weaknesses and Open Questions

**Trade-off Between Balanced and Specialized Performance**

The paper positions `MRoPE-I` as a "holistic" solution, but this comes with an inherent trade-off:

*   **Evidence:** VideoRoPE outperforms MRoPE-I on MLVU (66.05 vs 65.46) and shows better long-context extrapolation (Appendix D.4).
*   **Weakness:** For applications prioritizing video understanding over image/grounding tasks, specialized methods like VideoRoPE may still be preferable. `MRoPE-I` achieves the best *balance*, not the best *peak* performance in any single category.

**Mechanism of Spatial-Reset Not Fully Explained**

While Appendix D.5.1 shows that `spatial-reset` increases visual attention (Layer 28: 11.69% → 23.23% for MRoPE-I), the paper does not fully explain *why* this occurs:

*   **Open Question:** Is the improvement due to alignment with the LLM's attention sink bias, or does `spatial-reset` fundamentally change how spatial relationships are encoded? The paper offers both explanations (Section 2.2.3) but does not disentangle them experimentally.
*   **Open Question:** Does `spatial-reset` affect multi-image reasoning where relative positions *between* images matter? The paper tests single-image and single-video benchmarks but does not explore interleaved multi-image sequences extensively.

**Temporal Stride Optimization Unresolved**

Appendix D.5.3 tests temporal strides (δ = 0.5, 1, 2, dynamic) and finds δ = 1 optimal. However:

*   **Limitation:** This was tested only on MRoPE-I with a fixed set of video benchmarks. The optimal stride may vary by video resolution, frame rate, or task type.
*   **Open Question:** Should temporal stride be dynamic based on video content, or is fixed δ = 1 sufficient for all cases? The paper finds dynamic stride (from V2PE) provides no significant benefit (51.80 vs 52.36 overall), but this conclusion is limited to the tested benchmarks.

**Compatibility with Extrapolation Techniques**

Appendix D.3 notes that `MRoPE-I` is compatible with NTK-aware and YaRN extrapolation, requiring "a scaling factor for MRoPE-I that is approximately 3/4 of that used for vanilla RoPE" for 512×512 resolution inputs.

*   **Limitation:** This claim is not accompanied by empirical extrapolation results using YaRN or NTK-aware. The long-context experiments in Appendix D.4 test native extrapolation without these techniques.
*   **Open Question:** How do `MHRoPE` and `MRoPE-I` interact with other context extension methods (e.g., positional interpolation, window attention)? This remains untested.

**Failure Mode Quantification**

The paper diagnoses failure modes (modalities confusion, attention sink misalignment, textual prior breaking) but does not quantify their frequency:

*   **Open Question:** How often does "endless text repetition" occur in practice with diagonal layout designs? The paper describes it qualitatively but provides no statistics on failure rates across benchmarks.
*   **Open Question:** What is the threshold for "modality interval" that causes contextual irrelevance vs. modalities confusion? The ablation tests one enlarged interval but does not map the failure boundary.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes the key trade-offs practitioners should consider:

| Design Choice | Benefit | Cost |
|---------------|---------|------|
| **MRoPE-I over MHRoPE** | Simpler implementation, slightly better performance | Less scalable for many positional axes |
| **Balanced frequency allocation (24:20:20)** | Best overall multi-task performance | Suboptimal for specialized long-video tasks |
| **Preserving text RoPE compatibility** | Enables LLM knowledge transfer | Prevents text-specific optimization for VLM |
| **Spatial-reset** | Improves visual attention, prevents sink misalignment | May complicate multi-image relative positioning |
| **3D position design** | Enables spatial reasoning, visual grounding | Adds complexity; minimal gain for non-spatial tasks |

**Bottom Line:** `MRoPE-I` represents the best *general-purpose* solution for VLMs requiring balanced image, video, and grounding capabilities. However, for specialized applications (e.g., extreme long-video analysis, multimodal generation), the trade-offs documented above suggest that further customization or alternative methods may be warranted. The paper provides a strong foundation but leaves several optimization questions open for future work.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work does more than propose two new positional encoding variants; it establishes a diagnostic framework for evaluating Vision-Language Model (VLM) architectures. By decomposing multimodal Rotary Positional Embedding (`RoPE`) into **position design** and **frequency allocation**, the authors provide a checklist for future model development. This section outlines how this work shifts the field's design paradigm, identifies specific avenues for follow-up research, and offers practical guidance for integrating these methods into downstream applications.

### 7.1 Shifting the Design Paradigm

Prior to this work, multimodal positional encoding was often treated as a modular plug-in, with researchers proposing specialized fixes for specific tasks (e.g., video-only or generation-only methods). This paper shifts the landscape toward **systematic holistic design**.

*   **From Ad-Hoc to Diagnostic:** The introduction of three core guidelines—**positional coherence**, **full frequency utilization**, and **preservation of textual priors** (Abstract)—provides a standardized rubric for evaluating new methods. Future proposals should be able to articulate how they satisfy these three constraints. For instance, Table 1 now serves as a reference matrix where any new method can be plotted against these axes to identify potential weaknesses (e.g., does it break text compatibility?).
*   **Reframing Attention Sinks:** A subtle but profound shift is the treatment of the "attention sink" phenomenon. Traditionally, attention sinks (where models disproportionately attend to initial tokens) were viewed as a defect to be mitigated. This work demonstrates that **aligning visual content with existing sinks** via `spatial-reset` is more effective than trying to eliminate them (Section 4.1). This suggests future architectures should design input layouts that respect the inductive biases of the base LLM, rather than forcing the LLM to adapt to arbitrary visual layouts.
*   **Unified Decay Profiles:** By ensuring all positional axes (time, height, width) access the full frequency spectrum, `MRoPE-I` creates a **unified decay profile** for attention scores (Figure 4b). This simplifies the learning landscape, implying that future models may not need task-specific heads for spatial vs. temporal reasoning. The model can learn a single, consistent notion of "distance" across all modalities.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Avenues

The systematic analysis in this paper opens several specific doors for follow-up research. These are not just incremental improvements but fundamental questions raised by the findings.

*   **Scaling Laws for Positional Encoding:** All experiments in this paper use the **Qwen2.5 7B** backbone (Section 3.1). A critical open question is how these design choices scale to larger models (e.g., 72B parameters).
    *   *Hypothesis:* As model size increases, the sensitivity to frequency allocation may change. Larger models might tolerate suboptimal frequency partitioning better due to increased capacity, or conversely, they might require even stricter adherence to full frequency utilization to avoid bottlenecks.
    *   *Follow-up:* Replicate the ablation studies (Table 3, Table 4) on larger backbones to determine if the optimal frequency ratio (currently $t:h:w = 24:20:20$) holds constant across scales.
*   **Quantifying Generation Failures:** The paper diagnoses **"modalities confusion in generation"** (e.g., endless text repetition) caused by positional overlap in diagonal layouts (Section 3.3.1). However, this is currently supported by qualitative observation and benchmark drops (e.g., DocVQA falling from 82.94 to 60.13).
    *   *Hypothesis:* There is a measurable threshold of positional overlap that triggers generation collapse.
    *   *Follow-up:* Develop specific metrics for generation stability, such as **repetition rates** or **modality classification accuracy** during autoregressive decoding. This would turn a qualitative failure mode into a quantifiable safety metric for VLMs.
*   **Dynamic Frequency Allocation:** The ablation study in Appendix D.5.2 tests fixed allocation ratios (24:20:20, 32:16:16, 48:8:8). It shows that biasing too heavily toward temporal axes harms spatial grounding.
    *   *Hypothesis:* A static ratio is suboptimal for mixed batches containing both long videos and static images.
    *   *Follow-up:* Investigate **input-adaptive frequency allocation**, where the model dynamically adjusts the $t:h:w$ channel split based on the input modality (e.g., allocating more temporal channels for video inputs and more spatial channels for images).
*   **Beyond 3D Tuples:** The current position design assumes visual content fits a regular 3D tensor $(t, h, w)$. As VLMs expand to handle point clouds, graphs, or irregular panoramas, this tuple structure may be insufficient.
    *   *Follow-up:* Extend the **Multi-Head RoPE (MHRoPE)** strategy to accommodate $N$-dimensional inputs. Since MHRoPE partitions by attention head rather than channel, it is theoretically more scalable to adding new positional axes (Section 2.3.3). Empirical validation on non-rectangular data structures is needed.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Integration Guidance

For practitioners looking to adopt these methods, the choice between `MHRoPE` and `MRoPE-I` depends on the specific deployment constraints and task requirements.

*   **General-Purpose VLMs (Recommended: `MRoPE-I`):**
    *   **Use Case:** Models requiring balanced performance across image understanding, video reasoning, and visual grounding (e.g., assistant bots, document analysis tools).
    *   **Reasoning:** `MRoPE-I` achieves the best overall balance (Table 2) and is simpler to implement. It avoids the tensor parallelism complexities associated with `MHRoPE` (Appendix D.1).
    *   **Integration:** Adopt the interleaved frequency allocation with the optimal ratio of **$t:h:w = 24:20:20$** (Appendix D.5.2). Ensure `spatial-reset` is applied to visual tokens but **not** text tokens.
*   **Specialized Video Models (Consider: `VideoRoPE` or `MHRoPE`):**
    *   **Use Case:** Applications focused exclusively on extreme long-video understanding (e.g., surveillance, hour-long meeting analysis).
    *   **Reasoning:** While `MRoPE-I` is balanced, methods like `VideoRoPE` show slightly better extrapolation on long video benchmarks (Appendix D.4). `MHRoPE` also edges out `MRoPE-I` on video tasks (52.58 vs 52.36 overall video score) due to head-level partitioning (Table 2).
    *   **Trade-off:** Accept degraded performance on static image grounding tasks in exchange for improved temporal stability.
*   **Text-Heavy Multimodal Tasks (Stick to: `Vanilla RoPE`):**
    *   **Use Case:** Tasks where visual input is secondary to text reasoning (e.g., text-based QA with optional images).
    *   **Reasoning:** Table 2 shows `Vanilla RoPE` remains competitive on some benchmarks (e.g., AI2D, RealWorldQA). If the engineering cost of implementing multi-dimensional RoPE is high and spatial grounding is not required, the complexity may not be justified.

**Critical Integration Constraints:**
When integrating these methods, practitioners must adhere to the **preservation of textual priors** guideline (Section 2.4):
1.  **Match Base LLM:** The rotary base for text tokens must match the pre-trained LLM exactly (e.g., **$1,000,000$** in this study).
2.  **No Text Reset:** Do not apply `spatial-reset` to text tokens. Ablation shows this drops image benchmark performance from **65.69** to **58.27** (Table 3).
3.  **Freeze ViT:** During initial adaptation, freeze the visual encoder (ViT) to isolate positional encoding effects, as done in Section 3.1.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Resource Planning

For researchers aiming to reproduce or build upon this work, the following logistical details are critical for planning.

*   **Compute Requirements:** Each experiment in this study consumed approximately **512 Nvidia A100 GPU hours** (Section 3.1). This high barrier suggests that extensive hyperparameter searching (e.g., testing many frequency ratios) may be prohibitive for smaller labs.
    *   *Guidance:* Start with the recommended **$24:20:20$** ratio and **32K context length** before attempting modifications.
*   **Code Availability:** The authors state code will be available at `https://github.com/JJJYmmm/Multimodal-RoPEs` (Abstract). As of the paper's writing, users should verify the repository status.
    *   *Implementation Note:* Pay close attention to the **Group Query Attention (GQA)** handling. For `MHRoPE`, the partition is applied on KV heads and repeated on corresponding query heads (Footnote 1, Section 2.3.3). Incorrect implementation here could break the frequency allocation logic.
*   **Data Dependency:** The results rely on **~2M high-quality supervised fine-tuning (SFT) samples** (Section 3.1). Performance gains may diminish if trained on smaller or lower-quality datasets, as the model needs sufficient signal to learn the new positional relationships.
*   **Extrapolation Verification:** If deploying in long-context scenarios (>32K), verify compatibility with extrapolation techniques like **YaRN** or **NTK-aware scaling**. The paper claims `MRoPE-I` is compatible and requires a scaling factor approximately **3/4** of that used for vanilla RoPE for 512×512 resolution inputs (Appendix D.3), but this should be validated on your specific hardware and sequence lengths.

By adhering to these guidelines and recognizing the trade-offs outlined in this work, practitioners can avoid common pitfalls like modalities confusion and ensure robust transfer of knowledge from pre-trained LLMs to multimodal contexts. This paper provides not just a method, but a **standard of care** for positional encoding in future Vision-Language Models.
