## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the difficulty of creating effective zero-shot dense retrieval systems without relevance labels by proposing Hypothetical Document Embeddings (HyDE), which pivots retrieval through an instruction-following language model (e.g., InstructGPT) that generates a hypothetical document to be encoded by an unsupervised contrastive encoder (e.g., Contriever). Experiments across 11 query sets including TREC DL19/20, BEIR, and Mr.TyDi show HyDE significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art unsupervised retriever Contriever and achieves performance comparable to fine-tuned models like ContrieverFT, despite requiring no relevance supervision or training. This approach demonstrates that relevance modeling can be delegated to generative language models, enabling effective out-of-box retrieval for new tasks and languages where large labeled datasets like MS-MARCO are unavailable or restricted.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of this paper, we must first establish the fundamental mechanics of dense retrieval and the specific bottlenecks that arise when supervision data is unavailable. This section breaks down the supervision problem, critiques the prevailing "transfer learning" workaround, and explains how recent advances in language models create a new opportunity for zero-shot systems.

### The Supervision Bottleneck in Dense Retrieval

Dense retrieval operates by mapping queries and documents into a shared vector space where semantic similarity is measured via inner product. Formally, given a query $q$ and a document $d$, the system uses encoder functions $\text{enc}_q$ and $\text{enc}_d$ to produce vectors $v_q$ and $v_d$. The relevance score is calculated as:

$$
\text{sim}(q, d) = \langle \text{enc}_q(q), \text{enc}_d(d) \rangle = \langle v_q, v_d \rangle
$$

This formulation is described in Section 3.1. The core difficulty lies in learning these encoder functions. To ensure that relevant query-document pairs have high inner products while irrelevant pairs have low ones, models typically require massive amounts of labeled data (relevance judgments). Without these labels, optimizing the embedding space is intractable because there is no signal to tell the model which vectors should be close together.

This creates a significant barrier for deployment in real-world scenarios where labeled data does not exist. The paper argues in Section 1 that while dense retrieval is effective, creating a *fully zero-shot* system—one that works without any relevance labels for the target task—remains an open challenge.

### Limitations of Current "Zero-Shot" Approaches

When researchers discuss "zero-shot dense retrieval" in existing literature, they often refer to a **transfer learning** setup rather than a truly label-free setup. The standard practice involves:

*   **Training on High-Resource Data:** Models are fine-tuned on large, labeled datasets like MS-MARCO, which contains millions of query-document pairs (Section 1).
*   **Testing on New Tasks:** The trained model is then evaluated on different tasks (e.g., scientific fact verification, legal search) without further fine-tuning. This evaluation framework is standardized by the BEIR benchmark (Section 2).

While effective, this approach has critical weaknesses that motivate the HyDE proposal:

*   **Data Availability:** Large labeled datasets like MS-MARCO cannot always be assumed to exist for specific domains or languages (Section 1).
*   **Commercial Restrictions:** MS-MARCO restricts commercial use, preventing adoption in many real-world search products (Section 1).
*   **Domain Mismatch:** A model trained on web search queries (MS-MARCO) may not generalize well to specialized tasks like biomedical search or fact verification without additional supervision (Section 2).

Furthermore, purely unsupervised methods exist, such as **Contriever**, which uses contrastive learning to encode document-document similarity without labels (Section 2). However, these models still struggle with query encoding because queries are structurally different from documents (queries are short, documents are long). Without supervision to align query vectors with document vectors, unsupervised encoders often underperform compared to even classical lexical methods like BM25 (Table 1).

### Emerging Capabilities: LLMs and Contrastive Encoders

The motivation for HyDE stems from the convergence of two distinct advancements in deep learning that occurred independently prior to this work:

1.  **Instruction-Following Language Models (LLMs):** Models like InstructGPT have demonstrated the ability to generalize to unseen tasks based on natural language instructions (Section 1). For example, if instructed to "write a passage that answers the question," these models can generate coherent text that mimics a relevant document, even without specific training on that query type.
2.  **Unsupervised Contrastive Encoders:** Models like Contriever have proven capable of learning robust document embeddings using only raw text, capturing document-document similarity effectively (Section 1).

Prior work has not effectively combined these two capabilities for retrieval. **Generative retrieval** methods exist, but they typically require training the generative model to output document identifiers (IDs) and often need novel search indices (Section 2). Other concurrent works, such as "Task-aware Retrieval with Instructions," fine-tune dense encoders to accept instructions, which still requires training data (Section 2).

### Positioning HyDE: Composition Over Training

This paper positions HyDE not as a new training algorithm, but as a **composition of existing models** that circumvents the need for relevance labels entirely. The authors identify a gap: while we lack a way to map queries to document vectors without labels, we *do* have models that can map queries to hypothetical documents (LLMs) and models that can map documents to vectors (Contrastive Encoders).

HyDE bridges this gap by pivoting through a **hypothetical document**. Instead of trying to learn $\text{enc}_q$ directly, the system uses an LLM to generate a fake document that *would* answer the query. This generated text is then fed into the unsupervised document encoder. This approach relies on the insight that it is easier for an LLM to generate an example of relevance (a document) than it is for an encoder to learn a numerical relevance score without supervision (Section 3.2).

This positioning is critical for practical applications. As noted in Section 6, HyDE is designed for the "cold start" phase of a search system—where no search logs or relevance labels exist yet. It offers performance comparable to fine-tuned models immediately, without the cost or delay of data collection and model training. By removing the dependency on MS-MARCO or similar datasets, HyDE enables dense retrieval in restricted commercial environments and low-resource languages where labeled data is scarce (Section 1).

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

HyDE is a retrieval system that converts a user's search query into a fake but plausible answer document, then uses that generated text to find real matching documents in a corpus. The system solves the zero-shot retrieval problem by avoiding the need to learn query-to-document relevance directly—instead, it leverages a language model's ability to write example answers and a separate encoder's ability to recognize similar documents, chaining these two capabilities together without any training or relevance labels.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The HyDE system consists of three major components connected in a sequential pipeline. First, an **instruction-following language model** (e.g., InstructGPT) receives the user query along with a task-specific text instruction and generates one or more hypothetical documents that would answer the query. Second, an **unsupervised contrastive encoder** (e.g., Contriever or mContriever) takes each generated hypothetical document and converts it into a dense embedding vector in a fixed-dimensional space. Third, a **maximum inner product search (MIPS) index** containing pre-computed embeddings of all real corpus documents receives the query vector and returns the real documents with the highest vector similarity scores. Information flows in one direction: query → hypothetical document(s) → embedding vector(s) → retrieved real documents. No component is trained during HyDE operation; all models are used as-is from their prior pre-training.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

*   We first formalize the zero-shot dense retrieval problem to clarify exactly what HyDE must accomplish without supervision.
*   We then explain how HyDE decomposes the retrieval task into two separate sub-problems: generation and encoding.
*   We walk through the mathematical formulation of how query vectors are constructed from hypothetical documents.
*   We detail the role of the contrastive encoder as a lossy compressor that filters hallucinated content.
*   We explain the document-only embedding space and why this avoids the query-document alignment problem.
*   We conclude with implementation specifics including model choices, sampling parameters, and instruction design.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a systems architecture paper that proposes a novel composition of existing models rather than a new training algorithm, with the core idea being that relevance modeling can be delegated to a generative language model while retrieval remains the responsibility of a contrastive encoder.

#### Formal Problem Definition

The paper begins by formally defining dense retrieval in Section 3.1 to establish what must be solved. Given a query $q$ and a document $d$, traditional dense retrieval uses two encoder functions $\text{enc}_q$ and $\text{enc}_d$ to map them into $d$-dimensional vectors $v_q$ and $v_d$, where the similarity is measured by their inner product:

$$
\text{sim}(q, d) = \langle \text{enc}_q(q), \text{enc}_d(d) \rangle = \langle v_q, v_d \rangle
$$

For zero-shot retrieval, the system must define these mapping functions without access to any query set $Q_i$, document set $D_i$, or relevance judgment $r_{ij}$ across $L$ different query sets. The fundamental difficulty, as stated in Section 3.1, is that learning two embedding functions into the same space where inner product captures relevance becomes intractable without relevance judgments or scores to fit. This mathematical framing explains why prior unsupervised methods struggle: they cannot learn the query encoder $\text{enc}_q$ to align with the document encoder $\text{enc}_d$ without supervision signals.

#### Decomposing Retrieval into Generation and Encoding

HyDE circumvents this learning problem by performing search in a document-only embedding space that captures document-document similarity rather than query-document similarity. The system sets the document encoder $\text{enc}_d$ directly as a contrastive encoder $\text{enc}_{\text{con}}$, such as Contriever, which has been trained using unsupervised contrastive learning on raw text corpora. This encoder function is denoted as $f$ for simplicity, and it is shared across all incoming document corpora:

$$
v_d = f(d) \quad \forall d \in D_1 \cup D_2 \cup \ldots \cup D_L
$$

The critical innovation is how HyDE constructs the query vector. Instead of learning a query encoder directly, the system introduces an instruction-following language model, denoted as $\text{InstructLM}$, which takes a query $q$ and a textual instruction $\text{INST}$ and follows them to perform the task specified by the instruction. This is formally written as:

$$
g(q, \text{INST}) = \text{InstructLM}(q, \text{INST})
$$

The instruction is set to something like "write a paragraph that answers the question", which causes the language model to generate a hypothetical document rather than a direct answer. This generated document is not real and can contain factual errors or hallucinations, but it is expected to capture relevance patterns by providing an example of what a relevant document looks like. The paper explicitly states in Section 3.2 that this approach offloads relevance modeling from the representation learning model to a natural language generation model that generalizes more easily and effectively.

#### Query Vector Construction via Sampling

The hypothetical document generated by the language model is then encoded using the document encoder $f$. Formally, the expected query vector is defined as:

$$
\mathbb{E}[v_{q_{ij}}] = \mathbb{E}[f(g(q_{ij}, \text{INST}_i))]
$$

The function $g$ defines a probability distribution based on the chain rule of language generation. The paper assumes the distribution of $v_{q_{ij}}$ is uni-modal, meaning the query is not ambiguous, and studies of ambiguous queries are left to future work. In practice, Equation 5 is estimated by sampling $N$ documents from $g$, denoted as $[\hat{d}_1, \hat{d}_2, \ldots, \hat{d}_N]$, and averaging their embeddings:

$$
\hat{v}_{q_{ij}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{\hat{d}_k \sim g(q_{ij}, \text{INST}_i)} f(\hat{d}_k) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^{N} f(\hat{d}_k)
$$

The paper also considers an alternative formulation where the original query itself is treated as a possible hypothesis and included in the averaging:

$$
\hat{v}_{q_{ij}} = \frac{1}{N + 1} \left[ \sum_{k=1}^{N} f(\hat{d}_k) + f(q_{ij}) \right]
$$

This averaging across multiple generated documents helps stabilize the query representation and reduces variance from any single hallucinated output.

#### The Encoder as a Lossy Compressor

A critical design insight in HyDE is that the contrastive encoder $f$ serves as a lossy compressor that filters out incorrect or hallucinated details from the hypothetical document. When the generated document is encoded into a dense vector, the encoder's bottleneck forces it to capture only the most salient semantic features while discarding specific factual claims that may be wrong. This vector then identifies a neighborhood in the corpus embedding space where similar real documents are retrieved based on vector similarity. The paper states in the Abstract that this second step grounds the generated document to the actual corpus, with the encoder's dense bottleneck filtering out the incorrect details. This mechanism explains why factual errors in the hypothetical document do not catastrophically harm retrieval: the embedding captures the topic and semantic structure rather than specific claims.

#### Document-Only Embedding Space

With HyDE factorization, the query-document similarity score is no longer explicitly modeled or computed as in traditional dense retrieval. Instead, the retrieval task is cast into two natural language understanding and natural language generation tasks. The inner product is computed between the query vector $\hat{v}_{q_{ij}}$ and the set of all document vectors $\{f(d) | d \in D_i\}$, and the most similar documents are retrieved. This retrieval leverages document-document similarity encoded in the inner product during contrastive training of the encoder. The key distinction is that both the hypothetical document and the real documents are encoded by the same function $f$, so they inhabit the same embedding space by construction. This avoids the alignment problem that plagues unsupervised dense retrieval, where query and document encoders must be learned separately without supervision to bring them into alignment.

#### Implementation Configuration and Hyperparameters

The implementation details are provided in Section 4.1 and are essential for understanding how HyDE operates in practice. The system uses InstructGPT, specifically the `text-davinci-003` model from the instruct series, as the instruction-following language model. Sampling from InstructGPT uses the OpenAI playground default temperature of 0.7 for open-ended generations, which introduces sufficient diversity in the hypothetical documents while maintaining coherence. For English retrieval tasks, the system uses the English-only Contriever model, while for non-English tasks it uses the multilingual mContriever model. Retrieval experiments are conducted with the Pyserini toolkit, which provides efficient maximum inner product search capabilities at scale.

The paper notes in footnote 1 that no models were trained or fine-tuned in making this work, emphasizing that HyDE is a zero-shot composition of pre-existing models. The only supervision signals involved were in the instruction learning of the backbone LLM during its original training by OpenAI, not during HyDE deployment. This is a crucial distinction: HyDE itself requires no training, no relevance labels, and no access to the test-time corpora for training, which makes it applicable in scenarios where MS-MARCO or similar datasets cannot be used due to commercial restrictions or domain mismatch.

#### Instruction Design for Different Tasks

The system uses different instructions for each dataset, as noted in Section 4.1. These instructions share a similar structure but have different quantifiers to control the exact form of the generated hypothetical documents. The Appendix A.1 provides the specific instructions used for each task. For web search (TREC DL19/20), the instruction is "Please write a passage to answer the question" followed by the question. For SciFact, the instruction is "Please write a scientific paper passage to support/refute the claim" followed by the claim. For Arguana, the instruction is "Please write a counter argument for the passage" followed by the passage. For TREC-COVID, the instruction is "Please write a scientific paper passage to answer the question". For FiQA, the instruction is "Please write a financial article passage to answer the question". For DBPedia-Entity, the instruction is "Please write a passage to answer the question". For TREC-NEWS, the instruction is "Please write a news passage about the topic". For Mr.TyDi multilingual tasks, the instruction is "Please write a passage in [Language] to answer the question in detail" where the language is specified as Swahili, Korean, Japanese, or Bengali.

These task-specific instructions are critical because they guide the language model to generate hypothetical documents that match the expected format and style of relevant documents in each corpus. A hypothetical scientific paper passage will have different linguistic patterns than a hypothetical news passage, and matching these patterns helps the contrastive encoder place the hypothetical document in the correct region of the embedding space.

#### Why This Approach Over Alternatives

The paper explains in Section 2 why HyDE differs from and improves upon alternative approaches. Generative retrieval methods use neural generative models as search indices, generating document identifiers or substrings that map directly to real documents, but these require special training procedures over relevance data and may need novel forms of search indices. HyDE uses the standard MIPS index and requires no training or training data. Concurrent work on "Task-aware Retrieval with Instructions" fine-tunes dense encoders that can encode task-specific instructions prepended to queries, but this still requires training data. HyDE uses an unsupervised encoder and handles different tasks and their instructions with an instruction-following generative LLM.

The transfer learning setup used by most zero-shot retrieval research trains on MS-MARCO and evaluates on new tasks, but this assumes the existence of large labeled datasets that cannot always be assumed in practice. HyDE requires no relevance supervision and works out-of-box across tasks and languages. The paper argues in Section 6 that this makes HyDE particularly valuable at the beginning of a search system's life, where no search logs or relevance labels exist yet, offering performance comparable to fine-tuned models without the cost or delay of data collection and training.

#### Key Design Trade-offs and Limitations

The paper acknowledges several design trade-offs in Section 5. When using smaller language models like FLAN-T5 (11 billion parameters) or Cohere (52 billion parameters) instead of InstructGPT (175 billion parameters), the retrieval performance decreases, with larger models bringing larger improvements as shown in Table 4. This suggests that the quality of hypothetical document generation is critical to HyDE's success. The paper also notes in Section 4.4 that multilingual setup poses additional challenges: the small-sized contrastive encoder gets saturated as the number of languages scales, and the generative LLM can get under-trained for languages with fewer resources than English or French. This explains the performance gap between HyDE and fine-tuned mContrieverFT on multilingual tasks in Table 3.

Another limitation noted in Section 3.2 is that the paper assumes the distribution of query vectors is uni-modal, meaning queries are not ambiguous. The study of ambiguous queries and diversity is left to future work, which means HyDE may struggle with queries that have multiple valid interpretations or answer types. The paper also notes in Section 5.2 that HyDE with a fine-tuned encoder is not the intended usage, as HyDE is more powerful and irreplaceable when few relevance labels are present, though experiments show that InstructGPT can still bring performance improvements even when combined with fine-tuned encoders like ContrieverFT.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The HyDE paper does not merely propose a new model architecture; it introduces a fundamental shift in how we conceptualize the retrieval problem. While many contemporaneous works focus on incremental improvements to encoder training or negative sampling, HyDE questions the necessity of learning a query encoder at all. Below, we analyze the three most critical innovations that distinguish this work from prior art, distinguishing between fundamental paradigm shifts and practical engineering advances.

### 4.1 Fundamental Innovation: Reformulating Query-Document Alignment as Document-Document Similarity

The most significant theoretical contribution of HyDE is the reformulation of the dense retrieval objective. Traditional dense retrieval, as defined in Section 3.1, relies on learning two distinct functions, $\text{enc}_q$ and $\text{enc}_d$, to map queries and documents into a shared space where their inner product reflects relevance:

$$
\text{sim}(q, d) = \langle \text{enc}_q(q), \text{enc}_d(d) \rangle
$$

In a zero-shot setting without relevance labels, learning $\text{enc}_q$ to align with $\text{enc}_d$ is intractable because there is no supervision signal to pull the query vector toward relevant document vectors. Prior "zero-shot" works typically bypass this by transferring a query encoder trained on a different dataset (e.g., MS-MARCO), which assumes domain similarity that may not exist (Section 2).

HyDE fundamentally changes this equation by eliminating $\text{enc}_q$ entirely. Instead of learning to map a query to a vector, HyDE maps the query to a **hypothetical document** $d_{hyp}$ using a generative model, and then applies the document encoder to that text:

$$
v_q \approx \text{enc}_d(\text{LLM}(q))
$$

This transforms the problem from **Query-Document Alignment** (hard without labels) to **Document-Document Similarity** (easy with unsupervised contrastive learning). 

*   **Why this is fundamental:** Prior methods treat the query as a distinct semantic entity that must be learned. HyDE treats the query as a seed for generating a document-like entity. This allows the system to leverage unsupervised encoders (like Contriever) that are trained solely on document-document similarity, bypassing the need for any query-specific training data.
*   **Evidence of impact:** This reformulation allows HyDE to outperform the unsupervised Contriever baseline by large margins on TREC DL19/20 (Table 1), achieving a Recall@1k of 88.0 compared to Contriever's 74.6, despite using the exact same document encoder. The performance gain comes not from a better encoder, but from a better alignment strategy.

### 4.2 Mechanism Insight: Hallucination as Noise Filtered by Dense Bottlenecks

A counter-intuitive insight in HyDE is its treatment of generative hallucination. In most retrieval-augmented generation tasks, factual errors in generated text are catastrophic. However, HyDE posits that for retrieval, factual accuracy is secondary to semantic positioning.

The paper argues in the Abstract and Section 3.2 that the contrastive encoder acts as a **lossy compressor**. When the hypothetical document (which may contain false details) is passed through the encoder, the dense vector bottleneck filters out specific factual claims while retaining the high-level semantic topic.

*   **Distinct from prior work:** Generative retrieval methods (Section 2) often require the generated output to be a precise document identifier or substring, demanding high factual precision. HyDE only requires the generated text to occupy the correct **neighborhood** in the embedding space.
*   **Why this works:** Dense retrieval relies on coarse semantic similarity (e.g., "this text is about wisdom teeth removal") rather than fine-grained fact verification (e.g., "this text says 30 minutes"). As long as the hypothetical document uses vocabulary and syntactic structures similar to relevant corpus documents, the encoder will map it to the correct region.
*   **Evidence of robustness:** In Table 2, HyDE maintains strong performance on fact-verification tasks like SciFact (nDCG@10 of 69.1) even though the hypothetical documents are explicitly noted to be "unreal" and may contain false details. This suggests that the embedding space is robust to the specific noise introduced by instruction-following LLMs, provided the semantic intent is preserved.

### 4.3 Practical Paradigm: True Zero-Shot vs. Transfer Learning

The third key innovation is the redefinition of what constitutes "zero-shot" retrieval in practice. The literature often conflates **Zero-Shot** (no target task labels) with **Transfer Learning** (trained on source task labels, e.g., MS-MARCO).

*   **The Status Quo:** Most state-of-the-art "zero-shot" retrievers are actually fine-tuned on MS-MARCO (Section 2). While they are zero-shot relative to the target task, they are dependent on the existence of a massive, high-resource source dataset.
*   **The HyDE Approach:** HyDE requires **no relevance labels at any stage** of the target deployment. The only supervision exists in the pre-training of the backbone LLM (instruction tuning), which is general-purpose and not retrieval-specific.
*   **Significance:** This distinction is critical for commercial and low-resource applications. As noted in Section 1, MS-MARCO restricts commercial use. HyDE bypasses this restriction entirely. Furthermore, in low-resource languages where no large labeled dataset exists, transfer learning fails.
*   **Evidence of capability:** Table 3 shows results on Mr.TyDi (Swahili, Korean, Japanese, Bengali). HyDE achieves an MRR@100 of 41.7 in Swahili, outperforming mContriever (38.3) and even supervised transfer models like mDPR (7.3). This demonstrates that HyDE provides a viable path for dense retrieval in languages where collecting relevance labels for fine-tuning is impossible.

### 4.4 Critical Analysis: Limitations and Boundaries of the Innovation

While HyDE introduces fundamental innovations, it is important to recognize where the approach remains incremental or faces boundaries.

*   **Dependency on LLM Scale:** The effectiveness of the "pivot" relies heavily on the generative model's capability. Table 4 shows that reducing the LLM size from GPT-3 (175b) to FLAN-T5 (11b) drops nDCG@10 on DL19 from 61.3 to 48.9. This indicates that the innovation is currently coupled with the availability of large, instruction-tuned models. It is not yet a solution for resource-constrained environments where only small models can run.
*   **Latency Trade-off:** The pipeline introduces a generative step before retrieval. While the paper focuses on effectiveness, the inference cost of running a 175b parameter LLM for every query is significantly higher than a standard dual-encoder pass. This makes HyDE better suited for the "cold start" phase of a system (Section 6) rather than high-throughput production serving unless distillation is used.
*   **Ambiguity Handling:** As noted in Section 3.2, the current formulation assumes a uni-modal distribution of query vectors (i.e., the query is not ambiguous). If a query has multiple distinct intents, the averaging mechanism in Equation 7 might produce a vector that falls between clusters, retrieving irrelevant documents. This remains an open research question not fully solved by the current innovation.

In summary, HyDE's primary contribution is not a new loss function or architecture, but a **problem decomposition**. By offloading relevance modeling to a generative prior and retaining only semantic matching for the encoder, it achieves robust zero-shot performance where previous methods required expensive supervision.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

The experimental evaluation in this paper serves a dual purpose: first, to demonstrate that HyDE achieves practical retrieval effectiveness without any relevance labels; second, to validate the core hypothesis that pivoting through hypothetical documents can outperform direct query encoding in zero-shot settings. This section walks through the evaluation methodology, presents the quantitative results with specific numbers, and critically assesses whether the experiments support the paper's claims. We also examine ablation studies that reveal the boundaries of HyDE's effectiveness.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Datasets and Task Diversity

The authors evaluate HyDE across 11 query sets spanning three distinct categories, ensuring the results generalize beyond a single domain or language. This diversity is critical for a zero-shot method claim, as it tests whether the approach works "out-of-box" without task-specific tuning.

**Web Search Tasks (Table 1):**
- **TREC DL19 and DL20:** Two benchmark datasets based on the MS-MARCO collection, containing real web search queries with human relevance judgments. These represent high-resource, general-purpose search scenarios.
- These datasets are particularly important because they are the same datasets used to train many supervised baselines (like ContrieverFT), making them a challenging test for a truly label-free method.

**Low-Resource Retrieval Tasks (Table 2):**
- **Six BEIR datasets:** SciFact (scientific fact verification), Arguana (argument retrieval), TREC-Covid (biomedical search), FiQA (financial question answering), DBPedia (entity search), and TREC-NEWS (news search).
- These tasks vary significantly in document length, query type, and domain specificity. For example, SciFact requires retrieving scientific claims that support or refute a given statement, while Arguana involves finding counter-arguments to a passage.
- The BEIR benchmark is specifically designed for zero-shot evaluation, making it the standard testbed for this research area (Section 2).

**Multilingual Tasks (Table 3):**
- **Mr.TyDi dataset:** Four languages—Swahili, Korean, Japanese, and Bengali—representing varying levels of resource availability.
- This evaluation tests whether HyDE can generalize to languages where labeled training data is scarce or non-existent, a key motivation stated in Section 1.

#### Metrics and Measurement

The paper uses standard information retrieval metrics, each capturing different aspects of retrieval quality:

- **MAP (Mean Average Precision):** Measures precision across all recall levels, sensitive to the ranking of all relevant documents.
- **nDCG@10 (Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain at 10):** Emphasizes the quality of the top-10 results, with higher weight on top positions. This is critical for user-facing search where users rarely look beyond the first page.
- **Recall@1k:** Measures the fraction of relevant documents found in the top-1000 results. This is important for downstream tasks like reading comprehension where a retriever must surface all relevant passages.
- **MRR@100 (Mean Reciprocal Rank at 100):** Used for Mr.TyDi, measures the inverse of the rank of the first relevant document, emphasizing finding at least one relevant result quickly.

#### Baseline Systems

The experiments include three tiers of baseline systems, allowing for nuanced comparison:

**Tier 1: Unsupervised/Zero-Shot (No Relevance Labels)**
- **BM25:** Classical lexical retrieval using term frequency and inverse document frequency. This is a strong baseline that does not require any training data.
- **Contriever/mContriever:** Unsupervised contrastive encoders trained on raw text without relevance labels. These represent the state-of-the-art in label-free dense retrieval.

**Tier 2: Supervised Transfer Learning (Trained on MS-MARCO)**
- **DPR and ANCE:** Dense retrievers fine-tuned on MS-MARCO relevance data, then transferred to target tasks without further fine-tuning.
- **ContrieverFT/mContrieverFT:** The same Contriever models but fine-tuned on MS-MARCO. These represent the empirical upper bound for what unsupervised encoders can achieve with supervision.
- **mDPR, mBERT, XLM-R:** Multilingual models fine-tuned on MS-MARCO for cross-lingual transfer.

This tiered comparison is essential. If HyDE only outperformed unsupervised baselines but failed against supervised transfer models, its practical utility would be limited. The claim is that HyDE achieves performance *comparable* to fine-tuned models without any relevance labels.

#### Implementation Details

From Section 4.1, the experimental setup uses:
- **InstructGPT (text-davinci-003):** The instruction-following language model for generating hypothetical documents.
- **Temperature 0.7:** The default OpenAI playground setting for open-ended generation, balancing diversity and coherence.
- **Contriever for English, mContriever for non-English:** The same unsupervised encoders used in the baseline, ensuring fair comparison.
- **Pyserini toolkit:** For efficient maximum inner product search at scale.

Critically, footnote 1 states: *"No models were trained or fine-tuned in making this preprint."* This confirms HyDE is a zero-shot composition of existing models, not a new training method.

2. **Multilingual Limitations:** The gap between HyDE and mContrieverFT on Mr.TyDi (e.g., Swahili 41.7 vs 51.2) indicates that HyDE inherits the language limitations of its components. InstructGPT is primarily English-optimized, and mContriever's multilingual capacity is constrained. For truly low-resource languages, HyDE helps but does not fully close the gap with supervised fine-tuning.

3. **Ambiguous Queries:** Section 3.2 explicitly notes that the paper "assumes the distribution of $v_{q_{ij}}$ is uni-modal, i.e. the query is not ambiguous." The averaging mechanism in Equation 7 may produce vectors that fall between clusters for queries with multiple valid interpretations. This is acknowledged as future work, representing a known limitation.

4. **TREC-Covid Lexical Strength:** On TREC-Covid, BM25 (59.5) slightly edges HyDE (59.3). Biomedical search often relies heavily on precise terminology that lexical matching captures well. HyDE recovers most of Contriever's deficit (27.3 to 59.3) but does not fully surpass BM25. This suggests HyDE is not universally superior to lexical methods in all domains.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment: Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

The paper makes three primary claims, and the experiments provide varying levels of support for each:

**Claim 1: "HyDE significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art unsupervised dense retriever Contriever"**

**Support: Strong.** The evidence is unambiguous. Across all 11 query sets, HyDE improves over Contriever/mContriever. On web search (Table 1), the MAP improvement is 74% on DL19 (41.8 vs 24.0). On BEIR tasks (Table 2), improvements range from 8% to 130% depending on the dataset. On multilingual tasks (Table 3), improvements range from 9% to 57%. This claim is convincingly supported.

**Claim 2: "HyDE shows strong performance comparable to fine-tuned retrievers"**

**Support: Moderate to Strong, with Conditions.** The evidence is nuanced:
- On DL19, HyDE matches or exceeds ContrieverFT on MAP (41.8 vs 41.7) and Recall@1k (88.0 vs 83.6).
- On DL20, HyDE trails ContrieverFT by ~10% on MAP (38.2 vs 43.6).
- On BEIR, HyDE outperforms DPR and ANCE on most tasks but trails ContrieverFT on FiQA and DBPedia.
- On Mr.TyDi, HyDE outperforms mDPR and mBERT but trails mContrieverFT.

The claim holds for web search and many low-resource tasks but has exceptions in specialized domains (financial, entity search) and multilingual settings where fine-tuning provides an edge. The paper is appropriately cautious, using "comparable" rather than "superior."

**Claim 3: "HyDE works across various tasks and languages without relevance labels"**

**Support: Strong.** The evaluation spans 11 query sets across web search, fact verification, argument retrieval, biomedical search, financial QA, entity search, news search, and four languages. HyDE shows positive gains in all cases. The only exception is TREC-Covid where BM25 slightly edges HyDE, but HyDE still dramatically improves over Contriever. This claim is well-supported.

**Limitations in Experimental Design:**

1. **No Latency or Cost Analysis:** The paper focuses entirely on effectiveness metrics. Running InstructGPT (175b parameters) for every query has significant latency and cost implications compared to a single encoder pass. Section 6 acknowledges HyDE is for the "cold start" phase, but quantitative cost analysis would strengthen the practical case.

2. **Single LLM Configuration:** All main results use InstructGPT with temperature 0.7. The ablation in Table 4 tests different models but does not explore instruction variations systematically beyond those in Appendix A.1. Different instructions could yield different results, and the paper does not report sensitivity to instruction phrasing.

3. **No Ambiguity Analysis:** As noted in Section 3.2, ambiguous queries are not studied. Real-world search logs contain many ambiguous queries, and HyDE's averaging mechanism may struggle with these. This is an important gap for production deployment.

4. **Corpus Size Scaling:** The experiments use standard benchmark corpora. HyDE's performance at billion-scale corpora (where approximate nearest neighbor search introduces additional error) is not evaluated. The paper mentions MIPS efficiency in Section 2 but does not test scale effects.

### 5.5 Summary of Experimental Findings

The experimental evaluation provides robust evidence that HyDE achieves its core objective: effective zero-shot dense retrieval without relevance labels. The key quantitative takeaways are:

- **74% relative improvement** in MAP over Contriever on TREC DL19 (41.8 vs 24.0)
- **Competitive with fine-tuned models** on web search, matching ContrieverFT on DL19 MAP
- **Consistent gains across 11 query sets**, with improvements on all BEIR and Mr.TyDi tasks
- **Scale dependency**, with GPT-3 (175b) outperforming FLAN-T5 (11b) by 12.4 points on DL19 nDCG@10
- **Multilingual applicability**, with 37-57% improvements on Korean and Japanese over mContriever

The experiments convincingly demonstrate that pivoting through hypothetical documents is a viable strategy for zero-shot retrieval. However, the results also reveal boundary conditions: specialized domains may require more specific instructions, multilingual performance inherits LLM limitations, and ambiguous queries remain an open challenge. These limitations do not invalidate the core contribution but define the scope for future improvement.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While HyDE demonstrates that zero-shot dense retrieval is viable without relevance labels, it is not a universal solution that eliminates all constraints of traditional retrieval systems. The approach introduces new dependencies and trade-offs that practitioners must weigh against the benefits of label-free deployment. This section critically examines the assumptions HyDE relies on, the computational costs incurred, the scenarios where it struggles, and the open questions that remain.

### 6.1 Assumption of Uni-Modal Query Distributions

A fundamental theoretical assumption in HyDE is that a query corresponds to a single semantic intent. In Section 3.2, when defining the query vector construction, the authors state:

> "In this paper, we simply consider the expectation value, assuming the distribution of $v_{q_{ij}}$ is uni-modal, i.e. the query is not ambiguous."

This assumption drives the design of Equation 7, where multiple generated hypothetical documents are averaged into a single vector $\hat{v}_{q_{ij}}$.

*   **The Risk of Ambiguity:** If a user query is ambiguous (e.g., "Java" could refer to the programming language or the island), the instruction-following LLM might generate hypothetical documents covering different intents, or the averaging process might produce a vector that lies *between* distinct clusters in the embedding space.
*   **Consequence:** Instead of retrieving relevant documents for either intent, the system might retrieve documents that are semantically "average" and thus irrelevant to both specific intents.
*   **Status:** The paper explicitly flags this as future work. There is no mechanism in the current HyDE design to detect ambiguity or split the query into multiple retrieval paths. In production systems where ambiguous queries are common, this could lead to lower precision compared to systems that handle query clarification.

### 6.2 Computational Cost and Latency Trade-offs

The most significant practical trade-off in HyDE is the exchange of **training cost** for **inference cost**.

*   **Traditional Dense Retrieval:** Requires expensive training (fine-tuning on MS-MARCO) but cheap inference (two fast encoder forward passes).
*   **HyDE:** Requires zero training but expensive inference. For every query, the system must run a large instruction-following language model (e.g., InstructGPT `text-davinci-003`) to generate the hypothetical document before encoding it.

Although the paper does not provide explicit latency measurements in milliseconds, the computational implication is clear from the model choices in Section 4.1. Running a 175 billion parameter model (GPT-3) for generation is orders of magnitude slower and more costly than running a 110 million parameter encoder (Contriever).

*   **Positioning:** The authors acknowledge this trade-off in Section 6, positioning HyDE specifically for the **"cold start" phase** of a search system:
    > "At the very beginning of the life of the search system, serving queries using HyDE offers performance comparable to a fine-tuned model... As the search log grows, a supervised dense retriever can be gradually rolled out."

*   **Implication:** HyDE is not necessarily designed for high-throughput, low-latency production serving indefinitely. It is a bridge solution to provide high-quality retrieval until enough user interaction data (clicks, relevance judgments) is collected to train a cheaper, supervised dual-encoder model.

### 6.3 Dependency on Generative Model Scale and Quality

HyDE's effectiveness is tightly coupled to the capability of the underlying instruction-following language model. The retrieval quality cannot exceed the generative model's ability to produce plausible hypothetical documents.

*   **Evidence from Ablation:** Table 4 in Section 5.1 shows a clear correlation between model size and retrieval performance. When switching from InstructGPT (175b) to FLAN-T5 (11b), the nDCG@10 on TREC DL19 drops from 61.3 to 48.9.
*   **Constraint:** This creates a barrier for deployment in resource-constrained environments. Organizations that cannot access large proprietary LLMs (like GPT-3) or cannot afford to host open-source equivalents (like a 52b Cohere model) may not achieve the same performance gains.
*   **Noise Introduction:** Section 5.2 notes that when using less powerful instruction LMs with a *fine-tuned* encoder (ContrieverFT), performance can actually degrade (e.g., 60.2 vs 62.1 on DL19 with FLAN-T5). This indicates that poor-quality hypothetical documents can introduce noise that confuses even robust encoders. HyDE is not immune to garbage-in-garbage-out dynamics; the "pivot" document must be semantically coherent enough to land in the correct embedding neighborhood.

### 6.4 Sensitivity to Instruction Specification

While HyDE is marketed as working "out-of-box," the results suggest that performance varies depending on how well the text instruction matches the target corpus style.

*   **Domain Mismatch:** In Section 4.3, the authors note that HyDE underperforms compared to ContrieverFT on **FiQA** (financial QA) and **DBPedia** (entity search). They attribute this to the "under-specification of the instruction."
*   **Why this matters:** A generic instruction like "write a passage to answer the question" may not generate text that stylistically resembles financial forum posts or encyclopedia entries. Since the contrastive encoder relies on document-document similarity, a stylistic mismatch can push the hypothetical vector away from real corpus vectors, even if the semantic topic is correct.
*   **Requirement:** This implies that for specialized domains, practitioners cannot rely solely on a generic prompt. They must engineer task-specific instructions (as seen in Appendix A.1) to guide the LLM toward the correct document style. This adds a layer of manual effort that contradicts the fully "automatic" ideal.

### 6.5 Multilingual Constraints and Encoder Saturation

HyDE faces compounded challenges in multilingual settings, as evidenced by the Mr.TyDi results in Table 3 (Section 4.4).

*   **Encoder Limitation:** The unsupervised contrastive encoder (mContriever) "gets saturated as the number of languages scales." It struggles to maintain distinct, high-quality representations for many languages simultaneously without supervision.
*   **Generator Limitation:** The instruction-following LLM (InstructGPT) is primarily optimized for English. Section 4.4 states:
    > "We hypothesize this is because the non-English languages we considered are under-trained in both pre-training and instruction learning stages."
*   **Performance Gap:** Consequently, while HyDE improves over unsupervised baselines (e.g., Swahili MRR@100 improves from 38.3 to 41.7), it still trails fine-tuned multilingual models (mContrieverFT at 51.2).
*   **Takeaway:** HyDE mitigates the lack of labeled data in low-resource languages but does not fully solve the underlying representation gap caused by limited pre-training data in those languages. It is a helpful tool for low-resource settings, but not a complete substitute for language-specific fine-tuning where data is available.

### 6.6 Open Questions and Future Directions

The paper concludes in Section 6 by raising several theoretical questions that remain unanswered, highlighting the boundaries of current understanding:

*   **Relevance as a Statistical Artifact:** The authors ask, "Is numerical relevance just a statistical artifact of language understanding?" HyDE suggests that explicit relevance scoring might be unnecessary if generation is strong enough, but this needs further verification across more diverse tasks.
*   **Weak Retriever Sufficiency:** The paper asks, "Will a weak retriever theoretically suffice as the NLU & NLG models rapidly become stronger?" If LLMs become perfect at generating hypothetical documents, the burden on the retrieval encoder decreases. This suggests a future where the encoder could be much smaller or simpler, shifting almost all intelligence to the generative component.
*   **Complex Retrieval Tasks:** The current experiments focus on single-hop retrieval (query → document). The authors express excitement about generalizing to "multi-hop retrieval/QA and conversational search." It is unclear if HyDE's single-step generation pivot is sufficient for queries requiring reasoning across multiple documents or maintaining context over a conversation history.

In summary, HyDE trades the need for labeled data for increased inference cost and dependency on large generative models. It assumes queries are unambiguous and benefits significantly from careful instruction engineering. While it excels in cold-start and low-resource scenarios, it is positioned as a complementary tool rather than a permanent replacement for supervised retrieval systems where data and compute budgets allow for fine-tuning.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The HyDE paper does more than introduce a new retrieval technique—it challenges foundational assumptions about how dense retrieval systems should be designed and deployed. This section examines how HyDE reshapes the research landscape, identifies concrete follow-up directions it enables, outlines practical deployment scenarios, and provides guidance on when to adopt this approach versus alternatives.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Retrieval Landscape

HyDE fundamentally alters three long-held assumptions in the dense retrieval community:

**Assumption 1: Query Encoders Must Be Learned Separately**

Traditional dense retrieval treats query encoding as a distinct learning problem requiring alignment with document encoders through relevance supervision (Section 3.1). HyDE demonstrates that query encoding can be **eliminated entirely** by pivoting through generated text. This shifts the research focus from "how do we learn better query encoders?" to "how do we generate better hypothetical documents?"

> "With HyDE factorization, the query-document similarity score is no longer explicitly modeled nor computed. Instead, the retrieval task is cast into two NLU and NLG tasks." (Section 3.2)

This reframing opens new research questions about the minimum capabilities required for the generative component and whether retrieval-specific encoder training remains necessary as LLMs improve.

**Assumption 2: Zero-Shot Requires Transfer from High-Resource Datasets**

Prior "zero-shot" retrieval research predominantly relies on transfer learning from MS-MARCO (Section 2). HyDE proves that **true zero-shot retrieval**—with no relevance labels at any stage—is achievable and competitive. This has immediate implications for:

- **Commercial deployment:** MS-MARCO restricts commercial use (Section 1), blocking many real-world applications. HyDE bypasses this restriction entirely.
- **Domain specialization:** Medical, legal, and enterprise search often lack large labeled datasets. HyDE enables dense retrieval in these domains without collecting relevance judgments.
- **Low-resource languages:** Table 3 shows HyDE achieving MRR@100 of 41.7 in Swahili without any Swahili relevance labels, outperforming supervised transfer models like mDPR (7.3).

**Assumption 3: Hallucination Is Catastrophic for Retrieval**

In retrieval-augmented generation, factual errors in generated text typically degrade downstream task performance. HyDE inverts this: hallucinated details are **filtered out by the dense encoder bottleneck** (Abstract, Section 3.2). This suggests that retrieval may be more robust to generative noise than previously assumed, provided the semantic structure is preserved.

> "The encoder's dense bottleneck filtering out the incorrect details... grounds the generated document to the actual corpus." (Abstract)

This insight could influence how future systems integrate LLMs with retrieval components—perhaps prioritizing semantic coherence over factual precision in certain pipeline stages.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly and implicitly identifies several research avenues that build on HyDE's foundation:

#### 7.2.1 Handling Ambiguous Queries

Section 3.2 explicitly flags this as future work:

> "We simply consider the expectation value, assuming the distribution of $v_{q_{ij}}$ is uni-modal, i.e. the query is not ambiguous. The study of ambiguous queries and diversity is left to future work."

Current HyDE averages multiple generated documents into a single vector (Equation 7). For ambiguous queries (e.g., "Java" meaning programming language vs. island), this averaging may produce a vector falling **between clusters** in the embedding space, retrieving irrelevant documents for both intents.

**Potential Solutions:**
- **Cluster-based retrieval:** Generate multiple hypothetical documents, cluster their embeddings, and retrieve separately for each cluster center.
- **Query clarification:** Use the LLM to detect ambiguity and prompt the user for disambiguation before retrieval.
- **Multi-vector representations:** Instead of averaging, maintain multiple query vectors and merge results post-retrieval.

This direction is critical for production deployment where ambiguous queries are common.

#### 7.2.2 Multi-Hop and Conversational Retrieval

Section 6 expresses interest in generalizing HyDE to more complex tasks:

> "We are excited to see how this can be generalized further to more sophisticated tasks like multi-hop retrieval/QA and conversational search."

**Multi-hop retrieval** requires reasoning across multiple documents (e.g., "Who directed the movie that won Best Picture the year after Jaws?"). Current HyDE generates a single hypothetical document per query, which may not capture the multi-step reasoning required.

**Conversational search** involves maintaining context across multiple turns. HyDE would need to incorporate conversation history into the instruction, potentially requiring modifications to how hypothetical documents are generated.

**Research Questions:**
- Can HyDE generate hypothetical documents that explicitly reference multiple source documents?
- Should the instruction include conversation history, and if so, how much?
- Does multi-hop retrieval require multiple generation passes or a single complex generation?

#### 7.2.3 Instruction Engineering and Domain Adaptation

Table 2 reveals that HyDE underperforms ContrieverFT on FiQA (financial QA) and DBPedia (entity search). Section 4.3 attributes this to "under-specification of the instruction."

This suggests a research direction in **systematic instruction engineering**:

- **Instruction templates:** Develop reusable instruction templates for common domain types (scientific, legal, financial, news).
- **Automatic instruction optimization:** Use feedback signals (e.g., click-through rates) to iteratively refine instructions without relevance labels.
- **Style matching:** Ensure generated hypothetical documents match the linguistic style of the target corpus, not just the semantic topic.

The Appendix A.1 instructions show task-specific variations exist, but the paper does not explore how sensitive performance is to instruction phrasing. This is a practical gap for deployment.

#### 7.2.4 Reducing Inference Cost Through Distillation

Section 6 positions HyDE for the "cold start" phase, with supervised models rolled out as search logs grow:

> "As the search log grows, a supervised dense retriever can be gradually rolled out. As the dense retriever grows stronger, more queries will be routed to it, with only less common and emerging ones going to HyDE backend."

This suggests a **distillation pipeline**:

1. Use HyDE to collect initial retrieval results and user interactions.
2. Train a smaller dual-encoder model on the implicit feedback.
3. Route queries to the cheaper encoder, falling back to HyDE for edge cases.

**Research Questions:**
- How many queries are needed to train a competitive distilled encoder?
- Can HyDE-generated hypothetical documents themselves serve as training data for encoder distillation?
- What is the optimal routing strategy between HyDE and distilled models?

This direction addresses the latency and cost concerns noted in Section 6.

#### 7.2.5 Smaller LLMs with Better Instructions

Table 4 shows clear scale dependency: FLAN-T5 (11b) achieves nDCG@10 of 48.9 on DL19, while GPT-3 (175b) achieves 61.3. However, all tested LLMs improve over the Contriever baseline.

**Research Direction:** Can better instruction engineering or fine-tuning close the gap for smaller models?

- **Instruction fine-tuning:** Fine-tune smaller LLMs specifically on hypothetical document generation tasks.
- **Retrieval-aware prompting:** Develop prompting techniques that explicitly guide the LLM toward embedding-friendly text structures.
- **Hybrid architectures:** Combine smaller LLMs with retrieval feedback loops to iteratively refine generated documents.

This is critical for organizations that cannot access or afford 175b parameter models.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

HyDE is not universally optimal, but it excels in specific deployment scenarios. Below we outline concrete use cases where HyDE provides clear advantages.

#### 7.3.1 Cold-Start Search Systems

**Scenario:** A company launches a new internal search system with no historical query logs or relevance judgments.

**Why HyDE:** 
- Provides dense retrieval performance comparable to fine-tuned models (Table 1 shows HyDE matching ContrieverFT on DL19 MAP: 41.8 vs 41.7) without any training data.
- Enables immediate deployment while user interaction data is collected.
- Can be gradually replaced by supervised models as logs accumulate (Section 6).

**Integration Pattern:**
```
Phase 1 (Weeks 1-4): 100% HyDE routing
Phase 2 (Weeks 5-12): 80% HyDE, 20% distilled encoder
Phase 3 (Month 4+): 20% HyDE (edge cases), 80% distilled encoder
```

#### 7.3.2 Commercial Products with Licensing Restrictions

**Scenario:** A commercial search product cannot use MS-MARCO due to licensing restrictions (Section 1 explicitly notes this constraint).

**Why HyDE:**
- Requires no relevance labels, bypassing MS-MARCO dependency entirely.
- Outperforms BM25 on most tasks (Table 1, Table 2), providing semantic search capabilities without labeled data.
- Works across domains without domain-specific fine-tuning.

**Consideration:** The inference cost of running InstructGPT must be factored into pricing models. For high-volume applications, distillation (Section 7.2.4) becomes essential.

#### 7.3.3 Low-Resource Language Deployment

**Scenario:** A search system needs to support languages like Swahili, Bengali, or Korean where labeled retrieval datasets do not exist.

**Why HyDE:**
- Table 3 shows HyDE achieving MRR@100 of 41.7 in Swahili, outperforming mContriever (38.3) and mDPR (7.3).
- No language-specific relevance labels required—only the instruction needs translation.
- Leverages multilingual LLM capabilities that exist even for lower-resource languages.

**Limitation:** Performance still trails fine-tuned mContrieverFT (51.2 in Swahili). If labeled data becomes available, fine-tuning remains superior. HyDE is best when labeled data is **unavailable**, not just scarce.

#### 7.3.4 Specialized Domain Search

**Scenario:** Biomedical, legal, or enterprise search where domain expertise is required for relevance labeling.

**Why HyDE:**
- Table 2 shows strong performance on SciFact (nDCG@10: 69.1) and TREC-Covid (59.3) without domain-specific training.
- Instructions can be tailored to domain document styles (Appendix A.1 shows scientific paper passage instructions for SciFact and TREC-Covid).
- Avoids the cost and time of recruiting domain experts for relevance labeling.

**Caveat:** FiQA and DBPedia results (Section 4.3) show that generic instructions may underperform for specialized formats. Domain-specific instruction engineering is required.

### 7.5 Theoretical Questions Raised

Section 6 concludes by raising fundamental questions that extend beyond HyDE itself:

**Question 1: "Is numerical relevance just a statistical artifact of language understanding?"**

HyDE suggests that explicit relevance scoring may be unnecessary if generation is strong enough. If LLMs can generate text that occupies the correct embedding neighborhood, do we need to learn relevance scores at all? This challenges the foundation of learning-to-rank research.

**Question 2: "Will a weak retriever theoretically suffice as the NLU & NLG models rapidly become stronger?"**

If LLMs become perfect at generating hypothetical documents, the retrieval encoder's role diminishes. Future systems might use much smaller encoders, shifting intelligence to the generative component. This has implications for model architecture design and compute allocation.

**Question 3: How does this generalize to multi-hop and conversational settings?**

Current HyDE handles single-turn, single-hop retrieval. Extending to multi-hop reasoning or conversation history requires new generation strategies. This is an open research frontier that the paper explicitly identifies.

These questions suggest that HyDE is not an endpoint but a **pivot point** for rethinking retrieval architecture. The paper's contribution lies not just in the method itself, but in the research directions it opens for the broader community.

### 7.6 Summary: Positioning HyDE in the Retrieval Ecosystem

HyDE occupies a specific niche in the retrieval landscape:

- **Not a replacement** for supervised dense retrieval where labeled data and compute budgets allow fine-tuning.
- **Not a replacement** for BM25 in domains where lexical matching dominates (e.g., TREC-Covid where BM25 edges HyDE by 0.2 nDCG@10 in Table 2).
- **A bridge solution** for cold-start scenarios, licensing-restricted deployments, and low-resource languages.
- **A research catalyst** that challenges assumptions about query encoding, relevance modeling, and the role of generation in retrieval.

The paper's lasting impact may be less about HyDE itself and more about demonstrating that **problem decomposition**—splitting retrieval into generation and encoding—can achieve competitive results without supervision. This paradigm may inspire future work that similarly rethinks traditional retrieval components through the lens of modern LLM capabilities.
