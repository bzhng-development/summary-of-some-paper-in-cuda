## 1. Executive Summary

This work introduces a data distribution lens and `DataAlchemy`, a controllable environment for training LLMs from scratch (62K to 14B parameters), to demonstrate that Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning effectiveness is fundamentally governed by distribution discrepancy rather than model scale. Rigorous experiments reveal CoT performance collapses from 100% exact match on in-distribution data to 0% under moderate task, length, or format shifts, even in SOTA models like LLaMA3-8B and Qwen3-14B. These findings challenge the notion of CoT as genuine logical inference, characterizing it instead as a brittle mirage of memorized patterns that fails when pushed beyond training distributions.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Understanding When CoT Reasoning Actually Works

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting emerged as a breakthrough technique for eliciting structured reasoning from large language models. By simply appending cues like "Let's think step by step" to prompts, researchers observed that LLMs could decompose complex problems into intermediate steps, producing outputs resembling human-like reasoning chains. This capability appeared across diverse domains: logical inference, mathematical problem solving, and commonsense reasoning tasks. The empirical success was so compelling that CoT reasoning became viewed as a promising pathway toward artificial general intelligence.

However, a fundamental question remains unanswered: **Why does CoT reasoning succeed in some cases but fail in others?**

This paper addresses a critical gap in our understanding. While numerous studies have documented CoT failures, the field lacks a unified framework to explain *when* and *why* these failures occur. Without such understanding, practitioners cannot reliably predict whether CoT will work for their specific use case, and researchers cannot systematically improve reasoning capabilities.

### Why This Problem Matters: Both Theoretical and Practical Stakes

The importance of this question extends beyond academic curiosity into real-world deployment concerns:

**Theoretical Significance:** If CoT reasoning reflects genuine logical inference capabilities, then scaling models and improving training methods should yield progressively more robust reasoning. However, if CoT is merely pattern interpolation from training data, then fundamental architectural or training paradigm changes are needed to achieve true reasoning. This distinction shapes the entire research agenda for advancing LLM capabilities.

**Real-World Impact:** The paper's ethical considerations section explicitly warns about high-stakes applications in education, healthcare, law, and scientific decision-making. When an LLM produces fluent, step-by-step reasoning that appears coherent, users naturally develop trust in the output. However, if this reasoning is a "mirage" that collapses under modest distribution shifts, the consequences could be severe. A medical diagnosis system that produces plausible-looking reasoning chains for patients outside its training distribution could cause real harm while appearing authoritative.

**The False Confidence Problem:** As the authors note, "fluent nonsense" — plausible but logically flawed reasoning chains — can be more deceptive than outright incorrect answers because they project a false aura of dependability. Understanding the boundaries of CoT reliability is therefore essential for responsible deployment.

### Limitations of Prior Evaluation Approaches

Before this work, researchers attempted to understand CoT failures through several approaches, each with significant limitations:

**1. Narrowly Defined Settings:** Existing frameworks typically focus on specific tasks (e.g., mathematical reasoning only) evaluated using specific LLMs. This approach overlooks common structural patterns that might generalize across tasks and models. For instance, a study might show CoT fails on planning tasks but succeed on arithmetic, without identifying whether both failures stem from the same underlying mechanism.

**2. Data Entanglement:** Most evaluations occur in real-world scenarios where complexity precludes fully controlled experiments. When testing on natural language benchmarks, multiple factors vary simultaneously — task structure, text length, formatting, semantic content — making it impossible to isolate which factor caused failure. This confounding prevents precise diagnosis of CoT's limitations.

**3. Data Leakage:** Perhaps most critically, pre-trained LLMs suffer from benchmark contamination. Modern models are trained on vast internet corpora that often include test benchmarks, undermining evaluation validity. When a model performs well on a reasoning task, researchers cannot determine whether this reflects genuine reasoning capability or memorization of training examples. As the paper states, this problem "undermines the effectiveness and validity of evaluations."

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

This paper positions itself as providing the systematic framework that prior work lacks. The authors acknowledge a growing body of research questioning CoT's robustness:

- **Fragility Studies:** Work by Mirzadeh et al. (2025) and Tang et al. (2023) demonstrated that semantically irrelevant perturbations — distractor phrases or altered symbolic representations — can substantially degrade CoT performance.
- **Faithfulness Concerns:** Intervention-based analyses by Lanham et al. (2023) showed that final answers often remain unchanged when intermediate reasoning steps are corrupted or removed, a phenomenon termed the "illusion of transparency."
- **Scaling Limitations:** Shojaee et al. (2025) found that reasoning performance scales poorly with task difficulty, with models over-elaborating on simple problems while failing on harder ones.

However, these studies share the evaluation limitations described above. They document *that* CoT fails but cannot systematically explain *why* across different conditions.

**The Paper's Distinctive Contribution:** Rather than adding another empirical observation of CoT failure, this work proposes a **data distribution lens** as a unifying theoretical framework. The central hypothesis is that CoT reasoning reflects a structured inductive bias learned from in-distribution data, enabling models to conditionally generate reasoning trajectories that approximate those observed during training. Effectiveness is therefore "fundamentally governed by the nature and degree of distribution discrepancy between training data and test queries."

To test this hypothesis rigorously, the authors introduce **DataAlchemy** — an abstract, fully controllable environment that addresses all three limitations of prior evaluation approaches:

| Limitation | Prior Approaches | DataAlchemy Solution |
|------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Narrow settings | Task-specific benchmarks | Abstract representation system distilling NLP tasks into atoms, elements, and transformations |
| Data entanglement | Real-world complexity | Full control over task, length, and format dimensions independently |
| Data leakage | Pre-trained models | Training LLMs from scratch (62K to 3B parameters) on synthetic data |

This methodological innovation enables the paper to move beyond documenting failures toward establishing causal relationships between distribution discrepancy and CoT performance. The framework then validates findings on state-of-the-art models (LLaMA3-8B and Qwen3-14B) through fine-tuning experiments, bridging the gap between controlled synthetic environments and real-world LLM behavior.

By dissecting CoT reasoning along three specific dimensions — **task** (unseen task structures), **length** (different text and reasoning lengths), and **format** (query format variants) — the paper provides a structured vocabulary for discussing distribution shifts that prior work lacked. This positioning allows the authors to make stronger claims about the fundamental nature of CoT reasoning rather than cataloging isolated failure modes.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Theoretical Foundation: The Data Distribution Lens**

This paper is fundamentally a theoretical-experimental hybrid that proposes a new lens for understanding CoT reasoning failures rather than merely documenting them. The central theoretical claim is that CoT reasoning reflects a structured inductive bias learned from in-distribution data, enabling models to conditionally generate reasoning trajectories that approximate those observed during training. To formalize this view, the authors introduce notation for training and test distributions where $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ denotes the training distribution over input-output pairs $(x, y)$, with $x$ representing a reasoning problem and $y$ denoting the solution sequence including intermediate reasoning traces. During training, the model learns a parametric mapping $f_\theta(x) \approx y$ by minimizing the empirical training risk:

$$
\hat{R}_{\text{train}}(f_\theta) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \ell(f_\theta(x_i), y_i)
$$

where $(x_i, y_i) \sim \mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ are independent and identically distributed samples and $\ell$ is a loss function such as cross-entropy. The corresponding expected population training risk is defined as $R_{\text{train}}(f_\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}}[\ell(f_\theta(x), y)]$. At inference time, given a test query sampled from a potentially different distribution $\mathcal{D}_{\text{test}}$, the model generates a response with expected test risk $R_{\text{test}}(f_\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}}}[\ell(f_\theta(x), y)]$.

The key theoretical contribution is Definition 3.1, which defines distribution discrepancy as $\Delta(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}}) := \text{TV}(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}})$, where $\text{TV}(P, Q)$ is the total variation distance computed as $\text{TV}(P, Q) := \sup_A |P(A) - Q(A)| = \frac{1}{2} \int |dP - dQ|$. This mathematical formulation captures how different the test distribution is from the training distribution in a rigorous, measurable way. Building on this definition, Theorem 3.1 provides a Generalization Bound stating that if the loss is bounded (i.e., for all $(x, y)$, $0 \leq \ell(f_\theta(x), y) \leq B$), then for any $\delta \in (0, 1)$, with probability at least $1 - \delta$ over the draw of the training sample, the expected test risk satisfies:

$$
R_{\text{test}}(f_\theta) \leq \hat{R}_{\text{train}}(f_\theta) + 2B\Delta(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}}) + B\sqrt{\frac{\log(1/\delta)}{2n}}
$$

In plain language, this theorem states that test performance is bounded by three terms: the empirical training error, a term proportional to the distribution discrepancy, and a term that decreases with more training samples. The critical insight is that the distribution discrepancy term $\Delta(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}})$ directly governs how well the model generalizes, independent of model architecture or scale. The authors decompose this discrepancy into three components: $\Delta(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}}) = \Phi(\Delta_{\text{task}}, \Delta_{\text{length}}, \Delta_{\text{format}})$, where $\Phi$ is a monotonically increasing composition function that aggregates all discrepancies, and each $\Delta$ term measures distribution discrepancy introduced by unseen tasks, various lengths, and prompt format variants respectively.

**DataAlchemy: The Controllable Environment**

To empirically validate the data distribution lens, the authors introduce DataAlchemy, an abstract, controllable, and clean environment that distills real-world NLP tasks into basic components. The design choice to use an abstract symbolic system rather than natural language benchmarks addresses all three limitations of prior evaluation approaches: it avoids narrowly defined settings by providing a unified representation, eliminates data entanglement through full experimental control, and prevents data leakage by training models from scratch on synthetic data. The environment consists of three hierarchical layers that map to different levels of language abstraction.

At the lowest level are **basic atoms**, which abstract tokens in real-world NLP tasks into an alphabet of 26 letters $\mathcal{A} = \{A, B, C, \ldots, Z\}$. This choice of 26 atoms provides sufficient complexity to construct diverse elements while remaining simple enough for complete experimental control and interpretability. Based on atoms, the authors construct **elements** $e$ as ordered sequences of atoms with length $l$, reflecting the text space since text consists of tokens. Formally, an element is defined as $e = (a_0, a_1, \ldots, a_{l-1})$ where $a_i \in \mathcal{A}$ and $l \in \mathbb{Z}^+$. The paper notes that one can construct at most $|\mathcal{A}|^l$ distinct elements, which for length $l=4$ yields $26^4 = 456,976$ possible elements and for $l=6$ yields $26^6 = 308,915,776$ possible elements, providing a versatile approach for data curation by manipulating element length.

At the next level are **transformations**, which abstract operations that LLMs perform on text in the real world (e.g., summarize, paraphrase, reasoning) as functions that operate on elements $F: e \rightarrow \hat{e}$. The paper instantiates two fundamental transformations to avoid bias while maintaining experimental tractability. The first is the **ROT Transformation** (Definition 4.1), which takes an element $e = (a_0, \ldots, a_{l-1})$ and a rotation parameter $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, producing an element $\hat{e} = (\hat{a}_0, \ldots, \hat{a}_{l-1})$ where each atom $\hat{a}_i = \phi^{-1}((\phi(a_i) + n) \pmod{26})$. Here, $\phi: \mathcal{A} \rightarrow \mathbb{Z}_{26}$ is a bijective mapping where $\mathbb{Z}_{26} = \{0, 1, \ldots, 25\}$, such that $\phi(c)$ maps a character to its zero-based alphabetical index. This operation cyclically shifts each atom $n$ positions forward in alphabetical order. For example, if $e = (A, P, P, L, E)$ and $n = 13$, then $f_{\text{rot}}(e, 13) = (N, C, C, Y, R)$, which corresponds to the familiar ROT-13 cipher.

The second fundamental transformation is the **Cyclic Position Shift** (Definition 4.2), which takes an element $e = (a_0, \ldots, a_{l-1})$ and a shift parameter $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, producing an element $\hat{e} = (\hat{a}_0, \ldots, \hat{a}_{l-1})$ where each atom $\hat{a}_i = a_{(i-n) \pmod{l}}$. This transformation cyclically shifts the positions of the atoms within the sequence by $n$ positions to the right. For instance, if $e = (A, P, P, L, E)$ and $n = 1$, then $f_{\text{pos}}(e, 1) = (E, A, P, P, L)$. The paper notes that additional transformations are considered in Appendix F.1 to avoid bias, including a sequence reversal operator $f_3$ that reverses the element order.

To model multi-step reasoning, the authors define a **Generalized Compositional Transformation** (Definition 4.3) as the successive application of a sequence of operations. Let $S = (f_1, f_2, \ldots, f_k)$ be a sequence of operations, where each $f_i$ is one of the fundamental transformations $\mathcal{F} = \{f_{\text{rot}}, f_{\text{pos}}\}$ with its respective parameters. The compositional transformation $f_S$ for the sequence $S$ is the function composition $f_S = f_1 \circ f_2 \circ \cdots \circ f_k$. The resulting element $\hat{e}$ is obtained by applying the operations sequentially to an initial element $e$: $\hat{e} = f_k(f_{k-1}(\ldots(f_1(e))\ldots))$. This design enables the construction of arbitrary transformations with control over type, parameters, order, and length. Critically, the authors note that one can naturally acquire the CoT reasoning step by decomposing the intermediate process:

$$
f_S(e): \underbrace{e \xrightarrow{f_1} e^{(1)} \xrightarrow{f_2} e^{(2)} \cdots \xrightarrow{f_{k-1}} e^{(k-1)} \xrightarrow{f_k} \hat{e}}_{\text{Reasoning traces}}
$$

where the query is $f_S(e)$, the intermediate elements $e^{(1)}, \ldots, e^{(k-1)}$ represent reasoning traces, and $\hat{e}$ is the final answer. This structure mirrors how CoT prompting works in practice, where models produce intermediate reasoning steps before arriving at a final answer.

**Three Generalization Dimensions**

Guided by the data distribution lens, the paper dissects CoT reasoning via three dimensions along which distribution shifts may occur. For **task generalization**, the authors consider two sub-dimensions: transformation generalization and element generalization. Transformation generalization uses four progressive scenarios to formulate different distribution discrepancies. The In-Distribution (ID) scenario has test transformations identical to those observed during training, e.g., $f_1 \circ f_1 \rightarrow f_1 \circ f_1$. The Composition (CMP) scenario has test samples comprising novel compositions where basic transformations are observed during training, e.g., $\{f_1 \circ f_1, f_1 \circ f_2, f_2 \circ f_1\} \rightarrow f_2 \circ f_2$. The Partial Out-of-Distribution (POOD) scenario has test queries including compositions involving both seen and unseen basic transformations, e.g., $f_1 \circ f_1 \rightarrow f_1 \circ f_2$. The Out-of-Distribution (OOD) scenario has the test set containing entirely novel transformations not seen in training, e.g., $f_1 \circ f_1 \rightarrow f_2 \circ f_2$. Element generalization follows a similar pipeline, testing whether CoT reasoning can handle novel combinations of atoms when seeing all basic atoms in elements (e.g., $(A, B, C, D) \rightarrow (B, C, D, A)$) versus entirely unseen atoms.

For **length generalization**, the authors formulate two perspectives: text length generalization and reasoning step generalization. Text length distribution discrepancy is measured by element length difference, where models are trained on datasets with text length $l=4$ while fixing other factors and evaluated on a variety of lengths from $l=2$ to $l=6$. Reasoning steps are determined by the number of basic transformations $k$ in the compositional transformation. The authors mix data with various reasoning steps (e.g., $k=1, 2, 3$) and adjust the mix ratio while maintaining data size to create different distribution discrepancies. This allows them to test whether models trained on two-step reasoning can generalize to one-step or three-step reasoning.

For **format generalization**, the authors introduce four distinct perturbation modes to simulate real-world scenarios where surface-level variations appear in test queries. The Insert mode adds one noise token to the input. The Delete mode removes one original token. The Modify mode replaces one original token with a noise token. The Hybrid mode combines the above perturbation methods. These perturbations are applied with noise level $p$ on different areas of test queries including elements, transformations, and prompts. This tests whether CoT reasoning is robust to superficial changes that should not affect the underlying logical structure of the task.

**Model Training Configurations and Hyperparameters**

The paper employs a comprehensive range of model architectures and sizes to test whether the data distribution lens holds across different model configurations. For controlled experiments training from scratch, the authors employ decoder-only LLMs with GPT and LLaMA architectures and parameter sizes ranging from 62K to 3B. Table 3 in Appendix D provides detailed hyperparameter settings: GPT models include 68K (4 layers, 4 heads, hidden size 32), 589K (7 layers, 8 heads, hidden size 80), 4.8M (6 layers, 4 heads, hidden size 256), 35M (11 layers, 8 heads, hidden size 512), 540M (19 layers, 24 heads, hidden size 1536), and 3B (26 layers, 32 heads, hidden size 3072) variants. LLaMA models include 62K (2 layers, 4 heads, hidden size 48, intermediate size 128), 631K (8 layers, 4 heads, hidden size 80, intermediate size 216), 6M (6 layers, 6 heads, hidden size 288, intermediate size 768), 60M (12 layers, 10 heads, hidden size 640, intermediate size 1728), 623M (22 layers, 12 heads, hidden size 1536, intermediate size 4096), and 3B (26 layers, 24 heads, hidden size 3072, intermediate size 8192) variants.

For LLMs trained from scratch, the authors employ the AdamW optimizer in mixed precision (FP16). The default learning rate is $3 \times 10^{-3}$, and the schedule follows a cosine decay with a 10% warm-up ratio. Training is conducted using a batch size of 1024, and each model is optimized for 10 epochs. A weight decay of 0.01 is applied, and gradient norms are clipped at 1.0. During inference time, the temperature is set to $1 \times 10^{-5}$ to ensure deterministic outputs for evaluation. For fine-tuning state-of-the-art LLMs, the authors use a per-device batch size of 16 with 8 gradient accumulation steps (effective batch size of 128) and train for 24K optimization steps. The learning rate is set to $1 \times 10^{-4}$ with a cosine learning-rate schedule and 10% warm-up ratio. All fine-tuning experiments are conducted with bfloat16 (bf16) mixed-precision training. The authors note that given the scale of the data, results of controlled experiments are averaged over three independent runs to ensure statistical reliability.

**Evaluation Metrics and Measurement Approach**

The paper constructs elements with 2 to 6 basic atoms, which produce 676 to 308,915,776 data samples, providing sufficient scale for rigorous evaluation. The authors consider both hard metrics and soft metrics for evaluation. The hard metric is exact match rate, which measures whether the model output exactly matches the ground truth. The soft metrics include Levenshtein distance (edit distance), which measures the minimum number of single-character edits (insertions, deletions, or substitutions) required to change one string into another, and BLEU score, which measures n-gram overlap between model output and reference text. To enable fine-grained analysis, the authors evaluate reasoning traces, the final answer, and the full chain in the LLM response separately. This decomposition is critical because it allows the authors to detect cases where models produce correct reasoning paths but incorrect answers (or vice versa), revealing whether CoT reasoning is faithful to the actual computation or merely decorative.

**External Validation Strategy**

To establish external validity beyond the controlled DataAlchemy environment, the authors fine-tune two state-of-the-art LLMs: LLaMA3-8B-Instruct and Qwen3-14B. The key challenge for external validity is that the training data distributions of commercial or large proprietary models remain opaque, making direct estimation of distribution discrepancy between pretraining data and test queries inherently challenging. The authors address this by curating data unseen during training and using it to fine-tune the SOTA models. By interfering with data generated by DataAlchemy, where LLMs would produce totally random answers without fine-tuning, the authors can confirm that any performance comes from the fine-tuning rather than pretraining contamination. The results show that SOTA LLMs exhibit similar trends to models trained from scratch across task, length, and format generalization, indicating the external validity of the distribution lens despite the opacity of their original training data.

**Design Choices and Alternatives Considered**

The choice to use a symbolic abstract environment rather than natural language benchmarks represents a deliberate trade-off between ecological validity and experimental control. The authors acknowledge in their limitations section that DataAlchemy "may inevitably not fully capture the semantic richness, ambiguity, and compositional diversity present in natural language." However, this abstraction enables full and fine-grained control over distribution factors and avoids data leakage, which the authors prioritize for establishing causal relationships. The choice of 26 atoms (the alphabet) provides sufficient complexity for meaningful experiments while remaining interpretable for qualitative analysis of failure modes. The choice of ROT and Cyclic Position Shift as fundamental transformations provides orthogonal operations (one operates on atom values, one operates on positions) that can be composed to create complex reasoning tasks without introducing semantic confounds.

The decision to train models from scratch rather than use pre-trained models for the primary experiments directly addresses the data leakage problem that undermines prior CoT evaluation. When using pre-trained models, researchers cannot determine whether good performance reflects genuine reasoning capability or memorization of training examples that overlap with test benchmarks. By training from scratch on synthetic data with known distributions, the authors can precisely measure the distribution discrepancy between training and test data. The subsequent fine-tuning experiments on SOTA models then validate whether findings from the controlled environment generalize to real-world LLMs, bridging the gap between scientific rigor and practical relevance.

The choice to evaluate reasoning traces separately from final answers is a critical methodological decision that enables the authors to detect unfaithful reasoning. Prior work has documented the "illusion of transparency" where final answers remain unchanged when intermediate steps are corrupted, but this paper's controlled environment allows precise measurement of when reasoning traces diverge from correct computation paths. This granularity reveals that models often produce fluent but logically inconsistent reasoning steps, supporting the central claim that CoT reasoning can be a "mirage" rather than genuine inference.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper delivers contributions that extend beyond incremental empirical observations to fundamentally reshape how we understand Chain-of-Thought reasoning. Below, I distinguish between the foundational innovations that enable new research directions and the empirical insights that challenge prevailing assumptions about LLM capabilities.

### 4.1 The Data Distribution Lens: A Unifying Theoretical Framework

**What Makes This Different:** Prior work documented *that* CoT fails under various conditions — planning tasks (Stechly et al., 2024), puzzle complexity thresholds (Shojaee et al., 2025), or semantic perturbations (Mirzadeh et al., 2025) — but offered no unified explanation for *why* these failures occur. Each study treated failures as isolated phenomena specific to their task domain. This paper introduces the first theoretical framework that connects all these failure modes through a single mechanism: distribution discrepancy.

**The Core Innovation:** The paper formalizes CoT effectiveness as a function of $\Delta(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}})$, decomposing it into three measurable dimensions ($\Delta_{\text{task}}$, $\Delta_{\text{length}}$, $\Delta_{\text{format}}$). This is not merely a categorization scheme but a theoretically-grounded lens derived from Theorem 3.1's generalization bound, which proves that test risk is directly bounded by distribution discrepancy independent of model architecture.

**Why This Matters:** This framework transforms CoT evaluation from anecdotal failure documentation into systematic diagnosis. Practitioners can now ask: "What is the distribution discrepancy between my training data and deployment scenarios?" rather than "Will CoT work for my task?" The theoretical foundation means findings should generalize beyond the specific experimental setup, providing a principled basis for predicting CoT reliability in new domains.

**Fundamental vs. Incremental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in how the field conceptualizes reasoning. It shifts the research question from "How do we make CoT better?" to "Under what distributional conditions does CoT generalize?" — a qualitatively different framing that redirects the entire research agenda.

### 4.2 DataAlchemy: Solving the Three-Way Evaluation Trilemma

**What Makes This Different:** Previous evaluation approaches faced an impossible trade-off, illustrated in Figure 10's comparison. Real-world benchmarks offered ecological validity but suffered from data entanglement (multiple varying factors) and data leakage (benchmark contamination in pretraining corpora). Synthetic tasks offered control but lacked generality. No prior framework simultaneously achieved all three criteria: abstract representation, full experimental control, and training from scratch.

**The Core Innovation:** DataAlchemy distills NLP tasks into atoms (26-letter alphabet), elements (ordered sequences), and transformations (ROT, Cyclic Shift), enabling researchers to:
- Manipulate task, length, and format dimensions **independently** rather than having them confounded
- Train models from scratch on known distributions, eliminating leakage concerns
- Scale experiments across 62K to 14B parameters while maintaining complete control over training data

**Why This Matters:** The environment reveals failure modes that real-world evaluations cannot isolate. For instance, Table 1 shows exact match dropping from 100% (ID) to 0.01% (CMP) to 0% (POOD/OOD) — a gradient of failure that would be invisible in natural language benchmarks where task structure, length, and formatting vary simultaneously. This precision enables causal claims rather than correlational observations.

**Fundamental vs. Incremental:** This is a **fundamental methodological innovation**. DataAlchemy is positioned as a research platform analogous to ImageNet for vision or GLUE for NLP, but with the critical distinction of full controllability. The paper explicitly states it "can serve as a platform where researchers can rigorously explore the nature of LLMs, inspiring the discovery of universal principles." This elevates it from an experimental tool to a community infrastructure contribution.

### 4.3 CoT as Pattern Interpolation, Not Logical Inference

**What Makes This Different:** The debate over whether CoT reflects "genuine reasoning" versus "pattern matching" existed prior to this work, but evidence was indirect — intervention studies showing answers persist when reasoning is corrupted (Lanham et al., 2023), or performance drops under perturbations (Tang et al., 2023). This paper provides **direct mechanistic evidence** through controlled distribution shifts.

**The Core Innovation:** The fine-grained evaluation decomposing reasoning traces from final answers reveals a critical dissociation. Table 2 shows cases where models achieve 100% reasoning accuracy but 0.01% answer accuracy (e.g., $\{f_1 \circ f_1, f_1 \circ f_2, f_2 \circ f_1\} \rightarrow f_2 \circ f_2$), and conversely, 0% reasoning accuracy with 100% answer accuracy (e.g., $f_1 \circ f_2 \rightarrow f_2 \circ f_1$). This demonstrates that reasoning traces are not causally connected to answers but are independently generated patterns.

**Why This Matters:** The "mirage" characterization has profound implications for deployment. As the ethical considerations section warns, "fluent yet logically inconsistent reasoning traces" in high-stakes domains like healthcare or law could cause harm while appearing authoritative. The paper's finding that CoT is "a brittle mirage when it is pushed beyond training distributions" means practitioners cannot trust reasoning traces as explanations for model decisions outside narrowly defined scenarios.

**Fundamental vs. Incremental:** This is a **fundamental empirical insight** that challenges the theoretical foundation of CoT prompting. If CoT reflects learned inductive biases rather than reasoning capabilities, then scaling models or adding more training data will not produce genuine reasoning — architectural or training paradigm changes are required. This redirects the research community's efforts away from incremental CoT improvements toward more foundational solutions.

### 4.4 Distribution Sensitivity Persists Across Model Scale and Architecture

**What Makes This Different:** A common counterargument to CoT limitations is that failures reflect insufficient model capacity — larger models with more training data would overcome these issues. Prior studies typically examined single model families at fixed scales, leaving this question unresolved.

**The Core Innovation:** The paper tests the data distribution lens across:
- **6 model sizes** per architecture (62K, 589K, 4.8M, 35M, 540M, 3B for GPT; 62K to 3B for LLaMA)
- **2 architectures** (GPT-style and LLaMA-style decoder-only transformers)
- **Temperature ranges** from $1 \times 10^{-5}$ to 10
- **2 SOTA models** (LLaMA3-8B-Instruct and Qwen3-14B) via fine-tuning

Figure 8 and Figure 15 show that all models exhibit the same qualitative failure pattern: strong in-distribution performance with monotonic degradation as distribution discrepancy increases. Larger models achieve higher absolute scores in ID settings but show "diminishing returns as the discrepancy increases toward POOD and OOD settings" (Appendix F.5).

**Why This Matters:** This finding eliminates model scale as a confounding variable. The brittleness of CoT is not an artifact of small models or specific architectures but reflects a fundamental property of how transformers learn from distribution-bounded training data. Table 6 confirms this extends to SOTA models: LLaMA3-8B drops from 100% (ID) to 8.52% (CMP) to 0% (POOD/OOD), while Qwen3-14B drops from 100% to 0.01% to 0%.

**Fundamental vs. Incremental:** This is a **fundamental negative result** with significant implications. It demonstrates that the CoT limitation is not solvable through the current scaling paradigm. The paper's conclusion that "distribution discrepancy—rather than model choice—is the dominant factor governing when CoT reasoning succeeds or fails" means the research community must look beyond scaling laws for solutions.

### 4.5 Supervised Fine-Tuning as Distribution Patching, Not Generalization

**What Makes This Different:** Prior work showed SFT improves task performance but did not distinguish between expanding the model's reasoning capabilities versus expanding its in-distribution boundary. This paper precisely measures the distinction.

**The Core Innovation:** Figure 4 shows that even $\lambda = 1.5 \times 10^{-4}$ (0.015%) of unseen transformation data enables rapid generalization to novel compositions. However, Appendix F.2 Figure 12b reveals that SFT on novel elements produces mismatched accuracy between reasoning steps and answers during training, suggesting the model learns surface patterns rather than underlying operations.

**Why This Matters:** This finding reframes SFT from a generalization mechanism to a "distribution patch." As the Discussion section states: "Relying on SFT to fix every OOD failure is an unsustainable and reactive strategy that fails to address the core issue: the model's lack of abstract reasoning capability." Practitioners must recognize that SFT expands the in-distribution bubble slightly rather than teaching transferable reasoning skills.

**Fundamental vs. Incremental:** This is a **fundamental clarification** of what SFT accomplishes for reasoning tasks. It prevents misinterpretation of SFT gains as evidence of improved reasoning capacity, guiding practitioners toward more sustainable solutions like OOD testing protocols rather than reactive fine-tuning cycles.

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| Data Distribution Lens | Fundamental theoretical framework | Unifies disparate CoT failure observations under single mechanism |
| DataAlchemy Environment | Fundamental methodological platform | Enables causal claims through full experimental control |
| CoT as Pattern Interpolation | Fundamental empirical insight | Challenges assumption that CoT reflects genuine reasoning |

Together, these contributions shift the field from asking "How do we improve CoT?" to "What conditions enable CoT to generalize, and what architectural changes are needed for genuine reasoning?" — a qualitatively different research agenda with implications for both scientific understanding and practical deployment.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section walks through the experimental evidence that supports the paper's central claim: CoT reasoning effectiveness is governed by distribution discrepancy, not model scale or architecture. I will examine each generalization dimension systematically, presenting specific numbers from tables and figures, then assess whether the experiments convincingly support the claims.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Data Generation**

The paper does not use traditional benchmark datasets. Instead, DataAlchemy generates synthetic data through controlled manipulation of atoms, elements, and transformations. The scale is substantial:

- Elements constructed with 2 to 6 basic atoms produce **676 to 308,915,776 data samples** (Section 4.3)
- Training uses text length $l=4$ as the baseline for length generalization experiments
- Two fundamental transformations initialized as $f_1 = f_{\text{rot}}(e, 13)$ (ROT-13) and $f_2 = f_{\text{pos}}(e, 1)$ (cyclic shift by 1)
- Additional transformation $f_3$ (sequence reversal) introduced in Appendix F.1 to test non-commutative cases

**Metrics**

The paper employs both hard and soft metrics to capture different aspects of CoT performance:

- **Exact Match Rate**: Binary metric measuring whether model output exactly matches ground truth
- **Levenshtein Distance (Edit Distance)**: Measures minimum single-character edits required to transform model output into reference
- **BLEU Score**: Measures n-gram overlap between model output and reference text

Critically, the authors evaluate **three components separately**: reasoning traces, final answer, and full chain. This decomposition enables detection of unfaithful reasoning cases where correct answers emerge from incorrect reasoning paths (or vice versa).

**Baselines and Model Configurations**

The experimental design includes multiple model families to test generality:

| Architecture | Parameter Sizes | Count |
|--------------|-----------------|-------|
| GPT-style | 68K, 589K, 4.8M, 35M, 540M, 3B | 6 models |
| LLaMA-style | 62K, 631K, 6M, 60M, 623M, 3B | 6 models |
| SOTA (fine-tuned) | LLaMA3-8B-Instruct, Qwen3-14B | 2 models |

Training from scratch uses AdamW optimizer with learning rate $3 \times 10^{-3}$, batch size 1024, 10 epochs, and inference temperature $1 \times 10^{-5}$. Fine-tuning SOTA models uses learning rate $1 \times 10^{-4}$, effective batch size 128, and 24K optimization steps (Appendix D.1).

**Experimental Setup**

The paper structures experiments around three generalization dimensions, each with progressive distribution shifts:

1. **Task Generalization**: ID → CMP → POOD → OOD (four levels of transformation novelty)
2. **Length Generalization**: Text length ($l=2$ to $l=6$) and reasoning steps ($k=1, 2, 3$)
3. **Format Generalization**: Noise levels from 0% to 30% with Insert, Delete, Modify, and Hybrid perturbations

All controlled experiments average results over **three independent runs** to ensure statistical reliability (Appendix D.1).

---

### 5.2 Task Generalization Results

**Transformation Generalization: The Core Evidence**

Table 1 presents the most direct evidence for the data distribution lens. The performance collapse is dramatic and systematic:

| Scenario | Exact Match (%) | Edit Distance | BLEU Score |
|----------|-----------------|---------------|------------|
| ID | 100.00 | 0 | 1 |
| CMP | 0.01 | 0.1326 | 0.6867 |
| POOD | 0.00 | 0.1671 | 0.4538 |
| OOD | 0.00 | 0.2997 | 0.2947 |

**Key Observation**: Performance drops from perfect (100%) to essentially zero (0.01% → 0%) with only moderate distribution shifts. The CMP scenario — where basic transformations are seen but composed novelly — already causes a **99.99% drop** in exact match. This is not a gradual degradation but a cliff.

Figure 3 visualizes this trend, showing Edit Distance increasing and BLEU Score decreasing monotonically as distribution shift increases from ID through OOD. The graph makes clear that CoT reasoning does not gracefully degrade; it collapses.

**The Reasoning-Answer Dissociation**

Table 2 provides critical mechanistic evidence that CoT reasoning is not causally connected to answers:

| Transformation (Train → Test) | Reasoning (%) | Answer (%) | Full Chain (%) |
|-------------------------------|---------------|------------|----------------|
| $\{f_1 \circ f_1, f_1 \circ f_2, f_2 \circ f_1\} \rightarrow f_2 \circ f_2$ | 100.00 | 0.01 | 0.01 |
| $\{f_1 \circ f_2, f_2 \circ f_1, f_2 \circ f_2\} \rightarrow f_1 \circ f_1$ | 100.00 | 0.01 | 0.01 |
| $f_1 \circ f_2 \rightarrow f_2 \circ f_1$ | 0.00 | 100.00 | 0.00 |
| $f_2 \circ f_1 \rightarrow f_1 \circ f_2$ | 0.00 | 100.00 | 0.00 |

**Why This Matters**: The first two rows show models producing **100% correct reasoning traces** but **0.01% correct answers**. This is impossible if reasoning traces reflect genuine computation — correct intermediate steps should lead to correct answers. The last two rows show the reverse: **0% correct reasoning** but **100% correct answers**, enabled by commutativity of transformations ($f_1 \circ f_2$ produces the same result as $f_2 \circ f_1$).

The paper explains this in Section 5.1: "When an unseen transformation $f_1 \circ f_1$ is present, LLMs attempt to generalize based on the most similar transformation (i.e., $f_1 \circ f_2$) seen during training, which leads to correct reasoning paths yet incorrect answers." This is pattern matching, not reasoning.

**Non-Commutative Validation**

Appendix Table 4 introduces transformation $f_3$ (reversal) to test non-commutative cases where accidental correct answers cannot occur:

| Transformation (Train → Test) | Reasoning (%) | Answer (%) | Full Chain (%) |
|-------------------------------|---------------|------------|----------------|
| $\{f_2 \circ f_3, f_3 \circ f_2, f_3 \circ f_3\} \rightarrow f_2 \circ f_2$ | 6.66 | 10.25 | 6.66 |
| $f_2 \circ f_3 \rightarrow f_3 \circ f_2$ | 0.00 | 0.00 | 0.00 |
| $f_3 \circ f_2 \rightarrow f_2 \circ f_3$ | 0.00 | 0.00 | 0.00 |

Appendix F.1.2 states: "Unlike earlier commutativity-induced cases—where incorrect reasoning paths could still yield correct answers—these failures demonstrate that once superficial equivalences are removed, CoT reasoning no longer exhibits any meaningful transfer." This eliminates the counterargument that commutativity artifacts explain the earlier results.

**Supervised Fine-Tuning: How Little Data Is Needed?**

Figure 4 tests whether minimal exposure to unseen transformations enables generalization:

- With SFT data ratio $\lambda = 1.5 \times 10^{-4}$ (0.015% of unseen data), models rapidly generalize to unseen transformations
- The less discrepancy between training and testing data, the easier the model generalizes

**Critical Interpretation**: This finding cuts both ways. On one hand, it shows models can adapt quickly. On the other hand, as the Discussion section notes, this means SFT is "patching" the distribution boundary rather than teaching transferable reasoning. The model expands its in-distribution bubble slightly but does not acquire abstract reasoning capability.

---

### 5.3 Length Generalization Results

**Text Length Generalization: The Fixed-Length Bias**

Table 5 shows exact match performance across different text lengths when trained on $l=4$:

| Text Length | Exact Match (%) | Edit Distance | BLEU Score |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------|------------|
| 2 | 0.00 | 0.3772 | 0.4214 |
| 3 | 0.00 | 0.2221 | 0.5471 |
| 4 (training) | 100.00 | 0.0000 | 1.0000 |
| 5 | 0.00 | 0.1818 | 0.6220 |
| 6 | 0.00 | 0.3294 | 0.4763 |

**Stark Finding**: Even mild distribution shifts ($l=3$ or $l=5$, just one token different from training) cause **complete failure** (0% exact match). This is not extrapolation failure at extreme lengths — it is failure at adjacent lengths.

Figure 5 visualizes BLEU Score and Edit Distance across lengths. The graph shows a sharp peak at $l=4$ with symmetric degradation in both directions, consistent with the Gaussian degradation model proposed in Proposition E.2 (Appendix E.2).

**Qualitative Failure Mode**: Appendix G.2.1 provides a concrete example where a model trained on length-4 sequences, when given a length-5 query, outputs a length-4 answer. The model "rigidly adheres to the length constraint observed during training, outputting a sequence of four atoms instead of the required five." This reveals the model learned a length-specific pattern, not a length-agnostic procedure.

**Padding Strategy Ablation**

Figure 13 (Appendix F.3) tests three padding strategies:
- **None**: No padding
- **Padding**: Pad to max context window length
- **Group**: Group text and truncate into segments

The Group strategy shows improved performance compared to Padding, suggesting that how length is represented during training affects generalization. However, even the best strategy does not overcome the fundamental length-bounded nature of learned CoT.

**Reasoning Step Generalization: Depth Matters**

Figure 6 examines how mixing different reasoning depths ($k=1, 2, 3$ transformations) during training affects performance:

- When training data is 100% $k=1$, performance on $k=1$ is high but $k=2$ is near zero
- When training data is 100% $k=2$, performance on $k=2$ is high but $k=1$ and $k=3$ are near zero
- Performance systematically varies with the mix ratio

**Key Insight**: Models do not learn a general "apply transformations" procedure. They learn specific depth-bounded patterns. A model trained on two-step reasoning cannot reliably perform one-step or three-step reasoning, even though one-step reasoning is strictly simpler.

Table 7 confirms this pattern holds for SOTA models:

| Model | Reasoning Step ($k$) | Exact Match (%) |
|-------|---------------------|-----------------|
| LLaMA3-8B | 1 | 0.00 |
| LLaMA3-8B | 2 (training) | 100.00 |
| LLaMA3-8B | 3 | 0.00 |
| Qwen3-14B | 1 | 0.00 |
| Qwen3-14B | 2 (training) | 100.00 |
| Qwen3-14B | 3 | 0.26 |

Even Qwen3-14B's 0.26% at $k=3$ is negligible compared to 100% at $k=2$. This is not length-agnostic reasoning; it is template matching at a specific depth.

---

### 5.4 Format Generalization Results

**Surface Perturbations Cause Systematic Degradation**

Figure 7 shows performance across four perturbation modes (Insert, Delete, Modify, Hybrid) at varying noise levels:

- **Insert** causes the greatest performance degradation among perturbation types
- Performance degrades monotonically as noise level increases from 0% to 30%
- Perturbations applied to elements and transformations have larger effects than perturbations to other tokens

Table 8 provides precise numbers for SOTA models:

| Model | Noise Level (%) | Exact Match (%) | Edit Distance | BLEU Score |
|-------|-----------------|-----------------|---------------|------------|
| LLaMA3-8B | 0 | 100.00 | 0.00 | 1.00 |
| LLaMA3-8B | 5 | 32.30 | 0.41 | 0.04 |
| LLaMA3-8B | 10 | 17.74 | 0.45 | 0.04 |
| LLaMA3-8B | 30 | 1.09 | 0.60 | 0.03 |
| Qwen3-14B | 0 | 100.00 | 0.00 | 1.00 |
| Qwen3-14B | 5 | 9.96 | 0.22 | 0.41 |
| Qwen3-14B | 10 | 5.09 | 0.35 | 0.24 |
| Qwen3-14B | 30 | 0.22 | 0.62 | 0.06 |

**Notable Pattern**: LLaMA3-8B initially appears more robust at 5% noise (32.30% vs 9.96%), but both models converge to near-zero performance at 30% noise. Appendix F.6 notes: "The near-parallel degradation curves suggest that robustness to surface-level perturbations is constrained by distributional alignment."

**Why Format Sensitivity Matters**: Format perturbations should not affect the underlying logical structure of the task. A single inserted noise token does not change what transformation should be applied. Yet performance collapses. This indicates CoT reasoning is sensitive to surface form, not just semantic content — consistent with pattern matching rather than genuine inference.

### 5.5 Internal Validity: Does This Hold Across Model Scale?

**The Scaling Question**

A natural counterargument is that these failures reflect insufficient model capacity. Perhaps larger models would overcome distribution sensitivity. Figure 8 and Figure 15 directly test this:

- **6 GPT-style models**: 68K, 589K, 4.8M, 35M, 540M, 3B parameters
- **6 LLaMA-style models**: 62K, 631K, 6M, 60M, 623M, 3B parameters
- **Temperature range**: $1 \times 10^{-5}$ to 10 (Figure 14a)

**Consistent Finding**: All models exhibit the same qualitative failure pattern. Figure 8 shows three panels (task, length, format generalization) where every model size follows the same degradation curve. Larger models achieve higher absolute scores in ID settings but show "diminishing returns as the discrepancy increases toward POOD and OOD settings" (Appendix F.5).

**Temperature Robustness**: Figure 14a shows BLEU scores remain stable across temperatures from $1 \times 10^{-5}$ to 1.0 in CMP scenarios (0.687 across all temperatures), then drop sharply in POOD and OOD regardless of temperature. This indicates the failure is not a decoding artifact but reflects the learned representation.

**Appendix F.5 Conclusion**: "The persistence of these behaviors across settings further supports the internal validity of the data distribution lens: CoT reasoning behaves as a distribution-sensitive generative process rather than an architecture-specific reasoning mechanism."

---

### 5.6 External Validity: Do SOTA Models Show the Same Pattern?

**The Opacity Challenge**

As Section 8.2 acknowledges, external validity is challenging because "the training data distributions of commercial or large proprietary models remain uncovered." The authors cannot directly measure $\Delta(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}})$ for pre-trained models.

**The Solution**: Fine-tune SOTA models on DataAlchemy data where the training distribution is known. If SOTA models show the same sensitivity to distribution shifts despite their massive pretraining, this supports the generality of the data distribution lens.

**Results Match Controlled Experiments**

Figure 9 visualizes SOTA model performance across all three dimensions. The patterns mirror Figure 8 from models trained from scratch:

- **Task Generalization** (left panel): LLaMA3-8B and Qwen3-14B both show ID = 1.0 BLEU, dropping to ~0.6 (CMP) to ~0.3 (OOD)
- **Length Generalization** (middle panel): Both models peak at $k=2$ (training depth) with near-zero performance at $k=1$ and $k=3$
- **Format Generalization** (right panel): Both models show monotonic degradation with increasing noise

Table 6, 7, and 8 provide the exact numbers cited earlier in this section. Appendix F.6 concludes: "Despite their scale, architectural sophistication, and instruction tuning, SOTA LLMs exhibit the same qualitative behaviors as controlled models trained in DataAlchemy."

**Standard Deviations Are Small**: Table 6-8 report mean ± std values. For ID settings, standard deviation is 0.00 across all metrics, confirming deterministic mastery of in-distribution patterns. For OOD settings, standard deviations remain small (e.g., 0.00-0.01 for exact match), indicating consistently poor generalization rather than erratic behavior.

### 5.7 Failure Case Analysis

**Qualitative Evidence of Pattern Matching**

Appendix G provides concrete examples that illustrate the quantitative findings:

**Task Generalization Failure** (Appendix G.1.1):
- Query: `H U S P [F1] [F1] `
- Model Response: `U H F C [F2] <answer> H F C U`
- Ground Truth: `U H F C [F1] <answer> H U S P`

The model was trained on $f_1 \circ f_2$ composition but evaluated on $f_1 \circ f_1$. It incorrectly generates `[F2]` in the reasoning step, "mirroring the fixed pattern learned from the training data" rather than adapting to the new sequence.

**Element Generalization Failure** (Appendix G.1.2):
- Training used elements A–M only
- Test query: `N N N Q [F2] [F1] `
- Model outputs reasoning with atoms from A–M range, failing to generalize transformation $f_2$ to unseen elements N–Z

**Length Generalization Failure** (Appendix G.2.1):
- Training: length 4 sequences
- Test: length 5 query `I G L L Q [F1] [F2] `
- Model outputs 4-atom answer instead of required 5 atoms

These examples make the quantitative findings concrete: models are not learning operations that generalize; they are learning specific input-output mappings bounded by training distribution.

### 5.8 Assessment: Do Experiments Convincingly Support the Claims?

**Strengths of the Experimental Design**

1. **Causal Isolation**: By training from scratch on synthetic data, the paper eliminates data leakage and confounding variables. When performance drops from ID to OOD, the cause is unambiguously distribution shift, not benchmark contamination.

2. **Systematic Coverage**: The three-dimension framework (task, length, format) covers the major axes along which real-world distribution shifts occur. This is not cherry-picked failure modes but comprehensive probing.

3. **Scale Invariance Testing**: Testing 12 models from scratch plus 2 SOTA models across 62K to 14B parameters provides strong evidence that findings are not artifacts of small models.

4. **Fine-Grained Metrics**: Evaluating reasoning traces separately from answers enables detection of unfaithful reasoning — a critical contribution that prior work could not achieve in controlled settings.

5. **Non-Commutative Validation**: Appendix F.1.2's introduction of $f_3$ eliminates the counterargument that commutativity artifacts explain the reasoning-answer dissociation.

**Limitations and Caveats**

1. **Synthetic vs. Natural Language**: The paper acknowledges in Limitations that DataAlchemy "may inevitably not fully capture the semantic richness, ambiguity, and compositional diversity present in natural language." The observed brittleness "may manifest more stealthily, sophisticatedly, and task-dependently in more complex real-world settings."

2. **Distribution Discrepancy Measurement**: For SOTA models, the paper cannot directly measure $\Delta(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}})$ because pretraining data is opaque. The fine-tuning approach provides indirect evidence but not direct measurement.

3. **Limited Transformation Types**: Only 2-3 fundamental transformations are used (ROT, Cyclic Shift, Reversal). While Appendix F.1 notes additional transformations were considered, the space of possible operations is not exhaustively covered.

4. **Short Reasoning Chains**: Experiments use $k=1, 2, 3$ reasoning steps. Real-world CoT often involves much longer chains. The paper does not test whether longer training chains enable better extrapolation.

**Overall Assessment**

The experiments **convincingly support the core claim** that CoT reasoning effectiveness is governed by distribution discrepancy. The evidence is:

- **Consistent**: Same pattern across 14 different model configurations
- **Systematic**: Monotonic degradation with increasing distribution shift
- **Mechanistic**: Reasoning-answer dissociation demonstrates pattern matching, not inference
- **Replicable**: Small standard deviations across runs; code available on GitHub

The main limitation is ecological validity — whether these findings translate to natural language reasoning with semantic content. However, the external validity experiments with SOTA models provide strong evidence that the phenomenon extends beyond the synthetic environment.

**Conditional Findings**: The paper does not claim CoT never works. It claims CoT works when $\Delta(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}})$ is small. This is a conditional claim, and the experiments support it conditionally:
- **Condition Met** (near ID): 100% exact match across all models
- **Condition Violated** (moderate OOD): 0% exact match across all models

This conditionality is the paper's central contribution — not that CoT fails, but *when* and *why* it fails.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the assumptions, constraints, and open questions surrounding the data distribution lens and DataAlchemy framework. While the paper provides rigorous evidence for its central claims, several important limitations temper the scope of conclusions and point to directions for future work.

### 6.1 Core Assumptions Underlying the Approach

**Assumption 1: Distribution Discrepancy Is the Primary Driver of CoT Failure**

The paper's central hypothesis rests on the assumption that distribution discrepancy $\Delta(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}})$ is the dominant factor governing CoT effectiveness, rather than model architecture, scale, or training methodology. Theorem 3.1 provides theoretical support, but the bound includes three terms:

$$
R_{\text{test}}(f_\theta) \leq \hat{R}_{\text{train}}(f_\theta) + 2B\Delta(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}}) + B\sqrt{\frac{\log(1/\delta)}{2n}}
$$

The paper emphasizes the distribution discrepancy term while treating the empirical training error $\hat{R}_{\text{train}}(f_\theta)$ as effectively zero in controlled settings. However, in real-world scenarios where training error is non-zero, the relative contribution of each term may differ. The assumption that distribution discrepancy dominates may not hold when models are underfit or when training data quality varies significantly.

**Assumption 2: Symbolic Abstraction Captures Essential Reasoning Structure**

DataAlchemy distills natural language tasks into atoms, elements, and transformations. This design assumes that the symbolic structure captures the essential computational properties of reasoning without requiring semantic content. The paper acknowledges this trade-off in the Limitations section:

> "While this abstraction enables full and fine-grained control over distribution factors and avoids data leakage, it may inevitably not fully capture the semantic richness, ambiguity, and compositional diversity present in natural language."

This is a fundamental methodological trade-off: **control versus ecological validity**. The synthetic environment enables causal claims that natural language benchmarks cannot support, but findings may not fully transfer to semantically-rich tasks where meaning, context, and world knowledge interact with reasoning processes.

**Assumption 3: Reasoning Traces Are Evaluatable Separately from Answers**

The paper's fine-grained evaluation assumes that reasoning traces can be meaningfully assessed independent of final answers. This enables detection of unfaithful reasoning (correct answers from incorrect paths, or vice versa). However, this assumes a clear ground truth for what constitutes "correct" intermediate steps. In open-ended natural language reasoning, multiple valid reasoning paths may exist, making this separation less clean than in DataAlchemy's deterministic transformation chains.

### 6.2 Scenarios and Problem Settings Not Addressed

**Cross-Lingual and Multi-Modal Distribution Shifts**

The paper explicitly notes in its Limitations section that the analysis focused on three specific dimensions (task, length, format) and "did not explicitly model other forms of distribution shift, such as cross-lingual transfer, multi-modal reasoning, or shifts in cultural context." These represent significant gaps:

- **Cross-lingual transfer**: A model trained on English CoT may fail on the same logical task in another language, even with identical task structure. This introduces distribution shift at the token/atom level that DataAlchemy's 26-letter alphabet does not capture.
- **Multi-modal reasoning**: Tasks combining text with images, tables, or code introduce distribution shifts in input modality that the current framework does not address.
- **Cultural context**: Reasoning tasks embedded in different cultural contexts may share formal structure but differ in implicit assumptions, creating distribution shifts not captured by task/length/format dimensions.

**Long-Chain Reasoning Extrapolation**

The experiments test reasoning depths of $k=1, 2, 3$ transformations (Section 6.2). Real-world CoT prompting often involves significantly longer chains — mathematical proofs, code debugging, or legal analysis may require dozens of reasoning steps. The paper does not test whether:

- Training on longer chains ($k=10+$) enables better extrapolation to unseen depths
- The length generalization failure follows the same Gaussian degradation model (Proposition E.2) at larger scales
- There exists a critical chain length beyond which CoT becomes fundamentally unstable

The Limitations section acknowledges: "The observed brittleness of CoT reasoning under distribution shifts may manifest more stealthily, sophisticatedly, and task-dependently in more complex real-world settings."

**Semantic Perturbations Versus Surface Perturbations**

Format generalization experiments (Section 7) test surface-level perturbations: inserting, deleting, or modifying tokens. However, semantically meaningful perturbations — such as adding misleading context, changing problem framing, or introducing adversarial examples — may create distribution shifts that behave differently. The paper's finding that "Insert" perturbations cause the greatest degradation (Figure 7) suggests surface form matters, but does not establish how semantic content interacts with surface form in creating distribution discrepancy.

**Dynamic and Interactive Reasoning**

DataAlchemy evaluates static input-output mappings. Real-world reasoning often involves:
- **Interactive dialogue**: Multi-turn conversations where reasoning evolves based on feedback
- **Tool use**: Calling external APIs, calculators, or search engines during reasoning
- **Self-correction**: Models revising their own reasoning traces mid-generation

These dynamic settings introduce distribution shifts in the interaction pattern itself, not just the input format. The current framework does not model these scenarios.

### 6.3 Computational, Data, and Scalability Constraints

**Computational Cost of Controlled Experiments**

Appendix D.2 reports that experiments utilized "8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs (80 GB memory) and 4 NVIDIA H200 GPUs." Training hundreds of models from scratch across 12 architectures/sizes (Section 4.3), each for 10 epochs with batch size 1024, represents substantial computational investment. The paper states results are "averaged over three independent runs" for statistical reliability.

This creates a **replicability barrier**: researchers without access to similar compute resources cannot easily reproduce or extend these experiments. While the code is available on GitHub, the computational requirements limit who can validate findings or explore new distribution shift dimensions.

**Data Scale Versus Coverage Trade-off**

The paper constructs elements with 2 to 6 basic atoms, producing "676 to 308,915,776 data samples" (Section 4.3). This provides sufficient scale for rigorous evaluation within the defined space. However, the combinatorial explosion means that even 308 million samples covers only a fraction of the possible transformation compositions and element combinations.

For example, with 3 transformations ($f_1, f_2, f_3$) and compositions up to length $k=3$, there are $3^3 = 27$ possible transformation sequences. The paper tests specific subsets (ID, CMP, POOD, OOD scenarios) but cannot exhaustively cover all possible compositions. This means some distribution shift patterns may remain unexplored.

**SOTA Model Fine-Tuning Limitations**

External validity experiments fine-tune LLaMA3-8B-Instruct and Qwen3-14B (Section 8.2). However, the paper acknowledges:

> "The training data distributions of commercial or large proprietary models remain uncovered due to the opaque nature of training data and model weights. As a result, fully estimating the distribution discrepancy between pretraining data and test queries is inherently challenging."

This creates an **epistemic ceiling**: the paper cannot definitively establish how much of SOTA model performance on DataAlchemy tasks comes from pretraining versus fine-tuning. The fine-tuning approach mitigates this by using data "where LLMs produce totally random answers" without fine-tuning, but cannot eliminate uncertainty about pretraining contamination.

**Temperature and Hyperparameter Coverage**

Figure 14a tests temperatures from $1 \times 10^{-5}$ to 10, finding stability in the range $1 \times 10^{-5}$ to 1.0. However, other hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size, optimizer choice) are held relatively constant during controlled experiments (Appendix D.1: learning rate $3 \times 10^{-3}$, batch size 1024, AdamW optimizer). The paper does not systematically explore how hyperparameter choices interact with distribution shift sensitivity. Different optimization regimes might produce models with different generalization properties.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

**Weakness 1: SFT Effectiveness Is Not Fully Explained**

Figure 4 shows that $\lambda = 1.5 \times 10^{-4}$ (0.015%) of unseen data enables rapid generalization to novel transformations. Appendix F.2 Figure 12b reveals "mismatch of accuracy between the answer and the reasoning step during the training process." The paper interprets this as evidence that SFT is "patching" rather than teaching generalizable reasoning.

However, this raises an **unresolved mechanistic question**: What exactly changes in the model's representations during SFT? Does the model learn new transformation rules, or does it simply expand its pattern library? The paper documents the phenomenon but does not provide representational analysis (e.g., attention pattern changes, hidden state clustering) that would explain the mechanism.

**Weakness 2: No Positive Path to Genuine Reasoning**

The paper convincingly demonstrates that current CoT is a "mirage" bounded by training distributions. However, it does not propose or evaluate alternative approaches that might achieve genuine reasoning. The Discussion section states:

> "We believe that transparency about these limitations is essential for preventing misuse and misinterpretation of LLM-generated reasoning."

This is valuable for responsible deployment but leaves open the **constructive question**: What architectural or training changes would enable distribution-robust reasoning? The paper redirects the research agenda but does not provide a roadmap for the destination.

**Weakness 3: Binary Success/Failure May Oversimplify**

The exact match metric creates a binary assessment: 100% or 0%. Table 1 shows CMP scenario at 0.01% exact match — technically not zero, but treated as failure. This binary framing may obscure **partial generalization** phenomena where models exhibit degraded but non-zero performance on OOD tasks.

The soft metrics (Edit Distance, BLEU Score) capture gradation, but the paper's narrative emphasizes the collapse from 100% to 0%. In real-world deployment, a model that degrades gracefully (e.g., 60% accuracy on OOD vs. 100% on ID) may be more useful than the binary framing suggests.

**Weakness 4: Limited Analysis of Why Certain Shifts Matter More**

Figure 7 shows that "Insert" perturbations cause greater degradation than "Delete" or "Modify," and that perturbations to "elements and transformation play an important role, whereas the changes to other tokens have a lesser effect." However, the paper does not deeply analyze **why** these asymmetries exist. Is it positional encoding sensitivity? Token embedding structure? Attention pattern disruption? Understanding the mechanistic basis for differential sensitivity would strengthen the theoretical framework.

**Open Question 1: Does Pretraining on Diverse Distributions Help?**

The paper trains models from scratch on single-distribution data, then tests OOD generalization. An unexplored question is whether **multi-distribution pretraining** — training on diverse task/length/format combinations simultaneously — would produce more robust CoT. The data distribution lens suggests performance should still be bounded by the union of training distributions, but the boundary might be wider.

**Open Question 2: Is There a Phase Transition in Distribution Sensitivity?**

The Gaussian degradation model (Proposition E.2) suggests smooth performance decline with increasing distribution shift. However, some findings (e.g., the cliff from ID to CMP in Table 1) suggest possible **phase transitions** where small increases in discrepancy trigger catastrophic failure. The paper does not systematically map the discrepancy-performance curve at fine granularity to determine whether degradation is truly smooth or exhibits critical thresholds.

**Open Question 3: How Does This Relate to In-Context Learning?**

The paper focuses on models trained from scratch with supervised fine-tuning. It does not address **in-context learning** (few-shot prompting) where models generalize from examples in the prompt rather than gradient updates. Prior work (Garg et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024) suggests in-context learning also faces OOD limitations, but whether the data distribution lens applies equally to in-context versus fine-tuned generalization remains unexplored.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

| Trade-off | Benefit | Cost |
|-----------|---------|------|
| Synthetic environment (DataAlchemy) | Full experimental control, no data leakage | Reduced ecological validity for natural language |
| Training from scratch | Known training distribution, causal claims | High computational cost, limited replicability |
| Binary exact match metric | Clear success/failure determination | May obscure partial generalization |
| Three generalization dimensions | Systematic coverage of major shift types | Excludes cross-lingual, multi-modal, cultural shifts |
| Short reasoning chains ($k \leq 3$) | Tractable experimentation | May not capture long-chain reasoning dynamics |
| SFT for external validity | Bridges synthetic and real-world models | Cannot fully measure pretraining distribution discrepancy |

### 6.6 Fair Assessment

The paper's limitations are **acknowledged transparently** in the Limitations section rather than concealed. The authors explicitly note where their claims are bounded by methodological choices (synthetic environment, three dimensions, short chains). This honesty strengthens rather than weakens the contribution — it allows readers to appropriately calibrate confidence in findings.

The most significant limitation is the **ecological validity gap**: whether CoT brittleness manifests identically in semantically-rich natural language tasks. However, the external validity experiments with SOTA models (Figure 9, Tables 6-8) provide strong evidence that the phenomenon extends beyond DataAlchemy. The fact that LLaMA3-8B and Qwen3-14B show the same qualitative failure patterns despite their pretraining on diverse natural language corpora suggests the distribution sensitivity is fundamental rather than an artifact of the synthetic environment.

The paper's contribution is not diminished by these limitations — rather, the limitations define the **boundary conditions** for the claims. The data distribution lens is presented as a framework for understanding CoT, not as a complete theory of reasoning. Future work can extend the framework to address the open questions identified above while building on the rigorous foundation this paper establishes.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than document CoT failures — it fundamentally reframes how the research community should think about reasoning in language models. The data distribution lens shifts the question from "How do we make CoT better?" to "Under what distributional conditions does CoT generalize, and what architectural changes are needed for genuine reasoning?" This section explores how this reframing changes the research landscape, what concrete follow-up work it enables, and how practitioners should adjust their deployment strategies.

### 7.1 Paradigm Shift: From Scaling to Distribution Alignment

**The End of Scaling as a Solution Path**

Prior to this work, a common assumption in the field was that CoT limitations would resolve through continued scaling — larger models with more training data would eventually achieve robust reasoning. Figure 8 and Figure 15 directly challenge this assumption. The paper tests 12 models from scratch (62K to 3B parameters) across two architectures (GPT and LLaMA), plus 2 SOTA models (LLaMA3-8B and Qwen3-14B), and finds **qualitatively identical failure patterns** across all configurations.

Appendix F.5 states explicitly: "Model scaling consistently improves performance in ID and mildly shifted regimes... but provides diminishing returns as the discrepancy increases toward POOD and OOD settings." This is a **fundamental negative result** that redirects the research agenda. If distribution discrepancy — not model capacity — governs CoT effectiveness, then scaling alone cannot solve the reasoning problem.

**Reframing the Research Question**

The data distribution lens transforms CoT from a capability question into a **distributional alignment question**. Instead of asking:

> "Does this model have reasoning capabilities?"

Researchers should now ask:

> "What is $\Delta(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}, \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}})$ for this deployment scenario, and does it fall within the model's in-distribution boundary?"

This reframing has three concrete implications:

1. **Evaluation protocols must change.** Standard benchmarks that mirror training distributions (e.g., GSM8K for math reasoning) no longer suffice. The paper's three-dimension framework (task, length, format) provides a structured vocabulary for designing OOD test suites.

2. **Model claims require distributional specifications.** Just as image classifiers report accuracy on specific datasets, CoT-enabled systems should report performance as a function of measured distribution discrepancy. A model that achieves 100% exact match at $\Delta = 0$ but 0% at $\Delta > 0.1$ has fundamentally different capabilities than one that degrades gracefully.

3. **Architectural innovation becomes necessary.** If current transformer architectures learn distribution-bounded pattern matching rather than transferable reasoning operations, then achieving genuine reasoning requires architectural or training paradigm changes — not just more data or parameters.

**The Mirage Characterization Has Consequences**

Section 9's ethical considerations warn that "fluent yet logically inconsistent reasoning traces" in high-stakes domains could cause harm while appearing authoritative. This is not hypothetical — Table 2 shows models producing 100% correct reasoning traces with 0.01% correct answers. In a medical or legal context, such outputs could be catastrophic because they project false confidence.

The paper's contribution here is **actionable skepticism**: practitioners should treat CoT traces as **explanations only within narrowly validated distributional boundaries**, not as evidence of genuine understanding. This shifts the burden of proof from "Show me CoT fails" to "Show me CoT works for *my* distribution."

### 7.2 Concrete Research Directions Enabled by This Work

The data distribution lens and DataAlchemy environment open multiple research avenues that were previously intractable due to evaluation limitations. Below, I organize these by timescale and feasibility.

#### 7.2.1 Immediate Extensions (6–12 Months)

**1. Expanding the Distribution Shift Taxonomy**

The paper focuses on three dimensions (task, length, format) but explicitly notes in Limitations that it "did not explicitly model other forms of distribution shift, such as cross-lingual transfer, multi-modal reasoning, or shifts in cultural context." Follow-up work can extend DataAlchemy to cover:

- **Cross-lingual shifts**: Train on English atom sequences, test on translated equivalents. Does CoT transfer across languages when task structure is identical?
- **Multi-modal shifts**: Extend atoms to include visual tokens or structured data (tables, code). Does CoT brittleness persist when reasoning spans modalities?
- **Temporal shifts**: Train on time-bounded data, test on future distributions. This is critical for deployed systems where data distributions drift over time.

**2. Multi-Distribution Pretraining Studies**

The paper trains models on single-distribution data, then tests OOD generalization. An unexplored question is whether **multi-distribution pretraining** — training on diverse task/length/format combinations simultaneously — produces wider in-distribution boundaries. The data distribution lens predicts performance should still be bounded by the union of training distributions, but the boundary might be wider.

Concrete experiment design:
- Train Model A on $f_1 \circ f_1$ only (single distribution)
- Train Model B on $\{f_1 \circ f_1, f_1 \circ f_2, f_2 \circ f_1, f_2 \circ f_2\}$ (multi-distribution)
- Test both on unseen composition $f_3 \circ f_3$

If Model B shows better OOD performance, this suggests distributional diversity during training expands the in-distribution boundary — a practical insight for training protocol design.

**3. Representational Analysis of SFT "Patching"**

Figure 4 shows that $\lambda = 1.5 \times 10^{-4}$ (0.015%) of unseen data enables rapid generalization, but Appendix F.2 Figure 12b reveals "mismatch of accuracy between the answer and the reasoning step during the training process." The paper documents this phenomenon but does not explain the **representational mechanism**.

Follow-up work should analyze:
- How do attention patterns change during SFT on unseen transformations?
- Do hidden state clusters expand to include new transformation types, or does the model learn separate "patches"?
- Is there evidence of compositional generalization (recombining learned primitives) versus memorization?

Tools like attention rollout, probing classifiers, or representational similarity analysis could provide mechanistic explanations that the current paper lacks.

#### 7.2.2 Medium-Term Directions (1–2 Years)

**4. Alternative Architectures for Distribution-Robust Reasoning**

If current transformer architectures learn distribution-bounded pattern matching, what architectures might learn transferable reasoning operations? Promising directions include:

- **Neural symbolic hybrids**: Systems that explicitly separate symbolic reasoning engines from neural perception modules. The data distribution lens predicts these should show better OOD generalization because symbolic operations are inherently distribution-agnostic.
- **Algorithmic transformers**: Architectures with explicit positional encoding schemes designed for length generalization (e.g., Cho et al., 2024's position coupling). The paper cites this work in Section 2.3 as evidence that "strong arithmetic generalization emerges only when algorithmic biases are encoded in positional representations."
- **Meta-learning approaches**: Training models to learn *how to learn* new transformations from few examples, rather than learning specific transformations directly.

DataAlchemy provides an ideal testbed for comparing these architectures under controlled distribution shifts — something natural language benchmarks cannot support due to confounding variables.

**5. Phase Transition Mapping**

Table 1 shows a cliff from ID (100%) to CMP (0.01%) exact match — suggesting possible **phase transitions** rather than smooth degradation. Proposition E.2 proposes a Gaussian degradation model, but the empirical data may exhibit sharper thresholds.

Systematic mapping of the discrepancy-performance curve at fine granularity could reveal:
- Critical thresholds where performance collapses
- Whether different distribution dimensions (task vs. length vs. format) have different threshold characteristics
- Whether model size affects threshold location (even if qualitative patterns remain the same)

This has practical implications: if phase transitions exist, practitioners could identify "safe operating regions" bounded by measurable discrepancy thresholds.

**6. In-Context Learning vs. Fine-Tuned Generalization**

The paper focuses on supervised fine-tuning but does not address **in-context learning** (few-shot prompting) where models generalize from examples in the prompt rather than gradient updates. Prior work (Garg et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024) suggests in-context learning also faces OOD limitations, but whether the data distribution lens applies equally remains unexplored.

Concrete question: Does providing few-shot examples of unseen transformations in the prompt enable better OOD generalization than SFT with the same number of examples? This would inform when to use prompting versus fine-tuning for distribution shift scenarios.

#### 7.2.3 Long-Term Vision (3+ Years)

**7. Toward Genuine Reasoning: Architectural Requirements**

The paper's conclusion states: "We hope DataAlchemy can serve as a platform where researchers can rigorously explore the nature of LLMs, inspiring the discovery of universal principles." The ultimate goal is not just understanding CoT limitations but **designing systems that overcome them**.

Key questions for long-term research:
- What inductive biases enable true compositional generalization (applying learned operations to novel combinations)?
- Can models learn causal reasoning structures rather than correlational patterns?
- Is there a fundamental architectural change required, or can training paradigm modifications (e.g., curriculum learning, explicit reasoning supervision) achieve distribution-robust reasoning?

DataAlchemy provides the controlled environment needed to test hypotheses about these questions without confounding variables.

**8. Formal Verification of Reasoning Traces**

If CoT traces are not faithful to actual computation (as Table 2 demonstrates), then they cannot serve as explanations for model decisions. Long-term work should explore:
- Can reasoning traces be formally verified against ground-truth computation paths?
- What training objectives encourage faithfulness between traces and answers?
- Should systems emit uncertainty estimates when operating outside validated distributional boundaries?

This connects to broader AI safety concerns about interpretability and trustworthiness in deployed systems.

### 7.3 Practical Deployment Guidance

For practitioners deploying CoT-enabled systems, this paper provides actionable guidance that should directly influence development and deployment decisions.

#### 7.3.1 When to Use CoT (and When Not To)

**Appropriate Use Cases:**

| Scenario | Distribution Characteristics | Recommendation |
|----------|------------------------------|----------------|
| Narrow, well-defined tasks | Low $\Delta_{\text{task}}$, low $\Delta_{\text{length}}$, low $\Delta_{\text{format}}$ | CoT is appropriate; validate on OOD test suite |
| High-stakes decisions (medical, legal, financial) | Any distribution uncertainty | **Do not rely on CoT traces** as explanations; require human verification |
| Dynamic environments with distribution drift | Time-varying $\Delta$ | Implement continuous OOD monitoring; set up alerts when discrepancy exceeds thresholds |
| Novel task domains | High $\Delta_{\text{task}}$ | Assume CoT will fail; invest in task-specific fine-tuning with OOD validation |

**Key Principle:** Treat CoT as a **distribution-bounded capability**, not a general reasoning module. The paper's findings mean that CoT effectiveness cannot be assumed — it must be validated for each deployment scenario.

#### 7.3.2 OOD Testing Protocol

Section 9 states: "Standard validation practices, where the test set closely mirrors the training set, are insufficient to gauge the true robustness of a CoT-enabled system." The paper's three-dimension framework provides a structured approach:

**Step 1: Characterize Training Distribution**
- Document task structures seen during training (transformation compositions)
- Record text length distribution (mean, variance, range)
- Catalog format variations (prompt templates, token patterns)

**Step 2: Design OOD Test Suite**
- **Task shifts**: Test on novel task structures (e.g., unseen transformation compositions)
- **Length shifts**: Test on text lengths outside training range (±1, ±2 tokens minimum)
- **Format shifts**: Test with insertions, deletions, and modifications at 5–30% noise levels

**Step 3: Measure Degradation Curve**
- Plot performance (exact match, BLEU, edit distance) as function of distribution shift magnitude
- Identify critical thresholds where performance collapses
- Document reasoning-answer consistency (do traces remain faithful under shift?)

**Step 4: Set Deployment Boundaries**
- Define acceptable discrepancy thresholds based on degradation curve
- Implement runtime monitoring to detect when inputs exceed boundaries
- Establish fallback procedures for OOD inputs (e.g., human escalation, alternative models)

#### 7.3.3 Fine-Tuning Strategy

The paper's SFT findings (Figure 4, Appendix F.2) reframe fine-tuning from a generalization mechanism to a **distribution patching** tool. Practical implications:

**Do:**
- Use SFT to expand in-distribution boundary for known deployment scenarios
- Include OOD examples in fine-tuning data to widen the boundary proactively
- Monitor reasoning-answer consistency during SFT to detect unfaithful learning

**Do Not:**
- Treat SFT gains as evidence of improved reasoning capacity
- Rely on SFT to fix every OOD failure reactively
- Assume SFT on one task dimension (e.g., task) transfers to others (e.g., length)

**Resource Allocation:** The paper shows that $\lambda = 1.5 \times 10^{-4}$ (0.015%) of unseen data enables rapid adaptation. This suggests practitioners should invest in **diverse fine-tuning data** covering anticipated distribution shifts rather than maximizing data volume for in-distribution performance.

#### 7.3.4 Comparison with Alternative Approaches

| Approach | When to Prefer | Limitations |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| **CoT with OOD validation** (this paper's recommendation) | Narrow domains with well-characterized distributions | Requires systematic OOD testing; performance bounded by training distribution |
| **Neural symbolic hybrids** | Tasks with clear symbolic structure; high-stakes decisions | Higher engineering complexity; may sacrifice fluency for faithfulness |
| **Ensemble methods** | When uncertainty quantification is critical | Increased computational cost; does not address root cause of OOD failure |
| **Human-in-the-loop** | High-stakes domains where CoT cannot be validated | Slower throughput; requires expert availability |
| **Alternative architectures** (algorithmic transformers, etc.) | When distribution robustness is primary requirement | Less mature; may have lower in-distribution performance |

**Decision Framework:** Use CoT when:
1. Distribution characteristics are well-understood and stable
2. OOD testing validates performance within acceptable thresholds
3. Reasoning traces are not the sole basis for critical decisions

Avoid CoT (or use with extreme caution) when:
1. Deployment environment has high distribution uncertainty
2. Stakes are high and errors are costly
3. Regulatory or ethical requirements demand explainable, faithful reasoning

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

The paper provides substantial infrastructure for reproducibility, but practitioners should understand both the opportunities and constraints.

#### 7.4.1 Using DataAlchemy for Your Research

**Availability:** Code is available at GitHub: `https://github.com/ChengshuaiZhao0/DataAlchemy` (Section 1).

**When to Use DataAlchemy:**
- You need to isolate specific distribution shift factors without confounding variables
- You want to train models from scratch to avoid data leakage concerns
- You are studying fundamental reasoning mechanisms rather than task-specific performance
- You need to test architectural modifications under controlled conditions

**When Not to Use DataAlchemy:**
- Your research question requires semantic richness (DataAlchemy uses symbolic atoms, not natural language)
- You need to evaluate on standard NLP benchmarks for comparison with prior work
- You lack computational resources for training models from scratch (Appendix D.2 reports 8 A100 + 4 H200 GPUs)

**Integration Steps:**
1. Clone the repository and review environment setup (Appendix D.1)
2. Define your distribution shift dimensions using the paper's taxonomy (task, length, format)
3. Select model architecture and size from Table 3 options (62K to 3B parameters)
4. Configure training hyperparameters (learning rate $3 \times 10^{-3}$, batch size 1024, 10 epochs)
5. Design OOD test suite following Section 5's experimental structure
6. Evaluate using the paper's three-component metrics (reasoning, answer, full chain)

#### 7.4.2 Adapting the Framework to Natural Language Tasks

While DataAlchemy uses symbolic abstraction, the **methodological principles** transfer to natural language:

**Mapping DataAlchemy Concepts to NLP:**
| DataAlchemy | Natural Language Equivalent |
|-------------|----------------------------|
| Atoms (26 letters) | Tokens or subword units |
| Elements (atom sequences) | Sentences or paragraphs |
| Transformations (ROT, Cyclic Shift) | Reasoning operations (arithmetic, logical inference) |
| Composition | Multi-step reasoning chains |
| Format perturbations | Paraphrasing, noise injection, template variation |

**Practical Adaptation:**
1. Identify the core reasoning operations in your task (analogous to transformations)
2. Construct training data with controlled variation in task structure, length, and format
3. Design OOD test cases that systematically vary each dimension independently
4. Evaluate reasoning traces separately from final answers to detect unfaithfulness

**Example:** For mathematical reasoning:
- **Task shift**: Test on problem types not seen during training (e.g., train on addition, test on multiplication)
- **Length shift**: Test on problems with more/fewer steps than training distribution
- **Format shift**: Test with rephrased problem statements or inserted distractor text

#### 7.4.3 Computational Requirements

Appendix D.2 reports: "We conduct training, fine-tuning, and inference on utilizing 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs (80 GB memory) and 4 NVIDIA H200 GPUs." This represents substantial resources that may be barriers for some researchers.

**Cost-Saving Strategies:**
- Use smaller model sizes (62K to 6M parameters) for initial experiments; Figure 8 shows qualitative patterns hold across scales
- Reduce number of independent runs from 3 to 1 for exploratory work (though this reduces statistical confidence)
- Focus on one or two distribution dimensions rather than all three simultaneously
- Use fine-tuning of existing models rather than training from scratch when appropriate

**Cloud Cost Estimate:** Training a 3B parameter model for 10 epochs on the reported hardware configuration likely costs several thousand dollars in cloud compute. Smaller models (62K to 6M) would be orders of magnitude cheaper while still demonstrating the core phenomena.

#### 7.4.4 Extending the Evaluation Metrics

The paper uses exact match, edit distance, and BLEU score. For natural language applications, consider augmenting with:

- **Semantic similarity metrics** (e.g., BERTScore) for cases where exact match is too strict
- **Faithfulness metrics** measuring alignment between reasoning traces and answers
- **Uncertainty estimates** from model confidence scores or ensemble variance
- **Human evaluation** for cases where automated metrics cannot capture reasoning quality

**Important:** The paper's key insight is evaluating reasoning traces **separately** from answers. Whatever metrics you choose, maintain this decomposition to detect unfaithful reasoning.

### 7.5 Summary: A New Research Agenda

This paper does not close the door on CoT reasoning — it opens a new one. The data distribution lens transforms CoT from a black-box capability into a **measurable, bounded phenomenon** that can be systematically studied and improved. The research agenda shifts from:

| Before This Work | After This Work |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| "How do we make CoT work better?" | "Under what conditions does CoT generalize?" |
| "Scale models until reasoning emerges" | "Design architectures for distribution-robust reasoning" |
| "Evaluate on standard benchmarks" | "Evaluate on OOD test suites with measured discrepancy" |
| "Trust reasoning traces as explanations" | "Validate trace faithfulness before deployment" |

**For Researchers:** DataAlchemy provides a platform for rigorous investigation of LLM reasoning mechanisms. Use it to test hypotheses about architectural modifications, training paradigms, and generalization mechanisms under controlled conditions.

**For Practitioners:** Treat CoT as a distribution-bounded tool, not a general reasoning module. Implement OOD testing protocols, monitor deployment distributions, and avoid high-stakes decisions based solely on CoT traces without validation.

**For the Field:** This work establishes that distribution discrepancy — not model scale — governs CoT effectiveness. The path forward requires architectural innovation, not just scaling. DataAlchemy provides the infrastructure to pursue this path systematically.

The paper's final hope is that "DataAlchemy can serve as a platform where researchers can rigorously explore the nature of LLMs, inspiring the discovery of universal principles." Whether CoT reasoning can ever transcend its distribution-bounded nature remains an open question — but this paper provides the tools and framework to find out.
