## 1. Executive Summary

Hermes 4 introduces a family of open-weight hybrid reasoning models (14B, 70B, 405B) that address the challenge of integrating structured multi-turn reasoning with broad instruction-following capabilities using a 19 billion token dataset synthesized via the `DataForge` graph-based generator. The work demonstrates significant advancements in training efficiency and control, notably reducing overlong generation rates by 98.9% on benchmarks like AIME'24 through a novel 30k-token length-control fine-tuning stage while achieving frontier-level performance (e.g., 81.9 on AIME'24 for the 405B variant). This establishes a reproducible framework for transparent reasoning systems that match proprietary counterparts in both quantitative benchmarks and behavioral plasticity.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Bridging Reasoning and Instruction-Following

The Hermes 4 team addresses a fundamental tension in modern language model development: **how to build models that excel at structured, multi-turn reasoning while maintaining broad instruction-following capabilities**. This is not merely an academic question — it represents a critical gap in the open-weight model ecosystem.

As stated in the Introduction (Section 1), recent years have seen growing interest in "inference-time scaling mechanisms" — often called "reasoner" models — which dynamically adjust the amount of computation devoted to solving a task [35, 15]. However, the paper identifies a crucial limitation:

> Initially, reasoner models were available only from proprietary providers. More recently, a number of open-weight reasoning systems have been released, enabling the community to study, evaluate, and improve upon these methods in an open setting [57, 15, 48].

The gap Hermes 4 targets is twofold:

1. **Accessibility Gap**: Frontier reasoning capabilities remained concentrated in closed, proprietary systems, limiting reproducibility and community-driven improvement.

2. **Capability Gap**: Existing open-weight models often specialized in *either* reasoning *or* general instruction-following, but not both simultaneously. A model excellent at mathematical proofs might fail at creative writing or role-play scenarios requiring contextual fidelity.

### Why This Problem Matters

The importance of solving this problem extends beyond benchmark scores. Consider the practical implications:

**Real-World Impact**: Organizations deploying AI systems need models that can reason through complex problems (e.g., debugging code, analyzing legal documents) while also following nuanced instructions (e.g., "explain this to a non-expert," "format as a JSON response"). A model that reasons well but ignores formatting constraints breaks production pipelines. Conversely, a model that follows instructions but cannot reason through multi-step problems provides limited value for complex tasks.

**Theoretical Significance**: The paper implicitly challenges an assumption in the field: that reasoning and instruction-following are competing objectives requiring trade-offs. Hermes 4's hybrid approach suggests these capabilities can be jointly optimized through careful data synthesis and training methodology.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

To understand what Hermes 4 changes, we must examine what came before:

**Proprietary Reasoner Models**: Systems like those referenced in [35, 15] demonstrated that inference-time computation scaling could dramatically improve reasoning performance. However, these remained closed systems — researchers could observe outputs but not inspect training data, architecture modifications, or evaluation methodologies. This opacity hindered scientific understanding of *why* certain approaches worked.

**Open-Weight Reasoning Systems**: The release of models like Qwen3 [57] and DeepSeek-R1 [15] enabled community study. However, the Hermes 4 team identifies specific challenges encountered during their own development that likely affected prior work:

- **Data Curation at Scale**: The paper notes challenges in "data curation, synthesis, training, and evaluation" (Abstract). Prior open-weight efforts often relied on existing datasets without systematic synthesis strategies for hybrid reasoning + instruction data.

- **Reasoning Length Control**: Section 3.1 reveals a critical problem the team encountered: "when evaluated on LiveCodeBench in reasoning mode, our 14B model would reach its maximum context of 40,960 tokens 60% of the time." This overlong generation problem — where models produce excessive reasoning traces without terminating — represents a practical deployment barrier. The paper does not indicate whether prior open-weight reasoning models solved this; the fact that Hermes 4 dedicates an entire training stage (Section 3.1) to addressing it suggests this was an open challenge.

- **Behavioral Rigidity**: Section 5.1 documents what the authors term "policy rigidity" — "a tendency to foreground policy compliance even when faced with clearly fictional or controlled prompts." The paper observes that "large proprietary models such as GPT-5 and Opus 4.1 frequently issued disclaimers emphasizing their AI identity." This suggests prior models, both open and closed, sacrificed contextual fidelity for safety compliance, limiting their utility for creative or role-play applications.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

Hermes 4 is a family of three language models (14B, 70B, and 405B parameters) trained to produce structured reasoning traces — explicit step-by-step thinking enclosed in special tags — while simultaneously maintaining broad instruction-following capabilities across creative, factual, and conversational tasks. The system solves the dual challenge of teaching models to think deeply without rambling indefinitely and to follow diverse instructions without losing reasoning rigor, using a two-stage training pipeline that first builds hybrid capabilities from synthesized data and then applies targeted length-control fine-tuning to prevent excessive generation.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The Hermes 4 training system consists of four major components connected in a sequential pipeline. First, the **DataForge synthetic data generator** (Section 2.1) takes pre-training seed data from DCLM and FineWeb corpora and transforms it through a directed acyclic graph where each node applies a struct-to-struct mapping with PDDL-style preconditions and postconditions, producing instruction-answer pairs with optional reasoning traces. Second, the **Atropos rejection sampling environment** (Section 2.2) validates generated trajectories against approximately 1,000 task-specific verifiers across five specialized environments (Answer Format, Instruction Following, Internbootcamp, Schema Adherence, and Tool Use), keeping only trajectories that pass binary reward signals. Third, the **TorchTitan training framework** (Section 3) performs supervised fine-tuning on the curated dataset using First-Fit Decreasing sample packing with Flex Attention to achieve over 99.9% batch efficiency across 192 NVIDIA B200 GPUs. Fourth, the **Length-Control Fine-Tuning Stage** (Section 3.1) applies a second supervised fine-tuning pass that teaches the model to emit the closing `</think>` tag at exactly 30,000 tokens by masking gradients on all tokens except the termination decision, reducing overlong generation rates by 98.9% without degrading reasoning accuracy.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with **DataForge data synthesis** because understanding what the model learns requires knowing what data it sees — the graph-based generation process determines the structure and diversity of training signals.
- We then examine **rejection sampling environments** because these verifiers filter raw synthetic data into high-quality trajectories, directly shaping which reasoning patterns the model internalizes.
- Next we cover **core training infrastructure** (TorchTitan, packing, attention masking) because these engineering choices determine training efficiency and scale, which constrain what is practically achievable.
- We then detail **length-control fine-tuning** because this represents the most novel technical contribution — a targeted intervention that solves the overlong generation problem without collapsing reasoning quality.
- Finally we explain **evaluation infrastructure** because the reproducibility-focused design (OpenAI-compatible endpoints, detailed logging) ensures that reported results can be independently verified.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a systems engineering paper that demonstrates how to train open-weight reasoning models at frontier scale through careful orchestration of data synthesis, verification, and targeted fine-tuning — the core idea is that hybrid reasoning and instruction-following capabilities emerge not from architecture changes but from deliberate data curation and length-controlled training objectives.

#### Data Synthesis via DataForge

The DataForge system processes pre-training seed data through a graph-based synthetic data generator that produces conversational instruction-answer pairs with structured reasoning traces. Each datapoint is generated via a random walk through a directed acyclic graph (DAG) where each node implements a `struct → struct` map using the PDDL action interface, which defines preconditions and postconditions that determine valid data flow between nodes. An edge exists from node A to node B if and only if the postconditions guaranteed by A satisfy the preconditions of B, enabling declarative construction of complex agent graphs without hardcoding specific pathways. The system enforces that every graph has a single source node and single target node, which allows graphs to be nested as nodes within higher-order graphs to arbitrary depth — this compositional design means a complete data generation workflow can itself be treated as a node in a larger workflow.

The seed data comes from a biased sample of DCLM and FineWeb corpora, preferentially selecting more recent samples to ensure temporal relevance. Before entering the DataForge graph, seed data undergoes semantic deduplication using ModernBert embeddings at a cosine similarity threshold of $0.7$, then passes through an LLM judge that filters out incomplete or ill-formatted passages. For single-turn QA data generation, the workflow follows three stages: first, a passage transformation step converts the seed passage into a random target type (for example, transforming a Wikipedia article into a rap song); second, an instruction generator creates a task conditioned on the transformed passage, which can be either contextual (the transformed passage is embedded in the prompt) or standalone (the passage serves only as inspiration); third, a specialized answer generator produces a response using a system prompt tailored to its instruction type.

Each generated instruction-answer pair is then passed to an LLM judge specialized for that instruction type, which grades the sample based on a rubric covering coherence, relevance, complexity, style, and tone. If the judge fails the sample, the system iterates on the answer generation until receiving a passing score or reaching a maximum iteration count, at which point the sample is discarded. The team takes care to guarantee that the judge model always has different weights from the answer model as a precautionary measure against LLM judges recognizing and favoring their own generations — this addresses the self-preference bias documented in prior work [41]. For accepted QA pairs, the training data includes not only the final instruction-answer pair but also all intermediate LLM calls used to generate that pair, which gives Hermes 4 substantial specialization in both instruction generation and judging capabilities.

The final dataset consists of approximately 5 million samples totaling 19 billion tokens, composed of 3.5 million reasoning samples and 1.6 million non-reasoning samples. A significant portion of the Hermes 3 dataset was retained to ensure continuity in the model's capabilities. The reasoning samples were intentionally token-heavy, with an average of five times more tokens per sample than their non-reasoning counterparts, accommodating thinking traces up to 16,000 tokens long. This asymmetry in token distribution reflects the team's hypothesis that reasoning capability requires substantially more exposure to extended chain-of-thought patterns than general instruction-following.

#### Rejection Sampling with Atropos Environments

The team employs rejection sampling against roughly one thousand task-specific verifiers using Atropos, an open-source reinforcement learning environment microservice manager. Following the recipe presented in OpenThoughts, the system includes multiple unique trajectories to the same verified result, which increases diversity in the training signal without requiring additional base prompts. Five specialized environments handle different capability domains, each producing binary reward signals that determine whether a trajectory is accepted.

The **Answer Format Training environment** generates trajectories rewarded for succinctly presenting the final answer in whatever form the user requested, decoupling format compliance from semantic correctness through a binary reward signal where $1.0$ indicates valid format and $0.0$ indicates invalid format. An example requirement would be placing math answers in a `\boxed{}` LaTeX section, but over 150 output formats are sampled during training. This environment is also responsible for strict enforcement of the `` and `&lt;/think>` delimiters at the beginning and end of the assistant's reasoning generation — these special tokens mark the boundaries of explicit reasoning traces that the model learns to produce autonomously.

The **Instruction Following environment** leverages the RLVR-IFEval set of verifiable tasks to introduce a large set of constraint instructions, such as "Every Nth word of your response must be in French," matching the types measured by the IFEval benchmark. Although the environment implements an adaptive online curriculum training method, the Hermes 4 team limits its use to rejection sampling successful trajectories rather than full reinforcement learning, prioritizing data quality over online adaptation.

The **Internbootcamp environment** reformulates the Internbootcamp benchmark into an Atropos environment to create 70,000 rejection-sampled trajectories from a comprehensive collection of approximately 1,000 reasoning tasks. The team generated multiple solution trajectories for each task using DeepHermes and other larger models, then selected multiple winning paths per group that fit within the defined token budget based on correctness. The large diversity of tasks makes Internbootcamp ideal for teaching the model to approach problems methodically and produce well-structured solutions rather than relying on pattern matching.

The **Schema Adherence environment** facilitates two tasks: generation, where the model produces a valid JSON object from a natural language prompt and a schema, and editing, where the model identifies and corrects validation errors within a malformed JSON object. A key feature is dynamic schema handling — rather than relying on a fixed set of schemas, the environment compiles Pydantic models on-the-fly from executable Python code provided in each dataset entry. For the editing task, an error introduction system programmatically injects realistic validation failures such as type mismatches, constraint violations (string length, numeric bounds), format errors, and extraneous fields. The reward signal derives from programmatic validation: a binary score of $1.0$ is awarded if the model's output successfully instantiates the target Pydantic model without error and $0.0$ otherwise, with a supplemental penalty for excessive length.

The **Tool Use environment** trains the model to generate reasoning and then produce one or more tool calls, facilitating agentic behavior. The environment intercepts a special `<tool_call>` token and validates that the JSON has all required fields in the correct hierarchy with values matching the origin dataset. The reward is determined by whether the entirety of the JSON produced in the tool calls is equivalent to the origin, given as a binary reward based on accuracy. This environment was seeded with tasks from prior function-calling datasets and enables Hermes 4 to coordinate reasoning with external API calls.

#### Core Training Infrastructure

The Hermes 4 team uses a modified version of TorchTitan for training, beginning with the 405B and 70B versions of Llama 3.1 as base checkpoints, and the Qwen3 14B checkpoint for the smallest model. Training occurred on 192 NVIDIA B200 GPUs with a mixture of Distributed Data Parallelism, Tensor Parallelism, and Fully Sharded Data Parallelism depending on model size. The team follows a standard cosine learning rate schedule with 300 steps of warmup and a total of 9,000 steps with a global batch size of 384 samples at a 16,384 token context length.

Table 1 provides the specific training parameters for each model size:

| Model Size | Parallelism | Tokens | Learning Rate | B200 Hours |
|------------|-------------|--------|---------------|------------|
| 14B | FSDP | 56B | $5 \times 10^{-5}$ | 4,454 |
| 70B | FSDP+TP | 56B | $1 \times 10^{-5}$ | 12,864 |
| 405B | FSDP+TP | 56B | $5 \times 10^{-6}$ | 71,616 |

All three models train on exactly 56 billion tokens, but the learning rate scales inversely with model size — the 14B model uses a learning rate ten times higher than the 405B model. This follows the empirical observation that larger models require smaller learning rates to maintain training stability, and the B200 hour costs scale roughly linearly with parameter count.

The final training dataset exhibited a highly heterogeneous distribution of sample lengths, so to optimize efficiency per batch the team packs samples ahead of time using the First-Fit Decreasing method, which achieves greater than 99.9% batch efficiency on the dataset. First-Fit Decreasing is a bin-packing algorithm that sorts items by size in descending order, then places each item into the first bin that has sufficient remaining capacity — in this context, "bins" are training batches and "items" are individual training samples. The team uses Flex Attention to ensure that attention is restricted to within each sample of the packed batch, preventing tokens from different samples from attending to each other despite being processed together. Only tokens generated by the "assistant" role contribute to the final cross-entropy loss objective, which focuses the learning signal on the model's own generations rather than user prompts.

Figure 2 shows the training loss curves for the 70B and 405B models, with both converging smoothly over the 9,000 training steps. The loss values range from approximately 0.4 to 0.65, with the 405B model achieving slightly lower loss throughout training, consistent with the expectation that larger models fit the training distribution more closely.

#### Length-Control Fine-Tuning (Critical Innovation)

The team discovered a critical problem during evaluation: when tested on LiveCodeBench in reasoning mode, the 14B model would reach its maximum context of 40,960 tokens 60% of the time without producing a closing `</think>` tag. This overlong generation represents a practical deployment barrier — models that never terminate reasoning cannot return answers to users. The team's solution utilizes a second supervised fine-tuning stage to teach the model to stop reasoning at 30,000 tokens and generate an answer, but with a crucial modification to the training objective.

To create appropriate training data, the team generates synthetic reasoning traces from the current policy, inserting a `</think>` tag at exactly 30,000 tokens. By inserting the closing tag at a fixed token count, the learning signal focuses entirely on that single token position, not on the preceding reasoning chain. This approach draws from expert iteration principles where models improve through self-generated trajectories, but crucially differs by not training on the generated reasoning itself — only on the termination decision. This selective supervision avoids the model collapse risks identified in synthetic data literature, which show that recursive training on full self-generated outputs leads to distribution narrowing and quality degradation.

The mathematical intuition behind this approach can be expressed as follows. Let the standard cross-entropy loss be:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{standard}} = -\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log P(y_t | y_{&lt;t}, x)$$

where $x$ is the input prompt, $y_{1:T}$ is the output sequence, and $T$ is the sequence length. The length-control modification applies a mask $m_t$ such that:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{length-control}} = -\sum_{t=1}^{T} m_t \cdot \log P(y_t | y_{&lt;t}, x)$$

where $m_t = 1$ only for the `</think>` token and the `<eos>` token, and $m_t = 0$ for all other tokens in the reasoning chain. This means the model essentially learns a counting behavior — "after $N$ tokens, stop" — without altering its reasoning distribution, as the vast majority of tokens in each training example come directly from the model's own distribution and receive no gradient signal.

The team gathered around 300,000 prompts, mostly STEM and coding, across WebInstruct-Verified, rSTAR-Coder, and DeepMath-130k datasets. For rSTAR-Coder, they filtered for prompts with at least 2,000 characters and deduplicated to obtain a set of around 12,000 unique prompts. They then generated responses of up to 30,000 tokens for each prompt and filtered for those that did not terminate in a stop token. Two cases were handled: (1) for requests that terminated in a stop token but after the closing `</think>` tag (meaning they terminated in the middle of the final answer), the team allowed them to finish generating the answer; (2) for requests that terminated in a stop token but before reaching a closing `</think>` tag, they forced `\n</think>` and then had the model generate an answer — they found that the original policy would still generate an answer when conditioned on an incomplete reasoning trace followed by a closing think tag, even without additional training.

The team chose Axolotl as the training framework for this stage because of its convenient character-span interface for token-level masking, which enables precise control over which tokens receive gradient updates. | AIME'24 | 55.0 | 55.4 | +0.7% | 28.2% | 0.1% | −99.6% |

Notably, LiveCodeBench performance actually improved by 48.6%, suggesting that the length control may have helped the model produce more complete solutions within the context window. The team did not deem reasoning truncation training necessary for the 70B or 405B models, as they exhibited lower overlong rates at Stage 1.

Appendix B documents exploratory experiments with a 20,000-token budget that actually increased overlong rates — GPQA Diamond overlong rates increased from 18.2% to 49.6% after standard supervised fine-tuning on naive data mixes. The team hypothesizes that certain reasoning prefixes (such as "Alternatively, ...Alternatively, ...") can induce longer reasoning, and selecting for samples with long reasoning chains teaches the model to generate these prefixes at greater frequency. This failure mode motivated the 30,000-token budget and the `</think>`-only masking strategy for the final model.

#### Evaluation Infrastructure Design

The evaluation system assumes the existence of an OpenAI-compatible chat completions endpoint shared by all benchmarks, rather than running different inference engines for different evaluation suites. This design choice addresses a reproducibility problem: evaluation scores are a function of not only the model but also the inference engine and hardware on which it runs, and model performance can vary between different inference engines or between different configurations of the same engine. By having all benchmarks hit the same inference engine instance, the team avoids fragmented inference engine installations in different versions across benchmark-specific virtual environments.

The team uses lighteval for math and most multiple-choice evaluations, the EQBench collection for subjective evaluations, and Atropos for LiveCodeBench and custom or unmaintained evaluations. Each evaluation suite is prepared as a lightweight bash script taking model and OpenAI endpoint information and sampling parameters as arguments, avoiding cluster scheduler-specific syntax so that scripts remain portable and can be tested locally.

For reasoning and code benchmarks, the team evaluates at context length 40,960, and all other benchmarks at context 32,768. For all non-creative benchmarks, they use temperature 0.6, Top-P 0.95, and Top-K 20, following the work of the Qwen3 team. They deviate from these settings only when a model provider recommends different sampling settings — for example, DeepSeek-V3-0324 is evaluated at temperature 0.3, and DeepSeek-R1-0528 at temperature 0.6, Top-P 0.95. DeepSeek-V3-0324 and DeepSeek-R1-0528 are evaluated at their full context length of 163,840 tokens. For creative benchmarks, the team uses default settings at a context length of 32,768.

All benchmarks run on SGLang version 0.4.9.post3 using the Triton backend on B200 GPUs. Each data-parallel model replica is sharded at TP8 to minimize decode out-of-memory errors at long context. The team observed more repetition and degeneration issues on the default FlashInfer attention backend on B200s versus on H100s around May 2025, and switched to Triton as a mitigation — this infrastructure detail matters because attention backend choice can materially affect generation quality at scale.

For LiveCodeBench specifically, the team evaluates using test data from August 1, 2024 to May 1, 2025, consisting of 454 problems. For the verification step, each generation is scored by launching a Modal container and evaluating the code on every test case corresponding to the specific LiveCodeBench problem. Since problems can have hundreds of test cases, Modal prevents the resource-intensive task of evaluating LLM-generated code from competing with resources used for training and inference, and allows sandboxed execution of untrusted code. Following Magistral, the team overlaps inference and scoring — as soon as an inference worker finishes a generation, the completion is sent to a verifier in Modal and the inference worker immediately begins generating another completion simultaneously. By overlapping inference with verification, the evaluation process remains inference-compute-bound rather than verification-bound.

The team also developed an internal benchmark named RefusalBench by classifying 32 categories of requests that typically result in refusals from frontier models, then hand-crafting 166 prompts that cover these categories. They measure how often the model refuses the prompt using Sonnet 4 as an LLM-as-a-judge to identify refusals. Of the 32 categories, three were selected for conditional reward inversion — for prompts related to minor specific harm, exploitation and human trafficking, and suicide/self-harm, refusals are scored positively rather than negatively. This nuanced scoring reflects the team's judgment that refusing genuinely harmful requests should not penalize a model's overall refusal rate score.

#### Design Choice Rationale

Several non-obvious design choices warrant explicit explanation. First, the decision to train on intermediate LLM calls from DataForge (not just final QA pairs) gives Hermes 4 specialization in instruction generation and judging — this means the model learns not only to answer questions but to understand what makes a good question and a good evaluation, which may improve its ability to self-correct during reasoning.

Second, the `</think>`-only masking in length-control fine-tuning represents a deliberate trade-off: the team sacrifices the opportunity to improve reasoning quality during the second stage in exchange for stability. Training on the full reasoning chain with forced truncation at 30,000 tokens could have taught the model new reasoning patterns, but it also risked model collapse from recursive training on self-generated outputs. By masking all tokens except the termination decision, the team ensures that effects remain minimal and targeted.

Third, the choice of 30,000 tokens for the length budget (rather than 20,000 or 40,000) emerged from empirical iteration. Appendix B shows that 20,000 tokens actually increased overlong rates on some benchmarks, suggesting that overly aggressive truncation can induce pathological reasoning patterns. The 30,000-token budget represents a sweet spot where the model learns to terminate without sacrificing reasoning depth.

Fourth, the evaluation infrastructure prioritizes reproducibility over optimization — using a single OpenAI-compatible endpoint for all benchmarks means the team cannot use engine-specific optimizations for each evaluation type, but it ensures that results can be independently verified by other researchers using the same endpoint configuration. This transparency choice directly addresses the reproducibility crisis in AI evaluation.

Fifth, the team's decision to release all evaluation logs alongside model weights enables researchers to inspect not just aggregate scores but individual sample-level behavior — this level of transparency is rare in both open and closed model releases, and it allows the community to diagnose discrepancies between automated grading and human evaluation.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the Hermes 4 work into its most novel contributions, distinguishing between incremental engineering improvements and fundamental methodological advances. We focus on *why* each innovation matters for the field, not just *what* was built.

### 4.1 Termination Learning Without Distribution Collapse

**Innovation Type**: Fundamental methodological advance

**What Makes It Different**: Prior approaches to controlling reasoning length either accepted overlong generation as inherent to reasoning models or applied blanket truncation that degraded reasoning quality. The Hermes 4 team's `</think>`-only masking strategy (Section 3.1) represents a conceptual shift: instead of training the model on *what* to reason, they train only on *when to stop*.

The critical insight is that gradient updates on self-generated reasoning chains risk model collapse through distribution narrowing, as documented in synthetic data literature [64, 19]. By masking all tokens except the termination decision, the team exploits what they term "single-step augmentation stability" — the model learns a counting behavior ("after $N$ tokens, stop") without altering its reasoning distribution, since the vast majority of tokens receive no gradient signal.

**Why It's Significant**: This solves a deployment-critical problem that prior open-weight reasoning models left unaddressed. Table 2 shows overlong rates dropped from 28.2% to 0.1% on AIME'24 (a 99.6% relative reduction) while maintaining or improving accuracy on most benchmarks. The LiveCodeBench score actually *increased* by 48.6%, suggesting that forced termination within context windows enables more complete solutions rather than truncating mid-answer.

**Prior Work Gap**: The paper notes concurrent work on reasoning length by the Nemotron Nano 2 team [34] and Nous colleagues [33], but does not indicate that prior open-weight reasoning releases (Qwen3 [57], DeepSeek-R1 [15]) documented similar length-control mechanisms. The fact that Hermes 4 dedicates an entire training stage to this problem — and publishes the failure modes from 20k-token experiments in Appendix B — suggests this was an open challenge in the field.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental**: This is a **fundamental innovation** because it introduces a new training objective formulation for sequence termination that decouples length control from content generation. The mathematical formulation (masking all tokens except `</think>` and `<eos>`) could generalize to other sequence control problems beyond reasoning traces.

### 4.2 Compositional Data Synthesis via Nested Graphs

**Innovation Type**: Fundamental methodological advance

**What Makes It Different**: While DataForge draws inspiration from AgentInstruct [31], the Hermes 4 implementation introduces a critical extension: **higher-order graph nesting**. Because each graph enforces a single source node and single target node, every graph implements the `struct → struct` node interface, enabling graphs to be composed as nodes within larger graphs to arbitrary nesting depth (Section 2.1.3, Figure 1b).

This compositional design allows declarative construction of complex data generation workflows without hardcoding specific pathways. The PDDL-style preconditions and postconditions determine valid edges implicitly — an edge exists from node A to node B if and only if A's postconditions satisfy B's preconditions. This is distinct from prior synthetic data approaches that typically define fixed pipelines or require manual specification of all connections.

**Why It's Significant**: The nesting capability enables scalable complexity. A simple single-turn QA graph (Figure 1a) can become a node within a multi-turn dialogue graph, which can itself become a node within a curriculum-generation graph. This hierarchical structure mirrors how human instruction design scales — simple tasks compose into complex workflows — without requiring exponential growth in manual specification effort.

The resulting dataset of 5 million samples and 19 billion tokens (Section 2) includes not only final instruction-answer pairs but all intermediate LLM calls used to generate them. This gives Hermes 4 "substantial specialization in instruction generation and judging" — the model learns to evaluate its own reasoning quality, not just produce answers.

**Prior Work Gap**: PersonaHub [8] uses pre-training data to generate synthetic personas for instruction generation, but does not describe compositional graph structures. The Hermes 4 approach extends this by making the *generation workflow itself* composable, not just the personas or prompts.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental**: This is a **fundamental innovation** in data synthesis architecture. The graph-as-node abstraction enables arbitrary workflow complexity through composition rather than enumeration, which could generalize to other synthetic data generation domains beyond language model training.

---

### 4.3 Hybrid Capability Training with Token Asymmetry

**Innovation Type**: Fundamental methodological advance

**What Makes It Different**: The Hermes 4 dataset explicitly balances reasoning and non-reasoning capabilities through asymmetric token allocation: 3.5 million reasoning samples versus 1.6 million non-reasoning samples, with reasoning samples containing "an average of five times more tokens per sample than their non-reasoning counterparts" (Section 2). This produces thinking traces up to 16,000 tokens long while maintaining broad instruction-following proficiency.

Prior open-weight models typically specialized in *either* reasoning *or* general instruction-following. The Hermes 4 team's hypothesis — that reasoning capability requires substantially more exposure to extended chain-of-thought patterns than general instruction-following — led to this deliberate token asymmetry rather than sample-count balancing.

**Why It's Significant**: Table 3 and Table 4 demonstrate that this hybrid approach achieves frontier-level performance on both capability types simultaneously. The Hermes 4 405B model scores 81.9 on AIME'24 (reasoning) while also scoring 93.7 on Arena-Hard v1 (instruction-following/alignment) and 79.3 on CreativeWriting3 (creative generation). This contradicts the assumption that reasoning and instruction-following are competing objectives requiring trade-offs.

The behavioral analysis in Section 5 reveals that Hermes 4 exhibits "comparatively greater contextual fidelity" than proprietary models — it interprets fictional prompts as role-play without repeated safety disclaimers, while still maintaining reasoning rigor on STEM tasks. This behavioral plasticity emerges from the hybrid training, not from architecture modifications.

**Prior Work Gap**: The Introduction (Section 1) notes that "initially, reasoner models were available only from proprietary providers" and that open-weight reasoning systems enabled community study but does not indicate prior work systematically balanced reasoning and non-reasoning data with explicit token asymmetry. The retention of "a significant portion of the Hermes 3 [51] dataset" ensures capability continuity, but the 5x token ratio for reasoning samples is a new design choice.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental**: This is a **fundamental innovation** in capability balancing methodology. The token-asymmetry principle — that different capabilities require different exposure densities, not just different sample counts — could inform training strategies for other multi-capability models beyond reasoning systems.

---

### 4.4 Reproducibility-First Evaluation Infrastructure

**Innovation Type**: Incremental engineering improvement with significant field impact

**What Makes It Different**: Rather than optimizing each benchmark for maximum performance, the team built their evaluation harness around a single OpenAI-compatible chat completions endpoint shared by all benchmarks (Section 4.1). This design choice sacrifices some engine-specific optimization for reproducibility: "evaluation scores are a function of not only the model but also the inference engine and hardware on which it runs."

The team logs all samples generated at evaluation time and releases them concurrently with model weights on HuggingFace. Atropos provides "fine-grained logging of parsing and grading behavior at the level of individual samples," including explicit records of which span of output was extracted as the candidate answer and how it was scored (Section 4.3.1).

**Why It's Significant**: The paper documents a concrete reproducibility problem: "in our internal benchmarking of a popular open-source evaluation framework in June 2025, we found 7.3% disagreement between their GPQA parser and GPT-4o grading." Sample-level logging enables researchers to diagnose such discrepancies rather than accepting aggregate scores at face value.

The infrastructure design also addresses practical deployment concerns. By overlapping inference and scoring for LiveCodeBench (Section 4.3.3), the evaluation remains "inference-compute-bound rather than verification-bound," reducing evaluation costs without sacrificing rigor. The elastic inference cluster (Section 4.4) supports worker preemption with automatic requeueing, enabling efficient use of shared compute resources.

**Prior Work Gap**: Many evaluation frameworks (lighteval [11], standard harnesses) offer convenience but do not release sample-level logs or standardize across benchmarks with a single endpoint. The Hermes 4 approach prioritizes transparency over optimization — a deliberate trade-off that prior work has not systematically addressed.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental**: This is an **incremental engineering improvement** in terms of technical novelty — the components (OpenAI-compatible endpoints, sample logging, overlapping inference/scoring) exist in prior work. However, the *systematic integration* of these components for reproducibility represents a meaningful contribution to evaluation best practices. The significance lies in field impact rather than methodological innovation.

---

### 4.5 Behavioral Plasticity via Structural Prompt Cues

**Innovation Type**: Novel empirical finding with theoretical implications

**What Makes It Different**: Section 5.3.2 documents a surprising discovery: Hermes 4 behavior is "highly sensitive to the token used for the assistant's turn." By modifying the standard Llama 3 chat template from `<|start_header_id|>assistant<|end_header_id|>` to `<|start_header_id|>me<|end_header_id|>`, the model "frequently adopted a first-person, peer-like persona, generating responses with fewer meta-disclaimers and more consistent voice embodiment."

This sensitivity to structural prompt cues suggests "higher behavioral plasticity than is typically observed in large models." The finding emerged from qualitative probing rather than being a designed capability — the team discovered it through systematic behavioral analysis.

**Why It's Significant**: This challenges assumptions about model rigidity. Proprietary models like GPT-5 and Opus 4.1 exhibit "policy rigidity" — foregrounding policy compliance even for clearly fictional prompts (Section 5.1). Hermes 4's plasticity enables consistent persona embodiment when explicitly instructed, extending "beyond what can be captured by standard evaluation benchmarks."

The finding has practical implications for deployment: users can shape model behavior through template modifications without fine-tuning. It also has theoretical implications for understanding how chat templates interact with learned representations — the assistant token appears to activate different behavioral modes than previously recognized.

**Prior Work Gap**: The paper does not cite prior work documenting this level of template sensitivity in large models. This appears to be an emergent property of the Hermes 4 training approach rather than a designed feature, making it a novel empirical contribution.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental**: This is a **novel empirical finding** rather than a methodological innovation. The significance lies in what it reveals about model behavior — that structural prompt cues can activate distinct behavioral modes — which could inform future research on model interpretability and controllable generation.

---

### 4.6 Summary: What Hermes 4 Teaches the Field

Table 6 summarizes the innovation classification:

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| Termination learning without distribution collapse | Fundamental | Solves deployment-critical overlong generation with 98.9% reduction |
| Compositional data synthesis via nested graphs | Fundamental | Enables scalable workflow complexity through graph-as-node abstraction |
| Hybrid capability training with token asymmetry | Fundamental | Demonstrates reasoning + instruction-following can be jointly optimized |
| Reproducibility-first evaluation infrastructure | Incremental (engineering) | Addresses reproducibility crisis through sample-level logging |
| Behavioral plasticity via structural prompt cues | Empirical finding | Reveals template sensitivity as controllable behavioral mechanism |

The Hermes 4 work demonstrates that frontier reasoning capabilities do not require proprietary scale or opaque training. The three fundamental innovations — termination learning, compositional synthesis, and hybrid training — provide a reproducible framework that the open community can build upon. The incremental engineering improvements (evaluation infrastructure) and empirical findings (behavioral plasticity) add practical value and theoretical insight, respectively.

For researchers and practitioners, the key takeaway is that **targeted interventions** (like `</think>`-only masking) can solve specific deployment barriers without degrading core capabilities, and that **transparency by design** (released logs, standardized evaluation) enables community-driven improvement that proprietary systems cannot match.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the Hermes 4 team validated their claims through systematic evaluation. We will walk through the evaluation methodology, dissect the quantitative results with specific numbers, assess whether the experiments support the paper's claims, and examine ablation studies and failure cases that reveal the boundaries of the approach.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: Rigor Through Standardization

The Hermes 4 evaluation design prioritizes **reproducibility over optimization** — a deliberate choice that distinguishes this work from many model releases. Understanding the methodology is essential for interpreting the results correctly.

#### Benchmark Selection and Coverage

The team evaluates across six capability domains, ensuring comprehensive coverage of both reasoning and instruction-following abilities:

| Domain | Benchmarks | What It Measures |
|--------|------------|------------------|
| Math & Reasoning | MATH-500, AIME'24, AIME'25, GPQA Diamond | Multi-step mathematical reasoning, graduate-level science |
| Logic & Code | BBH, LiveCodeBench v6 Aug2024+ | Logical deduction, code generation and execution |
| Knowledge | MMLU, MMLU-Pro, SimpleQA | Factual knowledge across domains |
| Alignment | IFEval (Loose), Arena-Hard v1, RefusalBench, RewardBench | Instruction following, helpfulness, safety refusals |
| Reading Comprehension | DROP, MuSR, OBQA | Multi-hop reasoning over text |
| Creativity & Writing | EQBench3, CreativeWriting3 | Stylistic transfer, emotional intelligence |

This breadth matters because it tests the core claim: that Hermes 4 achieves **hybrid capabilities** rather than specializing in one domain at the expense of others.

#### Evaluation Conditions and Sampling

Section 4.5 specifies precise evaluation conditions that affect result interpretation:

- **Context Length**: Reasoning and code benchmarks run at $40,960$ tokens; all other benchmarks at $32,768$ tokens. This asymmetry reflects the longer reasoning traces required for math and coding tasks.

- **Sampling Parameters**: For non-creative benchmarks, temperature $0.6$, Top-P $0.95$, and Top-K $20$, following Qwen3 team recommendations [57]. The team deviates only when model providers specify different settings (e.g., DeepSeek-V3-0324 at temperature $0.3$).

- **Sample Counts for pass@1 Estimation**:
  - AIME'24 and AIME'25: $64$ samples per problem
  - MATH-500: $4$ samples per problem
  - GPQA Diamond: $8$ samples per problem
  - LiveCodeBench: $16$ samples per problem
  - EQBench3 and CreativeWriting3: $3$ samples per problem

These sample counts directly affect score variance — higher sample counts produce more stable pass@1 estimates but increase evaluation cost. The $64$ samples for AIME reflects the high variance of competition-math problems.

- **Infrastructure**: All benchmarks run on SGLang version $0.4.9$.post3 using the Triton backend on B200 GPUs. Each data-parallel model replica is sharded at TP8 to minimize decode out-of-memory errors at long context. The team notes they observed "more repetition and degeneration issues on the default FlashInfer attention backend on B200s vs. on H100" around May 2025, switching to Triton as mitigation (footnote 11).

**Why This Matters**: The explicit documentation of inference engine, backend, and hardware configuration addresses a reproducibility gap. As Section 4.1 states: "evaluation scores are a function of not only the model but also the inference engine and hardware on which it runs." Many model releases omit these details, making independent verification impossible.

#### Baseline Selection

The team compares Hermes 4 against both open-weight and proprietary models:

**Open-Weight Baselines** (Tables 3 and 4):
- DeepSeek-R1-0528 (671B parameters, reasoning-optimized)
- DeepSeek-V3-0324 (671B parameters, generalist)
- Qwen3 (235B for 405B comparison, 14B for 14B comparison)
- Cogito (405B and 70B variants)

**Proprietary Baselines** (Figure 4 and Section 5):
- GPT-5, Opus 4.1, Sonnet 4
- Gemini 2.5 Pro, Gemini 2.5 Flash
- Grok 4

This baseline selection is strategic: DeepSeek-R1 represents the frontier of open-weight reasoning models, while proprietary models establish the upper bound of what is achievable. The inclusion of Cogito (another Nous-affiliated model) enables ablation-style comparison of training methodologies.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results: Numbers That Matter

We now examine the core results, organizing by model size and highlighting both absolute performance and relative comparisons to baselines.

#### Hermes 4 405B: Frontier-Level Reasoning

Table 3 presents the 405B model results against comparable open-weight systems. The notation "R (N)" indicates reasoning mode scores followed by non-reasoning mode scores in parentheses.

**Math & Reasoning Performance**:

| Benchmark | Hermes 4 405B R (N) | DeepSeek-R1 671B | DeepSeek-V3 671B | Qwen3 235B R (N) |
|-----------|---------------------|------------------|------------------|------------------|
| MATH-500 | $96.2$ ($73.8$) | $97.5$ | $92.5$ | $97.5$ ($90.3$) |
| AIME'24 | $81.9$ ($11.4$) | $86.5$ | $50.6$ | $78.2$ ($34.1$) |
| AIME'25 | $78.1$ ($10.6$) | $83.1$ | $42.2$ | $71.8$ ($25.1$) |
| GPQA Diamond | $70.6$ ($39.4$) | $78.1$ | $68.0$ | $69.7$ ($57.7$) |

**Key Observations**:

1. **Reasoning Mode Advantage**: The gap between reasoning mode (R) and non-reasoning mode (N) scores is dramatic. On AIME'24, Hermes 4 405B scores $81.9$ in reasoning mode but only $11.4$ in non-reasoning mode — a $70.5$ point difference. This validates that the `` and `&lt;/think>` reasoning traces are not cosmetic; they enable substantive capability gains.

2. **Competitive with Larger Models**: Despite having $405$B parameters versus DeepSeek-R1's $671$B, Hermes 4 achieves $81.9$ on AIME'24 compared to R1's $86.5$ — a gap of only $4.6$ points despite a $266$B parameter disadvantage. On GPQA Diamond, the gap narrows to $7.5$ points ($70.6$ vs. $78.1$).

3. **Hybrid Capability Evidence**: The non-reasoning mode scores remain competitive on knowledge benchmarks. MMLU scores $73.6$ in non-reasoning mode versus $87.2$ in reasoning mode — the smaller gap ($13.6$ points) compared to AIME suggests general knowledge is less dependent on explicit reasoning traces.

**Alignment and Instruction Following**:

| Benchmark | Hermes 4 405B R (N) | DeepSeek-R1 | DeepSeek-V3 | Qwen3 235B R (N) |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------|-------------|------------------|
| IFEval (Loose) | $81.5$ ($84.9$) | $90.0$ | $90.4$ | $91.4$ ($91.2$) |
| Arena-Hard v1 | $93.7$ ($53.5$) | $95.0$ | $92.6$ | $93.9$ ($91.7$) |
| RefusalBench | $57.1$ ($43.2$) | $16.7$ | $28.1$ | $34.3$ ($15.3$) |
| RewardBench | $73.0$ ($64.5$) | $70.1$ | $68.1$ | $74.2$ ($69.2$) |

**Critical Finding**: The RefusalBench score of $57.1$ (reasoning mode) is substantially higher than all baselines — DeepSeek-R1 scores $16.7$, and GPT-5 scores $11.34$ (Figure 4). Higher scores indicate **fewer refusals**, meaning Hermes 4 is more willing to engage with requests that proprietary models reject. This supports the Section 5.1 claim about reduced "policy rigidity."

However, this comes with nuance: three categories (minor specific harm, exploitation/human trafficking, suicide/self-harm) use **inverted reward** where refusals are scored positively. The $57.1$ aggregate score blends these inverted categories with standard ones, so the interpretation requires care.

#### Hermes 4 70B and 14B: Scaling Behavior

Table 4 reveals how capabilities scale with model size:

**AIME'24 Performance Across Sizes**:

| Model | Reasoning Mode | Non-Reasoning Mode | Gap |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|-----|
| Hermes 4 405B | $81.9$ | $11.4$ | $70.5$ |
| Hermes 4 70B | $73.5$ | $9.5$ | $64.0$ |
| Hermes 4 14B | $55.4$ | $11.0$ | $44.4$ |
| Qwen3 14B | $77.5$ | $28.5$ | $49.0$ |

**Surprising Pattern**: The Qwen3 14B base model outperforms Hermes 4 14B on AIME'24 ($77.5$ vs. $55.4$ in reasoning mode). This appears counterintuitive given Hermes 4's reasoning-focused training. However, the paper notes the 14B model began with Qwen3 14B-base checkpoint and underwent the length-control fine-tuning that larger models did not require. The performance gap may reflect the trade-off from length-control training.

**LiveCodeBench Shows Different Pattern**:

| Model | Reasoning Mode | Non-Reasoning Mode |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| Hermes 4 405B | $61.4$ | $28.1$ |
| Hermes 4 70B | $50.5$ | $25.5$ |
| Hermes 4 14B | $42.5$ | $35.0$ |
| Qwen3 14B | $61.2$ | $29.2$ |

Here, Hermes 4 14B ($42.5$) underperforms Qwen3 14B ($61.2$) by $18.7$ points in reasoning mode. This is a notable weakness — the smallest Hermes 4 variant does not uniformly improve over its base model on code reasoning tasks.

**Knowledge Benchmarks Show Consistent Scaling**:

| Model | MMLU R (N) | MMLU-Pro R (N) |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| Hermes 4 405B | $87.2$ ($73.6$) | $80.6$ ($58.3$) |
| Hermes 4 70B | $88.4$ ($76.7$) | $80.7$ ($54.9$) |
| Hermes 4 14B | $84.1$ ($79.2$) | $74.3$ ($61.9$) |

Interestingly, the 70B model slightly outperforms the 405B on MMLU ($88.4$ vs. $87.2$), suggesting diminishing returns or potential overfitting at larger scales for certain knowledge tasks. The 14B model's non-reasoning MMLU score ($79.2$) is higher than its reasoning mode score ($84.1$), a smaller gap than observed for math benchmarks.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies: What the Failure Modes Reveal

The paper includes valuable ablation experiments in Section 3.1 and Appendix B that document **what did not work** — rare transparency in model reports.

#### Length-Control Ablation (Table 5, Appendix B)

The team experimented with a $20,000$-token budget before settling on $30,000$ tokens. Table 5 compares four conditions on the Hermes-4-Qwen3-14B model:

| Condition | AIME'24 Overlong % | AIME'24 Score | GPQA Overlong % | GPQA Score |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------|-----------------|------------|
| Stage 1 (baseline) | $28.2$ | $55.0$ | $18.2$ | $57.4$ |
| Standard Masking (20k) | $34.0$ | $51.3$ | $49.6$ | $44.3$ |
| `</think>`-only Masking (20k) | $0.6$ | $35.4$ | $0.4$ | $55.7$ |
| Control (random data) | $15.1$ | $56.5$ | $30.0$ | $52.6$ |

**Critical Insights**:

1. **Standard Masking Backfired**: Training on full reasoning chains with forced truncation at 20k tokens **increased** overlong rates from $28.2\%$ to $34.0\%$ on AIME'24 and from $18.2\%$ to $49.6\%$ on GPQA Diamond. The team hypothesizes that "certain reasoning prefixes (e.g., 'Alternatively, ...Alternatively, ...') can induce longer reasoning, and selecting for samples with long reasoning chains teaches the model to generate these prefixes at greater frequency."

2. **`</think>`-only Masking Suppressed Overlong Rates**: This condition reduced overlong rates to $0.6\%$ on AIME'24 and $0.4\%$ on GPQA — a $100\times$ reduction. However, AIME'24 accuracy dropped from $55.0$ to $35.4$ (a $20$ percentage point decrease), revealing the **accuracy vs. termination trade-off**.

3. **Control Run Anomaly**: Replacing overlong samples with random Stage 1 data **improved** LiveCodeBench scores by $18\%$ (from $28.6$ to $46.0$). The team notes: "We initially thought the Stage 1 result was a mistake, so we repeated the evaluation twice only to find the same result. This may point to a bug in our Stage 1 trainer." This candid admission of potential infrastructure bugs is unusual and valuable.

**Why This Matters**: The ablation demonstrates that naive length control (standard masking on truncated chains) can **degrade** both termination behavior and accuracy. The `</think>`-only approach succeeds at termination but sacrifices accuracy at aggressive budgets ($20$k tokens). The final $30$k-token budget represents an empirical sweet spot.

#### Length-Control Results at 30k Tokens (Table 2)

The production configuration uses $30,000$ tokens with `</think>`-only masking:

| Benchmark | Stage 1 Score | 30k-tuned Score | Δrel. | Stage 1 Overlong | 30k-tuned Overlong | Δrel. |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|-------|------------------|--------------------|-------|
| AIME'24 | $55.0$ | $55.4$ | $+0.7\%$ | $28.2\%$ | $0.1\%$ | $-99.6\%$ |
| AIME'25 | $48.7$ | $46.8$ | $-3.9\%$ | $25.9\%$ | $0.1\%$ | $-99.6\%$ |
| GPQA Diamond | $57.4$ | $60.2$ | $+4.7\%$ | $18.2\%$ | $0.2\%$ | $-98.9\%$ |
| LCBv6 Aug2024+ | $28.6$ | $42.5$ | $+48.6\%$ | $60.0\%$ | $0.1\%$ | $-99.8\%$ |

**Trade-off Analysis**: At $30$k tokens, the accuracy cost is minimal (max $-3.9\%$ on AIME'25) while overlong rates drop by $>98.9\%$ across all benchmarks. LiveCodeBench actually **improves** by $48.6\%$, suggesting that forced termination within context enables more complete solutions rather than truncating mid-answer.

**Conditional Conclusion**: The length-control intervention is **not universally beneficial** — it helped the 14B model (which had $60\%$ overlong rates on LiveCodeBench) but was "not deemed necessary for Hermes 4 70B or Hermes 4 405B" (Section 3.1). This indicates the overlong generation problem scales inversely with model size, possibly because larger models learn better termination heuristics from the base training.

### 5.4 Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

We now assess whether the experimental evidence convincingly supports the paper's three core claims from the Introduction:

#### Claim 1: "Hybrid reasoning + instruction-following capabilities"

**Evidence**: Tables 3 and 4 show strong performance on both reasoning benchmarks (AIME, GPQA) and instruction-following benchmarks (IFEval, Arena-Hard). The 405B model scores $81.9$ on AIME'24 and $93.7$ on Arena-Hard v1 simultaneously.

**Assessment**: **Strongly supported**. The quantitative results demonstrate frontier-level reasoning without sacrificing instruction-following. The RefusalBench score of $57.1$ (vs. $16.7$ for DeepSeek-R1) further supports reduced policy rigidity while maintaining reasoning capability.

**Caveat**: The 14B model underperforms its Qwen3 14B base on LiveCodeBench ($42.5$ vs. $61.2$), suggesting the hybrid training may not uniformly benefit all model sizes.

#### Claim 2: "Length-control fine-tuning reduces overlong generation"

**Evidence**: Table 2 shows overlong rates drop from $28.2\%$ to $0.1\%$ on AIME'24 ($-99.6\%$) with minimal accuracy loss ($+0.7\%$ relative). Appendix B documents the failure of alternative approaches.

**Assessment**: **Convincingly supported**. The ablation study (Table 5) strengthens the claim by showing what does not work, ruling out alternative explanations. The mechanism (`</think>`-only masking) is clearly described and the results are specific and reproducible.

**Caveat**: The intervention was only necessary for the 14B model. Larger models did not require length-control tuning, limiting generalizability of the technique's necessity.

#### Claim 3: "Open-weight models can match frontier systems"

**Evidence**: Hermes 4 405B achieves $81.9$ on AIME'24 vs. DeepSeek-R1's $86.5$ (a $4.6$ point gap with $266$B fewer parameters). On Arena-Hard v1, Hermes 4 scores $93.7$ vs. R1's $95.0$.

**Assessment**: **Partially supported**. The 405B model is competitive with but does not exceed the frontier (DeepSeek-R1). The claim holds for "comparable to frontier systems" but not "exceeds frontier." The transparency advantage (released logs, documented methodology) is a separate value proposition not captured in benchmark scores.

**Caveat**: Proprietary models (GPT-5, Opus 4.1) are not directly compared on reasoning benchmarks in Tables 3-4, only on RefusalBench (Figure 4). The full frontier comparison is incomplete.

### 5.5 Limitations and Unanswered Questions

The experiments leave several important questions unresolved:

**1. Compute Efficiency**: The paper reports B200 hours (Table 1) but does not compare inference-time compute costs. A model that reasons for $30,000$ tokens may be prohibitively expensive for production use compared to a model that answers in $1,000$ tokens. The trade-off between reasoning quality and inference cost is not quantified.

**2. Long-Context Reasoning**: While the model supports $40,960$ token contexts, the evaluation does not test reasoning tasks that **require** long contexts (e.g., reasoning over long documents). The context length may be necessary for termination but not leveraged for capability gains.

**3. Cross-Domain Generalization**: The hybrid training approach is validated on standard benchmarks, but real-world tasks often blend reasoning and instruction-following in novel ways (e.g., "debug this code and explain the fix to a junior developer"). The evaluation does not test such composite tasks.

**4. Safety-Utility Trade-off**: The high RefusalBench score ($57.1$) indicates fewer refusals, but the paper does not assess whether this increases harmful outputs. The three inverted-reward categories partially address this, but a comprehensive safety evaluation is absent.

**5. Reproducibility Gap**: While the team releases evaluation logs, independent researchers cannot fully reproduce results without access to 192 B200 GPUs. The methodology is transparent, but the compute barrier remains.

### 5.6 Summary: What the Numbers Tell Us

The Hermes 4 experiments provide **strong quantitative evidence** for the core technical claims:

| Claim | Support Level | Key Evidence |
|-------|--------------|--------------|
| Hybrid capabilities | Strong | Table 3: $81.9$ AIME'24 + $93.7$ Arena-Hard simultaneously |
| Length control works | Strong | Table 2: $99.6\%$ overlong reduction with $&lt;4\%$ accuracy cost |
| Matches frontier | Partial | $4.6$ point gap to DeepSeek-R1 on AIME'24 despite $266$B parameter disadvantage |
| Reduced policy rigidity | Strong | Figure 4: RefusalBench $57.1$ vs. GPT-5 $11.34$ |

The ablation studies (Appendix B) add credibility by documenting failure modes rather than only successes. The $20$k-token budget experiments that **increased** overlong rates demonstrate the team's willingness to report negative results — a practice that strengthens confidence in the positive findings.

However, the 14B model's underperformance on LiveCodeBench ($42.5$ vs. Qwen3's $61.2$) reveals that the approach does not uniformly benefit all model sizes. This suggests the hybrid training and length-control interventions interact with model capacity in ways not fully understood.

For practitioners, the key takeaway is that Hermes 4 delivers **production-ready reasoning** with controlled generation lengths, but the compute cost of $30,000$-token reasoning traces and the 14B model's code reasoning weakness should factor into deployment decisions. For researchers, the `</think>`-only masking technique and compositional data synthesis provide reproducible methods for future work.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section examines the constraints, assumptions, and unresolved weaknesses in the Hermes 4 approach. Understanding these limitations is essential for practitioners considering deployment and researchers building upon this work. We ground each point in specific evidence from the paper rather than speculation.

### 6.1 Model Size Dependency: Techniques Do Not Scale Uniformly

**Limitation**: The length-control fine-tuning technique — one of the paper's central innovations — was only necessary for the 14B model. Section 3.1 explicitly states: "We did not deem reasoning truncation training necessary for Hermes 4 70B or Hermes 4 405B."

**Evidence**: Table 2 shows the 14B model had Stage 1 overlong rates of 28.2% on AIME'24 and 60.0% on LiveCodeBench. The paper does not report equivalent Stage 1 overlong rates for the 70B or 405B models, implying they were acceptably low without intervention.

**Implication**: This reveals a critical scaling assumption: **larger models learn termination heuristics more effectively from base training alone**. The `</think>`-only masking technique solves a problem that may be specific to smaller models, limiting its generalizability as a universal solution for reasoning model deployment.

**Trade-off**: Practitioners training models below ~70B parameters should anticipate needing explicit length-control training. Those training larger models may incur unnecessary compute costs by applying this intervention without measuring overlong rates first.

---

### 6.2 Performance Degradation on Code Reasoning for Small Models

**Limitation**: The Hermes 4 14B model underperforms its Qwen3 14B base checkpoint on code reasoning tasks, suggesting the hybrid training approach sacrifices domain-specific capability for generalist performance.

**Evidence**: Table 4 shows:

| Model | LiveCodeBench v6 (Reasoning Mode) |
|-------|----------------------------------|
| Hermes 4 14B | $42.5$ |
| Qwen3 14B | $61.2$ |

This is an $18.7$ point deficit ($30.6\%$ relative underperformance) on the same benchmark where the 405B model competes closely with frontier systems.

**Implication**: The hybrid training strategy — balancing 3.5M reasoning samples with 1.6M non-reasoning samples — may dilute specialized capabilities at smaller scales. The 405B model achieves sufficient capacity to excel at both, but the 14B model appears to face a **capacity bottleneck** where hybrid training compromises domain excellence.

**Trade-off**: Organizations deploying the 14B model for code-intensive applications should consider whether the instruction-following benefits justify the code reasoning deficit. For pure code generation tasks, the Qwen3 14B base may be preferable despite lacking reasoning traces.

### 6.3 Compute Costs: Training and Inference Economics

### 6.4 Safety-Utility Trade-off: Reduced Refusals Without Comprehensive Safety Evaluation

**Limitation**: The high RefusalBench score ($57.1$ for Hermes 4 405B vs. $11.34$ for GPT-5 in Figure 4) indicates fewer refusals, but the paper does not assess whether this increases harmful output generation.

**Evidence**: Section 4.5.1 describes RefusalBench methodology:
- 32 categories of refusal-prone requests
- 166 hand-crafted prompts
- Three categories use **inverted reward** (minor specific harm, exploitation/human trafficking, suicide/self-harm) where refusals are scored positively

**Critical Gap**: The aggregate RefusalBench score blends inverted and non-inverted categories. A model could score highly by refusing genuinely harmful requests (inverted categories) while engaging with borderline-harmful requests (standard categories). The paper does not disaggregate these scores.

**Implication**: The "reduced policy rigidity" praised in Section 5.1 may reflect **reduced safety caution** rather than improved contextual judgment. The behavioral analysis shows Hermes 4 engages with fictional role-play scenarios that proprietary models refuse — this is framed as flexibility, but could also enable jailbreak attempts.

**Trade-off**: Organizations with strict safety requirements may prefer the "policy rigidity" of proprietary models despite reduced contextual fidelity. The Hermes 4 approach prioritizes utility over caution, which is appropriate for some deployments but not others.

### 6.6 Data Synthesis Assumptions: Judge Quality and Bias

**Limitation**: The DataForge pipeline relies on LLM judges to filter synthetic data, introducing potential bias from judge model capabilities and preferences.

### 6.7 Narrow Operating Range for Length Control

**Limitation**: The length-control technique has a narrow effective range — too aggressive truncation increases overlong rates rather than reducing them.

**Evidence**: Appendix B Table 5 shows the 20,000-token budget experiments:

| Condition | AIME'24 Overlong % | GPQA Overlong % |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Stage 1 (baseline) | $28.2$ | $18.2$ |
| Standard Masking (20k) | $34.0$ | $49.6$ |

The "Standard Masking" condition — training on full reasoning chains truncated at 20k tokens — **increased** overlong rates by $20.4$ percentage points on AIME'24 and $31.4$ points on GPQA.

**Team Hypothesis**: Section B.1 states: "We currently believe that there exist certain reasoning prefixes (e.g., 'Alternatively, ...Alternatively, ...') that can induce longer reasoning, and selecting for samples with long reasoning chains teaches the model to generate these prefixes at greater frequency."

**Implication**: The length-control intervention is **not monotonic** — more aggressive truncation does not produce better termination. The 30,000-token budget represents an empirical sweet spot that may not generalize to other model families or domains.

**Trade-off**: Practitioners applying this technique must tune the token budget for their specific model and task domain. The 30k value is not a universal constant.

**What Is Reproducible**:

- Model behavior (via published weights)
- Methodology (via detailed technical descriptions)

**What Is Not Reproducible**:
- Training from scratch (compute barrier)
- Ablation studies (require retraining)
- Data synthesis at scale (DataForge requires significant LLM API calls)

**Implication**: The paper advances **transparency** but not **accessibility**. Researchers can verify results and build upon the models, but cannot independently validate training dynamics or experiment with alternative training configurations without frontier-scale resources.

**Trade-off**: This is an inherent limitation of large-scale model research, not specific to Hermes 4. However, the paper's claim to "support open research" (Abstract) should be understood as enabling research *using* the released models, not research *replicating* the training process.

### 6.9 Behavioral Plasticity: Feature or Vulnerability?

**Implication**: The same mechanism that enables creative persona adoption could enable **jailbreak attacks** via template manipulation. The paper documents this as a capability but does not assess security implications.

**Trade-off**: Deployment in security-sensitive contexts should evaluate template robustness. The flexibility that makes Hermes 4 useful for role-play may make it vulnerable to adversarial prompting.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how Hermes 4 reshapes the open-weight model landscape, identifies concrete research directions the work enables, and provides practical guidance for practitioners deciding when to adopt this approach versus alternatives.

### 7.1 Landscape Shifts: What Changes for the Field

Hermes 4 demonstrates three fundamental shifts that alter assumptions about what open-weight model development can achieve:

#### Shift 1: Reasoning Capabilities No Longer Require Proprietary Opacity

**Prior Assumption**: Frontier reasoning performance required closed training data, undisclosed architecture modifications, and proprietary inference infrastructure. The gap between "what proprietary labs can do" and "what the open community can reproduce" was assumed to be structural.

**Hermes 4 Demonstrates**: The 405B model achieves $81.9$ on AIME'24 (Table 3) — within $4.6$ points of DeepSeek-R1's $86.5$ despite having $266$B fewer parameters — while publishing full evaluation logs, training methodology, and model weights. The Abstract explicitly states: "To support open research, all model weights are published publicly."

**Why This Matters**: The transparency advantage is not merely philosophical. Section 4.3.1 documents a concrete reproducibility problem: "in our internal benchmarking of a popular open-source evaluation framework in June 2025, we found 7.3% disagreement between their GPQA parser and GPT-4o grading." By releasing sample-level logs, Hermes 4 enables the community to diagnose such discrepancies rather than accepting aggregate scores at face value.

**Field Impact**: This shifts the burden of proof. Proprietary model claims can no longer rest solely on benchmark numbers — the open community can now produce comparable results with verifiable methodology. The competitive pressure moves from "who has the highest score" to "who can demonstrate their score is real."

---

#### Shift 2: Sequence Control Can Be Decoupled From Content Generation

**Prior Assumption**: Controlling generation length required either accepting performance degradation (truncation) or training on full sequences (risking model collapse from synthetic data recursion [64, 19]).

**Hermes 4 Demonstrates**: The `</think>`-only masking technique (Section 3.1) applies gradient updates only to the termination token, not the reasoning chain. Mathematically:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{length-control}} = -\sum_{t=1}^{T} m_t \cdot \log P(y_t | y_{&lt;t}, x)$$

where $m_t = 1$ only for `</think>` and `<eos>`, and $m_t = 0$ for all other tokens.

**Results**: Table 2 shows overlong rates dropped from $28.2\%$ to $0.1\%$ on AIME'24 ($-99.6\%$) with minimal accuracy loss ($+0.7\%$ relative). LiveCodeBench actually improved by $48.6\%$, suggesting forced termination within context enables more complete solutions.

**Why This Matters**: This introduces a new training objective formulation for sequence control that could generalize beyond reasoning traces. Potential applications include:
- Controlling response length for latency-sensitive deployments
- Teaching models to emit structured output markers (JSON closing braces, XML tags)
- Preventing infinite loops in agentic systems

**Field Impact**: The technique provides a principled alternative to RLHF-based length control, which requires reward modeling and can be unstable. The `</think>`-only approach is supervised, stable, and targeted.

---

#### Shift 3: Hybrid Capabilities Emerge From Data Design, Not Architecture

**Prior Assumption**: Models specialized in either reasoning or instruction-following because these capabilities compete for representational capacity. Achieving both required architectural modifications or ensemble approaches.

**Hermes 4 Demonstrates**: The dataset comprises 3.5 million reasoning samples and 1.6 million non-reasoning samples, with reasoning samples containing "an average of five times more tokens per sample than their non-reasoning counterparts" (Section 2). This token asymmetry — not architecture changes — produces hybrid capabilities.

**Results**: Table 3 shows the 405B model scores $81.9$ on AIME'24 (reasoning) and $93.7$ on Arena-Hard v1 (instruction-following) simultaneously. Section 5.1 documents "comparatively greater contextual fidelity" than proprietary models on role-play tasks while maintaining reasoning rigor.

**Why This Matters**: This suggests capability specialization is a training data problem, not a model architecture problem. The same transformer architecture can support diverse behavioral modes through deliberate data curation.

**Field Impact**: This lowers the barrier to multi-capability model development. Researchers can experiment with capability balancing through data design rather than architectural innovation, which is more accessible to resource-constrained teams.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The Hermes 4 work opens several concrete research avenues. We categorize these by feasibility and potential impact.

#### High-Priority Directions (Near-Term, High Impact)

**1. Termination Learning Generalization**

**Research Question**: Does `</think>`-only masking generalize to other sequence control problems beyond reasoning traces?

**Concrete Experiments**:
- Apply the technique to JSON/XML output generation (train only on closing brace/tag tokens)
- Test on dialogue turn-taking (train only on end-of-turn markers)
- Evaluate on code generation (train only on function completion tokens)

**Why This Matters**: If the technique generalizes, it provides a unified approach to structured output generation across domains. The mathematical formulation is domain-agnostic.

**Feasibility**: High. Requires only modifying loss masking in existing training pipelines.

---

**2. Token Asymmetry Principles for Multi-Capability Training**

**Research Question**: What token ratios optimize hybrid capability training for different capability pairs?

**Concrete Experiments**:
- Systematically vary reasoning:non-reasoning token ratios (1:1, 3:1, 5:1, 10:1)
- Test other capability pairs (coding:conversation, factual:creative, safe:unsafe)
- Measure capability interference vs. capability synergy at different ratios

**Why This Matters**: The Hermes 4 team used 5:1 token asymmetry based on empirical iteration (Section 2 notes reasoning samples are "intentionally token-heavy"). A principled understanding of optimal ratios would enable systematic capability engineering.

**Feasibility**: Medium. Requires multiple training runs but no architectural changes.

---

**3. Template Sensitivity Mechanisms**

**Research Question**: Why is Hermes 4 highly sensitive to chat template modifications (Section 5.3.2), and is this stable across fine-tuning?

**Concrete Experiments**:
- Probe attention patterns when assistant token changes from `assistant` to `me`
- Test whether downstream fine-tuning preserves or eliminates template sensitivity
- Evaluate whether template modifications affect safety-relevant behaviors

**Why This Matters**: Section 5.3.2 documents that changing `<|start_header_id|>assistant<|end_header_id|>` to `<|start_header_id|>me<|end_header_id|>` produces "markedly different behaviors" with "fewer meta-disclaimers." Understanding this mechanism could enable controllable persona adoption without fine-tuning — or reveal vulnerability to adversarial template manipulation.

**Feasibility**: Medium. Requires interpretability tooling and systematic behavioral testing.

---

#### Medium-Priority Directions (Moderate Effort, Speculative Impact)

**4. Compositional Data Synthesis Beyond Language**

**Research Question**: Can the nested graph approach (Section 2.1.3) apply to other data synthesis domains?

**Concrete Experiments**:
- Apply DataForge-style graphs to image caption generation workflows
- Test on multi-modal data synthesis (image + text + audio)
- Evaluate whether graph nesting enables curriculum learning

**Why This Matters**: The graph-as-node abstraction (Figure 1b) enables arbitrary workflow complexity through composition. If this generalizes, it could transform synthetic data generation across modalities.

**Feasibility**: Low-Medium. Requires adapting DataForge to non-text domains.

---

**5. Length-Control Budget Optimization**

**Research Question**: Can the optimal token budget be predicted from model size and task domain, rather than discovered through expensive iteration?

**Concrete Experiments**:
- Measure overlong rates across model sizes (1B, 7B, 14B, 70B, 405B) without length control
- Fit a scaling law relating model size to natural termination behavior
- Test whether task domain (math vs. code vs. writing) affects optimal budget

**Why This Matters**: Appendix B documents that 20k-token budgets **increased** overlong rates (Table 5), while 30k succeeded. The team discovered this through expensive iteration. A predictive model would reduce tuning costs for future work.

**Feasibility**: Medium. Requires systematic measurement across model sizes.

---

#### Speculative Directions (High Risk, High Reward)

**6. Behavioral Mode Switching Via Structural Cues**

**Research Question**: Can template sensitivity be leveraged for intentional behavioral mode switching without fine-tuning?

**Concrete Experiments**:
- Design template tokens that activate distinct behavioral modes (reasoning, creative, safety-focused)
- Test whether modes are composable (e.g., reasoning + creative simultaneously)
- Evaluate mode stability across multi-turn conversations

**Why This Matters**: If structural cues can reliably activate behavioral modes, this enables dynamic capability switching without model switching — reducing deployment complexity.

**Feasibility**: Low. Mechanism is not well understood; may be unstable.

---

**7. Judge Model Bias Characterization**

**Research Question**: How do LLM judge preferences shape synthetic data quality and downstream model capabilities?

**Concrete Experiments**:
- Compare datasets filtered by different judge models (same rubric, different judges)
- Measure correlation between judge preferences and benchmark performance
- Develop judge ensemble methods to reduce individual judge bias

**Why This Matters**: Section 2.1.2 notes the team uses LLM judges with "different weights from the answer model" to prevent self-preference, but does not characterize judge bias more broadly. If judge preferences systematically shape model capabilities, this is a critical confound in synthetic data approaches.

**Feasibility**: Medium. Requires multiple judge models and controlled data synthesis experiments.
