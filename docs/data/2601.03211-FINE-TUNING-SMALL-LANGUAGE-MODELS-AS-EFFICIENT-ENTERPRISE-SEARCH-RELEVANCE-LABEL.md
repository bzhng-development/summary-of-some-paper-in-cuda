## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the critical scarcity of high-quality labeled data for enterprise search by introducing a synthetic data generation pipeline that distills the relevance labeling capabilities of a large language model (GPT-4o) into a compact Small Language Model (`Phi-3.5 Mini Instruct`). By leveraging LLM-generated queries, BM25-retrieved hard negatives, and graded relevance scores, the authors create a domain-specific dataset that enables the fine-tuned SLM to achieve human-aligned performance (NDCG of 0.953 and pairwise accuracy of 63.81%) that matches or slightly exceeds the teacher model, while delivering a **17× increase in throughput** and being **19× more cost-effective**. This approach solves the bottleneck of expensive, slow LLM-based labeling and infeasible manual annotation, enabling scalable, rapid offline evaluation for complex, context-dependent enterprise retrieval systems.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this work, we must first distinguish between the well-trodden path of **web search** and the complex, under-served domain of **enterprise search**. While both involve retrieving information based on a user query, the underlying mechanics of relevance differ fundamentally, creating a specific data scarcity problem that standard solutions cannot solve.

### The Enterprise Search Gap: Context vs. Common Knowledge

In traditional web search, queries rely on global, common knowledge. If a user searches for "Juno release date," the intent is unambiguous: they are asking about the 2007 film. Public benchmarks like MS MARCO or TREC-CAsT have driven massive progress here because the data is public, the context is universal, and human annotators can easily judge relevance.

Enterprise search, however, operates in a closed, heterogeneous ecosystem of emails, chats, internal documents, and knowledge bases. Here, the same query—"Juno release date"—could refer to a software project codenamed "Juno," a colleague named Juno, or a specific internal milestone. As illustrated in **Table 1**, enterprise queries are:
*   **Persona-specific:** Relevance depends on who is asking (e.g., a project manager vs. an engineer).
*   **Context-dependent:** Understanding requires access to internal metadata (author, folder structure) and organizational history.
*   **Hybrid in nature:** They often combine semantic intent ("find the budget report") with strict lexical constraints (specific file names or author surnames).

This creates a **data vacuum**. Unlike web search, there are no public, high-quality datasets for enterprise search because:
1.  **Privacy Constraints:** Real enterprise query logs contain sensitive confidential information, making them impossible to share or use for training without rigorous (and often impractical) anonymization.
2.  **Annotation Difficulty:** Even if data were available, hiring human annotators to judge relevance is infeasible. Annotators lack the specific organizational context (e.g., knowing who "Lisa" is or what the "Iris project" entails) required to judge if a document is truly relevant.
3.  **Bias in Interaction Data:** Click logs, often used as weak supervision in web search, are highly biased in enterprise settings. Users frequently click the top result regardless of quality due to habit or desperation, making clicks a poor proxy for true relevance.

### The Limitations of Current Labeling Strategies

Faced with this lack of training data, the industry has largely turned to **Large Language Models (LLMs)** like GPT-4o to act as automated judges. These models can reason through complex contexts and assign relevance scores, effectively simulating a human expert. However, relying on frontier LLMs for large-scale labeling introduces two critical bottlenecks:

*   **Throughput Constraints:** LLMs are computationally heavy. Generating labels for millions of query-document pairs to evaluate a new ranking model can take days or weeks, severely slowing down the iteration cycle for search engineers.
*   **Prohibitive Costs:** The API costs for running massive labeling jobs on frontier models are astronomical. For example, the paper notes that labeling costs with GPT-4o are roughly **$2.50 per 1M input tokens** and **$10.00 per 1M output tokens**. At enterprise scale, this makes continuous offline evaluation economically unsustainable.

Conversely, **Small Language Models (SLMs)** offer a potential solution due to their speed and low cost. However, a naive SLM cannot simply be dropped into this role. Without specific fine-tuning on enterprise-style data, an SLM lacks the nuanced understanding of internal metadata and hybrid query structures. Prior work has successfully fine-tuned SLMs for *web* search relevance, but these models fail in the enterprise domain because they haven't seen the specific distribution of "keyword + semantic" queries found in corporate environments.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

This paper positions itself at the intersection of **synthetic data generation** and **model distillation**, addressing a gap left by previous research:

*   **vs. Manual Annotation:** The authors argue that manual labeling is effectively impossible at scale for enterprise data due to privacy and context barriers. Their method bypasses the need for human judges entirely after the initial benchmark creation.
*   **vs. Direct LLM Labeling:** While acknowledging that LLMs (the "teacher") provide high-quality labels, the paper argues they are inefficient for *production-scale* evaluation. The goal is not to replace the LLM's intelligence, but to **distill** it into a cheaper, faster student model.
*   **vs. Prior SLM Fine-tuning:** Previous studies (e.g., [10], [7]) have distilled LLMs into SLMs for re-ranking, but primarily for open-domain, semantic-only tasks. This paper is novel because it targets the **hybrid lexical-semantic** nature of enterprise search. It explicitly addresses the failure of standard SLMs to generate or judge hard negatives (documents that look relevant but aren't) in a domain where exact keyword matching on metadata is crucial.

The core hypothesis is that by synthesizing a dataset that mimics the specific quirks of enterprise search—using an LLM to generate realistic queries from seed documents and BM25 to mine difficult negatives—we can train a small model (`Phi-3.5 Mini`) to match the teacher's accuracy while unlocking the throughput necessary for real-world deployment. This shifts the paradigm from "buying more GPU hours for bigger models" to "engineering better synthetic data for efficient models."

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a **data-centric distillation strategy** where the core innovation is not a new model architecture, but a rigorous pipeline for generating high-fidelity synthetic training data that allows a Small Language Model (SLM) to mimic the relevance judgment capabilities of a Large Language Model (LLM) specifically for the hybrid lexical-semantic nature of enterprise search.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system being built is an automated "relevance labeler" that takes a user query and a document as input and outputs a precise relevance score from 0 to 4, effectively acting as a fast, cheap substitute for a human expert or a slow, expensive LLM. The solution solves the problem of data scarcity by reversing the traditional workflow: instead of finding queries and labeling them, it starts with internal documents, uses an LLM to invent realistic queries that would retrieve them, finds "hard negative" documents that look similar but are wrong, and then uses the LLM to grade the pairs, creating a perfect dataset to train a small, efficient model.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The architecture functions as a four-stage assembly line transforming raw internal documents into a fine-tuned SLM.
*   **Component 1: Positive Query Generator:** Takes a seed document (content + metadata) and uses a teacher LLM (GPT-4o) to synthesize multiple realistic enterprise queries that a user might type to find that document.
*   **Component 2: Hard Negative Miner:** Takes the newly generated synthetic queries and runs them through a traditional lexical search engine (BM25) against the document corpus to retrieve top-k documents that are lexically similar but not the original source, creating challenging "distractor" examples.
*   **Component 3: LLM Labeler (Teacher):** Takes the constructed query-document pairs (both the original positive match and the mined negatives) and assigns a graded relevance score (0–4) to each, creating the ground-truth labels.
*   **Component 4: SLM Student Fine-Tuner:** Takes the resulting dataset of triplets (Query, Document, Score) and performs supervised fine-tuning on a small model (`Phi-3.5 Mini`), optionally augmented with multi-task learning data, to distill the teacher's judgment logic into a compact, high-throughput model.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   First, we analyze why the intuitive approach of training an SLM to *generate* queries failed due to positivity bias, establishing the necessity of the LLM-driven pipeline.
*   Second, we detail the **Positive Query Generation** stage, explaining how metadata templates and revision prompts ensure query diversity and realism.
*   Third, we examine the **Hard Negative Mining** process using BM25, clarifying why lexical retrieval is preferred over semantic retrieval for creating difficult training examples in this domain.
*   Fourth, we describe the **LLM Labeling and Quality Control** mechanism, including the specific 0–4 scoring rubric and the filtering logic used to clean noisy synthetic data.
*   Fifth, we outline the **SLM Fine-Tuning Strategy**, covering the multi-task curriculum, hyperparameters (batch size, epochs), and the specific removal of reasoning traces to optimize for speed.
*   Finally, we define the **Evaluation Framework**, explaining how NDCG and Pairwise Accuracy are calculated to measure alignment with human judgments.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### The Failed Baseline: Why Direct SLM Query Generation Was Abandoned
Before settling on the final pipeline, the authors attempted a more direct approach: fine-tuning the SLM (`Phi-3.5 Mini`) to act as a query generator itself, conditioned on a document and a target relevance score.
*   The hypothesis was that an SLM could learn to generate both relevant queries (score 4) and irrelevant queries (score 0) if trained on synthetic examples created by GPT-4o.
*   However, empirical results showed this approach achieved only **60.8% binary relevance accuracy**, meaning nearly 40% of the generated "negative" queries were actually relevant to the document.
*   The failure stems from two specific mechanisms: first, instruction-tuned SLMs possess an inherent **positivity bias**, where they strive to be helpful and thus struggle to generate intentionally "bad" or irrelevant queries even when explicitly prompted to do so.
*   Second, the limited capacity of the small model prevents it from internalizing the subtle constraints required for enterprise search, such as generating a query that matches keywords but fails on specific metadata (e.g., wrong author or date), leading to a lack of diverse hard negatives.
*   Consequently, the authors abandoned training the SLM as a generator and instead reserved the SLM strictly for the role of a **relevance labeler**, relying on the larger teacher LLM for all data generation steps.

#### Stage 1: Positive Query Generation with Metadata Templates
The core of the data pipeline begins with a curated set of 1,500 proprietary enterprise documents, which serve as the "seed" for generating realistic search traffic.
*   The system does not ask the LLM to generate queries from scratch; instead, it employs a **template-based approach** derived from analyzing real enterprise query logs to ensure structural realism.
*   These templates define specific patterns of how employees search, such as `<author name> <file name>` or `<folder name> <document type> <keyword>`, reflecting the hybrid lexical-semantic nature of enterprise queries.
*   For each seed document, the system extracts up to **6 single-word keywords** from the content and retrieves structured metadata (Author, Title, File Type, Parent Folder).
*   The system performs **weighted sampling** over the template table, selecting patterns with probability proportional to their observed frequency in real traffic, ensuring the synthetic dataset mirrors the distribution of actual user behavior.
*   Once a template and document are paired, the system invokes **GPT-4o** with a specific prompt (outlined in **Box 3.1**) to generate **3 distinct queries** that fill the template slots using the extracted metadata and keywords.
*   To address the issue of low diversity—where the LLM might repeatedly select the same substring for a metadata field—the pipeline includes a mandatory **Query Revision Stage**.
*   In this revision step (detailed in **Box 3.2**), a second LLM call rewrites the three generated queries to ensure that each metadata component is phrased differently across the set, enforcing variation while maintaining strict adherence to the metadata order.
*   This two-step generation and revision process yields a pool of high-quality, diverse positive query-document pairs where the intent is unambiguously aligned with the source document.

#### Stage 2: Hard Negative Mining via BM25
A robust relevance labeler must learn to distinguish between documents that are truly relevant and those that merely look relevant; this requires "hard negatives."
*   For every synthetic query generated in Stage 1, the system executes a search against the entire document corpus using the **Okapi BM25** algorithm, a standard lexical retrieval function.
*   The system retrieves the top-$k$ documents, explicitly **excluding the original source document** from which the query was generated.
*   The choice of $k$ is critical; the authors empirically set **$k=4$**, finding that this specific value yields a uniform distribution of relevance scores (0–4) after the subsequent labeling step.
*   BM25 is chosen over dense (semantic) retrievers because enterprise search relies heavily on exact keyword matching (e.g., specific project codes or file names), and BM25 effectively surfaces documents that share lexical overlap but may differ in semantic intent.
*   The retrieved candidates form a spectrum of difficulty: some may be "weak positives" (discussing the same topic but wrong context), while others are "hard negatives" (sharing keywords but completely irrelevant), mimicking the ambiguity of real search results.
*   By treating these retrieved documents as unlabelled candidates rather than assuming they are irrelevant, the pipeline preserves the nuance required for graded relevance scoring.

#### Stage 3: LLM-Based Graded Labeling and Quality Control
With a set of query-document pairs (positives from Stage 1 and candidates from Stage 2), the system must assign ground-truth relevance scores to create the training signal.
*   The system prompts **GPT-4o** with a structured labeling instruction (**Box 3.3**) to assign a discrete integer score $r \in \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4\}$ to each pair.
*   The scoring rubric is ordinal: **0** indicates bad quality/irrelevance, **4** indicates ideal quality/perfect match, and intermediate scores represent varying degrees of partial relevance.
*   To ensure data integrity, an automated quality control loop checks the LLM outputs for formatting errors or missing scores; in a dataset of roughly **24,000 samples**, only **15 cases** required re-labeling due to invalid output.
*   A specific **filtering mechanism** handles contradictions: if a synthetically generated "positive" query (from Stage 1) receives a low score (0 or 1) from the teacher LLM, the system triggers a re-labeling attempt.
*   If the low score persists upon re-evaluation, the pair is retained as a valid negative example (acknowledging the LLM generator made a mistake); otherwise, the contradictory sample is discarded to prevent confusing the student model.
*   This process results in a finalized dataset of triplets $(q, d, r)$, where $q$ is the query, $d$ is the document, and $r$ is the teacher-assigned relevance score.

#### Stage 4: SLM Fine-Tuning and Multi-Task Curriculum
The final stage involves distilling the knowledge from the labeled dataset into the small model, `Phi-3.5 Mini Instruct`.
*   The training objective is supervised fine-tuning (SFT) where the model learns to predict the relevance score $r$ given the input pair $(q, d)$.
*   A crucial design choice is the **removal of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning** from the output; while the teacher LLM might explain its reasoning, the student model is trained to output **only the final score**.
*   This decision is driven by efficiency: including explanations increases token count and inference latency, and empirical analysis showed that explanations introduced inconsistent training signals without improving final accuracy.
*   To enhance generalization beyond the narrow synthetic enterprise domain, the authors employ a **Multi-Task Tuning** strategy illustrated in **Figure 3**.
*   The curriculum proceeds in two phases: first, the model is fine-tuned on the **INTERS** dataset (~250k samples across 19 tasks) to build broad query-document understanding capabilities.
*   Second, the model undergoes continuous fine-tuning on a mixture of three datasets: the **synthetic enterprise triplets**, **TREC-CAsT** (4k human-labeled conversational queries), and **MS MARCO** passages augmented with GPT-4o generated synthetic queries.
*   The inclusion of public web search data (TREC-CAsT, MS MARCO) acts as a regularizer, preventing the model from overfitting to the specific quirks of the synthetic enterprise data while reinforcing general relevance concepts.
*   **Hyperparameters:** The model is trained for **2 epochs** with a maximum sequence length of **4096 tokens**.
*   The effective batch size is **32**, achieved by setting a per-device batch size of **4** and using **8 gradient accumulation steps** on a cluster of **eight NVIDIA A100 GPUs**.
*   Training logs are recorded every **40 steps**, and evaluation is performed every **80 steps** on a held-out human-labeled test set, though no early stopping is applied based on these metrics.
*   The training throughput on this hardware configuration is approximately **5.84 samples per second**.

#### Mathematical Formulation of the Objective
The fine-tuning process optimizes the parameters $\theta$ of the SLM to maximize the likelihood of the correct relevance score given the query and document context.
*   Let $q$ denote the query text and $d$ denote the document text (including metadata).
*   Let $y \in \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4\}$ be the target relevance score assigned by the teacher LLM.
*   The model outputs a probability distribution over the possible scores, denoted as $P_\theta(y | q, d)$.
*   The loss function $\mathcal{L}$ used for optimization is the standard cross-entropy loss:
$$ \mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\sum_{(q, d, y) \in \mathcal{D}} \log P_\theta(y | q, d) $$
*   Here, $\mathcal{D}$ represents the mixed training dataset comprising synthetic enterprise triplets and the auxiliary public datasets.
*   Unlike generative tasks where the model predicts a sequence of tokens, here the output space is constrained to the discrete set of integers representing the relevance scale, effectively treating the problem as a 5-class classification task formulated within a generative framework.

#### Evaluation Metrics and Statistical Validation
To verify that the distilled SLM matches the teacher, the authors employ two primary metrics calculated against a gold-standard benchmark of **923 human-annotated query-document pairs**.
*   **Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG):** This metric evaluates the quality of the entire ranked list of documents for a query, rewarding models that place highly relevant documents (higher $y$) at the top of the list.
    *   The gain for a document at rank $i$ with relevance $rel_i$ is calculated as $(2^{rel_i} - 1) / \log_2(i + 1)$.
    *   The score is normalized by the ideal DCG (IDCG), ensuring the final value lies between 0 and 1, with higher values indicating better ranking alignment with human judgments.
*   **Pairwise Accuracy:** This metric measures the model's ability to correctly order any two documents $A$ and $B$ for a given query.
    *   For every pair $(A, B)$, if the ground truth states $A$ is more relevant than $B$ ($A > B$) and the model predicts $score(A) > score(B)$, the pair is counted as correct.
    *   The final accuracy is the proportion of correctly ordered pairs across all possible combinations for all queries, as detailed in **Table 3**.
*   **Statistical Significance:** To claim the SLM is "on par" with GPT-4o, the authors perform a **one-sided paired non-inferiority test** using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test.
    *   The null hypothesis $H_0$ assumes the SLM performs worse than GPT-4o by a margin $\Delta$.
    *   They set conservative margins of $\Delta = 0.1\%$ for accuracy and $\Delta = 0.0001$ for NDCG.
    *   With p-values of **0.012** (accuracy) and **0.00098** (NDCG), both below the 0.05 threshold, they reject the null hypothesis, statistically confirming that the SLM is non-inferior to the teacher model within negligible margins.

#### Efficiency Gains: Throughput and Cost
The architectural choice to distill into an SLM yields quantifiable operational benefits essential for enterprise scale.
*   **Throughput:** The fine-tuned `Phi-3.5 Mini` achieves **873.33 Requests Per Minute (RPM)** on a single A100 GPU, which scales to nearly **7,000 RPM** on the 8-GPU cluster.
*   This represents a **17× increase** in throughput compared to typical LLM labelers, which are often bottlenecked at a few hundred RPM due to their massive parameter counts and sequential generation requirements.
*   **Cost:** The cost analysis highlights a **19× reduction** in expenses.
    *   Input token costs are **$0.13** per 1M tokens for the SLM versus **$2.50** for GPT-4o.
    *   Output token costs are **$0.52** per 1M tokens for the SLM versus **$10.00** for GPT-4o.
*   These figures demonstrate that the system enables continuous, large-scale offline evaluation of ranking models—a task previously prohibitively expensive—by shifting the computational load from expensive inference-time LLM calls to a one-time training cost on efficient hardware.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper's primary contribution is not a new model architecture, but a **methodological shift** in how we approach data scarcity for domain-specific retrieval. By treating the data generation pipeline as the core innovation rather than the model itself, the authors demonstrate that small models can outperform large teachers when trained on high-fidelity, domain-mimicking synthetic data. The following insights distinguish this work from prior distillation efforts.

### 4.1 The Necessity of "Hard Negative" Mining via Lexical Retrieval
Prior work in synthetic data generation for retrieval (e.g., InPars, Promptagator) often relies on the teacher LLM to generate both queries and negative documents, or assumes that random documents serve as sufficient negatives. This paper identifies a critical flaw in that approach for **enterprise search**: LLMs struggle to generate "plausible but wrong" documents that respect the strict lexical constraints of internal data (e.g., specific project codes, file names).

*   **The Innovation:** Instead of asking the LLM to invent negatives, the authors decouple generation from retrieval. They use **BM25** (a traditional lexical retriever) to mine hard negatives *after* generating the query. As detailed in **Section 3.2**, this ensures the training set contains documents that share significant keyword overlap with the query (making them "hard") but fail on specific metadata or context (making them true negatives).
*   **Why It Matters:** This hybrid approach bridges the gap between semantic understanding (provided by the LLM generator) and lexical precision (enforced by BM25). Without this step, an SLM would likely learn to rely solely on semantic similarity, failing to distinguish between a "Budget Report 2023" and a "Budget Report 2022"—a distinction that is trivial for keyword matching but often subtle for pure semantic models. The result is a labeler that respects the **hybrid lexical-semantic nature** of enterprise queries, a capability missing in models trained only on semantic negatives.

### 4.2 Overcoming "Positivity Bias" in Small Model Generation
A counter-intuitive but vital finding in this work is the failure of Small Language Models (SLMs) to act as *generators* of diverse training data, even when fine-tuned.

*   **The Innovation:** The authors explicitly test and reject the hypothesis that an SLM can be fine-tuned to generate negative queries (queries that should *not* retrieve a document). As shown in **Section 3.1**, the SLM achieved only **60.8% accuracy** in this task due to an inherent **positivity bias**: instruction-tuned models are optimized to be helpful and cooperative, making them psychologically incapable of intentionally generating "bad" or irrelevant queries.
*   **Why It Matters:** This insight prevents a common pitfall in efficient AI development. Many practitioners might assume that distilling a generator into an SLM saves costs. This paper proves that for relevance labeling, **the teacher LLM must remain the sole generator** to ensure data diversity and correctness. The SLM's role is strictly limited to *judging* relevance. This architectural constraint—reserving the LLM for data creation and the SLM for inference—is a fundamental design principle for building reliable, low-cost labeling systems.

### 4.3 Data Quality > Data Quantity: The Diminishing Returns of Scale
In an era where "more data is better" is the dominant paradigm, this paper provides empirical evidence that for domain-specific fine-tuning, **data curation quality outweighs raw volume**.

*   **The Innovation:** The ablation study in **Table 4** reveals a striking plateau in performance. Increasing the synthetic dataset size from **14K to 24K samples** yielded negligible gains (NDCG moved from 0.953 to 0.954). However, removing the **Query Revision** step (which ensures diversity in metadata phrasing) caused a measurable drop in performance (NDCG fell to 0.943), even with the larger dataset.
*   **Why It Matters:** This challenges the assumption that scaling synthetic data indefinitely improves student models. The authors demonstrate that once a threshold of representative examples is reached, the **diversity and realism** of the queries (achieved via the revision prompt in **Box 3.2**) become the limiting factor. This insight allows enterprises to build effective models with manageable compute budgets, focusing engineering effort on prompt engineering and data filtering rather than massive data generation runs.

### 4.4 Distilling "Calibration" Rather Than Just Accuracy
Most distillation papers focus on matching the teacher's top-1 accuracy. This paper highlights a more subtle but operationally critical metric: **score calibration**, specifically the ability to assign low scores to truly irrelevant documents.

*   **The Innovation:** The qualitative analysis in **Section 4** notes that both the vanilla SLM and the teacher LLM (GPT-4o) tend to be "overly optimistic," rarely assigning the lowest relevance score (0) even to off-topic documents. The fine-tuned SLM, however, learns to confidently assign **0s** to irrelevant pairs, aligning better with human annotators.
*   **Why It Matters:** In search evaluation, a model that cannot distinguish between "bad" and "mediocre" is useless for filtering noise. By training on a dataset where hard negatives are explicitly labeled as 0 or 1 (via the BM25 mining process), the SLM learns a sharper decision boundary than its teacher. This suggests that **specialized fine-tuning can actually correct the teacher's biases**, producing a student model that is not just faster, but *more disciplined* in its judgments than the frontier model it mimics.

### 4.5 Multi-Task Tuning as a Regularizer for Synthetic Data
While the core dataset is synthetic, the paper innovates by using public, non-enterprise datasets not as the primary training signal, but as a **regularizer** to prevent overfitting to the synthetic distribution.

*   **The Innovation:** As illustrated in **Figure 3** and **Table 4** (Row 7), training *only* on public web data (MS MARCO, TREC-CAsT) fails to capture enterprise nuances. Conversely, training *only* on synthetic enterprise data risks overfitting to the specific templates used in generation. The solution is a **curriculum**: pre-training on the diverse **INTERS** dataset, then fine-tuning on a mix of synthetic enterprise data and public benchmarks.
*   **Why It Matters:** This approach leverages the breadth of public data to maintain general language understanding while using the synthetic data to inject domain specificity. It demonstrates that synthetic data does not need to replace public data; rather, the two are complementary. The public data acts as an anchor, ensuring the model doesn't learn spurious correlations present in the synthetic templates, while the synthetic data provides the specific signal needed for the enterprise domain.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical validation of the proposed pipeline. The authors do not merely claim their model is "better"; they construct a rigorous evaluation framework designed to answer three specific questions: Does the distilled Small Language Model (SLM) truly match the teacher's intelligence? Does it outperform the untrained baseline? And crucially, does it achieve this with the promised efficiency gains? The experimental design is notable for its reliance on a **human-annotated gold standard** rather than relying solely on LLM-as-a-judge metrics, which avoids the circular reasoning often found in synthetic data papers.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: The Gold Standard Benchmark

To fairly assess the model, the authors needed a test set that reflects the complexity of real enterprise search without leaking information from the training process.

*   **The Test Dataset:** The evaluation relies on a proprietary benchmark of **923 enterprise query–document pairs**. Unlike the training data, which was synthetically generated, these pairs were annotated by **trained human annotators**.
    *   **Scale:** The dataset covers **228 distinct queries**, ensuring that the evaluation measures generalization across different search intents rather than memorization of specific queries.
    *   **Annotation Protocol:** Human judges assigned relevance scores on the same **0–4 ordinal scale** used for training (0 = Irrelevant, 4 = Ideal). This alignment allows for a direct, apple-to-apples comparison between the model's output and human judgment.
    *   **Isolation:** This dataset was strictly held out from the training pipeline. It was used *only* for final evaluation and statistical testing, ensuring no data contamination.

*   **Baselines for Comparison:** The paper compares the proposed fine-tuned SLM against two critical baselines:
    1.  **Vanilla SLM:** The base `Phi-3.5 Mini Instruct` model without any domain-specific fine-tuning. This establishes the floor performance of a small model attempting this task zero-shot.
    2.  **Teacher LLM (GPT-4o):** The state-of-the-art large language model used to generate the training data. This is the "ceiling" the authors aim to reach. If the SLM cannot match GPT-4o, the distillation has failed.

*   **Metrics:** The authors employ two complementary metrics to capture different aspects of ranking quality, as detailed in **Section 2.3**:
    *   **Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG):** This metric evaluates the quality of the *entire ranked list* of documents for a query. It heavily penalizes models that place highly relevant documents (scores 3 or 4) lower in the list. The formula uses exponential gain $(2^{rel_i} - 1)$ discounted by logarithmic rank position $\log_2(i+1)$.
    *   **Pairwise Accuracy:** This metric measures the model's ability to correctly order *any two documents* $(A, B)$ for a given query. If the ground truth says $A$ is more relevant than $B$, does the model assign $score(A) > score(B)$? As illustrated in **Table 3**, this provides a granular view of the model's discriminative power, independent of the exact rank position.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results: Matching and Surpassing the Teacher

The core claim of the paper is that a small model, when trained on high-quality synthetic data, can match or exceed a large teacher model. The results in **Figure 4** and **Table 4** provide compelling evidence for this.

*   **The Distillation Leap:** The impact of the proposed pipeline is stark when comparing the Vanilla SLM to the Fine-Tuned SLM.
    *   **NDCG:** The score jumps from **0.815** (Vanilla) to **0.953** (Fine-Tuned). This massive increase indicates that the untrained SLM struggles significantly with enterprise nuances, while the fine-tuned version has successfully internalized the relevance criteria.
    *   **Pairwise Accuracy:** Similarly, accuracy improves from **42.15%** to **63.81%**. The baseline model is barely better than random guessing for pairwise comparisons, whereas the fine-tuned model demonstrates robust discriminative ability.

*   **Surpassing the Teacher:** Perhaps the most surprising result is that the student model slightly outperforms the teacher (GPT-4o) on the human benchmark.
    *   **GPT-4o Performance:** On the same human-annotated test set, GPT-4o achieves an NDCG of **0.944** and a Pairwise Accuracy of **62.58%**.
    *   **The Gap:** The Fine-Tuned SLM exceeds GPT-4o by **+0.009 in NDCG** and **+1.23% in Pairwise Accuracy**.
    *   **Interpretation:** This counter-intuitive result (student > teacher) is explained in the **Qualitative Analysis** (Section 4). The authors observe that GPT-4o suffers from "optimism bias," often hesitating to assign the lowest score (0) even to clearly irrelevant documents. The fine-tuned SLM, exposed to hard negatives explicitly labeled as 0 during training, learns to be more decisive and calibrated, aligning *better* with human annotators who are willing to mark bad results as completely irrelevant.

*   **Statistical Significance:** To ensure these differences are not due to random chance, the authors perform a **one-sided paired non-inferiority test** using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test.
    *   They set extremely conservative non-inferiority margins ($\Delta = 0.1\%$ for accuracy, $\Delta = 0.0001$ for NDCG).
    *   The resulting p-values are **0.012** (Accuracy) and **0.00098** (NDCG). Since both are well below the standard $\alpha = 0.05$ threshold, the null hypothesis (that the SLM is worse than GPT-4o) is rejected. This statistically confirms that the SLM is non-inferior to, and practically equivalent to, the frontier LLM.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies: Dissecting the Pipeline's Components

The paper includes a rigorous ablation study (**Table 4**) to determine which components of the pipeline are essential. This analysis prevents the "kitchen sink" problem, where success is attributed to the whole system without understanding the drivers.

*   **Data Quality vs. Quantity:** A key finding challenges the "more data is always better" heuristic.
    *   Comparing **Row 2 (14K samples)** and **Row 3 (24K samples)** in Table 4 (both with multi-task tuning and revision), the performance is nearly identical (NDCG 0.953 vs. 0.954).
    *   This suggests a **diminishing returns threshold**: once the dataset reaches ~14K high-quality, diverse examples, adding more synthetic data yields negligible gains. This is a crucial insight for cost-efficiency, implying that enterprises do not need to generate massive datasets to achieve peak performance.

*   **The Critical Role of Query Revision:** The importance of the "Query Revision" step (described in Section 3.2) is highlighted by comparing **Row 2** (with revision) and **Row 6** (without revision).
    *   Removing the revision step causes NDCG to drop from **0.953** to **0.943** and Accuracy from **63.81%** to **60.97%**.
    *   This drop confirms that **diversity in query phrasing** is more valuable than raw volume. Without the revision prompt forcing the LLM to vary how it uses metadata, the training data becomes repetitive, leading to overfitting on specific phrasing patterns.

*   **Necessity of Multi-Task Tuning:** The authors test whether the synthetic enterprise data alone is sufficient.
    *   Comparing **Row 2** (with multi-task tuning) and **Row 4** (without), we see a clear benefit: NDCG drops from **0.953** to **0.946** without the auxiliary datasets (INTERS, TREC-CAsT, MS MARCO).
    *   This indicates that while synthetic enterprise data provides the domain signal, the public datasets act as a **regularizer**, preventing the model from forgetting general language capabilities and improving robustness.

*   **The Failure of Public Data Alone:** **Row 7** of Table 4 shows the result of training *only* on public datasets (excluding the synthetic enterprise data).
    *   The model achieves an NDCG of **0.826** and Accuracy of **42.88%**, which is only marginally better than the Vanilla SLM (0.815 / 42.15%).
    *   This definitively proves that **public web search data cannot substitute for domain-specific synthetic data**. The unique lexical-semantic hybrid nature of enterprise queries (e.g., relying on internal project names) is simply not present in MS MARCO or TREC-CAsT.

### 5.4 Efficiency and Throughput: The Operational Win

While accuracy is paramount, the primary motivation for this work is efficiency. The results here are transformative for enterprise deployment.

*   **Throughput (RPM):** The fine-tuned `Phi-3.5 Mini` achieves **873.33 Requests Per Minute (RPM)** on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU.
    *   Extrapolated to the 8-GPU cluster used for training, this equals nearly **7,000 RPM**.
    *   In contrast, typical LLM labelers (like GPT-4o via API) are limited to hundreds of RPM due to latency and rate limits. The paper quantifies this as a **17× increase in throughput**. This speed enables "rapid offline evaluation," allowing search engineers to iterate on ranking algorithms daily rather than weekly.

*   **Cost Effectiveness:** The cost analysis breaks down the expense per million tokens:
    *   **Input Tokens:** SLM costs **$0.13** vs. GPT-4o at **$2.50**.
    *   **Output Tokens:** SLM costs **$0.52** vs. GPT-4o at **$10.00**.
    *   Aggregating these factors, the fine-tuned SLM is **19× more cost-effective** than the teacher LLM. For an enterprise processing billions of query-document pairs for evaluation, this difference shifts the task from "prohibitively expensive" to "operationally trivial."

### 5.5 Critical Assessment: Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

The experiments presented in this paper are **highly convincing** and directly support the central claims, though with some important nuances.

*   **Strengths of the Evidence:**
    *   **Human Ground Truth:** By evaluating against 923 human-annotated pairs rather than just comparing SLM output to LLM output, the authors avoid the trap of measuring "distillation fidelity" instead of "actual quality." The fact that the SLM beats GPT-4o on *human* metrics is the strongest possible validation.
    *   **Comprehensive Ablations:** The step-by-step removal of pipeline components (Revision, Multi-task, Synthetic Data) clearly isolates the value of each design choice. The data explicitly shows that the *method* of data generation matters more than the *amount*.
    *   **Statistical Rigor:** The use of non-inferiority testing adds a layer of statistical confidence often missing in applied AI papers, moving beyond "it looks better" to "it is statistically indistinguishable or better."

*   **Limitations and Conditions:**
    *   **Domain Specificity:** The success is conditional on the quality of the **seed documents** and the **template table**. The paper assumes access to 1,500 curated enterprise documents and a method to derive query templates (either from logs or heuristic generation). An organization without *any* historical query data to build templates might struggle to replicate the exact diversity achieved here, though the paper suggests heuristic templates are a viable fallback.
    *   **Model Choice:** The results are specific to `Phi-3.5 Mini` and `GPT-4o`. While the authors argue the *pipeline* is generalizable, the exact 17×/19× efficiency gains depend on the specific hardware and pricing of these models at the time of writing. However, the relative gap between small and large models is likely to persist.
    *   **The "Black Box" of Templates:** The paper mentions deriving templates from real traffic frequencies but does not disclose the specific templates or the exact distribution due to confidentiality. While the *method* is clear, the precise sensitivity of the results to template quality remains a slight unknown.

In summary, the experimental analysis robustly demonstrates that the proposed synthetic data pipeline successfully bridges the gap between small model efficiency and large model accuracy. The ablation studies prove that **data curation (revision, hard negative mining)** is the true engine of performance, not just model scaling. The result is a system that is not only accurate but operationally viable for the massive scale required by modern enterprise search.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the proposed pipeline demonstrates that Small Language Models (SLMs) can achieve state-of-the-art relevance labeling for enterprise search, the approach is not a universal panacea. Its success relies on specific architectural choices and data availability assumptions that introduce distinct trade-offs. Understanding these limitations is crucial for practitioners attempting to replicate or adapt this method for different domains.

### 6.1 Dependency on Seed Documents and Template Engineering

The most significant structural assumption of this work is the availability of a **curated set of seed documents** and a mechanism to derive **query templates**.

*   **The "Cold Start" Problem:** The pipeline is fundamentally **document-centric**, not query-centric. It starts with 1,500 proprietary enterprise documents (Section 2.2) and generates queries *backwards* from them. This assumes the organization has access to a representative corpus of internal content (emails, chats, docs) that can be legally and technically processed.
    *   *Trade-off:* While this bypasses the need for sensitive query logs, it introduces a dependency on document quality. If the seed documents are unrepresentative of the actual information users seek (e.g., missing niche technical manuals or recent project updates), the synthetic queries will fail to cover those "long-tail" search intents. The model will be excellent at judging relevance for common document types but may hallucinate or fail on under-represented topics.
*   **Template Derivation Complexity:** The quality of the synthetic queries hinges on the **query pattern template table** (Section 3.2). The authors note these were derived from analyzing real search traffic frequencies.
    *   *Limitation:* For an organization with **zero** historical query logs (a true cold start), constructing this template table requires manual heuristic engineering or guessing. The paper suggests one can "systematically enumerate combinations of document metadata fields," but acknowledges that without frequency weighting, the resulting distribution of query types might not match real user behavior. If the templates are too rigid or miss common enterprise phrasing (e.g., specific slang or acronym usage), the SLM will learn a distorted view of relevance.

### 6.2 The "Positivity Bias" Constraint on Model Roles

A critical, non-obvious limitation revealed in Section 3.1 is the **inability of SLMs to act as negative query generators**.

*   **Architectural Asymmetry:** The authors explicitly abandoned the idea of fine-tuning the SLM to generate *irrelevant* queries, finding it achieved only **60.8% accuracy** due to an inherent "positivity bias" in instruction-tuned models.
    *   *Trade-off:* This forces a rigid separation of duties: the expensive Teacher LLM (GPT-4o) *must* handle all data generation (both positive and negative examples), while the SLM is restricted strictly to the role of a **relevance judge**.
    *   *Implication:* You cannot fully distill the *generative* capabilities of the teacher into the student for this specific task. If an application requires an SLM to actively generate diverse negative training data on the fly (e.g., for active learning loops), this pipeline does not support it. The cost savings are realized only at inference time (labeling), not during the data creation phase, which remains dependent on the costly teacher model.

### 6.3 Scalability of the Data Generation Pipeline

While the *inference* of the fine-tuned SLM is highly scalable (17× throughput), the *construction* of the training dataset inherits the bottlenecks of the Teacher LLM.

*   **Multi-Step LLM Calls:** Generating a single training triplet involves multiple LLM invocations:
    1.  Keyword extraction and initial query generation (GPT-4o).
    2.  Query revision for diversity (GPT-4o).
    3.  Relevance labeling of the query-document pair (GPT-4o).
    4.  Potential re-labeling for quality control (Section 3.2).
*   **Upfront Cost vs. Long-term Gain:** Although the final model is 19× cheaper to run, the **upfront cost** to generate the ~24,000 high-quality triplets is substantial. The paper does not explicitly quantify the total dollar cost or time required to generate this dataset using GPT-4o APIs.
    *   *Limitation:* For smaller organizations or domains where the data distribution shifts rapidly (requiring frequent re-generation of the dataset), the recurring cost of using a frontier LLM for data synthesis might outweigh the savings from using a cheap SLM for inference. The approach is most viable for stable domains where the dataset is generated once and used for extensive offline evaluation over long periods.

### 6.4 Domain Specificity and Generalization Risks

The model's strength—its deep specialization in enterprise search—is also its primary weakness regarding generalization.

*   **Overfitting to Enterprise Hybrids:** The ablation study (Table 4, Row 7) proves that training *only* on public web data (MS MARCO, TREC-CAsT) fails completely for enterprise tasks. Conversely, the heavy reliance on synthetic enterprise data risks overfitting to the specific metadata structures (e.g., `<Author> <File Name>`) present in the seed documents.
    *   *Mitigation vs. Residual Risk:* While the authors use **Multi-Task Tuning** (INTERS, TREC-CAsT) as a regularizer to prevent this, the core signal remains synthetic. If real-world enterprise queries evolve to include modalities not captured in the templates (e.g., complex multi-turn conversational context that isn't just a simple keyword lookup), the SLM may struggle to adapt without re-training.
*   **Lack of Personalization Context:** The paper acknowledges that enterprise relevance is "persona-specific" (Section 1). However, the synthetic data generation treats documents as static entities.
    *   *Gap:* The current pipeline generates queries based on document content and metadata but does not explicitly simulate **user identity** or **organizational role** as a variable. A query like "Q3 Budget" has different relevance for a CFO vs. an intern. The synthetic dataset assumes a generic "enterprise user," potentially missing nuanced relevance signals that depend on *who* is asking, which human annotators naturally account for.

### 6.5 Evaluation Scope and "Black Box" Templates

Finally, there are limitations in how the results are validated and reported.

*   **Proprietary Benchmark Bias:** The evaluation relies on a **private benchmark of 923 human-annotated pairs**. While this is necessary due to privacy constraints, it limits the reproducibility of the results. The community cannot verify if the "human alignment" holds across different industries (e.g., healthcare vs. finance) or different enterprise software stacks (e.g., Slack-heavy vs. Email-heavy cultures).
*   **Undisclosed Template Details:** Due to confidentiality, the paper does not release the specific **query templates** or their frequency distributions (Section 3.2).
    *   *Open Question:* It remains unclear how sensitive the performance is to the *exact* phrasing of these templates. If the 0.953 NDCG score is heavily dependent on a few highly specific, manually crafted templates, the method's portability to other domains is reduced. The claim that "heuristic templates" work is promising but lacks the granular evidence provided for the other components.

### Summary of Trade-offs

| Feature | Benefit | Trade-off / Limitation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Synthetic Data** | Solves data scarcity & privacy issues. | Requires high-quality seed docs & template engineering; upfront LLM cost. |
| **SLM Distillation** | 17× throughput, 19× cost reduction. | SLM cannot generate negatives (positivity bias); teacher LLM still needed for data prep. |
| **BM25 Negative Mining** | Creates realistic "hard negatives." | Relies on lexical overlap; may miss semantic negatives that BM25 fails to retrieve. |
| **Multi-Task Tuning** | Prevents overfitting to synthetic data. | Adds complexity to the training pipeline; requires access to diverse public datasets. |
| **Template-Based Gen** | Ensures structural realism of queries. | Risk of missing novel query patterns not covered by templates; cold-start difficulty. |

In conclusion, while this approach successfully democratizes high-quality relevance labeling for enterprise search, it shifts the burden from **data collection** to **data engineering**. Practitioners must be prepared to invest effort in curating seed documents, designing query templates, and managing the upfront costs of teacher-led data generation to unlock the long-term efficiency gains of the distilled SLM.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper marks a pivotal shift in the philosophy of building enterprise search systems: it moves the field away from the "bigger is better" paradigm toward a **"data-centric distillation"** model. By demonstrating that a Small Language Model (SLM) can outperform a frontier Large Language Model (LLM) on human-aligned relevance tasks when trained on high-fidelity synthetic data, the authors redefine the bottleneck of enterprise retrieval. The constraint is no longer model capacity or inference cost, but rather the **engineering rigor of the data generation pipeline**. This section explores how this work reshapes the landscape, enables new research avenues, and offers concrete guidance for practitioners.

### 7.1 Shifting the Landscape: From Model-Centric to Data-Centric AI

The primary implication of this work is the decoupling of **intelligence** from **scale**. Historically, achieving high-quality relevance labeling required deploying massive, expensive models like GPT-4o at inference time. This paper proves that the "intelligence" required for enterprise search—understanding hybrid lexical-semantic queries, respecting metadata constraints, and distinguishing hard negatives—can be compressed into a compact model (`Phi-3.5 Mini`) if the training data is sufficiently representative.

*   **Democratization of Enterprise Search:** Previously, only organizations with massive budgets could afford continuous, large-scale offline evaluation using LLM judges. With a **19× cost reduction** and **17× throughput increase**, high-quality relevance labeling becomes accessible to smaller teams and resource-constrained environments. This lowers the barrier to entry for developing sophisticated internal search tools.
*   **The End of "LLM-as-a-Judge" at Scale:** While LLMs remain the gold standard for *creating* training data, this work suggests they should no longer be the default choice for *running* evaluation pipelines. The role of the LLM shifts from an operational engine to a **data synthesizer**. This architectural separation allows organizations to leverage the reasoning power of frontier models once (during data generation) while reaping the efficiency benefits of SLMs indefinitely (during evaluation).
*   **Validation of Synthetic Data Quality:** The finding that **14K high-quality synthetic samples** outperform or match larger datasets challenges the prevailing "scale at all costs" narrative. It implies that for domain-specific tasks, **diversity and realism** (achieved here via query revision and BM25 hard negative mining) are far more critical than raw volume. This encourages a shift in research focus toward **prompt engineering, data curation strategies, and quality control mechanisms** rather than simply gathering more data.

### 7.2 Enabling Follow-Up Research

This work opens several fertile grounds for future investigation, particularly in refining the synthetic data pipeline and expanding the scope of distillation.

*   **Automated Template Discovery:** A current limitation is the reliance on a manually derived or heuristically constructed **query template table** (Section 3.2). Future research could explore using unsupervised clustering on limited, anonymized query logs to automatically discover and weight these templates, reducing the engineering overhead for new domains. Alternatively, reinforcement learning could be used to optimize the template distribution to maximize SLM performance on a validation set.
*   **Dynamic Hard Negative Mining:** The current pipeline uses **BM25** for static hard negative mining. While effective for lexical constraints, it may miss semantically plausible but contextually wrong documents that dense retrievers would find. Future work could investigate a **hybrid retrieval strategy** for negative mining, combining BM25 with dense vector search to create an even more challenging and diverse set of training negatives, potentially pushing SLM performance further.
*   **Personalized Relevance Distillation:** The current synthetic data assumes a generic enterprise user. However, enterprise relevance is deeply **persona-specific** (Section 1). A promising direction is to extend the synthetic generation pipeline to include **user persona attributes** (e.g., role, department, seniority) as input conditions. This would allow training SLMs that can predict relevance *conditioned on who is asking*, enabling personalized search evaluation at scale.
*   **Active Learning Loops:** Since the SLM is fast and cheap, it enables **active learning** workflows where the model identifies uncertain query-document pairs (where its confidence is low) and flags them for human review or re-labeling by the teacher LLM. This creates a feedback loop where the synthetic dataset is continuously refined based on the student model's weaknesses, rather than being a static, one-time generation.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The immediate impact of this approach extends beyond simple relevance labeling to broader search infrastructure improvements.

*   **Rapid Offline Ranking Evaluation:** The most direct application is accelerating the **development cycle of ranking models**. Search engineers can now evaluate new ranking algorithms against millions of query-document pairs in hours rather than days. This enables rapid iteration, A/B testing of ranking features, and hyperparameter tuning that was previously computationally prohibitive.
*   **Synthetic Benchmark Creation for Privacy-Sensitive Domains:** Beyond enterprise search, this pipeline offers a blueprint for creating high-quality benchmarks in other **privacy-constrained domains** (e.g., healthcare, legal, finance). By starting with seed documents and generating synthetic queries, organizations can build robust evaluation datasets without exposing sensitive user query logs or requiring human annotators with specialized domain knowledge.
*   **On-Device or Edge Search Relevance:** The efficiency of the fine-tuned `Phi-3.5 Mini` suggests potential for **on-device relevance scoring** in scenarios where data cannot leave the local environment due to strict compliance requirements. While the current setup uses A100 GPUs, further quantization of the distilled model could enable high-quality relevance filtering on local servers or even edge devices.
*   **Continuous Monitoring and Drift Detection:** Because the SLM is cheap to run, it can be deployed as a **continuous monitoring tool** for production search systems. It can periodically score live traffic to detect relevance drift (e.g., if a new document type causes a drop in relevance scores) or identify systemic failures in the ranking pipeline, triggering alerts before user satisfaction degrades.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

For practitioners looking to adopt this method, the decision to use this pipeline depends on specific organizational constraints and goals.

**When to Prefer This Method:**
*   **Privacy is Paramount:** If your organization cannot share query logs with external annotators or use them directly for training due to GDPR, HIPAA, or internal policies, this **document-centric synthetic approach** is ideal. It requires only access to the document corpus, not user behavior data.
*   **High-Volume Evaluation Needed:** If you need to evaluate ranking models on **millions of pairs** regularly (e.g., daily regression tests), the **17× throughput gain** makes this approach mandatory. Direct LLM labeling would be too slow and expensive for continuous integration pipelines.
*   **Domain Specificity is High:** If your search queries rely heavily on **internal jargon, project codes, or specific metadata structures** (the "hybrid" nature described in Section 1), generic public datasets (MS MARCO) will fail. This pipeline explicitly targets those nuances.

**When to Consider Alternatives:**
*   **Zero Seed Data:** If you lack a curated set of representative seed documents or any historical query logs to derive templates, the "cold start" problem (Section 6.1) may make this approach difficult. In such cases, starting with generic public datasets and fine-tuning on small amounts of human-labeled data might be more feasible initially.
*   **Dynamic, Conversational Contexts:** If your primary use case involves complex, multi-turn conversational search where context shifts rapidly within a session, the current static query-document pair generation may be insufficient. While TREC-CAsT data was used for regularization, the core synthetic pipeline focuses on single-turn queries.
*   **Immediate Deployment without Training Infrastructure:** This approach requires a **one-time investment** in GPU resources for fine-tuning (e.g., 8x A100s for the duration of training). If an organization lacks any training infrastructure and needs an immediate, zero-setup solution, using a managed LLM API (despite the cost) might be the only viable short-term option.

**Integration Checklist:**
1.  **Curate Seed Documents:** Select ~1,500 diverse, high-quality internal documents that represent the breadth of your corpus.
2.  **Define Query Templates:** Analyze available (anonymized) logs or brainstorm heuristics to create a weighted list of query patterns (e.g., `<Author> <Topic>`).
3.  **Run the Pipeline:** Execute the four-stage process: LLM Query Generation → BM25 Negative Mining → LLM Labeling → SLM Fine-Tuning.
4.  **Validate with Humans:** Before trusting the SLM for large-scale evaluation, validate its outputs against a small set (e.g., 100-200 pairs) of human judgments to ensure alignment in your specific context.

In summary, this work provides a robust, scalable blueprint for overcoming the data scarcity and cost barriers of enterprise search. By shifting the focus to **synthetic data engineering**, it empowers organizations to build efficient, high-performance relevance labelers that are tailored to their unique internal ecosystems, paving the way for more agile and intelligent search experiences.
