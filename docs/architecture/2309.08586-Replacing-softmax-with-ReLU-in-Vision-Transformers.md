## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the accuracy degradation previously observed when replacing softmax with point-wise activations in Vision Transformers by introducing a sequence length scaling factor ($L^{-1}$) to the ReLU function. Experiments training ViT models ranging from S/32 to L/16 on ImageNet-21k demonstrate that this `ReLU-attention` approach matches the scaling performance of traditional softmax attention as a function of compute cost (Figure 1). This finding is significant because it enables more efficient parallelization over the sequence length dimension with fewer gather operations, maintaining competitive ImageNet-1k accuracy without the computational overhead of softmax normalization.

## 2. Context and Motivation

The transformer architecture has become ubiquitous in modern machine learning, serving as the backbone for models in natural language processing and computer vision [26]. At the heart of the transformer lies the **attention mechanism**, which allows the model to weigh the importance of different parts of the input sequence relative to each other. To understand the motivation for this paper, we must first examine how standard attention works and why it presents a computational bottleneck.

### The Cost of Softmax in Attention
In a standard transformer, attention transforms queries ($q$), keys ($k$), and values ($v$) into outputs. The critical step involves computing attention weights $\alpha_{ij}$ using a softmax function over the dot products of queries and keys. As described in Section 3, this is typically formulated as:

$$
\alpha_{ij} = \text{softmax}\left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}} [q_i^\top k_1, ..., q_i^\top k_L] \right)_j
$$

where $L$ is the **sequence length** (the number of tokens in the input) and $d$ is the dimensionality of the keys.

While effective, the softmax operation introduces two specific computational costs that hinder efficiency:
1.  **Exponential Calculation:** Computing $e^x$ for every element is more expensive than simple arithmetic operations.
2.  **Normalization Sum:** Softmax requires summing over the entire sequence length $L$ to ensure the weights form a probability distribution (summing to 1).

This summation creates a dependency across the sequence. In parallel computing environments (such as TPUs or GPUs), this requires a **gather operation**, where data must be communicated across different processor cores to compute the global sum before the division can occur. As noted in Section 1, this makes parallelization challenging and limits memory efficiency [24, 7].

### The Accuracy-Efficiency Trade-off
Given these costs, there is significant motivation to replace softmax with cheaper, **point-wise activations** (functions that operate on each element independently, without needing a sum over the sequence). If successful, this would eliminate the gather operation, allowing attention to be parallelized more efficiently over the sequence length dimension.

However, a gap exists between theoretical efficiency and practical performance. Previous research observed significant **accuracy degradation** when simply swapping softmax for point-wise activations like ReLU [25, 14]. This created a dilemma:
*   **Keep Softmax:** Maintain high accuracy but suffer computational bottlenecks.
*   **Remove Softmax:** Gain efficiency but lose model performance.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Several prior works attempted to resolve this trade-off, but each introduced specific limitations that this paper seeks to address:

*   **ReLU without Scaling:** Previous studies explored substituting softmax with ReLU [25, 14] or squared ReLU [15]. However, Section 2 notes that these approaches **did not divide by sequence length**. The authors find experimentally that this missing scaling factor is critical for reaching accuracy comparable to softmax.
*   **Linear Attention:** Extensive literature exists on removing activation functions altogether to achieve linear complexity [16, 22, 18]. While useful for very long sequences, the authors note that in their experiments, removing the activation entirely reduced accuracy (Section 2).
*   **Normalization Retention:** Some work replaced softmax but still required normalization over the sequence length axis to ensure weights sum to one [21]. While this stabilizes training, it retains the downside of requiring a gather operation, negating the parallelization benefits (Section 2).
*   **Gated Units:** Other variants introduced gated attention units [15] but similarly failed to scale by sequence length. As shown later in the paper (Figure 4), gating alone does not remove the need for sequence length scaling.

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself at the intersection of efficiency and accuracy by identifying a specific missing component in prior softmax-free methods: **sequence length scaling**.

The authors hypothesize that the degradation in prior ReLU-based attention methods was not due to the absence of softmax itself, but due to the change in the **magnitude of the attention weights**. Standard softmax ensures that the expected attention weight $E_j[\alpha_{ij}]$ is $O(L^{-1})$ because the weights sum to 1 over $L$ elements. When using ReLU without scaling, this condition is violated.

By introducing a scaling factor of $L^{-\alpha}$ (specifically finding $\alpha \approx 1$ works best, as seen in Figure 2), the paper demonstrates that ReLU-attention can preserve the statistical properties necessary for stable training without requiring a global sum operation. This distinguishes the work from prior art by showing that:
1.  Normalization (summing to 1) is not strictly necessary; preserving the **scale** ($O(L^{-1})$) is sufficient.
2.  This scaling can be applied as a simple multiplicative factor, avoiding the gather operations required by explicit normalization [21].

Consequently, this work reframes the problem from "how do we approximate softmax?" to "how do we maintain the correct signal scale without softmax?". This shift allows the model to match the scaling behavior of traditional attention as a function of compute (Figure 1) while unlocking potential hardware efficiencies.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an empirical architecture modification paper that proposes replacing the softmax function in Vision Transformer attention with a scaled ReLU activation. The core idea is that dividing ReLU outputs by sequence length ($L^{-1}$) preserves the statistical properties of attention weights without requiring the costly normalization sum that softmax demands.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a modified Vision Transformer attention mechanism that replaces the standard softmax normalization with a simpler ReLU activation scaled by sequence length. This solves the problem of computational inefficiency in softmax (which requires expensive exponentiation and cross-token summation) while maintaining the accuracy that previous softmax-free methods failed to achieve.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The modified Vision Transformer maintains the same high-level structure as a standard ViT, with one critical change in the attention computation pipeline. First, input image patches are embedded into token representations. Second, these tokens pass through multiple transformer blocks, each containing multi-head attention and feed-forward layers. Third, within each attention head, queries and keys are computed and their dot products are passed through the modified activation function ($L^{-\alpha} \cdot \text{ReLU}$) instead of softmax. Fourth, the resulting attention weights multiply the values to produce outputs. Fifth, these outputs are projected and passed to the next layer. The only component that changes from standard ViT is the activation function $\phi$ applied to the query-key dot products before weighting the values.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Attention Modification**

This paper modifies the transformer attention mechanism by replacing the softmax function with a point-wise activation scaled by sequence length. In standard transformer attention, the mechanism transforms $d$-dimensional queries, keys, and values $\{q_i, k_i, v_i\}_{i=1}^L$ through a two-step procedure where $L$ represents the sequence length (number of tokens) and $d$ represents the feature dimension. The first step produces attention weights $\alpha_{ij}$ via the following equation:

$$
\alpha_{ij} = \phi\left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}} [q_i^\top k_1, ..., q_i^\top k_L] \right)_j
$$

In this equation, $q_i$ represents the query vector for token $i$, $k_j$ represents the key vector for token $j$, $d$ is the dimensionality of the key vectors, and $\phi$ is the activation function that is typically softmax in standard transformers. The second step uses these attention weights to compute outputs $o_i$ by taking a weighted sum of the values:

$$
o_i = \sum_{j=1}^L \alpha_{ij} v_j
$$

This report explores point-wise alternatives to $\phi$ that do not require computing a sum over the sequence length. The authors observe that $\phi = L^{-1} \cdot \text{ReLU}$ is a promising alternative to $\phi = \text{softmax}$ in the attention weight equation. They refer to attention with $\phi = L^{-1} \cdot \text{ReLU}$ as `ReLU-attention` throughout the paper. More generally, their experiments explore a family of scaled point-wise activations defined as $\phi = L^{-\alpha} h$ where $\alpha \in [0, 1]$ controls the scaling exponent and $h$ is chosen from the set $\{\text{ReLU, ReLU}^2, \text{GELU, softplus, identity, ReLU6, sigmoid}\}$.

**Why Sequence Length Scaling Matters**

The central technical insight is that scaling by a term involving sequence length $L$ is beneficial for achieving high accuracy, and this scaling is absent from prior work that removes softmax. The justification for sequence length scaling is primarily empirical, but the authors provide analytical motivation based on initialization dynamics. Transformers are currently designed with softmax attention for which $\sum_{j=1}^L \alpha_{ij} = 1$, which implies that the expected attention weight $E_j[\alpha_{ij}] = L^{-1}$. While it is unlikely that this normalization condition is strictly necessary, the function $\phi = L^{-1} \cdot \text{ReLU}$ does ensure that $E_j[\alpha_{ij}]$ is $O(L^{-1})$ at initialization. Preserving this condition may alleviate the need to change other hyperparameters when replacing softmax.

At initialization, the elements of query vectors $q$ and key vectors $k$ are $O(1)$ in magnitude, and so the scaled dot product $\frac{\langle q_i, k_j \rangle}{\sqrt{d}}$ will also be $O(1)$. Activation functions such as ReLU preserve $O(1)$ magnitude (with the exception of squared ReLU), and so a factor of $L^{-1}$ is necessary for $E_j[\alpha_{ij}]$ to be $O(L^{-1})$. Without this scaling factor, the attention weights would be $O(1)$ rather than $O(L^{-1})$, which would cause the output magnitudes to grow with sequence length and destabilize training.

**Experimental Configuration and Training Setup**

The experiments use ImageNet-21k and ImageNet-1k training configurations from the BigVision codebase without modifying hyperparameters. For ImageNet-21k, models train for 30 epochs, and for ImageNet-1k, models train for 300 epochs. As a result, both training runs use a roughly similar number of steps of around $9 \times 10^5$ steps. The experiments use Vision Transformers with qk-layernorm, in which queries and keys are passed through LayerNorm before computing attention weights. The authors use qk-layernorm by default as it was previously found to be necessary to prevent instability when scaling up model size, though they later ablate that this is not an important component at the scales they test.

The paper evaluates Vision Transformer models ranging from small to large scales, specifically S/32, S/16, S/8, B/32, B/16, and L/16 configurations where the letter indicates model size (Small, Base, Large) and the number indicates patch size. The authors use i21k and i1k to mean ImageNet-21k and ImageNet-1k respectively. They report ImageNet-1k accuracy for ImageNet-21k models by taking the top class among those that are in ImageNet-1k, without fine-tuning. When evaluating transfer performance on downstream tasks, they use a 10-shot linear probe averaged over three seeds. The downstream tasks include Caltech Birds, Caltech-101, Stanford Cars, CIFAR-100, DTD, ColHist, Pets, and UC Merced.

**Activation Function and Scaling Exponent Ablation**

Figure 2 examines the effect of sequence length scaling for various point-wise alternatives to softmax by plotting ImageNet-1k accuracy against the scaling exponent $\alpha$ on the x-axis. The y-axis displays accuracy for the S/32, S/16, and S/8 Vision Transformer models trained on ImageNet-21k. The experiments test $\alpha \in [0, 2]$ with different activation functions $h \in \{\text{ReLU, ReLU}^2, \text{GELU, softplus, identity, ReLU6, sigmoid}\}$. The results show that the best accuracy is typically achieved when $\alpha$ is close to 1, which validates the choice of $L^{-1}$ scaling. There is no clear best non-linearity at $\alpha \approx 1$, so the authors use ReLU in their main experiment for its computational speed.

**Effect of qk-layernorm**

Figure 3 shows the effect of removing qk-layernorm on attention with ReLU and squared ReLU scaled by $L^{-\alpha}$. The results are shown for the S/32, S/16, and S/8 Vision Transformer models trained on ImageNet-21k. The figure compares four conditions: ReLU with qk-layernorm, squared ReLU with qk-layernorm, ReLU without qk-layernorm, and squared ReLU without qk-layernorm. The results indicate that qk-layernorm does not have a large effect for these model scales, though this may change at larger scales where instability was previously observed. This ablation is important because it suggests that the sequence length scaling itself provides sufficient stabilization, potentially reducing the need for additional normalization layers.

**Effect of Adding a Gating Mechanism**

Figure 4 investigates whether the presence of a gate removes the need for sequence length scaling. Previous work that removed softmax added a gated unit and did not scale by sequence length. Concretely, in the gated attention unit, an extra projection produces output which is combined through elementwise-multiplication before the output projection. The figure compares ReLU and squared ReLU with and without gating, all scaled by $L^{-\alpha}$. The overall observation is that the best accuracy is still achieved with sequence length scaling, with or without the gate. This demonstrates that gating alone cannot substitute for proper sequence length scaling. Note that gating increases the core hours required for the experiment by roughly 9.3% for the S/8 model with ReLU, which represents a non-trivial computational cost without clear accuracy benefits when proper scaling is already applied.

**Main Scaling Results**

Figure 1 illustrates that ReLU-attention matches the scaling trends for softmax attention for ImageNet-21k training. The x-axis displays the total TPU core hours required for the experiment, and the y-axis displays ImageNet-1k accuracy percentage. The figure shows results for S/32, S/16, B/32, B/16, and L/16 models, with both softmax and ReLU/sequence-length conditions plotted. The key finding is that ReLU-attention can approach or match the performance of softmax-attention in terms of scaling behavior as a function of compute. As an advantage, ReLU-attention enables parallelization over the sequence length dimension with fewer gather operations than softmax attention, which was the original motivation for exploring point-wise alternatives.

**Design Choices and Trade-offs**

The choice to use ReLU specifically (rather than other activations in the tested set) is driven by computational efficiency rather than accuracy differences. Since Figure 2 shows no clear best non-linearity at $\alpha \approx 1$, the authors select ReLU for its speed. The choice of $\alpha = 1$ (rather than learning this parameter) is based on the empirical observation that accuracy peaks near this value across multiple model sizes and activation functions. The decision to keep qk-layernorm in the main experiments despite the ablation showing minimal effect reflects a conservative approach, as the authors note this may change at larger scales. The training configuration deliberately avoids hyperparameter tuning to demonstrate that ReLU-attention works with standard ViT settings, which strengthens the claim that this is a drop-in replacement rather than a method requiring extensive re-tuning.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper contributes more than a simple hyperparameter tweak; it identifies a specific structural omission in prior research that obscured the viability of efficient attention mechanisms. Below are the three core innovations that distinguish this work from previous attempts to remove softmax.

### 1. Redefining Stability: Magnitude Preservation over Probability Normalization
The most fundamental conceptual contribution is the decoupling of **attention stability** from **probability normalization**. Standard transformer design assumes that attention weights must sum to one ($\sum_{j=1}^L \alpha_{ij} = 1$) to function correctly. This assumption drives the use of softmax, which enforces this constraint via a global normalization sum.

*   **The Innovation:** This work demonstrates that the summation constraint is sufficient but not necessary. The critical condition for stability is preserving the **expected magnitude** of the attention weights relative to sequence length. As derived in Section 3, standard softmax yields an expected weight magnitude of $E_j[\alpha_{ij}] = L^{-1}$. The paper shows that maintaining this scale ($O(L^{-1})$) is what prevents signal explosion or vanishing, not the probability distribution itself.
*   **Why It Matters:** This shifts the theoretical understanding of attention dynamics. It suggests that transformers are robust to the *scale* of the attention matrix rather than its *normalization*. This insight validates the use of unnormalized activations (like ReLU) provided they are scaled correctly, opening the door to architectures that do not require competitive normalization layers.

### 2. Rehabilitating Point-Wise Activations via Sequence Length Scaling
Prior literature reported significant accuracy degradation when replacing softmax with point-wise activations like ReLU [25, 14, 15]. This paper identifies the specific cause of those failures: the absence of sequence length scaling.

*   **The Innovation:** By introducing the factor $L^{-\alpha}$ (specifically finding $\alpha \approx 1$ works best, as shown in **Figure 2**), the authors correct the magnitude mismatch that plagued previous attempts. Prior work either omitted scaling entirely [15] or retained a normalization sum that negated efficiency gains [21].
*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** This is not an incremental improvement on ReLU; it is a correction of the experimental setup in prior art. The ablation in **Figure 2** shows that without the scaling factor (where $\alpha \approx 0$), accuracy drops significantly across all activation types. This indicates that the "failure of ReLU" reported in earlier studies was actually a "failure of scaling."
*   **Design Space Simplification:** A secondary insight from **Figure 2** is **activation agnosticism**. Once the scaling factor $\alpha \approx 1$ is applied, the choice of non-linearity ($h \in \{\text{ReLU, GELU, sigmoid, ...}\}$) has minimal impact on peak accuracy. This suggests the scaling law is the dominant factor, allowing practitioners to choose activations based on hardware speed (e.g., ReLU) rather than accuracy tuning.

### 3. Enabling Communication-Avoiding Parallelization
While the efficiency benefits of ReLU are obvious (no exponentiation), the systemic innovation lies in the **communication pattern** during distributed training.

*   **The Innovation:** Standard softmax attention requires a **gather operation** to compute the normalization sum across the sequence length dimension. In distributed settings (e.g., across TPU cores), this requires synchronizing data between processors, creating a communication bottleneck. ReLU-attention removes this dependency entirely.
*   **Systemic Impact:** As noted in the Abstract and **Figure 1**, this allows attention to be parallelized over the sequence length dimension with fewer gather operations. This changes the bottleneck from **communication bandwidth** (waiting for the sum) to **compute density** (processing the matrix multiply).
*   **Significance:** For large-scale models where sequence length $L$ is large, removing the global reduction operation reduces latency and energy consumption. This innovation makes efficient attention viable not just for inference, but for **training scaling**, as evidenced by the matching compute-scaling curves in **Figure 1**. It provides a path to scale vision transformers without incurring the communication overhead typically associated with increasing sequence length.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence provided in the paper. We examine not just *what* results were achieved, but *how* they were measured and whether the experimental design convincingly isolates the impact of the proposed `ReLU-attention` mechanism. The analysis focuses on three pillars: the evaluation protocol (ensuring fair comparison), the main scaling results (performance vs. compute), and ablation studies (validating design choices).

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Protocol

To claim that `ReLU-attention` is a viable replacement for softmax, the evaluation must rule out confounding variables like hyperparameter tuning or unequal compute budgets. The authors adopt a rigorous protocol to ensure the comparison is apples-to-apples.

*   **Datasets and Transfer:** The primary training dataset is **ImageNet-21k** (21,000 classes), with evaluation performed on **ImageNet-1k** (1,000 classes). Crucially, for models trained on ImageNet-21k, the authors report ImageNet-1k accuracy by "taking the top class among those that are in ImageNet-1k, **without fine-tuning**" (Section 4). This is a strict evaluation metric; it tests the quality of the learned representations directly, without allowing the model to adapt to the smaller dataset via additional training.
*   **Downstream Transfer:** To assess generalization beyond ImageNet, the authors evaluate performance on **8 downstream datasets**: Caltech Birds, Caltech-101, Stanford Cars, CIFAR-100, DTD, ColHist, Pets, and UC Merced (Section 4). The metric used is **average 10-shot linear transfer**, averaged over **3 seeds**. A "10-shot linear probe" means freezing the model weights and training only a simple linear classifier on top using 10 examples per class. This isolates the quality of the feature extractor from the capacity of the classifier head.
*   **Compute Normalization:** The most critical axis for comparison is **compute cost**, measured in **TPU core hours**. Standard accuracy comparisons can be misleading if one method requires significantly more training time. By plotting accuracy against core hours (Figure 1), the authors ensure that any performance match is not simply due to `ReLU-attention` training longer or harder.
*   **Baseline Consistency:** All experiments use the **BigVision codebase** configurations without modifying hyperparameters (Section 4). Both ImageNet-21k (30 epochs) and ImageNet-1k (300 epochs) runs utilize approximately **$9 \times 10^5$ steps**. This consistency ensures that the observed differences are due to the attention mechanism, not training schedule variations.

### 5.2 Main Scaling Results

The central claim of the paper is that `ReLU-attention` matches the scaling behavior of softmax attention. This is evidenced in **Figure 1**, which plots ImageNet-1k accuracy against TPU core hours for models ranging from **S/32** (Small, patch 32) to **L/16** (Large, patch 16).

*   **Scaling Trend:** The figure displays two curves: one for `softmax` and one for `relu/seqlen`. Visually, the `relu/seqlen` curve overlaps or closely tracks the `softmax` curve across all model sizes.
    > "Figure 1 illustrates that ReLU-attention matches the scaling trends for softmax attention for ImageNet-21k training." (Section 4)
*   **Compute Efficiency:** The x-axis represents the total computational budget. The fact that the curves align implies that for a fixed compute budget, `ReLU-attention` achieves comparable accuracy to softmax. This supports the abstract's claim that it can "approach or match the performance of softmax-attention in terms of scaling behavior as a function of compute."
*   **Transfer Performance:** The right panel of Figure 1 shows "Avg. 10-shot linear transfer on 8 datasets." Here too, the `relu/seqlen` model tracks the `softmax` baseline. This indicates that the feature representations learned by `ReLU-attention` are semantically similar in quality to those learned by softmax, not just optimized for the training dataset.

**Critical Note on Exact Figures:** While the trends are clear in Figure 1, the paper text does not tabulate exact accuracy percentages (e.g., "78.5% vs 78.2%"). The evidence is presented visually via scaling curves. This is appropriate for a scaling law analysis but makes it difficult to assess marginal gains or losses at specific model sizes without digitizing the plots.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Design Validation

The paper includes three critical ablation studies to justify specific design choices: the scaling exponent $\alpha$, the use of `qk-layernorm`, and the inclusion of gating mechanisms.

#### Sensitivity to Scaling Exponent ($\alpha$)
The hypothesis is that scaling by $L^{-\alpha}$ is necessary, with $\alpha \approx 1$ being optimal. **Figure 2** tests this by varying $\alpha$ from **0.0 to 2.0** on the x-axis.
*   **Peak Performance:** Across all model sizes (S/32, S/16, S/8) and activation functions (ReLU, GELU, etc.), the accuracy peaks when $\alpha$ is **close to 1**.
*   **Activation Agnosticism:** The figure plots multiple activation functions $h \in \{\text{relu, relu2, gelu, softplus, identity, relu6, sigmoid}\}$. The curves for different activations converge near $\alpha = 1$.
    > "There is no clear best non-linearity at $\alpha \approx 1$, so we use ReLU in our main experiment for its speed." (Section 4)
*   **Implication:** This validates the theoretical motivation in Section 3 that preserving the $O(L^{-1})$ magnitude is the dominant factor, not the specific shape of the non-linearity. If $\alpha = 0$ (no scaling), accuracy drops significantly, confirming that prior work failed due to missing this factor.

#### Necessity of qk-layernorm
The main experiments use `qk-layernorm` (LayerNorm applied to queries and keys before attention) because it was previously deemed necessary for stability at scale [8]. **Figure 3** ablates this component.
*   **Result:** The figure compares `relu` and `squared relu` with and without `qk-layernorm`. The curves overlap significantly.
    > "The results indicate that qk-layernorm does not have a large effect for these models, but this may change at scale." (Section 4)
*   **Interpretation:** This suggests that the $L^{-1}$ scaling itself provides sufficient stabilization for small-to-medium models (S/32 to S/8). However, the authors谨慎 (cautiously) retain it in main experiments because instability might reappear at larger scales (e.g., beyond L/16), which were not fully ablated here.

#### Impact of Gating
Some prior softmax-free methods used gated attention units [15]. **Figure 4** tests if gating removes the need for sequence length scaling.
*   **Scaling Still Required:** Even with gating, the accuracy peaks at $\alpha \approx 1$. Gating does not substitute for the $L^{-1}$ factor.
*   **Compute Cost:** Gating adds computational overhead without improving peak accuracy when scaling is applied.
    > "Note that gating increases the core hours required for the experiment by roughly 9.3% for the S/8 model with ReLU." (Section 4)
*   **Design Decision:** This justifies the choice to exclude gating in the main `ReLU-attention` formulation, as it incurs a **~9.3% compute penalty** without solving the scaling problem.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Evidence

The experiments provide strong empirical support for the paper's core claim, but there are limitations to consider when interpreting the results.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   **Compute-Normalized Comparison:** By plotting against TPU core hours rather than just epochs, the paper accounts for the potential speed differences between ReLU and softmax operations. This makes the "matching scaling behavior" claim robust.
    *   **Broad Activation Search:** Figure 2 demonstrates that the finding is not unique to ReLU but applies to a family of activations, strengthening the theoretical argument that *scaling* is the key variable.
    *   **Transfer Validation:** The inclusion of 10-shot linear transfer on 8 diverse datasets ensures the model is not overfitting to ImageNet statistics.

*   **Limitations and Open Questions:**
    *   **Scale Ceiling:** The largest model tested is **L/16** (Large). While `qk-layernorm` was designed for scaling to 22 billion parameters [8], this paper does not demonstrate `ReLU-attention` stability at that extreme scale. The authors explicitly note: "this may change at scale" regarding the removal of `qk-layernorm` (Section 4).
    *   **Theoretical Gap:** The Conclusion admits uncertainty on the mechanism: "we are unsure why the factor $L^{-1}$ improves performance or if this term could be learned." The justification is primarily empirical, supported by a brief initialization argument in Section 3.
    *   **Missing Exact Metrics:** As noted in Section 5.2, exact accuracy numbers are not provided in the text, relying on visual inspection of Figure 1. While the trends are clear, precise marginal differences (e.g., does ReLU slightly underperform at the largest scale?) are harder to quantify without tabulated data.
    *   **Sequence Length Variation:** The experiments use standard ViT sequence lengths (determined by image resolution and patch size). The paper does not explicitly test how `ReLU-attention` behaves when $L$ is varied independently (e.g., using the same model on different image resolutions), which would further validate the $L^{-1}$ scaling law.

In summary, the experimental analysis convincingly demonstrates that `ReLU-attention` with sequence length scaling is a viable, compute-efficient alternative to softmax for Vision Transformers up to the Large scale. The ablation studies successfully isolate the scaling factor $\alpha \approx 1$ as the critical component missing from prior work. However, the stability at extreme scales and the theoretical origin of the $L^{-1}$ term remain open areas for future research.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the paper demonstrates that `ReLU-attention` can match softmax scaling behavior, it is crucial to understand the boundaries of this claim. The proposed method is not a universal silver bullet; it relies on specific assumptions about model initialization, sequence length stability, and compute budgets. Below, we dissect the constraints, open questions, and trade-offs inherent in this approach.

### 6.1 Theoretical Opacity
The most significant limitation is the lack of a rigorous theoretical foundation for *why* the sequence length scaling factor works. While Section 3 provides a brief analytical motivation based on initialization dynamics, the authors explicitly acknowledge in the Conclusion that:

> "we are unsure why the factor $L^{-1}$ improves performance or if this term could be learned."

This empirical grounding presents two risks for practitioners:
*   **Generalization Uncertainty:** Because the justification is primarily empirical, it is unclear if the $L^{-1}$ factor holds under different initialization schemes or architecture modifications. If the mechanism is not fully understood, adapting it to non-ViT architectures (e.g., RNNs or state-space models) becomes guesswork.
*   **Fixed vs. Learned Scaling:** The paper treats the scaling exponent $\alpha$ as a fixed hyperparameter (typically $\alpha \approx 1$). If this term could be learned adaptively during training, it might offer better stability across varying sequence lengths. However, the paper does not explore learning this parameter, leaving potential performance gains on the table.

### 6.2 Scalability Boundaries
The experiments validate the method on Vision Transformers ranging from **S/32** to **L/16** (Figure 1). While this covers small to large models, it does not reach the extreme scales where transformer instability often becomes critical.

*   **The qk-layernorm Caveat:** The main experiments use `qk-layernorm` [8], which was originally designed to prevent instability when scaling to 22 billion parameters. However, the ablation in **Figure 3** shows that for the tested scales (up to S/8), removing `qk-layernorm` has little effect. The authors caution:
    > "The results indicate that qk-layernorm does not have a large effect for these models, but this may change at scale." (Section 4)

    This implies that `ReLU-attention` alone might not be sufficient for stability at extreme scales (e.g., billions of parameters). Practitioners scaling beyond **L/16** may still need to retain `qk-layernorm`, which adds computational overhead, potentially negating some efficiency gains from removing softmax.
*   **Missing Extreme Scale Data:** Unlike the prior work [8] which scaled to 22 billion parameters, this paper stops at the **L/16** configuration. Without evidence at the 22B scale, we cannot confirm if the `ReLU-attention` scaling laws hold when model size and sequence length grow simultaneously to their limits.

### 6.3 Dependency on Sequence Length ($L$)
The core mechanism relies on dividing by $L^\alpha$. This introduces a dependency on knowing the sequence length explicitly during computation.

*   **Variable Sequence Lengths:** In vision tasks, sequence length is determined by image resolution and patch size. If a model is trained on one resolution (fixed $L$) and evaluated on another (variable $L$), the scaling factor $L^{-1}$ changes. The paper uses fixed training configurations (30 epochs on ImageNet-21k, 300 on ImageNet-1k) with standard ViT resolutions (Section 4). It does not explicitly test how `ReLU-attention` behaves under **dynamic sequence length** conditions (e.g., fine-tuning on higher resolution images where $L$ increases).
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** The analytical motivation in Section 3 relies on the assumption that query and key elements are $O(1)$ at initialization. If a different initialization scheme is used where $\langle q_i, k_j \rangle$ scales differently with $d$, the optimal $\alpha$ might shift away from 1. The method assumes standard ViT initialization dynamics.

### 6.4 Compute vs. Accuracy Trade-offs
While `ReLU-attention` removes the softmax exponentiation and gather operations, the net compute benefit depends on the specific implementation and auxiliary components.

*   **Gating Overhead:** Some prior softmax-free methods used gated attention units [15]. This paper tests gating in **Figure 4** and finds it does not remove the need for sequence length scaling. Moreover, it adds cost:
    > "Note that gating increases the core hours required for the experiment by roughly 9.3% for the S/8 model with ReLU." (Section 4)

    This highlights a trade-off: adding complexity (gating) to stabilize attention incurs a **~9.3% compute penalty** without solving the core scaling issue. Thus, the efficiency gain of `ReLU-attention` is contingent on *not* using such auxiliary stabilization mechanisms.
*   **Wall-Clock Speed vs. Core Hours:** Figure 1 plots accuracy against **TPU core hours**, which normalizes for efficiency. However, the paper does not provide explicit wall-clock speedup percentages (e.g., "20% faster training"). The benefit is framed as "fewer gather operations" (Abstract), which is highly dependent on the hardware interconnect (e.g., TPU pod vs. single GPU). On hardware where communication is not the bottleneck, the removal of softmax might yield diminishing returns.

### 6.5 Activation Function Optimality
The choice of ReLU is driven by speed rather than proven optimality. **Figure 2** tests a variety of non-linearities ($h \in \{\text{relu, relu2, gelu, softplus, identity, relu6, sigmoid}\}$) and finds:

> "There is no clear best non-linearity at $\alpha \approx 1$, so we use ReLU in our main experiment for its speed." (Section 4)

This presents a limitation in performance ceiling:
*   **Potential Accuracy Loss:** Since ReLU is chosen for computational efficiency, it is possible that a slower activation (like GELU or softplus) might yield slightly better accuracy at the same scale, but this is not explored in depth.
*   **Unexplored Functions:** The Conclusion notes: "it is likely that there is a better activation function that we do not explore." This leaves open the possibility that the current `ReLU-attention` formulation is suboptimal compared to undiscovered activation functions tailored for this scaling regime.

### 6.6 Domain Specificity
Finally, it is important to note the scope of validation. The title specifies **Vision Transformers**, and all experiments are conducted on image datasets (ImageNet-21k, ImageNet-1k, and 8 downstream vision tasks).

*   **NLP Applicability:** While the transformer architecture is ubiquitous in NLP, the paper does not test `ReLU-attention` on language tasks. NLP sequences often have much higher variance in length $L$ compared to vision patches. The $L^{-1}$ scaling might behave differently when $L$ varies significantly within a batch (e.g., sentences of different lengths), whereas vision transformers often pad or fix sequence length more rigidly.
*   **Long-Sequence Regimes:** The paper mentions linear attention [16, 22, 18] is useful for "long sequence lengths," but notes removing activation entirely reduced accuracy in their experiments. The `ReLU-attention` method is validated on standard ViT sequence lengths, not the extreme lengths (e.g., $L > 10,000$) where linear attention typically dominates. It remains unclear if $L^{-1}$ scaling is sufficient to stabilize attention in those extreme regimes.

### Summary of Trade-offs
| Feature | Softmax Attention | ReLU-Attention (This Work) | Trade-off Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Normalization** | Explicit ($\sum \alpha = 1$) | Implicit Scale ($E[\alpha] \approx L^{-1}$) | ReLU avoids gather ops but relies on fixed $L$. |
| **Theoretical Basis** | Probability Theory | Empirical / Initialization Heuristic | ReLU mechanism is less understood (Conclusion). |
| **Stability at Scale** | Proven up to 22B [8] | Validated up to L/16 (Figure 1) | ReLU stability at extreme scale is unproven. |
| **Compute Cost** | High (Exponent + Sum) | Low (Point-wise) | ReLU saves compute, but gating adds ~9.3% (Section 4). |
| **Activation Choice** | Fixed (Softmax) | Flexible (ReLU, GELU, etc.) | ReLU chosen for speed, not peak accuracy (Figure 2). |

In conclusion, `ReLU-attention` offers a compelling path to efficient training by removing the softmax bottleneck, but it trades theoretical certainty for empirical performance. It is best suited for vision tasks with stable sequence lengths where communication overhead (gather operations) is a known bottleneck. For extreme-scale models or highly variable sequence lengths, the necessity of auxiliary stabilizers like `qk-layernorm` remains an open question.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than propose a specific activation function; it challenges a foundational assumption in transformer design: that attention weights must form a probability distribution. By demonstrating that preserving the **scale** of attention weights ($O(L^{-1})$) is sufficient for stability, this work opens new architectural design spaces that prioritize hardware efficiency without sacrificing accuracy. Below, we explore how this shifts the research landscape, what specific questions remain open, and how practitioners can integrate these findings.

### 7.1 Shifting the Paradigm of Attention Efficiency

Historically, efficient attention research has bifurcated into two camps: **approximating softmax** (e.g., low-rank approximations) or **removing normalization entirely** (linear attention) [16, 22, 18]. This paper identifies a middle ground that was previously overlooked.

*   **From Normalization to Scaling:** The core implication is that the global sum operation in softmax ($\sum_{j=1}^L \alpha_{ij} = 1$) is not strictly necessary for model convergence. As shown in **Figure 2**, accuracy peaks when the scaling exponent $\alpha \approx 1$, regardless of the specific non-linearity $h$. This suggests that the critical invariant for transformer stability is the **expected magnitude** of the attention weights relative to sequence length, not their normalization to a probability distribution.
*   **Hardware-Aware Architecture Design:** By removing the dependency on a global sum, `ReLU-attention` eliminates the **gather operation** required across processor cores during distributed training (Abstract, Section 1). This shifts the bottleneck from **communication bandwidth** (waiting for synchronization) to **compute density** (matrix multiplications). For large-scale training clusters where communication overhead is significant, this architectural change directly translates to improved utilization of TPU core hours, as evidenced by the matching scaling curves in **Figure 1**.

### 7.2 Avenues for Follow-up Research

The Conclusion explicitly states that "this report leaves many open questions." These gaps define the immediate roadmap for future research building on this work.

*   **Theoretical Justification for $L^{-1}$:** The paper admits, "we are unsure why the factor $L^{-1}$ improves performance" (Conclusion). While Section 3 provides an initialization argument (preserving $O(L^{-1})$ magnitude), a rigorous theoretical proof is missing. Follow-up work could investigate:
    *   Does this scaling factor prevent gradient explosion/vanishing in deeper networks?
    *   Is there a connection to signal propagation theory in deep neural networks?
*   **Learnable Scaling Factors:** Currently, $\alpha$ is a fixed hyperparameter (typically $\alpha = 1$). The authors ask, "if this term could be learned" (Conclusion). Future architectures could parameterize the scaling factor as a learnable scalar or even a token-dependent vector, allowing the model to adapt the attention scale dynamically during training.
*   **Optimized Activation Functions:** The paper notes, "it is likely that there is a better activation function that we do not explore" (Conclusion). Since **Figure 2** shows activation agnosticism at $\alpha \approx 1$, researchers can now search for activations that maximize hardware throughput (e.g., integer-friendly operations) rather than accuracy, knowing the scaling factor will compensate for architectural differences.
*   **Scaling Beyond L/16:** The experiments validate stability up to the **L/16** model size (Figure 1). However, the ablation on `qk-layernorm` suggests uncertainty at larger scales: "this may change at scale" (Section 4). Future work should test `ReLU-attention` at the 22 billion parameter scale [8] to determine if auxiliary stabilizers like `qk-layernorm` become mandatory again, which would impact the efficiency gains.
*   **Cross-Domain Validation:** All experiments are confined to Vision Transformers (ImageNet-21k/1k). Natural Language Processing (NLP) tasks often involve highly variable sequence lengths within a batch. Research is needed to verify if $L^{-1}$ scaling remains robust when $L$ fluctuates dynamically, or if domain-specific adjustments are required.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

For practitioners deciding whether to adopt `ReLU-attention`, the decision depends on the specific constraints of the training environment and task.

*   **Distributed Training Environments:** This method is most valuable in large-scale distributed training setups (e.g., TPU pods or GPU clusters) where the **gather operation** for softmax normalization creates a communication bottleneck. If your training pipeline is communication-bound rather than compute-bound, replacing softmax with `ReLU-attention` reduces synchronization overhead.
*   **Standard Vision Tasks:** For standard image classification or transfer learning tasks (like the 8 downstream datasets tested: Caltech Birds, CIFAR-100, etc.), this method is a viable drop-in replacement. The 10-shot linear transfer results in **Figure 1** confirm that feature quality remains comparable to softmax baselines.
*   **When to Avoid:**
    *   **Extreme Scale:** If scaling beyond **L/16** (e.g., to billions of parameters), retain `qk-layernorm` until further stability evidence is available (Section 4).
    *   **Gating Mechanisms:** Do not add gated attention units [15] unless necessary. The paper shows gating increases core hours by **~9.3%** for the S/8 model without improving accuracy when scaling is applied (Section 4).
    *   **Variable Sequence Lengths:** In tasks where sequence length $L$ varies significantly per sample (common in NLP), ensure the scaling factor $L^{-\alpha}$ is computed per-sample correctly, as the paper assumes fixed configurations during training (Section 4).

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

Integrating `ReLU-attention` into existing pipelines is straightforward, but specific implementation details are critical for success.

*   **Codebase Reference:** The experiments utilize the **BigVision codebase** [4]. Practitioners should reference the base configurations for ImageNet-1k and ImageNet-21k provided in the footnotes of Section 4 to ensure hyperparameter consistency.
*   **Implementation Step:** To implement this change, locate the attention weight calculation in your transformer block. Replace the softmax operation with the following logic:
    1.  Compute raw attention scores: $S = \frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d}}$.
    2.  Apply point-wise activation: $A = \text{ReLU}(S)$.
    3.  **Crucial Step:** Divide by sequence length: $A_{scaled} = A \cdot L^{-1}$.
    *   *Note:* Ensure $L$ represents the actual sequence length used in the attention computation.
*   **Hyperparameters:** The paper emphasizes that this works "without modifying hyperparameters" (Section 4). You do not need to re-tune learning rates or weight decay when switching from softmax to `ReLU-attention` with $L^{-1}$ scaling.
*   **Expected Outcome:** Based on **Figure 1**, you should expect matching scaling behavior in terms of compute (TPU core hours). If you observe accuracy degradation, verify that the scaling factor $L^{-1}$ is applied correctly; prior work failed primarily because this scaling was absent (Section 2).
*   **Verification:** To validate your implementation, reproduce the ablation in **Figure 2**. Train a small model (e.g., S/32) and sweep the scaling exponent $\alpha$ from 0.0 to 2.0. Your accuracy should peak near $\alpha = 1$. If the peak is elsewhere, check your initialization or scaling implementation.

By adopting this approach, practitioners can reduce the communication overhead of training vision transformers while maintaining state-of-the-art scaling performance. However, as the authors caution, the theoretical underpinnings remain an open field, inviting further exploration into why simple scaling laws so effectively replace complex normalization.
