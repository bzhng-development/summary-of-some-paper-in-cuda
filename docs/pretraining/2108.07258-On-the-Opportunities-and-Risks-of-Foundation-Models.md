## 1. Executive Summary

This report introduces the term "foundation models" in Section 1 to describe AI systems like BERT, GPT-3, and CLIP that are trained on broad data using self-supervision at scale and adapted to diverse downstream tasks, addressing the lack of terminology to capture this paradigm shift beyond standard deep learning. It highlights critical phenomena like emergence, where scale induces new capabilities (e.g., GPT-3's 175 billion parameters enabling in-context learning), and homogenization, which creates single points of failure across AI systems. The work matters because it provides a thorough interdisciplinary account of the opportunities and risks spanning capabilities, applications, technology, and society, urging deep collaboration to manage the societal impact of these increasingly deployed systems.

## 2. Context and Motivation

This report emerges from a critical inflection point in artificial intelligence (AI) research and deployment. While the technical capabilities of large-scale models have advanced rapidly, the community lacks a unified framework to understand their broader implications. This section details the specific conceptual gaps this paper addresses, the urgency driven by real-world deployment, the limitations of prior AI paradigms that necessitated this shift, and how this report positions itself as an interdisciplinary synthesis.

### 2.1 The Conceptual and Understanding Gap

Before the introduction of the term `foundation model`, the community relied on existing terminology such as `pretrained model` or `self-supervised model`. However, the authors argue these terms are insufficient. They capture the technical dimension but fail to convey the sociological impact or the "unfinished character" of these systems (Section 1.1.1). A foundation model is distinct because it is an intermediary asset—it is incomplete on its own and serves as a common basis from which many task-specific models are built via adaptation.

This terminological void reflects a deeper understanding gap. Despite the impending widespread deployment of these models, the report notes:

> We currently lack a clear understanding of how they work, when they fail, and what they are even capable of due to their emergent properties. (Section 1)

Existing literature at the time of writing covered only "bits and pieces" of the phenomenon (Section 1.4). Research was fragmented across disciplines: technical researchers focused on scaling laws, while social scientists examined bias in isolation. There was no single account that connected the technical principles (e.g., model architectures, training procedures) to societal impacts (e.g., inequity, legality). This report aims to fill that void by providing a "fuller picture" that highlights interdisciplinary connections (Section 1.4).

### 2.2 The Urgency of Deployment and Risk

The motivation for this comprehensive analysis is driven by the rapid transition from research prototypes to real-world infrastructure. Foundation models are no longer confined to academic labs; they are integrated into systems affecting billions of users. For example, the report highlights that Google Search, which boasts $4$ billion users, depends on foundation models like `BERT` as one of its signals (Section 1.2).

This widespread adoption creates unique risks centered on **homogenization**. Because almost all state-of-the-art models are adapted from one of a few foundation models (e.g., `BERT`, `RoBERTa`, `GPT-3`), any flaws in the foundation model are blindly inherited by all adapted models downstream (Section 1.1). This creates single points of failure across the AI ecosystem. If a foundation model contains a specific bias or security vulnerability, that defect propagates to myriad applications in healthcare, law, and education (Section 1.1).

Furthermore, the report emphasizes that reasoning about social impact is harder for foundation models than for specific deployed systems. Because foundation models are task-agnostic, their downstream use cases are often unforeseen at the time of training (Section 1.2). This uncertainty demands a proactive assessment of risks before deployment norms are fully established.

### 2.3 Limitations of Prior AI Paradigms

To understand why foundation models represent a distinct paradigm requiring new analysis, it is necessary to examine the evolution of AI approaches over the last 30 years (Section 1.1).

*   **Machine Learning (1990s):** The shift began when learning algorithms induced tasks from data rather than having rules specified by humans. This homogenized learning algorithms (e.g., logistic regression) but still required domain experts to perform "feature engineering" to convert raw data into suitable inputs (Section 1.1).
*   **Deep Learning (2010s):** The advent of deep neural networks allowed high-level features to emerge through training on raw inputs (e.g., pixels), reducing the need for bespoke feature engineering. However, this era relied heavily on **supervised learning** with annotated datasets (e.g., `ImageNet`). The report notes that the "non-trivial cost of annotation imposes a practical limit on the benefits of pretraining" (Section 1.1).
*   **Foundation Models (2018+):** The current paradigm is enabled by **self-supervised learning**, where the pretraining task is derived automatically from unannotated data (e.g., predicting a missing word in a sentence). This removes the annotation bottleneck, allowing models to scale to unprecedented sizes (e.g., `GPT-3` with $175$ billion parameters) (Section 1).

Prior approaches fall short because they cannot account for the **emergence** properties seen at this scale. For instance, `GPT-3` exhibits `in-context learning`—adapting to tasks via prompts without parameter updates—a capability that was neither specifically trained for nor anticipated in smaller models (Section 1.1). Existing theories of supervised learning are inadequate to explain the discrepancy between the pretraining phase and the adaptation phase inherent to foundation models (Section 4.10).

### 2.4 Interdisciplinary Positioning of this Report

This report positions itself not merely as a technical survey, but as a sociotechnical analysis requiring deep interdisciplinary collaboration (Section 1). The authors argue that the challenges posed by foundation models combine technical, ethical, legal, social, and political dimensions, which cannot be solved by technologists alone (Section 1.3).

The structure of the report reflects this positioning. It was written by over $100$ people from different backgrounds, organized into four distinct parts (Section 1.4, Figure 4):
1.  **Capabilities:** Examining linguistic, visual, and robotic abilities.
2.  **Applications:** Focusing on foundational societal pillars like healthcare, law, and education.
3.  **Technology:** Analyzing modeling, training, data, and safety.
4.  **Society:** Addressing inequity, misuse, environment, and ethics.

Crucially, the report adopts an **ecosystem view** rather than focusing solely on the model weights. Figure 3 illustrates this pipeline, extending from data creation (where people are the ultimate source) through curation, training, adaptation, and finally deployment (where people are the recipients of benefits and harms) (Section 1.2). This framing allows the authors to argue that different questions (e.g., whether a model is ethical) must be answered with respect to different stages of the pipeline, rather than treating the model as a black box (Section 1.2).

By synthesizing these perspectives, the report aims to establish professional norms for responsible development and deployment, bridging the gap between academic research and industry practice (Section 1.3). It explicitly acknowledges that while industry makes concrete deployment decisions, academia must provide distinctive guidance grounded in both technical and ethical considerations (Section 1.3).

## 3. Technical Approach

This report is a comprehensive sociotechnical analysis that examines foundation models as a new AI paradigm, where the core idea is that models trained on broad data using self-supervision at scale can be adapted to diverse downstream tasks, creating both unprecedented capabilities and systemic risks through emergence and homogenization.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

A foundation model is a large neural network trained on vast amounts of unannotated data (like text from the internet or images from the web) that learns general patterns it can later apply to specific tasks. It solves the problem of building AI systems without requiring expensive labeled datasets for every new application by learning general representations during pretraining that can be efficiently adapted through fine-tuning or prompting.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The foundation model ecosystem operates as a five-stage pipeline where information flows sequentially from raw data to deployed applications. First, data creation occurs when people generate content (emails, articles, photos) or when measurements are taken of people and environments. Second, data curation transforms this raw content into curated datasets through selection and filtering processes. Third, training consumes these curated datasets to produce the foundation model itself using self-supervised learning objectives. Fourth, adaptation converts the foundation model into task-specific models through methods like fine-tuning or prompting. Fifth, deployment delivers these adapted models to end users who experience the benefits and harms of the system.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the core definition and distinguishing characteristics of foundation models to establish what makes them different from prior AI systems.
- We then explain the training methodology, focusing on self-supervised learning and why it enables unprecedented scale compared to supervised approaches.
- We describe the Transformer architecture that underpins most foundation models and explain why it replaced earlier architectures like recurrent neural networks.
- We detail the adaptation mechanisms that convert foundation models into task-specific systems, including fine-tuning, prompting, and lightweight adaptation methods.
- We examine the evaluation framework that distinguishes between intrinsic evaluation of the foundation model itself and extrinsic evaluation of adapted derivatives.
- We conclude with the ecosystem perspective that shows how foundation models fit into broader sociotechnical systems beyond just model weights.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Definition and Distinguishing Characteristics**

A foundation model is any model that is trained on broad data, generally using self-supervision at scale, that can be adapted to a wide range of downstream tasks through methods like fine-tuning. The term "foundation model" was specifically chosen to capture the unfinished yet important status of these systems, distinguishing them from prior terminology like "pretrained model" or "self-supervised model" that failed to convey their sociological impact. Foundation models are intermediary assets that are incomplete on their own and serve as a common basis from which many task-specific models are built via adaptation. This distinguishes them from task-specific models that are trained directly on labeled data for a single purpose. Current examples include BERT, GPT-3 with 175 billion parameters, and CLIP, all of which demonstrate the ability to adapt to diverse tasks despite not being explicitly trained for many of those tasks.

**The Training Methodology: Self-Supervised Learning at Scale**

Self-supervised learning enables foundation models to train on unannotated data by deriving the pretraining task automatically from the data itself, removing the annotation bottleneck that limited prior approaches. For example, the masked language modeling task used to train BERT asks the model to predict a missing word in a sentence given its surrounding context, such as predicting the blank in "I like [blank] sprouts". This approach is more scalable than supervised learning because it depends only on unlabeled data rather than expensive human annotations. The importance of data availability cannot be underestimated, as the non-trivial cost of annotation imposed a practical limit on the benefits of pretraining in the supervised learning era. Self-supervised tasks are designed to force the model to predict parts of the inputs, making them richer and potentially more useful than models trained on a more limited label space. The training process requires three critical ingredients: improvements in computer hardware where GPU throughput and memory have increased 10× over the last four years, the development of the Transformer model architecture that leverages hardware parallelism, and the availability of much more training data. Scale is what makes foundation models powerful, as transfer learning alone existed for decades but only became transformative when combined with massive scale.

**The Transformer Architecture: Enabling Expressivity and Scalability**

The Transformer network architecture underpins most foundation models to date and demonstrates the importance of capturing long-range dependencies and pairwise or higher-order interactions between elements. Transformers build on the self-attention mechanism that enables shorter computation paths and provides direct means to compare elements far across the input data, such as linking a pronoun to its antecedent in a sentence. From another perspective, the multiplicative interaction embodied in attention offers a more flexible alternative to the rigid fixed-weight computation of multilayer perceptrons and convolutional neural networks, dynamically adapting the computation to the input at hand. This proves especially useful for language modeling where contextual understanding matters, such as updating the representation of the word "ate" depending on whether the prepositional phrase attachment is "spoon" or "strawberries". The general-purpose nature of attention and transformers contributes to their broad applicability across research problems and applications, as they are not strongly tied to a particular task or domain like convolutional or recurrent architectures. This captures a trade-off between task-specialization and expressivity, where models with weaker inductive biases learn more slowly but can scale to higher volumes of data and adapt to diverse domains. Leading approaches keep struggling with modeling extremely long-range dependencies, such as those occurring in books or DNA sequences, which may be attributed to the quadratic computation of contemporary transformer-based approaches. This challenge reflects the trade-off between efficiency and expressivity, where explicit modeling of long-distance interactions improves expressivity but comes at the expense of scalability due to increased connectivity.

**Model Properties: Five Key Attributes**

Foundation models should possess five essential properties to effectively distill large amounts of information and address downstream tasks. Expressivity concerns the theoretical and practical capacity of a network to model the data distribution it is trained over and represent it in a flexible manner, affected by depth, width, connectivity, and structural patterns. Scalability requires models to be easy-to-train by being resilient to noise and robust against instabilities like vanishing or exploding gradients, while also being easy-to-adapt by overcoming catastrophic forgetting and supporting few-shot learning. Multimodality serves as a key component of intelligence and is a crucial factor for developing thorough and broad comprehension of the world, as language learning is more effective when occurring in a grounded environment. Memory capacity addresses the challenge of representing large bodies of learned information through effective mechanisms for access, storage, retrieval, and manipulation of particular items or memories. Compositionality is the principle according to which the meaning of the whole is derived from the meaning of its constituent parts and the rules applied to combine them, underlying capabilities to plan, reason, and learn from few examples.

**Adaptation Mechanisms: Converting Foundation Models to Task-Specific Systems**

Adaptation is necessary because foundation models provide a powerful general-purpose engine for processing multi-modal information but must be converted before use for some applications. An adaptation procedure produces an adapted model by conditioning a foundation model on additional information, either by priming through new data or prompts in the input or by updating some or all of the foundation model's parameters. For example, in text summarization, appending a prompt such as "TL;DR" to the input article can improve foundation model performance by acting as a task specification. Alternatively, fine-tuning the parameters of a foundation model with an organization's internal, domain-specific data could improve accuracy by adding information relevant to the organization's use case. Three factors determine which adaptation procedure is appropriate: the compute budget (specifically storage and memory), the amount of task-specific data available, and the extent of access to foundation model gradients. For foundation models with billions or trillions of parameters, fine-tuning all model parameters may demand prohibitively large memory, leading to lightweight adaptation methods that freeze most pretrained parameters and only learn a relatively small number of task-specific parameters. Lightweight adaptation techniques sometimes achieve comparable performance to full fine-tuning despite updating 1000× fewer parameters, though we remain uncertain how these techniques scale as model size increases. When adaptation data is plentiful, traditional fine-tuning approaches or their lightweight counterparts may be applied, but in low-resource settings, combining prompting and fine-tuning has been shown to be promising. A well-tuned prompt can be worth around 100 training examples, and fine-tuning a carefully prompted foundation model is significantly more data-efficient than fine-tuning an unconditioned foundation model.

**Evaluation Framework: Intrinsic and Extrinsic Assessment**

Evaluation gives context to foundation models by serving as a means for tracking progress, understanding model behaviors, and documenting capabilities and biases for diverse stakeholders. The nature of foundation models introduces new challenges not generally encountered in other AI settings because foundation models must be adapted to perform tasks, making relative comparison complicated. The report distinguishes two classes of evaluation that arise from the abstraction of foundation models: intrinsic evaluation of the foundation model itself, which is inherently divorced from a specific task, and extrinsic evaluation of task-specific models, which is necessarily dependent on both the foundation model and the adaptation mechanism. One approach to intrinsic evaluation is to evaluate foundation models in terms of the task associated with the training objective, such as evaluating a language model based on perplexity on language modeling benchmarks. However, this approach has two fundamental limitations: relying on the training objective lacks generality because foundation models trained using different incompatible objectives cannot be readily compared, and evaluation relies upon a proxy relationship that may break down when assessing diverse capabilities. An alternative route is to adapt foundation models to a wide range of tasks and measure the performance of the resulting task-specific models, where performance in aggregate reflects on the nature and quality of the shared basis. Direct evaluation of intrinsic properties, divorced from specific tasks, is valuable for measuring capabilities or biases directly, such as measuring linguistic capabilities to identify syntactically valid and invalid sentences. Extrinsic evaluation should acknowledge adaptation resources, including all data used to inform adaptation and the level of access required to adapt the foundation model, to ensure fair comparisons between different approaches.

**The Ecosystem Perspective: Beyond Model Weights**

The foundation model ecosystem extends beyond just the model weights to encompass a sequence of stages from data creation to actual deployment, where people occupy both ends of the pipeline. Data creation is fundamentally a human-centric process where all data is created by people and most data is at least implicitly about people, whether as emails and articles or as measurements of people and environments. Data is then curated into datasets, where ensuring data relevance and quality while respecting legal and ethical constraints is critical but challenging. Training foundation models on these curated datasets is the celebrated centerpiece in AI research, though it is only one of many stages in the ecosystem. Adaptation creates a new model based on the foundation model that performs some task, where for deployment this requires potentially many different modules, custom rules, or classifiers beyond just the model itself. Deployment is where the direct social impact of an AI system occurs when it is deployed to people, and it is standard practice in large-scale deployments to conduct gradual releases to partially mitigate potential harms. This ecosystem view allows us to see that different questions about foundation models should be answered with respect to different stages, rather than treating the model as a black box. Many impacts come from decisions made in other stages in the pipeline beyond training, and thoughtful monitoring and intervention is needed at every stage. While large organizations might own the entire pipeline, each stage could be performed by a different organization, such as a company that specializes in creating custom foundation models for various domains.

**Mathematical Formulation of Training and Adaptation**

The pretraining phase minimizes the empirical pretraining loss on a large number of independent samples from the pretraining distribution. Let $p_{\text{pre}}$ denote the population pretraining distribution and $\hat{p}_{\text{pre}}$ denote the empirical distribution over samples. The empirical pretraining loss is defined as:

$$\hat{L}_{\text{pre}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \hat{p}_{\text{pre}}}[\ell_{\text{pre}}(x; \theta)]$$

where $\theta \in \Theta$ represents the model parameters and $\ell_{\text{pre}}$ is the pretraining loss function. The foundation model parameters are obtained by minimizing this loss:

$$\hat{\theta}_{\text{FM}} = \arg\min_{\theta \in \Theta} \hat{L}_{\text{pre}}(\theta)$$

The corresponding population pretraining loss is:

$$L_{\text{pre}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{pre}}}[\ell_{\text{pre}}(x; \theta)]$$

The adaptation phase is framed as a constrained optimization problem that depends on $\hat{\theta}_{\text{FM}}$. Given a downstream task distribution $p_{\text{task}}$ and empirical samples $\hat{p}_{\text{task}}$, adaptation minimizes an adaptation loss with respect to adapted parameters $\gamma \in \Gamma$:

$$\gamma_{\text{task}}(\hat{\theta}_{\text{FM}}) = \arg\min_{\gamma \in \Gamma, C(\gamma; \hat{\theta}_{\text{FM}}) \leq c_0} \hat{L}_{\text{adapt}}(\gamma, \hat{\theta}_{\text{FM}})$$

where $\hat{L}_{\text{adapt}}(\gamma, \hat{\theta}_{\text{FM}}) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \hat{p}_{\text{task}}}[\ell_{\text{adapt}}(x; \gamma, \hat{\theta}_{\text{FM}})]$ is the empirical adaptation loss and $C(\gamma, \hat{\theta}_{\text{FM}}) \leq c_0$ is an optional constraint controlling the complexity of adapted parameters. The final downstream task loss can be bounded by the minimal adaptation loss plus generalization error:

$$L_{\text{adapt}}(\gamma_{\text{task}}, \hat{\theta}_{\text{FM}}) \leq L^*_{\text{adapt}}(\hat{\theta}_{\text{FM}}) + \text{generalization error}$$

where $L^*_{\text{adapt}}(\hat{\theta}_{\text{FM}})$ is the minimal adaptation loss on the population distribution. This modularization separates the pretraining-adaptation interface from standard generalization theory, allowing independent analysis of each phase.

**Design Choices and Trade-offs**

The choice of self-supervised learning over supervised learning was driven by the need to remove the annotation bottleneck that limited prior approaches to pretraining benefits. The Transformer architecture was chosen over recurrent neural networks because of its higher degree of parallelism, which better leverages GPU hardware for training much more expressive models. The decision to scale model size to hundreds of billions of parameters was driven by the discovery that scale results in emergent capabilities like in-context learning that were neither specifically trained for nor anticipated. Lightweight adaptation methods were developed to address the prohibitive memory requirements of fine-tuning all parameters for models with billions or trillions of parameters. The distinction between intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation was introduced because standard evaluation paradigms are not designed for the setting where models must be adapted to perform tasks. The ecosystem perspective was adopted because reasoning about social impact is easier for specific deployed systems than for task-agnostic foundation models with unforeseen downstream uses. Each design choice reflects a trade-off between capability and risk, where emergence provides powerful leverage but demands caution because defects are inherited by all adapted models downstream.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper introduces several conceptual innovations that distinguish it from prior technical surveys. Rather than merely documenting existing capabilities, the report establishes new frameworks for understanding foundation models as sociotechnical systems. Below we identify the most novel contributions and explain their significance.

### 4.1 The "Foundation Model" Terminology as Conceptual Innovation

**What makes it different:** Prior terminology like `pretrained model` or `self-supervised model` captured only the technical dimension of these systems. The authors deliberately introduce `foundation model` to fill a terminological void that reflects the **sociological impact** and **unfinished character** of these systems (Section 1.1.1). This is not merely semantic—the term conveys that these models are intermediary assets that serve as a common basis from which many task-specific models are built, rather than complete solutions themselves.

**Why it's significant:** This conceptual framing shifts how the community reasons about responsibility and risk. By designating these models as "foundations," the report emphasizes that poorly-constructed foundations are a recipe for disaster, while well-executed ones provide reliable bedrock for future applications (Section 1.1.1). This terminology has since been adopted widely in both academic and industry discourse, demonstrating its utility in capturing a paradigm shift that existing terms could not. The innovation lies in recognizing that **naming shapes understanding**—the term itself becomes a tool for interdisciplinary communication between technologists, policymakers, and social scientists who previously lacked shared vocabulary.

### 4.2 The Emergence and Homogenization Framework

**What makes it different:** Prior AI literature discussed scaling effects and transfer learning separately. This report synthesizes them into two interacting phenomena: **emergence** (capabilities implicitly induced rather than explicitly constructed, like GPT-3's in-context learning at 175 billion parameters) and **homogenization** (consolidation of methodologies creating single points of failure across AI systems) (Section 1.1). The key insight is that these phenomena interact in "a potentially unsettling way"—homogenization provides enormous gains for domains with limited task-specific data, but any flaws in the foundation model are blindly inherited by all adapted models downstream (Section 1.1).

**Why it's significant:** This framework explains why foundation models introduce **systemic risk** beyond individual model failures. For example, the report notes that almost all state-of-the-art NLP models are adapted from one of a few foundation models (e.g., `BERT`, `RoBERTa`, `GPT-3`), meaning biases or vulnerabilities propagate across myriad applications in healthcare, law, and education simultaneously (Section 1.1). This is a fundamental innovation because it shifts risk assessment from evaluating individual deployed systems to evaluating the **shared infrastructure** underlying them. The framework has influenced subsequent research on model concentration risks and regulatory approaches to AI infrastructure.

### 4.3 The Ecosystem View: Beyond Model Weights

**What makes it different:** Traditional AI analysis focuses on model architectures and performance metrics. This report introduces an **ecosystem perspective** that extends from data creation through deployment, where people occupy both ends of the pipeline (Section 1.2, Figure 3). The five stages are: (1) data creation, (2) data curation, (3) training, (4) adaptation, and (5) deployment. Crucially, the report argues that different questions about foundation models (e.g., whether a model is ethical) must be answered with respect to **different stages** of the pipeline, rather than treating the model as a black box (Section 1.2).

**Why it's significant:** This reframing reveals that many impacts come from decisions made in stages beyond training. For instance, a problematic model capable of generating toxic content might be tolerable if appropriate precautions are taken during adaptation or deployment (Section 1.2). This insight has practical implications: it suggests that intervention points for mitigating harm exist throughout the pipeline, not just during model training. The ecosystem view also clarifies accountability—harms experienced by users result from specific adapted models, but attributing responsibility requires tracing back through multiple stages and potentially multiple organizations (Section 5.1). This represents a fundamental shift from model-centric to **process-centric** analysis of AI systems.

### 4.4 Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Evaluation Framework

**What makes it different:** Standard machine learning evaluation reports performance on held-out test sets for specific tasks. The report introduces a distinction between **intrinsic evaluation** (assessing the foundation model itself, divorced from specific tasks) and **extrinsic evaluation** (assessing task-specific adapted derivatives) (Section 4.4.1). This distinction is necessary because foundation models are intermediary assets that must be adapted to perform tasks, making direct comparison complicated by the adaptation process (Section 4.4.1).

**Why it's significant:** This framework addresses a critical gap in how the community measures progress. The report identifies that traditional task-based extrinsic evaluation does not provide a clear means by which emergent capabilities like in-context learning could have been identified—directly interacting with the foundation model appears necessary (Section 4.4.2). Furthermore, extrinsic evaluation should acknowledge **adaptation resources** (all data used to inform adaptation, access requirements for the foundation model), not just final performance, to ensure fair comparisons between different approaches (Section 4.4.3). This innovation has influenced subsequent benchmark design, encouraging evaluation protocols that account for the full cost of adaptation rather than just downstream accuracy.

### 4.5 The One Model–Many Models Paradigm for Interpretability

**What makes it different:** Interpretability research traditionally assumes task-specific models with clear input-output domains. The report introduces the **one model–many models** paradigm to capture the interpretability challenge for foundation models: they can be understood as either (1) single models utilizing generalizable mechanisms across tasks, or (2) large collections of independent expert models tailored to specific tasks (Section 4.11, Figure 23). This spectrum determines whether explanations of model behavior in one task are informative about behavior in other tasks (Section 4.11).

**Why it's significant:** This paradigm fundamentally changes how we approach understanding foundation models. If mechanisms are shared, understanding foundation models may be a tractable problem of characterizing these mechanisms and their relations. If mechanisms are independent, each adaptation must be analyzed independently, leading to profound uncertainty about the nature of any new adaptation (Section 4.11.3). This insight has influenced subsequent interpretability research, encouraging methods that probe whether different tasks rely on shared or distinct internal representations. The paradigm also highlights that interpretability methods presuming easy access to model parameters may centralize knowledge in the same hands that control the foundation model, raising equity concerns about who can interpret these systems (Section 4.11.4).

## 5. Experimental Analysis

**Critical Clarification:** This report is a **comprehensive survey and position paper**, not an empirical research study with original experiments. The authors synthesize quantitative findings from over 200 cited works to build their arguments about foundation models. This section summarizes the key quantitative evidence cited throughout the paper, organized by the claims they support, rather than describing original experimental methodology.

### 5.1 Evidence Synthesis Methodology

Rather than conducting new experiments, the report employs **systematic literature synthesis** across four domains (capabilities, applications, technology, society) with contributions from over 100 authors (Section 1.4). The quantitative evidence is drawn from:

- **Benchmark results** from model development papers (e.g., GPT-3, BERT, CLIP)
- **Industry deployment statistics** (e.g., Google Search usage, legal sector data)
- **Economic and environmental impact studies** (e.g., carbon emissions, training costs)
- **Social science research** (e.g., bias measurements, access to justice statistics)

This approach allows the report to make claims grounded in empirical evidence while acknowledging that the field lacks unified evaluation standards for foundation models themselves (Section 4.4).

### 5.2 Quantitative Evidence for Core Claims

#### 5.2.1 Scale and Emergence Claims

The report cites specific parameter counts and performance metrics to support the emergence hypothesis:

| Model | Parameters | Key Emergent Capability | Source |
|-------|-----------|------------------------|--------|
| GPT-2 | 1.5 billion | Limited in-context learning | Section 1.1 |
| GPT-3 | 175 billion | Robust in-context learning | Section 1, 1.1 |
| BERT | 340 million (base) | State-of-the-art NLP adaptation | Section 1, 2.1.2 |

The report notes that GPU throughput and memory increased **10× over 4 years**, enabling this scale (Section 1.1). The performance jump is quantified in Section 2.1.2:

> The best system for answering open-ended science questions in 2018, before foundation models, could get **73.1%** on the NY Regents 8th grade science exam. A year later in 2019, an adapted foundation model scored **91.6%** (Section 2.1.2).

This **18.5 percentage point improvement** in one year supports the claim that foundation models represent a paradigm shift rather than incremental progress.

#### 5.2.2 Homogenization Evidence

The report provides quantitative evidence for the homogenization claim:

- **Almost all state-of-the-art NLP models** are adapted from one of a few foundation models (BERT, RoBERTa, BART, T5) (Section 1.1)
- **Over 6,000 languages** exist worldwide, but multilingual foundation models are trained on only **around 100 languages** (Section 2.1.3, Figure 5)
- This creates representation gaps where languages like Fula (65 million speakers) have few or no NLP resources (Section 2.1.3)

The homogenization risk is illustrated through bias propagation: the report cites that BERT encodes an **Anglocentric similarity metric** by default, which could be harmful if applied across contexts (Section 5.6.1, citing Zhou et al. 2021a).

#### 5.2.3 Robustness and Distribution Shift Results

Section 4.8.1 provides specific quantitative evidence for foundation model robustness:

| Model | ImageNet Accuracy | ImageNetV2 | ImageNet Sketch |
|-------|------------------|------------|-----------------|
| ResNet50 | 76% | Baseline | Baseline |
| CLIP | 76% | **+6%** | **+35%** |

This shows CLIP achieves the same in-distribution accuracy but significantly better out-of-distribution performance, supporting the claim that foundation models trained on diverse data improve robustness to certain distribution shifts (Section 4.8.1). However, the report notes this does not extend to all shifts—spurious correlations and temporal drift remain challenging (Section 4.8.2).

### 5.3 Application Domain Evidence

#### 5.3.1 Healthcare and Biomedicine

The report cites quantitative data to motivate foundation model applications in healthcare:

- Healthcare expenditures account for **17% of US GDP** (Section 3.1)
- **30% of healthcare spending** may be wasteful due to administrative inefficiency and preventable medical errors (Section 3.1.1)
- Drug development takes **over 10 years** and costs **more than $1 billion** (Section 3.1.2)
- **80% of clinical trials fail** due to inability to show efficacy/safety or patient matching problems (Section 3.1.2)

These numbers support the claim that foundation models could provide significant value through improved efficiency, though the report acknowledges that medical datasets have a **long history of bias** across sexes, races, ethnicities, and socioeconomic backgrounds (Section 3.1.3).

#### 5.3.2 Legal Applications

Section 3.2 provides quantitative evidence for access to justice gaps:

- **1.3 million lawyers** in the US with annual legal services revenues exceeding **$300 billion** (Section 3.2)
- **86% of low-income individuals** with civil legal problems receive inadequate or no legal help (Section 3.2)
- **73% of county-based public defender offices** exceeded maximum recommended caseloads in 2007 (Section 3.2)
- US Supreme Court opinions average **4,700 words**; briefs can have **15,000 words**; law review articles **20,000-30,000 words** (Section 3.2.3)

The report includes a concrete failure case demonstrating current model limitations (Section 3.2.3, "Legal Reasoning with GPT-3"):

> When asked whether a **$1 million** liquidated damages clause for a **Toyota Corolla** is enforceable, GPT-3 incorrectly stated the amount was enforceable, failing to recognize it as exorbitant even when explicitly told the law requires such clauses to not be exorbitant.

This qualitative failure supports the quantitative claim that current models cannot reliably perform legal reasoning despite their scale.

#### 5.3.3 Education Sector Data

Section 3.3 cites:

- Private education debt held by US students reached **$1.6 trillion**, surpassing total credit card debt (Section 3.3)
- Approximately **13% of occupations** have a primary task related to writing, with a total wage bill of **over $675 billion** (Section 5.5.1)

These figures motivate the potential economic impact of foundation models on education and knowledge work.

### 5.4 Environmental and Economic Impact Evidence

#### 5.4.1 Carbon Emissions

Section 5.3 provides specific environmental cost estimates:

- Training one **BERT-base model** produces emissions offset by only **40 trees grown for 10 years** (Section 5.3, citing Strubell et al. 2019)
- A more energy-efficient translation model deployed at commercial scale could save between **78 kgCO2eq and 12,768 kgCO2eq per day** depending on the energy grid (Section 5.3)
- This is equivalent to the carbon sequestered by **1 to 211 trees grown for 10 years**, or **0.35 to 57.4 acres of forest in one year** (Section 5.3)

The report includes a cost-benefit framework (Figure 26, Equation 7):

$$V(M) = S(M) - C(M) - E(M) - O(M)$$

Where $S$ is social benefit, $C$ is social cost of carbon, $E$ is energy cost, and $O$ is second-order environmental effects (Section 5.3.2).

#### 5.4.2 Training Costs and Centralization

Section 5.5.3 (footnote 123) cites Lambda Lab estimates:

- GPT-3 training costs: **over $4.6 million**
- Research and development costs: **$11.4-27.6 million**
- Hardware to run GPT-3: **$100,000-150,000** (without electricity, cooling, backup)
- Annual running costs: **minimum $87,000**

These numbers support the centralization claim that only well-resourced institutions can train competitive foundation models (Section 5.5.3, 5.6.2).

### 5.5 Evaluation Framework Evidence

Section 4.4 discusses evaluation challenges with quantitative examples:

- Meta-benchmarks like **SuperGLUE** may not be sufficiently informative for policymakers (Section 4.4.2)
- A well-tuned prompt can be worth **around 100 training examples** (Section 4.3.1, citing Le Scao and Rush 2021)
- Lightweight adaptation techniques sometimes achieve comparable performance to full fine-tuning despite updating **1000× fewer parameters** (Section 4.3.1)

However, the report notes uncertainty: **we remain uncertain how lightweight adaptation techniques scale as model size increases** (Section 4.3.1).

### 5.6 Assessment of Evidence Quality

#### 5.6.1 Strengths of the Quantitative Synthesis

1. **Breadth of sources:** The report draws from over 200 citations across technical, economic, legal, and social science domains, providing multi-dimensional evidence for its claims.

2. **Specific metrics:** Rather than vague claims, the report provides concrete numbers (parameter counts, accuracy percentages, cost estimates, emission figures) that can be independently verified.

3. **Failure cases documented:** The GPT-3 legal reasoning failure (Section 3.2.3) and CLIP's "iPod apple" misclassification (Section 4.7.1) provide concrete examples of limitations alongside success metrics.

4. **Trade-offs quantified:** The report quantifies trade-offs like the efficiency-expressivity tension in transformers (Section 4.1.1) and the amortization point where foundation models become more energy-efficient than training from scratch (Figure 27 shows **~80 tasks** as the break-even point for BERT).

#### 5.6.2 Limitations and Gaps

1. **No original experiments:** The report does not conduct new experiments to validate its framework. Claims about emergence, homogenization, and societal impact rely on synthesizing others' work.

2. **Inconsistent measurement standards:** The report acknowledges that evaluation practices are fragmented—different papers use different benchmarks, making cross-model comparison difficult (Section 4.4).

3. **Temporal limitations:** Many statistics are from 2019-2021. Given the rapid pace of foundation model development, some numbers (e.g., training costs, parameter counts) may be outdated.

4. **Limited ablation evidence:** The report cites ablation studies from other papers (e.g., on adaptation methods in Section 4.3) but does not present systematic ablation analysis of its own claims about emergence or homogenization.

5. **Conditional robustness claims:** The robustness benefits of foundation models are **not universal**—Section 4.8.2 notes they do not address spurious correlations or temporal drift, and zero-shot transfer in CLIP **suffers greatly in satellite image domains** (Section 4.8.2).

### 5.7 Summary of Quantitative Support for Claims

| Claim | Quantitative Support | Confidence Level |
|-------|---------------------|------------------|
| Scale enables emergence | GPT-3 (175B) vs GPT-2 (1.5B) capability gap | High (multiple sources) |
| Homogenization creates systemic risk | Most SOTA models from few foundations | High (industry observation) |
| Foundation models improve robustness | CLIP +35% on ImageNet Sketch | Medium (task-dependent) |
| Significant environmental costs | BERT = 40 trees × 10 years | Medium (grid-dependent) |

| Legal reasoning limitations | GPT-3 fails liquidated damages test | High (documented failure) |
| Healthcare efficiency potential | 30% waste, 80% trial failure | Medium (projection) |

**Overall Assessment:** The quantitative evidence **convincingly supports** the report's descriptive claims about the current state of foundation models (scale, costs, performance). However, the **predictive claims** about societal impact, centralization trends, and long-term risks are necessarily more speculative, supported by economic reasoning and analogies to prior technologies rather than direct experimental validation. The report is transparent about these limitations, framing itself as establishing a framework for future research rather than providing definitive answers (Section 1.4, 6).

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This report provides a comprehensive framework for understanding foundation models, but it operates within important constraints and acknowledges significant unresolved questions. This section critically examines the assumptions underlying the analysis, identifies scenarios and problem settings not addressed, discusses computational and data constraints, and highlights weaknesses and open questions that remain.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions

The report's analysis rests on several key assumptions that shape its conclusions about opportunities and risks.

**Assumption 1: Scale Will Continue to Yield Capabilities**

The report assumes that scaling model size, data, and compute will continue to produce emergent capabilities, based on observed scaling laws (Section 1.1, 4.2.1). However, the authors acknowledge uncertainty about whether this trend will persist:

> We are still in the early days of understanding the principles that drive the scalability of learning algorithms, and while recent works have started to shed some light on these themes, much work remains to be done. (Section 4.1.2)

This assumption is critical because many claimed benefits (e.g., improved robustness, few-shot learning) depend on continued scaling. If scaling returns diminish or plateau, the economic and environmental costs documented in Section 5.3 may not be justified by corresponding capability gains.

**Assumption 2: Foundation Models Will Be Widely Adopted**

The homogenization risks described in Section 1.1 and 5.6.1 presuppose that foundation models will become the dominant infrastructure for AI systems across domains. However, the report acknowledges this is not inevitable:

> Homogenization is not inevitable. As model developers intentionally broaden the range of perspectives represented in their datasets, more research is needed on the capacity of foundation models to deliver a diversity of perspectives. (Section 5.6.1)

If alternative paradigms emerge (e.g., smaller specialized models, decentralized training), the systemic risk analysis may overstate the concentration of failure modes.

**Assumption 3: Adaptation Will Preserve Foundation Model Properties**

The ecosystem view (Section 1.2, 3.4) assumes that properties of the foundation model (biases, capabilities, vulnerabilities) propagate through adaptation to downstream applications. However, the report notes uncertainty about this propagation:

> We remain uncertain how lightweight adaptation techniques scale as model size increases. (Section 4.3.1)

If adaptation methods can effectively filter or modify foundation model properties, the homogenization risk may be mitigated. Conversely, if adaptation amplifies certain defects (as suggested in Section 5.1.3 regarding bias amplification), risks may be understated.

**Assumption 4: Interdisciplinary Collaboration Is Feasible**

The report's prescriptive recommendations depend on deep interdisciplinary collaboration between technologists, social scientists, ethicists, and policymakers (Section 1.3, 1.4). However, the authors acknowledge structural barriers:

> Academia has not been able to participate in the fullest way possible due to the loss in accessibility. (Section 1.3)

The resource gap between industry and academia (GPT-3 training costs over $4.6 million, Section 5.5.3 footnote 123) may prevent the collaborative governance the report advocates, limiting the practical implementation of its recommendations.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Problem Settings

The report explicitly acknowledges gaps in its coverage, both in terms of application domains and technical scenarios.

**Application Domain Gaps**

Section 1.4 explicitly notes that many applications are not included:

> Many applications (e.g., natural sciences, music, finance, agriculture) are not included, though they are as likely to be affected as the applications we have chosen to discuss. (Section 1.4)

This limitation is significant because different domains may face unique risks. For example:
- **Finance**: Algorithmic trading using foundation models could introduce systemic financial risks not analogous to healthcare or legal applications.
- **Agriculture**: Foundation models for crop monitoring may face different data quality and environmental impact considerations than urban applications.
- **Natural Sciences**: Scientific discovery applications may have different validation requirements than consumer-facing applications.

The report's focus on healthcare, law, and education (Section 3) provides depth but limits generalizability to other sectors.

**Geographic and Legal Limitations**

The legal analysis centers on US law:

> We restrict our discussion to legal applications in the United States because of the expertise of the authors. Some discussion here may apply to legal venues globally, however. (Section 3.2, footnote 31)

This limits the applicability of the legality analysis (Section 5.4) to other jurisdictions with different regulatory frameworks (e.g., EU's GDPR, China's AI regulations). The report acknowledges foreign privacy laws like GDPR will affect American model creators (Section 5.4.1), but does not provide equivalent analysis of non-US legal systems.

**Technical Edge Cases Not Fully Explored**

Several technical scenarios receive limited treatment:

1. **In-Context Learning Mechanisms**: The report identifies in-context learning as an emergent capability (Section 1.1) but acknowledges the theoretical understanding is incomplete:

   > What is the mechanism by which the model "learns" from the different examples by simply executing with the examples as inputs? (Section 4.10.3)

   The modularization proposed in Section 4.10 does not directly apply to in-context learning because there is no parameter optimization during adaptation.

2. **Multimodal Inconsistencies**: Section 4.7.1 notes that multimodality may increase attack surfaces through inconsistencies across modalities (e.g., CLIP classifying an apple with "iPod" text as an iPod), but does not provide systematic analysis of when these inconsistencies arise or how to mitigate them.

3. **Temporal Adaptation**: The report identifies temporal drift as a challenge (Section 4.3.2, 4.8.2) but acknowledges solutions are incomplete:

   > Classic techniques like re-weighting training data and dynamic evaluation can partially alleviate, but not fully solve, this problem. (Section 4.3.2)

   The report does not provide quantitative guidance on how frequently foundation models need updating or what the costs of continual adaptation entail.

### 6.3 Computational, Data, and Scalability Constraints

The report documents significant constraints that limit both the development and analysis of foundation models.

**Computational Barriers**

Section 4.5 and 5.5.3 detail computational constraints that create accessibility gaps:

| Resource | Requirement | Implication |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| GPT-3 Training | >$4.6 million | Only well-resourced institutions can train competitive models |
| Hardware to Run GPT-3 | $100,000-150,000 | Deployment costs exclude smaller organizations |
| Annual Running Costs | Minimum $87,000 | Ongoing operational burden |

These constraints create what Section 5.6.2 terms "concentration of power," where only venture-funded startups, dominant tech giants, and state governments can produce cutting-edge foundation models. This limits the diversity of perspectives in model development and may exacerbate the biases documented in Section 5.1.

**Data Quality and Documentation Gaps**

Section 4.6 identifies critical data constraints:

> At present, the relationship between the training data, along with associated data practices, and the intrinsic biases acquired by the foundation model remains unclear; future work is critically needed to clarify this relationship. (Section 5.1.3)

The report notes that foundation model training data is often selected using "unspecified or unclear principles with a general lack of transparency" (Section 4.6). This opacity limits the ability to:
- Trace bias sources (Section 5.1.3)
- Understand model failures (Section 4.11)
- Ensure legal compliance (Section 5.4.1)

**Scalability of Evaluation**

Section 4.4 acknowledges that evaluation practices have not scaled with model development:

> Standard paradigms for the evaluation of machine learning models are not designed explicitly for the setting of foundation models. (Section 4.4.1)

The report identifies specific evaluation gaps:
- **Intrinsic measures lack validation**: The predictive validity of intrinsic measures (their ability to predict downstream outcomes) is unclear (Section 4.4.2)
- **Adaptation resources not tracked**: Existing evaluations often do not account for all data used in adaptation or access requirements (Section 4.4.3)
- **Benchmark diversity limited**: Traditional benchmarks require large training datasets, but foundation models enable smaller, more diverse benchmarks that are not yet standard (Section 4.4.4)

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

The report identifies numerous unresolved questions that limit confidence in its conclusions.

**Theoretical Understanding Gaps**

Section 4.10 explicitly acknowledges theoretical limitations:

> The community currently has a limited theoretical understanding of foundation models, despite much recent progress. (Section 4.10)

Specific gaps include:
- **Pretraining-adaptation interface**: The report proposes a modularization but notes "the main challenge with foundation models" is understanding why small pretraining loss implies small adaptation loss (Section 4.10.2)
- **In-context learning**: Existing theoretical frameworks do not directly apply to adaptation without parameter updates (Section 4.10.3)
- **Mathematical tools**: The report notes "we generally lack mathematical tools for relating properties of foundation models to the structure in the population data distribution" (Section 4.10.4)

These gaps mean that many claims about foundation model behavior are empirical observations rather than theoretically grounded predictions, limiting generalizability.

**Robustness Limitations**

Section 4.8 provides nuanced assessment of robustness claims:

> We anticipate that foundation models are not a panacea for distribution shifts. (Section 4.8.2)

Specific limitations include:
- **Spurious correlations persist**: Foundation models can exacerbate biases present in training data (Section 4.8.2, 5.1.3)
- **Temporal drift unaddressed**: Models cannot handle changes to world knowledge without re-training (Section 4.8.2)
- **Domain-specific failures**: Zero-shot transfer in CLIP "suffers greatly in satellite image domains" despite strong ImageNet performance (Section 4.8.2)

The report does not provide clear guidance on when foundation models will improve robustness versus when they will fail, limiting practical deployment decisions.

**Interpretability Uncertainties**

Section 4.11 identifies fundamental interpretability challenges:

> We simply do not know the extent to which [adaptations] share common mechanisms. (Section 4.11.4)

The one model–many models paradigm (Section 4.11, Figure 23) creates uncertainty:
- If mechanisms are shared, understanding is tractable but requires characterizing these mechanisms
- If mechanisms are independent, each adaptation must be analyzed independently, "leading to profound uncertainty about the nature of any new adaptation" (Section 4.11.4)

This uncertainty limits the ability to provide assurances about model behavior in novel contexts.

**Mitigation Effectiveness Unknown**

Section 5.1.4 acknowledges limitations in bias mitigation:

> Technical mitigation of all forms at present is severely limited: methods that measure or combat intrinsic bias are brittle or ineffectual. (Section 5.1.4)

The report cites evidence that:
- Some technical interventions may be "simultaneously unsatisfiable, impossible, or may even exacerbate inequity" (Section 5.1.4)
- Methods for measuring intrinsic bias do not correlate with application bias (Section 5.1.4, citing Goldfarb-Tarrant et al. 2021)

This limits the practical utility of the report's fairness recommendations.

**Release Strategy Trade-offs Unresolved**

Section 5.6.4 discusses the tension between transparency and misuse risks:

> Does the benefit of release outweigh the potential for harm from actors sophisticated enough to use a released model or API but not sophisticated enough to create their own? We believe that the answer is yes. (Section 5.6.4)

However, this conclusion is presented as the authors' belief rather than empirically validated. The report acknowledges that "testing had exposed some but by no means all of the model's capabilities and limitations" even after eight months of staged release for GPT-2 (Section 5.6.4), suggesting uncertainty about optimal release strategies.

### 6.5 Summary of Key Limitations

| Category | Limitation | Impact on Claims |
|----------|------------|------------------|
| **Assumptions** | Continued scaling yields capabilities | Benefits may not materialize if scaling plateaus |
| **Assumptions** | Widespread adoption will occur | Systemic risk analysis depends on homogenization |
| **Coverage** | Applications beyond healthcare/law/education excluded | Generalizability to other domains uncertain |
| **Coverage** | US-centric legal analysis | Recommendations may not apply globally |
| **Computational** | High costs limit accessibility | Diversity of development perspectives constrained |
| **Data** | Training data opacity | Bias tracing and accountability limited |
| **Theoretical** | Limited understanding of pretraining-adaptation interface | Predictions about model behavior uncertain |
| **Robustness** | Not all distribution shifts addressed | Deployment risks may be understated |
| **Interpretability** | Unknown mechanism sharing across tasks | Assurance about novel adaptations limited |
| **Mitigation** | Bias interventions often ineffectual | Fairness recommendations may not achieve goals |

**Overall Assessment**: The report is transparent about its limitations, framing itself as "just the beginning of a paradigm shift" rather than providing definitive answers (Section 6). The limitations identified do not invalidate the report's contributions but indicate areas where future research is needed. The most critical gaps are theoretical (understanding why foundation models work), practical (effective mitigation strategies), and governance-related (how to achieve interdisciplinary collaboration given resource asymmetries). These limitations suggest that while the report establishes an important framework for understanding foundation models, many claims about risks and benefits remain provisional pending further empirical and theoretical work.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This report does more than document the current state of foundation models—it establishes a conceptual infrastructure that reshapes how the field approaches AI research, development, and governance. By introducing shared terminology, analytical frameworks, and methodological approaches, this work enables new questions to be asked and new collaborations to form across disciplines that previously operated in isolation. This section details how the report transforms the research landscape, identifies concrete follow-up research directions, outlines practical applications, and provides guidance on when foundation model approaches are appropriate versus alternative methods.

### 7.1 Transforming the Research Landscape

The report's contributions extend beyond technical documentation to fundamentally alter how the AI community conceptualizes and studies large-scale models. Three transformations are particularly significant.

#### 7.1.1 Establishing Shared Vocabulary for Interdisciplinary Discourse

Prior to this report, researchers from different disciplines lacked common terminology to discuss foundation models. Technical researchers used terms like `pretrained model` or `self-supervised model`, while policymakers and social scientists had no standardized vocabulary to engage with these systems (Section 1.1.1). The introduction of `foundation model` as a term serves a critical function:

> We chose the term foundation models to identify the models and the emerging paradigm that are the subject of this report. In particular, the word "foundation" specifies the role these models play: a foundation model is itself incomplete but serves as the common basis from which many task-specific models are built via adaptation. (Section 1.1.1)

This terminology enables **interdisciplinary communication** by capturing both the technical properties (trained on broad data, adaptable to downstream tasks) and the sociological implications (intermediary assets with unfinished character). The term has since been adopted widely in academic literature, industry communications, and policy documents, demonstrating its utility in bridging disciplinary divides.

The practical impact of this shared vocabulary is that it enables stakeholders to reason about **responsibility and accountability** in new ways. By designating these models as "foundations," the report emphasizes that poorly-constructed foundations create systemic risks, while well-executed ones provide reliable infrastructure (Section 1.1.1). This framing has influenced subsequent regulatory discussions about AI infrastructure governance.

#### 7.1.2 Shifting from Model-Centric to Ecosystem-Centric Analysis

Traditional AI research focuses on model architectures, training procedures, and performance metrics in isolation. This report introduces an **ecosystem perspective** that extends analysis across five stages: data creation, data curation, training, adaptation, and deployment (Section 1.2, Figure 3). This transformation has three implications:

1. **Intervention points multiply**: Rather than viewing model training as the sole locus of responsibility, the ecosystem view reveals that harms can be mitigated at multiple stages. For example, a model capable of generating toxic content might be tolerable if appropriate precautions are taken during adaptation or deployment (Section 1.2).

2. **Accountability becomes traceable**: The ecosystem framework enables tracing harms back through multiple stages and potentially multiple organizations. Section 5.1 notes that attributing responsibility requires understanding how intrinsic biases in foundation models propagate through adaptation to extrinsic harms in deployed applications (Section 5.1.2).

3. **Governance structures must evolve**: The report argues that different questions about foundation models must be answered with respect to different stages of the pipeline (Section 1.2). This implies that regulatory frameworks cannot treat foundation models as monolithic objects but must account for the distributed nature of development and deployment.

This shift has influenced subsequent research on AI governance, encouraging frameworks that account for the full lifecycle of AI systems rather than focusing solely on model weights or deployment decisions.

#### 7.1.3 Reframing Risk Assessment Around Systemic Infrastructure

Prior risk assessment in AI focused on individual deployed systems. The **emergence and homogenization framework** (Section 1.1) reframes risk around the shared infrastructure underlying AI systems. This transformation is significant because:

- **Homogenization creates single points of failure**: Almost all state-of-the-art NLP models are adapted from one of a few foundation models (e.g., `BERT`, `RoBERTa`, `GPT-3`), meaning biases or vulnerabilities propagate across myriad applications simultaneously (Section 1.1).

- **Emergence creates uncertainty**: Capabilities like in-context learning emerge at scale without being explicitly trained for, making it difficult to anticipate failure modes before deployment (Section 1.1).

- **Systemic risk requires systemic solutions**: The report argues that derisking is the central challenge in further development from both ethical (Section 5.6) and AI safety (Section 4.9) perspectives.

This reframing has influenced subsequent research on model concentration risks, regulatory approaches to AI infrastructure, and industry practices around model documentation and auditing.

### 7.2 Research Agenda: Follow-Up Directions Enabled

The report identifies numerous gaps in current understanding that define a research agenda for the coming years. Below we organize these directions by domain, with concrete questions and methodological approaches.

#### 7.2.1 Theoretical Foundations

Section 4.10 explicitly acknowledges that "the community currently has a limited theoretical understanding of foundation models" (Section 4.10). Key open questions include:

| Research Question | Why It Matters | Suggested Approach |
|------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Pretraining-adaptation interface**: Under what conditions does small population pretraining loss imply small minimal adaptation loss? (Section 4.10.2) | Explains why foundation models work; guides training objective design | Develop mathematical tools for relating properties of foundation models to structure in population data distribution (Section 4.10.4) |
| **In-context learning mechanisms**: What is the mechanism by which the model "learns" from examples without parameter updates? (Section 4.10.3) | Critical for understanding emergent capabilities; informs prompt design | Analyze pretraining distribution conditioned on in-context learning prompts using latent variable generative models (Section 4.10.3) |
| **Scaling laws for bias**: Do biases scale predictably with model size, similar to accuracy metrics? (Section 5.1.3) | Enables systematic study of bias at smaller scales to inform practices at larger scales | Establish scaling laws for bias akin to those for accuracy (Section 5.1.3) |

The report proposes a modularized analysis framework that separates pretraining from adaptation phases, allowing independent theoretical work on each (Section 4.10.1). This modularization enables theoreticians to work on tractable subproblems while maintaining connections to the full foundation model paradigm.

#### 7.2.2 Evaluation Methodologies

Section 4.4 identifies critical gaps in how foundation models are evaluated. Follow-up research should address:

**Intrinsic Evaluation Development**: The report argues for direct measurement of foundation model properties divorced from specific tasks (Section 4.4.2). Research directions include:
- Adapting psycholinguistic measures from human cognition to evaluate foundation model linguistic competencies (Section 4.4.2)
- Developing human-in-the-loop evaluation methods to assess generative and interactive capabilities (Section 4.4.2)
- Establishing predictive validity of intrinsic measures (their ability to predict downstream outcomes) (Section 4.4.2)

**Adaptation Resource Accounting**: Section 4.4.3 argues that extrinsic evaluation should acknowledge all resources used in adaptation, not just final performance. Research should:
- Develop standardized protocols for tracking adaptation data, access requirements, and computational costs
- Create benchmarks that enable fair comparison between different adaptation methods (e.g., fine-tuning vs. prompting vs. lightweight adaptation)
- Investigate how adaptation resource requirements scale with foundation model size

**Diverse Benchmark Design**: The report envisions a regime where benchmarks are smaller and more diverse, de-emphasizing quantity in favor of quality and diversity (Section 4.4.4). Research should:
- Develop methodologies for creating ecologically valid benchmarks that capture real-world use cases
- Create mechanisms for stakeholders to interact with and manipulate evaluation rankings to align with their values (Section 4.4.4)
- Design benchmarks that capture criteria beyond accuracy (robustness, fairness, efficiency, environmental impact) (Section 4.4.4)

#### 7.2.3 Interpretability and Mechanism Discovery

The **one model–many models paradigm** (Section 4.11, Figure 23) creates a research agenda around understanding whether different adaptations share common mechanisms:

| Question | If Shared Mechanisms | If Independent Mechanisms |
|----------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| **Interpretability approach** | Characterize finite set of generalizable mechanisms | Analyze each adaptation independently |
| **Assurance about novel tasks** | High confidence from mechanism characterization | Profound uncertainty (Section 4.11.4) |
| **Research priority** | Probe for shared representations across tasks | Develop scalable methods for per-task analysis |

Section 4.11.3 suggests specific methodological approaches:
- **Probing methods**: Hypothesis-driven approaches that quantify how much information nodes make available about specific concepts (e.g., grammatical features, demographic attributes) (Section 4.11.3)
- **Causal intervention**: Actively manipulating internal representations according to experimental protocols supporting causal inference (Section 2.6.3)
- **Compositional understanding**: Building up understanding of complex behaviors by characterizing individual mechanisms and their relations (Section 4.11.3)

The report emphasizes that interpretability research must consider **who can interpret** these systems. Methods presuming easy access to model parameters may centralize knowledge in the same hands that control the foundation model, raising equity concerns (Section 4.11.4).

#### 7.2.4 Mitigation and Intervention Strategies

Section 5.1.4 acknowledges that "technical mitigation of all forms at present is severely limited" (Section 5.1.4). Research priorities include:

**Bias Mitigation Effectiveness**: The report cites evidence that some technical interventions may be "simultaneously unsatisfiable, impossible, or may even exacerbate inequity" (Section 5.1.4). Research should:
- Systematically evaluate which mitigation strategies work for which types of bias
- Investigate why methods for measuring intrinsic bias do not correlate with application bias (Section 5.1.4)
- Develop interventions that target different stages of the pipeline (training data, modeling objectives, adaptation methods, test-time use) (Section 5.1.4)

**Feedback and Recourse Mechanisms**: Section 5.1.4 notes that "mechanisms are not in place to even communicate these harms to foundation model providers" (Section 5.1.4). Research should:
- Design reporting structures that enable downstream users to submit feedback to foundation model providers
- Develop frameworks for attributing responsibility for harms to foundation model providers vs. application developers (Section 5.1.4)
- Create avenues for recourse once harm has been attributed (Section 5.6.3)

**Release Strategy Optimization**: Section 5.6.4 discusses the tension between transparency and misuse risks. Research should:
- Evaluate the effectiveness of staged release programs for exposing capabilities and limitations
- Develop neutral third-party mechanisms for deciding who receives early access (Section 5.6.4)
- Quantify the trade-offs between open access for auditing and risks from less well-resourced actors (Section 5.6.4)

#### 7.2.5 Sociotechnical Integration

The report emphasizes that foundation models require **deep interdisciplinary collaboration** commensurate with their sociotechnical nature (Section 1). Research directions include:

**Governance Structures**: Section 5.6.3 notes that "professional norms for responsible foundation model development and deployment are not yet well established" (Section 5.6.3). Research should:
- Develop institutional mechanisms for norm establishment (funding structures, model repositories, conference requirements) (Section 5.6.3)
- Investigate how to operationalize interdisciplinary collaboration given resource asymmetries between industry and academia (Section 1.3)
- Design frameworks for "when not to build" that incorporate ethical, social, and technical considerations (Section 5.6.5)

**Legal and Regulatory Frameworks**: Section 5.4 identifies numerous unresolved legal questions. Research should:
- Clarify how existing laws (e.g., CFAA, copyright, privacy laws) apply to foundation model training and deployment (Section 5.4.1)
- Develop standards for liability allocation between foundation model providers and application developers (Section 5.4.2)
- Investigate how legal protections for model outputs (e.g., First Amendment, copyright) should be structured (Section 5.4.3)

**Economic Impact Assessment**: Section 5.5 notes that foundation models are poised to be an important general-purpose technology. Research should:
- Quantify productivity impacts across different occupations and industries (Section 5.5.1)
- Analyze effects on wage inequality and labor demand (Section 5.5.2)
- Investigate ownership concentration and its implications for economic mobility (Section 5.5.3)

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The report identifies three application domains as foundational pillars of society: healthcare, law, and education (Section 1.4.2, Section 3). Below we detail concrete use cases and implementation considerations for each.

#### 7.3.1 Healthcare and Biomedicine

Section 3.1 outlines opportunities where foundation models can improve efficiency and accuracy in healthcare delivery and biomedical research.

**Concrete Use Cases**:

| Application | Foundation Model Capability | Implementation Considerations |
|-------------|---------------------------|------------------------------|
| **EHR Interface** | Summarize patient visitation, retrieve relevant cases, suggest diagnoses (Section 3.1.1) | Must guarantee factual accuracy; requires explainability for clinical decisions (Section 3.1.3) |
| **Patient Question Answering** | Answer preventive care questions, provide medical explanatory information (Section 3.1.1) | Must quantify uncertainty and defer to experts when uncertain (Section 3.1.3) |
| **Drug Discovery** | Generate experimental protocols, design molecules given existing data (Section 3.1.2) | Requires integration of multimodal data (molecules, proteins, clinical trials) (Section 3.1.3) |
| **Clinical Trial Matching** | Automate matching of eligible patients based on multimodal profiles (Section 3.1.2) | Must observe privacy laws (e.g., HIPAA); requires representative training data (Section 3.1.3) |

**When to Use Foundation Models**: Foundation models are particularly valuable in healthcare when:
- **Expert time is expensive**: The sample efficiency of adaptation reduces the need for extensive labeled data from medical experts (Section 3.1)
- **Multimodal integration is required**: Foundation models can integrate diverse data types (images, text, molecules, time series) that would require separate models under traditional approaches (Section 3.1.3)
- **Rapid adaptation is needed**: During emergencies (e.g., COVID-19), foundation models can be quickly adapted to new diseases or diagnostic tasks (Section 3.1.3)

**When to Avoid**: Foundation models should not be deployed in healthcare when:
- **Factual accuracy cannot be guaranteed**: Hallucinations or incorrect medical advice could cause direct harm (Section 3.1.1)
- **Privacy cannot be ensured**: Patient health records must observe privacy laws; federated learning may be required (Section 3.1.3)
- **Representative data is unavailable**: Medical datasets have a long history of bias across sexes, races, ethnicities, and socioeconomic backgrounds (Section 3.1.3)

#### 7.3.2 Legal Applications

Section 3.2 identifies opportunities where foundation models can improve access to justice by reducing costs and extending the reach of legal services.

**Concrete Use Cases**:

| Application | Foundation Model Capability | Implementation Considerations |
|-------------|---------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Legal Document Review** | Evaluate contracts, review terms of service, find relevant patents (Section 3.2.1) | Requires precision and understanding of highly technical language (Section 3.2.1) |
| **Legal Research** | Recommend relevant citations and holding statements when writing legal texts (Section 3.2.1) | Must retrieve valid case law and understand which has been overruled (Section 3.2.3) |
| **Discovery Process** | Label whether documents should be produced to opposing party (Section 3.2.1) | Current systems are costly due to supervised learning; few-shot capabilities could reduce costs (Section 3.2.1) |
| **Public Defender Support** | Identify errors and automate simple tasks to reduce resource constraints (Section 3.2.1) | Must not replace human judgment in high-stakes decisions (Section 3.2.1) |

**When to Use Foundation Models**: Foundation models are particularly valuable in legal contexts when:
- **Labeled data is scarce and expensive**: Attorney time costs hundreds of dollars per hour; few-shot learning capabilities reduce annotation needs (Section 3.2.2)
- **Long documents must be processed**: Legal documents average 4,700-30,000 words; foundation models with long-context capabilities can process these efficiently (Section 3.2.3)
- **Rapid adaptation to new jurisdictions is needed**: Foundation models can adapt to different courts or legal perspectives with minimal retraining (Section 3.2, Figure 13)

**When to Avoid**: Foundation models should not be deployed in legal contexts when:
- **Logical reasoning is required**: GPT-3 fails to recognize that $1 million liquidated damages for a Toyota Corolla is exorbitant, even when explicitly told the law requires such clauses to not be exorbitant (Section 3.2.3)
- **Factual precision is critical**: Foundation models can create fake facts, which can lead to sanctions against attorneys (Section 3.2.3)
- **High-stakes decisions affect liberty**: Risk scoring in criminal law requires careful consideration of biases, especially when language data is included (Section 3.2.1)

#### 7.3.3 Education

Section 3.3 identifies opportunities where foundation models can provide inclusive, high-quality instruction at scale.

**Concrete Use Cases**:

| Application | Foundation Model Capability | Implementation Considerations |
|-------------|---------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Student Feedback** | Diagnose mistakes in student understanding based on answers (Section 3.3.2) | Requires ability to "notice" why students make specific mistakes (Section 3.3.2) |
| **Content Generation** | Generate educational problems, adapt curriculum to student needs (Section 3.3.3) | Must align with learning goals; avoid technology-aided plagiarism (Section 1.4.2) |
| **Teacher Support** | Provide feedback to teachers on classroom discourse (Section 3.3) | Must respect student privacy (FERPA, COPPA) (Section 3.3.1) |
| **Personalized Learning** | Tailor learning process to individual students' needs and dispositions (Section 3.3) | Must not remove teachers from the loop; socioemotional development matters (Section 3.3.1) |

**When to Use Foundation Models**: Foundation models are particularly valuable in education when:
- **Subject matter understanding is required**: Foundation models can efficiently understand fluent code, mathematical notation, or scientific concepts with few examples (Section 3.3.2)
- **Multimodal integration enhances learning**: Foundation models can integrate text, diagrams, videos, and interactive elements to support diverse learning styles (Section 3.3, Figure 16)
- **Adaptive instruction is needed**: Foundation models can fluidly adapt tone and language to different education contexts (5th-grade science vs. college physics) (Section 3.3.3)

**When to Avoid**: Foundation models should not be deployed in education when:
- **Student privacy cannot be ensured**: FERPA limits sharing of personally identifiable student work; model weights could leak private training data (Section 3.3.1)
- **Teacher-student interaction would be replaced**: Removing teachers from the loop could have adverse effects on socioemotional skill development (Section 3.3.1)
- **Plagiarism detection is impossible**: It becomes complex to understand the extent of student contribution when working with powerful generative models (Section 3.3.1)

### 7.4 Integration Guidance: When to Use Foundation Models vs. Alternatives

The report's ecosystem perspective and technical analysis provide a basis for deciding when foundation model approaches are appropriate versus alternative methods. Below we provide concrete guidance organized by decision criteria.

#### 7.4.1 Decision Framework

| Criterion | Prefer Foundation Models | Prefer Alternative Approaches |
|-----------|------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Data availability** | Limited labeled data available; abundant unlabeled data exists (Section 1.1) | Extensive labeled datasets available for the specific task |
| **Task diversity** | Multiple downstream tasks needed from same base capabilities (Section 1.1) | Single, well-defined task with stable requirements |
| **Adaptation speed** | Rapid adaptation to new tasks or domains required (Section 4.3) | Task requirements are stable; one-time training acceptable |
| **Resource constraints** | Organization can access foundation model APIs or pretrained weights (Section 1.3) | Organization has resources to train task-specific models from scratch |
| **Robustness requirements** | Distribution shifts expected; foundation model trained on diverse data (Section 4.8.1) | Test distribution matches training distribution closely |
| **Interpretability needs** | Intrinsic interpretability methods available; mechanisms can be probed (Section 4.11) | High-stakes decisions requiring transparent reasoning (Section 5.4.2) |
| **Environmental concerns** | Foundation model can be amortized over many tasks (>80 tasks for BERT, Figure 27) | Single-task use where training from scratch is more efficient |
| **Bias mitigation** | Bias sources can be traced and addressed at foundation level (Section 5.1.3) | Task-specific bias can be more directly addressed |

#### 7.4.2 Adaptation Method Selection

Section 4.3.1 identifies three factors that determine which adaptation procedure is appropriate. Below we provide specific guidance:

**Compute Budget Constraints**:
- **High memory available**: Full fine-tuning of all parameters may be appropriate
- **Limited memory**: Use lightweight adaptation methods (e.g., prompt tuning, adapter layers, LoRA) that freeze most pretrained parameters and only learn task-specific parameters (Section 4.3.1)
- **Very limited compute**: Use in-context learning with carefully designed prompts; a well-tuned prompt can be worth around 100 training examples (Section 4.3.1)

**Data Availability**:
- **Abundant labeled data**: Traditional fine-tuning or lightweight fine-tuning methods
- **Limited labeled data**: Combine prompting and fine-tuning; fine-tuning a carefully prompted foundation model is significantly more data-efficient (Section 4.3.1)
- **No labeled data**: Use zero-shot or few-shot in-context learning with task descriptions

**Access to Gradients**:
- **Full gradient access**: Fine-tuning all parameters or lightweight adaptation with gradient-based optimization
- **Input gradient access only**: Optimize continuous prompts or prefixes while freezing model parameters (Section 4.3.1)
- **No gradient access (API only)**: In-context learning with prompt engineering; validate prompts on adaptation data (Section 4.3.1)

#### 7.4.3 Deployment Considerations

Section 1.2 emphasizes that deployment is where direct social impact occurs. Key considerations include:

**Gradual Release**: Standard practice in large-scale deployments is to conduct gradual releases, where deployment happens to an increasing fraction of users to partially mitigate potential harms (Section 1.2).

**Monitoring and Intervention**: Thoughtful monitoring and intervention is needed at every stage of the ecosystem, not just during training (Section 1.2). Deployed foundation models that affect people's lives should be subject to much more rigorous testing and auditing than research models (Section 1.2).

**Attribution and Tracing**: Adapted derivatives should be consistently labeled in a way that allows impacted parties to trace problems to their source (Section 5.6.3). Without labeling, it would be impossible to attribute responsibility for harms (Section 5.6.3).

#### 7.4.4 Risk Assessment Checklist

Before deploying a foundation model-based system, practitioners should assess:

- [ ] **Homogenization risk**: Does this application rely on one of a few foundation models that creates single points of failure? (Section 1.1)
- [ ] **Emergence uncertainty**: Have emergent capabilities and failure modes been adequately tested? (Section 1.1)
- [ ] **Bias propagation**: Have intrinsic biases in the foundation model been characterized and mitigated? (Section 5.1.2)
- [ ] **Adaptation appropriateness**: Is the adaptation method suitable for the compute budget, data availability, and access constraints? (Section 4.3.1)
- [ ] **Evaluation completeness**: Have both intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations been conducted with appropriate resource accounting? (Section 4.4)
- [ ] **Environmental impact**: Has the carbon/energy cost been assessed and mitigation strategies considered? (Section 5.3)
- [ ] **Legal compliance**: Does the deployment comply with relevant laws (privacy, copyright, liability)? (Section 5.4)
- [ ] **Recourse mechanisms**: Are there mechanisms for users to report harms and receive recourse? (Section 5.1.4)

### 7.5 Summary: A Framework for Responsible Development

This report establishes that foundation models represent not merely a technical advance but a **paradigm shift** in how AI systems are built and deployed (Section 6). The implications extend across research, practice, and governance:

**For Researchers**: The report provides a research agenda that prioritizes theoretical understanding (Section 4.10), evaluation methodologies (Section 4.4), interpretability (Section 4.11), and sociotechnical integration (Section 5.6). The modularized analysis framework enables tractable subproblems while maintaining connections to the full paradigm (Section 4.10.1).

**For Practitioners**: The decision framework (Section 7.4.1) and adaptation guidance (Section 7.4.2) provide concrete criteria for when foundation models are appropriate. The risk assessment checklist (Section 7.4.4) ensures that deployment decisions account for systemic risks beyond individual model performance.

**For Policymakers**: The ecosystem perspective (Section 1.2) reveals that governance must account for multiple stages and potentially multiple organizations. The report's framework for attributing responsibility (Section 5.1) and establishing norms (Section 5.6.3) provides a basis for regulatory approaches.

**For Society**: The report emphasizes that the future of foundation models is not predetermined but depends on choices made by technologists, policymakers, managers, workers, and other stakeholders (Section 5.5.4). The question of "when not to build" (Section 5.6.5) is as important as the question of how to build.

The report concludes with a call for collaboration:

> To ensure the responsible development and deployment of these models on durable foundations, we envision collaboration between different sectors, institutions, and disciplines from the onset to be especially critical. (Section 6)

This collaboration is not optional but necessary, given the fundamentally sociotechnical nature of foundation models (Section 1). The frameworks, terminology, and research agenda established in this report provide the infrastructure for that collaboration to occur.
