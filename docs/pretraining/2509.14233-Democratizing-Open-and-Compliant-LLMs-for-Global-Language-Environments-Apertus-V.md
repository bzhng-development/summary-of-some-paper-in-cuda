## 1. Executive Summary

Apertus addresses systemic shortcomings in data compliance and multilingual representation by releasing fully open 8B and 70B parameter models pretrained on 15 trillion tokens from 1811 languages, with ~40% allocated to non-English content. The project ensures legal compliance and privacy through retroactive robots.txt filtering, PII removal, and the Goldfish objective to suppress verbatim memorization, distinguishing it from prior open-weight models that lack reproducible data pipelines. This release matters because it establishes a transparent, reproducible baseline for trustworthy AI aligned with regulations like the EU AI Act, providing all artifacts including data scripts and checkpoints to enable independent audit and extension.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of Apertus, we must first examine the current landscape of Large Language Model (LLM) release practices. The field has rapidly evolved from closed proprietary systems to a mix of "open-weights" and "fully open" models. However, the paper argues that this evolution has introduced systemic gaps in **reproducibility**, **legal compliance**, and **linguistic equity**. Apertus is positioned not just as a new model, but as a corrective intervention in this ecosystem.

### 2.1 The "Open-Weights" Illusion vs. True Openness

A central motivation for this work is the distinction between **open-weights** and **fully open** models. In the current ecosystem, many popular models (e.g., Llama 3, Qwen 2.5) are described as "open," but technically they are **open-weights**. This means the organization releases the final model parameters (the weights) but keeps the training data, data processing pipelines, and sometimes even the training code proprietary.

*   **The Problem:** Without access to the data and code, the scientific community cannot reproduce the results, audit the training process for biases, or verify legal compliance. As noted in Section 1, "Most of today's open models are, in fact, not open-source or reproducible, but only open-weights."
*   **The Consequence:** This opacity prevents independent verification. If a model exhibits harmful behavior or copyright infringement, researchers cannot trace the issue back to specific data sources or preprocessing decisions.
*   **Prior Approaches:** Fully open initiatives like **OLMo** (Open Language Model) and **BLOOM** have attempted to solve this by releasing full artifacts. However, these models often lag behind open-weights counterparts in scale and performance. For instance, Table 16 categorizes models by openness; while OLMo-2-32B is fully open, Apertus aims to match the performance of much larger open-weights models (like Llama-3.1-70B) while maintaining full transparency.

Apertus positions itself as the **first fully open model at the 70B scale**. This is a critical threshold because 70B parameters is widely considered the minimum scale required for complex reasoning and instruction following that rivals proprietary systems. By releasing weights, code, data preparation scripts, and checkpoints (Section 1), Apertus attempts to prove that high performance does not require sacrificing scientific transparency.

### 2.2 The Data Compliance Gap

The second major motivation is **legal and ethical compliance** regarding training data. LLMs are trained on massive web crawls, often incorporating content without explicit consent from copyright holders.

*   **The Problem:** Standard industry practice involves crawling web data and respecting `robots.txt` (a protocol websites use to tell crawlers which pages they can access) only at the time of the crawl. However, website owners often update their `robots.txt` files *after* data has been collected to opt-out of AI training. Most existing datasets do not respect these retroactive changes.
*   **Real-World Impact:** This creates legal risk under emerging regulations like the **EU AI Act**, which requires transparency and respect for copyright. It also ethical concerns regarding content owner rights.
*   **Apertus's Approach:** The paper introduces a mechanism called **"robots.txt with Hindsight"** (Section 3.1.1). Instead of checking permissions only at crawl time, Apertus retroactively applies the most recent crawling permissions (as of January 2025) to all historical data snapshots from 2013–2024.
    *   **Mechanism:** They ranked URL domains by volume, retrieved the current `robots.txt` for the top one million English and non-English domains, and filtered out any content blocked by AI-specific user agents (e.g., `GPTBot`, `CCBot`).
    *   **Cost of Compliance:** This rigor comes at a cost. The paper reports an estimated token loss of approximately **8% in English data** and **4% in multilingual data** due to these exclusions (Section 3.1.1). This quantifies the trade-off between maximum data volume and legal compliance.

By prioritizing this compliance, Apertus targets enterprise and institutional users who cannot risk deploying models trained on potentially infringing data. This distinguishes it from prior open models that may offer higher performance but carry undefined legal liabilities.

### 2.3 The Multilingual Deficit

The third motivation addresses **linguistic equity**. Despite the global nature of the internet, most high-performing LLMs are heavily skewed toward English.

*   **The Problem:** Prior models often focus on single languages or small subsets of high-resource languages (e.g., English, Chinese, Spanish). Section 1 notes that even multilingual models like BLOOM or Aya train on "**~10× fewer**" languages than Apertus.
*   **The Consequence:** This creates a "digital divide" where AI capabilities are accessible primarily to English speakers. Low-resource languages (e.g., many African or indigenous languages) are excluded, limiting the technology's utility for global users and preserving cultural biases inherent in English-centric data.
*   **Apertus's Approach:** Apertus expands the pretraining corpus to **1811 languages** (Section 1), allocating approximately **40% of pretraining data to non-English content**.
    *   **Data Source:** This relies heavily on the **FineWeb-2** dataset (Section 3.2.2), which preserves languages in their natural frequency rather than filtering them out for quality alone.
    *   **Post-Training Support:** The commitment extends beyond pretraining. The post-training mixture includes data from **149 languages** (Section 1), ensuring the model can follow instructions and interact safely in diverse linguistic contexts, not just complete text in those languages.

This focus is not merely additive; it changes the model's architecture and evaluation. For example, the tokenizer was specifically selected (Mistral-Nemo v3) based on **Gini coefficient** metrics to ensure equitable tokenization costs across languages (Section 2.2), preventing certain languages from being computationally disadvantaged during inference.

### 2.4 Positioning Relative to Existing Work

Apertus situates itself at the intersection of **scale**, **transparency**, and **safety**. The following comparison illustrates its positioning against key prior works:

| Dimension | Proprietary / Open-Weights (e.g., Llama 3, GPT-4) | Prior Fully Open (e.g., OLMo, BLOOM) | Apertus V1 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Weights** | Released | Released | Released |
| **Training Data** | Hidden / Partial | Released | **Fully Released & Auditable** |
| **Compliance** | Opaque / Risk Unclear | Variable | **Retroactive `robots.txt` + PII Filter** |
| **Scale** | Very High (100B+) | Moderate (7B–32B) | **High (70B)** |
| **Languages** | English-Dominant | Moderate Multilingual | **1811 Languages (40% Non-English)** |
| **Memorization** | Standard Cross-Entropy | Standard Cross-Entropy | **Goldfish Objective** |

*   **Versus Open-Weights:** Apertus sacrifices some potential performance gains from unfiltered data (due to compliance filtering) to gain auditability and legal safety. It matches the 70B scale of models like Llama-3.1-70B but provides the data lineage that Llama does not.
*   **Versus Prior Fully Open:** Apertus significantly increases the scale (from ~32B in OLMo-2 to 70B) and the linguistic breadth (from ~100 languages to 1811). It also introduces novel training objectives like **Goldfish Loss** (Section 2.3) to mitigate verbatim memorization, a feature not standard in prior open releases.

In summary, the motivation for Apertus is to demonstrate that **compliance and openness are not bottlenecks to performance**. By engineering solutions for data filtering (Section 3.1), memorization mitigation (Section 2.3), and multilingual tokenization (Section 2.2), the project aims to set a new baseline where "open" means scientifically reproducible and legally robust, not just downloadable weights.

## 3. Technical Approach

This technical report presents a **foundation model development methodology** that prioritizes data compliance, multilingual representation, and memorization mitigation while achieving state-of-the-art performance at 70B parameter scale. The core idea is that transparency and legal compliance need not sacrifice capability—through careful engineering of data pipelines, training objectives, and architecture choices, Apertus demonstrates that fully open models can compete with proprietary systems.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

Apertus is a pair of large language models (8 billion and 70 billion parameters) trained from scratch on 15 trillion tokens of text from 1811 languages, with every step of the process documented and released for independent verification. The system solves the problem of building high-performance AI that respects copyright, protects privacy, and serves non-English speakers by combining retroactive data filtering, a novel memorization-suppression training objective called Goldfish Loss, and architecture optimizations that improve training efficiency by 30–40% compared to standard approaches.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The Apertus development pipeline consists of four major components that operate sequentially. First, the **Data Compliance Pipeline** (Section 3.1) ingests raw web crawl data from FineWeb-2 and other sources, then applies three filtering layers: retroactive robots.txt exclusion (removing ~8% of English tokens and ~4% of multilingual tokens), PII anonymization using regex patterns for emails and bank accounts, and toxicity classification that removes the top 5% most harmful documents per language across nine languages. Second, the **Pretraining Engine** (Section 2) takes the filtered 15T token corpus and trains the Transformer model using the Goldfish objective with AdEMAMix optimizer over five staged data mixtures, producing base model checkpoints at 4k context length. Third, the **Long Context Extension** (Section 2.5) gradually extends the context window from 4k to 65k tokens through four phased training stages with increasing RoPE base values. Fourth, the **Post-Training Pipeline** (Section 4) applies supervised finetuning on 3.8 million instruction examples from 149 languages, followed by preference alignment using QRPO algorithm with constitutional AI principles from the Swiss AI Charter, yielding the final Instruct models.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **Model Architecture and Tokenizer**: We begin with the foundational building blocks—the Transformer variant and tokenization scheme—because these determine what the model can represent and how efficiently it processes multilingual text.
- **Training Objective and Optimizer**: Next we explain Goldfish Loss and AdEMAMix, as these are the novel algorithmic contributions that distinguish Apertus from prior work and directly address memorization and convergence speed.
- **Pretraining Data Pipeline**: We then detail the five-stage curriculum and compliance filtering, since data quality and composition fundamentally shape model capabilities and legal standing.
- **Long Context Extension**: We cover the phased context expansion separately because it requires distinct hyperparameters and data mixtures from main pretraining.
- **Post-Training and Alignment**: Finally we explain SFT and QRPO-based preference alignment, as these transform the base model into a safe, instruction-following system aligned with constitutional values.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This section provides a complete technical exposition of the Apertus system, moving from architectural foundations through training methodology to post-training alignment.

#### 3.4.1 Model Architecture

The Apertus models use a **dense decoder-only Transformer architecture**, meaning every parameter is active during every forward pass (unlike Mixture-of-Experts models where only subsets activate), and the model processes text autoregressively from left to right. The architecture adapts across two scales as shown in Table 1: the 8B model has 32 layers with 4096-dimensional hidden states and 32 query heads paired with 8 key-value heads, while the 70B model has 80 layers with 8192-dimensional hidden states and 64 query heads paired with 8 key-value heads. Both models support 65,536 token contexts after extension and use the **xIELU activation function** in their feedforward networks.

Several architectural modifications distinguish Apertus from standard Llama-style Transformers. First, **all bias terms are removed** from the architecture following Chowdhery et al. (2022), which reduces parameter count without sacrificing performance. Second, **Pre-Normalization** is applied before each residual connection rather than after, because Section 2.1 notes this provides better training stability than post-normalization (Xiong et al., 2020). Third, **RMSNorm** replaces LayerNorm, offering equivalent performance with improved computational efficiency by omitting the mean-centering operation. Fourth, **Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE)** encode position information with a base value of Θ = 500,000 during main pretraining, which is later extended to Θ = 12,000,000 for 64k context support (Section 2.5). Fifth, **Grouped-Query Attention (GQA)** uses fewer key-value heads than query heads (8 KV heads for both models) to improve inference efficiency without compromising performance, as established by Ainslie et al. (2023). Sixth, **input and output embedding weights are untied**, meaning the model learns separate representations for tokens when they appear as input versus when they are predicted as output, which improves performance at the cost of additional memory. Seventh, **QK-Norm** normalizes the query and key vectors in attention layers to prevent excessively large attention logits that could destabilize training (Henry et al., 2020; Dehghani et al., 2023).

The **xIELU activation function** is a novel contribution defined mathematically as:

$$
\text{xIELU}(x) :=
\begin{cases}
\alpha_p \sqrt{x^2 + 0.5x} & \text{if } x > 0, \\
\alpha_n (e^x - 1) - \alpha_n x + 0.5x & \text{if } x \leq 0.
\end{cases}
$$

where αp and αn are trainable scalars learned per layer. This function extends Squared ReLU to handle negative inputs smoothly, and Section 2.4 ablation studies show it individually reduces training loss from 2.037 to 1.997 at 1.5B scale compared to SwiGLU. The activation is applied in all MLP sublayers throughout the network.

Two special tokens structure document boundaries: **BoD (<s>)** is prepended to every document and **EoD (</s>)** is appended, with loss masked out on EoD tokens so they do not contribute to gradient updates. Having fixed tokens at context beginnings improves model quality and training stability by serving as attention sinks that can store global knowledge (Raffel et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2024). Additionally, **cross-document attention is prevented** through attention masks, ensuring tokens cannot attend to tokens from different documents within the same context window, which maintains document-level coherence.

#### 3.4.2 Tokenizer Selection

The tokenizer is a **byte-level Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) model** that segments documents into subword units, adapting the established v3 tekken tokenizer from Mistral-Nemo-Base-2407. The vocabulary contains exactly 2^17 = 131,072 subwords, with 47 custom special tokens modified to better support code and mathematical data. Section 2.2 explains that tokenizer selection was based on four intrinsic evaluation metrics computed on the FLORES+ development set covering 55 languages: **fertility rate** (average tokens per word, where lower is more efficient), **compression ratio** (raw text length divided by tokenized length, where higher is better), **vocabulary utilization** (fraction of vocabulary actually used when encoding text), and **Gini coefficient** (measuring inequality of tokenization costs across languages, where 0 is perfect equality and 1 is maximum inequality).

Figure 1 presents the comparison results across four tokenizers (Llama-3.1, Mistral-Nemo, Qwen-2.5, and Gemma-2). Mistral-Nemo achieves the **lowest Gini coefficient**, indicating more equitable tokenization costs across languages, which is critical for Apertus's multilingual mission. Mistral-Nemo also matches or outperforms other tokenizers in vocabulary utilization, fertility rate, and compression ratio. The team selected Mistral-Nemo over Gemma-2 despite similar efficiency metrics because Mistral-Nemo uses a smaller vocabulary (128k vs. 256k), making it more computationally efficient for pretraining without sacrificing performance. This tokenizer choice directly supports the goal of serving 1811 languages without systematically disadvantaging low-resource languages through inefficient tokenization.

#### 3.4.3 Goldfish Loss for Memorization Mitigation

A central innovation in Apertus is the **Goldfish objective**, which replaces standard cross-entropy loss during pretraining to suppress verbatim memorization of training data. Verbatim regurgitation raises copyright and privacy risks, as models might reproduce protected content or personally identifiable information. The Goldfish loss computes the causal language modeling objective on only a subset of tokens based on a binary mask, rather than computing loss on every token.

Mathematically, the Goldfish loss is defined as:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{i=1}^{L} G_i(x_i) \log P_\theta(x_i | x_{<i}),
$$

where $L$ is the sequence length, $x_i$ is the $i$-th token, $x_{<i}$ is the preceding context, and $G \in \{0, 1\}^L$ is a binary mask randomly sampled for each batch during training. When $G_i = 0$, the token is masked and does not contribute to the loss; when $G_i = 1$, the token is trained normally. This selective masking prevents the model from learning exact token-to-context mappings that enable verbatim recall.

Section 2.3 and Appendix F detail the implementation: through calibration by Xu (2025), the optimal configuration uses a **2% token masking rate** (achieved with $k = 50$, meaning 1 in 50 tokens is masked on average) and a **50-token context window for hashing** ($h = 50$). The hashing mechanism determines which tokens to mask based on the preceding context, ensuring that the same text appearing in different contexts receives different masking patterns. For efficiency, token masking is front-loaded during data loading rather than computed dynamically during pretraining.

Table F.5 demonstrates that Goldfish Loss preserves downstream performance: the 8B Goldfish model actually outperforms the standard 8B model on nearly all evaluated tasks (e.g., MMLU accuracy improves from 61.88% to 62.43%), suggesting the mitigation does not compromise and may even enhance model utility at scale. Figure F.3 shows that Goldfish Loss successfully suppresses verbatim recall across 1B, 3B, and 8B model scales, keeping Rouge-L scores at baseline levels (~0.18) regardless of exposure frequency (up to 128 repetitions) or prefix length (up to 5,000 tokens). Section 5.4.1 confirms this holds at 70B scale as well, with neither Apertus-8B nor Apertus-70B exhibiting memorization across any tested exposure frequency.

However, Section 5.4.2 identifies a key limitation: Goldfish Loss's deterministic hashing is fragile to near-duplicates. When training data contains multiple slightly varied versions of the same text (e.g., different formatting of public domain books), the hash changes and tokens masked in one version may be revealed in another. This explains why canonical works like Shakespeare's plays and the US Constitution—which appear repeatedly in the 15T corpus with varying formatting—account for all 22 sequences with Rouge-L ≥ 0.7 among the 10,672 Gutenberg probes tested.

#### 3.4.4 AdEMAMix Optimizer and Learning Rate Schedule

Apertus trains using the **AdEMAMix optimizer** (Pagliardini et al., 2025), which is a first for an LLM at this scale. AdEMAMix improves upon Adam by adding a long-term exponential moving average (EMA) in the form of an additional momentum vector, which better leverages old gradients for faster convergence especially in long training runs. Section 2.3 notes that optimizer benchmarking results demonstrate AdEMAMix consistently scales more favorably with model size, training duration, and batch size than other widely used alternatives (Semenov et al., 2025).

The optimizer hyperparameters are set as follows: β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999, β3 = 0.9999, α = 8.0, with both tβ3 and tα set to the total number of training steps. Section C explains that β2 and β3 are set higher than typical values (many prior settings use β2 = 0.95) because Semenov et al. (2025) shows higher values reduce variance in gradient estimates and improve stability when training spans millions of iterations. The α and β3 parameters require warmup for stable training, set to 100,000 steps (before the first checkpoint of the learning rate schedule), after which they remain unchanged.

The **learning rate schedule** follows the **Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD)** pattern (Hu et al., 2024; Zhai et al., 2022), which allows for continual training since the full training duration does not have to be specified in advance. The learning rate warmup starts from 0.1 times the peak learning rate and increases linearly for 16.8 billion tokens. For the final annealing (cooldown), Apertus uses a **negative square root shape** (denoted 1-sqrt), which Section 2.3 states reliably outperforms standard linear decay by balancing loss landscape exploration (Hägele et al., 2024; Dremov et al., 2025).

Table 2 specifies the peak learning rates: 1.1×10^-4 for the 8B model and 1.0×10^-5 for the 70B model. The batch sizes double during training: the 8B model starts at 4.2 million tokens and doubles to 8.4 million tokens after 8 trillion consumed tokens; the 70B model starts at 8.4 million tokens and doubles to 16.8 million tokens after 4.4 trillion consumed tokens. Section 2.3 explains that increasing batch size in later stages is beneficial (similar to a learning rate decrease) and increases hardware efficiency, allowing models to perform better under the same FLOP budget (Smith et al., 2018; McCandlish et al., 2018).

**Gradient clipping** is set to 0.1, which Section 2.6 notes is applied at almost every step due to AdEMAMix's sensitivity to gradient norm clipping (the added momentum keeps a much longer history of gradient values). While the team did not notice downstream influence from such aggressive clipping, they flag it as an interesting question for future understanding.

#### 3.4.5 Pretraining Data Pipeline and Curriculum

The pretraining corpus contains **15 trillion tokens** from **1811 languages** (1868 language-script pairs), with approximately **40% allocated to non-English content**. Section 3 describes a carefully designed pipeline guided by three principles: reproducibility (all preprocessing steps documented and code released), multilinguality (1811 languages represented), and compliance (only permissively-licensed content after filtering).

The data filtering pipeline (Figure 6) applies three compliance filters sequentially. First, **robots.txt with Hindsight** (Section 3.1.1) retroactively applies January 2025 opt-out preferences to all historical web snapshots from 2013–2024. The team ranked URL domains by volume, retrieved robots.txt files for the top one million English and one million non-English domains, and removed any content blocked by AI-specific user agents (including GPTBot, CCBot, ClaudeBot, Google-Extended, and others listed in Appendix B). This filtering results in an estimated token loss of approximately **8% in English data** and **4% in multilingual data**. Second, **PII removal** (Section 3.1.2) uses regular expressions to detect and anonymize email addresses, IP addresses, and IBAN bank account numbers, replacing them with markers like `<email-pii>`. Third, **toxicity filtering** (Section 3.1.3) trains language-specific binary classifiers for nine languages (English, Chinese, French, German, Italian, Dutch, Polish, Spanish, Portuguese) using annotated datasets from PleIAs and SWSR, then filters the top 5% most toxic documents per language. Figure 4 shows the toxicity score distributions across these languages, with Portuguese showing the highest mean toxicity (0.399) and Polish the lowest (0.159).

The pretraining curriculum divides the 15T tokens into **five stages** (Section 3.3), each focusing on different capabilities:

- **Stage 1 (0T–5T tokens)**: Builds foundation in natural language modeling with core math and code concepts. Uses FineWeb-Edu Score-2 (larger subset), FineWeb-2-HQ (33% highest quality), FineWeb-2 for other languages, FineMath CommonCrawl subset, and StarCoder data. Also includes Gutenberg-V1 and poison canary datasets for memorization analysis.

- **Stage 2 (5T–9T tokens)**: Expands diversity and quality of English data. Uses FineWeb-HQ (33% highest quality), FineWeb-Edu Score-3 (smaller, higher quality), and maintains multilingual/math/code mixture from Stage 1. Only used in the 70B run; the 8B model skips directly from Stage 1 to Stage 3.

- **Stage 3 (9T–12T tokens)**: Increases math ratio by adding InfiMM-WebMath subsets and LLM360-MegaMath web to the Stage 2 mixture. Includes Gutenberg-V2 canary data.

- **Stage 4 (12T–13.5T tokens)**: Further improves data quality and increases math/code content. Uses DCLM-Edu dataset, FineWeb-2-HQ (10% highest quality), and replaces LLM360-MegaMath web with LLM360-MegaMath web-pro.

- **Stage 5 (13.5T–15T tokens)**: Final cooldown stage refines quality by incorporating CommonPile/Stack v2 Edu, StarCoder datasets scored at 2+ (sampled twice for scores above 3), Clean Wikipedia, translation parallel data (Europarl and ParaDocs), and instruction/task data (Flan and EuroBlocks).

Table 6 provides exact token counts per dataset per stage. For example, Stage 1 uses 4,815 billion tokens from FineWeb-Edu Score-2, 3,557 billion from FineWeb-2-HQ/ FineWeb-2, 235 billion from StarCoder, 32 billion from FineMath, and 2 billion from Gutenberg V1 and poison data.

Section 3.3 describes **cooldown experiments** used to guide mixture selection for Stages 2–5. The team ran ablations on 1.5B model checkpoints, testing candidate datasets with a 70/30 setup (70% Stage 1 data plus 30% of the dataset being tested). Table 7 shows results: DCLM-edu gave the largest performance gain (Full Macro Accuracy 0.45383 vs. baseline 0.44738), while replacing FineWeb-Edu with FineWeb-HQ-33 consistently improved results. Because DCLM-edu is limited in size, the team adopted a phased approach: Stages 2–3 used FW-HQ with FineWeb-Edu Score-3, then fully switched to DCLM-edu in Stages 4–5 once large-scale availability was secured.

#### 3.4.6 Long Context Extension

After main pretraining at 4,096 token context, Apertus undergoes **long context extension** to support sequences up to 65,536 tokens (Section 2.5). The extension uses multiple phases characterized by progressively increasing context lengths, allowing the model to adapt smoothly without instability from sudden drastic increases.

Table 5 details the hyperparameters for each phase. For the 8B model, context extends through 8k, 16k, 32k, and 64k stages, with RoPE Θ increasing from 1M to 12M. The global batch size remains constant at 8M tokens throughout, and learning rate is set to 1.1×10^-5 (10% of peak pretraining LR) with a 1.2 billion token warmup at each stage. For the 70B model, the same context stages apply with 16M global batch size and 1.0×10^-6 learning rate.

The **data mixture for long context training** (Section 3.4) comprises three components with approximate ratios: 70% Pretraining Stage 5 data (ensuring continuity with cooldown distribution), 20% FineWeb-Long (documents exceeding 4k tokens from FineWeb-HQ top 10% and FineWeb-2-HQ top 10%, bucketed into length ranges 4k–8k, 8k–16k, 16k–32k, 32k–64k, and >64k), and 10% Institutional Books 1.0 (28.7B tokens of public-domain books published after 1900, filtered for quality and cleaned of non-content artifacts). Table 8 provides exact token counts per phase: for the 64k phase, the mixture includes 20.74B tokens from Stage 5, 5.58B from FineWeb-Long, and 2.96B from Institutional Books.

Section 2.5 explains that **inter-node context parallelism** is enabled along with intra-node tensor parallelism to keep memory consumption within device limits. The learning rate is set to the final value from the pretraining cooldown period to ensure stability, and additional warmup is employed at the beginning of each new phase.

#### 3.4.7 Supervised Finetuning

Post-training transforms the pretrained models into instruction-following systems through **supervised finetuning (SFT)** using a curated mixture of approximately **3.8 million examples** (Section 4.1.3, Table 12). The mixture balances general instruction-following, mathematical reasoning, code generation, and multilingual capabilities across six categories:

- **Foundation Instruction Data (529K examples, 9.56%)**: High-quality instruction datasets from OLMo2 and Tulu3, including WildChat (299K), SciRiff (30K), and TableGPT (25K). Mathematical datasets undergo post-processing to remove `\boxed{}` formatting from assistant responses.

- **Mathematical and Reasoning (771K examples, 11.60%)**: Filtered personas-based math problems from Tulu3 (125K), OpenMath GSM8K variants (50K), Llama-Nemotron mathematical reasoning (200K), and executable Python code extracted from NuminaMath solutions (63K) to enable tool-augmented problem solving.

- **Code and Technical (378K examples, 9.02%)**: Llama-Nemotron code examples (200K), function-calling datasets from xlam (60K) and Glaive (113K), and APIGen examples (5K).

- **Multilingual and Cultural (1.4M examples, 34.22%)**: SmolTalk2 conversational data (1.3M examples across 8 languages), EuroBlocks synthetic multilingual instructions (157K), and s1k 42 langs dataset (1K unique samples with non-English prompts/responses but English reasoning chains to encourage cross-lingual transfer).

- **Structured Knowledge (545K examples, 13.02%)**: The Tome dataset provides financial and web-based instruction-following examples.

- **Low-Resource and Regional Languages (944K examples, 22.54%)**: Multilingual Wikipedia Q&A (884K), Romansh language data (46K covering six written varieties), Swiss-German dialect instructions (6K), African language instructions (7K), and 226 constitutional alignment examples following the Swiss AI Charter.

Section 4.2 specifies SFT hyperparameters: global batch size of 512 or 1,024, learning rates of 5×10^-6 and 2×10^-6 respectively, linear decay schedule, maximum sequence length of 4,096 tokens, and AdEMAMix optimizer with β3 = 0.99, α = 8.0.

The **chat template format** (Section 4.2.1) extends common practice by using special tokens to delineate user, system, assistant, and a novel **developer message**. The developer message defines available tools, parameters, and contextual configurations, making the format flexible for both simple dialogue and complex multi-step agentic workflows.

#### 3.4.8 Preference Alignment with QRPO

After SFT, Apertus undergoes **preference alignment** using the **Quantile Reward Policy Optimization (QRPO)** algorithm (Matrenok et al., 2025), which optimizes an absolute reward signal while retaining the advantages of direct alignment methods (training stability, offline learning capability, reduced computational demands compared to online RL).

Section 4.3 explains that QRPO minimizes the following loss:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{QRPO}} = \mathbb{E}_{x,y} \left[ \left( R_q(x, y) - \beta_{\text{KL}} \log Z_q(x) - \beta_{\text{KL}} \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y | x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y | x)} \right)^2 \right],
$$

where $R_q(x, y)$ is the **quantile reward** representing the percentile rank of a candidate completion $y$ among a set of reference completions sampled from a reference policy $\pi_{\text{ref}}$, and $Z_q(x)$ is the corresponding partition function:

$$
R_q(x, y) = \text{Pr}_{y' \sim \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot|x)} \{ R(x, y') \leq R(x, y) \},
$$

$$
Z_q(x) = \beta_{\text{KL}} (\exp(1/\beta_{\text{KL}}) - 1).
$$

For each prompt $x_i$, the team generates $n = 10$ reference completions $y_{i,j} \sim \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot | x_i)$, which are annotated with rewards to construct the reference reward set. The quantile reward is then computed as the empirical cumulative distribution function of the reward over this reference set.

Apertus adopts a **length-normalized variant of QRPO**, inspired by Tülu 3, where the KL regularization coefficient βKL is normalized by the completion length |y|:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{QRPO-norm}} = \mathbb{E}_{x,y} \left[ \left( R_q(x, y) - \frac{\beta_{\text{KL}}}{|y|} \log Z_{q\text{-norm}}(x) - \frac{\beta_{\text{KL}}}{|y|} \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y | x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y | x)} \right)^2 \right].
$$

Section 4.3 reports that length normalization consistently improves downstream performance for both QRPO and DPO in ablation studies, and QRPO outperforms DPO in the 70B model (while achieving similar results for 8B). The final hyperparameters are: βKL = 5 with length normalization (yielding average βKL/|y| ≈ 0.03), AdEMAMix optimizer with β3 = 0.99, α = 8.0, and learning rates of 5×10^-7 for 8B and 1×10^-7 for 70B.

The alignment pipeline adapts two regimes (Section 4.3): first, **standard preference alignment** (Section 4.3.1) uses the Skywork-Reward-V2-Llama-3.1-8B reward model (trained on 26M preference pairs) to score non-controversial prompts; second, **constitutional alignment for controversial topics** (Section 4.3.2) uses an LLM-as-judge (Qwen3-32B) to score completions by their adherence to the **Swiss AI Charter**.

The **Swiss AI Charter** (Appendix O) consists of 11 articles summarizing principles for AI behavior informed by Swiss constitutional values (neutrality, consensus-building, federalism, multilingualism, respect for cultural diversity). Section 4.3.2 describes a survey of 163 Swiss residents showing 97.3% average agreement with the charter principles, with Article 4 (Preventing Harm) receiving 91.3% "always/definitely yes" responses. For controversial prompts, the LLM-as-judge rates completions on a 1–9 scale based on charter adherence, with pairwise ranking of top 5 scoring responses used for optimization.

#### 3.4.9 Infrastructure and Scaling

Training was enabled by the **Alps supercomputer** at the Swiss National Supercomputing Centre (CSCS), an HPE Cray EX system with 10,752 NVIDIA Grace-Hopper (GH200) GPUs interconnected by Slingshot-11 network with 200Gb/s injection bandwidth per GPU (Section 6.1). The ML platform leveraged a dedicated vCluster named Clariden with approximately 1,500 nodes (6,000 GPUs), though the 70B model training scaled up to **4,096 GPUs** at peak.

Section 6.2 estimates that training the 70B model for 15T tokens took **6.74 × 10^24 FLOPs** (details in Appendix E), consuming around **6 million GPU hours**. Once a production environment is set up, the team estimates the model can be trained in approximately **90 days on 4096 GPUs**, with an estimated **5 GWh power usage** for compute (assuming 560W power usage per Grace-Hopper module, below the 660W power limit). CSCS is almost carbon neutral, relying entirely on hydropower with sustainable cooling using water from Lake Lugano in a closed cycle.

Section 6.3 describes engineering challenges and solutions. **Systems-level fixes** included aligning NCCL plugin and libfabric versions to resolve communication inconsistencies, disabling access-counter-based page migration in GPU drivers to eliminate interrupt storms, and applying Linux kernel hot patches to fix race conditions causing node crashes. **Container robustness** was improved by moving Triton's JIT kernel caches from distributed filesystem to in-memory storage to eliminate race conditions. **Checkpointing strategies** placed datasets and caches on high-IOPS SSD storage while storing checkpoint files on high-capacity HDDs, with checkpointing every 250 iterations determined by the Young/Daly formula to balance overhead against mean time between failures.

Figure 13 shows **scaling efficiency**: strong scaling parallel efficiency reaches **80% at 4096 GPUs** with global batch size held constant at 16.8M tokens, achieving 723 tokens per second per GPU at this scale. Weak scaling efficiency is also characterized with global batch size varying proportionally to GPU count from 0.13M to 16.8M tokens.

#### 3.4.10 Design Choices and Trade-offs

Throughout the Apertus development, the team made several critical design choices that balance performance, compliance, and transparency:

- **Goldfish Loss vs. Standard Cross-Entropy**: The team chose Goldfish Loss despite its complexity because Section 5.4 demonstrates it effectively suppresses verbatim memorization even at 70B scale and after 128 exposures, while Table F.5 shows it preserves or even improves downstream task performance. The trade-off is slightly more complex data preprocessing (front-loaded masking) but significant reduction in copyright and privacy risk.

- **AdEMAMix vs. AdamW**: Section 2.4 ablations show AdEMAMix individually reduces loss from 2.037 to 2.002 at 1.5B scale, and Section 2.3 notes it scales more favorably with model size and training duration. The trade-off is additional hyperparameters (β3, α) requiring careful tuning and warmup.

- **Retroactive robots.txt vs. Crawl-Time Only**: Section 3.1.1 quantifies the cost: 8% English token loss and 4% multilingual token loss. The team accepted this reduction in data volume because it ensures compliance with emerging regulations like the EU AI Act and respects content owner rights, which is essential for enterprise and institutional adoption.

- **40% Non-English Data vs. English-Optimized**: Allocating 40% of pretraining to non-English content (compared to ~10% or less in most models) reduces English-only performance slightly but enables service to 1811 languages. Section 5.1 shows Apertus-70B achieves the highest score among all evaluated models on the multilingual XCOPA benchmark, validating this choice.

- **QRPO vs. DPO vs. PPO**: Section 4.3 explains QRPO was chosen because it optimizes absolute reward signals (more informative than DPO's relative preferences) while retaining offline training stability (unlike PPO's online RL which requires careful hyperparameter tuning). Ablation studies show QRPO outperforms DPO at 70B scale.

- **70B Scale vs. Smaller**: The team targeted 70B parameters because this is widely considered the minimum scale for complex reasoning rivaling proprietary systems. Section 5.2 shows the performance gap between 8B and 70B models is smaller than typically observed in other model families, suggesting efficient scaling.

Each choice reflects the core philosophy: **compliance and openness are not bottlenecks to performance** when engineered carefully. The ablation studies in Section 2.4 demonstrate that merging all successful changes (xIELU, QK-Norm, AdEMAMix, WSD schedule, Goldfish Loss) into a 3B model achieves the same training loss as baseline with 30–40% fewer tokens, validating the integrated approach.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

While the technical specifications of Apertus establish *how* the models were built, the empirical results reveal deeper insights about the mechanics of large-scale training, compliance, and alignment. These findings distinguish Apertus from prior work not just in scale, but in the scientific understanding of what is possible when transparency and safety are prioritized. Below, we analyze four key innovations that offer fundamental advances rather than incremental improvements.

### 4.1 Memorization Dynamics are Positionally Fragile (and Mitigatable)

Prior research on LLM memorization suggested that verbatim recall is heavily dependent on **positional fragility**: models memorize tokens at the beginning of a context window much more strongly than those further away, with recall decaying as the prefix offset increases (Xu et al., 2025). This implied that memorization was an inherent structural property of Transformer attention mechanisms that could only be managed, not eliminated.

Apertus challenges this assumption through the application of **Goldfish Loss** at scale. The evaluation in Section 5.4.1 reveals a critical scientific insight: **Goldfish Loss breaks the dependency on positional offset**.

*   **Evidence:** Figure 9 compares memorization heatmaps for Gutenberg sequences injected at different training stages. In standard models, recall drops sharply as the offset (tokens skipped before the prefix) increases. In Apertus, recall fluctuates within a narrow range regardless of offset.
*   **Significance:** This suggests that verbatim memorization is not an unavoidable consequence of next-token prediction, but rather a result of consistent token-to-context mappings. By randomly masking 2% of tokens during training (Section 2.3), Apertus prevents the model from forming the continuous long-range anchors required for verbatim recall.
*   **Innovation Level:** **Fundamental.** This is not just a safety filter; it is a modification of the learning objective that alters the model's memory formation dynamics. The result (Table F.5) shows this is achieved without performance degradation—in fact, the 8B Goldfish model outperforms the standard 8B model on MMLU (62.43% vs. 61.88%), contradicting the common assumption that safety mitigations necessarily reduce capability.

### 4.2 The Quantifiable Cost of Data Compliance

A major barrier to compliant AI development has been the uncertainty surrounding the "cost" of respecting copyright and opt-out mechanisms. Industry practice often assumes that filtering for `robots.txt` compliance or removing personally identifiable information (PII) would render datasets too small to train competitive models.

Apertus provides the first empirical quantification of this trade-off at the 70B scale.

*   **Evidence:** Section 3.1.1 reports that retroactive `robots.txt` filtering resulted in an estimated token loss of **approximately 8% in English data** and **4% in multilingual data**. This filtering was applied to the top one million domains per language category, respecting opt-outs from major AI crawlers (e.g., `GPTBot`, `CCBot`).
*   **Significance:** This metric establishes a **baseline for the "price of ethics"** in LLM training. It demonstrates that high-performance models can be trained on strictly compliant data with a manageable reduction in corpus size. Prior to this work, organizations had to guess whether compliance was feasible; Apertus proves it is viable with a known overhead.
*   **Innovation Level:** **Systemic.** While the filtering mechanism itself (checking `robots.txt`) is not new, applying it *retroactively* to historical crawls (Section 3.1.1) and quantifying the impact at this scale is a novel contribution to data governance. It shifts compliance from a legal abstraction to an engineering constraint with bounded costs.

### 4.3 Tokenizer Fairness as a Measurable Metric

Multilingual models often claim to support many languages, but few address the **computational inequity** inherent in tokenization. Some tokenizers require significantly more tokens to represent certain languages (e.g., Chinese or low-resource scripts) compared to English, increasing inference costs and latency for non-English users.

Apertus introduces **tokenizer fairness** as a first-class selection criterion using the **Gini coefficient**.

*   **Evidence:** Section 2.2 and Figure 1 detail the selection process. The team evaluated four tokenizers (Llama-3.1, Mistral-Nemo, Qwen-2.5, Gemma-2) using four metrics, including the Gini coefficient, which measures the inequality of tokenization costs across languages (where 0 is perfect equality). Mistral-Nemo was selected specifically because it achieved the **lowest Gini coefficient**, indicating more equitable costs across languages.
*   **Significance:** This moves multilingual support beyond simple data volume (e.g., "we trained on 100 languages") to **structural equity**. By ensuring that no language is systematically disadvantaged by inefficient tokenization, Apertus reduces the operational bias against low-resource languages during inference. This is critical for the model's goal of serving 1811 languages (Section 1) without imposing higher computational taxes on non-English speakers.
*   **Innovation Level:** **Methodological.** Using economic inequality metrics (Gini) to evaluate NLP tokenizers is a novel cross-disciplinary application. It provides a reproducible metric for future models to claim "multilingual fairness" rather than just "multilingual coverage."

### 4.4 Constitutional Alignment via Absolute Rewards

Standard alignment techniques like Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) or Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) typically rely on relative preferences (e.g., "Response A is better than Response B"). However, aligning models to abstract values (e.g., "neutrality," "consensus-building") is difficult when human annotators disagree on what those values mean in practice.

Apertus demonstrates that **constitutional values can be encoded via absolute reward signals** using the **QRPO algorithm** and an **LLM-as-Judge**.

*   **Evidence:** Section 4.3.2 describes the alignment pipeline for controversial topics. Instead of human preferences, the team used the **Swiss AI Charter** (Appendix O)—a codified set of 11 principles—as the ground truth. An LLM-as-Judge (Qwen3-32B) scored completions on a 1–9 scale based on adherence to the Charter. These absolute scores were then optimized using **QRPO** (Section 4.3), which minimizes the difference between the model's log-probabilities and the quantile rank of the reward.
*   **Significance:** This approach allows for **Sovereign AI alignment**. Organizations can define their own "constitutions" (e.g., corporate values, national laws) and align models to them without collecting massive new human preference datasets. The survey results (Table 13) show 97.3% agreement among Swiss residents on these principles, validating that codified values can reflect shared societal norms.
*   **Innovation Level:** **Fundamental.** This shifts alignment from **preference learning** (what humans like) to **principle adherence** (what values the model should uphold). By combining QRPO's ability to handle absolute rewards with a written charter, Apertus provides a blueprint for customizable, value-specific alignment that is more transparent than black-box human feedback loops.

### 4.5 Summary of Innovations

The table below summarizes how these insights differentiate Apertus from standard practices:

| Innovation | Standard Practice | Apertus Approach | Key Insight |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Memorization** | Accept verbatim recall as inevitable; filter outputs post-hoc. | **Goldfish Loss** during pretraining. | Memorization is positionally fragile and can be structurally mitigated without performance loss (Figure 9). |
| **Compliance** | Respect `robots.txt` only at crawl time; ignore retroactive opt-outs. | **Retroactive Filtering** (Hindsight). | The cost of full compliance is quantifiable (~8% English token loss) and manageable (Section 3.1.1). |
| **Multilingualism** | Optimize for English efficiency; treat other languages as secondary. | **Gini-Coefficient Tokenizer** Selection. | Tokenizer fairness is measurable and critical for equitable inference costs across 1811 languages (Figure 1). |
| **Alignment** | Optimize for helpfulness/harmlessness via human preferences (RLHF/DPO). | **Constitutional QRPO** via Charter. | Abstract values can be aligned via codified principles and LLM judges, enabling Sovereign AI (Section 4.3.2). |

These innovations collectively demonstrate that **transparency and safety are not bottlenecks to performance** when engineered as core components of the training pipeline rather than post-hoc additions. Apertus provides the empirical evidence that fully open, compliant, and multilingual models can compete with proprietary systems at the 70B scale.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section evaluates whether Apertus delivers on its core claims: that fully open, compliant models can match open-weight counterparts in performance while providing superior multilingual coverage and memorization mitigation. We analyze the evaluation methodology, quantitative results across multiple capability dimensions, and critical ablation studies that validate design choices.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

Apertus employs a **comprehensive, multi-phase evaluation strategy** spanning pretraining, post-training, and specialized capability assessments. The methodology is distinguished by three features:

**Benchmark Diversity.** The evaluation suite covers 94 languages across seven capability categories (Table 22): knowledge recall (MMLU, Global-MMLU, TruthfulQA), instruction following (IFEval, Multi-IFEval), commonsense reasoning (HellaSwag, XCOPA), coding (HumanEval, MBPP), mathematics (GSM8K, MATH, MathQA), general reasoning (BBH, ARC, DROP, ACPBench), and cultural knowledge (INCLUDE, CulturalBench, SwitzerlandQA). This represents one of the most linguistically diverse evaluations of an LLM to date.

**Baseline Categorization.** Section 5 distinguishes between two baseline categories (Table 16):
- **Fully Open Models:** Release weights, training data, code, and recipes (e.g., OLMo-2, EuroLLM, SmolLM3)
- **Open-Weight Models:** Release only weights, keeping data and code proprietary (e.g., Llama-3.1, Qwen2.5, Gemma-3)

This distinction is critical because Apertus claims to match open-weight performance while maintaining full openness.

**Evaluation Modes.** Pretraining evaluation uses **probabilistic scoring** (constraining answer options to probability distributions) to capture subtle improvements in internal representations during early training stages (Section 5.1). Post-training evaluation shifts to **open-generation mode** to assess instruction-following and generative capabilities (Section 5.2). All evaluations use the `lm-evaluation-harness` framework with default configurations unless specified.

**Held-Out Test Suite.** Table 21 reports results on benchmarks that were **held out during model development** and not used for hyperparameter tuning. This includes AGIeval, ARC Challenge (Chat and Multilingual), GPQA Main, GSM8K Platinum, and MLogiQA. This separation prevents overfitting to evaluation metrics—a common criticism of LLM development.

### 5.2 Pretraining Evaluation Results

Pretraining evaluation assesses capabilities acquired during the 15T token pretraining phase, focusing on general language understanding and factual knowledge acquisition.

**General Language Understanding (Table 14).** Apertus-70B achieves an **average score of 67.5%** across six benchmarks (ARC, HellaSwag, WinoGrande, XNLI, XCOPA, PIQA). This is competitive with OLMo2-32B (67.7%) despite OLMo2 having a more English-optimized data mixture. Notably, Apertus-70B achieves the **highest XCOPA score (69.8%)** among all evaluated models, including Llama-3.1-70B (66.7%) and Qwen2.5-72B (68.2%). This validates the 40% non-English data allocation claim from Section 1.

Apertus-8B scores 65.8% average, outperforming OLMo2-7B (64.0%) and EuroLLM-9B (62.8%) despite having fewer parameters than some competitors. This suggests efficient scaling from the architectural improvements (xIELU, QK-Norm, AdEMAMix) described in Section 3.4.1.

**Factual Knowledge Acquisition (Table 15).** On knowledge benchmarks, Apertus-70B achieves **58.9% average** across MMLU, Global-MMLU, INCLUDE V1/V2, CulturalBench, BLEND, and SwitzerlandQA. This trails OLMo2-32B (62.0%) and Llama-3.1-70B (66.7%), but the gap is smaller than the parameter difference would suggest.

The **multilingual knowledge benchmarks** show Apertus's strength:
- **INCLUDE V1:** Apertus-70B scores 57.0%, surpassing OLMo2-32B (50.6%) and Llama-3.1-70B (64.1%)
- **INCLUDE V2:** Apertus-70B scores 38.5%, comparable to OLMo2-32B (37.5%)
- **CulturalBench:** Apertus-70B scores 58.1%, trailing Llama-3.1-70B (62.3%) but outperforming OLMo2-32B (74.8%)

The **SwitzerlandQA** benchmark (custom-developed for this project, Section K) tests regional knowledge across five languages (German, French, Italian, Romansh, English). Apertus-8B scores 62.1% and Apertus-70B scores 60.2%, demonstrating that regional knowledge can be embedded without sacrificing general capabilities.

**Training Dynamics (Figure 7).** The loss curves show consistent improvement across all four evaluation categories (Global, English, EU, Swiss) throughout the 15T token training. Notably, the **multilingual evaluation curves (Global, EU, Swiss)** remain strong relative to English, confirming that the 40% non-English allocation does not degrade English performance disproportionately. The 70B model shows stable gradient norms even across data mixture changes and batch size doubling (Section 2.6), indicating robust training stability.

### 5.5 Verbatim Memorization Analysis

Section 5.4 evaluates whether Goldfish Loss successfully mitigates verbatim memorization at scale—a core claim of the paper.

**Memorization Patterns (Figure 8).** Both Apertus-8B and Apertus-70B maintain **baseline memorization levels (Rouge-L ≈ 0.18)** across all tested exposure frequencies (1–128 repetitions) and prefix lengths (50–5,000 tokens). This is comparable to unrelated Gutenberg texts, indicating no significant verbatim recall.

For comparison, Figure F.3 shows standard LLaMA models (without Goldfish Loss) exhibit Rouge-L scores exceeding 0.7 at high exposure frequencies (128 repetitions) and long prefix lengths (5,000 tokens). This contrast demonstrates Goldfish Loss's effectiveness.

**Decoding Strategy Robustness (Table 25).** The evaluation tests both greedy decoding and nucleus sampling (temperature=1.0, top-p=0.9) to rule out artifacts from decoding strategy.

Under **greedy decoding**, Apertus-70B shows text degeneration (TTR ≈ 0.22–0.31, increasing with exposure frequency), but Rouge-L and LCCS remain at baseline (~0.18 and ~0.01 respectively). Under **nucleus sampling**, TTR increases to ≈ 0.500 (close to ground truth 0.538), while Rouge-L and LCCS remain at baseline.

This confirms that **memorization mitigation is robust across decoding strategies** and not an artifact of greedy decoding producing low-diversity outputs.

**Positional Fragility Breaking (Figure 9).** Prior work (Xu et al., 2025) established that memorization decays sharply as prefix offset increases—tokens at the beginning of context windows are memorized more strongly. Figure 9 shows that for Apertus, recall **fluctuates within a narrow range** across offsets (0–2048 tokens) rather than exhibiting sharp decay.

The rightmost plot (V2 - V1) is predominantly blue, indicating sequences from the earlier training stage (V1, 0–9T tokens) were more strongly memorized than later sequences (V2, 9–12T tokens)—a **primacy effect**. However, the magnitude of this effect is small (Rouge-L difference < 0.1 in most cells), and the positional fragility pattern is absent.

**Failure Cases (Section 5.4.2).** Goldfish Loss has a key limitation: **deterministic hashing is fragile to near-duplicates**. When training data contains multiple slightly varied versions of the same text (e.g., different formatting of public domain books), the hash changes and tokens masked in one version may be revealed in another.

This explains why **all 22 sequences with Rouge-L ≥ 0.7** among the 10,672 Gutenberg probes were canonical works (Keats's poems, Shakespeare's plays, US Constitution, Bible) that appear repeatedly in the 15T corpus with varying formatting. Section 5.4.2 identifies two sources of divergence:
1. **Formatting divergence:** Gutenberg sequences follow fixed layout (~21.5 tokens per line), while web versions differ in line-breaking
2. **Tokenizer inconsistency:** Leading whitespace or subword segmentation produces different token IDs

This is a critical caveat: Goldfish Loss mitigates memorization but does not eliminate it entirely when near-duplicates exist in the training corpus.

**False Positives (Figure 10).** High Rouge-L scores can also occur for **structured, low-diversity content** (tables, recipe lists, contents pages) where template learning rather than true verbatim memorization is responsible. These cases have TTR ≤ 0.4 for 500-token suffixes and carry lower copyright/privacy risks than literary passage memorization.

### 5.6 Security and Safety Evaluation

Section 5.5 evaluates safety and security across multiple dimensions, acknowledging the unique challenges of open-weight models (guardrails can be removed via post-training).

**Bias and Toxicity (Table 26).** Apertus-70B-Instruct scores:
- **BBQ (bias):** 67.4% (comparable to EuroLLM-22B-Instruct-Preview at 66.3%, trailing Qwen3-32B at 78.4%)
- **ToxiGen (implicit toxicity):** 70.3% (competitive with fully open models, trailing open-weight models like Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct at 87.4%)
- **HarmBench:** 31.9% (worse than most fully open models like EuroLLM-9B-Instruct at 6.0%, but in line with open-weight models like Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct at 38.1%)
- **RealToxicityPrompts:** 0.2% (excellent, comparable to Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct at 0.0%)

The HarmBench result is notable: Apertus performs worse than most fully open models but comparably to open-weight models. Section 5.5.1 explains this is intentional—the team prioritizes **helpfulness vs. safety balance** and does not pursue jailbreak resistance (which must be delegated to production guardrails).

**Multilingual Safety (Tables 27, 28).** LinguaSafe evaluates safety across 12 languages and 5 harm categories (Crimes, Explicit Content, Fairness, Harm, Privacy). Scores are severity-weighted (lower is better).

Apertus-70B-Instruct ranges from **38.14% to 57.76%** across categories and languages, with Fairness & Justice showing the highest scores (most room for improvement). Apertus-8B-Instruct shows similar patterns, ranging from **40.94% to 63.98%**.

Section 5.5.1 notes that most safety work focuses on English, resulting in poor generalization to other languages. Apertus's multilingual safety evaluation is more comprehensive than prior open models, but the results indicate **significant room for improvement in non-English safety alignment**.

**Qualitative Spot-Testing (Section 5.6).** Manual testing focused on high-risk scenarios: CBRNE (chemical, biological, radiological, nuclear, explosive weapons), Dual Use, Medical Disinformation, Private Person Claims, and Information Operations in Low-Resource Languages.

The team found **no issues warranting release delay**, though potential for improvement was noted. For CBRNE, the jailbroken model returned "segmental instruction with concerning ingredients credible to the general public" but none were "comprehensive and specific enough to be more useful than other rapidly accessible sources." For Medical Misinformation, resilience was observed but "deteriorated for lower-resource languages."

### 5.7 Ablation Studies and Design Validation

Multiple ablation studies validate the architectural and training choices.

**Architecture and Recipe Ablations (Table 3, Figure 2).** At 1.5B scale with 100B tokens, individual modifications show:
- **AdEMAMix optimizer:** Reduces loss from 2.037 to 2.002
- **xIELU activation:** Reduces loss from 2.037 to 1.997
- **WSD schedule with 1-sqrt cooldown:** Reduces loss from 2.037 to 2.033

When merged into a 3B model (Apertus 3B row), all changes combined achieve **1.843 loss vs. 1.906 baseline**—matching baseline training loss with **30–40% fewer tokens** (Figure 2). This validates the integrated architecture approach.

**OLMo2 Comparison (Table 4).** To evaluate beyond internal baselines, Apertus was compared to OLMo2's 1B and 7B models using identical data and hyperparameters for the first 20k steps.

- **1B model:** Apertus matches OLMo2-1B loss (∼2.75 vs. ∼2.84) with **46% fewer training tokens**
- **7B model:** Apertus matches OLMo2-7B loss (∼2.51 vs. ∼2.56) with **30% fewer training tokens**

This demonstrates that Apertus's recipe (xIELU, Goldfish, Pre-Norm, AdEMAMix) achieves comparable performance with significantly less compute.

**Data Filtering Impact (Tables 10, 11).** Section 4.1.2 quantifies the cost of compliance filtering on post-training performance.

On English benchmarks (Table 10), adding license filtering to decontamination reduces average performance by **5.8%** (from 0.443 to 0.417). The largest drop is on **MMLU CoT** (0.513 → 0.253, a 51% decrease), likely because many chain-of-thought datasets have restrictive licenses.

On multilingual benchmarks (Table 11), license filtering reduces average performance by **4.3%** (from 0.511 to 0.489). **MGSM Native CoT** shows the largest relative drop (0.320 → 0.273, -14.7%), while CulturalBench declines only 5.4% and SwitzerlandQA drops just 1.9%.

Section 4.1.2 frames this as "the inherent tension between compliance and model capability, a trade-off we accept as necessary for responsible open-source model development." This is a critical finding: **compliance has a measurable performance cost**, but it is bounded and quantifiable.

**Cooldown Ablations (Table 7).** To guide data mixture selection for Stages 2–5, cooldown experiments on 1.5B models tested candidate datasets with 70/30 mixtures.

- **30% DCLM-edu:** Full Macro Accuracy 0.45383 (vs. baseline 0.44738)
- **30% FW-HQ-10:** Full Macro Accuracy 0.45304
- **Base-FW-HQ-33 + 30% DCLM-edu:** Full Macro Accuracy 0.45380

DCLM-edu gave the largest performance gain, validating its inclusion in Stages 4–5. However, because DCLM-edu is limited in size, the team adopted a phased approach (Section 3.3).

**Goldfish Loss Downstream Performance (Table F.5).** A critical concern is whether memorization mitigation degrades capabilities. Table F.5 shows Goldfish Loss **preserves or improves** downstream performance:

- **8B Goldfish vs. Standard 8B:** MMLU 62.43% vs. 61.88%, PIQA 76.89% vs. 75.97%, HellaSwag 66.61% vs. 65.74%

This contradicts the common assumption that safety mitigations necessarily reduce capability.

**QRPO vs. DPO (Section 4.3).** Ablation studies show length-normalized QRPO and DPO achieve similar results for the 8B model, but **QRPO outperforms DPO for the 70B model**. This validates the choice of QRPO for large-scale alignment.

### 5.8 Critical Assessment of Claims

The experimental results largely support the paper's core claims, with important caveats:

**Claim 1: "Fully open models can match open-weight counterparts at 70B scale."**
- **Supported:** Apertus-70B matches or exceeds OLMo2-32B on most benchmarks despite OLMo2's English optimization. On multilingual benchmarks (XCOPA, INCLUDE), Apertus leads all evaluated models.
- **Caveat:** On math and coding, Apertus trails open-weight models (Llama-3.3-70B, Qwen2.5-72B) by 15–20 percentage points. The paper acknowledges this is due to lack of RLVR training, not fundamental architecture limitations.

**Claim 2: "Goldfish Loss suppresses verbatim memorization without compromising performance."**
- **Strongly Supported:** Figure 8 and Table F.5 show Rouge-L remains at baseline (~0.18) across all exposure frequencies, and downstream task performance is preserved or improved.
- **Caveat:** Section 5.4.2 identifies failure cases with near-duplicates (canonical works with varying formatting), where Rouge-L can exceed 0.7. This is a known limitation of deterministic hashing.

**Claim 3: "Retroactive robots.txt filtering is feasible with bounded cost."**
- **Supported:** Section 3.1.1 quantifies the cost: 8% English token loss, 4% multilingual token loss. This is manageable and does not prevent competitive performance.
- **Caveat:** The performance cost of license filtering in post-training is higher (5.8% average drop, Table 10), particularly on MMLU CoT (51% drop). This suggests compliance costs compound across pipeline stages.

**Claim 4: "40% non-English data enables superior multilingual capabilities."**
- **Strongly Supported:** Apertus leads all fully open models on INCLUDE, CulturalBench, and Romansh translation (Tables 15, 20, 24). XCOPA score (69.8%) is highest among all evaluated models.
- **Caveat:** English-only benchmarks (MMLU, GSM8K) still trail English-optimized open-weight models, reflecting the trade-off.

**Claim 5: "Constitutional alignment via QRPO and Swiss AI Charter is effective."**
- **Partially Supported:** BBQ bias scores (67.4%) are competitive, and survey results (Table 13) show 97.3% agreement on charter principles.
- **Caveat:** Multilingual safety (Tables 27, 28) shows significant variation across languages, with Fairness & Justice scores reaching 57.76%. The LLM-as-judge approach has not been validated across all 149 post-training languages.

### 5.9 Summary of Experimental Findings

The evaluation demonstrates that Apertus achieves its core objectives with measurable trade-offs:

| Dimension | Claim | Evidence | Caveat |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Scale** | 70B fully open model | Matches OLMo2-32B on most benchmarks | Trails Llama-3.3-70B on math/coding |
| **Compliance** | Retroactive robots.txt feasible | 8% English, 4% multilingual token loss | 5.8% post-training performance drop |
| **Memorization** | Goldfish Loss effective | Rouge-L ≈ 0.18 at all exposures | Fails on near-duplicates (22/10,672 probes) |
| **Multilingual** | 40% non-English data | Leads on XCOPA, INCLUDE, Romansh BLEU | English benchmarks trail optimized models |
| **Safety** | Constitutional alignment | 97.3% charter agreement, competitive BBQ | Multilingual safety varies significantly |

The experiments convincingly support the paper's thesis that **transparency and compliance are not bottlenecks to performance** when engineered as core components. However, the results also reveal that **trade-offs are real and quantifiable**: compliance filtering reduces performance, Goldfish Loss has failure modes, and multilingual safety requires further work. These are not weaknesses but honest acknowledgments that enable future improvement.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Apertus demonstrates that fully open, compliant models can compete at scale, the project reveals significant constraints inherent to this design philosophy. The following analysis categorizes these limitations into fundamental assumptions, performance trade-offs, safety boundaries, and engineering constraints. Understanding these boundaries is critical for researchers attempting to replicate or extend this work.

### 6.1 Fundamental Assumptions and Dependencies

The Apertus pipeline relies on several external mechanisms and theoretical assumptions that, if violated, compromise the model's compliance or safety guarantees.

*   **Reliance on `robots.txt` as Consent Proxy:** The data compliance strategy (Section 3.1.1) assumes that website owners use `robots.txt` to express consent regarding AI crawling. However, this mechanism is imperfect:
    *   **Unmarked Content:** Content owners who are unaware of the protocol or lack technical resources to update their files remain included in the dataset. This leaves residual legal risk despite the "retroactive filtering" innovation.
    *   **Accessibility:** The filtering process depends on the domain being reachable at the time of filtering (January 2025). Domains that went offline between the crawl date (2013–2024) and the filtering date could not be verified, potentially retaining data from sources that would have opted out had they remained active.
*   **Goldfish Loss Hashing Stability:** The memorization mitigation strategy (Section 2.3) assumes that identical semantic content will produce identical token hashes. Section 5.4.2 identifies a critical failure mode: **near-duplicates**.
    *   **Mechanism Failure:** If a text (e.g., the US Constitution) appears in the training corpus with slight formatting variations (line breaks, whitespace), the 50-token context hash changes. Tokens masked in one version may be unmasked in another, allowing the model to reconstruct the full text across multiple exposures.
    *   **Evidence:** All 22 sequences with Rouge-L ≥ 0.7 among 10,672 probes were canonical works with multiple variants in the corpus (Section 5.4.2). This suggests Goldfish Loss mitigates but does not eliminate memorization risk for high-frequency public domain texts.
*   **LLM-as-Judge Dependency:** The constitutional alignment for controversial topics (Section 4.3.2) relies on Qwen3-32B to score adherence to the Swiss AI Charter.
    *   **Bias Transfer:** This introduces a dependency on the judge model's own biases and capabilities. If Qwen3-32B misinterprets a charter principle, Apertus will optimize for that misinterpretation.
    *   **Circularity:** Using an opaque model (Qwen) to align an open model (Apertus) on values creates a transparency gap in the alignment pipeline itself.

### 6.2 Performance Trade-offs

Achieving compliance and multilingual equity required sacrificing performance in specific domains. These are not accidental deficits but quantifiable costs of the design choices.

*   **Compliance vs. Capability:** Adhering to strict licensing and decontamination protocols directly reduced performance on certain benchmarks.
    *   **Quantified Cost:** Table 10 shows that adding license filtering to decontamination reduced average English benchmark performance by **5.8%** (from 0.443 to 0.417).
    *   **Specific Impact:** The **MMLU Chain-of-Thought (CoT)** score dropped by **51%** (0.513 → 0.253) because many high-quality CoT datasets have restrictive licenses incompatible with Apertus's openness standards (Section 4.1.2). This indicates that state-of-the-art reasoning capabilities are currently correlated with less permissive data sources.
*   **Multilingualism vs. English Optimization:** Allocating ~40% of pretraining data to non-English languages (Section 1) ensures global coverage but reduces the density of English signal compared to English-optimized models.
    *   **Evidence:** Table 15 shows Apertus-70B scoring **58.9%** on factual knowledge benchmarks, trailing Llama-3.1-70B (**66.7%**) and OLMo2-32B (**62.0%**).
    *   **Interpretation:** While Apertus leads on multilingual benchmarks (e.g., XCOPA), the trade-off is visible in English-centric knowledge tasks. Users prioritizing English-only performance may find open-weight, English-optimized models more capable per parameter.
*   **Math and Coding Deficit:** Apertus trails state-of-the-art open-weight models significantly in structured reasoning tasks.
    *   **Gap:** Table 18 shows Apertus-70B-Instruct averaging **54.4%** on coding/math, compared to **74.3%** for Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct and **76.3%** for Qwen3-32B.
    *   **Cause:** Section 5.2 attributes this to the lack of **Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR)**. Competitors use RLVR to boost math/coding, but Apertus prioritized a simpler, more reproducible post-training pipeline (QRPO). This suggests that matching SOTA reasoning may require complex RL stages that challenge the "fully open" reproducibility goal.

### 6.3 Safety and Alignment Boundaries

The "fully open" release model imposes inherent limits on safety guarantees that users must acknowledge before deployment.

*   **Open-Weights Safety Risk:** As noted in Section 5.5.1, releasing weights allows malicious actors to revert safety alignment via simple post-training (e.g., fine-tuning on unaligned data).
    *   **Implication:** Apertus cannot guarantee safety in downstream deployments. The project explicitly states it does **not pursue jailbreak resistance**, delegating this to production-level guardrails (Section 5.5.1). Users cannot rely on the model weights alone for safety-critical applications.
*   **Multilingual Safety Variance:** Safety alignment does not generalize equally across all 149 post-training languages.
    *   **Evidence:** Tables 27 and 28 show LinguaSafe severity-weighted scores varying significantly by language. For Apertus-70B-Instruct, "Fairness & Justice" scores range from **50.00% (Bengali)** to **57.76% (English)**.
    *   **Risk:** Lower-resource languages may exhibit weaker safety guardrails, increasing the risk of harmful outputs in non-English contexts. Section 5.6 notes that medical misinformation resilience "deteriorated for lower-resource languages."
*   **Constitutional Specificity:** The Swiss AI Charter (Appendix O) encodes specific cultural values (e.g., consensus-building, federalism).
    *   **Limitation:** While 97.3% of surveyed Swiss residents agreed with the principles (Table 13), these values may not align with other cultural contexts. Deploying Apertus globally without adjusting the constitutional alignment may impose Swiss-centric normative frameworks on other regions.

### 6.4 Scalability and Engineering Constraints

The infrastructure required to train Apertus reveals barriers to replication, even with open code.

*   **FP8 Training Instability:** The team attempted to use FP8 (8-bit floating point) precision to accelerate training but encountered instability.
    *   **Failure Mode:** After ~300 billion tokens, FP8 training caused a substantial loss increase and gradient norm instabilities (Appendix D, Figure D.1).
    *   **Consequence:** The project reverted to BF16 (Brain Floating Point 16), which is computationally more expensive. This highlights that mixed-precision optimization at this scale remains an open engineering challenge, limiting training speed for future iterations.
*   **Compute Barrier:** Despite open code, the resource requirements are prohibitive for most academic groups.
    *   **Scale:** Training the 70B model required **4,096 GPUs** (NVIDIA Grace-Hopper) for approximately **90 days**, consuming **6 million GPU hours** and an estimated **5 GWh** of power (Section 6.2).
    *   **Implication:** While the *methods* are open, the *capacity* to replicate pretraining from scratch is concentrated in a few institutions with access to supercomputing infrastructure (e.g., CSCS Alps).
*   **Learning Rate Sensitivity:** Section 2.6 notes that the 70B model's cooldown phase did not show the expected loss slope improvement.
    *   **Hypothesis:** The team suspects the peak learning rate was set too low, but due to project schedule constraints, they could not experiment with higher values at scale.
    *   **Open Question:** Optimal learning rate scaling rules for 70B+ models under the AdEMAMix optimizer remain undetermined, potentially leaving performance on the table.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Future Directions

Several critical questions remain unresolved by this technical report, defining the roadmap for future research.

*   **RLVR Compatibility:** Can Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards be integrated into a fully open pipeline without compromising reproducibility? The current math/coding gap (Section 5.2) suggests this is the next frontier for open models.
*   **Dynamic Compliance:** The current `robots.txt` filtering is a static snapshot (January 2025). Future work must address how to handle dynamic consent changes continuously without re-crawling the entire corpus.
*   **Near-Duplicate Mitigation:** How can Goldfish Loss be hardened against formatting variations? Potential solutions include fuzzy hashing or semantic-level masking, but these introduce computational overhead that must be balanced against training efficiency.
*   **Distillation Viability:** Section 7 proposes distilling the 70B model into smaller students. It remains unknown whether the compliance and multilingual properties will survive distillation, or if smaller models will revert to English-dominant, less compliant behaviors.

In summary, Apertus proves that **compliance and openness are feasible at scale**, but not free. The project incurs measurable costs in English-centric reasoning performance, math/coding capabilities, and engineering complexity. These trade-offs are explicit design choices rather than oversights, providing a realistic baseline for what "trustworthy open AI" currently costs in terms of capability and resources.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

Apertus V1 is not merely a model release; it is a **protocol for trustworthy AI development**. By demonstrating that 70B-scale performance is achievable with full data transparency, retroactive compliance, and memorization mitigation, this work fundamentally shifts the baseline for what constitutes "open" AI. The implications extend beyond academic reproducibility into legal compliance, sovereign AI development, and the engineering of safer training objectives. Below, we analyze how this work changes the landscape, the research roadmap it enables, and practical guidance for integrating these methods.

### 7.1 Redefining "Open" and Compliance Baselines

The most immediate impact of Apertus is the redefinition of **openness** in the LLM ecosystem. Prior to this work, the field operated on a spectrum where "open-weights" (releasing parameters only) was often conflated with "open-source." Apertus draws a hard line: true openness requires **auditability of the data pipeline**.

*   **Compliance as an Engineering Constraint:** Section 3.1.1 quantifies the cost of ethical data usage for the first time at scale. The finding that retroactive `robots.txt` filtering results in an **~8% token loss in English** and **~4% in multilingual data** transforms compliance from a legal abstraction into a bounded engineering budget.
    *   **Implication:** Future model developers can no longer claim compliance is "too costly" without evidence. Apertus proves that competitive performance (Table 14, Table 15) is achievable within these constraints. This sets a precedent for regulations like the **EU AI Act**, providing a concrete technical blueprint for "data governance by design."
*   **Memorization as a Training Objective:** By integrating **Goldfish Loss** directly into pretraining (Section 2.3), Apertus shifts memorization mitigation from a post-hoc filter (which can be bypassed) to a structural property of the model weights.
    *   **Implication:** This suggests that future safety standards may require evidence of **training-time mitigation** rather than just inference-time guardrails. The success of Goldfish Loss (Rouge-L ≈ 0.18 at 128 exposures, Section 5.4.1) demonstrates that copyright risk can be reduced at the source, potentially lowering legal liability for downstream deployers.

### 7.2 Enabling Sovereign and Regional AI

Apertus provides a replicable architecture for **Sovereign AI**—models aligned to specific national or regional values rather than a monolithic global default. The combination of multilingual data and constitutional alignment offers a blueprint for other jurisdictions.

*   **Constitutional Alignment Pattern:** The use of the **Swiss AI Charter** with **QRPO** (Section 4.3.2) demonstrates how abstract values (e.g., "consensus-building," "neutrality") can be encoded into model behavior without massive human preference datasets.
    *   **Follow-up Research:** This enables other regions to develop their own charters. For example, an **EU AI Charter** could encode specific regulations on transparency, while an **African Union Charter** could prioritize linguistic diversity and oral tradition preservation. The key innovation is the **LLM-as-Judge** pipeline (Section 4.3.2), which allows values to be scored programmatically.
    *   **Practical Application:** Enterprises can adopt this pattern for **corporate alignment**. Instead of generic "helpfulness," a medical company could define a charter prioritizing "caution" and "citation," using QRPO to align models to these specific operational values.
*   **Low-Resource Language Viability:** The performance on **Romansh** (Table 24), where Apertus-70B-Instruct outperforms Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct despite having similar scale, proves that dedicated post-training data (46K examples, Section 4.1.3) can overcome pretraining data scarcity.
    *   **Implication:** This validates the "pretrain broad, finetune specific" strategy for endangered languages. Governments and NGOs can now justify investing in small, high-quality instruction datasets for low-resource languages, knowing that large base models can effectively absorb this signal.

### 7.3 Research Roadmap: Closing the Capability Gap

While Apertus matches open-weight models on general knowledge and multilingual tasks, Section 5.2 identified a performance gap in **mathematics and coding** (Table 18). Addressing this without sacrificing openness is the primary research frontier.

*   **Open RL with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR):** The paper notes that competitors likely use **Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR)** to boost math/coding scores (Section 5.2).
    *   **Challenge:** RLVR typically requires proprietary reward models or private test sets, which conflicts with full openness.
    *   **Future Direction:** Research must focus on **open verifiers**. This involves releasing not just the model weights, but the **reward models and verification scripts** used during RL. Apertus's release of training code (Section 1) provides the infrastructure to experiment with this. The goal is to achieve Llama-3.3-level math performance (e.g., GSM8K > 90%) while maintaining the auditability of the reward signal.
*   **Hardening Goldfish Loss:** Section 5.4.2 identified a failure mode where **near-duplicates** (e.g., different formatting of the same public domain text) bypass the hashing mechanism.
    *   **Future Direction:** Developing **fuzzy hashing** or **semantic-level masking** could address this. Instead of hashing exact token sequences, the loss could mask tokens based on semantic embeddings, ensuring that paraphrased duplicates are also mitigated. This requires balancing computational overhead against training efficiency, but is critical for robust copyright protection.
*   **FP8 Stability at Scale:** Appendix D documents a failure where **FP8 training** caused loss spikes after 300B tokens.
    *   **Future Direction:** Stabilizing FP8 is essential for reducing the **compute barrier** (Section 6.4). Current training required 6 million GPU hours (Section 6.2); stable FP8 could reduce this by ~25%, making 70B training accessible to more institutions. Research into **outlier protection** (mentioned in Appendix D) and activation smoothing (e.g., reordered LayerNorm) is needed to make mixed-precision training reliable for long runs.

### 7.4 Practical Integration Guidance

For practitioners deciding whether to adopt Apertus or alternative models, the choice depends on the specific constraints of the deployment environment.

*   **When to Prefer Apertus:**
    *   **Compliance-Sensitive Deployments:** If your organization operates under strict copyright or privacy regulations (e.g., healthcare, finance, EU public sector), Apertus is the superior choice. The **retroactive robots.txt filtering** (Section 3.1.1) and **PII removal** (Section 3.1.2) provide a defensible data lineage that open-weight models cannot offer.
    *   **Multilingual Applications:** For applications serving non-English speakers, particularly in Europe or low-resource contexts, Apertus's **40% non-English data allocation** (Section 1) and **Gini-optimized tokenizer** (Section 2.2) yield better performance and lower inference costs than English-optimized models.
    *   **Audit Requirements:** If you need to debug model behavior or verify training dynamics, Apertus's release of **intermediate checkpoints** and **data scripts** (Section 1) allows for root-cause analysis that is impossible with closed weights.

*   **When to Consider Alternatives:**
    *   **Raw Math/Coding Performance:** If your use case relies heavily on complex reasoning or code generation (e.g., automated software engineering), current open-weight models (e.g., Qwen2.5-72B, Llama-3.3-70B) still hold a **15–20% advantage** on benchmarks like GSM8K and HumanEval (Table 18). Until Apertus integrates RLVR, these models may be more capable for pure reasoning tasks.
    *   **Resource-Constrained Environments:** While Apertus releases code, the **4096 GPU requirement** for pretraining (Section 6.2) is prohibitive for most. Smaller organizations should look toward **distillation** (proposed in Section 7 of the paper) to create smaller student models that inherit Apertus's compliance properties without the compute cost.

*   **Integration Best Practices:**
    *   **Adopt the Chat Template:** Apertus uses a specific chat template with a **developer message** for tool definition (Section 4.2.1). When fine-tuning Apertus, adhere to this format to maintain compatibility with the trained instruction-following behavior.
    *   **Layer Safety Guardrails:** Because Apertus is open-weights, safety alignment can be reverted (Section 5.5.1). Do not rely solely on the model weights for safety. Deploy **external guardrails** (e.g., input/output filters) in production, treating the model's internal alignment as a secondary layer of defense.
    *   **Leverage Intermediate Checkpoints:** The release of intermediate checkpoints allows for **early stopping** or **domain adaptation**. If your domain differs significantly from the web (e.g., legal text), you can resume training from a mid-training checkpoint (e.g., 10T tokens) rather than starting from scratch, saving compute while maintaining data compliance.

### 7.5 Conclusion: A Foundation for Accountable AI

Apertus V1 establishes that **transparency and performance are not mutually exclusive**. By engineering solutions for data compliance (Section 3.1), memorization mitigation (Section 2.3), and multilingual equity (Section 2.2), the project provides a reproducible baseline for the next generation of AI.

The path forward requires the community to build upon this foundation: hardening memorization defenses against near-duplicates, integrating verifiable reinforcement learning without sacrificing openness, and extending the constitutional alignment pattern to diverse cultural contexts. Apertus invites this collaboration not just through model weights, but through the complete **scientific artifacts** of its development cycle. In doing so, it shifts the burden of proof: future models must now demonstrate not only *what* they can do, but *how* they were built.
