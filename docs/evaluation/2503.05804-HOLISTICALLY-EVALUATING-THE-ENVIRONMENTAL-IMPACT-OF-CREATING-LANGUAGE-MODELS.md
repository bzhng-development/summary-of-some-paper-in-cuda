## 1. Executive Summary

This paper provides the first holistic environmental impact assessment for a series of Large Language Models (LLMs), ranging from `OLMo` 20M to `OLMo 2` 13B parameters, by quantifying not only final training costs but also the often-ignored "development" phase (hyperparameter tuning and experiments) and hardware manufacturing. The authors reveal that model development alone accounted for approximately 50% of the total carbon emissions of the final training runs, contributing to a combined total of 493 metric tons of CO2 equivalent and 2.769 million liters of water consumed across the project. This work matters because it exposes significant transparency gaps in the industry—where most reports omit development and embodied costs—and highlights critical grid-stability challenges caused by power consumption fluctuations of 15% to 85% during standard checkpointing operations.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Transparency Gap in AI Environmental Reporting

The central problem this paper addresses is the **incomplete accounting of environmental costs** in the development of Large Language Models (LLMs). While the AI community has increasingly acknowledged that training massive models consumes significant energy, current reporting standards provide a fragmented and often misleading picture of the true ecological footprint.

The specific gap lies in the omission of three critical lifecycle stages:
1.  **Model Development:** The extensive phase of hyperparameter tuning, architecture search, and failed experiments that precedes the final training run.
2.  **Embodied Impacts:** The carbon emissions and water usage associated with manufacturing the hardware (GPUs, servers) and constructing the data centers.
3.  **Granular Operational Dynamics:** The real-time fluctuations in power demand during training, which have implications for grid stability beyond simple total energy counts.

Most existing reports focus exclusively on the **final training run** of a released model. This creates a "survivorship bias" in environmental reporting, where the cost of the successful model is published, but the waste generated by the dozens of unsuccessful or preliminary runs required to reach that success is hidden. As the authors note, "comparatively little transparency [exists] into the impact of model development, hardware manufacturing, and total water usage throughout" (Abstract).

### Why This Problem Matters

The importance of closing this transparency gap is twofold: it affects **resource planning** at a global scale and informs **ethical decision-making** for developers and policymakers.

**Real-World Impact on Energy Infrastructure**
The energy demands of AI are no longer negligible; they are becoming a dominant factor in national energy grids. Projections suggest data centers could consume up to **11.7% of total US energy demand by 2030** (Introduction). This scale of consumption forces energy providers to make long-term infrastructure decisions, such as reopening nuclear power plants or extending the life of fossil fuel facilities. Without accurate data on *when* and *how* AI systems consume power (e.g., sudden spikes and drops), grid operators cannot efficiently balance supply and demand. If AI training causes rapid fluctuations, it may force grids to rely on "peaker plants" (often fossil-fuel-based) that can spin up quickly, thereby increasing the marginal carbon intensity of the electricity used.

**Ethical and Policy Implications**
For stakeholders ranging from researchers to legislators, incomplete data hinders the ability to mitigate harm. If development costs are ignored, the perceived efficiency of a model is artificially inflated. This misrepresentation affects:
*   **Policy:** Legislation like the EU AI Act and proposed US bills require accurate baselines to set effective environmental standards.
*   **Research Direction:** If the cost of experimentation is hidden, there is less incentive to develop methods that reduce the number of trials needed (e.g., better scaling laws or parameter-efficient tuning).
*   **Public Accountability:** The public cannot assess the trade-offs of AI advancement if the reported cost represents only a fraction of the actual resource extraction and pollution.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches

Before this work, the state-of-the-art in environmental auditing for AI suffered from several methodological shortcomings:

*   **Exclusion of Development Costs:** Previous studies typically calculated costs based on the formula $Cost(R) \propto E \cdot D$, where $E$ is the cost per example, $D$ is dataset size, and the number of experiments $H$ is assumed to be 1 or ignored (Section 3.1). In reality, $H$ is often large. For instance, while some works measured training emissions (Dodge et al., 2022), they did not account for the "failed runs" or hyperparameter searches that precede the final model.
*   **Theoretical vs. Measured Power Draw:** Many reports estimate energy consumption by assuming GPUs operate at **100% of their theoretical maximum power draw** throughout the entire training duration (e.g., Dubey et al., 2024 for Llama 3). This is a significant oversimplification. As this paper demonstrates, actual power usage fluctuates wildly between **~15% and ~85%** of maximum capacity due to operations like checkpointing (saving model states) and synchronization (Section 4.3). Assuming 100% utilization can lead to inaccurate estimates of both total energy and the specific timing of demand spikes.
*   **Lack of Water Metrics:** While carbon emissions have received attention, water consumption is frequently omitted. Water is consumed both onsite (for cooling) and offsite (for power generation, particularly in thermal and hydro plants). Prior estimates for water usage in models like GPT-3 were based on speculation regarding location and energy mix because training details were proprietary (Li et al., 2023).
*   **Opaque Embodied Carbon:** The carbon cost of manufacturing hardware is often estimated using broad averages or ignored entirely because chip manufacturers (like NVIDIA) do not release specific lifecycle data. Previous attempts (Luccioni et al., 2023) had to rely on assumptions, and this opacity persists.

### Positioning of This Work

This paper positions itself as the **first holistic assessment** that integrates all missing pieces of the lifecycle puzzle for a series of LLMs. It distinguishes itself from prior work through four key methodological advancements:

1.  **Inclusion of the "Hidden" Development Phase:** The authors explicitly measure and report the environmental cost of the development phase (hyperparameter tuning, scaling law experiments). They find this phase accounts for **~50%** of the emissions of the final training runs, a figure previously unreported in the literature (Abstract, Section 4.1).
2.  **Empirical Time-Series Measurement:** Instead of assuming constant 100% load, the authors measure power consumption at **sub-second intervals** throughout the training process. This allows them to capture the dynamic nature of energy demand and identify inefficiencies like the power drops during checkpointing (Section 3.1, Figure 2).
3.  **Comprehensive Scope of Impact:** The study calculates:
    *   **Scope 1 & 2:** Direct operational emissions and water usage (using specific regional carbon intensity and Water Usage Effectiveness (WUE) factors for their Texas and Iowa data centers).
    *   **Upstream Embodied Impacts:** Amortized carbon and water costs of GPU manufacturing and rare earth mining.
    *   **Downstream Inference Estimates:** Simulated costs of deploying the models, providing "breakeven" points where inference costs surpass training costs (Section 3.4, Table 3).
4.  **Focus on a Model Series:** Rather than analyzing a single massive model in isolation, the paper evaluates a ladder of models ranging from **20 million to 13 billion parameters**. This allows for an analysis of how environmental costs scale with model size and dataset size, revealing non-linear trends in efficiency and impact (Figure 1, Section 3.3).

By anchoring their analysis in the **OLMo** (Open Language Model) series, the authors leverage their unique access to internal logs and experimental history—data that is typically proprietary and inaccessible in closed-source models like GPT-4 or Llama. This transparency allows them to provide a ground-truth baseline against which the estimates of other models can be critically evaluated.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper is a **holistic environmental audit** that constructs a comprehensive accounting framework to measure the carbon emissions, water consumption, and energy usage across the entire lifecycle of Large Language Model (LLM) development. The core idea is to move beyond simple estimates of final training runs by integrating granular, sub-second power measurements with amortized hardware manufacturing costs and simulated downstream inference scenarios.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The "system" being analyzed is not a single neural network, but the entire industrial pipeline required to create a series of language models, ranging from tiny 20-million parameter prototypes to 13-billion parameter production models. This approach solves the problem of incomplete environmental reporting by treating model creation as a multi-stage manufacturing process—encompassing failed experiments, hardware production, active training, and future usage—rather than just the final successful training run.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The methodology functions as a data aggregation pipeline with four distinct processing modules that feed into a final impact calculator.
*   **Module 1: Operational Monitoring:** This component ingests real-time telemetry from GPU clusters (specifically the "Jupiter" cluster in Texas and "Augusta" in Iowa), logging power draw at sub-second intervals during both experimental development and final training.
*   **Module 2: Embodied Impact Estimator:** This module takes static hardware specifications (number of NVIDIA H100 GPUs, server nodes) and applies amortization factors over a assumed 4-year lifespan to distribute the manufacturing carbon and water costs across specific training hours.
*   **Module 3: Regional Conversion Engine:** This component translates raw energy data (kWh) into environmental impacts (tons of $CO_2$, liters of water) using location-specific coefficients for grid carbon intensity and Water Usage Effectiveness (WUE).
*   **Module 4: Inference Simulator:** This module runs the trained models in a controlled benchmark environment (using the SGLang serving engine) to project future energy costs based on varying user request rates.
*   **Final Aggregator:** All outputs are summed to produce total Scope 1, 2, and partial Scope 3 emissions, providing a unified view of the model's footprint.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   First, we define the **mathematical foundations** for converting electricity into carbon and water metrics, establishing the specific regional constants used for the Texas and Iowa data centers.
*   Second, we detail the **data collection mechanism** for operational impacts, explaining how sub-second power logging captures the dynamic fluctuations missed by theoretical estimates.
*   Third, we explain the **embodied impact model**, describing how the opaque costs of semiconductor manufacturing are estimated and amortized over time.
*   Fourth, we outline the **inference simulation protocol**, which constructs realistic usage scenarios to estimate downstream "Scope 3" emissions.
*   Finally, we synthesize these components to show how the authors calculate the total lifecycle impact for the OLMo model series.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Foundational Equations and Regional Constants
The authors ground their analysis in established environmental accounting formulas, adapting them specifically for the nuances of AI training workloads.
*   To calculate operational carbon emissions, the paper utilizes the standard equation $CO_2e = P \cdot PUE \cdot CI$, where $P$ represents the measured power consumption in kWh, $PUE$ is the Power Usage Effectiveness ratio of the data center, and $CI$ is the carbon intensity of the local electrical grid.
*   The term $PUE$ (Power Usage Effectiveness) is a metric defined as the ratio of total facility energy to the energy delivered to computing equipment; a value of 1.0 would indicate perfect efficiency with no overhead for cooling or lighting.
*   For water consumption, the authors employ the formula $Consumption = P \cdot PUE \cdot (WUE_{onsite} + WUE_{offsite})$, where $WUE$ stands for Water Usage Effectiveness, measured in liters of water consumed per kWh of electricity generated or used.
*   The term $WUE_{onsite}$ refers to water lost directly at the data center (e.g., via evaporative cooling towers), while $WUE_{offsite}$ accounts for water evaporated or polluted at the power plant generating the electricity.
*   The study relies on two distinct physical locations with different environmental profiles: the "Jupiter" cluster in Texas and the "Augusta" cluster in Iowa.
*   For the **Jupiter cluster** (Texas), the authors apply a conservative $PUE$ of 1.2, a carbon intensity ($CI$) of 0.332 kg $CO_2$ per kWh based on Austin Energy data, and an offsite water usage factor ($WUE_{offsite}$) of 1.29 L/kWh.
*   Crucially, because the Jupiter cluster uses a closed-loop cooling system with no evaporation, the authors set the onsite water usage ($WUE_{onsite}$) to exactly 0 L/kWh.
*   For the **Augusta cluster** (Iowa), which hosted the largest 13B parameter model, the authors use a trailing twelve-month average $PUE$ of 1.12, a state-level carbon intensity of 0.352 kg $CO_2$ per kWh, and a significantly higher $WUE_{offsite}$ of 3.10 L/kWh due to Iowa's specific power generation mix.

#### Operational Impact Measurement: Granular Time-Series Logging
Unlike prior studies that assume GPUs run at a constant 100% of their theoretical maximum power, this paper implements a high-frequency monitoring strategy to capture real-world inefficiencies.
*   The authors instrument their training runs to log power consumption data at **sub-second intervals** for every node in the cluster, rather than relying on aggregate billing data or theoretical TDP (Thermal Design Power) ratings.
*   This measurement strategy focuses specifically on GPU power draw, which the authors acknowledge serves as a lower bound for total system power since it excludes CPU, memory, and networking overhead not captured by the GPU sensors.
*   The data collection covers three distinct phases of the machine learning lifecycle: the **development phase** (hyperparameter tuning and ablation studies), the **final training run** (the full-scale training of the released model), and **inference** (simulated deployment).
*   By capturing time-series data, the system reveals that power usage is highly non-linear, fluctuating between approximately **15% and 85%** of the NVIDIA H100's maximum power draw of 700W.
*   The technical analysis identifies **checkpointing** (the process of saving model weights to disk) as a primary driver of these fluctuations; during active training, power averages over 600W, but drops sharply to just over 100W when the system pauses to write checkpoints.
*   The authors extrapolate the single-node time-series data to the full cluster size, which ranged from 2 to 128 nodes concurrently, to estimate the total energy consumption for each experiment.
*   This approach explicitly corrects the "100% utilization" assumption common in industry reports (such as those for Llama 3), which the authors argue leads to inaccurate modeling of grid demand spikes and total energy use.

#### Embodied Impact Modeling: Amortization of Hardware Costs
Because semiconductor manufacturers like NVIDIA do not publish specific lifecycle data for their chips, the authors construct an estimation model based on prior literature and material science assumptions.
*   The embodied impact calculation treats the manufacturing of hardware as a fixed carbon and water debt that is "amortized" (spread out) over the useful lifetime of the equipment.
*   The authors assume a **4-year lifespan** for their NVIDIA H100 GPUs, a standard industry expectation for high-performance computing hardware before obsolescence.
*   For carbon emissions, they adopt the estimate from Luccioni et al. (2023) of **3,700 kg of $CO_2e$ per 8-GPU server node**, which breaks down to approximately **463 kg of $CO_2e$ per individual GPU**.
*   To estimate water usage in manufacturing, the authors calculate based on semiconductor fabrication metrics, assuming a water usage rate of **12.33 liters per square centimeter** of hardware, resulting in an estimate of **100.4 liters per H100 GPU**.
*   The model also accounts for the extraction of rare earth metals, assuming an H100 GPU consists of **0.1% rare earth metal by mass**.
*   Using data from Browning et al. (2016), the authors assign a cost of **11 kL of water and 65.4 kg $CO_2e$ per kg** of rare earth material mined.
*   Based on the mass of a 12-inch silicon wafer (125 grams) yielding approximately 63 H100 dies, the mining contribution adds a negligible but tracked **2.2 liters of water and 0.013 kg $CO_2e$ per GPU**.
*   These total embodied costs are divided by the total operational hours expected over the 4-year lifespan to derive an hourly "tax" of **0.013 kg $CO_2e$ and 0.003 liters of water per GPU hour**.
*   This hourly rate is then multiplied by the actual GPU hours consumed during development and training (totaling 1.65 million GPU hours for the project) to assign a specific embodied footprint to the model series.

#### Inference Simulation: Projecting Downstream Scope 3 Impacts
Since the authors do not deploy their models publicly, they engineer a controlled simulation environment to estimate the environmental cost of users interacting with the models after release.
*   The simulation serves the models on a single **NVIDIA H100 GPU** using the **SGLang** inference engine, a framework optimized for structured language model execution.
*   The input data for the simulation consists of **2,400 prompts** sampled from the **ShareGPT dataset**, ensuring a realistic distribution of user queries rather than synthetic benchmarks.
*   The authors test three distinct request frequency scenarios to model different deployment pressures: infinite batching (all requests sent instantly), a moderate rate of **8 requests per second**, and a low rate of **1 request per second**.
*   For the non-batched scenarios, request arrivals follow a **Poisson distribution**, mimicking the stochastic nature of real-world user traffic.
*   The simulation measures cumulative energy consumption using **CodeCarbon** tracking, verified against the same time-series monitoring infrastructure used during training to ensure consistency.
*   Crucially, the measurement isolates the power consumed by active GPU processes, explicitly excluding idle overhead such as holding the model in VRAM or listening for network connections, thereby providing a lower-bound estimate.
*   The authors calculate a **"breakeven point"** for each model, defined as the number of inference requests required for the cumulative inference emissions to equal the total emissions generated during the model's training and development.
*   This methodology allows the paper to quantify the "Jevons Paradox" risk, where increased efficiency in smaller models might lead to vastly higher total consumption due to increased deployment volume.

#### Synthesis: The Holistic Accounting Framework
The final technical contribution is the integration of these disparate data streams into a unified ledger that attributes impact to specific stages of the ML lifecycle.
*   The framework categorizes impacts into **Scope 1 and 2** (direct operational emissions from electricity and cooling) and partial **Scope 3** (upstream manufacturing and downstream inference).
*   It explicitly separates **Development Costs** (the sum of all failed experiments, hyperparameter searches, and scaling law runs) from **Final Training Costs**, a distinction that reveals development can account for ~50% of the total footprint.
*   The system aggregates data across a "model ladder" ranging from **20 million to 13 billion active parameters**, trained on datasets ranging from **1.7 trillion to 5.6 trillion tokens**.
*   By applying the regional constants ($CI$, $PUE$, $WUE$) to the measured power ($P$) and adding the amortized embodied costs, the framework produces a final tally in metric tons of $CO_2e$ and kiloliters of water.
*   This approach enables the authors to assert that their series of models released **493 metric tons of carbon** and consumed **2.769 million liters of water**, figures that include the often-hidden costs of the iterative scientific process required to achieve the final result.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper moves beyond merely quantifying environmental costs; it fundamentally reshapes our understanding of *where* those costs originate and *how* they behave dynamically. The following insights represent a shift from static, post-hoc estimation to a dynamic, lifecycle-aware analysis of AI development.

### 4.1 The "Hidden" Half: Development Costs Equal Training Costs
The most startling empirical finding of this work is that the environmental impact of **model development**—the iterative phase of hyperparameter tuning, architecture search, and failed experiments—is roughly equivalent to the impact of the **final training run**.

*   **What makes this different:** Prior industry reports (e.g., for Llama or Gemma models) almost exclusively disclose the cost of the final, successful training job. This creates a "survivorship bias" where the public sees the cost of the winner but not the waste generated by the dozens of losers required to find it. As detailed in **Table 1**, the authors found that for their series of models, development runs consumed **680k GPU hours** and emitted **159 tCO2eq**, which is approximately **50%** of the emissions generated by the final training runs (**312 tCO2eq** in **Table 2**).
*   **Why it's significant:** This insight invalidates efficiency claims based solely on final training metrics. If a lab runs 50 experiments to find one optimal configuration, the true carbon cost is $51\times$ the cost of a single run, not $1\times$. This has profound implications for research methodology: it suggests that investments in **compute-efficient search strategies** (like better scaling laws or parameter-efficient fine-tuning) may yield greater environmental returns than simply optimizing the final training loop. It reframes "failed" experiments not as sunk costs, but as major contributors to the total ecological footprint.

### 4.2 The Dynamic Reality: Power Fluctuations and Grid Instability
The paper challenges the static assumption that AI training draws a constant, predictable load from the power grid. By analyzing sub-second time-series data, the authors reveal that training workloads are highly volatile, posing specific challenges for grid infrastructure.

*   **What makes this different:** Previous estimates often assume GPUs operate at 100% of their Thermal Design Power (TDP) continuously. In contrast, **Figure 2** and **Section 4.3** demonstrate that power usage oscillates dramatically between **~15% and ~85%** of the NVIDIA H100's maximum capacity (700W). These drops correspond directly to **checkpointing** events, where the system pauses computation to save model weights to disk.
*   **Why it's significant:** This is not just an efficiency issue; it is a **grid stability** issue. Power grids are designed for relatively smooth demand curves. Sudden, massive drops in demand (when thousands of GPUs simultaneously stop computing to checkpoint) force grid operators to rapidly curtail generation or dump excess energy, often requiring fast-response fossil fuel "peaker plants" to balance the subsequent spike when training resumes. This insight suggests that "green AI" requires not just low total energy, but **smooth energy profiles**. It calls for engineering innovations like **parallelized checkpointing** or staggered scheduling to flatten these demand spikes, a consideration entirely absent from prior static analyses.

### 4.3 The Inference Tipping Point: The Jevons Paradox in Action
The study provides a quantitative framework for understanding when the cost of *using* a model overtakes the cost of *creating* it, highlighting the risks of deploying highly efficient small models.

*   **What makes this different:** While prior work has estimated inference costs in isolation, this paper calculates specific **"breakeven points"**—the number of inference requests required for downstream emissions to equal the upfront training emissions. **Table 3** reveals that for smaller, deployment-optimized models (like `OLMo 1B`), this threshold can be as low as **441 million requests** (at 1 req/s), whereas larger models may require tens of billions.
*   **Why it's significant:** This data illustrates **Jevons' Paradox**: as models become more efficient to train and run, their usage volume increases so drastically that total resource consumption rises rather than falls. The authors note that large providers already generate hundreds of billions of tokens daily, meaning even small models can surpass their training footprint in **weeks or months**. This shifts the focus of environmental mitigation: for popular models, optimizing **inference efficiency** (e.g., via quantization or better serving engines) becomes far more critical than optimizing training, a strategic pivot that is often overlooked in favor of pretraining benchmarks.

### 4.4 Holistic Water Accounting: Beyond Carbon
By integrating water consumption into the audit, the paper exposes a critical, under-reported dimension of AI's environmental impact that varies significantly by geography.

*   **What makes this different:** Most AI environmental reports focus exclusively on carbon ($CO_2e$). This paper rigorously applies the **Water Usage Effectiveness (WUE)** metric, distinguishing between onsite cooling water and offsite water used in power generation. Crucially, it highlights how location dictates impact: the Iowa cluster (Augusta) had a $WUE_{offsite}$ of **3.10 L/kWh**, more than double that of the Texas cluster (Jupiter) at **1.29 L/kWh**, due to differences in the local power mix (e.g., thermal vs. hydro/nuclear).
*   **Why it's significant:** This reveals that a "low carbon" grid is not necessarily a "low water" grid. A data center located in a region with abundant renewable wind energy might still have a high water footprint if the backup or baseline generation relies on thermoelectric power. This insight complicates site-selection strategies for AI labs, suggesting that optimal placement requires a multi-objective optimization of both carbon intensity and water stress, rather than focusing on carbon alone.

### Summary of Innovation Types
*   **Fundamental Innovations:** The inclusion of **development costs** as a primary metric and the identification of **power fluctuation dynamics** represent paradigm shifts in how we define and measure AI's footprint. These change the core questions researchers ask about efficiency.
*   **Incremental but Critical Improvements:** The refined **water accounting** by region and the specific **inference breakeven calculations** build on existing methodologies but apply them with a level of granularity and context (the OLMo series) that transforms them from theoretical estimates into actionable data points for policy and engineering.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence presented in the paper, moving from the methodology of data collection to the specific quantitative results that underpin the authors' claims. Unlike traditional machine learning papers that evaluate model performance on benchmarks (e.g., accuracy on MMLU), this study evaluates the **environmental performance** of the model creation pipeline. The "experiments" here are the actual training runs, development iterations, and simulated deployment scenarios of the OLMo series.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Experimental Setup

The experimental design is structured around three distinct phases of the machine learning lifecycle, each with specific data collection protocols and baselines.

**Datasets and Model Series**
The core subjects of the analysis are the **OLMo series** of transformer language models, ranging from **20 million to 13 billion active parameters**.
*   **Training Data Scale:** The models were trained on datasets ranging from **1.7 trillion tokens** (for sub-billion parameter models) up to **5.6 trillion tokens** (for the 13B model). The Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) model (`OLMoE 0924`) was trained on **5 trillion tokens** (Section 3.3).
*   **Hardware Infrastructure:** Experiments were conducted on two primary clusters:
    *   **Jupiter (Texas):** Used for all models except the 13B variant. Equipped with NVIDIA H100 GPUs.
    *   **Augusta (Iowa):** Used exclusively for the `OLMo 2 13B` model.
*   **Server Configuration:** Models were trained on standard HGX servers containing **8 NVIDIA H100 GPUs** per node, with cluster sizes scaling from **2 to 128 nodes** concurrently depending on the model size (Section 3.3).

**Metrics and Measurement Tools**
The study employs a triad of environmental metrics, measured with varying degrees of granularity:
1.  **Energy Consumption:** Measured in Megawatt-hours (MWh) or kilowatt-hours (kWh). Data was collected via **sub-second time-series logging** of GPU power draw, extrapolated to the full cluster.
2.  **Carbon Emissions:** Reported in metric tons of $CO_2$ equivalent ($tCO_2e$). Calculated using Equation 2 ($CO_2e = P \cdot PUE \cdot CI$), applying region-specific Carbon Intensity ($CI$) factors: **0.332 kg/kWh** for Texas and **0.352 kg/kWh** for Iowa (Section 3.1).
3.  **Water Consumption:** Reported in kiloliters (kL) or liters. Calculated using Equation 3, incorporating Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) and Water Usage Effectiveness (WUE). Notably, onsite WUE was **0 L/kWh** (closed-loop cooling), while offsite WUE varied by region (**1.29 L/kWh** for Texas, **3.10 L/kWh** for Iowa) (Section 3.1).

**Baselines and Comparators**
To contextualize their findings, the authors compare their measured results against:
*   **Industry Reports:** Estimates provided for **Llama 2**, **Llama 3**, and **Gemma** models (Table 2). These serve as baselines to highlight the lack of transparency and the reliance on theoretical assumptions (e.g., 100% GPU utilization) in other works.
*   **Theoretical Maximums:** The paper implicitly compares measured power draw against the NVIDIA H100's Thermal Design Power (TDP) of **700W** to demonstrate utilization inefficiencies.
*   **Everyday Equivalencies:** To make abstract numbers comprehensible, results are converted into relatable units, such as "years of electricity for one US home" or "years of water usage for one US person" (Tables 1 and 2).

**Inference Simulation Protocol**
Since the models were not publicly deployed during the study, the authors engineered a controlled simulation to estimate downstream (Scope 3) impacts:
*   **Serving Engine:** Models were served using **SGLang** on a single H100 GPU.
*   **Workload:** The simulation processed **2,400 prompts** from the **ShareGPT dataset**.
*   **Scenarios:** Three request frequencies were tested to model different load conditions:
    1.  **Infinite Batching ($\infty$ req/s):** All requests sent simultaneously (maximum throughput).
    2.  **Moderate Load (8 req/s):** Requests following a Poisson distribution.
    3.  **Low Load (1 req/s):** Sparse requests, simulating low-traffic deployment.
*   **Metric:** The key output is the **"breakeven point"**: the number of inference requests required for the cumulative inference emissions to equal the total emissions generated during training and development (Table 3).

### 5.2 Quantitative Results: The Cost of Creation

The experimental results provide the first granular breakdown of environmental costs across the development lifecycle, challenging the industry norm of reporting only final training runs.

**The "Hidden" Cost of Development**
Table 1 presents the most critical finding regarding the development phase. The authors categorized development runs into five groups based on model scale (&lt;1B, 1B, 7B, 13B, MoE).
*   **Total Development Impact:** Across all model groups, the development phase consumed **680,000 GPU hours** and **459 MWh** of energy.
*   **Carbon and Water Footprint:** This phase resulted in **159 $tCO_2e$** emitted and **843 kL** of water consumed.
*   **Scale Disparity:** The impact was not evenly distributed. Approximately **70%** of the developmental environmental impact came from the **7B and 13B** model groups, driven by both the larger model size and the researchers' prioritization of tuning these specific architectures.
*   **Comparison to Training:** When compared to the final training runs (Table 2), which emitted **312 $tCO_2e$**, the development phase accounts for roughly **51%** of the training cost. This validates the claim that ignoring development leads to a ~50% underestimation of the true carbon footprint.

**Final Training Run Efficiency and Scale**
Table 2 details the costs of the final, released models. The data reveals a steep scaling law for environmental impact:
*   **Small Models:** The `OLMo 20M` model required only **0.8 MWh** and emitted **0.3 $tCO_2e$** (equivalent to 3 weeks of US home energy use).
*   **Large Models:** The `OLMo 2 13B` model required **230 MWh** and emitted **101 $tCO_2e$** (equivalent to 21 years of US home energy use).
*   **Water Intensity:** The water consumption scales similarly, from **1 kL** for the 20M model to **892 kL** for the 13B model.
*   **Anomalies and Efficiency:** The `OLMo 7B` model trained on the LUMI supercomputer (powered by hydro) shows **0 $tCO_2e$** and **0 kL** in the table (marked with *), highlighting how location can decouple energy use from carbon/water impact. Conversely, the `OLMo 1B` dense model and the `OLMoE` (MoE) model showed similar energy efficiency per trillion tokens, though the MoE was slightly less efficient due to routing overhead.

**Aggregate Project Impact**
Summing development, training, and embodied hardware costs (22 $tCO_2e$ from manufacturing), the total project footprint is:
*   **Carbon:** **493 $tCO_2e$**. The authors contextualize this as equivalent to burning **6.5 tanker trucks** of gasoline or powering **98.2 US homes** for one year.
*   **Water:** **2.769 million liters (2,769 kL)**. This equals the water consumption of an average US person over **24.5 years**.

### 5.3 Quantitative Results: Inference and The Breakeven Point

Table 3 provides the empirical data for the "Jevons Paradox" argument, quantifying how quickly usage can outpace creation costs.

**Breakeven Analysis**
The "breakeven point" is the number of inference requests needed for the downstream emissions to match the upfront training/development emissions. The results show a strong inverse relationship between model size and the speed at which this threshold is reached under low-load conditions:
*   **Small Models (Fast Breakeven):** For `OLMo 1 1B` at a low request rate (1 req/s), the breakeven point is **441 million inferences**. At high throughput ($\infty$), it jumps to **18.2 billion** due to better hardware utilization.
*   **Large Models (Slow Breakeven):** For `OLMo 2 13B`, the breakeven point is **1.89 billion inferences** at 1 req/s, and **22.1 billion** at infinite batching.
*   **The Danger Zone:** The authors note that major AI providers generate hundreds of billions of tokens daily. Consequently, even the larger models in this study could reach their breakeven point in **weeks to months** of production deployment. For smaller models deployed on edge devices or in high-volume APIs, this threshold is reached almost instantly relative to the model's lifespan.

**Efficiency vs. Utilization Trade-off**
The table reveals a subtle but critical insight: **smaller models are not always more efficient per request if utilization is low.**
*   At 1 req/s, the carbon emission per 100 requests for `Llama 3.2 1B` is **53.1 g $CO_2e$**, while for `OLMo 2 13B` it is **128.2 g $CO_2e$**. While the 13B model emits ~2.4x more, it is not 13x more expensive.
*   However, the *relative* cost compared to training is much higher for small models. Because the training cost of a 1B model is so low, a relatively small amount of inference usage dwarfs the training footprint. This supports the claim that deployment optimization is critical for small models, whereas training optimization remains key for large ones.

### 5.4 Dynamic Power Analysis: Evidence of Grid Instability

Beyond aggregate totals, the paper presents time-series evidence of operational inefficiency.
*   **Power Fluctuations:** As shown in **Figure 2**, GPU power draw is not constant. During active computation, the average power is **>600W** (>85% of the 700W max). However, during checkpointing (saving model state), power drops precipitously to **~100W** (~15% of max).
*   **Frequency of Spikes:** The authors note that checkpointing occurs frequently throughout long training runs. For a large-scale run, this results in **thousands of rapid power fluctuations**.
*   **Implication:** This empirical data contradicts the assumption of steady-state load used in grid planning. The magnitude of the drop (70% reduction) suggests that without mitigation (e.g., staggered checkpointing), large clusters act as massive, oscillating loads that complicate grid frequency regulation.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Validity

**Strengths: Convincing Support for Claims**
The experiments strongly support the paper's central thesis that current reporting is incomplete.
*   **Direct Measurement vs. Estimation:** By measuring sub-second power data rather than assuming 100% utilization, the authors provide a ground-truth baseline that exposes the inaccuracies of theoretical models. The clear visualization in Figure 2 makes the argument for grid instability undeniable.
*   **Comprehensive Scope:** The inclusion of development costs (Table 1) provides the missing link in lifecycle analysis. The fact that development costs ~50% of training is a robust finding derived from internal logs that are rarely accessible to external auditors.
*   **Regional Specificity:** The differentiation between Texas and Iowa water/carbon factors demonstrates that "one-size-fits-all" estimates are insufficient, adding credibility to the water consumption claims.

**Limitations and Conditional Results**
Several constraints temper the generalizability of the results:
*   **Embodied Impact Assumptions:** The calculation of hardware manufacturing costs relies on external estimates (Luccioni et al., 2023) and assumptions about rare earth metal content (0.1% by mass). As the authors admit in Section 5.1, NVIDIA does not release this data, so these figures (22 $tCO_2e$ total) are **estimates, not measurements**. If the actual manufacturing footprint is higher, the total impact is underestimated.
*   **Inference Simulation Constraints:** The inference results (Table 3) are simulations, not real-world deployments.
    *   They assume a single GPU setup, ignoring the overhead of multi-GPU serving or CPU/RAM bottlenecks which could increase energy use.
    *   They exclude idle overhead (holding model in memory), meaning the reported inference costs are **lower bounds**. Real-world servers running 24/7 would have higher baselines, potentially lowering the breakeven point further.
*   **Model Scale:** The largest model studied is 13B parameters. While the trends likely hold, the non-linearities of training at the 100B+ parameter scale (e.g., increased communication overhead, different failure rates) are not captured. The authors explicitly state in Appendix A.2 that they do not expect linear trends to hold tightly across all possible scales.
*   **Cooling System Specificity:** The assumption of **0 L/kWh** onsite water usage is specific to the Jupiter cluster's closed-loop system. Data centers using evaporative cooling (common in dry climates) would have significantly higher onsite water consumption, meaning the water results are specific to efficient infrastructure and may underestimate impacts for less modern facilities.

**Conclusion on Robustness**
Despite these limitations, the experiments are robust within their defined scope. The use of internal telemetry for power and the rigorous application of regional conversion factors provide a level of precision unseen in prior literature. The findings regarding development costs and power fluctuations are particularly resilient because they are derived from direct observation rather than modeling assumptions. The inference results, while simulated, use realistic workloads (ShareGPT) and standard serving engines (SGLang), making them a reliable proxy for potential real-world impacts.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper represents the most comprehensive environmental audit of Large Language Models (LLMs) to date, the authors are transparent about the inherent constraints of their methodology. The study relies on several critical assumptions, excludes specific lifecycle stages due to data opacity, and faces scalability limits in its experimental design. Understanding these limitations is essential for correctly interpreting the reported figures and recognizing where future research must focus.

### 6.1 Reliance on External Estimates for Embodied Impacts
The most significant assumption in this work concerns **embodied carbon and water usage**—the environmental cost of manufacturing the hardware (GPUs, servers) and extracting raw materials.

*   **The Data Void:** The authors explicitly state that NVIDIA and other hardware manufacturers do not release lifecycle assessment (LCA) data for their products. As noted in Section 5.1, "NVIDIA does not release the embodied carbon emissions or water consumption about the hardware it produces."
*   **The Assumption:** To fill this gap, the authors adopt estimates from prior work (Luccioni et al., 2023), assuming **3,700 kg of $CO_2e$ per 8-GPU server node** and calculating water usage based on semiconductor fabrication metrics (12.33 L/cm²). They further assume an H100 GPU contains **0.1% rare earth metals by mass** to estimate mining impacts.
*   **The Trade-off:** This introduces uncertainty into the total footprint. If the actual manufacturing process is more energy-intensive or water-heavy than these external estimates suggest, the paper's total embodied impact (reported as **22 $tCO_2e$** and **4.8 kL** water) is an **underestimate**. Conversely, if manufacturing has become more efficient since the cited studies, it could be an overestimate. Until chip manufacturers provide transparent supply chain data, this component of the audit remains an educated approximation rather than a measured fact.

### 6.2 Exclusion of Full Scope 3 Lifecycle Stages
Although the paper claims a "holistic" view, it necessarily omits several Scope 3 (indirect) emissions sources that are difficult to quantify without proprietary access across multiple industries.

*   **Transportation and Logistics:** The carbon emissions associated with shipping hardware from manufacturers (likely in Asia) to the data centers in Texas and Iowa, as well as the transport of personnel and physical media, are not included.
*   **Data Center Construction:** The embodied carbon of building the data center facilities themselves (concrete, steel, land use changes) is excluded. The analysis focuses only on the hardware *inside* the data center.
*   **End-of-Life Disposal:** The environmental impact of recycling or disposing of the GPUs after their 4-year lifespan is not accounted for. Given the toxicity of electronic waste and the energy required for recycling processes, this represents a missing "downstream" cost.
*   **Inference Overhead Simplifications:** In the inference simulations (Section 3.4 and Table 3), the authors measure only the GPU power consumed during active processing. They explicitly exclude:
    *   **Idle Power:** The energy required to keep the server running, holding the model in VRAM, and listening for network requests when no inference is happening.
    *   **CPU and RAM Overhead:** The power drawn by the host CPU, system memory, and networking equipment during inference.
    *   **Implication:** The authors acknowledge in Table 3 that their inference numbers are **lower bounds**. In a real-world production environment where servers run 24/7 regardless of traffic, the actual energy cost per request would be higher, meaning the "breakeven points" (where inference costs exceed training costs) would be reached even faster than reported.

### 6.3 Scalability and Linearity Constraints
The experimental analysis is bounded by the scale of the models studied, raising questions about whether the observed trends hold for the largest frontier models.

*   **Model Size Ceiling:** The largest model evaluated is **13 billion parameters** (`OLMo 2 13B`). While this is substantial, it is orders of magnitude smaller than current frontier models (e.g., those with 100B+ or trillion-scale parameters).
*   **Non-Linearity Risks:** In Appendix A.2, the authors caution that they "do not necessarily expect that this trend [linear scaling of costs] would hold tightly in all training settings across all possible scales."
    *   **Communication Overhead:** At massive scales (tens of thousands of GPUs), the energy cost of inter-node communication (synchronization, gradient averaging) may grow non-linearly, potentially increasing the power fluctuation issues identified in Section 4.3.
    *   **Failure Rates:** Larger training runs are more prone to hardware failures and preemptions, which could increase the number of "wasted" compute hours (re-starting from checkpoints) beyond the development ratios observed in this study.
*   **Generalizability:** While the *methodology* (sub-second logging, amortization) is scalable, the specific *ratios* (e.g., development being 50% of training) might shift for massive models where a single training run is so expensive that teams might invest even more heavily in small-scale development to avoid failure, or conversely, where the sheer duration of the final run dwarfs all development costs.

### 6.4 Specificity of Infrastructure and Geography
The results are heavily anchored to the specific infrastructure used by the Allen Institute for AI, which may not represent the global average.

*   **Cooling Efficiency:** The Jupiter cluster uses a **closed-loop cooling system** with no evaporative loss, leading to an assumed $WUE_{onsite}$ of **0 L/kWh**. Many data centers, particularly in arid regions, rely on evaporative cooling towers, which consume significant amounts of water onsite. Applying this paper's water metrics to such facilities would drastically **underestimate** their true water footprint.
*   **Grid Mix Variability:** The study uses specific carbon intensity values for Texas (0.332 kg/kWh) and Iowa (0.352 kg/kWh). While this demonstrates the importance of location, it limits the direct applicability of the absolute numbers to models trained in regions with vastly different grids (e.g., coal-heavy regions in Asia or hydro-dominated regions in Scandinavia). The *method* of using regional factors is robust, but the *results* are location-specific.

### 6.5 Simulation vs. Real-World Deployment
The inference analysis (Section 4.2) is a simulation, not an observation of live user traffic.

*   **Workload Simplification:** The simulation uses a static dataset (ShareGPT) with fixed prompt lengths and a Poisson distribution for request arrival. Real-world usage involves highly variable context lengths, complex multi-turn conversations, and bursty traffic patterns that may stress the hardware differently.
*   **Optimization Variance:** The simulation uses a specific serving engine (SGLang) with default settings. In practice, deployers use various optimizations (quantization, speculative decoding, distinct batching strategies) that could significantly alter the energy-per-token metric. The paper captures a "representative" scenario but cannot account for the full spectrum of deployment efficiencies or inefficiencies found in the wild.

### 6.6 Open Questions and Future Directions
The limitations of this study highlight several critical open questions for the field:
1.  **Hardware Transparency:** How can the community compel or incentivize hardware manufacturers to release verified Lifecycle Assessment (LCA) data? Without this, embodied impact calculations will remain speculative.
2.  **Dynamic Grid Interaction:** Given the observed power fluctuations (15%–85%), what specific engineering solutions (e.g., battery buffering, staggered checkpointing across clusters) are most effective at mitigating grid instability, and what is *their* environmental cost?
3.  **Water-Carbon Trade-offs:** As shown by the difference between the Texas and Iowa clusters, low-carbon grids are not always low-water. How should organizations optimize site selection when carbon and water goals conflict?
4.  **Scaling Laws for Waste:** Does the ratio of development-to-training cost increase or decrease as models scale to trillions of parameters? Understanding this trend is vital for predicting the future environmental trajectory of AI.

In summary, while this paper provides a rigorous lower-bound estimate and a superior methodological framework compared to prior work, the true environmental cost of LLMs is likely **higher** than reported due to unmeasured Scope 3 factors, idle inference overhead, and potential underestimates in hardware manufacturing impacts. The value of the work lies not just in the specific numbers reported, but in exposing the structural blind spots that have previously allowed the industry to under-report its footprint.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than quantify the carbon footprint of a specific set of models; it fundamentally alters the baseline for how the AI community must approach environmental accountability. By exposing the "hidden" costs of development and the dynamic instability of training loads, the work shifts the conversation from static efficiency metrics to a holistic, lifecycle-aware framework. The implications extend across research methodology, infrastructure engineering, public policy, and corporate strategy.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Research Landscape: From Survivorship to Holistic Accounting

The most immediate impact of this work is the dismantling of **survivorship bias** in environmental reporting. Prior to this study, the standard practice was to report the energy cost of the single successful training run, effectively hiding the ecological waste of the iterative scientific process.

*   **Redefining "Efficiency":** The finding that development costs account for **~50%** of total emissions (Section 4.1) forces a redefinition of what it means to be an "efficient" model. A model that requires fewer tokens to train but demands extensive hyperparameter searching may have a *higher* total footprint than a slightly larger model trained with a robust, pre-validated recipe. Future research must now optimize for **total project emissions** ($E_{dev} + E_{train}$) rather than just training efficiency ($E_{train}$).
*   **Methodological Shift:** This work establishes a new standard for reproducibility. Just as papers now routinely report FLOPs or parameter counts, future publications in top-tier venues (like ICLR or NeurIPS) will likely be expected to disclose their **development-to-training ratio**. Researchers who fail to report the number of failed experiments or scaling law runs will be viewed as providing incomplete data, similar to omitting validation set results today.
*   **Incentivizing Better Search Strategies:** The high cost of development creates a strong economic and ethical incentive to invest in **compute-efficient search methods**. Techniques like **scaling laws** (predicting large model performance from small runs), **parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT)**, and **neural architecture search (NAS)** that minimize trial-and-error will transition from niche optimization tools to essential environmental safeguards.

### 7.2 Engineering Frontiers: Grid-Aware Training and Hardware Design

The discovery of severe power fluctuations (dropping from **~85% to ~15%** utilization during checkpointing, Section 4.3) opens a new frontier in systems engineering: **Grid-Aware AI**.

*   **Smoothing Demand Curves:** The paper identifies checkpointing as a primary driver of grid instability. This suggests an urgent need for **staggered checkpointing protocols**, where nodes in a large cluster save their state at different times to flatten the aggregate power curve. Future systems research will likely focus on **asynchronous checkpointing** algorithms that decouple model state saving from global synchronization barriers, ensuring the cluster maintains a steady power draw.
*   **Dynamic Power Management:** The observation that GPUs sit at low utilization during I/O waits implies that current power management policies are too static. Future hardware firmware and drivers could implement **predictive power scaling**, rapidly throttling voltage and frequency during known low-compute phases (like checkpointing or data loading) to reduce stress on the grid without impacting training time.
*   **Hardware Transparency Mandates:** The reliance on external estimates for embodied carbon (Section 5.1) highlights a critical supply chain opacity. This work provides the empirical evidence needed to advocate for **mandatory Lifecycle Assessment (LCA) disclosures** from hardware vendors like NVIDIA and TSMC. Just as food labels require nutritional information, future procurement policies for AI labs may require "carbon labels" for GPUs, detailing the emissions and water usage incurred during manufacturing.

### 7.3 Policy and Corporate Strategy: Beyond Carbon Neutrality

For policymakers and corporate leaders, the granular data on water usage and inference breakeven points offers a roadmap for more effective regulation and strategic planning.

*   **Multi-Objective Site Selection:** The stark difference in Water Usage Effectiveness (WUE) between the Texas (**1.29 L/kWh**) and Iowa (**3.10 L/kWh**) clusters demonstrates that low-carbon grids are not automatically low-water. Policymakers promoting AI hubs must consider **water stress** alongside carbon intensity. Future zoning laws or incentives for data centers may require a dual-metric assessment, preventing the displacement of carbon problems with water scarcity issues.
*   **Regulating Scope 3 Emissions:** The inference breakeven analysis (Table 3) shows that for popular models, downstream usage emissions can surpass training emissions in weeks. This supports the argument for **Scope 3 reporting mandates** (e.g., under the EU AI Act or proposed US legislation). Regulators may soon require model providers to not only report training costs but also to implement and disclose **inference efficiency standards** (e.g., mandatory quantization or batching limits) for high-volume APIs.
*   **The "Jevons Paradox" Warning:** Corporate strategies focusing solely on making models cheaper to run may inadvertently increase total environmental harm by spurring massive usage volume. This paper suggests that sustainability goals should include **usage caps** or **efficiency taxes** for high-volume deployments, ensuring that efficiency gains lead to resource conservation rather than consumption explosions.

### 7.4 Enabling Follow-Up Research

This work serves as a foundational dataset and methodological template for several critical lines of future inquiry:

*   **Scaling Laws for Waste:** Researchers can now investigate the relationship between model scale and the **development-to-training cost ratio**. Does this 50% ratio hold for trillion-parameter models, or does the risk of failure make development even more dominant? Answering this is crucial for predicting the environmental trajectory of frontier AI.
*   **Grid-Interactive Data Centers:** The time-series power data enables simulations of how AI clusters interact with renewable energy sources. Future work could explore **load-shifting strategies**, where non-urgent training jobs or checkpointing operations are dynamically scheduled to coincide with peaks in solar or wind generation, turning AI clusters into grid assets rather than liabilities.
*   **Water-Carbon Trade-off Optimization:** With distinct metrics for both carbon and water, researchers can develop **multi-objective optimization algorithms** for job scheduling. For instance, a scheduler might choose to run a job in a region with higher carbon intensity but lower water stress during a drought, dynamically balancing ecological priorities based on real-time environmental conditions.

### 7.5 Practical Applications and Integration Guidance

For practitioners and organizations looking to apply these insights immediately, the paper offers clear guidance on when and how to adjust their workflows:

*   **When to Prefer Rigorous Development Tracking:**
    *   **Scenario:** You are running a research lab or an R&D division training multiple model variants.
    *   **Action:** Implement **per-experiment telemetry** (using tools like CodeCarbon or custom sub-second loggers) for *every* run, not just the final one.
    *   **Reasoning:** As shown in Table 1, ignoring the "failed" runs leads to a ~50% underestimation of your footprint. Use this data to identify inefficient search patterns (e.g., too many large-scale ablations) and shift towards smaller-scale proxy experiments.

*   **When to Prioritize Inference Optimization over Training:**
    *   **Scenario:** You are deploying a model expected to serve >1 million requests per month (e.g., a customer support bot or a consumer app).
    *   **Action:** Focus engineering resources on **inference efficiency** (quantization, distillation, efficient serving engines like SGLang) rather than squeezing out marginal gains in training loss.
    *   **Reasoning:** Table 3 demonstrates that for models of this scale, the "breakeven point" is reached quickly. The lifetime environmental cost is dominated by inference, making every watt saved during deployment far more impactful than watts saved during training.

*   **When to Re-evaluate Data Center Location:**
    *   **Scenario:** You are planning a new training cluster or selecting a cloud provider region.
    *   **Action:** Request specific **WUE (Water Usage Effectiveness)** and **PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness)** data for the specific availability zone, not just the regional carbon intensity.
    *   **Reasoning:** As evidenced by the Iowa vs. Texas comparison, a region with "green" energy might have a devastating water footprint due to its power generation mix (e.g., thermal cooling requirements). Optimize for the specific environmental constraint most critical to your local context (e.g., water stress in arid regions).

*   **Integration of Checkpointing Strategies:**
    *   **Scenario:** You are training large models (>7B parameters) on clusters with >100 GPUs.
    *   **Action:** Implement **staggered or asynchronous checkpointing** to avoid simultaneous power drops across all nodes.
    *   **Reasoning:** Figure 2 shows that synchronized checkpointing causes massive power swings (~70% drop). Smoothing this curve reduces strain on local infrastructure and potentially lowers the marginal carbon intensity of the electricity consumed by avoiding the need for fast-response peaker plants.

In conclusion, this paper transforms environmental impact from a vague ethical concern into a precise, measurable engineering constraint. It provides the tools and evidence necessary to move from "greenwashing" via selective reporting to genuine sustainability through holistic optimization. The path forward requires a collective shift: researchers must account for their failures, engineers must smooth their power curves, and policymakers must regulate the full lifecycle of AI systems.
