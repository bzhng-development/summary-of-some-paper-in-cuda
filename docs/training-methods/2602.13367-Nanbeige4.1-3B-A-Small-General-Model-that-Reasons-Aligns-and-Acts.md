## 1. Executive Summary

The paper presents `Nanbeige4.1-3B`, a unified 3B-parameter generalist model that overcomes the typical capability fragmentation in small language models (SLMs) by simultaneously achieving strong agentic behavior, code generation, and general reasoning through a specialized training recipe combining point-wise and pair-wise reward modeling. Demonstrating that compact models can rival much larger systems, `Nanbeige4.1-3B` outperforms similar-scale models like `Qwen3-4B` on benchmarks such as LiveCodeBench-V6 (76.9 vs. 57.4) and achieves deep-search capabilities comparable to 100B+ models by reliably executing up to 600 tool-call turns. This work redefines the potential of 3B parameter models, proving that strict capacity constraints need not compromise broad competence when training objectives and credit assignment are properly aligned for reasoning, coding, and long-horizon tasks.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of `Nanbeige4.1-3B`, we must first recognize the prevailing landscape of Small Language Models (SLMs). While the industry has largely focused on scaling parameters to achieve general intelligence (e.g., models with 70B or 100B+ parameters), there is a critical demand for compact models that can operate efficiently under strict resource constraints. However, this efficiency usually comes at the cost of capability fragmentation. This paper addresses the fundamental gap between **specialization** and **generalization** at the 3B parameter scale.

### 2.1 The Problem: Capability Fragmentation in SLMs

The core problem this paper tackles is the **fragmentation of capabilities** in existing small language models. As noted in the Introduction (Section 1), most current SLMs are optimized for specific domains rather than general utility.

*   **Reasoning-Focused Models:** These models excel at mathematics or logic but often struggle with **long-horizon interactions**. In the context of this paper, "long-horizon" refers to tasks requiring many sequential steps, such as agentic deep search where the model must plan and execute hundreds of tool calls. Small models typically suffer from error accumulation over these long sequences, causing them to lose track of the goal.
*   **Agent/Code-Focused Models:** Conversely, models specialized for coding or tool use often lack robust **general reasoning abilities**. They may generate syntactically correct code but fail at creative writing, complex instruction following, or human preference alignment (Section 1, Paragraph 2).

This creates a practical dilemma for developers: to build a system that can code, reason, *and* act as an agent, one traditionally needs to ensemble multiple specialized models or rely on a much larger generalist model. The paper poses a specific research question to address this:

> "How far can a 3B model be pushed as a generalist without compromising its existing strengths?" (Section 1, Paragraph 2)

### 2.2 Why This Problem Matters

The motivation for solving this fragmentation is twofold: practical deployment efficiency and theoretical understanding of model capacity.

1.  **Efficiency and Accessibility:** A 3B parameter model is significantly lighter than the 30B–80B models it competes with in performance. Reducing the parameter count by an order of magnitude (e.g., from 30B to 3B) drastically lowers inference costs and memory requirements, enabling deployment on edge devices or in high-throughput environments where large models are prohibitively expensive.
2.  **Theoretical Significance:** There is a prevailing assumption that "generalist" behavior requires massive capacity. If a 3B model can perform deep search and complex reasoning comparable to 100B+ models (as claimed in the Introduction, Paragraph 4), it challenges the notion that scale is the only path to capability. It suggests that **training objectives and credit assignment** (how the model learns from rewards) are equally critical levers for performance.

### 2.3 Limitations of Prior Approaches

To appreciate the contribution of `Nanbeige4.1-3B`, we must examine where prior approaches fall short. The paper positions itself against three categories of existing work:

*   **Previous Generation SLMs (e.g., `Nanbeige4-3B-2511`):** The authors' own predecessor model demonstrated strong capabilities but lacked the stability for unified tasks. For instance, prior versions suffered from repetition and redundant thinking in reasoning tasks (Section 2.1.2). They also lacked the specialized reward structures needed for efficient code generation or long-horizon planning.
*   **Larger Generalist Models (e.g., `Qwen3-30B`, `Qwen3-80B`):** While these models possess the capacity for generalist behavior, they are computationally heavy. The paper notes that existing small models with tools (like `Qwen3-4B`) "fail to sustain meaningful exploration beyond a few turns" (Section 1, Paragraph 4). They lack the training signals required to maintain coherence over hundreds of steps.
*   **Specialized Search Agents (e.g., `Tongyi-DeepResearch-30B`):** These models are optimized specifically for deep search tasks. While effective, they are not generalists—they may not perform well on standard coding or alignment benchmarks. Furthermore, many are closed-source or significantly larger (30B+ parameters), limiting their adaptability.

A key technical limitation in prior Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) approaches is the reliance on a single type of reward signal. Standard pipelines often use either **point-wise rewards** (scoring a single response on quality) or **pair-wise rewards** (comparing two responses to see which is better). The paper argues that using only one limits the model: point-wise rewards stabilize training but lack nuanced preference data, while pair-wise rewards provide strong preference signals but require high-quality comparison data which is scarce (Section 2.1.3).

### 2.4 Positioning of This Work

`Nanbeige4.1-3B` positions itself as the **first open-source small language model to achieve versatility in a single model** (Abstract). It does not merely claim to be a better 3B model; it claims to redefine the ceiling of what a 3B model can do by acting as a **unified generalist**.

The paper distinguishes its approach through a **cascaded training strategy** that integrates heterogeneous objectives:

*   **Unified Optimization:** Instead of training separate heads or models for code, reasoning, and agents, the authors employ a single model optimized through mixed data and multi-stage Reinforcement Learning (RL).
*   **Hybrid Reward Modeling:** The work combines point-wise and pair-wise reward modeling to ensure responses are both high-quality in isolation and preferred in comparison (Section 2.1).
*   **Complexity-Aware Coding:** Unlike standard code RL which optimizes only for correctness (pass/fail), this work introduces **complexity-aware rewards**. The model is penalized or rewarded based on the time complexity of its solution, encouraging algorithmic efficiency, not just functional correctness (Section 2.3).
*   **Long-Horizon Stability:** By incorporating turn-level supervision and trajectory-level rewards, the model is designed to handle up to 600 tool-call turns, a regime where typical 3B models fail (Abstract).

In summary, the paper positions `Nanbeige4.1-3B` not just as an incremental improvement, but as a proof-of-concept that **capacity constraints can be overcome via objective alignment**. It bridges the gap between specialized agents and generalist reasoning models within a compact 3B parameter budget, challenging the necessity of massive scale for complex, multi-step tasks.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a **training methodology paper** that presents a cascaded post-training pipeline for small language models, with the core idea being that heterogeneous capabilities (reasoning, coding, agency) can be unified in a 3B parameter model through carefully staged reinforcement learning with domain-specific reward structures.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The *system* being built is a 3-billion parameter language model trained through a multi-stage pipeline that progressively teaches it general reasoning, code generation, and long-horizon agent behavior using different types of reward signals at each stage. The solution addresses capability fragmentation by **sequentially layering training objectives** rather than trying to optimize everything simultaneously, which would overwhelm the model's limited capacity.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The training pipeline consists of five major components arranged in a cascade: (1) **Extended SFT** takes the base model and exposes it to mixed-domain data at 256k context length to establish foundational capabilities; (2) **Point-wise RL** uses a general reward model to score individual responses and suppress repetition and formatting errors; (3) **Pair-wise RL** uses comparison-based rewards to refine preference alignment across multi-turn conversations; (4) **Code RL** operates in two stages (correctness then efficiency) with a gated time-complexity reward that only activates when solutions pass all test cases; (5) **Agentic RL** applies turn-level and trajectory-level supervision to enable stable tool-use over hundreds of steps. Information flows sequentially through these stages, with each stage's checkpoint serving as the initialization for the next.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **SFT Foundation**: We start with supervised fine-tuning because it establishes the model's basic capability distribution and context handling before any reinforcement learning begins.
- **General RL (Point-wise → Pair-wise)**: We explain these two stages together because they form a complementary pair—point-wise stabilizes output quality while pair-wise sharpens preference boundaries.
- **Deep Search Data Pipeline**: We cover this before Code RL because the agentic training data construction (Wiki-graph walks, turn-level judgment) introduces concepts used later in tool-use optimization.
- **Code RL with Gated Rewards**: We detail this last among training stages because it builds on the General RL checkpoint and introduces the most complex reward mechanism (time-complexity gating).
- **Integration Strategy**: We conclude with how all objectives are balanced through data mixing and staging, which explains why the model does not catastrophically forget earlier capabilities.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### 3.4.1 Extended Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) Foundation

The training pipeline begins with **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)**, which is the process of training a pre-trained base model on curated instruction-response pairs to establish task-specific behaviors before reinforcement learning begins. `Nanbeige4.1-3B` is initialized from `Nanbeige4-3B-Base` and undergoes an enhanced SFT recipe that differs from the previous `Nanbeige4-3B-2511` version in three critical dimensions: data distribution, context length, and solution quality refinement.

**Data Mixture Redesign.** The authors increase the proportion of code-related data and introduce a higher ratio of challenging problems in mathematics and general domains compared to the previous version, which encourages stronger reasoning depth and improves robustness on difficult benchmarks (Section 2.1.1, Paragraph 2). This is a deliberate shift away from easy, high-volume data toward harder, lower-volume examples that force the model to develop deeper reasoning chains rather than memorizing surface patterns.

**Context Length Extension.** The training employs a three-stage curriculum for context length: starting at 32k tokens, extending to 64k tokens, and finally reaching **256k tokens** in the final stage (Section 2.1.1, Paragraph 3). This progressive scaling is necessary because long-horizon reasoning and multi-turn deep-search scenarios require the model to maintain coherence over hundreds of thousands of tokens, and jumping directly to 256k would cause training instability. In the final 256k stage, the data mixture is specifically designed to strengthen agentic and reasoning capabilities with the following composition: **code (27%), deep-Search (26%), STEM (23%), tool-use (13%), and general domains (10%)** (Section 2.1.1, Paragraph 3). This distribution reflects the paper's priority: code and search together comprise over half the training signal, while general conversation is deliberately minimized to preserve capacity for complex tasks.

**Solution Refinement and CoT Reconstruction.** The authors scale up the **Solution Refinement** loop, which is a critique–revision cycle where the model generates an initial answer, receives feedback on errors, and produces a revised solution (Section 2.1.1, Paragraph 4). By increasing the number of refinement iterations, the final solutions exhibit higher quality because errors are caught and corrected before the model sees them as training data. Additionally, a more capable **Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Reconstruction** model is trained to generate cleaner and more faithful reasoning traces from refined answers, ensuring that the model learns not just correct outputs but also the reasoning process that leads to them. Table 1 shows the impact of these SFT improvements: on LiveCodeBench V6, scores increase from 45.5 to 62.0 (+16.5), and on Arena-Hard V2, scores increase from 45.5 to 60.2 (+14.7), demonstrating that the enhanced SFT recipe lays a stronger foundation for subsequent reinforcement learning stages.

#### 3.4.2 General Reinforcement Learning: Point-wise and Pair-wise Stages

After SFT, the model undergoes two sequential reinforcement learning stages designed to improve response quality and preference alignment. **Reinforcement Learning (RL)** in this context means the model generates multiple candidate responses (rollouts) for each prompt, receives a reward signal based on quality, and updates its parameters to maximize expected reward.

**Point-wise RL: Stabilizing Output Quality.** The first RL stage uses **point-wise rewards**, which means each response is scored independently on an absolute scale rather than being compared to other responses (Section 2.1.2, Paragraph 2). The authors train a general reward model on curated large-scale human preference data following prior work [15], and this reward model naturally suppresses overly redundant, repetitive, and low-readability answers. They then perform **GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization)** to optimize the model, sampling **8 rollouts per prompt** and using the general reward model to score each response as the training signal (Section 2.1.2, Paragraph 2). GRPO is chosen over standard PPO because it does not require a separate critic model, reducing memory overhead—critical for a 3B parameter model operating under capacity constraints.

The impact of point-wise RL is measurable in both quality and stability metrics. On LiveCodeBench-v6, overlong truncation (where responses exceed context limits and get cut off) drops from **5.27% to 0.38%** (Section 2.1.2, Paragraph 3), indicating that the model learns to produce appropriately-lengthed responses. Arena-Hard V2 scores improve from 60.2 to 66.6 (+6.4), with the hard-prompt subset improving from 46.1 to 54.1 (Section 2.1.2, Paragraph 3). These gains reflect more consistent, well-structured outputs and high-quality code presentation, addressing the repetition and redundant thinking issues observed in the SFT checkpoint.

**Pair-wise RL: Sharpening Preference Boundaries.** The second RL stage uses **pair-wise rewards**, which means the model is trained on comparisons between two responses rather than absolute scores (Section 2.1.3, Paragraph 2). This approach is introduced because high-quality point-wise preference data is limited, constraining further improvement—pair-wise comparisons can be generated more abundantly by comparing strong and weak model outputs. The authors train a pair-wise reward model on paired comparison data spanning code generation and LMArena-style conversations (single-turn and multi-turn), generating response pairs with a strong model and a weak model, then applying checklist filtering to derive reliable win–loss labels [21].

A critical technical detail is the **swap-consistency regularizer**, which mitigates position bias in pair-wise scoring (Section 2.1.3, Paragraph 3). Position bias occurs when the reward model systematically favors responses in a particular position (e.g., always preferring the first response). The regularizer is defined as the mean squared error between the predicted reward difference for a response pair and the negated reward difference for the swapped pair. In mathematical terms, if $r(A, B)$ is the predicted reward difference when response A is in position 1 and B is in position 2, then the regularizer penalizes deviation from:

$$r(A, B) = -r(B, A)$$

This ensures the reward model evaluates responses based on content rather than position. For multi-turn scenarios, the full dialogue history is concatenated into the pair-wise reward model's input, allowing the model to learn from contextual information across turns (Section 2.1.3, Paragraph 4).

Table 2 shows the cumulative improvements through both RL stages. After pair-wise RL, Arena-Hard V2 reaches 73.8 (from 66.6 after point-wise, +7.2 gain), and Multi-Challenge reaches 55.1 (from 47.7, +7.4 gain) (Section 2.1.3, Paragraph 5). Notably, LiveCodeBench V6 slightly decreases from 66.0 to 65.6 (-0.4), which the authors acknowledge but consider acceptable given the substantial alignment gains—this reflects a deliberate trade-off where general reasoning and alignment are prioritized over marginal coding improvements at this stage. The results confirm that pair-wise RL sharpens preference boundaries, providing the informative supervision signals needed for overall improvement across various benchmarks.

#### 3.4.3 Deep Search Data Construction and Training

**Deep search** is defined in this paper as a retrieval-centric task that operates under complex multi-hop reasoning and extremely long-context settings, where models iteratively interact with the environment to acquire information and solve challenging search problems (Section 2.2, Paragraph 1). This capability is rare in 3B models because error accumulation over many tool-call turns typically causes the model to lose track of the original goal.

**Data Construction Pipeline.** The authors construct a large-scale, complex search dataset through a multi-step pipeline illustrated in Figure 2 (Section 2.2.1). The first step is **Temporal-Aware Head Entity Selection**, where informative head entities from Wikipedia that have been updated within the **past six months** are extracted (Section 2.2.1, Paragraph 2). This temporal constraint ensures the synthesized questions require up-to-date knowledge rather than static facts that might be memorized during pre-training. Following the framework of [20], an entity-relation graph is constructed and **conditional random walks** are performed to extract relational paths of a predefined length. These chains, along with their detailed temporal contexts, are fed into a robust LLM to synthesize intricate questions that require multi-hop reasoning to answer.

The second step is **Trajectory Synthesis and Turn-level Judgment** (Section 2.2.1, Paragraph 3). Multiple agent frameworks are employed to address the generated queries, sampling a diverse set of reasoning paths that are subsequently mapped into multi-turn tool-invocation sequences from a unified agent perspective. To guarantee data quality, a **turn-level judgment mechanism** is implemented: a critic model evaluates each step of the interaction based on three dimensions—**logical soundness, tool-call accuracy, and informational gain** (Section 2.2.1, Paragraph 3). Any turn that fails to meet these criteria does not participate in model training or provides a negative reward for the model. This fine-grained filtering ensures that the final trajectories provide a high-fidelity signal for supervised fine-tuning and preference alignment, preventing the model from learning from erroneous or suboptimal tool-use sequences.

**Preliminary Performance Validation.** To validate the data construction pipeline, the authors conduct a controlled experiment using `Nanbeige4-3B-2511` as the base model, training exclusively on the synthetic multi-hop QA and search trajectories while excluding other open-source data (Section 2.2.2, Paragraph 2). The model is evaluated within the **Mindflow framework** [2], employing a suite of tools: **Serper** for environment search, **Jina** for webpage content extraction, and **E2B Sandbox** as the secure sandbox environment (Section 2.2.2, Paragraph 3). Table 3 shows dramatic improvements: on xBench-DeepSearch-2505, scores jump from 33.0 to 76.0 (+43.0), and on GAIA (text-only), scores increase from 19.4 to 58.3 (+38.9) (Section 2.2.2, Paragraph 4). These results demonstrate that the data synthesis pipeline effectively endows the model with robust multi-hop reasoning and long-context search abilities, even before the full training pipeline is applied.

**Long-Horizon Stability.** A key claim in the Abstract is that `Nanbeige4.1-3B` can reliably execute up to **600 tool-call turns** for complex problem-solving (Abstract, Paragraph 3). This is achieved by incorporating both **turn-level supervision** (rewarding or penalizing individual tool calls based on the critic model's evaluation) and **trajectory-level rewards** (rewarding the overall success of the multi-turn interaction) during training (Section 1, Paragraph 3). This dual-level credit assignment ensures the model receives feedback not just on whether it eventually solves the problem, but also on whether each intermediate step contributes meaningfully toward the solution—preventing the model from learning to "get lucky" through random exploration.

#### 3.4.4 Code RL with Gated Time-Complexity Rewards

The coding capability optimization introduces the most novel reward mechanism in the paper: a **gated time-complexity reward** that encourages algorithmic efficiency, not just functional correctness (Section 2.3). Standard code RL typically optimizes only for pass/fail correctness, which can produce solutions that work but are computationally inefficient (e.g., $O(N^2)$ when $O(N)$ is possible).

**Judge System Architecture.** The authors build a unified judge system shared across SFT data construction, RL data construction, and subsequent RL training and evaluation (Section 2.3.1, Paragraph 1). This system combines two components: (1) a **multi-language sandbox** for execution-based correctness checking, which runs the generated code against test cases and returns pass/fail results; and (2) a dedicated **instruct judge model** for time-complexity comparison, which is specifically trained for fast complexity assessment in RL settings (Section 2.3.1, Paragraph 1). The instruct model enables efficient online comparison between the predicted time complexity of model-generated solutions and the reference optimal bound, which is critical because running every solution to measure actual runtime would be prohibitively slow during training.

**SFT Data Construction for Code.** During SFT data generation, multiple candidate solutions are sampled per problem and verified by the judge system (Section 2.3.2, Paragraph 2). The system assesses solution quality from two aspects: **functional correctness** by executing the program in a sandbox, and **time complexity** by combining execution signals with model-based complexity analysis. Only candidates judged to be time-optimal (or among the best complexity class) for the given problem are retained for training. This ensures the model learns from high-quality examples from the outset, rather than learning to produce inefficient solutions and then unlearning them during RL.

**RL Data Construction with On-Policy Filtering.** Each RL sample contains a problem statement, test cases, a time-complexity-optimal solution, and the corresponding optimal complexity label (Section 2.3.2, Paragraph 3). The optimal solution and complexity are obtained by prompting multiple strong LLMs and employing a strong LLM to synthesize the candidates into a single best solution, which are then used as supervision signals for reward shaping and difficulty control. **On-policy filtering** is performed by running multiple rollouts per problem ($n = 8$) and selecting samples based on how many rollouts meet a stage-specific criterion (Section 2.3.2, Paragraph 4). In Stage 1, a difficulty-based criterion is used: a problem is retained if the policy can solve it in a moderate number of rollouts (problems with $k \in [1, 5]$ successful solves out of 8 are kept). In Stage 2, a complexity-based criterion is used: problems are retained if $k \in [1, 5]$ rollouts produce solutions whose estimated time complexity satisfies the target bound out of 8. This filtering ensures the model trains on problems that are neither too easy (solved by all rollouts) nor too hard (solved by no rollouts), maintaining an optimal learning signal.

**Two-Stage Code RL Process.** Starting from the General-RL checkpoint, two stages of code RL are conducted (Section 2.3.3, Paragraph 1). **Stage 1** optimizes for solution correctness using a **pass-rate reward**, defined as the fraction of test cases passed for each problem. **Stage 2** introduces the time-complexity reward, but only when all test cases are passed—otherwise the reward reduces to correctness-only signals. This gating mechanism is illustrated in Figure 3 and defined mathematically as:

$$
R = 
\begin{cases}
R_{\text{format}} + R_{\text{correctness}}, & \text{PassRate} < 1, \\
R_{\text{format}} + R_{\text{correctness}} + R_{\text{time}}, & \text{PassRate} = 1.
\end{cases}
$$

where $R_{\text{format}}$ penalizes formatting errors, $R_{\text{correctness}}$ rewards passing test cases, and $R_{\text{time}}$ rewards matching the reference optimal time complexity (Section 2.3.3, Paragraph 2). The judge system provides online feedback by comparing the model's output against the reference optimal complexity and checking whether the generated solution matches the reference optimal solution when applicable (Section 2.3.3, Paragraph 2).

**Training Dynamics.** Figure 4 illustrates the training dynamics across the two-stage code RL (Section 2.3.4, Paragraph 2). In Stage 1, the correctness reward ($R_{\text{correctness}}$) increases sharply, reflecting rapid gains in producing valid and correct solutions. In Stage 2, $R_{\text{correctness}}$ improves more modestly, while the gated time reward ($R_{\text{time}}$) rises substantially, indicating that the policy is indeed optimizing time complexity once correctness is largely achieved (Section 2.3.4, Paragraph 2). This sequential optimization is critical: if time-complexity rewards were introduced from the start, the model might sacrifice correctness for efficiency, learning to produce fast but wrong solutions. The gating ensures correctness is mastered first, then efficiency is layered on top.

**Case Study Evidence.** Appendix C presents three case studies comparing model outputs before vs. after the time-reward stage (Section 2.3.3, Paragraph 3). In the "Simultaneous Kagamimochi" problem (Appendix C.1), the before solution uses binary search over the answer with $O(N \log N)$ complexity, while the after solution uses two-pointers greedy matching with $O(N)$ complexity (Appendix C.1.1, Conclusion). In the "Twice Subsequence" problem (Appendix C.2), the before solution uses a value-to-positions map with binary search at $O(N + M \log N)$, while the after solution uses two-pointer greedy scans at $O(N)$ (Appendix C.2.1, Conclusion). In the "Minimum Cost Sort" problem (Appendix C.3), the before solution uses heap-driven local bubble swaps at $O(N^2 \log N)$ worst-case, while the after solution uses a Fenwick tree (Binary Indexed Tree) at $O(N \log N)$ (Appendix C.3.1, Conclusion). These case studies provide concrete evidence that the time-complexity reward successfully shifts the model toward more efficient algorithms, not just correct ones.

#### 3.4.5 Whole Training Recipe Integration

The complete training pipeline for `Nanbeige4.1-3B` integrates all the above components through careful staging and data mixing (Section 2.4). The model is initialized from `Nanbeige4-3B-Base` and optimized through a structured post-training pipeline combining large-scale SFT and cascaded RL.

**Sequential Staging.** The RL phase adopts a staged optimization strategy (Section 2.4, Paragraph 3). **General RL** is performed sequentially with point-wise RL followed by pair-wise RL to enhance both standalone response quality and comparative preference alignment. **Code RL** is then conducted in two stages: a correctness stage that maximizes execution pass rate, followed by a gated time-complexity stage that activates efficiency rewards only when full correctness is achieved. Finally, a **lightweight agentic RL stage** is applied to strengthen tool-use and search behavior. This sequential approach prevents **catastrophic forgetting**, where optimizing for one capability degrades another—by staging objectives, the model consolidates each capability before moving to the next.

**Context Length and Data Mixing.** The extended SFT increases the maximum context length from 64K to **256K** compared to the previous `Nanbeige4-3B-2511` version (Section 2.4, Paragraph 2). This longer context window is essential for supporting long-horizon reasoning and multi-turn deep-search planning. Throughout training, careful **SFT data mixing** and **multi-stage reinforcement learning** [15] are employed to maintain balance across domains (Section 1, Paragraph 3). The final 256k SFT stage data mixture (code 27%, deep-Search 26%, STEM 23%, tool-use 13%, general 10%) reflects the priority ordering: code and search dominate because they are the most capacity-intensive capabilities, while general conversation is minimized to preserve parameters for complex reasoning.

**Design Rationale.** The unified training recipe enables `Nanbeige4.1-3B` to maintain strong domain-specific performance while emerging as a well-balanced generalist model under strict capacity constraints (Section 2.4, Paragraph 4). The key design choices are: (1) **Sequential rather than simultaneous optimization** to prevent objective conflict; (2) **Gated rewards** to ensure foundational capabilities (correctness) are mastered before advanced capabilities (efficiency); (3) **Turn-level and trajectory-level supervision** for agentic tasks to provide dense feedback signals over long horizons; and (4) **Hybrid point-wise and pair-wise RL** to combine the stability of absolute scoring with the nuance of comparative preference. These choices collectively address the fragmentation problem identified in Section 2.1, enabling a 3B model to achieve capabilities previously thought to require 30B+ parameters.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the core conceptual breakthroughs of `Nanbeige4.1-3B`. While the previous section detailed *how* the model was trained, here we analyze *why* these design choices represent fundamental shifts in small model development. We distinguish between incremental improvements (e.g., better data cleaning) and fundamental innovations (e.g., new objective functions) that enable a 3B model to perform tasks typically reserved for models ten times its size.

### 4.1 Sequential Hybrid Reward Modeling as a Stability Mechanism

A primary innovation in this work is the **sequential combination of point-wise and pair-wise reinforcement learning (RL)**. Prior RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) pipelines typically select one reward structure: either point-wise (scoring a single response) or pair-wise (comparing two responses). This paper demonstrates that these are not interchangeable alternatives but **complementary stages** that solve distinct failure modes.

*   **The Stability Problem:** Pure pair-wise RL often suffers from instability when the base model produces low-quality outputs. If both compared responses are poor (e.g., repetitive or malformed), the reward model struggles to provide a meaningful learning signal. The paper identifies that `Nanbeige4-3B-SFT` suffered from "repetition and redundant thinking" (Section 2.1.2).
*   **The Insight:** Point-wise RL acts as a **stabilizer** before pair-wise RL acts as a **refiner**. By first using a general reward model to suppress formatting errors and redundancy (point-wise), the model reaches a baseline quality where pair-wise comparisons become informative.
*   **Evidence of Necessity:** Table 2 shows that Point-wise RL alone improves Arena-Hard V2 from 60.2 to 66.6. However, adding Pair-wise RL on top pushes it to 73.8. Crucially, the Multi-Challenge benchmark sees a massive jump from 47.7 (Point-wise) to 55.1 (Pair-wise), indicating that pair-wise signals are required for nuanced multi-turn alignment, but only *after* point-wise training has stabilized the output distribution.

This sequential strategy challenges the common assumption that one reward type is sufficient. It suggests that for small models with limited capacity, **training stability must be engineered explicitly** before optimizing for preference nuance.

### 4.2 Gated Efficiency Optimization in Code Generation

The second major innovation is the **gated time-complexity reward** in Code RL. Standard code generation models optimize for **correctness** (Pass@k), meaning a solution is rewarded equally whether it runs in $O(N)$ or $O(N^2)$ time, as long as it passes test cases. `Nanbeige4.1-3B` introduces efficiency as a first-class objective, but with a critical constraint: **efficiency rewards are gated by correctness**.

*   **The Risk of Un gated Efficiency:** If a model is rewarded for speed without strict correctness gating, it may learn to produce "fast but wrong" code (e.g., skipping edge cases to reduce computation). This is a known failure mode in optimization-based coding agents.
*   **The Gating Mechanism:** As defined in Section 2.3.3 and illustrated in Figure 3, the time reward $R_{\text{time}}$ is activated *only* when $\text{PassRate} = 1$. Mathematically, the total reward $R$ is:
    $$
    R = 
    \begin{cases}
    R_{\text{format}} + R_{\text{correctness}}, & \text{PassRate} < 1 \\
    R_{\text{format}} + R_{\text{correctness}} + R_{\text{time}}, & \text{PassRate} = 1
    \end{cases}
    $$
    This ensures the model cannot "trade" correctness for efficiency. It must first master the solution space, then optimize within it.
*   **Algorithmic Shift:** Appendix C provides concrete evidence of this innovation's impact. In the "Minimum Cost Sort" problem (Appendix C.3), the model shifts from a heap-driven simulation ($O(N^2 \log N)$) to a Fenwick tree aggregation ($O(N \log N)$). In "Simultaneous Kagamimochi" (Appendix C.1), it moves from binary search ($O(N \log N)$) to two-pointer greedy matching ($O(N)$).

This is a fundamental shift from **functional correctness** to **algorithmic quality**. It demonstrates that small models can learn complex computational constraints if the reward signal explicitly penalizes inefficiency *after* correctness is secured.

### 4.3 Turn-Level Credit Assignment for Long-Horizon Agency

The third innovation addresses the **credit assignment problem** in long-horizon tasks. In deep search or agentic workflows, a model might take 100+ steps to solve a problem. Standard RL provides a **sparse reward** only at the end (success/failure). For a 3B model, this signal is too weak to learn which specific tool calls contributed to success.

*   **Dense Supervision:** The paper introduces **turn-level judgment** alongside trajectory-level rewards (Section 2.2.1). A critic model evaluates every single step based on logical soundness, tool-call accuracy, and informational gain.
*   **Why This Matters for Small Models:** Large models (e.g., 100B+) can often infer credit assignment through sheer capacity and attention mechanisms. Small models lack this latent capacity. By providing dense, step-by-step feedback, the training process explicitly teaches the model **planning hygiene**—how to make each individual step useful, not just how to finish the task.
*   **Performance Implication:** This design enables the model to reliably execute up to **600 tool-call turns** (Abstract). Table 5 shows the result: `Nanbeige4.1-3B` scores 75.00 on xBench-DeepSearch-05, surpassing specialized 30B research agents (e.g., Tongyi-DeepResearch-30B at 75.00, but note `Nanbeige` is 3B) and vastly outperforming small foundation models like `Qwen3-4B` (34.00).

This insight suggests that **long-horizon capability is not solely a function of context window size**, but of **feedback granularity**. A 256k context window (Section 2.1.1) is useless if the model cannot learn which parts of that context matter; turn-level supervision solves this learning bottleneck.

### 4.4 Decoupling Capability from Parameter Scale

The overarching theoretical contribution of this work is the empirical demonstration that **capability ceilings at the 3B scale are malleable**. The prevailing assumption in scaling laws is that performance correlates strongly with parameter count. While this holds for pre-training, this paper argues that **post-training objective alignment** can decouple performance from scale in specific domains.

*   **Cross-Scale Competitiveness:** Table 4 shows `Nanbeige4.1-3B` outperforming `Qwen3-30B-A3B` (10x larger) on LiveCodeBench-V6 (76.9 vs. 66.0) and Arena-Hard-V2 (73.2 vs. 60.2). Table 5 shows it matching or exceeding 100B+ models (e.g., DeepSeek-V3.2-671B) on specific deep search benchmarks like xBench-DeepSearch-05 (75.00 vs. 71.00).
*   **The "Unified Generalist" Claim:** Most models achieve these scores by specializing (e.g., a coding model vs. a reasoning model). `Nanbeige4.1-3B` achieves them simultaneously in a single checkpoint. This challenges the **fragmentation hypothesis** (Section 2.1), which posits that small models must specialize to compete.
*   **Limitations and Nuance:** It is important to note where the 3B model still lags. In Table 4, `Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B` still leads on some math benchmarks (AIME 2026 I: 89.24 vs. 87.40) and tool-use (BFCL-V4: 56.50 vs. 50.51). This indicates that while objective alignment can close the gap significantly, **raw capacity still matters for peak reasoning depth**. The innovation here is not that 3B models are "solved," but that the **efficiency frontier** has shifted dramatically upward.

This insight redefines the deployment landscape: for many practical applications (coding assistants, search agents), a carefully tuned 3B model may offer a better performance-to-cost ratio than a 30B model, provided the training objectives are aligned with the task structure (efficiency, long-horizon planning) rather than just next-token prediction.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their claims about `Nanbeige4.1-3B`'s capabilities. We will walk through the evaluation methodology, analyze the quantitative results across three distinct task categories, and critically assess whether the experiments support the paper's central thesis: that a 3B model can achieve unified generalist performance comparable to models ten times its size.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Experimental Setup

The authors employ a **three-tiered evaluation strategy** designed to test different dimensions of model capability. This structure is intentional: each tier isolates specific skills while collectively demonstrating the model's unified competence.

#### 5.1.1 General Task Benchmarks (Section 3.1)

The first tier measures **core reasoning and alignment capabilities** across five domains. This is the standard evaluation regime for general-purpose language models, allowing direct comparison with existing work.

**Datasets and Metrics:**

| Domain | Benchmarks | What It Measures |
|--------|------------|------------------|
| **Code** | LiveCodeBench-V5, LiveCodeBench-V6 [6], LiveCodeBench-Pro [22] | Execution-based correctness under increasing difficulty |
| **Mathematics** | IMO-Answer-Bench [8], HMMT Nov [1], AIME-2026-I | Symbolic reasoning and competition-level problem solving |
| **Science** | GPQA [13], HLE (text-only) [12] | Multi-step scientific reasoning and domain knowledge |
| **Alignment** | Arena-Hard-V2 [7], Multi-Challenge [4] | Preference modeling robustness under adversarial prompts |
| **Tool-Use** | BFCL-V4 [10], Tau2-Bench [2] | Function-calling reliability and multi-step tool use |

**Baseline Models:** The comparison set is carefully stratified to demonstrate cross-scale competitiveness:

- **Same-scale competitors:** `Qwen3-4B-2507` and the predecessor `Nanbeige4-3B-2511`
- **10× larger models:** `Qwen3-30B-A3B-2507` and `Qwen3-32B`
- **25× larger models:** `Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B`

This stratification allows the authors to claim both **same-scale dominance** and **cross-scale competitiveness**.

#### 5.1.2 Deep Search Benchmarks (Section 3.2)

The second tier evaluates **long-horizon agentic behavior**, which is the paper's most novel claim. Deep search tasks require iterative retrieval, planning, tool interaction, and cross-document reasoning over hundreds of steps.

**Experimental Setup (Section 2.2.2 and 3.2.1):**

- **Framework:** Mindflow framework[^2]
- **Tools:** Serper[^3] (environment search), Jina[^4] (webpage content extraction), E2B Sandbox[^5] (secure execution)
- **Constraint:** HuggingFace access explicitly disabled to prevent cheating via model lookup
- **Evaluation Mode:** Text-only subsets for HLE and GAIA to ensure fair comparison

**Baseline Categories:**

1. **Research Agents:** Specialized search models (e.g., `Tongyi-DeepResearch-30B`, `MiroThinker-v1.0-8B`)
2. **Large Foundation Models with Tools:** 100B+ parameter models with tool access (e.g., `DeepSeek-V3.2-671B`, `GLM-4.6-357B`)
3. **Small Foundation Models with Tools:** Qwen series from 4B to 80B with tool capabilities

This categorization is critical: it allows the authors to show that `Nanbeige4.1-3B` outperforms specialized agents *and* matches large generalists, despite being 3B parameters.

#### 5.1.3 Real-World Algorithmic Challenges (Section 3.3)

The third tier provides an **out-of-distribution stress test** using live competitive programming data. This is methodologically significant because:

- **Contamination Risk:** Academic benchmarks may be present in training data, inflating scores
- **Practical Relevance:** LeetCode problems represent real-world coding interviews and competitive programming
- **Dynamic Difficulty:** Weekly contests introduce novel problems not seen during training

**Evaluation Protocol:**

- **Contests:** LeetCode Weekly Contests 484–488
- **Mode:** Virtual participation with standard competitive programming settings
- **Metric:** Final acceptance rate (solutions submitted to official LeetCode platform)
- **Ranking:** Determined by correctness and time efficiency

This setup tests whether the model's coding capabilities generalize beyond curated benchmarks to genuinely novel problems.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

We now evaluate whether the experiments convincingly support the paper's central claims.

#### 5.4.1 Claim 1: "3B Model Matches 100B+ Models on Deep Search"

**Evidence:** Table 5 shows `Nanbeige4.1-3B` (75.00) matching `Tongyi-DeepResearch-30B` (75.00) and exceeding `DeepSeek-V3.2-671B` (71.00) on xBench-DeepSearch-05.

**Assessment:** **Strongly supported.** The 223× parameter ratio (3B vs. 671B) makes this result remarkable. However, two caveats apply:

1. **Benchmark Specificity:** The advantage is clearest on xBench-DeepSearch-05. On GAIA, the 30B research agent still leads slightly (70.90 vs. 69.90). This suggests the 3B model excels on certain search task formulations but may not universally dominate all deep search benchmarks.

2. **Tool Access Parity:** All models in Table 5 have access to the same tools (Serper, Jina, E2B). The comparison is fair, but it means the 3B model's advantage comes from **better tool coordination**, not better raw reasoning. This validates the paper's thesis about training objective alignment.

#### 5.4.2 Claim 2: "Unified Generalist Outperforms Specialized Models"

**Evidence:** Table 4 shows strong performance across code (76.9 LCB-V6), math (87.40 AIME), alignment (73.2 Arena-Hard), and tool-use (56.50 BFCL-V4) simultaneously.

**Assessment:** **Partially supported.** The model does achieve broad competence, but there are nuanced limitations:

- **Math Ceiling:** On HMMT Nov, the 80B model still leads (81.67 vs. 77.92). On AIME, the gap is narrow (89.24 vs. 87.40), but the 80B model retains an edge.
- **Tool-Use Variability:** On Tau2-Bench, the 80B model leads (57.40 vs. 48.57), while on BFCL-V4, the 3B model leads (56.50 vs. 50.51). This suggests **task-dependent capacity effects**: simpler tool-calling favors the optimized 3B model, but complex multi-step coordination still benefits from larger capacity.

The claim holds for **most** domains but not all. The paper is honest about this: Section 3.1 states the 80B model "retains advantages on selected mathematics and tool-use tasks."

#### 5.4.3 Claim 3: "600 Tool-Call Turns Reliably Executed"

**Evidence:** The Abstract claims the model "reliably execute[s] up to 600 tool-call turns for complex problem-solving."

**Assessment:** **Not directly validated in experiments.** While Table 5 shows strong deep search performance, the paper does not provide:
- A distribution of tool-call turns across successful trajectories
- Failure rate analysis at different turn counts (e.g., success rate at 100 vs. 300 vs. 600 turns)
- Comparison to baselines on turn-count robustness

This is a **missing ablation**. The claim is plausible given the turn-level supervision design (Section 2.2.1), but without empirical turn-count analysis, readers must trust the assertion. Future work should include a **turn-count vs. success rate** curve to validate this claim.

#### 5.4.4 Claim 4: "Time-Complexity Reward Improves Algorithmic Efficiency"

**Evidence:** Appendix C provides three case studies showing complexity improvements (e.g., $O(N^2 \log N)$ → $O(N \log N)$ for Minimum Cost Sort).

**Assessment:** **Strongly supported with concrete evidence.** The case studies are specific and verifiable:

- **Simultaneous Kagamimochi:** Binary search ($O(N \log N)$) → Two-pointer greedy ($O(N)$)
- **Twice Subsequence:** Value-to-positions map + binary search ($O(N + M \log N)$) → Two-pointer scans ($O(N)$)
- **Minimum Cost Sort:** Heap-driven swaps ($O(N^2 \log N)$) → Fenwick tree ($O(N \log N)$)

These are not marginal improvements but **fundamental algorithmic shifts**. Combined with the LeetCode 85% pass rate (Table 6), this validates that the gated time-complexity reward successfully teaches efficiency, not just correctness.

### 5.5 Limitations and Open Questions

A fair analysis must acknowledge where the experiments leave questions unanswered.

**1. Compute Budget Not Reported:** The paper does not specify the total compute cost (GPU hours, FLOPs) for training `Nanbeige4.1-3B`. While the model is smaller, the multi-stage RL pipeline with 256k context may be computationally intensive. Without this data, the **efficiency claim** (3B vs. 30B deployment cost) cannot be fully validated.

**2. Inference Latency Not Measured:** The paper focuses on capability, not inference speed. A 3B model should theoretically be faster than a 30B model, but the 256k context window and potential need for multiple tool-call iterations may offset this advantage. **End-to-end task completion time** is not reported.

**3. Generalization to Non-English Tasks:** All benchmarks appear to be English-language (except BrowseComp-ZH in Table 5, where the model scores 31.83 vs. Qwen3-4B's 7.92). While this shows multilingual capability, the paper does not systematically evaluate performance across diverse languages or cultural contexts.

**4. Catastrophic Forgetting Analysis:** The paper claims sequential staging prevents forgetting (Section 2.4), but does not provide **backward transfer metrics** (e.g., does Code RL degrade alignment performance?). Table 2 shows a minor coding decrease during Pair-wise RL, but the reverse effect is not measured.

**5. Long-Horizon Turn Distribution:** As noted in Section 5.4.3, the "600 tool-call turns" claim lacks empirical support. A histogram of successful trajectory lengths would strengthen this assertion.

### 5.6 Summary of Experimental Findings

The experiments provide **strong evidence** for the paper's core thesis, with some caveats:

| Claim | Support Level | Key Evidence |
|-------|---------------|--------------|
| 3B matches 100B+ on deep search | **Strong** | Table 5: 75.00 vs. 71.00 (DeepSeek-671B) on xBench-DS-05 |
| Unified generalist capability | **Moderate-Strong** | Table 4: Leads on code, alignment, BFCL; trails on some math/tool-use |
| Time-complexity optimization | **Strong** | Appendix C: Concrete algorithmic improvements; Table 6: 85% LeetCode pass rate |
| 600 tool-call turn stability | **Unverified** | Claimed in Abstract, no turn-count distribution in experiments |
| Sequential RL prevents forgetting | **Moderate** | Table 2 shows minor trade-offs; no backward transfer analysis |

The most convincing results are in **deep search** and **code efficiency**, where the 3B model not only matches but exceeds larger baselines. The more nuanced results are in **mathematics** and **complex tool coordination**, where capacity still provides an edge. This pattern suggests the paper's central insight is correct: **training objective alignment can decouple capability from scale in specific domains**, but raw capacity remains relevant for peak performance on the most demanding tasks.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While `Nanbeige4.1-3B` demonstrates remarkable capabilities for a 3B parameter model, a critical analysis reveals several important limitations, trade-offs, and open questions. Understanding these constraints is essential for practitioners considering deployment and for researchers building on this work. We organize this analysis around four dimensions: **architectural assumptions**, **unaddressed scenarios**, **resource constraints**, and **remaining weaknesses**.

### 6.1 Architectural Assumptions and Dependency Risks

The training pipeline relies on several critical components that introduce potential failure modes and deployment dependencies.

#### 6.1.1 Judge System Dependency for Code RL

The gated time-complexity reward mechanism (Section 2.3.3) depends on a **dedicated instruct judge model** trained specifically for fast complexity assessment. This introduces a hidden dependency:

- **Additional Model Overhead**: The judge system is not part of the 3B parameter count. Deploying `Nanbeige4.1-3B` for code generation with efficiency optimization requires maintaining and serving this separate judge model, adding inference latency and memory costs not reflected in the "3B" specification.
- **Judge Accuracy Assumption**: The reward signal assumes the judge model correctly identifies time complexity. If the judge misclassifies an $O(N^2)$ solution as $O(N)$, the policy receives incorrect training signals. The paper does not report judge model accuracy metrics or failure case analysis.
- **Sandbox Execution Costs**: The multi-language sandbox for correctness checking (Section 2.3.1) requires secure execution infrastructure. This limits deployment to environments where code execution is permitted, excluding air-gapped or highly restricted systems.

#### 6.1.2 Synthetic Data Quality Assumptions

The deep search capability relies heavily on **synthetic data construction** via Wiki-graph random walks and turn-level judgment (Section 2.2.1). This introduces several assumptions:

- **Distribution Match**: The synthetic QA pairs assume that Wikipedia entity-relation graphs adequately represent real-world search tasks. Domains with less structured knowledge (e.g., emerging technologies, niche academic fields) may not benefit from this data construction pipeline.
- **Critic Model Reliability**: The turn-level judgment mechanism employs a critic model to evaluate logical soundness, tool-call accuracy, and informational gain (Section 2.2.1, Paragraph 3). If the critic model has blind spots, erroneous trajectories may be incorporated into training, potentially teaching incorrect tool-use patterns.
- **Temporal Constraint**: The head entity selection focuses on Wikipedia entities updated within the **past six months** (Section 2.2.1, Paragraph 2). While this ensures timeliness, it may bias the model toward recent events and underrepresent stable, long-standing knowledge domains.

#### 6.1.3 Sequential Staging Assumptions

The cascaded training approach (SFT → Point-wise RL → Pair-wise RL → Code RL → Agentic RL) assumes that **capabilities can be cleanly separated and layered** without significant interference. However:

- **Objective Conflict Evidence**: Table 2 shows that Pair-wise RL causes a slight decrease in LiveCodeBench-V6 performance (66.0 → 65.6, ∆-0.4) while improving alignment (Section 2.1.3, Paragraph 5). This indicates that even with sequential staging, some capability trade-offs are unavoidable.
- **Order Sensitivity**: The paper does not ablate the **ordering of training stages**. It is unclear whether Code RL before General RL would produce different results, or whether the agentic RL stage should come earlier in the pipeline. The chosen order may be suboptimal for certain capability combinations.
- **Checkpoint Dependency**: Each stage initializes from the previous stage's checkpoint. If an early stage (e.g., SFT) produces a suboptimal foundation, later stages cannot fully recover. This creates a **single point of failure** in the training pipeline.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

Several important problem settings and edge cases are not evaluated or addressed in the paper.

#### 6.2.1 Long-Horizon Turn Count Validation Gap

The Abstract claims the model can "reliably execute up to **600 tool-call turns** for complex problem-solving." However:

- **No Turn Distribution Data**: The experiments section (Section 3) does not provide a histogram or distribution of tool-call turns across successful trajectories. We do not know the **median**, **mean**, or **variance** of turn counts in evaluated tasks.
- **No Failure Rate Analysis by Turn Count**: There is no data showing success rate at 100 turns vs. 300 turns vs. 600 turns. The claim that the model "reliably" handles 600 turns is not empirically validated with a **turn-count vs. success rate curve**.
- **Task-Specific Variation**: Deep search benchmarks (Table 5) vary in complexity. xBench-DeepSearch-05 may require fewer turns than xBench-DeepSearch-10. The paper does not correlate benchmark difficulty with turn count, making it unclear whether the 600-turn capability is necessary for the evaluated tasks.

This is a **significant validation gap**. Without turn-count analysis, practitioners cannot assess whether the model's long-horizon capability matches their specific use case requirements.

#### 6.2.2 Limited Multilingual Evaluation

While the paper includes BrowseComp-ZH (Chinese) in Table 5 with a score of 31.83 (vs. Qwen3-4B's 7.92), the evaluation is not systematically multilingual:

- **Single Non-English Benchmark**: Only one Chinese benchmark is included. There is no evaluation on other major languages (Spanish, French, Arabic, Hindi, etc.).
- **Cross-Lingual Transfer Unknown**: It is unclear whether the deep search and code generation capabilities transfer to non-English queries. The synthetic data construction uses Wikipedia, which has varying quality across languages.
- **Cultural Context Gaps**: Alignment benchmarks (Arena-Hard-V2, Multi-Challenge) are primarily English-centric. The model's performance on culturally specific prompts or non-Western preference distributions is not assessed.

For global deployment, this represents a **coverage limitation**. The model may perform well in English but exhibit degraded capabilities in other languages.

#### 6.2.3 Real-Time and Latency-Sensitive Applications

The paper focuses exclusively on **capability metrics** (accuracy, pass rate, benchmark scores) and does not address **inference efficiency**:

- **No Latency Measurements**: There is no data on tokens-per-second, time-to-first-token, or end-to-end task completion time. A 3B model with a 256k context window may have higher latency than expected due to attention computation over long sequences.
- **Tool-Call Iteration Overhead**: Deep search tasks require multiple tool invocations. The paper does not report the **wall-clock time** to complete a search task, which depends on both model inference speed and external API latency.
- **Batch Throughput Unknown**: For high-throughput deployments (e.g., serving thousands of concurrent requests), the model's batch processing capabilities are not characterized.

This omission limits the paper's utility for **production deployment planning**. Practitioners cannot estimate infrastructure costs or user-facing latency without this data.

#### 6.2.4 Domain Generalization Beyond Evaluated Benchmarks

The evaluation covers code, math, science, alignment, and deep search. However, several important domains are not assessed:

- **Creative Writing and Long-Form Generation**: The alignment benchmarks focus on preference modeling under adversarial prompts, not creative coherence over long documents.
- **Instruction Following for Complex Workflows**: While tool-use is evaluated (BFCL-V4, Tau2-Bench), complex multi-step instruction following without external tools is not specifically benchmarked.
- **Safety and Harmlessness**: The paper does not report evaluations on safety benchmarks (e.g., toxicity, bias, harmful instruction refusal). A model optimized for capability may have degraded safety properties.
- **Knowledge Cutoff and Factual Accuracy**: There is no evaluation of hallucination rates or factual consistency. The temporal-aware data construction (past six months) may improve recency but does not guarantee accuracy.

These gaps mean the model's suitability for **general-purpose assistant applications** remains partially unverified.

### 6.3 Computational and Data Constraints

The training approach introduces several resource constraints that affect reproducibility and scalability.

#### 6.3.1 Training Compute Budget Not Reported

The paper does not specify the **total compute cost** for training `Nanbeige4.1-3B`:

- **GPU Hours Unknown**: There is no mention of the number of GPU days, total FLOPs, or energy consumption for the multi-stage training pipeline.
- **Context Length Cost**: Extending context from 64k to 256k tokens (Section 2.1.1) significantly increases memory and computation requirements during training. The attention complexity scales quadratically with sequence length in standard transformers, making 256k training substantially more expensive than 64k.
- **RL Sampling Overhead**: GRPO samples **8 rollouts per prompt** (Section 2.1.2, Paragraph 2), and Code RL also uses $n=8$ rollouts per problem (Section 2.3.2, Paragraph 4). This 8× sampling multiplier significantly increases training compute compared to standard SFT.

Without compute budget data, the **efficiency claim** (3B vs. 30B deployment cost) cannot be fully validated. A 3B model trained with 10× more compute than a 30B model may not offer net efficiency gains.

#### 6.3.2 Data Construction Complexity

The synthetic data pipelines for deep search and code RL are **highly complex** and may be difficult to reproduce:

- **Wiki-Graph Infrastructure**: Building entity-relation graphs from Wikipedia and performing conditional random walks (Section 2.2.1) requires significant engineering effort and storage for the graph structure.
- **Critic Model Training**: The turn-level judgment mechanism requires training a separate critic model (Section 2.2.1, Paragraph 3). The paper does not specify the critic model's architecture, training data, or performance metrics.
- **Judge System for Code**: The time-complexity judge model (Section 2.3.1) is specifically trained for fast complexity assessment. Reproducing this requires either training a similar judge or using the authors' proprietary system.

These dependencies create a **reproducibility barrier**. Researchers without access to similar infrastructure may struggle to replicate the training approach.

#### 6.3.3 Scalability to Larger Models Unknown

The paper demonstrates that training objective alignment can improve 3B model performance. However:

- **No Scaling Analysis**: There is no ablation showing whether the same techniques (gated time-complexity rewards, turn-level supervision, sequential RL) would benefit larger models (e.g., 7B, 14B, 30B).
- **Capacity Ceiling Unclear**: It is unknown whether the 3B model has reached its **capability ceiling** with this training recipe, or whether further improvements are possible with more data or compute.
- **Diminishing Returns**: The techniques may exhibit diminishing returns at larger scales. For example, pair-wise RL may be more critical for small models that lack inherent preference understanding, while larger models may benefit less.

This limits the **generalizability of the methodology**. Practitioners training larger models cannot assume the same techniques will yield proportional gains.

### 6.4 Remaining Weaknesses and Open Questions

Several weaknesses are evident in the experimental results, and important questions remain unanswered.

#### 6.4.1 Mathematics Performance Ceiling

Table 4 shows that while `Nanbeige4.1-3B` performs competitively on mathematics, it does not universally exceed larger models:

| Benchmark | Nanbeige4.1-3B | Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B | Gap |
|-----------|----------------|--------------------|-----|
| AIME 2026 I | 87.40 | 89.24 | -1.84 |
| HMMT Nov | 77.92 | 81.67 | -3.75 |
| IMO-Answer-Bench | 53.38 | 58.00 | -4.62 |

**Interpretation**: On the most challenging mathematics benchmarks (HMMT, IMO), the 80B model retains a **3–5 point advantage**. This suggests that **raw capacity still matters for peak mathematical reasoning**, even with optimized training objectives. The 3B model can approach but not fully close the gap on the most demanding symbolic reasoning tasks.

**Open Question**: Is this gap fundamental to the 3B parameter budget, or could additional training stages (e.g., math-specific RL) close it further?

#### 6.4.2 Tool-Use Performance Variability

Table 4 shows inconsistent tool-use performance across benchmarks:

| Benchmark | Nanbeige4.1-3B | Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B | Result |
|-----------|----------------|--------------------|--------|
| BFCL-V4 | 56.50 | 50.51 | 3B leads by 5.99 |
| Tau2-Bench | 48.57 | 57.40 | 80B leads by 8.83 |

**Interpretation**: The 3B model excels on BFCL-V4 (function calling) but lags on Tau2-Bench (conversational agents in dual-control environments). This suggests **task-dependent capacity effects**:

- **BFCL-V4** may reward precise, single-turn function calls where the 3B model's optimization is effective.
- **Tau2-Bench** may require more complex multi-turn coordination and state tracking, where the 80B model's larger capacity provides an advantage.

**Open Question**: What specific aspects of tool-use (e.g., state tracking, multi-turn coherence, error recovery) are most sensitive to parameter count vs. training optimization?

#### 6.4.3 Catastrophic Forgetting Not Fully Analyzed

The paper claims sequential staging prevents catastrophic forgetting (Section 2.4), but the evidence is incomplete:

- **No Backward Transfer Metrics**: Table 2 shows that Pair-wise RL slightly decreases coding performance (LCB V6: 66.0 → 65.6). However, the paper does not report whether later stages (Code RL, Agentic RL) degrade earlier capabilities (e.g., alignment scores after Code RL).
- **No Longitudinal Tracking**: There is no tracking of capability metrics across all training stages. We see SFT → General RL progression (Table 2), but not the full pipeline from SFT through Code RL and Agentic RL.
- **No Interference Analysis**: The paper does not analyze whether optimizing for one capability (e.g., code efficiency) negatively impacts another (e.g., general reasoning).

**Open Question**: Does the final `Nanbeige4.1-3B` checkpoint retain all capabilities from intermediate stages, or are there unreported trade-offs?

#### 6.4.4 Safety and Alignment Robustness Unverified

The alignment benchmarks (Arena-Hard-V2, Multi-Challenge) measure **preference modeling** but not **safety**:

- **No Harmlessness Evaluation**: There is no reporting on toxicity, bias, or harmful instruction refusal rates.
- **No Jailbreak Resistance**: The model's robustness against adversarial prompts designed to bypass safety constraints is not assessed.
- **No Value Alignment Verification**: The preference data is curated for quality, but the underlying values encoded in the reward models are not analyzed.

**Open Question**: Does optimizing for capability and efficiency come at the cost of safety properties? A model that produces efficient code quickly is only useful if it also refuses harmful requests.

#### 6.4.5 Inference Optimization Not Addressed

The paper focuses on training but does not discuss **inference-time optimizations**:

- **No Quantization Results**: There is no evaluation of the model under INT8 or INT4 quantization, which is critical for edge deployment.
- **No Speculative Decoding**: Techniques like speculative decoding could improve throughput for a 3B model, but this is not explored.
- **No KV Cache Optimization**: With 256k context, KV cache memory becomes a bottleneck. The paper does not discuss attention optimizations (e.g., sliding window, sparse attention) for inference.

**Open Question**: Can the model maintain its capabilities under aggressive inference optimizations required for resource-constrained deployment?

### 6.6 Fair Assessment

Despite these limitations, `Nanbeige4.1-3B` represents a significant advance in small model capabilities. The limitations identified above are largely **gaps in evaluation and reporting** rather than fundamental flaws in the approach. The core contributions—sequential hybrid reward modeling, gated time-complexity optimization, and turn-level credit assignment—are sound and well-supported by the experimental evidence that **is** provided.

For practitioners, the key takeaway is that `Nanbeige4.1-3B` is **highly capable but not universally optimal**. It excels in code generation, deep search, and alignment tasks where the training objectives are well-aligned with the evaluation metrics. It may be less suitable for applications requiring:

- Peak mathematical reasoning (where 30B+ models retain an edge)
- Complex multi-step tool coordination (Tau2-Bench gap)
- Non-English language support (limited evaluation)
- Safety-critical deployments (unverified harmlessness)
- Resource-constrained inference (no quantization data)

For researchers, the open questions identified above represent fertile ground for future work. Addressing the validation gaps (turn-count analysis, compute budget, safety evaluation) would strengthen the paper's claims. Exploring the scalability of these techniques to larger models would determine whether this is a **small-model-specific breakthrough** or a **general training methodology improvement**.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how `Nanbeige4.1-3B` reshapes expectations for small language models, identifies concrete research directions it enables, and provides practical guidance for practitioners considering adoption. We move beyond what the model achieves today to explore what becomes possible because this work exists.

### 7.1 Shifting the Capability Frontier for Small Models

The most significant implication of this work is that it **decouples capability from parameter count** in specific domains. Prior to this paper, the prevailing assumption in the field was that complex capabilities like deep search and efficient code generation required massive scale (30B–100B+ parameters). `Nanbeige4.1-3B` demonstrates that **training objective alignment** can compensate for capacity limitations when the reward structure matches the task structure.

**Three Paradigm Shifts:**

1. **From Scale-Only to Objective-Aligned Training.** The paper shows that a 3B model can match or exceed 100B+ models on specific benchmarks (Table 5: xBench-DeepSearch-05 at 75.00 vs. DeepSeek-V3.2-671B at 71.00). This challenges the scaling law orthodoxy that performance is primarily a function of parameter count. The key insight is that **post-training optimization** can extract capabilities that pre-training alone does not reveal.

2. **From Specialization to Unified Generalism.** Section 1 notes that most SLMs exhibit "fragmented capabilities"—reasoning models struggle with agents, code models lack alignment. `Nanbeige4.1-3B` proves that a single 3B checkpoint can simultaneously excel at code (76.9 LCB-V6), math (87.40 AIME), alignment (73.2 Arena-Hard-V2), and deep search (75.00 xBench-DS-05). This eliminates the need for model ensembles in many deployment scenarios.

3. **From Correctness to Algorithmic Quality.** The gated time-complexity reward (Section 2.3.3) introduces a new standard for code generation evaluation. Prior work optimized for Pass@k (functional correctness); this work optimizes for **correctness + efficiency**. Appendix C demonstrates that small models can learn fundamental algorithmic improvements (e.g., $O(N^2 \log N) \rightarrow O(N \log N)$), not just surface-level pattern matching.

**What This Means for the Field:**

The success of `Nanbeige4.1-3B` suggests that future SLM research should focus less on increasing parameter count and more on:
- Designing **domain-specific reward structures** that capture task-relevant properties (efficiency, planning quality, turn-level coherence)
- Developing **sequential training pipelines** that layer capabilities without catastrophic forgetting
- Building **synthetic data pipelines** that generate high-quality training signals for capabilities not well-represented in natural data (e.g., 600-turn tool trajectories)

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper opens several concrete research avenues. We categorize these by their relationship to the current work: extensions, validations, and generalizations.

#### 7.2.1 Extensions: Building on the Training Pipeline

**Alternative Training Stage Orderings.** Section 6.1.3 notes that the paper does not ablate the ordering of training stages (SFT → Point-wise RL → Pair-wise RL → Code RL → Agentic RL). Follow-up work could explore:
- Does Code RL before General RL produce different trade-offs?
- Should agentic RL come earlier to establish tool-use foundations before fine-tuning alignment?
- Can stages be partially interleaved rather than strictly sequential?

**Dynamic Gating Mechanisms.** The time-complexity reward uses a hard gate ($\text{PassRate} = 1$) to activate efficiency rewards (Section 2.3.3). Future work could explore:
- **Soft gating**: Gradually increase $R_{\text{time}}$ weight as $\text{PassRate}$ approaches 1
- **Curriculum gating**: Start with loose gates, tighten as training progresses
- **Per-problem gating**: Adjust gates based on problem difficulty rather than uniform thresholds

**Multi-Objective RL Formulations.** The current pipeline treats objectives sequentially. Alternative formulations could include:
- **Pareto-optimal training**: Explicitly optimize for trade-off frontiers between capabilities
- **Gradient surgery**: Prevent gradient conflicts when optimizing multiple objectives simultaneously
- **Capability-aware learning rates**: Adjust update magnitudes based on which capability is being trained

#### 7.2.2 Validations: Strengthening Empirical Claims

**Turn-Count Distribution Analysis.** Section 5.4.3 identifies a critical gap: the "600 tool-call turns" claim (Abstract) lacks empirical validation. Follow-up work should:
- Plot **success rate vs. turn count** curves for deep search tasks
- Report median, mean, and variance of turn counts in successful trajectories
- Compare turn-count robustness against baselines (e.g., does Qwen3-4B fail at 50 turns while Nanbeige4.1-3B succeeds at 200?)

**Compute Budget Transparency.** Section 6.3.1 notes that training compute is not reported. Future papers should:
- Report total GPU hours, FLOPs, and energy consumption
- Compare training efficiency (performance per compute dollar) against larger models
- Analyze whether the multi-stage RL pipeline is compute-efficient or merely capability-efficient

**Safety and Harmlessness Evaluation.** Section 6.4.4 identifies that safety properties are unverified. Follow-up work should:
- Evaluate on toxicity benchmarks (e.g., ToxiGen, RealToxicityPrompts)
- Test jailbreak resistance against adversarial prompt attacks
- Measure harmful instruction refusal rates across domains (code, search, general conversation)

#### 7.2.3 Generalizations: Scaling and Transfer

**Scaling to Larger Models.** Section 6.3.3 notes uncertainty about whether these techniques benefit models beyond 3B. Key questions:
- Do gated time-complexity rewards improve 7B, 14B, or 30B code generation?
- Does turn-level supervision help larger models with long-horizon tasks, or is it only critical for capacity-constrained models?
- Are there **diminishing returns** where larger models need less explicit reward engineering?

**Cross-Lingual Transfer.** Section 6.2.2 notes limited multilingual evaluation (only BrowseComp-ZH). Follow-up work should:
- Evaluate on diverse language benchmarks (Spanish, French, Arabic, Hindi, etc.)
- Test whether synthetic data construction (Wiki-graph walks) transfers to non-English Wikipedias
- Analyze whether alignment preferences learned in English transfer to other languages

**Domain Generalization Beyond Evaluated Benchmarks.** The evaluation covers code, math, science, alignment, and deep search. Unexplored domains include:
- **Creative writing**: Long-form coherence, narrative consistency
- **Scientific reasoning**: Hypothesis generation, experimental design
- **Legal/medical reasoning**: Domain-specific constraint satisfaction
- **Multimodal tasks**: Image-text reasoning, visual search

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision: The Path to Efficient General Intelligence

Looking beyond immediate applications, `Nanbeige4.1-3B` points toward a broader research agenda: **capability-dense models** that maximize performance per parameter through objective alignment rather than scale.

**Three Research Pillars for the Field:**

1. **Objective Engineering Over Scale.** This work demonstrates that carefully designed reward structures can extract capabilities that pre-training alone does not reveal. Future research should treat **objective design** as a first-class research problem, not an implementation detail.

2. **Sequential Capability Layering.** The staged training approach (SFT → General RL → Code RL → Agentic RL) suggests that capabilities can be built incrementally without catastrophic forgetting. This challenges the prevailing "train everything at once" approach and opens questions about **optimal capability orderings**.

3. **Synthetic Data for Rare Capabilities.** The deep search data pipeline (Section 2.2.1) shows that synthetic data can teach capabilities not well-represented in natural data. This is critical for small models that cannot rely on emergent abilities from scale. Future work should explore synthetic data for other underrepresented capabilities (long-form reasoning, multi-agent coordination, etc.).

**The Efficiency Frontier:**

The paper redefines the **efficiency frontier** for language models. Prior to this work, the frontier was defined by scale: larger models = better performance. After this work, the frontier includes **training objective quality** as a dimension:

$$\text{Capability} = f(\text{Parameters}, \text{Data Quality}, \text{Objective Alignment})$$

Where `Objective Alignment` captures how well the training rewards match the task structure. `Nanbeige4.1-3B` shows that high `Objective Alignment` can compensate for low `Parameters` in specific domains.

**Open Challenge for the Community:**

The paper ends with a forward-looking statement in Section 4 (Conclusion):

> "Looking ahead, we aim to push the boundaries of compact models in complex coding and research agent scenarios. Concurrently, we focus on improving inference efficiency by enabling tasks to be solved with shorter outputs and fewer tool invocations, while exploring architectural innovations to further enhance the potential of the compact generalist model."

This identifies three concrete challenges for follow-up work:
1. **Output efficiency**: Same capability with fewer tokens generated
2. **Tool efficiency**: Same capability with fewer tool invocations
3. **Architectural efficiency**: Better performance without increasing parameters

The community is now invited to take up these challenges, using `Nanbeige4.1-3B` as both a baseline and a methodological template.
