## 1. Executive Summary

DeepSeekMath-V2 addresses the fundamental limitation of final-answer rewards in mathematical reasoning by introducing a self-verifiable framework where an LLM-based verifier assesses proof rigor and incentivizes the generator to resolve identified issues. Through meta-verification to ensure faithfulness and scaled verification compute to auto-label hard proofs, the model achieves gold-level performance on IMO 2025 and CMO 2024, alongside a near-perfect 118/120 score on Putnam 2024 that exceeds the highest human score of 90. This work establishes that training models to verify their own reasoning without reference solutions is a feasible path toward AI systems capable of rigorous theorem proving and scientific research.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of DeepSeekMath-V2, we must first examine the prevailing paradigm in mathematical AI and why it fails when moving from calculation to rigorous proof. This section unpacks the specific limitations of current reinforcement learning (RL) methods, the theoretical necessity of self-verification, and how this work positions itself against both natural language models and formal proof assistants.

### The Limitation of Final-Answer Rewards

The dominant approach for training mathematical reasoning in Large Language Models (LLMs) relies on **final-answer rewards**. In this framework, a model is rewarded only if its predicted numerical answer matches a ground-truth value. For example, if a problem asks "What is the sum of integers from 1 to 100?", the model receives a positive reward only if it outputs `$5050$`. This methodology has driven rapid progress, allowing frontier models to saturate quantitative competitions like the American Invitational Mathematics Examination (AIME) and the Harvard-MIT Mathematics Tournament (HMMT) within a single year (Section 1).

However, this success masks two fundamental flaws that prevent advancement into deeper mathematical reasoning:

*   **Correct Answers $\neq$ Correct Reasoning:** A model can arrive at the correct numerical result through flawed logic, lucky guesses, or hallucinated steps. Section 1 notes that this reward mechanism serves as an "unreliable proxy for reasoning correctness." If the goal is to trust AI with scientific derivation, the path to the answer matters more than the answer itself.
*   **Inapplicability to Theorem Proving:** Many mathematical tasks, particularly **theorem proving**, do not have numerical answers. Instead, the objective is a rigorous step-by-step derivation. As stated in Section 1, "rigorous derivation is the primary objective," making final-answer rewards inapplicable. Without a scalar score to optimize, standard RL methods cannot directly improve proof quality.

Consequently, LLMs trained solely on quantitative tasks frequently produce "mathematically invalid or logically inconsistent natural-language proofs" (Section 1). They lack the ability to distinguish between a valid proof and one that merely looks convincing.

### The Verification Gap and Open Problems

A critical bottleneck in improving reasoning models is the **generation-verification gap**. For a model to improve its own outputs, its ability to verify correctness must exceed its ability to generate solutions. If a generator cannot recognize its own errors, it cannot iteratively refine them.

Section 1 highlights that current models exhibit "high false-positive rates, often claiming incorrect proofs are valid even when they contain obvious logical flaws." This lack of verification capability hinders improvement because the model cannot learn from its mistakes without external supervision.

This limitation becomes catastrophic when tackling **open problems**—mathematical questions without known solutions. In such scenarios:
*   There is no ground-truth answer to reward.
*   Human experts may not be available to verify every step.
*   The system must rely on internal consistency and rigor.

The authors argue that "humans can identify issues in proofs even without reference solutions" (Section 1). Replicating this ability in LLMs is essential for scaling test-time compute on problems where the solution is unknown. DeepSeekMath-V2 is motivated by the hypothesis that a model can be trained to identify proof issues without reference solutions, enabling an iterative cycle of generation and self-correction.

### Prior Approaches and Their Shortcomings

To position DeepSeekMath-V2, we must distinguish it from two existing branches of mathematical AI: natural language reasoning models and formal proof assistants.

#### Natural Language Reasoning Models
Recent reasoning models (e.g., OpenAI, 2024; Guo et al., 2025) have excelled at quantitative benchmarks but struggle with informal proofs.
*   **Gemini-2.5 Pro:** Demonstrates some self-verification capabilities and can refine solutions (Huang and Yang, 2025), but the methodology remains largely proprietary and less focused on rigorous proof verification rubrics.
*   **DeepMind DeepThink:** Achieved gold-medal performance at IMO 2025 using pure natural language reasoning (Luong and Lockhart, 2025). While this serves as an "existence proof that LLM-based verification of complex proofs is achievable," the work is internal. DeepSeekMath-V2 positions itself by open-sourcing the training methodology and providing concrete steps toward self-verifiable reasoning (Section 4).

#### Formal Proof Assistants
Systems like **Lean** (de Moura et al., 2015) and **Isabelle** (Paulson, 1994) offer guaranteed correctness because proofs must be written in a formal language that a computer can compile.
*   **AlphaProof:** Specialized for formal proof search, achieving silver-level performance at IMO 2024 (AlphaProof and teams, 2024).
*   **Shortcoming:** Formal methods require "intensive computation" and demand that users (or AI) translate informal intuition into strict formal code (Section 4). This translation barrier limits accessibility and speed.

DeepSeekMath-V2 occupies a middle ground. It aims for the rigor of formal methods but operates entirely in **natural language**. The authors believe that "advancing natural language theorem proving will significantly benefit formal reasoning" by providing better informal insights to guide formal systems (Section 4).

### Positioning of This Work

DeepSeekMath-V2 distinguishes itself through a specific architectural and training philosophy focused on **faithfulness** and **scalability**:

1.  **Verifier-Generator Synergy:** Unlike models that only generate, this work explicitly trains a dedicated verifier `$\pi_\varphi$` to assess proofs using high-level rubrics (Section 2.1.1). This verifier is then used as a reward model to train the generator `$\pi_\theta$`.
2.  **Meta-Verification:** To prevent the verifier from hallucinating issues (a common failure mode where a verifier predicts the correct score but invents false reasons), the authors introduce a **meta-verifier** `$\pi_\eta$` (Section 2.1.2). This secondary model checks the verifier's analysis, ensuring the feedback given to the generator is trustworthy.
3.  **Automated Scaling:** Recognizing that human annotation is a bottleneck, the paper proposes scaling verification compute to automatically label hard-to-verify proofs (Section 2.3). By generating multiple verification analyses and using meta-verification to validate them, the system creates training data without constant human intervention.

In summary, the paper addresses the stagnation of natural-language mathematical reasoning caused by reliance on final-answer rewards. It positions itself as a bridge between the flexibility of LLMs and the rigor required for theorem proving, aiming to create systems capable of solving research-level mathematics where reference solutions do not exist.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a machine learning systems paper that presents a novel training framework for teaching large language models to both generate and verify mathematical proofs in natural language. The core idea is to create a self-improving cycle where a verifier model assesses proof quality, a generator model learns from this feedback, and both components are iteratively enhanced through automated data labeling without requiring constant human annotation.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

DeepSeekMath-V2 is a dual-model system consisting of a proof verifier that evaluates mathematical arguments and a proof generator that produces them, where both models are trained to operate without access to ground-truth solutions. The system solves the problem of training mathematical reasoning without final-answer rewards by replacing scalar answer matching with a learned verification process that assesses logical rigor and completeness.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The architecture consists of four primary components that interact in a cyclical training pipeline. First, the **Proof Verifier** (`$\pi_\varphi$`) takes a problem statement `$X$` and a candidate proof `$Y$` as input, then outputs a detailed analysis identifying logical issues followed by a quality score `$s \in \{0, 0.5, 1\}$`. Second, the **Meta-Verifier** (`$\pi_\eta$`) receives the problem `$X$`, proof `$Y$`, and the verifier's analysis `$V$`, then evaluates whether the identified issues are genuine and whether the score is justified. Third, the **Proof Generator** (`$\pi_\theta$`) takes only the problem `$X$` and produces both a proof `$Y$` and a self-analysis `$Z$` following the same format as the verifier. Fourth, the **Automated Labeling Pipeline** scales verification compute by generating multiple independent analyses per proof, using meta-verification to validate them, and aggregating results to create training labels without human intervention. Information flows from the verifier to the generator as reward signals during training, while newly generated proofs flow back to improve the verifier through automated labeling, creating a synergistic improvement cycle described in Section 2.3.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

*   We begin with proof verification training because the verifier serves as the foundation—the generator cannot learn to produce rigorous proofs without a reliable evaluation signal first existing.
*   We then explain meta-verification because it addresses a critical failure mode in the verifier (hallucinated issues) that would otherwise corrupt all downstream training.
*   We proceed to proof generation training to show how the verified reward signal is used to optimize the generator through reinforcement learning.
*   We detail the self-verification enhancement that teaches the generator to evaluate its own work with the same rigor as the dedicated verifier.
*   We conclude with the automated labeling pipeline that enables scalable improvement without human annotation bottlenecks.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Foundation: Training the Proof Verifier

The verifier training process establishes the evaluation capability that the entire system depends upon. The authors first constructed a cold-start dataset `$\mathcal{D}_v$` by crawling 17,503 problems from Art of Problem Solving (AoPS) contests, prioritizing math olympiads, team selection tests, and post-2010 problems explicitly requiring proofs (Section 2.1.1). This problem set is denoted as `$\mathcal{D}_p$`. Candidate proofs were generated using a variant of DeepSeek-V3.2-Exp-Thinking, which was not optimized for theorem proving and tended to produce concise but error-prone outputs, so the model was prompted to iteratively refine its proofs over multiple rounds to improve comprehensiveness and rigor. Mathematical experts then scored each proof according to high-level rubrics `$\mathcal{I}_v$` (detailed in Appendix A.2), creating the initial RL dataset `$\mathcal{D}_v = \{(X_i, Y_i, s_i)\}$`, where each item consists of a problem `$X_i$`, a proof `$Y_i$`, and an overall proof score `$s_i \in \{0, 0.5, 1\}$`.

The scoring rubric uses three discrete levels to classify proof quality. A score of `$1$` indicates complete and rigorous proofs with all logical steps clearly justified. A score of `$0.5$` indicates proofs with sound overall logic but minor errors or omitted details. A score of `$0$` indicates fundamentally flawed proofs containing fatal logical errors or critical gaps. This three-level scheme provides finer granularity than binary correct/incorrect labels while remaining tractable for model learning.

The verifier `$\pi_\varphi(\cdot | X, Y, \mathcal{I}_v)$` is trained using reinforcement learning with two reward components. The first component is a format reward `$R_{\text{format}}$`, which is an indicator function that enforces the model to generate both a summary of identified issues and a proof score by checking whether the final response contains the key phrase "Here is my evaluation of the solution:" as well as a score within `$\boxed{}$` following "Based on my evaluation, the final overall score should be:". This format constraint ensures consistent output structure that can be reliably parsed for downstream processing. The second component is a score reward `$R_{\text{score}}$` that rewards proximity between the predicted score `$s'_i$` and the annotated score `$s_i$`:

$$R_{\text{score}}(s'_i, s_i) = 1 - |s'_i - s_i|$$

This equation means the reward equals one minus the absolute difference between predicted and actual scores, so a perfect prediction yields reward `$1$`, a prediction off by 0.5 yields reward `$0.5$`, and a prediction off by 1.0 yields reward `$0$`. The complete RL objective for training the verifier is:

$$\max_{\pi_\varphi} \mathbb{E}_{(X_i, Y_i, s_i) \sim \mathcal{D}_v, (V'_i, s'_i) \sim \pi_\varphi(\cdot | X_i, Y_i)} \left[ R_{\text{format}}(V'_i) \cdot R_{\text{score}}(s'_i, s_i) \right]$$

where `$V'_i$` denotes the verifier's final response and `$s'_i$` is the proof score extracted from it. The multiplication of format and score rewards means the model receives zero total reward if it fails the format check, regardless of score accuracy, which strongly incentivizes adherence to the required output structure.

#### Critical Enhancement: Meta-Verification to Prevent Hallucination

The approach described above trains proof verification through RL to align predicted proof scores with expert annotations, but it provides no direct supervision on the identified issues themselves. This creates a critical vulnerability: when evaluating flawed proofs (where `$s_i < 1$`) during training, the verifier can receive full reward by predicting the correct scores while hallucinating non-existent issues, undermining its trustworthiness. If the verifier learns to invent plausible-sounding but false criticisms, the generator trained on this feedback will optimize for addressing imaginary problems rather than genuine logical gaps.

To address this problem, the authors introduce meta-verification: a secondary evaluation process that assesses whether issues identified by the verifier indeed exist and whether these issues logically justify the predicted proof score according to the evaluation rubrics `$\mathcal{I}_v$`. The complete meta-verification rubrics `$\mathcal{I}_{mv}$` are detailed in Appendix A.3. A dedicated meta-verifier `$\pi_\eta(\cdot | X, Y, V, \mathcal{I}_{mv})$` is trained using RL to perform this evaluation, where `$V$` is the verifier's analysis of proof `$Y$`. The meta-verifier produces a summary of issues found in the analysis itself, followed by a quality score `$ms_i \in \{0, 0.5, 1\}$` measuring how accurate and justified the verifier's analysis is.

The meta-verifier training process follows three steps. First, the team obtained an initial verifier `$\pi_\varphi$` following the procedure in Section 2.1.1. Second, mathematical experts scored the quality of verifier responses according to `$\mathcal{I}_{mv}$`, creating dataset `$\mathcal{D}_{mv} = \{(X_i, Y_i, V_i, ms_i)\}$`, where `$V_i$` is the analysis of proof `$Y_i$` and `$ms_i$` is the expert-annotated quality score. Third, they trained the meta-verifier using the same RL objective structure as the verifier training, with format and score rewards. The meta-verification prompt (Appendix A.3) instructs the meta-verifier to analyze the solution evaluation from three aspects: step restatement (checking whether the verifier accurately described the proof's content), defect analysis (checking whether identified errors actually exist), and expression analysis (checking whether the verifier's explanations are accurate).

Using the trained meta-verifier `$\pi_\eta$`, the authors enhanced the verifier training by integrating meta-verification feedback into the reward function:

$$R_V = R_{\text{format}} \cdot R_{\text{score}} \cdot R_{\text{meta}}$$

where `$R_{\text{meta}}$` is the quality score from the meta-verifier. This three-way multiplication means the verifier receives high reward only when it satisfies the format requirements, predicts accurate scores, AND provides faithful issue identification. The verifier is trained on both the verification dataset `$\mathcal{D}_v$` and the meta-verification dataset `$\mathcal{D}_{mv}$`, using the same reward mechanism on `$\mathcal{D}_{mv}$` as used for training the meta-verifier. The resulting model can perform both proof verification and meta-verification tasks. On a validation split of `$\mathcal{D}_v$`, the average quality score of the verifier's proof analyses—as evaluated by the meta-verifier—improved from 0.85 to 0.96, while maintaining the same accuracy in proof score prediction (Section 2.1.2). This result demonstrates that meta-verification successfully improves faithfulness without sacrificing score prediction accuracy.

#### Proof Generation Training with Verifier as Reward Model

With verifier `$\pi_\varphi$` serving as a generative reward model, the authors train a proof generator `$\pi_\theta(\cdot | X)$` with the RL objective:

$$\max_{\pi_\theta} \mathbb{E}_{X_i \sim \mathcal{D}_p, Y_i \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | X_i)} \left[ R_Y \right]$$

where `$R_Y$` is the proof score produced by `$\pi_\varphi(\cdot | X_i, Y_i, \mathcal{I}_v)$`. This objective means the generator learns to produce proofs that maximize the verifier's assessment, effectively distilling the verifier's evaluation criteria into generation behavior. The generator is initialized from a version of DeepSeek-V3.2-Exp-SFT which was supervised fine-tuned on reasoning data related to mathematics and code, then optimized with reinforcement learning using the verifier's scores as rewards.

#### Self-Verification: Teaching the Generator to Evaluate Itself

When a proof generator fails to produce a completely correct proof in one shot—common for challenging problems from competitions like IMO and CMO—iterative verification and refinement can improve results. This involves analyzing the proof with an external verifier and prompting the generator to address identified issues. However, the authors observed a critical limitation: when prompted to both generate and analyze its own proof in one shot, the generator tends to claim correctness even when the external verifier easily identifies flaws. In other words, while the generator can refine proofs based on external feedback, it fails to evaluate its own work with the same rigor as the dedicated verifier.

This observation motivated the authors to endow the proof generator with genuine verification capabilities. During training, they prompt the generator `$\pi_\theta$` to produce a proof `$Y$` followed by a self-analysis `$Z$` that follows the same format and rubrics `$\mathcal{I}_v$` as the verifier (see Appendix A.1). The proof score predicted in the self-analysis is denoted as `$s'$`. To ensure faithful self-evaluation, the verifier `$\pi_\varphi$` assesses both components: the proof `$Y$` receives score `$R_Y = s$`, and the self-analysis `$Z$` receives a meta-verification score `$R_{\text{meta}}(Z) = ms$`. The reward function combines these assessments:

$$R = R_{\text{format}}(Y, Z) \cdot (\alpha \cdot R_Y + \beta \cdot R_Z)$$

$$R_Z = R_{\text{score}}(s', s) \cdot R_{\text{meta}}(Z)$$

where `$R_{\text{format}}(Y, Z)$` verifies that both the proof and self-analysis follow the specified format, and `$R_{\text{score}}(s', s)$` rewards accurate self-assessment. The authors set `$\alpha = 0.76$` and `$\beta = 0.24$`. This reward structure creates specific incentives: faithful acknowledgment of errors is rewarded over false claims of correctness, the highest rewards come from producing correct proofs and accurately recognizing their rigor, and a good strategy to obtain high rewards for the proof generator is to identify and resolve as many issues as possible before finalizing the response. The asymmetry between `$\alpha$` and `$\beta$` prioritizes proof quality (`$76\%$` weight) while still incentivizing accurate self-evaluation (`$24\%$` weight), preventing the model from gaming the system by producing poor proofs with honest self-criticism.

The proof generation prompt (Appendix A.1) explicitly instructs the model that it already has the ability to rate its solution itself, so it is expected to reason carefully about how to solve a given problem, evaluate its method according to the instruction, and refine its solution by fixing issues identified until it can make no further progress. The prompt emphasizes that the worst final response would provide a wrong solution but lie that it's correct or claim that it's correct without careful error checking, and warns "You CAN'T cheat! If you cheat, we will know, and you will be penalized!" This explicit framing helps align the generator's incentives with truthful self-assessment.

#### Synergy Between Verification and Generation Through Automated Labeling

The proof verifier and generator create a synergistic cycle: the verifier improves the generator, and as the generator improves, it produces new proofs that challenge the verifier's current capabilities. These challenging cases—where the verifier may fail to identify issues in a single attempt—become valuable training data for enhancing the verifier itself. To retrain and improve the verifier, the system needs labeled correctness data for newly generated proofs. Manual annotation, while straightforward, becomes increasingly time-consuming as problems grow harder and errors become more subtle.

To boost annotation efficiency, the authors generated multiple verifier analyses per proof to surface potential issues for human review. From this AI-assisted annotation process, they recognized two facts that make it feasible to push the level of automation a step further. First, scaling verifier samples increases the probability of catching real issues in flawed proofs. Second, reviewing the verifier's identified issues is exactly meta-verification, which is easier than identifying issues from scratch, and meta-verification is also more sample-efficient for LLMs to master.

Building on these observations, the authors developed an automated labeling process with three steps. For each proof, the system generates `$n$` independent verification analyses. For analyses reporting issues (scores 0 or 0.5), the system generates `$m$` meta-verification assessments to validate the identified problems, where an analysis is deemed valid if the majority of meta-assessments confirm its findings. For each proof, the system examines analyses that assign the lowest score, and if at least `$k$` such analyses are deemed valid, the proof is labeled with that lowest score. If no legitimate issues are identified across all verification attempts, the proof is labeled with 1. Otherwise, the proof is discarded or routed to human experts for labeling. While the paper does not specify exact values for `$n$`, `$m$`, and `$k$`, Section 3.3.3 mentions using 64 verification analyses per proof in the high-compute search setting, suggesting these parameters scale with available compute budget.

In the last two training iterations, this fully automated pipeline replaced human annotation entirely. Quality checks confirmed that the automated labels aligned well with expert judgments. This automation is crucial for scalability: as the generator produces increasingly sophisticated proofs, human experts would struggle to identify subtle errors, but the scaled verification system can catch issues through aggregation of multiple independent analyses.

#### Training Iteration Structure

The training process employs Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) (Shao et al., 2024) for reinforcement learning, iteratively optimizing proof verification and generation capabilities as described in Section 3.1. In each iteration, the system first optimizes proof verification. The proof generator is then initialized from the verifier checkpoint and optimized for proof generation. Starting from the second iteration, the proof verifier is initialized with a checkpoint that consolidated both verification and generation capabilities from the previous iteration through rejection fine-tuning. This alternating optimization ensures that improvements in one component immediately benefit the other, maintaining the generation-verification gap as both models improve.

#### Key Design Choices and Their Rationale

Several non-obvious design decisions warrant explanation. The three-level scoring scheme (0, 0.5, 1) was chosen over binary scoring because it captures the reality that many proofs are mostly correct but contain minor gaps—distinguishing these from fundamentally flawed proofs provides more useful training signal. The meta-verification layer was added after observing that verifiers could achieve high score prediction accuracy while hallucinating issue descriptions, which would mislead the generator during refinement. The `$\alpha = 0.76, \beta = 0.24$` weighting in the generator reward function was tuned to prioritize proof quality while maintaining sufficient incentive for honest self-evaluation; too much weight on self-evaluation could encourage the model to produce poor proofs with accurate criticism, while too little weight would fail to develop genuine verification capabilities.

The decision to train the generator from the verifier checkpoint (rather than from the base model) leverages the verifier's learned evaluation capabilities, giving the generator a head start in understanding what constitutes rigorous reasoning. The automated labeling pipeline's reliance on majority voting across meta-verifications exploits the observation that LLMs are more reliable at reviewing existing analyses than generating critiques from scratch, similar to how humans find it easier to check someone else's work than to produce original solutions.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

DeepSeekMath-V2 introduces several contributions that distinguish it from prior mathematical reasoning systems. This section identifies the most novel innovations, explains why they represent fundamental advances rather than incremental improvements, and clarifies what makes each contribution significant for the field.

### 4.1 Meta-Verification: Preventing Verifier Hallucination Through Second-Order Evaluation

**What makes this novel:** Prior verifier training approaches optimize only for score prediction accuracy—the verifier learns to output the correct numerical score but receives no supervision on whether its identified issues are genuine. As Section 2.1.2 reveals, this creates a critical vulnerability: "when evaluating flawed proofs (where $s_i &lt; 1$) during training, the verifier can receive full reward by predicting the correct scores while hallucinating non-existent issues."

DeepSeekMath-V2 is the first system to introduce **meta-verification** as a training mechanism—a secondary model $\pi_\eta$ that evaluates whether the verifier's identified issues actually exist and whether they logically justify the predicted score. This is not merely adding another model layer; it fundamentally changes what the verifier optimizes for.

**Why this is a fundamental innovation:**

*   **Shifts optimization target from outcome to reasoning:** Traditional verifiers optimize $R_{\text{score}}$ alone, which rewards correct predictions regardless of reasoning quality. Meta-verification adds $R_{\text{meta}}$ to the reward function ($R_V = R_{\text{format}} \cdot R_{\text{score}} \cdot R_{\text{meta}}$), forcing the verifier to produce faithful issue identification, not just accurate scores.
*   ** measurable faithfulness improvement:** On a validation split of $\mathcal{D}_v$, the average quality score of verifier analyses—as evaluated by the meta-verifier—improved from 0.85 to 0.96 while maintaining the same accuracy in proof score prediction (Section 2.1.2). This demonstrates that meta-verification improves reasoning faithfulness without sacrificing prediction accuracy, addressing a failure mode that prior work did not explicitly tackle.
*   **Enables trustworthy iterative refinement:** Without meta-verification, a generator trained on verifier feedback could optimize for addressing imaginary problems. The meta-verifier ensures that refinement iterations target genuine logical gaps, making the self-improvement cycle reliable.

**Comparison to prior work:** Systems like Gemini-2.5 Pro demonstrate self-verification capabilities (Huang and Yang, 2025), but the paper does not describe mechanisms to prevent the verifier from hallucinating issues. DeepMind's DeepThink achieves gold-medal performance (Luong and Lockhart, 2025), but its methodology remains internal and unpublished. DeepSeekMath-V2 is the first to open-source a training framework that explicitly addresses verifier faithfulness through second-order evaluation.

### 4.2 Automated Labeling Pipeline: Scaling Verification Compute to Replace Human Annotation

**What makes this novel:** The automated labeling process described in Section 2.3 represents a fundamental shift in how training data for mathematical reasoning is created. Rather than relying on continuous human expert annotation—which becomes "increasingly time-consuming as problems grow harder and errors become more subtle"—the system uses scaled verification compute to automatically label newly generated proofs.

The pipeline exploits two key observations: (1) scaling verifier samples increases the probability of catching real issues in flawed proofs, and (2) meta-verification is more sample-efficient for LLMs than identifying issues from scratch. In the last two training iterations, "this fully automated pipeline replaced human annotation entirely" with quality checks confirming alignment with expert judgments.

**Why this is a fundamental innovation:**

*   **Breaks the human annotation bottleneck:** Prior mathematical reasoning systems require expert-labeled data for every training iteration. This approach enables continuous improvement without proportional increases in human labor, making it feasible to train on increasingly difficult problems where expert time is scarce.
*   **Leverages aggregation for reliability:** The system generates $n$ independent verification analyses per proof, then uses $m$ meta-verification assessments to validate identified issues. A proof receives the lowest score only if at least $k$ analyses are deemed valid. This aggregation strategy exploits the observation that LLMs are more reliable at reviewing existing analyses than generating critiques from scratch.
*   **Creates a self-sustaining improvement cycle:** As the generator produces harder proofs, the automated labeling system scales verification compute to catch subtler errors. This means the system can continue improving even as it approaches human expert capability, where finding annotators who can identify errors becomes prohibitively difficult.

**Comparison to prior work:** Formal proof assistants like Lean and Isabelle guarantee correctness through compilation, but they require proofs to be written in formal language—a significant barrier (Section 4). AlphaProof achieved silver-level IMO performance but required "intensive computation" with formal methods (AlphaProof and teams, 2024). DeepSeekMath-V2 achieves comparable rigor in natural language while automating the labeling process, making it more accessible and scalable.

### 4.3 Self-Verification Training: Incentivizing Honest Self-Assessment in Generators

**What makes this novel:** The paper identifies a critical failure mode that prior work did not address: "when prompted to both generate and analyze its own proof in one shot, the generator tends to claim correctness even when the external verifier easily identifies flaws" (Section 2.2.2). This means generators could refine proofs based on external feedback but lacked genuine self-evaluation capability.

DeepSeekMath-V2 addresses this by training the generator to produce both a proof $Y$ and a self-analysis $Z$, with the verifier assessing both components. The reward function $R = R_{\text{format}}(Y, Z) \cdot (\alpha \cdot R_Y + \beta \cdot R_Z)$ with $\alpha = 0.76$ and $\beta = 0.24$ creates specific incentives that prior systems did not implement.

**Why this is a fundamental innovation:**

*   **Explicitly rewards error acknowledgment:** The reward structure ensures that "faithful acknowledgment of errors is rewarded over false claims of correctness." This is counterintuitive—most systems reward only correct outputs, inadvertently incentivizing models to hide uncertainty. DeepSeekMath-V2 rewards honest self-assessment even when the proof is imperfect.
*   **Asymmetric weighting prevents gaming:** The $\alpha = 0.76, \beta = 0.24$ split prioritizes proof quality while maintaining sufficient incentive for accurate self-evaluation. Too much weight on self-evaluation could encourage the model to produce poor proofs with honest criticism; too little would fail to develop verification capabilities. This tuning represents a non-obvious design choice that balances competing objectives.
*   **Enables autonomous iterative refinement:** With genuine self-verification, the generator can refine proofs without external verifier calls during inference. Figure 2 demonstrates this: Pass@1 improves substantially as maximum sequential attempts increase from 1 to 8, showing that "self-verification effectively guides iterative improvement" without human intervention.

**Comparison to prior work:** Reasoning models like DeepSeek-R1 incentivize reasoning through reinforcement learning (Guo et al., 2025), but they optimize for final answer correctness, not self-evaluation faithfulness. The proof generation prompt (Appendix A.1) explicitly warns "You CAN'T cheat! If you cheat, we will know, and you will be penalized!"—a framing that directly addresses the incentive alignment problem that prior work left implicit.

### 4.4 Verifier-Generator Synergy: Alternating Optimization That Maintains the Generation-Verification Gap

**What makes this novel:** The training process described in Section 3.1 employs alternating optimization where "in each iteration, we first optimized proof verification. The proof generator was then initialized from the verifier checkpoint and optimized for proof generation." Starting from the second iteration, the verifier is initialized with a checkpoint that consolidated both capabilities from the previous iteration through rejection fine-tuning.

This creates a synergistic cycle where the verifier improves the generator, and improved generators produce new proofs that challenge the verifier, creating training data for further verifier improvement (Section 2.3).

**Why this is significant:**

*   **Maintains the generation-verification gap:** A core requirement for self-improvement is that verification capability must exceed generation capability. If the generator surpasses the verifier, it can produce proofs the verifier cannot evaluate, breaking the improvement cycle. The alternating optimization with verifier-first training ensures the verifier stays ahead.
*   **Checkpoint consolidation leverages cross-capability learning:** Initializing the verifier from a checkpoint that includes generation capabilities allows it to understand what errors generators typically make, improving its ability to identify subtle issues. This bidirectional knowledge transfer is not present in systems where verifier and generator are trained independently.
*   **Enables sustained improvement over multiple iterations:** The automated labeling pipeline (Innovation 4.2) combined with alternating optimization means the system can continue improving without human annotation bottlenecks. This is crucial for tackling problems at the frontier of mathematical research where expert annotators are scarce.

**Comparison to prior work:** Prior RL-based reasoning systems typically train a single model with fixed reward functions. DeepSeekMath-V2's alternating optimization between two specialized models—each improving the other—represents a structural innovation in how mathematical reasoning systems are trained.

### 4.5 Distinguishing Fundamental Innovations from Incremental Improvements

Not all contributions in DeepSeekMath-V2 represent fundamental advances. It is important to distinguish what is truly novel from what builds on existing techniques:

| Contribution | Innovation Type | Rationale |
|--------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Meta-verification training | **Fundamental** | First system to explicitly optimize verifier faithfulness through second-order evaluation, addressing a previously unacknowledged failure mode |
| Automated labeling pipeline | **Fundamental** | Replaces human annotation with scaled verification compute, enabling continuous improvement without proportional human labor |
| Self-verification with honest assessment incentives | **Fundamental** | First to explicitly reward error acknowledgment over false correctness claims, with tuned $\alpha/\beta$ weighting |
| Three-level scoring rubric (0, 0.5, 1) | **Incremental** | Finer granularity than binary scoring exists in prior work, but the specific values are design choices rather than conceptual advances |
| GRPO for RL optimization | **Incremental** | Group Relative Policy Optimization was introduced in prior DeepSeek work (Shao et al., 2024); this paper applies it to a new domain |
| Natural language theorem proving | **Incremental** | Prior work (DeepMind DeepThink, Gemini-2.5 Pro) demonstrated LLM-based proof generation; this work improves the training methodology |

The fundamental innovations—meta-verification, automated labeling, and self-verification incentives—work together to create a system that can improve itself without constant human supervision. This represents a meaningful step toward AI systems capable of tackling open mathematical problems where reference solutions do not exist.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their claims about DeepSeekMath-V2's capabilities. We will walk through the evaluation methodology, analyze the quantitative results with specific numbers, and critically assess whether the experiments convincingly support the paper's central thesis: that self-verifiable mathematical reasoning is achievable and scalable without constant human annotation.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The authors employ a multi-tiered evaluation strategy designed to test both routine theorem-proving ability and performance on frontier mathematical challenges. Understanding the benchmark structure is essential because different competitions test different skills.

#### Evaluation Benchmarks

The evaluation spans six distinct benchmark categories (Section 3.2):

*   **In-House CNML-Level Problems:** 91 theorem-proving problems comparable to the Chinese National High School Mathematics League. These are categorized by mathematical domain:
    *   Algebra: 13 problems
    *   Geometry: 24 problems
    *   Number Theory: 19 problems
    *   Combinatorics: 24 problems
    *   Inequality: 11 problems
    
    This dataset allows domain-specific performance analysis to verify that improvements are not concentrated in a single mathematical area.

*   **IMO 2025:** 6 problems from the International Mathematical Olympiad, the premier global mathematics competition for pre-university students.

*   **CMO 2024:** 6 problems from the China Mathematical Olympiad, China's national championship.

*   **Putnam 2024:** 12 problems from the William Lowell Putnam Competition, the preeminent undergraduate mathematics competition in North America.

*   **ISL 2024:** 31 problems from the IMO Shortlist, a collection of problems proposed by participating countries and considered for potential inclusion in IMO 2024.

*   **IMO-ProofBench:** 60 problems developed by DeepMind, divided into:
    *   Basic set: 30 problems (pre-IMO to IMO-Medium difficulty)
    *   Advanced set: 30 problems (challenging problems simulating complete IMO examinations, up to IMO-Hard level)

#### Metrics and Evaluation Protocol

The authors use two primary evaluation protocols depending on the computational budget:

**One-Shot Generation (Section 3.3.1):** For the CNML-level problems, the authors generated 8 proof samples per problem for each evaluated model. Proof correctness was measured by **majority voting across 8 verification analyses** produced by their final verifier. This approach reduces variance from individual sample quality and leverages the verifier's assessment capability rather than relying on human grading for every sample.

**Sequential Refinement (Section 3.3.2):** For challenging problems where single-attempt generation often fails, the authors evaluate iterative self-verification. For each problem, they launched 32 independent refinement threads. Two metrics are reported:
*   **Pass@1:** The average score of the final proof from each thread
*   **Best@32:** The score of the best proof per problem, selected by self-assigned scores across all threads

This distinction is important: Pass@1 measures typical performance, while Best@32 measures the model's ability to identify its own best output—a critical capability for autonomous problem-solving.

**High-Compute Search (Section 3.3.3):** For the most challenging competitions (IMO, CMO, Putnam), the authors scaled both verification and generation compute. The approach maintains a pool of candidate proofs initialized with 64 proof samples, with 64 verification analyses generated for each. In each refinement iteration, the 64 highest-scoring proofs are selected based on average verification scores and paired with 8 randomly selected analyses prioritizing those identifying issues (scores 0 or 0.5). This process continues for up to 16 iterations or until a proof passes all 64 verification attempts.

**Human Expert Validation:** To validate automated verification results, mathematical experts assessed the highest-scoring proofs for IMO 2025, CMO 2024, and Putnam 2024. This step is crucial because it confirms that the verifier's scores align with human judgment on frontier problems.

#### Baselines

The authors compare against several state-of-the-art systems (Section 3.3):

*   **GPT-5-Thinking-High** (OpenAI, 2025): A frontier reasoning model optimized for quantitative tasks
*   **Gemini 2.5-Pro** (DeepMind, 2025): A large language model with demonstrated self-verification capabilities
*   **DeepMind DeepThink (IMO Gold)** (Luong and Lockhart, 2025): An internal DeepMind variant that achieved gold-medal performance at IMO 2025
*   **Other baselines on IMO-ProofBench:** Claude Sonnet 4, DeepSeek R1, Qwen3-235B, Grok 4, and Gemini 2.5 Pro with various configurations (Figure 3)

It is important to note that some baseline results are sourced from Luong et al. (2025), while DeepSeekMath-V2 was evaluated by the authors' own experts following grading guidelines. This introduces a potential confound: different evaluation teams may apply scoring criteria differently, though the authors mitigate this by following published grading guidelines.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

The experimental results span three computational regimes: one-shot generation, sequential refinement, and high-compute search. Each regime tests different aspects of the system's capabilities.

#### One-Shot Generation Performance

Figure 1 presents average proof scores on CNML-level problems by category. DeepSeekMath-V2 consistently outperforms both GPT-5-Thinking-High and Gemini 2.5-Pro across all five mathematical domains:

| Category | DeepSeekMath-V2 | GPT-5-Thinking-High | Gemini 2.5-Pro |
|----------|-----------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Algebra | 0.54 | 0.36 | 0.35 |
| Geometry | 0.60 | 0.47 | 0.54 |
| Number Theory | 0.59 | 0.38 | 0.35 |
| Combinatorics | 0.52 | 0.35 | 0.32 |
| Inequality | 0.45 | 0.17 | 0.15 |

Several observations warrant attention:

*   **Consistent superiority:** DeepSeekMath-V2 achieves higher scores in every category, with margins ranging from 0.06 (Geometry vs. Gemini 2.5-Pro) to 0.30 (Inequality vs. GPT-5-Thinking-High). This consistency suggests the training methodology generalizes across mathematical domains rather than overfitting to specific problem types.

*   **Largest gains in Inequality:** The most dramatic improvement appears in Inequality problems, where DeepSeekMath-V2 scores 0.45 compared to 0.17 for GPT-5-Thinking-High and 0.15 for Gemini 2.5-Pro. Inequality proofs often require careful handling of boundary conditions and algebraic manipulations—precisely the type of rigorous step-by-step reasoning that self-verification training targets.

*   **Absolute performance levels:** Even the best scores (0.60 in Geometry) remain below 1.0, indicating that one-shot generation still struggles with CNML-level problems. This motivates the sequential refinement approach discussed below.

#### Sequential Refinement with Self-Verification

Figure 2 demonstrates proof quality improvement through sequential refinement on IMO Shortlist 2024 problems. The maximum sequential iterations vary from 1 (no refinement) to 8 (initial generation plus up to 7 refinements based on self-verification):

| Max Iterations | Pass@1 | Best@32 |
|----------------|--------|---------|
| 1 | 0.15 | 0.26 |
| 2 | 0.18 | 0.29 |
| 3 | 0.21 | 0.33 |
| 4 | 0.22 | 0.39 |
| 5 | 0.24 | 0.39 |
| 6 | 0.24 | 0.40 |
| 7 | 0.26 | 0.40 |
| 8 | 0.27 | 0.42 |

Two key patterns emerge:

*   **Pass@1 improvement:** The average proof score increases from 0.15 at 1 iteration to 0.27 at 8 iterations—an 80% relative improvement. This demonstrates that "self-verification effectively guides iterative improvement" (Section 3.3.2). The model does not merely generate more samples; it systematically addresses identified issues across iterations.

*   **Best@32 vs. Pass@1 gap:** At 8 iterations, Best@32 (0.42) substantially exceeds Pass@1 (0.27). The authors note this demonstrates "our generator's ability to accurately assess proof quality" (Section 3.3.2). If the model could not distinguish high-quality from flawed proofs, selecting the best output across 32 threads would not yield significantly better results than the average.

*   **Diminishing returns:** The improvement curve flattens after 4-5 iterations. Pass@1 increases by 0.07 from iteration 1 to 4, but only 0.05 from iteration 4 to 8. This suggests that some proofs require more fundamental restructuring than iterative refinement can provide, or that the model reaches a capability ceiling for certain problem types.

#### High-Compute Search Results

Table 1 presents the most significant results: performance on premier mathematics competitions with scaled test-time compute.

This represents the first time an AI system has surpassed the best human performance on this competition. The model solved 11 of 12 problems completely and the remaining problem with minor errors.

**IMO 2025 and CMO 2024:** Achieving gold-medal performance in both competitions (5 of 6 IMO problems, 4 of 6 CMO problems plus partial credit) demonstrates that the approach generalizes beyond a single competition format. Gold-medal thresholds typically require solving 4-5 of 6 problems, which DeepSeekMath-V2 achieves.

**IMO-ProofBench Comparison (Figure 3):** On the standardized IMO-ProofBench benchmark, DeepSeekMath-V2 (Heavy) achieves:

This is evident in both the IMO 2025 results (1 problem not fully solved) and the Advanced IMO-ProofBench results (61.9% vs. 65.7% for DeepThink). This limitation is important: while the approach achieves gold-medal performance overall, it does not yet solve every problem at the frontier of competition mathematics.

### 5.3 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

The experiments provide strong but conditional support for the paper's central claims. We must distinguish between what the results definitively demonstrate and what remains uncertain.

#### Claims Strongly Supported

**Claim 1: Self-verifiable reasoning improves proof quality.** The sequential refinement results (Figure 2) provide direct evidence. Pass@1 improves from 0.15 to 0.27 as iterations increase from 1 to 8, and this improvement is driven by the model's ability to identify and address issues in its own proofs. The Best@32 metric further confirms that the model can accurately assess which proofs are highest quality.

**Claim 2: The approach achieves competition-level performance.** The high-compute search results (Table 1) definitively demonstrate gold-medal performance on IMO 2025 and CMO 2024, plus a near-perfect score on Putnam 2024 that exceeds human performance. These are objective, externally-validatable results.

**Claim 3: Automated labeling can replace human annotation.** Section 2.3 states that "in the last two training iterations, this fully automated pipeline replaced human annotation entirely" with "quality checks confirmed that the automated labels aligned well with expert judgments." However, the paper does not provide quantitative metrics on this alignment (e.g., agreement rates between automated and human labels). This claim is plausible but less rigorously validated than the competition results.

#### Claims with Limited Support

**Claim 4: Meta-verification improves verifier faithfulness.** Section 2.1.2 reports that "the average quality score of the verifier's proof analyses—as evaluated by the meta-verifier—improved from 0.85 to 0.96, while maintaining the same accuracy in proof score prediction." This is strong evidence for the specific training intervention. However, the paper does not include an ablation study showing competition performance with vs. without meta-verification. We cannot quantify how much meta-verification contributed to the final IMO/Putnam results versus other factors like scaled compute or the verifier-generator synergy.

**Claim 5: The approach scales without human bottlenecks.** The automated labeling pipeline is described, but the paper does not report:
*   The exact values of `$n$`, `$m$`, and `$k$` parameters for the automated labeling process
*   Computational costs of the automated labeling vs. human annotation
*   How many training iterations were completed with fully automated labeling

Section 3.3.3 mentions using 64 verification analyses per proof in the high-compute search setting, suggesting these parameters scale with available compute, but specific training-time values are not provided.

#### Missing Ablation Studies

The paper lacks several ablation studies that would strengthen the causal claims:

*   **`$\alpha/\beta$` weighting:** The generator reward function uses `$\alpha = 0.76$` and `$\beta = 0.24$` (Section 2.2.2), but no ablation shows how different weightings affect performance. Would `$\alpha = 0.5, \beta = 0.5$` produce better self-verification at the cost of proof quality?

*   **Meta-verification impact:** As noted above, there is no comparison of final competition performance with vs. without the meta-verification layer.

*   **Verifier-first vs. generator-first training:** Section 3.1 describes alternating optimization starting with verifier training, but no ablation tests whether this ordering matters.

*   **Number of verification samples:** The high-compute search uses 64 verification analyses, but the paper does not explore how performance scales with fewer samples (e.g., 16, 32) to understand the compute-accuracy tradeoff.

These omissions are common in systems papers with significant engineering complexity, but they limit our ability to isolate which components drive the strongest improvements.

#### Failure Cases and Limitations

The authors are relatively transparent about limitations:

*   **Hardest problems remain challenging:** As noted in Section 3.3.3, "the hardest IMO-level problems remain challenging for our model." The Advanced IMO-ProofBench score of 61.9% (vs. 65.7% for DeepThink) indicates that at the frontier, the model still struggles with a meaningful fraction of problems.

*   **Context length constraints:** Section 3.3.2 notes that "models often cannot generate comprehensive and rigorous proofs in a single attempt within the 128K token limit." When this occurs, the generator "recognizes its proof is invalid through self-verification but lacks the context length to resolve all identified issues in a single attempt." This is a fundamental limitation of the current architecture.

*   **Compute requirements:** The high-compute search approach requires substantial resources (64 proof samples × 64 verification analyses × up to 16 iterations). The paper does not report the computational cost, making it difficult to assess practical deployability.

*   **Domain variation:** Figure 1 shows performance varies by mathematical domain, with Inequality (0.45) scoring lower than Geometry (0.60). The paper does not analyze why certain domains are more challenging or whether targeted training could close these gaps.

### 5.4 Summary of Experimental Evidence

The experiments provide compelling evidence for the feasibility of self-verifiable mathematical reasoning, with some important caveats:

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Key Supporting Data |
|-------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Self-verification improves proof quality | **Strong** | Figure 2: Pass@1 improves 80% from iteration 1 to 8 |
| Competition-level performance achievable | **Strong** | Table 1: 118/120 on Putnam, gold on IMO 2025 |
| Automated labeling replaces human annotation | **Moderate** | Section 2.3: "last two iterations" automated, but no quantitative alignment metrics |
| Meta-verification improves faithfulness | **Moderate** | Section 2.1.2: 0.85→0.96 quality score, but no competition-level ablation |
| Generalizes across mathematical domains | **Strong** | Figure 1: Consistent superiority across all 5 CNML categories |
| Surpasses human performance | **Strong** | Putnam 2024: 118/120 vs. human maximum of 90 |

The most convincing results are the competition performances, which are objective and externally comparable. The weakest evidence supports the scalability claims, as the paper provides limited detail on computational costs and automated labeling quality metrics.

For a reader seeking to understand whether this approach represents a genuine advance: the competition results definitively show that self-verifiable reasoning can achieve gold-medal performance. Whether this approach is more efficient or scalable than alternatives (e.g., formal proof assistants, pure scaling of generation compute) requires additional investigation beyond what this paper provides.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

No technical approach is without constraints. Understanding where DeepSeekMath-V2 struggles is as important as understanding where it succeeds—this reveals both the current boundaries of self-verifiable reasoning and the research questions that remain open. This section examines the assumptions underlying the approach, the scenarios it does not address, the computational and scalability constraints, and the weaknesses that future work must tackle.

### 6.1 Computational Trade-offs: Performance Requires Scaled Test-Time Compute

The most significant trade-off in DeepSeekMath-V2 is between **performance and computational cost**. The model's strongest results depend heavily on scaling test-time compute rather than relying on one-shot generation capability.

**Evidence from the paper:**

*   **One-shot vs. high-compute gap:** Figure 1 shows one-shot generation on CNML-level problems achieves mean proof scores between 0.45 and 0.60 across domains. However, Table 1 shows that achieving gold-medal performance on IMO 2025 and the 118/120 Putnam 2024 score required "scaled test-time compute" with:
    *   64 proof samples per problem
    *   64 verification analyses per proof
    *   Up to 16 refinement iterations
    
    This represents a massive computational multiplier. Each problem requires generating and verifying thousands of token sequences before producing a final answer.

*   **Sequential refinement diminishing returns:** Figure 2 demonstrates that Pass@1 improves from 0.15 at 1 iteration to 0.27 at 8 iterations on IMO Shortlist 2024 problems—an 80% relative improvement. However, the curve flattens significantly after 4-5 iterations, suggesting that additional compute yields progressively smaller gains for certain problem types.

**Why this matters:**

The computational requirements create a practical deployment constraint. While the approach proves that self-verifiable reasoning is *feasible*, it does not yet demonstrate that it is *efficient*. For applications requiring low-latency responses or operating under compute budgets, the one-shot performance (Figure 1) may be insufficient for high-stakes mathematical reasoning.

**Open question:** The paper does not report the exact computational cost of the high-compute search in terms of GPU hours or token counts. Without this information, it is difficult to assess whether the approach is economically viable for widespread deployment or remains a research demonstration requiring substantial resources.

### 6.2 Architectural Constraints: Context Length Limits Proof Complexity

A fundamental architectural limitation emerges from the **context window constraints** of the underlying language model. Section 3.3.2 explicitly states:

> "For challenging problems from competitions like IMO and CMO, models often cannot generate comprehensive and rigorous proofs in a single attempt within the 128K token limit. When this occurs, our proof generator recognizes its proof is invalid through self-verification but lacks the context length to resolve all identified issues in a single attempt."

**What this means:**

*   **Proofs exceed available context:** Some mathematical proofs—particularly those requiring extensive case analysis, multiple lemmas, or detailed algebraic manipulation—simply cannot fit within the model's context window while maintaining all necessary reasoning steps.

*   **Self-verification identifies but cannot fix:** The generator can recognize through self-verification that its proof is incomplete or erroneous, but it cannot generate a corrected version within the same context budget. This creates a failure mode where the model knows it is wrong but cannot produce a right answer.

*   **Iterative refinement hits ceiling:** The sequential refinement approach (Figure 2) helps, but each iteration consumes additional context. If the proof itself requires more than 128K tokens to express rigorously, no amount of refinement will succeed.

**Why this is a hard constraint:**

Unlike algorithmic improvements that can be addressed through better training or architecture tweaks, context length is a fundamental limitation of the transformer architecture. Until models can reliably handle longer contexts or compress reasoning more efficiently, some proofs will remain out of reach regardless of verification capability.

### 6.3 Domain Variation: Performance Is Not Uniform Across Mathematics

Figure 1 reveals significant **performance variation across mathematical domains**, suggesting that the approach does not generalize equally well to all types of mathematical reasoning:

| Category | DeepSeekMath-V2 Score |
|----------|----------------------|
| Geometry | 0.60 |
| Number Theory | 0.59 |
| Algebra | 0.54 |
| Combinatorics | 0.52 |
| Inequality | 0.45 |

**Key observations:**

*   **Inequality proofs are hardest:** The 0.45 score on Inequality problems is 25% lower than the 0.60 score on Geometry. Inequality proofs often require careful handling of boundary conditions, multiple cases, and subtle algebraic manipulations—precisely the type of reasoning where small errors cascade.

*   **Geometry advantages:** Geometry problems may benefit from more structured reasoning patterns (e.g., constructing auxiliary lines, applying known theorems) that are easier for the model to learn and verify.

*   **No explanation provided:** The paper does not analyze *why* these domain differences exist or propose targeted interventions to close the gaps. This leaves open whether the variation reflects inherent difficulty differences or limitations in the training data distribution.

**Implication for research-level mathematics:**

If the model struggles disproportionately with certain mathematical domains, its utility for open-ended research becomes constrained. A researcher working on inequality-heavy problems (common in analysis and number theory) would receive less reliable assistance than one working on geometry problems.

### 6.4 Verification Reliability: Natural Language Lacks Formal Guarantees

Unlike formal proof assistants such as **Lean** or **Isabelle** (Section 4), DeepSeekMath-V2 operates entirely in natural language. This creates a fundamental reliability trade-off:

| Aspect | Formal Proof Assistants | DeepSeekMath-V2 |
|--------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Correctness guarantee | Compilation ensures correctness | Verifier assessment can err |
| Accessibility | Requires formal language expertise | Natural language interface |
| Flexibility | Rigid syntax requirements | Flexible reasoning expression |
| Verification cost | Automated and instant | Requires LLM inference |

**The core vulnerability:**

Section 2.1.2 acknowledges that verifiers can hallucinate issues even with meta-verification. While the meta-verifier improved analysis quality scores from 0.85 to 0.96 on validation data, this still leaves a 4% error rate in verification assessments. For high-stakes applications (e.g., verifying proofs for publication or safety-critical systems), this uncertainty may be unacceptable.

**Evidence of remaining errors:**

*   Table 1 shows that on IMO 2025, one problem was not fully solved, and on CMO 2024, one problem received only partial credit. The paper states that "for problems not fully solved, our generator typically identifies the genuine issues in its proofs"—but this self-assessment cannot be independently verified without human expert review.

*   Section 3.3.3 notes that "mathematical experts assessed the highest-scoring proofs" for competition results, meaning the final validation still relies on human judgment rather than automated guarantees.

**Trade-off summary:**

DeepSeekMath-V2 sacrifices the formal correctness guarantees of systems like Lean in exchange for natural language accessibility and flexibility. For exploratory mathematics and education, this trade-off is favorable. For formal verification requiring absolute certainty, formal proof assistants remain necessary.

### 6.5 Training Data Dependencies: Early Iterations Require Human Annotation

While Section 2.3 emphasizes that "in the last two training iterations, this fully automated pipeline replaced human annotation entirely," the approach still depends on human expertise in several ways:

**Human annotation requirements:**

*   **Initial dataset creation:** Section 2.1.1 describes that mathematical experts scored proofs according to evaluation rubrics to create the initial RL dataset `$\mathcal{D}_v$`. This required expert time for 17,503 problems from AoPS contests.

*   **Meta-verifier training:** Section 2.1.2 states that "mathematical experts scored the quality of verifier responses according to `$\mathcal{I}_{mv}$`, creating dataset `$\mathcal{D}_{mv}$`." This represents additional expert annotation beyond the initial verifier training.

*   **Validation of automated labels:** Section 2.3 mentions "quality checks confirmed that the automated labels aligned well with expert judgments" but does not specify how many samples were checked or what agreement thresholds were used.

**Scalability concern:**

The claim that automated labeling "replaced human annotation entirely" applies only to the *last two* training iterations. Earlier iterations required substantial human expert time. For teams without access to mathematical experts (e.g., smaller research groups or commercial applications), replicating this training pipeline may be infeasible.

**Missing quantitative validation:**

Section 5.3 noted that the paper provides no quantitative metrics on automated vs. human label agreement (e.g., Cohen's kappa, percentage agreement). Without this data, readers cannot assess how closely the automated pipeline approximates expert judgment or whether errors compound over multiple training iterations.

### 6.6 Missing Ablation Studies: Component Contributions Are Unclear

A significant limitation of the paper is the **absence of ablation studies** isolating the contribution of individual components. Section 5.3 identified several missing comparisons:

| Missing Ablation | Why It Matters |
|-----------------|----------------|
| `$\alpha/\beta$` weighting in generator reward | The paper uses `$\alpha = 0.76, \beta = 0.24$` (Section 2.2.2) but does not test alternatives. Would different weightings improve self-verification or proof quality? |
| Meta-verification impact on competition performance | Section 2.1.2 shows meta-verification improves verifier faithfulness (0.85→0.96), but no ablation shows how this affects final IMO/Putnam scores. |
| Verifier-first vs. generator-first training order | Section 3.1 describes alternating optimization starting with verifier training, but no ablation tests whether this ordering is critical. |
| Number of verification samples | High-compute search uses 64 verification analyses (Section 3.3.3), but performance at 16 or 32 samples is not reported. |

**Why ablations matter:**

Without these comparisons, readers cannot determine:
*   Which components are essential vs. optional refinements
*   Whether the computational cost of meta-verification is justified by performance gains
*   How to adapt the approach under resource constraints (e.g., fewer verification samples)

This is a common limitation in systems papers with significant engineering complexity, but it limits the reproducibility and practical guidance the paper can offer to other researchers.

### 6.7 Frontier Problem Limitations: Hardest IMO Problems Remain Unsolved

Despite achieving gold-medal performance overall, Section 3.3.3 explicitly acknowledges:

> "We observe that the hardest IMO-level problems remain challenging for our model."

**Evidence:**

*   **IMO 2025:** 5 of 6 problems solved (83.3%), with one problem not fully solved (Table 1)
*   **IMO-ProofBench Advanced:** 61.9% score vs. DeepMind DeepThink's 65.7% (Figure 3)
*   **CMO 2024:** 4 of 6 problems fully solved, with one receiving partial credit (73.8% total)

**What this reveals:**

The approach achieves *competitive* performance at the frontier but does not yet *dominate* it. The hardest problems—typically those requiring novel insights, creative constructions, or multi-step reasoning chains—still defeat the model. This suggests that self-verification alone is insufficient for the most challenging mathematical reasoning; additional capabilities (e.g., better search strategies, external tool use, or architectural innovations) may be necessary.

**Implication for research mathematics:**

Competition mathematics, while challenging, has known solutions and well-defined correctness criteria. Open research problems lack both. If the model struggles with the hardest competition problems where solutions exist, its reliability on genuinely open problems (where no solution is known to verify against) remains uncertain.

### 6.8 Summary of Limitations and Trade-offs

| Limitation | Severity | Evidence | Mitigation Path |
|------------|----------|----------|-----------------|
| High computational cost | **High** | 64×64×16 compute multiplier (Section 3.3.3) | More efficient verification, better one-shot capability |
| Context length constraints | **High** | 128K token limit prevents some proofs (Section 3.3.2) | Longer context models, proof compression techniques |
| Domain variation | **Medium** | Inequality 0.45 vs. Geometry 0.60 (Figure 1) | Domain-specific training, targeted data augmentation |

**Fair assessment:**

These limitations do not invalidate the paper's contributions. DeepSeekMath-V2 demonstrates that self-verifiable mathematical reasoning is *feasible* and can achieve *competition-level performance*. However, the limitations reveal that the approach is not yet a complete solution for all mathematical reasoning challenges. The trade-offs—particularly between computational cost and performance, and between natural language flexibility and formal guarantees—define the research agenda for future work.

For practitioners considering this approach: it is well-suited for applications where high-quality mathematical reasoning is valuable and computational resources are available, but less suitable for low-latency applications or scenarios requiring formal correctness guarantees. For researchers: the identified limitations point to concrete directions for improvement, particularly in efficiency, domain generalization, and frontier problem capability.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

DeepSeekMath-V2 represents more than an incremental improvement in mathematical reasoning—it establishes a new paradigm for how AI systems can approach problems without reference solutions. This section examines how the work reshapes the research landscape, identifies concrete follow-up directions it enables, outlines practical applications, and provides guidance on when this approach should be preferred over alternatives.

### 7.1 Shifting the Research Landscape: From Answer Matching to Rigor Verification

The most significant contribution of this work is demonstrating that **self-verifiable reasoning without ground-truth answers is achievable**. This fundamentally changes what problems AI systems can meaningfully tackle.

#### The Paradigm Shift

Prior mathematical AI research operated under an implicit constraint: **problems must have verifiable answers**. As Section 1 notes, the dominant RL approach rewards models "based on whether their predicted final answers to quantitative reasoning problems match ground-truth answers." This limitation confined AI progress to domains where:

*   Numerical answers exist and can be checked programmatically
*   Reference solutions are available for training supervision
*   Correctness can be reduced to scalar metrics

DeepSeekMath-V2 breaks this constraint by showing that models can learn to assess **logical rigor** rather than answer correctness. The verifier training objective (Section 2.1.1) optimizes for identifying issues in proofs according to rubrics `$\mathcal{I}_v$`, not matching predetermined solutions. This enables:

*   **Open problem exploration:** Systems can evaluate proofs for problems where no solution is known—a prerequisite for AI-assisted mathematical research
*   **Theorem proving without formalization:** Unlike Lean or Isabelle (Section 4), proofs remain in natural language, lowering the barrier for mathematicians to engage with AI tools
*   **Iterative self-improvement:** The generator-verifier cycle (Section 2.3) creates a sustainable improvement loop that does not require expanding human annotation

#### Competitive Positioning

The results in Table 1 and Figure 3 position DeepSeekMath-V2 as a **competitive alternative to both pure natural language models and formal proof assistants**:

| Approach | Strengths | Weaknesses | DeepSeekMath-V2 Position |
|----------|-----------|------------|--------------------------|
| Final-answer RL (AIME/HMMT) | Fast training, clear rewards | Cannot handle theorem proving | Surpasses via verification-based rewards |
| Formal proof assistants (Lean) | Guaranteed correctness | Requires formal language, intensive computation | Offers natural language accessibility with high (but not guaranteed) rigor |
| Proprietary reasoning models (DeepThink) | Strong performance | Closed methodology | Open-sourced training framework (Section 1) |

The 118/120 Putnam 2024 score—exceeding the human maximum of 90 (Section 1)—serves as concrete evidence that natural language verification can match or exceed human expert performance on rigorous mathematical tasks. This result challenges the assumption that formal methods are necessary for frontier mathematical reasoning.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly acknowledges that "much work remains" (Abstract) and identifies several concrete research directions that build directly on its contributions.

#### 7.2.1 Improving Verification Efficiency

**Current limitation:** The high-compute search requires 64 proof samples × 64 verification analyses × up to 16 iterations (Section 3.3.3). This computational multiplier makes the approach impractical for many applications.

**Research questions:**
*   **Can verification sample counts be reduced?** The paper does not ablate the number of verification analyses (Section 5.3 notes this gap). Follow-up work should explore whether 16 or 32 analyses achieve comparable reliability to 64, potentially reducing compute costs by 50-75%.
*   **Can meta-verification be distilled?** The meta-verifier `$\pi_\eta$` improves faithfulness from 0.85 to 0.96 (Section 2.1.2), but requires separate inference. Research could explore distilling meta-verification capabilities into the primary verifier, reducing the inference overhead.
*   **Can early stopping be learned?** Currently, refinement continues for up to 16 iterations or until all 64 verifications pass. A learned stopping criterion could terminate refinement earlier for easier problems, saving compute.

#### 7.2.2 Addressing Domain Variation

**Current limitation:** Figure 1 shows significant performance variation across mathematical domains, with Inequality scoring 0.45 compared to Geometry at 0.60—a 25% gap.

**Research questions:**
*   **What causes domain differences?** The paper does not analyze why Inequality proofs are harder (Section 6.3). Follow-up work should examine whether this reflects training data distribution, inherent problem difficulty, or verification challenges specific to inequality reasoning (e.g., boundary conditions, case analysis).
*   **Can domain-specific training close gaps?** Targeted data augmentation for underperforming domains could improve overall capability. This is particularly important because inequality-heavy reasoning is common in analysis and number theory research.
*   **Should verifiers be domain-specialized?** A single verifier may not optimally assess all proof types. Research could explore ensemble verifiers where different models specialize in different mathematical domains.

#### 7.2.3 Extending to Open Research Problems

**Current limitation:** All evaluation benchmarks have known solutions (IMO, Putnam, etc.). The paper claims the approach enables work on open problems (Section 1), but this remains untested.

**Research questions:**
*   **Can the system identify promising conjectures?** Beyond verifying proofs, could the generator propose conjectures that the verifier assesses for plausibility? This would extend the framework from proof verification to conjecture generation.
*   **How does performance degrade without known solutions?** The automated labeling pipeline (Section 2.3) relies on verifier agreement, but without ground truth, systematic errors could compound. Research should quantify error accumulation over multiple training iterations without human validation.
*   **Can human-AI collaboration be optimized?** For research problems, mathematicians could review verifier-identified issues rather than full proofs. Studies should measure how this division of labor affects research productivity.

#### 7.2.4 Hybrid Natural Language + Formal Verification

**Current limitation:** Natural language verification lacks formal guarantees. Section 6.4 notes a 4% verification error rate remains even after meta-verification.

**Research opportunities:**
*   **Informal-to-formal translation:** DeepSeekMath-V2 could generate natural language proofs that are then translated to Lean or Isabelle for formal verification. The verifier's issue identification could guide the translation process, flagging steps requiring careful formalization.
*   **Formal guidance for informal reasoning:** Conversely, formal proof assistants could provide intermediate verification checkpoints during natural language proof generation, combining the accessibility of natural language with formal guarantees at critical steps.
*   **Confidence calibration:** Research could explore whether verifier confidence scores correlate with actual correctness, enabling the system to flag proofs requiring human review.

#### 7.2.5 Context Length and Proof Complexity

**Current limitation:** Section 3.3.2 explicitly states that "models often cannot generate comprehensive and rigorous proofs in a single attempt within the 128K token limit."

**Research directions:**
*   **Proof compression techniques:** Can proofs be represented more efficiently without losing rigor? This could involve structured representations, lemma abstraction, or hierarchical proof organization.
*   **Longer context architectures:** As models with larger context windows become available, research should quantify how proof quality scales with context length.
*   **Modular proof generation:** Instead of generating complete proofs in one context, the system could generate and verify lemmas separately, then compose them. This would require extending the verifier to assess proof composition validity.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

DeepSeekMath-V2's capabilities enable several concrete applications beyond competition mathematics.

#### 7.3.1 Mathematical Education and Tutoring

**Application:** The self-verification capability makes the system well-suited for educational contexts where explaining errors matters more than providing answers.

**Why this works:** The verifier's output format (Appendix A.2) requires detailed issue identification: "for erroneous steps, explain the reason for the error and the impact of that error on the solution." This matches pedagogical needs where students benefit from understanding *why* their reasoning is flawed.

**Implementation considerations:**
*   The three-level scoring rubric (0, 0.5, 1) provides nuanced feedback beyond binary correct/incorrect
*   Self-verification training (Section 2.2.2) means the system can model good self-assessment behavior for students
*   One-shot generation (Figure 1 scores 0.45-0.60) may suffice for educational problems, avoiding high-compute search costs

#### 7.3.2 Research Paper Review and Verification

**Application:** Mathematical journals could use verification systems to pre-screen submissions for logical errors before human peer review.

**Why this is feasible:** The meta-verification improvement (0.85→0.96, Section 2.1.2) demonstrates that automated systems can achieve high faithfulness in issue identification. For preliminary screening, catching obvious errors reduces reviewer burden.

**Limitations to acknowledge:**
*   The 4% remaining verification error rate (Section 6.4) means human review remains necessary for final acceptance
*   Novel proof techniques may be flagged as errors if they deviate from training distribution
*   The system should be positioned as an assistant to reviewers, not a replacement

#### 7.3.3 Formal Proof Development Assistance

**Application:** Mathematicians working in Lean or Isabelle could use DeepSeekMath-V2 to generate informal proof sketches before formalization.

**Why this adds value:** Section 4 notes that "advancing natural language theorem proving will significantly benefit formal reasoning" by providing better informal insights to guide formal systems. The workflow would be:

1.  Generate natural language proof with DeepSeekMath-V2
2.  Use verifier to identify and resolve issues informally
3.  Translate verified informal proof to formal language
4.  Complete formal verification in Lean/Isabelle

**Advantage over direct formal generation:** Informal reasoning is more flexible and accessible. Mathematicians can review and modify natural language proofs more easily than formal code, making the human-AI collaboration more efficient.

#### 7.3.4 Scientific Reasoning Beyond Mathematics

**Application:** The verification framework could extend to other domains requiring rigorous step-by-step reasoning, such as physics derivations, algorithm correctness proofs, or legal argument analysis.

**Transfer requirements:**
*   Domain-specific evaluation rubrics `$\mathcal{I}_v$` would need to be developed (similar to Appendix A.2)
*   Training data with expert-annotated quality scores would be required (similar to `$\mathcal{D}_v$` in Section 2.1.1)
*   The meta-verification framework (Section 2.1.2) should transfer directly, as it evaluates reasoning faithfulness rather than domain content

**Caution:** Domains with less formal structure than mathematics may present greater verification challenges. The success in mathematics relies on well-defined notions of logical validity that may not exist in all reasoning domains.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For researchers and practitioners considering this approach, the following guidance clarifies when DeepSeekMath-V2's methodology is appropriate and how to integrate it effectively.

#### 7.4.1 When to Prefer This Approach

**Choose DeepSeekMath-V2's methodology when:**

| Scenario | Rationale |
|----------|-----------|
| **No ground-truth answers available** | Final-answer RL cannot apply; verification-based rewards are necessary (Section 1) |
| **Natural language output required** | Formal proof assistants require formal language; this approach maintains accessibility (Section 4) |
| **Iterative refinement is feasible** | The approach excels when multiple verification passes are possible (Figure 2 shows 80% improvement from 1→8 iterations) |
| **Human expert time is limited** | Automated labeling (Section 2.3) reduces annotation burden after initial setup |
| **Proof rigor matters more than speed** | High-compute search achieves best results but requires substantial resources (Section 6.1) |

**Choose alternative approaches when:**

| Scenario | Better Alternative | Rationale |
|----------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Low-latency inference required** | Final-answer RL models | One-shot generation scores (0.45-0.60, Figure 1) may be insufficient; high-compute search is too slow |
| **Formal guarantees required** | Lean/Isabelle | Natural language verification has 4% error rate (Section 6.4); formal compilation provides certainty |
| **Simple quantitative problems** | Standard reasoning models | AIME/HMMT-style problems are well-served by final-answer rewards (Section 1) |
| **Limited compute budget** | Single-pass generation | The 64×64×16 compute multiplier (Section 3.3.3) may be prohibitive |

#### 7.4.2 Implementation Prerequisites

**Minimum requirements for reproducing this approach:**

*   **Base model:** The paper builds on DeepSeek-V3.2-Exp-Base (Section 1). Similar capability levels are likely necessary for the verification training to converge.
*   **Expert annotators:** Initial training requires mathematical experts to score proofs (Section 2.1.1: 17,503 problems) and verifier analyses (Section 2.1.2: meta-verification dataset `$\mathcal{D}_{mv}$`). The automated pipeline only replaces annotation in later iterations.
*   **RL infrastructure:** Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) is used (Section 3.1). Teams without RL training infrastructure would need to develop or license this capability.
*   **Compute budget:** The high-compute search configuration (Section 3.3.3) suggests substantial inference compute is needed for best results. Exact GPU-hour costs are not reported (Section 5.3 notes this gap).

#### 7.4.3 Key Hyperparameters to Consider

The paper specifies several critical hyperparameters that practitioners should understand:

| Parameter | Value | Role | Tuning Guidance |
|-----------|-------|------|-----------------|
| `$\alpha$` (proof quality weight) | 0.76 | Generator reward weighting (Section 2.2.2) | Higher values prioritize proof quality over self-evaluation accuracy |
| `$\beta$` (self-evaluation weight) | 0.24 | Generator reward weighting (Section 2.2.2) | Lower values risk underdeveloping verification capability; no ablation provided |
| Score levels | `{0, 0.5, 1}` | Verification granularity (Section 2.1.1) | Three levels capture minor errors; binary may lose signal, more levels may be harder to learn |
| Verification samples (high-compute) | 64 | Per-proof analyses (Section 3.3.3) | Higher values improve reliability but increase compute; optimal value not ablated |
| Refinement iterations | Up to 16 | Sequential refinement limit (Section 3.3.3) | Diminishing returns after 4-5 iterations (Figure 2); tune based on latency requirements |

**Critical note:** The `$\alpha/\beta$` weighting has no ablation study (Section 5.3). Practitioners should experiment with different values for their specific use cases, as the optimal balance may vary by domain.

#### 7.4.4 Integration Patterns

**Pattern 1: Verifier-Only Integration**
*   Use the trained verifier to assess proofs generated by other models
*   Lower integration cost than full generator training
*   Leverages the 0.96 faithfulness score (Section 2.1.2) for reliable evaluation

**Pattern 2: Generator with External Verification**
*   Use the generator with a separate verifier instance for refinement
*   Enables iterative improvement without training self-verification
*   Matches the sequential refinement evaluation (Figure 2)

**Pattern 3: Full Self-Verification**
*   Deploy the generator with built-in self-verification (Section 2.2.2)
*   Single model handles both generation and evaluation
*   Most efficient for inference but requires the full training pipeline

**Pattern 4: Human-in-the-Loop**
*   Use verifier to flag potential issues for human review
*   Humans make final correctness determinations
*   Balances automation with expert oversight for high-stakes applications

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision: Toward AI-Assisted Mathematical Research

The authors conclude by expressing hope that this research "contributes to the goal of creating self-verifiable AI systems that can solve research-level mathematics" (Section 5). This vision, while ambitious, is now grounded in demonstrated capability rather than speculation.

#### What This Work Enables

1.  **Proof exploration without reference solutions:** Researchers can use the system to explore proof strategies for open problems, with the verifier assessing rigor even when correctness cannot be confirmed against a known answer.

2.  **Scalable expertise:** The automated labeling pipeline (Section 2.3) means verification capability can improve without proportional increases in human expert time. This is essential for tackling problems where few humans possess the expertise to evaluate solutions.

3.  **Transparent reasoning:** Unlike black-box answer generation, the verifier's issue identification (Appendix A.2) provides interpretable feedback about *why* a proof succeeds or fails. This transparency is critical for mathematicians to trust and build upon AI-generated reasoning.

#### Remaining Gaps to Research-Level Mathematics

The paper is honest that "significant challenges remain" (Abstract). Key gaps include:

*   **Frontier problem performance:** The hardest IMO problems remain unsolved (Section 3.3.3), and IMO-ProofBench Advanced scores 61.9% vs. DeepThink's 65.7% (Figure 3). Research problems may exceed even IMO difficulty.
*   **Novelty vs. correctness:** Competition problems have known solutions; research problems require novel insights. The current system optimizes for correctness, not creativity.
*   **Collaboration workflows:** How mathematicians should interact with these systems—what to automate, what to review, how to integrate AI suggestions—remains an open design question.

#### A Measured Optimism

DeepSeekMath-V2 does not claim to solve mathematics autonomously. Instead, it demonstrates that **the foundational capability for self-verifiable reasoning exists**. The 118/120 Putnam score (Table 1) proves that natural language verification can exceed human performance on rigorous tasks. The meta-verification improvement (0.85→0.96, Section 2.1.2) shows that faithfulness can be systematically enhanced. The automated labeling pipeline (Section 2.3) indicates that improvement can continue without constant human annotation.

For the field of mathematical AI, this work shifts the question from *"Can LLMs verify proofs without reference solutions?"* to *"How do we scale and refine this capability for research applications?"* That shift—from feasibility to optimization—marks a meaningful inflection point in the pursuit of AI systems that can genuinely advance mathematical knowledge.
