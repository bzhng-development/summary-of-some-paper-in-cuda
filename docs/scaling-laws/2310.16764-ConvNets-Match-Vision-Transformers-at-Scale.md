## 1. Executive Summary

This paper challenges the belief that Vision Transformers (ViTs) outperform Convolutional Neural Networks (ConvNets) at web-scale by demonstrating that the `NFNet` architecture pre-trained on the JFT-4B dataset matches ViT performance with comparable compute budgets (Figure 1). The authors observe a log-log scaling law between validation loss and compute budgets up to 110k TPU-v4 core hours (Figure 2), with their strongest fine-tuned model (`NFNet-F7+`) achieving a Top-1 accuracy of 90.4% on ImageNet. This finding matters because it suggests that with sufficient data and compute, architecture inductive biases become less critical than total scale, reinforcing the "bitter lesson" of deep learning.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this work, we must first examine the historical trajectory of computer vision architectures and the specific gap in empirical evidence that this paper addresses. The authors are not merely proposing a new model; they are testing a prevailing hypothesis about the fundamental limits of convolutional architectures compared to transformers.

### The Shift from ConvNets to Vision Transformers

For roughly a decade prior to this work, **Convolutional Neural Networks (ConvNets)** were the dominant architecture in computer vision. 
- **Historical Context:** Deep ConvNets were deployed commercially over 20 years ago (LeCun et al., 1998). The field re-ignited with **AlexNet** in 2012 (Krizhevsky et al., 2017), and subsequently, **ResNets** (He et al., 2016a,b) dominated benchmarks for almost ten years.
- **The Challenger:** Recently, **Vision Transformers (ViTs)** (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) have increasingly replaced ConvNets. ViTs apply the transformer architecture—originally designed for natural language processing—to image patches, relying less on the spatial inductive biases (like locality and translation equivariance) that define ConvNets.

Simultaneously, the evaluation paradigm in computer vision shifted. 
- **Old Paradigm:** Researchers evaluated randomly initialized networks on specific datasets like **ImageNet**.
- **New Paradigm:** The community now evaluates networks **pre-trained on large general-purpose datasets** collected from the web (often called "web-scale" data). This shift is critical because modern foundation models are rarely trained from scratch on downstream tasks; they are pre-trained broadly and then fine-tuned.

### The Unverified Belief

Despite the rise of ViTs, the authors identify a specific misconception in the research community:

> "Many researchers believe that ConvNets perform well on small or moderately sized datasets, but are not competitive with Vision Transformers when given access to datasets on the web-scale."

This belief suggests that ConvNets have inferior **scaling properties**—meaning that as you add more data and compute, ViTs improve faster than ConvNets. However, the Introduction states there is "surprisingly little evidence to support this claim." The paper argues that the perceived superiority of ViTs may be an artifact of unfair experimental comparisons rather than inherent architectural advantages.

### Limitations of Prior Comparisons

The paper highlights two major shortcomings in how ConvNets and ViTs were previously compared:

1.  **Weak Baselines:** Many papers studying ViTs compare them against weak ConvNet baselines, typically the original **ResNet** architecture (He et al., 2016a), rather than modern, optimized ConvNets.
2.  **Unequal Compute Budgets:** The strongest ViT models were pre-trained using massive compute budgets, often exceeding `$500k$` **TPU-v3 core hours** (Zhai et al., 2022). In contrast, ConvNets were typically trained with significantly less compute. 

A **TPU-v4 core hour** is a unit of computational cost representing one hour of processing on a single Tensor Processing Unit v4 core. Comparing models trained with vastly different budgets confounds architectural efficiency with raw resource allocation. If a ViT is given `$10\times$` the compute of a ConvNet, outperforming the ConvNet does not prove the architecture is better; it only proves that more compute helps.

### Positioning This Work

This paper positions itself as a controlled experiment to isolate the variable of **architecture** while holding **data** and **compute** constant. 

- **Architecture:** They select the **NFNet** model family (Brock et al., 2021). This is a pure convolutional architecture published concurrently with the first ViT papers and was the last ConvNet to set a new State-Of-The-Art (SOTA) on ImageNet. Crucially, the authors make **no changes** to the NFNet architecture or training procedure beyond tuning simple hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, epoch budget).
- **Data:** They pre-train on **JFT-4B**, a large labelled dataset containing roughly `$4$` billion images from `$30k$` classes (Sun et al., 2017). This matches the scale of data used for top-tier ViT pre-training (e.g., JFT-3B).
- **Compute:** They consider pre-training compute budgets between `$0.4k$` and `$110k$` **TPU-v4 core hours**. 

By scaling the NFNet family across this range, the authors aim to determine if the "ConvNets don't scale" belief holds when the comparison is fair. They explicitly compare their results against reported ViT performance (Alabdulmohsin et al., 2023; Zhai et al., 2022) using estimated compute costs normalized to TPU-v4 hardware (Figure 1).

### Theoretical Significance: The "Bitter Lesson"

Underlying this investigation is a broader theoretical question about deep learning known as the **"bitter lesson"** (Tolstikhin et al., 2021). This concept suggests that the most important factors determining performance are the **compute and data** available for training, rather than specific human-designed inductive biases in the model architecture. 

If ConvNets can match ViTs at scale despite having different inductive biases, it reinforces the idea that scale often trumps architectural nuance. Conversely, if ViTs strictly outperform ConvNets under identical conditions, it would suggest that the transformer architecture possesses a fundamental advantage for visual representation learning at web-scale. This paper tests this boundary directly.

## 3. Technical Approach

This section dissects the experimental framework used to test the scaling properties of Convolutional Neural Networks (ConvNets) against Vision Transformers (ViTs). The authors do not propose a new architecture; instead, they construct a rigorous scaling experiment using an existing high-performance ConvNet family. The core idea is to isolate **compute** and **data** as the primary variables while holding architectural design constant, thereby testing whether ConvNets fundamentally fail to scale or simply haven't been given enough resources.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system is a large-scale machine learning experimental pipeline that trains a family of Convolutional Neural Networks (`NFNet`) on a massive dataset (`JFT-4B`) across varying computational budgets. It solves the problem of unfair architectural comparison by normalizing the compute costs of ConvNets and Transformers to the same hardware unit, revealing that ConvNets follow predictable scaling laws when given sufficient resources.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The experimental pipeline consists of four distinct stages that flow sequentially from data preparation to final evaluation.
*   **Data Preparation Module:** Ingests the `JFT-4B` dataset (4 billion images) and filters out near-duplicates of the downstream evaluation dataset (`ImageNet`) to prevent data leakage.
*   **Pre-training Engine:** Trains various `NFNet` model variants (ranging from `F0` to `F7+`) using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) with Adaptive Gradient Clipping, varying the number of epochs and model size to span a compute range of `$0.4k$` to `$110k$` TPU-v4 core hours.
*   **Scaling Analysis Unit:** Measures the validation loss on a held-out set of `$130k$` images to establish the relationship between compute budget and performance (the scaling law).
*   **Fine-tuning & Evaluation Module:** Takes the pre-trained checkpoints, fine-tunes them on `ImageNet` using Sharpness Aware Minimization (SAM) at higher resolutions, and reports Top-1 accuracy for comparison against published ViT benchmarks.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
To fully understand how the authors validate their hypothesis, we will examine the technical components in the following order:
*   **Model Family Specification:** We define the `NFNet` variants (`F0`–`F7+`) and how their depth and width are scaled to create different compute profiles.
*   **Data Curation & Integrity:** We explain the handling of the `JFT-4B` dataset, specifically the removal of duplicates to ensure fair evaluation.
*   **Pre-training Protocol:** We detail the optimizer, learning rate schedules, batch sizes, and resolution settings used during the initial large-scale training.
*   **Scaling Law Methodology:** We describe how compute budgets are calculated and how the relationship between loss and compute is measured.
*   **Fine-tuning Configuration:** We outline the distinct hyperparameters used for downstream adaptation, including regularization techniques like `SAM` and repeated augmentation.
*   **Compute Normalization:** We explain how the authors estimated the compute costs of competing ViT models to ensure an apples-to-apples comparison on Figure 1.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown
This experimental study is a controlled scaling law analysis that treats computational budget as the primary independent variable. The authors aim to determine if the performance gap between ConvNets and Transformers is due to architectural limits or simply unequal resource allocation.

**Model Family and Architectural Scaling**
The authors select the `NFNet` model family, a pure convolutional architecture known for high performance without normalization layers (Brock et al., 2021). To test scaling, they do not design a new network but instead utilize a series of existing variants with systematically increasing capacity. The model variants are named `F0`, `F1`, `F3`, `F3+`, `F7`, and `F7+`. The scaling strategy relies on doubling depth and increasing width:
*   `F1` is double the depth of `F0`.
*   `F3` is double the depth of `F1`.
*   `F7` is double the depth of `F3`.
*   The `+` variants (`F3+`, `F7+`) maintain the same depth as their base counterparts (`F3`, `F7`) but increase the network width.
This design allows the authors to traverse the compute budget landscape by selecting different points along the depth-width curve without introducing confounding architectural changes. The paper explicitly states, "We do not make any changes to the model architecture or the training procedure (beyond tuning simple hyper-parameters such as the learning rate or epoch budget)."

**Data Curation and Integrity**
The pre-training dataset is `JFT-4B`, which contains roughly `$4$` billion labelled images across `$30k$` classes. A critical technical detail for fair evaluation is the handling of data overlap between pre-training and evaluation sets. The authors note that they "removed near-duplicates of images in the training and validation sets of ImageNet from JFT-4B before training." This step is essential because if the model memorizes images during pre-training that later appear in the evaluation set, the reported accuracy would be artificially inflated. The validation loss during pre-training is measured on a held-out set of `$130k$` images from the JFT distribution.

**Pre-training Protocol and Hyperparameters**
The pre-training process is standardized across all model variants to ensure that performance differences arise from scale rather than tuning inconsistencies. The optimization algorithm is Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) with Momentum. To stabilize training at large batch sizes, the authors employ Adaptive Gradient Clipping (AGC). The global batch size is fixed at `$4096$` images.
*   **Resolution:** Images are processed at `$224 \times 224$` resolution during training and `$256 \times 256$` at evaluation.
*   **Epoch Budget:** Models are trained for a range of epoch budgets between `$0.25$` and `$8$` epochs.
*   **Learning Rate Schedule:** A cosine decay learning rate schedule is used. The base learning rate is not fixed; instead, it is "tuned separately for each epoch budget on a small logarithmic grid."
*   **Learning Rate Behavior:** The authors observe a predictable pattern in the optimal learning rate, denoted as `$\alpha$`. For small epoch budgets, all models show a similar optimal learning rate of `$\alpha \approx 1.6$`. However, "the optimal learning rate falls as the epoch budget rises, and for large models the optimal learning rate falls more quickly." This implies that as you scale compute by training longer, you must reduce the learning rate to maintain stability and performance.

**Scaling Law Methodology**
The core metric for establishing scaling behavior is the relationship between compute budget and validation loss. The authors estimate the compute required to train each model "by eye from the typical steps per second achieved by each model during training." The unit of measurement is **TPU-v4 core hours**. The total compute budgets range from `$0.4k$` to `$110k$` TPU-v4 core hours.
The paper reports a specific mathematical relationship observed in Figure 2:
> "Figure 2 shows a clear linear trend, consistent with a log-log scaling law between validation loss and pre-training compute."

This means that if you plot the logarithm of the validation loss against the logarithm of the compute budget, the result is a straight line. This matches scaling laws previously observed in language modelling. A key finding regarding optimal resource allocation is that "the optimal model size and the optimal epoch budget (which achieve the lowest validation loss) both increase in size as the compute budget increases." The authors propose a rule of thumb derived from this observation: "scale the model size and the number of training epochs at the same rate." They note that for overall compute budgets greater than roughly `$5k$` TPU-v4 core hours, the optimal epoch budget was greater than `$1$`.

**Fine-tuning Configuration**
Pre-training alone does not determine final utility; the models must adapt to downstream tasks. The fine-tuning protocol differs significantly from pre-training to maximize final accuracy on `ImageNet`.
*   **Optimizer:** The authors use Sharpness Aware Minimization (SAM) instead of standard SGD. SAM is an optimization technique that seeks parameters that lie in neighborhoods having uniformly low loss, improving generalization.
*   **Regularization:** The fine-tuning process includes stochastic depth and dropout, which are techniques to prevent overfitting by randomly ignoring parts of the network during training.
*   **Resolution Shift:** The resolution is increased significantly compared to pre-training. Training occurs at `$384 \times 384$` and evaluation occurs at `$480 \times 480$`. This higher resolution allows the model to capture finer details during the task-specific adaptation phase.
*   **Epochs:** Each model is fine-tuned for `$50$` epochs.
*   **Repeated Augmentation (RA):** For the strongest results, the authors introduce repeated augmentation during fine-tuning. This technique involves generating multiple augmented versions of the same image within a batch. For the best model (`NFNet-F7+`), they use an "augmentation multiplicity 4." To keep the computational cost constant, they "reduce the number of passes through the data such that the total computational cost of fine-tuning is constant."

**Compute Normalization and Comparison**
A major contribution of this technical approach is the method used to compare against Vision Transformers. Previous works often compared ConvNets trained on older hardware (TPU-v3) against ViTs trained on newer hardware, or simply cited raw hours without normalization. The authors explicitly evaluate the pre-training speed of ViT models on TPU-v4 hardware using the original authors' codebases to generate fair estimates.
*   **ViT-g/14:** Estimated to take `$120k$` TPU-v4 core hours.
*   **ViT-G/14:** Estimated to take `$280k$` TPU-v4 core hours.
*   **SoViT-400m/14:** Estimated to take `$130k$` TPU-v4 core hours.

These estimates allow the authors to plot ViT performance on the same x-axis as their ConvNets in Figure 1. This normalization reveals that the `NFNet-F7+` model, requiring roughly `$110k$` TPU-v4 core hours to pre-train and `$1.6k$` to fine-tune, achieves comparable performance to ViTs trained with similar normalized budgets. The authors also note a hardware-specific nuance: "NFNets were optimized for TPU-v4, and perform less well when evaluated on other devices," estimating that `NFNet-F7+` would require `$250k$` TPU-v3 core hours to pre-train for 8 epochs. This highlights that architectural efficiency can be hardware-dependent.

**Observation on Checkpoint Selection**
Finally, the technical approach includes a nuanced finding about model selection. The authors note that "the pre-trained checkpoints achieving the lowest validation loss on JFT-4B did not always achieve the highest Top-1 accuracy on ImageNet after fine-tuning." Specifically, under a fixed pre-training compute budget, the fine-tuning regime favored "slightly larger models and slightly smaller epoch budgets." The reasoning provided is intuitive: "larger models have more capacity and are therefore better able to adapt to the new task." This suggests that when optimizing for transfer learning, one should not strictly minimize pre-training loss but rather balance model capacity against training duration.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the conceptual breakthroughs of the paper, distinguishing between incremental engineering improvements and fundamental shifts in our understanding of ConvNet capabilities. While Section 3 detailed the experimental machinery, here we analyze *why* the results matter and how they correct prevailing misconceptions in the field. The authors do not merely report higher accuracy; they provide evidence that challenges the theoretical limits previously assigned to convolutional architectures.

### 4.1 Validation of Log-Log Scaling Laws for ConvNets
The most significant theoretical contribution is the empirical verification that ConvNets obey predictable scaling laws at web-scale, similar to Transformers. Prior to this work, a common belief was that ConvNets might saturate in performance earlier than Transformers as compute increases. 

*   **The Finding:** As shown in **Figure 2**, the relationship between validation loss and compute budget forms a clear linear trend on a log-log plot. This holds true across the entire tested range, from `$0.4k$` to `$110k$` TPU-v4 core hours.
*   **Why It Matters:** Scaling laws allow researchers to predict model performance without fully training it. If a model family follows a reliable scaling law, one can train small variants, fit a line, and extrapolate the expected performance of a larger model. By confirming this behavior for NFNets, the paper removes the theoretical argument that ConvNets inherently "cap out" before Transformers.
*   **Comparison to Prior Work:** This matches the scaling laws previously observed in language modelling with Transformers (Brown et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022). The innovation here is extending this empirical law to pure convolutional architectures on vision tasks, suggesting that the scaling behavior may be a property of deep neural networks generally, rather than specific to the attention mechanism.

### 4.2 A Compute-Optimal Scaling Strategy
Beyond confirming that performance scales, the paper provides a practical heuristic for *how* to allocate resources to maximize that performance. This moves beyond "train bigger models" to "train bigger models for the right amount of time."

*   **The Rule:** The authors observe that the optimal model size and the optimal epoch budget both increase as the total compute budget increases. They propose a specific rule of thumb: **"scale the model size and the number of training epochs at the same rate."**
*   **Evidence:** In **Figure 2**, the curves for different model sizes intersect such that larger models require more epochs to reach their potential. The text notes that for compute budgets greater than roughly `$5k$` TPU-v4 core hours, the optimal epoch budget exceeds `$1$`.
*   **Significance:** This challenges the practice of training massive models for only a single pass (1 epoch) over the data when compute is abundant. It suggests that under-training large models is inefficient. This insight directly mirrors the "Chinchua" scaling laws found in language models (Hoffmann et al., 2022), reinforcing that data repetition (multiple epochs) remains valuable even at billions of images.

### 4.3 The Disconnect Between Pre-training Loss and Transfer Accuracy
A subtle but critical methodological insight is the observation that minimizing pre-training loss does not guarantee optimal downstream performance. This finding complicates the standard practice of selecting checkpoints based solely on pre-training validation metrics.

*   **The Observation:** The authors state explicitly: **"the pre-trained checkpoints achieving the lowest validation loss on JFT-4B did not always achieve the highest Top-1 accuracy on ImageNet after fine-tuning."**
*   **The Mechanism:** Under a fixed compute budget, the fine-tuning regime favored **slightly larger models and slightly smaller epoch budgets**. The authors explain this intuitively: larger models possess more capacity to adapt to the new task distribution during fine-tuning, even if they did not minimize the pre-training loss as effectively as a smaller, longer-trained model.
*   **Practical Impact:** This warns practitioners against using pre-training validation loss as the sole proxy for model quality when transfer learning is the goal. It suggests that when budgeting compute, one should reserve some capacity for model size rather than spending it all on training duration, specifically to aid adaptability.

### 4.4 Standardized Compute Benchmarking Framework
Finally, the paper introduces a rigorous method for comparing architectures across different hardware generations, addressing a systemic source of bias in prior literature.

*   **The Problem:** Previous comparisons often cited raw training hours without accounting for hardware differences (e.g., comparing TPU-v3 hours to TPU-v4 hours) or relied on weak baselines. As noted in Section 2, strong ViT models were often trained with `$>500k$` TPU-v3 core hours, far exceeding ConvNet budgets.
*   **The Innovation:** The authors evaluated ViT pre-training speeds on **TPU-v4** using the original authors' codebases to generate normalized estimates. For example, they estimate that a **ViT-G/14** would require `$280k$` TPU-v4 core hours, while their **NFNet-F7+** required roughly `$110k$` TPU-v4 core hours to achieve comparable accuracy (Figure 1).
*   **Significance:** This creates an "apples-to-apples" comparison that isolates architectural efficiency from hardware advantages. It also highlights a hidden variable: hardware dependence. The authors note that **NFNets were optimized for TPU-v4**, estimating that the same model would require `$250k$` TPU-v3 core hours. This underscores that claims of "efficiency" are often tied to specific infrastructure, a nuance frequently omitted in broader claims about architectural superiority.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section critically examines the evidence presented to support the paper's central claim: that ConvNets can match Vision Transformers (ViTs) at scale when compute budgets are normalized. We dissect the evaluation methodology, analyze the quantitative results presented in the figures, and assess the robustness of the findings by highlighting anomalies and hardware-specific constraints noted by the authors.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Baseline Normalization

To fairly compare architectures, the authors establish a rigorous evaluation protocol that addresses two common sources of bias in prior literature: data leakage and hardware heterogeneity.

*   **Datasets and Metrics:**
    *   **Pre-training Validation:** Performance during pre-training is measured on a held-out set of `$130k$` images from the **JFT-4B** distribution. The metric is **validation loss**.
    *   **Downstream Evaluation:** Final performance is measured on **ImageNet** after fine-tuning. The metric is **Top-1 accuracy**.
    *   **Data Integrity:** To prevent data leakage, the authors explicitly "removed near-duplicates of images in the training and validation sets of ImageNet from JFT-4B before training." This ensures that the reported ImageNet accuracy reflects genuine generalization rather than memorization of overlapping samples.

*   **Baseline Normalization (The "Apples-to-Apples" Problem):**
    A critical component of this analysis is how the authors compare their ConvNets against existing ViT benchmarks. Prior works often reported compute in **TPU-v3 core hours**, while this study uses **TPU-v4 core hours**. Since TPU-v4 cores have roughly double the theoretical flops of TPU-v3 cores, raw hour comparisons are misleading.
    
    The authors do not simply convert based on theoretical flops. Instead, they "evaluated the pre-training speed for these models on TPU-v4 (using the original authors' codebase)" to generate empirical estimates. This normalizes the comparison to the same hardware infrastructure used for the NFNet experiments.
    *   **ViT-g/14:** Originally reported at `$210k$` TPU-v3 hours. Estimated by authors at `$120k$` TPU-v4 hours.
    *   **ViT-G/14:** Originally reported at `$>500k$` TPU-v3 hours. Estimated by authors at `$280k$` TPU-v4 hours.
    *   **SoViT-400m/14:** Originally reported at `$230k$` TPU-v3 hours. Estimated by authors at `$130k$` TPU-v4 hours.

    This normalization is essential for **Figure 1**, where the x-axis represents the compute budget. Without this step, the ConvNets might appear artificially efficient simply because they were trained on newer hardware.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results: Accuracy vs. Compute

The primary evidence for the paper's claim lies in **Figure 1**, which plots ImageNet Top-1 error against pre-training compute budget. The results demonstrate that NFNets occupy the same performance frontier as ViTs when compute is controlled.

*   **NFNet Performance:**
    The strongest model, `NFNet-F7+`, was pre-trained for 8 epochs.
    *   **Pre-training Cost:** Roughly `$110k$` TPU-v4 core hours.
    *   **Fine-tuning Cost:** `$1.6k$` TPU-v4 core hours (for 50 epochs).
    *   **Accuracy:** Achieves **$90.3\%$** Top-1 accuracy standard fine-tuning.
    *   **With Repeated Augmentation (RA):** When fine-tuned with repeated augmentation (multiplicity 4), accuracy improves to **$90.4\%$**.
    
    > "Our strongest fine-tuned model achieves a Top-1 accuracy of 90.4%."

    This result is significant because it surpasses the previous best reported NFNet result of `$86.8\%$` (Fort et al., 2021), demonstrating that the architecture benefits substantially from large-scale pre-training rather than hitting a capacity ceiling.

*   **ViT Comparison:**
    When plotted on the same normalized axes in **Figure 1**, the NFNet performance curve overlaps with ViT benchmarks:
    *   **ViT-g/14:** Achieves `$90.2\%$` at an estimated `$120k$` TPU-v4 hours. The `NFNet-F7+` matches this performance at `$110k$` hours.
    *   **SoViT-400m/14:** Achieves `$90.3\%$` at an estimated `$130k$` TPU-v4 hours.
    *   **ViT-G/14:** Achieves `$90.45\%$` but requires an estimated `$280k$` TPU-v4 hours.
    
    The data suggests that while massive ViTs (like ViT-G/14) can push accuracy slightly higher, they do so at significantly higher compute costs (more than `$2.5\times$` the budget of NFNet-F7+). At comparable budgets (`$110k$`–`$130k$`), ConvNets and ViTs are statistically competitive.

### 5.3 Verification of Scaling Laws

**Figure 2** provides the mechanistic evidence for *why* the ConvNets succeed at scale. It plots the JFT-4B validation loss against the compute budget on a log-log scale.

*   **Linearity:** The authors observe a "clear linear trend," confirming a log-log scaling law. This implies that performance improvements are predictable as compute increases, rather than saturating unexpectedly.
*   **Compute-Optimal Scaling:** The curves in **Figure 2** show that for any given compute budget, there is an optimal combination of model size and training duration.
    *   **Rule of Thumb:** The authors find that "the optimal model size and the optimal epoch budget... both increase in size as the compute budget increases."
    *   **Threshold:** For compute budgets greater than roughly `$5k$` TPU-v4 core hours, the optimal epoch budget exceeds `$1$`. This challenges the common practice in large-scale vision training of training massive models for only a single epoch. The data suggests that repeating data (multiple epochs) is beneficial even at the 4-billion image scale if the model size is scaled concurrently.

### 5.4 Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Tuning

**Figure 3** analyzes the behavior of the optimal learning rate `$\alpha$` across different model sizes and epoch budgets. This is crucial for practitioners attempting to replicate these scaling laws.

*   **Small Budgets:** For small epoch budgets, all models converge on a similar optimal learning rate of `$\alpha \approx 1.6$`.
*   **Large Budgets:** As the epoch budget rises, the optimal learning rate falls. This decay happens more quickly for larger models.
*   **Practical Implication:** The authors suggest that one can "efficiently tune the learning rate within 2 trials by assuming that the optimal learning rate falls slowly but monotonically as both the model size and the epoch budget increases." This reduces the hyperparameter search space for future scaling experiments, making them less computationally expensive to configure.

### 5.5 Anomalies, Limitations, and Robustness Checks

While the results support the main hypothesis, the authors transparently report several anomalies and constraints that qualify the findings.

*   **The F7+ Scaling Anomaly:**
    In **Figure 2**, the curve for `NFNet-F7+` is not perfectly smooth compared to smaller models. The authors attribute this to a data loading issue:
    > "We believe this arises because our data loading pipeline did not guarantee that each training example would be sampled once per epoch if the training run was pre-empted/restarted..."
    
    If a job restarted multiple times, some examples were under-sampled. This is a critical robustness check: it indicates that at extreme scales, infrastructure reliability (data loading guarantees) can become a bottleneck that obscures true scaling behavior.

*   **Pre-training Loss vs. Fine-tuning Accuracy Disconnect:**
    A subtle but important finding is that minimizing pre-training loss does not guarantee the best downstream performance.
    > "The pre-trained checkpoints achieving the lowest validation loss on JFT-4B did not always achieve the highest Top-1 accuracy on ImageNet after fine-tuning."
    
    Specifically, for a fixed compute budget, fine-tuning favored **slightly larger models** trained for **slightly fewer epochs**. The authors reason that larger models have more capacity to adapt to the new task distribution during fine-tuning, even if they didn't minimize the pre-training loss as effectively. This suggests that **transferability** and **pre-training loss** are not perfectly correlated metrics.

*   **Hardware Dependence:**
    The efficiency claims are specific to the hardware used. The authors note that `NFNets` were optimized for **TPU-v4**.
    > "We estimate that NFNet-F7+ would require 250k TPU-v3 core hours to pre-train for 8 epochs in our codebase."
    
    Compared to the `$110k$` TPU-v4 hours, this represents a `$2.27\times$` increase in cost on older hardware. This highlights that architectural efficiency is not absolute; it is relative to the underlying infrastructure (e.g., memory bandwidth, matrix multiplication units). A claim that "ConvNets are efficient" must always be qualified by "on which hardware?"

### 5.6 Assessment of Claims

Do the experiments convincingly support the paper's claims?

1.  **Claim:** *ConvNets match ViTs at scale.*
    *   **Verdict:** **Supported.** Figure 1 shows overlapping performance curves when compute is normalized. The `$90.4\%$` accuracy of NFNet-F7+ is within margin of error of ViT-g/14 (`$90.2\%$`) and SoViT (`$90.3\%$`) at similar compute costs.
2.  **Claim:** *ConvNets obey log-log scaling laws.*
    *   **Verdict:** **Supported.** Figure 2 demonstrates linear trends on log-log axes across two orders of magnitude in compute (`$0.4k$` to `$110k$` hours).
3.  **Claim:** *Prior beliefs about ConvNet saturation were due to unfair comparisons.*
    *   **Verdict:** **Plausible.** By controlling for compute and data scale, the performance gap disappears. The paper successfully isolates architecture as a variable and shows it is less critical than total scale.

However, the results are conditional. The ConvNet performance relies on specific optimizations (AGC, specific learning rate schedules) and hardware (TPU-v4). The anomaly in the F7+ curve also suggests that maintaining data integrity at this scale is non-trivial. The paper does not claim ConvNets are *better* than ViTs, only that they are *competitive*, which the data strongly supports.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the paper provides compelling evidence that ConvNets can compete with Vision Transformers (ViTs) at scale, the findings are subject to specific constraints, hardware dependencies, and scope limitations. Understanding these boundaries is crucial for interpreting the results correctly and avoiding overgeneralization. This section analyzes the assumptions underlying the approach, the scenarios not addressed, and the remaining open questions.

### 6.1 Hardware and Infrastructure Dependencies

A significant limitation of the reported efficiency is its dependence on specific hardware infrastructure. The authors explicitly state that the **NFNet** architecture was optimized for **TPU-v4** hardware.

*   **Hardware Specificity:** The compute estimates provided are not universal. The authors note:
    > "NFNets were optimized for TPU-v4, and perform less well when evaluated on other devices."
    
    Specifically, they estimate that the **NFNet-F7+** model would require **$250k$ TPU-v3 core hours** to pre-train for 8 epochs, compared to **$110k$ TPU-v4 core hours** on the preferred hardware. This represents a **$2.27\times$** increase in computational cost on older infrastructure. Consequently, claims about ConvNet efficiency are relative to the underlying matrix multiplication units and memory bandwidth of the accelerator used.
*   **Data Loading Robustness:** At extreme scales, infrastructure reliability becomes a bottleneck. In **Figure 2**, the scaling curve for the largest model (**NFNet-F7+**) is not as smooth as smaller variants. The authors attribute this to a data loading limitation:
    > "Our data loading pipeline did not guarantee that each training example would be sampled once per epoch if the training run was pre-empted/restarted..."
    
    This suggests that maintaining strict data integrity (ensuring uniform sampling) becomes increasingly difficult as training jobs grow longer and more prone to pre-emption. This infrastructure overhead is a hidden cost of scaling that may affect reproducibility.

### 6.2 Scope of Evaluation and Generalizability

The experimental design isolates specific variables to test the scaling hypothesis, but this necessarily excludes other important dimensions of computer vision performance.

*   **Architecture Generalizability:** The study evaluates only the **NFNet** model family. While NFNet was the last ConvNet to set a State-Of-The-Art (SOTA) on ImageNet prior to ViTs, the paper does not test other modern ConvNet variants (e.g., EfficientNets or ResNets) under the same conditions. Therefore, the conclusion that "ConvNets match ViTs" is strictly proven only for the NFNet architecture family. It remains an open question whether older ConvNet families would exhibit the same scaling laws without architectural modifications.
*   **Task Limitations:** The primary evaluation metric is **ImageNet Top-1 accuracy**. While this is a standard benchmark for classification, it does not capture performance on dense prediction tasks (e.g., object detection, segmentation) or generative tasks. The **Discussion** section acknowledges that ViTs may retain practical advantages in specific contexts:
    > "ViTs may have practical advantages in specific contexts, such as the ability to use similar model components across multiple modalities (Bavishi et al., 2023)."
    
    Because ViTs process images as sequences of patches, they align naturally with language transformers, facilitating multimodal models (e.g., text-image models). ConvNets, with their spatial inductive biases, may require more engineering effort to integrate into such unified architectures.
*   **Compute Ceiling:** The study tests compute budgets up to **$110k$ TPU-v4 core hours**. While this matches the budget of models like **ViT-g/14** (estimated **$120k$** hours), it is lower than the largest ViTs evaluated in prior work. For instance, **ViT-G/14** was estimated to require **$280k$ TPU-v4 core hours** to achieve **$90.45\%$** accuracy. The NFNet-F7+ achieved **$90.4\%$** at **$110k$** hours. While NFNet is more efficient at the **$110k$** scale, the paper does not definitively prove that ConvNets would continue to match ViTs if the budget were extended to **$300k+$** hours. The scaling law observed in **Figure 2** is linear within the tested range, but extrapolation beyond **$110k$** hours remains an assumption.

### 6.3 Optimization and Metric Trade-offs

The paper reveals a nuanced trade-off between pre-training objectives and downstream utility, challenging standard model selection practices.

*   **Pre-training Loss vs. Transfer Accuracy:** A critical finding is that minimizing pre-training loss does not guarantee optimal fine-tuning performance. The authors observe:
    > "The pre-trained checkpoints achieving the lowest validation loss on JFT-4B did not always achieve the highest Top-1 accuracy on ImageNet after fine-tuning."
    
    Specifically, under a fixed compute budget, the fine-tuning regime favored **slightly larger models** trained for **slightly fewer epochs**. This implies a trade-off: optimizing purely for pre-training convergence (loss) might yield a model that is less adaptable to downstream tasks than a model with higher capacity but slightly higher pre-training loss. Practitioners cannot rely solely on pre-training validation curves to select the best model for transfer learning.
*   **Fine-tuning Complexity:** The highest accuracy result (**$90.4\%$**) required **Repeated Augmentation (RA)** during fine-tuning. While the authors adjusted the number of passes to keep the computational cost constant, this adds complexity to the training pipeline. It suggests that matching ViT performance may require more sophisticated fine-tuning recipes than standard SGD, potentially increasing the engineering burden even if the pre-training is efficient.

### 6.4 Open Questions and Remaining Weaknesses

Several questions remain unanswered by this work, pointing to directions for future research.

*   **Scaling Law Stability:** The anomaly in the **NFNet-F7+** curve in **Figure 2** raises questions about the stability of scaling laws at the upper limits of the tested budget. If data loading inconsistencies can disrupt the linear trend, it suggests that observing true scaling behavior requires infrastructure guarantees that may not be available to all researchers.
*   **Learning Rate Tuning:** While the authors propose a rule of thumb for learning rate decay (Figure 3), they note that the optimal learning rate falls "slowly but monotonically." The exact functional form of this decay is not derived theoretically but observed empirically. This leaves some uncertainty for practitioners attempting to scale beyond the **$110k$** hour budget without extensive hyperparameter sweeps.
*   **Fairness of Compute Estimates:** The comparison relies on the authors' own estimates of ViT training speeds on TPU-v4 hardware using original codebases. While this is more rigorous than citing raw TPU-v3 hours, it still introduces a potential source of variance. If the ViT implementations used for estimation were not fully optimized for TPU-v4 compared to the NFNet implementation, the efficiency gap might be narrower than reported. However, the authors mitigate this by using the original authors' codebases for the speed evaluation.

In summary, while the paper successfully challenges the belief that ConvNets cannot scale, it does so within a specific hardware and task context. The efficiency gains are real but contingent on TPU-v4 optimization, and the architectural advantages of ViTs in multimodal settings remain unchallenged. The work shifts the burden of proof back to the community: claims of architectural superiority must now be validated against compute-matched ConvNet baselines trained on web-scale data.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work does more than report a new accuracy benchmark; it fundamentally challenges the prevailing narrative about architectural limits in computer vision. By demonstrating that `NFNet` models obey scaling laws and match `Vision Transformers` (ViTs) when compute and data are normalized, the paper forces a re-evaluation of how researchers prioritize model design versus resource allocation. This section outlines how these findings reshape the field, identifies critical gaps for future research, and provides actionable guidance for practitioners deciding between architectures.

### 7.1 Shifting the Paradigm: Scale Over Architecture

The most profound implication of this work is the reinforcement of the **"bitter lesson"** in deep learning: general methods that leverage computation are ultimately more effective than human-designed inductive biases. 

*   **Debunking the Saturation Myth:** Prior to this study, a common belief was that ConvNets inherently saturate in performance as compute increases, while ViTs continue to improve. **Figure 2** disproves this by showing a linear log-log scaling law for `NFNet` up to `$110k$` TPU-v4 core hours. This suggests that perceived architectural limitations were often artifacts of under-resourced experiments.
*   **Resource Allocation > Architectural Tweaks:** The findings imply that for large-scale vision tasks, the marginal gain from designing a new architecture may be smaller than the gain from simply increasing compute budget according to the observed scaling laws. The authors note that "the most important factors determining the performance of a sensibly designed model are the compute and data available for training."
*   **Re-evaluating Baselines:** Future papers claiming architectural superiority for ViTs (or any new model) must now control for compute budgets more rigorously. Comparing a new model against a baseline trained with `$10\times$` less compute is no longer sufficient evidence of architectural advantage.

### 7.2 Avenues for Future Research

While this paper closes the gap between ConvNets and ViTs on image classification, it opens several new questions that require empirical investigation. The authors explicitly highlight areas where ViTs may still hold advantages, providing a roadmap for subsequent work.

*   **Scaling Beyond `$110k$` Hours:** The study maxes out at `$110k$` TPU-v4 core hours. While `NFNet-F7+` matches `ViT-g/14` (estimated `$120k$` hours), the largest ViT reported (`ViT-G/14`) used an estimated `$280k$` TPU-v4 core hours to achieve `$90.45\%$` accuracy (Figure 1). 
    *   *Research Question:* Do ConvNets continue to follow the same scaling law beyond `$110k$` hours, or do ViTs pull ahead at extreme scales (e.g., `$500k+$` hours)?
    *   *Why It Matters:* If ViTs diverge positively at extreme scales, it would suggest that the attention mechanism offers a specific advantage for integrating massive amounts of information that convolution cannot replicate.
*   **Multimodal and Cross-Modal Tasks:** The Discussion section notes that ViTs have practical advantages in specific contexts, particularly "the ability to use similar model components across multiple modalities."
    *   *Research Question:* Can ConvNets be adapted to multimodal tasks (e.g., text-image retrieval, video-language modeling) as effectively as ViTs?
    *   *Why It Matters:* ViTs process images as sequences of patches, which aligns naturally with language transformers. If ConvNets require significant architectural modification to integrate with language models, their efficiency advantage in pure vision may be outweighed by engineering complexity in multimodal systems.
*   **Dense Prediction and Localization:** This study focuses on **ImageNet Top-1 accuracy**, a global classification metric. 
    *   *Research Question:* Do the scaling laws hold for dense prediction tasks like object detection or semantic segmentation?
    *   *Why It Matters:* ConvNets have strong spatial inductive biases (locality) that theoretically benefit pixel-level tasks. It is possible ConvNets might outperform ViTs on these tasks at scale, even if classification performance is equal.
*   **Hardware-Agnostic Optimization:** The authors note that `NFNet` performance is highly dependent on hardware, requiring `$250k$` TPU-v3 hours versus `$110k$` TPU-v4 hours for the same task.
    *   *Research Question:* Can ConvNet architectures be redesigned to be as hardware-agnostic as ViTs, or is their efficiency inherently tied to specific matrix multiplication units?
    *   *Why It Matters:* For researchers without access to TPU-v4 infrastructure, the efficiency claims of this paper may not hold. Developing hardware-robust ConvNets would broaden the applicability of these findings.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Decision Making

For practitioners deploying vision systems, this paper provides a data-driven framework for selecting architectures based on constraints rather than trends. The choice between `NFNet` (ConvNet) and `ViT` should depend on specific deployment requirements.

*   **When to Prefer ConvNets (`NFNet`):**
    *   **Pure Vision Tasks:** If the task is strictly image classification or recognition without language integration, ConvNets are now proven to be competitive at scale.
    *   **TPU-v4 Infrastructure:** If you have access to TPU-v4 hardware, `NFNet` offers comparable accuracy to ViTs at similar or slightly lower compute budgets (Figure 1).
    *   **Latency Constraints:** While not explicitly measured in this paper, ConvNets often have different inference latency profiles compared to ViTs due to their operational density. If profiling shows ConvNets are faster on your target edge device, this paper validates that you do not need to sacrifice accuracy to use them.
*   **When to Prefer Vision Transformers:**
    *   **Multimodal Systems:** If your pipeline involves combining vision with language (e.g., captioning, VQA), ViTs offer easier integration with existing language models.
    *   **Extreme Scale Budgets:** If you have access to compute budgets exceeding `$200k$` TPU-v4 hours, the `ViT-G/14` results suggest there may still be headroom for ViTs to outperform current ConvNet benchmarks.
    *   **Hardware Diversity:** If you must deploy across heterogeneous hardware (e.g., mixed TPU-v3, GPU, CPU), ViTs may offer more consistent performance given the hardware sensitivity observed in `NFNet`.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For teams attempting to replicate these results or apply the scaling strategies to their own models, the paper offers specific procedural insights that are critical for success. Simply copying the architecture is insufficient; the training dynamics must be respected.

*   **Adhere to Scaling Laws:** Do not train massive models for a single epoch. The authors found that for compute budgets greater than `$5k$` TPU-v4 core hours, the optimal epoch budget exceeds `$1$`. 
    *   *Guidance:* When increasing model size, increase the number of training epochs proportionally. "Scale the model size and the number of training epochs at the same rate."
*   **Learning Rate Tuning Strategy:** Hyperparameter tuning can be prohibitively expensive at scale. The authors provide a heuristic based on **Figure 3**:
    *   *Guidance:* Assume the optimal learning rate `$\alpha$` starts near `$1.6$` for small budgets and falls slowly as model size and epochs increase. You can "efficiently tune the learning rate within 2 trials" using this monotonic decay assumption rather than searching a wide grid.
*   **Checkpoint Selection Criteria:** Do not select your final model solely based on pre-training validation loss. 
    *   *Guidance:* The paper found that "pre-trained checkpoints achieving the lowest validation loss on JFT-4B did not always achieve the highest Top-1 accuracy on ImageNet." For transfer learning, prefer **slightly larger models** trained for **slightly fewer epochs** over models that minimize pre-training loss. This ensures the model retains capacity for adaptation during fine-tuning.
*   **Data Integrity at Scale:** The anomaly in the `NFNet-F7+` curve (Figure 2) highlights a hidden risk in large-scale training.
    *   *Guidance:* Ensure your data loading pipeline guarantees uniform sampling even if training jobs are pre-empted or restarted. Inconsistent data exposure can obscure scaling trends and degrade performance, making it appear as though the model has saturated when it has not.
*   **Fine-Tuning Recipe:** To match the reported `$90.4\%$` accuracy, standard fine-tuning is insufficient.
    *   *Guidance:* Use **Sharpness Aware Minimization (SAM)** and **Repeated Augmentation (RA)** during fine-tuning. The authors achieved their best results with an augmentation multiplicity of `$4$`, adjusting the number of passes to keep computational cost constant.

By following these guidelines, practitioners can avoid common pitfalls in large-scale training and ensure that their experimental results reflect true architectural capabilities rather than optimization artifacts. This paper ultimately empowers engineers to choose architectures based on empirical evidence rather than community consensus, prioritizing compute efficiency and task alignment over architectural trends.
