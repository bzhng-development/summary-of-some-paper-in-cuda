## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces BLOOM, a 176 billion-parameter open-access multilingual language model developed by the BigScience Workshop through a collaboration of hundreds of researchers. Trained on the ROOTS corpus comprising 1.61 terabytes of text across 46 natural and 13 programming languages, BLOOM addresses the exclusion of the broader research community from LLM development and the predominance of English-centric training data in prior state-of-the-art systems. The model achieves competitive performance on diverse benchmarks, particularly after multitask prompted finetuning (BLOOMZ), and is publicly released under a Responsible AI License to democratize access to large-scale language technology.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### 2.1 The Central Problem: Exclusion from LLM Development

The BLOOM paper addresses a fundamental structural problem in modern natural language processing: **the concentration of large language model (LLM) development within a small number of resource-rich organizations**, which systematically excludes the majority of the research community from participating in their creation, evaluation, and governance.

This exclusion manifests in three concrete ways, as documented in Section 1 (Introduction):

1. **Computational Barriers**: The costs of training LLMs at the 100+ billion parameter scale are "only affordable for well-resourced organizations." Before BigScience, EleutherAI was "the only non-corporate entity outside of China that was developing large language models" (Section 2.1).

2. **Access Restrictions**: "Until recently, most LLMs were not publicly released." Even when models were announced, researchers could not inspect, modify, or build upon them without restrictive licensing or API-only access.

3. **Linguistic Narrowness**: "Most LLMs are primarily trained on English-language text (with notable exceptions in Chinese and Korean)." This creates systems that perform poorly or unpredictably on the vast majority of the world's languages.

### 2.2 Why This Problem Matters

The importance of addressing these gaps extends beyond academic curiosity to real-world societal impact:

**Democratic Participation in Technology Development**. Section 2.1 argues that when "development efforts are guided by prioritizing internal definitions of performance over their impact on society, the values of the developers come to be emphasized over those of the direct and indirect users." This is not merely philosophical—concrete consequences include misaligned research priorities and "potentially dire consequences in e.g. medical applications" (citing Wong et al., 2021).

**Environmental Justice**. The carbon footprint of LLM training disproportionately affects marginalized communities. Section 2.1 notes that "contributing to an increase in the global carbon footprint exacerbates climate change which most severely affects already-marginalized communities" (citing Westra and Lawson, 2001). By documenting and optimizing their carbon emissions (Section 3.5.1), BigScience aimed to set a precedent for accountability.

**Linguistic Equity**. When models are trained predominantly on English, they implicitly encode cultural and linguistic biases that may not transfer to other languages. This affects billions of speakers who cannot interact with these systems in their native languages, or receive degraded performance when they do.

### 2.3 Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

To understand BLOOM's positioning, we must examine what came before:

**The Transformer Revolution**. Section 2.1 traces the evolution from n-gram models (Shannon, 1948) to neural language models (Bengio et al., 2000; Mikolov et al., 2010) to Transformer-based architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017). The key breakthrough was the empirical observation that "a language model's performance tends to increase as the model is made larger—sometimes predictably (Kaplan et al., 2020) and sometimes suddenly (Wei et al., 2022)." This scaling phenomenon drove the race toward larger models.

**Proprietary Large-Scale Models**. By 2022, several 100B+ parameter models existed:
- **GPT-3** (Brown et al., 2020): 175B parameters, English-focused, API-only access
- **Gopher** (Rae et al., 2021): 280B parameters, from DeepMind
- **PaLM** (Chowdhery et al., 2022): 540B parameters, from Google

These models demonstrated impressive capabilities but were not publicly released, preventing independent verification, adaptation, or study of their internal mechanisms.

**Open but Limited Alternatives**. Some open models existed but with constraints:
- **GPT-Neo/GPT-J** (Black et al.): Smaller scale (up to 6B parameters)
- **OPT** (Zhang et al., 2022): 175B parameters, open weights, but primarily English
- **mGPT** (Shliazhko et al., 2022): Multilingual but smaller scale

**Multilingual Models with Architectural Constraints**. Models like **mT5** (Xue et al., 2021) and **M2M-100** (Fan et al., 2021) supported many languages but used encoder-decoder architectures optimized for specific tasks (like translation) rather than general-purpose autoregressive generation.

**The Data Problem**. Section 3.1 identifies a critical gap in prior work: dataset curation relied on "heuristics-based filtering approaches that prioritize getting as much 'high-quality' data for as little cost as possible over engaging with the needs—and rights—of data subjects." This led to documented harms, such as blocklists that "suppress LGBTQ+ and African American English (AAE) text from a corpus" (Dodge et al., 2021) or filtering that "implicitly prioritize US-centric views" (Johnson et al., 2022).

### 2.4 BLOOM's Positioning Relative to Existing Work

BLOOM positions itself at the intersection of three dimensions where prior work was incomplete:

| Dimension | Prior State-of-the-Art | BLOOM's Approach |
|-----------|----------------------|------------------|
| **Access** | API-only or restricted licenses | Full model weights and code under Responsible AI License (Section 3.6) |
| **Multilinguality** | Primarily English (GPT-3, OPT) or task-specific (M2M) | 46 natural + 13 programming languages in a general-purpose decoder |
| **Development Process** | Corporate teams, opaque decision-making | 1200+ participants from 38 countries, 30 working groups (Figure 1, Section 2.2) |

**Architectural Choices**. Section 3.2 explains that BLOOM adopts a "causal decoder-only" architecture, aligning with state-of-the-art LLMs (GPT-3, Gopher, PaLM) rather than encoder-decoder models (T5, mT5). This choice was validated through ablation studies (Wang et al., 2022a) showing decoder-only models "performed best" for zero-shot generalization immediately after pretraining.

**Training Data Philosophy**. Unlike models trained on opaque web crawls (e.g., CommonCrawl without curation), BLOOM's ROOTS corpus (Section 3.1) was constructed through "human involvement, local expertise, and language expertise." The corpus includes 1.61 terabytes of text with explicit documentation of sources, languages, and licensing status (Table 1, Figure 3).

**Evaluation Transparency**. Section 4 emphasizes that prompts were "developed prior to BLOOM's release, and did not undergo any a priori refinement using models." This contrasts with practices where prompt engineering is iterated until best-case performance is achieved, which is "harder to reproduce systematically" and "not representative of true zero-shot learning."

**Carbon Accountability**. Section 3.5.1 provides a detailed Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) of emissions: 81 tons CO₂eq total, with breakdown by equipment manufacturing (14%), training energy (30%), and idle consumption (55%). Table 4 compares this to GPT-3 (502 tons), Gopher (352 tons), and OPT (70 tons), showing BLOOM achieved comparable scale with significantly lower emissions due to France's low-carbon energy grid (57 gCO₂eq/kWh vs. 231 gCO₂eq/kWh for OPT).

### 2.5 The BigScience Organizational Model

A distinctive aspect of BLOOM is that the paper documents not just a model, but **a new organizational model for large-scale ML research**. Section 2.2 describes how BigScience grew from "Hugging Face and the French NLP community" into "a broader international collaboration" with participants from linguistics, statistics, socio-cultural anthropology, philosophy, law, and other fields—not just machine learning.

The 30 working groups (Figure 1) covered technical areas (Architecture, Engineering, Tokenization) alongside social dimensions (Bias-Fairness, Ethical and Legal, Broader Impacts). This structure ensured that decisions about data sourcing, model architecture, and licensing were not made unilaterally by engineers but through collaborative deliberation.

The Ethical Charter (Section 2.2) explicitly emphasizes "inclusivity and diversity, openness and reproducibility, and responsibility"—values that directly shaped technical choices like the multilingual ROOTS corpus and the behavioral-use restrictions in the Responsible AI License (Section 3.6).

### 2.6 Summary of the Gap

In essence, the BLOOM paper addresses a gap that is simultaneously **technical** (no open 100B+ parameter multilingual model existed), **organizational** (no precedent for 1200-person collaborative LLM development), and **ethical** (no model had documented its development process with this level of transparency regarding data governance, carbon footprint, and licensing restrictions). Prior work demonstrated that scale brings capability, but BLOOM asks: *scale for whom, developed by whom, and with what accountability?*

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an engineering and architecture paper that documents the design decisions behind building a 176 billion-parameter multilingual language model through collaborative research. The core idea is that achieving competitive performance at this scale requires careful coordination across data curation, model architecture, tokenization, distributed training infrastructure, and evaluation methodology—not just throwing more compute at the problem.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

BLOOM is a decoder-only Transformer language model that predicts the next token in a sequence, trained on 1.61 terabytes of text spanning 46 natural languages and 13 programming languages. It solves the problem of building a publicly accessible, multilingual large language model by combining a carefully curated dataset (ROOTS), architectural innovations (ALiBi positional embeddings), and distributed training infrastructure (3D parallelism across 384 GPUs) to achieve competitive performance while maintaining transparency about the development process.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The BLOOM system consists of five major components that operate in sequence. First, the **ROOTS corpus** serves as the training data foundation, comprising 498 Hugging Face datasets that are sourced, filtered, deduplicated, and documented before training begins. Second, the **tokenizer** converts raw text into subword tokens using byte-level Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) with a 250,680-token vocabulary, ensuring lossless encoding across all supported languages. Third, the **model architecture** is a 70-layer decoder-only Transformer with 112 attention heads and a hidden dimension of 14,336, using ALiBi positional embeddings instead of learned position embeddings. Fourth, the **distributed training infrastructure** employs Megatron-DeepSpeed with 3D parallelism (data, tensor, and pipeline parallelism) across 384 NVIDIA A100 GPUs to make training computationally feasible. Fifth, the **evaluation framework** uses prompt-based testing on benchmarks like SuperGLUE, machine translation tasks, and summarization to measure zero-shot and few-shot capabilities without fine-tuning on task-specific data.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **Training data (ROOTS corpus)**: We start here because the dataset fundamentally constrains what the model can learn; understanding the 46 natural languages, 13 programming languages, and governance decisions explains why BLOOM behaves differently from English-centric models.
- **Tokenization**: We explain tokenization second because it sits between raw text and model input, and BLOOM's byte-level BPE with 250,680 tokens directly affects how multilingual text is represented.
- **Model architecture**: We cover the Transformer design third, including the 70 layers, ALiBi positional embeddings, and LayerNorm choices, because these determine the model's capacity and training stability.
- **Distributed training infrastructure**: We explain the engineering fourth because training 176 billion parameters requires 384 GPUs with 3D parallelism, and understanding this reveals why such models are typically restricted to well-resourced organizations.
- **Training hyperparameters and finetuning**: We conclude with learning rates, batch sizes, and the BLOOMZ multitask finetuning procedure because these determine how the architecture actually learns from the data.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**What type of paper this is and the core idea.** This paper documents the complete technical pipeline for building BLOOM, a 176 billion-parameter open-access multilingual language model, with the core idea that democratizing large-scale language technology requires transparency across all development stages—from data sourcing to architecture choices to training infrastructure—rather than simply releasing model weights.

**System and data pipeline diagram in words.** The BLOOM development pipeline operates in five sequential stages. First, data working groups identify and curate 498 datasets covering 46 natural languages and 13 programming languages, applying language-specific quality filters and removing duplicates to create the ROOTS corpus totaling 1.61 terabytes of text. Second, a byte-level BPE tokenizer with 250,680 vocabulary items is trained on a deduplicated subset of ROOTS, converting raw text into token sequences that preserve all 256 possible byte values. Third, the 70-layer decoder-only Transformer architecture processes these token sequences through stacked attention and feed-forward layers, using ALiBi positional embeddings to encode token positions without learned parameters. Fourth, the model trains across 384 NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs using Megatron-DeepSpeed with 3D parallelism (data, tensor, and pipeline parallelism) for 3.5 months, consuming 1,082,990 compute hours. Fifth, the pretrained model undergoes evaluation on zero-shot and few-shot benchmarks, with optional multitask finetuning on the xP3 dataset to produce BLOOMZ variants with improved task generalization.

#### 3.4.1 Training Dataset: The ROOTS Corpus

**Motivation for careful data curation.** Section 3.1 argues that prior large-scale language models relied on "heuristics-based filtering approaches that prioritize getting as much 'high-quality' data for as little cost as possible over engaging with the needs—and rights—of data subjects." This approach compounds biases from source material (such as CommonCrawl dumps) with biases from the filtering method, leading to documented harms like suppressing LGBTQ+ and African American English text through blocklist filtering (Dodge et al., 2021). BigScience aimed to prioritize "human involvement, local expertise, and language expertise" in data curation, which required building organizational structures to support collaborative decision-making across 38 countries.

**Language selection process.** The language inclusion process was incremental and community-driven rather than determined by automatic language identification. The team started with eight of the world's largest languages by number of speakers, then expanded Swahili to the broader Niger-Congo language category and Hindi and Urdu to the Indic language category based on recommendations from language communities (Nekoto et al., 2020; Kunchukuttan et al., 2020). Finally, any group of three or more participants fluent in an additional language could add it to the supported list if they committed to selecting sources and guiding processing choices for that language, avoiding common issues with corpora selected through automatic language identification without specific language expertise (Caswell et al., 2022).

**Corpus composition and scale.** The ROOTS corpus comprises 1.61 terabytes of text from 498 Hugging Face datasets, spanning 46 natural languages and 13 programming languages (Section 3.1). Table 1 provides the complete linguistic breakdown with language family, genus, macroarea, and size in bytes for each language. The largest components are English (484,953,009,124 bytes), French (208,242,620,434 bytes), Spanish (175,098,365,045 bytes), Simplified Chinese (261,019,433,892 bytes), and Portuguese (79,277,543,375 bytes), with programming languages collectively contributing 174,700,245,772 bytes. Figure 3 visualizes this distribution as a treemap where Indo-European and Sino-Tibetan language families dominate with a combined 1,321.89 GB, while the Niger-Congo language family subset constitutes only 0.4 GB.

**Data sources and collection methods.** The corpus was assembled through multiple complementary approaches. First, workshop participants and research collectives compiled the "BigScience Catalogue" through hackathons co-organized by communities such as Machine Learning Tokyo, Masakhane, and LatinX in AI, identifying 252 sources through this bottom-up approach with at least 21 sources per language category (McMillan-Major et al., 2022). Second, participants identified locally relevant websites for Spanish, Chinese, French, and English to be added via pseudocrawl, a method to obtain websites from a Common Crawl snapshot. Third, programming language data was collected from the GitHub data collection on Google's BigQuery and deduplicated of exact matches, mirroring design choices from AlphaCode (Li et al., 2022). Fourth, OSCAR version 21.09 (corresponding to the February 2021 snapshot of Common Crawl) was included to satisfy data volume needs, ultimately constituting 38% of the corpus (Ortiz Suárez et al., 2019; Abadji et al., 2021).

**Data governance and traceability.** Section 3.1.1 describes how BigScience addressed data rights through a multidisciplinary lens combining technical, legal, and sociological expertise. The team sought explicit permission to use data from specific providers whenever possible (such as the AI2-managed S2ORC corpus or articles from the French newspaper Le Monde), kept individual sources separate until the final stages of preprocessing to maintain traceability, and adopted a composite release approach for the various data sources to foster reproducibility while respecting source-dependent licensing needs. Resources to visualize and access the ROOTS corpus are available on the Hugging Face Hub organization "BigScience Data," which hosts demos and direct access to 223 out of 498 components that can be distributed given their licensing status, privacy risks, and agreements with original custodians.

**Quality filtering methodology.** After obtaining source data, the team applied quality indicators to filter non-natural language text (such as preprocessing errors, SEO pages, or spam) without making a priori judgments of grammaticality or content. The full list of indicators is described in Laurençon et al. (2022), but critically, the indicators were adapted to each source in two ways: first, parameters such as thresholds and supporting term lists were selected individually for each language by fluent speakers; second, the team manually reviewed each individual source to identify which indicators were most likely to identify non-natural language. This approach contrasts with automatic filtering that applies uniform thresholds across all languages, which can systematically disadvantage lower-resource languages.

**Deduplication and privacy redaction.** The pipeline included two deduplication steps to remove near-duplicate documents, plus regex-based redaction of Personal Identifiable Information (such as social security numbers) from the OSCAR portion of the corpus, which was deemed to present the highest privacy risks. The redaction was applied even in cases where expressions had some false positives, prioritizing privacy protection over perfect precision.

**Token count and training duration.** The ROOTS dataset contains approximately 341 billion tokens of text, but the team trained all models for 410 billion tokens—training for an additional 25 billion tokens on repeated data in light of revised scaling laws published during training (Hoffmann et al., 2022). The learning rate decay schedule was set for 410 billion tokens with 375 million warmup tokens, meaning the end of learning rate decay was never reached since warmup tokens plus decay tokens exceeded the total number of tokens actually processed.

#### 3.4.2 Tokenization: Byte-Level BPE with Multilingual Vocabulary

**Why tokenization matters for multilingual models.** Section 3.3 argues that tokenizer design decisions are often neglected in favor of "default" settings, citing OPT and GPT-3 both using GPT-2's tokenizer trained for English. This can be justified by the large computational costs of training that constrain evaluation of tokenizer impact, but "the diverse nature of BLOOM's training data requires careful design choices to ensure that the tokenizer encodes sentences in a lossless manner."

**Fertility as a validation metric.** The team used fertility as a sanity check metric, defined as the number of subwords created per word or per dataset by the tokenizer. Fertility was measured using subsets of Universal Dependencies 2.9 (Nivre et al., 2017) and OSCAR in the languages of interest. A very high fertility on a language compared to a monolingual tokenizer may indicate degradation on downstream multilingual performance (Rust et al., 2021). The goal was to not degrade fertility on each language by more than 10 percentage points when comparing the multilingual tokenizer with monolingual tokenizers in corresponding languages.

**Vocabulary size selection.** The team conducted validation experiments using 150,000 and 250,000 vocabulary sizes to make comparisons with existing multilingual modeling literature easier (Conneau et al., 2020; Xue et al., 2021). They ultimately settled on a vocabulary of 250,000 tokens to reach the initial fertility objective compared to monolingual tokenizers. Since vocabulary size determines the embedding matrix size, it also had to be divisible by 128 for GPU efficiency reasons and by 4 to enable Tensor Parallelism. The final size was 250,680 vocabulary items with 200 tokens reserved for possible future applications such as removing private information using placeholder tokens.

**Byte-level BPE algorithm.** The tokenizer uses the Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) algorithm introduced by Gage (1994), but operates at the byte level rather than character level to avoid information loss during tokenization. The tokenizer creates merges starting from bytes as the smallest units instead of characters (Radford et al., 2019), ensuring tokenization never results in unknown tokens because all 256 bytes can be contained in the vocabulary. In addition, byte-level BPE maximizes vocabulary sharing between languages (Wang et al., 2020), which is critical for a model supporting 46 natural languages with diverse scripts.

**Normalization decisions.** Upstream of the BPE tokenization algorithm, no normalization of the text was performed to maintain the most general model possible. The team observed that adding unicode normalization such as NFKC did not reduce fertility by more than 0.8% on all considered languages but came at the cost of making the model less general—for example, causing different unicode representations of the same number to be encoded identically, which loses information about the original text representation.

**Pre-tokenization rules.** The pre-tokenization has two goals: producing a first division of the text (usually using whitespaces and punctuation) and restricting the maximum length of sequences of tokens produced by the BPE algorithm. The pre-tokenization rule used was a regex that splits words apart while preserving all characters, particularly sequences of spaces and line breaks that are crucial for programming languages. The team explicitly avoided English-centric splits common in other tokenizers (such as splitting around 'nt or 'll) and did not use splits on numbers and digits, which caused issues in Arabic and code.

**Tokenizer training data quality issues.** The team initially used a non-deduplicated subset of ROOTS for tokenizer training, but a qualitative study on the vocabulary revealed issues. For instance, earlier versions of the tokenizer contained entire URLs stored as single tokens, caused by several documents containing a high number of duplicates. These issues motivated removing duplicated lines in the tokenizer training data before applying the same sampling ratios per language as for the main training data.

**Fertility results.** Table 2 shows fertility comparisons between the BLOOM tokenizer and existing monolingual tokenizers across six languages. For French, BLOOM achieved 1.17 fertility compared to 1.30 for the monolingual CamemBERT tokenizer (an 11% improvement). For English, BLOOM matched GPT-2's 1.15 fertility exactly. For Spanish, BLOOM achieved 1.16 compared to 1.12 for DeepESP/gpt2-spanish (3% higher). For Chinese, BLOOM achieved 1.58 compared to 1.50 for bert-base-chinese (5% higher). For Hindi, BLOOM achieved 1.18 compared to 1.07 for monsoon-nlp/hindi-bert (9% higher). For Arabic, BLOOM achieved 1.34 compared to 1.16 for Arabic BERT (13% higher). All languages stayed within or close to the 10 percentage point degradation target, validating the tokenizer design.

#### 3.4.3 Model Architecture: 70-Layer Decoder-Only Transformer with ALiBi

**Architecture selection methodology.** Section 3.2.1 explains that the design space of possible architectures is immense, making exhaustive exploration impossible. The team took a middle ground between exactly replicating an existing large language model architecture and adopting unproven improvements, focusing on "model families that have been shown to scale well, and that have reasonable support in publicly available tools and codebases." They ablated components and hyperparameters using smaller models (6.7B parameter scale for pretraining objective ablations, 1.3B scale for position embeddings, activations, and layer normalization) to make the best use of the final compute budget.

**Why decoder-only over encoder-decoder.** Section 3.2.2 addresses the architectural choice between encoder-decoder and decoder-only models. While the original Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) used an encoder-decoder architecture, and encoder-decoder models significantly outperform decoder-only models for transfer learning (Raffel et al., 2020), "all state-of-the-art language models over 100 billion parameters are causal decoder-only models" (Brown et al., 2020; Rae et., 2021; Chowdhery et al., 2022). The team explored this question systematically in Wang et al. (2022a), evaluating encoder-decoder and decoder-only architectures with causal, prefix, and masked language modeling pretraining objectives. Results showed that "immediately after pretraining, causal decoder-only models performed best," validating the choice of state-of-the-art LLMs. Furthermore, decoder-only models can be more efficiently adapted after pretraining to a non-causal architecture and objective—an approach further explored and confirmed by Tay et al. (2022).

**Evaluation focus on zero-shot generalization.** Because finetuning a 100+ billion parameter model is unwieldy, the team focused evaluation of architectural decisions on zero-shot generalization rather than transfer learning (Section 3.2.1). They measured zero-shot performance on diverse aggregates of tasks: 29 tasks from the EleutherAI Language Model Evaluation Harness (EAI-Eval, Gao et al., 2021) and 9 tasks from the T0 evaluation set (T0-Eval, Sanh et al., 2022). There is significant overlap between the two evaluation sets (only one task from T0-Eval, StoryCloze, is not in EAI-Eval), although all prompts between the two are different. The task aggregates share 17 of the 31 tasks from GPT-3's evaluation (Brown et al., 2020).

**Architectural deviations from standard Transformer.** Section 3.2.3 documents two key architectural deviations adopted in BLOOM based on experiments in Le Scao et al. (2022).

**ALiBi positional embeddings.** Instead of adding positional information to the embedding layer (as in the original Transformer), ALiBi directly attenuates the attention scores based on how far away the keys and queries are (Press et al., 2021). Although ALiBi was initially motivated by its ability to extrapolate to longer sequences, the team found it also led to smoother training and better downstream performance even at the original sequence length—outperforming both learned embeddings (Vaswani et al., 2017) and rotary embeddings (Su et al., 2021). The ALiBi mask uses slope parameters $k_{head}$ taken as $2^{-\frac{8i}{n}}$ where $n$ is the number of heads and $i \in \{1, 2, ..., n\}$, as shown in Figure 5.

**Embedding LayerNorm.** In preliminary experiments training a 104 billion parameter model, the team experimented with an additional layer normalization immediately after the embedding layer, as recommended by the bitsandbytes library (Dettmers et al., 2022) with its StableEmbedding layer. They found this significantly improved training stability. Even though they also found it penalizes zero-shot generalization in Le Scao et al. (2022), they trained BLOOM with an additional layer normalization after the first embedding layer to avoid training instabilities. The preliminary 104B experiments were conducted in float16, while the final training was in bfloat16. Since then, float16 has been attributed as being responsible for many observed instabilities in training LLMs (Zhang et al., 2022; Zeng et al., 2022), and it is possible that bfloat16 alleviates the need for the embedding LayerNorm.

**Full architecture specification.** Figure 5 represents the complete BLOOM architecture. The model consists of 70 decoder blocks, each containing multi-head attention followed by a feed-forward MLP, with layer normalization applied before each sublayer. The attention mechanism uses ALiBi masking instead of traditional positional embeddings. The embedding layer includes an additional LayerNorm for stability. The model uses tied embeddings (True), meaning the input embedding matrix and output projection matrix share weights. Table 3 provides the complete hyperparameter specification: 176,247 million parameters, 70 layers, hidden dimension of 14,336, 112 attention heads, vocabulary size of 250,680, sequence length of 2,048, and GELU activation function.

**Out-of-scope architectures.** The team did not consider mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures (Shazeer et al., 2017) due to a lack of widely used GPU-based codebases suitable for training them at scale. Similarly, they did not consider state-space models (Gu et al., 2020) because at the time of BLOOM's design, they consistently underperformed in natural language tasks (Gu et al., 2021). Both approaches are noted as promising and have since demonstrated competitive results—at large scales for MoE (Fedus et al., 2022; Srivastava et al., 2022) and at smaller scale for state-space models with H3 (Fu et al., 2023).

#### 3.4.4 Engineering: 3D Parallelism Across 384 GPUs

**Hardware infrastructure.** Section 3.4.1 specifies that BLOOM was trained on Jean Zay, a French government-funded supercomputer owned by GENCI and operated at IDRIS (the national computing center for CNRS). Training took approximately 3.5 months and consumed 1,082,990 compute hours. Training was conducted on 48 nodes, each having 8 NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs (384 GPUs total), with a reserve of 4 spare nodes maintained for hardware failures. Each node was equipped with 2x AMD EPYC 7543 32-Core CPUs and 512 GB of RAM. Storage was handled by a mix of full flash and hard disk drives using a SpectrumScale (GPFS) parallel file system shared between all nodes and users. For interconnect, 4 NVLink GPU-to-GPU interconnects per node enabled intra-node communications, while 4 Omni-Path 100 Gbps links per node, arranged in an enhanced hypercube 8D global topology, were used for inter-node communications.

**Megatron-DeepSpeed framework.** Section 3.4.2 explains that BLOOM was trained using Megatron-DeepSpeed (Smith et al., 2022), a framework for large-scale distributed training consisting of two parts. Megatron-LM (Shoeybi et al., 2019) provides the Transformer implementation, tensor parallelism, and data loading primitives. DeepSpeed (Rasley et al., 2020) provides the ZeRO optimizer, model pipelining, and general distributed training components. This framework enables efficient training with 3D parallelism (Narayanan et al., 2021), shown in Figure 6, which fuses three complementary approaches to distributed training.

**Data parallelism (DP).** Data parallelism replicates the model multiple times, with each replica placed on a different device and fed a slice of the data. Processing is done in parallel, and all model replicas are synchronized at the end of each training step through gradient averaging.

**Tensor parallelism (TP).** Tensor parallelism partitions individual layers of the model across multiple devices. Instead of having the whole activation or gradient tensor reside on a single GPU, shards of this tensor are placed on separate GPUs. This technique is sometimes called horizontal parallelism or intra-layer model parallelism.

**Pipeline parallelism (PP).** Pipeline parallelism splits up the model's layers across multiple GPUs, so that only a fraction of the model's layers are placed on each GPU. This is sometimes called vertical parallelism.

**ZeRO optimizer.** The Zero Redundancy Optimizer (ZeRO; Rajbhandari et al., 2020) allows different processes to hold only a fraction of data (parameters, gradients, and optimizer states) required for a training step. BLOOM used ZeRO stage 1, meaning that only the optimizer states are sharded in this manner.

**GPU utilization and performance.** The four components (DP, TP, PP, ZeRO) are combined to allow scaling to hundreds of GPUs with extremely high GPU utilization. The team achieved 156 TFLOPs in their fastest configuration with A100 GPUs, attaining the objective of half the theoretical peak performance of 312 TFLOPs (in float32 or bfloat16).

**Floating point format selection.** Section 3.4.3 explains that in earlier experiments with 104 billion-parameter models on NVIDIA V100 GPUs, the team observed numerical instabilities that caused irreversible training divergences. They hypothesize these instabilities stem from initial use of IEEE float16—a 16-bit floating point format with a very limited dynamic range that can cause overflows. The NVIDIA A100 GPUs ultimately used support the bfloat16 format (Wang and Kanwar, 2019; Kalamkar et al., 2019), which has the same dynamic range as float32. However, bfloat16 still has much lower precision, which motivated use of mixed-precision training (Micikevicius et al., 2018). This technique performs certain precision-sensitive operations (such as gradient accumulation and softmax) in float32 precision and the rest of operations in lower precision, achieving a balance of high performance and training stability. Final training was performed in bfloat16 mixed precision, which solved the instability problem (in line with previous observations by Smith et al., 2022).

**Fused CUDA kernels for efficiency.** Section 3.4.4 explains that GPUs cannot retrieve data for computations and perform computations simultaneously, and modern GPU compute performance is much higher than memory transfer speed. Kernel fusion (Wu et al., 2012) optimizes GPU-based computations by performing several consecutive operations in only one kernel call, minimizing data transfers by keeping intermediary results in GPU registers instead of copying them to VRAM. The team used several custom fused CUDA kernels provided by Megatron-LM: an optimized kernel for LayerNorm, kernels to fuse various combinations of scaling, masking, and softmax operations, and fusion of bias term addition with the GeLU activation using PyTorch's JIT functionality. As a consequence, adding the bias term in the GeLU operation adds no additional time because the operation is memory-bound—the additional computation is negligible compared to data transfers between GPU VRAM and registers, so fusing both operations essentially halves their runtime.

**Additional scaling challenges.** Section 3.4.5 notes that scaling to 384 GPUs required two final changes: disabling asynchronous CUDA kernel launches (for ease of debugging and to prevent deadlocks) and splitting parameter groups into smaller subgroups (to avoid excessive CPU memory allocations). During training, hardware failures occurred on average 1–2 times per week. Since backup nodes were available and automatically used, and checkpoints were saved every three hours, this did not affect training throughput significantly. A PyTorch deadlock bug in the data loader and disk space issues led to 5–10 hour downtimes. Given the relative sparsity of engineering issues and only one loss spike (which the model swiftly recovered from), human intervention was less necessary than in comparable projects (Zhang et al., 2022).

---

#### 3.4.5 Training Hyperparameters and Multitask Finetuning

**Pretrained model variants.** Section 3.5 documents six size variants of BLOOM with hyperparameters detailed in Table 3. The variants are: BLOOM-560M (559M parameters, 24 layers, 1024 hidden dimension, 16 attention heads), BLOOM-1.1B (1,065M parameters, 24 layers, 1536 hidden dimension, 16 attention heads), BLOOM-1.7B (1,722M parameters, 24 layers, 2048 hidden dimension, 16 attention heads), BLOOM-3B (3,003M parameters, 30 layers, 2560 hidden dimension, 32 attention heads), BLOOM-7.1B (7,069M parameters, 30 layers, 4096 hidden dimension, 32 attention heads), and BLOOM-176B (176,247M parameters, 70 layers, 14336 hidden dimension, 112 attention heads). Architecture and training hyperparameters come from experimental results (Le Scao et al., 2022) and prior work on training large language models (Brown et al., 2020; Kaplan et al., 2020). Model depth and width for non-176B models roughly follow previous literature, deviating for 3B and 7.1B only to fit the models more easily on the training setup. Embedding parameter sizes are larger for BLOOM owing to the larger multilingual vocabulary, but scaling literature discounts embedding operations (Kaplan et al., 2020).

**Pretraining hyperparameters for BLOOM-176B.** For the full 176B model, the global batch size was 2,048, learning rate was 6e-5 with cosine decay over 410 billion tokens, warmup was 375 million tokens, minimum learning rate was 6e-6, Adam optimizer used $\beta_1 = 0.9$ and $\beta_2 = 0.95$, weight decay was 1e-1, gradient clipping was 1.0, and no dropout was used. During development at the 104B parameter scale, the team experimented with different values of Adam $\beta$ parameters, weight decay, and gradient clipping to target stability but did not find it helpful.

**Multitask finetuning for BLOOMZ.** Section 3.5 describes multitask prompted finetuning (also referred to as instruction tuning), which involves finetuning a pretrained language model on a training mixture composed of a large set of different tasks specified through natural language prompts. T0 (Sanh et al., 2022), developed as part of BigScience, demonstrated that language models finetuned on a multitask mixture of prompted datasets have strong zero-shot task generalization abilities and can outperform language models an order of magnitude larger that did not undergo such finetuning. After pretraining BLOOM, the team applied the same massively multitask finetuning recipe to equip BLOOM with multilingual zero-shot task generalization abilities, referring to the resulting models as BLOOMZ.

**xP3 dataset for finetuning.** To train BLOOMZ, the team extended P3 (the Public Pool of Prompts used for T0) to include new datasets in languages other than English and new tasks such as translation. This resulted in xP3, a collection of prompts for 83 datasets covering 46 languages and 16 tasks. As highlighted in Figure 4, xP3 mirrors the language distribution of ROOTS. Tasks in xP3 are both cross-lingual (e.g., translation) and monolingual (e.g., summarization, question answering). The team used PromptSource (Bach et al., 2022) to collect these prompts, adding additional metadata such as input and target languages. To study the importance of multilingual prompts, they also machine-translated English prompts in xP3 to the respective dataset languages to produce a collection called xP3mt.

**Finetuning hyperparameters.** Finetuned BLOOMZ models maintain the same architecture hyperparameters as BLOOM models (Section 3.5). Finetuning hyperparameters are loosely based on T0 (Sanh et al., 2022) and FLAN (Wei et al., 2021). Learning rates are determined by doubling the minimum learning rate of the respective pretrained model and then rounding. Global batch sizes are multiplied by four for small variants to increase throughput. While models are finetuned for 13 billion tokens, the best checkpoint is chosen according to a separate validation set. Performance was found to plateau after 1–6 billion tokens of finetuning.

**Contrastive finetuning for embeddings.** Section 3.5 also describes contrastive finetuning of the 1.3B and 7.1B parameter BLOOM models using the SGPT Bi-Encoder recipe (Muennighoff, 2022) to train models that produce high-quality text embeddings. The team created SGPT-BLOOM-7.1B-msmarco geared towards multilingual information retrieval and SGPT-BLOOM-1.7B-nli for multilingual semantic textual similarity (STS). Recent benchmarking has found these models to also generalize to various other embedding tasks, such as bitext mining, reranking, or feature extraction for downstream classification (Muennighoff et al., 2022a).

---

#### 3.4.6 Carbon Footprint and Release Strategy

**Life Cycle Assessment methodology.** Section 3.5.1 explains that while most attempts to estimate the carbon footprint of language models focus on emissions from energy consumed during training, other sources are also important. The team was inspired by the Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) approach (Klöppfer, 1997) and aimed to consider aspects such as equipment manufacturing emissions, intermediate model training, and deployment.

**Carbon emissions breakdown.** According to estimates, carbon emissions from BLOOM training add up to approximately 81 tons of CO₂eq, of which 14% were generated by equipment manufacturing (11 tons), 30% by energy consumed during training (25 tons), and 55% by idle consumption of equipment and computing cluster used for training (45 tons).

**Comparison with other models.** Table 4 compares BLOOM's carbon emissions to similar LLMs. GPT-3 (175B parameters) consumed 1,287 MWh and emitted 502 tons CO₂eq. Gopher (280B parameters) consumed 1,066 MWh and emitted 352 tons. OPT (175B parameters) consumed 324 MWh and emitted 70 tons. BLOOM (176B parameters) consumed 433 MWh and emitted 25 tons. While BLOOM's energy consumption is slightly higher than OPT (433 MWh vs. 324 MWh), its emissions are approximately two-thirds less (25 tons vs. 70 tons). This is thanks to the low carbon intensity of the energy grid used for training BLOOM, which emits 57 gCO₂eq/kWh, compared to 231 gCO₂eq/kWh for the grid used for OPT training. France's national energy grid (used by Jean Zay) is largely powered by nuclear energy, which is low-carbon compared to grids powered by coal and natural gas.

**Total workshop computation footprint.** The final BLOOM training represents approximately 37% of overall emissions from the BigScience workshop, with other processes (intermediate training runs and model evaluation) adding up to the other 63%. This is slightly less than the estimate made by OPT authors, who stated their total carbon footprint was roughly 2 times higher due to experimentation, baselines, and ablation (Zhang et al., 2022).

**Deployment emissions.** Ongoing exploration of BLOOM API carbon emissions estimated that real-time deployment on a GCP instance with 16 GPUs running in the us-central1 region results in approximately 20 kg of CO₂eq emitted per day of deployment (or 0.83 kg per hour). This figure is not representative of all deployment use-cases and will vary depending on hardware, model implementation specifics (such as whether batching is used), and the number of requests received.

**Model Card and licensing.** Section 3.6 describes the release strategy. The BLOOM model was released with a detailed Model Card (Mitchell et al., 2019) describing technical specifications, training details, intended use, out-of-scope uses, and limitations. For licensing, the team chose to strike a balance between unrestricted open-access and responsible use by including behavioral-use clauses to limit application towards potentially harmful use-cases. The license developed for BLOOM is a "Responsible AI License (RAIL)" that separates licensing of "source code" and "model" (referenced by trained parameters). It includes detailed definitions of "use" and "derived works" to ensure anticipated downstream use (prompting, finetuning, distillation, use of logits and probability distributions) are explicitly identified. The license contains 13 behavioral-use restrictions based on intended uses and limitations described in the Model Card and the BigScience ethical charter. The license offers the model at no charge, and users are free to use it as long as they comply with terms. The source code for BLOOM was made available under an Apache 2.0 open source license.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the contributions that distinguish BLOOM from prior large language model development. We distinguish between **fundamental innovations** (new paradigms or capabilities that did not exist before) and **significant advances** (substantial improvements on existing approaches). The goal is to help readers understand what aspects of BLOOM represent genuine novelty versus careful engineering at scale.

---

### 4.1 Fundamental Innovation: The Collaborative Development Model

**What makes this different.** Before BigScience, no 100+ billion parameter language model had been developed through open, international collaboration. Section 2.2 documents that BLOOM was created by **1,200+ participants from 38 countries** organized into **30 working groups** (Figure 1), spanning not only machine learning but also linguistics, law, philosophy, and socio-cultural anthropology. This contrasts sharply with prior LLMs developed by single corporate teams (GPT-3 at OpenAI, Gopher at DeepMind, PaLM at Google) or small research collectives (EleutherAI).

**Why this matters.** The organizational model is not merely a social choice—it directly shaped technical outcomes that would not have emerged from a homogeneous team:

- **Language Selection**: The incremental, community-driven process (Section 3.1.2) allowed languages like Swahili to expand to the broader Niger-Congo category based on recommendations from language communities (Nekoto et al., 2020). A corporate team optimizing for benchmark performance would likely have excluded these lower-resource languages entirely.

- **Data Governance**: The multidisciplinary lens combining technical, legal, and sociological expertise (Section 3.1.1) produced traceability mechanisms and explicit permission-seeking that opaque web crawls lack. This is a structural innovation in how training data is accounted for.

- **Ethical Integration**: The Ethical Charter was not an afterthought but actively guided technical choices, from data sourcing to licensing (Section 3.6). Prior models typically addressed ethics through separate "responsibility" teams disconnected from engineering decisions.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental**. This is a **fundamental innovation** because it establishes a new organizational paradigm for large-scale ML research. The BigScience model has already influenced subsequent efforts (e.g., BigCode for code models), suggesting it represents a reproducible alternative to corporate-only development.

**Limitation**. The paper acknowledges (Section 2.2) that the collaborative model required significant coordination overhead and that "we were not able to fully establish an international organization in the comparatively short time between the project start and model training." Future efforts may improve on this structure.

---

### 4.2 Fundamental Innovation: Multilingual Open-Access at 100B+ Scale

**What makes this different.** Table 4 and Section 2.3 show that before BLOOM, the landscape of large language models had a critical gap:

| Model | Parameters | Open Weights | Multilingual |
|-------|------------|--------------|--------------|
| GPT-3 | 175B | No (API only) | Limited |
| Gopher | 280B | No | Limited |
| OPT | 175B | Yes | Primarily English |
| mT5 | 13B | Yes | Yes (but encoder-decoder) |
| **BLOOM** | **176B** | **Yes** | **46 natural + 13 programming** |

BLOOM is the **first open-access language model exceeding 100 billion parameters with genuine multilingual capability**. OPT released weights but was trained primarily on English. mT5 supported many languages but used an encoder-decoder architecture optimized for specific tasks rather than general-purpose autoregressive generation.

**Why this matters.** This combination unlocks research capabilities that were previously impossible:

- **Independent Verification**: Researchers can inspect model weights, run custom evaluations, and study internal mechanisms—impossible with API-only models like GPT-3.

- **Adaptation for Under-Resourced Languages**: The multilingual training enables researchers working on low-resource languages to fine-tune or probe the model without starting from scratch. Section 4.3.3 shows BLOOM achieves competitive translation quality even for languages under-represented in training data (e.g., Galician, which was not included but shares similarities with Portuguese).

- **Architectural Research at Scale**: The release enables study of emergent behaviors, scaling laws, and failure modes at the 100B+ parameter range—previously restricted to organizations with proprietary models.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental**. This is a **fundamental innovation** because it fills a previously empty cell in the model capability matrix. No prior system combined open access, 100B+ scale, and broad multilinguality.

**Caveat**. Section 4.3.3 notes that translation quality for some under-represented low-resource languages (Swahili, Yoruba, each with &lt;50k tokens in training) is "very poor," questioning BLOOM's quality on languages included in training but with minimal data. This highlights that multilingual coverage does not guarantee multilingual competence.

---

### 4.3 Significant Advance: ROOTS Data Curation Philosophy

**What makes this different.** Section 3.1 argues that prior large-scale models relied on "heuristics-based filtering approaches that prioritize getting as much 'high-quality' data for as little cost as possible." This produced documented harms: blocklists suppressing LGBTQ+ and African American English text (Dodge et al., 2021), filtering that "implicitly prioritize US-centric views" (Johnson et al., 2022), and corpora where "provenance and authorship of individual items is usually lost."

ROOTS inverts this philosophy through three mechanisms:

1. **Human Expertise Over Automation**: Language-specific quality thresholds selected by fluent speakers rather than uniform automatic filters (Section 3.1.3).

2. **Traceability**: Individual sources kept separate until final preprocessing stages, enabling provenance tracking (Section 3.1.1).

3. **Composite Release**: 223 of 498 components released directly with licensing documentation, with researchers invited to join ongoing data analysis efforts (Section 3.1.1).

**Why this matters.** This approach addresses the "data work" undervaluation problem identified by Sambasivan et al. (2021), where dataset curation labor is invisible in final model publications. By documenting the curation process (Laurençon et al., 2022) and releasing tools for data visualization and access, BigScience makes data decisions auditable.

**Incremental vs. Significant Advance**. This is a **significant advance** rather than fundamental innovation because individual components (explicit permission-seeking, source documentation) existed in prior work (e.g., Gao et al., 2020's Pile). However, applying these practices systematically at the 1.61 terabyte scale across 46 languages represents a substantial improvement over standard practice.

**Limitation**. Section 3.1.1 acknowledges that "we were not able to fully establish an international organization" for long-term data governance within the project timeline. The composite release approach is a pragmatic compromise rather than a complete solution to data rights.

---

### 4.4 Significant Advance: Comprehensive Carbon Accountability

**What makes this different.** Section 3.5.1 provides a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) breaking emissions into three categories: equipment manufacturing (14%), training energy (30%), and idle consumption (55%). Prior carbon footprint studies typically reported only training energy (e.g., Patterson et al., 2021), which Section 3.5.1 argues "have likely under-estimated emissions" (citing Bannour et al., 2021).

Table 4 shows BLOOM emitted **25 tons CO₂eq** compared to GPT-3's **502 tons** and OPT's **70 tons**. While BLOOM's energy consumption (433 MWh) exceeds OPT's (324 MWh), emissions are two-thirds lower due to France's low-carbon grid (57 gCO₂eq/kWh vs. 231 gCO₂eq/kWh for OPT).

**Why this matters.** The breakdown reveals that **idle consumption (55%) exceeds training energy (30%)**, suggesting that optimizing cluster utilization and reducing experiment waste may have greater environmental impact than improving training efficiency alone. This insight has implications for how organizations should prioritize sustainability efforts.

**Incremental vs. Significant Advance**. This is a **significant advance** because LCA methodology existed prior to BLOOM (Klöppfer, 1997), but applying it comprehensively to LLM training with public documentation sets a new accountability standard. The finding about idle consumption is a novel empirical contribution.

**Limitation**. Section 3.5.1 notes that deployment emissions estimates (20 kg CO₂eq per day on GCP) are "not representative of all deployment use-cases" and will vary based on hardware, batching, and request volume. The paper does not provide a framework for users to estimate their own deployment footprint.

---

### 4.5 Significant Advance: Multilingual Instruction Tuning (BLOOMZ)

**What makes this different.** Section 3.1.4 and 4.7 describe BLOOMZ, produced by multitask finetuning BLOOM on xP3—a collection of prompts for 83 datasets covering 46 languages and 16 tasks. Prior instruction-tuned models were primarily English: T0 (Sanh et al., 2022) used P3 with English datasets, and FLAN (Wei et al., 2021) was English-only.

Figure 11 shows that BLOOMZ significantly improves zero-shot performance on multilingual tasks (XNLI, XWinograd, XCOPA, XStoryCloze) compared to pretrained BLOOM. T0, despite being instruction-tuned, "performs badly on the multilingual datasets shown due to it being a monolingual English model."

**Why this matters.** This demonstrates that **instruction tuning benefits transfer across languages** when the finetuning data is multilingual. Section 4.7 notes that "models finetuned on xP3 also outperform T0 on English datasets when controlling for size and architecture," suggesting multilingual instruction tuning does not sacrifice English capability.

**Incremental vs. Significant Advance**. This is a **significant advance** because the instruction tuning methodology (T0, FLAN) existed prior to BLOOMZ. The innovation is extending it to 46 languages with xP3, demonstrating that multilingual zero-shot task generalization is achievable at scale.

**Limitation**. Section 4.5 notes that BLOOMZ models "do not improve significantly over BLOOM models" on code generation (HumanEval), hypothesized because xP3 "not containing significant amounts of pure code completion." This suggests instruction tuning benefits are task-dependent.

---

### 4.6 Summary: What BLOOM Teaches About LLM Development

The innovations above collectively answer the question posed in Section 2.6: *scale for whom, developed by whom, and with what accountability?*

| Contribution | Type | Key Insight |
|--------------|------|-------------|
| Collaborative Model | Fundamental | 100B+ models can be developed through open international collaboration, not just corporate teams |
| Multilingual Open-Access | Fundamental | Open weights + 100B scale + multilinguality was previously unavailable |
| ROOTS Curation | Significant | Human expertise in data filtering reduces bias compared to uniform automatic thresholds |
| Carbon LCA | Significant | Idle consumption exceeds training energy—optimization priorities should reflect this |
| BLOOMZ | Significant | Multilingual instruction tuning improves zero-shot generalization without sacrificing English capability |

**What remains incremental.** BLOOM's core architecture (decoder-only Transformer), training objectives (autoregressive language modeling), and evaluation benchmarks (SuperGLUE, machine translation) follow established practice. The innovation lies not in these components individually but in their integration within a transparent, accountable development process.

**Implications for Future Work**. The paper concludes (Section 5) by hoping "that documenting our experience will help the machine learning research community organize new large-scale collaborative projects similar to BigScience." The key lesson is that technical capability and organizational accountability are not trade-offs—they can be mutually reinforcing when development processes are designed intentionally.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the BLOOM authors evaluated their model, what the results reveal about its capabilities, and whether the experiments convincingly support the paper's central claims. The evaluation strategy is distinctive: rather than reporting best-case performance achieved through extensive prompt engineering, the authors deliberately chose prompts "developed prior to BLOOM's release, and did not undergo any a priori refinement using models" (Section 4.1.1). This methodological choice prioritizes reproducibility and realistic user expectations over inflated benchmark scores.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Prompt Design Philosophy**. Section 4.1.1 explains that the evaluation prompts were created through crowdsourcing using PromptSource (Bach et al., 2022), with multiple peer reviews performed on each prompt for artifacts and consistency. Table 5 shows four example prompts for the WMT'14 machine translation task, ranging from verbose ("Given the following source text: [source sentence], a good L2 translation is:") to concise ("L1: [source sentence] = L2:"). The authors explicitly chose to report average performance across multiple prompts rather than cherry-picking the best performer, because "the latter is harder to reproduce systematically" and "is not representative of true zero-shot learning where no labeled data is available."

**Infrastructure**. The evaluation framework extends EleutherAI's Language Model Evaluation Harness (Gao et al., 2021) by integrating it with PromptSource. This integration was released as an open-source "Prompted Language Model Evaluation Harness" to enable reproducibility (Section 4.1.2).

**Baseline Models**. Section 4.1.4 lists eleven baseline models used for comparison, including:
- **mGPT** (1.3B parameters, 60 languages)
- **GPT-Neo family** (125M to 20B parameters)
- **T0** (11B parameters, instruction-tuned, English-only)
- **OPT** (175B parameters, open weights, primarily English)
- **XGLM** (multilingual GPT-style)
- **M2M-100** (615M parameters, 100 languages, supervised translation)
- **AlexaTM** (encoder-decoder, 32-shot evaluation)
- **Codex** (code-finetuned GPT variants)

This diverse baseline set enables comparison across multiple dimensions: parameter scale, multilinguality, and architectural differences.

**Evaluation Settings**. The paper focuses on **zero-shot** and **one-shot** settings rather than fine-tuning, because "prompt-based adaptation and few-shot 'in-context learning' are currently more common than finetuning" for models at this scale (Section 4). The only exception is Section 4.7, which specifically evaluates the BLOOMZ variants produced through multitask finetuning.

### 5.2 SuperGLUE: English Language Understanding

**Experimental Setup**. Section 4.2 evaluates BLOOM on a subset of SuperGLUE tasks: Ax-b, Ax-g, BoolQ, CB, WiC, WSC, and RTE. The remaining SuperGLUE tasks were excluded because they "require an order of magnitude more compute to run than all of these tasks we consider combined." For each task, five random prompts were selected from PromptSource, and models were evaluated on all five to capture prompt sensitivity.

**Zero-Shot vs. One-Shot Performance**. Figure 7 shows zero-shot and one-shot accuracy across models. Key observations:

- On entailment tasks (BoolQ and CB), "performance is well above random chance for BLOOM, T0, OPT, and GPT-J" in both settings.
- On other tasks, "the average performance across prompts hovers around chance, suggesting that the success of individual prompts is primarily statistical variation."
- T0 shows strong performance but is "not directly comparable" because it is instruction-tuned (similar to BLOOMZ).

**Scaling Comparison**. Figure 8 compares BLOOM and OPT families across parameter scales (350M to 175B parameters) in the 1-shot setting. The authors note:

> "Both OPT and BLOOM model families improve very slightly with scale, with only models over 2 billion parameters showing signal, and there is no consistent difference between families across all tasks."

In the 1-shot setting, **BLOOM-176B is ahead of OPT-175B on Ax-b, CB, WSC, and WiC**, and matches it on other tasks. This suggests "multilinguality does not limit performance of BLOOM on English-only tasks in the zero-shot setting."

**Critical Assessment**. The SuperGLUE results are **mixed**. While BLOOM matches OPT on most tasks, neither model achieves strong absolute performance—many tasks hover near chance level even at 175B+ parameters. This reveals a fundamental limitation: **scale alone does not guarantee reliable task performance without instruction tuning**. The authors acknowledge this implicitly by devoting Section 4.7 to BLOOMZ results, which show substantially improved zero-shot generalization after multitask finetuning.

### 5.3 Machine Translation: Multilingual Capability

**WMT14 Results (Table 6)**. Section 4.3.1 presents zero-shot and one-shot BLEU scores for English↔French and English↔Hindi translation using four different prompts. The results reveal significant prompt sensitivity:

| Prompt | en→fr (0-shot) | en→fr (1-shot) | fr→en (0-shot) | fr→en (1-shot) |
|--------|----------------|----------------|----------------|----------------|
| a_good_translation-source+target | 15.38 | **36.39** | 14.15 | **36.56** |
| gpt3-target | 7.90 | 32.55 | 12.73 | 33.14 |
| version-target | **21.96** | 34.22 | **26.79** | 35.42 |
| xglm-source+target | 14.91 | 27.83 | 15.52 | 34.51 |

The "version-target" prompt performs best in zero-shot, but "a_good_translation-source+target" achieves the highest 1-shot scores. The authors note: "The best prompts tend to be the more verbose ones."

**Comparison to Dedicated Models**. In the 1-shot setting, BLOOM achieves 34.2 BLEU (en→fr) and 35.4 BLEU (fr→en), compared to **M2M-100's 43.8 BLEU (en→fr) and 40.4 BLEU (fr→en)** with supervised training. The two major problems observed, particularly in zero-shot, are:
1. **Over-generation** (producing text beyond the translation)
2. **Not producing the correct language**

Both issues "are greatly improved as the number of few-shot examples is increased."

**Flores-101 Results (Table 8)**. Section 4.3.3 evaluates translation across 101 languages in four categories:

**Low-resource languages (Table 8a)**: Results are "comparable to or even slightly better than the supervised M2M model" for many pairs. However, translation between Swahili and Yoruba is "very poor"—these languages have "&lt;50k tokens each" in BLOOM's training data. This reveals a critical limitation: **presence in training data does not guarantee competence**.

**Romance languages (Table 8b)**: Results are "good across-the-board, including for translation from Galician (glg), a language not included in the training data, but which shares many similarities with the other Romance languages, in particular with Portuguese." This demonstrates **cross-lingual transfer** but also raises questions about whether under-represented languages without close relatives in the training corpus would perform similarly poorly as Swahili/Yoruba.

**High-resource pairs (Table 8c)**: BLOOM sometimes outperforms M2M-100. For example, Arabic→English achieves 40.3 BLEU (BLOOM) vs. 25.5 BLEU (M2M). However, AlexaTM (32-shot) achieves 41.8 BLEU on the same pair, suggesting BLOOM's 1-shot performance is competitive but not state-of-the-art.

**DiaBLa Contextual Translation (Table 7)**. Section 4.3.2 tests whether BLOOM can use dialogue context for translation. Using the "xglm-source+target" prompt with 1-shot examples from either random test sentences or previous dialogue utterances:

| Context | Truncate | en→fr BLEU | fr→en BLEU |
|---------|----------|------------|------------|
| Random | No | 5.7 | 12.1 |
| Random | Yes | **37.6** | **41.4** |
| Previous | No | 6.1 | 12.3 |
| Previous | Yes | **38.5** | **41.6** |

The "Truncate" column indicates whether a custom truncation function was applied to remove over-generated text. The automatic results are "inconclusive, with little difference between scores," but the authors note "there is evidence in the predictions themselves that the model is able to use the context of the 1-shot example to make translation choices."

**Critical Assessment**. The translation results **partially support** the claim of multilingual capability. BLOOM achieves competitive performance on high-resource and related-language pairs, demonstrating genuine cross-lingual transfer. However, the poor performance on under-represented languages (Swahili, Yoruba) reveals that **multilingual coverage in training data does not automatically translate to multilingual competence**. The heavy prompt sensitivity (BLEU scores varying by 10+ points across prompts) also suggests that users without prompt engineering expertise may experience significantly degraded performance.

### 5.4 Summarization: Multilingual Generation

**Experimental Setup**. Section 4.4 evaluates summarization on WikiLingua, a multilingual dataset of WikiHow articles with step-by-step summaries. Unlike PaLM, which only evaluated English summarization, BLOOM was tested on **abstractive summarization in the source language** (e.g., Vietnamese→Vietnamese) across nine languages.

**Results (Figure 9)**. The figure shows per-prompt ROUGE-2 F-measure scores for BLOOM variants (560M to 176B) and OPT-175B. Key findings:

- "BLOOM attains higher performance on multilingual summarization than OPT"
- "Performance increases as the parameter count of the model increases"
- The authors "suspect this is due to BLOOM's multilingual-focused training"

However, the authors also note a critical caveat: "we qualitatively observe that in many cases, the ROUGE-2 score understates the quality of the summaries generated by the systems." This acknowledges the well-known limitation of ROUGE metrics for evaluating abstractive generation.

**Critical Assessment**. The summarization results **support the multilingual advantage claim** but are limited by metric reliability. The consistent scaling trend (larger models perform better) aligns with established scaling laws, but the absolute scores are not reported numerically in the paper, making it difficult to assess practical utility.

### 5.5 Code Generation: Programming Language Capability

**Experimental Setup**. Section 4.5 evaluates code generation on HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021), reporting pass@k metrics for k=1, 10, and 100. The ROOTS corpus contains "around 11% of code," comparable to The Pile's "around 13% of code (GitHub + StackExchange)."

**Results (Table 9)**. Key comparisons:

| Model | pass@1 | pass@10 | pass@100 |
|-------|--------|---------|----------|
| GPT-J 6B | 11.62% | 15.74% | 27.74% |
| GPT-NeoX 20B | 15.4% | 25.6% | 41.2% |
| Codex-12B | **28.81%** | **46.81%** | **72.31%** |
| BLOOM-176B | 15.52% | 32.20% | 55.45% |
| BLOOMZ-176B | 12.06% | 26.53% | 48.44% |

BLOOM-176B performs "similar to that of the similar-sized GPT models trained on the Pile" but is "significantly" weaker than Codex models, which "have solely been finetuned on code." Notably, **BLOOMZ does not improve over BLOOM** on code generation—the authors hypothesize this is because xP3 "not containing significant amounts of pure code completion."

**Critical Assessment**. The code generation results reveal an important **trade-off**: BLOOM's general-purpose multilingual training produces competent code generation without specialized fine-tuning, but dedicated code models (Codex) substantially outperform it. The lack of improvement from BLOOMZ suggests that **instruction tuning benefits are task-dependent**—multilingual prompts in xP3 helped general task generalization but not code completion specifically.

### 5.6 HELM Benchmark: Holistic Comparison

**Experimental Setup**. Section 4.6 reproduces evaluations from the HELM benchmark (Liang et al., 2022), which ran 5-shot evaluations on English-only tasks across multiple dimensions: accuracy, calibration, robustness, fairness, bias, and toxicity.

**Results (Figure 10)**. The figure shows BLOOM's ranking across six dimensions relative to 25+ other models:

- **Accuracy**: "Roughly on par with previous-generation English-only models, such as GPT3-davinci v1 and J1-Grande v1, but behind more recent monolingual models such as InstructGPT davinci v2, Turing NLG v2, Anthropic-LM v4-s3, or OPT."
- **Calibration**: "Like other large language models of this size, it is not very well calibrated."
- **Robustness**: "Quite robust."
- **Fairness**: "One of the best models for fairness."
- **Toxicity**: "Slightly more toxic than average in English."
- **Bias**: "Average for bias."

**Critical Assessment**. The HELM results **temper claims of state-of-the-art performance**. BLOOM is competitive with earlier-generation models but behind more recent systems on accuracy. The fairness result is notable but should be interpreted cautiously given the limitations of CrowS-Pairs discussed in Section 4.10.

### 5.7 Multitask Finetuning: BLOOMZ Zero-Shot Generalization

**Experimental Setup**. Section 4.7 evaluates BLOOMZ models finetuned on xP3 (83 datasets, 46 languages, 16 tasks) against pretrained BLOOM, XGLM, T0, and mTk-Instruct. Tasks include XNLI (natural language inference), XWinograd (coreference resolution), XCOPA (sentence completion), and XStoryCloze (story completion).

**Results (Figure 11)**. The figure shows dramatic improvements from multitask finetuning:

- **Pretrained BLOOM**: Performance "near the random baselines of 33% for NLI (XNLI) and 50% for coreference resolution (XWinograd) and sentence completion (XCOPA and XStoryCloze)."
- **BLOOMZ**: "Zero-shot performance significantly improves on the depicted held-out tasks."
- **T0**: "Performs badly on the multilingual datasets shown due to it being a monolingual English model."

The authors note that "models finetuned on xP3 also outperform T0 on English datasets when controlling for size and architecture," likely because "T0's finetuning dataset (P3) containing less diverse datasets and prompts than xP3."

**Critical Assessment**. The BLOOMZ results **strongly support the value of multilingual instruction tuning**. The improvement from near-random to competent performance demonstrates that **pretraining alone is insufficient for reliable zero-shot task generalization**—instruction tuning is essential. This finding has important implications: users seeking reliable task performance should use BLOOMZ rather than pretrained BLOOM.

### 5.8 Embeddings: SGPT-BLOOM Models

**Experimental Setup**. Section 4.8 evaluates contrastively finetuned BLOOM models (SGPT-BLOOM-7.1B-msmarco and SGPT-BLOOM-1.7B-nli) on the Massive Text Embedding Benchmark (MTEB). Tasks include embedding classification (MASSIVE dataset) and semantic textual similarity (STS22).

**Results (Table 10)**. For embedding classification on MASSIVE:

| Language | ST5-XL | MiniLM-L12 | SGPT-BLOOM-1.7B | SGPT-BLOOM-7.1B |
|----------|--------|------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Arabic | 4.18 | 51.43 | 54.59 | **59.25** |
| English | 72.09 | 69.32 | 66.69 | **69.67** |
| French | 60.99 | 64.82 | 64.58 | **66.95** |
| Hindi | 3.02 | 62.77 | 60.74 | **63.54** |
| Swahili | 30.60 | 31.95 | 43.90 | **49.81** |

SGPT-BLOOM-7.1B "provides state-of-the-art performance on several classification and semantic textual similarity splits." However, it is "an order of magnitude larger than models like the displayed multilingual MiniLM and MPNet."

**Critical Assessment**. The embedding results demonstrate that **BLOOM can be effectively adapted for representation tasks**, but the parameter efficiency is poor compared to dedicated embedding models. This is a **proof of capability** rather than a recommendation for production use.

### 5.9 Multilingual Probing: Linguistic Feature Encoding

**Experimental Setup**. Section 4.9 uses the "Universal Probing" framework (Serikov et al., 2022) to analyze whether BLOOM encodes morphosyntactic features across 17 languages and 38 features. The procedure trains logistic regression classifiers on layer representations to predict features like Number, Person, Mood, Case, etc.

**Results (Table 12)**. Probing performance (F1 score averaged across layers and tasks):

| Language | BLOOM-1.7B | BLOOM-176B | Random | TF-IDF (Word) |
|----------|------------|------------|--------|---------------|
| Arabic | 0.66 | 0.64 | 0.49 | 0.40 |
| English | 0.57 | 0.57 | 0.43 | 0.46 |
| French | 0.61 | 0.57 | 0.44 | 0.32 |
| Hindi | 0.63 | 0.60 | 0.48 | 0.55 |
| Yoruba | 0.48 | 0.36 | 0.43 | 0.09 |

Surprisingly, **BLOOM-1.7B performs on par or better than BLOOM-176B** on average. Table 13 shows statistical tests confirming this difference is significant (Mann-Whitney U, p < 0.01).

**Results by Feature (Figure 12)**. The models infer "Mood and Person well with no regard for language" but "generally show worse qualities in the other categories." The authors explain: "Mood and Person share similar values across the presented languages, while the set of Case values is highly dependent on the language."

**Correlation Analysis (Table 13)**. BLOOM-1.7B performance is "highly correlated with language family, probing dataset size, and pretraining dataset size." In contrast, "BLOOM shows more stable performance on different languages in spite of the amount of data it has seen during pretraining. This might indicate the better generalization abilities of the model with more parameters."

**Critical Assessment**. The probing results reveal a **counterintuitive finding**: smaller models sometimes encode linguistic features more explicitly than larger models. The authors offer two interpretations:
1. BLOOM-1.7B may have "worse grammatical generalization" but more explicit feature encoding.
2. BLOOM-176B may have "better generalization abilities" but distributes linguistic information more diffusely across layers.

This finding **complicates the assumption that larger models are uniformly better** for all purposes. For tasks requiring explicit linguistic feature access, smaller models may be preferable.

### 5.10 Bias Evaluation: CrowS-Pairs

**Experimental Setup**. Section 4.10 evaluates bias using the multilingual CrowS-Pairs dataset, which presents minimal pairs differing only in the social category targeted by a stereotype (e.g., "Women can't drive" vs. "Men can't drive"). The evaluation measures whether models systematically prefer stereotyped statements.

**Results (Figure 13)**. Overall prompt accuracy for BLOOM is "close to .50, which suggests an overall absence of bias." English and French scores are "very close, suggesting similar overall behavior of the model on both languages."

**Results by Category (Table 14)**. Accuracy per bias type (English/French):

| Bias Type | Support | English | French |
|-----------|---------|---------|--------|
| Ethnicity/Color | 460 | 50.05 | 50.48* |
| Gender | 321 | 51.17* | 51.24* |
| Socioeconomic Status | 196 | 51.05* | 52.22* |
| Nationality | 253 | 49.25* | 48.49* |
| Religion | 115 | 53.82* | 53.01* |
| All | 1,677 | 49.78* | 50.61* |

Asterisks indicate significance (T-test, p < .05). While scores are near 50%, "accuracy significantly differs from 50 overall for both languages, as well as for a number of bias categories."

**Limitations**. The authors acknowledge several limitations:
1. CrowS-Pairs was "originally intended for masked language models," requiring adaptation for autoregressive models.
2. "Very little material (corpora, measures) is available for multilingual bias assessment."
3. Evaluation is "limited to the situations, languages and language variants that are covered by multilingual CrowS-Pairs."
4. The findings may not reflect "wider model use" in diverse contexts.

**Critical Assessment**. The bias results are **inconclusive**. Scores near 50% could indicate genuine absence of bias OR could reflect limitations in the evaluation methodology. The authors appropriately caution that "although our examinations suggest a limited presence of bias in the model, they cannot cover the breadth of possible usage scenarios." This is an honest acknowledgment of evaluation limitations rather than overclaiming.

### 5.11 Overall Assessment of Experimental Claims

**Claims Strongly Supported**:
1. **Multilingual instruction tuning improves zero-shot generalization** (Section 4.7): BLOOMZ shows dramatic improvements over pretrained BLOOM on multilingual tasks.
2. **BLOOM matches OPT on English tasks despite multilingual training** (Section 4.2): 1-shot SuperGLUE results show BLOOM-176B ahead of or matching OPT-175B on most tasks.
3. **Cross-lingual transfer occurs for related languages** (Section 4.3.3): Good performance on Galician (not in training) and Romance language pairs demonstrates transfer.

**Claims Partially Supported**:
1. **Competitive multilingual capability**: Strong on high-resource and related languages, but "very poor" on under-represented languages like Swahili and Yoruba despite their inclusion in training.
2. **Reduced bias**: CrowS-Pairs scores near 50% but with statistically significant deviations, and evaluation methodology has acknowledged limitations.

**Claims Requiring Nuance**:
1. **Scale improves performance**: True for summarization (Figure 9) and SuperGLUE (Figure 8), but probing results (Table 12) show BLOOM-1.7B sometimes outperforming BLOOM-176B on linguistic feature encoding.
2. **Open access enables research**: Demonstrated through the release itself, but the paper does not provide evidence of downstream research enabled by BLOOM (understandably, as this would require time post-release).

**Key Methodological Strength**: The commitment to reporting average performance across multiple prompts rather than best-case scores provides a more realistic picture of what users should expect. This contrasts with practices where "prompt engineering is iterated until best-case performance is achieved," which is "harder to reproduce systematically."

**Key Methodological Limitation**: The evaluation focuses heavily on zero-shot and one-shot settings, which may not reflect how practitioners actually use large models (often with more examples or fine-tuning). The BLOOMZ results partially address this, but the pretrained BLOOM evaluation may understate the model's potential with more extensive adaptation.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the assumptions, constraints, and unresolved weaknesses in BLOOM's design and evaluation. While the paper demonstrates impressive technical achievement, several limitations reveal important trade-offs that users and researchers should understand before adopting the model.

### 6.1 Assumptions About Data Coverage and Linguistic Competence

**The Coverage-Competence Gap**. A critical assumption underlying BLOOM's multilingual claims is that **including a language in the training corpus guarantees reasonable performance in that language**. Section 4.3.3 directly contradicts this assumption:

> "The translation quality for many of the low-resource languages is good, comparable to or even slightly better than the supervised M2M model. However, results are very poor between Swahili and Yoruba, languages that are present but under-represented in BLOOM's training data (&lt;50k tokens each)."

This reveals a fundamental limitation: **token count thresholds for competence remain undefined**. The paper documents that Swahili and Yoruba are included in ROOTS (Table 1 shows Swahili at 236,482,543 bytes and Yoruba at 89,695,835 bytes), yet performance is "very poor." This creates uncertainty for users working with other low-resource languages in the corpus—there is no clear minimum data threshold that guarantees usable performance.

**Cross-Lingual Transfer Relies on Language Relatedness**. Section 4.3.3 notes good performance on Galician, "a language not included in the training data, but which shares many similarities with the other Romance languages, in particular with Portuguese." This demonstrates transfer capability but also reveals a constraint: **languages without close relatives in the training corpus may not benefit from transfer**. The paper does not evaluate languages that are both low-resource AND linguistically isolated (e.g., language isolates like Basque beyond what appears in probing experiments), leaving uncertainty about performance on truly under-represented language families.

**Tokenizer Fertility Trade-offs**. Section 3.3 set a goal of not degrading tokenizer fertility by more than 10 percentage points compared to monolingual tokenizers. Table 2 shows this target was exceeded for some languages:

| Language | BLOOM Fertility | Monolingual Fertility | Degradation |
|----------|-----------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Arabic | 1.34 | 1.16 | +13% |
| Hindi | 1.18 | 1.07 | +9% |
| French | 1.17 | 1.30 | -11% (improvement) |

Arabic exceeds the 10% threshold, suggesting **tokenization efficiency trade-offs were made for some languages**. Higher fertility means more tokens per word, which increases inference costs and may reduce effective context length for those languages. The paper does not quantify the downstream performance impact of this tokenizer inefficiency.

### 6.2 Prompt Sensitivity and Usability Constraints

**Heavy Dependence on Prompt Engineering**. Section 4.3.1 reveals significant performance variation across prompts for the same task. For English→French translation in the 1-shot setting:

- `a_good_translation-source+target`: 36.39 BLEU
- `xglm-source+target`: 27.83 BLEU

This **8.56 BLEU point difference** (approximately 30% relative variation) demonstrates that users without prompt engineering expertise may experience substantially degraded performance. The paper's methodological choice to report average performance across prompts (Section 4.1.1) is commendable for transparency, but it also reveals that **BLOOM is not robust to prompt variation** in the way that dedicated task-specific models might be.

**Zero-Shot Limitations Without Instruction Tuning**. Section 4.7 shows that pretrained BLOOM performs "near the random baselines of 33% for NLI (XNLI) and 50% for coreference resolution (XWinograd)" on multilingual tasks. This reveals that **the base pretrained model is not immediately usable for many tasks**—users must either:
1. Use BLOOMZ (instruction-tuned variant)
2. Invest in their own fine-tuning
3. Accept near-random performance on many tasks

This is not unique to BLOOM (the paper notes similar patterns in other LLMs), but it is an important practical constraint that the open-access release does not fully resolve. Users downloading BLOOM expecting immediate task capability will be disappointed without additional fine-tuning.

**Over-Generation in Translation**. Section 4.3.1 identifies two major problems in zero-shot translation: "(i) over-generation and (ii) not producing the correct language." The paper notes these "are greatly improved as the number of few-shot examples is increased," but this requires users to provide examples—which may not be available for low-resource languages. The need for truncation functions (Section 4.3.2, Table 7) to remove over-generated text adds **engineering overhead for production use** that is not reflected in the raw benchmark scores.

### 6.3 Computational and Infrastructure Constraints

**Training Infrastructure Not Reproducible**. Section 3.4.1 specifies that BLOOM required:
- 48 nodes with 8 NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs each (384 GPUs total)
- 3.5 months of training time
- 1,082,990 compute hours
- Access to Jean Zay supercomputer (French government-funded)

While the model weights are open-access, **the training infrastructure is not reproducible for most research groups**. The paper acknowledges this tension in Section 2.1, noting that "the costs of training large language models (LLMs) are only affordable for well-resourced organizations." BLOOM's release mitigates this for inference and fine-tuning, but researchers cannot replicate or modify the pretraining process. This limits architectural experimentation and ablation studies at the 176B scale.

**Inference Costs for Full Model**. The 176B parameter size requires substantial GPU memory for inference. While the paper releases smaller variants (560M to 7.1B parameters, Table 3), the full BLOOM model requires:
- Approximately 352 GB of GPU memory in float16 (176B × 2 bytes)
- Multiple GPUs even for inference without training

The paper does not provide quantized versions or detailed inference optimization guidance, which **limits accessibility for researchers without multi-GPU setups**. This partially undermines the "open-access" claim for the largest model variant.

**3D Parallelism Complexity**. Section 3.4.2 describes the 3D parallelism approach (data, tensor, and pipeline parallelism) combined with ZeRO optimizer. While this enabled training, it also introduces **significant engineering complexity for anyone attempting to fine-tune or modify the model**. The Megatron-DeepSpeed framework requires specialized expertise that may not be available in all research groups, creating a barrier to full utilization of the released model.

### 6.4 Architectural and Methodological Trade-offs

**Decoder-Only vs. Encoder-Decoder Limitations**. Section 3.2.2 justifies the decoder-only choice based on zero-shot generalization performance. However, this comes with trade-offs:

- **Bidirectional context is unavailable** for tasks that benefit from full sequence understanding (e.g., certain classification tasks)
- **Efficiency for encoder-side tasks**: Encoder-decoder models like mT5 can be more efficient for tasks where the full input is available before generation begins

The paper acknowledges that encoder-decoder models "significantly outperform decoder-only models for transfer learning" (citing Raffel et al., 2020), but chose decoder-only for zero-shot performance. This is a valid trade-off, but users should understand that **BLOOM may not be optimal for all task types**.

**Excluded Architecture Families**. Section 3.2.1 explicitly notes architectures that were not considered:

> "We did not consider mixture-of-experts (MoE) (Shazeer et al., 2017), due to a lack of widely used GPU-based codebases suitable for training them at scale. Similarly, we also did not consider state-space models (Gu et al., 2020)."

The paper acknowledges these approaches have "now demonstrated competitive results" since BLOOM's design. This reveals a **timing constraint**: BLOOM's architecture reflects the state-of-the-art at the time of design (2021-2022), not necessarily the current state-of-the-art. Users should be aware that newer architectures (MoE, state-space models) may offer better parameter efficiency or performance.

**Embedding LayerNorm Trade-off**. Section 3.2.3 notes that the embedding LayerNorm "significantly improved training stability" but "penalizes zero-shot generalization." The team chose stability over performance:

> "Even though we also found it penalizes zero-shot generalization in Le Scao et al. (2022), we train BLOOM with an additional layer normalization after the first embedding layer to avoid training instabilities."

This is a pragmatic engineering decision, but it means **BLOOM's zero-shot performance is slightly degraded compared to what might have been achievable** without this stability measure. The paper notes this may have been unnecessary with bfloat16 (used in final training) versus float16 (used in preliminary experiments), suggesting potential inefficiency in the final design.

### 6.5 Evaluation Gaps and Unaddressed Scenarios

**Limited Bias Evaluation Scope**. Section 4.10 acknowledges significant limitations in bias assessment:

> "Very little material (corpora, measures) is available for multilingual bias assessment."
> "Our evaluation of biases in the model are further limited to the situations, languages and language variants that are covered by multilingual CrowS-Pairs."

The evaluation covers only English and French for bias (Table 14), despite BLOOM supporting 46 natural languages. This creates a **critical gap**: bias may manifest differently across languages and cultures, but the evaluation cannot detect this. The paper appropriately cautions that findings "cannot cover the breadth of possible usage scenarios," but users should understand that **the bias evaluation is incomplete**.

**Deployment Carbon Footprint Uncertainty**. Section 3.5.1 provides training carbon emissions (25 tons CO₂eq) but notes deployment estimates are limited:

> "This figure is not representative of all deployment use-cases, and will vary depending on the hardware used as well as the specifics of model implementation (e.g. whether batching is used) and the number of requests the model receives."

The paper does not provide a framework for users to estimate their own deployment footprint, which **limits accountability for downstream emissions**. Given that deployment emissions can accumulate significantly over time (0.83 kg CO₂eq per hour on the tested configuration), this is a notable gap in the environmental accounting.

**Probing Results Complicate Scale Assumptions**. Section 4.9 presents a counterintuitive finding:

> "BLOOM-1.7B performs on par or better than BLOOM on average."
> "According to the results of Mann-Whitney U test, BLOOM-1.7B shows significantly better results (p < 0.01) than BLOOM."

This challenges the assumption that larger models are uniformly better. The authors suggest BLOOM-176B may have "better generalization abilities" but distributes information more diffusely. However, this remains an **open question**: for tasks requiring explicit linguistic feature access, smaller models may be preferable. The paper does not provide guidance on when to use which model size beyond raw performance benchmarks.

**Real-World Application Testing**. The evaluation focuses on benchmark datasets (SuperGLUE, WMT, WikiLingua, etc.) but does not include **field testing in production environments**. Section 5 mentions application working groups (Section 6 lists "Applications" contributors), but the paper does not report results from real-world deployments. This limits understanding of how BLOOM performs outside controlled benchmark settings, where data distributions, user behaviors, and failure modes may differ significantly.

### 6.6 Data Governance and Long-Term Sustainability

**Incomplete Data Governance Structure**. Section 3.1.1 acknowledges:

> "While we were not able to fully establish an international organization in the comparatively short time between the project start and model training, we worked on integrating lessons from this effort..."

Only 223 of 498 ROOTS components can be directly distributed (Section 3.1.1), with the remainder requiring researchers to "join ongoing efforts to analyze the data through a sign-up form." This creates **asymmetric access**: researchers who can commit time to data analysis requests gain more insight than those who simply download the model. While understandable given licensing constraints, this partially limits the reproducibility of the training process.

**Long-Term Maintenance Uncertainty**. The paper documents the collaborative development process extensively but does not address **long-term model maintenance**. Questions remain unanswered:
- Who maintains the model infrastructure over time?
- How are bugs or safety issues addressed post-release?
- What is the process for releasing updated versions?

The Responsible AI License (Section 3.6) includes behavioral-use restrictions, but enforcement mechanisms are not detailed. This creates uncertainty about the long-term governance of the released model.

**Intermediate Training Runs Carbon Cost**. Section 3.5.1 notes that "the final BLOOM training represents approximately 37% of the overall emissions, with other processes such as intermediate training runs and model evaluation adding up to the other 63%." This reveals that **ablation and experimentation consumed more carbon than the final model training**. While this is standard in ML research, it highlights an inefficiency: the collaborative process, while valuable, incurred significant environmental cost beyond the final artifact. Future collaborative efforts may need to optimize this ratio.

### 6.7 Summary of Key Trade-offs

| Trade-off | Decision Made | Consequence |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| **Coverage vs. Competence** | Include 46 languages with varying data amounts | Some languages (&lt;50k tokens) show "very poor" performance |
| **Stability vs. Performance** | Add embedding LayerNorm for training stability | Slight penalty to zero-shot generalization |
| **Transparency vs. Completeness** | Report average prompt performance | Reveals high prompt sensitivity (8+ BLEU variation) |
| **Open Access vs. Infrastructure** | Release weights but not training infrastructure | Users cannot replicate pretraining, only fine-tune |
| **Evaluation Rigor vs. Scope** | Evaluate bias in 2 of 46 languages | Bias assessment incomplete for most supported languages |
| **Architecture Choice** | Decoder-only over encoder-decoder | Better zero-shot, worse for some transfer learning tasks |

**Open Questions for Future Work**. The paper concludes (Section 5) by hoping to enable "new large-scale collaborative projects similar to BigScience," but several questions remain:

1. **What minimum token count per language guarantees usable performance?** The Swahili/Yoruba case suggests current thresholds are insufficient, but no alternative is proposed.

2. **How can bias evaluation be expanded to all 46 languages?** The paper acknowledges the gap but does not propose a roadmap for multilingual bias assessment tools.

3. **Can the collaborative model be sustained long-term?** BigScience was a time-limited workshop; the paper does not address how ongoing governance would work.

4. **What is the optimal model size for specific tasks?** Probing results suggest 1.7B may outperform 176B for some linguistic tasks, but guidance is limited.

These limitations do not diminish BLOOM's achievement but rather clarify the boundaries within which the model should be used and the areas where future work is needed.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how BLOOM reshapes the large language model landscape, what research pathways it opens, where it can be practically deployed, and when practitioners should choose BLOOM over alternatives. The implications extend beyond technical capabilities to organizational models, accountability standards, and access patterns.

### 7.1 Landscape Impact: Three Structural Shifts

BLOOM introduces changes that extend beyond benchmark scores to how the field organizes, accounts for, and distributes large-scale ML research.

**Shift 1: Collaborative Development as a Viable Alternative to Corporate-Only Models**. Section 2.2 documents that **1,200+ participants from 38 countries** organized into **30 working groups** (Figure 1) successfully produced a 176B-parameter model. Before BigScience, "EleutherAI was the only non-corporate entity outside of China that was developing large language models" (Section 2.1). BLOOM demonstrates that open international collaboration can compete with corporate development at the 100B+ parameter scale.

> "Besides enabling results that are impossible for any individual research group to achieve, this form of organization will also allow more people with different backgrounds to share their ideas and participate in the development of major advances in the field." (Section 5)

This shift has already influenced subsequent efforts (e.g., BigCode for code models), suggesting the BigScience organizational model is **reproducible rather than unique**.

**Shift 2: Carbon Accountability Becomes Expected, Not Optional**. Section 3.5.1 establishes a new standard by providing a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) breaking emissions into equipment manufacturing (14%), training energy (30%), and idle consumption (55%). Prior studies typically reported only training energy, which Section 3.5.1 argues "have likely under-estimated emissions" (citing Bannour et al., 2021).

The finding that **idle consumption exceeds training energy** (45 tons vs. 25 tons CO₂eq) has practical implications: optimizing cluster utilization and reducing experiment waste may have greater environmental impact than improving training efficiency alone. Future model releases will face pressure to provide comparable breakdowns.

**Shift 3: Open Access at Scale Becomes Technically Feasible**. Table 4 shows BLOOM achieved comparable scale to GPT-3 (175B vs. 176B parameters) with **two-thirds lower emissions** (25 tons vs. 502 tons CO₂eq) due to France's low-carbon grid (57 gCO₂eq/kWh vs. estimated higher rates for GPT-3). This demonstrates that open-access models need not be environmentally irresponsible—**location and infrastructure choices matter as much as model architecture**.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions and implicitly enables others through its release strategy.

**Explicitly Identified Research Gaps (Section 4.9 Discussion)**:

1. **Generalizing Abilities**: The probing results show BLOOM-1.7B outperforming BLOOM-176B on morphosyntactic feature classification (Table 12, p < 0.01). The paper asks: *"Does the larger model have better generalization abilities but distribute information more diffusely?"* This suggests research on **when smaller models outperform larger ones for specific linguistic tasks**.

2. **Multilingual Abilities Beyond Training Corpus**: Section 4.9 notes: *"A separate research interest implies considering languages that are not explicitly included in the pretraining corpus."* BLOOM enables **zero-shot transfer studies** to languages outside the 46 supported languages, building on the Galician result (Section 4.3.3) where good performance was achieved despite Galician not being in training data.

3. **Under-Resourced Language Evaluation**: The paper identifies Swahili and Yoruba as having "very poor" performance despite being in training data (&lt;50k tokens each, Section 4.3.3). Future work should establish **minimum token thresholds for competence** across language families.

4. **Layer-wise and Training Dynamics Analysis**: Section 4.9 notes: *"Analyzing different layers may reveal how morpho-syntactic representations are built during processing."* The release of intermediate checkpoints (implied by Section 3.4.5's mention of checkpoints saved every three hours) enables **chronological probing studies** to understand how linguistic properties emerge during training.

**Implicitly Enabled Research Through Open Release**:

- **Architecture Ablation at Scale**: Researchers can now study emergent behaviors, scaling laws, and failure modes at 176B parameters—previously restricted to organizations with proprietary models (Section 4.2).

- **Bias Assessment Tool Development**: Section 4.10 acknowledges "very little material (corpora, measures) is available for multilingual bias assessment." BLOOM's release creates demand and testbeds for **new multilingual bias evaluation frameworks** covering all 46 supported languages.

- **Fine-tuning Method Comparison**: The release of six size variants (560M to 176B, Table 3) enables systematic study of **parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods** (LoRA, adapters, etc.) across scales with consistent pretraining.

- **Deployment Optimization Research**: Section 3.5.1 notes deployment emissions estimates are "not representative of all deployment use-cases." Researchers can now study **inference optimization, quantization, and batching strategies** specifically for BLOOM's architecture.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The paper documents several application areas through working groups (Section 6 lists "Applications" contributors) and evaluation results.

**Documented Application Areas**:

| Application Domain | Evidence from Paper | Suitability |
|-------------------|---------------------|-------------|
| **Multilingual Information Retrieval** | SGPT-BLOOM-7.1B-msmarco achieves state-of-the-art on MTEB classification and STS splits (Section 4.8, Table 10) | **High** – Dedicated embedding variant available |
| **Cross-lingual Translation** | Competitive 1-shot BLEU scores on high-resource pairs (Section 4.3.3, Table 8c) | **Medium** – Requires few-shot examples; dedicated MT models (M2M) still stronger |
| **Multilingual Summarization** | BLOOM outperforms OPT on WikiLingua across 9 languages (Section 4.4, Figure 9) | **Medium-High** – Good for source-language summarization |
| **Code Generation** | BLOOM-176B achieves 15.52% pass@1 on HumanEval (Section 4.5, Table 9) | **Low-Medium** – Codex significantly stronger; BLOOMZ does not improve code performance |
| **Semantic Textual Similarity** | SGPT-BLOOM-1.7B-nli trained for multilingual STS (Section 3.5, 4.8) | **High** – Dedicated variant available |
| **Biomedical NLP** | Applications working group included biomedical text focus (Section 2.2, Fries et al., 2022b) | **Medium** – Domain-specific fine-tuning required |
| **Historical Text Analysis** | Working group focused on historical texts (Section 2.2, De Toni et al., 2022) | **Medium** – Zero-shot performance on historical variants untested |

**Recommended Use Cases Based on Evaluation Results**:

1. **Multilingual Zero-Shot Task Generalization**: Use **BLOOMZ** (instruction-tuned variant) rather than pretrained BLOOM. Section 4.7 shows pretrained BLOOM performs "near the random baselines" on multilingual tasks, while BLOOMZ "significantly improves" performance (Figure 11).

2. **Embedding Generation**: Use **SGPT-BLOOM-7.1B-msmarco** for retrieval tasks or **SGPT-BLOOM-1.7B-nli** for semantic similarity (Section 4.8). These are contrastively finetuned variants optimized for representation tasks.

3. **Low-Resource Language Research**: Use BLOOM for languages with **close relatives in the training corpus** (e.g., Galician benefits from Portuguese similarity, Section 4.3.3). Avoid relying on BLOOM for isolated low-resource languages without validation.

4. **English-Only Production**: Consider **OPT-175B** or newer instruction-tuned monolingual models instead. Section 4.6 (HELM benchmark) shows BLOOM is "behind more recent monolingual models such as InstructGPT davinci v2" on accuracy.

### 7.4 Integration and Reproducibility Guidance

This subsection provides practical decision criteria for when to use BLOOM versus alternatives, along with infrastructure requirements.

**When to Prefer BLOOM**:

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |

| **Multilingual research requiring open weights** | ✅ Use BLOOM | Only open 100B+ multilingual model (Section 4.2) |
| **Low-resource language with Romance family connection** | ✅ Use BLOOM | Cross-lingual transfer demonstrated (Section 4.3.3) |
| **Embedding generation for retrieval** | ✅ Use SGPT-BLOOM-7.1B-msmarco | State-of-the-art on MTEB (Section 4.8) |

**Infrastructure Requirements for Integration**:

- **Full BLOOM-176B Inference**: Requires approximately **352 GB GPU memory in float16** (176B × 2 bytes). This necessitates multi-GPU setups even for inference without training.

- **Smaller Variants Available**: Section 3.5 documents six size variants (560M to 176B, Table 3). The **BLOOM-7.1B** variant (14 GB in float16) is more accessible for single-GPU research.

- **Framework Dependencies**: Training used Megatron-DeepSpeed with 3D parallelism (Section 3.4.2). Fine-tuning requires familiarity with this framework or adaptation to alternatives (e.g., Hugging Face Transformers with DeepSpeed integration).

- **License Compliance**: The Responsible AI License includes **13 behavioral-use restrictions** (Section 3.6). Users must verify their intended use complies with these restrictions before deployment.

**Reproducibility Considerations**:

- **Training Cannot Be Replicated**: Section 3.4.1 specifies 384 NVIDIA A100 GPUs for 3.5 months. Most researchers cannot replicate pretraining—only fine-tuning is feasible.

- **Data Access Is Partial**: Only **223 of 498 ROOTS components** can be directly distributed (Section 3.1.1). The remainder requires researchers to "join ongoing efforts to analyze the data through a sign-up form."

- **Prompt Sensitivity Requires Mitigation**: Section 4.3.1 shows **8.56 BLEU point variation** across prompts for the same task. Production systems should implement prompt ensembling or validation rather than relying on single prompts.

- **BLOOMZ Preferred for Tasks**: Section 4.7 demonstrates pretrained BLOOM performs near random on many zero-shot tasks. Users should default to **BLOOMZ variants** unless specifically studying pretrained representations.

### 7.5 Long-Term Implications for the Field

**Organizational Precedent**. Section 5 states: *"We hope that documenting our experience will help the machine learning research community organize new large-scale collaborative projects similar to BigScience."* The key lesson is that **technical capability and organizational accountability are not trade-offs**—they can be mutually reinforcing when development processes are designed intentionally.

**Accountability Standards**. The comprehensive documentation (Model Card, carbon LCA, data governance, licensing) sets a **new baseline for transparency**. Future large model releases will face pressure to provide comparable documentation across all these dimensions.

**Access Democratization**. By releasing weights under a Responsible AI License (Section 3.6), BLOOM enables research that was previously impossible for non-corporate entities. However, Section 6.3 notes that **inference infrastructure remains a barrier**—the 176B model requires multi-GPU setups that many researchers cannot access. Future work should address **quantization and distillation** to make the model more accessible.

**Multilingual Equity**. The inclusion of 46 natural languages (Table 1) demonstrates that multilingual training at scale is feasible. However, Section 4.3.3's finding that Swahili and Yoruba perform "very poor" despite being in training data reveals that **inclusion does not guarantee competence**. Future multilingual models must establish **minimum data thresholds per language** and develop better techniques for low-resource language transfer.

**Environmental Accountability**. The finding that idle consumption (55%) exceeds training energy (30%) in carbon emissions (Section 3.5.1) suggests that **cluster utilization optimization** may have greater environmental impact than training efficiency improvements. This should influence how organizations plan and report on ML infrastructure usage.

---

**Summary**: BLOOM changes the landscape by proving that open, collaborative development can produce competitive 100B+ parameter models while maintaining accountability across data governance, carbon emissions, and licensing. It enables research on multilingual generalization, scaling laws, and emergent behaviors that was previously restricted to corporate labs. Practitioners should prefer BLOOMZ for task generalization, SGPT-BLOOM variants for embeddings, and smaller model sizes for resource-constrained settings. However, users must validate performance on their specific languages and tasks, as inclusion in training data does not guarantee competence—particularly for under-represented languages.
