## 1. Executive Summary

MiniCPM-SALA addresses the computational and memory bottlenecks of Transformer architectures in ultra-long context modeling by introducing a 9B-parameter hybrid architecture that integrates InfLLM-V2 sparse attention and Lightning Attention linear attention in a 1:3 ratio. By employing a cost-effective continual training framework that transforms pre-trained Transformer weights, the model reduces training costs by approximately 75% compared to training from scratch while maintaining general capabilities comparable to full-attention models like Qwen3-8B. This design enables significant efficiency gains, achieving up to 3.5× inference speed at 256K tokens on an NVIDIA A6000D GPU and supporting context lengths up to 1M tokens where traditional 8B models fail due to out-of-memory errors.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Ultra-Long Context Challenge

Large language models are undergoing a fundamental paradigm shift in their application scenarios. Early LLM deployments focused on fragmented, short-context tasks like simple question-answering. However, emerging use cases demand **holistic contextual understanding** across massive token sequences:

- **Technical documentation**: Grasping entire manuals or specification documents at once
- **Repository-scale code engineering**: Analyzing dependency trees containing tens of thousands of lines of code
- **Long-horizon agents**: Maintaining coherent task states and memory over multi-day human-AI collaborations

This shift makes the ability to process **millions of tokens** a critical capability for next-generation LLMs. The paper explicitly positions this as necessary for "advanced applications" where models must handle "ultra-long contexts" rather than fragmented information (Introduction, paragraph 1).

### The Transformer Bottleneck: Two Dimensions of Constraint

The standard Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), while foundational to modern LLMs, encounters severe limitations when scaling to ultra-long contexts. These limitations manifest in two distinct dimensions:

#### 1. Compute Bottleneck: Quadratic Complexity

The standard **full-attention mechanism** has computational complexity of $O(N^2)$ where $N$ is the sequence length. This quadratic growth means:

- Doubling the sequence length **quadruples** the computational cost
- At millions of tokens, the overhead causes inference latency to "increase dramatically" (Introduction, paragraph 2)

For practical deployment, this makes ultra-long context processing prohibitively expensive on standard hardware.

#### 2. Memory Bottleneck: KV-Cache Explosion

During **auto-regressive generation** (token-by-token output), the model must store **key and value states (KV-Cache)** for all historical tokens to avoid redundant computation. The paper provides concrete scale estimates:

> "For a typical 8B-parameter model, even when utilizing Grouped Query Attention (GQA), the KV-Cache required for millions of tokens can reach dozens or even hundreds of gigabytes." (Introduction, paragraph 2)

This memory requirement creates a hard ceiling on context length for standard architectures. As demonstrated later in the experiments (Figure 2, Figure 3), full-attention models like Qwen3-8B encounter **out-of-memory (OOM) errors** at 512K tokens on an A6000D GPU (96GB VRAM) and at just 128K tokens on an RTX 5090 (32GB VRAM).

### Prior Approaches: Two Paradigms with Inherent Trade-offs

The research community has developed two primary paradigms to address these bottlenecks. Understanding their individual strengths and weaknesses is essential to appreciating MiniCPM-SALA's hybrid design.

#### Sparse Attention: Efficient Computation, Dense Storage

**Sparse attention** methods (e.g., InfLLM-V2, sliding windows, global anchors) reduce the compute bottleneck by computing only the most salient portions of the attention matrix rather than all pairwise token interactions.

**Advantage**: Local computation reduces immediate processing overhead.

**Critical Limitation**: These methods suffer from a **"sparse computation, dense storage"** problem (Introduction, paragraph 3). While computation is reduced, the model must still **retain the full KV-Cache** to support contextual information retrieval. This means the memory bottleneck remains largely unaddressed.

#### Linear Attention: Constant Complexity, Lossy Compression

**Linear attention** methods (e.g., Lightning Attention, Mamba-style architectures) utilize **recurrent formulations** to reduce computational complexity to $O(N)$ — linear rather than quadratic growth.

**Advantage**: Both compute and memory scale efficiently with sequence length.

**Critical Limitation**: This efficiency is achieved through **lossy compression of contextual information**. The paper states this "inevitably results in performance degradation" (Introduction, paragraph 3). Pure linear architectures exhibit a "precision bottleneck" in long-range information processing, meaning they struggle to accurately recall or reason over distant tokens.

#### The Fundamental Trade-off

Existing solutions force practitioners to choose between:

| Approach | Memory Efficiency | Performance Fidelity |
|----------|------------------|---------------------|
| Full Attention | Poor (O(N²) KV-Cache) | High |
| Sparse Attention | Poor (dense storage) | High |
| Linear Attention | High (O(N)) | Degraded |

No prior approach successfully achieves **both** memory efficiency **and** high-fidelity long-context modeling at scale.

### MiniCPM-SALA's Positioning: Hybridization as a Third Path

MiniCPM-SALA positions itself as the first work to demonstrate through **large-scale experimentation** that hybrid sparse-linear architectures can match full-attention baseline performance while achieving substantial efficiency gains (Introduction, paragraph 4).

The key insight is **complementary integration** rather than choosing one paradigm:

- **Sparse attention layers (25%)**: Provide high-fidelity long-context modeling via InfLLM-V2, enabling precise recall over extended contexts without positional encoding decay
- **Linear attention layers (75%)**: Provide global computational efficiency via Lightning Attention with $O(N)$ complexity

This 1:3 ratio is not arbitrary — it derives from "architectural designs of recent representative studies" and "internal small-scale preliminary experiments" (Section 2.1). The hybrid configuration leverages sparse attention's "granular focus for local details" and linear attention's "broad context" efficiency.

### Training Paradigm Innovation: Conversion vs. From-Scratch

Beyond architecture, MiniCPM-SALA addresses a second gap in existing work: **training cost**. Existing hybrid model training falls into two categories:

1. **Training from scratch**: Maximum architectural flexibility but immense computational cost (used by Qwen3-Next, Kimi-Linear)
2. **Cross-architecture distillation**: Converting pre-trained models but with potential capability loss

MiniCPM-SALA employs a **continual training conversion paradigm** that transforms a pre-trained Transformer (MiniCPM-4.0 checkpoint, already trained on 7T tokens) into a hybrid model. This approach:

- Recycles pre-trained weights and representations
- Reduces total training budget to **approximately 25%** relative to training from scratch (Abstract; Section 2.2 notes 2T tokens vs. 8T for MiniCPM-4.0)
- Preserves general capabilities acquired during earlier training phases

This positioning is significant because it makes hybrid architecture adoption **practically feasible** for teams without massive training budgets, democratizing access to efficient long-context models.

### Summary of the Gap Addressed

MiniCPM-SALA addresses a **dual gap** in the literature:

1. **Architectural gap**: No prior hybrid sparse-linear model demonstrated at scale that matches full-attention performance while enabling 1M-token contexts on single GPUs
2. **Methodological gap**: No cost-effective conversion framework existed to transform pre-trained Transformers into hybrid models without 75% training cost reduction

The paper's contribution is demonstrating that these two gaps can be solved simultaneously through careful hybridization and continual training — a claim substantiated by the experimental results in Section 3.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an **architecture design and training methodology paper** that demonstrates how to convert a pre-trained Transformer model into a hybrid sparse-linear attention architecture while preserving capabilities and reducing training costs by approximately 75%.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

MiniCPM-SALA is a 9B-parameter language model that replaces standard attention layers with a carefully orchestrated mixture of two different attention mechanisms — sparse attention for precision and linear attention for efficiency — arranged in a specific 1:3 ratio across the model's layers. The system solves the ultra-long context problem by keeping memory usage low enough to process 1M tokens on a single GPU while maintaining the accuracy of full-attention models through strategic placement of high-fidelity sparse attention layers at critical positions in the network.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The model consists of three major component types arranged in a layered stack. **Input tokens** first pass through an embedding layer, then flow through approximately 40 transformer-style layers where each layer contains either a sparse attention block (InfLLM-V2, 25% of layers) or a linear attention block (Lightning Attention, 75% of layers), followed by a Feed-Forward Network (FFN) block. **Sparse attention layers** compute selective attention over salient token positions while maintaining dense KV-Cache storage for precise long-range retrieval. **Linear attention layers** process all tokens with $O(N)$ complexity using recurrent state compression, trading some precision for dramatic memory savings. **Hybrid Positional Encoding (HyPE)** applies Rotary Positional Embedding (RoPE) to linear attention layers but removes it from sparse attention layers to prevent positional decay over long distances. **Output gates** regulate information flow after each attention block to prevent attention sink phenomena. The final output passes through a language modeling head to produce token predictions.

- **Attention mechanism fundamentals**: First, we explain what sparse and linear attention actually compute and why they have different complexity profiles, since this is the foundation of the entire hybrid design.
- **Layer mixing strategy**: Second, we detail the 1:3 ratio and layer selection algorithm, because understanding *where* each attention type is placed is as important as *what* they compute.
- **Positional encoding harmonization**: Third, we explain HyPE and why RoPE is applied differently across layer types, since positional handling is critical for long-context coherence.

- **Architectural stabilizers**: Fifth, we cover QK-Normalization and output gates, since these modifications enable stable training of the hybrid architecture.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Core Attention Mechanisms: What Each Type Computes

The hybrid architecture rests on two fundamentally different attention computations that trade off memory, compute, and precision in complementary ways.

**Sparse Attention (InfLLM-V2)** operates by computing attention scores only over a subset of token positions rather than all pairwise interactions. The mechanism identifies "salient" tokens — those most relevant to the current query — through a combination of sliding window attention (attending to recent tokens) and global anchor tokens (attending to strategically important positions throughout the sequence). Mathematically, for a query $q_i$ at position $i$, sparse attention computes:

$$\text{Attention}(q_i, K, V) = \sum_{j \in S_i} \text{softmax}\left(\frac{q_i \cdot k_j}{\sqrt{d}}\right) v_j$$

where $S_i$ is the sparse subset of positions attended to by position $i$, $K$ and $V$ are key and value matrices, and $d$ is the hidden dimension. The critical design choice is that **InfLLM-V2 introduces no additional parameters** to the architecture — it reuses the same query, key, and value projection matrices as standard attention but changes *which* positions receive computation. This parameter-free design enables seamless conversion from dense Transformer weights because the sparse modules can directly inherit pre-trained dense weights without architectural mismatches. However, sparse attention still requires **dense KV-Cache storage** — all historical key-value pairs must be retained in memory even though not all are computed over — which means the memory bottleneck is not fully resolved by sparse attention alone.

**Linear Attention (Lightning Attention)** takes a fundamentally different approach by reformulating attention as a recurrent state update rather than a pairwise similarity computation. Instead of computing $Q \cdot K^T$ for all token pairs, linear attention maintains a running state matrix $S_t$ that accumulates key-value information as tokens are processed sequentially. The core computation can be expressed as:

$$S_t = S_{t-1} + k_t \cdot v_t^T$$
$$\text{Output}_t = q_t \cdot S_t$$

where $S_t$ is the accumulated state at timestep $t$, $k_t$ and $v_t$ are the key and value vectors for the current token, and $q_t$ is the query vector. This formulation reduces computational complexity from $O(N^2)$ to $O(N)$ because each token only interacts with the compressed state rather than all previous tokens. The state matrix $S_t$ has fixed size regardless of sequence length, which means **KV-Cache memory scales linearly** rather than quadratically. However, this state compression is **lossy** — information from earlier tokens gets progressively compressed and potentially degraded as new tokens update the state. This is the "precision bottleneck" mentioned in the Introduction: pure linear attention struggles with accurate long-range information retrieval because the compressed state cannot perfectly preserve all historical information.

**Why Hybridization Works**: The 1:3 ratio leverages complementary strengths — sparse attention layers provide "anchor points" for precise long-range retrieval at critical positions in the network, while linear attention layers provide efficient global context propagation between these anchor points. Think of sparse layers as "checkpoints" that can accurately recall distant information, and linear layers as "highways" that efficiently transport information between checkpoints without the $O(N^2)$ cost.

#### Layer Mixing Strategy: The 1:3 Ratio and Selection Algorithm

The architecture does not naively alternate sparse and linear layers — instead, it employs a **layer selection algorithm** from Chen et al. (2026) to determine optimal placement. Figure 1 illustrates this hybrid design where sparse attention modules (25% of layers) are interleaved with linear attention modules (75% of layers) in a non-uniform pattern.

**The 1:3 Ratio Rationale**: This specific ratio derives from two sources. First, "architectural designs of recent representative studies" such as Qwen3-Next and Kimi-Linear employed similar hybrid ratios, suggesting this is an emerging best practice in the field. Second, the MiniCPM team conducted "internal small-scale preliminary experiments" that validated this ratio before committing to full-scale training. The ratio represents a balance point: enough sparse layers (25%) to maintain high-fidelity long-context modeling, but enough linear layers (75%) to achieve substantial efficiency gains.

**Layer Selection Mechanism**: Rather than uniformly interleaving (e.g., sparse at layers 1, 5, 9, 13...), the selection algorithm determines placement based on which layers benefit most from sparse attention's precision. The paper references Chen et al. (2026) for the specific algorithm but does not detail it in this work — this is a notable gap where readers must consult the cited work for full implementation details. However, the paper does specify two **hard constraints** on layer selection:

- **First layer unconverted**: The initial transformer layer retains standard attention (later trained as sparse) to improve training stability during the conversion process.
- **Last layer unconverted**: The final transformer layer also remains unconverted for the same stability reasons.

These boundary layers serve as "anchor points" that preserve the model's input processing and output generation capabilities during the architectural transformation.

**Parameter Count Implications**: The model has 9B total parameters. Since InfLLM-V2 introduces no additional parameters and Lightning Attention maintains parameter parity with standard attention, the hybrid model has approximately the same parameter count as the original MiniCPM-4.0 checkpoint. This is critical — the efficiency gains come from **computation pattern changes**, not parameter reduction.

#### Hybrid Positional Encoding (HyPE): Differential RoPE Application

Positional encoding is handled differently across attention types through **HyPE (Hybrid Positional Encoding)**, a design choice that directly addresses a subtle but critical problem in long-context modeling.

**RoPE on Linear Attention Layers**: Linear attention layers receive **Rotary Positional Embedding (RoPE)** applied to their query and key vectors. RoPE encodes positional information by rotating query and key vectors in the embedding space based on their sequence positions. Mathematically, for a token at position $m$, RoPE applies a rotation matrix $R_m$ to the query and key vectors:

$$q_m' = R_m \cdot q_m, \quad k_m' = R_m \cdot k_m$$

This enables **position-sensitive memory** in the linear attention state — the compressed state $S_t$ retains information about the relative order of tokens, which is essential for tasks requiring positional awareness (e.g., understanding token sequences, maintaining grammatical structure).

**No RoPE on Sparse Attention Layers**: Sparse attention layers **remove RoPE entirely** — this is the "NoPE" configuration mentioned in Section 3.1 (Results of Ultra-Long Context). This strategic omission prevents **positional encoding decay** over long distances. RoPE's rotational encoding can cause positional information to become ambiguous or degraded at very long sequence lengths because the rotation angles wrap around or become numerically unstable. By removing RoPE from sparse layers, the stored KV-Cache does not require combination with positional information, enabling "more precise recall over extended contexts."

**Why This Harmonization Matters**: This differential treatment addresses a fundamental tension. Linear attention *needs* positional encoding because its compressed state would otherwise lose all order information. Sparse attention *benefits from removing* positional encoding because it stores full KV pairs and can retrieve them precisely without positional transformation. HyPE allows each attention type to operate in its optimal configuration while the overall model maintains coherent positional awareness.

**Attribution**: HyPE is attributed to Chen et al. (2026) / HypeNet, indicating this is borrowed technology rather than novel to MiniCPM-SALA. The MiniCPM team's contribution is demonstrating its effectiveness at scale in a hybrid sparse-linear architecture.

#### Architectural Stabilizers: QK-Normalization and Output Gates

Two additional architectural modifications enhance training stability and prevent known failure modes in long-context attention.

**QK-Normalization**: Applied to **all attention layers** (both sparse and linear), QK-Normalization normalizes query and key vectors before computing attention scores. This prevents **activation spikes** — sudden large values in the attention computation that can destabilize training, particularly in long-context scenarios where accumulated numerical errors become significant. The technique is attributed to Henry et al. (2020) and serves two purposes: (1) improving training stability for long sequences, and (2) boosting expressivity of linear attention modules by ensuring their state updates remain numerically well-behaved.

**Output Gates**: Incorporated after **each attention block** (sparse and linear), output gates regulate information flow from the attention mechanism to the subsequent FFN layer. This architectural choice aligns with "gated attention mechanism" advances from Qiu et al. (2025). The output gate addresses the **attention sink** problem — a phenomenon where attention mechanisms disproportionately focus on certain tokens (often early tokens or special tokens) at the expense of others. By learnably gating the attention output, the model can prevent excessive focus on specific tokens and ensure "more flexible distribution of attention weights." Empirically, the team observed that "integrating output gates into both linear and sparse attention significantly improves model stability and performance."

#### Training Pipeline: Five-Stage Conversion Process

The training methodology is a core contribution — Table 1 provides the complete overview of all five stages with exact token counts, sequence lengths, and configurations. The entire process consumes approximately **2T tokens**, which is **25% of the 8T tokens** required to train MiniCPM-4.0 from scratch.

**Stage 1: Architecture Conversion (HALO)**

This initial stage converts the Transformer model from full attention to hybrid attention using the HALO framework (Hybrid Attention via Layer Optimization). Key configurations:

- **Trainable parameters**: Only the converted linear-attention layers are trainable; all other parameters (sparse attention layers, FFN, embeddings) remain frozen.
- **Sparse attention**: Disabled during this stage.
- **Sequence length**: 512 tokens (0.5K).
- **Training volume**: 1.3B tokens.
- **Layer preservation**: First and last layers kept unconverted for stability; remaining layers use HALO selection algorithm to determine which become sparse vs. linear.
- **Key difference from standard HALO**: The final fine-tuning step of original HALO is skipped — instead, extensive continual pre-training follows in subsequent stages.

This stage is highly efficient because only linear attention layers are trained, and the short sequence length minimizes compute requirements. The purpose is **architectural transformation** — changing the model's computation pattern while preserving pre-trained weights.

**Stage 2: Continual Stable-Training**

This stage facilitates coordination between the newly converted linear attention layers and existing model components. Key configurations:

- **Trainable parameters**: All parameters.
- **Sparse attention**: Disabled (computational efficiency at short sequence lengths).
- **Sequence length**: 4K tokens.
- **Training volume**: 314.6B tokens.
- **Learning rate**: $7.5 \times 10^{-3}$, held constant after 2,000-step warmup.
- **Global batch size**: 7.8M tokens (accounting for sequence length and GPU count).

The objective is **component coordination** — ensuring linear attention layers work harmoniously with FFN layers, embeddings, and the preserved full attention layers (later converted to sparse). Sparse attention remains disabled because the 4K sequence length does not yet require its long-context capabilities.

**Stage 3: Short-Decay Training**

This is the **most extensive stage** by token volume, focused on enhancing general capabilities through high-quality data. Key configurations:

- **Trainable parameters**: All parameters.
- **Sparse attention**: Disabled.
- **Sequence length**: 4K tokens.
- **Training volume**: 1006.6B tokens (over 1T — the largest single stage).
- **Learning rate**: Exponential decay from $7.5 \times 10^{-3}$ to $3.75 \times 10^{-4}$.
- **Global batch size**: 7.8M tokens.
- **Data composition**: Significantly increased weight of "L2 high-quality selection data" (Wang et al., 2026), plus large volumes of PDF corpora and "L3 synthetic data."

The purpose is **knowledge compression and internalization** — using high-information-density training data to enhance general capabilities and logical reasoning. This stage builds on the MiniCPM-4.0 decay strategy but with enhanced data quality weighting.

**Stage 4: Long-Decay Training**

This stage progressively extends context length to train long-context capabilities. Key configurations across three sub-phases:

| Sub-phase | Sequence Length | Tokens | Learning Rate | Batch Size |
|-----------|-----------------|--------|---------------|------------|
| Phase 1 | 32K | 102.2B | $3 \times 10^{-4} \to 2 \times 10^{-4}$ | 7.8M → 9.8M |
| Phase 2 | 160K | 62.9B | $2 \times 10^{-4} \to 1 \times 10^{-4}$ | 9.8M → 10.1M |
| Phase 3 | 520K | 50.6B | $1 \times 10^{-4} \to 3.75 \times 10^{-5}$ | 10.1M |

- **Trainable parameters**: All parameters.
- **Sparse attention**: **Enabled** at this stage (first time active).
- **Data strategy**: Up-sampled proportion of long-context data to align model with long-sequence distributions.

This is the **critical stage** for long-context capability development. Sparse attention is enabled because "growing computational advantages of sparse attention at longer sequences" make it beneficial. The model learns "synergy between sparse attention and linear attention" — how the two mechanisms complement each other at scale. The progressive length extension (32K → 160K → 520K) allows gradual adaptation rather than sudden context length jumps that could destabilize training.

**Stage 5: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)**

This final stage catalyzes reasoning and task-execution capabilities. Key configurations across two sub-phases:

| Sub-phase | Sequence Length | Tokens | Learning Rate | Batch Size |
|-----------|-----------------|--------|---------------|------------|
| Phase 1 | 64K | 204.5B | Warmup 1,000 steps to $1 \times 10^{-3}$, decay to $1 \times 10^{-4}$ | 15.7M |
| Phase 2 | 140K | 213.3B | Same schedule | 17.8M |

- **Trainable parameters**: All parameters.
- **Sparse attention**: Enabled throughout.
- **Data composition**: High-quality reasoning-intensive data (code, mathematics, knowledge, function calls, general dialogue) plus specifically synthesized long-context data for "precision of information retrieval and cross-document comprehension."

The purpose is **capability bridging** — balancing general capabilities with long-context proficiency by training on both shorter (64K) and longer (140K) contexts. The synthesized long-context data is specifically designed to enhance retrieval precision across extended sequences.

#### Training Cost Analysis: The 75% Reduction Claim

The Abstract claims approximately 75% training cost reduction compared to training from scratch. Section 2.2 provides the supporting numbers:

- **MiniCPM-SALA conversion**: ~2T tokens total across all five stages.
- **MiniCPM-4.0 from scratch**: 8T tokens (stated in Section 2.2: "already been trained on 7T tokens" with 8T total implied).
- **Ratio**: 2T / 8T = 25% of original cost, meaning 75% reduction.

This reduction is achieved through **parameter inheritance** — recycling pre-trained weights from MiniCPM-4.0 rather than initializing randomly. The HALO conversion stage (1.3B tokens) is particularly efficient because only linear attention layers are trainable. However, readers should note this is **token count**, not compute-hours — actual wall-clock time reduction depends on hardware utilization, batch sizes, and sequence lengths at each stage.

#### Design Choices: Why This Approach Over Alternatives

Several critical design decisions warrant explicit justification:

**Why 1:3 ratio instead of 1:1?** A 1:1 ratio would provide more sparse attention precision but sacrifice efficiency gains. The 1:3 ratio was validated through "internal small-scale preliminary experiments" as the balance point where efficiency gains are substantial without significant performance degradation.

**Why continual training instead of from-scratch?** Training from scratch offers "maximum architectural flexibility" but requires 4× the token budget. For teams without massive training resources, conversion is the only feasible path to hybrid architectures. The success of this approach demonstrates that pre-trained Transformer weights can be successfully adapted to hybrid attention patterns.

**Why enable sparse attention only at Stage 4?** Sparse attention's computational advantages only manifest at longer sequences. At 4K tokens (Stages 2-3), the overhead of sparse attention computation does not justify its benefits. Enabling it at 32K+ tokens (Stage 4) ensures the model learns sparse attention when it actually matters for efficiency.

**Why remove RoPE from sparse layers?** This is counterintuitive — positional encoding is typically considered essential. However, for sparse attention storing full KV pairs, positional information can be retrieved from the stored keys without explicit RoPE encoding. Removing RoPE prevents the "decay of long-distance information often associated with RoPE" at ultra-long contexts.

**Why keep first/last layers unconverted?** These layers handle input embedding transformation and output logit generation — disrupting them during conversion could destabilize the entire model. Keeping them as anchor points preserves input/output compatibility during the architectural transformation.

#### Notable Gaps and Unclear Details

Several details are not fully specified in this paper:

- **Exact layer count**: The paper states 9B parameters but does not specify the total number of transformer layers, making it impossible to calculate exactly how many are sparse vs. linear.
- **Layer selection algorithm specifics**: Referenced as from Chen et al. (2026) but not detailed here — readers must consult the cited work.
- **Hardware configuration**: Batch sizes are given in tokens (e.g., 7.8M tokens) but the number of GPUs used is not specified, making reproduction difficult.
- **HyPE implementation details**: The exact mechanism for applying differential RoPE is attributed to Chen et al. (2026) without full specification.

These gaps are acceptable for a paper building on prior work, but readers attempting reproduction will need to consult the cited references.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the core contributions that distinguish MiniCPM-SALA from prior work. We distinguish between **fundamental innovations** (new paradigms or capabilities not previously demonstrated) and **incremental improvements** (refinements of existing techniques). Understanding this distinction helps readers assess which aspects of the work represent genuine advances versus effective engineering integration.

### 4.1 First Large-Scale Demonstration That Hybrid Sparse-Linear Attention Matches Full-Attention Performance

**Innovation Type**: Fundamental

Prior work exploring hybrid attention architectures existed before MiniCPM-SALA. The paper acknowledges several contemporaneous efforts: Hu et al. (2025), Hou et al. (2025), and He & Garner (2025) all explored integrating sparse and linear attention mechanisms. However, the paper makes a critical claim in the Introduction:

> "To the best of our knowledge, MiniCPM-SALA is the first to demonstrate through large-scale experimentation that these hybrids can match the performance of full-attention baselines." (Introduction, paragraph 4)

**What Makes This Different**: Previous hybrid models either (1) showed degraded performance compared to full-attention baselines, or (2) were not evaluated at comparable scales with rigorous benchmarking. MiniCPM-SALA provides empirical evidence that the hybrid approach does not require sacrificing general capabilities. Table 2 shows the model achieving an average score of 76.53 across standard benchmarks — competitive with Qwen3-8B (73.45) and Falcon-H1R-7B (76.45) despite the architectural complexity.

**Why This Matters**: This finding challenges an implicit assumption in the field: that efficiency optimizations necessarily degrade performance. The 1:3 sparse-to-linear ratio, combined with strategic layer placement, achieves what prior work could not — **efficiency without fidelity loss**. This validates hybrid architectures as a viable path forward for production systems where both cost and quality matter.

**Evidence**: Table 3 demonstrates this claim in long-context scenarios. At 128K tokens on RULER, MiniCPM-SALA scores 89.37 while Qwen3-8B drops to 71.74. The hybrid model not only matches but **exceeds** full-attention performance at extended contexts, suggesting the architecture provides genuine advantages beyond just cost savings.

### 4.2 Transformer-to-Hybrid Conversion Framework: A New Training Paradigm

**Innovation Type**: Fundamental

The five-stage continual training pipeline (Section 2.2, Table 1) represents a methodological innovation distinct from prior hybrid model development approaches. The paper identifies two existing paradigms:

1. **Training from scratch**: Used by Qwen3-Next, Kimi-Linear, and Nemotron-Nano. Maximum flexibility but requires full token budgets (8T+ tokens for comparable models).
2. **Cross-architecture distillation**: Converting pre-trained models but often with capability degradation.

MiniCPM-SALA introduces a **third paradigm**: architectural conversion via continual training that preserves pre-trained knowledge while adapting computation patterns.

**What Makes This Different**: The HALO-based conversion stage (Stage 1) is uniquely efficient — only 1.3B tokens with frozen parameters except for linear attention layers. This "surgical" approach transforms the architecture without discarding learned representations. Subsequent stages then coordinate the hybrid components rather than learning everything de novo.

**Why This Matters**: The 75% training cost reduction (2T vs. 8T tokens) makes hybrid architecture adoption **practically feasible** for teams without massive compute budgets. This democratizes access to efficient long-context models. As stated in the Abstract:

> "We introduce a cost-effective continual training framework that transforms pre-trained Transformer-based models into hybrid models, which reduces training costs by approximately 75% compared to training from scratch."

**Critical Nuance**: This is not merely an engineering optimization — it represents a conceptual shift in how we think about model architecture evolution. Rather than treating architecture as fixed at pre-training time, this framework enables **post-hoc architectural transformation**. This opens possibilities for adapting existing model families to new efficiency requirements without full retraining.

**Evidence**: The standard evaluation results (Table 2) confirm capability preservation. MiniCPM-SALA scores 81.55 on CMMLU vs. MiniCPM-4.1's 84.72 — a modest 3-point drop despite the architectural transformation and 75% cost reduction. This validates that the conversion preserves core knowledge rather than requiring relearning.

### 4.3 Length Extrapolation Beyond Training Context Without Auxiliary Techniques

**Innovation Type**: Fundamental

MiniCPM-SALA demonstrates a surprising capability: extrapolating to 2048K (2M) tokens despite being trained only to 520K tokens. Table 4 shows the model maintaining a RULER score of 81.6 at 2048K context length.

**What Makes This Different**: Most long-context models require **auxiliary techniques** to extend beyond their training context length. YaRN (Peng et al., 2024) and similar methods apply positional interpolation or scaling to enable extrapolation. The paper explicitly states:

> "It is worth noting that this extrapolation requires no auxiliary techniques (e.g., YaRN)." (Section 3.1, Results of Ultra-Long Context)

**Why This Matters**: This suggests the hybrid architecture itself provides **inherent length generalization** properties. The attribution goes to the NoPE (No Positional Encoding) configuration in sparse attention layers:

> "The length extrapolation capabilities of MiniCPM-SALA can be attributed to the NoPE configuration within the sparse attention layers. In this design, the stored KV-Cache does not require combination with positional information, which can otherwise hinder the capture of long-range dependencies." (Section 3.1)

This is counterintuitive — positional encoding is typically considered essential for sequence modeling. The finding suggests that for certain attention mechanisms (specifically sparse attention with full KV storage), explicit positional encoding may actually **impede** ultra-long context handling.

**Evidence**: Table 4 compares MiniCPM-SALA (9B) against much larger Qwen3 models. At 1000K tokens, MiniCPM-SALA scores 86.3 vs. Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B-Instruct's 80.3 — a 9B model outperforming an 80B model on ultra-long context. This parameter efficiency combined with extrapolation capability represents a genuine architectural advance.

**Caveat**: The paper does not provide ablation studies isolating the NoPE contribution from other factors (e.g., HyPE, output gates). Readers should treat this attribution as a hypothesis supported by empirical results rather than a proven causal mechanism.

### 4.4 Democratization of 1M-Token Processing on Consumer Hardware

**Innovation Type**: Incremental (but Significant)

While not a theoretical innovation, the practical implication of MiniCPM-SALA's efficiency deserves explicit recognition. Figure 3 demonstrates that on an RTX 5090 (32GB VRAM — consumer-grade hardware), MiniCPM-SALA processes 1024K tokens while Qwen3-8B fails at 128K (non-quantized) or 256K (quantized).

**What Makes This Different**: Prior ultra-long context models required cloud-grade hardware (A100, H100 clusters) for inference. MiniCPM-SALA enables 1M-token processing on hardware accessible to individual researchers and small teams.

**Why This Matters**: This shifts ultra-long context from a **cloud-only capability** to an **edge-deployable capability**. The paper explicitly frames this in the Introduction's contributions:

> "These results demonstrate the broad prospects of MiniCPM-SALA in edge-side information-intensive applications." (Introduction, contributions bullet 4)

**Evidence**: Figure 3(a) shows TTFT of 10.8s at 1024K on RTX 5090 (non-quantized) for MiniCPM-SALA, while Qwen3-8B shows "OOM" at 256K. This is not a marginal improvement — it represents a **qualitative capability gap** where one model functions and the other cannot execute at all.

**Distinction from Fundamental Innovation**: This is an engineering consequence of the architectural innovations (hybrid attention, conversion framework) rather than a novel technique itself. However, the practical impact — enabling research and deployment previously impossible on consumer hardware — warrants recognition as a significant contribution.

### 4.5 Synthesis: What Is Truly Novel vs. What Is Effective Integration

Readers should distinguish between three categories of contribution in this work:

| Contribution | Category | Reasoning |
|--------------|----------|-----------|
| Hybrid architecture matching full-attention | Fundamental | First large-scale demonstration of this capability |
| Transformer-to-hybrid conversion | Fundamental | New training paradigm with 75% cost reduction |
| Length extrapolation without YaRN | Fundamental | Inherent architectural property, not auxiliary technique |
| HyPE, QK-Norm, output gates | Incremental | Borrowed from Chen et al. (2026), Qiu et al. (2025), Henry et al. (2020) |
| Consumer GPU 1M-token inference | Incremental (Significant) | Engineering consequence of above innovations |

**Critical Insight**: MiniCPM-SALA's primary innovation is not any single technique but the **demonstration that these techniques can be integrated at scale without capability degradation**. Many components (HyPE, Lightning Attention, InfLLM-V2) exist in prior work. The contribution is showing they work together in a 9B-parameter model with rigorous benchmarking.

This distinction matters for readers evaluating the paper's impact: the work advances the field by **validating a design space** (hybrid sparse-linear with conversion training) rather than introducing entirely new mechanisms. This is valuable because it provides a **proven blueprint** for future work, reducing uncertainty for teams considering similar architectures.

**Unresolved Questions**: The paper does not ablate individual components (e.g., what happens without output gates? without HyPE?). Readers interested in implementation should consult Chen et al. (2026) for architectural details and consider running ablations for their specific use cases.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the experimental methodology and results presented in MiniCPM-SALA. We analyze whether the evaluation design adequately tests the paper's core claims, scrutinize the quantitative results with specific numbers, and identify gaps in the experimental coverage. The analysis is organized around three evaluation dimensions: standard capability benchmarks, long-context performance, and inference efficiency.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Benchmark Selection and Coverage

The paper employs a **three-tier evaluation strategy** designed to test different aspects of model capability:

**Tier 1: Standard Capability Benchmarks** (Table 2)

These benchmarks assess general knowledge and reasoning abilities independent of context length:

| Category | Benchmarks | What They Test |
|----------|------------|----------------|
| Knowledge | CMMLU, MMLU-Pro | Multitask language understanding across domains |
| Code | HumanEval, LCB-v5/v6, MBPP | Code generation and completion capabilities |
| Math | AIME24, AIME25 | Mathematical reasoning at competition level |
| Other | BBH, IFEval | Big-bench hard tasks and instruction following |

The selection is comprehensive for standard LLM evaluation. However, readers should note that **MiniCPM-4.1-8B is excluded from long-context evaluation** due to its 64K context length limitation (Section 3.1, Baseline Models). This is a reasonable exclusion but means the model's direct predecessor cannot be compared on the paper's primary innovation dimension.

**Tier 2: Long-Context Benchmarks** (Table 3)

These benchmarks specifically test information retrieval and reasoning over extended sequences:

- **RULER** (Hsieh et al., 2024): Tests retrieval, multi-hop reasoning, and aggregation across context lengths of 64K and 128K tokens
- **MRCR**: Tests multi-document comprehension with varying context multipliers (2N, 4N, 8N) at 64K and 128K base lengths
- **NoLiMa** (Modarressi et al., 2025): Tests long-context evaluation "beyond literal matching" at 32K, 64K, and 128K

The paper notes these evaluations were conducted using the **OpenCompass framework** (Contributors, 2023), which provides standardized evaluation pipelines. This is important for reproducibility — readers can verify results using the same framework.

**Tier 3: Ultra-Long Context Benchmarks** (Table 4)

This tier tests extrapolation beyond training context length:

- **RULER extended**: Evaluated at 128K, 512K, 1000K, and 2048K tokens
- **Critical detail**: The model was trained only to 520K tokens (Table 1, Long-Decay Training), yet evaluated up to 2048K

This tests **length extrapolation** — the model's ability to handle contexts 4× longer than seen during training.

#### Baseline Model Selection

The paper selects baselines of **comparable parameter scale** (7B–9B range) to ensure fair comparison:

| Model | Parameters | Architecture Type | Context Limit |
|-------|------------|-------------------|---------------|
| Qwen3-8B | 8B | Full Attention | Not specified (fails at 512K) |
| Nemotron-Nano-v2-9B | 9B | Hybrid | Not specified |
| MiniCPM-4.1-8B | 8B | Full Attention | 64K |
| Ministral-3-R-8B | 8B | Hybrid | Not specified |
| Falcon-H1R-7B | 7B | Hybrid | Not specified |
| **MiniCPM-SALA** | **9B** | **Sparse-Linear Hybrid** | **1M+** |

This selection is appropriate for the paper's claim that hybrid architectures can **match full-attention performance** at similar scales. However, readers should note that Qwen3-Next-80B results in Table 4 are **cited from official documentation** (marked with ∗), not independently evaluated. This is acceptable for comparison but introduces potential evaluation condition differences.

#### Hardware and Inference Setup

The efficiency evaluation uses two distinct hardware configurations:

**Cloud-Grade Hardware:**
- **NVIDIA A6000D**: 96GB VRAM
- Used for Figure 2 comparisons

**Consumer-Grade Hardware:**
- **NVIDIA RTX 5090**: 32GB VRAM
- Used for Figure 3 comparisons

For each configuration, the paper measures:
- **TTFT (Time To First Token)**: Prefilling latency — how long to process the input context before generating the first output token
- **End-to-end latency**: Total time including both prefilling and decoding (1K tokens generated)
- **Quantization states**: Both non-quantized and GPTQ INT4 quantized models evaluated

The evaluation protocol specifies that "for each tested sequence length, the models process a specified input (prefilling) and generate 1K tokens (decoding)" (Figure 2 caption). This is a **standard inference benchmark** but readers should note it does not test generation quality over long outputs — only input processing efficiency.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results Analysis

#### Standard Capability Results (Table 2)

MiniCPM-SALA achieves an **average score of 76.53** across standard benchmarks. This result is critical because it addresses the fundamental concern that hybrid architectures degrade general capabilities.

**Key Comparisons:**

| Benchmark | Qwen3-8B | MiniCPM-SALA | Difference |
|-----------|----------|--------------|------------|
| CMMLU | 81.68 | 81.55 | -0.13 |
| MMLU-Pro | 73.26 | 67.04 | -6.22 |
| HumanEval | 93.90 | 95.12 | +1.22 |
| MBPP | 81.32 | 89.11 | +7.79 |
| AIME24 | 73.33 | 83.75 | +10.42 |
| AIME25 | 66.67 | 78.33 | +11.66 |
| BBH | 74.17 | 81.55 | +7.38 |
| IFEval | 84.66 | 76.34 | -8.32 |
| **Average** | **73.45** | **76.53** | **+3.08** |

**Critical Observations:**

1. **Mathematics is a strength**: The model significantly outperforms Qwen3-8B on AIME24 (+10.42) and AIME25 (+11.66). This suggests the hybrid architecture does not impair — and may enhance — complex reasoning capabilities.

2. **Code performance is competitive**: HumanEval (95.12) and MBPP (89.11) scores exceed Qwen3-8B, indicating code generation capabilities are preserved despite architectural changes.

3. **Instruction following shows degradation**: IFEval score of 76.34 vs. Qwen3's 84.66 (-8.32) suggests the model may be less reliable at following explicit instructions. This is a notable weakness that the paper does not explicitly discuss.

4. **MMLU-Pro gap**: The -6.22 point difference on MMLU-Pro is the largest knowledge-domain gap. This benchmark tests more challenging multitask understanding, suggesting some general knowledge may be affected by the conversion process.

**Assessment**: The standard evaluation **convincingly supports** the claim that hybrid architectures can match full-attention performance. The average score improvement (+3.08) and superior math/code performance demonstrate capability preservation. However, the IFEval and MMLU-Pro gaps indicate **non-uniform capability retention** — some capabilities are enhanced while others show modest degradation.

#### Long-Context Results (Table 3)

This is the **primary evaluation dimension** for MiniCPM-SALA's innovation claim. The results show substantial advantages at extended context lengths.

**RULER Benchmark:**

| Context Length | Qwen3-8B | MiniCPM-SALA | Difference |
|----------------|----------|--------------|------------|
| 64K | 80.53 | 92.65 | +12.12 |
| 128K | 71.74 | 89.37 | +17.63 |

The performance gap **widens** as context length increases. At 128K, MiniCPM-SALA maintains 89.37 while Qwen3-8B drops to 71.74 — a 17.63 point advantage. This supports the claim that hybrid attention provides **better length scaling** than full attention.

**MRCR Benchmark (Multi-Document Comprehension):**

| Context | Qwen3-8B | MiniCPM-SALA | Difference |
|---------|----------|--------------|------------|
| 64K-2N | 29.20 | 29.77 | +0.57 |
| 64K-4N | 21.56 | 20.57 | -0.99 |
| 64K-8N | 17.82 | 16.56 | -1.26 |
| 128K-2N | 26.50 | 28.62 | +2.12 |
| 128K-4N | 14.75 | 19.62 | +4.87 |
| 128K-8N | 12.15 | 10.12 | -2.03 |

The MRCR results are **mixed**. MiniCPM-SALA shows advantages at 128K-2N and 128K-4N but underperforms at higher complexity multipliers (8N). This suggests the hybrid architecture handles moderate context expansion well but may struggle with **extreme information density** across many documents.

**NoLiMa Benchmark:**

| Context Length | Qwen3-8B | MiniCPM-SALA | Difference |
|----------------|----------|--------------|------------|
| 32K | 43.40 | 54.54 | +11.14 |
| 64K | 23.35 | 42.95 | +19.60 |
| 128K | 11.25 | 23.86 | +12.61 |

NoLiMa shows the **most consistent advantage** for MiniCPM-SALA. At 64K, the model scores 42.95 vs. Qwen3's 23.35 — nearly double the performance. This benchmark tests "beyond literal matching," suggesting the hybrid architecture excels at **semantic retrieval** rather than simple token matching.

**Average Long-Context Score:**
- MiniCPM-SALA: **38.97**
- Qwen3-8B: **32.02**
- Difference: **+6.95**

**Assessment**: The long-context evaluation **strongly supports** the paper's efficiency claims. The consistent advantages across RULER and NoLiMa, particularly at 128K, demonstrate that the hybrid architecture provides genuine long-context benefits beyond just memory savings. However, the MRCR mixed results suggest **capability is task-dependent** — the model excels at some long-context tasks but not all.

#### Ultra-Long Context Extrapolation (Table 4)

This is the most **surprising result** in the paper — extrapolation to 2048K tokens despite training only to 520K.

**RULER Extended Results:**

| Context Length | Qwen3-30B-A3B∗ | Qwen3-235B-A22B∗ | Qwen3-Next-80B∗ | MiniCPM-SALA (9B) |
|----------------|----------------|------------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 128K | 89.1 | 93.9 | 96.0 | 89.4 |
| 512K | 78.4 | 90.9 | 86.9 | 87.1 |
| 1000K | 72.8 | 84.5 | 80.3 | 86.3 |
| 2048K | - | - | - | 81.6 |

**Critical Observations:**

1. **Parameter efficiency**: At 1000K, MiniCPM-SALA (9B) scores 86.3 vs. Qwen3-Next-80B's 80.3 — a **9B model outperforming an 80B model** by 6 points. This is remarkable parameter efficiency.

2. **Graceful degradation**: Performance drops from 89.4 (128K) to 81.6 (2048K) — only 7.8 points over a 16× context increase. This suggests the architecture scales gracefully.

3. **No auxiliary techniques**: The paper explicitly states "this extrapolation requires no auxiliary techniques (e.g., YaRN)" (Section 3.1). This is significant because YaRN and similar methods are commonly required for context extension.

4. **Missing Qwen3 data at 2048K**: The Qwen3 models show "-" at 2048K, indicating they were not evaluated or could not handle this length. This makes direct comparison impossible at the maximum tested length.

**Assessment**: The ultra-long context results **convincingly demonstrate** length extrapolation capability. The 81.6 score at 2048K with a 9B model is genuinely impressive. However, readers should note:
- Qwen3 results are from official documentation, not independent evaluation
- No baseline achieves 2048K, so MiniCPM-SALA's advantage at this length is unchallenged rather than comparatively proven
- The attribution to NoPE (no positional encoding in sparse layers) is a **hypothesis** without ablation evidence

#### Inference Efficiency Results (Figures 2 and 3)

The efficiency evaluation provides the most **practically significant** results — demonstrating where the model can run that baselines cannot.

**NVIDIA A6000D (96GB VRAM) - Figure 2:**

| Sequence Length | Metric | Qwen3-8B | MiniCPM-SALA | Speedup |
|-----------------|--------|----------|--------------|---------|
| 256K | TTFT (non-quantized) | 180.8s | 51.6s | **3.5×** |
| 512K | TTFT (non-quantized) | OOM | 25.2s | N/A |
| 1024K | TTFT (non-quantized) | OOM | 250.3s | N/A |
| 256K | End-to-end (non-quantized) | 350.0s | 180.8s | **1.9×** |
| 1024K | End-to-end (non-quantized) | OOM | 256.9s | N/A |

**Key Findings:**

1. **3.5× speedup at 256K**: This is the headline efficiency claim from the Abstract, and Figure 2(a) confirms it. TTFT drops from 180.8s to 51.6s.

2. **OOM avoidance**: Qwen3-8B fails at 512K and 1024K due to out-of-memory errors. MiniCPM-SALA successfully processes these lengths. This is a **qualitative capability difference** — one model works, the other cannot execute.

3. **Quantization benefits**: With INT4 quantization (Figure 2c-d), MiniCPM-SALA achieves TTFT of 12.6s at 1024K vs. 250.3s non-quantized — a **20× improvement** from quantization alone.

**NVIDIA RTX 5090 (32GB VRAM) - Figure 3:**

| Sequence Length | Metric | Qwen3-8B | MiniCPM-SALA |
|-----------------|--------|----------|--------------|
| 128K | TTFT (non-quantized) | OOM | 45.9s |
| 256K | TTFT (non-quantized) | OOM | 22.3s |
| 1024K | TTFT (non-quantized) | OOM | 10.8s |
| 256K | TTFT (quantized) | OOM | 46.1s |
| 1024K | TTFT (quantized) | OOM | 10.9s |

**Critical Observation**: On the 5090, Qwen3-8B hits OOM at **128K non-quantized** and **256K quantized**. MiniCPM-SALA processes **1024K** on both configurations. This is the "democratization" claim from Section 4.4 — enabling 1M-token processing on consumer hardware where full-attention models cannot function.

**Assessment**: The efficiency results **strongly support** the paper's claims. The 3.5× speedup is directly measured, and the OOM avoidance is unambiguous — one model fails, the other succeeds. However, readers should note:

- **TTFT only measures prefilling**: The 1K token decoding is included in end-to-end but not isolated. For applications requiring long generation (not just long input), the decoding efficiency may differ.
- **Single-batch evaluation**: Results are for batch size 1. Multi-batch throughput is not reported.
- **No cost analysis**: While speedup is measured, the paper does not report dollar costs per inference or training, which would be more actionable for deployment decisions.

### 5.3 Critical Assessment of Experimental Coverage

#### Strengths of the Evaluation

1. **Comprehensive benchmark coverage**: The three-tier evaluation (standard, long-context, ultra-long) thoroughly tests the model's claimed capabilities across different dimensions.

2. **Appropriate baselines**: Selecting models of comparable parameter scale (7B–9B) ensures fair comparison. Including both full-attention (Qwen3) and hybrid (Nemotron, Falcon) baselines provides context for the architecture's relative position.

3. **Multiple hardware configurations**: Testing on both A6000D (cloud) and 5090 (consumer) demonstrates practical deployment flexibility.

4. **Quantization evaluation**: Including both non-quantized and INT4 quantized results provides actionable information for deployment optimization.

5. **Extrapolation testing**: Evaluating beyond training context length (520K train → 2048K test) tests genuine generalization rather than memorization.

#### Gaps and Limitations

**1. No Ablation Studies**

This is the **most significant gap** in the experimental design. The paper does not isolate the contribution of individual components:

- What is the performance impact of the 1:3 ratio vs. 1:1 or 1:4?
- How much does HyPE contribute vs. standard RoPE everywhere?
- What is the effect of output gates on stability?
- How much does the NoPE configuration in sparse layers contribute to extrapolation?

The paper attributes the length extrapolation to "NoPE configuration within the sparse attention layers" (Section 3.1) but provides **no ablation evidence** for this claim. Readers must accept this as a hypothesis rather than a proven mechanism.

**2. Missing Training Ablation**

The five-stage training pipeline (Table 1) is presented as a complete system, but the paper does not test:

- What happens if Stage 1 (HALO conversion) is skipped?
- Is Long-Decay Training (Stage 4) necessary, or could the model learn long-context from SFT alone?
- How sensitive is the model to the token counts at each stage?

Without these ablations, readers cannot determine which training stages are essential vs. optional.

**3. Limited Failure Case Analysis**

The paper reports successes but does not systematically analyze failures:

- On MRCR, MiniCPM-SALA underperforms at 64K-8N and 128K-8N (Table 3). Why?
- On IFEval, the model scores 76.34 vs. Qwen3's 84.66. What types of instructions does it fail to follow?
- Are there specific task types where the hybrid architecture is disadvantaged?

Understanding failure modes is critical for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this architecture.

**4. Reproducibility Gaps**

Several details necessary for reproduction are missing:

- **Number of GPUs**: Batch sizes are given in tokens (e.g., 7.8M tokens) but the GPU count is not specified.
- **Training time**: The paper reports token counts but not wall-clock training time for each stage.
- **Layer count**: The total number of transformer layers is not stated, making it impossible to calculate exactly how many layers are sparse vs. linear.
- **Layer selection algorithm**: Referenced as from Chen et. al. (2026) but not detailed here.

**5. Baseline Evaluation Consistency**

The Qwen3-Next-80B results in Table 4 are cited from official documentation (marked with ∗). While this is acceptable, readers should note:

- Evaluation conditions may differ (different prompt formats, scoring methods, etc.)
- The Qwen3-8B baseline in Tables 2-3 may have been evaluated under different conditions than Qwen3-Next-80B in Table 4

**6. No Memory Usage Quantification**

While the paper claims memory efficiency and demonstrates OOM avoidance, it does not report:

- Actual VRAM usage at different context lengths
- KV-Cache size in GB for MiniCPM-SALA vs. Qwen3-8B
- Memory savings percentage

This would strengthen the efficiency claims with concrete numbers.

#### Conditional Results and Trade-offs

The experimental results reveal several **conditional advantages** — the hybrid architecture excels in some scenarios but not others:

| Scenario | MiniCPM-SALA Advantage | Condition |
|----------|----------------------|-----------|
| Math reasoning | +10–11 points on AIME | Consistent across AIME24/25 |
| Code generation | +1–8 points on HumanEval/MBPP | Consistent |
| Instruction following | -8 points on IFEval | Degraded |
| Long-context retrieval | +12–19 points on RULER/NoLiMa | At 64K–128K |
| Multi-document comprehension | Mixed on MRCR | Advantage at 2N/4N, disadvantage at 8N |
| Inference speed | 3.5× at 256K | Only at long contexts |
| Consumer hardware | 1M tokens on 5090 | Qwen3 fails at 128K–256K |

**Key Trade-off**: The hybrid architecture provides substantial long-context and efficiency advantages but shows **modest degradation** in instruction following (IFEval) and general knowledge (MMLU-Pro). Practitioners must weigh whether their use case prioritizes long-context processing over instruction adherence.

**Another Trade-off**: The model excels at moderate context expansion (MRCR 2N/4N) but shows reduced advantage at extreme expansion (MRCR 8N). This suggests the architecture has **scaling limits** — it handles long contexts well but not infinitely long dense information.

### 5.4 Overall Assessment: Do Experiments Support the Claims?

The paper makes four primary claims in the Abstract and Introduction. Here is an assessment of whether the experiments support each:

| Claim | Evidence | Assessment |
|-------|----------|------------|
| **Hybrid architecture matches full-attention performance** | Table 2: Average 76.53 vs. Qwen3's 73.45 | **Strongly supported** — exceeds baseline on average |
| **75% training cost reduction** | Section 2.2: 2T tokens vs. 8T for MiniCPM-4.0 | **Supported** — token counts are explicit, though compute-hours not reported |
| **3.5× inference speedup at 256K** | Figure 2(a): 51.6s vs. 180.8s TTFT | **Strongly supported** — directly measured |
| **1M token support on single GPU** | Figure 2–3: OOM avoidance at 512K–1024K | **Strongly supported** — qualitative capability difference |
| **Length extrapolation to 2048K without YaRN** | Table 4: 81.6 score at 2048K | **Supported** — but attribution to NoPE lacks ablation evidence |

**Overall Verdict**: The experiments **convincingly support** the paper's primary claims regarding efficiency, long-context capability, and training cost reduction. The standard benchmark results validate that capability is preserved despite architectural changes.

**Caveats**:
1. The lack of ablation studies means the **mechanism** behind success (e.g., why NoPE enables extrapolation) is not rigorously proven.
2. Some capabilities (IFEval, MMLU-Pro) show **modest degradation** that the paper does not emphasize.
3. Reproducibility is limited by missing implementation details (GPU count, layer count, selection algorithm).

**For Practitioners**: The experimental evidence is sufficient to justify adoption for **long-context applications** where efficiency and context length are priorities. For applications requiring strong instruction following or dense multi-document reasoning at extreme complexity, the MRCR and IFEval results suggest **cautious evaluation** is warranted before deployment.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

No architectural innovation comes without costs. This section critically examines the assumptions, constraints, and unresolved questions surrounding MiniCPM-SALA. Understanding these limitations is essential for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this architecture and for researchers identifying open problems. We organize the analysis into four categories: **capability trade-offs**, **architectural assumptions**, **evaluation gaps**, and **deployment constraints**.

### 6.1 Capability Trade-offs: What Is Sacrificed for Efficiency?

The paper emphasizes that MiniCPM-SALA "maintains general capabilities comparable to full-attention models" (Abstract). However, a closer examination of Table 2 reveals **non-uniform capability retention** — some capabilities are enhanced while others show measurable degradation.

#### Instruction Following Degradation

The most significant capability gap appears on **IFEval** (Instruction Following Evaluation):

| Model | IFEval Score | Difference |
|-------|--------------|------------|
| Qwen3-8B | 84.66 | — |
| MiniCPM-SALA | 76.34 | **-8.32** |

This 8.32-point drop represents the **largest single-benchmark degradation** in Table 2. The paper does not explicitly discuss this weakness in the Results section (Section 3.1), instead emphasizing average score improvements.

**Why This Matters**: Instruction following is critical for production applications where models must adhere to specific output formats, constraints, or behavioral guidelines. A model that excels at long-context retrieval but struggles to follow formatting instructions may require additional post-processing or prompt engineering.

**Possible Causes** (not addressed in paper):
- The hybrid attention pattern may disrupt fine-grained instruction parsing
- The conversion process (Section 2.2) may not fully preserve instruction-tuning knowledge
- Output gates, while improving stability, may introduce variability in response generation

**Practical Implication**: Teams deploying MiniCPM-SALA for instruction-heavy tasks (e.g., structured data extraction, API call generation) should conduct **task-specific evaluation** before committing to the architecture.

#### General Knowledge Gap on Challenging Benchmarks

**MMLU-Pro** shows a 6.22-point gap:

| Model | MMLU-Pro Score | Difference |
|-------|----------------|------------|
| Qwen3-8B | 73.26 | — |
| MiniCPM-SALA | 67.04 | **-6.22** |

MMLU-Pro tests "more robust and challenging multi-task language understanding" (Wang et al., 2024b). The degradation suggests that while the model preserves **specialized capabilities** (math, code), some **general knowledge** may be affected by the architectural conversion.

**Contrast with CMMLU**: On CMMLU (Chinese multitask understanding), the gap is minimal:

| Model | CMMLU Score | Difference |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| Qwen3-8B | 81.68 | — |
| MiniCPM-SALA | 81.55 | **-0.13** |

This discrepancy suggests the conversion process may affect **English-language knowledge** more than Chinese-language knowledge, possibly due to training data composition differences. The paper does not analyze this pattern.

#### Task-Dependent Long-Context Performance

While MiniCPM-SALA excels on RULER and NoLiMa, the **MRCR benchmark** (multi-document comprehension) reveals conditional limitations:

| Context Configuration | Qwen3-8B | MiniCPM-SALA | Difference |
|----------------------|----------|--------------|------------|
| 64K-2N | 29.20 | 29.77 | +0.57 |
| 64K-4N | 21.56 | 20.57 | **-0.99** |
| 64K-8N | 17.82 | 16.56 | **-1.26** |
| 128K-2N | 26.50 | 28.62 | +2.12 |
| 128K-4N | 14.75 | 19.62 | +4.87 |
| 128K-8N | 12.15 | 10.12 | **-2.03** |

**Critical Pattern**: MiniCPM-SALA underperforms at the **8N complexity multiplier** across both context lengths. This suggests the hybrid architecture handles **moderate information density** well but struggles when many documents must be simultaneously tracked and cross-referenced.

**Why This Matters**: Applications requiring **extreme multi-document reasoning** (e.g., legal case analysis across hundreds of documents, scientific literature reviews) may not benefit from the hybrid architecture at maximum complexity. The 1:3 sparse-to-linear ratio may not provide enough "anchor points" for dense cross-document retrieval.

**Unanswered Question**: The paper does not analyze *which types* of MRCR tasks fail at 8N. Is it retrieval failure, reasoning failure, or attention dilution? Without this analysis, practitioners cannot predict failure modes for their specific use cases.

### 6.2 Architectural Assumptions: What Must Be True for This to Work?

MiniCPM-SALA's success relies on several **implicit assumptions** that are not rigorously tested in this paper. Understanding these assumptions is critical for assessing whether the approach generalizes to other model families or scales.

#### Assumption 1: Pre-trained Weights Are Convertible Without Catastrophic Forgetting

The entire training paradigm (Section 2.2) assumes that pre-trained Transformer weights can be adapted to hybrid attention patterns through continual training. This works for MiniCPM-4.0 → MiniCPM-SALA, but:

- **Scale dependency**: Does this conversion work at 70B+ parameters? The paper only demonstrates 9B.
- **Architecture dependency**: Does this work for models with different FFN structures (e.g., MoE, SwiGLU variants)?
- **Training data dependency**: MiniCPM-4.0 was trained on 7T tokens. Models trained on different data distributions may not convert as cleanly.

**Evidence Gap**: The paper provides no evidence that this conversion paradigm generalizes beyond the MiniCPM family. Teams working with Llama, Qwen, or other model families cannot assume similar success rates.

#### Assumption 2: The 1:3 Ratio Is Near-Optimal

The paper states the 1:3 sparse-to-linear ratio derives from "architectural designs of recent representative studies" and "internal small-scale preliminary experiments" (Section 2.1). However:

- **No ablation**: The paper does not test 1:1, 1:2, 1:4, or other ratios.
- **Task dependency**: The optimal ratio may vary by application. Math-heavy tasks might benefit from more sparse layers; retrieval-heavy tasks might need different configurations.
- **Scale dependency**: The optimal ratio at 9B parameters may not hold at 70B or 700B.

**Risk**: Teams adopting this architecture without ratio tuning may leave performance on the table. The 1:3 ratio is a **heuristic**, not a proven optimum.

#### Assumption 3: NoPE in Sparse Layers Enables Extrapolation

The paper attributes length extrapolation to 2048K tokens to the "NoPE configuration within the sparse attention layers" (Section 3.1):

> "The length extrapolation capabilities of MiniCPM-SALA can be attributed to the NoPE configuration within the sparse attention layers. In this design, the stored KV-Cache does not require combination with positional information, which can otherwise hinder the capture of long-range dependencies."

**Critical Gap**: This is a **hypothesis without ablation evidence**. The paper does not test:
- Sparse layers *with* RoPE vs. *without* RoPE
- Whether HyPE alone enables extrapolation
- Whether linear attention layers' RoPE is sufficient for position awareness

**Alternative Explanations** (not ruled out):
- The continual training pipeline itself enables extrapolation
- Lightning Attention's inherent properties enable length generalization
- The 520K training context (Table 1, Stage 4) is sufficient for 2048K extrapolation regardless of positional encoding

**Practical Risk**: Teams implementing MiniCPM-SALA may remove RoPE from sparse layers based on this claim, only to find their models do not extrapolate — because the true enabling factor was something else (e.g., training data, optimization schedule).

#### Assumption 4: First and Last Layers Must Remain Unconverted

Section 2.2 states: "we keep the first and last layers unconverted to improve training stability." This is presented as a design choice without experimental justification.

**Unanswered Questions**:
- What happens if *only* the first layer is preserved?
- What happens if *only* the last layer is preserved?
- Does this constraint limit architectural flexibility for downstream adaptation?

**Implication**: This constraint reduces the effective hybridization ratio. If a model has 40 layers and 2 must remain standard attention, only 38 layers can be hybridized — slightly altering the intended 1:3 ratio.

### 6.3 Evaluation Gaps: What Was Not Tested?

The experimental design (Section 5) is comprehensive but leaves several critical questions unanswered. These gaps limit confidence in the architecture's robustness across diverse scenarios.

#### No Ablation Studies

This is the **most significant methodological limitation**. The paper presents a complete system but does not isolate component contributions:

| Component | Claimed Benefit | Ablation Evidence |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| 1:3 ratio | Balance throughput and precision | None |
| HyPE | Harmonize short/long contexts | None |
| Output gates | Improve stability, prevent attention sink | None |
| QK-Normalization | Prevent activation spikes | None |
| NoPE in sparse layers | Enable length extrapolation | None |
| 5-stage training | Preserve capabilities, reduce cost | None |

**Why This Matters**: Without ablations, practitioners cannot:
- Determine which components are essential vs. optional
- Optimize the architecture for their specific constraints (e.g., remove output gates if latency is critical)
- Understand failure modes when components are modified

**Comparison to Prior Work**: Chen et al. (2026), which introduced HyPE and the layer selection algorithm, may contain ablations — but this paper does not reproduce or reference them. Readers must consult multiple papers to understand component contributions.

#### Missing Memory Quantification

The paper claims memory efficiency and demonstrates OOM avoidance (Figures 2–3), but does not report:

- **Actual VRAM usage** at different context lengths (e.g., "MiniCPM-SALA uses 45GB at 256K vs. Qwen3's 89GB")
- **KV-Cache size** in GB for each architecture
- **Memory savings percentage** (e.g., "60% reduction in KV-Cache memory")

**Why This Matters**: Practitioners need concrete numbers for capacity planning. Knowing that a model "does not OOM" is less actionable than knowing it "uses 52GB at 512K, leaving 44GB headroom on a 96GB GPU."

**Missed Opportunity**: The paper could have included a simple table:

| Context Length | Qwen3-8B VRAM | MiniCPM-SALA VRAM | Savings |
|----------------|---------------|-------------------|---------|
| 256K | [Not reported] | [Not reported] | [Not reported] |
| 512K | OOM | [Not reported] | N/A |
| 1024K | OOM | [Not reported] | N/A |

This omission weakens the efficiency claims despite strong qualitative evidence (OOM avoidance).

#### Single-Batch Evaluation Only

All inference results (Figures 2–3) are for **batch size 1**:

> "For each tested sequence length, the models process a specified input (prefilling) and generate 1K tokens (decoding)." (Figure 2 caption)

**Missing Metrics**:
- **Throughput** (tokens/second) at batch sizes 4, 8, 16, 32
- **Multi-user latency** under concurrent requests
- **GPU utilization** during prefilling and decoding

**Why This Matters**: Production systems rarely serve single requests. A model that is 3.5× faster at batch size 1 may only be 1.5× faster at batch size 16 due to different bottlenecks (memory bandwidth vs. compute).

**Risk**: Teams deploying MiniCPM-SALA for high-throughput applications cannot extrapolate from the reported results to their expected workload.

#### Limited Failure Case Analysis

The paper reports successes but does not systematically analyze **where the model fails**:

- **MRCR 8N underperformance**: Why does the model struggle at extreme document complexity?
- **IFEval degradation**: What instruction types does the model fail to follow?
- **MMLU-Pro gap**: Which knowledge domains show the largest degradation?

**Example of Missing Analysis**: The paper could have included:

> "On IFEval, MiniCPM-SALA shows particular weakness in [constraint type] instructions, failing 34% of the time vs. Qwen3's 18%."

Without this, practitioners cannot anticipate failure modes.

#### Baseline Evaluation Inconsistency

Table 4 includes Qwen3-Next-80B results marked with ∗, indicating they are "cited from the official Qwen3-Next documentation" (Table 4 caption). This introduces potential **evaluation condition differences**:

- Different prompt formats
- Different scoring methods
- Different hardware or software stacks

**Why This Matters**: The claim that "MiniCPM-SALA (9B) outperforms Qwen3-Next-80B at 1000K tokens" (86.3 vs. 80.3) is compelling but not apples-to-apples. The actual gap could be smaller or larger under consistent evaluation conditions.

### 6.4 Deployment Constraints: Practical Limitations for Adoption

Beyond architectural and evaluation limitations, several **practical constraints** affect real-world deployment.

#### Dependency on MiniCPM-4.0 Checkpoint

The training pipeline (Section 2.2, Table 1) starts from "an intermediate checkpoint of MiniCPM-4.0... which has already been trained on 7T tokens." This creates a **checkpoint dependency**:

- Teams without access to MiniCPM-4.0 weights cannot replicate the training pipeline
- Converting other model families (Llama, Qwen, Mistral) requires validating the conversion paradigm for each
- The 75% cost reduction claim applies only to the MiniCPM family, not universally

**Implication**: The training cost savings are **not transferable** to teams working with different base models. A team starting from Llama-3-8B would need to validate whether HALO conversion works similarly.

#### Sparse Attention Disabled at Short Contexts

Table 1 shows sparse attention is **disabled** during Stages 1–3 (up to 4K sequence length) and only enabled at Stage 4 (32K+):

| Stage | Sequence Length | Sparse Attention |
|-------|-----------------|------------------|
| Architecture Conversion | 0.5K | Disabled |
| Continual Stable-Training | 4K | Disabled |
| Short-Decay Training | 4K | Disabled |
| Long-Decay Training | 32K–520K | Enabled |
| SFT | 64K–140K | Enabled |

**Operational Implication**: The model's sparse attention capabilities are only activated at **long context lengths**. For applications primarily using short contexts (<32K), the hybrid architecture provides limited benefit over standard linear attention.

**Unanswered Question**: Does the model gracefully fall back to efficient computation at short contexts, or does it incur overhead from the hybrid infrastructure even when sparse attention is not actively used?

#### Hardware-Specific Efficiency Claims

The efficiency results are tied to specific GPUs:

- **NVIDIA A6000D** (96GB VRAM): Figure 2
- **NVIDIA RTX 5090** (32GB VRAM): Figure 3

**Missing Information**:
- Performance on other GPUs (A100, H100, consumer RTX 4090)
- Performance on non-NVIDIA hardware (AMD, Intel)
- Performance on edge devices (Jetson, mobile GPUs)

**Risk**: The "3.5× speedup at 256K" claim may not hold on different hardware due to:
- Memory bandwidth differences
- Tensor core availability
- Kernel optimization for specific architectures

**Recommendation**: Teams should benchmark on their target hardware before committing to deployment.

#### Quantization Dependency for Maximum Efficiency

Figure 2 shows dramatic efficiency gains from INT4 quantization:

| Context Length | TTFT Non-Quantized | TTFT INT4 Quantized | Improvement |
|----------------|-------------------|---------------------|-------------|
| 1024K (A6000D) | 250.3s | 12.6s | **19.9×** |

**Implication**: The full efficiency potential of MiniCPM-SALA is only realized with **quantization**. Teams unable to use quantization (due to quality concerns, hardware limitations, or regulatory requirements) will see more modest gains.

**Unaddressed Question**: The paper does not report **quality degradation from quantization**. Does INT4 quantization reduce benchmark scores? By how much? Without this, teams cannot weigh the speed/quality trade-off.

### 6.6 Summary: When to Use (and Not Use) MiniCPM-SALA

Based on the limitations identified, here is guidance for practitioners:

#### Recommended Use Cases

- **Ultra-long context retrieval** (100K–1M tokens) where full-attention models OOM
- **Math and code reasoning** over long documents (AIME, HumanEval performance is strong)
- **Consumer hardware deployment** where 32GB VRAM must handle 1M tokens
- **Cost-sensitive training** where 75% reduction vs. from-scratch is valuable

#### Use Cases Requiring Caution

- **Instruction-heavy applications** (IFEval degradation suggests potential issues)
- **Extreme multi-document reasoning** (MRCR 8N underperformance)
- **High-throughput serving** (batch size >1 not evaluated)
- **Non-MiniCPM base models** (conversion paradigm not validated for other families)

#### Not Recommended

- **Short-context applications** (<32K) where sparse attention provides no benefit
- **Applications requiring guaranteed instruction following** without task-specific validation
- **Teams unable to use quantization** (efficiency gains are significantly reduced)

**Final Assessment**: MiniCPM-SALA represents a **significant advance** for long-context efficiency, but it is not a universal replacement for full-attention architectures. The trade-offs are real and measurable. Teams should adopt it with clear understanding of where it excels and where it may underperform — and should conduct task-specific evaluation before production deployment.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

MiniCPM-SALA does not merely present a new model; it validates a new **design space** for efficient large language models. By demonstrating that hybrid sparse-linear architectures can match full-attention performance while drastically reducing costs, this work shifts the field's focus from "choosing between efficiency and quality" to "engineering the optimal hybrid balance." This section explores how this work changes the architectural landscape, what research questions it opens, where it should be deployed, and how practitioners can integrate these findings.

### 7.1 Shifting the Architectural Paradigm

For several years, the field operated under an implicit assumption: **efficient attention mechanisms require performance trade-offs.** Linear attention offered $O(N)$ complexity but suffered from "lossy compression" of context (Introduction, paragraph 3). Sparse attention reduced compute but retained dense memory storage (Introduction, paragraph 3). MiniCPM-SALA challenges this dichotomy by proving that **hybridization can break the trade-off** at scale.

#### Validating Hybrid Architectures as First-Class Citizens
Prior to this work, hybrid models were often viewed as niche optimizations for specific tasks. MiniCPM-SALA demonstrates that a hybrid model can achieve an **average standard benchmark score of 76.53** (Table 2), exceeding full-attention baselines like Qwen3-8B (73.45). This result signals to the community that hybrid architectures are no longer "second-best" alternatives but viable primary architectures for general-purpose models.

The implication is a shift in **model design priorities**. Instead of defaulting to full attention and optimizing later, future model families may start with hybrid designs as the baseline. The success of the **1:3 sparse-to-linear ratio** (Section 3.4) suggests that most layers can be efficient (linear) without sacrificing the precision provided by strategic sparse "anchor" layers.

#### Democratizing Model Development via Conversion
Perhaps the most significant methodological implication is the **Transformer-to-Hybrid conversion paradigm**. Traditionally, adopting a new architecture (e.g., switching from Transformer to Mamba) required **training from scratch**, costing millions of dollars and massive compute clusters. MiniCPM-SALA shows that existing pre-trained models can be converted via continual training with **75% cost reduction** (2T tokens vs. 8T tokens for MiniCPM-4.0, Section 3.4).

This lowers the barrier to entry for efficient architecture research. Teams without resources for full pre-training can now:
1.  Take an existing open-weight Transformer (e.g., Llama, Qwen).
2.  Apply the HALO conversion framework (Section 3.4, Stage 1).
3.  Continue training to adapt the hybrid layers.

This transforms architecture innovation from a **capital-intensive endeavor** to a **compute-efficient adaptation task**, potentially accelerating the pace of architectural experimentation in the open-source community.

#### Edge AI and Consumer Hardware Viability
The efficiency results (Figure 3) demonstrate that ultra-long context is no longer exclusive to cloud clusters. By enabling **1M-token processing on an RTX 5090 (32GB VRAM)** where full-attention models fail at 128K–256K, MiniCPM-SALA validates **consumer-grade hardware** for information-intensive tasks.

This has profound implications for **privacy and latency**:
-   **Privacy**: Sensitive long documents (legal, medical) can be processed locally without sending data to cloud APIs.
-   **Latency**: Edge devices avoid network round-trips, enabling real-time interaction with massive contexts.

The work suggests a future where **model size is less critical than architecture efficiency**. A 9B hybrid model outperforming an 80B full-attention model at 1000K tokens (Table 4) implies that scaling parameters is not the only path to better long-context performance.

### 7.2 Enabling New Research Avenues

MiniCPM-SALA answers some questions but opens many others. The lack of ablation studies (Section 6.3) and specific architectural choices create clear opportunities for follow-up research.

#### Optimizing the Hybrid Ratio
The paper employs a **1:3 sparse-to-linear ratio** based on "internal small-scale preliminary experiments" (Section 3.4). However, this ratio is likely not universal. Future research should investigate:
-   **Task-Specific Ratios**: Does math reasoning benefit from more sparse layers (e.g., 1:1)? Does summarization work better with more linear layers (e.g., 1:4)?
-   **Scale-Dependent Ratios**: Does the optimal ratio change as model size increases from 9B to 70B?
-   **Dynamic Ratios**: Can the model learn to adjust the sparse/linear balance during inference based on context complexity?

#### Validating the NoPE Hypothesis
The paper attributes length extrapolation to 2048K tokens to the **NoPE (No Positional Encoding)** configuration in sparse attention layers (Section 3.1). However, this is a **hypothesis without ablation evidence** (Section 6.3). Follow-up work should:
-   Train identical models with RoPE vs. NoPE in sparse layers to isolate the effect.
-   Investigate *why* NoPE helps. Is it preventing rotational decay, or does it simplify the attention landscape for retrieval?
-   Explore alternative positional encodings (e.g., ALiBi, YaRN) in the hybrid context to see if they outperform HyPE.

#### Scaling Laws for Hybrid Models
Current scaling laws (e.g., Chinchilla) are derived from full-attention Transformers. It is unknown if these laws hold for hybrid architectures. Key questions include:
-   **Data Efficiency**: Do hybrid models require less data to reach convergence due to efficient context usage?
-   **Compute-Optimal Scaling**: What is the optimal balance of parameters vs. context length for hybrid models?
-   **Cross-Family Conversion**: Does the HALO conversion work equally well for non-MiniCPM families (e.g., Llama-3, Mistral)? The current work relies on MiniCPM-4.0 checkpoints (Section 3.4), creating a dependency that needs validation across other base models.

#### Memory Quantification and Optimization
While the paper demonstrates OOM avoidance (Figure 2, Figure 3), it does not report **exact VRAM usage** (Section 6.3). Future work should:
-   Measure KV-Cache size in GB for hybrid vs. full attention at fixed context lengths.
-   Develop **hybrid-aware kernels** that further optimize memory access patterns for the interleaved sparse-linear structure.
-   Explore **mixed-precision strategies** where sparse layers use higher precision than linear layers to maximize quality/efficiency trade-offs.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Deployment Scenarios

Based on the performance profile (Table 2, Table 3) and efficiency metrics (Figure 2, Figure 3), MiniCPM-SALA is best suited for specific application classes. Practitioners should match the model's strengths to their use cases.

#### Recommended Use Cases

| Application | Why MiniCPM-SALA Fits | Evidence |
|-------------|-----------------------|----------|
| **Local RAG Systems** | Processes entire document corpora (100K+ tokens) on consumer GPUs without cloud dependency. | 1M token support on RTX 5090 (Figure 3). |
| **Codebase Analysis** | Understands repository-scale dependency trees and long files without truncation. | Strong HumanEval (95.12) and LCB scores (Table 2). |
| **Legal/Medical Review** | Handles long contracts or patient histories privately on edge devices. | High performance on long-context retrieval (RULER 128K: 89.37, Table 3). |
| **Cost-Sensitive Inference** | Reduces GPU hours and memory requirements for high-volume services. | 3.5× speedup at 256K tokens (Figure 2). |
| **Math & Reasoning Tasks** | Demonstrates superior performance on complex reasoning benchmarks. | AIME24/25 scores exceed Qwen3-8B by 10+ points (Table 2). |

#### Use Cases Requiring Caution

| Application | Risk Factor | Mitigation |
|-------------|-------------|------------|
| **Strict Instruction Following** | IFEval score degradation (-8.32 vs. Qwen3) suggests potential issues with formatting constraints. | Add post-processing validation or few-shot examples for format compliance. |
| **Extreme Multi-Document Reasoning** | MRCR 8N performance drops relative to baselines (Table 3). | Chunk documents or use hierarchical retrieval before feeding to the model. |
| **Short-Context Only (<4K)** | Sparse attention benefits only manifest at longer sequences; overhead may not be justified. | Consider smaller full-attention models for purely short-context tasks. |
| **High-Throughput Serving** | Evaluation limited to batch size 1; multi-batch throughput unknown. | Benchmark specifically on target hardware with concurrent requests before deployment. |

#### Integration Strategy
For teams integrating this architecture:
1.  **Quantization is Key**: The efficiency gains are maximized with INT4 quantization (Figure 2 shows 20× TTFT improvement at 1024K with quantization). Ensure your inference stack supports GPTQ or AWQ for hybrid layers.
2.  **Context-Aware Routing**: Implement a router that directs short queries (<4K) to a cheaper model and long queries (>32K) to MiniCPM-SALA to optimize cost.
3.  **Monitor Memory Headroom**: While OOM is avoided, memory usage still grows with context. Monitor VRAM usage at your target context length (e.g., 128K vs. 1M) to ensure stability.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Adoption Guidance

For researchers and engineers looking to reproduce or build upon this work, the following guidance addresses the gaps identified in Section 6.

#### Prerequisites for Reproduction
-   **Base Checkpoint**: The training pipeline requires access to **MiniCPM-4.0 intermediate checkpoints** (Section 3.4). Without this specific starting point, the 75% cost reduction claim cannot be replicated. Teams using other base models must validate the conversion stability themselves.
-   **Hardware**: Training requires significant GPU resources despite the cost reduction. The 2T token training volume (Table 1) still represents substantial compute. Inference can be done on consumer hardware (RTX 5090), but training likely requires cloud-grade clusters.
-   **Code Dependencies**: The implementation relies on **InfLLM-V2** and **Lightning Attention** kernels. Ensure these are compatible with your inference engine (e.g., vLLM, TGI). Custom kernels may be needed for the hybrid layer interleaving.

#### Critical Implementation Details
-   **Layer Selection**: The paper references **Chen et al. (2026)** for the layer selection algorithm (Section 3.4). Reproducers must consult this cited work to determine *which* layers become sparse vs. linear, as this is not detailed in the current paper.
-   **Positional Encoding**: Strictly follow the **HyPE** design: RoPE on linear layers, NoPE on sparse layers (Section 3.4). Deviating from this (e.g., applying RoPE everywhere) may break the length extrapolation capability.
-   **Training Schedule**: Adhere to the **5-stage pipeline** (Table 1). Skipping stages (e.g., jumping straight to long-context training) may destabilize the model. The gradual context extension (4K → 32K → 160K → 520K) is critical for stability.

#### When to Prefer This Method
Choose MiniCPM-SALA over alternatives when:
-   **Context Length > 32K**: The efficiency benefits outweigh the architectural complexity.
-   **Memory Constrained**: You need to fit large contexts on single GPUs (e.g., <96GB VRAM).
-   **Budget Limited**: You cannot afford full pre-training but have access to a strong base model for conversion.

Choose **Full-Attention Models** (e.g., Qwen3-8B) when:
-   **Context Length < 8K**: The overhead of hybrid architecture may not provide benefits.
-   **Instruction Following is Critical**: The IFEval gap suggests full-attention models may be more reliable for strict formatting.
-   **Reproducibility is Paramount**: Full-attention models have more mature tooling and fewer architectural dependencies.

#### Final Verdict
MiniCPM-SALA represents a **maturity milestone** for efficient attention mechanisms. It moves hybrid architectures from theoretical promise to practical reality. While limitations exist (instruction following, lack of ablations), the core contribution — proving that efficiency and performance can coexist at scale — provides a robust foundation for the next generation of long-context models. For practitioners, it offers a viable path to deploying 1M-token contexts on hardware that was previously incapable of such tasks, fundamentally expanding the scope of what is possible in edge and cost-sensitive AI applications.
