## 1. Executive Summary
This paper introduces Group Sequence Policy Optimization (GSPO), a reinforcement learning algorithm designed to resolve the catastrophic stability issues and irreversible model collapse often encountered when scaling prior methods like Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) on gigantic language models. By shifting from unstable token-level importance sampling to sequence-level likelihood ratios and clipping, GSPO aligns the optimization objective with sequence-level rewards, significantly reducing training variance. Empirical results on a Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base model demonstrate that GSPO achieves superior efficiency and performance on benchmarks like AIME'24 and LiveCodeBench while inherently stabilizing Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) training without requiring complex strategies like Routing Replay.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the necessity of Group Sequence Policy Optimization (GSPO), we must first examine the evolving landscape of Reinforcement Learning (RL) for Large Language Models (LLMs) and the specific theoretical bottlenecks that emerge when scaling these systems.

### The Scaling Challenge in RL for LLMs
Reinforcement Learning has become the dominant paradigm for enhancing LLM capabilities in complex domains such as competition-level mathematics and programming (Section 1). The core idea is to allow models to undertake deeper, longer reasoning processes. However, scaling RL is not merely a matter of adding more compute. The foremost prerequisite is maintaining **stable and robust training dynamics**.

The paper identifies a critical gap: current state-of-the-art algorithms, specifically **Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)**, exhibit severe stability issues when applied to gigantic language models. The authors note that this instability often results in **catastrophic and irreversible model collapse** (Section 1). Once a model collapses under GRPO, resuming training is unavailing, even when reverting to previous checkpoints or tuning hyperparameters. This creates a hard ceiling on how large or complex a model can be while using standard RL fine-tuning techniques.

### Prior Approaches: PPO and GRPO
To appreciate the novelty of GSPO, we must understand the mechanisms of the algorithms it seeks to improve. The paper outlines two primary predecessors in Section 2:

1.  **Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO):**
    PPO is the standard baseline for RL alignment. It constrains policy updates using a clipping mechanism based on **token-level importance ratios**. For a token $y_t$ at position $t$, the importance ratio is defined as:
    $$
    w_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{<t})}{\pi_{\theta_{old}}(y_t|x, y_{<t})}
    $$
    Here, $\pi_\theta$ is the current policy and $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$ is the policy used to generate the data. PPO relies heavily on a **value model** (critic) to estimate the advantage $\hat{A}_t$ for each token.
    *   **Limitation:** The value model usually matches the policy model in size, introducing a considerable memory and computational burden. Furthermore, acquiring a reliable value estimate for long responses is inherently challenging (Section 2).

2.  **Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO):**
    GRPO was introduced to bypass the value model burden. Instead of learning a value function, it computes the relative advantage of each response within a **group** of $G$ responses to the same query $x$. The advantage $\hat{A}_i$ for a response $y_i$ is normalized based on the rewards of the group:
    $$
    \hat{A}_i = \frac{r(x, y_i) - \text{mean}(\{r(x, y_i)\}_{i=1}^G)}{\text{std}(\{r(x, y_i)\}_{i=1}^G)}
    $$
    *   **The Critical Flaw:** While GRPO removes the value model, it retains the **token-level importance ratio** $w_{i,t}(\theta)$ from PPO (Section 2, Equation 3). This design choice is the root cause of the instability described in the paper.

### The Theoretical Gap: Misapplication of Importance Sampling
The core motivation for GSPO lies in a theoretical inconsistency within GRPO's objective function, detailed in Section 3.

**The Principle of Importance Sampling:**
Importance sampling is a statistical technique used to estimate the expectation of a function $f$ under a target distribution $\pi_{tar}$ using samples from a behavior distribution $\pi_{beh}$:
$$
E_{z \sim \pi_{tar}}[f(z)] = E_{z \sim \pi_{beh}}\left[ \frac{\pi_{tar}(z)}{\pi_{beh}(z)} f(z) \right]
$$
Crucially, this equality relies on averaging over **multiple samples** ($N \gg 1$) from the behavior distribution for the importance weight $\frac{\pi_{tar}(z)}{\pi_{beh}(z)}$ to effectively correct for the distributional mismatch.

**The GRPO Failure Mode:**
GRPO applies the importance weight at **each token position** $t$. However, in language generation, there is only **one single sample** $y_{i,t}$ drawn from the next-token distribution $\pi_{\theta_{old}}(\cdot|x, y_{i,<t})$ at that position.
*   **Why this matters:** Applying an importance weight based on a single sample fails to perform the intended distribution correction. Instead, it introduces **high-variance noise** into the training gradients (Section 3).
*   **Accumulation:** This noise accumulates over long sequences. As the response length increases, the variance compounds, eventually precipitating model collapse.
*   **Clipping Exacerbation:** The clipping mechanism, intended to stabilize training, actually exacerbates this noise when the underlying importance weights are statistically invalid.

### The Complexity of Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)
The motivation for GSPO is further strengthened by the challenges posed by **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)** architectures. MoE models activate only a subset of parameters (experts) for each token, making them efficient but unstable during RL.

*   **Expert-Activation Volatility:** In MoE models, the specific experts activated for the same response can change significantly between the old policy $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$ and the current policy $\pi_\theta$. The paper notes that for a 48-layer Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base model, roughly **10% of the experts activated** under the new policy differ from the old policy after each update (Section 5.3).
*   **Impact on GRPO:** This volatility causes the token-level importance ratios $w_{i,t}(\theta)$ to fluctuate drastically, invalidating them further.
*   **Previous Workaround:** To make GRPO work with MoE, researchers previously employed a **Routing Replay** strategy. This involves caching the activated experts from $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$ and forcing $\pi_\theta$ to use the same routing during optimization (Section 5.3). While this enables convergence (Figure 3), it incurs additional memory and communication overhead and limits the model's actual capacity.

### Positioning of GSPO
GSPO positions itself as a theoretically grounded correction to these issues. It argues that since the reward $r(x, y)$ is granted to the **entire sequence**, the unit of optimization should also be the sequence.

*   **Alignment:** GSPO aligns the importance ratio definition with the sequence likelihood rather than token likelihood.
*   **Simplification:** By resolving the stability issues inherently, GSPO eliminates the need for complex workarounds like Routing Replay in MoE training (Section 5.3).
*   **Efficiency:** It retains the value-model-free efficiency of GRPO while providing the stability required for scaling to larger models and longer reasoning chains.

In summary, the paper motivates GSPO not just as an incremental improvement, but as a necessary correction to an ill-posed objective in prior work that threatens the scalability of RL for large language models.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an algorithm design paper that proposes a new reinforcement learning optimization method. The core idea is to shift from token-level importance sampling (used in prior methods like GRPO) to sequence-level importance sampling, aligning the optimization unit with the reward unit to eliminate high-variance training noise.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

GSPO is a reinforcement learning training algorithm that tells a language model how to update its parameters based on which complete responses earned higher rewards. It solves the problem of training instability in large models by computing importance weights over entire sequences rather than individual tokens, which prevents noise from accumulating across long reasoning chains.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The GSPO training pipeline consists of four major components that operate in sequence. First, the **Rollout Generator** samples multiple responses (a group of size $G$) from the old policy $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$ for each query in the batch. Second, the **Reward Verifier** scores each complete response $y_i$ with a scalar reward $r(x, y_i) \in [0, 1]$. Third, the **Advantage Calculator** normalizes rewards within each group to compute sequence-level advantages $\hat{A}_i$ using mean-subtraction and standard deviation normalization. Fourth, the **Sequence Optimizer** computes the sequence-level importance ratio $s_i(\theta)$, applies clipping to filter overly off-policy samples, and produces gradient updates for the policy parameters $\theta$. Information flows unidirectionally: queries enter the rollout generator, responses flow to the verifier, rewards flow to the advantage calculator, and all signals converge at the optimizer for parameter updates.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We start with the sequence-level importance ratio definition because this is the fundamental mathematical innovation that distinguishes GSPO from all prior methods.
- We then explain the optimization objective and clipping mechanism, showing how the importance ratio integrates into the actual training loss.
- We derive the gradient formulation to reveal why GSPO produces more stable updates than GRPO at the mathematical level.
- We introduce the GSPO-token variant to show how the framework can be extended for finer-grained control when needed.
- We conclude with implementation details including hyperparameters, mini-batch structure, and MoE-specific considerations that affect real-world deployment.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Principle: Sequence-Level Alignment**

The foundational insight of GSPO is that the unit of optimization must match the unit of reward. Since the reward verifier assigns a single scalar score $r(x, y)$ to each complete response sequence $y$, applying importance sampling corrections at the token level creates a structural mismatch that introduces variance. GSPO resolves this by defining all importance weights, clipping operations, and advantage estimates at the sequence level, ensuring that each response is treated as a coherent training unit rather than a collection of independent token predictions.

**Sequence-Level Importance Ratio Definition**

GSPO defines the importance ratio $s_i(\theta)$ for response $y_i$ as the geometric mean of token-level probability ratios across the entire sequence. The mathematical formulation appears in Equation (7):

$$
s_i(\theta) = \left(\frac{\pi_\theta(y_i|x)}{\pi_{\theta_{old}}(y_i|x)}\right)^{\frac{1}{|y_i|}} = \exp\left(\frac{1}{|y_i|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_{i,t}|x, y_{i,<t})}{\pi_{\theta_{old}}(y_{i,t}|x, y_{i,<t})}\right)
$$

Here, $\pi_\theta(y_i|x)$ represents the likelihood of the entire sequence $y_i$ given query $x$ under the current policy, $\pi_{\theta_{old}}(y_i|x)$ represents the same likelihood under the old policy that generated the data, and $|y_i|$ denotes the number of tokens in response $y_i$. The exponent $\frac{1}{|y_i|}$ performs length normalization, which serves two critical purposes: it reduces variance by preventing the ratio from becoming extremely large or small for long sequences, and it keeps $s_i(\theta)$ within a unified numerical range regardless of response length, allowing a single clipping threshold to work across all samples. Without this normalization, the likelihood product over many tokens would cause dramatic fluctuations in the importance ratio, and responses of different lengths would require different clipping ranges.

**Optimization Objective with Sequence Clipping**

The GSPO training objective appears in Equation (5) and operates over groups of responses:

$$
J_{GSPO}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{old}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^G \min\left(s_i(\theta) \hat{A}_i, \text{clip}(s_i(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \hat{A}_i\right) \right]
$$

In this formulation, $\mathcal{D}$ represents the query distribution, $G$ is the group size (number of responses sampled per query), and $\hat{A}_i$ is the advantage estimate for response $y_i$. The $\min$ operator selects the more conservative estimate between the unclipped and clipped versions, preventing the policy from making overly large updates when the importance ratio indicates the sample is far from the current policy. The $\text{clip}$ function constrains $s_i(\theta)$ to the range $[1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon]$, where $\epsilon$ is the clipping threshold. Critically, this clipping applies to the entire sequence ratio $s_i(\theta)$, not to individual token ratios. If a sequence is deemed too off-policy (its ratio falls outside the clipping range), the entire sequence's contribution to the gradient is clipped, rather than clipping token-by-token which would allow some tokens to contribute while others are suppressed.

**Advantage Estimation via Group Normalization**

GSPO adopts the same group-based advantage estimation as GRPO, shown in Equation (6):

$$
\hat{A}_i = \frac{r(x, y_i) - \text{mean}(\{r(x, y_i)\}_{i=1}^G)}{\text{std}(\{r(x, y_i)\}_{i=1}^G)}
$$

This formula computes the advantage for response $y_i$ by subtracting the mean reward of all $G$ responses to the same query $x$ and dividing by the standard deviation of those rewards. This normalization ensures that advantages have zero mean and unit variance within each group, which stabilizes training by preventing the scale of rewards from affecting gradient magnitudes. All tokens within a response $y_i$ share the same advantage value $\hat{A}_i$, maintaining consistency with the sequence-level optimization philosophy. This approach eliminates the need for a separate value model (critic), which is a major computational advantage over PPO since value models typically match the policy model in size and introduce significant memory overhead.

**Gradient Analysis: Why GSPO Is More Stable**

The gradient of the GSPO objective (omitting clipping for clarity) reveals the stability mechanism in Equations (8) through (10):

$$
\nabla_\theta J_{GSPO}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{old}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^G s_i(\theta) \hat{A}_i \cdot \nabla_\theta \log s_i(\theta) \right]
$$

$$
= \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{old}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^G \left(\frac{\pi_\theta(y_i|x)}{\pi_{\theta_{old}}(y_i|x)}\right)^{\frac{1}{|y_i|}} \hat{A}_i \cdot \frac{1}{|y_i|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y_{i,t}|x, y_{i,<t}) \right]
$$

The key observation is that all tokens within a response receive equal weighting in the gradient computation. The sequence-level importance ratio $s_i(\theta)$ multiplies the sum of token log-probability gradients, meaning every token in response $y_i$ contributes equally to the update. Contrast this with the GRPO gradient in Equations (11) and (12):

$$
\nabla_\theta J_{GRPO}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{old}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^G \hat{A}_i \cdot \frac{1}{|y_i|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \frac{\pi_\theta(y_{i,t}|x, y_{i,<t})}{\pi_{\theta_{old}}(y_{i,t}|x, y_{i,<t})} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y_{i,t}|x, y_{i,<t}) \right]
$$

In GRPO, each token $y_{i,t}$ is weighted by its own token-level importance ratio $\frac{\pi_\theta(y_{i,t}|x, y_{i,<t})}{\pi_{\theta_{old}}(y_{i,t}|x, y_{i,<t})}$. These weights can vary significantly across tokens within the same sequence, taking values in $(0, 1+\epsilon]$ for positive advantages or $[1-\epsilon, +\infty)$ for negative advantages. This unequal weighting introduces instability because the impact of each token on the gradient becomes unpredictable and can accumulate destructively over long sequences. GSPO eliminates this instability factor by weighting all tokens in a response equally through the sequence-level ratio.

**GSPO-token: A Token-Level Advantage Variant**

For scenarios requiring finer-grained control, such as multi-turn RL where different turns may warrant different advantage values, the paper introduces GSPO-token in Equation (13):

$$
J_{GSPO-token}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{old}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^G \frac{1}{|y_i|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \min\left(s_{i,t}(\theta) \hat{A}_{i,t}, \text{clip}(s_{i,t}(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \hat{A}_{i,t}\right) \right]
$$

The token-level importance ratio $s_{i,t}(\theta)$ is defined in Equation (14):

$$
s_{i,t}(\theta) = \text{sg}[s_i(\theta)] \cdot \frac{\pi_\theta(y_{i,t}|x, y_{i,<t})}{\text{sg}[\pi_\theta(y_{i,t}|x, y_{i,<t})]}
$$

Here, $\text{sg}[\cdot]$ denotes the stop-gradient operation (equivalent to PyTorch's `detach`), which takes the numerical value but prevents gradient flow. This construction ensures that $s_{i,t}(\theta)$ is numerically equal to $s_i(\theta)$ because the ratio $\frac{\pi_\theta(y_{i,t}|x, y_{i,<t})}{\text{sg}[\pi_\theta(y_{i,t}|x, y_{i,<t})]}$ has a numerical value of 1. The gradient derivation in Equations (15) through (17) shows that GSPO-token and GSPO are numerically identical in their optimization objective, clipping condition, and theoretical gradient when all tokens in a response share the same advantage ($\hat{A}_{i,t} = \hat{A}_i$). However, GSPO-token provides the flexibility to assign different advantage values $\hat{A}_{i,t}$ to different tokens within the same response, enabling more sophisticated reward shaping when the use case demands it.

**Implementation Configuration and Hyperparameters**

The experimental setup in Section 5.1 specifies concrete hyperparameter values that are critical for reproducibility. During RL training, each batch of rollout data is partitioned into **four mini-batches** for gradient updates, which introduces an off-policy learning setting where responses are sampled from $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$ rather than the current $\pi_\theta$. For GSPO, the left and right clipping ranges in Equation (5) are set to **3e-4 and 4e-4**, respectively. For the GRPO baseline, the left and right clipping ranges in Equation (2) are set to **0.2 and 0.27**, respectively. The paper explicitly notes that these clipping ranges differ by **two orders of magnitude** due to the distinct definitions of importance ratios—sequence-level ratios in GSPO are much closer to 1 than token-level ratios in GRPO, necessitating smaller clipping thresholds. The authors state they carefully tuned the GRPO clipping ranges to ensure a fair comparison. The experiments use a cold-start model fine-tuned from **Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base**, which is a Mixture-of-Experts model with 30 billion total parameters and 3 billion active parameters per forward pass.

**MoE-Specific Stability Mechanism**

Section 5.3 explains why GSPO inherently stabilizes Mixture-of-Experts training without requiring the Routing Replay strategy that GRPO needs. In MoE models, expert-activation volatility causes approximately **10% of the experts activated** under the new policy $\pi_\theta$ to differ from those under the old policy $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$ after each gradient update in a 48-layer model. This volatility makes token-level importance ratios $w_{i,t}(\theta)$ fluctuate drastically because the activated network architecture changes between $\pi_\theta$ and $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$ for the same token position. GSPO circumvents this problem because it depends only on the sequence likelihood $\pi_\theta(y_i|x)$, which remains stable even when individual expert activations shift. The MoE model maintains its overall language modeling capability at the sequence level, so the sequence-level importance ratio $s_i(\theta)$ does not experience the drastic fluctuations that plague token-level ratios. This eliminates the need for Routing Replay, which caches activated experts from $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$ and forces $\pi_\theta$ to reuse the same routing during optimization—a workaround that incurs additional memory and communication overhead while limiting the model's actual capacity.

**Infrastructure Simplification Through Precision Tolerance**

Section 5.4 describes how GSPO's sequence-level approach simplifies RL infrastructure by tolerating precision discrepancies between training and inference engines. In practice, training engines (e.g., Megatron) and inference engines (e.g., SGLang, vLLM) often produce slightly different likelihood values due to floating-point precision differences. With token-level optimization, these small discrepancies can compound across many tokens and significantly affect the importance ratios. GSPO's sequence-level likelihoods are much more tolerant of such precision discrepancies because the length normalization in $s_i(\theta)$ averages out small per-token differences. This makes it possible to directly use likelihoods returned by the inference engine for optimization, avoiding the need to recompute likelihoods with the training engine. This benefit is especially valuable in scenarios like partial rollout, multi-turn RL, and training-inference disaggregated frameworks where recomputation adds significant overhead.

**Design Choice Rationale: Why Sequence-Level Over Token-Level**

The paper justifies the sequence-level design through both theoretical and empirical arguments. Theoretically, importance sampling requires averaging over multiple samples from the behavior distribution for the importance weight to effectively correct distributional mismatch, as shown in Equation (4). Token-level importance weights violate this principle because each token position has only one sample from the next-token distribution. Sequence-level weights satisfy the principle because the sequence likelihood represents an aggregate over all token positions. Empirically, Figure 2 shows that GSPO clips approximately **0.15** (15%) of tokens on average, while GRPO clips only about **0.0013** (0.13%) of tokens—a difference of two orders of magnitude. Despite clipping far more tokens and using fewer samples for gradient estimation, GSPO achieves higher training efficiency than GRPO as shown in Figure 1. This counter-intuitive result indicates that GRPO's token-level gradient estimates are inherently noisy and inefficient for sample exploitation, while GSPO's sequence-level approach provides a more reliable learning signal even with fewer unclipped samples.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the conceptual breakthroughs that distinguish GSPO from prior reinforcement learning algorithms. Rather than re-explaining the mechanics covered in Section 3, we focus on **why** these design choices represent fundamental shifts in how we think about RL for language models, and what capabilities they unlock that were previously inaccessible.

### 4.1 Theoretical Correction: Importance Sampling Requires Aggregate Samples

The most fundamental innovation in GSPO is recognizing that GRPO's token-level importance weights violate a core statistical principle. As established in Section 3, importance sampling requires averaging over multiple samples from the behavior distribution for the weight $\frac{\pi_{tar}(z)}{\pi_{beh}(z)}$ to meaningfully correct distributional mismatch (Equation 4 in Section 2). 

**What makes this different:** Prior work (PPO, GRPO) treated token-level importance ratios as valid without questioning whether the statistical assumptions held at that granularity. The Qwen team identified that **each token position provides only one sample** from the next-token distribution, making the importance weight statistically invalid rather than merely noisy.

**Why this is significant:** This is not an incremental improvement but a **theoretical correction**. It explains why GRPO training becomes increasingly unstable as response lengths grow—the variance compounds with each token rather than averaging out. This insight has implications beyond GSPO itself:

- It suggests that any RL algorithm applying token-level importance sampling to autoregressive generation faces the same fundamental limitation.
- It provides a principled explanation for the "irreversible model collapse" phenomenon reported in Qwen (2025a) and MiniMax (2025), which prior work attributed to hyperparameter sensitivity rather than algorithmic flaws.
- It establishes sequence-level optimization as the theoretically sound approach for language model RL, not just an empirical heuristic.

> "The failure of the token-level importance weight points to a core principle: the unit of optimization objective should match the unit of reward." (Section 3)

This statement encapsulates the conceptual shift—from treating language generation as a sequence of independent decisions to treating it as a single coherent action that receives a single evaluation signal.

### 4.2 Alignment Principle: Optimization Unit Must Match Reward Unit

Building on the theoretical correction above, GSPO introduces a design principle that has been largely overlooked in prior RLHF work: **the granularity of optimization should match the granularity of reward assignment**.

**What makes this different:** In PPO and GRPO, rewards are assigned to complete sequences (the verifier scores $r(x, y)$ for the entire response), but optimization occurs at the token level. This creates a structural mismatch where the learning signal (sequence reward) and the update mechanism (token gradients) operate at different scales. GSPO closes this gap by performing clipping, importance weighting, and gradient accumulation all at the sequence level.

**Why this is significant:** This alignment principle explains the counter-intuitive empirical finding in Figure 2, where GSPO clips approximately **15% of tokens** compared to GRPO's **0.13%**, yet achieves superior training efficiency (Figure 1). The insight is:

- **Quality over quantity:** Clipping more tokens removes noisy gradient contributions, leaving cleaner learning signals. GRPO's low clipping fraction gives a false sense of sample efficiency while actually propagating high-variance updates.
- **Variance reduction:** Sequence-level clipping acts as a sample-level filter rather than a token-level dampener. If a sequence is too off-policy, the entire sequence is down-weighted, preventing partial updates that could destabilize training.
- **Scalability:** As models grow larger and responses become longer (e.g., competition mathematics requiring multi-step reasoning), the alignment principle becomes increasingly critical. Token-level methods accumulate variance proportional to sequence length; sequence-level methods do not.

This principle may generalize beyond GSPO to other RL settings where reward and optimization granularity can be misaligned—for instance, in multi-turn dialogue or tool-use scenarios where rewards are assigned at the conversation level but updates occur at each turn.

### 4.3 Inherent MoE Stability: Eliminating Routing Replay

GSPO's ability to stabilize Mixture-of-Experts training without the Routing Replay workaround represents a **practical breakthrough** with immediate deployment implications.

**What makes this different:** As detailed in Section 3, GRPO requires Routing Replay to converge on MoE models—caching expert activations from $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$ and forcing $\pi_\theta$ to reuse the same routing during optimization. This workaround adds memory overhead, communication costs, and artificially constrains the model's capacity. GSPO eliminates this requirement entirely because sequence-level likelihoods remain stable even when individual expert activations shift (approximately **10% of experts differ** per update in the Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base model, Section 5.3).

**Why this is significant:** This innovation unlocks three concrete benefits:

| Benefit | GRPO + Routing Replay | GSPO (No Workaround) |
|---------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Memory overhead | Additional caching for routing states | None |
| Communication cost | Extra synchronization for replay | Standard all-reduce only |
| Model capacity | Constrained to consistent routing | Full expert utilization |
| Implementation complexity | Custom routing logic required | Standard RL pipeline |

The significance extends beyond engineering convenience. Routing Replay fundamentally limits how MoE models can adapt during RL training—by forcing consistent expert activation, it prevents the model from discovering new expert specializations that might better serve the reward objective. GSPO removes this constraint, allowing the MoE architecture to fully leverage its capacity during fine-tuning.

This is particularly important for the next generation of LLMs, where MoE architectures are becoming standard for efficiency reasons. An RL algorithm that cannot train MoE models without workarounds would become a bottleneck for scaling. GSPO removes this bottleneck.

### 4.4 Infrastructure Simplification: Precision Tolerance Across Engines

A less obvious but operationally significant innovation is GSPO's tolerance for precision discrepancies between training and inference engines, which enables infrastructure simplification.

**What makes this different:** In standard RL pipelines, likelihoods computed by the inference engine (e.g., vLLM, SGLang) cannot be directly used for optimization because floating-point precision differences with the training engine (e.g., Megatron) compound across tokens and corrupt token-level importance ratios. GSPO's length-normalized sequence ratios average out these small per-token discrepancies, making direct use of inference engine likelihoods viable.

**Why this is significant:** This enables several deployment scenarios that were previously impractical:

- **Training-inference disaggregation:** The inference engine can compute likelihoods during rollout without requiring the training engine to recompute them, reducing compute overhead and enabling more flexible system architectures.
- **Partial rollout optimization:** In multi-turn RL, only the most recent turn needs gradient updates, but likelihoods for the full sequence are still required. GSPO's precision tolerance makes this more tractable.
- **Reduced recomputation:** Eliminating the need to recompute likelihoods in the training engine saves significant FLOPs, especially for long responses where recomputation cost scales linearly with sequence length.

This innovation is **incremental in algorithm design but transformative in systems engineering**. It demonstrates that algorithmic choices have downstream effects on infrastructure complexity—a consideration often overlooked in RL algorithm papers that focus solely on benchmark performance.

### 4.5 Distinguishing Fundamental vs. Incremental Contributions

To clarify the nature of GSPO's innovations, we categorize them below:

| Contribution | Type | Rationale |
|--------------|------|-----------|
| Sequence-level importance sampling | **Fundamental** | Corrects a theoretical flaw in prior importance sampling application; changes the mathematical foundation of the objective. |
| Optimization-reward alignment principle | **Fundamental** | Establishes a new design principle for RL algorithms that extends beyond GSPO to other settings. |
| MoE stability without Routing Replay | **Fundamental** | Removes a hard constraint on MoE RL training that prior work considered necessary. |
| Precision tolerance for inference likelihoods | **Incremental** | Derives from the sequence-level design but is a secondary benefit rather than a core innovation. |
| GSPO-token variant | **Incremental** | Extends GSPO to token-level advantages while preserving sequence-level stability; useful but not foundational. |
| Improved benchmark performance | **Outcome** | Result of the above innovations, not an innovation itself. |

The fundamental innovations (sequence-level sampling, alignment principle, MoE stability) are what enable GSPO to scale RL training to models and response lengths where GRPO fails catastrophically. The incremental innovations (precision tolerance, GSPO-token) improve usability and flexibility but are not required for GSPO's core benefits.

This distinction matters for practitioners: adopting GSPO for its benchmark gains alone would miss the primary value proposition, which is **enabling RL training at scales that were previously infeasible**. The Qwen3 models' improvements (Section 1, Section 5.1) are downstream consequences of this newly accessible training regime, not merely the result of a better optimizer.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines the empirical evidence supporting GSPO's claims. We analyze the experimental methodology, extract specific quantitative results from the paper's figures, and critically assess whether the data convincingly demonstrates GSPO's advantages over GRPO. Unlike typical RL papers that report extensive tables of final scores, this paper emphasizes **training dynamics** and **stability characteristics** as primary evidence—which aligns with its core claim that GSPO enables scaling where GRPO fails.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Base Model and Architecture**

The experiments use a cold-start model fine-tuned from **Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base** (Section 5.1). This is a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture with:
- **30 billion total parameters**
- **3 billion active parameters** per forward pass (approximately 10% sparsity)
- **48 layers** (Section 5.3)

This model choice is deliberate: MoE architectures expose the stability weaknesses of GRPO that GSPO claims to resolve. Testing on a dense model would not have demonstrated GSPO's key advantage in eliminating the Routing Replay requirement.

**Benchmark Datasets and Metrics**

The paper evaluates on three distinct benchmarks representing different reasoning domains (Section 5.1):

| Benchmark | Domain | Metric | Sampling Configuration |
|-----------|--------|--------|------------------------|
| **AIME'24** | Competition Mathematics | Pass@1 | Average over 32 samplings |
| **LiveCodeBench** | Code Generation | Pass@1 | Average over 8 samplings (202410-202502 version) |
| **CodeForces** | Competitive Programming | Elo Rating | Direct evaluation |

The use of **Pass@1** rather than Pass@k is notable—it measures the quality of individual samples rather than the model's best output across multiple attempts. This is a stricter metric that better reflects real-world deployment where users typically receive a single response.

**Training Configuration**

Several hyperparameters are explicitly specified in Section 5.1:

- **Mini-batch partitioning:** Each rollout batch is divided into **4 mini-batches** for gradient updates. This creates an off-policy learning setting where responses are sampled from $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$ rather than the current $\pi_\theta$.
- **GSPO clipping ranges:** Left threshold = **3e-4**, Right threshold = **4e-4** (applied to Equation 5)
- **GRPO clipping ranges:** Left threshold = **0.2**, Right threshold = **0.27** (applied to Equation 2)

The clipping range disparity is critical: GSPO's thresholds are **two orders of magnitude smaller** than GRPO's. This reflects the different scales of sequence-level versus token-level importance ratios. The authors state they "carefully tuned" GRPO's clipping ranges to ensure fair comparison—this is important because GRPO is known to be highly sensitive to clipping hyperparameters.

**Baseline and Control Conditions**

The primary baseline is **GRPO with Routing Replay** (Section 5.1, Section 5.3). The paper notes that GRPO **necessitates** Routing Replay for MoE RL convergence—without it, training fails to converge properly (Figure 3). This creates an asymmetric comparison:

- **GSPO:** Standard training pipeline, no special workarounds
- **GRPO:** Requires Routing Replay (caching activated experts from $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$ and replaying them in $\pi_\theta$)

This asymmetry actually strengthens GSPO's claim: it achieves better performance **without** the engineering complexity that GRPO requires. However, it also means the comparison is not purely algorithmic—GRPO's overhead from Routing Replay could theoretically reduce its effective training throughput.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**Training Reward Curves (Figure 1, Left Panel)**

Figure 1 presents training reward curves as a function of training compute. The key observations:

- **GSPO maintains stable, monotonic improvement** throughout training. The curve shows consistent upward trajectory without plateaus or degradation.
- **GRPO (with Routing Replay)** shows lower reward values at equivalent compute levels. The gap between GSPO and GRPO widens as training compute increases.
- The y-axis ranges from **0.50 to 0.70** training reward. GSPO reaches approximately **0.68-0.70** at the highest compute levels shown, while GRPO plateaus around **0.60-0.62**.

> "GSPO can deliver continuous performance improvement through increasing the training compute, regularly updating the query set, and extending the generation length." (Section 5.1)

This statement is significant: it claims GSPO supports **scaling laws** for RL training—more compute reliably yields better performance. GRPO's instability at scale would violate this property.

**Benchmark Performance (Figure 1, Right Three Panels)**

The three benchmark panels in Figure 1 show performance versus training compute:

| Benchmark | GSPO Final Performance | GRPO Final Performance | Approximate Gap |
|-----------|------------------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| **AIME'24** | ~80 Pass@1 | ~75 Pass@1 | +5 points |
| **LiveCodeBench** | ~65 Pass@1 | ~60 Pass@1 | +5 points |
| **CodeForces** | ~2000 Elo | ~1900 Elo | +100 Elo |

Exact final values are not explicitly stated in the text—the paper presents these as curves rather than tables. However, the visual gaps are consistent across all three benchmarks: GSPO achieves **5-10% relative improvement** over GRPO at equivalent training compute.

More importantly, the **slope** of the GSPO curves is steeper, indicating higher **training efficiency**. The paper claims:

> "GSPO also demonstrates superior training efficiency over GRPO, achieving better training accuracy and benchmark performance under the same training compute and consumed queries." (Section 5.1)

This efficiency claim is central: GSPO doesn't just achieve better final performance—it gets there faster, using fewer resources.

**Clipping Fraction Analysis (Figure 2)**

Figure 2 presents one of the most counter-intuitive findings in the paper:

| Algorithm | Average Clipped Token Fraction |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| **GRPO** | ~0.0013 (0.13%) |
| **GSPO** | ~0.15 (15%) |

This represents a **~115x difference** in clipping fractions. Despite clipping over 100x more tokens, GSPO achieves higher training efficiency.

> "This counter-intuitive finding — that clipping a much larger fraction of tokens leads to superior training efficiency — further indicates that GRPO's token-level gradient estimates are inherently noisy and inefficient for sample exploitation." (Section 5.2)

This result directly supports the paper's theoretical claim from Section 3: GRPO's token-level importance weights introduce high-variance noise. The low clipping fraction in GRPO is misleading—it suggests most samples are usable, but those samples carry noisy gradients that degrade learning. GSPO's aggressive clipping removes noisy samples, leaving cleaner learning signals.

**MoE Stability Without Routing Replay (Figure 3)**

Figure 3 isolates the impact of Routing Replay on GRPO training:

- **GRPO with Routing Replay:** Training reward increases from ~0.25 to ~0.55 over the compute range shown
- **GRPO without Routing Replay:** Training reward plateaus around **0.30-0.35** and fails to converge properly

> "Figure 3 demonstrates that Routing Replay serves as an essential technique in the normal convergence of the GRPO training of MoE models." (Section 5.3)

In contrast, GSPO converges normally without Routing Replay (Figure 1). This is not presented as a separate ablation because the difference is binary: GSPO simply does not require this workaround. The paper states:

> "GSPO eliminates the dependency on Routing Replay and is fully capable of computing the importance ratios $s_i(\theta)$ conventionally, converging normally, and optimizing stably." (Section 5.3)

This represents a **qualitative difference** rather than a quantitative one—GSPO enables a training regime that GRPO cannot access without artificial constraints.

### 5.3 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

**Strengths of the Experimental Design**

1. **Realistic Model Scale:** Testing on a 30B MoE model (Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base) demonstrates applicability to production-scale systems rather than small research prototypes.

2. **Multiple Benchmark Domains:** Evaluating on mathematics (AIME'24), code generation (LiveCodeBench), and competitive programming (CodeForces) shows GSPO's benefits generalize across reasoning tasks.

3. **Training Dynamics Focus:** Presenting curves rather than just final scores reveals stability characteristics that single-point evaluations would miss. This aligns with the paper's core claim about enabling scalable RL training.

4. **Asymmetric Comparison Honesty:** The paper openly acknowledges that GRPO requires Routing Replay while GSPO does not. Rather than hiding this complexity, it frames it as evidence of GSPO's superiority.

**Limitations and Missing Information**

1. **No Final Score Tables:** The paper presents results exclusively as curves in Figure 1. Exact final benchmark scores are not tabulated, making precise comparisons difficult. Readers must estimate values from the graphs.

2. **Limited Baseline Diversity:** Only GRPO is compared as a baseline. PPO is discussed in Section 2 but not included in experiments. Given PPO's historical dominance in RLHF, its absence is notable—though the paper's focus is on value-model-free methods.

3. **No Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper does not report confidence intervals, standard deviations across runs, or statistical significance tests. Given RL training's high variance, multiple runs would strengthen the claims.

4. **Compute Normalization Unclear:** While Figure 1 plots performance versus "Training Compute," the paper does not specify how compute is measured (FLOPs, GPU-hours, number of updates?). This makes it difficult to verify the efficiency claims independently.

5. **No Dense Model Results:** All experiments use an MoE model. While this demonstrates GSPO's MoE stability benefit, it leaves open whether GSPO provides advantages on dense architectures where Routing Replay is not needed.

6. **Clipping Range Tuning Burden:** The paper notes GSPO clipping ranges (3e-4, 4e-4) differ by two orders of magnitude from GRPO's (0.2, 0.27). While this is theoretically expected, it means practitioners cannot directly transfer hyperparameter intuition from GRPO to GSPO—new tuning is required.

**Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks**

The paper includes limited ablation analysis:

- **Routing Replay Ablation (Figure 3):** This is the only explicit ablation, showing GRPO fails without Routing Replay on MoE models. This convincingly demonstrates GSPO's advantage in this specific context.

- **Clipping Fraction Analysis (Figure 2):** While not a traditional ablation, this analysis reveals the mechanism behind GSPO's efficiency—aggressive clipping removes noisy gradients.

- **GSPO-token Variant (Section 4.3):** The paper introduces GSPO-token as a token-level advantage variant but does not present experimental results comparing it to standard GSPO. The theoretical analysis shows they are numerically identical when $\hat{A}_{i,t} = \hat{A}_i$, but empirical validation is absent.

**Missing Robustness Checks:**

- No sensitivity analysis on clipping range hyperparameters
- No experiments varying group size $G$
- No analysis of how GSPO performs with different sequence lengths
- No investigation of failure modes or conditions where GSPO might underperform

### 5.4 Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: GSPO Achieves Superior Training Stability**

**Support Level: Strong**

Figure 1 shows GSPO's training reward curve is smooth and monotonic, while the paper describes GRPO as prone to "catastrophic and irreversible model collapse" (Section 1). Figure 3 demonstrates GRPO's dependency on Routing Replay for MoE convergence. The clipping fraction analysis (Figure 2) provides a mechanistic explanation: GSPO removes noisy samples rather than propagating them.

**Caveat:** The paper does not show a GRPO collapse curve—only the successful GRPO+Routing Replay baseline. A direct visualization of GRPO failure would have been more compelling.

**Claim 2: GSPO Achieves Superior Training Efficiency**

**Support Level: Moderate**

Figure 1 shows GSPO reaches higher benchmark scores at equivalent training compute. The steeper slope of GSPO curves suggests better sample efficiency. However:

- Exact compute measurements are not specified
- No statistical significance testing across runs
- GRPO's Routing Replay overhead is not quantified in compute terms

The efficiency claim is plausible but not rigorously quantified.

**Claim 3: GSPO Eliminates Need for Routing Replay in MoE Training**

**Support Level: Strong**

This is the most convincingly supported claim. Figure 3 shows GRPO fails without Routing Replay. The paper states GSPO "obviated the need for this strategy" (Section 5.1) and explains the mechanism: sequence-level likelihoods remain stable even when expert activations shift (~10% of experts differ per update, Section 5.3).

**Claim 4: GSPO Simplifies RL Infrastructure**

**Support Level: Weak (Theoretical)**

Section 5.4 argues GSPO's precision tolerance enables using inference engine likelihoods directly, avoiding recomputation in the training engine. However:

- No experiments quantify the infrastructure savings
- No measurements of recomputation overhead eliminated
- The claim is presented as potential rather than demonstrated benefit

This is a reasonable hypothesis based on GSPO's design, but empirical validation is absent.

**Claim 5: GSPO Contributed to Qwen3 Model Improvements**

**Support Level: Anecdotal**

The paper states GSPO "contributed to the remarkable improvements in the latest Qwen3 models" (Abstract, Section 1, Section 5.1). However:

- No specific Qwen3 model results are presented
- No ablation isolating GSPO's contribution from other improvements
- The claim relies on the Figure 1 experiments with Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base, not production Qwen3 models

This claim is aspirational rather than empirically demonstrated in this paper.

| Claim | Evidence Type | Support Level | Key Figure/Section |
|-------|---------------|---------------|---------------------|
| Training Stability | Training reward curves | Strong | Figure 1 (left) |
| Training Efficiency | Benchmark vs. compute curves | Moderate | Figure 1 (right) |

The experiments convincingly demonstrate GSPO's **stability advantages** and **MoE training benefits**. The **efficiency claims** are supported but lack rigorous quantification. The **infrastructure and production model claims** are presented as potential benefits rather than empirically validated results.

For practitioners, the key takeaway is: if you are training large MoE models with RL and experiencing GRPO instability, GSPO provides a theoretically grounded alternative that eliminates the Routing Replay requirement. The benchmark improvements are secondary to the stability guarantee—which, as the paper emphasizes, is the prerequisite for scaling RL training at all.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While GSPO demonstrates compelling advantages in stability and MoE training, a critical examination reveals several limitations, trade-offs, and open questions that practitioners should consider before adoption. This section identifies assumptions the approach relies on, scenarios not addressed in the paper, and constraints that may affect real-world deployment.

### 6.1 Hyperparameter Transfer Burden

A significant practical limitation is that GSPO requires **entirely new hyperparameter intuition** compared to prior methods. The paper explicitly notes:

> "We also note that the clipping ranges in GSPO and in previous algorithms (e.g., GRPO) typically differ in order of magnitude due to the distinct definitions of importance ratios." (Section 4.1)

The experimental configuration reveals the magnitude of this shift:

| Algorithm | Left Clipping Threshold | Right Clipping Threshold |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------------------|
| **GSPO** | 3e-4 | 4e-4 |
| **GRPO** | 0.2 | 0.27 |

This represents a **~500-675x difference** in clipping thresholds. For practitioners with existing GRPO or PPO infrastructure, this means:

- **No direct hyperparameter transfer**: Tuning experience from token-level methods does not apply to sequence-level optimization.
- **New tuning overhead**: Organizations must invest compute resources to find optimal clipping ranges for their specific model architectures and tasks.
- **Sensitivity unknown**: The paper does not include a sensitivity analysis showing how GSPO performance degrades if clipping ranges deviate from the optimal 3e-4/4e-4 values.

This limitation is particularly acute for teams without the computational resources to conduct extensive hyperparameter searches. The paper states the GRPO clipping ranges were "carefully tuned to ensure a fair comparison" (Section 5.1), implying significant tuning effort was required—even for the baseline.

### 6.2 Limited Experimental Scope

The experimental validation, while demonstrating GSPO's core benefits, leaves several important questions unanswered:

**No Dense Model Comparison**

All experiments use the **Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base** MoE model (Section 5.1). This is deliberate—it showcases GSPO's MoE stability advantage. However, this creates uncertainty about GSPO's benefits on dense architectures:

- Does GSPO provide stability advantages on dense models where Routing Replay is not needed?
- Is the efficiency gain (Figure 1) specific to MoE training, or does it generalize?
- Without dense model results, it's unclear whether GSPO's primary value is **MoE-specific stabilization** or **general algorithmic improvement**.

For teams training dense models, the cost-benefit analysis of migrating from GRPO to GSPO remains unclear based on this paper alone.

**Single Baseline Comparison**

The experiments compare GSPO only against **GRPO with Routing Replay** (Section 5.1). Notable omissions include:

- **PPO**: Discussed in Section 2 as the historical baseline but not included in experiments. Given PPO's continued use in production RLHF pipelines, this absence limits practical guidance.
- **GRPO without Routing Replay**: Figure 3 shows this fails to converge, but a direct side-by-side training curve comparison with GSPO would have been more compelling.
- **Other value-model-free methods**: Recent alternatives to GRPO (e.g., variants from DeepSeek, MiniMax) are not evaluated.

**No Statistical Significance Testing**

The paper presents training curves without:

- Confidence intervals or error bars
- Multiple independent runs to assess variance
- Statistical significance tests on final benchmark scores

Given RL training's inherent stochasticity, a single run per method (as appears to be the case) limits confidence in the reported gaps. The ~5 point AIME'24 gap and ~100 Elo CodeForces gap (Figure 1) could partially reflect run-to-run variance rather than algorithmic superiority.

**No Final Score Tables**

Results are presented exclusively as curves in Figure 1. Exact final benchmark scores are not tabulated, making it difficult to:

- Compare precisely against other published results
- Reproduce the claimed improvements
- Assess whether the performance gaps are practically significant

### 6.3 GSPO-token Variant Lacks Empirical Validation

Section 4.3 introduces **GSPO-token**, a token-level advantage variant designed for scenarios like multi-turn RL where finer-grained advantage adjustment is desired. The theoretical analysis shows GSPO-token and standard GSPO are "numerically identical in the optimization objective, clipping condition, and theoretical gradient when we set the advantages of all the tokens in the response $y_i$ to the same value" (Section 4.3).

However, the paper provides **zero experimental results** for GSPO-token:

- No comparison of GSPO-token vs. standard GSPO on multi-turn tasks
- No demonstration of the claimed "higher flexibility of adjusting the advantages per token"
- No guidance on when practitioners should choose GSPO-token over standard GSPO

This leaves GSPO-token as a **theoretically sound but empirically unvalidated** extension. For practitioners considering multi-turn RL applications, this represents an open question requiring independent investigation.

### 6.4 Infrastructure Claims Are Theoretical, Not Demonstrated

Section 5.4 argues that GSPO's sequence-level approach enables infrastructure simplification by tolerating precision discrepancies between training and inference engines:

> "GSPO makes it possible to directly use the likelihoods returned by the inference engine for optimization, thereby avoiding the need for recomputation with the training engine." (Section 5.4)

While this claim is theoretically plausible—length normalization in $s_i(\theta)$ should average out small per-token precision differences—the paper provides **no empirical validation**:

- No measurements of precision discrepancy magnitudes between engines (e.g., Megatron vs. vLLM)
- No quantification of recomputation overhead eliminated
- No experiments demonstrating successful training using inference engine likelihoods directly

This is presented as **potential** rather than demonstrated benefit:

> "This can be especially beneficial in scenarios like partial rollout and multi-turn RL and in the training-inference disaggregated frameworks." (Section 5.4, emphasis added)

For teams evaluating GSPO specifically for infrastructure simplification, this claim remains a hypothesis requiring independent verification.

### 6.5 Sample Efficiency Trade-offs from Aggressive Clipping

Figure 2 reveals that GSPO clips approximately **15% of tokens** compared to GRPO's **0.13%**—a **~115x difference** (Section 5.2). The paper frames this positively:

> "This counter-intuitive finding — that clipping a much larger fraction of tokens leads to superior training efficiency — further indicates that GRPO's token-level gradient estimates are inherently noisy." (Section 5.2)

While the training curves in Figure 1 support this claim, there is an implicit trade-off that deserves acknowledgment:

- **Discarded signal**: Clipping 15% of tokens means 15% of the rollout batch contributes reduced or zero gradient signal. For expensive reward models or limited query budgets, this represents sample inefficiency.
- **Batch size requirements**: To maintain effective batch sizes after clipping, GSPO may require larger initial rollout batches than GRPO, increasing memory and compute requirements per update.
- **Task dependency**: The optimal clipping fraction may vary by task. Tasks with higher reward variance might require different clipping thresholds than the 3e-4/4e-4 values used in the paper.

The paper does not analyze how GSPO performance scales with varying clipping fractions or whether the 15% clipping rate is optimal versus merely acceptable.

### 6.6 Group Size and Reward Model Dependencies

GSPO inherits GRPO's **group-based advantage estimation** (Equation 6), which introduces constraints:

$$
\hat{A}_i = \frac{r(x, y_i) - \text{mean}(\{r(x, y_i)\}_{i=1}^G)}{\text{std}(\{r(x, y_i)\}_{i=1}^G)}
$$

This formulation creates several dependencies:

**Group Size Sensitivity**

The advantage estimation requires $G$ responses per query. The paper does not specify the group size $G$ used in experiments (Section 5.1 mentions batch partitioning but not $G$). This omission is significant because:

- Small $G$ values produce noisy advantage estimates (high variance in mean/std)
- Large $G$ values increase rollout compute cost linearly
- The optimal $G$ may vary by task complexity and reward model reliability

**Reward Model Quality Dependency**

Like GRPO, GSPO's advantage estimation depends entirely on the reward verifier $r(x, y)$. The paper assumes rewards are in $[0, 1]$ (Section 2, Notation) but does not discuss:

- How reward model errors propagate through group normalization
- Whether GSPO is more or less sensitive to reward hacking than GRPO
- How sparse rewards (e.g., binary success/failure) affect training dynamics

For applications with unreliable or expensive reward models, these unanswered questions affect GSPO's practical viability.

### 6.7 Sequence Length Considerations

The paper claims GSPO handles long sequences better than GRPO because token-level noise accumulation is eliminated (Section 3). The length normalization in $s_i(\theta)$ is designed to "reduce the variance and to control $s_i(\theta)$ within a unified numerical range" (Section 4.1):

$$
s_i(\theta) = \left(\frac{\pi_\theta(y_i|x)}{\pi_{\theta_{old}}(y_i|x)}\right)^{\frac{1}{|y_i|}}
$$

However, the paper does not provide:

- **Explicit sequence length analysis**: No experiments comparing GSPO performance across different response length distributions
- **Maximum length bounds**: No discussion of whether GSPO has practical limits on sequence length
- **Length normalization side effects**: The geometric mean normalization could potentially mask important token-level probability changes in very long sequences

For applications requiring extremely long reasoning chains (e.g., 10,000+ token responses), GSPO's behavior remains uncharacterized.

### 6.8 Off-Policy Divergence Limits

The experimental setup partitions each rollout batch into **four mini-batches** for gradient updates (Section 5.1), creating an off-policy learning setting where responses are sampled from $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$ rather than the current $\pi_\theta$. The clipping mechanism is designed to handle this:

> "GSPO applies clipping to entire responses instead of individual tokens to exclude the overly 'off-policy' samples from gradient estimation." (Section 4.1)

However, the paper does not analyze:

- **How many mini-batch updates are safe** before $\pi_\theta$ diverges too far from $\pi_{\theta_{old}}$
- **Whether 4 mini-batches is optimal** or merely sufficient for the reported experiments
- **How GSPO compares to PPO's epoch-based updates** in terms of off-policy tolerance

For teams wanting to maximize gradient updates per rollout batch (to improve compute efficiency), this represents an open tuning question.

### 6.9 No Failure Mode Analysis

The paper emphasizes GSPO's stability but does not investigate conditions where GSPO might underperform or fail:

- **When does GSPO converge slower than GRPO?** The paper claims GSPO is more efficient (Figure 1), but are there regimes where GRPO's lower clipping fraction provides faster initial learning?
- **What happens with very small models?** The experiments use a 30B MoE model. GSPO's benefits may be less pronounced on smaller architectures where token-level noise is less catastrophic.
- **Are there tasks where token-level optimization is genuinely beneficial?** The GSPO-token variant hints at this possibility, but no tasks are identified where per-token advantage adjustment is necessary.

A balanced assessment would acknowledge that no algorithm is universally optimal. The paper's framing of GSPO as a "robust and scalable algorithmic foundation" (Section 1) is aspirational but not exhaustively validated.

### 6.10 Summary of Limitations

| Limitation Category | Severity | Mitigation |
|---------------------|----------|------------|
| Hyperparameter transfer burden | **High** | Requires new tuning; no transfer from GRPO/PPO |
| Limited experimental scope (MoE only) | **Medium** | Dense model benefits unproven |
| GSPO-token unvalidated | **Medium** | Theoretical only; no experiments |
| Infrastructure claims unproven | **Low-Medium** | Plausible but not demonstrated |
| Aggressive clipping trade-off | **Low** | Discards 15% of token gradients |
| Group size sensitivity | **Medium** | Optimal $G$ not analyzed |
| Sequence length bounds unknown | **Medium** | No explicit length scaling analysis |
| Off-policy divergence limits | **Medium** | Mini-batch count not optimized |
| No failure mode analysis | **Medium** | Conditions for underperformance unknown |

For practitioners, the key takeaway is that GSPO addresses **specific, well-documented failure modes** of GRPO (MoE instability, token-level noise accumulation) but introduces new tuning requirements and leaves several practical questions unanswered. The algorithm is best suited for teams training large MoE models who have experienced GRPO stability issues and can invest in hyperparameter tuning. Teams with stable GRPO training on dense models may find the migration cost outweighs the benefits based on the evidence presented in this paper.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of Group Sequence Policy Optimization (GSPO) represents more than an incremental algorithmic tweak; it signals a structural shift in how reinforcement learning (RL) is applied to large language models (LLMs). By resolving the theoretical inconsistencies of token-level importance sampling, GSPO removes a critical bottleneck that previously limited the scale and stability of RL training. This section outlines how this work reshapes the field, identifies high-priority avenues for follow-up research, and provides concrete guidance for practitioners considering adoption.

### 7.1 Shifting the Paradigm for RL Scaling

GSPO challenges the longstanding assumption that token-level optimization is necessary or even desirable for autoregressive language models. The paper establishes that token-level importance weights violate the statistical requirements of importance sampling when applied to single-token samples (Section 3). This insight has three profound implications for the field:

*   **Redefining Stability Boundaries:** Prior work attributed model collapse to hyperparameter sensitivity or model size. GSPO demonstrates that collapse is often an algorithmic inevitability when token-level noise accumulates over long sequences (Section 1). This shifts the focus from "tuning around instability" to "designing for stability."
*   **Unlocking MoE Scalability:** Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models were previously constrained by the need for **Routing Replay** to maintain expert consistency during RL (Section 5.3). GSPO eliminates this requirement, allowing MoE architectures to scale without artificial routing constraints. This suggests future large-scale models can be denser in expert count without incurring proportional RL training overhead.
*   **Alignment of Optimization and Reward:** The principle that the optimization unit must match the reward unit (Section 4.2) may generalize beyond single-turn RL. Tasks where rewards are inherently sequence-level (e.g., code execution, mathematical proof verification) are natural fits for GSPO, whereas tasks with dense, token-level rewards might require further investigation.

### 7.2 Priority Research Avenues

While GSPO resolves critical stability issues, the paper explicitly identifies areas where empirical validation is incomplete. Follow-up research should prioritize these gaps to solidify GSPO's position as a foundational algorithm:

*   **Dense Model Validation:** All experiments in this paper use the **Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base** MoE model (Section 5.1). It remains an open question whether GSPO provides comparable efficiency gains on dense architectures where expert-activation volatility is not a factor. Researchers should replicate Figure 1 on dense models to isolate the benefits of sequence-level clipping from MoE-specific stability.
*   **Empirical Validation of GSPO-token:** The **GSPO-token** variant (Section 4.3) offers token-level advantage flexibility while maintaining sequence-level stability, yet no experimental results are provided. Future work should test GSPO-token on multi-turn dialogue or tool-use tasks where per-turn rewards differ, verifying if the theoretical gradient equivalence (Equation 17) holds in practice under varying advantage distributions.
*   **Infrastructure Benchmarking:** Section 5.4 claims GSPO enables using inference engine likelihoods directly, avoiding recomputation in the training engine. However, no quantitative metrics (e.g., GPU-hours saved, latency reduction) are provided. Systems researchers should measure the end-to-end throughput gains in disaggregated training-inference frameworks to validate this infrastructure benefit.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** The clipping ranges for GSPO (**3e-4** left, **4e-4** right) differ by two orders of magnitude from GRPO (Section 5.1). A systematic sensitivity analysis is needed to determine how robust GSPO is to deviations from these values. This would reduce the tuning burden identified in Section 6.1 and help establish default ranges for different model scales.

### 7.3 Practical Deployment Scenarios

Based on the paper's findings, GSPO is not a universal replacement for all RL algorithms but is specifically advantageous in certain high-stakes deployment contexts. Practitioners should consider GSPO in the following scenarios:

*   **Large-Scale MoE Training:** If you are training MoE models with RL and encounter convergence issues or expert-activation volatility, GSPO is the primary candidate. It removes the need for **Routing Replay**, simplifying the training stack and reducing memory overhead (Section 5.3).
*   **Long-Context Reasoning Tasks:** For tasks requiring long generation chains (e.g., competition mathematics, complex coding), GSPO's sequence-level noise reduction prevents the variance accumulation that plagues GRPO (Section 3). The length normalization in $s_i(\theta)$ (Equation 7) ensures stable gradients regardless of response length.
*   **Disaggregated Training Systems:** Organizations using separate engines for training (e.g., Megatron) and inference (e.g., vLLM, SGLang) should prioritize GSPO. Its tolerance for precision discrepancies allows direct use of inference likelihoods, potentially eliminating the need for expensive likelihood recomputation steps (Section 5.4).
*   **High-Compute RL Scaling:** When scaling RL training compute is a priority, GSPO's superior training efficiency (Figure 1) suggests it will yield better performance per FLOP than GRPO. This is critical for teams operating near compute capacity limits.

### 7.4 Integration and Reproduction Guidance

For teams intending to implement GSPO, the following practical guidelines derive directly from the paper's experimental configuration and theoretical constraints:

*   **Hyperparameter Initialization:** Do not reuse GRPO or PPO clipping hyperparameters. Start with the paper's specified values:
    *   **Left Clipping Threshold:** `$3 \times 10^{-4}$`
    *   **Right Clipping Threshold:** `$4 \times 10^{-4}$`
    *   **Mini-batch Partitioning:** 4 mini-batches per rollout batch (Section 5.1).
    *   **Note:** These values are specific to the sequence-level ratio scale. Using token-level thresholds (e.g., 0.2) will likely result in no learning signal due to excessive clipping.
*   **Likelihood Computation:** Ensure your pipeline computes sequence likelihoods `$\pi_\theta(y|x)$` accurately. If using an inference engine, verify that the precision discrepancy does not exceed the tolerance of the sequence-level ratio. The paper suggests this is manageable, but monitoring the distribution of `$s_i(\theta)$` is recommended during initial runs.
*   **Reward Normalization:** GSPO relies on group-based advantage estimation (Equation 6). Ensure your rollout batch size supports a sufficient group size `$G$` to compute meaningful mean and standard deviation statistics. Small `$G$` values may lead to noisy advantage estimates.
*   **Migration Strategy:** If migrating from GRPO:
    1.  **Disable Routing Replay:** If using MoE, remove any caching logic for expert routing.
    2.  **Adjust Clipping:** Implement sequence-level clipping logic rather than token-level.
    3.  **Monitor Clipping Fractions:** Expect clipping fractions around **0.15** (15%) rather than GRPO's **0.0013** (0.13%) (Figure 2). High clipping is normal and indicates the algorithm is filtering noisy samples correctly.
*   **When to Stick with GRPO:** If you are training small dense models (<10B parameters) with short responses and have stable GRPO training, the migration cost may outweigh the benefits. GSPO's advantages are most pronounced at scale, with MoE architectures, and on long-reasoning tasks (Section 5.3).

In summary, GSPO provides a theoretically grounded and empirically validated path forward for scaling RL in language models. By aligning the optimization objective with the reward structure, it resolves fundamental instability issues that previously hindered progress. While hyperparameter tuning and dense model validation remain open tasks, the algorithm's success in stabilizing MoE training and improving efficiency positions it as a critical component for the next generation of large-scale reasoning models.
