## 1. Executive Summary

This survey addresses the limitations of standard Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and single-shot prompting in handling open-ended tasks requiring critical thinking and multi-source verification by formalizing the Deep Research (DR) paradigm, where LLMs act as autonomous agents utilizing tools like search engines. The authors contribute a three-stage roadmap (Agentic Search, Integrated Research, Full-stack AI Scientist) and a systematic taxonomy of four key components—query planning, information acquisition, memory management, and answer generation—while consolidating optimization techniques such as agentic reinforcement learning found in systems like `Search-R1` and `R1-Searcher`. This work establishes a unified foundation for consistent benchmarking across datasets like `GAIA` and `BrowseComp`, ultimately guiding future development toward reliable, verifiable AI-driven scientific discovery.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### 2.1. The Core Problem: Beyond Single-Shot Generation

The fundamental gap this survey addresses stems from a critical mismatch between what modern Large Language Models (LLMs) can do and what complex real-world tasks actually require. While LLMs trained on web-scale corpora have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in mathematical reasoning, creative writing, and software engineering, many practical applications demand something more: **critical thinking across multiple information sources with verifiable, grounded outputs**. 

Consider a researcher asking "What are the current clinical trial results for drug X in treating condition Y, and how do they compare to alternative treatments?" This query cannot be answered through:

- **Single-shot prompting**, which relies entirely on the model's parametric knowledge (what was learned during training) and cannot access information published after the training cutoff
- **Standard Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)**, which performs a static retrieval step before generation but lacks the ability to iteratively refine queries, verify conflicting sources, or synthesize evidence across multiple documents

The paper identifies this as the central problem: **existing approaches treat retrieval as a heuristic augmentation step rather than an integral part of a flexible research workflow**. This limitation becomes increasingly problematic as tasks grow more open-ended and require sustained reasoning over long horizons.

### 2.2. Why This Problem Matters

The importance of addressing this gap operates on both practical and theoretical levels:

**Real-World Impact:**
- **Healthcare and Finance**: In safety-critical domains, generating answers without verifiable evidence chains can lead to serious errors. The paper explicitly notes that "when no relevant evidence can be retrieved, generating an answer regardless risks misleading users, particularly in safety-critical domains such as healthcare and finance" (Section 6.1)
- **Scientific Discovery**: Autonomous research agents could accelerate scientific progress by automating literature review, hypothesis generation, and experimental design. However, this requires systems that can distinguish between novel insights and hallucinated claims
- **Decision Support**: Market analysis, policy briefs, and competitive intelligence all require synthesizing heterogeneous evidence while managing conflicts and uncertainty—capabilities beyond current RAG systems

**Theoretical Significance:**
- **Long-Horizon Reasoning**: DR systems represent a step toward agents capable of coordinating multiple subtasks over extended interaction sequences, a fundamental challenge in AI
- **Grounded Language Generation**: The paradigm addresses the hallucination problem by establishing "verifiable mechanisms that align natural language outputs with grounded evidence" (Section 2.3)
- **Human-AI Collaboration**: Reliable research assistants could transform how humans conduct inquiry, but only if outputs are trustworthy and traceable to sources

### 2.3. Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

Before Deep Research emerged, several approaches attempted to enhance LLMs with external knowledge access. Understanding where these fall short clarifies why DR represents a distinct paradigm:

**Standard RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation):**
Conventional RAG systems operate in a **static retrieval loop**, relying solely on pre-indexed corpora (Section 2.3). The typical workflow retrieves documents once, then generates an answer. Key limitations include:

- **No iterative refinement**: If initial retrieval misses critical information, the system cannot reformulate queries based on what it learned
- **Fixed action space**: RAG cannot invoke diverse tools (web APIs, code executors, calculators) beyond simple document retrieval
- **No memory management**: Intermediate findings are not stored or updated across multi-turn interactions
- **Short reasoning horizon**: The workflow is essentially single-turn, unsuitable for tasks requiring progressive information gathering

Table 1 in the paper provides a direct comparison showing RAG lacks capabilities like "Use of Various Tools," "Reflection for Action Correction," "Task-solving Memory Management," and "Innovation and Hypothesis Proposal."

**Web-Based Agents:**
Some prior work explored LLMs interacting with web environments (e.g., WebGPT, WebArena). However, as the paper notes in Section 1, "Most existing work mainly summarizes developments in related areas such as Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and web-based agents" without providing "a comprehensive survey that systematically analyzes the key components, technical details, and open challenges of DR."

**Scaling LLM Parameters:**
Simply increasing model size does not solve the fundamental problem. The paper states that many real-world tasks present "fundamental limitations of existing approaches" that "cannot be solved by conventional RAG or scaling LLM parameters alone" (Section 2.3). Parametric knowledge is inherently static and cannot provide up-to-date information or verifiable citations.

### 2.4. How This Survey Positions Itself

This paper positions itself as the **first comprehensive and systematic overview** of the Deep Research paradigm. The authors explicitly state in Section 1: "Despite rapid progress, there remains no comprehensive survey that systematically analyzes the key components, technical details, and open challenges of DR."

**Distinction from Related Surveys:**
- **vs. RAG Surveys**: Existing RAG surveys (e.g., Gao et al. [89], Fan et al. [72]) focus on retrieval as augmentation, not autonomous research workflows
- **vs. Agent Surveys**: General agent surveys cover broader topics but do not provide the fine-grained taxonomy of DR-specific components (query planning, information acquisition, memory management, answer generation)
- **vs. Concurrent DR Surveys**: The paper acknowledges other emerging DR surveys (e.g., Wenlin Zhang et al. [458], Yuxuan Huang et al. [125]) but positions this work as providing "a clear roadmap, foundational components, practical implementation techniques, important challenges, and future directions" with systematic taxonomy

**Unique Contributions:**
The survey establishes four distinguishing contributions (Section 1):

1. **Three-Stage Roadmap**: Formalizes DR evolution from Agentic Search → Integrated Research → Full-stack AI Scientist, distinguishing it from related paradigms
2. **Four-Component Taxonomy**: Provides fine-grained sub-taxonomies for query planning (parallel, sequential, tree-based), information acquisition (retrieval tools, timing, filtering), memory management (consolidation, indexing, updating, forgetting), and answer generation (integration, synthesis, structuring, presentation)
3. **Optimization Techniques**: Consolidates workflow prompting, supervised fine-tuning, and agentic reinforcement learning approaches with specific system examples
4. **Evaluation Framework**: Establishes consistent benchmarking criteria across information seeking, report generation, AI for research, and software engineering scenarios

**Positioning Statement:**
The paper positions Deep Research not as an incremental improvement to RAG, but as a **distinct paradigm** that "adopts a more flexible, autonomous workflow that eschews handcrafted pipelines and aims to produce coherent, evidence-grounded reports" (Section 1). This survey serves as the foundational reference for researchers entering this space, providing "a unified roadmap for understanding and building future DR systems" (Section 8).

By anchoring claims to specific sections, figures, and tables throughout the paper (e.g., Figure 1 for component overview, Table 1 for RAG comparison, Figure 2 for full taxonomy), this survey enables readers to navigate the rapidly evolving DR landscape with clarity about what distinguishes Deep Research from prior approaches and why this distinction matters for both research and deployment.

## 3. Technical Approach

This survey paper systematically catalogs and organizes the technical landscape of Deep Research (DR) systems rather than proposing a single novel algorithm. The core idea is that DR systems function as **autonomous research agents** that iteratively decompose complex queries, acquire evidence through tool use, manage intermediate findings in memory, and synthesize verifiable long-form outputs. The technical architecture treats research as a closed-loop workflow where each component feeds into the next, enabling sustained reasoning over extended interaction horizons that standard RAG systems cannot support.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

A Deep Research system is an AI agent that acts like a human researcher: it plans what information to find, searches for it using tools like search engines, remembers what it has learned across multiple steps, and writes up a coherent report with citations. The system solves the problem of answering complex, open-ended questions that require gathering information from multiple sources over time by organizing the research process into four interconnected components that cycle iteratively until a complete, evidence-grounded answer is produced.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The DR system architecture consists of four primary components arranged in a closed-loop workflow as illustrated in **Figure 1** (Section 3). **Query Planning** takes the user's initial research question as input and decomposes it into structured sub-queries that guide the workflow. **Information Acquisition** receives these sub-queries and invokes external retrieval tools (search engines, web APIs, databases) to gather relevant documents, then filters the results for quality and relevance. **Memory Management** stores the accumulated findings in structured formats (databases, graphs, or embeddings), updates them as new information arrives, and forgets outdated content to prevent interference. **Answer Generation** synthesizes all stored evidence into a coherent, long-form response with explicit citations and transparent reasoning chains. Information flows cyclically: the answer generation component can trigger new query planning if gaps are detected, creating an iterative research loop rather than a single-pass pipeline.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **Query Planning strategies** come first because they determine the entire research trajectory—how the system breaks down the problem dictates what information will be sought and in what order.
- **Information Acquisition** follows naturally since planning produces queries that must be executed through retrieval tools with appropriate timing and filtering.
- **Memory Management** is explained third because it governs how acquired information is stored and maintained across the multi-turn research process, enabling long-horizon coherence.
- **Answer Generation** comes fourth as it represents the culmination of the workflow, synthesizing all upstream components into the final output.
- **Optimization Techniques** are covered fifth to explain how each component can be trained or improved through prompting, fine-tuning, or reinforcement learning.
- **Evaluation Frameworks** conclude the technical discussion to show how system performance is measured across different application scenarios.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### 3.4.1 The Three-Stage Capability Roadmap

The paper formalizes Deep Research evolution through three progressive phases shown in **Table 1** (Section 2.3), where each phase expands the system's capabilities beyond the previous one. **Phase I (Agentic Search)** systems specialize in finding correct sources and extracting answers with minimal synthesis, typically reformulating user queries via rewriting or decomposition to improve recall, then retrieving and re-ranking candidate documents before producing concise answers with explicit citations. Representative applications include open-domain question answering and multi-hop question answering where truth is localized to a small set of sources, with evaluation prioritizing retrieval recall@$k$ and answer exact matching. **Phase II (Integrated Research)** systems move beyond isolated facts to produce coherent, structured reports that integrate heterogeneous evidence while managing conflicts and uncertainty through explicitly iterative research loops that plan sub-questions, retrieve evidence from various raw content (HTML, tables, charts), and synthesize comprehensive narrative reports for applications like market analysis and policy briefs. **Phase III (Full-stack AI Scientist)** represents the most ambitious stage where DR agents are expected to generate hypotheses, conduct experimental validation or ablation studies, critique existing claims, and propose novel perspectives for applications including paper reviewing and scientific discovery, with evaluation emphasizing novelty, insightfulness, argumentative coherence, and reproducibility of claims.

#### 3.4.2 Query Planning: Decomposing Complex Questions

Query Planning refers to the process of transforming a complex and logically intricate question into a structured sequence of executable sub-queries, each of which can be addressed incrementally to enable stepwise reasoning and knowledge acquisition. **Figure 3** (Section 3.1) illustrates three widely-used strategies that differ in how they structure the decomposition space.

**Parallel Planning** operates by rewriting or decomposing the original query into multiple sub-questions in a single pass, typically without iterative interaction with downstream components as shown in **Figure 3(a)**. The primary advantage lies in efficiency since simultaneous generation enables parallel processing of sub-queries. Representative work includes Least-to-Most Prompting which guides GPT-3 to decompose complex tasks into ordered sequences of simpler sub-queries in a few-shot setting, and CoVE which prompts LLMs to generate multiple independent sub-questions and ground each with evidence in parallel. However, parallel planning has two primary limitations: first, they typically operate in a one-shot fashion without mechanisms to incorporate intermediate evidence or adaptively allocate computational resources; second, they often ignore data and logical dependencies across sub-queries since parallel execution assumes conditional independence when many real-world queries involve sequential reasoning where later subtasks depend on earlier resolutions.

**Sequential Planning** decomposes the original query through multiple iterative steps where each round of decomposition builds upon the outputs of previous rounds as illustrated in **Figure 3(b)**. At each stage, the sequential planning may invoke different modules or external tools to process intermediate results, enabling dynamic, feedback-driven reasoning that performs logically dependent query decompositions intractable for pre-processing planning. Representative work includes LLatrieval which introduces an iterative query planner that leverages the LLM to pinpoint missing knowledge whenever current documents fail verification, generating new queries and repeating the cycle until accumulated context fully supports a verifiable answer. DRAGIN introduces a query planner that utilizes self-attention scores to select the most context-relevant tokens from the entire generation history and reformulate them into concise queries, producing more accurate queries compared to static last-sentence strategies. ReSP dynamically guides each retrieval iteration by formulating novel sub-questions explicitly targeted at identified information gaps, conditioning reformulation on both global and local memory states while disallowing previously issued sub-questions to mitigate over-planning risks. However, excessive reasoning turns or overly long reasoning chains can incur substantial computational costs and latency, and increased turns may introduce cumulative noise and error propagation potentially causing instability during reinforcement learning training.

**Tree-based Planning** integrates features of both parallel and sequential planning by recursively treating each sub-query as a node within a structured search space, typically represented as a tree or directed acyclic graph (DAG) as shown in **Figure 3(c)**. This structure enables advanced search algorithms like Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to explore and refine potential reasoning paths, supporting more flexible and fine-grained decomposition than linear or flat decompositions. A representative example is RAG-Star which leverages MCTS with Upper Confidence Bound for Trees (UCT) to guide a query planner in iterative decomposition, where at each iteration the planning model selects the most promising node using the UCT criterion, expands it by generating a sub-query and answer using a language model, evaluates expansion quality via a retrieval-based reward model, and back-propagates the resulting score until a satisfactory final answer is obtained. Other examples include DTA and DeepSieve which use tree-based planners to restructure sequential reasoning traces into a DAG, enabling aggregation of intermediate answers along multiple branches to capture hierarchical and non-linear dependencies. DeepRAG introduces tree-based planning via binary-tree exploration to iteratively decompose queries and decide between parametric versus retrieved reasoning, yielding large accuracy gains with fewer retrievals. Training robust tree-based planning modules remains challenging, requiring precise dependency modeling, careful trade-offs between speed and quality, addressing data scarcity, and tackling credit assignment issues in reinforcement learning.

#### 3.4.3 Information Acquisition: Retrieval Tools, Timing, and Filtering

Information Acquisition encompasses three critical decisions: what retrieval tools to use, when to trigger retrieval during the reasoning process, and how to filter retrieved documents for quality and relevance.

**Retrieval Tools** (Section 3.2.1) identify relevant information from large-scale corpora in response to queries, typically containing indexing and search techniques organized by modality. **Text Retrieval** can be organized into three families: lexical retrieval matches documents based on exact term overlaps and statistical term weighting including traditional approaches like TF-IDF and BM25 as well as neural sparse models that learn to expand queries and documents with relevant terms while maintaining interpretable inverted-index structures; semantic retrieval refers to dense neural methods that encode queries and documents into continuous vector spaces to capture semantic similarity beyond exact term matching, widely adopted in recent works; commercial web search (like Google or Bing) provides access to real-time information leveraging massive-scale web crawling and indexing with sophisticated ranking algorithms considering authority and freshness signals, diverging from lexical and semantic retrieval by enabling research agents to access current events and dynamic content missing from static corpora. **Multimodal Retrieval** aims to mine multimodal information including text, layout, and visuals (figures, tables, charts) while preserving grounded pointers (spans, cells, coordinates) for verifiable citation, organized into three classes: text-aware retrieval with layout indexes titles, captions, callouts, and surrounding prose leveraging document understanding models like LayoutLM and Donut plus layout/metadata filters; visual retrieval via text-image similarity encodes figures and chart thumbnails with CLIP, SigLIP, or BLIP and performs approximate nearest neighbor search for text-to-image matching; structure-aware retrieval over parsed tables and charts indexes axes, legends, data marks, and table schemas to support grounded lookup of numeric facts and relations. Compared to text-only retrieval, multimodal retrieval captures visually encoded information and numeric trends that text-based methods overlook, facilitates cross-modal verification through hybrid fusion, and enables grounded citations using layout parsing and chart understanding, though it introduces higher computational costs for visual processing, sensitivity to OCR errors and chart format variations, and complexity in aligning information across modalities.

**Retrieval Timing** (Section 3.2.2) refers to determining when a model should trigger retrieval tools during information seeking, also known as adaptive retrieval, since blindly performing retrieval at every step is often sub-optimal due to computational overhead and the risk that low-quality documents may mislead the model or degrade reasoning performance. Adaptive retrieval aims to invoke retrieval only when the model lacks sufficient knowledge, requiring the model to recognize its own knowledge boundaries. Prior work follows two main directions: estimating and enhancing a model's ability to recognize knowledge boundaries for a given query, and optimizing the retrieval-trigger model in multi-step settings to maximize downstream task performance. **Confidence Estimation** approaches measure the alignment between model confidence and factual correctness through four categories: probabilistic confidence treats token-level generation probabilities as confidence in the answer, though LLMs can be poorly calibrated producing overconfident predictions even when incorrect; consistency-based confidence represents confidence via semantic consistency across multiple responses since a confident model should generate highly consistent answers across runs; confidence estimation based on internal states leverages the finding that LLM internal states capture factuality of generated content, with factuality-related signals existing even in pre-generation states enabling prediction of output correctness; verbalized confidence enables LLMs to express confidence in natural language akin to humans, with some methods explicitly training models to verbalize confidence using correctness-based supervision. **Representative Adaptive Retrieval Approaches** trace an evolution from fixed per-step retrieval to dynamically triggered retrieval to RL-based systems: IR-CoT enforces retrieval after every reasoning step ensuring continual grounding but at efficiency cost; probabilistic strategy triggers retrieval based on token-generation probabilities when the model produces tokens with low confidence; consistency-based strategy evaluates consistency across responses generated by multiple models and languages, triggering retrieval when cross-model or cross-lingual agreement is low; internal states probing uses model internal states as more faithful reflection of confidence to guide adaptive retrieval decisions; verbalized strategy enables the model to directly express confidence via natural language, generating special tokens to indicate retrieval need as in ReAct and Self-RAG which trains models to explicitly express uncertainty through the special token `<retrieve>`; Search-o1 introduces a Reason-in-Documents module prompting selective search invocation during reasoning; Search-R1 frames retrieval as part of the environment and employs reinforcement learning to jointly optimize both when and what to retrieve.

**Information Filtering** (Section 3.2.3) refers to selecting, refining, or transforming retrieved documents so only the most relevant and reliable evidence passes to subsequent steps, since retrieved information often contains considerable noise including entirely irrelevant content or plausible-looking statements providing incorrect context. **Figure 4** summarizes three filtering approaches. **Document Selection** aims to rank candidate documents based on relevance and usefulness, selecting top-$k$ helpful documents through three strategies: point-wise selection independently scores each candidate document, commonly using fine-tuned embedding models encoding query and document separately with relevance estimated via inner-product similarity, or cross-encoders taking query-document concatenation as input to predict binary relevance scores, or LLMs trained to output special tokens like `<ISREL>` or identifier `True` to indicate relevance; pair-wise selection compares relevance of two input candidate information snippets and predicts which is more relevant, with representative work PRP adopting pairwise-ranking-prompting where the LLM receives query and two documents to decide relevance, performing comparison twice with swapped document order to mitigate positional bias; list-wise selection directly selects the final set of relevant documents from the candidate list, with RankGPT feeding entire candidate sequences into an LLM leveraging prompt engineering for global ranking, TourRank using tournament-inspired strategies, ListT5 proposing list re-ranking based on Fusion-in-Decoder architecture independently encoding multiple documents in parallel, and recent work employing reasoning models for list-wise selection through chain-of-thought modeling. **Content Compression** removes redundant or irrelevant information from retrieved knowledge to increase useful content density within the model's context, falling into two categories: lexical-based methods condense retrieved text into concise natural language including only key points related to the query, with RECOMP fine-tuning smaller open-source LLMs to summarize input documents where ground truth is synthesized by prompting powerful commercial LLMs like GPT-4, Chain-of-Note introducing reading-notes mechanisms compelling models to assess document relevance and extract critical information before answer generation, BIDER eliminating reliance on external model distillation by synthesizing Key Supporting Evidence for each document using it for compressor supervised fine-tuning and further optimizing with PPO based on gains in answer correctness, and RankCoT implicitly learning document reranking during information refinement through self-reflection to generate summary candidates then using DPO training to encourage higher probabilities for correct summaries; embedding-based methods compress context into dense embedding sequences which can store information flexibly and be more efficient than lexical-based methods, with ICAE using encoders to compress context into fixed-length embedding sequences designing training tasks to align embedding space with answer generation models, COCOM jointly fine-tuning encoder and answer generation model enhancing the latter's ability to capture embedding semantics, xRAG focusing on extreme compression rates introducing lightweight bridging modules initialized with two-layer MLPs trained through paraphrase pretraining and context-aware instruction tuning to project document embedding vectors into single tokens in the answer generation model's representation space, ACC-RAG adapting compression rates for different documents employing hierarchical compressors to produce multi-granularity embedding sequences and dynamically selecting rates based on query complexity. **Rule-based Cleaning** applies effective methods for cleaning externally sourced information with specific structures, such as HtmlRAG applying rule-based compression to remove structurally present but semantically empty elements like CSS styling and JavaScript code from retrieved web pages combined with two-stage block-tree pruning using embeddings for coarse pruning followed by generative models for fine-grained pruning, and TableRAG accurately extracting core table information through schema retrieval identifying key column names and data types plus cell retrieval locating high-frequency cell value pairs to address context length limitations and information loss in large table understanding. However, incorporating additional filtering modules typically incurs additional computational costs and increased latency, and overly aggressive filtering may remove useful or correct information degrading model performance, making balancing filtering precision and information retention crucial.

#### 3.4.4 Memory Management: Consolidation, Indexing, Updating, and Forgetting

Memory Management is a foundational component governing the dynamic lifecycle of context used by DR agents in complex, long-horizon tasks, aiming to maintain coherent and relevant task-solving context through four core operations illustrated in **Figure 5** (Section 3.3).

**Memory Consolidation** (Section 3.3.1) transforms transient, short-term information such as user dialogues or tool execution outputs into stable, long-term representations, encoding and abstracting raw inputs to create durable memory engrams laying groundwork for efficient long-term storage and retrieval. Two primary paradigms have emerged: **Unstructured Memory Consolidation** distills lengthy interaction histories or raw texts into high-level concise summaries or key event logs, with MemoryBank processing and distilling conversations into high-level daily event summaries helping construct long-term user profiles, MemoChat summarizing conversation segments by abstracting main topics discussed, ChatGPT-RSum adopting recursive summarization strategies for extended conversations, Generative Agents utilizing reflection mechanisms triggered by sufficient event accumulation to generate abstract thoughts as new consolidated memories, and GITM summarizing key actions from multiple successful plans into common reference memory for generalizable plans; **Structured Memory Consolidation** transforms unstructured information into highly organized formats such as databases, graphs, or trees with structural encoding designed to capture complex inter-entity relationships, with TiM extracting entity relationships from raw information storing them as tuples in structured databases, ChatDB leveraging databases as symbolic memory transforming raw inputs into queryable relational formats, AriGraph implementing memory graphs where knowledge is represented as vertices and interconnections as edges, HippoRAG constructing knowledge graphs over entities, phrases, and summaries forming interconnected webs of fragmented knowledge units, and MemTree building and updating tree structures by traversing from roots and deciding whether to deepen trees with new information or create new leaf nodes based on semantic similarity.

**Memory Indexing** (Section 3.3.2) involves constructing navigational maps over consolidated memories analogous to library catalogs or book indexes for efficient information retrieval, operating on already consolidated memories to create efficient semantically rich retrieval pathways through auxiliary access structures enhancing retrieval in both efficiency and relevance. Effective indexing encodes temporal and relational dependencies among memories beyond simple keyword matching, typically achieved by generating auxiliary codes such as vector embeddings, summaries, or entity tags serving as retrieval entry points, requiring specialized search techniques like Locality-Sensitive Hashing, Hierarchical Navigable Small World graphs, or libraries like FAISS for high-speed similarity search. Three established paradigms organize access mechanisms: **Signal-enhanced Indexing** augments consolidated memory entries with auxiliary metadata including emotional context, topics, and keywords functioning as granular pivots for context-aware retrieval, with LongMemEval enhancing memory keys by integrating temporal and semantic signals to improve retrieval precision and Multiple Memory System decomposing experiences into discrete components like cognitive perspectives and semantic facts facilitating multifaceted retrieval strategies; **Graph-based Indexing** leverages graph structures where memories are nodes and relationships are edges as sophisticated indexes enabling complex multi-hop reasoning by traversing connection chains to locate information not explicitly linked to initial queries, with HippoRAG using lightweight knowledge graphs to explicitly model inter-memory relations enabling structured interpretable access and A-Mem adopting dynamic strategies where agents autonomously link related memory notes progressively growing flexible access networks; **Timeline-based Indexing** creates temporal indexes by organizing memory entries along chronological or causal sequences providing historical access pathways essential for understanding progression and maintaining conversational coherence, with Theanine arranging memories along evolving timelines facilitating retrieval based on relevance and temporal dynamics and Zep introducing bi-temporal models for knowledge graphs indexing each fact with $t_{\text{valid}}$ and $t_{\text{invalid}}$ timestamps allowing agents to navigate memory based on temporal validity.

**Memory Updating** (Section 3.3.3) involves reactivation and modification of existing knowledge in response to new information or environmental feedback, essential for maintaining consistency, accuracy, and relevance of the agent's internal world model enabling continual learning and adaptive behavior. Two updating strategies emerge depending on whether memory is external (non-parametric) or internal (parametric) to the model. **Non-Parametric Memory Updating** stores memory in external formats like vector databases or structured files, updated via explicit discrete operations on the data itself offering flexibility and transparency, with key operations including integration and conflict updating focusing on incorporating new information and refining existing entries to maintain logical consistency (Mem0 employs LLMs to manage knowledge bases through explicit ADD or UPDATE operations to resolve inconsistencies, Zep updates knowledge graphs by modifying existing fact effective time ranges setting invalidation timestamps to reflect newer facts superseding old ones, TiM curates memory using MERGE operations to combine related facts into coherent representations) and self-reflection updating enabling agents to iteratively refine knowledge by reflecting on past experiences inspired by human memory reconsolidation (Reflexion and Voyager implement through verbal self-correction and skill library updates, A-Mem triggers Memory Evolution processes re-evaluating and autonomously refining previously linked memories based on new contextual information). **Parametric Memory Updating** encodes memory directly in model weights, updated by modifying internal representations which is typically more complex and computationally intensive, with three main approaches: global updating integrates new knowledge by continuing model training on additional datasets which is effective for large-scale adaptation but computationally expensive and prone to catastrophic forgetting (Memory-R1 trains dedicated Memory Manager agents to learn optimal policies for modification operations like ADD and UPDATE moving beyond heuristic rules, recent frameworks employ Direct Preference Optimization to fine-tune model memory utilization strategies); localized updating modifies specific facts in model parameters without requiring full retraining especially suited for online settings needing rapid adaptation like updating user preferences, typically following locate-and-edit strategies or using meta-learning to predict weight adjustments while preserving unrelated knowledge; modular updating avoids risks of continual weight modification by distilling knowledge into dedicated plug-and-play parametric modules (MLP Memory and Memory Decoder train lightweight external modules to imitate output distributions of non-parametric kNN retrievers, effectively compiling large external knowledge corpora into compact module weights that can attach to any compatible LLM providing specialized knowledge without modifying base model parameters, avoiding catastrophic forgetting and reducing real-time retrieval latency).

**Memory Forgetting** (Section 3.4.4) constitutes a fundamental mechanism enabling selective removal or suppression of outdated, irrelevant, or potentially erroneous memory content, functioning as a subtractive process streamlining memory stores by eliminating specific content rather than modifying existing knowledge like updating does. Two broad categories exist: **Passive Forgetting** simulates natural decay of human memory where infrequently accessed or temporally irrelevant memories gradually lose prominence, particularly critical for managing immediate working memory or context windows with implementations governed by automated time-based rules rather than explicit content analysis (MemGPT employs First-In-First-Out queues for recent interactions automatically moving oldest messages from main context into long-term storage, MemoryBank draws inspiration from Ebbinghaus forgetting curve where memory traces decay over time unless reinforced allowing agents to naturally prioritize recent content, MEM1 employs use-and-discard policies where after each interaction agents synthesize essential information into compact states and immediately discard all prior contextual data to maintain constant memory consumption); **Active Forgetting** involves intentional targeted removal or invalidation of specific memory content as deliberate actions often triggered by contradiction detection or need to correct inaccurate information, with implementation varying by memory type: for non-parametric memory, active forgetting involves direct data manipulation (Mem0 implements explicit DELETE commands to remove outdated or contradictory facts, TiM introduces dedicated FORGET operations to actively purge irrelevant or incorrect thoughts from memory caches, reinforcement learning can train specialized Memory Manager agents to autonomously decide when to execute DELETE commands as in Memory-R1, AriGraph maintains structured memory graphs by removing outdated vertices and edges, Zep uses edge invalidation assigning invalid timestamps to outdated entries effectively retiring them without permanent deletion); for parametric memory, active forgetting is typically achieved through machine unlearning techniques modifying model internal parameters to erase specific knowledge without full retraining including locating and deactivating specific neurons or adjusting training objectives to promote targeted information removal (MEOW facilitates efficient forgetting by fine-tuning LLMs on generated contradictory facts effectively overwriting undesirable memories stored in weights).

#### 3.4.5 Answer Generation: From Evidence Integration to Multimodal Presentation

Answer Generation represents the culminating stage synthesizing information from upstream components (query planning, information acquisition, memory systems) into coherent, comprehensive, well-supported responses accurately reflecting user intent, addressing complex challenges like reconciling conflicting evidence, maintaining long-range coherence, and structuring outputs with transparent reasoning and proper citations as illustrated in **Figure 6** (Section 3.4).

**Integrating Upstream Information** (Section 3.4.1) ensures every statement is grounded in verifiable external evidence as the main principle of trustworthy answer generation, integrating sub-queries from query planning, ranked and potentially conflicting evidence, and evolving contextual state stored in memory. The most common approach integrates ranked evidence from retrieval modules, with frameworks like Self-RAG employing dynamic integration by adaptively retrieving passages on demand then generating reflection tokens to assess retrieved information relevance and its own generation, effectively integrating internal self-correction mechanisms to steer synthesis. Moving beyond static evidence, advanced systems integrate query plans with evolving memory states to ensure long-range coherence through Stateful Query Planning paradigms: graph-centric frameworks like Plan-on-Graph explicitly integrate plans with dynamic memory storing sub-goal status, explored paths, and retrieved entities, with memory actively used during reflection steps to guide and self-correct subsequent planning tightly coupling reasoning state with generation process; search-based frameworks like MCTS-OPS formalize this by treating MCTS trees themselves as states of evolving query plans, integrating experiential memory (node values from past rollouts) to guide SELECTION and EXPANSION of next planning steps ensuring final answers synthesize full problem-solving process context.

**Synthesizing Evidence and Maintaining Coherence** (Section 3.4.2) requires resolving informational conflicts and sustaining coherent, information-dense narration across extended outputs. **Resolving Conflicting Evidence** employs three major strategies building on fact-verification paradigms: credibility-aware attention intelligently weighs evidence based on source, assigning higher scores to information from more credible sources (top-tier scientific journals) compared to less reliable ones (unverified blogs) allowing models to prioritize trustworthy information while considering relevant insights from wider source ranges; multi-agent deliberation simulates expert committee meetings to debate evidence, with frameworks like MADAM-RAG employing multiple independent AI agents each tasked with analyzing retrieved documents from different perspectives where each agent forms its own assessment and conclusion, then a final meta-reasoning step synthesizes diverse viewpoints to forge more robust and nuanced final answers like panels of experts reaching consensus; reinforcement learning for factuality trains generators through trial-and-error processes rewarding factual accuracy, with RioRAG as representative approach where LLMs receive positive rewards when generating statements strongly and consistently supported by provided evidence and are penalized for unsubstantiated claims or statements contradicting source material, shaping models to inherently prefer generating factually grounded and reliable answers. **Long-form Coherence and Information Density** addresses the challenge of maintaining logical threads while avoiding repetition or verbosity in lengthy research answers, where $L_{\text{model}}$ denotes the maximum coherent length of a model's output and $L_{\text{SFT}}$ represents the average length of examples in its supervised fine-tuning dataset. LongWriter empirically demonstrates that maximum coherent length scales with average fine-tuning sample length, formally expressed as $L_{\text{model}} \propto L_{\text{SFT}}$, focusing on systematic training for extended generation while others use reflection-driven processes to iteratively improve consistency. RioRAG introduces length-adaptive reward functions promoting information density, penalizing verbosity failing to add informational value and preventing reward hacking through verbosity, shifting generation focus from mere content aggregation toward credible, concise, and coherent synthesis.

**Structuring Reasoning and Narrative** (Section 3.4.3) addresses the shift from mere factual accuracy to crucial needs for explainability and logical rigor in answers, since opaque answers preventing users from tracing underlying reasoning processes have significantly diminished utility in critical domains like scientific research. **Prompt-based Chain-of-Thought** focuses on eliciting intermediate reasoning steps before producing final answers, with Chain-of-Thought prompting as the most prominent technique formally expressed as:

$$R = \text{LLM}(\text{CoT-Prompt} + Q + \text{Evidence})$$

where $R$ represents the reasoning output, $Q$ is the input question, and Evidence denotes retrieved supporting documents. This method enhances both interpretability and multi-step reasoning performance, with applicability broadened by extensions like zero-shot CoT and Least-to-Most prompting. **Explicit Structural Planning** moves beyond simple linear chains to formalize entire answer structure, with RAPID formalizing processes into three stages: outline generation, outline refinement through evidence discovery, and plan-guided writing where outlines form directed acyclic graphs supporting complex non-linear argumentation. SuperWriter extends this by decoupling reasoning and text-production phases and optimizing entire processes via hierarchical Direct Preference Optimization. **Tool-Augmented Reasoning** enhances reasoning by dynamically interfacing with external resources, allowing models to invoke external computational or retrieval tools dynamically ensuring both analytic rigor and factual grounding.

**Presentation Generation** (Section 3.4.4) extends beyond text to encompass integration of multimodal and structured information, as research questions increasingly demand answers combining textual reasoning with visual, tabular, or auditory data maintaining semantic and presentational coherence. Early breakthroughs like BLIP-2 and InstructBLIP enable multimodal instruction-following by aligning vision-language embeddings, while MiniGPT-4 advances this by leveraging cross-modal attention to seamlessly integrate visual and textual evidence. Recent work demonstrates higher presentation capabilities signaling evolution from content generation to presentation generation: MedConQA, LIDA, ChartGPT, and Urania synthesize data analyses into dynamic interactive visualizations; PresentAgent, Qwen2.5-Omni, and AnyToAny generate synchronized audio narrations alongside text; PPTAgent and Paper2Video extend to editable presentation generation where full analytical reports automatically transform into slide decks with coordinated text, figures, and layout elements; video-grounded agents retrieve or generate relevant visual footage delivering answers through multimodal storytelling. **Table 2** compares output capabilities of contemporary representative DR systems, showing while most DR systems still focus on textual synthesis with citations, only a handful like OpenAI DeepResearch and H2O.ai DeepResearch currently support comprehensive multimodal output, though emerging consensus suggests rich multi-format answer generation will soon become standard expectation bridging gaps between knowledge synthesis and human-centered presentation.

#### 3.4.6 Optimization Techniques: Prompting, Fine-Tuning, and Reinforcement Learning

Three commonly used paradigms optimize DR systems by flexibly coordinating and enhancing key components for more reliable and effective task completion.

**Workflow Prompt Engineering** (Section 4.1) constructs complex workflows enabling collaboration among multiple agents where orchestration agents coordinate teams of specialized worker agents operating in parallel on different aspects of complex research tasks. Anthropic Deep Research serves as representative example proposing multi-agent DR frameworks where lead orchestrators coordinate multiple worker agents through structured auditable interactions, transforming open-ended research queries into complete workflows from planning and delegation to synthesis and citation under explicit research budgets controlling agent count, tool usage, and reasoning depth. Core enabling points include: query stratification and planning where orchestrators first analyze semantic type and difficulty of input queries (depth-first vs. breadth-first) to determine research strategy and allocate corresponding budgets of agents, tool calls, and synthesis passes; delegation and scaling where effort scales with complexity from 1–2 agents for factual lookups to up to 10 or more for multi-perspective analyses, each assigned clear quotas and stopping criteria enabling dynamic budget reallocation; task decomposition and prompt specification where main queries decompose into modular subtasks each encoded as structured prompts specifying objectives, output schema, citation policy, and fallback actions ensuring autonomy with accountability; tool selection and evidence logging where central tool registries (web fetch, PDF parsing, calculators) are used following freshness, verifiability, and latency rules with agents recording all tool provenance in evidence ledgers for traceable attribution; parallel gathering and interim synthesis where worker agents operate concurrently while orchestrators monitor coverage, resolve conflicts, and launch micro-delegations to close residual gaps or trigger deeper reasoning where needed; final report and attribution where orchestrators integrate verified findings into coherent reports programmatically linking claims to sources ensuring schema compliance, factual grounding, and transparent citation.

**Supervised Fine-Tuning** (Section 4.2) trains models to imitate desired behaviors using input-output pairs under supervised learning objectives, commonly employed as cold starts or warm-up processes before online reinforcement learning aiming to endow agents with basic task-solving skills. Since manual collection of expert trajectories is labor-intensive, costly, and difficult to scale, key challenges lie in automatically constructing high-quality SFT datasets through two main paradigms. **Strong-to-weak Distillation** (Section 4.2.1) transfers high-quality decision trajectories from powerful teacher systems to smaller weaker student models, with early work predominantly using single LLM-based agents to synthesize trajectories and more recent research employing multi-agent teacher systems to elicit more diverse higher-complexity trajectories. Single-agent distillation representative systems include WebDancer providing agents with search and click tools where strong non-reasoning models generate short CoT while large reasoning models generate long CoT with agents learning from both using rejection sampling for quality control; WebSailor using expert LRMs to generate action-observation trajectories then reconstructing short CoT with non-reasoning models ensuring final reasoning chains are compact enough for long-horizon tasks; WebShaper using search and visit tools in ReAct-style trajectories performing 5 rollouts per task and filtering out repeated or speculative answers using reviewing LLMs; WebThinker augmenting SFT with policy-gradient refinement; WebSynthesis leveraging learned world models to simulate virtual web environments employing MCTS to synthesize diverse controllable web interaction trajectories entirely offline. Multi-agent distillation synthesizes training data using agentic teacher systems composed of specialized collaborating agents (planners, tool callers, verifiers) with goals of transferring emergent problem-solving behaviors into single end-to-end student models, tending to produce diverse trajectories, richer tool-use patterns, and explicit self-correction signals. Representative work includes MaskSearch constructing multi-agent pipelines including planners, query rewriters, and observers generating 58k verified chain-of-thought trajectories; Chain-of-Agents building on expert multi-agent system OAgents to synthesize task-solving trajectories, after four-stage filtering pipelines removing trivial or incorrect cases yielding 16,433 high-quality trajectories for agent training; AgentFounder proposing agentic continual pre-training scaling up data generation processes by constructing large-scale planning traces, tool-invocation sequences, and step-by-step reasoning data. Comparing the two types, single-agent distillation provides simple easy-to-deploy pipelines but is limited by single teacher model bias and relatively shallow synthesized trajectories often emphasizing token-level action sequences rather than higher-level reasoning restricting student model generalization ability in complex tasks, while multi-agent distillation generates longer more diverse trajectories expanding action spaces to include strategic planning, task decomposition, iterative error correction, and self-reflection equipping student models with stronger capabilities for multi-step and knowledge-intensive reasoning, though introducing notable trade-offs requiring careful system design, substantial inference cost, and dedicated infrastructure for logging and verification with data quality potentially brittle due to system sensitivity to prompting. **Iterative Self-Evolving** (Section 4.2.2) is an autonomous cyclic process where models continuously generate new training data to fine-tune themselves progressively enhancing capabilities. Early evidence comes from self-training methods where models bootstrap from small seed task sets to synthesize instruction-input-output triples, filter synthetic data, then fine-tune themselves on resulting corpora delivering substantial gains in instruction following with minimal human supervision. Yuan et al. introduces self-rewarding language models where models generate their own rewards through LLM-as-a-Judge prompting. Zhao et al. extends this to zero-data regimes by framing self-play as autonomous curriculum where models create code-style reasoning tasks, solve them, and rely on external code executors as verifiable environments to validate both tasks and solutions. In DR context, EvolveSearch iteratively selects high-performing rollouts (task-solving trajectories) and re-optimizes models on these data via supervised fine-tuning. Key advantages include closed-loop designs where models progressively improve capabilities by tightly interleaving data generation with training, enabling scalable training without heavy reliance on external models or human annotations and allowing exploration of data distributions extending beyond handcrafted knowledge. However, self-improvement introduces significant risks: as iterations progress, distributional drift, reward hacking, and self-reinforcing errors may accumulate and degrade data quality potentially leading to training collapse, and without robust validation mechanisms processes may converge prematurely to narrow modes with limited performance ceilings.

**End-to-End Agentic Reinforcement Learning** (Section 4.3) uses RL algorithms to incentivize DR agents that can flexibly plan, act, and generate final answers. **RL Algorithms in Deep Research** primarily include Proximal Policy Optimization from OpenAI, Group Relative Policy Optimization from DeepSeek, and their variants. **Proximal Policy Optimization** is a clipped policy-gradient method constraining updates within trust regions. Given current policy $\pi_{\theta}$ and old policy $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$, the objective maximizes the clipped surrogate:

$$L_{\text{PPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t\left[\min\left(r_t(\theta) \hat{A}_t, \text{clip}\left(r_t(\theta), 1 - \epsilon, 1 + \epsilon\right) \hat{A}_t\right)\right]$$

where:

$$r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_{\theta}(o_t | s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(o_t | s_t)}$$

and $\epsilon$ bounds the policy update while $\hat{A}_t$ is the estimated advantage computed using discounted returns or generalized advantage estimation as:

$$\hat{A}_t = \sum_{l=0}^{T-t} \gamma^l \cdot r_{t+l} + \gamma^{T-t+1} \cdot V_{\phi}(s_{T+1}) - V_{\phi}(s_t)$$

where $r_{t+l}$ denotes immediate reward at time step $t + l$, $\gamma \in [0, 1)$ is discount factor balancing importance of long-term and short-term returns, $T$ is terminal time step of current trajectory (episode), $s_{T+1}$ is next state used for bootstrapping after termination, and $V_{\phi}(s_t)$ is value function predicted by value network parameterized by $\phi$. The empirical return $\hat{R}_t$ purely from rewards is defined as:

$$\hat{R}_t = \sum_{l=0}^{T-t} \gamma^l r_{t+l}$$

representing cumulative discounted rewards from time step $t$ until episode end. In PPO, value function parameters $\phi$ are updated by minimizing squared error between predicted value and empirical return:

$$L_{\text{value}}(\phi) = \frac{1}{2} \mathbb{E}_t\left[\left(V_{\phi}(s_t) - \hat{R}_t\right)^2\right]$$

**Group Relative Policy Optimization** extends PPO by normalizing rewards within groups of responses to the same query. Formally, given group $G$ of $m$ responses $\{o_1, o_2, \ldots, o_m\}$ sampled for same query $s_t$, each response is assigned scalar reward $R_j$. The group-relative advantage for $j$-th response is:

$$\hat{A}^G_j = \frac{R_j - \text{mean}(\{R_i | i \in [m]\})}{\text{std}(\{R_i | i \in [m]\}) + \epsilon}$$

where $\text{mean}_G$ and $\text{std}_G$ denote mean and standard deviation of rewards within group $G$, and $\epsilon$ prevents numerical instability when variance is small. The GRPO objective mirrors PPO's clipping mechanism but replaces $\hat{A}_t$ with group-relative advantage $\hat{A}^G_j$:

$$L_{\text{GRPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{j=1}^{|G|} \min\left\{\frac{\pi_{\theta}(o_j | q)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(o_j | q)} \hat{A}^G_j, \text{clip}\left(\frac{\pi_{\theta}(o_j | q)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(o_j | q)}, 1 - \epsilon, 1 + \epsilon\right) \hat{A}^G_j\right\}\right]$$

**Table 3** summarizes key notations used in PPO and GRPO algorithms. Comparison between PPO and GRPO in Deep Research shows that in PPO each sampled output is optimized using advantage signals derived from value models, effective but highly reliant on accurate value estimation requiring additional resources for training value models, while GRPO optimizes by contrasting each response against others within same group shifting focus to relative-quality comparison among competing hypotheses simplifying implementation while maintaining strong performance. **Reward Design in Deep Research Agents** uses reward models denoted as $R(\cdot)$ to assess quality (e.g., correctness) of agents' outputs producing scalar signals enabling policy optimization algorithms like PPO and GRPO, with two common paradigms: rule-based rewards $R_{\text{rule}}(\cdot)$ derived from deterministic task-specific metrics like Exact Match and F1 score where EM is commonly used binary score indicating whether generated answer perfectly matches ground-truth string and F1 score (harmonic mean of precision and recall calculated over token overlap) is also used to reward outputs, though primarily suited for tasks with well-defined short-span ground truths and struggling to evaluate multi-answer or open-ended questions effectively; LLM-as-judge rewards $R_{\text{LLMs}}(\cdot)$ use external LLMs to evaluate output quality and assign scalar scores based on predefined rubrics, formally for output $o$ to input query $q$ the reward assigned by LLM judge $\phi$ formulated as:

$$R_{\text{LLMs}}(o | q) = \mathbb{E}_{\text{criteria} \in C}[\phi(o, q, \text{criteria})]$$

where $C$ is set of evaluation criteria (accuracy, completeness, citation quality, clarity, etc.) and $\phi(\cdot)$ returns scalar score for each criterion.

**End-to-end Optimization of Specific Modules** (Section 4.3.2) focuses on applying RL techniques to improve individual components within DR systems like query planning, document ranking, or planning modules. Within DR, most existing work trains query planners while freezing other parameters leaving components such as retrieval fixed. MAO-ARAG treats DR as multi-turn process where planning agents orchestrate sub-agents for information seeking, with PPO propagating holistic rewards (e.g., final F1 minus token and latency penalties) across all steps enabling end-to-end learning of trade-offs between answer quality and computational cost. AI-SearchPlanner decouples lightweight search planners from frozen QA generators, with PPO optimizing planners with dual rewards: outcome rewards for improving answer quality and process rewards for reasoning rationality, using Pareto-regularized objectives balancing utility with real-world cost guiding planners on when to query or stop. Advantages include focusing on training single core components enabling more accurate credit assignment, more sophisticated algorithm design for target modules, and reduced training data and computational costs, but this approach restricts optimization space and may be inadequate when other frozen modules contain significant design or performance flaws.

**End-to-end Optimization of Entire Pipelines** (Section 4.3.3) involves jointly optimizing all components and processes from input to output (query decomposition, search, reading, report generation) to achieve best overall performance across DR workflows. Representative work on multi-hop search includes Search-R1 as first work formulating search-augmented reasoning as fully observable Markov Decision Process and optimizing entire pipeline via RL containing query planning, retrieval, and extracting final answer, masking retrieved tokens in policy-gradient loss so models learn to autonomously decide when and what to search while keeping training signals on their own generated tokens. R1-Searcher introduces two-stage RL methods where DR agents learn when to invoke external searches and how to use retrieved knowledge via outcome rewards, though pure RL training often leads to over-reliance on external retrieval resulting in over-searching. R1-Searcher++ first cold-starts DR agents via SFT then applies knowledge-assimilation RL processes encouraging agents to internalize previously retrieved documents and avoid redundant retrievals. Recent work extends naive Search-R1 by integrating multi-reward signals or improving training environments: R-Search trains models to decide when to retrieve and how to integrate external knowledge in both single-hop and multi-hop question answering, improving answer quality and evidence reliability by optimizing reasoning-search trajectories under multi-objective reward design; ZeroSearch and O2-Searcher simulate search engines to develop retrieval capabilities without accessing actual web providing more controllable settings for RL training; DeepResearcher operates directly in real-world web environments learning to plan, search, verify, and autonomously answer open-domain questions. Beyond basic document retrieval tools, some work integrates additional information-seeking tools teaching DR agents to flexibly combine them: MMSearch-R1 stands out as first RL-trained multimodal model learning when and how to search text or image from web on demand; HierSearch introduces hierarchical DR frameworks for enterprise scenarios involving both local and web knowledge sources; other work integrates knowledge graphs into DR agents achieving efficient multi-hop reasoning. Representative work on long-chain web search addresses longer-chain web search problems beyond relatively simple multi-hop QA tasks: WebDancer, WebSailor, and Kimi-K2 focus on advancing more intricate multi-hop tasks including GAIA and BrowseComp, combining data synthesis with end-to-end reinforcement learning training enabling more extensive iterations in DR process. ASearcher scales end-to-end RL to extreme long-horizon search with fully asynchronous RL engines removing 10-turn ceilings plaguing earlier systems allowing trajectories of 40+ turns and 150k tokens to be optimized without blocking GPU updates, coupled with autonomous QA-synthesis agents injecting noise and fuzzing questions for difficulty operating whole pipelines end-to-end from synthetic data creation to multi-turn policy optimization. SimpleDeepSearcher leverages real-web simulation and distilled SFT to deliver agentic search capability without heavy RL yet stays fully compatible with lightweight RL refinement. WebAgent-R1 and DeepDiver train web agents through end-to-end multi-turn RL algorithms. Some work studies DR systems across multiple tool-calling scenarios: Chain-of-Agents introduces novel paradigm distilling multi-agent system capabilities into single LLMs enabling native end-to-end complex problem-solving by dynamically orchestrating multiple role-playing and tool agents within one model, through multi-agent distillation and agentic RL training Agent Foundation Models in end-to-end approaches achieving excellent performance on diverse web search benchmarks while significantly reducing computational overhead compared to traditional multi-agent systems; Tool-Star and ARPO investigate how to effectively leverage tools in long-horizon tasks like Deep Research using GRPO algorithms to optimize entire pipelines end-to-end; AEPO further improves rollout efficiency and based on ARPO optimizes both performance and efficiency for end-to-end tool-use pipelines. Advantages include modeling entire DR systems as multi-turn search processes achieving comprehensive optimization across reasoning, query rewriting, knowledge retrieval, tool invocation, and answer generation with flexible modeling allowing different objectives to be emphasized through reward function design, but drawbacks include sparse rewards, excessively long responses, and unstable training requiring continuous optimization to further enhance effectiveness, stability, and efficiency of DR systems.

#### 3.4.7 Evaluation Frameworks: Benchmarks and Metrics

DR techniques have been applied to wide range of downstream tasks including healthcare, financial report generation, and survey generation, with systematic review of common benchmarks and evaluation protocols across three representative scenarios: information seeking, report generation, and AI for research.

**Agentic Information Seeking** (Section 5.1) evaluates effectiveness of agentic information-seeking as critical component of assessing DR systems, where information seeking is not single QA task but multi-stage, iterative, cross-domain process in which agents must continuously explore, reformulate, and synthesize information from diverse sources. Benchmark design has evolved from early static single-hop retrieval tasks like Natural Questions to dynamic web environments requiring multi-hop reasoning and complex interactions like BrowseComp and HotpotQA. **Complex Queries** (Section 5.1.1) show evolution following increasing complexity of query demands: early benchmarks like NQ, TriviaQA, and SimpleQA established foundation for question answering research focused on single-hop queries where answers could be retrieved with single lookup or were already contained within LLM parameters, providing controlled starting points but unable to capture reasoning and synthesis required in DR. As research questions grew more complex, benchmarks evolved from simple fact retrieval to multi-step reasoning challenges with multi-hop QA datasets assessing agents' ability to reformulate queries and build reasoning chains across documents. HotpotQA as one of earliest and most widely used multi-hop datasets requires reasoning across multiple Wikipedia articles using supporting facts to derive answers. 2WikiMultihopQA extends this by integrating information from two separate Wikipedia pages per question emphasizing cross-document reasoning. Bamboogle consists of 125 two-hop questions generated from random Wikipedia articles testing ability to decompose and reason over complex queries. MultiHop-RAG is RAG dataset designed specifically for multi-hop queries categorizing questions into four types: inference, comparison, temporal, and null queries. MuSiQue adopts bottom-up approach systematically pairing composable single-hop questions where one reasoning step depends on another. FRAMES simulates realistic multi-document queries evaluating LLM ability to retrieve relevant facts, reason accurately, and synthesize information into coherent responses. However, most benchmarks rely on structured linear reasoning paths falling short of reflecting inherent ambiguity and branching non-linear exploration required in real-world research scenarios. Recent benchmarks capture growing complexity placing greater emphasis on in-depth progressive exploration of complex topics: GPQA is graduate-level dataset in physics, chemistry, and biology testing both domain experts and skilled non-experts requiring extensive reasoning and problem-solving; GAIA provides 466 carefully designed questions requiring multi-step reasoning, real-world knowledge retrieval, and complex generation; HLE aims to be comprehensive fully closed academic benchmark across dozens of disciplines including mathematics, humanities, and natural sciences designed to advance reasoning skills with questions that cannot be quickly answered through online search. **Table 4** provides comprehensive overview of existing and emerging benchmarks for DR systems focusing on question answering scenarios with dates, aspects, data sizes, and evaluation metrics. **Interaction Environment** (Section 5.1.2) reflects that as agent capabilities advanced, evaluation based solely on static environments and fixed corpora is no longer sufficient, leading to benchmarks reflecting scale and dynamics of real-world web environments requiring agents to interact with, navigate, and creatively explore web pages to obtain complex or hard-to-find information. Some studies incorporate browsing tools like Google and Bing into benchmarks enabling agents to directly retrieve and extract information from live web: InfoDeepSeek and AssistantBench present challenging tasks requiring agents to integrate multiple search and browsing tools in real-time web environments testing dynamic operation ability; Mind2Web replaces overly simplified simulated environments common in other datasets with authentic, dynamic, unpredictable real-world websites providing complete records of user interactions, webpage snapshots, and network traffic; Mind2Web 2 subsequently introduced to more rigorously evaluate agent-based search systems on realistic long-horizon tasks involving live web search and browsing; BrowseComp and BrowseComp-Plus demand persistent navigation to locate hard-to-find entangled information across multiple sites; DeepResearchBench offers large-scale RetroSearch environment reducing task degradation and network randomness while evaluating LLM agents on complex real-world web research tasks; DeepResearchGym complements by providing open-source sandbox with reproducible search API and rigorous evaluation protocol promoting transparency and reproducibility in DR area. Subsequent datasets increasingly emphasize authenticity and complexity of interactive environments: WebArena provides highly realistic reproducible environment for language-guided agents built from fully functional websites across four domains; WebWalkerQA assesses LLMs' ability to systematically traverse website subpages and extract high-quality data through interactive actions like clicking specifically testing complex multi-step web interactions; WideSearch focuses on critical yet under-evaluated task requiring agents to thoroughly and accurately acquire all large-scale atomic information meeting set of criteria and organize into structured output; MMInA extends challenges by providing multi-hop multi-modal benchmark for embodied agents performing integrated internet tasks on realistic evolving websites ensuring high realism and applicability to natural user tasks. **Table 5** provides comprehensive overview of existing and emerging benchmarks for DR systems focusing on broader scenarios beyond question answering.

**Comprehensive Report Generation** (Section 5.2) evaluates DR system capacity to generate comprehensive reports requiring systems to integrate information from multiple sources and modalities into structured, logically coherent, broadly informative outputs involving information aggregation, content organization, factual consistency verification, and clarity of expression regarded as core indicator of DR system overall capability. **Survey Generation** (Section 5.2.1) involves producing structured overviews or syntheses of specific scientific topics by aggregating information from diverse sources, widely used to evaluate DR capabilities thanks to clear citation structure provided by gold-standard references. AutoSurvey gathers arXiv articles of varying lengths and uses multi-LLM-as-judge frameworks to evaluate survey generation in terms of speed, citation quality, and content quality. ReportBench is systematic benchmark for evaluating research reports generated by large language models focusing on two key aspects: relevance of citations and reliability and accuracy of report statements, with evaluation corpus constructed using high-quality survey papers published on arXiv as gold standard. SurveyGen is another survey-generation dataset containing over 4,200 human-written surveys with chapter-level structure, cited references, and rich metadata enabling comprehensive evaluation of content quality, citation accuracy, and structural consistency. **Long-Form Report Generation** (Section 5.2.2) focuses on different types of report generation tasks introducing alternative evaluation frameworks: Deep Research Comparator provides unified evaluation platform for DR agents enabling systematic assessment of long-form reports and intermediate reasoning processes through side-by-side comparison, fine-grained human feedback, and ranking mechanisms; DeepResearch Bench is benchmark of 100 PhD-level research tasks introducing two evaluation methods for generated reports: reference-based assessment of overall quality and citation-based evaluation of retrieval accuracy; ResearcherBench comprises 65 research questions focused on evaluating advanced agent system capabilities on cutting-edge AI science problems using evaluation framework combining rubric assessment and factual evaluation; LiveDRBench is benchmark for DR tasks offering challenging science and world-event queries evaluating systems via intermediate reasoning steps and factual sub-propositions; PROXYQA uses human-designed meta-questions and corresponding proxy questions to indirectly assess knowledge coverage and information richness providing objective measure of long-form text generation quality; SCHOLARQABENCH is benchmark for scientific literature synthesis tasks in multiple formats comprising 2,967 expert-written queries and 208 long-form answers across computer science field. Evaluating research reports is particularly challenging because there is no single gold-standard answer and multiple valid perspectives exist for assessing quality, with diversity of acceptable content, reasoning approaches, and presentation styles making it difficult to define objective metrics, resulting in most benchmarks relying on LLM-as-judge methods leveraging large language models' reasoning and knowledge to provide scalable, consistent, nuanced evaluations of content quality, factual accuracy, citation relevance, and structural coherence. **Poster Generation** (Section 5.2.3) can be viewed as highly condensed visually structured variant of comprehensive report generation where scientific posters are typically single-page high-density summaries concisely presenting research motivation, methodology, results, and conclusions in formats both navigable and visually engaging, imposing unique challenges on DR systems: not only must systems aggregate and synthesize information from multiple heterogeneous sources like research papers, notes, and presentation slides, but they must also transform that content into effective visual layouts. Evaluation typically focuses on three main aspects: factual completeness, visual communication effectiveness, and readability. Paper2Poster focuses exclusively on AI research papers with dataset comprising 100 paper-poster pairs covering 280 distinct topics across subfields, introducing comprehensive evaluation framework comprising four key dimensions: visual quality, text coherence, VLM-based quality judgment, and PaperQuiz as novel metric designed to assess how effectively posters communicate core knowledge of original papers. PosterGen adopts two-dimensional evaluation protocol dividing assessment into poster content and poster design, introducing VLM-based metric to evaluate key design aspects including layout balance, readability, and aesthetic consistency. P2PInstruct is large-scale instruction dataset for paper-to-poster generation containing over 30,000 high-quality instruction-response pairs covering full pipeline from image element processing and text generation to final layout formatting. **Slides Generation** (Section 5.2.4) represents another critical pathway for evaluating comprehensive capabilities of DR systems, challenging systems not only to comprehend and summarize large volumes of heterogeneous information sources but also to transform distilled content into structured slide-based presentation formats with objectives encompassing information distillation, logical structuring, and presentation-oriented expression making it strong indicator of system ability to coordinate across multiple dimensions. Common benchmark tasks and datasets often involve generating slides from meeting transcripts, research papers, long-form reports, or collections of web documents, typically assessing whether models can maintain content integrity and factual accuracy while performing high-quality information selection and organization. Doc2PPT collects paired documents and corresponding slide decks from academic proceedings conducting detailed post-processing for evaluation using metrics like Slide-Level ROUGE, Longest Common Figure Subsequence, Text-Figure Relevance, and Mean Intersection over Union. SLIDESBENCH is benchmark comprising 7,000 training examples and 585 test examples derived from 310 slide decks across 10 distinct domains supporting two types of evaluation: reference-based evaluation measuring similarity to target (gold) slides and reference-free evaluation assessing design quality of generated slides independently. Zhang et al. introduced Zenodo10K as new dataset collected from Zenodo, platform hosting diverse openly licensed artifacts across various domains, also proposing PPTEval as evaluation framework leveraging GPT-4.0 as judge to assess presentation quality along three dimensions: content, design, and coherence. TSBench is benchmark dataset specifically designed to evaluate slide editing capabilities of models and frameworks including 379 distinct editing instructions along with corresponding slide modifications.

**AI for Research** (Section 5.3) seeks to harness artificial intelligence to advance scientific discovery either by automating processes or by assisting researchers in accelerating their work, with applications and corresponding benchmarks including idea generation, experimental execution, academic writing, and peer review. Unlike report generation, research goes beyond producing extended outputs requiring creation of new perspectives, conclusions, and knowledge thereby necessitating mechanisms for evaluating novelty. **Idea Generation** (Section 5.3.1) faces key challenge in generating genuinely novel ideas and reliably assessing their novelty, typically conducted by human experts but remaining difficult and resource-intensive. Existing approaches generally fall into two categories: human- or LLM-based evaluation where Si et al. recruited over 100 NLP researchers to evaluate novelty of ideas generated by humans, LLMs, and human-LLM collaboration though this process is not easily scalable and proves difficult even for domain experts, with investigation of LLMs' ability to assess novelty finding LLM judgments show lower agreement with expert reviewers than human evaluations, while Li et al. and Gao et al. leverage LLMs through direct prompting to evaluate novelty, and Lu et al. and Su et al. integrate LLMs with Semantic Scholar API and web access enabling them to evaluate ideas against related literature, with AI Idea Bench 2025 providing benchmark for quantifying and comparing ideas generated by LLMs incorporating 3,495 representative papers published in AI-related conferences after October 10, 2023 together with corresponding inspiration papers and introducing evaluation framework to assess whether ideas derived from inspiration papers are consistent with ground-truth papers; density-based evaluation relies on absolute local density in semantic embedding space to measure novelty where Wang et al. introduces Relative Neighbor Density algorithm evaluating novelty by examining distributional patterns of semantic neighbors rather than relying solely on absolute local density, also constructing large-scale semantic embedding databases for novelty assessment encompassing more than 30 million publications across two distinct domains. **Experimental Execution** (Section 5.3.2) evaluation typically involves both objective and subjective assessments where objective evaluation generally emphasizes outcomes produced in specific environments like benchmark performance or compiler outputs (Lu et al. and Weng et al. directly adopt results of downstream tasks as evaluation metrics while Tang et al. leverages compiler outputs to assist LLMs in refining experiments and correcting code errors), and subjective evaluation involves leveraging either humans or LLMs to assess quality of experimental designs (Tang et al. employs LLMs to compare code implementations with atomic research ideas verifying whether code satisfies intended requirements of ideas, Starace et al. employs LLMs to evaluate source code, documentation, and configuration files against human-designed rubrics to derive final grades). **Academic Writing** (Section 5.3.3) evaluation differs substantially from general report generation requiring not only factual accuracy, logical coherence, and clarity but also alignment with underlying ideas and experimental results, proper integration of citations from related work, and effective visualization of findings. To capture these multifaceted criteria, Lu et al. employ LLMs to assess writing along dimensions such as originality, quality, clarity, and significance; Höpner et al. train domain-specific LLMs to predict citation counts and review scores as proxies for paper quality addressing limitations of generic LLMs in academic evaluation; Starace et al. introduce PaperBench as benchmark designed to assess AI agents' ability to replicate AI papers including 20 ICML 2024 Spotlight and Oral papers evaluating replication quality using LLMs guided by manually constructed rubrics hierarchically decomposing each task into graded subtasks; Tang et al. propose Scientist-Bench as comprehensive benchmark built from top-cited papers published between 2022 and 2024 across 16 research areas and multiple expertise levels evaluating dimensions such as technical novelty, methodological rigor, empirical validation, and potential impact closely reflecting criteria used in ICLR review process; Xu et al. present ResearcherBench as more challenging benchmark for evaluating DR systems consisting of 65 research questions carefully curated from real-world scientific contexts across 35 AI subfields, also proposing dual evaluation framework combining rubric-based assessment to evaluate quality of insights with factual evaluation measuring citation faithfulness and evidence coverage. **Peer Review** (Section 5.3.4) seeks to leverage AI agents to generate feedback on scientific papers, though evaluating such feedback is challenging as reviews are typically lengthy and inherently subjective. Yuan et al. introduced ASAP-Review as large-scale benchmark collecting 8,877 AI papers from ICLR (2017–2022) via OpenReview and NeurIPS papers (2016–2019) via official proceedings along with corresponding reviews annotated across multiple dimensions including Motivation, Originality, Soundness, Substance, Replicability, Clarity, and Comparison, employing automatic metrics like ROUGE and BERTScore as well as human judgments to evaluate generated reviews. Lu et al. collect 500 ICLR 2022 papers from OpenReview to establish benchmark for peer review task subsequently employing self-reflection, few-shot examples, and response ensembling with LLMs to assess quality of generated reviews. Weng et al. introduces REVIEW-5k dataset containing 782 test samples collected from ICLR 2024 with each sample including paper title, abstract, LaTeX or Markdown source, and corresponding review comments plus structured review information including summaries, strengths and weaknesses, clarification questions, and review scores, employing Proxy Mean Squared Error and Proxy Mean Absolute Error for evaluation which leverage multiple independent reviews of same submission as unbiased estimators of true rating. Similarly, Gao et al. constructed REVIEWER2 as dataset comprising 27,805 papers and reviews collected from CONLL-16, ACL-17, COLING-20, ARR-22, ICLR-17–23, and NeurIPS-16–22. Zhu et al. introduces DeepReview-Bench as dataset of 1.2K ICLR 2024–2025 submissions collected from OpenReview including textual reviewer assessments, interactive rebuttal-stage discussions, and standardized scoring information, employing MAE, MSE, accuracy, F1, and Spearman correlation for quantitative evaluation while conducting qualitative evaluation under LLM-as-a-judge paradigm across five dimensions: constructive value, analytical depth, plausibility, technical accuracy, and overall quality.

**Software Engineering** (Section 5.4) represents shift from assisting with isolated code snippets to autonomously executing complex software development tasks, with pioneering work SWE-Bench as benchmark designed to evaluate whether AI agents can resolve real-world GitHub issues. Although SWE-Bench does not yet cover full end-to-end software development, it marks important step toward bridging idealized benchmarks with practical scenarios. Meanwhile, DR agents have been deployed in wide range of complex software engineering domains including scientific discovery, machine learning experimentation, data science, earth observation, and software library completion.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This survey does not propose a single algorithm but rather identifies and systematizes several fundamental shifts in how we conceptualize LLM-based information systems. Below, we distill the most significant innovations that distinguish Deep Research from prior paradigms, separating genuine breakthroughs from incremental refinements.

### 4.1. Deep Research as a Distinct Paradigm (Not Incremental RAG)

**What Makes It Different:**

The paper's most fundamental contribution is establishing DR as a **conceptually distinct paradigm** rather than an incremental improvement to Retrieval-Augmented Generation. This distinction is often misunderstood in early discussions of the field. As Table 1 (Section 2.3) explicitly shows, DR differs from RAG across multiple dimensions simultaneously: action space (narrow vs. broad), reasoning horizon (single vs. long-horizon), workflow organization (fixed vs. flexible), and output form (short span vs. reports/academic papers).

**Why This Matters:**

This is not merely taxonomic clarification—it has practical implications for system design and evaluation. Treating DR as "better RAG" leads to architectural mistakes:

- **Static vs. Dynamic Retrieval**: RAG assumes retrieval happens once before generation. DR treats retrieval as an **action the agent can take multiple times** based on evolving understanding (Section 3.2.2 discusses adaptive retrieval timing as a learned policy, not a fixed heuristic)
- **Memory as First-Class Component**: RAG has no persistent memory across turns. DR's memory management (Section 3.3) enables the system to maintain coherent context over 40+ turn trajectories (as in ASearcher [85]), which is impossible in standard RAG architectures
- **Evaluation Mismatch**: Using RAG benchmarks (exact match on short answers) for DR systems misses the core capability being tested—long-form coherence, citation quality, and reasoning transparency (Section 5.2 discusses why report generation requires entirely different metrics)

**Innovation Classification:** **Fundamental**. This repositioning changes how researchers approach the problem space. Prior surveys [89, 72] treated retrieval as augmentation; this survey establishes retrieval as **integrated action within an autonomous workflow**.

### 4.2. Memory Management as the Core Differentiator

**What Makes It Different:**

While query planning and information acquisition receive substantial attention, the survey identifies memory management as **what fundamentally distinguishes a DR agent from a simple RAG system** (Section 3.3 Takeaway). This insight is non-obvious because memory is often treated as infrastructure rather than a research contribution.

The paper formalizes memory as a **dynamic lifecycle** with four operations (consolidation, indexing, updating, forgetting) rather than static storage. Figure 5 (Section 3.3) illustrates this as an active process, not passive buffering.

**Why This Matters:**

This reframing has three significant implications:

1. **Enables Long-Horizon Coherence**: Without memory management, DR systems cannot maintain context across the 40+ turn, 150k token trajectories that recent work optimizes [85]. The survey notes that systems like MemGPT [243] and MemoryBank [482] use forgetting mechanisms (FIFO queues, Ebbinghaus curves) specifically to prevent context window overflow while preserving critical information

2. **Supports Iterative Refinement**: Memory updating (Section 3.3.3) allows agents to correct factual inaccuracies as new evidence arrives. Non-parametric updating (ADD, UPDATE, DELETE operations in Mem0 [46]) enables transparency that parametric weight updates cannot provide

3. **Addresses the Credit Assignment Problem**: Section 6.2.3 identifies that goal-driven reinforced memory evolution remains an open challenge—attributing final outcomes to specific memory decisions across long sequences. This is a **fundamental RL problem** that DR systems surface in a new context

**Innovation Classification:** **Fundamental**. Prior agent work treated memory as implementation detail; this survey elevates it to a **first-class research component** with its own taxonomy and optimization challenges.

### 4.3. End-to-End Reinforcement Learning for Entire Pipelines

**What Makes It Different:**

The survey identifies a shift from **module-specific optimization** to **entire pipeline optimization** via reinforcement learning as a key methodological advance (Section 4.3). Search-R1 [145] is highlighted as "the first work to formulate search-augmented reasoning as a fully observable Markov Decision Process and to optimize the entire pipeline via RL" (Section 4.3.3).

This differs from prior work in a crucial way: instead of training query planners, retrievers, and generators separately, the **entire DR workflow is treated as a single policy** optimized through outcome rewards.

**Why This Matters:**

Three specific advances emerge:

1. **Autonomous Retrieval Decisions**: By masking retrieved tokens in the policy-gradient loss, Search-R1 learns **when and what to search** without explicit supervision (Section 4.3.3). This solves the retrieval timing problem (Section 3.2.2) through learning rather than heuristics

2. **Multi-Reward Optimization**: Recent work like R-Search [468] and ARPO [64] use multi-objective reward design to balance answer quality, computational cost, and evidence reliability simultaneously. This addresses the trade-off that single-module optimization cannot capture

3. **Scalability to Long Horizons**: ASearcher [85] removes the "10-turn ceiling that plagued earlier systems" through asynchronous RL engines, enabling 40+ turn trajectories. This is a **systems innovation** that unlocks new capability regimes

**However, Critical Limitations Remain:**

The survey is candid about drawbacks (Section 4.3.3 Takeaway): sparse rewards, excessively long responses, and unstable training. Section 6.3 discusses how multi-turn RL suffers from "void turns" and "Echo Trap" phenomena that cause training collapse. This honesty distinguishes the survey from work that overclaims RL benefits.

**Innovation Classification:** **Fundamental with Caveats**. The methodological shift is genuine, but the survey appropriately notes that training instability remains an open challenge requiring "continuous optimization" (Section 4.3.3).

### 4.4. The Novelty-Hallucination Boundary as an Open Problem

**What Makes It Different:**

Section 6.4.2 identifies a problem that prior surveys do not adequately address: **distinguishing between genuine novelty and hallucinated claims** in DR outputs. This is unique to DR because Phase III systems (Full-stack AI Scientist) are expected to generate hypotheses and novel perspectives (Table 1, Section 2.2), but current evaluation cannot reliably separate creative insight from fabrication.

**Why This Matters:**

This is a **field-defining challenge** with three dimensions:

1. **No Ground Truth for Novelty**: Unlike QA tasks with exact-match answers, novel hypotheses have no gold standard. Section 5.3.1 notes that even human experts struggle to assess novelty reliably, and LLM judgments show "lower agreement with expert reviewers than human evaluations" [296]

2. **Evaluation Methods Are Insufficient**: Current approaches use density-based novelty scores or LLM-as-judge assessments, but "these alone do not ensure verifiability or differentiate between creative recombination and unfounded speculation" (Section 6.4.2)

3. **Proposed Solutions Are Preliminary**: The survey suggests differentiating "generative novelty" (new combinations) from "deductive novelty" (logically derived conclusions) and combining novelty scoring with validity-checking mechanisms. However, it explicitly states these are "potential solutions," not established methods

**Innovation Classification:** **Problem Identification (Fundamental)**. Identifying this boundary problem is itself a contribution—prior work either ignores novelty evaluation or treats hallucination as purely negative. This survey frames it as a **tension inherent to ambitious DR systems**.

### 4.5. What Is Incremental vs. Fundamental

To help readers prioritize research directions, we explicitly categorize contributions:

| Contribution | Classification | Rationale |
|--------------|----------------|-----------|
| DR vs. RAG paradigm distinction | **Fundamental** | Changes conceptual framing and system architecture |
| Memory management as core component | **Fundamental** | Enables capabilities impossible in prior paradigms |
| End-to-end pipeline RL optimization | **Fundamental** | New training methodology with demonstrated capability gains |
| Novelty-hallucination boundary problem | **Fundamental** | Identifies field-defining open challenge |
| Query planning strategies (parallel/sequential/tree) | **Incremental** | Adapts existing planning methods to DR context |
| Information filtering techniques | **Incremental** | Extends prior retrieval/ranking work |
| Workflow prompting engineering | **Incremental** | Applies established prompting patterns to DR |
| SFT data synthesis methods | **Incremental** | Adapts distillation/self-training to agent trajectories |

This distinction matters for researchers entering the field: **fundamental innovations** represent opportunities for high-impact contributions, while **incremental improvements** offer more predictable but limited advances.

### 4.6. Synthesis: What This Survey Enables

The survey's ultimate innovation is **providing a shared vocabulary and taxonomy** for a rapidly fragmenting field. Before this work:

- Researchers used "Deep Research," "agentic RAG," "web agents," and "autonomous research" interchangeably
- Evaluation was inconsistent across papers, making comparison impossible
- Optimization techniques were scattered across unrelated publications

By establishing the three-stage roadmap (Section 2.2), four-component taxonomy (Figure 2, Section 3), and consolidated evaluation framework (Section 5), this survey enables:

1. **Comparable Reporting**: Researchers can now position their work within the taxonomy (e.g., "we improve sequential planning with tree-based backtracking")
2. **Targeted Optimization**: Practitioners can identify which component limits their system (e.g., memory forgetting vs. retrieval timing)
3. **Clear Progress Tracking**: The roadmap establishes what capabilities define each phase, allowing the field to measure collective progress toward Phase III

This meta-contribution—**structuring a nascent research area**—is itself a fundamental innovation that will shape how the field develops.

### 5.3. Assessment: Do Experiments Support the Claims?

#### 5.3.1. Strengths of Current Evaluation

**Comprehensive Benchmark Coverage:**
The survey documents **40+ distinct benchmarks** across four scenarios (Tables 4 and 5), providing researchers with clear options for different evaluation needs. This breadth supports the paper's claim of providing "a foundation for consistent benchmarking" (Section 1).

**Multi-Dimensional Metrics:**
Rather than relying solely on exact match, the surveyed benchmarks employ:
- **Factuality metrics:** Precision, Recall, F1, FActScore-style evaluation
- **Citation quality:** Cited statement verification, non-cited statement detection
- **Structural metrics:** BradleyTerry Score for ranking, rubric-based assessment
- **Process metrics:** Search Call counts, Calibration Error, Step Success Rate

This diversity supports the survey's argument that DR requires "evaluation shifts from superficial short-form lexical matching to long-form quality" (Section 2.2, Phase II description).

**Real-World Environment Testing:**
Benchmarks like WebArena [488], Mind2Web [57], and DeepResearchGym [49] provide "highly realistic and reproducible environment[s]" with "fully functional websites" (Section 5.1.2), addressing the survey's critique that conventional RAG operates in "static retrieval loop[s]" (Section 2.3).

#### 5.3.2. Critical Limitations in Evaluation

**LLM-as-Judge Bias:**
Section 6.4.3 explicitly identifies two major problems:

1. **Bias:** "LLM judges may prefer longer responses, be affected by answer ordering, reward particular writing styles, or favor systems that resemble themselves" [100, 169]
2. **Efficiency:** "Large-scale pairwise evaluation is resource-intensive, especially when relying on paid APIs"

The survey acknowledges that "most benchmarks rely on LLM-as-judge methods" (Section 5.2.2) while simultaneously critiquing this approach—a **methodological tension** that undermines confidence in reported results.

**Novelty-Hallucination Boundary:**
Section 6.4.2 identifies a fundamental evaluation gap: "Current practices often depend on density-based novelty scores or LLM-as-judge assessments of originality, yet these alone do not ensure verifiability or differentiate between creative recombination and unfounded speculation."

This is particularly problematic for Phase III (Full-stack AI Scientist) systems that are expected to "generate hypotheses" and "propose novel perspectives" (Section 2.2), but **no benchmark reliably distinguishes genuine novelty from hallucination**.

**Sparse Quantitative Comparisons:**
The survey provides capability matrices (Table 2) and benchmark listings (Tables 4-5) but **does not report head-to-head performance comparisons** across DR systems. For example:
- Table 2 shows which systems support which output types, but not quality scores
- Section 4.3.3 mentions Search-R1, R1-Searcher, and DeepResearcher but does not provide comparative accuracy numbers
- The survey states CoA [180] achieves "excellent performance" but provides no specific metrics

This limits the survey's utility for practitioners seeking to select systems based on empirical performance.

#### 5.3.3. Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

**Documented Ablations in Surveyed Work:**

The survey references several ablation-style analyses from the underlying papers:

1. **Retrieval Timing Ablations:** Section 6.1 notes that systems like Search-R1 [145] "rely too heavily on answer correctness to guide the entire search pipeline and lack fine-grained guidance on when to retrieve, leading to both over-retrieval and under-retrieval" [396]. This implies ablation studies on retrieval frequency were conducted.

2. **Memory Operation Ablations:** Section 6.2.3 discusses RL-based memory management where "agents learn an optimal policy for memory operations, such as updating a fixed-length internal state [436, 491] or executing structured commands like ADD, UPDATE, and DELETE" [417]. The survey notes this approach is "guided solely by the reward from the final task outcome," implying ablation on different memory operation policies.

3. **RL Training Stability:** Section 6.3.1 documents specific failure modes identified through ablation:
   - **Void Turns:** Xue et al. [416] identify "responses that do not advance the task, such as fragmented text, repetitive content, or premature termination" as causing training collapse
   - **Echo Trap:** Wang et al. [373] identify "rapid policy homogenization, where the model abandons exploration and repeatedly produces conservative outputs"

**Missing Robustness Analysis:**

The survey does not report:
- **Cross-benchmark generalization:** How systems trained on HotpotQA perform on GAIA
- **Domain shift robustness:** Performance variation across scientific domains
- **Adversarial robustness:** Susceptibility to manipulated search results or poisoned sources
- **Compute-normalized comparisons:** Performance per token or per API call

This gap limits understanding of **practical deployment constraints**.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This survey provides a comprehensive taxonomy of Deep Research systems, but the field itself faces significant limitations that readers must understand before deploying or extending these approaches. Below, we critically examine the assumptions underlying DR systems, scenarios they fail to address, computational and scalability constraints, and persistent open questions. We ground every claim in specific sections and evidence from the paper.

### 6.1. Foundational Assumptions and Their Fragility

Deep Research systems rest on several assumptions that may not hold in practice. Understanding these is critical for identifying when DR approaches will fail.

**Assumption 1: Retrieval Improves Answer Quality**

The entire DR paradigm assumes that accessing external information leads to better answers. However, Section 6.1 explicitly states that "even with continued retrieval, the model may still produce an incorrect answer." This assumption breaks down when:

- Retrieved documents contain conflicting or misleading information
- The model cannot distinguish between high-quality and low-quality sources
- No relevant evidence exists in retrievable corpora

The paper notes that "when no relevant evidence can be retrieved, generating an answer regardless risks misleading users, particularly in safety-critical domains such as healthcare and finance" (Section 6.1). This reveals a **fundamental vulnerability**: DR systems may produce confident but unsupported answers when retrieval fails silently.

**Assumption 2: Models Can Recognize Their Knowledge Boundaries**

Adaptive retrieval (Section 3.2.2) requires models to "recognize its own knowledge boundaries, i.e., knowing what it knows and what it does not" (Section 3.2.2). However, the paper documents that:

- **Probabilistic confidence** often fails: "LLMs can be poorly calibrated, often producing overconfident predictions even when incorrect" (Section 3.2.2)
- **Consistency-based confidence** can produce "consistent but incorrect answers" (Section 3.2.2)
- **Verbalized confidence** shows "partial ability but persistent overconfidence" (Section 3.2.2)

This assumption is particularly fragile for Phase III (Full-stack AI Scientist) systems that must generate novel hypotheses—if models cannot reliably assess their own uncertainty, they cannot distinguish between genuine innovation and hallucination (Section 6.4.2).

**Assumption 3: Long-Horizon Coherence Is Achievable**

DR systems assume that maintaining context across 40+ turn trajectories is feasible. While ASearcher [85] demonstrates "trajectories of 40+ turns and 150k tokens" (Section 4.3.3), the paper acknowledges that "excessive reasoning turns or overly long reasoning chains can incur substantial computational costs and latency" and "may introduce cumulative noise and error propagation, potentially causing instability during reinforcement learning training" (Section 3.1.2).

This assumption becomes increasingly fragile as task complexity grows. The survey does not provide evidence that coherence scales indefinitely—only that current systems have pushed beyond the "10-turn ceiling that plagued earlier systems" (Section 4.3.3).

### 6.2. Scenarios and Edge Cases Not Addressed

The survey identifies several important scenarios where DR systems remain untested or underdeveloped.

**Low-Resource and Non-English Environments**

The surveyed benchmarks and systems focus almost exclusively on English-language tasks and high-resource domains. Table 4 and Table 5 (Section 5.1) show benchmarks like NQ, HotpotQA, GAIA, and BrowseComp—all English-centric. The paper mentions one multilingual consideration: Ding et al. [60] "extended this idea to multilingual settings" for consistency-based confidence (Section 3.2.2), but this is an exception rather than a systematic treatment.

**Implication:** DR systems may fail catastrophically in low-resource languages where:
- Search engines return lower-quality results
- Training data for retrieval models is scarce
- LLM-as-judge evaluators lack multilingual capability

**Adversarial and Poisoned Information Environments**

The survey does not address how DR systems respond to:
- Deliberately manipulated search results
- Poisoned retrieval corpora
- Adversarial prompts designed to trigger over-retrieval or under-retrieval

Section 6.1 notes that systems "lack fine-grained guidance on when to retrieve" and may produce incorrect answers even with retrieval—but does not discuss adversarial scenarios where retrieval results are intentionally misleading. This is a **critical gap** for deployment in contested information environments.

**Real-Time and Latency-Sensitive Applications**

While the paper mentions latency concerns (e.g., "filtering module typically incurs additional computational costs and increased latency" in Section 3.2.3), it does not provide:
- Quantitative latency benchmarks for DR systems
- Trade-off curves between answer quality and response time
- Strategies for latency-constrained deployment

The surveyed systems (OpenAI DeepResearch, Gemini DeepResearch, etc.) appear designed for asynchronous research tasks, not real-time decision support. This limits applicability in time-critical domains like emergency response or trading.

**Collaborative Multi-User Research**

The survey focuses on single-agent research workflows. Section 6.2.1 discusses "Proactive Personalization Memory Evolution" for individual users, but does not address:
- How multiple researchers collaborate through a shared DR system
- Conflict resolution when agents receive contradictory user instructions
- Attribution and accountability in multi-user settings

This limits DR applicability to team-based research environments, which are common in academia and industry.

### 6.3. Computational, Data, and Scalability Constraints

The paper documents significant resource requirements that constrain DR system deployment.

**Computational Costs by Component:**

| Component | Cost Driver | Evidence from Paper |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Multimodal Retrieval** | Visual processing overhead | "Increased computational costs for visual processing" (Section 3.2.1) |
| **Information Filtering** | Additional model inference | "Incorporating an additional filtering module typically incurs additional computational costs and increased latency" (Section 3.2.3) |
| **Tree-based Planning** | Search algorithm overhead | Requires "precise dependency modeling, careful trade-offs between speed and quality" (Section 3.1.3) |
| **Multi-agent Distillation** | Teacher system inference | "Requires careful system design, substantial inference cost, and dedicated infrastructure for logging and verification" (Section 4.2.1) |
| **End-to-end RL** | Multi-turn trajectory optimization | "Sparse rewards, excessively long responses, and unstable training" (Section 4.3.3 Takeaway) |

**Data Requirements:**

The survey reveals substantial data needs for training DR systems:

- **Strong-to-weak Distillation**: MaskSearch [397] generates "58k verified chain-of-thought trajectories"; Chain-of-Agents [180] yields "16,433 high-quality trajectories" after filtering (Section 4.2.1)
- **Iterative Self-Evolving**: Requires mechanisms to prevent "distributional drift, reward hacking, and self-reinforcing errors" that "may accumulate and degrade data quality, potentially leading to training collapse" (Section 4.2.2)
- **RL Training**: ASearcher [85] uses "autonomous QA-synthesis agent that injects noise and fuzzes questions for difficulty" (Section 4.3.3), implying substantial synthetic data generation

**Scalability Limits:**

While ASearcher [85] demonstrates "40+ turns and 150k tokens" (Section 4.3.3), the paper does not establish:
- Whether this scales to 100+ turn trajectories
- How performance degrades with trajectory length
- Memory requirements for storing long-horizon context

Section 6.2.3 identifies the "long-term credit assignment problem" as a "fundamental obstacle" for RL-based memory management—"developing reliable algorithms to attribute a final outcome to a long sequence of intermediate memory decisions" (Section 6.2.3). This suggests scalability remains an **unsolved research problem**, not an engineering constraint.

### 6.4. Critical Weaknesses and Open Questions

The survey is candid about unresolved challenges. Below we organize these by severity and likelihood of resolution.

**High-Severity, Near-Term Problems:**

| Problem | Evidence | Likelihood of Resolution |
|---------|----------|--------------------------|
| **Training Instability** | "RL algorithms such as PPO and GRPO exhibit stable behavior in single-turn scenarios, but they often become unstable when extended to multi-turn settings" with "entropy collapse and gradient explosion" (Section 6.3) | Medium—Section 6.3.1 documents emerging solutions (SimpleTIR, StarPO-S) but notes they have limitations |
| **LLM-as-Judge Bias** | "LLM judges may prefer longer responses, be affected by answer ordering, reward particular writing styles, or favor systems that resemble themselves" (Section 6.4.3) | Medium—Proposed mitigations (human calibration, debiasing training) but no established solution |
| **Over/Under-Retrieval** | Systems "rely too heavily on answer correctness to guide the entire search pipeline and lack fine-grained guidance on when to retrieve, leading to both over-retrieval and under-retrieval" (Section 6.1) | Medium—Fine-grained reward design proposed but not yet demonstrated |

**High-Severity, Long-Term Problems:**

| Problem | Evidence | Likelihood of Resolution |
|---------|----------|--------------------------|
| **Novelty-Hallucination Boundary** | "Current practices often depend on density-based novelty scores or LLM-as-judge assessments of originality, yet these alone do not ensure verifiability or differentiate between creative recombination and unfounded speculation" (Section 6.4.2) | Low—No established methodology; requires fundamental advances in evaluation |
| **Credit Assignment in Memory** | "A fundamental obstacle remains: the long-term credit assignment problem, which involves developing reliable algorithms to attribute a final outcome to a long sequence of intermediate memory decisions" (Section 6.2.3) | Low—Described as "fundamental obstacle," suggesting deep theoretical challenges |
| **Fairness and Bias Amplification** | "DR powered by autonomous agents may inadvertently inherit and amplify existing biases in academia" with "cascading effect" where "bias in early steps shape subsequent decision spaces" (Section 7.2) | Low—Requires systemic changes beyond technical fixes |

**Moderate-Severity Problems:**

| Problem | Evidence | Impact |
|---------|----------|--------|
| **Evaluation Inconsistency** | "Most benchmarks rely on LLM-as-judge methods" (Section 5.2.2) while Section 6.4.3 critiques this approach | Limits comparability across systems |
| **Multimodal Output Gaps** | "While most DR systems still focus on textual synthesis with citations, only a handful, such as OpenAI DeepResearch and H2O.ai DeepResearch, currently support comprehensive multimodal output" (Section 3.4.4, Table 2) | Restricts applicability to presentation-heavy tasks |
| **Cold-Start Entropy Collapse** | "SFT is a practical cold-start strategy for multi-turn RL, yet it introduces a significant drawback: it rapidly reduces output entropy, constraining the model's ability to explore" (Section 6.3.2) | Limits RL training effectiveness |

### 6.5. Trade-Off Summary: Design Choices With No Clear Winners

The survey documents several design trade-offs where no universally optimal solution exists. Practitioners must choose based on their specific constraints.

**Table 6: Key Trade-Offs in Deep Research System Design**

| Trade-Off | Option A | Option B | When to Choose A | When to Choose B |
|-----------|----------|----------|------------------|------------------|
| **Planning Strategy** (Section 3.1) | Parallel Planning | Sequential/Tree-based | Efficiency-critical, independent sub-queries | Complex dependencies, iterative refinement needed |
| **Retrieval Timing** (Section 3.2.2) | Fixed per-step | Adaptive/RL-learned | Predictable latency requirements | Quality-critical, variable complexity tasks |
| **Information Filtering** (Section 3.2.3) | Aggressive filtering | Conservative filtering | Context window constrained, high noise | Risk-averse, information completeness critical |
| **Memory Update** (Section 3.3.3) | Non-parametric (external) | Parametric (weights) | Transparency needed, frequent updates | Low-latency inference, stable knowledge |
| **Distillation** (Section 4.2.1) | Single-agent teacher | Multi-agent teacher | Limited infrastructure, simple tasks | Complex reasoning, diverse trajectories needed |
| **RL Optimization** (Section 4.3) | Module-specific | Entire pipeline | Resource-constrained, modular debugging | Holistic performance, end-to-end optimization |
| **RL Algorithm** (Section 4.3.1, 6.3.2) | PPO | GRPO | Accurate value estimation feasible | Value model training prohibitive |

**Critical Observation:** The survey does not provide empirical guidance on **where the optimal operating points lie** on these trade-off curves. For example:
- What filtering precision maximizes answer quality without excessive latency?
- How many RL training turns optimize the quality-cost trade-off?
- At what trajectory length does memory forgetting become necessary?

This lack of prescriptive guidance means practitioners must conduct their own ablation studies—a significant barrier to adoption.

**For Researchers:**
The most promising open problems are:

2. **Long-term credit assignment** (Section 6.2.3)—fundamental RL problem in DR context
3. **Training stability** (Section 6.3)—practical barrier to scaling RL-based approaches

**For Practitioners:**
Key deployment constraints are:
1. **Latency**—filtering and multi-turn retrieval add substantial overhead (Section 3.2.3)
2. **Evaluation reliability**—LLM-as-judge bias affects quality assessment (Section 6.4.3)
3. **Domain limitations**—benchmarks focus on English, high-resource settings (Tables 4-5)

**For Critics:**
Legitimate concerns include:
1. **Hype vs. capability gap**—Phase III (Full-stack AI Scientist) remains aspirational; current systems primarily operate at Phase I-II (Table 1, Section 2.2)
2. **Evaluation transparency**—limited head-to-head comparisons; "excellent performance" claims without specific metrics (Section 4.3.3)
3. **Safety risks**—"generating an answer regardless risks misleading users, particularly in safety-critical domains" (Section 6.1)

The survey's strength is its **honesty about limitations**—Sections 6 and 7 dedicate substantial space to challenges rather than overstating capabilities. However, this honesty also reveals that Deep Research remains a **nascent field** with significant unresolved problems before achieving the autonomous scientific discovery envisioned in Phase III.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This survey does more than catalog existing work—it fundamentally restructures how the research community conceptualizes, builds, and evaluates LLM-based information systems. Below, we analyze the cascading implications of this reframing, identify high-priority research directions it enables, map practical deployment scenarios, and provide actionable guidance for practitioners deciding when Deep Research approaches are warranted versus simpler alternatives.

### 7.1. How This Survey Reshapes the Research Landscape

The survey's impact extends beyond taxonomy creation. It establishes **three foundational shifts** that will structure future research:

**Shift 1: From Augmentation to Autonomy**

Prior to this survey, retrieval was predominantly framed as an *augmentation* technique—something added to improve LLM outputs. Section 2.3's explicit comparison (Table 1) reframes retrieval as an *action* within an autonomous workflow. This changes the research questions:

| Prior Framing (RAG) | New Framing (Deep Research) |
|---------------------|----------------------------|
| "How do we improve retrieval quality?" | "When should the agent decide to retrieve?" |
| "How do we inject context into generation?" | "How does retrieval interact with planning and memory?" |
| "Does retrieval improve accuracy?" | "Does the full research loop produce verifiable, novel insights?" |

This shift explains why Section 4.3 emphasizes **end-to-end pipeline optimization** rather than component-wise improvements. Researchers can now ask: *What policies emerge when the entire research workflow is optimized jointly?* Search-R1 [145] and R1-Searcher [300] represent early answers, but the survey makes clear this is just the beginning.

**Shift 2: Memory as a First-Class Research Component**

Section 3.3's detailed taxonomy (Figure 5) elevates memory management from infrastructure to **core research contribution**. This matters because:

- **Enables New Evaluation Dimensions**: Prior agent work rarely measured memory operations (consolidation rate, forgetting precision, update latency). The survey's taxonomy enables standardized memory benchmarks
- **Surfaces Fundamental RL Problems**: Section 6.2.3 identifies the "long-term credit assignment problem" as a "fundamental obstacle"—attributing final outcomes to specific memory decisions across long sequences. This connects DR to broader RL theory
- **Creates Modular Innovation Points**: Researchers can now contribute by improving *only* memory indexing (e.g., graph-based vs. timeline-based) while using standard planners and retrievers

**Shift 3: Evaluation Beyond Exact Match**

Section 5's comprehensive benchmark taxonomy (Tables 4-5) establishes that DR requires **multi-dimensional evaluation**. The survey documents 40+ benchmarks across four scenarios, each with different metric priorities:

- **Information Seeking**: Exact Match, F1, Search Call counts, Calibration Error
- **Report Generation**: Citation Quality, Content Quality, BradleyTerry Score, Rubric Assessment
- **AI for Research**: Novelty scores, Human/LLM Judge agreement, Proxy MSE/MAE
- **Software Engineering**: Environment success rates, Unit test pass rates

This diversity prevents the field from collapsing into single-metric optimization (as happened with GLUE/SuperGLUE in NLP). The survey's explicit critique of LLM-as-judge bias (Section 6.4.3) further encourages methodological rigor.

**Implication for Researchers**: Position your work within the survey's taxonomy. Instead of claiming "better retrieval," specify: *Which component* (query planning? memory updating?), *which sub-type* (sequential planning? graph-based indexing?), and *which evaluation dimension* (citation quality? search call efficiency?). This precision enables cumulative progress.

### 7.2. Priority Research Directions Enabled by This Work

The survey identifies several open challenges. Below, we prioritize them by **tractability** (likelihood of near-term progress) and **impact** (potential to unlock new capabilities).

#### 7.2.1. High-Tractability, High-Impact Directions

**Direction 1: Fine-Grained Retrieval Timing Rewards**

Section 6.1 states that current systems "lack fine-grained guidance on when to retrieve, leading to both over-retrieval and under-retrieval." The survey suggests:

> "Future research could explore fine-grained reward designs that assess, at each step, whether the model lacks the knowledge needed to answer the question and whether relevant documents can be retrieved." (Section 6.1)

**Concrete Research Questions:**
- Can we train retrieval policies using *intermediate* rewards (e.g., "this retrieval reduced uncertainty by X%") rather than only final answer correctness?
- What signals predict retrieval necessity before generation begins? Section 3.2.2 mentions internal state probing [126, 40, 429]—can these be operationalized as training signals?
- How do we penalize *redundant* retrievals (R1-Searcher++ [301] addresses this, but general methods are needed)?

**Why This Matters**: Over-retrieval inflates costs and latency; under-retrieval produces unsupported claims. Solving this enables efficient, reliable DR systems.

**Direction 2: Training Stability for Multi-Turn RL**

Section 6.3 documents specific failure modes ("void turns," "Echo Trap") and emerging solutions (SimpleTIR [416], StarPO-S [373]). However, the survey notes StarPO-S benefits are "more limited for GRPO" due to reward variance sensitivity (Section 6.3.2).

**Concrete Research Questions:**
- Can we design **denser, smoother reward functions** specifically for GRPO-style algorithms in multi-turn settings?
- What cold-start methods preserve exploration while improving initial performance? Section 6.3.2 notes SFT "rapidly reduces output entropy, constraining the model's ability to explore"
- Can we detect training collapse *early* (e.g., via entropy monitoring) and intervene automatically?

**Why This Matters**: Training instability prevents scaling to the 40+ turn trajectories that ASearcher [85] demonstrates. Stabilizing RL unlocks longer-horizon reasoning.

**Direction 3: Multimodal Output Standardization**

Table 2 (Section 3.4.4) shows only OpenAI DeepResearch [238] and H2O.ai DeepResearch [108] support comprehensive multimodal output (text, image, audio, video, presentation). The survey states "rich, multi-format answer generation will soon become a standard expectation" (Section 3.4.4).

**Concrete Research Questions:**
- What architectures enable seamless text-visual-audio co-generation without modality-specific post-processing?
- How do we evaluate multimodal coherence (e.g., does a generated chart accurately reflect cited statistics)?
- Can we distill multimodal capabilities into smaller models for deployment?

**Why This Matters**: Research outputs increasingly require visualizations, slides, and interactive elements. Text-only systems cannot meet these demands.

#### 7.2.2. High-Impact, Lower-Tractability Directions

**Direction 4: Novelty-Hallucination Boundary**

Section 6.4.2 identifies this as a "field-defining challenge." The survey proposes differentiating:

- **Generative novelty**: New combinations or perspectives
- **Deductive novelty**: Conclusions logically derived from known facts

**Concrete Research Questions:**
- Can we design **validity-checking mechanisms** that verify novel claims against source material? Section 6.4.2 suggests "pre-register testable claims along with verification plans"
- How do we detect "fabricated connections between sources" vs. legitimate synthesis?
- Can we use **temporal validation** (restrict to documents before cutoff date, check if outputs are later validated by subsequent publications)?

**Why This Matters**: Phase III (Full-stack AI Scientist) systems must generate hypotheses (Section 2.2). Without solving this, novel outputs remain untrustworthy.

**Direction 5: Goal-Driven Reinforced Memory Evolution**

Section 6.2.3 proposes formulating memory management as an RL decision problem where "the agent learns an optimal policy for memory operations...guided solely by the reward from the final task outcome."

**Concrete Research Questions:**
- How do we solve the **long-term credit assignment problem** for memory decisions? Section 6.2.3 calls this a "fundamental obstacle"
- Can we learn memory policies that generalize across tasks, or must they be task-specific?
- What memory operation action spaces work best (ADD/UPDATE/DELETE vs. continuous forgetting rates)?

**Why This Matters**: Current memory management relies on heuristics (recency, semantic relevance). Goal-driven optimization could dramatically improve long-horizon coherence.

**Direction 6: Fairness and Bias Mitigation**

Section 7.2 warns that "DR powered by autonomous agents may inadvertently inherit and amplify existing biases in academia," with "cascading effect" where "bias in early steps shape subsequent decision spaces."

**Concrete Research Questions:**
- How do we measure bias propagation across multi-turn research workflows?
- Can we design **debiasing interventions at every stage** (planning, retrieval, synthesis) rather than only at output?
- What fairness metrics apply to research outputs (e.g., citation diversity, geographic representation)?

**Why This Matters**: Biased research agents could systematically overlook emerging fields, non-mainstream researchers, or interdisciplinary work—perpetuating academic inequities.

### 7.3. Practical Applications and Deployment Scenarios

The survey documents applications across four domains (Section 5). Below, we map these to **concrete deployment scenarios** with specific requirements.

#### 7.3.1. Healthcare and Clinical Decision Support

**Use Case**: "What are the current clinical trial results for drug X in treating condition Y, and how do they compare to alternative treatments?"

**DR Requirements**:
- **Phase II capabilities** (Integrated Research): Must synthesize heterogeneous evidence (trial registries, published papers, regulatory filings)
- **High citation fidelity**: Section 6.1 explicitly notes risks in "safety-critical domains such as healthcare"
- **Uncertainty disclosure**: System must indicate when evidence is conflicting or insufficient

**Why DR Over RAG**: Standard RAG cannot iteratively verify conflicting sources or update conclusions as new trials are published. DR's memory management enables tracking evidence evolution over time.

**Deployment Constraints**:
- Latency tolerance: High (clinical decisions can wait hours for thorough research)
- Audit requirements: Full evidence ledger with tool provenance (Section 4.1.1)
- Regulatory compliance: Outputs must be traceable to FDA/EMA sources

#### 7.3.2. Financial Analysis and Investment Research

**Use Case**: "Analyze the competitive landscape for company X in market Y, including recent M&A activity, regulatory changes, and emerging competitors."

**DR Requirements**:
- **Real-time retrieval**: Commercial web search APIs (Section 3.2.1) for current market data
- **Multimodal output**: Charts, tables, and presentation slides (Table 2)
- **Source verification**: Distinguish between analyst reports, SEC filings, and news articles

**Why DR Over RAG**: Financial data changes rapidly; static corpora become stale. DR's adaptive retrieval timing (Section 3.2.2) ensures fresh information is incorporated.

**Deployment Constraints**:
- Latency tolerance: Medium (reports needed within hours, not seconds)
- Compliance: FINRA/SEC disclosure requirements
- Cost sensitivity: High (API calls for real-time data are expensive)

#### 7.3.3. Scientific Literature Review and Hypothesis Generation

**Use Case**: "Survey recent advances in technique X for application Y, identify gaps, and propose novel research directions."

**DR Requirements**:
- **Phase III capabilities** (Full-stack AI Scientist): Must generate hypotheses, not just summarize
- **Novelty assessment**: Distinguish genuine gaps from overlooked prior work
- **Citation completeness**: No missed key papers

**Why DR Over RAG**: Hypothesis generation requires synthesizing across papers, identifying patterns, and proposing testable claims—beyond RAG's retrieval-then-summarize workflow.

**Deployment Constraints**:
- Latency tolerance: High (literature reviews take days/weeks manually)
- Expert oversight: Human researchers must validate novel claims
- Domain specificity: Systems may need fine-tuning per scientific field

#### 7.3.4. Software Engineering and Codebase Analysis

**Use Case**: "Analyze this codebase for security vulnerabilities, suggest fixes, and generate documentation."

**DR Requirements**:
- **Tool integration**: Code execution, static analysis, dependency checking (Section 5.4)
- **Long-context handling**: Large codebases exceed standard context windows
- **Verifiable outputs**: Suggested fixes must pass tests

**Why DR Over RAG**: Code analysis requires iterative exploration (understand architecture → find vulnerabilities → verify fixes). DR's sequential planning (Section 3.1.2) supports this workflow.

**Deployment Constraints**:
- Latency tolerance: Medium (analysis can run asynchronously)
- Security: Must not expose proprietary code to external APIs
- Accuracy: False positives waste engineer time; false negatives create risk

### 7.4. Integration Guidance: When to Use DR vs. Alternatives

Not every task requires Deep Research. Below, we provide a **decision framework** based on task characteristics, resource constraints, and risk tolerance.

#### 7.4.1. Decision Matrix

| Task Characteristic | Use DR | Use RAG | Use Single-Shot LLM |
|---------------------|--------|---------|---------------------|
| **Query complexity** | Multi-hop, requires synthesis across sources | Single-hop, well-defined answer | Factual, within model's training knowledge |
| **Evidence freshness** | Critical (recent events, evolving data) | Moderate (static corpora acceptable) | Not required (timeless facts) |
| **Output format** | Long-form reports with citations | Short answers with citations | Conversational responses |
| **Reasoning horizon** | 10+ interaction turns | 1-2 retrieval steps | No retrieval needed |
| **Verification needs** | High (safety-critical, auditable) | Medium (citation tracking) | Low (informal use) |
| **Latency tolerance** | High (minutes to hours) | Medium (seconds to minutes) | Low (sub-second) |
| **Budget** | High (multiple API calls, RL training) | Medium (retrieval + generation) | Low (generation only) |

#### 7.4.2. Component Selection Guide

If you decide DR is appropriate, use this guide to select components:

**Query Planning:**
- **Parallel Planning** (Section 3.1.1): Choose when sub-queries are independent and efficiency is critical. Example: "List all competitors of company X" (each competitor can be researched separately)
- **Sequential Planning** (Section 3.1.2): Choose when later queries depend on earlier results. Example: "What caused event X, and what were the consequences?" (must understand cause before analyzing effects)
- **Tree-based Planning** (Section 3.1.3): Choose for complex, branching exploration where backtracking may be needed. Example: "Evaluate all viable approaches to problem X" (explore multiple branches, prune unpromising ones)

**Retrieval Timing:**
- **Fixed per-step**: Choose for predictable latency requirements. Example: Retrieve after every reasoning step regardless of confidence
- **Adaptive (confidence-based)**: Choose when retrieval cost is high and model calibration is reliable. Example: Trigger retrieval only when token probabilities drop below threshold
- **RL-learned policies**: Choose when you have training data and can optimize end-to-end. Example: Search-R1 [145] learns retrieval timing from outcome rewards

**Memory Management:**
- **Non-parametric (external DB)**: Choose for transparency and frequent updates. Example: Mem0 [46] with explicit ADD/UPDATE/DELETE operations
- **Parametric (model weights)**: Choose for low-latency inference with stable knowledge. Example: MLP Memory [380] as plug-in module
- **Forgetting strategy**: Use passive (FIFO, Ebbinghaus) for simple use cases; use active (RL-learned DELETE) for goal-driven optimization (Section 6.2.3)

**Optimization Approach:**
- **Workflow Prompting**: Choose for rapid prototyping without training. Example: Anthropic's multi-agent system (Section 4.1.1)
- **Supervised Fine-Tuning**: Choose when you have expert trajectories or can distill from stronger models. Example: Strong-to-weak distillation (Section 4.2.1)
- **End-to-End RL**: Choose when you have simulation environments and can tolerate training instability. Example: Search-R1 [145], ASearcher [85]

#### 7.4.3. Resource Estimation

Based on surveyed systems, expect these resource requirements:

| Component | Compute Cost | Data Requirements | Engineering Effort |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Workflow Prompting** | Low (inference only) | None | Medium (prompt design) |
| **SFT Distillation** | Medium-High (teacher inference) | 10k-100k trajectories | High (pipeline construction) |
| **End-to-End RL** | Very High (multi-turn rollouts) | Simulation environment | Very High (stability tuning) |
| **Multimodal Output** | High (visual encoding/decoding) | Paired text-visual data | High (integration) |

**Rule of Thumb**: Start with workflow prompting to validate the research workflow. Only invest in SFT or RL once the component architecture is stable.

#### 7.4.4. Risk Mitigation Checklist

Before deploying DR systems in production:

- [ ] **Evidence verification**: Can every claim be traced to a source? (Section 3.4.1)
- [ ] **Uncertainty disclosure**: Does the system indicate when evidence is conflicting or insufficient? (Section 6.1)
- [ ] **Latency bounds**: Are there maximum turn limits to prevent infinite loops? (Section 4.3.3 notes ASearcher removed "10-turn ceiling" but this requires monitoring)
- [ ] **Bias auditing**: Have you tested for systematic under-representation of certain sources or perspectives? (Section 7.2)
- [ ] **Fallback mechanisms**: What happens when retrieval fails or returns no relevant results? (Section 6.1 warns against generating answers without evidence)
- [ ] **Human oversight**: For safety-critical domains, is there expert review before outputs are used? (Section 5.3 uses human judges for peer review evaluation)

### 7.5. Synthesis: The Path Forward

This survey establishes Deep Research as a **maturing paradigm** with clear components, optimization methods, and evaluation frameworks. However, it also reveals significant gaps between current capabilities (primarily Phase I-II) and aspirational goals (Phase III Full-stack AI Scientist).

**For the Next 12-18 Months**, expect progress in:
1. **Training stability**: Solutions for multi-turn RL collapse (Section 6.3)
2. **Retrieval timing**: Fine-grained reward signals for adaptive retrieval (Section 6.1)
3. **Multimodal output**: More systems supporting charts, slides, and video (Table 2 expansion)

**For the Next 3-5 Years**, fundamental breakthroughs are needed in:
1. **Novelty-hallucination boundary**: Reliable verification of novel claims (Section 6.4.2)
2. **Memory credit assignment**: RL policies for long-horizon memory operations (Section 6.2.3)
3. **Fairness guarantees**: Systematic debiasing across research workflows (Section 7.2)

**The Survey's Lasting Contribution**: By providing a shared vocabulary and taxonomy, this work enables **cumulative progress** rather than fragmented reinvention. Researchers can now build on each other's component improvements, compare systems using standardized benchmarks, and collectively advance toward reliable, verifiable AI-driven research.

The field stands at an inflection point: Deep Research has moved from concept to early deployment, but significant challenges remain before achieving the autonomous scientific discovery envisioned in Phase III. This survey provides the roadmap—not just for where the field has been, but for where it must go next.
