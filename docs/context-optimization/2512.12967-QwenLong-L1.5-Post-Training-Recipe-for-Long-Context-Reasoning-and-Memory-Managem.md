## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces QwenLong-L1.5-30B-A3B, a model that addresses the critical gap in post-training systems for long-context reasoning by unifying a scalable data synthesis pipeline, stabilized reinforcement learning (RL), and a memory-augmented agent architecture. Achieving an average gain of 9.90 points over its Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking-2507 baseline across six benchmarks, the model matches flagship performance (e.g., Gemini-2.5-Pro) on tasks up to 128K tokens and extends reasoning capabilities to 1M~4M token contexts through its memory framework. This work demonstrates that systematic post-training innovations, rather than just architectural scaling, can enable open models to perform genuine multi-hop grounding and memory management over ultra-long sequences where standard attention mechanisms become intractable.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### 2.1 The Core Problem: A Post-Training Gap in Long-Context Reasoning

The paper identifies a specific and underexplored gap in the development of long-context capable language models. While the field has made substantial progress in extending context windows through **pre-training** (training on longer sequences from the start) and **mid-training** (continuing pre-training with longer contexts), there exists a critical absence of mature **post-training systems** specifically designed for long-context reasoning.

To understand why this matters, consider the distinction between these training stages:

- **Pre-training**: Learning general language patterns from vast corpora. Context extension here requires enormous computational resources and affects the model's foundational capabilities.
- **Mid-training**: Continuing pre-training with specific data distributions (e.g., longer documents). This bridges pre-training and fine-tuning but still requires significant compute.
- **Post-training**: The final stage where models are aligned for specific capabilities through methods like supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and reinforcement learning (RL). This is where task-specific behaviors are refined.

The paper argues that **long-context reasoning cannot be achieved through context extension alone**. A model may accept 128K tokens as input, but without specialized post-training, it will not learn to:
1. Locate and ground reasoning in globally distributed evidence across the full context
2. Perform multi-hop inference connecting information separated by tens of thousands of tokens
3. Manage memory iteratively when contexts exceed even extended window limits

This gap is explicitly stated in the Introduction:

> "A critical gap remains in the post-training stage: the absence of mature, end-to-end systems for long-context reasoning. Specifically, the field lacks a comprehensive post-training recipe that provides: (1) a scalable pipeline for synthesizing challenging, high-value long-context reasoning data; (2) RL-based methods tailored to the nuances of long-context reasoning; and (3) agent architectures designed to operate on information streams that exceed the context capacity."

### 2.2 Why This Problem Matters

The importance of long-context reasoning extends across both practical applications and theoretical understanding of model capabilities.

**Real-World Impact:**

Long-context reasoning enables models to handle tasks that are increasingly common in professional and research settings:

- **Document Analysis**: Corporate annual reports, legal contracts, and medical records often span hundreds of pages with critical information dispersed throughout. A model must connect a financial figure on page 12 with a risk disclosure on page 87 to provide accurate analysis.
- **Code Repository Understanding**: Large codebases contain interrelated functions, classes, and documentation spread across many files. Reasoning about system behavior requires integrating information from multiple locations.
- **Scientific Literature Review**: Research papers reference methods, results, and limitations across sections. Synthesizing findings requires multi-hop reasoning over distributed evidence.
- **Long-Form Dialogue**: Conversational agents must maintain coherence and recall information from earlier turns while processing new inputs, a capability directly tested by the LongMemEval benchmark where QwenLong-L1.5 achieves a +15.60 point gain (Table 8).

**Theoretical Significance:**

Beyond applications, this work addresses fundamental questions about how language models process and reason over extended information:

- **Credit Assignment in Long Sequences**: When a model produces an incorrect answer after processing 100K tokens, which tokens in the reasoning chain contributed to the error? The paper identifies this as a core challenge in Section 4.3, noting that correct and incorrect responses in long-context tasks have substantially higher similarity (ROUGE-L of 45.37 for DocMath vs. 27.71 for AIME24/25 in Table 3), making it difficult for RL algorithms to assign credit correctly.
- **Exploration-Exploitation Trade-offs**: Long-context RL exhibits unique instability patterns. The paper shows that high-entropy tokens correlate strongly with gradient norms (Spearman's ρ = 0.96, p < 0.0001 in Figure 9), meaning exploratory behavior during reasoning can destabilize training if not carefully controlled.
- **Memory as a Computational Primitive**: When contexts exceed physical limits (1M~4M tokens), the model must learn to compress and update information iteratively. This transforms reasoning from single-pass inference to sequential decision-making, fundamentally changing how we think about model architecture.

### 2.3 Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

To appreciate QwenLong-L1.5's contributions, we must understand what came before and where those approaches fall short.

**Pre-Training and Mid-Training Approaches:**

Prior work has focused heavily on extending context during pre-training phases. The paper cites Yang et al. (2025a), Team et al. (2025a), and Deepmind (2025) as examples of this direction. These approaches:

- **Require massive compute**: Extending context from 8K to 128K tokens during pre-training increases attention complexity quadratically, demanding orders of magnitude more resources.
- **Do not guarantee reasoning capability**: A model trained on long sequences may learn to attend over long ranges but not necessarily learn to *reason* over distributed evidence. The distinction between "can process" and "can reason" is critical.
- **Lack task-specific optimization**: Pre-training optimizes for next-token prediction, not for multi-hop grounding or memory management behaviors needed in downstream tasks.

**Architectural Innovations:**

Another line of work modifies model architecture to handle longer contexts more efficiently. The paper references Team et al. (2025b) and Qiu et al. (2025) as examples. These include:

- **Sparse attention mechanisms**: Reducing quadratic complexity by attending to only a subset of tokens.
- **Linear attention variants**: Approximating attention with linear-time operations.
- **Memory-augmented architectures**: Adding external memory modules to store and retrieve information.

While promising, these approaches:

- **Require retraining from scratch or extensive fine-tuning**: Architectural changes are not easily retrofitted to existing models.
- **May sacrifice accuracy for efficiency**: Approximations can degrade reasoning quality on complex tasks.
- **Do not address the data problem**: Even with efficient architectures, models need high-quality training data to learn long-context reasoning behaviors.

**Simple Retrieval and Needle-in-a-Haystack Tasks:**

Earlier benchmarks and training data often focused on simpler retrieval tasks. The paper explicitly distinguishes its approach from:

- **Needle-in-a-haystack retrieval** (Kamradt, 2023; Hsieh et al., 2024): Tasks where a single fact is hidden in a long context and must be retrieved. This tests attention but not reasoning.
- **Single-hop RAG** (Kočisk`y et al., 2018): Retrieval-augmented generation where information is fetched and used directly without multi-step inference.

The limitation is clear: these tasks do not require the model to **connect multiple pieces of evidence** or perform **multi-hop grounding**. QwenLong-L1.5's data synthesis pipeline explicitly moves beyond this by deconstructing documents into atomic facts and their relationships, then programmatically composing verifiable reasoning questions (Abstract).

**Previous RL Approaches for Reasoning:**

Standard reinforcement learning methods face specific challenges in long-context settings:

- **PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization)**: Relies on a value network and generalized advantage estimation (GAE). For long-context inputs, "the quadratic complexity of the attention mechanism renders PPO...computationally prohibitive" (Section 2.2).
- **GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization)**: Used by QwenLong-L1.5 as a base, GRPO samples groups of candidate responses and normalizes rewards within each group, avoiding the need for a value network. However, standard GRPO exhibits instability in long-context multi-task training due to:
  - Distributional drift across diverse task types
  - Reward estimation bias from group-level normalization
  - Uncontrolled entropy growth leading to training collapse

**Previous Memory Agent Work:**

The Memory Agent paradigm from Yu et al. (2025a) provides the foundation for QwenLong-L1.5's ultra-long context handling. However, the paper identifies a key limitation:

> "We found that mixing memory management training data and single-pass full-context processing training data together causes considerable damage to the overall RL training infrastructure efficiency and model training stability." (Section 4.1)

This observation led to the novel **multi-stage fusion training paradigm** where specialized experts are trained separately and then merged, rather than training a single model on mixed data.

### 2.4 Positioning Relative to Existing Work

QwenLong-L1.5 positions itself as the first **comprehensive post-training recipe** for long-context reasoning, unifying three previously disconnected components:

| Component | Prior Work | QwenLong-L1.5 Innovation |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------------|
| **Data Synthesis** | Human-annotated (1.6K samples in QwenLong-L1), simple retrieval tasks | LLM-synthesized (14.1K samples), multi-hop reasoning with globally distributed evidence |
| **RL Training** | Standard GRPO, unstable on long contexts | Task-balanced sampling, task-specific advantage estimation, AEPO for entropy control |
| **Memory Architecture** | Separate memory agent or full-context models | Multi-stage fusion RL training unifying both capabilities in one model |

The paper's positioning is evident in its comparison strategy. Table 7 benchmarks against:

- **Flagship reasoning models** (Gemini-2.5-Pro, GPT-5): Demonstrating that a 30B parameter model can approach their performance through systematic post-training.
- **Lightweight reasoning models** (Gemini-2.5-Flash-Thinking, DeepSeek-R1-0528): Showing advantages over similarly-sized competitors.
- **Direct baseline** (Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking-2507): Isolating the contribution of the post-training recipe itself, achieving a +9.90 point average gain.

Critically, the paper does not claim architectural superiority. The base model remains Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking. The gains come entirely from:
1. **Better training data** (Section 3): 14.1K synthesized samples vs. 1.6K in QwenLong-L1, with max input length increased from 59,563 to 119,932 tokens (Table 1).
2. **Better RL algorithms** (Section 4): Task-balanced sampling, task-specific advantage estimation, and AEPO addressing instability unique to long-context training.
3. **Better memory integration** (Section 2.3, 4.1): Multi-stage fusion training that preserves both single-pass and memory-agent capabilities.

This positioning is significant because it suggests that **post-training innovations can close the gap with much larger models** without requiring architectural changes or massive pre-training investments. The conclusion states:

> "Our comprehensive evaluation reveals that QwenLong-L1.5 achieves performance comparable to flagship models like GPT-5 and Gemini-2.5-Pro, with significant gains on tasks requiring multi-hop reasoning and information aggregation."

The work thus occupies a distinct niche: not competing on model scale or architecture, but on the **systematic engineering of the post-training pipeline** itself. This is both a practical contribution (enabling smaller models to achieve competitive performance) and a research contribution (identifying and solving previously unrecognized challenges in long-context RL).

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a **post-training methodology paper** that presents a complete system for teaching an existing language model to reason over extremely long documents. The core idea is that long-context reasoning capability emerges not from architectural changes alone, but from a carefully engineered pipeline combining synthetic data generation, stabilized reinforcement learning, and memory-augmented processing.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a **post-training recipe** that transforms a standard 30-billion parameter reasoning model (Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking) into a specialized long-context reasoning agent capable of processing documents up to 4 million tokens. The solution addresses three interconnected problems: how to generate training data that actually teaches multi-hop reasoning (not just retrieval), how to stabilize reinforcement learning when training on sequences 100× longer than typical RL tasks, and how to extend reasoning beyond physical context window limits through iterative memory management.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The QwenLong-L1.5 system consists of three major components that operate in sequence during training, then unify into a single model at deployment:

1. **Long-Context Data Synthesis Pipeline** (Section 3): Takes raw document corpora (82,175 documents, 9.2 billion tokens) as input and outputs 14,100 verified question-answer pairs. This component deconstructs documents into atomic facts, builds knowledge graphs or structured tables, then programmatically composes multi-hop reasoning questions that require connecting information across distant document sections.

2. **Stabilized Reinforcement Learning Framework** (Section 4): Takes the synthesized QA pairs and optimizes the policy model through four progressive training stages. This component implements task-balanced sampling, task-specific advantage estimation, and Adaptive Entropy-Controlled Policy Optimization (AEPO) to prevent training collapse on long sequences.

3. **Memory-Augmented Agent Architecture** (Section 2.3): Takes document chunks (32K tokens each) and a query as input, then iteratively updates a compact memory representation (15K tokens) while generating navigational plans. This component enables reasoning over 1M–4M token contexts that exceed the model's physical 128K window through sequential decision-making rather than single-pass inference.

The training pipeline first builds full-context reasoning capability (Stages 1–3), then trains a separate memory expert (Memory-RL), merges the two using the SCE algorithm, and performs a final full-context refinement (Stage 4) to produce a unified model proficient in both modes.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **Data Synthesis First**: We begin with the data pipeline because the RL training cannot succeed without high-quality, appropriately difficult training examples. Understanding how questions are constructed reveals what capabilities the model is actually learning.
- **RL Stabilization Second**: Once we know what data the model trains on, we explain why standard RL algorithms fail on this data and how task-balanced sampling, task-specific advantage estimation, and AEPO address these failures.
- **Memory Architecture Third**: We then cover how the model handles contexts exceeding its physical window, which requires a fundamentally different processing paradigm than single-pass reasoning.
- **Progressive Training Paradigm Fourth**: Finally, we explain how all components are integrated through multi-stage training with model merging, which resolves the conflict between full-context and memory-agent capabilities.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### 3.4.1 Long-Context Data Synthesis Pipeline

The data synthesis pipeline addresses a fundamental bottleneck: human annotators cannot reliably create or verify complex reasoning questions over contexts exceeding 32K tokens. The paper observes that for long-context tasks, humans struggle both to formulate challenging multi-hop questions and to exhaustively verify answers when relevant evidence is dispersed across hundreds of pages. To overcome this, the authors build an entirely LLM-driven synthesis system that scales from 1,600 training samples in QwenLong-L1 to 14,100 samples in QwenLong-L1.5 (Table 1).

**Corpus Collection and Preprocessing:**

The foundation is a heterogeneous document repository spanning five categories: code repositories (high-starred Python projects), academic literature (STEM, medicine, law, social sciences, arXiv papers), professional documents (annual reports, financial statements, product manuals, government publications), general knowledge and literature (novels, detective stories, Wikipedia), and dialogue data (LLM-simulated multi-turn conversations). After rule-based filtering and LLM-as-a-judge quality assessment, the final corpus contains 82,175 documents totaling approximately 9.2 billion tokens. This diverse corpus ensures the model learns reasoning patterns applicable across domains rather than overfitting to a single document type.

**Three QA Synthesis Methods:**

The pipeline employs three distinct synthesis strategies, each targeting different reasoning capabilities:

**Method 1: In-depth Multi-hop Reasoning QA (Knowledge Graph-based):** This method constructs questions requiring connections between facts separated by tens of thousands of tokens. The process begins with knowledge graph construction, where triplets (entity-relation-entity) are extracted from documents across diverse domains. These are aggregated into a cross-document knowledge graph via domain-level clustering to ensure graph quality. For reasoning path sampling, the system generates challenging multi-hop paths by sampling relation-relevant subgraphs centered on target entities using strategies like Random Walk and Breadth-First Search. Critically, path nodes are deliberately distributed sparsely across multiple documents to mandate cross-document information synthesis. Path complexity is heightened through information perturbation—for example, obfuscating entities temporally ("the year ending with 5 in the late 20th century") or institutionally ("a prestigious science university in Beijing"). Question generation then synthesizes QA pairs based on extracted paths, spanning multi-fact reasoning, temporal reasoning, causal analysis, and hypothetical scenarios. Quality is enforced through Blind Knowledge Screening (removing the source document to verify the model cannot answer from internal knowledge) and Scarce Knowledge Validation.

**Method 2: Corpus-level Numerical Reasoning QA (Structural Tabular Data Engine):** This method addresses complex calculation scenarios requiring global information integration. The process commences with document collection where unstructured documents undergo parsing and filtering to ensure sufficient token volume and rich statistical tables. Schema extraction then formalizes the underlying data structure and relational schema. Data table aggregation transforms disparate content into a unified, structured, cross-document corpus table. In parallel, a diverse pool of natural language queries is generated through LLM-based expansion from initial templates. These queries are translated into executable SQL statements during the NL2SQL execution stage. By executing SQL against aggregated tables, the system accurately simulates complex calculation processes such as statistical aggregation and numerical computation, deriving ground truth answers. Finally, relevant source documents are concatenated to form the long-context QA pair. These pairs specifically target scenarios involving statistical aggregation, numerical calculation, and temporal reasoning that inherently require advanced capabilities for global information integration.

**Method 3: General Long-Context Reasoning (Multi-Agent Self-Evolve Framework):** For tasks like viewpoint analysis and long in-context learning, the paper adopts the MASE framework from Yang et al. (2025b). As illustrated in Figure 5, this method proceeds iteratively: given a cluster of $n$ documents $C = \{c_i\}_{i=1}^n$ and a task type $\tau$, a proposer agent first generates new questions, then a solver agent attempts to solve them, and finally a verifier agent validates correctness. The proposer agent generates question-answer pairs in an iterative curriculum. In the first iteration, it is conditioned on all $n$ raw documents to produce a pair $(q, a)$. After each verified pair is created, it is appended to a history buffer $H$ storing valid QA pairs for the current document set. In subsequent iterations, the proposer is conditioned on both raw documents and stored QA pairs. These exemplars discourage redundancy and, via prompting, push the proposer to generate harder and more diverse questions than those already proposed. The solver agent attempts to solve the generated question based on documents, and a rule-based method extracts the final prediction $y$ from the response. The verifier agent estimates semantic equivalence between the solver's prediction $y$ and the proposer's reference answer $a$. Validated tasks are stored to the RL dataset, and corresponding QA pairs are saved to the history buffer.

**Data Verification Pipeline:**

All synthesized samples undergo two critical validation checks before entering the training set. The Knowledge Grounding Check temporarily removes the source document and tests if the model can still answer the question. Samples that could be answered correctly (i.e., relying on the model's internal knowledge) are filtered out to ensure the data specifically tests contextual reasoning rather than parametric memory. The Contextual Robustness Check expands the context with irrelevant documents and verifies the model's answer. Any sample where the answer accuracy (pass@k) drops to zero is discarded. This ensures the question and its answer are robust, not brittle to context changes. After this rigorous pipeline of multi-stage difficulty filtering, deduplication, and test set decontamination, the final training set contains 14,100 high-quality samples from an initial pool of 42,700 synthesized examples.

**Training Data Characteristics:**

Table 1 shows the substantial expansion from QwenLong-L1 to QwenLong-L1.5. The maximum input length increases from 59,563 tokens to 119,932 tokens, and the average input length increases from 11,441 tokens to 34,231 tokens. Figure 3 illustrates the training sample input length distributions, showing QwenLong-L1.5 substantially increases the volume of training data longer than 64K tokens. The question types expand beyond multi-fact reasoning and numerical calculation to include hypothetical scenarios, long in-context learning, temporal reasoning, causal analysis, viewpoint analysis, and dialogue needle-in-a-haystack tasks.

#### 3.4.2 Stabilized Reinforcement Learning Framework

The paper formulates long-context reasoning as a reinforcement learning problem where the goal is to optimize a policy model $\pi_\theta$ to generate a response $y$ that maximizes a reward function $r_\phi(c, q, y)$ given context $c$ and question $q$. The standard objective maximizes KL-regularized expected reward:

$$\max_{\pi_\theta} \mathbb{E}_{c,q \sim \mathcal{D}, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|c,q)} \left[ r_\phi(c, q, y) \right] - \beta D_{\text{KL}} \left[ \pi_\theta(y | c, q) \,||\, \pi_{\text{ref}}(y | c, q) \right]$$

where $\mathcal{D}$ is the training dataset, $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ denotes a reference policy, and $\beta$ controls the strength of KL regularization. However, for long-context inputs, the quadratic complexity of attention renders PPO (which relies on a value network) computationally prohibitive. Therefore, the paper employs GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization), which samples a group of $G$ candidate responses $\{y_i\}_{i=1}^G$ from the old policy $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ and estimates advantage through group-wise reward z-score normalization, obviating the need for a separate value network.

**GRPO Objective Simplification:**

The standard GRPO objective is:

$$J_{\text{GRPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{c,q \sim \mathcal{D}, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^G \frac{1}{|y_i|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \left( \min \left( \rho_{i,t}(\theta) A_i, \text{clip} \left( \rho_{i,t}(\theta), 1 - \varepsilon, 1 + \varepsilon \right) A_i \right) - \beta D_{\text{KL}}(\pi_\theta || \pi_{\text{ref}}) \right) \right]$$

where $\rho_{i,t}(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(y_{i,t}|c,q,y_{i,<t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_{i,t}|c,q,y_{i,<t})}$ is the importance sampling ratio for token $t$ in sequence $i$. The group-relative advantage $A_i$ is computed by normalizing sequence-level rewards:

$$A_i = \frac{r_i - \text{mean}(\{r_k\}_{k=1}^G)}{\text{std}(\{r_k\}_{k=1}^G)}$$

The paper makes two critical simplifications. First, consistent with recent findings that removing KL regularization improves exploration and accelerates convergence, they set $\beta = 0$. Second, they operate in a strictly on-policy setting, performing only a single gradient update per batch. This implies $\pi_\theta = \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$, so the importance sampling ratio $\rho_{i,t}(\theta) = 1$, making the clipping function inactive. Since the advantage $A_i$ is independent of $t$, the objective simplifies to:

$$J_{\text{GRPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{c,q \sim \mathcal{D}, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}} \left[ \frac{1}{\sum_{j=1}^G |y_j|} \sum_{i=1}^G A_i \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \rho_{i,t}(\theta) \right]$$

The paper integrates a token-level policy gradient loss from DAPO (Decoupled Clip and Dynamic Sampling Policy Optimization), which normalizes each token's contribution by the total number of tokens in the group. This ensures every token in the same group contributes equally to the final objective, preventing the learning signal from valuable tokens in high-quality, long responses from being diluted.

**Challenge 1: Multi-Task Distribution Imbalance**

Long-context data exhibits a multi-clustered distribution with significant divergence among different clusters (Figure 7), unlike traditional RL training data for mathematics or code. A traditional random sampler leads to distributional imbalances within each training batch, compromising training stability. This manifests as a rapid increase in instantaneous entropy (Figure 8), preventing effective training scale-up.

**Solution: Task-Balanced Sampling**

The paper implements a two-level balancing strategy. Prior to training, they perform pre-inference on data from each source using the base model, stratify the data into uniform bins based on resulting pass@k scores, and uniformly sample an equal number of training samples from each bin. During training, they replace the conventional random sampler with a task-balanced sampler that draws an equal number of samples from each of five designated task types: multiple choices, document multi-hop reasoning, general reading comprehension, dialogue memory, and corpus-level numerical calculation.

**Solution: Task-Specific Advantage Estimation**

In standard GRPO, group-level reward standard deviation normalization can introduce biased estimation. Batch-level normalization was proposed to mitigate this bias, but given the task-balanced sampling strategy, batch-level normalization can introduce potential noise due to reward distribution variance across different tasks. Therefore, the paper adopts a task-aware approach to compute reward standard deviation when estimating advantage. For the $i$-th response of the policy model, they modify the group-level standard deviation to the standard deviation of rewards from all samples belonging to the same task within the current training batch $\mathcal{B}_{\text{task}}$:

$$A_i^{\text{task}} = \frac{r_i^{\text{task}} - \text{mean}(\{r_k^{\text{task}}\}_{k=1}^G)}{\text{std}(r^{\text{task}} | r^{\text{task}} \in \mathcal{B}_{\text{task}})}, \quad \text{task} \in \{\text{mc}, \text{qa}, \text{niah}, \ldots\}$$

Compared to the group-level approach, this task-level method reduces bias caused by noisy samples. In contrast to batch-level standard error estimation, it isolates tasks with dense reward (e.g., NIAH tasks with reward range from 0 to 1) and sparse reward (e.g., QA or multiple-choice tasks), providing more accurate estimation for different tasks. Table 2 shows this combined approach achieves a 2.55-point average gain compared to the GRPO baseline, with particularly pronounced improvement on MRCR (a dense reward task).

**Challenge 2: Negative Gradient Instability**

Unlike short-context tasks, long-context tasks require models to first locate task-relevant information within the context and then perform reasoning. This constraint forces all responses to be grounded in the same context, resulting in higher similarity between correct and incorrect reasoning paths. Table 3 shows the phrase-level overlap (ROUGE-L) between correct and incorrect responses is substantially greater for DocMath (45.37) than for AIME24/25 (27.71). This high similarity implies incorrect responses contain numerous correct steps, exacerbating the reward credit assignment problem in RL and leading to training instability.

Figure 9 reveals a strong correlation between high-entropy tokens and their corresponding gradient norms during long-context RL (Spearman's $\rho = 0.96$, $p < 0.0001$). This indicates high-entropy tokens tend to produce large gradients, increasing parameter update variance and destabilizing optimization. Moreover, high-entropy tokens often reflect exploratory behavior in the reasoning process, and avoiding excessive penalization helps preserve the model's ability to explore and potentially correct originally erroneous paths.

**Solution: Negative Gradient Clipping**

The paper proposes clipping either high-entropy negative responses or high-entropy tokens within negative responses. The modified objective introduces an indicator function $I(i, t)$:

$$J_{\text{GRPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{c,q \sim \mathcal{D}, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}} \left[ \frac{1}{\sum_{j=1}^G |y_j|} \sum_{i=1}^G A_i \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \rho_{i,t}(\theta) I(t, i) \right]$$

where $I(i, t)$ is defined as:

$$I(i, t) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } A_i < 0 \text{ and } \left( (P_{\text{token\_level}} \land H(t|i) > \tau_{\text{token}}) \lor (\neg P_{\text{token\_level}} \land \bar{H}(i) > \tau_{\text{sequence}}) \right) \\ 1 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Here, $H(t|i) = -\sum_{v \in V} \pi_\theta(y_{i,t}|c, q, y_{i,<t}) \log \pi_\theta(y_{i,t}|c, q, y_{i,<t})$ and $\bar{H}(i) = \frac{1}{|y_i|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} H(t|i)$ denote the token-level and sequence-level entropies for response $i$, respectively. $\tau_{\text{token}}$ and $\tau_{\text{sequence}}$ are their corresponding thresholds. $P_{\text{token\_level}}$ is a boolean parameter selecting between token-level (True) and sequence-level (False) clipping.

Table 4 shows that in the token-level setting, clipping high-entropy tokens yields larger gains than clipping low-entropy tokens (particularly on MRCR, where clipping low-entropy tokens results in a 10-point drop). In the sequence-level approach, both strategies stabilize training, but removing too many negative gradient signals can cause entropy collapse and degrade results. The paper notes that with proper balance between exploration and exploitation, sequence-level clipping can better stabilize model optimization and provide additional performance gains.

**Solution: Adaptive Entropy-Controlled Policy Optimization (AEPO)**

Building on the negative gradient clipping findings, AEPO dynamically masks rollout sequences associated with negative advantages during online RL training, governed by policy entropy. Given a policy model $\pi_\theta$ and a training batch $\mathcal{B}$, the batch-level entropy is defined as:

$$H(\pi_\theta, \mathcal{B}) = -\frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|} \sum_{i=1}^{|\mathcal{B}|} \frac{1}{|y_i|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \sum_{v \in V} \pi_\theta(v|c, q, y_{i,<t}) \log \pi_\theta(v|c, q, y_{i,<t})$$

where $V$ denotes the vocabulary space. The paper establishes a target entropy range defined by a lower bound $H_{\text{low}}$ and an upper bound $H_{\text{high}}$. During training, if the average batch entropy exceeds $H_{\text{high}}$, AEPO masks all samples with negative advantages. The model is updated exclusively using positive samples, functioning as advantage-weighted online rejection sampling fine-tuning, effectively reducing the model's entropy. Conversely, when entropy drops below $H_{\text{low}}$, negative gradients are reintroduced to prevent entropy collapse.

Table 5 shows AEPO yields an average performance gain of 3.29 points over the GRPO baseline on Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507. Figure 11 illustrates that when applied to Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking, AEPO maintains an optimal balance between exploration (with negative gradient) and exploitation (without negative gradient), crucial for scaling RL training to larger step counts without degradation.

#### 3.4.3 Memory-Augmented Agent Architecture

Even with extended context windows, arbitrarily long sequences cannot be accommodated in a single pass. The paper recognizes that tasks exceeding 4M tokens require a fundamentally different processing paradigm. They adopt the Memory Agent paradigm from Yu et al. (2025a), which reframes reading comprehension as a sequential decision-making process rather than single-pass inference.

**Sequential Memory Processing:**

Given a long context and a query, the system first decomposes the user query into a core question $q_{\text{core}}$ and formatting instructions $q_{\text{inst}}$. This separation prevents format constraints (e.g., JSON schema) from interfering with flexible reasoning during iterative memory updates. While $q_{\text{inst}}$ is reserved for final generation to ensure format compliance, $q_{\text{core}}$ guides the reasoning process to maintain focus on answering the question. The document is partitioned into chunks $\{x_1, \ldots, x_K\}$. At each step $t$, the policy $\pi_\theta$ observes the current chunk $x_t$ and historical states to update the memory $m_t$. The model incorporates an explicit planning mechanism: alongside the memory update, the agent generates a navigational plan $p_t$ to guide attention and information extraction for the subsequent chunk $x_{t+1}$. The state transition is formalized as:

$$(m_t, p_t) \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid m_{t-1}, p_{t-1}, x_t, q_{\text{core}})$$

This recurrent mechanism effectively "folds" the global context into a compact representation while actively planning the reasoning path. After processing the final chunk $x_K$, the model generates the final answer $y$ by integrating the accumulated memory $m_K$ with the original formatting instructions:

$$y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid m_K, q_{\text{core}}, q_{\text{inst}})$$

**Memory Optimization via RL:**

To optimize the policy $\pi_\theta$, the paper employs GRPO based on trajectory-level rewards. For each question $(q_{\text{core}}, q_{\text{inst}})$ and document chunks $x = \{x_1, \ldots, x_K\}$, they sample $G$ distinct trajectories $\{\tau_1, \ldots, \tau_G\}$. Each trajectory follows the pattern: at step $t \leq K$, the agent processes chunk $x_t$ to produce memory update $m_{i,t}$ and navigational plan $p_{i,t}$ for the next chunk, and finally produces answer $y_i$. Thus:

$$\tau_i = \{(m_{i,1}, p_{i,1}), \ldots, (m_{i,K}, p_{i,K}), y_i\}$$

A trajectory-level reward $R(\tau_i)$ is computed based on the correctness of $y_i$. The trajectory-level advantage is calculated via group-wise normalization and broadcast as a consistent learning target to all actions in the trajectory. The policy is updated end-to-end according to the simplified GRPO objective.

**Training Configuration:**

For memory-RL training, the maximum input length is 128K tokens with a chunk size of 32K tokens and memory size of 15K tokens. This configuration allows the model to process documents far exceeding its physical context window through iterative compression and planning.

#### 3.4.4 Progressive Training Paradigm with Model Merging

The paper adopts a multi-stage length extension paradigm to avoid training instability caused by abrupt transitions from traditional short-input direct reasoning to multi-hop grounding patterns required for long-context reasoning. As input context length increases, reasoning content length exhibits a generally positive growth trend. Therefore, QwenLong-L1.5 synchronously extends the maximum rollout length for each RL stage.

**Four Full-Context RL Stages:**

The paper employs three different input-output length settings across the progressive stages: (1) 20K tokens input with 12K tokens output (Stage 1), (2) 60K tokens input with 20K tokens output (Stage 2), and (3) 120K tokens input with 50K tokens output (Stage 3). During transitions between different RL stages, they adopt the difficulty-aware retrospective sampling strategy from QwenLong-L1, applying difficulty filtering to training data using the input-output length settings of the subsequent stage.

**Memory-RL and Model Merging:**

The paper found that mixing memory management training data and single-pass full-context processing training data together causes considerable damage to overall RL training infrastructure efficiency and model training stability. Therefore, they adopted a paradigm of training specialized experts followed by model merging. After 3 stages of full-context RL training, they continued memory management RL training based on QwenLong-L1.5-RL-Stage3 to obtain an expert model specialized in memory management. They then utilized the SCE (Self-Correction Ensemble) algorithm to merge this expert model with QwenLong-L1.5-RL-Stage3. Finally, they performed full-context training on the merged model in the fourth stage, ultimately obtaining QwenLong-L1.5.

**Training Hyperparameters:**

The RL framework is implemented using VeRL. During generation, they employ a sampling temperature of 0.7 and a top-p value of 0.95. To balance rollout diversity and computational efficiency, they utilize a group size of $G = 8$. They conduct purely on-policy RL training with a batch size of 128 and a constant learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-6}$. They adopt a hybrid reward mechanism combining rule-based verification with LLM-as-a-judge. Specifically, rule-based verification checks whether the ground truth is contained within the model output, while gpt-oss-120b is employed as an external judge for questions where rule-based verification fails.

**Performance Dynamics:**

Table 10 shows performance at different post-training stages. A substantial portion of the performance gain is achieved after just the first stage (Full-context RL Stage-1), where the average score increases from 61.92 to 69.59. This demonstrates that the synthetic data used in Stage-1 activates the model's foundational ability to ground and reason over long documents. For tasks characterized by longer contexts and the need for global information aggregation (MRCR, CorpusQA), performance consistently improves as training progresses from Stage 1 to 3. The memory-RL stage provides significant gains in memory agent capability but leads to a trade-off, as full-context inference performance drops to 68.53. The model merging stage effectively resolves this conflict by restoring full-context capability to 71.18 while further improving the memory agent score to 21.68. Subsequent full-context RL training in Stage 4 does not degrade the acquired memory skill but enhances it to 22.53, yielding a single unified model proficient in both direct long-context reasoning and memory-augmented agent processing.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The contributions of QwenLong-L1.5 extend beyond incremental hyperparameter tuning; they represent fundamental shifts in how we approach post-training for long-context capabilities. Below, we distill the four most critical insights that distinguish this work from prior art. Each insight addresses a specific failure mode in existing systems—whether it be data quality, training stability, capability interference, or generalization—and offers a principled solution validated by empirical results.

### 4.1 Verification-Driven Data Synthesis Over Simple Scaling

A common misconception in scaling model capabilities is that simply increasing the volume of training data yields proportional gains. QwenLong-L1.5 challenges this by demonstrating that **data quality and verification logic matter more than raw scale** for reasoning tasks.

Prior long-context datasets often relied on "needle-in-a-haystack" retrieval tasks, where a single fact is hidden in a long document. While this tests attention, it does not test reasoning. The key innovation here is the **two-stage verification pipeline** described in Section 3, which ensures every training sample actually requires long-context grounding:

1.  **Knowledge Grounding Check:** The system temporarily removes the source document and tests if the model can answer the question using only internal parametric memory. If the model answers correctly without the context, the sample is discarded. This forces the model to learn reliance on external evidence rather than memorized facts.
2.  **Contextual Robustness Check:** The system expands the context with irrelevant documents and verifies that the answer accuracy (pass@k) does not drop to zero. This ensures the reasoning is robust to noise, not brittle.

This verification logic transforms data synthesis from a generation task into a **filtering task**. The result is a dataset where 14.1K samples (Table 1) carry significantly higher learning signal density than the 1.6K samples in QwenLong-L1. The significance is evident in the performance gains: the model achieves a +9.90 point average improvement over its baseline (Table 7), primarily driven by tasks requiring multi-hop grounding (e.g., +31.72 on MRCR). This insight suggests that for reasoning capabilities, **curating data that enforces specific cognitive behaviors is more effective than simply exposing the model to more text.**

### 4.2 Entropy as a Control Signal for RL Stability

Perhaps the most theoretically significant contribution is the identification of **policy entropy as a predictor of training instability** in long-context reinforcement learning.

In standard RL frameworks like GRPO, negative gradients (from incorrect responses) are typically treated uniformly. However, the authors uncover a critical physical property of long-context training landscapes: there is a strong positive correlation between token entropy and gradient norms in negative rollouts. As shown in Figure 9, the Spearman's correlation coefficient is $\rho = 0.96$ ($p < 0.0001$).

**Why this matters:**
*   **The Problem:** High-entropy tokens in incorrect responses indicate the model is "unsure" while being wrong. Penalizing these tokens heavily (via standard negative gradients) creates large parameter updates that destabilize the optimization trajectory, leading to training collapse.
*   **The Innovation:** Instead of static clipping, the authors propose **Adaptive Entropy-Controlled Policy Optimization (AEPO)** (Section 4.4). AEPO dynamically masks negative gradients when batch entropy exceeds a threshold $H_{\text{high}}$, effectively turning off learning signals that would cause instability. Conversely, it reintroduces them when entropy drops below $H_{\text{low}}$ to prevent entropy collapse.

This is a fundamental shift from **static regularization** (e.g., fixed KL penalties) to **dynamic state-dependent control**. The ablation results in Table 5 show AEPO yields a +3.29 point gain over standard GRPO on Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507. More importantly, Figure 11 demonstrates that AEPO maintains stable entropy dynamics over 200+ training steps, whereas baseline methods exhibit volatile spikes. This insight generalizes beyond long-context: any RL task where incorrect reasoning paths resemble correct ones (high similarity) may benefit from entropy-guided gradient masking.

### 4.3 Resolving Capability Interference via Expert Merging

A subtle but critical design choice in QwenLong-L1.5 is the recognition that **single-pass reasoning and iterative memory management are conflicting capabilities** that degrade each other when trained simultaneously.

**The Conflict:**
As noted in Section 4.1, mixing memory management training data with single-pass full-context data "causes considerable damage to the overall RL training infrastructure efficiency and model training stability." Intuitively, single-pass reasoning requires the model to attend globally across the entire window at once, while memory agents require the model to compress information sequentially and ignore past chunks once processed. Training on both objectives simultaneously creates gradient interference.

**The Solution:**
The paper introduces a **Multi-Stage Fusion Training Paradigm** (Figure 6):
1.  Train a "Full-Context Expert" (Stages 1–3).
2.  Train a separate "Memory-RL Expert" specializing in iterative processing.
3.  **Merge** the two experts using the SCE (Self-Correction Ensemble) algorithm.
4.  Perform a final refinement stage (Stage 4) on the merged model.

**The Evidence:**
Table 10 provides compelling evidence for this approach. After training the Memory-RL expert separately, the full-context performance drops to 68.53 (from 71.59 in Stage 3). However, after merging with SCE, full-context performance recovers to 71.18, and memory agent performance improves to 21.68. Crucially, the final Stage 4 refinement enhances *both* capabilities simultaneously (71.82 full-context, 22.53 memory agent).

This insight challenges the common practice of multi-task learning where all data is mixed in a single batch. It suggests that for distinct cognitive modes (global attention vs. sequential compression), **specialization followed by fusion** is superior to joint optimization. This allows a single 30B model to operate effectively in both regimes without architectural switching.

### 4.4 Long-Context Reasoning as a Foundational Skill

Finally, the paper provides empirical evidence that long-context reasoning is not an isolated capability but a **foundational skill that transfers to general domains**.

A common fear in specialized post-training is catastrophic forgetting—improving long-context skills at the expense of general knowledge. QwenLong-L1.5 demonstrates the opposite: enhancing long-context processing improves performance on short-context, reasoning-intensive benchmarks.

**Transfer Effects:**
*   **Mathematical Reasoning:** On AIME25 (a competitive math benchmark), the model gains +3.65 points over its baseline (Table 8).
*   **Agentic Memory:** On BFCL-V4 Memory-KV, the model gains +5.80 points.
*   **Dialogue Memory:** On LongMemEval, the gain is +15.60 points.

**The Mechanism:**
The authors attribute this to improved **information integration skills**. Long-context training forces the model to maintain coherence over extended sequences, identify key evidence amidst noise, and perform multi-hop inference. These skills are directly applicable to complex math problems (which require maintaining multiple constraints) and agentic tasks (which require tracking state over time). Figure 12 visually supports this via UMAP projection, showing that while the training data occupies a distinct semantic space from benchmarks like AIME or GPQA, the learned reasoning patterns generalize across these boundaries.

This insight reframes long-context training: it is not merely about handling larger inputs, but about **enhancing the model's core reasoning fidelity**. This suggests that future efforts to improve general reasoning capabilities might benefit from incorporating long-context synthesis pipelines, even if the target tasks are relatively short.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their claims about QwenLong-L1.5's capabilities. Understanding the experimental design is critical because long-context evaluation presents unique challenges: how do you measure "reasoning" versus "retrieval"? How do you test contexts that exceed physical memory limits? And how do you isolate the contribution of each component in a multi-stage pipeline? We will walk through the evaluation methodology, analyze the quantitative results with specific numbers, assess the strength of evidence, and examine ablation studies that reveal what actually drives performance.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The authors employ a comprehensive evaluation strategy spanning three categories: long-context reasoning benchmarks, general domain benchmarks, and ultra-long context memory tasks. This multi-faceted approach addresses a critical concern in specialized post-training: does improving long-context capabilities come at the expense of general abilities?

**Benchmark Categories:**

The evaluation suite consists of six primary long-context reasoning benchmarks, each targeting different capabilities:

| Benchmark | Task Type | Avg. Context Length | What It Tests |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|---------------|
| **MRCR** (Vodrahalli et al., 2024) | Needle-in-a-Haystack | 36.5K tokens | Finding and disambiguating multiple needles hidden in multi-turn synthetic conversations |
| **CorpusQA** (Tongyi-Zhiwen Team, 2025) | Multi-hop QA | 92.8K tokens | Global reasoning over evidence dispersed throughout a large corpus |
| **LongBench-V2** (Bai et al., 2025) | Multiple Choice | 85.5K tokens | Deep comprehension across six areas including code repository and structured data understanding |
| **Frames** (Krishna et al., 2025) | Multi-hop QA | Variable | Diverse Wikipedia topics requiring fact retrieval and reasoning |
| **DocMath** (Zhao et al., 2024) | Numerical Reasoning | 20.1K tokens | Mathematical reasoning within financial reports |
| **LongBench-V1-QA** (Bai et al., 2024) | Multi-hop QA | Variable | Five subsets including 2WikiMultihopQA, HotpotQA, Musique, NarrativeQA, Qasper |

**Critical Design Choice: Scoring Methodology**

The authors use different scoring methods tailored to each benchmark's format, which is important for fair comparison:

- **Multiple-choice tasks**: Standard accuracy (percentage of correct selections)
- **MRCR**: SequenceMatcher ratio (measures string similarity between predicted and ground truth answers)
- **Multi-hop QA tasks**: Maximum of Cover Exact Match (CEM) score and LLM-as-a-judge evaluation

The LLM-as-a-judge approach uses DeepSeek-V3 to evaluate semantic equivalence between predictions and ground truth. The prompt template (Table 6) explicitly instructs the judge to compare answers without solving the original problem, reducing bias. This is crucial because long-context answers may be semantically correct but phrased differently than the reference.

**Baseline Models:**

The evaluation compares QwenLong-L1.5-30B-A3B against three categories of models:

1. **Flagship Reasoning Models**: Gemini-2.5-Pro (72.40 avg), GPT-5 (74.74 avg), DeepSeek-R1-0528 (68.67 avg)
2. **Lightweight Reasoning Models**: Gemini-2.5-Flash-Thinking (68.73 avg), Qwen3-Max-Thinking-Preview (69.43 avg)
3. **Direct Baseline**: Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking-2507 (61.92 avg) — the same model before post-training

This comparison strategy isolates the contribution of the post-training recipe itself. Since the base model architecture remains unchanged, any performance gains must come from the data synthesis, RL stabilization, or memory integration methods.

**Evaluation Configuration:**

All models are evaluated with maximum input lengths of 128K tokens and maximum generation length of 50K tokens. For prompts exceeding the maximum context window, the authors employ middle truncation (preserving front and tail portions) from Bai et al. (2024). Generation uses temperature 0.7 and top-p 0.95. These standardized settings ensure fair comparison across models with different native context limits.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

The primary results appear in Table 7 and Figure 1, showing QwenLong-L1.5's performance across all six benchmarks. Let us analyze these numbers carefully, as they reveal both the strengths and limitations of the approach.

**Overall Performance:**

QwenLong-L1.5-30B-A3B achieves an average score of **71.82** across the six benchmarks. This represents a **+9.90 point improvement** over the Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking-2507 baseline (61.92). To put this in perspective:

- The model surpasses DeepSeek-R1-0528 (68.67) by **3.15 points**
- It exceeds Gemini-2.5-Flash-Thinking (68.73) by **3.09 points**
- It approaches Gemini-2.5-Pro (72.40) within **0.58 points** despite being a 30B model versus a likely much larger flagship

This is the central claim of the paper: systematic post-training can enable a 30B parameter model to reach performance previously associated with much larger systems. The numbers support this claim, but with important caveats we will discuss.

**Per-Benchmark Breakdown:**

The gains are not uniform across all tasks. Table 7 reveals a critical pattern:

| Benchmark | Baseline | QwenLong-L1.5 | Gain | Context Length |
|-----------|----------|---------------|------|----------------|
| MRCR | 51.27 | 82.99 | **+31.72** | 36.5K (avg) |
| CorpusQA | 71.56 | 81.25 | **+9.69** | 92.8K (avg) |
| LongBench-V2 | 49.11 | 55.27 | **+6.16** | 85.5K (avg) |
| Frames | 70.27 | 74.76 | **+4.49** | Variable |
| DocMath | 62.26 | 66.26 | **+4.00** | 20.1K (avg) |
| LongBench-V1-QA | 67.10 | 70.40 | **+3.30** | Variable |

**Key Observation:** The largest gains correlate with longer average context lengths. MRCR (+31.72) and CorpusQA (+9.69) show the most dramatic improvements. This validates the data synthesis pipeline's effectiveness: the model learns to ground reasoning in globally distributed evidence, not just retrieve isolated facts.

However, note that QwenLong-L1.5 does not outperform GPT-5 on all benchmarks. GPT-5 scores 84.59 on Frames versus 74.76 for QwenLong-L1.5, and 81.56 on CorpusQA versus 81.25. The claim of being "comparable to flagship models" is accurate on average but masks benchmark-specific gaps.

**Detailed Subset Analysis:**

Appendix Tables 11 and 12 provide finer-grained results. On LongBench-V2 (Table 11), the largest gain is on the **Medium length subset (+10.23 points)**, corresponding to the 32K–128K token range. This directly aligns with the synthetic data generation focus, confirming that targeted training data produces targeted improvements.

On LongBench-V1-QA (Table 12), the model achieves **+7.00 on Musique** (designed for multi-hop reasoning) and **+9.00 on NarrativeQA** (one of the longest contexts in the suite). However, there is a **-3.00 point decrease on Qasper**. The authors acknowledge this but do not provide a detailed analysis. This is a limitation: we do not know why Qasper performance degraded. Possible explanations include:
- Qasper's question types differ from the synthetic data distribution
- The model overfit to certain reasoning patterns that do not generalize to Qasper
- Evaluation noise (though unlikely given the systematic pattern elsewhere)

### 5.3 Generalization Beyond Long-Context

A critical concern in specialized post-training is catastrophic forgetting: does improving long-context capabilities degrade general knowledge? Table 8 addresses this directly by evaluating on three out-of-distribution domains.

**General Knowledge and Reasoning:**

The authors attribute this to improved information integration skills — the ability to maintain coherence over extended reasoning chains transfers to complex math problems even when they do not require long contexts.

Figure 12 supports this via UMAP projection, showing that QwenLong-L1.5 training data occupies a distinct semantic space from AIME/GPQA evaluation sets. The generalization is not due to data overlap but to learned reasoning patterns.

**Agentic Memory:**

**Dialogue Memory:**

On LongMemEval, the model achieves a **+15.60 point gain** (60.80 → 76.40). This is the largest generalization gain and directly validates the training objective: maintaining state and recalling information over extended conversations is a primary application for long-context models.

**Assessment:**

The generalization results are convincing. The model does not merely memorize long-context patterns but learns transferable reasoning skills. Table 9 evaluates performance on contexts exceeding 128K tokens, where full-attention mechanisms become intractable. This is the most ambitious claim: reasoning over 1M–4M tokens through iterative memory management.

### 5.5 Ablation Studies

The paper includes three critical ablation studies that isolate the contribution of each component. These are essential for understanding what actually drives performance.

**Multi-Task RL Strategies (Table 2):**

| Model | Avg. | MRCR | CorpusQA | LBV2 |
|-------|------|------|----------|------|
| Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507 (baseline) | 52.79 | 39.85 | 49.38 | 41.35 |
| + GRPO | 56.07 | 40.90 | 58.75 | 44.33 |
| + Task-balanced sampling | 56.86 | 51.66 | 56.25 | 42.94 |
| + Task-balanced sampling + Batch-std | 57.45 | 48.43 | 60.31 | 45.53 |
| + Task-balanced sampling + Task-batch-std | **58.62** | **53.23** | **60.94** | **43.54** |

The final configuration achieves a **+2.55 point gain** over standard GRPO (58.62 vs. 56.07). Notably, task-balanced sampling alone provides a +10.76 point gain on MRCR (51.66 vs. 40.90), confirming that distributional balance is critical for dense-reward tasks.

**Negative Gradient Clipping (Table 4):**

| Strategy | Avg. | MRCR | Notes |
|----------|------|------|-------|
| GRPO baseline | 56.07 | 40.90 | — |
| Clip low-entropy tokens | 55.56 | 36.29 | **-10 point drop on MRCR** vs. high-entropy |
| Clip high-entropy tokens | 57.02 | 46.20 | Better preservation of exploration |
| Clip low-entropy seqs (step 40) | 55.47 | 45.54 | Entropy collapse observed |
| Clip high-entropy seqs | **57.36** | **41.31** | Best stability/performance trade-off |

This ablation reveals a subtle but important insight: **clipping low-entropy negative gradients harms performance** because it removes high-confidence error signals that should be penalized. The 10-point drop on MRCR when clipping low-entropy tokens (36.29 vs. 46.20) is dramatic evidence.

**AEPO Algorithm (Table 5):**

| Model | Avg. | MRCR | CorpusQA |
|-------|------|------|----------|
| GRPO baseline | 56.07 | 40.90 | 58.75 |
| + AEPO | **59.36** | **47.88** | **64.69** |

AEPO provides a **+3.29 point average gain** over GRPO. The MRCR gain (+6.98) is particularly large, confirming that entropy control stabilizes training on dense-reward tasks.

**Progressive Training Stages (Table 10):**

This ablation shows performance at each post-training stage:

| Stage | Full-Context Avg. | Memory Agent (MRCR 512K–1M) |
|-------|-------------------|----------------------------|
| Baseline | 61.92 | 4.24 |
| Stage 1 (20K in / 12K out) | 69.59 | 17.14 |

**Critical Finding:** After Memory-RL training, full-context performance **drops from 71.59 to 68.53**. This confirms the capability interference hypothesis: training memory management degrades single-pass reasoning. The model merging step recovers full-context performance to 71.18 while improving memory agent score to 21.68. Stage 4 then enhances both simultaneously.

This is compelling evidence for the multi-stage fusion paradigm. Without model merging, the final model would have to choose between full-context and memory-agent capabilities.

### 5.6 Case Studies and Failure Modes

Appendix C provides qualitative case studies that reveal how the model succeeds and fails.

**Success Case: AIME 2025 (Figure C.1)**

The baseline model (Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking) becomes "entrenched in a qualitative calculus approach involving local extrema intervals" and ultimately resorts to heuristic approximations, producing an incorrect answer (188 vs. 240). QwenLong-L1.5 detects a logical contradiction between "exactly three discrete values" and the interval nature of local minima, triggers hypothesis revision, and shifts to algebraic synthesis, producing the correct answer.

This illustrates the **meta-cognitive improvement**: the model learns to recognize when its reasoning path is inconsistent with problem constraints and revise its approach.

**Failure Cases: High-Entropy Negative Rollouts (Figures 13, 14, 15)**

The paper shows three examples of incorrect responses with high entropy:

1. **Figure 13 (DocMath)**: The model correctly calculates cash proceeds ($8.5M) but then abruptly invalidates this with an erroneous self-correction, producing 55% instead of the correct percentage. The reasoning path is initially correct but collapses at the end.

2. **Figure 14 (Multi-Document QA)**: The model successfully identifies a computational error (2008 vs. 2007) but fails to rectify it, ultimately producing 1996 instead of 1995. The reasoning mirrors the correct solution but breaks down in final execution.

3. **Figure 15 (DocMath)**: The response exhibits frequent occurrences of '\n\n' in the reasoning process after arriving at the correct answer, degrading reasoning quality and leading to an incorrect final answer (7.54 years).

These cases illustrate the **credit assignment problem** discussed in Section 4.3: incorrect responses contain numerous correct steps, making it difficult for RL to identify which tokens contributed to the error. AEPO addresses this by masking high-entropy negative gradients, but these examples show the problem persists.

### 5.7 Assessment of Experimental Strength

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive benchmark coverage**: Six long-context benchmarks plus general domain tests provide robust evidence of capability improvements.

2. **Ablation studies isolate contributions**: Tables 2, 4, 5, and 10 clearly show the impact of each component (task-balanced sampling, AEPO, model merging).

3. **Ultra-long context evaluation**: Table 9 demonstrates capability at 1M–4M tokens, a regime where few models are evaluated.

4. **Generalization tests**: Table 8 addresses the catastrophic forgetting concern directly, showing positive transfer to math and agentic tasks.

**Limitations:**

1. **Qasper degradation unexplained**: The -3.00 point drop on LongBench-V1-QA Qasper subset (Table 12) is not analyzed. This raises questions about when the approach fails.

2. **Absolute performance on ultra-long tasks is modest**: A score of 14.29 on CorpusQA (4M) indicates significant room for improvement. The authors acknowledge this but do not propose concrete next steps.

3. **Proprietary model comparisons lack detail**: Gemini-2.5-Pro and GPT-5 scores are cited but their exact configurations (context window, temperature, etc.) are not specified, making direct comparison uncertain.

4. **AEPO hyperparameters not disclosed**: The target entropy range ($H_{\text{low}}$, $H_{\text{high}}$) is not specified, making reproduction difficult.

5. **Single random seed**: The paper does not report variance across multiple training runs. Given the instability concerns, showing standard deviations would strengthen claims.

**Overall Assessment:**

The experiments convincingly support the core claims:
- ✅ Systematic post-training improves long-context reasoning (+9.90 avg gain)
- ✅ Gains correlate with context length (MRCR +31.72, CorpusQA +9.69)
- ✅ Generalization to short-context reasoning occurs (AIME25 +3.65, LongMemEval +15.60)
- ✅ Memory agent extends capability to 1M–4M tokens (Table 9)
- ✅ Multi-stage fusion resolves capability interference (Table 10)

However, the experiments also reveal boundaries:
- ⚠️ Absolute performance on ultra-long tasks remains modest
- ⚠️ Some benchmarks show degradation (Qasper -3.00)
- ⚠️ Flagship models still outperform on several benchmarks (Frames, CorpusQA)

The paper is honest about these limitations, which strengthens credibility. The experimental design is rigorous, but future work should address the unexplained failures and provide more detailed hyperparameter disclosure for reproducibility.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While QwenLong-L1.5 demonstrates substantial progress in long-context reasoning, a critical examination reveals important assumptions, unaddressed scenarios, and remaining weaknesses. Understanding these limitations is essential for practitioners considering adoption and researchers building on this work.

### 6.1 Fundamental Assumptions

The approach rests on several assumptions that may not hold in all deployment scenarios:

**Assumption 1: Synthetic Data Adequately Represents Real-World Reasoning**

The entire training pipeline depends on LLM-synthesized question-answer pairs (14.1K samples from 42.7K synthesized examples, Section 3). The paper assumes that programmatically composed multi-hop questions capture the complexity of real-world long-context reasoning. However:

- The verification pipeline filters out samples where the model can answer from internal knowledge (Knowledge Grounding Check) or where accuracy drops with irrelevant context (Contextual Robustness Check). This ensures contextual dependence but **may bias the dataset toward artificial reasoning patterns** that differ from organic human queries.
- The UMAP projection in Figure 12 shows QwenLong-L1.5 training data occupies a "distinct semantic space" from evaluation benchmarks like AIME and GPQA. While the authors frame this as evidence of generalization, it also indicates **distributional mismatch** between training and real-world tasks.

**Assumption 2: Entropy Correlates Reliably with Training Instability**

AEPO's core mechanism depends on the observed correlation between token entropy and gradient norms (Spearman's $\rho = 0.96$, $p < 0.0001$ in Figure 9). The paper assumes this relationship is stable across:

- Different model scales (experiments primarily use Qwen3-4B-Thinking for ablations, with Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking for main results)
- Different task types (the correlation may vary between dense-reward tasks like MRCR and sparse-reward tasks like multi-hop QA)
- Different training stages (entropy dynamics in Figure 11 show stability over 200 steps, but long-term behavior beyond this is unverified)

If this correlation weakens in other settings, AEPO's entropy-based masking could inadvertently suppress useful learning signals or fail to prevent instability.

**Assumption 3: Full-Context and Memory-Agent Capabilities Are Initially Incompatible**

The multi-stage fusion paradigm (Figure 6) assumes that training full-context and memory-agent capabilities simultaneously causes "considerable damage to the overall RL training infrastructure efficiency and model training stability" (Section 4.1). While Table 10 supports this (full-context performance drops from 71.59 to 68.53 after Memory-RL), this may be:

- **Architecture-specific**: The Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking base model may have particular attention patterns that create interference. Other architectures might handle joint training better.
- **Training-hyperparameter-dependent**: The interference could stem from specific learning rates, batch sizes, or reward weighting rather than fundamental incompatibility.

This assumption necessitates the complex four-stage pipeline with model merging, increasing training complexity and compute requirements.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

Several important problem settings remain outside the scope of this work:

**Long-Input, Long-Output Tasks**

The paper explicitly acknowledges this limitation in Section 7.1:

> "Real-world applications often involve not only long inputs but also long-form outputs, such as chapter-level document revision, report generation from source materials, and complex tasks orchestrated by autonomous agents. Our current data synthesis pipeline is not yet optimized for these scenarios."

The training data focuses on question-answer pairs with bounded output lengths (12K–50K tokens across stages). Tasks requiring **generative output proportional to input length** (e.g., summarizing a 100K-token document into a 10K-token report) are not addressed. This is a significant gap for practical applications like legal document analysis or scientific literature review.

**Multi-Modal Long-Context Reasoning**

Section 7.1 states: "Furthermore, our current data is exclusively text-based. We plan to extend our framework to incorporate multi-modal data." This excludes:

- Documents with embedded figures, tables, or diagrams requiring visual reasoning
- Code repositories with both source files and visual architecture diagrams
- Scientific papers where claims depend on interpreting graphs and experimental results

As real-world long-context tasks increasingly involve multi-modal content, this limitation restricts deployment applicability.

**Subjective and Open-Ended Tasks**

The reward mechanism combines "rule-based verification with LLM-as-a-judge" (Section 5.1). Section 7.2 acknowledges:

> "This approach is effective for tasks with clear-cut correctness criteria, such as question answering. However, its efficacy diminishes for more open-ended and subjective tasks where 'correctness' is multifaceted."

Tasks like:
- Evaluating argument quality in legal briefs
- Assessing coherence in creative writing
- Judging appropriateness in dialogue responses

lack clear ground truth, making the current reward framework inadequate. The proposed "LLM-based rubric reward models" remain future work.

**Extreme Context Lengths Beyond 4M Tokens**

Table 9 shows performance at 4M tokens (CorpusQA score: 14.29), but the paper does not evaluate beyond this. The memory agent framework's scalability to 10M+ token contexts (e.g., entire codebases, multi-year document archives) is unknown. The graceful degradation observed (MRCR scores drop from 34.87 at 128K–512K to 20.72 at 1M) suggests performance may become impractical at extreme scales.

**Unexplained Benchmark Degradation**

Table 12 shows a **-3.00 point decrease on Qasper** (from 53.50 to 50.50). The paper acknowledges this but provides no analysis:

> "While a small performance decrease is observed on Qasper (-3.00), the overall trend supports the conclusion that our methodology enhances both specific reasoning patterns and general long-context processing."

This is a notable gap. Possible explanations include:
- Qasper's question types (research paper comprehension) differ from synthetic data distribution
- The model overfit to certain reasoning patterns that do not transfer to academic literature
- Evaluation variance (though systematic gains elsewhere make this less likely)

Without understanding this failure mode, practitioners cannot predict when QwenLong-L1.5 might underperform.

### 6.3 Computational and Data Constraints

The approach introduces significant resource requirements that may limit accessibility:

**Data Synthesis Costs**

The pipeline generated 42.7K synthesized examples to obtain 14.1K verified samples (Section 3), implying a **~67% rejection rate**. The paper notes in Section 7.1:

> "Although our data synthesis pipeline is automated, its scalability is constrained by practical bottlenecks, namely the API quotas of proprietary models and the computational cost of serving large open-source models for generating long-context data."

Specific constraints include:
- **LLM-as-a-judge costs**: Using gpt-oss-120b for verification (Section 5.1) requires significant API expenditure or self-hosting compute
- **Multi-agent framework overhead**: The MASE framework (Figure 5) requires three separate agent calls (proposer, solver, verifier) per iteration
- **Knowledge graph construction**: Extracting and clustering triplets across 82,175 documents (Section 3.1) demands substantial preprocessing

These costs create a barrier for researchers without access to large-scale compute infrastructure.

**Training Compute Requirements**

The four-stage progressive training paradigm (Figure 6) requires:
- Three full-context RL stages with increasing sequence lengths (20K → 60K → 120K input tokens)
- One memory-RL stage with chunked processing (32K chunks, 15K memory)
- One final full-context refinement stage
- Model merging using the SCE algorithm between stages

Each stage uses batch size 128, group size $G = 8$, and learning rate $2 \times 10^{-6}$ (Section 5.1). For 30B parameter models, this represents **orders of magnitude more compute** than standard supervised fine-tuning. The paper does not report total training hours or GPU requirements, making it difficult for practitioners to estimate resource needs.

**Memory Agent Inference Latency**

The memory agent framework processes documents iteratively (Section 2.3):
- Document partitioned into chunks $\{x_1, \ldots, x_K\}$
- At each step $t$, the policy updates memory $m_t$ and generates plan $p_t$
- Final answer generated from accumulated memory $m_K$

For a 4M token document with 32K chunks, this requires **125 sequential forward passes** plus a final generation pass. This introduces:
- **High latency**: Cannot parallelize chunk processing
- **Memory overhead**: Must store intermediate memory states and plans
- **Error accumulation**: Mistakes in early chunks propagate through subsequent updates

Table 9 shows the framework enables reasoning at 4M tokens, but the practical latency for real-time applications is not discussed.

### 6.4 Remaining Weaknesses and Open Questions

Several technical challenges remain unresolved:

**Credit Assignment Remains Approximate**

Section 7.2 acknowledges that AEPO is "a robust stabilization mechanism rather than a fundamental solution to the credit assignment problem":

> "The core challenge remains: our current approach assigns a uniform advantage signal to an entire reasoning step. A primary focus of our future research will be to develop a token-level credit assignment mechanism within the thinking trajectory."

The case studies in Appendix C (Figures 13, 14, 15) illustrate this problem: incorrect responses contain numerous correct steps, but the RL algorithm cannot distinguish which tokens contributed to the error. AEPO masks high-entropy negative gradients but does not solve the underlying attribution problem. This limits learning efficiency and may require more training data to achieve the same performance as a token-level credit assignment method.

**Hyperparameter Disclosure Gaps**

Critical hyperparameters are not specified, hindering reproducibility:
- **AEPO entropy thresholds**: $H_{\text{low}}$ and $H_{\text{high}}$ are mentioned in Section 4.4 but never given numerical values
- **Gradient clipping thresholds**: $\tau_{\text{token}}$ and $\tau_{\text{sequence}}$ in Equation 9 are not specified
- **Task-balanced sampling bins**: The number of pass@k bins for stratification (Section 4.2) is not disclosed
- **Model merging weights**: The SCE algorithm's merging coefficients are not detailed

Without these values, independent reproduction is difficult. The paper reports results from single training runs without variance across seeds, making it impossible to assess result stability.

**Flagship Model Gap Persists**

While QwenLong-L1.5 approaches flagship performance on average (71.82 vs. Gemini-2.5-Pro's 72.40), Table 7 reveals benchmark-specific gaps:

| Benchmark | QwenLong-L1.5 | Gemini-2.5-Pro | Gap |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|-----|
| Frames | 74.76 | 74.51 | +0.25 |
| CorpusQA | 81.25 | 80.62 | +0.63 |
| MRCR | 82.99 | 79.92 | +3.07 |
| LongBench-V2 | 55.27 | 65.72 | **-10.45** |
| DocMath | 66.26 | 62.38 | +3.88 |
| LongBench-V1-QA | 70.40 | 71.28 | -0.88 |

The **-10.45 point gap on LongBench-V2** is substantial and unexplained. This benchmark tests "deeper understanding and reasoning on realistic long-context multitasks" (Bai et al., 2025). The gap suggests QwenLong-L1.5 may struggle with certain task types within this suite (e.g., code repository understanding or long structured data understanding). Appendix Table 11 shows the largest gain is on Medium length subsets (+10.23), but performance on Hard difficulty subsets lags (+5.49 vs. +7.30 on Easy).

**Generalization Boundaries Unclear**

Table 8 shows positive transfer to most general domains, but with exceptions:
- AIME24: -0.31 point decrease
- Memory-Rec_Sum (BFCL-V4): -1.94 point decrease

The paper does not analyze these failures. Understanding **when long-context training helps vs. hurts** is critical for deployment decisions. The UMAP projection in Figure 12 shows semantic separation between training and evaluation data, but does not explain why some tasks benefit while others degrade.

**Dependency on Base Model Capabilities**

QwenLong-L1.5 builds on Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking, which already has strong reasoning capabilities. The paper does not evaluate whether the same post-training recipe works on weaker base models. Key questions remain:
- Would a 7B or 14B parameter model achieve similar relative gains?
- Does the approach require a base model with existing long-context attention mechanisms?
- How much of the performance comes from the base model vs. the post-training?

Without ablation across base model scales, the generalizability of the recipe is uncertain.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes the key trade-offs practitioners must consider:

| Trade-off | Benefit | Cost |
|-----------|---------|------|
| **Synthetic Data Pipeline** | Scalable generation of multi-hop reasoning tasks (14.1K samples) | ~67% rejection rate; API/compute costs for verification |
| **Multi-Stage Training** | Resolves capability interference (Table 10: 71.82 vs. 68.53) | 4× training stages + model merging complexity |
| **AEPO Entropy Control** | Stabilizes long-context RL (+3.29 points, Table 5) | Adds hyperparameter tuning burden; thresholds undisclosed |
| **Memory Agent Framework** | Enables 1M–4M token reasoning (Table 9) | Sequential processing latency; error accumulation |
| **Task-Balanced Sampling** | Prevents distributional drift (+2.55 points, Table 2) | Requires pre-inference on all training data; stratification overhead |

These trade-offs reflect deliberate engineering choices rather than fundamental flaws. The paper's contribution lies in identifying and systematically addressing challenges that prior work overlooked. However, practitioners should weigh these costs against their specific deployment requirements—particularly for applications involving long-output generation, multi-modal content, or subjective evaluation criteria where the current approach has known limitations.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section synthesizes what QwenLong-L1.5 means for the broader field of long-context language models, identifies concrete research opportunities it opens, and provides practical guidance for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this approach.

### 7.1 Shifting the Research Paradigm: From Architecture to Post-Training

QwenLong-L1.5 represents a meaningful shift in how the field approaches long-context capability development. Prior work has predominantly focused on two paths:

1.  **Architectural Scaling**: Building models with larger native context windows through pre-training (e.g., extending from 8K to 128K tokens during initial training)
2.  **Attention Mechanism Innovation**: Developing sparse attention, linear attention, or retrieval-augmented architectures to reduce quadratic complexity

QwenLong-L1.5 demonstrates a **third path**: systematic post-training on existing architectures. The implications are substantial:

**Democratization of Long-Context Capability:**

The paper shows that a 30B parameter model can approach flagship performance (71.82 vs. Gemini-2.5-Pro's 72.40 average) through post-training alone. This matters because:

- **Pre-training context extension is prohibitively expensive**: Extending context from 8K to 128K tokens during pre-training increases attention complexity quadratically, requiring orders of magnitude more compute. Most research groups cannot afford this.
- **Post-training is accessible**: The QwenLong-L1.5 recipe can be applied to existing open-weight models. A research team with access to a 30B model and moderate RL training infrastructure can replicate this approach without retraining from scratch.

**Redefining What "Long-Context" Means:**

The paper distinguishes between two capabilities that are often conflated:

| Capability | What It Tests | QwenLong-L1.5 Performance |
|------------|---------------|---------------------------|
| **Long-Context Attention** | Can the model attend to tokens 100K positions away? | Native 128K window |
| **Long-Context Reasoning** | Can the model connect evidence separated by 100K tokens? | +31.72 on MRCR, +9.69 on CorpusQA |

Prior benchmarks like "needle-in-a-haystack" primarily test the first capability. QwenLong-L1.5's data synthesis pipeline explicitly targets the second, revealing that **attention range does not guarantee reasoning capability**. This distinction should reshape how future long-context benchmarks are designed.

**RL Stability as a First-Order Concern:**

The identification of entropy-gradient correlation (Spearman's $\rho = 0.96$ in Figure 9) elevates training stability from an engineering detail to a research question. Future long-context RL work should:

- Report entropy dynamics alongside performance metrics
- Analyze gradient norm distributions across sequence positions
- Consider entropy-based masking as a standard stabilization technique

### 7.2 Concrete Research Directions Enabled

The paper explicitly identifies several open problems in Section 7, but we can expand these into specific, actionable research agendas:

**Direction 1: Token-Level Credit Assignment**

The paper acknowledges that AEPO is "a robust stabilization mechanism rather than a fundamental solution to the credit assignment problem" (Section 7.2). Current GRPO assigns a uniform advantage $A_i$ to all tokens in a sequence:

$$J_{\text{GRPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{1}{\sum_{j=1}^G |y_j|} \sum_{i=1}^G A_i \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \rho_{i,t}(\theta)\right]$$

This treats all tokens equally, even though only a subset may have contributed to an error. Follow-up work could explore:

- **Temporal Difference Learning for Reasoning**: Assign credit based on how each token changes the probability of eventual correctness
- **Attention-Based Attribution**: Use attention weights to identify which tokens influenced the final answer
- **Counterfactual Token Masking**: Systematically mask tokens during rollouts to measure their causal impact on rewards

**Direction 2: Closed-Loop Data Flywheel**

Section 7.1 proposes leveraging trained models to generate their own training data. This is more than a cost-saving measure—it enables **curriculum learning at scale**:

```
Iteration 1: Base model → Synthetic data → Trained model
Iteration 2: Trained model → Harder synthetic data → Better model
Iteration 3: Better model → Even harder data → State-of-the-art
```

Key research questions:
- How to prevent mode collapse when models train on self-generated data?
- What verification mechanisms ensure self-generated data maintains quality?
- Can models identify their own weaknesses and generate targeted training examples?

**Direction 3: Multi-Modal Long-Context Reasoning**

The paper's data is "exclusively text-based" (Section 7.1), but real-world long documents often include:
- Financial reports with embedded charts and tables
- Scientific papers with figures and experimental results
- Code repositories with architecture diagrams

Extending the synthesis pipeline to multi-modal content requires:
- **Vision-language grounding checks**: Verify the model cannot answer without viewing figures
- **Cross-modal reasoning paths**: Questions requiring connections between text and visual elements
- **Multi-modal memory compression**: How to compress visual information into the 15K token memory representation

**Direction 4: Reward Models for Subjective Tasks**

The current reward mechanism uses "rule-based verification with LLM-as-a-judge" (Section 5.1), which works for tasks with clear correctness criteria. For subjective tasks (e.g., evaluating argument quality, assessing coherence), the paper proposes "LLM-based rubric reward models." Research priorities:

- **Rubric design**: What dimensions should rubrics measure for different task types?
- **Reward model training**: How to train reward models that generalize across domains?
- **Calibration**: Ensuring reward scores correlate with human preferences

**Direction 5: Understanding Benchmark Degradation**

The -3.00 point drop on Qasper (Table 12) and -0.31 on AIME24 (Table 8) are unexplained. Systematic analysis could reveal:
- Which question types within Qasper cause failures?
- Does degradation correlate with specific reasoning patterns in the synthetic data?
- Can targeted data augmentation recover lost performance?

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The capabilities demonstrated by QwenLong-L1.5 map directly to several high-value application domains:

**Legal Document Analysis:**

Legal contracts, briefs, and case law often span hundreds of pages with critical clauses dispersed throughout. QwenLong-L1.5's multi-hop reasoning capability enables:

- **Clause cross-referencing**: Connecting defined terms in Section 2 with obligations in Section 47
- **Precedent synthesis**: Aggregating relevant case citations across a 200-page brief
- **Risk identification**: Finding contradictions between representations and warranties

The +9.69 point gain on CorpusQA (which tests global reasoning over dispersed evidence) suggests the model can handle this type of cross-document synthesis.

**Financial Due Diligence:**

Corporate annual reports, SEC filings, and financial statements require integrating information across sections:

- **Revenue recognition analysis**: Connecting revenue figures in the income statement with accounting policy disclosures in the notes
- **Risk factor aggregation**: Synthesizing risk disclosures from multiple sections of a 10-K filing
- **Comparative analysis**: Extracting comparable metrics across multiple years of reports

The DocMath benchmark (+4.00 point gain) specifically tests numerical reasoning within financial reports, validating this use case.

**Scientific Literature Review:**

Researchers must synthesize findings across multiple papers, each with methods, results, and limitations in different sections:

- **Meta-analysis support**: Extracting effect sizes and methodologies from dozens of papers
- **Gap identification**: Finding contradictions or unexplored areas across a research domain
- **Citation network reasoning**: Understanding how claims propagate through the literature

The Frames benchmark (+4.49 point gain) tests reasoning over diverse Wikipedia topics, demonstrating cross-domain synthesis capability.

**Long-Form Dialogue Agents:**

The +15.60 point gain on LongMemEval (Table 8) directly validates conversational applications:

- **Customer support**: Maintaining context across multi-session support interactions
- **Therapeutic chatbots**: Remembering client history and progress over extended engagements
- **Personal assistants**: Tracking user preferences, commitments, and ongoing projects

**Code Repository Understanding:**

Large codebases contain interrelated functions, classes, and documentation spread across many files:

- **Bug localization**: Tracing error symptoms through call graphs to root causes
- **Feature implementation**: Understanding existing patterns before adding new functionality
- **Documentation synthesis**: Generating comprehensive documentation from dispersed code comments

LongBench-V2 includes a code repository understanding subset where QwenLong-L1.5 achieves +3.34 points on the Short subset and +2.87 on the Long subset (Table 11).

### 7.4 When to Use This Approach: Integration Guidance

Practitioners should consider QwenLong-L1.5's post-training recipe under the following conditions:

**Prefer This Approach When:**

| Scenario | Why This Method Fits |
|----------|---------------------|
| **You have an existing 30B+ model** | Post-training leverages existing investments; no need for pre-training |
| **Your tasks require multi-hop reasoning** | The synthesis pipeline specifically targets cross-evidence connections |
| **Context lengths exceed 32K tokens regularly** | Training data includes samples up to 120K tokens with memory extension to 4M |
| **You need both single-pass and memory-agent modes** | Multi-stage fusion preserves both capabilities (Table 10) |
| **Compute budget is moderate** | Post-training is cheaper than pre-training context extension |

**Consider Alternatives When:**

| Scenario | Better Alternative | Reason |
|----------|-------------------|--------|
| **Native 1M+ token context required** | Models with extended pre-training (e.g., Qwen-Flash-Thinking-1M) | Memory agent introduces sequential latency |
| **Real-time inference is critical** | Shorter context models with retrieval | Memory agent requires 125+ sequential passes for 4M tokens |
| **Multi-modal content dominates** | Vision-language models with long context | Current pipeline is text-only |
| **Subjective evaluation criteria** | Models with preference-tuned reward models | Current reward mechanism requires clear ground truth |
| **Base model is <10B parameters** | Uncertain—no ablation on smaller models | Recipe validated on 30B; smaller model performance unknown |

**Integration Checklist:**

For teams adopting this approach, the following prerequisites should be in place:

1.  **Base Model**: Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking or similar 30B+ reasoning model with existing long-context attention
2.  **RL Infrastructure**: VeRL or equivalent framework supporting GRPO with group size $G = 8$
3.  **Verification Pipeline**: Access to LLM-as-a-judge (e.g., gpt-oss-120b or self-hosted equivalent) for data verification
4.  **Compute Allocation**: Four training stages plus model merging; batch size 128, learning rate $2 \times 10^{-6}$
5.  **Evaluation Suite**: At minimum, MRCR and CorpusQA for long-context; one general benchmark (e.g., AIME) for catastrophic forgetting detection

**Expected Resource Requirements:**

While the paper does not report exact training hours, we can estimate based on disclosed hyperparameters:

- **Data synthesis**: 42.7K generated → 14.1K verified (67% rejection rate); requires LLM API calls for proposer, solver, verifier agents
- **Training stages**: 4 stages with increasing sequence lengths (20K → 60K → 120K → 128K input tokens)
- **Memory-RL**: Additional stage with 32K chunks, 15K memory representation
- **Model merging**: SCE algorithm overhead between Memory-RL and Stage 3

Teams should budget for **significantly more compute than standard supervised fine-tuning**, but **substantially less than pre-training context extension**.

### 7.5 Open Questions for the Community

Finally, we identify several questions that extend beyond this paper's scope but are critical for field advancement:

**Scalability Boundaries:**

- Does the post-training recipe scale to 70B+ models with similar relative gains?
- At what context length does memory agent performance become impractical (10M? 100M tokens)?
- How does performance scale with training data volume beyond 14.1K samples?

**Generalization Limits:**

- Why do some benchmarks degrade (Qasper -3.00, AIME24 -0.31) while others improve?
- Can we predict which tasks will benefit from long-context training before deployment?
- Does long-context training improve or harm few-shot learning capabilities?

**Efficiency Trade-offs:**

- Can the memory agent's sequential processing be parallelized without losing accuracy?
- What is the optimal chunk size for different document types (32K may not be universal)?
- Can we reduce the 67% data rejection rate while maintaining quality?

**Reproducibility:**

The paper does not disclose several critical hyperparameters ($H_{\text{low}}$, $H_{\text{high}}$, $\tau_{\text{token}}$, $\tau_{\text{sequence}}$). Community efforts to reproduce and extend this work should:

- Report full hyperparameter configurations
- Include variance across multiple training seeds
- Release synthesized training data (or at least detailed generation prompts)

Addressing these questions will determine whether QwenLong-L1.5's approach becomes a standard recipe for long-context post-training or remains a promising but incomplete demonstration. The paper's honest acknowledgment of limitations (Section 7) and detailed ablation studies (Tables 2, 4, 5, 10) provide a strong foundation for this collective effort.
