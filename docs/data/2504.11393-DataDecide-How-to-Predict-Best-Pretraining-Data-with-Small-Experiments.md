## 1. Executive Summary

To solve the high cost of validating pretraining data choices for large language models, this paper releases `DATADECIDE`, a suite of 1,050 models trained on 25 corpora across 14 scales up to 1B parameters to empirically test decision-making methods. The authors find that ranking models at a single small scale (e.g., 150M parameters) predicts 1B-scale winners with ~80% accuracy, and using continuous likelihood metrics like `CORRECT PROB` makes benchmarks including MMLU and ARC >80% predictable with just 0.01% of the target compute. This establishes the first extensive open baseline for the compute-to-decision accuracy tradeoff, showing that simple small-scale experiments often exceed the performance of 8 baseline scaling law methods.

## 2. Context and Motivation

The central challenge driving this research is the prohibitive cost of validating development decisions for large language models (LMs). Pretraining state-of-the-art models requires massive computational resources, often costing millions of dollars and consuming significant energy. Among the various hyperparameters and architectural choices, the composition of the **pretraining dataset** is one of the most impactful levers for improving model performance. However, because training a full-scale model on a new dataset is so expensive, researchers cannot simply try every option at the target scale to see which works best. This creates a fundamental economic barrier to optimizing data quality.

### The Validation Gap
To solve this, the community relies on **small-scale experiments**—training smaller models on candidate datasets to predict which will perform best at a larger scale. Common practices include training a single small model size (e.g., `$150M$` parameters) or fitting **scaling laws** (mathematical curves describing how performance improves with compute) to extrapolate future performance. 

However, a critical gap exists in validating whether these cheap methods actually work. To know if a small-scale experiment correctly identified the best dataset, you need to know the **counterfactual outcome**: you must train the *large* target model on *all* candidate datasets to see which one truly wins. Most prior work avoids this cost by either:
1.  Training only the single "winner" predicted by the small-scale experiment and showcasing its impressive performance (Section 1).
2.  Validating on a very small number of large models, measuring only the error magnitude in predicted performance scores rather than the correctness of the decision itself.

Without a suite of comparable large models trained on many different datasets, it is impossible to measure **decision accuracy**—the rate at which a method correctly ranks dataset A above dataset B. This leaves practitioners relying on heuristics without empirical proof that their cost-saving methods preserve model quality.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations
Two predominant strategies exist for making data decisions at small scale, each with specific shortcomings that `DATADECIDE` aims to address:

*   **Single-Scale Ranking:** This approach trains models at one fixed small size (e.g., `$150M$` parameters) and assumes the ranking holds at larger scales (e.g., `$1B$` parameters). While cheap, it relies on the assumption that performance trends do not cross over as scale increases. Prior work often validates this by releasing only the final large model, leaving the failure rate of the decision method unknown (Section 1).
*   **Scaling Law Extrapolation:** This method fits functions to performance observed across multiple small scales to predict large-scale performance. Originally developed for language modeling loss (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022), recent extensions attempt to predict downstream task accuracy (Gadre et al., 2024; Bhagia et al., 2024). However, these methods are typically validated on prediction error (e.g., `$|predicted - actual|$`), which does not directly measure whether the method would lead a practitioner to choose the wrong data (Section 2.3).

Furthermore, existing model suites used for scaling research lack the control necessary to isolate data effects. For example, the **Pythia Suite** (Biderman et al., 2023) compared only 2 data recipes, while subsequent suites offered up to 6 recipes (Magnusson et al., 2024; Brandfonbrener et al., 2024). Crucially, off-the-shelf models often vary simultaneously in data, optimizer settings, and architecture, making it difficult to attribute performance differences solely to the data (Section 1).

### Positioning of DATADECIDE
`DATADECIDE` positions itself as a controlled empirical benchmark to close this validation gap. Unlike prior suites, it provides:
*   **Scale and Diversity:** A suite of `$1,050$` models trained on `$25$` distinct data recipes (Table 1), an order of magnitude more than previous work. These recipes cover common interventions like source mixing, deduplication, and quality filtering.
*   **Controlled Variables:** All models share the same architecture family (OLMo) and token-to-parameter ratio (`$100$`, or `$5\times$` the Chinchilla optimal ratio), ensuring performance differences arise from data rather than modeling choices (Section 2.1).
*   **Decision-Centric Metrics:** Instead of focusing solely on prediction error magnitude, the paper introduces **decision accuracy** as the primary metric. This measures the percentage of dataset pairs where the prediction method correctly identifies the winner, directly aligning with the practitioner's goal of choosing the best data (Section 2.3).

By releasing the full suite of checkpoints and evaluations, `DATADECIDE` allows future research to test new scaling laws or prediction methods against a ground-truth ranking of `$1B$` parameter models, transforming data decision-making from a heuristic process into an empirically measurable science.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an empirical benchmarking paper that builds a controlled experimental suite to measure how well small-scale language model experiments predict which pretraining datasets will perform best at larger scales. The core idea is to train many models across many data recipes and scales, then systematically test different prediction methods against ground-truth large-scale rankings.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is `DATADECIDE`, a comprehensive collection of 1,050 pretrained language models spanning 25 different datasets, 14 model sizes, and 3 random seeds, designed to serve as a testbed for evaluating data selection methods. This solves the problem that practitioners cannot afford to train large models on every candidate dataset by providing empirical evidence about which cheap small-scale experiments actually identify the best datasets for large-scale training.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The `DATADECIDE` pipeline consists of four major components that flow sequentially: (1) **Data Recipe Construction** takes source corpora and applies interventions like filtering, deduplication, and mixing to create 25 distinct pretraining datasets; (2) **Model Training Ladder** trains 14 model configurations from 4M to 1B parameters on each recipe using controlled hyperparameters; (3) **Evaluation Suite** measures all models on 10 downstream benchmarks using both discrete accuracy and continuous likelihood metrics; (4) **Prediction Method Testing** applies different decision-making strategies (single-scale ranking, scaling law extrapolation) to small-scale results and compares their predictions against the ground-truth 1B-scale rankings. Information flows from data recipes through training to evaluation, then prediction methods consume the evaluation results to produce dataset rankings that are scored for decision accuracy.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- First, I explain the data recipe design because understanding what is being compared is prerequisite to understanding how predictions are made.
- Second, I detail the model training configuration since the token-to-parameter ratio and hyperparameter choices directly affect what scaling behaviors can be observed.

- Fifth, I describe the decision accuracy metric that serves as the ultimate measure of prediction method success.
- Finally, I discuss the compute budgeting framework that allows fair comparison between methods using different amounts of experimental compute.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**What type of paper and core idea:** This is an empirical benchmarking paper that constructs a controlled experimental suite to measure the accuracy of different methods for predicting which pretraining datasets will yield the best large language models, using small-scale experiments as proxies for expensive large-scale training runs.

**Data Recipe Construction (25 Corpora):** The foundation of `DATADECIDE` is 25 distinct pretraining datasets called "data recipes" that systematically vary common data preparation choices practitioners face. Table 1 shows these recipes span multiple source corpora including Dolma 1.7 (a 2.3 trillion token corpus sampling common language model sources), DCLM-Baseline (a state-of-the-art Common Crawl corpus), C4 (heuristically filtered from April 2019 Common Crawl), FineWeb-Pro and FineWeb-Edu (model-driven cleaned datasets), and Falcon RefinedWeb (aggressively filtered Common Crawl through June 2023). Beyond using different source datasets, the recipes apply interventions that isolate specific data quality decisions: domain ablation removes code, math, Reddit, or Flan subsets from Dolma; quality filtering selects top 7%, 10%, or 20% of documents using different classifier implementations; dataset mixing creates fractional combinations like "$\lambda\%$ DCLM-Baseline + $(1-\lambda)\%$ Dolma1.7" for $\lambda \in \{25\%, 50\%, 75\%\}$. This design ensures the suite captures realistic data selection decisions rather than artificial dataset variations, allowing findings to generalize to practical model development scenarios.

**Model Training Ladder (14 Scales, 1,050 Models):** The paper trains models across 14 parameter sizes ranging from 4M to 1B parameters, with specific configurations detailed in Table 2. Each model size has predetermined hyperparameters for batch size, hidden dimension, learning rate, number of attention heads, and number of layers, following OLMo's model ladder approach that uses heuristics from the literature to set these values programmatically. All models maintain a token-to-parameter ratio of 100, which equals 5× the "Chinchilla" optimal ratio from Hoffmann et al. (2022), capturing the typical overtraining regime that practitioners favor for inference efficiency savings. The total training produces 1,050 models calculated as 25 data recipes × 14 model scales × 3 random seeds. For the target 1B parameter models, all 3 random seed runs train to completion to establish ground-truth performance that accounts for run-to-run variance from weight initialization and data ordering. For non-target scales (4M through 750M parameters), the second and third seed runs terminate early after 25% of the target compute budget to avoid impractical prediction costs while still measuring variance. The paper notes that standard deviation between 1B runs can reach 2 percentage points of accuracy for some recipes on most tasks, making the 3-seed averaging essential for reliable ground-truth rankings.

**Evaluation Suite (10 OLMES Benchmarks):** All models are evaluated on the OLMES suite of 10 multiple-choice question answering benchmarks, which the authors selected because these tasks produce non-trivial performance across the model scales examined. The benchmarks include MMLU (massive multitask language understanding), HellaSwag (sentence completion), ARC Challenge and ARC Easy (science questions), PIQA (physical commonsense), CommonsenseQA, SocialIQA (social interactions), OpenBookQA, BoolQ (yes/no questions), and WinoGrande (pronoun resolution). The underlying metric for each task is accuracy, but OLMES specifies different length normalization schemes per task to handle varying answer lengths fairly. The paper uses the "cloze" formulation accuracy with curated normalization, referred to as `ACCURACY`, as the target metric that prediction methods aim to forecast. Unlike the standard OLMES protocol which subsamples benchmark items, this work uses all available items in each specified split to reduce variance over the task distribution. While the paper focuses on these multiple-choice evaluations, the validation methodology can apply to other benchmarks, and `DATADECIDE` could identify new evaluations sensitive within the 4M–1B parameter scale range.

**Proxy Metrics for Small-Scale Signal (5 Metrics):** Section 2.5 introduces five proxy metrics that serve as alternative evaluation signals at small scales, motivated by prior work showing discrete metrics like accuracy can cause performance jumps across scales while continuous metrics show more predictable improvements. Table 3 provides formal definitions for each metric computed over $N$ benchmark items where $C^{(i)}$ is the set of possible continuations for item $i$:

- `CORRECT PROB` measures the average probability the model assigns to the correct continuation: $\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} P(c^{(i)}_{\text{correct}} \mid \text{context}_i)$
- `MARGIN` measures the average difference between correct and best incorrect continuation probabilities: $\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left(P(c^{(i)}_{\text{correct}} \mid \text{context}_i) - \max_{c' \neq c^{(i)}_{\text{correct}} \in C^{(i)}} P(c' \mid \text{context}_i)\right)$
- `NORM CORRECT PROB` normalizes the correct probability by the sum of all continuation probabilities: $\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{P(c^{(i)}_{\text{correct}} \mid \text{context}_i)}{\sum_{c \in C^{(i)}} P(c \mid \text{context}_i)}$
- `TOTAL PROB` sums probabilities across all continuations (correct and incorrect): $\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{c \in C^{(i)}} P(c \mid \text{context}_i)$
- `ACCURACY` is the discrete fraction where the correct continuation has highest probability: $\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbb{I}\left(\arg\max_{c \in C^{(i)}} P(c \mid \text{context}_i) = c^{(i)}_{\text{correct}}\right)$

Each metric has character-normalized and token-normalized variants where likelihoods are divided by character count or token count respectively, with character normalization empirically optimal for most tasks. The key insight is that continuous metrics like `CORRECT PROB` and `TOTAL PROB` provide smoother signal at small scales where models may not yet confidently select the single highest-probability answer, enabling prediction of task rankings that would be trivial using discrete accuracy alone.

**Prediction Method 1 — Single-Scale Ranking:** The first prediction approach trains models at one fixed small parameter size and ranks data recipes by their downstream performance at that scale, assuming the ranking extrapolates to the target scale. This method is employed by prior work like Li et al. (2024) and holds constant all modeling variables except the data recipe. The winning data recipe by downstream accuracy or proxy metrics at the experimental scale is selected as the predicted winner for the target scale. This approach cannot correctly predict when scaling trends of two data recipes cross over between the small experimental scale and target scale, which bounds its maximum decision accuracy. However, the empirical results show relatively little crossover occurs in practice, making single-scale ranking a surprisingly strong baseline. Each prediction attempt uses models with different random seeds, and the reported accuracy averages over 3 prediction attempts to account for initialization variance.

**Prediction Method 2 — Multi-Scale Scaling Law Extrapolation:** The second approach fits mathematical scaling laws to performance observed across multiple small scales for each data recipe, then extrapolates to predict performance at the target scale. The paper follows a two-step method from Bhagia et al. (2024) that first maps compute to language modeling loss, then maps loss to downstream accuracy. Equation 1 models loss as a function of compute $C$:

$$L(C) = \frac{A}{C^\alpha} + E$$

where $A$ is a scaling coefficient, $\alpha$ is the scaling exponent, and $E$ is the irreducible loss floor. Equation 2 maps loss to accuracy using a logistic function:

$$\text{Acc}(L) = \frac{a}{1 + e^{-k(L-L_0)}} + b$$

where $a$ and $b$ set the accuracy range, $k$ controls the steepness, and $L_0$ is the loss value at the inflection point. The paper fits Equation 1 only on final, fully-trained checkpoints because accounting for learning rate schedule effects on intermediate checkpoints would require additional parameters and more observations. Equation 2 fits on all observations including intermediate checkpoints. The authors explore 8 scaling law variants detailed in Appendix C: a baseline 3-parameter fit, a restricted 2-parameter fit removing $E$, a 5-parameter fit using parameters $N$ and $D$ directly instead of compute $C$, single-step variants that map compute directly to accuracy, and combinations using helper points or filtering early checkpoints. Table 4 shows prediction errors for these variants, with the simpler 3-parameter setup performing comparably to more complex variants.

**Decision Accuracy Metric (Primary Evaluation):** Unlike prior work that measures prediction error magnitude, this paper introduces decision accuracy as the primary metric because practitioners ultimately need to choose which dataset is better, not predict exact performance values. Decision accuracy measures the percentage of all pairs of data recipes $(A, B)$ where the prediction method correctly identifies which achieves higher performance at the target scale. Formally, let $P$ be the set of all data recipe pairs with observed mean performance $y_A, y_B$ and predicted performance $\hat{y}_A, \hat{y}_B$, then decision accuracy is:

$$\frac{1}{|P|} \sum_{(A,B) \in P} \mathbb{I}\left(\text{sign}(\hat{y}_A - \hat{y}_B) = \text{sign}(y_A - y_B)\right)$$

This metric ranges from 0 to 1 and is nearly equivalent to Kendall's $\tau$ correlation coefficient. The target-scale winner is determined by mean downstream performance averaged over 3 random seeds at 1B parameters, accounting for run-to-run variance. This metric directly captures whether a prediction method would lead a practitioner to make the correct data selection decision, which is the ultimate practical goal.

**Compute Budgeting Framework (%C):** To fairly compare prediction methods that use different amounts of experimental compute, the paper measures efficiency as the proportion of target compute budget consumed. Compute is measured in theoretical FLOPs using the simplifying assumption from scaling literature that training cost is captured by $\text{FLOPs} = 6ND$, based solely on number of parameters $N$ and tokens trained $D$. The efficiency metric is:

$$\%C = \frac{c}{C} \times 100\%$$

where $c$ is the experimental budget and $C$ is the target budget in FLOPs. This allows plotting decision accuracy against compute cost to identify the compute-to-decision-accuracy frontier. For single-scale predictions, each model size represents a different point on this frontier. For multi-scale scaling laws, the compute budget equals the sum of training costs for all model sizes used in the fit. The paper finds that intermediate checkpoints from a given parameter size provide decision accuracy equivalent to compute-matched final checkpoints from smaller models, giving practitioners flexibility in how they allocate their experimental budget.

**Design Choices and Rationale:** Several critical design choices enable the clean empirical comparisons in this work. First, fixing the token-to-parameter ratio at 100 across all scales eliminates confounding from different training regimes, though this limits generalization to other ratios. Second, using 3 random seeds for 1B models but early-terminating seeds for smaller models balances the need to measure ground-truth variance against the cost of running full prediction experiments. Third, evaluating on multiple-choice tasks with cloze formulation ensures tasks are appropriate for the 4M–1B parameter scale range, though different tasks would be needed for larger target scales. Fourth, releasing all 1,050 checkpoints enables future research to test new prediction methods without retraining, extending the utility of the compute investment. The choice to focus on decision accuracy rather than prediction error magnitude reflects the practical reality that practitioners need to select the best dataset, not predict exact performance numbers. The inclusion of 25 data recipes—an order of magnitude more than prior suites—provides sufficient statistical power to measure decision accuracy reliably across many pairwise comparisons.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the most significant contributions of `DATADECIDE` into actionable insights and methodological innovations. While the previous section detailed *how* the suite was constructed, this section explains *what we learned* from it and why these findings shift the standard practice for efficient model development. We distinguish between empirical surprises (insights) and new tools or frameworks (innovations) that enable future work.

### 4.1. Conceptual Innovation: Decision Accuracy Over Prediction Error

Prior scaling law research typically optimizes for **prediction error**—minimizing the difference between predicted and actual performance scores (e.g., $|predicted - actual|$). `DATADECIDE` introduces a fundamental shift by prioritizing **decision accuracy**, which measures whether a method correctly ranks one dataset above another (Section 2.3).

*   **Why this matters:** A method can have low prediction error but still make the wrong decision if the performance gap between two datasets is smaller than the error margin. Conversely, a method with higher error might still correctly identify the winner if the signal direction is preserved.
*   **The Shift:** By adopting decision accuracy as the primary metric, the paper aligns evaluation with the practitioner's actual goal: choosing the best data, not forecasting exact scores. This reveals that methods previously considered "accurate" based on error metrics might fail at the actual task of data selection.
*   **Significance:** This conceptual reframing allows for the discovery that simpler methods (like single-scale ranking) are sufficient for decision-making, even if complex scaling laws offer slightly better magnitude predictions. It establishes a new standard for validating scaling laws in practical development pipelines.

### 4.2. Empirical Insight: Single-Scale Ranking Dominates Scaling Laws

One of the most counter-intuitive findings is that complex multi-scale extrapolation does not outperform simple single-scale ranking for data decisions.

*   **The Result:** As shown in **Figure 3**, 8 baseline scaling law variants (including 2-parameter, 3-parameter, and 5-parameter fits) fail to exceed the **compute-decision frontier** set by ranking models at a single small scale (e.g., 150M parameters). The single-scale baseline achieves ~80% decision accuracy against 1B targets.
*   **Why this happens:** The paper notes that performance trends between data recipes rarely "crossover" between the small experimental scale and the 1B target scale in this regime. Because the ranking remains stable, the added complexity of fitting scaling laws provides diminishing returns for the extra compute cost.
*   **Significance:** This challenges the assumption that extrapolation is always necessary for efficient development. It suggests that for data selection specifically, practitioners can save significant compute by running fewer, larger small-scale experiments rather than many tiny ones for curve fitting. However, the authors note this establishes a baseline for *future* scaling laws to beat, rather than proving scaling laws are inherently useless (Section 3.2).

### 4.3. Methodological Innovation: Continuous Proxies Unlock Hard Tasks

The paper demonstrates that changing the evaluation metric at small scales—from discrete accuracy to continuous likelihoods—enables prediction for tasks that were previously unpredictable.

*   **The Capability:** For code generation tasks like `MBPP` and `HumanEval`, small models (4M–60M) typically achieve near-zero discrete accuracy, making ranking impossible. However, by using continuous proxies like `CORRECT PROB` (average probability of the correct continuation), decision accuracy jumps from trivial to **>80%** (Figure 6).
*   **The Mechanism:** Small models often assign higher likelihood to correct code snippets even when they cannot generate the exact correct token sequence to pass a test case. Discrete accuracy treats a 49% probability and a 1% probability identically (both are wrong), whereas continuous metrics capture this nuanced signal.
*   **Significance:** This innovation expands the range of benchmarks usable for early-stage decision making. It allows practitioners to validate data choices for specialized capabilities (like coding) using tiny models, reducing the compute required for these decisions by orders of magnitude (down to **0.01%** of target compute for some tasks, as noted in the Abstract).

### 4.4. Diagnostic Insight: Predictability is Driven by Noise and Spread

Beyond specific results, `DATADECIDE` provides a diagnostic framework for understanding *why* some tasks are easier to predict than others.

*   **The Framework:** **Figure 5** plots tasks based on two characteristics: **noise** (standard deviation over random seed runs) and **spread** (standard deviation of performance across different data recipes).
*   **The Insight:** High decision accuracy correlates with **low noise** and **wide spread**.
    *   Tasks like `MMLU` are predictable primarily because they have low run-to-run variance (low noise).
    *   Tasks like `ARC Easy` are predictable because different data recipes produce widely separated performance scores (wide spread).
    *   Tasks like `SocialIQA` are difficult because they suffer from high noise and low spread.
*   **Significance:** This explains the variability seen in **Figure 2**, where `ARC Easy` is predictable with 5 orders of magnitude less compute than `HellaSwag`. It provides a heuristic for practitioners: if a benchmark has high variance or clusters data recipes tightly together, it is a poor candidate for small-scale decision making, regardless of the prediction method used. Furthermore, it shows that proxy metrics like `CORRECT PROB` often improve predictability by simultaneously reducing noise and increasing spread (Section 3.4).

| Contribution | Type | Key Finding | Impact |

| **Decision Accuracy Metric** | Conceptual | Ranking correctness matters more than error magnitude. | Aligns evaluation with practical goals; reveals simplicity of single-scale methods. |

These contributions collectively transform data decision-making from a heuristic process into an empirically measurable science. By releasing the full suite of 1,050 models, `DATADECIDE` ensures these insights are not just theoretical claims but reproducible baselines for future research in efficient model development.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a detailed examination of the experimental methodology, quantitative results, and critical assessment of whether the evidence supports the paper's central claims. While Section 3 described the technical construction of `DATADECIDE` and Section 4 summarized the key insights, this section digs into the specific numbers, experimental conditions, and limitations that determine how much confidence practitioners should place in these findings.

### 5.1. Evaluation Methodology and Experimental Setup

**Dataset Coverage and Control:** The experiments span 25 data recipes (Table 1) that systematically vary across four dimensions of data preparation decisions practitioners actually face:

| Intervention Type | Examples from Table 1 |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Source Selection** | Dolma 1.7, DCLM-Baseline, C4, FineWeb-Pro, Falcon RefinedWeb |
| **Domain Ablation** | Dolma without code, without math/code, without Reddit, without Flan |
| **Quality Filtering** | Top 7%, 10%, 20% using different classifier implementations (DCLM, FineWeb-edu, Tulu-v3) |
| **Dataset Mixing** | $\lambda\%$ DCLM-Baseline + $(1-\lambda)\%$ Dolma1.7 for $\lambda \in \{25\%, 50\%, 75\%\}$ |

This coverage is an order of magnitude larger than prior suites (Pythia: 2 recipes, Paloma: 6 recipes), providing sufficient pairwise comparisons to measure decision accuracy with statistical confidence. All models share the same architecture family (OLMo) and token-to-parameter ratio of 100, which equals $5\times$ the Chinchilla optimal ratio from Hoffmann et al. (2022). This fixed ratio captures the typical overtraining regime practitioners favor for inference efficiency, but it also limits generalization to other training regimes (discussed in Section 5.4).

**Model Scale Grid:** Table 2 details the 14 model configurations ranging from 4M to 1B parameters. The compute budget for each scale varies from 0.4B tokens (4M model) to 100B tokens (1B model), representing a 250× range in training data. The total experimental suite comprises 1,050 models (25 recipes × 14 scales × 3 seeds), requiring approximately 820K H100 GPU hours according to the Impact Statement.

**Ground Truth Establishment:** A critical methodological choice is how the paper establishes "correct" decisions at the target scale. For 1B parameter models, all 3 random seed runs train to completion, and the ground-truth ranking uses the mean performance across these 3 seeds. The paper reports that standard deviation between 1B runs can reach 2 percentage points of accuracy for some recipes on most tasks (Section 2.1). This variance is non-trivial: if two datasets differ by less than 2% in mean performance, their ranking could flip due to initialization noise rather than true data quality differences. The 3-seed averaging mitigates but does not eliminate this concern.

**Prediction Method Implementation:** The paper tests two broad categories of prediction methods:

1.  **Single-Scale Ranking:** Uses models at one fixed parameter size (e.g., 150M) to rank datasets. Each point in Figure 1 (right) and Figure 2 represents the average decision accuracy over 3 prediction attempts using different random seeds at that scale.

2.  **Multi-Scale Scaling Laws:** Fits mathematical functions to performance across multiple small scales. The paper explores 8 variants (Appendix C), with the baseline 3-parameter fit following Bhagia et al. (2024):
    - Step 1: $L(C) = \frac{A}{C^\alpha} + E$ (compute to loss)
    - Step 2: $\text{Acc}(L) = \frac{a}{1 + e^{-k(L-L_0)}} + b$ (loss to accuracy)
    
    Unlike single-scale predictions, scaling law fits use only one prediction attempt with the default fully-trained random seed, because final checkpoints are required for fitting Step 1 but are not available for all seeds at non-target scales (Section 3.2).

**Compute Measurement:** All compute costs are measured in theoretical FLOPs using the standard scaling literature assumption: $\text{FLOPs} = 6ND$, where $N$ is parameters and $D$ is tokens trained (Kaplan et al., 2020). The efficiency metric $\%C = \frac{c}{C} \times 100\%$ expresses experimental budget as a proportion of the target 1B model budget. This allows direct comparison between methods using different model sizes and training durations.

### 5.2. Quantitative Results: Decision Accuracy vs. Compute

**Overall Compute-Decision Frontier (Figure 1, right):** The aggregate results across all 10 OLMES tasks show a positive, roughly log-linear relationship between experimental compute and decision accuracy. Key observations:

- At approximately $10^{-2}\%C$ (around 14M–20M parameter models), decision accuracy reaches 60–70%
- At approximately $1\%C$ (150M parameter models), decision accuracy reaches ~80%
- The shading in Figure 1 indicates standard deviation over 3 seed runs, showing that variance decreases as compute increases

This establishes the baseline: practitioners can achieve 80% correct dataset rankings using only 1% of the target compute budget through single-scale experiments at 150M parameters.

**Per-Task Predictability Varies Dramatically (Figure 2):** The 10 OLMES benchmarks show vastly different predictability profiles:

| Task | Predictability Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- |
| **ARC Easy** | Predictable with 5 orders of magnitude less compute than HellaSwag; reaches high decision accuracy at smallest scales |
| **MMLU** | Consistently predictable with low compute; benefits from low run-to-run variance |
| **HellaSwag** | Requires substantially more compute; shows distinct periods of insensitivity before log-linear increase |
| **SocialIQA** | Difficult to predict at all scales; remains near trivial decision accuracy |
| **BoolQ** | Only reaches beyond trivial decision accuracy for intermediate checkpoints of the target 1B runs |
| **WinoGrande** | Similar to HellaSwag; requires crossing a compute threshold before predictions improve |

This variation is critical: the Abstract's claim of ">80% predictable with 0.01% compute" applies to specific tasks (MMLU, ARC) but not universally. Practitioners must select benchmarks appropriate to their target capabilities.

**Scaling Law Variants Underperform (Figure 3, Table 4):** The paper tests 8 scaling law variants against the single-scale baseline:

| Scaling Law Variant | Relative Error (%) | Absolute Error (%) | Decision Accuracy Performance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 3-parameter with helpers and >50% checkpoints | 5.6 | 2.6 | Among top performers |
| 3-parameter with helper points | 6.0 | 2.8 | Among top performers |
| 3-parameter step 2 fit with >50% checkpoints | 5.9 | 2.9 | Among top performers |
| 3-parameter (baseline) | 6.5 | 3.1 | Among top performers |
| 2-parameter | 6.5 | 3.2 | Among top performers |
| 5-parameter, single step | 42.8 | 17.4 | Poor |
| 3-parameter, single step | 42.9 | 42.3 | Poor |
| 5-parameter (N, D) fit | 230.8 | 65.4 | Worst |

Table 4 shows that simpler variants (2-parameter, 3-parameter) achieve comparable prediction error to more complex variants. However, **Figure 3 demonstrates that none of the 8 scaling law variants exceed the compute-decision frontier set by single-scale ranking**. The stars in Figure 3 (representing scaling law predictions) fall on or below the curve established by single-scale experiments (colored lines).

This is a critical negative result: the added complexity of fitting scaling laws does not translate to better data selection decisions, despite achieving reasonable prediction error magnitudes.

**Proxy Metrics Enable Hard Task Prediction (Figure 4, Figure 6):** The paper's most significant positive finding concerns continuous proxy metrics:

- **Figure 4** shows per-task decision accuracy using 5 different proxy metrics. For 5 tasks (ARC Challenge, ARC Easy, HellaSwag, OpenBookQA, MMLU), `CORRECT PROB` and `TOTAL PROB` achieve decision accuracy at least as good as discrete `ACCURACY` at small scales.
- **Figure 6** demonstrates the dramatic impact on code tasks:
  - `MBPP` and `HumanEval` with `ACCURACY`: Decision accuracy remains near 0% (trivial) at 4M and 60M scales
  - `MBPP` and `HumanEval` with `CORRECT PROB`: Decision accuracy jumps to ~80% at 60M scale
  
This represents a 100×+ reduction in required compute for code capability validation. However, math benchmarks (`Minerva`, `GSM8K`) do not see the same benefit from proxy metrics when predicting `ACCURACY` targets. The paper notes they achieve >80% decision accuracy only if the *target metric* is also switched to `CORRECT PROB`, raising questions about whether this is justified for downstream use cases (Section 3.4).

**Noise-Spread Diagnostic (Figure 5):** Figure 5 plots tasks at 150M scale along two axes:
- **Noise:** Standard deviation over 3 random seed runs, averaged over all recipes
- **Spread:** Standard deviation among mean performance of different data recipes

Key observations from the scatter plot:
- `MMLU`: Low noise (~$10^{-2}$), moderate spread → high decision accuracy
- `ARC Easy`: Moderate noise, high spread (~$10^{-1}$) → high decision accuracy
- `SocialIQA`: High noise, low spread → low decision accuracy
- `HellaSwag`: Moderate noise, moderate spread → moderate decision accuracy

The paper reports that using `CORRECT PROB` often improves predictability by simultaneously reducing noise and increasing spread (Section 3.4). This provides an actionable diagnostic: if a benchmark clusters data recipes tightly (low spread) or shows high run-to-run variance (high noise), it is unsuitable for small-scale decision making regardless of the prediction method.

### 5.3. Critical Assessment: Do Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: "Single-scale ranking at 150M predicts 1B winners with ~80% accuracy"**

**Support Level: Strong.** Figure 1 (right) and Figure 2 provide direct empirical evidence. The 80% figure is an aggregate across 10 tasks; individual tasks range from ~50% (SocialIQA) to ~95% (ARC Easy). The 3-seed averaging for 1B ground truth and 3-seed averaging for predictions provides reasonable confidence that results are not due to initialization variance. However, the 2% standard deviation between 1B runs means some "incorrect" predictions may reflect noise in the ground truth rather than prediction method failure.

**Claim 2: "8 baseline scaling law methods do not exceed single-scale frontier"**

**Support Level: Strong, with caveats.** Figure 3 clearly shows scaling law variants (stars) do not exceed the single-scale curve. Table 4 confirms prediction errors are comparable or worse for scaling laws. However, the paper acknowledges this tests only 8 baseline variants following Bhagia et al. (2024). The authors explicitly state: "Improved future scaling laws may be able to advance the Pareto frontier on DATADECIDE" (Section 3.2). This is a measured claim—the experiments show current baseline scaling laws do not help, not that scaling laws are inherently incapable of helping.

**Claim 3: "Continuous likelihood metrics make benchmarks >80% predictable with 0.01% compute"**

**Support Level: Conditional.** The Abstract specifies "benchmarks including MMLU, ARC, HellaSwag, MBPP, and HumanEval." Figure 2 shows MMLU and ARC Easy achieve high decision accuracy at very low compute. Figure 6 shows MBPP and HumanEval achieve ~80% with `CORRECT PROB` at 60M scale (approximately 0.01% of 1B compute). However, Figure 4 shows this does not hold for all tasks—SocialIQA and BoolQ remain difficult even with proxy metrics. The claim is accurate for the specified tasks but should not be generalized to all benchmarks.

**Claim 4: "Predictability is driven by low noise and wide spread"**

**Support Level: Moderate.** Figure 5 provides correlational evidence supporting this framework. Tasks with high decision accuracy cluster in the low-noise or high-spread regions. The paper shows `CORRECT PROB` often improves these characteristics. However, this is an observational finding from one scale (150M) and one metric comparison. The paper does not provide causal evidence that manipulating noise or spread directly causes predictability changes, though the correlation is strong and theoretically grounded.

### 5.4. Ablation Studies, Failure Cases, and Robustness Checks

**Ablation: Scaling Law Variants (Appendix C, Table 4):** The paper systematically ablates scaling law design choices:
- Removing the irreducible loss term $E$ (2-parameter vs. 3-parameter): Minimal impact on decision accuracy
- Using helper points to anchor accuracy asymptote: Slight improvement in prediction error (5.6% vs. 6.5% relative error)
- Filtering early checkpoints (>50% only): Comparable performance to using all checkpoints
- Single-step vs. two-step fitting: Dramatically worse performance (42.8% vs. 6.5% relative error)
- Using $N$ and $D$ separately vs. compute $C$: Worst performance (230.8% relative error for 5-parameter)

These ablations justify the paper's choice of the simple 3-parameter baseline as the representative scaling law method.

**Failure Case: SocialIQA and BoolQ:** Two tasks consistently underperform across all prediction methods and scales. SocialIQA remains near trivial decision accuracy even at high compute (Figure 2). BoolQ only exceeds trivial accuracy for intermediate checkpoints of the target 1B runs. The paper attributes this to high noise and low spread (Figure 5). This is an important limitation: not all benchmarks are suitable for small-scale decision making, and practitioners should validate benchmark predictability before relying on it for data selection.

**Failure Case: Math Benchmarks:** Minerva and GSM8K do not benefit from `CORRECT PROB` when predicting `ACCURACY` targets (Figure 6). The paper notes they achieve >80% decision accuracy only if the target metric is also switched to `CORRECT PROB`. This raises an unresolved question: if the downstream use case requires discrete accuracy (e.g., passing math problems), is it valid to optimize for continuous likelihood proxies? The paper flags this for future work (Section 3.4).

**Robustness Check: Random Seed Variance:** The paper's inclusion of 3 random seeds for all 1B models is a significant robustness measure absent from prior work. The finding that standard deviation can reach 2% accuracy points means that dataset pairs differing by less than this threshold cannot be reliably ranked. This sets a fundamental limit on decision accuracy: if 20% of dataset pairs differ by less than 2%, the maximum achievable decision accuracy is 80% regardless of prediction method quality.

**Robustness Check: Intermediate vs. Final Checkpoints:** Section 3.1 states: "Decisions from intermediate checkpoints are as good as compute equivalent final checkpoints." This means practitioners can choose between training smaller models to completion or training larger models for fewer steps, providing flexibility in experimental design. The paper does not provide detailed numbers for this comparison in the main text, which is a minor gap.

### 5.5. Conditions and Trade-offs

**Token-to-Parameter Ratio Limitation:** All experiments use a fixed ratio of 100 (5× Chinchilla). Section 5 (Limitations) acknowledges this captures typical overtraining for inference savings but may not generalize to other regimes. If a practitioner targets a different ratio (e.g., 20× for maximum performance, or 1× for compute efficiency), the decision accuracy findings may not hold. This is a significant constraint on external validity.

**Scale Range Limitation:** The target scale is 1B parameters. While this is 250× larger than the smallest models (4M), it is still far smaller than frontier models (100B+). The paper acknowledges: "one would have to select different tasks when targeting a larger scale" (Section 2.4). Scaling behaviors observed at 1B may not extrapolate to 100B, particularly for capabilities that emerge at larger scales.

**Task Selection Limitation:** The 10 OLMES benchmarks are all multiple-choice tasks with cloze formulation. This is appropriate for the 4M–1B scale range but excludes generation tasks, retrieval tasks, or instruction-following evaluations. The paper states the methodology can apply to other benchmarks, but the specific decision accuracy numbers are task-dependent.

**Compute Investment Trade-off:** The 820K H100 GPU hours represents a massive investment that most research groups cannot replicate. The value of `DATADECIDE` is that it releases all checkpoints, allowing others to test new prediction methods without retraining. However, this also means the suite is fixed: new data recipes, model architectures, or evaluation benchmarks require new experiments. The paper invites future research to extend the suite (Section 1), but this depends on community adoption and compute availability.

**Prediction Method Selection Trade-off:** The results suggest single-scale ranking at 150M is sufficient for most data decisions. However, this conclusion holds for the specific regime tested (100 token-parameter ratio, 1B target, OLMES benchmarks). If scaling law methods improve in future work, or if practitioners target different scales or tasks, multi-scale extrapolation may become advantageous. The paper positions `DATADECIDE` as a benchmark for measuring such improvements, not as a final verdict on scaling laws.

### 5.6. Summary Assessment

The experiments provide strong empirical support for the paper's core claims within the tested regime. The 1,050-model suite with 3-seed averaging provides unprecedented statistical power for measuring decision accuracy. The negative result on scaling laws is particularly valuable: it prevents practitioners from investing in complex extrapolation methods that do not improve decisions. The positive result on continuous proxies opens new possibilities for early-stage capability validation.

However, the findings are conditional on the specific experimental design (100 token-parameter ratio, 1B target, multiple-choice tasks). Practitioners should verify these results hold for their specific use cases before adopting the recommendations. The release of all checkpoints and evaluation results enables such verification without requiring the full 820K GPU hour investment, which is the paper's most significant contribution to the research community.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While `DATADECIDE` provides unprecedented empirical grounding for data decision-making, several important limitations constrain how broadly its findings generalize. This section examines the assumptions underlying the approach, scenarios not addressed, computational constraints, and open questions that remain for future research.

### 6.1. Core Assumptions and Their Validity

**Fixed Token-to-Parameter Ratio:** All experiments use a token-to-parameter ratio of `$100$`, which equals `$5\times$` the Chinchilla optimal ratio from Hoffmann et al. (2022). Section 5 (Limitations) explicitly acknowledges this constraint:

> "The scope of our work is limited to just one ratio of tokens to parameters, 100 or 5× 'Chinchilla' optimal ratio. We believe this captures the typical case, as most models now favor overtraining for inference savings."

This assumption has significant implications:
- **Training Regime Specificity:** The compute-decision frontier established in Figure 1 may shift for models trained at different ratios. A model trained at `$20\times$` Chinchilla (maximum performance regime) or `$1\times$` Chinchilla (compute-efficient regime) could exhibit different scaling behaviors that affect prediction accuracy.
- **Overtraining Bias:** The `$5\times$` ratio captures overtraining favored for inference efficiency, but this may not reflect research settings where maximum capability is prioritized over deployment efficiency.
- **Unverified Generalization:** The paper provides no empirical evidence that single-scale ranking remains dominant at other token-to-parameter ratios. This is a critical gap for practitioners targeting non-standard training regimes.

**Stable Ranking Assumption:** Single-scale ranking's success depends on performance trends between data recipes not crossing over between small and target scales. Section 3.2 notes:

> "A priori we know that ranking single scale experiments cannot correctly predict when the scaling trend of one data recipe overtakes another at scales between our small experiments and target scale. Such crossovers bound the decision accuracy of this constant approximation of performance."

The paper observes relatively little crossover in the `$4M$`–`$1B$` regime, but this may not hold:
- **Larger Scale Gaps:** Crossing from `$150M$` to `$100B$` parameters (a `$667\times$` scale increase) could introduce crossovers not observed in the `$150M$` to `$1B$` (`$6.7\times$`) gap tested here.
- **Capability Emergence:** Some capabilities emerge only at larger scales. Data recipes that appear equivalent at `$1B$` may diverge significantly at `$100B$` when new capabilities activate.

**Multiple-Choice Task Assumption:** All 10 OLMES benchmarks use multiple-choice questions with cloze formulation. Section 2.4 states:

> "We chose these tasks based on their appropriateness to our range of model scales, and one would have to select different tasks when targeting a larger scale."

This limits applicability to:
- **Generation Tasks:** Code generation, summarization, or creative writing tasks where discrete accuracy metrics differ fundamentally from multiple-choice accuracy.
- **Instruction-Following:** Chat-based evaluations that require different evaluation protocols than cloze-formulation benchmarks.
- **Retrieval-Augmented Tasks:** Settings where model performance depends on external knowledge retrieval rather than parametric knowledge alone.

### 6.2. Scenarios and Edge Cases Not Addressed

**Frontier Model Scales:** The target scale of `$1B$` parameters is `$100\times$` smaller than many production models and `$1000\times$` smaller than frontier models. Several concerns arise:

- **Emergent Capabilities:** Section 2.4 acknowledges "one would have to select different tasks when targeting a larger scale." Capabilities that emerge abruptly at larger scales (Schaeffer et al., 2023) may not follow the smooth scaling trends observed in `DATADECIDE`.
- **Different Bottlenecks:** At `$100B+$` scales, data quality bottlenecks may differ from those at `$1B$`. Filtering strategies optimal for `$1B$` models may not transfer to frontier scales.
- **Unverified Extrapolation:** The paper provides no evidence that the `$80\%$` decision accuracy at `$1B$` translates to similar accuracy when predicting `$100B$` model performance from `$1B$` experiments.

**Domain-Specific Data Decisions:** The 25 data recipes focus on general web corpora with interventions like filtering, deduplication, and mixing. Several important data decision scenarios are not covered:

- **Specialized Domains:** Medical, legal, or scientific domain adaptation decisions are not represented in the recipe set.
- **Multilingual Data:** All benchmarks and recipes appear to focus on English-language performance. Data mixing decisions for multilingual models are not addressed.
- **Temporal Data:** The impact of data recency (e.g., including 2024 vs. 2020 web crawls) on model performance is not studied.
- **Synthetic Data:** The growing use of synthetic or model-generated training data is not represented in the recipe set.

**Architecture Variations:** All models use the OLMo architecture family with fixed architectural hyperparameters per scale (Table 2). This excludes:

- **Architecture Families:** Findings may not transfer to different architecture families (e.g., Mixture of Experts, recurrent architectures, or state-space models).
- **Architectural Hyperparameters:** The impact of varying attention heads, layer counts, or MLP ratios independently of parameter count is not studied.
- **Optimizer Choices:** All models use the same optimizer settings. Different optimizers could interact with data recipes in ways that affect predictability.

**Math Benchmark Anomaly:** Figure 6 reveals an unresolved edge case with math benchmarks:

> "Notably, two math benchmarks (Lewkowycz et al., 2022; Cobbe et al., 2021) do not see such a benefit. They do however give decision accuracy above 80% if we switch the target metric to CORRECT PROB, raising a question for future work to explore whether changing the target metric can be justified."

This raises a fundamental question: if the downstream use case requires discrete accuracy (solving math problems correctly), is it valid to optimize for continuous likelihood proxies during data selection? The paper flags this as an open question without resolution.

### 6.3. Computational and Scalability Constraints

**Non-Replicable Compute Investment:** The Impact Statement reports:

> "The pretraining experiments in our DATADECIDE required approximately 820K H100 GPU hours."

This creates several constraints:
- **Barrier to Extension:** Most research groups cannot afford to extend `DATADECIDE` with new data recipes, model scales, or evaluation benchmarks. The suite is effectively fixed unless major institutional compute becomes available.
- **Single-Point Validation:** The findings rest on one massive compute investment. Independent validation by other groups is computationally prohibitive.
- **Temporal Limitations:** The compute was available "over a long period of time in which compute became available for pretraining" (Section 5). Future compute availability may not support similar-scale experiments.

**Fixed Model Configuration Grid:** Section 5 (Limitations) states:

> "Due to compute limitations and the need for a standardized set of model configurations over a long period of time in which compute became available for pretraining, we opt for 14 specific configurations from 4M–1B parameter scale."

This creates gaps:
- **Scale Resolution:** The 14 configurations may miss important scaling behaviors between tested points. For example, no models exist between `$750M$` and `$1B$` parameters.
- **Early Stopping for Non-Target Scales:** Section 2.1 notes that for non-target scales, "second and third seed runs are terminated early after 25% of the target compute budget." This limits variance estimation at smaller scales.
- **No Ablation on Architecture:** The paper cannot test whether different architectural choices at the same parameter count would change decision accuracy findings.

**Scaling Law Variant Limitations:** Only 8 baseline scaling law variants are tested (Appendix C, Table 4). Section 3.2 acknowledges:

> "Improved future scaling laws may be able to advance the Pareto frontier on DATADECIDE as they are not bound by crossovers."

This means:
- **Method Under-Exploration:** The negative result on scaling laws applies only to the 8 tested variants. More sophisticated methods (e.g., incorporating architecture-aware parameters, task-specific scaling exponents, or Bayesian uncertainty) are not evaluated.
- **Baseline, Not Ceiling:** The single-scale frontier represents a baseline that future methods should exceed, not a proven upper bound on prediction efficiency.
- **Single-Step Failure:** Table 4 shows single-step variants perform dramatically worse (`$42.8\%$` vs. `$6.5\%$` relative error for 3-parameter). This suggests the two-step approach (compute → loss → accuracy) is important, but the paper does not deeply analyze why single-step methods fail.

### 6.4. Measurement and Evaluation Weaknesses

**Ground Truth Variance:** Section 2.1 reports:

> "We find that the standard deviation between runs at the 1B 5×C scale can be as high as 2% points of accuracy for some recipes on most tasks."

This creates a fundamental ceiling:
- **Maximum Achievable Accuracy:** If `$20\%$` of dataset pairs differ by less than `$2\%$` in mean performance, the maximum achievable decision accuracy is `$80\%$` regardless of prediction method quality. Some "incorrect" predictions may reflect ground truth noise rather than method failure.
- **3-Seed Insufficiency:** While 3 seeds is more than prior work, it may still be insufficient to precisely estimate mean performance for dataset pairs with small performance gaps.
- **Unreported Pair Distribution:** The paper does not report the distribution of performance gaps between dataset pairs. Without this, it is unclear how many pairs are fundamentally unrankable due to variance.

**Intermediate Checkpoint Gap:** Section 3.1 states:

> "Decisions from intermediate checkpoints are as good as compute equivalent final checkpoints."

However:
- **No Detailed Numbers:** The main text provides no quantitative comparison between intermediate and final checkpoint decision accuracy. This claim is not supported with specific figures or tables.
- **Learning Rate Schedule Effects:** Section 2.2 notes that accounting for learning rate schedule impacts on intermediate checkpoints "would require further parameters in the equation." This suggests intermediate checkpoints may have systematic biases that are not fully characterized.

**Proxy Metric Normalization:** Section 2.5 uses character-length normalization for proxy metrics:

> "Each of these can be computed with likelihoods normalized by number of tokens or characters; unless otherwise specified we use character length normalization."

This choice has implications:
- **Task Dependence:** The paper notes character normalization is "empirically optimal for most of the tasks that we observe" (Section 3.3), but does not specify which tasks favor token normalization.
- **Unexplored Alternative:** The paper does not test whether different normalization schemes for different tasks would improve overall decision accuracy.

**Evaluation Variance from Item Sampling:** Section 2.4 diverges from standard OLMES protocol:

> "We diverge from OLMES only in that we make use of all available items in the specified split of each benchmark rather than subsampling them, to reduce variance over the task distribution."

This creates a comparison issue:
- **Non-Standard Protocol:** Results may not directly compare to other work using standard OLMES subsampling.
- **Benchmark-Specific Effects:** Using all items may reduce variance differently across benchmarks, potentially affecting the noise-spread diagnostic in Figure 5.

### 6.5. Open Questions and Future Directions

**Scaling to Larger Targets:** The most critical open question is whether findings extrapolate to frontier scales:

- Does single-scale ranking at `$1B$` predict `$100B$` winners with similar accuracy?
- Do crossover events become more common at larger scale gaps?
- Do continuous proxy metrics remain effective for capabilities that emerge only at large scales?

**Scaling Law Improvement:** The paper explicitly invites future work:

> "DATADECIDE can measure improvement in future scaling laws." (Abstract)

Specific directions include:
- Can architecture-aware scaling laws (incorporating layer count, attention heads) improve prediction?
- Can task-specific scaling exponents improve multi-task decision accuracy?
- Can Bayesian methods that quantify prediction uncertainty improve decision-making under risk?

**Target Metric Justification:** The math benchmark anomaly raises a fundamental question:

> "raising a question for future work to explore whether changing the target metric can be justified." (Section 3.4)

This requires investigation:
- When is it valid to optimize data selection for continuous proxies when downstream use requires discrete accuracy?
- Does optimizing for `CORRECT PROB` at small scales lead to better large-scale `ACCURACY`, or does it optimize for a different objective?
- Are there tasks where proxy-target mismatch causes systematic degradation in final model capability?

**Benchmark Predictability Diagnosis:** Figure 5 provides a diagnostic framework, but several questions remain:

- Can benchmark predictability be improved through evaluation protocol changes (e.g., more items, different normalization)?
- Should practitioners avoid using low-predictability benchmarks for data decisions, or invest more compute to overcome noise?
- Are there new benchmarks specifically designed for high predictability at small scales?

**Data Recipe Generalization:** Section 5 (Limitations) acknowledges:

> "While our 25 data recipes is an order of magnitude more than previous suites, there is always the possibility that findings across these will not be representative of future data recipes."

Open questions include:
- Do findings hold for domain-specific data recipes (medical, legal, code-only)?
- Do findings hold for multilingual data mixing decisions?
- Do findings hold when synthetic data is included in recipes?

The release of all 1,050 checkpoints enables the community to test these boundary conditions without repeating the full compute investment, which is the paper's most significant mechanism for addressing its own limitations.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The `DATADECIDE` suite fundamentally shifts how the research community approaches data selection for language model pretraining. By providing empirical ground truth across 25 data recipes and 14 scales, this work transforms data decision-making from a heuristic practice into a measurable engineering discipline. This section examines how these findings reshape the field, what research they enable, and how practitioners should integrate these insights into their development workflows.

### 7.1. Landscape Impact: From Heuristics to Empirical Science

Prior to `DATADECIDE`, the field lacked a controlled benchmark for validating data selection methods. Most work validated decisions by training only the predicted "winner" at large scale and showcasing its performance, leaving the failure rate of the decision method unknown (Section 1). This created a validation gap where practitioners could not distinguish between methods that genuinely work and methods that appear to work due to selective reporting.

**The Shift to Decision-Centric Evaluation:** By introducing **decision accuracy** as the primary metric (Section 2.3), this work reframes the evaluation question from "How close is the predicted score to the actual score?" to "Does this method lead me to choose the right dataset?" This distinction matters because:

- A method can have low prediction error but still make wrong decisions if performance gaps between datasets are smaller than the error margin
- Conversely, a method with higher error might correctly identify winners if the signal direction is preserved
- Practitioners ultimately need to select data, not forecast exact performance numbers

The paper's finding that **single-scale ranking at 150M parameters achieves ~80% decision accuracy against 1B targets** (Figure 1, right) challenges the assumption that complex scaling law extrapolation is necessary for efficient development. This has three concrete implications:

1.  **Compute Reallocation:** Teams can redirect compute previously spent on multi-scale curve fitting toward running more data recipe ablations at a single well-chosen scale
2.  **Method Simplification:** The engineering overhead of implementing and maintaining scaling law fitting pipelines may not be justified for data selection tasks in the tested regime
3.  **Baseline Establishment:** Future scaling law research now has a clear target—any new method must exceed the single-scale frontier shown in Figure 3 to be practically valuable

**The Proxy Metric Breakthrough:** Perhaps the most transformative finding is that continuous likelihood metrics like `CORRECT PROB` enable prediction for tasks previously considered unpredictable at small scales. Figure 6 shows code tasks (`MBPP`, `HumanEval`) jumping from trivial to **>80% decision accuracy** when using `CORRECT PROB` instead of discrete `ACCURACY`. This expands the range of capabilities that can be validated early in development, potentially reducing the compute required for code capability decisions by **100× or more**.

**Open Infrastructure for Community Validation:** By releasing all 1,050 checkpoints, evaluation results, and data recipes, `DATADECIDE` enables the community to:
- Test new scaling law variants without retraining the full suite
- Evaluate new benchmarks on existing checkpoints to identify high-predictability tasks
- Extend the suite with additional small-scale experiments against the fixed 1B ground truth

This transforms the 820K H100 GPU hour investment into a shared community resource rather than a one-time research expenditure.

### 7.2. Enabled Research Directions

The `DATADECIDE` suite opens several concrete research avenues that were previously infeasible due to lack of controlled ground truth.

**Scaling Law Improvement Benchmark:** Section 3.2 explicitly positions `DATADECIDE` as a testbed for future scaling law methods:

> "Improved future scaling laws may be able to advance the Pareto frontier on DATADECIDE as they are not bound by crossovers."

Specific directions include:
- **Architecture-Aware Scaling:** Current variants treat all models as functions of compute $C$ alone. Future methods could incorporate architectural parameters (layer count, attention heads, MLP ratio) from Table 2 to improve prediction accuracy
- **Task-Specific Exponents:** The 10 OLMES tasks show vastly different predictability profiles (Figure 2). Scaling laws with task-specific parameters may outperform one-size-fits-all approaches
- **Uncertainty Quantification:** Bayesian methods that provide confidence intervals on predictions could help practitioners decide when a prediction is reliable enough to act upon
- **Crossover Detection:** Methods that explicitly model and detect when scaling trends between data recipes might cross over could overcome the fundamental limitation of single-scale ranking

**Benchmark Design for Predictability:** Figure 5's noise-spread diagnostic provides a framework for designing evaluation benchmarks optimized for small-scale decision making:

- **Low-Noise Benchmarks:** Tasks like `MMLU` achieve high decision accuracy primarily through low run-to-run variance. Future benchmark design could prioritize evaluation protocols that minimize variance (e.g., more items, standardized prompting)
- **High-Spread Benchmarks:** Tasks like `ARC Easy` separate data recipes widely, making rankings easier to predict. Benchmark creators could intentionally design tasks that amplify data quality differences
- **Predictability Certification:** New benchmarks could be evaluated on `DATADECIDE` checkpoints to certify their predictability before adoption in development pipelines

**Target Metric Alignment:** The math benchmark anomaly in Figure 6 raises an unresolved question about proxy-target metric alignment:

> "They do however give decision accuracy above 80% if we switch the target metric to CORRECT PROB, raising a question for future work to explore whether changing the target metric can be justified." (Section 3.4)

This opens research on:
- When is it valid to optimize data selection for continuous proxies when downstream use requires discrete accuracy?
- Does optimizing for `CORRECT PROB` at small scales lead to better large-scale `ACCURACY`, or does it optimize for a different objective?
- Are there systematic cases where proxy-target mismatch causes degradation in final model capability?

**Scale Extrapolation Validation:** The 1B parameter target is still far smaller than frontier models. Research directions include:
- Training additional large-scale models (e.g., 7B, 13B parameters) on a subset of data recipes to test whether 150M predictions hold at larger scales
- Investigating whether crossover events become more common at larger scale gaps (150M to 100B vs. 150M to 1B)
- Testing whether continuous proxy metrics remain effective for capabilities that emerge only at large scales

**Data Recipe Expansion:** The 25 recipes cover general web corpora but leave gaps:
- **Domain-Specific Recipes:** Medical, legal, scientific, or code-only corpora may exhibit different scaling behaviors
- **Multilingual Mixing:** Data mixing decisions for multilingual models are not addressed
- **Synthetic Data:** The growing use of model-generated training data is not represented
- **Temporal Effects:** The impact of data recency on model performance is not studied

Each of these extensions could be evaluated against the existing 1B checkpoints or by training new target-scale models on extended recipe sets.

### 7.3. Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The findings from `DATADECIDE` translate into concrete recommendations for practitioners making data decisions in real development pipelines.

**Recommended Decision-Making Pipeline:** Based on the empirical results, practitioners should follow this workflow:

1.  **Benchmark Selection:** Choose evaluation benchmarks with known high predictability for your target scale. For 4M–1B parameter experiments, `MMLU` and `ARC Easy` are reliable choices (Figure 2). Avoid `SocialIQA` and `BoolQ` which remain difficult across all scales.

2.  **Metric Selection:** Use continuous proxy metrics (`CORRECT PROB` or `TOTAL PROB`) for small-scale evaluations, especially for tasks where discrete accuracy is near-zero at small scales (e.g., code generation). Table 3 provides formal definitions for implementation.

3.  **Scale Selection:** Train models at approximately 150M parameters (1% of target 1B compute) for initial data screening. This achieves ~80% decision accuracy across aggregated tasks (Figure 1, right).

4.  **Variance Estimation:** Run at least 2–3 random seeds for your target-scale models to establish reliable ground truth. The paper finds standard deviation between 1B runs can reach **2 percentage points of accuracy** (Section 2.1), meaning dataset pairs differing by less than this threshold cannot be reliably ranked.

5.  **Noise-Spread Diagnostic:** Before relying on a benchmark for decisions, evaluate its noise (variance across seeds) and spread (variance across data recipes) at your experimental scale using the framework from Figure 5. High noise or low spread indicates the benchmark is unsuitable for small-scale decision making.

**Compute Budget Allocation:** The compute-decision frontier in Figure 1 provides guidance on budget allocation:

| Target Decision Accuracy | Recommended Compute (% of Target) | Model Size | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 60–70% | 0.01% | 14M–20M | Suitable for initial screening of many recipes |
| ~80% | 1% | 150M | Recommended baseline for most decisions |
| >85% | 10%+ | 300M–750M | Diminishing returns; consider if decisions are high-stakes |

For code capability validation specifically, Figure 6 shows that 60M models with `CORRECT PROB` achieve ~80% decision accuracy, representing approximately **0.01% of target compute**.

**When Scaling Laws May Still Be Useful:** While the 8 baseline scaling law variants tested do not exceed the single-scale frontier (Figure 3), there are scenarios where multi-scale extrapolation may still be warranted:

- **Large Scale Gaps:** When predicting from 150M to 100B+ parameters (667× gap vs. the 6.7× gap tested), crossover events may become more common
- **Emergent Capabilities:** For capabilities that emerge abruptly at larger scales, single-scale ranking may fail where scaling laws could potentially model the emergence
- **Resource-Constrained Settings:** If you cannot afford 150M parameter models but can train multiple smaller models (4M, 6M, 8M), scaling laws may provide better predictions than any single small scale
- **Future Method Improvements:** New scaling law variants not tested in this work may exceed the current frontier

**Integration with Existing Workflows:** The `DATADECIDE` checkpoints are available on HuggingFace (footnote 1, Section 1). Practitioners can:
- Download evaluation results to benchmark new prediction methods without training
- Run new evaluations on existing checkpoints to test benchmark predictability
- Pretrain additional small models to compare against the provided 1B targets
- Use the noise-spread diagnostic to evaluate their own benchmarks before deployment

### 7.4. Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

**When to Use Single-Scale Ranking:** Based on the empirical evidence, single-scale ranking at 150M parameters is the recommended default for:

- Data recipe selection in the 4M–1B parameter regime
- General web corpus filtering and mixing decisions
- Scenarios where compute budget allows training at least one 150M model to completion
- Tasks with known high predictability (MMLU, ARC, HellaSwag with proxy metrics)

**When to Consider Alternatives:**

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Target scale >10B parameters | Multi-scale scaling laws + 1B validation | Crossover risk increases with scale gap |
| Code capability decisions | `CORRECT PROB` at 60M scale | Figure 6 shows 80% accuracy at 0.01% compute |
| Math capability decisions | Uncertain—validate empirically | Figure 6 shows proxy metrics do not help for Minerva/GSM8K |
| Cannot afford 150M models | Multi-scale with smaller models | Better than single very-small-scale prediction |
| High-stakes decisions (>85% accuracy needed) | 300M+ single-scale or multi-scale | Diminishing returns but may justify cost |
| SocialIQA or BoolQ targets | Avoid for small-scale decisions | Figure 2 shows these remain unpredictable across scales |

**Implementation Checklist:** Before deploying a data decision method based on these findings:

- [ ] Verify your target scale and token-to-parameter ratio are similar to the tested regime (100 ratio, 5× Chinchilla)
- [ ] Confirm your evaluation benchmarks show acceptable noise-spread characteristics at your experimental scale
- [ ] Run 2–3 random seeds for target-scale models to establish reliable ground truth
- [ ] Use character-normalized `CORRECT PROB` as the default proxy metric for small-scale evaluations
- [ ] Document decision accuracy (not just prediction error) when reporting results
- [ ] Consider whether your data recipes fall within the types tested (general web corpora with filtering/mixing interventions)

**Limitations to Acknowledge in Deployment:** When applying these findings, practitioners should explicitly note:

- Results are validated for 1B parameter targets, not frontier-scale models
- Fixed token-to-parameter ratio of 100 may not match all training regimes
- Multiple-choice OLMES benchmarks may not represent all capability dimensions
- 2% standard deviation between 1B runs sets a fundamental ceiling on achievable decision accuracy
- Math benchmark proxy-target alignment remains an open question

**Community Extension Path:** The paper invites future research to extend `DATADECIDE` (Section 1):

> "Future research can extend DATADECIDE with little extra compute by running new evaluations on our checkpoints, pretraining additional small models to compare against the large target models we provide, or trying new prediction methods with lightweight manipulations such as smoothing and curve fitting on top of our released evaluation results."

This provides a low-barrier entry point for researchers who cannot afford large-scale pretraining but want to contribute to the data decision-making literature.

### 7.5. Summary: The Path Forward

`DATADECIDE` establishes the first empirically grounded baseline for data selection decision-making. The key takeaways for the field are:

1.  **Simplicity Wins:** Single-scale ranking at 150M parameters achieves ~80% decision accuracy, outperforming 8 baseline scaling law variants in the tested regime
2.  **Metrics Matter:** Continuous proxy metrics like `CORRECT PROB` unlock prediction for tasks previously considered unpredictable at small scales
3.  **Diagnostics Enable Better Decisions:** The noise-spread framework provides actionable guidance for benchmark selection and evaluation protocol design
4.  **Open Infrastructure Accelerates Progress:** Releasing all 1,050 checkpoints transforms the compute investment into a community resource for future method development

The work does not claim to solve data selection permanently. Instead, it provides the measurement infrastructure necessary to validate future improvements. As the authors state in the Abstract:

> "No scaling law methods among 8 baselines exceed the compute-decision frontier of single-scale predictions, but DATADECIDE can measure improvement in future scaling laws."

This positions `DATADECIDE` not as a final answer, but as the foundation for an empirical science of efficient model development—one where claims about prediction methods can be tested against ground truth rather than validated through selective reporting. The release of models, data, and evaluations ensures that this foundation is accessible to the broader research community, enabling progress that would otherwise be blocked by compute barriers.
