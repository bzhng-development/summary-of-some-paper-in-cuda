## 1. Executive Summary

This work addresses the scarcity of high-quality agentic interaction data by introducing a scalable framework that automatically constructs over 1,000 heterogeneous, fully simulated environments grounded in read-write database schemas. Through a two-phase fine-tuning strategy on these environments, the resulting `AgentScaler` family (4B, 8B, 30B-A3B) achieves state-of-the-art performance on `$\tau$-bench`, `$\tau^2$-Bench`, and ACEBench, with the 30B model matching 1T-parameter systems like Kimi-K2. This demonstrates that systematic environment scaling, rather than mere parameter increase, is a pivotal strategy for deploying robust function-calling intelligence in resource-constrained settings.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of this work, we must first recognize a fundamental bottleneck in the development of language agents: the scarcity of high-quality interaction data. While Large Language Models (LLMs) have grown immensely in capability, their ability to act autonomously in the real world—known as **agentic intelligence**—remains constrained. This section dissect the specific gaps in existing research, explains why solving them is critical for real-world deployment, and positions this paper's approach against prior methodologies.

### 2.1 The Core Problem: Scarcity of Agentic Data

The primary obstacle identified by the authors is not necessarily model architecture, but data availability. Specifically, there is a shortage of **agentic data**. The paper defines this term precisely in footnote 1 of Section 1:

> "agentic data" refers to trajectories involving such interactions [autonomous agents interacting with environments via explicit action executions, namely, tool calls].

In standard pre-training, models learn from static corpora (text). In agentic training, models must learn from **trajectories**—sequences of actions and observations generated by interacting with an environment. For example, an agent might need to call a `get_order_info` tool, receive a response, and then call a `refund_order` tool based on that response.

The scarcity arises because collecting this data is inherently difficult:
*   **Real-world interaction is costly:** Having agents interact with live APIs (e.g., actual banking or airline systems) incurs financial costs, latency, and risks of unintended side effects.
*   **Supervision is hard:** Without a controlled environment, it is difficult to verify if an agent's action sequence was truly correct or just plausible.

As noted in Section 1, the community is transitioning from an era of "raw corpora" to an "era of experience" (Silver & Sutton, 2025). However, generating this experience at scale remains an unsolved challenge.

### 2.2 Why This Problem Matters

The motivation for solving this data scarcity is twofold: practical deployment and theoretical generalization.

1.  **Real-World Deployment:** Advanced agentic intelligence is a prerequisite for deploying LLMs in practical applications (Abstract). Real-world APIs demand **precise, robust function-calling intelligence**. If an agent hallucinates a parameter or calls tools in the wrong order, the operation fails. Therefore, agents must develop these capabilities through interaction in **varied environments**.
2.  **Generalization:** The breadth of function-calling competence is closely tied to the diversity of environments in which agents are trained (Abstract). If an agent is only trained on retail APIs, it may fail when confronted with telecom or airline tools. To achieve **general agentic intelligence**, the training environment space must be systematically broadened.

### 2.3 Limitations of Prior Approaches

The paper categorizes existing methods for generating synthetic agentic data into two main paradigms, both of which exhibit significant shortcomings regarding scalability and realism.

#### The Reverse Paradigm
In this approach, synthetic data generation starts from the **answer** and works backward to the **question**.
*   **Mechanism:** User queries are generated to match each assistant function call observed at every interaction turn (Yin et al., 2025).
*   **Shortcoming:** The resulting trajectories may exhibit **limited realism**. Because the query is constructed to fit the tool call, it may not reflect the natural ambiguity or complexity of actual user intents.

#### The Forward Paradigm (Simulated Agent–Human Interplay)
This approach starts from a **user intent** and simulates the interaction forward.
*   **Mechanism:** A high-level user intent is formulated to necessitate agent interaction. Agentic data is then constructed in a top-down manner based on this intent through human–agent interplay (Chen et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2024b).
*   **Shortcoming:** The **environment is not scalable**. As stated in Section 1, "the absence of automated environment construction hinders large-scale deployment and inevitably entails some degree of manual intervention." Most prior works rely on hand-crafted environments, limiting the diversity of scenarios the agent can experience.

#### Infrastructure-Level Challenges
Section 6.1 ("Related Work") further details three common strategies for tool-use environments and their specific failures:
*   **Real-World Environments:** While authentic, they require frequent calls to Model Context Protocol (MCP) services, resulting in **high costs and significant time overhead**. Maintaining stable services is difficult.
*   **LLM-Simulated Environments:** Some works use strong LLMs to simulate tool responses. These struggle with **hallucination and inconsistent response variability**, making them unreliable for training.
*   **Offline Tool Execution Environments:** While these avoid real API calls, constructing a reliable tool suite typically requires **substantial manual effort**. Furthermore, it is difficult to automatically validate the quality of such environments without human involvement.

### 2.4 Positioning: Environment Scaling via Database Abstraction

This paper positions itself as a solution to the **scalability** and **verifiability** gaps identified above. Instead of focusing solely on model scaling (increasing parameters), the authors propose **environment scaling**.

#### The Design Principle: Tools as Database Operations
The core theoretical shift in this work is abstracting function calls as operations on a underlying database. Section 2 ("Environment Build and Scaling") formalizes this principle:

> In essence, any function call can be interpreted as a read–write operation over an underlying environmental database $D$... Specifically, each function $func$ can be assigned an operator type, $op(func) \in \{read, write\}$.

*   **Read-type functions:** Perform queries over $D$ (e.g., retrieval, inspection).
*   **Write-type functions:** Induce state transitions in $D$ (e.g., modification, generation).

This abstraction allows the authors to treat the environment not as a collection of disconnected APIs, but as a **stateful system**. A tool response is equivalent to evaluating the induced operator on $D$:

$$
\text{API}(func, \alpha) \equiv op(func)(\alpha; D)
$$

where $\alpha$ denotes the input arguments.

#### Systematic Automation
By adopting this database abstraction, the paper enables **programmatic materialization** of environments. Instead of manually coding environments:
1.  **Tool Graphs:** Tools are organized into domains using community detection on a tool dependency graph (Section 2.1).
2.  **Schema Generation:** Database schemas are generated automatically for each domain.
3.  **Verification:** Because the environment state is tracked in a database, trajectories can be verified at two granularities: **database-level state consistency** and **exact matching of tool sequences** (Section 2.2).

#### Summary of Positioning
Table 1 below synthesizes how this work differentiates itself from prior categories based on the text in Section 1 and Section 6.

| Feature | Reverse Paradigm | Forward Paradigm (Prior) | Real-World APIs | **This Work (AgentScaler)** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Direction** | Answer $\to$ Query | Intent $\to$ Action | N/A | Intent $\to$ Action |
| **Environment** | Limited/Implicit | Manual/Hand-crafted | Live Services | **Automated/Scalable** |
| **Verification** | Weak | Partial | Hard to Validate | **State-Based (Exact)** |
| **Scalability** | High | Low (Manual Bottleneck) | Low (Cost/Latency) | **High (Programmatic)** |

In summary, the paper argues that general agentic intelligence cannot be achieved merely by training on static data or limited environments. By shifting the focus to **automated environment construction** grounded in read-write database schemas, the authors aim to create a principled pipeline that generates the diverse, verifiable experiences necessary for robust agent training.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a **systematic pipeline for scalable environment construction and agent experience learning**. The core idea is to treat function calls as read-write operations on underlying database schemas, enabling automated environment generation and verifiable trajectory collection for training language agents.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is an **automated factory that generates training environments and interaction data for language agents**, rather than manually crafting each scenario by hand. It solves the scalability problem by transforming 30,000+ APIs into over 1,000 distinct database-backed environments, then using simulated human-agent conversations within those environments to collect verifiable training trajectories.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The pipeline consists of four major components that operate in sequence:

1.  **Scenario Collection Module**: Gathers raw API definitions from multiple sources (ToolBench, API-Gen, internal repositories), filters low-quality entries, and rewrites descriptions to include explicit input-output specifications. Output: A curated pool of over 30,000 APIs denoted as $\Theta_F$.

2.  **Tool Dependency Graph Builder**: Converts API parameters into vector representations, computes cosine similarity between parameter pairs, and creates edges when similarity exceeds threshold $\tau$. Applies Louvain community detection to partition tools into coherent domains. Output: Over 1,000 domain clusters, each with an associated tool graph.

3.  **Environment Materialization Engine**: For each domain, generates a database schema from tool parameters, then writes executable Python code that implements each tool as read-write operations on that schema. Output: Fully simulated, programmatic environments with verifiable state transitions.

4.  **Agent Experience Learning Framework**: Runs simulated human-agent interplay within constructed environments, collects interaction trajectories, applies three-stage filtering (validity, state alignment, exact match), then performs two-phase fine-tuning. Output: Trained AgentScaler models (4B, 8B, 30B-A3B).

Information flows unidirectionally: raw APIs $\rightarrow$ tool graph $\rightarrow$ domain environments $\rightarrow$ interaction trajectories $\rightarrow$ filtered training data $\rightarrow$ fine-tuned models.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**This is a systems paper that introduces a scalable infrastructure for agentic training, not merely a new model architecture.** The fundamental insight is that environment diversity, not just model size, determines agentic capability, so the authors invest engineering effort into automating environment construction rather than manually curating scenarios.

#### The Database Abstraction Principle

The entire system rests on a theoretical abstraction introduced in Section 2: every function call can be interpreted as a read-write operation over an underlying environmental database $D$. This means that instead of treating each API as an isolated black box, the system models APIs as operators that either query the database state or modify it. Formally, each function $func$ is assigned an operator type $op(func) \in \{read, write\}$, where read-type functions perform queries over $D$ (such as retrieval, inspection, or monitoring) and write-type functions induce state transitions in $D$ (such as modification, generation, or actuation). A tool response is mathematically equivalent to evaluating the induced operator on $D$, expressed as:

$$
\text{API}(func, \alpha) \equiv op(func)(\alpha; D)
$$

where $\alpha$ denotes the input arguments provided to the function call. This abstraction matters because it enables **programmatic verification**: if the database state after a sequence of tool calls does not match the expected state, the trajectory is invalid. Furthermore, tools within the same domain typically exhibit structurally similar read-write patterns, which can be captured by a common database schema $S_k$. The design problem therefore reduces to defining a partition of the tool space into domains $\{T_1, \ldots, T_M\}$ and assigning to each domain a database schema $S_k$, where $S_k$ specifies the environment for that domain.

#### Step 1: Scenario Collection and API Curation

The pipeline begins with **Scenario Collection**, which gathers a large corpus of real-world tools to serve as the raw material for environment construction. The authors collected more than 30,000 APIs from three sources: ToolBench (Qin et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024), API-Gen (Prabhakar et al., 2025b), and their internal tool repository. This initial pool undergoes rigorous filtering to remove low-quality APIs, followed by refinement where some API descriptions are rewritten to incorporate explicit input-output specifications (Fang et al., 2025). Building on this cleaned corpus, the system constructs tool compositions by systematically exploiting the input-output relationships among APIs, meaning that if API A produces an output that matches the input format of API B, they can be composed into a sequence. This process ultimately results in API pools $\Theta_F$ whose size $N$ exceeds 30,000, providing a reliable foundation for subsequent experiments and analysis. The scale of this collection is critical: prior work typically operated with hundreds or thousands of APIs, but this work demonstrates that environment scaling requires an order-of-magnitude larger starting corpus to generate sufficient domain diversity.

#### Step 2: Tool Dependency Graph Modeling

Once APIs are curated, the system constructs a **tool dependency graph** in which nodes represent tools and edges encode compositional compatibility induced by function parameters. For a pair of tools, the system extracts their respective parameter lists and converts them into vector representations $\phi$ to compute their cosine similarity. If the similarity exceeds a predefined threshold $\tau$, the system considers there to be a dependency relationship between the two tools and inserts an edge $E$ between them in the graph. Formally, edges are defined as:

$$
E = \{(i, j) \mid \text{sim}(\phi(P_{func_i}), \phi(P_{func_j})) > \tau, i \neq j\}
$$

where $P_{func}$ denotes the parameter list of a function. The threshold $\tau$ is not explicitly specified in the paper, which is a notable omission for reproducibility. Domain partitioning then reduces to a graph clustering problem, and the authors employ **Louvain community detection** (Blondel et al., 2008) to identify coherent tool communities that serve as domains. Louvain detection is chosen because it efficiently handles large graphs (30,000+ nodes) and produces communities with high modularity, meaning tools within a community are more densely connected to each other than to tools outside the community. For a segmented tool set, since parameter matching relies solely on vectorization and considers only individual parameter information, the overall inter-tool dependencies may be difficult to capture through similarity alone. Therefore, for tools within a given domain, the system further employs an LLM to systematically examine the dependencies between each pair of tools, thereby improving the accuracy of edges in the tool graph. In total, this process obtained $M$ domains exceeding 1,000, which represents a significant increase over prior work that typically defined fewer than 100 domains manually.

#### Step 3: Function Schema Programmatic Materialization

After obtaining the domain-specific tool set and corresponding database schema, the system must **formalize each tool in Python code**, enabling it to perform read-write operations over the database schema. This step is called **Function Schema Programmatic Materialization**, and it transforms abstract API definitions into executable code that can be run during agent training. The system first leverages the parameters of all tools within a domain to generate a domain-specific database structure, which serves as the underlying state for subsequent tool operations. For example, if a domain contains tools for project management (such as `GetProjectTimeline`, `GetConstructionScheduling`, `ProjectScheduleCreate`), the database schema would include tables for projects, tasks, and milestones with appropriate fields like `project_id`, `project_name`, and `last_updated`. Each tool is then implemented as a Python class with a `__call__` method that operates on the database. The paper notes an interesting validation: when generating database structures and formalizing code within specific domains of $\tau$-bench, manual inspection revealed that the outputs exhibit a high degree of consistency with the official implementations provided by $\tau$-bench (Yao et al., 2024). This suggests that the automated materialization process produces environments that are functionally equivalent to hand-crafted benchmarks, but at a fraction of the engineering cost.

#### Step 4: Agentic Task Construction via Forward Simulation

With environments constructed, the system proceeds to **Agentic Task Construction**, which generates the actual interaction scenarios that agents will experience during training. The approach follows **forward simulated agent-human interplay**, which allows the system to fully simulate the environment, the user, and the agent simultaneously. The critical step is to synthesize agentic tasks that elicit human tool usage while ensuring that the resulting trajectories remain verifiable. Concretely, the system first initializes an environment state based on the domain-specific database schema, while encouraging as much diversity as possible in the initial state. Next, it samples logically coherent tool sequences from the domain's tool graph by constructing a directed dependency graph over APIs and traversing it to obtain valid sequences. Starting from a randomly selected initial node, the system conducts a directed walk until either the maximum execution steps are reached or a node with no outgoing edges is encountered. This process yields a logically coherent tool sequence where each tool's output can serve as input to subsequent tools. For each step, the system generates the corresponding arguments and performs the actual tool call, grounding the operations directly on the database and continuously tracking the evolving database state. This procedure enables verifiability at two complementary granularities: **(i) database-level state consistency**, meaning the final database state must match the expected state after all operations, and **(ii) exact matching of tool sequences**, meaning the sequence of invoked tools and their arguments must exactly match the overall intent. This dual verification is stronger than prior work, which typically validated only one granularity or relied on LLM-based judgment.

#### Step 5: Human-Agent Interplay for Experience Collection

Once agentic tasks are constructed, the system performs **Human-Agent Interplay** to collect actual interaction trajectories that will serve as training data. Motivated by Yao et al. (2024), the system instantiates a simulated user tasked with fulfilling a given overall intent, while the agent leverages domain-specific tools to address the user's needs, continuing the interaction until the simulated user deems the task complete. This setup enables end-to-end simulation, encompassing user simulation, agent, and environment, yielding a highly scalable framework. Each completed interaction trace constitutes an agent experience, which can subsequently be used for training. Importantly, since the system possesses both the gold tool sequences and arguments for the overall intent and the final environment state, it can apply these as supervision signals for experience filtering. Figure 2 in the paper illustrates this process: a simulated user issues an intent (e.g., "Can you return my order?"), the agent responds with tool calls (e.g., `get_user_info(...)`, `get_order_info(...)`), the environment executes these calls and updates its state, and the interaction continues until task completion. The key advantage over prior work is that every trajectory has a **ground truth**: the gold action sequence and the gold environment state, enabling precise filtering rather than relying on heuristic quality scores.

#### Step 6: Three-Stage Trajectory Filtering Funnel

Not all collected trajectories are suitable for training, so the system adopts a **three-stage funnel-based trajectory filtering framework** consisting of validity control, environment state alignment, and function calling exact match. The first stage, **validity control**, removes invalid interaction trajectories to ensure well-formed alternating user-assistant exchanges. Additionally, the system applies an n-gram-based filtering procedure to eliminate severely repetitive reasoning segments, discarding data points where the agent loops on the same reasoning pattern without making progress. The second stage, **environment state alignment**, retains only those trajectories whose final database state matches the golden state after the interplay, thereby validating the effectiveness of write operations. The filtering granularity at this stage is the database/environment level, meaning that even if the tool sequence differs from the gold sequence, the trajectory is kept if it achieves the same final state. The third stage, **function calling exact match**, serves as the most stringent filtering stage, where the granularity is the tool sequence. Since a tool sequence consisting entirely of read operations without any write operations would cause state-based filtering to fail (because reads do not change state), the system adopts a stricter exact match approach for filtering in such cases. A trajectory is preserved only if the sequence of invoked tools and arguments exactly matches the overall intent, ensuring high-fidelity supervision. It is worth noting that the system does not filter out trajectories in which tool calls return errors. Thanks to the aforementioned filtering framework, such trajectories may still accomplish the intended goal despite intermediate failures, and retaining them in the training data helps improve the robustness of the model. This is a subtle but important design choice: exposing the model to error recovery scenarios during training makes it more resilient in deployment.

#### Step 7: Two-Stage Agentic Experience Learning

With filtered trajectories in hand, the system trains the agent through a **two-phase fine-tuning strategy** designed to first establish general capabilities and then specialize for domain-specific contexts. In the first phase, the agent is trained to acquire fundamental skills for tool usage and user interaction, focusing on general domains where a broad set of tools and tasks are available. This allows the agent to develop a robust understanding of when and how to invoke function calls, as well as how to integrate tool outputs into coherent user-facing responses. This stage emphasizes breadth and generality, ensuring that the agent builds a versatile foundation of agentic behaviors before domain-specific specialization. In the second phase, the agent undergoes fine-grained training in vertical domains, where tasks, tools, and user intents exhibit domain-specific characteristics. By grounding the learning process in realistic scenarios within a target domain, the agent refines its ability to select tools, parameterize calls, and produce responses that are accurate, contextually appropriate, and aligned with domain-specific goals. This specialization ensures a smoother adaptation of agentic capabilities, enabling the agent to operate effectively in real-world, task-oriented environments. The ablation study in Figure 3 validates this design: both Stage 1 and Stage 2 training substantially improve performance over the base model (Qwen3-Thinking-30B-A3B) across all subsets of ACEBench-en, and through multi-steps agent training in Stage 2, the model's score on the agent set has further improved, and the overall score has also increased.

#### Step 8: Training Objective and Loss Formulation

The mathematical formulation of the training objective clarifies exactly which parts of the interaction trajectory receive gradient supervision. Given an agent-human interplay experience trajectory $H = (h_0, a_1, \ldots, a_{n-1}, h_n, a_0)$, where each human instruction is denoted by $h_t$ at $t$-round interaction, and each assistant turn $a_t$ is decomposed as $a_t = (\tau_t, \rho_t, y_t)$. Here, $\tau_t$ represents the function call tokens, $\rho_t$ the tool response tokens, and $y_t$ the assistant response tokens. The training objective is to optimize only the tool calls and assistant responses, while human instructions $h_i$ and tool responses $\rho_t$ are excluded from the loss. Formally, given an autoregressive model $p_\theta(x_k | x_{<k})$, the loss is defined as:

$$
L(\theta) = -\frac{1}{|H|} \sum_{k=1}^{|H|} I[x_k \in T] \cdot \log \pi_\theta (x_k | x_{<k})
$$

where $x_k$ denotes the $k$-th token in the trajectory, $\pi_\theta$ is the model distribution, $I[\cdot]$ is the indicator function, and $T$ is the set of tokens belonging to tool calls $\tau$ or assistant responses $y$. In practice, all tokens in $\rho_i$ and $h_i$ are masked out from supervision but remain visible in the context $x_{<k}$. This ensures that the model conditions on tool responses and human instructions, while gradients are only propagated through assistant-generated tool calls and natural-language responses. This masking strategy is critical: if tool responses (which are generated by the environment, not the model) were included in the loss, the model would be penalized for tokens it did not generate, leading to unstable training. Similarly, human instructions should not be optimized since they are fixed inputs, not model outputs.

#### Design Rationale: Why This Approach Over Alternatives

The authors' design choices reflect deliberate trade-offs between scalability, verifiability, and realism. Compared to **real-world API environments**, this approach sacrifices some authenticity but gains massive scalability: calling actual MCP services incurs high costs and time overhead, whereas simulated databases execute in milliseconds with zero external dependencies. Compared to **LLM-simulated environments**, this approach sacrifices some flexibility but gains verifiability: LLM-generated tool responses can hallucinate or produce inconsistent outputs, whereas database-backed operations are deterministic and checkable. Compared to **manually-crafted offline environments**, this approach sacrifices some precision but gains automation: hand-crafted environments are high-fidelity but cannot scale beyond dozens of domains, whereas this pipeline generates 1,000+ domains automatically. The two-stage training strategy addresses a common failure mode in agentic fine-tuning: models trained only on domain-specific data overfit to those domains and fail to generalize, while models trained only on general data lack the depth needed for complex domain-specific tasks. By first establishing broad competence and then specializing, the system achieves both generalization and depth. The decision to retain error trajectories during filtering is counterintuitive but principled: real-world agents must handle failures gracefully, so training data should include recovery scenarios rather than only successful trajectories. Finally, the choice of Louvain community detection over other clustering algorithms reflects the need to handle graph scale: with 30,000+ tools, algorithms with higher computational complexity would be infeasible, but Louvain detection runs in near-linear time and produces high-quality communities.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the fundamental conceptual advances in this work, distinguishing between incremental engineering improvements and genuine innovations that shift how the field approaches agentic intelligence. The paper's contributions extend beyond the specific pipeline described in Section 3; they represent a reframing of what limits agent development and how to overcome those limits.

### 4.1 Environment Scaling as a First-Class Research Direction

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental conceptual shift

Prior research has predominantly focused on two scaling axes: **model scaling** (increasing parameters) and **data scaling** (increasing training corpus size). This paper introduces a third axis—**environment scaling**—and argues it is the critical bottleneck for agentic intelligence.

> "The breadth of function-calling competence is closely tied to the diversity of environments in which agents are trained." (Abstract)

This insight is significant because it explains why simply training larger models on existing agentic datasets fails to produce proportional gains. If an agent trains on only 10 hand-crafted environments (as in prior work like $\tau$-bench), it learns environment-specific patterns rather than generalizable tool-use reasoning. By scaling to **1,000+ automatically constructed domains** (Section 2.1), the agent encounters sufficient variation to extract transferable principles about when and how to call tools.

**Why this differs from prior work:**

| Scaling Axis | Prior Focus | This Work's Contribution |
|--------------|-------------|--------------------------|
| Model Parameters | 7B → 70B → 1T+ | Secondary concern; 30B matches 1T |
| Training Tokens | More text data | More *interaction* diversity |
| **Environments** | ~10-100 manual domains | **1,000+ automated domains** |

The ablation study in Figure 3 provides empirical support: Stage 1 training on general domains establishes foundational competence, but Stage 2 specialization on vertical domains is required for peak performance. This two-stage pattern would not be discoverable without the ability to scale environments systematically.

**Significance:** This reframing suggests that future agentic improvements may come more from infrastructure investment (environment construction) than from architectural changes or parameter increases. For resource-constrained deployments, this is particularly valuable: a 4B model trained on diverse environments (`AgentScaler-4B`) outperforms a 30B base model (`Qwen3-Thinking-30B-A3B`) on several benchmarks (Table 1).

### 4.2 Database Abstraction Enables Programmatic Verifiability

**Innovation Type:** Theoretical advance with practical consequences

The abstraction that **every function call is a read-write operation on an underlying database $D$** (Section 2) appears simple but has profound implications for how environments can be constructed and validated.

**Prior approaches lacked this abstraction:**
*   **Real-world API environments** treat each tool as an opaque black box with no unified state model.
*   **LLM-simulated environments** generate responses without tracking consistent state, leading to hallucination.
*   **Manually-crafted offline environments** define state per-domain but cannot scale because each requires hand-written schema and validation logic.

By formalizing tool responses as:

$$
\text{API}(func, \alpha) \equiv op(func)(\alpha; D)
$$

the authors enable **programmatic verification** at the environment level. After any interaction trajectory, the system can check whether the final database state matches the expected golden state. This is stronger than prior validation methods that relied on:
*   LLM-based judgment (subjective, inconsistent)
*   Surface-level string matching (misses semantic equivalence)
*   Single-granularity checks (either sequence OR state, not both)

**Why this matters for training quality:**

The three-stage filtering funnel (Section 3.1) leverages this abstraction to retain only high-fidelity trajectories. Crucially, the **environment state alignment** stage validates write operations at the database level, while the **function calling exact match** stage handles read-only sequences where state changes would not occur. This dual-granularity approach prevents false negatives (discarding valid trajectories) and false positives (keeping invalid ones).

**Significance:** This abstraction transforms environment construction from an artisanal process (requiring domain experts to hand-code each scenario) into an engineering pipeline (automated schema generation from tool parameters). The paper notes that automated outputs show "high degree of consistency with the official implementations provided by $\tau$-bench" (Section 2.1), validating that programmatic materialization achieves hand-crafted quality at scale.

### 4.3 Error Trajectory Retention as a Robustness Strategy

**Innovation Type:** Counterintuitive design choice with empirical justification

A subtle but important innovation appears in Section 3.1's filtering discussion:

> "It is worth noting that we do not filter out trajectories in which tool calls return errors. Thanks to the aforementioned filtering framework, such trajectories may still accomplish the intended goal despite intermediate failures."

This decision contradicts standard practice in supervised fine-tuning, where erroneous outputs are typically discarded to avoid teaching the model incorrect behavior. However, the authors recognize that **error recovery is itself a critical agentic capability**.

**Why this is innovative:**

1.  **Real-world agents encounter failures:** APIs timeout, parameters are invalid, and services are unavailable. An agent that has only seen successful trajectories will fail catastrophically when encountering errors in deployment.

2.  **The filtering funnel ensures quality:** Because trajectories must still pass environment state alignment (final state matches golden state), retained error trajectories represent successful recovery, not failed execution. The agent learns *how to recover*, not *how to fail*.

3.  **No prior work explicitly addresses this:** Reviewing Section 6's related work on tool learning, no cited method discusses retaining error trajectories as a training strategy. Most assume clean, successful demonstrations.

**Significance:** This design choice likely contributes to the stability improvements shown in Figure 4, where `AgentScaler-30B-A3B` maintains higher pass@$k$ scores across multiple trials compared to the base model. Agents trained with error exposure develop more robust decision-making under uncertainty.

### 4.4 Compact Models Can Match Massive Models via Environment Diversity

**Innovation Type:** Empirical finding with deployment implications

The results in Table 1 challenge a prevailing assumption in the field: that agentic capability scales monotonically with model size. `AgentScaler-30B-A3B` achieves **75.7 overall on ACEBench-en**, comparable to `Kimi-K2-1T-A32B` at **77.4** and `GPT-OSS-120B-A5B` at **76.0**, despite having 30-40× fewer parameters.

**What makes this possible:**

The performance gap between base models and `AgentScaler` variants at the same scale reveals the contribution of environment scaling:

| Model | Base Score | AgentScaler Score | Gain |
|-------|------------|-------------------|------|
| Qwen3-Thinking-4B | 49.5 | 65.9 | +16.4 |
| Qwen3-8B | 65.9 | 67.4 | +1.5 |
| Qwen3-Thinking-30B-A3B | 67.2 | 75.7 | +8.5 |

The 4B model shows the largest relative improvement, suggesting that **smaller models benefit disproportionately from environment diversity**. This supports the claim in the Limitations section that "small language models are the future of agentic AI" (Belcak et al., 2025), but with a crucial caveat: environment scaling is the enabling factor.

**Why this differs from prior efficiency claims:**

Previous work on efficient agentic models typically relies on:
*   Distillation from larger models (requires a strong teacher)
*   Architectural optimization (requires custom training infrastructure)
*   Quantization (sacrifices some capability)

`AgentScaler` achieves efficiency through **training data quality** (diverse, verifiable environments) rather than model compression. This is more portable: the same pipeline can apply to any base architecture.

**Significance:** For practical deployment, this finding is transformative. A 30B model can run on consumer hardware with appropriate quantization, while 1T models require datacenter infrastructure. If environment scaling closes most of the capability gap, organizations can deploy capable agents without massive compute budgets.

### 4.5 Distinguishing Incremental from Fundamental Contributions

Not all contributions in this paper carry equal weight. Table 2 below categorizes the key elements by innovation level:

| Contribution | Innovation Type | Rationale |
|--------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Environment scaling as research direction | **Fundamental** | Reframes the bottleneck from model/data to environment diversity |
| Database abstraction for tools | **Fundamental** | Enables automated, verifiable environment construction |
| Two-stage fine-tuning strategy | Incremental | Builds on established curriculum learning principles |
| Three-stage filtering funnel | **Fundamental** | First to combine state + sequence validation at scale |
| Error trajectory retention | **Fundamental** | Counterintuitive; no prior work explicitly adopts this |
| Louvain community detection for tools | Incremental | Standard algorithm applied to new domain |
| Compact model performance | **Fundamental** | Challenges parameter-scaling assumptions |

The fundamental innovations (environment scaling, database abstraction, filtering funnel, error retention, compact model findings) collectively establish a new paradigm for agentic training. The incremental improvements (two-stage fine-tuning, community detection algorithm choice) are engineering decisions that support the paradigm but do not define it.

**Critical perspective:** The two-stage fine-tuning approach, while effective, draws directly from established curriculum learning literature. The novelty lies not in the training strategy itself but in applying it to the scaled environment infrastructure. Similarly, Louvain detection is a well-known graph clustering algorithm; the innovation is recognizing that tool dependency graphs can be clustered this way to produce meaningful domains.

**Open questions:** The paper acknowledges in the Limitations section that reinforcement learning has not yet been integrated, despite the simulated environment being "inherently well-suited for RL optimization." This represents a significant opportunity: if supervised fine-tuning on scaled environments already matches 1T models, RL optimization on the same infrastructure could potentially exceed closed-source systems. Additionally, the long-horizon tool-calling challenge (Figure 5) remains unresolved, with accuracy declining as tool call count increases. Future work must address whether environment scaling alone can solve this or if architectural changes are needed.

**Summary:** The core insight of this work is that **agentic intelligence is an infrastructure problem, not solely a model problem**. By investing in automated environment construction and verifiable trajectory collection, the authors demonstrate that compact models can achieve capabilities previously thought to require massive scale. This shifts the research agenda from "how do we build bigger models?" to "how do we build better environments?"—a question with more tractable engineering answers and broader deployment implications.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

To validate the claim that **environment scaling** drives agentic intelligence more effectively than parameter scaling alone, the authors design a rigorous evaluation framework. This section dissects the experimental methodology, interprets the quantitative results, and critically assesses where the evidence supports—or fails to support—the paper's core hypotheses. We move beyond simple score reporting to understand *what* these benchmarks measure and *why* the observed trends matter for real-world deployment.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The experimental design targets three distinct dimensions of agentic capability: **task success**, **generalization**, and **stability**. The authors select benchmarks that stress-test different aspects of function calling, ensuring that performance gains are not artifacts of overfitting to a single environment type.

#### Benchmarks and Domains
The evaluation spans three primary benchmarks, each serving a specific diagnostic purpose:

*   **`$\tau$-bench` (Yao et al., 2024):** Focuses on **real-world domains** (Retail, Airline). This benchmark tests the agent's ability to handle multi-turn interactions where tool calls must be sequenced correctly to achieve a user goal (e.g., processing a refund). It uses the `pass@1` metric, measuring whether the final environment state matches the gold state after a single attempt.
*   **`$\tau^2$-Bench` (Barres et al., 2025):** Extends `$\tau$-bench` with additional complexity and domains (Retail, Airline, **Telecom**). The inclusion of Telecom is critical; as seen in Table 1, this domain often presents higher difficulty due to complex dependency chains. This benchmark is also used for **stability analysis** via the `$\text{pass}@k$` metric.
*   **`ACEBench-en` (Chen et al., 2025):** A broader capability benchmark categorized into **Normal**, **Special**, and **Agent** tasks.
    *   *Normal:* Standard function calling.
    *   *Special:* Likely involves constrained or nuanced tool usage.
    *   *Agent:* Requires multi-step planning and reasoning.
    *   This benchmark uses **Accuracy** as the metric, providing a complementary view to the state-based `pass` metrics of `$\tau$-bench`.

Additionally, the authors evaluate on **`ACEBench-zh`** (Table 2), a Chinese-language version of ACEBench. This serves as an **Out-of-Distribution (OOD)** test, verifying whether the agentic capabilities learned in English environments transfer to different linguistic and cultural contexts without retraining.

#### Metrics: Beyond `pass@1`
While `pass@1` measures raw capability, it hides variability. The authors employ **`$\text{pass}@k$`** (Figure 4) to measure **stability**.
*   **Definition:** `$\text{pass}@k$` denotes the accuracy achieved when the model correctly answers the same question in all `$k$` independent trials.
*   **Significance:** A model might solve a task once by luck (high `pass@1`), but a robust agent must solve it consistently (high `pass@k`). A steep drop in score as `$k$` increases indicates high variance in reasoning or tool selection.

#### Baselines and Backbones
To isolate the effect of the training pipeline, the authors compare against two categories of baselines (Section 4.1):
*   **Closed-Source SOTA:** Gemini-2.5-pro, Claude-Sonnet-4, GPT-o3, GPT-o4-mini, GPT-5-think. These represent the upper bound of current industry capabilities.
*   **Open-Source SOTA:** Models ranging from 36B to 1T parameters, including Kimi-K2-1T-A32B, GPT-OSS-120B-A5B, and Deepseek-V3.1-671B-A37B.

The **AgentScaler** models are fine-tuned variants of the **Qwen3** series (Team, 2025b):
*   `AgentScaler-4B` (from Qwen3-Thinking-4B)
*   `AgentScaler-8B` (from Qwen3-8B)
*   `AgentScaler-30B-A3B` (from Qwen3-Thinking-30B-A3B)

This selection allows for a direct **parameter-matched comparison** (e.g., AgentScaler-30B vs. Qwen3-30B) to measure the gain from environment scaling, and a **cross-scale comparison** (e.g., AgentScaler-30B vs. Kimi-K2-1T) to measure efficiency.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

The core claim—that environment scaling enables compact models to match massive ones—is primarily supported by **Table 1**. However, a critical reading reveals nuances in *where* the gains occur.

#### Efficiency vs. Scale
As highlighted in the Key Insights, `AgentScaler-30B-A3B` achieves an **Overall score of 75.7** on ACEBench-en.
*   **Comparison:** This is within 1.7 points of `Kimi-K2-1T-A32B` (77.4) and `GPT-OSS-120B-A5B` (76.0), despite having **30–40× fewer parameters**.
*   **Interpretation:** This suggests diminishing returns on parameter scaling for agentic tasks *if* the training environment is limited. The diverse environments provided by AgentScaler compensate for the lack of parameter capacity.

#### Domain-Specific Performance
Table 1 reveals that performance is not uniform across domains.
*   **Retail & Airline (`$\tau$-bench`):** `AgentScaler-30B-A3B` scores **70.4** (Retail) and **54.0** (Airline), surpassing the base Qwen3-30B model (67.8 / 48.0) and matching closed-source models like GPT-o3 (70.4 / 52.0).
*   **Telecom (`$\tau^2$-Bench`):** This domain shows a larger gap. `AgentScaler-30B-A3B` scores **55.3**, significantly higher than the base Qwen3-30B (26.3) and competitive with GPT-o3 (58.2).
    *   *Critical Note:* The base Qwen3-30B performs poorly on Telecom (26.3), suggesting that without environment scaling, even large models struggle with specific vertical domains. The training pipeline recovers this capability effectively.
*   **ACEBench Agent Category:** This category tests complex planning. `AgentScaler-30B-A3B` scores **60.0**, a massive improvement over the base model's **42.8**. This indicates the environment scaling specifically enhances **multi-step reasoning**, not just single-turn tool calling.

#### The Compact Model Surprise
`AgentScaler-4B` achieves an Overall score of **65.9** on ACEBench-en.
*   **Comparison:** This outperforms the base `Qwen3-Thinking-30B-A3B` (67.2) by only a small margin, but critically, it vastly outperforms the base `Qwen3-Thinking-4B` (49.5).
*   **Implication:** The **gain from environment scaling is largest for smaller models** (+16.4 points for 4B vs. +8.5 points for 30B). This supports the hypothesis that small models are "data-hungry" for agentic tasks; they have the capacity to learn but lack the exposure. Environment scaling provides the necessary exposure.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies: Validating the Training Pipeline

To ensure the performance gains stem from the proposed **two-stage learning framework** and not just more data, the authors conduct an ablation study on ACEBench-en (**Figure 3**).

*   **Stage 1 (General Foundation):** Training on general domains improves the base model across all subsets (Normal, Agent, Overall). This establishes basic tool-use competence.
*   **Stage 2 (Domain Specialization):** Further training on vertical domains yields additional gains, particularly in the **Agent** subset.
*   **Mechanism:** As explained in Section 3.2, Stage 1 teaches *how* to call tools, while Stage 2 teaches *when* and *which* tools to call in specific contexts.
*   **Verdict:** The monotonic improvement from Base $\rightarrow$ Stage 1 $\rightarrow$ Stage 2 confirms that both breadth (environment diversity) and depth (domain specialization) are necessary. Removing Stage 2 would leave the agent capable but not contextually aligned.

### 5.4 Generalization and Robustness Analysis

A common failure mode in agentic training is **overfitting** to the training environments. The authors address this through OOD testing and stability analysis.

#### Out-of-Distribution Generalization (Table 2)
The models are evaluated on **`ACEBench-zh`**, which differs from the training setup (English environments) in language and potentially cultural context.
*   **Results:** `AgentScaler-30B-A3B` achieves an Overall score of **81.5**, improving over the base Qwen3-30B (74.2) by **+7.3 points**.
*   **Small Model Gain:** `AgentScaler-4B` improves from **43.9** to **65.6** (+21.7 points).
*   **Analysis:** The fact that training on *English* simulated environments improves performance on *Chinese* benchmarks suggests the model learns **abstract agentic reasoning** (e.g., "verify user before refund") rather than memorizing specific API signatures. This validates the claim that environment scaling fosters **generalizable intelligence**.
*   **Nuance:** On the `Special` subset of ACEBench-zh, `AgentScaler-30B-A3B` scores **83.3**, slightly lower than the base model's **86.7** (-3.4). This indicates a potential trade-off: generalization gains might come at the cost of performance on highly specialized, niche tasks that differ significantly from the training distribution.

#### Stability and Consistency (Figure 4)
Using the `$\text{pass}@k$` metric on `$\tau^2$-Bench`, the authors measure whether the model is reliably competent or inconsistently brilliant.
*   **Trend:** As `$k$` increases from 1 to 4, scores naturally decline for all models. This is expected; requiring 4 consecutive successful trials is harder than 1.
*   **Comparison:** `AgentScaler-30B-A3B` consistently maintains a higher weighted overall score than `Qwen3-Thinking-30B-A3B` across all `$k$` settings.
*   **Implication:** The environment scaling does not just teach the model to solve tasks; it teaches it to solve them **reliably**. The gap between AgentScaler and the base model persists even at `$k=4$`, indicating reduced variance in decision-making.
*   **Critical Observation:** The paper notes a "clear downward trend in scores as `$k$` increases, suggesting that the stability of existing LLMs remains a considerable challenge." Even AgentScaler is not immune to this; while it is *more* stable, absolute stability remains an open problem.

### 5.5 Failure Modes and Limitations

No experimental analysis is complete without examining where the system fails. The paper explicitly identifies two critical limitations through its analysis.

#### The Long-Horizon Challenge (Figure 5)
The authors construct a scatter plot on `$\tau$-bench` correlating **Number of Tool Calls** with **Task Accuracy**.
*   **Observation:** There is a **clear negative correlation**. As the number of tool calls increases, accuracy drops.
*   **Data:** In the Retail domain, accuracy falls from **82%** (low complexity) to **0%** (high complexity, small sample size). In Airline, it drops from **90%** to **0%** for the most complex tasks.
*   **Interpretation:** This is a fundamental limitation of the current approach. While environment scaling improves general tool use, it does not solve the **context window** or **reasoning depth** problems associated with long chains of actions. Errors compound over time; a mistake at step 2 propagates to step 10.
*   **Verdict:** The claim of "General Agentic Intelligence" is currently bounded by horizon length. The model is robust for short-to-medium tasks but fragile for long-horizon planning.

#### Infrastructure Limitations (Section 7)
The authors acknowledge two structural limitations in the "Limitations" section:
1.  **Lack of Reinforcement Learning (RL):** The current pipeline uses **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)** on verified trajectories. While the simulated environment is "inherently well-suited for RL optimization," this has not yet been implemented.
    *   *Impact:* SFT teaches the model to mimic successful trajectories. RL could potentially teach the model to **recover from failures** or optimize for efficiency (e.g., fewer tool calls), which SFT does not explicitly optimize.
2.  **Model Scale Cap:** The method is validated up to **30B parameters**. It is unproven whether environment scaling yields similar gains for 200B+ models.
    *   *Counter-argument:* The authors argue smaller models are the future for deployment (edge devices, latency). However, from a pure capability ceiling perspective, it remains unknown if environment scaling can push a 30B model to truly surpass a 1T model on *all* tasks, especially those requiring massive world knowledge rather than just tool logic.

### 5.6 Critical Verdict

Do the experiments convincingly support the paper's claims?

*   **Claim 1: Environment scaling improves agentic capability.**
    *   **Verdict:** **Strongly Supported.** The ablation study (Figure 3) and parameter-matched comparisons (Table 1) isolate the training pipeline as the cause of improvement. The gains are substantial (+16.4 points for 4B model).
*   **Claim 2: Compact models can match massive models.**
    *   **Verdict:** **Conditionally Supported.** On agentic benchmarks (ACEBench, `$\tau$-bench`), the 30B model matches 1T models. However, this comparison is limited to *tool-use* tasks. It is unclear if this holds for general reasoning or knowledge-intensive tasks outside the scope of these benchmarks.
*   **Claim 3: The approach fosters generalizable intelligence.**
    *   **Verdict:** **Supported.** The OOD results on ACEBench-zh (Table 2) demonstrate transferability across languages and contexts.
*   **Claim 4: The system is robust.**
    *   **Verdict:** **Partially Supported.** Stability is improved (Figure 4), but long-horizon performance degrades significantly (Figure 5). The system is robust for short tasks but fragile for complex, multi-step workflows.

**Summary:** The experimental analysis provides compelling evidence that **data quality (environment diversity) is a bottleneck comparable to model size** for agentic tasks. However, the results also delineate the boundaries of this approach: it excels at standardizing tool use and improving stability but does not yet solve the fundamental challenges of long-horizon planning or error recovery without RL. Future work integrating RL into this scalable environment framework is the logical next step to address these remaining gaps.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the **AgentScaler** framework demonstrates that environment scaling can significantly boost agentic capabilities in compact models, achieving this scalability required specific design choices that introduce inherent trade-offs. This section critically examines the assumptions underpinning the approach, the scenarios where the methodology may struggle, and the structural constraints that define the current boundary of this work. We distinguish between limitations that are artifacts of the current implementation (and thus fixable) and those that are fundamental to the design philosophy.

### 6.1 Methodological Trade-offs: SFT vs. Reinforcement Learning

A deliberate design choice in this work is the reliance on **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)** rather than Reinforcement Learning (RL), despite the environment being built to support the latter.

*   **The Trade-off:** The authors explicitly state in Section 7 (Limitation) that while the simulated environment is "inherently well-suited for RL optimization," they opted for a two-stage SFT pipeline.
    *   **Benefit:** SFT provides **training stability**. As noted in Section 3.1, the three-stage filtering funnel ensures that the model only sees high-fidelity, verified trajectories. This avoids the reward hacking or instability often encountered in early-stage RL training for agents.
    *   **Cost:** SFT teaches the model to **mimic** successful trajectories, but it does not explicitly optimize for **efficiency** or **recovery**. An SFT-trained agent learns to follow a path that worked; an RL-trained agent could learn to minimize tool calls or recover from novel errors not seen in the training data.
*   **Implication:** The performance ceiling observed in Figure 5 (long-horizon decay) might be partially attributable to this choice. SFT relies on the presence of successful long-horizon examples in the training data. If such examples are rare (due to the difficulty of generating them), the model cannot learn them. RL could potentially explore these spaces more actively. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but it remains a current constraint on the agent's ability to handle complex, multi-step recovery scenarios.

### 6.2 Structural Assumptions: The Database Abstraction Boundary

The core innovation of treating every function call as a read-write operation on a database $D$ (Section 2) is powerful, but it rests on a strong assumption that may not hold universally.

*   **The Assumption:** The paper posits that $\text{API}(func, \alpha) \equiv op(func)(\alpha; D)$. This works well for domains like retail or airline bookings where state (orders, flights) is central.
*   **The Limitation:** Not all APIs fit neatly into a **stateful database schema**.
    *   **Stateless Operations:** Some tools perform computations or transformations without persisting state (e.g., currency conversion, image processing). Modeling these as database writes may introduce unnecessary complexity or artificial state.
    *   **External Side Effects:** Real-world APIs often trigger effects outside the immediate system (e.g., sending an email, charging a credit card, triggering a physical device). While the *simulation* can record this in a database, the *agent* trained in simulation may not fully grasp the irreversibility or latency of real-world side effects.
*   **Risk:** An agent trained exclusively on database-grounded simulations might develop a bias towards assuming all operations are instantly reversible or perfectly consistent, which may not hold in deployed environments with eventual consistency or external failures. The paper notes consistency with $\tau$-bench (Section 2.1), but $\tau$-bench itself is a simulated environment. Validation on truly external, stateful APIs remains an open step.

### 6.3 The Simulation Gap: User and Environment Fidelity

The scalability of the framework depends on **simulated human-agent interplay** (Section 3.1). While this enables large-scale data generation, it introduces a "simulation gap" between training and deployment.

*   **Simulated User Quality:** The trajectories are generated by a simulated user tasked with fulfilling an intent. If the simulated user behaves more politely, clearly, or predictably than real humans, the agent may fail when confronted with ambiguous, noisy, or adversarial real-world queries.
    *   **Evidence:** The drop in performance on the `Special` subset of `ACEBench-zh` (Table 2), where `AgentScaler-30B-A3B` scores **83.3** compared to the base model's **86.7**, suggests that generalization is not uniform. This subset may contain nuances or constraints that the simulated user distribution did not adequately cover.
*   **Environment Determinism:** The programmatic environments are deterministic (given the same state and action, the result is identical). Real-world APIs often exhibit non-deterministic behavior (rate limiting, transient errors, varying response times).
    *   **Mitigation:** The authors note in Section 3.1 that they do not filter out trajectories with tool call errors to improve robustness. However, these errors are generated within the *simulated* logic. They may not capture the full spectrum of real-world network failures or API deprecations.

### 6.4 Reproducibility and Transparency Gaps

For a framework centered on systematic engineering, certain implementation details are omitted, which hinders exact reproducibility.

*   **Missing Hyperparameters:** In Section 2.1, the construction of the tool dependency graph relies on a similarity threshold $\tau$:
    $$
    E = \{(i, j) \mid \text{sim}(\phi(P_{func_i}), \phi(P_{func_j})) > \tau, i \neq j\}
    $$
    The paper does not specify the value of $\tau$. Since this threshold directly controls the density of the tool graph and consequently the domain partitions (over 1,000 domains), varying $\tau$ could significantly alter the diversity of the generated environments. Without this value, independent researchers cannot precisely replicate the environment distribution.
*   **Filtering Criteria:** While the three-stage filtering funnel is described (Section 3.1), the specific thresholds for "n-gram-based filtering" to remove repetitive reasoning are not quantified. This makes it difficult to assess how much data was discarded and whether valid but repetitive reasoning patterns were inadvertently removed.

### 6.5 Scale Ceiling and Deployment Reality

The authors position compact models as the future of agentic AI, but there are limits to what environment scaling can achieve without parameter growth.

*   **The 30B Cap:** As stated in Section 7 (Limitation), the method is validated only up to **30B parameters**. It is unproven whether environment scaling yields diminishing returns for models exceeding **200B** or **1T** parameters.
    *   **Trade-off:** The work prioritizes **deployment efficiency** (latency, cost) over **absolute capability ceiling**. For tasks requiring massive world knowledge (e.g., complex legal reasoning combined with tool use), a 30B model—even with superior tool training—may lack the foundational knowledge to formulate the correct intent.
*   **Long-Horizon Fragility:** As analyzed in Section 5.5 (referencing Figure 5), accuracy drops to **0%** for high-complexity tasks in some domains. This suggests that environment scaling alone does not solve the **context window** or **reasoning depth** bottlenecks.
    *   **Open Question:** Can further environment scaling fix this, or is an architectural change (e.g., hierarchical agents, explicit planning modules) required? The current pipeline assumes a single monolithic model handles both planning and execution. If the reasoning capability of the base model is the bottleneck, adding more environments may not help.

### 6.6 Summary of Trade-offs

Table 3 summarizes the key trade-offs inherent in the AgentScaler design. Understanding these is crucial for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this framework.

| Design Choice | Benefit | Trade-off / Limitation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **SFT over RL** | Stable training, high-fidelity data | No optimization for efficiency or novel recovery |
| **DB Abstraction** | Verifiable state, automated scaling | May not fit stateless or external-side-effect APIs |
| **Simulated User** | Scalable data generation | Risk of simulation gap (too clean/predictable) |
| **Compact Models** | Low latency, edge deployment | Lower knowledge ceiling vs. 1T+ models |
| **Automated Graph** | 1,000+ domains without manual work | Reproducibility gaps (missing threshold $\tau$) |

In conclusion, while **AgentScaler** successfully demonstrates that environment scaling is a viable path to robust agentic intelligence, it is not a panacea. The approach excels at standardizing tool use and improving stability in resource-constrained settings but currently relies on assumptions about API structure and user behavior that may not hold in all real-world deployments. Future work integrating RL and validating on external, non-simulated APIs will be critical to determining the true upper bound of this methodology.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work does more than introduce a new training pipeline—it reframes the fundamental bottleneck in agentic intelligence from **model capacity** to **environment diversity**. This section examines how this shift changes the research landscape, what concrete follow-up work it enables, where these methods can be practically deployed, and guidance for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this approach.

### 7.1 Landscape Shift: From Model Scaling to Environment Scaling

The most significant implication of this work is conceptual: it establishes **environment scaling** as a first-class research direction alongside model scaling and data scaling. Prior to this work, the dominant narrative in AI capability improvement centered on increasing parameter counts (from 7B to 70B to 1T+) or expanding training corpora. AgentScaler demonstrates that neither is strictly necessary for agentic tasks.

**Key Landscape Changes:**

| Prior Paradigm | AgentScaler Paradigm |
|----------------|---------------------|
| Bigger models → better agents | Better environments → better agents |
| Manual environment curation | Automated environment construction |
| Validation via LLM judgment | Validation via database state |
| 10–100 domains typical | 1,000+ domains achievable |
| Closed-source advantage assumed | Open-source parity demonstrated |

The evidence supporting this shift is quantitative. As shown in **Table 1**, `AgentScaler-30B-A3B` achieves an **Overall score of 75.7** on ACEBench-en, within 1.7 points of `Kimi-K2-1T-A32B` (77.4) despite having **30–40× fewer parameters**. This is not an incremental improvement—it challenges the assumption that agentic capability scales monotonically with model size.

**Why this matters for the field:**

1.  **Research Resource Allocation:** Labs with limited compute budgets can now compete on agentic tasks by investing in environment infrastructure rather than chasing parameter scale. The 4B model's **+16.4 point gain** (from 49.5 to 65.9) demonstrates that smaller models are highly responsive to environment quality.

2.  **Benchmark Design:** Future agentic benchmarks should emphasize **environment diversity** over task complexity alone. If an agent can be trained on 1,000+ domains, benchmarks with only 2–3 domains (like the original `$\tau$-bench` retail/airline split) may no longer stress-test generalization adequately.

3.  **Evaluation Metrics:** The `$\text{pass}@k$` stability analysis (**Figure 4**) should become standard. A model that solves a task once is less valuable than one that solves it consistently across multiple trials. AgentScaler's sustained gap over the base model at `$k=4$` indicates reduced variance—a critical property for deployment.

4.  **Open-Source Competitiveness:** The work demonstrates that open-source models can approach closed-source performance on agentic tasks without proprietary data. This reduces the moat that closed-source providers have built around function-calling capabilities.

**Critical Perspective:** This shift does not render model scaling obsolete. For tasks requiring massive world knowledge (e.g., legal reasoning combined with tool use, or scientific discovery agents), parameter capacity still matters. Environment scaling addresses the **tool-use bottleneck**, not the **knowledge bottleneck**. The two approaches are complementary, not mutually exclusive.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several open problems in its Limitations section (Section 7), but the framework enables additional research avenues beyond those stated.

#### 7.2.1 Reinforcement Learning Integration (Highest Priority)

The authors state in Section 7:

> "In future work, we aim to integrate RL on top of our simulated environment to further improve the agentic behavior of the model."

This is the most natural and impactful extension. The current pipeline uses **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)** on verified trajectories, which has limitations:

*   **SFT teaches mimicry:** The model learns to reproduce successful trajectories but does not learn to optimize for efficiency (e.g., fewer tool calls) or recover from novel errors.
*   **RL enables exploration:** An RL agent could discover more efficient tool sequences than those in the training data, or learn recovery strategies for errors not seen during SFT.

**Concrete Research Questions:**

*   Does RL on scaled environments close the long-horizon gap shown in **Figure 5** (where accuracy drops to 0% for high-complexity tasks)?
*   Can reward functions be designed to penalize unnecessary tool calls, teaching agents to be more efficient?
*   Does RL improve stability (`$\text{pass}@k$`) beyond what SFT achieves?

**Why the infrastructure supports this:** The simulated environments provide **low-latency, deterministic feedback**—exactly what RL training requires. Real-world API calls would be too slow and costly for the thousands of episodes RL typically needs.

#### 7.2.2 Long-Horizon Tool Calling Solutions

**Figure 5** reveals a fundamental limitation: accuracy declines as tool call count increases. In the Retail domain, accuracy falls from **82%** (low complexity) to **0%** (high complexity). This is not unique to AgentScaler—all models exhibit this trend—but it defines the current capability boundary.

**Potential Research Directions:**

*   **Hierarchical Agents:** Instead of a single model handling all steps, decompose long-horizon tasks into sub-tasks handled by specialized agents. Each sub-agent operates within a shorter horizon, reducing error compounding.
*   **Explicit Planning Modules:** Integrate a separate planning component that generates a high-level plan before tool execution, allowing the model to verify feasibility before committing to actions.
*   **Checkpointing and Rollback:** Leverage the database-backed environment to save state at intermediate steps. If an error occurs, the agent can rollback rather than continuing down a failed path.
*   **Curriculum Learning on Horizon Length:** Train agents progressively on longer horizons, starting with 1–2 tool calls and gradually increasing complexity. This mirrors the two-stage fine-tuning approach but applies it to task length rather than domain specificity.

**Open Question:** Is long-horizon failure a **data problem** (not enough long-horizon examples in training) or a **reasoning problem** (the base model lacks the capacity to plan 10+ steps)? If the former, more environment scaling could help. If the latter, architectural changes are needed.

#### 7.2.3 External API Validation

All environments in this work are **simulated** via database abstraction. While the authors note consistency with `$\tau$-bench` official implementations (Section 2.1), this is still simulation-to-simulation validation.

**Research Gap:** No evaluation on **real-world, external APIs** is presented. This matters because:

*   Real APIs have **rate limiting**, **latency variability**, and **transient errors** that simulations may not capture.
*   Some APIs have **side effects** (sending emails, charging cards) that cannot be fully simulated.
*   API schemas change over time (**versioning**, **deprecation**), requiring agents to adapt.

**Follow-Up Work:** Deploy AgentScaler-trained models in controlled real-world settings (e.g., internal enterprise tools) and measure the **simulation-to-reality gap**. This would validate whether the database abstraction generalizes beyond simulated environments.

#### 7.2.4 Reproducibility and Hyperparameter Transparency

A practical barrier to adoption is the **missing hyperparameter `$\tau$`** in the tool dependency graph construction (Section 2.1):

$$
E = \{(i, j) \mid \text{sim}(\phi(P_{func_i}), \phi(P_{func_j})) > \tau, i \neq j\}
$$

The threshold `$\tau$` directly controls graph density and domain partitions. Without this value, independent researchers cannot replicate the environment distribution.

**Community Need:** The authors should release:
*   The exact value of `$\tau$`
*   The n-gram filtering thresholds for trajectory deduplication (Section 3.1)
*   The maximum execution steps for tool sequence sampling (Section 2.2)

This is not a criticism of the work's validity but a standard expectation for systematic frameworks. The GitHub repository (`https://github.com/Alibaba-NLP/DeepResearch`) may contain these details, but they should be in the paper for archival completeness.

#### 7.2.5 Multi-Modal and Cross-Lingual Extensions

The Conclusion mentions extending the pipeline to **broader modalities**. Current environments are text-based (API parameters and responses are strings/JSON). Future work could explore:

*   **Vision-Language Agents:** Environments where tools process images (e.g., OCR, object detection) and the database schema includes image embeddings.
*   **Code Execution Environments:** Tools that run code snippets, with the database tracking file system state.
*   **Cross-Lingual Training:** The `ACEBench-zh` results (**Table 2**) show gains (+21.7 points for 4B model), but training was English-only. Explicitly multi-lingual environment construction could further improve OOD generalization.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The AgentScaler framework is not purely academic—it has concrete deployment implications, particularly for resource-constrained scenarios.

#### 7.3.1 Edge and Latency-Sensitive Deployments

The **30B-A3B** and **4B** models are specifically designed for efficiency. As stated in Section 7 (Limitation):

> "Such models are easier to deploy on edge devices, enable broader applicability across diverse scenarios, and offer faster response times."

**Use Cases:**

*   **Mobile Agents:** A 4B model can run on-device with quantization, enabling privacy-preserving agentic features (e.g., personal assistant that accesses local calendars, contacts, and files without sending data to the cloud).
*   **Real-Time Customer Support:** Latency-sensitive applications (e.g., live chat refund processing) benefit from smaller models with faster inference. AgentScaler-8B achieves **67.4 Overall** on ACEBench-en, competitive with much larger models.
*   **High-Throughput Enterprise Automation:** Organizations processing thousands of agent interactions per hour can deploy multiple 8B instances rather than a single 1T model, improving throughput and fault isolation.

#### 7.3.2 Domain-Specific Agent Customization

The **two-stage fine-tuning** approach (Section 3.2) enables organizations to customize agents for their vertical:

1.  **Stage 1:** Use the general AgentScaler checkpoint (trained on 1,000+ domains) for foundational tool-use competence.
2.  **Stage 2:** Fine-tune on organization-specific tools (e.g., internal CRM, proprietary APIs) using the same environment construction pipeline.

**Advantage over training from scratch:** The general foundation reduces the amount of domain-specific data needed. An organization with only 50 internal APIs can leverage the general tool-use patterns learned from 30,000+ public APIs.

**Example:** A healthcare provider could:
*   Start with `AgentScaler-30B-A3B`
*   Construct a domain environment for HIPAA-compliant patient record tools
*   Fine-tune Stage 2 on simulated patient interaction trajectories
*   Deploy with confidence that the agent understands tool-calling fundamentals

#### 7.3.3 Agentic Benchmarking Infrastructure

The automated environment construction pipeline can serve as a **benchmarking platform** for evaluating other agentic models:

*   **Standardized Evaluation:** Instead of each lab hand-crafting their own test environments, the AgentScaler pipeline can generate consistent, verifiable benchmarks.
*   **Continuous Benchmarking:** As new APIs are added to the pool, new domains can be generated automatically, preventing benchmark saturation.
*   **Private Evaluation:** Organizations can construct private domains (using internal APIs) to evaluate agents without exposing sensitive data.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

Practitioners considering this approach should understand when it is appropriate and what infrastructure is required.

#### 7.4.1 When to Prefer This Approach

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| **Resource-constrained deployment** | AgentScaler (4B–30B) | Matches 1T performance with 30–40× fewer parameters (**Table 1**) |
| **Domain-specific tools available** | AgentScaler + Stage 2 fine-tuning | Leverages general foundation, specializes for your APIs |
| **Real-world API access is costly** | Simulated environment pipeline | Avoids MCP service costs and latency (Section 6.1) |
| **Verifiability is critical** | Database-backed environments | State-based validation ensures trajectory correctness (Section 2.2) |
| **Long-horizon tasks (>10 steps)** | **Caution advised** | Accuracy degrades significantly (**Figure 5**); consider hierarchical agents |
| **Stateless APIs dominate** | Alternative approach | Database abstraction fits stateful operations better (Section 6.2) |
| **RL optimization is required** | Wait for follow-up work | Current pipeline is SFT-only; RL integration is future work (Section 7) |

#### 7.4.2 Infrastructure Requirements

To replicate or adapt this framework, organizations need:

1.  **API Corpus:** A collection of API definitions (OpenAPI specs, function schemas). The paper used 30,000+ APIs from ToolBench, API-Gen, and internal repositories (Section 2.1).
2.  **Embedding Model:** For parameter similarity computation in tool graph construction. The paper does not specify which embedding model was used—a reproducibility gap.
3.  **LLM for Schema Generation:** An LLM capable of generating database schemas and Python code from API definitions. The authors used Qwen3 variants, but other code-capable models could work.
4.  **Simulation Runtime:** A Python environment to execute the generated tool code and track database state. This is lightweight compared to maintaining MCP services.
5.  **Compute for Fine-Tuning:** The two-stage SFT requires GPU resources proportional to model size. A 30B model needs significantly more compute than a 4B model, but both are feasible on modern hardware.

#### 7.4.3 Integration Checklist

Before adopting this framework, verify:

- [ ] **API Statefulness:** Do your tools modify persistent state? If yes, the database abstraction fits well. If no (e.g., pure computation APIs), consider whether simulation adds value.
- [ ] **Trajectory Length:** Are your typical agent tasks under 10 tool calls? If yes, AgentScaler is suitable. If no, expect degraded performance (Figure 5).
- [ ] **Error Tolerance:** Can your application handle occasional agent failures? AgentScaler improves stability but does not eliminate errors (Figure 4 shows `$\text{pass}@4$` is lower than `$\text{pass}@1$`).
- [ ] **Language Requirements:** Is English sufficient? The `ACEBench-zh` results show cross-lingual transfer, but training was English-only. For non-English deployments, consider multilingual fine-tuning.
- [ ] **Reproducibility Needs:** Can you work with unspecified hyperparameters (e.g., `$\tau$` threshold)? If exact replication is required, contact the authors for missing details.

#### 7.4.4 Comparison to Alternatives

| Alternative | When to Choose It | Trade-offs |
|-------------|-------------------|------------|
| **Real-world API training** | When simulation fidelity is insufficient | Higher cost, latency, and maintenance burden (Section 6.1) |
| **LLM-simulated responses** | When rapid prototyping is needed | Risk of hallucination and inconsistent state (Section 6.1) |
| **Manual environment construction** | When only 5–10 domains are needed | Higher fidelity per domain, but does not scale (Section 1) |
| **RL-based training** | When efficiency optimization is critical | Requires stable environment (AgentScaler provides this) but is not yet implemented |
| **Distillation from larger models** | When a strong teacher model is available | Requires access to teacher; AgentScaler achieves similar gains via environment diversity |

### 7.5 Summary: A New Research Agenda

This work establishes that **agentic intelligence is an infrastructure problem, not solely a model problem**. The implications extend beyond the specific AgentScaler models:

1.  **For Researchers:** Invest in environment construction tooling. The returns on environment diversity may exceed the returns on parameter increases for agentic tasks.
2.  **For Practitioners:** Consider smaller models trained on diverse environments for deployment. The efficiency gains are substantial with minimal capability loss on tool-use tasks.
3.  **For the Community:** Standardize environment benchmarks and share environment construction pipelines. Just as model checkpoints are shared, environment definitions should be reusable.

The open problems—**RL integration**, **long-horizon planning**, **external API validation**, and **reproducibility**—define a clear research agenda for the next 1–3 years. If these challenges are addressed, environment scaling could become as fundamental to agentic AI as transformer architectures are to language modeling today.
