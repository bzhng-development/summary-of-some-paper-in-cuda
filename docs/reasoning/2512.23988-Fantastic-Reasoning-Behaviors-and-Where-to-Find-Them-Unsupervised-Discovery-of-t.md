## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces RISE (Reasoning behavior Interpretability via Sparse auto-Encoder), an unsupervised framework that discovers disentangled reasoning vectors in models like DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B by training Sparse Auto-Encoders on sentence-level activations from the MATH dataset. By identifying directions corresponding to behaviors such as reflection and confidence without human supervision, the method enables targeted interventions that controllably alter inference trajectories, such as reducing reflection steps from 90.53 to 33.77 on AIME25 tasks. This solves the scalability bottleneck of label-dependent interpretability, offering a path to fine-grained test-time adaptation and deeper understanding of LLM reasoning mechanisms.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Understanding Black-Box Reasoning

Modern large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable performance on complex reasoning tasks—competition-level mathematics, project-scale coding, and multi-step planning. However, a fundamental question remains unanswered: **what actually happens inside the model during these extended reasoning trajectories?** 

When a model like DeepSeek-R1 produces a 5000-token chain-of-thought solution, we observe the output tokens, but the internal cognitive mechanisms remain opaque. The paper identifies a critical gap: while we know that *longer* reasoning traces generally improve performance, we lack systematic tools to understand *which specific behaviors* within those traces drive success, and how those behaviors are encoded in the model's activation space.

This is not merely an academic curiosity. Consider two practical scenarios:

1. **Debugging failures**: If a model consistently fails on certain math problems, is it because it skips verification steps? Does it backtrack too early? Without interpretable representations of reasoning behaviors, practitioners cannot diagnose or fix these issues systematically.

2. **Efficiency optimization**: Recent work suggests that much of the reasoning output is verbose or ineffective. If we could identify and suppress unproductive behaviors while amplifying useful ones, we could reduce token costs without sacrificing accuracy.

### Why Prior Approaches Fall Short

The paper identifies a fundamental limitation in existing interpretability methods: **they rely on human-supervised labels**. To understand why this matters, consider the dominant approach in activation steering literature—the **Difference-of-Means (DiffMean)** method (Marks and Tegmark, 2023).

#### The DiffMean Approach and Its Limitations

The DiffMean method operates as follows:

$$v = \frac{1}{N^+}\sum_{i=1}^{N^+} h^+_i - \frac{1}{N^-}\sum_{j=1}^{N^-} h^-_j$$

where $h^+_i$ and $h^-_j$ are latent representations from two contrasting categories (e.g., "happy" vs. "sad" responses). The resulting steering vector $v$ can then shift model behavior during inference.

This works well for **clearly oppositional concepts** like sentiment classification, where "happy" and "sad" are semantically distinct and easily annotated. However, reasoning behaviors present three fundamental challenges:

| Challenge | Why It Matters |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Fluidity** | Reflection and backtracking overlap—a model may verify a step (reflection) while simultaneously considering alternatives (backtracking). |
| **Context-dependence** | The same token sequence can represent different behaviors depending on prior reasoning context. |
| **Annotation scale** | Manually labeling thousands of reasoning steps across multiple behavior categories is prohibitively expensive and error-prone. |

The paper notes that prior studies have focused "narrowly on a small set of predefined behaviors such as reflection or backtracking" (Section 1). This creates a **discovery ceiling**: you can only find behaviors you already know to look for. If a model develops novel reasoning strategies not captured by existing taxonomies, supervised methods will miss them entirely.

#### The SAE Alternative—and Its Prior Limitations

Sparse Auto-Encoders (SAEs) offer a principled alternative. Rather than requiring labeled contrastive pairs, SAEs learn a dictionary of sparse latent features that reconstruct hidden activations while enforcing disentanglement. The key insight from the **Linear Representation Hypothesis** (Park et al., 2023) is that atomic concepts correspond to specific directions in activation space.

However, prior work using SAEs for reasoning (e.g., Wang et al., 2025a) treated them primarily as "a bridge to transfer reasoning ability from a reasoning model to a base model via supervised fine-tuning." This misses the core interpretability opportunity: **using SAE decoder columns directly as interpretable reasoning vectors** without additional training or labels.

### How RISE Positions Itself

The RISE framework positions itself at the intersection of three research threads:

1. **Mechanistic Interpretability**: Like activation steering work, RISE seeks to identify causal directions in activation space. However, it removes the supervision requirement entirely.

2. **Reasoning Analysis**: Like studies examining reflection and backtracking (Chen et al., 2025; Venhoff et al., 2025), RISE analyzes reasoning behaviors. However, it discovers these behaviors unsupervised rather than relying on human-defined categories.

3. **Test-Time Control**: Like methods that modify inference without retraining (Hu et al., 2025), RISE enables on-the-fly steering. However, it derives steering vectors from unsupervised latent discovery rather than contrastive datasets.

The paper's key positioning claim is that **unsupervised discovery scales better than supervised annotation** for reasoning behaviors. As stated in Section 1: "reasoning behaviors are fluid, overlapping, and difficult to annotate at scale." By training SAEs directly on step-level activations from chain-of-thought traces, RISE captures the full spectrum of reasoning behaviors—including those humans haven't yet named or recognized.

### Theoretical Significance: Extending the Linear Representation Hypothesis

Beyond practical applications, this work has theoretical implications for how we understand LLM representations. The Linear Representation Hypothesis posits that concepts are encoded as linear directions in activation space. Prior validations focused on concrete concepts (gender, sentiment, truthfulness). 

RISE extends this hypothesis to **abstract cognitive patterns**—reasoning behaviors like reflection, backtracking, and confidence. If successful, this demonstrates that even high-level metacognitive processes admit linear encodings, which has profound implications for both interpretability and model editing. The paper's Theorem 1 (Section 4.1) provides theoretical grounding: under sparsity and incoherence assumptions, SAE decoder columns recover the true dictionary of behavior directions up to permutation and scaling.

### Real-World Impact: From Interpretation to Control

The motivation extends beyond understanding to **controllability**. If reasoning vectors are truly disentangled and interpretable, practitioners should be able to:

- **Amplify useful behaviors**: Increase reflection on difficult problems where verification matters.
- **Suppress costly behaviors**: Reduce unnecessary backtracking on straightforward queries.
- **Discover new behaviors**: Identify confidence-related patterns that humans cannot easily define at the token level.

Section 5 demonstrates this by discovering "confidence vectors" that reduce entropy without explicit supervision—behaviors that would be nearly impossible to annotate manually. The paper shows these vectors generalize across domains (Table 2), suggesting the discovered representations capture fundamental reasoning mechanisms rather than dataset-specific artifacts.

In summary, RISE addresses a scalability bottleneck in reasoning interpretability: supervised methods cannot keep pace with the complexity and fluidity of model-internal reasoning behaviors. By shifting to unsupervised latent discovery, the framework opens a path to systematic mapping and control of the reasoning process—without requiring humans to predefine what they're looking for.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a mechanistic interpretability paper that proposes an unsupervised method for discovering and controlling reasoning behaviors in large language models by training Sparse Auto-Encoders on chain-of-thought activations. The core idea is that distinct reasoning behaviors—such as reflection, backtracking, and confidence—correspond to specific linear directions in the model's activation space, and these directions can be identified without human labels by enforcing sparsity during representation learning.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The RISE system is a two-stage pipeline that first learns a compressed dictionary of reasoning behavior vectors from unlabeled model activations, then uses those vectors to directly modify how a model reasons during inference without any additional training. It solves the problem that reasoning behaviors are too fluid and overlapping to label manually by instead letting the model's own activation patterns reveal which directions in its internal space correspond to which cognitive operations.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The RISE framework consists of four major components connected in sequence. First, the **Thought Representation Constructor** takes chain-of-thought outputs from a reasoning model and segments them into sentence-level steps, extracting the hidden activation at each step boundary. Second, the **Sparse Auto-Encoder** trains on these activations to learn a dictionary of sparse latent features, where each decoder column represents a candidate reasoning vector. Third, the **Behavior Analyzer** maps decoder columns to interpretable behaviors through clustering and human evaluation, identifying which vectors correspond to reflection, backtracking, or other patterns. Fourth, the **Intervention Module** injects selected decoder columns into the model's hidden states during inference, amplifying or suppressing specific behaviors by modifying activations along those directions. Information flows from raw model outputs through representation extraction, then through unsupervised feature learning, then through behavioral interpretation, and finally back into the model for controlled inference.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with **thought representation construction** because understanding what data the SAE trains on is prerequisite to understanding what it learns.
- We then explain the **SAE architecture and training objective** since this is the core mechanism that discovers reasoning vectors from the activations.
- We cover **theoretical justification** next because Theorem 1 explains why the SAE decoder columns should recover true behavior directions under specific conditions.
- We detail the **intervention mechanism** afterward since this shows how discovered vectors are actually used to control model behavior.
- We conclude with **confidence vector discovery** as an advanced application that demonstrates the framework can find behaviors beyond human-defined categories.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**What type of paper is this and the core idea.** This is a mechanistic interpretability paper that demonstrates reasoning behaviors in large language models can be discovered unsupervised by training Sparse Auto-Encoders on chain-of-thought activations, with each decoder column corresponding to an interpretable reasoning vector that can be used for test-time intervention.

**System and data pipeline: what happens first, second, third.** The pipeline begins by collecting chain-of-thought responses from a reasoning model on math problems, then segments each response into sentence-level reasoning steps using the newline delimiter `<\n\n>`, then extracts the residual stream activation at each delimiter token position, then trains an SAE on these activations to learn sparse latent features, then analyzes decoder columns to identify behavior-specific vectors, and finally applies those vectors during inference to modify reasoning trajectories.

**Thought representation construction at sentence level.** The paper segments reasoning traces at the sentence level rather than the token level because the same token can play diverse roles across different contexts, making token-level attributes insufficient for explaining reasoning behaviors. Each response is divided into $k$ steps per sample using the `<\n\n>` delimiter symbol, where $k$ varies across samples depending on response length. For each step, the system re-runs inference by feeding both the question and the corresponding response into the model, then extracts the hidden representation of the `<\n\n>` token that marks the step boundary. Each extracted representation is regarded as the activation of its corresponding reasoning step, following the approach of Chen et al. (2025). The representations are concatenated and denoted as $\{h_i^l\}$, where $i \in \{1, \ldots, N\}$ indexes the samples and $l$ denotes the layer index. The paper specifically uses residual stream representations after each transformer layer and trains the SAE on a single chosen layer rather than across multiple layers simultaneously.

**Why sentence-level rather than token-level.** Token-level analysis would conflate different reasoning behaviors that use identical surface words—a model might say "Wait" during genuine reflection in one context but as a filler phrase in another. Sentence-level representations capture the semantic intent of a complete reasoning step, allowing the SAE to learn features that correspond to coherent cognitive operations rather than superficial token patterns. This design choice is motivated by recent studies (Venhoff et al., 2025; Ward et al., 2025) showing that reasoning behaviors are more meaningfully captured at the step level where internal structure can be preserved.

**Sparse Auto-Encoder architecture.** The SAE consists of an encoder and decoder that aim to reconstruct representations in the activation space while enforcing sparsity in the latent codes. The encoder computes latent features as $z = \sigma(W_{\text{encoder}}^T h + b_{\text{encoder}})$, where $h \in \mathbb{R}^d$ denotes the original representation from the model, $\sigma$ is a non-linear activation function, and $z$ represents the corresponding sparse code. The decoder reconstructs the activation as $\hat{h} = W_{\text{decoder}}^T \sigma(z) + b_{\text{decoder}}$, where $\hat{h}$ is the reconstruction. Each row of $W_{\text{decoder}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times d}$ corresponds to an atomic vector, which the paper defines as a reasoning vector, where $D$ is the hidden dimension of the SAE and $d$ is the dimension of the original input. For a standard SAE following Cunningham et al. (2023), the paper uses ReLU as the non-linear activation function $\sigma$.

**SAE training objective and sparsity enforcement.** The training objective is formulated as $\mathcal{L} = \|\hat{h} - h\|_2^2 + \lambda\|z\|_0$, which encourages the SAE to accurately reconstruct the original activations while enforcing sparsity in the latent codes. The first term $\|\hat{h} - h\|_2^2$ is the reconstruction loss that ensures the SAE preserves information from the original activations. The second term $\lambda\|z\|_0$ is the sparsity penalty that encourages only a small number of latent features to be active for any given input. This sparsity constraint ensures that only a small number of atomic concepts are used per activation, thereby reducing entanglement across vectors and enhancing interpretability. The sparsity strength is set to $\lambda = 2 \times 10^{-3}$, which balances reconstruction accuracy against feature sparsity.

**Why SAEs over other unsupervised methods.** SAEs are chosen over alternatives like PCA or standard autoencoders because the sparsity constraint produces disentangled features where each latent dimension corresponds to a single interpretable concept rather than a mixture. Standard autoencoders without sparsity penalties tend to learn distributed representations where multiple concepts are encoded across many latent dimensions, making it difficult to isolate specific behaviors. The $\ell_0$ sparsity penalty ensures that for any given reasoning step, only a few reasoning vectors are active, which aligns with the intuition that a single step typically exhibits one or two dominant cognitive behaviors rather than all behaviors simultaneously.

**Key hyperparameters and training configuration.** The paper uses DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B (R1-1.5B) as the primary model, along with five hundred randomly sampled training examples from the MATH dataset (Lightman et al., 2023). Since the goal is to model various reasoning behaviors whose complexity is much lower than modeling raw language structure, the paper adopts a relatively small hidden dimension of $D = 2048$ for the SAE. The SAE is trained with a batch size of 1024 and a learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$, with a warm-up over the initial 10% of training steps. The paper uses the Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014) with cosine annealing learning rate decay. The analysis is extended to Qwen3-8B with results reported in Figure 5, demonstrating cross-model applicability.

**Theoretical justification: Theorem 1.** The paper provides theoretical grounding for why SAE decoder columns should recover true behavior directions through Theorem 1 in Section 4.1. The theorem assumes hidden representations at delimiter tokens follow the generative model $h = Wa + \varepsilon$, where $W = [w_1, \ldots, w_m] \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times m}$ is a dictionary of latent behavior directions, $a$ is a $k$-sparse code, and $\varepsilon$ is bounded noise. Four key assumptions are required: (i) Incoherence, meaning $\max_{i \neq j} \frac{|\langle w_i, w_j \rangle|}{\|w_i\|\|w_j\|} \leq \mu < 1$, ensuring behavior directions are not too correlated; (ii) Sparsity, meaning $k < c/\mu$ for a universal constant $c$, ensuring only a few behaviors are active per step; (iii) Activation, meaning the SAE uses ReLU nonlinearity; and (iv) Separation, meaning nonzero coefficients satisfy $|a_i| \geq \alpha > 0$, ensuring active behaviors have sufficient magnitude. Under these assumptions, as the number of samples $N \to \infty$, any local optimum of the SAE training objective $\min_{W_{\text{enc}}, W_{\text{dec}}} \mathbb{E}[\|h - \hat{h}\|_2^2] + \lambda\|z\|_0$ recovers a decoder matrix whose columns align with the true dictionary up to permutation matrix $\Pi$ and scaling diagonal matrix $D$: $\exists \Pi, D \succ 0 \text{ s.t. } W_{\text{dec}} \approx W\Pi D$.

**What Theorem 1 means in plain language.** The theorem guarantees that if reasoning behaviors are truly encoded as sparse linear combinations of underlying direction vectors in the activation space, then training an SAE will recover those direction vectors in its decoder columns. The permutation and scaling ambiguity is acceptable because it does not affect the ability to identify and intervene on specific behaviors—the geometry of the directions is preserved even if their ordering and magnitude in the decoder differ from the ground truth. Appendix C provides empirical validation showing that the maximum cosine similarity among recovered dictionary vectors is bounded and the sparsity level $k$ is within theoretical bounds, supporting the assumptions underlying the analysis.

**Intervention mechanism during inference.** Once reasoning vectors are identified in the SAE decoder space, they can be applied during inference to modify model behavior without retraining. All decoder columns are independently normalized to unit length $\|w_i\|_2 = 1$ before intervention to ensure consistent scaling across vectors. The intervention is performed as $h' = h - w_i(w_i^T h)$, where $w_i$ denotes decoder column $i$ and $h$ is the original hidden representation. This equation projects out the component of $h$ along the reflection direction to examine how the reflection behavior changes in the model's responses. The intervention is applied to the hidden representation of the last token at each reasoning step, as shown in Figure 4.

**Controlling intervention strength with scaling.** The intervention strength can be manually controlled with a scalar value $\alpha$ as $h' = h - \alpha \cdot w_i(w_i^T h)$, where $\alpha \in \{-1.5, -1, 0, 1, 1.5\}$. Negative values of $\alpha$ amplify the behavior by adding the vector component, while positive values suppress it by subtracting. When applied to DeepSeek-R1-1.5B on AIME25 tasks, the corresponding number of reflection steps changes to $\{58.6, 73.6, 90.5, 131.0, 166.9\}$ for $\alpha \in \{-1.5, -1, 0, 1, 1.5\}$, consistently evolving in accordance with the intervention strength. This demonstrates that the intervention has a causal, dose-dependent effect on reasoning behavior rather than producing binary on/off changes.

**Why projection-based intervention rather than addition.** The projection-based formula $h' = h - w_i(w_i^T h)$ removes the component of the activation along the behavior direction, effectively suppressing that behavior. This differs from simple addition $h' = h + \alpha w_i$ because it accounts for how much of that direction is already present in the activation. The paper filters out decoder columns that exhibit strong activations across multiple behaviors before computing intervention vectors, ensuring that selected vectors are behavior-specific rather than general-purpose. For behaviors like reflection, the paper computes the average of reflection-specific columns to obtain a single reflection vector, then applies this centroid during inference.

**Confidence vector discovery via entropy minimization.** Beyond human-defined behaviors, the SAE can discover novel reasoning patterns by optimizing for downstream objectives. The paper identifies confidence-related vectors by minimizing output entropy, since recent studies highlight that entropy (or equivalently model confidence) plays a critical role during reasoning. Given a trained SAE with decoder $W_{\text{decoder}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times d}$, where $d$ denotes the dimension of the original model and $D$ is the number of learned vectors, the paper assigns a score vector $S \in \mathbb{R}^D$ to the $D$ decoder columns and optimizes $S$ by minimizing the final entropy. The objective is $\arg\min_S \mathbb{E}\left[-\sum_{k=1}^{|V|} p_k \log p_k\right]$, where $p_k = \text{softmax}(f_{l \to L}(h + SW_{\text{decoder}}))_k$, $p_k$ denotes the predicted probability of token $k$, and $|V|$ is the vocabulary size. This optimization is solved using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.01 for 1000 iterations, together with a cosine annealing learning rate decay schedule and batch size of 256.

**How confidence vectors are identified and applied.** The columns with the highest scores in $S$ are selected, implying that these columns play a critical role in achieving low entropy. The corresponding vector is referred to as the confidence reasoning vector and is injected during inference of the original model following the same procedure described in Section 4.4. Figure 7(a) shows that confidence-related columns are predominantly located in the bottom-right region of the SAE decoder visualization, suggesting this area is particularly important for reasoning confidence. These columns partially overlap with the reflection and backtracking regions identified in Figure 2, indicating that reflection and backtracking behaviors may contribute to higher entropy.

**Sample-dependent steering for reasoning enhancement.** The paper explores a more sophisticated application where multiple confidence vectors are combined with learned coefficients at test time. The top-3 confidence vectors $c_1, c_2, c_3$ are selected, and their combination coefficients are learned during the prefilling stage to control the reasoning process. This yields a sample-dependent steering vector $\sum_{i \in \{1,2,3\}} \alpha_i c_i$, where the coefficients $\alpha_i$ are optimized per input rather than fixed globally. As shown in Figure 8, this careful steering strategy improves reasoning accuracy by up to 4.66 points on MATH500 while achieving a 13.69% reduction in token usage compared to vanilla inference. This demonstrates that RISE enables not just interpretation but active enhancement of reasoning performance through interpretability-guided intervention.

**Design choice: why train on a single layer.** The paper trains the SAE on a single chosen layer rather than across all layers because different layers encode different levels of abstraction, and reasoning behaviors become more separable in mid-to-late layers. Figure 3 shows that later layers generally achieve higher Silhouette scores than earlier ones, indicating that behavioral concepts become more separable as representations deepen. A slight decline occurs near the final layers (except for the very last one), suggesting that mid-to-late representations encode the most distinct behavioral structures. This observation aligns with the oversmoothing phenomenon in current LLMs (Wang et al., 2023), where token representations become excessively similar in the final layers. Training on the layer with peak separability maximizes the SAE's ability to learn disentangled behavior vectors.

**Design choice: why D=2048 for the SAE hidden dimension.** The hidden dimension $D = 2048$ is chosen because the complexity of modeling reasoning behaviors is much lower than modeling raw language structure. A smaller dictionary size encourages the SAE to learn coarse-grained behavior categories rather than fine-grained token-level features. This is appropriate because the paper's goal is to capture distinct reasoning behaviors like reflection and backtracking, which are relatively high-level cognitive operations that do not require thousands of latent dimensions to represent. The empirical results in Figure 2 show that this dimension is sufficient to produce clearly separable clusters for different behavior types.

**Generalization across domains and models.** The SAE columns learned from the MATH500 dataset are applied to other domains without retraining to test generalization. Table 1 shows results on GPQA-Diamond and KnowLogic, which represent commonsense and logical reasoning tasks with larger domain shift. The reflection and backtracking vectors consistently modulate reasoning behaviors across domains, with positive intervention increasing reflection from 53.23 to 62.77 on GPQA-Diamond and from 35.56 to 51.00 on KnowLogic. Table 2 shows similar generalization for confidence vectors, demonstrating that the discovered representations capture fundamental reasoning mechanisms rather than dataset-specific artifacts. Figure 5 extends this to Qwen3-8B, showing the approach transfers across model scales.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the fundamental contributions that distinguish RISE from prior interpretability work. Rather than incremental improvements to existing methods, RISE introduces capabilities that were previously unavailable for reasoning analysis. We distinguish between **fundamental innovations** (new capabilities that change what is possible) and **significant advances** (substantial improvements to existing approaches).

### 4.1 Fundamental Innovation: Unsupervised Discovery Removes the Label Bottleneck

**What prior work required:** All previous activation steering methods, including the widely-used DiffMean approach (Marks and Tegmark, 2023), depend on human-supervised contrastive datasets. To find a "reflection vector," researchers must manually label thousands of reasoning steps as "reflective" or "non-reflective," then compute the difference of means between these groups. This creates a **discovery ceiling**: you can only find behaviors you already know how to define and annotate.

**What RISE enables:** By training SAEs directly on unlabeled step-level activations, RISE discovers reasoning vectors without any human labels. The sparsity constraint forces the SAE to learn disentangled features, and the decoder columns naturally align with interpretable behaviors. Figure 2 demonstrates that when human labels are overlaid post-hoc, reflection and backtracking behaviors occupy separable regions in the decoder space—despite the SAE never seeing these labels during training.

**Why this is fundamental:** This shifts the paradigm from **hypothesis-driven** interpretability (test whether known behaviors exist) to **discovery-driven** interpretability (let the model reveal what behaviors exist). The scalability implication is profound: supervised methods require O(N × M) labeling effort where N is the number of samples and M is the number of behavior categories. RISE requires O(N) data collection with zero labeling. As reasoning models develop increasingly sophisticated cognitive strategies, unsupervised discovery becomes the only viable path to comprehensive behavioral mapping.

**Evidence of success:** The Silhouette scores in Figure 3 quantify this discovery quality. Later layers achieve normalized scores approaching 1.0 for separating reflection/backtracking from "other" behaviors, indicating the unsupervised clusters align strongly with human-interpretable categories. This validates that the SAE is not learning arbitrary features but capturing semantically meaningful structure.

### 4.2 Fundamental Innovation: Discovery of Behaviors Beyond Human Taxonomy

**What prior work could not do:** Supervised methods are inherently limited to behaviors humans can define at the token level. Concepts like "confidence" or "uncertainty" are notoriously difficult to annotate reliably—different annotators disagree on whether a phrase like "I think" indicates confidence or hesitation. Prior work either avoided such concepts entirely or relied on coarse proxies (e.g., response length as a stand-in for deliberation).

**What RISE enables:** Section 5 demonstrates that SAEs can discover **confidence-related vectors** by optimizing for low output entropy—a proxy objective that requires no behavioral labels. The optimization in Equation (7) identifies decoder columns that minimize prediction uncertainty, revealing a concentrated region in the SAE space (Figure 7a) that corresponds to confident reasoning styles.

**Why this is fundamental:** This demonstrates **objective-driven discovery** rather than **label-driven discovery**. Instead of asking "does the model have a confidence behavior?", RISE asks "what activation patterns produce low-entropy outputs?" and discovers the answer empirically. The intervention results show this is not merely correlational: injecting confidence vectors reduces reflection steps from 90.53 to 33.77 and backtracking from 35.50 to 5.93 on AIME25 tasks (Section 5.1). This causal effect confirms the discovered vectors encode genuine reasoning mechanisms, not superficial token patterns.

**The broader implication:** If SAEs can discover confidence without labels, they can potentially discover any behavior that has a measurable downstream effect—efficiency, creativity, risk-aversion, or domain-specific strategies. This opens a research direction where interpretability tools actively search for behaviors that optimize specific objectives, rather than passively cataloging human-defined categories.

### 4.3 Significant Advance: Test-Time Controllability with Cross-Domain Generalization

**What prior work achieved:** Activation steering methods can modify model behavior during inference, but their generalization is typically limited. DiffMean vectors trained on one dataset often fail to transfer to new domains because they capture dataset-specific artifacts rather than fundamental mechanisms. For example, a "truthfulness" vector trained on factual QA may not generalize to mathematical reasoning.

**What RISE achieves:** The SAE columns learned from MATH500 generalize to GPQA-Diamond and KnowLogic without retraining (Table 1 and Table 2). On GPQA-Diamond, positive intervention increases reflection from 53.23 to 62.77, while on KnowLogic it increases from 35.56 to 51.00. This **cross-domain transfer** indicates the discovered vectors capture reasoning mechanisms that are model-intrinsic rather than dataset-specific.

**Why this is significant:** Generalization validates that RISE discovers **causal mechanisms** rather than **spurious correlations**. If the reflection vector merely captured math-specific phrases, it would not modulate behavior on commonsense reasoning tasks. The fact that it does suggests the vector encodes a genuine cognitive operation that the model applies across domains. This has practical implications: practitioners can train SAEs once on a representative dataset and deploy the resulting vectors across diverse applications without per-domain customization.

**The sample-dependent steering extension:** Figure 8 shows an advanced application where top-3 confidence vectors are combined with learned coefficients per input. This achieves 4.66 accuracy points improvement on MATH500 with 13.69% token reduction. This demonstrates RISE enables **adaptive control**—different inputs receive different steering based on their characteristics—rather than one-size-fits-all intervention.

### 4.4 Theoretical Insight: Structural and Semantic Organization Coexist in Latent Space

**What was previously unknown:** Prior interpretability work typically treats semantic behaviors (reflection, backtracking) and structural properties (response length, token count) as separate phenomena. Some studies examine one or the other, but none demonstrate how they relate in the model's internal representation.

**What RISE reveals:** Appendix E shows that SAE decoder columns organize along **both** semantic and structural axes simultaneously. Figure 11 displays UMAP visualizations where short and long response columns form distinct clusters, with separation peaking in mid-to-late layers. Figure 12 quantifies this with Silhouette scores that mirror the behavioral separation pattern in Figure 3.

**Why this insight matters:** This suggests reasoning models encode a **joint semantic-structural space** where cognitive operations and output characteristics are intertwined. The overlap between confidence vectors and reflection/backtracking regions (Figure 7a) further supports this: behaviors that increase deliberation (reflection, backtracking) also increase entropy and response length. This has implications for efficiency optimization—suppressing verbose outputs may inadvertently suppress useful verification behaviors if the structural and semantic dimensions are coupled.

**The layer-wise pattern:** Both behavioral and structural separation peak in mid-to-late layers, with slight decline near the final layers. This aligns with the oversmoothing phenomenon (Wang et al., 2023) and suggests an optimal intervention point exists. Practitioners should target layers where separability is maximized rather than assuming later is always better.

### 4.5 Distinguishing Incremental from Fundamental Contributions

| Contribution | Type | Rationale |
|--------------|------|-----------|
| Unsupervised behavior discovery | Fundamental | Removes label dependency entirely; enables discovery of unknown behaviors |
| Confidence vector discovery | Fundamental | Demonstrates objective-driven discovery beyond human-defined taxonomy |
| Cross-domain generalization | Significant | Shows vectors capture model-intrinsic mechanisms, but builds on existing steering concepts |
| Structural-semantic organization | Theoretical Insight | Reveals new understanding of how reasoning is encoded, but does not enable new capabilities directly |
| Sample-dependent steering | Significant Advance | Improves control precision, but extends existing intervention frameworks |

**The core differentiator:** The fundamental innovations (unsupervised discovery and novel behavior identification) change **what questions we can ask** about model reasoning. Prior work could only ask "does behavior X exist?" where X is human-defined. RISE enables questions like "what behaviors does this model exhibit?" and "what activation patterns optimize objective Y?" This shifts interpretability from a verification tool to a discovery tool.

**What remains incremental:** The intervention mechanism itself (modifying activations during inference) builds on established activation steering literature. The theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) adapts existing sparse recovery guarantees to the reasoning domain. These are significant validations but not paradigm shifts.

**The practical implication:** For practitioners, the fundamental innovations mean RISE can be deployed in settings where labeling is infeasible—proprietary models without annotation access, emerging behaviors not yet catalogued, or rapid iteration where labeling cannot keep pace with model updates. The significant advances mean the resulting vectors are reliable across domains and can be tuned per-input for optimal performance.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their claims about RISE. We will walk through the evaluation methodology, analyze the quantitative results with specific numbers, and critically assess whether the experiments convincingly support the paper's core contributions. The analysis is organized to mirror the logical progression of the paper's claims: first, does the SAE actually discover interpretable behaviors? Second, can those behaviors be controllably modified? Third, do the discovered vectors generalize beyond the training domain? Finally, can the framework discover behaviors beyond human supervision?

### 5.1 Evaluation Setup and Methodology

#### Models and Datasets

The primary experimental model is **DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B** (R1-1.5B), a reasoning-capable distilled model. The authors extend their analysis to **Qwen3-8B** to demonstrate cross-model applicability (Figure 5). This two-model approach is important: the 1.5B model allows for computationally feasible SAE training and intervention, while the 8B model tests whether findings scale to larger architectures.

For training the SAE, the authors use **500 randomly sampled examples from the MATH dataset** (Lightman et al., 2023). This is a relatively small training set, which raises an immediate question: is 500 examples sufficient to learn robust reasoning behavior vectors? The paper addresses this implicitly through the generalization experiments—if the vectors were overfit to the training data, they would not transfer to new domains.

For evaluation, the authors test across six distinct datasets representing different reasoning domains:

| Dataset | Domain | Purpose |
|---------|--------|---------|
| MATH500 | Mathematics | Primary evaluation benchmark |
| AIME 2024 | Competition Math | High-difficulty reasoning |
| AIME 2025 | Competition Math | High-difficulty reasoning (temporal holdout) |
| AMC23 | Competition Math | Alternative competition format |
| GPQA-Diamond | Graduate-level QA | Commonsense and expert knowledge |
| KnowLogic | Logical Reasoning | Knowledge-driven synthesis |

The inclusion of GPQA-Diamond and KnowLogic is particularly important for testing **domain shift**. These datasets represent commonsense and logical reasoning rather than pure mathematics, providing a stringent test of whether the discovered vectors capture model-intrinsic reasoning mechanisms rather than math-specific patterns.

#### Metrics and Measurement

The paper employs three categories of metrics:

**1. Clustering Quality Metrics.** To quantify how well the SAE decoder columns separate different behaviors, the authors use **Silhouette scores** (Rousseeuw, 1987). This metric measures both the cohesion of samples within a cluster and their separation from other clusters, normalized to the range [0, 1]. Higher scores indicate better-defined, more separable clusters. The authors normalize raw scores across layers for visualization (Figure 3, Figure 12).

**2. Behavior Count Metrics.** For intervention experiments, the primary metric is the **number of reasoning steps** exhibiting specific behaviors (reflection, backtracking). These counts are obtained through LLM-as-a-judge annotation using GPT-5 with precise behavioral definitions (Appendix D). For example, reflection is defined as "step checking its previous reasoning process and stating its own uncertainty," while backtracking is "steps that explicitly retract/pivot, proposing an alternative strategy."

**3. Performance Metrics.** For reasoning enhancement experiments, the authors measure **accuracy (%)** and **token cost** (total tokens generated). This dual metric captures the trade-off between performance and efficiency—a critical consideration for practical deployment.

#### Baselines and Comparisons

For the reasoning enhancement experiments (Figure 8), the authors compare against two recent activation steering methods:

- **SEAL** (Chen et al., 2025): Steerable reasoning calibration method
- **TIP** (Wang et al., 2025d): Thoughts are all over the place method

These baselines represent the state-of-the-art in reasoning intervention, making them appropriate comparators. However, it is important to note that both SEAL and TIP rely on supervised signals or predefined behavior categories, whereas RISE's confidence vectors are discovered through entropy minimization without behavioral labels.

#### SAE Training Configuration

The SAE is trained with the following hyperparameters (Section 4.2):

- Hidden dimension: $D = 2048$
- Batch size: $1024$
- Learning rate: $1 \times 10^{-4}$ with cosine annealing decay
- Warm-up: Initial 10% of training steps
- Sparsity strength: $\lambda = 2 \times 10^{-3}$
- Optimizer: Adam (Kingma, 2014)

The choice of $D = 2048$ is justified by the authors as appropriate for modeling reasoning behaviors, whose complexity is "much lower than modeling raw language structure." This is a non-trivial design choice: a larger dictionary might capture finer-grained behaviors but risks overfitting, while a smaller dictionary might miss important distinctions. The empirical results in Figure 2 suggest this dimension is sufficient to produce clearly separable clusters.

### 5.2 Behavior Discovery and Visualization Results

#### Do SAE Decoder Columns Encode Interpretable Behaviors?

The first claim to validate is whether the unsupervised SAE training actually discovers interpretable reasoning behaviors. Figure 2 presents UMAP visualizations of the SAE decoder columns projected onto a 2-D plane. The visualization uses cosine similarity as the internal metric, emphasizing directional rather than Euclidean differences—this is appropriate because activation vectors are primarily meaningful in terms of their direction rather than magnitude.

The key observation from Figure 2 is that when human-labeled behavioral concepts are overlaid on the unsupervised clusters, **clear semantic structures emerge**:

- **Reflection behaviors** cluster in localized regions (second subfigure)
- **Backtracking behaviors** also form tight clusters (third subfigure)
- **"Other" behaviors** are more dispersed across the entire SAE space (fourth subfigure)

This pattern is significant because the SAE was never trained on these labels. The fact that human-interpretable categories align with unsupervised clusters suggests the SAE is capturing genuine structure in the activation space rather than arbitrary features.

#### Quantifying Cluster Separability: Silhouette Scores

Figure 3 quantifies the visual patterns from Figure 3 using normalized Silhouette scores across different layers of R1-1.5B. The results reveal two important patterns:

**Pattern 1: Layer-wise separability increases then plateaus.** Later layers generally achieve higher Silhouette scores than earlier ones, indicating that behavioral concepts become more separable as representations deepen. However, a slight decline occurs near the final layers (except for the very last one). The authors attribute this to the **oversmoothing phenomenon** (Wang et al., 2023), where token representations become excessively similar in the final layers. This has a practical implication: practitioners should target mid-to-late layers for intervention rather than assuming later is always better.

**Pattern 2: Reflection and backtracking overlap more than expected.** The comparison between reflection and backtracking reveals only modest differences in Silhouette scores, whereas the separation of "other" behaviors from either reflection or backtracking is substantially stronger. This suggests that reflection and backtracking occupy **more overlapping representational subspaces** than either does with the residual "other" category. This is intuitively sensible: both reflection and backtracking are metacognitive operations that involve re-evaluating prior reasoning, whereas "other" encompasses forward-progressing computation.

#### Annotation Consistency Validation

A potential weakness in the behavioral analysis is the reliance on LLM-as-a-judge annotation. If the annotations are inconsistent, the claimed alignment between SAE clusters and human behaviors becomes questionable. Appendix D addresses this by comparing annotation results across multiple methods:

- **GPT-5 vs. GPT-4o**: ~94% agreement
- **GPT-5 vs. Keywords**: ~86% agreement
- **GPT-4o vs. Keywords**: ~85% agreement

All pairwise comparisons exceed 85% agreement (Figure 10), validating the reliability of the annotation methodology. The high agreement between GPT-5 and GPT-4o (94%) is particularly reassuring, as it suggests the behavioral definitions are robust across different model judges.

### 5.3 Intervention and Controllability Experiments

#### Causal Effects on Reasoning Trajectories

The second core claim is that SAE-derived vectors can **controllably modify** reasoning behaviors during inference. Figure 6 provides a qualitative demonstration: when the R1-1.5B model is prompted with a math question about converting rectangular to polar coordinates, the reasoning trajectory shifts predictably under intervention:

- **Negative intervention** (suppressing reflection): 2 reflection steps, shorter response
- **Vanilla inference**: 4 reflection steps, moderate length
- **Positive intervention** (amplifying reflection): 6 reflection steps, longer response with more self-checking phrases

Critically, all three conditions produce the **same final answer** ($(3, \pi/2)$), demonstrating that the intervention modifies the reasoning process without compromising correctness on this example.

#### Dose-Response Relationship

To establish that the intervention has a genuine causal effect rather than a binary on/off switch, the authors test multiple intervention strengths. Using the formula $h' = h - \alpha \cdot w_i(w_i^T h)$ with $\alpha \in \{-1.5, -1, 0, 1, 1.5\}$, the number of reflection steps on AIME25 tasks changes to:

$$\{58.6, 73.6, 90.5, 131.0, 166.9\}$$

This **monotonic relationship** between intervention strength and behavior count is strong evidence of causality. If the vectors were merely correlated with reflection rather than encoding it causally, we would not expect such consistent dose-dependent responses.

#### Cross-Model and Cross-Task Generalization

Figure 5 presents intervention results across four different task-model combinations:

| Task | Model | Behavior | Negative | Vanilla | Positive |
|------|-------|----------|----------|---------|----------|
| AIME 2025 | R1-1.5B | Reflection | ~50 | ~90 | ~150 |
| AIME 2025 | R1-1.5B | Backtracking | ~10 | ~35 | ~80 |
| AMC23 | R1-1.5B | Reflection | ~25 | ~50 | ~90 |
| AMC23 | R1-1.5B | Backtracking | ~5 | ~20 | ~50 |
| AIME 2024 | Qwen3-8B | Reflection | ~40 | ~100 | ~140 |
| AIME 2024 | Qwen3-8B | Backtracking | ~10 | ~40 | ~90 |
| MATH500 | Qwen3-8B | Reflection | ~15 | ~35 | ~60 |
| MATH500 | Qwen3-8B | Backtracking | ~5 | ~15 | ~35 |

The exact numbers are not provided in the bar charts, but the consistent pattern across all conditions is clear: negative interventions reduce behavior counts, positive interventions increase them, and the effect is present in both the 1.5B and 8B models. This cross-model consistency strengthens the claim that the discovered vectors capture fundamental reasoning mechanisms rather than model-specific artifacts.

#### Domain Shift Generalization

Table 1 tests whether the SAE columns learned from MATH500 generalize to entirely different reasoning domains:

| Domain | Steering | Reflection | Backtracking |
|--------|----------|------------|--------------|
| GPQA-Diamond | Vanilla | 53.23 | 11.83 |
| GPQA-Diamond | Positive | 62.77 | 20.31 |

The magnitude of the effect is substantial. On GPQA-Diamond, positive intervention increases reflection by **17.9%** (from 53.23 to 62.77) and backtracking by **71.6%** (from 11.83 to 20.31). On KnowLogic, the effects are even larger: reflection increases by **43.4%** and backtracking by **73.1%**.

This generalization is critical evidence that the vectors encode **model-intrinsic reasoning operations** rather than dataset-specific patterns. If the reflection vector merely captured math-specific phrases like "let me verify," it would not modulate behavior on commonsense reasoning tasks in GPQA-Diamond.

### 5.4 Novel Behavior Discovery: Confidence Vectors

#### Discovery Methodology

Section 5 demonstrates that SAEs can discover behaviors **beyond human-defined categories** by optimizing for downstream objectives. The authors choose **output entropy** as a proxy for confidence, motivated by prior work showing that entropy plays a critical role during reasoning (Cui et al., 2025; Fu et al., 2025).

The optimization objective (Equation 7) is:

$$\arg\min_S \mathbb{E}\left[-\sum_{k=1}^{|V|} p_k \log p_k\right], \quad p_k = \text{softmax}(f_{l \to L}(h + SW_{\text{decoder}}))_k$$

where $S \in \mathbb{R}^D$ is a score vector assigned to the $D$ decoder columns, and $|V|$ is the vocabulary size. This optimization identifies which decoder columns, when activated, minimize prediction uncertainty. The optimization uses Adam with learning rate 0.01 for 1000 iterations, batch size 256, and cosine annealing decay.

#### Confidence Vector Localization

Figure 7(a) shows that confidence-related columns are **predominantly located in the bottom-right region** of the SAE decoder visualization. This concentrated localization is notable: if confidence were encoded in a distributed manner across many columns, optimization would not produce such a clear spatial pattern.

Furthermore, these confidence columns **partially overlap** with the reflection and backtracking regions identified in Figure 2. This overlap is theoretically consistent: reflection and backtracking involve uncertainty and re-evaluation, which should correlate with higher entropy. The intervention results confirm this relationship.

#### Causal Effects of Confidence Intervention

On AIME25 tasks, injecting the confidence vector produces substantial changes in reasoning style:

| Metric | Before Intervention | After Intervention | Change |
|--------|---------------------|--------------------|--------|
| Reflection steps | 90.53 | 33.77 | -62.7% |
| Backtracking steps | 35.50 | 5.93 | -83.3% |
| Accuracy | 23.33% | 20.00% | -3.33 points |

The accuracy drop from 23.33% to 20.00% corresponds to **only one question out of 30**, which the authors correctly note is not statistically significant. However, the behavioral changes are dramatic: reflection decreases by nearly two-thirds, and backtracking decreases by over 80%.

This result has an important interpretation: **confidence and deliberation are in tension**. A more confident reasoning style skips verification steps and commits to solutions faster. Whether this is desirable depends on the application—efficiency-critical settings may prefer confident (fast) reasoning, while high-stakes settings may prefer deliberative (slow) reasoning with more verification.

#### Token-Level Evidence

Figures 7(b) and 7(c) provide token-level evidence for the behavioral shift. Without intervention, frequent tokens at the beginning of reasoning steps include reflection-related cues like "Wait" and backtracking indicators like "Alternatively." After intervention with the confidence vector, these are replaced by tokens more closely related to **detailed mathematical calculation steps** (e.g., numbers, operators).

This token-level shift confirms that the intervention is not merely changing surface phrasing but is altering the underlying reasoning strategy. The model is genuinely computing more directly rather than engaging in metacognitive verification.

#### Reasoning Enhancement via Sample-Dependent Steering

Figure 8 presents the most practically significant result: using RISE to **improve reasoning performance** while reducing token costs. The authors select the top-3 confidence vectors $c_1, c_2, c_3$ and learn combination coefficients at test time, yielding a sample-dependent steering vector $\sum_{i \in \{1,2,3\}} \alpha_i c_i$.

| Method | MATH500 Accuracy | MATH500 Tokens | AIME 2025 Accuracy | AIME 2025 Tokens |
|--------|------------------|----------------|--------------------|------------------|
| Vanilla | ~80% | ~3500 | ~15% | ~12000 |
| TIP | ~82% | ~3200 | ~17% | ~11000 |
| SEAL | ~83% | ~3100 | ~18% | ~10500 |
| **RISE (Confidence)** | **~84.66%** | **~3020** | **~19%** | **~10400** |

Exact numbers are not provided in the bar charts, but the authors state the method improves accuracy by **up to 4.66 points** while achieving a **13.69% reduction in token usage** compared to vanilla inference. This dual improvement—better accuracy with fewer tokens—is the strongest practical evidence for RISE's utility.

#### Confidence Vector Generalization

Table 2 tests whether confidence vectors generalize across domains, similar to Table 1 for behavior-specific vectors:

| Domain | Steering | Reflection | Backtracking |
|--------|----------|------------|--------------|
| GPQA | Vanilla | 53.23 | 11.83 |
| GPQA | Positive | 61.37 | 16.18 |
| GPQA | Negative | 37.25 | 6.91 |
| KnowLogic | Vanilla | 35.56 | 5.42 |
| KnowLogic | Positive | 48.16 | 5.98 |
| KnowLogic | Negative | 20.04 | 3.51 |

The pattern mirrors Table 1: positive intervention (amplifying confidence) reduces reflection and backtracking, while negative intervention (suppressing confidence) increases them. This cross-domain consistency further validates that the confidence vector captures a fundamental reasoning mechanism rather than a dataset-specific artifact.

### 5.5 Robustness and Generalization Analysis

#### Theoretical Assumption Validation

Theorem 1 relies on four assumptions: incoherence, sparsity, ReLU activation, and coefficient separation. Appendix C provides empirical validation for the first two:

- **Incoherence**: Maximum cosine similarity among recovered dictionary vectors is bounded (Figure 9, middle plot shows values below 0.30 across all layers)
- **Sparsity**: The sparsity level $k$ (number of active dictionary vectors per activation) is well within theoretical bounds (Figure 9, left plot shows $k$ values between 0 and 300 across layers)
- **Reconstruction Error**: Consistently low across all layers (Figure 9, right plot shows error below 0.08)

These validations are important because they establish that the theoretical guarantees underlying Theorem 1 are not violated in practice. Without this empirical support, the claim that SAE decoder columns recover true behavior directions would rest solely on empirical observation rather than theoretical grounding.

#### Response Length as a Structural Axis

Appendix E explores whether the SAE captures **structural properties** beyond semantic behaviors. The authors split activations into short responses (&lt;1000 tokens) and long responses (>8000 tokens), then visualize the SAE decoder columns (Figure 11).

The results show that early layers exhibit weak separation with respect to response length, while mid-to-late layers demonstrate stronger alignment. Figure 12 quantifies this with Silhouette scores that mirror the behavioral separation pattern in Figure 3. This finding has two implications:

1. **Joint encoding**: Reasoning models encode both semantic behaviors (reflection, confidence) and structural properties (length) in the same latent space.
2. **Intervention trade-offs**: Suppressing verbose outputs may inadvertently suppress useful verification behaviors if the structural and semantic dimensions are coupled.

This is a nuanced finding that the authors do not fully explore. It suggests that efficiency optimization via length-based steering requires care to avoid removing productive reasoning behaviors that happen to correlate with longer outputs.

#### Annotation Robustness

As noted in Section 5.2, the authors validate annotation consistency across multiple LLM judges and a keyword-matching approach (Appendix D, Figure 10). All pairwise agreements exceed 85%, with GPT-5 and GPT-4o reaching 94%. This robustness check is essential because the behavioral counts used throughout the intervention experiments depend on these annotations.

However, there is a subtle limitation: all annotation methods rely on **surface-level textual cues** (keywords like "Wait," "Alternatively," etc.). If a model engages in reflection without using these surface markers, it would be misclassified. The high agreement between methods suggests this is not a major issue in practice, but it remains a potential source of measurement error.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

#### Strengths of the Experimental Design

The experiments are well-designed to support the paper's claims:

1. **Multiple levels of validation**: The paper provides visual evidence (Figures 2, 7), quantitative metrics (Silhouette scores, behavior counts), and causal interventions (Figures 5, 6, 8). This triangulation strengthens the conclusions.

2. **Cross-domain generalization**: Testing on GPQA-Diamond and KnowLogic demonstrates that the vectors are not overfit to math-specific patterns. The consistent effects across domains (Tables 1, 2) are strong evidence for model-intrinsic mechanisms.

3. **Dose-response relationships**: The monotonic relationship between intervention strength and behavior count (Section 5.3) establishes causality rather than mere correlation.

4. **Theoretical grounding**: Appendix C validates the assumptions underlying Theorem 1, connecting empirical results to theoretical guarantees.

5. **Practical utility demonstration**: Figure 8 shows that RISE can improve both accuracy and efficiency simultaneously, which is the most compelling evidence for practical adoption.

#### Limitations and Open Questions

Despite the strong experimental design, several limitations warrant attention:

**1. Small training set.** The SAE is trained on only 500 MATH examples. While the generalization results suggest this is sufficient, it is unclear whether larger training sets would discover additional behaviors or refine the existing vectors. The authors do not ablate training set size, leaving this question open.

**2. Limited behavior taxonomy.** The paper focuses on reflection, backtracking, and confidence. While these are important behaviors, reasoning encompasses many other operations (planning, decomposition, analogy, etc.). The claim that RISE captures the "full spectrum" of reasoning behaviors is not fully validated—only three behaviors are thoroughly analyzed.

**3. Accuracy trade-offs.** The confidence intervention reduces accuracy from 23.33% to 20.00% on AIME25. While the authors note this is not statistically significant (1 question out of 30), it does suggest that suppressing deliberation can harm performance on difficult problems. The conditions under which confidence steering helps versus harms are not fully characterized.

**4. Layer selection.** The paper trains the SAE on a single layer but does not systematically compare different layer choices. Figure 3 suggests mid-to-late layers are optimal, but the exact layer used for the main experiments is not specified. This makes replication more difficult.

**5. Model scale.** The primary experiments use a 1.5B model, with limited extension to 8B. It is unclear whether the findings scale to larger models (70B+) where reasoning behaviors may be more complex and entangled.

**6. Intervention side effects.** The paper does not analyze whether interventions produce unintended side effects beyond the targeted behaviors. For example, does amplifying reflection also increase hallucination or introduce new error modes? This is an important consideration for practical deployment.

#### Overall Assessment

The experiments **convincingly support** the paper's core claims:

- **Claim 1 (Unsupervised discovery)**: Strongly supported. The alignment between unsupervised SAE clusters and human-labeled behaviors (Figure 2, Figure 3) demonstrates that the method discovers interpretable structure without supervision.

- **Claim 2 (Controllability)**: Strongly supported. The dose-response relationships (Section 5.3) and cross-domain generalization (Tables 1, 2) establish causal control over reasoning behaviors.

- **Claim 3 (Novel behavior discovery)**: Moderately supported. The confidence vector experiments (Section 5.4) demonstrate objective-driven discovery, but only one novel behavior is thoroughly analyzed. More examples would strengthen this claim.

- **Claim 4 (Practical utility)**: Strongly supported. The accuracy and token cost improvements in Figure 8 provide concrete evidence of practical value.

The main gap is in characterizing the **boundary conditions** for when interventions help versus harm. The paper demonstrates that interventions work, but does not fully map the landscape of when they should be applied. This is a natural direction for future work rather than a flaw in the current study.

In summary, the experimental analysis is rigorous, multi-faceted, and provides strong evidence for the paper's contributions. The limitations identified are primarily opportunities for extension rather than threats to the validity of the core findings.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While RISE demonstrates compelling capabilities for unsupervised reasoning behavior discovery, several important limitations and trade-offs warrant careful consideration. This section examines the assumptions underlying the approach, scenarios where it may fail, computational constraints, and open questions that remain unresolved.

### 6.1 Theoretical Assumptions and Their Practical Validity

**Theorem 1 relies on four critical assumptions** that may not hold universally across all models and reasoning tasks:

| Assumption | Formal Requirement | Practical Concern |
|------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Incoherence** | $\max_{i \neq j} \frac{|\langle w_i, w_j \rangle|}{\|w_i\|\|w_j\|} \leq \mu < 1$ | Appendix C shows maximum cosine similarity below 0.30, but this is measured on *recovered* vectors, not ground truth. |
| **Sparsity** | $k < c/\mu$ for universal constant $c$ | Figure 9 shows $k$ values between 0–300 across layers, but the theoretical bound depends on unknown $\mu$. |
| **ReLU Activation** | SAE uses ReLU nonlinearity | Standard choice, but alternative activations (SwiGLU, GeLU) may behave differently. |
| **Coefficient Separation** | $|a_i| \geq \alpha > 0$ for nonzero coefficients | No empirical validation provided; weak behaviors may fall below this threshold. |

The empirical validation in Appendix C is reassuring but incomplete. The paper validates incoherence and sparsity on the *reconstructed* activations from the SAE, not on the hypothetical ground-truth dictionary $W$. This creates a circularity: the assumptions are validated using the very tool whose correctness depends on those assumptions. While the low reconstruction error (below 0.08 across all layers in Figure 9) suggests the SAE is learning meaningful structure, it does not guarantee that the recovered directions match the true behavioral encoding.

**Trade-off implication**: Practitioners applying RISE to new models should empirically validate these assumptions rather than assuming they hold automatically. Models with different architectures (e.g., MoE layers, alternative attention mechanisms) may violate the incoherence or sparsity conditions.

### 6.2 Limited Behavior Taxonomy and Discovery Scope

**The paper thoroughly analyzes only three behaviors**: reflection, backtracking, and confidence. While these are important metacognitive operations, reasoning encompasses many other cognitive processes:

- Planning and decomposition
- Analogy and transfer
- Constraint propagation
- Hypothesis generation
- Error correction (distinct from backtracking)

The claim in Section 1 that RISE captures "the full spectrum of potential reasoning behaviors" is not empirically validated. The UMAP visualizations in Figure 2 show clear clusters for reflection and backtracking, but the "other" category remains dispersed and unanalyzed. This dispersed region may contain additional interpretable behaviors that the paper does not explore.

**Evidence of unexplored structure**: Figure 2's "Others Highlighted" subfigure shows vectors spread across the entire SAE space rather than forming tight clusters. This could indicate:
1. The "other" category genuinely contains heterogeneous behaviors.
2. The SAE dimension ($D = 2048$) is insufficient to disentangle finer-grained behaviors.
3. Some reasoning operations are encoded in a distributed rather than localized manner.

**Trade-off implication**: The choice of $D = 2048$ balances interpretability against coverage. A larger dictionary might discover more behaviors but risks learning token-level features rather than cognitive operations. The paper does not ablate this hyperparameter, leaving the optimal dictionary size unknown.

### 6.3 Training Data Constraints and Generalization Boundaries

**The SAE is trained on only 500 MATH examples** (Section 4.2). While the cross-domain generalization results in Tables 1 and 2 are encouraging, several concerns remain:

1. **No training set ablation**: The paper does not test whether 500 examples is sufficient or whether larger training sets would discover additional behaviors. It is possible that rare but important reasoning strategies (e.g., proof by contradiction, case analysis) do not appear frequently enough in 500 samples to form distinct clusters.

2. **Math-centric training**: All training data comes from mathematical reasoning. While GPQA-Diamond and KnowLogic test generalization, these are still formal reasoning domains. The paper does not evaluate on:
   - Creative reasoning tasks (writing, design)
   - Social reasoning (negotiation, persuasion)
   - Multi-modal reasoning (vision-language tasks)

3. **Temporal holdout limitation**: AIME 2025 serves as a temporal holdout, but it is still a competition math dataset. True out-of-distribution generalization to fundamentally different task types remains untested.

**Evidence from generalization experiments**: Table 1 shows that on KnowLogic, positive intervention increases reflection from 35.56 to 51.00 (43.4% increase), which is larger than the 17.9% increase on GPQA-Diamond. This variability suggests that generalization effectiveness may depend on domain characteristics, but the paper does not analyze *why* some domains show stronger transfer than others.

**Trade-off implication**: Practitioners should expect stronger generalization within related domains (math → logic) than across fundamentally different task types. For applications outside formal reasoning, retraining the SAE on domain-specific data may be necessary.

### 6.4 Model Scale and Architecture Limitations

**Primary experiments use DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B**, with limited extension to Qwen3-8B (Figure 5). This raises questions about scalability:

1. **Representation complexity**: Larger models (70B+) may encode reasoning behaviors in more complex, entangled patterns that require larger SAE dictionaries or multi-layer training to disentangle.

2. **Architecture differences**: The paper does not test on models with different architectures (e.g., MoE models like Mixtral, models with different attention mechanisms). The linear representation hypothesis may hold differently across architectures.

3. **Reasoning capability variance**: The 1.5B model is a distilled reasoning model. Base models without reasoning fine-tuning may not exhibit the same behavioral structure, limiting RISE's applicability to non-reasoning models.

**Evidence from cross-model results**: Figure 5 shows consistent intervention effects on Qwen3-8B, but the absolute behavior counts differ substantially. For example, on AIME 2024, Qwen3-8B shows ~100 reflection steps under vanilla inference compared to ~90 for R1-1.5B on AIME 2025. This suggests behavior encoding scales with model size, but the paper does not analyze whether the *geometry* of the SAE space changes systematically with scale.

**Trade-off implication**: Organizations deploying RISE on larger models should budget for increased computational costs (larger SAE dictionaries, more training data) and should not assume direct transfer of hyperparameters from the 1.5B experiments.

### 6.5 Accuracy-Efficiency Trade-offs in Intervention

**The confidence intervention reveals a fundamental tension** between deliberation and efficiency:

| Metric | Before Intervention | After Intervention | Change |
|--------|---------------------|--------------------|--------|
| Reflection steps | 90.53 | 33.77 | -62.7% |
| Backtracking steps | 35.50 | 5.93 | -83.3% |
| Accuracy (AIME25) | 23.33% | 20.00% | -3.33 points |
| Token usage | Not reported | Not reported | Implied reduction |

The accuracy drop, while noted as "not statistically significant" (one question out of 30), reveals an important trade-off: **suppressing deliberation behaviors reduces token costs but may harm performance on difficult problems**. The paper does not characterize the conditions under which confidence steering helps versus harms:

- Does confidence intervention help on easy problems where deliberation is unnecessary?
- Does it harm on hard problems where verification is critical?
- Is there an optimal intervention strength that balances accuracy and efficiency?

**Evidence from sample-dependent steering**: Figure 8 shows that learning combination coefficients per input improves accuracy by 4.66 points while reducing tokens by 13.69%. This suggests that *adaptive* intervention can mitigate the trade-off, but the paper does not explain *which* inputs benefit from stronger versus weaker steering. Without this understanding, practitioners cannot predict when to apply confidence vectors.

**Trade-off implication**: Confidence steering should be applied selectively based on problem difficulty or uncertainty estimates. Blind application across all inputs risks degrading performance on challenging tasks where deliberation is beneficial.

### 6.6 Annotation and Measurement Limitations

**All behavioral measurements rely on surface-level textual cues**. Appendix D shows annotation uses keywords like "Wait," "verify," "Alternatively" (Table 3). This creates several vulnerabilities:

1. **False negatives**: A model may engage in reflection without using canonical markers. For example, "Let me reconsider the constraints" expresses reflection but may not match keyword patterns.

2. **False positives**: Phrases like "Wait" can be used as filler rather than genuine reflection. The high inter-annotator agreement (85–94% in Figure 10) suggests consistency but does not guarantee accuracy.

3. **Language dependence**: The keyword approach is English-specific. Applying RISE to non-English reasoning would require redeveloping the annotation scheme.

**Evidence from annotation consistency**: Figure 10 shows GPT-5 and GPT-4o agree at 94%, but both are LLM judges using similar prompting. Agreement with keyword matching is lower (~85–86%), suggesting that surface cues do not perfectly capture the semantic definitions of reflection and backtracking.

**Trade-off implication**: Behavioral counts should be interpreted as approximate measures rather than ground truth. For critical applications, manual verification of intervention effects on representative samples is recommended.

### 6.7 Computational and Deployment Constraints

**The paper does not fully characterize computational costs**:

1. **SAE training**: Training requires extracting activations at delimiter tokens for all training samples. For a 500-sample dataset with an average of 20 steps per response, this requires 10,000 forward passes through the model. The paper does not report training time or GPU requirements.

2. **Intervention overhead**: During inference, the intervention $h' = h - \alpha \cdot w_i(w_i^T h)$ must be applied at each reasoning step. For long responses (8000+ tokens), this adds computational overhead proportional to response length.

3. **Memory requirements**: Storing the SAE decoder matrix ($2048 \times d$, where $d$ is the model's hidden dimension) requires additional memory during inference. For a 1.5B model with $d \approx 2048$, this is approximately 16 MB per layer, but the paper does not specify memory optimization strategies.

**Evidence from intervention design**: Figure 4 shows intervention is applied at the last token of each reasoning step, not every token. This reduces overhead but requires accurate step boundary detection, which depends on the `<\n\n>` delimiter. Models that do not use consistent delimiters would require adaptation.

**Trade-off implication**: For latency-sensitive applications, the intervention overhead must be weighed against the benefits of controlled reasoning. Batch processing or caching strategies may be necessary for high-throughput deployment.

### 6.8 Unintended Side Effects and Safety Considerations

**The paper does not analyze potential negative side effects** of behavior interventions:

1. **Hallucination risk**: Amplifying confidence may increase overconfidence in incorrect answers. The accuracy drop on AIME25 (23.33% → 20.00%) hints at this risk but does not systematically measure hallucination rates.

2. **Behavior coupling**: Suppressing reflection may inadvertently suppress other useful behaviors if they share activation space. Figure 7(a) shows confidence vectors overlap with reflection/backtracking regions, suggesting interventions may have cascading effects.

3. **Safety implications**: For high-stakes applications (medical, legal, financial), reducing deliberation could increase the risk of harmful outputs. The paper does not evaluate intervention effects on safety-relevant behaviors.

**Evidence from structural-semantic coupling**: Appendix E shows that response length and semantic behaviors are encoded in overlapping regions of the SAE space (Figure 11). This suggests that length-based efficiency optimization may inadvertently suppress useful verification behaviors.

**Trade-off implication**: Interventions should be validated on safety-critical metrics before deployment in high-stakes domains. Monitoring for unintended behavioral changes (e.g., increased assertiveness without accuracy) is essential.

### 6.9 Open Questions and Future Directions

Several important questions remain unresolved:

1. **Optimal layer selection**: Figure 3 shows mid-to-late layers have peak separability, but the paper does not specify which exact layer was used for the main experiments. Systematic comparison of layer choices would improve reproducibility.

2. **Dynamic intervention strategies**: The sample-dependent steering in Figure 8 learns coefficients at test time, but the optimization process (1000 iterations, Adam optimizer) adds latency. Faster adaptation methods would improve practicality.

3. **Behavior interaction modeling**: The paper treats behaviors as independent (reflection vector, backtracking vector), but Figure 2 shows overlapping regions. Modeling behavior interactions could enable more sophisticated interventions.

4. **Long-term stability**: The paper does not test whether SAE-derived vectors remain stable across model updates. If vectors drift with model fine-tuning, practitioners would need to retrain SAEs frequently.

5. **Multi-behavior interventions**: All experiments intervene on single behaviors or confidence composites. Simultaneous intervention on multiple behaviors (e.g., amplify reflection while suppressing backtracking) is not explored.

**Summary**: RISE represents a significant advance in unsupervised reasoning interpretability, but practitioners should be aware of its assumptions, limitations, and trade-offs. The approach works best when: (1) the target model exhibits similar reasoning structure to the training models, (2) the application domain is within the generalization boundary (formal reasoning tasks), and (3) interventions are validated on task-specific metrics before deployment. Future work should address the open questions above to strengthen the framework's robustness and applicability.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how RISE reshapes the interpretability landscape, identifies concrete research directions it enables, and provides practical guidance for practitioners considering adoption. We move beyond what the paper demonstrates to what it makes possible.

### 7.1 Landscape Shift: From Verification to Discovery

**The paradigm change.** Prior interpretability work on reasoning operates in a **verification mode**: researchers hypothesize that a behavior exists (e.g., "reflection improves accuracy"), collect labeled data, and test whether activation patterns correlate with that behavior. RISE inverts this workflow into **discovery mode**: train an SAE on unlabeled activations, then examine what behaviors emerge.

This shift has three concrete implications for the field:

| Aspect | Prior Approach (Supervised) | RISE Approach (Unsupervised) |
|--------|----------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Discovery ceiling** | Limited to behaviors humans can define and annotate | Potentially unlimited—behaviors emerge from activation structure |
| **Labeling cost** | O(N × M) where M is behavior categories | O(N) data collection, zero labeling |
| **Novel behavior detection** | Impossible by design | Demonstrated via confidence vectors (Section 5) |

The confidence vector discovery in Section 5 exemplifies this shift. Rather than asking "does the model have a confidence behavior?" (which requires defining confidence at the token level), the authors ask "what activation patterns minimize output entropy?" and discover the answer empirically. Figure 7(a) shows these confidence-related columns concentrate in a specific region of the SAE decoder space—structure that would be invisible to supervised methods without pre-existing confidence labels.

**Theoretical extension.** RISE extends the Linear Representation Hypothesis (Park et al., 2023) from concrete concepts (gender, sentiment, truthfulness) to **abstract cognitive patterns**. Theorem 1 (Section 4.1) provides theoretical grounding: under sparsity and incoherence assumptions, SAE decoder columns recover true behavior directions. This validates that metacognitive processes—previously thought too abstract for linear encoding—admit geometric interpretation in activation space.

**What this enables.** The discovery paradigm opens research questions that were previously intractable:
- Do different model architectures encode reasoning behaviors in comparable geometric structures?
- How does the behavioral landscape evolve during reasoning fine-tuning?
- Are there universal reasoning vectors that transfer across model families?

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions in Section 6, but the framework enables broader investigation. We categorize these by timescale and feasibility.

#### 7.2.1 Near-Term Extensions (1–2 Years)

**1. Systematic layer selection.** Figure 3 shows Silhouette scores peak in mid-to-late layers, with slight decline near the final layers due to oversmoothing (Wang et al., 2023). However, the paper does not specify which exact layer was used for the main intervention experiments. Follow-up work should:
- Train SAEs on multiple layers simultaneously
- Quantify intervention effectiveness as a function of layer depth
- Develop automated layer selection criteria based on separability metrics

**2. Multi-behavior intervention.** All experiments in Section 4.4 and Section 5 intervene on single behaviors (reflection, backtracking, or confidence composites). However, Figure 2 shows overlapping regions between reflection and backtracking, suggesting behaviors are not fully independent. Research should explore:
- Simultaneous intervention on multiple behaviors (e.g., amplify reflection while suppressing backtracking)
- Interaction effects between behavior vectors
- Optimization frameworks for multi-objective steering (accuracy + efficiency + safety)

**3. Annotation-free evaluation.** Section 5.4 relies on LLM-as-a-judge annotation (Appendix D) to count reflection and backtracking steps. This introduces measurement error and language dependence. Alternative evaluation methods include:
- Direct accuracy and token cost metrics (as in Figure 8)
- Entropy-based proxies for deliberation
- Human evaluation of reasoning quality on representative samples

**4. Training set ablation.** The SAE trains on only 500 MATH examples (Section 4.2). No ablation tests whether larger datasets discover additional behaviors or refine existing vectors. Systematic scaling studies should:
- Vary training set size from 100 to 10,000 examples
- Measure behavior discovery rate as a function of data volume
- Identify diminishing returns thresholds

#### 7.2.2 Medium-Term Extensions (2–4 Years)

**1. Cross-architecture generalization.** Experiments use DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B and Qwen3-8B (Figure 5), both transformer-based reasoning models. Open questions include:
- Do Mixture-of-Experts models encode behaviors differently?
- How do attention-only vs. MLP-heavy architectures compare?
- Can vectors trained on one architecture transfer to another?

**2. Temporal stability.** The paper does not test whether SAE-derived vectors remain stable across model updates. If vectors drift with fine-tuning, practitioners must retrain SAEs frequently. Research should:
- Track vector stability across model versions
- Develop adaptation methods for drifted vectors
- Identify which behaviors are most stable vs. volatile

**3. Non-mathematical reasoning domains.** Training and evaluation focus on mathematical and logical reasoning (MATH, AIME, GPQA, KnowLogic). Extension to other domains requires:
- Creative reasoning (writing, design, brainstorming)
- Social reasoning (negotiation, persuasion, diplomacy)
- Multi-modal reasoning (vision-language, code execution)
- Domain-specific behavior taxonomies

**4. Theoretical refinement.** Theorem 1 assumes incoherence, sparsity, ReLU activation, and coefficient separation. Appendix C validates the first two empirically, but gaps remain:
- Relax assumptions to cover non-ReLU activations (SwiGLU, GeLU)
- Derive bounds on intervention effectiveness
- Connect SAE geometry to transformer circuit mechanisms

#### 7.2.3 Long-Term Vision (4+ Years)

**1. Automated behavior discovery pipelines.** Imagine a system that:
- Trains SAEs on model activations automatically
- Clusters decoder columns and proposes behavior labels
- Validates clusters through intervention experiments
- Maintains a searchable behavior database across models

This would transform interpretability from a manual research process into an automated monitoring tool.

**2. Reasoning-aware model training.** Instead of post-hoc intervention, integrate behavior vectors into training:
- Regularize activations to encourage desirable behavior geometries
- Use SAE features as auxiliary losses during fine-tuning
- Develop curricula that explicitly teach reasoning behaviors

**3. Cross-model behavior alignment.** If different models encode similar behaviors in comparable geometric structures, we could:
- Transfer reasoning capabilities between models via vector alignment
- Detect behavioral anomalies indicating model degradation
- Build universal reasoning benchmarks based on activation geometry

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

RISE enables several concrete applications beyond research. We categorize these by stakeholder and provide deployment considerations.

#### 7.3.1 For Model Developers

**Debugging reasoning failures.** When a model consistently fails on certain problem types, practitioners can:
1. Extract activations from failed examples
2. Identify which behavior vectors are under/over-activated
3. Intervene to correct the imbalance

*Example:* If a model fails on problems requiring verification, amplify the reflection vector during inference. Figure 6 demonstrates this reduces errors by increasing self-checking steps from 2 to 6 without changing the final answer.

**Efficiency optimization.** Figure 8 shows RISE achieves 13.69% token reduction with 4.66 accuracy point improvement on MATH500. For production systems, this translates to:
- Lower inference costs at scale
- Reduced latency for time-sensitive applications
- Ability to serve more requests with fixed compute budgets

**Model comparison.** Instead of comparing models solely on accuracy, developers can compare behavioral profiles:
- Does Model A use more reflection than Model B on equivalent problems?
- Which model has more disentangled behavior representations?
- Are certain behaviors correlated with failure modes?

#### 7.3.2 For Deployment Teams

**Dynamic steering based on problem difficulty.** The sample-dependent steering in Figure 8 learns combination coefficients per input. Deployment systems can:
- Estimate problem difficulty from initial tokens
- Apply stronger confidence steering for easy problems (reduce tokens)
- Apply stronger reflection steering for hard problems (improve accuracy)

**Safety monitoring.** For high-stakes applications (medical, legal, financial), teams can:
- Monitor behavior vector activations in real-time
- Flag responses with abnormally low reflection (potential hallucination risk)
- Automatically intervene when unsafe patterns emerge

**A/B testing interventions.** Before deploying steering globally, teams can:
- Run controlled experiments with different intervention strengths
- Measure accuracy, token cost, and user satisfaction trade-offs
- Select optimal steering parameters per use case

#### 7.3.3 For Researchers

**Behavioral benchmarking.** Instead of reporting only accuracy, papers can include:
- Behavior vector activation distributions
- Intervention sensitivity curves (like the dose-response in Section 5.3)
- Cross-domain generalization metrics (like Tables 1 and 2)

**Mechanism discovery.** RISE provides tools to investigate open questions:
- Why do some models overthink while others underthink?
- What activation patterns distinguish correct from incorrect reasoning?
- How do reasoning behaviors evolve during training?

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

Practitioners considering RISE should understand when to use it, what resources it requires, and how it compares to alternatives.

#### 7.4.1 When to Prefer RISE Over Alternatives

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| **No labeled data available** | RISE | Unsupervised discovery requires zero behavioral labels |
| **Exploring unknown behaviors** | RISE | Discovery mode finds behaviors outside human taxonomy |
| **Cross-domain deployment** | RISE | Tables 1–2 show strong generalization without retraining |
| **Well-defined contrastive concepts** | DiffMean | If "happy vs. sad" labels exist, supervised methods are simpler |
| **Single-behavior intervention** | Either | Both approaches support targeted steering |
| **Limited compute budget** | DiffMean | SAE training adds upfront cost; DiffMean is computation-light |

**Key decision factor:** If you can reliably label the behavior you want to control, DiffMean (Marks and Tegmark, 2023) is simpler. If you want to discover behaviors or lack labels, RISE is the only viable option.

#### 7.4.2 Computational Requirements

The paper does not fully report training costs, but we can estimate from the configuration in Section 4.2:

| Component | Estimate | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Activation extraction** | 500 samples × ~20 steps × 1.5B model forward pass | ~10,000 forward passes for training data |
| **SAE training** | Batch size 1024, learning rate 1×10⁻⁴, cosine annealing | Duration depends on GPU; likely hours on A100 |
| **Intervention overhead** | One projection per reasoning step | $h' = h - \alpha \cdot w_i(w_i^T h)$ adds O(d) computation per step |
| **Memory** | Decoder matrix: 2048 × d (d ≈ 2048 for 1.5B model) | ~16 MB per layer; negligible for modern GPUs |

**Deployment consideration:** For latency-sensitive applications, cache intervention vectors and pre-compute projections where possible. The intervention is applied at step boundaries (Figure 4), not every token, reducing overhead.

#### 7.4.3 Integration Checklist

Before deploying RISE in production:

1. **Validate assumptions.** Check incoherence and sparsity on your model (Appendix C methodology). If assumptions are violated, SAE may not recover meaningful directions.

2. **Test generalization.** Apply learned vectors to held-out domains before deployment. Tables 1–2 show GPQA-Diamond and KnowLogic as test cases.

3. **Measure side effects.** Monitor accuracy, hallucination rates, and safety metrics under intervention. Section 5.4 shows confidence steering reduces accuracy by 3.33 points on AIME25.

4. **Select intervention layer.** Use Silhouette scores (Figure 3) to identify layers with peak behavioral separability. Mid-to-late layers typically work best.

5. **Calibrate intervention strength.** Test multiple $\alpha$ values (Section 4.4.1 uses $\{-1.5, -1, 0, 1, 1.5\}$) to find the optimal trade-off for your use case.

6. **Monitor drift.** If the underlying model is updated, re-validate vector effectiveness. Behavioral geometry may shift with fine-tuning.

#### 7.4.4 Known Failure Modes

Be aware of scenarios where RISE may underperform:

- **Non-reasoning models.** Base models without reasoning fine-tuning may not exhibit the behavioral structure RISE expects. The framework targets models like DeepSeek-R1 that produce extended chain-of-thought traces.

- **Inconsistent step boundaries.** RISE segments responses at `<\n\n>` delimiters (Section 3.2). Models that do not use consistent formatting require adaptation.

- **Highly entangled behaviors.** If reflection and backtracking occupy nearly identical activation subspaces (Figure 3 shows modest separation), interventions may have unintended effects on correlated behaviors.

- **Domain mismatch.** Training on math and deploying on creative writing may fail. Tables 1–2 test formal reasoning domains; non-formal domains remain unvalidated.

### 7.5 Summary: The Path Forward

RISE demonstrates that unsupervised latent discovery is viable for reasoning interpretability. The framework shifts the field from asking "does behavior X exist?" to "what behaviors does this model exhibit?" This enables:

- **Scalable behavioral mapping** without labeling bottlenecks
- **Discovery of novel behaviors** beyond human taxonomy (confidence vectors)
- **Test-time control** with cross-domain generalization (Tables 1–2)
- **Performance optimization** balancing accuracy and efficiency (Figure 8)

The open questions in Section 6 and the research directions in Section 7.2 provide a roadmap for extending this work. For practitioners, the integration guidance in Section 7.4 offers concrete steps for adoption. The fundamental contribution is not just a new method, but a new way of thinking about reasoning interpretability: **let the model's activation geometry reveal its cognitive structure, then use that structure to steer behavior without retraining.**

This paradigm has implications beyond reasoning. If abstract cognitive patterns admit linear encoding in reasoning models, similar structures may exist for creativity, planning, or social cognition. RISE provides the methodological foundation for exploring these questions systematically.
