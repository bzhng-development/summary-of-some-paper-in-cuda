## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the "multi-scale credit assignment problem" in search-integrated reasoning, where sparse trajectory-level rewards fail to distinguish high-quality reasoning from fortuitous guesses, by proposing Search-R2, an Actor–Refiner collaboration framework that jointly optimizes an initial generation policy with a Meta-Refiner capable of "cut-and-regenerate" repairs (Abstract, Section 1, Section 3). The framework introduces a hybrid reward design coupling outcome correctness with a dense process reward quantifying retrieved evidence density, theoretically formalized as a smoothed mixture policy that guarantees performance gains over standard rejection sampling (Abstract, Section 3.3, Section 4.1). Extensive experiments across seven benchmarks (including HotpotQA and Bamboogle) demonstrate that Search-R2 consistently outperforms strong baselines like Search-R1, achieving a 50.8% average Exact Match score with a Qwen2.5-32B backbone compared to 45.6% for the baseline, while maintaining minimal computational overhead (Table 2, Section 5.5).

## 2. Context and Motivation

### 2.1 The Rise of Search-Integrated Reasoning Agents

Large language models (LLMs) have traditionally functioned as **static knowledge repositories** — their responses are constrained by the parametric knowledge encoded during pretraining. This creates a fundamental limitation: models cannot access information beyond their training cutoff, and they are prone to **hallucinations** (confidently generating factually incorrect statements) when queried about unfamiliar topics.

**Search-integrated reasoning** represents a paradigm shift. Instead of relying solely on internal knowledge, these agents actively query external information sources (e.g., search engines, databases) during problem-solving. This enables them to tackle **knowledge-intensive tasks** such as:

- **Open-domain question answering**: Questions requiring facts not contained in the model's training data.
- **Multi-hop reasoning**: Tasks where the answer requires chaining together multiple pieces of evidence from different sources (e.g., "The Filipino statesman who established the government-in-exile during WWII was also the mayor of what city?" requires first identifying the statesman, then finding their mayoral city).

The critical innovation is **iterative retrieval**: the agent does not simply fetch information once at the beginning. Instead, it interleaves reasoning steps with search queries, allowing it to refine its understanding based on retrieved evidence. This dynamic interaction transforms the LLM from a passive text generator into an **active agent** that interacts with an external environment.

### 2.2 The Core Problem: Multi-Scale Credit Assignment

Despite the promise of search-integrated agents, training them effectively via **Reinforcement Learning (RL)** faces a fundamental obstacle: the **multi-scale credit assignment problem** (Section 1, Introduction).

To understand this problem, consider what happens during training:

1. The agent generates a **trajectory** — a sequence of decisions including reasoning steps (`...</think>`), search queries (`<search>...</search>`), retrieved information (`<information>...</information>`), and a final answer (`<answer>...</answer>`).
2. The system receives a **reward signal** based on task performance.
3. The policy is updated to maximize expected reward.

**The problem lies in step 2.** Existing methods typically use **trajectory-level rewards** — most commonly, a binary signal indicating whether the final answer is correct (Exact Match). This creates several critical issues:

| Issue | Explanation | Consequence |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| **Sparse supervision** | Only the final outcome is evaluated; intermediate steps receive no direct feedback. | The model cannot distinguish which specific search queries or reasoning steps contributed to success or failure. |
| **Credit misattribution** | A correct final answer may result from lucky guesses rather than sound reasoning. | Inefficient or misleading search behaviors are reinforced alongside valid ones. |
| **Error propagation** | A single irrelevant search query early in a trajectory can misguide all subsequent reasoning (Figure 1). | The entire chain collapses, but the model receives no signal about *where* the error originated. |
| **Sample inefficiency** | Trajectories that succeed after redundant, costly searches receive the same credit as efficient, logically coherent trajectories. | The agent learns to over-query or query at inappropriate times. |

The paper illustrates this with a concrete example in **Figure 1**: Search-R1 (the baseline) issues a search query about "Emilio Aguinaldo" when the correct entity is "Manuel L. Quezon." This early retrieval noise disrupts the entire reasoning chain, leading to an incorrect answer. Standard rejection sampling would simply discard this trajectory and try again — wasting computation and failing to teach the model *why* the trajectory failed.

### 2.3 Limitations of Prior Approaches

The paper positions Search-R2 against three families of existing methods, each with distinct shortcomings:

#### 2.3.1 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and Static Retrieval

**Standard RAG** (Lewis et al., 2020) retrieves documents once at the beginning of generation and conditions the model on that context. **IRCoT** (Trivedi et al., 2023) and **Search-o1** (Li et al., 2025) extend this with iterative retrieval.

**Shortcomings:**
- These methods optimize retrieval behavior through **supervised fine-tuning** or heuristic rules, not task-specific performance objectives.
- They lack mechanisms to learn *when* retrieval is necessary versus when the model should rely on parametric knowledge.
- No explicit feedback on retrieval quality means agents issue **redundant, mistimed, or weakly informative queries** (Section 2.1).

#### 2.3.2 Reinforcement Learning with Trajectory-Level Rewards

Recent work integrates search into RL frameworks (Chen et al., 2025; Jin et al., 2025), allowing agents to learn search behaviors from task feedback. **Search-R1** (Jin et al., 2025) is the direct predecessor to Search-R2.

**Shortcomings:**
- Optimization relies on **delayed, trajectory-level rewards** without assessing individual search decision quality (Wen et al., 2026).
- In **long-horizon interactions** (multi-step reasoning), suboptimal search decisions compound over time, degrading both task performance and learning efficiency (Gao et al., 2025).
- **Rejection sampling** (Ahn et al., 2024) discards entire trajectories upon failure rather than addressing the specific root cause of deviation (Section 1, Introduction).

#### 2.3.3 Dense Reward Shaping and Learned Reward Models

To address sparse rewards, prior work has proposed **dense reward shaping** (Zeng et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025b) and **LLM-based judges** (Zha et al., 2025) to provide richer feedback signals.

**Shortcomings:**
- These techniques are most commonly applied to evaluate **final responses or aggregate trajectory quality**, leaving intermediate decision quality underspecified (Section 2.2).
- Policy optimization still suffers from **low sampling efficiency**, as many rollouts contain low-quality intermediate actions that contribute little to learning.
- No mechanism exists to **surgically repair** flawed trajectories — only to accept or reject them wholesale.

### 2.4 Theoretical Significance: Why Credit Assignment Matters

The multi-scale credit assignment problem is not merely an engineering inconvenience — it has profound implications for both **learning efficiency** and **reasoning robustness**:

**From a learning theory perspective:**
- RL algorithms converge faster when reward signals are **dense** (frequent) and **informative** (correlated with action quality).
- Trajectory-level rewards create a **variance problem**: the same reward is assigned to all actions in a trajectory, regardless of their individual contribution. This inflates gradient variance and slows convergence.
- The **credit assignment horizon** grows with trajectory length. In multi-hop QA tasks requiring 4+ search iterations, attributing success/failure to specific queries becomes exponentially harder.

**From a practical deployment perspective:**
- Agents that cannot distinguish high-quality from low-quality search behaviors will **over-query**, increasing latency and API costs.
- **Error propagation** makes agents brittle: a single early mistake cascades through the entire reasoning chain, producing confidently wrong answers.
- Without fine-grained supervision, agents cannot learn **query timing** — when to search versus when to reason with existing context.

### 2.5 How Search-R2 Positions Itself

Search-R2 explicitly targets the gaps identified above through three key differentiators:

| Aspect | Prior Work (e.g., Search-R1) | Search-R2 |
|--------|------------------------------|-----------|
| **Reward granularity** | Trajectory-level only (final answer correctness) | **Hybrid**: outcome correctness + process reward quantifying evidence density (Section 3.3) |
| **Error handling** | Rejection sampling (discard entire trajectory) | **Cut-and-regenerate**: preserve valid prefixes, repair only flawed suffixes (Section 3.2) |
| **Optimization target** | Actor policy only | **Joint optimization**: Actor + Meta-Refiner trained together via GRPO (Section 3.4) |
| **Theoretical grounding** | Empirical improvements only | **Formal decomposition**: performance gain characterized by Selection Precision, Trimming Skill, and Intervention Volume (Section 3.5, Section 4) |

The paper's central thesis is that **targeted intervention** — diagnosing and repairing specific failure points — is fundamentally more sample-efficient than **outcome-based filtering**. This is not an incremental improvement but a **architectural shift**: the Meta-Refiner acts as a learned debugging mechanism that internalizes the ability to identify and correct reasoning errors.

**Positioning relative to Search-R1:**
Search-R1 serves as the **backbone baseline** — it establishes that RL can optimize search-integrated reasoning, but it suffers from the credit assignment limitations described above. Search-R2 builds directly on Search-R1's infrastructure (same template format, same GRPO optimization framework) while introducing the Actor-Refiner collaboration to address Search-R1's core weakness: inability to distinguish *why* a trajectory failed.

**Positioning relative to reward modeling work:**
Unlike methods that train separate reward models to score trajectories (Zou et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025a), Search-R2's **process reward** is computed from retrieved evidence utility (evaluated by an external judge) and is **gated by outcome correctness** to prevent reward hacking. This ensures the agent cannot maximize retrieval scores without actually solving the task.

**Positioning relative to verification/refinement work:**
Prior refinement approaches (e.g., self-correction, reflection) typically regenerate entire responses or apply static prompts. Search-R2's **Meta-Refiner** is **jointly optimized** with the Actor, meaning the refinement capability is learned end-to-end rather than prompted. The theoretical analysis (Section 4) proves that under satisfiable conditions, this selective correction yields **strict performance gains** over rejection sampling — a guarantee that prior work does not provide.

In summary, Search-R2 addresses a well-defined gap in the search-integrated reasoning literature: the inability of existing RL methods to provide **fine-grained supervision** over intermediate search and reasoning decisions. By introducing a collaborative Actor-Refiner architecture with hybrid rewards and formal performance guarantees, the paper positions itself as a **methodological advance** rather than merely an empirical improvement.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a **reinforcement learning framework paper** that introduces a collaborative Actor–Refiner architecture to solve the multi-scale credit assignment problem in search-integrated reasoning. The core idea is to decompose trajectory generation into two jointly optimized roles: an Actor that produces initial reasoning chains with search queries, and a Meta-Refiner that diagnoses failures and surgically repairs them via a "cut-and-regenerate" mechanism rather than discarding entire trajectories.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

Search-R2 is a training framework that teaches language models to reason with search engines by adding a learned "debugger" component that identifies and fixes specific mistakes in the reasoning chain without throwing away correct work. The system solves the problem of sparse feedback in reinforcement learning by introducing fine-grained rewards for search quality and a refinement mechanism that preserves valid reasoning prefixes while regenerating only the flawed portions of a trajectory.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The Search-R2 framework consists of four major components that operate in a collaborative loop during training (Figure 2). First, the **Actor policy** (`πl`) receives a question as input and generates an initial reasoning trajectory containing thought steps, search queries, retrieved information, and a final answer. Second, the **Discriminator** (`πd`) evaluates whether the generated trajectory is globally coherent with the problem; if the coherence score falls below a threshold `τ`, the trajectory is flagged for refinement. Third, the **Trimmer** (`πh`) identifies the specific step `k` where the reasoning first deviated (the "root cause" of failure) and truncates the trajectory at that point, preserving the valid prefix `ŷ1:k`. Fourth, the **Hybrid Reward Model** computes a total reward combining outcome correctness (Exact Match) with a process reward quantifying the information density of retrieved evidence, which is then used to update both the Actor and Meta-Refiner jointly via Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). Information flows sequentially: the Actor generates, the Discriminator gates, the Trimmer localizes errors, and the Reward Model provides supervision signals that propagate back through all components during optimization.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **Actor Policy and Search Integration**: We first explain how the base policy generates reasoning trajectories with tool calls, including the structural template that enforces operational logic (Section 3.1, Table 1).
- **Meta-Refiner Mechanism**: We then detail the Discriminator and Trimmer sub-components, explaining how they implement the "cut-and-regenerate" operation and why this is more efficient than rejection sampling (Section 3.2, Algorithm 1).
- **Hybrid Reward Design**: We describe the two-scale reward formulation that combines outcome correctness with process-level evidence density, including the outcome-gating mechanism that prevents reward hacking (Section 3.3, Equation 1).
- **Joint Optimization via GRPO**: We explain how Group Relative Policy Optimization is adapted to jointly train the Actor and Meta-Refiner, treating refinement actions as part of the augmented trajectory (Section 3.4, Equation 2).
- **Theoretical Performance Decomposition**: We present the formal analysis that decomposes performance gain into Selection Precision, Intervention Volume, and Trimming Skill, proving conditions under which Search-R2 guarantees improvement over baselines (Section 3.5, Section 4, Equations 3–6).

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### 3.4.1 The Search-Integrated Reasoning Actor

The foundation of Search-R2 is an Actor policy, denoted as `$\pi_l(\cdot|x)$`, which is responsible for generating the initial reasoning trajectory `$\hat{y}$` given an input question `$x$` (Section 3.1). The Actor is trained to invoke an external search engine `$\Lambda$` autonomously following a standard tool-use paradigm, meaning it learns to decide when retrieval is necessary rather than following hardcoded rules. The model generates a chain of thought and, when it determines it lacks sufficient knowledge, emits a query within `<search>...</search>` tags that signals the system to halt generation and execute the query against the search engine `$\Lambda$`. After execution, the system appends the top-`$k$` retrieved results (where `$k=3$` passages by default, as stated in Section 5.1) within `<information>...</information>` tags and resumes generation from the Actor policy. This cycle of reasoning, searching, retrieving, and continuing repeats until the model outputs a final answer within `<answer>...</answer>` tags or reaches a predefined step limit of 4 assistant turns maximum (Section 5.1, Implementation Details).

To initialize `$\pi_l$`, the authors utilize a structural template (Table 1) that enforces a strict format: Reasoning → Search Call → Answer. This template acts as a **soft constraint** — it ensures adherence to the system's operational logic without imposing content-specific biases that might limit the model's ability to learn optimal search behaviors through reinforcement. The template explicitly instructs the model to conduct reasoning inside `` and `&lt;/think>` tags first, call the search engine via `&lt;search> query &lt;/search>` if knowledge is lacking, expect results in `&lt;information>...&lt;/information>` tags, and return the final answer in `&lt;answer>...&lt;/answer>` tags. This structured format is inherited from Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) to ensure fair comparison and compatibility with existing infrastructure.

The Actor shares underlying LLM weights with the Meta-Refiner components, which is a critical design choice that enables joint optimization — both components are updated from the same gradient signals during training, allowing them to co-adapt rather than operating as independent modules. The model backbones used in experiments span multiple scales: Qwen2.5-7B, Qwen3-8B, and Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct (Section 5.1, Methods), demonstrating that the framework is effective across different model capacities.

#### 3.4.2 The Meta-Refiner for Hierarchical Correction

A core premise of Search-R2 is that suboptimal search decisions often occur in intermediate steps and silently misguide subsequent reasoning, creating error propagation loops that standard rejection sampling cannot efficiently repair (Section 3.2). The Meta-Refiner addresses this by performing **targeted causal intervention** rather than blind regeneration — it identifies the specific point of failure and regenerates only the flawed suffix while preserving valid reasoning prefixes. The Meta-Refiner shares the underlying LLM with the Actor but is steered by distinct control prompts to perform two sub-objectives: global coherence checking via the Discriminator and local error localization via the Trimmer.

**Discriminator for Global Coherence Checking.** The Discriminator, denoted `$\pi_d(\hat{y} | x) \in [0, 1]$`, serves as a gate that enforces trajectory-level reasoning coherence (Section 3.2). Given a reasoning trajectory `$\hat{y}$` and input `$x$`, it estimates the probability that the reasoning remains globally coherent with the problem specification. The system accepts `$\hat{y}$` when `$\pi_d(\hat{y} | x) \geq \tau$`, where `$\tau$` is a predefined threshold; otherwise, the trajectory is flagged for refinement. Accordingly, the acceptance probability follows a Bernoulli distribution `$\alpha(\hat{y}|x) = P(\pi_d(\hat{y} | x) \geq \tau)$`. This discrimination step is crucial because it prevents the system from wasting computational resources on trajectories that are already sound, while ensuring that flawed trajectories receive corrective attention. The Discriminator is not a separate model but rather the same LLM weights operating under a different prompt context (Table 12 in Appendix L provides the Meta-Refiner prompt), which enables efficient joint training without additional parameter overhead.

**Trimmer for Local Error Localization.** To address error propagation, the Trimmer `$\pi_h(k | \hat{y}, x)$` identifies the specific search step `$k + 1$` where the reasoning or search query first deviated — this is the "root cause" of the failure (Section 3.2). The system preserves the valid prefix `$\hat{y}_{1:k}$`, truncates the flawed suffix, and regenerates a new suffix using the base policy `$\pi_l$`. This **"cut-and-regenerate" strategy** preserves valuable partial reasoning, significantly improving sample efficiency compared to discarding the entire trajectory as rejection sampling does. For example, if a trajectory contains 4 reasoning steps and the error occurs at step 2, the Trimmer cuts after step 1 and regenerates steps 2–4, rather than discarding all 4 steps and starting from scratch.

**Iterative Accept-or-Repair Procedure.** Together, the Discriminator and Trimmer implement an iterative accept-or-repair procedure formalized in Algorithm 1 (Section 3.2). For each candidate trajectory, the Discriminator first decides whether it is globally coherent. If it is rejected, the Trimmer localizes the earliest deviation and triggers cut-and-regenerate editing to produce a revised trajectory. This collaborative process induces a **smoothed mixture policy** `$q(y | x)$`, which is mathematically characterized in Section 4.1. The procedure repeats up to a budget `$N_{\text{max}}$` (default `$N_{\text{max}} = 1$` revision per trajectory, as stated in Section 5.1), yielding progressively improved trajectories and accumulating correction history that strengthens the Meta-Refiner's ability to localize errors over time. The iterative nature is important: even if the first refinement does not produce a perfect trajectory, the system can apply additional corrections within the budget, though experiments show diminishing returns beyond one revision (Section 5.4, Table 4).

**Why Cut-and-Regenerate Over Rejection Sampling.** The design choice to use cut-and-regenerate rather than rejection sampling is motivated by both theoretical and practical considerations. Rejection sampling discards entire trajectories upon failure, which wastes computation on valid prefixes and provides no learning signal about *where* the error occurred. In contrast, cut-and-regenerate preserves correct work and explicitly trains the Trimmer to identify failure points, creating a dense supervision signal for error localization. The theoretical analysis in Section 4 proves that under satisfiable conditions, this selective correction yields strict performance gains over rejection sampling — a guarantee that prior work does not provide.

#### 3.4.3 Hybrid Reward Modeling for Multi-Scale Supervision

To tackle the credit assignment issue where local search actions are conflated with global outcomes, Search-R2 introduces a hybrid reward `$R(y)$` that provides supervision at both trajectory and step levels (Section 3.3). This is the key innovation that enables fine-grained learning: instead of a single binary signal indicating final answer correctness, the system receives differentiated feedback about search quality independent of outcome.

**Global Outcome Reward.** The outcome reward component uses Exact Match (EM) between the predicted answer `$a_{\text{pred}}$` and ground truth `$a_{\text{gold}}$`:

$$
r_{\text{outcome}}(y) = \mathbb{I}(a_{\text{pred}} = a_{\text{gold}})
$$

where `$\mathbb{I}(\cdot)$` is the indicator function that returns 1 if the condition is true and 0 otherwise. This ensures the final output satisfies the user's intent and prevents the model from optimizing for search quality at the expense of task completion. The outcome reward is binary — either the answer matches exactly or it does not — which is standard for question answering benchmarks but creates the sparse supervision problem that the process reward is designed to mitigate.

**Local Process Reward.** To distinguish between trajectories that are correct by chance versus those supported by high-quality evidence, the system quantifies the utility of retrieved context (Section 3.3). For a set of retrieved chunks `$C = \{c_1, \ldots, c_M\}$`, where `$M$` is the number of search actions in the trajectory, an external judge evaluates the utility `$u_i \in \{0, 1\}$` of each chunk. The process reward is the density of useful information:

$$
r_{\text{process}}(y) = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{i=1}^{M} u_i
$$

Implementation specifics are outlined in Appendix K: the judge is a separate LLM (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B in the experiments) that evaluates each collection of retrieved documents against three strict criteria (Table 11). A collection is marked useful (`$u_i = 1$`) if it contains information or clues that help identify the correct answer, even partially. A collection is marked not useful (`$u_i = 0$`) if it is completely irrelevant or if it merely duplicates information from previous collections without adding new insights. This density-based approach incentivizes the model to perform efficient, non-redundant, and relevant searches rather than maximizing the number of queries.

**Overall Reward with Outcome Gating.** To prevent reward hacking — where the agent might maximize retrieval scores without actually solving the task — the process reward is gated by the outcome reward (Section 3.3, Equation 1):

$$
R(y) = r_{\text{outcome}}(y) \cdot (1 + r_{\text{process}}(y))
$$

This formulation explicitly reinforces the principle that high-quality search is a **necessary condition** for robust reasoning, not a substitute for it. If the final answer is incorrect (`$r_{\text{outcome}} = 0$`), the total reward is zero regardless of search quality, preventing the model from learning to retrieve useful information while failing to produce correct answers. If the answer is correct (`$r_{\text{outcome}} = 1$`), the process reward provides additional signal distinguishing efficient, high-density retrieval from redundant or lucky searches. The `$(1 + r_{\text{process}})$` term ensures the process reward acts as a multiplier that scales the outcome reward between 1.0 (correct answer, no useful retrieval) and 2.0 (correct answer, all retrieval useful), providing meaningful gradient signal without overwhelming the outcome objective.

**Why Hybrid Over Outcome-Only.** The design choice to combine outcome and process rewards addresses a fundamental limitation of trajectory-level RL: when only the final answer is evaluated, the model cannot distinguish between a correct answer derived from sound reasoning with high-quality evidence versus a correct answer derived from lucky guesses with irrelevant retrieval. The process reward provides **dense supervision** at the step level, enabling the model to learn which search behaviors are genuinely useful. The outcome gating prevents the model from optimizing search quality at the expense of task completion, ensuring the two objectives remain aligned.

#### 3.4.4 Joint Optimization via Group Relative Policy Optimization

Search-R2 leverages Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) to optimize the shared weights `$\theta$` of the Actor and Meta-Refiner jointly (Section 3.4, citing Shao et al., 2024). This is a critical architectural decision: rather than training the Actor and Meta-Refiner separately or freezing one while updating the other, both components learn from the same gradient signals, enabling them to co-adapt and develop complementary capabilities.

For each input `$x$`, the system samples a group of `$G$` trajectories `$\{y_1, \ldots, y_G\}$` from the mixture distribution `$q(\cdot|x)$` (Section 3.4). Crucially, each `$y_i$` is treated as an **augmented execution trace** comprising both the reasoning path from `$\pi_l$` and refinement actions sampled from the Discriminator `$\pi_d(y)$` and Trimmer `$\pi_h(k | \hat{y})$`. This means the policy gradient updates account for the entire interaction history — generation decisions, acceptance/rejection decisions, and cut-point selections — as a unified trajectory rather than separate optimization problems.

The optimization objective is to maximize:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{GRPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim q} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^{G} \frac{1}{L_i} \sum_{t=1}^{L_i} \mathcal{L}_t(y_i, \theta) \right]
$$

where `$L_i$` is the length of trajectory `$y_i$`, and the per-token loss is:

$$
\mathcal{L}_t(y_i, \theta) = \left[ r_t(\theta) \hat{A}_{i,t}, \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1 - \epsilon, 1 + \epsilon) \hat{A}_{i,t} \right] - \beta D_{\text{KL}}[\pi_l || \pi_{\text{ref}}]
$$

Here, `$r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t|s_t)}$` denotes the probability ratio measuring the deviation of the current policy from the old policy, `$\hat{A}_{i,t}$` is the advantage estimate computed via group normalization of the hybrid rewards, `$\epsilon$` is the clipping parameter (standard in PPO-style algorithms to prevent large policy updates), and `$\beta D_{\text{KL}}[\pi_l || \pi_{\text{ref}}]$` is a KL divergence penalty that regularizes the policy toward a reference distribution to prevent collapse (Section E, Appendix: coefficient 0.001).

**Key Training Hyperparameters.** The implementation uses specific configurations that are important for reproducibility (Section 5.1, Appendix E):
- Learning rate: `$1 \times 10^{-6}$` with a warmup ratio of 0.285
- GRPO training steps: 300
- Prompts sampled per step: 512
- Rollouts per prompt: `$n = 5$` (default), with comparison experiments at `$n = 10$` in Appendix G
- Maximum assistant turns per trajectory: 4
- Maximum revision number per rollout: 1 (default), with sensitivity analysis from 1 to 4 in Section 5.4
- Context length cap during rollout: 15,000 tokens to accommodate interleaved reasoning and retrieved evidence
- KL penalty coefficient: 0.001 (low-variance, applied as regularization rather than incorporated into reward)
- Entropy regularization: disabled

**Why Joint Optimization Over Separate Training.** The decision to jointly optimize the Actor and Meta-Refiner rather than training them separately is motivated by the need for **co-adaptation**. If the Meta-Refiner were trained on fixed Actor outputs, it would learn to correct errors specific to the initial policy but might not generalize as the Actor improves. Conversely, if the Actor were trained without Meta-Refiner feedback, it would not internalize the refinement capability. Joint optimization ensures that the Actor learns to generate trajectories that are easier to refine when necessary, while the Meta-Refiner learns to identify errors that the Actor is prone to make. This mutual feedback loop is enabled by treating refinement actions (accept/reject, cut-point selection) as part of the augmented trajectory that GRPO optimizes.

**Why GRPO Over Standard PPO.** The paper uses GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) rather than standard PPO because GRPO computes advantages via **group normalization** within each batch of rollouts for the same prompt, rather than relying on a separate value function critic. This reduces training complexity by eliminating the need to train a value model, which is particularly beneficial in search-integrated settings where the state space includes both text tokens and retrieval results. The group-relative advantage `$\hat{A}_i$` is computed by normalizing the hybrid rewards `$R(y_i)$` across the `$G$` trajectories for input `$x$`, providing a stable learning signal without additional model components.

#### 3.4.5 Mechanisms of Performance Gain: Theoretical Decomposition

Unlike prior work that optimizes only the Actor, Search-R2 jointly optimizes both the Actor and the Meta-Refiner, and the paper provides a formal theoretical analysis to justify this design (Section 3.5, Section 4). The analysis decomposes the total expected sample reward improvement `$\Delta J$` into three governing mechanisms, proving that net performance gain is not a byproduct of mere sampling volume but strictly depends on the agent's ability to satisfy specific covariance conditions.

The performance gain decomposition is characterized as (Section 3.5, Equation 3):

$$
\Delta J = \underbrace{A_{\text{prec}}}_{\text{Selection Precision}} + \underbrace{V_{\text{inter}}}_{\text{Intervention Volume}} \times \underbrace{S_{\text{trim}}}_{\text{Trimming Skill}}
$$

Each term is formally defined and has a specific interpretation:

**Selection Precision `$A_{\text{prec}}$`.** This term represents the system's capacity for global evaluation and is mathematically defined as `$\text{Cov}_{\pi_l}(\alpha(y), R(y) - J_{\text{trim}}(y))$` (Section 3.5, Definition D.1 in Appendix D). It measures the alignment between the Discriminator's acceptance probability `$\alpha(y)$` and the trajectory's relative quality (current reward minus expected reward after correction). A positive `$A_{\text{prec}}$` implies the Discriminator successfully distinguishes which trajectories are worth preserving while exposing chains requiring correction (e.g., those containing hallucinations or redundant steps) to the refinement process. By treating the entire interaction trace — reasoning, decision-to-accept, and decision-to-cut — as a single unified trajectory, GRPO naturally maximizes this covariance without requiring separate supervision signals for the Meta-Refiner.

**Trimming Skill `$S_{\text{trim}}$`.** This term quantifies the effectiveness of the "cut-and-regenerate" mechanism and is defined as `$\sum_k \text{Cov}(\pi_h(k|y), G_k(y))$` (Section 3.5, Definition D.2 in Appendix D). It measures the correlation between the selected cut-point `$k$` and the expected gain `$G_k(y)$` from regenerating at that specific step. Therefore, a positive `$S_{\text{trim}}$` indicates that the Trimmer precisely locates the specific low-quality search action that caused the reasoning collapse, such as a failed search query or a logic error, where the trajectory first deviated. This behavior is reinforced by propagating the outcome reward back to the specific cut-point selection `$k$`, encouraging the agent to target pivotal moments of failure rather than cutting at random positions.

**Intervention Volume `$V_{\text{inter}}$`.** Defined as `$1 - \mathbb{E}[\alpha(y)]$`, this term represents the volume of trajectories subjected to correction (Section 3.5, Definition D.3 in Appendix D). It acts as a multiplier in Equation 3. Even a highly skilled Trimmer (`$S_{\text{trim}} > 0$`) contributes little if the Discriminator is overly conservative (accepting flawed answers, `$V_{\text{inter}} \to 0$`). Conversely, if the Discriminator flags valid answers (`$V_{\text{inter}} \to 1$`) while the Trimmer is unskilled, the computational budget is wasted. The system must find a balance between exploration and exploitation, ensuring that it neither overlooks errors nor wastes resources. The joint optimization seeks an equilibrium where `$V_{\text{inter}}$` is sufficiently large to correct errors but constrained enough to preserve sample efficiency.

**Formal Performance Decomposition (Proposition 4.1).** Section 4.1 provides a rigorous derivation of the performance gain. Let the induced trajectory distribution `$q(y | x)$` of the Meta-Refiner be formalized as a mixture policy:

$$
q(y | x) = \pi_l(y | x)\alpha(y) + \int_{\hat{y}} \pi_l(\hat{y} | x)(1 - \alpha(\hat{y}))T'(y | x, \hat{y}) \, d\hat{y}
$$

where `$\pi_l$` is the base policy, `$\alpha(y) \in [0, 1]$` is the acceptance probability, and `$T'(y | x, \hat{y})$` is the normalized transition distribution of the Trimmer for cutting and regenerating a rejected sample `$\hat{y}$`. The expected reward `$J_{\text{meta}} = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim q}[R(y)]$` decomposes relative to the base performance `$J_{\text{base}} = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_l}[R(y)]$` as (Section 4.1, Equation 5):

$$
J_{\text{meta}} = J_{\text{base}} + \underbrace{\text{Cov}_{\pi_l}(\alpha(y), R(y) - J_{\text{trim}}(y))}_{\text{Selection Precision}} + \underbrace{(1 - Z_{\text{acc}})(\bar{J}_{\text{trim}} - J_{\text{base}})}_{\text{Correction Volume Gain}}
$$

where `$Z_{\text{acc}} = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_l}[\alpha(y)]$` is the global acceptance rate, `$J_{\text{trim}}(\hat{y}) = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim T'(\cdot|\hat{y})}[R(y)]$` is the expected reward after correcting `$\hat{y}$`, and `$\bar{J}_{\text{trim}} = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_l}[J_{\text{trim}}(\hat{y})]$`.

**Decomposition of Correction Volume Gain (Proposition 4.2).** Section 4.2 further analyzes the correction volume gain term `$\Delta J_{\text{trim}} = \bar{J}_{\text{trim}} - J_{\text{base}}$`. Let `$G_k(\hat{y}) = V_{\pi_l}(\hat{y}_{1:k}) - R(\hat{y})$` denote the regeneration gain at step `$k$`, where `$V_{\pi_l}(\hat{y}_{1:k})$` is the value of regenerating the suffix from `$k$`. The total correction gain decomposes as (Section 4.2, Equation 6):

$$
\Delta J_{\text{trim}} = \underbrace{\sum_{k=1}^{T} \text{Cov}_{\hat{y}}(\pi_h(k|\hat{y}), G_k(\hat{y}))}_{\text{Trimming Skill}} + \underbrace{\bar{G}(\hat{y})}_{\text{Baseline Gain}}
$$

where `$\bar{G}(\hat{y}) = \sum_k \mathbb{E}[\pi_h(k|\hat{y})]\mathbb{E}[G_k(\hat{y})]$` denotes the baseline gain. In high-dimensional reasoning tasks, arbitrarily truncating and regenerating a trajectory rarely improves the outcome (i.e., `$\mathbb{E}[G_k(\hat{y})] \approx 0$` for random `$k$`), implying that maximizing the correction gain `$\Delta J_{\text{trim}}$` relies almost entirely on the Trimmer's skill in selecting precise cut-points.

**Summary of Success Conditions.** Unlike standard RAG or Rejection Sampling, which rely solely on the Actor policy's generation probability, Search-R2 achieves a net positive gain (`$\Delta J > 0$`) for each rollout if and only if three conditions are met simultaneously (Section 3.5):
1. `$A_{\text{prec}} > 0$`: The Discriminator must successfully identify low-quality trajectories for correction.
2. `$S_{\text{trim}} > 0$`: The Trimmer must accurately locate the root cause of errors.
3. `$V_{\text{inter}}$` must be calibrated: The intervention volume must expose sufficient samples for refinement without suppressing high-quality outputs.

Furthermore, the Meta-Refiner supports iterative execution within `$N_{\text{max}}$`, where the posterior `$q(\cdot|x)$` from iteration `$t$` serves as the base policy for `$t + 1$`. The conditions for improvement remain valid in recursive settings, meaning the theoretical guarantees hold even when multiple refinement iterations are applied.

**Why Theoretical Analysis Matters.** This formal decomposition is not merely academic — it provides actionable insights for system design. If empirical performance plateaus, the decomposition indicates which component to target: low `$A_{\text{prec}}$` suggests the Discriminator needs better training signals, low `$S_{\text{trim}}$` suggests the Trimmer cannot localize errors accurately, and inappropriate `$V_{\text{inter}}$` suggests the acceptance threshold `$\tau$` needs tuning. This level of diagnostic granularity is absent from prior work that treats search-integrated reasoning as a black-box optimization problem.

#### 3.4.6 Design Choices and Alternatives Considered

Several critical design choices distinguish Search-R2 from alternative approaches, and the paper provides empirical and theoretical justification for each.

**Shared Weights vs. Separate Models.** The Actor and Meta-Refiner share underlying LLM weights rather than using separate models. This choice reduces parameter overhead and enables true joint optimization — gradient signals from refinement actions directly update the generation policy. A separate Meta-Refiner model would require distillation or alternating training, complicating the optimization landscape and increasing memory requirements.

**Cut-and-Regenerate vs. Full Regeneration.** The cut-and-regenerate mechanism preserves valid prefixes rather than regenerating entire trajectories. This is more sample-efficient because correct early reasoning steps are not discarded. The theoretical analysis (Section 4) proves that this selective correction yields strict performance gains over rejection sampling under satisfiable conditions, whereas full regeneration provides no such guarantee.

**Hybrid Reward vs. Outcome-Only.** The hybrid reward combines outcome correctness with process-level evidence density. Outcome-only rewards create the multi-scale credit assignment problem that Search-R2 is designed to solve. Process-only rewards would incentivize retrieval without task completion. The outcome-gated formulation (`$R(y) = r_{\text{outcome}} \cdot (1 + r_{\text{process}})$`) ensures both objectives are aligned.

**GRPO vs. PPO with Value Critic.** GRPO eliminates the need for a separate value function by computing advantages via group normalization. This reduces training complexity and avoids the instability that can arise from training a value model on sparse, delayed rewards in search-integrated settings.

**Single Revision Default vs. Multiple Iterations.** The default maximum revision is set to 1, though the system supports up to 4 iterations. Section 5.4 (Table 4) shows diminishing returns beyond one revision: increasing from 1 to 2 revisions yields 0.9 EM points, but increasing from 3 to 4 yields only 0.3 points. Figure 3 shows most trajectories trigger at most one revision even with higher limits. Setting `$N_{\text{max}} = 1$` captures most of the benefit at low computational cost, making the framework practical for deployment.

**External Judge for Process Reward vs. Learned Reward Model.** The process reward uses an external LLM judge (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B) rather than a learned reward model. This avoids the need to train a separate reward model and ensures consistent evaluation criteria across experiments. The judge prompt (Table 11) enforces strict criteria for relevance and non-redundancy, providing reliable supervision without additional training overhead.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the conceptual breakthroughs that distinguish Search-R2 from prior work. Rather than rehashing the mechanical details covered in Section 3, we focus on **why** these innovations matter — what fundamental assumptions they challenge, what new capabilities they enable, and what makes them more than incremental improvements.

### 4.1 The Actor–Refiner Paradigm: From Monolithic Generation to Learned Debugging

**What makes this novel:** Prior search-integrated reasoning systems treat trajectory generation as a **monolithic process** — the model produces a complete chain of thought and search queries, and the system either accepts or rejects the entire output. Search-R2 fundamentally restructures this by introducing a **learned debugging mechanism** that operates collaboratively with generation.

**Key distinction from prior work:**

| Aspect | Prior Approaches | Search-R2 |
|--------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Error handling** | Rejection sampling discards entire trajectories (Ahn et al., 2024) | **Surgical repair** preserves valid prefixes (Section 3.2) |
| **Refinement capability** | Static prompts or separate verification models (Zha et al., 2025) | **Jointly optimized** Meta-Refiner sharing weights with Actor |
| **Learning signal** | Outcome-only feedback | **Augmented traces** include refinement decisions as learnable actions |

**Why this is a fundamental innovation:** The Actor–Refiner architecture internalizes the ability to **diagnose and correct** reasoning errors rather than merely filtering them post-hoc. This is analogous to the difference between a student who erases an entire exam upon finding one mistake versus a student who identifies and corrects only the flawed steps. The theoretical analysis in Section 4 proves this yields **strict performance gains** over rejection sampling under satisfiable conditions — a guarantee that prior work does not provide.

**Significance:** This paradigm shift enables **sample-efficient learning** in long-horizon reasoning tasks. As shown in Table 2, Search-R2 with a 7B model (40.4% average EM) outperforms Search-R1 with an 8B model (40.0% average EM), demonstrating that the collaborative architecture compensates for reduced model scale by optimizing reasoning quality rather than relying on parameter count alone.

### 4.2 Theoretical Performance Decomposition: Provable Conditions for Improvement

**What makes this novel:** Most RL-based reasoning papers report empirical improvements without formal guarantees. Search-R2 provides a **mathematical decomposition** of performance gain into three interpretable components (Section 3.5, Equation 3):

$$
\Delta J = A_{\text{prec}} + V_{\text{inter}} \times S_{\text{trim}}
$$

where `$A_{\text{prec}}$` (Selection Precision), `$V_{\text{inter}}$` (Intervention Volume), and `$S_{\text{trim}}$` (Trimming Skill) are formally defined covariance terms.

**Key distinction from prior work:**

| Aspect | Prior Approaches | Search-R2 |
|--------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Performance analysis** | Empirical comparisons only | **Formal decomposition** with provable conditions |
| **Diagnostic granularity** | Black-box optimization | **Component-level attribution** of gains |
| **Guarantees** | No theoretical bounds | **Strict improvement** over baselines when `$A_{\text{prec}} > 0$`, `$S_{\text{trim}} > 0$` |

**Why this is a fundamental innovation:** The decomposition transforms system design from **heuristic tuning** to **diagnostic engineering**. If performance plateaus, the decomposition indicates which component to target:
- Low `$A_{\text{prec}}$` → Discriminator cannot distinguish good from bad trajectories
- Low `$S_{\text{trim}}$` → Trimmer cannot localize error roots accurately
- Inappropriate `$V_{\text{inter}}$` → Acceptance threshold `$\tau$` needs calibration

This level of interpretability is rare in RL-based reasoning systems, where performance improvements are often attributed to "better optimization" without mechanistic understanding.

**Significance:** Proposition 4.1 (Section 4.1) proves that the Meta-Refiner distribution `$q(y|x)$` is a **smoothed mixture policy** with expected reward strictly exceeding the base policy when the covariance conditions are satisfied. This provides a **theoretical foundation** for why targeted intervention outperforms brute-force sampling — not just an empirical observation but a mathematically characterized advantage.

### 4.3 Hybrid Reward with Outcome Gating: Solving the Reward Hacking Problem

**What makes this novel:** Process rewards (dense step-level feedback) are known to improve learning in long-horizon tasks, but they introduce a critical vulnerability: **reward hacking**, where the agent maximizes intermediate signals without completing the task. Search-R2's outcome-gated formulation (Section 3.3, Equation 1):

$$
R(y) = r_{\text{outcome}}(y) \cdot (1 + r_{\text{process}}(y))
$$

ensures that process rewards **only amplify** outcome rewards, never substitute for them.

**Key distinction from prior work:**

| Aspect | Prior Approaches | Search-R2 |
|--------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Process reward role** | Additive component (Zhang et al., 2025a) | **Multiplicative gate** requiring outcome correctness |
| **Reward hacking risk** | Agent can maximize process score with wrong answer | **Impossible** — `$r_{\text{outcome}} = 0$` zeroes total reward |
| **Supervision density** | Sparse (outcome-only) or dense (process-only) | **Multi-scale** — both trajectory and step levels |

**Why this is a fundamental innovation:** The gating mechanism enforces a **logical dependency**: high-quality search is a *necessary condition* for robust reasoning, not a substitute for it. This prevents the pathological behavior where agents issue many high-quality queries but fail to produce correct answers — a failure mode observed in prior process reward approaches (Wen et al., 2026).

**Significance:** The ablation study in Table 3 shows that adding the process reward to Search-R1 + Meta-Refiner yields consistent gains (+0.7% on Qwen2.5-7B, +0.6% on Qwen3-8B, +0.2% on Qwen2.5-32B), but the **full Search-R2 with joint optimization** achieves the highest accuracy. This confirms that the hybrid reward is most effective when the Actor and Meta-Refiner co-adapt to leverage both signal types — the process reward guides search quality while the outcome gate ensures task completion.

### 4.4 Sample Efficiency Through Targeted Intervention: Quality Over Quantity

**What makes this novel:** A common approach to improving RL performance is increasing **sampling volume** — generating more rollouts per prompt to find better trajectories. Search-R2 demonstrates that **targeted refinement** of existing trajectories is more efficient than brute-force sampling.

**Empirical evidence:** Appendix G (Table 7) compares Search-R2 (`$n=5$` rollouts, max revision = 1) against Search-R1 with **doubled rollouts** (`$n=10$`). Despite generating fewer total trajectories (~3,300 vs. 5,120 per step), Search-R2 achieves **6.28% higher** average EM at step 300 (50.8% vs. 47.8%).

**Key distinction from prior work:**

| Aspect | Prior Approaches | Search-R2 |
|--------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Scaling strategy** | Increase rollout count (brute-force) | **Refine existing trajectories** (surgical) |
| **Computational cost** | Linear in rollout count | **Sublinear** — most trajectories need ≤1 revision (Figure 3) |
| **Learning signal** | Outcome-only per trajectory | **Augmented** — refinement decisions provide additional supervision |

**Why this is a fundamental innovation:** The cut-and-regenerate mechanism transforms **failed computation into learning signal**. When rejection sampling discards a trajectory, all computation on valid prefixes is wasted. Search-R2 preserves this work and uses the failure point as a **teaching moment** for the Trimmer. This is reflected in the training time analysis (Table 5): Search-R2 increases training time by only 2.43–8.66% relative to Search-R1, yet achieves substantially higher accuracy.

**Significance:** The cost-effectiveness ratio `$\Delta \text{EM}(\%) / \Delta \text{Time}(\%)$` exceeds 1 for all models and **improves with scale** (1.78 at 7B → 4.69 at 32B, Table 5). This indicates that Search-R2 becomes **more efficient** for larger models, where the marginal cost of refinement is dwarfed by the value of corrected reasoning. For practical deployment, this means the framework scales favorably — a critical consideration for production systems where compute costs dominate.

### 4.5 Distinguishing Incremental vs. Fundamental Contributions

To clarify the novelty landscape, we categorize Search-R2's contributions:

| Contribution | Type | Rationale |
|--------------|------|-----------|
| Actor–Refiner architecture | **Fundamental** | Restructures generation as collaborative debugging, not monolithic output |
| Theoretical performance decomposition | **Fundamental** | Provides provable conditions for improvement, rare in this domain |
| Hybrid reward with outcome gating | **Fundamental** | Solves reward hacking while enabling dense supervision |
| Cut-and-regenerate mechanism | **Incremental over prior refinement work** | Builds on self-correction ideas but adds joint optimization and theoretical guarantees |
| GRPO-based joint training | **Incremental** | Adapts existing RL algorithm to new architecture |
| Empirical benchmarks | **Incremental** | Standard QA datasets, though comprehensive across scales |

**The core innovation** is the **Actor–Refiner collaboration with theoretical guarantees** — this is what distinguishes Search-R2 from prior search-integrated reasoning systems. The empirical improvements (Table 2) are the *consequence* of this architectural shift, not the innovation itself.

**What remains open:** The theoretical analysis assumes "satisfiable conditions" for `$A_{\text{prec}} > 0$` and `$S_{\text{trim}} > 0$`, but does not characterize how often these conditions hold in practice across different task domains. Additionally, the external judge for process rewards introduces inference overhead that is not fully accounted for in the efficiency analysis — Appendix K notes the judge is a separate 7B model, but Table 5 only reports Actor training time. These are important considerations for deployment that future work should address.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines whether Search-R2's empirical evaluation substantiates its theoretical claims. We analyze the experimental methodology, scrutinize the quantitative results with specific numbers, assess the ablation studies that isolate component contributions, and identify any limitations or conditions under which the approach succeeds or struggles.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### 5.1.1 Datasets and Task Categories

The authors evaluate Search-R2 across **seven benchmarks** spanning two distinct task categories (Section 5.1):

**General Question Answering (3 datasets):**
- **NQ** (Natural Questions, Kwiatkowski et al., 2019): In-domain training distribution, contains real Google search queries with human-annotated answers
- **TriviaQA** (Joshi et al., 2017): Out-of-domain, consists of trivia-style questions with verified answers
- **PopQA** (Mallen et al., 2022): Out-of-domain, focuses on popular entity-centric questions

**Multi-Hop Question Answering (4 datasets):**
- **HotpotQA** (Yang et al., 2018): In-domain training distribution, requires reasoning across multiple Wikipedia paragraphs
- **2WikiMultiHopQA** (Ho et al., 2020): Out-of-domain, explicitly constructed multi-hop questions with reasoning paths
- **Musique** (Trivedi et al., 2022): Out-of-domain, compositional multi-hop questions requiring 2-4 reasoning steps
- **Bamboogle** (Press et al., 2023): Out-of-domain, challenging multi-hop questions designed to break standard retrieval systems

**Training Configuration:** Models are trained on the **union of NQ and HotpotQA training splits**, then evaluated on validation or test splits of all seven datasets. This design enables measurement of both **in-domain performance** (NQ, HotpotQA) and **out-of-domain generalization** (the remaining five datasets). The inclusion of four multi-hop datasets is critical — these tasks are precisely where error propagation from early retrieval mistakes should be most damaging, making them ideal for testing Search-R2's refinement capabilities.

#### 5.1.2 Baselines and Comparison Groups

The paper compares Search-R2 against **four families of baselines** (Section 5.1, Table 2):

| Baseline Family | Methods | Purpose |
|-----------------|---------|---------|
| **Inference without retrieval** | Direct Inference, Chain-of-Thought (CoT) | Establishes lower bound — performance without any search capability |
| **Inference with retrieval** | RAG, IRCoT, Search-o1 | Tests against standard retrieval-augmented approaches with iterative search |
| **Fine-tuning based** | SFT, R1-base, R1-instruct, Rejection Sampling | Compares against RL-based training methods without refinement |
| **Reference backbone** | Search-R1 (Qwen2.5-7B, Qwen3-8B, Qwen2.5-32B) | Direct predecessor — isolates the contribution of Actor-Refiner collaboration |

**Critical baseline choice:** Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) serves as the **primary comparison** because it shares the same infrastructure (template format, GRPO optimization, search engine integration) but lacks the Meta-Refiner. This ensures that performance differences can be attributed to the Actor-Refiner architecture rather than implementation details. The paper tests all methods on **three model backbones** (Qwen2.5-7B, Qwen3-8B, Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct) to demonstrate scalability across model sizes.

#### 5.1.3 Metrics and Evaluation Protocol

**Primary Metric:** **Exact Match (EM)** — a binary score indicating whether the predicted answer exactly matches the ground truth (Section 5.1, Implementation Details). This is standard for question answering benchmarks but has limitations: it does not capture partial correctness or reasoning quality, which is why the authors supplement it with trajectory quality analysis (Section 5.6).

**Training Protocol:**
- **Optimizer:** GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) for 300 steps
- **Batch size:** 512 prompts sampled per step
- **Rollouts per prompt:** `$n = 5$` (default), with comparison experiments at `$n = 10$` in Appendix G
- **Maximum assistant turns:** 4 per trajectory
- **Maximum revisions:** 1 per trajectory (default), with sensitivity analysis from 1 to 4 in Section 5.4
- **Learning rate:** `$1 \times 10^{-6}$` with warmup ratio of 0.285
- **Context length cap:** 15,000 tokens during rollout to accommodate interleaved reasoning and retrieved evidence
- **KL penalty coefficient:** 0.001 (low-variance, applied as regularization)

**Retrieval Configuration:**
- **Retriever:** E5 (Wang et al., 2022)
- **Knowledge source:** 2018 Wikipedia dump (Karpukhin et al., 2020)
- **Retrieved passages per query:** `$k = 3$` (fixed across all methods for fairness)

**Trajectory Quality Evaluation (Section 5.6):**
Beyond EM scores, the authors use **GPT-5.1 as an automated judge** to evaluate trajectories across six dimensions:
1. Evidence Groundedness
2. Information Density
3. Non-Redundancy Efficiency
4. Query Timing Quality
5. Trajectory Coherence
6. Uncertainty Handling

For each of the seven datasets, 100 paired trajectories are sampled (700 pairs total), with Search-R1 and Search-R2 evaluated on the **same prompts** to enable direct comparison. The judge assigns independent three-level scores (0 = poor, 1 = acceptable, 2 = strong), and wins/failures are recorded based on which trajectory scores higher.

| Qwen2.5-7B | 35.0% | 40.4% | +5.4 points | +15.4% |

**Key observation 1 — Scale compensation:** Search-R2 with the **7B model (40.4%)** outperforms Search-R1 with the **8B model (40.0%)**, despite having fewer parameters. This confirms the paper's claim that the Actor-Refiner framework "effectively compensates for reduced model scale by optimizing reasoning quality" (Section 5.2). The refinement mechanism extracts more value from each trajectory, allowing smaller models to compete with larger ones.

**Key observation 2 — Consistent scaling:** When scaling from 7B to 32B, Search-R2 achieves a **+10.4 point gain** (40.4% → 50.8%), while Search-R1 achieves only **+10.6 points** (35.0% → 45.6%). The similar scaling slope indicates that Search-R2's benefits **compound with model capacity** rather than diminishing — larger models can leverage the refinement mechanism more effectively.

**Key observation 3 — Multi-hop advantage:** Performance gains are **more pronounced on complex reasoning tasks**. On Bamboogle (the most challenging multi-hop dataset):
- Qwen2.5-32B: Search-R1 = 45.0%, Search-R2 = 56.4% (**+11.4 points, +25.3% relative**)
- Qwen2.5-7B: Search-R1 = 36.0%, Search-R2 = 46.2% (**+10.2 points, +28.3% relative**)

- Qwen2.5-7B: Search-R1 = 29.7%, Search-R2 = 35.8% (**+6.1 points**)

This pattern supports the theoretical claim that Search-R2's cut-and-regenerate mechanism is most valuable when **error propagation risk is highest** — precisely the scenario in multi-hop reasoning where early retrieval mistakes cascade through subsequent steps.

The gap between Search-R2 and Rejection Sampling (+5.6 points at 7B) is particularly important — both methods use the same search engine and similar training infrastructure, but Search-R2's cut-and-regenerate mechanism extracts more value from failed trajectories rather than discarding them.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies (Table 3)

The authors conduct a **component-wise ablation** to isolate the contribution of each Search-R2 innovation. Table 3 shows results for incrementally adding modules to the Search-R1 baseline:

| Configuration | Qwen2.5-7B Avg | Qwen3-8B Avg | Qwen2.5-32B Avg |
|---------------|----------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Search-R1 (baseline) | 35.0% | 40.0% | 45.6% |
| + Meta-Refiner | 38.9% | 43.4% | 49.3% |
| + Meta-Refiner + Process Reward | 39.6% | 44.0% | 49.5% |
| **Search-R2 (Full: + Joint Optimization)** | **40.4%** | **44.6%** | **50.8%** |

**Key finding 1 — Meta-Refiner dominates:** Adding the Meta-Refiner alone yields the **largest single improvement**:
- Qwen2.5-7B: +3.9 points (35.0% → 38.9%, **+11.1% relative**)
- Qwen3-8B: +3.4 points (40.0% → 43.4%, **+8.5% relative**)
- Qwen2.5-32B: +3.7 points (45.6% → 49.3%, **+8.1% relative**)

This confirms the paper's central thesis: the ability to **surgically repair flawed trajectories** is the primary driver of performance gains, not the hybrid reward or joint optimization alone.

**Key finding 2 — Process Reward provides incremental gains:** Adding the process reward on top of Meta-Refiner yields smaller but consistent improvements:
- Qwen2.5-7B: +0.7 points (38.9% → 39.6%)
- Qwen3-8B: +0.6 points (43.4% → 44.0%)
- Qwen2.5-32B: +0.2 points (49.3% → 49.5%)

The diminishing returns at larger model scales suggest that **larger models already produce higher-quality retrieval** by default, so the process reward's guidance is less critical. However, even small gains are meaningful at the 32B scale where absolute improvements are harder to achieve.

**Key finding 3 — Joint Optimization is necessary:** The full Search-R2 configuration (with joint optimization of Actor and Meta-Refiner) achieves the highest accuracy across all models:
- Qwen2.5-7B: +0.8 points over Meta-Refiner + Process Reward alone (39.6% → 40.4%)
- Qwen3-8B: +0.6 points (44.0% → 44.6%)
- Qwen2.5-32B: +1.3 points (49.5% → 50.8%)

This validates the design choice to **train Actor and Meta-Refiner together** rather than freezing one component. The mutual feedback loop enables co-adaptation: the Actor learns to generate trajectories that are easier to refine, while the Meta-Refiner learns to identify errors the Actor is prone to make.

**Limitation noted by authors:** The ablation for intermediate configurations (Search-R1 + Meta-Refiner, + Process Reward) optimizes the policy "solely on reasoning traces, excluding intervention refinement from the Meta-Refiner" (Section 5.3). This means the ablation does not fully isolate the contribution of joint optimization — the Meta-Refiner is present but not fully integrated during training for intermediate steps. The authors acknowledge this space limitation and point to Appendix Table 8 for detailed per-dataset breakdowns.

### 5.4 Sensitivity to Maximum Revision Limit (Table 4, Figure 3)

The authors evaluate how performance changes with the **maximum number of revisions** allowed per trajectory, varying from 1 to 4 using Qwen2.5-32B as the backbone (Section 5.4). In these experiments, process reward modeling and joint optimization are disabled to isolate the effect of revision count.

| Max Revisions | NQ | TriviaQA | PopQA | HotpotQA | 2Wiki | Musique | Bamboogle | **Average** |
|---------------|----|----------|-------|----------|-------|---------|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | 50.8 | 69.4 | 49.0 | 47.6 | 49.4 | 24.2 | 54.4 | **49.3%** |
| 2 | 50.9 | 71.0 | 50.6 | 48.7 | 50.7 | 25.5 | 54.4 | **50.2%** |
| 3 | 51.4 | 71.2 | 50.4 | 49.3 | 51.4 | 25.7 | 54.4 | **50.6%** |
| 4 | 51.6 | 71.2 | 50.8 | 49.3 | 51.6 | 26.0 | 55.6 | **50.9%** |

**Key finding — Diminishing returns:** The absolute EM gain drops sharply with each additional revision:
- 1 → 2 revisions: **+0.9 points**
- 2 → 3 revisions: **+0.4 points**
- 3 → 4 revisions: **+0.3 points**

This pattern suggests that **early revisions correct easily-fixable errors** (retrieval noise, shallow hallucinations), while remaining failures are less responsive to repeated refinement. The authors note: "most trajectories trigger at most one revision" (Section 5.4, referencing Figure 3), meaning that even with higher limits, harder cases rarely activate further refinement.

**Efficiency trade-off:** Notably, **max revision = 4 reaches 50.9%**, which essentially matches the **fully optimized Search-R2 with a single revision (50.8%)** from Table 2. This comparison highlights that the **joint optimization strategy successfully distills the benefits of a larger revision budget into a more efficient policy** that achieves comparable accuracy with one correction step (Section 5.4).

**Design decision:** Based on this analysis, the authors set **max revision = 1 as the default operating point**, which "captures most of the benefit at low revision cost" (Section 5.4). This is a practical consideration for deployment — allowing multiple revisions would increase inference latency and training complexity with minimal accuracy gains.

A critical question for any refinement-based approach is whether the accuracy gains justify the **computational overhead**. Table 5 reports average training time per step for Search-R1 vs Search-R2:

| Model | Search-R1 (sec/step) | Search-R2 (sec/step) | Relative Change | ΔEM (%) | ΔTime (%) | **Cost-Effectiveness Ratio** |
|-------|---------------------|---------------------|-----------------|---------|-----------|------------------------------|
| Qwen2.5-7B | 177.8 | 193.2 | +8.66% | 15.4% | 8.66% | **1.78** |
| Qwen3-8B | 141.5 | 147.3 | +4.10% | 11.5% | 4.10% | **2.80** |
| Qwen2.5-32B | 458.4 | 469.5 | +2.43% | 11.4% | 2.43% | **4.69** |

**Key finding 1 — Modest overhead:** Search-R2 increases training time by only **2.43–8.66%** relative to Search-R1, despite the additional Meta-Refiner computation. The authors attribute this to the cut-and-regenerate mechanism, which "preserves valid prefixes rather than discarding entire trajectories, thereby reducing wasted computation" (Section 5.5).

**Key finding 2 — Overhead decreases with scale:** The relative overhead **drops from 8.66% at 7B to 2.43% at 32B**. This occurs because distributed training overhead (communication, synchronization) grows with model size, making the marginal refinement cost less significant. For large-scale deployment, Search-R2 becomes increasingly attractive.

**Key finding 3 — Cost-effectiveness improves with scale:** The ratio `$\Delta \text{EM}(\%) / \Delta \text{Time}(\%)$` measures accuracy improvement per unit increase in training time:
- 7B: 1.78 (accuracy gains 1.78× faster than time costs)
- 8B: 2.80
- 32B: 4.69

This indicates that Search-R2 becomes **more cost-effective for larger backbones** — a critical consideration for production systems where compute costs dominate. The authors conclude: "accuracy gains consistently outpace the added compute" (Section 5.5).

**Important caveat:** Table 5 reports **training time only**. The process reward computation requires an external LLM judge (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B, Appendix K), which introduces additional inference overhead during training that is **not included** in these numbers. The authors note this in Appendix K but do not quantify its impact on total training cost. For deployment, the Meta-Refiner is "decoupled" and introduces "no additional latency" at inference time (Section 5.5), but training costs are higher than Table 5 suggests.

### 5.6 Trajectory Quality Comparison (Figure 4, Table 9)

Beyond final answer accuracy, the authors evaluate **reasoning process quality** using GPT-5.1 as an automated judge across six dimensions (Section 5.6, Appendix I). Table 9 provides detailed win/loss counts for Search-R2 vs Search-R1:

| Dimension | Average Wins/Losses (Search-R2 / Search-R1) | Win Rate |
|-----------|---------------------------------------------|----------|
| Evidence Groundedness | 19.3 / 2.4 | 88.9% |
| Information Density | 36.4 / 6.3 | 85.2% |
| Non-Redundancy Efficiency | 32.0 / 4.7 | 87.2% |
| Query Timing Quality | 14.1 / 0.9 | 94.0% |
| Trajectory Coherence | 31.3 / 4.4 | 87.7% |
| Uncertainty Handling | 8.9 / 1.7 | 84.0% |

**Key finding — Consistent superiority:** Search-R2 outperforms Search-R1 across **all six dimensions** with win rates ranging from 84% to 94%. This confirms that the accuracy gains in Table 2 are accompanied by **genuinely improved reasoning processes**, not just lucky guesses.

**Notable pattern — Query Timing Quality:** The highest win rate (94.0%) is for Query Timing Quality, which measures whether searches are issued at appropriate moments with well-formed queries. This directly validates Search-R2's ability to learn **when to search** — a capability that trajectory-level rewards alone cannot teach.

**Notable pattern — Uncertainty Handling:** The lowest win rate (84.0%) is for Uncertainty Handling, which evaluates how the model responds to incomplete or ambiguous information. While still strong, this suggests that Search-R2's refinement mechanism is **less effective at teaching epistemic humility** — the model may still produce confident answers when evidence is insufficient. The authors do not discuss this limitation explicitly.

**Methodology concern:** The evaluation uses **GPT-5.1 as a judge**, which introduces potential bias — the judge may favor trajectories that resemble its own reasoning style. The authors mitigate this by using paired comparisons on the same prompts and providing detailed rubrics (Appendix I, Table 10), but the results should be interpreted with this limitation in mind.

### 5.7 Comparison Against Doubled Rollout Budget (Appendix G, Table 7)

To verify that Search-R2's gains stem from **targeted refinement** rather than increased computation, the authors compare against Search-R1 trained with **doubled rollouts** (`$n = 10$` vs default `$n = 5$`) (Appendix G, Table 7):

| Method | Rollouts | Max Revisions | Avg EM at Step 300 | Total Trajectories/Step | Time/Step (sec) |
|--------|----------|---------------|-------------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Search-R1 | 10 | 0 | 47.8% | 5,120 | 803.2 |
| Search-R2 | 5 | 1 | **50.8%** | ~3,300 | **469.5** |

**Key finding — Surgical correction beats brute-force sampling:** Search-R2 achieves **6.28% higher EM** (50.8% vs 47.8%) while generating **~35% fewer trajectories** (~3,300 vs 5,120) and requiring **41% less training time** (469.5 vs 803.2 seconds/step). This confirms the paper's claim that "surgical correction is substantially more sample-efficient than brute-force sampling" (Section 5.2).

**Why this matters:** A common approach to improving RL performance is increasing sampling volume — generating more rollouts to find better trajectories. This experiment demonstrates that **refining existing trajectories** is more efficient than generating more from scratch. The Meta-Refiner's ability to identify and correct specific flaws extracts more learning signal per trajectory than simply sampling more candidates.

### 5.8 Training Dynamics (Figure 5, Appendix F)

Appendix F visualizes EM scores across training steps (0 to 300 at 50-step intervals) for all three models and seven datasets (Figure 5). Key observations:

**Convergence pattern:** Performance **converges around 300 steps** across all models and datasets. Extending training beyond this point "yields negligible performance gains and increases the risk of model collapse due to instabilities such as train–inference mismatch and automatic mixed-precision overflow" (Appendix F). This suggests that 300 steps is an appropriate training budget for this framework.

**Scale gaps persist:** While Search-R2 enables smaller models to approach larger models' performance on some tasks (e.g., Qwen2.5-7B approaches Qwen2.5-32B on NQ and TriviaQA), **performance gaps between model sizes remain** on complex multi-hop tasks. This confirms that "parameter scale remains a critical factor in tool-use and reasoning" (Appendix F) — the Actor-Refiner architecture improves efficiency but does not eliminate the fundamental advantage of larger models.

#### 5.9.3 Limitations and Open Questions

**Limitation 1 — External judge cost not fully accounted for:** The process reward requires an external LLM judge (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B) to evaluate retrieved chunk utility (Appendix K). This introduces additional inference overhead during training that is **not included in Table 5's time measurements**. For large-scale training, this could be significant — evaluating 3 retrieved chunks per search across 5 rollouts per prompt for 512 prompts per step requires substantial compute. The authors should quantify this cost in future work.

**Limitation 2 — Theoretical conditions not empirically verified:** Section 3.5 derives conditions for performance improvement (`$A_{\text{prec}} > 0$`, `$S_{\text{trim}} > 0$`, calibrated `$V_{\text{inter}}$`), but the paper does not **measure these covariance terms** during training. Without empirical verification, we cannot confirm whether the theoretical conditions actually hold in practice or whether the observed gains arise from different mechanisms.

**Limitation 3 — Limited failure case analysis:** The paper does not systematically analyze **when Search-R2 fails**. For example:
- Does the Meta-Refiner ever "over-correct" valid trajectories?
- Are there task types where cut-and-regenerate is ineffective?
- What is the failure mode when both Actor and Meta-Refiner make errors?

Appendix F notes the risk of "model collapse" beyond 300 steps but does not characterize the failure patterns.

**Limitation 4 — Judge bias in trajectory quality evaluation:** Using GPT-5.1 as an automated judge introduces potential bias — the judge may favor trajectories that resemble its own reasoning style. While paired comparisons on the same prompts mitigate this, the results should be interpreted with caution. Human evaluation would provide stronger evidence but is not included.

**Limitation 5 — Generalization to non-QA tasks:** All seven datasets are question answering benchmarks. The paper claims Search-R2 applies to "knowledge-intensive tasks" broadly (Introduction), but there is **no evaluation on other task types** (e.g., code generation, mathematical reasoning, long-form writing). The effectiveness of cut-and-regenerate may differ for tasks without clear ground truth answers.

### 5.10 Summary of Experimental Findings

The experimental evaluation provides **strong empirical support** for Search-R2's core claims:

1. **Consistent accuracy gains:** Search-R2 outperforms Search-R1 by 4.6–5.4 percentage points across all model scales (Table 2), with larger gains on challenging multi-hop tasks (+11.4 points on Bamboogle at 32B).

2. **Component contributions are clear:** The Meta-Refiner provides the largest single improvement (+3.9 points at 7B), while process reward and joint optimization provide incremental but consistent gains (Table 3).

3. **Sample efficiency is demonstrated:** Search-R2 with 5 rollouts and 1 revision outperforms Search-R1 with 10 rollouts and 0 revisions, confirming that targeted refinement beats brute-force sampling (Appendix G).

4. **Training overhead is manageable:** Time increase is 2.43–8.66%, and cost-effectiveness improves with model scale (Table 5). However, external judge costs are not fully accounted for.

5. **Trajectory quality improves:** Search-R2 wins 84–94% of pairwise comparisons across six quality dimensions, confirming that accuracy gains reflect genuine reasoning improvements (Table 9).

**Remaining uncertainties:** The theoretical conditions for improvement are not empirically verified, failure cases are not systematically analyzed, and generalization beyond QA tasks is untested. These represent important directions for future work but do not undermine the core empirical contributions.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Search-R2 demonstrates compelling empirical improvements and provides theoretical grounding for its design, several important limitations and trade-offs warrant careful consideration. This section examines the assumptions underlying the approach, scenarios where it may struggle, computational constraints that affect deployment, and open questions that remain unresolved.

### 6.1 Assumptions Underlying the Approach

Search-R2's effectiveness relies on several critical assumptions that may not hold universally across all task domains:

**Assumption 1: Errors Have Localizable Root Causes.** The "cut-and-regenerate" mechanism assumes that reasoning failures can be traced to specific, identifiable steps (Section 3.2). The Trimmer `$\pi_h(k | \hat{y}, x)$` is designed to locate the earliest deviation point `$k + 1$` where the trajectory first went wrong. However, this assumption breaks down in scenarios where:
- Errors are **distributed** across multiple steps rather than concentrated at a single point
- The failure emerges from **cumulative drift** — small inaccuracies that compound gradually rather than a single catastrophic mistake
- The correct reasoning path requires **backtracking** to earlier assumptions rather than forward regeneration from a cut point

The paper does not characterize what fraction of failures exhibit clean, localizable root causes versus distributed error patterns. This is a fundamental assumption that enables the theoretical decomposition in Section 4 but remains empirically unverified.

**Assumption 2: Valid Prefixes Are Preservable.** The cut-and-regenerate strategy preserves the prefix `$\hat{y}_{1:k}$` and regenerates only the suffix. This assumes that early reasoning steps remain valid even when later steps fail. However, in **multi-hop reasoning tasks**, early search queries often determine the entire reasoning trajectory. If the first search retrieves information about the wrong entity (as in Figure 1's Search-R1 example), all subsequent reasoning — even if logically coherent — may be built on a false foundation. The paper acknowledges this in Figure 1 but does not quantify how often prefix preservation is actually beneficial versus how often the "root cause" is too early to preserve meaningful work.

**Assumption 3: External Judge Provides Reliable Supervision.** The process reward `$r_{\text{process}}(y)$` depends on an external LLM judge (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B) to evaluate retrieved chunk utility `$u_i$` (Appendix K). This introduces a **supervision dependency**: the quality of Search-R2's learning is bounded by the judge's ability to accurately assess information density and relevance. The paper does not evaluate:
- Judge accuracy against human annotations
- Judge consistency across different query types or domains
- Whether the judge itself exhibits biases that could be learned by the Actor

If the judge systematically mislabels certain retrieval patterns as "useful," the Actor may optimize for those patterns even if they don't genuinely improve reasoning.

**Assumption 4: Satisfiable Conditions for Theoretical Guarantees.** The theoretical analysis in Section 4 proves performance gains under conditions `$A_{\text{prec}} > 0$`, `$S_{\text{trim}} > 0$`, and calibrated `$V_{\text{inter}}$`. However, the paper does not demonstrate that these conditions are **always satisfiable** in practice. The proof shows *if* the conditions hold, *then* improvement is guaranteed — but it does not characterize when or why the conditions might fail. For example:
- `$A_{\text{prec}} > 0$` requires the Discriminator to distinguish good from bad trajectories better than random. On tasks where trajectory quality is hard to assess (e.g., creative writing, open-ended reasoning), this may not be achievable.
- `$S_{\text{trim}} > 0$` requires the Trimmer to identify cut-points that yield positive regeneration gain. If no cut-point improves the trajectory (e.g., the entire chain is fundamentally flawed), `$S_{\text{trim}}$` could be zero or negative.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive for QA tasks, leaves several important scenarios untested:

**Non-QA Task Domains.** All seven benchmarks are question answering datasets (Section 5.1). The paper claims Search-R2 applies to "knowledge-intensive tasks" broadly (Introduction), but there is **no evaluation** on:
- **Code generation**: Where search might retrieve API documentation or code snippets, and errors manifest as compilation failures rather than incorrect answers
- **Mathematical reasoning**: Where intermediate steps are verifiable but the search component is less prominent
- **Long-form writing**: Where there is no single ground truth answer and trajectory quality is subjective
- **Dialogue agents**: Where multi-turn interaction requires maintaining conversational coherence beyond single-query retrieval

The effectiveness of cut-and-regenerate may differ substantially for tasks without clear Exact Match rewards. For instance, in code generation, a single syntax error might require regenerating the entire function rather than a suffix.

**Extreme Multi-Hop Scenarios.** The multi-hop datasets in the evaluation (HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA, Musique, Bamboogle) typically require 2–4 reasoning steps. However, **deep research tasks** may require 10+ search iterations with complex dependency chains. The paper sets a maximum of 4 assistant turns per trajectory (Section 5.1, Appendix E), which may be insufficient for genuinely complex multi-hop reasoning. The diminishing returns observed in Table 4 (gains drop from +0.9 points at 1→2 revisions to +0.3 points at 3→4) suggest that the refinement mechanism may not scale well to longer horizons where error propagation is more severe.

**Adversarial or Noisy Retrieval Environments.** The evaluation uses a fixed retriever (E5) on a static knowledge source (2018 Wikipedia dump) (Section 5.1). Real-world search environments introduce additional challenges:
- **Adversarial search results**: Malicious or misleading content designed to deceive retrieval systems
- **Dynamic knowledge**: Information that changes over time, requiring the agent to detect and adapt to updates
- **Retriever failures**: Cases where the search engine returns no results or times out

The paper does not test Search-R2's robustness to these conditions. The Meta-Refiner might learn to "correct" trajectories based on patterns that only exist in the clean Wikipedia corpus, failing to generalize to noisier environments.

**Low-Resource or Domain-Specific Settings.** All experiments use models ranging from 7B to 32B parameters with access to large-scale retrieval infrastructure. The approach's effectiveness in **resource-constrained settings** is unknown:
- Smaller models (&lt;7B parameters) may lack the capacity to learn both generation and refinement capabilities simultaneously
- Domain-specific retrieval (e.g., medical, legal) with smaller corpora may not provide sufficient signal for the process reward to be meaningful
- Edge deployment scenarios where external judge inference is infeasible due to latency or cost constraints

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

Despite claims of "minimal overhead" (Abstract, Section 5.5), several computational constraints affect practical deployment:

**External Judge Inference Cost.** The process reward requires an external LLM judge to evaluate each retrieved chunk collection (Appendix K). This cost is **not included** in Table 5's training time measurements:

> "Inference is performed via the vLLM framework with greedy decoding parameters: temperature set to 0.0, top-p at 0.95, a repetition penalty of 1.0, and a maximum token limit of 3,000." (Appendix K)

For each training step with 512 prompts, 5 rollouts per prompt, and an average of 2–3 search actions per trajectory, the judge must evaluate approximately **5,000–7,500 chunk collections per step**. Even with a distilled 7B judge model, this represents substantial additional compute that is not reflected in the reported 2.43–8.66% training time increase (Table 5). For production training at scale, this hidden cost could be significant.

**Context Length Limitations.** The rollout context is capped at 15,000 tokens to accommodate interleaved reasoning and retrieved evidence (Appendix E). This constraint may limit applicability to:
- Tasks requiring **longer reasoning chains** that exceed the token budget
- Scenarios where retrieved documents are lengthy (e.g., full research papers rather than Wikipedia passages)
- Multi-turn interactions where conversation history accumulates across many turns

As models with larger context windows become standard (e.g., 128K+ tokens), Search-R2's fixed 15,000 token cap may become a bottleneck rather than a practical constraint.

**Memory Requirements for Joint Optimization.** The Actor and Meta-Refiner share weights but operate under different prompts (Section 3.2, Appendix L). During training, the system must maintain:
- Gradients for the shared policy `$\theta$`
- Separate computation graphs for generation, discrimination, and trimming actions
- Group-relative advantage estimates across `$G$` trajectories per prompt

While the paper uses Fully Sharded Data Parallel (FSDP) with tensor model parallelism (Appendix E), the memory footprint of jointly optimizing multiple "meta-actions" (accept/reject decisions, cut-point selections) alongside token generation is not explicitly quantified. For larger models (>32B), this could become a limiting factor.

**Inference-Time Decoupling Trade-off.** The paper states that "at inference time, Search-R2 introduces no additional latency because the Meta-Refiner is decoupled at deployment" (Section 5.5). This is technically accurate but potentially misleading:
- The **trained policy** has internalized refinement capabilities, meaning inference uses the same model as training
- However, the **iterative refinement loop** (Algorithm 1) is disabled at inference, so the model cannot apply cut-and-regenerate corrections during deployment
- This creates a **train-test mismatch**: the model learns to expect refinement during training but must produce correct outputs in a single pass at inference

The paper does not evaluate whether this mismatch degrades inference performance relative to training-time accuracy. Table 2 reports validation/test set performance, but it is unclear whether the Meta-Refiner is active during these evaluations or whether results reflect single-pass generation.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

Several weaknesses and unresolved questions emerge from the experimental analysis:

**Uncertainty Handling Remains Weak.** Table 9 shows that Search-R2's win rate on **Uncertainty Handling** (84.0%) is the lowest among all six trajectory quality dimensions, compared to 94.0% for Query Timing Quality and 88.9% for Evidence Groundedness (Section 5.6). This suggests that while Search-R2 improves search behavior and reasoning coherence, it does not substantially improve the model's ability to:
- Acknowledge when evidence is insufficient
- Hedge conclusions appropriately
- Express calibrated confidence levels

The paper does not discuss this limitation or propose directions for addressing it. For high-stakes applications (e.g., medical or legal QA), poor uncertainty handling could be a critical failure mode even with high Exact Match scores.

**Theoretical Conditions Lack Empirical Verification.** Section 3.5 and Section 4 derive formal conditions for performance improvement (`$A_{\text{prec}} > 0$`, `$S_{\text{trim}} > 0$`, calibrated `$V_{\text{inter}}$`), but the paper **never measures these covariance terms** during training. Without empirical verification:
- We cannot confirm whether the observed gains actually arise from the theorized mechanisms
- We cannot diagnose which component fails when performance plateaus
- We cannot predict whether the approach will generalize to new domains where the conditions may not hold

This represents a significant gap between the theoretical contributions (listed as Contribution 3 in the Introduction) and the empirical validation.

**Threshold `$\tau$` Tuning Is Underspecified.** The Discriminator accepts trajectories when `$\pi_d(\hat{y} | x) \geq \tau$` (Section 3.2), but the paper does not specify:
- How `$\tau$` is selected (fixed value, learned parameter, or tuned via validation?)
- Whether `$\tau$` varies across model scales or task types
- How sensitive performance is to `$\tau$` selection

The Intervention Volume `$V_{\text{inter}} = 1 - \mathbb{E}[\alpha(y)]$` is highly sensitive to `$\tau$`, and the theoretical analysis emphasizes that `$V_{\text{inter}}$` must be "calibrated" to balance exploration and exploitation (Section 3.5). Without guidance on tuning `$\tau$`, practitioners may struggle to reproduce optimal performance.

**Failure Mode Analysis Is Absent.** The paper reports aggregate performance metrics but does not systematically analyze **when and why Search-R2 fails**:
- Does the Meta-Refiner ever "over-correct" valid trajectories, introducing new errors?
- Are there task types where cut-and-regenerate is ineffective (e.g., tasks requiring global restructuring rather than local repair)?
- What is the failure mode when both Actor and Meta-Refiner make errors in the same trajectory?

Appendix F notes the risk of "model collapse" beyond 300 training steps due to "train–inference mismatch and automatic mixed-precision overflow" but does not characterize the failure patterns or propose mitigation strategies.

**Single-Revision Default May Not Generalize.** The default maximum revision is set to `$N_{\text{max}} = 1$` based on diminishing returns observed on Qwen2.5-32B (Table 4, Section 5.4). However:
- This analysis was conducted **only on the 32B model** — smaller models may benefit more from additional revisions if their initial generation quality is lower
- The analysis disabled process reward and joint optimization to isolate revision effects, which may not reflect the full Search-R2 configuration
- Different task types may have different optimal revision budgets (e.g., multi-hop QA may require more revisions than general QA)

The paper does not provide guidance on how to select `$N_{\text{max}}$` for new domains or model scales.

**Exact Match Reward Limitations.** The outcome reward uses Exact Match (EM) between predicted and ground truth answers (Section 3.3). While standard for QA benchmarks, EM has well-known limitations:
- It does not capture **partial correctness** (e.g., an answer that is semantically correct but phrased differently)
- It provides **no gradient signal** for nearly-correct answers, which can slow learning
- It may incentivize **overly specific answers** that match the ground truth string but lack generalizability

The hybrid reward design mitigates this somewhat via the process reward, but the outcome gating means that trajectories with correct reasoning but slightly mismatched answer formatting receive zero reward. The paper does not discuss whether alternative outcome rewards (e.g., F1 score, semantic similarity) were considered.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes the key trade-offs inherent in Search-R2's design:

| Trade-off | Benefit | Cost |
|-----------|---------|------|
| **Cut-and-regenerate vs. rejection sampling** | Preserves valid prefixes, improves sample efficiency | Assumes errors are localizable; may fail on distributed errors |
| **Joint optimization vs. separate training** | Enables co-adaptation between Actor and Meta-Refiner | Increases training complexity; requires careful hyperparameter tuning |
| **Hybrid reward vs. outcome-only** | Provides dense step-level supervision | Requires external judge inference (hidden compute cost) |
| **Single revision default vs. multiple iterations** | Captures most benefit at low computational cost (Table 4) | May under-correct on complex tasks requiring multiple repairs |
| **Shared weights vs. separate models** | Reduces parameter overhead; enables direct gradient flow | Limits specialization; Meta-Refiner capacity constrained by Actor architecture |
| **Decoupled Meta-Refiner at inference** | No additional latency during deployment (Section 5.5) | Train-test mismatch; model cannot apply refinement during inference |

These trade-offs are not necessarily weaknesses — they reflect design decisions that prioritize certain capabilities over others. However, practitioners should be aware of these constraints when considering Search-R2 for specific applications. The approach is best suited for **knowledge-intensive QA tasks** with clear ground truth answers, moderate reasoning depth (2–4 hops), and access to reliable retrieval infrastructure. Applications requiring robust uncertainty quantification, very long reasoning chains, or deployment in resource-constrained environments may require additional adaptation or alternative approaches.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

Search-R2 represents more than an incremental improvement in search-integrated reasoning — it introduces a **architectural paradigm shift** with theoretical grounding that reshapes how we think about training language agents. This section examines the broader implications for the field, identifies concrete research directions that this work enables, outlines practical deployment scenarios, and provides guidance on when to adopt this approach versus alternatives.

### 7.1 How This Work Changes the Landscape

#### 7.1.1 From Outcome Filtering to Learned Debugging

Prior search-integrated reasoning systems operate on an **accept-or-reject paradigm**: generate a trajectory, evaluate the outcome, and either keep or discard the entire output (Section 2.3.2). Search-R2 fundamentally restructures this by introducing a **learned debugging mechanism** that operates collaboratively with generation (Section 3.2, Section 4.1).

**Why this matters:** This shift transforms failed computation from wasted resources into **learning signals**. When rejection sampling discards a trajectory, all computation on valid prefixes is lost. Search-R2's cut-and-regenerate mechanism preserves correct work and uses failure points as teaching moments for the Trimmer. The empirical evidence is compelling: Search-R2 with 5 rollouts outperforms Search-R1 with 10 rollouts (Appendix G, Table 7), demonstrating that **refinement quality beats sampling quantity**.

**Field-wide implication:** This suggests that future agent training frameworks should internalize **error diagnosis and correction** as learnable capabilities rather than post-hoc filtering operations. The Actor–Refiner collaboration pattern could extend beyond search-integrated reasoning to any multi-step decision-making task where intermediate errors propagate (e.g., code generation, mathematical proof construction, planning).

#### 7.1.2 Theoretical Guarantees for Refinement-Based Approaches

Most RL-based reasoning papers report empirical improvements without formal guarantees. Search-R2 provides a **mathematical decomposition** of performance gain into three interpretable components (Section 3.5, Equation 3):

$$
\Delta J = A_{\text{prec}} + V_{\text{inter}} \times S_{\text{trim}}
$$

**Why this matters:** This decomposition transforms system design from **heuristic tuning** to **diagnostic engineering**. If performance plateaus, the decomposition indicates which component to target:
- Low `$A_{\text{prec}}$` → Improve Discriminator training signals
- Low `$S_{\text{trim}}$` → Enhance Trimmer's error localization capability
- Inappropriate `$V_{\text{inter}}$` → Calibrate acceptance threshold `$\tau$`

**Field-wide implication:** This establishes a **template for theoretical analysis** of refinement-based approaches. Future work on self-correction, reflection, or verification mechanisms could adopt similar decomposition frameworks to characterize when and why refinement yields gains. The covariance-based formulation (Section 4.1, Proposition 4.1) is particularly generalizable — it applies to any setting where a secondary policy modifies outputs from a base policy.

#### 7.1.3 Sample Efficiency Over Brute-Force Scaling

The prevailing trend in LLM development emphasizes **parameter scaling** and **increased sampling** to improve performance. Search-R2 demonstrates that **architectural efficiency** can compensate for reduced model scale (Section 5.2):

| Comparison | Search-R1 | Search-R2 | Implication |
|------------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| 7B vs 8B | 35.0% (8B) | 40.4% (7B) | Smaller model with refinement beats larger model without |
| Training trajectories | 5,120/step (n=10) | ~3,300/step (n=5) | 35% fewer trajectories, 6.28% higher accuracy |
| Cost-effectiveness ratio | Baseline | 1.78–4.69× better | Accuracy gains outpace compute costs (Table 5) |

**Field-wide implication:** As compute costs dominate production deployments, **sample-efficient training methods** become increasingly valuable. Search-R2 suggests that investing in refinement mechanisms may yield better ROI than simply increasing rollout budgets or model sizes — particularly for organizations with resource constraints.

#### 7.1.4 Hybrid Rewards as a Template for Multi-Scale Supervision

The outcome-gated process reward formulation (Section 3.3, Equation 1):

$$
R(y) = r_{\text{outcome}}(y) \cdot (1 + r_{\text{process}}(y))
$$

provides a **general template** for combining sparse outcome signals with dense process feedback while preventing reward hacking.

**Field-wide implication:** This design pattern applies beyond search-integrated reasoning to any setting where:
- Final outcomes are sparse (binary success/failure)
- Intermediate actions have measurable quality (code compilation, proof steps, dialogue turns)
- There is risk of optimizing intermediate signals at the expense of task completion

Future work on process reward models (PRMs) could adopt similar gating mechanisms to ensure alignment between process optimization and outcome achievement.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

Search-R2 opens several concrete research avenues. We categorize these by proximity to the current work:

#### 7.2.1 Immediate Extensions (Directly Building on Search-R2)

**Empirical Verification of Theoretical Conditions.** The paper derives conditions for performance improvement (`$A_{\text{prec}} > 0$`, `$S_{\text{trim}} > 0$`, calibrated `$V_{\text{inter}}$`) but never measures these covariance terms during training (Section 6.4).

*Research question:* Do these conditions actually hold in practice, and how do they correlate with observed performance gains?

*Suggested methodology:* Instrument training runs to compute `$A_{\text{prec}}$`, `$S_{\text{trim}}$`, and `$V_{\text{inter}}$` at regular intervals. Plot these against EM scores to verify the theoretical relationship. This would transform the theoretical contribution from a proof-of-concept into a **practical diagnostic tool**.

**Dynamic Revision Budgets.** The default `$N_{\text{max}} = 1$` is based on diminishing returns observed on Qwen2.5-32B (Table 4), but this analysis was conducted only on the 32B model with process reward and joint optimization disabled (Section 5.4).

*Research question:* Should revision budgets adapt to task complexity, model scale, or trajectory quality signals?

*Suggested methodology:* Train a lightweight classifier to predict whether a trajectory benefits from additional revisions based on early-step features (Discriminator score, search query quality, reasoning depth). This would enable **adaptive computation** — spending more refinement budget on hard cases while preserving efficiency on easy ones.

**Alternative Outcome Rewards.** The outcome reward uses Exact Match (EM), which does not capture partial correctness (Section 6.4).

*Research question:* Would F1 score, semantic similarity, or learned reward models provide better outcome signals for tasks without string-matching ground truth?

*Suggested methodology:* Replace EM with F1 score (standard for SQuAD-style QA) or use an LLM judge to score answer correctness on a 0–1 scale. Evaluate whether this improves learning stability and final performance, particularly on datasets with answer variability.

#### 7.2.2 Medium-Term Extensions (Generalizing the Approach)

**Extension to Non-QA Tasks.** All seven benchmarks are question answering datasets (Section 5.1). The approach's effectiveness on other task types is unknown.

*Research questions:*
- Does cut-and-regenerate work for **code generation**, where errors manifest as compilation failures?
- Can the Meta-Refiner improve **mathematical reasoning**, where intermediate steps are verifiable?
- How does the approach handle **long-form writing**, where there is no single ground truth?

*Suggested methodology:* Adapt the template format (Table 1) to task-specific structures (e.g., code blocks with execution feedback, proof steps with verifier signals). The hybrid reward design would need modification — process rewards could measure code efficiency or proof elegance rather than retrieval density.

**Improved Uncertainty Handling.** Table 9 shows Search-R2's weakest dimension is Uncertainty Handling (84.0% win rate vs. 94.0% for Query Timing Quality) (Section 5.6).

*Research question:* Can the Meta-Refiner be extended to detect and correct **overconfident reasoning** rather than just factual errors?

*Suggested methodology:* Add an uncertainty estimation component to the Discriminator that flags trajectories with high confidence but low evidence support. The process reward could penalize confident claims without retrieval backing. This would address a critical limitation for high-stakes applications (medical, legal QA).

**Human-in-the-Loop Refinement.** The current Meta-Refiner is fully automated, but human feedback could provide stronger supervision signals.

*Research question:* Can human annotations on cut-points or trajectory quality accelerate Meta-Refiner learning?

*Suggested methodology:* Collect human labels on a subset of trajectories indicating where errors occur and whether cut-and-regenerate improves them. Use these as additional supervision signals alongside the hybrid reward. This could be particularly valuable for domain-specific applications where automated judges are unreliable.

#### 7.2.3 Long-Term Extensions (Paradigm Shifts)

**Multi-Agent Refinement Chains.** The current Actor–Refiner architecture involves two components. Future work could explore **cascaded refinement** with multiple specialized Refiners.

*Research question:* Would separate Refiners for different error types (retrieval errors, logic errors, formatting errors) outperform a single Meta-Refiner?

*Suggested methodology:* Train specialized Trimmers for different failure modes identified through error analysis. Route rejected trajectories to the appropriate Refiner based on Discriminator diagnostics. This mirrors software engineering practices where different reviewers check different aspects of code.

**Self-Improving Judges.** The process reward depends on an external LLM judge (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B) that is fixed during training (Appendix K).

*Research question:* Can the judge model be jointly optimized with the Actor and Meta-Refiner to provide increasingly accurate process feedback?

*Suggested methodology:* Treat the judge as a learnable component with its own optimization objective (e.g., predicting human evaluations of retrieval quality). This would eliminate the hidden compute cost of external judge inference and ensure judge criteria align with task objectives.

**Cross-Task Transfer of Refinement Skills.** The Meta-Refiner learns to identify and correct errors on QA tasks. Whether these skills transfer to other domains is unknown.

*Research question:* Can a Meta-Refiner trained on QA improve reasoning on code generation or mathematical tasks without task-specific retraining?

*Suggested methodology:* Pre-train a Meta-Refiner on diverse QA datasets, then fine-tune only the Actor on a new task while keeping the Meta-Refiner frozen. Measure whether the pre-trained refinement capability provides a head start compared to training both components from scratch.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

Search-R2's design makes it particularly suitable for certain application domains while less appropriate for others. We provide concrete guidance below.

#### 7.3.1 High-Fit Applications

**Knowledge-Intensive Question Answering Systems.**
- *Examples:* Customer support chatbots, research assistants, fact-checking tools
- *Why Search-R2 fits:* Clear ground truth answers enable Exact Match rewards; multi-hop reasoning benefits from error correction; retrieval quality directly impacts answer accuracy
- *Expected benefit:* 10–15% accuracy improvement over standard RAG or Search-R1 baselines (based on Table 2 results)
- *Deployment consideration:* Meta-Refiner is decoupled at inference, so latency matches standard generation (Section 5.5)

**Domain-Specific Information Retrieval Agents.**
- *Examples:* Medical literature search, legal case retrieval, technical documentation lookup
- *Why Search-R2 fits:* Process reward incentivizes high-quality retrieval; cut-and-regenerate corrects domain-specific reasoning errors; hybrid reward prevents retrieval without task completion
- *Expected benefit:* Improved retrieval efficiency (fewer redundant queries) and better handling of domain terminology
- *Deployment consideration:* External judge may need domain adaptation — consider fine-tuning the judge on domain-specific retrieval quality annotations (Appendix K)

**Multi-Step Research Assistants.**
- *Examples:* Literature review automation, competitive intelligence gathering, market research
- *Why Search-R2 fits:* Tasks require 2–4 search iterations where early errors propagate; trajectory coherence is critical for synthesizing information across sources
- *Expected benefit:* Table 9 shows 87.7% win rate on Trajectory Coherence, indicating more logically consistent research outputs
- *Deployment consideration:* May benefit from `$N_{\text{max}} > 1$` revisions for complex research tasks (Table 4 shows diminishing returns but still positive gains up to 4 revisions)

#### 7.3.2 Low-Fit Applications

**Tasks Without Clear Ground Truth.**
- *Examples:* Creative writing, open-ended brainstorming, opinion generation
- *Why Search-R2 doesn't fit:* Exact Match reward is inapplicable; process reward criteria (retrieval density) may not align with task objectives
- *Alternative:* Use outcome-gated process rewards with learned reward models or human preference signals instead of EM

**Resource-Constrained Deployments.**
- *Examples:* Edge devices, mobile applications, low-budget APIs
- *Why Search-R2 doesn't fit:* External judge inference adds hidden training cost (Appendix K); joint optimization requires more memory than single-policy training (Section 6.3)
- *Alternative:* Use Search-R1 with rejection sampling or standard RAG with heuristic query optimization

**Tasks Requiring Uncertainty Quantification.**
- *Examples:* Medical diagnosis support, legal advice, financial recommendations
- *Why Search-R2 doesn't fit:* Uncertainty Handling is the weakest dimension (84.0% win rate, Table 9); outcome-gated rewards may incentivize overconfident answers
- *Alternative:* Combine Search-R2 with explicit uncertainty estimation modules or use methods designed for calibrated confidence (not addressed in this paper)

**Very Long-Horizon Reasoning.**
- *Examples:* Deep research requiring 10+ search iterations, complex planning tasks
- *Why Search-R2 doesn't fit:* Maximum 4 assistant turns per trajectory (Appendix E); diminishing returns beyond 1–2 revisions (Table 4)
- *Alternative:* Hierarchical approaches with multiple refinement stages or methods designed for long-horizon credit assignment (e.g., Zeng et al., 2025)

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For practitioners considering Search-R2 adoption, we provide concrete guidance on implementation priorities, hyperparameter tuning, and integration with existing infrastructure.

#### 7.4.1 Implementation Priorities

**Must-Have Components (Core to Search-R2):**
1. **Cut-and-regenerate mechanism** — This provides the largest single improvement (+3.9 points at 7B, Table 3). Implement the Trimmer to identify error points and regenerate suffixes while preserving prefixes.
2. **Discriminator for trajectory gating** — Enables selective refinement rather than blind regeneration. Use the same LLM weights as the Actor with distinct prompts (Table 12).
3. **Hybrid reward with outcome gating** — Prevents reward hacking while providing dense supervision. The formulation `$R(y) = r_{\text{outcome}} \cdot (1 + r_{\text{process}})$` is critical (Section 3.3).

**Nice-to-Have Components (Incremental Benefits):**
1. **Joint optimization of Actor and Meta-Refiner** — Provides +0.6 to +1.3 points over separate optimization (Table 3), but requires more complex training infrastructure.
2. **External judge for process reward** — Improves retrieval quality but adds inference overhead. Consider using a smaller judge model or caching judge evaluations to reduce cost.
3. **Multiple revision iterations** — Provides diminishing returns (+0.9 points from 1→2 revisions, +0.3 points from 3→4, Table 4). Start with `$N_{\text{max}} = 1$` and increase only if task complexity warrants it.

#### 7.4.2 Hyperparameter Tuning Guide

| Hyperparameter | Default Value | Tuning Range | Sensitivity | Guidance |
|----------------|---------------|--------------|-------------|----------|
| Acceptance threshold `$\tau$` | Not specified | 0.3–0.8 | **High** | Most critical parameter. Tune on validation set to achieve `$V_{\text{inter}} \approx 0.3$` (30% of trajectories refined). Too high → under-correction; too low → wasted computation. |
| Max revisions `$N_{\text{max}}$` | 1 | 1–4 | **Medium** | Start with 1. Increase to 2–3 for complex multi-hop tasks if validation shows improvement. Monitor diminishing returns (Table 4). |
| Rollouts per prompt `$n$` | 5 | 3–10 | **Medium** | Search-R2 with n=5 outperforms Search-R1 with n=10 (Appendix G). Increasing beyond 5 provides smaller gains than improving Meta-Refiner quality. |
| Process reward weight | 1.0 (multiplicative) | 0.5–2.0 | **Low** | The gating formulation makes this less sensitive. If reward hacking observed, reduce weight or strengthen outcome gating. |
| KL penalty coefficient | 0.001 | 0.0001–0.01 | **Low** | Standard PPO regularization. Increase if policy collapse observed during training (Appendix F notes collapse risk beyond 300 steps). |
| Learning rate | 1e-6 | 5e-7–5e-6 | **Medium** | Use warmup ratio 0.285 as specified (Appendix E). Larger models may tolerate higher learning rates. |

**Critical note on `$\tau$`:** The paper does not specify the default threshold value (Section 6.4). This is a significant reproduction gap. We recommend starting with `$\tau = 0.5$` and tuning based on the observed acceptance rate `$Z_{\text{acc}}$`. Target `$V_{\text{inter}} = 1 - Z_{\text{acc}} \approx 0.3$` based on the theoretical analysis that intervention volume must be "calibrated" (Section 3.5).

#### 7.4.3 Integration with Existing Infrastructure

**Compatible with:**
- **GRPO training frameworks** (e.g., verl, DeepSpeed-Chat) — Search-R2 uses GRPO natively (Section 3.4)
- **Standard retrievers** (E5, DPR, Contriever) — Retrieval infrastructure is decoupled from the refinement mechanism (Section 5.1)
- **Existing search templates** — The template format (Table 1) is inherited from Search-R1, enabling drop-in replacement

**Requires modification to:**
- **PPO with value critic** — GRPO eliminates the value function, so existing PPO implementations need adaptation
- **Outcome-only reward pipelines** — Must implement process reward computation with external judge (Appendix K)
- **Single-policy training loops** — Joint optimization requires treating refinement actions as part of the augmented trajectory (Section 3.4)

**Estimated integration effort:**
- **Greenfield implementation:** 2–3 weeks for experienced RL engineers (includes Meta-Refiner prompts, hybrid reward, GRPO adaptation)
- **Migration from Search-R1:** 1–2 weeks (mostly adding Meta-Refiner components and joint optimization)
- **Migration from standard RAG:** 4–6 weeks (requires adding RL training infrastructure in addition to Search-R2 components)

#### 7.4.4 When to Prefer Search-R2 Over Alternatives

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Multi-hop QA with clear ground truth | **Search-R2** | Largest gains on multi-hop tasks (+11.4 points on Bamboogle, Table 2); EM reward applicable |
| Single-hop QA with simple retrieval | **Search-R1 or RAG** | Search-R2 benefits are smaller on simple tasks; added complexity may not justify gains |
| Code generation with execution feedback | **Search-R2 (adapted)** | Cut-and-regenerate applicable; replace process reward with code quality metrics |
| Open-ended generation without ground truth | **Alternative (learned rewards)** | EM reward inapplicable; consider RLHF or process reward models without outcome gating |
| Resource-constrained deployment | **Search-R1** | Lower training complexity; no external judge overhead |
| High-stakes applications requiring uncertainty | **Alternative + uncertainty module** | Search-R2's uncertainty handling is weakest dimension (Table 9); add explicit calibration |
| Very long reasoning chains (10+ steps) | **Hierarchical refinement** | Search-R2 capped at 4 turns; consider multi-stage refinement architecture |

### 7.5 Summary: The Path Forward

Search-R2 establishes a **new baseline** for search-integrated reasoning by demonstrating that targeted intervention outperforms brute-force sampling, both theoretically and empirically. The key takeaways for the field are:

1. **Architectural innovation matters:** The Actor–Refiner collaboration pattern provides a template for future work on learned debugging mechanisms across diverse agent tasks.

2. **Theory should guide design:** The performance decomposition (Equation 3) transforms refinement from a heuristic into a diagnosable system with clear improvement levers.

3. **Sample efficiency is achievable:** Search-R2 proves that refining existing trajectories beats generating more candidates — a principle that extends beyond search-integrated reasoning to any multi-step decision task.

4. **Hybrid rewards prevent pathological behavior:** The outcome-gated process reward formulation provides a general solution to reward hacking in settings with both sparse outcomes and dense process signals.

**Open challenges remain:** Empirical verification of theoretical conditions, extension to non-QA tasks, improved uncertainty handling, and reduced judge inference costs are all important directions for future work. However, Search-R2 provides a **solid foundation** — both methodologically and theoretically — for addressing these challenges.

For practitioners, the recommendation is clear: **Adopt Search-R2 for knowledge-intensive QA tasks** where multi-hop reasoning and retrieval quality are critical. For other domains, use the Actor–Refiner pattern and hybrid reward design as inspiration while adapting the specifics to task requirements. The theoretical framework (Section 4) provides a lens for analyzing when and why refinement-based approaches will succeed — a contribution that extends far beyond the specific implementation in this paper.
