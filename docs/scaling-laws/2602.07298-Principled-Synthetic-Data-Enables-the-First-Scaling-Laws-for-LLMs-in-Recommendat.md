## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the critical absence of predictable scaling laws for Large Language Models (LLMs) in recommendation systems, identifying noisy and biased raw user interaction logs as the primary bottleneck. By introducing a layered synthetic data framework that generates pedagogical curricula, the authors demonstrate the first robust power-law scaling for Qwen3 models (0.6B to 8B parameters) trained on 163B tokens, with User Interaction History data exhibiting strong scaling exponents ($\alpha \approx 0.45–0.59$). This methodology fundamentally shifts development from mitigating data deficiencies to leveraging structured information, enabling standard sequential models like SASRec to outperform real-data baselines by +130% on Recall@100.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### 2.1 The Missing Foundation: Scaling Laws for Recommendation LLMs

To understand this paper's contribution, we must first establish what **scaling laws** are and why their absence matters. In deep learning, scaling laws describe predictable mathematical relationships between model performance and three key factors:

$$L(N, D) = A N^{-\alpha} + B D^{-\beta} + E$$

Where $L$ is the loss, $N$ is the number of parameters, $D$ is the number of training tokens, and $E$ is the irreducible loss floor. The exponents $\alpha$ and $\beta$ quantify how efficiently performance improves with scale. These laws, established in NLP by Kaplan et al. (2020) and refined by Hoffmann et al. (2022) in the Chinchilla paper, became **indispensable instruments** for guiding billion-dollar LLM development decisions. They allow practitioners to forecast compute budgets, data requirements, and expected performance before training begins.

**The critical gap:** Despite their established significance in NLP, **no robust scaling laws have been established for the continual pre-training (CPT) of LLMs within the recommendation domain** (Section 1). This forces researchers to depend on "costly trial-and-error methodologies" rather than scientific forecasting. The paper identifies this as a foundational barrier preventing systematic, predictable development of recommendation LLMs.

### 2.2 Why Raw User Data Fails as a Training Foundation

The paper's central insight is that prior scaling failures were **not primarily algorithmic but stemmed from a deficient data paradigm** (Section 1). To understand why, we must examine what makes raw user interaction logs fundamentally problematic for LLM training.

Unlike carefully curated NLP corpora, user interaction logs are **not a pure reflection of user preference** but rather a "distorted record heavily influenced by the very system that generated them" (Section 3.2). Table 1 in the paper catalogs five systemic pathologies:

| Bias Type | Mechanism | Consequence for Learning |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------------|
| **Position Bias** | Users interact more with top-ranked items regardless of relevance | Model learns "items in first position are preferable" rather than true preference |
| **Popularity Bias** | Popular items receive more exposure → more interactions → more popularity | Feedback loop suppresses long-tail content; top 1% of items receive 80% of interactions |
| **Exposure Bias** | Users can only interact with items they are shown | Training data is a censored view reflecting past algorithm decisions, not complete preference landscape |
| **Data Noise** | Accidental clicks, reflexive likes, contradictory ratings | Conflicting signals degrade representation quality |
| **Incompleteness & Sparsity** | Users interact across multiple platforms; profiles incomplete | Model lacks rich contextual information for nuanced reasoning |

**The amplification problem:** When an LLM is continually pre-trained on such biased logs, it does not merely learn the bias—its powerful pattern-matching capabilities **codify and amplify it** (Section 3.3). The model internalizes system flaws more effectively than simpler traditional models. If deployed, this newly biased LLM generates even more skewed recommendations, producing contaminated interaction data for subsequent training rounds. This creates a **vicious cycle of bias amplification** where system quality degrades over time.

### 2.3 Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper positions itself against three existing paradigms for scaling LLMs in recommendation (Section 2.1):

**Model-Centric Approaches (e.g., LUM - Large User Model):**
- Philosophy: Unlock scaling through sophisticated architecture and training objectives
- Example: Yan et al. (2025) introduced "next-condition-item prediction" to better capture contextual preferences
- **Limitation:** Still relies on raw user interaction logs as primary data source. The paper argues that "even the most advanced architectures... would struggle to exhibit predictable power-law scaling if trained on the pathologically flawed data"

**Distillation-Centric Approaches (e.g., SUAN):**
- Philosophy: Train a large "teacher" model, then distill knowledge to efficient "student" models
- Example: Lai et al. (2025) focused on CTR prediction with knowledge distillation
- **Limitation:** Does not address the root cause—data quality. Distillation transfers capabilities but cannot create information absent from the teacher's training data

**System-Centric Approaches (e.g., PLUM):**
- Philosophy: Adapt pre-trained LLMs through continual pre-training on domain-specific data
- Example: He et al. (2025) demonstrated CPT effectiveness for industrial-scale generative recommendations
- **Limitation:** Did not systematically study scaling laws or investigate data mixture ratios. Critically, PLUM encountered **sub-scaling** where a 3B parameter model "failed to consistently outperform its 900M counterpart"—a clear symptom of training on "information-poor, structurally biased data" (Section 1)

**Traditional Synthetic Data Methods:**
The paper also distinguishes itself from conventional synthetic data generation in recommender systems (Section 2.2):
- **GANs and VAEs** typically aim to "faithfully replicate the statistical properties of the flawed source data"
- Traditional applications focus on **data augmentation** (Qi et al. 2020) and **privacy preservation** (Adouani and Dagdia 2025)
- **Limitation:** The objective is mimicry, not pedagogy. Reproducing noisy, biased raw logs perpetuates the underlying problems

**Debiasing Techniques:**
Existing literature on mitigating bias includes inverse propensity scoring, causal inference, and adversarial training (Section 2.3). These approaches:
- Design more complex model architectures or loss functions
- Attempt to learn true preferences **despite** being trained on confounded data
- **Limitation:** Builds complexity into the model rather than solving the root data quality problem

### 2.4 This Paper's Positioning: A Data-Centric Paradigm Shift

The paper positions itself as **orthogonal and complementary** to existing work, proposing a "fundamentally data-centric solution" (Section 2.1). This represents a conceptual departure along multiple dimensions:

**From Robustness to Purification:**
Rather than designing models robust to data imperfections, the framework shifts focus to "constructing high-quality, 'pedagogical' data engineered to teach the model the principles of recommendation in a structured, unbiased manner" (Section 1). This aligns with the broader **"data-centric AI" movement** (Zha et al. 2023; Angelakis and Rass 2024), which shifts engineering focus from model to data itself.

**From Mimicry to Pedagogy:**
The synthetic data framework does not attempt to reproduce noisy, biased raw logs. Instead, it "deconstructs the recommendation problem into fundamental concepts—semantics, collaborative patterns, and unbiased sequential behavior—and generates a clean, structured curriculum" (Section 2.2). The objective is not statistical fidelity to flawed sources but **teaching efficiency**.

**From Augmentation to Foundation:**
Traditional synthetic data serves as augmentation to real data. This work treats synthetic data as the **primary training curriculum**, demonstrating that "standard sequential models trained exclusively on our synthetic data outperform models trained on real data" (Section 1 contributions). This provides direct empirical validation that the synthetic curriculum captures "more generalizable user preference patterns than raw interaction logs."

**Enabling Scientific Development:**
By establishing the first scaling laws for recommendation LLMs, the paper provides practitioners with a "quantitative roadmap for resource allocation and performance forecasting" (Section 1). This enables teams to "transition from speculative, heuristic-based approaches to a more scientific method" (Section 9), making large-scale system development "more predictable and justifiable."

### 2.5 The Sub-Scaling Phenomenon: Evidence of Data Quality Bottlenecks

The paper uses PLUM as a case study to illustrate the consequences of training on raw user data (Section 3.3). PLUM's MoE-3B model was "constrained by insufficient training data, ultimately preventing it from demonstrating clear superiority over the smaller MoE-900M model." This exemplifies the **"sub-optimal scaling law"** phenomenon identified in Chen et al. (2025b), where high data density and redundancy cause performance gains to decelerate.

Recent analysis shows that "models trained in high-density regimes deviate significantly from the anticipated power-law trajectory" (Section 3.3). Raw user interaction logs exhibit precisely these characteristics—popularity-driven feedback loops create redundancy, while exposure bias limits diversity. The marginal benefit of additional data diminishes rapidly, preventing the clean power-law relationships that enable predictable scaling.

**The critical insight:** When training data lacks sufficient quality and diversity, scaling laws simply do not manifest (Yang et al. 2024; Allen-Zhu and Li 2024a). This explains why prior recommendation LLM efforts failed to establish predictable scaling—the data itself was the inhibitor, not the model architecture or training procedure.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a **data-centric methodology paper** that introduces a layered synthetic data generation framework to enable predictable scaling laws for Large Language Models in recommendation systems. The core idea is to replace noisy, biased raw user interaction logs with a curated pedagogical curriculum that systematically teaches the model recommendation principles through structured, unbiased synthetic data.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a **two-layer synthetic data generation pipeline** that transforms raw e-commerce interaction logs into clean, structured training sequences that teach an LLM the fundamentals of user preference modeling without inheriting systemic biases. This solves the problem of unpredictable scaling behavior in recommendation LLMs by constructing high-fidelity training data that decouples true user preference signals from system-induced artifacts like position bias and popularity feedback loops.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The framework consists of four major components connected in a sequential pipeline. **First**, raw user interaction logs from the Merrec dataset (1.2B interactions, 65.7M items, 2.6M users) serve as the input source. **Second**, Layer 1 processes this data into two parallel streams: Item-Text Alignment Data (mapping semantic tokens to product descriptions) and Collaborative Filtering Data (mining association rules between items). **Third**, Layer 2 takes the purified collaborative signals from Layer 1 and constructs an item-item graph, then generates Synthetic User Interaction Histories via Node2Vec-based random walks. **Fourth**, all three synthetic data types (Item-Text, CF, UIH) are mixed with general domain text at specified ratios and used to continually pre-train Qwen3 models ranging from 0.6B to 8B parameters on 163B total tokens.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the **semantic tokenization foundation** because item representation is the vocabulary upon which all subsequent data generation depends.
- We then explain **Layer 1 (Item-Text Alignment and CF Data)** because these establish the foundational semantic and collaborative knowledge before sequential behavior is generated.
- We proceed to **Layer 2 (Synthetic UIH via graph walks)** because it builds upon Layer 1's purified signals to generate complete interaction sequences.
- We detail the **Node2Vec hyperparameter configuration** because the random walk parameters directly determine the quality and diversity of generated sequences.
- We conclude with the **training configuration and data mixture ratios** because these determine how the synthetic curriculum is actually consumed during model training.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### 3.4.1 Semantic Tokenization: The Foundation of Item Representation

Before any synthetic data can be generated, the system must establish a consistent vocabulary for representing items that the LLM can process. The paper evaluates two distinct strategies for generating discrete item tokens, ultimately selecting a **Sparse Autoencoder (SAE)** approach over Residual-Quantized methods after empirical ablation.

**The SAE approach (selected method):** This method utilizes a pre-trained Sparse Autoencoder originally trained on an internal multi-modal dataset comprising video, photo, and text data. The procedure involves passing each item's textual description through a pre-trained embedding model and then into the SAE, where the top-k activated features (concepts) are selected as the tokens. These tokens are then wrapped with special delimiter tokens `<RECTOKEN>` and `</RECTOKEN>` to mark semantic token boundaries within sequences. The paper selected this approach after ablation against RQ-kmeans because Figure 12 in Appendix B shows SAE consistently outperformed RQ-kmeans across all domains and training steps, with UIH Full achieving $L_\infty = 0.66$ for SAE versus $L_\infty = 0.76$ for RQ-kmeans on 4B models.

**The rejected RQ-kmeans alternative:** The paper also evaluated an In-Domain Residual-Quantized k-means approach trained from scratch specifically on the Merrec dataset. This method utilized Qwen2.5-7B to generate high-quality text embeddings for all 64.1M items, then trained a hierarchical RQ-VAE to quantize these embeddings into discrete codes using a residual codebook structure. Table 10 in Appendix B shows the configuration sweep: the selected configuration used 6 layers with 256 codes per layer, yielding $256^6$ (281 trillion) total possible codes with a collision rate of 3.5%. However, this approach was ultimately rejected because RQ-VAE was stuck at a 50.54% collision rate during training, indicating severe representational capacity issues.

**Why SAE over RQ-kmeans:** The design choice favors SAE despite the risk of semantic mismatch (SAE features were learned in a video-centric domain) because the empirical scaling law results demonstrate superior performance. The RQ-kmeans approach, while theoretically better aligned with the e-commerce domain, introduced training instability that manifested as higher asymptotic loss across all evaluation domains. This illustrates a key principle: theoretical alignment with the target domain does not guarantee better scaling behavior if the tokenization method introduces optimization difficulties.

#### 3.4.2 Layer 1: Grounding Semantic and Collaborative Knowledge

Layer 1 furnishes the LLM with the foundational "vocabulary" and "grammar" of the recommendation domain by teaching the model what items are and how they relate to one another through two parallel data generation streams.

**Item-Text Alignment Data (2.0B tokens, 9% of recommendation data):** The primary objective of this data type is to establish a robust semantic link between the abstract semantic tokens used to represent items and their real-world meaning as conveyed through textual descriptions. The generation process creates direct mappings between an item's tokenized representation and its descriptive text, formatted as natural language sequences. Each training example follows this structure:

```
This item <RECTOKEN> REC6594 REC5411 REC1547 REC941 REC7587 REC7639 
REC3383 REC6576 </RECTOKEN> is described as [redacted] lotus yoga seed bead 
bracelet, by Handmade in Women › Jewelry › Necklaces.
```

The textual descriptions are sourced from product catalogs, content creator metadata, or synthesized by other LLMs to ensure richness and consistency. This data provides the model with the fundamental building blocks of content understanding, enabling it to reason about content-based relationships rather than pure ID-based co-occurrence patterns. This step is critical for transcending the limitations of traditional collaborative filtering models and enabling superior generalization to new, long-tail, or cold-start items.

**Collaborative Filtering Data (7.6B tokens, 30% of recommendation data):** This data type explicitly teaches the model the statistical logic of collaborative filtering by translating user behavior patterns into a linguistic format that the LLM can process. Instead of requiring the model to implicitly infer these patterns from noisy sequences, this data makes the relationships explicit, providing a clear and direct learning signal. The process begins by mining association rules from the raw user interaction histories and existing item-to-item (I2I) data, then converting these statistical relationships into templated natural language statements. Each training example follows this structure:

```
When a user interacts with item <RECTOKEN> REC3078 REC3311 REC7479 REC2862 
REC5552 REC4015 REC6914 REC4637 </RECTOKEN>, there is a 4.9% probability 
they will also interact with item <RECTOKEN> REC3078 REC3311 REC7479 REC4015 
REC5211 REC2862 REC1723 REC6914 </RECTOKEN> (confidence: 0.049, lift: 652.45)
```

The confidence and lift metrics provide the model with explicit statistical signals about the strength of item associations. This process inherently averages over numerous user sessions, rendering it less susceptible to individual instances of position or presentation bias. Layer 1's CF Data component acts as an initial filter by extracting the core statistical signal of item co-occurrence from the noisy and biased raw logs, which Layer 2 then uses as the sole basis for generating entirely new behavioral data.

#### 3.4.3 Layer 2: Simulating Unbiased User Behavior via Graph Random Walks

Layer 2 builds upon the foundational knowledge established in Layer 1 by utilizing the learned collaborative relationships to generate complete interaction sequences that are realistic yet free from the contaminating artifacts present in raw data.

**Synthetic User Interaction Histories (2.8B tokens, 11% of recommendation data):** The objective of Synthetic UIH is to generate clean, diverse, and privacy-preserving user interaction sequences that directly address the core problems of missing positives and position bias found in real logs. Since the sequences are generated algorithmically from an aggregated graph model rather than from individual user histories, they are not tied to any single user's real activity, thus preserving privacy while eliminating exposure bias.

**The graph construction process:** First, a graph is constructed where items are represented as nodes, and the weighted edges between them are determined by the strength of the collaborative filtering relationships mined in Layer 1. This graph serves as the structural foundation for generating realistic user journeys. The edge weights encode the purified collaborative signal, having already averaged over numerous user sessions to reduce susceptibility to position or presentation bias.

**The Node2Vec random walk algorithm:** Realistic user journeys are simulated using 2nd-order biased random walks following the Node2Vec algorithm (Grover and Leskovec 2016). Unlike a 1st-order walk where the next step depends only on the current node, a 2nd-order walk's transition probability is conditioned on both the current node $v$ and the previous node $t$. The unnormalized transition probability $\pi_{v,x}$ to a next node $x$ is given by:

$$\pi_{v,x} = \alpha_{p,q}(t, x) \cdot w_{v,x}$$

Where $w_{v,x}$ is the static edge weight between nodes $v$ and $x$, and $\alpha_{p,q}(t, x)$ is the Node2Vec search bias function. The **return parameter $p$** controls the likelihood of returning to the previous node $t$, while the **in-out parameter $q$** controls exploration versus exploitation behavior (where $q > 1$ favors exploration and $q &lt; 1$ favors exploitation).

**Why 2nd-order over 1st-order walks:** The 2nd-order formulation allows the random walk to capture more sophisticated traversal patterns that better approximate real user behavior. A 1st-order walk would treat all neighbors of the current node equally based only on edge weights, while a 2nd-order walk can bias toward continuing in a particular direction or backtracking based on the previous step. This enables the generation of sequences that exhibit realistic sequential dependencies without inheriting the position bias present in raw interaction logs. The random walk generation process has no intrinsic concept of "rank," "position," or "presentation order," ensuring that the resulting Synthetic UIH data is free from these artifacts.

**The two-stage filtering mechanism:** This layered approach functions as a powerful data purification process that systematically decouples the true user preference signal from system-induced artifacts. Layer 1, particularly the CF Data component, acts as an initial filter by extracting the core statistical signal of item co-occurrence from the noisy and biased raw logs. Layer 2 then utilizes this purified signal as the sole basis for generating entirely new behavioral data. This two-stage filtering mechanism is the core mechanic that enables the creation of a clean, high-quality training curriculum, preserving valuable collaborative information while discarding contaminating biases.

#### 3.4.4 Node2Vec Hyperparameter Configuration and Selection

Appendix A documents a comprehensive hyperparameter sweep of the Node2Vec-based random walk algorithm to construct Synthetic UIH, evaluating 14 distinct configurations to identify the optimal parameter settings.

**Parameters swept:** The sweep varied two key parameters across all configurations. First, $\alpha_{\text{stop}}$ (alpha): the probability of terminating a walk at any given step, tested at values 0.10, 0.15, and 0.20. Second, `path_conf_threshold`: a minimum cumulative path confidence threshold used to filter out low-quality sequences, tested at values None, 1e-09, 2e-09, and 3e-09. The sweep also included a 1st-order (DeepWalk-style) baseline configuration for comparison against the 2nd-order BFS-like strategies (all with $p = 0.5$, $q = 2.0$).

**Selection criteria:** Table 9 in Appendix A shows the metrics used to evaluate each configuration: number of unique items covered (Items), Gini coefficient for item distribution (lower is better for diversity), total tokens generated, token Gini coefficient, and geometric mean lift (higher indicates stronger collaborative signals). The selected configuration was `1st_order_a015_thresh1e-09` with $\alpha = 0.15$, threshold = 1e-09, achieving 180K unique items, Gini = 0.635, 1,581 tokens per sequence, token Gini = 0.844, and geometric lift = 385.54.

**Why this configuration was selected:** Despite being a 1st-order walk (which might seem inferior to 2nd-order), this configuration achieved the best balance of item coverage (180K items), sequence diversity (Gini = 0.635, the lowest among all configurations), and collaborative signal strength (geometric lift = 385.54). The threshold of 1e-09 effectively filtered low-quality sequences without being so aggressive as to eliminate too many valid paths. This configuration was highlighted in gray in Table 9 as the selected choice, demonstrating that empirical validation trumps theoretical assumptions about walk order.

#### 3.4.5 Training Configuration and Data Mixture Ratios

The continual pre-training experiments were conducted with specific hyperparameters and data mixture ratios designed to enable clean scaling law analysis while preventing catastrophic forgetting of general language capabilities.

**Model architecture and scale:** The base models used were Qwen3 models scaling from 0.6B to 8B parameters (specifically 0.6B, 1.7B, 4B, and 8B), selected as "one of the best open-weight LLM models available now" according to Section 6.1. All models were trained on B200 GPU clusters.

**Training hyperparameters:** Continual pre-training was performed with a global batch size of 512 sequences and context windows of 512 tokens. The learning rate peaked at 1e-4 with a linear warmup over 100 steps followed by a cosine decay schedule. All models were trained with exactly 163B tokens, which represents over 20× the Chinchilla scaling multiplier for 8B models (Hoffmann et al. 2022 recommends approximately 20 tokens per parameter, so 8B × 20 = 160B tokens).

**Data mixture composition:** Table 2 in Section 6.1 describes the statistics of the training dataset, which consists of both general domain data and recommendation-specific synthetic data mixed at a 50%/50% ratio to avoid catastrophic forgetting (Ibrahim et al. 2024). The breakdown is as follows:

| Domain | Tokens | Ratio | Repeats |
|--------|--------|-------|---------|
| General (cosmopedia-v2, fineweb-edu-dedup, python-edu) | 128B | 50% | &lt; 1 |
| Item-Text | 2.0B | 9% | 6.2 |
| CF | 7.6B | 30% | 7.3 |
| UIH | 2.8B | 11% | 6.1 |

The general domain data comes from three subsets of SmolLM-Corpus (Ben Allal et al. 2024), while the recommendation data source was the Merrec dataset (Li et al. 2025), a publicly available C2C e-commerce dataset from HuggingFace. The recommendation data is repeated 6-7× during training due to the limited unique tokens available, which has important implications for overfitting behavior discussed in Section 7.2.

**Ablation study configurations:** Section 7.1 and Table 6 document ablation studies testing different combinations of the three recommendation data types. The four configurations tested were: (1) Item-text + CF + UIH at 9%/30%/11%, (2) CF + UIH at 0%/37%/13%, (3) Item-text + UIH at 22.5%/0%/27.5%, and (4) UIH only at 0%/0%/50%. All experiments maintained 50% general text and trained for 163B total tokens, with the recommendation domain ratios normalized accordingly to maintain the sum of 50%.

**UIH mixture ratio experiments:** Section 7.2 documents additional experiments testing the impact of UIH mixture ratio on scaling behavior using a reduced UIH dataset of 42M tokens (sampled from 2.7B tokens or 39K samples from 5.748M samples). The UIH mixture ratio was tested at 0.5%, 1%, 2%, 5%, and 15%, with the mixture ratio of other recommendation domains normalized accordingly. Table 7 shows that as the UIH mixture ratio increases, the number of repeats on the reduced UIH data increases proportionally (from 4.1 repeats at 0.5% to 122.3 repeats at 15%). This experiment revealed that perplexity on hold-out evaluation sets starts to increase when the UIH mixture ratio exceeds 2% (approximately 16 repeats), indicating overfitting occurs when the model sees the same synthetic sequences too many times.

#### 3.4.6 Evaluation Protocol and Test Set Construction

To properly measure scaling behavior across different domains, the paper constructed held-out test sets for each data modality with careful attention to ensuring no data leakage from training sets.

**General domain evaluation:** 200K entries (102.4M tokens) were randomly sampled from the general domain corpus for evaluation purposes, ensuring these entries were not included in the training data.

**Item-Text evaluation:** Item-text pairs for items not in the training set were collected, then 200K entries (102.4M tokens) were sampled for evaluation. This tests the model's ability to generalize semantic understanding to unseen items.

**Collaborative Filtering evaluation:** Three distinct test sets were constructed to measure different generalization capabilities. "CF both seen" tests edges between two items with both items presented in training data but edges not included in training data (200K entries, 102.4M tokens). "CF one unseen" tests edges between two items with one item presented in training data and the other item not (200K entries, 102.4M tokens). "CF both unseen" tests edges between two items with neither items nor edges presented in training data (75K entries, 38M tokens). This hierarchy allows measurement of how well the model learns generalizable collaborative patterns versus memorizing specific item pairs.

**User Interaction History evaluation:** Two test sets were constructed. "UIH OOD" contains UIH sampled only based on edges from the CF test set (48.3K entries, 24.7M tokens), testing out-of-distribution generalization. "UIH full graph" contains UIH sampled from both CF train and CF test set edges (51.7K entries, 26.5M tokens), testing overall sequential modeling capability.

**Why this evaluation structure matters:** The multi-tiered evaluation protocol enables the paper to distinguish between memorization and genuine learning. If a model only memorizes training sequences, it will perform well on "both seen" tests but poorly on "both unseen" tests. The scaling law analysis in Section 6.2 shows that models trained on the synthetic curriculum exhibit consistent power-law improvement across all test tiers, indicating genuine learning of generalizable patterns rather than memorization.

#### 3.4.7 Scaling Law Mathematical Formulation

The paper fits two types of scaling laws to characterize the relationship between model performance, data volume, and model size.

**Per-model data scaling:** For each fixed model size, the paper fits scaling laws of the form:

$$\ell(D) = L_\infty + A \cdot D^{-\alpha}$$

Where $\ell(D)$ is the loss as a function of training tokens $D$, $L_\infty$ is the asymptotic loss (the theoretical minimum loss achievable with infinite data), $A$ is a constant coefficient, and $\alpha$ is the scaling exponent measuring the rate of perplexity reduction per unit of training data. Higher $\alpha$ values indicate more efficient learning from additional data. Table 5 in Section 6.2.1 reports these parameters for each model size and domain combination.

**Joint scaling over model size and data:** To disentangle the contributions of model size ($N$) and training data ($D$), the paper fits joint scaling laws of the form:

$$\ell = E + A \cdot N^{-\alpha} + B \cdot D^{-\beta}$$

Where $E$ is the irreducible loss (the entropy floor of the domain), $A$ and $B$ are coefficient constants, $\alpha$ governs model-size scaling, and $\beta$ governs data scaling. Equations 2-5 in Section 6.2.2 provide the fitted parameters for each domain. For example, the UIH domain scaling law is:

$$\ell_{\text{UIH}} = 0.514 + 1.89 \cdot N^{-0.138} + 63.9 \cdot D^{-0.272}$$

This reveals that UIH is strongly data-dominant (high $\beta = 0.272$) but weakly model-size dependent (low $\alpha = 0.138$), meaning performance improves primarily by increasing data coverage rather than model capacity.

**Compute-optimal allocation:** Given a fixed compute budget $C = 6ND$ (following the standard transformer FLOP estimate), minimizing loss subject to this constraint yields the optimality condition:

$$\alpha A \cdot N^{-\alpha} = \beta B \cdot D^{-\beta}$$

This requires that the marginal loss reduction per FLOP be equal for model size and data. Table 4 in Section 6.2.2 reports the coefficients $\alpha A$ and $\beta B$ for each domain, revealing sharply different optimal strategies: General and Item-Text domains should allocate compute overwhelmingly toward model size, while UIH should prioritize data collection.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the fundamental discoveries enabled by the layered synthetic data framework. While Section 3 detailed *how* the data was constructed, these insights explain *why* those construction choices matter and what they reveal about the nature of learning recommendation tasks with LLMs. These findings distinguish between incremental improvements (better hyperparameters) and fundamental innovations (new understanding of scaling dynamics).

### 4.1 Pedagogical Data Outperforms Raw Data (The "TSTR > TRTR" Phenomenon)

The most counter-intuitive finding of this work is that models trained **exclusively** on synthetic data outperform models trained on real user logs when tested on real user interactions. This challenges the conventional wisdom that synthetic data is merely a supplement to real data.

*   **The Evidence:** In the "Train on Synthetic, Test on Real" (TSTR) evaluation, standard sequential models (GRU4Rec, SASRec, etc.) achieved significantly higher Recall@K than their "Train on Real, Test on Real" (TRTR) counterparts across all cutoff points (Section 5.2, Figure 1). Specifically, SASRec trained on synthetic data showed a **+130% improvement on Recall@100** compared to the real-data baseline (Section 1).
*   **Why This Matters:** This result provides direct empirical proof that raw user interaction logs are not just noisy, but **structurally damaging** for learning generalizable preference patterns. Real logs contain systemic biases (position, popularity, exposure) that act as "shortcuts" for the model—learning these biases minimizes training loss but harms generalization. By removing these artifacts, the synthetic curriculum forces the model to learn the underlying **collaborative logic** rather than the system's presentation artifacts.
*   **Innovation Level:** **Fundamental.** Prior synthetic data work focused on *augmentation* (adding more data) or *privacy* (hiding user identities). This work demonstrates synthetic data can be **superior** to real data for core learning objectives, shifting the goal from "mimicking reality" to "teaching principles."

### 4.2 Asymmetric Cross-Domain Transfer (CF → UIH)

The ablation studies reveal a specific, directional dependency between the data layers that validates the pedagogical design of the curriculum. The layers are not interchangeable; they serve distinct cognitive functions for the model.

*   **The Evidence:** When Collaborative Filtering (CF) data is included alongside User Interaction History (UIH) data, the asymptotic loss ($L_\infty$) on UIH evaluation sets drops from **0.95 to 0.66** (a 31% improvement) (Section 7.1, Figure 4). However, the reverse transfer does not hold: training on UIH data alone yields virtually no improvement on CF tasks ($\alpha \approx 0$) (Section 7.1).
*   **Why This Matters:** This asymmetry indicates that **global affinity patterns** (captured by CF data) provide a necessary scaffold for learning **sequential behavior** (captured by UIH data). The model needs to understand *which items are related* (CF) before it can effectively learn *how users traverse those items over time* (UIH). Without the CF layer, the sequential data is too sparse and noisy for the model to infer underlying item relationships.
*   **Innovation Level:** **Fundamental.** This discovery provides a theoretical justification for multi-task learning in recommendation. It suggests that sequence modeling alone is insufficient; it must be grounded in static collaborative signals to achieve optimal scaling. This explains why single-objective training often stalls in recommendation domains.

### 4.3 Divergent Scaling Bottlenecks: Data vs. Model Dominance

The joint scaling law analysis reveals that recommendation tasks obey different physical laws than general language tasks. This finding fundamentally alters how practitioners should allocate compute budgets for recommendation LLMs.

*   **The Evidence:** The joint scaling fit $\ell = E + A \cdot N^{-\alpha} + B \cdot D^{-\beta}$ shows opposite regimes for different domains (Section 6.2.2, Table 4):
    *   **General Domain:** Model-size dominant ($\alpha A = 8,432$ vs. $\beta B = 0.185$). Performance improves primarily by increasing parameters ($N$).
    *   **UIH Domain:** Data dominant ($\alpha A = 0.261$ vs. $\beta B = 17.38$). Performance improves primarily by increasing data coverage ($D$).
*   **Why This Matters:** For general language tasks, the standard strategy is to build larger models (e.g., moving from 8B to 70B parameters). However, for recommendation tasks like UIH, **scaling model size yields diminishing returns** compared to scaling data diversity. The irreducible loss ($E$) for UIH is 0.514, compared to 0.79 for General domain, suggesting user behavior is inherently more predictable than natural language—but only if the model sees enough diverse trajectories (Section 6.2.2).
*   **Innovation Level:** **Fundamental.** This contradicts the assumption that scaling laws are uniform across all tasks. It provides a quantitative roadmap: for recommendation CPT, resources should be prioritized toward **data curation and diversity** rather than purely architectural expansion. This explains the "sub-scaling" failure of prior works like PLUM, which likely hit a data ceiling rather than a model capacity ceiling (Section 3.3).

### 4.4 The Synthetic Reuse Boundary (The "16-Repeat Cliff")

While synthetic data enables scaling, this work identifies a hard limit on how many times synthetic sequences can be reused before performance degrades. This provides a critical safety boundary for data-constrained training.

*   **The Evidence:** In the UIH mixture ratio experiments, perplexity on hold-out evaluation sets begins to increase when the model sees the reduced UIH data more than **~16 times** (Section 7.2, Table 7). For example, at a 15% mixture ratio, degradation starts at 20B tokens (16 repeats); at a 2% ratio, it starts at 160B tokens (also 16 repeats) (Section 7.2, Figure 7).
*   **Why This Matters:** This threshold appears **independent of model size** (Figure 7), suggesting it is a property of the data distribution rather than model capacity. Exceeding this limit causes the model to memorize the synthetic sequences rather than learn generalizable patterns, leading to overfitting despite monotonically decreasing training loss (Section 7.2, Figure 6b). This aligns with findings by Yang et al. (2024) but specifies the boundary for recommendation data specifically.
*   **Innovation Level:** **Incremental but Critical.** While prior work noted diminishing returns with repetition, this paper quantifies a specific **failure mode threshold** for recommendation synthetic data. It warns practitioners that simply looping over high-quality synthetic data indefinitely is not a viable scaling strategy; diversity must be maintained beyond the 16-repeat boundary.

### 4.5 Establishment of Predictable Power Laws in Recommendation CPT

Perhaps the most significant overarching contribution is the mere existence of robust scaling laws in this domain. Prior to this work, the relationship between compute and performance in recommendation LLMs was unpredictable.

*   **The Evidence:** Across model scales from 0.6B to 8B parameters trained on 163B tokens, the empirical data points follow remarkably consistent power-law trends across seven evaluation domains (Section 6.2, Figure 2). The scaling exponents for UIH are strong ($\alpha \approx 0.45–0.59$), indicating continued benefit from additional tokens (Section 1, Table 5).
*   **Why This Matters:** Predictable scaling laws transform LLM development from a **heuristic process** into an **engineering discipline**. Practitioners can now forecast the compute budget required to achieve a target perplexity or recall metric. This removes the "trial-and-error" barrier that previously impeded industrial adoption of LLMs for recommendation (Section 1).
*   **Innovation Level:** **Fundamental.** This is the **first** demonstration of such laws in recommendation continual pre-training. It validates the hypothesis that data quality was the primary inhibitor of prior scaling efforts (Section 3.3). By fixing the data, the underlying physical laws of neural scaling become visible.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validated their claims through empirical evaluation. We will walk through the experimental design, unpack the quantitative results with specific numbers, and critically assess whether the evidence supports the paper's central thesis that high-quality synthetic data enables predictable scaling laws for recommendation LLMs.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: A Multi-Dimensional Testing Framework

The paper employs a comprehensive evaluation strategy designed to test three distinct claims: (1) synthetic data utility for downstream tasks, (2) existence of scaling laws, and (3) interactions between data layers. This multi-pronged approach is necessary because establishing scaling laws requires different evidence than demonstrating task performance.

**Datasets and Data Sources:**

The experiments rely on two primary data sources (Section 6.1):

- **Recommendation Domain:** The Merrec dataset (Li et al. 2025), a publicly available C2C e-commerce dataset containing 1.2B interactions from 65.7M items and 2.6M users. This serves as the source for generating all synthetic data types.
- **General Domain:** Three subsets from SmolLM-Corpus (Ben Allal et al. 2024): `cosmopedia-v2`, `fineweb-edu-dedup`, and `python-edu`. These prevent catastrophic forgetting of general language capabilities during continual pre-training.

**Training Configuration:**

All models were trained under consistent conditions to enable clean scaling law analysis (Section 6.1):

- **Model Family:** Qwen3 models at four scales: 0.6B, 1.7B, 4B, and 8B parameters
- **Total Training Tokens:** 163B tokens for all models (over 20× the Chinchilla scaling multiplier for 8B models)
- **Hardware:** B200 GPU clusters
- **Batch Size:** 512 sequences globally
- **Context Window:** 512 tokens
- **Learning Rate:** Peak of 1e-4 with linear warmup (100 steps) and cosine decay schedule
- **Data Mixture:** 50% general domain, 50% recommendation domain (Item-Text 9%, CF 30%, UIH 11%)

**Evaluation Metrics:**

The paper uses two distinct metric families for different evaluation purposes:

1. **Perplexity:** Primary metric for scaling law analysis, measured on held-out test sets across seven domains
2. **Recall@K:** Used for downstream ranking task evaluation (K = 10, 100, 1000)

**Test Set Construction (Section 6.1, Table 3):**

The evaluation protocol carefully separates training and test data to measure genuine generalization rather than memorization:

| Test Domain | Description | Tokens |
|-------------|-------------|--------|
| General | Random sample from general corpus | 102.4M |
| Item-Text | Item-text pairs for items **not** in training set | 102.4M |
| CF Both Seen | Edges between two items both in training, but edge not in training | 102.4M |
| CF One Unseen | Edge with one item in training, one item not | 102.4M |
| CF Both Unseen | Edge with neither item in training | 38M |
| UIH OOD | UIH sampled only from CF test set edges | 24.7M |
| UIH Full Graph | UIH sampled from both CF train and test edges | 26.5M |

This hierarchical test construction is critical: if models only memorize training sequences, they will perform well on "Both Seen" tests but poorly on "Both Unseen" tests. The scaling law analysis shows consistent improvement across all tiers, indicating genuine pattern learning.

**Baseline Comparisons:**

For downstream ranking evaluation (Section 5.2), the paper compares two training paradigms:

- **TSTR (Train on Synthetic, Test on Real):** Models trained exclusively on synthetic data, evaluated on real user interactions
- **TRTR (Train on Real, Test on Real):** Models trained on real user logs, evaluated on real user interactions

To ensure fair comparison, the test set was filtered to include only items present in the vocabulary of both synthetic and real training sets.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results: Evidence for Scaling Laws

The paper's central claim—that high-quality synthetic data enables predictable scaling laws—is supported by multiple converging lines of evidence. We will examine each in detail.

#### 5.2.1 Synthetic Data Superiority for Downstream Ranking

The most striking result appears in Section 5.2 (Figure 1), where standard sequential recommendation models trained **exclusively** on synthetic data outperform models trained on real data when both are tested on real user interactions.

**Specific Results:**

- **SASRec:** +130% improvement on Recall@100 when trained on synthetic vs. real data (Section 1)
- **All Models:** GRU4Rec, NARM, STAMP, and SASRec all show consistent TSTR > TRTR performance across all cutoff points (@10, @100, @1000)
- **Visual Evidence:** Figure 1 shows blue lines (TSTR) consistently above red lines (TRTR) for all four models

**Why This Result Matters:**

This finding directly challenges the assumption that real user data is inherently superior for training recommendation systems. The paper argues that raw interaction logs contain "structurally damaging" biases that act as learning shortcuts—models minimize training loss by learning position and popularity biases rather than true user preferences. By removing these artifacts, the synthetic curriculum forces models to learn underlying collaborative logic.

**Critical Assessment:**

This result is compelling but comes with an important caveat: the test set was filtered to items present in both vocabularies. This ensures fair comparison but may limit generalizability to truly cold-start scenarios. The paper acknowledges this constraint in Section 5.2.

#### 5.2.2 Power-Law Scaling Across Model Scales

Figure 2 (Section 6.2) presents the core scaling law evidence, showing perplexity as a function of training tokens for seven data modalities across four model sizes. Both axes use logarithmic scales, and the empirical data points follow remarkably consistent power-law trends.

**Scaling Exponents by Domain (Table 5, Section 6.2.1):**

| Model Size | General α | Item-Text α | CF α | UIH α |
|------------|-----------|-------------|------|-------|
| 0.6B | 0.027 | 0.169 | 0.282 | 0.453 |
| 1.7B | 0.023 | 0.141 | 0.315 | 0.514 |
| 4B | 0.026 | 0.126 | 0.357 | 0.564 |
| 8B | 0.020 | 0.214 | 0.358 | 0.588 |

**Key Observations:**

1. **UIH exhibits the strongest scaling:** α ranges from 0.453 to 0.588, indicating the model continues to benefit substantially from additional training tokens. This is the highest scaling exponent reported in the paper.

2. **General domain shows near-saturation:** α ≈ 0.02–0.03 across all model sizes. This is expected because the pretrained Qwen3 checkpoint already encodes substantial general-domain knowledge, leaving little room for improvement through continued pretraining on similar data.

3. **Larger models learn recommendation tasks more efficiently:** For CF and UIH domains, α increases with model size (e.g., UIH α goes from 0.453 at 0.6B to 0.588 at 8B). This indicates that increased model capacity improves the ability to absorb new recommendation knowledge from data.

4. **Asymptotic loss (L∞) remains stable across model sizes for recommendation domains:** For UIH, L∞ ranges from 0.64 to 0.66 across all model sizes. This suggests that even 0.6B parameters are sufficient to represent the underlying patterns at convergence—the bottleneck is data coverage, not model capacity.

**Joint Scaling Law Parameters (Section 6.2.2, Equations 2-5):**

The paper fits joint scaling laws of the form $\ell = E + A \cdot N^{-\alpha} + B \cdot D^{-\beta}$ to disentangle model size and data contributions:

$$\ell_{\text{UIH}} = 0.514 + 1.89 \cdot N^{-0.138} + 63.9 \cdot D^{-0.272}$$

$$\ell_{\text{CF}} = 0.193 + 21 \cdot N^{-0.277} + 6.79 \cdot D^{-0.148}$$

$$\ell_{\text{Item-Text}} = 0.473 + 5720 \cdot N^{-0.511} + 4.04 \cdot D^{-0.070}$$

$$\ell_{\text{General}} = 0.79 + 16500 \cdot N^{-0.511} + 3.85 \cdot D^{-0.048}$$

**Interpretation:**

The coefficients reveal opposite scaling regimes:
- **General/Item-Text:** High α (0.511), low β (0.048–0.070) → **Model-size dominant**
- **UIH:** Low α (0.138), high β (0.272) → **Data dominant**
- **CF:** Intermediate on both axes (α = 0.277, β = 0.148) → **Balanced regime**

This finding fundamentally alters compute allocation strategy: for recommendation tasks like UIH, scaling data diversity yields better returns than scaling model parameters.

#### 5.2.3 Compute-Optimal Allocation Analysis

Table 4 (Section 6.2.2) presents the compute-optimal trade-off coefficients derived from the joint scaling laws. The optimality condition requires that marginal loss reduction per FLOP be equal for model size and data:

$$\alpha A \cdot N^{-\alpha} = \beta B \cdot D^{-\beta}$$

| Domain | αA | βB | Implication |
|--------|------|------|-------------|
| General | 8,432 | 0.185 | Overwhelmingly model-size dominant |
| Item-Text | 2,923 | 0.283 | Strongly model-size dominant |
| CF | 5.82 | 1.005 | Moderately model-size dominant |
| UIH | 0.261 | 17.38 | **Strongly data dominant** |

**Concrete Example (Section 6.2.2):**

> "Halving the data-dependent loss term for General requires 21/0.048 ≈ 1.8×10⁶ times more data, whereas for UIH it requires only 21/0.272 ≈ 13 times more data."

This quantitative comparison illustrates why prior recommendation LLM efforts failed to exhibit clean scaling: they likely allocated compute sub-optimally, scaling model size when they should have prioritized data diversity.

#### 5.2.4 Irreducible Loss Reflects Domain Predictability

The irreducible loss parameter $E$ from the joint scaling fit captures the entropy floor of each domain (Section 6.2.2):

- **CF (E = 0.193):** Most predictable, consistent with the low-rank structure of user-item co-occurrence matrices
- **Item-Text (E = 0.473):** Moderately predictable, reflecting constrained vocabulary and repetitive structure
- **UIH (E = 0.514):** User behavior exhibits moderate stochasticity—users are somewhat predictable but not deterministic
- **General (E = 0.79):** Natural language has the highest inherent entropy

This hierarchy suggests that recommendation tasks are fundamentally more learnable than general language modeling—but only if trained on appropriate data.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies: Validating the Layered Curriculum Design

The paper conducts two critical ablation studies to validate the pedagogical design of the layered synthetic data framework.

#### 5.3.1 Data Layer Ablation (Section 7.1, Table 6, Figures 3-4)

The authors test four data mixture configurations to quantify the contribution of each layer:

| Configuration | Item-Text | CF | UIH |
|---------------|-----------|-----|-----|
| Full Mixture | 9% | 30% | 11% |
| CF + UIH | 0% | 37% | 13% |
| Item-Text + UIH | 22.5% | 0% | 27.5% |
| UIH Only | 0% | 0% | 50% |

**Key Finding: Asymmetric Cross-Domain Transfer**

Figure 4 (Section 7.1) reveals a critical asymmetry in how data layers interact:

- **CF → UIH Transfer:** Including CF data alongside UIH reduces asymptotic UIH loss from $L_\infty = 0.95$ (UIH only) to $L_\infty = 0.66$ (CF + UIH), a **31% improvement**
- **UIH → CF Transfer:** The reverse does not hold. Training on UIH data alone yields virtually no improvement on CF tasks ($\alpha \approx 0$)

**Interpretation:**

This asymmetry validates the pedagogical design: global affinity patterns (CF) provide a necessary scaffold for learning sequential behavior (UIH). The model needs to understand *which items are related* before it can effectively learn *how users traverse those items over time*. Without the CF layer, sequential data is too sparse for the model to infer underlying item relationships.

**Domain-Specific Data Remains Essential:**

Despite positive transfer from CF to UIH, the ablation shows that domain-specific training data remains critical:

- Models trained without CF data plateau at substantially higher CF loss ($L_\infty = 1.30$) compared to those with CF data ($L_\infty = 0.35$)
- Excluding item-text data leads to severe degradation on item-text evaluation ($L_\infty \approx 3$ vs. $L_\infty \approx 1.2$)

**Trade-off in Multi-Domain Training:**

Including item-text data introduces a modest trade-off (Section 7.1):

- **With Item-Text:** $L_\infty = 0.76$ for UIH (Item+CF+UIH configuration)
- **Without Item-Text:** $L_\infty = 0.66$ for UIH (CF+UIH configuration)

This suggests practitioners should consider downstream priorities when designing data mixtures. If UIH performance is the sole objective, excluding item-text may be beneficial. However, item-text prevents catastrophic forgetting of item semantics, which may be important for other tasks.

#### 5.3.2 UIH Mixture Ratio Ablation (Section 7.2, Table 7, Figures 5-7)

This experiment tests how the proportion of UIH data in the training mixture affects scaling behavior, using a reduced UIH dataset of 42M tokens.

**Mixture Ratios Tested:** 0.5%, 1%, 2%, 5%, and 15%

**Critical Finding: The 16-Repeat Cliff**

Table 7 (Section 7.2) shows that as UIH mixture ratio increases, the number of repeats on the reduced UIH data increases proportionally:

| UIH % | Tokens | Repeats | UIH L∞ | UIH α |
|-------|--------|---------|--------|-------|
| 0.5% | 0.82B | 4.1 | 0.75 | 0.423 |
| 1.0% | 1.63B | 8.2 | 0.78 | 0.485 |
| 2.0% | 3.26B | 16.3 | 0.81 | 0.576 |
| 5.0% | 8.15B | 40.8 | 0.84 | 0.685 |
| 15.0% | 24.45B | 122.3 | 0.90 | 0.754 |

**Overfitting Threshold:**

Figure 5 (Section 7.2) shows that perplexity on hold-out evaluation sets begins to **increase** when the UIH mixture ratio exceeds 2% (approximately 16 repeats):

- At 15% mixture ratio: degradation starts at 20B training tokens (~16 repeats)
- At 2% mixture ratio: degradation starts at 160B tokens (~16 repeats)
- At 5% mixture ratio: degradation starts at 80B tokens (~20 repeats)

**Model Size Independence:**

Figure 7 (Section 7.2) shows this threshold is **independent of model scale**. The perplexity on evaluation sets starts to increase at approximately 16 repeats regardless of whether the model is 0.6B, 1.7B, 4B, 8B, 14B, or 32B parameters. However, the degradation becomes more significant as models get larger.

**Interpretation:**

This finding aligns with Yang et al. (2024) and Muennighoff et al. (2025), who noted diminishing returns beyond ~4 repeats and sharp performance degradation beyond ~100× repetition. However, this paper specifies a **failure mode threshold** for recommendation synthetic data specifically. The key insight: simply looping over high-quality synthetic data indefinitely is not a viable scaling strategy; diversity must be maintained beyond the 16-repeat boundary.

#### 5.3.3 Tokenization Method Ablation (Appendix B, Figure 12)

The paper compares two tokenization strategies for representing items as semantic tokens:

- **SAE (Selected):** Pre-trained Sparse Autoencoder trained on multi-modal data (video, photo, text)
- **RQ-kmeans (Rejected):** In-domain Residual-Quantized k-means trained on Merrec dataset

**Results (Figure 12, Appendix B):**

| Domain | SAE L∞ | RQ-kmeans L∞ | SAE α | RQ-kmeans α |
|--------|--------|--------------|-------|-------------|
| General | 0.82 | 0.83 | 0.026 | 0.024 |
| Item-Text | 0.86 | 1.25 | 0.126 | 0.111 |
| CF Both Seen | 0.35 | 0.35 | 0.357 | 0.332 |
| UIH Full | 0.66 | 0.76 | 0.564 | 0.421 |
| UIH OOD | 0.69 | 0.80 | 0.660 | 0.472 |

**Why SAE Outperformed:**

Despite the theoretical risk of semantic mismatch (SAE features were learned in a video-centric domain), SAE consistently outperformed RQ-kmeans across all domains. The paper notes that RQ-VAE was stuck at a 50.54% collision rate during training, indicating severe representational capacity issues. This illustrates a key principle: theoretical alignment with the target domain does not guarantee better scaling behavior if the tokenization method introduces optimization difficulties.

### 5.4 Failure Cases and Limitations

The paper transparently reports several failure modes and limitations that are critical for practitioners to understand.

#### 5.4.1 Inverse Scaling on UIH OOD (Section 7.3, Figure 8)

One of the most surprising findings appears in the compute-optimal analysis (Figure 8, Section 7.3). For UIH evaluation on out-of-distribution items, the paper observes **inverse scaling**:

- **0.6B model (heavily over-trained at 454 tokens/param):** ~2.01 PPL
- **8B model (near Chinchilla-optimal at 20 tokens/param):** ~2.10 PPL

**Interpretation:**

The authors attribute this to the interaction between data repetition and model capacity. Larger models more readily memorize repeated behavioral sequences, leading to poorer OOD generalization. The extreme over-training regime of smaller models appears to act as implicit regularization against such memorization.

**Implication:**

This finding challenges the assumption that larger models always generalize better. For recommendation tasks with limited unique training data (repeated 6-7× in this study), smaller models may actually achieve better OOD performance due to reduced memorization capacity.

#### 5.4.2 CF Task Saturation at 4B Parameters (Section 7.3, Figure 8)

Collaborative filtering tasks saturate at models with 4B parameters (~1.47 PPL), indicating that the pattern complexity is bounded and fully learnable by 4B-parameter models given sufficient repetition.

**Implication:**

For CF tasks specifically, scaling beyond 4B parameters yields diminishing returns. This suggests that CF represents a fundamentally different learning problem than UIH, with lower complexity that can be fully captured by moderate-scale models.

#### 5.4.3 Training Loss vs. Evaluation Loss Divergence (Section 7.2, Figure 6b)

Figure 6(b) shows that training loss decreases monotonically even as evaluation perplexity begins to increase (indicating overfitting). This is a classic sign of memorization: the model continues to optimize on training data but loses generalization capability.

**Implication:**

Practitioners cannot rely on training loss alone to monitor model health when training on synthetic data with high repetition rates. Hold-out evaluation is essential to detect overfitting.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment: Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

The experimental evidence strongly supports the paper's central claims, but with important caveats that practitioners must consider.

**Claim 1: Synthetic data enables predictable scaling laws.**

**Support Level: Strong.** Figure 2 shows remarkably consistent power-law trends across seven evaluation domains and four model sizes. The scaling exponents are stable and interpretable, and the joint scaling law fits (Equations 2-5) provide quantitative predictions for compute allocation.

**Caveat:** The scaling laws are established for the specific data mixture and training configuration used in this study. Different data sources, mixture ratios, or model families may exhibit different scaling behavior.

**Claim 2: Synthetic data is superior to real data for learning generalizable preferences.**

**Support Level: Strong.** The TSTR > TRTR result (Figure 1, Section 5.2) is compelling, with SASRec showing +130% improvement on Recall@100. The ablation studies further validate that removing biases improves generalization.

**Caveat:** The test set was filtered to items present in both vocabularies, which may limit generalizability to cold-start scenarios. Additionally, the synthetic data was generated from the same source distribution (Merrec), so this is not a test of domain transfer.

**Claim 3: Layered curriculum design provides complementary learning signals.**

**Support Level: Strong.** The asymmetric transfer finding (CF → UIH but not UIH → CF) in Figure 4 provides direct evidence that the layers serve distinct cognitive functions. The 31% improvement in UIH L∞ when CF is included is substantial.

**Caveat:** The optimal mixture ratios may vary by domain and task. The paper acknowledges trade-offs (e.g., item-text inclusion slightly increases UIH loss).

**Claim 4: There is a hard limit on synthetic data reuse (~16 repeats).**

**Support Level: Moderate to Strong.** The 16-repeat threshold appears consistently across model sizes (Figure 7), suggesting it is a property of the data distribution. However, this threshold was identified on a reduced UIH dataset (42M tokens), and may vary with different data volumes or diversity levels.

**Overall Assessment:**

The experiments are well-designed and the results are internally consistent. The multi-dimensional evaluation (downstream ranking, scaling laws, ablation studies) provides converging evidence for the central thesis. The paper transparently reports failure cases (inverse scaling, overfitting thresholds) rather than hiding them, which strengthens credibility.

The primary limitation is that all experiments use the Merrec dataset and Qwen3 model family. Generalizability to other domains (e.g., video streaming, news recommendation) and model architectures remains an open question. However, the methodological framework is sufficiently detailed that practitioners can replicate and adapt it to their own contexts.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper establishes the first robust scaling laws for recommendation LLMs, the methodology relies on specific assumptions and introduces new constraints that practitioners must navigate. The shift from raw data to synthetic curricula solves the bias problem but creates challenges around data generation costs, reuse limits, and domain generalizability. This section critically examines the boundaries of the proposed framework.

### 6.1 Domain Specificity and Generalizability

The empirical validation is anchored to a single data domain, which limits the immediate generalizability of the findings.

*   **Single Dataset Source:** All scaling laws and synthetic data generation experiments rely on the **Merrec dataset**, a consumer-to-consumer (C2C) e-commerce corpus with 1.2B interactions (Section 6.1). While this is a large-scale dataset, e-commerce item interactions differ fundamentally from other recommendation domains such as video streaming (where watch time matters more than clicks) or news recommendation (where freshness is critical).
*   **Unverified Transfer:** The paper does not test whether the scaling exponents (e.g., $\alpha_{\text{UIH}} \approx 0.45–0.59$) hold for non-e-commerce domains. The hierarchical structure of e-commerce categories (e.g., `Women › Jewelry › Necklaces`) may facilitate the Item-Text alignment layer in ways that unstructured media (like video thumbnails or audio) do not.
*   **Implication:** Practitioners in non-e-commerce domains cannot assume the same data mixture ratios (e.g., 30% CF, 11% UIH) will be optimal. The "quantitative roadmap" (Section 1) may require recalibration for different interaction semantics.

### 6.2 The Synthetic Data Reuse Ceiling

A critical constraint identified in this work is that synthetic data cannot be reused indefinitely to scale performance. This imposes a hard ceiling on compute efficiency.

*   **The 16-Repeat Limit:** Section 7.2 establishes that model performance degrades when the model sees the same synthetic UIH sequences more than **~16 times**. Figure 7 shows this threshold is independent of model size, suggesting it is a property of the data distribution rather than model capacity.
*   **Scaling Implication:** In traditional scaling laws, one can often scale performance by repeating data if new data is unavailable (though with diminishing returns). Here, exceeding the repeat threshold causes **inverse scaling** where evaluation perplexity increases despite decreasing training loss (Section 7.2, Figure 6b).
*   **Operational Constraint:** To scale beyond this point, practitioners must generate **new** synthetic sequences rather than retraining on existing ones. This shifts the bottleneck from compute to the **data generation pipeline**. If generating high-quality synthetic walks (via Node2Vec) is computationally expensive, it may limit the speed of iteration compared to simply looping over static raw logs.

### 6.3 Model Size vs. Memorization Trade-off

Contrary to the standard assumption that larger models always generalize better, this paper identifies a regime where smaller models outperform larger ones on specific tasks.

*   **Inverse Scaling on OOD Tasks:** In the compute-optimal analysis (Section 7.3, Figure 8), the heavily over-trained 0.6B model achieved lower perplexity (~2.01 PPL) on UIH Out-of-Distribution (OOD) tasks than the near-optimal 8B model (~2.10 PPL).
*   **Mechanism:** The authors attribute this to **memorization**. Larger models have higher capacity to memorize the repeated synthetic sequences (6–7× repeats in the main experiment), which harms their ability to generalize to unseen item combinations. Smaller models, constrained by capacity, are forced to learn more generalizable patterns.
*   **Design Choice:** For recommendation CPT with limited unique data, scaling model size beyond a certain point (e.g., 4B for CF tasks, Section 7.3) may yield diminishing or negative returns. This challenges the industry trend of constantly increasing parameter counts for recommendation tasks.

### 6.4 Tokenization Stability vs. Semantic Alignment

The selection of the item tokenization method reveals a trade-off between optimization stability and semantic fidelity.

*   **Selected Method (SAE):** The paper selected a pre-trained Sparse Autoencoder (SAE) originally trained on **video-centric multi-modal data** (Appendix B.1). While this method yielded better scaling laws (Figure 12), the authors acknowledge a "Critical Risk (Semantic Mismatch)" where the extracted concepts might be mathematically proximal but semantically incongruous for e-commerce.
*   **Rejected Method (RQ-kmeans):** The in-domain Residual-Quantized k-means approach was theoretically better aligned with the target domain but suffered from high collision rates (50.54% for RQ-VAE vs. 3.5% for RQ-kmeans, Appendix B.1).
*   **Trade-off:** The framework prioritizes **training stability** (low collision, smooth scaling) over **semantic precision**. This means the model may learn robust statistical patterns without fully understanding the nuanced semantic differences between items (e.g., distinguishing "vintage" vs. "replica" beyond co-occurrence statistics).

### 6.5 Evaluation Boundaries and Cold-Start Claims

While the paper claims superiority for cold-start scenarios, the evaluation methodology partially constrains this claim.

*   **Vocabulary Filtering:** In the downstream ranking evaluation (Section 5.2), the test set was "filtered to include only items present in the vocabulary of both the synthetic and real training sets." This ensures a fair comparison but excludes true cold-start items (items never seen in any training data).
*   **Item-Text Generalization:** The paper relies on Item-Text alignment data to handle unseen items (Section 4.2.1). While Table 5 shows Item-Text has a positive scaling exponent ($\alpha \approx 0.13–0.21$), it is the weakest scaling domain. This suggests that while the model *can* generalize to new items via text, the learning signal is much weaker than collaborative signals.
*   **Limitation:** The +130% Recall@100 improvement (Section 1) applies to items within the known vocabulary. Performance on truly novel items (zero-shot recommendation) remains less empirically validated than the sequential modeling capabilities.

### 6.6 Static Graph vs. Dynamic Preferences

The synthetic data generation process assumes a static underlying structure of user preferences, which may not reflect reality.

*   **Static Graph Construction:** Layer 2 generates sequences using a graph where edge weights are determined by collaborative filtering relationships mined from historical logs (Section 4.3.1). This graph is effectively static during the data generation phase.
*   **Temporal Dynamics:** Real user preferences drift over time (e.g., seasonal trends, life events). The Node2Vec random walks simulate realistic sequences but do not explicitly model **temporal evolution** of the graph structure itself.
*   **Risk:** The model may learn stable, long-term preferences well but struggle with rapid trend shifts unless the underlying CF graph is frequently updated and regenerated. The paper does not specify the computational cost or frequency required to refresh this graph in a production setting.

### 6.7 Bias Mitigation Verification

While the framework is designed to remove bias, the paper acknowledges that verification is an ongoing responsibility.

*   **Downstream Audit Required:** Section 10 states that "practitioners must validate that downstream applications do not inadvertently introduce new biases through their choice of source data or graph construction."
*   **Graph Construction Bias:** The synthetic data is unbiased relative to *position* and *exposure*, but the underlying CF graph is still mined from raw logs (Section 4.2.2). If the raw logs contain inherent societal biases (e.g., gendered product associations), these could be encoded into the edge weights of the synthetic graph.
*   **Open Question:** The paper demonstrates removal of system-induced biases (position, popularity) but does not provide metrics for fairness across demographic groups. The "debiased" synthetic data may still propagate latent biases present in the aggregate interaction statistics.

### 6.8 Computational Overhead of Data Generation

The shift to synthetic data introduces a new computational cost center that is not fully quantified in the scaling laws.

*   **Generation Cost:** The pipeline requires mining association rules, training tokenizers (SAE/RQ), constructing graphs, and running Node2Vec walks (Appendix A). While the paper focuses on the *training* compute (163B tokens on B200 clusters, Section 6.1), the *preparation* compute is non-trivial.
*   **Trade-off:** The benefit is more efficient model training (predictable scaling). The cost is increased engineering complexity and upfront data generation time. For organizations with limited data engineering resources, the barrier to entry may be higher than simply fine-tuning on raw logs.

### 6.9 Summary of Constraints

| Constraint | Evidence | Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Reuse Limit** | Section 7.2 (16 repeats) | Cannot scale indefinitely via repetition; requires new data generation. |
| **Model Size** | Section 7.3 (Inverse Scaling) | Larger models (8B+) may overfit repeated synthetic data; 4B may be optimal for CF. |
| **Domain** | Section 6.1 (Merrec only) | Scaling exponents may not transfer to video/news domains without recalibration. |
| **Tokenization** | Appendix B (SAE vs. RQ) | Stability chosen over semantic alignment; potential nuance loss in item representation. |
| **Bias** | Section 10 (Impact Statement) | System biases removed, but latent societal biases in source data may persist in graph edges. |

These limitations do not invalidate the core contribution—the establishment of predictable scaling laws—but they define the operational boundaries within which these laws hold true. Future work must address whether these scaling relationships persist when the synthetic data generation pipeline is dynamically updated to reflect changing user behaviors.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than establish scaling laws—it fundamentally reorients how the recommendation community should approach LLM development. By demonstrating that data quality, not model architecture, was the primary inhibitor of predictable scaling, the work shifts the field from heuristic experimentation toward engineering science. This section examines the broader consequences of these findings, the research pathways they unlock, and practical guidance for practitioners considering adoption.

### 7.1 Transforming the Field: From Heuristics to Engineering Science

The establishment of predictable scaling laws for recommendation LLMs represents a **paradigm shift** comparable to the impact of Chinchilla scaling on general LLM development. Prior to this work, recommendation LLM development operated in what the paper calls a "costly trial-and-error" regime (Section 1). Practitioners had no quantitative basis for deciding whether to invest in larger models, more data, or architectural modifications.

**The new engineering discipline:** With the joint scaling laws established in Section 6.2.2, teams can now forecast performance before training begins. For example, using the UIH scaling law:

$$\ell_{\text{UIH}} = 0.514 + 1.89 \cdot N^{-0.138} + 63.9 \cdot D^{-0.272}$$

A practitioner can calculate that doubling training data ($D$) reduces loss by approximately $63.9 \cdot (2D)^{-0.272} - 63.9 \cdot D^{-0.272}$, while doubling model size ($N$) yields only $1.89 \cdot (2N)^{-0.138} - 1.89 \cdot N^{-0.138}$. This quantitative predictability transforms resource allocation from speculation into calculation.

**Redefining the research agenda:** The paper's findings suggest that prior model-centric approaches (LUM, SUAN) and system-centric approaches (PLUM) were optimizing the wrong variable. As Section 2.1 states, "even the most advanced architectures... would struggle to exhibit predictable power-law scaling if trained on the pathologically flawed data." This redirects the field's attention toward:

- **Data curation methodologies** rather than architectural novelty
- **Bias purification** rather than bias-robust modeling
- **Curriculum design** rather than loss function engineering

**Validation of data-centric AI:** This work provides some of the strongest empirical evidence to date for the "data-centric AI" movement (Zha et al. 2023; Angelakis and Rass 2024). By solving the data quality problem first, the authors enabled standard Qwen3 architectures to achieve predictable scaling—demonstrating that data quality can be more impactful than architectural innovation for certain domains.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

The framework and findings in this paper open several concrete research pathways that were previously inaccessible due to the absence of reliable scaling signals.

#### 7.2.1 Dynamic Curriculum Learning

The layered synthetic data framework (Section 4) presents items in a structured progression: semantics → collaborative patterns → sequential behavior. This suggests research into **adaptive curricula** that adjust the data mixture based on model learning progress.

**Open questions:**
- Can the model's current loss on each domain (Item-Text, CF, UIH) inform dynamic reweighting of the training mixture?
- Should the curriculum evolve during training, starting with high-CF ratios and shifting toward high-UIH as the model matures?
- What is the optimal "learning schedule" analogous to learning rate schedules?

The asymmetric transfer finding (CF → UIH improves $L_\infty$ by 31%, but not vice versa, Section 7.1) suggests that curriculum order matters. Future work could formalize this as a **pedagogical sequencing problem**.

#### 7.2.2 Cross-Domain Scaling Law Transfer

All experiments in this paper use the Merrec e-commerce dataset (Section 6.1). A critical open question is whether the scaling exponents transfer to other recommendation domains.

**Research agenda:**
- **Video streaming:** Does UIH scaling remain data-dominant ($\beta = 0.272$) when interactions represent watch time rather than clicks?
- **News recommendation:** How do temporal dynamics (freshness, trending) affect the irreducible loss $E$?
- **Social recommendation:** Do graph-based social connections introduce additional scaling dimensions?

If scaling exponents prove consistent across domains, this would establish **universal laws of recommendation learning**. If they vary systematically, the variation itself becomes a diagnostic tool for characterizing domain complexity.

#### 7.2.3 Synthetic Data Generation Efficiency

The paper identifies a hard **16-repeat ceiling** for synthetic UIH data (Section 7.2). Beyond this threshold, evaluation perplexity increases despite decreasing training loss. This creates a research imperative: how can we generate diverse synthetic sequences more efficiently?

**Potential directions:**
- **Procedural generation:** Can rule-based or grammar-driven methods generate infinite unique sequences while preserving collaborative signals?
- **LLM-based synthesis:** Could a separate LLM generate diverse user journeys conditioned on the CF graph, rather than relying solely on Node2Vec walks?
- **Incremental graph updates:** How frequently must the underlying CF graph be refreshed to reflect changing user preferences without invalidating prior training?

The computational cost of data generation (mining association rules, running Node2Vec, Section 4) is not quantified in the paper's scaling analysis. Future work should develop **generation-time scaling laws** that account for the full pipeline cost.

#### 7.2.4 Memorization vs. Generalization in Recommendation LLMs

The inverse scaling finding (Section 7.3)—where 0.6B models outperform 8B models on UIH OOD tasks—challenges fundamental assumptions about model capacity and generalization.

**Research opportunities:**
- **Memorization diagnostics:** Can we develop metrics to detect when a recommendation LLM is memorizing sequences rather than learning patterns?
- **Capacity-matched training:** Should model size be dynamically matched to data diversity, rather than scaled independently?
- **Regularization for recommendation:** What techniques (dropout, weight decay, data augmentation) best prevent memorization in the high-repetition regimes common to recommendation CPT?

This finding suggests that **optimal model size is task-dependent**, not universally "larger is better." For CF tasks, the paper shows saturation at 4B parameters (Section 7.3), implying that 8B+ models waste compute on this subtask.

#### 7.2.5 Fairness and Bias Auditing

While the framework removes system-induced biases (position, popularity, exposure), Section 10 acknowledges that "practitioners must validate that downstream applications do not inadvertently introduce new biases through their choice of source data or graph construction."

**Critical research needs:**
- **Demographic fairness metrics:** Does the synthetic graph encode latent societal biases (e.g., gendered product associations) from the aggregate interaction statistics?
- **Long-tail representation:** The paper notes that popularity bias suppresses niche content (Table 1). Does the synthetic curriculum actually improve long-tail item discovery, or does it merely remove position bias?
- **Bias propagation tracking:** Can we trace how biases in the raw Merrec logs propagate through the CF graph into the synthetic UIH sequences?

This is not merely an ethical concern—fairness issues could manifest as **scaling law violations** if certain user groups are systematically underrepresented in the synthetic curriculum.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Deployment Scenarios

The framework has immediate applicability across multiple recommendation contexts. Below we outline specific use cases and the conditions under which this approach provides maximum value.

#### 7.3.1 Industrial Recommendation Systems at Scale

**Best fit:** Large e-commerce platforms, streaming services, or content platforms with:
- Millions of items and users (similar to Merrec's 65.7M items, 2.6M users)
- Sufficient engineering resources to build and maintain the synthetic data pipeline
- Need for predictable performance forecasting to justify compute investments

**Expected benefits:**
- **Resource forecasting:** Use the scaling laws to calculate required tokens for target perplexity/recall metrics
- **Bias reduction:** Eliminate position and popularity biases that distort real interaction logs
- **Privacy compliance:** Synthetic UIH sequences are decoupled from individual user histories (Section 5.3)

**Integration path:**
1. Mine association rules from existing interaction logs to build the CF graph
2. Generate synthetic UIH sequences using Node2Vec (Appendix A provides hyperparameters)
3. Mix with item-text alignment data at approximately 9%/30%/11% ratios (Table 2)
4. Continually pre-train using the provided scaling law equations to determine optimal model size

#### 7.3.2 Cold-Start Item Onboarding

**Best fit:** Platforms with high item turnover (e.g., marketplace platforms, news sites) where new items lack interaction history.

**Why this framework helps:** The Item-Text alignment layer (Section 4.2.1) grounds item representations in language rather than ID co-occurrence. This enables the model to reason about content-based relationships for unseen items.

**Caveat:** The paper's evaluation filtered test sets to items present in both vocabularies (Section 5.2), so true zero-shot performance is less empirically validated. However, the Item-Text scaling exponent ($\alpha \approx 0.13–0.21$, Table 5) indicates positive learning signal for semantic generalization.

**Deployment consideration:** Maintain high-quality textual descriptions for all items. The Item-Text layer's effectiveness depends on description richness and consistency.

#### 7.3.3 Privacy-Constrained Environments

**Best fit:** Healthcare recommendations, financial product suggestions, or any domain where raw user interaction logs cannot be used for training due to regulatory constraints.

**Why this framework helps:** Synthetic UIH sequences are "generated from an aggregated graph model of item relationships, not from individual user histories" (Section 5.3). This decouples training data from identifiable user activity.

**Important note:** While the synthetic data itself is privacy-preserving, the underlying CF graph is mined from real logs. Organizations must ensure that the graph construction process does not leak individual-level information through edge weights or path structures.

#### 7.3.4 Multi-Task Recommendation Platforms

**Best fit:** Platforms that need to support multiple recommendation tasks (ranking, retrieval, explanation, personalization) from a single model.

**Why this framework helps:** The layered curriculum teaches distinct capabilities:
- **Item-Text:** Content understanding for explanation and cold-start
- **CF:** Global affinity patterns for retrieval and candidate generation
- **UIH:** Sequential behavior for ranking and next-item prediction

**Trade-off awareness:** Section 7.1 notes that including Item-Text data slightly increases UIH loss ($L_\infty = 0.76$ vs. $0.66$). Practitioners should weight the mixture based on downstream priorities. If ranking is the sole objective, a CF+UIH mixture may outperform the full three-layer curriculum.

### 7.4 Integration Guidance: When to Adopt This Framework

Not all recommendation scenarios benefit equally from this approach. The following decision framework helps practitioners determine when adoption is warranted.

#### 7.4.1 Favorable Conditions for Adoption

| Condition | Rationale | Evidence |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Compute budget > $100K** | Scaling laws enable ROI forecasting; small budgets cannot leverage predictable scaling | Section 6.1 uses 163B tokens on B200 clusters |
| **Item catalog > 1M items** | Synthetic data generation overhead amortized over large vocabulary | Merrec has 65.7M items (Section 6.1) |
| **Existing interaction logs > 100M** | Sufficient data to mine reliable CF associations | Merrec has 1.2B interactions |
| **Need for performance forecasting** | Scaling laws provide quantitative predictions | Section 6.2.2 equations enable compute-optimal allocation |
| **Privacy/regulatory constraints** | Synthetic UIH decouples from individual users | Section 5.3, Section 10 |
| **Bias mitigation priority** | Framework explicitly removes position/popularity/exposure bias | Table 1, Section 4.3.1 |

#### 7.4.2 Conditions Where Alternatives May Be Preferable

| Condition | Recommended Alternative | Rationale |
|-----------|------------------------|-----------|
| **Item catalog &lt; 10K items** | Traditional collaborative filtering | Synthetic data generation overhead not justified; CF matrices tractable |
| **Real-time model updates required** | Online learning on raw logs | Synthetic pipeline introduces latency; graph updates not instantaneous |
| **Limited data engineering resources** | Fine-tuning on raw logs | Framework requires association rule mining, graph construction, Node2Vec tuning (Appendix A) |
| **Domain is non-e-commerce** | Validate scaling exponents first | All experiments use Merrec; exponents may differ for video/news domains (Section 7.2.2) |
| **True cold-start is primary challenge** | Content-based filtering + human curation | Item-Text scaling is weakest ($\alpha \approx 0.13–0.21$); may not suffice for zero-shot |
| **Compute budget &lt; $10K** | Distillation from larger models | Scaling laws require substantial training to manifest; smaller budgets better served by knowledge transfer |

#### 7.4.3 Minimum Viable Implementation

For teams interested in adopting this framework without replicating the full experimental setup, the following represents a **minimum viable configuration** based on the paper's findings:

1. **Model size:** Start with 4B parameters. Section 7.3 shows CF tasks saturate at 4B, and UIH inverse scaling suggests 8B+ may overfit with limited unique data.

2. **Data mixture:** Use the full three-layer curriculum (Item-Text 9%, CF 30%, UIH 11%) plus 50% general domain text (Table 2). Adjust based on downstream priorities per Section 7.1 trade-offs.

3. **Training tokens:** Target at least 20 tokens per parameter (Chinchilla-optimal). For 4B models, this means ~80B tokens minimum. The paper used 163B tokens for all model sizes.

4. **UIH repeat limit:** Monitor evaluation perplexity and stop UIH data repetition at approximately **16 passes** through the unique synthetic sequences (Section 7.2). Beyond this, generate new sequences rather than repeating.

5. **Evaluation:** Construct held-out test sets at three generalization tiers (both seen, one unseen, both unseen) per Section 6.1 to distinguish memorization from genuine learning.

6. **Tokenization:** Use SAE-based semantic tokens if available. Appendix B shows SAE outperformed RQ-kmeans despite theoretical domain mismatch, likely due to training stability.

#### 7.4.4 Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Based on the paper's failure mode analysis, practitioners should watch for:

- **Ignoring the 16-repeat ceiling:** Continuing to train on the same synthetic sequences beyond ~16 passes causes evaluation perplexity to increase despite decreasing training loss (Section 7.2, Figure 6b).

- **Over-scaling model size:** For CF tasks, models larger than 4B show saturation (Section 7.3). For UIH OOD, larger models may generalize worse due to memorization (inverse scaling, Figure 8).

- **Relying on training loss alone:** Training loss decreases monotonically even during overfitting. Hold-out evaluation is essential (Section 5.4.3).

- **Assuming universal scaling exponents:** The exponents in Table 5 are specific to Merrec + Qwen3. Validate on your domain before using for forecasting.

- **Neglecting bias audits:** The synthetic graph inherits aggregate statistics from raw logs. Latent societal biases may persist even after removing system-induced biases (Section 6.7, Section 10).

### 7.5 Concluding Perspective: A Foundation for Scientific Recommendation

This paper's most enduring contribution may be methodological rather than technical. By establishing that recommendation LLMs obey predictable scaling laws when trained on appropriate data, the work transforms the field from **artisanal experimentation** into **engineering science**.

Prior to this work, a team deciding between a 4B and 8B model for recommendation had no quantitative basis for the decision. Now, the joint scaling law:

$$\ell = E + A \cdot N^{-\alpha} + B \cdot D^{-\beta}$$

provides explicit coefficients for each domain (Section 6.2.2, Table 4). Teams can calculate expected loss reduction per FLOP and allocate compute optimally.

**The remaining frontier:** The paper establishes *that* scaling laws exist for recommendation LLMs. The next phase of research must determine *how universal* these laws are across domains, model families, and data generation methodologies. If the exponents prove consistent, we will have discovered fundamental constants of recommendation learning. If they vary systematically, the variation itself becomes a diagnostic tool for characterizing domain complexity.

Either outcome advances the field beyond trial-and-error toward principled, predictable development. This is the hallmark of a mature engineering discipline—and this paper provides the first foundation for that maturity in recommendation LLMs.
