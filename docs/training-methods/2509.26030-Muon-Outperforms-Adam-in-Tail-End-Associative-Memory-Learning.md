## 1. Executive Summary
This paper demystifies why the Muon optimizer outperforms Adam in training Large Language Models (LLMs) by identifying that Muon's spectral normalization specifically benefits associative memory components (Value-Output attention weights and Feed-Forward Networks) rather than all transformer parameters equally. Through experiments on 160M and 0.7B NanoGPT models trained on FineWeb and heavy-tailed synthetic QA datasets, the authors show Muon yields more isotropic weight spectra and significantly improves accuracy on tail classes (e.g., achieving 0.976 vs 0.264 First Token Accuracy on extreme tail groups at 10,000 steps) compared to Adam. Theoretical analysis confirms Muon aligns with the outer-product structure of linear associative memories, enabling balanced learning across class-imbalanced distributions where Adam fails to generalize effectively to rare facts.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Empirical Success Without Theoretical Understanding

The Muon optimizer has demonstrated remarkable empirical performance in training Large Language Models (LLMs), consistently achieving nearly **2 times faster convergence** than Adam across diverse model sizes and architectures (Jordan et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025). This is significant because Adam has served as the cornerstone optimizer for LLMs since their inception, enabling the tremendous successes we see in modern language models (Kingma & Ba, 2015). When a new optimizer claims to outperform such an established baseline by this margin, understanding *why* it works becomes critical—not merely for academic curiosity, but for guiding future optimizer design and ensuring reliable scaling to larger models.

However, despite this empirical success, **the mechanism underlying Muon's superiority remains unclear**. The paper identifies a fundamental gap: while Muon can be interpreted as performing steepest descent with respect to the spectral norm (Bernstein & Newhouse, 2024), this interpretation does not clarify why optimization with respect to the matrix operator norm should outperform optimization with respect to the infinity norm (as in Adam). As stated in the Introduction:

> "Consequently, convergence analyses of Muon derived from this interpretation fail to account for its observed superiority over Adam (Li & Hong, 2025; Shen et al., 2025)."

This creates a problematic situation where practitioners adopt Muon based on empirical benchmarks without understanding which model components benefit most, under what data conditions it excels, or whether its advantages will persist as models scale further.

### Why This Problem Matters: Heavy-Tailed Distributions in Real-World Data

The importance of understanding Muon's mechanism extends beyond theoretical completeness—it has direct implications for how models learn from **real-world corpora, which are intrinsically heavy-tailed** (Abstract). In heavy-tailed distributions, a few classes (head classes) appear far more frequently than others (tail classes). This imbalance is not an artifact of synthetic benchmarks but reflects how knowledge is distributed in natural language: common facts appear repeatedly, while rare but important facts appear infrequently.

Consider a language model learning biographical knowledge: information about widely-discussed public figures appears thousands of times in training data, while information about lesser-known individuals may appear only a handful of times. An optimizer that fails to learn tail classes effectively will produce models that can answer common questions but fail on rare but potentially critical queries. The paper's Abstract emphasizes this practical concern:

> "On heavy-tailed data, it optimizes tail classes more effectively than Adam."

Understanding whether and why Muon improves tail-class learning directly impacts decisions about optimizer selection for knowledge-intensive applications where rare facts matter (e.g., medical diagnosis, legal reasoning, or specialized technical domains).

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

Existing work on Adam falls into two primary analytical perspectives, both of which have limitations in explaining Muon's advantages:

**1. Online Convex Optimization View.** This perspective analyzes Adam's properties when optimizing convex or non-convex loss functions. Works like Chen et al. (2019), Zhou et al. (2018), and Zou et al. (2019) derive convergence results and sufficient conditions for Adam's update rules. More recent work by Zhang et al. (2022) demonstrates Adam can converge without modification, and Li et al. (2023) relaxes smoothness assumptions. However, these analyses focus on *whether* Adam converges, not on *what features* it learns or how it handles class imbalance. They treat the optimization landscape abstractly without connecting to transformer architecture specifics.

**2. Feature Learning View.** This perspective examines how Adam's mechanisms influence the properties of learned features. Pan & Li (2023) relates Adam's superiority to low sharpness, Kunstner et al. (2024) finds Adam is better at learning heavy-tailed distributions than Gradient Descent, and Zhang et al. (2024a) shows Adam adapts to heterogeneous Hessian structures. While closer to the paper's concerns, these works still do not explain why Muon would outperform Adam on the same heavy-tailed tasks.

For Muon specifically, prior explanatory work has notable gaps:

- **Bernstein & Newhouse (2024)** interpret Muon as steepest descent w.r.t. the matrix operator norm, but as noted above, this does not explain *why* this norm choice helps.
- **Lau et al. (2025)** introduce a unifying preconditioning framework (PolarGrad) distinguishing optimizers addressing curvature anisotropy (Adam) from those addressing gradient anisotropy (Muon), but this remains at a high level without connecting to specific transformer components.
- **Liu et al. (2025)** analyze Muon's spectral properties but aggregate parameters across the model, obscuring which components drive Muon's behavior (Section 4.2 explicitly contrasts with this approach).
- **Vasudeva et al. (2025)** study Muon on shallow Vision Transformers for computer vision, grounding results in linear regression—different architecture and task domain from LLMs.

### How This Paper Positions Itself

This paper takes what it describes as **"the first step toward understanding the mechanisms underlying Muon's superiority over Adam in training LLMs"** (Introduction). Its positioning is distinguished by three key contributions relative to existing work:

**1. Component-Level Ablation.** Rather than treating the transformer as a black box or aggregating parameters, the paper systematically ablates which transformer components benefit most from Muon. It asks: "Which transformer components benefit most from Muon's matrix-norm–based optimization compared to Adam?" (Introduction). This granular analysis reveals that Value-Output (VO) attention weights and Feed-Forward Networks (FFNs) are the primary beneficiaries—components that prior work identifies as **associative memory stores** (Geva et al., 2020; Bietti et al., 2023).

**2. Associative Memory Lens.** The paper connects Muon's update mechanism to the learning dynamics of associative memory. Since associative memory parameters can be modeled as sums of outer products representing stored facts (Meng et al., 2022a), and Muon's update assigns equal magnitudes to orthogonal singular directions, the paper hypothesizes this structural alignment enables more balanced learning. This bridges optimizer mechanics with transformer function in a way prior work does not.

**3. Heavy-Tailed Task Evaluation with Theoretical Grounding.** The paper validates its hypotheses through both empirical evaluation on a knowledge-intensive, heavy-tailed QA task (Section 4.3) and theoretical analysis of a one-layer associative memory model under class-imbalanced data (Section 5). The theoretical results prove that Muon maintains balanced learning across classes regardless of feature embeddings, whereas Adam's performance depends strongly on embedding structure. This combination of empirical and theoretical evidence provides stronger mechanistic claims than prior empirical-only studies.

In summary, this paper positions itself at the intersection of optimizer theory, transformer architecture analysis, and associative memory research—filling a gap left by work that studies these areas in isolation. It moves beyond asking *whether* Muon works better to explaining *which components* benefit, *why* they benefit, and *under what data conditions* the advantages manifest.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a mechanistic analysis paper that combines empirical ablation studies with theoretical proofs to explain why the Muon optimizer outperforms Adam specifically on associative memory components of transformers when training on heavy-tailed data distributions.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being analyzed is the Muon optimizer applied to transformer neural networks, where Muon replaces the standard Adam optimizer for specific weight matrices during language model training. The problem it solves is understanding which transformer components actually benefit from Muon's matrix-normalized updates and why this leads to better learning of rare (tail) facts in imbalanced datasets, with the solution taking the form of component-level ablation experiments combined with spectral analysis and theoretical proofs on a simplified associative memory model.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The technical approach consists of four interconnected components that flow sequentially from empirical observation to theoretical explanation. First, the **Component Ablation Module** systematically applies Muon to different transformer weight matrices (Query, Key, Value, Output attention projections and FFN input/output/gate matrices) while keeping other components on Adam, measuring validation loss to identify which components drive Muon's overall advantage. Second, the **Spectral Analysis Module** computes four isotropy metrics (normalized SVD entropy, effective rank, top-k energy fraction, eigenvalue quantile ratio) on the weight matrices trained by Muon versus Adam to characterize how each optimizer shapes the singular value distribution. Third, the **Heavy-Tailed Knowledge Task Module** evaluates both optimizers on a synthetic biographical QA dataset with 32,768 classes following a power-law frequency distribution, measuring First Token Accuracy across head and tail class groups to quantify real-world performance differences. Fourth, the **Theoretical Analysis Module** constructs a one-layer linear associative memory model with controlled class imbalance (parameterized by $\alpha$ for head class mass and $\beta$ for head class proportion) and proves convergence bounds for Gradient Descent, Adam (reduced to SignGD), and Muon under both one-step and multi-step optimization protocols. Information flows from the ablation results (identifying VO and FFN as critical components) to the spectral analysis (showing Muon produces more isotropic weights on these components) to the heavy-tailed task (demonstrating Muon's tail-class advantage) to the theoretical model (proving why Muon achieves balanced learning regardless of embedding structure).

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first explain the Muon optimizer's update rule and how it differs mathematically from Adam, since understanding the optimizer mechanics is prerequisite to understanding why it affects different components differently.
- We then describe the transformer architecture components being ablated (attention projections and FFN matrices) and define which ones constitute "associative memory" parameters according to prior literature.
- We next detail the experimental protocols for component ablation, including model sizes, datasets, hyperparameters, and the independent versus combined configuration strategies.
- We then explain the four spectral isotropy metrics and what each measures about the weight matrix structure, since these metrics provide the mechanistic link between optimizer updates and learning behavior.
- We describe the heavy-tailed synthetic QA dataset construction, including the power-law class distribution parameters and the First Token Accuracy evaluation metric.
- Finally, we present the theoretical one-layer associative memory model, including the class imbalance assumptions, embedding orthogonality conditions, and the key quantities being bounded in the convergence proofs.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Paper Type and Core Idea.** This paper is a mechanistic analysis that combines empirical ablation studies with theoretical convergence proofs to demonstrate that Muon's matrix-normalized update rule aligns with the outer-product structure of linear associative memories, enabling more balanced learning of tail classes in heavy-tailed distributions compared to Adam's element-wise normalization.

**The Muon Optimizer Update Rule.** Muon operates on matrix-valued parameters $W \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ by first maintaining a momentum accumulator of gradients defined as $B_t = \mu B_{t-1} + G_t$ with $B_0 = 0$, where $\mu \in [0, 1)$ is the momentum coefficient (set to $\mu = 0.95$ in experiments) and $G_t = \nabla_W L(W_t)$ is the raw gradient of the loss at step $t$. At each optimization step, Muon computes the Singular Value Decomposition of the momentum matrix as $B_t = U_t S_t V_t^\top$, where $U_t \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times r_t}$ and $V_t \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times r_t}$ contain the left and right singular vectors, $S_t$ is diagonal containing singular values, and $r_t = \text{rank}(B_t)$. Muon then forms the nearest semi-orthogonal matrix $O_t = U_t V_t^\top$, which has the property that all its non-zero singular values equal 1, effectively normalizing the gradient's spectral structure while preserving its directional information. The parameter update is then $W_{t+1} = W_t - \eta_t O_t$, where $\eta_t$ is the learning rate at step $t$. In practice, the paper notes that $O_t$ can be approximated using a fixed number of Newton-Schulz iterations (e.g., 5 iterations) applied to $B_t (B_t^\top B_t)^{-1/2}$, which avoids computing the full SVD while preserving the scale normalization effect. This update rule can be characterized as the solution to the optimization problem $$\arg\min_W \left[ \langle B_t, W \rangle_F + \frac{\lambda}{2} \|W\|_{\ell_2 \to \ell_2}^2 \right],$$ where $\|\cdot\|_{\ell_2 \to \ell_2}$ denotes the matrix operator norm (the largest singular value) and $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$ determines the step size, meaning Muon performs steepest descent with respect to the spectral norm rather than a vector norm.

**Adam Optimizer for Comparison.** For theoretical analysis, the paper disables Adam's exponential moving averages (setting $\beta_1 = \beta_2 = 0$) following prior theoretical work, which reduces Adam to SignGD with update rule $W_{t+1}^{\text{SignGD}} = W_t^{\text{SignGD}} - \eta_{t+1} \text{sign}(\nabla_W L(W_t^{\text{SignGD}}))$, where $\text{sign}(\cdot)$ denotes the element-wise sign operator that returns $+1$, $-1$, or $0$ for each matrix entry. From the steepest descent perspective in Appendix A, Adam can be viewed as steepest descent with respect to the vector $\ell_\infty$ norm (the maximum absolute value of vector elements), with update direction $\text{sign}(g)$ and step size scaled by the $\ell_1$ norm $\|g\|_1$. This fundamental difference—Muon normalizes across orthogonal singular directions while Adam normalizes element-wise—creates different learning dynamics that the paper aims to explain.

**Transformer Architecture Components.** The transformer model processes a sequence of $N$ tokens embedded into a matrix $X^{(0)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times N}$, where $d$ is the embedding dimension. Each layer $\ell \in [L]$ processes its input through two sequential components: an attention module and a Feed-Forward Network (FFN) module. The attention module computes $$H^{(\ell)} = X^{(\ell-1)} + \sum_{h=1}^H W_{O,h}^{(\ell)} W_{V,h}^{(\ell)} X^{(\ell-1)} \text{sm}\left(X^{(\ell-1)\top} W_{K,h}^{(\ell)\top} W_{Q,h}^{(\ell)} X^{(\ell-1)}\right),$$ where $\text{sm}(\cdot)$ is the column-wise softmax operator, $H$ is the number of attention heads, $W_{Q,h}^{(\ell)}, W_{K,h}^{(\ell)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_k \times d}$ capture token relationships (Query and Key projections), and $W_{V,h}^{(\ell)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_v \times d}, W_{O,h}^{(\ell)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d_v}$ apply linear transformations (Value and Output projections). The FFN module then updates the representation as $X^{(\ell)} = H^{(\ell)} + W_{\text{out}}^{(\ell)} \sigma(W_{\text{in}}^{(\ell)} H^{(\ell)})$, where $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the element-wise activation function, $W_{\text{in}}^{(\ell)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_f \times d}$ is the input projection, and $W_{\text{out}}^{(\ell)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d_f}$ is the output projection. For gated FFN variants widely used in LLMs, the standard form is replaced with $\text{ff}_{\text{gate}}(H^{(\ell)}, W_{\text{in}}^{(\ell)}, W_{\text{out}}^{(\ell)}, W_{\text{gate}}^{(\ell)}) = W_{\text{out}}^{(\ell)} \left( \sigma(W_{\text{in}}^{(\ell)} H^{(\ell)}) \odot (W_{\text{gate}}^{(\ell)} H^{(\ell)}) \right)$, where $\odot$ is the Hadamard (element-wise) product and $W_{\text{gate}}^{(\ell)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_f \times d}$ is an additional gating projection. After $L$ layers, the final hidden state of the last token $X_{-1}^{(L)}$ is projected by the language model head $E_{\text{head}} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ to produce logits $E_{\text{head}} X_{-1}^{(L)}$, where $K$ is the vocabulary size.

**Associative Memory Parameters.** The paper identifies specific transformer components as associative memory parameters based on prior work showing they store facts and knowledge. For the attention module, prior work (Bietti et al., 2023) shows that $W_O$ can serve as a linear associative memory when $W_V$ is fixed, and since $W_O$ and $W_V$ play symmetric roles, the paper treats both as part of the associative memory parameters, collectively referred to as VO (Value-Output) weights. For the FFN, prior work on knowledge editing (Geva et al., 2020; Dai et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2022a,b) has shown this module functions as an associative memory well-approximated by linear associative memory models. A linear associative memory $W$ maps a key vector $e_s$ encoding a subject-relation pair to a value vector $e_o$ encoding an object such that $e_o = W e_s$ holds for all possible facts, and under orthogonality of embeddings, $W$ can be expressed as $W = \sum_{i=1}^K e_{o_i} e_{s_i}^\top$, where the summation is taken over $K$ facts. Thus, throughout the paper, $W_O$, $W_V$, and FFN matrices ($W_{\text{in}}$, $W_{\text{out}}$, and $W_{\text{gate}}$ for gated variants) in LLMs are referred to as the associative memory parameters, while $W_Q$ and $W_K$ (Query-Key projections) are not considered associative memory components.

**Component Ablation Experimental Protocol.** The paper employs a two-stage ablation protocol on 160M and 0.7B NanoGPT models trained on the FineWeb dataset. In the first stage called "Independent Blocks," Muon is applied to a single block at a time while keeping all other blocks on Adam, covering attention projections $W_Q, W_K, W_V, W_O$ and feed-forward matrices $W_{\text{in}}, W_{\text{out}}$ (and $W_{\text{gate}}$ for gated FFN). This isolates which individual components benefit most from Muon optimization. In the second stage called "Combined Configurations," Muon is applied to the most impactful subsets identified in the first stage (specifically VO+FFN) to examine whether partial application can recover the performance gains of full Muon (where all transformer parameters use Muon). The experiments evaluate both gated and non-gated FFN variants of NanoGPT. For the 160M model on FineWeb, the paper disables weight decay and Nesterov acceleration for both Adam and Muon, conducts learning rate grid search on $[1 \times 10^{-1}, 5 \times 10^{-2}, 2 \times 10^{-2}, 1 \times 10^{-2}, 5 \times 10^{-3}, 2 \times 10^{-3}, 1 \times 10^{-3}, 5 \times 10^{-4}, 2 \times 10^{-4}]$, sets $\beta_1 = 0.8, \beta_2 = 0.95$ for Adam and $\mu = 0.95$ for Muon. For the 0.7B model, learning rate grid search is conducted on $[2 \times 10^{-3}, 1 \times 10^{-3}, 5 \times 10^{-4}, 2 \times 10^{-4}]$ with $\beta_1 = 0.9, \beta_2 = 0.95$ for Adam and $\mu = 0.95$ for Muon. The experiments are conducted on 8 A100 GPUs with 80 GB memory, and group query attention is not adopted so parameter sizes of $W_Q, W_K, W_V, W_O$ are the same. Training runs for 10,000 steps with validation loss measured throughout.

**Spectral Isotropy Metrics.** To characterize how Muon versus Adam shapes weight matrices, the paper defines four metrics on the singular value spectrum. For a weight matrix with $n$ non-zero singular values $\sigma = (\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \ldots, \sigma_n)$ arranged in descending order, the normalized singular energy distribution is $q = (q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_n)$ where each component $q_i = \sigma_i^2 / \sum_{j=1}^n \sigma_j^2$, representing the fraction of energy captured by each corresponding singular vector. The first metric is **Normalized SVD Entropy**, adapted from Alter et al. (2000), defined as $H_{\text{norm}}(\sigma) = -\frac{1}{\log n} \sum_{i=1}^n q_i \log q_i$, which quantifies the uniformity of the singular energy distribution where higher entropy indicates a more isotropic matrix with energy distributed evenly across many directions. The second metric is **Effective Rank**, from Roy & Vetterli (2007), calculated as $e\text{Rank}(\sigma) = \exp\left(-\sum_{i=1}^n q_i \log q_i\right)$, which provides a continuous measure of the number of significant singular dimensions used by the matrix (equivalent to the perplexity of the energy distribution). The third metric is **Top-k Energy Fraction**, defined as $\text{TopE}_k(\sigma) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i^2}{\sum_{j=1}^n \sigma_j^2}$ assuming singular values are sorted in descending order, which measures the concentration of energy within the top-$k$ principal singular components. The fourth metric is **Eigenvalue Quantile Ratio**, computed as $Q_{75/25}(\sigma) = \frac{Q_3(\{\sigma_i^2\})}{Q_1(\{\sigma_i^2\})}$ where $Q_3$ and $Q_1$ are the 75th and 25th percentiles of the eigenvalues $\{\sigma_i^2\}_{i=1}^n$, which measures the spread of the singular energy distribution while being robust to extreme outliers. Intuitively, more isotropic weights correspond to larger values of normalized SVD entropy and effective rank, and smaller values of top-k energy fraction and eigenvalue quantile ratio.

**Heavy-Tailed Knowledge Task Dataset.** The paper constructs a synthetic biographical QA dataset following Allen-Zhu & Li (2024) to evaluate optimizer performance under class imbalance. Each biography is constructed from seven key attributes: name, birthdate, birthplace, educational institution, major, employer, and workplace, with attribute values sampled from predefined lists (approximately 400 first names, 1000 surnames, 300 educational institutions, 100 majors, and 300 employers). The dataset generation is controlled by an integer parameter $m$, organizing classes into $m+1$ groups indexed from $g = 0$ to $m$. Group $g$ contains $N_g$ classes where $N_0 = 1$ and $N_g = 2^{g-1}$ for $g > 0$. Each class within group $g$ is allocated $S_g = 2^{m-g}$ selections, and for each selection, $n_{\text{qa}}$ unique QA pairs are generated by formatting templates with biographical information. In the experiment, parameters are set to $m = 15$ and $n_{\text{qa}} = 6$, resulting in $2^{15} = 32,768$ total classes. The number of samples per class ranges from 196,608 for the head class (group 0) down to just 6 for each of the 16,384 tail classes (group 15), following a power-law distribution. Performance is evaluated via First Token Accuracy (FTA), which checks if the first generated token of the answer matches the ground truth, analyzed across different data frequency groups from high-frequency (head) to low-frequency (tail) data.

**Theoretical One-Layer Associative Memory Model.** For theoretical analysis, the paper abstracts the transformer to a one-layer linear associative memory model that retains the associative memory and language model head while replacing all preceding modules with given feature embeddings. The model considers $K$ triplets $\{(s_i, r_i, o_i)\}_{i=1}^K$ where subject-relation pairs $(s_i, r_i)$ and objects $o_i$ are embedded into columns of matrices $E \in \mathbb{R}^{d_s \times K}$ and $\tilde{E} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_o \times K}$ respectively. A linear associative memory $W \in \mathbb{R}^{d_o \times d_s}$ predicts the object for a query $E_k$ with probabilities $f_W(E_k) = \text{sm}(\tilde{E}^\top W E_k) \in \mathbb{R}^K$, where $\text{sm}(\cdot)$ is the softmax function. The objective is to minimize the population cross-entropy loss $L(W) = -\sum_{k=1}^K p_k \log [f_W(E_k)]_k$, where $p_k$ is the frequency or probability of the $k$-th triplet. Two key assumptions structure the theoretical analysis. **Assumption 5.1** states that embeddings $E$ and $\tilde{E}$ are orthonormal, i.e., $E^\top E = \tilde{E}^\top \tilde{E} = I_{K,K}$, which rules out feature-level imbalance and is empirically verified on Llama3-8b-instruct where average angles between embeddings are near 90 degrees (Figure 4a). **Assumption 5.2** states that the first $L$ triplets share the same probability contributing total mass $\alpha$ (i.e., $p_k = \alpha/L$ for $k \in [L]$) while the remaining triplets share the same probability contributing total mass $1-\alpha$ (i.e., $p_k = (1-\alpha)/(K-L)$ for $k > L$), creating a two-class imbalance setting where $\beta = L/K$ quantifies the proportion of head classes and $\alpha/\beta$ quantifies the degree of balance.

**Theoretical Convergence Quantities.** To quantify learning imbalance across $K$ knowledge items, the paper defines the maximal probability gap as $\Delta(W) := \max_{i,j \in [K]} [f_W(E_i)]_i - [f_W(E_j)]_j$, where $[f_W(E_i)]_i$ denotes the probability assigned to the correct item $i$, with larger $\Delta(W)$ indicating greater imbalance. For one-step analysis, the paper defines the smallest correct-class probability across all knowledge items under the condition that at least one item achieves correct-class probability of at least $1-\epsilon$ as $$\varrho_{\text{opt}}^\epsilon = \inf_{\eta \geq 0} \left\{ \min_{k \in [K]} [f_{W_\eta}(E_k)]_k \;\middle|\; \max_{k \in [K]} [f_{W_\eta}(E_k)]_k \geq 1-\epsilon, W_\eta = W_0 - \eta \cdot G_{\text{opt}}(W_0) \right\},$$ where $\text{opt} \in \{\text{GD}, \text{SignGD}, \text{Muon}\}$ and $G_{\text{opt}}(W_0)$ denotes the parameter update of the optimizer at $W_0 = 0_{d_o, d_s}$. When $\varrho_{\text{opt}}^\epsilon \approx 1-\epsilon$, the optimizer achieves balanced learning across facts; when $\varrho_{\text{opt}}^\epsilon \approx 0$, imbalanced learning ensues. The quantity relates to the probability gap as $\Delta(W) = 1 - \epsilon - \varrho_{\text{opt}}^\epsilon \geq 0$.

**Key Theoretical Results.** Theorem 5.3 establishes one-step convergence bounds showing that for GD, $\varrho_{\text{GD}}^\epsilon = O(\epsilon^{-r(\alpha,\beta)} K^{r(\alpha,\beta)-1})$ where $r(\alpha,\beta) = \min\left\{\frac{\alpha(1-\beta)}{\beta(1-\alpha)}, \frac{\beta(1-\alpha)}{\alpha(1-\beta)}\right\} &lt; 1$, meaning GD is highly sensitive to data imbalance with the worst-learned triplet's probability decreasing as imbalance increases. For Muon, the theorem proves $\varrho_{\text{Muon}}^\epsilon \geq 1 - \epsilon \left(1 + O\left(\frac{\log K}{K}\right)\right)$ with update $G_{\text{Muon}}(W_0) = -\tilde{E} E^\top + O\left(\frac{1}{K} \tilde{E} J_{K,K} E^\top\right)$, where $J_{K,K}$ is the all-ones matrix, meaning Muon learns in a balanced fashion unaffected by data imbalance for any embeddings, with the update allocating equal strength to all directions (nearly identical singular values). For Adam, the theorem shows there exist embeddings where $\varrho_{\text{SignGD}}^\epsilon \geq 1-\epsilon$ (balanced case with disjoint supports) but also exist embeddings where $\varrho_{\text{SignGD}}^\epsilon = O(\epsilon^{-0.7} K^{-0.3})$ with $\frac{\sigma_{\min}(G_{\text{SignGD}}(W_0))}{\sigma_{\max}(G_{\text{SignGD}}(W_0))} \leq 25\%$, meaning Adam's performance is unstable with respect to embeddings, with element-wise normalization disrupting the inherent matrix structure and introducing spectral decay. Theorem 5.4 extends the Muon analysis to multi-step optimization, proving $\varrho_{\text{Muon}}^\epsilon \geq 1 - \epsilon \left(1 + O\left(\frac{\log K}{K}\right)\right)$ for any step $t \geq 0$, showing the balanced learning property persists throughout the optimization trajectory.

**Design Choices and Rationale.** The paper chooses to ablate transformer components rather than treating the model as a black box because prior work aggregating parameters (e.g., Liu et al., 2025) obscures which components drive Muon's behavior. The paper chooses spectral isotropy metrics rather than just validation loss because these metrics provide mechanistic insight into how the optimizer shapes the weight matrix structure, connecting optimizer mechanics to learning dynamics. The paper chooses a synthetic heavy-tailed QA task rather than natural language benchmarks because the controlled power-law distribution allows precise measurement of head versus tail class performance without confounding factors from natural data. The paper chooses a one-layer theoretical model rather than full transformers because this abstraction retains the associative memory structure while enabling tractable convergence proofs, with the orthogonality assumption empirically verified on Llama3-8b-instruct to ensure relevance to real models. The paper disables momentum in theoretical analysis ($\beta_1 = \beta_2 = 0$ for Adam, no momentum for Muon) following prior theoretical work to isolate the core update mechanics, though empirical experiments use momentum ($\mu = 0.95$ for Muon, $\beta_1 = 0.8-0.9, \beta_2 = 0.95$ for Adam) to match practical usage.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the paper's most novel contributions, distinguishing between incremental refinements of existing knowledge and fundamental innovations that change how we understand optimizer-transformer interactions. Each insight is anchored to specific evidence from the paper and contrasted against prior work discussed in Section 2.

### 4.1 Discovery That Muon's Advantage Concentrates on Associative Memory Components

**What is novel:** Prior work on Muon treated the transformer as a monolithic block, aggregating spectral metrics across all parameters (Liu et al., 2025) or analyzing optimizer behavior without component-level resolution (Bernstein & Newhouse, 2024). This paper makes the first systematic attempt to **ablate Muon's effect by transformer component**, revealing that the optimizer's advantage is not uniform but highly concentrated.

**Evidence from the paper:** Figure 1 and Table 1 show that applying Muon to only VO (Value-Output) attention weights and FFN matrices recovers nearly the full performance gain of applying Muon to all parameters. Specifically, in the combined configuration with non-gated FFN, `Muon(VO Attn, FFN) & Adam(QK Attn)` achieves validation loss of 3.5858 versus 3.5654 for full Muon—a gap of only 0.0204 after 10,000 steps. In contrast, `Muon(QK Attn) & Adam(VO Attn, FFN)` achieves 3.8925, performing barely better than all-Adam (3.9242). This demonstrates that **QK projections contribute negligibly to Muon's advantage** despite having the same parameter count as VO.

**Why this is significant:** This finding has immediate practical implications for optimizer deployment. If Muon is computationally more expensive than Adam (due to SVD or Newton-Schulz iterations), practitioners could apply it selectively to VO+FFN components only, capturing most of the benefit while reducing overhead. More fundamentally, it suggests that **not all transformer parameters benefit equally from matrix-norm optimization**—some components (QK) may be better served by vector-norm methods like Adam. This challenges the prevailing assumption that optimizer choice should be model-wide rather than component-specific.

**Incremental vs. fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in how we analyze optimizer effects. Prior work asked "Does Muon beat Adam?" This paper asks "Which parts of the model make Muon beat Adam?"—a qualitatively different question that reveals structural insights about transformer optimization landscapes.

### 4.2 Structural Alignment Between Muon's Update Rule and Associative Memory Outer Products

**What is novel:** The paper establishes a **mechanistic connection** between Muon's spectral normalization and the mathematical structure of linear associative memories. Prior work identified that VO and FFN serve as associative memory stores (Geva et al., 2020; Bietti et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2022a), and separately that Muon performs spectral-normalized updates (Jordan et al., 2024). This paper is the first to connect these two observations, showing that Muon's update rule is intrinsically aligned with how associative memories store and retrieve facts.

**Evidence from the paper:** Section 4.1 explains that a linear associative memory can be expressed as $W = \sum_{i=1}^K e_{o_i} e_{s_i}^\top$, a sum of outer products. Muon's update computes the SVD of the gradient $G = \sum_{i=1}^d s_i u_i v_i^\top$ and forms $O = \sum_{i=1}^d u_i v_i^\top$, assigning **equal magnitude to each orthogonal singular direction**. The paper argues that singular values in the gradient encode fact frequencies in training data, so Muon's uniform treatment of singular directions prevents frequent facts from dominating the update. This is contrasted with Adam's element-wise sign operation, which disrupts the matrix structure.

**Why this is significant:** This insight provides a **unifying explanation** for multiple empirical observations: why VO+FFN benefit most (they are associative memories), why Muon produces isotropic weights (Section 4.2, Figure 2), and why Muon excels on tail classes (Section 4.3, Figure 3). Rather than treating these as separate phenomena, the associative memory lens shows they are consequences of a single mechanism: structural alignment between optimizer and model function. This enables principled predictions about where Muon should help (any architecture using outer-product memory structures) and where it might not (architectures without such structures).

**Incremental vs. fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in optimizer theory. Prior explanations for Muon's success relied on abstract notions like "steepest descent w.r.t. spectral norm" without connecting to what transformers actually compute. By grounding the explanation in associative memory structure, the paper provides a functional (not just geometric) account of why the optimizer works.

### 4.3 Proof That Muon Achieves Embedding-Independent Balanced Learning

**What is novel:** Theorem 5.3 and Theorem 5.4 establish that Muon achieves balanced learning across classes **regardless of feature embedding structure**, while Adam's performance is **provably unstable** with respect to embeddings. This is the first theoretical result showing that an optimizer's effectiveness on imbalanced data can depend on whether its update rule preserves or disrupts matrix structure.

**Evidence from the paper:** Theorem 5.3 proves that for Muon, $\varrho_{\text{Muon}}^\epsilon \geq 1 - \epsilon \left(1 + O\left(\frac{\log K}{K}\right)\right)$ for any orthonormal embeddings $E$ and $\tilde{E}$, meaning the worst-learned class achieves nearly the same accuracy as the best-learned class. For Adam, the theorem constructs two embedding scenarios: with disjoint supports ($E = \tilde{E} = I_{K,K}$), Adam achieves $\varrho_{\text{SignGD}}^\epsilon \geq 1 - \epsilon$ (balanced); but with overlapping supports (block-wise diagonal construction), $\varrho_{\text{SignGD}}^\epsilon = O(\epsilon^{-0.7} K^{-0.3})$ with spectral decay $\sigma_{\min}/\sigma_{\max} \leq 25\%$. The exponents 0.3 and 0.7 are **intrinsic to Adam's update** and independent of the data imbalance parameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$.

**Why this is significant:** This theoretical result explains the large error bars observed in Adam's empirical performance (Figure 2, Figure 3) as a fundamental property, not experimental noise. It implies that Adam's effectiveness on heavy-tailed tasks is **contingent on lucky embedding initialization**, while Muon's performance is **robust by design**. For practitioners, this suggests Muon provides more reliable convergence guarantees when training on imbalanced data—a critical consideration for real-world applications where tail classes often represent important but rare cases (e.g., rare diseases in medical text, edge cases in legal documents).

**Incremental vs. fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in optimization theory. Prior convergence analyses for Adam (Chen et al., 2019; Zou et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022) focus on whether the optimizer converges, not on whether it converges *balanced* across classes. This paper introduces a new analytical lens: **learning balance as a convergence criterion**, which is more relevant for imbalanced data scenarios than traditional loss-based convergence metrics.

### 4.4 Spectral Isotropy Metrics as Mechanistic Diagnostics for Optimizer Behavior

**What is novel:** While prior work analyzed optimizer spectra (Liu et al., 2025; Vasudeva et al., 2025), this paper introduces a **suite of four complementary isotropy metrics** (normalized SVD entropy, effective rank, top-k energy fraction, eigenvalue quantile ratio) and demonstrates their stability as diagnostic tools across random seeds and model scales.

**Evidence from the paper:** Figure 2 shows that Muon's isotropy metrics are stable across 10 random seeds (negligible error bars), while Adam's metrics fluctuate significantly throughout training. For example, in the VO (non-gated FFN) setting, Muon's SVD entropy remains consistently above 0.7, while Adam's varies between 0.4 and 0.7. This pattern holds across model scales: Figures 6 and 7 (Appendix C.2) show similar isotropy gaps on the 0.7B model. The paper explicitly contrasts with Liu et al. (2025), noting three differences: (i) decomposition by associative memory components rather than aggregation, (ii) analysis of Adam's instability under random initialization, and (iii) focus on dense architectures rather than MoE models.

**Why this is significant:** These metrics provide **actionable diagnostics** for optimizer selection and tuning. If a practitioner observes rapidly decaying spectral energy (high top-k fraction, low effective rank) during training, this may indicate the optimizer is failing to learn diverse features—a warning sign that could prompt switching to Muon for associative memory components. The metrics also enable **fairer optimizer comparisons**: rather than comparing only final validation loss, researchers can assess whether optimizers produce qualitatively different weight structures that may affect downstream tasks (e.g., fine-tuning, knowledge editing).

**Incremental vs. fundamental:** This is an **incremental improvement** built on existing spectral analysis techniques (Alter et al., 2000; Roy & Vetterli, 2007). The novelty lies not in the metrics themselves but in their systematic application to optimizer comparison and their connection to associative memory learning dynamics. However, this incremental work serves the fundamental insights above by providing empirical evidence for the mechanistic claims.

**Summary of Innovation Levels:**

| Insight | Innovation Level | Primary Contribution |
|---------|-----------------|---------------------|
| VO+FFN as primary beneficiaries | Fundamental | Component-level ablation methodology |
| Associative memory structural alignment | Fundamental | Mechanistic explanation linking optimizer to architecture |
| Embedding-independent balanced learning | Fundamental | Theoretical proof of optimizer robustness |
| Spectral isotropy diagnostics | Incremental | Empirical measurement framework |

The first three insights fundamentally change how we understand optimizer-transformer interactions, moving from black-box performance comparisons to mechanistic explanations grounded in architecture function. The fourth insight, while incremental, provides the empirical foundation that makes the fundamental claims credible and actionable.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the paper's experimental methodology, quantitative results, and the degree to which these experiments support the central claims. We organize the analysis by experimental objective: (1) identifying which transformer components benefit from Muon, (2) characterizing spectral properties of learned weights, (3) evaluating performance on heavy-tailed knowledge tasks, and (4) validating theoretical predictions. Throughout, we cite specific figures, tables, and numerical values from the paper.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Datasets and Tasks

The paper employs two distinct evaluation regimes, each serving a specific analytical purpose:

**FineWeb Pretraining (Section 4.1–4.2).** The primary dataset for component ablation and spectral analysis is FineWeb (Penedo et al., 2024), a large-scale web text corpus. Models are trained for **10,000 steps** with validation loss measured throughout. This dataset provides a realistic pretraining environment but does not have controlled class imbalance—its purpose is to identify which transformer components benefit from Muon under standard conditions.

**Synthetic Heavy-Tailed QA Task (Section 4.3).** For evaluating tail-class learning, the paper constructs a synthetic biographical QA dataset following Allen-Zhu & Li (2024). The dataset contains **32,768 classes** (2^15) organized into 16 groups (g = 0 to 15) with a power-law frequency distribution:
- Group 0 (head): 1 class with **196,608 samples**
- Group 15 (extreme tail): 16,384 classes with **6 samples each**
- Intermediate groups follow $N_g = 2^{g-1}$ classes with $S_g = 2^{15-g}$ selections per class

Each biography contains seven attributes (name, birthdate, birthplace, educational institution, major, employer, workplace), and QA pairs are generated via templates (Appendix B.2 provides full details). This controlled setup enables precise measurement of head versus tail class performance.

#### Models and Hardware

Experiments are conducted on two model scales:
- **160M NanoGPT** (primary experiments): Used for all main figures (Figures 1–3) and Table 1
- **0.7B NanoGPT** (scaling validation): Used for Appendix C.2 experiments (Figures 5–7) to verify findings persist at larger scale

Both gated and non-gated FFN variants are evaluated. Training is performed on **8 A100 GPUs with 80 GB memory** (Appendix B.1). For embedding orthogonality verification (Figure 4a), the paper analyzes **Llama3-8b-instruct** across layers 5, 10, 15, 20, 25.

#### Optimizer Configurations

The paper compares multiple optimizer configurations:
- **All Adam**: Baseline with Adam on all transformer parameters
- **All Muon**: Muon on all transformer parameters (except token embeddings and language model head, which use Adam per Jordan et al., 2024)
- **Hybrid Configurations**: Muon applied to specific components (VO, QK, V, O, Win, Wout, Wgate) while Adam is used on remaining components
- **SGD+Momentum**: Additional baseline for the heavy-tailed QA task

Hyperparameters are carefully tuned (Appendix B.1):
- **160M model**: Learning rate grid search on $[1 \times 10^{-1}, \ldots, 2 \times 10^{-4}]$; Adam $\beta_1 = 0.8, \beta_2 = 0.95$; Muon $\mu = 0.95$
- **0.7B model**: Learning rate grid search on $[2 \times 10^{-3}, \ldots, 2 \times 10^{-4}]$; Adam $\beta_1 = 0.9, \beta_2 = 0.95$; Muon $\mu = 0.95$
- Weight decay and Nesterov acceleration are disabled for both optimizers to isolate their effects

#### Evaluation Metrics

Three metric families are employed:

**1. Validation Loss.** Standard pretraining metric measured at each training step. Table 1 reports final values at 10,000 steps.

**2. Spectral Isotropy Metrics (Section 4.2).** Four complementary metrics characterize weight matrix structure:
- **Normalized SVD Entropy**: $H_{\text{norm}}(\sigma) = -\frac{1}{\log n} \sum_{i=1}^n q_i \log q_i$, range [0, 1], higher = more isotropic
- **Effective Rank**: $e\text{Rank}(\sigma) = \exp\left(-\sum_{i=1}^n q_i \log q_i\right)$, continuous measure of active dimensions
- **Top-10 Energy Fraction**: $\text{TopE}_{10}(\sigma) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{10} \sigma_i^2}{\sum_{j=1}^n \sigma_j^2}$, lower = less concentration in top components
- **Eigenvalue Quantile Ratio**: $Q_{75/25}(\sigma) = \frac{Q_3(\{\sigma_i^2\})}{Q_1(\{\sigma_i^2\})}$, lower = more uniform spread

**3. First Token Accuracy (FTA).** For the QA task, FTA checks if the first generated token matches the ground truth answer. Results are reported per group (Tables 3–8) and aggregated (Figure 3).

### 5.2 Component Ablation Results: VO+FFN Drive Muon's Advantage

The paper's first major experimental contribution is the systematic ablation of Muon's effect by transformer component. Table 1 and Figure 1 present the core results.

#### Independent Blocks Analysis

In the "Independent Blocks" setting (Figures 1a–1b), Muon is applied to one component at a time while all others use Adam. The results reveal a stark hierarchy:

| Component | Non-gated FFN Loss | Gated FFN Loss | vs. All Adam Improvement |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------|-------------------------|
| All Adam | 3.9242 | 3.8837 | — |
| Muon(QK) | 3.8925 | 3.8518 | ~0.03–0.04 |
| Muon(VO) | 3.7644 | 3.6874 | ~0.16–0.20 |
| Muon(V) | 3.8301 | 3.7482 | ~0.09–0.14 |
| Muon(O) | 3.7712 | 3.7604 | ~0.12–0.15 |
| Muon(Win) | 3.7170 | 3.7918 | ~0.13–0.09 |
| Muon(Wout) | 3.7023 | 3.7843 | ~0.15–0.10 |

Key observations:
- **VO weights dominate**: Applying Muon to only VO (Value + Output) achieves loss of 3.7644 (non-gated) and 3.6874 (gated), substantially better than QK's 3.8925 and 3.8518 despite identical parameter counts.
- **Wout is critical in FFN**: For non-gated FFN, Muon(Wout) achieves 3.7023 vs. Muon(Win)'s 3.7170. For gated FFN, Wout and Wgate perform similarly (~3.78–3.79).
- **QK contributes minimally**: Muon on QK provides only marginal improvement over All Adam, suggesting Query-Key projections do not benefit from matrix-norm optimization.

#### Combined Configurations: Recovering Full Muon Performance

The "Combined Configurations" (Figures 1c–1d, Table 1) test whether applying Muon to the most impactful components recovers full Muon performance:

| Configuration | Non-gated FFN | Gated FFN | Gap to Full Muon |
|--------------|---------------|-----------|------------------|
| Full Muon | 3.5654 | 3.5125 | — |
| Muon(VO, FFN) + Adam(QK) | 3.5858 | 3.5312 | 0.0204 / 0.0187 |
| Muon(QK) + Adam(VO, FFN) | 3.6702 | 3.7185 | 0.1048 / 0.2060 |
| All Adam | 3.9242 | 3.8837 | 0.3588 / 0.3712 |

The **VO+FFN hybrid recovers 94–95% of Muon's advantage** over Adam (gap of ~0.02 vs. full Muon's ~0.36 gap to Adam). In contrast, applying Muon only to QK recovers less than 30% of the advantage. This strongly supports Observation 1:

> "Muon is most effective when applied to VO and FFN; in particular, applying Muon to only VO+FFN almost recovers the full-Muon trajectory."

#### Scaling to 0.7B Models

Appendix C.2 (Figure 5) validates these findings on a 0.7B model. The VO+FFN hybrid again nearly matches full Muon, while QK-only Muon provides minimal benefit. This confirms the component-level insights are not artifacts of model scale.

#### Assessment: Do These Results Support the Claims?

**Yes, convincingly.** The ablation design is rigorous:
- **Controls for parameter count**: QK and VO have identical parameter sizes, yet VO shows 4–5× larger improvement.
- **Tests both FFN variants**: Gated and non-gated architectures show consistent patterns.
- **Validates at two scales**: 160M and 0.7B models agree.
- **Rules out confounding factors**: Appendix C.1 shows MaxLogit values remain stable (5.6–8.4 across layers), ruling out attention instability as an explanation for QK's underperformance.

One limitation: the paper notes the small remaining gap between VO+FFN hybrid and full Muon "may arise because VO+FFN uses the same learning rate as full Muon without further tuning." This is a minor caveat—retuning could potentially close the gap entirely.

### 5.3 Spectral Analysis: Muon Produces More Isotropic Weights

Figure 2 presents spectral dynamics for VO and Wout matrices during 10,000 training steps, averaged over 10 random seeds. The results support Observation 2:

> "Muon consistently yields more isotropic weight matrices with broadly distributed spectral energy than Adam, both throughout training and across random initializations."

#### Quantitative Spectral Comparisons

While exact numerical values are not tabulated, Figure 2 shows clear patterns:

**SVD Entropy (higher = more isotropic):**
- Muon: Stable around 0.7–0.8 for VO, 0.8–0.9 for Wout
- Adam: Fluctuates between 0.4–0.7 for VO, 0.6–0.8 for Wout
- **Error bars**: Muon shows negligible variance across seeds; Adam shows substantial variance

**Effective Rank (higher = more dimensions used):**
- Muon: Reaches 300–400 for VO, 400–600 for Wout
- Adam: Reaches 100–200 for VO, 200–400 for Wout
- Muon's eRank is **2–3× higher** than Adam's by step 10,000

**Top-10 Energy Fraction (lower = less concentration):**
- Muon: Drops to ~0.2–0.3 by step 10,000
- Adam: Remains at ~0.5–0.7
- Adam concentrates **2–3× more energy** in top-10 singular components

**Q75/Q25 Ratio (lower = more uniform):**
- Muon: Stabilizes around 10–100 (log scale)
- Adam: Remains at 100–1000 (log scale)
- Adam's ratio is **10× higher**, indicating much less uniform spectral distribution

#### Stability Across Random Seeds

A critical finding is Adam's **sensitivity to initialization**. Figure 2 shows large error bars for Adam across 10 seeds, while Muon's error bars are negligible. This empirically validates the theoretical claim (Theorem 5.3) that Adam's performance depends on embedding structure, while Muon's does not.

#### Appendix C.3: Additional Spectral Results

Figure 8 extends spectral analysis to Win and Wgate matrices, showing consistent patterns: Muon produces higher SVD entropy and eRank, lower Top10E and Q75/Q25 ratios across all associative memory components.

#### Assessment: Do These Results Support the Claims?

**Yes, with strong empirical evidence.** The four-metric approach provides comprehensive characterization:
- **Convergent evidence**: All four metrics point in the same direction (Muon = more isotropic).
- **Temporal dynamics**: Isotropy differences appear from the start of training and persist throughout.
- **Seed stability**: Muon's consistency across seeds vs. Adam's variance supports the theoretical instability claim.

One limitation: The paper does not report statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values) for spectral metric differences. However, the effect sizes are large enough (2–3× differences in eRank, 10× in Q75/Q25) that statistical significance is virtually certain.

### 5.5 Theoretical Validation: One-Layer Model Results

Section 5 complements empirical results with theoretical analysis on a one-layer associative memory model. Figure 4 presents empirical validation of the theoretical setup.

#### Embedding Orthogonality Verification (Figure 4a)

The paper verifies Assumption 5.1 (orthonormal embeddings) on Llama3-8b-instruct by computing average angles between embedding vectors $E_i$ and $\tilde{E}_i$ in FFN layers 5, 10, 15, 20, 25 for 3,000 Counterfact knowledge items. Results show angles **near 90 degrees** across all layers, confirming approximate orthogonality in real models.

#### One-Step and Multi-Step Optimization (Figures 4b–4c)

Figures 4b–4c plot population loss $L(W)$ against maximal probability gap $\Delta(W)$ for GD, SignGD (Adam), and Muon under coupled and decoupled embeddings:
- **GD**: Shows substantial imbalance (large $\Delta(W)$) regardless of embedding type
- **SignGD**: Unstable—balanced for decoupled embeddings, imbalanced for coupled embeddings
- **Muon**: Consistently balanced ($\Delta(W)$ near 0) for both embedding types

This empirically validates Theorem 5.3's claim that Muon achieves embedding-independent balanced learning while Adam's performance depends on embedding structure.

#### Assessment: Do These Results Support the Claims?

**Yes, as theoretical grounding.** The one-layer model experiments:
- **Validate assumptions**: Embedding orthogonality holds in real models (Figure 4a).
- **Confirm theoretical predictions**: Muon's balance is embedding-independent; Adam's is not (Figures 4b–4c).
- **Bridge theory and practice**: The simplified model captures the same patterns observed in full transformers (Figures 2–3).

Limitation: The theoretical model is highly abstract (one-layer, controlled imbalance parameters $\alpha = 0.8, \beta = 0.2$). However, the empirical correspondence with full transformer results suggests the abstraction captures essential dynamics.

#### Robustness Checks

The paper includes several robustness validations:
1. **Model scale**: 160M and 0.7B models show consistent patterns (Appendix C.2).
2. **FFN architecture**: Gated and non-gated variants agree (Figures 1, 5; Tables 1, 6–8).

The paper acknowledges several limitations:
1. **VO+FFN gap**: The small remaining gap between VO+FFN hybrid and full Muon (0.02 loss difference) is not fully explained, though the paper suggests learning rate retuning could close it.
2. **Synthetic dataset**: The heavy-tailed QA task is synthetic; natural language heavy-tailed distributions may have additional complexities.

#### Mixed or Conditional Results

No major mixed results are reported—all experiments consistently support the central claims. The only conditional finding is Adam's embedding-dependent performance (Theorem 5.3), which is itself a key claim: Adam works well with disjoint-support embeddings but fails with overlapping supports.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper provides a compelling mechanistic explanation for Muon's superiority, a critical understanding requires acknowledging where the evidence ends and where practical constraints arise. The following analysis highlights the assumptions underpinning the theoretical claims, the scenarios not covered by the experiments, and the computational trade-offs inherent in adopting Muon.

### 6.1 Theoretical Assumptions and Simplifications

The theoretical guarantees in Section 5 rely on specific abstractions that, while empirically motivated, simplify the complexity of full-scale LLM training.

*   **Orthonormal Embedding Assumption.** Theorem 5.3 and Theorem 5.4 assume that the key and value embeddings ($E$ and $\tilde{E}$) are orthonormal (Assumption 5.1). The paper validates this empirically on Llama3-8b-instruct, showing average angles between embeddings are near $90^\circ$ (Figure 4a). However, this is an approximation. In early training or for highly correlated concepts, embeddings may not be orthogonal. The theory states techniques can apply without this requirement, but the explicit bounds rely on it. If embeddings are highly collinear, the "balanced learning" guarantee for Muon might weaken, though empirical results suggest robustness.
*   **Two-Class Imbalance Model.** The theoretical analysis models data imbalance as a binary split: head classes with total mass $\alpha$ and tail classes with mass $1-\alpha$ (Assumption 5.2). Real-world heavy-tailed distributions (like the synthetic QA task in Section 4.3) follow a power law across many groups (16 groups in Figure 3a). The paper argues the multi-class case follows directly from the proof, but the explicit convergence rates $\varrho_{\text{opt}}^\epsilon$ are derived for the two-class setting. Complex multi-modal distributions might introduce interactions not captured by the binary split.
*   **One-Layer Abstraction.** The theoretical model abstracts the transformer to a single linear associative memory layer (Section 5). Real LLMs involve deep stacking of attention and FFN blocks (e.g., 12 layers in 160M NanoGPT, 32+ in Llama3). The theory does not account for gradient flow through deep networks, layer normalization interactions, or residual connections. While the empirical results on deep models match the theory, the mathematical proof does not strictly cover deep architecture dynamics.
*   **Momentum Discrepancy.** For theoretical tractability, the analysis disables momentum for both Adam (reducing it to SignGD) and Muon (Section 5). However, all empirical experiments use momentum ($\mu = 0.95$ for Muon, $\beta_1 = 0.8\text{--}0.9$ for Adam, Appendix B.1). While momentum is standard for convergence, the theoretical guarantees technically apply to the non-momentum variants. If momentum interacts differently with spectral normalization than with element-wise normalization, the theoretical bounds might not perfectly predict empirical behavior.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

The paper's scope is deliberately focused, leaving several important architectural and data regimes unexplored.

*   **Dense Architectures vs. Mixture-of-Experts (MoE).** The experimental analysis focuses on dense transformer architectures (NanoGPT). Section 4.2 explicitly contrasts this with Liu et al. (2025), which centers on MoE models. MoE introduces routing dynamics where only a subset of parameters is active per token. It remains an open question whether Muon's benefits on associative memory parameters hold when those parameters are sparsely activated. The gradient statistics in MoE might differ significantly from dense models, potentially altering the spectral properties Muon relies on.
*   **Natural Language Tail Facts.** While the pretraining uses real web text (FineWeb), the evaluation of tail-class performance uses a **synthetic biographical QA dataset** (Section 4.3, Appendix B.2). This allows controlled power-law distributions (196,608 samples for head vs. 6 for tail). The paper does not report performance on natural language "tail facts" (e.g., rare entities in FineWeb or specific knowledge benchmarks like TruthfulQA). It is possible that natural language tails have different semantic structures than the synthetic biographical facts, which could affect how well associative memory mechanisms generalize.
*   **Downstream Task Performance.** The evaluation metrics are validation loss (Figure 1) and synthetic QA accuracy (Figure 3). The paper does not report standard downstream benchmarks (e.g., MMLU, GSM8K, or human preference ratings). Improved tail-class learning in pretraining does not automatically guarantee better fine-tuning performance or reasoning capabilities. The isotropic weights might benefit knowledge retrieval but could theoretically impact other features needed for complex reasoning.
*   **Token Embeddings and LM Head.** The paper notes that in standard Muon implementations, token embeddings and the language model head are optimized with Adam, not Muon (Section 4.1). This is treated as a given constraint. However, these components also participate in the memory retrieval process (the head projects memory to logits). Excluding them from Muon optimization might leave performance gains on the table, or conversely, applying Muon there might cause instability not addressed in this work.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

Adopting Muon involves trade-offs in computational cost and implementation complexity that are not fully quantified in the experimental section.

*   **Per-Step Computational Overhead.** The Introduction cites prior work stating Muon is "nearly 2 times faster" than Adam, but this refers to **steps-to-convergence**, not wall-clock time per step. Muon requires computing a nearest orthogonal matrix $O_t$ via SVD or Newton-Schulz iterations (Section 3). While Newton-Schulz avoids full SVD, it still adds matrix operations compared to Adam's element-wise divisions. The paper does not provide a breakdown of wall-clock training time per step for the 160M or 0.7B models. For very large matrices (e.g., in 70B models), the cost of approximating $O_t$ could become a bottleneck, potentially offsetting the convergence speedup.
*   **Scaling Beyond 0.7B.** The largest model evaluated is 0.7B NanoGPT (Appendix C.2). Modern LLMs scale to 7B, 70B, and beyond. Spectral properties can shift with scale. For instance, the rank of gradient matrices might scale differently than the dimension, affecting the efficiency of Newton-Schulz iterations. The paper acknowledges this implicitly by stopping at 0.7B, leaving the behavior at multi-billion parameter scales as an open empirical question.
*   **Memory Footprint.** Muon maintains a momentum accumulator $B_t$ and requires temporary storage for SVD factors ($U_t, V_t$) or Newton-Schulz iterates. While likely comparable to Adam's state (which also stores momentum and variance), the intermediate matrices for orthogonalization could increase peak memory usage. In memory-constrained training regimes (e.g., large batch sizes on limited GPUs), this overhead might necessitate smaller batch sizes, indirectly affecting convergence.

### 6.4 Open Questions and Weaknesses

Several observations in the paper point to unresolved mechanisms or minor inconsistencies that warrant further investigation.

*   **The VO+FFN Performance Gap.** In Table 1, the hybrid configuration `Muon(VO Attn, FFN) & Adam(QK Attn)` achieves validation loss of 3.5858, while Full Muon achieves 3.5654 (non-gated). There is a small but consistent gap (~0.02 loss). The paper suggests this "may arise because VO+FFN uses the same learning rate as full Muon without further tuning" (Section 4.1). However, this is not experimentally verified. It remains unclear if perfect recovery is possible with hyperparameter tuning or if QK parameters contribute a small but non-zero benefit that cannot be replicated by VO+FFN alone.
*   **Why QK Parameters Do Not Benefit.** The paper robustly shows that Query-Key (QK) projections do not benefit from Muon (Figure 1, Table 1). The explanation offered is that QK weights are not part of the linear associative memory mechanism (Section 4.2). However, QK matrices still have spectral structures. The paper does not explain *why* spectral normalization is neutral or slightly negative for QK weights. Do QK weights benefit from the anisotropy that Adam preserves? Understanding this could refine optimizer design further (e.g., automatically detecting which layers need spectral vs. element-wise normalization).
*   **Spectral Decay in Adam.** Theorem 5.3 shows Adam can induce spectral decay where $\sigma_{\min} / \sigma_{\max} \leq 25\%$ under certain embeddings. While this explains Adam's instability, the paper does not explore whether this decay is always harmful. In some optimization landscapes, concentrating energy in dominant directions (anisotropy) might accelerate learning on head classes even if it hurts tail classes. The paper prioritizes balanced learning, but for tasks where head class performance is paramount, Adam's behavior might sometimes be preferable—a trade-off not fully discussed.
*   **Generalization to Higher-Order Structures.** The Conclusion suggests Muon's alignment with outer products might extend to "higher-order tensor products." This is speculative. Modern architectures sometimes use multi-linear interactions beyond matrix multiplication. Whether Muon's matrix-centric update rule generalizes to tensor-valued parameters without modification remains an open theoretical question.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

| Aspect | Muon Advantage | Trade-off / Limitation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Convergence** | Faster steps-to-convergence on heavy-tailed data (Abstract) | Per-step wall-clock cost not quantified; SVD approx overhead exists. |
| **Tail Learning** | 3.7× improvement on extreme tail classes (Table 5) | Validated on synthetic QA; natural language tail facts not tested. |
| **Stability** | Isotropic weights stable across seeds (Figure 2) | Theory assumes orthonormal embeddings; real embeddings are approximate. |
| **Architecture** | Works on dense transformers (Section 4) | MoE architectures not evaluated; QK parameters do not benefit. |
| **Scale** | Consistent up to 0.7B (Appendix C.2) | Not validated on multi-billion parameter models (7B+). |

In summary, while the paper provides strong evidence for Muon's mechanism in dense, knowledge-intensive settings, practitioners should weigh the unquantified computational overhead and the lack of validation on large-scale MoE models or natural language tail benchmarks before widespread adoption. The theoretical guarantees are robust within their assumptions but rely on abstractions that simplify deep network dynamics.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper's mechanistic explanation of Muon's superiority extends beyond academic understanding—it reshapes how practitioners should think about optimizer selection, model architecture design, and training strategies for knowledge-intensive applications. The following analysis outlines how this work changes the optimization landscape, what research directions it enables, and concrete guidance for practitioners considering Muon adoption.

### 7.1 Shifting the Optimizer Design Paradigm

**From Model-Wide to Component-Specific Optimization.** Prior to this work, optimizer selection was treated as a model-wide decision: practitioners chose Adam, Muon, or another optimizer and applied it uniformly across all transformer parameters. This paper's central finding—that **VO+FFN components recover 94–98% of Muon's advantage** while QK parameters contribute negligibly (Table 1, Section 5.2)—challenges this paradigm fundamentally.

The implication is that **hybrid optimization strategies** may outperform uniform optimizer application. A practitioner could apply Muon selectively to associative memory components (Value, Output, and FFN matrices) while keeping Query-Key projections and embeddings on Adam. This approach would capture most of Muon's tail-class learning benefits while potentially reducing computational overhead from SVD or Newton-Schulz iterations on parameters that do not benefit from spectral normalization.

This component-specific view also suggests that **future optimizer design should be architecture-aware**. Rather than developing universally superior optimizers, researchers might design optimizers tailored to specific parameter types: spectral-normalized updates for associative memory components, element-wise adaptive methods for attention routing parameters, and potentially different strategies for embeddings and language model heads.

**From Loss-Based to Structure-Based Evaluation.** Traditional optimizer comparison focuses on validation loss convergence curves. This paper introduces **spectral isotropy metrics** (SVD entropy, effective rank, top-k energy fraction, eigenvalue quantile ratio) as mechanistic diagnostics that reveal *how* optimizers shape weight matrices, not just *whether* they converge (Section 4.2, Figure 2).

The landscape shift here is toward **interpretable optimizer evaluation**. Practitioners can now monitor spectral metrics during training to detect whether an optimizer is producing diverse feature representations or collapsing energy into dominant directions. Early signs of spectral decay (rapidly increasing top-k energy fraction, decreasing effective rank) could prompt optimizer switching or hyperparameter adjustment before validation loss deteriorates.

**From Convergence to Balanced Learning as a Criterion.** Theoretical analysis in Section 5 introduces **learning balance** ($\varrho_{\text{opt}}^\epsilon$) as a convergence criterion distinct from traditional loss-based metrics. Theorem 5.3 proves that Adam can achieve low loss while maintaining large probability gaps between head and tail classes ($\varrho_{\text{SignGD}}^\epsilon = O(\epsilon^{-0.7} K^{-0.3})$ under overlapping embeddings), whereas Muon achieves balanced learning ($\varrho_{\text{Muon}}^\epsilon \geq 1 - \epsilon(1 + O(\frac{\log K}{K}))$) regardless of embedding structure.

This reframes optimizer evaluation for **imbalanced data scenarios**. For applications where tail classes matter (rare diseases in medical text, edge cases in legal documents, specialized technical terminology), balanced learning metrics may be more relevant than aggregate loss. Future optimizer benchmarks should report per-class or per-group performance distributions, not just average accuracy.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

The paper's findings open several concrete research avenues that build directly on its mechanistic insights:

**1. Hybrid Optimizer Implementations.** The VO+FFN hybrid configuration (Muon on associative memory components, Adam on QK and embeddings) recovers nearly full Muon performance with potentially reduced computational cost (Section 5.2, Tables 3–8). Future work should:
- Implement and benchmark hybrid optimizers in standard frameworks (PyTorch, JAX)
- Quantify wall-clock time per step for hybrid vs. full Muon vs. Adam at various scales
- Determine whether learning rate retuning can close the remaining 0.02 loss gap between hybrid and full Muon (Section 4.1 notes this as unverified)

**2. Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) Extension.** Section 4.2 explicitly contrasts this work with Liu et al. (2025), which focuses on MoE models. This paper's dense-architecture findings raise open questions:
- Do Muon's benefits persist when associative memory parameters are sparsely activated via routing?
- How does gradient sparsity in MoE affect spectral properties that Muon relies on?
- Should Muon be applied only to active experts, or to all expert parameters regardless of activation?

**3. Scale Beyond 0.7B.** The largest model evaluated is 0.7B NanoGPT (Appendix C.2). Modern LLMs scale to 7B, 70B, and beyond. Research should investigate:
- Whether spectral isotropy gaps between Muon and Adam persist at multi-billion parameter scales
- Whether Newton-Schulz iteration costs become prohibitive for very large matrices
- Whether the VO+FFN concentration of benefits holds when parameter counts grow non-uniformly across components

**4. Natural Language Tail Facts.** The heavy-tailed evaluation uses synthetic biographical QA (Section 4.3, Appendix B.2). Follow-up work should validate findings on:
- Natural language knowledge benchmarks (e.g., TruthfulQA, PopQA, EntityQuestion)
- Rare entity recognition in pretraining corpora
- Long-tail factual consistency in generated text

**5. Downstream Task Transfer.** This paper evaluates pretraining loss and synthetic QA accuracy. Research should examine whether Muon's isotropic weights improve:
- Fine-tuning efficiency on downstream tasks
- Knowledge editing capabilities (since VO and FFN are identified as knowledge stores)
- Robustness to distribution shift in deployment

**6. Higher-Order Tensor Generalization.** The Conclusion speculates that Muon's alignment with outer products "may extend beyond outer products to higher-order tensor products." This suggests:
- Analyzing Muon on architectures with multi-linear interactions (e.g., tensor attention, hypernetworks)
- Developing tensor-normalized optimizers that generalize Muon's spectral approach to higher-order parameters
- Proving whether balanced learning guarantees extend to tensor-valued associative memories

**7. Automatic Component Detection.** The paper manually identifies VO+FFN as associative memory components. Future work could:
- Develop automated methods to detect which parameters function as associative memories in arbitrary architectures
- Create optimizer selection policies that adaptively apply spectral normalization based on detected parameter roles
- Investigate whether associative memory roles shift during training (e.g., do QK parameters become more memory-like in later layers?)

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Deployment Guidance

Based on this paper's findings, practitioners can make informed decisions about when and how to adopt Muon:

**When to Prefer Muon Over Adam:**

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| **Knowledge-intensive pretraining** | Use Muon on VO+FFN | Section 5.4 shows 3.7× improvement on extreme tail classes (Group 15: 0.976 vs 0.264 FTA at 10k steps) |
| **Heavy-tailed data distributions** | Use Muon (full or hybrid) | Theorem 5.3 proves Muon achieves embedding-independent balanced learning; Adam is unstable |
| **Stability-critical training** | Use Muon | Figure 2 shows Muon's spectral metrics are stable across 10 seeds; Adam fluctuates significantly |
| **Resource-constrained settings** | Use Adam or Muon(QK) only | Muon's SVD/Newton-Schulz overhead may not justify benefits if tail classes are unimportant |
| **MoE architectures** | Unclear—await further research | Section 4.2 notes this paper focuses on dense architectures; MoE dynamics may differ |
| **Head-class-only tasks** | Adam may suffice | Figure 3 shows Adam matches Muon on head classes; tail-class advantage is Muon's differentiator |

**Integration Checklist for Practitioners:**

1. **Identify Associative Memory Components.** In your transformer architecture, locate Value ($W_V$), Output ($W_O$), and FFN ($W_{\text{in}}$, $W_{\text{out}}$, $W_{\text{gate}}$) matrices. These are the primary targets for Muon optimization (Section 3, Section 4.1).

2. **Consider Hybrid Configuration.** Apply Muon to VO+FFN while keeping Query-Key ($W_Q$, $W_K$), token embeddings, and language model head on Adam. This recovers 94–98% of full Muon's advantage with potentially reduced overhead (Table 1, Tables 3–8).

3. **Monitor Spectral Metrics.** During training, track effective rank and top-k energy fraction on VO and Wout matrices. If these metrics show rapid decay (energy concentrating in top singular components), consider switching to Muon for those components (Section 4.2, Figure 2).

4. **Tune Learning Rates Separately.** The paper notes the VO+FFN hybrid uses the same learning rate as full Muon without tuning (Section 4.1). For optimal hybrid performance, consider separate learning rate search for Muon-applied components.

5. **Validate on Tail Classes.** If your application involves rare but important cases, evaluate optimizer performance stratified by class frequency—not just aggregate accuracy. Tables 3–8 provide a template for per-group evaluation.

6. **Verify Embedding Orthogonality.** The theoretical guarantees assume orthonormal embeddings (Assumption 5.1). While Figure 4a shows this holds approximately in Llama3-8b-instruct, verify embedding angles in your model if theoretical guarantees are critical.

**Expected Trade-offs:**

- **Convergence Speed:** Muon achieves faster steps-to-convergence on heavy-tailed tasks (Abstract cites "nearly 2 times faster" from prior work), but per-step wall-clock time may be higher due to SVD approximation.
- **Memory Overhead:** Muon maintains momentum accumulator $B_t$ and requires temporary storage for SVD factors. This is likely comparable to Adam's state but should be verified for your specific implementation.
- **Implementation Complexity:** Muon requires SVD or Newton-Schulz iterations, which are not natively supported in all optimizer libraries. Hybrid configurations add further complexity in parameter group management.

### 7.4 Open Challenges for the Community

Several unresolved questions from this paper represent opportunities for community contribution:

**1. Quantifying Computational Overhead.** The paper does not report wall-clock time per training step for Muon vs. Adam (Section 6.3 notes this limitation). The community should benchmark:
- Newton-Schulz iteration cost vs. full SVD at various matrix sizes
- Hybrid optimizer overhead vs. full Muon vs. Adam
- Scaling behavior from 160M to 70B+ parameter models

**2. Natural Language Tail Benchmarks.** The synthetic QA dataset enables controlled evaluation but may not capture natural language complexity. Community efforts should:
- Curate benchmarks with verified heavy-tailed entity distributions
- Establish standard metrics for tail-class performance reporting
- Compare optimizer effects on factual consistency in generated text

**3. MoE and Sparse Architecture Validation.** As MoE models become standard for large-scale training, understanding Muon's behavior in sparse settings is critical:
- Does gradient sparsity alter spectral properties that Muon relies on?
- Should Muon be applied to all experts or only activated experts?
- How does routing gradient interact with spectral normalization?

**4. Theoretical Extensions to Deep Networks.** The one-layer theoretical model (Section 5) abstracts away deep architecture dynamics. Extending proofs to:
- Multi-layer transformers with residual connections
- Layer normalization interactions with spectral normalization
- Gradient flow through deep associative memory stacks

**5. Automated Optimizer Selection.** Building on the component-specific insight, develop:
- Tools that automatically detect associative memory parameters in arbitrary architectures
- Policies that adaptively switch optimizers based on spectral metric monitoring
- Frameworks that enable per-parameter-group optimizer assignment without manual configuration

**6. Reproducibility Across Frameworks.** This paper uses NanoGPT implementations. Community validation should:
- Reproduce findings in standard frameworks (Hugging Face Transformers, JAX/Flax, Megatron-LM)
- Verify results across different hardware (TPU, alternative GPU architectures)
- Test with different tokenization schemes and vocabulary sizes

---

**Summary:** This paper transforms Muon from an empirically superior but poorly understood optimizer into a mechanistically grounded tool whose advantages are predictable, targetable, and theoretically justified. The key takeaway for practitioners is that **optimizer choice should be component-aware, not model-wide**, and that **tail-class performance requires explicit evaluation, not just aggregate metrics**. For researchers, the paper provides a foundation for architecture-aware optimizer design, spectral diagnostics for training monitoring, and theoretical frameworks for analyzing learning balance in imbalanced data scenarios. The community's next steps should focus on scaling validation, MoE extension, natural language benchmark development, and automated tooling for hybrid optimizer deployment.
