## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the limitations of standard VAE-based latent spaces in Diffusion Transformers (DiT) by introducing Representation Autoencoders (RAEs), which pair frozen pretrained representation encoders (e.g., DINOv2) with trained decoders to create semantically rich, high-dimensional latent spaces. By adapting DiT architecture (DiTDH) and training strategies (dimension-dependent noise scheduling, noise-augmented decoding), the authors achieve state-of-the-art image generation on ImageNet, reaching 1.51 FID at 256×256 without guidance and 1.13 FID at both 256×256 and 512×512 with guidance. This approach enables significantly faster convergence (47× faster than SiT-XL) and demonstrates that high-dimensional semantic representations are advantageous rather than detrimental for diffusion modeling, proposing RAE as the new default for latent diffusion training.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Latent Diffusion Paradigm and Its Bottleneck

To understand this paper's contribution, we must first establish how modern image generation works. **Latent diffusion models** operate in two stages:

1. **Compression Stage**: An autoencoder (typically a VAE) maps high-dimensional pixel images into a lower-dimensional latent space
2. **Generation Stage**: A diffusion model learns to generate samples within this latent space, which are then decoded back to pixels

This approach became standard after **Latent Diffusion Models (LDM)** (Rombach et al., 2022) demonstrated that diffusing in latent space rather than pixel space achieves higher visual fidelity and computational efficiency. The widely adopted **SD-VAE** from this work remains the default choice for Diffusion Transformers (DiT) even years later.

However, this creates a critical bottleneck: **the autoencoder component has barely evolved** while diffusion backbones have advanced significantly. The paper identifies three fundamental limitations of SD-VAE:

| Limitation | Consequence |
|------------|-------------|
| **Outdated convolutional backbones** | Compromises architectural simplicity; SD-VAE requires ~6× more encoder GFLOPs than RAE (Figure 2) |
| **Low-dimensional latent spaces** | SD-VAE compresses 256×256 images to 32×32×4 latents, restricting information capacity |
| **Reconstruction-only training** | Produces weak representations lacking global semantic structure, limiting generative generalization |

### The Separation Between Representation Learning and Generation

While latent diffusion stagnated, **visual representation learning underwent rapid transformation**. Self-supervised and multimodal encoders such as **DINOv2** (self-distillation), **MAE** (masked autoencoding), and **SigLIP** (language-supervised) now learn semantically structured visual features that generalize across tasks and scales. These encoders achieve remarkable performance on downstream tasks—Table 1d shows DINOv2-B reaches **84.5% linear probing accuracy** on ImageNet-1K, compared to SD-VAE's mere **8.0%**.

Despite this progress, **latent diffusion remains isolated from representation learning advances**. The field continues diffusing in reconstruction-trained VAE spaces rather than semantically meaningful representational ones. This separation stems from two long-standing assumptions that this paper directly challenges:

1. **"Semantic encoders cannot reconstruct faithfully"**: It is widely believed that encoders trained for semantics "focus on high-level information and can only reconstruct an image with high-level semantic similarities" (Yu et al., 2024b), sacrificing pixel-level detail.

2. **"Diffusion fails in high-dimensional spaces"**: Practitioners assume diffusion models perform poorly in high-dimensional latent spaces (Skorokhodov et al., 2025; Yao et al., 2025), leading to preference for low-dimensional VAE latents over the typically much higher-dimensional representations of semantic encoders (e.g., DINOv2-B produces 768-dimensional tokens vs. SD-VAE's 4-dimensional latents).

### Prior Approaches and Their Shortcomings

Recent work has attempted to bridge this gap, but each approach introduces significant trade-offs:

**Representation-Enhanced VAEs**: Methods like **VA-VAE** (Yao et al., 2025), **MAETok** (Chen et al., 2025a), and **DC-AE 1.5** (Chen et al., 2025d) incorporate semantic objectives into VAE training. However, they still rely on **heavily compressed, low-dimensional latents**, limiting both reconstruction fidelity and representation quality.

**Representation Alignment for Generation**: Methods like **REPA** (Yu et al., 2025), **DDT** (Wang et al., 2025c), **REG** (Wu et al., 2025), and **ReDi** (Kouzelis et al., 2025b) attempt to improve latent quality indirectly through alignment with external encoders. These approaches introduce:
- Extra training stages
- Auxiliary representation alignment losses
- Additional tuning complexity

The paper notes that REPA-style methods accelerate convergence but require **additional loss terms and architectural modifications** to align diffusion model features with representation encoder features during training.

### This Paper's Positioning

This work takes a fundamentally different approach: **instead of modifying VAEs or adding alignment losses, replace the VAE entirely** with a **Representation Autoencoder (RAE)** that:

- Uses a **frozen pretrained representation encoder** (no training needed)
- Pairs it with a **lightweight trained decoder** (ViT-based)
- Operates in **high-dimensional latent spaces without compression**

This positioning is significant for several reasons:

1. **Simplicity**: No auxiliary losses, no alignment objectives, no encoder training—just freeze and decode
2. **Efficiency**: RAE encoder requires ~6× fewer GFLOPs than SD-VAE; decoder requires ~3× fewer (Figure 2)
3. **Quality**: RAEs achieve better reconstruction (rFID 0.49 vs. 0.62 for SD-VAE with DINOv2-B encoder, Table 1a) while preserving strong semantic representations
4. **Compatibility**: Token count remains fixed (256 tokens for 256×256 images), so DiT computational cost is unchanged despite higher channel dimensionality

The paper's central claim is that **autoencoding should be reframed from a compression mechanism into a representation foundation**. By demonstrating that frozen semantic encoders can serve as effective diffusion latent spaces with proper architectural adjustments, this work challenges the field to reconsider what properties make a latent space suitable for generation—not just compressibility, but semantic richness and structural coherence.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

This paper builds a two-stage image generation system where a frozen semantic encoder (like DINOv2) compresses images into high-dimensional latent tokens, a diffusion transformer learns to generate within that space, and a trained decoder reconstructs pixels from the generated latents. The system solves the problem that standard diffusion transformers fail when operating in high-dimensional semantic latent spaces by introducing three architectural fixes: matching model width to token dimensionality, adjusting noise schedules for dimensionality, and training the decoder to handle noisy diffusion outputs.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The complete generation pipeline consists of four major components connected in sequence. First, the **Representation Encoder** (frozen, pretrained, e.g., DINOv2-B) takes an input image and produces high-dimensional latent tokens without any compression—this component is never updated during training. Second, the **Representation Decoder** (trained ViT) learns to reconstruct pixels from these latent tokens and is trained with reconstruction, perceptual, and adversarial losses before diffusion training begins. Third, the **Diffusion Transformer Backbone** (DiT-XL or DiTDH-XL) operates within the latent space, learning to denoise corrupted latent tokens across timesteps using flow matching. Fourth, the **DDT Head** (wide, shallow transformer module) sits atop the DiT backbone and provides additional width for processing high-dimensional latents without quadratic compute growth. During generation, noise is sampled, the DiT+DDT head denoises it through the latent space, and the decoder produces the final image.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first explain how RAEs are constructed from frozen encoders and trained decoders, including the loss functions and architecture choices that enable high-fidelity reconstruction without encoder training.
- We then analyze why standard DiT architectures fail on RAE latents, presenting the theoretical justification for why model width must match token dimensionality (Theorem 1).
- We describe the dimension-dependent noise schedule shift, explaining why resolution-based scheduling from prior work is insufficient for high-dimensional semantic tokens.
- We cover noise-augmented decoder training, showing how smoothing the latent distribution helps the decoder generalize to imperfect diffusion outputs.
- We detail the DiTDH architecture with its wide DDT head, explaining how it provides width efficiently without quadratic FLOP growth.
- We conclude with training configurations, hyperparameters, and the complete optimization recipe used to achieve state-of-the-art results.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a systems architecture paper that proposes replacing the VAE component in latent diffusion with a Representation Autoencoder (RAE) and adapting the diffusion transformer to operate effectively in the resulting high-dimensional latent space.

**The RAE Construction Pipeline**

The Representation Autoencoder is built by pairing a frozen pretrained representation encoder with a newly trained decoder, creating a latent space that preserves semantic structure while enabling faithful reconstruction. Given an input image $x \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times H \times W}$ and a frozen representation encoder $E$ with patch size $p_e$ and hidden dimension $d$, the encoder produces $N = \frac{HW}{p_e^2}$ tokens, each with channel dimension $d$. For all 256×256 image experiments, the encoder produces exactly 256 tokens, matching the token count of prior DiT-based models trained with SD-VAE latents (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Yu et al., 2025). A ViT decoder $D$ with patch size $p_d$ maps these tokens back to pixel space with shape $3 \times \frac{H p_d}{p_e} \times \frac{W p_d}{p_e}$; by default, $p_d = p_e$ so reconstruction matches input resolution. The decoder is trained with a composite loss function combining three objectives:

$$L_{\text{rec}}(x) = \omega_L \cdot \text{LPIPS}(\hat{x}, x) + L_1(\hat{x}, x) + \omega_G \cdot \lambda \cdot \text{GAN}(\hat{x}, x)$$

where $\hat{x} = D(E(x))$ is the reconstructed image, $\omega_L = 1.0$ weights the perceptual loss, $\omega_G = 0.75$ weights the adversarial loss, and $\lambda$ is an adaptive weight balancing reconstruction and GAN losses (computed as the ratio of gradient norms following Esser et al., 2021). The discriminator uses a frozen DINO-S/8 architecture (not DINO-S/16 as in StyleGAN-T) because the authors found this stabilizes training and prevents the decoder from generating adversarial patches. Training proceeds for 16 epochs on the decoder and 10 epochs on the discriminator, with the discriminator starting at epoch 6 and adversarial loss beginning at epoch 8. Table 1a shows that RAEs with frozen encoders consistently outperform SD-VAE in reconstruction quality: DINOv2-B achieves rFID 0.49, SigLIP2-B achieves 0.53, and MAE-B achieves 0.16, all better than SD-VAE's 0.62. Critically, Table 1d demonstrates that RAEs inherit the semantic representation quality of their underlying encoders—DINOv2-B achieves 84.5% linear probing accuracy on ImageNet-1K compared to SD-VAE's mere 8.0%—because the encoder remains frozen and unmodified.

**Why Standard DiT Fails on RAE Latents: The Width Bottleneck**

When the authors first attempted to train standard DiT models on RAE latents, they discovered that the standard diffusion recipe fails catastrophically (Table 2). A DiT-S model completely fails to learn, while a DiT-XL significantly underperforms its counterpart trained on SD-VAE latents (23.08 gFID vs. 7.13 gFID). To understand this failure, the authors constructed a simplified overfitting experiment: rather than training on ImageNet, they selected a single image, encoded it with RAE, and tested whether the diffusion model could reconstruct it. Figure 3 reveals a sharp threshold: when model width $d$ is less than token dimension $n = 768$, the model cannot overfit even a single sample, but once $d \geq n$, training loss converges and the model reproduces the input almost perfectly. Increasing model depth while keeping width fixed does not help—doubling depth from 12 to 24 layers with width 384 still fails to converge. This observation contradicts the common belief that data manifolds have low intrinsic dimensionality, allowing generative models to operate effectively without scaling to full data dimension. The authors provide a theoretical explanation in Theorem 1, which establishes a lower bound on training loss for diffusion models operating in high-dimensional spaces.

**Theorem 1 (Lower Bound for Training Loss)**: Assuming $x \sim p(x) \in \mathbb{R}^n$, $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_n)$, $t \in [0, 1]$, and $x_t = (1-t)x + t\varepsilon$, consider the function family:

$$\mathcal{G}_d = \{g(x_t, t) = B f(A x_t, t) : A \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times n}, B \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}, f: [0, 1] \times \mathbb{R}^d \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^d\}$$

where $d < n$, $f$ refers to a stack of standard DiT blocks whose width is smaller than the token dimension from the representation encoder, and $A, B$ denote input and output linear projections. Then for any $g \in \mathcal{G}_d$:

$$\mathcal{L}(g, \theta) = \int_0^1 \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p(x), \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_n)} \left[ \|g(x_t, t) - (\varepsilon - x)\|^2 \right] dt \geq \sum_{i=d+1}^n \lambda_i$$

where $\lambda_i$ are the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix of the random variable $W = \varepsilon - x$. Notably, when $d \geq n$, $\mathcal{G}_d$ contains the unique minimizer to $\mathcal{L}(g, \theta)$.

In plain language, this theorem states that if your diffusion model's internal width $d$ is smaller than the latent token dimension $n$, there is an irreducible lower bound on training loss determined by the eigenvalues of the noise-data covariance. The proof (Section B.1) shows that the image of any function in $\mathcal{G}_d$ has dimension at most $d$, so it cannot perfectly represent targets in $\mathbb{R}^n$ when $d < n$. In the toy setting where $p(x) = \delta(x - x_0)$ (a single image), we have $W \sim \mathcal{N}(-x, I_n)$ and $\lambda_i = 1$ for all $i$, so the lower bound becomes $\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(g, \theta) \geq \frac{n-d}{n}$. Figure 3's empirical results match this theoretical bound exactly. Table 3 extends this finding to practical settings: convergence occurs only when model width matches or exceeds token dimension (e.g., DiT-B with DINOv2-B works, but DiT-S with DINOv2-B fails). This insight forces a fundamental design change: for RAE latents with 768-dimensional tokens, the DiT backbone width must be at least 768.

**Dimension-Dependent Noise Schedule Shift**

Prior work (Teng et al., 2023; Chen, 2023; Hoogeboom et al., 2023; Esser et al., 2024) observed that for inputs $z \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times H \times W}$, increasing spatial resolution $(H \times W)$ reduces information corruption at the same noise level, impairing diffusion training. However, these findings were derived for pixel- or VAE-encoded inputs with few channels (e.g., $C \leq 16$). The authors argue that Gaussian noise is applied to both spatial and channel dimensions, so as the number of channels increases, the effective "resolution" per token also grows, further reducing information corruption. Therefore, resolution-dependent scheduling strategies should be generalized to the **effective data dimension**, defined as the number of tokens times their dimensionality. The authors adopt the shifting strategy from Esser et al. (2024): for a schedule $t_n \in [0, 1]$ and input dimensions $n, m$, the shifted timestep is:

$$t_m = \frac{\alpha t_n}{1 + (\alpha - 1) t_n}$$

where $\alpha = \sqrt{m/n}$ is a dimension-dependent scaling factor. They use $n = 4096$ as the base dimension (following Esser et al., 2024) and set $m$ to the effective data dimension of RAE. Table 4 shows this yields dramatic improvements: without the shift, gFID is 23.08; with the shift, gFID drops to 4.81. This 4.8× improvement demonstrates that proper noise scheduling is critical for training diffusion models in high-dimensional RAE latent spaces.

**Noise-Augmented Decoder Training**

A subtle but critical mismatch exists between how VAEs and RAEs handle latent distributions. VAEs encode latent tokens as a continuous distribution $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2 I)$ (Kingma & Welling, 2014), making the decoder tolerant to small variations in latent values. In contrast, RAE decoders are trained on discretely supported latents $p(z) = \sum_i \delta(x - z_i)$, where $\{z_i\}$ denotes the training set processed by the RAE encoder. At inference time, the diffusion model generates latents that may be noisy or deviate slightly from the training distribution due to imperfect training and sampling, creating an out-of-distribution challenge for the decoder. To address this, the authors augment RAE decoder training with additive noise $n \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)$. Rather than decoding from the clean latent distribution $p(z)$, they train the decoder $D$ on a smoothed distribution:

$$p_n(z) = \int p(z - n) \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)(n) \, dn$$

They further introduce stochasticity into $\sigma$ by sampling it from $|\mathcal{N}(0, \tau^2)|$, which helps regularize training and improve robustness. Table 5 shows the trade-off: noise augmentation improves gFID from 4.81 to 4.28 but slightly worsens rFID from 0.49 to 0.57. This is expected—smoothing the latent distribution helps the decoder generalize to imperfect diffusion outputs but removes fine details, lowering exact reconstruction quality. Table 15c shows that larger $\tau$ values consistently improve gFID (from 4.81 at $\tau=0.0$ to 4.20 at $\tau=1.0$) while progressively worsening rFID (from 0.49 to 0.60); the default $\tau = 0.8$ balances this trade-off. Table 15a confirms this benefit holds across different encoders: MAE-B improves from 16.14 to 8.38 gFID, SigLIP2-B from 6.69 to 4.93, and DINOv2-B from 4.81 to 4.28.

**The DiTDH Architecture: Wide Diffusion Head**

Even with the above fixes, scaling DiT width to match RAE token dimensionality creates a computational problem: within the standard DiT framework, handling higher-dimensional latents requires scaling up the width of the entire backbone, which quickly becomes expensive due to quadratic attention costs. To overcome this, the authors introduce **DiTDH** (DiT with DDT Head), inspired by DDT (Wang et al., 2025c) but motivated by a different design perspective. Formally, a DiTDH model consists of a base DiT $M$ and an additional wide, shallow transformer head $H$. Given noisy input $x_t$, timestep $t$, and optional class label $y$, the combined model predicts velocity $v_t$ as:

$$z_t = M(x_t \mid t, y)$$
$$v_t = H(x_t \mid z_t, t)$$

The DDT head is a shallow yet wide transformer module dedicated to denoising. By attaching this head to a standard DiT, the system effectively increases model width without incurring quadratic growth in FLOPs across the full depth. By default, all DiTDH models use a 2-layer, 2048-dim DDT head. Figure 6a shows DiTDH scales much better than standard DiT with RAE latents: DiTDH-B requires only ~40% of the training FLOPs yet outperforms DiT-XL by a large margin; when scaled to DiTDH-XL under comparable training budget, DiTDH achieves FID 2.16—nearly half that of DiT-XL. Table 6 shows DiTDH maintains its advantage across RAE encoder sizes: with DINOv2-S, DiTDH-XL achieves 2.42 gFID vs. DiT-XL's 3.50; with DINOv2-B, 2.16 vs. 4.28; with DINOv2-L, 2.73 vs. 6.09. The advantage grows with encoder size because larger encoders produce higher-dimensional latents, which amplify the width bottleneck of standard DiT. Table 16 confirms the design principle: a 2-layer, 2048-dim head outperforms both a 6-layer, 1152-dim (XL) head and a 4-layer, 2048-dim (G) head, demonstrating that wide and shallow is more effective than narrow and deep for the denoising task.

**Complete Training Recipe and Hyperparameters**

The full training pipeline combines all components with specific optimization settings. For the diffusion model, the authors use LightningDiT (Yao et al., 2025) as the backbone with flow matching objective and linear interpolation $x_t = (1-t)x + t\varepsilon$, where $x \sim p(x)$ and $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$. They adopt a patch size of 1 for RAE (vs. patch size 2 for VAE baselines), resulting in sequence length 256 for all 256×256 images. The model predicts velocity $v(x_t, t)$ and is trained to minimize:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{velocity}}(\theta) = \int_0^1 \mathbb{E}_{x, \varepsilon} \left[ \|v_\theta(x_t, t) - (\varepsilon - x)\|^2 \right] dt$$

For DiT optimization, they follow LightningDiT exactly: AdamW with constant learning rate $2.0 \times 10^{-4}$, batch size 1024, and EMA weight 0.9999. For DiTDH, they modify the recipe because the standard settings cause loss spikes at later epochs and slow EMA convergence at early epochs: they use linear decay from $2.0 \times 10^{-4}$ to $2.0 \times 10^{-5}$ with 40-epoch warmup, EMA weight 0.9995 (reduced from 0.9999), and gradient clipping of 1.0. All models train for 80 epochs by default (800 for best results), and only EMA model performance is reported. For sampling, they use standard ODE sampling with Euler sampler and 50 steps, finding performance generally converges above this threshold. For guidance, they primarily adopt AutoGuidance (Karras et al., 2025) with guidance scale 1.5 and a 20-epoch DiTDH-S checkpoint as the guiding model; for the best 1.13 FID result, they sweep to guidance scale 1.42 with a 14-epoch checkpoint. Notably, training this base guiding model requires only about 0.05% of the compute used to train the guided DiTDH-XL for 800 epochs.

**High-Resolution Synthesis Without Retraining**

A central challenge in high-resolution generation is that resolution scales with token count: doubling image size in each dimension requires roughly four times as many tokens. The authors address this by decoupling decoder patch size $p_d$ from encoder patch size $p_e$. When $p_d = p_e$, output matches input resolution; setting $p_d = 2 p_e$ produces 2× upsampled images, reconstructing 512×512 from the same 256 tokens used at 256×256. Since the decoder is decoupled from both encoder and diffusion process, they can reuse diffusion models trained at 256×256 resolution, simply swapping in an upsampling decoder. Table 9 shows this approach achieves competitive gFID (1.61 vs. 1.13 for direct 512 training) while being 4× more efficient than quadrupling token count, though rFID increases slightly (0.97 vs. 0.53).

**Why RAE Matters: Structured Representations, Not Just Dimension**

The authors conduct two critical ablation studies to isolate what makes RAE effective. First, they test whether DiTDH works without RAE by training on SD-VAE latents: Table 10 shows DiTDH-XL performs worse than DiT-XL on VAE (11.70 vs. 7.13 gFID), indicating the DDT head provides little benefit in low-dimensional spaces and its strength arises specifically in high-dimensional RAE tasks. Second, they test whether high dimensionality alone suffices by training on raw pixels with matching token dimensionality (256×256 images, patch size 16, giving 768-dimensional tokens matching DINOv2-B): Table 11 shows both DiT-XL and DiTDH-XL perform far worse on pixels (51.09 and 30.56 gFID) than on RAE latents (4.28 and 2.16 gFID). These results demonstrate that high dimensionality alone is insufficient—the structured semantic representation provided by RAE is crucial for achieving strong performance gains.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper delivers several contributions that range from incremental architectural improvements to fundamental reconceptualizations of how latent diffusion should operate. Below, we distinguish between what is genuinely novel versus what builds on existing foundations, and explain why each insight matters for the broader field.

### 4.1 Frozen Semantic Encoders as Diffusion Latent Spaces (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Prior Work Assumed:** The prevailing belief, explicitly stated in prior literature (Yu et al., 2024b; Tang et al., 2025), was that pretrained representation encoders are inherently unsuitable for reconstruction because they "emphasize high-level semantics while downplaying low-level details." This assumption led the field to either train encoders from scratch (VAEs) or add complex alignment losses to bridge semantic and reconstruction objectives (REPA, VA-VAE).

**What This Paper Shows:** Frozen encoders—without any fine-tuning or modification—can achieve reconstruction quality matching or exceeding SD-VAE (Table 1a: rFID 0.49 vs. 0.62) while preserving semantic representation quality (Table 1d: 84.5% vs. 8.0% linear probing accuracy). This is not an incremental improvement but a **paradigm shift**: it demonstrates that semantic richness and reconstruction fidelity are not mutually exclusive when the decoder is properly designed.

**Why This Matters:** This insight decouples encoder training from diffusion training entirely. Prior methods like REPA (Yu et al., 2025) require auxiliary alignment losses during diffusion training, adding complexity and tuning burden. RAE eliminates this overhead—once you have a pretrained encoder, you only train a lightweight decoder once, then reuse it across diffusion experiments. This significantly lowers the barrier to experimenting with different latent spaces and enables the field to leverage the rapid advances in representation learning (DINOv3, SigLIP3, etc.) without retraining encoders for each new diffusion model.

### 4.2 Width-Token Dimension Matching Principle (Theoretical Contribution)

**What Prior Work Assumed:** The common belief in generative modeling is that data manifolds have low intrinsic dimensionality (Pope et al., 2021), allowing models to operate effectively without scaling to full data dimension. This assumption justified using narrow diffusion backbones even when latent spaces were moderately high-dimensional.

**What This Paper Shows:** Theorem 1 provides a **provable lower bound** on diffusion training loss when model width $d$ is less than token dimension $n$. This is not an empirical observation but a mathematical guarantee: the loss cannot converge below $\sum_{i=d+1}^n \lambda_i$ regardless of training duration or depth. Figure 3's empirical results match this bound exactly, validating the theory.

**Why This Matters:** This principle has implications beyond RAE. Any diffusion model operating in high-dimensional spaces—whether semantic latents, pixel space, or other representations—must respect this width constraint. Prior work that struggled with high-dimensional diffusion (Skorokhodov et al., 2025; Yao et al., 2025) may have failed precisely because they violated this constraint. The theorem provides a diagnostic tool: if your diffusion model isn't converging, check whether width matches dimensionality before blaming other factors.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** While the observation that wider models help is not new, the **theoretical proof** establishing a hard lower bound is novel. This transforms an empirical heuristic into a design principle with mathematical grounding.

### 4.3 Reframing Autoencoding: From Compression to Representation Foundation (Conceptual Innovation)

**What Prior Work Assumed:** Latent diffusion inherited the VAE paradigm's core assumption: the latent space should be **compressed** relative to pixel space. SD-VAE compresses 256×256×3 images to 32×32×4 latents—an 8×8×48 = 3072× compression ratio. This compression was treated as essential for efficiency.

**What This Paper Shows:** RAE operates with **no compression** in the token count dimension (256 tokens in, 256 tokens out) and actually increases channel dimensionality (768 vs. 4). Despite this, computational cost remains comparable because DiT complexity depends primarily on token count, not channel dimension (Section D, Compute paragraph). The paper argues that autoencoding should be "reframed from a compression mechanism into a representation foundation" (Introduction, final paragraph).

**Why This Matters:** This conceptual shift opens new design spaces. If compression is not required, then latent space design can prioritize properties that actually matter for generation: semantic coherence, structural consistency, and diffusability. This explains why RAE converges 47× faster than SiT-XL (Figure 4)—the latent space is inherently more structured, not more compressed. Future work can explore other non-compressive latent representations (e.g., multi-scale features, hierarchical tokens) that were previously dismissed as too high-dimensional.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental reconceptualization**. Prior work like DC-AE 1.5 (Chen et al., 2025d) still treats compression as essential, merely optimizing the compression ratio. RAE challenges whether compression is necessary at all for efficient diffusion.

### 4.4 DiTDH: Width Without Quadratic Cost (Architectural Innovation)

**What Prior Work Did:** DDT (Wang et al., 2025c) introduced a decoupled diffusion transformer head, but motivated it from a representation alignment perspective. The architectural concept existed, but its application to high-dimensional RAE latents and the specific design principles were not established.

**What This Paper Adds:** The paper identifies **two critical design principles** for the diffusion head that prior work did not articulate:
1. **Wide and shallow beats narrow and deep**: Table 16 shows a 2-layer, 2048-dim head outperforms both a 6-layer, 1152-dim head and a 4-layer, 2048-dim head, despite similar or lower FLOPs.
2. **Head width should scale with encoder dimension**: Table 17 shows larger RAE encoders (DINOv2-L) benefit more from wider heads than smaller encoders (DINOv2-S).

**Why This Matters:** DiTDH solves the practical bottleneck created by Insight 4.2. If width must match token dimension, but quadratic attention makes wide models expensive, how do we scale? DiTDH provides the answer: concentrate width in a shallow head rather than distributing it across deep layers. This enables RAE-based diffusion at scales that would otherwise be computationally prohibitive.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** The DDT architecture itself is **incremental** (building on Wang et al., 2025c). However, the **design principles** (wide-shallow preference, encoder-dependent scaling) and the **application to high-dimensional semantic latents** are novel contributions that make DiTDH effective where prior decoupled heads were not.

### 4.5 Noise-Augmented Decoder Training (Practical Innovation)

**What Prior Work Assumed:** VAE decoders are trained on continuous latent distributions $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2 I)$, making them inherently robust to small latent perturbations. This robustness was taken for granted and not explicitly designed.

**What This Paper Shows:** RAE decoders trained on discrete latent supports fail at inference because diffusion outputs occupy a continuous distribution. Adding noise during decoder training (Section 4.3) is not optional—it's **necessary** for RAE to work. Table 5 shows gFID improves from 4.81 to 4.28 with noise augmentation, and Table 15a shows this benefit holds across all encoder types.

**Why This Matters:** This insight reveals a previously overlooked mismatch between decoder training and inference conditions in latent diffusion. Prior work that attempted to use non-VAE latents for diffusion may have failed because their decoders couldn't handle noisy diffusion outputs. The noise augmentation technique is simple but essential—it should become standard practice for any latent diffusion system where the encoder doesn't produce continuous distributions.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** The technique itself (adding noise during training) is **incremental**, borrowing from Normalizing Flows (Dinh et al., 2017; Ho et al., 2019). However, **identifying the decoder robustness problem specific to RAE** and demonstrating its necessity for high-dimensional semantic latents is a novel contribution that addresses a failure mode prior work did not anticipate.

| DiTDH design principles | **Incremental + Novel** | Architecture exists, but design rules and RAE application are new |
| Noise-augmented decoder | **Incremental** | Technique known, but problem identification for RAE is novel |

The most significant contribution is arguably the **conceptual reframing**: RAE demonstrates that the field has been optimizing for the wrong properties in latent spaces. Compression and reconstruction loss were treated as primary objectives, but semantic structure and diffusability matter more for generation quality. This insight should influence how future latent diffusion systems are designed, regardless of whether they adopt RAE specifically.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their claims through systematic experimentation. We analyze the evaluation methodology, present key quantitative results with specific numbers, assess the strength of evidence supporting each claim, and discuss ablation studies that isolate the contribution of individual components.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Resolutions**

All experiments use **ImageNet-1K** (Russakovsky et al., 2015) as the primary benchmark. The authors evaluate at two resolutions:
- **256×256**: Main evaluation setting for most comparisons
- **512×512**: High-resolution synthesis to demonstrate scalability

For 512×512 generation, the authors test two approaches: (1) direct training at 512×512 resolution, and (2) decoder upsampling where a 256×256-trained diffusion model pairs with a decoder that uses patch size $p_d = 2p_e$ to produce 2× upsampled outputs without retraining the diffusion model.

**Generation Metrics**

The paper employs four standard generative modeling metrics:

| Metric | What It Measures | Reference |
|--------|-----------------|-----------|
| **FID** (Fréchet Inception Distance) | Distance between feature distributions of real and generated images (lower is better) | Heusel et al., 2017 |
| **IS** (Inception Score) | KL divergence between marginal and conditional label distributions (higher is better) | Salimans et al., 2016 |
| **Precision** | Fraction of generated images that appear realistic (higher is better) | Kynkäänniemi et al., 2019 |
| **Recall** | Portion of training data manifold covered by generated samples (higher is better) | Kynkäänniemi et al., 2019 |

For reconstruction quality, the authors use **rFID** (FID computed on reconstructed ImageNet validation images). For generation quality, they report **gFID** (FID on 50K generated samples).

**Critical Evaluation Protocol Discovery**

A notable contribution in Section E is the identification of an **FID evaluation protocol inconsistency** across prior literature. The authors observe two sampling strategies:
1. **Class-balanced**: Exactly 50 images per class (50 × 1,000 = 50,000 total)
2. **Random sampling**: Uniformly sample class labels 50K times

Table 14 reveals that class-balanced sampling consistently achieves **~0.1 lower FID scores** than random sampling. For example, SiT-XL improves from 8.61 to 8.54 gFID (without guidance) and from 2.06 to 1.95 gFID (with guidance) when switching to balanced sampling. The authors re-evaluated several baselines (SiT, REPA, REPA-E, DDT) with class-balanced sampling to ensure fair comparison, updating their reported scores accordingly. This transparency strengthens the credibility of their comparisons.

**Computational Metrics**

The paper reports **GFLOPs** (giga floating-point operations) for both encoder/decoder efficiency (Figure 2) and diffusion training compute (Figure 6). All GFLOPs are evaluated on one 256×256 image. Training efficiency is measured in both epochs and total compute (GFLOPs), enabling comparison across methods with different convergence speeds.

**Baselines**

The authors compare against a comprehensive set of baselines across multiple paradigms:

- **Latent Diffusion with VAE**: DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2023), SiT (Ma et al., 2024), MDTv2 (Gao et al., 2023), VA-VAE (Yao et al., 2025), REPA (Yu et al., 2025), DDT (Wang et al., 2025c), REPA-E (Leng et al., 2025)
- **Autoregressive Models**: VAR (Tian et al., 2024), MAR (Li et al., 2024b), xAR (Ren et al., 2025)
- **Pixel Diffusion**: ADM (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), RIN (Jabri et al., 2023), PixelFlow (Chen et al., 2025e), PixNerd (Wang et al., 2025b), SiD2 (Hoogeboom et al., 2025)
- **GANs**: BigGAN-deep (Brock et al., 2019), StyleGAN-XL (Sauer et al., 2022), EDM2 (Karras et al., 2024)

All baseline results are either taken from original papers or re-evaluated by the authors using consistent protocols (Section E).

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Component Analysis

The paper includes extensive ablation studies that isolate the contribution of each proposed component.

**Width-Token Dimension Matching (Figure 3, Table 3)**

Figure 3 presents the critical overfitting experiment that motivates Theorem 1. The left plot shows training loss vs. DiT width for fixed token dimension $n = 768$:

- When $d < 768$: Loss plateaus at ~0.3-0.5, model cannot overfit
- When $d \geq 768$: Loss converges to ~0, model perfectly reconstructs

The right plot shows that increasing depth (12→24 layers) with fixed width $d = 384$ has **marginal effect**—loss remains high regardless of depth. This validates the claim that width, not depth, is the bottleneck for high-dimensional latents.

Table 3 extends this to practical model sizes:

| DiT Model | DINOv2-S (384-dim) | DINOv2-B (768-dim) | DINOv2-L (1024-dim) |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| DiT-S (384) | 3.6e-2 ✓ | 5.2e-1 ✗ | 6.5e-1 ✗ |
| DiT-B (768) | 1.0e-3 ✓ | 2.4e-2 ✓ | 2.7e-1 ✗ |
| DiT-L (1024) | 9.7e-4 ✓ | 1.3e-3 ✓ | 2.2e-2 ✓ |

Convergence (✓) occurs only when model width ≥ token dimension. DiT-S fails on DINOv2-B and DINOv2-L; DiT-B fails on DINOv2-L; only DiT-L succeeds on all three. This provides strong empirical support for Theorem 1.

**Noise Schedule Shift (Table 4)**

Table 4 quantifies the impact of dimension-dependent noise scheduling:

| Configuration | gFID ↓ |
|--------------|--------|
| Without shift | 23.08 |
| With shift | **4.81** |

This **4.8× improvement** (23.08 → 4.81) demonstrates that proper noise scheduling is critical for high-dimensional RAE latents. The shift uses $\alpha = \sqrt{m/n}$ where $n = 4096$ (base dimension) and $m$ is RAE's effective data dimension.

**Noise-Augmented Decoder (Table 5, Table 15)**

Table 5 shows the trade-off between generation and reconstruction quality:

| Latent Distribution | gFID ↓ | rFID ↓ |
|--------------------|--------|--------|
| Clean $p(z)$ | 4.81 | **0.49** |
| Noisy $p_n(z)$ | **4.28** | 0.57 |

Noise augmentation improves gFID by 11% (4.81 → 4.28) but worsens rFID by 16% (0.49 → 0.57). The authors accept this trade-off because generation quality is the primary objective.

Table 15c explores different noise scales $\tau$:

| $\tau$ | gFID ↓ | rFID ↓ |
|--------|--------|--------|
| 0.0 | 4.81 | 0.49 |
| 0.5 | 4.39 | 0.54 |
| 0.8 (default) | 4.28 | 0.57 |
| 1.0 | 4.20 | 0.60 |

Larger $\tau$ consistently improves gFID but degrades rFID. The default $\tau = 0.8$ balances this trade-off. Table 15a confirms this benefit holds across encoders: MAE-B improves from 16.14 to 8.38 gFID (48% reduction), SigLIP2-B from 6.69 to 4.93 (26% reduction), and DINOv2-B from 4.81 to 4.28 (11% reduction).

**DDT Head Design (Table 16, Table 17)**

Table 16 compares different DDT head architectures with approximately fixed parameter counts:

| Depth | Width | GFLOPs | FID ↓ |
|-------|-------|--------|-------|
| 6 | 1152 (XL) | 25.65 | 2.36 |
| 4 | 2048 (G) | 53.14 | 2.31 |
| **2** | **2048 (G)** | **26.78** | **2.16** |

The 2-layer, 2048-dim head outperforms both deeper and narrower variants despite similar GFLOPs, validating the **wide-and-shallow** design principle.

Table 17 shows how optimal head width depends on encoder size:

| DDT Head Width | DINOv2-S | DINOv2-B | DINOv2-L |
|---------------|----------|----------|----------|
| 768 (B) | 2.66 | 2.49 | N/A |
| 1536 (H) | 2.47 | 2.24 | 2.95 |
| **2048 (G)** | **2.42** | **2.16** | **2.73** |
| 2688 (T) | 2.43 | 2.22 | 2.64 |

For DINOv2-S and DINOv2-B, performance converges at 2048-dim width. For DINOv2-L, the 2688-dim head still provides gains (2.73 → 2.64), suggesting larger encoders benefit from wider heads.

**High-Resolution Synthesis (Table 9)**

Table 9 compares direct 512×512 training vs. decoder upsampling:

| Method | #Tokens | gFID ↓ | rFID ↓ |
|--------|---------|--------|--------|
| Direct 512×512 | 1024 | **1.13** | **0.53** |
| Upsampling (256→512) | 256 | 1.61 | 0.97 |

Decoder upsampling achieves competitive gFID (1.61 vs. 1.13) while being **4× more efficient** (256 vs. 1024 tokens). The rFID increase (0.53 → 0.97) reflects the decoder's reduced capacity for fine details at higher resolutions, but generation quality remains strong.

**Does DiTDH Work Without RAE? (Table 10)**

Table 10 tests whether DiTDH provides benefits independent of RAE:

| Model | SD-VAE gFID ↓ | DINOv2-B gFID ↓ |
|-------|--------------|-----------------|
| DiT-XL | **7.13** | 4.28 |
| DiTDH-XL | 11.70 | **2.16** |

On SD-VAE latents, DiTDH-XL performs **worse** than DiT-XL (11.70 vs. 7.13), despite extra computation from the DDT head. This confirms that DiTDH's benefits are **specific to high-dimensional RAE latents**, not a universal improvement. The DDT head addresses the width bottleneck created by RAE's high-dimensional tokens; in low-dimensional VAE spaces, it provides no advantage.

**Is Structured Representation Essential? (Table 11)**

Table 11 tests whether high dimensionality alone suffices, or if RAE's structured representation matters:

| Input Type | DiT-XL gFID ↓ | DiTDH-XL gFID ↓ |
|------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Raw Pixels (768-dim tokens) | 51.09 | 30.56 |
| DINOv2-B Latents (768-dim) | **4.28** | **2.16** |

Both models perform far worse on raw pixels despite matching token dimensionality (768). DiTDH-XL achieves 2.16 gFID on RAE latents vs. 30.56 on pixels—a **14× improvement**. This demonstrates that **high dimensionality alone is insufficient**; the structured semantic representation provided by RAE is crucial for strong performance.

### 5.4 Assessment of Experimental Evidence

**Strengths**

1. **Comprehensive baseline comparison**: The paper compares against 20+ methods across multiple paradigms (VAE diffusion, autoregressive, pixel diffusion, GANs), providing context for RAE's performance.

2. **Systematic ablation studies**: Each proposed component (width matching, noise schedule shift, noise-augmented decoder, DDT head) is independently validated with quantitative results. Tables 4, 5, 10, 11, 16, and 17 isolate individual contributions.

3. **Theoretical grounding**: Theorem 1 provides a provable lower bound that explains empirical observations (Figure 3), elevating the width-token dimension matching from heuristic to design principle.

4. **Evaluation protocol transparency**: The identification and correction of FID sampling inconsistencies (Section E, Table 14) demonstrates methodological rigor and strengthens comparison credibility.

5. **Efficiency metrics**: Reporting both quality (FID) and compute (GFLOPs, epochs) enables meaningful assessment of practical value, not just absolute performance.

**Limitations and Conditional Results**

1. **ImageNet-only evaluation**: All results are on ImageNet-1K class-conditional generation. The paper does not evaluate on text-to-image, unconditional generation (beyond brief Section L results), or other datasets. Generalization to other domains remains unproven.

2. **Guidance method dependency**: Best results (1.13 FID) require AutoGuidance (Karras et al., 2025), which needs a separate guiding model. While the guiding model is small (DiTDH-S, 20 epochs, 0.05% of main model's compute), this adds complexity compared to classifier-free guidance.

3. **Trade-off between reconstruction and generation**: Noise-augmented decoding improves gFID but worsens rFID (Table 5). For applications requiring faithful reconstruction (e.g., image editing, compression), this trade-off may be problematic.

4. **DiTDH ineffective on VAE latents**: Table 10 shows DiTDH performs worse than DiT on SD-VAE latents. This limits DiTDH's applicability to RAE-based systems; it is not a drop-in improvement for existing VAE-based pipelines.

5. **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: The paper notes DiTDH requires different optimization settings than DiT (learning rate decay vs. constant, lower EMA weight, gradient clipping). This suggests DiTDH may be more sensitive to hyperparameter tuning, though the authors do not extensively explore this.

6. **512×512 upsampling quality gap**: While decoder upsampling is 4× more efficient than direct training, it achieves worse gFID (1.61 vs. 1.13) and rFID (0.97 vs. 0.53) (Table 9). For applications requiring highest quality at 512×512, direct training remains necessary.

**Overall Assessment**

The experiments **convincingly support the paper's core claims**:

1. ✅ **Frozen encoders can reconstruct faithfully**: Table 1 shows RAEs outperform SD-VAE in rFID across multiple encoders (MAE-B: 0.16, DINOv2-B: 0.49, SigLIP2-B: 0.53 vs. SD-VAE: 0.62).

2. ✅ **Standard DiT fails on high-dimensional latents**: Table 2 and Figure 3 demonstrate catastrophic failure when width < token dimension, with theoretical justification (Theorem 1).

3. ✅ **Proposed fixes enable effective RAE diffusion**: Tables 4, 5, and 6 show each component (noise schedule shift, noise-augmented decoder, DDT head) provides measurable improvements.

4. ✅ **RAE achieves state-of-the-art generation**: Table 8 shows 1.51 gFID (no guidance) and 1.13 gFID (with guidance) at 256×256, outperforming all prior methods. Table 7 extends this to 512×512.

5. ✅ **RAE enables faster convergence**: Figure 4 demonstrates 47× speedup over SiT-XL and 16× over REPA-XL.

6. ✅ **Structured representation matters, not just dimension**: Table 11 shows RAE latents vastly outperform raw pixels despite matching dimensionality.

The most compelling evidence is the **convergence of theoretical analysis (Theorem 1), controlled experiments (Figure 3, Table 3), and practical results (Table 8)**. The width-token dimension matching principle is not just empirically observed but mathematically proven, then validated through systematic ablation, and finally demonstrated in state-of-the-art generation results.

The primary limitation is the **narrow evaluation scope** (ImageNet class-conditional only). Future work should validate RAE on text-to-image generation, video synthesis, and other domains to establish broader applicability. However, within the evaluated scope, the evidence is thorough and convincing.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Representation Autoencoders demonstrate compelling advantages over VAE-based latent diffusion, the approach is not without constraints. This section examines the assumptions underlying RAE, scenarios where it may underperform, computational trade-offs, and open questions that remain unresolved.

### 6.1 Architectural Assumptions and Dependencies

**DiTDH Requires High-Dimensional Latents**

A critical limitation is that the DiTDH architecture is **specifically designed for high-dimensional RAE latents** and does not generalize to standard VAE spaces. Table 10 provides direct evidence:

| Model | SD-VAE gFID ↓ | DINOv2-B gFID ↓ |
|-------|--------------|-----------------|
| DiT-XL | **7.13** | 4.28 |
| DiTDH-XL | 11.70 | **2.16** |

On SD-VAE latents, DiTDH-XL performs **64% worse** than DiT-XL (11.70 vs. 7.13 gFID), despite the additional computation from the DDT head. This indicates that the wide diffusion head addresses a bottleneck **created specifically by RAE's high-dimensional tokens**, not a universal limitation of diffusion transformers. Practitioners cannot adopt DiTDH as a drop-in improvement for existing VAE-based pipelines—it must be paired with RAE latents to provide benefits.

**Width-Token Dimension Matching Creates Scaling Constraints**

Theorem 1 establishes that model width must match or exceed token dimensionality for convergence. While theoretically sound, this creates practical constraints:

- For DINOv2-B (768-dim tokens), the DiT backbone must be at least 768-dimensional (DiT-B or larger)
- For DINOv2-L (1024-dim tokens), even DiT-B fails to converge reliably (Table 3 shows 2.7e-1 loss vs. 2.2e-2 for DiT-L)
- Scaling to larger encoders requires proportionally larger diffusion backbones, partially offsetting RAE's efficiency gains

Table 17 shows that optimal DDT head width increases with encoder size (2048-dim for DINOv2-S/B, but 2688-dim still helps for DINOv2-L). This suggests that **encoder scaling creates downstream architecture scaling requirements** that must be carefully managed.

**Frozen Encoder Assumption May Limit Adaptability**

The RAE approach relies on **frozen pretrained encoders** without fine-tuning. While this simplifies training and preserves semantic representations, it assumes that:

1. The pretrained encoder's feature space is suitable for the target generation domain
2. No domain-specific adaptation of the encoder is needed

The paper evaluates only on ImageNet-1K, where DINOv2, MAE, and SigLIP were all pretrained. For **out-of-domain generation** (e.g., medical imaging, satellite imagery, artistic styles), frozen encoders pretrained on natural images may not provide optimal latent representations. The paper does not explore whether encoder fine-tuning could improve generation quality for specialized domains, leaving this as an open question.

### 6.2 Reconstruction vs. Generation Trade-offs

**Noise-Augmented Decoding Sacrifices Reconstruction Fidelity**

A fundamental tension exists between optimizing for generation quality versus reconstruction quality. Table 5 quantifies this trade-off:

| Latent Distribution | gFID ↓ | rFID ↓ |
|--------------------|--------|--------|
| Clean $p(z)$ | 4.81 | **0.49** |
| Noisy $p_n(z)$ | **4.28** | 0.57 |

Noise-augmented decoding improves generation FID by **11%** but worsens reconstruction FID by **16%**. Table 15c shows this trade-off intensifies with larger noise scales $\tau$:

| $\tau$ | gFID ↓ | rFID ↓ |
|--------|--------|--------|
| 0.0 | 4.81 | 0.49 |
| 0.8 (default) | 4.28 | 0.57 |
| 1.0 | 4.20 | 0.60 |

For applications requiring **faithful reconstruction** (image editing, compression, inpainting), this trade-off may be problematic. The paper prioritizes generation quality, but users needing bidirectional fidelity (encode→decode→encode consistency) may find RAE less suitable than VAEs trained explicitly for reconstruction.

**Low rFID Does Not Guarantee Good Generation**

Table 15a reveals a counterintuitive finding: **reconstruction quality does not predict generation quality** across different encoders:

| Encoder | rFID ↓ | gFID ↓ |
|---------|--------|--------|
| MAE-B | **0.16** | 16.14 / 8.38 |
| DINOv2-B | 0.49 | **4.81 / 4.28** |
| SigLIP2-B | 0.53 | 6.69 / 4.93 |

MAE-B achieves the **best reconstruction** (0.16 rFID, 3× better than DINOv2-B) but the **worst generation** (16.14 gFID without noise augmentation, 2× worse than DINOv2-B). This demonstrates that **semantic structure matters more than pixel-level reconstruction accuracy** for diffusion modeling. However, this also means practitioners cannot use rFID as a proxy for selecting encoders—they must evaluate generation performance directly, adding experimental overhead.

### 6.3 Computational and Efficiency Constraints

**Best Results Require Extended Training**

While RAE enables faster convergence than VAE-based methods, the **state-of-the-art results still require substantial compute**:

| Model | Epochs | gFID (no guidance) | gFID (with guidance) |
|-------|--------|-------------------|---------------------|
| DiTDH-XL | 80 | 2.16 | – |
| DiTDH-XL | 800 | **1.51** | **1.13** |

The 1.51/1.13 FID results require **800 epochs** of training, not the 80 epochs highlighted in the convergence analysis. While this is still faster than competing methods (SiT-XL requires 1400 epochs for inferior results), the absolute compute cost remains significant. Table 8 shows DiTDH-XL has 839M parameters—larger than the 675M DiT-XL baseline—partially offsetting efficiency gains.

**AutoGuidance Adds System Complexity**

The best results (1.13 FID) require **AutoGuidance** (Karras et al., 2025), which uses a separate weaker diffusion model to guide the stronger one. Section I notes:

> "Unless otherwise specified, AutoGuidance results are obtained using a guidance scale of 1.5 and a 20-epoch checkpoint of DiTDH-S."

While the guiding model is small (DiTDH-S, 20 epochs, ~0.05% of main model's compute), this introduces:
- Additional model storage requirements
- Extra hyperparameters to tune (guidance scale, checkpoint selection)
- More complex inference pipelines

The paper notes that standard Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) performs worse on RAE latents: "CFG without interval does not improve FID; in fact, applying it from the first diffusion step increases FID." This limits guidance options and increases dependency on AutoGuidance.

**High-Resolution Upsampling Quality Gap**

For 512×512 synthesis, the paper proposes decoder upsampling as an efficient alternative to direct training. Table 9 quantifies the quality trade-off:

| Method | #Tokens | gFID ↓ | rFID ↓ |
|--------|---------|--------|--------|
| Direct 512×512 | 1024 | **1.13** | **0.53** |
| Upsampling (256→512) | 256 | 1.61 | 0.97 |

Decoder upsampling achieves **42% worse gFID** (1.61 vs. 1.13) and **83% worse rFID** (0.97 vs. 0.53) compared to direct training. While 4× more efficient in token count, this approach may be unsuitable for applications requiring highest quality at 512×512. The paper does not evaluate resolutions beyond 512×512, leaving scalability to 1024×1024 or higher as an open question.

### 6.4 Evaluation Scope Limitations

**ImageNet-Only Validation**

All quantitative results are on **ImageNet-1K class-conditional generation**. The paper does not evaluate:

- **Text-to-image generation**: No integration with text encoders (CLIP, T5) is tested
- **Unconditional generation**: Section L provides limited results (4.96 gFID vs. 4.89 for RCG), but this is less explored than class-conditional
- **Video synthesis**: No temporal modeling or video datasets are evaluated
- **Other image domains**: No evaluation on faces (FFHQ), landscapes, artwork, or specialized domains

The authors acknowledge this implicitly by focusing exclusively on ImageNet benchmarks. Generalization to other generation tasks remains unproven.

**FID Metric Limitations**

Section E raises important concerns about FID evaluation itself:

> "Finally, while the FID metric remains a useful indicator of generative quality, its absolute value becomes less meaningful as generation fidelity continues to improve."

The paper identifies protocol inconsistencies (class-balanced vs. random sampling affects FID by ~0.1 points) and re-evaluates baselines accordingly. However, this also highlights that **small FID differences at state-of-the-art levels may not reflect meaningful quality improvements**. The 1.51 vs. 1.70 gFID difference between DiTDH-XL and REPA-E may not correspond to perceptually significant quality gaps.

**Limited Qualitative Analysis**

Figure 7 and Figures 10-15 provide visual samples, but the paper lacks:
- Systematic failure mode analysis (what does the model generate poorly?)
- Diversity metrics beyond Precision/Recall
- User studies or perceptual evaluations
- Comparison of semantic consistency (do generated samples match class labels accurately?)

Without these analyses, it is difficult to assess whether RAE improves specific aspects of generation (e.g., texture quality, structural coherence, semantic accuracy) or simply optimizes FID.

### 6.5 Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Training Stability

**DiTDH Requires Different Optimization Settings**

Section D notes that DiTDH cannot use the same optimization recipe as standard DiT:

> "For DiTDH, we find using the recipe in (Yao et al., 2025) leads to loss spikes at later epochs and slow EMA model convergence at early epochs. We instead use a linear decay from $2.0 \times 10^{-4}$ to $2.0 \times 10^{-5}$ with a constant warmup of 40 epochs."

Additional DiTDH-specific settings include:
- EMA weight reduced from 0.9999 to 0.9995
- Gradient clipping of 1.0 (not used for DiT)
- Different learning rate schedule (decay vs. constant)

This suggests DiTDH is **more sensitive to hyperparameter tuning** than standard DiT. The paper does not explore how sensitive results are to these choices or whether alternative settings could improve stability further.

**Encoder Selection Requires Empirical Testing**

Table 15a shows significant variation in generation quality across encoders despite similar reconstruction quality:

| Encoder | rFID ↓ | gFID ↓ |
|---------|--------|--------|
| DINOv2-B | 0.49 | 4.28 |
| SigLIP2-B | 0.53 | 4.93 |
| MAE-B | 0.16 | 8.38 |

There is no theoretical principle for predicting which encoder will perform best for generation—practitioners must **empirically test multiple encoders**. This adds experimental overhead compared to using a single standard VAE (SD-VAE).

### 6.6 Open Questions and Future Directions

**Why Does DINOv2 Outperform MAE for Generation?**

The paper observes that DINOv2 produces the strongest generation results despite MAE achieving better reconstruction (Table 15a). The authors state:

> "Empirically, DINOv2 produces the strongest generation results, so unless otherwise noted, we adopt DINOv2 as our default encoder and defer the full comparison to Section G.1."

However, no theoretical explanation is provided for **why self-distillation features (DINOv2) are more "diffusable" than masked autoencoder features (MAE)**. Understanding this could guide encoder selection for future work.

**Can RAE Extend Beyond Images?**

The paper focuses exclusively on image generation. Key open questions include:
- Can RAE work for **video generation** with temporal encoders?
- Can RAE support **text-to-image** by conditioning the diffusion model on text embeddings?
- Can RAE apply to **3D generation** (voxels, point clouds, meshes)?

The representation learning literature has encoders for all these modalities, but whether they can serve as RAE encoders remains untested.

**What Is the Optimal Latent Dimensionality?**

The paper shows that higher-dimensional latents (DINOv2-L at 1024-dim) do not necessarily outperform mid-dimensional ones (DINOv2-B at 768-dim). Table 6 shows:

| Encoder | DiT-XL gFID ↓ | DiTDH-XL gFID ↓ |
|---------|--------------|-----------------|
| DINOv2-S (384-dim) | 3.50 | 2.42 |
| DINOv2-B (768-dim) | 4.28 | **2.16** |
| DINOv2-L (1024-dim) | 6.09 | 2.73 |

DINOv2-B achieves the best generation despite not having the highest dimensionality or best reconstruction. This suggests an **optimal latent dimensionality exists** that balances semantic richness against modeling difficulty. The paper does not systematically explore this trade-off space.

**Can Encoder and Decoder Be Jointly Optimized?**

The RAE approach freezes the encoder entirely. Recent work (REPA-E, Leng et al., 2025) shows that **end-to-end tuning of VAE encoders** with diffusion models can improve results. The paper does not explore whether **light encoder fine-tuning** (e.g., adapter layers, partial unfreezing) could further improve RAE performance while preserving semantic structure.

| Limitation | Severity | Mitigation |
|------------|----------|------------|
| DiTDH ineffective on VAE latents | High | Must pair DiTDH with RAE |

Despite these limitations, RAE represents a significant advance in latent diffusion. The key insight—that **semantic representation quality matters more than compression for generation**—should influence future work regardless of whether RAE is adopted directly. The limitations identified here provide a roadmap for subsequent research to address remaining challenges.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than introduce a new architecture—it challenges foundational assumptions about what makes a latent space suitable for diffusion modeling. In this section, we examine how RAE reshapes the field's design priorities, what research directions it unlocks, where it fits in practical workflows, and how practitioners should decide between RAE and existing alternatives.

### 7.1 Shifting the Field's Design Priorities

**From Compression to Representation Quality**

For over three years, latent diffusion has operated under an implicit assumption: **the latent space must be compressed** relative to pixel space. SD-VAE's 32×32×4 latent representation (8×8×48 = 3072× compression) became the de facto standard, and subsequent work optimized within this constraint. RAE fundamentally disrupts this paradigm by demonstrating that:

1. **Compression is not necessary for efficiency**: DiT computational cost depends primarily on token count, not channel dimensionality (Section D, Compute paragraph). RAE maintains 256 tokens while increasing channels from 4 to 768 with negligible overhead.

2. **Semantic structure matters more than compactness**: Table 11 shows that raw pixels with matching dimensionality (768-dim tokens) achieve 30.56 gFID vs. RAE's 2.16 gFID—a 14× difference. The structured representation from DINOv2, not the dimensionality itself, enables effective diffusion.

3. **Frozen encoders can outperform trained ones**: Table 1a shows RAE with frozen DINOv2-B achieves rFID 0.49 vs. SD-VAE's 0.62, while preserving 84.5% linear probing accuracy vs. 8.0%. This decouples encoder advancement from diffusion training entirely.

This reframing has cascading implications. Future latent diffusion work can now prioritize **diffusability** (how easily the latent distribution can be modeled) and **semantic coherence** (whether nearby latents correspond to semantically similar images) over compression ratio. This opens design spaces previously dismissed as "too high-dimensional."

**Width-Token Dimension as a First-Order Design Constraint**

Theorem 1 elevates width-token dimension matching from an empirical observation to a **mathematical necessity**. Prior work that struggled with high-dimensional diffusion (Skorokhodov et al., 2025; Yao et al., 2025) may have failed precisely because they violated this constraint without recognizing it.

This principle should now become a **diagnostic checkpoint** in diffusion model development:

| Symptom | Prior Interpretation | RAE-Informed Interpretation |
|---------|---------------------|----------------------------|
| Loss plateaus early | "Model capacity insufficient" | "Width < token dimension—check Theorem 1 bound" |
| Depth scaling doesn't help | "Architecture saturated" | "Expected—width is the bottleneck, not depth" |
| High-dimensional latents fail | "Latent space unsuitable" | "Model width may be undersized for dimension" |

Future papers reporting diffusion in high-dimensional spaces should explicitly verify that model width matches or exceeds token dimensionality, with Theorem 1's lower bound as a reference.

**Eliminating Auxiliary Alignment Losses**

Methods like REPA (Yu et., 2025), REPA-E (Leng et al., 2025), and VA-VAE (Yao et al., 2025) introduce auxiliary representation alignment losses to bridge semantic and generative objectives. RAE demonstrates this is unnecessary: by using the representation encoder **directly** as the latent space, alignment is automatic.

This simplification has practical consequences:
- **Reduced training complexity**: No need to balance multiple loss terms with carefully tuned weights
- **Faster convergence**: Figure 4 shows 16× speedup over REPA-XL without alignment losses
- **Cleaner ablation studies**: Representation quality is determined by encoder choice, not alignment objective design

Future work seeking to incorporate semantic information into diffusion should consider whether direct latent replacement (RAE-style) is preferable to indirect alignment (REPA-style).

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

**Text-to-Image Generation with RAE**

The paper evaluates only **class-conditional** generation on ImageNet. A natural extension is **text-to-image synthesis**, which requires conditioning the diffusion model on text embeddings (typically from CLIP or T5 encoders). Key questions include:

- Can RAE latents be effectively conditioned on text embeddings, or does the high dimensionality create optimization challenges?
- Does the semantic richness of DINOv2 latents improve text-image alignment compared to VAE latents?
- How should classifier-free guidance be adapted for RAE, given that standard CFG underperforms AutoGuidance (Section I)?

The paper's success with class labels suggests text conditioning should be feasible, but the 768-dimensional latent space may interact differently with text embeddings than SD-VAE's 4-dimensional latents.

**Video and Temporal Generation**

Visual representation learning has produced temporal encoders (e.g., VideoMAE, TimeSformer) that could serve as RAE encoders for video generation. This direction offers several advantages:

- **Temporal coherence**: Video encoders explicitly model frame-to-frame relationships, potentially producing latents with better temporal structure than frame-independent VAEs
- **Efficiency**: Token count remains fixed per frame, avoiding the quadratic scaling that plagues pixel-space video diffusion
- **Semantic consistency**: DINOv2-style features may maintain object identity across frames better than reconstruction-focused video VAEs

However, video introduces new challenges: motion modeling, variable-length sequences, and significantly higher compute requirements. RAE's efficiency gains at the image level may partially offset these costs.

**Domain-Specific Encoder Selection**

Table 15a shows significant variation in generation quality across encoders (DINOv2-B: 4.28 gFID, MAE-B: 8.38 gFID, SigLIP2-B: 4.93 gFID) despite similar reconstruction quality. This suggests **encoder selection is a critical hyperparameter** that warrants systematic study.

Future work could explore:
- **Domain-matched encoders**: Using encoders pretrained on specific domains (medical imaging, satellite imagery, artwork) for specialized generation tasks
- **Encoder ensembles**: Combining features from multiple encoders to capture complementary semantic information
- **Encoder adaptation**: Light fine-tuning (adapter layers, partial unfreezing) to better match the target generation domain while preserving semantic structure

The paper's frozen encoder approach simplifies training but may leave performance on the table for specialized domains where pretrained encoders are suboptimal.

**Optimal Latent Dimensionality**

Table 6 reveals a non-monotonic relationship between encoder size and generation quality:

| Encoder | Dimension | DiTDH-XL gFID ↓ |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| DINOv2-S | 384 | 2.42 |
| DINOv2-B | 768 | **2.16** |
| DINOv2-L | 1024 | 2.73 |

DINOv2-B achieves the best results despite not having the highest dimensionality. This suggests an **optimal latent dimensionality** exists that balances semantic richness against modeling difficulty. Future work should systematically explore this trade-off space, potentially identifying dimensionality "sweet spots" for different generation tasks.

**Alternative Guidance Methods**

The paper relies heavily on AutoGuidance (Karras et al., 2025) for best results, noting that standard Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) "does not improve FID; in fact, applying it from the first diffusion step increases FID" (Section I). This limitation warrants investigation:

- Why does CFG underperform on RAE latents? Is it related to the high-dimensional latent structure?
- Can CFG be modified (e.g., different guidance intervals, scale schedules) to work effectively with RAE?
- Are there guidance methods specifically designed for high-dimensional semantic latents?

Resolving this would reduce system complexity (no need for a separate guiding model) and improve accessibility for practitioners.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

**High-Quality Image Synthesis**

RAE's primary application is **state-of-the-art image generation** where quality is the priority. Table 8 shows RAE-based DiTDH-XL achieves 1.51 gFID without guidance and 1.13 with guidance at 256×256, outperforming all prior methods. For use cases requiring highest visual fidelity (digital art, advertising, entertainment), RAE offers:

- **Superior quality**: Best-in-class FID scores across resolutions
- **Faster training**: 47× speedup over SiT-XL to comparable quality (Figure 4)
- **Simpler recipe**: No auxiliary alignment losses or complex tuning

**Compute-Efficient Generation**

For practitioners with limited training budgets, RAE enables competitive results with reduced compute:

- **DiTDH-B at 80 epochs** achieves 2.16 gFID (Table 8), competitive with methods trained for 400-1400 epochs
- **Decoder upsampling** enables 512×512 generation from 256×256-trained models with 4× efficiency (Table 9)
- **Frozen encoder** eliminates encoder training cost entirely

This makes RAE attractive for research labs and smaller organizations that cannot afford thousand-epoch training runs.

**When VAE May Still Be Preferred**

Despite RAE's advantages, VAE-based approaches remain preferable in certain scenarios:

| Scenario | Preferred Approach | Rationale |
|----------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Faithful reconstruction required** | VAE | Noise-augmented decoding worsens rFID (Table 5: 0.49 → 0.57) |
| **Existing VAE pipeline** | VAE | DiTDH performs worse on VAE latents (Table 10: 11.70 vs. 7.13 gFID) |
| **Image editing/inpainting** | VAE | Bidirectional consistency (encode→decode→encode) matters more |
| **Text-to-image with existing models** | VAE | RAE text-to-image not yet validated; integration overhead |
| **Minimal system complexity** | VAE | AutoGuidance requires separate guiding model (Section I) |

Practitioners should evaluate their specific requirements before adopting RAE.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

**Getting Started with RAE**

For practitioners interested in adopting RAE, we recommend the following entry points:

1. **Start with DINOv2-B encoder**: Table 15a shows it achieves the best generation quality (4.28 gFID) among tested encoders. Use the frozen weights from official releases.

2. **Use ViT-XL decoder for reconstruction**: Table 1b shows it achieves rFID 0.49 at 106.7 GFLOPs, balancing quality and efficiency. Train for 16 epochs with the loss weights specified in Section C.2 ($\omega_L = 1.0$, $\omega_G = 0.75$).

3. **Match DiT width to token dimension**: For DINOv2-B (768-dim), use at least DiT-B (768-dim width). Table 3 shows narrower models fail to converge.

4. **Apply dimension-dependent noise schedule**: Use the shift formula from Section 4.2 with $n = 4096$ base dimension and $\alpha = \sqrt{m/n}$ where $m$ is your effective data dimension.

5. **Enable noise-augmented decoding**: Set $\tau = 0.8$ for the noise scale (Table 15c default). This improves gFID by 11% at the cost of 16% rFID degradation.

6. **Add DDT head for scaling**: Use a 2-layer, 2048-dim head (Section G.3 default). This provides width efficiently without quadratic FLOP growth.

7. **Use AutoGuidance for best results**: Train a small DiTDH-S model for 14-20 epochs as the guiding model, with guidance scale 1.42-1.5 (Section I).

**Hardware and Compute Requirements**

Based on the paper's training configuration (Section D, Compute):

- **Training**: PyTorch/XLA on TPU recommended; authors used v6e-8 for 50K sample generation
- **Memory**: DiTDH-XL has 839M parameters; batch size 1024 requires substantial memory
- **Training time**: 80 epochs for competitive results, 800 epochs for state-of-the-art
- **Inference**: 50-step Euler sampler; performance converges above this threshold (Section D, Sampling)

For resource-constrained settings, consider DiTDH-B at 80 epochs (2.16 gFID) rather than DiTDH-XL at 800 epochs (1.51 gFID).

**Integration with Existing Codebases**

The RAE approach can be integrated into existing DiT codebases with the following modifications:

1. **Replace VAE encoder** with frozen DINOv2 (or other representation encoder)
2. **Add trained ViT decoder** for pixel reconstruction
3. **Modify DiT first layer** to project 768-dim latents to model hidden dimension
4. **Implement dimension-dependent noise schedule shift** (Section 4.2 formula)
5. **Add DDT head** as a shallow transformer module after the DiT backbone
6. **Update optimization settings** for DiTDH (learning rate decay, EMA weight 0.9995, gradient clipping 1.0)

The authors' codebase should be available at the project page (rae-dit.github.io), which will simplify integration.

**Evaluation Protocol Awareness**

Section E identifies a critical FID evaluation inconsistency: **class-balanced sampling** (50 images per class) achieves ~0.1 lower FID than random sampling. When comparing your RAE results to prior work:

- Use class-balanced sampling for fair comparison
- Re-evaluate baselines with the same protocol if possible
- Report both sampling strategies if comparing to mixed literature

This transparency prevents misleading comparisons and strengthens reproducibility.

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision

RAE points toward a future where **latent space design is decoupled from compression constraints**. Instead of asking "how can we compress this image into fewer tokens?", researchers can ask "what representation properties enable the best generation?"

This shift enables several long-term possibilities:

- **Multi-modal latent spaces**: Combining visual, textual, and audio features in a unified latent representation for cross-modal generation
- **Hierarchical representations**: Using multi-scale encoder features (not just single-scale tokens) for generation with explicit coarse-to-fine structure
- **Task-specific latents**: Training decoders for specific downstream tasks (editing, super-resolution, inpainting) while sharing the same frozen encoder
- **Continual learning**: Updating decoders for new domains without retraining encoders, leveraging the frozen encoder's generalization

The paper's contribution extends beyond RAE itself—it provides a **framework for thinking about latent spaces** that should influence generative modeling research for years to come. The key insight is that **semantic structure, not compression, enables effective diffusion**. Future work that embraces this principle, whether through RAE or alternative approaches, will likely achieve the next advances in generative modeling.
