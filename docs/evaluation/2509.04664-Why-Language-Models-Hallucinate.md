## 1. Executive Summary

This paper demonstrates that hallucinations in state-of-the-art models like DeepSeek-V3 and GPT-4o originate statistically during pretraining as inevitable binary classification errors, where generative error rates are mathematically lower-bounded by Is-It-Valid misclassification rates (e.g., $err \gtrsim 2 \cdot err_{iiv}$). The authors argue these errors persist because 9 out of 10 dominant benchmarks (including GPQA, MMLU-Pro, and SWE-bench) use binary grading that penalizes uncertainty, incentivizing models to guess rather than abstain. To mitigate this, the paper proposes modifying existing leaderboard scoring to explicitly credit abstentions via confidence targets, rather than introducing separate hallucination evaluations.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand why this paper is necessary, we must first clarify what is meant by "hallucination" in this specific technical context, and why existing solutions have failed to eliminate it. The authors define hallucinations not as mystical failures, but as **plausible falsehoods**—statements that look correct but are factually wrong (Section 1). This is distinct from generating nonsense (e.g., gibberish), which state-of-the-art models rarely do. The core problem is that models consistently produce these falsehoods even when instructed to admit uncertainty, such as responding with a specific date when asked "If you know, just respond with DD-MM" (Section 1).

### The Problem Gap: Persistence Despite Progress
Despite significant engineering progress, hallucinations remain pervasive in state-of-the-art systems (OpenAI, 2025a). The gap this paper addresses is twofold:
1.  **Theoretical Gap:** There is no unified statistical explanation for why errors arise during **pretraining**, even if the training data were perfect. Prior explanations often attribute errors to noisy data or specific architectural flaws, but this paper argues errors are inherent to the statistical objective of density estimation.
2.  **Incentive Gap:** There is a misalignment between what we want models to do (admit uncertainty) and how we evaluate them. The paper identifies that hallucinations persist through **post-training** because the dominant benchmarks reward guessing over honesty (Section 1.2).

### Why This Problem Matters
The importance of this work spans both practical utility and theoretical understanding:
*   **Trust and Utility:** Hallucinations undermine trust in AI systems. If a model cannot be trusted to distinguish fact from fiction, its utility in high-stakes domains (like medicine or law) is limited. The paper uses the analogy of a student taking an exam: if a student guesses on every question rather than leaving blanks when unsure, they may score higher on a multiple-choice test, but they have not demonstrated knowledge (Section 1.2).
*   **Demystification:** By framing hallucinations as standard statistical errors, the authors remove the "mystery" surrounding them. If hallucinations are simply binary classification errors in disguise, then decades of computational learning theory can be applied to understand and bound them (Section 1.1).

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations
The field has attempted to address hallucinations through various lenses, but the authors argue these approaches are insufficient.

#### 1. Descriptive Causes vs. Fundamental Causes
Surveys have cataloged many contributing factors to hallucinations, including:
*   Model overconfidence (Yin et al., 2023)
*   Decoding randomness (Lee et al., 2022)
*   Snowballing effects (Zhang et al., 2023)
*   Spurious correlations (Li et al., 2022)

While accurate, these are often descriptive rather than foundational. They explain *how* a specific error might propagate, but not *why* the model is statistically pressured to generate errors in the first place. This paper seeks a lower bound on errors that applies regardless of these specific mechanisms (Section 1.1).

#### 2. Theoretical Precedents
Previous theoretical work has touched on related issues but lacks the scope of this analysis:
*   **Kalai and Vempala (2024):** This prior work connected hallucinations to missing mass estimates (Good, 1953). However, it was a special case that did not account for **prompts** or **abstentions** (IDK responses). The current paper generalizes this to include prompts and explicitly models the choice to abstain (Section 1.1, Section 3.3.1).
*   **Trade-off Studies:** Recent work (Kalavasis et al., 2025; Kleinberg and Mullainathan, 2024) formalizes a trade-off between consistency (avoiding invalid outputs) and breadth (generating diverse content). This paper complements that by showing how evaluation incentives skew this trade-off toward breadth (guessing) at the cost of consistency.
*   **Interactive Learning:** Hanneke et al. (2018) proposed algorithms that query a validity oracle to minimize hallucinations. While statistically efficient, these methods are not computationally efficient for modern large-scale models (Section 2).

#### 3. Mitigation Techniques
Engineering solutions have been deployed to reduce hallucinations, including:
*   **Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF):** Shown to reduce conspiracy theories and misconceptions (Ouyang et al., 2022).
*   **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG):** Augments models with search to ground responses (Lewis et al., 2020).

However, the authors argue these are partial fixes. For example, Observation 1 in Section 4.1 proves that even models with search capabilities will optimize for guessing if the evaluation metric penalizes abstention. If a search tool fails to find a confident answer, a binary grading scheme still rewards a "best guess" over an "I don't know" response.

#### 4. The Evaluation Blind Spot
Perhaps the most critical limitation in prior work is the focus on evaluation metrics. The 2025 AI Index report notes that hallucination benchmarks "have struggled to gain traction within the AI community" (Maslej et al., 2025). The paper argues this is because researchers have been searching for a "perfect hallucination eval" while ignoring the **primary evaluations** that drive model development.

Table 2 (Section 4.1) analyzes ten dominant benchmarks (including GPQA, MMLU-Pro, and SWE-bench). The analysis reveals:
*   **9 out of 10** use binary grading (correct/incorrect).
*   **None** give full credit for abstentions (IDK).
*   **WildBench** is the only exception, offering partial credit, but its rubric may still score hallucinations higher than honest uncertainty (Section F.1).

This creates an "epidemic of penalizing uncertainty" (Section 1.2). As long as leaderboards are dominated by metrics where $Score(\text{Guess}) > Score(\text{IDK})$, models will be optimized to hallucinate.

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself as a bridge between statistical learning theory and socio-technical system design.
*   **Novel Reduction:** It introduces a novel reduction from supervised learning (binary classification) to unsupervised learning (density estimation). This allows the authors to apply known bounds from classification theory to generative models (Section 2).
*   **Holistic Scope:** Unlike prior work that focuses solely on pretraining statistics or post-training alignment, this paper connects the two. It shows how pretraining creates the *capacity* for error, and how post-training evaluation incentives *select* for that error (Section 1.2).
*   **Actionable Mitigation:** Rather than proposing a new benchmark that might be ignored, the authors propose modifying the scoring of **existing** mainstream evaluations. By explicitly stating confidence targets (e.g., "Answer only if > 75% confident"), the field can realign incentives without discarding current leaderboards (Section 4.2).

In summary, the paper argues that hallucinations are not just a bug to be patched, but a feature of the current optimization landscape. Solving them requires changing the objective function of the entire ecosystem, not just the model architecture.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a theoretical analysis paper that uses computational learning theory to prove hallucinations arise from fundamental statistical pressures in language model training, rather than treating them as mysterious architectural failures. The core idea is a mathematical reduction showing that generative error rates are lower-bounded by binary classification error rates, which then connects to how evaluation incentives select for overconfident guessing during post-training.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The "system" being analyzed is the standard two-stage language model training pipeline: pretraining (learning a text distribution from a corpus) followed by post-training (refining behavior through techniques like RLHF). The paper solves the problem of explaining why hallucinations persist even in well-trained models by proving they are statistically inevitable during pretraining and then incentivized during post-training through misaligned evaluation metrics.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The analysis proceeds through four connected components that build on each other. First, the **Is-It-Valid (IIV) Classifier** takes any language model and converts it into a binary classifier that distinguishes valid outputs from errors by thresholding the model's probability assignments. Second, the **Error Reduction Framework** mathematically relates the generative error rate to the IIV misclassification rate, establishing that if a model cannot reliably classify validity, it must generate errors. Third, the **Prompt-Generalized Analysis** extends this reduction to include contextual prompts and abstention responses (like "I don't know"), making the theory applicable to real-world question-answering scenarios. Fourth, the **Evaluation Incentive Model** analyzes how binary grading schemes in benchmarks create optimization pressure that favors guessing over honest uncertainty, explaining why hallucinations survive post-training. Information flows from the statistical pretraining analysis (Components 1-3) to the socio-technical post-training analysis (Component 4), showing how errors originate statistically and then persist through incentive misalignment.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **Start with the IIV reduction** because it is the foundational mathematical insight that connects generative modeling to supervised learning, enabling all subsequent bounds.
- **Explain the calibration term $\delta$** next because it clarifies why well-trained base models naturally satisfy the conditions for the error bounds to apply.
- **Generalize to prompts and abstentions** because real-world hallucinations occur in prompted settings where models should sometimes say "I don't know."
- **Analyze specific error factors** (arbitrary facts, poor models, computational hardness) to show how the abstract bounds manifest in concrete scenarios.
- **Connect to post-training incentives** because this explains why hallucinations persist even when pretraining errors could theoretically be corrected.
- **Present the confidence target proposal** as the actionable mitigation that follows logically from the incentive analysis.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### The Is-It-Valid Reduction: Connecting Generation to Classification

The paper's central technical contribution is a reduction that treats any language model as a binary classifier for output validity. To understand this, consider that generating a valid output implicitly requires the model to distinguish valid strings from invalid ones across all candidate responses. The authors formalize this intuition through the **Is-It-Valid (IIV) problem**, which is a supervised binary classification task where each example $x$ from a set of plausible strings $\mathcal{X}$ is labeled as either valid ($+$) if $x \in \mathcal{V}$ or error ($-$) if $x \in \mathcal{E}$, where $\mathcal{X} = \mathcal{E} \cup \mathcal{V}$ partitions plausible strings into errors and valid outputs (Section 3.1).

The training distribution for IIV is constructed as a 50/50 mixture: half the examples come from the valid training distribution $p$ (where $p(\mathcal{E}) = 0$, assuming error-free training data), and half come from uniformly random errors from $\mathcal{E}$. Formally, the IIV distribution $D$ is defined as:

$$D(x) := \begin{cases} p(x)/2 & \text{if } x \in \mathcal{V}, \\ 1/(2|\mathcal{E}|) & \text{if } x \in \mathcal{E}. \end{cases}$$

The target function $f: \mathcal{X} \to \{-, +\}$ simply indicates membership in $\mathcal{V}$, where $f(x) = +$ if $x \in \mathcal{V}$ and $f(x) = -$ if $x \in \mathcal{E}$. Any base language model $\hat{p}$ can be converted into an IIV classifier $\hat{f}$ by thresholding the model's probability at $1/|\mathcal{E}|$:

$$\hat{f}(x) := \begin{cases} + & \text{if } \hat{p}(x) > 1/|\mathcal{E}|, \\ - & \text{if } \hat{p}(x) \leq 1/|\mathcal{E}|. \end{cases}$$

This threshold is not arbitrary—it represents the probability mass a uniform distribution over errors would assign to each error string. The generative error rate is defined as $err := \hat{p}(\mathcal{E}) = \Pr_{x \sim \hat{p}}[x \in \mathcal{E}]$, which measures the probability that the model generates an error when sampling from its distribution. The IIV misclassification rate is $err_{iiv} := \Pr_{x \sim D}[\hat{f}(x) \neq f(x)]$, which measures how often the thresholded classifier makes mistakes on the 50/50 mixture distribution.

The key mathematical result (Corollary 1, Section 3.1) establishes the following lower bound:

$$err \geq 2 \cdot err_{iiv} - \frac{|\mathcal{V}|}{|\mathcal{E}|} - \delta,$$

where $\delta := |\hat{p}(A) - p(A)|$ for $A := \{x \in \mathcal{X} \mid \hat{p}(x) > 1/|\mathcal{E}|\}$. In plain language, this inequality states that the generative error rate is at least twice the IIV misclassification rate, minus two correction terms: the ratio of valid to error strings (which is typically small when there are many possible errors), and the calibration term $\delta$ (which measures how well the model's probability assignments match the training distribution on high-probability examples).

The factor of 2 arises from the 50/50 mixture in the IIV distribution—since half the IIV examples are errors, a misclassification on an error example contributes less to $err_{iiv}$ than to $err$. The ratio $|\mathcal{V}|/|\mathcal{E}|$ is typically negligible for factual questions: for birthday facts, there is 1 correct date but 364 incorrect dates, so $|\mathcal{V}|/|\mathcal{E}| \approx 1/364$ (Section 3.1). The calibration term $\delta$ is shown to be small for models trained with cross-entropy loss because if $\delta \neq 0$, rescaling probabilities would reduce the loss, contradicting the assumption that training reached a local minimum (Section 3.1, "Calibration" paragraph).

#### Why Calibration Matters: The Role of $\delta$

The calibration term $\delta$ requires careful explanation because it determines whether the error bound is meaningful. Calibration, in this context, measures whether the model's probability assignments on high-confidence examples match the empirical frequency of those examples in the training distribution. The authors justify why $\delta$ is typically small after pretraining by considering a rescaling transformation $\hat{p}_s$ that multiplies probabilities above the threshold by a factor $s > 0$ and renormalizes. They show that $\delta$ equals the magnitude of the derivative of the cross-entropy loss with respect to $s$, evaluated at $s = 1$:

$$\delta = \left| \frac{d}{ds} \mathcal{L}(\hat{p}_s) \bigg|_{s=1} \right|.$$

If $\delta \neq 0$, then some $s \neq 1$ would reduce the loss, meaning the model has not reached a local minimum. For any model class powerful enough to approximate such rescaling (which modern language models are), local optimization should yield small $\delta$. This is weaker than full calibration notions like Expected Calibration Error (ECE) because it only checks one threshold $t = 1/|\mathcal{E}|$, but it is sufficient for the bound to apply (Section 3.1, "Calibration" paragraph).

Figure 2 (Section 3.1) provides empirical support: GPT-4's pretrained model shows well-calibrated histograms before reinforcement learning, while post-trained models deviate from calibration due to RLHF objectives that prioritize helpfulness over probability accuracy. This observation is critical—it suggests that base models are calibrated (small $\delta$), but post-training can increase $\delta$, which ironically might reduce hallucinations but at the cost of breaking the statistical assumptions that make models useful for other tasks.

#### Generalizing to Prompts and Abstentions

The initial reduction assumes no prompts, but real-world hallucinations occur when models respond to specific questions. Section 3.2 generalizes the framework by introducing a prompt distribution $\mu$ over contexts $c \in \mathcal{C}$. Each example is now a pair $x = (c, r)$ consisting of a prompt $c$ and a response $r$. For each prompt $c$, the valid responses are $\mathcal{V}_c := \{r \mid (c, r) \in \mathcal{V}\}$ and error responses are $\mathcal{E}_c := \{r \mid (c, r) \in \mathcal{E}\}$.

The training distribution and base model become conditional response distributions $p(r \mid c)$ and $\hat{p}(r \mid c)$. The IIV distribution $D$ now selects, with equal probability, either a valid example from $p$ or a uniformly random error $r \in \mathcal{E}_c$ for a randomly sampled prompt $c \sim \mu$. The classifier threshold becomes $1/\min_c |\mathcal{E}_c|$, using the minimum number of errors across all prompts to ensure the threshold is well-defined.

Theorem 1 (Section 3.2) generalizes Corollary 1 to the prompted setting:

$$err \geq 2 \cdot err_{iiv} - \frac{\max_c |\mathcal{V}_c|}{\min_c |\mathcal{E}_c|} - \delta,$$

where $\delta := |\hat{p}(A) - p(A)|$ for $A := \{(c, r) \in \mathcal{X} \mid \hat{p}(r \mid c) > 1/\min_c |\mathcal{E}_c|\}$. The structure is identical to Corollary 1, but the ratio term now uses the worst-case prompt (maximum valid responses divided by minimum error responses). This generalization is essential because it allows the theory to cover question-answering scenarios where the model should sometimes abstain with "I don't know" responses.

Crucially, the framework explicitly includes abstention responses in $\mathcal{V}_c$. For a birthday question, $\mathcal{V}_c$ might include both the correct date and "I don't know," while $\mathcal{E}_c$ contains all incorrect dates. This means the model can avoid errors by abstaining, but the post-training analysis (Section 4) shows that evaluation incentives discourage this behavior.

#### Error Factors: When Do Bounds Become Tight?

The abstract bounds become concrete when applied to specific error scenarios. Section 3.3 identifies three primary factors that determine how large $err_{iiv}$ must be, and therefore how large $err$ must be.

**Arbitrary Facts (Section 3.3.1):** When there is no learnable pattern in the data—such as individual birthdays—epistemic uncertainty arises because the necessary knowledge is absent from training. The paper defines the **singleton rate** $sr = |S|/N$, where $S$ is the set of prompts that appear exactly once in the $N$ training examples (excluding abstentions). This builds on Alan Turing's "missing mass" estimator (Good, 1953), which uses the fraction of samples appearing exactly once to estimate the probability of unseen events.

Theorem 2 (Section 3.3.1) provides a concrete bound for arbitrary facts:

$$err \geq sr - \frac{2}{\min_c |\mathcal{E}_c|} - \frac{35 + 6 \ln N}{\sqrt{N}} - \delta.$$

In plain language, if 20% of birthday facts appear exactly once in training data, base models should hallucinate on at least approximately 20% of birthday facts (minus small correction terms). The theorem also provides an upper bound showing there exists a calibrated algorithm achieving $err \leq sr \cdot \frac{L}{L+1} + O(1/\sqrt{N})$, where $L = \max_c |\mathcal{E}_c|$. This means hallucination rates are tightly controlled by the singleton rate—facts seen only once cannot be reliably distinguished from errors.

**Poor Models (Section 3.3.2):** Errors also arise when the model family cannot represent the target function well, even with sufficient data. The paper uses **agnostic learning** theory, which defines $opt(\mathcal{G}) := \min_{g \in \mathcal{G}} \Pr_{x \sim D}[g(x) \neq f(x)]$ as the minimal error rate achievable by any classifier in a given family $\mathcal{G}$. For language models, $\mathcal{G}$ is the family of thresholded classifiers $g_{\theta, t}(c, r) = +$ if $\hat{p}_\theta(r \mid c) > t$.

Theorem 3 (Section 3.3.2) shows that for pure multiple-choice settings (one correct answer per prompt, $C$ total choices):

$$err \geq 2 \left(1 - \frac{1}{C}\right) \cdot opt(\mathcal{G}).$$

Corollary 2 applies this to trigram models on a pronoun agreement task: with $C = 2$ choices and $opt(\mathcal{G}) = 1/2$ (trigrams cannot distinguish the contexts), any trigram model must have generation error rate at least $1/2$. This explains why older n-gram models produced ungrammatical sentences—they lacked the representational capacity to capture long-range dependencies, and the error bound quantifies this limitation.

The letter-counting example from the introduction illustrates this factor: DeepSeek-V3 incorrectly counts D's in "DEEPSEEK" because modern language models tokenize text (e.g., D/EEP/SEE/K) rather than processing individual characters, while DeepSeek-R1's reasoning chain-of-thought overcomes this representational limitation (Section 3.3.2).

**Additional Factors (Section 3.4):** The paper briefly addresses computational hardness (some problems are intractable regardless of model capacity, like decrypting ciphertext without a key), distribution shift (test prompts differ from training distribution), and GIGO (training data contains errors that models replicate). For computational hardness, Observation 2 (Appendix D) shows that if a classifier cannot $\beta$-break an encryption scheme, the language model will output erroneous decryptions with probability at least $1 - \beta - 2/(|\mathcal{M}| - 1) - \delta$, where $|\mathcal{M}|$ is the message space size.

#### Post-Training: Why Hallucinations Persist

Section 4 shifts from statistical analysis to incentive analysis, explaining why hallucinations survive post-training even when they could theoretically be reduced. The core argument is that binary grading schemes in benchmarks create optimization pressure that favors guessing over abstention.

The paper defines a **binary grader** $g_c: \mathcal{R}_c \to \mathbb{R}$ as a scoring function where $\{g_c(r) \mid r \in \mathcal{R}_c\} = \{0, 1\}$ and $g_c(r) = 0$ for all abstention responses $r \in \mathcal{A}_c$ (e.g., "I don't know"). Observation 1 (Section 4.1) proves that for any distribution $\rho_c$ over binary graders, the optimal response is never an abstention:

$$\mathcal{A}_c \cap \arg\max_{r \in \mathcal{R}_c} \mathbb{E}_{g_c \sim \rho_c}[g_c(r)] = \emptyset.$$

The proof (Appendix E) is straightforward: since abstentions always score 0 and at least one non-abstention scores 1 with non-zero probability, the expected score of guessing strictly exceeds the expected score of abstaining. This creates what the authors call an "epidemic of penalizing uncertainty" (Section 1.2).

Table 2 (Section 4.1) analyzes ten dominant benchmarks and finds that 9 out of 10 use binary grading with no credit for abstentions:
- **GPQA, MMLU-Pro, BBH, MuSR, HLE:** Multiple-choice accuracy with no IDK option.
- **SWE-bench:** Patches either pass unit tests (1) or fail (0), with no partial credit for indicating uncertainty.
- **IFEval, Omni-MATH, MATH (L5):** Exact-match or equivalence grading with no IDK credit.
- **WildBench:** The only exception, using a 1-10 LM-graded rubric, but the rubric may score hallucinated responses (5-6: "fair but has some issues") higher than honest uncertainty (3-4: "poor and does not help") (Section F.1).

This analysis explains why adding dedicated hallucination benchmarks is insufficient: even if a model performs well on hallucination-specific evals, it will be optimized for the primary benchmarks that dominate leaderboards and drive model selection. The 2025 AI Index Report notes that hallucination benchmarks "have struggled to gain traction within the AI community" (Maslej et al., 2025), which the authors attribute to this incentive misalignment.

#### Confidence Targets: The Proposed Mitigation

Section 4.2 proposes modifying existing evaluations to include explicit confidence targets in the instructions, rather than creating new benchmarks. The proposal appends statements like:

> "Answer only if you are $> t$ confident, since mistakes are penalized $t/(1-t)$ points, while correct answers receive 1 point, and an answer of 'I don't know' receives 0 points."

For $t = 0.5$, the penalty is 1 point (symmetric); for $t = 0.75$, the penalty is 2 points; for $t = 0.9$, the penalty is 9 points. A simple calculation shows that the expected score of offering an answer exceeds IDK (score 0) if and only if the model's confidence (probability of being correct) exceeds $t$. This makes the confidence threshold explicit and auditable, unlike implicit penalties that vary across evaluations.

The authors argue this approach has two advantages over prior work on hallucination evaluation. First, explicit thresholds support objective grading—even if the specific $t$ values are somewhat arbitrary, all model creators can agree on the scoring rule. Second, incorporating confidence targets into **existing** mainstream evaluations (like SWE-bench) reduces the penalty for appropriate uncertainty expressions, amplifying the effectiveness of hallucination-specific evals rather than competing with them.

The paper introduces the concept of **behavioral calibration**: rather than requiring models to output probabilistic confidence estimates (which can be unnatural, e.g., "I'm 1/365 certain"), models should formulate responses they are at least $t$ confident in. This can be audited by comparing accuracy and error rates across thresholds, and it circumvents the problem that there may be exponentially many ways to phrase correct responses (Farquhar et al., 2024).

#### Design Choices and Alternatives

The paper makes several deliberate design choices that warrant explanation. First, the reduction uses a 50/50 mixture for the IIV distribution rather than the natural training distribution. This choice ensures the bound is symmetric and does not depend on the (unknown) error rate in the wild—using the natural distribution would make $err_{iiv}$ trivially small if errors are rare, obscuring the relationship between classification and generation errors.

Second, the threshold $1/|\mathcal{E}|$ is chosen because it represents the uniform distribution over errors. Alternative thresholds would change the constants in the bound but not the qualitative result—the key insight is that any fixed threshold creates a classifier, and classification error lower-bounds generation error.

Third, the paper focuses on modifying existing benchmarks rather than proposing new hallucination-specific evals. This is a strategic choice: the 2025 AI Index Report shows hallucination benchmarks struggle for adoption (Maslej et al., 2025), so changing the incentives in dominant benchmarks (GPQA, MMLU, SWE-bench) is more likely to influence model development than adding niche evals that model creators can ignore.

Fourth, the analysis assumes error-free training data ($p(\mathcal{E}) = 0$) to establish lower bounds. This is conservative—if training data contains errors (the realistic case), error rates would be higher, so the bounds still apply. The authors note this explicitly in Section 1.1: "With realistic training data containing shades of error, one may expect even higher error rates."

Finally, the paper does not distinguish between degrees of hallucination (one falsehood vs. many falsehoods in a response). Section 5 acknowledges this as a limitation—the formalism uses a false trichotomy (correct/incorrect/IDK) for simplicity, though open-ended generations with multiple errors could be handled by defining any response containing one or more falsehoods as an error.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper contributes three fundamental innovations that distinguish it from prior theoretical and engineering-focused work on hallucinations. Rather than offering incremental improvements to detection tools or model architectures, it reframes hallucinations as a statistical inevitability compounded by systemic incentive misalignment. The following insights explain why this shift in perspective is necessary for meaningful progress.

### 4.1 Generalizing the Statistical Lower Bound to Realistic Settings

The most significant theoretical advance is the generalization of the hallucination lower bound to include **prompts** and **abstentions**, overcoming limitations in prior theoretical work.

*   **Prior Limitation:** Previous theoretical analysis by Kalai and Vempala (2024) established a connection between hallucinations and missing mass estimates (Good, 1953). However, that work was a special case: it assumed no prompts (unconditional generation) and did not model the ability to abstain (say "I don't know"). This made the bounds difficult to apply to modern question-answering systems where context and uncertainty expression are critical.
*   **This Paper's Innovation:** The authors extend the reduction to the **Is-It-Valid (IIV)** problem with prompts (Section 3.2). By introducing a prompt distribution $\mu$ and explicitly including abstention responses in the set of valid outputs $\mathcal{V}_c$, the paper derives a bound that applies to realistic training pipelines:
    $$err \geq 2 \cdot err_{iiv} - \frac{\max_c |\mathcal{V}_c|}{\min_c |\mathcal{E}_c|} - \delta$$
    This generalization is crucial because it validates the theory against modern usage patterns. It proves that even if a model *could* abstain, the statistical pressure from pretraining (captured by $err_{iiv}$) pushes it toward errors unless the evaluation incentives change.
*   **Significance:** This moves the field from vague assertions that "hallucinations are inevitable" to precise, quantifiable lower bounds based on data properties (like the singleton rate $sr$). It demystifies hallucinations by showing they are not magical failures of neural networks, but standard errors in binary classification that arise from density estimation objectives (Section 1.1).

### 4.2 Diagnosing the "Epidemic of Penalizing Uncertainty"

While many papers identify *that* benchmarks are flawed, this work provides empirical evidence that **primary** benchmarks actively reinforce hallucinations, overriding specialized hallucination evaluations.

*   **Prior Limitation:** The community has responded to hallucinations by creating dedicated hallucination benchmarks (e.g., HalluLens, TruthfulQA). The implicit assumption was that adding these metrics would steer model development. However, the 2025 AI Index report notes these benchmarks "have struggled to gain traction" (Maslej et al., 2025).
*   **This Paper's Innovation:** The authors analyze the **dominant** leaderboards that actually drive model selection (Table 2, Section 4.1). They find that 9 out of 10 influential benchmarks (including GPQA, MMLU-Pro, SWE-bench) use **binary grading** where abstentions receive zero credit.
    > "Model B [which always guesses] will outperform A [which signals uncertainty] under 0-1 scoring, the basis of most current benchmarks." (Section 1.2)
    This creates an "epidemic" where optimizing for leaderboard performance mathematically requires guessing when uncertain (Observation 1, Section 4.1).
*   **Significance:** This insight explains *why* post-training techniques like RLHF have failed to eliminate hallucinations. Even if RLHF reduces falsehoods, the optimization objective remains aligned with binary accuracy. As long as the primary incentives reward guessing, specialized hallucination evals will be ignored by model developers focused on leaderboard rankings. This shifts the blame from model architecture to the evaluation ecosystem.

### 4.3 Reframing Mitigation: Behavioral Calibration via Explicit Confidence Targets

The proposed mitigation is innovative because it modifies the **scoring rule** of existing tests rather than proposing new tests or complex model architectural changes.

*   **Prior Limitation:** Existing mitigation strategies fall into two camps:
    1.  **Model-Centric:** Train models to output probabilistic confidence (e.g., "I am 80% sure"). This often leads to unnatural language and poor calibration (Section 5).
    2.  **Evaluation-Centric:** Add new hallucination benchmarks. As noted above, these struggle for adoption against primary benchmarks.
*   **This Paper's Innovation:** The authors propose appending **explicit confidence targets** to existing benchmark instructions (Section 4.2). For example:
    > "Answer only if you are $> t$ confident, since mistakes are penalized $t/(1-t)$ points..."
    This introduces **behavioral calibration**: the model is not required to output a probability number, but其行为 (behavior) must align with a confidence threshold (i.e., it should only answer when it is actually $> t$ likely to be correct).
*   **Significance:** This approach is pragmatically superior for two reasons:
    1.  **Adoption:** It modifies existing, high-traffic benchmarks (like SWE-bench) rather than asking the community to adopt niche evals.
    2.  **Auditability:** Behavioral calibration is easier to verify than probabilistic claims. One can measure accuracy on answered questions across different thresholds $t$ without needing the model to verbalize its confidence (Section 4.2).
    This represents a shift from trying to "fix the student" (the model) to "fixing the exam" (the benchmark), acknowledging that the current exam design is the root cause of the guessing behavior.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This paper is primarily theoretical, deriving mathematical bounds on hallucination rates rather than training new models. However, the authors support their theoretical claims with a **meta-evaluation of existing benchmarks**, **calibration analysis of prior model releases**, and **illustrative case studies** of state-of-the-art systems. This section details the methodology behind these empirical components, summarizes the specific quantitative findings, and critically assesses the strength of this evidence.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The empirical support in this paper comes from three distinct sources, each serving a different role in validating the theoretical framework.

#### 5.1.1 Benchmark Audit (Appendix F)
To substantiate the claim that "evaluations reinforce hallucination" (Section 4.1), the authors conducted a systematic audit of influential leaderboards. The methodology involved:
*   **Selection Criteria:** Benchmarks were chosen based on their presence in major leaderboards (e.g., HELM Capabilities, Open LLM Leaderboard) and model cards (e.g., OpenAI, Google DeepMind). The paper notes that two curated leaderboards had **50% overlap** in their selected evaluations, indicating a concentrated set of dominant metrics (Appendix F).
*   **Grading Analysis:** Each benchmark was analyzed for its scoring method, specifically checking for **binary grading** (correct/incorrect) and **IDK credit** (whether abstentions receive any score).
*   **Scope:** The audit covered **10 dominant benchmarks**, including GPQA, MMLU-Pro, SWE-bench, and Humanity's Last Exam (HLE). Detailed breakdowns were provided for HELM Capabilities (5 scenarios), Open LLM Leaderboard (6 benchmarks), and standalone benchmarks like SWE-bench (2,294 problems) and HLE (2,500 questions) (Appendix F.1–F.3).

#### 5.1.2 Calibration Re-analysis (Figure 2)
To support the claim that base models are calibrated (small $\delta$) but post-training deviates, the authors re-analyzed public data from **OpenAI (2023a)**.
*   **Data Source:** GPT-4 calibration histograms before and after reinforcement learning (Figure 2, Section 3.1).
*   **Task:** Multiple-choice queries with 4 plausible responses (A, B, C, D).
*   **Metric:** Comparison of predicted confidence vs. actual accuracy. The pretrained model showed well-calibrated histograms, while the post-trained model deviated, supporting the theoretical assumption that $\delta \approx 0$ for base models but may increase during alignment (Section 3.1).

#### 5.1.3 Illustrative Case Studies (Introduction & Section 3)
To ground the theory in concrete failures, the authors tested state-of-the-art models on specific prompts.
*   **Birthday Fact:** DeepSeek-V3 (600 B parameters) was queried 3 times for Adam Tauman Kalai's birthday with the instruction "If you know, just respond with DD-MM." All 3 responses were incorrect ("03-07", "15-06", "01-01"), despite the correct date being in Autumn (Section 1).
*   **Letter Counting:** DeepSeek-V3, Meta AI, and Claude 3.7 Sonnet were queried "How many Ds are in DEEPSEEK?" over **10 independent trials**. DeepSeek-V3 returned "2" or "3" in all trials; other models returned values as high as "6" or "7". In contrast, the reasoning model DeepSeek-R1 correctly counted using chain-of-thought (Section 1, Section 3.3.2).
*   **Dissertation Title:** Three models (ChatGPT/GPT-4o, DeepSeek, Llama) were asked for the title of Adam Kalai's dissertation. All generated incorrect titles and years (e.g., "Algebraic Methods in Interactive Machine Learning" at Harvard, 2005), whereas the correct title was "Probabilistic and on-line methods in machine learning" (CMU, 2001) (Table 1).

### 5.2 Quantitative Results

The empirical data provides specific magnitudes for the theoretical claims, particularly regarding the prevalence of binary grading and the persistence of errors.

#### 5.2.1 Benchmark Incentive Structure (Table 2)
The audit of 10 dominant benchmarks reveals a stark imbalance in how uncertainty is treated.
*   **Binary Grading:** **9 out of 10** benchmarks use strict binary grading (Yes/No). Only **WildBench** uses a non-binary rubric (1-10 scale).
*   **IDK Credit:** **9 out of 10** benchmarks give **None** for abstentions. WildBench offers **Partial** credit, but the rubric suggests IDK may score lower (3-4) than a "fair" response with hallucinations (5-6) (Section F.1).
*   **Specific Benchmarks:**
    *   **SWE-bench:** Grades 2,294 software engineering problems on patch success (pass/fail). No credit for indicating uncertainty (Appendix F.3).
    *   **HLE (Humanity's Last Exam):** 2,500 questions across dozens of fields. Reported scores were **below 30% accuracy**, with calibration error rates **above 70%** (Appendix F.3).
    *   **HELM Capabilities:** 4 out of 5 scenarios give virtually no credit for IDK. The fifth (WildBench) may penalize IDK relative to hallucinated answers (Appendix F.1).

#### 5.2.2 Error Rates in Case Studies
The illustrative examples demonstrate that hallucination rates are non-trivial even in high-capacity models.
*   **Birthday Hallucination:** **100% error rate** (3/3 attempts) on a specific factual query where the model was instructed to abstain if unsure (Section 1).
*   **Letter Counting:** **100% error rate** (10/10 trials) for DeepSeek-V3 on a simple counting task, despite the model having 600 B parameters. This supports the "Poor Models" hypothesis (Section 3.3.2) rather than just arbitrary facts, as the pattern is learnable but the tokenization obstructs it.
*   **Dissertation Title:** **100% error rate** across 3 different model families (OpenAI, DeepSeek, Meta), indicating this is not an isolated architectural flaw but a systemic issue (Table 1).

#### 5.2.3 Calibration Deviation (Figure 2)
The re-analysis of GPT-4 data shows a qualitative shift in probability assignments.
*   **Pretrained:** Histograms of predicted confidence match actual accuracy (well-calibrated).
*   **Post-trained:** Histograms shift, indicating the model assigns high confidence to incorrect answers more frequently after RLHF. This empirically supports the theoretical claim that post-training can increase the calibration term $\delta$ or simply optimize for accuracy over calibration (Section 3.1).

### 5.3 Assessment of Evidence

The empirical components effectively ground the theoretical claims, though they serve different purposes.

#### 5.3.1 Strengths
*   **Real-World Relevance:** The benchmark audit (Table 2) targets the **actual** metrics driving model development (e.g., those cited in OpenAI and Google model cards), not obscure academic proposals. This validates the "incentive gap" claim—that developers are rationally optimizing for binary accuracy because that is what leaderboards measure (Appendix F).
*   **Concrete Failure Modes:** The case studies (Table 1, Birthday) provide tangible examples of the theoretical bounds. For instance, the birthday example aligns with the **Arbitrary Facts** bound (Theorem 2), where singleton facts (rare birthdays) lead to errors. The letter-counting example aligns with the **Poor Models** bound (Theorem 3), where representational limits (tokenization) cause errors despite sufficient data.
*   **Calibration Support:** Figure 2 provides direct evidence that base models are calibrated (supporting the assumption $\delta \approx 0$ in Corollary 1) and that post-training disrupts this. This is crucial for the argument that hallucinations persist due to post-training incentives rather than just pretraining limitations.

#### 5.3.2 Limitations and Missing Experiments
*   **No Prospective Mitigation Test:** The paper proposes **explicit confidence targets** (Section 4.2) but does **not** empirically validate this solution. There is no experiment showing that modifying SWE-bench to include confidence penalties actually reduces hallucination rates in a trained model. The claim rests on theoretical incentive alignment (Observation 1) rather than empirical demonstration.
*   **Small Sample Size for Case Studies:** The illustrative examples (e.g., 3 birthday attempts, 10 letter-counting trials) are anecdotal. While they demonstrate existence, they do not provide statistical power to estimate the singleton rate $sr$ or the misclassification rate $err_{iiv}$ for these models. The paper cites follow-up work (Miao and Kearns, 2025) for empirical studies of singleton rates, acknowledging this gap (Section 3.3.1).
*   **Reliance on Cited Data:** The calibration analysis (Figure 2) relies on data from OpenAI (2023a), not new measurements. While credible, it limits the authors' ability to probe specific details (e.g., exact $\delta$ values) that would tighten the theoretical bounds.
*   **Binary Simplification:** The benchmark audit classifies grading as "Binary" or "Not Binary." In reality, some benchmarks (like WildBench) use LLM judges that may have nuanced behaviors. The paper acknowledges this, noting LM judges can incorrectly grade incorrect long responses as correct (Appendix F), which adds noise to the audit but does not invalidate the core finding that IDK is penalized.

### 5.4 Robustness and Failure Cases

The paper addresses potential counterarguments through its theoretical framework, but some empirical robustness checks are absent.

*   **Search and RAG:** The authors explicitly note that their incentive analysis holds even for models with Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). Observation 1 (Section 4.1) proves that if search fails to yield a confident answer, binary grading still rewards guessing. However, there is no empirical ablation comparing RAG models under binary vs. confidence-target scoring to verify this claim.
*   **Reasoning Models:** The letter-counting example contrasts DeepSeek-V3 (failure) with DeepSeek-R1 (success). This suggests that **reasoning capabilities** can mitigate the "Poor Models" factor (Section 3.3.2). However, the paper does not quantify whether reasoning models also succumb to the **Arbitrary Facts** bound (e.g., would R1 hallucinate birthdays at the singleton rate?). This remains an open empirical question.
*   **Calibration Error vs. Hallucination:** The analysis of HLE (Appendix F.3) notes that calibration error is not a proper hallucination metric. A model could hallucinate 100% of the time with 0 calibration error if it always claims 0% confidence. This highlights a limitation in using calibration alone as a proxy for truthfulness, reinforcing the need for the proposed behavioral calibration (Section 4.2).

In summary, the experimental analysis provides strong **corroborative evidence** for the theoretical claims, particularly through the benchmark audit and calibration re-analysis. However, it stops short of **validating the proposed solution**, leaving the effectiveness of explicit confidence targets as a hypothesis for future empirical work.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This paper provides a rigorous statistical framework for understanding hallucinations, but like all theoretical analyses, it relies on simplifying assumptions and leaves certain practical questions unanswered. Understanding these limitations is essential for correctly interpreting the bounds and for identifying where future work should focus. This section examines the assumptions underlying the analysis, the scenarios not addressed, scalability constraints, and open questions that remain.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions

The theoretical bounds depend on several key assumptions that determine when and how the results apply.

#### Assumption 1: Error-Free Training Data

The analysis assumes the training distribution $p$ contains no errors, i.e., $p(\mathcal{E}) = 0$ (Section 3.1). This is a **conservative assumption** designed to establish lower bounds: if hallucinations arise even with perfect training data, they will certainly persist with noisy data. The authors acknowledge this explicitly:

> "With realistic training data containing shades of error, one may expect even higher error rates than our lower bounds." (Section 1.1)

**Implication:** The bounds are **minimum** error rates, not expected rates. In practice, training corpora contain factual errors, conspiracy theories, and outdated information (Lin et al., 2022b; Levy et al., 2021), which would increase $err$ beyond the theoretical lower bound. This means the paper's bounds may **underestimate** real-world hallucination rates.

**Trade-off:** By assuming error-free data, the analysis isolates the statistical pressure from the training **objective** (density estimation) from the pressure introduced by training **data quality** (GIGO). This clarifies that hallucinations are not solely a data contamination problem, but it also means the bounds do not capture the full magnitude of the issue.

#### Assumption 2: Small Calibration Term $\delta$

The error bounds rely on the calibration term $\delta := |\hat{p}(A) - p(A)|$ being small (Section 3.1). The paper justifies this by showing that $\delta$ equals the derivative of the cross-entropy loss with respect to probability rescaling, so $\delta \neq 0$ would imply the model has not reached a local minimum:

$$\delta = \left| \frac{d}{ds} \mathcal{L}(\hat{p}_s) \bigg|_{s=1} \right|.$$

**Implication:** This assumption holds for **well-trained base models** optimized with cross-entropy loss, but may fail for:
- **Post-trained models** that deviate from cross-entropy due to RLHF objectives (Figure 2 shows post-trained GPT-4 is less calibrated than pretrained).
- **Models trained with alternative objectives** (e.g., direct preference optimization) that do not minimize cross-entropy.
- **Undertrained models** that have not converged to a local minimum.

**Trade-off:** The assumption enables clean mathematical bounds but limits applicability to the post-training regime where hallucinations are most problematic. The paper acknowledges this tension: base models are calibrated (small $\delta$) but post-trained models are not, yet post-training is where we expect hallucination reduction (Section 3.1, "Hallucinations are inevitable only for base models").

#### Assumption 3: Finite Plausible String Set $\mathcal{X}$

The analysis assumes $\mathcal{X}$ is finite for simplicity (Section 3.1, footnote 5). This is standard in computational learning theory but does not reflect the infinite space of possible text strings.

**Implication:** The bounds apply to a **discretized** view of language (e.g., tokens or fixed-length sequences), not the full continuous space of possible outputs. For open-ended generation tasks, the size of $\mathcal{E}$ (error strings) could be effectively infinite, making the threshold $1/|\mathcal{E}|$ approach zero.

**Trade-off:** The finite assumption simplifies the mathematics and makes the IIV reduction tractable, but it abstracts away the combinatorial explosion of possible hallucinations in open-ended contexts.

#### Assumption 4: Known Error Set Structure

The bounds depend on quantities like $|\mathcal{E}_c|$ (number of error responses per prompt) and $|\mathcal{V}_c|$ (number of valid responses). For birthday facts, $|\mathcal{E}_c| = 364$ is clear, but for open-ended questions, these quantities are ill-defined.

**Implication:** The theory applies most cleanly to **closed-world** settings (multiple-choice, factual queries with specific formats) rather than open-ended generation. The paper acknowledges this in Section 5:

> "For simplicity, the examples presented in this paper are oriented towards a single factual question. However, hallucinations often arise for open-ended prompts, such as 'Write a biography about....'" (Section 5)

**Trade-off:** The framework provides precise bounds for constrained tasks but requires extension to handle the full range of language model applications.

### 6.2 Scenarios and Edge Cases Not Addressed

Several important scenarios fall outside the scope of the analysis, either by design or due to inherent complexity.

#### Open-Ended Generations with Multiple Errors

The formalism treats a response as either valid or erroneous (a binary classification), but does not distinguish between **degrees of hallucination**. A response with one falsehood is treated the same as a response with ten falsehoods.

> "Our formalism does not distinguish between errors of different magnitudes or degrees of uncertainty. Clearly, the correct/incorrect/IDK categories are also incomplete." (Section 5)

**Why this matters:** In applications like medical advice or legal analysis, the **severity** of a hallucination matters more than its presence. A model that gets 9 out of 10 facts correct may be acceptable in some contexts but dangerous in others. The current framework cannot capture this nuance.

**Open question:** How should the bounds be extended to account for **partial correctness**? One approach would be to define $\mathcal{E}$ as responses exceeding an error threshold, but this introduces subjectivity in defining the threshold.

#### Latent Context and Hidden Intent

The analysis judges errors based solely on the prompt $c$ and response $r$, without considering **external context** that might affect correctness.

> "Some errors cannot be judged by the prompt and response alone. For example, suppose a user asks a question about phones and the language model provides a response about cellphones, but the question was intended to be about land lines." (Section 5)

**Why this matters:** Many real-world hallucinations arise from **ambiguous intent** rather than factual errors. A model might provide a factually correct answer to the wrong interpretation of the question, which the current framework would classify as valid.

**Open question:** Extending the model to include "hidden context" would require access to user intent signals or clarification dialogues, which are not part of standard benchmark evaluations.

#### Nuanced Uncertainty Expressions

The framework models abstention as a binary choice: either output a factual response or output "I don't know." In practice, there are many ways to signal uncertainty:

> "There are numerous ways to signal uncertainty, such as hedging, omitting details, and asking questions." (Section 5)

**Why this matters:** A model that says "I'm not entirely certain, but it might be..." is behaviorally different from one that says "I don't know," even though both express uncertainty. The current formalism treats all non-abstentions as confident answers, which may misclassify hedged responses.

**Open question:** How should the IIV classifier handle **graded uncertainty**? One approach would be to introduce multiple thresholds corresponding to different confidence levels, but this complicates the reduction and the resulting bounds.

#### Reasoning and Search-Augmented Models

The paper explicitly states that the analysis applies to models with Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and reasoning capabilities (Section 5). However, it does not quantify **how much** these techniques reduce the singleton rate $sr$ or the misclassification rate $err_{iiv}$.

> "Search (and reasoning) are not panaceas.... Moreover, search may not help with miscalculations such as in the letter-counting example, or other intrinsic hallucinations." (Section 5)

**Why this matters:** The letter-counting case study shows DeepSeek-R1 (a reasoning model) succeeds where DeepSeek-V3 fails (Section 3.3.2). This suggests reasoning can mitigate the "Poor Models" factor but not necessarily the "Arbitrary Facts" factor. The paper does not provide bounds for reasoning models specifically.

**Open question:** Does chain-of-thought reasoning reduce $err_{iiv}$ by making the IIV classification problem easier (e.g., by decomposing it into verifiable sub-steps)? Empirical work is needed to answer this.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

While the paper is primarily theoretical, several practical constraints affect the applicability of its proposals.

#### Computing the Threshold $1/|\mathcal{E}|$

The IIV classifier requires thresholding at $1/|\mathcal{E}|$ (or $1/\min_c |\mathcal{E}_c|$ with prompts). For factual queries like birthdays, $|\mathcal{E}| = 364$ is known. For open-ended questions, $|\mathcal{E}|$ is **unknown and potentially infinite**.

**Implication:** The exact threshold cannot be computed for general tasks, making the IIV reduction difficult to apply as a diagnostic tool. The paper notes this is not necessary for the **lower bounds** to be meaningful (Section 3.1), but it limits practical auditing.

**Workaround:** The paper suggests using approximate thresholds or empirical estimates, but this introduces additional error terms not captured in the theoretical bounds.

#### Auditing Behavioral Calibration

The proposed mitigation (explicit confidence targets) requires auditing models across multiple thresholds $t$ to verify behavioral calibration (Section 4.2). This requires:

- Running the model on many examples at each threshold.
- Computing accuracy on answered questions (excluding abstentions).
- Comparing accuracy across thresholds to detect miscalibration.

**Scalability concern:** For large benchmarks like SWE-bench (2,294 problems) or HLE (2,500 questions), this auditing process is computationally expensive, especially if repeated across multiple $t$ values (e.g., $t = 0.5, 0.75, 0.9$).

**Trade-off:** The paper prioritizes **auditability** over efficiency, arguing that objective scoring is more important than computational cost. However, this may limit adoption in resource-constrained settings.

#### Benchmark Modification Overhead

The proposal to modify existing benchmarks with confidence targets requires coordination across the AI community:

> "This is a socio-technical problem in the sense that, not only do the existing evaluations need to be modified, but these changes need to be adopted in the influential leaderboards." (Section 4)

**Practical constraint:** Leaderboards like the Open LLM Leaderboard and HELM are maintained by different organizations with different update cycles. Achieving consensus on confidence thresholds (e.g., should $t = 0.75$ be standard?) requires community coordination that may be slow or contentious.

**Trade-off:** The paper argues that modifying existing benchmarks is more likely to drive change than creating new hallucination-specific evals (which "have struggled to gain traction" per Maslej et al., 2025). However, this still requires overcoming institutional inertia.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

Several weaknesses in the analysis point to directions for future work.

#### No Empirical Validation of Confidence Targets

The most significant gap is the lack of **empirical validation** for the proposed mitigation. The paper proves that binary grading incentivizes guessing (Observation 1) and argues that confidence targets would realign incentives, but it does not:

- Train a model with confidence-target scoring and measure hallucination reduction.
- Compare hallucination rates under binary vs. confidence-target grading on the same benchmark.
- Demonstrate that behavioral calibration is achievable in practice.

> "The paper proposes explicit confidence targets (Section 4.2) but does not empirically validate this solution." (Section 5.3.2)

**Why this matters:** Theoretical incentive alignment does not guarantee practical effectiveness. Models may find ways to "game" the confidence targets (e.g., by abstaining on easy questions to preserve accuracy on hard ones), or the penalties may be too weak to overcome optimization pressure from other objectives.

**Open question:** What penalty magnitude is required to meaningfully reduce hallucinations without degrading overall performance? The paper suggests $t/(1-t)$ but does not test this empirically.

#### Singleton Rate Estimation

The Arbitrary Facts bound (Theorem 2) depends on the singleton rate $sr$, which measures the fraction of prompts appearing exactly once in training data. However:

- $sr$ is **not directly observable** for proprietary models (training data is often secret).
- Even for open models, computing $sr$ requires access to the full training corpus, which may be petabytes in size.
- The paper cites follow-up work (Miao and Kearns, 2025) for empirical studies of singleton rates, acknowledging this gap (Section 3.3.1).

**Implication:** The bound is **theoretically precise** but **practically difficult to apply** for auditing specific models. One cannot compute the expected hallucination rate without knowing $sr$.

**Open question:** Can $sr$ be estimated from model outputs alone (e.g., via consistency checks across multiple samples), or is training data access required?

#### Calibration Error vs. Hallucination Distinction

The paper notes that calibration error is not a proper hallucination metric (Appendix F.3):

> "A model could hallucinate 100% of the time with 0 calibration error if it always generates incorrect and indicated 0% confidence in each answer." (Appendix F.3)

**Weakness:** This creates ambiguity in interpreting calibration metrics. A model with good calibration may still hallucinate frequently if it correctly reports low confidence in its errors. Conversely, a model with poor calibration may be highly accurate but overconfident.

**Open question:** How should calibration and hallucination metrics be combined to provide a unified measure of trustworthiness? The paper proposes behavioral calibration as a partial answer but does not provide a composite metric.

#### Treatment of Post-Training Techniques

The analysis treats post-training as a black box that may increase $\delta$ (calibration error) but does not model specific techniques like RLHF, DPO, or RLAIF in detail.

> "Post-trained models which may deviate from cross-entropy in favor of reinforcement learning." (Section 3.1)

**Weakness:** Different post-training methods may have different effects on hallucination rates. For example, RLHF optimized for helpfulness may increase hallucinations, while RLHF optimized for truthfulness may decrease them. The current framework does not distinguish between these cases.

**Open question:** Can the IIV reduction be extended to model the post-training objective explicitly, providing bounds on how much RLHF can reduce $err$ given a fixed $err_{iiv}$?

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

The table below summarizes the key trade-offs in the paper's approach:

| **Design Choice** | **Benefit** | **Cost** |
|-------------------|-------------|----------|
| Error-free training data assumption | Establishes clean lower bounds | Underestimates real-world error rates |
| Binary valid/error classification | Enables mathematical reduction | Ignores degrees of hallucination |
| Focus on existing benchmarks | Higher adoption potential | Requires community coordination |
| Behavioral calibration (vs. probabilistic) | More natural language output | Harder to verify than explicit probabilities |
| Finite $\mathcal{X}$ assumption | Tractable analysis | Does not capture open-ended generation |
| No empirical validation of mitigation | Faster theoretical contribution | Unproven practical effectiveness |

### 6.6 Directions for Future Work

Based on the limitations identified, several research directions emerge:

1. **Empirical validation of confidence targets:** Train models with explicit confidence penalties and measure hallucination rates compared to binary-graded baselines.

2. **Singleton rate estimation:** Develop methods to estimate $sr$ from model outputs or public data, enabling practical application of Theorem 2.

3. **Extended formalism for open-ended generation:** Generalize the IIV reduction to handle responses with multiple facts, where errors can be partial rather than binary.

4. **Post-training modeling:** Incorporate specific post-training objectives (RLHF, DPO) into the theoretical framework to bound their effect on $err$ and $\delta$.

5. **Reasoning model analysis:** Quantify how chain-of-thought and other reasoning techniques affect $err_{iiv}$ and whether they can overcome the Arbitrary Facts bound.

6. **Community coordination mechanisms:** Develop practical processes for updating leaderboards with confidence targets, including threshold selection and audit protocols.

In conclusion, while this paper provides a rigorous foundation for understanding hallucinations, it is best viewed as a **starting point** rather than a complete solution. The theoretical bounds clarify why hallucinations arise, and the incentive analysis explains why they persist, but significant empirical and engineering work remains to translate these insights into trustworthy AI systems.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper fundamentally reframes hallucinations from a mysterious architectural flaw to a predictable outcome of statistical objectives and misaligned incentives. This shift changes not only how we diagnose errors but also how we design the ecosystem surrounding language models. The implications extend beyond theoretical bounds to concrete changes in benchmark design, model evaluation, and deployment strategies.

### 7.1 Shifting the Research Landscape: From Detection to Incentive Alignment

The most profound implication of this work is the realization that **detection is insufficient without incentive realignment**. Prior research has heavily invested in hallucination detection tools (e.g., semantic entropy, self-consistency checks) and specialized benchmarks (e.g., HalluLens). However, Table 2 (Section 4.1) demonstrates that 9 out of 10 dominant benchmarks still use binary grading that penalizes uncertainty.

This creates a systemic contradiction: researchers may develop models that *can* express uncertainty, but the leaderboards that drive funding and adoption reward models that *guess*. The paper argues that the field must pivot from asking "How do we detect hallucinations?" to "How do we stop rewarding them?"

*   **The "Exam Design" Metaphor:** Just as educational reformers realized that multiple-choice exams encourage guessing, AI researchers must recognize that binary accuracy metrics encourage hallucination. The solution is not to add a separate "honesty test" that models can ignore, but to modify the primary exams (GPQA, MMLU, SWE-bench) to include penalties for incorrect guesses (Section 4.2).
*   **Socio-Technical Mitigation:** The paper explicitly labels this a "socio-technical" problem (Section 1.2). Technical fixes (like better training data) are undermined by social structures (leaderboards). Future work must address the governance of benchmarks—who decides the scoring rules, and how are changes adopted across the community?

### 7.2 Concrete Research Avenues

The theoretical framework opens several specific lines of inquiry that move beyond the paper's current scope. These directions address the gaps identified in Section 6 while leveraging the tools developed in Section 3.

#### 7.2.1 Empirical Validation of Confidence Targets
The paper proposes explicit confidence targets (Section 4.2) but acknowledges the lack of empirical validation (Section 6.4). Follow-up research should:
*   **A/B Testing Scoring Rules:** Train identical models using binary grading vs. confidence-target grading (e.g., penalty $t/(1-t)$ for errors). Measure not just accuracy, but the rate of appropriate abstentions.
*   **Threshold Sensitivity:** Determine optimal values for $t$. The paper suggests $t=0.5, 0.75, 0.9$ as natural candidates, but real-world applications may require different risk tolerances. For medical advice, $t=0.9$ (penalty 9) might be appropriate; for creative writing, $t=0.5$ (penalty 1) may suffice.
*   **Behavioral Calibration Audits:** Develop standardized protocols to measure behavioral calibration across thresholds. Instead of asking models "Are you confident?", auditors should measure accuracy *conditional on answering* at different implicit confidence levels.

#### 7.2.2 Estimating the Singleton Rate ($sr$)
Theorem 2 (Section 3.3.1) ties hallucination rates to the singleton rate $sr$ (the fraction of facts appearing exactly once in training). However, $sr$ is difficult to compute for proprietary models. Research should focus on:
*   **Proxy Metrics:** Can $sr$ be estimated from model outputs alone? For example, measuring consistency across multiple samples might reveal which facts are "singleton-like" (high variance) vs. "memorized" (low variance).
*   **Data Contamination Studies:** Quantify how training data contamination affects $sr$. If test questions leak into training data, $sr$ drops, and hallucination bounds tighten. This provides a theoretical justification for strict data hygiene in benchmarking.

#### 7.2.3 Modeling Post-Training Objectives
The paper notes that post-training (e.g., RLHF) can increase the calibration term $\delta$ (Section 3.1, Figure 2). Future theory should:
*   **Extend the Reduction to RLHF:** Incorporate the reward model into the IIV framework. Does RLHF optimize for $err_{iiv}$ (validity classification) or purely for $err$ (generative error)? The paper suggests RLHF often optimizes for helpfulness, which may conflict with validity.
*   **Quantify the Trade-off:** Establish bounds on how much hallucination reduction is possible via post-training given a fixed pretraining $err_{iiv}$. This would help practitioners decide when to stop fine-tuning and start modifying evaluations.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Integration Guidance

For practitioners deploying language models, this paper offers actionable guidance on when to trust models and how to configure evaluations.

#### 7.3.1 When to Prefer Confidence-Targeted Evaluations
Not all applications require the same level of certainty. Use the following criteria to decide whether to implement confidence targets:

| **Application Type** | **Risk of Hallucination** | **Recommended Scoring** | **Rationale** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **High-Stakes (Medical, Legal)** | Critical | **Explicit Penalty ($t \geq 0.9$)** | False positives cause harm. Abstention is safer than guessing. |
| **Search & Retrieval (RAG)** | Moderate | **Implicit Penalty** | Search reduces $sr$, but binary grading still rewards guessing when search fails (Section 5.4). |
| **Creative Writing** | Low | **Binary ($t = 0$)** | Hallucination (fiction) is acceptable or desired. No penalty needed. |
| **Code Generation (SWE-bench)** | High | **Explicit Penalty** | Incorrect patches break builds. IDK is better than broken code. |

*   **Implementation Tip:** To implement confidence targets without retraining, use system prompts that explicitly state the penalty structure (Section 4.2):
    > "Answer only if you are > 75% confident. Mistakes are penalized 3 points; correct answers receive 1 point; 'I don't know' receives 0 points."

#### 7.3.2 Interpreting Base Model vs. Post-Trained Behavior
The calibration analysis (Figure 2, Section 3.1) suggests base models are often better calibrated than post-trained models.
*   **Use Base Models for Uncertainty Estimation:** If your application requires reliable confidence scores (e.g., routing queries to humans), consider using the base model's probabilities rather than the post-trained model's outputs.
*   **Caution with RLHF:** Be aware that RLHF may optimize for "helpfulness" at the cost of truthfulness. Monitor the calibration term $\delta$ during post-training. If $\delta$ increases significantly, the model may be becoming overconfident.

#### 7.3.3 Limitations of Search and Reasoning
Practicians often rely on Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) or reasoning models (like DeepSeek-R1) to fix hallucinations. This paper clarifies their limits:
*   **RAG Does Not Solve Incentives:** Even with search, if the evaluation metric is binary, the model will guess when search fails (Observation 1, Section 4.1). RAG reduces $sr$ by providing context, but it does not change the incentive to guess.
*   **Reasoning Helps "Poor Models" Only:** Reasoning chains help with representational limits (e.g., letter counting, Section 3.3.2) but do not solve "Arbitrary Facts" (e.g., birthdays) where the information is simply absent (Section 5.4). Do not expect reasoning to eliminate hallucinations on unseen facts.

### 7.4 Long-Term Vision: Behavioral Calibration as a Standard

The ultimate goal of this research direction is to make **behavioral calibration** a standard metric alongside accuracy. Just as we now report latency and throughput, future model cards should report:
*   **Accuracy at Threshold $t=0.5$**
*   **Accuracy at Threshold $t=0.9$**
*   **Abstention Rate**

This shift acknowledges that a model's utility depends on *when* it speaks, not just *what* it says. By adopting the modifications proposed in Section 4.2, the field can steer toward systems that are not only capable but also trustworthy—models that know what they know, and crucially, know when to say "I don't know."
