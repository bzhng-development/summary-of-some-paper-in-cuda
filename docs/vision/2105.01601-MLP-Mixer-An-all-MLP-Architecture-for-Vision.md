## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces `MLP-Mixer`, a vision architecture composed exclusively of multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) that removes both convolutional layers and self-attention mechanisms. It challenges the assumption that convolutions or attention are necessary for state-of-the-art computer vision, showing that simple MLPs applied across image patches and channels suffice when scaled properly. Pre-trained on the JFT-300M dataset, the `Mixer-H/14` model reaches 87.94% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet ILSVRC2012 with inference costs competitive with Vision Transformers and CNNs (Table 2).

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of `MLP-Mixer`, we must first examine the historical trajectory of computer vision architectures and the specific theoretical questions this paper seeks to answer. The field has long been dominated by specific architectural choices that are often treated as necessities rather than design options.

### The Dominance of Convolutional Neural Networks
For over a decade, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have served as the "de-facto standard for computer vision" (Section 1). The success of models like AlexNet [24] established a paradigm where **convolutional layers** are essential for processing visual data. 

CNNs rely heavily on **inductive biases**—assumptions built into the model architecture that help it learn specific patterns without seeing every possible example. In the context of CNNs, the primary inductive biases are:
*   **Locality:** Convolutional kernels (e.g., $3 \times 3$) assume that pixels close to each other are more related than distant pixels.
*   **Translation Invariance:** The same filter is applied across the entire image, assuming features (like edges or textures) are meaningful regardless of their position.

While effective, these biases are "hand-crafted visual features" (Section 1) embedded in the math of convolution. The field has spent years optimizing these structures (e.g., ResNets with skip-connections [15], EfficientNets), but the core operation remains the convolution.

### The Challenger: Vision Transformers
Recently, the **Vision Transformer (ViT)** [14] challenged CNN dominance by replacing convolutions with **self-attention layers**. ViT represents a shift toward removing inductive biases:
*   It treats an image as a sequence of patches, similar to words in a sentence.
*   Self-attention allows every patch to interact with every other patch immediately, removing the strict locality bias of CNNs.

However, ViT introduces its own complexities. As noted in Section 2, the computational complexity of self-attention is **quadratic** in the number of input patches. If an image is split into $S$ patches, the attention mechanism typically scales as $O(S^2)$. This makes high-resolution processing computationally expensive. Furthermore, ViT models often require massive datasets (e.g., JFT-300M) to overcome the lack of inductive biases that help CNNs generalize from smaller data.

### The Core Research Question
The central gap this paper addresses is fundamental: **Are convolutions or attention mechanisms actually necessary for high-performance vision?**

> "In this paper we show that while convolutions and attention are both sufﬁcient for good performance, neither of them are necessary." (Abstract)

Prior to this work, attempts to use purely fully-connected networks (MLPs) for vision existed but were limited in scope:
*   **Lin et al. [27]:** Achieved reasonable performance on CIFAR-10 using fully connected networks but relied heavily on auto-encoder pre-training.
*   **Neyshabur [30]:** Trained fully-connected networks on small-scale tasks using custom regularization.

These prior efforts did not demonstrate that MLPs could compete with state-of-the-art CNNs or Transformers on large-scale benchmarks like ImageNet when scaled appropriately. The community lacked evidence that a simple MLP-based architecture could reach the "accuracy/cost trade-off" of modern models (Abstract).

### Positioning MLP-Mixer
`MLP-Mixer` positions itself as a **conceptually and technically simple alternative** (Section 1) that strips away the complexity of both convolutions and attention. Its motivation is twofold:

1.  **Theoretical Simplicity:** The architecture relies only on basic matrix multiplication routines, data layout changes (reshapes and transpositions), and scalar nonlinearities (Section 1). By removing specialized layers like convolutions (which require costly reduction to matrix multiplication) or attention (which requires quadratic memory), the authors test whether raw learning capacity from data can substitute for architectural inductive biases.
2.  **Computational Efficiency:** Unlike ViT, `MLP-Mixer` maintains **linear complexity** with respect to the number of input patches. As described in Section 2, the token-mixing MLP maps $\mathbb{R}^S \mapsto \mathbb{R}^S$ with a hidden width $D_S$ selected independently of the number of patches $S$. This avoids the quadratic bottleneck of self-attention, potentially offering better inference throughput on hardware optimized for matrix multiplications (TPUs).

The paper aims to "spark further research beyond the realms of well established CNNs and Transformers" (Abstract). By demonstrating that an "all-MLP" architecture can attain competitive scores when trained on large datasets (Section 1), it forces a re-evaluation of what components are truly critical for visual representation learning.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an architecture design paper that proposes a novel neural network structure for image classification. The core idea is to replace both convolutional layers and self-attention mechanisms with only multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs), while maintaining competitive performance through careful separation of spatial and channel-wise information mixing.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

MLP-Mixer is a neural network that processes images by splitting them into fixed-size patches and then applying two alternating types of simple fully-connected layers: one that mixes information across different image locations, and one that mixes information across different feature channels. It solves the problem of visual representation learning without relying on the specialized operations (convolutions or attention) that have dominated computer vision for over a decade, instead demonstrating that basic matrix multiplication applied in structured ways suffices when scaled appropriately.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The MLP-Mixer architecture consists of three major stages arranged sequentially. First, a **patch embedding stem** takes an input image and divides it into non-overlapping square patches, then linearly projects each patch into a fixed-dimensional vector, producing a table of shape "number of patches × hidden dimension." Second, a stack of identical **Mixer layers** processes this table, where each Mixer layer contains two sub-components: a token-mixing MLP that operates across spatial locations (columns of the table) and a channel-mixing MLP that operates across feature dimensions (rows of the table), with skip-connections and layer normalization surrounding each sub-component. Third, a **classification head** applies global average pooling across all patches to produce a single feature vector, then passes it through a final linear layer to produce class logits. Information flows unidirectionally from the raw image through the patch embedding, through multiple Mixer layers that progressively transform the representations, and finally to the classification output (Figure 1).

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first explain how raw images are converted into the patch-based input representation, since this determines the fundamental data structure that all subsequent layers operate on.
- We then detail the token-mixing MLP mechanism, which is the novel component responsible for spatial information exchange and distinguishes Mixer from standard MLPs.
- We next describe the channel-mixing MLP, which performs feature transformation similar to standard network layers but with explicit parameter sharing.
- We explain how these two MLP types are combined into a complete Mixer layer with normalization and skip-connections.
- We cover the model scaling configurations from Table 1 to show how the architecture adapts to different compute budgets.
- We conclude with training and fine-tuning procedures, including the critical technique for handling resolution changes during transfer learning.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Input Representation and Patch Embedding**

The architecture begins by converting a 2D image into a sequence of vectors that can be processed by standard matrix operations. Given an input image with height $H$ and width $W$, the model divides it into $S$ non-overlapping square patches, each with resolution $P \times P$ pixels. The number of patches is calculated as $S = HW / P^2$, which means a $224 \times 224$ image with $16 \times 16$ patches produces $S = 196$ patches (Table 1, "B/16" configuration). Each patch is flattened into a 1D vector and then linearly projected using a shared projection matrix to a hidden dimension $C$, which varies by model scale from 512 for small models to 1280 for the largest model (Table 1). This produces a 2D real-valued input table $X \in \mathbb{R}^{S \times C}$, where each row corresponds to one image patch and each column corresponds to one feature channel across all patches. Critically, all patches use the same projection matrix, which provides a form of translation invariance similar to convolutional weight sharing. Unlike Vision Transformers, MLP-Mixer does not add learnable position embeddings to the patch embeddings because the token-mixing MLPs described below are inherently sensitive to the order of input tokens (Section 2).

**Token-Mixing MLP: Spatial Information Exchange**

The token-mixing MLP is responsible for allowing different spatial locations (patches) to communicate with each other, which is essential for building global image representations. This MLP operates on the columns of the input table $X$, meaning it processes all $S$ patches for one feature channel at a time. To implement this, the architecture transposes the input so that each column becomes a row, applies the MLP independently to each row, then transposes back. Formally, for each channel $i$ from 1 to $C$, the token-mixing MLP computes:

$$U_{*,i} = X_{*,i} + W_2 \sigma(W_1 \text{LayerNorm}(X)_{*,i})$$

where $X_{*,i}$ denotes the $i$-th column of $X$ (all patches for channel $i$), $W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{D_S \times S}$ is the first weight matrix, $W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{S \times D_S}$ is the second weight matrix, $\sigma$ is the GELU nonlinearity, and $D_S$ is a tunable hidden dimension for the token-mixing MLP (Equation 1, Section 2). The key design choice here is that the same weight matrices $W_1$ and $W_2$ are shared across all $C$ channels, meaning every feature channel uses identical mixing patterns across spatial locations. This parameter tying is unusual because it prevents the model from learning channel-specific spatial relationships, but it provides significant memory savings and surprisingly does not hurt empirical performance (Section 2, paragraph on parameter tying). The hidden dimension $D_S$ is selected independently of the number of input patches $S$, which ensures the computational complexity remains linear in $S$ rather than quadratic like self-attention (Section 2). For example, in the Mixer-B/16 configuration, $S = 196$ but $D_S = 384$, and these values scale independently across model sizes (Table 1).

**Channel-Mixing MLP: Feature Transformation**

The channel-mixing MLP performs feature transformation at each spatial location independently, similar to how $1 \times 1$ convolutions or standard MLP layers operate in other architectures. This MLP acts on the rows of the input table, meaning it processes all $C$ feature channels for one patch at a time without mixing information across different patches. Formally, for each patch $j$ from 1 to $S$, the channel-mixing MLP computes:

$$Y_{j,*} = U_{j,*} + W_4 \sigma(W_3 \text{LayerNorm}(U)_{j,*})$$

where $U_{j,*}$ denotes the $j$-th row of $U$ (all channels for patch $j$), $W_3 \in \mathbb{R}^{D_C \times C}$ is the first weight matrix, $W_4 \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times D_C}$ is the second weight matrix, and $D_C$ is a tunable hidden dimension for the channel-mixing MLP (Equation 1, Section 2). The same weight matrices $W_3$ and $W_4$ are shared across all $S$ patches, which provides positional invariance—a prominent feature of convolutions where the same filter applies everywhere in the image (Section 2). This is a natural design choice because it allows the model to learn feature transformations that are meaningful regardless of where they appear in the image. The hidden dimension $D_C$ is typically larger than $D_S$ and scales with the model size; for instance, Mixer-B/16 uses $D_C = 3072$ while Mixer-H/14 uses $D_C = 5120$ (Table 1). Since $D_C$ is independent of the patch size, the overall computational complexity remains linear in the number of pixels in the image, matching typical CNN behavior (Section 2).

**Complete Mixer Layer Structure**

A single Mixer layer combines both the token-mixing and channel-mixing MLPs with normalization and residual connections to form a complete processing unit. The layer first applies layer normalization to the input, then passes it through the token-mixing MLP with a skip-connection, producing an intermediate representation $U$. It then applies layer normalization to $U$, passes it through the channel-mixing MLP with another skip-connection, producing the final output $Y$ (Equation 1, Section 2). This ordering means spatial mixing happens before feature mixing within each layer, though the paper does not ablate this specific ordering choice. Each MLP block within the layer contains exactly two fully-connected layers with one GELU nonlinearity between them, as shown in Figure 1. The architecture uses standard layer normalization applied on the channels dimension, consistent with Transformer architectures. Skip-connections are applied around each MLP block separately (not around the entire Mixer layer), which helps with gradient flow during training of deep networks. The JAX/Flax implementation code in Supplementary E shows this structure explicitly, with `MixerBlock` containing two sequential applications of `MlpBlock` separated by normalization and addition operations.

**Model Scaling Configurations**

The paper evaluates seven different model scales ranging from small to huge, following naming conventions similar to Vision Transformers (Table 1). The "B", "L", and "H" suffixes denote Base, Large, and Huge model scales respectively, while the number suffix (e.g., "/16", "/32", "/14") indicates the patch resolution $P \times P$. Smaller patch sizes produce more patches ($S$ increases), which increases computational cost but may capture finer-grained spatial information. The number of Mixer layers ranges from 8 for small models (S/32, S/16) to 32 for the largest model (H/14). Hidden dimension $C$ scales from 512 to 1280, while the token-mixing MLP dimension $D_S$ scales from 256 to 640, and the channel-mixing MLP dimension $D_C$ scales from 2048 to 5120. Total parameters range from 18 million for S/16 to 431 million for H/14, not including the classifier head weights (Table 1). The "isotropic" design means all layers maintain the same hidden dimension $C$ throughout the network, unlike most CNNs which use a pyramidal structure with decreasing resolution and increasing channels in deeper layers (Section 2). This design choice aligns Mixer more closely with Transformers and deep RNNs, which also use fixed width throughout their depth.

**Training and Optimization Details**

All models are pre-trained at resolution 224 using the Adam optimizer with $\beta_1 = 0.9$ and $\beta_2 = 0.999$ (Section 3, Pre-training). The learning rate schedule uses linear warmup for 10,000 steps followed by linear decay to zero. Batch size is fixed at 4,096 across all pre-training runs, and gradient clipping is applied at global norm 1 to prevent exploding gradients. Weight decay is used during pre-training but not during fine-tuning (Section 3, Fine-tuning). For large-scale pre-training on JFT-300M, images are pre-processed using random cropping from Szegedy et al. [45] plus random horizontal flipping. For ImageNet and ImageNet-21k pre-training, additional regularization techniques are employed including RandAugment with magnitude swept in $\{0, 10, 15, 20\}$, mixup with strength swept in $\{0.0, 0.2, 0.5, 0.8\}$, dropout at rates $\{0.0, 0.1\}$, and stochastic depth with drop probability linearly increasing from 0.0 to $s \in \{0.0, 0.1\}$ across layers (Supplementary B, Table 4). These regularization techniques are necessary because Mixer tends to overfit without them, similar to observations for ViT (Section 3.1).

**Fine-tuning and Resolution Adaptation**

Fine-tuning uses momentum SGD with batch size 512, gradient clipping at global norm 1, and a cosine learning rate schedule with linear warmup (Section 3, Fine-tuning). A critical technical challenge arises when fine-tuning at higher resolutions than used during pre-training: since patch size remains fixed, higher resolution images produce more patches ($S$ increases to $S'$), but the token-mixing MLP weight matrix $W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{D_S \times S}$ expects a specific input dimension $S$ (Section 3, Fine-tuning). The paper solves this by increasing the hidden layer width from $D_S$ to $D_S'$ in proportion to the number of patches, then initializing the new larger weight matrix $W_2' \in \mathbb{R}^{D_S' \times S'}$ with a block-diagonal matrix containing copies of the original $W_2$ on its diagonal (Section 3, Fine-tuning). This initialization scheme allows the model to process the larger sequence by effectively applying the pre-trained mixing pattern independently to $K^2$ spatial regions, where $K$ is the integer resolution increase factor. This particular scheme only allows for $S' = K^2 S$ with $K \in \mathbb{N}$, meaning resolution can only increase by integer factors (Section 3, Fine-tuning). On the VTAB-1k benchmark, models are fine-tuned at resolution 224 for datasets with small input images and 448 for datasets with large input images, following the BiT-HyperRule [22] (Section 3, Fine-tuning).

**Design Choices and Alternatives Considered**

The paper explicitly documents several design alternatives that were tested but did not improve performance, providing valuable negative results for the research community (Supplementary A). The authors tested untying the token-mixing MLP parameters so that each of the $C$ channels would have independent weights instead of sharing one MLP across all channels, but observed no noticeable improvements despite multiplying the parameter count by $C$ (Supplementary A.1). They also tested grouping channels together by concatenating $G$ neighboring columns before token-mixing, which would allow the MLP to look at several channels simultaneously when mixing tokens, but this improved linear 5-shot top-1 accuracy on ImageNet by less than 1-2% (Supplementary A.1). A more sophisticated version using $G$ different learned "views" on every token improved top-5 ImageNet accuracy by 3-4% for the smallest Mixer-S/32 architecture but showed no improvements for larger scales (Supplementary A.1). The authors also experimented with pyramidal architectures where token-mixing MLPs reduce the number of tokens in deeper layers, similar to pyramidal ViTs [52], but were unable to transfer these findings from JFT-300M to ImageNet or ImageNet-21k (Supplementary A.1). During fine-tuning, they tested mixup and Polyak averaging but found these did not lead to consistent improvements, so they were dropped (Supplementary A.2). These ablation studies demonstrate that the simple, parameter-sharing design is not merely a computational shortcut but actually represents an effective architectural choice.

**Computational Complexity Analysis**

The computational complexity of MLP-Mixer is linear in the number of input patches, which distinguishes it fundamentally from Vision Transformers. The token-mixing MLP maps $\mathbb{R}^S \mapsto \mathbb{R}^S$ with hidden width $D_S$ selected independently of $S$, so the complexity scales as $O(S \cdot D_S)$ rather than $O(S^2)$ for self-attention (Section 2). Since $D_S$ does not grow with $S$, the overall complexity is linear in the number of patches. The channel-mixing MLP operates independently on each of the $S$ patches with complexity $O(C \cdot D_C)$ per patch, giving total complexity $O(S \cdot C \cdot D_C)$. Since $S = HW/P^2$ and $C$ is independent of patch size, the overall complexity is linear in the number of pixels $HW$, matching typical CNN behavior (Section 2). This linear complexity enables higher throughput during inference: Mixer-H/14 achieves 40 images/sec/core on TPU-v3 while ViT-H/14 achieves only 15 images/sec/core, making Mixer 2.5 times faster despite similar accuracy (Table 2). The architecture relies only on basic matrix multiplication routines without the additional costly reduction operations required for convolutions or the quadratic memory requirements of attention (Section 1).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

Beyond the specific architectural blueprint, `MLP-Mixer` offers broader theoretical and practical insights into deep learning design. These contributions challenge established assumptions about what components are strictly necessary for computer vision. The following points distinguish between fundamental innovations—changes in how we conceptualize vision models—and incremental improvements in performance or efficiency.

### 1. Explicit Decoupling of Spatial and Channel Mixing
Most successful vision architectures prior to this work couple spatial mixing (interacting across image locations) and channel mixing (interacting across feature dimensions) within the same operation. 
*   **CNNs:** A standard $3 \times 3$ convolution simultaneously aggregates information from neighboring spatial locations and across input channels.
*   **Vision Transformers (ViT):** Self-attention layers mix spatial information (via attention maps) and channel information (via Query/Key/Value projections) jointly.

`MLP-Mixer` introduces a fundamental architectural innovation by **strictly separating** these two operations into distinct layers. As described in the technical approach, token-mixing MLPs operate *only* across spatial locations (columns of the input table), while channel-mixing MLPs operate *only* across features (rows). This decoupling simplifies the learning problem: the model does not need to learn spatial relationships and feature transformations simultaneously in a single weight matrix. 

This design choice is significant because it demonstrates that complex, coupled operations like convolution or attention are not required to build high-level representations. By reducing the core operations to simple matrix multiplications on transposed data views, the architecture achieves a level of conceptual simplicity comparable to early perceptrons, yet scales to state-of-the-art performance. This suggests that the *capacity* to mix information globally is more critical than the *mechanism* used to mix it.

### 2. Data Scaling Can Substitute for Inductive Bias
A central theoretical contribution of this work is empirical evidence regarding the trade-off between **inductive biases** (assumptions built into the model) and **data scale**. CNNs rely heavily on inductive biases like locality (nearby pixels are related) and translation invariance to generalize well from limited data. `MLP-Mixer` removes these biases, relying instead on raw learning capacity from data.

The results reveal a clear scaling law:
*   **Small Data Regime:** When pre-trained on smaller datasets (e.g., ImageNet-21k), `MLP-Mixer` tends to overfit more than CNNs. For instance, `Mixer-B/16` trained from scratch on ImageNet achieves 76.44% top-1 accuracy, which is 3% behind `ViT-B/16` and behind state-of-the-art CNNs like `NFNet` (Section 3.1). This confirms that without inductive biases, the model requires more data to generalize.
*   **Large Data Regime:** As the pre-training dataset grows, the performance gap closes and eventually vanishes. Figure 2 (Right) illustrates that `Mixer` models improve more rapidly with data size than `BiT` (CNN) models. When pre-trained on the massive JFT-300M dataset, `Mixer-H/14` reaches 87.94% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet, matching CNNs and Transformers (Table 2).

This insight validates the hypothesis that "convolutions and attention are both sufficient... neither of them are necessary" (Abstract) provided sufficient data is available. It suggests that in the limit of large-scale data, the specific inductive biases of CNNs may become less critical than the total learning capacity and optimization stability of the architecture.

### 3. Linear Complexity for Global Receptive Fields
From an efficiency standpoint, `MLP-Mixer` offers a significant innovation over attention-based models. Self-attention mechanisms typically have computational complexity quadratic in the number of patches, $O(S^2)$, because every patch must compute a relationship with every other patch. This becomes a bottleneck for high-resolution images where $S$ is large.

`MLP-Mixer` achieves a **global receptive field**—allowing any patch to influence any other patch—but maintains **linear complexity** $O(S)$ with respect to the number of patches. This is achieved because the token-mixing MLP maps $\mathbb{R}^S \mapsto \mathbb{R}^S$ using a hidden dimension $D_S$ that is independent of $S$ (Section 2). 
*   **Performance Impact:** This efficiency gain translates directly to hardware throughput. Table 2 shows that `Mixer-H/14` achieves 40 images/sec/core on TPU-v3, whereas `ViT-H/14` achieves only 15 images/sec/core. 
*   **Significance:** `Mixer` runs approximately 2.5 times faster than a comparable ViT while attaining similar accuracy (87.94% vs 88.55% on ImageNet). This demonstrates that global context aggregation does not inherently require quadratic cost, opening pathways for more efficient high-resolution vision models.

### 4. Robustness to Aggressive Parameter Sharing
A subtle but critical design choice in `MLP-Mixer` is the parameter sharing strategy within the token-mixing MLP. The same weight matrices are applied to every channel independently. Intuitively, one might expect different feature channels to require different spatial mixing patterns (e.g., low-level edges might need different spatial aggregation than high-level semantic features).

However, ablation studies in Supplementary A.1 reveal that **untying these parameters provides no noticeable improvement**. The authors tested introducing separate MLPs for each of the $C$ channels, which would multiply the parameter count by $C$, but observed no performance gain. 
*   **Why this matters:** This finding challenges the assumption that channel-specific spatial filters are necessary for vision. It suggests that a single, shared spatial mixing strategy is sufficient to propagate information across the image, regardless of the feature type. 
*   **Memory Efficiency:** This choice leads to significant memory savings, allowing the model to scale to larger hidden dimensions without exploding the parameter count. It reinforces the architecture's philosophy of simplicity: unless complexity demonstrably improves performance, it is omitted.

### Critical Limitation: Dependence on Regularization and Scale
While these innovations are significant, the paper is critical about its own limitations. The success of `MLP-Mixer` is contingent on **large-scale pre-training** and **modern regularization**. 
*   Without techniques like RandAugment, mixup, and stochastic depth, the model overfits severely on medium-sized datasets (Section 3.1). 
*   On smaller datasets without extensive pre-training, specialized CNN architectures still outperform `Mixer`. 

This nuance is crucial for practitioners: `MLP-Mixer` is not a universal replacement for CNNs in all settings. Its innovations shine primarily in the large-data regime where computational efficiency and raw capacity outweigh the generalization benefits of convolutional inductive biases.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

To validate the claim that an all-MLP architecture can compete with Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Transformers, the authors design a comprehensive evaluation framework. This section dissects the experimental methodology, benchmarks, and specific results. We focus not only on the headline accuracy numbers but also on the computational costs, scaling behaviors, and robustness checks that determine the practical viability of `MLP-Mixer`.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The experimental design follows a standard **transfer learning** setup, which is critical for comparing architectures fairly in modern computer vision. The process involves two distinct phases:

1.  **Pre-training:** Models are trained on large-scale upstream datasets to learn general visual representations.
2.  **Fine-tuning:** The pre-trained weights are adapted to specific downstream classification tasks.

This separation is crucial because it isolates the quality of the learned representations from the specific optimization of the final classifier.

#### Datasets
The authors utilize a hierarchy of datasets to test performance across different data scales (Section 3, "Downstream tasks" and "Pre-training"):
*   **Upstream (Pre-training):**
    *   **ImageNet (ILSVRC2012):** 1.3M training examples, 1k classes. Used for smaller scale pre-training.
    *   **ImageNet-21k:** A superset containing 14M images and 21k classes.
    *   **JFT-300M:** A proprietary dataset with 300M examples and 18k classes. This represents the "large data" regime where `MLP-Mixer` is expected to excel.
*   **Downstream (Fine-tuning):**
    *   **Standard Benchmarks:** ImageNet validation, CIFAR-10/100, Oxford-IIIT Pets, Oxford Flowers-102.
    *   **VTAB-1k:** The Visual Task Adaptation Benchmark consists of 19 diverse datasets, each with only 1k training examples. This tests the model's ability to generalize to new tasks with limited data.

#### Metrics
The paper evaluates three primary quantities to assess the **accuracy/cost trade-off** (Section 3):
1.  **Accuracy:** Top-1 validation accuracy on downstream tasks. For VTAB-1k, the average across 19 tasks is reported.
2.  **Pre-training Cost:** Measured in **TPUv3 core-days**. This metric combines theoretical FLOPs, hardware efficiency, and data efficiency into a single monetary/computational cost figure.
3.  **Inference Throughput:** Measured in **images/sec/core** on TPU-v3. This indicates how fast the model can process data during deployment.

#### Baselines
`MLP-Mixer` is compared against three classes of architectures (Section 3, "Models"):
*   **Attention-based:** Vision Transformers (ViT) [14], HaloNets [51].
*   **Convolution-based:** Big Transfer (BiT) ResNets [22], NFNets [7].
*   **Hybrid/Other:** EfficientNet variants pre-trained with Meta Pseudo Labels (MPL) [34] or ALIGN [21].

### 5.2 State-of-the-Art Comparison

The primary evidence for `MLP-Mixer`'s competitiveness is presented in **Table 2**. This table compares the largest `Mixer` models against state-of-the-art CNNs and Transformers pre-trained on similar datasets.

#### Large-Scale Performance (JFT-300M)
When pre-trained on the massive JFT-300M dataset, `Mixer-H/14` achieves performance comparable to the best existing models:
*   **Accuracy:** `Mixer-H/14` reaches **87.94%** top-1 accuracy on ImageNet. This is **0.5% higher** than `BiT-R152x4` (87.54%) and only **0.5% lower** than `ViT-H/14` (88.55%).
*   **Throughput:** `Mixer-H/14` achieves **40 images/sec/core**, which is **2.5 times faster** than `ViT-H/14` (15 images/sec/core) and roughly **1.5 times faster** than `BiT-R152x4` (26 images/sec/core).
*   **Cost:** The pre-training cost for `Mixer-H/14` is **1.01k core-days**. This is less than half the cost of `ViT-H/14` (2.30k core-days) and significantly lower than `BiT-R152x4` (9.90k core-days).

> "Remarkably, Mixer-H/14 runs 2.5 times faster than ViT-H/14 and almost twice as fast as BiT." (Section 3.1)

These numbers support the claim that `MLP-Mixer` sits on the **Pareto frontier** of accuracy versus cost. As visualized in **Figure 2 (Left)**, `Mixer` models (pink dots) align closely with the dashed frontier line established by ViT, NFNet, and HaloNet models.

#### Medium-Scale Performance (ImageNet-21k)
When pre-trained on the smaller ImageNet-21k dataset, `Mixer` remains competitive but shows signs of the regularization dependence discussed in Section 4:
*   `Mixer-L/16` achieves **84.15%** top-1 on ImageNet.
*   This is slightly inferior to `ViT-L/16` (85.30%) and `BiT-R152x4` (85.39%) under similar pre-training conditions.
*   The authors note that regularization (RandAugment, mixup, dropout) is **necessary** in this regime; without it, `Mixer` overfits significantly (Section 3.1).

#### Transfer to Small Tasks (VTAB-1k)
The VTAB-1k benchmark tests how well the learned representations transfer to diverse, low-data tasks.
*   `Mixer-H/14` scores **75.33%** average accuracy across 19 tasks.
*   This is comparable to `ViT-L/16` (72.72% on ImageNet-21k pre-training) and `BiT-R152x4` (70.64%).
*   However, it still trails the best proprietary models like `ALIGN` (79.99%), which uses contrastive learning on web data.

### 5.3 Scaling Laws: Data and Model Size

A critical part of the analysis is understanding how `MLP-Mixer` behaves as resources scale. The paper provides evidence that `Mixer` benefits from scale more aggressively than CNNs.

#### Data Scaling (Figure 2, Right)
To isolate the effect of data size, the authors pre-trained `Mixer` models on random subsets of JFT-300M containing **3%, 10%, 30%, and 100%** of the data. To ensure fair comparison, every model was pre-trained for the same total number of steps (adjusting epochs inversely to dataset size).
*   **Metric:** They use **Linear 5-shot ImageNet Top-1** accuracy as a proxy for transfer quality. This involves freezing the pre-trained weights and training only a linear classifier on 5 examples per class.
*   **Observation:** As shown in **Figure 2 (Right)**, `Mixer` models (solid lines) improve more rapidly with data size than `BiT` ResNets (dotted lines).
*   **Comparison with ViT:** The performance gap between `Mixer-L/16` and `ViT-L/16` shrinks as data scale increases. At the smallest subset, `Mixer` overfits strongly (similar to ViT), but at 100% JFT-300M, the gap is minimal.
*   **Implication:** This supports the hypothesis that `Mixer` has weaker inductive biases than CNNs. It struggles with small data but catches up when data is abundant, similar to ViT but potentially with a steeper improvement curve.

#### Model Scaling (Table 3 and Figure 3)
The authors evaluate various model sizes from `S/32` (Small) to `H/14` (Huge).
*   **Small Model Struggle:** When trained from scratch on ImageNet (no pre-training), `Mixer-B/16` achieves **76.44%** top-1 accuracy. This is **3% behind** `ViT-B/16` (79.67%) (Table 3).
*   **Overfitting:** Training curves (not reported in detail but mentioned in Section 3.2) show that `Mixer-B/16` achieves similar training loss to ViT but worse validation accuracy, indicating higher overfitting.
*   **High-End Convergence:** At the large scale (JFT-300M), the gap closes. `Mixer-H/14` fine-tuned at 224 resolution is only **0.3% behind** `ViT-H/14` (86.32% vs 86.65%) while running **2.2 times faster** (Section 3.2).

**Figure 3** visualizes this trade-off. The left plot shows Accuracy vs. Pre-training Compute. `Mixer` models sit slightly below the frontier at low compute budgets but join the frontier at high compute budgets. The right plot shows Accuracy vs. Throughput, where `Mixer` dominates due to its linear complexity.

### 5.4 Inductive Bias and Robustness Checks

To verify the claim that `MLP-Mixer` lacks the spatial inductive biases of CNNs, the authors conduct a **permutation invariance experiment** (Section 3.4).

#### Experimental Setup
They train `Mixer-B/16` and `ResNet50x1` on JFT-300M using modified input pipelines:
1.  **Patch + Pixel Shuffling:** The order of $16 \times 16$ patches is shuffled, and pixels within each patch are permuted using a shared permutation.
2.  **Global Shuffling:** All pixels in the entire image are permuted globally.

#### Results (Figure 4)
*   **Patch Invariance:** `Mixer` is invariant to the order of patches. The learning curves for "original" and "patch + pixel shuffling" match perfectly for `Mixer` (blue and green curves in Figure 4, bottom left). This confirms that `Mixer` does not rely on local spatial structure in the same way CNNs do.
*   **CNN Sensitivity:** `ResNet50` performance drops significantly when patches are permuted (Figure 4, bottom right), confirming its reliance on local pixel adjacency.
*   **Global Robustness:** Surprisingly, when pixels are shuffled globally, `Mixer`'s performance drops by **~45%**, whereas `ResNet` drops by **~75%**.
    *   **Interpretation:** While `Mixer` does not need local patch order, it still benefits from some global structure or statistical properties of the image that are destroyed by global shuffling. It is not completely permutation-invariant to individual pixels, likely because the initial patch embedding projects local pixel groups into channels.

### 5.5 Representation Visualization

To understand what the `token-mixing` MLPs learn, the authors visualize the weights of the hidden units (Section 3.5, **Figure 5**).

*   **Structure:** Each unit in the token-mixing MLP has 196 weights (for a $14 \times 14$ patch grid). These weights can be reshaped into a $14 \times 14$ image to visualize spatial sensitivity.
*   **Findings:**
    *   Some features operate on the **entire image** (global receptive field), unlike CNN filters which are local.
    *   Similar to CNNs, many feature detectors appear in **pairs with opposite phases** (e.g., a light-dark edge vs. a dark-light edge).
    *   Deeper layers show less clearly identifiable structure compared to early layers.
*   **Patch Size Effect:** Supplementary Figure 7 shows that models using larger patches ($32 \times 32$) learn more structured, low-frequency projection units (Gabor-like), while smaller patches ($16 \times 16$) show less structure. This suggests the initial patch embedding plays a significant role in the inductive bias of the system.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment of Evidence

The experiments provide strong but conditional support for the paper's claims.

#### Strengths of the Evidence
1.  **Cost-Accuracy Frontier:** The data in **Table 2** and **Figure 2** convincingly shows that `MLP-Mixer` achieves state-of-the-art accuracy per unit of compute. The throughput advantage (40 vs 15 img/sec) is a tangible benefit for deployment.
2.  **Scaling Consistency:** The scaling curves in **Figure 2 (Right)** demonstrate that the architecture behaves predictably with data scale, validating the design choice to remove inductive biases in favor of data-driven learning.
3.  **Ablation of Complexity:** The negative results in **Supplementary A** (e.g., untying parameters did not help) support the claim that the simple, shared-weight design is sufficient, rather than just a computational shortcut.

#### Limitations and Conditions
1.  **Data Hunger:** The experiments clearly show `Mixer` underperforms on smaller datasets without extensive pre-training. `Mixer-B/16` trained from scratch on ImageNet (76.44%) is behind specialized CNNs like `NFNet` (86.5%) (Section 3.1). Practitioners without access to JFT-300M scale data may not see the same benefits.
2.  **Regularization Dependence:** The success on ImageNet-21k is contingent on modern regularization (RandAugment, mixup, stochastic depth). **Table 4** details these hyperparameters. Without them, the model overfits, indicating that the architecture itself does not generalize well without external constraints.
3.  **Resolution Constraints:** The fine-tuning procedure for higher resolutions requires a specific block-diagonal initialization scheme (Section 3, Fine-tuning) and only allows integer scaling factors ($S' = K^2 S$). This is less flexible than CNNs, which can naturally handle arbitrary input sizes via sliding windows.

#### Conclusion on Experimental Validity
The experiments successfully demonstrate that convolutions and attention are **not necessary** for high-performance vision, provided sufficient data and compute are available. However, they also confirm that these removed components (convolutions/attention) provide valuable **inductive biases** that aid learning in data-scarce regimes. The evidence supports `MLP-Mixer` as a highly efficient alternative for large-scale settings, but not necessarily as a universal replacement for CNNs in all computer vision tasks.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While `MLP-Mixer` demonstrates that convolutions and attention are not strictly necessary for high-performance vision, this freedom comes with specific architectural costs and assumptions. Understanding these limitations is crucial for practitioners deciding when to adopt this architecture over established alternatives like CNNs or Transformers. The following analysis breaks down the fundamental trade-offs inherent in the design.

### 6.1 The Data Efficiency Trade-off
The most significant limitation of `MLP-Mixer` is its reliance on massive datasets to compensate for the lack of **inductive biases**. As discussed in Section 2, inductive biases are assumptions built into the model (like locality in CNNs) that help it generalize from limited data. By removing these biases, `Mixer` shifts the burden of learning spatial relationships entirely onto the data.

*   **Performance Gap in Low-Data Regimes:** When data is scarce, `Mixer` underperforms compared to biased architectures. Table 3 shows that when trained from scratch on ImageNet (1.3M images), `Mixer-B/16` achieves **76.44%** top-1 accuracy, lagging **3.23%** behind `ViT-B/16` (79.67%) and significantly behind specialized CNNs like `NFNet` (86.5%) (Section 3.1).
*   **Regularization Dependence:** To mitigate overfitting on medium-sized datasets (e.g., ImageNet-21k), `Mixer` requires aggressive regularization techniques such as RandAugment, mixup, and stochastic depth (Supplementary B, Table 4). Without these, the model fails to generalize. This implies that the architecture itself does not constrain the hypothesis space enough to prevent memorization without external help.
*   **Practical Implication:** For practitioners without access to large-scale pre-training datasets (like JFT-300M) or extensive computational resources for regularization tuning, `Mixer` may be a suboptimal choice compared to CNNs which are more data-efficient.

### 6.2 Architectural Rigidity in Input Resolution
Unlike Convolutional Neural Networks, which are naturally **fully convolutional** and can handle arbitrary input sizes via sliding windows, `MLP-Mixer` has a rigid relationship between input resolution and model weights.

*   **Fixed Sequence Length:** The token-mixing MLP uses a weight matrix $W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{D_S \times S}$, where $S$ is the number of patches (Section 3). This means the model is technically trained for a specific sequence length.
*   **Complex Fine-tuning for Resolution Changes:** To fine-tune at higher resolutions (increasing $S$ to $S'$), the authors must employ a specific **block-diagonal initialization** scheme (Section 3, Fine-tuning). This involves copying the pre-trained weights along the diagonal of a larger matrix to simulate processing independent spatial regions.
*   **Discrete Scaling Constraint:** This initialization scheme only supports resolution increases by integer factors $K$, such that $S' = K^2 S$ (Section 3, Fine-tuning). Arbitrary resolution changes (e.g., from $224 \times 224$ to $300 \times 300$) are not natively supported without interpolation or cropping, unlike ViT (which can interpolate position embeddings) or CNNs (which are size-agnostic).
*   **Trade-off:** This rigidity simplifies the architecture (no position embeddings needed) but reduces flexibility during deployment where input resolutions may vary dynamically.

### 6.3 Limitations in Dense Prediction and Multi-Scale Tasks
The paper focuses primarily on **image classification**, where a single output label is produced for the entire image. However, many computer vision tasks require **dense prediction** (e.g., semantic segmentation, object detection), where predictions are made at every pixel or region.

*   **Isotropic Design:** `MLP-Mixer` uses an **isotropic** architecture, meaning all layers maintain the same hidden dimension $C$ and spatial resolution (Section 2). In contrast, successful CNNs and some Transformers (e.g., Pyramid ViT [52]) use a **pyramidal structure**, progressively reducing spatial resolution while increasing channel depth. This multi-scale representation is critical for detecting objects of varying sizes.
*   **Failed Pyramid Experiments:** The authors explicitly tested pyramidal variants where token-mixing MLPs reduce the number of tokens in deeper layers. While this reduced training time on JFT-300M, they were "unable to transfer these findings to ImageNet or ImageNet-21k" (Supplementary A.1).
*   **Implication:** The current `Mixer` design may struggle with tasks requiring multi-scale feature aggregation. Until pyramidal variants are successfully stabilized, `Mixer` is primarily validated for classification tasks (like VTAB-1k) rather than dense prediction benchmarks (like COCO or Cityscapes), which are not reported in the paper.

### 6.4 Hardware and Implementation Constraints
The efficiency claims of `MLP-Mixer` are tightly coupled with specific hardware characteristics, particularly Tensor Processing Units (TPUs).

*   **TPU Optimization:** The reported throughput metrics (e.g., 40 images/sec/core for `Mixer-H/14`) are measured on **TPU-v3** accelerators (Table 2). TPUs are highly optimized for dense matrix multiplications, which form the core of `Mixer` operations.
*   **Memory Bandwidth Sensitivity:** While the computational complexity is linear $O(S)$, the token-mixing MLP requires transposing the input tensor ($S \times C$ to $C \times S$) to apply weights across patches (Section 3, Equation 1). On hardware where memory bandwidth is the bottleneck (rather than compute), these transpositions and large matrix multiplications could incur latency penalties not fully captured by FLOP counts.
*   **Generalizability:** The paper does not provide latency or throughput data for GPUs or edge devices. Practitioners deploying on hardware less optimized for large dense matmuls (compared to TPUs) might not see the same 2.5x throughput advantage over ViT claimed in Table 2.

### 6.5 Open Theoretical Questions
The success of `MLP-Mixer` raises several unanswered questions about the nature of visual learning that the paper acknowledges but does not resolve.

*   **Feature Interpretability:** While Section 3.5 visualizes token-mixing weights, the authors note that deeper layers "appear to have no clearly identifiable structure" compared to CNN filters. It remains unclear what representations these layers are learning if not localized edges or textures.
*   **Domain Generalization:** The Conclusion (Section 5) explicitly asks whether this design works in **Natural Language Processing (NLP)** or other domains. Since `Mixer` treats patches similarly to tokens in a sequence, it theoretically could apply to text. However, the lack of explicit positional embeddings (relying instead on order-sensitive MLPs) might behave differently in language where positional relationships are strictly sequential rather than spatial.
*   **Inductive Bias Necessity:** The results suggest inductive biases are less critical with enough data. However, the paper does not quantify the **exact data threshold** where `Mixer` surpasses CNNs. Figure 2 (Right) shows the trend, but the crossover point depends on model scale and regularization, leaving practitioners without a clear rule of thumb for when to switch architectures.

### 6.6 Summary of Trade-offs
The following table summarizes the key trade-offs identified in the paper, helping practitioners weigh `MLP-Mixer` against alternatives:

| Feature | MLP-Mixer | CNNs (e.g., ResNet) | Vision Transformers (ViT) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Inductive Bias** | None (Data-driven) | High (Locality, Translation) | Low (Global attention) |
| **Data Efficiency** | Low (Needs large data) | High (Works on small data) | Low (Needs large data) |
| **Complexity** | Linear $O(S)$ | Linear $O(S)$ | Quadratic $O(S^2)$ |
| **Resolution Flexibility** | Low (Fixed $S$ or block-init) | High (Fully convolutional) | Medium (Interpolate pos-emb) |
| **Hardware Efficiency** | High on TPUs (MatMul) | High on GPUs (Conv) | Moderate (Attention ops) |
| **Task Scope** | Classification (Validated) | Classification & Dense | Classification & Dense |

In conclusion, `MLP-Mixer` is not a universal replacement for CNNs or Transformers. It is a specialized architecture that excels in **large-scale classification settings** where computational efficiency and throughput are prioritized over data efficiency and architectural flexibility. Its primary contribution is proving that simplicity—when scaled—can compete with complexity, but it does not eliminate the value of inductive biases in resource-constrained environments.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of `MLP-Mixer` extends beyond a single architectural variant; it challenges foundational assumptions about what components are strictly required for visual representation learning. By demonstrating that neither convolutions nor attention are necessary for state-of-the-art performance, the work reshapes the design space for future vision models. This section outlines how this shift influences the field, identifies specific open research questions raised by the authors, and provides practical guidance on when to integrate this architecture into real-world pipelines.

### 7.1 Shifting the Architectural Paradigm

The primary theoretical implication of this work is the decoupling of **performance** from **specialized operators**. For over a decade, progress in computer vision was tightly linked to improvements in convolutional operators (e.g., depth-wise separable convolutions) or attention mechanisms. `MLP-Mixer` demonstrates that:

*   **Simplicity Scales:** Complex operations like self-attention (quadratic complexity) or convolutions (specialized memory access) can be replaced by simple matrix multiplications (`MLP` layers) without sacrificing accuracy, provided the model is scaled appropriately (Section 5).
*   **Data Over Bias:** The results reinforce the trend that **inductive biases** (like locality in CNNs) become less critical as data scale increases. Figure 2 (Right) shows that `Mixer` models improve more rapidly with data size than `BiT` ResNets, suggesting that in the large-data regime, raw learning capacity outweighs architectural priors.
*   **Linear Complexity is Achievable:** The architecture proves that a **global receptive field** (where every patch can interact with every other patch) does not inherently require quadratic cost $O(S^2)$. By using token-mixing MLPs with fixed hidden width $D_S$, `Mixer` maintains linear complexity $O(S)$ while allowing global information flow (Section 2).

This paradigm shift encourages researchers to revisit "simple" architectures that were previously discarded due to poor performance on small datasets, recognizing that their potential may only unlock at massive scale.

### 7.2 Promising Avenues for Future Research

The authors explicitly highlight several open questions in the Conclusion (Section 5) and through negative results in the Supplementary material. These form a roadmap for follow-up work:

*   **Cross-Domain Applicability (NLP):** The Conclusion (Section 5) poses the question: "It would be particularly interesting to see whether such a design works in NLP or other domains." Since `Mixer` treats image patches similarly to tokens in a sequence, it is theoretically compatible with language tasks. However, unlike Transformers, `Mixer` lacks explicit positional embeddings, relying instead on the order-sensitivity of the token-mixing MLP. Future research must determine if this implicit positional handling is sufficient for strictly sequential data like text.
*   **Dense Prediction and Pyramidal Structures:** The current `Mixer` uses an **isotropic** design (fixed resolution throughout), which is suboptimal for dense prediction tasks like object detection or segmentation that benefit from multi-scale features. The authors attempted **pyramidal variants** (reducing tokens in deeper layers) but were "unable to transfer these findings to ImageNet or ImageNet-21k" (Supplementary A.1). Stabilizing pyramidal `Mixer` architectures remains a critical open challenge for expanding beyond classification.
*   **Understanding Learned Inductive Biases:** While `Mixer` removes explicit biases, it still learns structure. Section 3.5 visualizes token-mixing weights that show global patterns and opposite-phase pairs (similar to CNN filters). Future work should analyze **what** biases emerge implicitly from the optimization process versus those hard-coded in CNNs. This could lead to a deeper theory of generalization in over-parameterized networks.
*   **Hardware-Aware Optimizations:** The throughput advantages reported (Table 2) are specific to **TPU-v3** accelerators, which are highly optimized for dense matrix multiplications. Future work should investigate `Mixer` performance on GPUs or edge devices where memory bandwidth or specific convolution kernels might be more optimized. The tensor transpositions required for token-mixing (Section 3) could become bottlenecks on different hardware architectures.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

Based on the experimental evidence in Section 3 and Section 5, `MLP-Mixer` is not a universal replacement for CNNs or Transformers but excels in specific niches:

*   **Large-Scale Image Classification:** For organizations with access to massive pre-training datasets (e.g., >100M images like JFT-300M or ImageNet-21k), `Mixer` offers a competitive accuracy/cost trade-off. Table 2 shows `Mixer-H/14` achieves 87.94% ImageNet accuracy with significantly lower pre-training cost (1.01k core-days) compared to `ViT-H/14` (2.30k core-days).
*   **High-Throughput Inference on TPUs:** In deployment scenarios where inference speed is critical and hardware is TPU-based, `Mixer` provides a tangible advantage. The linear complexity allows `Mixer-H/14` to run at **40 images/sec/core**, roughly 2.5× faster than `ViT-H/14` (15 images/sec/core) (Table 2).
*   **Research Baselines:** Due to its conceptual simplicity (only MLPs, no attention masks or convolutional strides), `Mixer` serves as an excellent baseline for isolating the effects of data scaling and regularization from architectural complexity.

**When NOT to use MLP-Mixer:**
*   **Small Data Regimes:** If pre-training data is limited (e.g., &lt;10M images), CNNs or specialized hybrids likely offer better data efficiency due to their inductive biases (Section 3.1).
*   **Dynamic Resolution Requirements:** If the application requires handling arbitrary input resolutions without integer scaling factors, CNNs are more flexible. `Mixer` requires specific block-diagonal initialization to change resolution (Section 3, Fine-tuning).
*   **Dense Prediction Tasks:** Until pyramidal variants are validated, `Mixer` is not recommended for object detection or segmentation where multi-scale feature maps are standard.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For practitioners aiming to reproduce results or integrate `MLP-Mixer` into existing workflows, the paper provides specific technical constraints and hyperparameters that are critical for success.

*   **Code Availability:** The authors provide a compact implementation in **JAX/Flax** (Supplementary E, Listing 1). The core logic relies on `einops.rearrange` for transposing between token and channel dimensions. Integrating this into PyTorch or TensorFlow requires careful handling of the transpose operations to ensure the token-mixing MLP operates on columns (spatial) and channel-mixing on rows (features).
*   **Regularization is Mandatory:** A common failure mode is under-regularization. Section 3.1 notes that `Mixer` "tends to overfit" without modern regularization. Practitioners must employ the specific techniques listed in **Table 4** (Supplementary B):
    *   **RandAugment:** Magnitude $m \in \{10, 15, 20\}$ depending on dataset.
    *   **Mixup:** Strength $p \in \{0.2, 0.5\}$.
    *   **Stochastic Depth:** Drop probability linearly increasing to $s \in \{0.1\}$.
    *   **Dropout:** Rates up to $0.1$.
    *   Ignoring these will likely result in performance similar to a vanilla ResNet50 (76.4%) rather than the state-of-the-art scores reported (Table 3).
*   **Fine-tuning Resolution:** To achieve the highest accuracy (e.g., 87.94% in Table 2), models must be fine-tuned at higher resolutions (448px) than pre-training (224px). This requires implementing the **block-diagonal weight initialization** described in Section 3 (Fine-tuning) to expand the token-mixing MLP weights $W_1$ from $\mathbb{R}^{D_S \times S}$ to $\mathbb{R}^{D_S' \times S'}$.
*   **Optimizer Settings:** Pre-training uses **Adam** ($\beta_1=0.9, \beta_2=0.999$) with gradient clipping at global norm 1 (Section 3). Fine-tuning switches to **momentum SGD** without weight decay. Deviating from this two-stage optimization strategy may hinder convergence.

By adhering to these constraints, practitioners can leverage `MLP-Mixer` to achieve efficient, high-performance vision systems in large-scale settings, while contributing to the broader understanding of how simple architectures behave when scaled.
