## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces **MMMU**, a massive benchmark comprising **11.5K** college-level multimodal questions across **30 subjects** and **6 disciplines** (including Engineering, Medicine, and Art), designed to evaluate Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) on expert-level perception and reasoning rather than simple commonsense tasks. The study reveals a significant performance gap between current AI and human experts: while college students achieve up to **88.6%** accuracy, even advanced proprietary models like **GPT-4V** and **Gemini Ultra** score only **55.7%** and **59%** respectively, with open-source models lagging further behind at approximately **34%**. By requiring models to interpret highly heterogeneous image types (e.g., chemical structures, sheet music, medical scans) and apply deep domain knowledge, MMMU establishes a necessary baseline for measuring progress toward "Expert AGI," highlighting that current systems still struggle fundamentally with complex, multi-disciplinary visual reasoning.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Definition and Measurement of "Expert AGI"
The central motivation for this work stems from the lack of a shared, operationalizable definition for **Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)**. While rapid advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) have sparked debates about whether AGI is imminent, these discussions often remain abstract. The authors anchor their work in the leveled taxonomy proposed by Morris et al. [57], which defines AGI progress based on **generality** (breadth of tasks) and **performance** (depth of capability).

Within this framework, **Level 3**, or **Expert AGI**, represents a critical milestone. It is defined as an AI system that performs at least at the **90th percentile of skilled adults** across a broad range of tasks. Reaching this level implies the system has crossed the "substitution threshold," meaning it could theoretically replace human labor in many industries, carrying significant societal and economic implications. To monitor progress toward this specific level, the community requires benchmarks that strictly test for **expert-level** capabilities, not just general competence.

### The Gap in Existing Multimodal Benchmarks
Prior to MMMU, the landscape of multimodal evaluation was dominated by benchmarks that failed to meet the depth requirements for Expert AGI. The paper identifies two distinct categories of prior work, both of which fall short:

1.  **Commonsense and Daily Knowledge Benchmarks:**
    Existing large-scale benchmarks like **VQA-v2**, **ScienceQA**, and **MMBench** primarily focus on visual questions answerable with everyday commonsense or elementary knowledge.
    *   *The Shortfall:* While models like CogVLM achieve impressive scores (e.g., 92% on ScienceQA-IMG), these tasks often resemble elementary or middle school-level problems. They test whether a model can identify a object or recall a basic fact, but they do not require the deliberate, multi-step reasoning or deep domain expertise characteristic of a human professional (e.g., a radiologist or an electrical engineer).
    *   *Example:* A model might identify a "beaker" in an image (commonsense), but fail to calculate the voltage in a circuit diagram containing that beaker if it requires applying Kirchhoff's laws (expert reasoning).

2.  **Text-Only Expert Benchmarks:**
    Benchmarks like **MMLU** and **AGIEval** successfully test college-level knowledge across diverse disciplines.
    *   *The Shortfall:* These benchmarks are strictly text-based. However, real-world expert tasks are inherently multimodal. A doctor diagnoses patients using MRI scans; an engineer analyzes blueprints; an art historian interprets paintings. By ignoring the visual modality, these benchmarks fail to evaluate the **joint perception and reasoning** capabilities required for true Expert AGI. A model could theoretically pass MMLU by memorizing text facts without ever understanding a diagram or chart.

### The Specific Problem: Lack of "Deep" Multimodal Evaluation
The specific problem MMMU addresses is the absence of a benchmark that simultaneously demands:
*   **Breadth:** Coverage across multiple professional disciplines (not just science or math).
*   **Depth:** Questions requiring college-level subject knowledge and complex reasoning chains.
*   **Heterogeneous Perception:** The ability to interpret diverse, non-natural image types (e.g., chemical structures, sheet music, geometric proofs) rather than just photographs of everyday scenes.
*   **Interleaved Understanding:** The capacity to reason over inputs where text and images are deeply intertwined, rather than treating the image as a simple captioning target.

Without such a benchmark, the field risks overestimating model capabilities. A model might appear "general" because it performs well on easy, daily-life visual tasks, while completely failing when presented with the complex, specialized visual-language problems faced by actual experts.

### Positioning MMMU Relative to Prior Work
MMMU positions itself as the necessary successor to both commonsense VQA datasets and text-only expert exams. It differentiates itself through four key dimensions (illustrated in **Figure 1** and **Figure 3** of the paper):

*   **Source of Data:** Unlike synthesized datasets or web-scraped common images, MMMU is meticulously curated from **college exams, quizzes, and textbooks**. This ensures the questions are designed by humans to test specific expert competencies.
*   **Disciplinary Scope:** It covers **6 core disciplines** (Art & Design, Business, Science, Health & Medicine, Humanities & Social Science, Tech & Engineering) and **30 subjects**, ensuring the "generality" aspect of AGI is tested across the full spectrum of human expertise, not just STEM fields.
*   **Visual Complexity:** The benchmark introduces **30 highly heterogeneous image types**. While prior work focuses on photographs, MMMU includes **chemical structures, sheet music, medical scans (MRI/CT), circuit diagrams, and political cartoons**. This forces models to develop specialized perceptual modules rather than relying solely on generic object recognition.
*   **Reasoning Depth:** The questions are explicitly designed to require **deliberate reasoning**. For instance, a question might present a circuit diagram and ask for a specific voltage calculation ($V_{CE}$), requiring the model to first perceive the components, recall the relevant physics formulas (e.g., Ohm's law, Kirchhoff's laws), and then execute a multi-step calculation. This contrasts sharply with benchmarks where the answer can be guessed from visual cues alone.

By bridging the gap between "visual perception" and "expert reasoning," MMMU aims to serve as a rigorous filter: if a model cannot perform well on MMMU, it cannot yet be considered an **Expert AGI** candidate, regardless of its performance on simpler tasks.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a **data-centric benchmark construction methodology** rather than a new model architecture, where the core idea is to curate a massive, high-quality dataset of college-level multimodal problems that rigorously tests the intersection of expert domain knowledge, complex visual perception, and deliberate reasoning. The "system" being built is the **MMMU benchmark pipeline**, a structured process that transforms raw educational materials into a standardized evaluation suite capable of distinguishing between superficial pattern matching and genuine expert-level understanding.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The MMMU system is a rigorous data curation and evaluation framework designed to convert diverse college-level exam questions containing images into a unified, machine-readable benchmark for testing AI models. It solves the problem of inflated performance metrics on easy tasks by enforcing a "depth-first" selection strategy that filters out commonsense questions, ensuring only problems requiring specialized subject knowledge and multi-step reasoning remain.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The MMMU construction pipeline operates as a sequential filter with four major components:
1.  **Subject Scope Definition Module:** Determines the disciplinary boundaries by selecting 30 specific subjects across 6 broad disciplines where visual inputs are essential for problem-solving, explicitly excluding text-heavy fields like Law.
2.  **Human-in-the-Loop Collection Engine:** Deploys a team of over 50 college student annotators to harvest questions from textbooks and exams, ensuring source diversity and copyright compliance while targeting questions without immediately visible answers.
3.  **Multi-Stage Quality Control Filter:** Applies a three-step cleaning process involving lexical overlap detection, format standardization, and difficulty-based pruning to remove duplicates, very easy questions, and formatting errors.
4.  **Standardized Evaluation Interface:** Formats the curated data into a zero-shot testing protocol with robust regular-expression-based answer extraction to fairly assess Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) and text-only LLMs augmented with OCR or captioning tools.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Disciplinary Selection and Scope Definition
The construction of MMMU begins with a strategic definition of scope intended to maximize both the breadth of general intelligence and the depth of expert capability. The authors initiated the process by reviewing common university majors to identify subjects where visual inputs are not merely decorative but constitute essential information for solving problems. Based on this principle, the team selected **30 distinct subjects** spanning **6 core disciplines**: Art & Design, Business, Science, Health & Medicine, Humanities & Social Science, and Tech & Engineering. This selection process explicitly excluded disciplines such as Law and Linguistics, as the authors determined it was difficult to find a sufficient volume of relevant multimodal problems in these fields that met their criteria for visual dependency. The resulting coverage includes **183 subfields**, ranging from "Clinical Radiology" and "Analog Electronics" to "Art History" and "Macroeconomics," ensuring that the benchmark tests a truly wide spectrum of human expertise rather than focusing narrowly on STEM fields.

#### Data Curation and Annotation Protocol
The core of the MMMU construction is a human-in-the-loop data collection process designed to gather high-quality, college-level problems from authentic educational sources. The authors recruited a team of **50 college students**, including co-authors, who specialized in the target majors to serve as annotators. These annotators were instructed to collect multimodal questions from three primary sources: major textbooks, online educational resources, and lecture materials. To ensure the benchmark tests genuine reasoning rather than memorization of common web data, annotators were specifically advised to select questions where the answers were not immediately available in the same context, such as those with solutions located in separate documents or at the very end of textbooks. This strategy directly addresses the concern of **data contamination**, where models might have inadvertently seen the question-answer pairs during their pre-training on web corpora. Furthermore, annotators were strictly instructed to adhere to copyright and license regulations, explicitly avoiding data from sources that prohibit copying or redistribution. When existing questions were insufficient or did not meet the visual complexity requirements, annotators were empowered to create new questions based on their own subject matter expertise. This initial collection phase yielded a diverse pool of approximately **13,000 questions**.

#### Multi-Stage Quality Control and Filtering
To transform the raw collection of 13,000 questions into a rigorous benchmark, the authors implemented a comprehensive three-stage quality control pipeline.
*   **Stage 1: Deduplication:** The first stage employed algorithmic checks for **lexical overlap** and **source URL similarity** to identify potential duplicate problems. Any suspected duplicates flagged by these automated metrics were then manually reviewed by the authors to confirm and eliminate redundancy, ensuring that the test set does not contain repeated instances that could skew evaluation results.
*   **Stage 2: Format Standardization:** In the second stage, the cleaned problems were distributed among different co-authors for a detailed format and typo check. This step ensured that all questions adhered to a standardized structure, correcting any deviations in formatting, image resolution, or textual clarity to maintain consistency across the diverse dataset.
*   **Stage 3: Difficulty Calibration and Pruning:** The final and most critical stage involved categorizing every problem into one of four difficulty levels: "very easy," "easy," "medium," and "hard." The authors explicitly defined their design criteria to exclude simplistic questions that could be solved with basic commonsense. Consequently, approximately **10% of the problems**, classified as "very easy," were excluded from the final benchmark. This pruning step is vital for maintaining the "expert-level" nature of MMMU, ensuring that the remaining **11,550 questions** genuinely require college-level knowledge and deliberate reasoning.

#### Dataset Statistics and Composition
The final MMMU benchmark consists of **11,550** meticulously curated multimodal questions, split into three distinct sets to support different evaluation needs. The **development set** contains **150 questions** (5 per subject) intended for few-shot prompting examples. The **validation set** comprises **900 questions**, designed for hyperparameter selection and model debugging. The **test set** is the largest component, containing **10,500 questions** for final performance reporting. The dataset exhibits a balanced yet realistic distribution of difficulty, with **28%** of questions labeled as "Easy," **45%** as "Medium," and **27%** as "Hard." In terms of question format, the benchmark is predominantly multiple-choice, with **10,861 questions (94.03%)** offering discrete options, while **689 questions (5.97%)** are open-ended, requiring the model to generate a specific value or short phrase. Notably, **2,035 questions (17.62%)** include detailed explanations, which serve as valuable resources for error analysis and chain-of-thought training.

The visual complexity of MMMU is defined by its coverage of **30 highly heterogeneous image types**. Unlike previous benchmarks dominated by natural photographs, MMMU includes specialized visual formats such as **chemical structures, sheet music, circuit diagrams, medical scans (MRI, CT, X-ray), geometric proofs, and political cartoons**. The distribution of these image types is heavily skewed towards **Diagrams (3,466 samples)** and **Tables (2,474 samples)**, reflecting the prevalence of structured visual data in technical and business disciplines. Other significant categories include **Plots and Charts (936)**, **Photographs (870)**, and **Chemical Structures (609)**. The dataset also features complex input structures: **97.52%** of questions contain at least one image in the question body, while **3.37%** include images within the answer options. Furthermore, **7.39%** of the examples involve **multiple images** that must be jointly interpreted, and **36.92%** of questions feature images interleaved in the middle of the text, requiring the model to maintain context across modalities rather than processing the image as a standalone caption.

#### Evaluation Methodology and Baselines
The evaluation protocol for MMMU is designed to assess the zero-shot capabilities of models, measuring their ability to generalize to expert tasks without task-specific fine-tuning. The authors evaluated a total of **28 open-source Large Multimodal Models (LMMs)** alongside proprietary systems like **GPT-4V(ision)** and **Gemini**. For each model family, the evaluation utilized the latest, largest, and best-performing checkpoint available at the time of the study. The baseline models span a wide architectural spectrum, including:
*   **Projection-based models:** Such as **LLaVA-1.5**, which linearly projects visual embeddings into the LLM's word embedding space, and **MiniGPT-4**, which uses a similar projection layer atop a Vicuna backbone.
*   **Query-based models:** Like **BLIP-2** and **InstructBLIP**, which introduce learnable visual queries to bridge frozen image encoders (e.g., CLIP ViT) with frozen LLMs (e.g., FLAN-T5).
*   **Deeply integrated models:** Such as **CogVLM**, which adds trainable visual layers directly into the textual Transformer blocks to achieve deeper modality alignment, and **Qwen-VL**, which uses a cross-attention module to support interleaved inputs.
*   **Text-only LLMs with Augmentation:** To isolate the contribution of visual understanding, the authors also evaluated text-only LLMs (e.g., **Llama2-7B**, **GPT-4 Text**) augmented with external tools. Specifically, they deployed **MMOCR** for Optical Character Recognition (OCR) and **LLaVA-1.5** for image captioning, feeding the resulting text descriptions into the LLMs to determine if explicit text extraction could bridge the performance gap.

The evaluation metric is **micro-averaged accuracy**, calculated separately for multiple-choice and open-ended questions. To handle the variability in model generations, especially for open-ended responses or models that output lengthy reasoning chains, the authors developed a robust, rule-based evaluation pipeline. This system uses **regular expressions** to extract key phrases, numerical values, or conclusion markers from the model's raw output, matching them against the ground truth. If a model fails to produce a valid answer that can be parsed, the system treats the response as incorrect for open questions or performs a random selection for multiple-choice questions as a penalty. This approach mitigates the influence of formatting inconsistencies and ensures that the score reflects the model's actual correctness rather than its ability to follow a specific output template. The study also includes **Random Choice** and **Frequent Choice** baselines, where the former selects an option uniformly at random and the latter selects the most frequent option within each subject based on the validation set distribution, providing a lower bound for performance.

#### Design Choices and Rationale
The technical design of MMMU reflects several deliberate choices aimed at maximizing its utility as a measure of Expert AGI. First, the decision to source questions from **college exams and textbooks** rather than synthesizing them or scraping the open web ensures that the problems have been vetted by human educators to test specific learning outcomes. This contrasts with datasets like **ScienceQA**, which, while diverse, often contain elementary-level questions that do not challenge advanced models. Second, the exclusion of "very easy" questions (the bottom 10%) is a critical design choice to prevent the benchmark from being saturated with tasks that current models can solve via simple object recognition or keyword matching. By enforcing a minimum difficulty threshold, MMMU forces models to demonstrate **deliberate reasoning**—the ability to chain together multiple steps of logic and apply domain-specific formulas. Third, the inclusion of **interleaved text-image inputs** and **multiple images per question** addresses a limitation in earlier benchmarks where images were typically appended at the end of the prompt. This design compels models to develop fine-grained grounding capabilities, linking specific text segments to specific regions of an image, which is essential for real-world expert tasks like interpreting a complex engineering schematic or a multi-panel medical report. Finally, the use of a **zero-shot evaluation setting** ensures that the results reflect the inherent capabilities of the pre-trained models, avoiding the confounding factor of overfitting to the benchmark during a fine-tuning phase. This strict protocol provides a clear, unbiased signal of how close current technologies are to the "substitution threshold" of human expert labor.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The introduction of MMMU yields several fundamental insights that challenge the prevailing narrative of rapid progress toward Artificial General Intelligence (AGI). Unlike prior works that often highlight incremental gains on specific tasks, MMMU exposes systemic weaknesses in current Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) when faced with the rigors of professional expertise. The following insights distinguish between superficial capabilities and the deep, integrated reasoning required for Expert AGI.

### 4.1 The "Expertise Gap": A Stark Disparity Between Commonsense and Professional Reasoning
The most significant innovation of MMMU is its ability to quantify the **Expertise Gap**—the massive drop in performance when models transition from daily commonsense tasks to college-level professional problems.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** Previous benchmarks like **VQA-v2** or **ScienceQA** suggested that models were nearing human-level performance, with some achieving over 90% accuracy. However, these datasets largely consist of elementary school-level questions or natural images (e.g., "What color is the car?"). MMMU reveals that this high performance is brittle; it does not transfer to expert domains.
*   **The Insight:** There is a non-linear relationship between task difficulty and model capability. While models perform reasonably well on "Easy" questions (e.g., **GPT-4V** achieves **76.1%** on Easy items), their performance collapses as complexity increases. On "Hard" questions requiring multi-step derivation and deep domain knowledge, GPT-4V drops to **31.2%**, barely outperforming random guessing in some subfields.
*   **Significance:** This finding fundamentally alters the assessment of AGI readiness. It demonstrates that current models have mastered **pattern recognition** (identifying objects or reading text) but have not yet achieved **deliberate reasoning** (applying abstract principles to novel scenarios). The gap between the best human experts (**88.6%** accuracy) and the best AI (**55.7%** for GPT-4V, **59%** for Gemini Ultra) indicates that we are far from the "substitution threshold" for professional labor, contrary to claims based on simpler benchmarks.

### 4.2 The Failure of "Text-Only" Augmentation: Vision Requires Joint Embedding, Not Just OCR
A critical technical insight from MMMU is the inadequacy of decoupling visual perception from language reasoning. The study explicitly tests whether text-only Large Language Models (LLMs) can solve these problems if aided by external tools like Optical Character Recognition (OCR) or image captioning.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** Many earlier approaches assumed that if an LLM could simply "read" the text in an image (via OCR) or get a description (via a captioner), it could solve the problem using its vast textual knowledge base. This treated vision as a pre-processing step rather than an integral part of the reasoning loop.
*   **The Insight:** Augmenting text-only LLMs (like **Llama2** or **GPT-4 Text**) with OCR or LLaVA-generated captions yields **negligible performance improvements**. For instance, adding OCR to FLAN-T5-XXL only improved scores marginally, failing to bridge the gap to dedicated LMMs.
*   **Significance:** This proves that solving expert-level multimodal problems requires **joint interpretation**, not just sequential processing. The visual information (e.g., the spatial arrangement of components in a circuit diagram, the slope of a curve in a calculus problem, or the specific shading in an MRI scan) carries semantic meaning that is lost when converted to linear text. The failure of OCR-based baselines confirms that future architectures must integrate visual encoders deeply into the reasoning engine, rather than treating images as mere containers for text.

### 4.3 Heterogeneous Perception: The "Long Tail" of Visual Formats as a Bottleneck
MMMU introduces the concept of **Heterogeneous Perception** as a primary bottleneck for AGI. Unlike previous datasets dominated by natural photographs, MMMU forces models to confront a "long tail" of 30 distinct, non-natural image types.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** Standard benchmarks typically evaluate models on photographs of everyday scenes (dogs, cars, street signs). MMMU diversifies this to include **sheet music, chemical structures, geometric proofs, political cartoons, and medical scans**.
*   **The Insight:** Model performance is highly correlated with the familiarity of the image type. Models perform relatively well on **Photographs** and **Paintings** (domains heavily represented in pre-training data like LAION) but suffer catastrophic failures on specialized formats. For example, on **Geometric Shapes** and **Sheet Music**, even advanced models score near random chance.
*   **Significance:** This highlights a critical data bias in current foundation models. True Expert AGI cannot rely solely on internet-scale web data, which is skewed toward natural images. Achieving expert performance requires curating training data that specifically covers these specialized visual languages. The inability to interpret a **chemical structure** or a **circuit diagram** is not a minor edge case; it represents a fundamental lack of "visual literacy" in professional domains.

### 4.4 Error Taxonomy: Perception vs. Knowledge vs. Reasoning
Through a detailed error analysis of 150 cases from GPT-4V, MMMU provides a novel taxonomy of failure modes that disentangles where the reasoning chain breaks. This moves beyond a simple "correct/incorrect" metric to diagnose the root cause of errors.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** Most evaluations stop at accuracy scores. MMMU categorizes errors into **Perceptual (35%)**, **Lack of Knowledge (29%)**, and **Reasoning (26%)**.
*   **The Insight:**
    *   **Perceptual Errors (35%):** Surprisingly, the largest category involves basic visual misinterpretations, such as misreading the order of items in a diagram or confusing a "double circle" (accept state) in a DFA with a standard node. This shows that even state-of-the-art models lack robust fine-grained grounding.
    *   **Domain-Specific Knowledge (29%):** Models often hallucinate facts or apply incorrect domain rules (e.g., misinterpreting medical symptoms or historical contexts), revealing gaps in their parametric knowledge despite massive training corpora.
    *   **Reasoning Flaws (26%):** Even when perception and knowledge are correct, models fail to chain logical steps together, often skipping critical intermediate calculations or drawing non-sequitur conclusions.
*   **Significance:** This taxonomy directs future research priorities. It suggests that simply scaling model size or adding more text data will not fix **perceptual grounding** issues. Instead, the community must develop better mechanisms for **visual grounding** (linking text tokens to specific image regions) and **structured reasoning** (perhaps via neuro-symbolic approaches or improved chain-of-thought prompting) to address the distinct failure modes identified.

### 4.5 The Necessity of Interleaved and Multi-Image Context
MMMU innovates by enforcing **interleaved text-image inputs** and **multi-image scenarios**, challenging the standard "image + question" paradigm.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** Traditional VQA formats typically present an image followed by a text question. MMMU includes cases where images are embedded in the middle of the text, or where multiple images (e.g., three different MRI views: T1, T2, and subtraction) must be compared simultaneously to derive an answer.
*   **The Insight:** Models struggle significantly when the context window requires switching attention back and forth between modalities or synthesizing information across multiple visual sources. The performance drop in these scenarios indicates that current attention mechanisms are not yet optimized for complex, non-linear multimodal contexts.
*   **Significance:** This establishes a new requirement for next-generation architectures: **dynamic multimodal attention**. To achieve Expert AGI, models must be able to treat images and text as a unified, interleaved stream of information, capable of referencing specific parts of an image while generating text, and integrating evidence from multiple visual sources to form a coherent conclusion.

In summary, MMMU does not just provide a harder test; it redefines the metrics of success for multimodal AI. It shifts the focus from "can the model see?" to "can the model think like an expert?" The insights derived reveal that while we have made strides in visual recognition, the path to Expert AGI requires breakthroughs in **joint multimodal reasoning**, **specialized visual literacy**, and **robust logical chaining**.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evaluation conducted in the MMMU paper. The authors do not merely report accuracy scores; they construct a multi-dimensional analysis to diagnose *why* models fail. The experiments are designed to isolate specific capabilities: broad domain knowledge, handling of heterogeneous visual formats, resilience to difficulty scaling, and the distinct contributions of perception versus reasoning.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Experimental Setup

The experimental design prioritizes a **zero-shot** setting to measure the inherent generalization capabilities of pre-trained models, avoiding the confounding variable of overfitting to the benchmark during fine-tuning.

**Datasets and Splits:**
The evaluation utilizes the full **11,550** questions of the MMMU benchmark, partitioned as follows:
*   **Development Set (150 questions):** Used exclusively for few-shot prompting examples (5 per subject).
*   **Validation Set (900 questions):** Used for hyperparameter selection and prompt engineering optimization.
*   **Test Set (10,500 questions):** The primary dataset for reporting final performance metrics.

**Baselines and Model Diversity:**
The study evaluates a comprehensive suite of **28 open-source Large Multimodal Models (LMMs)** alongside leading proprietary systems. The baselines are categorized to test different architectural hypotheses:
1.  **State-of-the-Art LMMs:** Includes proprietary models like **GPT-4V(ision)**, **Gemini 1.5 Pro**, and **Claude 3 Opus**, and open-source models like **LLaVA-1.6-34B**, **InternVL-Chat-V1.2**, and **Qwen-VL-MAX**. These models integrate visual encoders directly with language backbones.
2.  **Text-Only LLMs with Augmentation:** To test if vision is strictly necessary or if text extraction suffices, the authors evaluate text-only models (e.g., **Llama2-7B**, **GPT-4 Text**, **FLAN-T5-XXL**) augmented with:
    *   **OCR:** Using `MMOCR` to extract text from images.
    *   **Captioning:** Using `LLaVA-1.5` to generate detailed image descriptions.
    This setup isolates whether the bottleneck is *reading* the image or *reasoning* with visual semantics.
3.  **Human Experts:** A crucial baseline for "Expert AGI" calibration. The authors recruited **90 college senior students** (3 per subject) to solve the validation set. They were allowed to consult textbooks but prohibited from internet searches. Their performance establishes the "90th percentile of skilled adults" target.
4.  **Random Baselines:** "Random Choice" (uniform selection) and "Frequent Choice" (selecting the most common option per subject) serve as lower bounds.

**Metrics and Evaluation Pipeline:**
The primary metric is **micro-averaged accuracy**. Recognizing that models often generate verbose reasoning chains before stating an answer, the authors implemented a robust, rule-based evaluation pipeline:
*   **Extraction:** Regular expressions are used to parse model outputs, extracting key numerical values, option letters (A/B/C/D), or specific conclusion phrases.
*   **Penalty:** If a model fails to produce a parsable answer, it is marked incorrect for open-ended questions. For multiple-choice questions, a random selection is assigned as a penalty to prevent models from gaming the system by refusing to answer.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results: The Expertise Gap

The core finding of the experiments is the stark disparity between human expert performance and current AI capabilities, validating the benchmark's difficulty.

**Overall Performance Ceiling:**
As detailed in **Table 2**, even the most advanced proprietary models fall significantly short of human experts.
*   **Human Experts:** Achieved an average accuracy of **82.6%** (Medium performance) to **88.6%** (Best performance) on the validation set. This confirms the questions are solvable by the target demographic (skilled adults).
*   **Proprietary Leaders:** **GPT-4V** achieves **55.7%** on the test set, while **Gemini 1.5 Pro** reaches **62.2%**. While these scores are well above the random baseline (~24%), they represent a massive gap of nearly **30 percentage points** compared to human experts.
*   **Open-Source Models:** The best open-source models at the time of submission, such as **LLaVA-1.6-34B** and **InternVL-Chat-V1.2**, achieved **44.7%** and **46.2%** respectively. Smaller models (e.g., **Kosmos2**, **Fuyu-8B**) hovered near **27%**, barely outperforming random guessing.

**The "Hard" Problem Collapse:**
**Table 3** decomposes performance by difficulty level (Easy, Medium, Hard), revealing a non-linear collapse in capability as complexity increases.
*   **Easy Questions:** GPT-4V performs respectably at **76.1%**, suggesting it has mastered basic visual recognition and simple fact retrieval.
*   **Medium Questions:** Performance drops to **55.6%**.
*   **Hard Questions:** Performance plummets to **31.2%**. At this level, the gap between GPT-4V and open-source models (which score ~26-29%) nearly vanishes. This indicates that for truly complex, multi-step expert reasoning, current scaling laws have not yet yielded a decisive advantage; even the largest models struggle to chain logic effectively.

**Disciplinary Disparities:**
Performance is not uniform across the six disciplines (**Table 2**).
*   **High Performance Domains:** Models perform relatively better in **Art & Design** (GPT-4V: **65.3%**) and **Humanities & Social Science** (GPT-4V: **76.3%**). These fields often rely on "natural" images (paintings, cartoons) and contextual knowledge that is well-represented in web-scale training data.
*   **Low Performance Domains:** Significant struggles occur in **Tech & Engineering** (GPT-4V: **41.7%**) and **Science** (GPT-4V: **48.4%**). These domains require interpreting abstract diagrams (circuits, chemical structures) and applying rigorous mathematical logic, areas where current LMMs show fundamental weaknesses.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

The paper includes critical ablation studies that challenge common assumptions about how to improve multimodal performance.

**The Failure of OCR and Captioning Augmentation:**
A pivotal experiment tested whether text-only LLMs could match LMMs if provided with perfect text extraction.
*   **Setup:** The authors fed OCR output or LLaVA-generated captions into strong text-only models like **GPT-4 Text** and **FLAN-T5-XXL**.
*   **Result:** As shown in **Table 2**, adding OCR to FLAN-T5-XXL improved its score only marginally (from **31.2%** to **31.9%**). Similarly, adding captions to Vicuna-13B yielded negligible gains.
*   **Implication:** This convincingly supports the claim that **joint visual-language embedding** is essential. The spatial relationships in a circuit diagram or the specific shading in an MRI scan carry semantic information that is lost when converted to linear text. Simply "reading" the image is insufficient for expert reasoning; the model must "see" and reason over the visual structure directly.

**Few-Shot Learning Inefficacy:**
In **Appendix G (Table 14)**, the authors tested few-shot learning on models capable of in-context learning (OpenFlamingo, Otter).
*   **Result:** Contrary to expectations, increasing the number of shots (from 0 to 5) did not improve performance. OpenFlamingo dropped from **26.3%** (0-shot) to **25.6%** (1-shot), and Otter declined from **29.1%** to **25.8%** (5-shot).
*   **Implication:** This suggests that the reasoning patterns required for MMMU are too complex for current models to infer from a handful of examples. The tasks demand deep parametric knowledge and robust reasoning engines that cannot be quickly "primed" by context alone.

### 5.4 Error Analysis: Diagnosing the Failure Modes

To move beyond accuracy scores, the authors performed a granular error analysis on **150 randomly sampled error cases** from GPT-4V. The results, visualized in **Figure 5**, provide a taxonomy of failure that guides future research.

1.  **Perceptual Errors (35%):** Surprisingly, the largest category of errors is not reasoning, but basic perception.
    *   *Nature of Error:* Models fail to correctly identify visual elements or their spatial relationships. For example, in **Figure 6**, GPT-4V correctly understands the concept of "egoism vs. altruism" but fails to map the text description "left to right, top to bottom" to the correct panel in a comic strip. In **Figure 83** (Computer Science), the model fails to recognize a "double circle" as an accept state in a DFA diagram.
    *   *Significance:* This indicates that even state-of-the-art models lack robust **fine-grained grounding**. They can hallucinate objects or misinterpret simple geometric arrangements, breaking the reasoning chain before it begins.

2.  **Lack of Knowledge (29%):**
    *   *Nature of Error:* The model perceives the image correctly but lacks the specific domain fact. For instance, in **Figure 13** (Art History), GPT-4V identifies the painting *Las Meninas* but incorrectly states its original placement, hallucinating a plausible but false historical context.
    *   *Significance:* This highlights gaps in the parametric knowledge of foundation models regarding specialized, non-commonsense domains.

3.  **Reasoning Errors (26%):**
    *   *Nature of Error:* The model has the correct perception and knowledge but fails to execute the logical derivation. In **Figure 26** (Finance), GPT-4V correctly identifies the efficient frontier but fails to select the optimal portfolio point due to a flaw in comparing risk-return trade-offs.
    *   *Significance:* This confirms that **deliberate reasoning**—chaining multiple logical steps—is a distinct bottleneck separate from perception or knowledge retrieval.

4.  **Other Errors (10%):** Includes textual misunderstanding, refusal to answer, and extraction errors.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Validity

Do these experiments convincingly support the paper's claims?

*   **Support for "Expert AGI" Gap:** **Yes.** The inclusion of human expert baselines (scoring ~88%) alongside model scores (~55%) provides irrefutable evidence that current models are not yet at the "substitution threshold" for professional labor. The gap is too large to be attributed to noise.
*   **Support for "Joint Perception" Necessity:** **Yes.** The ablation study showing that OCR/Captioning fails to boost text-only LLMs is a strong control experiment. It effectively rules out the hypothesis that "better text extraction" is the solution, forcing the conclusion that integrated visual reasoning is required.
*   **Support for "Heterogeneous Perception" Challenge:** **Yes.** The breakdown by image type (Figure 4, Table 13 in Appendix) shows a clear correlation: models succeed on natural images (Photos, Paintings) and fail on specialized diagrams (Chemical Structures, Sheet Music). This validates the claim that current training data biases models toward natural scenes.

**Limitations and Conditional Results:**
*   **Evaluation Metric Rigidity:** The reliance on regex-based extraction, while necessary for scale, may penalize models that provide correct reasoning but format the final answer slightly differently. However, the "random selection" penalty for unparseable answers likely balances this by preventing false positives.
*   **Dynamic Leaderboard:** The paper acknowledges that results are a snapshot. As noted in the **Version Change Log (Appendix J)**, newer models (e.g., GPT-4o, Claude 3.5) released after the initial submission have narrowed the gap (GPT-4o reaching **69.1%**). While this shows progress, the fundamental gap with human experts (~20 points) and the specific failure modes (perception vs. reasoning) identified in the analysis remain relevant.
*   **Subjectivity in Error Analysis:** The categorization of errors (Perception vs. Knowledge) involves human judgment. While the authors used expert annotators, the boundary between "misseeing" a diagram and "misunderstanding" its domain implication can sometimes be blurry.

In conclusion, the experimental analysis is rigorous and multi-faceted. It successfully moves the field beyond simple accuracy leaderboards to a diagnostic understanding of *why* models fail. The data convincingly argues that achieving Expert AGI requires breakthroughs not just in scaling, but in **fine-grained visual grounding**, **specialized domain knowledge injection**, and **robust multi-step logical chaining**.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While MMMU establishes a rigorous new standard for evaluating Expert AGI, the authors explicitly acknowledge several critical limitations, trade-offs, and unresolved challenges inherent in their approach. These constraints define the boundaries of what the benchmark can currently prove and highlight areas where future iterations must evolve.

### 6.1 The "Necessary but Not Sufficient" Constraint
The most fundamental limitation of MMMU is its scope relative to the definition of **Expert AGI**. The authors clarify that strong performance on MMMU is a **necessary** condition for Expert AGI, but it is **not sufficient**.
*   **The Mapping Gap:** The definition of Expert AGI (Level 3 in the Morris et al. taxonomy) requires performance at the **90th percentile of skilled adults** across a *broad range of tasks*. While MMMU covers 30 subjects, it is impossible to map a specific accuracy score on this benchmark directly to the "90th percentile" metric across all human labor. College exams represent a specific subset of expert tasks (academic problem solving) but do not encompass the full spectrum of professional duties, such as real-time decision-making under uncertainty, physical manipulation, or complex social negotiation.
*   **Task Diversity:** As noted in the **Introduction**, college exams are not the *only* tasks an AGI must tackle. A model could theoretically achieve high scores on MMMU by memorizing textbook patterns while failing at novel, out-of-distribution real-world applications that require adaptive creativity rather than standardized testing skills. Therefore, MMMU serves as a high-bar filter, but passing it does not guarantee general substitutability for human labor.

### 6.2 Data Curation Biases and Subjectivity
The construction of MMMU relies heavily on human judgment, introducing potential biases that affect the benchmark's neutrality and generalizability.
*   **Manual Curation Bias:** The dataset was curated by **50 college students** and co-authors. While this ensures quality, it introduces subjectivity in two key areas:
    1.  **Difficulty Calibration:** The exclusion of the "very easy" ~10% of questions (Section 3.2) relied on human annotators categorizing difficulty. What one annotator considers "medium" reasoning, another might view as "hard," potentially skewing the distribution of challenge levels.
    2.  **Subject Selection:** The decision to exclude disciplines like **Law** and **Linguistics** (Section 3.2) because they lacked sufficient *multimodal* problems creates a disciplinary bias. The benchmark over-represents fields where visual data is structural (e.g., Engineering, Medicine) and under-represents fields where expertise is primarily textual or argumentative. This limits the benchmark's ability to claim total "generality" across *all* human expertise.
*   **Copyright and Source Constraints:** To avoid data contamination, annotators were instructed to avoid sources prohibiting redistribution and to seek answers hidden in separate documents. While this improves evaluation integrity, it restricts the pool of available questions. The resulting dataset may inadvertently favor older textbooks or specific types of educational resources that adhere to these licensing constraints, potentially missing cutting-edge problems from modern, proprietary, or open-web educational platforms.

### 6.3 Evaluation Rigidity and the "Reasoning vs. Formatting" Trade-off
The evaluation methodology employs a robust, rule-based pipeline using **regular expressions** to extract answers from model generations (Section 4.1). While this ensures scalability and consistency for 11.5K questions, it introduces a trade-off between **formatting strictness** and **reasoning recognition**.
*   **Penalty for Verbose Reasoning:** Models that generate correct, detailed chain-of-thought reasoning but fail to format the final answer exactly as the regex expects (e.g., missing a specific delimiter or placing the conclusion in an unexpected location) are penalized. For open-ended questions, if the parser fails to extract a valid answer, the response is marked incorrect.
*   **Random Selection Penalty:** For multiple-choice questions where no valid answer is extracted, the system assigns a **random selection** as a penalty. While this prevents models from gaming the system by refusing to answer, it adds statistical noise to the results, particularly for models that frequently hallucinate or refuse to answer due to safety filters.
*   **Lack of Partial Credit:** The binary nature of the evaluation (correct/incorrect) does not account for **partial correctness**. In complex engineering or medical problems, a model might execute 90% of the derivation correctly but make a minor arithmetic error at the end. MMMU treats this as a total failure, which may underestimate a model's actual competency in domain logic.

### 6.4 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases
MMMU focuses on static, single-turn problem solving, leaving several dynamic and interactive expert scenarios unaddressed.
*   **Static vs. Dynamic Context:** All questions in MMMU are static images and text. Real-world expert tasks often involve **dynamic visual inputs** (e.g., analyzing a video of a mechanical failure, monitoring a real-time patient vitals dashboard) or **interactive exploration** (e.g., zooming into specific regions of a high-resolution histology slide, rotating a 3D molecular structure). MMMU does not test a model's ability to actively query visual information or handle temporal sequences.
*   **Tool Use and External Computation:** The benchmark evaluates the model's *internal* reasoning capabilities in a zero-shot setting. It does not assess the model's ability to **use external tools** (e.g., calling a Python interpreter for complex calculus, using a specialized medical database API, or running a circuit simulator). An "Expert AGI" in practice would likely leverage such tools to overcome internal computational limitations. By isolating the model, MMMU measures raw parametric capability but not the potential of an agentic system augmented with tools.
*   **Multi-Image Limitations:** While MMMU includes examples with multiple images (**7.39%** of the dataset), the complexity is limited to a small number of static views (e.g., three MRI slices). It does not address scenarios requiring the synthesis of dozens of images or the integration of visual data with large-scale tabular databases, which are common in fields like genomics or climate science.

### 6.5 Computational and Scalability Constraints
The sheer scale and heterogeneity of MMMU impose significant computational burdens on evaluation.
*   **Inference Cost:** Evaluating 10,500 test questions with large multimodal models (especially those with high-resolution image encoders) requires substantial GPU resources. The paper notes the use of **NVIDIA A100 GPUs** for experiments. As models grow larger and image resolutions increase to capture fine details in diagrams or medical scans, the cost of running the full benchmark becomes prohibitive for many research groups, potentially limiting who can participate in the leaderboard.
*   **Context Window Limits:** The interleaved nature of the data (images in the middle of text, multiple images per question) tests the limits of model context windows. While current models handle the average question length (~59 tokens + images), future expansions to more complex, document-level multimodal reasoning may exceed the context capacity of existing architectures, requiring new efficient attention mechanisms that are not yet standard.

### 6.6 Open Questions and Future Directions
The results of MMMU raise several open questions that remain unresolved:
*   **The "Perception Ceiling":** With **35%** of GPT-4V's errors attributed to basic perceptual failures (Figure 5), it is unclear whether this is a data scarcity issue (lack of specialized images in training) or an architectural limitation (inability to ground fine-grained details). Resolving this requires determining if scaling data alone can fix these errors or if new inductive biases are needed.
*   **The Path to "Hard" Problems:** The collapse in performance on "Hard" questions (Table 3) suggests that current scaling laws may not automatically yield better multi-step reasoning. It remains an open question whether **neuro-symbolic approaches**, **explicit planning modules**, or **agentic workflows** are required to bridge the gap from ~30% to human-level performance on complex derivations.
*   **Dynamic Benchmarking:** As noted in the **Version Change Log (Appendix J)**, models like **GPT-4o** have already improved scores significantly post-submission. This raises the question of benchmark saturation. How quickly will MMMU become "easy" for next-generation models? The community must consider mechanisms for continuously curating new, harder questions to maintain the benchmark's utility as a measure of the frontier.

In summary, while MMMU provides the most comprehensive assessment of Expert AGI capabilities to date, it is a snapshot of a specific type of academic expertise. Its limitations in dynamic interaction, tool use, and disciplinary breadth remind us that it is a **stepping stone**, not the final destination, in the journey toward Artificial General Intelligence.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of MMMU fundamentally shifts the trajectory of multimodal AI research from a focus on **commonsense recognition** to **expert-level reasoning**. By demonstrating that state-of-the-art models fail catastrophically on college-level tasks despite high scores on elementary benchmarks, MMMU forces a recalibration of what constitutes "progress" toward Artificial General Intelligence (AGI). The implications extend beyond evaluation metrics, dictating new priorities for model architecture, data curation, and practical deployment strategies.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Research Landscape: From "Seeing" to "Thinking"
MMMU dismantles the prevailing assumption that scaling data and parameters alone will naturally yield expert capabilities. The benchmark reveals three critical paradigm shifts required for the field:

*   **The End of "Visual Captioning" as a Proxy for Understanding:**
    Prior to MMMU, success in multimodal tasks was often equated with the ability to generate accurate image captions or answer simple "what/where" questions. MMMU proves that **captioning is insufficient for reasoning**. A model can describe a circuit diagram perfectly yet fail to calculate the voltage because it cannot map the visual topology to physical laws. Future research must move beyond treating vision as a pre-processing step for text generation and instead develop architectures where visual features are **first-class citizens in the reasoning loop**, capable of supporting multi-step logical derivations.

*   **Redefining the "Long Tail" of Visual Data:**
    The field has historically optimized for natural images (photos of objects, scenes, people) due to the abundance of web-scale datasets like LAION. MMMU exposes a severe **data bias**: models perform well on photographs but collapse on specialized formats like **sheet music, chemical structures, and geometric proofs** (Section 4.3). This implies that the path to Expert AGI requires a deliberate shift in data collection strategies. Researchers must curate **domain-specific visual corpora** that cover the "long tail" of professional visual languages, rather than relying on passive web scraping which under-represents these critical formats.

*   **From Zero-Shot Generalization to Specialized Alignment:**
    The failure of few-shot learning on MMMU (Appendix G) suggests that expert reasoning cannot be "prompted" into existence with a few examples. The gap between "Easy" and "Hard" questions indicates that current models lack the **parametric knowledge** and **structural inductive biases** required for complex domains. This shifts the research focus from prompt engineering to **domain-adaptive pre-training** and **fine-tuning on expert reasoning chains**, where models are explicitly trained to link visual cues to domain-specific axioms (e.g., Kirchhoff's laws, medical diagnostic criteria).

### 7.2 Enabling Follow-Up Research Directions
MMMU provides a rigorous testbed that enables and necessitates several specific lines of inquiry:

*   **Fine-Grained Visual Grounding and Layout Analysis:**
    With **35%** of errors attributed to basic perceptual failures (Figure 5), such as misidentifying nodes in a graph or misreading the order of panels in a comic, there is an urgent need for research into **fine-grained grounding**. Future work should explore:
    *   **Region-of-Interest (ROI) Attention:** Mechanisms that allow models to dynamically zoom into or attend to specific sub-regions of a complex diagram (e.g., a specific resistor in a circuit) before reasoning.
    *   **Structure-Aware Encoders:** Developing visual encoders specifically trained to recognize structural relationships (connectivity in graphs, hierarchy in trees, spatial logic in blueprints) rather than just semantic content.

*   **Neuro-Symbolic and Tool-Augmented Reasoning:**
    The **26%** rate of reasoning errors (Figure 5), where models fail to chain logical steps even with correct perception, suggests that pure neural approaches may hit a ceiling. MMMU encourages the integration of **neuro-symbolic methods**:
    *   **External Tool Use:** Training models to recognize when a problem requires precise calculation (e.g., calculus, circuit analysis) and offloading that step to a Python interpreter or symbolic solver, rather than attempting to compute it via next-token prediction.
    *   **Explicit Planning Modules:** Architectures that generate a structured plan (e.g., "1. Identify components, 2. Retrieve formula, 3. Substitute values, 4. Calculate") before executing the solution, ensuring logical consistency.

*   **Curriculum Learning for Multimodal Models:**
    The clear stratification of difficulty in MMMU (Easy vs. Medium vs. Hard) offers a unique opportunity for **curriculum learning**. Researchers can now train models on a progression of tasks, starting with commonsense visual QA and gradually introducing college-level problems with interleaved text and images. This could help models build the foundational visual literacy required for expert tasks without being overwhelmed by complexity early in training.

*   **Bias Mitigation in Domain Knowledge:**
    The **29%** error rate due to lack of knowledge highlights gaps in the training corpora. Follow-up research should investigate **knowledge injection techniques**, such as Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) tailored for multimodal contexts, where models can retrieve relevant textbook excerpts or domain manuals conditioned on the visual input to fill parametric knowledge gaps.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases
While MMMU is an academic benchmark, the capabilities it tests are directly transferable to high-value industrial and societal applications:

*   **Automated Expert Assistants:**
    The primary downstream use case is the development of **AI co-pilots for professionals**.
    *   **Medical Diagnostics:** Models trained to pass the "Health & Medicine" section of MMMU could assist radiologists in interpreting MRI/CT scans (Section 4.3), reducing diagnostic errors and workload.
    *   **Engineering Design:** Systems capable of solving the "Tech & Engineering" problems could automate the initial review of circuit diagrams, structural blueprints, or thermodynamic simulations, flagging potential design flaws before human review.
    *   **Legal and Financial Analysis:** Although Law was excluded, the reasoning skills tested in Business and Humanities sections are applicable to analyzing financial charts, contract diagrams, and economic models for due diligence.

*   **Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS):**
    MMMU's source material (college exams and textbooks) makes it an ideal foundation for **next-generation educational AI**. Unlike current chatbots that often hallucinate or provide superficial answers, a model validated on MMMU could:
    *   Provide step-by-step solutions to complex STEM problems.
    *   Generate personalized practice questions based on a student's weak areas (e.g., "You struggled with organic chemistry mechanisms; here is a new problem").
    *   Evaluate student answers not just for correctness, but for the validity of their reasoning chain.

*   **Scientific Discovery Automation:**
    The ability to interpret heterogeneous data types (charts, chemical structures, microscopic images) is crucial for **automated literature review and hypothesis generation**. An MMMU-capable model could scan thousands of research papers, extract data from diverse figure types, and synthesize findings across disciplines to propose new research directions or identify conflicting results.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance
For researchers and practitioners looking to adopt MMMU, the following guidance outlines when and how to integrate this benchmark into their workflows:

*   **When to Prefer MMMU Over Alternatives:**
    *   **Use MMMU when:** Your goal is to evaluate **expert-level reasoning** or **domain-specific visual literacy**. If your application involves medical imaging, engineering schematics, or financial charts, MMMU is the necessary stress test. It is superior to benchmarks like **ScienceQA** (which is too elementary) or **MMLU** (which is text-only) for these use cases.
    *   **Do NOT use MMMU when:** You are evaluating basic object detection, image captioning, or commonsense VQA. For tasks like "identify the dog in the image" or "what color is the car," simpler benchmarks (e.g., COCO, VQA-v2) are more efficient and appropriate. MMMU is overkill for non-expert tasks and will yield uninformative low scores.

*   **Integration Strategy:**
    *   **Baseline Establishment:** Start by running the **zero-shot** evaluation protocol described in Section 4.1 using the provided development set for prompt tuning. Compare your model against the reported baselines (Table 2) to gauge its position on the "Expertise Gap."
    *   **Error-Driven Improvement:** Do not just optimize for overall accuracy. Use the **error taxonomy** (Perception vs. Knowledge vs. Reasoning) to diagnose failures.
        *   If **Perception** errors dominate: Invest in higher-resolution image encoders or fine-tune on domain-specific visual datasets (e.g., chemical structure databases).
        *   If **Reasoning** errors dominate: Implement Chain-of-Thought (CoT) fine-tuning or integrate external tools (calculators, code interpreters).
    *   **Dynamic Evaluation:** Given the rapid pace of model improvement (e.g., GPT-4o's gains noted in Appendix J), treat MMMU as a **living benchmark**. Regularly re-evaluate models against the latest leaderboard and consider contributing new, harder questions to the community to prevent saturation.

*   **Practical Constraints:**
    *   **Compute Requirements:** Evaluating the full 10.5K test set with high-resolution images requires significant GPU resources (e.g., NVIDIA A100s). For rapid iteration, use the **900-question validation set** for hyperparameter tuning and reserve the test set for final reporting.
    *   **Data Licensing:** Adhere strictly to the copyright guidelines outlined in the data curation protocol (Appendix H). While the benchmark questions are available for research, ensure that any derived models or commercial applications respect the underlying intellectual property of the source textbooks and exams.

In conclusion, MMMU serves as both a **mirror** reflecting the current limitations of multimodal AI and a **compass** pointing toward the necessary innovations for Expert AGI. By shifting the focus from "can the model see?" to "can the model reason like an expert?", it challenges the community to build systems that are not just broadly competent, but deeply knowledgeable and logically robust.
