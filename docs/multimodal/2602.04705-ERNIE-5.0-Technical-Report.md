## 1. Executive Summary

ERNIE 5.0 introduces a natively autoregressive, trillion-parameter foundation model that unifies multimodal understanding and generation across text, image, video, and audio under a single next-group-of-tokens prediction objective, overcoming the fragmentation of modality-specific decoders in prior late-fusion approaches. Leveraging an ultra-sparse mixture-of-experts (MoE) architecture with modality-agnostic routing and a novel elastic training paradigm, the model enables flexible deployment of sub-models with varying depths and sparsity within a single pre-training run, achieving over 15% decoding speedup with minor accuracy loss while maintaining strong performance on benchmarks like MMLU-Pro and GenEval. This work represents the first production-scale realization of a unified autoregressive model supporting both multimodal understanding and generation, providing a scalable pathway for efficient large-scale deployment under diverse resource constraints.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Fragmentation Problem in Multimodal AI

The fundamental challenge ERNIE 5.0 addresses is a structural fragmentation that has persisted in multimodal foundation models: **the decoupling of understanding from generation**. While recent large language and vision-language models have demonstrated impressive capabilities in multimodal *understanding* (interpreting images, audio, and video alongside text), their *generation* capabilities remain constrained. Most existing systems produce text-centric outputs even when processing rich multimodal inputs, which severely limits how these models can interact with the world.

Consider the practical implication: a model that can analyze a medical scan and write a report (understanding) but cannot generate annotated visual explanations or synthesize related audio descriptions (generation) is fundamentally limited in its utility. This asymmetry creates what the paper terms an "ability seesaw" problem — improvements in one modality often come at the expense of another, and generation capabilities lag behind understanding capabilities.

### Why Late-Fusion Approaches Fall Short

To understand why this problem has been difficult to solve, we must examine the dominant architectural paradigm: **late-fusion designs**. As described in the Introduction (Section 1), most existing multimodal systems follow this pattern:

1. Start with a pre-trained language model backbone
2. Augment it with modality-specific decoders or generators
3. Connect these components through late-fusion mechanisms

This approach appears practical but introduces several critical weaknesses:

- **Decoupled optimization**: Understanding and generation are trained under different objectives. Understanding typically uses autoregressive next-token prediction, while generation often relies on non-autoregressive objectives (e.g., diffusion losses for images). This creates inconsistent optimization trajectories that hinder deep cross-modal integration.

- **Modality-specific heuristics**: Each modality requires its own expert allocation rules, routing mechanisms, and architectural tweaks. As the paper notes, this becomes "non-trivial in practice, especially when more than two modalities are involved" (Section 2.1).

- **Core language degradation**: There is an inherent trade-off between multimodal integration and core language performance. Strengthening visual or audio capabilities often weakens text reasoning, forcing practitioners to choose between specialization and generality.

- **Training from scratch impossibility**: Because these systems augment pre-trained language models, they cannot train all modalities simultaneously from scratch. This prevents the collective evolution of capabilities across modalities.

The paper explicitly references works like Xu et al. (2025a) and Seedream et al. (2025) as examples of this late-fusion paradigm, noting that while effective for individual modalities, these designs "hinder deep cross-modal integration."

### The Unified Autoregressive Challenge

Against this backdrop, the paper positions **unified autoregressive modeling** as the solution — but acknowledges this remains "a prominent open challenge." A truly unified framework must satisfy four simultaneous requirements:

1. **Native support for both understanding and generation** across all modalities (not just text)
2. **Preservation of strong unimodal capabilities** (the model should not become a "jack of all trades, master of none")
3. **Scalability** as model and data sizes continue to grow
4. **Deployment flexibility** under diverse hardware, memory, and latency constraints

The fourth requirement is particularly important and often overlooked. A trillion-parameter model is useless if it cannot be deployed in resource-constrained environments. Traditional approaches address this through post-hoc compression (pruning, distillation), but this requires separate training runs for each target size, multiplying computational costs.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

ERNIE 5.0 positions itself distinctly from the competitive landscape. Table 1 and Table 2 in Section 6 compare against models including:

- **DeepSeek V3.2** (Liu et al., 2024; 2025a)
- **Kimi K2** 
- **Gemini 2.5-Pro and 3-Pro** (DeepMind, 2025a;b)
- **GPT-5** (OpenAI, 2025)
- **Qwen series** (Yang et al., 2025; Bai et al., 2025)

The key differentiator is not just performance on benchmarks, but architectural philosophy. While competitors may achieve strong results on specific tasks, ERNIE 5.0 claims to be "the first production-scale realization of a trillion-parameter unified autoregressive model that supports both multimodal understanding and generation" (Abstract).

This claim rests on three technical pillars that distinguish it from prior work:

| Aspect | Prior Approaches | ERNIE 5.0 |
|--------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Training paradigm** | Augment pre-trained LLM with modality-specific components | Train all modalities from scratch under unified objective |
| **Routing mechanism** | Modality-specific or modality-isolated expert allocation | Modality-agnostic expert routing conditioned on token representations |
| **Deployment flexibility** | Separate models or post-hoc compression for different sizes | Elastic training produces family of sub-models in single run |

The modality-agnostic routing is particularly significant. As explained in Section 2.1, routing decisions are "conditioned on unified token representations rather than modality identifiers." This eliminates heuristic modality-specific expert allocation and allows tokens from various modalities to be dispatched to a shared pool of experts. The paper notes this "promotes cross-modal knowledge generalization and improves single-modality performance through the emergent specialization of shared experts."

### Theoretical and Practical Significance

The importance of this work extends beyond benchmark performance:

**Theoretically**, it demonstrates that heterogeneous modalities with substantially different token semantics, temporal structures, and optimization dynamics can be unified under a single autoregressive framework without catastrophic interference. The success of modality-agnostic routing challenges the assumption that explicit modality boundaries are necessary for stable optimization.

**Practically**, the elastic training paradigm (Section 3.3) addresses a critical deployment gap. Instead of the traditional "train-then-compress" pipeline, elastic training optimizes a family of sub-networks during pre-training. As Figure 4 illustrates, this supports elasticity along three dimensions:
- **Depth**: Varying the number of active transformer layers
- **Width**: Varying the total number of experts in each MoE layer
- **Sparsity**: Varying the number of activated experts per token

The paper reports that reducing routing top-k to 25% during inference yields "over 15% decoding speedup with only minor accuracy loss" (Introduction), while the fully elastic model operates with "only 53.7% activated parameters and 35.8% total parameters" while maintaining competitive performance (Section 6.4.2).

This positions ERNIE 5.0 not just as a model, but as a **deployment paradigm** — a single pre-trained checkpoint that can be instantiated at multiple scales without retraining. For organizations facing diverse hardware constraints (from cloud GPUs to edge devices), this represents a fundamental shift in how foundation models can be produced and deployed.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems architecture paper presenting a unified multimodal foundation model; the core idea is to train text, image, video, and audio from scratch under a single autoregressive objective using an ultra-sparse mixture-of-experts backbone with modality-agnostic routing and elastic training for flexible deployment.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

ERNIE 5.0 is a trillion-parameter AI system that processes text, images, videos, and audio through one shared neural network backbone rather than separate specialized components. It solves the fragmentation problem in multimodal AI by training all modalities together from scratch under a unified prediction objective, while simultaneously learning multiple model sizes within a single training run to enable flexible deployment across different hardware constraints.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The system consists of four major components connected in a sequential pipeline. First, **modality-specific tokenizers** (vision encoder, audio encoder, text embedding) convert raw inputs from each modality into discrete token sequences that share a common representation space. Second, the **unified autoregressive backbone** processes all tokens through stacked transformer layers with ultra-sparse mixture-of-experts (MoE) structure, where a router dynamically assigns each token to a subset of experts from a shared pool. Third, **modality-specific output heads** (text head, vision head, audio head) generate predictions appropriate to each modality's format. Fourth, the **elastic training infrastructure** dynamically samples sub-models with varying depth, width, and sparsity during training, enabling a single checkpoint to produce multiple deployable variants. Information flows from raw multimodal inputs through tokenization into the shared backbone, where cross-modal interactions occur through attention mechanisms, then out through the appropriate output head for generation or understanding tasks.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the unified autoregressive backbone and MoE architecture because this is the core innovation that enables all modalities to share parameters while maintaining specialization through emergent expert behavior.
- We then examine visual modeling (tokenization, understanding, generation) because vision introduces the most complex spatial-temporal structure that the unified framework must accommodate.
- We proceed to audio modeling because it shares conceptual similarities with vision (hierarchical tokenization) but introduces unique depth-wise prediction challenges.
- We cover the pre-training recipe and elastic training paradigm next because these explain how the model learns effectively at scale and produces flexible deployment variants.
- We conclude with post-training reinforcement learning techniques because these address the stability challenges unique to applying RL on ultra-sparse MoE architectures with multimodal inputs.
- Throughout, we reference the infrastructure innovations that make training at this scale computationally feasible.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Unified Autoregressive Backbone with Ultra-Sparse Mixture-of-Experts

The foundation of ERNIE 5.0 is a shared transformer backbone that processes all modalities under a single optimization objective called **Next-Group-of-Tokens Prediction**, which generalizes the standard next-token prediction used in language models to handle modalities that naturally produce groups of tokens rather than single tokens. For text generation, the model adheres to standard **Next-Token Prediction (NTP)** augmented by a **Multi-Token Prediction (MTP)** mechanism that enhances both output quality and inference efficiency, as described in Section 2.1. For vision and audio modalities, generation is formulated as a group-of-tokens prediction task to align their generative processes with the text autoregressive objective, avoiding the inconsistent optimization trajectories that plague late-fusion approaches.

The backbone employs an **ultra-sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture** to scale model capacity efficiently while controlling both training and inference costs. At the core of this architecture is **modality-agnostic expert routing**, where routing decisions are conditioned on unified token representations rather than explicit modality identifiers. This design choice is critical: instead of telling the router "this is an image token" or "this is an audio token," the router examines the token's learned representation and dispatches it to experts based purely on semantic content. The router dispatches tokens from different modalities to a shared pool of experts, enabling effective cross-modal parameter sharing without requiring heuristic modality-specific expert allocation.

The sparsity level is a key configuration: ERNIE 5.0 achieves an **activation rate below 3%**, meaning that for any given forward pass, less than 3% of the total parameters are actively computing. This allows the model to substantially expand its effective capacity (reaching trillion-parameter scale) without incurring a proportional increase in computational overhead. The training is further stabilized by an **auxiliary-loss-free load balancing** strategy (referencing Wang et al., 2024c), which ensures robust expert utilization at scale without requiring explicit regularization losses that can interfere with optimization.

A subtle but important design choice addresses the tension between understanding and generation tasks. Multimodal understanding focuses on abstract and semantic-level concepts, whereas generation requires accurate modeling of fine-grained perceptual details. ERNIE 5.0 is designed to learn a unified representation that captures high-level semantics while preserving fine-grained details, enabling both comprehension and synthesis in a unified manner. In this framework, semantic-level signals guide generative modeling toward global consistency, while generative training strengthens fine-grained perception and detail-sensitive reasoning through mutual reinforcement.

#### Visual Modeling Pipeline

Visual modeling in ERNIE 5.0 treats images as a special case of video (e.g., a single-frame video), sharing the unified design philosophy of visual understanding and generation as shown in Figure 2 (Section 2.2). The visual pipeline consists of three stages: tokenization, understanding representation, and generation prediction.

**Vision Tokenization** employs a **Next-Frame-and-Scale Prediction (NFSP)** paradigm, where image generation is formulated as a Next-Scale Prediction problem, and video generation extends this with Next-Frame Prediction along the temporal dimension (Section 2.2.1). The system first trains a causal 2D multi-scale tokenizer for images through large-scale image pre-training, then inflates it into a causal 3D convolutional tokenizer to unify image and video tokenization within a single model. This progressive design preserves spatial modeling capabilities learned from images while introducing temporal perception for videos, leading to faithful reconstruction of high-level visual elements such as scene text and human faces.

During tokenizer training, two auxiliary supervision signals enhance representation quality and training stability. First, an **adversarial loss** from GAN-based discriminators improves distributional fidelity. Second, a **semantic regularization loss** derived from large-scale vision foundation models preserves high-level semantic consistency. These complementary objectives improve the learnability and stability of visual tokens, facilitating effective autoregressive modeling in the unified backbone.

The tokenizer uses a **bit-wise quantization strategy** that quantizes the unified visual latent representation into a group of bit-codes, where the number of bits directly corresponds to the size of the discrete vocabulary (referencing Han et al., 2025). During ERNIE 5.0 training, a **progressive tokenizer switching strategy** is adopted: training starts with a low-bit tokenizer (small vocabulary) and gradually transitions to higher-bit variants (larger vocabularies). By first learning coarse-grained, low-bit representations and progressively introducing finer-grained, higher-bit tokenizers, the backbone follows a smoother optimization trajectory, effectively alleviating early-stage training instability.

**Visual Understanding** addresses a critical limitation observed in prior systems: visual features are typically compressed by a downsampling module into low-dimensional representations before quantization, and this compression inevitably leads to loss of fine-grained semantic information (Section 2.2.2). ERNIE 5.0 directly leverages **dual-path visual features prior to quantization**, integrating perceptual features extracted by Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with semantic features encoded by a Vision Transformer (ViT). However, naive MLP-based fusion of CNN and ViT features fails to fully exploit their complementary strengths and introduces representational interference.

To solve this, the paper introduces an **Attention-based Patch Merger**. Formally, given a spatial token in an image or a spatio-temporal token in a video, two sets of features are extracted: $F_{cnn} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times K \times D_{cnn}}$ and $F_{vit} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times K \times D_{vit}}$, where $N$ is the number of visual understanding tokens, $K$ is the number of local patches grouped for each token, and $D_{cnn}$ and $D_{vit}$ are the feature dimensions. In image understanding tasks, $K = 4$ spatially adjacent patches are grouped, while in video understanding tasks, $K = 16$ patches spanning 4 neighboring frames are grouped. Before feature fusion, CNN features are projected to match the ViT feature space, then concatenated along the patch dimension to obtain $F_{mrg} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times 2K \times D_{vit}}$. Multi-head self-attention is applied to the concatenated patch tokens:

$$Z = \text{Attn}(F_{mrg})$$

where the attention mechanism jointly models correlations between CNN and ViT features, as well as spatial and temporal dependencies among the group of patches. Finally, mean pooling over the patch dimension produces a compact representation $F_{out} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times D_{vit}}$, which is then projected to align with the embedding dimension of the unified backbone. This attention-based aggregation consistently outperforms both CNN-only and ViT-only baselines without introducing noticeable computational overhead, with particularly pronounced gains in document and chart understanding.

**Visual Generation** employs the NFSP paradigm where the model predicts visual tokens across multiple spatial scales within each image (or each frame) for image generation, while performing frame-wise prediction along the temporal dimension for video generation (Section 2.2.3). When predicting tokens at a certain scale, the model takes previously generated scales as input, and a **scale-wise causal attention mask** is applied: tokens within the current scale are bidirectionally visible and predicted in parallel, while tokens from all previous scales and historical frames are visible in a causal (uni-directional) manner. This disentangles spatial and temporal modeling: intra-frame prediction from low-resolution to high-resolution captures fine-grained spatial structures, whereas next-frame prediction models inter-frame temporal dependencies.

Positional modeling uses a **Unified Spatiotemporal Rotary Positional Embedding (Uni-RoPE)** applied to all tokens in ERNIE 5.0. For a unified sequence of length $N$, the positional encoding of the $i$-th token is defined as:

$$\text{Uni-RoPE}_i = (t_i, h_i, w_i), \quad i \in \{1, \ldots, N\}$$

For text and audio tokens, $t_i = h_i = w_i$, where the shared value follows token index in the sequence. For visual tokens, $t_i$ is used for frame indexing (increasing monotonically to preserve temporal ordering), and $(h_i, w_i)$ corresponds to spatial locations within each frame. To ensure spatial consistency across multi-scales, a **center-aligned coordinate strategy** is adopted, where tokens at different scales are aligned based on the geometric centers of the scale.

To mitigate error accumulation over extremely long token sequences in autoregressive visual generation, the model employs **corruption-based training**: historical tokens are corrupted during training by randomly flipping their bits, while supervising the model to self-correct toward the ground-truth tokens of the current scale. Additionally, a **loss reweighting strategy** emphasizes early-stage predictions to alleviate token imbalance introduced by multi-scale tokenization. For video generation, **windowed temporal attention** and **random historical frame masking** encourage the model to focus on relevant temporal context and improve robustness.

A fundamental challenge in token-based visual generation is that increasing visual resolution enlarges the token sequence length, which reduces the effective training batch size and degrades optimization stability. To address this, ERNIE 5.0 adopts a **cascaded diffusion refiner** on top of the autoregressive backbone. The backbone generates low-resolution samples with precise semantics and structural layout, while the refiner focuses on enhancing fine-grained visual details at higher resolution. The diffusion refiner is trained separately from the backbone using paired low-resolution samples with controlled degradation and their corresponding high-resolution images or videos. This decoupled training scheme enables high-fidelity refinement while preserving the complete semantic and structure produced by the autoregressive model, avoiding optimization conflicts caused by introducing autoregressive and diffusion losses within a shared backbone.

#### Audio Modeling Pipeline

Audio modeling follows a similar unified autoregressive, token-based framework that supports understanding and high-fidelity generation (Section 2.3). Audio signals are represented as hierarchical discrete codec tokens that capture higher-level semantics and fine-grained acoustic details, inspired by neural audio codecs (Kumar et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b).

**Audio Tokenization** maps the continuous audio signal into a sequence of discrete tokens using a codec-style tokenizer with a token rate of 12.5 Hz (Section 2.3.1). The audio quantization module follows a **Residual Vector Quantization (RVQ)** design, decomposing the signal into multiple tokens at different levels of granularity. The first token is explicitly assigned to encode high-level audio semantics, while the remaining tokens encode residual acoustic information with progressively finer details. To ensure the first token captures rich semantic information (including linguistic and phonetic cues) required for audio-text modeling, knowledge is distilled from a pretrained Whisper model (Radford et al., 2023). Specifically, the representation of the first audio token is aligned with the encoder outputs of Whisper, with average pooling applied to match the token rate of the audio tokenizer, resolving the temporal mismatch between teacher and student models. Complementary to the first semantic token, residual acoustic tokens preserve fine-grained characteristics such as timbre and prosody.

**Audio Understanding and Generation** utilizes a **depth-wise autoregressive architecture** to model audio tokens for both understanding and generation, drawing inspiration from coarse-to-fine prediction paradigms developed in visual generation (Section 2.3.2). Instead of flattening all residual audio tokens into a single long sequence, ERNIE 5.0 distributes the prediction of residual codes across transformer layers, with each layer modeling audio information at a specific level of granularity.

For audio understanding tasks, text tokens are embedded using standard text embedding layers, while audio tokens are represented through a **depth-wise additive embedding mechanism**. Each audio token consists of multiple discrete codes corresponding to different residual levels. At each level, the code is mapped to an embedding through level-specific embedding matrices, and embeddings from all levels are summed to form the final codec representation. The additive aggregation reflects the residual nature of audio representation, where each depth contributes complementary information at different granularities from coarse to fine.

For audio generation tasks, ERNIE 5.0 introduces **Next-Codec Prediction (NCP)** to generate hierarchical audio tokens in a coarse-to-fine manner (Figure 3, Section 2.3.2). Multiple audio heads are inserted into the top transformer layers to support depth-wise prediction. Conditioned on the multimodal context, the model first predicts the first semantic code and then sequentially generates codes for subsequent residual levels. After each prediction, the generated code is mapped to its corresponding embedding and added back to the hidden state, which then conditions the prediction at the next level. During training, teacher forcing is applied, and the feedback embedding is derived from the ground-truth code. This iterative process continues until all levels are predicted, allowing high-level semantic information to guide the synthesis of increasingly fine-grained acoustic details. For speech synthesis, a speaker embedding is inserted as part of the conditioning context to enable controllable voice timbre without altering deep semantic content or depth-wise prediction structure.

#### Pre-Training Recipe and Elastic Training

The pre-training phase forms the foundation of ERNIE 5.0, where the model learns generalizable representations across multiple modalities (Section 3). The pre-training data comprises trillions of text tokens and multimodal instances, categorized into text data (multilingual web crawls, curated corpora, books, scientific publications, code repositories, structured knowledge sources) and multimodal data (paired image-text, video-text, audio-text, and diverse interleaved multimodal sequences).

**Training Recipe** follows a multi-stage pre-training strategy to progressively extend context length while maintaining stable optimization dynamics (Section 3.2). 

**Stage 1: 8K Pre-Training** uses a maximum context length of 8K tokens with a **Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD)** learning rate schedule. The learning rate is linearly warmed up for 2,000 steps from zero to a peak value of $1 \times 10^{-4}$, then kept constant for the remainder of the 8K training stage. A batch size scheduling strategy gradually increases the global batch size from 14M tokens to 56M tokens during early training. To facilitate seamless long-context extension, the RoPE base is set to 1,000,000 starting from the 8K stage, avoiding the need for reparameterization or interpolation during subsequent context length expansion.

**Stage 2: 32K & 128K Mid-Training** progressively extends the context length to 32K and 128K tokens while keeping the global batch size unchanged. During this phase, the system switches to a cosine learning rate schedule and anneals the learning rate from $1 \times 10^{-4}$ to $1 \times 10^{-5}$. For MoE-specific optimization, the bias update speed for auxiliary-loss-free load balancing is set to $1 \times 10^{-4}$ in the 8K pre-training stage and reduced to $1 \times 10^{-5}$ during mid-training, effectively suppressing iteration-level oscillations. The MTP loss weight is decreased from 0.3 in the 8K stage to 0.1 during mid-training, ensuring stable adaptation as the model scales to longer contexts. A **posterior-based loss weighting strategy** rescales the autoregressive losses of different modalities to the same interval, improving training stability and preventing imbalance across modalities.

**Elastic Training** is a novel paradigm that enables a single pre-training run to produce a family of models with varying capacity-efficiency trade-offs (Section 3.3, Figure 4). Rather than optimizing a static architecture, elastic training dynamically samples sub-models with varying depth, width, and routing sparsity for each training instance, guided by a pre-defined schedule. Both the sampled sub-models and the full-model are optimized in one backpropagation process under the same autoregressive objective.

Elasticity operates along three orthogonal dimensions:

1. **Elastic Depth**: The number of active transformer layers is randomly varied during training. The full model is trained with a probability of 75%, while a reduced-depth sub-network is activated with a probability of 25%. Through this scheme, intermediate representations remain informative even when some layers are bypassed, supporting flexible deployment across different depth configurations.

2. **Elastic Width**: The total number of experts in each MoE layer is varied. With a probability of 80%, all experts participate in routing (full-width configuration). In the remaining 20% of cases, routing is restricted to a randomly sampled subset of experts, leading to a narrower effective model width suitable for memory-constrained environments.

3. **Elastic Sparsity**: The number of activated experts per token is varied. The default routing configuration is applied with a probability of 80%. With a probability of 20%, the routing top-k is randomly sampled from a predefined range where $k$ is smaller than the standard configuration, improving robustness during latency-constrained inference.

The experimental results in Section 6.4.2 demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach. Reducing routing top-k to 25% during inference yields over 15% decoding speedup with only minor accuracy loss. The fully elastic model operates with only 53.7% activated parameters and 35.8% total parameters while maintaining competitive performance (average score of 75.17 compared to 75.55 for the full model on the evaluated benchmarks in Table 12).

#### Post-Training with Unified Multimodal Reinforcement Learning

After unified pre-training, ERNIE 5.0 follows a multi-stage post-training pipeline combining supervised fine-tuning (SFT) with unified multimodal reinforcement learning (UMRL) (Section 4). The coexistence of heterogeneous multimodal inputs and ultra-sparse MoE architecture introduces substantial optimization challenges, increasing UMRL sensitivity to sampling bias, sparse reward signals, and entropy collapse.

**Enhancing Rollout Efficiency with Unbiased Replay Buffer (U-RB)** addresses the computational bottleneck where rollout generation accounts for more than 90% of total RL training time, often limited by long-tail distribution of response lengths (Section 4.1, Figure 5). Recent work like APRIL (Zhou et al., 2025) mitigates long-tail inefficiency by over-provisioning rollout requests and terminating generation once a target number of responses is collected. However, APRIL tends to update model parameters using trajectories with shorter reasoning steps (easier queries), while longer-horizon samples are deferred, leading to non-stationary data difficulty distribution.

U-RB introduces a data-ordering constraint under which only the data group assigned to the current iteration at initialization is allowed to participate in subsequent training. U-RB builds two modules: a high-throughput inference pool $P_{infer}$ with capacity $\Omega_{RBS} = \Omega_{BS} \times N$ (where $\Omega_{BS}$ is the training batch size and $N$ is the buffer size), and a training pool $P_{train}$ with capacity $\Omega_{BS}$ that collects completed trajectories. At iteration $t$, the inference engine $\pi_{infer;\theta_t}$ populates the inference pool by generating rollouts in parallel. Inference proceeds until the terminal state ([EOS]) is reached for the longest rollout belonging to the data group $D_t$ assigned to iteration $t$. At this point, rollouts associated with $D_t$ are moved from $P_{infer}$ to $P_{train}$, enabling the training engine $\pi_{train;\theta_t}$ to update model parameters. This prevents computational idleness while maintaining an unbiased data distribution.

**Stabilizing Training with Mitigated Entropy Collapse** addresses the phenomenon where policy entropy rapidly increases or decreases during early RL stages, eroding the model's ability to fuse information across modalities (Section 4.2, Figure 6). Two techniques are introduced:

**Multi-granularity Importance Sampling Clipping (MISC)** revises the J$_{GSPO}^{IcePop}$ objective to J$_{Mixed}^{IcePop}$ to prevent rapid entropy collapse caused by sequence-level truncated importance sampling. The key modification modulates the trust region according to modality sensitivity:

$$J_{Mixed}^{IcePop}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{infer}(\cdot|x;\theta_{old})} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^G \left[ M_{j \in [1, |y_i|]} \left( \frac{\pi_{train}(y_{i,j}|x, y_{i,&lt;j}; \theta_{old})}{\pi_{infer}(y_{i,j}|x, y_{i,&lt;j}; \theta_{old})}; \alpha, \beta \right) \cdot \min(s_i(\theta) \hat{A}_i, \text{clip}(s_i(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \hat{A}_i) \right] \right]$$

where $s_i(\theta)$ denotes the importance ratio and $M(k)$ is a masking function that returns $k$ if $k \in [\alpha, \beta]$ and 0 otherwise. This achieves a more balanced exploration-exploitation trade-off and avoids premature convergence to suboptimal strategies.

**Well-learned Positive Sample Mask (WPSM)** prevents the model from over-optimizing on already mastered queries by tracking proficiency through success-rate maintenance for each query. For a given query $x$ with rollout group $Y^x = \{y^x_1, y^x_2, \ldots, y^x_G\}$, if the average accuracy $acc^x_t$ in iteration $t$ exceeds a threshold $\tau$, the rollout $y^x_i$ is flagged as "well-learned" when its policy entropy $H_{y^x_i}(\pi_\theta)$ falls below a stability bound $\eta$. During training, well-learned responses are masked:

$$J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{old}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^G \left[ 1 - M^{mask}_i \right] \min(s_i(\theta) \hat{A}_i, \text{clip}(s_i(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \hat{A}_i) \right]$$

$$M^{mask}_i = \begin{cases} \alpha & \text{if } H_{y^x_i}(\pi_\theta) < \eta \text{ and } acc^x_t > \tau \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

where $\alpha \in [0, 1]$ controls the degree of supplementary learning. This shifts gradient budget toward harder samples with sparse rewards or diverse reasoning paths.

**Boosting Sample Efficiency with Hint-based Learning (AHRL)** addresses the limitation where RL methods fail to provide effective gradient signals when all rollouts receive zero reward on challenging tasks (Section 4.3, Figure 7). AHRL injects partial think sketches into queries during RL training by augmenting query $x$ into $\tilde{x}(p)$ by attaching the first $p_{hint}$ tokens of the thinking trajectory to the original query. The probability $p_{hint}$ follows an annealing schedule:

$$p_{hint}(x_t) = p_{initial} \cdot \exp(-\gamma \cdot t \cdot pass^{initial}_x)$$

where $t$ is the training iteration, $\gamma$ is the decay rate, and $pass^{initial}_x$ is the pass@k score of query $x$ evaluated on the SFT model. As training progresses and model performance improves, the fraction of revealed hints is gradually reduced, transitioning the model to full self-exploration. This provides scaffolding to bridge the gap between initial exploration and successful task completion, preventing training from stalling on complex tasks.

#### Infrastructure for Scale

Training ERNIE 5.0 at trillion-parameter scale requires specialized infrastructure addressing four key challenges (Section 5).

**Hybrid Parallelism for Training at Scale** combines 4-way tensor parallelism, 12-way pipeline parallelism with virtual stages, 64-way expert parallelism, ZeRO-1 data parallelism, and context parallelism for long context training (Section 5.1). DeepEP is used for efficient inter-node communication, while virtual pipeline parallelism minimizes pipeline bubbles. To ensure memory sufficiency, FP8 mixed-precision training stores activation tensors in FP8 format, and dynamic adaptive offloading enables selective activation tensor offloading when out-of-memory events occur. To reduce memory fragmentation, sub-batch computations decompose large memory allocation requests into smaller requests, and automatic memory defragmentation based on CUDA virtual memory management ensures successful allocation under extreme conditions.

**Disaggregation Architecture for Multimodal Training** decouples tokenizers from the MoE backbone by deploying them as independent, horizontally scalable services on dedicated compute nodes under a data-parallel configuration (Section 5.2). During training, the backbone interacts with tokenizer services via remote calls to retrieve encoded representations. This architectural separation allows each component to adopt parallelization strategies suited to its own workload, improving scalability and efficiency.

**FlashMask for Flexible Multimodal Attention** addresses heterogeneous attention patterns where text modeling relies on causal attention while visual features employ bidirectional attention with local spatial dependencies (Section 5.3). FlashMask achieves up to 200% speedup over FlexAttention at the operator-level and delivers more than 20% end-to-end training acceleration. Integration with context parallelism at the kernel level achieves 80% performance improvement compared to the Megatron-LM solution.

**Scalable and Disaggregated RL Infrastructure** coordinates training, inference, and environment interaction through a fully disaggregated control plane built around a centralized RL controller (Section 5.4). A unified FP8 execution engine employs identical high-performance operators across both training and inference stages, minimizing numerical mismatch. An unbiased replay buffer preserves original data order to mitigate sequence-length bias from asynchronous completion. Elastic CPU pooling isolates and virtualizes idle CPU capacity from the cluster to power logic-intensive tasks such as RL environment interactions, reducing wall-clock time of training iterations while improving total cost of ownership efficiency.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the fundamental contributions of ERNIE 5.0 beyond the technical components described in Section 3. We distinguish between **incremental improvements** (engineering refinements that enhance existing paradigms) and **fundamental innovations** (architectural or methodological shifts that challenge prior assumptions). The significance of each contribution is evaluated not only by benchmark performance but by what it reveals about scalable multimodal modeling.

### 4.1 Modality-Agnostic Expert Routing: Challenging the Modality Boundary Assumption

**Prior Assumption:** Existing MoE architectures for multimodal models typically employ **modality-specific routing** — either explicitly routing tokens based on modality identifiers (e.g., "this is an image token, send it to vision experts") or maintaining separate expert pools for different modalities. This design reflects an implicit assumption that heterogeneous modalities require distinct parameter subspaces for stable optimization.

**ERNIE 5.0's Innovation:** The modality-agnostic routing mechanism conditions routing decisions **solely on unified token representations**, without any explicit modality identifiers. As Section 6.4.1 reveals through expert utilization analysis (Figure 8 and Figure 9), this produces a counterintuitive outcome: despite the absence of modality-specific routing rules, experts **naturally self-organize** into specialization patterns shaped by task requirements rather than modality boundaries.

**Why This Matters:**

- **Theoretical Significance:** The emergence of specialization without explicit modality boundaries challenges the assumption that heterogeneous modalities require architecturally enforced separation. Section 6.4.1 shows that text exhibits higher co-activation with audio than with visual modalities in early layers, and this overlap increases in deeper layers. This suggests that **semantic similarity**, not modality type, drives expert allocation — a finding that contradicts the design philosophy of modality-isolated routing in prior work (ERNIE Team, 2025).

- **Practical Significance:** Eliminating heuristic modality-specific expert allocation removes a significant engineering burden. As the number of modalities grows (text, image, video, audio, and potentially others), manually designing routing rules becomes "non-trivial in practice, especially when more than two modalities are involved" (Section 2.1). Modality-agnostic routing scales naturally to new modalities without architectural modifications.

- **Performance Trade-off:** The ablation in Section 6.4.1 demonstrates that this approach does not sacrifice single-modality performance. Visual understanding and generation tasks show concentrated expert activation patterns comparable to modality-specific routing, while cross-modal tasks benefit from shared expert capacity. This resolves the "ability seesaw" problem described in Section 2.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation**. Prior MoE work (Lepikhin et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024c) focused on load balancing and sparsity within single-modality contexts. ERNIE 5.0 extends this to multimodal settings without modality-specific heuristics — a qualitatively different approach that enables emergent cross-modal knowledge sharing.

### 4.2 Elastic Training as a Principled Deployment Paradigm

**Prior Assumption:** The standard approach to producing models of varying sizes follows a **"train-then-compress" pipeline**: train a large model, then apply pruning (Sajjad et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2023), knowledge distillation (Gu et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024), or fusion-based compression (Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024d;e) to create smaller variants. Each target size requires a separate compression process, multiplying computational costs and engineering complexity.

**ERNIE 5.0's Innovation:** Elastic training optimizes a **family of sub-networks during pre-training**, producing multiple deployable variants from a single checkpoint. As Figure 4 illustrates, elasticity operates along three orthogonal dimensions (depth, width, sparsity), and Section 6.4.2 demonstrates that the fully elastic model retains competitive performance with only 35.8% of total parameters activated.

**Why This Matters:**

- **Paradigm Shift:** Elastic training reframes model compression from a **post-hoc operation** to a **training-time objective**. This is not merely an engineering convenience — it fundamentally changes how we think about model capacity. The full model and its sub-networks are co-optimized under the same autoregressive objective, ensuring that intermediate representations remain informative even when layers or experts are bypassed.

- **Deployment Flexibility:** Table 9, Table 10, and Table 11 in Section 6.4.2 show that elastic sub-models exhibit "smooth and predictable performance degradation" across all three dimensions. This enables organizations to instantiate models at different scales **without retraining**, adapting to diverse hardware constraints (cloud GPUs, edge devices, latency-sensitive applications) from a single pre-trained checkpoint.

- **Efficiency Gains:** The paper reports that reducing routing top-k to 25% yields "over 15% decoding speedup with only minor accuracy loss" (Introduction). More significantly, the fully elastic variant (ERNIE 5.0-Exp-EA35.8%) achieves an average score of 75.17 compared to 75.55 for the full model (Table 12) — a **0.5% performance drop** for a **64% reduction in total parameters**. This trade-off is substantially more favorable than typical pruning or distillation approaches.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This builds on Once-For-All concepts (Devvrit et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2024) but extends them to **pre-training at trillion-parameter scale** with ultra-sparse MoE architectures. The combination of elastic depth, width, and sparsity in a unified multimodal context represents a **fundamental innovation** in how foundation models can be produced and deployed.

**Critical Limitation:** The paper does not report whether elastic sub-models can be fine-tuned independently after instantiation, or whether they must remain tied to the full-model checkpoint. This deployment detail would significantly impact practical adoption.

### 4.3 Unified Next-Group-of-Tokens Prediction: True Multimodal Autoregression

**Prior Assumption:** Most multimodal systems use **hybrid objectives**: autoregressive next-token prediction for text understanding, but non-autoregressive objectives (e.g., diffusion losses, reconstruction losses) for image and audio generation. This creates "inconsistent optimization trajectories that hinder deep cross-modal integration" (Section 2.1). Late-fusion approaches augment pre-trained language models with modality-specific decoders, preventing simultaneous training from scratch.

**ERNIE 5.0's Innovation:** All modalities are trained **from scratch** under a unified **Next-Group-of-Tokens Prediction** objective. Text uses standard Next-Token Prediction (NTP) with Multi-Token Prediction (MTP) augmentation, while vision uses Next-Frame-and-Scale Prediction (NFSP) and audio uses Next-Codec Prediction (NCP). Despite these modality-specific formulations, all are optimized as autoregressive sequence prediction tasks within the same backbone.

**Why This Matters:**

- **Training from Scratch:** Unlike approaches that augment pre-trained LLMs (Xu et al., 2025a; Seedream et al., 2025), ERNIE 5.0 trains all modalities simultaneously from initialization. This prevents the "ability seesaw" problem where strengthening one modality degrades another, ensuring that "all modalities evolve collectively without sacrificing performance" (Section 1).

- **Deep Cross-Modal Integration:** The unified objective enables token-level interactions across modalities throughout the backbone, not just at fusion points. Section 6.4.1's expert collaboration analysis (Figure 9) shows increasing cross-modality overlap in deeper layers, indicating that the model learns **unified semantic representations** rather than modality-specific features that are later combined.

- **Generation Quality:** Table 5 and Table 6 in Section 6.2 demonstrate that ERNIE 5.0 achieves competitive image and video generation quality (GenEval: 90.1, VBench-Semantic: 83.40) compared to specialized models. This validates that autoregressive generation can match diffusion-based approaches when properly formulated.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in multimodal modeling. Prior unified models either sacrificed generation quality for understanding capability, or required modality-specific objectives. ERNIE 5.0 demonstrates that a single autoregressive framework can support both without architectural fragmentation.

**Critical Trade-off:** The paper acknowledges that autoregressive visual generation is "susceptible to error accumulation over extremely long token sequences" (Section 2.2.3). The cascaded diffusion refiner addresses this but introduces a hybrid element (autoregressive backbone + diffusion refiner) that partially undermines the pure autoregressive claim. This is a pragmatic compromise, but worth noting for future work aiming for fully autoregressive generation.

### 4.4 RL Stability Techniques for Ultra-Sparse MoE Architectures

**Prior Assumption:** Reinforcement learning for large language models (GRPO, DAPO) assumes relatively dense architectures where training-inference numerical consistency is manageable. The application of RL to **ultra-sparse MoE models** with **multimodal inputs** introduces unique challenges: dynamic routing amplifies numerical mismatch, heterogeneous modalities create sparse reward distributions, and trillion-parameter scale makes rollout generation prohibitively expensive.

**ERNIE 5.0's Innovation:** The paper introduces three synergistic techniques specifically designed for RL on ultra-sparse MoE multimodal models:

1. **Unbiased Replay Buffer (U-RB)** — Addresses long-tail rollout inefficiency while preserving data distribution (Section 4.1, Figure 5)
2. **Multi-granularity Importance Sampling Clipping (MISC)** — Prevents entropy collapse by modulating trust regions per token position (Section 4.2, Figure 6)
3. **Adaptive Hint-based RL (AHRL)** — Provides scaffolding for hard tasks with sparse rewards through annealed hint revelation (Section 4.3, Figure 7)

**Why This Matters:**

- **MoE-Specific Challenges:** The paper explicitly notes that "the ultra-sparse MoE architecture exacerbates the training–inference discrepancy and undermines stability" (Section 4). Standard RL techniques like IcePop (Ling-Team et al., 2025) cause "rapid entropy collapse" when directly applied to ERNIE 5.0 (Figure 6, light-blue line). MISC revises the objective to prevent this, demonstrating that MoE architectures require RL formulations tailored to their sparsity characteristics.

- **Multimodal Reward Sparsity:** Different modalities exhibit different reward distributions. Audio and video generation tasks may have sparse or delayed rewards compared to text reasoning. AHRL's hint-based approach provides auxiliary guidance when all rollouts receive zero reward, preventing training from stalling on complex multimodal tasks.

- **Scale Efficiency:** Rollout generation accounts for "more than 90% of the total training time in RL" (Section 4.1). U-RB's unbiased replay mechanism prevents GPU idleness from long-tail responses while maintaining data distribution integrity — a critical efficiency gain at trillion-parameter scale.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** These are **incremental innovations** built on existing RL frameworks (GRPO, GSPO, IcePop). However, their adaptation to ultra-sparse MoE multimodal contexts addresses challenges not previously studied at this scale. The significance lies in **enabling** RL post-training for unified multimodal models, which was previously infeasible due to stability issues.

**Critical Limitation:** The paper does not provide ablation studies isolating the contribution of each RL technique. It is unclear whether MISC, WPSM, or AHRL is most critical, or whether all three are necessary. This limits reproducibility and makes it difficult for practitioners to prioritize implementation efforts.

### 4.5 Synthesis: What ERNIE 5.0 Reveals About Multimodal Foundation Models

Taken together, these innovations suggest several broader insights about the future of multimodal foundation models:

| Insight | Prior Belief | ERNIE 5.0's Evidence |
|---------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Modality boundaries** | Require explicit architectural enforcement | Emerge naturally from task requirements via modality-agnostic routing |
| **Model compression** | Requires post-hoc pruning or distillation | Can be optimized during pre-training via elastic training |
| **Generation objectives** | Need modality-specific losses (e.g., diffusion) | Can be unified under autoregressive next-group prediction |
| **RL for MoE** | Directly applicable from dense model techniques | Requires MoE-specific stability techniques (MISC, U-RB, AHRL) |

**The Fundamental Contribution:** ERNIE 5.0 demonstrates that **unification is achievable without fragmentation**. Prior work forced trade-offs between multimodal integration and single-modality performance, between understanding and generation, or between model capacity and deployment flexibility. ERNIE 5.0's architecture shows these are not inherent trade-offs but consequences of architectural choices that can be redesigned.

**Remaining Open Questions:**

1. **Compute Efficiency:** While elastic training reduces deployment costs, the pre-training compute for the full trillion-parameter model is not disclosed. How does this compare to training separate specialized models?

2. **Modality Scaling:** The paper evaluates four modalities (text, image, video, audio). How does modality-agnostic routing scale to 10+ modalities? Does expert specialization remain stable?

3. **Elastic Fine-Tuning:** Can elastic sub-models be fine-tuned independently after instantiation, or must they remain tied to the full checkpoint? This impacts practical deployment significantly.

4. **RL Generalization:** The RL techniques are validated on ERNIE 5.0 but not tested on other MoE architectures. Are these techniques generalizable, or do they depend on ERNIE 5.0's specific routing mechanism?

These questions define the research agenda that ERNIE 5.0 opens for the community — not just as a model to benchmark against, but as an architectural paradigm to build upon.

### 5.4 Assessment: Do Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: "First production-scale realization of a trillion-parameter unified autoregressive model supporting both multimodal understanding and generation" (Abstract)**

**Support Level: Strong.** The breadth of benchmarks (30+ language, 20+ vision, 15+ audio) and competitive performance across modalities substantiates this claim. The GenEval (90.1) and VBench-Semantic (83.40) results are particularly compelling, as they demonstrate generation quality matching specialized models.

**Caveat:** The trillion-parameter claim is not independently verifiable, as the exact parameter count is not disclosed in the evaluation section.

**Claim 2: "Modality-agnostic expert routing eliminates the need for heuristic modality-specific expert allocation" (Section 2.1)**

**Support Level: Moderate.** Figure 8 and Figure 9 demonstrate that experts self-organize into specialization patterns without modality identifiers. However, the paper does not ablate modality-agnostic routing against modality-specific routing to quantify the performance impact. The claim is supported qualitatively but not quantitatively.

**Claim 3: "Elastic training enables flexible deployment with only minor accuracy loss" (Introduction)**

**Support Level: Strong.** Table 12 shows that the EA35.8% variant achieves 75.17 average score vs 75.55 for the full model (0.5% drop) with 35.8% total parameters. The small-scale ablation (Tables 9-11) demonstrates graceful degradation across all three elasticity dimensions.

**Caveat:** Latency measurements (the claimed 15% speedup) are not provided in Section 6.4.2, only in the Introduction.

**Claim 4: "Unified training retains strong modality-specific performance without architectural fragmentation" (Introduction)**

**Support Level: Strong.** Table 1 shows ERNIE 5.0-Base outperforming specialized baselines on most language benchmarks. Tables 5-8 show competitive performance on vision and audio generation. The model does not exhibit the "ability seesaw" problem described in Section 1.

**Caveat:** On extreme reasoning benchmarks (GPQA-Diamond, AIME 2025, HMMT 2025), ERNIE 5.0 trails Gemini 3-Pro by 5-14 points (Table 2). The paper acknowledges this but frames it as a design choice (balanced capability vs extreme reasoning optimization).

### 5.5 Limitations and Open Questions

**Missing Information:**
1. **Exact parameter count:** The paper claims "trillion-parameter" but does not disclose the exact total and activated parameter counts for the main ERNIE 5.0 model (only for ERNIE 5.0-Exp in Section 6.4.2).
2. **Training compute:** The total FLOPs or GPU-hours for pre-training are not disclosed, making it difficult to assess efficiency relative to baselines.
3. **RL ablation:** No quantitative ablation of individual RL techniques (U-RB, MISC, WPSM, AHRL).
4. **Elastic fine-tuning:** The paper does not clarify whether elastic sub-models can be fine-tuned independently after instantiation.

**Potential Biases:**
1. **Internal evaluation framework:** All evaluations use "ERNIE-Eval" (Section 6), an internal framework that may differ from public evaluation protocols.
2. **Proprietary baselines:** Comparisons against GPT-5 and Gemini 3-Pro rely on publicly reported numbers, which may use different evaluation protocols.
3. **Selection bias:** The paper does not report benchmarks where ERNIE 5.0 underperforms (beyond the acknowledged reasoning gap).

**Reproducibility Concerns:**
1. **Data composition:** The pre-training data is described at a high level ("trillions of text tokens and multimodal instances") but exact proportions are not disclosed.
2. **Hyperparameters:** Key training hyperparameters (e.g., exact MoE top-k, number of experts, hidden dimensions) are not fully specified.
3. **Elastic training schedule:** The pre-defined schedule for sampling sub-models during elastic training is not detailed.

### 5.6 Summary Assessment

The experimental evidence **largely supports** ERNIE 5.0's core claims, particularly regarding:
- Unified multimodal understanding and generation capability (Tables 1-8)
- Elastic training efficiency (Tables 9-12)
- Modality-agnostic routing emergent specialization (Figures 8-10)

However, the experiments have **notable gaps** that limit full verification:
- Missing RL technique ablations
- Undisclosed training compute costs
- Limited latency measurements for elastic variants
- Internal evaluation framework limiting independent verification

The paper is strongest on **demonstrating feasibility** (unified autoregressive multimodal modeling works at scale) and **deployment flexibility** (elastic training produces efficient sub-models). It is weaker on **isolating causal contributions** of individual design choices and **transparent reporting** of training costs.

For practitioners, the key takeaway is that ERNIE 5.0 provides empirical evidence that unified multimodal autoregressive modeling is viable at production scale, with elastic training offering a practical path to deployment flexibility. For researchers, the missing ablations and hyperparameter details represent opportunities for follow-up work to validate and extend these findings.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While ERNIE 5.0 demonstrates significant advances in unified multimodal modeling, a critical examination reveals several assumptions, constraints, and open questions that define the boundaries of its contributions. This section identifies where the approach succeeds, where it compromises, and what remains unresolved.

### 6.1 Architectural Assumptions and Their Consequences

**Assumption 1: Unified Token Representations Enable Cross-Modal Generalization**

The modality-agnostic routing mechanism assumes that tokens from different modalities can be meaningfully compared in a shared representation space without explicit modality identifiers. While Section 6.4.1 shows experts self-organize into specialization patterns, this assumption carries risks:

- **Representation Quality Dependency**: The routing quality depends entirely on the learned token representations. If early training produces poor cross-modal alignments, the router may make suboptimal expert assignments that compound throughout training. The paper does not analyze failure cases where modality-agnostic routing might underperform modality-specific routing.

- **Modality Imbalance**: Section 6.4.1 (Figure 8) reveals that expert activations for image, video, and audio inputs are "noticeably more concentrated than those for text-only inputs." This suggests the model may allocate disproportionate capacity to certain modalities, potentially creating hidden imbalances not captured by aggregate benchmarks.

**Assumption 2: Elastic Sub-Networks Remain Viable After Instantiation**

The elastic training paradigm assumes that sub-models extracted from the full checkpoint can be deployed and potentially fine-tuned independently. However, the paper leaves critical questions unanswered:

- **Fine-Tuning Independence**: Section 3.3 states elastic sub-models serve as "effective starting points for mid-training or fine-tuning," but does not demonstrate whether a 35.8% parameter sub-model can be fine-tuned without access to the full model's parameters. If sub-models require the full checkpoint for continued training, deployment flexibility is significantly constrained.

- **Performance predictability**: While Tables 9-11 show "smooth and predictable performance degradation" on a small-scale model, Table 12 reveals the fully elastic ERNIE 5.0-Exp-EA35.8% actually achieves a *higher* average score (75.17) than the 25% sparsity variant (74.43), despite using fewer parameters. This non-monotonic relationship suggests the interaction between depth, width, and sparsity elasticity is not fully understood.

**Assumption 3: Autoregressive Generation Suffices for All Modalities**

The unified next-group-of-tokens prediction objective assumes autoregressive modeling can handle all generation tasks. However, Section 2.2.3 acknowledges a critical limitation:

> "Empirically, auto-regressive visual generation is susceptible to error accumulation over extremely long token sequences."

To address this, ERNIE 5.0 introduces a **cascaded diffusion refiner** trained separately from the backbone. This creates a hybrid architecture that partially undermines the "pure autoregressive" claim:

- The backbone generates low-resolution samples with precise semantics
- The diffusion refiner enhances fine-grained visual details at higher resolution
- The refiner is trained with "paired low-resolution samples with controlled degradation"

This is a pragmatic compromise, but it means ERNIE 5.0 is not *fully* autoregressive for visual generation. The paper does not quantify how much generation quality depends on the diffusion refiner versus the autoregressive backbone.

### 6.2 Computational and Data Constraints

**Training Compute Opacity**

The paper claims ERNIE 5.0 is a "trillion-parameter" model but does not disclose:

- Exact total and activated parameter counts for the main model (only ERNIE 5.0-Exp variants in Section 6.4.2)
- Total FLOPs or GPU-hours for pre-training
- Comparison of training compute versus training separate specialized models

Without this information, it is impossible to assess whether unified training is more or less efficient than modality-specific approaches. The efficiency gains from elastic training (Section 6.4.2) apply to *deployment*, not pre-training — the full trillion-parameter model still requires massive compute to train initially.

**Data Composition Uncertainty**

Section 3.1 describes the pre-training data at a high level ("trillions of text tokens and multimodal instances") but does not specify:

- Exact proportions of text vs. image vs. video vs. audio data
- Quality thresholds for multimodal data filtering
- Whether data proportions were adjusted during elastic training

The posterior-based loss weighting strategy (Section 3.2) "rescales the autoregressive losses of different modalities to the same interval," suggesting modality imbalance is a real concern. However, the paper does not reveal what imbalance levels were observed or how aggressively losses were rescaled.

**Memory and Communication Overhead**

Section 5.1 acknowledges the ultra-sparse MoE architecture poses two major challenges:

> "ultra-sparse expert activation leads to heavy inter-node communication, and large-scale expert parameters impose significant memory pressure."

The paper describes mitigation techniques (FP8 training, activation offloading, memory defragmentation), but notes that "out-of-memory (OOM) issues sometimes occur due to unbalanced expert routing, especially in the initial stage of training." This indicates the system operates near hardware limits, and the "below 3% activation rate" (Section 2.1) may be partly driven by memory constraints rather than purely architectural choice.

### 6.3 Performance Trade-offs and Benchmark Gaps

**Reasoning Capability Gap**

Table 2 in Section 6.1 reveals a consistent pattern: ERNIE 5.0 excels on knowledge retrieval and instruction-following but trails on extreme reasoning benchmarks:

| Benchmark | Gemini 3-Pro | ERNIE 5.0 | Gap |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|-----|
| GPQA-Diamond | 91.90 | 86.36 | -5.54 |
| AIME 2025 | 95.00 | 89.06 | -5.94 |
| HMMT 2025 | 93.33 | 79.58 | -13.75 |
| LiveCodeBench v6 | 86.34 | 76.21 | -10.13 |
| MMMU-Pro | 81.00 | 68.63 | -12.37 |

The paper acknowledges this in Section 6.1:

> "a moderate gap persists on the most challenging reasoning benchmarks compared to models such as Gemini 3-Pro. Our future work will further leverage architectural design and advanced training strategies to better support complex, long-horizon reasoning."

This is a significant trade-off: ERNIE 5.0 prioritizes **balanced capability development** over **extreme reasoning optimization**. For applications requiring competition-level math or deep scientific reasoning, this gap may be prohibitive.

**Vision Reasoning Gap**

A similar pattern appears in vision benchmarks (Table 3, Section 6.2):

| Benchmark | Gemini 3-Pro | ERNIE 5.0 | Gap |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|-----|
| MathVista | 89.20 | 84.80 | -4.40 |
| MathVision | 87.27 | 74.34 | -12.93 |
| VisualPuzzle | 71.48 | 64.82 | -6.66 |
| MMStar | 82.96 | 75.54 | -7.42 |

ERNIE 5.0 achieves best-in-class performance on VLMAreBlind (91.38) and DocVQAval (95.45), indicating strong visual *perception* and document understanding. However, the reasoning gaps suggest the unified architecture may not fully support the deep multi-step reasoning required for complex visual math problems.

**Audio Generation vs. Specialized TTS**

Table 8 in Section 6.3 shows ERNIE 5.0's speech generation performance:

| Model | test-zh WER | test-en WER |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| CosyVoice 3 (specialist) | 0.71 | 1.45 |
| ERNIE 5.0 | 1.35 | 1.54 |

While ERNIE 5.0 demonstrates "reliable content preservation without task-specific TTS optimization" (Section 6.3), it does not match specialist systems. This is expected for a unified model, but practitioners should note that applications requiring highest-quality speech synthesis may still need dedicated TTS systems.

### 6.4 Reinforcement Learning Stability Challenges

**Entropy Collapse Sensitivity**

Section 4.2 reveals that applying standard RL techniques to ERNIE 5.0 causes rapid entropy collapse:

> "our experiments show that directly applying J$_{GSPO}^{IcePop}$ to RL training on ERNIE 5.0 leads to rapid entropy collapse, as illustrated by the light-blue line in Figure 6."

This required developing three stabilization techniques (MISC, WPSM, AHRL). However, the paper does not provide ablation studies isolating each technique's contribution. Key unanswered questions:

- Is MISC essential, or do WPSM and AHRL suffice?
- Do these techniques generalize to other MoE architectures, or are they specific to ERNIE 5.0's routing mechanism?
- What is the computational overhead of maintaining the unbiased replay buffer and hint-based scaffolding?

**Rollout Efficiency Limits**

Section 4.1 states rollout generation accounts for "more than 90% of the total training time in RL." While U-RB improves efficiency by preventing GPU idleness from long-tail responses, the fundamental bottleneck remains:

- Long-horizon reasoning tasks still require extensive rollout generation
- The unbiased replay buffer requires additional memory ($\Omega_{RBS} = \Omega_{BS} \times N$)
- The paper does not report absolute training time reductions, only that U-RB "prevents computational idleness"

For organizations with limited RL training budgets, this 90% rollout cost may still be prohibitive at trillion-parameter scale.

### 6.5 Scalability and Generalization Questions

**Modality Scaling Beyond Four Modalities**

ERNIE 5.0 evaluates four modalities (text, image, video, audio). Section 6.4.1 shows experts exhibit specialization patterns, but the paper does not address:

- How does modality-agnostic routing scale to 10+ modalities (e.g., adding sensor data, 3D point clouds, molecular structures)?
- Does expert specialization remain stable, or does routing become increasingly chaotic?
- At what point does adding modalities degrade core language performance?

The paper claims modality-agnostic routing "scales naturally to new modalities without architectural modifications" (Section 4.1), but this is an assertion without empirical validation beyond four modalities.

**Context Length Scaling**

Section 3.2 describes progressive context extension from 8K to 32K to 128K tokens. However:

- The paper does not evaluate performance at context lengths beyond 128K
- No analysis of how elastic training affects long-context capabilities (do shallow sub-models maintain long-context reasoning?)
- The RoPE base is set to 1,000,000 from the 8K stage, but the paper does not report whether this causes optimization difficulties at shorter contexts

**Elastic Training at Different Scales**

The elastic training ablation (Section 6.4.2) uses ERNIE 5.0-Exp, described as an "experimental variant," not the full ERNIE 5.0 reported in Tables 1-3. This raises questions:

- Do the efficiency gains (35.8% parameters, 0.5% performance drop) transfer to the full model?
- How does elastic training interact with the trillion-parameter scale? Does the 3% activation rate change for sub-models?
- Can elastic sub-models be mixed within a single deployment (e.g., some users get 35.8% models, others get 100%)?

### 6.6 Reproducibility and Transparency Gaps

**Missing Hyperparameters**

Several critical training details are not fully specified:

- Exact MoE configuration (number of experts, top-k default value for full model)
- Hidden dimensions and layer count for the full trillion-parameter model
- Elastic training sampling schedule (the "pre-defined schedule" mentioned in Section 3.3)
- Diffusion refiner architecture and training details

**Internal Evaluation Framework**

All evaluations use "ERNIE-Eval" (Section 6), an internal framework. While the benchmarks listed are standard (MMLU, GPQA, etc.), the evaluation protocol may differ from public implementations:

- Prompt templates and few-shot examples may vary
- Scoring criteria for open-ended tasks may differ
- Comparison against proprietary models (GPT-5, Gemini 3-Pro) relies on publicly reported numbers that may use different protocols

**Training Data Transparency**

Section 3.1 describes data categories but does not disclose:

- Exact token counts per modality
- Data source proportions (web crawls vs. curated corpora vs. books)
- Filtering thresholds for quality control
- Decontamination procedures to prevent benchmark leakage

This limits the ability to assess whether performance gains stem from architectural innovations or data scale/composition advantages.

### 6.7 Summary of Key Trade-offs

| Trade-off | Benefit | Cost |
|-----------|---------|------|
| **Modality-agnostic routing** | Eliminates heuristic expert allocation; enables cross-modal knowledge sharing | May create hidden modality imbalances; routing quality depends on representation learning |
| **Elastic training** | Single checkpoint for multiple deployment sizes; 64% parameter reduction with 0.5% performance drop | Pre-training compute still massive; fine-tuning independence unclear |
| **Unified autoregressive objective** | Deep cross-modal integration; training from scratch | Requires diffusion refiner for high-quality visual generation; susceptible to error accumulation |
| **Balanced capability development** | Strong across diverse tasks; no "ability seesaw" | Trails on extreme reasoning benchmarks by 5-14 points |
| **Ultra-sparse MoE (3% activation)** | Trillion-parameter capacity without proportional compute | Heavy inter-node communication; OOM issues in early training |

### 6.8 Open Research Questions

The limitations above define a research agenda for follow-up work:

1. **Causal Attribution**: Which design choices (modality-agnostic routing, elastic training, unified objective) contribute most to performance? Ablation studies isolating each factor are needed.

2. **Elastic Fine-Tuning**: Can sub-models be fine-tuned independently after instantiation? What is the performance impact of fine-tuning a 35.8% model vs. fine-tuning the full model then extracting a sub-model?

3. **Modality Scaling**: How does the architecture behave with 10+ modalities? Does expert specialization remain stable, or does routing degrade?

4. **RL Technique Generalization**: Do MISC, WPSM, and AHRL generalize to other MoE architectures, or are they specific to ERNIE 5.0's routing mechanism?

5. **Compute Efficiency**: How does unified training compute compare to training separate specialized models? Is the unified approach more or less efficient overall?

6. **Long-Context Elasticity**: Do elastic sub-models maintain long-context reasoning capabilities, or does reducing depth/width disproportionately affect context handling?

These questions are not criticisms of ERNIE 5.0 but rather opportunities the work opens for the research community. The paper's greatest contribution may be demonstrating that unified multimodal autoregressive modeling is *feasible* at production scale — the optimization of this paradigm remains an active research frontier.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

ERNIE 5.0 represents more than a single model release — it establishes a new architectural paradigm for multimodal foundation models. This section examines how the work reshapes the research landscape, identifies concrete follow-up directions it enables, outlines practical deployment scenarios, and provides integration guidance for practitioners evaluating whether to adopt this approach.

### 7.1 Landscape Shifts: From Fragmentation to Unified Autoregression

ERNIE 5.0 fundamentally challenges three entrenched assumptions in multimodal AI development:

**Assumption 1: Understanding and Generation Require Separate Architectures**

Prior to ERNIE 5.0, the dominant design pattern treated multimodal understanding and generation as architecturally distinct problems. Understanding used autoregressive transformers; generation used diffusion models, VQ-VAEs, or other non-autoregressive objectives. ERNIE 5.0 demonstrates that a **single next-group-of-tokens prediction objective** can support both capabilities without the "ability seesaw" problem described in Section 1.

This shifts the research question from *"How do we connect understanding and generation modules?"* to *"How do we optimize a unified objective for both tasks?"* The implications are substantial:

- Model development pipelines can consolidate from multiple specialized training runs to a single pre-training process
- Cross-modal reasoning improves because understanding and generation share the same representation space throughout the backbone (Section 6.4.1, Figure 9 shows increasing cross-modality expert overlap in deeper layers)
- Deployment complexity decreases because a single checkpoint serves both inference modes

**Assumption 2: Modality Boundaries Require Explicit Architectural Enforcement**

The modality-agnostic expert routing mechanism (Section 2.1) demonstrates that heterogeneous modalities do not require modality-specific routing rules or separate expert pools. Experts self-organize into specialization patterns based on task requirements rather than modality identifiers (Section 6.4.1, Figure 8).

This challenges the design philosophy underlying most multimodal MoE systems, which typically employ:
- Explicit modality tokens or identifiers in the routing decision
- Separate expert pools for text vs. vision vs. audio
- Heuristic rules for cross-modal expert allocation

ERNIE 5.0 shows these are unnecessary. The routing network learns to dispatch tokens appropriately based purely on representation content. This simplifies architecture design and enables natural scaling to new modalities without modifying routing logic.

**Assumption 3: Model Compression Requires Post-Hoc Processing**

The elastic training paradigm (Section 3.3, Figure 4) reframes model compression from a **post-training operation** to a **training-time objective**. Instead of training a large model then pruning or distilling it into smaller variants, ERNIE 5.0 optimizes a family of sub-networks simultaneously during pre-training.

The efficiency gains are compelling: Table 12 in Section 6.4.2 shows the fully elastic variant (ERNIE 5.0-Exp-EA35.8%) achieves 75.17 average score with only 35.8% of total parameters, compared to 75.55 for the full model — a **0.5% performance drop for a 64% parameter reduction**. This trade-off is substantially more favorable than typical pruning approaches, which often incur 5-10% performance drops at similar compression levels.

**Cumulative Impact:** Together, these shifts suggest a new design template for foundation models:

| Design Dimension | Prior Paradigm | ERNIE 5.0 Paradigm |
|-----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Objective** | Hybrid (autoregressive + diffusion/reconstruction) | Unified next-group prediction |
| **Routing** | Modality-specific expert allocation | Modality-agnostic routing |
| **Deployment** | Separate models per size constraint | Elastic sub-models from single checkpoint |
| **Training** | Augment pre-trained LLM | Train all modalities from scratch |

This template is now available for the community to build upon, adapt, or challenge.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The limitations and open questions identified in Section 6 define a concrete research agenda. Below are high-priority directions that ERNIE 5.0 enables or motivates:

#### 7.2.1 Causal Attribution Studies

**Gap:** Section 6.4 notes the paper does not ablate individual design choices (modality-agnostic routing, elastic training, unified objective) to quantify their isolated contributions.

**Research Direction:** Systematically ablate each component:
- Train a variant with modality-specific routing (explicit modality identifiers in routing decisions)
- Train a variant without elastic training (static architecture)
- Train a variant with hybrid objectives (autoregressive understanding + diffusion generation)

**Expected Contribution:** Quantify how much each innovation contributes to final performance. This would help practitioners prioritize which components to adopt based on their constraints.

#### 7.2.2 Elastic Fine-Tuning Independence

**Gap:** Section 6.1 notes the paper does not demonstrate whether elastic sub-models can be fine-tuned independently after instantiation, or whether they require access to the full checkpoint.

**Research Direction:** Investigate elastic sub-model fine-tuning:
- Extract a 35.8% parameter sub-model from ERNIE 5.0-Exp
- Fine-tune it on domain-specific data without access to full model parameters
- Compare performance to fine-tuning the full model then extracting a sub-model

**Expected Contribution:** Determine whether elastic training truly enables independent deployment, or whether sub-models remain coupled to the full checkpoint. This has significant implications for practical adoption.

#### 7.2.3 Modality Scaling Beyond Four Modalities

**Gap:** Section 6.5 notes ERNIE 5.0 evaluates only four modalities (text, image, video, audio). The claim that modality-agnostic routing "scales naturally to new modalities" (Section 4.1) lacks empirical validation.

**Research Direction:** Extend the architecture to additional modalities:
- 3D point clouds (LiDAR, molecular structures)
- Sensor data (time-series from IoT devices)
- Structured data (tables, graphs, code execution traces)

**Expected Contribution:** Determine whether expert specialization remains stable with 10+ modalities, or whether routing becomes increasingly chaotic. Identify the modality count at which performance degradation occurs.

#### 7.2.4 RL Technique Generalization

**Gap:** Section 6.4 notes the RL stabilization techniques (MISC, WPSM, AHRL) are validated only on ERNIE 5.0. It is unclear whether they generalize to other MoE architectures.

**Research Direction:** Apply MISC, WPSM, and AHRL to other ultra-sparse MoE models:
- DeepSeek-V3 series
- Mixtral-style architectures
- Other trillion-parameter MoE systems

**Expected Contribution:** Determine whether these techniques are ERNIE 5.0-specific or represent general principles for stable RL on sparse architectures. This would significantly broaden their impact.

#### 7.2.5 Long-Context Elasticity

**Gap:** Section 6.5 notes the paper does not analyze how elastic training affects long-context capabilities. Do shallow sub-models maintain 128K context reasoning?

**Research Direction:** Evaluate elastic sub-models on long-context benchmarks:
- Needle-in-a-haystack retrieval at 128K tokens
- Multi-document reasoning tasks
- Code context understanding across large codebases

**Expected Contribution:** Determine whether depth elasticity disproportionately affects long-context capabilities. This would inform deployment decisions for applications requiring extended context windows.

#### 7.2.6 Compute Efficiency Comparison

**Gap:** Section 6.2 notes the paper does not disclose total training compute (FLOPs, GPU-hours) or compare unified training efficiency to training separate specialized models.

**Research Direction:** Conduct compute-normalized comparisons:
- Train unified model (ERNIE 5.0 architecture) on combined multimodal data
- Train separate specialized models (text-only, vision-only, audio-only) on equivalent data
- Compare total FLOPs, wall-clock time, and final performance

**Expected Contribution:** Determine whether unified training is more or less compute-efficient than specialized training. This has significant implications for organizations with limited training budgets.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

ERNIE 5.0's capabilities enable several application categories that were previously difficult or impossible with fragmented architectures:

#### 7.3.1 Multimodal Interactive Assistants

**Use Case:** AI assistants that can understand and generate across all modalities within a single conversation.

**Example Workflow:**
1. User uploads a chart image and asks for analysis (visual understanding)
2. Model generates a written summary (text generation)
3. User requests an audio explanation for accessibility (audio generation)
4. Model creates an annotated version of the chart with highlights (visual generation)

**Why ERNIE 5.0 Excels:** Prior systems would require separate models for each step, with potential consistency issues between modalities. ERNIE 5.0's unified architecture ensures semantic consistency across all outputs because understanding and generation share the same representation space.

**Deployment Advantage:** Elastic training enables deploying the same model at different scales — full-capacity for cloud servers, reduced-capacity (35.8% parameters) for edge devices — from a single checkpoint.

#### 7.3.2 Content Creation Pipelines

**Use Case:** End-to-end multimedia content generation with consistent semantics across modalities.

**Example Workflow:**
1. Provide a text script for a product video
2. Model generates storyboard images (visual generation)
3. Model generates voiceover audio (audio generation)
4. Model assembles into video with synchronized audio (video generation)

**Why ERNIE 5.0 Excels:** The unified next-group-of-tokens objective ensures semantic alignment between generated modalities. Section 6.2 shows ERNIE 5.0 achieves best-in-class VBench-Semantic score (83.40), indicating strong semantic consistency in video generation.

**Deployment Advantage:** The cascaded diffusion refiner (Section 2.2.3) enables high-fidelity output while the autoregressive backbone maintains semantic precision — combining the strengths of both approaches.

#### 7.3.3 Accessibility and Translation Services

**Use Case:** Real-time multimodal translation and accessibility support.

**Example Workflow:**
1. User speaks a question in one language (audio input)
2. Model transcribes and translates to text (audio understanding + text generation)
3. Model generates visual diagrams to support comprehension (visual generation)
4. Model produces audio output in target language (audio generation)

**Why ERNIE 5.0 Excels:** Section 6.3 shows state-of-the-art ASR performance (AISHELL-1 WER: 0.31, Fleurs-zh WER: 0.83) combined with strong multilingual text understanding (MMMLU: 78.94, Table 1). The unified architecture ensures translation consistency across modalities.

**Deployment Advantage:** Elastic sparsity enables >15% decoding speedup (Introduction) with minor accuracy loss, critical for real-time applications.

#### 7.3.4 Educational and Training Systems

**Use Case:** Adaptive learning systems that respond to student inputs across modalities.

**Example Workflow:**
1. Student submits a handwritten math solution (image input)
2. Model evaluates correctness and identifies errors (visual understanding)
3. Model generates step-by-step explanation (text generation)
4. Model creates practice problems with diagrams (visual + text generation)

**Why ERNIE 5.0 Excels:** Strong performance on document understanding (DocVQAval: 95.45, Table 3) and math reasoning (MATH CoT: 73.89, Table 1) supports accurate evaluation. The unified architecture enables seamless transitions between evaluation and explanation generation.

**Deployment Advantage:** Elastic width enables deploying smaller variants (32 experts vs. 64 experts, Table 10) on school district hardware with memory constraints.

#### 7.3.5 Research and Analysis Tools

**Use Case:** Automated analysis of scientific papers with figures, tables, and supplementary audio/video.

**Example Workflow:**
1. Upload a research paper with figures and tables (document understanding)
2. Model extracts key findings and methodology (text understanding)

**Why ERNIE 5.0 Excels:** Strong performance on scientific reasoning benchmarks (GPQA-Diamond: 86.36, Table 2) combined with document understanding capabilities supports accurate extraction. The unified architecture ensures consistency between extracted findings and generated summaries.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

Practitioners evaluating whether to adopt ERNIE 5.0's approach should consider the following decision framework:

#### 7.4.1 When to Prefer Unified Autoregressive Architecture

**Choose ERNIE 5.0's approach when:**

| Condition | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Multiple modalities required** | Unified architecture eliminates integration overhead between separate models |
| **Cross-modal consistency critical** | Shared representation space ensures semantic alignment across modalities (Section 6.4.1) |
| **Both understanding and generation needed** | Single objective supports both without "ability seesaw" (Section 1) |
| **Deployment flexibility required** | Elastic training enables multiple deployment sizes from one checkpoint (Section 3.3) |
| **Training from scratch feasible** | Unified training requires simultaneous exposure to all modalities from initialization (Section 2.1) |

**Choose alternative approaches when:**

| Condition | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Single modality sufficient** | Specialized models may achieve higher performance on individual tasks |
| **Extreme reasoning required** | ERNIE 5.0 trails on GPQA-Diamond (-5.54), AIME 2025 (-5.94), HMMT 2025 (-13.75) vs. Gemini 3-Pro (Table 2) |
| **Highest-quality TTS needed** | Specialist systems (CosyVoice 3: 0.71 WER) outperform ERNIE 5.0 (1.35 WER, Table 8) |
| **Limited training compute** | Unified training requires massive compute; exact FLOPs not disclosed (Section 6.2) |
| **Post-hoc compression required** | If you already have a trained model, pruning/distillation may be more efficient than retraining |

#### 7.4.2 Integration Considerations

**Infrastructure Requirements:**

Based on Section 5, practitioners should anticipate:

- **Memory:** FP8 mixed-precision training and activation offloading are essential for trillion-parameter MoE (Section 5.1)
- **Communication:** DeepEP or equivalent for efficient inter-node expert communication (Section 5.1)
- **Attention:** FlashMask or equivalent for heterogeneous attention patterns across modalities (Section 5.3)
- **RL Infrastructure:** Disaggregated control plane for stable RL post-training (Section 5.4)

**Training Recipe Adaptation:**

If adapting ERNIE 5.0's approach:

- **Stage 1:** Start with 8K context, WSD learning rate schedule, peak LR $1 \times 10^{-4}$ (Section 3.2)
- **Stage 2:** Extend to 32K/128K with cosine schedule, anneal LR to $1 \times 10^{-5}$ (Section 3.2)
- **MoE Load Balancing:** Use auxiliary-loss-free load balancing with bias update speed $1 \times 10^{-4}$ (Stage 1) → $1 \times 10^{-5}$ (Stage 2) (Section 3.2)
- **Elastic Training:** Sample full model 75% of time, reduced-depth 25%; full experts 80%, reduced 20% (Section 3.3)

**RL Post-Training:**

If applying RL to ultra-sparse MoE:

- **Use U-RB:** Prevents GPU idleness from long-tail responses while maintaining unbiased data distribution (Section 4.1)
- **Apply MISC:** Prevents entropy collapse that occurs with standard importance sampling (Section 4.2, Figure 6)
- **Consider AHRL:** Provides scaffolding for hard tasks with sparse rewards (Section 4.3)

### 7.5 Summary: A Paradigm, Not Just a Model

ERNIE 5.0's most significant contribution is demonstrating that **unified multimodal autoregressive modeling is feasible at production scale**. Prior work forced trade-offs between understanding and generation, between multimodal integration and single-modality performance, or between model capacity and deployment flexibility. ERNIE 5.0 shows these are not inherent constraints but consequences of architectural choices that can be redesigned.

The research agenda it opens — causal attribution, elastic fine-tuning, modality scaling, RL generalization, compute efficiency — defines the next frontier for foundation model development. Whether ERNIE 5.0's specific design choices become standard or are superseded by future innovations, the paradigm it establishes — unified objectives, modality-agnostic routing, elastic training — will shape how the field thinks about multimodal foundation models for years to come.

For practitioners, the key takeaway is that unified architectures are now a viable alternative to fragmented systems. For researchers, the open questions represent opportunities to validate, extend, or challenge ERNIE 5.0's design principles. The work invites the community not just to benchmark against it, but to build upon its architectural foundation.
