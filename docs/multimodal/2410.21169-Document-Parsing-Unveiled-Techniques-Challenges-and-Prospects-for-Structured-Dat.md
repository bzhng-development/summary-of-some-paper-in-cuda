## 1. Executive Summary

This survey provides a comprehensive taxonomy of document parsing methodologies, systematically evaluating the transition from modular pipeline systems—comprising distinct stages for layout analysis, OCR, and table recognition using models like `DocLayout-YOLO` and `LayoutLMv3`—to end-to-end Vision-Language Models (VLMs) such as `Nougat`, `GOT`, and `Fox`. By consolidating over 50 datasets (including `PubLayNet`, `UniMER-1M`, and `OmniDocBench`) and defining specific evaluation metrics like Tree-Edit-Distance-based Similarity (TEDS) for tables and Character Detection Matching (CDM) for mathematical expressions, the paper addresses the critical fragmentation in current research that hinders the construction of reliable knowledge bases for Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems. This work matters because it establishes a unified framework for converting unstructured documents into structured formats like Markdown and JSON, directly enabling the next generation of multimodal AI applications that require high-fidelity extraction of complex elements such as formulas, nested tables, and charts.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: The Unstructured Data Bottleneck
The fundamental challenge addressed by this paper is the **"unstructured data bottleneck"** in the era of Large Language Models (LLMs). While digital transformation has shifted information exchange to electronic formats (PDFs, scanned images), a vast reservoir of human knowledge remains trapped in unstructured or semi-structured documents. These include legal contracts, academic papers, invoices, and historical records.

The specific problem is not merely reading text; it is **preserving structural fidelity** during conversion. A standard document contains complex spatial relationships:
*   **Multi-column layouts** where reading order is non-linear.
*   **Nested tables** with merged cells and implicit headers.
*   **Mathematical expressions** (both inline and displayed) requiring 2D spatial reasoning.
*   **Charts and diagrams** where visual data must be extracted as structured JSON or tables.

Current systems often fail to convert these elements into machine-readable formats (like Markdown, LaTeX, or JSON) without losing critical context. For instance, a modular system might extract the text from a table but lose the row-column associations, rendering the data useless for downstream analysis. This gap prevents the reliable construction of knowledge bases required for advanced applications like **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)**, where the quality of the retrieved context directly dictates the accuracy of the generated answer.

### Real-World Impact and Theoretical Significance
The importance of solving document parsing extends beyond simple digitization; it is a prerequisite for the next generation of intelligent systems.

1.  **Enabling RAG and Knowledge Base Construction:** As noted in the introduction, RAG systems rely on retrieving precise context to ground LLM responses. If the parsing layer fails to distinguish between a figure caption and the main text, or misinterprets a table's hierarchy, the LLM receives "noisy" context, leading to hallucinations or incorrect reasoning. High-fidelity parsing is the foundation of trustworthy AI agents.
2.  **Training Data Generation:** The paper highlights that document parsing is essential for generating training data for multimodal models. To train a model to understand scientific papers, one needs massive datasets where text, formulas, and figures are correctly aligned and labeled. Manual annotation is prohibitively expensive; automated, accurate parsing is the only scalable solution.
3.  **Accessibility and Preservation:** Converting scanned historical records or image-based legal documents into structured, searchable text ensures long-term preservation and accessibility, allowing for efficient information retrieval that was previously impossible.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Before the advancements surveyed in this paper, the field was characterized by fragmentation and rigid architectures. The authors identify two primary categories of prior work, both of which exhibit significant shortcomings:

#### 1. Outdated or Narrow Surveys
Existing literature reviews suffer from two main defects:
*   **Obsolescence:** Many surveys describe pipelines that lack rigor by modern standards, failing to capture the rapid evolution of deep learning techniques over the last few years.
*   **Siloed Focus:** High-quality reviews often focus exclusively on specific sub-tasks. For example, some surveys cover only **Document Layout Analysis (DLA)** [19, 159], others only **Mathematical Expression Recognition** [2, 108], **Table Structure Recognition** [101, 154], or **Chart Analysis** [45].
    *   *The Gap:* This siloed approach ignores the **interdependence** of these tasks. In a real document, a table might contain a mathematical formula, which is embedded within a specific layout column. Analyzing these components in isolation fails to address the holistic challenge of end-to-end document understanding.

#### 2. Architectural Limitations of Traditional Systems
*   **Modular Pipeline Fragility:** Traditional systems rely on a cascade of independent modules (e.g., Layout Detection $\rightarrow$ OCR $\rightarrow$ Table Recognition).
    *   *Error Propagation:* If the layout analysis module incorrectly identifies a text block, the subsequent OCR module receives cropped, misaligned input, compounding the error.
    *   *Integration Complexity:* Merging outputs from different models (often trained on different datasets with different coordinate systems) requires complex, rule-based post-processing. These rules often break when encountering complex layouts, such as multi-column academic papers or documents with irregular reading orders.
*   **Lack of Semantic Understanding:** Early deep learning approaches treated documents purely as images, focusing on visual features (bounding boxes) while ignoring semantic context (e.g., knowing that a block of text is a "footer" vs. a "caption" based on content, not just position).

### Positioning of This Survey
This paper positions itself as the **first holistic review** that bridges the gap between fragmented sub-task research and the emerging paradigm of end-to-end multimodal learning.

*   **From Modular to End-to-End:** Unlike previous works that catalog isolated techniques, this survey systematically traces the evolution from **Modular Pipeline Systems** (Section 3–6) to **End-to-End Vision-Language Models (VLMs)** (Section 7). It explicitly analyzes how models like `Nougat`, `GOT`, and `Fox` are replacing complex pipelines with unified architectures that process raw document images directly into structured Markdown.
*   **Consolidation of Resources:** The paper addresses the lack of standardized benchmarks by consolidating widely used **datasets** (e.g., `PubLayNet`, `UniMER-1M`, `OmniDocBench`) and defining appropriate **evaluation metrics** for each sub-task (e.g., `TEDS` for tables, `CDM` for formulas). This provides researchers with a clear map of the available resources, which was previously scattered across domain-specific papers.
*   **Critical Analysis of Challenges:** Rather than simply listing successes, the survey critically examines current limitations, such as the struggle of VLMs with high-density text, the difficulty of handling non-Latin scripts, and the computational inefficiency of processing multi-page documents.

By providing this comprehensive landscape, the paper serves as both an **introductory guide for newcomers** to understand the full pipeline and a **strategic roadmap for practitioners** aiming to build robust document parsing systems for real-world applications.

## 3. Technical Approach

This survey paper does not propose a single new algorithm but rather constructs a comprehensive technical taxonomy that categorizes document parsing into two distinct architectural paradigms: **Modular Pipeline Systems**, which decompose the task into sequential, specialized sub-tasks, and **End-to-End Vision-Language Models (VLMs)**, which utilize unified transformer architectures to map raw document images directly to structured text. The core idea driving this classification is the trade-off between the granular control and interpretability of modular systems versus the semantic coherence and reduced error propagation of end-to-end large models.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system described in this paper is a conceptual framework for converting unstructured document images (like scanned PDFs) into machine-readable structured data (like Markdown or JSON) by either chaining together specialized AI models for specific tasks or using a single massive model to handle the entire process at once. It solves the problem of information loss during digitization by providing a rigorous map of how to detect layout, recognize text, parse tables, and interpret formulas, ensuring that the spatial and logical relationships within the original document are preserved in the final output.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The architecture of document parsing, as analyzed in this survey, can be visualized as a flow of information starting from a raw document image and branching into two primary processing paths.
*   **Input Layer:** The process begins with a **Document Image** (often high-resolution), which may undergo pre-processing steps like deskewing or binarization.
*   **Path A: Modular Pipeline System:** The image first enters a **Layout Analysis** module that identifies bounding boxes for text, tables, and figures; these cropped regions are then routed to specialized **Content Extraction** modules (OCR for text, Table Recognition for grids, MER for formulas); finally, a **Relation Integration** module reassembles these extracted pieces into a coherent structured format based on spatial coordinates.
*   **Path B: End-to-End VLM:** The raw image is fed directly into a **Vision Encoder** (such as a ViT or Swin Transformer) that converts pixels into feature tokens; these tokens are then passed to a **Large Language Model Decoder** which autoregressively generates the structured text output (e.g., Markdown) in a single pass, implicitly learning layout and content simultaneously.
*   **Output Layer:** Both paths converge on a **Structured Representation** (Markdown, LaTeX, JSON) that preserves the document's logical hierarchy and content fidelity.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **First**, we will dissect the **Modular Pipeline System**, starting with **Document Layout Analysis (DLA)** because identifying *where* elements are located is the foundational step that dictates how subsequent modules process the image.
*   **Second**, we will examine **Optical Character Recognition (OCR)** in detail, breaking down its evolution from simple text detection to semantic-aware recognition, as this remains the most critical component for extracting the bulk of document content.
*   **Third**, we will explore specialized extraction modules for **Mathematical Expressions** and **Tables**, which require unique architectural adaptations (like encoder-decoder structures with attention) to handle 2D spatial relationships that standard OCR cannot capture.
*   **Fourth**, we will analyze **Chart Perception**, a distinct sub-field focused on converting visual data representations back into numerical tables, highlighting the shift from rule-based to deep learning approaches.
*   **Fifth**, we will transition to **End-to-End Vision-Language Models**, explaining how architectures like `Nougat` and `GOT` unify these disparate tasks into a single transformer framework, eliminating the need for explicit modular boundaries.
*   **Finally**, we will discuss the **Integration and Evaluation** mechanisms, detailing how modular outputs are reassembled and how specific metrics like TEDS and CDM are mathematically defined to measure success in these complex tasks.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### The Modular Pipeline Paradigm
The modular approach treats document parsing as a cascade of independent problems, where the output of one stage serves as the input for the next, allowing for specialized optimization at each step but introducing the risk of error propagation.

**Document Layout Analysis (DLA): The Spatial Foundation**
Layout analysis is the critical first step that segments a document page into semantic regions (text blocks, tables, images, formulas) and determines their reading order.
*   **Visual Feature-Based Methods:** Early and current high-performance methods treat the document page purely as an image, using object detection architectures to draw bounding boxes around elements.
    *   **CNN-Based Detection:** Models adapted from general object detection, such as the **YOLO series** (specifically `DocLayout-YOLO`), utilize a **Global-to-Local Controllable Receptive Module (GL-CRM)** to effectively detect targets of varying scales, balancing high inference speed with accuracy for elements ranging from small footnotes to large tables.
    *   **Transformer-Based Detection:** Architectures like **DiT (Document Image Transformer)** split the document image into patches and apply self-attention mechanisms to capture global dependencies, allowing the model to understand the relationship between a title and its corresponding paragraph even if they are far apart spatially, though this comes at a higher computational cost than CNNs.
    *   **Graph-Based Methods:** To incorporate structural relationships beyond simple visual proximity, models like **Doc-GCN** and **GLAM** represent document components as nodes in a graph, where edges encode spatial or semantic relationships, enabling the model to reason about the document structure using Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs).
    *   **Grid-Based Methods:** Approaches like **BERTGrid** and **VGT** represent the layout as a 2D grid of tokens, preserving precise spatial coordinates within the embedding space, which helps in retaining fine-grained layout details that might be lost in standard pooling operations.
*   **Semantic-Integrated Methods:** Recognizing that visual features alone are insufficient for complex logical structures, newer models fuse text, image, and layout information.
    *   **Multimodal Fusion:** The **LayoutLM** series (v1, v2, v3) integrates text embeddings, 2D position embeddings, and image embeddings into a single Transformer framework, allowing the model to classify a region as a "header" not just by its position at the top of the page, but by analyzing the actual text content and visual style simultaneously.
    *   **Advanced Aggregation:** Models like **UniDoc** and **VSR** employ cross-modal attention mechanisms to align linguistic and visual features, while **LayoutLLM** leverages large language models to inject high-level semantic reasoning into the layout analysis process, improving the classification of ambiguous elements.

**Optical Character Recognition (OCR): From Detection to Semantics**
Once layout analysis identifies text regions, OCR converts the pixel data within those regions into machine-encoded characters, a process divided into detection, recognition, and spotting.
*   **Text Detection:** This stage localizes text within an image, handling challenges like arbitrary orientations and curved text.
    *   **Regression-Based Methods:** Single-stage detectors like **TextBoxes** and **DRRG** directly predict the coordinates or aspect ratios of text boxes from image features, bypassing the need for region proposals to achieve faster inference speeds.
    *   **Segmentation-Based Methods:** Approaches like **PAN** and **CRAFT** treat text detection as a pixel-wise segmentation problem, classifying each pixel as text or background, which provides superior flexibility for handling irregular and curved text shapes.
    *   **Hybrid Methods:** Architectures like **EAST** combine regression and segmentation outputs, using position-aware non-maximum suppression to optimize detection across different scales, while newer methods like **CentripetalText** use centripetal shifts to refine text localization accuracy.
*   **Text Recognition:** This stage transcribes the cropped text images into character sequences.
    *   **Vision-Feature Based:** Traditional methods like **CRNN** use Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to extract visual features, which are then processed by Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) to generate sequences, often optimized with **Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC)** loss to handle variable-length outputs without explicit character alignment.
    *   **Sequence-to-Sequence (Seq2Seq):** Modern approaches utilize encoder-decoder architectures with attention mechanisms, such as **SAR** and **TrOCR**, which allow the decoder to focus on specific parts of the image feature map when generating each character, significantly improving performance on irregular text.
    *   **Semantic Integration:** To handle blurred or occluded text, models like **ABINet** and **SEED** incorporate language models or semantic modules that provide contextual clues, effectively "guessing" missing characters based on the surrounding words, while **VisionLAN** uses masked language modeling during pre-training to enhance contextual awareness.
*   **Text Spotting:** This unified approach combines detection and recognition into a single end-to-end network.
    *   **Two-Stage Methods:** Architectures like **Mask TextSpotter** share feature maps between detection and recognition heads, using Region of Interest (RoI) mechanisms (e.g., **RoIAlign**, **BezierAlign**) to extract features for arbitrary-shaped text regions, allowing for joint training that improves consistency between locating and reading text.
    *   **One-Stage Methods:** Models like **SPTS** and **TESTR** eliminate the RoI cropping step entirely, decoding character sequences directly from the shared feature map using point-based queries or Transformer architectures, which simplifies the pipeline and reduces computational overhead.

**Mathematical Expression Detection and Recognition (MED/MER)**
Mathematical expressions pose a unique challenge due to their two-dimensional structure and complex symbol relationships, requiring specialized handling distinct from standard text.
*   **Detection:** The system must first distinguish between **inline expressions** (embedded within text lines) and **displayed expressions** (standing alone).
    *   **Object Detection Adaptations:** Models like **DS-YOLOv5** adapt single-stage detectors with deformable convolutions to handle the varying scales of formulas, while **FormulaDet** reframes the problem as entity and relation extraction to better capture the context-aware nature of mathematical notation.
    *   **Segmentation Approaches:** Early methods used **U-Net** architectures for end-to-end detection, segmenting formula pixels from the background, though these often struggle with noise in scanned documents.
*   **Recognition:** The goal is to convert the image of a formula into a structured format like LaTeX.
    *   **Encoder Strategies:** Encoders utilize deep CNNs (e.g., **ResNet**, **DenseNet**) or Vision Transformers (e.g., **Swin Transformer**) to extract rich visual features that capture the spatial arrangement of symbols, which is crucial for distinguishing between subscripts, superscripts, and fractions.
    *   **Decoder Strategies:** Decoders typically employ RNNs with attention or Transformer architectures to generate the LaTeX sequence, where the attention mechanism allows the model to focus on specific parts of the formula (e.g., the numerator) while generating the corresponding code.
    *   **Enhancement Techniques:** Performance is further boosted by incorporating **character and length hints** as auxiliary inputs, utilizing **stroke order information** for handwritten expressions, and applying aggressive **data augmentation** to simulate various handwriting styles and distortions.

**Table Detection and Structure Recognition**
Tables require the extraction of both content and the underlying grid structure (rows, columns, merged cells), a task that goes beyond simple text recognition.
*   **Table Detection:** This step locates table regions within the document, often treated as an object detection task using models like **Faster R-CNN** or **YOLO**, enhanced with features specific to document layouts (e.g., integrating PDF coordinate features).
*   **Structure Recognition:** Once a table is detected, the system must reconstruct its logical structure.
    *   **Row and Column Segmentation:** Top-down approaches first identify the table boundary and then segment it into rows and columns using CNNs or Transformers (e.g., **DETR**-based models like **DQ-DETR**), often employing fusion modules to predict cell merging probabilities in tables without explicit grid lines.
    *   **Cell-Based Methods:** Bottom-up approaches detect individual cells as objects and then use Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) or clustering algorithms to associate them into rows and columns, which is particularly effective for complex tables with irregular structures but can be computationally expensive.
    *   **Image-to-Sequence:** Inspired by OCR, models like **MASTER** and **VAST** treat table recognition as an image-to-sequence problem, using encoder-decoder architectures to directly generate LaTeX or HTML code that represents the table structure, leveraging attention mechanisms to align visual cells with structural tags.

**Chart Perception**
Chart parsing involves interpreting graphical representations of data (bar charts, line charts, pie charts) and extracting the underlying numerical values.
*   **Classification and Detection:** The system first classifies the chart type and detects composite charts (multiple sub-charts in one image) using CNNs or Vision Transformers (e.g., **Swin-Chart**), which excel at capturing both local textures and global structural patterns.
*   **Element Recognition:** Specialized detectors identify chart components such as axes, legends, bars, and data points, often using object detection frameworks like **Faster R-CNN** adapted for fine-grained chart elements.
*   **Data Extraction:** The final step correlates text labels with visual elements to reconstruct the data table.
    *   **Correlation Matching:** Advanced methods use Transformer architectures to model the relationship between text labels (e.g., axis ticks) and their corresponding visual bars or lines, resolving ambiguities in crowded charts.
    *   **Structure Extraction:** For flowcharts and diagrams, models like **FR-DETR** simultaneously detect symbols and connecting edges, reconstructing the logical flow of the diagram into a structured format.

#### The End-to-End Vision-Language Model Paradigm
In contrast to the modular approach, end-to-end models leverage the capabilities of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) to process the entire document in a unified manner, implicitly learning the relationships between layout, text, and structure.

**Architecture and Mechanism**
*   **Unified Encoding:** Models like **Nougat**, **Donut**, and **GOT** utilize a powerful **Vision Encoder** (often a Swin Transformer or ViT) to convert the raw document image into a sequence of visual tokens, eliminating the need for explicit cropping or region proposal.
*   **Autoregressive Decoding:** These visual tokens are fed into a **Large Language Model Decoder** (e.g., BART, mBART, or LLaMA-based), which is trained to autoregressively generate the structured output (Markdown, LaTeX, JSON) token by token, effectively "translating" the image into text.
*   **Implicit Layout Learning:** Unlike modular systems that explicitly calculate bounding boxes, end-to-end models learn the concept of layout and reading order implicitly through the training objective, allowing them to handle complex multi-column layouts and nested structures more naturally.
*   **Specialized Adaptations:**
    *   **Nougat:** Specifically designed for academic documents, it integrates mathematical expression recognition and page relationship organization directly into the generation process, converting scientific papers directly to Markdown.
    *   **GOT (General OCR Theory):** Treats all characters (text, formulas, tables, musical scores) as uniform objects, utilizing a massive dataset of 5 million text-image pairs and a three-stage training strategy (pre-training, joint training, fine-tuning) to achieve universal OCR capabilities.
    *   **Fox:** Addresses multi-page document understanding by leveraging multiple pre-trained visual vocabularies and hybrid data generation strategies, enabling it to maintain context across page boundaries for tasks like cross-page summarization.
    *   **OmniParser:** Introduces a two-stage decoder architecture that decouples OCR from structural sequence processing, enhancing the extraction of key information and table structures while reducing inference time compared to purely autoregressive models.

**Integration and Relation Reconstruction**
*   **Modular Integration:** In pipeline systems, the **Relation Integration** module is a critical post-processing step that uses the spatial coordinates from layout analysis to order the extracted text blocks logically, often employing rule-based systems or specialized reading order models to resolve ambiguities in complex layouts.
*   **End-to-End Coherence:** In VLMs, relation reconstruction is inherent to the generation process; the model learns to output text in the correct reading order and format (e.g., placing a table caption before the table) based on the statistical patterns learned during training on large-scale document datasets.

**Evaluation Metrics and Mathematical Formulations**
The paper emphasizes that standard metrics like accuracy are insufficient for document parsing, necessitating specialized metrics for different components.
*   **Layout Analysis:** Performance is measured using **Intersection over Union (IoU)** to evaluate bounding box overlap, defined as:
    $$ \text{IoU} = \frac{\text{Area of Overlap}}{\text{Area of Union}} $$
    along with **Mean Average Precision (mAP)** calculated over a range of IoU thresholds to assess detection robustness.
*   **OCR:** Text recognition quality is evaluated using **Character Error Rate (CER)** and **Word Error Rate (WER)**, which measure the edit distance between the predicted and ground truth sequences:
    $$ \text{CER} = \frac{S + D + I}{N} $$
    where $S$, $D$, and $I$ represent the number of substitutions, deletions, and insertions, and $N$ is the total number of characters in the ground truth.
*   **Table Recognition:** Structural fidelity is assessed using **Tree-Edit-Distance-based Similarity (TEDS)**, which calculates the similarity between the tree structures of the predicted and ground truth tables:
    $$ \text{TEDS}(T_1, T_2) = 1 - \frac{\text{TED}(T_1, T_2)}{\max(\text{size}(T_1), \text{size}(T_2))} $$
    where $\text{TED}$ is the tree edit distance, capturing errors in row/column spanning and hierarchy that simple text matching would miss.
*   **Mathematical Expression:** To address the issue of multiple valid LaTeX representations for the same formula, the paper highlights **Character Detection Matching (CDM)**, which converts the generated LaTeX back into an image and compares it with the ground truth image structure:
    $$ \text{CDM} = \frac{2 \times TP}{2 \times TP + FP + FN} $$
    providing a more fair and intuitive evaluation of structural correctness than string-based metrics.

This technical breakdown illustrates that while modular systems offer precision and interpretability through specialized components like `DocLayout-YOLO` and `CRNN`, end-to-end models like `GOT` and `Nougat` represent a paradigm shift towards unified, semantic-aware parsing that better handles the complexity and variability of real-world documents.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This survey distills the rapid evolution of document parsing into several fundamental shifts that distinguish current state-of-the-art approaches from prior art. The transition is not merely incremental; it represents a rethinking of how machines perceive, structure, and reason about document content. The following insights highlight the most significant conceptual and architectural breakthroughs.

### 4.1 The Paradigm Shift: From Cascaded Error Propagation to Unified Semantic Generation
The most profound innovation identified in this survey is the architectural transition from **Modular Pipeline Systems** to **End-to-End Vision-Language Models (VLMs)**.

*   **Prior Work Limitation:** Traditional systems (Sections 3–6) operate on a "detect-then-recognize" philosophy. They rely on a cascade of independent modules: Layout Analysis $\rightarrow$ Cropping $\rightarrow$ OCR/MER/Table Recognition $\rightarrow$ Rule-based Reassembly.
    *   *The Fatal Flaw:* This architecture suffers from **irreversible error propagation**. If the layout module misidentifies a column boundary (a common issue in complex academic papers), the OCR module receives a truncated image, guaranteeing a transcription error that no downstream module can fix. Furthermore, integrating these modules requires fragile, hand-crafted rules to determine reading order, which often fail on non-standard layouts.
*   **The Innovation:** End-to-end models like `Nougat`, `GOT`, and `Fox` (Section 7) replace this cascade with a **unified generative process**.
    *   *Mechanism:* Instead of explicitly detecting bounding boxes, these models use a Vision Encoder to tokenize the entire page and a Large Language Model decoder to autoregressively generate the structured output (e.g., Markdown).
    *   *Significance:* This eliminates the "coordination problem" between modules. The model learns **implicit layout reasoning**; it understands that a formula belongs to a specific paragraph not because of coordinate overlap, but because of semantic context. This shift allows for the recovery of global document structure (like multi-page continuity) that modular systems inherently miss due to their page-by-page processing constraints.

### 4.2 Universal OCR: Treating Heterogeneous Content as a Single Object Class
A critical theoretical advance highlighted in the discussion of `GOT` (General OCR Theory) is the move toward **Universal OCR**, which fundamentally changes how diverse document elements are categorized.

*   **Prior Work Limitation:** Historically, the field has been siloed. Text recognition, mathematical expression recognition (MER), table structure recognition, and even music score parsing were treated as distinct tasks requiring separate models, datasets, and evaluation metrics. A system needed a dedicated `CRNN` for text, a specialized encoder-decoder for formulas, and a graph network for tables.
*   **The Innovation:** `GOT` introduces the concept that **all visual semantic units are objects**. Whether it is a Chinese character, a Greek letter in a formula, a cell in a table, or a musical note, the model treats them uniformly as tokens to be predicted.
    *   *Mechanism:* By training on a massive, heterogeneous dataset of 5 million image-text pairs covering text, formulas, tables, and diagrams, the model learns a shared visual vocabulary. It does not switch modes; it simply predicts the next token in the sequence, regardless of whether that token represents text or a LaTeX command.
    *   *Significance:* This unification drastically reduces system complexity and enables **zero-shot generalization** to rare content types (e.g., chemical structures or geometric figures) that previously required bespoke models. It represents a move from "specialized tools" to a "generalist agent" for document understanding.

### 4.3 Evaluation Rigor: Moving Beyond String Matching to Structural Fidelity
The survey makes a crucial methodological contribution by exposing the inadequacy of standard NLP metrics for document parsing and championing **structure-aware evaluation metrics**.

*   **Prior Work Limitation:** Early evaluations relied heavily on string-based metrics like BLEU or exact match rates.
    *   *The Fatal Flaw:* These metrics are brittle for structured data. In mathematical expressions, `$\frac{a}{b}$` and `{a \over b}` are semantically identical but string-distinct, leading to false negatives in evaluation. Similarly, for tables, a model might extract all correct numbers but swap two columns; a string metric would penalize this heavily, while a structural metric might reveal the logic is mostly intact, or vice versa.
*   **The Innovation:** The paper consolidates and advocates for metrics that measure **structural equivalence** rather than textual identity.
    *   **CDM (Character Detection Matching) for Formulas:** Instead of comparing LaTeX strings, CDM renders the predicted LaTeX back into an image and compares the spatial arrangement of characters against the ground truth image. This bypasses the "multiple valid representations" problem entirely.
    *   **TEDS (Tree-Edit-Distance-based Similarity) for Tables:** TEDS represents tables as tree structures (capturing rows, columns, and spanning cells) and calculates the edit distance between trees.
    *   *Significance:* This shift forces the community to optimize for **semantic correctness** rather than syntactic memorization. It provides a fairer benchmark for models that generate valid but stylistically different code (e.g., different LaTeX styling), ensuring that progress in the field reflects true improvements in data fidelity.

### 4.4 High-Resolution Multi-Page Context: Breaking the Single-Page Barrier
A subtle but vital innovation discussed in the context of models like `Fox` and `DocOwl` is the capability to process **multi-page documents with high-resolution fidelity**, addressing a major bottleneck in real-world applications.

*   **Prior Work Limitation:** Most VLMs and modular systems process documents **page-by-page**. This creates a "context window" problem where the model loses information spanning across pages (e.g., a table continuing from page 3 to page 4, or a reference list linking to citations on previous pages). Additionally, resizing high-resolution document images to fit standard vision encoder inputs (e.g., 224x224 or 336x336) results in the loss of fine-grained text details.
*   **The Innovation:** Newer architectures employ **dynamic resolution strategies** and **cross-page attention mechanisms**.
    *   *Mechanism:* Techniques like sliding windows (used in `Ureader` and `TextMonkey`) and hybrid visual vocabularies allow models to ingest high-resolution crops without losing global context. `Fox` specifically utilizes hybrid data generation to synthesize multi-page training examples, enabling the model to learn dependencies that span page boundaries.
    *   *Significance:* This capability transforms document parsing from a "digitization" task into a true "comprehension" task. It enables downstream applications like **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)** to retrieve context that relies on synthesizing information from multiple pages, a requirement for analyzing legal contracts, long-form academic papers, and technical manuals.

### 4.5 Data-Centric Breakthrough: The Rise of Synthetic and Hybrid Corpora
Finally, the survey highlights that recent performance leaps are driven as much by **data engineering** as by architectural changes.

*   **Prior Work Limitation:** The field was historically constrained by the scarcity of high-quality, densely annotated real-world data. Annotating layout, text, formulas, and table structures simultaneously is prohibitively expensive and slow, leading to small datasets that caused models to overfit.
*   **The Innovation:** The community has shifted toward **large-scale synthetic and hybrid dataset generation**.
    *   *Mechanism:* Projects like `DocSynth-300K` and the datasets used by `GOT` and `Nougat` leverage programmatic rendering engines to generate millions of diverse document images with perfect ground-truth labels. They combine real-world textures and fonts with synthetic layouts to create data that is both diverse and precisely annotated.
    *   *Significance:* This data-centric approach has allowed models to learn robust features for rare classes (e.g., complex nested tables or handwritten formulas) that were underrepresented in previous benchmarks. It suggests that future gains in document parsing will depend less on tweaking model hyperparameters and more on the diversity and scale of the training corpora.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This survey does not present a single monolithic experiment with a unified training run; rather, it synthesizes experimental evidence from dozens of primary research papers to validate the efficacy of different parsing paradigms. The "experiment" here is a meta-analysis of performance across three distinct dimensions: **Modular Pipeline Components**, **End-to-End Vision-Language Models (VLMs)**, and **Evaluation Metric Robustness**. The analysis relies on the consolidated datasets and metrics detailed in the Appendix (Sections 11.1 and 11.2) to draw conclusions about the state of the field.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: A Fragmented Landscape Standardized

The experimental landscape of document parsing is historically fragmented, with sub-tasks (layout, OCR, tables, formulas) utilizing disjointed benchmarks. This survey consolidates these into a coherent evaluation framework.

#### Datasets: From Synthetic to Real-World Complexity
The survey categorizes datasets by task, highlighting a critical shift from small, real-world collections to massive, synthetic-hybrid corpora.
*   **Layout Analysis:** Experiments rely on **PubLayNet** (360,000 instances) and **DocBank** (500,000 instances) for general layout, but newer models like `DocLayout-YOLO` are validated on **DocSynth-300K**, a synthetic dataset designed to cover rare layout configurations that real-world data misses. For comprehensive benchmarking, the survey highlights **OmniDocBench** (981 PDF pages, 100,000 annotations) as the new gold standard, covering 9 document types and 19 layout tags to fairly evaluate both modular and end-to-end systems.
*   **OCR & Text Spotting:** While scene text datasets like **ICDAR 2015** and **Total-Text** remain standard for robustness, document-specific evaluation increasingly uses **LOCR** (7 million instances) and **ICDAR2019-ReCTS** to test performance on dense, structured document text rather than natural scene text.
*   **Mathematical Expressions:** The field has moved from small sets like **InftyCDB-1** (467 images) to massive corpora like **UniMER-1M** (1.06 million instances), which combines printed and handwritten expressions to train universal recognizers.
*   **Tables:** Evaluation spans from **ICDAR2013** (150 complex tables) to web-scale datasets like **PubTabNet** (568,000 tables) and **PubTables-1M** (1 million tables), enabling the training of deep learning models that generalize across diverse column structures and merged cells.
*   **Charts:** Benchmarks include **Chart2019** (real scientific charts) and **LINEEX430k** (430,000 line charts), focusing on the ability to extract underlying numerical data rather than just classifying chart types.

#### Metrics: The Shift to Structural Fidelity
A key methodological contribution of this survey is the critique of string-based metrics and the endorsement of structure-aware alternatives.
*   **Layout & Detection:** Standard **Intersection over Union (IoU)** and **Mean Average Precision (mAP)** are used, often calculated over a range of thresholds (e.g., `mAP@IoU[0.5:0.95]`) to ensure robustness against slight localization errors.
*   **OCR:** **Character Error Rate (CER)** and **Word Error Rate (WER)** remain standard, defined as:
    $$ \text{CER} = \frac{S + D + I}{N} $$
    where $S, D, I$ are substitutions, deletions, and insertions. However, the survey notes that for document parsing, **BLEU** and **METEOR** are increasingly used to capture semantic similarity when exact character matching is too brittle.
*   **Tables:** The survey emphasizes **TEDS (Tree-Edit-Distance-based Similarity)** as the definitive metric. Unlike accuracy, TEDS measures the structural distance between the predicted and ground-truth tree representations of a table:
    $$ \text{TEDS}(T_1, T_2) = 1 - \frac{\text{TED}(T_1, T_2)}{\max(\text{size}(T_1), \text{size}(T_2))} $$
    This captures errors in row/column spanning that simple cell-content accuracy misses. Additional metrics like **CAR (Cell Adjacency Relations)** and **Aall** (requiring all four logical positions of a cell to be correct) provide granular insights into structural reconstruction.
*   **Mathematical Expressions:** The survey highlights **CDM (Character Detection Matching)** as a superior alternative to ExpRate or BLEU. CDM renders the predicted LaTeX back into an image and compares the spatial character distribution with the ground truth:
    $$ \text{CDM} = \frac{2 \times TP}{2 \times TP + FP + FN} $$
    This resolves the "multiple valid LaTeX representations" problem, where syntactically different strings (e.g., `\frac{a}{b}` vs. `{a \over b}`) represent the same visual structure.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results: Modular vs. End-to-End

The survey synthesizes results to compare the performance ceilings of modular pipelines against emerging end-to-end models.

#### Modular Pipeline Performance
Modular systems, when optimized with state-of-the-art components, achieve high precision on specific tasks but struggle with integration.
*   **Layout Analysis:** `DocLayout-YOLO`, leveraging the **GL-CRM** module, achieves leading results in inference speed and accuracy on multi-scale targets. On synthetic datasets like **DocSynth-300K**, it demonstrates superior detection of small elements (footnotes, page numbers) compared to standard YOLO variants, though exact mAP figures vary by dataset complexity.
*   **Table Recognition:** Models like **TableMaster** and **VAST**, utilizing dual-decoder architectures for structure and content, report **TEDS scores exceeding 90%** on clean datasets like **PubTabNet**. However, performance drops significantly on "in-the-wild" datasets like **Wired Table in the Wild**, where deformation and occlusion reduce TEDS scores, highlighting the fragility of modular approaches to noise.
*   **Mathematical Expression:** Specialized models trained on **UniMER-1M** achieve near-perfect **ExpRate** on printed formulas but show a marked decline in **CDM** scores when processing handwritten or low-resolution screen-captured expressions, indicating a gap in robustness.

#### End-to-End VLM Performance
End-to-end models trade some granular precision for holistic coherence and reduced engineering overhead.
*   **Nougat:** As the first end-to-end Transformer for academic documents, Nougat demonstrates the ability to convert complex scientific papers directly to Markdown. While it struggles with non-Latin scripts and exhibits slower conversion speeds due to high computational demands, it successfully integrates formula recognition and page relationships that modular systems often disconnect.
*   **GOT (General OCR Theory):** By treating all content as objects, GOT achieves universal OCR capabilities. Trained on **5 million image-text pairs**, it surpasses task-specific models in handling heterogeneous content (e.g., a page containing text, a table, and a musical score simultaneously). Its three-stage training strategy (pre-training, joint training, fine-tuning) allows it to generalize to unseen domains like geometric figures and chemical structures, areas where modular pipelines typically fail without retraining.
*   **Fox:** Addressing the multi-page limitation, Fox utilizes hybrid visual vocabularies to process documents across page boundaries. It outperforms earlier DELMs (Document Extraction Large Models) in cross-page translation and summary generation, proving that end-to-end models can maintain context where modular systems reset state at every page break.
*   **OmniParser:** This model introduces a two-stage decoder that decouples OCR from structural processing. Experimental comparisons show it outperforms task-specific models like **TESTER** and **SwinTextSpotter** in both text detection and table recognition while reducing inference time, suggesting that hybrid end-to-end architectures may offer the best balance of speed and accuracy.

### 5.3 Critical Assessment: Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

The experimental evidence presented in the survey strongly supports the claim that **end-to-end models are closing the gap with modular systems** while offering superior scalability and semantic coherence.

*   **Support for Universal OCR:** The performance of `GOT` on diverse tasks (text, formulas, music scores) validates the hypothesis that a unified visual vocabulary can replace specialized heads. The use of massive datasets (5M+ pairs) is shown to be the primary driver of this generalization, supporting the paper's emphasis on data-centric breakthroughs.
*   **Validation of Structural Metrics:** The survey's argument for **TEDS** and **CDM** is compelling. By demonstrating that string-based metrics (BLEU, ExpRate) penalize semantically correct but syntactically different outputs, the paper justifies the adoption of image-based and tree-based evaluation. The inclusion of **OmniDocBench** results further reinforces this, showing that models optimized for structural fidelity perform better on downstream tasks like RAG.
*   **The Multi-Page Advantage:** Results from `Fox` and `DocOwl` provide concrete evidence that end-to-end models can overcome the "single-page barrier," a limitation inherent to most modular pipelines. The ability to generate coherent summaries across pages is a qualitative leap that quantitative metrics like BLEU only partially capture.

However, the experiments also reveal **conditional limitations**:
*   **High-Density Text:** VLMs still lag behind modular OCR engines (like PaddleOCR or Tesseract) in recognizing extremely dense text blocks or diverse font styles (bold, italics) within a single line. The survey notes that VLMs often produce repeated outputs or formatting errors in long-document generation, attributed to frozen LLM parameters and inadequate alignment for high-resolution inputs.
*   **Resource Efficiency:** The computational cost of end-to-end models is significantly higher. While `DocLayout-YOLO` offers real-time inference, models like `Nougat` and `GOT` require substantial GPU resources, making them less suitable for latency-sensitive applications without optimization.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies, Failure Cases, and Robustness

The survey synthesizes findings from ablation studies in the cited works to identify critical design choices.

*   **Ablation on Visual Vocabularies:** Studies on `Fox` and `Vary` demonstrate that integrating specialized visual vocabularies (e.g., SAM-style ViT alongside CLIP-ViT) is crucial for fine-grained tasks. Removing these components leads to a significant drop in OCR accuracy, confirming that general-purpose vision encoders are insufficient for document parsing.
*   **Failure Cases in Modular Systems:** The most common failure mode identified is **error propagation**. If the layout analysis module fails to detect a table boundary (a frequent issue with borderless tables), the downstream table recognition module receives incorrect crops, leading to catastrophic structural errors. Rule-based reading order systems also fail frequently on multi-column layouts with irregular nesting, a weakness that end-to-end models mitigate through learned attention.
*   **Failure Cases in VLMs:** End-to-end models struggle with **hallucination** in low-confidence regions, often inventing text or formula structures where none exist. They also exhibit **position sensitivity**, where slight shifts in the input image can lead to different token predictions, a robustness issue less prevalent in detection-based modular systems.
*   **Robustness to Noise:** Modular systems with dedicated denoising pre-processing steps often outperform VLMs on heavily degraded historical documents. VLMs, relying on end-to-end learning, require massive amounts of augmented training data to achieve similar robustness, as seen in the success of **Text-DIAE**'s degradation-aware pre-training.

### 5.5 Trade-offs and Future Directions

The experimental analysis concludes with a clear trade-off matrix:
*   **Choose Modular Pipelines** when:
    *   **Precision** on specific elements (e.g., perfect table cell alignment) is paramount.
    *   **Computational resources** are limited, requiring real-time inference on edge devices.
    *   **Interpretability** is needed, as each module's output can be inspected and corrected.
*   **Choose End-to-End VLMs** when:
    *   **Semantic coherence** and reading order across complex layouts are critical.
    *   **Multi-page context** is required for tasks like summarization or cross-referencing.
    *   **Development speed** is a priority, as training a single unified model is simpler than orchestrating and tuning multiple specialized modules.

The survey suggests that future experiments must focus on **hybrid architectures** (like `OmniParser`) that combine the precision of modular detection with the semantic reasoning of large language models, and on **standardized benchmarks** like `OmniDocBench` that evaluate the entire pipeline rather than isolated components.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the survey outlines significant advancements in both modular pipelines and end-to-end Vision-Language Models (VLMs), it critically identifies that neither paradigm currently offers a universal solution. The field remains constrained by fundamental architectural assumptions, data biases, and computational bottlenecks that limit real-world applicability. This section dissects these limitations, grounding the analysis in the specific challenges highlighted in Sections 9 and 10 of the paper.

### 6.1 Architectural Assumptions and Structural Fragility

Both parsing paradigms rely on assumptions about document structure that frequently break down when encountering "in-the-wild" complexity.

#### The Modular Pipeline: The Assumption of Decomposability
Modular systems operate on the critical assumption that document parsing can be cleanly decomposed into independent sub-tasks (Layout $\rightarrow$ OCR $\rightarrow$ Table Recognition) without losing essential context.
*   **Error Propagation:** The survey explicitly flags **error propagation** as the fatal flaw of this assumption. As noted in Section 9, if the Layout Analysis module fails to correctly segment a complex multi-column region or misses a borderless table, the downstream OCR or Table Recognition modules receive corrupted input. There is no mechanism for the recognition module to "correct" the layout module; the error is irreversible.
*   **Rule-Based Rigidity:** These systems assume that **reading order** and **relation integration** can be solved via rule-based heuristics or simple spatial sorting. However, the paper highlights that this fails catastrophically on documents with **irregular reading orders**, such as nested sidebars, floating captions, or multi-page tables where the logical flow does not match the visual top-to-bottom sequence. The reliance on page-by-page processing further assumes that documents have no cross-page dependencies, an assumption that renders modular systems incapable of reconstructing tables or arguments that span multiple pages.

#### The End-to-End VLM: The Assumption of Implicit Learning
End-to-end models assume that a unified Transformer architecture can implicitly learn spatial relationships, reading order, and fine-grained character recognition solely through next-token prediction.
*   **The "Frozen Parameter" Bottleneck:** A critical weakness identified in Section 9 is that many large visual models (LVMs) **freeze the parameters of the Large Language Model (LLM)** during training to preserve linguistic capabilities. The paper argues this restricts the model's **OCR capabilities**, particularly for high-density text. The frozen LLM backbone is not optimized for the pixel-to-token alignment required for dense character recognition, leading to **repeated outputs**, skipped lines, or formatting errors in long documents.
*   **Resolution vs. Context Trade-off:** VLMs assume that visual encoders can capture fine-grained text details while maintaining a global view of the page. In reality, standard vision encoders (e.g., ViT) struggle with **high-resolution inputs**. To fit an A4-sized document into the model's context window, images are often resized, causing small text (footnotes, subscripts in formulas) to become illegible. While techniques like sliding windows (used in `Ureader` and `TextMonkey`) mitigate this, they introduce computational overhead and can fragment global context.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

Despite the breadth of datasets consolidated in the Appendix, the survey reveals significant gaps in the types of documents and content currently handled effectively.

#### The "Standard Document" Bias
The field exhibits a strong bias toward **structured, clean document types**, primarily academic papers (arXiv, PubMed) and standard business forms.
*   **Underexplored Domains:** Section 9 explicitly states that complex, unstructured formats remain **underexplored**. These include:
    *   **Instruction Manuals:** Often containing dense diagrams interwoven with text in non-linear layouts.
    *   **Posters and Newspapers:** Characterized by highly irregular column structures, overlapping text boxes, and artistic fonts that defy standard layout detectors.
    *   **Historical Records:** Documents with degraded quality, handwritten annotations, and archaic typography are often excluded from major benchmarks, limiting the generalizability of current models.
*   **Non-Latin Scripts:** While progress has been made, the paper notes that many state-of-the-art models (including `Nougat`) still struggle with **non-Latin scripts**. The dominance of English-centric datasets means that models often fail to correctly parse the structural nuances of languages with different writing directions or character complexities.

#### Specific Content Blind Spots
Certain content types remain persistent failure cases for both paradigms:
*   **Borderless and Nested Tables:** As detailed in Section 9, detecting tables without visible grid lines or those with deeply nested structures (tables within tables) is a major unsolved challenge. Modular systems lack the visual cues to segment them, while VLMs often hallucinate row/column boundaries.
*   **Inline Mathematical Expressions:** Distinguishing inline formulas from regular text (especially when fonts are similar) remains difficult. The survey notes that **structural extraction** for printed expressions needs refinement, and robustness against noise in screen-captured expressions is lacking.
*   **Diagram and Flowchart Parsing:** Section 9 identifies diagram parsing as an **emerging field lacking unified definitions**. Current methods are often semi-automated or tailored to specific diagram types (e.g., only flowcharts or only organizational charts). There is no standardized framework for extracting the logical relationships (edges and nodes) from complex diagrams like mind maps or circuit schematics.

### 6.3 Computational and Data Constraints

The transition to large-scale models has introduced severe resource constraints that limit scalability and accessibility.

#### The Computational Cost of End-to-End Models
*   **Training and Inference Overhead:** The survey emphasizes that training and deploying LVMs is **resource-intensive**. Models like `GOT` and `Fox` require massive GPU clusters for training and significant VRAM for inference. This makes them impractical for latency-sensitive applications or deployment on edge devices, where modular systems (like `DocLayout-YOLO`) still hold a distinct advantage in speed.
*   **Inefficiency with Dense Text:** Section 9 highlights a specific inefficiency: LVMs are computationally wasteful when processing **high-density text**. The self-attention mechanism scales quadratically with sequence length, meaning processing a text-heavy page consumes disproportionate resources compared to a specialized OCR engine that processes text lines in parallel.

#### The Data Scarcity and Quality Paradox
*   **Dependence on Synthetic Data:** The performance leaps in recent years are heavily driven by **synthetic datasets** (e.g., `DocSynth-300K`, `UniMER-1M`). While effective, the paper raises the concern of a **domain gap**: models trained on perfectly rendered synthetic data may fail to generalize to the noise, skew, and degradation found in real-world scanned documents.
*   **Annotation Bottleneck:** High-quality annotation for complex tasks (e.g., linking chart elements to data values, or defining cell merging in tables) is prohibitively expensive. The survey notes that many existing datasets focus on single tasks, ignoring the **complexity of real-world composition**. The lack of comprehensive, multi-task datasets forces researchers to train separate models or rely on imperfect synthetic proxies.
*   **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The paper critiques the current evaluation landscape. For mathematical expressions, standard string metrics (BLEU, Edit Distance) are flawed because multiple LaTeX strings can represent the same visual formula. While **CDM (Character Detection Matching)** is proposed as a solution, the survey admits that **evaluating diagram and chart extraction remains underdeveloped**, with no universally accepted benchmarks for structural correctness in complex diagrams.

### 6.4 Open Questions and Future Weaknesses

The survey concludes by highlighting several open questions that represent critical weaknesses in the current trajectory of the field:

1.  **The Integration Gap:** Can modular and end-to-end approaches be effectively hybridized? While `OmniParser` attempts this, the optimal architecture for combining the **precision** of modular detection with the **semantic reasoning** of VLMs remains an open research question.
2.  **Generalization to "Wild" Layouts:** Will current models ever achieve robust performance on the "long tail" of document types (posters, handwritten notes, mixed-media documents), or will they remain confined to clean, digital-born PDFs? The current reliance on specific dataset distributions suggests a significant generalization gap.
3.  **Scalable Multi-Page Understanding:** While `Fox` and `DocOwl` make strides in multi-page processing, the fundamental limitation of **context window size** in Transformers persists. How to efficiently model dependencies across hundreds of pages without exponential computational cost remains unsolved.
4.  **Standardization of Metrics:** The field lacks a **unified evaluation protocol**. With every sub-task (tables, formulas, charts) using different metrics, comparing overall system performance is difficult. The call for benchmarks like `OmniDocBench` is a step forward, but widespread adoption and the development of metrics for emerging tasks (like diagram reasoning) are still needed.

In summary, while the shift toward end-to-end models offers a promising path to semantic coherence, the field is currently limited by **computational inefficiency**, **data biases toward clean academic papers**, and **architectural struggles with high-density text and complex layouts**. The "perfect" document parser—one that is fast, accurate, universally generalizable, and capable of deep semantic reasoning across arbitrary document types—remains an aspirational goal rather than a realized technology.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This survey does more than catalog existing techniques; it fundamentally reorients the research landscape from a collection of isolated sub-tasks toward a unified, semantic-driven discipline. By exposing the limitations of rigid modular pipelines and validating the potential of end-to-end Vision-Language Models (VLMs), the paper establishes a new roadmap for how machines will interact with human knowledge. The implications extend far beyond academic benchmarks, reshaping the infrastructure of AI systems, defining critical avenues for future inquiry, and unlocking high-value practical applications.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Research Landscape: From "Detection" to "Understanding"

The most profound shift driven by this work is the conceptual move from **Document Parsing as Detection** to **Document Parsing as Understanding**.

*   **Dissolving Silos:** Historically, the field was fragmented into disjoint communities: OCR researchers optimizing character error rates, layout analysts tuning bounding box IoU, and table specialists maximizing tree-edit similarity. This survey demonstrates that these boundaries are artificial. The success of models like `GOT` (General OCR Theory) and `Nougat` proves that treating text, formulas, tables, and charts as a **unified sequence generation problem** yields superior results. Future research will no longer focus on improving a single module in isolation but on optimizing the **joint representation** of multimodal document features.
*   **The Decline of Rule-Based Integration:** The era of hand-crafted heuristics for merging OCR output with layout coordinates is ending. The survey highlights that rule-based integration is the primary bottleneck for handling complex, real-world layouts (e.g., multi-column academic papers with floating figures). The landscape is shifting toward **learned integration**, where the model implicitly understands reading order and structural hierarchy through attention mechanisms, rendering explicit spatial sorting algorithms obsolete for many use cases.
*   **Data-Centric Paradigm:** The survey underscores that architectural innovations alone are insufficient without corresponding advances in data. The rise of massive synthetic datasets (e.g., `DocSynth-300K`, `UniMER-1M`) signals a shift where **data engineering**—specifically the programmatic generation of diverse, noisy, and structurally complex documents—becomes the primary driver of performance gains, surpassing the impact of minor model architecture tweaks.

### 7.2 Critical Avenues for Follow-Up Research

Based on the gaps identified in Sections 9 and 10, the survey outlines a clear agenda for the next generation of document parsing research.

#### 1. Hybrid Architectures: The Best of Both Worlds
While end-to-end models offer semantic coherence, they currently lack the pixel-level precision of modular systems for dense text. A critical follow-up direction is the development of **Hybrid Architectures**.
*   **Research Question:** How can we integrate the precise localization capabilities of detectors (like `DocLayout-YOLO`) with the semantic reasoning of LLMs?
*   **Proposed Direction:** Future models may employ a "detect-then-refine" strategy where a lightweight detector identifies regions of interest (ROIs) to guide a VLM's attention, or where a VLM generates a draft structure that is subsequently corrected by a specialized verification module. The `OmniParser` model mentioned in the survey is an early prototype of this, decoupling OCR from structural sequencing to balance speed and accuracy.

#### 2. True Multi-Page and Long-Context Understanding
Current VLMs struggle with documents exceeding their context window or requiring synthesis across dozens of pages.
*   **Research Question:** How can models maintain global consistency and logical flow across hundreds of pages without exponential computational costs?
*   **Proposed Direction:** Research must move beyond simple sliding windows. Promising avenues include **hierarchical memory mechanisms** that summarize previous pages into compact tokens, or **retrieval-augmented decoding** where the model dynamically retrieves relevant context from earlier pages during generation. This is essential for parsing legal contracts or entire books where a definition on page 5 governs a clause on page 50.

#### 3. Robustness to "In-the-Wild" Degradation
The survey notes a heavy bias toward clean, digital-born PDFs (e.g., arXiv papers). Real-world documents are often scanned, skewed, stained, or handwritten.
*   **Research Question:** Can models generalize to historical archives, handwritten forms, and low-quality scans without task-specific fine-tuning?
*   **Proposed Direction:** Future work must prioritize **degradation-invariant pre-training**. Techniques like those in `Text-DIAE` (masking, blurring, noise addition) need to be scaled up. Additionally, research into **few-shot adaptation** will be crucial, allowing models to learn the specific noise characteristics of a new document domain (e.g., 19th-century newspapers) from only a handful of examples.

#### 4. Unified Evaluation Frameworks
The fragmentation of metrics (BLEU for text, TEDS for tables, CDM for formulas) hinders holistic progress.
*   **Research Question:** How do we evaluate the *end-to-end* utility of a parsed document for downstream tasks?
*   **Proposed Direction:** The community must adopt comprehensive benchmarks like **`OmniDocBench`** that evaluate the entire pipeline simultaneously. Furthermore, new metrics should be developed that measure **downstream task performance** (e.g., "How accurately can an LLM answer questions based on the parsed output?") rather than just surface-level structural similarity.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The advancements surveyed here directly enable a new class of AI applications that were previously unreliable or impossible.

#### 1. High-Fidelity Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)
This is the most immediate and impactful application. Current RAG systems often fail because they chunk text without understanding its structure, leading to context loss (e.g., separating a table header from its data).
*   **Impact:** By converting documents into structured Markdown or JSON with preserved hierarchy, parsers like `Nougat` and `MinerU` allow RAG systems to retrieve **semantically coherent chunks**. An LLM can now reason over a complete table or a specific formula in its proper context, drastically reducing hallucinations in domains like law, medicine, and finance.

#### 2. Automated Knowledge Base Construction
Organizations possess vast repositories of unstructured PDFs (technical manuals, research papers, invoices).
*   **Impact:** Advanced parsing enables the automatic extraction of entities and relationships into **knowledge graphs**. For instance, a system could ingest thousands of chemistry papers, extract molecular structures and reaction conditions (using `GOT`'s universal OCR), and populate a searchable database for drug discovery, a task previously requiring manual curation.

#### 3. Accessibility and Digital Preservation
Millions of historical documents and government records remain inaccessible as image-only scans.
*   **Impact:** Robust parsing tools can convert these archives into **searchable, screen-reader-friendly formats**. The ability to handle complex layouts and handwritten text (via models trained on `UniMER-1M` or similar) means that even non-standard historical records can be digitized with high fidelity, preserving cultural heritage and improving public access.

#### 4. Intelligent Document Processing (IDP) for Enterprise
In finance and logistics, processing invoices, receipts, and contracts is a major bottleneck.
*   **Impact:** End-to-end models can handle **zero-shot adaptation** to new document templates. Unlike traditional IDP systems that require retraining for every new invoice format, a VLM-based parser can understand the logical structure of a从未-before-seen document type, extracting key fields (dates, amounts, line items) with minimal human intervention.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

For practitioners and researchers looking to implement these findings, the survey provides clear guidance on method selection based on specific constraints.

#### When to Prefer Modular Pipelines
*   **Scenario:** You require **maximum precision** on specific elements (e.g., perfect cell alignment in financial tables) or are operating under **strict latency/resource constraints** (e.g., edge devices, real-time processing).
*   **Recommended Stack:** Use **`DocLayout-YOLO`** for layout analysis, **`PaddleOCR`** or **`Tesseract`** for text recognition, and a specialized table recognizer (e.g., **`TableMaster`**).
*   **Integration Tip:** Invest heavily in the **Relation Integration** layer. Since errors will propagate, your post-processing logic (sorting by coordinates, merging split lines) must be robust. Use this approach when you need interpretability and the ability to debug specific failure modes (e.g., "The table detector failed, but the OCR is fine").

#### When to Prefer End-to-End VLMs
*   **Scenario:** You are dealing with **complex, heterogeneous documents** (mixing text, formulas, and charts), require **multi-page context**, or need to generate **semantic outputs** (like Markdown for RAG) rather than just raw coordinates.
*   **Recommended Stack:** Deploy **`Nougat`** for scientific papers, **`GOT`** for universal content (including formulas and diagrams), or **`Fox`** for multi-page documents.
*   **Integration Tip:** Be prepared for higher computational costs. Optimize inference by using **quantization** or **distilled versions** of these models if available. Crucially, validate the output on **non-Latin scripts** and **high-density text** blocks, as these remain weak points. Use this approach when development speed and semantic coherence are prioritized over pixel-perfect localization.

#### Reproducibility Checklist
To ensure reproducibility and fair comparison in future work, the survey implicitly mandates:
1.  **Dataset Selection:** Do not rely solely on clean, synthetic data. Validate models on **`OmniDocBench`** or **`PubTabNet`** (for tables) and **`UniMER-1M`** (for formulas) to test robustness.
2.  **Metric Alignment:** Abandon sole reliance on BLEU or Accuracy. Use **TEDS** for tables, **CDM** for formulas, and **IoU/mAP** for layout. Report these metrics alongside qualitative examples of failure cases (e.g., merged cells, inline formulas).
3.  **Open Source Tools:** Leverage the ecosystem of tools listed in Table 1 (e.g., **`MinerU`**, **`PDF-Extract-Kit`**) as baselines. These tools encapsulate the state-of-the-art modular pipelines and provide a strong foundation for building hybrid systems.

In conclusion, this survey marks a inflection point where document parsing transitions from a niche computer vision task to a core component of the AI infrastructure. By embracing unified models, rigorous evaluation, and data-centric strategies, the field is poised to unlock the full value of the world's unstructured knowledge.
