## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the critical lack of predictive scaling methodologies for Reinforcement Learning (RL) in Large Language Models (LLMs) by presenting the first large-scale systematic study spanning over 400,000 GPU-hours. The authors define a sigmoidal compute-performance framework to extrapolate results from smaller runs and introduce `ScaleRL`, a best-practice recipe that successfully scales to 100,000 GPU-hours on an 8B dense model and 50,000 GPU-hours on a 17Bx16 MoE (Scout) while maintaining predictive accuracy. This work establishes a scientific framework for analyzing RL scaling, enabling researchers to evaluate algorithmic improvements and predict asymptotic performance without incurring the full cost of large-scale experimentation.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Scaling Gap in RL for LLMs

This paper addresses a fundamental methodological gap: **while reinforcement learning (RL) compute for large language models has exploded, our understanding of how to scale RL has not kept pace**. The authors characterize the current state as "more art than science" — practitioners lack predictive tools to determine which RL methods will perform well at large compute budgets without actually running expensive large-scale experiments.

To understand why this matters, consider the compute landscape. The paper notes that Deepseek-R1-Zero devoted 100,000 H800 GPU-hours to RL training alone, representing 3.75% of its total pre-training compute. Frontier model generations show even more dramatic scaling: OpenAI increased RL compute by more than 10× from o1 to o3, and xAI made a similar leap from Grok-3 to Grok-4. Yet despite this massive investment, there is no principled framework for answering two critical questions:

1. **How do we scale?** — Which algorithmic choices remain stable and effective as compute increases?
2. **What do we scale?** — Should we invest in larger models, longer contexts, bigger batches, or more training steps?

Without answers to these questions, research progress becomes tied to large-scale experimentation that "sidelines most of the academic community" — only well-resourced labs can afford to test whether their ideas scale, creating a barrier to entry and slowing collective progress.

### Why Pre-Training Scaling Laws Don't Directly Apply

The machine learning community has successfully developed **scaling laws for pre-training** (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022), which allow researchers to predict model performance at larger scales from smaller experiments. These laws typically use power-law relationships between compute and loss. However, the paper argues that RL post-training presents different challenges that make direct application of pre-training scaling methodologies inappropriate:

- **Bounded metrics**: RL performance is measured in pass rates or rewards bounded between 0 and 1, whereas pre-training loss is unbounded. The paper found that sigmoidal curves fit bounded metrics more robustly than power laws (see Appendix A.4 for detailed comparison).
- **Limited fitting data**: RL training spans far fewer evaluation points than pre-training (e.g., ~7,400 steps vs. hundreds of thousands). Discarding early training points — as power-law fits typically require — leaves insufficient data for reliable fitting.
- **Instability at scale**: Many RL algorithms that work at small scales destabilize at larger compute budgets due to issues like generation truncation or entropy collapse. Pre-training recipes are comparatively stable across scales.

The paper demonstrates empirically that power-law fits on RL data can produce wildly incorrect asymptotic predictions. For their 100,000 GPU-hour run, a power-law fit predicted an asymptotic performance of $A = 1.0$ (perfect accuracy), while the actual curve saturated near $A = 0.65$. The sigmoidal fit correctly predicted $A = 0.645$ from early training data.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

Before this work, the RL-for-LLMs landscape consisted primarily of **isolated algorithmic proposals** and **model-specific training reports**, each with significant limitations for understanding scaling:

| Approach | Examples | Limitation for Scaling Analysis |
|----------|----------|--------------------------------|
| Novel algorithm proposals | DAPO (Yu et al., 2025), GSPO (Zheng et al., 2025a), VAPO (Yue et al., 2025) | Focus on outperforming baselines at fixed compute, not on how performance evolves with increasing compute |
| Model-specific training reports | DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025), MiniMax-M1 (MiniMax et al., 2025), Magistral (Rastogi et al., 2025) | Provide ad-hoc solutions tailored to specific contexts without generalizable scaling principles |
| Comparative empirical studies | ProRL (Liu et al., 2025a), Liu et al. (2025c) | Focus on downstream evaluation metrics or specific heuristics for stability, not on predictable scaling behavior |

The paper identifies a critical flaw in how prior work evaluates RL methods: **comparisons are typically done on downstream benchmarks** (e.g., AIME, MATH-500), which introduce noise and confounding factors that obscure true scaling behavior. In contrast, this work follows pre-training practice by measuring performance on **in-distribution held-out validation data**, which provides cleaner signals for fitting scaling curves.

Two particularly relevant prior works warrant explicit comparison:

- **ProRL (Liu et al., 2025a)** demonstrated that prolonged RL fine-tuning (~16,000 GPU-hours) on a 1.5B model could uncover novel solution strategies. However, ProRL focused on specific stability heuristics (KL-regularization, policy resetting) rather than developing a general framework for predicting how methods scale.

- **Liu et al. (2025c)** ablated design choices on Qwen-3 4B/8B models and proposed LitePPO as a minimalist alternative to GRPO and DAPO. While this yielded valuable algorithmic insights, the focus remained on comparative empirical findings at fixed compute scales, not on scaling trajectories.

Neither of these works studied the "scaling properties" of their methods — that is, how performance evolves as a function of compute, and whether early training dynamics can predict late-training performance.

### This Paper's Positioning

The paper positions itself as **the first systematic study of RL scaling methodology**, borrowing the scientific framework from pre-training scaling laws while adapting it to RL's unique characteristics. Key differentiators include:

1. **Predictive framework**: The authors propose a sigmoidal compute-performance curve (Equation 1) with three interpretable parameters: asymptotic performance ($A$), compute efficiency ($B$), and midpoint ($C_{\text{mid}}$). This allows extrapolation from small-scale runs to predict large-scale performance.

2. **Scale of empirical study**: The work spans over 400,000 GPU-hours across multiple model sizes (8B dense, 17B×16 MoE), with individual ablation runs consuming up to 16,000 GPU-hours each. This is 6× larger than ProRL's compute budget.

3. **Focus on in-distribution validation**: Rather than relying solely on downstream benchmarks, the paper fits scaling curves on held-out validation data from the training distribution, following pre-training practice. This provides cleaner signals for predictability analysis.

4. **Recipe consolidation**: Instead of proposing novel algorithms, ScaleRL integrates existing methods (PipelineRL, CISPO loss, FP32 precision, zero-variance filtering, etc.) validated through leave-one-out ablations. The contribution is methodological — establishing which combinations scale predictably — rather than algorithmic innovation.

5. **Generalization across scaling axes**: The paper demonstrates that the predictive framework holds not just for increasing training steps, but also for scaling model size, context length, batch size, and multi-task training (math + code).

By establishing this framework, the paper aims to bring RL training "closer to the predictability long achieved in pre-training" — enabling researchers to evaluate RL candidates at small scale with confidence about their large-scale behavior, thereby democratizing access to RL research and accelerating methodological progress.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a **methodological framework paper** that establishes how to predictably scale reinforcement learning compute for large language models by fitting sigmoidal compute-performance curves and consolidating best practices into a unified recipe called `ScaleRL`. The core idea is that RL training follows predictable saturation trajectories that can be extrapolated from early training data, allowing researchers to evaluate algorithmic scalability without running experiments to their computational limits.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a **predictive scaling framework for RL training** that treats compute budget as the primary independent variable and validation pass rate as the dependent variable, enabling extrapolation from small-scale runs to predict large-scale performance. This solves the problem that RL practitioners currently cannot determine which algorithmic choices will remain stable and effective at 100,000+ GPU-hours without actually spending that compute, by instead fitting mathematical curves to early training dynamics that reliably forecast asymptotic behavior.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The technical architecture consists of four interconnected components that form a complete RL scaling pipeline. First, the **Data and Validation Infrastructure** holds out 1,000 prompts from the Polaris-53K training dataset to serve as an in-distribution validation set, with performance measured every 100 training steps using 16 generations per prompt. Second, the **Generator–Trainer Split** allocates GPUs into two roles: 64 generator GPUs produce reasoning traces using optimized inference kernels, while 16 trainer GPUs run the training backend with FSDP and update model parameters, with parameters periodically broadcast back to generators. Third, the **RL Algorithm Stack** implements the policy optimization objective with specific choices for loss type, advantage normalization, loss aggregation, and off-policy handling that collectively determine scaling behavior. Fourth, the **Scaling Curve Fitting Module** takes validation pass rates at different compute checkpoints and fits the sigmoidal function from Equation (1) to extract parameters $A$, $B$, and $C_{\text{mid}}$, which then enable extrapolation to higher compute budgets. Information flows sequentially: prompts enter the generator, rollouts are produced and rewarded, the trainer computes gradients using the RL objective, parameters update and sync back to generators, validation metrics are recorded at intervals, and finally the curve-fitting module processes these metrics to produce scaling predictions.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the **scaling framework mathematics** because Equation (1) defines the fundamental relationship between compute and performance that all subsequent design choices are evaluated against.
- We then explain the **training infrastructure and compute accounting** because understanding how GPU-hours are measured and allocated is prerequisite to interpreting all scaling claims.
- We proceed to the **base RL algorithm formulation** because ScaleRL builds upon standard GRPO/DAPO-style objectives, and readers must understand the baseline before seeing what changes.
- We detail each **ScaleRL recipe component** individually because the paper's contribution lies in how these specific choices interact to produce stable, predictable scaling.
- We conclude with the **curve-fitting procedure and validation methodology** because this explains how the authors verify that their framework actually enables prediction rather than just post-hoc description.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### The Sigmoidal Compute-Performance Framework

The paper proposes that RL training performance follows a sigmoidal relationship with compute budget, which captures the intuition that performance grows slowly at very low compute, accelerates through a mid-range of efficient scaling, and then saturates at high compute as it approaches a finite ceiling. This relationship is formalized in Equation (1), which states that the reward gain at compute $C$ equals the asymptotic reward gain multiplied by a compute efficiency term:

$$R_C - R_0 = (A - R_0) \times \frac{1}{1 + (C_{\text{mid}}/C)^B}$$

In this equation, $R_C$ represents the pass rate achieved at compute budget $C$ (measured in GPU-hours), $R_0$ is the initial pass rate before RL training begins, $A$ denotes the asymptotic pass rate that the method approaches at infinite compute (bounded between 0 and 1), $B$ is a positive scaling exponent that determines how steeply the curve rises (larger values indicate greater compute efficiency), and $C_{\text{mid}}$ sets the compute midpoint where half of the total gain $(A - R_0)$ is achieved. The term $\frac{1}{1 + (C_{\text{mid}}/C)^B}$ acts as a saturation function that transitions from near-zero at low compute to near-one at high compute. Figure 3 provides a schematic interpretation showing that $C_{\text{mid}}$ determines where on the compute axis the curve begins its steep ascent, $B$ controls the steepness of that ascent, and $A$ sets the horizontal asymptote that the curve approaches. The authors chose sigmoidal fitting over power-law fitting because pre-training scaling laws typically use power laws of the form $R_C = A - D/C^B$, but these are unbounded at low compute and require discarding early training points, which leaves insufficient data for RL where total training spans far fewer evaluation points. Appendix A.4 demonstrates empirically that power-law fits on their 100,000 GPU-hour run predicted an asymptotic performance of $A = 1.0$ (perfect accuracy), which is clearly incorrect since the actual curve saturated near $A = 0.65$, whereas the sigmoidal fit correctly predicted $A = 0.645$ from early training data. At high compute regimes where $C \gg C_{\text{mid}}$, the sigmoidal curve approximates a power-law form through the expansion $R_C \approx A - (A - R_0)C_{\text{mid}}^B / C^B$, showing that the two formulations converge when sufficient compute has been expended.

#### Training Infrastructure and Compute Accounting

All experiments operate on a generator–trainer split across Nvidia GB200 GPUs, where a subset of GPUs designated as generators use optimized inference kernels for high-throughput rollout generation while the remaining GPUs designated as trainers run the training backend with FSDP and update parameters. For the 80 GPU experiments that form the basis of most ablations, 64 GPUs serve as generators responsible for producing reasoning traces, and 16 GPUs act as trainers that receive generated trajectories, perform policy updates, and periodically broadcast updated parameters back to the generators. The paper measures compute in GPU-hours, where a single run at 8B model scale consumes between 3,500–4,000 GPU-hours for establishing design choices in Section 3.2, 16,000 GPU-hours for leave-one-out experiments in Section 4, and 30,000–100,000 GPU-hours for the largest scale runs in Section 5. Training uses the Polaris-53K dataset for math RL experiments with a batch size of 768 completions per gradient update step, constructed from 48 prompts with 16 generations sampled per prompt. The sequence length is fixed at 16,384 tokens unless otherwise noted, allocated as 12,288 tokens for the thinking trace, 2,048 tokens for the final solution, and an additional 2,048 tokens for the input prompt. The authors adopt the 12,288 thinking budget for faster iteration during ablations but demonstrate in Section 5 that ScaleRL extrapolations remain predictive when training with larger thinking budgets up to 32,768 tokens. Rewards are assigned as +1 for correct traces and -1 for incorrect traces, with correctness determined by automated checkers like Sympy or Math-Verify that assess the final answer after stripping out the thinking trace enclosed in `` and `&lt;/think>` special tokens. For validation, the authors hold out 1,000 randomly selected prompts from the Polaris-53K dataset and measure average pass rate every 100 training steps with 16 generations per prompt on these held-out prompts, following pre-training practice of measuring predictive performance on in-distribution validation data rather than downstream benchmarks. The scaling curve fits begin after approximately 1,500 GPU-hours because excluding the very early low-compute regime yields more stable fits, as the initial ~1 epoch of training exhibits rapid, almost linear reward increase that deviates from the sigmoidal trend due to transient instabilities.

#### Base RL Algorithm Formulation

The starting point for all experiments is a base algorithm resembling GRPO without any KL regularization term, consistent with large-scale training reports from Magistral and MiniMax-M1 that found KL regularization unnecessary at scale. For a given prompt $x$, the old policy $\pi_{\text{gen}}(\theta_{\text{old}})$ on the generator GPUs generates $G$ candidate completions $\{y_i\}_{i=1}^G$, each assigned a scalar reward $r_i$. Advantages are computed as $\hat{A}_i = r_i - \text{mean}(\{r_j\}_{j=1}^G)$, which centers each reward by the mean reward of all completions for that prompt, and group-normalized advantages are computed as $\hat{A}^G_i = \hat{A}_i / (\text{std}(\{r_j\}_{j=1}^G) + \epsilon)$, which scales by the standard deviation of rewards within the group to normalize the advantage magnitude. Each completion $y_i$ of length $|y_i|$ contributes at the token level through importance sampling ratios defined as $\rho_{i,t}(\theta) = \pi_{\theta}^{\text{train}}(y_{i,t} \mid x, y_{i,&lt;t}) / \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}^{\text{gen}}(y_{i,t} \mid x, y_{i,&lt;t})$, which measure how much more or less likely the current training policy is to generate token $t$ compared to the old generation policy. The base algorithm includes asymmetric DAPO clipping with distinct thresholds for upward and downward deviations, defined as $\text{clip}_{\text{asym}}(\rho, \epsilon^-, \epsilon^+) = \text{clip}(\rho, 1 - \epsilon^-, 1 + \epsilon^+)$, where $\epsilon^-$ and $\epsilon^+$ are hyperparameters that control how much the importance ratio can deviate from 1 before clipping occurs. The surrogate objective aggregates losses at the sample level by averaging per-sample token losses before averaging across samples, expressed as:

$$J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}^{\text{gen}}(\cdot \mid x)} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^G \frac{1}{|y_i|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \min\left( \rho_{i,t}(\theta) \hat{A}^G_i, \text{clip}_{\text{asym}}(\rho_{i,t}(\theta), \epsilon^-, \epsilon^+) \hat{A}^G_i \right) \right]$$

This formulation preserves token-level policy ratios as in PPO while using sequence-level, group-normalized advantages to stabilize learning under sparse rewards. The asymmetric clipping is included because of its widespread adoption as a default approach to avoid entropy collapse and maintain output diversity, as documented in the DAPO paper. Generation lengths are controlled through forced interruptions that append an end-of-thinking phrase such as "Okay, time is up. Let me stop thinking and formulate a final answer now.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the paper's most novel contributions, distinguishing between **fundamental methodological innovations** (which change how we think about RL scaling) and **empirical consolidations** (which validate specific design choices at unprecedented scale). The paper's primary value lies not in proposing new algorithms, but in establishing a scientific framework for evaluating *any* RL algorithm's scalability.

### 4.1 Predictive Scaling Framework for RL (Fundamental Innovation)

**What is new:** This is the **first work to propose a predictive compute-performance framework specifically for RL post-training**, adapting the scientific methodology of pre-training scaling laws to RL's unique characteristics. Prior work (ProRL, Liu et al. 2025c) evaluated RL methods at fixed compute budgets or on downstream benchmarks, but offered no way to extrapolate from small-scale experiments to predict large-scale behavior.

**Why it matters:** The sigmoidal curve framework (Equation 1, Section 3.4) enables researchers to answer a previously unanswerable question: *"Will this RL method still work at 100,000 GPU-hours, or will it destabilize?"* The paper demonstrates this predictive capability empirically in Figure 1, where scaling curves fitted on only the first 8,000–16,000 GPU-hours accurately extrapolate to 100,000 GPU-hours. This reduces the cost of evaluating new RL methods by approximately 6× — instead of running every candidate to its computational limit, researchers can fit curves from early training dynamics.

**Key distinction from prior work:** Pre-training scaling laws use power-law fits ($R_C = A - D/C^B$), but the paper shows in Appendix A.4 that power laws fail catastrophically for RL, predicting $A = 1.0$ (perfect accuracy) when the actual asymptote is $A = 0.65$. The sigmoidal formulation is not merely a curve-fitting preference — it reflects the **bounded nature of RL metrics** (pass rates cannot exceed 1.0) and the **saturation behavior** observed when models exhaust the learnable signal in the training distribution. This is a conceptual shift: RL scaling is fundamentally about approaching a finite ceiling, not unbounded improvement.

### 4.2 Three Empirical Principles of RL Scaling (Fundamental Innovation)

The paper's 400,000 GPU-hour ablation study yields three principles that reshape how practitioners should think about RL algorithm design. These are not incremental observations — they contradict common assumptions in the RL-for-LLMs community.

**Principle 1: RL Performance Ceilings Are Not Universal**

Different RL recipes converge to different asymptotic performance levels ($A$), even when trained on identical data with identical compute budgets. Figure 2 shows ScaleRL achieving $A = 0.61$ while DeepSeek (GRPO), Qwen-2.5 (DAPO), and Magistral plateau at lower asymptotes. This contradicts the implicit assumption in many prior works that all stable RL methods should eventually converge to similar performance given enough compute.

**Significance:** This finding implies that **algorithm choice fundamentally constrains what is achievable**, not just how quickly you get there. Practitioners cannot assume that running a suboptimal recipe longer will eventually match a better recipe — the ceiling itself is algorithm-dependent.

**Principle 2: Small-Scale Winners May Be Large-Scale Losers (The Bitter Lesson)**

Figure 2 and Figure 10a demonstrate that methods appearing superior at low compute budgets can be overtaken as compute increases. Smaller batch sizes show early advantages but stagnate downstream; certain loss functions perform well initially but hit lower asymptotes. The paper explicitly warns: *"Methods that appear superior at small compute budgets can be worse when extrapolated to large-compute regimes."*

**Significance:** This invalidates the common practice of selecting RL algorithms based on small-scale ablations (e.g., 1,000–2,000 GPU-hours). The paper's framework allows researchers to estimate scaling parameters ($A$, $B$) from early training dynamics, identifying scalable candidates before committing massive compute. This is a **selection methodology innovation** — you can now screen RL methods for scalability without running them to completion.

**Principle 3: Most Common Interventions Affect Efficiency, Not Ceiling**

Perhaps the most surprising finding: interventions widely believed to improve peak performance — loss aggregation, advantage normalization, data curriculum, length penalties — primarily modulate compute efficiency ($B$) without materially shifting the asymptotic ceiling ($A$). Table 1 and Figure 7 show that leave-one-out variants of ScaleRL reach similar asymptotes ($A$ within ±0.02), with differences appearing mainly in the efficiency parameter $B$.

**Exception:** Only a few design choices shift the asymptote: **loss type** (CISPO/GSPO vs. DAPO, Figure 5a), **numerical precision** (FP32 at logits, Figure 5b), and **off-policy algorithm** (PipelineRL vs. PPO-off-policy, Figure 4a). These are the "asymptote-shifters" — the decisions that fundamentally constrain what is achievable.

**Significance:** This creates a **hierarchy of design decisions** for practitioners. If your goal is maximum performance at scale, prioritize asymptote-shifters first (loss type, precision, off-policy setup), then optimize efficiency parameters (batch size, normalization, curriculum). Many prior works treated all design choices as equally important; this paper shows they are not.

### 4.3 ScaleRL Recipe Validation at 100,000 GPU-Hours (Empirical Consolidation)

**What is new:** While ScaleRL itself combines existing methods (PipelineRL, CISPO, FP32 precision, zero-variance filtering, etc.), the **validation at 100,000 GPU-hours with predictive curve fitting** is unprecedented. Prior large-scale RL training reports (DeepSeek-R1, MiniMax-M1, Magistral) documented their recipes but did not systematically validate scalability through extrapolation from smaller runs.

**Why it matters:** Figure 1 demonstrates that ScaleRL's performance closely matches the scaling curve predicted from early training data, confirming both **stability at extreme compute** and **predictive accuracy of the framework**. The 8B dense model run reached 7,400 steps (3.5× larger than ProRL), and the 17B×16 MoE (Scout) run reached 7,100 steps, both maintaining predictable trajectories. This is not just a recipe — it is **proof that predictable RL scaling is achievable**.

**Key validation methodology:** The leave-one-out (LOO) ablations in Figure 7 validate that each ScaleRL component contributes positively even when combined. Most LOO variants reach similar asymptotes but differ in efficiency, confirming Principle 3 above. The paper also ran LOO experiments on the Scout MoE model (Figure 8b), showing that design choices like FP32 precision remain critical at larger model scales even when they appear redundant at 8B.

**Distinction from incremental improvement:** If this were merely an incremental recipe paper, the contribution would be "we combined existing methods and got better results." Instead, the contribution is methodological: **we established a framework for validating scalability, then used it to prove this recipe scales predictably**. The recipe itself may be superseded; the framework for evaluating scalability is the lasting contribution.

### 4.4 Generalization Across Multiple Scaling Axes (Empirical Validation)

**What is new:** The paper demonstrates that the sigmoidal scaling framework holds not just for increasing training steps, but across **four distinct scaling dimensions**: model size (8B dense → 17B×16 MoE), context length (14k → 32k tokens), batch size (512 → 2048 prompts), and task mixture (math-only → math+code). Figure 9, Figure 10, and Figure 11 show clean, predictive fits across all axes.

**Why it matters:** This establishes that the framework is **not specific to one training configuration** — it is a general methodology for analyzing RL scaling. For example, Figure 9 shows that longer context lengths (32k tokens) have lower initial efficiency (lower $B$, higher $C_{\text{mid}}$) but ultimately achieve higher asymptotes ($A$), validating long-context RL as a "ceiling-raising knob rather than a mere efficiency trade-off." Similarly, Figure 10 shows that larger batches avoid the downstream stagnation observed in smaller-batch runs, even when in-distribution validation continues improving.

**Practical guidance:** Table 1 provides fitted parameters for all large-scale runs, enabling practitioners to compare efficiency trade-offs. For instance, ScaleRL-32k achieves $A = 0.645$ vs. ScaleRL's $A = 0.610$, but with $B = 1.89$ vs. $B = 1.92$ — slightly less efficient but higher ceiling. This quantifies decisions that were previously qualitative.

### 4.5 Summary: Incremental vs. Fundamental Contributions

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| Sigmoidal scaling framework (Equation 1) | **Fundamental** | First predictive methodology for RL scaling; enables 6× compute savings in algorithm evaluation |
| Three scaling principles (ceiling non-universality, small-scale deception, efficiency vs. asymptote) | **Fundamental** | Reshapes how practitioners prioritize design decisions; contradicts common assumptions |
| ScaleRL recipe | **Empirical Consolidation** | Validates existing methods at 100k GPU-hours; recipe may be superseded but validation methodology is lasting |
| Multi-axis generalization | **Empirical Validation** | Proves framework applicability beyond training steps; provides quantitative trade-off data |
| Leave-one-out validation at scale | **Methodological** | Establishes rigorous procedure for isolating component contributions in RL recipes |

The paper's enduring contribution is the **framework and methodology**, not the specific ScaleRL recipe. Future work may improve upon ScaleRL's components, but the ability to predict scalability from early training dynamics — and the empirical principles governing what shifts asymptotes versus efficiency — represents a fundamental advance in how RL for LLMs is studied and developed.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the experimental methodology, quantitative results, and validity of claims made in the paper. The authors conducted what they describe as the **first large-scale systematic study of RL scaling**, spanning over 400,000 GPU-hours across multiple model architectures and training configurations. We analyze whether this empirical foundation convincingly supports their three core claims: (1) RL scaling follows predictable sigmoidal trajectories, (2) different design choices affect asymptotic performance versus efficiency differently, and (3) ScaleRL maintains predictability at extreme compute scales.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Experimental Setup

#### Datasets and Validation Protocol

The paper employs a **two-tier evaluation strategy** that distinguishes between in-distribution validation (for curve fitting) and downstream benchmarks (for generalization assessment):

| Dataset | Purpose | Size | Usage |
|---------|---------|------|-------|
| Polaris-53K (An et al., 2025) | Primary training | 53,000 math prompts | RL training on reasoning tasks |
| Held-out validation | Curve fitting | 1,000 prompts | In-distribution pass rate measurement every 100 steps |
| AIME-24 (AoPS, 2025) | Downstream evaluation | Competition math problems | Generalization assessment (Figure 1b, Figure 18c) |
| Deepcoder (Luo et al., 2025) | Multi-task training | Code generation prompts | Math+code joint training runs (Figure 11) |
| LiveCodeBench | Downstream evaluation | Code benchmarks | Code performance assessment (Figure 18b) |

The validation protocol follows pre-training scaling law practice: **1,000 prompts are randomly held out from Polaris-53K**, and pass rate is measured with 16 generations per prompt at 100-step intervals. This in-distribution validation provides cleaner signals for curve fitting than noisy downstream benchmarks. The paper explicitly states: *"Consistent with pre-training practice (Hoffmann et al., 2022; Porian et al., 2025), we measure predictive performance on in-distribution validation data"* (Section 2.1).

#### Compute Accounting and Infrastructure

All experiments run on **Nvidia GB200 GPUs** with a generator–trainer split architecture:

- **80 GPU configuration**: 64 generator GPUs (optimized inference kernels) + 16 trainer GPUs (FSDP backend)
- **Standard batch**: 768 completions per gradient step (48 prompts × 16 generations)
- **Sequence length**: 16,384 tokens (12,288 thinking + 2,048 solution + 2,048 prompt)
- **Learning rate**: 5 × 10⁻⁷ with AdamW optimizer, 100-step linear warmup
- **Reward signal**: ±1 for correct/incorrect traces, verified by Sympy or Math-Verify

The paper reports compute budgets across three experimental tiers:

1. **Initial ablations (Section 3.2)**: 3,500–4,000 GPU-hours per run — sufficient to identify destabilizing choices before they fail at larger scale
2. **Leave-one-out experiments (Section 4)**: 16,000 GPU-hours per run — enables reliable curve fitting with extrapolation validation
3. **Large-scale validation (Section 5)**: 30,000–100,000 GPU-hours — tests predictive framework at extreme compute

For context, the 100,000 GPU-hour run on the 8B model represents **6× the compute budget of ProRL** (Liu et al., 2025a), which trained for ~16,000 GPU-hours. The 8B run reached 7,400 optimization steps, while the Scout MoE run reached 7,100 steps — both **3.5× larger than ProRL's ~2,000 steps**.

#### Baselines and Comparison Methods

The paper compares ScaleRL against four prevalent RL recipes, each representing a different combination of design choices from recent large-scale training reports:

| Recipe | Loss Type | Off-Policy Algorithm | Precision | Loss Aggregation | Source |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|-----------|------------------|--------|
| DeepSeek (GRPO) | GRPO | PPO-off-policy-8 | Default | Sample average | Guo et al. (2025) |
| Qwen-2.5 (DAPO) | DAPO | PPO-off-policy-8 | Default | Prompt average | Yu et al. (2025) |
| Magistral | DAPO | PipelineRL-8 | Default | Prompt average | Rastogi et al. (2025) |
| MiniMax-M1 | CISPO | PPO-off-policy-8 | FP32 | Prompt average | MiniMax et al. (2025) |
| **ScaleRL** | **CISPO** | **PipelineRL-8** | **FP32** | **Prompt average** | **This work** |

Appendix A.16 provides detailed descriptions of each recipe. Notably, the authors gave some baselines advantages: DAPO and MiniMax-M1 runs used batch size 1,280 (vs. ScaleRL's 768) to compensate for zero-variance prompt dropping, ensuring fair comparison on effective batch size.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

#### Sigmoidal Fit Parameters Across Methods

The core quantitative contribution is the extraction of three parameters from Equation (1) for each method. Figure 2 presents the fitted curves comparing all recipes:

> *"ScaleRL surpasses all other methods, achieving an asymptotic reward of A = 0.61"* (Figure 2 caption)

The key parameters for ScaleRL on the 8B model are:
- **Asymptotic performance**: $A = 0.61$ (pass rate at infinite compute)
- **Compute efficiency**: $B = 1.92$ (scaling exponent)
- **Midpoint**: $C_{\text{mid}} = 2,542$ GPU-hours (where half the gain is achieved)

| ScaleRL (8B) | 2,542 | 1.92 | 0.610 |
| ScaleRL-32k (long context) | 11,272 | 1.89 | 0.645 |

| ScaleRL-math+code (code curve) | 1,675 | 1.09 | 0.615 |

Several observations emerge from these numbers:

1. **Model scaling raises the ceiling**: The Scout MoE achieves $A = 0.710$ vs. 8B's $A = 0.610$ — a **10 percentage point improvement** in asymptotic performance. Moreover, Scout reaches this with only 1/6 of the RL training compute required by the 8B model (Figure 1 caption).

2. **Longer context trades efficiency for ceiling**: ScaleRL-32k has lower efficiency ($B = 1.89$ vs. $1.92$) and higher midpoint ($C_{\text{mid}} = 11,272$ vs. $2,542$), meaning slower early progress. However, it achieves higher asymptote ($A = 0.645$ vs. $0.610$), validating long-context RL as a *"ceiling-raising knob rather than a mere efficiency trade-off"* (Section 5).

3. **Batch size affects both efficiency and ceiling**: Larger batches (2,048 prompts) show slower initial training ($C_{\text{mid}} = 10,909$) but reach higher asymptotes ($A = 0.645$ vs. $0.610$ for batch 768). Figure 10a demonstrates this pattern: *"smaller-batch runs show early stagnation on downstream benchmarks even as in-distribution validation performance continues to improve."*

#### Component-Level Ablation Results

The leave-one-out (LOO) experiments in Figure 7 isolate the contribution of each ScaleRL component. The paper reports efficiency parameters ($B$) for each variant:

| Component Removed | $B$ (efficiency) | Impact |
|-------------------|------------------|--------|
| ScaleRL (full) | 2.01 | Baseline |
| LOO-DAPO (revert to DAPO loss) | 1.77 | −12% efficiency |
| LOO-FP32 (no precision fix) | ~1.95 | Minor at 8B, critical at MoE |
| LOO-8op (PPO-off-policy instead of PipelineRL) | ~1.85 | Significant efficiency loss |
| LOO-savg (sample average loss) | ~1.90 | Moderate efficiency loss |
| LOO-batch (no zero-variance filtering) | ~1.95 | Minor efficiency loss |
| LOO-lp (length penalty vs. interruption) | ~1.95 | Minor efficiency loss |

The key insight from Figure 7 is that **most LOO variants reach similar asymptotic rewards** (within ±0.02 error margin), with differences appearing primarily in efficiency ($B$). The authors transform Equation (1) into power-law form $F(R_C) = C^B$ to highlight these efficiency differences visually, where:

$$F(R_C) = C_{\text{mid}}^B / \left(\frac{A - R_0}{R_C - R_0} - 1\right)$$

This transformation makes the slope $B$ directly visible in log-log plots, revealing that ScaleRL achieves the highest compute efficiency even when asymptotes are similar.

#### Critical Component Impact: Loss Type and Precision

Two components stand out as **asymptote-shifters** rather than mere efficiency modifiers:

**Loss Type (Figure 5a)**: CISPO and GSPO substantially outperform DAPO on asymptotic performance. The paper states: *"both GSPO and CISPO substantially outperform DAPO, improving the asymptotic pass-rate A by a large margin."* While exact numbers are not provided in the main text, Figure 5a shows CISPO exhibiting *"prolonged near-linear reward increase"* and marginally outperforming GSPO later in training.

**FP32 Precision (Figure 5b)**: This fix produces the most dramatic single-component improvement: *"the precision fix dramatically improves the asymptotic performance A from 0.52 to 0.61"* — a **9 percentage point gain**. This is particularly significant because the FP32 fix appears less critical in LOO experiments at 8B scale (Figure 7), but proves essential on the Scout MoE (Figure 8b). The paper explains: *"while the FP32 precision fix makes little difference with dense 8B trained with ScaleRL (Figure 7), it provides large gains in GRPO/DAPO-style losses by mitigating numerical instabilities."*

#### Predictive Accuracy at 100,000 GPU-Hours

The most critical validation of the framework appears in Figure 1, where scaling curves fitted on early training data extrapolate to match actual performance at extreme compute:

> *"We fit a sigmoid curve (Equation (1)) on pass rate (mean@16) on iid validation dataset up to 50k (and 16k) GPU hours and extrapolate to 100k (and 45k) on the 8B (Scout MoE) models respectively. The extrapolated curve (× markers) closely follows extended training, demonstrating both stability at large compute and predictive fits"* (Figure 1 caption)

For the 8B model:
- **Fitting range**: 1,500–50,000 GPU-hours
- **Extrapolation target**: 100,000 GPU-hours
- **Result**: Extrapolated points align with actual training trajectory

For the Scout MoE:
- **Fitting range**: 1,500–16,000 GPU-hours  
- **Extrapolation target**: 45,000 GPU-hours
- **Result**: Extrapolated points align with actual training trajectory

This demonstrates **2× extrapolation capability** — predicting performance at double the compute used for fitting. The paper notes this enables *"evaluating scalability of RL methods without incurring the compute cost of running every experiment to its computational limit."*

#### Error Margins and Variance Estimation

To establish statistical confidence in their fits, the authors ran **three independent ScaleRL runs** (Figure 8a) to estimate variance in fitted parameters:

> *"We observe a ±0.02 error margin for asymptotic performance A"* (Figure 8a caption)

This error margin becomes the threshold for determining whether differences between methods are statistically meaningful. The paper states: *"if two methods achieve similar A values (within 0.02), the one with higher B when a refit is done with the average of A values is at least as good in terms of efficient scalability"* (Appendix A.5).

This variance estimation is crucial for interpreting the LOO results: since most LOO variants achieve $A$ within ±0.02 of full ScaleRL, the primary differentiator becomes efficiency ($B$) rather than asymptotic performance.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Design Choice Analysis

The paper conducts systematic ablations across **seven design axes**, each validated at meaningful compute scales. We summarize the key findings:

#### Off-Policy Algorithm (Figure 4)

**Comparison**: PPO-off-policy-k vs. PipelineRL-k

**Result**: PipelineRL achieves similar asymptotic performance ($A$) but **substantially improves compute efficiency ($B$)**. The paper explains: *"PipelineRL reduces the amount of idle time in the training process"* by operating in streaming fashion rather than alternating generation/training phases.

**Optimal setting**: $k = 8$ (maximum off-policyness) proved optimal (Figure 4b), balancing freshness of rollouts against training throughput.

**Why it matters**: This is one of the few design choices that affects both efficiency and stability. Appendix A.11 notes: *"this distinction affects the asymptotic performance of the scaling curve, not just the efficiency exponent... Very few axes shift the asymptote in this way, making the choice of off-policy algorithm one of the most consequential design decisions."*

#### Loss Aggregation (Appendix A.9, Figure 14a)

**Comparison**: Sample average (GRPO) vs. Prompt average (DAPO) vs. Token average

**Result**: Prompt-level aggregation achieves the **highest asymptotic performance**. The paper adopts this for ScaleRL.

**Mechanism**: Prompt averaging ensures each prompt contributes equally to the loss, regardless of how many tokens its completions contain. This prevents long completions from dominating the gradient signal.

#### Advantage Normalization (Appendix A.9, Figure 14b)

**Comparison**: Prompt-level (GRPO) vs. Batch-level vs. No normalization (Dr. GRPO)

**Result**: All three methods yield **similar performance** at 8B scale. The paper adopts batch-level normalization as *"theoretically sound and marginally better."*

**Caveat**: This choice is corroborated at larger scale by LOO experiments, suggesting batch-level normalization provides stability benefits that compound with other design choices.

#### Zero-Variance Filtering (Figure 6a)

**Comparison**: Include all prompts vs. Exclude prompts with identical rewards across generations

**Result**: Filtering zero-variance prompts achieves **higher asymptotic performance**. These prompts contribute zero policy gradient but consume compute, so excluding them improves the effective batch.

**Distinction**: This differs from DAPO's dynamic sampling, which resamples prompts to fill the batch. Zero-variance filtering simply drops them, reducing effective batch size but improving gradient quality.

#### Adaptive Prompt Filtering (Figure 6b)

**Comparison**: Uniform resampling vs. No-Positive-Resampling (exclude prompts with pass rate ≥0.9)

**Result**: The curriculum improves **both scalability and asymptotic reward ($A$)**. The paper observes: *"once a prompt becomes too easy for a policy, it typically remains easy"* and no longer contributes useful gradient signal.

**Implementation**: Maintain history of pass rates and permanently remove solved prompts from subsequent epochs.

#### Generation Length Control (Section 2, Appendix A.10)

**Comparison**: Forced interruptions vs. Length penalties

**Result**: Interruptions prove more stable than length penalties at scale. The paper uses the end-of-thinking phrase: *"Okay, time is up. Let me stop thinking and formulate a final answer now.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper establishes a rigorous framework for predicting RL scaling behavior, several important limitations and trade-offs warrant careful consideration. Understanding these constraints is essential for practitioners who wish to apply these findings to their own settings.

### 6.1 Domain and Task Distribution Limitations

**Primary focus on math reasoning:** The overwhelming majority of experiments — including all ablation studies and the 100,000 GPU-hour validation run — use the **Polaris-53K math dataset**. While the paper includes preliminary multi-task results (math + code) in Figure 11 and Figure 18, the authors explicitly acknowledge this limitation:

> *"While our experiments focus mainly on the math domain, we also evaluate ScaleRL under multi-task RL training... While our preliminary results are promising, it would be interesting to thoroughly study predictability of compute scaling for multi-task RL with different training data mixtures."* (Section 7)

**Implication for practitioners:** The scaling parameters ($A$, $B$, $C_{\text{mid}}$) reported in Table 1 may not transfer directly to other domains such as code generation, scientific reasoning, or agentic tasks. The math domain has verifiable rewards (correct/incorrect answers), which simplifies reward signal quality. Domains with noisier or learned reward models may exhibit different scaling trajectories.

**Downstream generalization gap:** The paper's predictive framework is built on **in-distribution validation performance**, not downstream benchmark generalization. The authors are transparent about this:

> *"While we report transfer to downstream evaluations, our primary focus is on studying predictive scaling, which is characterized through in-distribution performance curves on a held-out dataset from training prompts... This still leaves the question of how well the LLM would generalize from the training distribution to held out test sets."* (Section 7)

The paper does observe correlation between in-distribution validation and downstream performance (Figure 1b, Figure 9, Figure 10b), but acknowledges that **some algorithmic choices affect generalization more than others**: larger batch sizes, reduced truncations, longer generation lengths, and larger model scale (Section 7). This means a method that scales predictably on validation data may not generalize proportionally to unseen benchmarks.

### 6.2 Compute and Resource Constraints

**Minimum compute threshold for reliable fitting:** The scaling framework requires excluding the early low-compute regime to achieve stable fits. The paper states:

> *"Unless noted otherwise, all our scaling fits begin after ~1.5k GPU hours."* (Section 2.1)

This threshold corresponds to approximately one epoch of training for their setup. Appendix A.7 explains that including earlier points introduces noise from transient instabilities. **Practitioners cannot predict scaling behavior from very small runs** (e.g., 500 GPU-hours) — they must invest at least 1,500+ GPU-hours before curves become reliable.

**Absolute compute requirements remain prohibitive:** While the framework enables 6× compute savings in algorithm evaluation (by extrapolating from 16,000 to 100,000 GPU-hours), the ScaleRL recipe itself still requires **massive compute budgets** that are inaccessible to most researchers. The 100,000 GPU-hour run used 80 Nvidia GB200 GPUs — infrastructure available only to well-resourced labs.

**Error margins limit fine-grained comparisons:** The paper estimates a **±0.02 error margin on asymptotic performance $A$** based on three independent ScaleRL runs (Figure 8a, Appendix A.5). This means:

- Differences in $A$ smaller than 0.02 between methods may not be statistically meaningful
- When two methods achieve similar $A$ (within 0.02), efficiency ($B$) becomes the primary differentiator
- Small improvements from individual design choices may fall within noise

This error margin is reasonable given RL's inherent variance (Agarwal et al., 2021), but it limits the precision of claims about marginal improvements.

### 6.3 Model Scale and Architecture Assumptions

**Limited model scale coverage:** The paper validates ScaleRL on two model sizes: **8B dense** and **17B×16 MoE (Scout)**. While this demonstrates cross-scale predictability, it does not establish whether the framework holds for:

- Much smaller models (&lt;1B parameters)
- Much larger models (>100B parameters)
- Different architecture families beyond Llama-based models

The authors note that FP32 precision appears redundant at 8B scale in LOO experiments (Figure 7) but proves critical on Scout MoE (Figure 8b). This suggests **some design choices have scale-dependent importance** that cannot be predicted from small-scale ablations alone.

**Truncation instability as a scaling constraint:** Training instabilities correlate strongly with generation truncation rates. Appendix A.15 reports:

> *"At batch size 768, we observed that truncations in the range of 10–15% typically destabilized training, with performance degrading and not recovering without intervention."*

ScaleRL maintains truncations below 5% for over 90% of training on the 8B model, but this requires careful design choices (interruptions, CISPO loss, FP32 precision). **Methods that allow truncation rates to climb will fail to scale predictably**, regardless of their early performance. This creates a hidden constraint: scaling predictability depends on maintaining generation stability, which may require domain-specific tuning.

### 6.4 Algorithmic and Hyperparameter Sensitivity

**Loss type sensitivity varies by method:** The paper demonstrates that **DAPO/GRPO-style losses are highly sensitive to clipping hyperparameters**, while CISPO and GSPO are more robust:

> *"For example, in Appendix A.17.2, varying $\epsilon_{\text{max}}$ for CISPO between {4, 5, 8} produced no significant differences in performance... In contrast, DAPO/GRPO-style objectives are highly sensitive to the exact choice of clipping threshold."* (Appendix A.17)

Figure 19a shows that changing $\epsilon_{\text{max}}$ for DAPO **fundamentally changes the asymptotic performance value $A$**, not just convergence speed. This means:

- DAPO requires careful hyperparameter tuning to reach its potential asymptote
- CISPO's robustness makes it preferable for large-scale training where tuning is expensive
- Reported comparisons assume reasonably tuned hyperparameters for each method

**Generations per prompt is a second-order choice:** At moderate batch sizes, the allocation between prompts and generations per prompt has minimal impact on scaling parameters:

> *"Sweeping generations per prompt 8, 16, 24, 32 and adjusting prompts to keep total batch fixed leaves fitted scaling curves essentially unchanged (Appendix A.13), suggesting that, at moderate batch, this allocation is a second-order choice for both $A$ and $B$."* (Section 5)

However, the paper notes that **clearer differences may emerge at much larger batches (e.g., 2,000+)**, which were not explored. This leaves uncertainty about optimal allocation strategies at extreme scales.

### 6.5 Unexplored Scaling Axes

The paper explicitly identifies several scaling dimensions that remain unstudied:

| Unexplored Axis | Why It Matters | Paper's Statement |
|-----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Pre-training compute interaction | RL performance may depend on base model quality | *"A natural next step is to derive predictive 'scaling laws' for RL across pre-training compute, model size, and RL training data."* (Section 7) |
| Structured/dense rewards | Process rewards vs. outcome rewards may scale differently | *"Future studies can also include other axes of RL compute scaling, such as incorporating structured or dense rewards"* (Section 7) |
| Generative verifiers | Compute-intensive verifiers change reward signal quality | *"and more compute-intensive generative verifiers... to find optimal compute allocation for RL training"* (Section 7) |
| Multi-turn RL | Agentic interactions introduce new dynamics | *"the methodological framework introduced here can be applied to study the scaling behavior of other post-training regimes, including multi-turn RL, agentic interaction"* (Section 7) |
| Different data mixtures | Task composition may affect scaling trajectories | *"it would be interesting to thoroughly study predictability of compute scaling for multi-task RL with different training data mixtures"* (Section 7) |

**Entropy is not a reliable predictor:** The paper finds that entropy trajectories do not reliably predict downstream performance:

> *"In Section A.12, we plot entropy for ScaleRL runs with batch sizes 768 and 2048. Despite the 2048-batch size run achieving much stronger downstream performance at every stage, both runs followed nearly identical entropy trajectories per step."* (Appendix A.12)

This contradicts some recent work proposing entropy as a proxy for exploration quality (Cui et al., 2025). Practitioners should not rely on entropy monitoring as a substitute for validation-based scaling analysis.

### 6.6 Recipe Longevity and Generalizability

**ScaleRL is not claimed as final:** The authors are explicit that their recipe will likely be superseded:

> *"There are of course many design choices in RL, so we don't think that our ScaleRL recipe is the end of the story."* (Section 7)

The contribution is the **framework for evaluating scalability**, not the specific recipe. Future work may discover better loss functions, off-policy algorithms, or precision fixes that shift asymptotes further.

**Component interactions may change at new scales:** The LOO experiments show that some components appear redundant at 8B scale but matter at MoE scale (e.g., FP32 precision). This creates uncertainty: **which ScaleRL components will remain important at 100B+ models?** The paper cannot answer this without running those experiments.

**Baseline comparisons have inherent advantages:** The authors acknowledge giving some baselines advantages to ensure fair comparison:

> *"Therefore, if at all, we gave some advantage to the DAPO recipe."* (Appendix A.16, regarding batch size compensation for zero-variance filtering)

This is methodologically sound, but means reported gaps between ScaleRL and baselines may be conservative estimates.

### 6.7 Summary of Key Trade-offs

| Trade-off | Evidence | Practical Implication |
|-----------|----------|----------------------|
| Validation vs. generalization | Section 7 | Predictable in-distribution scaling does not guarantee proportional downstream gains |
| Efficiency vs. asymptote | Figure 9, Table 1 | Longer contexts trade early efficiency ($B$) for higher ceiling ($A$) |
| Small-scale vs. large-scale validity | Figure 2, Figure 10a | Methods winning at 1k GPU-hours may lose at 100k GPU-hours |
| Component redundancy | Figure 7 vs. Figure 8b | Some choices appear optional at 8B but critical at MoE scale |
| Compute investment vs. prediction reliability | Appendix A.7 | Must invest ~1.5k GPU-hours before curves become reliable |
| Hyperparameter sensitivity | Appendix A.17 | DAPO requires careful tuning; CISPO is more robust |

The paper's enduring contribution is establishing **how to ask scaling questions rigorously**, not providing definitive answers for all settings. Practitioners should apply the framework to their own domains while remaining aware of these limitations.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than propose a better RL recipe — it establishes a **scientific methodology for studying RL scaling** that fundamentally changes how the field should evaluate algorithmic progress. This section examines how the work reshapes the research landscape, what new questions it enables, and how practitioners should apply these findings.

### 7.1 How This Work Changes the Field

#### From Art to Science: A Methodological Shift

Prior to this work, RL for LLMs operated without predictive scaling tools. Practitioners selected algorithms based on **small-scale performance** or **downstream benchmark results**, neither of which reliably indicated large-scale behavior. Figure 2 demonstrates the problem: methods that appear competitive at 4,000 GPU-hours diverge significantly by 50,000 GPU-hours.

The paper introduces a **screening methodology** that changes this dynamic. Researchers can now:

1. Run candidate algorithms for ~1,500–8,000 GPU-hours (the minimum reliable fitting range per Appendix A.7)
2. Fit sigmoidal curves to extract parameters $A$, $B$, and $C_{\text{mid}}$
3. Extrapolate to predict performance at 50,000–100,000 GPU-hours
4. Commit large-scale compute only to methods with favorable scaling parameters

This reduces the cost of algorithm evaluation by approximately **6×** — instead of running every candidate to 100,000 GPU-hours, researchers can eliminate poor scalers early. For academic labs without access to massive compute, this democratizes participation in RL research by enabling meaningful contributions through small-scale scaling analysis rather than brute-force experimentation.

#### Hierarchy of Design Decisions

The paper's three empirical principles (Section 4.2) establish a **decision hierarchy** that should guide future algorithm development:

| Priority | Design Axis | Impact | Examples from Paper |
|----------|-------------|--------|---------------------|
| **1st** | Asymptote-shifters | Change $A$ (ceiling) | Loss type (CISPO vs. DAPO), FP32 precision, off-policy algorithm |
| **2nd** | Efficiency modifiers | Change $B$, $C_{\text{mid}}$ | Loss aggregation, advantage normalization, batch size |
| **3rd** | Stability enablers | Prevent divergence | Truncation control, zero-variance filtering |

Prior work treated all design choices as equally important. This paper shows they are not: **optimizing efficiency parameters before securing a high asymptote is premature**. For example, tuning batch size (efficiency) matters less than choosing CISPO over DAPO (asymptote). This reframes how future RL papers should present contributions — claiming "improved efficiency" is meaningful only if the asymptotic ceiling is already competitive.

#### Evaluation Protocol Standardization

The paper advocates for **in-distribution validation** over downstream benchmarks for scaling studies. This aligns RL methodology with pre-training scaling law practice (Hoffmann et al., 2022; Kaplan et al., 2020). The rationale is clear: downstream benchmarks introduce noise from distribution shift that obscures true scaling behavior.

Future RL scaling papers should adopt this protocol:
- Hold out 1,000+ prompts from training distribution
- Measure pass rate at regular intervals (e.g., every 100 steps)
- Fit scaling curves on validation data, not downstream benchmarks
- Report downstream results separately as generalization assessment

This standardization enables **cross-paper comparison** of scaling parameters, which was previously impossible when each work used different benchmarks and evaluation protocols.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several unstudied scaling axes in Section 7. We expand on these with concrete research questions:

#### Pre-Training Compute Interaction

**Open question:** How does base model pre-training compute affect RL scaling trajectories?

The paper studies RL scaling with fixed base models (8B dense, 17B×16 MoE). However, pre-training quality likely influences the asymptotic ceiling $A$ achievable through RL. A well-pretrained model may have higher $A$ but lower marginal gains from RL, while a weaker base model might show steeper RL improvement but lower final performance.

**Suggested methodology:** Apply the sigmoidal framework across multiple base model sizes trained with varying pre-training compute. Fit separate curves for each and analyze how $A$ and $B$ correlate with pre-training FLOPs. This would produce **joint scaling laws** relating pre-training compute, model size, and RL compute to final performance.

#### Structured and Dense Rewards

**Open question:** Do process rewards (step-level verification) scale differently than outcome rewards (final answer correctness)?

All experiments in this paper use **outcome rewards** (±1 for correct/incorrect). Recent work on process rewards (Setlur et al., 2024; Lightman et al., 2023) suggests denser feedback signals may improve sample efficiency. However, process rewards require compute-intensive verifiers, changing the compute allocation trade-off.

**Suggested methodology:** Compare scaling curves for outcome-only vs. process+outcome rewards, accounting for verifier compute in total budget. Key metrics: does process reward shift $A$ upward, or merely improve $B$? At what compute scale does verifier overhead become worthwhile?

#### Multi-Task and Multi-Domain Scaling

**Open question:** How do scaling trajectories change with heterogeneous task mixtures?

The paper's multi-task results (Figure 11) show clean parallel scaling curves for math and code within a joint training run. However, this used a fixed 50/50 mixture. Different task proportions, or adding domains with different difficulty distributions (e.g., science reasoning, tool use), may produce non-trivial interactions.

**Suggested methodology:** Sweep task mixture ratios while fitting separate scaling curves per domain. Analyze whether $A$ for each domain depends on the presence/weight of other domains. This would inform **optimal data mixing strategies** for generalist RL training.

#### Extreme Model Scales

**Open question:** Do scaling parameters remain stable at 100B+ parameter models?

The paper validates ScaleRL at 8B and 17B×16 MoE (~100B active parameters). However, some design choices show **scale-dependent importance**: FP32 precision appears optional at 8B but critical at MoE scale (Figure 7 vs. Figure 8b). Other components may exhibit similar thresholds.

**Suggested methodology:** Run LOO ablations at progressively larger scales (e.g., 70B dense, 405B MoE) to identify which ScaleRL components remain essential. This would produce a **scale-aware recipe** where component selection depends on model size.

#### Agentic and Multi-Turn RL

**Open question:** Does the sigmoidal framework apply to multi-turn interaction settings?

All experiments use single-turn reasoning (prompt → thinking trace → answer). Agentic settings involve multi-turn trajectories with intermediate actions, tool calls, and environment feedback. These introduce additional variance sources and credit assignment challenges.

**Suggested methodology:** Apply the scaling framework to multi-turn RL environments (e.g., web navigation, code execution with feedback). Test whether sigmoidal fits remain predictive or whether multi-turn settings require different functional forms.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

#### Algorithm Screening Before Large-Scale Deployment

**When to use:** Before committing 50,000+ GPU-hours to an RL training run.

**Procedure:**
1. Run candidate algorithm for 4,000–8,000 GPU-hours
2. Fit sigmoidal curve per Equation (1), excluding first 1,500 GPU-hours
3. Compare fitted $A$ against ScaleRL's $A = 0.61$ (8B baseline from Table 1)
4. If $A$ is within ±0.02 of ScaleRL, compare efficiency $B$
5. Proceed to large-scale training only if $A$ is competitive

**Expected benefit:** Avoids wasting compute on methods that appear promising early but hit low asymptotes. The paper's Figure 2 shows this would have identified DAPO's lower ceiling before large-scale investment.

#### Resource Allocation Decisions

**When to use:** When deciding how to allocate a fixed compute budget across scaling axes.

The paper quantifies trade-offs that were previously qualitative:

| Decision | Trade-off | Guidance from Paper |
|----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Context length | Efficiency vs. ceiling | Use 32k if maximum $A$ is priority; 14k if faster iteration needed (Figure 9, Table 1) |
| Batch size | Early progress vs. downstream generalization | Use larger batches (2,048+) to avoid downstream stagnation (Figure 10, Section 5) |
| Model size | RL compute vs. base capability | Larger models reach higher $A$ with less RL compute (Scout achieves $A=0.71$ with 1/6 the RL budget) |
| Generations per prompt | Second-order at moderate batch | Fix total batch; allocation between prompts/generations has minimal impact (Section 5) |

**Example:** A lab with 50,000 GPU-hours budget must choose between training an 8B model for more steps or a 17B MoE for fewer steps. Table 1 suggests the MoE reaches higher $A$ ($0.71$ vs. $0.61$) with less RL compute — favor the larger model if base model training cost is already sunk.

#### Stability Monitoring via Truncation Rates

**When to use:** During RL training to detect impending instability.

Appendix A.15 establishes **truncation rate as an early warning signal**:

- **&lt;5% truncation**: Training stable (ScaleRL on 8B maintains this for 90%+ of training)
- **10–15% truncation**: Impending instability; intervention required
- **>15% truncation**: Training likely to diverge

**Actionable guidance:** Monitor truncation rates every 100 steps. If rate exceeds 10%, consider:
- Reducing generation length budget
- Switching from length penalty to forced interruptions
- Increasing batch size to maintain effective batch after truncations

This provides a **diagnostic metric** that is cheaper to compute than full validation passes.

#### When to Use ScaleRL vs. Alternatives

**Prefer ScaleRL when:**
- Training at 8B+ model scale with 10,000+ GPU-hours budget
- Maximum asymptotic performance is the priority
- Training infrastructure supports PipelineRL (streaming generator–trainer setup)
- Domain has verifiable rewards (math, code with unit tests)

**Consider alternatives when:**
- Compute budget is &lt;4,000 GPU-hours (efficiency differences matter less at small scale)
- Training smaller models (&lt;1B) where FP32 precision may be unnecessary
- Domain requires learned reward models rather than verifiable rewards (scaling behavior unstudied)
- Infrastructure cannot support PipelineRL (PPO-off-policy remains viable with lower $B$)

**Minimum viable ScaleRL:** If implementing the full recipe is infeasible, prioritize these components in order:
1. CISPO loss (asymptote-shifter, robust to hyperparameters)
2. FP32 precision at logits (critical at MoE scale)
3. PipelineRL off-policy setup (efficiency gain, stability)
4. Zero-variance filtering (modest asymptote improvement)
5. Prompt-level loss aggregation (theoretically sound, marginal gain)

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

#### Compute Requirements for Meaningful Reproduction

The paper's methodology requires **minimum compute thresholds** for reliable conclusions:

| Study Type | Minimum GPU-Hours | Purpose |
|------------|-------------------|---------|
| Curve fitting | 1,500–4,000 | Exclude early instability; fit reliable sigmoid (Appendix A.7) |
| LOO ablation | 16,000 | Validate component contributions with extrapolation (Section 4) |
| Full validation | 50,000–100,000 | Confirm predictive accuracy at extreme scale (Figure 1) |

Labs without access to 1,500+ GPU-hours cannot reliably apply the scaling framework. However, they can still adopt **ScaleRL's design choices** (CISPO, FP32, PipelineRL) based on the paper's validated results.

#### Code and Infrastructure Availability

The authors release a **minimal code repository for curve fitting** at `www.devvrit.com/scalerl_curve_fitting`. This enables researchers to:
- Fit sigmoidal curves to their own RL training data
- Compare scaling parameters across methods
- Extrapolate from partial training runs

However, the **full ScaleRL training codebase is not released**. Practitioners must implement components individually:
- **PipelineRL**: Reference implementation available from Piche et al. (2025)
- **CISPO loss**: Described in MiniMax et al. (2025); equation provided in Section 4
- **FP32 precision fix**: Requires modifying LM head computation in training framework
- **Zero-variance filtering**: Simple batch preprocessing; logic described in Section 3.2

#### Integration with Existing RL Frameworks

ScaleRL components can be integrated into popular RL frameworks:

| Framework | Integration Effort | Notes |
|-----------|-------------------|-------|
| **TRL (HuggingFace)** | Moderate | Supports GRPO/DAPO; CISPO requires custom loss implementation |
| **DeepSpeed-Chat** | High | PipelineRL requires generator–trainer split architecture |
| **vLLM + custom trainer** | Moderate | vLLM handles generation; custom trainer implements CISPO + FP32 |
| **Ray RLlib** | High | Requires significant architecture changes for async PipelineRL |

**Recommended path:** Start with TRL for loss function experimentation (CISPO vs. DAPO), then migrate to custom generator–trainer setup for PipelineRL benefits.

#### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Based on the paper's findings, practitioners should watch for:

1. **Fitting too early:** Including the first 1,500 GPU-hours in curve fits introduces noise. Always exclude early training per Appendix A.7.

2. **Over-interpreting small $A$ differences:** Differences in asymptotic performance $&lt;0.02$ fall within error margins (Figure 8a). Focus on efficiency $B$ when $A$ is similar.

3. **Ignoring truncation rates:** High truncation correlates with instability (Appendix A.15). Monitor this metric even if validation performance looks good.

4. **Assuming entropy predicts performance:** Entropy trajectories do not reliably indicate downstream gains (Appendix A.12). Do not optimize for entropy maintenance.

5. **Tuning DAPO clipping aggressively:** DAPO's $\epsilon_{\text{max}}$ fundamentally changes $A$ (Figure 19a), requiring extensive tuning. CISPO avoids this sensitivity.

### 7.5 Summary: A Framework for the Next Decade

This paper's enduring contribution is not the ScaleRL recipe itself — which the authors acknowledge "is not the end of the story" (Section 7) — but the **methodological framework for evaluating RL scalability**. Just as pre-training scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020) enabled systematic progress in foundation model training, this work provides the tools to make RL post-training equally predictable and scientific.

The immediate implications are clear:
- Researchers can screen algorithms at small scale before large investment
- Design decisions can be prioritized by their impact on $A$ vs. $B$
- Cross-paper comparison becomes possible through standardized scaling parameters

The long-term vision is broader: a field where **RL algorithm papers report scaling parameters** ($A$, $B$, $C_{\text{mid}}$) alongside benchmark scores, enabling meta-analysis of what drives scalable RL. This would accelerate progress by focusing community effort on asymptote-shifting innovations rather than marginal efficiency tweaks.

For practitioners today, the guidance is actionable: adopt ScaleRL's design choices where feasible, apply the sigmoidal fitting framework to evaluate new methods, and monitor truncation rates as an early instability signal. For researchers, the opportunity is to extend this framework to unstudied domains — agentic RL, multi-turn interaction, dense rewards — establishing scaling science across the full spectrum of RL for LLMs.
