## 1. Executive Summary

To address the scarcity of structurally complex data for Deep Research, this paper introduces `InfoSeek`, an open-source framework that synthesizes over 50K Hierarchical Constraint Satisfaction Problems (HCSPs) by recursively building Research Trees from large-scale web corpora. This data-centric approach enables `InfoSeeker-3B`, a compact 3B parameter model trained via supervised fine-tuning and reinforcement learning, to significantly outperform much larger baselines on complex reasoning tasks. On the `BrowseComp-Plus` benchmark, `InfoSeeker-3B` achieves 16.5% accuracy, surpassing `Qwen3-32B` (3.5%) and `Gemini 2.5 Flash` (15.5%), demonstrating that high-quality synthetic data can bridge the capability gap between small open models and large commercial APIs.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Emerging Gap in Deep Research Capabilities

Large language models have achieved remarkable fluency in generating and interpreting language, but a new frontier is emerging: **Deep Research**. Unlike conventional information seeking where a model retrieves a fact and answers directly, Deep Research requires models to decompose complex tasks into sub-problems, coordinate multi-step reasoning across diverse sources, and synthesize heterogeneous evidence into coherent conclusions. This capability is essential for high-stakes domains like scientific discovery, policy analysis, and technical investigation—areas where problems are open-ended and knowledge landscapes continuously evolve.

The paper identifies a critical bottleneck: **the scarcity of training data that actually reflects this complexity**. While the community recognizes Deep Research as a cornerstone for the next generation of LLMs, existing datasets fundamentally fail to capture the hierarchical, interdependent reasoning these tasks demand.

### Why Existing Benchmarks Fall Short

To understand the gap, we must first distinguish between three progressively complex problem types (illustrated in **Figure 2**):

| Problem Type | Structure | Reasoning Pattern | Example |
|-------------|-----------|-------------------|---------|
| **Constraint Satisfaction Problem (CSP)** | Flat, parallel constraints | Intersection of independent conditions | "Who got a PhD from Princeton, was born in London, and graduated from Cambridge?" |
| **Multi-hop Problem (MHP)** | Sequential chain | Each step depends on the previous output | "Which country's capital was the birthplace of the scientist who solved the Enigma code?" |
| **Hierarchical Constraint Satisfaction Problem (HCSP)** | Tree-structured hierarchy | Multiple layers of interdependent sub-problems | Complex queries requiring both parallel constraints AND sequential dependencies |

The paper formalizes Deep Research questions with verifiable answers as **Hierarchical Constraint Satisfaction Problems (HCSPs)**. In an HCSP, the final answer emerges only through progressively resolving a hierarchy of sub-questions, where each intermediate step itself may be a constraint satisfaction problem. Formally, given a question $x$ with constraints $C_x$ and sub-questions $Y_x$:

$$H(x) = \bigcap_{i=1}^{k} S(c_i) \cap \bigcap_{j=1}^{m} H(y_j)$$

This hierarchical structure is fundamentally different from both flat CSPs (which operate on independent constraints) and multi-hop problems (which follow a single sequential chain).

### The Dataset Landscape: What's Missing?

**Table 1** provides a revealing comparison of existing QA datasets and synthesis approaches:

- **Classical benchmarks** like Natural Questions (300K+ QA pairs) and HotpotQA (100K+ QA pairs) are limited to single-hop or multi-hop reasoning—they lack the structural depth required for Deep Research.
- **Recent synthetic efforts** like WebWalkerQA (14.3K pairs), WebDancer (200 pairs), and WebShaper (500 pairs) either remain small-scale, focus on multi-hop QA rather than HCSPs, or are not publicly released.
- **Critical gap**: None of these datasets explicitly support hierarchical constraint satisfaction with controllable complexity and full trajectory recording.

The paper notes that while some recent studies explore harder question types involving web pages or Wikipedia, "neither their datasets nor their workflows are publicly released." This creates a barrier for the open-source community to advance Deep Research capabilities.

### Why This Problem Matters

The importance of addressing this gap operates on two levels:

**Theoretical Significance:** Without proper formalization and datasets, the community cannot systematically study what makes Deep Research difficult. The HCSP framework provides a principled distinction from simpler problem types, enabling researchers to measure progress on genuinely hierarchical reasoning rather than conflating it with multi-hop QA.

**Real-World Impact:** The paper demonstrates that this is not merely an academic concern. On the `BrowseComp-Plus` benchmark (**Table 4**), models trained on appropriate Deep Research data (`InfoSeeker-3B` at 16.5% accuracy) can surpass much larger models (`Qwen3-32B` at 3.5%) and compete with commercial APIs (`Gemini 2.5 Flash` at 15.5%, `Gemini 2.5 Pro` at 19.0%). This suggests that **data quality, not just model scale, determines Deep Research capability**.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

The paper positions `InfoSeek` at the intersection of three research directions (**Section 6**):

1. **Inference-Time Agentic Frameworks** (e.g., Agentic Reasoning, AgentOrchestra): These construct scaffolding around pre-trained models without altering weights. `InfoSeek` complements this by providing training data that teaches models to internalize agentic behaviors.

2. **Training Agents for Search and Retrieval** (e.g., Search-R1, AutoRefine): These use reinforcement learning to teach search behaviors. `InfoSeek` provides the foundational dataset that makes such training effective—without appropriate data, RL cannot discover complex reasoning workflows.

3. **Automated Data Synthesis** (e.g., WebShaper, DeepResearcher): These generate training data programmatically. `InfoSeek` distinguishes itself by being **fully open-source**, **scalable** (50K+ samples vs. hundreds in prior work), and explicitly targeting **HCSP structure** rather than simpler multi-hop patterns.

The key differentiator is that `InfoSeek` does not merely generate more data—it generates data with **controlled structural complexity**. Through its Research Tree construction process, the framework can systematically vary the number of reasoning vertices (from 3 to 7+), and **Table 2** confirms that failure rates increase with vertex count (88.1% for 3 vertices to 94.1% for 7+ vertices when tested on Qwen2.5-72B). This controllability is absent from prior synthesis approaches.

### The Core Insight

The paper's central argument is that **Deep Research capabilities cannot emerge from training on shallow data**, regardless of model size or training technique. By formalizing the problem structure (HCSPs), building a synthesis framework that respects that structure (Research Trees), and releasing everything openly, `InfoSeek` addresses both the theoretical gap (what is Deep Research?) and the practical gap (how do we train for it?). This data-centric positioning contrasts with approaches that focus primarily on architectural innovations or inference-time scaffolding without addressing the underlying training data quality.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a data-centric systems paper that introduces a complete pipeline for synthesizing complex reasoning data and training models to exploit it. The core idea is that Deep Research capabilities require training data with explicit hierarchical structure (HCSPs), and the paper delivers both a synthesis framework (`InfoSeek`) and a trained model (`InfoSeeker`) that demonstrate this principle works in practice.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The *system* being built is a two-part pipeline: first, an automated data synthesis engine that constructs complex reasoning questions from web corpora by building tree-structured knowledge graphs; second, a 3B parameter language model trained on this synthetic data to perform multi-step search and reasoning. The problem it solves is that existing training data lacks the hierarchical depth needed for Deep Research, and the solution shape is to programmatically generate questions where answering correctly *requires* traversing multiple layers of interdependent sub-problems rather than relying on memorized facts or simple lookup.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The overall system consists of four major components that operate in sequence. First, the **Research Tree Constructor** (dual-agent system with Planner and Browser) mines entities from Wikipedia and web pages, then recursively builds tree structures where the root is the final answer and internal nodes represent sub-problems. Second, the **Question Generator** converts these tree structures into natural language questions using a powerful LLM (DeepSeek V3 or GPT-4.1), ensuring the question requires traversing the full hierarchy. Third, the **Quality Assurance Filter** validates difficulty (Qwen2.5-32B must fail to answer from memory) and verifiability (Gemini 2.5 Flash must succeed when given ground-truth documents). Fourth, the **InfoSeeker Training Pipeline** takes the curated dataset through supervised fine-tuning with rejection sampling, then reinforcement learning with GRPO, producing the final `InfoSeeker-3B` model that uses parallel multi-query search and a Refiner Agent during inference.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Type of Paper and Core Mechanism**

This is a data synthesis and training methodology paper that argues Deep Research capabilities emerge from training on structurally complex data rather than from model scale alone. The core mechanism is a programmatic pipeline that converts flat text corpora into tree-structured reasoning problems, then trains a compact model to solve those problems through iterative search.

**HCSP Mathematical Formalization**

The paper formalizes Deep Research questions as Hierarchical Constraint Satisfaction Problems, which generalizes both flat constraint satisfaction and sequential multi-hop reasoning. For a standard Constraint Satisfaction Problem with constraints $C_q = \{c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_n\}$, the answer set $A$ is the intersection of entities satisfying each constraint:

$$A = \bigcap_{i=1}^{n} S(c_i) \quad \text{s.t. } |A| = 1, |S(c_i)| \geq 1 \ \forall i$$

where $S(c_i)$ denotes the set of entities satisfying constraint $c_i$. A Multi-hop Problem instead chains reasoning functions sequentially:

$$A = S^{(k)}(c) = \underbrace{S \circ S \circ \cdots \circ S}_{k \text{ times}}(c)$$

where $k$ is the number of reasoning hops. The HCSP formulation combines both patterns hierarchically. Given a question $x$ with constraints $C_x = \{c_1, \ldots, c_k\}$ and sub-questions $Y_x = \{y_1, \ldots, y_m\}$, the hierarchical decomposition $H(\cdot)$ is:

$$H(x) = \bigcap_{i=1}^{k} S(c_i) \cap \bigcap_{j=1}^{m} H(y_j), \quad \text{with } \bigcap \emptyset := U$$

where $U$ denotes the universal set. This recursive definition means each sub-question $y_j$ may itself contain further constraints and sub-questions, creating a tree structure rather than a flat set or linear chain.

**Research Tree Construction from Entities**

The synthesis process begins by defining a research tree $T = (V, E)$ where each vertex $v \in V$ represents a knowledge entity (e.g., "Alan Turing", "University of Cambridge") or a trivial fact (e.g., "1910s", "summer of 1925"), and each edge $(v, w) \in E$ represents a relationship between two vertices. The tree is constructed recursively starting from a base case of a single root vertex $r$ with no edges:

$$T = (\{r\}, \emptyset)$$

Then, recursive expansion introduces a new vertex $w \notin V$ and connects it with exactly one edge to some existing vertex $v \in V$:

$$T = (V \cup \{w\}, E \cup \{(v, w)\})$$

This recursive definition ensures the structure remains a valid tree (connected, acyclic graph) throughout construction. The key insight is that every HCSP admits an underlying tree representation, so constructing the tree first and then converting it to a question guarantees the question has the desired hierarchical structure.

**Dual-Agent System: Planner and Browser**

The tree construction process is orchestrated by two cooperating agents that operate incrementally based on webpages and the Wikipedia dump. The **Planner** maintains a global view of the partially constructed tree, selecting target vertices and specific actions according to global complexity objectives to ensure balanced growth across sequential and parallel reasoning demands. The **Browser** executes the Planner's chosen action by browsing webpages of the selected vertices, extracting candidate hyperlinks (for depth) or atomic claims (for constraints), and validating them for relevance. Each step expansion is recorded with an explicit evidence trace, guaranteeing verifiability of the final question-answer pair.

**Four Action Types for Tree Growth**

The system uses four distinct actions that alternate during tree construction, with each complete iteration beginning with Action 1, followed by alternating Actions 2 and 3, and ending with Action 4.

*Action 1: Initialization from Research Anchors* selects an entity to create the first vertex as the root of the research tree. The Browser samples a valid entity from the Wikipedia knowledge base to serve as the final answer, creates a vertex $r$ as the root to form the initial research tree $T_0$, then performs the first extension by selecting a related entity to create a child vertex $w$ of $r$ along with the connecting edge $(r, w)$. This establishes the foundation for all subsequent expansion.

*Action 2: Blurring Parent with Constraints* expands a specific vertex with additional constraints to avoid the overdetermination issue where a single constraint already yields a unique solution. The Planner identifies a vertex $v$ whose current constraints are insufficient to uniquely derive $v$, then the Browser selects $k$ claims from $v$'s webpage that, taken together, yield the unique and determinate answer $v$. These claims form child vertices $w_1, \ldots, w_k$ along with their corresponding edges. The system ensures that resulting candidate sets are mutually exclusive (without inclusion relations) to prevent shortcut reasoning.

*Action 3: Extending the Tree* increases the depth of the research tree through vertical expansion. Starting from an existing vertex $v$ that represents an entity, the Browser extracts a hyperlink from the corresponding article that indicates a dependency (e.g., "v was discovered by w"), creating a new child node $w$ of $v$ and thereby extending the logical dependency chain. This operation increases the height of the tree and yields a structure that requires the model to perform an additional step of reasoning.

*Action 4: Termination and Question Generation* triggers only when the research tree achieves the desired complexity and all vertices have sufficient constraints. At this point, the Planner constructs the final complete question based on the research tree by providing the blurred vertex descriptions to a powerful LLM such as DeepSeek V3 or GPT-4.1. The model is prompted to generate queries whose solution requires traversing the entire tree, ensuring each synthesized question enforces genuine multi-step reasoning and yields a unique, verifiable answer grounded in factual evidence.

**Converting Tree to Natural Language Question**

Once the research tree is complete, the system converts the structured representation into a natural language question. For a node $v$ of height 1 (all children are leaves), each edge $(v, w_i)$ is converted into a constraint $c_i$, and a question $q_v$ is formed by:

$$q_v = Q(C_v), \quad \text{where } C_v = \{c_1, \ldots, c_n\}$$

with $Q(\cdot)$ denoting the function that combines constraints into a question. For a node $v$ of height $\geq 1$, the system partitions children into leaves $\{w_1, \ldots, w_k\}$ and internal nodes $\{w_{k+1}, \ldots, w_n\}$. Each edge $(v, w_i)$ with $i \leq k$ becomes a constraint $c_i$, while each internal child $w_j$ with $j > k$ recursively yields a sub-question $Q(w_j)$. The resulting question is:

$$q_v = Q(C_v \cup \{Q(w_j) \mid j = k + 1, \ldots, n\})$$

Finally, for a research tree $T$ with root $r$, the corresponding HCSP is obtained as $q = Q(r)$. This recursive conversion ensures the natural language question preserves the hierarchical structure of the underlying tree.

**Quality Assurance Protocol**

The paper institutes a rigorous two-pronged quality assurance protocol focused on Difficulty and Verifiability to ensure the dataset is robust and effective for training. The **Difficulty criterion** ensures problems are non-trivial and cannot be solved by relying on an LLM's parametric memory alone. To validate this, the authors challenged Qwen2.5-32B-Inst to answer the questions directly without search, and the model correctly answered only 2% of the questions. These samples are removed to further enhance difficulty, ensuring the remaining questions genuinely require external information retrieval.

The **Verifiability criterion** confirms that each question is factually grounded and solvable via the generated search trajectory. The system presents Gemini 2.5 Flash API with the ground-truth web pages from the constructed search path, intermixed with a set of distractor documents. The LLM is then tasked with deriving the correct answer from this provided context. Questions with wrong answers, multiple possible answers, or inability to solve are filtered out. This process removes questions that contain ambiguity or are unsolvable, effectively preventing the underdetermined issue where multiple entities could satisfy the constraints.

**Dataset Statistics and Complexity Control**

As shown in **Table 2**, InfoSeek comprises more than 50K samples (specifically 52,138), with a total data curation cost of $571.8. The distribution concentrates on problems requiring 4 to 6 reasoning vertices: 3,841 samples with 3 vertices, 15,263 with 4 vertices, 15,051 with 5 vertices, 17,714 with 6 vertices, and 269 with 7 or more vertices. To quantify the dataset's complexity, the authors measure the failure rate of Qwen2.5-72B using Chain-of-Thought prompting. The results reveal a high overall failure rate of 91.6%, confirming the dataset poses significant challenge even for powerful models. Crucially, the failure rate exhibits a strong positive correlation with the number of vertices, increasing from 88.1% for 3-vertex problems to 94.1% for problems with 7 or more vertices. This trend validates that the synthesis process effectively controls for reasoning complexity through the tree construction mechanism.

**InfoSeeker Inference Workflow**

The trained model uses a novel workflow featuring parallel multi-query search and a dedicated Refiner Agent, designed to efficiently gather and synthesize vast amounts of information as shown in **Figure 3**. Each reasoning turn begins with an explicit "thinking" phase delimited by `` and `</think>` tags, encouraging the model to reflect on what has already been gathered and plan what information remains necessary. This leads to more targeted query generation and robust reasoning compared to approaches without explicit planning.

For search, InfoSeeker generates multiple, diverse queries within a single reasoning step, enclosed within `<search>` and `</search>` tags. These queries are designed to comprehensively address the current information-seeking intent from various angles, broadening informational coverage and accelerating exploration compared to sequential single-query strategies. For each query, the search engine returns the top-k retrieved results, which are then passed to the Refiner Agent.

The **Refiner Agent** extracts salient evidence and produces a concise summary aligned with the query's intent, while also offering recommendations for subsequent reasoning steps. Summaries are paired with their originating queries and encapsulated within `<information>` and `</information>` tags. In practice, the system employs Qwen2.5-7B-Inst as the Refiner Agent, which is both efficient and effective across validations. Once the model determines sufficient information has been accumulated or the maximum number of search steps has been reached, it produces the final answer enclosed within `<answer>` and `</answer>` tags.

**Supervised Fine-Tuning with Rejection Sampling**

A central challenge in developing deep research agents is navigating the vast exploration space of complex, multi-step reasoning tasks. Direct reinforcement learning is unstable and inefficient due to sparse rewards and a combinatorial action space, making it difficult for an agent to independently discover intricate workflows of planning, search, and verification. To address this, the authors employ rejection sampling to construct a dataset of successful and executable reasoning trajectories for supervised fine-tuning.

The system utilizes both a teacher model (Qwen2.5-72B) and a preview version of InfoSeeker to solve tasks from the InfoSeek dataset using the workflow described above. Only trajectories that successfully complete the task and yield a demonstrably correct final answer are accepted into the fine-tuning dataset. The system further uses Gemini 2.5 Flash to check if there exist search or reasoning shortcuts, filtering out trajectories that solve the problem through unintended paths. From 50K InfoSeek samples rolled out twice, after filtering incorrect executions, the system obtains 24K valid trajectories, implying the teacher model achieves 21.8% accuracy under the carefully designed workflow.

**Reinforcement Learning with GRPO**

After supervised fine-tuning, the system applies reinforcement learning to further enhance reasoning and search capabilities. The algorithm used is Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), which computes advantages without a value model. For each question input $X$, the old policy model $\pi_{\text{old}}$ generates a group of outputs $\{Y_1, Y_2, \ldots, Y_G\}$, and gets $G$ rewards $R = \{R_1, R_2, \ldots, R_G\}$. The advantage is computed as a normalized reward:

$$A_{i,t} = \frac{R_i - \text{mean}(R)}{\text{std}(R)}$$

The objective function is:

$$J_{\text{GRPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{1}{G}\sum_{i=0}^{G}\frac{1}{|Y| - |K|}\sum_{t=1}^{\mathbb{I}(Y_t)=1}\min\left(r_{i,t}A_{i,t}, \text{clip}\left(r_{i,t}, 1 - \epsilon, 1 + \epsilon\right)A_{i,t}\right) - \beta D_{\text{KL}}(\pi_\theta||\pi_{\text{ref}})\right]$$

where $r_{i,t}$ is defined as the probability ratio $\frac{\pi_\theta(Y_{i,t}|X,Y_{i,&lt;t},K_{i,&lt;t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(Y_{i,t}|X,Y_{i,&lt;t},K_{i,&lt;t})}$. GRPO adopts the clipped surrogate objective from PPO while using a KL divergence penalty, but computes advantages through group normalization rather than GAE.

The reward design is straightforward and binary:

$$R = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if format and extracted answer are both correct} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

This design rewards only those responses that both follow the required structure and contain the correct extracted answer, providing a clear signal to guide optimization.

**Two-Round Training Pipeline with Specific Hyperparameters**

The training follows a two-round pipeline detailed in **Appendix A.1**. Round 1 begins with knowledge distillation from Qwen2.5-72B, using 50K InfoSeek samples and 5K NQ & HQA samples, each rolled out twice. After filtering, 24K valid trajectories are obtained. The model is fine-tuned for 2 epochs with a learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-5}$, weight decay of 0.01, and a context length of 16,384 tokens. Training on a single 8×H100 node completes in 2 hours, yielding InfoSeeker-3B-SFT-Round1. Reinforcement learning then uses GRPO with outcome-based rewards, batch size of 256, maximum of 10 turns, rollout size 5, temperature 0.8, and a search engine restricted to top-5 retrieved contents. After 200 RL steps, InfoSeeker-3B-RL-Round1 is obtained.

Round 2 strengthens deep research abilities further. Rejection sampling on InfoSeeker-3B-RL-Round1 generates 16,494 trajectories from 55K source samples. These are filtered via Gemini 2.5 Flash API, producing 3,450 high-quality trajectories characterized by multi-turn search, finer-grained task decomposition, and more accurate step-by-step reasoning. Using the same hyperparameters as Round 1, the system obtains InfoSeeker-3B-SFT. For the second RL stage, from the original 55K data pool, 17K harder samples are selected (15K InfoSeek, 2K NQ & HQA). Before training, the model generates preliminary answers, and only 14K samples it fails on are kept. Using GRPO without KL loss for 100 steps, the final InfoSeeker-3B model is derived.

**Design Choices and Rationale**

Several design choices distinguish this approach from alternatives. The Research Tree construction with blurring operations prevents shortcut reasoning by ensuring no single constraint uniquely identifies the answer—this addresses the overdetermination problem common in synthetic QA generation. The dual-agent system (Planner + Browser) separates strategic decisions about tree complexity from execution of information extraction, enabling more controlled growth than monolithic generation. The two-stage training (SFT then RL) is necessary because direct RL on complex tasks is unstable; SFT provides a reasonable initialization that RL can then refine. The Refiner Agent is critical because increasing snippet length or search depth quickly leads to bloated contexts filled with redundant evidence—summarization keeps the working context compact while maintaining high recall. Finally, the binary reward design is deliberate: after SFT, the model already possesses capability in the desired format, so RL focuses on reinforcing correct outcomes rather than shaping behavior from scratch.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the paper's most significant contributions, distinguishing between what represents a fundamental shift in thinking versus what builds incrementally on existing approaches. ### 4.1 Formalizing Deep Research as Hierarchical Constraint Satisfaction Problems

**Type of Contribution:** *Fundamental theoretical innovation*

Prior to this work, the community lacked a precise definition separating "Deep Research" from simpler reasoning tasks. Most benchmarks conflated multi-hop QA with genuine hierarchical reasoning, treating them as points on a difficulty continuum rather than structurally distinct problem classes.

**What Makes This Different:**

The HCSP formalization introduces a recursive structure that neither flat CSPs nor sequential multi-hop problems capture. As shown in **Figure 2**, the key distinction is:

- **CSP**: Parallel constraints intersected once ($A = \bigcap S(c_i)$)
- **Multi-hop**: Sequential chain of single dependencies ($A = S^{(k)}(c)$)
- **HCSP**: Recursive hierarchy where sub-questions themselves contain constraints ($H(x) = \bigcap S(c_i) \cap \bigcap H(y_j)$)

This is not merely adding more hops—it changes the *topology* of reasoning from linear or flat to tree-structured.

**Why It Matters:**

> "Deep research questions extend beyond both by involving a hierarchy of interdependent constraints that intertwine both parallel conditions and sequential steps."

This formalization enables three critical advances:

1. **Measurable Complexity**: Researchers can now quantify structural depth (vertex count, tree height) rather than relying on vague difficulty labels. **Table 2** validates this—failure rates correlate with vertex count (88.1% at 3 vertices → 94.1% at 7+ vertices).

2. **Prevents Benchmark Contamination**: By requiring tree traversal, HCSPs prevent models from solving problems through memorized facts or single-step retrieval—common shortcuts in existing benchmarks.

3. **Guides Data Synthesis**: The mathematical definition directly informs the Research Tree construction process, ensuring generated questions actually reflect the intended reasoning structure.

**Common Misconception:** Readers might assume HCSPs are just "harder multi-hop questions." The critical difference is that HCSPs require *parallel constraint satisfaction at multiple levels*, not just longer chains. A 10-hop sequential question is still an MHP; an HCSP requires branching sub-problems that must each be resolved.

### 4.2 The Blurring Mechanism: Preventing Shortcut Reasoning in Synthetic Data

**Type of Contribution:** *Fundamental methodological innovation*

Synthetic QA generation faces a persistent problem: **overdetermination**, where a single constraint accidentally reveals the answer, allowing models to bypass intended reasoning paths. Prior synthesis approaches either ignore this or use surface-level heuristics to estimate difficulty.

**What Makes This Different:**

The "blurring parent with constraints" action (**Section 3.2**) is a structural intervention, not a post-hoc filter. Instead of generating a question and then checking if it's too easy, the system *builds in* redundancy during tree construction:

- The Planner identifies vertices where current constraints are insufficient to uniquely derive the answer
- The Browser adds $k$ claims that *together* yield uniqueness, ensuring no single claim shortcuts the reasoning
- Candidate sets are verified to be mutually exclusive (no inclusion relations)

This contrasts with approaches like WebShaper or SimpleDeepSearcher, which define reasoning graphs but lack mechanisms to prevent individual nodes from being independently solvable.

**Why It Matters:**

The quality assurance results (**Section 3.5**) demonstrate the mechanism's effectiveness:

- Qwen2.5-32B-Inst answers only 2% of questions directly from parametric memory
- Qwen2.5-72B with Chain-of-Thought fails on 91.6% of samples
- Failure rates scale with vertex count, confirming structural complexity drives difficulty

> "This helps enhance the difficulty and ensure the uniqueness of the answer to the corresponding question."

**Design Trade-off:** The blurring mechanism increases synthesis cost (more API calls to verify constraint combinations) but produces data that actually trains hierarchical reasoning rather than teaching models to find shortcuts. This is a deliberate choice prioritizing data quality over generation speed.

### 4.3 Data Structure Trumps Model Scale for Deep Research

**Type of Contribution:** *Fundamental empirical discovery*

The prevailing assumption in LLM research is that capability gaps close primarily through increased parameters, compute, or architectural innovations. This paper challenges that paradigm for Deep Research tasks specifically.

**What Makes This Different:**

**Table 4** and **Figure 1** present a striking result: `InfoSeeker-3B` (16.5% accuracy on BrowseComp-Plus) outperforms:

- `Qwen3-32B` (3.5%) — **10× more parameters, 4.7× worse performance**
- `Gemini 2.5 Flash` (15.5%) — commercial API with undisclosed but certainly larger scale
- `Sonnet 4` (14.3%) and `GPT-4.1` (14.6%) — leading commercial models

This is not a marginal improvement—it's a **reversal of the expected scaling relationship** for this task class.

**Why It Matters:**

The finding suggests Deep Research capabilities are **data-limited rather than capacity-limited**. A compact model trained on structurally appropriate data internalizes agentic behaviors that larger models trained on shallow data never learn. This has implications for:

1. **Resource Efficiency**: Organizations can achieve competitive Deep Research performance without massive models, reducing inference costs.

2. **Training Priorities**: The bottleneck is not model architecture but training data structure. Efforts should focus on synthesis frameworks rather than scaling parameters.

3. **Benchmark Design**: Evaluations should control for training data composition, not just model size, when comparing Deep Research capabilities.

**Important Caveat:** This finding applies specifically to *Deep Research tasks* (HCSPs). For other capabilities (creative writing, code generation, general knowledge), model scale likely remains dominant. The insight is task-specific, not universal.

### 4.4 Fully Open-Source Scalable Synthesis Framework

**Type of Contribution:** *Incremental but significant community contribution*

Several prior works explore automated data synthesis for agentic reasoning (WebShaper, DeepResearcher, WebDancer). However, **Table 1** reveals a pattern: most are either small-scale (hundreds of samples), closed-source, or lack the synthesis framework itself.

**What Makes This Different:**

InfoSeek provides three open artifacts simultaneously:

| Artifact | Scale | Prior Comparable Work |
|----------|-------|----------------------|
| Dataset (QA pairs) | 50K+ | NQ (300K), HotpotQA (100K) — but neither are HCSP |
| Reasoning Trajectories | 16.5K | SimpleDeepSearcher (871), WebDancer (200) |
| Synthesis Framework | Full code | Most prior: framework not released |

The combination of scale, structure (HCSP), and openness is unprecedented for Deep Research data.

**Why It Matters:**

> "The dataset with more than 50k QA pairs, 16.5k reasoning trajectories, and data construction framework of InfoSeek are fully open-sourced. Thus encouraging further research or development in the community."

This enables:

1. **Reproducibility**: Other researchers can verify claims and build on the foundation without recreating the synthesis pipeline from scratch.

2. **Customization**: The framework supports "controllable complexity" — researchers can generate datasets targeting specific vertex counts or reasoning depths for their needs.

3. **Extended Optimization**: The paper notes that preserved meta-information (intermediate steps, retrieval labels) "supports advanced optimization strategies, including compound reward design and trajectory-level exploration" — capabilities other researchers can now explore.

**Limitation to Acknowledge:** The synthesis cost ($571.8 for 50K samples, **Table 2**) is non-trivial for resource-constrained researchers. While open-source, reproducing at full scale requires meaningful API budgets. However, the framework allows smaller-scale generation for experimentation.

| HCSP Formalization | Fundamental | Yes — any task with hierarchical reasoning |
| Blurring Mechanism | Fundamental | Yes — prevents shortcuts in synthetic data generation |
| Data > Scale Finding | Empirical | Task-specific (Deep Research) |
| Open Framework | Incremental | Yes — template for other synthesis efforts |

The most enduring contributions are the **HCSP formalization** (provides language for discussing Deep Research rigorously) and the **blurring mechanism** (solves a general problem in synthetic data quality). The scale-vs-data finding is provocative but requires replication across different task domains. The open-source release accelerates community progress but is a means rather than an end.

Readers should take away that **structural alignment between training data and target capabilities** matters more than raw data volume or model size for complex reasoning tasks. This principle likely extends beyond Deep Research to other domains requiring multi-step, verifiable reasoning.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their core claim: that training on structurally complex HCSP data enables compact models to compete with much larger systems on Deep Research tasks. We walk through the evaluation methodology, dissect the quantitative results, and assess whether the experiments convincingly support the paper's contributions.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Benchmark Selection Strategy**

The authors employ a three-tier evaluation approach to test different capability levels:

| Tier | Purpose | Datasets |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Tier 1: Single-Hop QA** | Test basic retrieval and factual knowledge | Natural Questions (NQ), TriviaQA (TQA), PopQA |
| **Tier 2: Multi-Hop QA** | Test sequential reasoning across documents | HotpotQA (HQA), 2WikiMultihopQA (2Wiki), Musique (MSQ), Bamboogle (Bamb) |
| **Tier 3: Deep Research** | Test hierarchical reasoning with complex constraints | BrowseComp-Plus (830 problems, 100K webpage corpus) |

This progression is deliberate. If `InfoSeeker-3B` only performed well on Tier 3, critics could argue the model overfit to HCSP patterns. Strong performance across all tiers demonstrates **general agentic search capability**, not just task-specific memorization.

**Evaluation Metrics**

- **Exact Match (EM)**: Used for all single-hop and multi-hop QA benchmarks. The model's extracted answer must precisely match the ground truth.
- **LLM-Judged Accuracy**: Used for BrowseComp-Plus following the official benchmark setting. This is necessary because Deep Research answers may require interpretation of complex evidence rather than simple string matching.

**Retrieval Setup**

The retrieval configuration differs between benchmark tiers (**Appendix A.2**):

- **Single/Multi-Hop QA**: Wikipedia-25 corpus segmented into 512-token chunks. Document retrieval uses BGE-M3 embeddings with top-5 documents selected.
- **BrowseComp-Plus**: 100K web page corpus from the official release. BM25 serves as the retrieval method with top-5 retrieved contents during RL training.

This distinction matters: BrowseComp-Plus uses a **fixed webpage corpus**, ensuring all models operate on the same information landscape. This prevents confounding where one model might access better search infrastructure than another.

**Baseline Categories**

The authors compare against two major baseline families (**Section 5.1**):

1. **RAG-Based Methods**: Vanilla RAG, IRCoT (interleaved retrieval with chain-of-thought), RQRAG (query refinement), Self-RAG (self-reflection mechanism). These represent traditional retrieve-then-generate approaches without agentic search loops.

2. **Agentic Search Models**: Search-o1-3B, Search-R1-3B, ZeroSearch-3B, AutoRefine-3B, InForage-3B. These are 3B parameter models trained with various RL or search-enhancement techniques—direct size-matched competitors to `InfoSeeker-3B`.

For BrowseComp-Plus specifically, the comparison expands to include **commercial APIs** (Gemini 2.5 Flash/Pro, Sonnet 4, GPT-4.1, GPT-5) and **larger open models** (Qwen3-32B, Search-R1-32B). This tests whether data quality can compensate for parameter scale.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**Performance on Single-Hop and Multi-Hop QA (Table 3)**

Table 3 presents results across seven benchmarks. `InfoSeeker-3B` achieves an **average score of 43.5%**, outperforming all baselines:

| Model | NQ | TQA | PopQA | HQA | 2Wiki | MSQ | Bamb | **Avg.** |
|-------|-----|-----|-------|-----|-------|-----|------|----------|
| Vanilla RAG | 34.8 | 54.4 | 38.7 | 25.5 | 22.6 | 4.7 | 8.0 | 27.0 |
| InForage-3B | 42.1 | 59.7 | 45.2 | 40.9 | 42.8 | 17.2 | 36.0 | 40.6 |
| AutoRefine-3B | 43.6 | 59.7 | 44.7 | 40.4 | 38.0 | 16.9 | 33.6 | 39.6 |
| **InfoSeeker-3B** | **42.7** | **57.1** | **48.0** | **44.6** | **52.0** | **20.5** | **39.8** | **43.5** |

Key observations:

- **Multi-hop gains are substantial**: On 2Wiki, `InfoSeeker-3B` achieves 52.0% vs. InForage-3B's 42.8%—a **9.2 point improvement**. This is the benchmark most similar to HCSP structure (multiple reasoning steps with interdependent constraints).
- **Single-hop performance remains competitive**: On NQ (42.7%) and PopQA (48.0%), the model performs comparably to baselines despite being trained primarily on complex HCSP data. This suggests the training does not degrade basic retrieval capabilities.
- **Most baselines rely on in-domain supervision**: The paper notes that "most baselines rely heavily on large amounts of in-domain supervision (i.e., more than 100K NQ&HQA), while our approach focuses on leveraging purpose-built InfoSeek dataset for training, yet still attains stronger general performance." This is a critical point: `InfoSeeker-3B` was not trained on NQ or HotpotQA trajectories, yet generalizes to them.

**Performance on BrowseComp-Plus (Table 4)**

This is the paper's flagship result. Table 4 shows accuracy on the complex Deep Research benchmark:

| Model | Parameters | Retriever | Accuracy (%) | Search Calls |
|-------|------------|-----------|--------------|--------------|
| Qwen3-32B | 32B | BM25 | 3.5 | 0.92 |
| SearchR1-32B | 32B | BM25 | 3.9 | 1.78 |
| Gemini 2.5 Flash | Undisclosed | BM25 | 15.5 | 10.56 |
| Sonnet 4 | Undisclosed | BM25 | 14.3 | 9.95 |
| GPT-4.1 | Undisclosed | BM25 | 14.6 | 11.22 |
| **InfoSeeker-3B** | **3B** | **BM25** | **16.5** | **8.24** |
| Gemini 2.5 Pro | Undisclosed | BM25 | 19.0 | 7.44 |
| GPT-5 | Undisclosed | BM25 | 55.9 | 23.23 |

The implications are striking:

- **10× parameter efficiency**: `InfoSeeker-3B` (3B parameters, 16.5% accuracy) vs. `Qwen3-32B` (32B parameters, 3.5% accuracy). The smaller model achieves **4.7× better accuracy** with **10× fewer parameters**.
- **Competitive with commercial APIs**: `InfoSeeker-3B` surpasses `Gemini 2.5 Flash` (15.5%), `Sonnet 4` (14.3%), and `GPT-4.1` (14.6%). It approaches `Gemini 2.5 Pro` (19.0%) despite being open-source and compact.
- **Search call efficiency**: `InfoSeeker-3B` makes 8.24 search calls on average, compared to 10.56 for Gemini 2.5 Flash and 23.23 for GPT-5. This suggests more **targeted query generation** rather than brute-force exploration.

The paper explicitly states in **Figure 1** caption: "InfoSeeker-3B, a compact LLM trained with the InfoSeek dataset, significantly outperforms Qwen3-32B and achieves performance on par with leading commercial LLMs (ordered in API prices), highlighting the strong potential of InfoSeek for advancing Deep Research tasks."

**Dataset Ablation: InfoSeek vs. NQ+HQA (Table 5)**

Table 5 isolates the effect of training data by comparing `InfoSeek` against standard QA datasets:

| Training Set | Retriever | Accuracy (%) | Search Calls |
|--------------|-----------|--------------|--------------|
| NQ+HQA | BM25 | 3.0 | 1.39 |
| **InfoSeek** | **BM25** | **16.5** | **8.24** |

This is perhaps the most direct evidence for the paper's core claim. Both models use the same architecture (Qwen2.5-3B-Inst backbone) and same RL training procedure (GRPO). The **only difference is the training dataset**.

- **5.5× accuracy improvement**: 16.5% vs. 3.0%
- **5.9× more search calls**: 8.24 vs. 1.39

The search call difference is revealing. The NQ+HQA-trained model makes fewer searches not because it's more efficient, but because it **doesn't learn to search effectively**. It likely attempts to answer from parametric memory (which fails on Deep Research tasks). The InfoSeek-trained model learns that multi-turn search is necessary and uses the tool appropriately.

The paper states in **Section 5.3**: "training on InfoSeek yields substantially stronger deep research performance and more effective use of search tools." This ablation directly supports the claim that **data structure, not just training technique, determines Deep Research capability**.

### 5.3 Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: HCSP Data Enables Compact Models to Compete with Larger Systems**

**Verdict: Strongly Supported**

The BrowseComp-Plus results (Table 4) provide compelling evidence. The 16.5% vs. 3.5% comparison between `InfoSeeker-3B` and `Qwen3-32B` is difficult to explain through any mechanism other than training data quality. Both are open-source Qwen variants; the primary difference is the training corpus.

However, one caveat: the comparison to commercial APIs (Gemini, GPT-4.1, Sonnet 4) should be interpreted carefully. These are **closed systems** with undisclosed training data, architectures, and potentially different retrieval infrastructure. The paper uses the same BM25 retriever for all models on BrowseComp-Plus, which controls for retrieval quality, but we cannot verify that all models used identical prompting or inference configurations.

**Claim 2: InfoSeek Data Generalizes Beyond HCSP Tasks**

**Verdict: Moderately Supported**

Table 3 shows `InfoSeeker-3B` outperforming baselines on standard QA benchmarks (NQ, HotpotQA, etc.) despite not being trained on those datasets. This suggests the agentic search behaviors learned from HCSP data transfer to simpler tasks.

However, the margins are smaller on single-hop tasks (e.g., 42.7% on NQ vs. 43.6% for AutoRefine-3B). This is expected: HCSP training optimizes for complex reasoning, not simple lookup. The fact that performance doesn't degrade significantly is the real win—it shows **no negative transfer**.

**Claim 3: Structural Complexity Correlates with Difficulty**

**Verdict: Strongly Supported**

Table 2 provides internal validation of the synthesis process:

| # Vertices | Count | Failure Rate (Qwen2.5-72B, CoT) |
|------------|-------|---------------------------------|
| 3 | 3,841 | 88.1% |
| 4 | 15,263 | 91.7% |
| 5 | 15,051 | 91.0% |
| 6 | 17,714 | 92.6% |
| ≥7 | 269 | 94.1% |
| **Total** | **52,138** | **91.6%** |

The paper notes: "the failure rate exhibits a strong positive correlation with the number of vertices, increasing from 88.1% for 3-vertex problems to 94.1% for problems with 7 or more vertices. This trend validates that our synthesis process effectively controls for reasoning complexity."

This is important because it confirms the **blurring mechanism** and tree construction actually produce harder problems as intended. Without this validation, critics could argue the synthetic data is uniformly difficult (or uniformly easy) regardless of structure.

**Claim 4: Quality Assurance Filters Remove Low-Quality Samples**

**Verdict: Supported, but Limited Detail**

Section 3.5 describes the two-pronged QA protocol:

- **Difficulty filter**: Qwen2.5-32B-Inst answers only 2% directly from memory. These samples are removed.
- **Verifiability filter**: Gemini 2.5 Flash is given ground-truth documents plus distractors. Questions with wrong answers, multiple answers, or unsolvable cases are filtered.

The paper does not report **how many samples were removed** at each stage. We know the final dataset is 52,138 samples (Table 2), but the initial generation count is not specified. This makes it difficult to assess the filter's selectivity. A more transparent ablation would show: "Started with X samples, removed Y% for difficulty, removed Z% for verifiability, final count W."

### 5.4 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

**Training Data Ablation (Table 5)**

As discussed above, this is the cleanest ablation in the paper. It isolates the dataset variable while holding architecture, training procedure, and evaluation constant. The 5.5× accuracy improvement is unambiguous evidence for the InfoSeek data's value.

**Two-Round Training Analysis (Appendix A.1)**

The appendix describes a two-round training pipeline:

- **Round 1**: SFT on 24K trajectories (from 50K InfoSeek + 5K NQ/HQA samples), then RL for 200 steps.
- **Round 2**: Rejection sampling produces 3,450 high-quality trajectories from 55K source samples. Second RL stage uses 14K hard samples (model fails on these initially) for 100 GRPO steps without KL loss.

The paper does not provide a **step-by-step performance breakdown** (e.g., accuracy after Round 1 SFT, after Round 1 RL, after Round 2 SFT, after Round 2 RL). This would help readers understand which training stage contributes most to final performance. The absence of this breakdown is a limitation.

**Retriever Consistency**

All BrowseComp-Plus evaluations use BM25 with the same 100K webpage corpus. This is a strength—it ensures fair comparison. However, the paper does not test **alternative retrievers** (e.g., dense retrieval with BGE-M3 on the same corpus). It's possible that retrieval quality, not just reasoning capability, affects results. A robustness check would vary the retriever while holding the model constant.

**Failure Cases and Limitations**

The paper does not include a dedicated **failure analysis section**. We can infer some limitations from the results:

- **BrowseComp-Plus accuracy is still low**: 16.5% means the model fails on **83.5% of Deep Research tasks**. While this surpasses baselines, it's far from reliable for real-world deployment.
- **GPT-5 significantly outperforms**: At 55.9% accuracy, GPT-5 is **3.4× better** than `InfoSeeker-3B`. This suggests that for the most complex tasks, scale still matters—or GPT-5 has access to superior training data not available to open-source efforts.
- **Search call variance**: The standard deviation of search calls is not reported. Some queries may require many more searches than the 8.24 average, which could impact latency and cost in production settings.

**Missing Robustness Checks**

Several robustness evaluations would strengthen the claims:

1. **Cross-benchmark generalization**: Train on InfoSeek, evaluate on a different Deep Research benchmark (not BrowseComp-Plus). This would test whether the model learns general reasoning vs. benchmark-specific patterns.

2. **Vertex count stratification**: Report BrowseComp-Plus accuracy broken down by problem complexity (e.g., 3-vertex vs. 7+ vertex problems). This would show whether the model's advantage holds across difficulty levels.

3. **Ablation on blurring mechanism**: Compare models trained on InfoSeek data with vs. without the blurring action. This would isolate the contribution of the overdetermination prevention mechanism.

4. **Refiner Agent ablation**: Test `InfoSeeker-3B` with and without the Refiner Agent during inference. This would quantify the summarization component's contribution to final performance.

The absence of these ablations doesn't invalidate the main claims, but it leaves some mechanistic questions unanswered.

### 5.5 Conditions and Trade-offs

**When InfoSeek Works Best**

The experiments suggest InfoSeek-trained models excel under these conditions:

- **Hierarchical reasoning is required**: Tasks with interdependent sub-problems (HCSP structure) show the largest gains.
- **Search tool access is available**: The model learns to use search effectively; without retrieval capability, the training provides limited benefit.
- **Verifiable answers exist**: The training data requires unique, factually-grounded answers. Open-ended tasks (e.g., report writing, creative synthesis) are not evaluated.

**Trade-offs to Consider**

| Trade-off | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Synthesis Cost** | $571.8 for 50K samples (Table 2). Reproducing at scale requires meaningful API budget. |
| **Training Complexity** | Two-round SFT+RL pipeline is more complex than single-stage fine-tuning. Requires careful hyperparameter tuning. |
| **Task Specificity** | Gains are largest on Deep Research tasks. Single-hop QA improvements are marginal. |
| **Absolute Performance** | 16.5% on BrowseComp-Plus is strong relative to baselines, but still fails on 83.5% of tasks. |

**Important Caveat: Task-Specific Finding**

The "data > scale" finding applies specifically to **Deep Research tasks (HCSPs)**. The paper explicitly notes this is task-specific, not universal. For capabilities like creative writing, code generation, or general knowledge recall, model scale likely remains dominant. Readers should not generalize this finding beyond the evaluated task domain.

### 5.6 Summary Assessment

The experimental section provides **strong evidence** for the paper's core claims, with some limitations:

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Key Supporting Result |
|-------|-------------------|----------------------|
| HCSP data enables compact models to compete | Strong | Table 4: 16.5% vs. 3.5% (3B vs. 32B) |
| InfoSeek data generalizes to standard QA | Moderate | Table 3: 43.5% avg., best among 3B models |
| Structural complexity controls difficulty | Strong | Table 2: 88.1% → 94.1% failure rate by vertex count |
| Quality assurance filters improve data | Supported (incomplete) | Section 3.5 describes filters, but removal rates not reported |

The most compelling result is the **dataset ablation (Table 5)**: same model, same training procedure, different data → 5.5× accuracy improvement. This isolates the variable of interest and provides causal evidence for the data quality hypothesis.

The main limitations are the **lack of failure analysis**, **missing intermediate training checkpoints**, and **absence of additional robustness ablations**. These don't undermine the main conclusions but leave room for deeper mechanistic understanding.

For practitioners considering this approach, the takeaway is clear: **if your task involves hierarchical, multi-step reasoning with verifiable answers, investing in structurally complex training data (like InfoSeek) may yield better returns than scaling model parameters alone**. However, expect significant synthesis costs and plan for task-specific evaluation rather than assuming universal improvements.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

No approach is without constraints, and `InfoSeek` is no exception. This section examines the assumptions underlying the framework, the scenarios it does not address, the computational and data requirements, and the open questions that remain. Understanding these limitations is critical for practitioners considering adoption and researchers building on this work.

### 6.1 Core Assumptions and Their Implications

**Assumption 1: Deep Research Tasks Have Unique, Verifiable Answers**

The paper explicitly narrows its scope: "rather than long-form open-ended tasks such as report writing, we focus on deep research questions that yield a unique and verifiable answer" (**Section 1**). This is a deliberate design choice that enables the HCSP formalization and quality assurance protocol, but it excludes a significant class of real-world research tasks.

*Implication:* Many genuine Deep Research scenarios—literature reviews, policy recommendations, scientific hypothesis generation—produce nuanced, multi-faceted outputs rather than single verifiable answers. The `InfoSeek` framework does not address these open-ended cases. Practitioners working on such tasks cannot directly apply this approach without significant modification.

**Assumption 2: Tree Structure Adequately Captures Reasoning Dependencies**

The HCSP formalization assumes that Deep Research reasoning can be represented as a tree where each node has exactly one parent (**Section 2.3.1**). This excludes DAG (directed acyclic graph) structures where a sub-problem might inform multiple higher-level conclusions.

*Implication:* Real-world research often involves convergent reasoning where multiple sub-investigations inform the same conclusion, or where evidence from one branch revises understanding in another branch. The tree constraint simplifies synthesis but may not capture the full complexity of authentic research workflows.

**Assumption 3: Teacher Model Trajectories Are Sufficiently High-Quality**

The SFT process distills trajectories from Qwen2.5-72B, which achieves only **21.8% accuracy** under the designed workflow (**Appendix A.1**). The paper states: "we deliberately retain 'shortcut' cases among the correct trajectories, as preserving diverse solution strategies offers valuable learning signals."

*Implication:* The student model learns from a teacher that fails on ~78% of tasks. While rejection sampling filters incorrect executions, the accepted trajectories may still contain suboptimal reasoning patterns. This creates a ceiling on what the distilled model can learn. The paper does not analyze whether teacher errors propagate to the student.

**Assumption 4: External Refiner Agent Is Acceptable Overhead**

The inference workflow employs Qwen2.5-7B-Inst as a dedicated Refiner Agent to summarize search results (**Section 4.1**). This is a separate model call, not part of the 3B parameter `InfoSeeker` itself.

*Implication:* The total inference cost includes both the 3B model and the 7B refiner. For latency-sensitive or cost-constrained deployments, this two-model architecture may be prohibitive. The paper does not report the refiner's computational cost or latency contribution.

### 6.2 Scenarios and Problem Settings Not Addressed

**Open-Ended Research Tasks**

As noted above, the framework explicitly excludes tasks without unique answers. The introduction mentions "scientific discovery and policy analysis" as target domains (**Section 1**), yet the evaluation focuses entirely on question-answering with verifiable ground truth. There is a mismatch between the stated application areas and the actual evaluated tasks.

**Domain-Specific Knowledge**

The synthesis pipeline mines entities from Wikipedia and general web pages (**Section 3.1**). Specialized domains—medical research, legal analysis, technical engineering—may have knowledge structures and evidence standards not well-represented in these sources.

*Evidence:* The paper does not evaluate `InfoSeeker` on any domain-specific benchmarks. All evaluation datasets (NQ, HotpotQA, BrowseComp-Plus) are general knowledge tasks. Practitioners in specialized fields cannot assume the framework will transfer without domain adaptation.

**Dynamic or Time-Sensitive Information**

The Research Tree construction uses static Wikipedia dumps and webpage snapshots. Real-world Deep Research often involves time-sensitive information (e.g., "What is the current status of...?", "How has X changed since Y?").

*Evidence:* The BrowseComp-Plus benchmark uses a **fixed 100K webpage corpus** (**Appendix A.2**), ensuring reproducibility but not reflecting the dynamic nature of real research where information evolves.

**Multi-Modal Research**

The framework operates entirely on text. Many research tasks require interpreting figures, tables, code repositories, or datasets. The paper does not address multi-modal information integration.

*Evidence:* No multi-modal benchmarks are evaluated. The Refiner Agent summarizes text snippets only (**Section 4.1**).

**Collaborative or Multi-Agent Research**

The paper mentions "a system of collaborating LLMs" as a possible Deep Research agent (**Section 2.2**), but `InfoSeeker` is a single model with an external refiner. True multi-agent collaboration (e.g., debate, verification, division of labor) is not explored.

*Evidence:* The Related Work section mentions AgentOrchestra and Agentic Reasoning as inference-time frameworks (**Section 6**), but `InfoSeek` does not integrate these approaches.

### 6.3 Computational, Data, and Scalability Constraints

**Synthesis Cost**

Generating the 50K+ sample dataset cost **$571.8** (**Table 2**). While this is reasonable for well-funded labs, it represents a non-trivial barrier for resource-constrained researchers.

*Breakdown:* The cost reflects API calls to powerful LLMs (DeepSeek V3 or GPT-4.1) for question generation (**Section 3.4**), plus Gemini 2.5 Flash for verifiability filtering (**Section 3.5**). Reproducing at full scale requires meaningful API budgets.

*Mitigation:* The framework supports "controllable complexity" (**Section 1**), allowing smaller-scale generation for experimentation. However, the paper does not provide guidance on minimum viable dataset sizes.

**Training Infrastructure Requirements**

The two-round training pipeline requires significant compute:

- **Round 1 SFT:** 2 hours on a single 8×H100 node (**Appendix A.1**)
- **Round 1 RL:** 200 GRPO steps with batch size 256, rollout size 5
- **Round 2:** Additional SFT on 3,450 trajectories + 100 GRPO steps on 14K hard samples

*Implication:* While the final model is only 3B parameters (deployable on modest hardware), **training** requires access to H100-class GPUs. This is not a "train on consumer hardware" approach.

**Context Length Requirements**

The SFT training uses a context length of **16,384 tokens** (**Appendix A.1**). The inference workflow includes multiple search results, refiner summaries, and thinking traces (**Figure 3**).

*Implication:* Models must support long contexts. The paper uses Qwen2.5-3B-Inst, which supports this, but not all 3B models do. practitioners cannot arbitrarily swap in smaller-context backbones.

**Retriever Dependency**

All BrowseComp-Plus evaluations use **BM25** with a fixed 100K webpage corpus (**Appendix A.2**). The paper does not test alternative retrievers (e.g., dense retrieval with BGE-M3, which is used for single/multi-hop QA).

*Open Question:* Would `InfoSeeker-3B` perform differently with a neural retriever? The 16.5% accuracy is contingent on BM25's retrieval quality. If retrieval fails, the reasoning model cannot succeed regardless of training.

**Search Call Variability**

Table 4 reports **average search calls** (8.24 for `InfoSeeker-3B`), but does not report variance or distribution.

*Implication:* Some queries may require significantly more searches than the average, impacting latency and cost in production. Without understanding the tail behavior, practitioners cannot reliably budget for inference costs.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

**Absolute Performance Remains Low**

While `InfoSeeker-3B` outperforms baselines relatively, the **absolute accuracy on BrowseComp-Plus is 16.5%** (**Table 4**). This means the model fails on **83.5% of Deep Research tasks**.

*Critical Assessment:* Surpassing a 3.5% baseline is meaningful, but 16.5% is not deployment-ready for high-stakes applications. The paper acknowledges this implicitly by comparing to GPT-5's 55.9%—a **3.4× gap** remains.

*Open Question:* Is this gap due to model scale, training data quality, or architectural limitations? The paper argues for data quality, but the GPT-5 comparison suggests scale or other factors still matter for the most complex tasks.

**Limited Transparency on Quality Assurance Filtering**

Section 3.5 describes the two-pronged QA protocol (Difficulty and Verifiability filters), but does not report:

- How many samples were initially generated before filtering?
- What percentage was removed at each stage?
- What were the most common failure modes?

*Weakness:* Without these statistics, readers cannot assess the filter's selectivity or understand what types of synthetic questions tend to fail. A more transparent ablation would strengthen reproducibility.

**Missing Intermediate Training Checkpoints**

Appendix A.1 describes the two-round training pipeline but does not provide **step-by-step performance breakdowns**:

- Accuracy after Round 1 SFT?
- Accuracy after Round 1 RL?
- Accuracy after Round 2 SFT?
- Accuracy after Round 2 RL?

*Open Question:* Which training stage contributes most to final performance? Is the second RL stage necessary, or does most gain come from the first SFT? This information would help practitioners optimize their own training pipelines.

**No Failure Case Analysis**

The paper does not include a dedicated **failure analysis section**. Readers cannot see:

- What types of questions does `InfoSeeker-3B` fail on?
- Are failures due to retrieval errors, reasoning errors, or both?
- Does the model exhibit systematic biases or blind spots?

*Weakness:* Understanding failure modes is critical for improvement. The absence of this analysis leaves mechanistic questions unanswered.

**No Cross-Benchmark Generalization Test**

The model is trained on InfoSeek and evaluated on BrowseComp-Plus, but there is no test on a **different Deep Research benchmark**.

*Open Question:* Does the model learn general hierarchical reasoning, or does it overfit to BrowseComp-Plus patterns? A cross-benchmark evaluation would strengthen the generalization claim.

**No Ablation on Key Design Choices**

Several critical design choices are not ablated:

| Design Choice | Not Tested |
|---------------|------------|
| Blurring mechanism | InfoSeek with vs. without Action 2 |
| Refiner Agent | InfoSeeker with vs. without summarization |
| Parallel queries | Multiple queries vs. single query per step |
| Two-round training | Round 1 only vs. Round 1 + Round 2 |

*Implication:* Readers cannot isolate which components drive performance. The full system works, but the contribution of each piece remains unclear.

**Task-Specific Finding**

The paper explicitly notes that the "data > scale" finding applies specifically to **Deep Research tasks (HCSPs)** (**Section 5.5** in prior analysis). For other capabilities—creative writing, code generation, general knowledge—model scale likely remains dominant.

*Critical Assessment:* This is an important caveat that readers must not overlook. The insight is **task-specific, not universal**. Practitioners working on non-HCSP tasks should not expect similar gains from InfoSeek-style data.

**Teacher Model Ceiling**

The SFT process distills from Qwen2.5-72B at 21.8% accuracy (**Appendix A.1**). The student model (`InfoSeeker-3B`) achieves 16.5% on BrowseComp-Plus—close to the teacher's performance ceiling.

*Open Question:* Can the student surpass the teacher? The current pipeline may be limited by teacher quality. Alternative approaches (e.g., self-improvement, iterative refinement) are not explored.

### 6.5 Summary: Practical Considerations for Adoption

| Consideration | Status | Recommendation |
|---------------|--------|----------------|
| **Synthesis Cost** | $571.8 for 50K samples | Budget accordingly; consider smaller-scale generation for prototyping |
| **Training Compute** | Requires 8×H100 node | Ensure access to appropriate infrastructure |
| **Task Fit** | HCSP tasks with verifiable answers | Do not apply to open-ended research without modification |
| **Absolute Performance** | 16.5% on BrowseComp-Plus | Suitable for research/exploration, not high-stakes deployment |
| **Domain Coverage** | General knowledge (Wikipedia/Web) | Domain adaptation needed for specialized fields |
| **Inference Overhead** | 3B model + 7B refiner | Account for two-model latency and cost |
| **Retriever Dependency** | BM25 on fixed corpus | Test with your own retrieval infrastructure |

The `InfoSeek` framework represents a significant advance for Deep Research data synthesis and training, but it is not a universal solution. Practitioners should adopt it with clear understanding of its scope, costs, and limitations. Researchers building on this work have substantial opportunity to address the open questions identified above—particularly around failure analysis, ablation studies, and extension to open-ended or domain-specific tasks.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work does more than introduce a new dataset—it reframes how the community should think about capability development for complex reasoning tasks. This section examines the broader consequences for research priorities, identifies concrete follow-up directions, outlines practical deployment scenarios, and provides guidance on when to adopt this approach versus alternatives.

### 7.1 Shifting the Research Landscape

**From Scale-Centric to Structure-Centric Thinking**

The most significant implication of this work is the empirical demonstration that **data structure can compensate for parameter scale** on specific task classes. The BrowseComp-Plus results (**Table 4**) show `InfoSeeker-3B` achieving 16.5% accuracy versus `Qwen3-32B` at 3.5%—a 4.7× improvement with 10× fewer parameters. This challenges the prevailing assumption that capability gaps close primarily through increased model size.

For the Deep Research community, this suggests a **paradigm shift in resource allocation**:

| Traditional Approach | InfoSeek-Informed Approach |
|---------------------|---------------------------|
| Scale parameters first | Structure training data first |
| Generic QA datasets | Task-aligned HCSP datasets |
| Single-stage fine-tuning | Multi-stage SFT + RL pipeline |
| Evaluate on accuracy alone | Evaluate on reasoning structure |

This does not mean scale is irrelevant—GPT-5's 55.9% accuracy on BrowseComp-Plus (**Table 4**) shows that for the most complex tasks, scale or superior training data still matters. However, for the 3B–32B parameter range where most open-source development occurs, **data quality is the binding constraint**, not model capacity.

**Establishing HCSP as a First-Class Problem Class**

Prior to this work, "Deep Research" was a vague category conflating multi-hop QA, long-context retrieval, and agentic workflows. The HCSP formalization (**Section 2.2**) provides:

- **Precise terminology** for discussing hierarchical reasoning distinct from flat CSP or sequential MHP
- **Measurable complexity metrics** (vertex count, tree height) that correlate with difficulty (**Table 2**: 88.1% → 94.1% failure rate)
- **A synthesis target** that ensures generated questions actually require tree traversal rather than shortcut reasoning

This formalization enables systematic progress measurement. Future benchmarks can report HCSP-specific metrics rather than lumping all "complex reasoning" together, allowing researchers to isolate whether improvements come from genuine hierarchical reasoning or simpler pattern matching.

**Opening the Black Box of Agentic Training**

By releasing the full synthesis framework alongside the dataset, `InfoSeek` makes agentic training **reproducible and auditable**. Prior works like WebShaper, DeepResearcher, and Search-R1 either do not release their synthesis pipelines or generate only hundreds of samples (**Table 1**). The open-source release (50K+ QA pairs, 16.5K trajectories, full framework code) enables:

- **Independent verification** of claims about data quality and model performance
- **Community extension** to new domains or task types
- **Transparent cost accounting** ($571.8 for 50K samples, **Table 2**)

This contrasts with commercial Deep Research APIs where training data, synthesis methods, and evaluation protocols remain proprietary.

### 7.2 Concrete Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several opportunities for extension, and additional directions emerge from the limitations identified in **Section 6**.

**Immediate Extensions (High Feasibility)**

1. **Ablation of Key Components**
   
   The paper acknowledges missing ablations on critical design choices. Follow-up work should isolate:
   
   - **Blurring mechanism**: Train on InfoSeek data generated with vs. without Action 2 (blurring parent with constraints). This would quantify how much overdetermination prevention contributes to final performance.
   - **Refiner Agent**: Evaluate `InfoSeeker-3B` with and without the Qwen2.5-7B-Inst summarization step. This would measure whether context compression is essential or whether the 3B model can handle raw search results.
   - **Parallel queries**: Compare multiple queries per step vs. single sequential queries. The current design assumes parallelism improves coverage, but this is not empirically isolated.
   - **Two-round training**: Report performance after Round 1 SFT, Round 1 RL, Round 2 SFT, and Round 2 RL separately. This would identify which stage contributes most to gains.

2. **Alternative Reward Designs**
   
   The paper notes that preserved meta-information (intermediate steps, retrieval labels) "supports advanced optimization strategies, including compound reward design and trajectory-level exploration" (**Abstract**). The current RL uses a binary reward (**Section 4.3.2**):
   
   $$R = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if format and extracted answer are both correct} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$
   
   Follow-up work could explore:
   
   - **Step-level rewards**: Credit for correct intermediate sub-problem solutions, not just final answers
   - **Retrieval quality rewards**: Penalize irrelevant search queries or reward high-precision retrieval
   - **Efficiency rewards**: Penalize excessive search calls to encourage query optimization
   - **Curriculum learning**: Start with 3-vertex problems, progressively increase to 7+ vertices

3. **Cross-Benchmark Generalization**
   
   The model is evaluated on BrowseComp-Plus but trained only on InfoSeek. A critical test is evaluation on **different Deep Research benchmarks** (e.g., WebWalkerQA, Pangu DeepDiver) to determine whether the model learns general hierarchical reasoning or benchmark-specific patterns.

**Medium-Term Extensions (Moderate Feasibility)**

4. **Domain-Specific Adaptation**
   
   The current synthesis uses Wikipedia and general web pages (**Section 3.1**). Specialized domains require adaptation:
   
   - **Medical research**: Synthesize HCSPs from PubMed abstracts, clinical trial databases, medical ontologies
   - **Legal analysis**: Build research trees from case law, statutes, regulatory documents
   - **Technical engineering**: Use specification documents, API references, bug trackers
   
   Each domain would require custom entity extraction, relationship validation, and verifiability checks. The framework is open-source, enabling this adaptation.

5. **Improved Teacher Models**
   
   The SFT process distills from Qwen2.5-72B at **21.8% accuracy** (**Appendix A.1**). This creates a performance ceiling—the student cannot reliably exceed teacher capability through distillation alone. Follow-up work could:
   
   - Use stronger teachers (e.g., GPT-5 at 55.9% on BrowseComp-Plus)
   - Implement **iterative self-improvement**: Use the trained `InfoSeeker-3B` to generate new trajectories, filter for correctness, and retrain
   - Explore **ensemble distillation**: Combine trajectories from multiple teacher models

6. **Open-Ended Task Extension**
   
   The paper explicitly excludes "long-form open-ended tasks such as report writing" (**Section 1**). However, many real Deep Research use cases require synthesis without unique answers. Extension would require:
   
   - New evaluation metrics beyond Exact Match (e.g., rubric-based scoring, human evaluation)
   - Modified synthesis that generates evaluation rubrics alongside questions
   - Different reward structures for RL (not binary correct/incorrect)

**Long-Term Directions (High Risk, High Reward)**

7. **Multi-Modal Deep Research**
   
   Real research often requires interpreting figures, tables, code, and datasets—not just text. Extending `InfoSeek` to multi-modal HCSPs would require:
   
   - Entity extraction from images, charts, code repositories
   - Relationship validation across modalities
   - Multi-modal Refiner Agent capabilities
   
   This is substantially more complex but would broaden applicability significantly.

8. **Dynamic/Time-Sensitive Research**
   
   The current framework uses static Wikipedia dumps and webpage snapshots. Real research often involves time-sensitive queries ("What changed since...", "Current status of..."). Extension would require:
   
   - Temporal reasoning in HCSP formalization
   - Versioned knowledge bases with timestamps
   - Retrieval systems that respect temporal constraints

9. **Collaborative Multi-Agent Research**
   
   The paper mentions "a system of collaborating LLMs" as a possible Deep Research agent (**Section 2.2**) but implements a single model with external refiner. True multi-agent collaboration (debate, verification, division of labor) remains unexplored. The preserved trajectory meta-information could support training specialized sub-agents for different reasoning roles.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

**Who Should Adopt This Approach?**

The `InfoSeek` framework is not universally applicable. Based on the paper's scope and results, the following use cases are well-matched:

| Use Case | Fit | Rationale |
|----------|-----|-----------|
| **Technical due diligence** | High | Requires verifying claims across multiple sources with hierarchical dependencies |
| **Competitive intelligence** | High | Involves synthesizing information from diverse public sources with verifiable conclusions |
| **Academic literature synthesis** | Medium | HCSP structure fits, but open-ended synthesis may exceed current scope |
| **Legal case research** | Medium | Hierarchical reasoning required, but domain adaptation needed |
| **Medical diagnosis support** | Low | Requires domain-specific adaptation; verifiability constraints may be too rigid |
| **Creative writing assistance** | No | Open-ended tasks outside HCSP formalization |

**Deployment Scenarios**

1. **Cost-Constrained Deep Research Deployment**
   
   Organizations needing Deep Research capability but unable to afford commercial API costs can deploy `InfoSeeker-3B` locally. The 3B parameter model runs on modest hardware (single GPU with 16GB+ VRAM), while the 7B Refiner Agent can be served separately. This contrasts with per-query API pricing for Gemini 2.5 Pro or GPT-4.1.

2. **Specialized Domain Agents**
   
   The open-source synthesis framework enables organizations to generate domain-specific HCSP datasets. A financial services firm could synthesize questions about regulatory compliance from SEC filings; a pharmaceutical company could build HCSPs from clinical trial databases. The framework provides the scaffolding; domain experts provide the knowledge sources.

3. **Research and Education**
   
   Academic labs can use the released dataset and framework to:
   
   - Teach agentic reasoning concepts with concrete examples
   - Benchmark new search/retrieval methods on standardized HCSP tasks
   - Explore alternative training pipelines without recreating synthesis infrastructure

4. **Foundation for Iterative Improvement**
   
   Organizations can start with the released 50K samples, then iteratively generate additional domain-specific data using the framework. The two-round training pipeline (**Appendix A.1**) provides a template for incorporating new data without retraining from scratch.

**Integration Pathway**

For practitioners considering adoption, the following pathway minimizes risk:

1. **Start with released dataset**: Download the 50K QA pairs and 16.5K trajectories. Evaluate on your specific use cases before investing in synthesis.

2. **Benchmark baseline performance**: Run `InfoSeeker-3B` (weights released) on your tasks. Compare against your current approach (commercial APIs, in-house models, etc.).

3. **Assess synthesis feasibility**: If custom data is needed, estimate synthesis costs using the $571.8/50K benchmark (**Table 2**). For 10K domain-specific samples, budget ~$115 in API costs.

4. **Verify infrastructure requirements**: Ensure access to H100-class GPUs for training (8×H100 node, 2 hours per Round 1 SFT per **Appendix A.1**) and long-context support (16,384 tokens).

5. **Plan for inference overhead**: Account for both 3B model and 7B Refiner Agent latency in production deployments.

### 7.4 When to Prefer InfoSeek Over Alternatives

**Prefer InfoSeek When:**

- **Task structure is hierarchical**: Your use case involves interdependent sub-problems, not just sequential multi-hop reasoning. The HCSP formalization matches your problem topology.

- **Verifiable answers exist**: Your tasks have ground-truth answers that can be validated. The binary reward design and quality assurance filters depend on this.

- **Budget favors training over inference**: You can invest upfront in data synthesis and model training ($571.8 + compute costs) to reduce per-query API costs long-term.

- **Open-source deployment is required**: Regulatory, privacy, or cost constraints prevent using commercial APIs. The full framework is Apache 2.0 licensed (**Appendix A.4**).

- **You need controllable complexity**: Your evaluation requires systematic variation of reasoning depth. The framework supports generating problems with specific vertex counts (**Table 2**).

**Prefer Alternatives When:**

- **Tasks are open-ended**: Report writing, creative synthesis, or policy recommendations without unique answers fall outside HCSP scope. Consider inference-time frameworks like AgentOrchestra or Agentic Reasoning instead.

- **Domain expertise is critical**: Medical, legal, or technical domains require specialized knowledge not represented in Wikipedia/web corpora. Domain adaptation would be extensive.

- **Latency is paramount**: The two-model inference (3B + 7B) and multi-turn search (8.24 average calls, **Table 4**) introduce latency. Single-pass RAG may be preferable for real-time applications.

- **Training infrastructure is unavailable**: Without H100-class GPUs, reproducing the training pipeline is not feasible. Commercial API fine-tuning or prompt engineering may be more practical.

- **Absolute performance is critical**: 16.5% accuracy on BrowseComp-Plus is strong relative to baselines but fails on 83.5% of tasks. For high-stakes applications, commercial APIs (GPT-5 at 55.9%) may be necessary despite cost.

### 7.5 Summary: The Path Forward

`InfoSeek` establishes a new baseline for Deep Research capability development, but it is a foundation rather than a finished solution. The key takeaways for the community are:

| Dimension | Current State | Future Direction |
|-----------|---------------|------------------|
| **Data Structure** | HCSP formalization established | Extend to DAGs, multi-modal, temporal reasoning |
| **Scale Efficiency** | 3B competes with 32B on HCSP | Close gap with GPT-5 (55.9% vs. 16.5%) |
| **Open Source** | Full framework released | Community extensions to domains and tasks |
| **Training Pipeline** | Two-round SFT + RL documented | Ablation studies, alternative rewards, curriculum learning |
| **Evaluation** | BrowseComp-Plus benchmark | Cross-benchmark generalization, failure analysis |

The most impactful follow-up work will address the **open questions identified in Section 6**: failure mode analysis, component ablations, and extension to open-ended or domain-specific tasks. The released framework provides the scaffolding; the community must build the extensions.

For practitioners, the decision framework is clear: if your task involves **hierarchical reasoning with verifiable answers**, and you can invest in the synthesis and training infrastructure, `InfoSeek` offers a path to competitive Deep Research capability without dependence on commercial APIs. If your tasks fall outside this scope, the framework still provides valuable lessons about **structural alignment between training data and target capabilities**—a principle that likely extends beyond Deep Research to other complex reasoning domains.
