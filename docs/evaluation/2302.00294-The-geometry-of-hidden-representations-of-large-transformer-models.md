## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of identifying semantically rich representations within self-supervised transformer architectures, specifically analyzing ESM-2 protein language models (up to 3B parameters) and iGPT image models (up to 1.4B parameters) trained on ProteinNet and ImageNet. The authors demonstrate that semantic information—such as protein remote homology and image class labels—is maximally encoded at intermediate layers corresponding to a relative minimum in the Intrinsic Dimension (ID) profile, rather than in the final reconstruction layers. This geometric insight provides an unsupervised strategy to select optimal layers for downstream tasks, yielding approximately 6% improvement in protein homology search accuracy without requiring labeled data for layer selection.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Where Does Semantic Information Live in Self-Supervised Transformers?

Self-supervised transformer models have become foundational tools across multiple domains—from protein sequence analysis to image generation to natural language processing. These models are trained without explicit labels, using objectives like masked language modeling (MLM) or next-token prediction to learn from raw data. However, this creates a fundamental ambiguity: **which layer's representation should practitioners use for downstream tasks?**

The paper identifies a critical gap in understanding self-supervised transformer architectures:

> "In models trained by self-supervision, the last representation generally has the role of allowing reconstruction of the input representation. Therefore, the most semantically rich representation is likely to arise within the intermediate hidden layers of the network." (Introduction)

This observation creates a practical problem. Consider a biologist using ESM-2 (a protein language model with up to 3B parameters) to search for remote homologs—proteins that share evolutionary ancestry despite having dissimilar amino acid sequences. Or consider a computer vision researcher using iGPT (an image transformer with up to 1.4B parameters) for image classification. Both face the same question: should they extract features from layer 5, layer 20, or the final layer? Without labeled data to validate each choice, there is no principled way to decide.

### Why This Matters: Real-World and Theoretical Significance

The importance of this problem operates at two levels:

**Practical Impact:**
- **Annotation scarcity**: Large-scale datasets where these transformers excel often have limited or no labels. The paper notes their method enables layer selection "without supervision" for "transformers trained on large datasets where the annotation is scarce" (Introduction).
- **Downstream task performance**: Using suboptimal layers directly impacts task accuracy. The authors demonstrate that searching for protein homologs in plateau layers (where ID is low) improves accuracy by approximately 6% compared to using the last hidden layer (Appendix Section C, Figure S2).
- **Computational efficiency**: Extracting and storing representations from all layers is expensive. Knowing which layers matter reduces storage and computation for deployment.

**Theoretical Significance:**
- **Understanding representation learning**: The paper seeks to characterize "how the semantic structure of the dataset emerges from a sequence of transformations between one representation and the next" (Abstract). This addresses a fundamental question about how deep networks organize information.
- **Geometric interpretation**: By connecting intrinsic dimension to semantic content, the work provides a geometric lens for understanding what happens inside transformers—moving beyond black-box characterization.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

**Supervised Probe Methods:**
Previous work established that intermediate layers can encode semantic information, but required labeled data to identify which layers. Chen et al. [10] demonstrated with linear probes that features from intermediate iGPT layers encode semantic concepts. Similarly, Rives et al. [6] observed that Euclidean distances in the last hidden layer of protein language models encode remote homology information. However, these approaches require:
- Labeled datasets for probe training
- Task-specific validation to compare layers
- Cannot generalize to unlabeled domains

**Convolutional Network Analysis:**
Studies on convolutional architectures provided foundational insights about representation geometry. Ansuini et al. [13] measured intrinsic dimension across CNN layers trained on ImageNet, finding ID values between 13 and 24 at the output, correlated with classifier accuracy. Doimo et al. [14] introduced neighborhood overlap metrics showing that neighbor structure rearranges significantly only in the last layers of supervised CNNs, where representations must comply with ground-truth classification.

However, these findings do not directly transfer to transformers for two reasons:
1. **Architecture differences**: Transformers use self-attention mechanisms rather than convolutional filters, with different information flow patterns.
2. **Training objective differences**: Self-supervised transformers optimize for reconstruction, not classification. As the paper notes, "in deep convolutional models, this rate [of neighborhood change] is significant only in the very last layers [14], where the representation is forced to comply with the ground-truth classification of the images." In contrast, self-supervised transformers show substantial neighborhood rearrangement in early layers (Figure 2).

**NLP Layer Analysis:**
The NLP literature has explored which transformer layers encode different types of linguistic information. Hewitt and Manning [32] showed syntactic information peaks in middle layers using structural probes. However, findings about semantic information localization have been inconsistent across studies [33–37], possibly due to language complexity and rapidly evolving model architectures. The paper notes: "the localization of semantic information has yielded contrasting results [33–37] possibly due to the complexity of language data and the ongoing evolution of model size and architectures" (Discussion).

### How This Paper Positions Itself

This work distinguishes itself from prior approaches through three key contributions:

**1. Unsupervised Layer Selection:**
Unlike probe-based methods requiring labels, this paper proposes using intrinsic dimension profiles as an unsupervised indicator. The key insight: "representations at intermediate layers corresponding to a relative minimum of the ID profile are more suitable for downstream learning tasks" (Abstract). This enables layer selection even when no labeled validation data exists.

**2. Cross-Domain Geometric Analysis:**
The paper analyzes both protein language models (ESM-2 family: 35M, 650M, 3B parameters) and image transformers (iGPT family: 76M, 455M, 1.4B parameters), finding consistent geometric patterns across domains. This cross-domain consistency suggests the findings reflect general principles of transformer representation learning rather than domain-specific artifacts.

**3. Connecting Geometry to Semantics:**
While prior work measured ID in CNNs [13] or analyzed semantic content with probes [10], this paper explicitly connects the two: low intrinsic dimension regions correspond to high semantic content. Figure 4 demonstrates this relationship quantitatively—for iGPT models, the overlap with ground truth labels ($\chi^{l,gt}_k$) peaks at relative depth ~0.4, precisely where ID reaches its minimum (Figure 1, right).

**4. Training Dynamics:**
The paper extends beyond static analysis of trained models to examine how ID profiles evolve during training (Figure 3). This reveals that the ID peak emerges early in training, while the compression to low-ID plateau regions occurs in a second phase—tightly linked to "the emergence of semantic information" (Section 3.1).

By positioning intrinsic dimension as a proxy for semantic richness, this work provides both a practical tool for practitioners and a theoretical framework for understanding how self-supervised transformers organize information across their depth.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an analytical study paper that measures geometric properties of transformer representations rather than building a new system. The core idea is to track how the intrinsic dimension and neighborhood structure of data representations change across transformer layers, using these geometric signatures to identify which layers contain the most semantically meaningful information without requiring labeled data.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The "system" being analyzed consists of pre-trained transformer models (ESM-2 for proteins, iGPT for images) whose internal representations are measured using geometric metrics at each layer. This approach solves the problem of identifying semantically rich layers in self-supervised models by measuring how data manifolds expand and contract through the network depth, rather than relying on labeled validation data to test each layer's performance.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The analysis pipeline operates through four major components working in sequence. First, **pre-trained transformer models** (ESM-2 or iGPT) serve as the source of representations, with each model consisting of a stack of identical self-attention blocks that transform input data through successive layers. Second, a **representation extraction module** pulls hidden state vectors from after the first normalization layer of each block, then applies average pooling along the sequence dimension to convert variable-length sequences into fixed-dimensional vectors in $\mathbb{R}^d$. Third, **geometric measurement tools** compute two key metrics on these extracted representations: the intrinsic dimension (ID) using the TwoNN estimator, and the neighborhood overlap ($\chi$) measuring how nearest neighbor relationships change between layers. Fourth, a **semantic validation layer** compares these geometric measurements against ground-truth labels (protein superfamilies or image classes) to verify that low-ID regions correspond to high semantic content. Information flows unidirectionally: raw sequences or images enter the transformer, representations are extracted at each layer, geometric metrics are computed on these representations, and the resulting profiles are correlated with semantic measures to identify optimal layers.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the intrinsic dimension estimator because it is the primary metric driving the paper's main finding about layer selection.
- We then explain the neighborhood overlap metric because it provides complementary evidence about how representation structure evolves across layers.
- We detail the transformer architectures and representation extraction process because understanding what is being measured is essential before interpreting the measurements.
- We describe the datasets and ground-truth labels because semantic validation requires knowing what "meaningful" representations look like in each domain.
- We conclude with the mathematical formulations and implementation details because these determine the reliability and reproducibility of the geometric measurements.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Methodology: Measuring Representation Geometry Across Layers**

This paper introduces a measurement framework rather than a new learning algorithm, treating trained transformer models as objects of analysis rather than systems to be improved. The fundamental insight is that semantic information compression manifests as geometric compression in the representation space, which can be detected through intrinsic dimension measurements without requiring labeled data. The approach rests on two complementary metrics: intrinsic dimension captures the global geometry of the data manifold at each layer, while neighborhood overlap captures the local rearrangement of data points between consecutive layers.

**Intrinsic Dimension Estimation Using the TwoNN Estimator**

The intrinsic dimension (ID) represents the minimum number of coordinates needed to specify a data point approximately without information loss, effectively measuring the dimensionality of the manifold on which the data lies. The paper employs the TwoNN estimator introduced by Facco et al. [21], which requires only the distances from each point to its first two nearest neighbors. For each data point $x_i$ in the representation space, the method computes $r_{i1}$ (distance to the first nearest neighbor) and $r_{i2}$ (distance to the second nearest neighbor). The ratio $\mu_i = r_{i2} / r_{i1}$ follows a Pareto distribution under the assumption of locally constant density, with the shape parameter equal to the intrinsic dimension $d$. The probability density function is given by:

$$p(\mu_i | d) = d \cdot \mu_i^{-d-1}$$

The intrinsic dimension can be inferred from the cumulative distribution function $F(\mu) = 1 - \mu^{-d}$ using linear regression. Specifically, the ID equals the slope of the line through the origin obtained by regressing $\log(\mu_{\sigma(i)})$ against $-\log(1 - F^{\text{emp}}(\mu_{\sigma(i)}))$, where $\mu_{\sigma(i)}$ are the ratios sorted in ascending order and $F^{\text{emp}}(\mu_{\sigma(i)}) = i/N$ is the empirical cumulative distribution function value. The paper validates the constant density assumption using the Point Adaptive kNN (PAk) method, which revealed that density can be considered constant within the first 6 neighboring data points on average, justifying the use of first and second nearest neighbors for ID estimation. To reduce noise from the estimator's local nature, the authors apply TwoNN on datasets decimated by a factor of 4, selecting the ID value where the estimate exhibits a plateau across different dataset sizes.

**Neighborhood Overlap Metric for Tracking Representation Evolution**

The neighborhood overlap $\chi^{l,m}_k$ measures the similarity between two representations at layers $l$ and $m$ by computing the average fraction of common $k$-nearest neighbors for each data point. For an activation vector $x^l_i$ at layer $l$, the adjacency matrix $A^l$ has entries $A^l_{ij} = 1$ if $x^l_j$ belongs to the first $k$-nearest neighbors of $x^l_i$ in Euclidean distance, and 0 otherwise. The neighborhood overlap between representations $l$ and $m$ is computed as:

$$\chi^{l,m}_k = \frac{1}{N} \sum_i \frac{1}{k} \sum_j A^l_{ij} A^m_{ij}$$

where $A^l_{ij} A^m_{ij}$ counts the number of common $k$-neighbors of point $x_i$ between the two representations. Both $\chi^{l,m}_k$ and $\chi^{l,gt}_k$ (overlap with ground truth) lie in the range $[0, 1]$ and depend on the neighborhood size $k$. The paper uses $k = 10$ when analyzing overlap with protein superfamilies from the SCOPe dataset, and $k = 30$ for transformers trained on ImageNet, consistently with values used in prior work by Doimo et al. [14]. Appendix Figure S5 demonstrates that the qualitative trend of the overlap is robust to changes in $k$ across the range $\{1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 50\}$, though lower values occur at $k = 50$ for ESM-2 because certain superfamilies have fewer than 50 elements.

**Transformer Architectures and Representation Extraction**

The study analyzes two families of transformer models with similar architectural structures but trained on different data modalities. The ESM-2 protein language models (pLMs) use a vocabulary of approximately 20 amino acids, while iGPT models use 512 colors obtained by k-means clustering of RGB pixel values. An embedding layer encodes each sequence token into a vector of size $d$, which is then added to a learned positional embedding common to all sequences. A sequence of $l$ embedded tokens of size $\mathbb{R}^{l \times d}$ is processed by a stack of architecturally identical blocks, each composed of a self-attention map followed by a multi-layer perceptron (MLP). Appendix Table 1 provides the specific configurations: ESM-2 (35M) has 12 blocks with embedding dimension 480 and 20 heads, ESM-2 (650M) has 33 blocks with dimension 1280 and 20 heads, and ESM-2 (3B) has 36 blocks with dimension 2560 and 40 heads. For iGPT models, iGPT-S (76M) has 24 blocks with dimension 512 and 8 heads, iGPT-M (455M) has 36 blocks with dimension 1024 and 8 heads, and iGPT-L (1.4B) has 48 blocks with dimension 1536 and 16 heads.

The paper extracts hidden representations after the first normalization layer of each block, then applies average pooling along the sequence dimension to reduce each sequence into a single data point embedded in $\mathbb{R}^d$. This pooling strategy enables computation of Euclidean distances between sequences of different lengths, which is necessary for protein datasets where sequence length varies. Appendix Section D validates that while average pooling quantitatively decreases the ID (due to the averaging procedure), the fundamental shape of the ID profile does not change qualitatively. The appendix also demonstrates that ID profiles do not quantitatively change if representations are extracted after the attention maps versus after the MLP blocks (Appendix Figure S4), indicating robustness to the specific extraction point within each transformer block.

**Datasets and Ground-Truth Labels for Semantic Validation**

The analysis requires datasets with ground-truth labels to validate that low-ID regions correspond to high semantic content, even though the layer selection method itself is unsupervised. For protein language models, the paper uses two benchmark datasets. ProteinNet contains 25,299 protein sequences for evaluating relationships between sequences and structures, and the training set is used to analyze ID curves and compute neighborhood overlap of consecutive layers. The Astral SCOPe v2.08 dataset contains genetic domain sequences hierarchically classified into fold, superfamily, and family levels. After preprocessing with the filtering procedure from [28], the authors select proteins belonging to superfamilies with at least ten members, ensuring each superfamily consists of at least two families, resulting in 10,256 sequences and 288 superfamilies used to evaluate $\chi^{l,gt}_k$ in Figure 4. For image transformers, the authors select 90,000 images from the ImageNet training set, randomly choosing 300 classes and keeping 300 images per class.

**Design Choices and Rationale**

The choice of intrinsic dimension over alternative complexity measures reflects several considerations. Unlike entropy-based measures that require density estimation in high-dimensional spaces, the TwoNN estimator operates on distance ratios that are robust to changes in curvature and density of the data. The method has been validated effectively for analyzing representations in deep neural networks in prior work [13, 14, 22–24]. The neighborhood overlap metric was selected because it directly measures how the local structure of the data manifold changes between layers, providing complementary information to the global ID measurement. In convolutional architectures, neighborhood rearrangement occurs primarily in the last layers where representations must comply with ground-truth classification, but the paper finds that self-supervised transformers show substantial rearrangement in early layers corresponding to the ID peak phase.

The average pooling strategy, while convenient for handling variable-length sequences, represents a trade-off. Alternative approaches for comparing sequences of variable lengths exist [31, 22], but average pooling provides sufficient biological information to address homology, structural, and evolutionary tasks directly or after fine-tuning. For iGPT where all inputs have the same sequence length, reducing to a $d$-dimensional vector is also computationally necessary: calculating distance matrices for 90,000 images in the full feature space ($l \times d = 524,288$) would be unfeasible given the available computational resources (2 Intel Xeon Gold 6226 processors, 256GB RAM, 2 Nvidia V100 GPUs with 32GB memory).

**Mathematical Connection Between Geometry and Semantics**

The paper's central claim rests on an empirical correlation rather than a theoretical derivation: layers where ID reaches a local minimum correspond to representations where semantic properties are most pronounced. This relationship is quantified through the overlap with ground-truth labels $\chi^{l,gt}_k$, computed using an adjacency matrix $A^{gt}_{ij} = 1$ if $y_i = y_j$ (same class) and 0 otherwise. For protein remote homology detection, the "gt" representation excludes neighbors in the same family to focus specifically on remote homology at the superfamily level. Figure 4 demonstrates this correlation quantitatively: for ESM-2 models, $\chi^{l,gt}_k$ reaches a stationary maximum of approximately 0.8 in the plateau phase where ID is low (between 5 and 7), then decreases to 0.4 in the final ascent phase. For iGPT models, the peak value of $\chi^{l,gt}_k$ is 0.15 in iGPT-S, 0.27 in iGPT-M, and 0.35 in iGPT-L, all occurring at relative depth ~0.4 where ID reaches its minimum.

**Training Dynamics and Checkpoint Analysis**

To understand how the geometric properties emerge during training rather than only in converged models, the paper analyzes checkpoints at different training iterations. For ESM-2 (650M), checkpoints correspond to $[0, 0.1, 0.3, 1, 5] \times 10^5$ training iterations, with the last checkpoint representing the fully converged model. Figure 3 (left) shows that in initial training stages, a peak forms rapidly in early layers while the ID curve in remaining layers resembles an untrained model. Between $1 \times 10^4$ and $3 \times 10^4$ training steps, the ID of plateau layers substantially decreases, while ID in final layers progressively increases toward the input ID. For iGPT-L, checkpoints at $[0, 0.13, 0.26, 0.52, 1] \times 10^6$ training iterations show similar dynamics: before training the ID curve is flat, the first ID peak emerges in early layers before $1.3 \times 10^5$ iterations, and a second peak emerges in the final third of hidden layers during later training phases. This two-stage emergence (peak first, then compression) suggests that semantic compression is a distinct learning phase rather than a byproduct of initial feature expansion.

**Reproducibility and Implementation Details**

All experiments are performed on a machine with 2 Intel(R) Xeon(R) Gold 6226 processors, 256GB of RAM, and 2 Nvidia V100 GPUs with 32GB memory. The authors provide code to reproduce experiments and analysis at github.com/diegodoimo/geometry_representations. The ESM-2 models were trained by Lin et al. [15] on the Uniref50 dataset with a masked language model objective and are publicly released at github.com/facebookresearch/esm. The iGPT networks were trained by Chen et al. [10] with a next token prediction objective on the ImageNet dataset and are available at github.com/openai/image-gpt. For the intrinsic dimension estimation, the authors adopt the TwoNN implementation from DADApy [25], with technical details and validation of the constant density assumption provided in Appendix Section B.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the paper's most novel contributions, distinguishing between incremental refinements of existing methods and fundamental advances in understanding transformer representation learning. Each insight is anchored to specific evidence from the results and positioned against prior work discussed in Section 2.

---

### 4.1 Unsupervised Layer Selection via Intrinsic Dimension Minima

**What is novel:** The paper introduces the first principled, unsupervised method for identifying semantically rich layers in self-supervised transformers. Prior approaches required labeled validation data to test each layer's performance with linear probes [10] or task-specific fine-tuning [6]. This method requires only the model's internal representations—no labels needed.

**Why it matters:** This addresses a critical practical bottleneck. As noted in Section 2, large-scale datasets where transformers excel often have scarce or no annotations. The paper demonstrates this works quantitatively:

> "Searching for the closest homologs in the plateau layers where protein relationships are better expressed can improve state-of-the-art methods based on representations from the last hidden layer. The Appendix shows that the entity of such improvement is approximately 6%" (Section 3.2, referencing Figure S2).

This 6% gain is achieved **without any additional training or labeled validation**—simply by extracting features from layers where ID reaches a local minimum rather than the default final layer.

**Distinction from prior work:** Ansuini et al. [13] measured ID in CNNs but did not propose it as a layer selection criterion. Chen et al. [10] showed intermediate iGPT layers encode semantics but required linear probes with labels to identify which layers. This paper closes the loop: ID minima *predict* semantic richness without probes.

**Innovation level:** **Fundamental.** This changes how practitioners can deploy self-supervised models in label-scarce domains (e.g., rare disease genomics, specialized scientific imaging).

---

### 4.2 Cross-Domain Geometric Consistency Reveals General Learning Principles

**What is novel:** The paper demonstrates that ESM-2 protein language models and iGPT image transformers—trained on completely different data modalities with different objectives (masked language modeling vs. next-token prediction)—exhibit remarkably similar ID profiles. Figure 1 shows both model families display an early ID peak followed by compression to lower dimensions.

**Why it matters:** This consistency suggests transformers learn a **domain-agnostic computational strategy** for self-supervised reconstruction, not architecture-specific or data-specific artifacts. The paper explicitly states:

> "We find that the representations evolve similarly in transformers trained on protein language tasks and image reconstruction tasks" (Abstract).

The quantitative agreement is striking: plateau phase ID values cluster between 5–7 for ESM-2 models regardless of embedding dimension (480 to 2560), and iGPT minimum ID values (~22) align with CNN classifier outputs on ImageNet [13].

**Distinction from prior work:** Previous geometric analyses focused on single domains—either CNNs on images [13, 14] or language models on text [32–37]. No prior work systematically compared representation geometry across such different modalities. The cross-domain consistency strengthens the claim that these are general principles of transformer learning rather than domain-specific phenomena.

**Innovation level:** **Fundamental.** This provides evidence for universal representation learning dynamics in transformers, supporting theoretical frameworks about how self-supervision shapes internal representations.

---

### 4.3 Discovery of a Three-Phase Computational Strategy

**What is novel:** The paper identifies a consistent three-phase processing pattern across all analyzed models:

1. **Expansion phase:** Data manifold expands to high intrinsic dimension in early layers (ID peak)
2. **Compression phase:** Representations contract to low-dimensional, semantically meaningful manifolds (ID minimum/plateau)
3. **Decoding phase:** ID increases again as the model prepares for reconstruction (final layers)

This pattern emerges from simultaneous analysis of ID profiles (Figure 1) and neighborhood overlap dynamics (Figure 2).

**Why it matters:** This provides a **mechanistic interpretation** of what self-supervised transformers compute, analogous to understanding an autoencoder's encoder-decoder structure. The paper draws an explicit parallel:

> "Large transformers behave essentially like sophisticated autoencoders, in which the data are first encoded into a low-dimensional and abstract representation and are successively decoded from it" (Section 4).

The expansion phase resembles kernel methods that implicitly expand feature spaces [16], while the compression phase extracts abstract semantics. This framing helps explain *why* intermediate layers outperform final layers for downstream tasks: the final layers prioritize reconstruction fidelity over semantic abstraction.

**Distinction from prior work:** Doimo et al. [14] observed neighborhood rearrangement in CNNs but only in final layers during supervised training. This paper shows self-supervised transformers rearrange neighbors early (during expansion) and again late (during decoding), with stable semantics in between. The three-phase model is a new conceptual framework for transformer internals.

**Innovation level:** **Fundamental.** This provides a theoretical lens for interpreting transformer representations that extends beyond empirical observation to mechanistic understanding.

---

### 4.4 Training Dynamics Reveal Two-Stage Emergence of Semantic Compression

**What is novel:** The paper analyzes how ID profiles evolve *during training*, not just in converged models. Figure 3 reveals a two-stage learning process:

1. **Stage 1 (early training):** The ID peak emerges rapidly in early layers while remaining layers resemble an untrained model
2. **Stage 2 (mid-training):** Plateau layers compress to low ID while final layers expand toward input ID

For ESM-2 (650M), the plateau compression occurs between $1 \times 10^4$ and $3 \times 10^4$ training steps—after the peak has already formed.

**Why it matters:** This shows that **semantic compression is a distinct learning phase**, not a byproduct of initial feature learning. The paper explicitly connects this to semantics:

> "This compression is tightly related to the emergence of semantic information that we discuss in Sec. 3.2" (Section 3.1).

This has implications for understanding training dynamics, checkpoint selection, and potentially early stopping criteria. If semantic content emerges during the compression phase, monitoring ID during training could indicate when models have learned meaningful representations.

**Distinction from prior work:** Prior geometric analyses [13, 14] examined only fully trained models. This paper's training dynamics analysis reveals *when* and *how* the geometric structure emerges, providing temporal resolution absent from static analyses.

**Innovation level:** **Incremental but significant.** While building on existing ID estimation methods, the training dynamics analysis adds a new dimension to understanding representation learning that prior static analyses could not capture.

---

### 4.5 Quantitative Correlation Between Geometry and Semantics

**What is novel:** The paper establishes an explicit, quantitative relationship between intrinsic dimension and semantic content across layers. Figure 4 shows that overlap with ground-truth labels ($\chi^{l,gt}_k$) peaks precisely where ID reaches its minimum—not at the final layer, not at random intermediate points, but at the geometric compression point.

**Why it matters:** Prior work measured ID [13] OR semantic content [10] separately. This paper demonstrates they are **inversely correlated**: lower ID corresponds to higher semantic overlap. For iGPT-L, the peak $\chi^{l,gt}_k = 0.35$ occurs at relative depth ~0.4, exactly where Figure 1 shows the ID minimum. For ESM-2, remote homology overlap reaches $\chi^{l,gt}_k \sim 0.8$ in the plateau phase, then drops to 0.4 in the final layer.

This provides empirical validation for the theoretical intuition that semantic abstraction involves compression—formalizing the "information bottleneck" concept in transformer representations.

**Distinction from prior work:** Rives et al. [6] showed last-layer embeddings encode homology but did not compare across layers. Chen et al. [10] used linear probes to find semantic layers but required labels. This paper shows geometry *predicts* semantics without labels, with quantitative correlation curves.

**Innovation level:** **Fundamental.** This establishes intrinsic dimension as a proxy for semantic richness, enabling unsupervised diagnosis of representation quality.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section details the empirical validation of the paper's central hypothesis: that geometric properties (Intrinsic Dimension and neighborhood structure) predict semantic richness in self-supervised transformers. We break down the experimental setup, present the quantitative results linking geometry to semantics, and evaluate the robustness of these findings through ablation studies and cross-domain comparisons.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

The experiments are designed to test whether geometric compression (low ID) correlates with semantic abstraction across different data modalities. The evaluation relies on three components: pre-trained models, benchmark datasets with ground-truth labels, and geometric metrics.

**Models and Architectures**
The study analyzes two families of transformer models to ensure cross-domain validity. Specific architectural details are provided in **Appendix Table 1**:
*   **Protein Language Models (pLMs):** The ESM-2 family with three scales:
    *   Small: 35M parameters, 12 blocks, embedding dimension $d=480$.
    *   Medium: 650M parameters, 33 blocks, embedding dimension $d=1280$.
    *   Large: 3B parameters, 36 blocks, embedding dimension $d=2560$.
*   **Image Transformers (iGPT):** Three scales trained on ImageNet:
    *   Small (S): 76M parameters, 24 blocks, $d=512$.
    *   Medium (M): 455M parameters, 36 blocks, $d=1024$.
    *   Large (L): 1.4B parameters, 48 blocks, $d=1536$.

**Datasets and Ground Truth**
To validate semantic content, the authors require datasets with known labels, even though the layer selection method itself is unsupervised.
*   **ProteinNet:** Used for analyzing ID curves and neighborhood overlap dynamics. Contains 25,299 protein sequences.
*   **SCOPe (Astral v2.08):** Used for semantic validation ($\chi^{l,gt}_k$). After filtering for superfamilies with $\ge 10$ members and $\ge 2$ families, the dataset contains 10,256 sequences across 288 superfamilies. This allows testing for **remote homology** (structural similarity despite sequence dissimilarity).
*   **ImageNet:** A subset of 90,000 images is used for iGPT analysis. The authors randomly select 300 classes and keep 300 images per class to make distance matrix computation feasible.

**Metrics and Hyperparameters**
*   **Intrinsic Dimension (ID):** Estimated using the TwoNN estimator on representations average-pooled along the sequence dimension.
*   **Neighborhood Overlap ($\chi$):** Computed with specific neighborhood sizes $k$ to balance local sensitivity and statistical stability:
    *   $k=10$ for protein superfamilies (SCOPe).
    *   $k=30$ for ImageNet classes.
*   **Relative Depth:** All layer indices are normalized by the total number of blocks (e.g., layer 12 in a 36-block model is relative depth $12/36 \approx 0.33$) to allow comparison across model sizes.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

The experiments yield three primary sets of results: the shape of the ID profile, the dynamics of neighborhood rearrangement, and the correlation between geometry and semantic labels.

**1. The Intrinsic Dimension Profile (Figure 1)**
The ID profiles reveal a consistent geometric structure across all models, contradicting the assumption that representation complexity monotonically decreases or increases with depth.
*   **Expansion Phase:** In early layers (relative depth $< 0.3$), the ID rapidly increases. For ESM-2 3B, the ID peaks at approximately 32. For iGPT-L, the peak reaches 32 as well.
*   **Compression Phase:** Following the peak, the ID contracts significantly.
    *   In pLMs, the ID stabilizes into a **plateau** with values between 5 and 7. Remarkably, this low ID range is consistent across model sizes (35M to 3B), despite embedding dimensions varying from 480 to 2560.
    *   In iGPT models, the ID reaches a local minimum around relative depth 0.4. For iGPT-L, this minimum is approximately 22.
*   **Final Ascent:** In the last layers, the ID increases again. For pLMs, it returns close to the input ID. For iGPT-L, it forms a second, shallower peak before the output.

**2. Neighborhood Rearrangement Dynamics (Figure 2)**
The neighborhood overlap $\chi^{l,l+1}_k$ measures how much the local structure changes between consecutive layers. This metric validates that the ID phases correspond to distinct computational activities.
*   **High Rearrangement (Early Layers):** During the ID peak phase (first 40% of layers), $\chi^{l,l+1}_k \approx 0.5$ for pLMs and $\approx 0.7$ for iGPTs. This indicates that roughly half (or more) of the nearest neighbors change at every layer, signaling a profound reorganization of the data manifold.
*   **Stability (Intermediate Layers):** During the ID plateau/minimum, stability increases dramatically. For larger pLMs, over 90% of neighbors are shared between consecutive layers ($\chi^{l,l+1}_k > 0.9$). This suggests the representation stabilizes semantically during this phase.
*   **Final Rearrangement:** In the last layers, $\chi^{l,l+1}_k$ drops again as the model prepares for reconstruction, mirroring the final ID ascent.

**3. Semantic Correlation (Figure 4)**
This is the critical experiment linking geometry to meaning. The authors compute the overlap with ground-truth labels ($\chi^{l,gt}_k$) across layers.
*   **Protein Remote Homology:** For ESM-2 models, the overlap with SCOPe superfamilies rises during the ID peak and reaches a stationary maximum of $\chi^{l,gt}_k \sim 0.8$ in the plateau phase. Crucially, this score drops to $\sim 0.4$ in the final hidden layer. This indicates that the **last layer is significantly worse** at capturing remote homology than the intermediate plateau layers.
*   **Image Classification:** For iGPT models, the overlap with ImageNet labels peaks at relative depth $\sim 0.4$, coinciding with the ID minimum. The peak overlap scales with model size:
    *   iGPT-S: 0.15
    *   iGPT-M: 0.27
    *   iGPT-L: 0.35
*   **Interpretation:** The data confirms that semantic information is maximized where the intrinsic dimension is minimized. The final layers, optimized for pixel/token reconstruction, sacrifice semantic abstraction for detail recovery.

### 5.3 Validation of Unsupervised Layer Selection

The paper claims that ID minima can identify optimal layers without labeled data. To validate this, the authors compare downstream task performance using features from different layers.

**Protein Homology Search (Appendix Figure S2)**
The authors mimic a remote homology search task using the ProtT5-XL-U50 model (a related pLM) on SCOPe domains.
*   **Baseline:** Using representations from the last hidden layer (standard practice).
*   **Proposed Method:** Using representations from a plateau layer (identified via ID minimum).
*   **Result:** Searching for closest homologs in the plateau layer improves accuracy by approximately **6%** compared to the last layer.
*   **Significance:** This gain is achieved **without any fine-tuning or labeled validation** to select the layer. It demonstrates that the geometric signal (low ID) is a reliable proxy for task performance.

**Training Dynamics (Figure 3)**
To ensure these geometric properties are learned features rather than architectural artifacts, the authors analyze checkpoints during training.
*   **ESM-2 (650M):** The ID peak forms early (within $10^4$ steps). The compression to the low-ID plateau occurs later (between $1 \times 10^4$ and $3 \times 10^4$ steps).
*   **iGPT-L:** Similar dynamics. The first peak emerges early; the local minimum at depth 0.4 forms later; a second peak emerges in the final third during later training phases.
*   **Implication:** The semantic compression is an active learning phase, not a static property of the initialization.

### 5.4 Robustness and Ablation Studies

The authors conduct several checks to ensure the findings are not artifacts of specific implementation choices.

**1. Impact of Average Pooling (Appendix Figure S3)**
Since sequences vary in length, the authors average-pool representations to a fixed vector size $d$.
*   **Concern:** Pooling might artificially reduce dimensionality.
*   **Test:** Compared ID profiles on CIFAR10 using a Wide-ResNet28-8 with and without average pooling.
*   **Result:** Pooling quantitatively lowers the ID (downward shift), but the **qualitative shape** (bell curve/peak structure) remains unchanged. This validates that the observed phases are not caused by the pooling operation.

**2. Extraction Point Within Blocks (Appendix Figure S4)**
Representations can be extracted after the attention mechanism or after the normalization layer.
*   **Test:** Compared ID and $\chi^{l,gt}_k$ profiles for iGPT-L extracting post-attention vs. post-normalization.
*   **Result:** Profiles are consistent. This indicates the geometric properties are stable across the internal structure of a transformer block.

**3. Neighborhood Size Sensitivity (Appendix Figure S5)**
The overlap metric depends on the hyperparameter $k$.
*   **Test:** Varied $k \in \{1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 50\}$ for ESM-2 and iGPT.
*   **Result:** The qualitative trends (high rearrangement early/late, stability in middle) remain robust across all $k$. Lower values at $k=50$ for proteins are attributed to some superfamilies having fewer than 50 members, not a methodological failure.

**4. Cross-Architecture Consistency (Appendix Figure S1)**
*   **Test:** Analyzed other pLM families (ProtBert, ProtT5, ESM-1v) trained on different datasets (UniRef90, UniRef100).
*   **Result:** All exhibit the three-phase ID behavior (peak, plateau, ascent). This confirms the phenomenon is general to self-supervised transformers, not specific to ESM-2.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

The experiments provide strong support for the paper's claims, though certain limitations and conditions apply.

**Strengths**
*   **Cross-Domain Validation:** The consistency between protein sequences (1D discrete tokens) and images (2D spatial data) is compelling evidence that the three-phase strategy is a general property of self-supervised transformers.
*   **Quantitative Correlation:** The alignment between ID minima and semantic peaks (Figure 4) is precise. The drop in protein homology performance from 0.8 to 0.4 overlap between plateau and final layers is a stark demonstration that "deeper is not always better."
*   **Unsupervised Utility:** The 6% improvement in homology search (Appendix Fig S2) validates the practical utility of the method in label-scarce environments.

**Limitations and Conditions**
*   **NLP Complexity:** The paper notes in the Discussion that Natural Language Processing (NLP) models show more complex profiles. Preliminary analysis on Llama-2-70B (Appendix Figure S6) shows **three peaks and two minima**. While the first minimum still aligns with sentiment semantics, the geometry is less clean than in pLMs or iGPTs. This suggests the method may require more nuanced interpretation for language tasks.
*   **Computational Cost:** Estimating ID via pairwise distances scales poorly with dataset size ($O(N^2)$). The authors had to subsample ImageNet to 90,000 images because computing distance matrices for the full feature space ($l \times d = 524,288$) was unfeasible on their hardware (2 Nvidia V100 GPUs). This limits the method's applicability to massive datasets without further approximation.
*   **Pooling Artifacts:** While Appendix Fig S3 shows qualitative robustness, average pooling does quantitatively reduce ID. For tasks requiring token-level precision (e.g., named entity recognition), the pooled representation might not be suitable, even if the layer selection is correct.

**Conclusion on Experimental Validity**
The experiments convincingly demonstrate that intrinsic dimension is a viable unsupervised proxy for semantic richness in vision and protein transformers. The consistency across model sizes (35M to 3B) and domains strengthens the claim that this is a fundamental learning dynamic. However, the increased complexity observed in large language models (Llama-2) indicates that while the principle holds, the geometric signature may become more complex as tasks and data modalities increase in complexity.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper establishes intrinsic dimension as a powerful unsupervised diagnostic tool for transformer representations, several important limitations and trade-offs constrain its applicability. Understanding these boundaries is essential for practitioners who wish to apply these findings to new domains or model architectures.

### 6.1 Assumptions Underlying the Intrinsic Dimension Estimator

The TwoNN estimator relies on mathematical assumptions that may not hold universally:

**Local Constant Density Assumption:**
The TwoNN method assumes that data density remains approximately constant within the neighborhood of each point. The paper validates this using the Point Adaptive kNN (PAk) method, finding that "on average, the density can be considered constant within the first 6 neighboring data points" (Appendix Section B). However, this validation is performed on their specific datasets (ProteinNet, SCOPe, ImageNet subset). In domains with highly non-uniform data distributions—such as long-tail class distributions in natural language or imbalanced medical imaging datasets—this assumption may fail, potentially biasing ID estimates.

**Pareto Distribution of Distance Ratios:**
The estimator assumes the ratio $\mu_i = r_{i2} / r_{i1}$ follows a Pareto distribution $p(\mu_i | d) = d \cdot \mu_i^{-d-1}$ (Appendix Section B). This derivation assumes a Poisson point process in the embedding space. If representations exhibit clustering or hierarchical structure that violates this assumption, the ID estimates may be systematically biased. The paper acknowledges this by applying multiscale analysis (dataset decimation) to identify stable plateaus, but this adds computational overhead.

### 6.2 Domain-Specific Variations in ID Profiles

The clean three-phase pattern observed in protein and image transformers does not generalize uniformly across all domains:

**Natural Language Complexity:**
The paper's preliminary analysis of Llama-2-70B reveals significantly more complex geometry:

> "In this case, the evolution of the ID profile is more complex, showing three peaks and two local minima across the hidden layers" (Discussion, referencing Appendix Figure S6).

While the first local minimum still aligns with sentiment semantics (overlap peaks at 0.3 relative depth), the presence of multiple minima complicates layer selection. Practitioners working with large language models cannot simply apply the "minimum ID = best layer" heuristic without additional validation. The paper explicitly states: "After the second peak, the ID profile shows a more complex behavior, which we are currently investigating" (Discussion).

**Training Objective Differences:**
The ID profile shape varies between masked language modeling (pLMs) and next-token prediction (iGPTs):
- pLMs show a three-phase pattern with final ID ascent returning toward input values
- iGPTs show a four-phase pattern with a second shallow peak in the final third

The paper attributes this to "distinct training objectives... and the different data modalities used for training" (Discussion). This means practitioners cannot assume identical ID profiles across different self-supervised objectives, even within the same data modality.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

The geometric analysis approach has significant computational costs that limit its applicability:

**Pairwise Distance Computation:**
Computing intrinsic dimension requires pairwise distance matrices, which scale as $O(N^2)$ with dataset size. The paper explicitly notes this constraint:

> "Calculating distance matrices for many images (e.g., 90,000) in the entire feature space ($l \times d = 524,288$) would have been unfeasible given our current computational resources" (Discussion).

The authors subsampled ImageNet to 90,000 images (300 classes × 300 images) specifically due to this limitation. Their hardware configuration (2 Nvidia V100 GPUs with 32GB memory, 256GB RAM) represents substantial resources, yet still proved insufficient for full-scale analysis. For practitioners working with million-scale datasets, this method requires either:
- Significant subsampling (potentially losing rare class information)
- Approximate nearest neighbor methods (introducing estimation error)
- Access to specialized high-memory computing infrastructure

**Model Size Scaling:**
While the paper analyzes models up to 3B parameters (ESM-2) and 1.4B parameters (iGPT-L), modern foundation models now exceed 70B parameters (Llama-2) and even 175B+ parameters. Extracting and storing representations from all layers of such models requires substantial memory. The paper does not address how the method scales to these larger models beyond the preliminary Llama-2 analysis in Appendix Figure S6.

### 6.4 Representation Extraction Trade-offs

The choice of how to extract representations from transformers introduces several trade-offs:

**Average Pooling Effects:**
To handle variable-length sequences (proteins), the authors apply average pooling along the sequence dimension. Appendix Figure S3 shows this "quantitatively decreases the ID" while preserving qualitative shape. However, this pooling operation:
- Loses token-level positional information
- May obscure local semantic structures important for tasks like named entity recognition or residue-level protein function prediction
- The paper acknowledges: "other approaches for comparing sequences of variable lengths have been explored" (Discussion), citing [31] and [22], but does not compare these alternatives

**Extraction Point Within Blocks:**
The paper extracts representations "after the first normalization layer of each block" (Section 2.3). Appendix Figure S4 shows consistency between post-attention and post-normalization extractions for iGPT-L. However, this validation is limited to one model family. Different transformer variants (e.g., Pre-LN vs. Post-LN architectures) may show different sensitivities to extraction point choice.

### 6.5 Validation Requires Some Labeled Data

While the layer selection method itself is unsupervised, validating that low-ID layers are semantically rich requires ground-truth labels:

> "The analysis we performed could be further reinforced, for example, by analyzing the similarity of representations in the ID minima with those generated in models trained in a supervised setting" (Discussion).

The paper's semantic validation experiments (Figure 4) rely on:
- SCOPe superfamily labels for proteins (288 superfamilies)
- ImageNet class labels for images (300 classes)

In truly label-scarce domains where no ground truth exists, practitioners cannot validate whether the ID minimum corresponds to meaningful semantics—they must trust the geometric signal alone. The 6% improvement in homology search (Appendix Figure S2) was validated on SCOPe, which has established labels. For novel domains without any labeled benchmarks, this validation step is impossible.

### 6.6 Open Questions and Unaddressed Scenarios

Several important questions remain unresolved:

**Why Does the Second Peak Emerge in iGPT?**
The paper observes a second shallow ID peak in large iGPT models but not in pLMs:

> "This makes this model akin to a symmetric autoencoder, in which the decoder performs operations on the representations that are dual to those performed by the encoder" (Discussion).

However, the mechanistic explanation for why this symmetry emerges in image transformers but not protein transformers remains speculative. Understanding this difference could inform architecture design decisions.

**Relationship to Supervised Representations:**
The paper notes that ID values at the minimum (~22 for iGPT) are "compatible with the values observed by Ansuini et al. [13] at the output of a broad range of convolutional classifiers trained on the ImageNet dataset" (Section 3.1). However, direct comparison of representation similarity between self-supervised ID minima and supervised classifier outputs is not performed. This comparison would strengthen the claim that self-supervised models learn similar semantic abstractions to supervised models.

**Task-Specific Optimal Layers:**
The paper demonstrates that ID minima work well for remote homology search and image classification. However, different downstream tasks may require different types of information:
- Fine-grained discrimination tasks might benefit from higher-ID representations
- Generation tasks might require the reconstruction-focused final layers
- The paper does not explore whether ID profiles can predict optimal layers for diverse task types beyond classification and similarity search

**Dynamic Training Monitoring:**
Figure 3 shows ID profiles evolve during training, with semantic compression occurring in a distinct mid-training phase. The paper suggests this "could indicate when models have learned meaningful representations" but does not develop this into a practical training monitoring tool. Questions remain:
- Can ID monitoring enable early stopping?
- Does the compression phase timing correlate with downstream task performance?
- How does this interact with learning rate schedules and other training hyperparameters?

### 6.7 Summary of Practical Trade-offs

| Aspect | Benefit | Cost/Limitation |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| Unsupervised layer selection | No labels needed for selection | Validation still requires some labels |
| ID as semantic proxy | Works across protein and image domains | More complex in language models (multiple minima) |
| TwoNN estimator | Robust to density variations | Requires $O(N^2)$ distance computation |
| Average pooling | Handles variable-length sequences | Loses token-level information; quantitatively reduces ID |
| Cross-domain consistency | Suggests general learning principles | Profile shapes vary by training objective |

The paper's contributions are significant within their validated scope—protein language models and image transformers up to ~3B parameters. However, practitioners should treat the ID-minimum heuristic as a strong prior rather than a universal rule, particularly when working with language models, extremely large architectures, or domains with non-uniform data distributions. The method's computational requirements also mean it is best suited for offline analysis of selected models rather than real-time deployment monitoring.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work fundamentally shifts how practitioners approach feature extraction in self-supervised transformers. By establishing intrinsic dimension (ID) as a unsupervised proxy for semantic richness, the paper moves the field away from heuristic defaults (e.g., "always use the last layer") toward geometry-guided diagnostics. This section outlines how these findings reshape the research landscape, identifies concrete avenues for future investigation, and provides actionable guidance for integrating these methods into real-world workflows.

### 7.1 Shifting the Paradigm: From Depth to Geometry

The most immediate implication of this work is the invalidation of the assumption that deeper layers always yield better representations in self-supervised models. Prior to this study, the standard practice was to extract features from the final hidden layer, assuming that deeper processing equates to higher abstraction. The paper demonstrates that this is incorrect for reconstruction-based objectives.

**The Geometric Diagnostic:**
The ID profile serves as a diagnostic tool analogous to a loss curve, but for representation quality rather than optimization progress.
*   **Previous Assumption:** Semantic information accumulates monotonically with depth.
*   **New Understanding:** Semantic information peaks at geometric compression points (ID minima/plateaus) and degrades during reconstruction phases.
*   **Evidence:** In protein language models, the overlap with remote homology labels drops from $\chi^{l,gt}_k \sim 0.8$ in the plateau phase to $\sim 0.4$ in the final layer (Section 3.2, Figure 4).

This shift enables **unsupervised model auditing**. Practitioners can now analyze a trained model without any labeled data to determine if it has learned meaningful abstractions. If the ID profile lacks a clear compression phase (e.g., remains flat or high throughout), it suggests the model may not have learned structured semantic representations, even if the training loss is low.

### 7.2 Enabling New Research Avenues

The findings open several specific lines of inquiry that extend beyond the scope of the current paper. These directions address the limitations identified in Section 6 and leverage the mechanistic insights from Section 4.

**1. Resolving Natural Language Complexity**
The paper notes that large language models (LLMs) exhibit more complex geometric signatures than protein or image transformers. Preliminary analysis on Llama-2-70B shows "three peaks and two local minima across the hidden layers" (Discussion, Appendix Figure S6).
*   **Open Question:** Do multiple minima correspond to different types of linguistic information (e.g., syntax vs. semantics vs. pragmatics)?
*   **Future Work:** Systematic probing of each minimum in LLMs to map geometric features to linguistic capabilities. The paper observes the first minimum aligns with sentiment overlap, but the function of subsequent peaks remains "currently investigating" (Discussion).

**2. Training Dynamics as Early Stopping Criteria**
Figure 3 reveals that semantic compression occurs in a distinct mid-training phase, separate from the initial feature expansion.
*   **Observation:** For ESM-2 (650M), the plateau compression occurs between $1 \times 10^4$ and $3 \times 10^4$ training steps, after the initial ID peak forms (Section 3.1).
*   **Future Work:** Develop monitoring tools that track ID during training. If compression stalls or fails to emerge, it may indicate optimization issues before validation loss reflects them. This could enable **geometry-based early stopping**, reducing compute costs for large-scale pre-training.

**3. Architecture Design for Compression**
The consistency of the three-phase strategy (Expansion $\rightarrow$ Compression $\rightarrow$ Decoding) suggests it is a computational necessity for self-supervised reconstruction.
*   **Hypothesis:** Architectures that facilitate faster or more stable compression might learn semantic representations more efficiently.
*   **Future Work:** Experiment with architectural modifications (e.g., bottleneck layers, attention head adjustments) to encourage earlier ID minima. The paper notes the ID minimum for iGPT (~22) aligns with supervised CNN outputs [13], suggesting a target dimensionality for optimal semantic encoding.

**4. Connection to Supervised Representations**
The Discussion suggests reinforcing the analysis by "analyzing the similarity of representations in the ID minima with those generated in models trained in a supervised setting."
*   **Future Work:** Directly compare the geometry of self-supervised ID minima against supervised classifier outputs. If they converge to similar manifolds, it would confirm that self-supervision implicitly learns supervised-like features without labels.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The unsupervised nature of this method makes it particularly valuable in domains where labeled data is scarce or expensive to obtain.

**1. Protein Science and Drug Discovery**
*   **Use Case:** Remote homology search for functional annotation of novel proteins.
*   **Impact:** Using plateau layer representations improves accuracy by approximately **6%** compared to last-layer representations (Appendix Figure S2).
*   **Context:** In rare disease genomics, labeled structures are limited. This method allows researchers to maximize search sensitivity without needing labeled validation sets to tune layer selection.
*   **Implementation:** Extract features from layers where ID stabilizes between 5 and 7 (for ESM-2 family), rather than the final layer (Section 3.1).

**2. Specialized Computer Vision**
*   **Use Case:** Feature extraction for classification in specialized imaging (e.g., medical imaging, satellite data) where ImageNet pre-training is used but target labels are few.
*   **Impact:** For iGPT-L, semantic overlap peaks at relative depth ~0.4 where ID is minimal (Figure 4).
*   **Context:** Practitioners fine-tuning iGPT or similar models can freeze layers up to the ID minimum as a robust feature extractor, avoiding the reconstruction-focused layers that add noise to semantic tasks.

**3. Model Selection and Debugging**
*   **Use Case:** Choosing between multiple pre-trained checkpoints or architectures.
*   **Impact:** A model that exhibits a clearer, lower ID plateau may encode more robust semantics than one with a noisy profile.
*   **Context:** When selecting a model for deployment in label-scarce environments, compute the ID profile on a small unlabeled subset. Prefer models with distinct compression phases.

### 7.4 Integration Guidance for Practitioners

To apply these findings effectively, practitioners must understand when the method is appropriate and how to implement it efficiently.

**When to Prefer This Method:**
*   **Self-Supervised Models:** The method is designed for models trained on reconstruction objectives (MLM, next-token prediction). Supervised models may exhibit different geometry (e.g., rearrangement only in final layers [14]).
*   **Label-Scarce Domains:** If you have no labeled validation data to test layer performance, ID profiling is the only principled selection method available.
*   **Sequence and Image Data:** The method is validated for protein sequences (1D) and images (2D). Use with caution for other modalities until validated.

**When to Exercise Caution:**
*   **Massive Datasets:** ID estimation via TwoNN requires pairwise distance matrices, scaling as $O(N^2)$. The authors subsampled ImageNet to 90,000 images due to memory constraints (2 Nvidia V100 GPUs, 256GB RAM) (Section 2.3, Discussion). For datasets $>10^5$ samples, use subsampling or approximate nearest neighbor methods.
*   **Token-Level Tasks:** The analysis uses average pooling along the sequence dimension to handle variable lengths (Section 2.3). This loses positional information. For tasks like named entity recognition or residue-level function prediction, token-level ID estimation is needed but computationally heavier.
*   **Complex Language Models:** For LLMs (e.g., Llama-2), the presence of multiple minima (Appendix Figure S6) requires careful interpretation. Do not assume a single minimum exists; probe multiple candidates.

**Implementation Checklist:**
1.  **Extract Representations:** Pull hidden states after the normalization layer of each block (Section 2.3).
2.  **Pool Sequences:** Apply average pooling to convert variable-length inputs to fixed vectors in $\mathbb{R}^d$ (validated in Appendix Figure S3).
3.  **Compute ID:** Use the TwoNN estimator on a subsampled dataset (e.g., factor of 4 decimation) to reduce noise (Appendix Section B).
4.  **Identify Minima:** Look for the relative minimum after the initial peak (relative depth $\sim 0.3$ to $0.5$).
5.  **Validate (If Possible):** If *any* labels exist, compute neighborhood overlap $\chi^{l,gt}_k$ to confirm the geometric signal aligns with semantics (Section 2.2).

By treating intrinsic dimension not just as a statistical metric but as a map of semantic compression, practitioners can navigate the internal geometry of transformers with unprecedented precision. This work provides the compass; future research will chart the detailed territories of language, multimodal data, and dynamic training landscapes.
