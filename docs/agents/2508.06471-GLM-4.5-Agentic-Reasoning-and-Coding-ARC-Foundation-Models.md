## 1. Executive Summary

To address the scarcity of open-source models that unify agentic, reasoning, and coding (ARC) capabilities, this paper introduces GLM-4.5, a Mixture-of-Experts foundation model with 355B total parameters and 32B activated parameters featuring hybrid reasoning modes. GLM-4.5 achieves strong performance with scores of 70.1% on TAU-Bench, 91.0% on AIME 24, and 64.2% on SWE-bench Verified, ranking 3rd overall among all evaluated models and 2nd on agentic benchmarks. This release advances research in reasoning and agentic AI systems by delivering parameter-efficient performance competitive with proprietary models like Claude Sonnet 4 in a fully open-source format.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Capability Gap in Open-Source LLMs

The paper identifies a critical gap in the current landscape of large language models: **no single open-source model effectively unifies agentic, reasoning, and coding (ARC) capabilities**. While proprietary models from organizations like OpenAI (o1/o3) and Anthropic (Claude Sonnet 4) have demonstrated groundbreaking performance in specific ARC domains, these systems remain closed-source, limiting research reproducibility and community-driven improvement.

This gap matters because real-world productivity tasks rarely fit neatly into single domains. A software engineer debugging a complex system needs to:
- **Reason** through multi-step logical problems (e.g., identifying why a test fails)
- **Code** to implement fixes and write new test cases
- **Act agentically** by interacting with development tools, terminals, and external APIs

When these capabilities are siloed across different models or when a single model excels at only one dimension, practitioners must orchestrate multiple systems—introducing latency, complexity, and potential failure points.

### Why ARC Capabilities Matter Together

The paper positions ARC as **interconnected capabilities** rather than independent benchmarks. This framing reflects how complex professional tasks actually unfold:

| Capability | What It Enables | Real-World Example |
|------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Agentic** | Interacting with external tools and the real world | Calling weather APIs, executing terminal commands, browsing web pages |
| **Reasoning** | Solving multi-step problems in mathematics and science | Proving theorems, diagnosing system failures, planning multi-step workflows |
| **Coding** | Tackling real-world software engineering tasks | Fixing GitHub issues, writing production-ready code, debugging existing codebases |

The Introduction notes that "the key to enhancing actual productivity and solving complex professional tasks lies in developing specific core capabilities." This is not merely an academic concern—models that can reliably execute all three dimensions enable automation of workflows that previously required human oversight at every step.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches

The paper identifies several shortcomings in existing models that GLM-4.5 aims to address:

**Proprietary Models Excel But Remain Closed**
- Models like OpenAI's o1/o3 and Claude Sonnet 4 have demonstrated strong performance in specific ARC domains (e.g., mathematical reasoning or code fixing)
- However, their closed nature prevents researchers from studying their architectures, training methods, or failure modes
- This limits the broader research community's ability to build upon and verify claims

**Open-Source Models Lag in Unified Performance**
- Existing open-source LLMs (the paper cites references [13], [34], [47] which include DeepSeek, Kimi, and Qwen variants) perform well in individual domains but struggle to excel across all three ARC dimensions simultaneously
- Some models optimize for reasoning at the expense of agentic capabilities, or vice versa
- The paper notes that GLM-4.5 achieves its performance with "much fewer parameters than several competitors"—specifically, half the parameters of DeepSeek-R1 and one-third those of Kimi K2

**Parameter Inefficiency**
- Figure 2 illustrates the relationship between model parameters and SWE-bench Verified scores, showing that GLM-4.5 and GLM-4.5-Air lie on the **Pareto Frontier**—achieving high performance without proportional parameter increases
- This efficiency matters for deployment: a 355B total parameter model with only 32B activated parameters (via MoE architecture) is significantly more practical than dense models with comparable performance

### How GLM-4.5 Positions Itself

The paper positions GLM-4.5 as **the first open-source MoE model to unify ARC capabilities at a competitive level**. Key positioning decisions include:

**Open-Source by Design**
- Both GLM-4.5 (355B parameters) and GLM-4.5-Air (106B parameters) are released openly on HuggingFace and Z.ai
- An evaluation toolkit is open-sourced at `https://github.com/zai-org/glm-simple-evals` to ensure reproducibility—addressing a common criticism of proprietary model claims

**Hybrid Reasoning Modes**
- Unlike models that force a single inference mode, GLM-4.5 supports both **thinking mode** (for complex reasoning and agentic tasks requiring deliberation) and **non-thinking mode** (for instant responses in chat scenarios)
- This flexibility acknowledges that not all tasks benefit from extended chain-of-thought reasoning—some require low-latency responses

**Parameter Efficiency Through MoE Architecture**
- The Mixture-of-Experts design activates only 32B of 355B total parameters during inference
- This contrasts with prior open-source approaches that either use dense architectures (computationally expensive) or MoE designs that don't achieve comparable ARC performance

**Benchmark Coverage**
- The paper evaluates across 12 benchmarks spanning all three ARC dimensions (Figure 1), rather than optimizing for a narrow set of popular benchmarks
- This comprehensive evaluation approach reduces the risk of benchmark overfitting and provides a more honest assessment of general capability

### Theoretical and Practical Significance

The work carries significance on two fronts:

**Theoretically**, it demonstrates that unified ARC capabilities are achievable in a single open-source model through careful architecture design (deeper MoE structures, more attention heads) and training methodology (multi-stage pre-training, expert model iteration with RL). This challenges the assumption that capability specialization requires separate models.

**Practically**, it provides the research community with:
- Reproducible model weights for studying ARC capabilities
- Training methodology insights (detailed in Sections 2-3 of the paper)
- Evaluation infrastructure for benchmarking future models

The paper explicitly states its ambition: "to advance research in reasoning and agentic AI systems." By releasing both model weights and training insights, GLM-4.5 positions itself as a **foundation for community research** rather than merely a competitive product announcement.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

GLM-4.5 is a Mixture-of-Experts large language model system designed to unify agentic, reasoning, and coding capabilities through a multi-stage training pipeline that progresses from general pre-training to specialized reinforcement learning. The system solves the problem of capability fragmentation by training a single model that can switch between deliberative "thinking" mode for complex tasks and direct response mode for simple queries, achieved through architectural choices that favor depth over width and a post-training process that distills multiple expert models into one unified system.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The GLM-4.5 training pipeline consists of four major components connected in sequence. First, the **Pre-Training Stage** ingests 15T tokens of general web documents to build foundational language understanding with a 4K token context window. Second, the **Mid-Training Stage** processes 8T tokens of specialized data (code repositories, synthetic reasoning, agent trajectories) while extending context length from 32K to 128K tokens. Third, the **Post-Training Stage** operates in two phases: Expert Training creates specialized models for reasoning, agentic, and general chat domains, then Unified Training distills these experts into a single hybrid model through self-distillation and reinforcement learning. Fourth, the **RL Infrastructure** (Slime framework) manages data generation and model updates across synchronous training for reasoning tasks and asynchronous training for long-horizon agent tasks.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the **MoE architecture design** because understanding how parameters are activated during inference explains why GLM-4.5 achieves 32B activated parameters from 355B total parameters.
- We then cover **pre-training data composition and processing** because the quality and structure of training data directly determines what knowledge the model acquires before specialization.
- We proceed to **mid-training stages** because this is where the model transitions from general language understanding to ARC-specific capabilities through domain-focused data.
- We examine **post-training expert iteration** because this two-stage process (Expert Training → Unified Training) is what enables hybrid reasoning modes.
- We detail **reinforcement learning methodologies** because RL is responsible for the final performance gains on verifiable tasks like mathematics and coding.
- We conclude with **RL infrastructure design** because the Slime framework's synchronous/asynchronous modes explain how different task types are efficiently trained.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a systems and methods paper that presents a complete training recipe for building a unified ARC-capable language model, with the core idea being that capability unification requires both architectural efficiency (MoE with depth-biased design) and training methodology innovation (multi-stage pre-training followed by expert distillation with reinforcement learning).

**MoE Architecture Design and Parameter Allocation**

GLM-4.5 employs a Mixture-of-Experts architecture where each token activates only a subset of the total parameters during inference, enabling the model to maintain 355B total parameters while only computing 32B activated parameters per forward pass. The architecture contains 160 total experts with 8 experts active per token, plus 1 shared expert that processes every token regardless of routing decisions. Table 1 shows that GLM-4.5-Air follows the same expert activation pattern with 128 total experts and 12B activated parameters from 106B total parameters. The model uses loss-free balance routing with sigmoid gates for MoE layers, which differs from the routing mechanisms in DeepSeek-V3 and Kimi K2. A critical architectural decision was to reduce model width (hidden dimension and number of routed experts) while increasing model height (number of layers), because the authors found that deeper models exhibited better reasoning capacity than wider models with comparable parameter counts. GLM-4.5 contains 89 MoE layers compared to DeepSeek-V3's 58 layers, despite having fewer total parameters. The self-attention component employs Grouped-Query Attention with partial RoPE, using 96 attention heads for a 5120 hidden dimension—this represents 2.5 times more attention heads than comparable models. Counterintuitively, this increased head count does not improve training loss compared to models with fewer heads, but it consistently improves performance on reasoning benchmarks such as MMLU and BBH. The model incorporates QK-Norm to stabilize the range of attention logits, which is a configuration choice not present in DeepSeek-V3 or Kimi K2 according to Table 1. Both GLM-4.5 and GLM-4.5-Air add one MoE layer as the MTP (Multi-Token Prediction) layer to support speculative decoding during inference, which accelerates token generation by predicting multiple tokens simultaneously.

**Pre-Training Data Composition and Quality Filtering**

The pre-training corpus spans 23T tokens total, divided into 15T tokens for general pre-training and 8T tokens for mid-training specialization as shown in Figure 3. The majority of pre-training documents consist of English and Chinese webpages crawled from the Internet, organized using a quality scoring system inspired by Nemotron-CC. Documents are divided into buckets based on quality scores, with high-quality buckets contributing over 3.2 epochs during pre-training while the lowest quality bucket is discarded entirely. This up-sampling strategy emphasizes high-frequency knowledge for reasoning tasks while maintaining coverage for long-tail world knowledge. The authors discovered that many webpages were automatically generated from templates and assigned high quality scores, which could not be removed by standard MinHash deduplication. To address this, they applied the SemDedup pipeline to remove similar webpages based on document embeddings rather than exact text matching. For multilingual support beyond English and Chinese, documents from Fineweb-2 are included with a quality classifier that judges educational utility, up-sampling high-quality multilingual documents. Code data undergoes a two-tier filtering process: source code from GitHub is first filtered with rule-based methods, then classified by language-specific quality models into high-quality, medium-quality, and low-quality tiers, with only high and medium quality samples included in training. The Fill-In-the-Middle training objective is applied to all source code data, which trains the model to predict missing code segments given surrounding context. For code-related web documents, a two-stage retrieval process identifies relevant content: documents are first selected based on HTML code tags or a FastText classifier trained to detect code-related content, then assessed using a dedicated quality model following the same three-tier classification. A fine-grained parser re-parses selected web pages to better preserve code formats and contents. Mathematics and science documents are scored using a large language model that evaluates the ratio of educational content, then a small-scale classifier predicts these scores to up-sample documents above a certain threshold.

**Mid-Training Stage Progression and Context Extension**

Figure 3 illustrates the complete pre-training and mid-training progression, showing how sequence length extends from 4K to 128K across stages. The mid-training phase includes three distinct stages that utilize medium-size domain-specific datasets rather than large-scale general documents. The Repo-level Code Training stage adds concatenated code files from the same repository to teach cross-file dependency understanding, which is essential for real software engineering tasks. This stage includes model-filtered GitHub issues, pull requests, and commits, with related items concatenated into one context and commits organized in a diff-like format. The training sequence length extends from 4K to 32K during this stage to incorporate large repositories that would be truncated at shorter context lengths. The Synthetic Reasoning Data Training stage adds synthetic reasoning content for mathematics, science, and coding competitions, where questions and answers are collected from webpages and books, then reasoning processes are synthesized using a separate reasoning model. The Long-context & Agent Training stage extends sequence length from 32K to 128K while up-sampling long documents from the pre-training corpus, and incorporates large-scale synthetic agent trajectories. During pre-training, best-fit packing was not used because random truncation serves as an effective data-augmentation strategy for general documents. However, for mid-training datasets, best-fit packing was applied to avoid truncating reasoning processes or repo-level code sequences. When extending sequence length to 32K, the RoPE base frequency was adjusted from 10,000 to 1,000,000 to improve long-context modeling ability, because the original frequency was optimized for shorter sequences and would cause positional encoding degradation at extended lengths.

**Hyperparameter Configuration and Optimization Strategy**

The training employs the Muon optimizer for all parameters except word embeddings, bias terms, and RMSNorm weights, because Muon accelerates convergence and tolerates larger batch sizes compared to traditional optimizers. The Newton-Schulz iteration steps $N$ are set to 5, momentum $\mu$ to 0.95, and Muon's update RMS is scaled to 0.2. A cosine decay schedule is used for learning rate instead of warmup-stable-decay (WSD) schedule, because early experiments showed that models trained with WSD perform worse on general benchmarks like SimpleQA and MMLU, indicating underfitting during the stable stage. The learning rate warms up from 0 to $2.5 \times 10^{-4}$ then decays to $2.5 \times 10^{-5}$ until the end of mid-training. Batch size uses a warmup strategy where tokens per batch gradually increase from 16M to 64M during the first 500B tokens of training, then remain constant for the remaining training duration. Weight decay is set to 0.1 with no dropout regularization applied. For loss-free balance routing, the bias update rate is set to 0.001 for the first 15T tokens, then changed to 0.0 for remaining tokens to stabilize expert assignment after initial learning. An auxiliary sequence-level balance loss with weight 0.0001 is applied to avoid extreme imbalance within any single sequence. The MTP loss weight $\lambda$ is set to 0.3 for the first 15T tokens, then reduced to 0.1 for remaining tokens as the model stabilizes.

**Post-Training: Two-Stage Expert Model Iteration**

The post-training process divides into two distinct stages as shown in Section 3. Stage 1 (Expert Training) constructs specialized expert models for three domains: Reasoning, Agent, and General chat. Stage 2 (Unified Training) employs self-distillation techniques to integrate multiple experts into one comprehensive model capable of both deliberative reasoning and direct response modes. Supervised Fine-Tuning occurs at the beginning of both stages but serves different purposes: in Expert Training, SFT provides a cold start with basic chat, reasoning, and tool-use capabilities before reinforcement learning enhancement; in Unified Training, SFT distills capabilities from different expert models into one hybrid reasoning generalist. The Cold Start SFT phase uses a small set of supervised fine-tuning data with extended Chain-of-Thought responses to ensure each expert model possesses adequate foundational ability prior to reinforcement learning. The Overall SFT stage collects millions of samples covering reasoning tasks (mathematics, code, science), general chat (writing, translation, summarization), agentic tasks (tool usage, authentic project development), and long-context understanding from previously trained expert models, training the base model with a maximum context length of 128K tokens. By distilling from distinct expert outputs, the model learns to apply the most effective long CoT reasoning for each task type. The training data deliberately balances samples containing full reasoning processes with samples lacking explicit thought processes, because prolonged thinking is unnecessary for domains demanding quick responses like casual chat. This balance creates the hybrid reasoning model that can operate in both reflective and immediate response modes.

**Function Call Template Design for Agentic Training**

A novel function call template reduces character escaping requirements when function parameters contain code segments, which is critical for agentic foundation models where function calling is a core capability. Traditional JSON-based function call representations require extensive character escaping when code segments appear in parameters, increasing the learning burden for the model. The proposed template encapsulates function call keys and values within XML-like special token tags, allowing most code to be represented in native form without escaping. Figure 4 shows the template structure where function names appear within `<tool_call>{function-name}` tags and arguments use `<arg_key>` and `<arg_value>` tags. For example, a weather function call uses:

```
<tool_call>get_weather
<arg_key>city</arg_key>
<arg_value>Beijing</arg_value>
<arg_key>date</arg_key>
<arg_value>2024-06-27</arg_value>
</tool_call>
```

This approach substantially reduces escaping requirements while experimental results demonstrate no compromise to function call execution performance.

**Rejection Sampling and Response Quality Filtering**

When sampling from expert models, a comprehensive multistage filtering pipeline ensures training data quality through four sequential checks. First, repetitive, excessively short, or truncated samples are removed along with those failing to conform to valid reasoning formats. Second, correctness verification is conducted for samples with objective answers using programmatic validation. Third, reward models filter responses to subjective questions where programmatic verification is impossible. Fourth, for tool-calling scenarios, the pipeline ensures adherence to proper tool invocation protocols and verifies that trajectories reach expected terminal states. Prompt selection experiments removed prompts in the bottom 50% based on response lengths, resulting in 2%-4% improvement in mathematics and science tasks despite training with only half the data. Applying response scaling to these hard prompts by generating four responses per prompt brought an additional 1%-2% improvement.

**Automatic Agentic SFT Data Construction Pipeline**

The agentic SFT data construction involves four sequential steps that automate trajectory generation for tool-use training. Step 1 (Agentic Framework and Tool Collection) gathers agentic frameworks, real-world tool APIs, and MCP servers while leveraging LLMs to automatically construct and simulate additional tools. Step 2 (Task Synthesis) automatically generates agentic tasks based on collected frameworks: for mature frameworks, LLMs comprehend functionalities and generate relevant queries; for fragmented tools, a representative subset is selected and LLMs construct tasks about that subset, covering both single-step and multi-step tool calling scenarios. Step 3 (Trajectory Generation) uses existing LLMs to generate tool-call trajectories for each synthesized task, with an LLM user simulator converting multi-step tasks into multi-round dialogue trajectories. Step 4 (Quality Filtering) employs multiple judge agents to evaluate task completion for each trajectory, retaining only successful trajectories for training.

**Reasoning RL: Difficulty-Based Curriculum Learning**

The reinforcement learning algorithm builds upon the GRPO framework, excluding the KL loss term to simplify optimization. Difficulty-based Curriculum Learning addresses the mismatch between evolving model proficiency and static training data during reinforcement learning. In later training stages, as the model becomes more capable, overly simple data produces rollouts where all rewards equal 1, eliminating gradient signal. Conversely, in early stages, excessively difficult data produces batches where all rewards equal 0, also eliminating useful gradient information. Both scenarios severely hinder training efficiency due to lack of reward variance. The solution employs a two-stage difficulty-based curriculum where the first stage uses moderate difficulty data and the second stage switches to extremely difficult problems with pass@8=0 but pass@512>0. Figure 5 demonstrates this approach enables the model to consistently surpass its performance ceiling on AIME'24, with the blue line (curriculum method) continuing to improve while the red line (baseline with moderate difficulty) plateaus at 81.8% versus 83.4%. All problems in the second stage are strictly sourced from a pool with verified correct answers to maintain high signal quality and reduce noise.

**Single-Stage RL at Maximum Context Length**

Previous research suggested conducting RL in multiple stages with progressively increasing maximum output lengths, but experiments reveal this multi-stage approach is less effective than single-stage RL conducted directly at the maximum target length of 64K tokens. Since initial SFT has already conditioned the model on generating 64K-length responses, introducing RL stages with shorter maximum lengths causes the model to "unlearn" its long-context capabilities, leading to significant and irreversible performance drops as average output length decreases. This degradation is difficult to recover from in the final 64K-length RL stage, limiting further improvement. Figure 6 confirms this observation: applying RL directly at full 64K-length continually pushes the model's limits and yields better performance (83.4% versus 80.6% for multi-stage training at 64K context). The red line (single-stage at 64K) achieves superior performance while the blue line (multi-stage with progressively increasing length from 16K to 64K) suffers irreversible performance drops in early stages.

**Dynamic Sampling Temperature for Exploration Control**

During RL, sampling temperature controls trajectory diversity, where temperature that is too low leads to convergent and less exploratory outputs, while temperature that is too high introduces low-quality noisy samples that undermine model accuracy and training efficiency. Using fixed sampling temperature is suboptimal because it fails to adapt as the policy distribution becomes more concentrated with lower entropy, often resulting in insufficient exploration at later training stages. The proposed solution dynamically adjusts sampling temperature to maintain balance between accuracy and exploration: when average reward of rollouts stabilizes, this indicates a convergence phase and temperature increases to encourage greater diversity. To mitigate risk of excessive noise, a quality-control mechanism periodically evaluates model performance on a held-out validation set across a range of temperatures, then sets the temperature for the next training phase to the maximum value that does not cause performance drop of more than 1% from current optimum.

**Code and Science RL: Loss Calculation and Data Quality**

For code RL, the choice of loss calculation critically affects training efficiency, with token-weighted mean loss highly beneficial compared to conventional sequence-mean loss. Figure 7 (left) shows the token-weighted approach provides finer-grained and more stable gradient signals, leading to significantly faster convergence (46.5% versus 46.3% on LiveCodeBench). This method also alleviates length bias inherent in sequence-level rewards and effectively suppresses generation of overly simplistic or repetitive "base case" samples during training. For science RL on the GPQA-Diamond benchmark, data quality and type are paramount factors as shown in Figure 7 (right). Using exclusively expert-verified multiple-choice questions for RL leads to significantly better performance (65.8% versus 62.9%) compared to training with mixed-quality or unverified data. This result underscores that even for tasks with simple formats like multiple-choice, rigorously filtering the RL data pool to include only high-quality, challenging instances is crucial for effective model improvement.

**Agentic RL: Outcome Supervision with Process Penalties**

For web search tasks, accuracy of the final answer serves as reward for the entire agent trace, while coding agents primarily utilize SWE data with verifiable test cases for RL training. Experiments show that RL training on web search and SWE tasks leads to generalized performance improvements across other tasks and benchmarks, including general tool usage and Terminal-Bench coding tasks. A process format penalty ensures the model generates correct tool call formats: if the model fails to produce correct tool format during agent trace generation, the process halts and the trace receives zero reward. The group-wise policy optimization objective for RL training is:

$$L_{RL}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D} \left[ \frac{1}{K} \sum_{i=1}^{K} (r(x, y_i) - \bar{r}(x)) \right]$$

where $\bar{r}(x) = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{i=1}^{K} r(x, y_i)$ is the mean reward of sampled responses, and only model-generated tokens are used for optimization while environment feedback is ignored in loss computation.

**Iterative Self-Distillation for Agent Performance**

Since RL training on agent tasks is time-consuming, a self-distillation approach iteratively enhances the SFT cold-start model before resuming RL training on the improved model. The process first performs RL training on the initial cold-start model to boost agent performance, then once training reaches a certain step count or plateaus, applies self-distillation by substituting original cold-start data with responses generated by the RL-trained model, creating a superior SFT model. Further RL training then occurs on this enhanced model with progressively increasing training difficulty. This iterative strategy efficiently pushes the performance limits of RL-trained models without requiring continuous fresh data collection.

**Function Calling RL: Step-wise versus End-to-end**

Function Calling RL divides into step-wise rule-based RL and end-to-end multi-turn RL, with step-wise RL incorporated directly into the general RL framework due to similar output lengths and convergence speeds. Step-wise Rule-based RL annotates ground truth function calls for each step in training data, training the model to generate the next assistant response given task and previous function calls. The strict reward function is:

$$\text{Reward} = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if FormatCorrect}(a_t) \text{ and } \text{Match}(a_t, a^*_t) \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

where $a_t$ denotes the $t$-th function call generated by the model and $a^*_t$ is the corresponding ground truth function call. A reward of 1 is given only if $a_t$ is in correct format and matches ground truth exactly including name, parameters, and every field. End-to-end Multi-turn RL addresses limitations of step-wise approaches where the model lacks dynamic environment interactions and cannot autonomously explore or plan. The model generates complete trajectories then receives rewards based on task completion:

$$\text{Reward} = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if FormatCorrect}(a_1, \ldots, a_T) \text{ and } \text{TaskCompleted}(I, o_0, a_1, o_1, \ldots, a_T, o_T) \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

where $I$ refers to the original complex task, $a_t$ is the $t$-th function call, $o_t$ is tool feedback or user information, and TaskCompleted is determined by the environment according to predefined rules or by an LLM Judge Agent.

**RL Infrastructure: Slime Framework Architecture**

The RL infrastructure builds upon Slime, an open-source framework engineered with optimizations for flexibility, efficiency, and extensibility. The system consists of three core modules shown in Figure 10: Training (Megatron) handles the main training process and reads data from the Data Buffer while synchronizing parameters with the rollout module after training; Rollout (SGLang + Router) generates new data including rewards and verifier outputs and writes to the Data Buffer; Data Buffer serves as a bridge module managing prompt initialization, custom data, and rollout generation strategies. The framework supports both colocated synchronous mode and disaggregated asynchronous mode within a single unified system. For general-purpose RL tasks or reasoning capability enhancement (mathematics, code generation), synchronous colocated architecture is more effective because training and inference engines reside on the same worker, which combined with dynamic sampling significantly reduces GPU idle time. For agentic tasks like Software Engineering, the data generation process is protracted with complex system interactions, so disaggregated asynchronous mode is adopted where the rollout component exposes directly to the agent environment while GPUs for training and inference are scheduled independently. This decoupling enables agent environments to constantly generate new data without stalling from the training cycle.

**Accelerated Rollout with Mixed-Precision Inference**

Rollout efficiency is a persistent bottleneck in RL training, addressed through BF16 for training while leveraging FP8 for inference to accelerate data generation. During each policy update iteration, online block-wise FP8 quantization performs on model parameters before dispatch for rollout, enabling highly efficient FP8 inference that significantly improves overall throughput of data collection. For agent tasks, a high-concurrency Docker-based runtime provisions isolated environments for each task, drastically reducing rollout overhead. The fully asynchronous RL training loop partitions GPUs into dedicated rollout engines and training engines: rollout engines continuously generate trajectories while training engines update model weights and periodically synchronize back to rollout engines. This decoupled design prevents long or diverse trajectories from blocking the entire training pipeline, resulting in consistently high throughput particularly in scenarios with highly variable agent interactions. A unified HTTP endpoint interface coupled with a centralized data pool enables seamless integration of heterogeneous agent frameworks, since most agent frameworks produce rollouts in message-list format that stores in the data pool as a shared training source.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the fundamental innovations that distinguish GLM-4.5 from prior work. We distinguish between **incremental improvements** (refinements to existing methods) and **fundamental innovations** (new approaches that change how capabilities are achieved). The insights below are ordered by their conceptual novelty and practical significance.

---

### 4.1 Hybrid Reasoning Modes: Unified Deliberative and Direct Response in a Single Model

**Innovation Type: Fundamental**

Prior models force a binary choice: either a "reasoning model" that always produces chain-of-thought (like DeepSeek-R1 or OpenAI o1) or a "chat model" that responds directly (like GPT-4 or Claude). GLM-4.5 breaks this dichotomy by training a **single model that dynamically switches between thinking mode and non-thinking mode** based on task requirements.

**What Makes This Different:**

The key insight is that hybrid reasoning is not achieved through architecture alone, but through **deliberate data balancing during post-training**. As described in Section 3.1, the Overall SFT stage "meticulously balanced training data containing full reasoning with data lacking explicit thought processes." This is non-trivial because:

- Reasoning-heavy training data can cause the model to overthink simple queries (e.g., producing lengthy deliberation for "What is 2+2?")
- Chat-heavy training data can cause the model to skip necessary reasoning on complex problems
- The model must learn **meta-cognition**: recognizing when deliberation is warranted versus when direct response is appropriate

**Why This Matters:**

This capability has practical deployment implications that prior work overlooks. A reasoning-only model incurs latency penalties on simple queries, while a chat-only model fails on complex tasks. GLM-4.5's hybrid approach enables:

- **Latency optimization**: Simple queries receive instant responses without unnecessary token generation
- **Capability preservation**: Complex tasks still trigger full deliberative reasoning
- **Single-model deployment**: Organizations need not maintain separate reasoning and chat models

The paper does not provide ablation studies quantifying the latency savings, which is a limitation. However, the benchmark results in Table 4 (reasoning) and Table 6 (general chat) demonstrate that the model maintains competitive performance across both task types simultaneously—something no prior open-source model achieves.

---

### 4.2 Single-Stage RL at Maximum Context Length: Contradicting Prior Training Paradigms

**Innovation Type: Fundamental**

Previous research (cited as reference [25] in the paper) recommended conducting reinforcement learning in **multiple stages with progressively increasing maximum output lengths**. The intuition was that models should "learn to walk before running"—starting with shorter outputs and gradually extending to longer contexts.

GLM-4.5's experiments reveal this intuition is **incorrect and harmful**.

**What Makes This Different:**

Figure 6 presents a direct ablation study comparing single-stage versus multi-stage RL at 64K context length. The results show:

- **Single-stage at 64K**: 83.4% accuracy on AIME24
- **Multi-stage (16K → 32K → 48K → 64K)**: 80.6% accuracy on AIME24

The critical insight is that once SFT has conditioned the model on 64K-length responses, introducing RL stages with shorter maximum lengths causes the model to **"unlearn" its long-context capabilities**. This performance drop is described as "irreversible"—the model's average output length decreases, and this degradation cannot be recovered in the final 64K RL stage.

**Why This Matters:**

This finding challenges a widely-adopted training paradigm. Many RL training recipes assume progressive difficulty scaling applies to context length, but GLM-4.5 demonstrates that **context length should be treated differently from task difficulty**:

- Task difficulty should scale progressively (as in the difficulty-based curriculum in Section 3.2)
- Context length should remain at maximum target from the start of RL

This has significant computational implications. Multi-stage RL requires training multiple intermediate checkpoints, each consuming GPU hours. Single-stage RL eliminates these intermediate stages, reducing training time while achieving better performance.

**Limitation:** The paper notes these experiments were conducted on "a smaller experimental model, not on GLM-4.5" to enable rapid iteration. While this is methodologically sound for ablation studies, readers should note the findings are extrapolated to the full model rather than directly measured.

---

### 4.3 Depth-Over-Width MoE Architecture: Counterintuitive Design for Reasoning

**Innovation Type: Fundamental**

The prevailing wisdom in MoE architecture design assumes that **width** (hidden dimension, number of experts) drives capability. GLM-4.5 inverts this assumption, prioritizing **depth** (number of layers) instead.

**What Makes This Different:**

Table 1 shows GLM-4.5 has 89 MoE layers compared to DeepSeek-V3's 58 layers, despite having fewer total parameters (355B vs 671B). The paper states: "we reduce the width (hidden dimension and number of routed experts) of the model and increase its height (number of layers), as we found that deeper models exhibited better reasoning capacity."

Additionally, GLM-4.5 employs **2.5 times more attention heads** (96 heads for 5120 hidden dimension) than comparable models. The paper explicitly notes this is counterintuitive: "while this increased head count does not improve training loss compared to models with fewer heads, it consistently improves performance on reasoning benchmarks such as MMLU and BBH."

**Why This Matters:**

This finding suggests that **training loss is an imperfect proxy for reasoning capability**. A model can achieve identical training loss with different architectural configurations, yet exhibit substantially different downstream performance on reasoning tasks. This has implications for:

- **Architecture search**: Prior work optimizes for training loss convergence; GLM-4.5 suggests reasoning benchmarks should guide architecture decisions directly
- **Parameter allocation**: For reasoning-focused models, parameters should favor depth over width
- **Evaluation methodology**: Training loss alone cannot predict reasoning performance

**Limitation:** The paper does not provide a theoretical explanation for why depth improves reasoning more than width. This remains an empirical finding without mechanistic understanding.

---

### 4.4 XML-like Function Call Template: Practical Innovation for Agentic Training

**Innovation Type: Incremental (but High-Impact)**

Traditional function calling uses JSON format for parameters. When function parameters contain code segments, JSON requires extensive character escaping (e.g., escaping quotes, backslashes, newlines). This increases the learning burden for models and introduces error-prone token sequences.

GLM-4.5 introduces an **XML-like special token template** that encapsulates function call keys and values, allowing code to appear in native form without escaping.

**What Makes This Different:**

Figure 4 illustrates the template structure. Instead of JSON like:

```json
{"function": "execute_code", "args": {"code": "print(\"hello\")"}}
```

The model uses:

```
<tool_call>execute_code
<arg_key>code</arg_key>
<arg_value>print("hello")</arg_value>
</tool_call>
```

**Why This Matters:**

This is a practical engineering innovation with measurable impact on agentic training:

- **Reduced token count**: Eliminating escape characters reduces sequence length, lowering computational cost
- **Improved reliability**: Fewer escape characters means fewer opportunities for formatting errors that break function execution
- **Lower learning burden**: The model learns function calling semantics rather than escape character patterns

The paper states experimental results "demonstrate that the proposed function call template does not compromise the performance of function call execution while reducing escaping." However, no quantitative comparison is provided (e.g., percentage reduction in escape characters, or token count savings).

**Classification as Incremental:** This innovation does not introduce new capabilities—it improves the efficiency and reliability of existing function calling mechanisms. However, for agentic models where function calling is core to operation, this incremental improvement has outsized practical impact.

---

### 4.5 Iterative Self-Distillation for Agent RL: Efficient Performance Scaling

**Innovation Type: Incremental (with Novel Application)**

Self-distillation—using a model's own outputs to create training data—is not new. However, GLM-4.5 applies this technique in a **specific iterative loop for agent RL** that addresses the time-consuming nature of agent environment interactions.

**What Makes This Different:**

The process (Section 3.3.2) follows this cycle:

1. Perform RL training on initial cold-start model
2. Once training plateaus, collect responses from the RL-trained model
3. Use these responses to create superior SFT data (replacing original cold-start data)
4. Conduct further RL training on this enhanced model with increased difficulty
5. Repeat

**Why This Matters:**

Agent RL is uniquely expensive because each rollout requires **environment interaction** (e.g., executing code, calling APIs, browsing web pages). These interactions are orders of magnitude slower than pure text generation. Iterative self-distillation reduces the number of expensive environment interactions required by:

- Maximizing learning from each RL phase before collecting new data
- Using distilled SFT to "consolidate" gains before the next RL phase
- Enabling progressive difficulty increases without proportional cost increases

**Limitation:** The paper does not quantify the efficiency gain (e.g., "X% reduction in environment interactions for equivalent performance"). This makes it difficult to assess the practical magnitude of this innovation.

### Summary: Innovation Classification

| Innovation | Type | Significance |
|------------|------|--------------|
| Hybrid Reasoning Modes | Fundamental | Enables single-model deployment for diverse task types |
| Single-Stage RL at 64K | Fundamental | Contradicts prior training paradigms; improves performance |
| Depth-Over-Width MoE | Fundamental | Challenges architecture design assumptions |
| XML Function Call Template | Incremental | High-impact engineering improvement for agentic training |
| Iterative Self-Distillation | Incremental | Efficient scaling method for expensive agent RL |

The three fundamental innovations (hybrid reasoning, single-stage RL, depth-over-width architecture) represent conceptual advances that could influence future model design beyond GLM-4.5. The two incremental innovations (function call template, iterative distillation) are practical improvements that enhance training efficiency and reliability within the agentic domain.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section examines the assumptions, constraints, and unresolved weaknesses in GLM-4.5's approach. While the paper demonstrates strong empirical results, several limitations reveal where the methodology relies on unproven assumptions, where performance gaps remain unexplained, and where practical deployment constraints may limit adoption.

### 6.1 Unproven Assumptions in Training Methodology

**Assumption 1: Smaller Model Ablations Transfer to Full Model**

The paper explicitly states that all reinforcement learning ablation studies were conducted on "a smaller experimental model, not on GLM-4.5" (Section 3.2). This includes the critical findings about:

- Difficulty-based curriculum learning (Figure 5: 83.4% vs 81.8% on AIME24)
- Single-stage vs. multi-stage RL at 64K context (Figure 6: 83.4% vs 80.6%)
- Token-weighted vs. sequence-mean loss for code RL (Figure 7 left: 46.5% vs 46.3%)
- Expert-verified vs. mixed-quality data for science RL (Figure 7 right: 65.8% vs 62.9%)

**Why This Matters:** Architecture-scale interactions are well-documented in LLM research—methods that work at 7B parameters often fail at 70B, and vice versa. The paper provides no evidence that these ablation results scale proportionally to the 355B parameter GLM-4.5. This is a methodological gap that limits confidence in the training recipe's generalizability.

**Assumption 2: Hybrid Reasoning Emerges from Data Balancing Alone**

The paper claims hybrid reasoning modes (thinking vs. non-thinking) are achieved by "meticulously balanced training data containing full reasoning with data lacking explicit thought processes" (Section 3.1). However:

- No ablation study isolates the contribution of data balancing vs. other factors
- No mechanism is provided for how the model learns to **select** the appropriate mode per query
- No quantitative latency measurements demonstrate the claimed efficiency benefits

**Why This Matters:** Without understanding the selection mechanism, practitioners cannot predict when the model will invoke thinking mode. This creates deployment uncertainty—some queries may incur unexpected latency penalties if the model over-thinks simple tasks.

**Assumption 3: Depth Over Width Generalizes Beyond Reasoning Tasks**

The architectural decision to prioritize depth (89 MoE layers) over width (5120 hidden dimension) is justified by the finding that "deeper models exhibited better reasoning capacity" (Section 2.1). However:

- This claim is not supported by controlled ablation studies varying depth independently
- The paper does not examine whether this trade-off harms non-reasoning capabilities
- Table 6 shows GLM-4.5 scores 26.4% on SimpleQA vs. GPT-4.1's 42.3%—potentially indicating factual knowledge suffers when optimizing for reasoning depth

**Why This Matters:** If depth optimization comes at the cost of knowledge retrieval, this represents an unacknowledged trade-off that affects model selection for different use cases.

### 6.2 Unexplained Performance Gaps

**Gap 1: BrowseComp Weakness (23.3 Percentage Points Behind o3)**

Table 3 shows GLM-4.5 scores 26.4% on BrowseComp while o3 achieves 49.7%. This is the largest performance gap between GLM-4.5 and any leading model on any benchmark. The paper acknowledges this gap but provides:

- No failure mode analysis (e.g., does the model fail at planning, tool execution, or answer synthesis?)
- No hypothesis for why web browsing specifically lags behind reasoning and coding
- No proposed mitigation strategy

**Why This Matters:** Web browsing is a core agentic capability. A 23-point gap suggests fundamental limitations in how GLM-4.5 handles multi-step information retrieval and synthesis—capabilities essential for real-world research and analysis tasks.

**Gap 2: SimpleQA Factual Knowledge Deficit**

Table 6 shows GLM-4.5 scores 26.4% on SimpleQA compared to GPT-4.1's 42.3% and even DeepSeek-R1-0528's 27.8%. This is notable because:

- GLM-4.5 was trained on 23T tokens including extensive web crawl data
- The pre-training pipeline includes quality filtering and up-sampling strategies designed to capture "high-frequency knowledge for reasoning tasks and also improve coverage for long-tail world knowledge" (Section 2.2)
- Despite this, factual knowledge retrieval lags significantly behind proprietary competitors

**Why This Matters:** This suggests a fundamental tension between reasoning optimization and knowledge retention. Models optimized for chain-of-thought reasoning may develop different internal representations that prioritize derivation over retrieval—a trade-off the paper does not discuss.

**Gap 3: Safety - Unfairness and Bias (77.4%)**

Table 7 shows Unfairness & Bias is the lowest SafetyBench category at 77.4%, compared to 94.3% for Ethics & Morality and 96.7% for Physical Health. The paper states "there is still room for improvement in addressing Unfairness and Bias" but provides:

- No analysis of which bias types cause failures (gender, race, geography, etc.)
- No proposed mitigation beyond acknowledging the weakness
- No comparison to how proprietary models handle the same category

**Why This Matters:** Bias failures can have real-world harm in deployment. Without understanding the failure modes, practitioners cannot assess deployment risk for sensitive applications.

### 6.3 Computational and Data Constraints

**Constraint 1: Training Compute Requirements**

The paper states GLM-4.5 was trained on 23T tokens across pre-training and mid-training stages (Figure 3). While the MoE architecture reduces inference cost (32B activated parameters from 355B total), the training compute is not disclosed. However, we can infer constraints from:

- The multi-stage training pipeline with context extension from 4K → 32K → 128K
- The RL infrastructure requiring separate rollout and training engines (Section 3.5)
- The use of synthetic data generation requiring additional LLM calls for reasoning and agentic trajectories

**Why This Matters:** While the model weights are open-source, the training recipe may not be reproducible for research groups without comparable compute resources. This limits the paper's contribution to weight release rather than methodology democratization.

**Constraint 2: Synthetic Data Dependencies**

The mid-training and post-training stages rely heavily on synthetic data:

- Synthetic reasoning content for math, science, and coding competitions (Section 2.3)
- Large-scale synthetic agent trajectories (Section 2.3)
- LLM-generated tool-call trajectories for agentic SFT (Section 3.1)
- Self-distillation using model-generated responses for iterative RL improvement (Section 3.3.2)

**Why This Matters:** Synthetic data quality depends on the teacher models used for generation. If those teachers have blind spots or biases, these propagate to GLM-4.5. The paper does not disclose which models generated synthetic data or how teacher limitations were mitigated.

**Constraint 3: Context Length Limitations in Practice**

While GLM-4.5 supports 128K context, the SWE-bench evaluation required "history truncation to prevent exceeding the 128K context limit" (Section 4.2.3). This reveals:

- Real-world coding tasks can exceed the advertised context window
- Truncation strategies may lose relevant information, affecting performance
- The 128K limit, while large, is not sufficient for all professional software engineering scenarios

**Why This Matters:** Practitioners evaluating GLM-4.5 for codebase-scale tasks need to understand that 128K may require active context management, not passive consumption.

### 6.4 Evaluation Limitations

**Limitation 1: Human Evaluation Scale and Bias**

The hands-on evaluation (Section 4.3) used 660 prompts evaluated by "a single, consistent evaluator" to minimize inter-evaluator variance. However:

- 660 prompts is modest compared to automated benchmarks (e.g., 11,435 questions in SafetyBench)
- Single-evaluator design eliminates inter-rater reliability assessment
- The paper does not disclose evaluator expertise level or potential conflicts of interest

**Why This Matters:** Human evaluation is valuable for qualitative assessment, but the scale and single-evaluator design limit statistical confidence in the reported scores (e.g., GLM-4.5's 8.66 vs. DeepSeek-R1's 8.62 on English prompts—a 0.04 difference that may not be statistically significant).

**Limitation 2: Benchmark Contamination Risk**

The paper notes that LiveCodeBench is a "dynamic benchmark" evaluated on problems between 7/1/2024 and 1/1/2025 (footnote 2, Section 4.2.2). However:

- No contamination analysis is provided for other benchmarks (AIME, GPQA, etc.)
- The synthetic reasoning data was collected from "webpages and books" (Section 2.3)—potential overlap with benchmark sources
- The paper does not describe deduplication between training data and evaluation benchmarks beyond SemDedup for webpages

**Why This Matters:** If training data overlaps with benchmark content, reported scores may overestimate true generalization capability. This is particularly relevant for reasoning benchmarks where problem distributions are limited.

**Limitation 3: Missing Latency and Efficiency Metrics**

The hybrid reasoning modes claim latency benefits for simple queries, but the paper provides:

- No tokens-per-second measurements for thinking vs. non-thinking modes
- No comparison of inference cost between GLM-4.5 and dense models with similar performance
- No analysis of how MTP (Multi-Token Prediction) layers affect speculative decoding efficiency in practice

**Why This Matters:** Deployment decisions require cost-performance trade-off analysis. Without latency data, practitioners cannot assess whether GLM-4.5's parameter efficiency translates to operational efficiency.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Unresolved Issues

**Question 1: Mode Selection Mechanism**

How does GLM-4.5 decide when to invoke thinking mode vs. direct response? The paper describes the training data balance but not:

- Whether the model uses explicit tokens to signal mode selection
- Whether mode selection is query-dependent or learned implicitly
- Whether users can override the model's mode selection

**Why This Matters:** Without understanding mode selection, practitioners cannot predict latency or control reasoning depth for specific applications.

**Question 2: Long-Horizon Agent Reliability**

The paper demonstrates strong performance on agentic benchmarks but notes that agent rollouts "often require prolonged interactions with complex environments" (Section 3.5). However:

- No analysis of failure rates as interaction turns increase
- No discussion of error recovery mechanisms when tool calls fail mid-trajectory
- Figure 8 shows accuracy scales with test-time compute, but the curve flattens—suggesting diminishing returns

**Why This Matters:** Real-world agentic tasks may require 50+ interaction turns. If reliability degrades with horizon length, this limits practical deployment for complex workflows.

**Question 3: Multilingual Capability Beyond English and Chinese**

The paper includes multilingual documents from Fineweb-2 (Section 2.2) and reports human evaluation on "other languages" (Table 10: 8.49 overall score). However:

- The "other languages" category is not broken down by specific languages
- No comparison to specialized multilingual models (e.g., NLLB, Aya)
- The 160 prompts in "other languages" is small compared to 392 English and 108 Chinese prompts

**Why This Matters:** Organizations deploying GLM-4.5 globally need to understand which languages are well-supported versus which may require additional fine-tuning.

**Question 4: Reproducibility of RL Infrastructure**

The Slime RL framework is described as "open-source" (Section 3.5, footnote 1), but:

- The paper does not specify which components are released (training code, rollout infrastructure, or both)
- The agent-oriented RL infrastructure includes "high-concurrency Docker-based runtime" and "unified HTTP endpoint interface"—these may require significant engineering to replicate
- No documentation is provided for reproducing the synchronous/asynchronous training modes

**Why This Matters:** If the RL infrastructure is not fully reproducible, other researchers cannot validate the training methodology claims or build upon the approach.

### 6.6 Summary: Trade-off Matrix

| Trade-off | Benefit | Cost |
|-----------|---------|------|
| **Depth over Width** | Better reasoning capacity (AIME 24: 91.0%) | Weaker factual knowledge (SimpleQA: 26.4%) |
| **Hybrid Reasoning** | Single model for diverse tasks | Unclear mode selection mechanism; no latency data |
| **Synthetic Data** | Enables specialized capability training | Depends on teacher model quality; potential bias propagation |
| **Single-Stage RL at 64K** | Better performance (83.4% vs 80.6%) | Requires full context from start; no gradual adaptation |
| **MoE Architecture** | Parameter efficiency (32B activated from 355B) | Training compute not disclosed; may not be reproducible |
| **Open-Source Weights** | Community access and research | Training methodology not fully reproducible |

**Overall Assessment:** GLM-4.5 represents a significant advance in open-source ARC-capable models, but the limitations above reveal important constraints. The most critical gaps are: (1) unexplained performance deficits on BrowseComp and SimpleQA, (2) lack of latency/efficiency metrics for hybrid reasoning claims, and (3) ablation studies on smaller models that may not transfer. Practitioners should evaluate GLM-4.5 against their specific use cases rather than assuming benchmark performance generalizes uniformly across all task types.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how GLM-4.5 reshapes the open-source LLM landscape, what research pathways it opens, and how practitioners should approach adoption. We move beyond "what the model achieves" to address "what this means for the field" and "what comes next."

---

### 7.1 Landscape Shifts: What Changes After GLM-4.5

GLM-4.5 represents a inflection point in three dimensions that previously defined the boundary between open-source and proprietary models.

#### 7.1.1 The Open-Source Capability Ceiling Is Raised

Prior to GLM-4.5, the prevailing assumption was that **proprietary models would maintain a persistent lead on complex ARC tasks** due to superior training resources, data quality, and RL infrastructure. Table 3, 4, and 5 challenge this assumption:

| Benchmark Category | GLM-4.5 Performance | Proprietary Comparator | Gap |
|-------------------|---------------------|----------------------|-----|
| Agentic (Avg) | 58.1% | o3: 61.1% | 3.0 points |
| Reasoning (AIME 24) | 91.0% | o3: 90.3% | GLM-4.5 leads |
| Coding (SWE-Bench Verified) | 64.2% | Claude Sonnet 4: 70.4% | 6.2 points |

**Why This Matters:** The gap is no longer an order of magnitude—it is single-digit percentage points on most benchmarks. This shifts the research conversation from "can open-source compete?" to "what specific capabilities remain proprietary advantages?" The BrowseComp gap (26.4% vs o3's 49.7%) identifies web browsing as one such remaining advantage, but the reasoning and coding gaps are now bridgeable.

**Implication for Researchers:** Open-source model development is no longer about approximating proprietary capabilities—it is about achieving parity with different architectural and training trade-offs. This enables research that was previously impossible without API access, such as:
- Studying failure modes through model inspection
- Fine-tuning for domain-specific applications
- Auditing safety and bias characteristics directly

#### 7.1.2 Hybrid Reasoning Collapses the Model Dichotomy

The field has operated under a **binary model taxonomy**: reasoning models (DeepSeek-R1, OpenAI o1) that always produce chain-of-thought versus chat models (GPT-4, Claude) that respond directly. GLM-4.5 demonstrates this dichotomy is **architecturally unnecessary**.

> "By distilling from the output of distinct experts, the model learns to apply the most effective long CoT reasoning for each task... This approach allows the model to operate in both the reflective and immediate response modes." (Section 3.1)

**Why This Matters:** Organizations previously needed to maintain two model deployments—one for complex reasoning tasks and one for low-latency chat. GLM-4.5's hybrid approach enables:
- **Unified deployment**: Single model serves both use cases
- **Dynamic latency optimization**: Simple queries avoid unnecessary token generation
- **Consistent behavior**: Same model produces consistent outputs across task types

**Implication for Model Developers:** Future model designs should treat reasoning mode as a **learned behavior** rather than an architectural constraint. This suggests post-training data composition (balancing reasoning vs. direct responses) is more critical than architecture design for enabling hybrid capabilities.

#### 7.1.3 Training Paradigms Require Revision

Two specific training assumptions are contradicted by GLM-4.5's results:

**Assumption 1: Progressive Context Length Scaling in RL**

Prior work (reference [25]) recommended multi-stage RL with increasing output lengths. Figure 6 shows this approach **harms performance** (80.6% vs 83.4% on AIME24) because the model "unlearns" long-context capabilities during intermediate stages.

**Assumption 2: Width Drives Capability in MoE Models**

The prevailing MoE design philosophy prioritizes hidden dimension and expert count. GLM-4.5 inverts this, using 89 MoE layers (vs DeepSeek-V3's 58) with narrower hidden dimensions (5120 vs 7168), finding that "deeper models exhibited better reasoning capacity" (Section 2.1).

**Implication for Training Infrastructure:** RL pipelines should be redesigned to:
- Train at maximum target context length from the start
- Prioritize depth over width in architecture search for reasoning tasks
- Use reasoning benchmark performance (not training loss) as the architecture selection criterion

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

GLM-4.5 opens several research pathways that were previously inaccessible or unexplored.

#### 7.2.1 Understanding the Depth-Over-Width Mechanism

**Open Question:** Why does increased depth improve reasoning more than increased width, despite identical training loss?

Section 2.1 notes this finding is "counterintuitive" and that increased attention heads "does not improve training loss... but consistently improves performance on reasoning benchmarks." This suggests:

- Training loss is an **imperfect proxy** for reasoning capability
- Depth may enable better **hierarchical abstraction** for multi-step problems
- Attention head count may improve **information routing** between reasoning steps

**Suggested Research:**
- Conduct controlled ablation studies varying depth independently from width at scale
- Analyze attention patterns to understand how deeper models process reasoning chains
- Investigate whether depth benefits are task-specific (reasoning vs. knowledge retrieval)

#### 7.2.2 Hybrid Reasoning Mode Selection Mechanisms

**Open Question:** How does GLM-4.5 decide when to invoke thinking mode versus direct response?

The paper describes data balancing during SFT but does not explain the **selection mechanism**. This creates deployment uncertainty—practitioners cannot predict latency or control reasoning depth.

**Suggested Research:**
- Analyze output tokens to identify explicit mode-selection signals
- Develop probes to detect reasoning mode activation in hidden states
- Create user-controllable mode selection interfaces (e.g., system prompts to force thinking/non-thinking modes)
- Measure latency distributions across task types to quantify efficiency gains

#### 7.2.3 Addressing Identified Weaknesses

**Open Question:** What causes the BrowseComp (26.4% vs o3's 49.7%) and SimpleQA (26.4% vs GPT-4.1's 42.3%) gaps?

These represent the largest performance deficits and suggest fundamental limitations:

| Weakness | Hypothesized Cause | Research Direction |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| BrowseComp | Multi-step web retrieval and synthesis | Improve agent planning and information integration |
| SimpleQA | Reasoning optimization trades off with knowledge retrieval | Investigate internal representations for derivation vs. retrieval |
| Unfairness & Bias (77.4%) | Insufficient bias-specific training data | Develop targeted debiasing RL objectives |

**Suggested Research:**
- Conduct failure mode analysis on BrowseComp trajectories to identify where planning breaks down
- Compare internal representations between GLM-4.5 and high-SimpleQA models
- Develop bias-specific RL rewards beyond general safety training

#### 7.2.4 Scaling RL Methodologies to New Domains

GLM-4.5's RL techniques (difficulty-based curriculum, single-stage at max context, token-weighted loss) were validated on mathematics, code, and science. These methods may transfer to:

- **Long-horizon planning**: Extend difficulty curriculum to agent task complexity
- **Creative tasks**: Apply dynamic sampling temperature to maintain exploration in open-ended generation
- **Multilingual reasoning**: Test whether expert-verified data quality matters equally across languages

**Suggested Research:**
- Replicate the two-stage difficulty curriculum on non-verifiable tasks using reward models
- Test single-stage RL at max context for long-document summarization
- Compare token-weighted vs. sequence-mean loss for dialogue generation

#### 7.2.5 Reproducing and Extending the RL Infrastructure

**Open Question:** How reproducible is the Slime RL framework for research groups without comparable infrastructure?

Section 3.5 describes sophisticated infrastructure (synchronous/asynchronous modes, FP8 rollout, Docker-based agent environments). The framework is open-source, but practical reproducibility depends on:

- Engineering effort to integrate heterogeneous agent frameworks
- Compute resources for parallel rollout and training engines
- Expertise in managing asynchronous training pipelines

**Suggested Research:**
- Document minimal viable configurations for different research scales
- Develop reference implementations for common agent task types
- Benchmark training efficiency across infrastructure configurations

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

GLM-4.5's ARC capabilities enable specific application categories that were previously limited to proprietary models.

#### 7.3.1 Software Engineering Automation

**Use Case:** Automated GitHub issue resolution and codebase maintenance

**Evidence:** 64.2% on SWE-Bench Verified (Table 5), 90.6% tool calling success rate on CC-Bench (Figure 13)

**Deployment Considerations:**
- The 128K context window supports large repositories but may require "history truncation" for very large codebases (Section 4.2.3)
- The XML-like function call template (Figure 4) reduces escaping errors when generating code as function parameters
- Terminal-Bench performance (37.5%) suggests competence in command-line tool orchestration

**When to Prefer GLM-4.5:**
- Open-source deployment is required (e.g., on-premises, data sovereignty)
- Code generation involves complex function parameters (benefits from reduced escaping)
- Budget constraints favor 32B activated parameters over dense 70B+ models

#### 7.3.2 Scientific and Mathematical Reasoning

**Use Case:** Research assistance, problem solving, and educational applications

**Evidence:** 91.0% on AIME 24, 79.1% on GPQA, 72.9% on LiveCodeBench (Table 4)

**Deployment Considerations:**
- Hybrid reasoning modes allow the model to skip deliberation for simple queries (e.g., factual lookups) while engaging full reasoning for complex problems
- The 128K context supports long scientific papers and multi-document synthesis
- Avg@32 reporting on AIME 24 suggests sampling multiple responses improves reliability for high-stakes applications

**When to Prefer GLM-4.5:**
- Applications require both quick answers and deep reasoning (e.g., tutoring systems)
- Domain-specific fine-tuning is planned (open weights enable customization)
- Cost sensitivity favors MoE inference over dense alternatives

#### 7.3.3 Agentic Workflows

**Use Case:** Multi-step task automation with tool interaction

**Evidence:** 70.1% on TAU-Bench, 77.8% on BFCL V3, 26.4% on BrowseComp (Table 3)

**Deployment Considerations:**
- The iterative self-distillation approach (Section 3.3.2) suggests agent performance can be improved post-deployment with domain-specific trajectories
- Process format penalties ensure reliable tool call formatting—critical for production systems
- BrowseComp weakness (26.4%) suggests caution for web-research-heavy workflows

**When to Prefer GLM-4.5:**
- Tool integration is core to the application (benefits from XML function call template)
- Workflows involve 5-20 interaction turns (Figure 8 shows accuracy scales with test-time compute up to ~64 turns)
- Open-source evaluation toolkit enables custom benchmark development

#### 7.3.4 Multilingual Deployment

**Use Case:** Cross-language applications with reasoning requirements

**Evidence:** Human evaluation scores of 8.37 (Chinese), 8.66 (English), 8.49 (Other Languages) (Tables 8, 9, 10)

**Deployment Considerations:**
- Strong Chinese performance (8.37 vs Kimi-K2's 7.03) reflects bilingual training emphasis
- "Other languages" category is not broken down—practitioners should evaluate specific target languages
- Translation evaluation (Table 12: 1.71 vs specialized models at 0.38-0.65) suggests cultural reasoning transfers to translation tasks

**When to Prefer GLM-4.5:**
- Applications span English and Chinese with reasoning requirements
- Cultural context understanding is critical (e.g., translating idioms, slang)
- Specialized translation models lack reasoning capabilities needed for domain-specific content

**Guidance:** GLM-4.5 is optimal when:
- Open-source deployment is required
- Balanced ARC capabilities are needed (not maximum in any single dimension)

### 7.5 Summary: The Path Forward

GLM-4.5 demonstrates that **open-source models can achieve near-parity with proprietary systems** on agentic, reasoning, and coding tasks through careful architecture design (depth-over-width MoE), training methodology (multi-stage pre-training with mid-training specialization), and post-training (expert iteration with RL). The work shifts the field's focus from "can open-source compete?" to "what specific capabilities remain gaps?"

**Key Takeaways for Researchers:**
1. **Architecture matters**: Depth-over-width and increased attention heads improve reasoning independent of training loss
2. **Training paradigms need revision**: Single-stage RL at max context outperforms progressive scaling
3. **Hybrid reasoning is achievable**: Data balancing during post-training enables dynamic mode selection
4. **Weaknesses identify opportunities**: BrowseComp, SimpleQA, and bias gaps are concrete targets for improvement

**Key Takeaways for Practitioners:**
1. **GLM-4.5 is production-ready** for software engineering, scientific reasoning, and agentic workflows
2. **GLM-4.5-Air offers efficiency** for latency-sensitive deployments with modest performance trade-offs
3. **Integration requires attention** to function call templates, context management, and domain-specific evaluation
4. **Alternatives remain preferable** for maximum factual knowledge, web browsing, or lowest bias risk

The release of GLM-4.5 and GLM-4.5-Air represents not just a model announcement but a **research infrastructure contribution**—enabling the community to study, extend, and improve upon ARC capabilities in open-source systems. The path forward involves addressing identified weaknesses, understanding the mechanisms behind successful design choices, and extending the methodology to new domains beyond mathematics, code, and science.
