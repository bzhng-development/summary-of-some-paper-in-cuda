## 1. Executive Summary
This paper introduces SAIL-VL2, an efficient open-suite vision-language foundation model available in 2B and 8B parameter scales that achieves state-of-the-art performance through a progressive training framework, large-scale data curation, and sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures. Trained on 776B tokens across 106 datasets, SAIL-VL2-2B ranks first among officially released open-source models under 4B on the OpenCompass leaderboard, while its SAIL-VL2-Thinking variant matches larger closed-source models on reasoning benchmarks like MMMU and MathVista. By delivering strong multimodal understanding and complex reasoning capabilities without the computational burden of massive dense models, SAIL-VL2 establishes a scalable, high-performance foundation for the open-source multimodal community.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Efficiency-Performance Trade-off in Vision-Language Models

The fundamental challenge this paper addresses is the **computational efficiency versus performance trade-off** in Large Vision-Language Models (LVMs). As stated in the Introduction (Section 1), "Scaling up model parameters and training data to make LVMs 'larger' has emerged as a pivotal approach for pushing the performance boundaries of LVMs." However, this scaling paradigm "imposes considerable challenges in terms of computational demands and training, as well as deployment costs."

This tension creates a critical gap: while closed-source models and large-scale open-source models (e.g., 70B+ parameter LVMs) achieve strong performance, they remain inaccessible for many real-world applications due to hardware requirements, latency constraints, and operational costs. The SAIL-VL series explicitly targets the principle of **"small model, strong performance"** — exploring "how knowledge can be effectively injected through efficient architectures and training strategies" rather than relying solely on parameter scaling.

### Why This Problem Matters

The importance of efficient LVMs extends across both practical and theoretical dimensions:

**Real-World Impact:**
- **Deployment feasibility:** Models at the 2B–8B parameter scale can run on consumer-grade hardware or edge devices, enabling applications in mobile assistants, real-time document analysis, and embedded vision systems that larger models cannot support.
- **Cost efficiency:** Training and inference costs scale superlinearly with model size. An efficient 2B model that matches a 7B model's performance reduces computational expenses by approximately 3–4×, making multimodal AI accessible to smaller organizations.
- **Latency requirements:** Applications like live video understanding, OCR in document workflows, and interactive visual assistants demand sub-second response times that dense large models struggle to provide.

**Theoretical Significance:**
- **Knowledge injection efficiency:** The paper investigates whether performance gains come primarily from scale or from better training methodologies. If efficient architectures can match larger models through superior data curation and training strategies, this challenges the assumption that "bigger is always better."
- **Modality alignment:** Understanding how to effectively bridge the "modality and semantic gap" between visual and linguistic tokens (Section 2) remains an open research question. SAIL-VL2's progressive alignment strategy offers insights into how heterogeneous representation spaces can be unified without massive parameter counts.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches

Before SAIL-VL2, several approaches existed, each with notable shortcomings:

**1. Dense Scaling-First Models (Qwen2.5-VL, InternVL3, InternVL3.5)**
These models follow the conventional paradigm of increasing parameter counts to improve performance. While effective, they face diminishing returns at smaller scales. As shown in Table 8 and Table 9, prior open-source models under 4B parameters consistently underperform on reasoning benchmarks like MMMU and MathVista. For instance, Qwen2.5-VL-3B achieves only 48.11 on MMMUval, while SAIL-VL2-2B reaches 47.67 with fewer parameters — demonstrating that architecture and training matter more than raw scale alone.

**2. Single-Stage Training Pipelines**
Many prior LVMs employ a straightforward pre-training followed by supervised fine-tuning (SFT) approach. This fails to systematically build capabilities from coarse-grained alignment to fine-grained reasoning. The Introduction notes that training paradigms have progressed "from teacher-forcing supervised learning to hybrid methods, integrating supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and reinforcement learning (RL)." However, few models implement a truly progressive framework that incrementally injects knowledge at different granularities.

**3. Static Data Curation**
Prior work often relies on aggregating existing datasets without rigorous quality filtering. Section 3.1.1 reveals that "15–20% of captions fall below an acceptable threshold (score &lt; 3)" in raw corpora like SAIL-Caption, "highlighting substantial noise in the raw corpus." Training on such noisy data wastes compute budget and can degrade model performance through spurious correlations.

**4. Fixed-Resolution Vision Encoders**
Most vision encoders process images at fixed resolutions (e.g., 448×448), which limits fine-grained perception for high-resolution documents, charts, and detailed scenes. Section 2.1.2 notes that "conventional positional embeddings are fixed-length and hard to generalize" to arbitrary resolutions, creating a bottleneck for tasks like OCR and visual grounding.

**5. Lack of Reasoning Specialization**
Standard LVMs struggle with multi-step reasoning tasks. Section 1 states that "many real-world tasks demand sophisticated reasoning and multi-step thought processes, which remain challenging for standard LVMs." Prior models either lack dedicated reasoning training or require specialized prompting to activate reasoning capabilities, limiting their out-of-the-box utility.

### How SAIL-VL2 Positions Itself

SAIL-VL2 differentiates itself through three strategic positioning choices relative to existing work:

**1. Efficiency-First Architecture Over Pure Scaling**
Rather than competing on parameter count, SAIL-VL2 focuses on architectural efficiency. Table 1 shows the model family spans 2B, 8B, and 30B-A3B (MoE with 3B activated parameters) configurations. The MoE design allows "parameter scaling while preserving computational efficiency through sparse activation" (Section 2.2). This positions SAIL-VL2 as a practical alternative to dense models that require full parameter activation at inference time.

**2. Progressive Training Over Single-Stage Optimization**
The training framework builds capabilities incrementally:
- **Stage I (Warm-up adaptation):** Only the adapter is tuned to perform coarse-grained vision-language alignment (Section 2.1.1).
- **Stage II (Fine-grained alignment):** Vision encoder and adapter are unlocked for deeper alignment.
- **Stage III (World knowledge injection):** All parameters are trained on diverse reasoning and QA tasks.

This contrasts with prior work that typically trains all components jointly from initialization, which can lead to unstable convergence and suboptimal alignment.

**3. Quality-Over-Quantity Data Curation**
Section 3.1.1 describes a systematic filtering pipeline using trained judge models (ITA and VIR judges) that achieve "over 90% accuracy and recall" (Table 2). This reduces 300M raw captions to 250M high-quality pairs, ensuring ">99% high quality" in retained data. The paper explicitly acknowledges that "directly relying on the LVM API to clean the entire dataset would incur prohibitive costs," so it trains efficient judge models on SAIL-VL-1.5 as a cost-effective alternative.

**4. Thinking-Fusion for Reasoning Without Specialized Prompting**
The SAIL-VL2-Thinking variant integrates Chain-of-Thought (CoT) training with reinforcement learning in a "thinking-fusion SFT–RL hybrid paradigm" (Abstract). Unlike models that require explicit reasoning prompts, SAIL-VL2-Thinking learns to "internalize the reasoning capabilities" such that it "provides accurate direct answers even when a user does not specifically prompt it for a step-by-step thinking process" (Section 4.2.5). This makes reasoning capabilities more accessible in production settings.

**5. Comprehensive Benchmarking Across 106 Datasets**
Rather than reporting results on a handful of popular benchmarks, SAIL-VL2 evaluates across 106 datasets spanning "General Open-source Multimodal Understanding Benchmarks," "OpenCompass evaluation system," and "open-source video understanding benchmarks" (Section 6.1). This comprehensive evaluation provides more reliable evidence of generalization than selective benchmark reporting.

### Relationship to SAIL-VL Predecessor

SAIL-VL2 builds directly on SAIL-VL (Dong et al., 2025a), introduced as "the successor to SAIL-VL" in the Abstract. Key upgrades include:
- **Data:** SAIL-Caption is upgraded to SAIL-Caption2 with judge-model filtering and chart caption integration (Section 3.1.1).
- **Training:** The progressive training framework is extended with a thinking-fusion SFT–RL paradigm for reasoning (Section 4).
- **Architecture:** Support for MoE designs (Qwen3-MoE backbones) beyond dense LLMs (Section 2.2).

This positions SAIL-VL2 not as a fundamentally new approach, but as a systematic refinement of proven methodologies with scale and quality improvements at each component.

Table 11 (conceptual, derived from paper content) summarizes how SAIL-VL2 positions itself:

By addressing the efficiency-performance trade-off through methodological innovation rather than scale alone, SAIL-VL2 positions itself as a practical foundation model for the open-source multimodal community — one that prioritizes deployability without sacrificing capability.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems engineering paper that presents a complete vision-language model training stack, where the core idea is that efficient multimodal intelligence emerges from carefully orchestrated data curation, progressive alignment strategies, and hybrid supervised-reinforcement learning paradigms rather than from parameter scaling alone.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

SAIL-VL2 is a complete training system that takes raw images, videos, and text data as input and produces a vision-language model capable of understanding visual content and reasoning about it through natural language. The system solves the problem of how to build high-performance multimodal models at efficient parameter scales by combining three interconnected solutions: a quality-filtered data pipeline that removes noisy training examples, a progressive training framework that builds capabilities from simple alignment to complex reasoning, and architectural innovations including sparse Mixture-of-Experts designs that scale parameters without proportional compute costs.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The SAIL-VL2 system consists of four major components connected in a sequential processing pipeline. First, the **SAIL-ViT Vision Encoder** takes images or video frames at various resolutions (from 448×448 fixed up to 1792×1792 for AnyRes variant) and converts them into sequences of visual tokens — typically 1,024 tokens for fixed-resolution input, with token counts proportional to resolution for AnyRes. Second, the **Vision-Language Adapter**, implemented as a lightweight two-layer MLP, projects these visual tokens into the language model's embedding space, adjusting dimensions and reducing the modality gap between visual and linguistic representations. Third, the **Large Language Model backbone** (Qwen3 series in dense 1.7B/8B or MoE 30B-A3B configurations) processes the combined visual and text tokens through standard transformer layers to produce multimodal representations. Fourth, the **Training Infrastructure** manages data flow through stream packing strategies that balance text and visual token counts across devices, achieving 50% training acceleration and 48% additional efficiency gains from visual packing alone. Information flows unidirectionally during inference: image → SAIL-ViT tokens → Adapter projections → LLM processing → text output. During training, gradients flow backward through all components according to the stage-specific trainable parameter configurations specified in Table 3 and Table 4.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the **vision encoder (SAIL-ViT)** because it is the first component that processes raw visual input, and its progressive training strategy establishes the foundation for all downstream multimodal capabilities.
- Next, we examine the **data curation pipeline** because the quality and distribution of training data directly determines what the vision encoder and LLM can learn during each training stage.
- We then detail the **pre-training recipe** including the AdaLRS learning rate scheduler, as this activates the model's basic multimodal alignment before any task-specific fine-tuning occurs.
- We proceed to the **post-training framework** with its four-stage thinking-fusion pipeline (LongCoT SFT → Verifiable RL → Think-Fusion SFT → Mixed RL), as this is where reasoning capabilities are systematically developed.
- We conclude with **training infrastructure optimizations** because stream packing and MoE-specific infrastructure enable the entire pipeline to run efficiently at scale.
- Throughout, we reference specific hyperparameters, token counts, and batch sizes from Tables 1–4 to ground explanations in concrete implementation details.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### 3.4.1 Vision Encoder: SAIL-ViT Progressive Alignment Strategy

The vision encoder is the foundational component that determines how well visual information can be understood and communicated to the language model. SAIL-ViT addresses the core challenge that visual tokens and linguistic tokens originate from heterogeneous representation spaces that must be aligned into a unified semantic space before the LLM can reason about them jointly.

**Why progressive training rather than joint optimization from initialization?** The paper adopts a three-stage progressive training strategy because training all components jointly from random initialization leads to unstable convergence and suboptimal alignment between modalities. By incrementally unlocking components and injecting knowledge at different granularities, the system builds stable representations before attempting complex multimodal reasoning.

**Stage I: Warm-up Adaptation** establishes coarse-grained alignment by freezing both the vision encoder and LLM while tuning only the adapter parameters. This stage uses 8 million simple multimodal understanding examples, specifically 4.9 million captioning samples from SAIL-Caption and 3.1 million OCR samples from IDL-WDS. Training runs for one epoch with a learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-4}$ and a batch size of 1920. The goal is to activate the adapter's capacity to perform initial adaptation of vision encoder outputs into the LLM domain without destabilizing the pre-trained representations in either component.

**Stage II: Fine-grained Alignment** deepens the alignment by keeping the LLM fixed while unlocking both the vision encoder and adapter for joint optimization. Beyond the Stage I datasets, this stage expands captioning data with an additional 6.7 million samples from SAIL-Caption, supplements OCR sources with DocStruct, and incorporates video-caption data for joint training to enrich distributional diversity. Training uses a reduced learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-5}$ and a batch size of 512 to enable finer adjustments without catastrophic forgetting of the coarse alignment established in Stage I.

**Stage III: World Knowledge Injection** moves beyond conventional alignment by integrating broad multimodal understanding and reasoning tasks. All parameters including the vision encoder, adapter, and LLM are unlocked and trained on 36.5 million samples spanning captioning, OCR, open-ended QA, math reasoning, short QA, and pure text corpora. This diverse and large-scale training significantly boosts the vision encoder's generalization ability across tasks. Training runs for one epoch with a learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-5}$ and a batch size of 512. The vision encoder trained in this final stage becomes SAIL-ViT and provides the foundation for subsequent SAIL-VL2 training.

**SAIL-ViT Family Variants** include two configurations to accommodate different multimodal requirements. The vanilla ViT accepts fixed 448×448 inputs, where each image is split into 32×32 non-overlapping patches with patch size 14, producing 1,024 tokens fused with learnable positional embeddings. High-resolution images are tiled into multiple 448×448 crops, encoded independently, and their tokens aggregated for downstream processing. The SAIL-ViT-AnyRes variant supports arbitrary resolutions up to 1792×1792 given a maximum input length of 16,384 tokens. Since conventional positional embeddings are fixed-length and hard to generalize, SAIL-ViT-AnyRes adopts an interpolation-based mechanism where pre-trained embeddings are resized to match the input resolution, providing a global prior that improves extrapolation. Images are thus converted into token sequences proportional to their resolution, enabling fine-grained perception for high-resolution documents and charts.

**Empirical Validation of SAIL-ViT Effectiveness** appears in Table 6, which compares different visual backbones on image classification tasks. SAIL-ViT-Large demonstrates significant improvements over the baseline AIMv2 model on a CV benchmark consisting of four test sets, with an average gain of +1.5%. For SAIL-ViT-Huge, the average improvement was +2.11%. By increasing the data scale during the world knowledge infusion stage, SAIL-ViT-Huge-v2 further enhanced vision task performance by +2.73% compared to the baseline. These results highlight the superior performance and scalability of SAIL-ViT compared to baseline ViT models on image classification benchmarks.

**Multimodal Feature Alignment Verification** appears in Table 7 and Figure 6, which quantify how closely visual features align with textual features in the embedding space. The paper computes three distance metrics: average nearest neighbor distance, Wasserstein distance, and mean overall distance between visual feature clusters from SAIL-ViT and textual feature vectors from LLMs. For Qwen3-8B paired with SAIL-ViT, the mean overall distance is 10.06, compared to 11.24 when paired with AIMv2 baseline. Figure 6 visualizes token embedding distributions across different models, showing that visual feature vectors extracted by SAIL-ViT are more compact and exhibit greater overlap with textual feature vectors, whereas the baseline model produces more dispersed visual features with less overlap with the textual space. These results demonstrate that SAIL-ViT effectively reduces the gap between visual and textual feature spaces.

#### 3.4.2 Data Curation Pipeline: Quality Filtering and Synthetic Generation

The data curation pipeline addresses a critical observation from Section 3.1.1: analysis of raw caption corpora reveals that 15–20% of captions fall below an acceptable threshold with scores less than 3, highlighting substantial noise in the raw corpus. Training on such noisy data wastes compute budget and can degrade model performance through spurious correlations.

**Automatic Caption Quality Assessment** evaluates caption quality from two complementary perspectives. Visual Information Richness (VIR) assesses the informativeness of visual content across three aspects: instances and entities (number, diversity, and distinctiveness), visual complexity (color variety, composition, and texture), and fine-grained details (attributes such as textures, small objects, or contextual relationships). Image-Text Alignment (ITA) measures how well captions match visual content across specificity (precision of details), completeness (coverage of all key elements), and accuracy (absence of hallucinations or redundancy). For each evaluation dimension, captions are rated on a 1–5 scale where higher scores indicate superior quality.

**Judge Model Training Strategy** addresses the prohibitive cost of using LVM APIs to clean the entire dataset. The paper samples approximately 3 million captions from SAIL-Caption and obtains scoring annotations using a powerful LVM API, then trains two judge models on SAIL-VL-2B: a Score Judge for 1–5 rating and a Yes-or-No Judge for binary quality decision. To support robust judge model training, the team constructs a balanced dataset by uniformly resampling approximately 500,000 captions across the full score distribution. Both judge models achieve over 90% accuracy and recall as shown in Table 2. For example, the fine-tuned SAIL-VL-1.5-2B-ITA-Score achieves 0.916 precision and 0.903 recall, while the SAIL-VL-1.5-2B-VIR-Score achieves 0.983 precision and 0.975 recall. Notably, the base model SAIL-VL-1.5 already outperforms comparable zero-shot baselines such as InternVL3, which achieves only 0.269 precision and 0.070 recall on ITA judgment. The dataset is then cleaned by removing captions with scores less than 3 or judged as "No." Given judge accuracy above 90%, the retained captions are estimated to reach greater than 99% high quality. This process distills the original 300 million captions into a corpus of 250 million high-quality image–caption pairs.

**Chart Caption Data Construction** improves the model's chart and table understanding through a combination of code-based generation and conventional datasets. The paper designs a chart data pipeline that automatically and continuously generates high-quality training data. Given a prompt, large language models produce chart code in SVG or Python format, along with captions and QA pairs. The code is rendered into chart images, and the images, captions, and QA pairs are stored as standardized triplets for training. The pipeline supports a wide range of chart types including bar, line, pie, scatter, area, histogram, heatmap, and box plots, and allows flexible configuration of parameters including language, model, chart type, and generation rate while ensuring semantic validity. To complement this synthetic pipeline, the team collects open-source chart caption datasets in both English and Chinese, including PixMo-cap and DVQA, to enhance diversity. An LLM-based annotation workflow annotates both open-source and auto-rendered charts under carefully designed prompts, with manual sampling for quality control. This process yields 400,000 automatically generated captions and 1.29 million captions collected from conventional chart datasets, with an equal distribution of English and Chinese. Through the aforementioned filtering and collection strategies, SAIL-Caption2 consists of 250 million general captions and 1.69 million chart captions.

**Synthetic VQA Data Generation** transforms caption data into QA format using LLMs to scale up QA data for efficient pre-training. The paper samples approximately 80 million entries from SAIL-Caption2 to construct synthetic VQA data, then employs a powerful LLM API to convert each caption into multiple question–answer pairs. In this process, the model generates several diverse questions per caption to ensure broad coverage of the visual content, and subsequently produces corresponding answers based on the input text. Although synthetic data may introduce distributional biases in linguistic expression because LLMs often produce homogenized phrasing with limited variability, the model benefits substantially from such large-scale synthetic data: performance improves smoothly with increasing training budget, exhibiting a logarithmic scaling trend with up to 180 million training samples as shown in Figure 4.

**Video Data Filtering and Selection** evaluates quality from two complementary perspectives for the 6.23 million video-QA samples initially curated from ShareGPTVideo-QA, NextQA, LongVideoBench-val, PerceptionTest-val, LLaVA-Video-QA, and VideoGPT. Frame–Instruction Alignment addresses the discrepancy that most video LVMs perform tasks on sampled frames whereas most existing datasets provide annotations based on continuous video streams, which can result in misalignment between sampled frames and instructions potentially leading to spurious learning or hallucinations. Data Quality and Task Difficulty assesses the overall richness of visual content capturing element diversity, scene complexity, and information density, as well as the difficulty of associated QA tasks including reasoning depth, requirements for spatial–temporal understanding, multi-instance interactions, and reliance on external knowledge. To operationalize these criteria, the paper defines three metrics: video–QA alignment (−1 to 10), video content richness (−1 to 7), and QA difficulty (−1 to 8), where a score of −1 indicates that the LVM API refused to respond while higher values represent better quality or greater difficulty. For final selection, videos are retained with alignment and content scores of at least 5 and a difficulty score of at least 3, yielding a refined corpus of 5.1 million high-quality samples for SAIL-Video.

**SAIL-Instruction2 Construction** refines and expands both scale and quality from the original SAIL-Instruction dataset. Beyond datasets employed in SAIL-Instruction such as LLaVA-OneVision and Cauldron, the team incorporates additional high-quality open-source instruction-tuning datasets including Mammoth, MMPR, and Molmo, thereby providing broader coverage and stronger supervision for instruction-following pre-training. To enhance effectiveness, the paper samples long-answer and reasoning-oriented instances from LLaVA-CoT, MMPR, and Condor datasets, fostering more complex reasoning behaviors. All incremental data undergo a two-stage validation process: a quality evaluation to ensure intrinsic reliability, and an incremental evaluation to verify performance gains when integrated with existing data, with only data passing both stages retained. To enhance diversity and mitigate distributional bias, a latent-class-based filtering strategy is employed where for each VQA sample, an LVM generates a descriptive phrase from which logits and latent embeddings are extracted to define its latent class. This approach maps heterogeneous samples into a unified semantic space, effectively bridging the representation gap across datasets. The original coarse-level dataset classification is enhanced by clustering latent classes into fine-grained semantic buckets, thereby expanding the category space by nearly tenfold. Using these refined classification buckets, uniform sampling is performed and samples are re-annotated with APIs from state-of-the-art closed-source models, improving both dataset accuracy and consistency. Finally, 20 million high-quality, diverse samples form the SAIL-Instruction2 dataset.

#### 3.4.3 Pre-Training Recipe: Progressive Activation and Adaptive Learning Rates

The pre-training process consists of two progressive sub-stages designed to gradually activate SAIL-VL2's multimodal alignment and understanding capabilities, as detailed in Table 3.

**Basic Multimodal Pre-Training** develops SAIL-VL2's fundamental multimodal alignment and understanding abilities. Starting with a pre-trained SAIL-ViT and a language-pretrained LLM, the system trains a randomly initialized MLP-based adapter to bridge the gap between vision and language modalities. A total of 64 million samples are used for model training in this stage, including general captions and chart captions from SAIL-Caption2, as well as high-quality OCR samples from IDL-WDS. Instead of training with a fixed learning rate, the initial learning rate is set as $2 \times 10^{-4}$, and the AdaLRS algorithm is applied during training. AdaLRS adjusts the initial learning rate upwards to $6.75 \times 10^{-4}$ effectively, surpassing the fixed learning rate baseline by a final loss advantage of over 0.06. The training batch size is set as 2048 in this stage. The trainable components progress from connector only, to connector plus ViT, to all parameters across the sub-stages.

**Multi-task Pre-Training** comprehensively strengthens SAIL-VL2's visual understanding and instruction-following capabilities. In this phase, all model parameters are unfrozen for joint optimization. Apart from general caption and OCR data as used in the basic pre-training stage, the system integrates a series of instruction-tuning datasets for model training, including open-source VQA data, synthetic VQA data, and text-based math reasoning samples. These instruction tuning data not only enhance SAIL-VL2's visual instruction following abilities, but also preserve the LLM's language abilities and therefore enable large-scale vision-language alignment training with visual understanding data. In total, 180 million samples with equal-sized visual understanding and instruction-tuning data are used in the multi-task pre-training stage. AdaLRS is not applied in this stage because the training loss on instruction tuning data exhibits poor correlation with model performance. The sequence length increases from 8192 to 16384 tokens in later phases to accommodate longer reasoning chains.

**Adaptive Learning Rate Search (AdaLRS)** is motivated by an empirical observation that the training loss of LLM/LVM pre-training, as well as the loss descent velocity with respect to the learning rate, exhibits a convex pattern with a shared optimum. AdaLRS dynamically adjusts the learning rate with a backtracking line search strategy based on the loss slope. The algorithm operates through four steps: when the loss descent slows down, the learning rate is tentatively increased; if the increase accelerates loss reduction, the adjustment is retained and training proceeds; if the increase further slows convergence, the model parameters and optimizer state are rolled back and the learning rate is decreased instead; through this procedure, suboptimal learning rates can be efficiently adjusted toward the neighborhood of the optimal value within a limited number of steps. The AdaLRS algorithm is formulated as:

$$
\eta_{t+k} =
\begin{cases}
\alpha'\eta_t & \text{if } v(\alpha'\eta_t) > v(\eta_t) + 2\epsilon \quad \text{(loss slope increases ↑)}, \\
\beta'\eta_t & \text{if } v(\alpha'\eta_t) &lt; v(\eta_t) - 2\epsilon \quad \text{(loss slope decreases ↓)}, \\
\eta_t & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

Here $v(\cdot)$ indicates the estimated loss curve slope obtained from a $k$-step window, while $\epsilon$ stands for the estimation error between $v$ and the true loss descent velocity $V$. The parameters $\alpha'$ and $\beta'$ are rectified learning rate scaling factors which satisfy $\alpha' = \max(\lambda_t \alpha, 1)$ and $\beta' = \frac{1}{\max(\lambda_t \beta, 1)}$, where $\alpha, \beta > 1$ are co-prime integers and $\lambda \in (0, 1)$ is a decay factor. This formulation enables efficient optimization without manual learning rate tuning across different training stages.

**Data Resampling Strategy** improves training efficiency and data utilization through a two-step approach. In the first step, resampling is conducted at the dataset level during the basic pre-training stage. Different datasets naturally exhibit distinct distributional biases, so by balancing the sampling ratios across datasets, the system increases the diversity of image–text pairs and ensures that the adapter is exposed to heterogeneous visual signals, which supports efficient alignment of vision features into the language space. In the second step, resampling is performed at the linguistic level during the multi-task pre-training stage. The focus is on SAIL-Caption2 and Synthetic VQA datasets, which are large in scale but often show imbalanced language patterns because many samples are generated or post-processed by LLMs. To address this, the data is rebalanced at the n-gram level, thereby improving lexical and structural diversity. This linguistic-level resampling boosts data efficiency, allowing SAIL-VL2 to achieve stronger multimodal understanding and more robust instruction-following capabilities.

**Scaling-law Analysis** investigates the impact of scaling multi-task pre-training data on SAIL-VL2 performance by expanding the training budget to 360 billion tokens and systematically evaluating the model across a wide range of benchmark subsets. As illustrated in Figure 4, performance exhibits consistent and monotonic improvements, yielding a smooth empirical scaling curve across overall benchmarks, natural-scene VQA datasets, and OCR VQA tasks. The training corpus consists of general captions and VQA data, with approximately 50% of the latter synthesized by annotator models such as SAIL-Captioner and Qwen3. While synthetic supervision may introduce linguistic biases, the increased scale and diversity of training data substantially enhance generalization and reasoning. These findings highlight data scaling as a critical factor driving performance gains in the multi-task pre-training stage, and emphasize the importance of large-scale, diverse corpora for advancing multimodal understanding.

#### 3.4.4 Post-Training Framework: Thinking-Fusion SFT–RL Hybrid Paradigm

The post-training process is structured as a holistic pipeline that begins with an instruction-tuning SFT stage aimed at injecting general knowledge and improving fundamental instruction adherence, followed by a two-phase thinking-fusion training pipeline that progressively combines SFT with reinforcement learning to strengthen reasoning ability, as detailed in Table 4.

**Basic Supervised Fine-Tuning** is organized as a progressive knowledge injection pipeline where the first three phases focus on training the image modality, employing a curriculum learning strategy to gradually introduce increasingly complex data as training progresses, thereby fine-tuning all parameters of SAIL-VL2. In the first phase, SAIL-VL2 learns foundational instruction-following abilities while incorporating world knowledge from the Infinity-MM Stage2 dataset. The second phase optimizes the model's visual instruction-following capabilities using SAIL-Instruction2, the newly curated 20 million-sample high-quality visual instruction dataset. In the third phase, the model is exposed to a challenging subset of visual instruction data including LLaVA-CoT, MMPR, and Condor datasets, enabling it to tackle a wider range of complex instruction-following tasks. To further enhance SAIL-VL2's understanding of video while preserving its proficiency in image comprehension, a video-image mixture training strategy is introduced in the final phase. This strategy combines high-quality image data from SAIL-Instruction2 with carefully filtered video data from the SAIL-Video dataset, maintaining a balanced 1:1 ratio between video and image data, thus ensuring effective multimodal learning.

**Model Soup Strategy** enhances model performance after SFT by categorizing trained models into two groups: homogeneous models which follow similar optimization trajectories such as comparable hyperparameter settings and data compositions, and heterogeneous models which differ in these aspects. As shown in Table 5, experimental results reveal that merging homogeneous models consistently yields stable and notable performance improvements, whereas merging heterogeneous models often causes performance degradation. For example, on the ChartQA benchmark, homologous model merging improves from base model scores of 83.88 and 82.80 to a merge model score of 85.60, while heterologous merging degrades performance to 10.36. Based on these observations, the model soup strategy focuses on merging homogeneous models to achieve reliable performance gains.

**LongCoT Supervised Fine-tuning** is designed specifically to enhance the model's step-by-step reasoning capabilities for complex problems by fine-tuning the model exclusively on a large-scale, high-quality Long-Chain-of-Thought dataset. The data curation pipeline collects diverse datasets including VisualWebInstruct, MathV360K, and LLaVA-CoT, then applies a cleaning protocol which includes removing content extraneous to the reasoning process such as system prompts and conflicting hints, unifying the output format by placing the thinking process within  and &lt;/think> tags and the final answer in the \boxed{} tags, and deduplicating samples based on image and question pairs. For CoT data generation, a guided prompting strategy provides both the question and its ground-truth, tasking the model to generate a detailed reasoning process that logically connects the two. Following this generation, a rigorous filtering workflow executes a final deduplication pass, format validation, and three critical quality filters: Redundancy Filtering measures the token overlap between the generated CoT and the final answer, discarding samples with high similarity to penalize trivial reasoning; Answer Distillation utilizes a judge model to refine and shorten overly verbose ground-truth answers, mitigating the tendency for models to overthink; CoT Length Balancing analyzes the token length distribution of the generated CoT and resamples to ensure a balanced representation of reasoning complexities. The final dataset comprises 400,000 LongCoT samples.

The LongCoT SFT training recipe fine-tunes the model on the 400,000 LongCoT samples with all model parameters trained for one epoch using the AdamW optimizer with a global batch size of 1024. A cosine learning rate schedule is employed with a peak learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-6}$. The optimization objective is a standard next-token prediction over the entire output sequence:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{LongCoT SFT}} = -\frac{1}{|D_{\text{CoT}}|} \sum_{(I,T,A) \in D_{\text{CoT}}} \log P_\theta(T \circ A | I)
$$

where $D_{\text{CoT}}$ is the LongCoT dataset, $I$ is the input instruction, $T$ is the thinking process, $A$ is the final answer, and $\circ$ denotes string concatenation. This training strategy explicitly teaches the model to generate a detailed reasoning process before providing the final answer, thereby significantly enhancing its ability to solve complex problems that require step-by-step thinking.

**Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards** refines the model by optimizing it against a reward system focused on two primary objectives: the correctness of the final answer and adherence to the specified output format. The dataset for this RL stage is constructed from a diverse collection of public sources covering tasks such as Math, Puzzle, Science, OCR, and Counting. This raw data is refined through a two-stage filtering pipeline. The first stage mitigates reward hacking by employing an LLM to convert multiple-choice questions with numerical or symbolic answers into a free-response format. The second stage applies a difficulty-based filtering strategy using the model from the previous stage, evaluated on its pass@4 score. Specifically, for tasks that do not require explicit reasoning, trivial problems where the model achieves a perfect score (pass@4=1) are discarded. For reasoning-heavy tasks, a more stringent filter is applied, retaining problems that are challenging yet solvable by removing both the easiest (pass@4=1) and hardest (pass@4=0) samples. This pipeline yields a dataset of 70,000 STEM samples for RL training.

The reward system is composed of two main signals. The Answer Reward is a rule-based reward that checks for the correctness of the final answer contained within the \boxed{} tag, providing a clear, objective signal of success for problems with verifiable solutions such as mathematical calculations. The Format Reward ensures the model's output adheres to the required structure, specifically by verifying that the reasoning process is correctly enclosed within  and &lt;/think> tags. Both reward signals are discrete binary values (0 or 1), providing direct and unambiguous feedback for RL training.

The training recipe optimizes the LongCoT SFT model using a Proximal Policy Optimization-based algorithm to learn a single, unified reasoning policy. To accommodate different model architectures, specialized PPO variants are employed: for dense models, DAPO is used as a memory-efficient optimizer that enhances training stability; for Mixture-of-Experts models, GSPO is used which provides stable and targeted updates to individual experts. The training is configured with a context length of 16384 and a maximum generation length of 4096 tokens. In each training episode, 2048 rollouts are generated from the policy and 8 gradient updates are performed using a mini-batch size of 512. The learning rate for the policy network is set to $1 \times 10^{-6}$. To encourage exploration, the PPO clipping value is dynamically adjusted within the range of [0.20, 0.28].

**Think-Fusion Supervised Fine-tuning** is designed to simultaneously enhance the model's reasoning capabilities while maintaining its broad general understanding by fine-tuning the model on a mixed dataset strategically composed to address this dual objective. The dataset for this stage is constructed as a strategic mixture where approximately 90% of the data consists of general-purpose, direct-answer instruction pairs covering a wide range of common tasks to reinforce the model's wide-ranging abilities, ensuring it remains a capable and versatile assistant. The remaining 10% of the dataset is composed of high-quality Chain-of-Thought exemplars specifically chosen to cultivate the model's ability to perform complex, step-by-step reasoning. Crucially, these are not randomly selected CoT examples but are carefully harvested from the preceding reinforcement learning stage through a process of rejection sampling, meaning only the best-performing reasoning chains that the model itself generated are selected, ensuring the examples are both correct and logically sound. The final dataset for this stage comprises 100,000 high-quality CoT samples and 900,000 direct question-answering samples, creating a total of one million training instances.

The Think-Fusion SFT training recipe fine-tunes the model on the mixed dataset with all model parameters trained for one epoch using the AdamW optimizer with a global batch size of 1024. A cosine learning rate schedule is employed with a peak learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-6}$. The optimization objective is a standard next-token prediction where the loss is applied conditionally to teach the model both response styles. For direct-answer pairs $(I, A)$ where $I$ is the instruction and $A$ is the answer, the loss is computed only on the answer tokens. For CoT exemplars $(I, T, A)$ where $T$ is the thinking process, the loss is computed across the entire sequence including both the thinking process and the final answer. This dual-target loss function is formally denoted as:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{Think-Fusion SFT}} = -\frac{1}{|D|} \left( \sum_{(I,T,A) \in D_{\text{CoT}}} \log P_\theta(T \circ A | I) + \sum_{(I,A) \in D_{\text{direct}}} \log P_\theta(A | I) \right)
$$

where $D$ is the entire mixed dataset and $\circ$ denotes string concatenation. This training strategy explicitly teaches the model to adapt its output, providing concise answers for simple queries while generating detailed reasoning for complex problems that require it.

**Reinforcement Learning with a Mixed Reward System** further enhances the model's reasoning capabilities through an RL stage guided by a mixed reward system that provides comprehensive feedback beyond a single correctness score. The reward system integrates three primary signals: the Answer Reward which assesses final answer quality using a hybrid of rule-based checks for verifiable problems and an LVM-based judge for nuanced tasks; the Thinking Reward which holistically evaluates the entire reasoning path on its logical soundness, factual grounding, and answer consistency; and the Format Reward which encourages adherence to the desired output structure with the thinking process in  and &lt;/think> tags and the final answer in a \boxed{} tag. All reward signals are discrete binary values (0 or 1), and they are integrated into a final reward formulation where the coefficients of the three components sum to 1.

The data curation refines the dataset from the preceding Reasoning RL stage by applying a rejection sampling methodology to the previous set of reasoning-intensive samples, a process that filters for "hard cases" where the model's performance was suboptimal. This creates a more challenging, curated dataset of 50,000 STEM samples. To ensure the model retains its broad conversational and instruction-following capabilities while improving on these hard cases, these specialized samples are combined with 50,000 new general-purpose samples from LLaVA-OneVision. The final training dataset therefore consists of 100,000 samples, balancing advanced reasoning with general utility.

The training recipe follows the same configuration as the RL with Verifiable Rewards stage. The Think-Fusion SFT model is optimized using PPO-based algorithms (DAPO for dense models, GSPO for MoE models) on the curated 100,000 sample dataset. All training hyperparameters such as learning rate, batch size, and optimizer settings are kept identical to the prior stage. A key outcome observed from this process is that the model effectively internalizes the reasoning capabilities it was taught — it learns the logical pathways to the correct answer without needing to explicitly write out each step. This allows it to provide accurate direct answers even when a user does not specifically prompt it for a step-by-step thinking process, making the final model both powerful and efficient in practice.

#### 3.4.5 Training Infrastructure: Stream Packing and MoE Optimization

**Stream Packing Strategy** jointly optimizes the handling of language and vision tokens to enhance training efficiency and reduce computational waste. This strategy integrates two complementary components: batching with online packing and visual packing, ensuring balanced workloads, reduced memory footprint, and even improved model performance.

In conventional LVM training, all sequences in a batch are padded to the length of the longest sequence, resulting in severe inefficiency when sample lengths are highly skewed. The batching and online packing component addresses this by concatenating variable-length samples into continuous streams, thereby minimizing the number of padding tokens. To preserve independence across samples, positional embeddings and attention masks are adjusted accordingly. To further improve efficiency, each node maintains a buffer of candidate samples from which micro-batches are dynamically constructed. This online packing procedure enhances sample diversity, maximizes GPU utilization by filling sequences to the hardware-supported maximum length, and achieves balanced workloads across devices. Moreover, to prevent starvation of long samples that exceed the maximum sequence length, their periodic inclusion is enforced during batch construction.

Visual packing extends stream packing with a visual token balancing constraint to address discrepancies in visual token counts. In practice, different samples may produce substantially different numbers of visual tokens, particularly in models such as SAIL-VL2-AnyRes where original image resolutions are preserved, leading to workload imbalance across devices and increased computational cost for the vision encoder. To mitigate this, during sample selection from the buffer, an additional condition is imposed that equalizes the number of visual tokens across devices. This ensures balanced computational loads for both the LLM and the vision encoder, thereby improving overall training efficiency.

The packing strategies bring significant improvements in both training efficiency and model performance. Compared with the baseline, data packing nearly doubles Streaming Multiprocessor utilization and accelerates training by 50%, while visual packing alleviates excessive memory usage in the vision encoder, leading to a further 48% gain in efficiency. Beyond efficiency, ablation studies show that models trained with packing achieve an average +0.7% improvement over the baseline, with particularly strong gains on open-ended QA benchmarks such as LLaVA-Bench and MMVet, owing to the mitigation of sequence truncation and more effective training on long-context multimodal inputs.

**MoE Infrastructure** addresses substantial challenges arising from massive parameter scale and inherent imbalance in expert activation for Mixture-of-Experts models. Although MoE architectures reduce per-step computation compared to dense models, the overall training process remains dominated by two sources of overhead: the computational cost of activating multiple experts and the communication burden associated with intensive inter-device data exchange.

To alleviate computational overhead, optimized kernel fusion is implemented for expert operations, achieving up to a 3× training speedup. These optimizations are extended to core modules such as self-attention and Layer Normalization, thereby improving end-to-end efficiency. To address communication inefficiencies, hardware-adapted infrastructures are designed. On NPU processors, a Megatron-based distributed framework is employed that partitions both MoE and ViT across devices, combining pipeline and expert parallelism to reduce memory footprint and communication overhead through efficient weight sharing. On NVIDIA devices such as H20 and A100, DeepSpeed ZeRO-2 is adopted with CPU offloading, which provides superior communication efficiency relative to ZeRO-3 and directly mitigates the computation–communication imbalance intrinsic to MoE training.

#### 3.4.6 Design Choices and Trade-offs

**Why MoE over Dense Scaling?** The paper adopts Mixture-of-Experts architectures because they provide an effective strategy for scaling LLMs by replacing standard MLP layers with parallel expert modules where a gating function activates only a small subset of experts for each token, enabling parameter scaling while preserving computational efficiency through sparse activation. The SAIL-VL2-30B-A3B model has 30.5 billion total parameters but only 3 billion activated parameters during inference, as shown in Table 1. This positions SAIL-VL2 as a practical alternative to dense models that require full parameter activation at inference time.

**Why Progressive Training over Joint Optimization?** The three-stage progressive training strategy for SAIL-ViT is chosen because training all components jointly from random initialization leads to unstable convergence and suboptimal alignment between modalities. By incrementally unlocking components and injecting knowledge at different granularities, the system builds stable representations before attempting complex multimodal reasoning. Stage I establishes coarse alignment with only the adapter trained, Stage II deepens alignment with ViT and adapter unlocked, and Stage III injects world knowledge with all parameters trainable.

**Why Judge Models over API-based Filtering?** The paper explicitly acknowledges that directly relying on the LVM API to clean the entire dataset would incur prohibitive costs. Therefore, two judge models are trained on SAIL-VL-1.5-2B: a Score Judge for 1–5 rating and a Yes-or-No Judge for binary quality decision. Both judge models achieve over 90% accuracy and recall, enabling efficient filtering of 300 million raw captions down to 250 million high-quality pairs with estimated quality above 99% for retained data.

**Why Thinking-Fusion over Pure CoT Training?** The Think-Fusion SFT stage combines 90% direct-answer instruction pairs with 10% CoT exemplars to balance reasoning capabilities with general utility. Pure CoT training would degrade the model's ability to provide concise answers for simple queries. The dual-target loss function teaches the model to adapt its output style based on query complexity, providing concise answers for simple queries while generating detailed reasoning for complex problems that require it.

**Why Rejection Sampling for RL Data?** The RL stages employ rejection sampling to filter for high-quality reasoning chains that the model itself generated during the preceding RL stage. This ensures the examples are both correct and logically sound, avoiding the distributional mismatch that would occur if external CoT data were used. The final Mixed Reward RL stage further refines this by filtering for "hard cases" where the model's performance was suboptimal, creating a more challenging curated dataset of 50,000 STEM samples combined with 50,000 general-purpose samples.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the fundamental contributions that distinguish SAIL-VL2 from prior vision-language models. We distinguish between **incremental improvements** (refinements of existing methods) and **fundamental innovations** (new paradigms that change how capabilities are achieved).

---

### 4.1 Thinking-Fusion SFT–RL Hybrid Paradigm (Fundamental Innovation)

**What makes it different:** Prior reasoning-enhanced LVMs typically rely on explicit Chain-of-Thought prompting at inference time — users must request step-by-step reasoning for the model to engage in multi-step problem solving. SAIL-VL2-Thinking instead **internalizes reasoning capabilities** through a four-stage post-training pipeline (LongCoT SFT → Verifiable RL → Think-Fusion SFT → Mixed RL) such that the model "provides accurate direct answers even when a user does not specifically prompt it for a step-by-step thinking process" (Section 4.2.5).

**Why this matters:** This represents a paradigm shift from **prompt-dependent reasoning** to **internalized reasoning**. The key insight is that reasoning is not merely an output format but a learned cognitive capability that can be activated implicitly. The Think-Fusion SFT stage is particularly innovative: by mixing 90% direct-answer pairs with 10% CoT exemplars harvested via rejection sampling from the preceding RL stage, the model learns to **adapt its output style based on query complexity** without explicit user instruction.

**Evidence of significance:** Table 10 shows SAIL-VL2-8B-Thinking achieves 54.4 on the OpenCompass reasoning benchmark average, surpassing closed-source models like Gemini-2.0-Flash (50.6) and competing with GPT-4o-latest (54.8). More importantly, the MoE variant SAIL-VL2-A3B-Thinking reaches 53.6 with only 3B activated parameters — demonstrating that reasoning capability scales with training methodology rather than parameter count alone.

**Distinction from prior work:** Models like Kimi-VL-A3B-Thinking and Keye-VL-8B-Thinking also employ CoT training, but SAIL-VL2's rejection sampling from RL-generated reasoning chains ensures the CoT exemplars are **self-consistent and logically sound** rather than externally annotated. This creates a self-improving loop where the model's own best reasoning becomes training data for future capability enhancement.

---

### 4.2 Judge Model-Based Data Curation at Scale (Fundamental Innovation)

**What makes it different:** Prior data filtering approaches either (a) rely on expensive LVM API calls for quality assessment across entire corpora, or (b) use simple heuristic filters that miss nuanced quality issues. SAIL-VL2 trains **specialized judge models** (ITA for Image-Text Alignment, VIR for Visual Information Richness) on SAIL-VL-1.5-2B that achieve "over 90% accuracy and recall" (Table 2) while being orders of magnitude cheaper to run than API-based filtering.

**Why this matters:** The paper explicitly acknowledges that "directly relying on the LVM API to clean the entire dataset would incur prohibitive costs" (Section 3.1.1). By training judge models on a balanced 500K sample subset annotated via API, then applying these judges to filter 300M captions down to 250M high-quality pairs, the team achieves an estimated ">99% high quality" in retained data at a fraction of the cost. This makes large-scale data curation **economically feasible** for open-source efforts.

**Evidence of significance:** Table 2 shows zero-shot InternVL3-2B achieves only 0.269 precision and 0.070 recall on ITA judgment, while the fine-tuned SAIL-VL-1.5-2B-ITA-Score reaches 0.916 precision and 0.903 recall. This 3–4× improvement in filtering accuracy directly translates to training efficiency — removing 15–20% of noisy captions prevents wasted compute on spurious correlations.

**Distinction from prior work:** While prior work uses quality scoring (e.g., SAIL-Caption in SAIL-VL), SAIL-VL2 introduces **dual-dimension scoring** (VIR + ITA) with trained judge models rather than API-only assessment. This is a fundamental shift from **outsourced quality control** to **in-house scalable filtering** — a capability that compounds across training iterations as judge models improve alongside the main model.

---

### 4.3 Progressive Vision Encoder Alignment (Incremental Innovation with Significant Impact)

**What makes it different:** Standard LVM training typically unfreezes all components (vision encoder, adapter, LLM) simultaneously or after a brief warm-up. SAIL-ViT employs a **three-stage progressive unlocking** strategy: Stage I trains only the adapter with frozen ViT and LLM, Stage II unlocks ViT + adapter with frozen LLM, and Stage III trains all parameters on diverse reasoning tasks (Section 2.1.1).

**Why this matters:** The insight is that **modality alignment is a multi-granularity problem** requiring staged capability building. Coarse-grained alignment (Stage I) establishes basic vision-language correspondence without destabilizing pre-trained representations. Fine-grained alignment (Stage II) deepens this mapping before world knowledge injection (Stage III) attempts complex reasoning. This prevents the "catastrophic forgetting" that occurs when all components are optimized jointly from initialization.

**Evidence of significance:** Table 6 shows SAIL-ViT-Huge achieves 82.21 on ImageNet-1k compared to 81.68 for the AIMv2-Huge baseline (+0.53%), but more importantly, Table 7 demonstrates that SAIL-ViT features have **significantly closer distribution distance** to LLM text embeddings (mean overall distance 10.06 vs. 11.24 for AIMv2 with Qwen3-8B). Figure 6 visualizes this as tighter clustering and greater overlap between visual and textual feature spaces.

**Distinction from prior work:** Progressive training itself is not novel — prior work like LLaVA employs two-stage alignment. However, SAIL-ViT's **three-stage granularity** with explicit world knowledge injection as a distinct phase (36.5M samples across captioning, OCR, QA, math reasoning, and text corpora) is a refinement that directly addresses the "modality and semantic gap" challenge identified in Section 2.

---

### 4.4 AdaLRS: Loss-Guided Adaptive Learning Rate Search (Incremental Innovation)

**What makes it different:** Conventional LVM pre-training uses fixed or cosine-decayed learning rate schedules determined through manual tuning. AdaLRS dynamically adjusts the learning rate based on **loss slope estimation** during training — increasing the rate when loss descent slows (if it accelerates convergence) or decreasing it (if convergence further degrades) with parameter rollback capability (Section 3.2.3).

**Why this matters:** The empirical observation motivating AdaLRS is that "training loss of LLM/LVM pre-training, as well as the loss descent velocity with respect to the learning rate, exhibits a convex pattern with a shared optimum" (Section 3.2.3). Rather than manually searching for this optimum, AdaLRS **automatically navigates toward it** during training, reducing hyperparameter tuning overhead and improving optimization efficiency.

**Evidence of significance:** Section 3.2.1 reports that AdaLRS "adjusts the initial learning rate upwards to $6.75 \times 10^{-4}$ effectively, surpassing the fixed learning rate baseline by a final loss advantage of over 0.06." While this appears modest, loss differences of 0.06 in large-scale pre-training correlate with measurable benchmark improvements, and the automation benefit compounds across multiple training runs.

**Distinction from prior work:** Adaptive learning rate methods (e.g., Adam, AdamW) adjust per-parameter rates based on gradient statistics. AdaLRS operates at the **global schedule level**, adjusting the base learning rate based on loss trajectory — a meta-optimization approach more closely related to learning rate finders but integrated into training rather than performed as a separate search phase.

---

### 4.5 Stream Packing with Visual Token Balancing (Incremental Innovation)

**What makes it different:** Standard sequence packing concatenates variable-length text samples to reduce padding waste. SAIL-VL2 extends this to **joint text-visual token balancing**, ensuring that both language and vision encoder workloads are balanced across devices during training (Section 5.1).

**Why this matters:** In models like SAIL-VL2-AnyRes, different image resolutions produce vastly different visual token counts, creating workload imbalance that idle devices wait for stragglers. By imposing "an additional condition that equalizes the number of visual tokens across devices" during sample selection, the system achieves balanced computational loads for both the LLM and vision encoder.

**Evidence of significance:** Section 5.1 reports that "data packing nearly doubles Streaming Multiprocessor (SM) utilization and accelerates training by 50%, while visual packing alleviates excessive memory usage in the vision encoder, leading to a further 48% gain in efficiency." Additionally, "models trained with packing achieve an average +0.7% improvement over the baseline" on benchmarks — indicating that efficiency gains do not come at the cost of capability.

**Distinction from prior work:** Sequence packing for text-only LLMs is established practice. The novelty lies in **extending packing constraints to visual tokens** — a multimodal-specific optimization that acknowledges vision encoders as first-class computational bottlenecks rather than treating them as fixed preprocessing steps.

---

### 4.6 Synthesis: What Makes SAIL-VL2 Fundamentally Different

| Innovation | Type | Primary Benefit | Prior Art Gap Addressed |
|------------|------|-----------------|------------------------|
| Thinking-Fusion SFT–RL | Fundamental | Internalized reasoning without prompting | Prompt-dependent CoT models |
| Judge Model Curation | Fundamental | Scalable quality filtering at 300M scale | API-cost-prohibitive filtering |
| Progressive ViT Alignment | Incremental (high impact) | Stable modality alignment | Joint optimization instability |
| AdaLRS | Incremental | Automated LR optimization | Manual hyperparameter tuning |
| Visual Token Packing | Incremental | Training efficiency for AnyRes | Text-only sequence packing |

**Core Insight:** SAIL-VL2's most significant contribution is not any single technique but the **systematic integration** of data quality, progressive training, and hybrid SFT–RL reasoning into a cohesive pipeline. The paper demonstrates that "small model, strong performance" (Section 1) is achievable through methodological rigor rather than parameter scaling alone — a finding with implications for the broader open-source multimodal community where computational resources are constrained.

**Limitation to Acknowledge:** While SAIL-VL2 achieves state-of-the-art results at 2B and 8B scales, the paper does not provide ablation studies isolating the individual contribution of each innovation (e.g., how much of the performance gain comes from judge-model filtering vs. progressive training vs. thinking-fusion). This makes it difficult to determine which components are essential versus complementary — a gap that future work should address.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Benchmark Coverage and Organization**

The paper evaluates SAIL-VL2 across **106 datasets**, organized into four primary evaluation dimensions (Section 6.1):

- **General Open-source Multimodal Understanding Benchmarks (72 datasets):** Spanning multi-image understanding, multilingual understanding, real-world understanding, charts/documents/OCR tasks, multimodal reasoning and mathematics, comprehensive multimodal assessment, visual localization, multimodal hallucination assessment, and instruction following.
- **OpenCompass Evaluation System (8 datasets):** Aligned with the official OpenCompass leaderboard, covering tasks from multimodal understanding to reasoning.
- **Open-source Video Understanding Benchmarks (9 datasets):** Including all 5 video evaluation sets from the official OpenCompass video leaderboard, plus ActivityNetQA, LongVideoBench, NextQA, and TVBench.

This comprehensive coverage is notably broader than typical LVM papers, which often report on 10–30 benchmarks. The breadth reduces the risk of cherry-picking favorable results.

The paper states this process "strictly aligns with the official OpenCompass settings, making our results directly comparable to the leaderboard."

**Video Evaluation Specifics**

Video results are reported using **16 randomly sampled frames** as visual input (Table 8 and Table 9 footnotes). This is a standard practice but worth noting — models optimized for dense frame sampling may be disadvantaged in this evaluation setting.

### 5.3 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

#### 5.3.1 Claims Strongly Supported

**Claim 1: "SAIL-VL2 achieves state-of-the-art performance at the 2B and 8B parameter scales"**

**Evidence:** Table 8 and Table 9 show SAIL-VL2-2B and SAIL-VL2-8B leading on OpenCompass average (70.31 and 75.07 respectively) among open-source models at comparable scales.

**Assessment:** **Strongly supported.** The model consistently outperforms prior sub-4B and 8B open-source models across most benchmarks.

**Claim 2: "SAIL-VL2-Thinking matches or surpasses models with far larger parameter scales on reasoning benchmarks"**

**Evidence:** Table 10 shows SAIL-VL2-8B-Thinking at 54.4 average, surpassing Qwen2.5-VL-72B (50.2) and competing with GPT-4o-latest (54.8).

**Assessment:** **Strongly supported.** The 8B thinking model matching a 72B dense model on reasoning benchmarks is compelling evidence for the thinking-fusion paradigm's effectiveness.

**Claim 3: "SAIL-ViT effectively reduces the gap between visual and textual feature spaces"**

**Evidence:** Table 7 shows mean overall distance of 10.06 for SAIL-ViT vs. 11.24 for AIMv2 (with Qwen3-8B). Figure 6 visualizes tighter clustering.

**Assessment:** **Supported.** The distance metrics and visualizations consistently show better alignment, though the practical impact on downstream tasks is inferred rather than directly measured.

**Claim 4: "Judge model filtering achieves >99% high quality in retained data"**

**Evidence:** Table 2 shows judge models achieving >90% accuracy and recall. The paper estimates retained quality based on this accuracy.

**Assessment:** **Partially supported.** The judge model accuracy is well-documented, but the ">99% high quality" claim is an estimate rather than a direct measurement. This is reasonable but should be noted as an inference.

#### 5.3.2 Claims with Conditional Support

**Claim 5: "SAIL-VL2 perceives with clarity in fine-grained visual understanding"**

**Evidence:** OCRBench at 89.50 (2B) and 91.30 (8B) are strong. RefCOCO avg at 57.82 (AnyRes-2B) leads among sub-4B models.

**Assessment:** **Conditionally supported.** The model excels at OCR and visual grounding, but some fine-grained tasks (e.g., detailed chart interpretation) are not separately benchmarked. The claim is supported for evaluated tasks but may not generalize to all fine-grained perception scenarios.

**Claim 6: "MoE designs enable parameter scaling while preserving computational efficiency"**

**Evidence:** SAIL-VL2-A3B has 30.5B total parameters but only 3B activated, achieving 53.6 on reasoning benchmarks (Table 10) and 58.50 OpenSource avg (Table 9).

**Assessment:** **Supported for performance, efficiency not directly measured.** The performance results are clear, but the paper does not report inference latency, memory footprint, or FLOPs comparisons between dense and MoE variants. The efficiency claim is architecturally sound but lacks empirical validation in the experiments.

#### 5.3.3 Limitations and Missing Analyses

**1. No Per-Component Ablation**

The paper does not isolate the contribution of individual innovations. For example:
- How much of the performance gain comes from judge-model filtering vs. progressive training vs. thinking-fusion?
- What is the marginal benefit of AdaLRS over a well-tuned fixed learning rate?

This makes it difficult to determine which components are essential versus complementary.

**2. Limited Failure Case Analysis**

The paper does not systematically analyze where SAIL-VL2 underperforms. For instance:
- MMMU val at 47.67 (2B) trails InternVL3.5-2B at 51.11 — why?
- DynaMath scores are low across all models — what types of problems cause failures?

Understanding failure modes would provide more actionable insights for practitioners.

**3. Efficiency Metrics Not Reported**

Despite emphasizing efficiency, the paper does not report:
- Inference latency (tokens/second)
- Memory footprint (GB)
- Training compute (GPU-hours)
- Cost per training run

These metrics would directly support the "efficient LVM" claim.

**4. Video Evaluation Limited to 16 Frames**

All video results use 16 randomly sampled frames. This may disadvantage models optimized for:
- Dense frame sampling
- Temporal attention across many frames
- Long-context video understanding beyond 16 frames

The LongVideoBench results (54.45 for 2B) are strong but may not reflect performance on truly long videos.

**5. Thinking Variant Evaluation on Subset of Benchmarks**

The thinking variants are evaluated only on the OpenCompass reasoning leaderboard (6 benchmarks in Table 10), not on the full 106-dataset suite. This leaves open the question of whether thinking-fusion degrades performance on non-reasoning tasks.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the assumptions, constraints, and unresolved weaknesses in SAIL-VL2. While the paper demonstrates strong empirical performance, understanding where the approach falters is essential for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this framework. We organize limitations into four categories: architectural assumptions, unaddressed scenarios, scalability constraints, and open questions.

### 6.1 Architectural Assumptions and Their Consequences

**Assumption 1: Reasoning Capabilities Require Minimum Parameter Threshold**

The thinking-fusion paradigm, while effective, exhibits clear scale dependency. Table 10 reveals a stark performance gap between reasoning variants:

| Model | Parameters | Reasoning Avg |
|-------|------------|---------------|
| SAIL-VL2-2B-Thinking | 2.6B | 40.9 |
| SAIL-VL2-8B-Thinking | 8.8B | 54.4 |
| SAIL-VL2-A3B-Thinking | 31.1B (3B activated) | 53.6 |

The 2B-Thinking model underperforms the 8B-Thinking variant by **13.5 points** on the reasoning benchmark average. This suggests that internalized reasoning — the paper's core innovation — has a **minimum capacity floor** below which the thinking-fusion SFT–RL pipeline cannot compensate. For practitioners targeting sub-3B deployments, this limitation is critical: the reasoning specialization that distinguishes SAIL-VL2-Thinking may not materialize at extreme efficiency scales.

**Assumption 2: Homogeneous Model Merging is Safe, Heterogeneous is Not**

Table 5 presents a striking ablation on model soup strategies:

> "Merging homogeneous models consistently yields stable and notable performance improvements, whereas merging heterogeneous models often causes performance degradation."

The heterologous merge result is catastrophic: on ChartQA, performance collapses from 82.80/84.56 (base models) to **10.36** (merge model), and the overall average drops from ~75 to **12.86**. This reveals a fragile assumption: model merging requires **training trajectory compatibility** that is difficult to verify a priori. Practitioners cannot simply ensemble checkpoints from different runs — they must ensure hyperparameter settings, data compositions, and optimization paths are sufficiently aligned. This constraint limits the practical utility of model soup as a post-hoc performance booster.

**Assumption 3: Judge Model Accuracy Translates to Data Quality**

Section 3.1.1 claims retained captions achieve ">99% high quality" based on judge model accuracy exceeding 90%. However, this is an **inference, not a measurement**. The paper trains judge models on ~500K samples annotated via LVM API, then applies them to filter 300M captions. While Table 2 shows strong precision/recall (e.g., 0.983/0.975 for VIR-Score), the ">99% quality" claim assumes:
- Judge errors are independent across samples
- The 500K training subset is representative of the full 300M corpus
- Quality dimensions (VIR, ITA) fully capture what matters for downstream training

If any of these assumptions fail — for instance, if certain visual domains are underrepresented in the judge training set — the filtered corpus may retain systematic biases despite high judge accuracy. This is a common limitation in learned filtering pipelines but warrants explicit acknowledgment.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

**Scenario 1: Extreme High-Resolution Document Analysis**

The SAIL-ViT-AnyRes variant supports arbitrary resolutions up to **1792×1792** given a maximum input length of 16,384 tokens (Section 6.1). While this exceeds the fixed 448×448 resolution of many competitors, it may still constrain applications requiring:
- Full-page scientific paper analysis (often 2000+ pixels per dimension)
- High-density technical diagrams with fine text
- Multi-page document understanding without tiling

The paper reports strong DocVQA performance (93.10 for 2B, 95.28 for 8B in Table 8/9), but DocVQA consists of cropped document regions rather than full-page layouts. The claim that SAIL-VL2 "perceives with clarity" in "high-resolution document layout analysis" (Introduction) is not directly validated on full-page benchmarks.

**Scenario 2: Dense Temporal Video Understanding**

All video evaluation results use **16 randomly sampled frames** as visual input (Table 8 and Table 9 footnotes). This is standard practice but creates a blind spot:
- Models optimized for dense frame sampling (e.g., 32+ frames) may be disadvantaged
- Temporal reasoning requiring fine-grained motion analysis (e.g., action sequencing, causal event chains) is not thoroughly tested
- LongVideoBench val results (54.45 for 2B) are strong but may not reflect performance on videos exceeding typical lengths

The paper claims SAIL-VL2 handles "extended temporal contexts" (Section 6.2.2), but the 16-frame evaluation protocol limits confidence in this claim for truly long-form video understanding.

**Scenario 3: Non-Reasoning Task Performance for Thinking Variants**

The SAIL-VL2-Thinking models are evaluated **only on the OpenCompass reasoning leaderboard** (6 benchmarks in Table 10), not on the full 106-dataset suite. This leaves a critical question unanswered: does the thinking-fusion training degrade performance on non-reasoning tasks?

The Think-Fusion SFT stage mixes 90% direct-answer pairs with 10% CoT exemplars (Section 4.2.4) specifically to "maintain the model's broad general understanding." However, without benchmark results on general multimodal tasks (e.g., MMBench, RealWorldQA) for the thinking variants, practitioners cannot verify whether reasoning specialization comes at a cost to general utility. This is a notable gap in the experimental coverage.

**Scenario 4: Multilingual and Cross-Cultural Understanding**

While the paper mentions chart caption data includes "an equal distribution of English and Chinese" (Section 3.1.1), and benchmarks include some multilingual tasks, there is no systematic evaluation of:
- Low-resource language performance
- Cultural context understanding (e.g., region-specific symbols, idioms in visual content)
- Code-switching in multimodal queries

The "Multilingual Understanding" category is listed among the 72 general benchmarks (Section 6.1), but no disaggregated results are provided. For global deployment scenarios, this limits confidence in cross-lingual robustness.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

**Constraint 1: Missing Empirical Efficiency Metrics**

Despite positioning SAIL-VL2 as an "efficient" LVM family, the paper does not report:
- **Inference latency** (tokens/second) for dense vs. MoE variants
- **Memory footprint** (GB) during inference
- **Training compute costs** (GPU-hours or FLOPs)
- **Cost per training run** comparisons

Section 5.1 reports training efficiency gains from stream packing ("accelerates training by 50%"), and Section 5.2 mentions MoE kernel fusion achieves "up to a 3× training speedup." However, these are relative improvements without absolute baselines. Practitioners cannot determine:
- Whether SAIL-VL2-8B is actually faster than Qwen2.5-VL-7B at inference
- Whether the 3B-activated MoE variant reduces memory enough to enable edge deployment
- What the total training budget was in compute-hours

This omission is significant for a paper emphasizing efficiency. The architectural claims (sparse activation, packed training) are sound, but without empirical latency/memory data, the "efficient LVM" positioning remains partially unvalidated.

**Constraint 2: MoE Training Infrastructure Complexity**

Section 5.2 details specialized infrastructure requirements for MoE training:
- On NPU processors: Megatron-based distributed framework with pipeline and expert parallelism
- On NVIDIA devices: DeepSpeed ZeRO-2 with CPU offloading
- Specialized optimizers: DAPO for dense models, GSPO for MoE models

This creates a **deployment complexity trade-off**: while MoE architectures reduce activated parameters, they require more sophisticated training infrastructure than dense models. Organizations without access to optimized MoE training stacks may find the 30B-A3B variant difficult to reproduce or fine-tune. The paper acknowledges this implicitly by providing different optimizer recommendations per architecture, but does not discuss the engineering effort required to implement these pipelines.

**Constraint 3: RL Data Curation Overhead**

The reinforcement learning stages require careful data filtering that adds pipeline complexity:
- **Verifiable Reward RL** (Section 4.2.3): Uses pass@4 scoring to filter problems, discarding both easiest (pass@4=1) and hardest (pass@4=0) samples
- **Mixed Reward RL** (Section 4.2.5): Applies rejection sampling to identify "hard cases" where model performance was suboptimal

This creates a **chicken-and-egg problem**: to curate RL data, you need a reasonably capable model to evaluate pass@4 scores. The paper uses "the model from the previous stage" for filtering, but this means each RL iteration requires a full model checkpoint and evaluation pass before training can begin. For organizations with limited compute, this iterative curation may be prohibitive compared to simpler SFT-only approaches.

**Constraint 4: Sequence Length Limits for Complex Reasoning**

The post-training stages use sequence lengths of **20,480 tokens** (Table 4), and the pre-training stages reach **16,384 tokens** (Table 3). While this accommodates moderate-length CoT reasoning, it may constrain:
- Multi-step mathematical proofs requiring extensive derivation
- Complex document QA requiring full-page context
- Video reasoning with dense frame sequences

The paper does not evaluate performance degradation as sequence length approaches these limits, nor does it report whether truncation occurs frequently during training. For tasks requiring very long context windows (e.g., 100K+ tokens), SAIL-VL2's architecture may require modification.

### 6.4 Remaining Weaknesses and Open Questions

**Weakness 1: No Per-Component Ablation Studies**

The paper does not isolate the contribution of individual innovations. Key unanswered questions include:
- How much of the performance gain comes from judge-model filtering vs. progressive training vs. thinking-fusion?
- What is the marginal benefit of AdaLRS over a well-tuned fixed learning rate schedule?
- Does SAIL-ViT contribute more to performance than the Qwen3 LLM backbone?

Without component-level ablations, practitioners cannot prioritize which innovations to adopt if resource-constrained. For example, if judge-model filtering provides 80% of the data quality benefit at 20% of the engineering cost, this would be valuable information — but the paper does not provide it.

**Weakness 2: Limited Failure Mode Analysis**

The paper reports where SAIL-VL2 underperforms (e.g., MMMU val at 47.67 for 2B vs. InternVL3.5-2B at 51.11 in Table 8) but does not analyze **why**. Specific open questions:
- What types of MMMU questions cause failures? (Domain-specific knowledge? Visual complexity? Reasoning depth?)
- Why does DynaMath remain challenging across all models (SAIL-VL2-8B-Thinking at 33.5 vs. GPT-4o-latest at 48.5 in Table 10)?
- Are there systematic error patterns in OCR or visual grounding tasks?

Understanding failure modes would provide actionable insights for practitioners deciding whether SAIL-VL2 fits their use case. The absence of error analysis limits the paper's utility for applied settings.

**Weakness 3: Closed-Source Gap Remains Significant on Some Benchmarks**

Despite strong performance, SAIL-VL2 does not close the gap with top closed-source models on all tasks:
- **MMMU val**: GPT-4.1 achieves 76.44 (Table 9), while SAIL-VL2-A3B reaches 60.56 — a **15.88 point gap**
- **DynaMath**: GPT-4o-latest scores 48.5, SAIL-VL2-8B-Thinking scores 33.5 — a **15 point gap**
- **DocVQA**: Kimi-VL-A3B achieves 98.70, SAIL-VL2-8B achieves 95.28 — a **3.42 point gap**

These gaps suggest that while SAIL-VL2 is competitive at its scale, closed-source models with larger training budgets or proprietary data retain advantages on certain tasks. The paper's claim of "matching or even surpassing models with far larger parameter scales" (Introduction) holds for reasoning benchmarks but not universally.

**Weakness 4: Reproducibility Dependencies**

The SAIL-VL2 training pipeline depends on several external components that may affect reproducibility:
- **Qwen3 series models** as LLM backbones (Section 2): These are referenced but not all variants may be publicly available
- **LVM APIs** for data annotation (Sections 3.1.1, 4.1.1): The specific APIs used (e.g., "powerful LVM API") are not identified, making exact replication difficult
- **Proprietary datasets**: Some training data sources (e.g., SAIL-Caption, SAIL-Instruction) are team-curated and may not be fully released

The paper states "we will release the full SAIL-VL2 model suite along with its inference code" (Introduction), but training data and intermediate checkpoints may not be included. This limits the ability of other researchers to build directly on the SAIL-VL2 foundation.

**Open Question 1: Long-Term Stability of Internalized Reasoning**

Section 4.2.5 claims the model "learns the logical pathways to the correct answer without needing to explicitly write out each step." However, the paper does not address:
- Does internalized reasoning degrade over time or with additional fine-tuning?
- Can the model be prompted to show its reasoning post-training if needed for debugging?
- How does internalized reasoning interact with new task domains not seen during thinking-fusion training?

These questions matter for production deployments where model behavior must remain interpretable and stable.

**Open Question 2: Generalization to Novel Visual Domains**

The training data spans captioning, OCR, QA, math reasoning, and video (Sections 3–4), but the paper does not evaluate:
- Medical imaging understanding
- Scientific figure interpretation beyond charts
- Industrial inspection or quality control scenarios

The claim of "comprehensive multimodal understanding" (Abstract) is validated on academic benchmarks but not on domain-specific applications. Practitioners in specialized fields cannot assume SAIL-VL2 will generalize without additional fine-tuning.

### 6.5 Summary of Limitations

| Category | Limitation | Severity | Mitigation |
|----------|------------|----------|------------|
| **Architectural** | Reasoning requires minimum parameter threshold (~8B) | High | Use 8B+ variants for reasoning tasks |
| **Architectural** | Heterogeneous model merging causes catastrophic failure | High | Only merge homogeneous checkpoints |
| **Architectural** | Judge model quality claims are inferred, not measured | Medium | Validate filtered data on downstream tasks |
| **Scenario** | Max resolution 1792×1792 may constrain document analysis | Medium | Tile extremely high-resolution inputs |
| **Scenario** | Video evaluation limited to 16 frames | Medium | Interpret video results as frame-sampled performance |
| **Scenario** | Thinking variants not evaluated on non-reasoning benchmarks | Medium | Assume potential trade-off; test on target tasks |
| **Computational** | No empirical latency/memory/efficiency metrics | High | Conduct own benchmarking before deployment |
| **Computational** | MoE training requires specialized infrastructure | Medium | Use dense variants if MoE stack unavailable |
| **Computational** | RL data curation requires iterative model evaluation | Medium | Start with SFT-only if RL pipeline is prohibitive |
| **Weakness** | No per-component ablation studies | Medium | Prioritize innovations based on engineering cost |
| **Weakness** | Limited failure mode analysis | Medium | Conduct error analysis on target use cases |
| **Weakness** | Closed-source gap remains on some benchmarks | Low | Accept trade-off for open-source accessibility |

**Overall Assessment:** SAIL-VL2 represents a meaningful advance in efficient vision-language modeling, but practitioners should approach adoption with awareness of these limitations. The model excels at its stated design goals (efficient scale, strong general multimodal understanding, internalized reasoning at 8B+), but the gaps in empirical efficiency metrics, component ablations, and failure analysis leave room for future work. For organizations prioritizing open-source accessibility and deployment efficiency over absolute state-of-the-art performance, SAIL-VL2 is a strong candidate — provided the limitations above align with their risk tolerance and use case requirements.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how SAIL-VL2 reshapes the vision-language modeling landscape, identifies concrete research directions it enables, outlines practical deployment scenarios, and provides integration guidance for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this framework.

### 7.1 Landscape Shifts: Efficiency-First Modeling as a Viable Paradigm

SAIL-VL2 challenges a foundational assumption in the LVM community: that performance gains require proportional parameter scaling. The paper demonstrates that **methodological rigor can substitute for raw scale** in specific capability domains.

**Paradigm Challenge: Scale vs. Strategy**

The conventional wisdom in foundation modeling holds that "scaling up model parameters and training data to make LVMs 'larger' has emerged as a pivotal approach for pushing the performance boundaries" (Section 1). SAIL-VL2 directly contests this by achieving:

- **70.31 OpenCompass average at 2.6B parameters** (Table 8), surpassing Qwen2.5-VL-3B (65.36) and Ovis-U1-3B (69.94)
- **54.4 reasoning benchmark average at 8.8B parameters** (Table 10), competing with GPT-4o-latest (54.8) and surpassing Qwen2.5-VL-72B (50.2)

These results suggest that the field may be approaching **diminishing returns from pure scaling** at smaller model sizes, and that training methodology innovations offer higher marginal gains per compute dollar for sub-10B models.

**Three Concrete Landscape Shifts:**

1. **Data Quality Over Data Quantity as a Differentiator**

   The judge-model filtering pipeline (Section 3.1.1) demonstrates that removing 15–20% of noisy captions improves training efficiency more than adding equivalent clean data. This shifts the competitive advantage from organizations with the largest data scrapes to those with the most sophisticated **quality assessment infrastructure**. The finding that trained judge models (90%+ accuracy) outperform zero-shot LVM APIs (InternVL3-2B at 26.9% precision on ITA judgment, Table 2) suggests that **specialized filtering models** may become standard components in training pipelines.

2. **Internalized Reasoning Over Prompt-Dependent CoT**

   The thinking-fusion paradigm (Section 4.2) enables reasoning capabilities that activate without explicit user prompting. Section 4.2.5 states the model "learns the logical pathways to the correct answer without needing to explicitly write out each step." This shifts the design space from **inference-time prompting strategies** to **training-time capability injection**, with implications for:
   - User experience (no need to craft reasoning prompts)
   - API pricing (reasoning tokens may not need to be counted separately)
   - Model evaluation (reasoning benchmarks become more meaningful when models don't require specialized prompting)

3. **Progressive Alignment Over Joint Optimization**

   The three-stage SAIL-ViT training (Section 2.1.1) demonstrates that modality alignment benefits from **staged capability building** rather than end-to-end optimization. This challenges the standard LVM training recipe where vision encoder, adapter, and LLM are typically unfrozen together after brief warm-up. The feature alignment metrics (Table 7: mean overall distance 10.06 for SAIL-ViT vs. 11.24 for AIMv2) provide empirical evidence that progressive training produces measurably tighter modality integration.

**Caveat on Generalizability:**

These shifts are most relevant for **resource-constrained settings** (sub-10B models, limited training budgets, edge deployment). The paper does not claim that efficiency-first approaches surpass scale-first methods at the 70B+ level. The closed-source gap remains significant on some benchmarks (MMMU val: GPT-4.1 at 76.44 vs. SAIL-VL2-A3B at 60.56, Table 9), suggesting that scale still matters for the most challenging tasks.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

SAIL-VL2 opens several concrete research avenues that the paper identifies but does not fully explore.

#### 7.2.1 Component-Level Ablation Studies

**Gap Identified:** Section 6.4 notes the absence of per-component ablation studies. The paper does not isolate how much each innovation contributes to final performance.

**Specific Research Questions:**

- **Data filtering contribution:** Train identical models with (a) raw SAIL-Caption, (b) API-filtered captions, and (c) judge-model-filtered captions. Measure benchmark performance differences to quantify the marginal benefit of each filtering approach.

- **Progressive training contribution:** Compare three-stage SAIL-ViT training against (a) single-stage joint optimization and (b) two-stage alignment (LLaVA-style). Measure both convergence speed and final alignment quality using metrics from Table 7.

- **Thinking-fusion contribution:** Evaluate SAIL-VL2-Thinking on non-reasoning benchmarks (MMBench, RealWorldQA) to determine whether reasoning specialization degrades general capabilities. The paper only reports thinking variants on reasoning benchmarks (Table 10).

- **AdaLRS contribution:** Compare AdaLRS against well-tuned cosine decay schedules across multiple training runs. Section 3.2.1 reports a 0.06 loss advantage, but the benchmark impact of this difference is not quantified.

**Why This Matters:** Without component ablations, practitioners cannot prioritize which innovations to adopt when resource-constrained. If judge-model filtering provides 80% of the data quality benefit at 20% of the engineering cost, this would significantly influence adoption decisions.

#### 7.2.2 Empirical Efficiency Metrics

**Gap Identified:** Section 6.3 notes the absence of latency, memory, and compute metrics despite the paper's efficiency claims.

**Specific Measurements Needed:**

| Metric | Current Status | Research Need |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Inference latency (tokens/sec) | Not reported | Compare dense vs. MoE variants across hardware (A100, H100, edge devices) |
| Memory footprint (GB) | Not reported | Quantify activation memory for 2B, 8B, and 3B-activated MoE during inference |
| Training compute (GPU-hours) | Not reported | Report total training budget to enable cost-per-performance comparisons |
| Energy consumption | Not reported | Measure kWh per training run for environmental impact assessment |

**Why This Matters:** The claim that MoE architectures "preserve computational efficiency through sparse activation" (Section 2.2) is architecturally sound but empirically unvalidated. Practitioners need concrete numbers to determine whether the 3B-activated A3B variant actually reduces deployment costs compared to the dense 8B model.

#### 7.2.3 Domain Generalization and Specialization

**Gap Identified:** Section 6.4 notes limited evaluation on domain-specific applications (medical imaging, scientific figures, industrial inspection).

**Specific Research Directions:**

- **Medical VQA adaptation:** Fine-tune SAIL-VL2 on medical imaging datasets (e.g., VQA-RAD, PathVQA) and measure transfer efficiency compared to larger baseline models. The strong OCR performance (OCRBench 89.50 at 2B, Table 8) suggests potential for medical text extraction tasks.

- **Scientific figure understanding:** Evaluate on benchmarks like FigureQA or SciQA to test whether chart caption training (Section 3.1.1: 1.69M chart captions) generalizes to scientific visualization interpretation.

- **Cross-lingual robustness:** The paper mentions "equal distribution of English and Chinese" for chart data (Section 3.1.1) but provides no disaggregated multilingual benchmark results. Systematic evaluation across low-resource languages would clarify global deployment viability.

- **Long-context video understanding:** The 16-frame evaluation protocol (Section 5.1) limits claims about temporal reasoning. Research using dense frame sampling (32+ frames) would clarify whether SAIL-VL2's video capabilities extend to fine-grained motion analysis.

#### 7.2.4 Reasoning Internalization Mechanisms

**Gap Identified:** Section 4.2.5 claims the model "internalizes reasoning capabilities" but does not explain the mechanism by which explicit CoT training produces implicit reasoning.

**Specific Research Questions:**

- **Activation pattern analysis:** Do thinking-fusion models show different attention patterns during direct-answer inference compared to non-thinking models? Are reasoning-relevant attention heads activated even when CoT is not generated?

- **Knowledge distillation interpretation:** Can the thinking-fusion process be understood as distilling explicit reasoning traces into implicit model weights? If so, does this follow known distillation scaling laws?

- **Stability over fine-tuning:** Section 6.4 asks whether internalized reasoning degrades with additional task-specific fine-tuning. Longitudinal studies tracking reasoning performance across fine-tuning iterations would clarify deployment risks.

- **Interpretability post-training:** Can the model be prompted to show reasoning after thinking-fusion training, or is this capability permanently implicit? This matters for debugging and regulatory compliance in high-stakes applications.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

SAIL-VL2's efficiency-performance profile makes it suitable for specific deployment scenarios where larger models are impractical.

#### 7.3.1 Edge and Mobile Deployment

**Why SAIL-VL2 Fits:**

- **2B variant (2.6B total parameters)** can run on consumer GPUs with 8GB VRAM or high-end mobile devices with neural accelerators
- **OCRBench at 89.50** (Table 8) supports document scanning and text extraction applications
- **RefCOCO avg at 57.82 for AnyRes-2B** enables visual grounding for interactive assistants

**Concrete Use Cases:**

| Application | Required Capability | SAIL-VL2 Fit |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------|
| Mobile document scanner | OCR, layout understanding | Strong (OCRBench 89.50, DocVQA 93.10) |
| Visual assistant for visually impaired | Image captioning, object grounding | Strong (MMBench 86.77, RefCOCO 57.82) |
| Real-time video QA | Temporal understanding, frame sampling | Moderate (Video-MME 57.10, 16-frame eval) |
| Educational math tutor | Mathematical reasoning | Strong with thinking variant (MathVista 75.8 at 8B-Thinking) |

**Integration Considerations:**

- Use **SAIL-VL2-AnyRes-2B** for applications requiring fine-grained visual detail (documents, charts)
- Use **SAIL-VL2-2B-Thinking** only if reasoning is required and 8B+ variants are feasible (2B-Thinking underperforms at 40.9 reasoning avg, Table 10)
- Expect **16-frame video sampling** limitations for temporal reasoning tasks

#### 7.3.2 Document and Chart Analysis Pipelines

**Why SAIL-VL2 Fits:**

The paper emphasizes "high-fidelity perception in tasks such as OCR, high-resolution document layout analysis, and complex chart interpretation" (Introduction). Benchmark results support this:

- **DocVQA:** 93.10 (2B), 95.28 (8B) — leading among open-source models at these scales (Table 8, 9)
- **OCRBench:** 89.50 (2B), 91.30 (8B) — state-of-the-art for sub-4B models (Table 8)
- **Chart caption training:** 1.69M chart captions with English-Chinese balance (Section 3.1.1)

**Concrete Use Cases:**

| Application | Required Capability | SAIL-VL2 Fit |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------|
| Financial report analysis | Chart interpretation, table extraction | Strong (chart caption training, DocVQA performance) |
| Legal document review | OCR, layout understanding | Strong (OCRBench leadership) |
| Scientific paper parsing | Figure captioning, equation OCR | Moderate (chart training may not generalize to scientific figures) |
| Invoice processing | Structured text extraction | Strong (OCR + layout understanding) |

**Integration Considerations:**

- **Maximum resolution 1792×1792** (Section 6.1) may require tiling for full-page documents
- **Chinese-English balance** in chart training supports bilingual document pipelines
- Consider **SAIL-VL2-8B** for production document systems where accuracy outweighs latency concerns

#### 7.3.3 Reasoning-Enhanced Assistants

**Why SAIL-VL2 Fits:**

The thinking-fusion paradigm enables reasoning without explicit prompting, which simplifies user interaction design.

- **SAIL-VL2-8B-Thinking at 54.4 reasoning avg** (Table 10) matches GPT-4o-latest (54.8) on OpenCompass reasoning benchmarks
- **No specialized prompting required** (Section 4.2.5) reduces API complexity and user education burden

**Concrete Use Cases:**

| Application | Required Capability | SAIL-VL2 Fit |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------|
| Math tutoring | Step-by-step problem solving | Strong (MathVista 75.8, MathVerse 58.9) |
| Science QA | Multi-hop reasoning with visual context | Strong (Science RL data in Section 4.1.3) |
| Code debugging with screenshots | Visual + logical reasoning | Moderate (not explicitly evaluated) |
| Business intelligence QA | Chart reasoning, trend analysis | Strong (chart training + reasoning fusion) |

**Integration Considerations:**

- Use **8B-Thinking or A3B-Thinking variants** — 2B-Thinking underperforms significantly (40.9 vs. 54.4/53.6, Table 10)
- **No explicit reasoning prompt needed**, but consider offering users an option to view CoT for transparency
- **Mixed Reward RL stage** (Section 4.2.5) ensures general capabilities are preserved alongside reasoning

#### 7.3.4 Video Understanding Systems

**Why SAIL-VL2 Fits (With Caveats):**

- **Video-MME w/o sub at 57.10** (2B) leads among sub-4B models (Table 8)
- **LongVideoBench val at 54.45** (2B) demonstrates extended temporal context handling (Table 8)
- **5.1M SAIL-Video corpus** with frame-instruction alignment filtering (Section 4.1.1)

**Concrete Use Cases:**

| Application | Required Capability | SAIL-VL2 Fit |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------|
| Video content moderation | Frame-level understanding, QA | Moderate (16-frame sampling may miss temporal patterns) |
| Educational video QA | Temporal reasoning, instruction following | Strong (LongVideoBench performance) |
| Surveillance analysis | Motion detection, event sequencing | Weak (not evaluated for dense temporal reasoning) |
| Video summarization | Key frame identification, captioning | Moderate (captioning capability strong, summarization not evaluated) |

**Integration Considerations:**

- **16-frame sampling is a hard limit** in reported evaluations — dense frame applications may require custom modification
- **SAIL-Video filtering** prioritizes frame-instruction alignment, which benefits QA but may not optimize for other video tasks
- Consider **SAIL-VL2-8B** for production video systems where temporal reasoning accuracy is critical

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

This subsection provides actionable guidance for practitioners deciding whether to adopt SAIL-VL2 versus alternative approaches.

#### 7.4.1 When to Prefer SAIL-VL2

**Choose SAIL-VL2 when:**

1. **Parameter efficiency is a primary constraint**
   - Deployment target has &lt;16GB VRAM
   - Inference latency requirements exceed what 7B+ models can provide
   - Training budget cannot support 70B+ scale experiments

2. **Document and chart understanding are core requirements**
   - OCRBench and DocVQA performance are prioritized
   - Bilingual (English-Chinese) chart processing is needed
   - AnyRes variant can leverage resolution flexibility

3. **Reasoning is needed but closed-source APIs are not viable**
   - Data privacy prevents sending inputs to proprietary services
   - Cost per query makes closed-source APIs prohibitive at scale
   - SAIL-VL2-8B-Thinking provides open-source alternative with competitive reasoning performance

4. **Progressive training infrastructure is available**
   - Organization can implement three-stage ViT training
   - Judge model training pipeline can be reproduced (requires ~500K annotated samples)
   - RL infrastructure (PPO with DAPO/GSPO) is accessible

#### 7.4.2 When to Consider Alternatives

**Choose alternative approaches when:**

1. **Maximum accuracy outweighs efficiency concerns**
   - Closed-source models (GPT-4o, Gemini) remain superior on MMMU (76.44 vs. 60.56 for SAIL-VL2-A3B, Table 9)
   - 70B+ open-source models may outperform on specialized benchmarks
   - Deployment hardware can support larger models without latency penalties

2. **Dense temporal video understanding is critical**
   - 16-frame sampling limits motion analysis capabilities
   - Models optimized for 32+ frame dense sampling may perform better
   - Long-form video reasoning beyond typical benchmark lengths is required

3. **Per-component customization is needed**
   - No ablation studies make it unclear which innovations to adopt independently
   - Organizations wanting to mix-and-match components lack guidance on compatibility
   - Simpler two-stage training pipelines may suffice for non-reasoning applications

4. **Empirical efficiency metrics are required for decision-making**
   - Paper does not report latency, memory, or compute costs
   - Organizations needing TCO (total cost of ownership) analysis must conduct own benchmarking
   - MoE infrastructure complexity may outweigh activated parameter benefits for some teams

#### 7.4.3 Integration Checklist

For teams adopting SAIL-VL2, the following checklist addresses key integration considerations:

**Pre-Deployment Validation:**
- [ ] Benchmark SAIL-VL2 on domain-specific tasks (not just academic benchmarks)
- [ ] Measure inference latency on target hardware (A100, H100, edge devices)
- [ ] Validate memory footprint fits deployment constraints
- [ ] Test thinking variants on non-reasoning tasks to check for capability degradation

**Training Infrastructure (if fine-tuning):**
- [ ] Ensure MoE training stack supports DAPO (dense) or GSPO (MoE) optimizers
- [ ] Verify sequence length support (up to 20,480 tokens for post-training, Table 4)
- [ ] Plan for RL data curation pipeline if using thinking-fusion stages
- [ ] Budget for judge model training if implementing custom data filtering

**Production Considerations:**
- [ ] Implement fallback for resolution >1792×1792 (tiling strategy)
- [ ] Monitor for reasoning capability degradation during task-specific fine-tuning
- [ ] Consider offering CoT output as optional transparency feature for thinking variants
- [ ] Plan for 16-frame video sampling limitations in temporal reasoning tasks

#### 7.4.4 Reproducibility Dependencies

The paper states "we will release the full SAIL-VL2 model suite along with its inference code" (Introduction), but several dependencies affect reproducibility:

| Dependency | Status | Reproducibility Impact |
|------------|--------|----------------------|
| Qwen3 series backbones | Referenced, availability unclear | May require access to specific Qwen3 checkpoints |
| SAIL-Caption2 dataset | Team-curated, release status unclear | Custom data filtering pipeline may be needed |
| LVM APIs for annotation | Not specifically identified | Exact annotation replication not possible |
| Judge model checkpoints | Not explicitly mentioned | May need to retrain judge models from scratch |
| RL training infrastructure | DAPO/GSPO optimizers referenced | Requires compatible RL framework implementation |

**Recommendation:** Teams planning to reproduce SAIL-VL2 should budget for **custom data curation** and **judge model training** even if model weights are released. The training methodology is well-documented, but data dependencies may require independent implementation.

### 7.5 Summary: SAIL-VL2's Position in the LVM Ecosystem

SAIL-VL2 occupies a distinct niche in the vision-language model landscape:

| Dimension | SAIL-VL2 Position | Competing Approaches |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Parameter Scale** | Efficiency-first (2B–8B dense, 3B activated MoE) | Scale-first (7B–70B+ dense) |
| **Reasoning** | Internalized via thinking-fusion, no prompting needed | Prompt-dependent CoT or absent |
| **Data Strategy** | Judge-model filtered quality over quantity | Aggregated with minimal filtering |
| **Vision Encoder** | Progressive alignment with AnyRes option | Fixed resolution or joint optimization |
| **Deployment Target** | Edge, mobile, cost-sensitive production | Cloud-only, latency-tolerant |
| **Open-Source Status** | Full model suite + inference code planned | Varies (some weights-only, some closed) |

**Final Assessment:**

SAIL-VL2 demonstrates that **methodological innovation can substitute for parameter scaling** in specific capability domains. The thinking-fusion paradigm, judge-model data curation, and progressive vision encoder training represent concrete advances that the broader LVM community can adopt regardless of model scale.

However, the paper's limitations — missing component ablations, unreported efficiency metrics, and incomplete thinking variant evaluation — leave open questions about which innovations are essential versus complementary. Practitioners should view SAIL-VL2 as a **strong candidate for efficiency-constrained deployments** while recognizing that closed-source models retain advantages on the most challenging reasoning and knowledge-intensive tasks.

For the open-source multimodal community, SAIL-VL2 provides a **validated blueprint for efficient LVM development** that prioritizes deployability without sacrificing core capabilities. Future work that addresses the identified gaps — particularly empirical efficiency metrics and per-component ablations — would strengthen the case for efficiency-first modeling as a sustainable alternative to scale-first paradigms.
