## 1. Executive Summary

This paper tackles the prevalence of logical hallucinations in multi-step reasoning by demonstrating that process supervision, which provides feedback on each intermediate step, significantly outperforms outcome supervision, which relies solely on final answers. Using a GPT-4-based process-supervised reward model (PRM), the authors achieve a 78.2% solve rate on a representative subset of the challenging MATH dataset, surpassing outcome-supervised baselines. To facilitate further research, they release PRM800K, a dataset of 800,000 step-level human feedback labels, and show that active learning improves data efficiency by 2.6×.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this work, we must first establish the specific failure mode it addresses and the landscape of existing solutions. While large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in generating coherent text, their application to rigorous logical reasoning remains fraught with reliability issues. This section outlines the core problem of reasoning hallucinations, the existing framework for mitigating them via reward models, and the specific gap in prior literature that this paper aims to fill.

### 2.1 The Problem: Logical Hallucinations in Multi-Step Reasoning

The central challenge addressed in this paper is the tendency of LLMs to produce **logical hallucinations** during complex, multi-step tasks. Unlike factual hallucinations (inventing non-existent facts), logical hallucinations occur when a model generates a reasoning step that appears plausible but is mathematically or logically incorrect.

This problem is particularly acute in domains like mathematics or coding, where reasoning is sequential. As noted in the **Introduction**, a single logical error early in a chain of thought is sufficient to derail the entire solution. Even if the model recovers later, the final answer is likely incorrect. This creates a **brittleness** in model performance: as the number of required reasoning steps increases, the probability of producing a completely correct solution decreases exponentially unless the error rate per step is near zero.

The authors argue that simply scaling model size or prompting techniques (such as Chain-of-Thought) is insufficient to solve this reliability gap. While Chain-of-Thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022) allows models to externalize their reasoning steps, it does not inherently verify the correctness of those steps. Therefore, a verification mechanism is required to distinguish valid reasoning paths from invalid ones.

### 2.2 Prior Approaches: Reward Models and Supervision Types

The standard approach to improving reliability involves training a **Reward Model (RM)**. An RM is a separate model trained to assign a scalar score to a generated solution, indicating its quality. This score can then be used to:
*   Select the best solution from multiple samples (rejection sampling or best-of-$N$).
*   Fine-tune the generator via Reinforcement Learning (RL).

However, the effectiveness of the RM depends entirely on how it is trained. The paper distinguishes between two fundamental supervision strategies, originally formalized by Uesato et al. (2022):

1.  **Outcome Supervision (ORM):** The reward model receives feedback only on the **final result** of the solution.
    *   *Mechanism:* If the final answer matches the ground truth, the entire sequence is labeled positive. If not, it is labeled negative.
    *   *Limitation:* This creates a **credit assignment problem**. If a solution is incorrect, the ORM knows *that* it failed, but not *where* it failed. It must infer which specific step caused the error. Furthermore, ORMs can be fooled by "lucky guesses" where incorrect reasoning coincidentally leads to the correct final answer (false positives).

2.  **Process Supervision (PRM):** The reward model receives feedback for **each intermediate step** in the chain of thought.
    *   *Mechanism:* Human labelers (or a superior model) verify each step individually. A solution is considered correct only if every step is valid.
    *   *Hypothesized Advantage:* PRMs provide precise error localization. They theoretically offer a richer learning signal because they distinguish between a solution that fails at step 1 versus one that fails at step 10. This aligns better with human interpretability and AI safety goals, as it rewards the *process* of reasoning rather than just the outcome (**Section 6.2**).

### 2.3 The Gap: Inconclusive Prior Evidence

Despite the theoretical advantages of process supervision, prior empirical evidence was mixed. The key reference point for this work is **Uesato et al. (2022)**, which conducted a direct comparison of ORMs and PRMs in the domain of grade-school mathematics (specifically the GSM8K dataset).

Uesato et al. found that:
*   Process supervision achieved similar final performance to outcome supervision.
*   Process supervision was more data-efficient (required fewer labels to reach that performance).

This result left a critical question unanswered: **Does process supervision scale better than outcome supervision on harder tasks?** The GSM8K dataset consists of relatively simple problems solvable in a few steps. It is possible that the benefits of process supervision are masked on easy tasks where the credit assignment problem is trivial for an ORM. Additionally, Uesato et al. were limited by the amount of human feedback data they could collect.

This creates a uncertainty for researchers and practitioners:
*   **Cost vs. Benefit:** Process supervision is significantly more expensive to collect (labeling every step vs. labeling one final answer). If the performance gain is negligible on hard tasks, the extra cost is hard to justify.
*   **Alignment Implications:** If ORMs are sufficient for performance, but PRMs are better for alignment (interpretable reasoning), we need to know if choosing PRMs incurs an "alignment tax" (a reduction in capability).

### 2.4 Positioning of This Work

This paper positions itself as a rigorous re-evaluation of the ORM vs. PRM trade-off under conditions designed to maximize the potential of process supervision. The authors argue that the previous null result (where PRMs did not outperform ORMs) may have been due to limitations in model capability, dataset difficulty, or data scale.

They differentiate their approach from Uesato et al. (2022) in three critical ways (**Introduction**):
1.  **Base Model Capability:** They utilize a much more capable base model (GPT-4) compared to the models available in prior work.
2.  **Task Difficulty:** They evaluate on the **MATH dataset** (Hendrycks et al., 2021), which contains significantly more challenging competition-level mathematics problems compared to GSM8K.
3.  **Data Scale:** They collect a much larger dataset of human feedback (`PRM800K`, containing 800,000 step-level labels), allowing them to test if process supervision benefits from scale more than outcome supervision.

Furthermore, the paper explicitly narrows its scope to **training the reward model itself**, rather than using the reward model to fine-tune the generator via Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF). As stated in **Section 2.1**, the goal is to determine how to train the *most reliable reward model possible* for use in best-of-$N$ search. This isolates the quality of the supervision signal from the complexities of RL optimization.

By addressing these factors, the paper seeks to determine if process supervision is not just theoretically superior, but practically necessary for achieving state-of-the-art reliability in complex reasoning domains.

## 3. Technical Approach

This section provides a complete technical breakdown of how the authors designed, trained, and evaluated their reward models. We will walk through the entire pipeline from base model preparation to final evaluation, explaining not just what was done but why each design choice was made.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

This paper builds a verification system that scores mathematical solutions step-by-step rather than just checking the final answer. The solution involves training a reward model on human-labeled reasoning steps, then using that model to select the best solution from many candidates generated by a language model.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The system consists of four major components arranged in a sequential pipeline:

1.  **Base Model + MathMix Pretraining**: A foundation language model (GPT-4 for large-scale) is further pretrained on 1.5 billion tokens of math-specific content to improve mathematical reasoning capabilities before any reward modeling begins.

2.  **Generator Model**: A fine-tuned version of the base model that produces solutions in a newline-delimited, step-by-step format, making it easy to parse individual reasoning steps for verification.

3.  **Reward Model (ORM or PRM)**: A separate model trained to score solutions, either by predicting the correctness of the final answer (Outcome-supervised Reward Model, ORM) or by predicting the correctness of each intermediate step (Process-supervised Reward Model, PRM).

4.  **Best-of-N Selection**: At test time, the generator produces $N$ candidate solutions per problem, the reward model scores each one, and the highest-scoring solution is selected as the final output.

Information flows unidirectionally: the base model becomes the generator, the generator produces solutions, humans or a larger model label those solutions, the reward model learns from those labels, and finally the reward model selects the best solutions at test time.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

*   We begin with **base model preparation** because all downstream components depend on having a capable foundation model with mathematical knowledge.
*   We then explain **generator training** because the format of generated solutions determines how reward models can parse and score individual steps.
*   We proceed to **data collection methodology** because the quality and strategy of label collection fundamentally determines reward model performance.
*   We detail **ORM and PRM training mechanics** to show exactly how the two supervision types differ in their learning objectives.
*   We cover **scoring and evaluation** to explain how step-level predictions are aggregated into solution-level scores for best-of-N selection.
*   We conclude with **active learning strategy** because this represents a key innovation for improving data efficiency in process supervision.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is an empirical comparison paper that investigates whether process supervision produces more reliable reward models than outcome supervision for mathematical reasoning tasks. The core idea is that by providing feedback at each reasoning step rather than only on the final answer, the reward model learns a more precise understanding of what constitutes correct reasoning, which translates to better solution selection at test time.

**Base Model Preparation and MathMix Pretraining**

All large-scale models are fine-tuned from the base GPT-4 model, which has been pretrained solely to predict the next token and has not been pretrained with any Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (**Section 2.2**). The small-scale base models use a similar architecture to GPT-4 but were pretrained with roughly 200 times less compute, enabling the authors to conduct ablation studies that would be prohibitively expensive at the large scale. Before any reward model training begins, all models undergo an additional pretraining step on a dataset called `MathMix`, which contains approximately 1.5 billion tokens of math-relevant content (**Section 2.2**). This dataset consists of individual math problems and their solutions, free-form text discussing math problems and concepts, and synthetic data, but explicitly excludes general natural language data (**Appendix A, Table 2**). The authors found that this math-specific pretraining improves mathematical reasoning capabilities, similar to findings from Lewkowycz et al. (2022). For large-scale experiments, models train on MathMix for roughly 3 billion tokens (2 epochs), while small-scale experiments train for 6 epochs (roughly 6.6 billion tokens) due to the smaller model size (**Appendix A**).

**Generator Training for Step-Level Parsing**

To enable process supervision, the authors must be able to parse individual reasoning steps from generated solutions, which requires a consistent output format. They train the generator to produce solutions in a newline-delimited step-by-step format by few-shot generating solutions to MATH training problems, filtering to only those that reach the correct final answer, and fine-tuning the base model on this dataset for a single epoch (**Section 2.3**). This training step is explicitly not intended to teach the generator new mathematical skills, but only to teach it to produce solutions in the desired format for easier parsing. At each model scale, a single fixed generator model is used to produce all solutions, and the authors do not attempt to improve the generator with reinforcement learning (**Section 2.1**). This scope restriction is intentional: the paper focuses exclusively on training the most reliable reward model possible, not on using that reward model to fine-tune the generator via RL, which would confound the comparison between supervision types.

**Data Collection and the PRM800K Dataset**

Process supervision requires human feedback because there is no simple way to automate step-level correctness checking, unlike outcome supervision where final answers can be automatically verified against ground truth. The authors present human data-labelers with step-by-step solutions sampled from the large-scale generator, and the labelers assign each step one of three labels: positive, negative, or neutral (**Section 2.4**). A positive label indicates the step is correct and reasonable, a negative label indicates the step is incorrect or unreasonable, and a neutral label indicates ambiguity (such as a subtly misleading step that is technically still valid). The complete dataset of step-level labels is called `PRM800K`, containing 800,000 step-level labels across 75,000 solutions to 12,000 problems (**Abstract**, **Section 2.4**). To minimize overfitting, the authors include data from 4,500 MATH test problems in the PRM800K training set and evaluate only on the remaining 500 held-out MATH test problems (**Section 2.4**, **Appendix C**). The data collection was split into two phases: Phase 1 collected labels for multiple alternative completions at each step (about 5% of PRM800K, or 40,000 labels), while Phase 2 scaled up by pre-generating whole solutions and ending labeling as soon as the first negative step is encountered (**Appendix B**).

**Active Learning Strategy for Data Collection**

Rather than uniformly sampling solutions for human labeling, the authors employ an active learning strategy designed to maximize the information gained from each label. They specifically surface "convincing wrong-answer solutions" to data-labelers, where "convincing" means solutions rated highly by the current best PRM and "wrong-answer" means solutions that reach an incorrect final answer (**Section 2.4**). The intuition is that labeling these solutions provides more information because the PRM is known to be mistaken about at least one step in each such solution. At each iteration of data collection, they generate $N$ solutions per problem and surface only the top $K$ most convincing wrong-answer solutions to data-labelers, either filtering at a problem level ($K$ solutions per problem) or globally across the dataset ($K$ solutions in total) (**Section 2.4**). The PRM is iteratively re-trained using the latest data at several points during data collection, though the authors note they observed instability when iteratively retraining the selector model and could not diagnose the issue (**Section 4.2**). This active learning approach is estimated to provide a 2.6× improvement in data efficiency compared to uniform data labeling, as measured by comparing the slopes of the performance curve with and without active learning (**Section 4.2**, **Figure 4a**).

**Outcome-Supervised Reward Model (ORM) Training**

ORMs are trained following a methodology similar to Cobbe et al. (2021), where the model learns to predict whether an entire solution is correct or incorrect based solely on the final answer (**Section 2.5**). The training procedure uniformly samples a fixed number of solutions per problem from the generator (100 samples per problem for the large-scale ORM to ensure a strong baseline), and correctness is determined by automatically checking the final answer against ground truth (**Section 3**). At training time, the reward model makes predictions for every token in the context, and the target for each token in a solution is the same binary label based on whether the solution is correct or incorrect (**Appendix E**). The ORM is trained for a single epoch on each dataset without dropout and without jointly learning a language modeling objective, as the authors found performance is not sensitive to most other hyperparameters within a reasonable range (**Appendix E**). At test time, the ORM's prediction at the final token is used as the overall score for the solution, which means the ORM must learn to propagate information about the final answer correctness back through all preceding tokens (**Section 2.5**). A critical limitation of this approach is that false positive solutions (those that reach the correct answer with incorrect reasoning) will be misgraded as correct, potentially teaching the ORM to reward flawed reasoning paths.

**Process-Supervised Reward Model (PRM) Training**

PRMs are trained to predict the correctness of each step after the last token in each step, with the prediction taking the form of a single token classification (**Section 2.6**). The training objective maximizes the log-likelihood of the target tokens (positive, negative, or neutral) during training, which means the PRM can be trained in a standard language model pipeline without any special accommodations. A critical design choice is that process supervision is deliberately provided only up to the first incorrect step, which keeps the comparison between outcome and process supervision more straightforward (**Section 2.6**). For correct solutions, both methods provide the same information (every step is correct), while for incorrect solutions, both reveal the existence of at least one mistake, but process supervision additionally reveals the precise location of that mistake. This decision also keeps labeling costs similar for humans, since determining the correctness of a solution without relying on an easy-to-check final answer is equivalent to identifying its first mistake. The PRM is trained for 2 epochs on all datasets, as the authors found this improves final performance over training for just 1 epoch on smaller datasets, though the benefits diminish on larger datasets (**Appendix F.1**). Low learning rates were found to be important for stable PRM training because fine-tuning a language model from its ordinary language modeling task to a classification task represents a large distribution shift (**Appendix F.1**).

**PRM Scoring and Solution-Level Aggregation**

While the PRM produces step-level predictions, best-of-N selection requires a single score for each complete solution, which necessitates an aggregation strategy. The authors define the PRM score for a solution to be the product of the correctness probabilities for each step, implemented as:

$$
\text{Score}_{\text{solution}} = \prod_{i=1}^{M} P(\text{step}_i \text{ is correct} \mid \text{PRM})
$$

where $M$ is the number of steps in the solution and $P(\text{step}_i \text{ is correct} \mid \text{PRM})$ is the probability that step $i$ receives a positive label according to the PRM (**Section 2.6**). The authors experimented with four scoring strategies combining two design choices: whether to treat neutral labels as positive or negative, and whether to use the product or minimum over step-level scores as the reduction operation (**Appendix F.2, Table 4**). The best-performing strategy is to take the product of step-level scores and to consider neutral labels as positives, achieving 78.2% on best-of-1860 selection, though the difference in performance between all four strategies is minor (ranging from 77.4% to 78.2%) (**Appendix F.2, Table 4**). Using the product instead of the minimum as the reduction does create a slight bias against solutions with a larger number of steps, since multiplying more probabilities together tends to produce smaller values, but this effect was deemed acceptable given the overall performance.

**Large-Scale vs. Small-Scale Experimental Regimes**

The authors conduct experiments in two separate regimes to balance between achieving state-of-the-art performance and enabling controlled ablation studies. At large-scale, all models are fine-tuned from GPT-4 to advance the state-of-the-art by training the most reliable ORM and PRM possible, but the training sets for these reward models are not directly comparable (**Section 2**). The large-scale PRM training set (PRM800K) was constructed using active learning and is biased towards wrong-answer solutions, while the large-scale ORM training set uses 100 uniform samples per problem and is an order of magnitude larger (**Section 3**). To address this flaw and enable apples-to-apples comparison, the authors also train models at small-scale where they can conduct more direct comparisons by using the large-scale PRM (called `PRM_large`) to supervise smaller model training (**Section 2**, **Section 4**). This setup removes dependence on costly human feedback for ablations and enables the authors to isolate confounding factors such as training set composition and supervision type. For the small-scale ablations, `PRM_large` is used as a labeling oracle to provide either process supervision or outcome supervision to smaller reward models trained on identical datasets, with the only difference being the form of supervision (**Section 4.1**).

**Evaluation Methodology and Best-of-N Selection**

Reward models are evaluated by their ability to perform best-of-N search over uniformly sampled solutions from the generator, which directly measures their practical utility for improving model reliability (**Section 2.1**). For each test problem, the generator produces $N$ candidate solutions, the reward model scores each one, and the solution ranked highest by the reward model is selected as the final output. The selected solution is automatically graded based on its final answer, and the fraction of correct selections is reported as the performance metric. This evaluation approach isolates reward model quality from generator quality, since the generator is fixed and the only variable is which solution the reward model selects. The authors evaluate on a specific subset of 500 MATH test problems that is representative of the full test set in terms of difficulty levels and subjects, as shown in **Appendix C, Figure 5**. For the large-scale comparison in **Figure 3**, they generated 1,860 solutions per problem in total, and for $N \leq 1000$ they visualize the variance across many subsamples of these 1,860 solutions. The process-supervised model achieves 78.2% on best-of-1860 selection, compared to 72.4% for the outcome-supervised model and 69.6% for majority voting (**Figure 3**).

**Out-of-Distribution Generalization Testing**

To measure whether the reward models generalize beyond the MATH dataset distribution, the authors evaluate on a held-out set of 224 STEM questions from recent AP Physics, AP Calculus, AP Chemistry, AMC10, and AMC12 exams (**Section 5**). These tests were released after the pretraining dataset was compiled, providing high confidence that the models have not seen these problems during training. The evaluation uses best-of-100 selection, and the PRM outperforms both the ORM and majority voting across all subject areas, with aggregate performance of 72.9% for PRM versus 63.8% for ORM (**Table 1**). This demonstrates that the PRM can tolerate a modest amount of distribution shift and that its strong performance holds up on fresh test questions that are guaranteed to be uncontaminated. The authors also note that the PRM regularly surfaces correct solutions to MATH problems that have a low single-digit percentage solve-rate under the generator, which serves as additional evidence against significant test set contamination (**Section 6.3**).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper does more than simply report higher accuracy numbers; it fundamentally shifts our understanding of how supervision signals scale with task complexity and cost. While the technical pipeline described in **Section 3** establishes *how* the models were built, this section explains *why* these results matter for the broader field of reliable AI. We identify four key insights that distinguish this work from prior literature, ranging from theoretical scaling behaviors to practical economic implications for AI safety.

### 4.1 Process Supervision Scales with Problem Complexity

One of the most critical contributions of this work is resolving the apparent contradiction with prior findings by **Uesato et al. (2022)**. As noted in **Section 2.3**, Uesato et al. found that outcome and process supervision yielded similar performance on grade-school math (GSM8K). This led to uncertainty about whether the added cost of process supervision was justified. This paper demonstrates that the benefit of process supervision is not static; it scales with the difficulty of the reasoning task.

*   **The Credit Assignment Hypothesis:** The authors argue that outcome supervision faces a **credit assignment problem** that becomes exponentially harder as solution length and complexity increase (**Section 6.1**). On simple problems (like GSM8K), an outcome-supervised reward model (`ORM`) can relatively easily infer which step caused an error because there are few steps. On complex problems (like MATH), a solution may have many steps, and most generated solutions contain errors. An `ORM` receives a negative label for the whole sequence but lacks the signal to identify *which* step was flawed.
*   **Empirical Evidence of Scaling:** This hypothesis is supported by **Figure 3**, which shows that the performance gap between the process-supervised reward model (`PRM`) and the `ORM` widens as the number of samples $N$ increases. When searching over 1,860 solutions, the `PRM` achieves **78.2%** accuracy compared to **72.4%** for the `ORM`.
*   **Difficulty Breakdown:** Further evidence appears in **Appendix G, Figure 6**, which breaks down performance by problem difficulty quintiles. The `PRM` outperforms the `ORM` across all difficulty levels, but the robustness of the `PRM` is particularly notable on the hardest quintiles where the `ORM` struggles to distinguish valid reasoning from invalid reasoning that happens to reach the correct answer.

This insight suggests that process supervision is not merely an incremental improvement but a **fundamental requirement** for reliable reasoning on complex, multi-step tasks where credit assignment via outcome feedback becomes intractable.

### 4.2 Active Learning Makes Process Supervision Economically Viable

A major barrier to adopting process supervision is the cost of human feedback. Labeling every step of a solution is significantly more expensive than checking a final answer. This paper introduces a specific active learning strategy that directly addresses this economic bottleneck, transforming process supervision from a theoretical ideal into a practical engineering choice.

*   **Targeting Model Uncertainty:** Instead of uniformly sampling solutions for labeling (which wastes labels on obvious errors), the authors surface **"convincing wrong-answer solutions"** to labelers (**Section 2.4**). These are solutions that the current `PRM` rates highly but which have incorrect final answers.
*   **Information Density:** The insight here is that these specific errors contain the highest information density. Since the `PRM` believes the solution is correct, correcting a single step in these solutions provides a strong gradient signal to update the model's decision boundary. Labeling obvious errors provides little new information because the model already knows they are bad.
*   **Quantifiable Efficiency:** As shown in **Figure 4a** and discussed in **Section 4.2**, this strategy yields a **2.6× improvement in data efficiency** compared to uniform labeling. This means researchers can achieve the same performance with less than half the human labeling effort.

This innovation is significant because it lowers the marginal cost of process supervision. Without this efficiency gain, the superior performance of the `PRM` might be offset by prohibitive data collection costs, limiting its adoption in real-world systems.

### 4.3 Synthetic Supervision as a Methodological Proxy

Conducting ablation studies on human feedback is typically prohibitively expensive. If researchers want to test how changing the data mixture affects performance, they would normally need to collect new human labels for every variation. This paper introduces a methodological innovation that bypasses this constraint: using a large-scale reward model to supervise smaller models.

*   **The Oracle Model:** The authors train a large-scale `PRM` (referred to as `PRM_large`) using the full human dataset. They then use this model to generate labels for smaller models trained on subsets of data (**Section 4**).
*   **Enabling Ablations:** This setup allows the authors to run experiments that would otherwise be infeasible. For example, **Figure 4a** compares four different series of reward models across varying training set sizes. Doing this with human labelers would require collecting multiple distinct datasets of hundreds of thousands of labels. Using `PRM_large` as a proxy allows for rapid iteration on data collection strategies.
*   **Validity of the Proxy:** The paper validates this approach by showing that the trends observed with synthetic supervision align with the large-scale human results. For instance, **Figure 4b** shows that `PRM` supervision outperforms `ORM` supervision even when both are supervised by `PRM_large`, mirroring the human-supervised results in **Figure 3**.

This contribution is a **tool for future research**. It demonstrates that once a high-quality reward model exists, it can be used to accelerate the development of subsequent models, reducing reliance on continuous human data collection for every experimental tweak.

### 4.4 Eliminating the Alignment Tax

In AI safety research, there is often a perceived trade-off between capability and alignment, known as the **alignment tax**. Safer methods (like interpretability or strict constraints) are assumed to reduce model performance, creating a disincentive for deployment. This paper provides empirical evidence that process supervision may incur a **negative alignment tax**, meaning it improves both safety and performance simultaneously.

*   **Safety Mechanisms:** Process supervision is inherently safer than outcome supervision because it rewards models for following a human-endorsed chain of thought rather than optimizing for a final answer proxy (**Section 6.2**). This reduces the risk of the model learning to "game" the reward signal (e.g., producing correct answers via flawed reasoning).
*   **Performance Gains:** Despite these stricter constraints, the `PRM` outperforms the `ORM` on standard benchmarks (**Table 1**, **Figure 3**).
*   **Deployment Incentives:** This finding is crucial for adoption. If safety methods reduce performance, companies face pressure to deploy the more capable (but less safe) model. By showing that the safer method (`PRM`) is also the more capable method, the paper aligns economic incentives with safety goals.

However, the authors caution that it is unknown how broadly these results generalize beyond math (**Section 6.2**). While this insight is powerful within the domain of logical reasoning, future work must verify if the negative alignment tax holds for open-ended tasks like creative writing or coding, where "correctness" is less well-defined.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a critical examination of the experimental evidence presented in the paper. We move beyond the high-level claims to scrutinize the specific methodologies, quantitative results, and boundary conditions that support the authors' conclusions. Our goal is to determine whether the data convincingly demonstrates the superiority of process supervision and to identify where the evidence remains tentative.

### 5.1 Evaluation Framework and Metrics

The core experimental design relies on **Best-of-$N$ selection** as the primary metric for reward model reliability. As established in **Section 3**, the generator produces $N$ candidate solutions, and the reward model selects the highest-scoring one. The final performance is the percentage of selected solutions that match the ground truth final answer.

*   **Why Best-of-$N$?** This metric isolates the reward model's ability to *discriminate* correct reasoning from incorrect reasoning, independent of the generator's ability to *produce* correct reasoning. It serves as a proxy for how well the reward model would perform if used in a Reinforcement Learning (RL) pipeline, without the instability of actual RL training (**Section 2.1**).
*   **Test Sets:**
    *   **MATH Subset:** The primary evaluation uses 500 problems held out from the MATH test set. The authors explicitly included 4,500 other MATH test problems in the training data to prevent overfitting on the small original training split, evaluating only on the remaining 500 (**Section 2.4**, **Appendix C**).
    *   **OOD STEM Set:** To check for memorization and generalization, a separate set of 224 questions from recent AP exams (Calculus, Chemistry, Physics) and AMC competitions (10/12) is used. These were released after the model's pretraining cutoff (**Section 5**).

### 5.2 Primary Performance Comparison (Large-Scale)

The central claim—that process supervision outperforms outcome supervision—is anchored in **Figure 3**. This figure plots the percentage of problems solved against the number of samples $N$ used for selection.

*   **Peak Performance:** At the maximum budget of $N=1860$ samples per problem:
    *   **Process-Supervised RM (PRM):** 78.2%
    *   **Outcome-Supervised RM (ORM):** 72.4%
    *   **Majority Voting Baseline:** 69.6%
*   **Scaling Behavior:** The graph reveals a critical dynamic: the performance gap between PRM and ORM **widens as $N$ increases**. At low $N$ (e.g., $N=10$), the models perform similarly. As $N$ grows to $1000$, the PRM continues to find better solutions, while the ORM plateaus. This suggests the PRM is better at identifying rare, correct reasoning paths hidden among many incorrect ones.
*   **Variance Analysis:** For $N \leq 1000$, the paper visualizes variance across many subsamples of the 1860 solutions. The error bars (standard deviation) are visible but relatively tight, indicating the results are stable across different random seeds of solution generation.
*   **Critical Observation:** The ORM performs only slightly better than Majority Voting (72.4% vs 69.6%). This implies that simply training on final answers adds limited value over a simple consensus mechanism on this dataset. The PRM's significant leap (78.2%) demonstrates that step-level feedback provides information that consensus cannot capture.

### 5.3 Controlled Ablations (Small-Scale)

To address the confounding factor that the large-scale PRM and ORM training sets differed in size and composition (active learning vs. uniform sampling), the authors conducted small-scale experiments using synthetic supervision (**Section 4**). These results are presented in **Figure 4**.

*   **Apples-to-Apples Comparison:** In **Figure 4a**, reward models are trained on identical datasets but with different labels provided by a large oracle model (`PRM_large`).
    *   **Process Supervision:** Consistently outperforms both outcome supervision variants across all training set sizes (1 to 200 samples per problem).
    *   **Outcome Supervision Variants:** The authors compare outcome supervision derived from `PRM_large` vs. automatic final-answer checking. `PRM_large` outcome supervision performs better than final-answer checking, likely because it filters out "lucky guesses" (correct answers with bad reasoning) that confuse the final-answer ORM (**Section 4.1**).
*   **Active Learning Efficiency:** **Figure 4a** also compares uniform sampling vs. active learning (surfacing "convincing wrong-answer solutions").
    *   **Metric:** The authors estimate efficiency by comparing the slopes of the line of best fit for performance vs. data size.
    *   **Result:** Active learning yields a **2.6× improvement** in data efficiency. This means an actively trained model achieves the same performance with less than 40% of the data required for uniform training.
    *   **Diminishing Returns:** At the largest active learning size (200 samples/problem), performance slightly underperforms the trend line. The authors hypothesize this is due to reduced diversity, as 200 samples represent a significant fraction of the 1000-sample selection pool (**Section 4.2**).

### 5.4 Generalization and Robustness

A common critique of high performance on benchmark datasets is overfitting or test set contamination. The authors address this through out-of-distribution (OOD) testing and difficulty breakdowns.

*   **OOD Generalization (Table 1):** The PRM maintains its advantage on the 224-question STEM set released after pretraining.
    *   **Aggregate:** PRM (72.9%) vs. ORM (63.8%) vs. Majority (61.3%).
    *   **Subject Breakdown:** The PRM outperforms the ORM in every category:
        *   **AP Calculus:** 86.7% (PRM) vs. 68.9% (ORM)
        *   **AP Physics:** 86.7% (PRM) vs. 77.8% (ORM)
        *   **AP Chemistry:** 80.0% (PRM) vs. 68.9% (ORM)
        *   **AMC10/12:** 53.2% (PRM) vs. 49.1% (ORM)
    *   **Analysis:** The performance drop on AMC problems (competition math) compared to AP exams suggests that extremely high-difficulty competition problems remain challenging for all methods, but the relative gain of process supervision holds.
*   **Difficulty Quintiles (Figure 6):** The authors split the MATH test set into quintiles based on the generator's pass rate (easiest to hardest).
    *   **Robustness:** The PRM outperforms the ORM across **all five quintiles**.
    *   **Adversarial Sensitivity:** On the easiest quintile (Quintile 1), the ORM's performance actually *decreases* slightly as $N$ increases. The authors suggest this is because the ORM is fooled by adversarial examples (plausible but incorrect reasoning) that become more likely to appear as $N$ grows. The PRM remains robust, indicating it is less susceptible to being "tricked" by smooth but incorrect reasoning chains (**Appendix G**).

### 5.5 Scoring Sensitivity Analysis

Aggregating step-level scores into a solution-level score requires design choices. **Table 4** in **Appendix F.2** ablates these choices to ensure the main result isn't an artifact of a specific scoring formula.

*   **Variables:**
    1.  **Reduction:** Product vs. Minimum of step probabilities.
    2.  **Neutral Handling:** Treat neutral labels as Positive vs. Negative.
*   **Results:**
    *   **Best:** Product + Neutral=Positive (78.2%)
    *   **Worst:** Product + Neutral=Negative (77.4%)
    *   **Range:** The total spread is only 0.8 percentage points.
*   **Conclusion:** The superiority of process supervision is **not sensitive** to the specific aggregation strategy. While the product rule biases against longer solutions (multiplying more probabilities $&lt; 1$ reduces the score), this bias does not significantly harm performance compared to the minimum rule.

### 5.6 Limitations and Failure Modes

While the results are strong, a critical analysis must acknowledge where the system fails. The paper provides qualitative examples in **Appendix I** that reveal the boundaries of the PRM's capability.

*   **False Positives:** The PRM is not perfect. **Appendix I.3** showcases cases where the PRM assigns high scores to incorrect solutions.
    *   **Counting Errors:** In Problem 9, the model misses a factor of 2 in a combinatorics argument. The reasoning looks plausible, fooling the PRM.
    *   **Algebraic Manipulation:** In Problem 10, the model fails to update the right-hand side of an equation during simplification. The PRM fails to catch this silent error.
    *   **Implication:** Process supervision reduces hallucinations but does not eliminate them. The reward model itself can hallucinate correctness if the error is subtle enough.
*   **Test Set Contamination:** The authors acknowledge in **Section 6.3** that some MATH problems may exist in the pretraining data (MathMix) despite decontamination efforts.
    *   **Mitigation:** They argue contamination is unlikely to drive the results because: (1) The generator's pass rate on many solved problems is very low (e.g., 0.1% in **Appendix I.1**), suggesting the model hasn't memorized the solution; (2) OOD results on fresh STEM exams (**Table 1**) mirror the MATH results.
    *   **Caveat:** While relative comparisons (PRM vs. ORM) likely remain valid regardless of contamination, the absolute performance numbers (78.2%) might be slightly inflated.
*   **Domain Specificity:** All experiments are confined to mathematics, where answers are verifiable and steps are discrete. The authors explicitly state in **Section 6.2** that it is unknown how broadly these results generalize to domains like creative writing or open-ended coding, where "correctness" is subjective and step boundaries are less clear.

### 5.7 Assessment of Claims

Based on the experimental evidence provided:

1.  **Claim:** *Process supervision outperforms outcome supervision.*
    *   **Verdict:** **Strongly Supported.** The gap is consistent across large-scale human feedback (**Figure 3**), small-scale synthetic feedback (**Figure 4**), and OOD datasets (**Table 1**). The ablation studies rule out data size and composition as the primary drivers.
2.  **Claim:** *Active learning improves data efficiency.*
    *   **Verdict:** **Supported.** The 2.6× efficiency gain is quantified in **Section 4.2**. However, the instability noted in iterative retraining of the selector model suggests this technique requires careful engineering to maximize.
3.  **Claim:** *Process supervision reduces alignment tax.*
    *   **Verdict:** **Plausible but Domain-Limited.** The data shows higher performance with a safer method (process supervision) in math. However, without experiments in non-verifiable domains, the claim that this eliminates the alignment tax generally remains an open hypothesis.

In summary, the experiments are rigorous within the domain of mathematical reasoning. The use of controlled ablations (small-scale) validates the large-scale findings, and the OOD tests mitigate concerns about overfitting. The primary limitation is not the experimental design, but the scope of the domain; the results prove the value of process supervision for *verification*, but not necessarily for all forms of *generation*.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the paper demonstrates strong empirical results for process supervision in mathematical reasoning, adopting this approach in broader contexts requires navigating significant structural assumptions, resource constraints, and open methodological questions. This section analyzes the trade-offs inherent in the design choices and identifies the boundaries where the proposed methods may struggle or require adaptation.

### 6.1 Structural Assumptions about Reasoning

The effectiveness of process supervision relies on specific assumptions about how reasoning is structured and represented. These assumptions simplify the problem for the reward model but may not hold in all domains.

*   **Discretization of Reasoning Steps:** The methodology assumes that complex reasoning can be cleanly segmented into discrete, newline-delimited steps (**Section 2.3**). The generator is explicitly fine-tuned to produce output in this format to facilitate parsing.
    *   *Trade-off:* This enforces a rigid structure on the model's thought process. In domains like creative writing, open-ended coding, or strategic planning, reasoning steps may be interdependent, non-linear, or difficult to bound with clear delimiters. Forcing such tasks into a step-by-step format might constrain the model's flexibility or introduce artificial boundaries that do not align with logical correctness.
*   **The "First Error" Stopping Rule:** During data collection, human labelers are instructed to stop labeling a solution as soon as the first incorrect step is encountered (**Section 2.6**).
    *   *Rationale:* This keeps labeling costs comparable to outcome supervision, as verifying a solution without a ground truth answer is equivalent to finding its first mistake.
    *   *Limitation:* This strategy discards information about steps occurring after the first error. While logical consistency usually breaks after the first mistake, later steps might still contain valid sub-routines or recoverable logic. By truncating labels, the reward model receives less data per solution, potentially requiring more solutions to achieve the same coverage of reasoning patterns.
*   **Neutral Label Ambiguity:** The labeling scheme includes a "neutral" category for steps that are technically valid but unhelpful or ambiguous (**Section 2.4**).
    *   *Trade-off:* While this reduces labeler cognitive load by allowing them to defer judgment on edge cases, it introduces ambiguity during training. The authors resolve this by treating neutrals as positives during scoring (**Appendix F.2**), but this is a heuristic choice. In domains where "unhelpful" steps are more detrimental (e.g., inefficient code), treating them as positive could incentivize verbose or迂回 reasoning.

### 6.2 Domain Dependence and Verifiability

The success of process supervision in this paper is tightly coupled with the specific properties of the MATH dataset. Extending these results to other domains faces distinct hurdles.

*   **Reliance on Ground Truth for Outcome Baselines:** The outcome-supervised reward models (ORMs) are trained using automatic verification of final answers (**Section 2.5**).
    *   *Constraint:* This is feasible in mathematics because answers are easily checkable. In domains without automatic verifiers (e.g., summarization, dialogue), outcome supervision would also require human feedback, narrowing the cost gap between ORM and PRM. The relative advantage of PRM might diminish if outcome supervision becomes equally expensive.
*   **Generalization Beyond Math:** The authors explicitly state that it is unknown how broadly these results generalize beyond the domain of math (**Section 6.2**).
    *   *Open Question:* Mathematical reasoning is deterministic; a step is either correct or incorrect based on formal rules. In subjective domains, "correctness" is often a matter of preference or style. Process supervision in those domains would require defining what constitutes a "valid step" in the absence of formal logic, which may reintroduce the ambiguity that process supervision aims to solve.
*   **Test Set Contamination Uncertainty:** Despite decontamination efforts, the authors cannot guarantee that the MATH test set is entirely absent from the pretraining data (**Section 6.3**).
    *   *Implication:* While relative comparisons (PRM vs. ORM) likely remain valid, the absolute performance numbers (e.g., 78.2% solve rate) might be inflated. Users deploying this method should expect potentially lower absolute performance on strictly unseen data, even if the relative gain of process supervision holds.

### 6.3 Resource and Scalability Constraints

While active learning improves efficiency, process supervision remains resource-intensive compared to fully automated methods.

*   **Human Feedback Bottleneck:** Despite the 2.6× improvement in data efficiency from active learning (**Section 4.2**), the approach still fundamentally relies on human labelers for the initial dataset (`PRM800K`).
    *   *Trade-off:* Collecting 800,000 step-level labels is significantly more costly than collecting outcome labels. For organizations without access to large-scale human feedback infrastructure, this barrier to entry remains high. The release of `PRM800K` mitigates this for research, but commercial applications requiring domain-specific data must still bear this cost.
*   **Compute Requirements for Base Models:** The state-of-the-art results rely on fine-tuning from GPT-4 (**Section 2.2**).
    *   *Constraint:* This limits reproducibility for researchers without access to comparable foundational models. While the small-scale experiments show similar trends with smaller models (**Section 4**), the absolute performance ceiling is tied to the capability of the base generator. If the generator cannot produce correct reasoning steps even occasionally, the reward model has no positive examples to learn from.
*   **Oracle Dependency for Ablations:** The small-scale ablations rely on using the large-scale PRM (`PRM_large`) as a labeling oracle (**Section 4**).
    *   *Limitation:* This creates a dependency chain where improving smaller models requires access to a superior model. In scenarios where a large oracle is not available, researchers cannot easily simulate the data collection process to optimize their active learning strategies, potentially leading to less efficient data usage.

### 6.4 Methodological Weaknesses and Open Questions

Several aspects of the methodology showed promise but encountered practical difficulties that remain unresolved.

*   **Instability in Iterative Active Learning:** The authors attempted to iteratively retrain the selector model (`PRMselector`) throughout data collection to improve sample selection dynamically.
    *   *Weakness:* They observed instability in this process which they were unable to diagnose, and the resulting models performed no better than non-iterative versions (**Section 4.2**).
    *   *Implication:* This suggests that the current active learning pipeline is not fully optimized. A stable iterative process could theoretically yield even greater data efficiency than the reported 2.6×, but achieving this remains an open research problem.
*   **Reward Model False Positives:** The PRM itself is not infallible. **Appendix I.3** provides examples where the PRM assigns high scores to incorrect solutions (e.g., missing a factor of 2 in counting problems, or failing to update equation sides).
    *   *Critical Limitation:* Process supervision reduces hallucinations but does not eliminate them. The reward model can hallucinate correctness if the error is subtle enough. This creates a ceiling on reliability: the system cannot select solutions that the reward model itself fails to verify. In high-stakes domains, this residual error rate necessitates additional verification layers.
*   **Scoring Bias Against Length:** The chosen scoring strategy uses the product of step-level probabilities (**Section 2.6**).
    *   *Trade-off:* Mathematically, multiplying probabilities $p &lt; 1$ results in a smaller score as the number of steps increases. This introduces an implicit bias against longer solutions (**Appendix F.2**).
    *   *Impact:* While the authors note the performance difference is minor compared to other scoring strategies, this bias could penalize complex problems that inherently require more reasoning steps. In domains where verbose reasoning is necessary for correctness, this scoring rule might inadvertently suppress valid solutions.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

| Feature | Benefit | Cost / Limitation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Process Supervision** | Higher reliability, better credit assignment (**Figure 3**) | Higher human labeling cost per solution |
| **Active Learning** | 2.6× data efficiency (**Section 4.2**) | Requires initial model to select samples; iterative stability issues |
| **First-Error Labeling** | Reduces labeling time (**Section 2.6**) | Discards information about steps after the first mistake |
| **Product Scoring** | Simple aggregation, best performance (**Table 4**) | Biases against longer solutions |
| **GPT-4 Base Model** | State-of-the-art performance (**Section 3**) | High compute cost, limited accessibility for replication |

In conclusion, while process supervision offers a clear path to more reliable reasoning models, it is not a universal solution. Its efficacy is highest in domains with verifiable steps and ground truth answers. Practitioners must weigh the increased data collection cost against the reliability gains, and remain cautious about the residual error rate of the reward model itself. Future work must address the instability in iterative active learning and verify whether these benefits persist in non-mathematical domains.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work represents a pivot point in how we approach reliability in large language models. Rather than focusing solely on scaling the generator to reduce error rates, the authors demonstrate that scaling the **verification mechanism** yields disproportionate gains in reliability. This section outlines how these findings reshape the research landscape, identifies concrete avenues for follow-up work, and provides guidance for practitioners considering integration.

### 7.1 Shifting the Paradigm on Verification

The primary implication of this work is that **process supervision should be the default standard for complex reasoning tasks**, superseding outcome supervision where step-level verification is feasible. Prior to this paper, the community operated under the assumption derived from Uesato et al. (2022) that process supervision offered data efficiency but not necessarily performance gains on hard tasks. **Figure 3** overturns this by showing a widening performance gap as compute budgets increase, with the process-supervised reward model (`PRM`) reaching **78.2%** accuracy compared to **72.4%** for the outcome-supervised model (`ORM`).

This shifts the research focus in two ways:
*   **From Generator-Centric to Verifier-Centric:** Instead of only asking "how do we make the model generate better answers?", researchers must now ask "how do we make the model verify better reasoning?". The release of **`PRM800K`** (Abstract, Section 8) lowers the barrier for this shift by providing a large-scale dataset of step-level labels, which was previously a bottleneck.
*   **Reframing the Alignment Tax:** In AI safety, there is often a trade-off between capability and alignment (the "alignment tax"). This paper provides empirical evidence of a **negative alignment tax** in the domain of math (**Section 6.2**). Because process supervision enforces human-endorsed reasoning chains while simultaneously improving performance, it aligns economic incentives with safety goals. This suggests that safety interventions which improve interpretability (like step-by-step verification) may not require sacrificing capability.

### 7.2 Critical Avenues for Follow-up Research

The paper explicitly identifies several areas where the current methodology is incomplete or untested. These represent high-priority directions for the community.

*   **Domain Generalization Beyond Math:** The authors caution that it is unknown how broadly these results generalize beyond mathematical reasoning (**Section 6.2**). Math is unique because steps are discrete and correctness is objectively verifiable. Follow-up work must test process supervision in domains like:
    *   **Code Generation:** Where steps correspond to function calls or logical blocks, and correctness can be verified via unit tests.
    *   **Scientific Reasoning:** Where steps involve hypothesis formation and experimental design.
    *   **Open-Ended Tasks:** Where "correctness" is subjective (e.g., writing, negotiation). In these domains, defining a "positive" step is non-trivial and may require new labeling schemas beyond the binary/neutral scheme used in **Section 2.4**.
*   **Reinforcement Learning Fine-Tuning:** The paper explicitly restricts its scope to training the reward model for best-of-$N$ selection, noting that "finetuning the generator with RL is a natural next step" (**Section 2.1**). The current results imply that using a `PRM` for Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) should yield superior generators compared to using an `ORM`. Future work should quantify the stability and performance gains of **Process RLHF**, where the policy is optimized to maximize the product of step-level scores:
    $$
    \mathcal{L}_{RL} = -\mathbb{E} \left[ \sum_{t=1}^{T} \log P(\text{step}_t \text{ is correct} \mid \text{PRM}) \right]
    $$
*   **Stabilizing Active Learning:** The authors attempted to iteratively retrain the selector model (`PRMselector`) during data collection but observed instability they could not diagnose (**Section 4.2**). They state, "We consider this a compelling direction for future research." Solving this instability could push the data efficiency gain beyond the reported **2.6×** improvement, making process supervision viable even for domains with scarce human labeling resources.
*   **Automated Step Segmentation:** The current approach relies on a generator fine-tuned to produce newline-delimited steps (**Section 2.3**). Future work should investigate whether reward models can learn to segment reasoning steps autonomously, removing the need for forced formatting which may constrain the model's natural reasoning flow.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The techniques demonstrated in this paper are immediately applicable to systems requiring high-reliability reasoning.

*   **High-Stakes Decision Support:** In domains like legal analysis or medical diagnosis, knowing *where* a model went wrong is as important as the final conclusion. The `PRM` provides step-level scores that can be visualized to highlight potential errors (as shown in **Figure 2**). This enables human-in-the-loop systems where reviewers focus only on low-scoring steps rather than reading entire solutions.
*   **Automated Grading and Tutoring:** The `PRM800K` dataset and methodology can be adapted for educational technology. A process-supervised model can provide feedback on specific steps in a student's solution, offering more granular guidance than a simple "correct/incorrect" marker.
*   **Code Verification Pipelines:** While the paper focuses on math, the architecture translates well to code. A `PRM` could verify individual lines or blocks of code against logical constraints before execution, reducing the risk of runtime errors or security vulnerabilities caused by logical hallucinations.
*   **Data Efficiency for Custom Domains:** Organizations building vertical-specific models (e.g., finance, engineering) can adopt the **active learning strategy** from **Section 4.2**. By surfacing only "convincing wrong-answer solutions" for human review, they can minimize the cost of collecting domain-specific step-level labels while maximizing reward model performance.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For practitioners considering adopting these methods, the following guidelines distill the paper's experimental findings into actionable advice.

*   **When to Prefer PRM over ORM:**
    *   **Use PRM** when tasks involve multi-step reasoning ($>5$ steps) and step-level correctness is well-defined (e.g., math, logic, code). The performance gap widens with complexity (**Appendix G, Figure 6**).
    *   **Use ORM** when tasks are single-step or when step boundaries are ambiguous (e.g., creative writing). The overhead of step-level labeling may not be justified if the credit assignment problem is trivial.
*   **Data Collection Strategy:**
    *   If human labels are available, prioritize **active learning** by filtering for solutions that fool your current best model (**Section 2.4**).
    *   If human labels are scarce, consider **synthetic supervision** using a larger model as an oracle (**Section 4**). The paper shows that `PRM_large` can effectively supervise smaller models, enabling ablations and training without continuous human feedback.
*   **Compute vs. Latency Trade-off:**
    *   The reported results rely on **best-of-$N$ selection** with $N$ up to 1860 samples (**Figure 3**). This is computationally expensive and introduces latency. For real-time applications, practitioners may need to distill the `PRM` into a smaller verifier or limit $N$ to a feasible budget (e.g., $N=10$), accepting lower absolute performance.
*   **Scoring Configuration:**
    *   When aggregating step scores, use the **product rule** treating neutral labels as **positive** (**Appendix F.2, Table 4**). This configuration yielded the highest performance (78.2%) in the authors' ablations. Be aware this introduces a slight bias against longer solutions, but the effect was deemed minor compared to the benefits.
*   **Accessing Resources:**
    *   The **`PRM800K`** dataset is publicly available to support related research (Abstract, Section 8). Researchers should leverage this baseline before collecting new data to ensure comparability with state-of-the-art results.

In summary, this paper establishes process supervision as a superior method for training reliable reasoning models in verifiable domains. While challenges remain in generalization and active learning stability, the release of data and clear empirical evidence provides a robust foundation for the next generation of aligned, high-capability AI systems.
