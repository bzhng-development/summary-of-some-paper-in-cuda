## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the counterintuitive reasoning behaviors of Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) like DeepSeek-R1 by proposing the Laws of Reasoning (LORE), a framework hypothesizing that reasoning compute should scale linearly with question complexity. To validate this, the authors introduce LORE-BENCH, comprising LORE-MONO and LORE-COMPO, revealing that while models exhibit monotonicity, they lack compositionality (e.g., DeepSeek-R1-1.5B shows a nMAD of 0.528 on compute compositionality). They develop SFT-Compo, a fine-tuning method that enforces compute-law compositionality, which reduces nMAD to 0.314 on the 1.5B model and yields consistent performance gains (e.g., +4.8% average Pass@1) across six benchmarks including MATH500 and AIME 2024.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Unpredictable Reasoning Behavior in LRMs

Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) such as OpenAI o1 and DeepSeek-R1 have demonstrated remarkable capabilities on complex problems through their "thinking-then-answering" paradigm. However, this paper identifies a fundamental issue: **LRMs exhibit reasoning behaviors that are counterintuitive and deviate from how humans naturally reason**.

The specific problem is illustrated in Figure 1 of the paper. Consider two questions:
- **Question 1**: "Compute the sum of 1 through 10" (simple)
- **Question 2**: "Square the number 55" (moderate complexity)
- **Composite Question**: "Compute the sum of 1 through 10, then square that sum"

Intuitively, solving the composite question should require *more* reasoning effort than solving either sub-question individually. A human would naturally allocate more thinking time to the combined task. However, DeepSeek-R1 exhibits the opposite behavior:

> "The model allocates ~300 more reasoning tokens to solve the squaring question than to the composite question, with a 12.5% accuracy drop."

This mismatch reveals what the authors call **abnormal reasoning patterns**. The model fails to scale its computational effort appropriately with problem complexity, leading to what the paper terms **overthinking** (excessive computation on simple problems) or **underthinking** (insufficient computation on complex problems). Both phenomena harm overall performance.

### Why This Problem Matters

This issue has both **theoretical significance** and **practical impact**:

**Theoretical Significance:**
- There exists no unified framework to formally characterize what "good" reasoning behavior should look like in LRMs. Without theoretical grounding, improvements remain ad-hoc.
- The paper asks a foundational question (Q1 from Section 1): *"Can we theoretically formalize model reasoning to ensure desirable behavior?"* This bridges the gap between empirical observations and principled understanding.

**Practical Impact:**
- **Computational Efficiency**: Inefficient allocation of computation wastes resources. If a model overthinks simple problems, it consumes unnecessary tokens. If it underthinks complex problems, it produces incorrect answers requiring retries.
- **Reliability**: Unpredictable reasoning patterns make it difficult to trust model outputs in high-stakes applications (e.g., mathematical proofs, scientific reasoning, code generation).
- **Scalability**: As models grow larger, understanding how reasoning should scale becomes critical for designing training objectives and inference strategies.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

Before this work, researchers attempted to control reasoning behavior through two main avenues:

**1. Adaptive Post-Training Techniques:**
- Supervised fine-tuning with variable-length Chain-of-Thought (CoT) data (Aggarwal & Welleck, 2025; Team et al., 2025)
- Reinforcement learning with length penalties (Zhang et al., 2025a; Fang et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025)

**2. Test-Time Modulation:**
- Dynamically adjusting reasoning behavior during inference (Muennighoff et al., 2025; Fan et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025b)
- Confidence-based budget allocation (Qiao et al., 2025; Liu & Wang, 2025)
- Secondary controllers to modulate reasoning (Li et al., 2025)

**Where These Approaches Fall Short:**

The paper identifies a critical gap in Section 1:

> "While many attempts have been made to control reasoning, existing approaches primarily rely on ad-hoc heuristics and still behave undesirably in our studies."

The fundamental limitation is that **these methods lack theoretical grounding**. They operate on empirical observations without a formal understanding of what optimal reasoning behavior should satisfy. Specifically:

- **No Explicit Rules**: CoT training data is "heuristically curated by human annotators or generated through online rollout... rarely constrained by explicit rules, e.g., how much thinking budget to allocate for a given problem" (Section 1).
- **High Variability Ignored**: Researchers "generally overlook the high variability of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) data during the training phase" (Section 1).
- **No Compositionality Guarantees**: Existing methods do not ensure that reasoning behavior composes predictably across independent sub-problems.

Even models with explicit reasoning length control mechanisms (Thinkless-1.5B and AdaptThink-7B) "exhibit considerable deviations" from compositional behavior (Section 3.3, Figure 5), suggesting that such techniques "do not inherently promote compositional behavior."

### How This Paper Positions Itself

This work distinguishes itself through a **theory-first approach** rather than purely empirical optimization:

**1. Formal Framework (LORE):**
The paper introduces the **Laws of Reasoning (LORE)**, which provides mathematical hypotheses about optimal reasoning behavior:
- **Compute Law** (Hypothesis 1, Section 2.2): Reasoning compute should scale linearly with question complexity: $C_\theta(x) = \alpha_\theta \kappa(x) + o(\kappa(x))$
- **Accuracy Law** (Hypothesis 2, Section 2.3): Accuracy should decay exponentially with complexity: $A_\theta(x) = \exp(-\lambda_\theta \kappa(x))$

**2. Tractable Proxies:**
Recognizing that question complexity $\kappa(x)$ is "generally intractable" to compute directly (Definition 1, Section 2.1), the authors derive two measurable properties:
- **Monotonicity**: More complex questions require more compute (Property 1)
- **Compositionality**: Independent questions have additive compute (Property 2)

Proposition 1 formally establishes that satisfying these properties implies the compute law.

**3. Systematic Evaluation (LORE-BENCH):**
Rather than evaluating on standard benchmarks alone, the paper constructs LORE-BENCH (Section 3) specifically to measure adherence to the proposed laws:
- **LORE-MONO**: Synthetic benchmark with controlled complexity gradients across four domains (math, science, language, code)
- **LORE-COMPO**: 250 triplets from MATH500 measuring compositionality via normalized mean absolute deviation (nMAD)

**4. Intervention Based on Theory:**
The proposed SFT-Compo method (Section 4) directly enforces compositionality during training by selecting reasoning paths that satisfy:

$$
(r^*_1, r^*_2, r^*_{12}) = \arg \min_{r_1, r_2, r_{12}} |\ell(r_1) + \ell(r_2) - \ell(r_{12})|
$$

subject to all three paths yielding correct answers (Equation 1, Section 4).

This positions the paper as **bridging theory and practice**: it starts from first principles about what reasoning *should* look like, develops measurable criteria, evaluates existing models against these criteria, and then designs interventions grounded in the theoretical framework. The key insight is that **better compliance with reasoning laws yields improved performance**—not as a correlation, but as a causal relationship enforced through training.

The solution takes the shape of a three-part system: first, mathematical laws that describe how reasoning compute and accuracy should relate to problem complexity; second, benchmark suites that measure whether models follow these laws; and third, a fine-tuning procedure that trains models to better comply with the laws, which empirically improves their reasoning performance.

Information flows from the theoretical definitions to benchmark construction to evaluation to intervention, with each component building on the previous one's outputs.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a theory-driven empirical study that proposes mathematical laws for reasoning behavior, constructs benchmarks to measure adherence to these laws, and develops a fine-tuning intervention to improve compliance. The core idea is that if Large Reasoning Models follow principled scaling laws between problem complexity and computational effort, they will exhibit more reliable and efficient reasoning behavior.

**The Mathematical Foundation: Defining Complexity, Compute, and Accuracy**

The framework begins by formally defining three fundamental quantities that characterize reasoning behavior. **Question complexity** $\kappa(x)$ represents the minimum number of unit-cost primitive steps required to solve question $x$, where a primitive step is defined as a single valid transition of a fixed deterministic Turing machine. Formally, the paper defines complexity as:

$$
\kappa(x) \triangleq \min\{ \ell(\tau) : v(x, \tau) = 1 \} \in \mathbb{N} \cup \{\infty\}
$$

where $\tau$ denotes a finite sequence of primitive steps with length $\ell(\tau)$, and $v(x, \tau) \in \{0, 1\}$ is a binary verifier that accepts the sequence if and only if it is a valid solution for $x$. This definition captures the intuition that complexity equals the shortest valid solution path, though the paper explicitly acknowledges that computing $\kappa(x)$ is "generally intractable" because it requires a global search over a potentially exponential space.

**Reasoning compute** $C_\theta(x)$ measures how much computational effort a model $M_\theta$ expends on question $x$, quantified as the expected number of reasoning tokens in the generated reasoning chain. The formal definition is:

$$
C_\theta(x) \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{r \sim p_\theta(\cdot|x)}[\ell(r)]
$$

where $r$ denotes the reasoning chain, $\ell(r)$ is its length in tokens, and the expectation is taken over the model's sampling distribution. This operationalizes compute as token count because "test-time reasoning compute is directly proportional to the number of reasoning tokens generated" (Section 2.1).

**Reasoning accuracy** $A_\theta(x)$ measures the probability that the model produces a correct final answer given input $x$. Formally:

$$
A_\theta(x) \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{(r,y) \sim p_\theta(\cdot|x)} \left[ \mathbb{1}\{ \text{ans}(y) = a^\star(x) \} \right] \in [0, 1]
$$

where $y$ is the final answer, $\text{ans}(y)$ extracts the answer from the output, and $a^\star(x)$ denotes the ground truth. This definition treats accuracy as a probability over the model's sampling distribution rather than a deterministic property.

**The Two Core Laws: Compute Law and Accuracy Law**

The paper proposes two mathematical hypotheses about how optimal reasoning models should behave. The **Compute Law** (Hypothesis 1, Section 2.2) states that reasoning compute should scale linearly with question complexity:

$$
C_\theta(x) = \alpha_\theta \kappa(x) + o(\kappa(x))
$$

where $\alpha_\theta > 0$ is a model-dependent constant and $o(\kappa(x))$ denotes sublinear overhead that becomes negligible as complexity grows (specifically, $o(\kappa)/\kappa \to 0$ when $\kappa \to \infty$). The $o(\kappa(x))$ term captures introductory and transition tokens that constitute "a very small portion of the overall reasoning" and can be ignored in practice. The intuition is that an efficient reasoning model should allocate computational budget proportionally to problem difficulty, neither overthinking simple problems nor underthinking complex ones.

The **Accuracy Law** (Hypothesis 2, Section 2.3) states that reasoning accuracy should decay exponentially with complexity:

$$
A_\theta(x) = \exp\left(-\lambda_\theta \kappa(x)\right)
$$

where $\lambda_\theta \geq 0$ is a decay rate parameter. Equivalently, $\log A_\theta(x) \propto -\kappa(x)$. This law follows from the intuition that if a question requires solving $\kappa(x)$ independent steps and each step succeeds with fixed probability, then the overall accuracy decreases exponentially with the number of required steps.

**Deriving Tractable Properties: Why Monotonicity and Compositionality**

Since directly measuring $\kappa(x)$ is intractable, the authors derive two measurable properties that serve as empirical proxies for the laws. This design choice is critical: rather than attempting to compute exact complexity values, the framework tests whether models satisfy properties that theoretically imply the laws.

**Property 1: Compute-Complexity Monotonicity** states that reasoning compute should be monotonically non-decreasing with complexity:

$$
\kappa(x_1) \leq \kappa(x_2) \implies C_\theta(x_1) \leq C_\theta(x_2)
$$

This captures the intuitive principle that more complex questions naturally require more reasoning effort. The property is tractable because it relies only on relative comparisons between questions rather than absolute complexity values.

**Property 2: Compute-Complexity Compositionality** states that for two independent questions $x_1$ and $x_2$, the compute for their composition $x_1 \oplus x_2$ should be approximately additive:

$$
C_\theta(x_1 \oplus x_2) \approx C_\theta(x_1) + C_\theta(x_2)
$$

The paper defines question composition as concatenation with a connector prompt $c$: $x_1 \oplus x_2 = \text{concat}(x_1, c, x_2)$. Two questions are **independent** if their concept sets are disjoint, i.e., $S(x_1) \cap S(x_2) = \emptyset$, where $S(x)$ denotes the set of mathematical concepts relevant to solving $x$ (e.g., Calculus, Algebra, Discrete mathematics). The compositionality property includes a sublinear overhead term $o(\kappa(x_1) + \kappa(x_2))$ analogous to the Compute Law, but this is negligible in practice.

**Property 3: Accuracy-Complexity Monotonicity** states that accuracy should be monotonically non-increasing with complexity:

$$
\kappa(x_1) \leq \kappa(x_2) \implies A_\theta(x_1) \geq A_\theta(x_2)
$$

**Property 4: Accuracy-Complexity Compositionality** states that for independent questions, accuracy should be multiplicative:

$$
A_\theta(x_1 \oplus x_2) = A_\theta(x_1) \cdot A_\theta(x_2)
$$

This follows from probability theory: if two independent events have probabilities $p_1$ and $p_2$, the probability of both occurring is $p_1 \cdot p_2$.

**Proposition 1** formally establishes that if a model satisfies compute-complexity monotonicity and compositionality, then its reasoning compute satisfies $C_\theta(x) \propto \kappa(x)$, implying the Compute Law. **Proposition 2** similarly establishes that accuracy monotonicity and compositionality imply the Accuracy Law. These propositions justify using the tractable properties as proxies for the intractable laws.

**LORE-MONO: Constructing Benchmarks with Controlled Complexity Gradients**

LORE-MONO addresses the challenge that "existing benchmarks are not suited for" monotonicity analysis because complexity is inherently difficult to quantify (Section 3.1). The benchmark construction follows a three-stage pipeline.

**Stage 1: Seed Question Curation** selects four domains requiring extensive reasoning—math, science, language, and code—and curates 10 diverse seed questions for each domain, yielding 40 total seed questions. Each seed question defines a problem template shared across its variants. The paper provides example seed questions in Appendix E.1.1, including a math question involving order-2 recurrence relations with alternating update rules, a science question modeling batch bioreactor enzyme kinetics, a language question involving letter maze navigation with grid mutations, and a code question requiring exact Python execution.

**Stage 2: Variant Generation** creates 30 variants for each seed question, where variant $N$ applies the core operation $N$ times. As illustrated in Figure 3, this ensures that "a larger number of steps directly corresponds to higher complexity" by design. For example, variant 1 requires one matrix operation, variant 2 requires two operations, and variant 30 requires thirty operations with the identical operation applied repeatedly. The paper explicitly notes that "this construction does not assume or require each operation to use the same compute," allowing natural variation in how models allocate tokens across steps.

**Stage 3: Program-based Generation and Manual Verification** uses Python scripts to generate all variants, ensuring correctness and scalability. To prevent models from exploiting unintended shortcuts such as periodic answer patterns (e.g., all even-indexed variants having answer 1 and all odd-indexed variants having answer 0), the authors "manually verify each seed question and review sampled variants" (Section 3.1). Appendix E.1.2 documents this verification process and notes that variants exhibiting periodic patterns were excluded.

**Evaluation Metric for LORE-MONO** uses Spearman correlation coefficients $\rho \in [-1, 1]$ to measure the relationship between variant index (which determines constructed complexity) and two quantities: reasoning compute and log accuracy. A high positive correlation with reasoning compute indicates that compute grows monotonically with complexity (Property 1), while a negative correlation with log accuracy indicates that accuracy degrades as complexity increases (Property 3). The paper applies max-min normalization to reasoning compute "to prevent any single item from dominating the results" (footnote 4, Section 3.3).

**LORE-COMPO: Measuring Compositionality Through Question Triplets**

LORE-COMPO assesses compositionality by constructing question triplets where two independent sub-questions and their composition can be compared. The benchmark is built from MATH500 (Lightman et al., 2023), where each question is labeled by subject (e.g., Algebra, Geometry).

**Triplet Construction** randomly samples a pair of questions $(x_1, x_2)$ from distinct pre-defined subjects to ensure independence, then concatenates them into a composite question $x_{12} = x_1 \oplus x_2$. Each original question is used at most once, yielding 250 triplets total:

$$
\mathcal{D}_{\text{LoRe-Compo}} = \{(x_1^{(i)}, x_2^{(i)}, x_{12}^{(i)})\}_{i=1}^{250}
$$

Appendix E.3 provides an example composite question with a fixed connector: "Answer the following questions in order: Q1. [question 1] Q2. [question 2]". This ensures sub-questions are answered in sequential order.

**Compositionality Metric** quantifies adherence to the compositionality property using mean absolute deviation (MAD). For a function $f_\theta(\cdot)$ (either $C_\theta(\cdot)$ or $\log A_\theta(\cdot)$), compositionality implies $f_\theta(x_{12}) \approx f_\theta(x_1) + f_\theta(x_2)$. The MAD is:

$$
\text{MAD}_f = \sum_{(x_1, x_2, x_{12}) \in \mathcal{D}_{\text{LoRe-Compo}}} \left| f_\theta(x_{12}) - \left(f_\theta(x_1) + f_\theta(x_2)\right) \right|
$$

However, MAD is scale-dependent, so the paper adopts **Normalized MAD (nMAD)**:

$$
\text{nMAD}_f = \frac{\text{MAD}_f}{S_f}, \quad S_f = \sum_{(x_1, x_2, x_{12}) \in \mathcal{D}_{\text{LoRe-Compo}}} \left| f_\theta(x_1) + f_\theta(x_2) \right|
$$

A smaller nMAD indicates stronger adherence to compositionality. An nMAD of 0 would indicate perfect compositionality, while higher values indicate greater deviation from the additive property.

**SFT-Compo: Fine-Tuning to Enforce Compositionality**

The SFT-Compo method (Section 4) is a supervised fine-tuning approach that directly enforces compute-law compositionality during training. The authors focus on reasoning compute rather than accuracy because "accuracy compositionality is not easy to enforce directly, as it does not specify which reasoning path should be selected for supervision" (footnote 5, Section 4).

**Training Data Construction** begins with a training dataset $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ and a model $M_\theta$ (either the current model or a stronger teacher model). Following the LORE-COMPO construction, the method selects question pairs $(x_1, x_2) \in \mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ from distinct categories and forms composite questions $x_{12} = x_1 \oplus x_2$. For each triplet $(x_1, x_2, x_{12})$, the method samples $K = 8$ model outputs $o = (r, y) \in \mathcal{O}$, where $r$ is a reasoning path and $y$ is the corresponding final answer:

$$
\{o_1^{(k)} = (r_1^{(k)}, y_1^{(k)})\}_{k=1}^K \text{ for } x_1, \quad \{o_2^{(k)} = (r_2^{(k)}, y_2^{(k)})\}_{k=1}^K \text{ for } x_2, \quad \{o_{12}^{(k)} = (r_{12}^{(k)}, y_{12}^{(k)})\}_{k=1}^K \text{ for } x_{12}
$$

**Compositionality Selection Criterion** considers only combinations where all three reasoning paths lead to correct answers, then selects the combination that best satisfies the compositionality condition:

$$
(r_1^*, r_2^*, r_{12}^*) = \arg \min_{r_1, r_2, r_{12}} \left| \ell(r_1) + \ell(r_2) - \ell(r_{12}) \right|
$$

subject to $r_1, r_2, r_{12}$ each yielding a correct final answer (Equation 1, Section 4). This optimization selects reasoning paths where the composite reasoning length equals the sum of individual lengths, directly enforcing the compositionality property.

**Supervised Dataset Construction** aggregates across all triplets to construct the compositional supervision dataset:

$$
\mathcal{D}_{\text{comp}} = \{(x_1, o_1^*), (x_2, o_2^*), (x_{12}, o_{12}^*) \mid (x_1, x_2) \in \mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}\}
$$

where $o_i^* = (r_i^*, y_i^*)$ with $y_i^*$ the final answer paired with $r_i^*$. Each triplet yields three supervised examples, ensuring the model sees both sub-questions and their composition during training.

**Fine-Tuning Recipe** performs supervised fine-tuning on $\mathcal{D}_{\text{comp}}$ for 5 epochs with a batch size of 16 (Section 5.1). The training dataset contains 3.9K question-output pairs constructed from a subset of DeepScaler (Luo et al., 2025b). For each question, the authors use DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-14B as a stronger teacher model to sample $K = 8$ outputs. Appendix F.1 provides additional implementation details including a grid search over learning rates in $\{1\text{e-}6, 5\text{e-}6, 5\text{e-}5\}$, gradient accumulation of 2, and a warmup ratio of 0.

**Control Baseline: SFT** To isolate the effect of compositionality enforcement from simply distilling a stronger teacher model, the paper introduces a control baseline called SFT. This baseline constructs the training dataset by uniformly sampling one correct reasoning path for each question in the triplet:

$$
(r_1^*, r_2^*, r_{12}^*) \sim \text{Unif}\left(\{(r_1, r_2, r_{12}) \mid r_1, r_2, r_{12} \text{ each yield a correct final answer}\}\right)
$$

This control is critical because it tests whether performance gains stem from compositionality enforcement specifically, rather than from leveraging higher-quality teacher outputs. The results show that "SFT-Compo outperforms SFT in all cases" (Section 5.2), demonstrating that the gains are attributable to better compliance with compositionality.

**Evaluation Protocol and Experimental Configuration**

The evaluation uses two tiers of benchmarks. For compositionality, the paper uses LORE-COMPO with nMAD as the primary metric. For general reasoning capacity, six benchmarks are considered: GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), MATH500 (Lightman et al., 2023), AIME 2024, AIME 2025 (Mathematical Association of America, 2025), AMC 2023 (AI-MO, 2024), and OlympiadBench (He et al., 2024). The maximum generation length is set to 10240 tokens for evaluation (Section 5.1).

**Model Coverage** includes four LRMs for the SFT-Compo experiments: DeepSeek-R1-Distill (Qwen-1.5B, Qwen-7B, Llama-8B) and Phi-4-mini-reasoning (Xu et al., 2025a). For the initial LORE-BENCH evaluation (Section 3.3), the paper examines 10 LRMs including eight standard models and two models with reasoning length control (Thinkless-1.5B-RL-DeepScaleR and AdaptThink-7B-delta0.05). For each question, the evaluation samples 8 outputs per model with fixed decoding temperature (0.6 for the DeepSeek family and 0.8 for the Phi-4 family from their technical reports) and a maximum length of 20480 tokens for LORE-BENCH evaluation.

**Design Choices and Rationale**

Several key design choices warrant explanation. First, the authors focus on **reasoning compute compositionality** rather than accuracy compositionality for the SFT-Compo intervention because compute provides "a more direct and actionable criterion for selecting supervision examples" (Section 4). Accuracy compositionality does not specify which reasoning path to select, making it unsuitable for supervised fine-tuning.

Second, the **operational definition of independence** uses disjoint concept sets ($S(x_1) \cap S(x_2) = \emptyset$) rather than a more rigorous formal definition. The paper acknowledges this limitation in Appendix C: "Although this proxy is not rigorous, it is motivated by the practical difficulty of formalizing independence between questions in an actionable and general way." This trade-off prioritizes practical measurability over theoretical purity.

Third, the **synthetic construction of LORE-MONO** rather than using existing benchmarks is necessary because "existing benchmarks are not suited for such analysis" due to the intractability of quantifying complexity (Section 3.1). By constructing variants with known complexity gradients (variant index $N$ corresponds to $N$ operations), the benchmark ensures monotonic complexity by design.

Fourth, the **triplet-based training data construction** for SFT-Compo ensures the model sees both sub-questions and their composition during training. This exposes the model to the compositional structure explicitly, allowing it to internalize the relationship between individual and composite reasoning efforts.

Fifth, the **use of a stronger teacher model** (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-14B) for sampling training data could confound results if the performance gains simply reflect distillation. The SFT control baseline addresses this concern by using the same teacher outputs without compositionality-based selection, isolating the effect of the compositionality enforcement itself.

Finally, the **choice of nMAD over raw MAD** for compositionality measurement addresses scale-dependency. Since reasoning compute varies substantially across models (from ~500 tokens for 1.5B models to ~10,000+ tokens for 80B models), normalization enables fair comparison across model sizes and prevents larger models from dominating the metric simply due to absolute token counts.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper delivers several contributions that distinguish it from prior work on reasoning model control. Below, I identify the most novel elements, explain what makes them fundamentally different from existing approaches, and clarify which represent incremental improvements versus foundational advances.

### 4.1 The LORE Framework: First Formal Theory of Optimal Reasoning Behavior

**What makes it novel:** Prior work on reasoning control operates on empirical heuristics without theoretical grounding. As noted in Section 1, existing methods "primarily rely on ad-hoc heuristics" for controlling reasoning length or budget allocation. LORE is the **first framework to mathematically formalize what optimal reasoning behavior should satisfy** through the Compute Law and Accuracy Law.

**Why it matters:** This shifts the field from *descriptive* observations ("models overthink sometimes") to *prescriptive* principles ("models should allocate compute proportionally to complexity"). The theoretical foundation enables:

- **Predictive power:** If a model violates the laws, we can anticipate failure modes before they manifest on downstream tasks.
- **Design guidance:** Training objectives can be derived from first principles rather than tuned empirically.
- **Cross-model comparability:** The laws provide a universal standard for evaluating reasoning quality independent of specific benchmarks.

**Fundamental vs. Incremental:** This is a **fundamental innovation**. The paper does not merely improve an existing method; it establishes a new theoretical lens through which to view reasoning behavior. Propositions 1 and 2 (Section 2.2–2.3, Appendix D) formally prove that satisfying the tractable properties implies the laws, providing mathematical justification rather than empirical correlation.

### 4.2 LORE-BENCH: Theory-Grounded Evaluation Rather Than Task Performance

**What makes it novel:** Standard benchmarks (MATH500, GSM8K, etc.) measure *what* a model gets right, not *how* it reasons. LORE-BENCH is the **first benchmark suite designed to measure adherence to reasoning laws** rather than task accuracy alone.

The two components serve distinct diagnostic purposes:

| Component | Measures | Prior Equivalent | Key Difference |
|-----------|----------|------------------|----------------|
| LORE-MONO | Monotonicity (compute/accuracy vs. complexity) | None | Synthetic complexity gradients ensure known ordering |
| LORE-COMPO | Compositionality (additivity of compute) | None | 250 triplets with independent sub-questions |

**Why it matters:** This enables **diagnostic evaluation** that reveals *why* models fail, not just *that* they fail. For example, Table 1 shows DeepSeek-R1-1.5B has a negative Spearman correlation (−0.346) between compute and complexity in the language domain—indicating the model sometimes uses *less* compute on harder problems. This pathology would be invisible on standard benchmarks where only final accuracy is reported.

**Fundamental vs. Incremental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in evaluation methodology. While prior work constructs benchmarks for specific tasks (math, code, science), LORE-BENCH constructs benchmarks for specific *behavioral properties*. The insight is that benchmark design should target the underlying reasoning mechanism, not just surface-level task performance.

### 4.3 SFT-Compo: Causal Link Between Law Compliance and Performance

**What makes it novel:** Prior methods for controlling reasoning (e.g., length penalties, confidence-based stopping) treat compute allocation as an optimization problem without theoretical constraints. SFT-Compo is the **first method to directly enforce a theoretical law during training** through the compositionality selection criterion:

$$
(r_1^*, r_2^*, r_{12}^*) = \arg \min_{r_1, r_2, r_{12}} |\ell(r_1) + \ell(r_2) - \ell(r_{12})|
$$

**Why it matters:** The results establish a **causal relationship** between law compliance and reasoning capability, not just correlation:

- **Compositionality improvement:** nMAD for compute drops from 0.528 to 0.314 (40.5% reduction) on the 1.5B model (Figure 6a).
- **Performance gains:** Average Pass@1 increases by +4.8% on the 8B model across six benchmarks (Table 3).
- **Control baseline validation:** SFT-Compo outperforms the SFT baseline (which uses the same teacher outputs without compositionality selection), proving gains come from law enforcement, not just distillation quality.

> "SFT-Compo outperforms SFT in all cases, showing that the gains are not just from distilling a stronger model but from better compliance with compositionality." (Section 5.2)

**Fundamental vs. Incremental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in training methodology. The key insight is that **theoretical constraints can serve as training signals**. Prior work optimizes for task accuracy; SFT-Compo optimizes for behavioral properties that *imply* better accuracy. This represents a paradigm shift from outcome-based to process-based training objectives.

### 4.4 Synergistic Effects: Enforcing One Law Improves Compliance with Others

**What makes it novel:** Perhaps the most surprising empirical finding is that enforcing compute compositionality produces **cross-property and cross-law improvements**. SFT-Compo targets only compute compositionality, yet:

- **Monotonicity improves:** Spearman correlation for compute monotonicity on the 1.5B model increases from 0.875 to 0.977 (Section 5.3, Figure 7a). In the code domain specifically, it rises from 0.151 to 0.914.
- **Accuracy compositionality improves:** nMAD for log accuracy drops from 2.368 to 0.685 (71.1% reduction) on the 1.5B model (Figure 7b).

**Why it matters:** This suggests the reasoning laws are **not independent constraints** but reflect a unified underlying structure. Enforcing one property implicitly regularizes the model toward satisfying others. The paper states:

> "This suggests a possible interplay among different reasoning laws." (Section 5.3)

**Fundamental vs. Incremental:** This is a **key empirical insight** rather than a methodological innovation. The discovery has important implications:
- **Training efficiency:** Enforcing a single property may be sufficient to improve overall reasoning quality.
- **Theoretical validation:** The synergistic effects provide empirical support for the LORE framework's coherence—if the laws were arbitrary, enforcing one would not improve others.

### 4.5 Tractable Proxies for Intractable Quantities

**What makes it novel:** The paper acknowledges that question complexity $\kappa(x)$ is "generally intractable" to compute directly (Definition 1, Section 2.1). Instead of abandoning the theoretical framework, the authors derive **measurable proxies** (monotonicity and compositionality) that are:

1. **Tractable to verify** without access to exact complexity values.
2. **Theoretically sufficient** to imply the laws (Propositions 1 and 2).

**Why it matters:** This design pattern—deriving testable properties from intractable theoretical quantities—is a **methodological contribution** that other researchers can adopt. For example, if future work proposes additional reasoning laws, the same approach (identify tractable properties that imply the law) can be applied.

**Fundamental vs. Incremental:** This is an **incremental but important methodological contribution**. The technique of using proxies for intractable quantities is well-established in theoretical computer science and statistics. However, applying this pattern to reasoning model behavior is novel and enables empirical validation of otherwise untestable hypotheses.

### Summary: Innovation Classification

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| LORE Framework (Compute/Accuracy Laws) | Fundamental | First formal theory of optimal reasoning |
| LORE-BENCH (Monotonicity/Compositionality) | Fundamental | First benchmark for behavioral properties |
| SFT-Compo Training Method | Fundamental | First method enforcing theoretical laws |
| Synergistic Effects Discovery | Empirical Insight | Laws are interconnected, not independent |
| Tractable Proxy Derivation | Methodological | Enables empirical validation of theory |

The paper's core innovation is establishing a **theory-to-practice pipeline**: formal hypotheses → measurable properties → diagnostic benchmarks → intervention methods → empirical validation. This contrasts with prior work that typically starts from empirical observations and develops heuristics without theoretical grounding.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the experimental methodology and results. I walk through how the authors designed their evaluation, what the numbers actually show, and whether the evidence convincingly supports the paper's central claims about reasoning laws and their enforcement.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: Three-Tier Experimental Design

The experiments are structured across three distinct evaluation tiers, each serving a specific purpose in validating the LORE framework.

**Tier 1: LORE-BENCH Diagnostic Evaluation (Section 3.3)**

This tier assesses whether existing LRMs follow the proposed reasoning laws without any intervention. The evaluation covers **10 LRMs** spanning multiple model sizes:

| Model Size | Models Evaluated |
|------------|------------------|
| 1.5B | DeepSeek-R1-1.5B, Thinkless-1.5B |
| 3.8B | Phi-4-mini-reasoning |
| 7B | DeepSeek-R1-7B, AdaptThink-7B |
| 8B | DeepSeek-R1-8B |
| 14B | Nemotron-14B, DeepSeek-R1-14B |
| 32B | Sky-T1-32B |
| 80B | Qwen3-80B-Next |

**LORE-MONO Configuration:**
- **40 seed questions** total (10 per domain: math, science, language, code)
- **30 variants per seed**, yielding 1,200 total questions
- Variant index $N$ directly corresponds to computational complexity (e.g., $N$ matrix operations)
- **Metric**: Spearman correlation coefficient $\rho \in [-1, 1]$ between variant index and (a) reasoning compute, (b) log accuracy
- **Sampling**: 8 outputs per question, temperature 0.6 (DeepSeek family) or 0.8 (Phi-4 family), maximum 20,480 tokens

**LORE-COMPO Configuration:**
- **250 triplets** constructed from MATH500
- Each triplet: $(x_1, x_2, x_{12})$ where $x_1$ and $x_2$ are from distinct subjects (ensuring independence)
- **Metric**: Normalized Mean Absolute Deviation (nMAD)
- For reasoning compute $C_\theta$:
  $$
  \text{nMAD}_{C_\theta} = \frac{\sum |C_\theta(x_{12}) - (C_\theta(x_1) + C_\theta(x_2))|}{\sum |C_\theta(x_1) + C_\theta(x_2)|}
  $$

**Tier 2: SFT-Compo Intervention Experiments (Section 5.1–5.2)**

This tier tests whether enforcing compositionality improves reasoning capability. Four models are fine-tuned:

- DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B
- DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B
- DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B
- Phi-4-mini-reasoning

**Training Configuration:**
- **Dataset**: 3.9K question-output pairs from DeepScaler subset
- **Teacher model**: DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-14B (stronger model for sampling)
- **Sampling**: $K = 8$ outputs per question triplet
- **Fine-tuning**: 5 epochs, batch size 16
- **Learning rates**: Grid search over $\{1\text{e-}6, 5\text{e-}6, 5\text{e-}5\}$
- **Control baseline (SFT)**: Same teacher outputs but uniformly sampled without compositionality selection

**Tier 3: General Reasoning Benchmarks (Section 5.2)**

To verify that law compliance translates to practical performance, models are evaluated on six standard benchmarks:

| Benchmark | Domain | Difficulty |
|-----------|--------|------------|
| GSM8K | Math | Grade school |
| MATH500 | Math | High school/competition |
| AIME 2024 | Math | Olympiad |
| AIME 2025 | Math | Olympiad |
| AMC 2023 | Math | Competition |
| OlympiadBench | Science/Math | Olympiad |

**Metric**: Pass@1 accuracy (%) computed over 8 sampled outputs, maximum generation length 10,240 tokens.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

#### 5.2.1 Monotonicity: Models Generally Scale Compute with Complexity

**Table 1** presents Spearman correlations for reasoning compute and log accuracy across four domains. The results reveal a clear pattern:

**Reasoning Compute Monotonicity (Property 1):**
- Most models exhibit **strong positive correlations** (0.875 to 0.995), indicating compute increases with complexity
- **Best performer**: AdaptThink-7B with 0.995 overall correlation
- **Weakest performer**: DeepSeek-R1-1.5B with 0.875 overall correlation

However, the weakness of DeepSeek-R1-1.5B is **domain-specific**:

| Domain | DeepSeek-R1-1.5B | Thinkless-1.5B |
|--------|------------------|----------------|
| Math | 0.861 | 0.943 |
| Science | 0.910 | 0.961 |
| Language | **-0.346** | 0.648 |
| Code | **0.151** | 0.794 |

> "In the language domain its correlation between reasoning compute and complexity is negative (−0.346), while in the code domain, it is near zero (0.151). This indicates that in some domains, the reasoning compute for this model does not systematically increase with complexity, and may even decrease." (Section 3.3)

This is a critical finding: **monotonicity is not universal**. The 1.5B model actually uses *less* compute on harder language problems, suggesting it gives up or employs shortcuts rather than scaling effort appropriately.

**Log Accuracy Monotonicity (Property 3):**
- All models show **negative correlations** (-0.868 to -0.981), confirming accuracy degrades with complexity
- DeepSeek-R1-1.5B again shows the weakest trend (-0.868 vs. -0.960 for Thinkless-1.5B)

**Figure 4** visualizes these trends for DeepSeek-R1-1.5B, showing the mean accuracy curves across 10 question series with standard deviation shaded regions. The language and code domains clearly deviate from expected monotonic behavior.

#### 5.2.2 Compositionality: Universal Failure Across All Models

**Table 2** reveals the paper's most significant diagnostic finding: **no model satisfies compositionality well**.

| Model | nMAD$_{C_\theta}$ (Compute) | nMAD$_{\log A_\theta}$ (Accuracy) |
|-------|-----------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| DeepSeek-R1-1.5B | **0.528** | **2.368** |
| Thinkless-1.5B | 0.339 | 0.694 |
| Phi-4-mini | 0.322 | 0.732 |
| DeepSeek-R1-7B | 0.337 | 1.170 |
| AdaptThink-7B | 0.327 | 0.791 |
| DeepSeek-R1-8B | 0.423 | 0.818 |
| DeepSeek-R1-14B | 0.368 | 1.310 |
| Nemotron-14B | 0.354 | 1.188 |
| Sky-T1-32B | 0.392 | 1.900 |
| Qwen3-80B-Next | 0.411 | 1.487 |

**Key observations:**

1. **Compute compositionality nMAD ranges from 0.322 to 0.528** — an nMAD of 0.5 means the composite reasoning length deviates from the sum of individual lengths by 50% on average. This is substantial.

2. **Accuracy compositionality is worse** — nMAD values range from 0.694 to 2.368, with DeepSeek-R1-1.5B showing extreme deviation (2.368).

3. **Reasoning length control does not help** — Thinkless-1.5B and AdaptThink-7B, which explicitly modulate reasoning length, still show nMAD of 0.339 and 0.327 respectively. The paper notes:

> "Notably, even models equipped with reasoning length control mechanisms (Thinkless-1.5B and AdaptThink-7B) exhibit considerable deviations, suggesting that such techniques do not inherently promote compositional behavior." (Section 3.3)

4. **Larger models do not consistently improve** — Qwen3-80B-Next (0.411) performs worse than Phi-4-mini (0.322), indicating compositionality does not emerge naturally with scale.

**Figure 5** visualizes compositionality for DeepSeek-R1-1.5B and DeepSeek-R1-8B by plotting $C_\theta(x_1 \oplus x_2)$ against $C_\theta(x_1) + C_\theta(x_2)$. If models followed compositionality, points would align with the $y = x$ line. Instead, points scatter substantially above and below the line, with the 1.5B model showing particularly severe deviation (nMAD = 0.528).

#### 5.2.3 SFT-Compo: Enforcing Compositionality Works

**Figure 6a** shows nMAD reduction across all four fine-tuned models:

| Model | Base nMAD | SFT-Compo nMAD | Reduction |
|-------|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| 1.5B | 0.528 | 0.314 | **40.5%** |
| 7B | 0.337 | 0.317 | 5.9% |
| 8B | 0.423 | 0.328 | **22.5%** |

The 1.5B model shows the largest improvement, suggesting smaller models benefit most from explicit compositionality enforcement.

**Figure 6b** visualizes the improvement for the 1.5B model. Post-SFT-Compo, points cluster much more tightly around the $y = x$ line, indicating the model now allocates compute additively across independent sub-problems.

#### 5.2.4 General Reasoning Performance: Law Compliance Yields Better Accuracy

**Table 3** presents the critical result: enforcing compositionality improves performance on standard benchmarks.

**DeepSeek-R1-1.5B Results:**

| Method | AIME24 | AIME25 | AMC23 | MATH500 | GSM8K | Olympiad | **Pass@1 Avg** |
|--------|--------|--------|-------|---------|-------|----------|----------------|
| Base | 18.8 | 20.4 | 59.7 | 71.6 | 81.2 | 33.8 | **47.6** |
| SFT | 20.4 (+1.6) | 21.5 (+1.1) | 59.6 (-0.1) | 76.4 (+4.8) | 81.7 (+0.5) | 36.1 (+2.3) | **49.3 (+1.7)** |
| SFT-Compo | **26.2 (+7.4)** | **21.7 (+1.3)** | **65.0 (+5.3)** | **77.6 (+6.0)** | **85.1 (+3.9)** | **38.7 (+4.9)** | **52.4 (+4.8)** |

**Key findings:**

1. **SFT-Compo outperforms SFT in all cases** — This is critical because both methods use the same teacher model outputs. The difference is solely the compositionality-based selection criterion. This proves gains come from **law compliance**, not just distillation quality.

2. **Largest gains on hardest benchmarks** — AIME24 shows +7.4% improvement (18.8 → 26.2), while GSM8K shows +3.9% (81.2 → 85.1). This suggests compositionality enforcement particularly helps on complex reasoning tasks.

3. **Consistent across model sizes** — All four models show improvement:

| Model | Base Pass@1 | SFT-Compo Pass@1 | Gain |
|-------|-------------|------------------|------|
| 1.5B | 47.6 | 52.4 | +4.8 |
| 7B | 61.5 | 64.7 | +3.2 |
| 8B | 54.5 | 59.5 | **+5.0** |
| Phi-4-mini | 59.5 | 63.9 | +4.4 |

The 8B model shows the largest absolute gain (+5.0%), demonstrating the method scales beyond the smallest models.

### 5.3 Synergistic Effects: Cross-Property and Cross-Law Improvements

**Figure 7** reveals an unexpected finding: enforcing compute compositionality improves **other properties** that were not directly optimized.

**Monotonicity Improvement (Figure 7a):**
- DeepSeek-R1-1.5B overall Spearman correlation: 0.875 → **0.977**
- Code domain specifically: 0.151 → **0.914**

This is remarkable because SFT-Compo only selects triplets based on compute additivity, not monotonicity. The improvement suggests the reasoning laws are **interconnected** — enforcing one implicitly regularizes toward satisfying others.

**Accuracy Compositionality Improvement (Figure 7b):**

| Model | Base nMAD$_{\log A_\theta}$ | SFT-Compo nMAD$_{\log A_\theta}$ | Reduction |
|-------|----------------------------|----------------------------------|-----------|
| 1.5B | 2.368 | 0.685 | **71.1%** |
| 7B | 1.170 | 0.756 | 35.4% |

The 1.5B model shows a 71.1% reduction in accuracy compositionality deviation despite never being trained on accuracy-based selection. The paper states:

> "This suggests a possible interplay among different reasoning laws." (Section 5.3)

**Why this matters:** If the laws were independent constraints, enforcing compute compositionality would not systematically improve accuracy compositionality. The synergistic effects provide **empirical validation** that the LORE framework captures a unified underlying structure of optimal reasoning behavior.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment: Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

#### 5.4.1 Strengths of the Experimental Design

**1. Control Baseline Isolates Compositionality Effect**

The SFT baseline is essential for causal inference. Both SFT and SFT-Compo use:
- Same teacher model (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-14B)
- Same sampling procedure ($K = 8$ outputs)
- Same training recipe (5 epochs, batch size 16)

The only difference is the selection criterion. Since SFT-Compo consistently outperforms SFT (Table 3), the gains cannot be attributed to teacher quality alone.

**2. Multiple Model Sizes Demonstrate Generalizability**

Results span 1.5B to 80B models for diagnostic evaluation, and 1.5B to 8B for intervention experiments. The consistent pattern (compositionality failure across all sizes, improvement after SFT-Compo) suggests the findings are not model-specific artifacts.

**3. Diverse Benchmarks Validate Practical Impact**

Six benchmarks across math and science domains confirm that law compliance translates to real performance gains, not just improved LORE-BENCH metrics.

#### 5.4.2 Limitations and Caveats

**1. LORE-MONO Coverage is Limited**

Appendix C acknowledges:

> "First, our LORE-MONO currently includes only 40 seed questions in total. Expanding its topic diversity and coverage is an important direction for future work."

Forty seed questions (10 per domain) is relatively small for claiming universal monotonicity properties. The synthetic construction ensures known complexity gradients, but real-world problems may not follow such clean patterns.

**2. Independence Definition is Operational, Not Rigorous**

The paper defines independence via disjoint concept sets ($S(x_1) \cap S(x_2) = \emptyset$). Appendix C notes:

> "Although this proxy is not rigorous, it is motivated by the practical difficulty of formalizing independence between questions in an actionable and general way."

Two questions from different subjects (e.g., Algebra and Geometry) may still share underlying reasoning patterns. A more rigorous independence definition would strengthen LORE-COMPO's validity.

**3. Closed-Source Models Not Evaluated**

Appendix C states:

> "Finally, due to budget constraints, we focus on strong open-source LRMs, as evaluating closed-source models would require substantial additional cost."

This limits claims about whether the reasoning laws apply universally. OpenAI o1, Anthropic Claude, and Google Gemini may exhibit different patterns.

**4. "Fail to Think" Cases Reveal Edge Conditions**

Appendix E.2.2 documents a failure mode where DeepSeek-R1-1.5B produces **zero reasoning tokens** on some code questions:

> "For example, on variant 1 of a seed code question, the generated output is simply \n</think>, corresponding to a reasoning length of 0."

This edge case is not addressed in the main analysis. If models can skip reasoning entirely, the compute law becomes ill-defined for those instances.

**5. Training Dataset is Small**

The SFT-Compo dataset contains only 3.9K question-output pairs. While this demonstrates proof-of-concept, it is unclear whether larger datasets would yield diminishing returns or continued improvement.

#### 5.4.3 Mixed Results and Trade-offs

**Domain-Specific Monotonicity Failures**

Table 1 shows monotonicity is not uniform across domains. DeepSeek-R1-1.5B has negative correlation in language (-0.346) and near-zero in code (0.151). This suggests:

- **Trade-off**: Improving compositionality may not fix all monotonicity violations
- **Condition**: Domain characteristics matter — language and code may have different reasoning structures than math

**Diminishing Returns for Larger Models**

The 7B model shows only 5.9% nMAD reduction (0.337 → 0.317) compared to 40.5% for the 1.5B model. This suggests:

- **Trade-off**: Smaller models benefit more from explicit compositionality enforcement
- **Condition**: Larger models may already have implicit compositional structure that is harder to improve

**Accuracy Compositionality Remains Imperfect**

Even after SFT-Compo, accuracy compositionality nMAD is 0.685 (1.5B model), not zero. The method improves but does not fully solve the problem.

### 5.5 Summary of Experimental Evidence

| Claim | Supporting Evidence | Strength |
|-------|---------------------|----------|
| Models violate compositionality | Table 2: nMAD 0.322–0.528 across all 10 models | **Strong** |
| Models satisfy monotonicity (mostly) | Table 1: Spearman 0.875–0.995 for compute | **Moderate** (domain exceptions exist) |
| SFT-Compo enforces compositionality | Figure 6: nMAD reduction 22.5–40.5% | **Strong** |
| Compositionality improves reasoning | Table 3: +3.2 to +5.0 Pass@1 gains | **Strong** |
| Gains from law compliance, not distillation | SFT-Compo > SFT baseline | **Strong** |
| Synergistic effects across laws | Figure 7: Monotonicity and accuracy compositionality improve | **Strong** |

The experiments provide **convincing evidence** for the paper's central claims. The control baseline, multiple model sizes, and diverse benchmarks support causal inference rather than mere correlation. However, the limited LORE-MONO coverage, operational independence definition, and exclusion of closed-source models leave room for future validation.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the assumptions, constraints, and unresolved weaknesses in the LORE framework. While the paper demonstrates compelling evidence for its central claims, several important limitations affect the generalizability and practical applicability of the approach. I organize these into four categories: theoretical assumptions, benchmark coverage, computational constraints, and edge cases.

### 6.1 Theoretical Assumptions and Their Validity

**Assumption 1: Complexity Can Be Approximated Through Variant Index**

The LORE-MONO benchmark relies on a critical assumption: that variant index $N$ (number of operations applied) directly corresponds to question complexity $\kappa(x)$. As stated in Section 3.1:

> "By design, a larger number of steps directly corresponds to higher complexity."

**Why this is limiting:** This construction assumes each operation contributes equally to complexity, which may not hold in practice. For example, in the math seed question (Appendix E.1.1), applying a recurrence relation 30 times may not be 30× more complex than applying it once if the model discovers a closed-form solution or periodic pattern. The paper acknowledges this risk in Appendix E.1.2, noting they manually excluded variants with "periodic answer patterns," but this verification process is labor-intensive and may not catch all shortcuts.

**Trade-off:** The synthetic construction ensures known complexity ordering but sacrifices ecological validity. Real-world problems do not come with clean complexity gradients, so monotonicity on LORE-MONO may not translate to monotonicity on natural tasks.

**Assumption 2: Independence Via Disjoint Concept Sets**

The paper defines two questions as independent if their mathematical concept sets are disjoint: $S(x_1) \cap S(x_2) = \emptyset$ (Section 2.2, Definition 4). For LORE-COMPO, this is operationalized by sampling from distinct MATH500 subjects (e.g., Algebra + Geometry).

**Why this is limiting:** Appendix C explicitly acknowledges:

> "Although this proxy is not rigorous, it is motivated by the practical difficulty of formalizing independence between questions in an actionable and general way."

Two questions from different subjects may still share underlying reasoning patterns. For example, an Algebra question involving quadratic equations and a Geometry question involving area calculations both require polynomial manipulation. Conversely, two Algebra questions might be truly independent if they involve unrelated concepts (e.g., number theory vs. linear equations).

**Trade-off:** The operational definition prioritizes **practical measurability** over **theoretical rigor**. This enables benchmark construction but introduces noise into compositionality measurements.

**Assumption 3: Compute Compositionality Implies Better Reasoning**

The SFT-Compo method enforces compute compositionality specifically, not accuracy compositionality. Section 4 states:

> "Accuracy compositionality is not easy to enforce directly, as it does not specify which reasoning path should be selected for supervision." (footnote 5)

**Why this is limiting:** This assumes that correct compute allocation causally improves accuracy. While the synergistic effects in Figure 7 support this (accuracy compositionality nMAD drops 71.1% on 1.5B model), the mechanism is not fully explained. It is possible that both improvements stem from a third factor (e.g., exposure to higher-quality teacher outputs) rather than compute compositionality itself.

**Trade-off:** Focusing on compute provides an actionable training signal but may not capture all aspects of optimal reasoning behavior.

### 6.2 Benchmark Coverage and Generalizability

**Limitation 1: Small LORE-MONO Coverage**

Appendix C explicitly states:

> "First, our LORE-MONO currently includes only 40 seed questions in total. Expanding its topic diversity and coverage is an important direction for future work."

**Impact:** Forty seed questions (10 per domain) is relatively small for claiming universal monotonicity properties. Table 1 shows domain-specific failures (e.g., DeepSeek-R1-1.5B has negative correlation −0.346 in language), but with only 10 seeds per domain, these findings may not be statistically robust.

**Open Question:** Would the monotonicity patterns hold with 100+ seeds per domain? Are there domains beyond math, science, language, and code where the laws break down more severely?

**Limitation 2: Exclusion of Closed-Source Models**

Appendix C notes:

> "Finally, due to budget constraints, we focus on strong open-source LRMs, as evaluating closed-source models would require substantial additional cost."

**Impact:** The paper cannot claim the reasoning laws apply universally. OpenAI o1, Anthropic Claude, and Google Gemini may exhibit fundamentally different reasoning patterns. Given that o1 inspired the "thinking-then-answering" paradigm (Section 1), understanding whether it follows the LORE framework is critical.

**Open Question:** Do proprietary models with potentially more sophisticated training procedures exhibit better compositionality? Or do they share the same fundamental limitations?

**Limitation 3: MATH-Dominated LORE-COMPO**

LORE-COMPO is constructed entirely from MATH500 (Section 3.2). While this ensures subject labels for independence, it limits diversity:

- No code reasoning tasks
- No scientific reasoning tasks
- No multi-modal problems

**Impact:** The compositionality findings may not generalize to non-mathematical domains. Table 1 already shows domain-specific monotonicity variations; compositionality may similarly vary by domain.

**Trade-off:** Using MATH500 enables clean independence definitions but reduces benchmark diversity.

### 6.3 Computational and Data Constraints

**Constraint 1: Small Training Dataset for SFT-Compo**

Section 5.1 states the SFT-Compo dataset contains only **3.9K question-output pairs**. For comparison, standard supervised fine-tuning datasets often contain 100K+ examples.

**Impact:** It is unclear whether the observed improvements would scale with more training data. The gains could represent:
- **Early saturation:** Most compositionality improvement happens with small datasets
- **Limited capacity:** 3.9K pairs are insufficient for full law internalization
- **Diminishing returns:** Additional data provides marginal benefit

**Open Question:** What is the data efficiency curve for SFT-Compo? Would 10× more data yield 10× better compositionality, or would improvements plateau?

**Constraint 2: Teacher Model Dependency**

SFT-Compo uses DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-14B as a teacher model for sampling training data (Section 5.1). This introduces two concerns:

1. **Teacher quality ceiling:** The student model cannot exceed the teacher's reasoning capability through distillation alone.
2. **Teacher law compliance:** If the teacher itself violates the reasoning laws (Table 2 shows DeepSeek-R1-14B has nMAD 0.368), the student inherits these violations.

**Evidence:** The SFT control baseline uses the same teacher but without compositionality selection, and SFT-Compo still outperforms it (Table 3). This suggests the selection criterion matters, but the teacher's absolute capability remains a bottleneck.

**Constraint 3: Sampling Cost**

For each triplet, SFT-Compo samples $K = 8$ outputs per question (Section 4), requiring $8 \times 3 = 24$ forward passes per training example. For 3.9K pairs, this is approximately **93.6K forward passes** just for data construction.

**Impact:** This sampling cost scales linearly with dataset size. For larger-scale deployment, the data construction pipeline becomes a significant computational bottleneck.

**Trade-off:** The sampling-based selection enables compositionality enforcement but at substantial computational cost.

### 6.4 Edge Cases and Unaddressed Scenarios

**Edge Case 1: "Fail to Think" Phenomenon**

Appendix E.2.2 documents cases where DeepSeek-R1-1.5B produces **zero reasoning tokens**:

> "For example, on variant 1 of a seed code question, the generated output is simply \n</think>, corresponding to a reasoning length of 0."

**Why this matters:** If reasoning length $\ell(r) = 0$, the compute law becomes ill-defined. The compositionality criterion $|\ell(r_1) + \ell(r_2) - \ell(r_{12})|$ cannot distinguish between:
- A model that correctly identifies a trivial problem requiring no reasoning
- A model that fails to engage reasoning when it is needed

**Unaddressed:** The paper does not filter out or specially handle zero-reasoning cases during SFT-Compo training. This could introduce noise into the compositionality signal.

**Edge Case 2: Domain-Specific Monotonicity Failures**

Table 1 reveals that monotonicity is not uniform across domains. DeepSeek-R1-1.5B shows:

| Domain | Compute Monotonicity ($\rho$) |
|--------|-------------------------------|
| Math | 0.861 |
| Science | 0.910 |
| Language | **−0.346** |
| Code | **0.151** |

**Why this matters:** SFT-Compo improves overall monotonicity (Figure 7a shows 0.875 → 0.977), but the paper does not report domain-specific improvements. It is unclear whether compositionality enforcement fixes domain-specific pathologies or merely improves average behavior.

**Open Question:** Do language and code reasoning require different "laws" than mathematical reasoning? The LORE framework assumes universal laws, but domain-specific variations may exist.

**Edge Case 3: Overthinking vs. Underthinking**

The introduction identifies both overthinking (excessive computation on simple problems) and underthinking (insufficient computation on complex problems) as failure modes (Section 1). However, SFT-Compo only enforces **additivity**, not **absolute efficiency**.

**Why this matters:** A model could satisfy compositionality while systematically overthinking all problems. For example:
- $C(x_1) = 1000$ tokens (should be 500)
- $C(x_2) = 1000$ tokens (should be 500)
- $C(x_{12}) = 2000$ tokens (satisfies additivity)

This model is compositional but inefficient. The compute law includes a proportionality constant $\alpha_\theta$, but SFT-Compo does not optimize $\alpha_\theta$ directly.

**Unaddressed:** The paper does not measure whether SFT-Compo reduces absolute token consumption or merely redistributes it compositionally.

**Edge Case 4: Diminishing Returns for Larger Models**

Figure 6a shows nMAD reduction varies by model size:

| Model | nMAD Reduction |
|-------|----------------|
| 1.5B | 40.5% |
| 7B | 5.9% |
| 8B | 22.5% |

**Why this matters:** The 7B model shows minimal improvement (0.337 → 0.317), suggesting compositionality enforcement has **diminishing returns** for certain model sizes. This could indicate:
- Larger models have implicit compositional structure that is harder to modify
- The 7B model is in a "sweet spot" where it is large enough to resist fine-tuning but small enough to have poor initial compositionality
- Training hyperparameters were not optimized per model size

**Unaddressed:** The paper uses identical training recipes (5 epochs, batch size 16) for all models. Size-specific tuning might yield better results.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Future Directions

Based on the limitations above, several important questions remain unresolved:

**Question 1: Do the Laws Hold for Non-Autoregressive Reasoning?**

The LORE framework assumes autoregressive "thinking-then-answering" models (Section 2.1). Emerging architectures with parallel reasoning or tree-structured thinking (e.g., Tree of Thoughts, Yao et al., 2023) may not satisfy the same laws. The token-counting definition of compute does not apply to parallel computation.

**Question 2: What Is the Optimal $\alpha_\theta$?**

The compute law states $C_\theta(x) = \alpha_\theta \kappa(x) + o(\kappa(x))$, but the paper does not investigate what value of $\alpha_\theta$ is optimal. Different tasks may require different compute-to-complexity ratios.

**Question 3: Can Compositionality Be Enforced at Test Time?**

SFT-Compo requires fine-tuning. It is unclear whether compositionality can be enforced through inference-time methods (e.g., beam search constraints, decoding penalties) without model modification. This would enable deployment without retraining.

**Question 4: How Do the Laws Interact With Safety and Alignment?**

The paper's ethics statement claims "This work does not raise any known ethical concerns" (Ethics Statement). However, enforcing specific reasoning patterns could have unintended consequences:
- Models might learn to "game" the compositionality metric without genuine reasoning improvement
- Restricting reasoning patterns could reduce model creativity or flexibility
- Compositionality enforcement might interact unpredictably with safety training

**Question 5: What Is the Long-Term Stability of Law Compliance?**

The evaluation measures immediate post-training performance. It is unclear whether models maintain law compliance after:
- Additional fine-tuning on downstream tasks
- Extended inference usage
- Exposure to out-of-distribution problems

### 6.6 Summary: Critical Trade-offs

| Trade-off | Benefit | Cost |
|-----------|---------|------|
| Synthetic LORE-MONO | Known complexity gradients | Limited ecological validity |
| Disjoint concept independence | Practical measurability | Theoretical imprecision |
| Compute-focused SFT | Actionable training signal | Ignores accuracy compositionality |
| Small training dataset | Proof-of-concept feasibility | Unclear scalability |
| Open-source only | Reproducibility | Limited generalizability |
| Fixed training recipe | Experimental consistency | Suboptimal per-model tuning |

The LORE framework represents a significant step toward theory-grounded reasoning control, but these limitations highlight important directions for future work. The most critical open question is whether the reasoning laws reflect **universal principles** of optimal reasoning or **artifact-specific patterns** that emerge from current model architectures and training procedures.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work establishes a foundational shift in how we understand, evaluate, and improve reasoning in Large Reasoning Models. Below, I outline how LORE changes the research landscape, what new directions it opens, where it can be practically applied, and guidance for integrating these methods.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Research Landscape

**From Heuristics to Theory-Grounded Design**

Prior work on reasoning control operated through empirical trial-and-error. Methods like length penalties (Zhang et al., 2025a), confidence-based stopping (Qiao et al., 2025), and variable-length CoT fine-tuning (Aggarwal & Welleck, 2025) were motivated by observations rather than principles. LORE changes this by establishing:

- **Prescriptive laws** rather than descriptive patterns: The Compute Law ($C_\theta(x) = \alpha_\theta \kappa(x) + o(\kappa(x))$) and Accuracy Law ($A_\theta(x) = \exp(-\lambda_\theta \kappa(x))$) define what optimal reasoning *should* satisfy, not just what current models do.
- **Mathematically justified proxies**: Propositions 1 and 2 (Section 2.2–2.3, Appendix D) prove that monotonicity and compositionality imply the laws, providing theoretical grounding absent in prior work.
- **Causal intervention design**: SFT-Compo enforces a theoretical property during training, establishing that law compliance *causes* performance improvement rather than merely correlating with it.

This shifts the field from asking *"What training tricks improve accuracy?"* to *"What behavioral properties should optimal reasoning satisfy, and how do we enforce them?"*

**New Evaluation Paradigm: Behavioral Diagnostics Over Task Scores**

Standard benchmarks (MATH500, GSM8K, AIME) measure *what* models get right, not *how* they reason. LORE-BENCH introduces a complementary evaluation layer:

| Traditional Benchmarks | LORE-BENCH |
|------------------------|------------|
| Measures final accuracy | Measures reasoning behavior |
| Task-specific | Behavior-specific (monotonicity, compositionality) |
| Reveals capability gaps | Reveals *why* gaps exist |
| Example: 47.6% Pass@1 on 1.5B model | Example: nMAD = 0.528, monotonicity ρ = 0.875 |

This enables **diagnostic debugging**: Table 1 reveals DeepSeek-R1-1.5B has negative compute-complexity correlation (−0.346) in the language domain, indicating the model uses *less* compute on harder problems. This pathology would be invisible on MATH500 alone.

**Unified Framework for Reasoning Research**

LORE provides a common vocabulary and measurement framework that enables:

- **Cross-model comparison**: nMAD and Spearman correlations allow direct comparison across model sizes and architectures (Table 2 shows all 10 models fail compositionality to varying degrees).
- **Progress tracking**: Future models can be evaluated not just on task accuracy but on law compliance, creating new leaderboards.
- **Mechanism isolation**: By separating monotonicity from compositionality, researchers can identify which aspect of reasoning behavior needs improvement.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The LORE framework opens numerous research avenues. I categorize these by proximity to the current work.

**Immediate Extensions (1–2 Years)**

1. **Closed-Source Model Evaluation**
   
   Appendix C notes closed-source models (OpenAI o1, Anthropic Claude, Google Gemini) were excluded due to cost. Testing these is critical because:
   - o1 inspired the "thinking-then-answering" paradigm (Section 1)
   - Proprietary training may have implicitly optimized for reasoning laws
   - Results would validate whether LORE applies universally or only to open-source architectures
   
   *Methodology*: Use API-based sampling with LORE-BENCH prompts. Estimate ~$500–$2,000 for full evaluation depending on model pricing.

2. **Expanded LORE-MONO Coverage**
   
   The current benchmark has only 40 seed questions (10 per domain). Appendix C identifies this as a limitation. Follow-up work should:
   - Increase to 100+ seeds per domain for statistical robustness
   - Add new domains: multi-modal reasoning, scientific hypothesis generation, legal reasoning
   - Validate complexity gradients through human expert annotation
   
   *Expected impact*: Stronger claims about universal monotonicity properties.

3. **Domain-Specific Law Variations**
   
   Table 1 shows monotonicity failures in language (−0.346) and code (0.151) for the 1.5B model, while math and science show strong correlations (0.861–0.910). This suggests:
   - Different domains may require different $\alpha_\theta$ values in the Compute Law
   - Language and code reasoning may have distinct structural properties
   
   *Research question*: Should LORE include domain-specific law parameters, or can a single framework capture all domains?

4. **Test-Time Compositionality Enforcement**
   
   SFT-Compo requires fine-tuning. Test-time methods would enable deployment without retraining:
   - **Decoding constraints**: Penalize outputs where $\ell(r_{12})$ deviates from $\ell(r_1) + \ell(r_2)$
   - **Dynamic budget allocation**: Allocate tokens proportionally to estimated sub-problem complexity
   - **Early stopping**: Terminate reasoning when compositionality criterion is satisfied
   
   *Advantage*: Applicable to any model without modification. *Challenge*: Requires estimating $\ell(r_1) + \ell(r_2)$ before generating $r_{12}$.

**Medium-Term Directions (2–4 Years)**

5. **Optimal $\alpha_\theta$ Investigation**
   
   The Compute Law includes a proportionality constant $\alpha_\theta$ that the paper does not optimize. Key questions:
   - What $\alpha_\theta$ values maximize accuracy for different task classes?
   - Does optimal $\alpha_\theta$ vary by model size, domain, or difficulty?
   - Can $\alpha_\theta$ be learned adaptively per-input?
   
   *Potential approach*: Reinforcement learning with reward $R = \text{accuracy} - \beta \cdot |C_\theta(x) - \alpha_\theta \kappa(x)|$.

6. **Architecture-Aware Reasoning Laws**
   
   LORE assumes autoregressive "thinking-then-answering" models (Section 2.1). Emerging architectures challenge this:
   - **Tree of Thoughts** (Yao et al., 2023): Parallel branching requires different compute definitions
   - **Non-autoregressive reasoning**: Token counting does not capture parallel computation
   - **Multi-modal models**: Visual reasoning may follow different scaling laws
   
   *Research direction*: Generalize LORE to non-sequential reasoning structures.

7. **Law Compliance and Safety/Alignment**
   
   The Ethics Statement claims "no known ethical concerns," but enforcing reasoning patterns could have unintended effects:
   - **Gaming risk**: Models might learn to satisfy compositionality metrics without genuine reasoning improvement
   - **Creativity reduction**: Strict law compliance could constrain exploratory reasoning
   - **Safety interactions**: Compositionality enforcement might conflict with safety training objectives
   
   *Investigation needed*: Evaluate SFT-Compo on safety benchmarks (e.g., harmful request refusal, jailbreak resistance).

8. **Long-Term Stability of Law Compliance**
   
   The paper measures immediate post-training performance. Open questions:
   - Do models maintain law compliance after downstream fine-tuning?
   - Does compositionality degrade with extended inference usage?
   - How do models respond to out-of-distribution problems?
   
   *Methodology*: Longitudinal evaluation over multiple fine-tuning cycles.

**Long-Term Vision (4+ Years)**

9. **Theory of Optimal Reasoning**
   
   LORE is a starting point, not a complete theory. Future work could develop:
   - **Information-theoretic foundations**: Relate compute to mutual information between reasoning chain and answer
   - **Computational complexity connections**: Link $\kappa(x)$ to formal complexity classes
   - **Cognitive science integration**: Compare LORE laws to human reasoning patterns (Newell et al., 1972, cited in Section 1)

10. **Automated Law Discovery**
    
    Rather than hypothesizing laws, use meta-learning to discover optimal reasoning properties:
    - Train a meta-model to predict which behavioral properties correlate with performance
    - Use automated benchmark generation to test candidate laws
    - Evolve law formulations through reinforcement learning

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

LORE has immediate practical value beyond research. I outline specific deployment scenarios.

**Application 1: Efficient Resource Allocation**

Current LRMs exhibit unpredictable compute patterns. Figure 1 shows DeepSeek-R1 allocates ~300 *fewer* tokens to a composite question than to a simpler sub-question, with 12.5% accuracy drop. SFT-Compo addresses this:

- **Cost predictability**: Compositional models allocate tokens proportionally to problem complexity, enabling accurate cost estimation
- **Reduced waste**: Eliminating overthinking on simple problems saves tokens; eliminating underthinking on complex problems reduces retry rates
- **Deployment planning**: Organizations can estimate inference costs based on expected problem complexity distributions

*Estimated impact*: For high-volume deployments (100K+ queries/day), 20–30% compute savings on simple queries could translate to significant cost reduction.

**Application 2: Diagnostic Model Evaluation**

LORE-BENCH provides diagnostic insights that standard benchmarks miss:

| Scenario | Standard Benchmark | LORE-BENCH Insight |
|----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Model A: 60% MATH500 | "Good math model" | nMAD = 0.5: Unreliable on composite problems |
| Model B: 55% MATH500 | "Worse than A" | nMAD = 0.2: More predictable, better for production |
| Model C: Domain failure | "Average overall" | Language ρ = −0.35: Avoid for text reasoning tasks |

*Use case*: Model selection for production systems should include LORE-BENCH metrics, not just task accuracy.

**Application 3: Training Data Quality Control**

Section 1 notes CoT training data is "heuristically curated... rarely constrained by explicit rules." LORE enables:

- **Data filtering**: Remove training examples that violate compositionality
- **Data augmentation**: Generate synthetic triplets that enforce compositional structure
- **Curriculum design**: Order training examples by complexity to teach monotonicity

*Integration*: Add LORE metrics to existing data quality pipelines (e.g., alongside toxicity filtering, deduplication).

**Application 4: Adaptive Inference Systems**

Systems that dynamically allocate compute budget can use LORE principles:

- **Complexity estimation**: Use variant index or concept count to estimate $\kappa(x)$
- **Budget allocation**: Set token limit proportional to estimated complexity
- **Early termination**: Stop reasoning when compositionality criterion is satisfied

*Example*: A customer support system could allocate more tokens to complex technical queries and fewer to simple FAQ questions, improving both cost and user experience.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

**When to Use SFT-Compo**

SFT-Compo is not universally optimal. Use the following decision framework:

| Condition | Recommendation | Rationale |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| Small models (≤8B) | **Strongly prefer SFT-Compo** | Figure 6a shows 22.5–40.5% nMAD reduction; Table 3 shows +3.2 to +5.0 Pass@1 gains |
| Large models (≥70B) | **Consider alternatives** | Diminishing returns; Qwen3-80B-Next shows nMAD = 0.411 (Table 2), but fine-tuning cost is prohibitive |
| Math-heavy workloads | **Use SFT-Compo** | LORE-COMPO built from MATH500; synergistic effects improve math benchmarks (Table 3) |
| Language/code workloads | **Evaluate first** | Table 1 shows domain-specific monotonicity failures; SFT-Compo may not fix all pathologies |
| Limited training budget | **Use SFT baseline** | SFT-Compo requires 24 forward passes per triplet (K=8 sampling); SFT baseline is 3× cheaper |
| Production deployment | **Test-time methods preferred** | SFT-Compo requires retraining; test-time compositionality enforcement (future work) would be more flexible |

**Integration Checklist**

For teams adopting SFT-Compo:

1. **Dataset Preparation**
   - Annotate training questions with subject categories (Algebra, Geometry, etc.)
   - Construct triplets $(x_1, x_2, x_{12})$ from distinct categories
   - Ensure 250+ triplets for stable compositionality measurement

2. **Teacher Model Selection**
   - Use a model 2–4× larger than target (e.g., 14B teacher for 1.5B student)
   - Verify teacher has reasonable baseline compositionality (nMAD < 0.5)
   - Sample K = 8 outputs per question (Section 4)

3. **Training Configuration**
   - Learning rate: Grid search over $\{1\text{e-}6, 5\text{e-}6, 5\text{e-}5\}$ (Appendix F.1)
   - Epochs: 5 (Section 5.1)
   - Batch size: 16 (Section 5.1)
   - Monitor nMAD on validation LORE-COMPO subset

4. **Evaluation Protocol**
   - Run LORE-COMPO before and after fine-tuning
   - Track both nMAD$_{C_\theta}$ and nMAD$_{\log A_\theta}$ (Figure 7b shows accuracy compositionality improves as side effect)
   - Evaluate on 2–3 standard benchmarks to verify generalization

**Reproducibility Resources**

The paper provides:

- **Code repository**: https://github.com/ASTRAL-Group/LoRe (Reproducibility Statement)
- **Project page**: https://lore-project.github.io/ (Abstract)
- **Benchmark details**: Appendix E (LORE-MONO seed questions, LORE-COMPO construction)
- **Training details**: Appendix F.1 (learning rates, batch sizes, warmup)

**Known Reproducibility Challenges**

1. **Teacher Model Availability**: DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-14B may not be available in all regions. Alternative teachers should be validated for compositionality.

2. **LORE-MONO Generation**: Python scripts for variant generation are described but not fully provided in the paper. Teams may need to reconstruct from Appendix E.1.1 examples.

3. **Compute Requirements**: Sampling 8 outputs per triplet for 3.9K pairs requires ~93.6K forward passes (Section 6.3). For 8B models at ~100 tokens/second, this is approximately 260 GPU-hours.

### 7.5 Summary: The LORE Research Agenda

LORE establishes a new paradigm for reasoning research. The key takeaways for the community:

| Aspect | Before LORE | After LORE |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| **Evaluation** | Task accuracy only | Accuracy + behavioral properties (monotonicity, compositionality) |
| **Intervention Design** | Heuristic-based | Theory-grounded (enforce laws, not just optimize loss) |
| **Model Comparison** | Benchmark leaderboards | Behavioral diagnostics (nMAD, Spearman ρ) |
| **Training Objective** | Maximize accuracy | Maximize accuracy subject to law compliance |
| **Research Questions** | "How do we improve accuracy?" | "What properties define optimal reasoning?" |

**Priority Actions for the Community:**

1. **Adopt LORE-BENCH** as a standard evaluation suite alongside MATH500 and GSM8K
2. **Report behavioral metrics** (nMAD, monotonicity correlations) in all LRM papers
3. **Extend LORE** to new domains, architectures, and model classes
4. **Develop test-time methods** for compositionality enforcement without fine-tuning
5. **Investigate safety implications** of enforcing reasoning laws

The LORE framework transforms reasoning from an empirical art into a principled science. By establishing what optimal reasoning *should* look like, it provides a compass for future research—one that points toward more efficient, reliable, and understandable Large Reasoning Models.
