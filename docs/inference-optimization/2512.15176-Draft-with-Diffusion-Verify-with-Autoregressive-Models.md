## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces DEER, a speculative decoding framework that replaces inherently sequential autoregressive drafters with a discrete-space diffusion LLM to eliminate step-wise uncertainty accumulation and the "gradual collapse of trust" between drafter and target model. Through a two-stage alignment pipeline, DEER enables one-step block drafting that achieves acceptance lengths of up to 32 tokens—far exceeding the 10 tokens of EAGLE-3—and delivers a 5.54× speedup on HumanEval with Qwen3-30B-A3B compared to EAGLE-3's 2.41×. This advancement matters because it fundamentally overcomes the structural latency bottlenecks of autoregressive decoding while preserving lossless output distribution.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of this paper, we must first establish the computational bottleneck inherent in modern Large Language Models (LLMs) and why existing solutions fail to fully resolve it. This section walks through the latency problem, the mechanics of speculative decoding, the specific failure mode of current drafters, and how DEER positions itself against prior art.

### 2.1. The Autoregressive Latency Bottleneck

Modern LLMs generate text using **autoregressive (AR) decoding**. In this paradigm, the model produces tokens sequentially: to generate token $t$, the model must have already generated tokens $1$ through $t-1$. This creates a fundamental hardware inefficiency. While modern GPUs are designed for massive parallel computation, AR decoding forces them to perform one small matrix multiplication at a time, leaving most compute units idle during memory-bound steps.

This latency becomes critical in **agentic and reasoning systems**. As noted in the Introduction (Section 1), these applications often require extended context and complex multi-step reasoning. If each step waits for the previous token to finish generating, the end-to-end response time grows linearly with sequence length, creating a poor user experience and limiting the practicality of real-time agents.

### 2.2. Speculative Decoding and the Drafter Problem

**Speculative decoding** emerged as a primary solution to this bottleneck. The core idea is lossless acceleration:
1.  A lightweight **drafter model** proposes a block of candidate tokens in parallel.
2.  The large **target model** (the actual LLM being used) verifies these tokens in a single pass.
3.  Accepted tokens are kept; rejected tokens are resampled.

Theoretically, if the drafter is accurate, the system generates multiple tokens per target model forward pass, achieving speedups proportional to the **acceptance length** ($\tau$), defined as the average number of tokens accepted per drafting cycle.

However, the paper identifies a critical flaw in how drafters are currently designed. Existing methods (e.g., EAGLE-3, Medusa) rely on **AR drafters**. Even though the target model verifies in parallel, the *drafter itself* still generates tokens left-to-right. This reintroduces the very sequential dependency speculative decoding aims to solve.

### 2.3. The "Gradual Collapse of Trust"

The central theoretical motivation for DEER is a phenomenon the authors term the **gradual collapse of trust** (Section 1). This describes how uncertainty accumulates in AR-based drafters.

*   **Mechanism:** An AR drafter conditions each new token on its *own previously generated draft tokens*, which have not yet been verified by the target model.
*   **Error Propagation:** If the drafter makes a small error at position $i$, that error becomes part of the context for position $i+1$. Because the drafter's distribution differs slightly from the target model's distribution, this discrepancy is recursively amplified.
*   **Visual Evidence:** Figure 2 (Left) illustrates this dependence-based strategy. As the draft sequence grows, the draft trajectory drifts outside the "acceptance region" of the target model.
*   **Consequence:** The target model rejects later tokens in the draft block at increasing rates. This limits the effective acceptance length ($\tau$). For instance, Figure 1 shows that even advanced AR-based methods like EAGLE-3 cap out at around $\tau \approx 3.05$ to $3.54$ tokens on larger models (Qwen3-14B/30B), severely constraining speedup potential.

In short, AR drafters suffer from **step-wise uncertainty accumulation**. The deeper into the draft block you go, the less likely the tokens are to match what the target model would have produced.

### 2.4. Limitations of Prior Approaches

The paper categorizes existing attempts to solve this efficiency problem in Section 2 (Related Work), highlighting specific gaps that DEER addresses.

#### 2.4.1. Autoregressive and Tree-based Drafting
Methods like **Medusa** (Cai et al., 2024) and **EAGLE** (Li et al., 2024a;b; 2025b) use auxiliary AR heads or manipulate hidden states to predict tree-structured continuations.
*   **Shortcoming:** While they optimize the verification step, the drafting remains inherently sequential. They cannot escape the left-to-right uncertainty accumulation described above. As noted in Section 2.1, "early uncertainties propagate and corrupt the draft chain."

#### 2.4.2. N-gram and Heuristic Drafting
Approaches like **Lookahead** (Fu et al., 2024) construct drafts via $n$-gram matching or retrieval.
*   **Shortcoming:** These methods lack global context. They rely on surface-level pattern matching rather than semantic understanding, causing acceptance rates to drop sharply on complex sequences requiring reasoning or long-range coherence (Section 2.1).

#### 2.4.3. Previous Diffusion-based Drafting
Some prior work, such as **Speculative Diffusion Decoding (SDD)** (Christopher et al., 2025), attempted to use diffusion models for drafting.
*   **Shortcoming:** SDD operates in **continuous space** and requires **multi-step denoising**.
    *   **Continuous Space:** Makes precise temperature control difficult, which is critical for matching the target AR model's sampling behavior.
    *   **Multi-step:** Introduces its own sequential latency during the drafting phase, negating some efficiency gains.
    *   **Distribution Mismatch:** Standard diffusion models are trained for global sequence generation (denoising a full sentence), not **prefix-conditioned local continuation** (continuing from a specific prompt). Naïvely using them leads to drafts that are misaligned with the target AR distribution (Section 1).

#### 2.4.4. Hybrid and Self-Drafting Architectures
Methods like **TiDAR** (Liu et al., 2025) retrain AR models to jointly perform diffusion-style generation.
*   **Shortcoming:** This requires expensive dual-objective training that can induce conflict, potentially degrading the base model's performance. Other self-drafting methods (e.g., **SSDD**) exploit intermediate noisy states which are often unaligned with the final autoregressive objective (Section 2.2).

### 2.5. DEER's Positioning: Discrete Diffusion with Alignment

DEER positions itself as the first framework to exclusively use a **discrete-space diffusion LLM (dLLM)** as the drafter, specifically engineered to overcome the structural limits of AR drafters without incurring the costs of hybrid retraining.

*   **Parallelism:** Unlike AR drafters, a dLLM can theoretically generate an entire sequence of tokens in a single denoising process. This eliminates the left-to-right dependency during drafting (Section 1).
*   **Independence:** In DEER's block-wise formulation, the proposal at position $i$ is independent of previously drafted tokens within the same block ($q_\theta(\hat{y}_i | x_{1:j}, \hat{y}_{1:i-1}) = q_\theta(\hat{y}_i | x_{1:j})$). This prevents the "collapse of trust" because early draft errors do not condition later draft tokens (Section 3.2).
*   **Alignment:** Recognizing that pretrained dLLMs do not naturally match AR continuation behavior, DEER introduces a specialized **Diffusion-to-AR Alignment** pipeline. This distinguishes it from prior diffusion attempts (like SDD) by ensuring the drafter is explicitly trained for **prefix-conditioned continuation** rather than global generation (Section 3.1).

By combining the parallel generation capabilities of diffusion models with a rigorous alignment strategy, DEER aims to push acceptance lengths beyond the ~10 token barrier of AR drafters (achieving up to 32 tokens, as shown in Table 4) while maintaining the lossless guarantee required for production LLM deployment.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

DEER is a speculative decoding system that replaces the traditional autoregressive draft model with a diffusion-based language model capable of generating entire token blocks in a single parallel step. It solves the uncertainty accumulation problem inherent in sequential drafters by training the diffusion model through a two-stage alignment pipeline that teaches it to mimic prefix-conditioned continuation behavior while maintaining the parallel generation advantages of diffusion architectures.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The DEER system consists of three major components connected in a training-then-inference pipeline. First, the **Diffusion Drafter** (a 0.5B-parameter discrete-space dLLM) receives a text prefix and generates a block of candidate tokens in parallel through a single denoising step. Second, the **Target AR Verifier** (the actual LLM being accelerated, ranging from Qwen3-4B to Qwen3-30B-A3B) receives the same prefix plus draft tokens and performs token-by-token acceptance verification using standard speculative decoding rejection sampling. Third, the **D2A Alignment Pipeline** (Diffusion-to-Autoregressive) sits between training and inference, transforming a pretrained diffusion model into a prefix-conditioned drafter through Stage 1 (AR-Style Continuation Distillation) and Stage 2 (Scribe Refinement). Information flows from the training corpus through the alignment pipeline to produce the aligned drafter, which then operates at inference time alongside the frozen target model to produce accelerated output.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the core mathematical formulation of speculative decoding to establish what DEER must preserve (lossless output distribution) while accelerating.
- We then explain the diffusion-to-AR alignment pipeline in detail, covering both training stages and their distinct objectives.
- Next, we walk through the inference procedure step-by-step, including the acceptance mechanism and how block-wise drafting differs from token-wise drafting.
- We examine the key hyperparameters and design choices, including the exponential weighting factor $\alpha$ and block size selection.
- Finally, we address the theoretical guarantee of losslessness and why DEER's independence assumption prevents uncertainty accumulation.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a systems paper that introduces a novel speculative decoding framework, and its core idea is that discrete-space diffusion language models can serve as superior drafters compared to autoregressive models because they generate tokens in parallel without conditioning on their own unverified outputs.

#### 3.4.1. Foundational Mechanics: Speculative Decoding with Diffusion Drafters

Speculative decoding operates on a simple principle: if a lightweight model can accurately predict what a larger model would generate, the larger model can verify multiple predictions in a single forward pass rather than generating each token sequentially. DEER instantiates this principle with a diffusion drafter $q_\theta$ and an autoregressive target $p_{AR}$, where the drafter proposes a block of $k$ tokens simultaneously rather than one at a time.

The key mathematical requirement for any speculative decoding method is **losslessness**: the final output distribution must exactly match sampling directly from the target model. For each token position $j+i$ in a draft block, DEER computes an acceptance probability using the standard speculative decoding formula:

$$\alpha_i = \min\left(1, \frac{p_{AR}(\hat{y}_{j+i} | x_{1:j+i-1})}{q_\theta(\hat{y}_{j+i} | x_{1:j})}\right)$$

This equation (Eq. 5 in Section 3.2) compares the target model's probability for the draft token against the drafter's probability for the same token. If a uniform random sample $u \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ satisfies $u \leq \alpha_i$, the draft token is accepted; otherwise, it is rejected and resampled from a residual distribution. The residual distribution ensures that even rejected tokens contribute to maintaining the exact target distribution, as formally proven in Appendix E (Theorem E.2).

What makes DEER fundamentally different from autoregressive drafters lies in the conditioning structure of the drafter's probability distribution. For an autoregressive drafter, the probability of token $i$ depends on all previously drafted tokens: $q_{AR}(\hat{y}_i | x_{1:j}, \hat{y}_{1:i-1})$. This means any error at position 1 propagates to position 2, then to position 3, and so on. In contrast, DEER's diffusion drafter uses block-wise masked conditioning where $q_\theta(\hat{y}_i | x_{1:j}, \hat{y}_{1:i-1}) = q_\theta(\hat{y}_i | x_{1:j})$. The proposal at position $i$ is independent of previously drafted tokens within the same block, depending only on the verified prefix $x_{1:j}$. This independence is the mechanism that prevents uncertainty accumulation, as explained in Section 3.2 under "Uncertainty accumulation vs. stable block proposals."

#### 3.4.2. The D2A Alignment Pipeline: Stage 1 (AR-Style Continuation Distillation)

A pretrained diffusion language model is not immediately usable as a speculative decoder because it is trained for **global sequence generation** (denoising an entire corrupted sentence) rather than **prefix-conditioned local continuation** (continuing from a specific prompt). This distribution mismatch would cause the drafter to produce tokens that do not align with what the target AR model expects, leading to low acceptance rates. DEER resolves this through a two-stage training pipeline called Diffusion-to-Autoregressive (D2A) Alignment, illustrated in Figure 3.

Stage 1 adapts the diffusion model to behave like an autoregressive continuation model. The training procedure works as follows: given a teacher-generated answer $A = \{a_{1:L}\}$ from the target AR model, the training pipeline randomly truncates the answer at some point and appends a special **SEP token** to mark the continuation boundary. The diffusion model observes a noisy version $x_t$ of the tokens and is trained to denoise only the masked continuation portion (tokens after the prefix), not the entire sequence.

The Stage 1 loss function is defined as:

$$L_{\text{Distill}} = -\mathbb{E}_{t,x_0,x_t}\left[\frac{1}{t}\sum_{i=l_q}^{L-1} 1[x_t^i = \text{M}] r_i\right]$$

where $r_i = \log p_\theta(x_0^i | x_t)$ (Eq. 1 and Eq. 2 in Section 3.1.1). Here, $l_q$ represents the prefix/question length, $L$ is the full length of the teacher-generated answer, M denotes the mask token, and $t$ is the diffusion timestep. The indicator function $1[x_t^i = \text{M}]$ ensures the model is only penalized for errors on masked positions, not on the observed prefix.

This stage fundamentally changes how the diffusion model operates. Instead of learning to reconstruct arbitrary corrupted spans anywhere in a sequence, it learns to predict only the future given a fixed past. This aligns the drafter's behavior with the requirements of speculative decoding, where the prefix is known and verified, and only the continuation needs to be proposed. The training uses the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$ for 1 epoch over the code training corpus (for code generation tasks) or 5 epochs for mathematical reasoning tasks (Appendix A).

#### 3.4.3. The D2A Alignment Pipeline: Stage 2 (Scribe Refinement)

While Stage 1 enables causal continuation, speculative acceptance is particularly sensitive to the tokens that appear **immediately after the prefix**. These early tokens in the draft block are the first to be verified by the target model, and errors here cause the entire block to be rejected sooner. Stage 2, called **Scribe Refinement** (or Prefix-Conditioned Accuracy Refinement), specifically enhances accuracy in this critical region through a masked-span curriculum with position-dependent weighting.

The Stage 2 training procedure differs from Stage 1 in two key ways. First, instead of masking the entire suffix after the prefix, Stage 2 masks only the last $R$ tokens of the answer, where $R$ is sampled uniformly from the range 1 to 96: $R \sim \text{Uniform}(1, 96)$. This focuses the model's capacity on learning to predict shorter continuations with higher precision. Second, and more importantly, tokens closer to the prefix boundary are emphasized with exponentially increasing weights during loss computation.

The exponential weighting scheme is defined as:

$$w_i = \alpha^{R-i}, \quad i = 1, \ldots, R$$

(Eq. 3 in Section 3.1.2). Here, $i$ indexes positions within the masked region, with $i=1$ being closest to the prefix and $i=R$ being furthest. The scaling factor $\alpha$ controls how strongly the loss emphasizes recent tokens. When $\alpha > 1$, tokens near the prefix boundary (small $i$) receive higher weights because $R-i$ is larger for those positions.

The Stage 2 refinement loss incorporates these weights:

$$L_{\text{Refine}} = -\mathbb{E}_{t,x_0,x_t}\left[\frac{1}{t}\sum_{i=l_q}^{L-1} w_i 1[x_t^i = \text{M}] r_i\right]$$

(Eq. 4 in Section 3.1.2). This loss function is identical to Stage 1 except for the inclusion of $w_i$, which modulates the contribution of each masked position to the total loss.

The choice of $\alpha$ is critical and surprisingly sensitive. Figure 6 in Section 4.3.2 shows the training loss trajectories for different $\alpha$ values. When $\alpha = 1.01$, optimization is stable and the loss decreases smoothly. Increasing $\alpha$ to 1.02 yields noticeably noisier training curves with slight upward drift. Setting $\alpha = 1.05$ leads to early divergence. This narrow stability window exists because overly aggressive weighting amplifies gradients near the masked boundary, destabilizing optimization. Within the stable regime ($\alpha \approx 1.01$), the refinement stage consistently improves suffix alignment without requiring additional data or extended training. Stage 2 uses AdamW with a learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-5}$ for code tasks or $1 \times 10^{-4}$ for math tasks, training for 1 additional epoch on a subset of 100k examples (Appendix A).

The ablation study in Table 3 (Section 4.3.1) demonstrates the concrete impact of Stage 2. On HumanEval with Qwen3-30B-A3B, enabling Stage 2 increases the average acceptance length from 5.38 to 6.58 tokens (a 22% improvement). The gap grows with benchmark difficulty, with the largest improvements on HumanEval (1.20 tokens) and LiveCodeBench (1.16 tokens). This confirms that Stage 2 encourages the dLLM to produce suffixes more tightly aligned with the AR teacher, especially in settings requiring complex or long-range structure.

#### 3.4.4. Inference Procedure: Block-Wise Draft-Then-Verify

At inference time, DEER alternates between parallel block proposals from the dLLM and token-wise validation by the AR model. Algorithm 1 in Section 3.2 provides the complete inference routine, which we now walk through step-by-step.

Given a current verified prefix $x_{1:j}$, the dLLM proposes a block of $k$ tokens in parallel: $\hat{y}_{j+1:j+k} \sim q_\theta(\cdot | x_{1:j})$. This is a **single-step** generation process, meaning the diffusion model performs one denoising operation to produce all $k$ tokens simultaneously. This contrasts with multi-step diffusion methods like SDD (Christopher et al., 2025), which require iterative denoising and introduce their own sequential latency.

For each token $i$ in the draft block (from $i=1$ to $k$), the system computes the acceptance probability $\alpha_i$ using Eq. 5 as described earlier. A uniform random sample $u \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ is drawn, and if $u \leq \alpha_i$, the draft token $\hat{y}_{j+i}$ is accepted and appended to the prefix. If $u > \alpha_i$, the draft token is rejected and replaced by a sample from the residual distribution:

$$\hat{y}_{j+i} \propto \max\left(0, p_{AR}(\cdot | x_{1:j+i-1}) - q_\theta(\cdot | x_{1:j})\right)$$

(Eq. 6 in Section 3.2). This residual sampling ensures that even when the draft is rejected, the token selection process maintains the exact target distribution $p_{AR}$.

After processing all $k$ tokens (or encountering an EOS token), the prefix length $j$ is updated to the new sequence length, and the process repeats. Most computation is shifted to the parallelizable drafting step, while the AR model is used only for lightweight verification. This is the source of DEER's speedup: if the drafter is accurate, multiple tokens are generated per AR forward pass.

The block size $k$ is a configurable hyperparameter that trades off between drafting efficiency and acceptance rate. Figure 8 in Appendix B.2 shows how average acceptance length scales with block size across different model scales. For Qwen3-8B, increasing block size from 4 to 32 tokens improves acceptance length gradually. For Qwen3-30B-A3B, the improvement is more pronounced, with acceptance length increasing from approximately 4.5 to over 6.5 tokens. This suggests that larger backbone models have stronger output determinism, enabling the dLLM to more effectively fit deterministic token patterns. The maximum acceptance length achieved by DEER is 32 tokens across all tested models (Table 4, Section 4.2.2), far exceeding EAGLE-3's limit of 7-8 tokens.

#### 3.4.5. Design Choices and Alternative Considerations

Several design choices in DEER warrant explicit explanation, as they distinguish it from alternative approaches in the literature.

**Discrete Space vs. Continuous Space:** DEER operates in discrete token space rather than continuous embedding space. Prior diffusion-based drafting methods like SDD use continuous-space diffusion, which prohibits precise temperature control and introduces step-wise drift from the AR verifier (Section 2.1). Discrete-space diffusion allows DEER to match the target model's sampling behavior exactly, which is critical for maintaining high acceptance rates. The discrete formulation also enables direct compatibility with standard tokenizers and vocabulary without requiring embedding space projections.

**One-Step vs. Multi-Step Denoising:** DEER uses single-step decoding to generate draft blocks, whereas methods like SDD require multiple denoising iterations. Multi-step denoising introduces sequential latency during the drafting phase itself, partially negating the parallelism benefits. DEER's one-step approach is enabled by the D2A alignment pipeline, which trains the diffusion model to produce high-quality outputs in a single pass. This is a critical efficiency consideration: if drafting takes multiple steps, the speedup from parallel verification is reduced by the drafting overhead.

**Two-Stage vs. Single-Stage Training:** The D2A pipeline uses two distinct training stages rather than a single unified objective. Stage 1 resolves the fundamental distribution mismatch for prefix-conditioned continuation, while Stage 2 provides fine-grained local accuracy through exponentially weighted suffix masking. This separation of concerns allows each stage to optimize for a specific capability. A single-stage approach would need to balance these objectives simultaneously, potentially compromising either global continuation ability or local boundary accuracy. The ablation in Table 3 confirms that Stage 2 provides measurable improvements beyond Stage 1 alone.

**Modular Drafter vs. Hybrid Architecture:** DEER employs a modular design with a dedicated, lightweight dLLM drafter (0.5B parameters) separate from the target AR model. This contrasts with hybrid approaches like TiDAR (Liu et al., 2025), which retrain AR models to jointly perform diffusion-style generation. Hybrid training is computationally expensive and often induces objective conflicts that degrade base model performance (Section 2.2). DEER's modular approach avoids expensive retraining of the target LLM and preserves its original capabilities without modification. Table 7 in Appendix C shows that DEER's drafter size (470M parameters) is comparable to EAGLE-3's AR draft heads (140M-610M depending on target model), confirming that improvements come from the drafting mechanism rather than simply using a larger drafter.

**KV Cache Considerations:** The current DEER implementation does not leverage KV caching for the diffusion drafter, as mature frameworks for efficient dLLM+KV-cache deployment are not yet widely available (Section 4.4, Appendix F). Both the baseline autoregressive decoding and DEER are run without KV-cache in batch inference experiments, so the comparison reflects raw model throughput. However, recent work like Fast-dLLM (Wu et al., 2025) and dInfer (Ma et al., 2025) has begun exploring KV caching for diffusion LLMs. Once such techniques are integrated into mainstream inference frameworks like vLLM or SGLang, DEER is expected to exhibit substantial additional advantages in batched inference scenarios (Appendix F).

#### 3.4.6. Theoretical Guarantee: Losslessness Proof

DEER provides a formal guarantee that its output distribution exactly matches the target AR model's distribution, making it **lossless** in the same sense as standard speculative decoding. Appendix E presents a three-part proof establishing this property.

The proof begins with Lemma E.1 (One-step lossless speculative sampling), which shows that for any proposal distribution $P(x)$ satisfying the support condition ($p(x) > 0 \Rightarrow P(x) > 0$), the draft-then-verify step with acceptance probability $\alpha(\cdot)$ and residual distribution $p_{\text{res}}(\cdot)$ produces exactly the target distribution $p(\cdot)$ at each position. The acceptance probability is defined as $\alpha(Y) = \min(1, p(Y)/P(Y))$, which matches Eq. 5 used in DEER.

The proof then instantiates this lemma with DEER's specific proposal distribution. The target conditional is $p(x) = p_{AR}(x_{j+i} = x | x_{1:j+i-1})$, while the DEER drafter proposes according to $P(x) = q_\theta(x_{j+i} = x | x_{1:j})$. The D2A alignment training ensures that whenever $p_{AR}(x_{j+i} = x | x_{1:j+i-1}) > 0$, we also have $q_\theta(x_{j+i} = x | x_{1:j}) > 0$, satisfying the support condition (Section E.2).

Finally, Theorem E.2 extends the one-step result to the whole generated sequence through induction on token position $t$. The base case ($t=1$) holds by construction, and the inductive step uses Lemma E.1 to show that conditioned on any realized prefix $x_{1:t-1}$, the conditional distribution of the next token matches the AR model exactly. Therefore, for any finite sequence $x_{1:T}$:

$$P_{\text{DEER}}(x_{1:T}) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} p_{AR}(x_t | x_{1:t-1})$$

This establishes that DEER produces exactly the same joint distribution over outputs as direct autoregressive sampling from $p_{AR}$, despite using a fundamentally different drafting mechanism.

#### 3.4.7. Emergent Capability: Reliable Block Regeneration

An interesting emergent behavior observed in dLLMs trained with DEER is what the authors term **reliable block regeneration** (Section 1, Section 4.5). This refers to the dLLM's ability to repeatedly accept partially masked suffixes and regenerate them in a coherent manner, even without architectural modifications such as padding tokens or altered attention patterns.

Figure 5 in Section 4.5 illustrates this capability with a quicksort generation example. Different colors represent tokens produced at successive denoising iterations, showing that the dLLM can extend partial code blocks without requiring a full-sentence prompt. The model treats block-level continuation as a natural generation mode, incrementally refining and extending the partial block across iterations.

This capability emerges from the D2A alignment pipeline rather than being explicitly architected. Stage 1's prefix-conditioned training teaches the model to generate coherent continuations from arbitrary prefixes, while Stage 2's refinement ensures stability near the verification boundary. Together, these stages produce a dLLM that can perform genuinely blockwise generation from incrementally masked suffixes. This is distinct from standard diffusion generation, which typically denoises a fixed-length sequence in one pass. The reliable block regeneration capability suggests that DEER-trained dLLMs may have applications beyond speculative decoding, such as iterative code refinement or interactive completion systems.

#### 3.4.8. Training Configuration Summary

For completeness, we summarize all key training configurations mentioned throughout the paper. The draft model for code tasks uses a 0.5B-parameter diffusion drafter obtained by modifying the open-dCoder checkpoint into a discrete diffusion language model (Appendix A). Stage 1 training uses AdamW optimizer with learning rate $1 \times 10^{-4}$ for 1 epoch over the code training corpus (OpenCodeInstruct dataset). Stage 2 continues training the same drafter with AdamW, learning rate $5 \times 10^{-5}$, and 1 epoch on a subset of 100k examples.

For mathematical reasoning tasks, the draft model is initialized from Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct, converted into a diffusion language model, and trained on the UltraChat dataset for 40 epochs total. Stage 1 applies AR-style continuation distillation with AdamW, learning rate $1 \times 10^{-4}$, for 5 epochs. Stage 2 performs refinement with AdamW, learning rate $1 \times 10^{-4}$, for 1 additional epoch.

The exponential weighting factor $\alpha$ in Stage 2 is set to 1.01 based on the sensitivity analysis in Figure 6. Block sizes tested range from 4 to 32 tokens, with larger block sizes generally yielding longer acceptance lengths but with diminishing returns (Figure 8). The maximum acceptance length achieved is 32 tokens across all tested models (Table 4).

All experiments are conducted on a cluster equipped with eight NVIDIA A100 GPUs with 80 GB of memory each (Appendix A). Training time comparisons in Table 8 (Appendix D) show that DEER requires approximately 240 GPU hours for Qwen3-8B and Qwen3-14B backbones, which is comparable to or faster than competing methods like EAGLE-3 (696 GPU hours for 8B, 1440 GPU hours for 14B).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

DEER represents a fundamental shift in speculative decoding architecture rather than a mere incremental optimization of existing drafters. While prior work focused on refining autoregressive (AR) draft heads or tree structures, DEER identifies the sequential dependency of the drafter itself as the primary bottleneck. The following insights distinguish DEER's contributions from prior art, separating structural innovations from performance tuning.

### 4.1. Structural Decoupling of Draft Dependencies

The most significant theoretical innovation in DEER is the elimination of **step-wise uncertainty accumulation** within the draft block. As detailed in Section 2.3, AR-based drafters (like EAGLE-3) suffer from a "gradual collapse of trust" because each draft token conditions on previous unverified draft tokens. Mathematically, an AR drafter computes $q_{AR}(\hat{y}_i | x_{1:j}, \hat{y}_{1:i-1})$, meaning errors compound recursively.

DEER fundamentally breaks this chain by leveraging the independence property of diffusion masking. The dLLM drafter computes proposals such that $q_\theta(\hat{y}_i | x_{1:j}, \hat{y}_{1:i-1}) = q_\theta(\hat{y}_i | x_{1:j})$. This means the probability of generating token $i$ depends *only* on the verified prefix, not on the potentially erroneous draft tokens preceding it within the same block.

*   **Significance:** This structural change is why DEER achieves acceptance lengths ($\tau$) of up to **32 tokens** (Table 4, Section 4.2.2), whereas EAGLE-3 caps at **7–8 tokens**. The improvement is not due to a "smarter" model but a **stable** one. The draft quality does not degrade as the block depth increases, allowing the verifier to accept longer contiguous sequences before encountering a mismatch.
*   **Distinction:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in drafting mechanics. Prior methods like Medusa or Hydra optimize *how* AR heads predict, but they cannot escape the left-to-right dependency inherent to autoregression. DEER changes the probabilistic modeling class entirely.

### 4.2. Boundary-Specific Alignment via Exponential Weighting

A critical insight driving DEER's success is the recognition that speculative decoding failure is not uniform across a draft block; it is concentrated at the **verification boundary**. Standard fine-tuning treats all tokens equally, but in speculative decoding, the first few tokens after the prefix determine whether the rest of the block is even evaluated.

DEER addresses this through **Stage 2: Scribe Refinement** (Section 3.1.2), which employs an exponentially decaying loss weight $w_i = \alpha^{R-i}$. This design choice prioritizes accuracy for tokens closest to the prefix (where $i$ is small and weight is high).

*   **Significance:** The ablation study in Table 3 (Section 4.3.1) shows that enabling Stage 2 increases acceptance length on HumanEval from **5.38 to 6.58 tokens** (a 22% gain). This confirms that global alignment (Stage 1) is insufficient; the model must be specifically optimized for the local continuation boundary.
*   **Sensitivity Insight:** The effectiveness of this approach hinges on a narrow hyperparameter window. Figure 6 (Section 4.3.2) reveals that the scaling factor $\alpha$ must be kept near **1.01**. Increasing $\alpha$ to 1.05 causes training divergence due to gradient amplification near the mask boundary. This highlights that alignment for speculative decoding is a delicate optimization problem, not solvable by brute-force loss weighting.
*   **Distinction:** This is a **novel training strategy** specific to speculative decoding. Prior alignment methods (e.g., standard distillation) do not account for the asymmetric importance of tokens within a draft block relative to the verifier's acceptance mechanism.

### 4.3. Discrete One-Step Drafting vs. Continuous Multi-Step

DEER clarifies a common misconception in diffusion-based acceleration: that diffusion drafting inherently requires multiple denoising steps or continuous space operations. Prior work like Speculative Diffusion Decoding (SDD) (Christopher et al., 2025) utilized continuous-space, multi-step denoising, which introduced two penalties:
1.  **Latency:** Multiple drafting steps reduce the net speedup.
2.  **Distribution Mismatch:** Continuous spaces make precise temperature control difficult, hindering alignment with the discrete AR target.

DEER demonstrates that **discrete-space, one-step decoding** is viable for drafting when paired with the D2A alignment pipeline (Section 3.1).

*   **Significance:** By reducing drafting to a single forward pass (Section 3.2), DEER maximizes the parallelism benefit. The speedup on HumanEval with Qwen3-30B-A3B reaches **5.54×** (Figure 1), compared to **2.41×** for EAGLE-3. This magnitude of gain is only possible because the drafting overhead is minimized to a single step while maintaining high acceptance rates.
*   **Distinction:** This is a **system-level innovation**. It proves that diffusion models do not need to be slow, iterative processes to be effective drafters. The shift to discrete space ensures compatibility with standard tokenizers and verification logic without requiring complex embedding-space projections.

### 4.4. Performance Gains Are Mechanism-Driven, Not Scale-Driven

A potential critique of acceleration methods is that performance gains might simply stem from using a larger or more expensive drafter model. DEER explicitly addresses this in Appendix C (Table 7), comparing drafter parameter counts against EAGLE-3.

*   **Evidence:** For the Qwen3-14B target, EAGLE-3 uses a **610M** parameter drafter, while DEER uses a **470M** parameter dLLM. Despite using a *smaller* drafter, DEER achieves significantly higher acceptance lengths (Table 2, Section 4.2.1). For the 30B target, DEER's drafter (470M) is larger than EAGLE-3's (140M), but the ratio of drafter-to-target parameters remains small, and the acceptance length gap (6.58 vs 3.21 on HumanEval) far exceeds what parameter scaling alone would predict.
*   **Significance:** This confirms that the **independence assumption** and **alignment pipeline** are the primary drivers of performance, not model capacity. It suggests DEER's approach is scalable to even larger target models without requiring proportionally larger drafters.
*   **Distinction:** This is a **critical validation** of the method's efficiency. It distinguishes DEER from approaches that might achieve better acceptance simply by burning more FLOPs on a heavier drafter network.

### 4.5. Emergent Capability: Reliable Block Regeneration

Beyond acceleration, DEER uncovers an emergent generative capability termed **reliable block regeneration** (Section 4.5). Standard diffusion models typically denoise a fixed-length sequence in one pass. However, DEER-trained dLLMs learn to accept partially masked suffixes and regenerate them coherently across iterations.

*   **Evidence:** Figure 5 (Section 4.5) illustrates a quicksort generation task where the model extends incomplete code blocks without full-sentence prompts. Different colors denote tokens produced at successive denoising iterations, showing incremental refinement.
*   **Significance:** This suggests the D2A alignment pipeline imbues the dLLM with a robust understanding of **prefix-conditioned continuity** that persists beyond the specific task of speculative decoding. While the paper focuses on acceleration, this capability hints at broader applications for dLLMs in interactive coding assistants or iterative refinement tasks where partial regeneration is valuable.
*   **Distinction:** This is a **new capability** rather than an optimization. It demonstrates that aligning diffusion models for AR-style tasks can unlock behaviors not present in standard pretrained dLLMs, expanding the utility of diffusion architectures in language modeling.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section evaluates whether DEER's theoretical advantages—specifically the elimination of step-wise uncertainty accumulation—translate into measurable performance gains in practice. We examine the experimental methodology, dissect the primary quantitative results, analyze ablation studies that validate the design choices, and assess the framework's robustness across different tasks and scales.

### 5.1. Evaluation Methodology

To rigorously test DEER, the authors constructed a comprehensive evaluation suite covering code generation, mathematical reasoning, and system-level scalability.

*   **Datasets:**
    *   **Code Generation:** The primary focus. Training uses the `OpenCodeInstruct` dataset. Evaluation spans four benchmarks: `HumanEval`, `MBPP`, `LiveCodeBench`, and the Python subset of `CodeAlpaca` (Section 4.1).
    *   **Mathematical Reasoning:** Used to test generalization beyond code. Training uses `UltraChat` and `ShareGPT`. Evaluation spans `GSM8K`, `Math500`, and `Minerva Math` (Section 4.1, Section 4.6).
*   **Target Models:** The framework is tested across a range of model scales to ensure scalability: `Qwen3-4B`, `Qwen3-8B`, `Qwen3-14B`, and `Qwen3-30B-A3B` (Section 4.1).
*   **Draft Model:** A consistent `0.5B`-parameter discrete diffusion model is used as the drafter across all target scales. For code, it is based on `open-dCoder`; for math, it is a converted `Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct` (Section 4.1, Appendix A).
*   **Baselines:** DEER is compared against state-of-the-art speculative decoding methods with publicly available code: `EAGLE-3`, `Medusa`, and `Hydra` (Section 4.1).
*   **Metrics:**
    *   **Speedup Ratio:** The end-to-end tokens-per-second gain compared to standard autoregressive decoding.
    *   **Average Acceptance Length ($\tau$):** The average number of tokens accepted per drafting cycle. This is the direct proxy for how well the drafter aligns with the target (Section 4.1).
*   **Hardware:** Experiments run on a cluster with eight `NVIDIA A100 GPUs (80 GB)` (Appendix A).
*   **KV Cache:** Main efficiency results (Tables 1 & 2) report performance **with KV cache** enabled. However, batch scaling experiments (Table 5) note that mature dLLM KV cache integration is still emerging, so those specific comparisons run without KV cache to ensure a fair raw throughput baseline (Section 4.4, Appendix F).

### 5.2. Primary Performance Results: Code Generation

The core claim of DEER is that diffusion drafting eliminates the "gradual collapse of trust," allowing for significantly longer acceptance lengths ($\tau$) and higher speedups. The results strongly support this.

#### 5.2.1. Acceptance Length and Speedup
Table 2 (Temperature=0) provides the most direct comparison of efficiency. Across all model scales, DEER outperforms EAGLE-3 in both speedup and $\tau$.

*   **Qwen3-30B-A3B on HumanEval:** This is the flagship result. DEER achieves a **5.54× speedup** with an acceptance length of **$\tau = 6.58$**. In contrast, EAGLE-3 achieves only **2.41× speedup** with **$\tau = 3.21$**.
    > "On HumanEval with Qwen3-30B-A3B, DEER attains a 5.54× speedup, while EAGLE-3 achieves only 2.41×." (Abstract)
*   **Consistency Across Scales:** The advantage holds for smaller models too. On Qwen3-4B, DEER reaches **$\tau = 4.61$** (2.77× speedup) versus EAGLE-3's **$\tau = 3.31$** (2.40× speedup) (Figure 1).
*   **Temperature Robustness:** At Temperature=0.6 (Table 1), where sampling is more stochastic, DEER still maintains a lead. For Qwen3-30B-A3B, DEER achieves **$\tau = 4.45$** (3.62× speedup) compared to EAGLE-3's **$\tau = 2.40$** (2.01× speedup).

**Why this matters:** The acceptance length $\tau$ is the bottleneck for speculative decoding. Standard AR drafters typically cap out around 7–8 tokens because errors accumulate. DEER pushing $\tau$ to >6 on average (and much higher in peak cases) directly validates the hypothesis that block-wise independence prevents uncertainty accumulation.

#### 5.2.2. Distribution of Accepted Lengths
Figure 4 and Table 4 provide deeper insight into *how* DEER achieves these averages.

*   **Maximum Acceptance:** Table 4 shows DEER consistently reaches a maximum acceptance length of **32 tokens** across all models. EAGLE-3 is capped at **7–8 tokens**.
    > "DEER achieves acceptance lengths of up to 32 tokens, far surpassing the 10 tokens achieved by EAGLE-3." (Abstract)
*   **Long-Block Probability:** Figure 4 shows the proportion of drafts accepted that are $\ge 8$ tokens. For Qwen3-30B-A3B, **16.5%** of DEER's drafts are this long, compared to a negligible amount for AR-based methods. This "long-block resurgence" (Appendix B.1) confirms that later tokens in DEER's drafts are not penalized by earlier errors, as they would be in an AR drafter.

### 5.3. Ablation Studies and Sensitivity

The paper includes critical ablations to verify that the performance gains come from the proposed mechanisms (D2A alignment) rather than incidental factors.

#### 5.3.1. Impact of Stage II (Scribe Refinement)
Stage II of the training pipeline applies exponential weighting to tokens near the prefix boundary. Table 3 isolates its impact on Qwen3-30B-A3B.

*   **Quantitative Gain:** Enabling Stage II increases $\tau$ on HumanEval from **5.38 to 6.58** (+22%). On LiveCodeBench, it increases from **3.87 to 5.03** (+30%).
*   **Interpretation:** This confirms that global alignment (Stage I) is insufficient. The verifier is most sensitive to the first few tokens of the draft; optimizing specifically for this boundary (Stage II) is necessary to maximize acceptance.

#### 5.3.2. Hyperparameter Sensitivity ($\alpha$)
The exponential weighting factor $\alpha$ in Stage II is a critical hyperparameter. Figure 6 shows the training loss stability for different values.

*   **Stability Window:** $\alpha = 1.01$ yields stable convergence. $\alpha = 1.02$ introduces noise, and $\alpha = 1.05$ causes divergence.
*   **Lesson:** This highlights a subtle design constraint. While weighting near-boundary tokens is beneficial, aggressive weighting destabilizes the diffusion training dynamics. The success of DEER relies on tuning this within a narrow regime.

#### 5.3.3. Drafter Size Fairness
A common critique of acceleration methods is that gains might simply come from using a larger drafter model. Appendix C (Table 7) addresses this.

*   **Parameter Count:** DEER uses a fixed **470M** parameter drafter. EAGLE-3 uses **400M** (for 8B target), **610M** (for 14B target), and **140M** (for 30B target).
*   **Conclusion:** On the 14B target, EAGLE-3 uses a *larger* drafter (610M vs 470M) yet performs worse. This confirms DEER's gains are due to the **diffusion drafting mechanism**, not model capacity.

### 5.4. Generalization and System Scalability

DEER is tested beyond standard code generation to ensure the approach is robust.

#### 5.4.1. Mathematical Reasoning
Section 4.6 evaluates DEER on math benchmarks (GSM8K, Math500) using a draft model converted from `Qwen2.5-0.5B`.

*   **Results:** Despite the draft model being "partially converged" (Section 4.6), DEER outperforms EAGLE-3. On Math500, speedup improves from **1.89× (EAGLE-3)** to **2.12× (DEER)**.
*   **Significance:** This demonstrates that DEER's one-step drafting mechanism produces stable proposals even when the underlying dLLM is not perfectly optimized for the domain. It suggests the framework is resilient to draft model imperfections.

#### 5.4.2. Batch Inference Scalability
Table 5 measures throughput (tokens/s) as batch size increases from 2 to 16 (without KV cache for fairness).

*   **Scaling:** At Batch Size 8, DEER reaches **159.87 tokens/s**, nearly **4×** the baseline AR throughput of **38.35 tokens/s**.
*   **Efficiency:** The speedup grows with batch size because the parallel drafting step better exploits GPU parallelism when processing multiple sequences simultaneously. Fixed overheads become negligible at larger batches (Section 4.4).

#### 5.4.3. Training Cost
Appendix D (Table 8) compares the training cost (GPU hours) to produce the drafters.

*   **Efficiency:** For Qwen3-14B, DEER requires **240 GPU hours**, whereas EAGLE-3 requires **1440 GPU hours**. Medusa and Hydra encountered Out-Of-Memory (OOM) errors at this scale under default configs.
*   **Implication:** DEER is not only faster at inference but also cheaper to train. The two-stage alignment on a fixed 0.5B model is more efficient than training larger AR draft heads for specific target scales.

### 5.5. Critical Assessment

The experimental evidence convincingly supports the paper's core claims, though some practical constraints remain.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   **Mechanism Validation:** The drastic increase in maximum acceptance length (32 vs 8 tokens) directly validates the theoretical claim that diffusion drafting avoids uncertainty accumulation.
    *   **Lossless Guarantee:** Appendix E provides a formal proof that the output distribution matches the target AR model exactly, ensuring no quality degradation.
    *   **Efficiency:** Both inference speedup (5.54×) and training cost (240 vs 1440 GPU hours) favor DEER.

*   **Limitations and Conditions:**
    *   **KV Cache Maturity:** Appendix F notes that mature KV cache support for dLLMs is still emerging. While main results claim KV cache usage, batch scaling experiments were run without it. Full system integration (e.g., into vLLM) is required to realize the full potential in production environments.
    *   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The narrow stability window for $\alpha$ (Figure 6) suggests that deploying DEER on new model families may require careful tuning of the refinement stage.
    *   **Draft Model Capacity:** While the 0.5B drafter is efficient, the paper does not extensively explore whether scaling the drafter itself (e.g., to 1B or 3B) would yield diminishing returns or further gains. The current results suggest the mechanism is the bottleneck, not the drafter size, but this is an open question for future work.

In summary, the experiments demonstrate that DEER is not merely an incremental improvement but a structural advancement in speculative decoding. By shifting from autoregressive to diffusion drafting, it unlocks acceptance lengths previously unattainable, translating directly into superior end-to-end latency without compromising output quality.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While DEER demonstrates substantial improvements in acceptance length and end-to-end speedup, adopting a diffusion-based drafter introduces distinct system constraints and dependencies that differ from traditional autoregressive (AR) speculative decoding. Understanding these trade-offs is critical for practitioners considering deployment, as the theoretical benefits of parallel drafting must be weighed against infrastructure maturity and training sensitivity.

### 6.1. Infrastructure Maturity and KV Cache Support

The most significant practical barrier to deploying DEER in production environments is the current immaturity of inference infrastructure for diffusion language models (dLLMs).

*   **KV Cache Limitations:** Efficient LLM serving relies heavily on **KV caching** to avoid recomputing attention keys and values for previously generated tokens. As explicitly stated in Appendix F, "At present, the community lacks mature support for diffusion language models (DLLMs) with KV caching in mainstream inference frameworks."
*   **Impact on Batch Scaling:** While Table 5 shows DEER scales well with batch size (reaching 159.87 tokens/s at batch size 8), these specific experiments were conducted **without KV cache** to ensure a fair comparison with baselines that also lacked dLLM-compatible caching. The authors note that existing frameworks like `vLLM` and `SGLang` do not yet fully support dLLM KV caching.
*   **Future Dependency:** DEER's full potential is contingent on the integration of emerging techniques like `Fast-dLLM` (Wu et al., 2025) and `dInfer` (Ma et al., 2025) into mainstream engines. Until this integration occurs, DEER may incur higher memory bandwidth costs during inference compared to highly optimized AR-based speculative decoding systems that already leverage mature KV cache implementations.

### 6.2. Training Sensitivity and Hyperparameter Fragility

The performance gains of DEER rely heavily on the **Diffusion-to-AR Alignment (D2A)** pipeline, specifically Stage 2 (Scribe Refinement). This stage introduces a hyperparameter sensitivity that does not exist in standard AR drafter training.

*   **Narrow Stability Window:** The exponential weighting factor $\alpha$ controls how strongly the loss emphasizes tokens near the prefix boundary. Figure 6 (Section 4.3.2) reveals a fragile optimization landscape:
    *   $\alpha = 1.01$: Stable convergence.
    *   $\alpha = 1.02$: Noisy training curves with slight upward drift.
    *   $\alpha = 1.05$: Early divergence.
*   **Deployment Implication:** This narrow window implies that adapting DEER to new model families or domains may require careful hyperparameter tuning. Unlike standard fine-tuning where learning rate adjustments are common, the structural weighting in Stage 2 must be precise to avoid destabilizing the diffusion process. This adds a layer of complexity to the training pipeline compared to methods like EAGLE-3, which do not require position-dependent loss weighting.

### 6.3. Domain Dependency on Pretrained dLLMs

DEER's performance is intrinsically linked to the availability of high-quality pretrained diffusion models in the target domain.

*   **Code vs. Math Disparity:** The speedup gains vary significantly by domain. On code generation (HumanEval, Qwen3-30B-A3B), DEER achieves a **5.54× speedup** (Section 4.2.1). However, on mathematical reasoning (Math500), the speedup drops to **2.12×** (Table 6, Section 4.6).
*   **Root Cause:** The paper attributes this gap to the availability of base models. For code, a dedicated `open-dCoder` checkpoint was available. For math, the authors had to convert an AR model (`Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct`) into a diffusion model and train it for 40 epochs, resulting in a "partially converged dLLM" whose standalone generations were "not yet semantically reliable" (Section 4.6).
*   **Generalization Risk:** This suggests that DEER's effectiveness in specialized domains (e.g., legal, medical, scientific reasoning) is capped by the current ecosystem of pretrained dLLMs. If a high-fidelity discrete dLLM does not exist for a specific domain, the cost of converting and aligning an AR model may offset the inference speedup gains.

### 6.4. Diminishing Returns on Block Size

While DEER eliminates the "gradual collapse of trust" allowing for longer drafts, increasing the draft block size $k$ does not yield linear improvements in acceptance length.

*   **Saturation Effect:** Appendix B.2 (Figure 8) shows that as block size increases from 4 to 32 tokens, the average acceptance length grows but the **growth rate gradually slows down**. For Qwen3-30B-A3B, acceptance length increases from approximately 4.5 to over 6.5 tokens, but the curve flattens significantly at larger block sizes.
*   **Compute Trade-off:** Larger blocks require more computation from the drafter in a single step. Since the acceptance length does not scale linearly with block size, there is an optimal operating point where the marginal gain in accepted tokens no longer justifies the increased drafting latency. Practitioners must tune block size per hardware configuration rather than simply maximizing it.

### 6.5. Drafter Scaling and Target Model Matching

DEER uses a fixed **0.5B-parameter** discrete diffusion model as the drafter across all target model scales (Appendix C). While this demonstrates efficiency, it raises questions about scalability to much larger target models.

*   **Parameter Ratio:** For the Qwen3-30B-A3B target, the drafter is 470M parameters (Table 7). This is a ratio of roughly 1:64. While effective here, it is unclear if a 0.5B drafter can sufficiently model the distribution of a 100B+ parameter target model.
*   **Comparison to AR Drafters:** EAGLE-3 scales its drafter size with the target (e.g., 140M for 30B target, 610M for 14B target). DEER's fixed-size approach simplifies deployment but may hit a ceiling where the drafter's capacity is insufficient to propose high-quality tokens for extremely large targets, potentially capping the maximum achievable acceptance length $\tau$.
*   **Open Question:** The paper does not explore whether scaling the drafter itself (e.g., to 1B or 3B) would yield further gains or if the mechanism itself is the primary bottleneck. This remains an open area for future optimization.

### 6.6. One-Step vs. Multi-Step Quality Trade-off

DEER commits to **one-step decoding** for the drafter to maximize latency benefits (Section 3.2). While this is efficient, it theoretically limits the quality of the draft compared to multi-step diffusion processes.

*   **Quality Ceiling:** Multi-step denoising typically allows diffusion models to refine samples iteratively, potentially correcting early errors within the generation process. By restricting drafting to a single step, DEER sacrifices this refinement capability.
*   **Mitigation:** The paper argues that the D2A alignment pipeline compensates for this by training the model to produce high-fidelity outputs in one pass. However, in scenarios where the target distribution is highly multimodal or complex, a one-step drafter might struggle to capture the full diversity of the target model compared to a multi-step alternative, potentially limiting acceptance rates in high-entropy contexts (e.g., creative writing vs. code).

In summary, DEER trades **infrastructure maturity** and **training simplicity** for **structural parallelism**. It solves the fundamental latency bottleneck of AR drafters but introduces dependencies on emerging dLLM infrastructure and sensitive alignment hyperparameters. For teams operating in domains with existing dLLM checkpoints and the capacity to manage custom inference kernels, DEER offers a superior efficiency frontier. For others, the infrastructure overhead may currently outweigh the theoretical speedup benefits.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

DEER represents more than an incremental speedup—it signals a paradigm shift in how we think about speculative decoding architecture. By demonstrating that discrete-space diffusion models can serve as superior drafters to autoregressive models, this work fundamentally challenges the assumption that AR is the only viable drafting mechanism. This section explores how DEER reshapes the research landscape, what new directions it enables, and when practitioners should consider adopting this approach.

### 7.1. Paradigm Shift: From Sequential to Parallel Drafting

The most profound implication of DEER is that it breaks the **autoregressive monopoly** on speculative decoding. For years, the field operated under an implicit constraint: drafters must be AR because verification requires AR. DEER proves this assumption false by showing that a diffusion drafter can propose tokens in parallel while maintaining lossless verification through standard rejection sampling.

This shift has three cascading effects on the field:

*   **Redefining the Bottleneck:** Prior work focused on optimizing AR draft heads (e.g., EAGLE's feature uncertainty modeling, Medusa's multiple decoding heads). DEER reveals that the true bottleneck was not drafter accuracy but **drafter architecture**. The "gradual collapse of trust" (Section 2.3) is a structural property of AR conditioning, not a tunable hyperparameter. This redirects research attention from incremental AR improvements to exploring alternative probabilistic models for drafting.

*   **Decoupling Drafting from Verification:** DEER establishes that the drafter and verifier need not share the same generative paradigm. The verifier remains AR (preserving compatibility with existing LLMs), while the drafter operates under different probabilistic rules. This modular separation allows each component to be optimized independently—a flexibility that hybrid architectures like TiDAR (Liu et al., 2025) sacrifice by entangling both objectives in a single model.

*   **Validating Discrete Diffusion for Production:** Prior diffusion language model work (e.g., D3PM, Diffusion-LM) remained largely academic due to efficiency concerns. DEER demonstrates that discrete dLLMs can be practical for latency-sensitive applications when paired with appropriate alignment strategies. This legitimizes discrete diffusion as a viable alternative to AR for specific inference tasks.

### 7.2. Follow-Up Research Directions

DEER opens several concrete research avenues that build on its core innovations:

#### 7.2.1. Scaling the Drafter Model

The paper uses a fixed **0.5B-parameter** dLLM drafter across all target scales (Appendix C). While this demonstrates efficiency, it leaves open whether larger drafters would yield further gains.

*   **Research Question:** Does scaling the drafter to 1B, 3B, or 7B parameters improve acceptance length ($\tau$) for very large targets (e.g., 100B+ models)?
*   **Hypothesis:** The independence mechanism should continue to provide benefits, but drafter capacity may become limiting for highly complex target distributions.
*   **Methodology:** Replicate the D2A alignment pipeline with larger base dLLMs (e.g., LLADA, Dream 7B) and measure $\tau$ scaling curves.

#### 7.2.2. Multi-Step Drafting with Early Exit

DEER commits to **one-step decoding** for maximum latency benefit (Section 3.2). However, this sacrifices the iterative refinement capability of multi-step diffusion.

*   **Research Question:** Can adaptive multi-step drafting—where the number of denoising steps varies per block based on uncertainty—improve acceptance rates without negating speedup?
*   **Potential Approach:** Implement an early-exit mechanism where blocks with high initial confidence accept after one step, while uncertain blocks undergo additional refinement. This would require a confidence estimation head on the dLLM.
*   **Trade-off:** Added drafting latency vs. higher acceptance length. The optimal balance depends on target model size and hardware characteristics.

#### 7.2.3. Domain-Specific dLLM Pretraining

The performance gap between code generation (5.54× speedup) and mathematical reasoning (2.12× speedup) in Section 4.6 highlights a critical dependency: **dLLM ecosystem maturity**.

*   **Research Need:** Dedicated pretrained dLLMs for domains beyond code (e.g., math, science, legal, medical).
*   **Current Limitation:** For math, the authors converted an AR model (`Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct`) and trained for 40 epochs, resulting in a "partially converged dLLM" (Section 4.6). This caps DEER's effectiveness.
*   **Opportunity:** Organizations with domain-specific AR models could invest in converting them to diffusion architectures, unlocking DEER-style acceleration for specialized applications.

#### 7.2.4. KV Cache Integration for dLLMs

Appendix F explicitly notes that "the community lacks mature support for diffusion language models (DLLMs) with KV caching in mainstream inference frameworks."

*   **Immediate Priority:** Integrate DEER with emerging dLLM KV cache implementations like `Fast-dLLM` (Wu et al., 2025) and `dInfer` (Ma et al., 2025).
*   **Expected Impact:** Batch inference throughput (Table 5) should improve significantly once KV caching is enabled, particularly for larger batch sizes where memory bandwidth becomes the bottleneck.
*   **Integration Challenge:** dLLM KV caching differs from AR caching because diffusion models may attend to masked positions differently. Framework developers need to account for this in their attention implementations.

#### 7.2.5. Beyond Speculative Decoding: Block Regeneration Applications

Section 4.5 identifies **reliable block regeneration** as an emergent capability of DEER-trained dLLMs—the ability to extend partially masked suffixes coherently across iterations (Figure 5).

*   **Application 1: Interactive Code Completion:** IDEs could allow developers to mask and regenerate specific code blocks without retyping surrounding context.
*   **Application 2: Iterative Refinement:** Users could request "make this function more efficient" and the model would regenerate only the relevant block while preserving the rest.
*   **Application 3: Controlled Editing:** Similar to image inpainting, text editing systems could mask regions for targeted modification while maintaining global coherence.

This capability suggests DEER-trained dLLMs have utility beyond acceleration—they represent a new interaction paradigm for language models.

### 7.3. Practical Applications and Deployment Scenarios

DEER's characteristics make it particularly well-suited for specific use cases. Understanding these helps practitioners decide when to adopt this approach.

#### 7.3.1. Ideal Deployment Contexts

| Scenario | Suitability | Rationale |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Code Generation APIs** | High | Strong dLLM checkpoints exist (`open-dCoder`); deterministic output benefits from long acceptance lengths (Table 4: 32 tokens max) |
| **Real-Time Agents** | High | Latency is critical; 5.54× speedup (Figure 1) directly improves response time for multi-turn interactions |
| **Batch Inference Services** | High | Table 5 shows DEER scales well with batch size (159.87 tokens/s at batch 8 vs 38.35 for AR) |
| **Mathematical Reasoning** | Medium | Speedup limited to ~2× due to immature dLLM base models (Section 4.6) |
| **Creative Writing** | Low | High-entropy generation may reduce acceptance rates; one-step drafting limits refinement |
| **Memory-Constrained Devices** | Medium | 0.5B drafter is lightweight, but dLLM KV cache immaturity may increase memory bandwidth costs |

#### 7.3.2. Integration Requirements

Teams considering DEER should verify they have:

*   **GPU Infrastructure:** Experiments used NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs (Appendix A). Smaller GPUs may require batch size adjustments.
*   **Training Capacity:** DEER requires ~240 GPU hours for Qwen3-14B (Table 8), significantly less than EAGLE-3's 1440 hours. This is feasible for most research teams.
*   **Inference Framework Flexibility:** Since mainstream frameworks (vLLM, SGLang) lack mature dLLM support, teams may need to implement custom inference kernels or wait for integration updates (Appendix F).
*   **Domain Checkpoints:** Access to a pretrained discrete dLLM in the target domain, or capacity to convert an AR model to diffusion architecture.

### 7.4. When to Prefer DEER Over Alternatives

Based on the experimental evidence, we can provide concrete guidance on method selection:

#### 7.4.1. Choose DEER When:

*   **Target Model is Large (14B+):** Table 2 shows DEER's advantage grows with model scale. On Qwen3-30B-A3B, DEER achieves 5.54× vs EAGLE-3's 2.41× speedup. The independence mechanism provides more value when target model latency is the dominant bottleneck.
*   **Long Context Generation:** If applications require generating hundreds or thousands of tokens per request, DEER's higher acceptance length ($\tau = 6.58$ vs $3.21$ on HumanEval) compounds into substantial end-to-end savings.
*   **Training Budget is Limited:** DEER's 240 GPU-hour training cost (Table 8) is 6× lower than EAGLE-3's 1440 hours for Qwen3-14B. This makes DEER more accessible for teams without large training clusters.
*   **Domain Has Mature dLLMs:** Code generation has `open-dCoder` and similar checkpoints. If your domain has equivalent dLLM resources, DEER is the clear choice.

#### 7.4.2. Choose AR-Based Methods (EAGLE-3, Medusa) When:

*   **Production Infrastructure is Fixed:** If your serving stack already uses vLLM or SGLang with optimized AR KV caching, switching to DEER requires custom integration work that may not be justified by the speedup gain.
*   **Domain Lacks dLLM Checkpoints:** For specialized domains (legal, medical, scientific), converting an AR model to diffusion architecture may offset inference gains. AR drafters can be trained directly on domain data without this conversion step.
*   **Target Model is Small (&lt;8B):** Table 2 shows DEER's advantage is smaller on Qwen3-4B (2.77× vs 2.40×). For small models, the infrastructure complexity of DEER may not justify the marginal speedup.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning Capacity is Limited:** Stage 2's exponential weighting factor $\alpha$ requires careful tuning (Figure 6: stable at 1.01, divergent at 1.05). Teams without tuning bandwidth may prefer AR methods with more forgiving training dynamics.

### 7.5. Long-Term Vision: A Hybrid Inference Ecosystem

DEER points toward a future where inference systems dynamically select drafting strategies based on context:

*   **Code Blocks:** Use DEER with diffusion drafting for maximum parallelism.
*   **Reasoning Chains:** Use AR-based drafting when step-by-step verification is critical.
*   **Creative Generation:** Use multi-step diffusion when sample diversity matters more than latency.

This hybrid approach would require inference frameworks to support multiple drafting backends and switch between them seamlessly. DEER's modular design—separate drafter and verifier—makes it compatible with such a vision.

The broader implication is that **no single generative paradigm dominates all inference tasks**. AR excels at sequential reasoning; diffusion excels at parallel proposal generation. DEER demonstrates that combining these paradigms—using each for its strengths—can outperform homogeneous architectures. This insight may extend beyond speculative decoding to other areas of LLM inference optimization, such as caching strategies, memory management, and multi-model collaboration.

### 7.6. Reproducibility Checklist

For teams attempting to reproduce or extend DEER, the following items are critical:

*   **Base Model:** Use `open-dCoder` for code tasks or convert `Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct` for math (Section 4.1, Appendix A).
*   **Stage 1 Training:** AdamW optimizer, learning rate $1 \times 10^{-4}$, 1 epoch for code or 5 epochs for math (Appendix A).
*   **Stage 2 Training:** AdamW optimizer, learning rate $5 \times 10^{-5}$ for code or $1 \times 10^{-4}$ for math, 1 epoch on 100k examples (Appendix A).
*   **Exponential Weighting:** Set $\alpha = 1.01$ for Stage 2. Values above 1.02 risk training instability (Figure 6).
*   **Block Size:** Start with $k = 16$ tokens; tune based on acceptance length saturation (Figure 8, Appendix B.2).
*   **Temperature:** Main results use temperature = 0 for deterministic evaluation (Table 2). Temperature = 0.6 results are in Table 1.
*   **Hardware:** Eight NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs recommended for training (Appendix A).

The authors have committed to releasing code, models, and demos at `https://czc726.github.io/DEER/` (Abstract). Until then, the above specifications provide a baseline for independent implementation.

---

In summary, DEER does not merely accelerate LLM inference—it redefines what architectures are viable for drafting. By proving that diffusion models can draft more effectively than autoregressive models when properly aligned, this work opens a new research frontier. The immediate practical benefits (5.54× speedup, 32-token acceptance) are substantial, but the long-term implications—modular drafting, domain-specific dLLMs, hybrid inference ecosystems—may prove more transformative. Teams with the infrastructure to adopt DEER today gain a significant efficiency advantage; teams that invest in the underlying dLLM ecosystem position themselves for the next generation of inference optimization.
