## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the stagnation of Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) in large language models under sparse 0/1 rewards, where identical rollout outcomes cause advantage collapse, by proposing SAGE (Self-hint Aligned GRPO with Privileged Supervision), which injects training-time privileged hints to increase outcome diversity without altering test-time deployment. Experiments across 6 benchmarks with Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, and Qwen3-4B-Instruct demonstrate that SAGE consistently outperforms standard GRPO, achieving average accuracy gains of +2.0, +1.2, and +1.3 respectively, while effectively utilizing hard prompts that typically provide no learning signal.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Advantage Collapse in Sparse-Reward RL

To understand what SAGE solves, we first need to understand how Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) works and where it breaks down. GRPO has become a practical method for aligning large language models with verifiable objectives—tasks where correctness can be automatically checked, such as math problems with exact answers, code with unit tests, or reasoning tasks with automated checkers (Section 1).

The mechanism is straightforward: for each prompt $x$, the policy generates a group of $G$ rollouts (candidate solutions) $\{\tau_i\}_{i=1}^G$. Each rollout receives a reward $R_i = R(x, \tau_i)$, typically binary ($0$ or $1$) for verifiable tasks. GRPO then computes **standardized advantages** within each group:

$$A_i = \frac{R_i - \bar{R}}{s + \epsilon}$$

where $\bar{R}$ is the group mean reward, $s$ is the group standard deviation, and $\epsilon$ is a numerical stabilizer (Section 2.1, Equation 2).

**Here lies the critical failure mode:** When all rollouts in a group receive identical rewards (e.g., all zeros on a difficult prompt), the standard deviation $s = 0$, causing all advantages $A_i = 0$. The policy gradient vanishes, and the model receives **no learning signal** for that prompt. This is not a rare edge case—Figure 2 shows that for weaker models like Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, over 40% of prompts provide no training signal throughout standard GRPO training.

The paper formalizes this as a **finite-sample pathology**. The probability that a group contains mixed outcomes (some correct, some incorrect) is approximately $G \cdot p_\theta(x)$ when the success probability $p_\theta(x)$ is small (Section 2.1). When $G \cdot p_\theta(x) \ll 1$, updates vanish systematically on hard prompts. This creates a vicious cycle: the model cannot learn from difficult examples because it never samples correct solutions, but it needs to learn from them to improve.

### Why This Matters: Both Theoretical and Practical Significance

This problem matters for two interconnected reasons:

**Theoretical significance:** GRPO's advantage standardization assumes within-group variance exists. When this assumption fails under sparse rewards, the estimator becomes degenerate—not because the underlying objective is flat, but because finite sampling provides no signal. Section 3 analyzes this as a "gated update" mechanism: training only proceeds when the "gate opens" (i.e., when $s > 0$). Proposition 3.2 shows the gate-opening probability is maximized at $p_\theta = 0.5$, yet sparse-reward tasks often operate at $p_\theta \ll 0.5$ for challenging prompts.

**Practical impact:** Hard prompts are precisely where improvement matters most. Table 2 quantifies prompt utilization: standard GRPO leaves 40.2% of prompts unused for Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct. Prior work (cited in Section 1) shows that RL on prompt sets with lower pass rates yields better final performance. If the training procedure systematically ignores difficult examples, the model's ceiling is artificially capped. This directly limits reasoning capabilities on benchmarks like AIME, Olympiad math, and other challenging domains where the paper evaluates.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper identifies three categories of existing remedies, each with distinct shortcomings (Section 1):

| Approach | Mechanism | Key Limitation |
|----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Skip and resample** | Discard degenerate groups, sample new prompts | Biases training toward easier prompts; wastes hard examples (Yu et al., 2025; Xiong et al., 2025a) |
| **Adaptive sampling / curriculum** | Allocate more rollouts to difficult prompts | Requires careful scheduling; does not solve the underlying sampling problem (Yao et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025) |
| **External guidance** | Inject trajectories from stronger models or offline buffers | Introduces distribution mismatch; off-policy instability (Zhang et al., 2025a; Yan et al., 2025) |

The most directly related baseline is **LUFFY** (Yan et al., 2025), which replaces one on-policy trajectory with a correct trajectory from a stronger model (DeepSeek-R1). While this ensures positive rewards exist in each group, Section 5.1 notes LUFFY exhibits training instability due to off-policy mismatch—Figure 4 shows LUFFY has "excessively high entropy" for Llama-3.2 and "very low rewards at the beginning" for Qwen3. The policy imitates an external distribution that may not align with its own capabilities.

Another relevant method is **Scaf-GRPO** (Zhang et al., 2025b), which incorporates hints from GPT-5. However, Section 5.1 observes Scaf-GRPO has "the lowest entropy among all methods, indicating limited exploration"—the external hints may reveal excessive information, constraining the model's learning trajectory. Additionally, relying on a stronger external model increases infrastructure complexity and cost.

### How SAGE Positions Itself

SAGE occupies a distinct design space that addresses the limitations above while preserving GRPO's on-policy foundation:

**Privileged information only at training time.** SAGE injects hints $h$ during training but deploys with $h = \emptyset$ (Section 2.3). This differs from methods that permanently modify the input distribution or require external models at inference. The hint is a "lossy compression of a reference solution $\tau^\star$" (Section 2.2)—for example, a procedural plan or decomposition that guides reasoning without revealing the answer (Appendix A shows concrete examples).

**On-policy conditioning.** Critically, SAGE samples rollouts from $\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x, h)$ and optimizes $\log \pi_\theta(y_t \mid x, h, y_{<t})$ (Section 4.1). Section 5.2 ablates this: an off-policy variant that rolls out with hints but optimizes $\log \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)$ drops from 59.2% to 56.5% accuracy (Figure 5). This confirms that matching the sampling context to the optimization objective is essential for stability.

**Self-generated, policy-dependent hints.** Unlike Scaf-GRPO's fixed external hints, SAGE generates hints from the policy itself (or a lagged copy), refreshed during training (Section 4.2). Figure 3 demonstrates online self-hinting consistently outperforms offline self-hinting and external GPT-5 hints across hint levels. Proposition 3.3 provides theoretical justification: the optimal hint distribution depends on the current policy parameters $\theta$, so a fixed hint generator becomes miscalibrated as training progresses.

**Adaptive hint strength scheduling.** SAGE does not apply hints uniformly. Section 4.3 describes two schemes: SAGE-LIGHT uses epoch-level accuracy thresholds, while SAGE uses a group-degeneracy trigger that activates hints only when $\sum_{i=1}^G R_i = 0$ (Algorithm 1, lines 9-13). This creates an "automatic curriculum" (Section 2.3)—hints are used precisely when the learning gate is closed, then withdrawn as the model improves. Figure 6 shows the number of prompts requiring hints decreases over training steps, confirming the model progressively solves problems without assistance.

**No test-time overhead.** Perhaps most practically, SAGE requires no architectural changes or additional inference cost. The deployed policy is $\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)$, identical to standard GRPO (Section 2.3, Algorithm 1 line 19). All hinting machinery exists only during training.

In summary, SAGE positions itself as a **minimal intervention** that reshapes the rollout distribution to prevent advantage collapse while maintaining on-policy correctness, avoiding external dependencies, and incurring zero deployment cost. The following sections detail how this is achieved and validate its effectiveness empirically.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

SAGE is a training framework that temporarily gives a language model "cheat sheets" (hints) during reinforcement learning to help it discover correct solutions on difficult problems, then removes those hints entirely before deployment. The solution reshapes what the model sees during training—injecting privileged information only when the model is stuck—while guaranteeing the final deployed system operates without any hint infrastructure whatsoever.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The SAGE system consists of four interconnected components that operate in a specific training-time sequence. First, the **Hint Generator** (`q_φ`) takes a prompt `x`, its reference solution `τ*`, and a strength level `ℓ` to produce a hint `h`—this is a compressed procedural guide that does not reveal the final answer. Second, the **Hint Scheduler** monitors the policy's recent performance on each prompt and decides whether to activate hinting (setting `ℓ > 0`) or use the standard no-hint setting (`ℓ = 0`). Third, the **Hint-Conditioned Policy** (`π_θ(· | x, h)`) generates a group of `G` rollouts conditioned on the hint-augmented prompt, producing candidate solutions that are more likely to include correct answers on hard problems. Fourth, the **GRPO Loss Computer** calculates standardized advantages from the group's rewards and updates the policy parameters `θ` using the hint-conditioned log-probabilities, ensuring the training remains on-policy. At deployment, the scheduler is disabled, the hint generator is discarded, and the policy runs as `π_θ(· | x)` with no hint input.

- We describe the two hint-strength scheduling schemes (SAGE-LIGHT and SAGE) and when each activates hints based on policy performance (Section 3.4.3).

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is an algorithmic paper that proposes a training-time intervention for reinforcement learning with language models, where the core idea is to inject privileged hints during training to increase within-group outcome diversity while keeping the test-time policy unchanged.

#### 3.4.1 The Privileged Hinting Mechanism

**What is a hint in SAGE?** A hint `h` is a lossy compression of a reference solution `τ*` that provides procedural guidance without revealing the final answer (Section 2.2). For example, on a math problem requiring algebraic manipulation, a Level-2 hint might state "Convert the base-b numerals into the form Ab + C, then use linear combinations to cancel the b-term" without showing the actual computation or final sum (Appendix A, Case Study). The hint is appended to the prompt as additional context, transforming the input from `x` to `(x, h)`.

**Hint strength levels.** The paper controls hint informativeness using a discrete strength variable `ℓ ∈ {0, 1, ..., L}` where `L = 3` in all experiments (Section 5, Implementation details). Level `ℓ = 0` corresponds to the no-hint setting where `h = ∅` deterministically. Higher levels provide progressively more guidance: Level 1 offers minimal conceptual direction, Level 2 provides intermediate methodological steps, and Level 3 gives detailed procedural guidance while still requiring the model to complete the solution (Appendix B shows the exact prompt template for generating these three levels). The hint generator samples `ℓ ~ p(ℓ)` and then produces `h ~ q(h | x, τ*, ℓ)` conditioned on the chosen strength (Section 2.2, Equation 3).

**Why hints do not change the task reward.** Critically, the task reward `R(x, τ)` remains unchanged regardless of whether a hint was used during rollout generation (Section 2.2). The verifier checks only whether the final answer in trajectory `τ` is correct for prompt `x`—it does not know or care that the model saw hint `h` during generation. This design ensures that at test time, when `h = ∅`, the policy is optimized for the same objective it saw during training. The hint only reshapes the rollout distribution `π_θ(· | x, h)` to increase the probability of observing correct solutions under finite sampling, without modifying what counts as "correct."

**Hint generation procedure.** The hint generator `q_φ` is implemented by prompting the policy model `π_θ` itself to produce a procedure-only plan aligned with the reference trajectory `τ*` (Section 4.2). Appendix B provides the exact system prompt: the model acts as a "tutoring assistant" that generates three levels of hints progressively, with explicit guidelines to never reveal the final answer and to tailor difficulty to bridge the gap between student capability and solution. During training, the system uses the reference solution `τ*` (available from the training dataset) to condition hint generation, ensuring hints are grounded in valid reasoning paths.

#### 3.4.2 Mathematical Foundation: Why Hints Prevent Advantage Collapse

**The advantage collapse problem restated.** Standard GRPO computes standardized advantages for each rollout `i` in a group of size `G` as:

$$A_i = \frac{R_i - \bar{R}}{s + \epsilon}$$

where `R_i ∈ {0, 1}` is the binary reward, `R̄` is the group mean, `s` is the group standard deviation, and `ϵ ≥ 0` is a numerical stabilizer (Section 2.1, Equation 2). When all rollouts receive identical rewards (e.g., all zeros on a hard prompt), the standard deviation `s = 0`, causing all advantages `A_i = 0` regardless of `ϵ`. The policy gradient vanishes because there is no within-group variance to exploit.

**Gate-opening probability.** Section 3 formalizes this as a "gated update" mechanism where training proceeds only when the group contains mixed outcomes. Let `p_θ(x, h) = Pr[R(x, τ) = 1 | x, h]` be the success probability under the hint-conditioned policy. Proposition 3.2 derives the probability that a group has non-zero variance (i.e., the "gate opens") as:

$$\text{Pr}[s > 0 \mid x, h] = 1 - (1 - p_\theta(x, h))^G - p_\theta(x, h)^G$$

This expression captures two degenerate cases: all-zero rewards (probability `(1 - p)^G`) and all-one rewards (probability `p^G`). In the sparse-reward regime where `p ≪ 1`, this simplifies to approximately `G · p_θ(x, h)` (Section 3.1, Proposition 3.2). When `G · p ≪ 1`, updates vanish systematically.

**How hints shift the gate probability.** Hinting increases the effective success probability from `p_θ(x)` (no hint) to `p_θ^(ℓ)(x)` (with hint level `ℓ`). The paper shows empirically in Figure 3 that any hint level improves performance over no-hint training on extremely hard prompts where correct trajectories are never sampled without hints. By raising `p_θ^(ℓ)(x)` sufficiently, hints make `G · p_θ^(ℓ)(x)` no longer tiny, causing non-degenerate groups to become common and RL to receive updates. However, hints should not be so strong that `p_θ ≈ 1`, which would also close the gate (all-one degeneracy). Proposition 3.3 shows the gate probability is maximized at `p = 0.5`, so optimal hints calibrate difficulty to keep success rates near this midpoint.

**Why one hint per prompt per epoch.** Remark 3.4 provides a theoretical justification for sampling a single hint realization per prompt per epoch rather than multiple hints. The gate probability function `u(p) = 1 - (1 - p)^G - p^G` is strictly concave for `G ≥ 2`. By Jensen's inequality, for any hint distribution `q`:

$$\mathbb{E}_{h \sim q}[u(p_\theta(x, h))] \leq u(\mathbb{E}_{h \sim q}[p_\theta(x, h)])$$

This means additional randomness across hints can only reduce the expected frequency of non-degenerate groups at a fixed mean success rate. The paper therefore spends compute on increasing group size `G` or better strength scheduling rather than sampling multiple hints per prompt (Remark 3.4, Equation 11).

#### 3.4.3 Policy-Dependent Hint-Strength Scheduling

**Why scheduling is necessary.** Applying hints uniformly to all prompts would be inefficient and potentially harmful. Easy prompts that already provide learning signals do not need hints, and overly strong hints on solvable problems could push `p_θ` too close to 1, reducing exploration. The scheduler activates hints only when the current policy provides insufficient learning signal on a specific prompt, creating an "automatic curriculum" that tracks the learner's bottlenecks (Section 2.3).

**Scheme 1: SAGE-LIGHT (epoch-level threshold).** This compute-efficient scheme updates hint strength once per epoch based on the previous epoch's empirical success rate (Section 4.3, Scheme 1). Let `p̂_{t-1}(x)` denote the empirical success rate of prompt `x` measured in epoch `t-1`. Given a target threshold `α ∈ (0, 1)`, the hint level updates as:

$$\ell_t(x) = \min\{\ell_{t-1}(x) + 1, L\} \quad \text{if } \hat{p}_{t-1}(x) < \alpha$$

Otherwise, `ℓ_t(x) = ℓ_{t-1}(x)`. The paper sets `α = 0.35` in experiments (Section 5, Implementation details). This scheme requires no additional rollouts beyond standard training, making it efficient, but it reacts slowly to sudden reward collapse since it only checks performance at epoch boundaries.

**Scheme 2: SAGE (group-degeneracy trigger).** This more reactive scheme uses a local trigger based on whether a probe group contains any positive samples (Section 4.3, Scheme 2; Algorithm 1, lines 9-13). For a small probe group of `G` rollouts at the current hint level `ℓ_{t-1}(x)`, define `z(x) = 1` if `∑_{i=1}^G R(x, τ_i) = 0` (i.e., the group has no positive rollouts). The hint level updates as:

$$\ell_t(x) = \min\{\ell_{t-1}(x) + 1, L\} \quad \text{if } z(x) = 1$$

This rule targets the specific finite-sample pathology of sparse rewards: when a group contains no positives, standardized GRPO advantages collapse and the policy-gradient estimator vanishes. The scheme is more reactive than SAGE-LIGHT but incurs additional compute for probe rollouts. Section 5.1 reports that SAGE (Scheme 2) "typically recovers faster from stalled training on hard prompts and yields better performance" than SAGE-LIGHT.

**Per-prompt hint level tracking.** Algorithm 1 (line 1) initializes a per-prompt level map `ℓ(x) ← 0` for all prompts in the training set. This allows different prompts to have different hint levels simultaneously—easy prompts remain at `ℓ = 0` while hard prompts progressively increase to `ℓ = 1, 2,` or `3`. Figure 6 shows that the number of prompts using hints decreases over training steps on Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, confirming the model becomes more capable and requires less assistance as training progresses.

#### 3.4.4 Online Self-Hinting vs. Offline and External Hints

**Three hint generation variants.** Section 4.2 evaluates three approaches to generating hints:

1. **Fixed privileged hints (offline):** The hint generator `q_φ` is derived from the initial policy `π_{θ_0}` and frozen after initialization. This is simple but does not adapt to the learner's evolving capabilities.
2. **Online privileged hints (SAGE):** The hint generator `q_φ` is derived from the current policy `π_θ` and refreshed periodically during training. This keeps hints calibrated to what the learner can currently understand.
3. **External teacher hints:** The hint generator is produced by a stronger frozen model (e.g., GPT-5 in Scaf-GRPO baseline). This provides high-quality hints but introduces infrastructure complexity and potential distribution mismatch.

**Why online self-hinting outperforms.** Figure 3 (left) shows online self-hinting consistently achieves the best performance across all hint levels compared to offline self-hinting and external GPT-5 hints. For example, at hint level `ℓ = 2` with extended training (400 steps), online self-hinting reaches 58.3% average accuracy on Qwen3-4B-Instruct versus 55.4% for offline self-hinting with multiple hints and 56.5% for GPT-5 hints (Table C.1 in Appendix C). Proposition 3.3 provides theoretical justification: the optimal hint distribution `q*_θ` depends on the current policy parameters `θ` because `p_θ(x, h)` changes as the policy learns. A fixed hint generator becomes miscalibrated over training, while online refresh maintains alignment with the learner's current support.

**Hint diversity ablation.** Section 5.2 tests whether online self-hinting's advantage comes merely from increased hint diversity. The authors create an "offline with more hints" variant where the base LLM generates 10 hints per prompt before training (temperature=1.0), and a different hint is used for the same prompt at each step. This increases diversity but still underperforms online self-hinting by 2.0 percentage points (Figure 5). The conclusion is that hint quality and calibration matter more than raw diversity—hints generated by the current policy better match what the learner can currently utilize.

#### 3.4.5 Complete Loss Function and Training Algorithm

**The SAGE loss function.** Given a prompt `x`, hint level `ℓ`, and hint `h`, the policy generates a group of `G` rollouts `τ_i ~ π_θ(· | x, h)` for `i = 1, ..., G`. The loss conditioned on hints is (Section 2.3, Equation 5):

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{1}{G}\sum_{i=1}^G A_i \sum_{t=1}^{T_i} \log \pi_\theta(y_{i,t} \mid x, h, y_{i,<t})\right] + \beta \mathbb{E}\left[\text{KL}(\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x, h) \parallel \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot \mid x, h))\right]$$

where the expectation is over prompts `x ~ D`, hint sampling from Equation 3, and rollouts from the hint-conditioned policy. The first term is the policy gradient loss weighted by advantages `A_i`, and the second term is a KL regularization against a reference policy `π_ref` with weight `β`. Following DAPO (Yu et al., 2025), the paper sets `β = 0` in all experiments, disabling the KL term (Section 5, Implementation details).

**On-policy conditioning requirement.** Section 4.1 emphasizes that the loss must optimize `log π_θ(y_t | x, h, y_{<t})`—the log-probability conditioned on the hint. An ablation in Figure 5 tests an off-policy variant that rolls out with hint `h` but optimizes `log π_θ(· | x)` (dropping `h` inside the log-prob). This off-policy variant drops from 59.2% accuracy (on-policy SAGE) to 56.5%, and even underperforms a single fixed hint level baseline at 58.3%. The mismatch behaves like an off-policy update and is "markedly less stable under sparse rewards" (Section 4.1).

**Algorithm 1 implementation details.** Algorithm 1 summarizes the full training procedure. Key steps include:
- Initialize policy parameters `θ` and per-prompt hint level map `ℓ(x) ← 0` for all prompts (line 1).
- For each minibatch, determine hint level using either SAGE-LIGHT (epoch threshold, lines 5-6) or SAGE (group-degeneracy trigger, lines 8-13).
- Sample hint `h_b ~ q_φ(h | x_b, τ*_b, ℓ(x_b))` (line 7 or 13).
- Generate `G` rollouts `τ_{b,i} ~ π_θ(· | x_b, h_b)` and compute rewards `R_{b,i}` (line 14 for SAGE-LIGHT, implicit in line 11 for SAGE).
- Compute standardized advantages `A_{b,i}` using Equation 2 (line 15).
- Update policy parameters using gradient of `L_pg + β L_kl` (line 18).
- At deployment, set `ℓ = 0` so `h = ∅` and run `π_θ(· | x)` (line 19).

**Hyperparameters and compute.** Section 5 (Implementation details) reports the following configuration:
- Group size `G = 8` trajectories per prompt (reduced to `G = 4` for Qwen3-4B-Instruct due to slower training from long responses).
- Maximum hint level `L = 3`.
- Threshold `α = 0.35` for SAGE-LIGHT.
- Batch size = 128 prompts per update.
- Training steps = 500 total, with evaluation every 50 steps.
- Maximum response length = 8096 tokens for main results (required by LUFFY baseline), 2048 tokens for other experiments.
- Asymmetric clipping: `ϵ_low = 0.2`, `ϵ_high = 0.28` (following DAPO).
- Hardware: 8 A100 GPUs, using verl for training and vLLM for sampling.

**Training time overhead.** Table 3 reports training time on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct relative to GRPO baseline (25.3 hours = 1.0×). SAGE incurs 2.3× overhead due to hint generation and potential multi-level sampling for hard prompts. SAGE-LIGHT requires only 1.2× overhead since it samples from a single hint level per epoch based on prior accuracy. This trade-off allows practitioners to choose based on efficiency requirements—both variants consistently outperform baselines (Section 5.2).

#### 3.4.6 Ablation Evidence Validating Design Choices

**Same-level hint vs. adaptive scheduling.** Figure 5 compares SAGE's adaptive scheduling against using a constant hint level (e.g., online `ℓ = 2` throughout training). The adaptive strategy achieves 59.2% accuracy versus 58.3% for fixed `ℓ = 2`, a +0.9 gain. This confirms that progressively weakening hints as the model improves (down to `ℓ = 0`) enables more effective learning than static assistance. The design allows the LLM to "gradually solve difficult problems without the help of a hint" as Figure 6 demonstrates.

**Offline with more hints vs. online.** As noted in Section 3.4.4, generating 10 diverse hints offline and rotating them during training improves over standard offline self-hinting by +0.9 points (56.3% vs. 55.4% in Figure 5). However, online self-hinting still outperforms this diversity-focused variant by 2.0 points (58.3% vs. 56.3%). This validates that hint calibration to the current policy matters more than raw hint diversity.

**Off-policy optimization penalty.** The ablation testing off-policy optimization (roll out with `h`, optimize `log π_θ(· | x)`) shows a clear drop to 56.5% from 59.2% (Figure 5). This validates Section 4.1's claim that matching the sampling context to the optimization objective is "what keeps training on-policy for the augmented context." Without this match, the update "no longer corresponds to the gradient of any on-policy objective under the sampling process."

**Prompt utilization improvement.** Table 2 quantifies how SAGE improves training signal utilization. For Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, standard GRPO leaves 40.2% of prompts without any training signal (no correct trajectories sampled), while SAGE reduces this to 30.0%—a 10.2 percentage point improvement. For Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, the improvement is from 10.3% to 8.2%, and for Qwen3-4B-Instruct from 1.3% to 1.0%. Section 5.1 notes that "these hard prompts play a critical role in RL," aligning with prior work favoring RL on prompt sets with lower pass rates. The larger improvement on weaker models reflects that they benefit more from hint assistance on difficult problems.

**Response length and reasoning quality.** Figure 4 shows SAGE exhibits "faster response-length growth than GRPO for both Llama-3.2 and Qwen2.5, due to learning from hard prompts that fail to provide any signal under GRPO" (Section 5.1). Longer responses typically indicate more detailed reasoning chains. SAGE also maintains "mild entropy" comparable to GRPO, while LUFFY shows "excessively high entropy" (instability) and Scaf-GRPO shows "the lowest entropy" (limited exploration). This suggests SAGE preserves healthy exploration dynamics while improving sample efficiency on hard problems.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the fundamental conceptual advances SAGE introduces to reinforcement learning (RL) for language models. While Section 3 detailed the mechanical implementation, here we examine *why* these design choices represent novel contributions compared to standard RL practices. We distinguish between incremental engineering improvements and fundamental shifts in how sparse-reward optimization is conceptualized and executed.

### 4.1 Theoretical Insight: Optimizing the "Learnability" Gate

Standard RL objectives focus exclusively on maximizing expected reward $\mathbb{E}[R]$. SAGE introduces a critical secondary objective: maximizing the **probability of receiving a learning signal**.

In sparse-reward settings (e.g., binary correctness), the policy gradient estimator depends entirely on within-group variance. As derived in Section 3.4.2, the update magnitude is governed by a "gate" function $u(p) = 1 - (1-p)^G - p^G$, where $p$ is the success probability. When $p \ll 1/G$, this gate closes ($u(p) \approx 0$), and the optimizer receives zero gradient regardless of the potential reward.

**Why this is innovative:**
*   **Shift in Objective:** Prior methods treat sparse rewards as a sampling problem (sample more trajectories to find a positive one). SAGE treats it as a **variance availability problem**. The hint mechanism is not primarily designed to increase reward directly, but to shift $p$ into a range where $u(p)$ is non-zero (ideally near $p=0.5$ as shown in Proposition 3.2).
*   **Finite-Sample Correction:** This insight addresses a specific finite-sample pathology. Theoretically, the expected gradient might be non-zero, but the *estimator* is degenerate with finite $G$. SAGE corrects the estimator's variance properties rather than just the reward distribution.
*   **Quantifiable Impact:** This theoretical reframing explains the results in Table 2, where SAGE reduces the percentage of prompts with *no training signal* from 40.2% to 30.0% for Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct. The innovation lies in recognizing that **utilizing hard prompts** (by opening the gate) is more valuable than skipping them, even if the immediate reward gain is marginal.

### 4.2 Methodological Innovation: Hint Generators as Dynamic Policy Components

Most prior work using external guidance (e.g., Scaf-GRPO, LUFFY) treats hints or reference trajectories as **static data** generated once by a stronger model. SAGE treats the hint generator $q_\phi$ as a **dynamic component** that must evolve alongside the policy $\pi_\theta$.

**Why this is innovative:**
*   **Policy-Dependent Optimality:** Proposition 3.3 proves that the optimal hint distribution $q^\star_\theta$ depends on the current policy parameters $\theta$. As the policy learns, the set of hints that calibrate $p_\theta \approx 0.5$ changes. A fixed hint generator (offline) inevitably becomes miscalibrated, either providing hints that are too weak (gate remains closed) or too strong (gate closes due to lack of variance).
*   **Calibration Over Quality:** Figure 3 demonstrates that **online self-hinting** (58.3% accuracy) outperforms **external GPT-5 hints** (56.5% accuracy) despite GPT-5 presumably generating higher-quality reasoning. This counter-intuitive result highlights that **alignment with the learner's current capability** is more critical than raw hint quality.
*   **Self-Referential Loop:** By using the policy itself (or a lagged copy) to generate hints, SAGE creates a self-referential learning loop. The model teaches itself using its own evolving understanding of the task, avoiding the distribution mismatch that plagues off-policy guidance methods (Section 5.1 notes LUFFY's instability due to this mismatch).

### 4.3 Architectural Innovation: Privileged Training with Clean Deployment

SAGE strictly separates training-time information from test-time deployment. While "privileged information" (data available during training but not testing) exists in classical RL, SAGE implements this in LLMs without architectural modifications or inference overhead.

**Why this is innovative:**
*   **Zero Test-Time Cost:** Unlike methods that require retrieving external documents, calling auxiliary models, or maintaining larger context windows at inference, SAGE deploys as $\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)$ with $h = \emptyset$ (Algorithm 1, line 19). The hint infrastructure is entirely discarded after training.
*   **On-Policy Integrity:** Section 4.1 emphasizes that hints must be included in the conditioning context for *both* sampling and loss computation ($\log \pi_\theta(y_t \mid x, h)$). This maintains on-policy correctness. Ablation results in Figure 5 show that violating this (off-policy variant) drops accuracy from 59.2% to 56.5%. The innovation is successfully integrating privileged context while preserving the theoretical guarantees of on-policy gradient estimation.
*   **Scalability:** This design allows SAGE to scale to deployment environments where latency and cost are critical. The training cost increase (2.3× for SAGE, Table 3) is a one-time investment, whereas methods requiring external guidance at test time incur perpetual costs.

### 4.4 Efficiency Innovation: Variance-Triggered Automatic Curriculum

Curriculum learning typically relies on pre-defined difficulty metrics (e.g., prompt length, lexical complexity) or performance thresholds (e.g., accuracy < 50%). SAGE introduces a **signal-driven curriculum** triggered directly by the optimizer's state.

**Why this is innovative:**
*   **Direct Gradient Signal:** The SAGE scheduling scheme (Section 4.3, Scheme 2) activates hints only when the group variance collapses ($\sum R_i = 0$). This bypasses the need for heuristic difficulty labels. The system detects "learning stagnation" directly from the reward variance rather than inferring it from prompt features.
*   **Automatic Withdrawal:** As the model improves, the variance naturally increases, causing the trigger to fire less often. Figure 6 visualizes this: the number of prompts using hints decreases monotonically over training steps. This creates a natural "fading" of assistance without manual scheduling.
*   **Compute Efficiency Trade-off:** The paper offers two variants: SAGE (reactive, 2.3× cost) and SAGE-LIGHT (epoch-level, 1.2× cost). This provides a novel design space for practitioners. SAGE-LIGHT achieves nearly comparable performance (e.g., +10.7 vs. +11.5 average gain on Llama-3.2, Table 1) with significantly lower overhead, demonstrating that **approximate signal detection** is often sufficient for curriculum benefits.

### 4.5 Summary of Contributions

| Contribution | Prior Standard | SAGE Innovation | Significance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Objective** | Maximize Reward $\mathbb{E}[R]$ | Maximize Signal Frequency $\text{Pr}[s>0]$ | Solves finite-sample degeneracy in sparse RL |
| **Hint Source** | Fixed / External Model | Online Self-Generated | Ensures calibration to learner's evolving capability |
| **Deployment** | Often requires hints/context | No hints ($h=\emptyset$) | Zero inference overhead, scalable deployment |
| **Curriculum** | Heuristic / Performance-based | Variance-Triggered | Automatic, signal-driven assistance withdrawal |

These innovations collectively position SAGE not merely as a hyperparameter tweak for GRPO, but as a structural correction to how on-policy RL handles sparse verification signals in large language models. The empirical gains (e.g., +6.1 average accuracy on Llama-3.2, Table 1) validate that addressing the **variance collapse** mechanism is as critical as improving the reward model itself.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines whether SAGE's theoretical advantages translate into measurable improvements across diverse models and benchmarks. We analyze the experimental methodology, present quantitative results with specific numbers, evaluate ablation studies that validate design choices, and assess the strength of evidence supporting the paper's claims.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Models and Training Configuration.** The paper evaluates three LLMs representing different levels of mathematical specialization (Section 5, Models):

| Model | Math Specialization | Parameters |
|-------|--------------------|------------|
| Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct | Low | 3B |
| Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct | Moderate | 7B |
| Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 | High (extensively RL-trained) | 4B |

All models use identical training configurations where possible: batch size of 128 prompts, 8 trajectories per prompt (reduced to 4 for Qwen3 due to long response lengths), 500 training steps total, with evaluation every 50 steps (Section 5, Implementation details). The KL regularization weight is set to $\beta = 0$ following DAPO (Yu et al., 2025), and asymmetric clipping uses $\epsilon_{\text{low}} = 0.2$ and $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.28$. Maximum response length is 8096 tokens for main results (required by LUFFY baseline) and 2048 tokens for other experiments.

**Training Data.** The training set derives from OpenR1-Math-220k, combining prompts from NuminaMath 1.5 and reasoning traces from DeepSeek-R1 (Section 5, Training set). After filtering with Math-Verify to remove incorrectly verified traces, 64k prompts remain. Due to resource constraints, experiments use a subsampled 15k prompt set with DeepSeek-R1 traces under 8,192 tokens. Critically, prompts are **not filtered by pass rate**, preserving a wide difficulty distribution that resembles practical RL training datasets. This design choice matters: if the authors had filtered to only solvable prompts, the advantage collapse problem would be less severe, and SAGE's benefits would be harder to demonstrate.

**Evaluation Benchmarks.** The paper uses eight benchmarks split into two categories (Section 5, Evaluation sets):

**In-distribution (6 benchmarks):**
- AIME24 and AIME25 (MAA Committees, 2024, 2025)
- AMC23 (Li et al., 2024)
- MATH-500 (Hendrycks et al., 2021)
- Minerva Math (Lewkowycz et al., 2022)
- OlympiadBench (He et al., 2024)

**Out-of-distribution (2 benchmarks):**
- GPQA-diamond (Rein et al., 2024)
- MMLU-Pro (Wang et al., 2024)

The out-of-distribution benchmarks assess generalization beyond the math-focused training distribution. Notably, **hints are used only during training**—evaluation uses the no-hint policy $\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)$ with $h = \emptyset$ (Algorithm 1, line 19). This ensures fair comparison with baselines and validates that SAGE does not create test-time dependencies.

**Baselines.** The paper compares against four baselines representing different approaches to sparse-reward RL (Section 5, Baselines):

| Baseline | Mechanism | External Model Required |
|----------|-----------|------------------------|
| **SFT** | Supervised fine-tuning on DeepSeek-R1 traces | Yes (DeepSeek-R1) |
| **GRPO** | Standard group relative policy optimization, no hints | No |
| **LUFFY** | Replaces one on-policy trajectory with correct DeepSeek-R1 trajectory | Yes (DeepSeek-R1) |
| **Scaf-GRPO** | Hints from GPT-5 under low-reasoning-effort setting | Yes (GPT-5) |

SAGE and SAGE-LIGHT are the only methods that **do not require external models**, generating hints from the policy itself. For fair comparison, LUFFY and Scaf-GRPO are reproduced using open-source implementations on the same 15k prompt set, with batch size and training steps aligned (Section 5, Baselines).

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**Overall Performance Gains.** Table 1 presents the primary results across all models and benchmarks. The key metric is average accuracy improvement over the base model (denoted as $\Delta$ in the table).

For **Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct** (the weakest model, most affected by advantage collapse):
- Base model average: 17.8% (in-distribution), 22.5% (out-of-distribution)
- GRPO: 21.9% (+4.1 over base)
- **SAGE: 23.9% (+6.1 over base, +2.0 over GRPO)**
- SAGE-LIGHT: 23.0% (+5.2 over base, +1.1 over GRPO)

For **Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct** (moderate math capability):
- Base model average: 37.8% (in-distribution), 46.7% (out-of-distribution)
- GRPO: 41.1% (+3.3 over base)
- **SAGE: 42.3% (+4.5 over base, +1.2 over GRPO)**
- SAGE-LIGHT: 41.9% (+4.1 over base, +0.8 over GRPO)

For **Qwen3-4B-Instruct** (strongest model, least affected by collapse):
- Base model average: 65.8% (in-distribution), 64.3% (out-of-distribution)
- GRPO: 68.7% (+2.9 over base)
- **SAGE: 70.0% (+4.2 over base, +1.3 over GRPO)**
- SAGE-LIGHT: 68.9% (+3.1 over base, +0.2 over GRPO)

**Key observation:** The performance gap between SAGE and GRPO is largest for the weakest model (Llama-3.2: +2.0) and smallest for the strongest (Qwen3: +1.3). This pattern supports the paper's core hypothesis: SAGE primarily helps when advantage collapse is most severe. Table 2 quantifies this directly.

**Prompt Utilization.** Table 2 measures the percentage of prompts that never provide any training signal (no correct trajectories sampled during entire training):

| Method | Llama-3.2-3B | Qwen2.5-7B | Qwen3-4B |
|--------|-------------|------------|----------|
| Base | 56.9% | 29.8% | 21.8% |
| GRPO | 40.2% | 10.3% | 1.3% |
| **SAGE** | **30.0%** | **8.2%** | **1.0%** |

For Llama-3.2, SAGE reduces unused prompts by 10.2 percentage points compared to GRPO (40.2% → 30.0%). This directly correlates with the larger performance gain (+2.0 accuracy). For Qwen3, the improvement is only 0.3 percentage points (1.3% → 1.0%), yet SAGE still achieves +1.3 accuracy gain. Section 5.1 explains: "These hard prompts play a critical role in RL," meaning even marginal improvements in utilizing the hardest examples yield disproportionate accuracy gains. This aligns with prior work (Xiong et al., 2025b; Yu et., 2025) favoring RL on prompt sets with lower pass rates.

**Out-of-Distribution Generalization.** The out-of-distribution results (GPQA and MMLU-Pro) show similar patterns to in-distribution benchmarks. SAGE achieves the best OOD performance across all models:
- Llama-3.2: SAGE 34.0% vs. GRPO 33.1% (+0.9)
- Qwen2.5: SAGE 48.6% vs. GRPO 47.4% (+1.2)
- Qwen3: SAGE 65.2% vs. GRPO 64.5% (+0.7)

This indicates SAGE improves general reasoning capabilities rather than overfitting to the training distribution. The paper notes this is significant because SAGE does not modify the test-time input distribution—improvements come from better training dynamics, not test-time augmentation.

**Comparison to External-Guidance Baselines.** SAGE outperforms both LUFFY and Scaf-GRPO despite not requiring external models:

- **LUFFY** underperforms the base model on Llama-3.2 (14.7% vs. 17.8%, $\Delta = -3.1$) and Qwen3 (60.6% vs. 65.8%, $\Delta = -5.2$). Section 5.1 attributes this to off-policy instability: "LUFFY exhibits the second-largest degree of off-policy behavior... this off-policy setting introduces training instability due to the misalignment between the policy model and the stronger model." Figure 4 shows LUFFY has "excessively high entropy" for Llama and "very low rewards at the beginning" for Qwen3.

- **Scaf-GRPO** performs better than LUFFY but worse than SAGE. On Llama-3.2, Scaf-GRPO achieves 21.5% vs. SAGE's 23.9%. Section 5.1 notes Scaf-GRPO has "the lowest entropy among all methods, indicating limited exploration"—external hints may reveal excessive information, constraining the model's learning trajectory.

**SFT Performance.** SFT yields the worst performance across all models, underperforming even the base LLM. For Llama-3.2, SFT achieves 8.2% average accuracy vs. 17.8% for the base model ($\Delta = -9.6$). Section 5.1 explains: "SFT yields the worst performance... due to its tendency to overfit training data. In contrast, SAGE preserves the RL characteristics, selectively sharpening the model's distribution to correct trajectories." This validates that RL with proper exploration (preserved by SAGE) outperforms pure imitation learning on reasoning tasks.

### 5.3 Training Dynamics Analysis

**Reward Trajectories.** Figure 4 plots training rewards over 500 steps for all methods. The paper cautions: "For the training rewards, one should focus on the trend instead of the value, since adding hint (SAGE and Scaf-GRPO) modifies the prompt difficulty, and using a correct off-policy trajectory (LUFFY) increases the reward." Key observations:

- **SAGE** shows stable, monotonic reward growth across all models, indicating consistent learning without collapse.
- **LUFFY** shows the most instability: "very high entropy for Llama and a very low reward at the beginning of training for Qwen3."
- **Scaf-GRPO** shows the lowest entropy, suggesting limited exploration.
- **GRPO** shows slower growth than SAGE, particularly for Llama-3.2 and Qwen2.5.

**Response Length Growth.** Figure 4 also tracks response length over training. SAGE exhibits "faster response-length growth than GRPO for both Llama-3.2 and Qwen2.5, due to learning from hard prompts that fail to provide any signal under GRPO" (Section 5.1). Longer responses typically indicate more detailed reasoning chains. For Qwen3, response length growth is similar between SAGE and GRPO, consistent with the smaller performance gap (Qwen3 already handles most prompts without collapse).

**Entropy Dynamics.** Figure 4 shows entropy over training steps:
- **LUFFY** on Llama-3.2 displays "excessively high entropy and highly oscillatory response lengths," indicating instability from off-policy mismatch.
- **Scaf-GRPO** maintains the lowest entropy across all methods, suggesting hints constrain exploration.
- **SAGE** retains entropy "comparable to GRPO" (Section 5.1, footnote 3 notes GRPO entropy on Llama-3.2 is "abnormally high"), indicating healthy exploration dynamics.

**Hint Usage Over Time.** Figure 6 plots the number of prompts using hints during training on Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct. The curve shows monotonic decrease: "the LLM use less and less hint during the training. It indicates that self-hinting indeed enhances RL. LLM becomes more and more powerful and can gradually solve difficult problems without the help of a hint" (Section 5.2). This validates the automatic curriculum hypothesis: as the policy improves, fewer prompts trigger the degeneracy condition, and hint strength naturally fades without manual scheduling.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies

**On-Policy vs. Off-Policy Optimization.** Figure 5 ablates whether the policy gradient matches the sampling context. The off-policy variant rolls out with hint $h$ but optimizes $\log \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)$ (dropping $h$ inside the log-probability). Results on Qwen3-4B-Instruct:
- **On-policy SAGE:** 59.2%
- **Off-policy variant:** 56.5%
- **Single hint level (online $\ell=2$):** 58.3%

The 2.7 percentage point drop validates Section 4.1's claim: "If one instead samples $\tau \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x, h)$ but evaluates $\log \pi_\theta(\tau \mid x)$... the update no longer corresponds to the gradient of any on-policy objective under the sampling process. In practice this mismatch behaves like an off-policy update and is markedly less stable under sparse rewards."

**Adaptive Scheduling vs. Fixed Hint Level.** Figure 5 compares SAGE's adaptive scheduling against using a constant hint level throughout training:
- **SAGE (adaptive):** 59.2%
- **Fixed $\ell=2$:** 58.3%

The +0.9 gain confirms that "progressively weakening hints as the model improves (down to $\ell=0$) enables more effective learning than static assistance" (Section 3.4.6). This design allows the model to "gradually solve difficult problems without the help of a hint" as Figure 6 demonstrates.

**Online vs. Offline Self-Hinting.** Figure 3 (left) and Table C.1 compare hint generation strategies at different hint levels. At $\ell=2$ with extended training (400 steps, denoted with $*$ in Table C.1):
- **Online self-hinting:** 58.3%
- **Offline self-hinting (multiple hints):** 56.3%
- **Offline self-hinting (single hint):** 55.4%
- **GPT-5 external hints:** 56.5%

Online self-hinting outperforms offline by 2.0 percentage points despite offline using 10 diverse hints per prompt (Section 5.2, "Offline with more hints"). Section 5.2 concludes: "hint generated in an online manner offer more benefit than diversity." Proposition 3.3 provides theoretical justification: the optimal hint distribution $q^\star_\theta$ depends on current policy parameters $\theta$, so a fixed generator becomes miscalibrated as training progresses.

**SAGE vs. SAGE-LIGHT.** Table 3 reports training time on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct:
- **GRPO:** 1.0× (25.3 hours)
- **SAGE-LIGHT:** 1.2×
- **SAGE:** 2.3×
- **LUFFY:** 1.2×
- **Scaf-GRPO:** 1.5×

SAGE-LIGHT achieves nearly comparable performance to SAGE (e.g., +10.7 vs. +11.5 average gain on Llama-3.2, Table 1) with significantly lower overhead. Section 5.2 explains the trade-off: "SAGE-LIGHT is compute-efficient because it updates the hint strength only at the epoch level (no additional computation for rollouts), but it can react slowly to sudden reward collapse. SAGE is more reactive and directly targets GRPO's failure mode via a no-positives trigger, at the cost of additional probe rollouts." Practitioners can choose based on efficiency requirements—both variants consistently outperform baselines.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

**Strengths of the Experimental Design.**

1. **Multiple models and benchmarks.** The paper evaluates three models across eight benchmarks, reducing the risk that results are specific to one architecture or task distribution. The consistent pattern (SAGE > GRPO across all models) strengthens confidence in the method's generality.

2. **Ablation studies validate key claims.** The on-policy vs. off-policy ablation (Figure 5) directly tests Section 4.1's theoretical claim about conditioning context. The online vs. offline hint ablation validates Proposition 3.3's claim about policy-dependent optimality. These are not just performance comparisons—they test the mechanistic hypotheses underlying SAGE.

3. **Training dynamics provide mechanistic evidence.** Figure 4 and Figure 6 show not just final accuracy but *how* learning progresses. The decreasing hint usage over time (Figure 6) confirms the automatic curriculum hypothesis. The stable reward growth for SAGE vs. instability for LUFFY validates the on-policy stability claim.

4. **Prompt utilization metrics directly measure the core problem.** Table 2 quantifies the exact pathology SAGE addresses: prompts without training signal. The correlation between reduced unused prompts and improved accuracy (especially for Llama-3.2) provides direct evidence that advantage collapse is the bottleneck SAGE resolves.

**Limitations and Open Questions.**

1. **Training cost is substantial.** SAGE requires 2.3× the training time of GRPO (Table 3). While this is a one-time cost (no test-time overhead), it may be prohibitive for large-scale deployments. SAGE-LIGHT reduces this to 1.2× but with slightly lower performance. The paper does not analyze whether the accuracy gains justify the compute cost in practical settings.

2. **Hint generation quality is not independently evaluated.** The paper assumes the policy can generate useful hints from reference solutions, but does not evaluate hint quality directly. Appendix B shows the prompt template, but there is no human evaluation or automated metric for hint helpfulness. It is possible that some hints are misleading or uninformative, and the method's success depends on the hint generator's reliability.

3. **Limited analysis of failure cases.** The paper does not systematically analyze prompts where SAGE fails to improve over GRPO. For Qwen3, the improvement is only +1.3 accuracy despite 2.3× training cost. Understanding when SAGE provides diminishing returns would help practitioners decide when to use it.

4. **Reference solution dependency.** SAGE requires reference solutions $\tau^\star$ for hint generation (Section 2.2). This limits applicability to tasks where ground-truth solutions are available. The paper does not discuss how SAGE would work in settings where only a verifier exists without reference solutions (e.g., some code generation or interactive tasks).

5. **Hyperparameter sensitivity.** The paper sets $L=3$ hint levels and $\alpha=0.35$ threshold for SAGE-LIGHT (Section 5, Implementation details), but does not explore sensitivity to these choices. Different tasks or models might require different hint granularity or activation thresholds.

**Overall Assessment.** The experiments convincingly support the paper's core claims:

- **Claim 1:** SAGE improves over GRPO on sparse-reward RL tasks. **Supported:** Consistent gains across all models and benchmarks (Table 1).
- **Claim 2:** Improvements come from better prompt utilization. **Supported:** Table 2 shows reduced unused prompts, correlating with accuracy gains.
- **Claim 3:** On-policy conditioning is essential. **Supported:** Off-policy ablation drops 2.7 points (Figure 5).
- **Claim 4:** Online self-hinting outperforms fixed hints. **Supported:** Online beats offline by 2.0 points despite offline using more diverse hints (Figure 5, Section 5.2).
- **Claim 5:** No test-time overhead. **Supported:** Deployment uses $\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)$ with $h = \emptyset$ (Algorithm 1, line 19).

The main caveat is the training cost trade-off. For applications where training compute is abundant but inference latency is critical, SAGE is compelling. For resource-constrained settings, SAGE-LIGHT offers a middle ground. The paper would benefit from more analysis of when the accuracy gains justify the compute cost, but the core technical contributions are well-validated empirically.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While SAGE demonstrates consistent improvements over standard GRPO across multiple models and benchmarks, the approach carries important assumptions, constraints, and open questions that practitioners must consider before adoption. This section critically examines where SAGE may not apply, what costs it imposes, and which weaknesses remain unaddressed.

### 6.1 Computational Overhead: Training-Time Cost vs. Test-Time Savings

The most immediate trade-off is **training compute**. Table 3 quantifies the overhead on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct:

| Method | Relative Training Time | Absolute Time |
|--------|----------------------|---------------|
| GRPO | 1.0× | 25.3 hours |
| SAGE-LIGHT | 1.2× | ~30.4 hours |
| **SAGE** | **2.3×** | **~58.2 hours** |
| LUFFY | 1.2× | ~30.4 hours |
| Scaf-GRPO | 1.5× | ~38.0 hours |

**Why the overhead exists.** Section 5.2 explains that SAGE incurs higher cost because "for highly complex prompts, SAGE may sample hints across multiple levels (from $l = 0$ to $l = 3$), which increases computational overhead." The group-degeneracy trigger (Algorithm 1, lines 9-13) may require multiple probe rollouts at different hint levels before finding a non-degenerate group. In contrast, SAGE-LIGHT samples from only a single hint level per epoch based on prior accuracy, reducing overhead to 1.2×.

**The trade-off decision.** Section 5.2 notes that "SAGE-LIGHT achieves slightly lower accuracy but improves efficiency." For Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, SAGE achieves +11.5 average gain while SAGE-LIGHT achieves +10.7 (Table 1)—a marginal 0.8 percentage point difference for nearly half the training cost. Practitioners must decide whether the additional accuracy justifies the compute investment.

**Test-time advantage.** The paper emphasizes that SAGE incurs **zero inference overhead** (Algorithm 1, line 19: "Deployment: set $\ell = 0$ so $h = \emptyset$"). This contrasts with methods requiring external model calls or retrieved context at inference. However, the paper does not quantify whether the one-time training cost is amortized favorably compared to perpetual inference costs of alternative approaches. For deployment scenarios with high inference volume, this trade-off likely favors SAGE; for one-off training runs, the 2.3× overhead may be prohibitive.

### 6.2 Reference Solution Dependency: Applicability Constraints

SAGE requires **reference solutions $\tau^\star$** during training to generate hints. Section 2.2 states: "When a reference trajectory $\tau^\star$ is available during training, we generate a hint $h$ as a lossy compression of $\tau^\star$."

**Tasks where this applies:**
- Mathematics problems with verified solutions (the paper's evaluation domain)
- Code generation with unit tests and reference implementations
- Formal reasoning tasks with ground-truth proofs

**Tasks where this does NOT apply:**
- Open-ended generation (creative writing, dialogue)
- Tasks with only a verifier but no reference solution (e.g., some code optimization problems)
- Interactive environments where ground-truth trajectories are unavailable

Section 5 (Training set) notes the training data derives from OpenR1-Math-220k with "reasoning traces generated by DeepSeek-R1." The paper does not discuss how SAGE would function if reference solutions were noisy, incomplete, or generated by a weaker model. This is a meaningful constraint: many real-world RL applications have verifiers but lack high-quality reference solutions.

**Potential workaround not explored.** The paper does not investigate whether hints could be generated from **successful on-policy rollouts** rather than reference solutions. If the model occasionally solves a prompt without hints, could those successful trajectories serve as $\tau^\star$ for future hint generation? This would extend SAGE to verifier-only settings but is not addressed.

### 6.3 Diminishing Returns on Capable Models

The performance gains from SAGE are **inversely correlated with model capability**. Table 1 shows:

| Model | Base Accuracy | GRPO Gain | SAGE Gain over GRPO |
|-------|--------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct | 17.8% | +4.1% | **+2.0%** |
| Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct | 37.8% | +3.3% | **+1.2%** |
| Qwen3-4B-Instruct | 65.8% | +2.9% | **+1.3%** |

**Why this pattern occurs.** Section 5.1 explains: "This effect is particularly pronounced for the weaker LLM, Llama-3.2: by leveraging self-generated hints, SAGE successfully utilizes 10% more prompts, leading to the largest performance improvement over GRPO." Table 2 confirms that Qwen3 already has only 1.3% of prompts without training signal under standard GRPO, leaving little room for improvement (SAGE reduces this to 1.0%).

**Implication for practitioners.** If you are training a already-capable model on a relatively easy prompt distribution, SAGE's benefits may not justify the 2.3× training cost. The method is most valuable when:
1. The model is weak relative to task difficulty
2. The prompt distribution contains many hard examples
3. Standard GRPO leaves significant prompts unused

Section 5.1 acknowledges this: "For the stronger model, Qwen3, SAGE behaves more similarly to GRPO, with nearly identical prompt utilization. Nevertheless, despite using only 0.3% more prompts, SAGE still achieves a +1.3 accuracy gain over GRPO." The paper argues these hard prompts are critical, but the cost-benefit ratio is less favorable than for weaker models.

### 6.4 Hint Quality and Calibration: Unverified Assumptions

The paper assumes the policy can generate **useful hints** from reference solutions, but does not independently evaluate hint quality.

**What we know:**
- Appendix B provides the exact prompt template for hint generation (a "tutoring assistant" that generates 3 levels progressively)
- Section 4.2 states hints are "procedure-only plan aligned with the reference trajectory"
- Figure 3 shows online self-hinting outperforms offline, suggesting calibration matters more than raw hint quality

**What we don't know:**
- No human evaluation of hint helpfulness or clarity
- No automated metric for hint quality independent of downstream accuracy
- No analysis of failure cases where hints are misleading or uninformative

Section 5.2 ("Offline with more hints") notes that generating 10 diverse hints offline improves over single offline hints by +0.9 points, but still underperforms online self-hinting by 2.0 points. The paper concludes "hint generated in an online manner offer more benefit than diversity," but this does not rule out that some hints are ineffective.

**Risk of hint degradation.** If the policy's hint-generation capability degrades during training (e.g., due to catastrophic forgetting or optimization instability), the entire curriculum mechanism could fail. The paper does not monitor hint quality over training steps—only downstream accuracy. A concerning scenario: the model could learn to generate hints that artificially inflate success rates without improving actual reasoning capability (a form of Goodhart's law).

### 6.5 Hyperparameter Sensitivity: Unexplored Design Choices

Several hyperparameters are fixed without sensitivity analysis:

| Hyperparameter | Value Used | Purpose |
|---------------|------------|---------|
| $L$ (max hint levels) | 3 | Controls hint granularity |
| $\alpha$ (SAGE-LIGHT threshold) | 0.35 | Determines when to increase hint strength |
| $G$ (group size) | 8 (4 for Qwen3) | Affects gate-opening probability |
| $\epsilon_{\text{low}}, \epsilon_{\text{high}}$ | 0.2, 0.28 | PPO-style clipping bounds |

**Why this matters.** Proposition 3.2 shows the gate-opening probability $\text{Pr}[s > 0 \mid x, h] = 1 - (1 - p)^G - p^G$ depends critically on $G$. If $G$ is too small, even hinted prompts may not produce mixed outcomes. If $G$ is too large, compute waste increases. The paper does not explore how SAGE's benefits scale with different group sizes.

Similarly, the threshold $\alpha = 0.35$ for SAGE-LIGHT determines when hints activate. A higher threshold would activate hints more aggressively (more compute, potentially faster learning); a lower threshold would be more conservative. Section 4.3 describes the mechanism but provides no ablation on $\alpha$.

**Hint level granularity.** The paper uses exactly 3 hint levels (minimal, medium, detailed in Appendix B). It is unclear whether finer granularity (e.g., 5 levels) would enable smoother curriculum progression, or whether coarser granularity (2 levels) would suffice with less overhead.

### 6.6 Domain Generalization: Math-Centric Evaluation

All six in-distribution benchmarks are **mathematics-focused** (AIME, AMC, MATH-500, Minerva, Olympiad). The two out-of-distribution benchmarks (GPQA-diamond, MMLU-Pro) include non-math tasks but are evaluated only for final accuracy, not for domain-specific learning dynamics.

**Unanswered questions:**
- Does SAGE improve reasoning in non-mathematical domains (e.g., code generation, scientific reasoning, logical puzzles)?
- Are the hint templates in Appendix B transferable to other domains, or do they require domain-specific redesign?
- Does the "procedure-only plan" hint structure work for tasks without clear procedural solutions (e.g., creative writing, open-ended analysis)?

Section 6 (Related Work) notes that "leveraging intermediate guidance, such as plans or gold solutions, has a rich history in RL," but does not discuss whether SAGE's specific implementation generalizes beyond math. The hint generation prompt in Appendix B explicitly references "mathematical problems" implicitly through its examples, suggesting potential domain specificity.

### 6.7 Off-Policy Sensitivity: Fragility of Training Dynamics

Section 4.1 emphasizes that on-policy conditioning is essential: "If one instead samples $\tau \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x, h)$ but evaluates $\log \pi_\theta(\tau \mid x)$ (i.e., dropping $h$ inside the log-prob), the update no longer corresponds to the gradient of any on-policy objective." Figure 5 confirms this: the off-policy variant drops from 59.2% to 56.5% accuracy.

**Implication:** SAGE is **sensitive to implementation correctness**. Unlike methods that tolerate some off-policy mismatch (e.g., experience replay buffers), SAGE requires strict alignment between sampling context and loss computation. This increases the risk of subtle bugs that could degrade performance without obvious error signals.

**Comparison to LUFFY.** Section 5.1 notes LUFFY exhibits "training instability due to the misalignment between the policy model and the stronger model." SAGE avoids this by using self-generated hints, but the ablation shows that even small conditioning mismatches (dropping $h$ from the loss) cause measurable degradation. Practitioners implementing SAGE must ensure the hint context is correctly threaded through both sampling and gradient computation.

### 6.8 Summary of Trade-offs

| Dimension | SAGE Advantage | SAGE Limitation |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Training Cost** | One-time investment | 2.3× overhead vs. GRPO |
| **Inference Cost** | Zero overhead ($h = \emptyset$) | N/A |
| **Data Requirements** | Uses hard prompts effectively | Requires reference solutions $\tau^\star$ |
| **Model Capability** | Largest gains on weaker models | Diminishing returns on strong models |
| **Implementation** | Clean on-policy objective | Sensitive to conditioning mismatches |
| **Domain Scope** | Validated on math reasoning | Untested on non-procedural tasks |
| **Hyperparameters** | Simple scheduling rules | No sensitivity analysis provided |

**Bottom line for practitioners.** SAGE is most compelling when:
1. Training compute is available but inference latency/cost is critical
2. The model is weak relative to task difficulty (significant advantage collapse expected)
3. Reference solutions are available for the training distribution
4. The task has verifiable objectives with procedural solution structure

SAGE is less compelling when:
1. Training budget is severely constrained
2. The model already solves most prompts without collapse (e.g., Qwen3 on this dataset)
3. Only verifiers exist without reference solutions
4. The task domain is open-ended without clear procedural hints

The paper's contributions are well-validated within its evaluated scope, but the method's generality beyond math reasoning and its cost-effectiveness in resource-constrained settings remain open questions for future work.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how SAGE reshapes the approach to sparse-reward reinforcement learning for language models, identifies concrete research directions it opens, and provides actionable guidance for practitioners considering adoption. We move beyond what the paper demonstrates to what it enables.

### 7.1 Shifting the RL Optimization Paradigm

SAGE introduces a fundamental reframing of what sparse-reward RL optimization should target. Traditional RL objectives focus exclusively on maximizing expected reward $\mathbb{E}[R]$. SAGE demonstrates that **maximizing the probability of receiving a learning signal** is equally critical—and sometimes more impactful.

**The learnability gate concept.** Section 3 formalizes training as a "gated" procedure where updates occur only when within-group variance exists ($s > 0$). The gate-opening probability is:

$$\text{Pr}[s > 0 \mid x, h] = 1 - (1 - p_\theta(x, h))^G - p_\theta(x, h)^G$$

This insight shifts optimization from purely reward-focused to **variance-aware**. The implication extends beyond GRPO: any policy-gradient method relying on within-batch variance for advantage estimation faces similar collapse under sparse rewards. SAGE's hinting mechanism provides a template for addressing this across RL algorithms.

**Privileged information without deployment cost.** The paper demonstrates that training-time privileged information need not create test-time dependencies. Algorithm 1, line 19 explicitly states: "Deployment: set $\ell = 0$ so $h = \emptyset$". This contrasts with methods requiring external model calls, retrieved context, or architectural modifications at inference. The design principle—**asymmetric information access between training and deployment**—could apply to other domains:

- Training with chain-of-thought supervision, deploying with direct answers
- Training with oracle verifiers, deploying with learned critics
- Training with multi-turn interaction, deploying with single-turn inference

**Self-generated guidance outperforms external teachers.** Figure 3 and Table C.1 show online self-hinting (58.3%) outperforms external GPT-5 hints (56.5%) despite presumably lower raw hint quality. Section 5.2 concludes: "hint generated in an online manner offer more benefit than diversity." This challenges the assumption that stronger external models always provide better training signals. The key insight: **calibration to the learner's current capability matters more than absolute hint quality**. This could reshape how distillation, curriculum learning, and data augmentation are designed for LLMs.

### 7.2 Concrete Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions and implicitly suggests others through its limitations. We organize these by feasibility and potential impact.

#### 7.2.1 Immediate Extensions (Low-Hanging Fruit)

**Hint generation without reference solutions.** Section 6.2 notes SAGE requires reference solutions $\tau^\star$ for hint generation. A natural extension: could hints be generated from **successful on-policy rollouts** instead? When the model occasionally solves a prompt without hints (even at low probability), those trajectories could serve as $\tau^\star$ for future hint generation. This would extend SAGE to verifier-only settings where ground-truth solutions are unavailable.

**Adaptive hint level granularity.** The paper fixes $L = 3$ hint levels without ablation (Section 5, Implementation details). Research could explore:
- Finer granularity (5-10 levels) for smoother curriculum progression
- Continuous hint strength rather than discrete levels
- Per-prompt optimal level prediction rather than reactive scheduling

**SAGE-LIGHT optimization.** Table 3 shows SAGE-LIGHT achieves nearly comparable performance to SAGE (+10.7 vs. +11.5 on Llama-3.2) at 1.2× vs. 2.3× training cost. Further optimization could close this gap:
- More responsive epoch-level triggers (e.g., rolling window instead of full epoch)
- Hybrid schemes that use SAGE's trigger only for the hardest prompts
- Early stopping for prompts that stabilize at certain hint levels

#### 7.2.2 Domain Generalization (Medium-Term)

**Non-mathematical reasoning tasks.** All six in-distribution benchmarks are mathematics-focused (Section 5, Evaluation sets). Key questions:
- Does the "procedure-only plan" hint structure (Appendix B) transfer to code generation, scientific reasoning, or logical puzzles?
- Do different domains require different hint templates or strength definitions?
- How does SAGE perform on tasks without clear procedural solutions (e.g., creative writing, open-ended analysis)?

**Interactive and multi-turn environments.** The paper evaluates single-turn question-answer tasks. Extending to multi-turn settings (e.g., tool use, agent workflows) raises new questions:
- Should hints be provided per-turn or for the entire trajectory?
- How does hint conditioning interact with environment state changes?
- Can hints guide exploration in environments with delayed rewards?

**Verifier-only tasks.** Many practical RL applications have verifiers but no reference solutions (e.g., code optimization, some robotics tasks). Research could explore:
- Generating hints from partial successful trajectories
- Using verifier feedback to construct synthetic reference solutions
- Learning hint generators from verifier signals alone

#### 7.2.3 Theoretical Foundations (Long-Term)

**Generalizing the gate-opening analysis.** Section 3 analyzes GRPO's advantage collapse under Bernoulli rewards. Extensions could cover:
- Continuous or multi-level reward distributions
- Alternative advantage normalization schemes (not just standardization)
- Other policy-gradient estimators beyond GRPO

**Optimal hint distribution characterization.** Proposition 3.3 shows the optimal hint distribution $q^\star_\theta$ depends on current policy parameters $\theta$, but does not characterize how $q^\star_\theta$ evolves during training. Understanding this trajectory could enable:
- Predictive hint scheduling (anticipating when hints will be needed)
- Automated hint level selection without manual thresholds
- Theoretical bounds on sample efficiency improvements

**Connection to curriculum learning theory.** SAGE's automatic curriculum emerges from variance triggers rather than pre-defined difficulty metrics. This could connect to formal curriculum learning frameworks:
- When does signal-driven curriculum outperform performance-based curriculum?
- What are the convergence guarantees for variance-triggered scheduling?
- How does SAGE's curriculum compare to self-paced learning or teacher-student frameworks?

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

SAGE's design makes it suitable for specific deployment scenarios. We categorize by application domain and resource constraints.

#### 7.3.1 High-Value Application Domains

**Mathematical reasoning models.** This is the paper's evaluated domain, and SAGE shows consistent gains across all three models (Table 1). Organizations building math-focused LLMs (e.g., for education, tutoring, or research assistance) should prioritize SAGE adoption, especially for:
- Models below 10B parameters (where advantage collapse is most severe)
- Training distributions with wide difficulty ranges
- Scenarios where inference latency is critical (zero test-time overhead)

**Code generation with unit tests.** Code generation shares key characteristics with the evaluated math tasks:
- Binary verifiability (tests pass/fail)
- Reference solutions often available (existing implementations)
- Procedural hint structure transfers naturally (e.g., "use dynamic programming" rather than full code)

SAGE could improve sample efficiency on hard coding problems where standard RL leaves many prompts unused.

**Scientific reasoning and formal verification.** Tasks with clear correctness criteria and reference solutions (e.g., theorem proving, constraint satisfaction, formal methods) align well with SAGE's requirements. The hint structure could guide proof strategies or constraint decomposition without revealing complete solutions.

#### 7.3.2 Resource-Constrained Deployment Scenarios

**Edge deployment with strict latency budgets.** Since SAGE incurs zero inference overhead, it is ideal for scenarios where:
- Inference must complete within strict time limits
- External model calls are prohibited (cost, privacy, or connectivity)
- Model size cannot increase to accommodate hint processing

**High-volume inference pipelines.** For applications serving millions of queries daily, even small per-query latency increases compound significantly. SAGE's training-time-only intervention avoids this perpetual cost, making the 2.3× training overhead (Table 3) a favorable one-time investment.

**Multi-tenant or shared infrastructure.** When inference infrastructure is shared across multiple models or applications, avoiding per-model hint infrastructure simplifies deployment and reduces operational complexity.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

Based on the paper's experimental design and limitations, we provide concrete recommendations for practitioners considering SAGE adoption.

#### 7.4.1 When to Prefer SAGE Over Alternatives

| Scenario | Recommended Method | Rationale |
|----------|-------------------|-----------|
| Weak model ($<10$B) on hard prompts | **SAGE** | Largest gains where advantage collapse is severe (Table 2: 40.2% → 30.0% unused prompts for Llama-3.2) |
| Training compute constrained | **SAGE-LIGHT** | 1.2× vs. 2.3× overhead with marginal accuracy difference (Table 1: +10.7 vs. +11.5 on Llama-3.2) |
| Strong model on easy prompts | **GRPO** | Diminishing returns (Qwen3: only 1.3% unused prompts under GRPO) |
| No reference solutions available | **GRPO or LUFFY** | SAGE requires $\tau^\star$ for hint generation (Section 2.2) |
| Inference latency critical | **SAGE** | Zero test-time overhead vs. methods requiring external calls |
| External model access available | **Compare SAGE vs. Scaf-GRPO** | SAGE outperforms external hints (58.3% vs. 56.5% in Table C.1) despite no external dependency |

#### 7.4.2 Implementation Checklist

**Required infrastructure:**
- [ ] Reference solutions $\tau^\star$ for training prompts (Section 2.2)
- [ ] Hint generation pipeline (Appendix B provides prompt templates)
- [ ] Per-prompt hint level tracking (Algorithm 1, line 1)
- [ ] Modified GRPO loss to condition on hints (Section 2.3, Equation 5)

**Key hyperparameters to tune:**
- [ ] Maximum hint levels $L$ (paper uses 3; consider 2-5 based on task complexity)
- [ ] SAGE-LIGHT threshold $\alpha$ (paper uses 0.35; higher = more aggressive hinting)
- [ ] Group size $G$ (paper uses 8; affects gate-opening probability per Proposition 3.2)
- [ ] Probe group size for SAGE trigger (Section 4.3, Scheme 2)

**Validation metrics to monitor:**
- [ ] Percentage of prompts without training signal (Table 2 metric)
- [ ] Hint usage over training steps (should decrease; Figure 6)
- [ ] Response length growth (indicates reasoning complexity; Figure 4)
- [ ] Entropy dynamics (should remain stable; Figure 4)

#### 7.4.3 Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**Off-policy conditioning mismatch.** Section 4.1 emphasizes: "If one instead samples $\tau \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x, h)$ but evaluates $\log \pi_\theta(\tau \mid x)$... the update no longer corresponds to the gradient of any on-policy objective." Figure 5 shows this drops accuracy from 59.2% to 56.5%. Ensure hints are included in **both** sampling and loss computation.

**Hint strength too high.** Proposition 3.2 shows gate probability is maximized at $p_\theta = 0.5$. Hints that push success rate too close to 1.0 also close the gate (all-one degeneracy). Monitor per-level success rates and adjust hint templates if certain levels are too informative.

**Insufficient training steps.** Figure 3 (right) shows training without hints "degrades the performance as the training goes" on hard prompts. SAGE requires adequate training steps to realize benefits—the paper uses 500 steps with evaluation every 50 steps (Section 5, Implementation details).

**Reference solution quality.** The paper filters training data using Math-Verify to remove incorrectly verified traces (Section 5, Training set). Poor reference solutions will generate misleading hints, potentially harming rather than helping learning.

### 7.5 Broader Impact on RL for Language Models

SAGE's contributions extend beyond its immediate performance gains. The work establishes several principles that could influence future RL research for LLMs:

**Finite-sample awareness.** The paper explicitly frames advantage collapse as a "finite-sample pathology" (Section 1). This encourages researchers to analyze RL algorithms through the lens of estimator behavior under realistic sampling budgets, not just asymptotic convergence properties.

**Training-test asymmetry as a design pattern.** SAGE demonstrates that asymmetric information access between training and deployment can be leveraged without creating deployment dependencies. This pattern could apply to other training interventions: auxiliary losses, privileged critics, or multi-task supervision that is removed at inference.

**Self-referential learning loops.** By using the policy itself to generate hints, SAGE creates a self-referential system where the model teaches itself using its own evolving understanding. This contrasts with static distillation or fixed curriculum approaches and could inspire similar self-improvement mechanisms in other domains.

**Variance as a first-class optimization target.** Rather than treating variance as noise to be reduced, SAGE treats it as a signal to be maintained. This reframing could influence how exploration, regularization, and curriculum design are approached in future work.

The code is available at `https://github.com/BaohaoLiao/SAGE`, enabling direct reproduction and extension. Given the consistent improvements across models and benchmarks, SAGE represents a meaningful step forward in making sparse-reward RL practical for language model alignment—particularly for scenarios where inference efficiency and deployment simplicity are paramount.
