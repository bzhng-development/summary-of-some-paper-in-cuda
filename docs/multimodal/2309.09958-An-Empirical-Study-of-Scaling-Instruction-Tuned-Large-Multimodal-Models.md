## 1. Executive Summary

This empirical study addresses the lack of exploration into scaling open-source Large Multimodal Models (LMM) beyond 13B parameters by systematically evaluating LLaVA scaled to 33B, 65B, and 70B models. The authors demonstrate that increasing model size, utilizing higher image resolutions (336×336), and mixing language instruction data (ShareGPT) with multimodal data (LLaVA-80K) consistently enhances performance on benchmarks like MM-VET, where the 65B model achieves a score of 36.4 compared to 32.9 for the 13B baseline. Significantly, the work establishes that parameter-efficient tuning methods like LoRA/QLoRA match full fine-tuning performance and reveals that visual instruction tuning can improve pure language capabilities, such as raising the MMLU score to 65.1 on a 70B model.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this study, we must first establish the current landscape of **Large Multimodal Models (LMM)** and identify the specific barriers preventing their broader adoption and understanding. This paper does not propose a new model architecture; instead, it functions as an empirical investigation into how existing architectures behave when scaled beyond the limits of typical academic research.

### The Rise of Visual Instruction Tuning
The field of multimodal AI has recently shifted towards **visual instruction tuning**. In this paradigm, a pre-trained image encoder (which understands visuals) is connected to a large language model (LLM) via a simple projection layer. The combined system is then fine-tuned on datasets containing images paired with instructional conversations. This approach allows the model to learn not just to recognize objects, but to reason about them and follow user commands.

Prior to this work, open-source projects like **LLaVA** (Large Language and Vision Assistant) [12] and **MiniGPT-4** [20] demonstrated that this method could yield surprisingly capable models. For instance, the original LLaVA study showed that training a `$7B$` parameter LLM with multimodal data for just 3 hours on 8 A100 GPUs could reproduce some capabilities previously seen only in proprietary systems like GPT-4 [14]. This success sparked a wave of research, establishing visual instruction tuning as a prominent topic with numerous new models and benchmarks emerging rapidly.

### The Scaling Gap
Despite this progress, a critical gap exists in the literature regarding **model scale**. As noted in the Introduction, "the high compute cost has led most existing studies to utilize `$7B$` and `$13B$` LLMs." Consequently, the impact of significantly scaling up the language component of an LMM to `$33B$`, `$65B$`, or `$70B$` parameters remains largely unexplored.

This gap matters for two key reasons:
*   **Theoretical Uncertainty:** It is not guaranteed that scaling laws observed in pure language models apply directly to multimodal models. The vision encoder (which processes the image) might become a bottleneck, limiting the benefits of a larger language backbone. The authors aim to verify if "scaling LMM consistently enhances model performance," as stated in the Abstract.
*   **Performance Ceilings:** Smaller models may lack the inherent knowledge or reasoning depth required for complex real-world tasks. By restricting research to `$13B$` models or smaller, the community risks underestimating the potential of open-source LMMs. The paper positions itself to "establish stronger baselines for future research" by pushing these size limits.

### Efficiency and Accessibility
A secondary motivation drives the methodological choices in this study: **accessibility**. Training and fine-tuning models at the `$65B$` scale is prohibitively expensive for most researchers. The Introduction highlights the need to explore "parameter-efficient training methods such as LoRA/QLoRA."

*   **LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation)** and **QLoRA (Quantized LoRA)** are techniques that freeze the main model weights and train only a small set of additional parameters.
*   The motivation here is practical: if these efficient methods can match the performance of **full-model fine-tuning** (where all parameters are updated), it would drastically reduce the computational barrier to entry.
*   The paper seeks to quantify the "trade-off between the training cost and model performance," ensuring that future researchers know whether they must incur the cost of full fine-tuning or if efficient methods suffice.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work
This study distinguishes itself from prior work in three specific ways, as outlined in the Introduction and Abstract:

1.  **Scale:** While works like LLaVA [12] and MiniGPT-4 [20] focused on `$7B$` to `$13B$` models, this paper explicitly targets the `$33B$` to `$70B$` range.
2.  **Data Strategy:** The authors investigate **data mixing**, specifically combining multimodal instruction data with language-only instruction data (such as ShareGPT). Prior approaches typically used only multimodal data in the second stage of training. This study questions whether maintaining pure language capabilities requires explicit language data mixing.
3.  **Resolution:** The paper explores the impact of higher image resolutions (`$336 \times 336$` vs. `$224 \times 224$`). The Abstract notes that existing studies often overlook how resolution interacts with model scale, yet the authors find it highlights "the importance of higher image resolutions... to improve LMM performance."

By addressing these dimensions, the paper aims to make "state-of-the-art LMM research at a larger scale more accessible." It serves as a reference guide for the community, detailing not just *what* performance is achievable, but *how* to achieve it efficiently through specific choices in resolution, data mixing, and parameter-efficient tuning.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper is an empirical scaling study rather than a novel architecture proposal, systematically investigating how Large Multimodal Models (LLaVA) behave when the language model component is scaled from 7B/13B parameters up to 33B, 65B, and 70B parameters. The core idea is to measure performance gains across three scaling dimensions—model size, image resolution, and data mixing—while quantifying the trade-offs between full fine-tuning and parameter-efficient methods like LoRA/QLoRA.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being studied is LLaVA (Large Language and Vision Assistant), which connects a frozen image encoder to a large language model through a trainable projection layer, enabling the combined system to understand images and respond to visual questions in natural language. The problem this paper solves is determining how to scale such multimodal systems efficiently: it reveals that larger language models consistently improve performance, higher image resolutions add 2-3 points of accuracy, mixing language-only data preserves linguistic capabilities, and parameter-efficient tuning methods can match full fine-tuning at a fraction of the computational cost.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The LLaVA architecture consists of three major components connected in sequence. First, a **pre-trained image encoder** (CLIP ViT) processes the input image and produces visual feature vectors of dimension 1024. Second, a **trainable linear projection layer** maps these visual features into the language model's embedding space, with output dimensions matching the target LLM size (6656 for 33B models, 8192 for 65B models). Third, a **large language model** (Vicuna or LLaMA-2) receives the projected visual embeddings alongside text tokens and generates natural language responses. During training, the image encoder remains frozen while the projection layer and optionally the LLM weights are updated based on instruction-following datasets containing image-question-answer triplets.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin by explaining the **two-stage training procedure** (Stage 1: Feature Alignment, Stage 2: Visual Instruction Tuning) because understanding the training pipeline is prerequisite to understanding what is being scaled.
- We then detail the **model checkpoint selection and preparation**, including how the 33B, 65B, and 70B language backbones were obtained, since model initialization affects final performance.
- Next, we break down the **tuning methods** (full fine-tuning vs. LoRA/QLoRA), explaining the mathematical mechanism of parameter-efficient training and why the authors chose specific hyperparameters.
- We then cover **data mixing strategies**, describing how multimodal and language-only data are combined and why this matters for preserving language capabilities.
- Finally, we explain the **hyperparameter configurations and compute infrastructure**, including learning rates, batch sizes, and GPU requirements, because these determine reproducibility and cost.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Two-Stage Training Pipeline**

The LLaVA training process follows a two-stage approach inherited from the original LLaVA work [12], and this paper applies the same pipeline to larger model scales. In Stage 1, called **Pre-training for Feature Alignment**, only the linear projection layer is trained while both the image encoder and language model remain frozen. The purpose of this stage is to teach the projection layer to map visual features from the image encoder into the word embedding space of the language model, effectively creating a "visual vocabulary" that the LLM can understand. The projection layer transforms feature vectors from dimension 1024 (the output of the CLIP ViT image encoder before its last layer) to dimension 6656 for the 33B model or 8192 for the 65B model, matching the embedding dimension of each respective language model. This stage uses the concept-balanced subset of LAION-CC-SBU data containing 558K samples, which provides image-caption pairs for learning the visual-language alignment without requiring instruction-following behavior.

In Stage 2, called **Visual Instruction Tuning**, the model learns to follow diverse instructions and complete real-world tasks using the LLaVA-80K multimodal instruction dataset. This stage fine-tunes the model to produce helpful, contextually appropriate responses to visual questions rather than just generating image descriptions. The authors explore various training schedules in this stage to enable the model to handle diverse instruction types encountered in wild deployment scenarios. Critically, this is where the paper introduces experimental variations: some models are trained on multimodal data only, while others mix in language-only instruction data from ShareGPT to preserve pure language capabilities.

**Model Checkpoint Selection and Preparation**

For the 33B model, the authors employ the open-source Vicuna-33B checkpoint to perform the two-stage training, using approximately 125K conversations collected from ShareGPT.com for the language model initialization. For the 65B model, no public 65B Vicuna checkpoint existed at the time of this work, so the authors conducted their own training of a Vicuna-65B model utilizing ShareGPT data that they independently processed. This custom-trained 65B model used 159M tokens during training, which the authors note is substantially fewer than the 370M tokens reported for training Vicuna-33B, representing a potential limitation in the 65B model's language pre-training quality. For the 70B variant, the authors train LLaVA-70B based on the LLaMA-2-70B-Chat checkpoint, which allows them to compare against a different language model family and evaluate whether visual instruction tuning can improve upon an already strong chat model.

Once the instruction-tuned LLM is obtained, the authors follow the LLaVA methodology to perform the two-stage lightning training described above. The key insight here is that the vision encoder (CLIP ViT) remains frozen throughout both stages—only the projection layer and the language model weights are updated during training. This design choice reduces computational cost and preserves the pre-trained visual representations that CLIP has already learned from massive image-text datasets.

**Tuning Methods: Full Fine-Tuning vs. Parameter-Efficient Training**

The paper explores two fundamentally different approaches to updating model weights during Stage 2 training. In **full-model fine-tuning**, all parameters of the language model are trainable, meaning every weight in the 33B, 65B, or 70B parameter model receives gradient updates during backpropagation. This approach maximizes the model's capacity to adapt to the visual instruction task but requires substantial GPU memory and compute resources. For full fine-tuning, the authors use a total batch size of 512 distributed across 4 A100 nodes, where each node is equipped with 8 A100-80G GPUs, totaling 32 GPUs for the training run.

In **parameter-efficient training**, the authors employ LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) and QLoRA (Quantized LoRA) methods, which freeze the main model weights and train only a small set of additional parameters inserted into the model. LoRA works by decomposing the weight update matrix into two low-rank matrices, reducing the number of trainable parameters from billions to millions. Mathematically, instead of updating a weight matrix $W$ directly, LoRA represents the update as $W + \Delta W$ where $\Delta W = BA$, with $B$ and $A$ being low-rank matrices of dimensions $d \times r$ and $r \times d$ respectively, where $r$ is the LoRA rank (much smaller than $d$). QLoRA extends this by quantizing the base model weights to 4-bit precision, further reducing memory requirements.

The authors systematically evaluate LoRA with different rank values to understand the performance-cost trade-off. For models with 33B parameters and above, they train with LoRA rank 8 and rank 64 for 1 epoch on the LLaVA-80K instruction-tuning dataset. The results in Table 4 show that as LoRA rank increases, both performance and computational cost increase until approaching the maximum performance achievable by full-model tuning for a specific model size. For the 13B model, they find that a rank of 64 delivers comparable performance to full-model tuning, suggesting that smaller models require relatively higher-rank adaptations to match full fine-tuning.

A critical hyperparameter finding emerges from their LoRA experiments: the LoRA alpha value and learning rate have substantial impact on performance. The authors conducted hyperparameter search and found that larger LoRA alpha or equivalently larger learning rate was crucial to get the best performance. Specifically, they use LoRA alpha equals 2 times the LoRA rank, and a learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$, which works best for all models. To illustrate the magnitude of this effect, they report that with the same rank=64, if they reduce the learning rate to $2 \times 10^{-5}$ and alpha to 16, the performance decreases from 71.8 to 65.5 on LLaVA-Bench—a drop of 6.3 points from suboptimal hyperparameter selection. They also test whether increasing rank beyond 64 helps, finding that increasing rank from 64 to 128 and 512 improves performance only marginally from 65.5 to 66.1 and 68.1 respectively under the suboptimal learning rate setting, indicating diminishing returns from rank increases alone.

The computational cost analysis in Table 4 reveals important insights about efficiency. The cost is reported as GPU hours per node, where the unit can be equivalent to the price $13.63/hour (ND A100 v4 series) on Azure. The total cost can be estimated by multiplying the number of hours and number of epochs. The authors observe that cost is more related to the total number of parameters than the number of trainable parameters, because even with LoRA, the full model must be loaded into memory during forward and backward passes. For example, increasing the LoRA rank from 8 to 64 nearly matches the performance of LoRA fine-tuning a 65B model with the same rank, but only requires 50% of the 65B model's training cost. In practice, they find that tuning the 33B model provides a good trade-off between cost and performance.

Different LoRA variations show similar performance characteristics, but QLoRA requires lower GPU memory cost and running-time cost than standard LoRA. When large models like 65B are trained with DeepSpeed ZeRO2 mode, they can fit into GPU memory with QLoRA but yield out-of-memory (OOM) issues with standard LoRA. This makes QLoRA particularly valuable for scaling experiments where GPU memory is the limiting factor rather than compute time.

**Data Mixing Strategies**

Typically, only multimodal instruction data is used in Stage 2 of LLaVA training. This paper extends that approach by mixing language-only instruction data from ShareGPT with the LLaVA-80K multimodal instruction data. The motivation is to gain an in-depth understanding of the trade-off between models' language and multimodal capabilities—specifically, whether training exclusively on visual tasks causes the model to degrade in pure language reasoning.

Table 3(a) shows the impact of data mixing on LLaVA-Bench performance. When comparing models trained at 336×336 resolution without data mixing versus with data mixing, the 33B model improves from 72.0 to 73.9 (a 1.9 point gain) and the 65B model improves from 72.3 to 74.2 (a 1.9 point gain). The authors explain that larger models tend to have higher capability of fitting the instruction data, so by mixing language-only data with multimodal data, they can improve model performance by approximately 2 points compared to training on multimodal instruction data only.

Table 5 provides deeper insight into what data mixing accomplishes. When comparing LLaVA-33B without data mixing to LLaVA-33B with data mixing, the multimodal capability (LLaVA-Bench) improves from 72.0 to 73.9 and MM-VET improves from 32.9 to 34.1. However, the pure language capability shows a different pattern: Vicuna-80 score drops from 85.3 to 80.3, while MMLU improves from 56.1 to 58.6. The authors note this is surprising because mixing language instruction data boosts LLaVA's multimodal ability but not necessarily the language ability measured by Vicuna-80. They attribute the maintained language capability partially to the inclusion of complex reasoning questions and long-form answers in LLaVA-Instruct-158K, which helps preserve the language capabilities of LLaVA even without explicit language-only data mixing.

For the 70B model based on LLaMA-2-70B-Chat, the results show mixed outcomes on multimodal and language abilities. Interestingly, the authors improve LLaMA-2-70B-Chat by 2.4 points on MMLU through visual instruction tuning, yielding an overall MMLU score of 65.1, which they claim is the best performance for the 70B model size according to the Chatbot Arena Leaderboard. To the best of their knowledge, this is the first reported result showing that visual instruction tuning can improve the language ability of large-scale LMMs, challenging the assumption that multimodal training necessarily degrades pure language performance.

**Hyperparameter Configurations and Compute Infrastructure**

The training process for both stages utilizes the DeepSpeed library and employs the ZeRO3 optimizer for memory efficiency, except for QLoRA runs where they use ZeRO2. The maximum sequence length is set to 2048 tokens for all experiments, which limits the total context window including both image tokens and text tokens.

For Stage 1 (Feature Alignment), both the 33B and 65B models are trained with a learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$ with no weight decay. The learning rate schedule uses linear decay with linear warmup for 3% of training steps in total, meaning the learning rate gradually increases from zero to the peak value over the first 3% of training, then linearly decreases to zero by the end of training. This warmup strategy helps stabilize training in the early phases when gradients may be noisy.

For Stage 2 (Visual Instruction Tuning), the hyperparameters differ between full fine-tuning and LoRA/QLoRA approaches. For full fine-tuning, they use a learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-5}$ and train for 1 epoch across all model sizes. For LoRA/QLoRA runs, they use a higher learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$, which aligns with their finding that parameter-efficient methods benefit from larger learning rates. The batch size for full fine-tuning is 512 total, distributed across 4 A100 nodes with 8 GPUs each. For LoRA/QLoRA runs, they use a total batch size of 64 on 1 A100 node for the 33B model and 2 nodes for the 65B model, reflecting the reduced memory requirements of parameter-efficient methods.

**Image Resolution Experiments**

The authors investigate the impact of image resolution by fixing the CLIP ViT image encoder and comparing variants pre-trained to accept image resolutions of 224×224 versus 336×336 pixels. Table 3(a) shows that higher resolution consistently yields 2-3 points improvement across all four LLM sizes (7B, 13B, 33B, 65B). For example, the 7B model improves from 63.6 to 65.9 (2.3 points), the 13B model improves from 67.1 to 70.1 (3.0 points), the 33B model improves from 69.3 to 72.0 (2.7 points), and the 65B model improves from 70.3 to 72.3 (2.0 points). This finding highlights that resolution is an important scaling factor independent of model size, and the authors recommend using 336×336 resolution for optimal performance despite the increased computational cost of processing larger images through the vision encoder.

**Design Choices and Rationale**

Several design choices in this study warrant explanation. First, the authors choose to keep the vision encoder frozen rather than fine-tuning it, which follows the original LLaVA approach and reduces computational complexity. This decision assumes that the pre-trained CLIP encoder already captures sufficient visual features, and the projection layer plus language model can learn to interpret these features for instruction-following tasks. Second, they use a linear projection layer rather than more complex fusion mechanisms (like cross-attention), prioritizing simplicity and training efficiency. Third, they evaluate on multiple benchmarks (LLaVA-Bench, MM-VET, MM-Bench, Vicuna-80, MMLU) to capture different aspects of model capability rather than optimizing for a single metric.

The choice to explore LoRA rank values of 8 and 64 represents a practical range: rank 8 is very parameter-efficient but may limit adaptation capacity, while rank 64 approaches the capacity needed to match full fine-tuning without requiring all parameters to be trainable. Their finding that rank 64 on a 13B model matches full fine-tuning, but larger models benefit from continued rank increases, suggests that the optimal LoRA rank scales with model size.

One limitation the authors acknowledge is that the training datasets used in this study are relatively small compared to what might be optimal for 65B+ models. They note that "if we only train on LLaVA-80K data, we see smaller gain when model size becomes larger," conjecturing that larger data size is essential to train a larger model effectively. This suggests their findings represent a lower bound on what larger LMMs could achieve with more extensive training data.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper delivers several findings that challenge conventional assumptions about Large Multimodal Model (LMM) training. Below, we distinguish between fundamental innovations that shift how we think about LMMs and incremental improvements that refine existing practices.

### 4.1 Visual Instruction Tuning Can Improve Pure Language Capability (Fundamental Innovation)

**What makes this different:** Prior to this work, the prevailing assumption was that multimodal training would either maintain or degrade pure language capabilities. The intuition was straightforward: dedicating model capacity to visual understanding might come at the expense of linguistic reasoning. As stated in Section 3.2, this paper reports "the first reported result which shows visual instructing tuning improve language ability of large-scale LMM."

**Why it's significant:** The 70B model trained with visual instruction tuning achieved an MMLU score of `$65.1$`, improving upon the base LLaMA-2-70B-Chat checkpoint by `$2.4$` points. This is not a marginal gain—it represents the best performance for the 70B model size according to the Chatbot Arena Leaderboard cited in the paper.

**The mechanism behind the finding:** The authors suggest this improvement stems from the nature of the instruction data itself. The LLaVA-Instruct-158K dataset contains "complex reasoning questions, and long-form answers" that exercise the model's reasoning capabilities beyond simple visual recognition. When a model learns to reason about visual scenes in natural language, it simultaneously strengthens its general reasoning pathways. This reveals that multimodal training is not merely adding a new modality—it can enhance the underlying cognitive architecture.

**Common misconception to avoid:** This does not mean *all* multimodal training improves language capability. The paper notes that mixing language-only instruction data (ShareGPT) with multimodal data boosts multimodal ability but does not necessarily improve language scores on Vicuna-80 (Table 5 shows Vicuna-80 dropping from `$85.3$` to `$80.3$` for the 33B model with data mixing). The language improvement appears specific to certain model architectures and instruction data compositions.

### 4.2 Systematic Scaling Beyond 13B Parameters (Fundamental Innovation)

**What makes this different:** As noted in the Introduction, "most existing studies of open-source LMM are performed using models with 13B parameters or smaller." This paper is the first to systematically evaluate LLaVA scaled to 33B, 65B, and 70B parameters with controlled experimental conditions.

**Why it's significant:** The performance gains are consistent and meaningful. On MM-VET (Table 2), the 65B model with data mixing achieves `$36.4$` compared to `$32.9$` for the 13B baseline—a `$3.5$` point improvement on a benchmark where many competing models differ by less than `$1$` point. On LLaVA-Bench (Table 1), the 65B model achieves `$74.4$` overall compared to `$73.5$` for 13B with beam search.

**The scaling pattern revealed:** The improvements are not uniform across all capabilities. Section 3.1 notes that "the most significant improvements are observed when evaluating the capabilities of knowledge and generation, followed by recognition and OCR. The performance on spatial and math remains comparable." This tells us that scaling the language component primarily enhances knowledge-intensive and generative tasks, while vision-specific capabilities (spatial reasoning, math) may require scaling the vision encoder itself—a direction the authors leave for future work.

**What this means for the field:** This establishes that LMMs follow scaling laws similar to pure language models, but with modality-specific ceilings. Researchers can now confidently invest in larger LMMs knowing the returns are predictable, but they should also recognize that vision encoder scaling remains an open problem.

### 4.3 Parameter-Efficient Tuning Matches Full Fine-Tuning (Practical Innovation)

**What makes this different:** Prior work often assumed full fine-tuning was necessary for optimal LMM performance, especially at larger scales. This paper demonstrates that LoRA and QLoRA can achieve "comparable to the performance of full-model fine-tuning" (Abstract) while drastically reducing computational requirements.

**Why it's significant:** The cost implications are substantial. Table 4 shows that for the 65B model, full fine-tuning requires `$13.50$` GPU hours per node, while LoRA rank 64 requires only `$9.17$` hours—a `$32\%$` reduction. More importantly, QLoRA enables training that would otherwise cause out-of-memory (OOM) issues. As Section 3.2 states: "When large models (e.g., 65B) are trained with DeepSpeed ZeRO2 mode, they can fit into GPU with QLoRA, while yield the OOM issue with LoRA."

**The critical hyperparameter insight:** The paper reveals that LoRA performance is highly sensitive to learning rate and alpha values—a detail often overlooked in prior work. With rank=64, reducing the learning rate from `$1 \times 10^{-4}$` to `$2 \times 10^{-5}$` and alpha from `$128$` (2× rank) to `$16$` causes performance to drop from `$71.8$` to `$65.5$` on LLaVA-Bench—a `$6.3$` point degradation from suboptimal hyperparameters alone. This explains why some prior studies may have concluded LoRA underperforms: they likely used default hyperparameters unsuitable for LMMs.

**Incremental vs. fundamental:** This is a practical innovation rather than a theoretical breakthrough. The mechanism (LoRA/QLoRA) is well-established from prior work [4, 7]. The contribution is empirical validation at LMM scale with specific hyperparameter recommendations that make these methods viable for the multimodal domain.

### 4.4 Data Mixing and Resolution as Independent Scaling Factors (Practical Innovation)

**What makes this different:** Prior LLaVA training typically used only multimodal instruction data in Stage 2 and often defaulted to `$224 \times 224$` image resolution. This paper treats data mixing and resolution as tunable scaling dimensions alongside model size.

**Why it's significant:** Both factors yield consistent 2-3 point improvements independent of model size. Table 3(a) shows that moving from `$224 \times 224$` to `$336 \times 336$` resolution improves the 7B model by `$2.3$` points, the 13B model by `$3.0$` points, the 33B model by `$2.7$` points, and the 65B model by `$2.0$` points. Similarly, data mixing improves 33B and 65B models by approximately `$1.9$` points each.

**The interaction effect:** These factors are additive rather than redundant. A researcher can combine larger model size, higher resolution, and data mixing for compounding gains. The 65B model with both higher resolution and data mixing achieves `$74.2$` on LLaVA-Bench compared to `$70.3$` for the same model at lower resolution without mixing—a `$3.9$` point total gain from these two factors alone.

**What this means for practitioners:** If computational constraints prevent scaling model size, researchers can still achieve meaningful improvements through resolution and data strategy. This provides a "scaling ladder" where each rung is accessible depending on available resources.

### 4.5 Distinguishing Incremental from Fundamental Contributions

| Contribution | Type | Reasoning |
|-------------|------|-----------|
| Visual tuning improves language capability | **Fundamental** | Challenges core assumption about modality trade-offs; first empirical evidence at this scale |
| Scaling LMM beyond 13B parameters | **Fundamental** | First systematic study at 33B-70B scale; establishes scaling laws for LMMs |
| LoRA/QLoRA matching full fine-tuning | **Practical** | Validates existing methods in new domain; hyperparameter insights are novel |
| Data mixing strategy | **Practical** | Refines training procedure; builds on existing instruction tuning concepts |
| Resolution impact | **Practical** | Confirms known vision model behavior in LMM context; quantifies gains precisely |

**The broader implication:** The fundamental innovations (sections 4.1 and 4.2) reshape how we conceptualize LMMs—they are not just language models with vision attached, but integrated systems where multimodal training can enhance core reasoning. The practical innovations (sections 4.3 and 4.4) provide an actionable recipe for researchers with varying computational budgets to achieve state-of-the-art results.

**A limitation worth noting:** The authors acknowledge in Section 4 that "the training datasets used in this study is small. So, our findings are still preliminary." This means the reported performance represents a lower bound—larger datasets may reveal even stronger scaling effects or different interaction patterns between the factors studied. Future work with more extensive data curation could amplify or modify these insights.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the experimental design, quantitative results, and robustness of the claims made in the paper. While previous sections outlined *what* the authors found, here we examine *how* they measured it, whether the data supports their conclusions, and where the experimental boundaries lie. We focus on the benchmarks used, the specific numerical gains observed, and the trade-offs revealed through ablation studies.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Baselines

To validate the performance of scaled Large Multimodal Models (LMM), the authors employ a multi-faceted evaluation strategy covering both multimodal and pure language capabilities. This is critical because scaling the language component should ideally improve both vision-language tasks and retain (or improve) text-only reasoning.

**Multimodal Benchmarks**
*   **LLaVA-Bench (In-the-Wild):** This benchmark consists of `$24$` images and `$60$` questions covering diverse scenarios (indoor/outdoor scenes, memes, paintings). It evaluates three task types: conversation, detailed description, and complex reasoning.
    *   *Scoring:* Answers are scored by **GPT-4 (gpt4-0314)** comparing model outputs against gold responses. Scores are reported as relative percentages.
    *   *Baseline:* The authors compare against commercial systems like **Microsoft BingChat** and **Google Bard**, as well as prior open-source LLaVA versions.
*   **MM-VET:** Designed to evaluate integrated capabilities, this benchmark contains `$200$` images and `$218$` questions across six core skills: recognition, OCR, knowledge, generation, spatial awareness, and math.
    *   *Scoring:* An LLM-based evaluator (**gpt4-0613**) scores open-ended outputs.
    *   *Validation:* The authors verify their evaluation pipeline consistency by reproducing results for LLaVA-13B (LLaMA-2) reported in the original MM-VET paper (`$32.9 \pm 0.1$` vs. their `$32.6 \pm 0.1$`), ensuring their experimental setup is reliable (Table 2).
*   **MM-Bench:** A larger-scale benchmark with `$2,974$` questions evaluating six reasoning categories (logic, attribute, relation, fine-grained perception, etc.). This provides a more statistically significant sample size than LLaVA-Bench.

**Language Benchmarks**
To isolate the impact of visual instruction tuning on pure language skills, the authors use:
*   **Vicuna-80:** Evaluates instruction-following ability in real-world language tasks.
*   **MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding):** Evaluates multitask language knowledge across diverse domains.

**Compute Metrics**
For efficiency analysis, cost is quantified using **GPU Hours per Node**. The paper notes that one unit equates to approximately `$13.63$/hour` (ND A100 v4 series on Azure). This allows readers to translate experimental time into monetary cost.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results Breakdown

The results are organized by the three scaling factors investigated: model size, image resolution, and data mixing. We also examine the efficiency of parameter-efficient tuning.

#### 5.2.1 Model Scaling: 13B vs. 33B vs. 65B
The core hypothesis is that larger language backbones improve LMM performance. Table 1 and Table 2 provide the primary evidence.

*   **MM-VET Performance:** The scaling trend is clear and consistent.
    *   **LLaVA-13B (LLaMA-2):** `$32.9 \pm 0.1$`
    *   **LLaVA-33B:** `$32.9 \pm 0.3$` (Note: Without data mixing, 33B matches 13B here)
    *   **LLaVA-65B (Data Mixing):** `$36.4 \pm 0.2$`
    *   *Analysis:* The largest model with data mixing achieves a `$3.5$` point improvement over the 13B baseline. The paper notes the most significant improvements occur in **knowledge** (`$26.5 \to 30.4$`) and **generation** (`$29.3 \to 32.3$`), confirming that larger LLMs contribute primarily to knowledge-intensive and generative tasks rather than pure vision tasks like spatial reasoning (Table 2).

*   **LLaVA-Bench Performance:** The results here are more nuanced.
    *   **LLaVA-13B (beam=5):** Overall `$73.5$`
    *   **LLaVA-33B (beam=5):** Overall `$74.8$`
    *   **LLaVA-65B (beam=5):** Overall `$74.4$`
    *   *Critical Observation:* While the 33B model outperforms the 13B model, the 65B model actually scores slightly *lower* in overall performance than the 33B model (`$74.4$` vs. `$74.8$`), despite having higher reasoning scores (`$88.7$` vs. `$83.5$`). This suggests diminishing returns or potential saturation on this specific small benchmark (only 60 questions), highlighting the importance of using multiple evaluation datasets like MM-VET and MM-Bench.

*   **MM-Bench Performance:** Table 3(b) shows a stronger scaling signal.
    *   **LLaVA-7B:** Overall `$36.2$`
    *   **LLaVA-65B:** Overall `$56.0$`
    *   *Analysis:* This `$19.8$` point gap demonstrates that on a larger evaluation set, the benefits of scaling from 7B to 65B are substantial and unambiguous.

#### 5.2.2 Image Resolution and Data Mixing
Table 3(a) isolates the impact of resolution and data mixing independent of model size.

*   **Resolution Impact:** Increasing input resolution from `$224 \times 224$` to `$336 \times 336$` yields consistent gains across all model sizes.
    *   **7B Model:** `$63.6 \to 65.9$` (`$+2.3$` points)
    *   **13B Model:** `$67.1 \to 70.1$` (`$+3.0$` points)
    *   **33B Model:** `$69.3 \to 72.0$` (`$+2.7$` points)
    *   **65B Model:** `$70.3 \to 72.3$` (`$+2.0$` points)
    *   *Takeaway:* Resolution provides a "free" performance boost independent of parameter count. The gain is roughly `$2-3$` points universally, suggesting the vision encoder's input quality is a bottleneck even for large language backbones.

*   **Data Mixing Impact:** Mixing language-only data (ShareGPT) with multimodal data (LLaVA-80K) in Stage 2 training.
    *   **33B Model:** `$72.0 \to 73.9$` (`$+1.9$` points)
    *   **65B Model:** `$72.3 \to 74.2$` (`$+1.9$` points)
    *   *Takeaway:* Larger models have higher capacity to fit instruction data. Providing pure language data prevents the model from overfitting to visual tasks and maintains general instruction-following fluency, adding another `$~2$` points of performance.

#### 5.2.3 Efficiency: LoRA and QLoRA vs. Full Fine-Tuning
Table 4 presents a critical trade-off analysis between performance and compute cost.

*   **Performance Parity:** For the 13B model, LoRA with rank `$64$` achieves `$70.1$`, matching full fine-tuning (`$70.1$`). For the 33B model, LoRA rank `$64$` achieves `$71.8$`, approaching full fine-tuning (`$72.0$`).
*   **Cost Reduction:**
    *   **65B Full Fine-Tuning:** `$13.50$` GPU hours/node.
    *   **65B LoRA (rank 64):** `$9.17$` GPU hours/node.
    *   **33B LoRA (rank 64):** `$4.79$` GPU hours/node.
    *   *Analysis:* Using a 33B model with LoRA costs less than **36%** of the 65B full fine-tuning cost (`$4.79$` vs. `$13.50$`), while delivering competitive performance (33B LoRA `$71.8$` vs. 65B Full `$72.3$`). This supports the claim that parameter-efficient tuning is a viable strategy for resource-constrained settings.
*   **Memory Constraints:** The paper notes a specific failure case for standard LoRA: "When large models (e.g., 65B) are trained with DeepSpeed ZeRO2 mode, they can fit into GPU with QLoRA, while yield the OOM [Out Of Memory] issue with LoRA." This makes QLoRA not just cheaper, but *necessary* for scaling to 65B on limited hardware.

#### 5.2.4 Language Capability Retention
Table 5 addresses the fear that multimodal training degrades language skills.

*   **MMLU Scores:**
    *   **LLaMA-2-70B-Chat:** `$63.1$`
    *   **LLaVA-70B (Visual Instruction Tuned):** `$65.1$`
    *   *Significance:* This `$+2.0$` point improvement supports the novel claim that visual instruction tuning can *enhance* pure language reasoning, likely due to the complex reasoning required in multimodal tasks.
*   **Vicuna-80 Scores (Trade-off):**
    *   **Vicuna-33B:** `$85.6$`
    *   **LLaVA-33B (No Mix):** `$85.3$`
    *   **LLaVA-33B (Data Mixing):** `$80.3$`
    *   *Nuance:* While MMLU improved, Vicuna-80 scores *dropped* when data mixing was applied. This indicates that "language capability" is not monolithic; general knowledge (MMLU) may improve while specific chat stylistic capabilities (Vicuna-80) may shift. The authors attribute the maintained capability in non-mixed models to the inclusion of complex reasoning in the LLaVA-Instruct-158K dataset.

### 5.3 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

Do the experiments convincingly support the paper's conclusions?

**Strengths:**
1.  **Multi-Benchmark Validation:** The authors do not rely on a single metric. The scaling trend is visible across MM-VET, MM-Bench, and LLaVA-Bench (with minor exceptions), strengthening the validity of the scaling claim.
2.  **Pipeline Verification:** By reproducing MM-VET baseline scores (Table 2 footnote), they establish trust in their evaluation harness, ruling out evaluation bias as a source of performance gains.
3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper explicitly details the impact of LoRA hyperparameters. They show that suboptimal settings (learning rate `$2 \times 10^{-5}$`, alpha `$16$`) drop performance from `$71.8$` to `$65.5$`. This transparency prevents readers from replicating the work with default settings and failing to achieve reported results.

**Limitations and Conditions:**
1.  **Benchmark Sample Size:** The authors acknowledge that LLaVA-Bench is small ("24 images with 60 questions"), admitting "the comparison might not be statistically significant." This explains the anomaly where 65B scores lower than 33B on this specific benchmark. Readers should prioritize MM-VET and MM-Bench results for robust conclusions.
2.  **Data Scale Constraint:** In Section 4, the authors state, "the training datasets used in this study is small. So, our findings are still preliminary." They conjecture that "larger data size is essential to train a larger model." This implies the reported performance is a **lower bound**. The 65B model might underperform relative to its potential because it was not trained on a dataset proportional to its size (e.g., the 65B Vicuna backbone used 159M tokens vs. 370M for the 33B version).
3.  **Vision Encoder Bottleneck:** The study scales the *language* model but keeps the *vision* encoder (CLIP ViT) frozen. The results show spatial and math capabilities remain comparable across sizes (Table 2 analysis). This suggests that without scaling the vision encoder, language scaling alone cannot solve vision-heavy reasoning tasks. This is a boundary condition for the scaling laws presented.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

The paper includes several implicit ablations that guide practical implementation:

*   **LoRA Rank Ablation:** Table 4 compares rank 8 vs. rank 64.
    *   For 33B/65B models, increasing rank from 8 to 64 increases performance (`$70.3 \to 71.8$` for 33B) but also cost.
    *   For 13B models, rank 64 is sufficient to match full fine-tuning.
    *   *Recommendation:* The optimal rank scales with model size. Using a small rank on a large model leaves performance on the table.
*   **Beam Search Impact:** Table 1 reports results for beam search sizes 1 and 5.
    *   **LLaVA-33B:** Beam 1 (`$73.9$`) vs. Beam 5 (`$74.8$`).
    *   *Observation:* Increasing beam search provides a `$~1$` point gain with "negligible increase of inference latency." This is a low-cost inference-time optimization recommended for deployment.
*   **Resolution Ablation:** Table 3(a) effectively ablates resolution while holding model size constant. The consistent `$2-3$` point gain across all sizes confirms this is a robust improvement strategy not dependent on model scale.

### 5.5 Summary of Experimental Trade-offs

The experiments reveal a clear Pareto frontier for researchers designing LMMs:

| Goal | Recommended Configuration | Evidence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Max Performance** | 65B Model + 336px Resolution + Data Mixing | Table 2 (MM-VET 36.4), Table 3 (LLaVA-Bench 74.2) |
| **Cost Efficiency** | 33B Model + LoRA (rank 64) + 336px | Table 4 (50% cost of 65B, comparable perf) |
| **Memory Constrained** | 65B Model + QLoRA | Section 3.2 (Avoids OOM issues with standard LoRA) |
| **Language Retention** | Mix ShareGPT Data + LLaVA-80K | Table 5 (MMLU improvement), Table 3 (Multimodal gain) |

The experimental analysis confirms that scaling LMMs is effective but subject to diminishing returns on small benchmarks and vision-specific bottlenecks. The data strongly supports the use of parameter-efficient tuning and higher resolution as standard practices for future large-scale multimodal research.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

No empirical study is without boundaries, and this paper is transparent about the constraints that shape its findings. Understanding these limitations is critical for researchers who wish to build upon this work or apply its recommendations to their own settings. We organize the limitations into four categories: data and training constraints, architectural bottlenecks, evaluation boundaries, and practical deployment trade-offs.

### 6.1 Data and Training Constraints

**Small Training Datasets Relative to Model Size**

The authors explicitly acknowledge in Section 4 (Conclusions and Limitations) that "the training datasets used in this study is small. So, our findings are still preliminary." This is not a minor caveat—it fundamentally shapes how we interpret the scaling results.

The core issue is a mismatch between model capacity and training data volume:
*   The 65B Vicuna backbone was trained on only **159M tokens**, compared to **370M tokens** for the 33B Vicuna model (Section 2, Experiment Setup).
*   Stage 2 visual instruction tuning uses only **LLaVA-80K** (approximately 80,000 instruction samples) for all model sizes.

The authors observe the consequence directly in Section 3.2: "if we only train on LLaVA-80K data, we see smaller gain when model size becomes larger." This suggests the reported performance represents a **lower bound** on what larger LMMs could achieve. A 65B model trained on proportionally larger instruction datasets might show stronger scaling effects than what this study captures.

**Implication for practitioners:** Do not treat the 65B results as the ceiling of what is achievable. The scaling gains reported here may underestimate the true potential of larger models when paired with appropriately scaled training data.

**Incomplete Language Pre-training for 65B Model**

The custom-trained Vicuna-65B checkpoint introduces a confounding variable. Because no public 65B Vicuna checkpoint existed, the authors trained their own using independently processed ShareGPT data. However, the token count discrepancy (159M vs. 370M for 33B) means the 65B model may have weaker baseline language capabilities before visual instruction tuning even begins.

This creates ambiguity in interpreting results: when the 65B LLaVA model underperforms expectations on certain benchmarks, is it because scaling the language model has diminishing returns for LMMs, or because the 65B backbone was undertrained relative to the 33B version? The paper does not isolate this variable.

### 6.2 Architectural Bottlenecks

**Frozen Vision Encoder Limits Vision-Centric Capabilities**

A critical architectural decision in this study is keeping the CLIP ViT image encoder **frozen** throughout both training stages. Only the linear projection layer and the language model weights are updated. While this reduces computational cost and follows the original LLaVA methodology, it introduces a hard ceiling on vision-specific capabilities.

The evidence for this bottleneck appears in the MM-VET results (Table 2 analysis in Section 3.1): "The performance on spatial and math remains comparable" across model sizes. While knowledge and generation capabilities improve significantly with larger language models (knowledge: `$26.5 \to 30.4$`, generation: `$29.3 \to 32.3$` from 13B to 65B with data mixing), spatial awareness and mathematical reasoning show minimal gains.

The authors acknowledge this explicitly in Section 4: "We leave it to future work how to scale the vision encoder to enhance the visual capabilities and improve model performance on vision recognition and understanding tasks."

**Implication for practitioners:** Scaling the language model alone will not solve vision-heavy reasoning tasks. If your application requires strong spatial reasoning or visual mathematics, you may need to explore architectures that also scale or fine-tune the vision encoder—not just the language backbone.

**Fixed Sequence Length**

All experiments use a maximum sequence length of **2048 tokens** (Section 2, Hyper-parameters). This limits the total context window available for both image tokens and text tokens combined. For applications requiring long-form visual reasoning (e.g., analyzing multiple images, processing lengthy documents with embedded figures, or extended multi-turn conversations), this constraint may become a bottleneck.

The paper does not explore how longer sequence lengths interact with model scaling. A 65B model with 4096 or 8192 token context might exhibit different scaling characteristics than what is reported here.

### 6.3 Evaluation Boundaries

**Small Benchmark Sample Sizes**

The authors are transparent about statistical limitations in Section 3.1: "Despite the fact that LLaVA-Bench is small (thus the comparison might not be statistically significant), the results are encouraging." LLaVA-Bench contains only **24 images and 60 questions** total.

This small sample size manifests in anomalous results that complicate interpretation:
*   On LLaVA-Bench with beam search 5, the 33B model scores **74.8** overall while the 65B model scores **74.4**—the larger model performs *worse* (Table 1).
*   However, on MM-VET (200 images, 218 questions) and MM-Bench (2,974 questions), the scaling trend is clear and consistent.

**Implication for practitioners:** Prioritize MM-VET and MM-Bench results when evaluating scaling benefits. LLaVA-Bench is useful for qualitative inspection but should not be the sole metric for comparing model sizes due to high variance from small sample sizes.

**Language Capability is Not Monolithic**

Table 5 reveals that "language capability" cannot be treated as a single dimension. The paper measures language skills using two different benchmarks with divergent results:

| Model | Vicuna-80 | MMLU |
|-------|-----------|------|
| Vicuna-33B | 85.6 | 59.0 |
| LLaVA-33B (No Mix) | 85.3 | 56.1 |
| LLaVA-33B (Data Mixing) | 80.3 | 58.6 |

Data mixing improves MMLU (`$56.1 \to 58.6$`) but *degrades* Vicuna-80 (`$85.3 \to 80.3$`). Meanwhile, the LLaVA-70B model improves MMLU to **65.1** (best for 70B size) but the paper does not report its Vicuna-80 score for direct comparison.

This divergence suggests that visual instruction tuning affects different language capabilities differently. General knowledge reasoning (MMLU) may improve while chat-style instruction following (Vicuna-80) may shift or degrade depending on data mixing strategy.

**Implication for practitioners:** Evaluate language capabilities using multiple benchmarks. Optimizing for one (e.g., MMLU) may come at the expense of another (e.g., conversational fluency measured by Vicuna-80).

### 6.4 Practical Deployment Trade-offs

**Compute Cost vs. Performance Pareto Frontier**

Table 4 quantifies the trade-off between performance and computational cost, but the optimal choice depends on resource constraints:

| Configuration | LLaVA-Bench Score | GPU Hours/Node | Relative Cost |
|--------------|-------------------|----------------|---------------|
| 33B Full Fine-tune | 72.0 | 5.80 | 1.0× |
| 33B LoRA (rank 64) | 71.8 | 4.79 | 0.83× |
| 65B Full Fine-tune | 72.3 | 13.50 | 2.33× |
| 65B LoRA (rank 64) | 72.2 | 9.17 | 1.58× |

The 33B model with LoRA achieves **99.7%** of the 65B full fine-tuning performance at only **35%** of the cost. However, for absolute maximum performance, the 65B model still holds a slight edge.

**Implication for practitioners:** The 33B + LoRA configuration represents the efficiency frontier for most applications. Reserve 65B models for scenarios where every fractional performance point matters and budget is secondary.

**Memory Constraints Force QLoRA at Large Scales**

The paper notes a specific failure mode in Section 3.2: "When large models (e.g., 65B) are trained with DeepSpeed ZeRO2 mode, they can fit into GPU with QLoRA, while yield the OOM [Out Of Memory] issue with LoRA."

This means standard LoRA is not viable for 65B models on typical hardware configurations—QLoRA's 4-bit quantization is *necessary*, not optional, at this scale. However, QLoRA introduces its own considerations:
*   Quantization may affect model quality in ways not fully explored in this study.
*   Inference with quantized weights may have different latency characteristics than full-precision models.

**Hyperparameter Sensitivity in LoRA**

A subtle but critical limitation is the sensitivity of LoRA performance to hyperparameter selection. The authors found that "larger LoRA alpha or equivalently larger learning rate was crucial to get the best performance" (Section 2). The magnitude of this effect is substantial:

*   Optimal settings (rank=64, LR=`$1 \times 10^{-4}$`, alpha=128): **71.8** on LLaVA-Bench
*   Suboptimal settings (rank=64, LR=`$2 \times 10^{-5}$`, alpha=16): **65.5** on LLaVA-Bench

This **6.3 point drop** from hyperparameter choices alone exceeds the performance gain from scaling 13B to 33B in some configurations. Researchers replicating this work with default LoRA hyperparameters from other domains may fail to achieve reported results.

**Implication for practitioners:** Do not use default LoRA hyperparameters from language-only fine-tuning. The multimodal domain requires higher learning rates and alpha values specific to the LMM training regime.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Future Directions

The paper concludes by identifying several unresolved questions that warrant future investigation:

1.  **Vision Encoder Scaling:** How does performance change when the vision encoder is also scaled or fine-tuned, rather than kept frozen? The current study isolates language model scaling but leaves the vision-language interaction incomplete.

2.  **Data Scaling Laws:** What is the optimal ratio of training data to model parameters for LMMs? The authors conjecture "larger data size is essential to train a larger model" but do not provide empirical data on what data volumes are needed for 65B+ models.

3.  **Generalizability Beyond LLaVA:** All experiments use the LLaVA architecture with CLIP ViT + Vicuna/LLaMA-2. Do these scaling findings transfer to other LMM architectures (e.g., Flamingo-style cross-attention, different vision encoders like SigLIP)?

4.  **Long-Context Visual Reasoning:** How do scaled LMMs perform on tasks requiring analysis of multiple images or very long visual documents? The 2048 token limit leaves this question unaddressed.

5.  **Real-World Deployment Characteristics:** The study measures benchmark performance but does not report inference latency, throughput, or serving costs at scale. A 65B model may achieve higher accuracy but be impractical for real-time applications.

### 6.6 Summary: When to Use This Approach

Based on the limitations identified, here is guidance on when the scaling approach in this paper is appropriate versus when alternative strategies may be needed:

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| Resource-constrained research | 33B + LoRA (rank 64) | Best performance-cost trade-off (Table 4) |
| Maximum accuracy required | 65B + QLoRA + Data Mixing | Highest MM-VET score (36.4) |
| Vision-heavy tasks (spatial, math) | Consider alternative architectures | Language scaling alone shows limited gains (Table 2) |
| Production deployment | 33B or smaller with beam search=5 | Negligible latency increase for ~1 point gain (Table 1) |
| Language capability critical | Evaluate on multiple language benchmarks | Vicuna-80 and MMLU show divergent trends (Table 5) |
| Long-context applications | Await future work | 2048 token limit not addressed in this study |

The limitations identified here do not diminish the value of this empirical study—they clarify the boundaries within which its findings apply. Researchers building on this work should treat the reported performance as a baseline that can be improved through larger datasets, vision encoder scaling, and architecture-specific optimizations.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This empirical study does more than report performance numbers—it reshapes how the research community should approach Large Multimodal Model (LMM) development. By systematically scaling LLaVA beyond the 13B parameter ceiling that dominated prior work, the authors establish new baselines and reveal scaling patterns that will guide future research investments. This section examines how the findings change the field's trajectory, what research directions they enable, and how practitioners should integrate these insights into real-world systems.

### 7.1 Shifting the Field's Scaling Paradigm

**From Language-Only to Multimodal Scaling Laws**

Prior to this work, scaling laws were well-established for pure language models but remained unverified for multimodal systems. The Introduction notes that "the impact of significantly scaling up the model size to e.g., 33B and 65B remains unexplored." This study fills that gap with empirical evidence that LMMs follow predictable scaling patterns, but with important modality-specific caveats.

The key paradigm shift is recognizing that **scaling the language component alone produces asymmetric improvements**. Section 3.1 analyzing MM-VET results states: "The most significant improvements are observed when evaluating the capabilities of knowledge and generation, followed by recognition and OCR. The performance on spatial and math remains comparable." This reveals that:

- **Knowledge-intensive tasks** benefit directly from larger language backbones (more parameters = more stored knowledge)
- **Vision-centric tasks** hit a ceiling when the vision encoder remains frozen (CLIP ViT becomes the bottleneck)

This finding redirects future research investment: scaling LMMs effectively requires coordinating growth across both modalities, not just the language component. The authors explicitly acknowledge this in Section 4: "We leave it to future work how to scale the vision encoder to enhance the visual capabilities."

**Parameter-Efficient Tuning as a First-Class Strategy**

Before this study, LoRA and QLoRA were often treated as compromise solutions—acceptable when full fine-tuning was infeasible, but suspected to underperform optimal results. Table 4 overturns this assumption for LMMs:

> "Performance of LoRA/QLoRA tuning of LMM are comparable to the performance of full-model fine-tuning." (Abstract)

The data supports this claim concretely:
- **13B Model:** LoRA rank 64 achieves `$70.1$`, matching full fine-tuning at `$70.1$`
- **33B Model:** LoRA rank 64 achieves `$71.8$`, within `$0.2$` points of full fine-tuning at `$72.0$`
- **65B Model:** LoRA rank 64 achieves `$72.2$`, within `$0.1$` points of full fine-tuning at `$72.3$`

This changes the field's computational economics. Researchers can now pursue 65B-scale LMM experiments without requiring the full memory and compute budget previously assumed necessary. The cost reduction is substantial: Table 4 shows 65B LoRA training requires `$9.17$` GPU hours/node versus `$13.50$` for full fine-tuning—a `$32\%$` reduction.

**Visual Instruction Tuning as Language Enhancement**

Perhaps the most surprising finding challenges a core assumption about modality trade-offs. Section 3.2 reports:

> "To the best of our knowledge, this is the first reported result which shows visual instructing tuning improve language ability of large-scale LMM."

The LLaVA-70B model improved MMLU scores from `$63.1$` (base LLaMA-2-70B-Chat) to `$65.1$` after visual instruction tuning—a `$2.0$` point gain that the authors note is "the best performance for the 70B model size, according to [17] and the Chatbot Arena Leaderboard."

This finding reframes multimodal training from a potential liability (risking language capability degradation) to a potential asset (enhancing general reasoning through complex visual-language tasks). The mechanism, as suggested in Section 3.2, is that "the inclusion of complex reasoning questions, and long-form answers in LLaVA-Instruct-158K, which helps maintain the language capabilities of LLaVA."

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

**Vision Encoder Scaling Studies**

The most immediate follow-up research direction is scaling the vision component. This study kept the CLIP ViT encoder frozen at fixed resolutions (`$224 \times 224$` or `$336 \times 336$`), creating a controlled experiment for language scaling but leaving vision scaling unexplored.

Future work should investigate:
- **Larger vision encoders:** Does replacing CLIP ViT-L/14 with ViT-H/14 or larger architectures improve spatial and math capabilities that plateaued in this study?
- **Fine-tuned vision encoders:** Does unfreezing the vision encoder during Stage 2 training unlock additional performance, and at what computational cost?
- **Multi-resolution training:** Can models benefit from training on mixed resolutions rather than a single fixed resolution?

The MM-VET results in Table 2 provide a clear target: spatial awareness and math scores showed minimal improvement across model sizes (spatial: `$29.6 \to 33.0$`, math: `$7.7 \to 15.0$` from 13B to 65B with data mixing), suggesting these capabilities are vision-limited rather than language-limited.

**Data Scaling Laws for LMMs**

The authors explicitly flag data volume as a limiting factor in Section 4:

> "Note that the training datasets used in this study is small. So, our findings are still preliminary. In future work, we will experiment using much larger datasets to investigate in detail whether and how different methods of training data selection and mixing can improve the quality of much larger LMM."

This opens several research questions:
- **Optimal data-to-parameter ratios:** What is the minimum instruction data volume needed to effectively train a 65B LMM? The 65B Vicuna backbone used only 159M tokens versus 370M for 33B (Section 2), potentially undertraining the larger model.
- **Data mixing proportions:** What ratio of language-only (ShareGPT) to multimodal (LLaVA-80K) data maximizes both capabilities? Table 5 shows data mixing improved multimodal scores but had mixed effects on language benchmarks.
- **Data quality versus quantity:** Does curated high-quality instruction data outperform larger volumes of noisier data for LMM training?

**Architecture Variations Beyond LLaVA**

All experiments in this study use the LLaVA architecture: frozen CLIP ViT + linear projection + Vicuna/LLaMA-2. The scaling findings may not transfer directly to alternative architectures:

- **Cross-attention fusion:** Models like Flamingo [1] use Perceiver Resampler layers rather than simple linear projections. Do scaling laws differ for these architectures?
- **Alternative vision encoders:** SigLIP, DINOv2, or ensemble vision encoders may interact differently with scaled language backbones.
- **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE):** How do sparse MoE language models scale in the multimodal context? The compute efficiency of MoE might change the cost-performance trade-offs reported in Table 4.

Researchers should treat this study's findings as specific to the LLaVA architecture family until validated on alternative designs.

**Long-Context Visual Reasoning**

All experiments use a maximum sequence length of **2048 tokens** (Section 2, Hyper-parameters). This constraint leaves important questions unanswered:

- How do scaled LMMs perform on tasks requiring analysis of multiple images?
- Can larger models effectively utilize 4096 or 8192 token contexts for visual document understanding?
- Does the 2048 token limit mask potential scaling benefits that would emerge with longer contexts?

As language models increasingly support 32K+ token contexts, understanding how LMMs scale with context length becomes critical for applications like visual document QA, multi-image reasoning, and video understanding.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Deployment Guidance

**Model Size Selection by Use Case**

The scaling results provide clear guidance for selecting model sizes based on application requirements:

| Application Type | Recommended Model | Rationale |
|-----------------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Real-time chatbots** | 7B or 13B + LoRA | Low latency, adequate performance for conversational tasks |
| **Knowledge-intensive QA** | 33B or 65B | Table 2 shows knowledge scores improve significantly with size (`$23.5 \to 26.2 \to 30.4$`) |
| **Detailed image description** | 33B+ with 336px | Table 3(a) shows 2-3 point gains from resolution; larger models generate richer descriptions |
| **Spatial/math reasoning** | Alternative architectures needed | Table 2 shows these capabilities plateau; language scaling alone insufficient |
| **Resource-constrained deployment** | 33B + LoRA rank 64 | Table 4 shows 99.7% of 65B performance at 35% of cost |

**When to Use Parameter-Efficient Tuning**

Based on Table 4 and Section 3.2 findings, LoRA/QLoRA should be preferred over full fine-tuning in these scenarios:

1. **GPU Memory Limited:** QLoRA enables 65B training where standard LoRA causes OOM (Section 3.2: "they can fit into GPU with QLoRA, while yield the OOM issue with LoRA")
2. **Iterative Experimentation:** When testing multiple hyperparameter configurations, the `$32\%$` cost reduction per run compounds significantly
3. **Production Fine-tuning:** For domain-specific adaptation of pre-trained LLaVA checkpoints, LoRA provides comparable performance with lower serving memory requirements

However, full fine-tuning may still be warranted when:
- Maximum possible performance is required regardless of cost
- The target domain differs significantly from training data (full adaptation may generalize better)
- Inference latency is critical (LoRA adds slight overhead during generation)

**Critical Hyperparameter Settings for LoRA**

A key practical contribution of this study is the hyperparameter guidance for LoRA in the LMM context. Section 2 states:

> "We conducted a set of hyperparameter search and for LoRA runs, and found larger LoRA alpha or equivalently larger learning rate was crucial to get the best performance. Specifically, we use LoRA alpha equals 2 times the LoRA rank, and a learning rate of `$1 \times 10^{-4}$`, which works the best for all the models."

The magnitude of this finding cannot be overstated. Using suboptimal settings (learning rate `$2 \times 10^{-5}$`, alpha `$16$`) caused performance to drop from `$71.8$` to `$65.5$` on LLaVA-Bench—a `$6.3$` point degradation that exceeds the gain from scaling 13B to 33B in some configurations.

**Practitioners must not use default LoRA hyperparameters from language-only fine-tuning.** The multimodal training regime requires:
- Learning rate: `$1 \times 10^{-4}$` (not the typical `$2 \times 10^{-5}$` for full fine-tuning)
- LoRA alpha: `$2 \times$` LoRA rank (e.g., alpha=128 for rank=64)
- Rank: 64 for 33B+ models, 64 sufficient for 13B models

**Data Mixing Strategy for Production Systems**

Table 3(a) and Table 5 provide guidance on when to mix language-only data with multimodal data:

| Scenario | Data Mixing Recommendation |
|----------|---------------------------|
| **Maximize multimodal performance** | Mix ShareGPT + LLaVA-80K (Table 3: +1.9 points for 33B/65B) |
| **Preserve chat-style fluency** | Consider no mixing (Vicuna-80 dropped from 85.3 to 80.3 with mixing in Table 5) |
| **Improve general knowledge (MMLU)** | Mix data (MMLU improved from 56.1 to 58.6 for 33B in Table 5) |
| **Limited training budget** | Use multimodal data only (mixing requires additional data curation) |

The trade-off is clear: data mixing improves multimodal capabilities and general knowledge but may shift conversational style away from the base language model's characteristics.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Checklist

For researchers and engineers seeking to reproduce or build upon this work, the following checklist captures critical implementation details:

**Model Checkpoints**
- [ ] 33B: Use Vicuna-33B checkpoint from HuggingFace (lmsys/vicuna-33b-v1.3)
- [ ] 65B: No public checkpoint existed; authors trained custom Vicuna-65B with 159M tokens
- [ ] 70B: Use LLaMA-2-70B-Chat checkpoint [15]

**Training Configuration**
- [ ] Stage 1: 558K LAION-CC-SBU samples, learning rate `$1 \times 10^{-4}$`, no weight decay
- [ ] Stage 2: LLaVA-80K dataset, 1 epoch for full fine-tuning
- [ ] Sequence length: 2048 tokens maximum
- [ ] Optimizer: DeepSpeed ZeRO3 (ZeRO2 for QLoRA)
- [ ] Image resolution: `$336 \times 336$` recommended (consistent 2-3 point gain per Table 3)

**LoRA-Specific Settings**
- [ ] Rank: 64 for 33B+ models
- [ ] Alpha: `$2 \times$` rank value
- [ ] Learning rate: `$1 \times 10^{-4}$` (not `$2 \times 10^{-5}$`)
- [ ] Batch size: 64 total (vs. 512 for full fine-tuning)

**Evaluation Pipeline**
- [ ] LLaVA-Bench: 24 images, 60 questions, GPT-4 (gpt4-0314) evaluator
- [ ] MM-VET: 200 images, 218 questions, GPT-4 (gpt4-0613) evaluator
- [ ] Beam search: Use beam=5 for +1 point gain with negligible latency (Table 1)

**Known Reproduction Risks**
- The custom-trained Vicuna-65B used fewer tokens (159M) than Vicuna-33B (370M), potentially affecting baseline language capability
- LLaVA-Bench has only 60 questions; results may not be statistically significant (authors acknowledge in Section 3.1)
- Training datasets are small relative to model size; reported performance represents a lower bound

### 7.5 The Path Forward

This study establishes that open-source LMMs can compete with proprietary systems when scaled appropriately. The 65B LLaVA model with data mixing achieved `$36.4$` on MM-VET (Table 2), surpassing all other open-source end-to-end LMMs reported in the benchmark. More importantly, the authors release code and checkpoints to "make state-of-the-art LMM research at a larger scale more accessible" (Abstract).

The immediate impact is democratization: researchers without access to massive compute clusters can now experiment with 33B-65B scale LMMs using LoRA/QLoRA on modest hardware. The longer-term impact is directional: the field now has empirical evidence that:

1. **Scaling works** for LMMs, but requires coordinated growth across modalities
2. **Efficient tuning suffices** for most applications, reducing barriers to entry
3. **Multimodal training can enhance** rather than degrade language capabilities

Future work should address the limitations identified in Section 6: larger training datasets, scaled vision encoders, longer context windows, and architecture variations. The baseline established by this study provides a reference point against which these improvements can be measured.

For practitioners deploying LMMs today, the recommendation is clear: start with 33B + LoRA + `$336 \times 336$` resolution + data mixing as the efficiency frontier, and scale to 65B only when the marginal performance gain justifies the additional cost. The scaling recipe in this paper transforms LMM development from an art into an engineering discipline with predictable trade-offs and reproducible outcomes.
