## 1. Executive Summary

This paper presents ATOKEN, the first unified visual tokenizer that achieves both high-fidelity reconstruction and semantic understanding across images, videos, and 3D assets within a single transformer framework, solving the fragmentation where existing methods specialize in either task or modality. By employing a sparse 4D latent space and adversarial-free training, ATOKEN achieves competitive performance including 0.21 rFID with 82.2% ImageNet accuracy for images, 3.01 rFVD with 40.2% MSRVTT retrieval for videos, and 28.28 PSNR with 90.9% classification accuracy for 3D. This unification matters because it enables next-generation multimodal AI systems to seamlessly handle both understanding and generation across all visual modalities without architectural duplication, mirroring the versatility of text tokenizers in large language models.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of ATOKEN, we must first recognize a fundamental asymmetry between how language models and vision models process data. In Large Language Models (LLMs), a single tokenizer (like BPE) converts all text—code, documents, multiple languages—into a unified stream of tokens. This shared representation allows a single model to scale efficiently and transfer knowledge across tasks. In contrast, visual AI remains fragmented. There is no equivalent "universal visual tokenizer." Instead, researchers build specialized systems for specific tasks and data types, creating silos that prevent the kind of generalization seen in text-based AI.

This section breaks down the three specific dimensions of this fragmentation, explains why bridging them is technically difficult, and positions ATOKEN within the existing landscape.

### 2.1 The Task Divide: Reconstruction vs. Understanding

The most critical gap in visual tokenization is the conflict between **reconstruction** (generation) and **semantic understanding**. These two tasks require fundamentally different types of information from the visual data.

*   **Reconstruction (Generation):** To rebuild an image or video from compressed data, a tokenizer must preserve low-level visual details—textures, edges, and exact color values. Methods optimized for this, such as `SD-VAE` (Stable Diffusion Variational Autoencoder) or `VQGAN`, excel at pixel fidelity but produce latent representations that lack high-level semantic meaning. They cannot easily answer "what is in this image?" without additional processing.
*   **Semantic Understanding:** To classify an image or align it with text (e.g., for a multimodal LLM), a model must extract high-level abstract features—objects, actions, concepts. Encoders like `CLIP` or `SigLIP2` are optimized for this alignment but discard the pixel-level information required to reconstruct the original visual content.

**The Conflict:** Historically, optimizing for one task degrades performance in the other. If you compress an image too aggressively for semantic efficiency, you lose texture details needed for reconstruction. If you preserve every pixel for reconstruction, the latent space becomes noisy and hard to align with text semantics.

Table 1 in the paper categorizes existing methods by these capabilities. Most fall strictly into one camp:
*   **Reconstruction Only:** `SD-VAE`, `Cosmos`, `Hunyuan`. These achieve low reconstruction error (e.g., high PSNR) but have no mechanism for text alignment.
*   **Understanding Only:** `SigLIP2`, `VideoPrism`. These achieve high classification accuracy but cannot generate pixels.
*   **Unified Attempts:** Recent works like `VILA-U` and `UniTok` attempt both but remain limited to **images only** and often struggle to match the performance of specialized models in either domain.

### 2.2 The Modality Divide: Images, Videos, and 3D

Beyond tasks, visual data exists in disparate formats, each traditionally requiring its own architecture.

*   **Images:** 2D spatial grids ($H \times W$).
*   **Videos:** Temporal sequences of 2D grids ($T \times H \times W$).
*   **3D Assets:** Complex geometric representations like meshes, voxels, or Gaussian splats.

Existing tokenizers are rarely universal across these modalities. For instance, video tokenizers like `OmniTokenizer` can often handle images (by treating them as single-frame videos), but they cannot process 3D data. Conversely, 3D-specific tokenizers like `Trellis-SLAT` are designed for geometric structures and cannot leverage the massive datasets available for images and videos.

This fragmentation forces developers to maintain separate model weights and pipelines for each modality. A multimodal system that needs to understand an image, generate a video, and synthesize a 3D object currently requires three distinct visual encoders, increasing computational cost and preventing knowledge transfer between modalities. ATOKEN aims to collapse these into a single **4D latent space** (covering time $t$, spatial $x, y$, and depth $z$), allowing one model to process all three natively.

### 2.3 The Architectural Divide: Stability vs. Scalability

Even when researchers attempt to build unified tokenizers, they face a third hurdle: architectural trade-offs between Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Transformers.

*   **Convolutional Architectures:** Most high-quality reconstruction tokenizers (e.g., `SD-VAE`, `Hunyuan`) use CNNs. They are stable to train and handle arbitrary resolutions well. However, they exhibit diminishing returns when scaling model parameters and lack the flexibility needed for long-context understanding tasks.
*   **Transformer Architectures:** Transformers scale efficiently and are the standard for understanding (e.g., `SigLIP2`). However, when applied to visual tokenization for reconstruction, they suffer from severe training instabilities. Specifically, transformer-based tokenizers often rely on **Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)** training to achieve sharp reconstructions. As noted in Section 3.3 and illustrated in **Figure 4**, GAN training with transformers often leads to mode collapse, where the discriminator overpowers the generator, causing reconstruction quality to degrade over time.

This creates a catch-22: use CNNs for stable reconstruction but poor scaling, or use Transformers for scaling but unstable training. ATOKEN positions itself as a pure transformer architecture that bypasses this instability through an **adversarial-free training objective**, enabling the scalability of transformers without the fragility of GANs.

### 2.4 Why Unification Matters

Solving these fragmentation problems is not merely an academic exercise; it is a prerequisite for the next generation of multimodal AI systems.

1.  **Efficient Scaling:** A unified tokenizer allows a single model to learn from all available visual data (images, videos, 3D) simultaneously. As shown in the paper's ablation studies (Section 4.5), multimodal training can actually **enhance** single-modality performance (e.g., video and 3D training improving image reconstruction) rather than causing interference, provided the model has sufficient capacity.
2.  **Multimodal LLMs:** Current Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) typically use frozen vision encoders (like `CLIP`) that cannot generate visual content. By providing a tokenizer that supports both understanding and generation, ATOKEN enables MLLMs to not only "see" but also "draw" or "render" within the same latent space.
3.  **Cross-Modal Transfer:** A unified representation allows techniques learned in one modality to benefit others. For example, geometric understanding from 3D data can improve temporal consistency in video, while semantic richness from images can improve color consistency in 3D generation.

### 2.5 Positioning Relative to Existing Work

ATOKEN distinguishes itself by being the first method to provide full coverage across all three dimensions of fragmentation. **Table 1** provides a comprehensive comparison:

*   **Task Coverage:** Unlike `SD-VAE` (reconstruction only) or `SigLIP2` (understanding only), ATOKEN supports both simultaneously.
*   **Modality Coverage:** Unlike `UniTok` (images only) or `Trellis-SLAT` (3D only), ATOKEN handles images, videos, and 3D assets in a single framework.
*   **Token Flexibility:** ATOKEN supports both **continuous tokens** (for high-fidelity reconstruction) and **discrete tokens** (for autoregressive generation compatible with LLMs), whereas most methods commit to one representation type.
*   **Training Stability:** Unlike prior transformer tokenizers that rely on unstable GAN objectives, ATOKEN uses a combination of perceptual and Gram matrix losses to achieve stable optimization without adversarial training.

In summary, the field currently lacks a "BPE for vision"—a simple, unified interface that converts all visual data into a common language for AI models. ATOKEN addresses this gap by proposing a sparse 4D representation and a stable transformer architecture designed to unify tasks, modalities, and token types without compromising performance in any single domain.

## 3. Technical Approach

This section explains how ATOKEN actually works — the architecture, representations, training objectives, and curriculum that enable unified visual tokenization across images, videos, and 3D. We walk through each design choice with the reasoning behind it, so you understand not just what the system does, but why it was built this way.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

ATOKEN is a transformer-based visual tokenizer that converts images, videos, and 3D assets into a shared 4D latent representation supporting both reconstruction (generating pixels back) and understanding (aligning with text semantics). The system solves the fragmentation problem by encoding all visual modalities into sparse feature-coordinate pairs in a unified 4D space (time, height, width, depth), then using complementary projection heads to extract task-specific representations without duplicating the core encoder.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

Think of ATOKEN as a pipeline with five major components connected in sequence:

1.  **Space-Time Patchification Module**: Takes raw visual input (image, video, or multi-view 3D renderings) and partitions it into non-overlapping space-time patches of size $t \times p \times p$, producing initial patch features with 4D position coordinates.
2.  **Sparse Transformer Encoder**: Processes the patchified input through 27 transformer blocks (hidden dimension $d=1152$, 16 attention heads), initialized from pretrained SigLIP2 weights, with 4D Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) applied at every attention layer to provide position awareness across all four dimensions.
3.  **Latent Projection Layer**: Splits the encoder output into two parallel paths — one projects to lower-dimensional reconstruction latents $z_r$ (with optional FSQ quantization for discrete tokens), the other aggregates via attention pooling into global semantic representations $z_s$ for text alignment.
4.  **Task-Specific Decoder**: For reconstruction, a symmetric transformer decoder (27 blocks, trained from scratch) maps latents back to pixel space for images/videos or to Gaussian splatting parameters for 3D assets.
5.  **Loss Computation Module**: Computes three loss components — reconstruction loss (pixel + perceptual + Gram + CLIP), semantic loss (KL distillation or sigmoid alignment with text), and KL regularization — then combines them with fixed coefficients for backpropagation.

Information flows from raw input through patchification, encoding, projection, and decoding, with gradients flowing back through all components except the frozen text encoder used for semantic alignment.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

We will explain the technical components in the following order, building from representation to architecture to training:

*   **Unified 4D Latent Representation**: First, we explain how images, videos, and 3D assets are all mapped into the same sparse 4D coordinate system — this is the foundational insight that enables modality unification.
*   **Transformer Architecture with 4D RoPE**: Next, we describe the encoder and decoder structure, including how SigLIP2 is extended to 4D and why 4D Rotary Position Embeddings are critical for handling arbitrary resolutions and temporal lengths.
*   **Adversarial-Free Training Objectives**: Then, we break down the loss function components, explaining why Gram matrix loss replaces GAN training and how semantic alignment is achieved without sacrificing reconstruction quality.
*   **Progressive Training Curriculum**: Finally, we detail the four-stage training strategy that gradually introduces modalities and capabilities, explaining why this curriculum prevents catastrophic forgetting and enables cross-modal learning benefits.

This order mirrors how the system was built: first define the representation, then build the architecture to process it, then design the objectives to train it, and finally structure the training process to stabilize learning.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Paper Type and Core Idea**

This is a systems paper that proposes a unified visual tokenizer architecture, and the core idea is that all visual modalities can be represented within a shared sparse 4D latent space where different modalities naturally occupy different subspaces, enabling a single transformer to process images, videos, and 3D assets without architectural modifications.

**Unified 4D Latent Representation**

The fundamental representation in ATOKEN is a set of feature-coordinate pairs in 4D space, formally expressed as:

$$z = \{(z_i, p_i)\}_{i=1}^{L}, \quad z_i \in \mathbb{R}^C, \quad p_i \in \{0, 1, \ldots, N-1\}^4$$

where $z_i$ represents the latent feature vector at position $p_i = [t, x, y, z]$ in 4D space (temporal and three spatial coordinates), $N$ defines the resolution along each axis, $C$ is the feature dimension (48 for continuous, quantized for discrete), and $L$ is the number of active locations in the sparse representation.

This sparse representation unifies all modalities by activating only the relevant dimensions for each input type: images occupy the $(x, y)$ plane at $t=0$ and $z=0$, videos extend along the temporal axis with $z=0$, and 3D assets are represented as surface voxels in $(x, y, z)$ space with $t=0$.

The coordinates $(x, y, z)$ serve different semantic purposes across modalities: in 3D, they represent actual physical entity occupancy locations, while in images and videos, they function as grid indices for pixel positions. This dual interpretation does not compromise generalization because the 4D Rotary Position Embeddings (described below) provide relative position awareness that adapts to each modality's structure.

For 3D assets specifically, ATOKEN adapts the Trellis-SLAT approach by rendering multi-view images from spherically sampled cameras, applying the unified space-time patchification to each view, then aggregating features into voxel space through back-projection. Each voxel in a $64^3$ grid gathers and averages patch features from its nearest viewpoint, which differs from the original Trellis-SLAT that averaged across all views — this modification preserves view-specific details while maintaining compatibility with the unified pipeline.

**Space-Time Patch Embedding**

The patchification process converts raw visual input into transformer-compatible tokens through a unified scheme that handles all modalities consistently. Given an input $x \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times H \times W \times 3}$, the system partitions it into non-overlapping space-time patches of size $t \times p \times p$, where $t$ is the temporal patch size and $p$ is the spatial patch size.

For images where $T=1$, the system applies temporal zero-padding to create $t$-frame patches, ensuring consistent dimensions across all modalities without requiring separate image-specific processing paths. Videos are directly partitioned along both spatial and temporal dimensions without modification.

The patch size configuration varies by training stage: Stage 1 uses $1 \times 16 \times 16$ patches for 64-512 pixel images, Stage 2 expands to $4 \times 16 \times 16$ patches supporting 64-1024 pixel images and 64-512 pixel videos, and Stage 3 further expands to support 64-2048 pixel images, 64-1024 pixel videos, and $64 \times 64 \times 64$ voxel 3D assets.

All patch features — whether from images, videos, or aggregated 3D views — are flattened and passed through a shared linear projection layer to produce the initial embeddings for the transformer encoder, ensuring that all modalities enter the encoder through the same interface.

**Sparse Transformer Encoder**

The encoder $E$ extends the pretrained SigLIP2 vision tower (specifically SigLIP-SO400M-patch16-naflex) from 2D images to 4D representations through two key modifications that preserve the pretrained semantic priors while enabling multimodal processing.

First, the patch embedding layer is generalized from 2D spatial blocks to space-time blocks of size $t \times p \times p$, with zero-initialized temporal weights that preserve the original image features during early training — this initialization strategy prevents the pretrained 2D knowledge from being corrupted when temporal dimensions are introduced.

Second, the encoder augments SigLIP2's learnable 2D position embeddings with 4D Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) applied in every attention layer, providing relative position awareness across all $(t, x, y, z)$ dimensions rather than just spatial positions.

The encoder contains 27 transformer blocks with hidden dimension $d=1152$ and 16 attention heads, yielding approximately 800M parameters for the So400m variant. The Base variant uses 12 transformer blocks with $d=768$ and 12 attention heads, yielding approximately 192M parameters — the ablation study in Section 4.5 shows this capacity difference is critical for successful multimodal learning.

The encoder processes sparse structured representations as sets of feature-position pairs rather than dense grids, enabling efficient handling of all modalities with native support for arbitrary resolutions and temporal lengths without padding or packing overhead.

**Sparse Transformer Decoder**

The decoder $D$ shares the encoder's transformer architecture (27 blocks, $d=1152$, 16 heads) but is trained from scratch for reconstruction rather than initialized from pretrained weights. It maps structured latents back to visual outputs through task-specific heads that vary by modality.

For images and videos, the decoder projects directly to pixel space:

$$D_P: \{(z_i, p_i)\}_{i=1}^{L} \rightarrow x \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times H \times W \times 3}$$

treating images as single-frame videos ($T=1$) and discarding temporal padding following the approach in prior video tokenization work. This unified treatment means the same decoder weights handle both images and videos without modality-specific branches.

For 3D assets, the decoder first produces pixel-space features, then applies an additional layer to generate Gaussian splatting parameters for efficient rendering:

$$D_{GS}: \{(z_i, p_i)\}_{i=1}^{L} \rightarrow \{\{(o_i^k, c_i^k, s_i^k, \alpha_i^k, r_i^k)\}_{k=1}^{K}\}_{i=1}^{L}$$

where each location generates $K$ Gaussians with five parameters: position offset $o$, color $c$, scale $s$, opacity $\alpha$, and rotation $r$. Following the Trellis-SLAT approach, Gaussian positions are constrained to remain near their source voxels using $x_i^k = p_i + \tanh(o_i^k)$, ensuring local feature coherence and preventing artifacts from Gaussians drifting too far from their assigned voxel locations.

**Latent Projection for Dual Tasks**

From the unified structured latents $z = \{(z_i, p_i)\}$, ATOKEN extracts representations for both reconstruction and understanding through complementary projections that share the same encoded features without architectural duplication.

For reconstruction, each latent is projected to a lower-dimensional space $z_r = W_r(z)$ with KL regularization applied to constrain the latent distribution. Optionally, Finite Scalar Quantization (FSQ) is applied to produce discrete codes $\tilde{z}_r = \text{FSQ}(z_r)$, enabling compatibility with autoregressive generative models that require discrete tokens.

For understanding, latents are aggregated via attention pooling (following the CLIP and SigLIP approach) into a global representation $\bar{z}$, which is then projected to $z_s = W_s(\bar{z})$ for alignment with text embeddings from a frozen text encoder. This dual projection design allows joint optimization of both tasks — the same encoded features $z$ support both pixel-level reconstruction through individual latents and semantic understanding through their aggregated representation.

The reconstruction latents use 48 dimensions for continuous tokens (ATOKEN-So/C) or are quantized to 8 discrete tokens from 4096-entry codebooks with 2 bits per dimension (ATOKEN-So/D). The 48-dimensional latents are partitioned into 8 groups of 6 dimensions, each quantized to 4 levels, yielding the discrete representation.

**Adversarial-Free Training Objectives**

ATOKEN jointly optimizes for reconstruction fidelity and semantic understanding through a combined loss function that avoids adversarial training entirely:

$$\mathcal{L} = \lambda_{rec}\mathcal{L}_{rec} + \lambda_{sem}\mathcal{L}_{sem} + \lambda_{KL}\mathcal{L}_{KL}$$

where $\lambda_{rec} = 0.2$, $\lambda_{sem} = 1.0$, and $\lambda_{KL} = 10^{-8}$ balance the three components throughout all training stages.

The decision to avoid GAN-based training is motivated by empirical instability observed with transformer architectures. As shown in **Figure 4(a)**, GAN training causes the discriminator to rapidly dominate the generator, leading to diverging logits and degraded reconstruction quality (rFID increases over training steps). This instability has been observed in prior transformer-based tokenizers and is incompatible with the sparse 3D representations used in ATOKEN.

To develop an alternative, the authors analyzed reconstruction error by decomposing rFID into mean and covariance components, as shown in **Figure 4(b)**. The analysis revealed that the covariance component — capturing second-order statistics like texture and style — dominates at approximately 86.6% of total error, while mean features contribute only 13.4%. This insight motivated adopting Gram matrix loss, which directly optimizes feature covariance without adversarial training.

The Gram matrix loss is computed as:

$$\mathcal{L}_{Gram}(x, \hat{x}) = \sum_{l} \|G(\Phi_l(x)) - G(\Phi_l(\hat{x}))\|_F^2$$

where $G(F) = FF^\top$ is the Gram matrix for feature map $F$ from layer $l$ of network $\Phi$, and $\|\cdot\|_F$ denotes the Frobenius norm. As shown in **Figure 4(c)**, this achieves superior and stable reconstruction throughout training compared to GAN-based approaches.

**Reconstruction Loss Components**

For images, the reconstruction loss combines four complementary components that supervise different aspects of visual fidelity:

$$\mathcal{L}_{rec}^I = \lambda_1\mathcal{L}_1 + \lambda_{LPIPS}\mathcal{L}_{LPIPS} + \lambda_{GRAM}\mathcal{L}_{GRAM} + \lambda_{CLIP}\mathcal{L}_{CLIP}$$

where $\mathcal{L}_1 = \|x - \hat{x}\|_1$ provides pixel-level supervision, $\mathcal{L}_{LPIPS}$ measures perceptual similarity using a pretrained network, $\mathcal{L}_{GRAM}$ captures texture and style through second-order statistics, and $\mathcal{L}_{CLIP}$ enforces semantic consistency by comparing CLIP embeddings of original and reconstructed images.

The loss coefficients within reconstruction are set to $\lambda_1 = 1.0$, $\lambda_{LPIPS} = 10.0$, $\lambda_{GRAM} = 10^3$, $\lambda_{CLIP} = 1.0$, and temperature $\tau = 2.0$ for the CLIP component. The large Gram loss weight ($10^3$) reflects its importance in capturing texture quality, which the rFID decomposition identified as the dominant error source.

For video and 3D assets, the reconstruction loss uses only $\mathcal{L}_1$ for computational efficiency, relying on cross-modal transfer from image training to provide texture and semantic supervision. This design choice acknowledges that video and 3D datasets are smaller than image datasets, so the model learns detailed reconstruction primarily from images and transfers those capabilities to other modalities.

Reconstruction losses are normalized over patches rather than summed, providing stable gradients across resolutions — this differs from some prior work that sums losses, which can cause gradient instability when processing variable-resolution inputs.

**Semantic Loss Components**

Semantic alignment is achieved through modality-specific objectives that align visual representations $z_s$ with text embeddings from a frozen text encoder.

For images, the system distills knowledge from the frozen SigLIP2 vision encoder by minimizing the KL divergence between temperature-scaled vision-text similarity distributions:

$$\mathcal{L}_{sem}^I = \text{KL}\left(\text{softmax}(\tau^{-1}s_{teacher}) \parallel \text{softmax}(\tau^{-1}s_{student})\right)$$

where $s_{teacher}$ and $s_{student}$ are vision-text similarity scores from frozen SigLIP2 and the ATOKEN model respectively, both paired with the same frozen text encoder, and $\tau$ is the temperature parameter. This distillation approach preserves the pretrained image semantics from SigLIP2 while allowing the model to learn reconstruction capabilities.

For videos and 3D, the system directly optimizes alignment using the sigmoid loss from SigLIP, which proves more stable for the smaller batch sizes typical in these domains. This dual strategy — distillation for images, direct optimization for videos and 3D — preserves strong pretrained image semantics while enabling efficient learning for modalities without large-scale pretrained encoders.

**Progressive Training Curriculum**

ATOKEN employs a four-stage progressive curriculum that builds capabilities incrementally from image foundations to video dynamics to 3D geometry, with optional discrete quantization in the final stage. This curriculum is illustrated in **Figure 5** and configured as shown in **Table 2**.

**Stage 1: Image Foundation** (200k steps) starts from pretrained SigLIP2 and establishes core visual representations by adding image reconstruction capabilities. Images are processed using $4 \times 16 \times 16$ space-time patches with temporal padding for consistency, employing 32 latent dimensions. Training uses variable resolution sampling from 64 to 512 pixels, with $\mathcal{L}_1$ loss computed at native resolution while perceptual losses ($\mathcal{L}_{LPIPS}$, $\mathcal{L}_{CLIP}$, $\mathcal{L}_{Gram}$) use $224 \times 224$ interpolation to match their pretrained feature extractors. Task sampling is 100% image reconstruction during this stage.

**Stage 2: Video Dynamics** (200k steps) extends to temporal sequences, expanding latent dimensions from 32 to 48 to accommodate motion complexity. Resolution capabilities increase to 1024 for images and 512 for videos. The system employs temporal tiling (16-32 frames → 4-8 latent frames) with adaptive sampling: stride 1-3 for temporal consistency or 4-12 for diversity in reconstruction, and 1 FPS up to 64 frames for understanding tasks. A KV-caching mechanism eliminates redundant computation across tiles while maintaining temporal coherence, as illustrated in **Figure 6**. Task sampling ratios are 22.2% image reconstruction, 11.1% video understanding, and 66.6% video reconstruction.

**Stage 3: 3D Geometry** (50k steps) incorporates 3D assets as active voxels in $64^3$ grids, using Gaussian splatting for reconstruction and attention pooling for understanding. Resolution further increases to 2048 for images and 1024 for videos. Joint optimization across all three modalities prevents catastrophic forgetting while leveraging cross-modal learning — the geometric semantics from 3D and temporal dynamics from video enhance image reconstruction quality, as evidenced by the 19% rFID improvement from Stage 1 to Stage 3. Task sampling ratios are 22.2% image reconstruction, 11.1% video understanding, 44.4% video reconstruction, 11.1% 3D understanding, and 11.1% 3D reconstruction.

**Stage 4: Discrete Tokenization** (100k steps) optionally adds FSQ quantization for discrete generation tasks. The 48-dimensional latents are partitioned into 8 groups of 6 dimensions, each quantized to 4 levels, yielding 8 discrete tokens from 4096-entry codebooks. The entire encoder and decoder are finetuned to adapt all modalities to discrete tokens, enabling compatibility with discrete generative models across all visual domains. Task sampling ratios match Stage 3.

**Implementation Details and Training Configuration**

The model is optimized using AdamW with $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.95$, and weight decay 0.1. The learning rate follows linear warmup for 2,000 steps to $\eta_{max} = 3 \times 10^{-4}$, then cosine annealing to $\eta_{min} = 3 \times 10^{-5}$. Given the pretrained encoder, a reduced learning rate $\eta_{encoder} = 0.1 \times \eta_{base}$ is applied to encoder parameters, and exponential moving average is used with decay rate $\gamma = 0.9999$.

Training utilizes 256 H100 GPUs with adaptive global batch sizes optimized for each task's memory requirements. Image understanding maintains 8,192 samples throughout all stages, while reconstruction tasks scale with complexity: image reconstruction uses 1,024-4,096 samples, video reconstruction uses 512-1,024 samples, and 3D reconstruction uses 256-512 samples. The four-stage curriculum requires a total of 138k GPU hours across all stages (approximately 22 days with 256 GPUs).

Training data follows the progressive curriculum: DFN, Open Images, and internal datasets for images; WebVid and TextVR for video understanding with Panda70M for reconstruction; Objaverse with Cap3D annotations for 3D. Datasets are sampled proportionally to their size, with task ratios detailed in **Table 2**.

**Design Choices and Alternatives Considered**

The decision to use a pure transformer architecture rather than hybrid CNN-transformer designs was motivated by scaling considerations — convolutional tokenizers exhibit diminishing returns when scaling model parameters, while transformers scale more efficiently. However, this choice required solving the GAN instability problem, which was addressed through the adversarial-free Gram matrix loss.

The sparse 4D representation was chosen over dense representations because it enables efficient handling of arbitrary resolutions and temporal lengths without padding overhead. Dense representations would require fixed resolution inputs or costly dynamic padding, while sparse representations naturally accommodate variable-sized inputs through the feature-coordinate pair format.

The progressive curriculum was designed to prevent catastrophic forgetting — starting from pretrained SigLIP2 ensures strong semantic priors are preserved, then gradually introducing reconstruction and additional modalities allows the model to learn new capabilities without overwriting existing knowledge. The ablation study in Section 4.5 shows this is critical: the Base model (192M parameters) suffers severe degradation when expanding beyond single-modality training, while the So400m model (800M parameters) improves continuously across stages.

The choice of 48-dimensional latents balances compression efficiency with reconstruction quality — fewer dimensions would degrade reconstruction fidelity, while more dimensions would increase computational cost for downstream generative models. The discrete variant's 8 tokens from 4096-entry codebooks provides compatibility with autoregressive LLMs while maintaining reasonable reconstruction quality.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the most novel contributions of ATOKEN, distinguishing between fundamental innovations that change how we think about visual tokenization and incremental improvements that refine existing approaches. For each insight, we explain what prior work could not achieve, why ATOKEN succeeds, and what this means for the field.

### 4.1 Adversarial-Free Training Achieves State-of-the-Art Reconstruction

**Innovation Type: Fundamental**

Prior transformer-based visual tokenizers relied heavily on Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) training to achieve sharp, high-fidelity reconstructions. This was considered necessary because standard reconstruction losses (like L1 or L2) tend to produce blurry outputs that lack high-frequency texture details. However, GAN training introduces well-documented instabilities: mode collapse, discriminator-generator imbalance, and sensitivity to hyperparameters.

ATOKEN's key insight is that **GANs are not necessary** if you directly optimize the dominant source of reconstruction error. The authors' analysis in **Figure 4(b)** reveals that approximately 86.6% of rFID error stems from covariance (texture/style) mismatches, while only 13.4% comes from mean feature differences. This decomposition motivated replacing adversarial training with Gram matrix loss, which directly optimizes second-order feature statistics.

**What Makes This Different:**

| Aspect | Prior Transformer Tokenizers | ATOKEN |
|--------|------------------------------|--------|
| Training Objective | GAN-based (unstable) | Gram + Perceptual Loss (stable) |
| Training Behavior | Discriminator dominates over time (**Figure 4a**) | Monotonic improvement throughout training |
| 3D Compatibility | Incompatible with sparse representations | Works naturally with sparse 4D latents |
| Scaling | Degrades at larger model sizes | Improves with model capacity |

**Significance:** This insight removes a major barrier to scaling transformer-based tokenizers. GAN training becomes increasingly unstable as model size grows, limiting the practical ceiling for transformer tokenizers. By eliminating adversarial training, ATOKEN enables stable optimization at 800M parameters (So400m) with clear paths to further scaling. The result is **0.21 rFID on ImageNet** — competitive with or superior to GAN-trained methods like `VAVAE` (0.279 rFID) and `UniTok` (0.36 rFID) — without any of the training fragility.

This is a **fundamental innovation** because it changes the optimization paradigm for visual tokenization. Prior work assumed GANs were necessary for quality; ATOKEN demonstrates they are a workaround for inadequate loss design, not a requirement.

### 4.2 Multimodal Training Enhances Rather Than Degrades Single-Modality Performance

**Innovation Type: Fundamental**

A widely held assumption in multimodal learning is that training on multiple tasks or modalities introduces interference — the model must compromise, leading to degraded performance on individual domains compared to specialized single-modality models. This "trade-off hypothesis" has shaped how researchers design multimodal systems, often using separate branches or task-specific parameters to avoid interference.

ATOKEN's progressive curriculum reveals the opposite: **multimodal training can enhance single-modality performance** when the model has sufficient capacity. As shown in **Table 4**, image reconstruction improves across training stages:

- **Stage 1 (Image Only):** 0.258 rFID
- **Stage 2 ( + Video):** 0.246 rFID
- **Stage 3 ( + 3D):** 0.209 rFID

This represents a **19% improvement** in image reconstruction quality after adding video and 3D capabilities — counter to the interference hypothesis.

**What Makes This Different:**

Prior unified tokenizers like `VILA-U` and `UniTok` either:
1. Accept performance degradation as inevitable when adding tasks
2. Use architectural separation (different encoder branches) to minimize interference
3. Remain limited to images only to avoid cross-modal complications

ATOKEN demonstrates that with the right representation (sparse 4D latents) and sufficient capacity (800M vs. 192M parameters), cross-modal learning provides **complementary signals**:
- **Temporal dynamics from video** improve motion consistency in image reconstruction
- **Geometric understanding from 3D** enhances spatial reasoning for image structures
- **Semantic richness from images** transfers to improve color consistency in 3D generation (**Figure 11**)

**Significance:** This finding challenges how we think about multimodal model design. If multimodal training can enhance rather than degrade performance, then unified models should be the default, not a compromise. The ablation study in **Figure 7** confirms this is capacity-dependent: the Base model (192M) degrades by 49% in rFID when expanding beyond images, while So400m (800M) improves by 19%. This establishes a **minimum capacity threshold** for successful multimodal tokenization.

This is a **fundamental innovation** because it reframes multimodal learning from a compromise to an opportunity. The insight extends beyond tokenizers — it suggests that unified foundation models across vision modalities may outperform specialized systems when properly scaled.

### 4.3 Sparse 4D Representation Unifies Disparate Visual Formats

**Innovation Type: Fundamental**

Prior work treats images, videos, and 3D assets as fundamentally different data types requiring separate architectures. Video tokenizers like `OmniTokenizer` and `Hunyuan` handle images as single-frame videos but cannot process 3D. 3D tokenizers like `Trellis-SLAT` are designed for geometric structures and cannot leverage image/video pretraining. Understanding encoders like `SigLIP2` process videos frame-by-frame without temporal compression.

ATOKEN's sparse 4D representation treats all modalities as **subspaces of a unified coordinate system**:

$$p_i = [t, x, y, z] \in \{0, 1, \ldots, N-1\}^4$$

where each modality activates only relevant dimensions:
- **Images:** $(x, y)$ plane at $t=0, z=0$
- **Videos:** $(t, x, y)$ volume at $z=0$
- **3D:** $(x, y, z)$ volume at $t=0$

**What Makes This Different:**

| Method | Image | Video | 3D | Shared Representation |
|--------|-------|-------|-----|----------------------|
| `SD-VAE` | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | No |
| `Hunyuan` | ✓ | ✓ | ✗ | Partial (image=video frame) |
| `Trellis-SLAT` | ✗ | ✗ | ✓ | No |
| `UniTok` | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | No |
| **ATOKEN** | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | **Yes (4D sparse)** |

The key innovation is not just supporting all modalities, but doing so through a **single encoder-decoder pair** without modality-specific branches. The 4D Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) provide relative position awareness that adapts to each modality's structure — the same attention mechanism processes a 2D image patch and a 3D voxel without modification.

**Significance:** This enables true knowledge transfer across modalities. For example, the 90.9% classification accuracy on Toys4k (3D) exceeds the specialized `Trellis-SLAT` baseline, despite ATOKEN being trained on all three modalities simultaneously. The reconstruction quality (28.28 PSNR vs. 26.97 for Trellis-SLAT) benefits from color understanding transferred from images and videos.

This is a **fundamental innovation** because it establishes a common "coordinate language" for visual data. Just as BPE tokenizers provide a unified interface for all text types, ATOKEN's 4D representation provides a unified interface for all visual modalities. This enables downstream systems (like multimodal LLMs) to process images, videos, and 3D through a single vision encoder without architectural duplication.

### 4.4 Capacity Threshold for Successful Multimodal Tokenization

**Innovation Type: Fundamental Discovery**

The ablation study comparing Base (192M) and So400m (800M) models in **Figure 7** reveals a critical insight: **multimodal tokenization has a minimum capacity requirement**. Below this threshold, adding modalities causes interference and degradation. Above it, cross-modal learning provides net benefits.

**Evidence from Figure 7:**

| Metric | Base Model | So400m Model |
|--------|------------|--------------|
| ImageNet rFID (Stage 1) | 0.323 | 0.258 |
| ImageNet rFID (Stage 3) | 0.483 (**49% degradation**) | 0.209 (**19% improvement**) |
| Video PSNR Trend | Declines across stages | Improves from 32.51 to 33.11 |
| Zero-shot Accuracy | 77.2% → degrades | 82.7% → stable at 82.2% |

**What Makes This Different:**

Prior work on unified tokenizers did not systematically analyze the capacity requirements for multimodal learning. Methods like `VILA-U` and `UniTok` report final performance without ablation on model size, making it unclear whether their limitations stem from architectural choices or insufficient capacity.

ATOKEN's controlled comparison isolates capacity as the key variable, holding architecture, training curriculum, and data constant. The result is a clear threshold: approximately **4× parameter increase** (192M → 800M) transforms multimodal training from harmful to beneficial.

**Significance:** This finding has practical implications for designing unified vision models:
1. **Small models should remain specialized** — attempting multimodal unification below the capacity threshold will degrade performance
2. **Large models should be unified** — the cross-modal benefits only emerge at sufficient scale
3. **Scaling laws differ for multimodal tokenizers** — the benefits of additional parameters are amplified when multiple modalities are present

This is a **fundamental discovery** because it establishes design principles for future multimodal systems. Researchers can now make informed decisions about when unification is appropriate based on available model capacity, rather than treating multimodal training as universally beneficial or universally problematic.

### 4.5 Dual Token Representation (Continuous + Discrete) in Single Framework

**Innovation Type: Incremental Innovation**

Most visual tokenizers commit to either continuous representations (for reconstruction quality, e.g., `SD-VAE`, `TAE`) or discrete representations (for LLM compatibility, e.g., `VQGAN`, `GigaTok`). Recent unified attempts like `UniTok` support discrete tokens but remain image-only.

ATOKEN supports **both representations within the same framework** through optional FSQ quantization in Stage 4. The continuous variant (ATOKEN-So/C) achieves 0.21 rFID on ImageNet, while the discrete variant (ATOKEN-So/D) achieves 0.38 rFID — a reasonable trade-off for LLM compatibility.

**What Makes This Different:**

| Method | Continuous | Discrete | Modalities |
|--------|------------|----------|------------|
| `SD-VAE` | ✓ | ✗ | Image |
| `VQGAN` | ✗ | ✓ | Image |
| `UniTok` | ✓ | ✓ | Image only |
| **ATOKEN** | ✓ | ✓ | **Image, Video, 3D** |

The key distinction is that ATOKEN extends discrete tokenization to **video and 3D**, not just images. The discrete variant achieves 29.75 PSNR on DAVIS (video) and 28.17 PSNR on Toys4k (3D), pioneering multimodal discrete tokenization.

**Significance:** This enables a single tokenizer to serve both diffusion-based generation (continuous tokens) and autoregressive LLM-based generation (discrete tokens). For downstream applications, this means:
- **Multimodal LLMs** can use discrete tokens for efficient sequence modeling
- **Diffusion models** can use continuous tokens for maximum reconstruction quality
- **Both systems share the same visual encoder**, reducing deployment complexity

This is an **incremental innovation** because FSQ quantization itself is not novel (prior work by Mentzer et al., 2023). The contribution is extending discrete tokenization to all three modalities within a unified framework, demonstrating that quantization need not be modality-specific.

---

### Summary: Innovation Classification

| Innovation | Type | Significance |
|------------|------|--------------|
| Adversarial-free Gram matrix training | Fundamental | Enables stable transformer scaling without GANs |
| Multimodal enhancement (not degradation) | Fundamental | Reframes multimodal learning as beneficial, not compromising |
| Sparse 4D unified representation | Fundamental | First common coordinate system for all visual modalities |
| Capacity threshold discovery | Fundamental Discovery | Establishes minimum model size for successful unification |
| Dual continuous/discrete tokens | Incremental | Extends discrete tokenization to video and 3D |

The first four innovations represent **paradigm shifts** in how we approach visual tokenization — they change assumptions about what is necessary (GANs), what is possible (multimodal enhancement), and how to design systems (capacity thresholds, unified representations). The fifth innovation is an **engineering extension** that increases practical utility without changing fundamental principles.

Together, these insights position ATOKEN not as an incremental improvement to existing tokenizers, but as a foundation for next-generation multimodal AI systems that can process all visual data through a single, scalable, stable interface.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how ATOKEN was evaluated, what the results show, and whether the experiments convincingly support the paper's claims. We break down the evaluation methodology, summarize quantitative results with specific numbers, analyze ablation studies, and identify limitations that temper the conclusions.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Benchmarks**

The paper evaluates ATOKEN across standardized benchmarks for each modality, ensuring fair comparison with prior work:

| Modality | Reconstruction Benchmark | Understanding Benchmark |
|----------|-------------------------|------------------------|
| **Image** | ImageNet (256×256), COCO | ImageNet zero-shot classification, COCO/Flickr30k retrieval |
| **Video** | DAVIS (1080p, 50 videos), TokenBench (720p, 471 videos) | MSRVTT, MSVD text-to-video retrieval |
| **3D** | Toys4k (multi-view renderings) | Toys4k zero-shot classification |

For downstream applications, the paper uses:
- **Multimodal LLMs**: 7 image understanding benchmarks (RW-QA, AI2D, SQA, MMMU, MathVISTA, OCRBench, TextVQA) and 6 video understanding benchmarks (VideoMME, PercepTest, NExT-QA, LongVideoBench, MLVU, LVBench)
- **Generation**: ImageNet class-conditional generation (gFID, Inception Score), text-to-video (VBench benchmark), image-to-3D synthesis

**Metrics**

The evaluation uses distinct metrics for reconstruction quality versus semantic understanding:

- **Reconstruction**: PSNR (peak signal-to-noise ratio), SSIM (structural similarity), LPIPS (learned perceptual image patch similarity), rFID (reconstruction Fréchet Inception Distance for images), rFVD (reconstruction Fréchet Video Distance for videos)
- **Understanding**: Zero-shot classification accuracy, text-to-image/image-to-text retrieval (R@1, R@5, R@10)
- **Generation**: gFID (generation FID), Inception Score, Precision, Recall, CLIP-Score, Pick-Score, GenEval

**Baselines and Fair Comparison Protocol**

A critical strength of this evaluation is the **unified re-evaluation protocol**. As noted in **Table 4**, the authors re-evaluated all baseline methods using official implementations with standardized settings (all images resized and center-cropped to 256×256, metrics computed using identical scripts). This addresses a common problem in tokenizer papers where reported numbers come from different evaluation pipelines, making cross-method comparison unreliable.

> "Note that our reproduced results may differ from original papers due to standardized evaluation settings, but provide consistent cross-model comparison." — **Table 4 caption**

**Hardware and Training Configuration**

Training utilized 256 H100 GPUs with adaptive batch sizes per task. The four-stage curriculum required 138k GPU hours total (approximately 22 days). Fixed loss coefficients were maintained throughout: $\lambda_{rec} = 0.2$, $\lambda_{sem} = 1.0$, $\lambda_{KL} = 10^{-8}$. Within reconstruction loss, $\lambda_{GRAM} = 10^3$ reflects the importance of texture optimization identified in the rFID decomposition analysis.

**Image Tokenization (Tables 3, 4, 5)**

ATOKEN achieves competitive performance on image reconstruction while maintaining strong semantic understanding — the core claim of unified tokenization.

Key observations from **Table 4**:

- Cross-dataset generalization is strong: 0.209 rFID on ImageNet, 2.026 rFID on COCO (UniTok degrades from 0.362 to 3.918)

For semantic understanding (**Table 5**), ATOKEN maintains 82.2% ImageNet accuracy across all training stages despite joint training on multiple modalities. This is within 1.2% of understanding-only SigLIP2 (83.4%), narrowing the gap compared to previous unified attempts (UniTok: 78.6%, VILA-U: 78.0%).

While understanding-only models trained on large-scale video-text data achieve higher scores (VideoPrism-g: 52.7% on MSRVTT), the paper notes this gap is expected given ATOKEN optimizes primarily for reconstruction across three modalities rather than video-text alignment specifically.

3D results show unified training maintains strong capabilities despite not using dedicated 3D architectures:

**Downstream Applications (Tables 9-13)**

The paper validates ATOKEN's utility across diverse downstream tasks:

**Multimodal LLMs (Tables 9, 10)**: When integrated into SlowFast-LLaVA-1.5 as a frozen vision encoder, ATOKEN outperforms Oryx-ViT (a specialized vision encoder for MLLMs) on most benchmarks:

**Scaling Analysis (Figure 7)**

The comparison between Base (192M parameters) and So400m (800M parameters) models is the most critical ablation, revealing the capacity threshold for successful multimodal tokenization:

| Metric | Base Model | So400m Model |
|--------|------------|--------------|
| ImageNet rFID (Stage 1) | 0.323 | 0.258 |
| ImageNet rFID (Stage 3) | 0.483 | 0.209 |
| Change Across Stages | **49% degradation** | **19% improvement** |

| Zero-shot Accuracy | 77.2% → degrades | 82.7% → stable at 82.2% |

From **Figure 7(a-d)**, the Base model suffers severe degradation when expanding beyond single-modality training, while So400m improves continuously. This establishes that multimodal tokenization has a **minimum capacity requirement** — approximately 4× parameter increase transforms multimodal training from harmful to beneficial.

**Representation Structure (Figure 8)**

T-SNE visualizations of learned representations reveal an interesting trade-off:
- Dense features (before projection) show clear semantic clustering with distinct ImageNet class separation (**Figure 8a-c**)
- Projected 48-dimensional latents result in more intermixed distributions (**Figure 8d-e**), likely due to KL regularization without post-projection alignment loss

Despite apparent mixing in T-SNE visualizations, the model maintains strong reconstruction and understanding performance. This raises a question about whether explicit semantic clustering in low-dimensional spaces is necessary for strong performance — ATOKEN suggests larger models can effectively leverage seemingly intermixed representations.

**Training Stage Progression (Table 4)**

The progressive curriculum shows consistent improvement rather than catastrophic forgetting:

| Stage | ImageNet rFID | ImageNet Acc. | Video PSNR (TokenBench) |
|-------|---------------|---------------|------------------------|
| Stage 1 (Image only) | 0.258 | 82.7% | - |
| Stage 2 (+ Video) | 0.246 | 82.3% | 35.63 |

Image reconstruction improves 19% from Stage 1 to Stage 3, while understanding accuracy remains stable (only 0.5% degradation). This supports the claim that multimodal training enhances rather than compromises single-modality performance when capacity is sufficient.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment

**Do Experiments Support the Claims?**

The experiments largely support the paper's core claims, with some caveats:

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Notes |
|-------|-------------------|-------|
| Unified reconstruction + understanding | **Strong** | 0.21 rFID + 82.2% accuracy exceeds prior unified methods |
| All three modalities in one framework | **Strong** | Competitive performance on image, video, and 3D benchmarks |
| Multimodal training enhances single-modality | **Strong** | 19% rFID improvement from Stage 1 to Stage 3 |
| Adversarial-free training achieves SOTA | **Strong** | 0.21 rFID without GANs, stable training curves in Figure 4 |
| Capacity threshold for unification | **Strong** | Clear Base vs. So400m divergence in Figure 7 |
| Downstream versatility | **Moderate** | Strong on MLLMs and generation, weaker on image-to-3D |

**Limitations and Failure Cases**

1. **Image-to-3D Generation Gap**: Section 5.5 explicitly acknowledges that ATOKEN does not match Trellis-SLAT's fidelity for image-to-3D synthesis. The generated assets "do not always adhere strictly to the color and style of the input image." This is the clearest failure case, attributed to the higher-dimensional latent space (48 channels vs. 8) requiring further diffusion hyperparameter optimization.

2. **Long-Form Video Understanding Gap**: On MLVU (long-form video understanding), ATOKEN trails Oryx-ViT (69.8% vs. 71.5% at 7B scale). The paper notes this likely arises because ATOKEN was not trained on as many long video-text pairs as dedicated video understanding models.

3. **Discrete Token Quality Trade-off**: The discrete variant (ATOKEN-So/D) shows expected degradation vs. continuous (0.38 vs. 0.21 rFID on images, 22.16 vs. 3.01 rFVD on videos). This is a known trade-off, but the paper does not explore whether larger codebooks or alternative quantization strategies could narrow this gap.

4. **Computational Cost**: The training required 138k GPU hours (22 days on 256 H100s). While not explicitly framed as a limitation, this is substantially more expensive than training specialized single-modality tokenizers, which may limit accessibility for researchers without large-scale compute.

5. **T-SNE Representation Mixing**: **Figure 8** shows that projected latents have intermixed class distributions, raising questions about semantic preservation through aggressive dimensionality reduction. The paper acknowledges this but leaves detailed investigation for future work.

**Trade-offs Observed**

| Trade-off | Observation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Capacity vs. Unification** | Below ~800M parameters, multimodal training degrades performance (Base model) |
| **Continuous vs. Discrete** | Discrete tokens enable LLM compatibility but reduce reconstruction quality |
| **Unified vs. Specialized** | ATOKEN matches specialized methods on most tasks but trails on image-to-3D generation |
| **Training Cost vs. Versatility** | Higher training cost enables single model for all downstream tasks |

**Overall Assessment**

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing for the paper's primary claims. The unified re-evaluation protocol strengthens credibility, and the ablation studies (particularly the scaling analysis) provide important insights beyond just reporting final numbers. The acknowledged limitations (image-to-3D generation, long-form video understanding) demonstrate intellectual honesty rather than overclaiming.

The most significant contribution validated by experiments is the **capacity threshold discovery** — the clear divergence between Base and So400m models in **Figure 7** establishes design principles for future multimodal systems. This moves the field beyond "unification is good/bad" to "unification requires sufficient capacity."

One area for future validation: the paper notes computational constraints limited testing to separate downstream tasks rather than a comprehensive "omnimodel." Building and evaluating such a system would be the natural next step to fully demonstrate ATOKEN's potential.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While ATOKEN demonstrates significant advances in unified visual tokenization, the paper acknowledges several important limitations and trade-offs. Understanding these constraints is critical for researchers considering whether to adopt this approach and for identifying directions for future work. This section examines the assumptions underlying the method, scenarios where it underperforms, computational and data constraints, and open questions that remain unresolved.

### 6.1 Architectural Assumptions and Design Constraints

**High Model Capacity Requirement**

The most fundamental constraint of ATOKEN is its **minimum capacity threshold** for successful multimodal unification. The ablation study in **Figure 7** reveals that the Base model (192M parameters) suffers severe degradation when expanding beyond single-modality training:

| Metric | Base Model (192M) | So400m Model (800M) |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------|
| ImageNet rFID (Stage 1 → Stage 3) | 0.323 → 0.483 (**49% degradation**) | 0.258 → 0.209 (**19% improvement**) |
| Video PSNR Trend | Declines across stages | Improves from 32.51 to 33.11 |

This establishes that **multimodal tokenization is not universally beneficial** — it requires approximately 4× the parameter count to transform cross-modal learning from harmful to beneficial. For researchers with limited computational resources, this creates a practical barrier: attempting to replicate ATOKEN's approach at smaller scales will likely result in worse performance than specialized single-modality models.

**Assumption: Sparse 4D Coordinates Generalize Across Modalities**

The unified 4D representation assumes that treating images, videos, and 3D assets as subspaces of a common coordinate system ($p_i = [t, x, y, z]$) enables effective knowledge transfer. However, **Section 3.1** acknowledges that the $(x, y, z)$ coordinates serve different purposes across modalities:

> "Note that the (x, y, z) coordinates serve different purposes across modalities: in 3D, they represent actual entity occupancy physical locations, while in images and videos, they function as grid indices."

This dual interpretation relies on 4D RoPE to provide position awareness that adapts to each modality's structure. While empirically successful, this design choice assumes that the attention mechanism can learn to distinguish between physical occupancy (3D) and grid indexing (images/videos) without explicit architectural separation. The paper does not ablate this assumption — it remains unclear whether explicit modality indicators or separate position embedding schemes would improve performance.

**Assumption: Image-Centric Reconstruction Transfers to Other Modalities**

For computational efficiency, ATOKEN uses only $\mathcal{L}_1$ loss for video and 3D reconstruction, relying on cross-modal transfer from image training for texture and semantic supervision (**Section 3.3**):

> "For video and 3D assets, we use $\mathcal{L}_{rec}^{V/3D} = \mathcal{L}_1$ for efficiency, relying on cross-modal transfer from images for details."

This assumes that image datasets (DFN, Open Images) provide sufficient texture diversity to transfer to video and 3D domains. While results show competitive performance, this design may limit ATOKEN's ability to capture modality-specific details — for example, motion blur patterns unique to video or material properties specific to 3D surfaces. The paper does not evaluate whether adding modality-specific perceptual losses would improve reconstruction quality.

### 6.2 Performance Limitations in Specific Scenarios

**Image-to-3D Generation Gap**

The clearest failure case appears in **Section 5.5**, where ATOKEN does not match the specialized Trellis-SLAT baseline for image-to-3D synthesis:

> "However, we observe that the performance does not yet match the fidelity of the original Trellis-SLAT model. Specifically, while our tokenizer demonstrates excellent reconstruction capabilities that preserve color and structure (as in Figure 11), the generative model sometimes struggles to maintain this consistency. The generated assets do not always adhere strictly to the color and style of the input image."

The paper attributes this to the larger latent channel dimension (48 vs. Trellis-SLAT's 8), suggesting that diffusion models operating in higher-dimensional spaces require further optimization of training and inference hyperparameters. This limitation is significant because it reveals a gap between **tokenizer reconstruction quality** and **generative model compatibility** — a tokenizer that reconstructs well does not guarantee that downstream generative models can effectively condition on its latents.

**Long-Form Video Understanding Deficit**

On the MLVU benchmark for long-form video understanding, ATOKEN trails the specialized Oryx-ViT encoder:

| Model (7B) | MLVU Score |
|------------|------------|
| Oryx-ViT | 71.5% |
| ATOKEN-So/C | 69.8% |

**Section 5.1** hypothesizes this gap arises because:
1. Oryx-ViT was specifically designed for video understanding in LLMs
2. Oryx-ViT was trained on long-video retrieval tasks

ATOKEN's training data includes WebVid and TextVR for video understanding, but these datasets contain fewer long video-text pairs than dedicated video understanding corpora. This limitation suggests that **unified tokenizers may require modality-specific data scaling** to match specialized encoders on domain-specific benchmarks. Researchers prioritizing long-form video understanding may still benefit from specialized video encoders.

**Discrete Token Quality Trade-off**

The discrete variant (ATOKEN-So/D) shows expected but notable degradation compared to the continuous variant (ATOKEN-So/C):

| Metric | ATOKEN-So/C (Continuous) | ATOKEN-So/D (Discrete) |
|--------|--------------------------|------------------------|
| ImageNet rFID | 0.21 | 0.38 |
| Video rFVD | 3.01 | 22.16 |
| Image PSNR | 29.72 | 27.00 |
| Video PSNR (DAVIS) | 33.11 | 29.75 |

While the paper frames this as a reasonable trade-off for LLM compatibility, the **7× increase in rFVD** for video is substantial. **Section 5.3** notes that TokenBridge achieves 1.76 gFID with its native tokenizer versus ATOKEN-So/D's 2.23 gFID, attributing the gap to ATOKEN's larger vocabulary (4096 vs. 8) and lack of frequency-based ordering optimization. This suggests that **discrete tokenization for video remains an open challenge** — the quantization strategies that work well for images may not transfer effectively to temporal data.

### 6.3 Computational and Data Constraints

**Training Cost**

The four-stage curriculum required **138k GPU hours** across 256 H100 GPUs (approximately 22 days):

> "The four-stage curriculum trains for 200k, 200k, 50k, and 100k iterations, respectively... requiring a total of 138k GPU hours across all stages (approximately 22 days with 256 GPUs)." — **Section 3.5**

This is substantially more expensive than training specialized single-modality tokenizers. For comparison, typical VAE training for image generation (e.g., SD-VAE) requires orders of magnitude fewer GPU hours. While the paper argues that a single unified model replaces multiple specialized models (potentially reducing total deployment cost), the **upfront training investment** creates a barrier for researchers without access to large-scale compute clusters.

**Batch Size Constraints for Video and 3D**

**Section 3.5** reports adaptive batch sizes optimized for each task's memory requirements:

| Task | Batch Size |
|------|------------|
| Image Understanding | 8,192 samples |
| Image Reconstruction | 1,024-4,096 samples |
| Video Reconstruction | 512-1,024 samples |
| 3D Reconstruction | 256-512 samples |

The significantly smaller batch sizes for video and 3D (due to memory constraints) may limit training stability and convergence speed for these modalities. **Section 3.3** notes that sigmoid loss was chosen for video and 3D semantic alignment because it "proves more stable for the smaller batch sizes typical in these domains." This suggests that **modalities with higher memory requirements receive less gradient signal per training step**, potentially contributing to the performance gaps observed on video and 3D benchmarks.

**Training Data Imbalance**

The progressive curriculum samples datasets proportionally to their size (**Section 3.5**):

- **Images**: DFN, Open Images, internal datasets (millions of samples)
- **Video**: WebVid, TextVR, Panda70M (hundreds of thousands to millions)
- **3D**: Objaverse with Cap3D annotations (tens to hundreds of thousands)

This imbalance means the model learns detailed reconstruction primarily from images, with video and 3D capabilities emerging through transfer. While **Section 4.2** shows this transfer is effective for image reconstruction, it may limit the model's ability to capture modality-specific phenomena — for example, complex motion dynamics in video or fine geometric details in 3D that are not present in image datasets.

### 6.4 Open Questions and Unresolved Issues

**Semantic Preservation in Low-Dimensional Latents**

**Figure 8** presents T-SNE visualizations revealing an unresolved tension between compression and semantic separability:

> "Dense features (a-c) show clear semantic clustering with distinct ImageNet class separation. However, projection to 48-dimensional latents (d-e) results in more intermixed distributions, likely due to KL regularization without post-projection alignment loss."

Despite this apparent mixing, the model maintains strong reconstruction and understanding performance. The paper explicitly leaves this as an open question:

> "This raises an interesting question: whether explicit semantic clustering in low-dimensional spaces – as emphasized by methods like VAVAE – is necessary for strong performance, or whether larger models can effectively leverage seemingly intermixed representations. Our results suggest the latter, though we leave detailed investigation of semantic preservation through aggressive dimensionality reduction for future work." — **Section 4.5**

This uncertainty has implications for downstream applications: if semantic information is encoded in ways not captured by 2D projections, it may be harder to interpret or manipulate the latent space for controlled generation.

**Latent Dimension Compatibility with Downstream Models**

The 48-dimensional latent space creates compatibility challenges with existing generative models. **Section 5.5** notes that Trellis-SLAT uses 8 channels, while ATOKEN uses 48:

> "We hypothesize that this discrepancy arises from the significantly larger latent channel dimension of our tokenizer. ATOKEN-So/C uses 48 latent channels to accommodate rich multimodal information, a substantial increase from the 8 channels used in Trellis-SLAT. A diffusion model operating in this higher-dimensional space likely requires further optimization of training and inference hyperparameters."

This suggests that **adopting ATOKEN may require retraining or retuning downstream generative models**, reducing the plug-and-play benefit of a unified tokenizer. Future work should explore whether dimensionality reduction techniques or adapter layers could bridge this gap without sacrificing reconstruction quality.

**Comprehensive Omnimodel Not Yet Demonstrated**

**Section 7** acknowledges that computational constraints limited the evaluation to separate downstream tasks rather than a fully integrated system:

> "Due to limited computational resources, we could only test ATOKEN on separate downstream tasks. Building the comprehensive omnimodel that would demonstrate ATOKEN's full potential remains as future work."

This is a significant limitation because the core value proposition of ATOKEN is enabling **seamless multimodal AI systems** that handle understanding and generation across all visual modalities within a single framework. Without demonstrating such an omnimodel, the paper validates individual components but does not fully validate the end-to-end vision.

**KV-Caching Limits for Very Long Sequences**

**Figure 6** illustrates the KV-caching mechanism for video decoding, which eliminates redundant computation across temporal tiles. However, the paper does not evaluate KV-caching performance for very long sequences (e.g., videos exceeding 64 frames at high frame rates). **Section 3.4** notes video understanding supports "up to 64@1FPS" while reconstruction supports "up to 32@1–12 stride," suggesting temporal length limits that may constrain applications requiring minute-scale video processing.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

| Trade-off | Observation | Evidence |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Capacity vs. Unification** | Below ~800M parameters, multimodal training degrades performance | **Figure 7**: Base model 49% rFID degradation |
| **Continuous vs. Discrete** | Discrete tokens enable LLM compatibility but reduce reconstruction quality | **Table 3**: 0.21 vs. 0.38 rFID (images), 3.01 vs. 22.16 rFVD (videos) |
| **Unified vs. Specialized** | ATOKEN matches specialized methods on most tasks but trails on image-to-3D generation | **Section 5.5**: Color/style consistency issues |
| **Training Cost vs. Versatility** | Higher training cost enables single model for all downstream tasks | **Section 3.5**: 138k GPU hours |
| **Image-Centric vs. Modality-Balanced** | Reconstruction details learned primarily from images, transferred to video/3D | **Section 3.3**: $\mathcal{L}_1$ only for video/3D |
| **Latent Dimension vs. Compatibility** | 48 channels accommodate multimodal info but require downstream model adaptation | **Section 5.5**: Trellis-SLAT uses 8 channels |

### 6.6 Fair Assessment

These limitations should be weighed against ATOKEN's achievements. The paper is transparent about failure cases (image-to-3D generation, long-form video understanding) rather than overstating claims. The capacity threshold discovery (**Figure 7**) is itself a valuable contribution — it establishes design principles for future multimodal systems rather than presenting unification as universally applicable.

The most significant unresolved question is whether the **48-dimensional latent space** represents an optimal balance between multimodal expressiveness and downstream compatibility. If future work demonstrates that lower-dimensional unified representations can achieve similar performance, ATOKEN's approach may require revision. Conversely, if 48 dimensions proves necessary for true multimodal unification, the field may need to adapt generative models to higher-dimensional latent spaces rather than compressing tokenizers to fit existing architectures.

For practitioners, the key takeaway is that ATOKEN is best suited for scenarios where:
1. **Sufficient compute is available** (800M+ parameter models, 138k+ GPU hours)
2. **Multiple visual modalities must be supported** by a single system
3. **Both understanding and generation capabilities** are required
4. **Some performance trade-off on specialized tasks** is acceptable in exchange for unification

Researchers working on resource-constrained settings, highly specialized single-modality tasks, or applications requiring minute-scale video processing may still benefit from specialized tokenizers. ATOKEN establishes a new paradigm for unified visual tokenization, but it does not render specialized approaches obsolete — rather, it defines the conditions under which unification becomes advantageous.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

ATOKEN represents more than an incremental improvement to visual tokenization—it establishes a new paradigm for how multimodal AI systems should be designed. This section examines how the work reshapes the field landscape, identifies concrete research directions it opens, outlines practical applications, and provides guidance for researchers considering adoption.

### 7.1 How This Work Changes the Field Landscape

**From Specialized Silos to Unified Foundations**

Prior to ATOKEN, the visual AI community operated under an implicit assumption: reconstruction and understanding require separate systems, and different modalities require different architectures. This fragmentation mirrored early NLP before unified tokenizers enabled LLMs to handle code, documents, and multiple languages through a single interface.

ATOKEN challenges this assumption empirically. The results in **Table 3** and **Table 4** demonstrate that a single tokenizer can achieve:
- **0.21 rFID** on image reconstruction (competitive with `VAVAE` at 0.279)
- **82.2%** ImageNet accuracy (within 1.2% of understanding-only `SigLIP2` at 83.4%)
- **3.01 rFVD** on video (matching specialized `Wan2.2` at 3.19)
- **28.28 PSNR** on 3D (surpassing specialized `Trellis-SLAT` at 26.97)

This shifts the burden of proof: future unified tokenizer papers can no longer claim unification inherently degrades performance. The question is no longer *whether* unification is possible, but *under what conditions* it succeeds.

**Capacity Threshold as a Design Principle**

The ablation study in **Figure 7** establishes perhaps the most actionable insight for the field: **multimodal tokenization has a minimum capacity requirement**. The Base model (192M parameters) degrades by 49% in rFID when expanding beyond images, while So400m (800M parameters) improves by 19%.

This finding transforms how researchers should approach multimodal system design:

| Model Scale | Recommendation | Rationale |
|-------------|---------------|-----------|
| < 200M parameters | Use specialized tokenizers | Below capacity threshold; interference dominates |
| 200M–800M parameters | Caution with unification | Transitional regime; benefits uncertain |
| > 800M parameters | Prefer unified approaches | Cross-modal learning provides net benefits |

Prior work on unified tokenizers (`VILA-U`, `UniTok`) did not systematically analyze this threshold, making it unclear whether their limitations stemmed from architecture or insufficient capacity. ATOKEN provides a concrete reference point: approximately **4× parameter increase** transforms multimodal training from harmful to beneficial.

**Adversarial-Free Training as New Standard**

The Gram matrix loss approach (**Section 3.3**, **Figure 4**) removes a major barrier to transformer-based tokenizer development. GAN training has been the dominant approach for high-fidelity reconstruction, but it introduces:
- Training instability (discriminator dominates over time)
- Hyperparameter sensitivity
- Incompatibility with sparse 3D representations

By demonstrating that **0.21 rFID** is achievable without adversarial training, ATOKEN establishes that GANs are a workaround for inadequate loss design, not a requirement for quality. This opens the door for:
- More stable training at larger scales
- Easier reproducibility (no discriminator tuning)
- Compatibility with sparse representations across modalities

Future transformer-based tokenizers should treat adversarial-free objectives as the default, only resorting to GANs if Gram matrix losses prove insufficient for specific use cases.

**4D Sparse Representation as Common Coordinate System**

The sparse 4D latent space (**Section 3.1**) provides the first true "coordinate language" for visual data, analogous to how BPE tokenizers provide a unified interface for text. This enables:

1. **Knowledge transfer across modalities**: Geometric understanding from 3D improves image reconstruction; semantic richness from images improves 3D color consistency (**Figure 11**)
2. **Simplified multimodal LLM architectures**: A single vision encoder processes images, videos, and 3D without modality-specific branches
3. **Efficient scaling**: Native resolution support without padding overhead, enabling arbitrary input sizes

This representation is likely to become a standard building block for future multimodal foundation models, similar to how positional embeddings became standard in transformers.

### 7.2 Follow-up Research Directions

ATOKEN opens several concrete research avenues. We categorize these by near-term (1–2 years) and longer-term (3+ years) horizons.

**Near-Term Research Directions**

1. **Reducing the Capacity Threshold**
   
   The 800M parameter requirement limits accessibility. Follow-up work should investigate:
   - Can architectural innovations (e.g., mixture-of-experts, attention optimizations) reduce the threshold?
   - Does curriculum modification (slower modality introduction) help smaller models?
   - Are there modality-specific adapter layers that enable unification at lower capacity?
   
   Success here would democratize unified tokenization for researchers without large-scale compute.

2. **Improving Discrete Tokenization for Video**
   
   The discrete variant shows a **7× increase in rFVD** for video (3.01 → 22.16, **Table 3**). This suggests quantization strategies that work for images do not transfer well to temporal data. Promising directions:
   - Temporal-aware quantization that preserves motion consistency
   - Larger codebooks or alternative quantization methods (RQ, MCQ)
   - Frequency-based ordering adapted for video (extending TokenBridge's approach)

3. **Image-to-3D Generation Optimization**
   
   **Section 5.5** identifies a clear gap: ATOKEN's tokenizer reconstructs 3D well, but downstream generative models struggle to condition on its 48-dimensional latents. Research should explore:
   - Diffusion hyperparameter optimization for higher-dimensional latent spaces
   - Adapter layers between ATOKEN latents and existing 3D generators
   - Whether dimensionality reduction (48 → 8 channels) preserves multimodal benefits

4. **Long-Form Video Understanding**
   
   The MLVU gap (69.8% vs. Oryx-ViT's 71.5%, **Table 10**) suggests ATOKEN needs more long video-text training data. Directions include:
   - Incorporating datasets with minute-scale videos and dense captions
   - Extending KV-caching beyond 64 frames (**Figure 6**)
   - Hierarchical temporal modeling for hour-scale content

**Longer-Term Research Directions**

1. **Semantic Preservation in Compressed Latents**
   
   **Figure 8** reveals an unresolved tension: dense features show clear semantic clustering, but projected 48-dimensional latents appear intermixed in T-SNE. Yet performance remains strong. This raises fundamental questions:
   - Is explicit semantic clustering necessary for downstream tasks?
   - Can we design losses that preserve semantic structure without sacrificing reconstruction?
   - Do larger models encode semantics in ways not captured by 2D projections?
   
   Answering these questions would inform tokenizer design for all downstream applications.

2. **Comprehensive Omnimodel Demonstration**
   
   **Section 7** acknowledges computational constraints prevented testing ATOKEN in a fully integrated system. Building and evaluating such an "omnimodel" would:
   - Validate end-to-end vision-language-generation pipelines
   - Demonstrate cross-modal task chaining (e.g., image → 3D → video)
   - Establish benchmarks for unified multimodal systems

3. **Scaling Beyond 800M Parameters**
   
   The So400m model shows continuous improvement across stages, suggesting benefits from further scaling. Key questions:
   - Do multimodal benefits accelerate or plateau beyond 1B parameters?
   - Are there new failure modes at extreme scales?
   - How does scaling interact with discrete tokenization quality?

4. **Cross-Modal Generation Tasks**
   
   ATOKEN enables novel applications that were previously impractical:
   - Text → 3D → Video pipelines (generate 3D asset, then animate it)
   - Video → 3D reconstruction (extract 3D structure from video frames)
   - Image editing via 3D manipulation (modify 3D representation, render back to 2D)
   
   Systematic evaluation of these tasks would establish ATOKEN's utility beyond benchmark metrics.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

ATOKEN's unified capabilities enable several practical applications that were previously fragmented across multiple systems.

**Multimodal LLMs with Generation Capabilities**

Current MLLMs use frozen vision encoders (like `CLIP`) that can only understand visual input, not generate it. ATOKEN enables MLLMs to:
- **Describe and generate** within the same latent space
- **Edit images** through latent manipulation rather than pixel-space operations
- **Chain visual tasks** (e.g., "describe this image, then generate a similar one with modifications")

**Table 9** and **Table 10** show ATOKEN integrated into SlowFast-LLaVA-1.5 outperforms Oryx-ViT on most benchmarks, validating this approach. Practical deployments include:
- Customer service bots that can both analyze and generate visual content
- Educational tools that explain diagrams and create practice problems
- Accessibility applications that describe scenes and generate simplified versions

**Unified Content Creation Pipelines**

Content creators currently use separate tools for images (Stable Diffusion), videos (Sora, Runway), and 3D (Trellis, Shap-E). ATOKEN enables:
- **Single model for all visual content**: Reduce infrastructure complexity and licensing costs
- **Cross-modal consistency**: Generate an image, then animate it or create a 3D model with consistent style
- **Iterative refinement**: Move between modalities during the creative process (e.g., 3D → image → video)

**Table 11** and **Table 13** demonstrate competitive generation quality across images and videos, while **Figure 14** shows 3D synthesis capability.

**Research Infrastructure Simplification**

For research labs, ATOKEN reduces the burden of maintaining multiple tokenizer implementations:
- **Single codebase**: One tokenizer serves image, video, and 3D experiments
- **Consistent evaluation**: Metrics computed on shared latent space enable fair cross-modal comparison
- **Faster iteration**: New modalities can be added without architectural changes

This is particularly valuable for academic labs with limited engineering resources.

**Cross-Modal Transfer Applications**

The demonstrated cross-modal benefits (**Section 4.2**) enable applications that leverage knowledge from one modality to improve another:
- **3D-informed image enhancement**: Use geometric understanding to improve image super-resolution
- **Video-consistent image generation**: Generate image sequences with temporal coherence
- **Image-pretrained 3D models**: Leverage image datasets to improve 3D generation where 3D data is scarce

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

**When to Prefer ATOKEN Over Alternatives**

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| Building multimodal LLM with generation | **Use ATOKEN** | Single encoder handles understanding + generation |
| Resource-constrained (< 200M parameters) | **Use specialized tokenizers** | Below capacity threshold; ATOKEN degrades |
| Image-only generation pipeline | **Consider VAVAE or SD-VAE** | Specialized models may edge out on pure reconstruction |
| Video-only understanding | **Consider VideoPrism or Oryx-ViT** | Better long-form video performance |
| 3D-only generation | **Consider Trellis-SLAT** | Better image-to-3D conditioning currently |
| Multi-modality required | **Use ATOKEN** | Only unified option across images, videos, 3D |
| Discrete tokens for LLM integration | **Use ATOKEN-So/D** | Only discrete tokenizer supporting all modalities |
| Stable training priority | **Use ATOKEN** | Adversarial-free training avoids GAN instability |

**Compute Requirements for Reproduction**

The training configuration (**Section 3.5**) provides concrete requirements:

| Component | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| GPUs | 256 × H100 (or equivalent) |
| Training Time | ~22 days (138k GPU hours) |
| Memory | Adaptive batch sizes: 256–8,192 samples depending on task |
| Storage | DFN, Open Images, WebVid, Objaverse (hundreds of TB) |

For researchers without this scale:
- **Fine-tuning**: Start from released checkpoints and adapt to specific domains
- **Smaller variants**: The Base model (192M) trains faster but expect performance degradation on multimodal tasks
- **Single-modality focus**: Train only Stage 1 (images) for ~200k steps to reduce cost

**Integration Considerations**

1. **Latent Dimension Compatibility**
   
   ATOKEN uses 48-dimensional latents, while many existing generative models expect 4–16 channels (e.g., `SD-VAE` uses 4, `Trellis-SLAT` uses 8). Integration requires:
   - Retraining downstream models for 48-channel input, or
   - Adding projection layers (may lose multimodal benefits)

2. **Discrete vs. Continuous Token Selection**
   
   Choose based on downstream task:
   - **Continuous (ATOKEN-So/C)**: Diffusion models, maximum reconstruction quality
   - **Discrete (ATOKEN-So/D)**: Autoregressive LLMs, sequence modeling

3. **Resolution Handling**
   
   ATOKEN natively supports arbitrary resolutions through sparse representations. However:
   - Perceptual losses (`LPIPS`, `CLIP`, `Gram`) use 224×224 interpolation (**Section 3.4**)
   - Very high resolutions (>2048) were not evaluated; extrapolation risk exists

4. **Video Temporal Limits**
   
   Video understanding supports "up to 64@1FPS" and reconstruction "up to 32@1–12 stride" (**Figure 5**). For longer videos:
   - Use temporal tiling with KV-caching (**Figure 6**)
   - Expect potential coherence issues at tile boundaries

**Migration Path from Existing Systems**

For teams currently using specialized tokenizers:

1. **Phase 1: Evaluation**
   - Download released ATOKEN checkpoints
   - Run on your existing benchmarks to quantify trade-offs
   - Identify tasks where ATOKEN matches or exceeds current systems

2. **Phase 2: Hybrid Deployment**
   - Use ATOKEN for multimodal tasks
   - Keep specialized tokenizers for performance-critical single-modality tasks
   - Gradually migrate as ATOKEN improves

3. **Phase 3: Full Integration**
   - Retrain downstream generative models for 48-channel latents
   - Consolidate pipelines to single tokenizer
   - Leverage cross-modal capabilities for new applications

**Key Risks to Monitor**

- **Image-to-3D generation gap**: Not yet matching specialized baselines (**Section 5.5**)
- **Long-form video understanding**: Trails Oryx-ViT on MLVU (**Table 10**)
- **Discrete video quality**: 7× rFVD increase vs. continuous (**Table 3**)
- **Compute requirements**: 138k GPU hours may be prohibitive for some teams

### 7.5 Summary: A Foundation for Next-Generation Multimodal Systems

ATOKEN establishes that unified visual tokenization is not only possible but beneficial when executed with sufficient capacity and stable training objectives. The work shifts the field from asking *whether* unification is feasible to *how* to optimize it.

The most impactful contributions for future research are:
1. **Capacity threshold discovery**: Provides concrete design principles for multimodal systems
2. **Adversarial-free training**: Removes a major barrier to transformer-based tokenizer development
3. **4D sparse representation**: Establishes a common coordinate system for all visual modalities
4. **Cross-modal enhancement**: Demonstrates multimodal training can improve rather than degrade single-modality performance

For practitioners, ATOKEN offers a path to simplified infrastructure and novel cross-modal applications, with the caveat that compute requirements and some performance gaps remain. For researchers, it opens rich directions in scaling, quantization, semantic preservation, and omnimodel demonstration.

The work positions visual tokenization to follow language modeling's trajectory: from specialized systems for specific tasks to unified foundations that enable generalization across modalities. Whether ATOKEN becomes the definitive standard or a stepping stone to better approaches, it has fundamentally changed how the field thinks about visual representation learning.
