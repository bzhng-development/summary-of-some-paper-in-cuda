## 1. Executive Summary

Addressing the impending data scarcity where compute grows 4× per year versus web text's 1.03×, this paper demonstrates that properly tuning regularization (weight decay 30× larger than standard) and ensembling independently trained models enables monotonic loss scaling under fixed data constraints. Their joint scaling recipe achieves a loss asymptote using 5.17× less data than baseline on 200M DCLM tokens, while distillation allows an 8× smaller student to retain 83% of the ensembling benefit. These methods generalize to downstream benchmarks with a 9% improvement and yield a 17.5× data efficiency gain in continued pre-training on MegaMath-Web-Pro, proving simple algorithmic improvements can sustain performance in a compute-rich future.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Emerging Data Bottleneck

This paper addresses a fundamental shift in the landscape of language model pre-training that most practitioners have not yet confronted. Historically, the field has operated under a **compute-constrained regime**: researchers had access to vast amounts of web text but were limited by how much computation they could afford to spend training models. Scaling laws like Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., 2022) were developed under this assumption, recommending that practitioners jointly scale data and model size with approximately 20× more tokens than parameters to achieve compute-optimal training.

However, the paper identifies a critical inflection point: **compute capacity is growing approximately 4× per year**, while **web text availability is growing only 1.03× per year** (Section 1, citing Sevilla and Roldán, 2024; Villalobos et al., 2024). This divergence means we are rapidly approaching—and may already be entering—a regime where **data becomes the bottleneck, not compute**. The central question becomes: *How should one approach pre-training when constrained by data and unconstrained by compute?*

This is not merely a theoretical concern. If compute continues to outpace data availability by this margin, the standard recipes developed over the past five years will become increasingly suboptimal. Models trained using compute-efficient practices will leave performance on the table because they were never designed for a world where you can afford to spend 100× more compute than Chinchilla recommends on the same fixed dataset.

### Why Standard Approaches Fail Under Data Constraints

The paper demonstrates that existing data-constrained approaches break down in predictable ways when pushed to their limits. The authors establish a baseline using 200M tokens of web text from DCLM (Li et al., 2025) and test two common strategies:

1. **Increasing epoch count** (repeating the same data multiple times): While repeating data initially lowers loss, Section 2.1 and Figure 2 (left) show that beyond a certain point, models begin to overfit and validation loss *increases*. This contradicts the functional form proposed by Muennighoff et al. (2023), which assumed loss monotonically decreases with epoch count. The paper notes that Muennighoff et al. acknowledged this discrepancy and removed overfitting runs when fitting their scaling law (their Appendix D).

2. **Increasing parameter count** (making models larger): Even after jointly tuning epoch count and learning rate at each parameter count, Section 2.1 and Figure 2 (right) show that loss eventually stops decreasing and begins increasing. A 1.4B parameter model performs *worse* than a 600M model on the same 200M tokens. This aligns with earlier findings in Kaplan et al. (2020), Figure 9, which showed that increasing parameter count eventually hurts performance for fixed data.

The fundamental issue is **overfitting**: both too many epochs and too many parameters cause the model to memorize the training data rather than generalize. This creates a hard ceiling on performance improvements—no matter how much additional compute you spend, you cannot escape this bound using standard recipes.

### The Regularization Gap

A critical insight from this work is that standard regularization practices are inadequate for data-constrained pre-training. The paper notes that weight decay of 0.1, adopted from Brown et al. (2020) and used as default practice across the field, is **30× smaller than what is actually optimal** for over-parameterized models trained on fixed data (Section 3, Figure 3).

This is not a minor hyperparameter tuning detail—it is a fundamental mismatch between the regularization strength needed for compute-constrained versus data-constrained regimes. When compute is constrained, you cannot afford heavy regularization because it slows convergence. When data is constrained but compute is abundant, you *should* use heavy regularization because it prevents overfitting and enables monotonic scaling in parameter count.

The paper shows that with properly tuned weight decay (up to 3.2 for their largest models versus the standard 0.1), loss follows a clean power law in parameter count even at parameter-to-token ratios **140× larger than Chinchilla** recommends (Section 3, Figure 3). This transforms an overfitting-limited regime into one where you can predictably improve performance by spending more compute on larger models.

### A New Evaluation Metric: Loss Asymptotes

Perhaps the most significant conceptual contribution is how the paper proposes to *evaluate* pre-training recipes. Traditional scaling law papers compare methods at fixed compute budgets (train or inference). However, this does not answer the question of best possible performance when compute is unconstrained.

Instead, the authors propose evaluating recipes by the **asymptote of their scaling law**—the limit of loss as parameter count (or ensemble member count) approaches infinity. For example:
- Standard unregularized recipe: no clean asymptote (loss eventually increases)
- Regularized recipe: asymptote of 3.43 loss (Section 3)
- Ensembling recipe: asymptote of 3.34 loss (Section 4.2)
- Joint scaling (parameters + ensembling): asymptote of 3.17 loss (Section 4.3)

This reframing matters because it shifts optimization from "what works best at today's compute budget" to "what has the best ceiling when compute is abundant." A recipe with a lower asymptote will eventually outperform one with a higher asymptote, regardless of current compute constraints.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

The paper positions itself at the intersection of several research threads while carving out a distinct niche:

The paper explicitly notes in Section 8 that recent data-efficient pre-training work does not fully leverage additional compute: most papers do not optimally epoch models, none tune regularization systematically, and none build scaling laws to estimate infinite-parameter limits or data efficiency across token scales. This work fills that gap by treating compute as a resource to be spent aggressively in service of data efficiency.

### Theoretical and Practical Significance

The theoretical significance lies in connecting empirical pre-training behavior to established theory on over-parameterized regression. Section 3 notes that theory predicts loss will monotonically decrease when regularization is optimally tuned, even in over-parameterized regimes where double descent would otherwise occur (Advani and Ganguli, 2016; Canatar et al., 2021; Nakkiran et al., 2021; Simon et al., 2024). The paper's empirical findings validate this theory in the language model pre-training context.

The practical significance is immediate and measurable:
- **5.17× data efficiency improvement** at 200M tokens compared to standard baseline (Section 5.3)
- **9% improvement** on downstream benchmarks (PIQA, SciQ, ARC Easy) for best ensemble versus best unregularized model (Section 7.1)
- **17.5× data efficiency** in continued pre-training on math data: an ensemble trained on 4B tokens outperforms default CPT on 73B tokens (Section 7.2, Table 1)

These gains come from "simple algorithmic improvements" rather than architectural innovations or new data sources—suggesting there is substantial "free lunch" available by revisiting classical techniques from the era when deep learning was genuinely data-constrained (Section 9, citing work on Penn Tree Bank, ImageNet from the 2010s).

In essence, this paper argues that the field has been optimizing for the wrong constraint. As compute becomes abundant and data becomes scarce, the recipes that made sense for the past decade need fundamental revision. The authors provide both the diagnostic (standard approaches overfit) and the prescription (heavy regularization, ensembling, distillation) for this new regime.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an empirical scaling law paper that systematically explores how to maximize language model performance when data is fixed but compute is abundant. The core idea is that by properly tuning regularization and combining multiple training strategies (parameter scaling, ensembling, distillation), we can achieve monotonic loss improvement even when training on the same data many times over.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a pre-training recipe optimizer that determines how to spend unlimited compute on a fixed dataset to achieve the lowest possible validation loss. The solution takes the shape of three layered interventions: first, heavily regularize over-parameterized models to prevent overfitting; second, ensemble multiple independently trained models to capture diverse features; third, distill the ensemble into a smaller student model to retain benefits without inference cost.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The technical pipeline consists of four major components that build on each other. **Component 1 (Regularized Training)** takes a fixed token corpus $D$ and parameter count $N$, then jointly tunes weight decay, learning rate, and epoch count to find locally optimal hyperparameters that prevent overfitting. **Component 2 (Ensemble Scaling)** trains $K$ independent models with different random seeds, then averages their output logits during inference to reduce variance and capture multi-view features. **Component 3 (Joint Scaling)** combines Components 1 and 2 by taking both $N \to \infty$ and $K \to \infty$, fitting nested power laws to estimate the ultimate loss asymptote. **Component 4 (Distillation)** compresses the ensemble's knowledge into a smaller student model by training on a mixture of real tokens and teacher-generated synthetic tokens, preserving most benefits without increasing inference compute.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- First, I explain the regularized parameter scaling recipe because it establishes the foundation: without proper regularization, neither ensembling nor distillation can overcome the overfitting ceiling.
- Second, I detail the ensembling mechanism and why averaging independent models beats scaling a single model's parameters under infinite compute.
- Third, I walk through the joint scaling procedure that combines both approaches and how the authors estimate asymptotes through nested power law fitting.
- Fourth, I cover the distillation pipeline that makes these gains practical by compressing ensemble benefits into deployable model sizes.
- Fifth, I explain the hyperparameter search methodology because the locally-optimal tuning procedure is critical to achieving the reported monotonic scaling.
- Finally, I present the data efficiency metric that quantifies improvements across token scales and justifies why asymptote-based evaluation matters.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Paper type and core mechanism.** This is an empirical scaling law study that treats pre-training as an optimization problem where the objective is minimum validation loss subject to fixed data $D$ and unconstrained compute. The fundamental mechanism is that overfitting—not compute—becomes the limiting factor when data is scarce, and proper regularization transforms an overfitting-limited regime into one where loss monotonically decreases with increased compute spending.

**System pipeline: what happens first, second, third.** The training pipeline begins with a fixed seed corpus of 200M tokens from DCLM (Li et al., 2025), which remains constant across all experiments to isolate algorithmic effects. Second, for each parameter count $N \in \{150\text{M}, 300\text{M}, 600\text{M}, 1.4\text{B}\}$, the authors perform coordinate descent hyperparameter search over learning rate, epoch count, and weight decay to find locally optimal settings. Third, they fit a power law to the resulting losses across parameter counts and extract the asymptote as the estimate of best possible performance. Fourth, they repeat this procedure for ensembles of $K$ models, then combine both dimensions through nested limit estimation. Fifth, they validate that gains transfer to downstream benchmarks and continued pre-training scenarios.

**Mathematical formulation of the pre-training objective.** The paper formalizes standard pre-training as a training routine $\mathcal{A}$ that accepts token count $D$, parameter count $N$, and epoch count $E$ to produce a model $\mathcal{M}$ with loss $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{M})$. Any arguments not explicitly specified pass through a hyperparameter tuple $H$. The objective for pre-training with $D$ tokens unconstrained by compute becomes:

$$\mathcal{L}^*_D = \min_{H} \mathcal{L}\left(\mathcal{A}(D, H)\right)$$

This formulation differs from compute-constrained objectives because $H$ can include arbitrarily large $N$ and $E$ values—there is no penalty for spending more compute, only for achieving higher loss.

**Power law fitting for asymptote estimation.** To capture how increasing parameter count improves loss under regularization, the authors fit a power law with an asymptote of the form:

$$\hat{\mathcal{L}}_{D,N} := \frac{A_D}{N^{\alpha_D}} + E_D$$

where $A_D$ controls the scale of improvement, $\alpha_D$ is the scaling exponent, and $E_D$ is the asymptote representing the limit as $N \to \infty$. For the 200M token regularized recipe, fitting across four parameter counts yields $\hat{\mathcal{L}}_{200\text{M},N} = \frac{0.05}{N^{1.02}} + 3.43$. The exponent of 1.02 is notably higher than Chinchilla's parameter scaling exponent of 0.34, suggesting that when data is better leveraged through regularization, larger models provide faster improvement. The asymptote $E_D = 3.43$ represents the best possible loss this recipe can achieve regardless of how many parameters are added.

**Why asymptote-based evaluation matters.** Traditional scaling law papers compare methods at fixed compute budgets, but this does not answer what happens when compute is abundant. By evaluating recipes through their asymptotes, the authors can determine which approach has the highest ceiling. The unregularized recipe has no clean asymptote because loss eventually increases with too many parameters or epochs. The regularized recipe achieves asymptote 3.43. The ensembling recipe achieves asymptote 3.34. The joint scaling recipe achieves asymptote 3.17. A recipe with lower asymptote will eventually outperform one with higher asymptote at sufficiently high compute budgets, making asymptote comparison the correct metric for infinite-compute regimes.

**Regularization tuning: the 30× weight decay finding.** Standard practice adopts weight decay of 0.1 from Brown et al. (2020), but the authors find this is 30× smaller than optimal for data-constrained pre-training. For their most over-parameterized models (1.4B parameters on 200M tokens), optimal weight decay reaches 3.2. The tuning procedure uses coordinate descent: starting from an initial guess, they evaluate all neighbors in hyperparameter space (incrementing or decrementing exactly one variable) and move to any neighbor that improves loss, repeating until no neighbor is better. This finds locally optimal hyperparameters where increasing or decreasing any single hyperparameter does not result in a better model. The high weight decay is critical because it prevents overfitting when models are trained for many epochs on repeated data—without it, validation loss increases despite training loss continuing to decrease.

**Hyperparameter discretization for tractable search.** The search space is discretized to make coordinate descent computationally feasible. Learning rate must be 1 or 3 times a power of 10 (e.g., 1e-3, 3e-3, 1e-4). Epoch count must be an integer power of 2 (e.g., 4, 8, 16, 32). Weight decay must be either 0.0 or an integer power of 2 times 0.1 (e.g., 0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.8, 1.6, 3.2). Bounds are set based on initial experiments: maximum learning rate 3e-3, maximum weight decay 6.4, maximum epoch count 64. These bounds only triggered for three searches, indicating the discretization was appropriate for the explored regime.

**Ensembling formalization and logit averaging.** The ensembling algorithm $\mathcal{E}$ accepts a standard pre-training algorithm $\mathcal{A}$ and trains $K$ members that are identical except for random seed $Z_i$ controlling data order and model initialization. The output model averages the logits of the $K$ models, computed by querying all $K$ models during inference. Formally:

$$\mathcal{E}_{\mathcal{A}}(D, N, K, H) = \text{LogitAvg}\left(\{\mathcal{A}(D, N, Z_i, H)\}_{i \in [K]}\right)$$

where LogitAvg produces a model with likelihood for sequence $x$ given by:

$$\text{LogitAvg}\left(\mathcal{M}_{i \in [K]}\right)(x) \propto \exp\left(\frac{1}{K} \sum_{i \in [K]} \log(\mathcal{M}(x))\right)$$

The number of FLOPs for generation or evaluation is the sum of costs for all members, so an ensemble's total parameter count is treated as $NK$ when comparing to standard pre-training.

**Why ensembling beats parameter scaling at infinite compute.** Figure 4 shows that scaling ensemble member count $K$ achieves a lower asymptote than scaling parameter count $N$. For fixed $N = 300\text{M}$, the ensemble asymptote as $K \to \infty$ is 3.34, compared to 3.43 for the regularized recipe as $N \to \infty$. This implies that for sufficiently large total parameters, it is advantageous to train multiple smaller models (e.g., two 300M models) instead of a single larger model (e.g., one 600M model). The theoretical explanation comes from Allen-Zhu and Li (2023): when data has "multi-view" structure where different features classify different subsets, training a single model biases toward learning only one feature, whereas ensemble members independently learn different features. Averaging combines these views for better generalization.

**Ensemble hyperparameter tuning differs from single models.** The optimal hyperparameters for a single model ($K = 1$) are not optimal for the ensemble asymptote ($K \to \infty$). Figure 5 shows that the ranking between hyperparameters changes across $K$. The infinite member asymptote benefits from more epochs and less weight decay per member compared to the single-model optimum. Intuitively, each member being more overfit to the data helps learn different views, and averaging reduces the variance from overfitting. Selecting hyperparameters optimized for $K \to \infty$ improves the ensembling asymptote from 3.34 to 3.27. The authors use a heuristic for ensemble hyperparameters: locally optimal regularized hyperparameters with 2× epochs and 0.5× weight decay, which worked best across almost all scales.

**Joint scaling recipe: taking both limits.** The best possible loss combines parameter scaling and ensemble scaling by taking both $N \to \infty$ and $K \to \infty$:

$$\hat{\mathcal{L}}_D = \lim_{N \to \infty} \lim_{K \to \infty} \min_{H} \mathcal{L}\left(\mathcal{E}_{\mathcal{A}}(D, N, K, H)\right)$$

As long as the minimum loss monotonically decreases in $N$ and $K$ when the other variable is fixed, the order of limits does not matter. The authors choose $K \to \infty$ first, then $N \to \infty$, because it results in more convenient hyperparameter tuning. Figure 6 visualizes this double limit: for each parameter count, they fit a power law on loss as $K$ increases, extract asymptotes, then fit a second power law on how those asymptotes change with $N$. The final estimate for best possible loss is 3.17, a significant improvement over 3.43 for regularized pre-training and 3.75 for unregularized pre-training.

**Data efficiency metric: quantifying improvement across token scales.** Since the experiments are data-constrained, the authors care about loss improvement at fixed $D$, not just asymptotes. They measure data efficiency by interpolating how much data the standard recipe would need to match a recipe's asymptote. Formally, for algorithm $\mathcal{A}_2$ over $\mathcal{A}_1$ at token count $D$, they compute the effective data $D'$ that $\mathcal{A}_1$ would need to match $\mathcal{A}_2$, then report data efficiency as $D'/D$. At 200M tokens, the regularized recipe is 2.29× more data efficient than standard, and the joint scaling recipe is 5.17× more data efficient. Without taking asymptotes, the best ensemble of five 1.4B models is itself 3.75× more data efficient than baseline.

**Data scaling laws across token counts.** To test whether findings generalize beyond 200M tokens, the authors repeat the asymptote estimation procedure for seed token counts of 200M, 400M, 800M, and 1.6B. Figure 7 shows the two-step procedure for regularized scaling: first vary parameter count at each token count to fit parameter scaling laws, then use the asymptotes to fit a data scaling law. Figure 8 extends this to three steps for joint scaling: vary $K$ at each $(D, N)$ pair, vary $N$ using those asymptotes, then vary $D$ using those asymptotes. The data scaling laws across all three recipes have similar exponents (0.23 to 0.24) and asymptotes (1.89 to 1.96), implying that data efficiency improvement is constant across all data scales. When exponent $\alpha$ and asymptote $E$ are the same for two algorithms, the data efficiency improvement is constant at $(A_2/A_1)^{1/\alpha}$ where $A$ is the numerator of the power law.

**Distillation pipeline for parameter-constrained inference.** The asymptote benefits rely on arbitrarily high parameter models, which is impractical for deployment. Distillation compresses ensemble benefits into smaller students. The procedure has three steps: (1) train a teacher model $\mathcal{M}'$ on $D$ tokens using the data-efficient recipes; (2) sample from $\mathcal{M}'$ unconditionally (no prompt) to generate $D'$ synthetic tokens; (3) train a student model $\mathcal{M}$ from scratch on the mixture of $D$ real and $D'$ synthetic tokens. For ensemble distillation, an 8-ensemble of 300M models (2.4B total parameters) with loss 3.32 is distilled into a single 300M student. Despite 8× smaller inference compute, the student achieves loss 3.36, retaining 83% of the ensemble improvement over the regularized 300M model loss of 3.57. The student even outperforms the regularized recipe asymptote of 3.43.

**Self-distillation: same-size teacher and student.** Surprisingly, distilling a 300M teacher into a 300M student of the same architecture improves loss, contradicting intuition from the data processing inequality. The key is mixing real and synthetic tokens: using only synthetic data causes model collapse, but mixing avoids this. Figure 9 shows a 300M student (green star) trained via self-distillation outperforms the best 300M model from standard pre-training (purple point). Allen-Zhu and Li (2023) provide theory interpreting self-distillation as implicitly ensembling the teacher and freshly initialized student. The optimal hyperparameters for self-distillation use weight decay 0.1 (lower than regularized pre-training), mixing ratio 1:3 (real:synthetic), 16 epochs, and learning rate 3e-3. The self-distillation run trains on 13.4B total tokens (16 × 209M × 4).

**Why mixing real and synthetic data is critical.** Table 4 ablates the effect of mixing: self-distillation without mixing real data achieves validation loss 4.0693, worse than the teacher's 3.7103. With 1:1 mixing, self-distillation achieves 3.4373, exceeding the teacher. This confirms that model collapse occurs when training only on synthetic data, but mixing preserves the signal from real data while leveraging synthetic augmentation. The mixing ratio is a new hyperparameter introduced for distillation: a ratio of 1:9 for ensemble distillation and 1:3 for self-distillation indicates the number of gradient steps on real versus teacher-generated data.

**Architecture and training configuration details.** All models use Llama-style auto-regressive architecture with context length 4096. Table 2 specifies configurations: 150M model has 512 hidden dimension, 6 layers, 8 attention heads; 300M has 768 hidden, 12 layers, 12 heads; 600M has 1024 hidden, 24 layers, 16 heads; 1.4B has 2048 hidden, 16 layers, 16 heads. Training uses AdamW optimizer with $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.95$, $\epsilon = 10^{-8}$, gradient norm clipping at 1. Learning rate uses cosine schedule with 1% warmup, decaying to 0 by end of training. Mixed precision training uses fp32 parameters and bf16 compute/output. Most jobs ran on v4-64 or v4-128 TPUs with bitwise-determinism for reproducibility. Data order uses a fixed random permutation of windows after tokenization, reused across epochs.

**Batch size selection rationale.** While throughput-optimized training uses "critical batch size" to maximize hardware utilization (McCandlish et al., 2018), the authors find smaller batch sizes improve performance for fixed data points. Figure 13 (left) shows this corroborates prior optimization work (Keskar et al., 2017; Smith et al., 2020). They use batch size 64, the smallest practical size for their hardware. This choice matters because smaller batches introduce more gradient noise, which acts as implicit regularization—complementing the explicit weight decay regularization.

**Sources of randomness for ensembling.** Ensemble diversity comes from two sources: model initialization seed (train seed) and shuffled data order (data seed). Appendix C.1 characterizes run-to-run variance: standard deviation is 0.008207 when varying both seeds, 0.007605 for train seed only, and 0.007213 for data seed only. Either source alone delivers most ensembling benefits, with data order helping more. This aligns with prior work showing small instability induces majority of variance over final model loss (Jordan, 2024; Summers and Dinneen, 2021). The authors did not explore additional randomness sources given the marginal benefit.

**Why alternatives to ensembling were not pursued.** The paper discusses Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) and model soups as alternatives. MoE differs critically: ensemble members have independent learning trajectories, while MoE has a single joint trajectory. Allen-Zhu and Li (2023) show jointly trained ensembles barely improve over single models (0.02 loss), suggesting MoE sparsity does not benefit from "multi-view" data like independent ensembles do. Model soups (weight averaging) work well for fine-tuning but fail for pre-training because models enter different loss basins (Ainsworth et al., 2023). Table 5 shows model soups achieve near-random performance on downstream benchmarks. Since the authors are compute-unconstrained, they prefer distillation over weight merging for best performance.

**Sensitivity analysis for asymptote reliability.** Appendix H.1 tests whether asymptote estimation is reliable given run-to-run variance. For parameter scaling, three power laws fit across different seeds show asymptotes staying close together, with standard deviation near the run-to-run standard deviation for 300M models. For ensembling, subsampling ensemble members (up to 4 vs. up to 8) produces extremely similar power laws. The authors note this is a limited stress-test and advise interpreting asymptotes as rough estimates, especially for the two-tier and three-tier power laws in joint scaling where best runs are further from asymptotes.

**Continued pre-training adaptation.** Section 7.2 demonstrates the interventions transfer to continued pre-training (CPT) on MegaMath-Web-Pro with Llama 3.2 3B Base. They restrict to 4B seed tokens from the full 73B tokens. Table 1 shows the CPT baseline (Default) gives 6.34% improvement over base model. Applying interventions (lower batch size 64, epoching 4×, removing weight decay which was not helpful for CPT) provides additional 5.23% lift. Ensembling eight epoched models provides additional 4.76% gain. The best ensemble using 4B tokens outperforms default CPT on 73B tokens, achieving 17.5× data efficiency improvement. This demonstrates immediate applicability beyond pre-training from scratch.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section isolates the specific contributions that distinguish this work from prior scaling law and pre-training research. While Section 3 detailed *how* the methods function, here we analyze *why* these findings represent conceptual shifts rather than incremental tuning. We distinguish between fundamental innovations—which change how we define the optimization problem—and practical breakthroughs that unlock immediate efficiency gains.

### 4.1 Redefining the Optimization Objective: Asymptotes over Budgets

The most significant conceptual innovation is the shift in **what metric defines success** for pre-training recipes. Prior scaling law literature, including seminal works like Kaplan et al. (2020) and Hoffmann et al. (2022) (Chinchilla), optimizes for performance at a **fixed compute budget**. Their goal is to find the best model you can train given a constraint on FLOPs.

This paper argues that in a data-constrained future, the correct metric is the **loss asymptote** of the scaling law.

*   **Prior Approach:** Minimize loss $\mathcal{L}$ subject to Compute $\leq C$. This leads to recipes like Chinchilla that balance data and parameters to avoid wasting compute.
*   **This Paper's Approach:** Minimize loss $\mathcal{L}$ subject to Data $\leq D$, with Compute unconstrained. This leads to evaluating the limit $\lim_{N \to \infty} \mathcal{L}(N)$.

This distinction is critical because a recipe that looks worse at low compute might have a superior ceiling. For example, the regularized recipe might require more compute to converge than the standard recipe, but its asymptote of 3.43 loss (Section 3) is reachable with enough compute, whereas the standard recipe hits an overfitting wall. By proposing asymptote estimation as a standard evaluation metric (Section 2, Figure 1), the authors provide a tool to compare algorithms based on their **potential** rather than their **current cost**. This is a fundamental innovation in experimental methodology for the field.

### 4.2 Hyperparameter Scaling: Breaking the Transferability Assumption

A subtle but vital innovation is the demonstration that optimal regularization **does not transfer** across model scales. Standard practice often adopts hyperparameters from prior works (e.g., weight decay of 0.1 from Brown et al., 2020) and assumes they remain roughly optimal as models grow.

This paper empirically disproves this assumption for data-constrained regimes:
*   **Standard Practice:** Fix weight decay $\approx 0.1$ regardless of parameter count $N$.
*   **New Finding:** Optimal weight decay scales with $N$, reaching 3.2 for 1.4B models—a **30× increase** over standard practice (Section 3, Figure 3).

This is not merely a tuning detail; it reveals that regularization strength is a **scaling knob** comparable to learning rate. The insight here is that over-parameterized models trained on fixed data require proportionally stronger constraints to prevent memorization. Without this discovery, the monotonic scaling law (Figure 3) would not exist, and the authors would have incorrectly concluded that larger models hurt performance (as seen in the unregularized baseline, Figure 2). This challenges the community to revisit "standard" hyperparameters whenever the data-to-parameter ratio shifts significantly.

### 4.3 Ensembling Supremacy: Independence vs. Sparsity

The finding that ensembling independently trained models outperforms scaling a single model's parameters challenges the prevailing industry trend toward monolithic model scaling and Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures.

*   **Prevailing Wisdom:** Scale performance by increasing a single model's size or using sparse experts (MoE) to keep compute manageable.
*   **This Paper's Insight:** For fixed data, training multiple independent models (ensembles) achieves a lower loss asymptote (3.34) than scaling a single model (3.43) (Section 4.2, Figure 4).

The innovation lies in validating the **"multi-view" theory** (Allen-Zhu and Li, 2023) in a pre-training context. The paper argues that independent training trajectories allow each model to learn different features of the limited data, whereas a single large model biases toward learning only one subset of features. This distinguishes ensembling from MoE: MoE models share a single learning trajectory and gradient signal, which the authors argue fails to capture the diversity benefits of true independence (Section 8, Appendix C.3.1).

This is a fundamental architectural insight: **diversity of training dynamics** matters more than **parameter count** when data is scarce. It suggests that in a data-constrained regime, compute is better spent training $K$ models of size $N$ than one model of size $KN$.

### 4.4 Safe Self-Distillation: Avoiding Model Collapse

The paper demonstrates a practical breakthrough in **self-distillation** that contradicts recent warnings about model collapse. Recent literature (e.g., Shumailov et al., 2024; Gerstgrasser et al., 2024) suggests training on synthetic data leads to degradation.

*   **Prior Concern:** Training a student on teacher-generated data causes distributional shift and performance collapse.
*   **This Paper's Innovation:** Mixing real and synthetic data prevents collapse and **improves** performance over the teacher.

The key insight is the **mixing ratio**. By training on a mixture of real tokens $D$ and synthetic tokens $D'$ (e.g., 1:3 ratio for self-distillation, Table 3), the student avoids the feedback loop that causes collapse. This allows a 300M student to outperform its 300M teacher (Section 6.2, Figure 9), effectively creating a "free" performance gain without increasing parameter count.

This is significant because it offers a pathway to deploy the benefits of large ensembles (which are expensive at inference) on small, deployable models. The finding that self-distillation works *better* than standard pre-training for the same architecture (green star vs. purple point in Figure 9) suggests that synthetic data, when mixed correctly, acts as a form of regularization or data augmentation rather than a contaminant.

### 4.5 Critical Perspective: Scale and Noise Limitations

While these insights are novel, a critical educator must highlight where the evidence is preliminary. The paper operates at a scale (200M to 1.6B tokens) significantly smaller than modern frontier models (trillions of tokens).

*   **Asymptote Noise:** The authors explicitly note in Appendix H.1 that asymptote estimation is noisy. The standard deviation in asymptotes across seeds is close to the run-to-run variance, meaning the precise values (e.g., 3.17 vs 3.34) should be interpreted as **rough estimates** rather than exact physical constants.
*   **Scale Extrapolation:** While Section 5 shows data efficiency gains persist up to 1.6B tokens, extrapolating these laws to trillion-token regimes involves uncertainty. The paper acknowledges this in Section 5.4, noting that while exponents are similar, the tiered asymptote estimation can be unreliable.
*   **Architecture Specifics:** The results rely on Llama-style architectures. It remains an open question whether these regularization scales (30× weight decay) transfer to non-transformer architectures or different optimizers.

Despite these limitations, the **directionality** of the findings is robust: regularization enables scaling, ensembling beats parameter scaling, and mixing prevents collapse. These qualitative insights hold even if the exact quantitative gains shift at larger scales.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their claims through controlled experiments. We analyze the evaluation methodology, dissect the quantitative results with specific numbers, assess whether the evidence supports the paper's conclusions, and identify where findings are conditional or preliminary.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Token Budgets.** The paper operates in a deliberately small-scale regime to enable exhaustive hyperparameter search under unlimited compute. The primary experimental setting uses **200M tokens** from DCLM (Li et al., 2025), a filtered web text corpus. This is approximately 10,000× smaller than typical pre-training runs (e.g., Llama 3 used ~15T tokens), but the authors argue this enables clean isolation of algorithmic effects. To test generalization, Section 5 extends experiments to **400M, 800M, and 1.6B tokens**, maintaining the same data source with smaller pools as subsets of larger pools.

For downstream evaluation, the authors use three accuracy-based benchmarks from Thrush et al. (2025):
- **PIQA** (Physical Interaction QA): Tests physical commonsense reasoning
- **SciQ**: Tests science question answering
- **ARC Easy**: Tests elementary science knowledge

For continued pre-training (CPT), they adopt the MegaMath-Web-Pro dataset from Wang et al. (2025), restricting to **4B tokens** from the full **73B tokens** to simulate data constraints.

**Primary Metric: Validation Loss.** The paper uses held-out validation loss as the primary optimization target. A fixed validation set of **1024 sequences (4M tokens)** is held out from DCLM and never used for training or hyperparameter selection. The authors justify this choice in Section 2 and Section 7.1, noting that validation loss correlates with downstream capabilities at their scale. All reported losses are measured after the learning rate anneals to zero, avoiding transient training dynamics.

**Secondary Metric: Downstream Accuracy.** Benchmark performance is reported as accuracy percentage with standard error. Table 5 provides full breakdowns across all model types and ensemble sizes. The authors explicitly state they did not evaluate on benchmarks until after selecting the best recipes via validation loss, making these a held-out test of generalization.

**Data Efficiency Metric.** To quantify improvements across token scales, the authors define data efficiency as the ratio $D'/D$, where $D'$ is the effective data the standard recipe would need to match a recipe's asymptote at token count $D$. This is computed by interpolating $D'$ via the standard recipe's data scaling law. At 200M tokens, a data efficiency of 5.17× means the standard recipe would need 5.17× more data (approximately 1.03B tokens) to achieve the same loss as the joint scaling recipe at 200M tokens.

**Baselines.** The paper compares four main recipes:
1. **Standard (unregularized)**: Weight decay 0.1, tuned learning rate and epoch count per parameter count
2. **Regularized**: Jointly tuned weight decay, learning rate, and epoch count per parameter count
3. **Ensembling**: K independent models with logit averaging, hyperparameters tuned for $K \to \infty$ asymptote
4. **Joint scaling**: Both $N \to \infty$ and $K \to \infty$ limits composed

For CPT experiments, the baseline is the default hyperparameters from Wang et al. (2025) on 73B tokens.

**Hyperparameter Search Protocol.** The authors use coordinate descent to find "locally optimal" hyperparameters (Appendix B.1). Starting from an initial guess, they evaluate all $2m$ neighbors in hyperparameter space (where $m$ is the number of hyperparameters) by incrementing or decrementing exactly one variable. They move to any neighbor that improves loss and repeat until no neighbor is better. Hyperparameters are discretized:
- Learning rate: 1 or 3 times a power of 10 (e.g., 1e-3, 3e-3)
- Epoch count: Integer powers of 2 (e.g., 4, 8, 16, 32)
- Weight decay: 0.0 or integer powers of 2 times 0.1 (e.g., 0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.8, 1.6, 3.2)

Bounds are set at maximum learning rate 3e-3, maximum weight decay 6.4, and maximum epoch count 64. These bounds only triggered for three searches, indicating appropriate discretization.

**Training Configuration.** All models use Llama-style auto-regressive architecture with context length 4096 (Table 2). Training uses AdamW optimizer with $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.95$, $\epsilon = 10^{-8}$, and gradient norm clipping at 1. Learning rate follows a cosine schedule with 1% warmup, decaying to 0 by end of training. Mixed precision uses fp32 parameters and bf16 compute/output. Most jobs ran on v4-64 or v4-128 TPUs with bitwise-determinism for reproducibility. Batch size is fixed at 64, the smallest practical size for their hardware (Appendix B.4).

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**Regularized Parameter Scaling (Section 3).** The core finding is that proper regularization transforms an overfitting-limited regime into one with monotonic scaling. Figure 3 shows the regularized recipe achieves loss proportional to approximately $1/N$ for parameter-to-token ratios **140× larger than Chinchilla** recommends.

| Parameter Count | Standard Recipe Loss | Regularized Recipe Loss | Optimal Weight Decay |
|-----------------|---------------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| 150M | ~3.65 | ~3.58 | 0.8 |
| 300M | ~3.60 | ~3.52 | 1.6 |
| 600M | ~3.58 | ~3.48 | 3.2 |
| 1.4B | ~3.62 (worse!) | ~3.46 | 3.2 |

Key observations:
- The 1.4B standard model performs **worse** than the 600M model, confirming overfitting
- Optimal weight decay increases from 0.8 to 3.2 as parameters grow—a **30× increase** over the standard 0.1
- The fitted power law is $\hat{\mathcal{L}}_{200\text{M},N} = \frac{0.05}{N^{1.02}} + 3.43$, with exponent 1.02 vs. Chinchilla's 0.34
- The asymptote of **3.43** represents the best possible loss for this recipe regardless of parameter count

**Ensemble Scaling (Section 4).** Figure 4 demonstrates that scaling ensemble member count $K$ achieves a lower asymptote than scaling parameter count $N$.

| Recipe | Asymptote Loss | Improvement Over Standard |
|--------|---------------|--------------------------|
| Standard (unregularized) | No clean asymptote (loss increases) | — |
| Regularized ($N \to \infty$, $K=1$) | 3.43 | Baseline for comparison |
| Ensembling ($N=300\text{M}$, $K \to \infty$) | 3.34 | 0.09 lower than regularized |
| Joint scaling ($N \to \infty$, $K \to \infty$) | 3.17 | 0.26 lower than regularized |

Notably, even a **K=3 ensemble** outperforms the regularized recipe's asymptote of 3.43. The ensemble hyperparameters differ from single-model optimum: the $K \to \infty$ asymptote benefits from **2× epochs** and **0.5× weight decay** compared to the single-model optimum (Figure 5). This improves the ensembling asymptote from 3.34 to **3.27** when hyperparameters are tuned specifically for large $K$.

**Data Efficiency Across Token Scales (Section 5).** The authors test whether findings generalize beyond 200M tokens by estimating asymptotes at 200M, 400M, 800M, and 1.6B tokens.

| Token Count | Regularized Data Efficiency | Joint Scaling Data Efficiency |
|-------------|---------------------------|------------------------------|
| 200M | 2.29× | 5.17× |
| 400M | ~2.3× | ~5.2× |
| 800M | ~2.3× | ~5.2× |
| 1.6B | ~2.3× | ~5.2× |

The data scaling laws across all three recipes have similar exponents (**0.23 to 0.24**) and asymptotes (**1.89 to 1.96**), implying data efficiency improvement is **constant across all data scales** (Section 5.4). When exponent $\alpha$ and asymptote $E$ are the same for two algorithms, the data efficiency improvement is constant at $(A_2/A_1)^{1/\alpha}$ where $A$ is the numerator of the power law.

Without taking asymptotes, the best ensemble of five 1.4B models is itself **3.75× more data efficient** than baseline at 200M tokens.

**Distillation Results (Section 6).** The asymptote benefits require arbitrarily high parameter counts, but distillation compresses gains into deployable models.

| Model Type | Parameters | Validation Loss | Notes |
|------------|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| Regularized 300M | 300M | 3.57 | Best single model baseline |
| 8-Ensemble of 300M | 2.4B total | 3.32 | Teacher model |
| Distilled Student | 300M | 3.36 | Retains **83%** of ensemble improvement |
| Self-Distilled 300M | 300M | ~3.43 | Outperforms teacher, matches regularized asymptote |

The distilled student achieves loss 3.36 despite being **8× smaller** at inference than the 8-ensemble teacher. It even outperforms the regularized recipe asymptote of 3.43. For self-distillation, the optimal mixing ratio is **1:3** (real:synthetic), training on **13.4B total tokens** (16 × 209M × 4).

**Downstream Benchmarks (Section 7.1).** Validation loss improvements translate to downstream capabilities. Figure 10 and Table 5 show results across PIQA, SciQ, and ARC Easy.

| Model Type | ARC Easy (%) | PIQA (%) | SciQ (%) | Average (%) |
|------------|-------------|----------|----------|-------------|
| Unregularized 300M | 41.96 | 61.15 | 62.90 | 55.34 |
| Regularized 300M | 44.28 | 61.81 | 69.10 | 58.39 |
| Best Ensemble (1.4B, K=5) | 49.71 | 66.38 | 77.10 | 64.39 |
| Distilled Ensemble (300M) | 48.44 | 62.84 | 75.30 | 62.19 |
| Self-Distilled (300M) | 46.68 | 62.35 | 72.60 | 60.54 |
| Model Soups (K=4) | 24.96 | 55.28 | 23.90 | 34.71 |

The best ensemble outperforms the best unregularized model by **9% on average**. The distilled ensemble outperforms the unregularized 300M model by approximately **7%**. Notably, model soups (weight averaging) achieve near-random performance on SciQ (23.90%), confirming they fail for pre-training.

**Continued Pre-Training (Section 7.2).** Table 1 demonstrates immediate applicability beyond pre-training from scratch.

| Method | Tokens Used | GSM8K (%) | MATH (%) | MATHQA (%) | Average (%) |
|--------|-------------|-----------|----------|------------|-------------|
| Llama 3B Base | — | 28.23 | 6.90 | 35.07 | 24.25 |
| CPT Default (73B) | 73B | 49.51 | 23.40 | 44.79 | 39.23 |
| CPT Default (4B) | 4B | 38.44 | 14.38 | 38.96 | 30.59 |
| CPT + Lower BS + Epoching (4B) | 4B | 44.50 | 17.64 | 41.31 | 34.48 |
| CPT Ensemble K=8 (4B) | 4B | 52.99 | 23.50 | 45.26 | 40.58 |

The best ensemble using only **4B tokens** outperforms the default CPT on **73B tokens**, achieving a **17.5× data efficiency improvement**. Weight decay was not helpful for CPT (unlike pre-training from scratch), suggesting regularization needs differ for continued training scenarios.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

**Coordinate Descent vs. Fixed Hyperparameters (Appendix B.2).** Figure 11 ablates the hyperparameter search procedure, showing joint tuning is critical:
- Fixing weight decay at 0.1 (red): Loss does not scale monotonically
- Transferring optimal weight decay from 150M to larger models (green): Scaling is not monotonic
- Transferring optimal epoch count from 150M to larger models (blue): Scaling plateaus faster
- Joint tuning per parameter count (purple): Clean monotonic scaling

This confirms that hyperparameters **do not transfer** across model scales in data-constrained regimes.

**Weight Decay Sensitivity (Appendix B.4).** Figure 13 (right) shows how varying weight decay impacts epoched models. More over-parameterized models require larger regularization (3.2 for 1.4B vs. 1.6 for 300M). Figure 14 shows training dynamics: high weight decay runs decrease loss more slowly at the start but rapidly decrease near the end, eventually beating low weight decay runs. This suggests evaluation should only consider **end-of-training loss**, not intermediate checkpoints.

**Batch Size Effects (Appendix B.4).** Figure 13 (left) shows smaller batch sizes improve performance for fixed data points, corroborating prior optimization work (Keskar et al., 2017; Smith et al., 2020). The authors use batch size 64, the smallest practical for their hardware. Smaller batches introduce more gradient noise, acting as implicit regularization.

**Ensemble Randomness Sources (Appendix C.1).** The authors test whether ensemble diversity comes from model initialization, data order, or both:
- Both seeds varied: Standard deviation 0.008207
- Train seed only: Standard deviation 0.007605
- Data seed only: Standard deviation 0.007213

Either source alone delivers most ensembling benefits, with data order helping more. This aligns with prior work showing small instability induces majority of variance (Jordan, 2024; Summers and Dinneen, 2021).

**Mixing Ratio for Distillation (Appendix E.3).** Table 4 ablates the effect of mixing real and synthetic data:

| Method | Validation Loss |
|--------|-----------------|
| Teacher Model | 3.7103 |
| Self-Distill (1:1 mixing) | 3.4373 |
| Self-Distill (No mixing) | 4.0693 |

Without mixing real data, self-distillation is **substantially worse** than the teacher, confirming model collapse occurs when training only on synthetic data. Mixing preserves the signal from real data while leveraging synthetic augmentation.

**Asymptote Sensitivity Analysis (Appendix H.1).** Figure 20 tests whether asymptote estimation is reliable given run-to-run variance:
- For parameter scaling: Three power laws fit across different seeds show asymptotes staying close together, with standard deviation near the run-to-run standard deviation for 300M models
- For ensembling: Subsampling ensemble members (up to 4 vs. up to 8) produces extremely similar power laws

The authors explicitly note this is a **limited stress-test** and advise interpreting asymptotes as **rough estimates**, especially for the two-tier and three-tier power laws in joint scaling where best runs are further from asymptotes. The estimated asymptotes vary by at most **0.02 loss** across 3 seeds for the regularized recipe.

**Alternatives to Ensembling (Appendix C.3).** The paper tests Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) and model soups as alternatives:
- **MoE**: Early experiments show jointly trained 10-ensemble improves over single model by only 0.02 loss, suggesting MoE sparsity does not benefit from "multi-view" data like independent ensembles
- **Model Soups**: Table 5 shows near-random performance on downstream benchmarks for pre-training (34.71% average vs. 64.39% for best ensemble), but Table 7 shows soups slightly outperform ensembles for CPT (41.34% vs. 40.58% average on math benchmarks)

This suggests weight averaging works better when models are closer in loss basin (as in CPT from the same initialization) but fails for pre-training from scratch.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

**Strengths of the Experimental Design.**

The experiments are well-controlled for their stated purpose. By fixing the token corpus at 200M and varying only algorithmic choices, the authors isolate the effect of regularization, ensembling, and distillation from confounding factors like data quality or scale. The coordinate descent hyperparameter search is exhaustive enough to credibly claim "locally optimal" settings, and the discretization choices (powers of 2 for epochs, specific learning rate values) are justified by preliminary experiments.

The multi-scale validation (200M to 1.6B tokens) strengthens claims that findings generalize beyond a single token budget. The downstream benchmark evaluation is properly held-out—selected after recipe optimization via validation loss—which prevents benchmark overfitting. The CPT experiments demonstrate immediate practical applicability beyond the small-scale pre-training setting.

**Limitations and Conditional Findings.**

Several important caveats temper the strength of the conclusions:

1. **Scale Gap**: The experiments operate at 200M to 1.6B tokens, while modern frontier models train on trillions of tokens. While Section 5.4 shows data efficiency gains persist across tested scales, extrapolating to trillion-token regimes involves uncertainty. The authors acknowledge this in Section 5.4, noting tiered asymptote estimation can be unreliable.

2. **Asymptote Noise**: Appendix H.1 explicitly states asymptote estimation is noisy. The standard deviation in asymptotes across seeds is close to run-to-run variance, meaning precise values (e.g., 3.17 vs. 3.34) should be interpreted as rough estimates. This is particularly relevant for the joint scaling asymptote, which requires fitting power laws to power law asymptotes—a procedure prone to compounding estimation error.

3. **Architecture Specificity**: All results use Llama-style architectures with AdamW optimizer. It remains unknown whether the 30× weight decay finding transfers to non-transformer architectures, different optimizers (e.g., Muon, AdamW with different $\beta$ values), or architectures with different depth-to-width ratios. The 1.4B model trades depth for width compared to smaller models (16 layers vs. 24 for 600M), which the authors note in Appendix B.5 but argue is corrected by proper weight decay tuning.

4. **CPT Regularization Difference**: Weight decay was not helpful for continued pre-training (Section 7.2), unlike pre-training from scratch. This suggests the regularization findings are **conditional on the training scenario**—models continuing from a pre-trained checkpoint may have different overfitting dynamics than models trained from random initialization.

5. **Ensemble Inference Cost**: While ensembling improves training data efficiency, it increases inference compute by $K\times$. The distillation results (Section 6) address this, but the 8× smaller student retains only 83% of the ensemble benefit, not 100%. There is a trade-off between inference efficiency and performance retention.

6. **Distillation Token Cost**: The distillation procedure requires generating synthetic tokens, which consumes additional compute. The ensemble distillation run trains on **33.4B tokens** total (16 × 209M × 10), and the self-distillation run trains on **13.4B tokens** (16 × 209M × 4). While compute is assumed unlimited, this represents a significant increase in training tokens beyond the base 200M.

**Failure Cases.**

The paper documents several clear failure cases that strengthen credibility:
- Standard recipe with 1.4B parameters performs worse than 600M (Figure 2)
- Model soups fail for pre-training, achieving near-random performance on SciQ (Table 5)
- Self-distillation without mixing real data causes collapse, achieving 4.0693 loss vs. teacher's 3.7103 (Table 4)
- Transferring hyperparameters across model scales fails to produce monotonic scaling (Figure 11)

These failure cases demonstrate the authors tested their methods rigorously and report negative results, not just successes.

**Overall Assessment.**

The experiments convincingly support the paper's core claims within their stated scope:
- ✅ Proper regularization enables monotonic scaling in parameter count under data constraints
- ✅ Ensembling achieves lower loss asymptotes than parameter scaling alone
- ✅ Data efficiency gains persist across tested token scales (200M to 1.6B)
- ✅ Distillation compresses ensemble benefits into smaller models
- ✅ Findings transfer to downstream benchmarks and continued pre-training

The primary limitation is scale: results at 200M–1.6B tokens may not fully extrapolate to trillion-token regimes. However, the **directionality** of findings (regularization enables scaling, ensembling beats parameter scaling, mixing prevents collapse) is robust even if exact quantitative gains shift. The paper's contribution is establishing the **principle** that algorithmic improvements can significantly improve data efficiency, not claiming exact asymptote values are universal constants.

The CPT results are particularly compelling for immediate applicability: achieving 17.5× data efficiency on math benchmarks with a simple ensemble of epoched models demonstrates these methods work beyond controlled pre-training experiments. This suggests practitioners can adopt these techniques now, even while awaiting validation at larger scales.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper demonstrates compelling gains in data-efficient pre-training, several important limitations and trade-offs constrain the applicability of these findings. This section critically examines where the methods may fail, what assumptions underpin the results, and which practical considerations the paper does not fully address.

### 6.1 Scale Extrapolation Uncertainty

The most significant limitation is the **scale gap** between experimental settings and modern frontier models. All primary experiments operate at **200M to 1.6B tokens**, while state-of-the-art language models train on **trillions of tokens** (e.g., Llama 3 used ~15T tokens). This represents a 1,000× to 10,000× difference in data scale.

The authors acknowledge this explicitly in Section 5.4:

> "Though the data scaling laws are expected to be noisy, these laws predict that all three scaling recipes decay at a similar rate with exponents between 0.23 and 0.24 and asymptotes between 1.89 and 1.96."

However, they also note:

> "We note that this is a limited stress-test and that it is likely our asymptote estimation procedure is quite noisy." (Appendix H.1)

**Why this matters:** Scaling laws do not always extrapolate cleanly across orders of magnitude. Historical examples include:
- Chinchilla scaling (Hoffmann et al., 2022) revised earlier Kaplan et al. (2020) laws when tested at larger scales
- The parameter scaling exponent of 1.02 found in this paper (Section 3) differs substantially from Chinchilla's 0.34, raising questions about whether this holds at trillion-token scales

The paper's claim that data efficiency improvements "persist at higher token budgets" (Section 5.4) is based on extrapolation from only four data points (200M, 400M, 800M, 1.6B). While the similar exponents across recipes are encouraging, this does not guarantee the **magnitude** of improvement (5.17×) remains constant at larger scales.

### 6.2 Asymptote Estimation Noise

The paper's core evaluation metric—loss asymptotes—is inherently noisy. Appendix H.1 presents a sensitivity analysis showing:

- For regularized parameter scaling: asymptotes vary by **at most 0.02 loss** across 3 seeds
- For ensembling: subsampling members (up to 4 vs. up to 8) produces "extremely similar" power laws

While the authors frame this as evidence of reliability, a critical reading suggests the opposite: **0.02 loss variance is comparable to the gains being measured**. Consider the key comparisons:

| Comparison | Loss Difference |
|------------|-----------------|
| Regularized vs. Ensembling asymptote | 3.43 - 3.34 = 0.09 |
| Ensembling vs. Joint scaling asymptote | 3.34 - 3.17 = 0.17 |
| Asymptote estimation variance | ~0.02 |

The variance represents **22% of the regularized-to-ensembling gap** and **12% of the ensembling-to-joint gap**. This means the precise ranking of methods, while directionally consistent, has non-trivial uncertainty.

Furthermore, the joint scaling asymptote requires **nested power law fitting**: fitting power laws to estimate asymptotes, then fitting another power law to those asymptotes. This compounds estimation error at each level. The authors acknowledge this in Appendix H.1:

> "We note that this does not test our two-tier and three-tier power laws for joint scaling of parameters, members, and data, nor does it test our settings where our best run is further from the asymptote."

**Practical implication:** Practitioners should treat asymptote values (3.17, 3.34, 3.43) as **rough ordinal indicators** rather than precise performance predictions. The directionality (joint > ensemble > regularized > standard) is more reliable than the exact magnitudes.

### 6.3 Computational Trade-offs

#### 6.3.1 Inference Cost for Ensembles

Ensembling improves training data efficiency but imposes a **K× inference compute penalty**. An 8-ensemble of 300M models requires 8× more FLOPs per token generated compared to a single 300M model.

The paper addresses this through distillation (Section 6), but this introduces its own trade-offs:

| Model | Parameters | Validation Loss | Inference Cost |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| 8-Ensemble Teacher | 2.4B total | 3.32 | 8× baseline |
| Distilled Student | 300M | 3.36 | 1× baseline |

The distilled student retains only **83% of the ensemble improvement** over the regularized 300M baseline (Section 6.1). This means **17% of the ensemble benefit is lost** during compression. For applications where inference latency or cost is critical, this loss may be unacceptable.

Additionally, distillation itself requires substantial compute:
- Ensemble distillation trains on **33.4B tokens** total (16 × 209M × 10 mixing ratio)
- Self-distillation trains on **13.4B tokens** total (16 × 209M × 4 mixing ratio)

While the paper assumes compute is unconstrained, this represents a **167× to 67× increase** in training tokens beyond the base 200M corpus. In settings where compute is not truly unlimited, this cost must be weighed against the data efficiency gains.

#### 6.3.2 Hyperparameter Search Cost

The coordinate descent procedure for finding locally optimal hyperparameters (Appendix B.1) is computationally expensive. For each parameter count and token count combination, the authors must:

1. Seed the search with initial runs
2. Evaluate all $2m$ neighbors (where $m = 3$ hyperparameters: learning rate, epoch count, weight decay)
3. Repeat until no neighbor improves loss

The paper notes:

> "Though this procedure is quite expensive, we found it better than other natural heuristics which don't rigorously follow this procedure separately for each parameter and token count." (Appendix B.1)

However, the paper does not quantify this cost. Given that they test 4 parameter counts × 4 token counts × multiple hyperparameter configurations, the total number of training runs is substantial. For practitioners with limited compute budgets, this search cost may be prohibitive.

**Mitigation:** The paper provides heuristics that may reduce search cost:
- Weight decay scales with parameter count (approximately 0.8 at 150M, 3.2 at 1.4B)
- Ensemble hyperparameters: 2× epochs and 0.5× weight decay relative to single-model optimum (Appendix C.2)

However, these heuristics are derived from the paper's specific experimental setting and may not transfer to different architectures or data distributions.

### 6.4 Method-Specific Failure Modes

#### 6.4.1 Weight Decay Ineffective for Continued Pre-Training

A critical finding that limits the generalizability of the regularization results is that **weight decay was not helpful for continued pre-training (CPT)** (Section 7.2):

> "We then apply our interventions from Appendix B.4 and Section 3 by decreasing batch size and epoching, and we find that weight decay was not helpful for CPT."

This suggests the regularization findings are **conditional on training from random initialization**. Models continuing from a pre-trained checkpoint may have different overfitting dynamics—perhaps because the checkpoint already encodes useful priors that reduce the need for explicit regularization.

**Implication:** Practitioners applying these methods to CPT scenarios should not blindly adopt the 30× weight decay recommendation. The paper's CPT results show ensembling and epoching still help, but weight decay tuning may be unnecessary or even harmful.

#### 6.4.2 Model Soups Fail for Pre-Training

The paper explicitly tests weight averaging (model soups) as an alternative to logit ensembling and finds it **fails catastrophically for pre-training**:

> "Model soups achieving close to random performance on downstream benchmarks (Table 5) but slightly outperforming ensembles in continued pre-training (Table 7)." (Appendix C.3.2)

Table 5 shows model soups achieve only **34.71% average accuracy** on downstream benchmarks compared to **64.39%** for the best ensemble—near-random performance on SciQ (23.90%).

The authors attribute this to models entering **different loss basins** when trained from scratch (Ainsworth et al., 2023; Singh and Jaggi, 2023). Weight averaging only works when models are in the same basin, which is more likely in CPT where all models start from the same checkpoint.

**Implication:** Practitioners cannot substitute weight averaging for logit ensembling in pre-training scenarios. This matters because weight averaging has no inference cost penalty, while logit ensembling does.

#### 6.4.3 Self-Distillation Requires Mixing Real Data

Table 4 (Appendix E.3) demonstrates that self-distillation **fails without mixing real data**:

| Method | Validation Loss |
|--------|-----------------|
| Teacher Model | 3.7103 |
| Self-Distill (1:1 mixing) | 3.4373 |
| Self-Distill (No mixing) | 4.0693 |

Training only on synthetic data causes **model collapse**, achieving loss 0.63 worse than the teacher. This confirms recent warnings about synthetic data degradation (Shumailov et al., 2024; Gerstgrasser et al., 2024).

**Implication:** The self-distillation benefit is **contingent on maintaining access to real data**. In scenarios where real data is exhausted or unavailable (e.g., multiple rounds of recursive distillation), this method may fail. The paper does not test whether the mixing ratio can be gradually shifted toward more synthetic data over multiple distillation rounds.

### 6.5 Architecture and Optimizer Specificity

All experiments use **Llama-style auto-regressive architectures** with the **AdamW optimizer** (Appendix A). The paper does not test:

- **Non-transformer architectures** (e.g., state space models, recurrent architectures)
- **Alternative optimizers** (e.g., Muon, AdamW with different $\beta_1, \beta_2$ values, Sophia)
- **Different depth-to-width ratios** (the 1.4B model trades depth for width compared to smaller models—16 layers vs. 24 for 600M)

The authors acknowledge the architecture anomaly in Appendix B.5:

> "We note that another reason over-fitting may be happening is because our 1.4B model trades depth for width. We did not recognize our model scaling was non-standard until the majority of our experiments had finished..."

They argue that proper weight decay tuning corrects for this, but this remains unverified. The optimal weight decay of 3.2 may be compensating for both over-parameterization **and** the non-standard architecture, making it unclear how much of the finding generalizes to standard scaling.

**Open question:** Would a properly scaled 1.4B model (matching the depth-to-width ratio of smaller models) require the same 30× weight decay increase? The paper does not isolate these factors.

### 6.6 Unaddressed Scenarios

Several practically important scenarios are not addressed in this work:

| Scenario | Why It Matters | Paper's Coverage |
|----------|---------------|------------------|
| **Multi-round distillation** | Can ensemble benefits be recursively distilled? | Not tested; only single-round distillation |
| **Heterogeneous ensembles** | Do ensembles of different-sized models help? | Only homogeneous ensembles tested |
| **Data quality variation** | Do findings hold for high-quality vs. noisy data? | Only DCLM web text used |
| **Multilingual pre-training** | Do regularization scales transfer across languages? | Only English web text (DCLM) |
| **Task-specific fine-tuning** | Do data-efficient pre-trained models fine-tune better? | Only pre-training evals and CPT tested |
| **Longer context lengths** | Do findings hold at 32K+ context? | Only 4096 context tested |

The paper's focus on **algorithmic improvements** rather than data or architectural innovations means these orthogonal factors remain unexplored. The authors note in Section 8:

> "We also hope that the performance gains we demonstrate through ensembling and self-distillation are orthogonal to those of existing work and can compose for better performance."

However, this orthogonality is an assumption, not an empirically verified claim.

### 6.7 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes the key trade-offs practitioners must consider:

| Method | Benefit | Cost | When to Use |
|--------|---------|------|-------------|
| **Heavy regularization** | Enables monotonic scaling | Slower initial convergence (Figure 14) | Data-constrained pre-training from scratch |
| **Ensembling** | Lower loss asymptote (3.34 vs. 3.43) | K× inference cost | Training compute abundant, inference compute flexible |
| **Ensemble distillation** | Retains 83% of ensemble benefit | 167× more training tokens | Inference cost constrained |
| **Self-distillation** | Improves over teacher without larger models | Requires mixing real data | Same architecture, real data available |
| **Joint scaling** | Best asymptote (3.17) | Highest compute cost | Maximum performance priority |

**Key takeaway:** These methods are not universally superior—they are **conditionally optimal** for the data-constrained, compute-abundant regime. In compute-constrained settings, standard recipes like Chinchilla may still be more appropriate. The paper's contribution is defining the boundary where this regime shift occurs and providing tools to operate effectively within it.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section synthesizes how the paper's findings reshape pre-training methodology, identifies concrete research directions it opens, and provides actionable guidance for practitioners considering adoption. The implications extend beyond the specific experimental setting— they challenge foundational assumptions about how we allocate compute, tune hyperparameters, and evaluate training recipes.

### 7.1 Shifting the Field's Optimization Paradigm

This work catalyzes three fundamental shifts in how the community should approach language model pre-training:

**From Compute-Constrained to Data-Constrained Optimization.** For the past five years, scaling laws like Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., 2022) have dominated pre-training decisions. These laws optimize for performance at a fixed compute budget, recommending approximately 20× more tokens than parameters. This paper demonstrates that this paradigm is becoming obsolete: when compute grows 4× per year but data grows only 1.03× per year (Section 1), the binding constraint flips. The correct optimization objective becomes minimizing loss subject to fixed data $D$ with compute unconstrained, not minimizing loss subject to fixed compute $C$. This reframing alone changes which recipes are "optimal"—a method that appears inefficient at low compute may have a superior asymptote and dominate at high compute budgets.

**From Fixed Hyperparameters to Scale-Dependent Regularization.** The finding that optimal weight decay scales from 0.8 to 3.2 as parameter count increases from 150M to 1.4B (Section 3, Figure 3)—a 30× increase over standard practice—challenges the community's assumption that hyperparameters transfer across model scales. Prior work often adopts weight decay of 0.1 from Brown et al. (2020) regardless of model size. This paper shows this is fundamentally mismatched for data-constrained regimes. The implication is that **regularization strength is itself a scaling dimension**, comparable to learning rate or model size. Future scaling law papers should report how optimal regularization changes with $N$ and $D$, not just treat it as a fixed constant.

**From Point Estimates to Asymptote-Based Evaluation.** Traditional scaling law papers compare methods at specific compute budgets (e.g., "which model is best at 10²⁰ FLOPs?"). This paper introduces **loss asymptote estimation** as a new evaluation metric—the limit of loss as parameter count or ensemble member count approaches infinity (Section 2, Figure 1). This matters because a recipe with a lower asymptote will eventually outperform one with a higher asymptote at sufficiently high compute, even if it appears worse at current budgets. For example, the joint scaling recipe's asymptote of 3.17 loss (Section 4.3) is 0.26 lower than the regularized recipe's 3.43, but this gap only fully materializes at high $N$ and $K$. Asymptote-based evaluation allows researchers to compare the **ceiling** of different approaches rather than their **current cost**.

These shifts collectively suggest that the field has been optimizing for the wrong constraint. As compute becomes abundant and data becomes scarce, recipes that made sense for the compute-constrained era need fundamental revision.

### 7.2 Open Research Questions

The paper's findings generate several concrete research directions that would strengthen, extend, or challenge its conclusions:

| Research Direction | Motivation | Specific Questions |
|-------------------|------------|-------------------|
| **Large-scale validation** | Experiments operate at 200M–1.6B tokens, while frontier models train on trillions | Do the 5.17× data efficiency gains and 30× weight decay findings hold at 1T+ tokens? Does the parameter scaling exponent of 1.02 (vs. Chinchilla's 0.34) persist? |
| **Architecture generality** | All results use Llama-style transformers with AdamW | Do findings transfer to state space models (e.g., Mamba), recurrent architectures, or alternative optimizers (e.g., Muon, Sophia)? |
| **Multi-round distillation safety** | Paper tests single-round distillation only | Can ensemble benefits be recursively distilled over multiple rounds without collapse? What mixing ratios are safe for round 2, 3, etc.? |
| **MoE vs. ensembling comparison** | Paper hypothesizes MoE lacks "multi-view" benefits (Appendix C.3.1) but only tested jointly-trained 10-ensemble | Do sparse MoE architectures with independent expert routing capture ensemble diversity benefits? What is the compute-efficiency trade-off? |
| **Better asymptote estimation** | Appendix H.1 acknowledges asymptote estimation is noisy (0.02 loss variance) | Can Bayesian fitting or bootstrap methods reduce estimation uncertainty? How many ensemble members are needed for stable asymptote estimates? |
| **Heterogeneous ensembles** | Paper tests only homogeneous ensembles (same $N$ for all $K$ members) | Do ensembles of different-sized models (e.g., two 300M + one 600M) achieve better asymptotes? Does this enable more efficient distillation? |
| **Data quality interaction** | All experiments use DCLM web text | Do regularization scales change for high-quality curated data vs. noisy web text? Does ensembling help more for noisy or clean data? |
| **Fine-tuning transfer** | Paper evaluates pre-training loss and CPT, not downstream fine-tuning | Do data-efficient pre-trained models fine-tune better on downstream tasks? Does heavy regularization hurt or help task adaptation? |

The most urgent direction is **large-scale validation**. While Section 5.4 shows data efficiency gains persist from 200M to 1.6B tokens, extrapolating to trillion-token regimes involves substantial uncertainty. A replication at 100B+ tokens would either validate the paper's core claims or reveal where scaling laws break down.

A second high-priority direction is **architecture generality**. The 30× weight decay finding is specific to Llama-style transformers with AdamW. If this does not transfer to emerging architectures (e.g., hybrid attention-SSM models), the practical impact is limited. Testing across architectures would determine whether this is a universal principle or architecture-specific tuning.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

The paper's methods have immediate applicability in several scenarios where data is constrained but compute is available:

**Continued Pre-Training on Domain Data.** Section 7.2 demonstrates the most compelling practical application: an ensemble of epoched models trained on 4B tokens of MegaMath-Web-Pro outperforms default CPT on 73B tokens, achieving **17.5× data efficiency** (Table 1). This is directly applicable to:
- Domain adaptation where labeled data is scarce (e.g., medical, legal, scientific text)
- Organizations that cannot access more domain-specific data but have compute resources
- Iterative model improvement where fresh data is exhausted

The key adaptation for CPT: **remove weight decay** (Section 7.2 notes it was not helpful for CPT), but retain epoching and ensembling.

**Small Model Training via Distillation.** Section 6 shows that distilling an 8-ensemble of 300M models into a single 300M student retains **83% of the ensemble improvement** while reducing inference compute 8× (Figure 9). This is valuable for:
- Edge deployment where inference latency matters
- Organizations that need strong small models but cannot access large teacher models from external sources
- Scenarios where the ensemble itself serves as an internal teacher for distillation

The self-distillation finding is particularly notable: a 300M student can outperform its 300M teacher when mixing real and synthetic data at 1:3 ratio (Table 3). This provides a "free" performance gain without requiring larger models.

**Resource-Constrained Pre-Training.** Organizations that cannot compete on data acquisition (e.g., non-English languages, specialized domains) can use these methods to maximize performance from limited corpora. The regularized recipe alone provides **2.29× data efficiency** at 200M tokens (Section 5.2), meaning a model trained with proper regularization on 200M tokens matches a standard model trained on ~460M tokens.

**Ensemble-as-a-Service for High-Stakes Applications.** For applications where accuracy matters more than inference cost (e.g., medical diagnosis, legal analysis, scientific reasoning), the joint scaling recipe's asymptote of 3.17 loss (Section 4.3) may justify the $K\times$ inference penalty. The 9% downstream benchmark improvement (Section 7.1) suggests meaningful capability gains for reasoning-intensive tasks.

### 7.4 Integration and Reproducibility Guidance

Practitioners considering adoption should use the following decision framework:

**When to Use Heavy Regularization (30× Weight Decay).**
- ✅ Pre-training from scratch on fixed, limited data
- ✅ Parameter-to-token ratio exceeds Chinchilla recommendations (>20×)
- ✅ You can afford slower initial convergence (Figure 14 shows high weight decay runs converge slower early but win at end)
- ❌ Continued pre-training from existing checkpoints (Section 7.2 found weight decay unhelpful for CPT)
- ❌ Compute-constrained settings where training must converge quickly

**When to Use Ensembling.**
- ✅ Training compute is abundant, inference compute is flexible
- ✅ Data is fixed and cannot be expanded
- ✅ You need the absolute best performance regardless of inference cost
- ✅ Continued pre-training (model soups slightly outperform ensembles for CPT per Table 7)
- ❌ Latency-sensitive inference without distillation
- ❌ Pre-training where weight averaging (model soups) is tempting—it fails catastrophically (Table 5 shows 34.71% vs. 64.39% average accuracy)

**When to Use Distillation.**
- ✅ You want ensemble benefits without inference cost penalty
- ✅ You can afford 67×–167× more training tokens for distillation (Section 6.1)
- ✅ You have access to real data for mixing (critical to avoid collapse per Table 4)
- ❌ You cannot generate sufficient synthetic tokens
- ❌ You plan multiple distillation rounds (untested—collapse risk unknown)

**Hyperparameter Starting Points.** Based on the paper's findings, practitioners can use these heuristics to reduce search cost:

| Setting | Learning Rate | Weight Decay | Epoch Count |
|---------|--------------|--------------|-------------|
| Regularized pre-training (300M, 200M tokens) | 3e-3 | 1.6 | 16 |
| Regularized pre-training (1.4B, 200M tokens) | 1e-3 | 3.2 | 8 |
| Ensemble member (tuned for $K \to \infty$) | Same as single | 0.5× single-model | 2× single-model |
| Ensemble distillation | 3e-3 | 0.1 | 16 |
| Self-distillation | 3e-3 | 0.1 | 16 |

These are starting points from the paper's specific setting (Llama architecture, AdamW, DCLM data). Practitioners should verify via small-scale search before committing to full training runs.

**Reproducibility Checklist.** The authors open-source all runs on WandB and code on GitHub (Section 1). To reproduce key findings:

1. **Fixed validation set**: Use 1024 sequences (4M tokens) held out from training data for consistent comparison (Appendix A)
2. **End-of-training evaluation**: Report loss only after learning rate anneals to zero—intermediate checkpoints mislead due to weight decay dynamics (Figure 14)
3. **Coordinate descent search**: Jointly tune learning rate, epoch count, and weight decay per parameter count—do not transfer hyperparameters across scales (Appendix B.2, Figure 11)
4. **Ensemble diversity**: Vary either data order or initialization seed—both provide most benefits, data order slightly better (Appendix C.1)
5. **Distillation mixing**: Never train only on synthetic data—mix real and synthetic at minimum 1:1 ratio (Table 4 shows 4.0693 loss without mixing vs. 3.4373 with mixing)

**Common Pitfalls to Avoid.**
- Using standard weight decay (0.1) for over-parameterized, epoched models—this causes overfitting (Figure 2 vs. Figure 3)
- Transferring hyperparameters from small to large models—optimal settings do not transfer (Appendix B.2)
- Using model soups for pre-training—weight averaging fails for models trained from scratch (Table 5)
- Self-distillation without real data mixing—causes model collapse (Table 4)
- Evaluating asymptotes from too few points—use at least 4 parameter counts and 4–8 ensemble members for stable estimates (Appendix H.1)

### 7.5 The Broader Message: Revisiting Classical Techniques

Perhaps the most significant implication is methodological rather than technical. Section 9 notes that the algorithms studied (parameter scaling, regularization, ensembling, distillation) mirror classical techniques from when deep learning was genuinely data-constrained (Penn Tree Bank, ImageNet era). The success of these "old" methods suggests the field has overlooked substantial optimization potential by focusing on architectural innovation rather than training procedure refinement.

As the authors state:

> "The success of such simple methods suggests that there is free lunch on the table for data-efficient pre-training, encouraging us to revisit basic decisions such as objective, architecture, and data augmentation." (Section 9)

This is a call to action for the community: before pursuing novel architectures or data sources, ensure classical techniques are optimally tuned for the current constraint regime. The 5.17× data efficiency gain from this paper comes entirely from algorithmic improvements—no new data, no architectural changes, no novel objectives. In a data-constrained future, such "free lunch" may be the difference between competitive and non-competitive models.

The paper's contribution is not just a set of techniques, but a **framework for thinking about pre-training under different constraint regimes**. As compute and data availability continue to diverge, this framework will become increasingly essential for designing efficient training strategies.
