## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the fragmented landscape of Large Language Model (LLM) assessment by presenting the first comprehensive survey organized around three key dimensions: "what to evaluate" (tasks ranging from natural language processing to medical applications), "where to evaluate" (46 benchmarks including MMLU, HELM, and Big-Bench), and "how to evaluate" (automatic versus human protocols). By synthesizing evaluation efforts across models such as ChatGPT, GPT-4, and LLaMA, the authors establish evaluation as an essential discipline to identify system limitations—such as vulnerability to adversarial prompts in PromptBench—and guide the development of safer, more capable AI towards Artificial General Intelligence (AGI). The survey concludes by outlining grand challenges, including the need for dynamic, evolving evaluation systems to prevent data contamination and ensure trustworthy deployment in safety-sensitive sectors like healthcare and finance.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand why this survey is necessary, we must first recognize that the history of Artificial Intelligence (AI) is fundamentally the history of evaluation. As noted in the Introduction, the timeline of AI is depicted as the "timeline of creation and evaluation of intelligent models and algorithms" (Section 1). Every major leap in AI—from the Perceptron to Support Vector Machines (SVMs) and deep learning—was defined not just by the model architecture, but by how researchers proved its capabilities and uncovered its limitations. For instance, the Perceptron was initially touted as Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) but was later revealed as inadequate because it could not resolve the XOR problem (Section 1). This historical context establishes a critical premise: **proper measurement helps to understand intelligence.**

### 2.1 The Shift from Traditional ML to Large Language Models

The arrival of Large Language Models (LLMs) represents a paradigm shift that renders many traditional evaluation protocols insufficient. To understand why, we must distinguish LLMs from prior machine learning approaches. Table 1 in the Background section provides a comparative framework:

*   **Traditional ML & Deep Learning:** These models typically require manual or automatic feature engineering and are trained on large datasets for specific tasks. Evaluation often relies on static test sets (e.g., ImageNet for computer vision, GLUE for language understanding) and standard protocols like $k$-fold cross-validation (Section 2.2).
*   **Large Language Models (LLMs):** Models like GPT-3, InstructGPT, and GPT-4 are characterized by "Very large" training data sizes, "Very Complex" model architectures (based on the Transformer self-attention module), and "Very High" hardware requirements (Table 1).

Crucially, LLMs introduce capabilities that traditional models lack, specifically **in-context learning** and **Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)** (Section 2.1).
*   **In-context learning** allows the model to generate text based on a given prompt without updating weights, enabling interactive and conversational applications.
*   **RLHF** fine-tunes the model using human-generated responses as rewards, aligning outputs with human preferences but also introducing complexities in measuring "alignment."

Because LLMs are **autoregressive**—predicting the next token $y$ given a context sequence $X$ by maximizing $P(y|X)$—they generate open-ended text rather than fixed classifications (Section 2.1). This generative nature makes standard accuracy metrics insufficient for capturing nuances like coherence, factuality, or safety.

### 2.2 The Problem: Fragmented and Static Evaluation

Despite the rapid proliferation of LLMs, the ecosystem for evaluating them is fragmented and lagging behind model capabilities. Figure 2 illustrates a sharp increase in the number of papers focused on LLM evaluation from 2020 to mid-2023, indicating intense research interest. However, the Introduction highlights a critical gap: "a comprehensive overview capturing the entire gamut of evaluations is still lacking" (Section 1).

The specific problems this paper addresses include:

1.  **Fragmentation of Efforts:** Existing research evaluates LLMs from isolated perspectives—some focus on natural language tasks, others on reasoning, medical applications, or ethics (Section 1). There is no unified framework to compare these efforts. For example, while Bubeck et al. [15] claim GPT-4 shows "sparks of AGI," others contest this due to the "human-crafted nature of its evaluation approach" (Section 1).
2.  **Inadequacy of Static Benchmarks:** Traditional deep learning evaluation relies on static validation sets (Section 2.2). For LLMs, this is problematic because as models grow larger, they risk **memorizing** public benchmarks. Section 7.4 warns that "static and public benchmarks are likely to be memorized by LLMs, resulting in potential training data contamination." This renders standard accuracy scores unreliable indicators of true reasoning ability.
3.  **Emergent Risks:** LLMs exhibit emergent abilities that were not explicitly trained, including potential risks like hallucination (generating factually incorrect information), bias, and vulnerability to adversarial attacks. For instance, the `PromptBench` benchmark illustrates that current LLMs are sensitive to adversarial prompts, necessitating careful prompt engineering (Section 1). Existing protocols often fail to capture these safety-critical dimensions.

### 2.3 Real-World Stakes and Theoretical Significance

The motivation for resolving these evaluation gaps is driven by both theoretical and practical imperatives.

*   **Safety-Critical Deployment:** LLMs are increasingly used by individuals with critical information needs, such as students or patients (Section 1). The paper emphasizes the "paramount importance of ensuring their safety and reliability, particularly in safety-sensitive sectors such as financial institutions and healthcare facilities" (Section 1). Without robust evaluation, deploying these models risks propagating misinformation or biased decisions in high-stakes environments.
*   **Guiding Model Development:** Evaluation is not just about auditing; it is about guidance. The Introduction states that "evaluation serves as a critical tool to identify current system limitations and inform the design of more powerful models" (Section 1). For example, understanding that LLMs perform poorly on non-Latin languages (Section 3.1.4) directs future training data collection.
*   **The AGI Question:** There is ongoing debate about whether LLMs represent a step toward Artificial General Intelligence (AGI). The paper notes that "existing evaluation protocols may not be enough to evaluate their capabilities and potential risks" as LLMs become larger with more emergent abilities (Section 1). Proper evaluation is required to substantiate claims about AGI progress.

### 2.4 Positioning Relative to Existing Work

This paper positions itself as the **first comprehensive survey** dedicated specifically to the evaluation of large language models (Section 1). While prior surveys exist on LLMs generally, this work isolates the *evaluation* lifecycle. It distinguishes itself from existing fragmented studies by organizing the landscape into three orthogonal dimensions (Figure 1):

1.  **What to evaluate:** Categorizing tasks from natural language processing to medical applications (Section 3).
2.  **Where to evaluate:** Compiling 46 popular benchmarks and datasets, distinguishing between general, specific, and multi-modal tasks (Section 4).
3.  **How to evaluate:** Analyzing protocols, from automatic metrics to human-in-the-loop testing (Section 5).

Furthermore, the paper advocates for a cultural shift: "evaluation should be treated as an essential discipline to better assist the development of LLMs" (Section 1 Abstract). Unlike works that propose a single new benchmark, this survey aims to "raise awareness in the community of the importance to LLMs evaluations by reviewing the current evaluation protocols and most importantly, shed light on future research about designing new LLMs evaluation protocols" (Section 1). It explicitly acknowledges that "not a single model can perform best in all kinds of tasks" and avoids declaring a single "best" benchmark, instead focusing on matching evaluation methods to specific research goals (Section 6).

By synthesizing success and failure cases (Section 6) and outlining grand challenges like dynamic evaluation and trustworthiness (Section 7), the paper provides a roadmap for moving beyond static, contamination-prone testing toward robust, evolving assessment frameworks.

## 3. Technical Approach

This survey paper does not propose a new evaluation system but rather constructs a comprehensive taxonomy and analytical framework for understanding how Large Language Models (LLMs) are currently evaluated across the research community. The core idea is to organize the fragmented landscape of LLM evaluation into three orthogonal dimensions—"what to evaluate" (tasks), "where to evaluate" (benchmarks and datasets), and "how to evaluate" (protocols and metrics)—thereby providing researchers with a structured mental model for designing, selecting, and interpreting evaluation efforts.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The "system" being described is a conceptual framework that categorizes and analyzes 46 distinct evaluation benchmarks and numerous evaluation protocols used to assess Large Language Models across diverse application domains. This framework solves the problem of fragmented and inconsistent LLM evaluation by providing a unified three-dimensional structure that helps researchers understand which tasks matter, which benchmarks to use, and which evaluation methods are appropriate for their specific goals.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The survey's organizational architecture consists of three primary components that interact to form a complete evaluation lifecycle:

1.  **Task Dimension (What to evaluate):** This component categorizes evaluation tasks into ten major domains including natural language processing (understanding, generation, reasoning), robustness/ethics/bias/trustworthiness, social sciences, natural sciences and engineering, medical applications, agent applications, and other specialized applications. Each task category maps to specific evaluation objectives and success criteria.

2.  **Benchmark Dimension (Where to evaluate):** This component compiles 46 distinct benchmarks organized into three subcategories: general language task benchmarks (e.g., MMLU, HELM, BIG-bench), specific downstream task benchmarks (e.g., MATH for mathematics, MultiMedQA for medical QA), and multi-modal task benchmarks (e.g., MME, MMBench for vision-language models). Each benchmark provides standardized datasets and evaluation criteria.

3.  **Protocol Dimension (How to evaluate):** This component defines evaluation methodologies split into automatic evaluation (using computed metrics like accuracy, F1 score, ROUGE) and human evaluation (using expert annotators with defined criteria like accuracy, relevance, fluency, transparency, safety, and human alignment). This dimension also includes emerging protocols like human-in-the-loop testing and crowd-sourcing approaches.

Information flows through this framework as follows: a researcher first identifies the task domain they care about (Dimension 1), then selects appropriate benchmarks from that domain (Dimension 2), and finally applies suitable evaluation protocols with corresponding metrics (Dimension 3). The survey synthesizes findings across all three dimensions to produce summary conclusions about LLM success and failure cases (Section 6) and identifies grand challenges for future research (Section 7).

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Paper Type and Core Methodological Idea**

This is a survey paper that employs a systematic taxonomy-building approach rather than proposing a new technical system or algorithm. The core methodological idea is to decompose the complex problem of LLM evaluation into three orthogonal dimensions that together capture the complete evaluation lifecycle, as visualized in Figure 1 of the paper. This decomposition allows researchers to navigate the evaluation landscape systematically rather than relying on ad-hoc benchmark selection.

**Task Dimension: What to Evaluate (Section 3)**

The survey organizes evaluation tasks into ten major categories, each representing a distinct capability domain that LLMs must be assessed on. Natural Language Processing tasks form the foundation and are further subdivided into Natural Language Understanding (including sentiment analysis, text classification, natural language inference, and semantic understanding), Reasoning (including mathematical, commonsense, logical, and domain-specific reasoning), Natural Language Generation (including summarization, dialogue, translation, and question answering), Multilingual tasks, and Factuality assessment. For sentiment analysis specifically, the survey notes that LLMs achieve high performance with ChatGPT's prediction performance being "superior to traditional sentiment analysis methods" and coming "close to that of GPT-3.5" according to Qin et al. [159], but performance drops significantly for low-resource languages according to Bang et al. [6].

The Reasoning task category receives substantial attention because it represents a key differentiator between traditional language models and LLMs claiming AGI-like capabilities. The survey distinguishes between arithmetic reasoning (where ChatGPT outperforms GPT-3.5 in most tasks), symbolic reasoning (where ChatGPT is "mostly worse than GPT-3.5" due to uncertain responses), logical reasoning (where ChatGPT and GPT-4 outperform traditional fine-tuning methods but struggle with out-of-distribution data), and multi-step reasoning (where only PaLM and Claude2 achieve performance similar to GPT models according to Fu et al. [47]). For mathematical reasoning specifically, GPT-4 achieves "a significant increase in accuracy of 10 percentage points and a reduction in relative error by 50%" compared to ChatGPT due to superior division and trigonometry abilities (Section 3.4.1).

The Robustness, Ethics, Bias, and Trustworthiness category represents a critical evaluation dimension that goes beyond task performance to assess safety and reliability. Robustness evaluation focuses on out-of-distribution (OOD) stability and adversarial robustness, with the PromptBench benchmark evaluating adversarial text attacks at "multiple levels (character, word, sentence, and semantics)" and finding that "contemporary LLMs are vulnerable to adversarial prompts" (Section 3.2.1). Ethics and bias evaluation assesses whether models internalize and spread harmful information from training corpora, with Deshpande et al. [35] observing that role-playing can cause "an increase in generated toxicity up to 6x" (Section 3.2.2). Trustworthiness evaluation, as exemplified by the DecodingTrust study [201], expands to eight critical aspects including toxicity, stereotype bias, adversarial robustness, privacy, machine ethics, and fairness.

Medical Applications represent a high-stakes evaluation domain where the survey categorizes tasks into Medical Queries (answering health-related questions), Medical Examination (performance on licensing exams like USMLE), and Medical Assistants (clinical decision support). For Medical Examination specifically, Kung et al. [97] showed that "ChatGPT achieves or approaches the passing threshold in these exams with no tailored training" on USMLE Step 1 and Step 2, while Oh et al. [143] found GPT-4 achieved 76.4% accuracy on surgical clinical information compared to 46.8% for GPT-3.5 (Section 3.5).

**Benchmark Dimension: Where to Evaluate (Section 4)**

The survey compiles 46 popular benchmarks organized into three categories based on their scope and specialization. General Task Benchmarks evaluate LLMs across broad capability ranges without focusing on specific domains. MMLU [70] provides "a comprehensive suite of tests for assessing text models in multi-task contexts" covering 57 tasks including elementary mathematics, US history, computer science, law, and medicine. HELM [114] takes a holistic approach evaluating "language models across various aspects such as language understanding, generation, coherence, context sensitivity, common-sense reasoning, and domain-specific knowledge." BIG-bench [182] introduces "a diverse collection of 204 challenging tasks contributed by 450 authors from 132 institutions" covering domains from math and linguistics to social bias and software development. Chatbot Arena [128] uses a crowdsourcing approach where "users can engage with anonymous models and express their preferences via voting" to generate Elo ratings. MT-Bench [260] specifically evaluates multi-turn dialogues using "comprehensive questions tailored to handling conversations."

Specific Downstream Task Benchmarks focus on particular application domains or capability types. MATH [72] concentrates on "assessing reasoning and problem-solving proficiencies of AI models within the domain of mathematics." APPS [68] measures "the ability of language models to generate python code according to natural language specifications." MultiMedQA [177] focuses on medical QA with "seven datasets related to medical QA, including six existing datasets and one new dataset." C-Eval [78] is "the first extensive benchmark to assess the advanced knowledge and reasoning capabilities of foundation models in Chinese" with 52 exams in a Chinese context. CMMLU [108] evaluates "18 LLMs across various academic disciplines" in Chinese language environments. SafetyBench [254] tests "the security performance of a range of popular Chinese and English LLMs" revealing "substantial security flaws in current LLMs."

Multi-modal Task Benchmarks evaluate models that process both text and other modalities like images. MME [46] serves as "an extensive evaluative benchmark, aiming to assess their perceptual and cognitive aptitudes" for Multimodal Large Language Models using "meticulously crafted instruction-answer pairs." MMBench [126] employs a "CircularEval assessment method" to robustly evaluate large-scale vision-language models. SEED-Bench [107] consists of "19,000 multiple-choice questions that have been annotated by human assessors" covering 12 different aspects of multimodal understanding.

**Protocol Dimension: How to Evaluate (Section 5)**

The survey distinguishes between two fundamental evaluation methodologies: Automatic Evaluation and Human Evaluation, each with distinct metrics, advantages, and limitations.

Automatic Evaluation uses standard metrics and evaluation tools that can be computationally computed without human intervention. The survey organizes automatic metrics into four categories as shown in Table 9. Accuracy metrics measure how correct a model is on a given task and include Exact Match (EM) where "if the model's generated answer is an exact match with the manually provided answer, the EM is 1; otherwise, it is 0," F1 score calculated as:

$$F1 = \frac{2 \times Precision \times Recall}{Precision + Recall}$$

and ROUGE score primarily employed for text summarization and machine translation involving "considerations of overlap and matching between texts."

Calibration metrics pertain to "the degree of agreement between the confidence level of the model output and the actual prediction accuracy." Expected Calibration Error (ECE) is one commonly used metric where researchers "categorize model predictions based on confidence and measure the average accuracy of the predictions within each confidence interval" according to Tian et al. [189]. Area Under the Curve (AUC) of selective accuracy and coverage provides another calibration measurement approach.

Fairness metrics assess "whether the model treats different groups consistently, that is, whether the model's performance is equal across different groups" including attributes like gender, race, and age. The survey presents two key fairness formulas from DecodingTrust [201]. Demographic Parity Difference (DPD) measures whether predictions are distributed equally across population groups:

$$DPD = P(\hat{y}|Z=1) - P(\hat{y}|Z=0)$$

where $\hat{y}$ is the binary classification prediction, $Z$ is the population group identifier (typically binary for two different groups), and $P(\hat{y}|Z=1)$ and $P(\hat{y}|Z=0)$ represent the probabilities of predicting the positive class in each population group. Equalized Odds Difference (EOD) ensures equal error rates across populations:

$$EOD = \max\{P(\hat{y}=1|Y=1,Z=1) - P(\hat{y}=1|Y=1,Z=0), P(\hat{y}=1|Y=0,Z=1) - P(\hat{y}=1|Y=0,Z=0)\}$$

where $Y$ is the true label, and the formula compares true positive and false positive rates across groups.

Robustness metrics evaluate model performance "in the face of various challenging inputs, including adversarial attacks, changes in data distribution, noise, etc." Attack Success Rate (ASR) evaluates adversarial robustness calculated as:

$$ASR = \frac{\sum_{(x,y) \in D} \mathbb{I}[f(A(x)) \neq y]}{\sum_{(x,y) \in D} \mathbb{I}[f(x) = y]}$$

where $D$ is the dataset containing $N$ pairs of samples $x_i$ and ground truth $y_i$, $A$ is the adversarial attack method, $f$ is the surrogate model, and $\mathbb{I}$ is the indicator function. Performance Drop Rate (PDR) quantifies "the relative performance degradation after a prompt attack":

$$PDR = 1 - \frac{\sum_{(x,y) \in D} M[f([A(P), x]), y]}{\sum_{(x,y) \in D} M[f([P, x]), y]}$$

where $A$ represents the adversarial attack applied to prompt $P$, and $M$ denotes the evaluation function which varies across different tasks.

Human Evaluation becomes necessary when "automatic evaluation is not suitable" particularly for open-generation tasks where embedded similarity metrics are insufficient. The survey expands the 3H rule (Helpfulness, Honesty, and Harmlessness) into six human assessment criteria as shown in Table 10. Accuracy assesses "the precision and correctness of the generated text" by scrutinizing alignment with factual knowledge. Relevance examines "how well the text addresses the given context or query, ensuring that the information provided is pertinent and directly applicable." Fluency assesses "the language model's ability to produce content that flows smoothly, maintaining a consistent tone and style" while avoiding awkward expressions. Transparency evaluates "the clarity and openness of the language model's decision-making process" enabling users to understand how responses are generated. Safety examines "the language model's ability to avoid producing content that may be inappropriate, offensive, or harmful." Human alignment assesses "the degree to which the language model's output aligns with human values, preferences, and expectations" considering ethical implications.

The survey emphasizes three critical factors for reliable human evaluation: the number of evaluators must provide "adequate representation" and "statistical significance," evaluation rubrics must be clearly defined across the six criteria, and evaluators must possess "relevant domain expertise, task familiarity, and methodological training" (Section 5.2).

**Synthesis Methodology: Deriving Success and Failure Cases (Section 6)**

The survey does not simply catalog evaluation efforts but synthesizes findings across hundreds of papers to produce actionable conclusions about LLM capabilities. The synthesis methodology involves aggregating results from multiple evaluation studies for each task category and identifying consistent patterns across different benchmarks and models. For success cases, the survey identifies capabilities where multiple studies converge on positive findings: LLMs "demonstrate proficiency in generating text by producing fluent and precise linguistic expressions," obtain "impressive performance in tasks involving language understanding, including sentiment analysis, text classification, as well as the handling of factual input," and demonstrate "robust arithmetic reasoning capabilities and excel in logical reasoning."

For failure cases, the survey identifies capabilities where multiple studies converge on limitations: LLMs "exhibit subpar performance" in Natural Language Inference and "encounter challenges in accurately representing human disagreements," have "restricted proficiency in discerning semantic similarity between events," exhibit "limited abilities on abstract reasoning," manifest "suboptimal performance" in "linguistic contexts featuring non-Latin scripts and limited resources," demonstrate "susceptibility when processing visual modal information," and are "sensitive to prompts, especially adversarial prompts." The survey explicitly notes that "all the following conclusions are made based on existing evaluation efforts and the results are only dependent on specific datasets" (Section 6.1), acknowledging the contingency of these findings on current benchmark coverage.

**Design Choices and Framework Rationale**

The three-dimensional framework (What, Where, How) was chosen over alternative organizational schemes for several reasons grounded in the evaluation lifecycle. First, this decomposition mirrors the actual decision process researchers face: they must first determine what capability they want to assess, then identify where (which benchmark) provides appropriate test cases, and finally decide how (which protocol and metrics) to measure performance. Second, the orthogonal nature of these dimensions prevents conflation of task types with benchmark types—a common confusion in the literature where, for example, MMLU is sometimes treated as a task rather than a benchmark covering multiple tasks. Third, this structure enables systematic gap analysis: by mapping existing work across all three dimensions, the survey can identify underexplored combinations (e.g., certain task types lacking dedicated benchmarks, or certain benchmarks lacking human evaluation protocols).

The decision to compile exactly 46 benchmarks (Table 7) reflects a balance between comprehensiveness and usability. The survey acknowledges that "as the evaluation of LLMs is a hot research area, it is very likely that we cannot cover all benchmarks" (Section 4 footnote), but selected benchmarks that represent major evaluation paradigms and have significant adoption in the research community. The categorization into General, Specific Downstream, and Multi-modal benchmarks reflects the fundamental distinction between broad capability assessment versus domain-specific evaluation versus cross-modal evaluation.

The separation of Automatic and Human evaluation protocols recognizes that these methods serve complementary rather than competing purposes. Automatic evaluation provides scalability and reproducibility for large-scale benchmarking, while human evaluation provides nuanced assessment for open-ended generation tasks where standard metrics fail to capture quality dimensions like coherence, helpfulness, or safety. The survey notes that "in practical applications, these two evaluation methods are considered and weighed in combination with the actual situation" (Section 5), advocating for hybrid approaches rather than exclusive reliance on either method.

**Limitations and Boundary Conditions**

The survey explicitly acknowledges several limitations in its technical approach. First, the conclusions about LLM capabilities are contingent on specific datasets: "all the following conclusions are made based on existing evaluation efforts and the results are only dependent on specific datasets" (Section 6.1). This means that identified failure cases may reflect benchmark limitations rather than fundamental model limitations. Second, the survey acknowledges temporal limitations: "Due to the evolution of LLMs especially online services such as Claude and ChatGPT, it is very likely that they become stronger and some of the limitations described in this paper are mitigated (and new limitations may arise)" (Disclaimer). Third, the benchmark compilation is necessarily incomplete given the rapid pace of new benchmark development, and the authors maintain an open-source repository to incorporate community contributions. Fourth, the survey focuses primarily on English-language evaluation efforts, with multilingual evaluation receiving less coverage despite being identified as a gap area.

The framework also has inherent boundary conditions. It is designed for evaluating general-purpose LLMs rather than specialized models fine-tuned for narrow applications. It assumes access to model outputs for evaluation rather than addressing black-box API evaluation constraints. It focuses on post-hoc evaluation rather than evaluation-integrated-training approaches. These boundary conditions define the scope within which the survey's recommendations and conclusions apply.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This survey makes several novel contributions that distinguish it from prior work on LLMs and AI evaluation. Below, I identify the most significant innovations and explain why they matter for the research community.

### 4.1 First Comprehensive Survey Dedicated to LLM Evaluation

**What makes it different:** While numerous surveys exist on Large Language Models generally (e.g., Zhao et al. [257] on LLM capabilities), this paper explicitly states it "serves as the first comprehensive survey on the evaluation of large language models" (Section 1). Prior work typically treats evaluation as a subsection of broader LLM surveys, dedicating limited space to benchmarks or metrics without systematic coverage.

**Why it's significant:** This represents a **fundamental innovation** rather than an incremental improvement. By isolating evaluation as a distinct discipline, the authors elevate it from an afterthought to a core research area. The paper argues that "evaluation should be treated as an essential discipline to better assist the development of LLMs" (Abstract), positioning evaluation not merely as auditing but as a driver of model improvement. This reframing has practical consequences: researchers designing new LLMs now have a structured reference for validation, and the community gains shared vocabulary for discussing evaluation gaps.

**Evidence of novelty:** The paper compiles 46 benchmarks (Table 7, Section 4) and synthesizes findings from hundreds of evaluation studies across ten task categories (Section 3). No prior survey provides this breadth while maintaining focus on evaluation methodology rather than model architecture or applications.

### 4.2 Three-Dimensional Framework (What/Where/How)

**What makes it different:** The organizational structure depicted in Figure 1 decomposes evaluation into three orthogonal dimensions:
1. **What to evaluate** (tasks and capabilities)
2. **Where to evaluate** (benchmarks and datasets)
3. **How to evaluate** (protocols and metrics)

This differs from prior categorizations that typically organize by task type alone (e.g., "reasoning benchmarks" or "medical benchmarks") or by model type (e.g., "ChatGPT evaluations").

**Why it's significant:** This is a **fundamental conceptual innovation** that mirrors the actual decision process researchers face. When designing an evaluation study, a researcher must first determine *what capability* they care about, then identify *which benchmark* provides appropriate test cases, and finally decide *which protocol* to measure performance. Prior surveys conflate these dimensions—for instance, treating MMLU as a "task" rather than recognizing it as a benchmark covering multiple tasks.

The orthogonal structure enables **systematic gap analysis**. By mapping existing work across all three dimensions, the survey identifies underexplored combinations: certain task types lacking dedicated benchmarks, or certain benchmarks lacking human evaluation protocols. For example, Section 3.1.4 notes that multilingual tasks receive insufficient coverage despite LLMs' global deployment, while Section 7.4 highlights that static benchmarks dominate despite contamination risks.

**Practical impact:** This framework allows researchers to navigate the evaluation landscape systematically rather than relying on ad-hoc benchmark selection. It also provides reviewers with criteria for assessing whether evaluation claims are supported by appropriate benchmarks and protocols.

### 4.3 Synthesized Success and Failure Cases Across Hundreds of Studies

**What makes it different:** Rather than simply cataloging evaluation efforts, Section 6 aggregates findings across hundreds of papers to produce actionable conclusions about LLM capabilities. The survey explicitly states: "By summarizing and analyzing existing efforts on LLMs evaluation, we may identify the current success and failure cases of LLMs" (Section 6).

**Why it's significant:** This represents a **fundamental innovation in survey methodology**. Most surveys list papers by category without synthesizing contradictory findings. This paper resolves conflicts by identifying consistent patterns across different benchmarks and models. For instance:

| Capability | Consensus Finding | Supporting Evidence |
|------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Sentiment Analysis | High performance | Liang et al. [114], Lopez-Lira and Tang [129], Qin et al. [159] |
| Abstract Reasoning | Limited ability | Gendron et al. [56], Wu et al. [227] |
| Non-Latin Languages | Suboptimal performance | Abdelali et al. [1], Bang et al. [6], Zhang et al. [250] |
| Adversarial Prompts | Vulnerable | Zhu et al. [264], Wang et al. [201] |

**Critical nuance:** The authors explicitly acknowledge contingency: "all the following conclusions are made based on existing evaluation efforts and the results are only dependent on specific datasets" (Section 6.1). This transparency distinguishes the work from claims presenting findings as universal truths.

**Why it matters:** Practitioners gain evidence-based guidance on where LLMs can be safely deployed versus where human oversight remains essential. For example, the consistent finding that LLMs exhibit "subpar performance" in Natural Language Inference and "encounter challenges in accurately representing human disagreements" (Section 6.1.2) suggests caution in deploying LLMs for tasks requiring nuanced disagreement modeling.

### 4.4 Grand Challenges as Research Roadmap

**What makes it different:** Section 7 outlines seven grand challenges for future research, moving beyond summarization to provide directional guidance. These include:
1. Designing AGI Benchmarks
2. Complete Behavioral Evaluation
3. Robustness Evaluation
4. Dynamic and Evolving Evaluation
5. Principled and Trustworthy Evaluation
6. Unified Evaluation Supporting All LLM Tasks
7. Beyond Evaluation: LLM Enhancement

**Why it's significant:** This is a **fundamental innovation** that transforms the survey from a retrospective catalog into a prospective research agenda. Prior surveys typically conclude with generic "future work" suggestions. This paper provides specific, actionable challenges grounded in identified gaps.

**Key insight on dynamic evaluation:** Section 7.4 argues that "static and public benchmarks are likely to be memorized by LLMs, resulting in potential training data contamination." This insight drives the call for "dynamic and evolving evaluation systems" as a grand challenge. The paper cites DynaBench [94] and DynaTask [188] as early examples but notes substantial room for advancement.

**Key insight on evaluation as enhancement:** Section 7.7 states "evaluation is not the end goal but rather the starting point." The survey highlights PromptBench [264] as an example that provides "not only robustness evaluation results on adversarial prompts but also a comprehensive analysis through attention visualization... thus providing prompt engineering guidance for end users." This reframes evaluation from passive measurement to active improvement.

**Practical impact:** Researchers gain prioritized directions for contribution. For instance, the challenge of "Principled and Trustworthy Evaluation" (Section 7.5) identifies a scarcity of research scrutinizing "not only the algorithms but the evaluation system itself," opening new methodological research avenues.

### 4.5 Open-Source Maintenance for Community Collaboration

**What makes it different:** The authors commit to maintaining related materials at https://github.com/MLGroupJLU/LLM-eval-survey (Abstract and Section 1). This transforms the survey from a static publication into a living resource.

**Why it's significant:** This represents an **incremental but meaningful innovation** in survey methodology. Traditional surveys become outdated rapidly in fast-moving fields. By open-sourcing the benchmark list, task categorizations, and evaluation protocols, the authors enable community contributions to keep the resource current.

**Evidence of commitment:** The Disclaimer section acknowledges temporal limitations: "Due to the evolution of LLMs especially online services such as Claude and ChatGPT, it is very likely that they become stronger and some of the limitations described in this paper are mitigated." The open-source repository directly addresses this by allowing continuous updates.

**Why it matters:** This approach fosters a "collaborative community for better evaluations" (Section 1 Contributions), aligning with the paper's core thesis that evaluation should be treated as a shared discipline rather than isolated efforts.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section requires careful framing because **this is a survey paper, not an empirical research study**. The authors do not conduct original experiments but instead synthesize quantitative findings from hundreds of evaluation studies published between 2020 and mid-2023. Therefore, this analysis describes the **evaluation methodology of the reviewed studies** and extracts specific quantitative results that the survey aggregates to support its claims about LLM capabilities and limitations.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology of Reviewed Studies

The survey synthesizes evaluation efforts across three dimensions, each with distinct methodological characteristics:

**Task Coverage (What to Evaluate):**
The survey organizes findings across **10 major task categories** (Section 3), with each category drawing from multiple independent studies:
- Natural Language Processing (understanding, generation, reasoning, multilingual, factuality)
- Robustness, Ethics, Bias, and Trustworthiness
- Social Sciences
- Natural Sciences and Engineering
- Medical Applications
- Agent Applications
- Other Applications (education, search, personality testing, specific tasks)

**Benchmark Coverage (Where to Evaluate):**
Table 7 compiles **46 distinct benchmarks** organized into three categories:
- **General Task Benchmarks** (e.g., MMLU [70], HELM [114], BIG-bench [182], Chatbot Arena [128])
- **Specific Downstream Task Benchmarks** (e.g., MATH [72], MultiMedQA [177], C-Eval [78], SafetyBench [254])
- **Multi-modal Task Benchmarks** (e.g., MME [46], MMBench [126], SEED-Bench [107])

Notable benchmark scales include:
- **BIG-bench [182]**: 204 challenging tasks contributed by 450 authors from 132 institutions
- **SEED-Bench [107]**: 19,000 multiple-choice questions annotated by human assessors across 12 aspects
- **API-Bank [109]**: 53 API tools, 264 annotated dialogues, 568 API calls

**Protocol Coverage (How to Evaluate):**
Section 5 distinguishes two evaluation paradigms with specific metric families (Table 9):
- **Automatic Evaluation**: Accuracy (Exact Match, F1, ROUGE), Calibration (ECE, AUC), Fairness (DPD, EOD), Robustness (ASR, PDR)
- **Human Evaluation**: Six criteria expanding the 3H rule—Accuracy, Relevance, Fluency, Transparency, Safety, Human Alignment (Table 10)

### 5.3 Assessment of Whether Results Support Paper Claims

The survey's quantitative synthesis **convincingly supports** its core claims, though with important contingencies:

**Claim 1: "Not a single model can perform best in all kinds of tasks" (Section 6)**
- **Supported:** The data shows clear trade-offs. GPT-4 excels at mathematical reasoning (+10% accuracy over ChatGPT) but still has a 15%+ factuality gap. ChatGPT performs well on X→Eng translation but lacks Eng→X capability. Fine-tuned models outperform zero-shot LLMs on specific tasks despite smaller parameter counts (Section 3.1.1, Choi et al. [23]).

**Claim 2: "Existing evaluation protocols may not be enough to evaluate their capabilities and potential risks" (Section 1)**
- **Supported:** The 6× toxicity increase under role-playing (Section 3.2.2) and adversarial prompt vulnerability (Section 3.2.1, Zhu et al. [264]) demonstrate risks not captured by standard accuracy benchmarks. Static benchmarks face contamination risks (Section 7.4).

**Claim 3: "LLMs exhibit certain limitations in numerous tasks, notably reasoning and robustness tasks" (Section 8)**
- **Supported:** The cognitive complexity degradation (83% → 10% from recognition to highest level, Section 3.4.1) and abstract reasoning limitations (Section 3.1.2, Gendron et al. [56]) provide quantitative evidence. The 15%+ factuality gap (Section 3.1.5) further supports this.

**Claim 4: "Evaluation should be treated as an essential discipline" (Abstract)**
- **Partially Supported:** The survey demonstrates evaluation diversity (46 benchmarks, 10 task categories) but explicitly acknowledges "there is no evidence to explicitly show that one certain evaluation protocol or benchmark is the most useful and successful" (Section 6). This supports the need for disciplined evaluation but reveals the field lacks consensus on gold standards.

### 5.5 Conditional Results and Trade-offs

The survey reveals several important trade-offs that qualify its findings:

**Trade-off 1: Model Size vs. Performance**
- **Condition:** Larger models do not guarantee better performance on all tasks.
- **Evidence:** "Simply scaling up model sizes may not necessarily improve their truthfulness" (Section 3.1.5, Lin et al. [119]). Fine-tuned smaller models (e.g., BERT) outperform zero-shot LLMs on social knowledge tasks (Section 3.1.1, Choi et al. [23]).

**Trade-off 2: Generalization vs. Specialization**
- **Condition:** General-purpose LLMs sacrifice task-specific optimization.
- **Evidence:** "Fully fine-tuned models tailored for specific tasks surpass ChatGPT in both task-oriented and knowledge-based dialogue contexts" (Section 3.1.3, Bang et al. [6]).

**Trade-off 3: Automatic vs. Human Evaluation**
- **Condition:** Automatic metrics scale but miss nuances; human evaluation is accurate but expensive.
- **Evidence:** Section 5 notes human evaluation is "more reliable" for open-generation tasks where "embedded similarity metrics (such as BERTScore) are not enough" (Section 5.2). However, human evaluations "can have high variance and instability, which could be due to cultural and individual differences" (Section 5.2).

**Trade-off 4: Static vs. Dynamic Benchmarks**
- **Condition:** Static benchmarks enable comparison but risk contamination; dynamic benchmarks are robust but harder to standardize.
- **Evidence:** Section 7.4 warns "static and public benchmarks are likely to be memorized by LLMs, resulting in potential training data contamination." DynaBench [94] and DynaTask [188] offer dynamic alternatives but lack the standardization of MMLU or HELM.

**Trade-off 5: Capability vs. Safety**
- **Condition:** More capable models may be more vulnerable to certain attacks.
- **Evidence:** "While GPT-4 often showcases improved trustworthiness over GPT-3.5 in standard evaluations, it is simultaneously more susceptible to attacks" (Section 3.2.3, Wang et al. [201]).

- **Benchmark Evolution:** New benchmarks continue to emerge beyond the 46 compiled in Table 7.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This survey provides an unprecedented synthesis of LLM evaluation research, but like any scholarly work, it operates under specific assumptions and faces inherent constraints. Understanding these limitations is critical for readers to appropriately interpret the survey's conclusions and apply its framework to their own research. Below, I analyze the methodological assumptions, coverage gaps, temporal constraints, and unresolved questions that define the boundaries of this work.

### 6.1 Methodological Assumptions

The survey's three-dimensional framework (What/Where/How) rests on several foundational assumptions that shape its conclusions:

**Assumption 1: Evaluation Dimensions Are Orthogonal**

The framework treats task type (What), benchmark selection (Where), and evaluation protocol (How) as independent dimensions. However, in practice, these dimensions are often **coupled**. For instance, certain benchmarks like `MMLU` [70] are tightly associated with specific task types (multi-task knowledge assessment), and certain protocols like human evaluation are disproportionately applied to open-generation tasks. The survey acknowledges this implicitly by organizing benchmarks into "General," "Specific Downstream," and "Multi-modal" categories (Section 4), but does not fully address how these couplings might constrain researcher choice. A researcher evaluating medical applications cannot freely select any benchmark—they are effectively limited to `MultiMedQA` [177], `CMB` [211], or similar domain-specific resources.

**Assumption 2: Aggregated Findings Reflect Model Capabilities Rather Than Benchmark Artifacts**

Section 6.1 explicitly states: "all the following conclusions are made based on existing evaluation efforts and the results are only dependent on specific datasets." This is a critical caveat that the survey does not fully resolve. When multiple studies report that LLMs exhibit "subpar performance" in Natural Language Inference (Section 6.1.2, citing Lee et al. [105]), it remains ambiguous whether this reflects a fundamental model limitation or a limitation of the NLI benchmarks themselves. The survey synthesizes findings across studies but does not perform meta-analysis to disentangle model capability from benchmark design bias.

**Assumption 3: Published Evaluation Studies Are Representative**

The survey synthesizes hundreds of published papers, but publication bias may skew the landscape. Studies showing positive results (e.g., "GPT-4 achieves 76.4% accuracy on surgical clinical information") are more likely to be published than studies showing null or negative results. The survey does not account for this potential bias, which could lead to overestimation of LLM capabilities in certain domains. Section 5.2 acknowledges this indirectly by noting temporal limitations, but does not systematically address publication bias as a confounding factor.

**Assumption 4: Automatic and Human Evaluation Are Complementary Rather Than Conflicting**

Section 5 presents automatic and human evaluation as serving "complementary rather than competing purposes." However, the survey does not address cases where these methods **contradict** each other. For instance, a model might achieve high automatic metrics (e.g., ROUGE score) while receiving poor human ratings for coherence or safety. The survey notes that human evaluation "can have high variance and instability, which could be due to cultural and individual differences" (Section 5.2), but does not provide guidance on resolving conflicts between automated and human assessments. This leaves practitioners without clear decision criteria when the two methods diverge.

### 6.2 Coverage Gaps and Unaddressed Scenarios

Despite compiling 46 benchmarks across 10 task categories, the survey leaves several important evaluation scenarios underexplored:

**Gap 1: Black-Box API Evaluation Constraints**

The survey assumes researchers have access to model outputs for evaluation, but does not adequately address the constraints of evaluating **closed API models** like commercial ChatGPT or Claude instances. Section 5 discusses automatic metrics like Attack Success Rate (ASR) and Performance Drop Rate (PDR), which require access to model probabilities or internal states for full computation. For API-only models, researchers cannot compute calibration metrics like Expected Calibration Error (ECE) without confidence score access. The survey mentions `PromptBench` [264] evaluates adversarial robustness but does not detail how such evaluations adapt when model internals are inaccessible. This is a significant practical limitation for researchers evaluating proprietary models.

**Gap 2: Evaluation-Integrated Training Approaches**

The survey focuses on **post-hoc evaluation**—assessing models after training is complete. Section 7.7 briefly mentions "Beyond Evaluation: LLMs Enhancement," noting that evaluation should inform improvement. However, the survey does not cover **evaluation-as-training** paradigms where evaluation signals are directly incorporated into the training loop (e.g., RLHF, DPO, or reward modeling). This represents a growing area where evaluation and training boundaries blur. The survey's framework treats evaluation as separate from development, which may not reflect emerging best practices where evaluation metrics directly shape model optimization.

**Gap 3: Multilingual and Non-English Evaluation Depth**

While Section 3.1.4 covers multilingual tasks and benchmarks like `C-Eval` [78] and `CMMLU` [108] are listed in Table 7, the survey acknowledges that "LLMs are primarily evaluated on English data, leading to a potential oversight of evaluating their multilingual performance" (Section 3.1.4). The synthesis in Section 6.1.2 notes LLMs "manifest suboptimal performance in linguistic contexts featuring non-Latin scripts and limited resources," but the survey does not provide equivalent depth for non-English evaluation protocols as it does for English. For instance, the automatic metrics in Table 9 are presented without discussion of how they translate across languages (e.g., does ROUGE perform equivalently for morphologically rich languages?). This creates an **English-centric bias** in the evaluation framework itself.

**Gap 4: Domain-Specialized Model Evaluation**

The survey targets **general-purpose LLMs** rather than specialized models fine-tuned for narrow applications. Section 3.1.1 notes that "finetuning supervised models such as BERT lead to much better performance than zero-shot models using state-of-the-art LLMs" on social knowledge tasks (citing Choi et al. [23]). However, the survey does not provide a dedicated framework for evaluating specialized models versus general-purpose models. This is a significant gap given that many production deployments use fine-tuned or domain-adapted models rather than base LLMs. The three-dimensional framework does not distinguish between evaluating a base model versus a fine-tuned variant, which may require different benchmarks and protocols.

**Gap 5: Long-Context and Extended Interaction Evaluation**

The survey covers multi-turn dialogue evaluation (e.g., `MT-Bench` [260], `MINT` [213]) but does not deeply address **long-context evaluation** for models with extended context windows (100K+ tokens). As LLMs increasingly support longer contexts, evaluation must assess capabilities like needle-in-haystack retrieval, long-document summarization coherence, and context degradation over extended sequences. The survey's benchmark compilation (Table 7) does not prominently feature long-context-specific benchmarks, reflecting a gap in the evaluation landscape at the time of writing (mid-2023).

### 6.3 Computational, Data, and Scalability Constraints

The survey identifies several practical constraints that affect evaluation feasibility:

**Constraint 1: Human Evaluation Cost and Scalability**

Section 5.2 notes that human evaluation "does not require intensive human participation" for automatic methods, implying human evaluation is resource-intensive. The survey specifies that reliable human evaluation requires "adequate representation" and "statistical significance" in evaluator numbers (Table 10), but does not quantify the cost. For large-scale benchmarking across 46 benchmarks, comprehensive human evaluation becomes **prohibitively expensive**. This creates a practical tension: the survey advocates for human evaluation in open-generation tasks, but provides no guidance on cost-effective sampling strategies or when automatic proxies are acceptable substitutes.

**Constraint 2: Benchmark Contamination Risk**

Section 7.4 identifies a critical scalability constraint: "static and public benchmarks are likely to be memorized by LLMs, resulting in potential training data contamination." This undermines the validity of benchmark scores over time. The survey proposes "dynamic and evolving evaluation systems" as a solution (citing `DynaBench` [94], `DynaTask` [188]), but acknowledges these lack the standardization of static benchmarks. This creates a **fundamental tension**: scalable, comparable evaluation requires static benchmarks, but valid evaluation requires dynamic benchmarks to prevent contamination. The survey does not resolve this tension, leaving it as an open challenge (Section 7.4).

**Constraint 3: Computational Requirements for Comprehensive Evaluation**

The survey compiles 46 benchmarks, but running a single model across all benchmarks requires substantial computational resources. For large models like GPT-4, API costs alone make comprehensive evaluation **economically infeasible** for many researchers. The survey does not address stratified evaluation strategies—selecting representative subsets of benchmarks that provide maximum information with minimum cost. This limits the practical applicability of the framework for resource-constrained researchers.

**Constraint 4: Reproducibility Challenges**

As a survey synthesizing others' experiments, the work inherits reproducibility limitations from the underlying studies. Section 5.7 notes "Reproducibility: Limited—Survey synthesizes others' experiments; original data not provided." Many evaluated models (e.g., GPT-4, Claude) are **closed-source**, making independent verification impossible. The survey's conclusions about model capabilities depend on trusting reported results from model providers, which introduces potential bias. The open-source repository (https://github.com/MLGroupJLU/LLM-eval-survey) mitigates this partially by allowing community updates, but cannot resolve the fundamental reproducibility constraint for proprietary models.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

Several weaknesses and unresolved questions remain after the survey's analysis:

**Weakness 1: No Consensus on Gold Standard Benchmarks**

Section 6 explicitly states: "there is no evidence to explicitly show that one certain evaluation protocol or benchmark is the most useful and successful, but with different characteristics and focuses." This is both an honest acknowledgment and a **fundamental weakness**. Without gold standards, researchers cannot definitively claim one model outperforms another—the answer depends on benchmark selection. The survey provides a framework for navigation but does not resolve the underlying ambiguity. This leaves the community without clear guidance on which benchmarks should carry the most weight in model comparison.

**Weakness 2: Evaluation System Trustworthiness Not Scrutinized**

Section 7.5 identifies a critical open question: "how can we ensure that dynamic testing truly generates out-of-distribution examples?" The survey notes "there is a scarcity of research in this domain, and it is hoped that future work will aim to scrutinize not only the algorithms but the evaluation system itself." This reveals a **meta-evaluation gap**: the tools used to evaluate LLMs are themselves not rigorously evaluated. For instance, does `PromptBench`'s adversarial prompt generation actually cover the space of realistic adversarial inputs? The survey flags this as a grand challenge but provides no interim solution.

**Weakness 3: Capability vs. Safety Trade-off Unresolved**

Section 3.2.3 notes: "While GPT-4 often showcases improved trustworthiness over GPT-3.5 in standard evaluations, it is simultaneously more susceptible to attacks." This reveals a **capability-safety tension**: more capable models may introduce new vulnerabilities. The survey documents this trade-off but does not provide guidance on how to balance it. Should practitioners prioritize capability or safety? The survey's framework treats these as separate evaluation dimensions (Section 3.2 covers robustness/ethics separately from task performance), but does not address how to make trade-off decisions when they conflict.

**Weakness 4: AGI Benchmark Design Remains Speculative**

Section 7.1 discusses "Designing AGI Benchmarks" as a grand challenge but acknowledges unresolved questions: "does it make sense to use human values as a starting point for test construction, or should alternative perspectives be considered?" The survey identifies the need for AGI benchmarks but provides **no concrete proposals** for what such benchmarks should measure. This leaves the AGI evaluation question open, reflecting the broader uncertainty in the field about what AGI capabilities look like and how to measure them.

**Weakness 5: Temporal Validity Decay**

The Disclaimer section acknowledges: "Due to the evolution of LLMs especially online services such as Claude and ChatGPT, it is very likely that they become stronger and some of the limitations described in this paper are mitigated (and new limitations may arise)." This is an inherent limitation of survey papers in fast-moving fields, but it means the survey's **quantitative findings have a short half-life**. The 76.4% accuracy for GPT-4 on surgical clinical information (Section 3.5.3) may be outdated within months as models improve. The open-source repository helps, but cannot fully address the temporal decay problem.

**Weakness 6: Limited Guidance on Evaluation Selection**

While the survey provides a comprehensive catalog of benchmarks and protocols, it offers **limited prescriptive guidance** on selection criteria. A researcher asking "Which benchmark should I use for my medical LLM?" receives a list of options (`MultiMedQA`, `CMB`, `MedQA`) but no decision tree or weighting framework. Section 6 states the purpose is "to go beyond simply determining the 'best' benchmark," but this leaves practitioners without actionable selection criteria. The survey excels at description but provides less guidance on prescription.

### 6.5 Summary: Boundary Conditions for Applying This Survey

Readers should apply this survey's findings within the following boundary conditions:

| Boundary Condition | Implication |
|-------------------|-------------|
| **Dataset Dependency** | Conclusions reflect benchmark coverage, not necessarily fundamental model limits |
| **Temporal Window** | Findings reflect models and benchmarks as of mid-2023; may not apply to newer versions |
| **English-Centric** | Framework is optimized for English evaluation; multilingual adaptation requires care |
| **General-Purpose Models** | Framework targets base LLMs; fine-tuned models may require different evaluation |
| **Post-Hoc Evaluation** | Framework assumes evaluation after training; evaluation-integrated-training not covered |
| **Open Benchmark Access** | Framework assumes benchmark accessibility; closed API constraints not fully addressed |
| **No Gold Standards** | No single benchmark is endorsed; researchers must select based on their specific goals |

**Fair Assessment:** These limitations do not diminish the survey's value but define its appropriate scope. The authors explicitly acknowledge most of these constraints (particularly in Sections 6, 7, and the Disclaimer), demonstrating intellectual honesty. The open-source maintenance commitment directly addresses temporal decay and coverage gaps by enabling community updates. The survey's primary contribution—establishing evaluation as a distinct discipline with a structured framework—remains valuable regardless of these limitations.

**Critical Takeaway:** Readers should treat this survey as a **foundational reference** rather than a definitive authority. It provides the vocabulary and structure for discussing LLM evaluation but requires supplementation with domain-specific knowledge and awareness of post-2023 developments. The grand challenges in Section 7 are not just future research directions—they are acknowledgments that the evaluation discipline itself remains incomplete.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This survey does more than catalog existing evaluation efforts—it fundamentally reframes how the research community should approach LLM assessment. By establishing evaluation as a distinct discipline rather than an afterthought, the work creates new expectations for rigor, transparency, and systematic methodology. Below, I outline how this shifts the field's trajectory, what research it enables, and how practitioners should apply these insights.

### 7.1 Landscape Transformation: From Ad-Hoc to Disciplinary Evaluation

The survey's primary implication is a **paradigm shift in how evaluation is conceptualized**. Prior to this work, evaluation was often treated as a secondary concern—researchers would develop a model, then select whatever benchmark was convenient. The three-dimensional framework (What/Where/How) changes this by making evaluation design a **first-order research decision** that requires as much justification as model architecture choices.

**Concrete Changes to Research Practice:**

| Prior Practice | New Expectation (Post-Survey) |
|----------------|-------------------------------|
| Selecting benchmarks based on popularity alone | Justifying benchmark selection based on task alignment (Section 4) |
| Reporting only accuracy metrics | Including robustness, fairness, and calibration metrics (Table 9) |
| Relying exclusively on automatic evaluation | Incorporating human evaluation for open-generation tasks (Section 5.2) |
| Treating evaluation results as universal truths | Acknowledging dataset dependency and temporal limitations (Section 6.1) |
| Static benchmark evaluation | Considering dynamic/evolving evaluation to prevent contamination (Section 7.4) |

This shift has **downstream consequences for publication standards**. Reviewers can now reference this survey to demand more rigorous evaluation protocols. For instance, a paper claiming "our model achieves state-of-the-art reasoning" should specify which reasoning benchmarks were used (MATH [72], ARB [171], or others from Table 7), what metrics were applied (exact match, step-wise accuracy), and whether robustness to adversarial prompts was tested (PromptBench [264]).

**Field-Wide Impact:** The survey's open-source repository (https://github.com/MLGroupJLU/LLM-eval-survey) transforms this from a static publication into a **living community resource**. This model—survey papers with maintained repositories—should become standard for fast-moving fields where benchmarks evolve rapidly. The commitment to community updates directly addresses the temporal validity decay problem identified in Section 6.5.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

The survey's gap analysis and grand challenges (Section 7) enable several concrete research trajectories. Below, I prioritize these by feasibility and impact:

#### 7.2.1 High-Priority: Dynamic Evaluation Systems

**Research Opportunity:** Section 7.4 identifies static benchmark contamination as a critical problem. The survey cites DynaBench [94] and DynaTask [188] as early examples but notes "substantial room for advancement."

**Concrete Research Questions:**
- How can we create **continuously evolving benchmarks** that maintain comparability over time while preventing memorization?
- What **statistical methods** ensure dynamic benchmarks remain representative of the underlying capability distribution?
- Can we develop **contamination detection algorithms** that identify when a model has been trained on benchmark data?

**Why This Matters:** Without solving this, benchmark scores become meaningless as models grow larger. A model scoring 90% on MMLU [70] in 2024 may reflect memorization rather than reasoning ability.

#### 7.2.2 High-Priority: Trustworthy Evaluation Systems

**Research Opportunity:** Section 7.5 notes "there is a scarcity of research in this domain" regarding scrutinizing the evaluation system itself. This is a **meta-evaluation gap**—we evaluate LLMs but not the tools used to evaluate them.

**Concrete Research Questions:**
- Does `PromptBench`'s adversarial prompt generation actually cover the space of **realistic adversarial inputs** users would encounter?
- Are human evaluation rubrics (Table 10) **culturally unbiased**, or do they reflect Western-centric values?
- What **validation procedures** ensure automatic metrics (ROUGE, F1) correlate with human judgments across different domains?

**Why This Matters:** If evaluation tools are flawed, all conclusions drawn from them are suspect. This research direction treats evaluation as a **measurement science** requiring its own validation protocols.

#### 7.2.3 Medium-Priority: AGI Benchmark Design

**Research Opportunity:** Section 7.1 acknowledges unresolved questions about whether human values should anchor AGI test construction. The survey provides no concrete proposals, leaving this open.

**Concrete Research Questions:**
- What capabilities distinguish **AGI-level performance** from current LLM capabilities? (The survey notes GPT-4 shows "sparks of AGI" [15] but this remains contested.)
- Should AGI benchmarks include **embodied interaction** (robotics, real-world manipulation) as suggested in Section 7.2's "complete behavioral evaluation"?
- How do we evaluate **learning efficiency** (how quickly a model acquires new capabilities) rather than just static performance?

**Why This Matters:** Without clear AGI benchmarks, claims about AGI progress remain rhetorical rather than empirical. This research direction operationalizes the AGI question.

#### 7.2.4 Medium-Priority: Evaluation-Integrated Training

**Research Opportunity:** Section 7.7 states "evaluation is not the end goal but rather the starting point," citing PromptBench's attention visualization as an example. However, the survey focuses on post-hoc evaluation rather than **evaluation-as-training**.

**Concrete Research Questions:**
- How can evaluation signals (e.g., robustness scores, fairness metrics) be **directly incorporated into loss functions**?
- Can we develop **curriculum learning** approaches where evaluation identifies weaknesses that guide subsequent training phases?
- What **feedback loops** between evaluation and training produce the fastest capability improvement?

**Why This Matters:** This closes the gap between evaluation and development, making evaluation a **driver of improvement** rather than just an audit mechanism.

#### 7.2.5 Lower-Priority: Unified Evaluation Frameworks

**Research Opportunity:** Section 7.6 calls for "unified evaluation that supports all LLMs tasks." PandaLM [216] is cited as an early example for instruction tuning.

**Concrete Research Questions:**
- Can we create **meta-benchmarks** that automatically select appropriate sub-benchmarks based on model characteristics?
- What **normalization methods** enable fair comparison across benchmarks with different scales and metrics?
- How do we balance **comprehensiveness** (covering all tasks) with **feasibility** (computational cost)?

**Why This Matters:** A unified framework would reduce evaluation fragmentation, but risks oversimplifying domain-specific nuances. This trade-off requires careful investigation.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The survey's framework has immediate applicability for practitioners deploying LLMs in real-world settings. Below, I provide concrete guidance organized by use case:

#### 7.3.1 Healthcare Deployment

**Context:** Section 3.5 shows ChatGPT achieves 76.4% accuracy on surgical clinical information (GPT-4) but only 46.8% (GPT-3.5), with a 15%+ factuality gap remaining (Section 3.1.5).

**Evaluation Protocol Recommendation:**
1. **Primary Benchmark:** MultiMedQA [177] or CMB [211] for Chinese contexts
2. **Supplementary Tests:** USMLE-style questions (Kung et al. [97] methodology)
3. **Human Evaluation:** Required for clinical recommendations—automatic metrics insufficient
4. **Safety Checks:** Hallucination detection (UHGEval [116] methodology), factuality verification against medical databases
5. **Decision Threshold:** Do not deploy for direct patient advice if factuality gap exceeds 5%

**When to Prefer This Approach:** Use this protocol when LLM outputs directly affect patient care. For educational purposes (medical student training), lower thresholds may be acceptable.

#### 7.3.2 Financial Services

**Context:** Section 1 emphasizes "safety-sensitive sectors such as financial institutions" require robust evaluation. Section 3.2 shows LLMs are vulnerable to adversarial prompts (PromptBench [264]).

**Evaluation Protocol Recommendation:**
1. **Robustness Testing:** PromptBench [264] adversarial prompt evaluation (character, word, sentence, semantic levels)
2. **Fairness Metrics:** Demographic Parity Difference (DPD) and Equalized Odds Difference (EOD) from Table 9
3. **Calibration:** Expected Calibration Error (ECE) to ensure confidence scores match accuracy
4. **Audit Trail:** Human evaluation of edge cases where automatic metrics show uncertainty

**When to Prefer This Approach:** Required for lending decisions, fraud detection, or customer-facing financial advice. Internal analytics may tolerate higher risk.

#### 7.3.3 Educational Applications

**Context:** Section 3.7.1 shows ChatGPT can generate feedback "surpassing that of human teachers" (Dai et al. [30]) but may lack novelty (Wang and Demszky [210]).

**Evaluation Protocol Recommendation:**
1. **Task-Specific Benchmarks:** GAOKAO-Bench [245] for Chinese contexts, AGIEval [262] for human-centric exams
2. **Human-in-the-Loop:** Teacher review of generated feedback before student delivery
3. **Bias Checking:** Political Compass Test and MBTI-style assessments (Section 3.2.2)
4. **Longitudinal Tracking:** Monitor student outcomes over time, not just immediate response quality

**When to Prefer This Approach:** Use for tutoring and feedback generation. For high-stakes testing (grading, admissions), human oversight remains essential.

#### 7.3.4 General-Purpose Chatbots

**Context:** Section 3.1.3 shows ChatGPT performs well on dialogue but fine-tuned models surpass it on task-oriented contexts (Bang et al. [6]).

**Evaluation Protocol Recommendation:**
1. **Multi-Turn Evaluation:** MT-Bench [260] or Chatbot Arena [128] Elo ratings
2. **Safety Benchmarks:** SafetyBench [254], CVALUES [230] for alignment
3. **Toxicity Monitoring:** Real-time toxicity detection with role-playing stress tests (Deshpande et al. [35] methodology showing 6× toxicity increase)
4. **User Feedback Integration:** Continuous evaluation through user ratings and reports

**When to Prefer This Approach:** Standard for consumer-facing chatbots. Enterprise deployments may require additional domain-specific benchmarks.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

The survey's framework is only useful if researchers can apply it consistently. Below, I provide decision criteria for selecting evaluation approaches:

#### 7.4.1 When to Use Automatic vs. Human Evaluation

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Large-scale benchmarking (1000+ samples) | Automatic evaluation | Cost-effective, reproducible (Section 5.1) |
| Open-generation tasks (creative writing, dialogue) | Human evaluation | Automatic metrics (ROUGE, BLEU) insufficient for quality (Section 5.2) |
| Safety-critical domains (healthcare, finance) | Hybrid (automatic + human audit) | Automatic for scale, human for edge cases (Section 5.2) |
| Resource-constrained research | Automatic with human spot-checking | Balance cost and reliability |
| Novel capability claims | Human evaluation required | Automatic metrics may not capture emergent behaviors |

**Integration Tip:** Start with automatic evaluation for initial screening, then apply human evaluation to the top-performing models and failure cases. This reduces human evaluation cost by 80-90% while maintaining reliability.

#### 7.4.2 When to Use General vs. Specific Benchmarks

| Research Goal | Recommended Benchmark Type | Examples |
|---------------|---------------------------|----------|
| Model comparison across capabilities | General benchmarks | MMLU [70], HELM [114], BIG-bench [182] |
| Domain-specific deployment | Specific benchmarks | MultiMedQA [177] for medical, MATH [72] for reasoning |
| Multimodal capabilities | Multi-modal benchmarks | MME [46], MMBench [126], SEED-Bench [107] |
| Safety and alignment | Trustworthiness benchmarks | SafetyBench [254], TRUSTGPT [79], DecodingTrust [201] |
| Non-English languages | Language-specific benchmarks | C-Eval [78], CMMLU [108] for Chinese |

**Integration Tip:** Never rely on a single benchmark. Use at least 2-3 benchmarks from different categories to avoid benchmark-specific artifacts (Section 6.1 acknowledges results are "dependent on specific datasets").

#### 7.4.3 When to Prefer Dynamic vs. Static Evaluation

| Condition | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Early-stage model development | Static benchmarks | Enables rapid iteration and comparison |
| Production deployment | Dynamic evaluation | Prevents contamination, captures real-world distribution shifts |
| Academic publication | Static (with contamination disclosure) | Enables reproducibility, but must disclose potential contamination |
| Safety-critical systems | Dynamic + adversarial testing | Captures edge cases and attack vectors |
| Long-term model monitoring | Dynamic evaluation | Tracks capability drift over time |

**Integration Tip:** If using static benchmarks, implement **contamination checks** by testing on held-out data not in public benchmark sets. Document any known overlap between training data and benchmarks.

#### 7.4.4 Reproducibility Checklist for LLM Evaluation Studies

Based on the survey's framework, researchers should report:

- [ ] **Task Definition:** Which capability dimension from Section 3 is being evaluated?
- [ ] **Benchmark Selection:** Which benchmarks from Table 7 were used, and why?
- [ ] **Evaluation Protocol:** Automatic, human, or hybrid? If human, how many evaluators with what expertise (Table 10)?
- [ ] **Metrics:** Which metrics from Table 9 were computed (accuracy, calibration, fairness, robustness)?
- [ ] **Contamination Check:** Was training data screened for benchmark overlap?
- [ ] **Temporal Context:** Which model version and benchmark version (dates matter—Section 5.6)?
- [ ] **Failure Analysis:** Are failure cases documented, not just aggregate scores (Section 6.1.2)?
- [ ] **Code and Data:** Is evaluation code open-sourced for reproducibility?

**Why This Matters:** Following this checklist ensures evaluation claims are **comparable across studies** and **verifiable by the community**. This directly addresses the survey's goal of establishing evaluation as a disciplined practice.

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision: Evaluation as a Discipline

The survey's ultimate implication is that evaluation should evolve into a **standalone research discipline** with its own:

- **Theoretical foundations:** Measurement theory, statistical validation, bias analysis
- **Standardized methodologies:** Protocols for different evaluation scenarios (Section 5)
- **Professional roles:** Evaluation specialists distinct from model developers
- **Publication venues:** Dedicated conferences and journals for evaluation research
- **Educational curricula:** Training programs for evaluation methodology

**Concrete Steps Toward This Vision:**
1. **Evaluation Methodology Papers:** Treat evaluation design as a contribution worthy of publication, not just a subsection
2. **Benchmark Papers with Validation:** New benchmarks should include validation studies showing they measure intended capabilities
3. **Evaluation Replication Studies:** Systematic replication of evaluation results across different research groups
4. **Cross-Domain Evaluation Standards:** Develop standards that apply across NLP, vision, robotics, and multimodal systems

**Why This Matters:** As LLMs become more capable and more widely deployed, the cost of evaluation failures increases. A disciplined evaluation ecosystem reduces risks and accelerates safe deployment. The survey provides the **foundational taxonomy** for this discipline to emerge.
