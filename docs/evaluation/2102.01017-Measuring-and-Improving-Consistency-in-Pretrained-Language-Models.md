## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the critical lack of factual consistency in Pretrained Language Models (PLMs) when queried with paraphrased inputs by introducing `PARAREL`, a high-quality benchmark containing 328 cloze-style paraphrases across 38 relations. Experiments reveal that standard models like BERT, RoBERTa, and ALBERT exhibit poor consistency (e.g., BERT-large achieves only 61.1%), indicating their internal representations are not robust enough for reliable knowledge base applications despite high accuracy. To mitigate this, the authors propose a novel consistency loss for continual pretraining that significantly improves performance on unseen relations, raising BERT-base consistency from 58.2% to 64.0% and demonstrating a viable path toward more stable language understanding systems.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this work, we must first establish the role Pretrained Language Models (PLMs) play in modern Natural Language Processing (NLP) and why "consistency" is a critical, yet often overlooked, property of these systems.

### The Promise and Peril of PLMs as Knowledge Bases
PLMs like `BERT` and `RoBERTa` operate under a **pretrain-finetune paradigm**. As described in Section 1, these models are first trained on massive text corpora to learn language structures, and then adapted to specific tasks. A growing body of research (e.g., Petroni et al., 2019) suggests that during pretraining, these models implicitly store factual knowledge, effectively functioning as **Knowledge Bases (KBs)**.

However, treating a neural network as a KB introduces specific expectations. A traditional KB is structured and logical; if you query it for a fact, it should return a consistent answer regardless of how you phrase the query. In contrast, PLMs are trained using a **Masked Language Modeling (MLM)** objective. This objective trains the model to predict missing words based on local context to minimize log-likelihood loss. Crucially, **nothing in this standard training process explicitly targets consistency**. The model is rewarded for predicting probable tokens, not for ensuring that semantically equivalent inputs yield semantically equivalent outputs.

This creates a fundamental gap: while we expect PLMs to "abstract away from language" and map queries with identical intent to the same answer, the training signal does not enforce this invariance.

### Defining Consistency in This Context
In this paper, **consistency** is defined specifically as the invariance of a model's behavior under meaning-preserving alterations (paraphrases) in its input. 
*   **Consistent Behavior:** If a model predicts "Showtime" for the query `"Homeland originally aired on [MASK]"`, it should also predict "Showtime" for `"Homeland premiered on [MASK]"`.
*   **Inconsistent Behavior:** If the model predicts "Showtime" for the first query but "ABC" for the second, it is inconsistent.

Figure 1 in the paper illustrates this dichotomy visually. The authors note that while consistency is vital for tasks like question answering, coreference resolution, and summarization, current models often fail to maintain it. This failure is not just a performance issue; it indicates deeper representational problems. As stated in Section 1, inconsistencies may point to issues such as:
*   Similarity between antonyms and synonyms in the representation space.
*   Overestimating events due to reporting bias in training data.
*   Poor structure in the model's internal representational spaces.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Before this work, several approaches attempted to analyze model knowledge and consistency, but each had specific limitations that this paper aims to address.

1.  **Single-Pattern Probing (e.g., LAMA):** 
    Early work like LAMA (Petroni et al., 2019) established that PLMs contain factual knowledge. However, LAMA typically uses a **single pattern** per relation (e.g., one way to ask "born in"). This setup measures *accuracy* (does the model know the fact?) but cannot measure *consistency* (does the model know the fact regardless of phrasing?). Without multiple paraphrases, inconsistency is impossible to detect.

2.  **Automatic Paraphrase Generation (e.g., LPAQA):** 
    Some resources, like LPAQA (Jiang et al., 2020), generate paraphrases automatically using techniques like back-translation. While scalable, Section 4 of this paper notes that automatically generated patterns are often noisy—"some LPAQA patterns are not correct paraphrases." If the evaluation data itself is semantically ambiguous, measuring model consistency becomes unreliable.

3.  **Downstream Task Consistency:** 
    Other research (e.g., Ribeiro et al., 2019; Asai and Hajishirzi, 2020) examines consistency in downstream tasks like Question Answering (QA) or Natural Language Inference (NLI). These studies often involve **finetuning** the model on specific datasets. While valuable, finetuning introduces biases from the task-specific data. This paper argues for **zero-shot evaluation** to inspect the consistency acquired *during pretraining* directly, without the confounding variable of task-specific fine-tuning.

4.  **Negation and Logical Consistency:** 
    Work by Ettinger (2020) and Kassner and Schütze (2020) explores consistency regarding negation (e.g., ensuring a model doesn't predict both "Birds can fly" and "Birds cannot fly"). While related, this paper broadens the scope to **factual knowledge across a wide range of relations** (38 relations vs. specific logical constructs) and investigates the structural reasons behind the inconsistencies.

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself at the intersection of **knowledge probing** and **model robustness**. It distinguishes itself from prior work through three key design choices:

*   **High-Quality Manual Curation:** Instead of relying on automatic paraphrases, the authors introduce `PARAREL`, a manually curated resource (Section 4). This ensures that when a model fails, it is due to model inconsistency, not ambiguous input data.
*   **Zero-Shot Probing:** By avoiding finetuning, the analysis isolates the properties inherent to the pretrained models themselves (Section 1). This allows for a fair comparison between different model architectures (`BERT`, `RoBERTa`, `ALBERT`) based on their pretraining alone.
*   **Focus on Masked Language Models (MLMs):** The study specifically targets MLMs rather than autoregressive models (like GPT) or text-to-text models, allowing for a controlled analysis of how the masking objective interacts with knowledge retrieval.

By addressing these gaps, the paper aims to not only measure the deficit in current models but also propose a method to improve consistency directly during the pretraining phase, moving beyond mere diagnosis to remediation.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
This paper builds a two-part system: a high-quality benchmark dataset called `PARAREL` to measure inconsistency, and a modified training algorithm to fix it. The solution addresses the problem where language models give different answers to the same question asked in different ways by first quantifying the error rate across paraphrases and then forcing the model's output distributions to match during continued training.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The technical workflow consists of three primary components connected in a linear pipeline.
*   **The `PARAREL` Resource:** This is the data engine, responsible for generating pairs of semantically equivalent queries (paraphrases) for specific factual relations.
*   **The Consistency Framework:** This is the evaluation engine, which takes the `PARAREL` queries, feeds them into a Pretrained Language Model (PLM), and compares the outputs to calculate consistency scores.
*   **The Consistency Loss Module:** This is the improvement engine, which modifies the model's training objective to penalize differences in output distributions between paraphrases, thereby updating the model weights to be more robust.

Information flows from the `PARAREL` resource into the Framework for measurement, and then the same resource is used by the Loss Module to update the model parameters via continual pretraining.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **First,** we will dissect the construction of the `PARAREL` resource, explaining how the authors ensured the paraphrases were high-quality enough to trust the evaluation results.
*   **Second,** we will walk through the evaluation framework, detailing how raw model predictions are converted into consistency metrics and why restricted candidate sets are necessary.
*   **Third,** we will analyze the improvement method, specifically deriving the consistency loss function and explaining why standard classification loss is insufficient for this task.
*   **Finally,** we will outline the experimental configuration, including hyperparameters and data splits, to show how the method was validated on unseen relations.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown
This work is fundamentally a measurement and intervention study that combines dataset creation with algorithmic modification of the training objective. The following breakdown explains the mechanics of the `PARAREL` resource, the evaluation framework, and the consistency loss function.

#### The `PARAREL` Resource Construction
The foundation of this approach is the `PARAREL` dataset, which serves as the probe for model behavior. Unlike prior datasets that relied on single templates or automatically generated paraphrases, `PARAREL` is manually curated to ensure semantic equivalence. The construction process followed four distinct steps to guarantee quality, as detailed in Section 4.
*   **Base Patterns:** The authors started with patterns from LAMA (Petroni et al., 2019), selecting one "base-pattern" per relation (e.g., `"X was born in Y"`).
*   **Augmentation:** They augmented these base patterns with paraphrases from LPAQA (Jiang et al., 2020), but critically filtered them to include only correct paraphrases, rejecting those generated via noisy back-translation.
*   **Extraction:** Using SPIKE, a syntax-based search engine over Wikipedia, the authors searched for sentences containing subject-object tuples from the T-REx knowledge base and manually extracted new patterns from these sentences.
*   **Expert Review:** Finally, linguistic experts added additional paraphrases and reviewed all patterns, engaging in discussion until agreement was reached on every item.

The resulting resource contains **38 relations** and a total of **328 paraphrases**, averaging **8.63 patterns per relation** (Table 1). To validate the quality, a human annotation study was conducted where graduate students compared new patterns against the base pattern. The agreement scores were **95.5% for paraphrases** and **98.3% for controls**, indicating that the dataset reliably represents semantically equivalent queries. This high quality is essential because if the paraphrases were not truly equivalent, model disagreement would be justified rather than inconsistent.

#### The Consistency Evaluation Framework
Once the data is prepared, the authors employ a specific framework to probe the models, illustrated in Figure 2. The goal is to test whether a model predicts the same object for a given subject across all paraphrases of a relation.

**Input Formatting and Cloze-Style Queries**
The framework uses a cloze-style format where the object is replaced by a mask token. For a relation $r_i$ (e.g., `born-in`), a subject $s$ (e.g., `Marie Curie`), and a pattern $p$ (e.g., `"X was born in Y"`), the input becomes `"Marie Curie was born in [MASK]"`. The model must predict the token that fills the `[MASK]`. This format aligns with the Masked Language Modeling (MLM) objective used to train models like `BERT`.

**Restricted Candidate Sets**
A critical design choice in this framework is the use of restricted candidate sets, described in Section 3.2. PLMs are trained to predict any word in their vocabulary, but many valid words are not valid knowledge base entities (e.g., predicting "tv" instead of "Showtime"). To isolate knowledge consistency from general language modeling capability, the authors restrict the model's output vocabulary to only the set of possible gold objects for that specific relation from the underlying knowledge base.
*   **Mechanism:** For each relation, the vocabulary is filtered to keep only objects known to be valid answers (e.g., for `born-in`, only city names like `Paris`, `London`).
*   **Rationale:** This makes the task easier for the model, meaning that poor consistency in this setup strongly implies even worse consistency in an open-vocabulary setting. It ensures that errors are due to inconsistency, not vocabulary mismatch.

**Evaluation Metrics**
The paper defines three distinct metrics to capture different facets of performance, as outlined in Section 5.2.
*   **Accuracy:** This measures the standard `acc@1`, or whether the top-ranked prediction matches the gold object using the original LAMA patterns. This establishes a baseline for how much knowledge the model possesses.
*   **Consistency:** This is the core metric, defined as the percentage of consistent predictions across all pattern pairs for a relation. For a relation with $n$ patterns, the model is tested on all $n(n-1)/2$ pairs. If the model predicts "Paris" for pattern A and "Paris" for pattern B, it is consistent; if it predicts "Paris" for A and "London" for B, it is inconsistent.
*   **Consistent-Acc:** This is a stricter metric that combines the previous two. An object is counted as correct only if the model predicts it correctly **and** consistently across all patterns for that relation. This captures the intersection of knowing the fact and being robust to phrasing.

#### The Consistency Improvement Method
After establishing that models are inconsistent, the authors propose a method to improve consistency via continual pretraining, detailed in Section 8. The core idea is to introduce a new loss function that explicitly penalizes divergence between the output distributions of paraphrases.

**The Consistency Loss Function**
Standard training minimizes the difference between the prediction and the ground truth label. However, consistency requires minimizing the difference between two predictions for the same fact. A naive approach would be to force the `argmax` (the top prediction) to be identical. However, the `argmax` operation is discrete and non-differentiable, making it impossible to optimize using gradient descent.

To solve this, the authors relax the objective. Instead of forcing the top tokens to match, they force the entire probability distributions over the candidate set to match. They use the **Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence**, a measure of how one probability distribution differs from a second, expected probability distribution. The consistency loss $L_c$ for a relation $r_i$ with $k$ patterns is defined as:

$$
L_c = \sum_{n=1}^{k} \sum_{m=n+1}^{k} D_{KL}(Q_{n}^{r_i} || Q_{m}^{r_i}) + D_{KL}(Q_{m}^{r_i} || Q_{n}^{r_i})
$$

Where:
*   $Q_{n}^{r_i}$ is the predicted probability distribution (softmax output) for pattern $n$ of relation $r_i$.
*   $D_{KL}$ is the KL Divergence function.
*   The sum iterates over all unique pairs of patterns $(n, m)$ for the relation.

This is a two-sided KL divergence (also known as Jensen-Shannon divergence components), ensuring that the distributions converge toward each other regardless of which one is treated as the reference.

**Combining with MLM Loss**
To prevent the model from forgetting its general language capabilities (catastrophic forgetting), the consistency loss is combined with the standard Masked Language Modeling loss ($L_{MLM}$). The final loss function $L$ is a weighted sum:

$$
L = \lambda L_c + L_{MLM}
$$

Here, $\lambda$ is a hyperparameter that controls the strength of the consistency penalty. The authors experiment with $\lambda \in \{0.1, 0.5, 1\}$ to find the optimal balance.

**Training Configuration**
The improvement method is validated using a specific training setup designed to test generalization, as described in Section 8.2.
*   **Data Split:** The 38 relations are split into training, validation, and test sets. The model is trained on only **3 relations** (`original-language`, `named-after`, `original-network`) chosen randomly from non-location relations to prevent leakage.
*   **Testing:** The model is evaluated on **25 unseen relations** to verify that the consistency improvement generalizes beyond the specific facts seen during training.
*   **Batching:** To efficiently compute the consistency loss, the authors batch together $l=8$ tuples from the same relation. This allows the loss to be computed across multiple examples of the same relation simultaneously.
*   **Duration:** The model is trained for **3 epochs**, and the best model is selected based on the `Consistent-Acc` metric on the validation set.

**Ablation and Design Choices**
The authors perform ablation studies to justify each component of their approach (Table 7).
*   **Without Consistency Loss:** Finetuning without $L_c$ improves accuracy slightly but fails to improve consistency significantly, proving the loss is necessary for robustness.
*   **Without Typing:** Applying the loss over the entire vocabulary instead of the restricted candidate set reduces performance, confirming that focusing on relevant entities helps maintain original capabilities.
*   **Without MLM:** Removing the $L_{MLM}$ term increases consistency scores artificially (up to 80.8%) but devastates Accuracy (down to 16.9%). This demonstrates that the standard MLM loss acts as a regularizer, preventing the model from collapsing into a state where it is consistent but wrong.

This technical approach ensures that the model learns to be invariant to paraphrasing without sacrificing its factual knowledge or general language understanding. By separating the measurement (`PARAREL`) from the intervention (Consistency Loss), the paper provides both a diagnostic tool and a remediation strategy for PLM inconsistency.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the most significant conceptual advances and empirical discoveries from the paper. While the previous sections detailed *how* the authors constructed their benchmark and loss functions, here we focus on *what* these tools revealed about the nature of Pretrained Language Models (PLMs) and why these findings shift our understanding of model reliability. We distinguish between incremental improvements (e.g., slightly better scores) and fundamental innovations (e.g., new ways of interpreting model representations).

### 4.1 The Decoupling of Knowledge and Consistency
One of the most critical insights from this work is the empirical demonstration that **factual accuracy does not imply consistency**. Prior research, such as LAMA (Petroni et al., 2019), primarily focused on whether a model *could* predict a correct fact (Accuracy). This paper reveals that knowing a fact in one phrasing does not guarantee knowing it in another.

*   **The Performance Gap:** Table 3 illustrates this disconnect clearly. For `BERT-large`, the **Accuracy** is $48.1\%$, suggesting the model knows nearly half the facts. However, the **Consistent-Acc** (requiring both correctness and consistency) drops to $29.5\%$. This $\approx 19$ percentage point gap indicates that a significant portion of the model's "knowledge" is brittle—it relies on specific surface forms rather than robust semantic understanding.
*   **Significance:** This finding challenges the assumption that high accuracy on a benchmark implies reliable knowledge storage. For applications treating PLMs as Knowledge Bases (KBs), this is a fundamental limitation. A KB that returns "Showtime" for one query and "ABC" for a paraphrased version of the same query is functionally unreliable, regardless of how often it gets "Showtime" right. This insight necessitates a shift in evaluation standards: accuracy metrics alone are insufficient for assessing knowledge robustness.

### 4.2 Representational Clustering by Surface Form
Beyond behavioral metrics, the paper provides a structural explanation for *why* consistency fails. Through representation analysis (Section 7.2), the authors uncover that PLMs organize their internal spaces based on **syntax patterns** rather than **semantic entities**.

*   **Visual Evidence:** Figure 3 presents a t-SNE visualization of encoded patterns for the `capital` relation. Ideally, representations should cluster by **subject** (e.g., all queries about "Paris" should group together), indicating the model focuses on the entity. Instead, the visualization shows clustering by **pattern shape** (e.g., all queries using the phrase "capital of" group together, regardless of the city).
*   **Mechanism of Failure:** This suggests that the model's prediction is heavily influenced by the specific wording of the prompt (the pattern) rather than the underlying fact. When the pattern changes (e.g., from "capital of" to "capital city"), the representation shifts significantly enough to alter the output distribution.
*   **Innovation:** This is a novel diagnostic insight. Prior work often attributed errors to lack of knowledge or attention mechanisms. This analysis points to the **geometry of the representation space** itself as the culprit. It implies that improving consistency requires reshaping this space to be invariant to syntactic transformations, not just feeding the model more data.

### 4.3 Training Data Composition Over Model Scale
A counter-intuitive finding in this study challenges the prevailing "bigger is better" narrative in NLP. The results indicate that **training data composition** is more critical for consistency than model size or total training volume.

*   **The BERT Superiority:** As shown in Table 3, `BERT-large` achieves a consistency score of $61.1\%$, outperforming `RoBERTa-large` ($56.3\%$) and `ALBERT-xxlarge` ($52.1\%$). This is notable because RoBERTa and ALBERT are generally considered superior architectures trained on more data.
*   **The Wikipedia Factor:** The authors hypothesize (Section 6.2) that this difference stems from data sources. `BERT` was trained primarily on **Wikipedia**, a unified, fact-dense corpus. `RoBERTa` and `ALBERT` include large amounts of CommonCrawl data, which is noisier and may contain contradictory facts.
*   **Supporting Evidence:** Table 4 supports this by showing consistency trends across RoBERTa models trained on varying data sizes. While accuracy generally improves with more data, consistency does not scale linearly; the model trained on 1 million tokens was sometimes more consistent than those trained on 100 million.
*   **Significance:** This insight suggests that for knowledge-intensive tasks, **data curation** (ensuring factual consistency in the pretraining corpus) may be more impactful than architectural scaling. It warns against assuming that larger models automatically inherit better logical properties.

### 4.4 Consistency as a Transferable Skill
Perhaps the most significant methodological innovation is the demonstration that consistency can be learned as a **generalizable skill** rather than a fact-specific memorization task. The proposed consistency loss (detailed in Section 3) does not just fix errors on seen data; it transfers to unseen relations.

*   **Generalization Setup:** The authors trained their improved model on only **3 relations** (e.g., `original-language`) but evaluated it on **25 unseen relations** (Section 8.2). This strict split ensures the model cannot simply memorize the answers for the test set.
*   **Performance Gain:** Table 7 shows that `BERT-ft` (the improved model) achieves $64.0\%$ consistency on unseen relations, compared to $58.2\%$ for the baseline `BERT-base`. While a $5.8$ point gain might seem modest, it is statistically significant ($p \ll 0.01$) and achieved with minimal supervision (only 3 training relations).
*   **The Regularization Effect:** The ablation study in Table 7 reveals a subtle but crucial detail: removing the standard MLM loss (`-MLM`) spikes consistency to $80.8\%$ but crashes Accuracy to $16.9\%$. This indicates that without the standard language modeling objective, the model learns to be consistent by collapsing its output distribution (always predicting the same thing), sacrificing correctness.
*   **Innovation:** This confirms that **consistency is a structural property** of the model that can be optimized independently of specific facts. The innovation lies in the loss function design (Section 3.4) which successfully decouples the ability to be consistent from the specific knowledge being retrieved. This opens a new avenue for research: optimizing models for behavioral properties (like consistency or fairness) via auxiliary losses during pretraining, rather than relying solely on downstream finetuning.

### 4.5 Limitations and Open Questions
While the paper offers profound insights, it also highlights boundaries in our current understanding that warrant critical attention.

*   **Downstream Transfer Gap:** Despite improving consistency in the zero-shot probing setting, the authors note in Section 8.3 that these gains **did not translate** to downstream tasks like SQuAD when evaluated on paraphrased questions. This suggests that consistency in knowledge probing does not automatically guarantee robustness in complex reasoning tasks. The gap between "knowing a fact consistently" and "reasoning consistently" remains an open problem.
*   **Representation-Behavior Disconnect:** In Section 7.2, the authors report that the correlation between representation clustering (V-measure) and consistency scores is close to zero. This implies that while we can *observe* pattern-based clustering (Figure 3), we cannot yet fully *predict* model behavior solely from these global representation metrics. The specific neurons or mechanisms driving consistency remain opaque.
*   **Scope of Consistency:** The paper defines consistency strictly as invariance to paraphrasing. As noted in Section 9, this excludes other forms of logical consistency, such as negation (e.g., ensuring "Birds can fly" and "Birds cannot fly" yield different answers). The insights here are specific to **paraphrastic consistency** and may not generalize to logical entailment or contradiction detection without further adaptation.

These insights collectively argue that while PLMs are powerful knowledge stores, their reliability is compromised by surface-form sensitivity. The paper's true innovation is not just measuring this flaw, but demonstrating that it can be mitigated through objective design, paving the way for more robust pretraining strategies.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the experimental methodology and results. We walk through how the authors validated their claims about PLM inconsistency, analyze the quantitative evidence across multiple model architectures, and critically assess whether the experimental design supports the paper's conclusions.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Models and Baselines
The authors evaluate **seven pretrained model variants** across three architecture families, plus a controlled baseline (Section 5.1):

| Model Family | Variants Tested |
|--------------|-----------------|
| BERT | base, large, large-whole-word-masking |
| RoBERTa | base, large |
| ALBERT | base, xxlarge |
| Baseline | Majority (always predicts most common object) |

This selection is deliberate: it allows comparison across model scales (base vs. large), architectural variants (whole-word-masking), and different pretraining strategies (BERT's Wikipedia-focused data vs. RoBERTa/ALBERT's broader corpora).

#### Data Configuration
The evaluation uses **T-REx knowledge graph tuples** with specific filtering criteria to ensure fair comparison:

*   **Total tuples:** 26,813 (after removing objects that are not single tokens across all model vocabularies)
*   **Relations:** 38 total (31 N-1 relations for consistency, 7 N-M relations for determinism)
*   **Patterns per relation:** Average of 8.63 (minimum 2, maximum 20) — see Table 1

The restriction to single-token objects is a critical design choice. Multi-token predictions introduce additional complexity (how to aggregate token probabilities?), so this constraint isolates consistency from tokenization artifacts.

#### Metrics Defined
Section 5.2 establishes three primary metrics that capture different aspects of model behavior:

1.  **Accuracy:** Standard `acc@1` — whether the top prediction matches the gold object using original LAMA patterns. This measures *knowledge possession*.
2.  **Consistency:** Percentage of pattern pairs where the model predicts the same object. For a relation with $n$ patterns, this evaluates $n(n-1)/2$ pairs per tuple. This measures *robustness to phrasing*.
3.  **Consistent-Acc:** The intersection of the above — an object counts as correct only if predicted correctly **and** consistently across all patterns. This is the strictest metric.

Additionally, the authors report auxiliary metrics in Table 2:
*   **Succ-Patt:** Percentage of patterns that successfully predicted the right object at least once (tests pattern quality)
*   **Succ-Objs:** Percentage of entities predicted correctly by at least one pattern (tests knowledge coverage)
*   **Unk-Const:** Consistency for tuples where **no** pattern predicted the correct answer
*   **Know-Const:** Consistency for tuples where **at least one** pattern predicted the correct answer

This split is insightful: it tests whether models are more consistent when they "know" the answer versus when they don't.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

#### Overall Consistency is Poor
Table 3 presents the core findings. The numbers reveal a significant gap between knowledge and consistency:

| Model | Accuracy | Consistency | Consistent-Acc |
|-------|----------|-------------|----------------|
| Majority Baseline | 23.1% | 100.0% | 23.1% |
| BERT-base | 45.8% | 58.5% | 27.0% |
| BERT-large | 48.1% | 61.1% | 29.5% |
| BERT-large-wwm | 48.7% | 60.9% | 29.3% |
| RoBERTa-base | 39.0% | 52.1% | 16.4% |
| RoBERTa-large | 43.2% | 56.3% | 22.5% |
| ALBERT-base | 29.8% | 49.8% | 16.7% |
| ALBERT-xxlarge | 41.7% | 52.1% | 23.8% |

**Key observations:**

*   **Best consistency:** BERT-large achieves 61.1%, meaning it gives contradictory answers for roughly **39% of paraphrase pairs**. This is the best-performing model, and it still fails over one-third of the time.
*   **Consistent-Acc gap:** For BERT-large, Accuracy is 48.1% but Consistent-Acc is only 29.5%. This **18.6 percentage point drop** quantifies how much "knowledge" is lost due to inconsistency.
*   **Baseline behavior:** The majority baseline achieves 100% consistency by construction (it always predicts the same answer), but its Accuracy is only 23.1%. This shows that consistency alone is insufficient — the model must also be correct.

#### Knowledge Extraction Quality (Table 2)
Table 2 reveals that patterns are well-designed but models don't fully utilize them:

*   **Succ-Patt:** All PLMs achieve 100%, confirming the patterns are valid and can extract knowledge.
*   **Succ-Objs:** Ranges from 45.8% (ALBERT-base) to 65.7% (BERT-large). This means **34-54% of facts are not retrievable** by any pattern, even though the patterns themselves are valid.
*   **Know-Const vs. Unk-Const:** For BERT-large, Know-Const is 65.2% while Unk-Const is 48.1%. This **17.1 point difference** shows models are more consistent when they actually know the answer — but even then, consistency is far from perfect.

#### BERT Outperforms Newer Architectures
A striking finding is that BERT consistently outperforms RoBERTa and ALBERT on both Accuracy and Consistency, despite the latter being newer and trained on more data:

*   BERT-large Consistency: **61.1%**
*   RoBERTa-large Consistency: **56.3%** (4.8 points lower)
*   ALBERT-xxlarge Consistency: **52.1%** (9.0 points lower)

The authors hypothesize (Section 6.2) this is due to **training data composition**: BERT's heavy reliance on Wikipedia (a unified, fact-dense source) versus RoBERTa/ALBERT's inclusion of CommonCrawl (noisier, potentially contradictory). This challenges the assumption that more training data always improves model quality.

#### Effect of Pretraining Corpus Size (Table 4)
Table 4 tests RoBERTa models trained on increasing data volumes:

| Model | Training Tokens | Accuracy | Consistency | Consistent-Acc |
|-------|-----------------|----------|-------------|----------------|
| RoBERTa-med-small | 1M | 11.2% | 37.1% | 2.8% |
| RoBERTa-base | 10M | 17.3% | 29.8% | 3.2% |
| RoBERTa-base | 100M | 22.1% | 31.5% | 3.7% |
| RoBERTa-base | 1B | 38.0% | 50.6% | 18.0% |

**Non-monotonic consistency:** The 1M-token model is **more consistent** (37.1%) than the 10M (29.8%) and 100M (31.5%) models, despite having lower Accuracy. This suggests that adding more data can introduce contradictions that harm consistency, even as accuracy improves. Only at 1B tokens does consistency recover substantially.

#### Syntactic Variation Sensitivity (Table 5)
Table 5 tests whether models handle syntactic changes differently from lexical changes:

| Model | Diff-Syntax | No-Change |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| BERT-large | 67.5% | 78.7% |
| RoBERTa-large | 69.7% | 80.3% |
| ALBERT-xxlarge | 51.7% | 67.3% |

When only syntax differs (e.g., active vs. passive voice), consistency is **10-15 points lower** than when both syntax and lexicon are identical. This confirms that PLMs are sensitive to surface-form variations, not just semantic content.

### 5.3 Improvement Method Results (Table 7)

The authors test their consistency loss on **25 unseen relations**, training on only 3 relations to verify generalization:

| Model | Accuracy | Consistency | Consistent-Acc |
|-------|----------|-------------|----------------|
| BERT-base (baseline) | 45.6% | 58.2% | 27.3% |
| BERT-ft (with consistency loss) | 47.4% | 64.0% | 33.2% |
| -consistency (ablation) | 46.9% | 60.9% | 30.9% |
| -typed (ablation) | 46.5% | 62.0% | 31.1% |
| -MLM (ablation) | 16.9% | 80.8% | 9.1% |

**Improvement magnitude:**
*   Consistency increases by **5.8 points** (58.2% → 64.0%)
*   Consistent-Acc increases by **5.9 points** (27.3% → 33.2%)
*   Accuracy improves slightly by **1.8 points** (45.6% → 47.4%)

All improvements are statistically significant ($p \ll 0.01$) per McNemar's test.

#### Critical Ablation: The -MLM Result
The `-MLM` ablation reveals a crucial trade-off. Removing the standard MLM loss while keeping only the consistency loss produces:
*   **Consistency: 80.8%** (highest of all configurations)
*   **Accuracy: 16.9%** (lowest of all configurations)
*   **Consistent-Acc: 9.1%** (worst overall)

This demonstrates that the model learns to be consistent by **collapsing its output distribution** — always predicting the same token regardless of input. The MLM loss acts as a **regularizer** that prevents this collapse, maintaining the model's ability to distinguish between different facts while improving consistency.

### 5.4 Assessment of Experimental Validity

#### Strengths of the Experimental Design

1.  **Zero-Shot Evaluation:** By avoiding finetuning on task-specific data, the authors isolate properties inherent to pretraining. This allows fair comparison across models.

2.  **Restricted Candidate Sets:** Limiting predictions to valid KB entities (Section 3.2) makes the task easier, meaning poor consistency in this setup implies even worse performance in open-vocabulary settings. This is a conservative evaluation choice.

3.  **Unseen Relation Testing:** The improvement method is validated on 25 relations not seen during training (Section 8.2), demonstrating that consistency generalizes as a skill rather than memorized fact patterns.

4.  **Multiple Metrics:** Using Accuracy, Consistency, and Consistent-Acc together provides a nuanced view. A model could game any single metric, but improving all three simultaneously is harder.

#### Limitations and Caveats

1.  **Downstream Transfer Gap:** Section 8.3 notes that consistency improvements **did not transfer** to SQuAD paraphrase evaluation. The improved model performed "on par with the baseline" on downstream QA tasks. This is a significant limitation: consistency in knowledge probing does not guarantee robustness in complex reasoning tasks.

2.  **Representation-Behavior Disconnect:** Section 7.2 reports that the correlation between representation clustering (V-measure) and consistency scores is **close to zero** (except for BERT-large-wwm at 39.5%). This means the t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) are descriptive but not predictive — we cannot yet use representation analysis to forecast model behavior.

3.  **Single-Token Restriction:** By filtering to single-token objects, the evaluation excludes many valid KB entities (e.g., "New York" is two tokens). While this simplifies analysis, it limits the generalizability of results to real-world applications.

4.  **Limited Training Relations:** The improvement method trains on only 3 relations. While this tests generalization, it also means the absolute improvement (5.8 points) may not reflect the ceiling of what's achievable with more training data.

5.  **MLM-Specific Findings:** All results apply to Masked Language Models. The paper explicitly excludes autoregressive models (GPT) and text-to-text models (T5), so findings may not generalize to those architectures.

#### Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: "PLM consistency is poor"** — **Strongly supported.** The best model (BERT-large) achieves only 61.1% consistency, failing on 39% of paraphrase pairs. This is unambiguous quantitative evidence.

**Claim 2: "BERT outperforms RoBERTa/ALBERT on consistency"** — **Supported.** Table 3 shows consistent advantages across all BERT variants. The hypothesis about Wikipedia data quality is plausible but not definitively proven (it remains a correlation).

**Claim 3: "Consistency loss improves model behavior"** — **Supported with caveats.** Table 7 shows statistically significant improvements on unseen relations. However, the failure to transfer to SQuAD (Section 8.3) limits the practical significance of this claim.

**Claim 4: "Representations cluster by pattern, not entity"** — **Partially supported.** Figure 3 visually demonstrates pattern-based clustering, but the near-zero correlation between V-measure and consistency scores means this observation doesn't fully explain model behavior.

### 5.5 Summary of Trade-offs

The experiments reveal several important trade-offs that practitioners should consider:

| Trade-off | Observation | Implication |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Accuracy vs. Consistency | Higher accuracy doesn't guarantee consistency (Table 3 gap) | Evaluate both metrics; don't assume accuracy implies robustness |
| Data Volume vs. Consistency | More pretraining data can hurt consistency (Table 4) | Data quality matters more than quantity for knowledge tasks |
| Consistency Loss vs. MLM Loss | Consistency loss alone causes collapse (Table 7 -MLM) | Must balance auxiliary losses with standard objectives |
| Probing vs. Downstream | Consistency improvements don't transfer to SQuAD | Knowledge probing consistency ≠ task robustness |

These trade-offs suggest that consistency is a distinct property from accuracy, requiring dedicated optimization rather than emerging naturally from scale or standard training.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper makes significant contributions to understanding and improving PLM consistency, several important limitations and trade-offs constrain the applicability and interpretation of its findings. This section critically examines the assumptions underlying the approach, identifies scenarios the methodology does not address, and highlights open questions that remain unresolved.

### 6.1 Core Assumptions and Their Implications

The paper's methodology rests on several foundational assumptions that, while reasonable, introduce constraints on how we interpret the results.

**Assumption 1: PARAREL Patterns Are Truly Semantically Equivalent**

The entire evaluation framework depends on the premise that paraphrases in `PARAREL` express identical factual queries. If two patterns are not truly equivalent, model disagreement would be justified rather than inconsistent. The authors address this through manual curation and human agreement scores (95.5% for paraphrases, 98.3% for controls, Section 4), but this introduces its own limitations:

*   **Expert Bias:** The patterns were curated by NLP experts, whose linguistic intuitions may not generalize to all users or domains. What experts consider semantically equivalent may differ from how end-users phrase queries.
*   **Context Dependence:** Some paraphrases may be equivalent in certain contexts but not others. For example, `"X died in Y"` and `"X expired at Y"` (Table 8, Appendix B) are treated as equivalent for the `place of death` relation, but `"expired"` carries different connotations in medical vs. legal contexts.
*   **Quasi-Paraphrase Definition:** Section 3.1 explicitly uses the term "quasi-paraphrase" (from Bhagat and Hovy, 2013), acknowledging that these are "fuzzy" paraphrases rather than strict logical equivalents. This flexibility is practical but introduces ambiguity into what counts as an inconsistency.

**Assumption 2: Restricted Candidate Sets Reflect True Knowledge**

Section 3.2 restricts model outputs to valid KB entities for each relation. While the authors acknowledge this "makes the task easier for the PLM," this design choice has deeper implications:

*   **Artificial Constraint:** Real-world queries do not come with predefined answer sets. A model that is consistent only when its vocabulary is restricted may fail catastrophically in open-ended settings.
*   **Vocabulary Mismatch:** By filtering to single-token objects (Section 5.1), the evaluation excludes multi-token entities like "New York" or "United States." This represents a significant portion of real-world knowledge and limits the generalizability of findings.
*   **Conservative Estimates:** The authors argue that poor consistency in this setup "strongly implies that consistency would be even lower without restricting candidates." While logically sound, this means the reported 61.1% consistency for BERT-large is likely an **upper bound** on real-world performance.

**Assumption 3: Consistency Loss Generalizes Across Relations**

The improvement method trains on only **3 relations** and tests on **25 unseen relations** (Section 8.2). This tests generalization but assumes:

*   **Relation Homogeneity:** The method assumes that consistency learned on `original-language`, `named-after`, and `original-network` transfers to relations like `capital-of` or `born-in`. While Table 7 shows improvement, the 5.8 point gain is modest, suggesting transfer is incomplete.
*   **Pattern Diversity:** The 3 training relations may not capture the full syntactic and lexical diversity of all 38 relations. Relations with more complex patterns or rarer entities may not benefit equally.

### 6.2 Scenarios and Problem Settings Not Addressed

The paper's scope, while valuable, leaves several important scenarios unexamined.

**Downstream Task Transfer**

The most significant gap is the failure to demonstrate that consistency improvements transfer to downstream applications. Section 8.3 states:

> "However, the results perform on par with the baseline model, both on SQuAD and the paraphrase questions. More research is required to show that consistent PLMs can also benefit downstream tasks."

This is a critical limitation. The paper improves consistency in a **probing setting** (zero-shot factual queries) but does not show benefits for:

*   **Question Answering:** Real QA systems involve multi-hop reasoning, not single-fact retrieval.
*   **Text Generation:** Consistency in generation (e.g., summarization, dialogue) requires maintaining coherence across multiple tokens, not just single-mask predictions.
*   **Multi-Turn Interactions:** Conversational systems must maintain consistency across turns, not just within a single query.

The disconnect suggests that **probing consistency ≠ task robustness**. A model can be consistent on factual queries while still failing on complex reasoning tasks.

**Other Forms of Consistency**

Section 9 explicitly acknowledges that the paper focuses on one type of consistency:

> "In this work we focus on one type of consistency, that is, consistency in the face of paraphrasing; however, consistency is a broader concept."

Unaddressed forms include:

*   **Negation Consistency:** As noted in Section 9, a consistent model should return different answers for `"Birds can [MASK]"` vs. `"Birds cannot [MASK]"`. This work does not evaluate whether the consistency loss affects negation handling.
*   **Temporal Consistency:** Facts change over time (e.g., `"The president of the US is [MASK]"`). The paper does not address how models should handle temporally sensitive queries.
*   **Logical Consistency:** The paper does not evaluate whether models maintain consistency across logically related facts (e.g., if `X is the capital of Y`, then `Y has capital X`).
*   **Cross-Modal Consistency:** For multi-modal models, consistency across text and image inputs is not addressed.

**Model Architecture Limitations**

Section 1 explicitly limits the scope to Masked Language Models:

> "We study the consistency of factual knowledge in PLMs, specifically in Masked Language Models (MLM)... as opposed to other strategies such as standard language modeling (Radford et al., 2019) or text-to-text (Raffel et al., 2020)."

This excludes:

*   **Autoregressive Models (GPT family):** These models generate text sequentially and may exhibit different consistency patterns due to their causal attention structure.
*   **Text-to-Text Models (T5, etc.):** These models frame all tasks as text generation and may handle paraphrases differently.
*   **Retrieval-Augmented Models:** Systems that combine PLMs with external knowledge bases (e.g., RAG, Neural Databases) are not evaluated, yet these may offer alternative paths to consistency.

### 6.3 Computational, Data, and Scalability Constraints

The proposed improvement method introduces practical constraints that affect its real-world applicability.

**Continual Pretraining Costs**

The consistency improvement requires **continual pretraining** with the new loss function (Section 8). This has several costs:

*   **Compute Requirements:** Even for BERT-base, training for 3 epochs with the additional consistency loss requires significant GPU resources. The paper does not report exact training times or costs, but continual pretraining is substantially more expensive than standard finetuning.
*   **Data Requirements:** The method requires curated paraphrase patterns (like `PARAREL`) for training. Creating such resources for new domains or languages requires expert annotation, which does not scale easily.
*   **Energy Consumption:** Large-scale pretraining has significant environmental costs. Adding auxiliary losses increases training time and energy usage.

**Limited Training Relations**

The method trains on only **3 relations** out of 38 (Section 8.2). While this tests generalization, it also means:

*   **Incomplete Coverage:** The model may not achieve optimal consistency because it sees limited pattern diversity during training.
*   **Domain Specificity:** The 3 training relations (`original-language`, `named-after`, `original-network`) are not location-based, but they may not represent the full range of relation types (e.g., temporal, numerical, hierarchical).
*   **Scaling Unknown:** It is unclear whether training on more relations would yield diminishing returns or continued improvement. The paper does not ablate the number of training relations.

**Vocabulary Restriction Overhead**

The consistency loss operates on restricted candidate sets (Section 8.1). This requires:

*   **Per-Relation Configuration:** Each relation needs a predefined set of valid objects, which requires access to a knowledge base.
*   **Dynamic Filtering:** During training, the model must filter its output vocabulary for each batch, adding computational overhead.
*   **Limited Applicability:** This approach works for factual knowledge but is less applicable to open-ended generation tasks where the answer space is not predefined.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

Several weaknesses in the methodology and unresolved questions limit the paper's conclusions.

**Representation-Behavior Disconnect**

Section 7.2 reveals a fundamental gap in understanding:

> "However, the correlation between these variables [consistency scores and V-measure of representations] is close to zero... therefore not explaining the models' behavior."

This means:

*   **Descriptive, Not Predictive:** The t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) show pattern-based clustering, but we cannot use this to predict which models or relations will be more consistent.
*   **Mechanism Unclear:** We do not know *which* neurons or attention heads drive consistency. The representation analysis is observational rather than mechanistic.
*   **Intervention Difficulty:** Without understanding the mechanism, it is hard to design more targeted interventions beyond the brute-force consistency loss.

**Wikipedia Dependency Hypothesis Unproven**

Section 6.2 hypothesizes that BERT's superiority stems from its Wikipedia-focused training data:

> "We hypothesize this result is caused by the different sources of training data... for BERT it is the main data source, but for RoBERTa and ALBERT it is only a small portion."

However:

*   **Correlation, Not Causation:** This is a plausible hypothesis but not experimentally verified. The paper does not ablate training data sources to isolate the Wikipedia effect.
*   **Confounding Factors:** BERT and RoBERTa differ in multiple ways (architecture, tokenization, training objectives), not just data. The consistency difference may stem from these other factors.
*   **Generalizability:** If Wikipedia is key to consistency, this limits the applicability of findings to domains where Wikipedia-like corpora exist.

**Statistical Significance vs. Practical Significance**

The improvements, while statistically significant ($p \ll 0.01$), are modest in absolute terms:

*   **5.8 Point Gain:** Consistency improves from 58.2% to 64.0% (Table 7). This means the model still fails on **36% of paraphrase pairs**.
*   **Consistent-Acc Ceiling:** Even after improvement, Consistent-Acc is only 33.2%, meaning only 1 in 3 facts are both correct and consistent.
*   **Downstream Null Result:** The failure to improve SQuAD performance (Section 8.3) suggests the practical impact may be limited.

**Knowledge Base Limitations**

Section 9 acknowledges that using PLMs as KBs has inherent limitations:

> "It lacks important properties of standard KBs: (1) the ability to return more than a single answer and (2) the ability to return no answer."

This means:

*   **N-M Relations:** Many-to-many relations (e.g., `shares-border-with`) are excluded from consistency evaluation (Section 3.1), limiting the scope of applicable knowledge.
*   **Uncertainty Modeling:** PLMs cannot express uncertainty or ignorance. A traditional KB can return "unknown," but PLMs must always predict something.
*   **Answer Ordering:** For relations with multiple valid answers, the model should return results in a deterministic order. The paper measures this (Section 6.2) but does not propose improvements.

### 6.5 Summary of Key Limitations

| Limitation Category | Specific Issue | Evidence |
|---------------------|----------------|----------|
| **Downstream Transfer** | Consistency gains do not improve SQuAD performance | Section 8.3 |
| **Representation Understanding** | Near-zero correlation between representations and behavior | Section 7.2 |
| **Scope** | Only paraphrastic consistency, not negation or logical consistency | Section 9 |
| **Architecture** | Only MLMs evaluated, not autoregressive or text-to-text models | Section 1 |
| **Data Constraints** | Single-token objects only, excluding multi-token entities | Section 5.1 |
| **Training Scale** | Only 3 relations used for improvement method | Section 8.2 |
| **Practical Impact** | Modest absolute improvements (5.8 points) despite statistical significance | Table 7 |
| **KB Limitations** | PLMs cannot return "no answer" or multiple answers naturally | Section 9 |

### 6.6 Open Questions for Future Research

The paper concludes with several directions that remain unexplored:

1.  **Mechanism Discovery:** What specific model components (attention heads, layers, neurons) drive consistency? Can we intervene more precisely than with a global loss?

2.  **Downstream Bridging:** How can consistency improvements in probing settings be translated to downstream task robustness? Is additional task-specific adaptation required?

3.  **Data Quality vs. Quantity:** Can we definitively prove that Wikipedia-focused data improves consistency? What other corpus characteristics matter?

4.  **Architecture Generalization:** Do the findings hold for autoregressive models, multi-modal models, or retrieval-augmented systems?

5.  **Consistency Types:** How do different forms of consistency (paraphrastic, negation, temporal, logical) relate to each other? Can a single method improve all types?

6.  **Scaling Laws:** How does consistency scale with model size, training data, and training duration? Is there a point where consistency emerges naturally?

These limitations do not diminish the paper's contributions but rather define the boundaries within which its findings should be interpreted. The work establishes a foundation for measuring and improving consistency, but significant challenges remain before consistent PLMs become a practical reality for real-world applications.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work does more than identify a flaw in Pretrained Language Models (PLMs); it fundamentally shifts how we evaluate and train models for knowledge-intensive tasks. By demonstrating that accuracy is insufficient and consistency is optimizable, the paper opens new avenues for research and practical application. This section outlines how these findings change the landscape of NLP, what specific research paths they enable, and how practitioners should integrate these insights into real-world systems.

### 7.1 Redefining Model Reliability: Beyond Accuracy
The most immediate implication of this work is the necessity to decouple **knowledge possession** from **knowledge reliability**. Prior to this study, the standard metric for evaluating PLMs as Knowledge Bases (KBs) was accuracy (e.g., LAMA benchmarks). This paper proves that high accuracy can coexist with low consistency, creating a false sense of security.

*   **The Reliability Gap:** As shown in **Table 3**, `BERT-large` achieves $48.1\%$ accuracy but only $29.5\%$ **Consistent-Acc**. This $\approx 19$ percentage point drop represents "brittle knowledge"—facts the model knows only under specific phrasing conditions.
*   **Paradigm Shift:** Future evaluations of knowledge-intensive models must include consistency metrics alongside accuracy. A model that is $90\%$ accurate but $50\%$ consistent is less reliable than a model that is $80\%$ accurate and $90\%$ consistent for applications like fact-checking or automated knowledge base population.
*   **Benchmarking Standard:** The `PARAREL` resource (Section 4) sets a new standard for benchmark construction. It demonstrates that **manual curation** of paraphrases is critical for reliable evaluation. Automatic paraphrase generation (like back-translation) introduces noise that confounds consistency measurement. Future benchmarks should adopt similar rigorous validation steps (e.g., the $95.5\%$ human agreement reported in Section 4) to ensure input equivalence.

### 7.2 Optimizing Behavior via Objectives
A key methodological contribution is the demonstration that behavioral properties like consistency can be directly optimized via **auxiliary loss functions** during pretraining. This moves the field beyond relying on emergent properties from scale.

*   **Loss Design as a Control Knob:** The success of the consistency loss (Section 8) shows that we can enforce invariance properties mathematically. The loss function forces the output distributions of paraphrases to converge:
    $$
    L_c = \sum_{n=1}^{k} \sum_{m=n+1}^{k} D_{KL}(Q_{n} || Q_{m}) + D_{KL}(Q_{m} || Q_{n})
    $$
    This approach is generalizable. Researchers can design similar losses to optimize for other desirable behaviors, such as **fairness** (invariance to demographic markers), **safety** (invariance to jailbreak attempts), or **robustness** (invariance to adversarial perturbations).
*   **Regularization is Critical:** The ablation study in **Table 7** (`-MLM` row) provides a crucial lesson for multi-objective training. Removing the standard Masked Language Modeling (MLM) loss caused consistency to spike to $80.8\%$ while accuracy crashed to $16.9\%$. This indicates that without the standard objective, the model learns a trivial solution (always predicting the same token). Future work using auxiliary losses must always include a **regularizing baseline loss** to prevent capability collapse.
*   **Continual Pretraining vs. Fine-tuning:** The improvement method uses continual pretraining rather than task-specific fine-tuning. This implies that behavioral robustness is best instilled **early** in the model lifecycle. Trying to fix consistency solely during downstream fine-tuning may be insufficient, as the underlying representations remain sensitive to surface forms (Figure 3).

### 7.3 The Data Quality Hypothesis
The finding that `BERT` outperforms `RoBERTa` and `ALBERT` on consistency challenges the prevailing "scale is all you need" narrative. It suggests that **data composition** matters more than raw volume for knowledge reliability.

*   **Unified vs. Noisy Corpora:** The authors hypothesize (Section 6.2) that `BERT`'s reliance on Wikipedia (a unified, fact-checked source) aids consistency, whereas `RoBERTa`'s inclusion of CommonCrawl (noisier, potentially contradictory) harms it. **Table 4** supports this, showing non-monotonic consistency gains as training data increases.
*   **Implication for Data Curation:** For tasks requiring factual reliability, practitioners should prioritize **high-quality, curated corpora** over massive, unfiltered web scrapes. This might mean reverting to smaller, denser datasets or developing better filtering mechanisms to remove contradictory facts from pretraining corpora.
*   **Conflict Resolution:** This highlights a need for better **data deduplication and conflict resolution** techniques during pretraining. If a model sees "X was born in Y" in one document and "X was born in Z" in another, it learns to be inconsistent. Future pretraining pipelines should incorporate logic checks to minimize factual contradictions in the training data itself.

### 7.4 Roadmap for Future Research
Based on the limitations identified in **Section 6** and the discussion in **Section 9**, several critical research directions emerge.

1.  **Bridging the Downstream Gap:**
    The most pressing open question is why consistency improvements did not transfer to SQuAD (Section 8.3). Future work must investigate the **representation mismatch** between probing tasks (single-token prediction) and downstream tasks (span extraction, generation).
    *   *Research Question:* Does consistency need to be enforced at the **representation level** (e.g., aligning hidden states) rather than just the **output level** to benefit downstream tasks?
    *   *Potential Approach:* Apply the consistency loss to intermediate layers of the transformer, not just the final output distribution.

2.  **Mechanistic Interpretability:**
    Section 7.2 found a near-zero correlation between global representation clustering (V-measure) and consistency scores. This means we can observe inconsistency but not yet predict it from representations.
    *   *Research Question:* Which specific neurons, attention heads, or layers encode surface-form sensitivity vs. semantic invariance?
    *   *Potential Approach:* Use techniques like **amnesic probing** (Elazar et al., 2021) or **causal mediation analysis** to identify and edit the specific mechanisms causing paraphrase sensitivity.

3.  **Expanding the Scope of Consistency:**
    This paper focuses on **paraphrastic consistency**. Section 9 explicitly calls for broader definitions.
    *   **Negation Consistency:** Ensuring models distinguish between `"X is Y"` and `"X is not Y"` (Ettinger, 2020).
    *   **Temporal Consistency:** Handling facts that change over time (e.g., `"The CEO of X is [MASK]"`).
    *   **Logical Consistency:** Ensuring transitivity (if `A > B` and `B > C`, then `A > C`).
    *   *Research Question:* Can a single unified loss function optimize for all these consistency types simultaneously, or do they conflict?

4.  **Architecture Generalization:**
    The study is limited to Masked Language Models (MLMs).
    *   *Research Question:* Do autoregressive models (GPT family) or text-to-text models (T5) exhibit similar consistency failures?
    *   *Hypothesis:* Autoregressive models might show different failure modes due to causal attention, potentially making them more susceptible to early-token bias in paraphrases.

### 7.5 Practical Integration Guidance
For practitioners looking to apply these findings, the following guidelines summarize when and how to use the methods proposed in this paper.

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Knowledge Base Population** | **Use `PARAREL` + Consistency Loss** | If extracting facts to populate a structured KB, consistency is critical to avoid contradictory entries. The consistency loss (Section 8) directly addresses this. |
| **Open-Domain QA** | **Evaluate with `PARAREL`** | Before deploying a QA model, probe it with `PARAREL` to estimate reliability. If consistency is low, expect user-facing errors on paraphrased queries. |
| **Generative Tasks (Summarization)** | **Caution Advised** | The paper notes consistency gains did not transfer to SQuAD (Section 8.3). Do not assume probing consistency guarantees coherent generation. Additional task-specific alignment is needed. |
| **Resource-Constrained Settings** | **Prioritize Data Quality** | Based on the BERT vs. RoBERTa results (Table 3), a smaller model trained on cleaner data (Wikipedia) may outperform a larger model on noisy data for knowledge tasks. |
| **Multi-Token Entities** | **Extend Candidate Sets** | The paper restricted evaluation to single-token objects (Section 5.1). For real-world use, extend the restricted candidate set logic to handle subword tokenization aggregation for entities like "New York". |

**Implementation Note:**
To integrate the consistency loss, practitioners should:
1.  **Prepare Paraphrase Pairs:** Create or source paraphrased prompts for your target relations (following `PARAREL` guidelines in Section 4).
2.  **Define Candidate Sets:** Restrict the vocabulary to valid answers per relation to prevent the model from learning trivial consistency (Section 8.1).
3.  **Balance Losses:** Use a weighted sum $L = \lambda L_c + L_{MLM}$. The paper suggests $\lambda \in \{0.1, 0.5, 1\}$ (Section 8.2). Start with $\lambda=0.1$ to avoid catastrophic forgetting of language capabilities.
4.  **Monitor Both Metrics:** Track both Accuracy and Consistency during training. If Accuracy drops while Consistency rises, increase the weight of the MLM loss.

### 7.6 Conclusion on Impact
This work establishes that **consistency is a measurable, optimizable property** distinct from accuracy. By providing both the diagnostic tool (`PARAREL`) and the remediation strategy (Consistency Loss), the authors equip the community to move beyond "does the model know?" to "does the model know reliably?". While challenges remain—particularly in transferring these gains to complex downstream tasks—the paper lays the foundational framework for building language models that are not just knowledgeable, but trustworthy.
