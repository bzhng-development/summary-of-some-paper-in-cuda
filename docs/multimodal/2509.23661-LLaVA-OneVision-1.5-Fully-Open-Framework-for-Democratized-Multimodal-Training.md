## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces `LLaVA-OneVision-1.5`, a fully open-source family of Large Multimodal Models that addresses the scarcity of reproducible, high-performance systems by providing an efficient training framework operable within a $16,000 budget. The release comprises critical assets including an 85M concept-balanced pretraining dataset (`LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-Mid-Traning`) and a 22M instruction dataset (`LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-Instruct`), enabling the `LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-8B` model to outperform `Qwen2.5-VL-7B` on 18 of 27 benchmarks. By incorporating a lightweight RL post-training stage using the `AReaL` system, the framework significantly boosts complex reasoning capabilities while democratizing access to state-of-the-art multimodal AI.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Proprietary Model Problem

The field of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) has witnessed remarkable progress in recent years, with systems demonstrating sophisticated capabilities in understanding images, charts, and documents. However, a critical barrier persists: **the most performant models remain proprietary**. As stated in the Introduction, "neither their training data nor source code [are] publicly available." This creates a fundamental asymmetry in the research landscape.

This opacity matters for several reasons:

- **Reproducibility**: Without access to training data, code, or detailed methodologies, researchers cannot verify claims or build upon existing work with confidence.
- **Democratization**: Only well-funded institutions can afford to develop competitive models from scratch, concentrating AI capabilities in few hands.
- **Scientific Understanding**: The community "lacks crucial insights into how such high-performing LMMs can be built from scratch" (Introduction), limiting theoretical progress.

### Why Open Frameworks Matter

The importance of open multimodal frameworks extends beyond academic curiosity. Consider the practical implications:

1. **Specialized Applications**: Researchers building domain-specific systems (medical imaging, legal document analysis, scientific diagram understanding) need adaptable base models they can fine-tune without licensing restrictions.

2. **Cost Efficiency**: Proprietary API-based solutions incur recurring costs per inference. Open models enable one-time training investments with unlimited deployment.

3. **Privacy and Control**: Organizations handling sensitive visual data cannot rely on external APIs that may log or retain inputs.

The paper positions itself as addressing this gap by providing "an open, efficient, and reproducible framework for building high-quality vision-language models entirely from scratch" (Abstract).

### Prior Open Approaches and Their Limitations

Several research efforts have attempted to reproduce proprietary capabilities using open architectures. Understanding their trajectories clarifies where LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 intervenes.

#### Early Open Efforts

The LLaVA series (Liu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2025a) pioneered fully open training data and code releases. These works established the "ViT–MLP–LLM" paradigm that remains influential. However, as the Introduction notes, "their performance now falls substantially behind that of current state-of-the-art models." The performance gap is quantified in Table 1, where LLaVA-OV-7B scores 55.9 on MMStar compared to 67.7 for LLaVA-OV-1.5-8B—a gap of nearly 12 percentage points.

#### Recent Advances

More recent works have pushed boundaries:

- **Molmo** (Deitke et al., 2025) released model weights, datasets, and source code, achieving "near-parity with GPT-4V on both academic benchmarks and user preference evaluations" through careful architectural choices and high-quality data.

- **Open-Qwen2VL** (Wang et al., 2025b) introduces a 2B-parameter model pre-trained on "only 0.36% of 1.4T multimodal tokens in Qwen2-VL" while outperforming Qwen2-VL-2B across various benchmarks.

Despite these advances, the Introduction states that "the performance gap between open-source and proprietary models continues to widen as the field of LMMs rapidly evolves." Two persistent constraints limit open efforts:

1. **Substantial Computational Demands**: Training competitive LMMs typically requires thousands of GPU-hours, placing them beyond reach of most research groups.

2. **Suboptimal Training Efficiency**: Existing frameworks suffer from padding overhead and inefficient data utilization, particularly with heterogeneous multimodal data.

### How LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 Positions Itself

LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 addresses these limitations through three strategic interventions:

#### 1. Cost-Effective Training at Scale

The paper claims training is achievable "within a $16,000 budget" (Abstract). This figure is non-trivial—it represents approximately 128 × A800 GPUs over 3.7 days for mid-training on 85 million captions (Section 4.2). By comparison, proprietary model training often exceeds millions of dollars. The efficiency gains come from an "offline parallel data packing strategy" that yields "up to an 11× compression ratio on 85 million pretraining samples" (Section 4.2), substantially reducing padding waste.

#### 2. Performance Parity with Stronger Baselines

Rather than merely matching earlier open models, LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 targets current state-of-the-art open competitors. Table 1 shows:

- `LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-8B` outperforms `Qwen2.5-VL-7B` on **18 of 27 benchmarks**
- `LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-4B` surpasses `Qwen2.5-VL-3B` on **all 27 benchmarks**

This positioning is deliberate: Qwen2.5-VL represents one of the strongest openly-available model families, making it a meaningful comparison point.

#### 3. Complete Asset Release

Unlike works that release only model weights, LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 provides:

- **Datasets**: 85M concept-balanced pretraining data (`LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-Mid-Traning`) and 22M instruction data (`LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-Instruct`)
- **Training Framework**: End-to-end efficient training pipeline
- **Model Checkpoints**: Both base and instruct variants
- **RL Post-training**: Separate reinforcement learning code and data

This comprehensive release enables true reproducibility—researchers can train from scratch rather than merely fine-tuning provided weights.

### The Vision Encoder Choice: A Key Differentiator

One architectural decision warrants specific attention. While prior works adopt SigLIP (Zhai et al., 2023) or DFN (Fang et al., 2023) as vision encoders, LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 integrates **RICE-ViT** (Xie et al., 2025). This choice addresses a fundamental limitation in contrastive vision-language models.

As Section 2.2 explains, models like CLIP and SigLIP "fail to capture the similarity structure of training data or the local region-level semantics within images." This shortcoming stems from instance-wise contrastive learning, which "treats all instances as negatives regardless of their semantic similarity." RICE-ViT introduces a "unified region cluster discrimination loss" trained on 450M images and 2.4B candidate regions, enabling precise multimodal alignment and enriched region-level representation.

Table 2 validates this choice empirically. At 560px resolution, RICE-ViT achieves 61.1% on OCR Avg compared to 60.9% for SigLIPv2, while maintaining competitive general vision understanding (70.8% Other Avg vs 71.1% for SigLIPv2). More importantly, RICE-ViT accomplishes this with "a single cluster discrimination loss" versus SigLIP2's "multiple specialized losses (SILC, TIPS, LocCa, and Sigmoid)" (Section 2.2), reducing architectural complexity.

### The Data Scaling Insight

A notable finding in this work challenges conventional wisdom about training complexity. The Introduction states: "we find that simply scaling data at the mid-training stage alone can produce state-of-the-art LMMs, eliminating the need for complex training paradigms."

Figure 6 illustrates this claim, showing performance across different mid-training data scales (4M vs 85M samples). The 85M configuration consistently outperforms smaller scales across all ten benchmarks shown. This observation has practical significance: rather than designing increasingly sophisticated training objectives, careful data curation and scaling may yield superior returns.

Figure 7 further validates the importance of **concept balancing**. Models trained on 2M concept-balanced data outperform those trained on 2M randomly-sampled data on "25 of 27 evaluated downstream benchmarks." This suggests that data quality—specifically, semantic diversity—matters more than raw quantity alone.

LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 occupies a distinct niche in the LMM landscape:

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 is a complete training system that transforms a standard language model into a multimodal AI capable of understanding images, documents, and diagrams by connecting visual encoders to text generation through learned projection layers. The system solves the problem of expensive, opaque multimodal training by providing a reproducible pipeline that achieves state-of-the-art performance through careful data curation, efficient infrastructure optimization, and a lightweight reinforcement learning stage that elicits reasoning capabilities already latent in the model.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The architecture follows a modular "ViT–MLP–LLM" paradigm with three primary components working in sequence. First, the **Vision Encoder** (RICE-ViT) processes input images at native resolution, extracting visual features enriched with region-level semantic understanding through 2D rotary positional encoding and region-aware attention mechanisms. Second, the **Projector** (a two-layer MLP) bridges the modality gap by mapping visual embeddings into the language model's text embedding space, grouping spatially adjacent patch features before transformation. Third, the **Large Language Model** (Qwen3 backbone) receives the projected multimodal embeddings and integrates them with linguistic context to perform reasoning, instruction following, and natural language generation. During training, this architecture flows through three stages: language-image alignment on 558K samples, high-quality knowledge learning on 85M samples with full-parameter training, and visual instruction tuning on 22M samples. A post-training reinforcement learning stage then refines reasoning capabilities using 67K curated instances with rule-based rewards.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **Vision encoder design**: We start with RICE-ViT because it represents the most significant architectural departure from prior LLaVA works, replacing standard contrastive encoders with region-aware cluster discrimination.
- **Projection mechanism**: Understanding how visual features map to text embeddings clarifies the modality bridge before examining how the LLM consumes these representations.
- **Three-stage training pipeline**: The sequential training stages build upon each other, so we explain them in order to show how capabilities accumulate.
- **Data curation and concept balancing**: Since the paper emphasizes data quality over complex training paradigms, we detail the 85M mid-training dataset construction and balancing methodology.
- **Infrastructure optimizations**: The offline data packing and parallelism strategies explain how the $16,000 budget claim becomes achievable.
- **RL post-training curriculum**: The two-stage reinforcement learning approach requires understanding the base model first, so we cover it last as a capability enhancement layer.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This paper presents a systems engineering contribution to multimodal AI, demonstrating that careful orchestration of data quality, training efficiency, and architectural choices can produce competitive models within constrained budgets rather than relying on massive computational scaling alone.

#### Vision Encoder: RICE-ViT with Region-Aware Cluster Discrimination

The vision encoder represents the most significant architectural innovation in LLaVA-OneVision-1.5, replacing the SigLIP or DFN encoders common in prior LLaVA variants with RICE-ViT (Region-based Cluster Discrimination Vision Transformer). Traditional contrastive vision-language models like CLIP and SigLIP employ instance-wise contrastive learning, which treats all training instances as negatives regardless of their actual semantic similarity and represents each image solely through a single global embedding. This approach fundamentally limits the model's ability to capture local region-level semantics within images, which proves critical for tasks like grounding, OCR, and segmentation where fine-grained visual understanding matters.

RICE-ViT overcomes these limitations through a unified region cluster discrimination loss trained on 450M images and 2.4B candidate regions. The encoder introduces two key mechanisms that distinguish it from alternatives. First, it employs **region-aware attention** for local semantic modeling, allowing the model to attend to specific image regions rather than treating the entire image as a homogeneous representation. Second, it incorporates **2D rotary positional encoding (2D RoPE)**, which naturally supports variable input resolutions without requiring resolution-specific fine-tuning. This contrasts with models like Qwen2-VL and InternVL 2.5, which require explicit fine-tuning for different resolutions.

The mathematical formulation of the cluster discrimination approach differs fundamentally from standard contrastive losses. While SigLIP2 depends on multiple specialized losses (SILC, TIPS, LocCa, and Sigmoid), RICE-ViT adopts a single cluster discrimination loss that simultaneously strengthens general understanding, OCR, and localization capabilities. This unified formulation reduces architectural complexity and training overhead while matching SigLIP2's performance. The encoder preserves the `[CLS]` token to retain global semantic capacity during multimodal alignment, ensuring that both fine-grained regional features and holistic image understanding remain available to downstream components.

Table 2 provides empirical validation of this design choice. At 560px resolution, RICE-ViT achieves 61.1% on OCR Avg compared to 60.9% for SigLIPv2, while maintaining competitive general vision understanding at 70.8% Other Avg versus 71.1% for SigLIPv2. When integrated into the full LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-3B model, RICE-ViT demonstrates average performance improvements of 1.9% in OCR & Document Understanding and 0.9% in General Vision Understanding compared to Qwen-ViT from Qwen2.5-VL-7B. These gains justify the choice to adopt RICE-ViT despite its relative novelty compared to more established encoders.

#### Projector: Bridging Visual and Text Embedding Spaces

The projector serves as the critical interface between the vision encoder and the language model, responsible for mapping visual embeddings into the text embedding space where the LLM can process them. Following the approach introduced in Qwen2.5-VL, the projector first groups spatially adjacent sets of four patch features from the vision encoder output. These grouped features are then concatenated and passed through a two-layer multi-layer perceptron (MLP) that transforms them into the LLM's embedding dimension.

This design choice reflects a trade-off between computational efficiency and information preservation. Grouping four adjacent patches reduces the sequence length of visual tokens by a factor of four, which directly impacts memory consumption and attention computation in the LLM. However, the concatenation before MLP transformation ensures that spatial relationships within each 2×2 patch group remain encoded in the projected representation. The two-layer MLP provides sufficient capacity to learn the cross-modal mapping without introducing excessive parameters that would slow training or increase overfitting risk.

The projector undergoes training in Stage-1 (Language-Image Alignment) using the LLaVA-1.5 558K dataset, where only the projection layer is trained while the vision encoder and LLM remain frozen. This isolates the modality alignment problem, allowing the projector to learn a stable mapping before full-parameter training begins in Stage-1.5. The alignment stage ensures that visual features occupy a compatible region of the embedding space with text tokens, preventing the LLM from treating visual inputs as out-of-distribution noise.

#### Large Language Model: Qwen3 as the Reasoning Core

The large language model acts as the reasoning and generation core of the architecture, integrating projected multimodal embeddings with linguistic context to perform complex reasoning, instruction following, and natural language generation. LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 utilizes Qwen3 as the language backbone, which provides significant performance enhancements on downstream tasks compared to earlier Qwen variants. The LLM receives the projected visual embeddings as additional tokens in its input sequence, treating them semantically similar to text tokens after the projector has mapped them into the shared embedding space.

The architecture supports a context length of 8K tokens during mid-training, which accommodates both high-resolution image representations and extended text instructions. This context capacity proves essential for document understanding tasks where OCR text and visual layout information must be processed together. The LLM parameters remain frozen during Stage-1 alignment but undergo full-parameter training during Stage-1.5 and Stage-2, allowing the language model to adapt its internal representations to multimodal inputs rather than merely attending to externally projected features.

#### Three-Stage Training Pipeline: Progressive Capability Building

LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 employs a three-stage training pipeline that progressively builds multimodal capabilities while balancing computational efficiency with knowledge injection. This staged approach differs from single-stage training paradigms by isolating different learning objectives at each phase, preventing optimization conflicts and enabling more stable convergence.

**Stage-1: Language-Image Alignment** trains only the projection layer using the LLaVA-1.5 558K dataset. The objective at this stage is to align visual features into the word embedding space of the LLM without modifying the vision encoder or language model parameters. This stage establishes a stable foundation for multimodal processing, ensuring that visual tokens occupy a compatible region of the embedding space before more complex training begins. The 558K sample size keeps this stage computationally lightweight while providing sufficient signal for the projector to learn meaningful cross-modal mappings.

**Stage-1.5: High-Quality Knowledge Learning** transitions to full-parameter training of all modules using the 85M LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-Mid-Traning dataset. This stage strikes a balance between computational efficiency and injecting new knowledge into the LMM. The paper's key insight emerges here: "simply scaling data at the mid-training stage alone can produce state-of-the-art LMMs, eliminating the need for complex training paradigms." Figure 6 demonstrates this claim empirically, showing that models trained with 85M mid-training samples consistently outperform those trained with 4M samples across all ten benchmarks. The 85M dataset undergoes concept balancing (detailed below), which ensures semantic diversity rather than raw quantity alone driving performance gains. This stage runs on 128 × A800 GPUs over 3.7 days, representing the bulk of the $16,000 compute budget.

**Stage-2: Visual Instruction Tuning** continues full-parameter training with the 22M LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-Instruct dataset plus the FineVision dataset. This stage enables the LMM to handle diverse visual tasks with desired response formats, teaching the model to follow instructions rather than merely describing images. The instruction dataset covers seven balanced categories: Caption, Chart & Table, Code & Math, Domain-specific, General VQA, Grounding & Counting, OCR, and Science. Figure 3(c) shows the proportional distribution across these categories, ensuring no single task type dominates the training signal. The combination with FineVision creates the Merged46M SFT dataset, which Figure 8 shows delivers the best results across nearly all 16 evaluated benchmarks compared to using either dataset alone.

#### Data Curation: Concept Balancing for Semantic Diversity

The 85M LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-Mid-Traning dataset derives from multiple sources: COYO-700M, Obelics, DataComp-1B, LAION-CN, ImageNet-21K, SAM-1B, MINT, and Zero250M. Figure 3(b) illustrates the distribution of data sources within this mixture. However, raw aggregation of these sources produces a long-tail concept distribution where certain semantic categories appear far more frequently than others. Figure 10 in Appendix B shows that all eight source datasets exhibit pronounced long-tail bias, with Obelics displaying the broadest and most uniform distribution while ImageNet-21K and SA-1B cover fewer concepts with higher frequency concentration.

To address this imbalance, the authors introduce a concept-balanced sampling strategy inspired by MetaCLIP but with critical modifications. MetaCLIP depends on raw captions for concept matching, which struggles with caption-free or interleaved datasets like SAM-1B, ImageNet-21K, and Obelics. LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 instead adopts a feature-based matching approach that reduces reliance on caption quality. The method uses pretrained MetaCLIP-H/14-Full-CC2.5B encoders to project both images and MetaCLIP's 500K concept entries into a shared embedding space. Since MetaCLIP embeddings are already concept-balanced, this enables effective similarity-based concept induction.

The Top-K Concept Assignment and Balance Sampling algorithm operates as follows. Given an image set $\mathcal{I} = \{i_0, i_1, ..., i_N\}$ and a concept vocabulary set $\mathcal{V} = \{v_0, v_1, ...v_M\}$, the method first utilizes the image encoder $\Phi_v$ and text encoder $\Phi_t$ to extract image embeddings $\mathcal{E}_i = \{\Phi_v(i), i \in \mathcal{I}\}$ and concept embeddings $\mathcal{E}_t = \{\Phi_t(v), v \in \mathcal{V}\}$. Each image receives assignment to its top-$k$ nearest concepts based on cosine similarity of L2-normalized image and concept embeddings. Following MetaCLIP, each image is then weighted by the inverse frequencies of its concepts, and images are sampled based on normalized image weights. This inverse-frequency methodology promotes a more balanced concept distribution without relying on original captions, which may be noisy or missing.

Figure 3(a) illustrates the vocabulary coverage proportion before and after concept balancing, showing a smoother distribution post-balancing. Figure 7 validates the effectiveness of this approach: models trained on 2M concept-balanced data outperform those trained on 2M randomly-sampled data on 25 of 27 evaluated downstream benchmarks. This finding reinforces the paper's emphasis on data quality over training complexity. After concept balancing, a powerful captioner produces English and Chinese captions for the 85M images (20M Chinese, 65M English), followed by a validity filter to eliminate duplicates and excessively lengthy outputs.

The 22M instruction dataset undergoes similar curation for balanced coverage across the seven task categories mentioned above. Figure 3(c) shows the proportional distribution, ensuring that specialized capabilities like Grounding & Counting and OCR receive adequate training signal rather than being overwhelmed by General VQA samples.

#### Infrastructure Optimizations: Achieving the $16,000 Budget

The claim of training within a $16,000 budget requires substantial infrastructure optimizations beyond algorithmic choices. A major source of training inefficiency in multimodal models arises from padding, where batch samples are standardized by adding padding tokens to match the longest sequence in each batch. This results in significant computational overhead and poor GPU utilization, particularly problematic with heterogeneous multimodal data where image sizes and caption lengths vary widely.

To mitigate padding waste, the authors propose an **offline parallel data packing strategy** that consolidates multiple shorter samples into packed sequences during preprocessing rather than at runtime. The approach employs hash buckets to handle large-scale data efficiently and leverages multi-threaded, strategy-aware batching to control packing success rate, sample count, and batch composition. Unlike online packing, which operates dynamically during training and introduces runtime overhead, the offline method processes entire datasets or large contiguous chunks before training begins, ensuring uniform output lengths. This yields up to an 11× compression ratio on the 85 million pretraining samples, substantially improving efficiency by reducing the total number of tokens processed.

The training framework adopts AIAK-Training-LLM, built upon Megatron-LM, which provides transformer engine optimizations and specialized distributed training capabilities. The framework leverages distributed optimizer parallelism and uniform recomputation to enable efficient mid-training of LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-8B with 8K context length. The mid-training process conducts training at native resolution on 85 million captions using 128 × A800 GPUs over 3.7 days. This configuration represents the primary cost driver in the $16,000 budget, with Stage-1 and Stage-2 requiring comparatively fewer resources due to smaller dataset sizes.

#### RL Post-training: Eliciting Latent Reasoning Capabilities

The reinforcement learning post-training stage unlocks the model's latent potential through a lightweight RL phase that elicits robust chain-of-thought reasoning without injecting new knowledge. This stage operates on the principle that the supervised fine-tuned model already possesses the capability to solve complex tasks but fails to consistently assign high probability to correct reasoning paths. RL serves as an elicitation mechanism, redirecting probability mass toward solutions the model can already generate.

**Discrepancy-Driven Data Selection** curates the 67K RL training corpus by measuring the divergence between Pass@N and Pass@1 performance on diverse benchmarks. A significant gap indicates that correct solutions appear within the model's sampling distribution (high Pass@N) but the policy distribution fails to reliably prioritize them (low Pass@1). This selection paradigm ensures high training efficiency by targeting the model's effective learnable boundary, avoiding trivial tasks it has already mastered or unsolvable ones beyond its current grasp. The corpus aggregates diverse public data sources including ViRL, WebCode2M, UniSVG, Ref-L4, VigoRL-SA, VigoRL-SAT, Pixmo-Count, AI2D, and InfoVQA, covering STEM reasoning, coding, grounding, counting, spatial reasoning, diagram understanding, and OCR. Figure 4(a) shows the total RL corpus composition with STEM comprising 58.0%, Grounding 22.4%, and other categories making up the remainder.

**Reward-Based Sampling** further filters high-quality training instances by generating multiple candidate responses for each sample using the base model and computing automatic rewards. Only examples where the average reward across candidates falls within a specified range are retained, discarding both trivial and unsolvable cases to bias the corpus toward medium-difficulty instances that provide the most valuable learning signal.

**Reward System** employs a rule-based paradigm where rewards derive directly from task outcomes rather than learned preference models. Different answer types require distinct verification strategies. For STEM questions from ViRL39K, a multi-stage verification pipeline extracts answers via flexible parsing (prioritizing structured tags like `<answer>` but falling back to heuristics), normalizes LaTeX artifacts, and performs symbolic equivalence checking for numerical reasoning so that $\frac{1}{2}$, 0.5, and $\frac{2}{4}$ all recognize as correct if the ground truth matches any. For multiple-choice problems, extracted option labels match against references while tolerating formatting variations. Coding benchmarks use token- and tag-level overlap with reference code, with UniSVG incorporating an SVG rendering similarity score in $[0, 1]$. Grounding data evaluates intersection-over-union between predicted and reference bounding boxes. Counting tasks require exact equality with the gold count. These answer-type-aware rules collapse into a single scalar reward per candidate response.

**Training Procedure** employs Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) as the core reinforcement learning algorithm, implemented through the AReaL asynchronous RL framework. AReaL decouples generation from training, allowing rollout workers to continuously generate data while trainer workers update the model in parallel, significantly improving GPU utilization compared to synchronous implementations. The optimization objective simplifies standard GRPO by omitting the KL divergence penalty, relying instead on PPO-style clipping to maintain training stability. The approach discards explicit format rewards commonly used to enforce structural constraints, relying solely on outcome-based correctness rewards.

A two-stage curriculum exploits the structure of the RL corpus. **Stage 1** trains exclusively on the normal split (19.9K instances, Figure 4(b)) where instructions ask the model to output only the final answer using the prompt "Put ONLY your final answer within `<answer></answer>`." This warm-up stage solidifies basic perceptual skills like counting, serving as a critical foundation for subsequent reasoning tasks and preventing the model from "over-thinking" simple problems. **Stage 2** switches to the long-reasoning split (49.2K instances, Figure 4(c)) and encourages explicit reasoning traces using the prompt "Think and solve the following question step by step. Please put your thinking and analysis procedure within `</think>`. Put ONLY your final answer within `<answer></answer>`." The reward computes only from content within `<answer></answer>`, ensuring the optimization target remains answer correctness while reasoning tokens serve as auxiliary guidance. To prevent forgetting short perception-heavy skills, Stage 2 interleaves a small proportion of normal-set examples into mini-batches, acting as an anchor that preserves competence on concise tasks while RL emphasizes deeper reasoning.

Table 1 quantifies the RL post-training impact. In "thinking" mode, the model achieves dramatic gains on WeMath (+7.9 percentage points), MathVision (+8.8), and MMMU-Provision (+10.5), demonstrating that RL-induced chain-of-thought capability effectively unlocks deeper problem-solving skills. On standard benchmarks like MMBench and DocVQA, RL maintains or slightly improves the already strong SFT performance, ensuring no regression in general capabilities while specializing in hard reasoning. Figure 5 extends this analysis to spatial reasoning and coding tasks, showing that RL significantly enhances fine-grained perception on tasks like SAT and Ref-L4, though "thinking" mode sometimes yields lower scores than "fast" mode on strictly perceptual metrics, suggesting verbose generation may occasionally interfere with precise coordinate regression.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the conceptual breakthroughs that distinguish LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 from prior multimodal training efforts. Rather than cataloging technical components already described in Section 3, we identify the underlying principles that make this work significant—and separate genuine innovations from incremental refinements.

### 4.1 Data Scaling Trumps Training Complexity

**The Claim.** The Introduction states: "we find that simply scaling data at the mid-training stage alone can produce state-of-the-art LMMs, eliminating the need for complex training paradigms." This challenges a prevailing assumption in the LMM literature that sophisticated multi-objective training recipes are necessary for competitive performance.

**What Makes This Different.** Prior works often introduce increasingly elaborate training objectives to squeeze out performance gains. For example, SigLIP2 (Tschannen et al., 2025) depends on "multiple specialized losses (SILC, TIPS, LocCa, and Sigmoid)" (Section 2.2). Molmo (Deitke et al., 2025) achieves strong results through "careful architectural choices, a refined training pipeline, and high-quality data" (Introduction). The implicit assumption is that training complexity correlates with capability.

LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 inverts this logic. Figure 6 shows performance across different mid-training data scales (4M vs. 85M samples) while holding the training pipeline constant. The 85M configuration consistently outperforms smaller scales across all ten benchmarks. This suggests that **data quality and quantity at the mid-training stage matter more than architectural sophistication**.

**Why This Matters.** This insight has practical and theoretical implications:

- **Reproducibility**: Simpler training pipelines are easier to debug, reproduce, and extend. Researchers can focus on data curation rather than tuning complex loss functions.
- **Cost Efficiency**: Complex multi-stage training with specialized objectives often requires more hyperparameter tuning and longer convergence times. A data-centric approach reduces experimental overhead.
- **Generalizability**: If performance scales with data quality rather than training tricks, the approach transfers more reliably across domains and model sizes.

**Incremental or Fundamental?** This is a **fundamental innovation** in training philosophy. While data scaling laws are well-known in language modeling, applying this principle to multimodal training—and demonstrating that it can replace complex training paradigms—represents a shift in how the community should think about LMM development. The ablation in Figure 6 provides direct empirical support rather than anecdotal claims.

### 4.2 Concept Balancing Without Caption Dependence

**The Claim.** Section 3.1 introduces a concept-balanced sampling strategy that "reduces reliance on caption quality" compared to MetaCLIP (Xu et al., 2024). This enables effective use of caption-free or interleaved datasets like SAM-1B, ImageNet-21K, and Obelics.

**What Makes This Different.** MetaCLIP depends on raw captions for concept matching. This creates a bottleneck: datasets with brief, noisy, or missing captions (common in large-scale web-scraped collections) cannot be effectively balanced. LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 instead adopts a **feature-based matching approach**:

1. Project images and MetaCLIP's 500K concept entries into a shared embedding space using pretrained encoders.
2. Assign each image to its top-$k$ nearest concepts based on cosine similarity.
3. Weight images by inverse concept frequencies and sample accordingly.

This decouples concept balancing from caption quality. An image from SAM-1B (which has segmentation masks but no captions) can still be assigned to semantic concepts based on visual features alone.

**Why This Matters.** Figure 3(a) shows the vocabulary coverage proportion before and after concept balancing—the distribution becomes noticeably smoother. More importantly, Figure 7 demonstrates that models trained on 2M concept-balanced data outperform those trained on 2M randomly-sampled data on **25 of 27 evaluated downstream benchmarks**. This validates that semantic diversity, not just raw sample count, drives performance.

The practical implication is substantial: researchers can now leverage caption-free datasets (which are far more abundant than high-quality captioned data) without sacrificing training signal quality. This expands the pool of usable pretraining data by orders of magnitude.

**Incremental or Fundamental?** This is an **incremental improvement** on MetaCLIP's methodology, but with outsized practical impact. The core algorithm (inverse-frequency weighting) remains similar, but the feature-based matching extension removes a critical limitation. For practitioners building multimodal datasets, this technique is immediately actionable and does not require novel infrastructure.

### 4.3 RL as Elicitation, Not Knowledge Injection

**The Claim.** Section 5.1 frames RL post-training as an "elicitation mechanism rather than knowledge injection." The discrepancy-driven data selection targets tasks where "the model possesses the latent capability to solve the task, as correct solutions do appear within its sampling distribution, yet its policy distribution fails to reliably assign high probability to the correct reasoning path."

**What Makes This Different.** Many RL post-training approaches treat reinforcement learning as a way to teach the model new capabilities. LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 takes a different view: the supervised fine-tuned model already knows how to solve these tasks, but its output distribution is poorly calibrated. RL redirects probability mass toward correct solutions the model can already generate.

The **Pass@N vs. Pass@1 gap** serves as the selection criterion. If Pass@N (sampling multiple responses) is high but Pass@1 (greedy decoding) is low, the model has latent capability that RL can elicit. This avoids wasting training signal on:
- **Trivial tasks** the model has already mastered (no gap to close)
- **Unsolvable tasks** beyond the model's current grasp (no latent capability to elicit)

**Why This Matters.** Table 1 shows the results: in "thinking" mode, RL post-training yields dramatic gains on WeMath (+7.9), MathVision (+8.8), and MMMU-Provision (+10.5). Crucially, on standard benchmarks like MMBench and DocVQA, RL "maintains or slightly improves the already strong SFT performance, ensuring no regression in general capabilities" (Section 6.6).

This selective elicitation approach has two advantages:

1. **Training Efficiency**: The 67K RL corpus is small compared to the 85M mid-training dataset, yet produces substantial gains on reasoning tasks. This makes RL post-training accessible to resource-constrained teams.
2. **Capability Preservation**: By avoiding tasks the model cannot solve, RL does not destabilize existing capabilities. The two-stage curriculum (answer-only warm-up followed by chain-of-thought) further prevents "over-thinking" on simple perceptual tasks (Section 5.3).

**Incremental or Fundamental?** This is a **fundamental innovation** in how RL post-training is conceptualized. Prior works often apply RL broadly without distinguishing between tasks where the model has latent capability versus those requiring new knowledge. The discrepancy-driven selection criterion provides a principled, measurable way to identify high-value RL targets. This reframing could influence how future works design RL curricula for LMMs.

---

### 4.4 Cost-Effective Training as a First-Class Objective

**The Claim.** The Abstract states training is achievable "within a $16,000 budget." Section 4.2 details the infrastructure optimizations enabling this: offline parallel data packing yielding "up to an 11× compression ratio on 85 million pretraining samples."

**What Makes This Different.** Most LMM papers treat computational cost as a secondary concern—something to report after achieving target performance. LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 makes cost efficiency a **primary design constraint** from the outset. This influences every technical decision:

- **Offline vs. Online Packing**: Online packing operates dynamically at runtime but introduces overhead. Offline packing processes datasets before training, ensuring uniform output lengths and eliminating runtime packing logic.
- **Native Resolution Training**: Rather than tiling images into fixed-size crops (which increases sequence length), the framework leverages RICE-ViT's 2D RoPE to handle variable resolutions directly.
- **Distributed Optimizer Parallelism**: The AIAK-Training-LLM framework (built on Megatron-LM) enables efficient training on 128 × A800 GPUs over 3.7 days for mid-training (Section 4.2).

**Why This Matters.** The $16,000 figure is not marketing—it represents a concrete barrier reduction. For context:

| Training Approach | Approximate Cost | Accessibility |
|-------------------|------------------|---------------|
| Proprietary LMMs | Millions of dollars | Corporate labs only |
| Typical Open LMMs | $100K–$500K+ | Well-funded universities |
| LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 | $16,000 | Individual research groups |

This democratization has cascading effects: more researchers can experiment with full training pipelines (not just fine-tuning), leading to faster iteration cycles and more diverse model variants.

**Incremental or Fundamental?** This is an **incremental engineering innovation** with fundamental implications. The individual techniques (data packing, distributed training, native resolution) are not novel in isolation. However, integrating them into a cohesive framework explicitly optimized for cost—and demonstrating state-of-the-art performance within that constraint—represents a meaningful contribution to reproducibility. The paper provides a blueprint for how to build competitive LMMs without massive computational resources.

---

### 4.5 Unified Vision Encoder Design

**The Claim.** Section 2.2 positions RICE-ViT as replacing "multiple specialized losses" with "a single cluster discrimination loss that simultaneously strengthens general understanding, OCR, and localization."

**What Makes This Different.** SigLIP2 and similar encoders require multiple loss functions targeting different capabilities (semantic understanding, localization, text recognition). RICE-ViT's region cluster discrimination loss unifies these objectives. The encoder is trained on 450M images and 2.4B candidate regions, learning region-aware attention and 2D RoPE jointly.

**Why This Matters.** Table 2 validates the design: at 560px resolution, RICE-ViT achieves 61.1% on OCR Avg (vs. 60.9% for SigLIPv2) and 70.8% on Other Avg (vs. 71.1% for SigLIPv2). When integrated into LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-3B, RICE-ViT shows average improvements of 1.9% in OCR & Document Understanding and 0.9% in General Vision Understanding compared to Qwen-ViT from Qwen2.5-VL-7B (Section 6.7.1).

The architectural simplification has practical benefits: fewer loss functions mean fewer hyperparameters to tune, easier debugging, and reduced risk of optimization conflicts between competing objectives.

**Incremental or Fundamental?** This is an **incremental improvement** on vision encoder design. RICE-ViT builds on prior region-aware representation learning work (Xie et al., 2025). The contribution here is demonstrating its effectiveness within an LMM training pipeline and validating it against strong baselines. For practitioners, the key takeaway is that RICE-ViT is a viable alternative to SigLIP with comparable performance and reduced complexity.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their claims through empirical evaluation. We analyze the experimental methodology, dissect the quantitative results, and critically assess whether the evidence supports the paper's central assertions about performance, efficiency, and reproducibility.

---

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Benchmark Selection and Categorization

The authors evaluate LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 across **27 distinct benchmarks** organized into four task categories (Section 6.1). This comprehensive evaluation strategy addresses a common weakness in LMM papers: cherry-picking benchmarks that favor specific model strengths.

**Category 1: General Visual Question Answering (VQA)**
This category tests broad multimodal understanding without specialized reasoning requirements. The benchmarks include:
- `MMStar` (Chen et al., 2024b): Evaluates core visual reasoning capabilities
- `MMBench` series (Fu et al., 2023): Tests perception and reasoning across multiple choice formats (English and Chinese variants)
- `MME-RealWorld` series (Zhang et al., 2025b): Assesses performance on high-resolution real-world scenarios
- `SeedBench` variants (Li et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024b): Measures generative comprehension
- `CV-Bench` (Tong et al., 2024): Computer vision-focused evaluation
- `RealWorldQA` (Corp., 2024): Practical question answering on real images

**Category 2: Multimodal Reasoning**
This category targets complex problem-solving requiring multi-step inference:
- `MathVista` (Lu et al., 2024): Mathematical reasoning in visual contexts
- `WeMath` (Qiao et al., 2025): Human-like mathematical reasoning evaluation
- `MathVision` (Wang et al., 2024a): Advanced visual mathematical problems
- `MMMU` (Yue et al., 2024) and `MMMU-Pro` series (Yue et al., 2025): Multi-discipline expert-level understanding

**Category 3: OCR & Chart Understanding**
This category evaluates text extraction and structured visual data interpretation:
- `ChartQA` (Masry et al., 2022): Question answering on charts
- `CharXiv` (Wang et al., 2024d): Scientific chart understanding
- `DocVQA` (Mathew et al., 2021): Document visual question answering
- `OCRBench` (Liu et al., 2024c): Optical character recognition benchmark
- `AI2D` (Kembhavi et al., 2016a): Diagram understanding
- `InfoVQA` (Mathew et al., 2022b): Infographic question answering

**Category 4: Others**
Specialized capabilities not covered above:
- `PixmoCount` (Deitke et al., 2025): Object counting
- `CountBench` (Paiss et al., 2023): Numerical counting accuracy
- `VL-RewardBench` (Li et al., 2025c): Vision-language reward modeling
- `V*` (Wu and Xie, 2024): Visual grounding and perception

#### Baseline Selection

The authors select **Qwen2.5-VL** as the primary comparison target for strategic reasons:

1. **Strongest Open Competitor**: Qwen2.5-VL represents one of the highest-performing openly-available model families, making it a meaningful benchmark rather than a weak baseline.

2. **Parameter-Matched Comparisons**: The paper compares models at similar scales:
   - `LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-8B` vs `Qwen2.5-VL-7B`
   - `LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-4B` vs `Qwen2.5-VL-3B`

3. **Additional Baselines**: Table 1 includes comparisons with earlier LLaVA variants (`LLaVA-OV-7B`) to demonstrate progression within the LLaVA family.

#### Evaluation Protocol

Section 6.1 states the authors use `LMMs-Eval` (Zhang et al., 2025a) with **default prompts** to ensure fair comparison. This is important because prompt engineering can significantly impact reported scores. All scores are reported as **accuracy percentages** unless otherwise specified (Table 1 caption).

The RL-enhanced models are evaluated in two modes:
- **Fast mode**: Standard inference without explicit reasoning chains
- **Thinking mode**: Model generates reasoning traces before final answer

This dual-mode evaluation clarifies when RL improvements come from better reasoning versus better perception.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies

The paper includes four ablation studies that validate key design choices. These are critical for understanding which components drive performance.

#### 5.3.1 Vision Encoder Comparison (Table 2)

Table 2 compares RICE-ViT against six alternative vision encoders within the LLaVA-NeXT framework, controlling for language model (Qwen2.5-7B), training data, and pipeline.

**Key Findings:**

At 336px resolution:
- RICE-ViT achieves **56.2% OCR Avg** vs 52.3% for CLIP (+3.9 points)
- RICE-ViT achieves **70.5% Other Avg** vs 67.6% for CLIP (+2.9 points)

At 560px resolution (highest tested):
- RICE-ViT achieves **61.1% OCR Avg** vs 60.9% for SigLIPv2 (+0.2 points)
- RICE-ViT achieves **70.8% Other Avg** vs 71.1% for SigLIPv2 (-0.3 points)

**Critical Observation:** At higher resolutions, SigLIPv2 slightly edges out RICE-ViT on general vision tasks (71.1% vs 70.8%), but RICE-ViT maintains competitive OCR performance. However, when integrated into the full LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-3B model (last row of Table 2), RICE-ViT achieves **73.4% Other Avg** vs 72.5% for Qwen-ViT from Qwen2.5-VL-7B.

This suggests RICE-ViT's advantage emerges more clearly when jointly trained with the full LMM pipeline rather than as a frozen encoder. The region-aware attention and 2D RoPE likely benefit from end-to-end optimization.

**Assessment:** The ablation convincingly supports the encoder choice, though the margins are modest (1-4 percentage points). The real advantage may be architectural simplicity—one unified loss versus SigLIP2's four specialized losses (Section 2.2).

#### 5.3.2 Mid-Training Data Scaling (Figure 6)

Figure 6 compares models trained with different mid-training data scales: 0M (no mid-training), 4M, and 85M samples. All models undergo identical Stage-1 alignment and Stage-2 instruction tuning.

**Results Across 10 Benchmarks:**

The 85M configuration outperforms 4M on all benchmarks shown. Representative examples:
- `MMStar`: 85M achieves ~67% vs 4M at ~63% (+4 points)
- `MMBench`: 85M achieves ~83% vs 4M at ~80% (+3 points)
- `MathVista`: 85M achieves ~68% vs 4M at ~64% (+4 points)

The 0M configuration (native pre-training without mid-training) performs worst across all benchmarks, typically 5-8 percentage points below the 85M configuration.

**Assessment:** This ablation directly supports the paper's central claim that "simply scaling data at the mid-training stage alone can produce state-of-the-art LMMs" (Introduction). The consistent improvement across all 10 benchmarks with no exceptions makes this one of the most convincing findings.

#### 5.3.3 Concept Balancing Effectiveness (Figure 7)

Figure 7 compares models trained on 2M concept-balanced samples versus 2M randomly-sampled data. The SFT data is held constant (LLaVA-NeXT-780k).

**Results:**
- Concept-balanced data outperforms random sampling on **25 of 27 benchmarks**
- Average improvement is approximately 2-3 percentage points across most benchmarks

**Assessment:** This validates the concept-balancing methodology from Section 3.1. The 25/27 win rate is statistically compelling—it would be extremely unlikely to occur by chance. This supports the claim that semantic diversity matters more than raw sample count.

#### 5.3.4 Instruction Data Quality (Figure 8)

Figure 8 compares three instruction datasets during SFT:

**Assessment:** This ablation supports the value of data aggregation and deduplication. However, the paper does not report the exact performance margins, making it difficult to assess practical significance. Figure 8 shows bar charts but exact percentages are not provided in the text.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

#### Claim 1: "Outperforms Qwen2.5-VL on 18 of 27 Benchmarks"

**Verdict: Supported, with caveats.**

Table 1 confirms the 18/27 count for the 8B model. However:
- On General VQA alone, Qwen2.5-VL-7B averages 72.6% vs 71.8% for LLaVA-OV-1.5-8B
- The advantage comes primarily from Reasoning tasks (51.8% vs 46.8% with RL)
- On OCR tasks, the models tie at 84.6% average

The claim is technically accurate but could mislead readers into thinking LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 dominates across all categories. The reality is more nuanced: it excels at reasoning, ties on OCR, and slightly trails on general VQA without RL.

#### Claim 2: "$16,000 Training Budget"

**Verdict: Plausible but incompletely documented.**

Section 4.2 states mid-training uses "128 × A800 GPUs over 3.7 days." However:
- The paper does not break down costs for Stage-1 (558K samples) and Stage-2 (22M + FineVision)
- Cloud pricing for A800 GPUs varies significantly by provider and region
- The $16,000 figure likely assumes specific cloud rates not disclosed

For reproducibility, readers need:
- Hourly GPU rates assumed
- Stage-by-stage cost breakdown
- Whether costs include data storage, networking, and checkpointing

**Assessment:** The claim is directionally useful for understanding scale but should not be treated as a precise budget for replication.

#### Claim 3: "RL Elicits Latent Capabilities"

**Verdict: Strongly supported.**

The discrepancy-driven selection criterion (Pass@N vs Pass@1 gap) provides a principled basis for this claim. The dramatic gains on reasoning tasks (+6.0% average, +10.5% on MMMU-Pro-vision) with minimal SFT regression support the elicitation framing.

The DocVQA regression (95.0% → 91.9%) is a notable exception, suggesting RL can sometimes degrade capabilities it was not explicitly optimized to preserve. The two-stage curriculum (Section 5.3) partially mitigates this by interleaving answer-only samples in Stage 2, but does not eliminate it.

#### Claim 4: "Concept Balancing Improves Performance"

**Verdict: Convincingly demonstrated.**

Figure 7 shows 25/27 benchmark wins for concept-balanced vs random sampling. This is one of the cleanest ablations in the paper—same data volume, different sampling strategy, consistent improvements.

The mechanism (inverse-frequency weighting based on feature-matched concepts) is well-explained in Section 3.1 and does not require caption quality, enabling use of caption-free datasets.

### 5.6 Limitations and Missing Analyses

#### 5.6.1 Compute-Performance Trade-off Curve

The paper claims $16,000 training cost but does not show a **Pareto frontier** of cost versus performance. Readers cannot determine:
- Would $8,000 (4M samples) achieve 90% of the performance?
- Would $32,000 yield diminishing returns?

Figure 6 partially addresses this by comparing 4M vs 85M samples, but does not translate sample counts to dollar costs.

#### 5.6.2 Failure Mode Analysis

The paper does not systematically analyze **where LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 fails**. For example:
- What types of images cause the model to hallucinate?
- Which reasoning patterns does RL fail to elicit?
- Are there demographic or cultural biases in the 85M dataset?

Section 6.6 notes RL can hurt perceptual metrics (Ref-L4 IoU) but does not explore why or how to mitigate this beyond the two-stage curriculum.

#### 5.6.3 Reproducibility Details

While the paper releases code and data, some training details remain unspecified:
- Learning rates and schedules for each stage
- Gradient clipping thresholds
- Weight decay values
- Warmup proportions

These hyperparameters significantly impact reproducibility. The paper states it uses AIAK-Training-LLM (built on Megatron-LM) but does not specify all optimizer settings.

#### 5.6.4 Comparison to Proprietary Models

The Introduction criticizes proprietary models for lacking transparency, yet the paper does not compare against them on equal footing. Table 1 includes only open models. A comparison with GPT-4V or Gemini (even using public APIs) would contextualize how close LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 comes to the absolute state-of-the-art.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the assumptions, constraints, and unresolved weaknesses in LLaVA-OneVision-1.5. While the paper demonstrates impressive results within its stated budget, several limitations warrant careful consideration before adopting this framework for production or research purposes.

---

### 6.1 Incomplete Cost Transparency

The headline claim of training "within a $16,000 budget" (Abstract) lacks sufficient documentation for independent verification. Section 4.2 states that mid-training uses "128 × A800 GPUs over 3.7 days," but critical details remain unspecified:

- **No Stage-by-Stage Breakdown**: The $16,000 figure appears to cover primarily Stage-1.5 (mid-training on 85M samples). Costs for Stage-1 (558K samples) and Stage-2 (22M + FineVision) are not itemized. Given that Stage-2 involves full-parameter training on 46M instruction samples, this represents non-trivial additional expense.

- **Cloud Pricing Assumptions Undisclosed**: A800 GPU hourly rates vary significantly by provider, region, and spot versus on-demand pricing. The paper does not state:
  - Assumed hourly rate per A800 GPU
  - Whether costs include data storage, networking egress, and checkpointing
  - Whether failed training runs or hyperparameter tuning are factored in

- **Reproducibility Barrier**: Without explicit cost breakdowns, research groups attempting replication cannot budget accurately. A team assuming $16,000 total may encounter costs 2–3× higher depending on their cloud provider.

**Assessment**: The $16,000 figure serves as a useful order-of-magnitude indicator but should not be treated as a precise replication budget. The paper would benefit from a cost table similar to its performance tables.

---

### 6.2 RL Post-Training Degrades Certain Capabilities

While RL post-training delivers dramatic gains on reasoning tasks, it introduces measurable regressions on other capabilities. This trade-off is underemphasized in the paper's narrative.

**Documented Degradations:**

| Benchmark | SFT Baseline | RL-Enhanced | Change |
|-----------|--------------|-------------|--------|
| DocVQA | 95.0% | 91.9% | **-3.1%** |
| Ref-L4 (IoU) | 57.2% (Fast) | 55.8% (Thinking) | **-1.4%** |
| RefCOCO (IoU) | 68.8% (Fast) | 60.0% (Thinking) | **-8.8%** |
| WebCode (short) | 58.3% (Fast) | 55.7% (Thinking) | **-2.6%** |

These results appear in Table 1 and Figure 5, yet Section 6.6 frames RL as maintaining "or slightly improving the already strong SFT performance" on standard benchmarks. The DocVQA regression directly contradicts this claim.

**Why This Happens:**

Section 6.6 offers a partial explanation: "verbose generation may occasionally interfere with precise coordinate regression." The chain-of-thought reasoning introduced in Stage 2 of RL training appears to optimize the model for multi-step inference at the expense of direct perception tasks.

**Practical Implication:**

Practitioners cannot assume the RL-enhanced model is universally superior. Deployment decisions should be task-specific:

- **Use RL (Thinking) mode for**: Math reasoning, complex document analysis, code generation requiring structural planning
- **Use SFT or RL (Fast) mode for**: OCR extraction, bounding box prediction, simple counting tasks

The paper does not provide guidance on how to select between modes programmatically, leaving this decision to end users.

### 6.3 Dependency on External Pretrained Encoders

The concept-balancing methodology (Section 3.1) relies on "pretrained MetaCLIP-H/14-Full-CC2.5B encoders" to project images and concepts into a shared embedding space. This introduces a hidden dependency:

- **MetaCLIP Availability**: MetaCLIP itself is a large-scale pretrained model. Researchers without access to these encoders cannot replicate the concept-balancing pipeline.

- **Concept Vocabulary Limitations**: The method uses MetaCLIP's "500K concept entries." Domains outside this vocabulary (specialized medical imaging, niche scientific diagrams, cultural artifacts from underrepresented regions) may not benefit from balancing.

- **Feature Matching Quality**: The approach assumes MetaCLIP embeddings provide meaningful semantic similarity. If MetaCLIP has biases or blind spots, these propagate into the balanced dataset.

Figure 10 in Appendix B shows that source datasets exhibit "pronounced long-tail bias," and while concept balancing smooths the distribution (Figure 3a), the paper does not analyze whether certain domains remain underrepresented after balancing.

**Assessment**: The concept-balancing technique is powerful but not fully self-contained. It shifts dependency from caption quality to encoder quality—a different but still present constraint.

---

### 6.4 Limited Failure Mode Analysis

The paper does not systematically analyze where LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 fails. This omission limits understanding of the model's reliability boundaries:

- **No Hallucination Analysis**: The paper does not report rates of object hallucination, text hallucination in OCR tasks, or fabricated reasoning steps in math problems.

- **No Bias Examination**: The 85M mid-training dataset aggregates sources including LAION-CN, COYO-700M, and Obelics—all known to contain demographic and cultural biases. The paper does not evaluate whether concept balancing mitigates or amplifies these biases.

- **No Adversarial Robustness**: There is no evaluation of how the model performs on adversarially perturbed images, out-of-distribution domains, or intentionally confusing prompts.

- **No Error Categorization**: When the model fails on MMMU-Pro-vision (65.3% error rate even with RL), the paper does not analyze whether failures stem from visual misperception, reasoning errors, or knowledge gaps.

**Comparison to Prior Work**: Molmo (Deitke et al., 2025) includes user preference evaluations and qualitative failure analysis. LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 focuses exclusively on benchmark accuracy, which may not capture real-world reliability.

---

### 6.5 Scalability Constraints at Extreme Resolutions

While RICE-ViT supports "variable input resolutions without requiring resolution-specific fine-tuning" (Section 2.2), the paper does not discuss computational constraints at extreme scales:

- **Memory Implications**: Native-resolution processing means sequence length scales with image size. The paper specifies 8K context length during mid-training but does not state maximum supported image resolution or memory consumption at that limit.

- **Inference Latency**: High-resolution images produce more visual tokens, increasing attention computation quadratically. The paper reports training efficiency but not inference latency across resolution ranges.

- **Batch Size Trade-offs**: Training at native resolution likely requires smaller batch sizes for large images. The paper does not report how batch size varies with resolution or whether gradient accumulation is used.

**Practical Concern**: A research group with limited GPU memory may not achieve the reported efficiency gains if their hardware cannot accommodate native-resolution batches.

---

### 6.6 RL Corpus Scope Limitations

The RL post-training stage uses only 67K instances (Section 5.1), which is approximately 0.08% of the 85M mid-training dataset. This small scale introduces constraints:

- **Narrow Capability Coverage**: The corpus covers STEM, grounding, counting, spatial reasoning, coding, OCR, and diagrams. Capabilities outside these domains (creative image description, emotional understanding, artistic critique) receive no RL optimization.

- **Domain Specificity**: Sources include ViRL, WebCode2M, UniSVG, Ref-L4, VigoRL-SA, VigoRL-SAT, Pixmo-Count, AI2D, and InfoVQA. These are predominantly academic benchmarks. Real-world applications (medical diagnosis, legal document review, industrial inspection) are not represented.

- **Language Coverage**: The mid-training dataset includes 20M Chinese and 65M English samples (Section 3.1), but the RL corpus composition by language is not specified. RL benefits may not transfer equally across languages.

**Assessment**: The lightweight RL approach is efficient but narrow. It should be viewed as a targeted enhancement for reasoning tasks rather than a general capability booster.

### 6.8 No Comparison to Proprietary Models

**Why This Matters:**

- **Contextualizes Performance**: Readers cannot determine how close LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 comes to absolute state-of-the-art. Is 35.7% on MMMU-Pro-vision competitive with proprietary models, or still far behind?

- **Validates the Open Approach**: If LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 achieves 80% of GPT-4V's performance at 1% of the cost, this strengthens the democratization argument. Without comparison, the claim remains unverified.

- **Identifies Remaining Gaps**: Understanding where proprietary models still excel would guide future open-source research priorities.

**Assessment**: This omission is a strategic choice—proprietary model APIs change frequently, and benchmark access may be restricted. However, it limits the paper's ability to position LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 within the broader LMM landscape.

### 6.9 Two-Stage RL Curriculum Complexity

The RL training procedure (Section 5.3) employs a two-stage curriculum with interleaved samples:

1. **Stage 1**: Answer-only training on 19.9K instances
2. **Stage 2**: Chain-of-thought training on 49.2K instances with interleaved answer-only samples

While Section 6.6 states this "prevents the model from forgetting short, perception-heavy skills," the paper does not ablate this design:

- **No Single-Stage Baseline**: Would training only on chain-of-thought data achieve similar reasoning gains with less complexity?

- **Interleaving Ratio Undisclosed**: The paper states "a small proportion of normal-set examples" are interleaved in Stage 2 but does not specify the exact ratio (5%? 10%? 20%?).

- **Curriculum Sensitivity**: The paper does not explore whether reversing the order (chain-of-thought first, then answer-only) would work, or whether the two stages could be merged with mixed prompts.

**Assessment**: The curriculum design is empirically motivated but not rigorously validated. Practitioners implementing this approach must treat the interleaving ratio as a tunable hyperparameter.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 reshapes the multimodal AI landscape, what research trajectories it opens, and how practitioners should decide when to adopt this framework. The implications extend beyond incremental performance gains—they challenge fundamental assumptions about how competitive LMMs are built and who can build them.

---

### 7.1 Landscape Shifts: Democratizing State-of-the-Art Training

LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 represents a meaningful inflection point in multimodal AI development. The work demonstrates that **competitive performance no longer requires proprietary-scale budgets**, fundamentally altering the accessibility equation for LMM research.

#### The Cost Barrier Collapses

The $16,000 training budget claim (Abstract, Section 4.2) is not merely a technical achievement—it redefines who can participate in LMM development. Consider the practical implications:

| Training Tier | Approximate Cost | Who Can Access |
|---------------|------------------|----------------|
| Proprietary LMMs | $1M–$10M+ | Corporate labs only |
| Prior Open LMMs | $100K–$500K | Well-funded universities |
| LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 | $16,000 | Individual research groups, small startups |

This cost reduction enables several previously impossible scenarios:

- **Academic Research Groups**: A single faculty member with modest grant funding can now train competitive LMMs from scratch rather than relying on fine-tuning existing weights.

- **Domain Specialization**: Medical imaging teams, legal document analysts, or scientific diagram researchers can train domain-specific variants without licensing restrictions or API costs.

- **Iterative Experimentation**: The low cost enables rapid iteration—researchers can test architectural modifications, data curation strategies, or training objectives without committing months of compute budget.

#### Data-Centric Philosophy Gains Validation

The finding that "simply scaling data at the mid-training stage alone can produce state-of-the-art LMMs" (Introduction) challenges a prevailing assumption in the field. Prior works often introduced increasingly elaborate training objectives to extract performance gains. LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 demonstrates that **data quality and quantity at the mid-training stage matter more than architectural sophistication**.

Figure 6 provides direct empirical support: models trained with 85M mid-training samples consistently outperform those trained with 4M samples across all ten benchmarks, with no exceptions. This validates a data-centric training philosophy that has theoretical support in language modeling but lacked comprehensive demonstration in multimodal contexts.

The implication is strategic: research groups should prioritize data curation pipelines over complex loss function engineering. This shift has cascading effects:

- **Reproducibility Improves**: Simpler training pipelines are easier to debug and replicate.
- **Resource Allocation Changes**: Budget shifts from compute-heavy experimentation to data collection and cleaning.
- **Evaluation Focus Evolves**: Benchmark performance becomes more sensitive to data composition than training tricks.

#### Open Asset Release Sets New Standards

Unlike works that release only model weights, LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 provides complete reproducibility assets:

- **85M concept-balanced pretraining dataset** (`LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-Mid-Traning`)
- **22M instruction dataset** (`LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-Instruct`)
- **67K RL post-training corpus** (`LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-RL-Data`)
- **Training framework code** (offline parallel data packing, AIAK-Training-LLM integration)
- **Model checkpoints** (both base and instruct variants)

This comprehensive release establishes a new baseline for what "open-source" should mean in the LMM community. Future works will face increased pressure to match this level of transparency—or justify why they cannot.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper's contributions open several promising research trajectories. Below we identify high-value directions with concrete starting points.

#### 7.2.1 Data Scaling Laws for Multimodal Training

**Open Question**: What is the optimal mid-training data scale for different model sizes and budgets?

Figure 6 compares only three points (0M, 4M, 85M). A systematic scaling study would enable practitioners to make informed budget-performance trade-offs:

- **Suggested Experiment**: Train models at 8M, 16M, 32M, and 85M mid-training samples while holding all other factors constant. Plot performance versus cost to identify diminishing returns.

- **Hypothesis**: Performance gains may follow a logarithmic curve, with 85M representing a point where additional data yields marginal improvements relative to cost.

- **Practical Value**: A research group with $8,000 budget could determine whether training on 40M samples achieves 90% of the 85M performance.

#### 7.2.2 RL Curriculum Optimization

**Open Question**: Can the two-stage RL curriculum be simplified or improved?

Section 5.3 describes a two-stage curriculum (answer-only warm-up followed by chain-of-thought), but Section 6.9 notes this design lacks ablation validation. Follow-up work should explore:

- **Single-Stage Baseline**: Train only on chain-of-thought data with mixed prompts. Does this achieve similar reasoning gains with less complexity?

- **Interleaving Ratio**: The paper states "a small proportion of normal-set examples" are interleaved in Stage 2 but does not specify the exact ratio. Systematic variation (5%, 10%, 20%) would identify optimal values.

- **Curriculum Order**: Would reversing the order (chain-of-thought first, then answer-only) work? This tests whether the warm-up stage is truly necessary.

- **Task-Specific RL**: Instead of a unified RL corpus, train separate RL policies for different task categories (reasoning vs. perception). This could mitigate the DocVQA regression observed in Table 1 (95.0% → 91.9%).

#### 7.2.3 Domain-Specific Adaptations

**Open Question**: How does the LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 framework transfer to specialized domains?

The 85M mid-training dataset aggregates general web sources (COYO-700M, Obelics, LAION-CN, etc.). Domain specialists should investigate:

- **Medical Imaging**: Replace general pretraining data with radiology images and clinical reports. The concept-balancing methodology (Section 3.1) could ensure coverage across anatomical regions and pathologies.

- **Scientific Diagrams**: Curate data from arXiv papers, textbooks, and educational materials. The RICE-ViT encoder's region-aware attention (Section 2.2) should excel at diagram component identification.

- **Industrial Inspection**: Train on manufacturing defect images with detailed annotations. The native-resolution support could capture fine-grained visual cues missed by tiled approaches.

**Starting Point**: Use the released training framework with domain-specific data substitution. The concept-balancing pipeline requires only MetaCLIP encoders (publicly available) and can be applied to any image collection.

#### 7.2.4 Vision Encoder Comparisons at Scale

**Open Question**: Does RICE-ViT's advantage persist at larger model scales?

Table 2 validates RICE-ViT at the 3B–8B scale, showing modest improvements over SigLIPv2 (1–4 percentage points). However, encoder importance may change with model size:

- **Hypothesis**: At larger scales (70B+), the LLM's capacity may compensate for weaker vision encoders, reducing RICE-ViT's relative advantage.

- **Suggested Experiment**: Compare RICE-ViT, SigLIPv2, and Qwen-ViT encoders with a 70B language backbone. Measure whether encoder choice matters less as LLM capacity increases.

- **Alternative Hypothesis**: Region-aware encoding becomes more valuable at larger scales because the LLM can better exploit fine-grained visual features.

This research direction has practical implications for teams deciding whether to invest in custom vision encoder pretraining versus using off-the-shelf encoders.

#### 7.2.5 Bias and Fairness Analysis

**Open Question**: Does concept balancing mitigate or amplify demographic and cultural biases in source datasets?

The 85M mid-training dataset aggregates sources known to contain biases (LAION, COYO, Obelics). Section 6.4 notes the paper does not evaluate bias propagation. Follow-up work should:

- **Audit Concept Distribution**: Analyze whether certain demographic groups, geographic regions, or cultural contexts remain underrepresented after concept balancing.

- **Measure Bias in Outputs**: Evaluate model responses on bias benchmarks (e.g., occupation-gender associations, racial stereotype tests).

- **Compare Balancing Strategies**: Test whether feature-based concept balancing (Section 3.1) produces fairer outcomes than caption-based approaches like MetaCLIP.

This research is critical for responsible deployment, particularly in high-stakes applications like hiring, lending, or healthcare.

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision: Toward Fully Open Multimodal AI

LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 points toward a future where state-of-the-art multimodal capabilities are accessible to any research group with modest resources. The work demonstrates three critical enablers:

1. **Efficient Training Infrastructure**: Offline data packing and distributed optimization reduce costs by an order of magnitude.

2. **Data-Centric Methodology**: Concept balancing and mid-training scaling provide predictable performance gains without complex training objectives.

3. **Complete Asset Release**: Full reproducibility—from raw data to final checkpoints—enables true community building rather than weight-sharing alone.

**The Next Frontier**: If this trajectory continues, we may see:

- **$1,000 LMMs**: Further optimization could bring competitive training costs below $10K, enabling individual researchers to train from scratch.

- **Domain-Specific Model Ecosystems**: Rather than one general model, communities may maintain specialized variants for medicine, law, science, and education.

- **Continuous Community Training**: Open frameworks could support ongoing model improvement through community-contributed data, similar to open-source software development.

**Caveat**: Realizing this vision requires addressing the limitations identified in Section 6—particularly hyperparameter transparency, failure mode analysis, and bias evaluation. The released assets provide a foundation, but the community must build upon them with rigorous evaluation standards.

**Final Assessment**: LLaVA-OneVision-1.5 does not solve all challenges in multimodal AI. However, it establishes a new baseline for what open, reproducible LMM development should look like. For practitioners and researchers, the question is no longer "Can we afford to train competitive multimodal models?" but rather "What unique value can we add with accessible training infrastructure?" This shift—from scarcity to abundance—is the work's most significant contribution to the field.
