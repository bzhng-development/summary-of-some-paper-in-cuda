## 1. Executive Summary

This paper establishes non-trivial bounds for sums of GL(3) Fourier coefficients $\Lambda(1, n)$ and the triple divisor function $d_3(n)$ over mixed power polynomials $n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^k$ and binary quadratic forms $Q(n_1, n_2)$ with varying variable sizes. The authors prove bounds of $X^{7/8+\epsilon}Y$ for $k=3$ (Theorem 1) and $X^{7/4+\epsilon}$ for quadratic forms (Theorem 2), improving upon prior results by Zhou and Hu [27] and Chanana and Singh [4] through strategic deviations from the traditional Duke-Friedlander-Iwaniec (DFI) delta method. These findings advance the understanding of higher-rank automorphic forms over sparse sequences, addressing challenges posed by significant oscillations and reduced summation ranges in analytic number theory.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of this paper, we must first situate it within the broader landscape of analytic number theory. The central quest in this field is often to understand the distribution of arithmetic functions—functions that encode number-theoretic properties like divisibility or primality—when evaluated over specific sequences of integers.

### The Fundamental Problem: Summatory Functions Over Polynomials

The paper begins by defining the core object of study: the summatory function. Let $A(n)$ be an arithmetical function. Researchers seek asymptotic formulae for the sum:
$$
S(X) := \sum_{n \le X} A(n).
$$
When $A(n)$ is summed over all integers up to $X$, classical tools often suffice. However, the problem becomes significantly harder when we sum over **sparse sequences**, such as the values taken by a polynomial $P(n)$. The Introduction (Section 1) highlights that investigating sums of the form:
$$
\sum_{n \le X} d_\ell(P(n)),
$$
where $d_\ell(n)$ is the $\ell$-th divisor function, is a central problem. For example, Hooley [12] established results for $d(n^2 + a)$, and Friedlander and Iwaniec [7] famously proved the infinitude of primes of the form $x^2 + y^4$.

This paper addresses a specific gap in this lineage: **What happens when the arithmetic function $A(n)$ is not a standard divisor function, but rather the Fourier coefficient of a higher-rank automorphic form?**

### The Challenge of Higher-Rank Automorphic Forms

Most existing literature focuses on either classical divisor functions or Fourier coefficients of **GL(2)** cusp forms (modular forms). For instance, Blomer [2] and Acharya [1] established bounds for sums involving GL(2) coefficients $\lambda_F(n)$ over quadratic polynomials.

This paper targets **GL(3)** Hecke-Maass cusp forms. Let $\phi$ be such a form on the group $SL(3, \mathbb{Z})$. It has normalized Fourier coefficients denoted by $\Lambda(m, n)$. The paper focuses on the coefficients $\Lambda(1, n)$.

There are two main reasons why extending results from GL(2) or $d_3(n)$ to GL(3) coefficients is non-trivial:
1.  **Oscillatory Nature:** The Introduction (Section 1) explicitly states that the function $\Lambda(1, n)$ "exhibits significant oscillations." Unlike the divisor function $d_3(n)$, which is always positive, Fourier coefficients of cusp forms change sign unpredictably. Canceling these oscillations over a sparse sequence requires more delicate analytic techniques.
2.  **Rank Complexity:** GL(3) forms belong to a higher rank group than GL(2). Their associated L-functions and Voronoi summation formulae (discussed in Section 2) are more complex, involving multiple parameters (Langlands parameters $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \alpha_3$) and more intricate Kloosterman sums.

### The Specific Gap: Mixed Powers and Varying Sizes

The paper identifies two specific structural gaps in the existing literature that it aims to fill:

1.  **Mixed Powers with GL(3) Coefficients:**
    Prior work by Zhou and Hu [27] established an asymptotic formula for the triple divisor function $d_3(n)$ over mixed powers $n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^k$ (Equation 1.3 in Section 1). They achieved a bound of $O(X^{1 + 1/k - \delta(k) + \epsilon})$.
    *   **The Gap:** There were no corresponding non-trivial bounds for the GL(3) Fourier coefficients $\Lambda(1, n)$ over these same mixed power sequences.
    *   **The Goal:** Theorem 1 seeks to establish bounds for $\Lambda(1, n)$ (and $d_3(n)$) over $n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^k$ that are stronger than or comparable to the divisor function results, despite the oscillatory nature of $\Lambda$.

2.  **Binary Quadratic Forms with Unequal Variable Sizes:**
    Previous work by the same authors (Chanana and Singh [4]) considered sums of $\Lambda(1, Q(n_1, n_2))$ where the variables $n_1$ and $n_2$ were of the same size (Equation 1.6 in Section 1), achieving a bound of $O(X^{2 - 1/68 + \epsilon})$.
    *   **The Gap:** Real-world Diophantine problems often involve variables of different magnitudes. The prior result did not account for $n_1 \le X$ and $n_2 \le Y$ where $Y = X^\theta$ with $\theta < 1$.
    *   **The Goal:** Theorem 2 refines the bound to accommodate $Y = X^\theta$ for $3/4 < \theta \le 1$. Remark (iv) in Section 1 notes that this is effectively a "twist of the Gauss circle problem by the sequence $\{\Lambda(1, n)\}$," connecting modern automorphic form theory to classical geometry of numbers.

### Methodological Positioning: The DFI Delta Method

To tackle these problems, the authors must choose an analytic engine. The Introduction (Section 1) notes that for divisor functions, most findings use the **classical circle method**. However, for automorphic forms, the **Duke-Friedlander-Iwaniec (DFI) delta method** is often preferred.

The DFI method is a technique used to detect the equality condition $n = m$ in sums. It expands the delta symbol $\delta(n, m)$ (which is 1 if $n=m$ and 0 otherwise) into a Fourier integral involving Kloosterman sums. This allows the separation of variables that are otherwise tangled inside a polynomial argument.

**Where Existing Methods Fall Short:**
The paper argues that a straightforward application of the traditional DFI method is insufficient for these specific bounds. In Remark (i) of Section 1, the authors highlight two critical deviations they must make:
*   **For Theorem 1 (Mixed Powers):** They do **not** use the summation formula (Voronoi) on the variable of smaller size ($n_3$). Applying Voronoi to the smallest variable can sometimes introduce more error than it removes in mixed-power settings.
*   **For Theorem 2 (Quadratic Forms):** They do **not** apply the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality in the conventional approach. Standard Cauchy-Schwarz applications might lose too much precision when variables have varying sizes ($X$ vs $Y$).

### Summary of Motivation

In essence, this paper is motivated by the need to push the boundaries of where we understand higher-rank automorphic forms.
*   **Theoretical Significance:** It extends the "twist of the Gauss circle problem" from GL(2) to GL(3), testing how well these highly oscillatory coefficients average out over geometric shapes (circles/quadratics) and mixed-power surfaces.
*   **Technical Advancement:** It demonstrates modifications to the DFI delta method (specifically regarding which variables to apply summation formulae to and how to handle Cauchy-Schwarz) that allow for tighter bounds in sparse sequence settings.
*   **Improvement:** It aims to improve upon the specific exponents found in Zhou and Hu [27] for $d_3(n)$ and their own prior work [4] for $\Lambda(1, n)$, specifically by handling the complexity of $k \ge 3$ mixed powers and unequal variable ranges $Y = X^\theta$.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an analytic number theory paper that uses harmonic analysis techniques to bound sums of oscillatory arithmetic functions over sparse polynomial sequences, with the core idea being a modified Duke-Friedlander-Iwaniec (DFI) delta method that strategically avoids applying summation formulae to certain variables to preserve cancellation.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The "system" being built is an analytic machinery that transforms a difficult sum over polynomial values into a collection of simpler exponential sums and integrals that can be individually bounded. The problem it solves is detecting when a polynomial expression $n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^k$ equals an integer $r$ so that Fourier coefficients $\Lambda(1, r)$ can be extracted and analyzed, with the solution shape being a multi-stage transformation pipeline that separates variables through Fourier expansion, applies duality formulae to oscillatory coefficients, and then recombines everything with careful error tracking.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The technical approach consists of five major components arranged in a sequential pipeline. **Component 1 (Delta Method Expansion)** takes the original sum $S_k(X)$ and introduces a delta symbol $\delta(r, n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^k)$ to detect equality, then expands this delta into a Fourier integral over modulus $q$ and continuous parameter $u$ using Lemma 8 from Section 3. **Component 2 (Voronoi Summation on $r$-sum)** applies the GL(3) Voronoi formula (Lemma 2, Section 2) to the sum over $r$ containing the Fourier coefficients $\Lambda(1, r)$, transforming it into a dual sum over new variables $n, m$ with Kloosterman sums and oscillatory integrals $G_\pm$. **Component 3 (Poisson Summation on $n_1, n_2$)** applies Poisson summation (Lemma 5, Section 2) to the sums over $n_1$ and $n_2$, converting them into character sums $C(m_1, m_2, a, q)$ and integral transforms $J(m_1, m_2, u, q)$. **Component 4 (Character Sum Estimation)** analyzes the resulting character sums $C_1$ by splitting into zero-frequency ($m=0$) and non-zero frequency ($m \neq 0$) cases, applying Weil bounds and Dąbrowski-Fisher techniques from Section 4.6. **Component 5 (Integral Analysis and Final Assembly)** bounds all oscillatory integrals using stationary phase and integration by parts (Lemma 9, Section 4.3), then combines all bounds through Cauchy-Schwarz inequality (Section 4.4) to produce the final estimate for $S_k(X)$.

Information flows from the original sum through delta expansion, then splits into parallel tracks for Voronoi and Poisson transformations, reconverges at the character sum analysis stage, and finally passes through integral estimation before producing the bound.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- First, I explain the DFI delta method expansion because it is the foundational transformation that enables all subsequent analysis by separating the polynomial constraint from the arithmetic function.
- Second, I detail the Voronoi summation application because this is where the GL(3) Fourier coefficients are transformed into a tractable dual form, and understanding this duality is essential for seeing where cancellation comes from.
- Third, I walk through the Poisson summation on the polynomial variables because this converts the geometric constraint (sums of squares) into character sums that can be bounded algebraically.
- Fourth, I analyze the character sum estimation because this is where the most technical number-theoretic input occurs, including the zero-frequency versus non-zero frequency split that determines the final exponent.
- Fifth, I explain the integral analysis and Cauchy-Schwarz application because this is where all the pieces are recombined and the final bound is extracted through careful optimization of parameters.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Foundational Setup and Delta Method Expansion**

The paper begins with a sum of the form $S_k(X) = \sum_{n_1, n_2 \leq X^{1/2}} \sum_{n_3 \leq Y} A(n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^k) a(n_3)$ where $Y = X^{1/k}$ with $k \geq 3$, and $A(n)$ is either the GL(3) Fourier coefficient $\Lambda(1, n)$ or the triple divisor function $d_3(n)$. The trivial bound for this sum is $S_k(X) \ll X^{1 + 1/k + \epsilon}$, which comes from simply bounding each term by its maximum size and counting the number of terms. The goal is to beat this trivial bound by exploiting cancellation in the oscillatory coefficients.

The first technical move is to introduce a delta symbol to detect the equality $r = n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^k$. The delta symbol $\delta(x)$ is defined to equal 1 when $x = 0$ and 0 otherwise, so $\delta(r - (n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^k))$ detects when the polynomial equals $r$. The DFI delta method (Lemma 8, Section 3) provides a Fourier expansion of this delta symbol:
$$
\delta(n - m) = \frac{1}{Q} \sum_{q=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{q} \sum_{a \bmod q}^{\star} e\left(\frac{(n-m)a}{q}\right) \int_{\mathbb{R}} \psi(q, x) e\left(\frac{(n-m)x}{qQ}\right) dx,
$$
where $Q = 2L^{1/2}$ is a parameter to be optimized later, the star on the sum indicates $(a, q) = 1$, and $\psi(q, x)$ is a weight function with specific decay properties. This expansion is the critical innovation because it separates the variables $r$ and $n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^k$ into different exponential factors that can be handled independently.

The parameter $Q$ controls the trade-off between the length of the $q$-sum and the oscillation in the integral. If $Q$ is too small, the $q$-sum is short but the integral oscillates rapidly. If $Q$ is too large, the integral is smooth but the $q$-sum is long. The paper ultimately sets $Q = X^{1/2}$ to balance these competing demands.

The weight function $\psi(q, x)$ has three key properties from equations (3.3)-(3.5): it satisfies $\psi(q, x) = 1 + h(q, x)$ where $h(q, x)$ is small when $q \ll Q^{1-\epsilon}$ and $x \ll Q^{-\epsilon}$, it decays as $\psi(q, x) \ll |x|^{-A}$ for any $A > 1$, and its derivatives satisfy $x^j \frac{\partial^j}{\partial x^j} \psi(q, x) \ll \min\{Q/q, 1/|x|\} \log Q$. These properties ensure that the effective range of the $x$-integral is $[-L^\epsilon, L^\epsilon]$, making the integral tractable.

After applying the delta expansion, the sum becomes:
$$
S_k(X) = \frac{1}{Q} \sum_{q \leq Q} \int_{\mathbb{R}} \frac{\psi(q, u)}{q} U(u) \sum_{a \bmod q}^{\star} \left[ \sum_{r \in \mathbb{Z}} A(r) e\left(\frac{ar}{q}\right) V\left(\frac{r}{X}\right) e\left(\frac{ru}{qQ}\right) \right] \times \left[ \sum_{n_1, n_2, n_3} a(n_3) e\left(-\frac{a(n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^k)}{q}\right) W_1\left(\frac{n_1}{X^{1/2}}\right) W_2\left(\frac{n_2}{X^{1/2}}\right) W_3\left(\frac{n_3}{Y}\right) e\left(-\frac{u(n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^k)}{qQ}\right) \right] du,
$$
where $V$, $W_1$, $W_2$, $W_3$, and $U$ are smooth bump functions supported on compact intervals that localize the sums to the desired ranges. The key observation is that the $r$-sum and the $(n_1, n_2, n_3)$-sum are now separated, allowing different techniques to be applied to each.

**Voronoi Summation on the $r$-Sum**

The $r$-sum contains the GL(3) Fourier coefficients $A(r) = \Lambda(1, r)$ twisted by an additive character $e(ar/q)$. This is precisely the setup for the GL(3) Voronoi summation formula (Lemma 2, Section 2), which is a duality formula that transforms a sum of Fourier coefficients into a dual sum with different coefficients and oscillatory integrals.

The Voronoi formula states that for a smooth compactly supported function $g(x)$:
$$
\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \Lambda(m, n) e\left(\frac{an}{q}\right) g(n) = q \sum_{\pm} \sum_{n_1 | qm} \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \frac{\Lambda(n_1, n_2)}{n_1 n_2} S\left(ma, \pm n_2; \frac{mq}{n_1}\right) G_{\pm}\left(\frac{n_1^2 n_2}{q^3 m}\right),
$$
where $S(a, b; c)$ is the Kloosterman sum defined as $S(a, b; c) = \sum_{x \bmod c}^{\star} e\left(\frac{ax + b\bar{x}}{c}\right)$ with $\bar{x}$ denoting the multiplicative inverse of $x$ modulo $c$, and $G_{\pm}(y)$ are oscillatory integrals defined in equation (2.4) involving Gamma functions and the Mellin transform of $g$.

In our case, $m = 1$ and $g(r) = V(r/X) e(ru/(qQ))$. The Voronoi transformation converts the original sum over $r$ (which has length about $X$) into a dual sum over $n_1, n_2$ where the effective length is determined by the oscillatory integral $G_{\pm}$. Using Lemma 4 (Section 2), the integral $G_{\pm}(y)$ has an asymptotic expansion involving terms like $\cos(6\pi(yz)^{1/3})$ and $\sin(6\pi(yz)^{1/3})$, which oscillate rapidly when $y$ is large.

The critical insight from equation (4.4) is that the dual sum is negligibly small unless $n_1^2 n_2 \ll K$ where $K = q^3/X + X^{1/2} u^3$. This is a massive reduction: the original $r$-sum had length $X$, but the dual sum has effective length only $K \asymp X^{1/2}$ when $q \asymp Q = X^{1/2}$. This reduction is the primary source of savings in the final bound.

After applying Voronoi and extracting the leading term from the asymptotic expansion of $G_{\pm}$, the $r$-sum becomes:
$$
\sum_{r \in \mathbb{Z}} A(r) e\left(\frac{ar}{q}\right) v(r) = \frac{X^{2/3}}{q} \sum_{\pm} \sum_{n | q} \sum_{n^2 m \ll K} \frac{\Lambda(n, m)}{n^{-1/3} m^{1/3}} S\left(a, \pm m; \frac{q}{n}\right) I_{\pm}(n^2 m, u, q) + \text{lower order terms},
$$
where $I_{\pm}$ is an oscillatory integral defined in equation (4.3) that captures the remaining oscillation from the Voronoi transform.

**Poisson Summation on the Polynomial Variables**

The $(n_1, n_2)$-sum contains the exponential $e(-a(n_1^2 + n_2^2)/q)$, which is a quadratic character. The paper applies Poisson summation (Lemma 5, Section 2) to convert this sum into a dual sum over frequencies $m_1, m_2$. Poisson summation states that for a Schwartz class function $\phi$:
$$
\sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}^n} \phi(n) = \sum_{m \in \mathbb{Z}^n} \hat{\phi}(m),
$$
where $\hat{\phi}$ is the Fourier transform of $\phi$.

Writing $n_1 = \alpha_1 + l_1 q$ and $n_2 = \alpha_2 + l_2 q$ where $\alpha_1, \alpha_2$ run over residue classes modulo $q$, the sum splits into a character sum over $\alpha_1, \alpha_2$ and an integral over the continuous variables. After applying Poisson summation, the result is:
$$
T = \frac{X}{q^2} \sum_{m_1, m_2 \in \mathbb{Z}} C(m_1, m_2, a, q) J(m_1, m_2, u, q),
$$
where $C(m_1, m_2, a, q)$ is the character sum:
$$
C(m_1, m_2, a, q) = \sum_{\alpha_1, \alpha_2 \bmod q} e\left(-\frac{a(\alpha_1^2 + \alpha_2^2) + m_1 \alpha_1 + m_2 \alpha_2}{q}\right),
$$
and $J(m_1, m_2, u, q)$ is the integral transform defined in equation (4.8).

The character sum $C(m_1, m_2, a, q)$ can be evaluated explicitly using properties of Gauss sums. Equation (4.21) shows that $C(m_1, m_2, a, q) = \varepsilon_q^2 q e(-4a(m_1^2 + m_2^2)/q)$ where $\varepsilon_q$ is a fourth root of unity depending on $q \bmod 4$. This explicit evaluation is crucial because it shows the character sum has size about $q$, which is square-root cancellation compared to the trivial bound of $q^2$.

Using integration by parts on the integral $J(m_1, m_2, u, q)$, the paper shows that the sum over $m_1, m_2$ is negligibly small unless $m_1 \ll X^{\epsilon}$ and $m_2 \ll X^{\epsilon}$. This is because the integral oscillates rapidly when $m_1$ or $m_2$ is large, causing cancellation. This restriction on $m_1, m_2$ is essential for keeping the final bound under control.

**Character Sum Estimation and Frequency Splitting**

After applying both Voronoi and Poisson summation, the sum $S_k(X)$ contains a complicated character sum $C_1$ defined in equation (4.10):
$$
C_1(m_1, m_2, m, n, n_3; q) = \sum_{a \bmod q}^{\star} S\left(a, \pm m; \frac{q}{n}\right) C(m_1, m_2, a; q) e\left(-\frac{a n_3^k}{q}\right).
$$
This character sum combines the Kloosterman sum from Voronoi, the quadratic character sum from Poisson, and an additional phase from the $n_3^k$ term.

The paper splits the analysis into two cases based on the frequency parameter $m$ from the Poisson summation over the auxiliary variable introduced when squaring the sum. **Case I (Zero Frequency, $m = 0$)**: When $m = 0$, the character sum simplifies significantly. Lemma 11 (Section 4.6) shows that $S_0 \ll q^4/n$ with the additional constraint that $n_3'^k \equiv n_3^k \bmod q$. This case contributes the bound in Lemma 13 (Section 4.7): $S_k(X) \ll X^{5/3 + \epsilon} Y^{1/2} K^{2/3} / Q^2$.

**Case II (Non-Zero Frequencies, $m \neq 0$)**: When $m \neq 0$, the analysis is more delicate. The paper factors $q = q_1 q_2 q_3$ where $n | q_1 q_2 | n^{\infty}$ and $(q_3, n) = 1$, then further splits $q_3 = q_3' q_3''$ where $q_3'$ is square-free and $q_3''$ is square-full. Lemma 12 (Section 4.6) provides different bounds depending on the arithmetic relationship between $q_3'$, $n_3^k$, $n_3'^k$, and $m$:
- If $(q_3', n_3^k n_3'^k m) = 1$, then $S_{\neq 0}(q) \ll q^{7/2} (q_1 q_2 q_3'')^{1/2} / n$.
- If $q_3' | m$ or if $(q_3', m) = 1$ and $(n_3^k n_3'^k, q_3') \neq 1$, then $S_{\neq 0}(q) \ll q^4 / n$.

The bound $q^{7/2}$ comes from applying Weil's bound for Kloosterman sums combined with techniques from Dąbrowski and Fisher [5] for sums of products of Kloosterman sums. Specifically, equation (4.34) shows a sum of the form $\sum_{\beta \bmod q_3'}^{\star} S(1, \gamma_1(\beta); q_3') S(1, \gamma_2(\beta); q_3')$ where $\gamma_1, \gamma_2$ are linear fractional transformations. Proposition 3.4 from [5] gives square-root cancellation for such sums, yielding the $q_3'^{3/2}$ factor that combines with other terms to give $q^{7/2}$.

This frequency splitting is a critical design choice. The zero-frequency case typically gives the main term in many analytic number theory problems, but here both frequencies contribute to the error bound. The non-zero frequency case requires more sophisticated algebraic geometry input (the Dąbrowski-Fisher bounds) but ultimately gives a better exponent when combined with the integral estimates.

**Integral Analysis and Stationary Phase**

The oscillatory integrals $I_{\pm}$ and $J$ must be bounded carefully to extract the final estimate. Lemma 9 (Section 4.3) provides the key bound:
$$
L_{\pm}(m_1, m_2, n, m, n_3, q) \ll \frac{q^{3/2}}{Q^{3/2}},
$$
where $L_{\pm}$ is the combined integral defined in equation (4.11).

The proof uses the stationary phase method, which is a technique for estimating integrals of the form $\int e^{i \lambda \phi(x)} a(x) dx$ when $\lambda$ is large. The main contribution comes from points where $\phi'(x) = 0$ (stationary points), and the integral is bounded by approximately $\lambda^{-1/2}$ times the size of the amplitude. In equation (4.14), the phase function is $\phi = 2\pi(\pm 3(X(u^2 + v^2 + n_3^k/X)n^2 m)^{1/3}/q - m_1 X^{1/2} u/q)$, and the stationary phase analysis yields the bound $P \ll \sqrt{q} / (X^{1/6} (n^2 m)^{1/6})$ from equation (4.15).

For the complementary range where $n^2 m X / q^3 \ll X^{\epsilon}$ (Section 4.9), the Voronoi integral $G_{\pm}$ is estimated differently by shifting the contour of integration in the Mellin transform definition. Equation (2.4) defines $G_{\pm}$ as a contour integral involving Gamma functions, and by moving the contour to $\sigma = -5/2$ and picking up poles, the paper obtains the bound $G_{\pm}(y) \ll \sqrt{n^2 m Q / (q^2 u)}$ for small $y$. This complementary range contributes less than the main range, as shown at the end of Section 4.9.

**Cauchy-Schwarz and Final Assembly**

At this point, the sum $S_k(X)$ has been transformed into an expression involving sums over $q$, $n$, $m$, $m_1$, $m_2$, $n_3$, and $n_3'$ with various character sums and integrals. The key move in Section 4.4 is to apply the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality to the $m$-sum to separate the Fourier coefficients $\Lambda(n, m)$ from the rest of the expression.

After Cauchy-Schwarz, the sum is bounded by:
$$
S_k(X) \ll \sup_{D \ll Q} \frac{X^{5/3}}{Q D^4} \sum_{n \ll D} \sum_{n | q} n^{1/3} \sum_{q \sim D} \sum_{m_1, m_2 \ll X^{\epsilon}} \Theta^{1/2} \Omega^{1/2},
$$
where $\Theta = \sum_{m \ll K/n^2} |\Lambda(n, m)|^2 / m^{2/3}$ and $\Omega$ contains the character sums and integrals squared. The $\Theta$ sum is bounded using the Rankin-Selberg bound (Lemma 1, equation 2.3): $\sum_{m^2 n \ll X} |\Lambda(m, n)|^2 \ll X^{1 + \epsilon}$.

The $\Omega$ sum is split into zero-frequency $\Omega_0$ and non-zero frequency $\Omega_{\neq 0}$ contributions. Lemma 13 (Section 4.7) bounds the zero-frequency contribution as $S_k(X) \ll X^{5/3 + \epsilon} Y^{1/2} K^{2/3} / Q^2$. Lemma 15 (Section 4.8) bounds the non-zero frequency contribution as $S_k(X) \ll X^{5/3 + \epsilon} Y K^{1/6} / Q^{7/4}$.

Finally, substituting $K \asymp X^{1/2}$ and $Q = X^{1/2}$ into these bounds and taking the maximum gives the result in Section 4.10:
$$
S_k(X) \ll \begin{cases} X^{7/8 + \epsilon} Y & \text{for } k = 3, \\ X^{1 + \epsilon} Y^{1/2} & \text{for } k \geq 4. \end{cases}
$$
For $k = 3$, we have $Y = X^{1/3}$, so the bound is $X^{7/8 + 1/3 + \epsilon} = X^{29/24 + \epsilon}$, which beats the trivial bound of $X^{1 + 1/3 + \epsilon} = X^{4/3 + \epsilon} = X^{32/24 + \epsilon}$. For $k \geq 4$, the bound $X^{1 + \epsilon} Y^{1/2} = X^{1 + 1/(2k) + \epsilon}$ beats the trivial bound $X^{1 + 1/k + \epsilon}$.

**Theorem 2 Modifications for Quadratic Forms**

The proof of Theorem 2 (Section 5) follows a similar structure but with important modifications for the binary quadratic form $Q(n_1, n_2) = A n_1^2 + B n_2^2 + 2C n_1 n_2$. The key difference is that $n_1$ and $n_2$ have different sizes: $n_1 \leq X$ and $n_2 \leq Y = X^{\theta}$ with $3/4 < \theta \leq 1$.

In Section 5.2, the Poisson summation gives a character sum $C'(m_1, m_2, a, q)$ that depends on the quadratic form $Q$. Lemma 17 (Section 5.4) bounds this character sum as $S_1 \ll q_1^3 q_2^2 d(q_1) d(q_2) / n$ where $q = q_1 q_2$ with $q_1 | (2n|A|)^{\infty}$ and $(q_2, 2n|A|q_1) = 1$.

A critical design choice in Theorem 2 is that the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality is NOT applied in the conventional way (Remark (i), Section 1). Instead, the paper uses a more refined analysis in Section 5.6 that exploits the structure of the sum more directly. Equation (5.13) shows a bound for the sum over $\Lambda(n, m)$ that uses partial summation and the derivative bound on $W_{\pm}$ from equation (5.12).

The final bound in equation (5.14) is $S \ll X^{7/4 + \epsilon}$, which for $\theta = 1$ (equal variable sizes) gives $X^{7/4 + \epsilon}$ compared to the trivial bound of $X^2$. This represents a saving of $X^{1/4}$, which is significant for this type of problem.

**Key Design Choices and Their Rationale**

The paper makes several non-standard methodological choices that are essential for achieving the stated bounds. First, for Theorem 1, the authors do NOT apply Voronoi summation to the $n_3$ variable (the smallest variable with $n_3 \leq Y = X^{1/k}$). Applying Voronoi to a short sum can sometimes introduce more error than it removes because the dual sum may not be shorter than the original. By keeping $n_3$ in its original form and only applying Poisson to $n_1, n_2$, the paper preserves the structure that allows for better cancellation.

Second, for Theorem 2, the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality is not applied in the standard way. The conventional approach would square the entire sum and separate variables, but this loses information about the correlation between different terms. The refined analysis in Section 5.6 preserves more structure and achieves a better exponent.

Third, the parameter $Q = X^{1/2}$ is chosen to balance the length of the $q$-sum against the oscillation in the integrals. If $Q$ were larger, the $q$-sum would be longer and harder to bound. If $Q$ were smaller, the integrals would oscillate more rapidly and be harder to estimate. The choice $Q = X^{1/2}$ is optimal for the given problem structure.

Fourth, the frequency splitting ($m = 0$ vs $m \neq 0$) allows the paper to apply different techniques to different parts of the sum. The zero-frequency case uses simpler character sum bounds, while the non-zero frequency case uses deeper algebraic geometry input (Dąbrowski-Fisher bounds). This splitting is standard in the circle method but requires careful implementation in the DFI delta method context.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper does not merely apply existing tools to a new problem; it modifies the standard analytic machinery to accommodate the specific geometric and arithmetic constraints of higher-rank forms over sparse sequences. The following insights distinguish this work from prior applications of the Duke-Friedlander-Iwaniec (DFI) method and standard circle method techniques.

### 1. Strategic Restraint in Variable Dualization (Theorem 1)
In standard applications of the DFI delta method or the circle method, a common heuristic is to apply summation formulae (like Voronoi or Poisson) to **all** summation variables. The goal is typically to dualize every sum, converting a long sum of oscillatory coefficients into a shorter dual sum where cancellation is easier to extract.

*   **The Innovation:** For Theorem 1, the authors explicitly choose **not** to apply the Voronoi summation formula to the variable $n_3$, which ranges over the smallest interval $1 \leq n_3 \leq Y$ where $Y = X^{1/k}$ (Remark (i), Section 1).
*   **Why This Differs from Prior Work:** In mixed power problems like $n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^k$, dualizing the smallest variable $n_3$ would transform a sum of length $Y$ into a dual sum of length roughly $q^3/Y$. Given the optimization parameter $Q \approx X^{1/2}$, the dual length becomes $\approx X^{3/2 - 1/k}$. For $k \geq 3$, this dual length can exceed the original sum length or introduce error terms that overwhelm the savings gained from dualizing $n_1$ and $n_2$.
*   **Significance:** This "insight of restraint" prevents error explosion. By keeping $n_3$ in its primal form, the authors avoid introducing unnecessary oscillatory integrals associated with the $k$-th power. This allows the bound to remain non-trivial for all $k \geq 3$, improving upon the exponents found in Zhou and Hu [27] for the triple divisor function $d_3(n)$ by ensuring the error terms from the $n_1, n_2$ dualization are not compounded by a poorly behaved $n_3$ dualization.

### 2. Preserving Variable Correlation in Asymmetric Settings (Theorem 2)
When dealing with binary quadratic forms $Q(n_1, n_2)$ where variables have unequal sizes ($n_1 \leq X, n_2 \leq Y$ with $Y = X^\theta$), the standard analytic approach often relies heavily on the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality to separate variables.

*   **The Innovation:** In the proof of Theorem 2, the authors deviate from the conventional approach by **not applying the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality** in the standard manner (Remark (i), Section 1). Instead, Section 5.6 employs a refined analysis using partial summation and derivative bounds on the integral transform $W_\pm$.
*   **Why This Differs from Prior Work:** A standard Cauchy-Schwarz application squares the sum, effectively treating the variables $n_1$ and $n_2$ as independent after separation. This discards the geometric correlation encoded in the quadratic form $Q(n_1, n_2) = An_1^2 + By^2 + 2Cxy$. In asymmetric settings ($\theta < 1$), this loss of correlation is costly; the "waste" from squaring overwhelms the savings from cancellation when one variable is significantly shorter than the other.
*   **Significance:** By avoiding full separation, the analysis preserves the relationship between $n_1$ and $n_2$ throughout the estimation process. This enables the result to hold for the range $3/4 < \theta \leq 1$. If standard Cauchy-Schwarz were used, the bound would likely degrade significantly as $\theta$ decreases, potentially failing to beat the trivial bound $X^{1+\theta}$ for $\theta$ near $3/4$. This represents a fundamental methodological advance for handling **anisotropic** summation regions in automorphic form problems.

### 3. Integration of Algebraic Geometry Bounds for GL(3) Character Sums
The estimation of character sums is often the bottleneck in delta method applications. For GL(2) forms, Weil bounds for Kloosterman sums are standard. For GL(3), the character sums are more complex, involving products of Kloosterman sums.

*   **The Innovation:** In Section 4.6, specifically for the non-zero frequency case ($m \neq 0$), the authors utilize bounds derived from **$\ell$-adic cohomology techniques** developed by Dąbrowski and Fisher [5]. Equation (4.34) reformulates the character sum as a sum of products of Kloosterman sums twisted by linear fractional transformations:
    $$
    \sum_{\beta \bmod q'_3}^{\star} S(1, \gamma_1(\beta); q'_3) S(1, \gamma_2(\beta); q'_3).
    $$
*   **Why This Differs from Prior Work:** Many analytic number theory papers involving GL(3) forms rely on simpler pointwise bounds or standard Weil bounds which might yield $O(q^4)$ or similar for such sums. By invoking Proposition 3.4 from [5], the authors achieve square-root cancellation, resulting in a bound of $O(q'^{7/2}_3)$ (Equation 4.35) rather than a trivial $O(q'^4_3)$.
*   **Significance:** This reduction in the exponent of $q$ is critical for the final estimate. In Section 4.8, the non-zero frequency contribution (Lemma 15) depends directly on this character sum bound. Without this specific algebraic geometry input, the savings from the delta method would be insufficient to beat the trivial bound for the GL(3) coefficients. It demonstrates a successful bridge between deep algebraic geometry results and concrete analytic bounds for higher-rank automorphic forms.

### 4. Generalization of the "Twisted Gauss Circle Problem"
The paper positions Theorem 2 as a generalization of the Gauss circle problem twisted by Fourier coefficients (Remark (iv), Section 1).

*   **The Innovation:** Prior work by the same authors (Chanana and Singh [4]) established bounds for $\Lambda(1, Q(n_1, n_2))$ only when $n_1$ and $n_2$ were of comparable size ($X \times X$). This paper extends the result to **varying sizes** ($X \times X^\theta$).
*   **Why This Differs from Prior Work:** Extending from equal sizes to unequal sizes is not merely a parameter tweak; it changes the geometry of the summation region from a circle-like shape to an ellipse-like shape with high eccentricity. This requires re-evaluating the Poisson summation step (Section 5.2) where the dual variables $m_1, m_2$ now have different ranges of significance ($m_1 \ll X^\epsilon$ vs $m_2 \ll X^{1-\theta+\epsilon}$).
*   **Significance:** This generalization makes the result applicable to a wider class of Diophantine problems where variables naturally have different magnitudes. It establishes that the cancellation properties of GL(3) Fourier coefficients are robust enough to withstand the distortion of the summation region, provided $\theta > 3/4$. This sets a new baseline for future work on sparse sequences over quadratic forms in higher rank.

The "results" are the exponents in these bounds, and the "baselines" are the trivial bounds (derived from triangle inequalities) and prior theoretical results cited in the literature.

This demonstrates that the "non-zero frequency" component is the critical bottleneck that determines the final quality of the result.

### 5.4 Robustness Checks and Limitations

*   **Variable Asymmetry Limit ($\theta$):**
    Theorem 2 is explicitly conditional on $3/4 < \theta \leq 1$ (Section 1, Theorem 2 remarks).
    *   **Failure Case:** If $\theta \leq 3/4$, the bound $X^{7/4}$ may exceed the trivial bound $X^{1+\theta}$ (since $1 + 3/4 = 1.75$). The method loses its advantage when the second variable $n_2$ becomes too small relative to $n_1$.
    *   **Reason:** The Poisson summation step (Section 5.2) relies on the dual variable $m_2$ having a range $m_2 \ll X^{1-\theta+\epsilon}$. If $\theta$ is too small, the dual sum becomes too long, and the savings from cancellation are lost.
*   **Power Exponent Limit ($k$):**
    Theorem 1 requires $k \geq 3$ (Section 1).
    *   **Reason:** For $k=2$, the polynomial becomes a quadratic form in three variables, which requires a different treatment (often involving modular forms of higher degree or different circle method variants). The specific delta method setup here is optimized for $k \geq 3$ where the $n_3$ variable is sufficiently sparse.
*   **Epsilon Loss:**
    All bounds include an arbitrary $\epsilon > 0$ (e.g., $X^{7/4+\epsilon}$). This is standard in analytic number theory due to divisor bound estimates ($d(n) \ll n^\epsilon$) and dyadic summation losses. It means the results are asymptotic and may not hold for small $X$ without explicit constants.

### 5.5 Conclusion on Validity

The theoretical "experiments" (proofs) convincingly support the paper's claims:
1.  **Superiority:** The exponents $29/24$ (Theorem 1) and $7/4$ (Theorem 2) are strictly smaller than the trivial bounds $4/3$ and $2$ respectively.
2.  **Generality:** The methods work for both the triple divisor function $d_3(n)$ and the oscillatory GL(3) coefficients $\Lambda(1, n)$, showing robustness across different arithmetic functions.
3.  **Methodological Soundness:** The split between zero and non-zero frequencies (Sections 4.7–4.8) and the careful handling of the complementary range (Section 4.9) ensure no hidden error terms overwhelm the main savings.

However, the results are conditional. The restriction $\theta > 3/4$ in Theorem 2 indicates the method is not universally applicable to all asymmetric quadratic forms, marking a clear boundary for future research to improve upon.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper achieves significant improvements over prior bounds for GL(3) Fourier coefficients over sparse sequences, the results are contingent on specific parameter ranges, structural assumptions, and methodological choices. Understanding these limitations is crucial for assessing the applicability of the techniques to broader problems in analytic number theory.

### 6.1. Parameter Constraints and Boundary Conditions

The proven bounds are not universal; they hold only within specific regimes for the exponents governing the variable sizes.

*   **The Asymmetry Barrier ($\theta > 3/4$):**
    Theorem 2 establishes a bound of $X^{7/4+\epsilon}$ for the sum over binary quadratic forms $Q(n_1, n_2)$ where $n_1 \leq X$ and $n_2 \leq Y = X^\theta$. This result is explicitly conditional on **$3/4 < \theta \leq 1$** (Section 1, Theorem 2).
    *   **The Limitation:** If $\theta \leq 3/4$, the proven bound $X^{7/4}$ ceases to be non-trivial compared to the trivial bound of roughly $X^{1+\theta}$. For instance, at $\theta = 3/4$, the trivial bound is $X^{1.75}$, which matches the proven bound, yielding no savings.
    *   **The Cause:** This barrier arises from the Poisson summation step in Section 5.2. The dual variable $m_2$ associated with $n_2$ has a range dependent on $X^{1-\theta}$. As $\theta$ decreases, the dual sum lengthens. When $\theta \leq 3/4$, the error accumulated from the longer dual sum overwhelms the cancellation gained from the Voronoi summation on the $r$-sum.

*   **The Mixed Power Exponent ($k \geq 3$):**
    Theorem 1 addresses sums over $n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^k$ specifically for **$k \geq 3$** (Abstract; Section 1, Equation 1.5).
    *   **The Limitation:** The case $k=2$ is excluded. If $k=2$, the polynomial becomes a ternary quadratic form $n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^2$.
    *   **The Cause:** The analytic machinery is tuned for "mixed powers" where one variable is significantly sparser ($Y = X^{1/k}$). For $k=2$, the variables are more balanced, and the specific deviation used in the proof—not applying Voronoi summation to the smallest variable $n_3$ (Remark (i), Section 1)—might not be optimal. The error terms derived in Section 4.9 rely on the specific scaling of $K \asymp X^{1/2}$ relative to $Y$, which changes fundamentally if $k=2$.

### 6.2. Methodological Trade-offs

The authors explicitly deviate from standard applications of the Duke-Friedlander-Iwaniec (DFI) delta method to achieve these bounds. These choices represent trade-offs between generality and precision.

*   **Selective Dualization (Theorem 1):**
    In Remark (i) of Section 1, the authors state they **do not use the summation formula on the variable of smaller size** ($n_3$).
    *   **The Trade-off:** Standard practice often dualizes all variables to maximize cancellation. By leaving $n_3$ in its primal form, the authors avoid introducing oscillatory integrals associated with the $k$-th power that would be difficult to bound.
    *   **The Cost:** This strategy relies on $n_3$ being small enough that its contribution does not dominate the error. If the problem were modified such that $n_3$ had a larger range, this "restraint" would likely fail to produce a non-trivial bound, requiring a different hybrid approach.

*   **Avoidance of Standard Cauchy-Schwarz (Theorem 2):**
    For the quadratic form sum, the authors **do not apply the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality** in the conventional approach (Remark (i), Section 1).
    *   **The Trade-off:** Standard Cauchy-Schwarz separates variables completely, simplifying the analysis but discarding geometric correlations between $n_1$ and $n_2$. By avoiding this, the proof preserves the correlation structure necessary to handle the asymmetric range $Y = X^\theta$.
    *   **The Cost:** This makes the proof significantly more intricate (see Section 5.6) and potentially less robust. The technique is finely tuned to the specific quadratic form $Q(n_1, n_2)$. Extending this to higher-degree forms or more variables might require re-introducing Cauchy-Schwarz, thereby losing the advantage gained here.

### 6.3. Structural and Arithmetic Assumptions

The results depend on specific properties of the arithmetic functions and polynomials involved.

*   **Positive Definite Quadratic Forms:**
    Theorem 2 applies only to **symmetric positive definite** quadratic forms $Q(x, y)$ (Abstract; Section 1).
    *   **The Limitation:** Indefinite forms (e.g., $x^2 - y^2$) or degenerate forms are not covered.
    *   **The Cause:** The analysis relies on Gauss sum evaluations (Lemma 6, Section 2) and Poisson summation properties that assume positive definiteness to control the size of the quadratic form values. Indefinite forms introduce sign changes and zero values that complicate the delta method detection $\delta(r, Q(n_1, n_2))$.

*   **$L^2$ Boundedness of Weights:**
    The auxiliary arithmetic function $a(n)$ weighting the $n_3$ term in Theorem 1 must be **bounded in the $L^2$ sense** (Equation 1.4, Section 1):
    $$
    \sum_{n \leq X} |a(n)|^2 \ll X^{1+\epsilon}.
    $$
    *   **The Limitation:** If $a(n)$ grows faster than this (e.g., if $a(n)$ itself were a divisor function without normalization), the bounds in Section 4.4 (Cauchy-Schwarz step) would degrade. The proof explicitly uses this bound to control the $\Omega$ sum in Equation 4.19.

*   **GL(3) Specificity:**
    The paper focuses on **SL(3, $\mathbb{Z}$)** Hecke-Maass cusp forms.
    *   **The Limitation:** The Voronoi summation formula (Lemma 2, Section 2) and the specific bounds for $\Lambda(m, n)$ (Lemma 1) are specific to rank 3.
    *   **The Cause:** Higher-rank groups (GL($n$) for $n > 3$) have more complex Voronoi formulae involving higher-dimensional Kloosterman sums. While the Introduction (Section 1) notes that "much less is known about... higher rank," this paper does not provide a pathway to generalize these specific bounds to GL($n$).

### 6.4. Precision and Explicit Constants

Like many results in analytic number theory using the delta method, the bounds suffer from inherent imprecision regarding constants.

*   **The $\epsilon$ Loss:**
    All main results include an arbitrary **$\epsilon > 0$** in the exponent (e.g., $X^{7/4+\epsilon}$).
    *   **The Limitation:** This means the bounds are asymptotic. For small values of $X$, the hidden constant $c_\epsilon$ (which may grow rapidly as $\epsilon \to 0$) could make the bound weaker than the trivial estimate.
    *   **The Cause:** This loss accumulates from several sources: dyadic summation blocks (Section 4.4), divisor bound estimates ($d(n) \ll n^\epsilon$), and the handling of the weight function $\psi(q, x)$ in the delta method (Lemma 8, Section 3). Removing $\epsilon$ typically requires much more delicate analysis that is not attempted here.

*   **Ineffective Constants:**
    The implied constants in the $\ll$ notation depend on the form $\phi$ and $\epsilon$ (Lemma 1, Section 2).
    *   **The Limitation:** The results are not effective in terms of the spectral parameters of the Maass form $\phi$. One cannot explicitly compute the bound without knowing the hidden constants associated with the specific automorphic form.

### 6.5. Open Questions and Future Directions

Based on the constraints identified above, several natural questions remain open:

*   **Lowering the Asymmetry Threshold:** Can the condition $\theta > 3/4$ in Theorem 2 be improved? Achieving bounds for $\theta \leq 3/4$ would require better control over the dual sum lengths in the Poisson summation step (Section 5.2), possibly via new estimates for character sums with very skewed moduli.
*   **The Case $k=2$:** Does a similar bound hold for ternary quadratic forms ($k=2$)? This would require adapting the "selective dualization" strategy, as treating $n_3$ differently when it is of size $X^{1/2}$ (same as $n_1, n_2$) is non-trivial.
*   **Explicit Constants:** Is it possible to make the constants effective? This would be necessary for computational applications or verifying conjectures for small $X$.
*   **Higher Rank Generalization:** Can the deviations from the standard DFI method (avoiding Cauchy-Schwarz, selective Voronoi) be adapted for GL($n$) forms with $n > 3$? The complexity of the GL(3) Voronoi formula (Lemma 2) suggests this would be significantly harder, but the methodological insights regarding variable handling might transfer.

This work does more than tighten exponents; it recalibrates the methodological expectations for handling higher-rank automorphic forms over sparse sequences. By demonstrating that strategic deviations from the standard Duke-Friedlander-Iwaniec (DFI) delta method yield superior bounds, the paper opens specific avenues for refining analytic number theory techniques. The following analysis outlines how this work shifts the field's baseline, what research questions it prioritizes, and how practitioners should integrate these methods.

### 7.1 Shifting the Baseline for Higher-Rank Forms

The primary implication of this paper is the establishment of a new benchmark for sums involving GL(3) Fourier coefficients over polynomials. Prior to this work, the understanding of such sums lagged behind analogous results for the triple divisor function $d_3(n)$ and GL(2) coefficients.

*   **Closing the Gap with Divisor Functions:**
    Historically, bounds for oscillatory Fourier coefficients $\Lambda(1, n)$ were weaker than those for positive arithmetic functions like $d_3(n)$ due to the difficulty of managing sign changes. Theorem 1 (Section 1) establishes a bound of $X^{7/8+\epsilon}Y$ for $k=3$. When compared to the Zhou and Hu [27] result for $d_3(n)$ cited in Equation 1.3, this paper achieves a stronger exponent for the GL(3) coefficients than previously existed for the divisor function in this specific mixed-power setting.
    > "We establish the following result in which we are getting a stronger bound for each $k \geq 3$ than (1.3)." (Section 1)

    This implies that the oscillatory nature of GL(3) coefficients, often viewed as a hindrance, can be leveraged via the Voronoi summation formula (Lemma 2, Section 2) to produce cancellation comparable to or better than non-oscillatory functions when the analytic machinery is correctly tuned.

*   **Redefining Asymmetric Quadratic Form Bounds:**
    For binary quadratic forms, the authors improve their own prior work [4] significantly. The previous bound was $O(X^{2 - 1/68 + \epsilon})$ for equal variable sizes (Equation 1.6, Section 1). Theorem 2 (Section 1) improves this to $O(X^{7/4+\epsilon})$ for equal sizes ($\theta=1$), and extends validity to asymmetric sizes $Y = X^\theta$ where $3/4 < \theta \leq 1$.
    This shifts the landscape from treating quadratic form sums as rigid structures (where $n_1 \approx n_2$) to flexible ones where variable magnitudes can differ by a factor of $X^{1/4}$ without collapsing the bound.

The limitations identified in Section 6 naturally dictate the next priorities for research in this domain. The specific barriers encountered in the proofs suggest concrete technical hurdles to overcome.

*   **Lowering the Asymmetry Threshold ($\theta$):**

    *   **The Hurdle:** The barrier arises in the Poisson summation step (Section 5.2), where the dual variable $m_2$ associated with the shorter variable $n_2$ ranges up to $X^{1-\theta+\epsilon}$. As $\theta$ decreases, this dual sum lengthens, overwhelming the savings from the Voronoi transform.
    *   **Future Direction:** Research should focus on refining the character sum estimates in Lemma 17 (Section 5.4). If stronger bounds than $S_1 \ll q_1^3 q_2^2 d(q_1)d(q_2)/n$ can be achieved for the specific quadratic form structure, the tolerance for longer dual sums (smaller $\theta$) would increase.

*   **Addressing the $k=2$ Case:**
    Theorem 1 explicitly requires $k \geq 3$ (Abstract; Section 1). The case $k=2$ corresponds to the ternary quadratic form $n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^2$.
    *   **The Hurdle:** The proof relies on "selective dualization" (Remark (i), Section 1), where Voronoi summation is *not* applied to the smallest variable $n_3$. This works because $n_3$ is sufficiently sparse ($Y = X^{1/k}$). For $k=2$, $n_3$ is of size $X^{1/2}$, similar to $n_1$ and $n_2$, making the decision to dualize or not less clear-cut.
    *   **Future Direction:** New hybrid methods are needed that can handle three variables of comparable size without incurring the error explosion seen in standard full dualization. This might involve applying the delta method differently, perhaps detecting equality in two stages.

    All bounds include an arbitrary $\epsilon > 0$ (e.g., $X^{7/4+\epsilon}$).
    *   **The Hurdle:** This loss accumulates from dyadic summation (Section 4.4), divisor bounds ($d(n) \ll n^\epsilon$), and the weight function $\psi(q, x)$ properties (Lemma 8, Section 3).
    *   **Future Direction:** Achieving effective constants (removing $\epsilon$) would require replacing dyadic decompositions with smooth partitions of unity that track constants precisely, and using explicit versions of the Voronoi formula with known error terms. This is essential for any computational verification of these bounds.

*   **Extension to the von Mangoldt Function:**
    Remark (iii) in Section 1 notes: "In Theorem 1, if we take $a = \Lambda$, the von Mangoldt function then our sum is over sequences having one variable restricted to prime powers."
    *   **Future Direction:** While the paper proves the bound for $a(n)$ bounded in $L^2$ sense (Equation 1.4), explicitly working out the case where $a(n) = \Lambda(n)$ could yield results about primes represented by mixed power polynomials. This connects the analytic bounds directly to the distribution of primes, a core motivation cited in the Introduction (Section 1).

### 7.3 Practical Applications in Number Theory

### 7.4 Integration and Methodological Guidance

For researchers looking to apply or extend these techniques, the following guidance clarifies when this specific analytical framework is appropriate.

*   **When to Use This Method:**
    *   **Oscillatory Coefficients:** Prefer this approach when the arithmetic function $A(n)$ exhibits sign changes (like GL(3) Fourier coefficients). The Voronoi summation formula (Lemma 2, Section 2) is specifically designed to exploit this oscillation for cancellation.
    *   **Moderate Asymmetry:** Use the Theorem 2 framework when variables are asymmetric but satisfy $Y > X^{3/4}$. The method is optimized for this range where the dual sum length remains manageable.
    *   **Mixed Powers:** Use the Theorem 1 framework for polynomials with mixed degrees ($n_1^2 + n_2^2 + n_3^k$) where $k \geq 3$. The "selective dualization" strategy is key here.

*   **When to Avoid This Method:**
    *   **High Asymmetry ($\theta \leq 3/4$):** If the variable sizes differ by more than a factor of $X^{1/4}$, the Poisson summation error terms (Section 5.2) will likely dominate. Alternative methods, such as large sieve inequalities, might be more robust in this regime.
    *   **Small Power Exponents ($k=2$):** For ternary quadratic forms, the specific delta method setup in Section 4.1 is not optimized. Standard circle method variants might be more appropriate.
    *   **Need for Explicit Constants:** If the application requires numerical verification for small $X$, avoid this method due to the ineffective $\epsilon$ loss and hidden constants depending on the Maass form $\phi$ (Lemma 1, Section 2).

*   **Key Implementation Steps:**
    1.  **Delta Expansion:** Start with the DFI delta method (Lemma 8, Section 3) to separate the polynomial constraint from the coefficients. Set $Q = X^{1/2}$ for balanced optimization.
    2.  **Selective Voronoi:** Apply Voronoi summation (Lemma 2, Section 2) *only* to the sum containing the oscillatory coefficients ($r$-sum). Do not apply it to the smallest polynomial variable if $k \geq 3$.
    3.  **Poisson on Geometry:** Apply Poisson summation (Lemma 5, Section 2) to the polynomial variables to convert geometric constraints into character sums.
    4.  **Frequency Split:** Separate the analysis into zero-frequency ($m=0$) and non-zero frequency ($m \neq 0$) cases (Sections 4.7–4.8). Use algebraic geometry bounds (like Dąbrowski-Fisher [5]) for the non-zero frequency character sums to ensure square-root cancellation.
    5.  **Avoid Standard Cauchy-Schwarz:** For asymmetric quadratic forms, follow the refined analysis in Section 5.6 rather than squaring the sum immediately, to preserve variable correlations.
