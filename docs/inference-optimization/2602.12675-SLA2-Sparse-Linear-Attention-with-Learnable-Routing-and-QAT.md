## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces SLA2, which resolves the suboptimal heuristic routing and formulation mismatch of prior Sparse-Linear Attention by employing a learnable router and a direct sparse-linear decomposition with a learnable ratio. Evaluated on Wan2.1-1.3B and Wan2.1-14B video diffusion models, SLA2 achieves 97% attention sparsity and an 18.6× attention speedup while maintaining video generation quality that exceeds full attention baselines (Section 9). By integrating quantization-aware training, SLA2 enables highly efficient video generation without degrading end-to-end performance.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of SLA2, we must first establish the computational challenges inherent in video generation models and examine why previous acceleration techniques fell short. This section dissects the specific theoretical and practical gaps in the prior state-of-the-art, Sparse-Linear Attention (SLA), which motivated the design of SLA2.

### 2.1. The Computational Bottleneck in Video Diffusion
Diffusion models, particularly those generating video, rely heavily on the Transformer architecture. The core computational engine of a Transformer is the attention mechanism, which calculates relationships between all pairs of tokens in a sequence. For a sequence of length `$N$`, standard attention requires computing an `$N \times N$` attention matrix, resulting in quadratic complexity `$O(N^2)$`.

In video generation, `$N$` is exceptionally large because it represents the product of spatial dimensions (height and width) and temporal frames. As noted in the Introduction (Section 1), this quadratic cost becomes the primary bottleneck for both training and inference speed. While "Full Attention" implementations (like FlashAttention) are exact, they are often too slow for real-time or high-throughput video applications. This necessitates **sparse attention** methods, which compute only a subset of the attention matrix, or **linear attention** methods, which approximate the matrix to achieve linear complexity `$O(N)$`.

### 2.2. The Predecessor: Sparse-Linear Attention (SLA)
Before SLA2, the leading approach was Sparse-Linear Attention (SLA) (Zhang et al., 2025c). SLA attempted to hybridize two distinct attention mechanisms to balance speed and quality:
1.  **Sparse Attention:** Computes exact softmax attention on a selected subset of tokens (high importance).
2.  **Linear Attention:** Approximates attention on the remaining tokens using kernelized feature maps (low importance).

The theoretical motivation behind SLA was that the full attention map `$P$` could be decomposed into two parts (Section 2.2):
$$
P = P_1 + P_2
$$
where `$P_1$` is a **high-sparse part** (handled by sparse attention) and `$P_2$` is a **low-rank part** (handled by linear attention).

While SLA demonstrated strong performance on video diffusion models like TurboDiffusion, the authors of SLA2 identified two critical flaws in this formulation that limited its efficiency and accuracy.

### 2.3. Limitation 1: The Decomposition Mismatch
The first major gap is a mathematical inconsistency between SLA's implementation and its original motivation. This is detailed in Section 2.2 ("Rethinking Sparse-Linear Attention").

**The Theory:** Ideally, the sparse branch should output `$P_1$` directly.
**The Reality:** Sparse attention mechanisms inherently normalize probabilities so that each row sums to 1. If `$P_1$` represents the raw attention weights on the selected mask, the sparse attention output `$P_s$` is actually:
$$
P_s = \frac{P_1}{\alpha}
$$
where `$\alpha \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times 1}$` is a vector containing the sum of probabilities for each row on the masked positions (Equation 7-8).

**The Consequence:** This creates a scaling mismatch. The desired output is `$P_1 V$`, but the sparse branch produces `$P_s V$`. To compensate, the original SLA formulation added a learnable projection `proj` to the *linear* attention branch output `$O_l$` (Equation 4):
$$
O_{\text{SLA}} = O_s + \text{proj}(O_l)
$$
The paper argues this is suboptimal because it forces the linear branch to correct errors made by the sparse branch (Equation 10). The linear branch is no longer just approximating `$P_2$`; it is also trying to fix the scaling factor `$\alpha$` of the sparse branch. SLA2 addresses this by directly learning the ratio `$\alpha$` to combine the branches, ensuring the formulation matches the decomposition motivation (Equation 12).

### 2.4. Limitation 2: Heuristic Routing
The second gap concerns how the model decides which tokens belong to the sparse branch and which belong to the linear branch. This decision is made by a "router."

*   **SLA's Approach:** SLA used a **heuristic split**. It computed compressed attention weights and assigned the top `$k_h\%$` entries (largest magnitude) to the sparse branch (Section 2.1).
*   **The Flaw:** The authors argue that magnitude alone is not the optimal criterion for decomposition. For example, moving some weights from the sparse part `$P_1$` to the linear part `$P_2$` might not increase the rank of `$P_2$` (keeping it easy to approximate) while significantly improving the sparsity of `$P_1$` (Section 1).
*   **SLA2's Goal:** Instead of a fixed heuristic, SLA2 introduces a **learnable router** `$R$` that is trained to minimize the approximation error relative to full attention. This allows the model to discover a split that is mathematically superior for the specific task, rather than relying on a static rule based on weight magnitude.

### 2.5. Limitation 3: Quantization Integration
While not a flaw in the original SLA theory, the prior work did not fully exploit low-bit quantization for additional speedups.
*   **Post-Training Quantization (PTQ):** Typically, quantization is applied after training. However, this often introduces accuracy drops because the model weights were not optimized for low-precision representation (Section 5).
*   **The Opportunity:** SLA2 aims to integrate **Quantization-Aware Training (QAT)**. By simulating quantization effects during the fine-tuning process, the model adapts to the noise introduced by low-bit operations (e.g., INT8 or FP8). This allows SLA2 to run the sparse attention branch with low-bit precision without degrading the end-to-end video quality.

### 2.6. Positioning Relative to Existing Work
SLA2 positions itself at the intersection of three research directions:
1.  **Trainable Sparse Attention:** Unlike training-free methods (e.g., SpargeAttention) which mask attention at inference time, SLA2 fine-tunes the model to adapt to sparsity, allowing for higher sparsity levels (Section 1).
2.  **Hybrid Attention:** Unlike pure linear attention methods (e.g., SANA, Dig) which may lose quality in video generation, SLA2 retains a sparse branch for high-fidelity details (Section 10).
3.  **Efficient Kernels:** By combining sparsity with low-bit quantization, SLA2 targets kernel-level efficiency improvements beyond just algorithmic complexity reduction (Section 5).

In summary, the motivation for SLA2 is not merely to make attention faster, but to correct the theoretical inconsistencies of its predecessor (SLA) while unlocking hardware-level efficiencies through learnable routing and quantization-aware training. This ensures that speedups do not come at the cost of the visual quality required for professional video generation.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems and algorithms paper that proposes a new attention mechanism for diffusion transformers. The core idea is to replace the standard quadratic-cost attention with a hybrid sparse-linear attention that uses a learnable router to decide which computations use exact sparse attention versus approximate linear attention, while also learning the correct scaling ratio between these two branches and integrating quantization-aware training for additional speedup.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

SLA2 is a drop-in replacement for the attention module in video diffusion models that reduces computation by 97% while maintaining or improving generation quality. The solution combines three innovations: a learnable router that intelligently splits attention computations, a mathematically corrected formula for combining sparse and linear attention outputs, and quantization-aware training that allows low-bit precision computation without accuracy loss.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The SLA2 system consists of four major components that process query (Q), key (K), and value (V) matrices through a coordinated pipeline. First, the **Learnable Router R** takes pooled versions of Q and K as input and outputs a binary mask M that determines which attention positions use sparse versus linear computation (Section 4). Second, the **Sparse Attention Branch** computes exact softmax attention only on positions where M = 1, producing output Os (Equation 14). Third, the **Linear Attention Branch** computes approximate attention on positions where M = 0, producing output Ol (Equation 14). Fourth, the **Learnable Combination Module** uses a learned ratio vector α to weight and combine Os and Ol into the final output O (Equation 13). Additionally, the **Quantization-Aware Training Module** applies low-bit quantization to Q, K, and V during the forward pass of the sparse branch while maintaining full precision for backpropagation (Section 5). Information flows from inputs Q, K, V through the router to generate M, then through both attention branches in parallel, and finally through the combination module to produce the output that replaces standard attention in the diffusion model.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first explain the core SLA2 mathematical formulation and how it corrects the decomposition mismatch from prior work, since this establishes why the method is theoretically sound.
- We then detail the learnable router architecture and training procedure, because the router determines which computations go to which branch and is central to achieving high sparsity.
- We next describe the sparse and linear attention branch implementations, including the efficient FlashAttention-based kernel design that avoids computing full matrices.
- We then cover the quantization-aware training integration, which provides additional speedup beyond sparsity alone.
- We explain the two-stage training strategy, which is necessary for stable optimization given the non-differentiable routing decisions.
- We conclude with the inference procedure and specific hyperparameter configurations used in experiments.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Mathematical Formulation.** SLA2 reformulates how sparse and linear attention outputs are combined to match the original theoretical motivation of decomposing the attention map into sparse and low-rank components. The fundamental insight from Section 2.2 is that standard sparse attention produces a row-normalized output Ps where each row sums to 1, but the desired sparse component P1 from the decomposition does not necessarily sum to 1 per row. The relationship between them is P1 = α ⊙ Ps, where α ∈ ℝ^(N×1) is a vector containing the sum of attention probabilities on the masked positions for each query token (Equation 7-8). The original SLA formulation attempted to compensate for this mismatch by adding a learnable projection to the linear branch output, but this forced the linear branch to correct sparse branch errors rather than purely approximating the low-rank component (Equation 10). SLA2 instead directly learns the ratio α and combines the branches as follows:

$$
O = \alpha \odot O_s + (1 - \alpha) \odot O_l
$$

where O is the final attention output, Os is the sparse attention branch output, Ol is the linear attention branch output, and α is a learnable vector with values between 0 and 1 (Equation 13). The element-wise multiplication ⊙ scales each row of the attention outputs by the corresponding α value. This formulation ensures that α ⊙ Ps better matches the desired P1 component from the original decomposition, removing the row-wise scaling mismatch that plagued the prior approach (Section 2.2). The term (1 - α) ensures that the combined output remains row-normalized, avoiding magnitude drift that could destabilize the diffusion model's generation process.

**Sparse and Linear Branch Computation.** The sparse attention branch Os computes exact softmax attention only on positions selected by the mask M, while the linear attention branch Ol handles the remaining positions using kernelized approximation. Formally, the branch outputs are computed as:

$$
O_s = \text{softmax}(QK^\top / \sqrt{d} \odot M)V
$$

$$
O_l = \text{norm}(\phi(Q)\phi(K)^\top \odot (1 - M))V
$$

where d is the head dimension, ϕ(·) is the activation function for linear attention (the paper uses softmax), and norm normalizes the row sums of the matrix to 1 (Equation 14). The mask M ∈ {0, 1}^(N×N) is binary, with M[i,j] = 1 indicating that position (i,j) should use sparse attention and M[i,j] = 0 indicating linear attention. Critically, the implementation does not compute the full QK^⊤ matrix before applying the mask, as this would defeat the purpose of sparsity. Instead, the system uses a block-wise FlashAttention-style algorithm that only performs matrix multiplications for positions where M = 1 in the sparse branch and where M = 0 in the linear branch (Section 3, "Implementation of getting Os and Ol"). For the sparse branch, the system computes QK^⊤ and PV only for masked positions. For the linear branch, the system first computes K^⊤V according to positions where M = 0, then multiplies Q with the result, avoiding the full N×N score matrix computation entirely (Algorithm 2).

**Learnable Router Architecture.** The router R is a learnable module that dynamically outputs the mask M based on the input Q and K matrices, replacing the heuristic magnitude-based routing of prior work (Section 4). The router takes Q and K as inputs because attention weights are fundamentally determined by the score matrix S = QK^⊤/√d followed by row-wise softmax, making Q and K the natural and sufficient signals for routing decisions while V does not affect the formation of the attention map P (Section 8, insight 1.a). However, operating on full Q and K would incur O(N²) complexity, which is prohibitively expensive for long sequences. To reduce computational cost, the router applies mean pooling over consecutive tokens in Q and K, exploiting the observation that adjacent tokens in diffusion transformers often exhibit similar distributions (Section 4, Equation 15):

$$
\bar{Q} = \text{pool}(Q) \in \mathbb{R}^{N/b_q \times d}, \quad \bar{K} = \text{pool}(K) \in \mathbb{R}^{N/b_k \times d}
$$

where bq and bk are block sizes (the paper uses bq = 128 and bkv = 64 from Section 9.1). The pooled queries and keys are then projected through learnable linear transformations projq, projk ∈ ℝ^(d×d) before computing compressed attention scores:

$$
P_c = \text{proj}_q(\bar{Q}) \text{proj}_k(\bar{K})^\top
$$

$$
M_c = \text{Top-k}(k\%, P_c) \in \{0, 1\}^{N/b_q \times N/b_k}
$$

where Top-k is applied row-wise, setting the top k% positions to 1 and others to 0 (Equation 16). The compressed mask Mc is then expanded to the full N×N mask M for use in attention computation. The learnable projections projq and projk are critical design choices: setting them to identity matrices would recover the original heuristic routing, but learning these projections under the training objective allows the router to discover task-adaptive representations where Top-k selection better matches the desired sparse-linear decomposition (Section 8, insight 1.c).

**Differentiable Routing During Training.** A key technical challenge is that the Top-k operator is non-differentiable, which blocks gradient backpropagation during training. SLA2 addresses this by replacing Top-k with a differentiable SoftTop-k operator during the training phase (Section 6):

$$
\text{SoftTop-k}(k\%, P_c)_{ij} = \sigma\left(\frac{(P_c)_{ij}}{\tau} + \lambda_i\right)
$$

where σ denotes the sigmoid function, τ is a temperature parameter (the paper uses τ = 0.1 from Section 9.1), and λi is solved via binary search to ensure that each row sums to k% × N/bk (Equation 17). The gradient of SoftTop-k is computed using the reparameterization trick, which enables gradient backpropagation while retaining key properties of Top-k such as enforcing the row-wise sparsity constraint (Section 6). During inference, the system switches back to hard Top-k from Equation 16, ensuring that the trained model matches the inference-time computation logic (Section 7). This train-inference consistency is important because using different operators during training and inference could introduce distribution shift that degrades performance.

**Quantization-Aware Training Integration.** SLA2 integrates low-bit quantization into the sparse attention branch to achieve additional speedup beyond sparsity alone, using a quantization-aware training (QAT) approach rather than post-training quantization (Section 5). Post-training quantization applies quantization after a model is fully trained, which often introduces accuracy drops because the model weights were not optimized for low-precision representation. In contrast, QAT incorporates quantization effects during training, allowing the model to adapt its parameters to the quantization error and thereby improve low-bit accuracy at inference time. Concretely, during the forward pass, SLA2 applies low-bit quantized attention to the sparse branch:

$$
\hat{Q}, s_Q = \text{quant}(Q), \quad \hat{K}, s_K = \text{quant}(K)
$$

$$
S = \text{dequant}(\hat{Q}\hat{K}^\top / \sqrt{d}, s_Q, s_K)
$$

$$
P = \text{softmax}(S \odot M)
$$

$$
\hat{P}, s_P = \text{quant}(P), \quad \hat{V}, s_V = \text{quant}(V)
$$

$$
O_s = \text{dequant}(\hat{P}\hat{V}, s_P, s_V)
$$

where quant(·) maps an FP16 tensor to a low-bit tensor (e.g., INT8 or FP8) along with its scale, and dequant(·) rescales the result back to FP16 (Section 5). The quantization/dequantization scheme follows SageAttention2++ (Zhang et al., 2025g). Critically, the backward pass is computed entirely in FP16 using the original FP16 inputs (Q, K, V) and the forward output Os, which minimizes the end-to-end accuracy drop caused by low-bit quantization while still enabling the model to adapt to quantization noise during training (Section 5, "Backward (FP16-only)"). The paper reports that low-bit quantization provides approximately 1.3× kernel speedup in addition to the sparsity speedup (Section 9.4).

**Two-Stage Training Strategy.** SLA2 adopts a two-stage training strategy to ensure both training stability and train-inference consistency (Section 6, Algorithm 1). In Stage 1, the system initializes the router R and combination ratio α by sampling Q, K, V tensors from every attention layer at each diffusion timestep as training data, then training R and α under different sparsity settings (k% = 5%, 4%, and 3%) by minimizing the mean squared error between FullAttn(Q, K, V) and SLA2(Q, K, V, k%, R, α) (Algorithm 1, lines 1-4). This stage is necessary because before end-to-end fine-tuning of the entire diffusion model, R should be reasonably initialized; otherwise, unstable and poor routing can make subsequent fine-tuning difficult (Section 8, insight 2). In Stage 2, the system replaces the attention modules in the diffusion model with SLA2 and fine-tunes the entire diffusion model end-to-end using the standard diffusion loss over all model parameters Θ, including α but excluding R (Algorithm 1, lines 5-7). The router is excluded from Stage 2 fine-tuning because the inference router uses hard Top-k, which is non-differentiable and blocks gradient propagation; after obtaining good initialization in Stage 1 using SoftTop-k, Stage 2 keeps the routing computation aligned with inference by using hard Top-k for routing (Section 8, insight 2). The paper fine-tunes each method for 500 steps with batch size 64 for the 1.3B model and 15 for the 14B model (Section 9.1).

**Efficient Kernel Implementation.** The actual GPU kernel implementation of SLA2 is built on top of the FlashAttention algorithm to avoid computing the full score matrix S before applying mask M, instead skipping unnecessary computations entirely (Section 5, Section 7, Algorithm 2). Algorithm 2 describes the forward pass of SLA2, which divides Q, K, V into blocks and processes them in a tiled manner. For each block pair (i, j), the system checks Mc[i, j] to determine whether to use sparse or linear computation (Algorithm 2, lines 10-21). If Mc[i, j] = 1, the system performs quantized sparse attention computation for that block (lines 13-18). If Mc[i, j] = 0, the system accumulates the linear attention terms Hi and Zi (lines 19-20). After processing all blocks, the system normalizes the sparse output Os and computes the linear output Ol, then combines them using the learned ratio α (lines 23-27). The backward pass (Algorithm 3) manually derives gradients with respect to Q, K, V, Qϕ, and Kϕ, while all remaining gradients are computed via PyTorch's automatic differentiation (Appendix A). The backward pass precomputes dHi and dZi such that the main procedure involves only a single matrix addition, improving computational efficiency (Appendix A, line 14).

**Sparsity Levels and Configuration.** The paper experiments with multiple sparsity levels to evaluate the trade-off between efficiency and quality. The k% parameter controls what percentage of attention positions are assigned to the sparse branch, with the remaining positions handled by linear attention. Section 9.1 specifies that the paper uses k% of 5%, 4%, and 3% for SLA2, which corresponds to 95%, 96%, and 97% attention sparsity respectively (since sparsity = 100% - k%). Table 1 reports results at 90%, 95%, and 97% sparsity levels for comparison with baselines. The block sizes are set to bq = 128 and bkv = 64 (Section 9.1). The paper notes that 97% sparsity corresponds to about 96.7% computation savings after accounting for the linear-attention branch in SLA2 (Abstract, "Result" paragraph). On the Wan2.1-1.3B-480P model, SLA2 achieves an 18.7× speedup over FlashAttn2 at 97% sparsity (Section 9.3, Figure 4). The end-to-end video generation latency is reduced from 97s to 7s (13.9× speedup) on the 1.3B model, enabling a 2.30× reduction in overall end-to-end latency (Section 9.3, Figure 5a). On the 14B model, SLA2 achieves 4.35× end-to-end speedup (Section 9.3, Figure 5b).

**Design Choices and Alternatives.** Several design choices in SLA2 reflect careful consideration of alternatives. The decision to use Q and K as router inputs rather than including V is based on the observation that attention weights are determined by S = QK^⊤/√d followed by softmax, making V irrelevant for routing decisions (Section 8, insight 1.a). The decision to pool adjacent tokens in the router rather than operating on full QK^⊤ is based on the observation that nearby tokens in diffusion transformers often have similar distributions, making pooling effective while reducing complexity from O(N²) to O((N/b)²) (Section 8, insight 1.b). The decision to use learnable projections projq and projk rather than identity matrices allows the router to learn task-adaptive representations that better match the desired sparse-linear decomposition, generalizing the heuristic routing as a special case (Section 8, insight 1.c). The decision to use two-stage training rather than single-stage end-to-end training ensures both stable router initialization and train-inference consistency, as the non-differentiable hard Top-k used at inference cannot be directly optimized during end-to-end fine-tuning (Section 8, insight 2). The decision to use QAT rather than PTQ allows the model to adapt to quantization noise during training, reducing the accuracy drop at inference time (Section 5).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

SLA2 is not merely an engineering optimization of Sparse-Linear Attention (SLA); it represents a correction of foundational assumptions about how sparse and linear attention should interact. While prior work focused on masking strategies or post-hoc quantization, SLA2 introduces co-design principles that align mathematical theory with hardware reality. Below, we distinguish between the fundamental theoretical corrections and the practical system innovations that enable SLA2's performance.

### 4.1. Fundamental Innovation: Correcting the Decomposition Mismatch
The most critical theoretical contribution of SLA2 is the realization that the original SLA formulation was mathematically inconsistent with its own motivation.

*   **The Prior Assumption:** SLA assumed that a learnable projection on the *linear* branch (`proj(Ol)`) could compensate for errors in the *sparse* branch (Section 2.2). This forced the linear attention mechanism to perform two conflicting tasks: approximating the low-rank residual **and** correcting the scaling errors of the sparse attention.
*   **The SLA2 Insight:** The mismatch arises because sparse attention inherently normalizes rows to sum to 1, whereas the target sparse component `$P_1$` does not. SLA2 resolves this by explicitly learning the scaling ratio `$\alpha$` to combine the branches (Equation 12).
*   **Why It Matters:** This is a **fundamental innovation** because it decouples the optimization objectives of the two branches. By removing the burden of error correction from the linear branch, the model can focus each branch on its strength: sparse attention on high-magnitude details and linear attention on global context.
*   **Evidence:** The ablation study in **Table 2** shows that removing the learnable components degrades quality, but the theoretical analysis in **Section 2.2** confirms that this change aligns the implementation with the decomposition `$P = P_1 + P_2$`. Without this fix, higher sparsity levels would likely amplify the scaling error, limiting the maximum achievable speedup.

### 4.2. Algorithmic Innovation: Learning What to Compute vs. Heuristics
SLA2 shifts the paradigm of attention sparsity from static rules to dynamic optimization.

*   **The Prior Assumption:** Previous methods, including SLA, relied on **heuristic routing**. They assumed that attention weights with the largest magnitude were always the most important to compute exactly (Section 1). This is a greedy approach that ignores the global structure of the attention matrix.
*   **The SLA2 Insight:** Importance is task-dependent. SLA2 introduces a **learnable router** `$R$` with learnable projections `proj_q` and `proj_k` (Section 4). Instead of simply picking the largest values, the router learns to identify which positions are *hardest for linear attention to approximate*.
*   **Why It Matters:** This is an **algorithmic innovation** that allows the model to discover non-obvious sparse patterns. For instance, a weight with moderate magnitude might be crucial for temporal consistency in video, while a large weight might be redundant. By training the router to minimize approximation error (Algorithm 1, Stage 1), SLA2 finds a sparsity pattern that is optimal for the specific diffusion task rather than generic attention statistics.
*   **Evidence:** **Table 2** directly compares the "Learnable router" against a "Topk-router" (heuristic). The learnable router achieves significantly higher quality metrics (e.g., IQ 66.64 vs. 63.66), proving that magnitude alone is insufficient for optimal routing.

### 4.3. Systems Innovation: Quantization-Aware Sparsity
SLA2 demonstrates that sparsity and quantization are not independent optimizations but should be co-designed.

*   **The Prior Assumption:** Quantization is typically applied **post-training (PTQ)** (Section 5). The model is trained in full precision, and weights are clipped or rounded afterward. This often leads to accuracy drops because the model never learned to tolerate quantization noise.
*   **The SLA2 Insight:** Quantization effects should be simulated **during training (QAT)** specifically within the sparse branch. SLA2 quantizes `$Q, K, V$` in the forward pass but maintains full precision gradients in the backward pass (Section 5).
*   **Why It Matters:** This is a **systems innovation** that unlocks hardware efficiency without sacrificing quality. Low-bit operations (e.g., INT8) are significantly faster on modern GPUs than FP16. By adapting the model to quantization noise during fine-tuning, SLA2 enables the use of these faster kernels at inference time.
*   **Evidence:** The ablation study in **Table 2** ("w/o QAT") shows a noticeable drop in quality metrics (e.g., VR drops from 0.1039 to 0.0850) when QAT is removed. Furthermore, **Section 9.4** notes that low-bit quantization provides an additional **1.3× kernel speedup** on top of the sparsity gains, compounding the total efficiency improvement.

### 4.4. Practical Innovation: Two-Stage Training for Stability
The training strategy itself is a novel contribution necessary to make the learnable router viable.

*   **The Challenge:** The router uses a `Top-k` operation to select sparse positions, which is non-differentiable. Using a differentiable approximation (SoftTop-k) during training creates a mismatch with inference (which uses hard Top-k).
*   **The SLA2 Insight:** SLA2 decouples the training into two stages (Section 6, Algorithm 1):
    1.  **Stage 1:** Train the router `$R$` and ratio `$\alpha$` using SoftTop-k to minimize attention approximation error.
    2.  **Stage 2:** Fine-tune the diffusion model using hard Top-k (matching inference) while keeping the router fixed.
*   **Why It Matters:** This is a **practical innovation** that ensures train-inference consistency. If the model were fine-tuned entirely with SoftTop-k, it would rely on the "soft" probabilities that do not exist at inference time, leading to performance collapse. Conversely, trying to train the router from scratch with hard Top-k during end-to-end fine-tuning would be unstable due to the lack of gradients.
*   **Evidence:** **Section 8 (Insight 2)** explicitly justifies this design, noting that Stage 1 provides a "reasonable initialization" for `$R$`, while Stage 2 ensures the "routing computation [is] aligned with inference." This strategy allows SLA2 to achieve 97% sparsity without the instability that typically plagues trainable sparse attention methods.

### 4.5. Summary of Impact
The combination of these innovations allows SLA2 to break the traditional trade-off between efficiency and quality.

*   **Theoretical:** Fixes the scaling mismatch that limited prior sparse-linear methods.
*   **Algorithmic:** Replaces static heuristics with learned routing decisions.
*   **Systems:** Integrates quantization into the training loop for hardware acceleration.

As shown in **Table 1**, these insights culminate in a system that not only matches Full Attention quality but often exceeds it (e.g., IQ 67.70 vs. 63.67 on Wan2.1-1.3B), while delivering an **18.6× attention speedup** (Abstract). This suggests that efficient attention is not just about computing less, but about computing *smarter* through learned decomposition.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate SLA2's claims through rigorous experimentation. We will walk through the evaluation methodology, dissect the quantitative results, and critically assess whether the evidence supports the paper's assertions about efficiency and quality preservation.

### 5.1. Evaluation Methodology

**Models and Datasets.** The authors fine-tune SLA2 and all baseline methods on two video diffusion models from the Wan2.1 family (Wan et al., 2025):
- **Wan2.1-T2V-1.3B-480P**: A smaller model generating 480p resolution videos.
- **Wan2.1-T2V-14B-720P**: A larger model generating 720p resolution videos.

For training and evaluation data, the authors use a **private video dataset of 3,000 videos**, each approximately **5 seconds in length**, collected from public sources (Section 9.1). To construct text–video pairs for conditioning, captions are generated for each video using **Qwen3-VL-Flash**. This approach ensures that the text prompts accurately describe the video content being generated, providing consistent conditioning for both fine-tuning and evaluation.

**Baseline Methods.** The paper compares SLA2 against four key baselines (Section 9.1):
1.  **Full Attention**: Implemented with FlashAttn2, representing 0% sparsity (no acceleration).
2.  **SLA**: The prior Sparse-Linear Attention method (Zhang et al., 2025c).
3.  **VSA**: Trainable sparse attention for video diffusion (Zhang et al., 2025i).
4.  **VMoBA**: Mixture-of-block attention for video diffusion models (Wu et al., 2025).

All baseline results are obtained using official open-source implementations to ensure fair comparison.

**Quality Metrics.** Video generation quality is evaluated using multiple dimensions from **VBench** (Zhang et al., 2024), a comprehensive benchmark for video generation (Section 9.1):
-   **IQ (Imaging Quality)**: Measures the visual fidelity of individual frames.
-   **OC (Overall Consistency)**: Evaluates temporal coherence across frames.
-   **AQ (Aesthetic Quality)**: Assesses the artistic and visual appeal.
-   **MS (Motion Smoothness)**: Measures the fluidity of motion in the video.
-   **SC (Subject Consistency)**: Evaluates whether subjects remain consistent throughout the video.

Additionally, human preference is assessed using the **Vision Reward (VR)** metric (Xu et al., 2024), which provides a learned proxy for human judgment of video quality.

**Efficiency Metrics.** Computational efficiency is quantified through three measures (Section 9.1):
-   **FLOPs**: Floating-point operations, representing theoretical computational cost.
-   **Kernel Speed (C/t)**: Measured in **TOPS** (trillion operations per second), where `$C = 4N^2d$` denotes the theoretical computation amount and `$t$` is execution latency.
-   **End-to-End Latency**: Total video generation time in seconds, including all model components.

**Training Configuration.** All methods are fine-tuned for **500 steps** (Section 9.1). The batch size is set to **64 for the 1.3B model** and **15 for the 14B model**. Block sizes are configured as `$b_q = 128$` and `$b_{kv} = 64$`. For SLA2, the sparsity parameter `$k\%$` is set to **5%, 4%, and 3%**, corresponding to **95%, 96%, and 97% attention sparsity** respectively. The temperature parameter `$\tau$` in SoftTop-k is set to **0.1**.

### 5.2. Main Quantitative Results

**Video Quality at High Sparsity.** Table 1 presents the core quality and efficiency comparisons. The most striking result is that SLA2 not only preserves quality at high sparsity but often **exceeds Full Attention** performance.

On the **Wan2.1-1.3B-480P** model at **97% sparsity**:
-   SLA2 achieves **IQ = 66.64**, compared to Full Attention's **IQ = 63.67** (a **+2.97 point improvement**).
-   SLA2 achieves **VR = 0.1039**, compared to Full Attention's **VR = 0.1084** (only a **-0.0045 difference**, essentially equivalent).
-   SLA2 achieves **MS = 98.04**, compared to Full Attention's **MS = 98.95** (a minor **-0.91 point decrease**).

This quality preservation is remarkable given that SLA2 computes only **3% of attention positions** using exact sparse attention, with the remaining 97% handled by the approximate linear branch.

**Comparison Against Baselines.** SLA2 consistently outperforms all baseline methods across sparsity levels. At **90% sparsity** on the 1.3B model:
-   SLA2 achieves **IQ = 67.70**, while SLA achieves **IQ = 63.10**, VSA achieves **IQ = 59.57**, and VMoBA achieves **IQ = 65.31**.
-   SLA2's **VR = 0.1093** exceeds all baselines (SLA: 0.0872, VSA: -0.0881, VMoBA: 0.0936).

Even more impressively, **SLA2 at 97% sparsity outperforms all baselines at 90% sparsity**. For example, SLA2 (97%) achieves **IQ = 66.64**, while the best baseline at 90% (VMoBA) achieves only **IQ = 65.31**. This demonstrates that SLA2's learnable routing and corrected formulation enable higher sparsity without the quality degradation that plagues other methods.

**Efficiency Gains.** The efficiency results are equally compelling. Figure 4 shows kernel-level speed measurements on an **RTX5090 GPU**:
-   At **97% sparsity**, SLA2 achieves **4079 TOPS**, compared to FlashAttn2's **219 TOPS**.
-   This represents an **18.7× speedup** in attention kernel execution.
-   SLA2 at 97% is also **11.7× faster than VMoBA at 95%** (348 TOPS) and **2.6× faster than VSA at 95%** (1553 TOPS).

The paper notes that **97% sparsity corresponds to about 96.7% computation savings** after accounting for the linear-attention branch overhead (Abstract). This slight reduction from the nominal 97% reflects the fact that linear attention still requires some computation, though at much lower complexity than full attention.

**End-to-End Latency.** Figure 5 presents end-to-end video generation latency, which is the metric that matters most for practical deployment:
-   On the **Wan2.1-1.3B-480P** model, attention latency is reduced from **97s to 7s** (a **13.9× speedup**).
-   This enables a **2.30× reduction in overall end-to-end latency** (from 62s to 27s approximately, based on the "Others" + "Attention" breakdown in Figure 5a).
-   On the **Wan2.1-14B-720P** model, SLA2 achieves a **4.35× end-to-end speedup** (Figure 5b).

The larger end-to-end speedup on the 14B model (4.35× vs. 2.30×) reflects the fact that attention constitutes a larger proportion of total computation in larger models, making attention optimizations more impactful.

**Visual Quality Examples.** Figures 2 and 3 provide qualitative comparisons:
-   **Figure 2** (1.3B model): Videos produced by SLA2 at 97% sparsity "exhibit the highest quality and maintain content similar to that generated by Full Attention" (Section 9.2). In contrast, videos from other methods "either differ noticeably from Full Attention or show clear distortions."
-   **Figure 3** (14B model): SLA2 at 95% and 97% sparsity brings "almost no degradation in video quality" compared to Full Attention.

These visual examples support the quantitative metrics, demonstrating that the high VBench scores translate to perceptually similar outputs.

### 5.3. Ablation Studies

Table 2 presents ablation experiments that isolate the contribution of each SLA2 component. These results are critical for validating the paper's claims about *why* SLA2 works.

**Quantization-Aware Training (QAT).** The row labeled **"w/o QAT"** in Table 2 shows the quality when SLA2 is fine-tuned without quantization-aware training but still performs quantized inference:
-   **IQ drops from 66.64 to 65.28** (a **-1.36 point decrease**).
-   **VR drops from 0.1039 to 0.0850** (a **-0.0189 decrease**, representing an **18% relative degradation**).
-   **AQ drops from 64.62 to 61.85** (a **-2.77 point decrease**).

This confirms that QAT is essential for maintaining quality when using low-bit attention. The paper reports that low-bit quantization provides an **approximately 1.3× kernel speedup** in addition to sparsity gains (Section 9.4). Without QAT, this speedup would come at an unacceptable quality cost.

**Learnable Router.** The row labeled **"Topk-router"** compares SLA2's learnable router against the heuristic Top-k router used in the original SLA:
-   **IQ drops from 66.64 to 63.66** (a **-2.98 point decrease**, nearly matching Full Attention's baseline of 63.67).
-   **VR drops from 0.1039 to 0.0876** (a **-0.0163 decrease**).
-   **SC drops from 94.83 to 94.26**.

This ablation directly validates the paper's claim that **magnitude-based heuristic routing is suboptimal**. The learnable router, trained to minimize approximation error, discovers sparsity patterns that better preserve video quality. This is one of the most important findings in the paper: the routing decision cannot be left to a static rule.

**Varying Sparsity Levels.** Table 2 also shows SLA2 performance at different sparsity levels:
-   **85% sparsity**: IQ = 67.97, VR = 0.1135
-   **90% sparsity**: IQ = 67.70, VR = 0.1093
-   **95% sparsity**: IQ = 67.04, VR = 0.1023
-   **97% sparsity**: IQ = 66.64, VR = 0.1039

The trend shows that **lower sparsity consistently leads to better performance**, as expected. However, the degradation is gradual rather than abrupt. Notably, even at 97% sparsity, SLA2 still outperforms all baselines at 90% sparsity (Table 1), demonstrating the effectiveness of the learnable components.

The slight increase in VR from 95% to 97% (0.1023 to 0.1039) while IQ decreases (67.04 to 66.64) suggests some metric variance, but the overall trend confirms the expected trade-off between sparsity and quality.

### 5.4. Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

**Do the Experiments Support the Claims?**

The experimental evidence strongly supports the paper's three main claims:

1.  **Claim: SLA2 achieves 97% sparsity with 18.6× speedup.**
    -   **Supported.** Figure 4 shows 18.7× kernel speedup at 97% sparsity. The Abstract reports 18.6× attention runtime speedup. The slight discrepancy (18.6× vs. 18.7×) likely reflects different measurement conditions, but both confirm the order of magnitude.

2.  **Claim: SLA2 preserves generation quality at high sparsity.**
    -   **Supported.** Table 1 shows SLA2 at 97% sparsity achieves IQ = 66.64, exceeding Full Attention's IQ = 63.67. The VR metric (0.1039 vs. 0.1084) shows minimal degradation. Visual examples in Figures 2 and 3 confirm perceptual similarity.

3.  **Claim: Learnable components (router, α, QAT) are essential.**
    -   **Supported.** Table 2 ablations show significant quality drops when each component is removed:
        -   Without QAT: VR drops 18%
        -   Without learnable router: IQ drops 2.98 points
        -   This validates the design choices in Sections 3-5.

**Potential Limitations and Conditions.**

Despite the strong results, several conditions and limitations should be noted:

1.  **Fine-Tuning Dataset Quality.** The authors acknowledge that sparse attention methods can outperform Full Attention after fine-tuning, attributing this to "the higher quality of the fine-tuning dataset compared to that used during pretraining" (Section 9.2). This suggests that some quality improvements may come from the fine-tuning data rather than the attention mechanism itself. A more controlled experiment would fine-tune Full Attention on the same dataset to isolate the attention mechanism's contribution.

2.  **Single Dataset.** All experiments use a private dataset of 3,000 videos. While this is reasonable for proprietary research, it limits reproducibility and generalizability. Results on public benchmarks (e.g., UCF-101, Kinetics) would strengthen the claims.

3.  **Hardware-Specific Results.** The kernel speed measurements are on an **RTX5090**, which was not commercially available at the time of writing (the paper is dated February 2026). This raises questions about reproducibility on more common hardware (e.g., A100, H100). The efficiency gains may vary significantly across GPU architectures.

4.  **Sequential CPU Offloading for 14B Model.** For the Wan2.1-14B-720P model, the authors note that it "exceeds the VRAM capacity of a single RTX5090," requiring "sequential CPU offloading during evaluation" (Section 9.3). The reported latency "already excludes the offloading overhead." This means the 4.35× end-to-end speedup does not account for data transfer costs, which could be significant in practice.

5.  **Limited Sparsity Range.** The experiments test sparsity levels from 85% to 97%. It is unclear whether SLA2 can maintain quality at even higher sparsity (e.g., 99%), which would be valuable for extreme efficiency scenarios.

**Trade-Offs.**

The experiments reveal clear trade-offs that practitioners should consider:

-   **Sparsity vs. Quality**: Table 2 shows a gradual quality decrease as sparsity increases from 85% to 97%. For applications requiring maximum fidelity, 90-95% sparsity may be preferable. For latency-critical applications, 97% sparsity offers the best efficiency with acceptable quality.

-   **Training Cost vs. Inference Speed**: SLA2 requires two-stage fine-tuning (500 steps), which adds training overhead. This is a one-time cost that pays off during inference, but it means SLA2 is not a "plug-and-play" solution for already-trained models.

-   **Model Size Dependence**: The end-to-end speedup is larger for the 14B model (4.35×) than the 1.3B model (2.30×). This suggests SLA2 provides greater relative benefits for larger models where attention dominates computation.

### 5.5. Summary of Experimental Findings

The experimental analysis provides robust evidence for SLA2's effectiveness:

| Metric | Full Attention | SLA2 (97%) | Improvement |
|--------|---------------|------------|-------------|
| IQ (1.3B) | 63.67 | 66.64 | +2.97 |
| VR (1.3B) | 0.1084 | 0.1039 | -0.0045 |
| Kernel Speed | 219 TOPS | 4079 TOPS | 18.7× |
| End-to-End (1.3B) | 62s | ~27s | 2.30× |
| End-to-End (14B) | 493s | ~113s | 4.35× |

The ablation studies confirm that each component—learnable router, learnable ratio α, and QAT—contributes meaningfully to the final performance. The visual examples in Figures 2 and 3 provide qualitative confirmation that the quantitative metrics reflect perceptual quality.

However, practitioners should be aware of the conditions: results depend on fine-tuning quality, hardware specifics, and the sparsity-quality trade-off. For production deployment, the 95% sparsity configuration may offer the best balance, achieving near-maximum speedup with slightly better quality than 97% sparsity (Table 2: IQ 67.04 vs. 66.64).

Overall, the experiments convincingly demonstrate that SLA2 achieves its stated goals of high sparsity, significant speedup, and quality preservation, making it a practical solution for efficient video generation in diffusion models.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While SLA2 demonstrates impressive efficiency gains and quality preservation, a critical examination reveals several assumptions, constraints, and open questions that practitioners should consider before adoption. This section dissects the limitations inherent in the approach, grounding each point in evidence from the paper.

### 6.1. Training Overhead and Fine-Tuning Dependency

**SLA2 is not a plug-and-play solution.** Unlike training-free sparse attention methods (e.g., SpargeAttention, Section 10), SLA2 requires a two-stage fine-tuning process to function correctly. This introduces several practical constraints:

- **Training Cost:** The paper specifies fine-tuning for **500 steps** with batch sizes of **64 (1.3B model)** and **15 (14B model)** (Section 9.1). While this is modest compared to full pretraining, it represents non-trivial computational overhead that must be incurred before inference speedups can be realized.

- **Stage 1 Initialization Requirement:** The router `$R$` and ratio `$\alpha$` must be initialized in Stage 1 before end-to-end fine-tuning (Algorithm 1). Section 8 (Insight 2) explicitly states: *"Otherwise, unstable and poor routing can make subsequent fine-tuning difficult."* This suggests that SLA2 cannot be trained in a single pass, adding complexity to the training pipeline.

- **Router Fixed in Stage 2:** During Stage 2 fine-tuning, the router `$R$` is **not updated**—only the diffusion model parameters `$\Theta$` and `$\alpha$` are optimized (Algorithm 1, lines 5-7). This means the router cannot adapt to task-specific patterns discovered during end-to-end training. If the Stage 1 initialization is suboptimal, there is no mechanism to correct it during Stage 2.

**Trade-off:** The training overhead is a one-time cost that pays off during inference, but this makes SLA2 unsuitable for scenarios where models must be deployed immediately without fine-tuning, or where compute resources for fine-tuning are unavailable.

### 6.2. Dataset and Quality Attribution Concerns

**The source of quality improvements is ambiguous.** In Section 9.2, the authors make a notable admission:

> *"Interestingly, we observe that sparse attention methods can even outperform Full Attention on many metrics after fine-tuning. We attribute this to the higher quality of the fine-tuning dataset compared to that used during pretraining."*

This statement reveals a critical limitation:

- **Confounding Variable:** The reported quality improvements (e.g., IQ 66.64 vs. 63.67 for Full Attention in Table 1) may not be solely attributable to SLA2's attention mechanism. The fine-tuning dataset itself—**3,000 videos from public sources** with captions generated by Qwen3-VL-Flash (Section 9.1)—may be of higher quality than the original pretraining data.

- **Missing Control Experiment:** The paper does not include an experiment where **Full Attention is fine-tuned on the same dataset** for 500 steps. Without this control, it is impossible to isolate how much of the quality gain comes from SLA2's architecture versus the fine-tuning data quality.

- **Private Dataset:** The dataset is **private**, limiting reproducibility. Other researchers cannot verify whether the results generalize to different video domains or caption qualities.

**Trade-off:** Practitioners should expect that SLA2's quality advantages may depend heavily on access to high-quality fine-tuning data. Organizations without similar datasets may not achieve the same performance gains.

### 6.3. Hardware and Infrastructure Constraints

**The efficiency results are hardware-specific and may not generalize.** Several infrastructure-related limitations affect the practical applicability of SLA2:

- **RTX5090 Dependency:** All kernel speed measurements (Figure 4) are conducted on an **RTX5090 GPU** (Section 9.3). This GPU was not commercially available at the time of writing (paper dated February 2026), raising reproducibility concerns. Performance on widely deployed hardware (e.g., A100, H100) is not reported, and the speedup ratios may vary significantly across architectures.

- **VRAM Limitations for Large Models:** For the Wan2.1-14B-720P model, the paper states: *"Since the Wan2.1-14B-720P model exceeds the VRAM capacity of a single RTX5090, we enable sequential CPU offloading during evaluation"* (Section 9.3). Critically, **the reported latency excludes the offloading overhead.** In practice, CPU-GPU data transfer can introduce significant latency, especially for large models. The **4.35× end-to-end speedup** (Figure 5b) may be optimistic for deployments without sufficient VRAM.

- **Block Size Sensitivity:** The paper uses fixed block sizes `$b_q = 128$` and `$b_{kv} = 64$` (Section 9.1). These values are not ablated, and their optimality for different sequence lengths or resolutions is unknown. Suboptimal block sizes could reduce the achieved speedup.

**Trade-off:** Organizations deploying on hardware with limited VRAM or different GPU architectures may experience lower speedups than reported. The 14B model results, in particular, should be interpreted with caution due to excluded offloading costs.

### 6.4. Sparsity Range and Extrapolation Uncertainty

**The tested sparsity range is limited.** The paper evaluates SLA2 at sparsity levels from **85% to 97%** (Table 2), but does not explore:

- **Higher Sparsity (98%-99%+):** It is unknown whether SLA2 can maintain quality at extreme sparsity levels. For applications requiring maximum efficiency (e.g., mobile deployment), knowing the failure point is critical.

- **Lower Sparsity (<85%):** The paper does not test sparsity below 85%, though this regime may be relevant for applications prioritizing quality over speed.

- **Dynamic Sparsity:** The sparsity parameter `$k\%$` is fixed per model (5%, 4%, or 3% for 95%, 96%, 97% sparsity respectively, Section 9.1). SLA2 does not support **adaptive sparsity** that adjusts based on content complexity or latency requirements at inference time.

**Trade-off:** Practitioners must choose a fixed sparsity level at fine-tuning time. The 95% sparsity configuration may offer a better quality-efficiency balance than 97% (Table 2: IQ 67.04 vs. 66.64), but this requires maintaining multiple fine-tuned model variants.

### 6.5. Router Design Limitations

**The learnable router has inherent constraints that may limit its effectiveness:**

- **Q and K Only:** The router `$R$` takes only `$Q$` and `$K$` as inputs, excluding `$V$` (Section 4). Section 8 (Insight 1.a) justifies this by noting that attention weights are determined by `$S = QK^\top / \sqrt{d}$` followed by softmax. However, `$V$` contains semantic information that could potentially inform routing decisions—for example, tokens with high-value magnitude might warrant sparse attention regardless of their attention score.

- **Pooling Loss:** The router operates on **pooled** `$\bar{Q}$` and `$\bar{K}$` to reduce complexity (Equation 15). While Section 8 (Insight 1.b) notes that adjacent tokens often have similar distributions, pooling inevitably loses fine-grained information. For videos with rapid motion or sharp transitions, the pooled representation may miss important routing signals.

- **SoftTop-k Train-Inference Mismatch:** During Stage 1 training, SLA2 uses **SoftTop-k** (differentiable), but inference uses **hard Top-k** (Section 6-7). While Section 8 (Insight 2) argues that Stage 2 fine-tuning with hard Top-k ensures consistency, there remains a potential distribution shift between Stage 1 router training and final inference behavior.

**Trade-off:** The router's design prioritizes efficiency over expressiveness. Applications with highly variable attention patterns (e.g., fast-action videos) may benefit from a more expressive router, but this would increase routing overhead.

### 6.6. Quantization Scheme Specificity

**The quantization implementation has unstated constraints:**

- **Bit-Width Ambiguity:** Section 5 mentions low-bit tensors (e.g., **INT8 or FP8**) but does not specify which bit-width was used in the main experiments. The ablation study (Table 2, "w/o QAT") confirms QAT's importance but does not report results for different bit-widths.

- **SageAttention2++ Dependency:** The quantization/dequantization scheme follows **SageAttention2++** (Zhang et al., 2025g, Section 5). This creates a dependency on external implementation details that are not fully described in this paper. Changes to the quantization scheme could affect SLA2's performance.

- **Sparse Branch Only:** QAT is applied only to the **sparse attention branch** `$O_s$` (Section 5). The linear branch `$O_l$` remains in full precision. This asymmetry is not ablated, and it is unknown whether quantizing the linear branch could provide additional speedup.

**Trade-off:** The 1.3× kernel speedup from low-bit quantization (Section 9.4) is contingent on the specific SageAttention2++ implementation. Organizations using different quantization libraries may not achieve the same gains.

### 6.7. Scalability to Longer Sequences

**The paper does not address very long sequence scenarios:**

- **Sequence Length Not Specified:** The paper reports sequence length `$N$` in notation (Section 2.1) but does not specify the actual sequence lengths used in experiments. Video diffusion models can have sequences ranging from thousands to tens of thousands of tokens depending on resolution and frame count.

- **Linear Attention Approximation Error:** The linear attention branch approximates the remaining 97% of attention positions. For very long sequences, approximation errors could accumulate, potentially degrading temporal consistency. The paper does not evaluate SLA2 on videos longer than **5 seconds** (Section 9.1 dataset description).

- **Memory Scaling:** While sparsity reduces computation, the paper does not report memory consumption at different sequence lengths. The router's compressed mask `$M_c \in \{0, 1\}^{N/b_q \times N/b_k}$` still scales quadratically with respect to the number of blocks, which could become a bottleneck for extremely long sequences.

**Trade-off:** SLA2 is validated for ~5-second videos at 480p/720p resolution. Applications requiring longer videos (e.g., 30+ seconds) or higher resolutions may experience different efficiency-quality trade-offs that are not characterized in this work.

### 6.8. Summary of Key Trade-offs

| Limitation | Impact | Mitigation |
|------------|--------|------------|
| Two-stage fine-tuning | Adds training overhead | Accept as one-time cost for inference gains |
| Dataset quality dependency | Results may not generalize | Fine-tune on domain-specific data |
| RTX5090-specific benchmarks | Hardware reproducibility concerns | Validate on target deployment hardware |
| CPU offloading excluded (14B) | Latency may be underestimated | Account for data transfer costs in planning |
| Fixed sparsity per model | No adaptive inference | Maintain multiple model variants |
| Router excludes V | May miss semantic routing signals | Accept as efficiency trade-off |
| QAT bit-width unspecified | Quantization gains may vary | Test with target quantization scheme |
| 5-second video limit | Long-form video untested | Evaluate on target video lengths |

### 6.9. Open Questions for Future Work

Based on the limitations identified, several open questions remain:

1. **Can SLA2 maintain quality at 99%+ sparsity?** The paper stops at 97%, but extreme sparsity would be valuable for edge deployments.

2. **How does SLA2 perform on public benchmarks?** Results on Kinetics, UCF-101, or other standard video datasets would improve reproducibility.

3. **What is the optimal block size for different resolutions?** The fixed `$b_q = 128, b_{kv} = 64$` configuration may not be optimal for all use cases.

4. **Can the router be made adaptive at inference time?** Dynamic sparsity adjustment based on content complexity could improve the quality-efficiency trade-off.

5. **How does SLA2 scale to multi-GPU or distributed inference?** The paper evaluates single-GPU performance; distributed deployment characteristics are unknown.

In summary, SLA2 represents a significant advance in efficient attention for video diffusion, but its practical deployment requires careful consideration of training costs, hardware constraints, and domain-specific validation. The limitations identified here should guide practitioners in setting realistic expectations and planning appropriate evaluation protocols for their specific use cases.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

SLA2 represents more than an incremental efficiency improvement—it signals a paradigm shift in how we approach attention acceleration for diffusion models. By demonstrating that **learned decomposition** outperforms heuristic sparsity, this work reframes efficiency as an optimization problem rather than a masking strategy. This section explores how SLA2 changes the research landscape, what follow-up work it enables, and when practitioners should adopt this approach.

### 7.1. Shifting the Research Landscape

**From Heuristics to Learned Routing.** Prior sparse attention methods relied on static rules: mask tokens with low attention magnitude (Section 2.4), use fixed patterns (e.g., sliding windows), or apply training-free pruning. SLA2 demonstrates that **magnitude alone is insufficient** for optimal sparsity—the ablation study in Table 2 shows a **2.98-point IQ drop** when replacing the learnable router with heuristic Top-k. This finding challenges a foundational assumption in the field and suggests that future sparse attention methods should treat routing as a **learnable component** rather than a fixed preprocessing step.

**Correcting Theoretical Inconsistencies.** SLA2's reformulation of the sparse-linear combination (Equation 13) addresses a mathematical mismatch that went uncorrected in the original SLA (Section 2.3). By explicitly learning the ratio `$\alpha$` instead of forcing the linear branch to compensate via `proj(Ol)`, SLA2 decouples the optimization objectives of the two branches. This insight extends beyond video diffusion: **any hybrid attention mechanism** that combines multiple approximation strategies should verify that its formulation aligns with its theoretical motivation. Future work on mixture-of-experts attention or multi-kernel attention should adopt similar consistency checks.

**Quantization as Co-Design, Not Afterthought.** The **1.3× kernel speedup** from QAT (Section 9.4) combined with the **18% VR degradation** when QAT is removed (Table 2) demonstrates that quantization cannot be treated as a post-training optimization. SLA2 establishes a template for **hardware-aware training**: simulate low-bit effects during forward pass while maintaining full-precision gradients for backpropagation (Section 5). This approach should become standard for any efficiency-focused architecture targeting deployment on edge devices or latency-sensitive applications.

**Trainable vs. Training-Free Trade-offs.** SLA2 joins a growing class of **trainable sparse attention** methods (Section 10) that accept fine-tuning overhead in exchange for higher sparsity. The paper's results suggest this trade-off is favorable: trainable methods achieve **97% sparsity** while training-free methods typically cap at **90-95%** with greater quality loss. This shifts the field's focus from "can we avoid training?" to "what is the minimum fine-tuning required for deployment-specific optimization?"

### 7.2. Follow-Up Research Directions

SLA2 opens several concrete research questions that the paper explicitly or implicitly raises:

**1. Adaptive Sparsity at Inference.** The current SLA2 implementation uses a **fixed sparsity level** (`$k\% = 3\%, 4\%, \text{or } 5\%$`) determined at fine-tuning time (Section 9.1). A natural extension is **dynamic sparsity** that adjusts based on content complexity—for example, using higher sparsity for static scenes and lower sparsity for fast motion. This would require:
- A mechanism to estimate scene complexity from `$Q$` and `$K$` before routing
- Training strategies that support multiple sparsity levels in a single model
- Kernel implementations that can switch between sparsity configurations without recompilation

**2. Router Expressiveness vs. Overhead.** The current router `$R$` uses **pooled `$Q$` and `$K$`** to reduce complexity (Equation 15), excluding `$V$` entirely (Section 8, Insight 1.a). Future work could explore:
- Whether including `$V$` improves routing for semantically complex videos
- Alternative pooling strategies (e.g., attention-based pooling vs. mean pooling)
- Hierarchical routers that make coarse-to-fine routing decisions

**3. Generalization Across Model Families.** SLA2 is validated on **Wan2.1-1.3B and 14B** models (Section 9.1). Open questions include:
- Does SLA2 transfer to other video diffusion architectures (e.g., Sora, CogVideoX)?
- Can a router trained on one model family generalize to another, reducing fine-tuning costs?
- How does SLA2 scale to image diffusion models or text-to-image tasks?

**4. Extreme Sparsity Boundaries.** The paper tests sparsity up to **97%** (Table 2), but does not explore the failure point. Follow-up work should:
- Identify the **maximum achievable sparsity** before quality collapses
- Determine whether different video genres (e.g., talking heads vs. action scenes) have different sparsity tolerances
- Explore whether combining SLA2 with other compression techniques (e.g., KV-cache quantization) enables 99%+ sparsity

**5. Long-Form Video Generation.** The evaluation dataset contains **~5-second videos** (Section 9.1). SLA2's behavior on longer sequences is unknown:
- Does approximation error accumulate over hundreds of frames?
- Can the linear attention branch maintain temporal consistency for 30+ second videos?
- Are there sequence-length-dependent routing patterns that the current router misses?

**6. Multi-GPU and Distributed Inference.** All experiments use **single-GPU evaluation** (Section 9.3). For production deployment:
- How does SLA2 scale across multiple GPUs?
- Does the router's block-wise computation introduce communication bottlenecks?
- Can SLA2's sparsity pattern be exploited for model parallelism?

### 7.3. Practical Applications and Use Cases

SLA2's efficiency profile makes it suitable for several deployment scenarios:

**Real-Time Video Generation.** The **2.30× end-to-end speedup** on the 1.3B model (Figure 5a) reduces generation time from **62s to ~27s**. For interactive applications (e.g., video editing tools, real-time avatars), this brings diffusion-based generation closer to acceptable latency thresholds. The **4.35× speedup** on the 14B model (Figure 5b) is even more impactful for high-quality production pipelines.

**Edge and Mobile Deployment.** The combination of **sparsity + low-bit quantization** reduces both computation and memory footprint. While the paper evaluates on RTX5090, the **1.3× kernel speedup from QAT** (Section 9.4) suggests SLA2 could run on hardware with limited FP16 throughput, such as mobile GPUs or NPUs. Practitioners should validate performance on target hardware, as the RTX5090-specific benchmarks may not generalize (Section 6.3).

**High-Throughput Batch Generation.** For applications generating many videos in parallel (e.g., marketing content, personalized video ads), SLA2's **18.6× attention speedup** (Abstract) translates directly to throughput gains. The fixed sparsity per model means organizations can maintain multiple variants (e.g., 90%, 95%, 97% sparsity) and select based on latency budgets.

**Fine-Tuning as a Service.** Since SLA2 requires **500 steps of fine-tuning** (Section 9.1), cloud providers could offer SLA2 adaptation as a service: customers upload a base diffusion model, and the provider returns an SLA2-optimized variant. This abstracts away the two-stage training complexity (Algorithm 1) while enabling efficiency gains.

| **No fine-tuning budget** | Training-free sparse attention (e.g., SpargeAttention) | SLA2 requires 500-step fine-tuning (Section 9.1) |
| **Maximum quality priority** | Full Attention or SLA2 at 85-90% sparsity | Table 2 shows IQ drops from 67.97 (85%) to 66.64 (97%) |
| **Latency-critical deployment** | SLA2 at 95-97% sparsity | 18.6× attention speedup with acceptable quality loss |
| **Large model (10B+)** | SLA2 (greater relative benefit) | 4.35× end-to-end speedup on 14B vs. 2.30× on 1.3B (Figure 5) |
| **Small model (<2B)** | Evaluate cost-benefit carefully | Non-attention components dominate latency |
| **Variable content types** | Maintain multiple SLA2 variants | Fixed sparsity per model; no adaptive inference |
| **Public benchmark evaluation** | Use SLA2 + report fine-tuning data | Quality gains may reflect dataset quality (Section 9.2) |

**Reproduction Checklist.** Practitioners attempting to reproduce or extend SLA2 should note:

1. **Hardware Requirements.** Kernel benchmarks use **RTX5090** (Section 9.3). Results on A100/H100 may differ. For the 14B model, ensure sufficient VRAM or account for CPU offloading overhead (excluded from reported latency).

2. **Training Data.** The paper uses a **private 3,000-video dataset** with Qwen3-VL-Flash captions (Section 9.1). Reproduction requires comparable quality data; lower-quality fine-tuning data may not achieve the reported quality improvements over Full Attention.

3. **Two-Stage Training.** Do not skip Stage 1 router initialization. Section 8 (Insight 2) warns that *"unstable and poor routing can make subsequent fine-tuning difficult."* Use SoftTop-k in Stage 1, hard Top-k in Stage 2.

4. **Block Sizes.** The paper uses `$b_q = 128$`, `$b_{kv} = 64$` (Section 9.1). These are not ablated; different sequence lengths or resolutions may require tuning.

5. **Quantization Scheme.** QAT follows **SageAttention2++** (Section 5). Bit-width (INT8 vs. FP8) is not specified in main experiments. Test with your target quantization library.

6. **Sparsity Configuration.** The paper tests `$k\% = 3\%, 4\%, 5\%$` (97%, 96%, 95% sparsity). For new applications, start at 90% sparsity and increase gradually while monitoring quality metrics.

**Key Integration Pitfalls to Avoid.**

- **Skipping Stage 1:** Training the router from scratch during end-to-end fine-tuning leads to instability (Section 8).
- **Using SoftTop-k at Inference:** Inference must use hard Top-k for consistency; SoftTop-k is training-only (Section 6-7).
- **Ignoring Offloading Costs:** For models exceeding VRAM, reported latency excludes CPU-GPU transfer time (Section 9.3).
- **Assuming Generalization:** Router trained on one model may not transfer to another without re-initialization (Section 6.2).

### 7.5. Long-Term Vision

SLA2 points toward a future where **efficiency is learned, not engineered**. Rather than manually designing sparse patterns or quantization schedules, models will discover optimal computation strategies through task-specific optimization. This vision extends beyond attention:

- **Learned Computation Graphs:** Similar principles could apply to MLP layers, normalization, or activation functions.
- **Hardware-Co-Designed Architectures:** Training simulations of target hardware (e.g., INT8 kernels, memory bandwidth) could produce architectures optimized for specific deployment environments.
- **Dynamic Model Selection:** A single model could contain multiple sparsity/quantization configurations, selecting at inference time based on latency budgets.

SLA2 demonstrates that this future is achievable for attention mechanisms. The challenge for the field is to extend these principles to the full model architecture while maintaining the theoretical rigor that makes SLA2's contributions robust.
