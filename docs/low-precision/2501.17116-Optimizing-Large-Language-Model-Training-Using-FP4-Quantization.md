## 1. Executive Summary

This work introduces the first feasible FP4 training framework for Large Language Models, addressing the extreme computational costs of training by overcoming the significant quantization errors inherent to 4-bit precision through a Differentiable Gradient Estimator and Outlier Clamping and Compensation strategy. Experimental results demonstrate that the framework achieves accuracy comparable to BF16 baselines while scaling effectively to 13B-parameter LLaMA 2 models trained on 100B tokens from the DCLM dataset, despite the format supporting only 16 distinct representable values. By validating ultra-low precision training stability, this research sets a foundation for efficient model development on next-generation hardware like NVIDIA's B200 GPUs that natively support FP4 tensor cores.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this work, we must first examine the escalating computational demands of modern AI and the specific technical barriers preventing the adoption of ultra-low precision arithmetic. This section breaks down the economic and engineering pressures driving the research, the mathematical constraints of 4-bit formats, and the gaps in prior literature that this paper aims to fill.

### 2.1. The Computational Bottleneck of LLM Scaling

The primary driver for this research is the unsustainable cost of training Large Language Models (LLMs) at scale. Theoretical analyses and empirical evidence consistently show that increasing model size leads to substantial performance improvements (Kaplan et al., 2020; Bi et al., 2024). However, this scaling law comes with a heavy infrastructure tax.

Consider the resource requirements for recent state-of-the-art models:
*   **Llama 3 405B:** Trained on up to `$16,000$` NVIDIA H100 GPUs for `$54$` days (Dubey et al., 2024).
*   **GPT-4:** Estimated at `$1$` trillion parameters, requiring an extraordinary amount of computational power (Achiam et al., 2023).

These examples highlight a critical inefficiency: traditional training systems rely on `$\text{FP32}$` (32-bit floating point) or `$\text{BF16}$` (16-bit brain floating point) data formats. While precise, these formats consume significant memory bandwidth and energy. As model sizes grow, the memory capacity required to store weights, gradients, and optimizer states often becomes the limiting factor, not just raw compute speed. This creates an urgent need for methods that reduce the bit-width of arithmetic operations without sacrificing model quality.

### 2.2. The Quantization Hierarchy and the FP4 Barrier

Quantization is the process of mapping high-precision numbers (like `$\text{BF16}$`) to lower-precision formats (like `$\text{FP8}$` or `$\text{FP4}$`). The goal is to reduce memory usage and accelerate matrix multiplications, which account for over `$95\%$` of the computational workload in Transformer training (Section 4.1).

Recent hardware advancements have begun to support lower precisions:
*   **NVIDIA H100 (Hopper):** Supports `$\text{FP8}$` tensor cores, offering a `$2\times$` speed-up compared to `$\text{FP16}$` (Nvidia, 2023).
*   **NVIDIA B200 (Blackwell):** Extends support to `$\text{FP6}$` and `$\text{FP4}$`, with `$\text{FP4}$` offering the potential to double computational throughput over `$\text{FP8}$` (Nvidia, 2024).

However, moving from `$\text{FP8}$` to `$\text{FP4}$` is not a linear step; it represents a fundamental cliff in representational capacity. To understand why, we must look at the IEEE 754 floating-point structure. A format is denoted as `$\text{E}x\text{M}y$`, where `$x$` is exponent bits and `$y$` is mantissa bits.
*   **$\text{BF16}$:** `$\text{E8M7}$` (High dynamic range, high precision).
*   **$\text{FP8}$:** Typically `$\text{E4M3}$` or `$\text{E5M2}$` (64 to 256 distinct values).
*   **$\text{FP4}$:** Uses `$\text{E2M1}$` format (Section 2).

The `$\text{E2M1}$` configuration supports only `$2^4 = 16$` distinct representable values. This extreme sparsity creates two specific technical challenges:
1.  **Limited Dynamic Range:** The maximum absolute value (`$\text{MAX}_{\text{FP4}}$`) is only `$6.0$`. Values exceeding this threshold overflow, while very small values underflow to zero.
2.  **Quantization Error:** With only 16 steps to represent a continuous distribution, the rounding error introduced during quantization is massive compared to `$\text{FP8}$` or `$\text{BF16}$`.

The severity of this issue is visualized in **Figure 1**. The paper shows that "Directly casting to `$\text{FP4}$` results in significantly higher training loss," whereas the proposed method achieves accuracy comparable to the `$\text{BF16}$` baseline. This gap demonstrates that naive quantization is insufficient; the training algorithm itself must be modified to accommodate the precision loss.

### 2.3. Limitations of Prior Approaches

Before this work, several strategies existed to reduce precision, but each fell short of enabling stable `$\text{FP4}$` *pretraining* for LLMs.

#### 2.3.1. FP8 Training Frameworks
Work such as **FP8-LM** (Peng et al., 2023) and NVIDIA's **Transformer Engine** (Nvidia, 2022) successfully demonstrated `$\text{FP8}$` training. These methods rely on scaling factors to manage dynamic range. However, techniques that work for `$\text{FP8}$` (which has 64+ values) often fail at `$\text{FP4}$` due to the coarser granularity. The paper notes that while `$\text{FP8}$` tensor cores are feasible, "the application of `$\text{FP4}$` tensor cores in model training remains an open research question" (Section 1).

#### 2.3.2. Inference vs. Training Quantization
A significant portion of 4-bit research focuses on **inference** rather than training.
*   **Post-Training Quantization (PTQ):** Quantizes a already-trained model (Dettmers et al., 2022). This does not reduce the cost of the initial training run.
*   **Quantization Aware Training (QAT):** Fine-tunes a model to recover accuracy after quantization (Liu et al., 2023b). While effective for deployment, QAT still requires the initial high-precision pretraining phase.

This paper distinguishes itself by targeting **Fully Quantized Training (FQT)** from scratch. The goal is to accelerate the pretraining process itself, not just the inference phase.

#### 2.3.3. Integer vs. Floating Point
Some prior work explored 4-bit training using integer formats (INT4). For example, Xi et al. (2023) proposed an INT4 training framework. However, their focus was on **fine-tuning tasks** with limited applicability to full LLM pretraining (Section 5). Floating-point formats (like `$\text{FP4}$`) are generally preferred for training because the exponent bits allow for a wider dynamic range, which is critical for handling the varying scales of gradients and activations during optimization.

#### 2.3.4. Differentiable Quantization Methods
Existing differentiable quantization methods often rely on **learnable quantization parameters** updated through backpropagation (Gong et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2022). While flexible, this approach introduces overhead and complexity that is "not friendly to specialized hardware designs" (Section 5). This paper opts for a fixed quantization function with a corrected gradient estimator, which is more efficient for hardware acceleration.

### 2.4. Positioning of This Work

This paper positions itself as the **first `$\text{FP4}$` training framework tailored for LLMs** validated from scratch (Section 5). It bridges the gap between theoretical hardware capabilities (upcoming `$\text{FP4}$` tensor cores) and practical software stability.

The work differentiates itself through three key contextual contributions:
1.  **Hardware Readiness:** It anticipates the availability of next-generation hardware (NVIDIA B-series) by validating the software stack now, using `$\text{FP8}$` cores to emulate `$\text{FP4}$` (Section 1).
2.  **Activation Stability:** It addresses the specific issue of **activation outliers**, which are values substantially larger than the rest of the tensor. Prior inference methods handle outliers via offline preprocessing (e.g., SmoothQuant), but this is incompatible with dynamic pretraining. This work introduces a dynamic **Outlier Clamping and Compensation (OCC)** strategy (Section 3.2).
3.  **Gradient Accuracy:** It replaces the standard Straight-Through Estimator (STE) with a **Differentiable Gradient Estimator (DGE)** to mitigate vanishing gradients caused by the non-differentiable nature of 4-bit quantization (Section 3.1).

By addressing these specific gaps, the paper moves beyond proving that `$\text{FP4}$` is *possible* (which prior inference work showed) to proving that it is *viable for large-scale pretraining* without accuracy degradation.

## 3. Technical Approach

This section provides a complete technical breakdown of the FP4 training framework. We explain not only what components exist, but why each design choice was made, how the mathematical mechanisms operate, and how they interact to enable stable ultra-low precision training.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

This paper presents a software framework that enables training large language models using 4-bit floating-point arithmetic instead of the standard 16-bit or 32-bit formats. The solution addresses two fundamental problems: gradient computation becomes unstable at 4-bit precision due to non-differentiable quantization, and activation values contain extreme outliers that destroy quantization accuracy—both solved through a differentiable gradient estimator for weights and an outlier clamping strategy for activations.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The FP4 training framework operates as a modified training pipeline with five major components working in sequence. First, the **Quantization Module** converts high-precision tensors (BF16) into FP4 format using absmax scaling and a look-up table, producing both quantized values and scaling factors. Second, the **FP4 Tensor Core Computation** performs the actual matrix multiplication (GeMM) using the quantized weights and activations, leveraging hardware acceleration. Third, the **Differentiable Gradient Estimator (DGE)** intervenes during backpropagation to correct weight gradients using a differentiable approximation function rather than the standard Straight-Through Estimator. Fourth, the **Outlier Clamping and Compensation (OCC)** module processes activation tensors before quantization by identifying extreme values via quantile analysis, clamping them to a threshold, and storing residuals in a sparse high-precision matrix. Fifth, the **Mixed-Precision Orchestrator** manages which operations run at which precision—FP4 for GeMM, FP8 for gradient communication, FP16/BF16 for non-GeMM operations—ensuring numerical stability throughout the training loop.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

We will explain the technical components in the following order to build understanding progressively:

*   **FP4 Format Fundamentals**: We start with the E2M1 floating-point representation itself, since all subsequent techniques depend on understanding its 16-value constraint and 6.0 dynamic range limit.
*   **Quantization Pipeline**: We explain how tensors are converted from BF16 to FP4, including the absmax scaling method and look-up table implementation.
*   **Differentiable Gradient Estimator**: We detail the weight gradient correction mechanism, showing why the standard Straight-Through Estimator fails and how the proposed differentiable approximation works mathematically.
*   **Outlier Clamping and Compensation**: We explain the activation handling strategy, including quantile-based clamping, sparse compensation matrices, and the trade-offs involved.
*   **Mixed-Precision Training Scheme**: We describe how different operations use different precisions to balance efficiency and stability.
*   **Quantization Granularity**: We explain why vector-wise scaling (token-wise for activations, channel-wise for weights) is required instead of tensor-wise scaling.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### 3.4.1 FP4 Format Fundamentals (E2M1 Configuration)

The foundation of this work is the specific 4-bit floating-point format chosen for training. According to the IEEE 754 standard, a binary floating-point number consists of three components: a 1-bit sign (S), exponent bits (E), and mantissa bits (M), commonly represented as ExMy where x and y denote the number of bits for each component (Section 2). This paper adopts the **E2M1 format** for FP4 representation, meaning 2 bits for the exponent and 1 bit for the mantissa (Section 2). This configuration produces exactly `$2^4 = 16$` distinct representable values, which can be enumerated in a look-up table (Table 4 in Appendix A). The maximum absolute value in this format, denoted as `$\text{MAX}_{\text{FP4}}$`, is calculated to be `$6.0$` (Section 2). This limited dynamic range is the root cause of training instability: any tensor value exceeding `$6.0$` will overflow, while values near zero will underflow to zero due to the coarse quantization intervals. Unlike integer quantization, floating-point quantization features uneven quantization intervals and a larger dynamic range, which is why FP4 is preferred over INT4 for training despite having the same bit width (Section 2). The paper explicitly notes that the E2M1 format was selected because it "offers a balanced trade-off between dynamic range and quantization precision" compared to alternatives like E1M2 or E3M0 (Appendix A, Figure 7).

#### 3.4.2 Quantization Pipeline: From BF16 to FP4

The quantization process converts high-precision tensors into FP4 format before matrix multiplication operations. The paper employs the **absmax method** for quantization, which is commonly used in prior work (Dettmers et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2023). The quantization function is defined as:

$$x_{\text{FP4}} = Q(x_{\text{FP16}} \cdot \gamma), \quad \gamma = \frac{\text{MAX}_{\text{FP4}}}{\max(|x_{\text{FP16}}|)}$$

where `$\gamma$` serves as the scaling factor that normalizes the input tensor to fit within the FP4 dynamic range (Section 2). The quantization function `$Q(\cdot)$` is implemented using a look-up table in a custom CUDA kernel, since the FP4 format supports only 16 distinct values and cannot be computed through standard arithmetic operations (Section 2). Figure 2 illustrates the forward pass structure: a high-precision tensor (such as BF16) is quantized into FP4 format using look-up table quantization, then both weight and activation tensors are quantized into FP4 to leverage FP4 tensor cores during GeMM computation, and finally two scaling factors are applied to the final result to ensure computational correctness. The quantization is applied along distinct dimensions to align with matrix multiplication logic: activations are quantized **token-wise** (along the sequence length dimension), while weights are quantized **channel-wise** (along the output channels dimension) (Section 4.1). This dimension-specific quantization is critical because it allows each token or channel to have its own scaling factor, reducing quantization error compared to tensor-wise scaling where one scaling factor applies to the entire tensor.

#### 3.4.3 Differentiable Gradient Estimator (DGE) for Weight Updates

The Differentiable Gradient Estimator addresses a fundamental mathematical problem: quantization functions are inherently non-differentiable, which prevents gradients from flowing backward during training. To understand why this matters, consider a linear layer computation `$Y = A \cdot W_q$` where `$W_q = f(W)$` is the quantized weight tensor (Section 3.1). During backpropagation, the chain rule gives us the weight gradient:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial W} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial W_q} \cdot \frac{\partial W_q}{\partial W} = \left(A^T \frac{\partial L}{\partial Y}\right) \frac{\partial W_q}{\partial W}$$

The term `$\frac{\partial W_q}{\partial W}$` represents the derivative of the quantization function `$f$`, but since `$f$` is a step function (non-differentiable), its derivative is almost everywhere zero, leading to **vanishing gradients** that cause weight updates to fail (Section 3.1, Equation 6). The standard solution in prior work is the **Straight-Through Estimator (STE)**, which bypasses this issue by assuming `$f'(W) \equiv 1$`, effectively treating the quantized tensor's gradient as equivalent to the original tensor's gradient (Bengio et al., 2013). However, this simplification introduces inaccuracies in low-bit settings, as noted in prior studies (Yin et al., 2019; Gong et al., 2019), and becomes particularly problematic at 4-bit precision where quantization error is substantial.

This paper proposes the **Differentiable Gradient Estimator (DGE)** as an alternative that reduces estimation errors while maintaining hardware efficiency. The DGE maintains direct quantization for forward computation (preserving hardware efficiency) while introducing a gradient correction term derived from a differentiable approximation of the quantization function during backpropagation. Specifically, the paper uses the following function to simulate the quantization behavior:

$$f(x) = \frac{\delta}{2} \cdot \left(1 + \text{sign}\left(\frac{2x}{\delta} - 1\right) \cdot \left|\frac{2x}{\delta} - 1\right|^{\frac{1}{k}}\right)$$

where `$\delta$` represents the quantization interval and `$k$` is a parameter that controls the degree of approximation (Section 3.1, Equation 7). Figure 3(a) illustrates this function under `$k = 5$` for the range `[0, 0.5]`, which represents the first positive quantization interval in the E2M1 scheme. As `$k$` increases, the function curve becomes sharper and more closely resembles the behavior of the original hard quantization function. The derivative of this function, which is used for gradient correction, can be expressed as:

$$f'(x) = \frac{1}{k} \cdot \left|\frac{2x}{\delta} - 1\right|^{\frac{1}{k} - 1}$$

Figure 3(b) and Figure 3(c) show the complete quantization curve `$f(x)$` and its derivative `$f'(x)$` under `$k = 5$` within the full E2M1 quantization framework, which consists of 14 distinct quantization intervals (Section 3.1). In practice, the magnitude of `$f'(x)$` is capped at `$3.0$` to prevent infinite gradient spikes at the `$\delta/2$` point, impacting only a very small subset of elements (Section 3.1). During actual model training, the DGE is seamlessly integrated: the forward pass retains the hard quantization function for computational efficiency, while the backward pass applies a correction term derived from Equation 8 to the weight gradient calculation following Equation 6 (Section 3.1). For the hyperparameter `$k$`, experiments show that a moderate value of `$k = 5$` gives better final performance—larger `$k$` can better model the quantization function but leads to a more unstable correction term for the gradient (Section 4.3, Figure 6b).

#### 3.4.4 Outlier Clamping and Compensation (OCC) for Activations

Activation tensors present a significantly greater quantization challenge than weight tensors during LLM training. This difficulty arises from the complex distribution of activation tensor values, which are often dominated by **outliers**—specific values that are substantially larger than the rest of the tensor (Section 3.2). Outliers pose a critical problem for tensor quantization by disproportionately expanding the dynamic range of the target tensor, causing most values to underflow to zero after quantization. Figure 4 illustrates this impact based on a real activation tensor extracted from the first transformer layer's output of the LLaMA 1.3B model after 30,000 training iterations: without outlier clamping, quantization leads to severe loss of information, whereas after applying outlier clamping, the tensor structure is effectively preserved.

To address this issue, the paper proposes the **Outlier Clamping and Compensation (OCC)** method to restrict the range of activation tensors and mitigate the underflow problem. The clamping function is defined as:

$$Y_c = \text{clamp}(Y, \text{max} = \alpha, \text{min} = 1 - \alpha)$$

where `$\alpha$` is a pre-defined quantile threshold (Section 3.2, Equation 9). Specifically, the method identifies outliers—values with the largest absolute magnitudes—through quantile identification and clamps them to a predefined threshold. In the experiments, the quantile clamping threshold `$\alpha$` is set to `$0.99$`, meaning the top 1% of values by magnitude are clamped (Section 4.1). However, the paper observes that while clamping effectively reduces quantization error, it inherently introduces some error by disregarding the outlier values entirely. To further preserve accuracy, the method compensates for this error using a **sparse outlier matrix**. The residual matrix `$\Delta Y = Y - Y_c$` is highly sparse, with only about `$0.2\% \sim 2\%$` non-zero elements when `$\alpha$` is set around `$0.99 \sim 0.999$` (Section 3.2). During computation, the clamped matrix `$Y_c$` is processed using FP4 GeMM, while `$\Delta Y$` is handled with high-precision sparse matrix multiplication.

Table 1 provides quantitative analysis of cosine similarity (SIM), mean squared error (MSE), and signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) between original and quantized activation tensors across 30,000 training iterations of the LLaMA 1.3B model. The data shows that outlier clamping significantly improves both cosine similarity (from 92.19% to 98.83% at `$\alpha = 0.999$`) and SNR (from 8.31 to 14.25). Moreover, incorporating outlier compensation further reduces quantization loss (SIM improves to 99.61%, MSE drops to 0.0245, SNR increases to 15.31). Notably, lowering the quantile threshold increases the compensation scale, further reducing quantization loss—at `$\alpha = 0.97$`, SIM reaches 100% and SNR reaches 20.88. However, this introduces a trade-off between computational efficiency and numerical accuracy that must be carefully considered, as a smaller `$\alpha$` implies stronger compensation but at increased computational cost (Section 4.3). The paper selects `$\alpha = 0.99$` as the optimal choice for comprehensive computational performance considerations, corresponding to approximately 2% non-zero elements in the sparse compensation matrix (Section 4.3, Figure 6c).

#### 3.4.5 Mixed-Precision Training Scheme

The framework employs a mixed-precision training strategy to balance efficiency with numerical stability across different operations. During LLM training, General Matrix Multiplication (GeMM) accounts for over 95% of the computational workload, with this proportion increasing for larger models (Section 4.1). Consistent with prior works (Xi et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2020; Dettmers et al., 2022), the paper focuses on 4-bit quantization specifically for GeMM operations, which are the core feature of FP4 tensor cores. Non-GeMM operations, which account for a minor computational fraction, are performed at higher precision to preserve accuracy (Section 4.1). Following the framework in Peng et al. (2023), the method performs gradient communication in FP8 format to reduce bandwidth usage and adopts a mixed-precision Adam optimizer to conserve GPU memory (Section 4.1). Specifically, gradients and first-order moments are stored in FP8, while second-order moments are stored in FP16 (Section 4.1). Remaining operations, comprising a smaller computational portion, are executed in FP16 or BF16 for stability and precision. This hybrid approach ensures that the most computationally intensive operations benefit from FP4 acceleration while maintaining numerical stability in operations sensitive to precision loss.

#### 3.4.6 Quantization Granularity: Vector-wise vs. Tensor-wise Scaling

The granularity of FP4 quantization plays a critical role in training stability, and this represents a key design difference from FP8 training schemes. While FP8 training schemes (Peng et al., 2023; Nvidia, 2022) achieve sufficient accuracy with coarse-grained tensor-wise quantization (one scaling factor per entire tensor), Figure 6(d) shows that tensor-wise scaling in FP4 introduces significant errors (Section 4.3). To address this, the paper adopts **vector-wise scaling**, with token-wise quantization for activations and channel-wise quantization for weights, aligning with GeMM computation rules as discussed in Section 4.1. Notably, applying coarse-grained quantization to activations alone results in more severe accuracy degradation than applying it to weights alone, revealing that activations are harder to quantize than weights—consistent with the activation outlier issue described in Section 3.2 (Section 4.3). This finding is supported by the tensor distribution analysis in Appendix D, which shows that weight tensors generally exhibit a smaller dynamic range and follow a normal distribution, while activation tensors have a significantly larger dynamic range with irregular distributions and numerous outliers (Figures 8-13 in Appendix D). The heatmap visualization in Figure 14 further confirms that outliers in activation tensors during LLM training tend to appear in specific channels, validating the need for channel-wise or token-wise quantization granularity rather than tensor-wise (Appendix D).

#### 3.4.7 Implementation Details and Hyperparameters

The experimental implementation includes several specific hyperparameter choices that are critical for reproducibility. For the Differentiable Gradient Estimator, the paper uses `$k = 5$` based on ablation studies showing this provides optimal balance between gradient accuracy and stability (Section 4.1, Section 4.3). For the Outlier Clamping and Compensation, the quantile threshold is set to `$\alpha = 0.99$` (Section 4.1). Input sequences are fixed at 2048 tokens, and the batch size is 2048, comprising approximately 4M tokens per batch (Section 4.1). The learning rate follows a warm-up and cosine decay schedule, with the warm-up phase spanning 5% of total steps and the learning rate gradually decreasing to 10% of its peak over the remaining 90% (Section 4.1). The peak learning rate is `$3 \times 10^{-4}$`, with a weight decay of 0.1 (Section 4.1). For the Adam optimizer, the parameters are `$\beta_1 = 0.9$`, `$\beta_2 = 0.95$`, and `$\epsilon = 1 \times 10^{-8}$` (Section 4.1). Since FP4 Tensor Cores are unavailable at the time of publication, the paper validates FP4 performance using NVIDIA H-series GPUs' FP8 Tensor Cores, which encompass FP4's dynamic range and enable accurate simulation (Section 4.1). The theoretical speedup analysis in Appendix B estimates approximately 2.95× speedup after accounting for DGE and OCC overhead, compared to an ideal 3.12× speedup without overhead (Appendix B). The DGE overhead accounts for approximately 0.1% of total computation, while the OCC overhead contributes approximately 5.6% (Appendix B).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the conceptual breakthroughs that distinguish this work from prior quantization research. Rather than re-explaining the mechanics covered in Section 3, we focus on what makes each contribution novel, why prior approaches could not achieve the same results, and what these innovations enable for future research.

### 4.1. First Demonstrated FP4 Pretraining Framework (Fundamental Innovation)

The most significant contribution is establishing that **FP4 pretraining from scratch is feasible** for LLMs up to 13B parameters. This represents a fundamental capability shift, not merely an incremental improvement.

**What makes this different from prior work:**

*   **Pretraining vs. Inference:** Prior 4-bit research focused almost exclusively on **inference** (PTQ, QAT) or **fine-tuning** (Xi et al., 2023). These approaches still require an initial high-precision pretraining phase, which is the most computationally expensive stage. This paper demonstrates that the entire pretraining process can operate at FP4 precision without accuracy degradation.

*   **Floating-Point vs. Integer:** While Xi et al. (2023) explored INT4 training, their work targeted fine-tuning tasks with limited applicability to full LLM pretraining (Section 5). FP4's exponent bits provide the dynamic range necessary for stable gradient propagation during extended training runs, which integer formats struggle to maintain.

*   **Scale Validation:** The framework scales to **13B parameters** trained on **100B tokens** (Section 4.2). Prior low-bit training demonstrations typically validated on smaller models (e.g., 400M in Figure 1) or fewer tokens. This scale validation is critical because quantization errors compound over training steps—what works at 1B parameters may diverge at 13B.

**Why this matters:**

This innovation shifts the conversation from "Can we quantize trained models?" to "Can we train models in quantized form?" The latter question addresses the root cause of computational cost rather than treating symptoms post-training. If hardware vendors adopt FP4 tensor cores (as NVIDIA's B200 suggests), this framework provides the software foundation to immediately leverage that capability for pretraining, not just inference.

### 4.2. Differentiable Gradient Estimator Without Learnable Parameters (Algorithmic Innovation)

The DGE method (Section 3.1) introduces a novel approach to handling non-differentiable quantization that differs fundamentally from both STE and prior differentiable quantization methods.

**What makes this different from prior work:**

| Approach | Gradient Handling | Learnable Parameters | Hardware Friendliness |
|----------|------------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| **STE** (Bengio et al., 2013) | Assumes `$f'(x) \equiv 1$` | None | High |
| **Prior Differentiable Quantization** (Gong et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2022) | Learns quantization parameters via backprop | Yes | Low |
| **DGE (This Work)** | Fixed differentiable approximation with correction term | None | High |

The key distinction is that prior differentiable quantization methods rely on **learnable quantization parameters** updated through backpropagation. While flexible, this introduces overhead and complexity that is "not friendly to specialized hardware designs" (Section 5). DGE achieves gradient accuracy without this overhead by using a **fixed quantization function** with a corrected gradient estimator.

**Why this matters:**

This design choice reflects a deeper insight about hardware-software co-design. For ultra-low precision training to be practical, the software stack must align with hardware constraints. Learnable parameters require additional memory storage and update operations, which undermines the efficiency gains from quantization. DGE preserves the efficiency benefits while correcting the gradient estimation error that causes STE to fail at 4-bit precision. The ablation study in Figure 6(b) confirms this: direct quantization with STE shows convergence issues, while DGE achieves stable training with minimal overhead (0.1% of total computation per Appendix B).

### 4.3. Dynamic Outlier Handling Compatible with Pretraining (Methodological Innovation)

The OCC method (Section 3.2) addresses activation outliers in a way that is uniquely suited for **pretraining** rather than inference or fine-tuning.

**What makes this different from prior work:**

Prior outlier handling strategies for LLMs target **inference** and rely on **offline preprocessing**:

*   **SmoothQuant** (Xiao et al., 2023) uses calibration datasets to compute smoothing factors before inference.
*   **QuaRot** (Ashkboos et al., 2024) and **SpinQuant** (Liu et al., 2024) apply rotation matrices learned from calibration data.
*   **AWQ** (Lin et al., 2024a) identifies important channels using a calibration set.

These methods are **incompatible with pretraining** because:
1.  Pretraining has no fixed calibration dataset—the data distribution evolves throughout training.
2.  Offline preprocessing assumes a static model, but pretraining involves continuous weight updates.
3.  Calibration-based methods add significant overhead when applied at every training step.

OCC handles outliers **dynamically during real-time training** without requiring separate calibration datasets (Section 5). The quantile-based clamping threshold adapts to the current activation distribution at each step, and the sparse compensation matrix captures residual information without full-precision storage.

**Why this matters:**

This innovation reveals that activation outlier handling cannot be treated as a one-time preprocessing step for training workloads. The paper's analysis in Appendix D (Figures 11-14) shows that activation distributions vary significantly across layers and training iterations, with outliers appearing in specific channels that change over time. A static calibration approach would quickly become outdated. OCC's dynamic approach ensures that outlier handling remains effective throughout the entire pretraining process, which is essential for maintaining stability over 100B tokens.

### 4.4. Precision-Dependent Quantization Granularity Requirements (Empirical Discovery)

The ablation study on quantization granularity (Figure 6d) reveals a critical insight: **FP4 requires finer-grained scaling than FP8**, and this requirement is not linear with bit reduction.

**What makes this different from prior work:**

FP8 training frameworks (Peng et al., 2023; Nvidia, 2022) achieve sufficient accuracy with **tensor-wise quantization** (one scaling factor per entire tensor). This paper demonstrates that the same approach fails catastrophically at FP4:

> "While FP8 training schemes achieve sufficient accuracy with coarse-grained tensor-wise quantization, Figure 6(d) shows that tensor-wise scaling in FP4 introduces significant errors." (Section 4.3)

The ablation shows that applying coarse-grained quantization to activations alone results in more severe accuracy degradation than applying it to weights alone, confirming that activations are harder to quantize than weights (Section 4.3).

**Why this matters:**

This finding establishes that there are **precision thresholds** where quantization strategies must fundamentally change, not just scale proportionally. The transition from FP8 to FP4 is not simply "half the bits, half the accuracy"—it requires different algorithmic approaches (vector-wise vs. tensor-wise scaling). This insight has implications for future hardware design: FP4 tensor cores may need to support finer-grained scaling factor storage and application compared to FP8 cores. It also suggests that FP6 (an intermediate format mentioned in Section 1) might serve as a transition point where tensor-wise scaling remains viable, though this remains unexplored in the paper.

| Differentiable Gradient Estimator | **Algorithmic** | Solves gradient vanishing without learnable parameter overhead |
| Dynamic Outlier Handling | **Methodological** | Adapts outlier compensation for dynamic pretraining vs. static inference |
| Granularity Requirements | **Empirical Discovery** | Reveals non-linear relationship between bit width and quantization strategy |

Together, these innovations demonstrate that ultra-low precision training requires co-designed solutions across algorithms, data handling, and granularity choices—not simply applying existing quantization techniques at lower bit widths.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validated their FP4 training framework. We will walk through the evaluation methodology, analyze the quantitative results with specific numbers, assess whether the experiments convincingly support the paper's claims, and examine the ablation studies that reveal what components are essential versus optional. Understanding the experimental design is critical because ultra-low precision training claims are easy to make but difficult to substantiate—the devil is in the details of token counts, model scales, and evaluation tasks.

### 5.1. Evaluation Methodology and Experimental Setup

The authors designed a comprehensive evaluation strategy to test three core questions: Does FP4 training converge? Does it match BF16 accuracy? Does it scale to realistic model sizes? The experimental design addresses each question systematically.

#### 5.1.1. Model Architectures and Scales

The paper evaluates three model sizes based on the LLaMA 2 architecture (Touvron et al., 2023):
*   **1.3B parameters:** Used for ablation studies and detailed analysis
*   **7B parameters:** Mid-scale validation
*   **13B parameters:** Largest model tested, representing practical pretraining scale

Figure 1 also shows results from a **400M parameter model** for initial feasibility demonstration, though this is not the focus of the main claims. The choice of LLaMA 2 is strategic—it is a widely recognized architecture with reproducible baselines, making comparisons meaningful.

#### 5.1.2. Training Dataset and Token Budget

All models are trained from scratch on the **DCLM dataset** (Li et al., 2024), described as "a comprehensive dataset well-suited for language model pretraining" (Section 4.1). The main experiments train on **100B tokens**, which is substantial but not at the trillion-token scale of production models like Llama 3. This token budget is sufficient to observe convergence behavior and compare precision effects, though the authors acknowledge this limitation (Section 6).

For ablation studies, a subset of DCLM is used with **10B tokens** to accelerate convergence analysis on the 1.3B model (Section 4.3).

#### 5.1.3. Hyperparameters and Training Configuration

The authors maintain identical hyperparameters across precision settings to ensure fair comparison. Key configuration details from Section 4.1 include:

*   **Sequence length:** 2048 tokens (fixed)
*   **Batch size:** 2048 sequences, comprising approximately **4M tokens per batch**
*   **Learning rate schedule:** Warm-up for 5% of total steps, then cosine decay to 10% of peak
*   **Peak learning rate:** `$3 \times 10^{-4}$`
*   **Weight decay:** 0.1
*   **Adam optimizer:** `$\beta_1 = 0.9$`, `$\beta_2 = 0.95$`, `$\epsilon = 1 \times 10^{-8}$`
*   **FP4-specific hyperparameters:** `$k = 5$` for DGE, `$\alpha = 0.99$` for OCC quantile threshold

This hyperparameter consistency is crucial—without it, accuracy differences could be attributed to tuning rather than precision effects.

#### 5.1.4. Hardware and Simulation Strategy

A critical constraint shapes the experimental design: **native FP4 tensor cores do not exist in available hardware** at the time of publication. The authors state: "Since FP4 Tensor Cores are unavailable, we validate FP4 performance using Nvidia H-series GPUs' FP8 Tensor Cores, which encompass FP4's dynamic range and enable accurate simulation" (Section 4.1).

This simulation approach has implications:
*   **Accuracy:** The FP8 cores can accurately represent FP4 values, so numerical results are valid.
*   **Speed:** The reported speedup is **theoretical**, not measured. Actual runtime includes simulation overhead from extra precision casting (Section 6).
*   **Overhead:** Appendix B estimates the simulation introduces computational overhead, reducing ideal 3.12× speedup to 2.95× after accounting for DGE (0.1% overhead) and OCC (5.6% overhead).

#### 5.1.5. Evaluation Metrics and Baselines

The paper uses three categories of metrics to assess performance:

**Training Loss:** Measured throughout pretraining to assess convergence behavior. Figure 5 presents training curves for all three model sizes.

**Zero-Shot Accuracy:** Models are evaluated on nine downstream tasks using the lm-evaluation-harness library (Gao et al., 2024):
*   PiQA (physical commonsense reasoning)
*   HellaSwag (sentence completion)
*   OpenBookQA (science question answering)
*   Arc-C and Arc-E (reasoning challenges, challenge and easy variants)
*   BoolQ (yes/no questions)
*   LogiQA (logical reasoning)
*   SciQ (science questions)
*   Lambada (word prediction requiring discourse context)

Table 2 reports average accuracy and per-task breakdowns.

**Perplexity:** Measured on four datasets to assess language modeling quality:
*   Lambada OpenAI (Lbd.OAI)
*   Lambada standard (Lbd.std)
*   Pile 10k
*   Wikitext

Table 3 reports perplexity scores, where lower values indicate better performance.

**Baselines for comparison:**
*   **BF16 mixed-precision training:** The primary baseline, representing standard practice.
*   **MS-AMP FP8** (Peng et al., 2023): State-of-the-art FP8 training framework.
*   **Transformer-Engine FP8** (Nvidia, 2022): NVIDIA's official FP8 training implementation.
*   **Direct-cast FP4 (W4A4):** Naive quantization without DGE or OCC, demonstrating the necessity of the proposed methods.

### 5.2. Main Quantitative Results

The experimental results address the central claim: Can FP4 training achieve accuracy comparable to BF16? The answer, supported by multiple metrics, is yes—with minor caveats.

#### 5.2.1. Training Loss Convergence

Figure 5 presents training loss curves for all three model sizes over 100B tokens. The curves for BF16 and FP4 "largely overlap across different model sizes, with the FP4 curve exhibiting a slightly higher training loss compared to the BF16 curve" (Section 4.2).

The specific final training losses after 100B tokens are:

| Model Size | FP4 Loss | BF16 Loss | Gap |
|------------|----------|-----------|-----|
| 1.3B | 2.55 | 2.49 | +0.06 |
| 7B | 2.17 | 2.07 | +0.10 |
| 13B | 1.97 | 1.88 | +0.09 |

Several observations emerge from these numbers:
*   The absolute loss gap is small (0.06 to 0.10), representing approximately **2-5% relative difference**.
*   The gap does not grow disproportionately with model size—the 13B model's gap (0.09) is similar to the 7B model's gap (0.10), suggesting the method **scales stably**.
*   All models show consistent downward trends, indicating **no divergence or instability** during training.

Figure 1 provides additional context by showing that "Directly casting to FP4 results in significantly higher training loss" compared to the proposed method. The direct-cast curve diverges substantially from the BF16 baseline, while "our proposed FP4 method achieves accuracy comparable to the BF16 baseline." This visual comparison establishes that the DGE and OCC methods are not optional refinements—they are essential for FP4 training to function at all.

#### 5.2.2. Zero-Shot Downstream Task Performance

Table 2 presents zero-shot evaluation results across nine downstream tasks. The average accuracy scores are:

| Model Size | Precision | Average Accuracy |
|------------|-----------|------------------|
| 1.3B | BF16 | 53.23% |
| 1.3B | FP4 (Ours) | 53.13% |
| 7B | BF16 | 53.87% |
| 7B | FP4 (Ours) | 54.42% |
| 13B | BF16 | 54.44% |
| 13B | FP4 (Ours) | 54.95% |

These results contain a surprising finding: **FP4-trained models match or slightly exceed BF16 performance** on downstream tasks, despite having slightly higher training loss. The 7B FP4 model achieves 54.42% vs. 53.87% for BF16 (+0.55%), and the 13B FP4 model achieves 54.95% vs. 54.44% for BF16 (+0.51%).

This apparent contradiction (higher training loss but better downstream accuracy) warrants careful interpretation:
*   **Training loss** measures fit to the training distribution.
*   **Zero-shot accuracy** measures generalization to unseen tasks.
*   The slight training loss gap may reflect different optimization trajectories rather than reduced model quality.
*   Small variations (0.5%) may fall within experimental variance—the paper does not report multiple seeds or confidence intervals.

Looking at individual tasks in Table 2 reveals more nuance. For the 13B model:
*   **PiQA:** FP4 = 73.78% vs. BF16 = 72.80% (FP4 better)
*   **HellaSwag:** FP4 = 54.12% vs. BF16 = 53.56% (FP4 better)
*   **BoolQ:** FP4 = 67.89% vs. BF16 = 67.97% (essentially equal)
*   **LogiQA:** FP4 = 55.90% vs. BF16 = 57.40% (BF16 better by 1.5%)

The performance is **task-dependent**, with FP4 showing advantages on some reasoning tasks but slight disadvantages on others. This pattern suggests that FP4 training does not uniformly degrade or improve performance—it produces a slightly different model that generalizes comparably overall.

Table 3 presents perplexity (PPL) results on four evaluation datasets. Lower perplexity indicates better language modeling. The average perplexity scores are:

| 1.3B | FP4 (Ours) | 36.86 |

| 13B | BF16 | 33.69 |

The perplexity results show a different pattern than zero-shot accuracy:
*   **1.3B model:** FP4 achieves lower (better) perplexity: 36.86 vs. 37.38
*   **7B model:** BF16 achieves lower perplexity: 35.06 vs. 35.62
*   **13B model:** BF16 achieves lower perplexity: 33.69 vs. 33.99

The differences are small (0.3 to 0.6 PPL points), representing approximately **1-2% relative difference**. This pattern aligns with the training loss results—BF16 has a slight advantage in language modeling quality at larger scales, but the gap is minimal.

#### 5.2.4. Assessment of Experimental Claims

Do these experiments convincingly support the paper's central claim that FP4 training is feasible with minimal accuracy degradation?

**Strengths of the evidence:**
*   **Multiple model scales:** Results span 1.3B to 13B parameters, demonstrating scalability.
*   **Substantial token budget:** 100B tokens is sufficient to observe convergence behavior.
*   **Multiple evaluation metrics:** Training loss, zero-shot accuracy, and perplexity provide complementary views of model quality.
*   **Fair comparison:** Identical hyperparameters across precision settings isolate the effect of quantization.
*   **Baseline inclusion:** Direct-cast FP4 demonstrates that naive approaches fail, validating the necessity of DGE and OCC.

**Limitations and caveats:**
*   **No native hardware:** Speedup claims are theoretical, not measured. The actual runtime includes simulation overhead.
*   **Single seed:** The paper does not report multiple training runs or confidence intervals, making it difficult to assess statistical significance of small differences (e.g., 0.5% accuracy gaps).
*   **Scale limitation:** 13B parameters and 100B tokens are substantial but below production-scale models (e.g., Llama 3 405B on trillions of tokens). The authors acknowledge this in Section 6.
*   **Task variance:** Some downstream tasks show FP4 advantages while others show BF16 advantages, suggesting the results are not uniformly superior.

Overall, the experiments **convincingly demonstrate feasibility** but leave open questions about performance at extreme scales. The consistency across model sizes and metrics supports the claim that FP4 training is viable, though the theoretical speedup awaits hardware validation.

### 5.3. Ablation Studies and Component Analysis

Section 4.3 presents ablation studies that isolate the contribution of each component. These experiments are critical because they reveal which innovations are essential versus optional, and they expose failure modes that the full method avoids.

All ablation experiments use the **LLaMA 1.3B model trained on 10B tokens** with a reduced batch size of 256 (vs. 2048 in main experiments) to accelerate convergence analysis (Section 4.3).

#### 5.3.1. Precision Framework Comparison (Figure 6a)

Figure 6(a) compares training curves across different precision frameworks:
*   **BF16:** Baseline performance.
*   **MS-AMP FP8:** FP8 training from Peng et al. (2023).
*   **Transformer-Engine FP8:** NVIDIA's FP8 implementation.
*   **W4A4 (Direct Cast):** Naive FP4 quantization without improvements.
*   **W4A4+DGE+OCC (Ours):** Full proposed method.

The results show:
*   **FP8 methods and proposed FP4:** All "maintain pretraining accuracy" with loss curves close to BF16.
*   **Direct-cast FP4:** Shows "a significant training loss gap," confirming that naive quantization fails.

This ablation establishes that **both DGE and OCC are necessary**—removing either would likely result in behavior closer to the direct-cast failure mode, as shown in subsequent ablations.

#### 5.3.2. Weight Quantization and DGE (Figure 6b)

Figure 6(b) isolates the effect of the Differentiable Gradient Estimator on weight-only 4-bit quantization (W4A8, meaning 4-bit weights and 8-bit activations):

*   **W4A8 direct quantization:** "Doesn't introduce a substantial training loss gap," suggesting weights are easier to quantize than activations.
*   **W4A8+DGE(k=3, 5, 10):** All DGE variants improve convergence over direct quantization.
*   **Optimal k value:** `$k = 5$` gives better final performance than `$k = 3$` or `$k = 10$`.

The paper explains this trade-off: "A larger `$k$` can better model the quantization function, but it can also lead to a more unstable correction term for the gradient" (Section 4.3). This reveals a subtle design insight—the gradient correction must balance accuracy (higher `$k$`) against stability (lower `$k$`).

**Key takeaway:** Weights are more forgiving than activations, but DGE still provides measurable improvement. The optimal `$k = 5$` was selected for all main experiments.

#### 5.3.3. Activation Quantization and OCC (Figure 6c)

Figure 6(c) isolates the effect of Outlier Clamping and Compensation on activation-only 4-bit quantization (W8A4, meaning 8-bit weights and 4-bit activations). This ablation reveals the most dramatic findings:

*   **W8A4 direct quantization:** Results in **curve divergence**, where "the loss values turn into NaN (Not a Number) after certain training steps."
*   **W8A4+OCC:** All variants with outlier clamping "effectively reduces this loss gap, ensuring a good convergence."

This is a critical finding: **activations cannot be quantized to FP4 without outlier handling**. The divergence to NaN indicates numerical instability severe enough to halt training entirely. This validates the design choice to prioritize activation handling in the methodology.

The ablation also tests different quantile thresholds `$\alpha$`:
*   **`$\alpha = 0.999$`:** 0.2% non-zero elements in compensation matrix.
*   **`$\alpha = 0.99$`:** 2% non-zero elements (chosen for main experiments).
*   **`$\alpha = 0.97$`:** 6% non-zero elements.

Figure 6(c) shows that "a smaller `$\alpha$` leads to better model accuracy," consistent with Table 1's quantitative analysis. However, the authors select `$\alpha = 0.99$` as "a better choice for comprehensive computational performance considerations" (Section 4.3). This represents an explicit **accuracy-efficiency trade-off**: stronger compensation (lower `$\alpha$`) improves accuracy but increases the computational cost of sparse matrix multiplication.

**Key takeaway:** OCC is not optional—it prevents training collapse. The `$\alpha = 0.99$` threshold balances accuracy gains against computational overhead.

#### 5.3.4. Quantization Granularity (Figure 6d)

Figure 6(d) examines the impact of quantization granularity, comparing tensor-wise vs. vector-wise scaling:

*   **Tensor-wise scaling (W:fine A:fine with coarse granularity):** "Introduces significant errors" in FP4.
*   **Vector-wise scaling (token-wise for activations, channel-wise for weights):** Required for FP4 stability.
*   **Coarse on activations vs. weights:** "Applying coarse-grained quantization to activations alone result in more severe accuracy degradation than applying it to weights alone."

This finding reveals that **FP4 has different requirements than FP8**. The paper notes: "While FP8 training schemes achieve sufficient accuracy with coarse-grained tensor-wise quantization, Figure 6(d) shows that tensor-wise scaling in FP4 introduces significant errors" (Section 4.3).

The asymmetry between activations and weights is consistent with the activation outlier issue from Section 3.2. Activations have larger dynamic range and more outliers (Appendix D, Figures 11-14), making them more sensitive to coarse scaling.

**Key takeaway:** Granularity is not a minor implementation detail—it is a fundamental requirement for FP4 stability. Future FP4 hardware must support fine-grained scaling factor storage.

#### 5.3.5. Summary of Ablation Findings

| Component | Without Component | With Component | Necessity |
|-----------|------------------|----------------|-----------|
| **DGE (weights)** | Convergence issues, suboptimal | Stable convergence, better accuracy | Important |
| **OCC (activations)** | Training diverges to NaN | Stable convergence | Critical |
| **Vector-wise scaling** | Significant errors | Stable training | Critical |
| **Optimal k=5** | Less stable (k=10) or less accurate (k=3) | Balanced performance | Important |
| **Optimal α=0.99** | Higher overhead (α=0.97) or lower accuracy (α=0.999) | Balanced trade-off | Important |

The ablation studies demonstrate that the proposed framework is not a collection of optional refinements—**OCC and vector-wise scaling are essential for training to function at all**, while DGE and hyperparameter tuning optimize performance within the feasible regime.

### 5.4. Failure Cases and Limitations in Results

While the main results are positive, the paper documents several failure modes and limitations that provide important context for interpreting the claims.

#### 5.4.1. Direct Quantization Failure (Figure 1 and Figure 6c)

The most dramatic failure case is naive FP4 quantization without the proposed methods:
*   **Figure 1:** Direct casting shows "significantly higher training loss" compared to both BF16 and the proposed FP4 method.
*   **Figure 6(c):** Activation-only FP4 quantization without OCC "results in curve divergence, where the loss values turn into NaN."

These failures establish a **baseline of infeasibility** that the proposed methods overcome. They also reveal that activations are the primary source of instability—weight-only quantization (Figure 6b) does not diverge, though it underperforms without DGE.

#### 5.4.2. Hardware Simulation Overhead (Section 6 and Appendix B)

A significant limitation is the absence of native FP4 hardware:
> "One primary limitation of this work lies in the absence of dedicated FP4 Tensor Cores in existing hardware. Consequently, we are unable to directly measure the potential speedup and energy efficiency gains achievable with native FP4 support." (Section 6)

The simulation on FP8 cores "introduce[s] additional computational overhead due to extra precision casting and significantly prolong[s] runtime" (Section 6). Appendix B estimates the theoretical speedup:
*   **Ideal FP4 speedup:** 3.12× (based on FLOP analysis)
*   **Adjusted for overhead:** 2.95× (accounting for DGE at 0.1% and OCC at 5.6%)

While 2.95× is still substantial, the actual measured speedup on current hardware is lower due to simulation overhead. The true benefits await next-generation hardware like NVIDIA's B200 GPUs.

#### 5.4.3. Scale and Token Budget Limitations (Section 6)

The authors acknowledge:
> "Due to constraints on computational resources, we have not yet extended our experiments to extremely large-scale models or to datasets comprising trillions of tokens." (Section 6)

The 13B parameter, 100B token experiments are substantial but below production scale. Quantization errors can compound over longer training runs, and larger models may exhibit different sensitivity to precision loss. The paper's claims are validated for the tested scale but remain **conditional on future validation** at larger scales.

#### 5.4.4. Task-Dependent Performance Variance (Table 2)

Table 2 reveals that FP4 performance is not uniformly better or worse than BF16—it varies by task:
*   **FP4 advantages:** PiQA (+0.98% at 13B), HellaSwag (+0.56% at 13B)
*   **BF16 advantages:** LogiQA (+1.5% at 13B), BoolQ (essentially equal)

This variance suggests that FP4 training produces models with **slightly different generalization characteristics** rather than strictly degraded versions of BF16 models. The average accuracy masks this task-level variation, which may matter for specific applications.

### 5.5. Critical Assessment of Experimental Rigor

The experimental design demonstrates strong rigor in several dimensions but has notable gaps that affect confidence in the claims.

**Strengths:**
*   **Controlled comparisons:** Identical hyperparameters across precision settings isolate the quantization effect.
*   **Multiple scales:** Testing 1.3B, 7B, and 13B models demonstrates scalability.
*   **Comprehensive ablations:** Each component (DGE, OCC, granularity) is isolated and validated.
*   **Multiple metrics:** Training loss, zero-shot accuracy, and perplexity provide complementary evaluation.
*   **Failure mode documentation:** The paper explicitly shows what happens without the proposed methods (divergence, NaN).

**Weaknesses:**
*   **No statistical significance testing:** Single-seed results make it difficult to assess whether small differences (e.g., 0.5% accuracy) are meaningful or noise.
*   **Theoretical speedup only:** No measured runtime comparisons on actual hardware.
*   **Limited scale:** 13B parameters and 100B tokens are below production-scale models.
*   **No long-term stability analysis:** The paper does not report whether FP4 training remains stable beyond 100B tokens.

**Overall assessment:** The experiments **convincingly demonstrate feasibility** at the tested scale but leave open questions about performance at production scale and on native hardware. The ablation studies are particularly strong—they clearly establish which components are essential (OCC, vector-wise scaling) versus optimizing (DGE hyperparameters). The documentation of failure modes (NaN divergence without OCC) adds credibility by showing what the methods prevent rather than only what they achieve.

For practitioners considering FP4 training, the experimental evidence supports adoption **with the caveat** that results are validated up to 13B parameters and 100B tokens, with speedup benefits contingent on future hardware availability. The framework provides a solid foundation, but extreme-scale validation remains future work.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

No technical solution is without constraints. This section examines the assumptions underlying the FP4 training framework, identifies scenarios where the approach may not apply, analyzes the computational and scalability trade-offs inherent in the design, and highlights open questions that remain for future research. Understanding these limitations is critical for practitioners evaluating whether FP4 training is appropriate for their use cases and for researchers identifying directions for future work.

### 6.1. Hardware Dependency and Simulation Constraints

The most significant limitation is that the framework's efficiency benefits **cannot be realized on current hardware**. The authors explicitly state:

> "One primary limitation of this work lies in the absence of dedicated FP4 Tensor Cores in existing hardware. Consequently, we are unable to directly measure the potential speedup and energy efficiency gains achievable with native FP4 support." (Section 6)

This constraint shapes every aspect of the experimental validation:

*   **Simulation Overhead:** All experiments run on NVIDIA H100 GPUs using FP8 tensor cores to emulate FP4 operations. This simulation "introduce[s] additional computational overhead due to extra precision casting and significantly prolong[s] runtime" (Section 6). The theoretical speedup analysis in Appendix B estimates approximately **2.95× speedup** after accounting for overhead, compared to an ideal **3.12× speedup** without overhead. However, actual measured runtime on current hardware is slower than BF16 due to simulation costs.

*   **Hardware Assumption:** The framework assumes next-generation hardware (specifically NVIDIA's B200/Blackwell series) will provide native FP4 tensor core support. If hardware vendors delay or deprioritize FP4 support, the practical value of this software framework diminishes significantly.

*   **Validation Gap:** Without native hardware, certain performance characteristics cannot be validated:
    *   Memory bandwidth savings from reduced weight storage
    *   Energy efficiency gains from lower-precision arithmetic
    *   Real-world throughput improvements in production training pipelines

This creates a **chicken-and-egg problem**: software frameworks need hardware validation to gain adoption, but hardware vendors need software validation to justify silicon investment. The paper addresses this by providing theoretical analysis, but practitioners must accept uncertainty about real-world performance until FP4-capable GPUs become available.

### 6.2. Scale and Token Budget Limitations

The experimental validation, while substantial, does not reach production-scale training regimes. The authors acknowledge:

> "Due to constraints on computational resources, we have not yet extended our experiments to extremely large-scale models or to datasets comprising trillions of tokens." (Section 6)

This limitation has several implications:

*   **Model Size Gap:** The largest model tested is **13B parameters** (Section 4.2). Production models like Llama 3 405B are **30× larger**. Quantization errors can compound differently at extreme scales—phenomena that only emerge at 100B+ parameters cannot be detected in 13B experiments.

*   **Token Budget Gap:** Experiments train on **100B tokens** (Section 4.2). State-of-the-art pretraining runs often use **trillions of tokens**. The paper does not address whether FP4 training remains stable over extended training schedules where small numerical errors might accumulate.

*   **Scaling Law Uncertainty:** The paper validates that FP4 training works at tested scales, but does not establish whether the **scaling laws** (relationship between model size, tokens, and performance) remain identical to BF16 training. If FP4 introduces subtle efficiency losses that compound with scale, the gap between FP4 and BF16 could widen at 405B+ parameters.

This limitation is understandable given computational costs—training a 405B model even once is prohibitively expensive for most research groups. However, it means the claim that FP4 training is "comparable to BF16" is **conditional on the tested scale range** and should not be assumed to hold universally.

### 6.3. Accuracy-Efficiency Trade-offs in Method Design

The framework incorporates several tunable parameters that represent explicit trade-offs between accuracy and computational efficiency. These are not bugs—they are design choices that practitioners must understand when deploying the framework.

#### 6.3.1. Outlier Clamping Threshold (α)

The OCC method uses a quantile threshold `$\alpha$` to determine which activation values are clamped and compensated. Table 1 and Figure 6(c) show the trade-off:

| `$\alpha$` Value | Non-Zero Compensation Elements | Cosine Similarity | SNR | Computational Cost |
|-----------------|-------------------------------|-------------------|-----|-------------------|
| 0.999 | 0.2% | 99.61% | 15.31 | Lowest |
| 0.99 | 2% | 100% | 18.38 | Moderate (chosen) |
| 0.97 | 6% | 100% | 20.88 | Highest |

The paper selects `$\alpha = 0.99$` as "a better choice for comprehensive computational performance considerations" (Section 4.3). This reveals an important insight: **stronger compensation improves accuracy but increases sparse matrix multiplication overhead**. The OCC overhead already accounts for approximately **5.6% of total computation** (Appendix B)—lowering `$\alpha$` further would increase this percentage.

Practitioners deploying this framework must decide: Is the marginal accuracy gain from `$\alpha = 0.97$` worth the additional computational cost? The paper does not provide guidance for different use cases (e.g., safety-critical applications vs. cost-sensitive deployments).

#### 6.3.2. Differentiable Gradient Estimator Parameter (k)

The DGE method uses parameter `$k$` to control the steepness of the differentiable approximation. Figure 6(b) shows the trade-off:

*   **`$k = 3$`:** Smoother approximation, more stable gradients, but less accurate quantization modeling
*   **`$k = 5$`:** Balanced performance (chosen for main experiments)
*   **`$k = 10$`:** Sharper approximation, better quantization modeling, but "more unstable correction term for the gradient" (Section 4.3)

The paper explains: "A larger `$k$` can better model the quantization function, but it can also lead to a more unstable correction term for the gradient" (Section 4.3). This represents a **gradient accuracy vs. training stability** trade-off. The DGE overhead is minimal (0.1% of total computation per Appendix B), so the primary concern is training dynamics rather than efficiency.

#### 6.3.3. Quantization Granularity Requirements

FP4 requires **vector-wise scaling** (token-wise for activations, channel-wise for weights) rather than the **tensor-wise scaling** used in FP8 training. This has implications:

*   **Storage Overhead:** Vector-wise scaling requires storing more scaling factors. For a typical linear layer with `$d_{model} = 4096$`, token-wise scaling requires `$\text{sequence\_length} \times 1$` scaling factors per layer vs. `$1$` for tensor-wise.
*   **Hardware Complexity:** FP4 tensor cores must support fine-grained scaling factor application. The paper notes that "FP8 training schemes achieve sufficient accuracy with coarse-grained tensor-wise quantization, Figure 6(d) shows that tensor-wise scaling in FP4 introduces significant errors" (Section 4.3).
*   **Implementation Complexity:** Vector-wise quantization adds software complexity compared to tensor-wise approaches.

This requirement is non-negotiable for FP4 stability but represents a **complexity cost** that FP8 training does not incur.

### 6.4. Scope Limitations: What This Framework Does Not Address

The paper focuses specifically on **pretraining from scratch** for **decoder-only language models**. Several important scenarios fall outside this scope:

#### 6.4.1. Fine-Tuning and Adaptation

The framework is validated for pretraining but not for **fine-tuning** tasks. Fine-tuning has different characteristics:
*   Smaller token budgets (often millions vs. billions of tokens)
*   Different data distributions (task-specific vs. general web text)
*   Different stability requirements (catastrophic forgetting concerns)

While the authors note that prior INT4 work focused on fine-tuning (Xi et al., 2023), they do not validate whether their FP4 framework transfers to fine-tuning scenarios. Practitioners cannot assume the same hyperparameters or stability guarantees apply.

#### 6.4.2. Model Architecture Variations

All experiments use **LLaMA 2 architecture** (Section 4.1). The framework has not been validated on:
*   **Encoder-decoder models** (e.g., T5, BART)
*   **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)** architectures (e.g., Mixtral, Grok)
*   **Multi-modal models** (e.g., LLaVA, GPT-4V)
*   **Alternative attention mechanisms** (e.g., MQA, GQA variants)

Different architectures have different activation distributions and gradient dynamics. The OCC method, which relies on quantile-based outlier detection, may behave differently in architectures with different layer structures.

#### 6.4.3. Inference Deployment

The paper explicitly distinguishes itself from inference-focused quantization work (Section 5). While FP4 training produces models that could theoretically be deployed at FP4 for inference, the paper does not:
*   Validate inference latency or throughput
*   Test inference accuracy on edge devices
*   Address inference-specific optimizations (e.g., KV cache quantization)

Practitioners interested in end-to-end FP4 pipelines (training + inference) must validate the inference stage separately.

#### 6.4.4. Alternative FP4 Formats

The paper adopts the **E2M1 format** (2 exponent bits, 1 mantissa bit) based on prior studies (Rouhani et al., 2023b;a). Appendix A (Table 4, Figure 7) shows alternative FP4 formats exist:
*   **E1M2:** More precision, less dynamic range
*   **E3M0:** More dynamic range, less precision

The paper states E2M1 "offers a balanced trade-off" but does not extensively compare alternatives. Different workloads might benefit from different format choices—scientific computing might prefer E3M0 for dynamic range, while NLP might prefer E1M2 for precision. This design space remains unexplored.

### 6.5. Statistical and Reproducibility Concerns

The experimental methodology has several limitations that affect confidence in the results:

*   **Single-Seed Results:** The paper does not report multiple training runs or confidence intervals. Small differences in accuracy (e.g., 0.5% in Table 2) could reflect random variation rather than systematic effects. Without statistical significance testing, it is difficult to determine whether FP4 truly matches BF16 or simply falls within experimental noise.

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper uses identical hyperparameters across precision settings for fair comparison. However, it does not explore whether **FP4-specific hyperparameter tuning** could close the remaining accuracy gap. The optimal learning rate, batch size, or optimizer parameters for FP4 training might differ from BF16.

*   **Dataset Specificity:** All experiments use the **DCLM dataset** (Li et al., 2024). Different datasets have different token distributions and vocabulary characteristics. The framework's performance on domain-specific datasets (e.g., code, scientific papers, medical text) remains unvalidated.

### 6.6. Open Questions for Future Research

Several important questions remain unanswered by this work:

| Question | Why It Matters | Current Evidence |
|----------|---------------|------------------|
| **Does FP4 training remain stable beyond 100B tokens?** | Production models train on trillions of tokens; errors could compound | Only 100B tokens tested (Section 6) |
| **How does FP4 scale to 100B+ parameter models?** | Largest tested model is 13B; production models are 30× larger | Only up to 13B parameters validated (Section 4.2) |
| **What is the true speedup on native FP4 hardware?** | Current speedup is theoretical; simulation overhead masks real benefits | Simulation only; no native hardware available (Section 6) |
| **Can FP4 training work for MoE architectures?** | MoE models are increasingly common; sparse activation patterns may interact differently with OCC | Only dense LLaMA 2 architecture tested (Section 4.1) |
| **Does FP4 affect model calibration or uncertainty estimation?** | Safety-critical applications require reliable confidence scores | Only accuracy and perplexity measured; no calibration analysis |
| **What is the energy efficiency gain?** | Environmental impact is a key motivation for quantization | No energy measurements; only theoretical FLOP analysis (Appendix B) |

### 6.7. Summary of Limitations

| Limitation Category | Severity | Mitigation |
|--------------------|----------|------------|
| **No native FP4 hardware** | High | Wait for B200 GPUs; use simulation for now |
| **Scale limited to 13B/100B tokens** | Medium | Accept conditional validity; await larger-scale validation |
| **Accuracy-efficiency trade-offs (α, k)** | Low | Tune based on use case requirements |

| **Architecture scope (LLaMA 2 only)** | Medium | Validate on target architecture before deployment |

The framework represents a **significant advance** in ultra-low precision training, but practitioners should treat it as a **foundational contribution** rather than a turnkey solution. The limitations identified here are not failures—they are natural constraints of pioneering work that opens new research directions. As hardware support matures and larger-scale validation becomes possible, many of these limitations will be addressed by follow-up research.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work does more than demonstrate a new quantization technique; it fundamentally shifts the conversation around large language model (LLM) development from "how do we compress trained models?" to "how do we train models efficiently from the start?" By validating FP4 pretraining feasibility, this paper opens specific pathways for hardware co-design, algorithmic refinement, and sustainable AI development. This section outlines how the field changes because of this work, what research questions it enables, where it applies practically, and how practitioners should approach integration.

### 7.1. Shifting the Paradigm of LLM Training Efficiency

Historically, quantization has been treated as a post-training optimization—a way to shrink models for deployment (inference) after the expensive training process is complete. This paper challenges that assumption by proving that **Fully Quantized Training (FQT)** at 4-bit precision is viable for pretraining.

*   **Redefining the Bottleneck:** Traditional training is often memory-bandwidth bound because moving 16-bit or 32-bit weights and gradients dominates runtime. By reducing precision to 4-bit, the theoretical memory bandwidth requirement drops by `$4\times$` compared to FP16. The paper's theoretical analysis in Appendix B estimates a **`$2.95\times$` speedup** after accounting for algorithmic overhead (DGE and OCC). This shifts the bottleneck from memory movement to compute throughput, aligning perfectly with the design of next-generation tensor cores.
*   **Hardware-Software Co-Design Signal:** The paper explicitly positions itself as a foundation for upcoming hardware. The authors state: "With the emergence of next-generation hardware supporting FP4, our framework sets a foundation for efficient ultra-low precision training" (Abstract). This signals to hardware vendors (like NVIDIA with the Blackwell B200) that the software stack is ready to leverage FP4 tensor cores immediately upon release, reducing the adoption lag time typically seen with new precision formats.
*   **Sustainability Impact:** The Impact Statement notes that FP4-based methods can contribute to "energy conservation and reduced carbon emissions in AI development" by lowering computational demand. If the theoretical `$3\times$` speedup translates to energy savings in production, this could significantly reduce the environmental cost of training foundational models, which currently require tens of thousands of GPU days (Section 2.1).

### 7.2. Enabled Research Pathways

By solving the stability issues of FP4 training (vanishing gradients and activation outliers), this work removes the primary barriers to entry for future research. Several critical questions are now open for investigation:

*   **Extreme Scale Validation:** The current experiments validate up to **13B parameters** and **100B tokens** (Section 4.2). The immediate next step is validating stability at **70B+ parameters** and **trillions of tokens**. Quantization errors can compound over long training schedules; future work must determine if the `$0.09$` training loss gap observed at 13B (Section 5.2.1) remains constant or widens at 405B scale.
*   **Architecture Generalization:** All experiments use the **LLaMA 2 architecture** (Section 4.1). Future research should test FP4 training on:
    *   **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE):** Sparse activation patterns in MoE models might interact differently with the Outlier Clamping and Compensation (OCC) method.
    *   **Encoder-Decoder Models:** Architectures like T5 have different activation distributions than decoder-only models, potentially requiring adjusted clamping thresholds (`$\alpha$`).
    *   **Multi-Modal Models:** Vision-language models involve different data modalities (images vs. text) that may exhibit distinct outlier behaviors.
*   **Adaptive Quantization Formats:** The paper adopts the **E2M1 format** (2 exponent bits, 1 mantissa bit) as a balanced trade-off (Appendix A). However, Appendix A (Table 4) shows alternatives like **E3M0** (wider dynamic range) or **E1M2** (higher precision). Future work could explore **dynamic format switching**, where layers with heavy outliers use E3M0 while stable layers use E2M1, optimizing the precision-range trade-off per layer.
*   **Hyperparameter Automation:** Currently, DGE uses a fixed `$k=5$` and OCC uses a fixed `$\alpha=0.99$` (Section 4.1). Research could explore **learnable or adaptive hyperparameters** that adjust `$k$` and `$\alpha$` during training based on observed gradient noise or outlier density, potentially closing the remaining accuracy gap with BF16.

### 7.3. Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

Understanding where this framework fits in the current ecosystem is crucial for practitioners. The applicability depends heavily on hardware availability and training stage.

*   **Foundational Model Pretraining:** This is the primary use case. Cloud providers training new base models from scratch (e.g., on DCLM dataset as in Section 4.1) stand to gain the most once native FP4 hardware is available. The framework allows them to reduce memory footprint, potentially fitting larger models on the same cluster or reducing cluster size for the same model.
*   **Research and Prototyping:** Even without native hardware, the framework allows researchers to simulate large-model training dynamics on smaller hardware budgets. While simulation adds overhead (Section 6.1), it enables algorithmic research on ultra-low precision behaviors that were previously impossible due to instability (NaN divergence shown in Figure 6c).
*   **Energy-Constrained Environments:** For organizations prioritizing carbon footprint over raw speed, FP4 training offers a pathway to reduce energy consumption. The Impact Statement highlights this as a key motivation: "democratize access to advanced AI systems while promoting environmental sustainability."
*   **Not Yet for Fine-Tuning or Inference:** It is critical to note that this framework is validated for **pretraining**.
    *   **Fine-Tuning:** The paper distinguishes itself from fine-tuning work (Section 5). While FP4 weights could theoretically be used for fine-tuning, the OCC method is designed for pretraining dynamics. Practitioners should not assume this framework transfers directly to instruction tuning without validation.
    *   **Inference:** While FP4 inference is possible, this work focuses on the training loop (gradients, optimizer states). Inference pipelines have different constraints (e.g., KV cache quantization) not addressed here.

### 7.4. Integration and Reproducibility Guidance

For teams considering adopting this approach, the following guidance synthesizes the paper's technical requirements and limitations into actionable advice.

*   **Hardware Prerequisite:** Do not deploy this for production speedup on current-generation hardware (e.g., H100). The paper states: "All current experiments rely on FP4 simulations, which introduce additional computational overhead... and significantly prolong runtime" (Section 6). Wait for native FP4 tensor core support (e.g., NVIDIA B200/Blackwell series) to realize the theoretical `$2.95\times$` speedup (Appendix B).
*   **Mandatory Components:** You cannot simply cast tensors to 4-bit. The ablation studies prove that naive quantization fails:
    *   **Without OCC:** Training diverges to NaN (Figure 6c). You must implement outlier clamping and sparse compensation.
    *   **Without DGE:** Convergence is suboptimal (Figure 6b). You must replace the Straight-Through Estimator (STE) with the Differentiable Gradient Estimator.
    *   **Without Vector-wise Scaling:** Tensor-wise scaling introduces significant errors (Figure 6d). You must implement token-wise scaling for activations and channel-wise scaling for weights.
*   **Hyperparameter Starting Points:** If reproducing this work, start with the paper's validated settings (Section 4.1):
    *   **DGE Parameter:** `$k = 5$` (balances gradient accuracy and stability).
    *   **OCC Quantile:** `$\alpha = 0.99$` (balances accuracy and sparse matrix overhead).
    *   **Format:** `$\text{E2M1}$` (balanced dynamic range and precision).
*   **Code Availability:** The authors indicate their training framework can be found at `aka.ms/MS.AMP` (Section 1). Practitioners should refer to this repository for the custom CUDA kernels implementing the FP4 look-up table quantization (Appendix A) and sparse matrix compensation logic.
*   **Risk Mitigation:** Monitor training loss closely for the first 10B tokens. The paper shows FP4 loss is slightly higher than BF16 (Section 5.2.1). If the gap widens significantly beyond the reported `$0.06 \sim 0.10$` range, consider adjusting the OCC threshold `$\alpha$` to capture more outliers (e.g., `$\alpha = 0.97$`), acknowledging the computational cost increase (Section 5.3.3).

By treating this framework as a foundational layer rather than a plug-and-play solution, practitioners can leverage FP4 precision to build more efficient training pipelines while contributing to the broader goal of sustainable AI development. The work proves the concept is sound; the next phase of innovation lies in scaling it to production realities.
