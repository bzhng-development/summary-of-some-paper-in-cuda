## 1. Executive Summary

This paper identifies critical limitations in Process Reward Model (PRM) development, demonstrating that Monte Carlo estimation-based data synthesis yields inferior step-wise error identification compared to LLM-as-a-judge and human annotation on benchmarks like PROCESSBENCH. To address this, the authors introduce a consensus filtering mechanism that integrates both methods, releasing `Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-72B` which achieves 78.3% Mean F1 on PROCESSBENCH and 69.3% Best-of-8 Mean Acc, significantly outperforming prior open-source PRMs. These findings matter because they expose biases in conventional Best-of-N evaluation and establish new best practices for data construction and evaluation in mathematical reasoning supervision.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Process Supervision in Mathematical Reasoning

Large Language Models have made remarkable advances in mathematical reasoning, yet they remain fundamentally unreliable in how they reach conclusions. The critical issue this paper addresses is not simply that models get wrong answers — it's that **even when models produce correct final answers, their reasoning processes can contain serious errors**. Consider a student solving an algebra problem: they might accidentally cancel terms incorrectly but then make a compensating error that leads to the right answer. The final result looks correct, but the reasoning is flawed. This phenomenon undermines trust in LLM outputs and limits their deployment in high-stakes applications where reasoning transparency matters.

Process Reward Models (PRMs) emerged as a solution to this problem. Unlike Outcome Reward Models (ORMs) that score only the final answer, PRMs provide **fine-grained supervision by evaluating the correctness of each intermediate reasoning step**. The promise is clear: if we can identify and penalize erroneous steps during training or inference, we should produce models with more reliable reasoning processes. However, the paper reveals that the community's approach to building and evaluating PRMs contains fundamental flaws that have gone largely unexamined.

### Why This Problem Matters

The importance of this work operates on two levels:

**Practical Impact:** Mathematical reasoning capabilities underpin many critical applications — from automated tutoring systems to scientific computation assistance. If PRMs cannot reliably distinguish correct from incorrect reasoning steps, then systems built on top of them will propagate errors silently. The paper shows that existing PRMs often fail to detect process errors in 40-50% of cases where answers are correct but reasoning is flawed (Figure 6, Section 3.2.1). This is not a marginal improvement problem; it's a foundational reliability issue.

**Theoretical Significance:** The paper exposes a conceptual confusion in the field between **process verification** (is this step correct?) and **value estimation** (will this step lead to a correct answer?). These are fundamentally different tasks, but common data construction methods conflate them. Understanding this distinction is crucial for anyone building reasoning supervision systems, not just for mathematics but for any domain requiring step-by-step verification.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper systematically examines three approaches to constructing PRM training data, each with distinct trade-offs:

**1. Human Annotation (Lightman et al., 2023)**

The PRM800K dataset represents the gold standard approach: human annotators with detailed instructions verify each reasoning step. This method produces high-quality labels but faces a prohibitive cost barrier. The paper notes PRM800K contains approximately 265k samples after deduplication — substantial, but expensive to scale. Despite the smaller dataset size, models trained on human annotation demonstrate superior generalization on complex tasks like OlympiadBench and Omni-MATH (Table 4, Section 3.1.2).

**2. Monte Carlo (MC) Estimation (Math-Shepherd, Wang et al., 2024b)**

This automated approach has become the dominant method in practice. The mechanism works as follows:

$$
\text{Step Label} = \frac{\text{Number of completions yielding correct answer}}{\text{Total completions}}
$$

For each reasoning step, the method samples multiple completions (typically 8) from that point forward and estimates step correctness based on the empirical probability of reaching the correct final answer. The appeal is obvious: no human annotation required, scalable to millions of samples.

However, the paper identifies a **fundamental conceptual flaw**: MC estimation evaluates deterministic current-step correctness based on potential future outcomes. This introduces two critical failure modes:

- **False positives:** The completion model may generate correct answers from incorrect steps (lucky guesses, compensating errors)
- **False negatives:** The completion model may generate incorrect answers from correct steps (downstream errors unrelated to the current step)

The experimental results are striking: PRMs trained on 860k MC-estimated samples underperform models trained on 264k human-annotated samples on PROCESSBENCH (40.1% vs 56.5% Mean F1, Table 4), despite having 3× more training data.

**3. LLM-as-a-Judge (Zheng et al., 2023)**

This approach uses a large language model to directly verify each reasoning step's correctness through prompting. The paper employs Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct as the judge model. While more expensive than MC estimation (requiring forward passes through a large model), it avoids the fundamental mismatch between step correctness and outcome probability. On PROCESSBENCH, LLM-as-a-judge achieves 46.5% Mean F1 — substantially better than MC estimation's 40.1% despite using the same underlying data (Table 4).

### The Evaluation Problem: Best-of-N Bias

Beyond data construction, the paper identifies a second critical gap: **evaluation methodology**. The field has predominantly relied on Best-of-N (BoN) evaluation, where N candidate responses are generated and the highest-scored response (according to the PRM) is selected. The metric `prm@N` measures the accuracy of this selected response.

The paper reveals three systematic biases in BoN evaluation:

**Bias 1: Misalignment Between BoN and PRM Objectives**

BoN evaluation inherently rewards selecting responses with correct final answers. However, PRMs are supposed to verify reasoning processes. When policy models generate responses with correct answers but flawed processes (which Figure 6 shows occurs in 5.1% to 43.4% of cases depending on task difficulty), a PRM that correctly penalizes these flawed processes will actually perform *worse* on BoN evaluation. This creates perverse optimization incentives.

**Bias 2: Inflated Performance from Limited Discrimination**

PRMs with weak process verification capabilities tend to assign high scores to all responses with correct answers, regardless of reasoning quality. This tolerance inflates BoN scores while masking the PRM's actual inability to detect process errors. Table 5 shows that except for the authors' released PRMs, all other open-source PRMs demonstrate detection accuracy below 50% for cases with correct answers but erroneous reasoning.

**Bias 3: Process-to-Outcome Shift**

Perhaps most critically, the paper shows that PRMs optimized for BoN evaluation gradually shift from process-based to outcome-based assessment. Figure 8 reveals that for several existing PRMs (EurusPRM-Stage1, EurusPRM-Stage2, Math-Shepherd-PRM-7B, Skywork-PRM-7B), over 40% of minimum step scores occur at the final answer step. This indicates these models have effectively become ORMs in practice — they're evaluating the final answer rather than the reasoning process.

### How This Paper Positions Itself

This work positions itself as a **corrective intervention** in the PRM development pipeline. Rather than proposing a novel architecture or training objective, the authors systematically audit existing practices and demonstrate where they fail. Their contributions are methodological:

1. **Empirical Demonstration:** Through controlled experiments comparing MC estimation, LLM-as-a-judge, and human annotation on identical model architectures, they isolate the impact of data construction methods (Tables 3-4).

2. **Consensus Filtering Mechanism:** They propose retaining only instances where both MC estimation and LLM-as-a-judge agree on error step locations. This hybrid approach achieves comparable PROCESSBENCH performance to LLM-as-a-judge alone while using only 40% of the data (Figure 2, Section 3.1.3).

3. **Evaluation Framework Reform:** They advocate for combining response-level metrics (BoN) with step-level metrics (PROCESSBENCH) to avoid optimization bias. Their released PRMs achieve 78.3% Mean F1 on PROCESSBENCH while maintaining competitive BoN performance (Figure 1).

4. **Practical Guidelines:** The paper provides actionable recommendations — use hard labels rather than soft labels for MC estimation, set the negative label threshold to 0 (any successful completion indicates correctness), and employ different scoring strategies depending on data construction method (Figure 9, Section 3.2.4).

The paper's stance is deliberately critical of prevailing practices while constructive in offering alternatives. It does not claim MC estimation is useless — rather, it demonstrates that MC estimation requires stringent filtering and should not be the sole data source. Similarly, it does not reject BoN evaluation entirely but argues it must be supplemented with step-level assessment to ensure PRMs actually learn process verification rather than outcome prediction.

This positioning matters because the field has been optimizing for metrics that may not reflect the actual capability we care about. The paper forces a reckoning: if we want PRMs that verify reasoning processes, we must measure and optimize for process verification directly, not proxy metrics that can be gamed through outcome-based shortcuts.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a methodological audit and improvement framework for Process Reward Model development, with the core idea being that combining Monte Carlo estimation with LLM-as-a-judge through consensus filtering produces higher-quality training data than either method alone.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a Process Reward Model that assigns correctness scores to individual reasoning steps in mathematical problem solutions, rather than scoring only the final answer. The solution addresses the problem of noisy training data by implementing a two-phase pipeline: first generating candidate step labels through automated methods, then filtering to retain only instances where multiple labeling approaches agree on where errors occur.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The technical pipeline consists of four major components connected in sequence. First, the **Query Collection Module** gathers approximately 500,000 mathematical problems with verified golden answers. Second, the **Response Generation Module** produces 6-8 diverse solution attempts per query using Qwen2-Math-Instruct and Qwen2.5-Math-Instruct models at both 7B and 72B parameter scales. Third, the **Step Labeling Module** operates in parallel through two channels: Monte Carlo estimation generates 8 completions from each reasoning step to estimate correctness probabilities, while LLM-as-a-judge uses Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct to directly verify each step through prompting. Fourth, the **Consensus Filtering Module** compares labels from both channels and retains only the approximately 40% of instances where they agree on error step locations. Finally, the **PRM Training Module** initializes from Qwen2.5-Math-Instruct base models, replaces the language modeling head with a two-layer scalar-value head, and trains using cross-entropy loss on the last token of each reasoning step.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with data construction because the paper's central claim is that data quality, not model architecture, determines PRM performance.
- We then explain the Monte Carlo estimation mechanism in detail, including why it fails and what threshold settings work best.
- We describe the LLM-as-a-judge prompting approach and how it differs fundamentally from MC estimation in what it measures.
- We detail the consensus filtering mechanism that combines both methods, including the exact retention rates and performance trade-offs.
- We cover the model architecture and training objectives, explaining why hard labels outperform soft labels after filtering.
- We conclude with evaluation methodology, showing why different scoring strategies suit different data construction approaches.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Data Construction Pipeline: Phase One (Expansion)**

The data construction procedure begins with collecting a large-scale dataset of approximately 500,000 queries that have verified golden answers. For each query, the system generates 6-8 diverse responses by mixing outputs from the Qwen2-Math-Instruct and Qwen2.5-Math-Instruct series models, spanning both 7B and 72B parameter sizes to ensure response diversity. These responses are systematically split into individual reasoning steps using the delimiter "\n\n", which creates discrete units that can be labeled independently. To assess the correctness of each step, the system conducts 8 independent completions starting from that step using Qwen2.5-Math-Instruct series models with the corresponding model size. The step labels are estimated based on the empirical probabilities of each step yielding the correct final answer across these completions. For hard labels, the system treats a step as correct if any one of the 8 completions yields the correct final answer, and negative otherwise, which corresponds to a threshold of 0 out of 8 successful completions. For soft labels, the system determines the value between 0 and 1 as the proportion of completions leading to the correct final answers, calculated as:

$$
\text{Soft Label} = \frac{\text{Number of completions with correct final answer}}{8}
$$

The system eliminates all steps subsequent to those labeled as incorrect (label 0), as their validity becomes irrelevant after an error occurs, and this removal prevents potential model confusion during training.

**Data Construction Pipeline: Phase Two (Filtering)**

The filtering phase employs an LLM instantiated by Qwen2.5-Instruct-72B to serve as a critic that verifies the reasoning process for all responses step by step. The LLM-as-a-judge mechanism uses a specific prompt template (provided in Appendix C) that instructs the model to review each paragraph of the solution in sequence, analyzing, verifying, and critiquing the reasoning in detail. The prompt requires the model to provide analyses for each paragraph and a final conclusion of "Correct" or "Incorrect", with the instruction to stop analyzing subsequent paragraphs once an error is detected. The consensus filtering mechanism retains instances only when both LLM-as-a-judge and MC estimation show consensus on the error reasoning step locations in the solution. As demonstrated in Figure 2, approximately 40% of the data are preserved after consensus filtering, reducing the 860k sample dataset to approximately 350k samples. This filtering strategy yields performance benefits across both soft and hard label schemes, with the reduced dataset after consensus filtering significantly outperforming MC estimation alone on PROCESSBENCH while achieving comparable performance to LLM-as-a-judge while using only 40% of the data.

**Monte Carlo Estimation: Mechanism and Limitations**

Monte Carlo estimation attempts to estimate the potential of reaching the correct final answer in the future from the current step, which fundamentally differs from what a Process Reward Model should measure. A PRM should function as a deterministic evaluator of current step correctness, while MC estimation operates as a predictive estimator of future solution potential. This methodological mismatch introduces performance and generalization limitations because MC estimation relies heavily on the completion model's performance. The completion model may generate correct answers based on incorrect steps through lucky guesses or compensating errors, introducing false positive labels. Conversely, the completion model may generate incorrect answers based on correct steps due to downstream errors unrelated to the current step, introducing false negative labels. The paper investigates threshold selection for distinguishing between positive and negative labels based on the MC estimation result of 8 completions, conducting experiments with threshold values from 1/8 to 7/8 at 1/8 intervals. As shown in Figure 5, as the threshold increases, performance deteriorates on both Best-of-8 and PROCESSBENCH, indicating that using an MC estimated value of 0 as the negative label and all others as positive labels yields the best results. Therefore, the paper recommends setting the threshold to 0, meaning that a step is considered correct if any completion starting from this step reaches the correct final answer.

**Hard Labels vs. Soft Labels: Training Objective Design**

The paper constructs 3 million training data using MC estimation, where for each reasoning step it performs 8 completions, then applies the consensus filtering strategy to reduce the dataset to 1.5 million samples. The system trains PRMs using both soft labels and hard labels on both the 3 million and 1.5 million datasets to compare their effectiveness. Before data filtering, the performance difference between soft and hard labels is not significant, which the authors attribute to the high noise level masking their distinctions. However, after data filtering, hard labels substantially outperform soft labels on both Best-of-8 and PROCESSBENCH, as shown in Figure 3 and Figure 4. The paper identifies two limitations of soft labels: first, the correctness of steps should be deterministic, and training PRMs with soft labels that represent future possibilities introduces additional noise. For instance, when numerous completely correct steps are assigned soft labels lower than 1, it reduces the model's ability to discriminate between positive and negative labels. Second, only 8 completions for step correctness estimation exhibit high variance and are relatively crude, and although better estimation accuracy can be achieved by increasing the number of completions, the associated costs may outweigh the incremental benefits. The training objective employs cross-entropy loss on the tokens at the end of each step for the binary classification task using hard labels.

**Model Architecture and Initialization**

The trained PRMs are initialized from the supervised fine-tuned Qwen2.5-Math-7B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-Math-72B-Instruct models. The system replaces the original language modeling head, which is used for next token prediction, with a scalar-value head consisting of two linear layers. This architectural change converts the model from a generative language model to a discriminative reward model that outputs scalar scores rather than token probabilities. The model calculates cross-entropy loss on the last tokens of each step for the binary classification task, which trains the model to predict whether each reasoning step is correct or incorrect. Both 7B and 72B parameter PRMs are trained following this architecture, with the 72B model showing slightly better overall performance on Best-of-8 evaluation and substantially better performance on PROCESSBENCH (78.3% vs 73.5% Mean F1, Table 7).

**Evaluation Methodology: Best-of-N Scoring Strategies**

In Best-of-N evaluation, the overall solution score is derived by combining individual step scores, and the paper identifies that different PRMs require different optimal scoring strategies. When each step's score represents the probability of that specific step being correct, it is generally acceptable to combine these step-level scores through methods like product or minimum to calculate the overall solution score. However, the situation becomes different when using MC estimation, where each step's score actually estimates the probability of reaching the correct final answer in the future from the current position. Given this forward-looking nature of MC estimation, the system should neither multiply the estimated probabilities across steps, as these estimates are dependent on each other, nor simply take the minimum estimated value from a particular step as the overall score. Instead, the estimated value from the final step naturally integrates information from the entire solution process, making it more suitable as the final score for the complete solution. As shown in Figure 9, for MC estimation-based PRMs, using the last score shows significantly better performance than product and minimum approaches across multiple models. The trend is opposite for human annotation and LLM-as-a-judge trained PRMs, where product and minimum scoring are more appropriate. This suggests that if a PRM is trained via MC estimation and evaluated in BoN, the last score strategy may be more reasonable and effective, though this use of PRM in BoN has deviated from PRM's original intended purpose.

**Evaluation Methodology: PROCESSBENCH Step-Level Assessment**

The paper employs PROCESSBENCH as a complement to Best-of-N evaluation to measure the capability of models to identify erroneous steps in mathematical reasoning. PROCESSBENCH requires models to identify the first step that contains an error or conclude that all steps are correct, which directly assesses process verification capability rather than outcome selection. Following the evaluation methods for PRMs in PROCESSBENCH, the system locates the first erroneous step from predict scores yielded by PRMs. The paper also extracts instances from PROCESSBENCH where answers are correct but processes are erroneous to analyze the detection accuracy of PRMs for these specific cases. As shown in Table 5, except for the released PRMs Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-7B and Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-72B, all other open-sourced PRMs demonstrate detection accuracy rates below 50% for these cases. This limited discriminative capability indicates that PRMs struggle to differentiate between genuinely correct responses and those with merely superficial answer correctness in BoN evaluations.

**Design Choice Rationale: Why Consensus Filtering Works**

The consensus filtering mechanism works because it leverages the complementary strengths of MC estimation and LLM-as-a-judge while mitigating their individual weaknesses. MC estimation provides scalable, automated labeling but suffers from noise due to its reliance on completion model performance. LLM-as-a-judge provides more direct step correctness assessment but may have its own biases and is computationally expensive. By retaining only instances where both methods agree on error step locations, the system filters out cases where either method produces unreliable labels. The approximately 60% of data discarded through this filtering represents instances where the two labeling approaches disagree, which are precisely the cases most likely to contain noisy or ambiguous labels. This filtering achieves comparable PROCESSBENCH performance to LLM-as-a-judge alone while using only 40% of the data, demonstrating improved data efficiency. The filtering also improves Best-of-8 performance for hard label models from 65.4% to 67.2% Mean Acc (Figure 3), showing that data quality matters more than data quantity for PRM training.

**Design Choice Rationale: Why BoN Alone Is Insufficient**

The paper argues that Best-of-N evaluation alone is insufficient for PRM assessment because it creates optimization incentives that shift PRMs from process-based to outcome-based verification. During the BoN selection process based on PRM-predicted scores, the lowest step score acts as the key limiting factor that affects the selection criteria of PRMs, regardless of whether the system employs the minimum score or the product of scores to evaluate the full solution. As shown in Figure 8, for several existing PRMs including EurusPRM-Stage1, EurusPRM-Stage2, Math-Shepherd-PRM-7B, and Skywork-PRM-7B, over 40% of minimum step scores occur at the final answer step. This indicates these models have effectively become Outcome Reward Models in practice, evaluating the final answer rather than the reasoning process. The paper identifies three sources of this bias: unreliable policy models generate responses with correct answers but flawed processes, creating misalignment between BoN evaluation criteria and PRM objectives; PRMs with limited process verification capability demonstrate tolerance for these cases, resulting in inflated BoN performance; and model optimization solely focused on BoN evaluation leads PRMs to drift toward prioritizing final answers over reasoning processes. Therefore, the paper advocates supplementing response-level BoN evaluation with step-level assessment methods like PROCESSBENCH to avoid this process-to-outcome shift.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the conceptual breakthroughs that extend beyond the specific experimental setup. While the Technical Approach detailed *how* the system was built, these insights explain *why* certain design choices fundamentally alter our understanding of Process Reward Models (PRMs). We distinguish between incremental improvements (better hyperparameters) and fundamental innovations (new conceptual frameworks or evaluation standards).

### 4.1 Conceptual Clarification: PRMs are Not Value Models

The most significant theoretical contribution of this work is the rigorous distinction between **Process Reward Models** and **Value Models**, a distinction often blurred in prior literature.

*   **Prior Assumption:** Previous works (e.g., Math-Shepherd) treated Monte Carlo (MC) estimation as a valid method for training PRMs. The underlying assumption was that if a step leads to a correct answer with high probability, the step itself must be correct.
*   **This Paper's Insight:** The authors demonstrate that MC estimation inherently trains a **Value Model**, not a PRM.
    *   A **PRM** should answer: "Is this specific step logically valid?" (Deterministic, current-state verification).
    *   A **Value Model** answers: "What is the probability of success from this state?" (Probabilistic, future-state estimation).

This distinction explains the performance divergence observed in Table 4 (Section 3.1.2). MC-based models scale well with data quantity (860k samples) but fail to generalize on process verification tasks (40.1% Mean F1) because they are optimizing for outcome probability, not logical correctness. Conversely, human-annotated models (264k samples) achieve 56.5% Mean F1 because they train on deterministic correctness labels.

**Why This Matters:** This insight forces a reevaluation of existing open-source PRMs. Many models claiming to be PRMs may actually be functioning as step-level Value Models. This explains why they perform well on Best-of-N (which rewards outcome probability) but fail on PROCESSBENCH (which rewards process verification). Future research must explicitly choose which objective they are optimizing, as the data construction methods are mutually incompatible for these distinct goals.

### 4.2 Consensus Filtering: Disagreement as a Noise Signal

While combining data sources is common, the specific mechanism of **Consensus Filtering** introduced here represents a methodological innovation in data efficiency.

*   **Prior Approach:** Researchers typically choose one annotation method (e.g., solely MC estimation for scale or solely Human/LLM for quality).
*   **This Paper's Innovation:** The authors treat **disagreement** between MC estimation and LLM-as-a-judge as a proxy for label noise. Instead of merging labels (e.g., via voting), they discard instances where the methods disagree.

The efficiency gain is substantial. As shown in Figure 2 (Section 3.1.3), retaining only the ~40% of data where both methods agree allows the model to match the performance of LLM-as-a-judge trained on 100% of the data (46.3% vs 46.5% Mean F1 on PROCESSBENCH).

**Why This Matters:** This transforms a limitation (MC estimation noise) into a utility (noise detection). It provides a practical pathway for scaling high-quality process supervision without incurring the full cost of LLM-as-a-judge or human annotation for every sample. It suggests that in high-stakes reasoning tasks, **data precision outweighs data volume**—a counter-intuitive finding in an era focused on scaling laws.

### 4.3 Evaluation Reform: The Necessity of Step-Level Metrics

The paper provides empirical evidence that the field's standard evaluation metric, Best-of-N (BoN), is actively harmful when used in isolation for PRM development.

*   **Prior Standard:** PRM performance was almost exclusively measured by `prm@N` (accuracy of the selected response in BoN).
*   **This Paper's Finding:** Optimizing solely for BoN induces a **Process-to-Outcome Shift**. Figure 8 (Section 3.2.3) shows that for several existing PRMs, over 40% of minimum step scores occur at the final answer step. This indicates the models have degenerated into Outcome Reward Models (ORMs), ignoring intermediate reasoning.

**Why This Matters:** This insight mandates a change in evaluation standards for the entire community. Relying on BoN alone allows models to "game" the metric by focusing on the final answer, masking their inability to verify reasoning steps. The paper advocates for a dual-metric framework:
1.  **Response-Level:** BoN (for utility).
2.  **Step-Level:** PROCESSBENCH (for capability verification).

Without step-level metrics, future PRM research risks optimizing for proxies that do not reflect genuine process supervision capabilities.

### 4.4 Operational Nuance: Scoring Strategies Must Match Data Source

A subtle but critical operational insight is that the optimal scoring strategy for aggregating step scores depends entirely on the data construction method.

*   **Prior Practice:** Most works universally applied `product` or `minimum` scoring to aggregate step scores into a solution score, assuming step scores represent independent probabilities of correctness.
*   **This Paper's Finding:** Figure 9 (Section 3.2.4) reveals a divergence:
    *   **MC-Based PRMs:** Perform best with **`last` step scoring**. Since MC estimates future success probability, the final step's score already integrates information from the entire trajectory. Multiplying probabilities compounds estimation errors.
    *   **Human/LLM-Based PRMs:** Perform best with **`product` or `minimum` scoring**. Since these labels represent deterministic step correctness, the solution is only valid if *all* steps are valid (logical AND operation).

**Why This Matters:** This prevents a common implementation error. Applying `product` scoring to an MC-based PRM (which acts like a Value Model) artificially penalizes long reasoning chains due to probability multiplication, even if the model is confident in the outcome. Recognizing that **data semantics dictate aggregation logic** ensures that inference-time strategies align with training-time objectives.

### 4.5 Summary of Contributions

| Innovation | Type | Prior State | This Work's Advance | Evidence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **PRM vs. Value Model** | Fundamental | Conflated tasks | Distinct objectives requiring distinct data | Table 4 (F1 gap) |
| **Consensus Filtering** | Methodological | Single-source data | Disagreement as noise filter | Figure 2 (40% data efficiency) |
| **Evaluation Framework** | Community Standard | BoN only | BoN + PROCESSBENCH required | Figure 8 (Process-to-Outcome shift) |
| **Scoring Strategy** | Operational | Universal `product`/`min` | Data-dependent (`last` vs. `product`) | Figure 9 (Performance divergence) |

These insights collectively shift the focus from "building bigger PRMs" to "building correctly specified PRMs." The performance gains reported (e.g., 78.3% Mean F1 on PROCESSBENCH for `Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-72B`) are not merely the result of scale, but of aligning data construction, training objectives, and evaluation metrics with the true definition of process supervision.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines the empirical evidence supporting the paper's claims. We analyze the evaluation methodology, present quantitative results with specific numbers, assess whether experiments convincingly support the conclusions, and identify limitations in the experimental design.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Benchmarks**

The paper employs a multi-tiered evaluation framework spanning response-level and step-level assessment:

| Benchmark | Purpose | Key Metrics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **GSM8K, MATH, Minerva Math** | Standard math reasoning | Accuracy (%) |
| **OlympiadBench, Omni-MATH** | Complex problem solving | Accuracy (%) |
| **PROCESSBENCH** | Step-wise error identification | Mean F1 (%) |
| **Chinese Benchmarks** | Cross-lingual generalization | Accuracy (%) |
| **AIME24, AMC23** | Competition-level difficulty | Accuracy (%) |

The response-level evaluation uses **Best-of-N (BoN)** sampling with N=8 as the primary metric, denoted as `prm@8`. The upper bound is `pass@8` (proportion of test samples where any of 8 samples yields correct answers), and the baseline is `maj@8` (majority voting among 8 samples).

**Baseline Models**

The comparison spans multiple model scales and training approaches:

- **1.5B scale:** Skywork-PRM-1.5B
- **7B-8B scale:** Math-Shepherd-PRM-7B, RLHFlow-PRM-Mistral-8B, RLHFlow-PRM-Deepseek-8B, Skywork-PRM-7B, EurusPRM-Stage1/2, Qwen2.5-Math-7B-Math-Shepherd, Qwen2.5-Math-7B-PRM800K
- **72B scale:** Qwen2.5-Math-RM-72B (ORM), Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-72B (authors' model)
- **LLM-as-a-judge:** GPT-4o-0806, o1-mini, Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct, QwQ-32B-Preview

**Policy Models**

Experiments are conducted on two policy models:
- **Qwen2.5-Math-7B-Instruct** (primary evaluation)
- **Qwen2.5-Math-72B-Instruct** (supplementary evaluation, Appendix B.1)

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Validity

**Strengths**

1. **Comprehensive Baseline Coverage:** The paper compares against 10+ existing PRMs across multiple scales, plus proprietary LLMs (GPT-4o, o1-mini). This breadth makes the performance claims difficult to dismiss as cherry-picked comparisons.

2. **Dual-Metric Evaluation:** By reporting both BoN and PROCESSBENCH results, the paper avoids the optimization bias it criticizes. The divergence between these metrics (e.g., MC estimation performs better on BoN but worse on PROCESSBENCH) directly supports the claim that BoN alone is insufficient.

3. **Systematic Ablations:** The hard/soft label comparison (Figures 3-4), threshold selection (Figure 5), and scoring strategy analysis (Figure 9) provide actionable guidance rather than just final results.

4. **Cross-Validation Across Policy Models:** Results are reported for both 7B and 72B policy models (Tables 6, 9), showing the approach generalizes across scales.

**Limitations and Unanswered Questions**

1. **Training Compute Not Reported:** The paper does not specify training duration, GPU hours, or compute costs for the PRMs. This makes it difficult to assess whether the performance gains justify the additional expense of LLM-as-a-judge annotation.

2. **LLM-as-a-Judge Cost Hidden:** While consensus filtering reduces data volume by 60%, the filtering process itself requires forward passes through Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct for all 860k samples. The net compute savings compared to pure LLM-as-a-judge training is not quantified.

3. **Single Judge Model:** All LLM-as-a-judge annotations use Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct. The paper does not test whether smaller judge models (e.g., 7B) could achieve similar consensus filtering effectiveness at lower cost.

4. **PROCESSBENCH Test Set Contamination Risk:** The paper notes PRM800K was deduplicated against the test set, but does not explicitly state whether the 860k MC-estimated queries overlap with PROCESSBENCH. Some performance gains could reflect data leakage rather than genuine capability improvements.

5. **Reinforcement Learning Integration Unexplored:** The Limitations section acknowledges that "best practices for utilizing PRMs in reinforcement learning remain unexplored." All results use BoN selection, not RLHF or process-supervised RL. This limits practical applicability claims.

6. **Error Type Analysis Missing:** PROCESSBENCH reports aggregate F1 but does not break down performance by error type (calculation errors vs. logical errors vs. hallucinated steps). Understanding which errors the PRM detects would inform deployment decisions.

7. **Confidence Calibration Not Measured:** The paper treats PRM scores as ranking signals but does not evaluate whether the scores are well-calibrated probabilities. A PRM assigning 0.9 to correct steps and 0.1 to incorrect steps is more useful than one assigning 0.6 and 0.4, even if ranking accuracy is identical.

**Mixed Results and Trade-offs**

The experiments reveal genuine trade-offs that the paper acknowledges but does not fully resolve:

| Trade-off | Observation | Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **BoN vs. PROCESSBENCH** | MC estimation: 65.9% BoN, 40.1% F1. Human: 64.9% BoN, 56.5% F1 | Optimizing for one metric degrades the other |
| **Data Quantity vs. Quality** | 860k MC samples underperform 264k human samples on PROCESSBENCH | More data does not compensate for noisy labels |
| **7B vs. 72B PRM** | 7B PRM on 72B policy: 69.9% BoN (below maj@8 at 70.2%) | Smaller PRMs struggle to supervise larger policies |
| **Consensus Filtering** | Retains 40% data, matches LLM-as-a-judge performance | Discarding 60% of data may waste potentially useful signal |

The most significant unresolved tension is between **BoN utility** and **process verification capability**. The authors' PRMs achieve both, but the ablation studies show these objectives can conflict. Future work must determine whether this tension is inherent to the task or an artifact of current methods.

### 5.5 Do Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: MC Estimation Yields Inferior Process Verification**

**Supported.** Table 4 shows MC estimation (40.1% F1) underperforms both LLM-as-a-judge (46.5% F1) and human annotation (56.5% F1) on PROCESSBENCH, despite using 3× more data than human annotation. The gap widens on harder tasks (Omni-MATH: 19.8% vs 44.3% F1).

**Claim 2: BoN Evaluation Alone Is Biased**

**Supported.** Figure 8 shows 40-54% of minimum scores occur at the final step for existing PRMs, indicating outcome-focused behavior. Table 5 shows these same PRMs detect correct-answer/flawed-process cases at below 50% accuracy. The divergence between BoN and PROCESSBENCH rankings (Figure 7) confirms the metrics measure different capabilities.

**Claim 3: Consensus Filtering Improves Data Efficiency**

**Supported.** Figure 2 demonstrates that 350k consensus-filtered samples achieve 46.3% F1, matching 860k LLM-as-a-judge samples (46.5% F1) with 60% less data. This is a direct efficiency gain.

**Claim 4: Hard Labels Outperform Soft Labels After Filtering**

**Supported.** Figures 3-4 show hard labels achieve 67.2% BoN and 66.5% F1 after filtering, versus 65.4% BoN and 49.3% F1 for soft labels. The 17.2 point F1 gap is substantial.

**Claim 5: Released PRMs Are State-of-the-Art**

**Supported.** Table 6 shows `Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-72B` achieves 69.3% BoN average, exceeding all open-source PRMs. Table 7 shows 78.3% PROCESSBENCH F1, also exceeding all open-source alternatives. The models are publicly released at the provided HuggingFace links.

**Claim 6: Scoring Strategy Should Match Data Source**

**Partially Supported.** Figure 9 shows the trend (MC-based prefers `last`, human/LLM-based prefers `product`/`min`), but the absolute differences are modest (1-2 percentage points). The practical impact of this recommendation is unclear without larger-scale deployment data.

### 5.6 Summary

The experimental analysis is thorough and largely convincing. The paper's strength lies in its systematic comparison of data construction methods under controlled conditions, isolating the variable of interest (annotation approach) while holding architecture constant. The dual-metric evaluation framework (BoN + PROCESSBENCH) is a significant contribution that should become standard practice.

However, the absence of compute cost reporting, the unexplored RL integration, and the lack of error-type breakdown limit the practical actionability of the results. The trade-off between BoN performance and process verification capability remains unresolved—the authors' models achieve both, but the ablation studies show these objectives can conflict, and the conditions under which this occurs are not fully characterized.

For practitioners, the clearest takeaway is: **if process verification is the goal, use PROCESSBENCH for evaluation and prefer human/LLM annotation over pure MC estimation.** For researchers, the open questions around RL integration and confidence calibration represent fertile ground for future work.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the boundaries of the paper's contributions. While the authors demonstrate significant improvements in PRM development, their approach rests on specific assumptions, leaves important scenarios unaddressed, and faces practical constraints that practitioners must understand before deployment. We distinguish between limitations the authors explicitly acknowledge and those that emerge from careful reading of the experimental results.

### 6.1 Core Assumptions Underlying the Approach

The consensus filtering mechanism and the resulting PRM performance depend on several foundational assumptions that may not hold universally.

**Assumption 1: LLM-as-a-Judge Provides Reliable Ground Truth**

The entire consensus filtering pipeline treats LLM-as-a-judge annotations as a higher-quality signal than MC estimation. However, the paper provides no independent validation of the judge model's accuracy. The Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct model serves as the sole critic, but:

- No inter-annotator agreement metrics are reported between the LLM judge and human annotators
- The paper does not test whether smaller judge models (e.g., 7B or 32B) could achieve similar filtering effectiveness
- Table 7 shows Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct achieves only 61.2% Mean F1 on PROCESSBENCH as a direct step verifier, yet its annotations are treated as ground truth for filtering

This creates a circular dependency: the PRM is trained to match LLM-as-a-judge labels, then evaluated on PROCESSBENCH where the same LLM family performs moderately. If the judge model has systematic blind spots (e.g., certain error types it consistently misses), the PRM will inherit and amplify these biases.

**Assumption 2: Disagreement Indicates Noise, Not Ambiguity**

The consensus filtering mechanism discards approximately 60% of data where MC estimation and LLM-as-a-judge disagree (Figure 2, Section 3.1.3). This treats disagreement as a noise signal. However, some disagreements may reflect genuine ambiguity in the reasoning process:

- A step might be mathematically valid but inefficient (MC says correct, LLM says incorrect due to style)
- A step might contain a minor error that doesn't propagate (LLM says incorrect, MC says correct because completions recover)

By discarding these cases, the approach potentially removes valuable training signal about edge cases and boundary conditions. The paper does not analyze the discarded 60% to characterize what types of disagreements occur most frequently.

**Assumption 3: Step Independence for Scoring Aggregation**

The recommendation to use `product` or `minimum` scoring for human/LLM-based PRMs assumes that step correctness is logically independent—that a solution is valid only if all steps are valid. However, mathematical reasoning often contains dependencies where:

- Later steps can compensate for earlier minor errors
- Some steps are optional or can be reordered
- Errors in intermediate calculations may cancel out

The `product` scoring strategy penalizes long reasoning chains more heavily than short ones, even if all steps are equally reliable. For a solution with 10 steps each scored 0.9, the product is $0.9^{10} \approx 0.35$, while a 3-step solution scores $0.9^3 \approx 0.73$. This creates an implicit bias against complex multi-step reasoning that the paper does not address.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

Several important problem settings and deployment scenarios fall outside the paper's experimental scope.

**Reinforcement Learning Integration**

The Limitations section explicitly acknowledges that "the best practices for utilizing PRMs in reinforcement learning remain unexplored." All experimental results use Best-of-N selection, not process-supervised RL. This is a significant gap because:

- BoN selection is a post-hoc filtering mechanism, while RL integrates rewards into the training loop
- PRM gradients in RL may behave differently than PRM scores in BoN selection
- The computational cost of step-level rewards during RL training is substantially higher than outcome-level rewards

The paper releases PRMs but provides no guidance on how to integrate them into PPO, DPO, or other RL frameworks. Practitioners cannot directly apply these models to RL-based improvement pipelines without additional research.

**Cross-Model Supervision**

Table 9 (Appendix B.1) reveals a critical limitation: the 7B PRM struggles to supervise a 72B policy model. When evaluating on Qwen2.5-Math-72B-Instruct:

| Model | BoN Avg. | vs. maj@8 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| maj@8 | 70.2% | — |
| Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-7B | 69.9% | **-0.3 points** |
| Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-72B | 70.7% | +0.5 points |

The 7B PRM performs *worse* than simple majority voting when supervising the 72B policy. This suggests PRMs must be scaled proportionally to the policy models they supervise—a costly requirement for large-scale deployment. The paper does not explore whether distillation or other techniques could enable smaller PRMs to supervise larger policies effectively.

**Error Type Specificity**

PROCESSBENCH reports aggregate F1 scores but does not break down performance by error type. Mathematical reasoning errors fall into distinct categories:

- Calculation errors (arithmetic mistakes)
- Logical errors (invalid deductions)
- Hallucinated steps (fabricated facts or theorems)
- Missing steps (skipped reasoning)

Table 5 shows the authors' PRMs achieve 53.9-58.1% detection accuracy for correct-answer/flawed-process cases, but we do not know which error types are detected reliably. A PRM that catches calculation errors but misses logical fallacies would have very different deployment implications than one with the inverse profile. This granularity is absent from the analysis.

**Non-Mathematical Reasoning Domains**

All experiments focus on mathematical reasoning benchmarks. The paper makes no claims about generalization to:

- Code generation and debugging
- Scientific reasoning (physics, chemistry)
- Legal or medical reasoning
- Multi-hop question answering

The consensus filtering mechanism may not transfer to domains where:
- Golden answers are not easily verifiable (unlike math problems)
- Step boundaries are less clear than "\n\n" delimiters
- LLM-as-a-judge lacks domain expertise

The approach's applicability beyond mathematics remains an open question.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

The paper's methodology introduces significant computational costs that are not fully quantified.

**LLM-as-a-Judge Annotation Costs**

While consensus filtering reduces training data by 60%, the filtering process itself requires forward passes through Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct for all 860k samples. The paper does not report:

- Total GPU hours for LLM-as-a-judge annotation
- Comparison of annotation costs between pure MC estimation, pure LLM-as-a-judge, and consensus filtering
- Whether the 40% retained data justifies the compute spent annotating the discarded 60%

For practitioners with limited compute budgets, the net cost savings of consensus filtering versus pure LLM-as-a-judge training is unclear. The efficiency gain is in data volume, not necessarily in total compute.

**Training Compute Not Reported**

The paper specifies model architectures (7B and 72B parameter PRMs) but does not report:

- Training duration in GPU hours
- Number of training steps or epochs
- Batch sizes and learning rate schedules
- Total FLOPs consumed

Without this information, practitioners cannot estimate the infrastructure requirements for reproducing or extending this work. The performance gains (e.g., 78.3% vs 56.5% PROCESSBENCH F1) may come with proportional compute increases that are not justified for all use cases.

**Inference-Time Overhead**

Process Reward Models introduce latency during inference that Outcome Reward Models do not:

- ORMs score a complete response in one forward pass
- PRMs must score each reasoning step separately, requiring multiple forward passes or careful token-level extraction

For Best-of-8 evaluation, a PRM must process all 8 candidate responses step-by-step. If a response contains 10 steps on average, this requires approximately 10× more scoring operations than an ORM. The paper does not measure this latency overhead or discuss optimization strategies (e.g., batch scoring, caching).

**Scalability to Larger N Values**

Table 10 evaluates Best-of-64, showing the authors' PRMs maintain superiority. However, the computational cost scales linearly with N:

$$
\text{Total Scoring Operations} = N \times \text{Average Steps per Response}
$$

For N=64 with 10 steps per response, this requires 640 scoring operations per query. At some point, the marginal accuracy gain from larger N will not justify the compute cost. The paper does not identify this inflection point or provide guidance on optimal N selection for different deployment constraints.

### 6.4 Remaining Weaknesses and Open Questions

Several weaknesses persist even in the authors' improved PRMs, and important research questions remain unanswered.

**Performance Gap to Upper Bound**

The Limitations section notes "a considerable performance gap between our PRMs and the BoN upper bound (pass@8)." Table 6 quantifies this gap:

| Metric | Average Accuracy |
| :--- | :--- |
| pass@8 (Upper Bound) | 74.7% |
| Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-72B | 69.3% |
| **Gap** | **5.4 points** |

A 5.4 percentage point gap represents substantial room for improvement. The paper does not analyze where the PRM fails to select the correct response among the 8 candidates:

- Does it mis-score correct responses as low quality?
- Does it fail to distinguish between multiple correct responses?
- Are there systematic problem types where the PRM underperforms?

Understanding these failure modes would guide future improvements.

**Confidence Calibration Unmeasured**

The paper treats PRM scores as ranking signals but does not evaluate calibration quality. A well-calibrated PRM should assign scores that reflect true probabilities:

- Steps scored 0.9 should be correct approximately 90% of the time
- Steps scored 0.1 should be correct approximately 10% of the time

Poor calibration limits PRM utility in:
- Threshold-based filtering (what score cutoff ensures 95% step accuracy?)
- Uncertainty-aware decoding (when should the model request human intervention?)
- RL reward scaling (how should raw scores be normalized for training stability?)

The absence of calibration analysis leaves practitioners without guidance on interpreting PRM scores quantitatively.

**Data Contamination Risk**

The paper notes PRM800K was "deduplicated against the test set" (Section 3.1.2), but does not explicitly state whether the 860k MC-estimated queries overlap with PROCESSBENCH. Given that both datasets draw from similar mathematical problem sources (MATH, GSM8K, etc.), some contamination is possible. If the PRM was trained on problems similar to PROCESSBENCH test cases, the reported 78.3% Mean F1 may overestimate true generalization capability.

**Human Annotation Under-Utilization**

The Limitations section acknowledges that "the efficient utilization of existing high-quality human annotation data is still largely under-explored." The paper compares human annotation (PRM800K) against MC estimation and LLM-as-a-judge but does not explore:

- Combining human annotations with MC/LLM data in a single training run
- Using human data for validation while scaling with automated labels
- Active learning strategies to prioritize which samples deserve human annotation

The suggestion to "gradually expand high-quality datasets through weakly supervised methods" is mentioned but not investigated. This represents a clear direction for future work that the current paper leaves open.

**Process-to-Outcome Shift Not Fully Resolved**

While the authors' PRMs show lower proportions of minimum scores at the final step compared to prior work (Figure 8), they do not eliminate this phenomenon entirely:

| Model | Minimum at Final Step (%) |
| :--- | :--- |
| EurusPRM-Stage1 | 54.6% |
| Math-Shepherd-PRM-7B | 52.9% |
| Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-7B | 18.0% |
| Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-72B | 17.5% |

The 17.5-18.0% rate for the authors' models is substantially better but still indicates some outcome-focused behavior. The paper does not analyze what drives these remaining cases or whether they represent genuine process failures or appropriate final-answer verification.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes the key trade-offs practitioners must navigate when adopting this approach:

| Trade-off | Observation | Practical Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Data Quality vs. Quantity** | 264k human samples outperform 860k MC samples on PROCESSBENCH (56.5% vs 40.1% F1) | Prioritize annotation quality over scale for process verification tasks |
| **BoN vs. PROCESSBENCH Optimization** | MC estimation: 65.9% BoN, 40.1% F1. Human: 64.9% BoN, 56.5% F1 | These objectives can conflict; choose based on deployment needs |
| **PRM Scale vs. Policy Scale** | 7B PRM underperforms maj@8 on 72B policy (69.9% vs 70.2%) | Match PRM capacity to policy model size |
| **Consensus Filtering Efficiency** | 40% data retained matches 100% LLM-as-a-judge performance | Reduces training data but requires full annotation pass |
| **Scoring Strategy** | MC-based: `last` step. Human/LLM-based: `product`/`min` | Must align inference strategy with training data semantics |

These limitations do not invalidate the paper's contributions but define the boundaries within which the approach is effective. Practitioners should adopt the consensus filtering methodology and dual-metric evaluation framework while remaining aware that reinforcement learning integration, cross-domain generalization, and compute-efficient scaling remain open research challenges.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work fundamentally alters the trajectory of Process Reward Model (PRM) research by shifting the focus from scaling data quantity to optimizing data quality and evaluation rigor. The findings necessitate a reevaluation of standard practices in data synthesis, model assessment, and deployment strategies for reasoning supervision. This section outlines how the landscape changes, identifies critical follow-up research, details practical applications, and provides concrete guidance for reproducing and integrating these methods.

### 7.1 Shifting the Field's Priorities

The paper establishes that the prevailing "scale-at-all-costs" mentality for PRM training data is counterproductive when the data source is noisy. The experimental evidence forces three specific shifts in community priorities:

*   **From Quantity to Precision:** Prior work assumed that increasing the volume of Monte Carlo (MC) estimated data would linearly improve performance. This paper demonstrates that 860k MC-estimated samples yield lower process verification capability (40.1% Mean F1 on PROCESSBENCH) than 264k human-annotated samples (56.5% Mean F1), as shown in **Table 4**. The implication is clear: **data precision outweighs data volume** for process supervision. Future datasets should prioritize verification accuracy over sheer sample count.
*   **From Single-Metric to Dual-Metric Evaluation:** The field has predominantly optimized for Best-of-N (BoN) accuracy (`prm@N`). This work proves that BoN alone induces a **process-to-outcome shift**, where PRMs degenerate into Outcome Reward Models (ORMs). **Figure 8** reveals that existing PRMs concentrate over 40% of minimum scores at the final step, indicating they ignore intermediate reasoning. The new standard must require **dual-metric evaluation**: BoN for utility and step-level benchmarks like PROCESSBENCH for capability verification.
*   **From Conflated to Distinct Objectives:** The paper clarifies the conceptual boundary between **Process Reward Models** (verifying current step correctness) and **Value Models** (estimating future success probability). MC estimation inherently trains Value Models, not PRMs. Future research must explicitly declare which objective is being optimized, as the data construction methods (MC vs. Human/LLM) are mutually incompatible for these distinct goals.

### 7.2 Critical Avenues for Follow-up Research

The paper identifies several high-impact areas where current knowledge is insufficient, providing a roadmap for next-generation reasoning systems.

*   **Process-Supervised Reinforcement Learning (RL):** The Limitations section explicitly states that "best practices for utilizing PRMs in reinforcement learning remain unexplored." All results in this paper use BoN selection, a post-hoc filtering mechanism. Integrating PRMs into RL loops (e.g., PPO, DPO) introduces unique challenges:
    *   **Credit Assignment:** How to distribute reward signals across steps when only the final outcome is known?
    *   **Gradient Stability:** Step-level rewards may introduce higher variance gradients than outcome-level rewards.
    *   **Compute Cost:** RL requires multiple forward passes per training step; step-level scoring amplifies this cost.
    Follow-up work must establish stable training protocols for process-supervised RL, potentially leveraging the consensus-filtered data to reduce noise in reward signals.

*   **Judge Model Efficiency:** The consensus filtering mechanism relies on Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct as the judge, which is computationally expensive. **Figure 2** shows that filtering retains only ~40% of data, but the annotation cost applies to 100% of samples. Research is needed to determine if smaller judge models (e.g., 7B or 32B) can achieve similar filtering effectiveness. If a 7B judge can identify the same noisy samples as a 72B judge, the net compute cost of consensus filtering could decrease by an order of magnitude.

*   **Confidence Calibration:** The paper treats PRM scores as ranking signals but does not evaluate calibration quality. A well-calibrated PRM should satisfy:
    $$
    P(\text{Step Correct} \mid \text{Score} = s) \approx s
    $$
    Poor calibration limits utility in threshold-based filtering (e.g., "reject any step with score $&lt; 0.5$") and uncertainty-aware decoding. Future work should measure calibration error (e.g., Expected Calibration Error) and explore loss functions that encourage probability matching rather than just ranking accuracy.

*   **Cross-Domain Generalization:** All experiments focus on mathematical reasoning where golden answers are easily verifiable. Extending consensus filtering to domains like code generation or scientific reasoning requires adapting the MC estimation component. For code, "correctness" might mean passing unit tests; for science, it might mean consistency with known facts. Research must define robust verification mechanisms for domains without closed-form solutions.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The improved process verification capabilities of the released PRMs (`Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-7B/72B`) enable specific downstream applications where reasoning transparency is critical.

*   **Automated Tutoring Systems:** In educational contexts, identifying *where* a student (or model) went wrong is more valuable than knowing *that* they got it wrong. The PRMs achieve 78.3% Mean F1 on PROCESSBENCH (**Table 7**), substantially higher than prior open-source alternatives (e.g., 56.5% for PRM800K). This capability allows tutoring systems to provide targeted feedback on specific erroneous steps rather than generic "incorrect" messages.
*   **Step-Wise Search Algorithms:** While BoN is a parallel search method, PRMs are theoretically better suited for sequential search algorithms like Depth-First Search (DFS) or Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). **Table 8** (Appendix A) shows PRM-guided greedy search achieves 66.6% average accuracy, slightly superior to ORM-based BoN (66.5%). For complex problems where generating 64 candidates (BoN-64) is too expensive, step-wise search allows early pruning of incorrect trajectories, potentially reducing inference compute.
*   **High-Stakes Verification:** In domains like legal or medical reasoning, hallucinated steps can have severe consequences even if the final conclusion appears plausible. The PRMs' ability to detect correct-answer/flawed-process cases (58.1% accuracy for 72B model, **Table 5**) provides a safety layer that ORMs cannot offer. Systems can be configured to flag responses where the final answer is correct but the process score is low for human review.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

Practitioners seeking to adopt these methods should follow specific guidelines to avoid common pitfalls identified in the paper. The following decision framework outlines when to prefer this approach over alternatives.

*   **When to Use Consensus Filtering:**
    *   **Preferred:** When you have access to a large LLM (e.g., 70B+) for judging and need high-quality process labels without human annotation costs. **Figure 2** shows this method matches LLM-as-a-judge performance using only 40% of the data.
    *   **Avoid:** If compute budget is extremely constrained. The filtering process requires annotating 100% of samples to discard 60%. If you cannot afford the initial annotation pass, pure human annotation (higher quality per sample) or pure MC estimation (lower quality but cheaper) may be more appropriate.

*   **When to Use PRMs vs. ORMs:**
    *   **Use PRMs:** When you need step-level interpretability, are implementing step-wise search (DFS/MCTS), or need to detect flawed reasoning in correct answers.
    *   **Use ORMs:** When inference latency is the primary constraint. PRMs require scoring each step, which increases inference time proportional to reasoning length. ORMs score the entire response in one pass. **Table 6** shows PRM-72B (69.3%) slightly edges ORM-72B (68.9%) on BoN, but the latency trade-off must be weighed.

*   **Scoring Strategy Selection:**
    *   **For Human/LLM-Annotated PRMs:** Use `product` or `minimum` scoring. These labels represent deterministic step correctness, so a solution is valid only if *all* steps are valid. **Figure 9** shows `product` scoring achieves 69.3% BoN for Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-72B, outperforming `last` step scoring (68.5%).
    *   **For MC-Estimated PRMs:** Use `last` step scoring. MC estimates future success probability, so the final step's score integrates information from the entire trajectory. Multiplying probabilities compounds estimation errors. **Figure 9** shows MC-based models perform best with `last` scoring (e.g., 65.5% vs 64.2% for product).

*   **Label Thresholding:**
    *   If using MC estimation, set the negative label threshold to **0**. **Figure 5** demonstrates that performance deteriorates as the threshold increases from 0/8 to 7/8. A step should be considered correct if *any* completion starting from that step reaches the correct final answer.
    *   Prefer **hard labels** over soft labels after filtering. **Figures 3-4** show hard labels achieve 67.2% BoN and 66.5% F1 after filtering, versus 65.4% BoN and 49.3% F1 for soft labels. Soft labels introduce noise when step correctness should be deterministic.

*   **Model Scaling:**
    *   Match PRM capacity to policy model size. **Table 9** (Appendix B.1) shows a 7B PRM underperforms majority voting (69.9% vs 70.2%) when supervising a 72B policy. For large policy models, use large PRMs (e.g., 72B) to ensure the verifier has sufficient capacity to detect subtle errors in the policy's reasoning.

By adhering to these guidelines, practitioners can avoid the optimization biases and data quality pitfalls that have hindered prior PRM development. The released models and methodologies provide a robust foundation for building reasoning systems that are not only accurate but also verifiable and trustworthy.
