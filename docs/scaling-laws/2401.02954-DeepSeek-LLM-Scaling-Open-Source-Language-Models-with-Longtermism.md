## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the varying conclusions in prior LLM scaling laws by establishing that data quality dictates optimal model/data allocation, a finding used to guide the pre-training of the DeepSeek LLM series (7B and 67B) on 2 trillion tokens. The resulting DeepSeek 67B model surpasses LLaMA-2 70B across benchmarks like code and mathematics, while the Chat variant outperforms GPT-3.5 in open-ended evaluations, demonstrating that open-source models can achieve state-of-the-art performance through rigorous scaling analysis.

## 2. Context and Motivation

The development of Large Language Models (LLMs) has rapidly transitioned from research curiosities to foundational technologies for Artificial General Intelligence (AGI). However, the path to scaling these models efficiently remains obscured by inconsistent theoretical guidance. This paper situates itself at the intersection of open-source accessibility and rigorous scaling science, aiming to resolve ambiguities that hinder systematic progress.

### The Core Problem: Inconsistent Scaling Guidance
The primary gap this paper addresses is the lack of consensus on **scaling laws**—empirical relationships that predict how model performance improves as computational resources increase. While the open-source community has successfully produced fixed-size models (e.g., 7B, 70B parameters), it has largely neglected the underlying principles governing how to scale them effectively.

The authors identify a critical inconsistency in prior literature. Early foundational works on scaling laws, such as Kaplan et al. (2020) and Hoffmann et al. (2022), reached **varying conclusions** regarding the optimal allocation of compute budget between model size and data volume (Abstract). Specifically, there is disagreement on the exponents that dictate how model parameters ($N$) and data tokens ($D$) should grow relative to the total compute budget ($C$). This inconsistency casts a "dark cloud over scaling LLMs," making it difficult for researchers to know whether to invest resources in larger models or larger datasets (Abstract).

Furthermore, prior studies inadequately addressed **hyperparameter scaling**. The optimal settings for training dynamics, such as batch size and learning rate, are not static; they shift as the compute budget changes. Previous works often lacked complete descriptions of these settings, leaving uncertainty about whether reported performances were truly optimal or artifacts of suboptimal tuning (Section 3).

### Why This Problem Matters
Resolving these ambiguities is vital for both theoretical and practical reasons:

*   **Resource Efficiency:** Training large models requires massive computational budgets. Without accurate scaling laws, researchers risk wasting compute on suboptimal model/data configurations. The paper notes that current open-source models are merely at the "initial stage of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) development," implying that efficient scaling is necessary to reach higher capabilities (Section 1).
*   **Predictability:** Reliable scaling laws allow researchers to predict the performance of larger models before training them. This reduces the risk associated with investing in massive training runs. The authors aim to lay the groundwork for future scaling by providing a framework that accurately forecasts performance (Section 1).
*   **Open-Source Competitiveness:** Closed-source products like ChatGPT and Claude are developed with extensive resources. For open-source initiatives to compete, they must maximize the utility of every unit of compute. Neglecting scaling research in favor of simply training fixed-size models limits the potential of the open-source ecosystem (Section 1).

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
To understand the contribution of this work, it is necessary to examine where previous methodologies fell short. The paper highlights three specific limitations in existing scaling law research:

1.  **Imprecise Model Scale Representation:**
    Prior works typically represented model scale using parameter counts, either non-embedding parameters ($N_1$) or complete parameters ($N_2$). The compute budget was approximated as $C = 6ND$. However, the authors argue this introduces significant approximation errors.
    *   $6N_1$ ignores the computational overhead of attention operations.
    *   $6N_2$ includes vocabulary computation, which contributes less to model capacity.
    These inaccuracies can lead to statistical errors when fitting scaling curves, particularly for small-scale models where discrepancies can reach up to 50% (Section 3.2, Table 3).

2.  **Neglect of Data Quality Impact:**
    Existing scaling laws often assume a uniform data distribution. DeepSeek's investigation reveals that **data quality significantly influences the optimal scaling strategy**. The paper finds that as data quality improves, the compute budget should be allocated more towards model scaling rather than data scaling. This finding suggests that previous varying conclusions might stem from differences in dataset quality rather than fundamental contradictions in physics-like scaling laws (Section 3.3, Table 4).

3.  **Static Hyperparameter Assumptions:**
    Early empirical observations for setting batch size and learning rate were found to have limited applicability when scaling compute budgets. The paper notes that optimal hyperparameters are not constant; for instance, the optimal batch size $B$ increases while the optimal learning rate $\eta$ decreases as compute budget $C$ grows. Failing to adjust these dynamically prevents models from reaching near-optimal performance at different scales (Section 3.1).

### Positioning of DeepSeek LLM
Relative to existing work, this paper positions itself as a comprehensive recalibration of scaling principles specifically tailored for open-source development with a "long-term perspective" (Abstract).

*   **Methodological Rigor:** Instead of accepting existing scaling exponents, the authors re-derive scaling laws using a more precise model scale representation: **non-embedding FLOPs/token** ($M$). This metric accounts for attention overhead while excluding vocabulary computation, leading to a compute budget formula of $C = MD$ rather than the approximate $C = 6ND$ (Section 3.2).
*   **Hyperparameter Integration:** The project integrates hyperparameter scaling directly into the scaling law framework. They establish power law relationships between compute budget and optimal batch size/learning rate, ensuring that models at different scales are compared at their respective optima (Section 3.1).
*   **Empirical Validation:** The findings are not purely theoretical. They are applied to train two specific model configurations, 7B and 67B parameters, on a dataset of 2 trillion tokens (Abstract, Section 1). This allows the authors to validate that small-scale experiments can accurately predict the performance of models with 1000× the compute budget (Section 3.2, Figure 5).
*   **Transparency:** Unlike closed-source efforts, DeepSeek LLM aims to release as much information as possible, including scaling insights, to facilitate community reference and future advancements (Section 1).

By addressing the inconsistencies in prior literature and refining the metrics used to measure scale, this work seeks to transform scaling laws from a source of confusion into a reliable engineering tool for the open-source community.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents an empirical engineering study that recalibrates scaling laws for open-source LLMs and applies those findings to build the DeepSeek LLM family (7B and 67B parameters) from scratch. The core idea is that data quality fundamentally changes how compute should be allocated between model size and training data, and that hyperparameters must scale dynamically with compute budget rather than remain fixed.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a family of open-source large language models trained from scratch on 2 trillion tokens using scaling laws that account for data quality and dynamic hyperparameter tuning. This solves the problem of inconsistent guidance in prior scaling research by establishing that higher-quality data warrants allocating more compute to model size rather than data volume, while also providing formulae to select optimal batch size and learning rate for any compute budget.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The DeepSeek LLM development pipeline consists of four major components connected in sequence. First, the **Data Pipeline** collects, deduplicates, filters, and remixes raw text into a 2-trillion-token bilingual corpus (Chinese and English), producing tokenized sequences ready for training. Second, the **Pre-training Engine** consumes these tokens using the HAI-LLM framework with parallelism strategies (data, tensor, sequence, pipeline) and trains transformer models with specific architectures (7B: 30 layers, 67B: 95 layers with Grouped-Query Attention). Third, the **Scaling Law Analyzer** runs IsoFLOP experiments across compute budgets from 1e17 to 3e20 FLOPs to fit power-law relationships between compute, model scale, data scale, and hyperparameters, outputting optimal configuration formulae. Fourth, the **Alignment Pipeline** applies supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on 1.5 million instruction instances followed by direct preference optimization (DPO) to convert base models into chat-capable models. Information flows unidirectionally: raw data enters the pipeline, scaling laws guide pre-training configuration, and alignment transforms base models into deployable chat models.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the data pipeline because model performance fundamentally depends on training data quality, and the paper's key insight is that data quality affects optimal scaling strategy.
- We then examine model architecture to understand what is being scaled, including the specific design choices that differentiate DeepSeek from LLaMA.
- We proceed to hyperparameter selection and scaling laws, which represent the paper's primary scientific contribution and explain how configuration changes with compute budget.
- We cover the infrastructure layer to explain how 2-trillion-token training is made computationally feasible.
- We conclude with the alignment pipeline to show how base models are transformed into chat models through SFT and DPO.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Framing statement:** This is an empirical systems paper that combines scaling law research with full-stack model development, where the scientific contribution (recalibrated scaling laws) directly informs the engineering output (7B and 67B parameter models).

**Data Pipeline: Three-Stage Processing for Quality and Diversity**

The data pipeline operates in three sequential stages: deduplication, filtering, and remixing, each serving a distinct purpose in enhancing dataset quality. Deduplication removes duplicate documents to prevent the model from overfitting to repeated content, and the authors employ an aggressive strategy that spans across multiple Common Crawl dumps rather than within individual dumps. Table 1 shows that deduplicating across 91 dumps eliminates 89.8% of duplicate documents, which is four times more effective than deduplicating within a single dump (22.2% removal rate). Filtering applies linguistic and semantic quality criteria to assess document quality from both individual and global perspectives, increasing the information density of the training corpus. Remixing adjusts the proportions of different data domains to address imbalances, ensuring underrepresented domains receive adequate presence in the final dataset. The tokenizer uses Byte-level Byte-Pair Encoding (BBPE) from the tokenizers library, with pre-tokenization to prevent merging tokens across different character categories such as newlines, punctuation, and Chinese-Japanese-Korean symbols. Numbers are split into individual digits following LLaMA's approach, and the vocabulary contains 100,000 conventional tokens plus 15 special tokens for a total of 100,015, though the model's vocabulary size is configured to 102,400 to reserve space for future special tokens and ensure computational efficiency. The tokenizer was trained on a multilingual corpus of approximately 24 GB, and the final pre-training dataset consists of 2 trillion tokens primarily in Chinese and English.

**Model Architecture: LLaMA-Inspired Design with Strategic Modifications**

The DeepSeek LLM architecture largely follows LLaMA's micro design but introduces macro-level modifications optimized for training and inference efficiency. Both the 7B and 67B models use a Pre-Norm structure with RMSNorm normalization function, SwiGLU activation in the Feed-Forward Network with intermediate layer dimension of $\frac{8}{3}d_{model}$, and Rotary Embedding for positional encoding. Table 2 provides the complete specifications: the 7B model has 30 layers, model dimension $d_{model} = 4096$, 32 attention heads, 32 key-value heads (standard Multi-Head Attention), context length 4096, sequence batch size 2304, and learning rate 4.2e-4. The 67B model has 95 layers, $d_{model} = 8192$, 64 attention heads, but only 8 key-value heads using Grouped-Query Attention (GQA) instead of traditional Multi-Head Attention to optimize inference cost. The layer count adjustments (30 for 7B, 95 for 67B) maintain parameter consistency with other open-source models while facilitating model pipeline partitioning for optimized training and inference. Unlike most works using GQA that widen the intermediate width of FFN layers, DeepSeek expands the 67B model's parameters in network depth, aiming for better performance. Both models are trained on 2 trillion tokens with context length 4096, and the 67B model's GQA configuration (64 query heads, 8 key-value heads) means each key-value head is shared among 8 query heads, reducing memory bandwidth requirements during inference.

**Hyperparameter Selection: Multi-Step Scheduler and Dynamic Scaling**

The hyperparameter configuration deviates from common practice in the learning rate scheduler while maintaining standard optimizer settings. DeepSeek LLM is initialized with standard deviation 0.006 and trained using AdamW optimizer with $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.95$, and weight_decay = 0.1. Instead of the typical cosine learning rate scheduler, a multi-step learning rate scheduler is employed where the learning rate reaches its maximum value after 2000 warmup steps, then decreases to 31.6% of maximum after processing 80% of training tokens, and further reduces to 10% of maximum after 90% of tokens. Figure 1(a) demonstrates that despite differences in loss reduction trends during training, the final performance using multi-step scheduler is essentially consistent with cosine scheduler. The multi-step scheduler offers unique convenience for continual training because when adjusting training scale while keeping model size fixed, training from the first phase can be reused. Figure 1(b) shows that adjusting the proportions of different stages can yield slightly better performance, but the authors chose 80%, 10%, 10% distribution to balance reuse ratios in continual training and model performance. Gradient clipping is set to 1.0 during training, and batch size and learning rate vary with model size according to the scaling laws derived in Section 3.1.

**Scaling Laws for Hyperparameters: Power-Law Relationships with Compute Budget**

The paper establishes that optimal hyperparameters are not static but scale predictably with compute budget, addressing a gap in prior scaling law research. The authors first conducted grid search experiments for batch size and learning rate on small-scale experiments with compute budget 1e17 FLOPs, and Figure 2(a) shows that generalization error remains stable across a wide range of batch sizes and learning rates, indicating near-optimal performance can be achieved within a relatively wide parameter space. Models whose generalization error exceeded the minimum by no more than 0.25% were regarded as having near-optimal hyperparameters, and these parameters were fitted with respect to compute budget $C$. Figure 3 reveals that optimal batch size $B$ gradually increases with compute budget $C$, while optimal learning rate $\eta$ gradually decreases, which aligns with intuitive empirical settings for scaling up models. The final fitted formulae are:

$$\eta_{opt} = 0.3118 \cdot C^{-0.1250}$$

$$B_{opt} = 0.2920 \cdot C^{0.3271}$$

These equations mean that for every increase in compute budget, the learning rate should decrease by a power of -0.1250 while batch size should increase by a power of 0.3271. The authors validated these formulae on models with 1e20 compute budget, and Figure 2(b) shows the fitted parameters are centered in the optimal parameter space. However, the paper notes an important limitation: factors beyond compute budget $C$ may affect optimal hyperparameters, and models with the same compute budget but different model/data allocations show slightly varying optimal parameter spaces, suggesting further research is needed.

**Scaling Laws for Model and Data: Non-Embedding FLOPs as Scale Metric**

The paper's most significant methodological contribution is introducing non-embedding FLOPs per token ($M$) as a more accurate model scale representation than parameter counts. Prior works used either non-embedding parameters $N_1$ or complete parameters $N_2$, with compute budget approximated as $C = 6ND$. However, $6N_1$ ignores attention operation computational overhead, and $6N_2$ includes vocabulary computation which contributes less to model capacity. The new representation $M$ includes attention overhead but excludes vocabulary computation, with compute budget expressed as $C = MD$. The specific formulae are:

$$6N_1 = 72 \cdot n_{layer} \cdot d_{model}^2$$

$$6N_2 = 72 \cdot n_{layer} \cdot d_{model}^2 + 6 \cdot n_{vocab} \cdot d_{model}$$

$$M = 72 \cdot n_{layer} \cdot d_{model}^2 + 12 \cdot n_{layer} \cdot d_{model} \cdot l_{seq}$$

where $n_{layer}$ is number of layers, $d_{model}$ is model width, $n_{vocab}$ is vocabulary size, and $l_{seq}$ is sequence length. Table 3 shows that both $6N_1$ and $6N_2$ either overestimate or underestimate computational cost in models of different scales, with discrepancies reaching up to 50% in small-scale models. Using the IsoFLOP profile approach from Chinchilla, the authors selected 8 different compute budgets ranging from 1e17 to 3e20 FLOPs and designed around 10 different model/data scale allocations for each budget. Figure 4 demonstrates the IsoFLOP curve and model/data scaling curves fitted using optimal model/data allocation for each compute budget. The specific formulae for optimal model scale $M_{opt}$ and optimal tokens $D_{opt}$ are:

$$M_{opt} = M_{base} \cdot C^a, \quad M_{base} = 0.1715, \quad a = 0.5243$$

$$D_{opt} = D_{base} \cdot C^b, \quad D_{base} = 5.8316, \quad b = 0.4757$$

These exponents indicate that as compute budget increases, approximately 52.43% should be allocated to model scaling and 47.57% to data scaling for the current high-quality dataset. Figure 5 shows that small-scale experiments can accurately predict the performance of models with 1000× compute budget, providing confidence for training larger models.

**Data Quality Impact on Scaling: The Key Discovery**

The paper discovers that data quality fundamentally changes the optimal model/data scaling allocation strategy, which may explain varying conclusions in prior scaling law research. The authors studied scaling laws using three different datasets: early in-house data, current in-house data, and OpenWebText2 (used in Kaplan et al., 2020). Internal assessment revealed that current in-house data has higher quality than early in-house data, and OpenWebText2 quality surpasses current in-house data due to smaller scale allowing more meticulous processing. Table 4 shows the scaling exponents vary systematically with data quality:

| Dataset | Model exponent $a$ | Data exponent $b$ |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| OpenAI (OpenWebText2) | 0.73 | 0.27 |
| Chinchilla (MassiveText) | 0.49 | 0.51 |
| DeepSeek (Early Data) | 0.450 | 0.550 |
| DeepSeek (Current Data) | 0.524 | 0.476 |
| DeepSeek (OpenWebText2) | 0.578 | 0.422 |

As data quality improves, the model scaling exponent $a$ increases while data scaling exponent $b$ decreases, suggesting increased compute budget should be allocated more to model size rather than data volume. The intuitive explanation is that high-quality data implies logical clarity and less predictive difficulty after sufficient training, making it more advantageous to scale up model size when increasing compute budget. This finding suggests caution should be exercised when generalizing scaling laws across datasets, as dataset choice remarkably affects scaling behavior.

**Infrastructure: Parallelism and Efficiency Optimizations**

The training framework HAI-LLM integrates multiple parallelism strategies to make 2-trillion-token training computationally feasible. Data parallelism, tensor parallelism, sequence parallelism, and 1F1B pipeline parallelism are integrated following Megatron-LM approaches. Flash Attention technique is leveraged to improve hardware utilization, and ZeRO-1 is exploited to partition optimizer states over data parallel ranks. Computation and communication are overlapped to minimize waiting overhead, including the backward procedure of the last micro-batch and reduce-scatter operation in ZeRO-1, and GEMM computation with all-gather/reduce-scatter in sequence parallel. Some layers and operators are fused to speed up training, including LayerNorm, GEMM whenever possible, and Adam updates. The model is trained in bf16 precision but accumulates gradients in fp32 precision for training stability. In-place cross-entropy is performed to reduce GPU memory consumption by converting bf16 logits to fp32 precision on the fly in the cross-entropy CUDA kernel rather than beforehand in HBM. Model weights and optimizer states are saved every 5 minutes asynchronously, meaning no more than 5 minutes of training is lost in case of hardware or network failures. The system supports resuming training from a different 3D parallel configuration to cope with dynamic changes in computing cluster load. For evaluation, vLLM is employed in generative tasks, and continuous batching is used in non-generative tasks to avoid manual batch size tuning and reduce token padding.

**Alignment Pipeline: Two-Stage Fine-Tuning and DPO**

The alignment pipeline transforms base models into chat models through supervised fine-tuning followed by direct preference optimization. Approximately 1.5 million instruction data instances were collected in English and Chinese, covering helpfulness and harmlessness topics. The helpful data contains 1.2 million instances with distribution: 31.2% general language tasks, 46.6% mathematical problems, and 22.2% coding exercises. Safety data consists of 300,000 instances covering various sensitive topics. For supervised fine-tuning, the 7B model is fine-tuned for 4 epochs but the 67B model only for 2 epochs because overfitting is more serious on the larger model. Learning rate is 1e-5 for 7B and 5e-6 for 67B models. The authors observed that GSM8K and HumanEval scores improve consistently for the 7B model during SFT, while the 67B model hits the upper bound sooner. A repetition ratio metric was monitored using 3,868 Chinese and English prompts to determine the proportion of generated responses that fail to terminate and endlessly repeat text sequences. The repetition ratio tends to rise as math SFT data quantity increases because math SFT data occasionally includes similar reasoning patterns that weaker models struggle to grasp. To address this, two-stage fine-tuning was implemented for the 7B model: stage-1 uses all data, and stage-2 excludes math and code data. Table 12 shows this reduces repetition ratio from 2.0% to 1.4% while maintaining benchmark scores. For the 67B model, repetition ratio is already below 1% after stage-1, and stage-2 hurts benchmark scores, so only one stage of SFT is performed. Direct Preference Optimization is then applied for 1 epoch with learning rate 5e-6 and batch size 512, using learning rate warmup and cosine scheduler. DPO strengthens the model's open-ended generation skill while engendering little difference in standard benchmark performance, as shown in Table 17.

**System Prompt Design and Model Size Effects**

The system prompt is slightly modified from LLaMA-2's introduction to guide the model toward helpful, respectful, and honest responses. The prompt states: "You are DeepSeek Chat, a helpful, respectful and honest AI assistant developed by DeepSeek. The knowledge cut-off date for your training data is up to May 2023." Table 14 reveals an intriguing phenomenon: the 7B model experiences slight performance degradation when the system prompt is introduced (MT Bench score drops from 7.15 to 7.11), while the 67B model shows significant improvement (score increases from 8.35 to 8.58). The explanation is that larger models possess better understanding of the intended meaning behind system prompts, enabling them to follow instructions more effectively, while smaller models struggle to grasp the system prompt adequately and the inconsistency between training and testing negatively impacts their performance.

**Evaluation Methodology: Three-Tier Assessment Strategy**

The evaluation framework employs three complementary assessment strategies to comprehensively measure model capabilities. Public benchmark evaluation uses perplexity-based methods for multiple-choice datasets (calculating perplexity of each option and selecting the lowest) and generation-based methods for open-ended tasks (using greedy decoding). Held-out evaluation uses recently published test sets to assess data contamination and benchmark overfitting, including LeetCode Weekly Contest problems (126 problems from July to November 2023), Hungarian National High-School Exam (33 problems), and Google's instruction following evaluation dataset (25 types of verifiable instructions, approximately 500 prompts). Open-ended evaluation uses AlignBench for Chinese (8 primary categories, 36 secondary categories, 683 questions, rated by GPT-4) and MT-Bench for English (8 categories of multi-turn questions). Safety evaluation uses a 2400-question test set constructed by a 20-person expert team across 5 major categories and multiple subcategories, with both the "Do-Not-Answer" dataset and custom safety taxonomy used for assessment.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper contributes more than just a new set of model weights; it offers a recalibrated framework for understanding how large language models scale. While some architectural choices (such as using SwiGLU or Rotary Embeddings) are incremental improvements adopting established best practices, the core innovations lie in the **empirical refinement of scaling laws** and the **integration of data quality into scaling theory**. Below, we distinguish between the fundamental conceptual shifts and the engineering validations that make this work significant for the broader research community.

### 4.1 Data Quality as a Fundamental Scaling Variable
**Type:** Fundamental Innovation

Prior scaling law literature (e.g., Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022) often treated scaling exponents as near-universal constants, implying that the optimal balance between model size and data volume was intrinsic to the transformer architecture itself. DeepSeek challenges this assumption by demonstrating that **data quality modulates the scaling exponents**.

*   **The Insight:** The optimal allocation of compute budget is not static; it shifts based on the information density of the training corpus. As shown in **Table 4** of the paper, the model scaling exponent $a$ increases from $0.450$ (Early Data) to $0.524$ (Current Data) and up to $0.578$ (OpenWebText2) as data quality improves. Conversely, the data scaling exponent $b$ decreases.
*   **Why It Matters:** This finding resolves the "varying conclusions" observed in prior literature (Abstract). It suggests that conflicting scaling laws in previous works were not necessarily contradictions in physics-like laws, but rather artifacts of differing dataset qualities.
*   **Implication for Researchers:** Future scaling studies cannot simply fit curves on arbitrary datasets and claim universal applicability. Researchers must report data quality metrics alongside scaling exponents. For practitioners, this implies that improving data quality justifies investing more compute into larger models rather than simply training on more tokens.

### 4.2 Computational Fidelity: FLOPs/Token vs. Parameter Count
**Type:** Methodological Correction

A subtle but critical source of error in prior scaling research was the representation of model scale. Previous works predominantly used parameter counts ($N$), approximating compute budget as $C \approx 6ND$. DeepSeek identifies this as a significant source of statistical noise, particularly for smaller models.

*   **The Insight:** By adopting **non-embedding FLOPs per token** ($M$) as the scale metric, the authors eliminate approximation errors related to attention overhead and vocabulary computation. **Table 3** highlights that using parameter-based estimates ($6N_1$ or $6N_2$) can misestimate computational cost by up to 50% in small-scale models compared to the $M$ metric.
*   **Why It Matters:** This correction stabilizes the fitting of scaling curves. When the x-axis (compute budget) is noisy, the resulting power laws are unreliable. By ensuring the compute budget $C = MD$ is accurately measured, the resulting predictions for larger models become statistically robust.
*   **Implication for Researchers:** This establishes a new standard for reporting scale. Comparing models solely by parameter count is insufficient for scaling law analysis; computational cost per token must be accounted for to ensure fair IsoFLOP comparisons across different architectures (e.g., models with vs. without Grouped-Query Attention).

### 4.3 Dynamic Hyperparameter Scaling Laws
**Type:** Fundamental Innovation

While many scaling law studies focus on model and data size, they often treat hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size) as static or heuristically chosen. DeepSeek formalizes the relationship between compute budget and optimal hyperparameters.

*   **The Insight:** Optimal hyperparameters are not fixed; they follow power laws relative to the compute budget $C$. As illustrated in **Figure 3**, the optimal batch size $B$ increases while the optimal learning rate $\eta$ decreases as compute grows. The paper provides explicit formulae (referenced in Section 3.1) to calculate these values for any budget.
*   **Why It Matters:** This prevents suboptimal training configurations when scaling up. If a researcher scales a model from 1B to 10B parameters but keeps the learning rate constant, they may fail to reach the true performance frontier, leading to incorrect conclusions about the model's scaling behavior.
*   **Implication for Researchers:** This transforms hyperparameter tuning from a grid-search burden into a predictable calculation. It ensures that performance comparisons across different model sizes are fair, as each model is operating at its respective optimal training dynamics.

### 4.4 Predictive Validity Across Three Orders of Magnitude
**Type:** Engineering Validation

Many scaling law papers fit curves to small models and theorize about large ones without always validating the prediction on the actual large model. DeepSeek provides a rare end-to-end validation of scaling theory.

*   **The Insight:** The scaling laws derived from small-scale experiments (compute budgets around 1e17 FLOPs) accurately predicted the performance of the 67B model (compute budget ~1000× larger). **Figure 5** shows the actual performance of the DeepSeek 7B and 67B models (blue stars) aligning closely with the predicted scaling curve (dotted line).
*   **Why It Matters:** This validates scaling laws as a practical **engineering tool** rather than just a theoretical observation. It demonstrates that researchers can confidently plan massive training runs based on small-scale proxies, significantly reducing financial and computational risk.
*   **Implication for Practitioners:** Open-source groups with limited resources can use these laws to estimate the performance of a target model size before committing to training. It shifts the paradigm from "train and see" to "predict and verify."

### 4.5 Distinction Between Incremental and Fundamental Contributions
To fully appreciate the paper's value, it is necessary to distinguish between what is novel versus what is best-practice engineering:

*   **Incremental Improvements:**
    *   **Architecture:** The use of Pre-Norm, RMSNorm, SwiGLU, and Rotary Embeddings follows the LLaMA design (**Section 2.2**). These are proven choices but not novel contributions of this work.
    *   **Alignment:** The use of SFT and DPO follows standard industry practices (**Section 4**). The specific finding that system prompts help 67B models but hurt 7B models (**Table 14**) is a useful operational detail, but not a fundamental theoretical shift.
    *   **Infrastructure:** The use of Flash Attention, ZeRO-1, and pipeline parallelism (**Section 2.4**) represents standard high-performance computing optimization.

*   **Fundamental Innovations:**
    *   **Data-Dependent Scaling:** The discovery that scaling exponents $a$ and $b$ are functions of data quality (**Section 3.3**).
    *   **Metric Correction:** The shift from parameter count to non-embedding FLOPs/token for scaling analysis (**Section 3.2**).
    *   **Hyperparameter Power Laws:** The explicit derivation of batch size and learning rate as functions of compute budget (**Section 3.1**).

By separating these contributions, we see that DeepSeek LLM's primary legacy lies not in the specific weights released, but in the **methodological rigor applied to the scaling process**. It provides a blueprint for how open-source initiatives can systematically compete with closed-source labs: not by copying architectures, but by optimizing the fundamental relationship between data, compute, and model size.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the DeepSeek team validated their scaling law findings through comprehensive evaluation. The evaluation strategy is notably rigorous, employing four distinct assessment tiers that test different aspects of model capability. Understanding this methodology is crucial because it reveals how the authors separate genuine capability improvements from benchmark overfitting.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: A Four-Tier Assessment Framework

The DeepSeek team employs a layered evaluation approach designed to capture both standardized benchmark performance and real-world utility. Each tier serves a distinct purpose in validating the model's capabilities.

**Tier 1: Public Benchmark Evaluation**

The first tier uses established academic benchmarks across English and Chinese languages. The evaluation framework distinguishes between three evaluation methods based on task type:

*   **Perplexity-based evaluation** for multiple-choice datasets (MMLU, C-Eval, CMMLU, HellaSwag, PIQA, ARC, OpenBookQA, CHID, C3, CCPM). This method calculates the perplexity of each option and selects the lowest as the model prediction. For ARC and OpenBookQA, unconditional normalization is applied; other datasets use length normalization.
*   **Generation-based evaluation** for open-ended tasks (TriviaQA, NaturalQuestions, DROP, MATH, GSM8K, HumanEval, MBPP, BBH, AGIEval, CLUEWSC, CMath). Models generate free text using greedy decoding, and results are parsed from the output.
*   **Language-modeling-based evaluation** for Pile-test, calculating bits-per-byte on the test corpus.

Maximum sequence length is set to 2048 or 4096 tokens depending on the benchmark (Section 5.1, Appendix A.6).

**Tier 2: Open-Ended Evaluation**

Recognizing that benchmark scores do not fully capture conversational quality, the authors employ two specialized benchmarks:

*   **AlignBench** for Chinese evaluation: Contains 8 primary categories, 36 secondary categories, and 683 questions. GPT-4 rates responses using professional reference answers and rating templates. Temperature is set to 0.7 for role-playing, writing, and open-ended questions; 0.1 for other tasks (Section 5.2.1).
*   **MT-Bench** for English evaluation: Contains 8 categories of multi-turn questions. This benchmark is particularly valuable because it tests multi-turn conversation coherence rather than single-turn accuracy (Section 5.2.2).

**Tier 3: Held-Out Evaluation**

To address data contamination and benchmark overfitting concerns, the team uses recently published test sets that models could not have encountered during training:

*   **LeetCode Weekly Contest**: 126 problems from Weekly Contest 351-372 and Bi-Weekly Contest 108-117 (July to November 2023), with over 20 test cases per problem. Pass@1 score is used as the metric (Section 5.3).
*   **Hungarian National High-School Exam**: 33 mathematics problems scored through human annotation following the official solution.pdf metric (Section 5.3).
*   **IFEval (Instruction Following Evaluation)**: Google's dataset with 25 types of verifiable instructions across approximately 500 prompts. Prompt-level loose metric is used (Section 5.3).

**Tier 4: Safety Evaluation**

A 20-person expert team constructed a 2400-question safety test set across 5 major categories and multiple subcategories (Table 10). The team also used the "Do-Not-Answer" dataset (939 risk-categorized prompts) for comparative safety assessment (Section 5.4).

The paper includes several ablation studies that validate specific design decisions. These are critical for understanding which choices matter and which are optional.

### 5.9 Critical Assessment: Do Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claims That Are Well-Supported:**

1. **"DeepSeek LLM 67B surpasses LLaMA-2 70B across a range of benchmarks"** (Abstract): **Strongly supported**. Table 5 shows consistent advantages, particularly in code (HumanEval: 42.7 vs. 28.7), math (MATH: 18.7 vs. 13.5), and Chinese benchmarks (C-Eval: 66.1 vs. 51.4).

2. **"DeepSeek LLM 67B Chat exhibits superior performance compared to GPT-3.5"** (Abstract): **Supported for open-ended evaluation**. MT-Bench shows 8.76 vs. 8.39, and AlignBench shows 6.69 vs. 6.08. However, on some standard benchmarks, the comparison is less clear due to different evaluation protocols.

3. **"Data quality impacts optimal model/data scaling allocation"** (Section 3): **Supported by Table 4**. The systematic relationship between data quality and scaling exponents (a increases from 0.450 to 0.578 as quality improves) is compelling evidence.

4. **"Small-scale experiments can accurately predict large model performance"** (Section 3.2): **Supported by Figure 5**. The DeepSeek 7B and 67B models (blue stars) align closely with the predicted scaling curve derived from smaller models.

**Claims That Are Conditionally Supported:**

1. **"Scaling laws provide reliable guidance for open-source LLM development"**: The validation is strong for the specific configurations tested (7B and 67B), but the paper acknowledges limitations: "We have not yet considered the impact of factors beyond the compute budget C on the optimal hyperparameters" (Section 3.1). The scaling laws may not generalize to all architectures or data distributions.

2. **"DPO improves conversational performance"**: Supported for open-ended evaluation (MT-Bench: 8.35 → 8.76) but **not** for standard benchmarks (Table 17 shows minimal or negative changes). The benefit is domain-specific.

**Limitations and Unanswered Questions:**

1. **Compute Budget Transparency**: The paper does not explicitly state the total FLOPs or GPU hours required for training, making it difficult to assess cost-efficiency claims.

2. **Data Composition Details**: While the paper mentions 2 trillion tokens primarily in Chinese and English, the exact proportion and sources are not fully disclosed, limiting reproducibility.

3. **Long-Context Evaluation**: The models are trained with 4096 context length, but evaluation on long-context tasks is not presented. This is a gap given the emphasis on "longtermism."

4. **Non-English, Non-Chinese Performance**: The paper acknowledges "the model's proficiency in other languages remains delicate and should be approached with caution" (Section 6), but does not provide quantitative assessment of this limitation.

**Overall Assessment:**

The experimental analysis is **comprehensive and convincing** for the claims made. The four-tier evaluation framework effectively separates benchmark performance from real-world utility, and the held-out evaluation provides strong evidence against benchmark overfitting. The ablation studies validate specific design choices and reveal important scale-dependent effects (e.g., two-stage fine-tuning benefits 7B but not 67B). The primary limitation is not in the experimental rigor but in the transparency of training resources and data composition, which affects reproducibility for the broader research community.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the DeepSeek LLM project demonstrates that rigorous scaling laws can produce competitive open-source models, the approach relies on specific assumptions and incurs inherent trade-offs. Understanding these boundaries is critical for practitioners who intend to deploy or extend this work. The limitations fall into four categories: fundamental model constraints, scaling law generalizability, alignment-induced capability shifts, and architectural boundaries.

### 6.1 Fundamental Model Constraints
Like all large language models trained via next-token prediction, DeepSeek LLM inherits structural limitations regarding knowledge and truthfulness. The authors explicitly acknowledge these in **Section 6**:

*   **Static Knowledge Cut-off:** The model's knowledge is frozen at the end of pre-training. The system prompt specifies a knowledge cut-off date of **May 2023** (**Section 5.5**). The model lacks mechanisms for ongoing knowledge updates post-training, meaning it cannot access events or data emerging after this date without external retrieval tools.
*   **Hallucination Risk:** The model possesses a "tendency to produce hallucinations" and may generate "non-factual information such as unverified advice" (**Section 6**). This is an inherent risk of generative models optimizing for likelihood rather than factual verification. While safety training mitigates harmful outputs, it does not eliminate factual errors.
*   **Language Coverage:** The pre-training dataset consists of **2 trillion tokens** primarily in Chinese and English (**Section 1**). The authors state that "the model's proficiency in other languages remains delicate and should be approached with caution" (**Section 6**). This bilingual focus creates a performance disparity; while the model excels in Chinese and English tasks, its ability to reason or generate text in low-resource languages is not guaranteed and remains unevaluated in this work.

### 6.2 Scaling Law Assumptions and Generalizability
The scaling laws derived in **Section 3** provide a robust framework for the specific configurations tested (7B and 67B), but they rely on assumptions that may not hold universally.

*   **Hyperparameter Dependency on Compute Only:** The derived formulae for optimal batch size $B_{opt}$ and learning rate $\eta_{opt}$ model these hyperparameters solely as functions of the compute budget $C$ (**Section 3.1**). However, the authors note: "we have not yet considered the impact of factors beyond the compute budget $C$ on the optimal hyperparameters" (**Section 3.1**). They observe that models with the same compute budget but different model/data allocations show slightly varying optimal parameter spaces. This implies the current scaling laws are approximations that may require adjustment for architectures significantly different from the LLaMA-based design used here.
*   **Data Quality as a Variable:** The paper establishes that data quality impacts scaling exponents (**Section 3.3**). **Table 4** shows the model scaling exponent $a$ shifting from $0.450$ (Early Data) to $0.524$ (Current Data). However, the authors admit this relationship requires further study: "We will continue to pay close attention to the changes in data quality and its impact on scaling laws" (**Section 3.3**). Practitioners cannot simply plug arbitrary datasets into these formulae; the scaling exponents are valid only for data of comparable quality to the DeepSeek corpus.
*   **Model Scale Representation:** While using non-embedding FLOPs/token $M$ is more accurate than parameter count $N$ (**Section 3.2**), it still abstracts away hardware-specific efficiencies. The formula $C = MD$ assumes a theoretical compute cost that may vary depending on the specific GPU cluster efficiency, memory bandwidth, and parallelism strategy employed during actual training.

### 6.3 Alignment-Induced Capability Trade-offs
The transition from Base model to Chat model via Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) involves explicit trade-offs between specialized capabilities and general language modeling.

*   **Base vs. Chat Performance Shifts:** Alignment improves instruction following but can degrade performance on tasks that rely on raw next-token prediction. **Table 6** shows that while GSM8K scores improve significantly (63.4 → 84.1 for 67B), tasks like HellaSwag (84.0 → 75.7) and CLUEWSC (81.0 → 60.0) decline (**Section 5.1.2**). The authors explain that "pure language models are better equipped to handle such tasks" involving cloze or sentence completion (**Section 5.1.2**). Users requiring strong completion capabilities for legacy NLP tasks may prefer the Base model over the Chat model.
*   **Exclusion of Multiple-Choice Data:** To avoid benchmark overfitting, the team chose to exclude multiple-choice (MC) data from pre-training and fine-tuning (**Section 5.5**). **Table 13** shows that adding 20 million MC questions improves MC benchmark scores (MMLU: 49.4 → 60.9) but does not improve generative evaluations (TriviaQA: 57.9 → 57.9). The trade-off is deliberate: the model sacrifices optimized benchmark scores to maintain "true intelligence" in conversational interactions (**Section 5.5**). This means DeepSeek LLM may appear to underperform on leaderboards compared to models that aggressively tune on MC data, despite having comparable real-world utility.
*   **Scale-Dependent Fine-Tuning Strategies:** The optimal alignment strategy differs by model size. For the 7B model, a two-stage fine-tuning process (excluding math/code in stage 2) reduced repetition ratios from 2.0% to 1.4% (**Table 12**). However, for the 67B model, "the second stage hurts the model score on the benchmark" (**Section 5.1.2**). This indicates that alignment recipes are not transferable across scales; a strategy that stabilizes a small model may degrade a large one.

### 6.4 Architectural and Contextual Boundaries
The architectural choices made to optimize training efficiency and inference cost impose hard limits on the model's operational envelope.

*   **Context Length Limit:** The model architecture supports a context sequence length of **4096 tokens** (**Table 2**). While sufficient for standard conversations and document snippets, this limits the model's ability to process long documents, full codebases, or extended legal texts in a single pass. This is a constraint shared with many models of this generation but remains a limitation for tasks requiring long-range dependency modeling.
*   **Grouped-Query Attention (GQA) Trade-off:** The 67B model employs GQA with 64 query heads and 8 key-value heads (**Section 2.2**). While this optimizes inference memory bandwidth and speed, it is an approximation of standard Multi-Head Attention. In extreme cases, this compression of key-value states could theoretically reduce the model's capacity to attend to fine-grained details in very long contexts, though the evaluation results suggest the performance impact is negligible for standard tasks.
*   **Language Conflict in Smaller Models:** The bilingual training strategy introduces a "language conflict" that affects smaller models more severely. **Section 5.1.1** notes that "the advantage of DeepSeek 67B over LLaMA2 70B is larger than that of DeepSeek 7B over LLaMA2 7B," highlighting the "greater influence of language conflict on smaller models." Practitioners deploying the 7B variant should anticipate potentially lower performance ceilings compared to monolingual equivalents of the same size due to the capacity shared between Chinese and English representations.

### 6.5 Open Questions for Future Iterations
The paper identifies several areas where the current work is incomplete, marking them as priorities for future versions (**Section 6**):

*   **Reasoning and Code Depth:** While performance is strong, the authors note that "capabilities may be primarily focused on code completion and algebraic questions" (**Section 5.1.2**). Developing a "comprehensive understanding of mathematics and coding" requires incorporating a more diverse range of data during pre-training, which is left as future work.
*   **Chinese Data Exhaustiveness:** The authors acknowledge that the "initial version of Chinese data is not exhaustive," which may result in "suboptimal performance on certain Chinese-specific topics" (**Section 6**). This suggests that niche cultural or domain-specific Chinese knowledge may still be lacking compared to models trained on more specialized corpora.
*   **Reinforcement Learning for Reasoning:** The current alignment pipeline uses SFT and DPO. The authors mention that "initial experiments prove that reinforcement learning could boost model complex reasoning capability" (**Section 6**), implying that the current DPO-only approach may not fully unlock the model's reasoning potential compared to RLHF-based methods.

In summary, DeepSeek LLM represents a highly optimized point in the design space defined by its specific data quality, architecture, and scaling laws. Users must weigh the trade-offs between benchmark optimization and genuine conversational ability, and recognize that the scaling laws provided are contingent on maintaining similar data quality and architectural families.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The DeepSeek LLM paper does more than release a set of model weights; it fundamentally shifts how the open-source community should approach model development. By treating scaling laws as dynamic engineering tools rather than static theoretical observations, this work provides a blueprint for efficient resource allocation. This section explores how these findings reshape the landscape, what research paths they open, and how practitioners should integrate these insights into real-world applications.

### 7.1 Shifting the Open-Source Paradigm

Historically, open-source LLM development has focused on training fixed-size models (e.g., 7B, 70B) with high-quality data, often neglecting the underlying scaling principles that govern performance growth. DeepSeek challenges this approach by demonstrating that **rigorous scaling analysis allows open-source initiatives to compete with closed-source giants**.

*   **From Fixed-Size to Scaling-Guided Training:** The paper shows that small-scale experiments (compute budgets around 1e17 FLOPs) can accurately predict the performance of models trained with 1000× more compute (Section 3.2, Figure 5). This shifts the paradigm from "train and see" to "predict and verify," significantly reducing the financial risk associated with large-scale training runs.
*   **Data Quality as a Lever:** Prior scaling laws often treated data as a homogeneous commodity. DeepSeek establishes that data quality directly modulates scaling exponents (Section 3.3, Table 4). This implies that improving data quality is not just about better convergence; it fundamentally changes the optimal compute allocation, justifying investment in larger models rather than just more tokens.
*   **Competitive Performance:** The result is a model that surpasses LLaMA-2 70B across benchmarks and outperforms GPT-3.5 in open-ended evaluations (Section 5.2.2, Table 8). This proves that open-source models can achieve state-of-the-art performance without proprietary data monopolies, provided scaling laws are respected.

### 7.2 Enabling Follow-Up Research

The findings in this paper resolve some ambiguities in scaling theory while opening new avenues for investigation. Future research should focus on generalizing these findings and addressing the identified limitations.

*   **Quantifying Data Quality:** While the paper demonstrates that data quality affects scaling exponents (Section 3.3), it does not provide a universal metric for quantifying "quality." Future work should develop standardized metrics (e.g., entropy, diversity scores, or semantic density) that allow researchers to predict scaling exponents $a$ and $b$ before training begins. This would make the scaling laws portable across different datasets.
*   **Hyperparameter Generalization:** The derived hyperparameter formulae (Section 3.1) are specific to the LLaMA-based architecture and the specific data distribution used.
    $$ \eta_{opt} = 0.3118 \cdot C^{-0.1250} $$
    $$ B_{opt} = 0.2920 \cdot C^{0.3271} $$
    Researchers should validate whether these power laws hold for different architectures, such as Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models, which DeepSeek plans to explore in future reports (Section 6). The paper notes that factors beyond compute budget $C$ may affect optimal hyperparameters (Section 3.1), suggesting room for refinement.
*   **Alignment Trade-offs:** The observation that alignment (SFT/DPO) improves instruction following but degrades raw language modeling capabilities (e.g., HellaSwag scores drop in Table 6) highlights a tension between utility and base capability. Future research should investigate methods to mitigate this degradation, potentially through multi-task learning or modified loss functions that preserve base model knowledge during fine-tuning.
*   **Long-Context Scaling:** The current models are limited to 4096 tokens context length (Section 2.2, Table 2). As applications demand longer context windows, research is needed to understand how scaling laws apply to context length. Does increasing context length require reallocating compute from model depth to attention mechanisms? This remains an open question for the next version of DeepSeek LLM (Section 6).

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

Based on the evaluation results, DeepSeek LLM is particularly well-suited for specific downstream applications where its strengths align with user needs.

*   **Bilingual Development Assistants:** With strong performance in both Chinese (C-Eval 66.1) and English (MMLU 71.3) benchmarks (Section 5.1.1, Table 5), DeepSeek 67B is an ideal candidate for multinational enterprises requiring a single model for both languages. Its superior performance on code tasks (HumanEval 73.8 after Chat tuning) makes it suitable for AI pair programming tools.
*   **Mathematical and Logical Reasoning:** The model shows significant gains in mathematics (GSM8K 84.1, MATH 32.6) and reasoning (BBH 71.7) after alignment (Section 5.1.2, Table 6). This suggests applicability in educational technology, automated tutoring systems, and scientific reasoning assistants where logical accuracy is paramount.
*   **Safety-Critical Deployments:** The high safety scores on the Do-Not-Answer dataset (97.8, surpassing GPT-4's 96.5) and the custom 2400-question test set (Section 5.4, Table 11) indicate robustness against harmful prompts. This makes the model a strong candidate for enterprise customer service or public-facing applications where safety and compliance are critical constraints.
*   **Resource-Constrained Scaling:** Organizations planning to train their own models can use the provided scaling laws to estimate performance before committing resources. By running small-scale IsoFLOP experiments (as described in Section 3.2), teams can predict the optimal model/data split for their specific compute budget, avoiding wasted expenditure on suboptimal configurations.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For practitioners looking to adopt DeepSeek LLM or replicate its methodology, the following guidance clarifies when and how to use these techniques.

*   **When to Prefer DeepSeek LLM:**
    *   **Choose DeepSeek 67B Chat** if you need a balance of strong reasoning, coding capability, and bilingual (Chinese/English) support. It is preferable to LLaMA-2 70B for code and math tasks (Section 5.1.1).
    *   **Choose DeepSeek 7B** only if resource constraints are severe, but be aware of the "language conflict" impact on smaller models (Section 5.1.1). The 7B model shows larger performance gaps compared to LLaMA-2 7B than the 67B model does against LLaMA-2 70B.
    *   **Avoid** if your application requires context lengths exceeding 4096 tokens, as the model architecture does not currently support long-context windows (Section 2.2).

*   **Integrating Scaling Laws into Training:**
    *   **Metric Selection:** When fitting scaling curves for your own projects, adopt the **non-embedding FLOPs/token** ($M$) metric rather than parameter count ($N$) to avoid approximation errors, especially for small models (Section 3.2, Table 3).
    *   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Use the provided power laws for batch size and learning rate as a starting point (Section 3.1). However, validate them on your specific dataset, as data quality influences optimal allocation (Section 3.3).
    *   **Scheduler Choice:** Consider the **multi-step learning rate scheduler** (80%, 10%, 10% decay) if you plan for continual training, as it allows reuse of early training phases (Section 2.3, Figure 1).

*   **Infrastructure Considerations:**
    *   The paper uses the internal **HAI-LLM** framework (Section 2.4). For reproduction, practitioners should use equivalent frameworks like Megatron-LM or DeepSpeed that support 3D parallelism (data, tensor, pipeline) and Flash Attention.
    *   Ensure mixed precision training (bf16 for weights, fp32 for gradient accumulation) is configured to maintain stability, as described in the infrastructure section (Section 2.4).

*   **Alignment Strategy:**
    *   **For Models &lt; 10B:** Implement **two-stage fine-tuning** (Stage 1: All data, Stage 2: Conversational data only) to reduce repetition ratios without harming benchmark scores (Section 5.1.2, Table 12).
    *   **For Models > 50B:** A **single stage of SFT** followed by DPO is sufficient, as larger models exhibit lower repetition ratios naturally (Section 5.1.2).
    *   **Avoid Multiple-Choice Data:** Do not include multiple-choice questions in fine-tuning if your goal is conversational intelligence. While they boost benchmark scores, they do not improve generative capabilities and risk overfitting (Section 5.5, Table 13).

By adhering to these guidelines, practitioners can leverage the methodological rigor of DeepSeek LLM to build efficient, high-performing models tailored to their specific constraints and objectives.
