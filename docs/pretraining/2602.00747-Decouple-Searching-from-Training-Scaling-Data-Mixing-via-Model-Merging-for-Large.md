## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces DeMix, a framework that solves the prohibitive computational cost of optimizing data mixtures for Large Language Model (LLM) pre-training by decoupling mixture search from training via weighted model merging. Instead of training expensive proxy models for every sampled mixture like RegMix or CLIMB, DeMix trains only seven component models on candidate datasets and merges them to simulate unlimited mixtures, achieving comparable proxy accuracy with approximately 6× less computation budget (200B vs 1200B tokens). This approach yields superior data mixtures that outperform state-of-the-art methods on general, math, and code benchmarks while releasing the 22T-token DeMix Corpora with validated mixtures for open research.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of this paper, we must first establish why **data mixture optimization** is a critical bottleneck in modern Large Language Model (LLM) development, and why existing solutions fail to solve it efficiently.

### The Core Problem: Data Composition vs. Scale
While the success of LLMs is often attributed to massive scale (parameters and tokens), the **composition** of the pre-training corpus is equally critical. A model trained on 1 trillion tokens of low-quality or poorly balanced data will underperform a model trained on a carefully curated mixture. The specific challenge is determining the optimal ratio of different data domains—such as general web text, mathematical reasoning, code, and multilingual content—to balance general competence with specialized skills.

Formally, if we have $N$ candidate datasets $\{D_1, D_2, \dots, D_N\}$, the goal is to find a mixture distribution:

$$
D_{\text{mix}} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \alpha_i D_i
$$

where $\alpha_i \geq 0$ and $\sum \alpha_i = 1$, such that the resulting model performs best on target benchmarks. The search space for these weights $\alpha_i$ is continuous and high-dimensional, making brute-force exploration impossible.

### Why Existing Approaches Fail
The paper identifies two primary strategies historically used to solve this optimization problem, both of which suffer from significant drawbacks (Section 1, Section 6.1):

1.  **Large-Scale Proxy Experiments:**
    *   **Method:** Researchers train mid-sized models (e.g., 8B parameters) on sampled data mixtures with substantial token budgets (e.g., 100B tokens) to estimate how a full-scale model would perform.
    *   **Limitation:** While accurate, this is **prohibitively expensive**. Systematically searching the mixture space would require training hundreds of these proxy models, consuming computational resources that could otherwise be used for the final model training.

2.  **Automated Regression-Based Methods (e.g., RegMix, CLIMB):**
    *   **Method:** To reduce cost, methods like RegMix and CLIMB train **tiny-scale proxies** (smaller models, reduced data budgets). They use these cheap experiments to train a regression predictor that maps data mixture ratios to performance scores.
    *   **Limitation:** The validity of these lightweight proxies is increasingly questioned. There is a **capability gap** between the tiny proxy and the target large model. As noted in the Introduction, these automated strategies "often fail to generalize to complex tasks such as math and code" because the small models lack the emergent reasoning abilities of larger models.

Figure 1 illustrates this trade-off visually. Methods like RegMix and CLIMB require extensive proxies (e.g., 112 proxies) to achieve reasonable accuracy. Scaling these proxies to be more faithful (larger models) leads to an "unaffordable overall budget." Conversely, keeping them small sacrifices accuracy on hard tasks.

### The Missing Link: Sufficiency, Accuracy, and Efficiency
The paper positions DeMix as a solution that breaks the traditional trade-off between three competing constraints (Introduction):

*   **Sufficiency:** The ability to evaluate unlimited sampled mixtures. Regression methods need many samples to train a predictor, but training costs limit how many samples can be generated.
*   **Accuracy:** The proxy must faithfully reflect the performance ranking of the actual large model. Tiny proxies fail here, especially on math and code benchmarks.
*   **Efficiency:** The search process must not consume the budget allocated for the final model training.

> "DeMix breaks the trade-off between sufficiency, accuracy and efficiency, obtaining the optimal mixture with higher benchmark performance at lower search cost." (Abstract)

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work
DeMix differentiates itself by changing the **mechanism of proxy creation**. Instead of training a new model for every sampled mixture (which costs time and compute), DeMix leverages **model merging**.

*   **Prior Work (RegMix/CLIMB):** Train $\rightarrow$ Evaluate $\rightarrow$ Repeat for every mixture sample.
*   **DeMix:** Train component models once $\rightarrow$ Merge weights mathematically to simulate any mixture $\rightarrow$ Evaluate.

This approach relies on the empirical observation that if parameter updates are small relative to the initialization scale, the weight delta of a model trained on a mixture of datasets approximates the weighted sum of weight deltas from models trained on individual datasets (Section 2.3). By decoupling the search from the training cost, DeMix allows for "unlimited sampled mixtures without extra training burden," facilitating a more thorough search than prior regression-based methods allow (Abstract).

Furthermore, the paper addresses a secondary gap in the ecosystem: the lack of benchmarked corpora with validated mixture ratios. While public corpora exist (e.g., FineWeb-Edu, DCLM), they often lack validated mixtures for balancing multi-domain capabilities. DeMix positions itself not just as a method, but as a resource provider by releasing the **DeMix Corpora**, a 22T-token dataset with validated mixtures (Abstract, Section 5).

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a **methodology paper** that proposes a novel framework for optimizing pre-training data mixtures by replacing expensive proxy model training with computationally free model merging operations. The core idea is that instead of training a new model for every candidate data mixture, DeMix trains a small set of component models once and then mathematically combines their weights to simulate any mixture ratio, enabling unlimited search trials without additional training cost.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

DeMix is a **data mixture optimization system** that uses weighted model merging to predict which combination of training datasets will produce the best-performing language model. The solution replaces the traditional approach of training hundreds of proxy models (which is expensive) with a two-stage process: first train seven specialized component models, then merge their weights mathematically to evaluate thousands of mixture candidates at virtually zero cost.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The DeMix pipeline consists of four sequential components that transform raw data into an optimized mixture configuration:

1.  **Dataset Preprocessing Module:** Takes heterogeneous data sources (web text, math, code, multilingual) as input, applies cleaning filters (deduplication, perplexity filtering, FastText quality classification), and outputs categorized candidate datasets ready for training.

2.  **Component Model Preparation Module:** Takes the N candidate datasets and produces N component models, where each model is trained on one specific dataset mixed with 50% general data to maintain foundational capabilities while specializing in its target domain.

3.  **Model Merging Proxy Module:** Takes the trained component models and mixture weight samples as input, performs weighted arithmetic averaging of model parameters, and outputs proxy models that can be evaluated on benchmarks without any additional training.

4.  **Mixture Weight Optimization Module:** Takes the benchmark scores from merged proxy models, trains a LightGBM regression predictor to map mixture ratios to performance rankings, and iteratively refines predictions to output the final optimal mixture configuration.

Information flows linearly through these stages: cleaned data → component models → merged proxies → optimized weights → final mixture for pre-training.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Mathematical Foundation**

The entire DeMix framework rests on a mathematical proposition connecting data mixing to model merging through parameter updates. To understand this, we must first define what happens when a model trains on data. Let $\Theta_{\text{base}} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ denote the parameter vector of a pre-trained base model. For any dataset $D$, we can define a training operator $\mathcal{T}: \mathcal{D} \times \mathbb{R}^d \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^d$, where $\mathcal{T}(D, \Theta_{\text{base}})$ produces the model parameters after training on dataset distribution $D$ starting from $\Theta_{\text{base}}$. The **weight delta** (or parameter update vector) for dataset $D_i$ is defined as:

$$
\Delta(D_i) \triangleq \mathcal{T}(D_i, \Theta_{\text{base}}) - \Theta_{\text{base}}
$$

This equation simply states that the weight delta equals the final trained parameters minus the starting parameters. Consequently, an individually trained component model has parameters $\Theta_i = \Theta_{\text{base}} + \Delta(D_i)$.

The objective of data mixing is to find an optimal mixture distribution where the target model parameters would be:

$$
\Theta_{\text{mix}} = \mathcal{T}(D_{\text{mix}}, \Theta_{\text{base}}) \quad \text{where} \quad D_{\text{mix}} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \alpha_i D_i
$$

Here, $\alpha_i \geq 0$ and $\sum_{i=1}^{N} \alpha_i = 1$, meaning the mixture weights form a valid probability distribution over datasets.

The critical insight enabling DeMix is that **if parameter updates remain small relative to the initialization scale**, then training on a mixture of datasets approximates the weighted average of training on each dataset separately. The paper quantifies this small-update assumption with a ratio $\delta$:

$$
\delta = \frac{\sum |\mathcal{T}(D, \Theta_{\text{base}}) - \Theta_{\text{base}}|}{\sum |\mathcal{T}(D, \Theta_{\text{base}})| + \sum |\Theta_{\text{base}}|} \ll 1
$$

In the DeMix experiments, $\delta$ is approximately **10%**, which satisfies this small-update constraint. When $\delta \ll 1$, empirical studies show that the arithmetic sum of weight deltas from models trained on separate datasets closely approximates the weight delta from training on their union:

$$
\Delta(D_i \cup D_j) \approx \Delta(D_i) + \Delta(D_j)
$$

This proposition indicates that model parameters trained on a weighted mixture $D_{\text{mix}} = \sum \alpha_i D_i$ can be approximated by the weighted average of parameters $\sum_{i=1}^{N} \alpha_i \Theta_i$ trained on separate datasets. Therefore, merging component models $\{\Theta_i\}$ at ratio $\{\alpha_i\}$ produces a proxy model $M_{\text{mix}}^j$ calculated as:

$$
M_{\text{mix}}^j = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \alpha_i^j \Theta_i
$$

where $\Theta_i$ represents the component model parameters for candidate dataset $D_i$. This equation is the computational heart of DeMix: it allows unlimited proxy models to be generated through simple arithmetic operations rather than expensive training runs.

**Component Model Training Protocol**

The component model preparation follows a carefully designed two-step training protocol to ensure robust merging behavior. First, all component models are initialized from a **shared base model** trained from scratch on a general-purpose dataset $D_{\text{base}}$, which provides foundational language capabilities. In the DeMix experiments, this base model is trained on **50 billion tokens** of general data using the Qwen3-1.7B architecture.

Second, each component model is further trained on its corresponding domain-specific candidate dataset (e.g., mathematics or code), but crucially **mixed with general data at a fixed ratio $\beta = 0.5$**. This means each component model sees 50% domain-specific data and 50% general data during its specialized training phase. This design choice serves two purposes: it encourages each component model to specialize in its target domain while retaining general language competence, and it prevents the models from diverging too far from the base model, which would violate the small-update assumption required for valid merging.

The training configuration for component models uses a global batch size of **512** and a sequence length of **8192** tokens. The initial learning rate is set to **3e-4** and optimized via a cosine learning rate schedule that retains a minimum of **20%** of the initial learning rate at the end of training. Each component model trains on **50 billion tokens**, ensuring they develop sufficient emergent capabilities to solve challenging math and code problems that tiny-scale proxies would miss.

**Model Merging as Proxy Evaluation**

Once the N component models are trained, DeMix can construct proxy models for any sampled mixture ratio without additional training. The merging procedure adopts a **straightforward weighted linear merging strategy**, which the ablation studies show outperforms more complex methods like Multi-SLERP, DARE, DELLA, and TIES in this specific context. The linear merging is hyperparameter-free, making it simpler to deploy than alternatives requiring tuning.

For evaluation, DeMix uses a benchmark suite covering three capability domains. General language understanding is measured using **ARC-E, HellaSwag, WinoGrande, PIQA, and SIQA**. Code generation is evaluated with **HumanEval and MBPP**. Mathematical reasoning uses **GSM8K and MATH**. The paper defines the **average ranking** of the merged model across this benchmark suite as the gold-standard evaluation signal, choosing ranking-based evaluation for its robustness to scale mismatches and direct relevance to mixture selection.

The computational cost of benchmarking is negligible compared to training. Table 1 shows that one benchmarking run costs **0.3 GPU hours** on H800 hardware, which is equivalent to training only **0.013 billion tokens**. This means DeMix can evaluate hundreds of merged proxies for less cost than training a single proxy model from scratch.

**Iterative Mixture Weight Optimization**

The optimization process searches for the best mixture weights through an iterative prediction-refinement loop. Step 1 randomly samples a large set of mixture weight ratios $\{\alpha_i^j\}$ uniformly from the simplex (the geometric space where all weights sum to 1). Step 2 constructs a proxy model for each sampled ratio by weighted merging using Equation 6 and evaluates it on the benchmark suite to obtain an average ranking score $r^j$.

Step 3 trains a predictor function $f$ that maps mixture weights to ranking scores using the collected pairs $(\alpha_i^j, r^j)$. DeMix adopts **LightGBM** as the regression model, following prior work in RegMix. The LightGBM configuration uses a learning rate of **0.02** and **300 iterations**. Step 4 uses the trained predictor $f$ to score a large number of newly sampled mixture ratios, selects the top ratios according to predicted $r^j$, and iteratively executes Steps 2-4 **three times** to refine predictions for high-ranking ratios.

The specific sampling schedule across iterations is: **64 mixtures** in the first iteration, **32 mixtures** in the second iteration, and **16 mixtures** in the third iteration. After the final iteration, DeMix samples a large number of mixture ratios using the trained predictor and selects the top-ranked candidates. The final optimal mixture ratio is computed as the **average of the top 128 candidates**, which defines the final mixed dataset for pre-training.

**Why This Approach Over Alternatives**

DeMix makes several deliberate design choices that distinguish it from prior regression-based methods like RegMix and CLIMB. The fundamental difference is **decoupling search from training**: RegMix and CLIMB must train a new proxy model for every sampled mixture, which limits how many samples can be evaluated within a fixed budget. DeMix trains component models once and then generates unlimited proxies through merging, enabling more thorough exploration of the mixture space.

The ablation studies in Section 4.3 justify the choice of linear merging over more sophisticated methods. Table 4 compares six merging strategies: Linear, Multi-SLERP, Breadcrumbs, DARE, DELLA, and TIES. Linear merging achieves the best **capability recovery rate of 0.845** and the highest macro-average Spearman's $\rho$ of **0.787**, while also being hyperparameter-free. More complex methods like DARE, DELLA, and TIES require hyperparameter tuning and do not improve proxy accuracy in this pre-training context, likely because they are designed for fine-tuning scenarios where parameter updates have different characteristics.

Another critical design choice is the **50% general data regularization** in component model training. Table 5 shows that reducing the general data proportion to 25% drops proxy accuracy to $\rho = 0.667$ with capability recovery of 0.796. Removing general data entirely (0%) further degrades performance to $\rho = 0.652$. This validates that without sufficient general data, component models diverge too far from the base model, violating the small-update assumption and reducing merging fidelity.

**Complete Configuration Summary**

Table 2 provides the full cost breakdown comparing DeMix to training-based proxies. DeMix with component models trained on 30B tokens achieves a macro-average Spearman's $\rho$ of **0.81** with a total token budget of **212B**. To reach similar performance, training-based approaches require **1344B tokens**, representing a **6.4× increase in cost**. The DeMix configuration uses 7 component models (one per candidate dataset category), each trained on 30B tokens, giving a pre-cost of $30 \times 7 = 210$ billion tokens plus benchmarking overhead.

For the final mixture quality experiments in Table 3, DeMix with **224 merged proxies** achieves the best average rank of **24.00** across all benchmarks. This outperforms both RegMix and CLIMB even when those methods use 8B-parameter proxy models with 448B token budgets. The DeMix total training budget remains roughly **211-214B tokens** regardless of proxy count, since merging is computationally free, whereas training-based methods scale linearly with proxy count.

The DeMix Corpora released alongside this framework contains **22 trillion tokens** total, with **15 trillion original tokens** after cleaning and **22 trillion mixture tokens** after applying the validated mixture ratios across three training stages. Table 8 details the composition: general data dominates Stage 1 (approximately 14T tokens), while math and code proportions increase in Stages 2 and 3 (approximately 6T and 2T tokens respectively). This staged approach reflects the understanding that early training prioritizes diversity while later stages prioritize high-quality specialized data.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the fundamental conceptual shifts and empirical discoveries that distinguish DeMix from prior data mixture optimization methods. While Section 3 detailed the mechanical implementation, here we analyze *why* these design choices succeed and what they reveal about the nature of pre-training dynamics. We distinguish between incremental improvements (better hyperparameters) and fundamental innovations (changing the optimization paradigm).

### 4.1 Decoupling Search Cost from Training Cost (Fundamental Innovation)

The most significant contribution of DeMix is not merely a performance gain, but a structural change in how data mixture search is formulated economically. Prior automated methods like RegMix and CLIMB treat search and training as coupled processes: to evaluate a new mixture ratio, one must train a new proxy model. This creates a linear relationship between search resolution and computational cost. If you want to sample 1,000 mixture ratios, you must train 1,000 proxies.

DeMix breaks this coupling by treating model weights as vector spaces that can be interpolated. Once the component models are trained, the cost of evaluating a new mixture ratio drops from "training a model" to "averaging weights."

*   **Economic Implication:** This shifts the bottleneck from *training budget* to *evaluation budget*. As shown in **Table 1**, the cost of one benchmarking run is equivalent to training only `0.013B` tokens. This allows DeMix to evaluate unlimited proxies (e.g., 448 or more) without increasing the training budget, whereas training-based methods hit a hard ceiling.
*   **Search Space Exploration:** Because the marginal cost of sampling a new ratio is near-zero, DeMix can explore the mixture simplex much more densely. **Figure 1** illustrates this: while RegMix/CLIMB must limit themselves to 112 proxies to stay within budget, DeMix can generate unlimited proxies from the same 7 component models. This enables the discovery of optimal mixtures that sparse sampling might miss.

This is a **fundamental innovation** because it changes the complexity class of the search problem. It transforms an optimization problem constrained by GPU-hours into one constrained only by inference throughput.

### 4.2 Validity of Model Merging in the Pre-training Regime (Empirical Discovery)

Prior literature on model merging (Section 6.2) has predominantly focused on **fine-tuning** scenarios, where models diverge from a base to solve specific downstream tasks. Applying this to **pre-training** mixture search is non-trivial because pre-training involves massive parameter updates over trillions of tokens, potentially violating the linearity assumptions required for merging.

DeMix provides critical empirical evidence that merging remains faithful even in this high-scale regime, provided specific constraints are met.

*   **Small-Update Assumption Holds:** The theoretical foundation relies on the parameter update magnitude $\delta$ being small relative to the base weights (Section 2.3). **Table 2** validates this empirically: DeMix proxies achieve a Spearman's $\rho$ of `0.81` (macro avg) compared to reference models trained on real mixtures. This high correlation confirms that the superposition principle $\Delta(D_i \cup D_j) \approx \Delta(D_i) + \Delta(D_j)$ holds sufficiently well for ranking purposes, even after 50B tokens of training.
*   **Ranking vs. Absolute Accuracy:** A subtle but crucial insight is that the proxy does not need to match the *absolute* scores of the reference model, only the *relative ranking*. **Table 2** shows that while capability recovery rates vary (e.g., `0.83` for 211B budget), the ranking consistency remains high. This distinguishes DeMix from methods that require precise loss prediction; it only needs to identify which mixture is *better*, not exactly how good it is.

This is a **significant empirical discovery** because it extends the applicability of model merging from post-training adaptation to core pre-training infrastructure, validating a theoretical assumption that was previously untested at this scale.

### 4.3 Simplicity Outperforms Complexity in Merging Strategies (Counter-Intuitive Insight)

In the broader model merging literature, sophisticated methods like TIES (Yadav et al., 2023) or DARE (Yu et al., 2024) are often preferred over simple linear averaging. These methods employ mechanisms like task vector sparsification or interference resolution to handle conflicting parameter updates between models.

However, DeMix finds that **Linear Merging** is superior for pre-training mixture search.

*   **Ablation Evidence:** **Table 4** compares six merging methods. Linear merging achieves the highest capability recovery (`0.845`) and proxy accuracy ($\rho = 0.787$), outperforming TIES (`0.786` recovery) and DELLA (`0.778` recovery).
*   **Reasoning:** This suggests that pre-training updates on diverse domains (math, code, general) are less conflicting than fine-tuning updates on distinct tasks. In fine-tuning, models often overwrite each other's knowledge (interference). In pre-training, the updates appear to be more additive, meaning complex interference resolution mechanisms introduce noise rather than correcting conflicts.
*   **Hyperparameter Efficiency:** Linear merging is hyperparameter-free (HP-Free), whereas methods like DARE require tuning sparsity rates. **Table 4** highlights this distinction. For a search framework that relies on generating thousands of proxies, removing hyperparameter tuning from the merging step itself reduces the risk of overfitting the search process.

This is a **counter-intuitive insight** because it contradicts the prevailing trend in model merging research where complexity is often equated with performance. It suggests that the "signal" in pre-training data mixing is robust enough that simple arithmetic averaging preserves it better than aggressive pruning.

### 4.4 General Data as a Regularizer for Mergeability (Design Constraint)

A critical design choice in DeMix is mixing 50% general data into every component model during training (Section 3.4). While this might seem like a standard practice to prevent forgetting, the ablation study reveals it is actually a **constraint for mergeability**.

*   **Divergence Control:** **Table 5** shows that reducing the general data proportion to 25% drops proxy accuracy to $\rho = 0.667$, and removing it (0%) drops it further to `0.652`.
*   **Mechanism:** If component models train exclusively on specialized data (e.g., 100% code), their parameter updates $\Delta(D_i)$ diverge significantly from the base model and from each other. This increases the update magnitude $\delta$, violating the small-update assumption required for linear merging to work. By anchoring all component models with 50% general data, DeMix ensures they remain in a proximal region of the weight space, making their linear interpolation valid.
*   **Trade-off:** This introduces a constraint on the search space. DeMix cannot search for mixtures where specialized data dominates beyond 50% per component. However, as noted in Appendix A.1, this aligns with the consensus that non-general data should not dominate the pre-training corpus.

This is a **critical design insight** because it identifies a failure mode for model merging: excessive specialization destroys mergeability. It teaches practitioners that when building component models for merging, maintaining a shared "base" capability is not just about model performance, but about mathematical compatibility for interpolation.

### 4.5 Validated Mixtures as a Community Resource (Infrastructure Contribution)

Beyond the algorithmic contribution, DeMix addresses a reproducibility gap in the LLM community. While many public corpora exist (e.g., FineWeb-Edu, DCLM), they provide raw data without validated mixture ratios for balancing capabilities.

*   **Gap Identification:** **Table 6** compares public datasets. Most lack validated mixtures (marked with ✗). Researchers often have to guess ratios or rely on proprietary knowledge from large labs.
*   **Deliverable:** The **DeMix Corpora** provides 22T tokens with mixtures validated through the DeMix framework. **Table 7** shows this corpus achieves an average rank of `24.00`, outperforming mixed datasets like SmolLM-Corpus (`31.33`) and Nemotron-Pretrain (`36.00`).
*   **Significance:** This lowers the barrier to entry for independent researchers. Instead of spending months and millions of dollars searching for a mixture, researchers can start with a validated baseline.

This is an **infrastructure innovation**. It shifts the community focus from "how do we find a good mixture?" to "how do we improve the data itself?", accelerating overall research progress by standardizing the baseline mixture configuration.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section evaluates whether DeMix's experimental design convincingly validates its core claims: that model merging can serve as an accurate proxy for data mixture search, and that this approach yields superior mixtures at lower computational cost than prior methods. We examine the evaluation methodology, quantitative results, ablation studies, and limitations.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The paper employs a two-tiered evaluation strategy to assess both the **proxy fidelity** (does merging accurately predict performance?) and the **mixture quality** (does the final mixture produce a better model?).

**Benchmark Suite (Section 3.1)**

DeMix evaluates models across three capability domains using established benchmarks:

| Domain | Benchmarks | Purpose |
|--------|------------|---------|
| General Language | ARC-E, HellaSwag, WinoGrande, PIQA, SIQA | Measure foundational understanding |
| Code | HumanEval, MBPP | Assess programming capability |
| Math | GSM8K, MATH | Evaluate reasoning skills |

The paper uses **average ranking** across this suite as the primary optimization signal, rather than raw scores. This choice is deliberate: ranking is more robust to scale mismatches between proxy and reference models, and mixture selection fundamentally requires identifying *which* ratio is better, not predicting exact performance values.

**Reference Model Construction (Section 3.3.1)**

To validate proxy accuracy, the authors train **96 reference models** on 50B tokens each, using randomly sampled mixture ratios. These serve as ground-truth performance standards. Proxy models (either merged or training-based) are then compared against these references using **Spearman's rank correlation coefficient** ($\rho$):

$$
\rho = \text{correlation}(\text{proxy rankings}, \text{reference rankings})
$$

A higher $\rho$ indicates the proxy correctly identifies which mixtures perform better. The paper also reports **Top 25% Spearman's $\rho$**, which measures accuracy specifically for high-performing mixtures—critical since the goal is to find the *best* mixture, not rank all mixtures equally well.

**Capability Recovery Rate (Section 3.3.1)**

Beyond ranking consistency, the paper introduces a metric for absolute performance preservation:

$$
\text{Capability Recovery} = \frac{\text{Average benchmark score of proxy}}{\text{Average benchmark score of reference}}
$$

This measures how much of the reference model's actual performance the proxy retains. A recovery rate of 0.85 means the merged proxy achieves 85% of the reference model's benchmark scores.

**Baselines (Section 3.2)**

The paper compares against two state-of-the-art automated mixture methods:

- **RegMix (Liu et al., 2024):** Samples mixture ratios, trains proxy models on each, fits a regression predictor.
- **CLIMB (Diao et al., 2025):** Uses iterative sampling to focus on high-performing regions of the mixture space.

Both baselines are modified to align with DeMix's setup (same model architecture, data, and training budget) for fair comparison. The paper also includes **Uniform** (equal mixture) and **Heuristic** (expert-designed ratios) strategies as untuned baselines.

**Computational Cost Accounting (Table 1)**

A critical aspect of the evaluation is transparent cost reporting. Table 1 breaks down GPU hours for training vs. benchmarking:

| Budget / Proxy | Training Cost | Benchmarking Cost |
|----------------|---------------|-------------------|
| 2B tokens | 46 GPUh | 0.3 GPUh |
| 0.013B tokens | 0.3 GPUh | 0.3 GPUh |

> "The cost of one benchmarking run is equivalent to training 0.013B tokens." (Section 3.1)

This quantification is essential: it shows that evaluating a merged proxy is approximately **150× cheaper** than training a 2B-token proxy model, enabling the "unlimited proxy" claim.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**Proxy Accuracy: DeMix Outperforms Training-Based Methods at Lower Cost (Table 2)**

Table 2 presents the core comparison between DeMix and training-based proxies (RegMix/CLIMB style). The results strongly support the claim that model merging provides more faithful proxies per unit of compute:

| Method | Total Budget (B tokens) | No. Proxies | Budget / Proxy (B) | Macro Avg $\rho$ | Top 25% $\rho$ | Capability Recovery |
|--------|------------------------|-------------|-------------------|------------------|----------------|---------------------|
| Trained Proxy | 224 | 112 | 2 | 0.17 | 0.20 | 0.77 |
| Trained Proxy | 1344 | 112 | 12 | 0.57 | 0.57 | 0.87 |
| **DeMix (30B components)** | **211** | **112** | **0.01†** | **0.81** | **0.59** | **0.83** |
| DeMix (50B components) | 351 | 112 | 0.01 | 0.80 | 0.50 | 0.85 |

Key observations:

1. **At comparable budget (~211B vs 224B):** DeMix achieves $\rho = 0.81$ vs 0.17 for trained proxies—a **4.8× improvement** in ranking consistency. This is a decisive result showing that tiny-scale training proxies (2B tokens each) fail to capture the performance landscape accurately.

2. **To match DeMix accuracy:** Training-based methods require 1344B tokens, representing a **6.4× increase in cost** relative to DeMix's 211B. This validates the efficiency claim in the Abstract.

3. **Capability Recovery:** DeMix maintains recovery rates of 0.83–0.85, meaning merged proxies retain 83–85% of the reference model's absolute performance. This confirms that merging does not catastrophically degrade capabilities.

4. **Diminishing returns on component training:** Increasing component model training from 30B to 50B tokens (211B → 351B total budget) does not improve proxy accuracy ($\rho$ drops slightly from 0.81 to 0.80). This suggests 30B tokens per component is sufficient, and the paper correctly selects this configuration for subsequent experiments (Section 4.1).

**Mixture Quality: DeMix Yields Better Final Models (Table 3)**

Table 3 evaluates the downstream performance of models trained on mixtures discovered by different methods. This is the ultimate test: does the search method actually find better data ratios?

| Method | Train Cost (B) | No. Proxies | Macro Avg Rank ↓ |
|--------|---------------|-------------|------------------|
| Uniform | - | - | 36.67 |
| Heuristic | - | - | 42.33 |
| RegMix (best) | 448 | 56 | 28.00 |
| CLIMB (best) | 448 | 56 | 27.67 |
| **DeMix (best)** | **212** | **224** | **24.00** |

Key findings:

1. **DeMix achieves the best rank (24.00)** with less than half the training budget of RegMix/CLIMB (212B vs 448B). This is a strong result supporting the efficiency claim.

2. **Scaling proxy count improves quality:** For DeMix, increasing proxies from 56 → 224 improves rank from 29.33 → 24.00. This demonstrates the benefit of "unlimited" proxy evaluation—more samples enable better optimization. However, at 448 proxies, rank degrades to 27.67, suggesting **overfitting to noise** in the predictor (Section 4.2).

3. **Small proxies underperform:** RegMix/CLIMB with 112 proxies at 2B tokens each achieve ranks of 38.00 and 34.67, worse than DeMix. This confirms the Introduction's claim that tiny-scale proxies "fail to generalize to complex tasks such as math and code."

4. **Domain-specific performance:** Looking at individual domains in Table 3, DeMix achieves particularly strong math performance (rank 14 for GSM8K/MATH average at 224 proxies), while CLIMB with 56 proxies at 8B achieves rank 11. This suggests DeMix's advantage is most pronounced in general and code domains, with math being more competitive.

**DeMix Corpora Validation (Table 7, Figure 3)**

The paper validates the released DeMix Corpora by training a Qwen3-1.7B model on 50B tokens and comparing against other public corpora:

| Dataset | General | Code | Math | Avg Rank ↓ |
|---------|---------|------|------|------------|
| SmolLM-Corpus | 59.13 | 21.01 | 9.14 | 31.33 |
| Nemotron-Pretrain | 57.67 | 28.35 | 16.12 | 36.00 |
| **DeMix Corpora** | **58.77** | **22.49** | **15.76** | **24.00** |

DeMix Corpora achieves the best **balanced performance** (rank 24.00), while competitors excel in one domain at the expense of others:
- SmolLM-Corpus has strong general scores (59.13) but poor math (9.14).
- Nemotron-Pretrain has better math (16.12) but weaker general capabilities (57.67).

Figure 3 further shows that the high-quality general subset of DeMix Corpora outperforms other general datasets (FineWeb-Edu, DCLM-Baseline, etc.) when training from scratch, validating the data curation pipeline.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies

The paper conducts two critical ablation studies to identify design choices essential for DeMix's success.

**Merging Method Comparison (Table 4)**

Table 4 compares six merging strategies, testing whether sophisticated methods outperform simple linear averaging:

| Method | HP-Free | $\rho$ | Capability Recovery |
|--------|---------|--------|---------------------|
| **Linear** | ✓ | **0.787** | **0.845** |
| Multi-SLERP | ✓ | 0.785 | 0.813 |
| Breadcrumbs | ✓ | 0.735 | 0.831 |
| DARE | ✗ | 0.757 | 0.835 |
| DELLA | ✗ | 0.784 | 0.778 |
| TIES | ✗ | 0.783 | 0.786 |

This is a **counter-intuitive result**: Linear merging, the simplest method, achieves the best performance. The paper reasons that pre-training updates are more additive than conflicting (unlike fine-tuning), so interference-resolution mechanisms in TIES/DARE introduce unnecessary complexity. This finding has practical implications: practitioners should not assume complex merging is better without empirical validation in their specific regime.

**General Data Proportion (Table 5)**

Table 5 tests the impact of mixing general data into component model training:

| General Data % | $\rho$ | Capability Recovery |
|----------------|--------|---------------------|
| **50%** | **0.787** | **0.845** |
| 25% | 0.667 | 0.796 |
| 0% | 0.652 | 0.795 |

This ablation reveals a **critical failure mode**: without sufficient general data, proxy accuracy collapses. The mechanism is that component models diverge too far from the base model, violating the small-update assumption ($\delta \ll 1$) required for valid merging. This is not merely a performance consideration—it is a **mathematical constraint** for the approach to work.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive baseline comparison:** The paper compares against current state-of-the-art (RegMix, CLIMB) with aligned configurations, not straw-man baselines.

2. **Two-tier validation:** Evaluating both proxy fidelity (Table 2) and final mixture quality (Table 3) provides strong evidence that the method works end-to-end.

3. **Transparent cost accounting:** Table 1's GPU-hour breakdown enables readers to verify efficiency claims independently.

4. **Ablation studies:** Tables 4 and 5 isolate critical design choices, preventing the results from being attributed to unspecified "magic."

**Limitations and Open Questions:**

1. **Single model scale:** All experiments use Qwen3-1.7B. The paper does not validate whether DeMix scales to larger models (7B, 70B). The small-update assumption ($\delta \approx 10\%$) may not hold at larger scales where parameter updates could be more substantial.

2. **Fixed component count:** Experiments use 7 component models. The paper acknowledges in Section 4.2 that "expanding the pool of candidate datasets will increase the dimensionality of the mixture ratios, which in turn increases the number of proxies required." It is unclear how DeMix performs with 20+ candidate datasets.

3. **Overfitting at high proxy counts:** Performance degrades at 448 proxies (rank 27.67 vs 24.00 at 224). The paper attributes this to "overfitting noise" but does not propose mitigation strategies (e.g., regularization in the LightGBM predictor).

4. **Math performance gap:** While DeMix excels overall, Table 3 shows CLIMB with 56 proxies at 8B achieves better math rank (11 vs 14 for DeMix). This suggests that for math-heavy optimization, larger proxy models may still be necessary.

5. **Three-stage mixture not fully validated:** Appendix A.2 describes a three-stage pre-training pipeline (14T → 6T → 2T tokens), but Table 3 only evaluates single-stage 50B token training. The effectiveness of DeMix-optimized mixtures across multi-stage pre-training is not directly measured.

**Overall Assessment:**

The experiments **convincingly support** the core claims:
- Model merging provides accurate proxies at lower cost than training-based methods (Table 2).
- DeMix discovers better mixtures than RegMix/CLIMB within the same budget (Table 3).
- Linear merging and 50% general data regularization are critical design choices (Tables 4, 5).

However, the **generalizability** of these results to larger models and more complex dataset taxonomies remains an open question. The paper is transparent about this limitation in Section 4.2, noting that "further performance gains can be reasonably anticipated, yet such gains would be accompanied by substantial computational overhead."

The release of DeMix Corpora (22T tokens with validated mixtures) partially mitigates this by providing a baseline for future research to build upon, even if the search method itself requires further validation at scale.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While DeMix demonstrates compelling advantages over prior data mixture optimization methods, the approach relies on several assumptions and faces constraints that limit its applicability in certain scenarios. This section critically examines these limitations, grounding each point in evidence from the paper.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions

**The Small-Update Assumption ($\delta \ll 1$)**

The entire mathematical foundation of DeMix rests on the proposition that parameter updates remain small relative to the base model weights. Section 2.3 formalizes this as:

$$
\delta = \frac{\sum |\mathcal{T}(D, \Theta_{\text{base}}) - \Theta_{\text{base}}|}{\sum |\mathcal{T}(D, \Theta_{\text{base}})| + \sum |\Theta_{\text{base}}|} \ll 1
$$

In the DeMix experiments, $\delta$ is approximately **10%**, which satisfies this constraint. However, this assumption may not hold universally:

- **Scale Dependency**: All experiments use the Qwen3-1.7B architecture (Section 3). Larger models (7B, 70B, or beyond) may exhibit different parameter update dynamics during pre-training. The paper does not validate whether $\delta$ remains small at these scales.
- **Training Duration**: Component models train on 30–50B tokens. If pre-training extends to hundreds of billions of tokens per component, the cumulative parameter updates could violate the small-update assumption, degrading merging fidelity.

This is a **critical theoretical constraint**: if $\delta$ grows too large, the superposition principle $\Delta(D_i \cup D_j) \approx \Delta(D_i) + \Delta(D_j)$ breaks down, and merged proxies no longer faithfully represent real data mixtures.

**Linearity of Pre-training Updates**

DeMix assumes that pre-training updates across different domains are additive rather than conflicting. The ablation study in Table 4 supports this for the tested regime—Linear merging outperforms interference-resolution methods like TIES and DARE. However:

- This may not generalize to **highly divergent domains**. For example, merging models trained on formal logic versus creative writing might exhibit more interference than the math/code/general domains tested.
- The paper acknowledges this implicitly by noting that model merging literature has "predominantly focused on **fine-tuning** scenarios" (Section 6.2), where interference is more pronounced. The pre-training regime is less understood.

### 6.2 Scalability Constraints

**Component Model Dimensionality**

The experiments use **7 component models** corresponding to 7 candidate dataset categories (Appendix A.1). Section 4.2 explicitly acknowledges the scalability challenge:

> "Expanding the pool of candidate datasets will increase the dimensionality of the mixture ratios, which in turn increases the number of proxies required to conduct an effective search."

This creates a practical bottleneck:

- With 7 components, the mixture simplex is 6-dimensional. With 20+ components (e.g., fine-grained domain categories like biology, chemistry, physics, law, medicine), the search space grows exponentially.
- While merging is computationally free, **benchmarking cost accumulates**. Table 1 shows each benchmarking run costs 0.3 GPUh. At 448 proxies (where performance already degrades), this equals **134.4 GPUh** just for evaluation—not including the component model training cost.

**Proxy Count and Overfitting**

Table 3 reveals a non-monotonic relationship between proxy count and mixture quality:

| Proxy Count | Macro Avg Rank |
|-------------|----------------|
| 56 | 29.33 |
| 112 | 25.67 |
| 224 | **24.00** |
| 448 | 27.67 |

Performance **degrades at 448 proxies**, which Section 4.2 attributes to "overfitting noise." This indicates:

- The LightGBM predictor (300 iterations, 0.02 learning rate) may lack sufficient regularization for very large sample sizes.
- There is an **optimal proxy count range** beyond which additional sampling harms rather than helps. The paper does not provide a principled method for determining this range a priori.

### 6.3 Search Space Restrictions

**The 50% General Data Constraint**

Table 5 demonstrates that component models require 50% general data to maintain mergeability:

| General Data % | $\rho$ | Capability Recovery |
|----------------|--------|---------------------|
| 50% | 0.787 | 0.845 |
| 25% | 0.667 | 0.796 |
| 0% | 0.652 | 0.795 |

This imposes a **hard constraint on the searchable mixture space**. DeMix cannot discover mixtures where specialized data (math, code, etc.) dominates beyond 50% per component. Appendix A.1 justifies this by citing consensus that "non-general data should not dominate the pre-training corpus," but this remains a limitation:

- Some use cases may **require specialized dominance**. A model designed specifically for mathematical reasoning might benefit from 70%+ math data, which DeMix cannot explore.
- The constraint is **architectural**, not just empirical. Removing it would violate the small-update assumption and break the merging mechanism.

**Domain Coverage Gaps**

The 7 component models cover general, multilingual, math, and code domains (Figure 4, Appendix A.1). However:

- **Specialized domains** like scientific literature, legal texts, or medical records are not explicitly represented as separate components.
- Table 8 shows these may be subsumed under "General" or "Others," but this reduces the granularity of mixture optimization. A researcher wanting to optimize specifically for biomedical QA cannot fine-tune the mixture for that domain independently.

### 6.4 Performance Trade-offs

**Math Capability Gap**

While DeMix achieves the best overall rank (24.00), Table 3 reveals a domain-specific weakness:

| Method | Math Rank ↓ |
|--------|-------------|
| CLIMB (56 proxies, 8B) | **11** |
| DeMix (224 proxies) | 14 |

This suggests that for **math-heavy optimization**, larger proxy models (8B vs. 1.7B) may still be necessary. The 1.7B component models may lack the emergent reasoning capabilities needed to accurately rank math-focused mixtures, even with merging.

**Single-Stage Validation**

Appendix A.2 describes a **three-stage pre-training pipeline** (14T → 6T → 2T tokens) with dynamically adjusted mixtures. However, Table 3 only evaluates **single-stage 50B token training**. The paper does not directly measure:

- Whether DeMix-optimized mixtures maintain their advantage across multi-stage pre-training.
- Whether the optimal mixture ratios change between stages (the paper adjusts them heuristically in Appendix A.2, not via DeMix search).

This is a **validation gap**: the released DeMix Corpora uses the three-stage pipeline, but the search method itself is only validated on single-stage training.

### 6.5 Reproducibility and Transparency

**Data Curation Complexity**

The DeMix Corpora release is a significant contribution, but Appendix A reveals the curation pipeline is highly complex:

- Multiple filtering stages (deduplication, perplexity filtering, FastText classification, Chinese-specific quality assessment).
- Hierarchical instance-level labeling using Qwen3-235B-A22B annotations distilled into a 4B labeler.
- Stage-specific mixture adjustments based on quality tiers.

While the paper provides detailed descriptions, **exact replication** would require:

- Access to the same base models (Qwen3-0.6B for perplexity, Qwen3-32B for Chinese labeling).
- Significant computational resources for the labeling pipeline itself.
- The original source datasets, some of which may have licensing restrictions.

**Predictor Hyperparameters**

The LightGBM configuration (0.02 learning rate, 300 iterations) is specified in Section 3, but:

- No ablation study validates these choices. Different regression models or hyperparameters might improve predictor accuracy.
- The paper does not release the trained predictor weights, only the final mixture ratios.

### 6.6 Open Questions

**Generalization to Larger Models**

The most significant open question is whether DeMix scales. The paper is transparent about this limitation in Section 4.2:

> "Further performance gains can be reasonably anticipated, yet such gains would be accompanied by substantial computational overhead."

However, this refers to proxy count scaling, not model scale. The **1.7B architecture** may not be representative of production LLMs, which often exceed 7B parameters. Key questions remain:

- Does $\delta \approx 10\%$ hold for 70B models trained on trillions of tokens?
- Do merging fidelity and proxy accuracy degrade at larger scales?
- Would component models need to be larger (e.g., 7B each) to maintain validity, multiplying the base training cost by 7×?

**Dynamic Mixture Optimization**

DeMix optimizes a **static mixture ratio** for each training stage. Recent work suggests that **curriculum-based** or **dynamic** mixture adjustment during training may outperform static ratios (Feng et al., 2024; Basant et al., 2025, cited in Section 6.1). DeMix does not address:

- Whether mixture ratios should change mid-stage based on model learning signals.
- How to integrate DeMix with online data selection methods like DoReMi (Xie et al., 2023).

**Alternative Merging Mechanisms**

Table 4 shows Linear merging outperforms alternatives, but this is validated only for the specific pre-training regime tested. Open questions include:

- Would more sophisticated merging help if component models trained on **more divergent domains**?
- Could adaptive merging strategies (e.g., layer-wise merging weights) improve fidelity?
- Does the optimal merging method change with model scale or training duration?

### 6.7 Summary of Trade-offs

| Trade-off | DeMix Choice | Consequence |
|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| Model Scale | 1.7B components | May not generalize to 7B+ models |
| General Data | 50% per component | Cannot search specialized-dominant mixtures |
| Proxy Count | 224 optimal | Degrades at 448 (overfitting) |
| Merging Method | Linear | May not suit highly divergent domains |
| Validation | Single-stage 50B | Three-stage pipeline not directly validated |
| Domain Coverage | 7 categories | Fine-grained domains not independently optimizable |

These limitations do not invalidate DeMix's contributions but define the **boundary conditions** for its effective application. Practitioners should verify that their use case falls within these constraints before adopting the framework.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of DeMix represents a structural shift in how the community approaches pre-training data optimization. By decoupling the search process from the training process, it transforms data mixture selection from a resource-constrained bottleneck into a computationally tractable optimization problem. This section analyzes how this work changes the research landscape, identifies specific avenues for follow-up research, outlines practical applications, and provides guidance on when to integrate this method into existing pipelines.

### 7.1 Shifting the Optimization Paradigm

DeMix fundamentally alters the economics of data mixture search. Prior to this work, the fidelity of mixture optimization was directly coupled to the available training budget. Methods like RegMix and CLIMB faced a hard ceiling: to improve search accuracy, one had to train larger or more numerous proxy models, linearly increasing costs. DeMix breaks this coupling by shifting the computational burden from **training** to **inference**.

*   **From Training Budget to Inference Throughput:** As demonstrated in **Table 1**, the cost of benchmarking a merged proxy is equivalent to training only `0.013B` tokens. This allows researchers to evaluate hundreds of mixture ratios (e.g., 224 proxies in **Table 3**) for the cost of training a single small proxy. The constraint is no longer "how many models can I afford to train?" but "how many models can I afford to evaluate?"
*   **Democratization of Mixture Search:** The reported **6.4× cost reduction** (211B vs 1344B tokens for comparable accuracy, **Table 2**) lowers the barrier for independent research groups. Institutions without the resources to train hundreds of 8B proxy models can now perform rigorous mixture optimization using a fixed set of component models.
*   **Focus on Component Quality:** Since the merging process is linear and hyperparameter-free (**Table 4**), the quality of the final mixture depends almost entirely on the quality and diversity of the **7 component models**. This shifts research focus from designing complex search algorithms to curating high-quality, domain-specific datasets that produce robust weight deltas.

### 7.2 Enabling Follow-up Research

The validation of model merging in the pre-training regime opens several specific avenues for future investigation. The paper identifies gaps in **Section 6** that serve as a roadmap for next-generation methods.

*   **Scaling Laws for Model Merging:** The current experiments validate the small-update assumption ($\delta \approx 10\%$) on a **1.7B architecture** (**Section 3**). A critical follow-up direction is verifying whether $\delta$ remains small for larger models (7B, 70B, or larger). If parameter updates scale non-linearly with model size, the linear merging approximation $\Delta(D_i \cup D_j) \approx \Delta(D_i) + \Delta(D_j)$ may degrade. Research is needed to establish **merging scaling laws** that predict fidelity loss as component model size increases.
*   **Dynamic and Curriculum-Based Mixing:** DeMix optimizes static mixture ratios for defined training stages (**Appendix A.2**). However, recent work suggests that dynamic data selection during training may outperform static mixtures. Future research could integrate DeMix with online selection methods (e.g., DoReMi) by using merged proxies to rapidly evaluate **time-varying mixture schedules** without retraining.
*   **High-Dimensional Mixture Spaces:** The current implementation uses **7 candidate datasets** (**Section 4.2**). Expanding this to 20+ fine-grained domains (e.g., separating biology, chemistry, and physics instead of a single "science" category) increases the dimensionality of the simplex. Follow-up work should investigate whether the LightGBM predictor (**Section 3.4**) requires architectural changes to handle higher-dimensional input without overfitting, especially given the performance degradation observed at 448 proxies (**Table 3**).
*   **Interference Resolution in Divergent Domains:** While linear merging outperformed methods like TIES and DARE in this study (**Table 4**), this was validated on domains that are likely complementary (general, math, code). Research is needed to test if interference-resolution merging becomes necessary when combining highly divergent domains (e.g., formal logic vs. creative writing) where parameter updates might conflict rather than add.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

The release of the **DeMix Corpora** (22T tokens) and the framework itself enables several immediate practical applications for practitioners.

*   **Baseline for Specialized Model Development:** Organizations building specialized models (e.g., for code generation or mathematical reasoning) can use the **DeMix Corpora** as a validated starting point. **Table 7** shows this corpus achieves a balanced average rank of **24.00**, outperforming existing public corpora like SmolLM-Corpus (**31.33**) and Nemotron-Pretrain (**36.00**). This eliminates the need for initial mixture search for general-purpose pre-training.
*   **Efficient Domain Adaptation:** Companies with proprietary data in specific domains (e.g., legal or medical text) can train a single component model on their proprietary data and merge it with the public DeMix components. This allows them to estimate the optimal inclusion ratio of their proprietary data without training full models from scratch, leveraging the existing public components as a base.
*   **Resource-Constrained Pre-training:** For research groups with limited compute, DeMix offers a path to optimize mixtures within a fixed budget. By training only **7 component models** (e.g., on 30B tokens each), they can search the mixture space exhaustively rather than relying on heuristics or uniform distributions, which **Table 3** shows perform significantly worse ( ranks **36.67** and **42.33** respectively).

### 7.4 Integration and Reproducibility Guidance

Adopting DeMix requires careful consideration of your specific constraints and goals. The following guidance helps practitioners decide when to prefer DeMix over alternatives like RegMix, CLIMB, or heuristic approaches.

*   **When to Prefer DeMix:**
    *   **Budget Constraints:** If your total search budget is limited (e.g., <500B tokens), DeMix is superior. **Table 2** shows training-based methods require >1300B tokens to match DeMix's proxy accuracy.
    *   **Need for Search Resolution:** If you require fine-grained mixture optimization (e.g., distinguishing between 15% vs 20% code data), DeMix allows unlimited sampling. Training-based methods are limited to the number of models you can afford to train.
    *   **Balanced Capabilities:** If your goal is a generalist model balancing math, code, and language, DeMix is validated for this. **Table 3** shows it achieves the best macro-average rank.

*   **When to Consider Alternatives:**
    *   **Specialized Dominance:** If your target application requires a mixture where specialized data dominates (>50%), DeMix may not be suitable. **Table 5** shows proxy accuracy collapses when general data drops below 50% in component training, imposing a hard constraint on the searchable space.
    *   **Math-Heavy Optimization:** If mathematical reasoning is the sole priority, CLIMB with larger proxies (8B) achieved a better math rank (**11**) than DeMix (**14**) in **Table 3**. For single-domain optimization, larger training-based proxies may still be more accurate.
    *   **Large-Scale Production:** If you are pre-training a 70B+ model, the 1.7B component models used in DeMix may not capture the necessary emergent behaviors. Until scaling laws are established, heuristic mixing or large-scale proxy experiments may be safer for production-grade large models.

*   **Reproducibility Checklist:**
    *   **Base Model:** Ensure all component models share the **same initialization** ($\Theta_{\text{base}}$). Merging models trained from different initializations violates the weight delta assumption (**Section 2.3**).
    *   **General Data Regularization:** Maintain the **50% general data mix** in component training. Reducing this to 25% drops Spearman's $\rho$ from **0.787** to **0.667** (**Table 5**).
    *   **Merging Strategy:** Use **Linear Merging**. Despite the availability of complex methods, linear averaging achieved the highest capability recovery (**0.845**) and is hyperparameter-free (**Table 4**).
    *   **Proxy Count:** Limit the number of sampled proxies to **~224**. Performance degraded at 448 proxies due to overfitting (**Section 4.2**).

By adhering to these guidelines, practitioners can leverage DeMix to efficiently optimize data mixtures while avoiding the known failure modes identified in the paper. The release of the **DeMix Corpora** further ensures that these results are not just theoretical but grounded in a tangible, reusable resource for the community.
