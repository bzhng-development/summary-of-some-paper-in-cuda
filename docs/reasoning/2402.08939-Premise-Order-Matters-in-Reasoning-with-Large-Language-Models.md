## 1. Executive Summary

This paper identifies a critical fragility in Large Language Models (LLMs) where reasoning performance drops by over 30% when the order of premises is permuted, even though the logical validity of the task remains unchanged. Through systematic evaluations on deductive reasoning benchmarks and a newly introduced mathematical dataset called `R-GSM`, the authors demonstrate that models like `GPT-4-turbo`, `PaLM 2-L`, and `Gemini 1.0 Pro` achieve peak accuracy only when premises are ordered to match the sequential steps of the ground-truth proof (forward chaining). This finding matters because it reveals that current LLMs struggle with non-linear reasoning contexts, failing to robustly handle tasks that require looking back and forth across information rather than processing it strictly left-to-right.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Brittle Reasoning in Logically Invariant Tasks
The fundamental problem this paper addresses is a specific failure mode in Large Language Models (LLMs): **brittleness to premise ordering**. In formal logic and mathematics, the validity of a conclusion depends solely on the set of premises provided, not the sequence in which they are presented. For example, if a problem states "If A then B," "If B then C," and "A is true," a reasoning engine should derive "C is true" regardless of whether these statements appear as (1, 2, 3) or (3, 1, 2).

However, the authors identify that state-of-the-art LLMs violate this principle of logical invariance. The specific gap is that while LLMs have achieved near-human or superhuman performance on standard reasoning benchmarks, their success is heavily contingent on the **alignment between the input order and the reasoning path**. When premises are shuffled such that the model cannot simply read left-to-right to construct a proof, performance collapses. This suggests that current models are not performing robust, abstract reasoning but are instead relying on superficial patterns correlated with token sequence.

### Why This Matters: Theoretical and Practical Implications
The significance of this fragility extends beyond academic curiosity to critical limitations in deploying LLMs for reliable reasoning:

*   **Theoretical Significance (Auto-regressive Bias):** The findings challenge the assumption that LLMs have internalized logical rules. Instead, the results suggest that the **auto-regressive nature** of these models (predicting the next token based on previous tokens) creates a strong bias toward **forward chaining**. The models appear "comfortable" only when the context allows them to derive the next step immediately from the most recently read information. If the required premise for the next step appeared earlier in the context, the model struggles to retrieve and apply it, indicating a failure in non-linear context utilization.
*   **Real-World Impact:** In practical applications—such as legal analysis, code debugging, or scientific hypothesis testing—information is rarely presented in the optimal order for derivation. Users provide facts, constraints, and goals in natural, often disordered language. If an LLM's accuracy drops by over 30% (as observed in this study) simply because a user listed a constraint at the beginning of a prompt rather than the middle, the model cannot be trusted for high-stakes decision-making without extensive prompt engineering or preprocessing.
*   **Cognitive Bias Mimicry:** Interestingly, this flaw mirrors specific human cognitive biases where premise order affects reasoning speed and accuracy, but the paper highlights that LLMs are **far more susceptible** than humans. While humans might slow down with disordered premises, they generally maintain accuracy on simple modus ponens tasks. LLMs, conversely, fail completely, revealing a qualitative difference in how they process logical structures compared to biological intelligence.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches and Related Work
Before this study, several related phenomena had been identified, but none fully isolated the impact of premise permutation on multi-step reasoning:

*   **The Reversal Curse:** Berglund et al. (2023) identified that models trained on "A is B" often fail to infer "B is A." While this highlights an order sensitivity, it focuses on the directionality of a single factual relationship within training data, whereas this paper examines the permutation of multiple distinct premises within a single inference task.
*   **Distractibility:** Shi et al. (2023) showed that adding irrelevant context degrades performance. This paper builds on that by showing that even without irrelevant context, the *ordering* of relevant context alone is sufficient to cause failure. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that adding distracting rules **magnifies** the ordering effect, creating a compounding difficulty.
*   **Lost-in-the-Middle:** Liu et al. (2024) found that models struggle to utilize information placed in the middle of long contexts. The authors of this paper explicitly distinguish their findings from this phenomenon. Their logical reasoning benchmarks involve short contexts (under 300 tokens), well within the range where "lost-in-the-middle" should not occur. Appendix D of the paper confirms via ablation studies that placing relevant rules at the beginning, middle, or end yields similar results *if the relative order is preserved*, proving that the issue is the **sequence relative to the proof steps**, not the absolute position in the context window.
*   **Scrambled Text Robustness:** Prior work (e.g., Cao et al., 2023) suggested LLMs are robust to scrambled words or unnatural text. This paper counters that while models can handle syntactic noise, they are surprisingly fragile to **semantic reordering** that preserves grammatical correctness but disrupts the logical flow.

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself as a systematic isolation of the **premise order variable**. Unlike previous works that observed general reasoning failures or context length limitations, this study:
1.  **Constructs Controlled Benchmarks:** It adapts the `SimpleLogic` dataset to create variants where the *only* variable changed is the permutation of premises, quantified by the **Kendall tau distance ($\tau$)** relative to the ground-truth proof order.
2.  **Introduces `R-GSM`:** It extends the investigation beyond synthetic logic to real-world mathematical word problems by creating `R-GSM`, a reordered version of the `GSM8K` benchmark. This demonstrates that the ordering effect is not limited to formal logic but permeates natural language mathematical reasoning.
3.  **Differentiates Model Behaviors:** It reveals that while all tested models (`GPT-4-turbo`, `PaLM 2-L`, `Gemini 1.0 Pro`, `GPT-3.5-turbo`) prefer the forward order, they exhibit distinct secondary preferences. For instance, GPT-4 shows a surprising competence with **backward ordering** (reverse of the proof), suggesting it can sometimes perform backward chaining, whereas `PaLM 2-L` fails catastrophically in that same configuration.

By rigorously controlling for these factors, the paper moves the discourse from "LLMs make reasoning errors" to "LLMs fail specifically when the input structure does not align with their inherent left-to-right derivation bias," providing a precise diagnosis of the current architectural limitations.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a systematic empirical study rather than a new model architecture, utilizing controlled benchmark construction to isolate the variable of premise ordering in Large Language Models (LLMs). The core idea is to generate multiple variants of the same logical or mathematical problem where the set of facts remains identical, but their sequence is permuted according to specific metrics, allowing the authors to measure performance degradation as a function of order misalignment.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The "system" under investigation is not a new AI model, but rather a rigorous experimental framework designed to stress-test existing state-of-the-art LLMs (such as `GPT-4-turbo` and `PaLM 2-L`) by feeding them logically equivalent problems with shuffled sentence orders. This approach solves the problem of distinguishing between genuine reasoning capabilities and superficial pattern matching by ensuring that any drop in accuracy can be attributed solely to the order of information, not the complexity of the logic itself.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The experimental pipeline consists of three primary components operating in sequence:
1.  **Problem Generation & Permutation Engine:** This component takes a base logical or mathematical problem and algorithmically generates variants by reordering the premises based on their alignment with the ground-truth proof steps, quantified by the Kendall tau distance ($\tau$).
2.  **Inference Interface:** This module feeds the generated problem variants into various pre-trained LLMs using a strict zero-shot prompting strategy with greedy decoding, requesting explicit step-by-step derivations.
3.  **Verification & Error Analysis Module:** This final component parses the model outputs to check for valid proofs, categorizing failures into specific error types such as "fact hallucination" or "wrong refutation," and aggregates accuracy statistics across different ordering configurations.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   First, we define the **Logical Reasoning Benchmark**, explaining how the authors adapted `SimpleLogic` to create a controlled environment where premise order is the only variable, and introducing the mathematical metric ($\tau$) used to quantify this order.
*   Second, we detail the construction of **R-GSM**, the mathematical reasoning dataset derived from `GSM8K`, which validates whether the ordering effect persists in natural language word problems beyond synthetic logic.
*   Third, we outline the **Experimental Configuration**, specifying the models tested, the decoding parameters (temperature, sampling), and the strict evaluation criteria used to determine correctness.
*   Fourth, we explain the **Error Taxonomy**, describing how the authors classify model failures to understand the mechanistic reasons behind the ordering sensitivity (e.g., sequential bias vs. retrieval failure).

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### The Logical Reasoning Benchmark Design
The authors construct a dedicated benchmark for deductive reasoning by adapting the `SimpleLogic` dataset, specifically restricting the problem space to propositional logic with definite clauses to ensure all problems are solvable via **modus ponens** (the rule: if $P \implies Q$ and $P$ is true, then $Q$ is true). Unlike the original `SimpleLogic` which treats reasoning as a binary classification task (True/False), this study requires the LLM to generate a complete, step-by-step proof, marking any output as incorrect if it contains hallucinated rules, hallucinated facts, or fails to derive the conclusion. Each problem instance consists of three distinct components: a set of initial facts ($A_1, \dots, A_n$), a set of conditional rules (e.g., "If $X$ and $Y$, then $Z$"), and a target conclusion ($C$) that must be proven true.

To systematically vary the premise order, the authors define a "forward order" as the specific sequence where premises appear in the exact order they are consumed by the ground-truth proof (i.e., the rule needed for step 1 appears first, the rule for step 2 appears second, etc.). They then quantify the deviation from this ideal order using the **Kendall tau distance** ($\tau$), a statistical measure of correlation between two rankings, normalized here to the range $[-1, 1]$. A value of $\tau = 1$ represents the perfect forward order, while $\tau = -1$ represents the "backward order" (the exact reverse of the proof sequence), and $\tau \approx 0$ indicates a random or uncorrelated ordering. The benchmark explicitly evaluates models at five specific points along this spectrum: $\tau \in \{1, 0.5, 0, -0.5, -1\}$.

The complexity of the logical tasks is scaled along two dimensions to test the limits of the ordering effect. First, the **number of required rules** in the ground-truth proof varies from 4 to 12, testing whether the ordering penalty compounds as the reasoning chain lengthens. Second, the authors introduce **distracting rules**—valid logical statements that are irrelevant to the specific conclusion—to simulate real-world noise; experiments are run with 0, 5, and 10 distracting rules interleaved with the relevant ones. For each combination of rule count and distraction level, the authors generate 200 base problems, and by creating 15 ordering variants per problem, the total dataset comprises approximately 27,000 unique logical reasoning instances.

#### Construction of the R-GSM Mathematical Benchmark
To verify that the premise order effect is not an artifact of synthetic logic datasets, the authors introduce **R-GSM**, a reordered version of the standard `GSM8K` grade-school math benchmark. The construction process begins by filtering the `GSM8K` test set to include only problems with at least 5 sentences in the description, ensuring sufficient length for reordering to have an impact. The authors then manually identify problems where the sentence order can be permuted without altering the causal logic or the ground-truth answer, excluding cases where the narrative relies on a strict chronological sequence of events (e.g., "John bought apples, then ate them").

For each selected problem, the last sentence (which typically contains the question) is kept fixed, while the preceding sentences describing the scenario are rewritten in a different order. This rewriting involves minor grammatical edits to ensure fluency but strictly preserves the numerical values and logical relationships. The authors employed an automated assistance tool to enumerate potential permutations and identify those that caused model failures, which were then manually verified and refined. The final **R-GSM** dataset contains 220 pairs of problems (original and reordered), with problem lengths ranging from 5 to 8 sentences and reasoning steps ranging from 2 to 8 steps, providing a realistic testbed for mathematical reasoning under disordered contexts.

#### Experimental Configuration and Inference Protocol
The study evaluates four state-of-the-art Large Language Models: `GPT-4-turbo`, `GPT-3.5-turbo` (by OpenAI), `PaLM 2-L` (by Google), and `Gemini 1.0 Pro` (by Google). To isolate the effect of premise ordering from other variables, the authors employ a **zero-shot prompting** strategy, meaning no examples are provided in the prompt to guide the model's format. For the logical reasoning tasks, the prompt includes a specific instruction asking the model to provide a derivation that explicitly states which premise is used at each step, whereas the R-GSM tasks are presented with only the problem description and no additional instructions.

The inference process uses **greedy decoding** with a temperature setting of $0$, ensuring that the models produce deterministic outputs for repeated runs and eliminating randomness as a confounding factor in the accuracy measurements. The evaluation metric is strict: for logical reasoning, a prediction is correct only if the generated proof is completely valid and leads to the conclusion without hallucinating non-existent facts or rules; for R-GSM, the numerical answer must match the ground truth. This rigorous setup ensures that partial credit is not awarded for correct answers derived via flawed logic, which is crucial for detecting whether the model is truly reasoning or merely guessing based on token proximity.

#### Error Taxonomy and Analysis Methodology
To understand the mechanisms behind the performance drops, the authors categorize model failures into three distinct error types based on the output analysis. The first category, **wrong refutation**, occurs when the LLM incorrectly claims that the conclusion cannot be proved, essentially giving up on the task despite the necessary information being present. The second category, **rule hallucination**, involves the model generating conditional rules that do not exist in the provided prompt, often inventing logical bridges to connect disjointed premises. The third and most prevalent category is **fact hallucination**, where the model asserts intermediate facts as true without valid derivation, typically because it attempts to force a sequential application of rules that do not logically follow in the given order.

In the context of R-GSM, the error analysis focuses on two specific failure modes related to sequential processing bias. The **temporal order** error occurs when the model blindly processes numbers in the order they appear in the text, ignoring the actual chronological or logical dependencies described (e.g., using a value before it has been defined in the narrative). The **unknown variable** error happens when the model attempts a calculation step requiring a quantity that has not yet been introduced in the current sequential scan, revealing an inability to look ahead or retrieve information from later in the context. By quantifying the frequency of these specific errors across different $\tau$ values, the authors link the performance degradation directly to the models' inability to perform non-linear context retrieval.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper moves beyond simply documenting that LLMs fail; it provides a precise diagnosis of *how* and *why* they fail when the structure of information conflicts with their auto-regressive nature. The following insights represent fundamental shifts in our understanding of LLM reasoning capabilities, distinguishing between superficial pattern matching and robust logical deduction.

### 4.1 The Discovery of "Forward-Chaining Dependency" as a Structural Limit
While prior work established that LLMs struggle with long contexts or irrelevant information, this paper isolates a more granular and damaging flaw: **Forward-Chaining Dependency**. This is the finding that LLMs do not merely "prefer" ordered premises; they structurally depend on them to function.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** Previous studies on the "Reversal Curse" (Berglund et al., 2023) focused on the directionality of learned facts (e.g., knowing "A is B" but not "B is A"). Others, like Shi et al. (2023), showed that *distractors* hurt performance. This paper demonstrates that even in the **absence** of distractors, simply permuting the order of *necessary* premises causes accuracy to collapse. For instance, on the logical benchmark with 12 rules, `GPT-3.5-turbo` drops from **30.0%** accuracy in the forward order ($\tau=1$) to **1.2%** in a shuffled order ($\tau \approx 0$), despite the logical difficulty being identical (Table 6).
*   **Significance:** This reveals that current LLMs are not performing abstract reasoning over a set of propositions. Instead, they are executing a fragile, linear scan where the validity of step $N$ depends heavily on premise $N$ appearing immediately after premise $N-1$ in the token stream. This suggests that "reasoning" in these models is often a proxy for "sequential pattern completion." If the input does not align with the derivation path, the model cannot "look back" effectively to retrieve the necessary antecedent, leading to a systemic failure mode that is distinct from simple forgetting or distraction.

### 4.2 Divergent Reasoning Architectures Revealed by Backward Ordering
A subtle but profound innovation in this work is the use of **backward ordering** ($\tau = -1$) as a diagnostic tool to reveal architectural differences between models that appear similar on standard benchmarks. By reversing the premise order to align with *backward chaining* (starting from the goal and working back to facts), the authors uncover distinct reasoning strategies.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** Most benchmarks treat all errors as equal failures. This paper shows that the *type* of failure under reversed ordering varies significantly by model.
    *   **`GPT-4-turbo`** exhibits a unique capability: it often performs *better* on backward-ordered premises ($\tau=-1$) than on randomly shuffled ones ($\tau=0$). For problems with 12 rules and no distractors, `GPT-4-turbo` maintains **84.0%** accuracy in the backward order, compared to **80.8%** in the shuffled order (Table 6). This suggests `GPT-4` has implicitly learned to perform backward chaining or can effectively reverse its attention mechanism to suit the input structure.
    *   **`PaLM 2-L`**, conversely, treats backward ordering as catastrophically as random shuffling, dropping from **88.0%** (forward) to **57.5%** (backward) (Table 6).
*   **Significance:** This insight proves that "reasoning ability" is not a monolithic scalar. Some models (like GPT-4) have developed flexible inference strategies that can adapt to the input flow (either forward or backward), while others are rigidly locked into a single forward-pass heuristic. This finding provides a new metric for evaluating model sophistication: not just "how accurate is it?", but "how invariant is it to the direction of logical flow?"

### 4.3 The Mechanism of Failure: Sequential Hallucination vs. Retrieval Failure
The paper provides a mechanistic explanation for *why* shuffling causes failure, moving the conversation from "the model gets confused" to "the model hallucinates to preserve sequentiality." The error analysis reveals that the primary failure mode is not an inability to find the right rule, but an **active fabrication of facts** to force a linear derivation.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** Standard error analysis often groups all incorrect outputs together. Here, the authors dissect the errors to show that **fact hallucination** is the dominant error type and scales inversely with $\tau$. As the premise order deviates from the forward chain, fact hallucination spikes. For `GPT-3.5-turbo` with 12 rules, fact hallucination rises from **35.5%** in the forward order to **47.0%** in the backward order (Table 1).
*   **Significance:** This indicates that the model's objective function prioritizes maintaining a coherent, left-to-right narrative flow over logical fidelity. When the next required premise is not immediately available in the sequence, the model invents an intermediate fact to bridge the gap, allowing it to continue its auto-regressive generation without breaking stride. This "hallucination to maintain flow" is a critical vulnerability for safety-critical applications, as the model produces confident, step-by-step proofs that are logically unsound at the foundational level.

### 4.4 Generalization to Natural Language: The R-GSM Benchmark
Perhaps the most impactful practical contribution is the demonstration that this fragility is not confined to synthetic logic puzzles but permeates natural language mathematical reasoning. The creation of **R-GSM** proves that reordering sentences in a standard word problem—without changing any numbers or logic—causes significant performance degradation.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** Prior robustness checks often involved scrambling words or adding noise (Cao et al., 2023), which are syntactically unnatural. R-GSM maintains perfect grammatical and semantic validity, altering only the *informational sequence*. The results show that even `GPT-4-turbo`, which solves **94.1%** of original GSM8K problems, drops to **85.0%** on the reordered versions (Table 2). For weaker models like `GPT-3.5-turbo`, the drop is from **67.3%** to **51.8%**.
*   **Significance:** This invalidates the assumption that high performance on benchmarks like GSM8K indicates robust mathematical understanding. It suggests that current SOTA models are heavily reliant on the *narrative structure* of word problems (where information is typically presented in the order of usage). In real-world scenarios where users describe problems non-linearly (e.g., stating the goal first, then providing constraints), these models will underperform significantly. This insight forces a re-evaluation of how we benchmark "reasoning," suggesting that true robustness requires invariance to narrative structure.

### 4.5 Decoupling "Position Bias" from "Order Sensitivity"
Finally, the paper rigorously disentangles the premise order effect from the recently discovered "Lost-in-the-Middle" phenomenon (Liu et al., 2024), clarifying a common misconception in the field.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** One might assume that shuffling premises hurts performance simply because relevant information gets buried in the middle of the context window. The authors explicitly test this in Appendix D by placing relevant rules at the beginning, middle, and end of the context while *preserving their relative order*. They find that performance remains stable regardless of absolute position (e.g., `PaLM 2-L` achieves ~67-68% accuracy regardless of whether the forward-ordered block is at the start, middle, or end).
*   **Significance:** This confirms that the performance drop is driven strictly by the **relative sequence** of premises ($\tau$), not their absolute position in the context window. This is a crucial distinction for system designers: simply moving important facts to the start or end of a prompt (a common mitigation for "Lost-in-the-Middle") will **not** solve the premise order problem if the internal logic of those facts is still presented in a non-derivable sequence. The solution requires reordering the logic itself, not just relocating the text block.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence supporting the paper's central claim: that Large Language Models (LLMs) are structurally dependent on premise ordering. We move beyond the high-level trends to examine the specific quantitative breakdowns, error taxonomies, and ablation studies that reveal the mechanics of this failure. The analysis covers two distinct domains—synthetic logical reasoning and natural language mathematical problem solving—to demonstrate the universality of the phenomenon.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

The experimental design rigorously isolates **premise order** as the independent variable while holding logical complexity constant.

*   **Datasets:**
    *   **Logical Reasoning Benchmark:** Adapted from `SimpleLogic`, this dataset contains propositional logic problems requiring modus ponens. The key innovation is the generation of 15 variants per problem, systematically permuted to achieve specific **Kendall tau distances ($\tau$)** relative to the ground-truth proof. The values tested are $\tau \in \{1, 0.5, 0, -0.5, -1\}$, where $\tau=1$ is the ideal forward chain and $\tau=-1$ is the exact reverse. Complexity is scaled by varying the number of required rules (4 to 12) and the number of distracting rules (0, 5, or 10). The total corpus comprises **27,000 problems**.
    *   **R-GSM:** A novel benchmark derived from **GSM8K**. The authors selected 220 problems with 5–8 sentences where sentence order could be permuted without altering the causal logic or answer. Each problem exists as a pair: the original GSM8K version and a manually rewritten "reordered" version.
*   **Models:** Four state-of-the-art models were evaluated: `GPT-4-turbo`, `GPT-3.5-turbo`, `PaLM 2-L`, and `Gemini 1.0 Pro`.
*   **Protocol:** All experiments used **zero-shot prompting** with **greedy decoding** (temperature $T=0$) to ensure deterministic outputs. For logical reasoning, prompts explicitly requested a step-by-step derivation citing the premises used.
*   **Metrics:**
    *   **Logical Reasoning:** Strict accuracy. A prediction is correct *only* if the generated proof is valid, uses only existing facts/rules, and reaches the conclusion. Any hallucination or logical gap renders it incorrect.
    *   **R-GSM:** Numerical accuracy (exact match with ground truth).

### 5.2 Logical Reasoning: The Collapse of Accuracy Under Permutation

The results on the logical reasoning benchmark provide the most granular evidence of the ordering effect. The data reveals that accuracy is not merely "noisy" under shuffling; it systematically collapses as the premise order deviates from the forward chain.

#### The Magnitude of the Drop
The performance gap between the optimal order ($\tau=1$) and suboptimal orders is stark, particularly as problem complexity increases.
*   **Scale of Failure:** For problems requiring **12 rules** with no distractors, `GPT-3.5-turbo` achieves **30.0%** accuracy in the forward order but plummets to **1.2%** in a shuffled order ($\tau \approx 0$) (Table 6d). Similarly, `Gemini 1.0 Pro` drops from **16.5%** to **0.2%** (Table 6c).
*   **Model Hierarchy:** Stronger models are more robust but still significantly affected. `GPT-4-turbo` maintains **96.5%** accuracy in the forward order for 12-rule problems but drops to **80.8%** when shuffled (Table 6a). This represents a **~16 percentage point** drop solely due to reordering, despite the logical task being identical.
*   **Complexity Amplification:** Figure 3 and Table 6 show that the ordering effect is negligible for short proofs (4–5 rules), where all models perform near 100% regardless of order. However, as the number of rules increases to 10–12, the divergence between forward and shuffled orders widens dramatically. This confirms that the "forward-chaining dependency" becomes a bottleneck only when the reasoning chain exceeds the model's immediate working memory or attention span for non-sequential retrieval.

#### The Impact of Distracting Rules
The presence of irrelevant premises (distractors) acts as a force multiplier for the ordering effect.
*   **Compounding Difficulty:** When 10 distracting rules are added to 12-rule problems, `PaLM 2-L`'s accuracy in the forward order drops from **88.0%** (0 distractors) to **36.5%** (Table 6b vs. Table 8b). In the shuffled order, it collapses further to **18.2%** (Table 8b).
*   **Mechanism:** The distractors force the model to perform premise selection *in addition* to reordering. Since the model struggles to look back and forth, the presence of irrelevant rules interspersed with relevant ones increases the likelihood that the model will grab a nearby but incorrect rule to maintain its linear flow, leading to hallucination.

#### Divergent Behaviors: The Backward Chaining Anomaly
A critical insight emerges when analyzing the **backward order** ($\tau = -1$). Not all non-forward orders are treated equally by all models.
*   **GPT-4-turbo's Flexibility:** Unlike other models, `GPT-4-turbo` shows a distinct preference for the backward order over random shuffling. For 12-rule problems without distractors, it achieves **84.0%** accuracy in the backward order, compared to **80.8%** in the shuffled order (Table 6a). In some configurations (e.g., 4–6 rules), its backward accuracy matches or exceeds its forward accuracy (Table 6a). This suggests `GPT-4` has implicitly learned **backward chaining** strategies or possesses an attention mechanism capable of reversing the logical flow.
*   **PaLM 2-L's Rigidity:** In contrast, `PaLM 2-L` treats the backward order as catastrophically as random noise. Its accuracy drops monotonically as $\tau$ decreases from 1 to -1. For 12 rules, it scores **57.5%** in the backward order, significantly lower than `GPT-4` (Table 6b).
*   **Implication:** This divergence indicates that "reasoning capability" is not a single dimension. `GPT-4` demonstrates a more flexible inference engine that can adapt to the input structure (forward or backward), whereas `PaLM 2-L` and `Gemini` appear locked into a strict left-to-right heuristic.

#### Error Taxonomy: Hallucination as a Coping Mechanism
The error analysis in **Table 1** provides the "smoking gun" for *why* shuffling causes failure. The dominant error type is not "giving up" (wrong refutation) but **fact hallucination**.
*   **Escalation of Hallucination:** As the premise order deviates from the forward chain ($\tau$ decreases), the rate of fact hallucination increases. For `GPT-3.5-turbo` with 12 rules, fact hallucination rises from **35.5%** in the forward order to **47.0%** in the backward order. For `Gemini 1.0 Pro`, it jumps from **50.5%** to **60.5%**.
*   **The "Sequential Narrative" Bias:** This pattern supports the hypothesis that LLMs prioritize maintaining a coherent, left-to-right narrative flow over logical fidelity. When the next required premise is not immediately available in the sequence, the model invents an intermediate fact to bridge the gap, allowing it to continue generating text without breaking its auto-regressive stride. The model would rather hallucinate a fact than pause to retrieve a distant premise.

### 5.3 R-GSM: Generalization to Natural Language Mathematics

The paper validates that this fragility is not an artifact of synthetic logic datasets by testing on **R-GSM**, a reordered version of grade-school math word problems.

#### Quantitative Degradation
The results in **Table 2** confirm that reordering sentences in natural language problems causes significant accuracy drops across all models.
*   **Overall Drop:** `GPT-4-turbo` drops from **94.1%** on original GSM8K problems to **85.0%** on reordered versions. `GPT-3.5-turbo` suffers a massive drop from **67.3%** to **51.8%**.
*   **The "Solved Subset" Metric:** To isolate the effect of reordering from inherent difficulty, Table 2b analyzes only the subset of problems that each model solved correctly in the original format. Even on this "easy" subset, models fail on the reordered versions. `GPT-3.5-turbo` fails on **35.1%** of problems it originally solved (dropping from 100% to 64.9% on this subset). This proves that the failure is strictly due to the *presentation order*, not the mathematical complexity.

#### Complexity and Length Sensitivity
Figures 7 and 8 (supported by Tables 12 and 13) show that the ordering effect scales with problem complexity.
*   **Reasoning Steps:** For `GPT-4-turbo`, the gap between original and reordered accuracy widens as the number of reasoning steps increases. For problems with $\ge 6$ steps, the gap is **16.3%** (89.8% vs. 73.5%), whereas for $\ge 2$ steps, it is **9.1%** (Table 12a).
*   **Sentence Count:** Similarly, longer problem descriptions (more sentences) exacerbate the issue. For `Gemini 1.0 Pro`, the drop for problems with $\ge 7$ sentences is **18.2%** (72.7% vs. 54.5%), compared to **11.4%** for problems with $\ge 5$ sentences (Table 13c).
*   **Interpretation:** Longer contexts require more non-linear retrieval (looking back to earlier sentences for values defined previously). As the distance between the definition of a variable and its usage increases due to reordering, the model's ability to track state degrades.

#### Specific Failure Modes in Math
The error analysis in **Table 3** categorizes *how* models fail on R-GSM:
1.  **Temporal Order Errors (Dominant):** The most common error (e.g., **45.0%** for `GPT-4-turbo`) occurs when the model blindly processes numbers in the order they appear in the text, ignoring the actual chronological or logical dependencies. For example, if a problem states "John has 5 apples" in the last sentence but asks for a calculation in the first step, the model often fails to retrieve the "5" and instead uses a number from an earlier, irrelevant sentence.
2.  **Unknown Variable Errors:** This occurs when the model attempts a calculation requiring a quantity that hasn't been introduced yet in its sequential scan. As seen in **Figure 9**, `GPT-3.5-turbo` tries to calculate the number of gerbils using the number of rabbits because the sentence defining the fish (needed for the gerbil calculation) appears later in the text. The model cannot "look ahead" to find the missing variable.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

To ensure the observed effects are truly due to premise ordering and not confounding factors, the authors conducted critical ablation studies.

#### Ruling Out "Lost-in-the-Middle"
A potential alternative explanation is the "Lost-in-the-Middle" phenomenon (Liu et al., 2024), where models ignore information placed in the middle of long contexts. The authors explicitly test this in **Appendix D (Table 5)**.
*   **Experiment:** They varied the *absolute position* of the relevant rule block (beginning, middle, end) while keeping the *relative order* of premises fixed (Forward, Backward, Shuffled).
*   **Result:** For `PaLM 2-L` with 8 relevant rules, accuracy remained stable regardless of position: **68.0%** (beginning), **67.0%** (middle), **67.0%** (end) for the forward order. However, changing the *relative order* from Forward to Backward caused a massive drop to **~40%**, regardless of position.
*   **Conclusion:** The performance drop is driven by the **sequence relative to the proof steps** ($\tau$), not the absolute position in the context window. Since the logical reasoning inputs are short (<300 tokens), context length is not the limiting factor; the limitation is the model's inability to reorder information internally.

#### Consistency Across Models
While the magnitude varies, the *direction* of the effect is consistent across all four tested models (`GPT-4`, `GPT-3.5`, `PaLM 2`, `Gemini`). This universality suggests the issue is fundamental to the **auto-regressive transformer architecture** or the training objective (next-token prediction), rather than a quirk of a specific model's training data.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Claims

Do the experiments convincingly support the paper's claims?

*   **Strength of Evidence:** The evidence is overwhelming. The use of the Kendall tau metric allows for a precise, continuous measurement of the ordering effect, moving beyond binary "shuffled vs. ordered" comparisons. The sheer scale of the dataset (27k logical problems) and the strict evaluation criteria (requiring valid proofs, not just correct answers) eliminate ambiguity. The drop from ~90% to ~1% for weaker models on shuffled 12-rule problems is too large to be statistical noise.
*   **Causality:** The ablation study in Appendix D successfully rules out the "Lost-in-the-Middle" confounder, isolating premise order as the causal factor. The error analysis (hallucination rates scaling with $\tau$) provides a mechanistic explanation that aligns with the auto-regressive nature of LLMs.
*   **Generalizability:** The replication of results on R-GSM confirms that this is not just a synthetic logic puzzle issue but a fundamental limitation in handling natural language reasoning tasks where information is rarely presented in optimal logical order.
*   **Limitations:** The study focuses on **deductive** reasoning (modus ponens) and **grade-school math**. It does not address inductive reasoning or more complex mathematical proofs where the "ground truth order" might be ambiguous or non-unique. Additionally, the R-GSM dataset is relatively small (220 pairs) compared to the logical benchmark, though the effect sizes are large enough to be statistically significant.

In summary, the experimental analysis rigorously demonstrates that current LLMs are **brittle reasoners** whose performance is inextricably linked to the alignment between input token sequence and the logical derivation path. They do not reason over a set of facts; they reason over a *sequence* of tokens, and when that sequence breaks the chain of inference, they resort to hallucination to maintain the flow.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper provides a rigorous diagnosis of premise order sensitivity, its findings are bounded by specific experimental constraints and scope choices. Understanding these limitations is crucial for correctly interpreting the severity of the flaw and avoiding overgeneralization to reasoning domains not covered in the study.

### 6.1 Scope of Reasoning: Deductive vs. Inductive and Abductive
The most significant constraint of this work is its exclusive focus on **deductive reasoning**, specifically problems solvable via **modus ponens** (if $P \implies Q$ and $P$, then $Q$).
*   **The Assumption:** The study assumes that the "ground truth proof" has a unique, linear sequence of steps. This allows the authors to define a clear "forward order" ($\tau=1$) and measure deviation from it.
*   **The Gap:** This framework does not address **inductive reasoning** (generalizing from examples) or **abductive reasoning** (inferring the best explanation), where the "correct" order of premises is often ambiguous or non-existent. In these domains, there may be no single $\tau=1$ configuration to serve as a baseline. Consequently, we cannot determine from this paper whether the observed brittleness is a fundamental flaw in handling *any* disordered information or specifically a failure to align with a *pre-determined* logical chain.
*   **Implication:** The claim that "premise order matters" is proven for tasks with a rigid derivation path. It remains an open question whether models exhibit similar catastrophic drops when the reasoning path is flexible or when multiple valid proof orders exist.

### 6.2 Context Length and the "Short-Context" Regime
The experiments are conducted in a regime of relatively short contexts, which limits the ability to disentangle order sensitivity from long-context retrieval failures.
*   **Evidence:** As noted in **Appendix D**, the longest inputs in the logical reasoning benchmark (12 relevant rules + 10 distracting rules) contain **no more than 300 tokens**. This is well within the context window limits of all tested models (which typically support thousands of tokens).
*   **The Trade-off:** By keeping contexts short, the authors successfully isolate the *ordering* effect from the *retrieval* effect (i.e., ensuring the model isn't failing simply because it "forgot" information buried in a massive document). However, this creates a blind spot: we do not know if the premise order effect **compounds** with the "Lost-in-the-Middle" phenomenon in longer contexts (e.g., 10k+ tokens).
*   **Open Question:** Does the penalty for shuffling premises diminish when the context is so long that *no* ordering allows for perfect linear processing? Or does the ordering effect remain the dominant failure mode regardless of context length? The paper leaves this interaction unexplored.

### 6.3 Dataset Constraints: Synthetic Logic and Small-Scale Math
The generalizability of the findings relies on two datasets with distinct limitations regarding scale and complexity.
*   **Logical Benchmark (SimpleLogic adaptation):** While the dataset is large (27k problems), the problems are **synthetically generated** and restricted to propositional logic. Real-world logical reasoning (e.g., legal contracts, code verification) often involves nested quantifiers, negation, and complex semantic dependencies that are not captured by simple definite clauses. The clean, noise-free nature of these synthetic problems may exaggerate the ordering effect compared to messy real-world data where models might rely on semantic cues rather than strict structural alignment.
*   **R-GSM Scale:** The **R-GSM** dataset, used to validate findings in natural language, contains only **220 problem pairs**. While the performance drops are statistically significant (e.g., `GPT-3.5-turbo` dropping from 67.3% to 51.8%), the small sample size limits the granularity of the analysis. For instance, the breakdown by reasoning steps in **Table 12** shows sparse data for problems with $\ge 6$ steps (only 23 problems total in the dataset, per **Table 4**). This makes it difficult to draw robust conclusions about how the ordering effect scales for highly complex mathematical proofs requiring 10+ steps.

### 6.4 The "Ground Truth Order" Ambiguity
The entire methodology hinges on the ability to define a single **ground truth proof order** to calculate the Kendall tau distance ($\tau$).
*   **The Assumption:** The paper assumes that for every problem, there is one optimal sequence of premises that matches the human-derived ground truth proof.
*   **The Limitation:** In many reasoning tasks, multiple valid proof paths exist. For example, if a problem provides premises $A \implies B$, $B \implies C$, $A \implies D$, and $D \implies C$, one could prove $C$ via $B$ or via $D$. In such cases, a permutation that is "shuffled" relative to proof path 1 might be the "forward order" for proof path 2.
*   **Consequence:** The metric $\tau$ may misclassify valid alternative reasoning paths as "disordered," potentially inflating the measured error rate. The paper does not account for models that successfully find an *alternative* valid proof path that differs from the specific ground truth annotation used to generate the $\tau$ values.

### 6.5 Lack of Mitigation Strategies
A critical trade-off in the paper's design is its focus on **diagnosis over solution**.
*   **The Gap:** The authors explicitly state in the Introduction and Conclusion that they leave "proposing new training and modeling techniques to mitigate the premise order effect as future work."
*   **Implication:** While the paper convincingly demonstrates *that* the problem exists and *why* it likely happens (auto-regressive bias), it offers no empirical evidence on how to fix it. Common prompt engineering techniques like "Chain-of-Thought" were not systematically tested as a mitigation strategy in this specific context (the study uses zero-shot prompting with a request for derivation, but does not compare this against few-shot CoT examples that might model reordering behavior).
*   **Open Question:** Can simple interventions, such as asking the model to "list all facts first" or "reorder the premises logically before solving," recover the lost performance? The paper identifies the fragility but stops short of testing the resilience of current mitigation strategies.

### 6.6 Model Selection and Training Data Opacity
The study evaluates four specific models (`GPT-4-turbo`, `GPT-3.5-turbo`, `PaLM 2-L`, `Gemini 1.0 Pro`), but the inability to access their training data or architectural details limits the depth of the causal analysis.
*   **The Constraint:** The authors attribute the differences in performance (e.g., `GPT-4`'s backward chaining capability vs. `PaLM 2`'s rigidity) to "auto-regressive model design or the reasoning bias learned from the training corpus." However, without access to the training data mixture or specific architectural variations (e.g., attention head configurations), this remains a hypothesis.
*   **Limitation:** We cannot determine if `GPT-4`'s superior performance on backward ordering is due to a specific architectural innovation, a larger scale, or simply exposure to more diverse reasoning patterns in its training data. This opacity prevents the community from deriving specific design principles to build more robust models based solely on this paper's findings.

In summary, while the paper conclusively proves that premise order is a critical vulnerability in deductive reasoning tasks with unique solution paths, its findings are bounded by short context lengths, synthetic problem structures, and a lack of explored mitigations. The extent to which these findings translate to open-ended, multi-path, or long-context reasoning remains an important area for future investigation.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The findings of this paper fundamentally alter our understanding of Large Language Model (LLM) reasoning capabilities, shifting the narrative from "models can reason" to "models can reason only when the input structure aligns with their auto-regressive bias." This section outlines how this discovery reshapes the research landscape, identifies critical avenues for future inquiry, and provides concrete guidance for practitioners deploying these models in real-world scenarios.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Landscape: From Robustness to Structural Fragility

This work forces a paradigm shift in how the community evaluates and interprets LLM performance on reasoning benchmarks.

*   **Re-evaluating Benchmark Scores:** High accuracy on standard datasets like `GSM8K` or `SimpleLogic` can no longer be taken as evidence of robust logical understanding. As demonstrated by the **R-GSM** results, where `GPT-4-turbo` drops from **94.1%** to **85.0%** simply by reordering sentences (Table 2), current benchmarks likely overestimate model capability because they implicitly favor the "forward order" bias present in human-written problems. Future evaluations must treat premise order as a controlled variable; a model is not truly robust unless its performance is invariant to $\tau$ (Kendall tau distance).
*   **The Myth of Abstract Reasoning:** The paper dismantles the assumption that LLMs perform abstract manipulation of logical sets. The drastic increase in **fact hallucination** as $\tau$ decreases (Table 1) reveals that models are not retrieving and applying rules from a global context window. Instead, they are executing a fragile, local **sequential narrative**. When the next logical step is not immediately available in the token stream, the model fabricates facts to maintain the flow. This suggests that "reasoning" in current LLMs is better described as **context-aware pattern completion** rather than deductive inference.
*   **Architectural Diagnosis:** The divergence between `GPT-4-turbo` (which handles backward ordering $\tau=-1$ reasonably well) and `PaLM 2-L` (which fails catastrophically on it) provides a new lens for comparing model architectures. It suggests that scaling or specific training regimes (likely in the OpenAI family) have enabled a form of **bidirectional inference** or flexible attention retrieval that other models lack. This moves the field toward analyzing *how* models traverse context, not just *what* they predict.

### 7.2 Critical Avenues for Future Research

The identification of premise order sensitivity opens several high-priority research directions aimed at both understanding the root cause and developing mitigations.

#### A. Architectural Interventions for Non-Linear Retrieval
The primary hypothesis for this failure is the **auto-regressive constraint**, which forces the model to process information strictly left-to-right. Future work should explore:
*   **Iterative Refinement Loops:** Investigating architectures that allow the model to perform multiple passes over the context—first to build an internal representation (e.g., a graph of facts and rules) and second to perform deduction—could break the linear dependency.
*   **Attention Mechanism Modifications:** Research into attention heads specifically trained to retrieve antecedents regardless of position could mitigate the "look-back" failure. The success of `GPT-4` on backward ordering suggests this is learnable; explicit architectural inductive biases could generalize this to all models.
*   **Non-Autoregressive Decoding for Reasoning:** Exploring decoding strategies that allow the model to generate proof steps in an order independent of the input sequence might reduce the pressure to hallucinate intermediate facts.

#### B. Training Objectives and Data Curation
The paper notes that the effect may stem from biases in the training corpus, where natural language narratives typically present premises in the order of usage.
*   **Counterfactual Data Augmentation:** A direct follow-up is to train or fine-tune models on datasets where premises are systematically shuffled (varying $\tau$). If models are exposed to valid proofs derived from randomly ordered premises during training, they may learn to decouple input order from derivation order.
*   **Synthetic Logic Curricula:** Incorporating the **Logical Reasoning Benchmark** (27k problems) directly into pre-training or instruction tuning could force models to develop robust premise selection mechanisms rather than relying on positional heuristics.

#### C. Theoretical Analysis of Hallucination Mechanisms
The strong correlation between low $\tau$ and high **fact hallucination** (Table 1) warrants deep theoretical investigation.
*   **Loss Landscape Analysis:** Researchers should investigate whether the training objective inherently penalizes "pausing" to retrieve distant information more than it penalizes generating a plausible but false intermediate fact.
*   **Attention Entropy:** Analyzing attention patterns in failed vs. successful runs could reveal if models with low $\tau$ performance exhibit "attention collapse," where they attend only to the most recent tokens rather than scanning the full context.

#### D. Extending Beyond Deductive Logic
This study focused on **modus ponens** and grade-school math. Future work must test if this fragility extends to:
*   **Inductive and Abductive Reasoning:** Does premise order affect the ability to generalize patterns or infer causes?
*   **Multi-Path Proofs:** How do models behave when multiple valid proof orders exist? Does shuffling cause them to miss valid alternative paths, or do they hallucinate a path that matches the shuffled input?
*   **Long-Context Interaction:** While Appendix D rules out "Lost-in-the-Middle" for short contexts, the interaction between premise order and extreme context lengths (100k+ tokens) remains unexplored. Does the ordering penalty compound with retrieval failures in massive documents?

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

For practitioners deploying LLMs in high-stakes domains, this paper provides immediate, actionable insights.

*   **Legal and Compliance Analysis:** In legal reasoning, facts and precedents are rarely presented in the optimal logical order. Relying on an LLM to derive conclusions from a disordered case file without preprocessing is risky, as the model may hallucinate connections to force a linear narrative.
    *   *Action:* Implement a preprocessing step that extracts entities and rules, then explicitly reorders them logically before feeding them to the model.
*   **Code Debugging and Verification:** When analyzing code dependencies or bug reports, the relevant definitions often appear far from their usage. The finding that models struggle with "unknown variables" (Section 5.3) suggests LLMs will fail to debug code if the variable definition appears after the error site in the prompt.
    *   *Action:* Use static analysis tools to reorder code snippets or documentation so that definitions precede usage before prompting the LLM.
*   **Scientific Hypothesis Testing:** Researchers using LLMs to synthesize findings from multiple papers must be aware that the order in which findings are presented in the prompt could bias the model's conclusion.
    *   *Action:* Employ **ensemble prompting**: run the same query with multiple random permutations of the input premises and aggregate the results. If the output varies significantly across permutations, the reasoning is unreliable.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

For researchers and engineers looking to integrate these findings or reproduce the results, the following guidance is essential:

*   **When to Prefer Reordering Strategies:**
    *   **High-Stakes Deduction:** If your application involves strict logical deduction (e.g., verifying safety constraints, legal contract analysis), **never** rely on the model's raw ability to handle disordered premises. Always implement an external reordering module (e.g., using a solver or heuristic sorter) to align premises with the likely derivation path ($\tau \approx 1$).
    *   **Benchmarking:** When evaluating new models or fine-tuning strategies, do not use standard static benchmarks alone. Integrate the **R-GSM** methodology: take your test set, generate reordered variants, and measure the **performance gap** ($\text{Acc}_{\text{original}} - \text{Acc}_{\text{reordered}}$). A robust model should have a gap near zero.
*   **Integration of the Benchmark:**
    *   The authors have released the **R-GSM** dataset (220 pairs) and the logical reasoning generation code. Integrating these into your evaluation pipeline is low-cost and high-value.
    *   *Protocol:* For any new reasoning task, generate at least 3–5 permutations of the input context. If the model's confidence or output consistency varies across these permutations, flag the instance as "brittle."
*   **Prompt Engineering Mitigations (Immediate Workarounds):**
    *   While the paper focuses on zero-shot results, practitioners can experiment with **Chain-of-Thought (CoT)** prompts that explicitly instruct the model to "First, list all available facts and rules. Second, determine the logical order. Third, solve." This forces an explicit reordering step, potentially offloading the cognitive burden from the model's implicit attention mechanism to its explicit generation stream.
    *   *Caveat:* This mitigation is not guaranteed to work if the model's underlying representation remains order-sensitive, but it is the most accessible immediate fix pending architectural updates.

In conclusion, this paper serves as a critical warning: **LLMs are not yet logic engines; they are sequence engines.** Until models can decouple the order of information from the order of inference, their deployment in complex reasoning tasks requires careful structural management of the input context. The path forward lies in either adapting the input to the model's bias (reordering) or adapting the model to the input's complexity (architectural change).
