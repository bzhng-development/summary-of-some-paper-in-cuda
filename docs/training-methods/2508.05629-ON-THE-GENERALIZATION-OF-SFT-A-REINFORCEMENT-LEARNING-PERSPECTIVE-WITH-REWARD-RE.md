## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the limited generalization of Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) compared to Reinforcement Learning (RL) by proposing Dynamic Fine-Tuning (DFT), a method that stabilizes gradient updates by dynamically rescaling the SFT objective with token probabilities to rectify an implicit, ill-posed reward structure. Demonstrating significant improvements across models like Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B and Qwen2.5-Math-7B on benchmarks such as OlympiadBench and AIME 2024, DFT achieves average accuracy gains over 5.9× larger than standard SFT (e.g., +15.66 vs. +2.09 points on Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B) while competing with complex RL pipelines like GRPO and PPO without requiring reward models. By bridging theoretical insights with a practical one-line code change, this work advances SFT capabilities in math reasoning, code generation, and multi-modal tasks where traditional fine-tuning often degrades performance.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: SFT's Generalization Gap

This paper tackles a fundamental limitation in how Large Language Models (LLMs) are currently fine-tuned. **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)** has become the standard post-training paradigm for adapting models to expert demonstrations (Section 1). However, despite its popularity and ease of implementation, SFT consistently shows **limited generalization compared to Reinforcement Learning (RL)**.

The gap is not merely empirical — it has theoretical roots. The authors reveal through mathematical analysis that standard SFT gradients implicitly encode a **problematic reward structure** that may severely restrict the model's generalization capabilities (Abstract). Specifically, when viewing SFT through an RL lens, the implicit reward is:

1. **Sparse** — only non-zero for exact matches to expert output
2. **Inversely proportional to the model's probability of expert actions** (Equation 6)

This inverse-probability weighting creates a critical issue: when the model assigns low probability to expert actions, the gradient becomes **excessively large**, yielding an ill-posed reward structure and unstable optimization (Section 1). This explains why SFT often memorizes training data rather than learning generalizable patterns.

### Why This Problem Matters

The significance of this problem operates on two levels:

**Practical Impact:**
- SFT remains the **only viable option** when datasets contain only positive demonstrations, with no negative samples or reward model available (Section 1)
- RL requires substantial computation, careful hyperparameter tuning, and explicit reward signals — conditions often **impractical in real-world settings** (Section 1)
- On challenging benchmarks like OlympiadBench, AIME 2024, and AMC 2023, standard SFT often **degrades performance** compared to the base model, while the proposed method consistently improves it (Section 1, Table 1)

**Theoretical Significance:**
- The trade-off between SFT and RL is **central to the alignment of large language models** (Section 2)
- A systematic comparison by Chu et al. (2024) concisely summarizes the distinction as **"SFT memorizes while RL generalizes"** (Section 2)
- Understanding the mathematical connection between SFT and RL gradients could enable simpler methods that capture RL's generalization benefits without RL's complexity

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The literature has pursued several strategies to address the SFT-RL trade-off, each with notable shortcomings:

**Hybrid SFT+RL Methods:**
- The most common strategy involves **SFT pretraining followed by RL-based refinement** with a learned reward model, as popularized by InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022) (Section 2)
- More recent methods **interleave SFT and RL updates** to improve stability and performance (Sheng et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025; Qiu et al., 2025)
- **Limitation:** These approaches still require reward signals, preference pairs, or negative samples. They enrich the training pipeline but **do not fundamentally improve SFT in its native setting** (Section 2)

**Preference-Based Methods:**
- **Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)** bypasses reward modeling entirely by directly optimizing policies on preference data (Rafailov et al., 2023)
- **Negative-aware Fine-Tuning (NFT)** models incorrect generations via an implicit negative policy (Chen et al., 2025b)
- **Limitation:** These methods **rely on reward signals, preference pairs, or negative samples** — unavailable when only positive demonstrations exist (Section 2)

**Theoretical Unification Attempts:**
- Du et al. (2025) reinterpret RLHF as a reward-weighted variant of SFT, preserving reliance on an explicit reward
- Wang et al. (2025a) show that SFT can be cast as RL with an implicit reward, proposing adjustments such as smaller learning rates
- **MixCE** combines forward and reverse KL divergences to form a unified objective (Zhang et al., 2023)
- **GOLD** adopts offline RL with demonstrations, introducing reliance on an unknown demonstration distribution and a restrictive 1/N assumption (Pang & He, 2021)
- **Limitation:** While these works establish connections between SFT and RL through weighting, they **do not provide a precise mathematical equivalence between the SFT gradient and the offline policy gradient** (Section 2)

### This Paper's Positioning

The work positions itself distinctly from prior approaches through three key differentiators:

**1. Fundamental SFT Improvement Without External Signals:**
> "Our work instead focuses on enhancing SFT itself without requiring external feedback." (Section 2)

Unlike hybrid methods or preference-based approaches, this paper asks: **can SFT itself be fundamentally improved?** This is crucial for scenarios where only positive demonstrations are available — a common real-world constraint.

**2. Precise Mathematical Characterization:**
> "In contrast, our work offers a more formal perspective on this connection, highlighting the role of the inverse-probability weighting term in shaping the difference between SFT and RL-like updates." (Section 2)

The authors derive an exact mathematical equivalence between the SFT gradient and a policy gradient with importance sampling (Equation 5-6, Appendix A.2). This precise characterization reveals the **inverse-probability weighting term** $1/\pi_\theta$ as the key culprit behind SFT's instability — a detail prior theoretical work did not isolate with this clarity.

**3. Minimal Implementation Overhead:**
> "This perspective motivates a simple adjustment: multiplying the loss by the model's token probability to neutralize the weighting." (Section 2)

The resulting method, **Dynamic Fine-Tuning (DFT)**, requires just a **single-line change** to the standard cross-entropy loss. This contrasts sharply with:
- RL methods requiring reward models, large batch sizes, and iterative sampling
- iw-SFT (a concurrent method) requiring a **separate reference model** to compute importance weights (Appendix A.4)
- Hybrid pipelines requiring multiple training stages

**Philosophical Shift from Prior Loss Designs:**

Interestingly, the method inverts the weighting philosophy of the widely used **Focal Loss** (Lin et al., 2017):

| Method | Loss Form | Weighting Philosophy |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| Focal Loss | $-(1-p)^\gamma \log(p)$ | Downweights well-classified samples to emphasize hard cases |
| DFT | $-p \log(p)$ | Downweights poorly classified samples to encourage generalization |

> "This inversion reflects a fundamental shift in the LLM era: while underfitting was once a central challenge, overfitting and memorization now dominate, demanding a rethinking of objective design." (Section 2)

This positioning makes the work both theoretically grounded (establishing precise SFT-RL equivalence) and practically accessible (one-line implementation), addressing a gap that prior work left open: improving SFT's generalization **without** requiring the infrastructure, data, or complexity of RL-based approaches.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a theoretical-methods paper that establishes a precise mathematical equivalence between Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) gradients and Reinforcement Learning policy gradients, then uses this insight to derive a simple loss modification that stabilizes training and improves generalization. The core idea is that standard SFT implicitly applies an inverse-probability weighting to gradients that becomes unstable when the model is uncertain about expert actions, and multiplying the loss by the model's own token probability cancels this problematic weighting.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a modified training objective for language models that changes how gradient updates are computed during supervised fine-tuning, requiring only a single-line code change to the standard cross-entropy loss. This solves the problem that standard SFT produces unstable, overly large gradients when the model assigns low probability to correct tokens, and the solution shape is to dynamically rescale each token's contribution to the loss by its own predicted probability before computing gradients.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The DFT training pipeline consists of four sequential components that transform the standard SFT workflow. First, the **Input Processing Module** receives expert demonstration pairs $(x, y^\star)$ where $x$ is a query and $y^\star$ is the reference response, tokenizing them into sequences for model consumption. Second, the **Forward Pass Engine** computes token-level probabilities $\pi_\theta(y^\star_t | y^\star_{<t}, x)$ for each position $t$ in the reference sequence using the current model parameters $\theta$. Third, the **Dynamic Reweighting Module** applies the stop-gradient operator to these probabilities and multiplies them with the log-probability terms, producing the reweighted loss $-\text{sg}(\pi_\theta) \log \pi_\theta$ instead of standard cross-entropy $-\log \pi_\theta$. Fourth, the **Gradient Computation Module** backpropagates through this modified loss to produce parameter updates that avoid the inverse-probability amplification present in standard SFT. Information flows unidirectionally from input tokens through probability computation, through dynamic reweighting, to gradient updates, with no iterative sampling or external reward models required at any stage.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- First, I establish the mathematical foundations by defining standard SFT and RL objectives separately, showing their conventional formulations and gradient expressions (Section 3.1 of paper).
- Second, I derive the key theoretical insight by rewriting the SFT gradient using importance sampling to reveal its hidden equivalence to a policy gradient with implicit reward and importance weighting (Equation 5-6).
- Third, I explain why this equivalence exposes a fundamental problem: the inverse-probability weight $1/\pi_\theta$ causes excessively large gradients when the model is uncertain about expert tokens.
- Fourth, I present the DFT solution as a corrective reweighting that multiplies by $\pi_\theta$ to cancel the problematic $1/\pi_\theta$ term, deriving both sentence-level and token-level loss formulations (Equation 7-9).
- Fifth, I clarify the stop-gradient operator's role and explain why token-level weighting is chosen over sentence-level weighting for numerical stability.
- Sixth, I contrast DFT with alternative approaches like Focal Loss and iw-SFT to justify why this specific design choice achieves better generalization without additional computational overhead.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**This paper is a theoretical-methods contribution that first establishes a precise mathematical equivalence between SFT and RL, then derives a practical loss modification from this equivalence.** The authors do not propose a new architecture or training pipeline, but rather show that a subtle change to the objective function itself — motivated by theoretical analysis — produces substantial empirical improvements. Understanding this work requires following the mathematical derivation carefully, as the practical method emerges directly from the theoretical insight.

**The foundation begins with standard Supervised Fine-Tuning, which minimizes sentence-level cross-entropy over expert demonstrations.** Let $\mathcal{D} = \{(x, y^\star)\}$ denote a corpus of expert demonstrations, where $y^\star$ is the complete reference response to query $x$. The SFT objective is:

$$L_{\text{SFT}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(x, y^\star) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ -\log \pi_\theta(y^\star | x) \right]$$

Here, $\pi_\theta(y^\star | x)$ represents the model's probability of generating the entire reference sequence $y^\star$ given input $x$, parameterized by weights $\theta$. The gradient of this loss with respect to parameters is:

$$\nabla_\theta L_{\text{SFT}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(x, y^\star) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ -\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y^\star | x) \right]$$

This gradient is computed under the fixed demonstration distribution, meaning updates are determined solely by the expert data without considering what the model itself would sample.

**To reveal the connection to Reinforcement Learning, the authors rewrite this SFT gradient using importance sampling to convert it into an on-policy expectation.** In RL, the policy objective maximizes expected reward over samples drawn from the policy itself:

$$J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}_x, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | x)} \left[ r(x, y) \right]$$

The corresponding policy gradient is:

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}_x, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | x)} \left[ \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y | x) \, r(x, y) \right]$$

The critical difference is that RL gradients are expectations over the model's own sampling distribution $\pi_\theta$, while SFT gradients are expectations over the fixed expert distribution. The authors bridge this gap by inserting an importance weight that compares the expert distribution (which is a Dirac delta placing all mass on $y^\star$) with the model distribution.

**Equation 5 performs the key mathematical transformation by rewriting the SFT gradient as an on-policy expectation with importance weighting.** The derivation, provided in Appendix A.2, shows:

$$\mathbb{E}_{(x, y^\star) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ -\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y^\star | x) \right] = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}_x} \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | x)} \left[ \frac{1[y = y^\star]}{\pi_\theta(y | x)} \left( -\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y | x) \right) \right]$$

The term $\frac{1[y = y^\star]}{\pi_\theta(y | x)}$ is the importance sampling weight, where $1[y = y^\star]$ is an indicator function equal to 1 only when the sampled output exactly matches the expert output. By defining auxiliary variables $w(y | x) = \frac{1}{\pi_\theta(y | x)}$ and $r(x, y) = 1[y = y^\star]$, the authors reorganize this into:

$$\nabla_\theta L_{\text{SFT}}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}_x, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | x)} \left[ w(y | x) \, \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y | x) \, r(x, y) \right]$$

This form (Equation 6) closely resembles the RL policy gradient, revealing that standard SFT can be interpreted as an on-policy gradient method with two critical characteristics.

**The RL-style characterization exposes two problematic properties of the implicit SFT reward structure that explain its limited generalization.** First, the reward $r(x, y) = 1[y = y^\star]$ is extremely sparse — it is non-zero only for exact matches to the expert output, causing optimization to overfit rare exact-match samples. Second, and more critically, the importance weight $w(y | x) = 1/\pi_\theta(y | x)$ is inversely proportional to the model's probability of the expert action. When the model assigns low probability to the expert response (indicating uncertainty or disagreement), this weight becomes excessively large, producing disproportionately large gradients. This creates an ill-posed reward landscape that leads to training instability and explains why SFT often fails to generalize beyond memorizing training data.

**The proposed solution, Dynamic Fine-Tuning (DFT), neutralizes this skewed reward by multiplying the loss by a corrective factor equal to the policy probability.** The authors introduce a corrective inverse ratio $1/w = \pi_\theta(y^\star | x)$ that cancels the problematic $1/\pi_\theta$ weighting. The dynamically fine-tuned gradient becomes:

$$\nabla_\theta L_{\text{SFT}}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}_x, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | x)} \left[ \text{sg}\left(\frac{1}{w}\right) \cdot w(y | x) \, \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y | x) \, r(x, y) \right]$$

Here, $\text{sg}(\cdot)$ denotes the stop-gradient operator, ensuring that gradients do not flow through the reward scaling term. Since the indicator function leaves all cases where $y \neq y^\star$ as zero, the authors write $1/w$ as $\pi_\theta(y^\star | x)$ instead of $\pi_\theta(y | x)$. This yields the corrected SFT loss, called Dynamic Fine-Tuning:

$$L_{\text{DFT}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(x, y^\star) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ -\text{sg}\left(\pi_\theta(y^\star | x)\right) \log \pi_\theta(y^\star | x) \right]$$

**However, computing importance weights over the entire sequence trajectory induces numerical instability in practice, so the final DFT formulation applies weighting at the token level.** This treatment follows the approach adopted in PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), which also uses token-level importance sampling for stability. The final DFT loss is:

$$L_{\text{DFT}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(x, y^\star) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ -\sum_{t=1}^{|y^\star|} \text{sg}\left(\pi_\theta(y^\star_t | y^\star_{<t}, x)\right) \log \pi_\theta(y^\star_t | y^\star_{<t}, x) \right]$$

Here, $|y^\star|$ is the length of the reference sequence, and $\pi_\theta(y^\star_t | y^\star_{<t}, x)$ is the conditional probability of token $t$ given all previous tokens and the input. This token-level formulation is what practitioners would implement as a one-line change to standard cross-entropy.

**The stop-gradient operator plays a crucial role in ensuring the reweighting factor does not introduce additional gradient paths that would complicate optimization.** During the forward pass, $\text{sg}(\pi_\theta(y^\star_t | \cdot))$ equals $\pi_\theta(y^\star_t | \cdot)$ numerically, but during backpropagation, this term is treated as a constant. Appendix A.3 shows that this makes DFT mathematically equivalent to directly maximizing the model probability of the target token rather than its log-probability. The gradient becomes:

$$\nabla_\theta L_{\text{DFT}} = -\nabla_\theta \pi_\theta(y^\star | x)$$

In contrast, standard cross-entropy yields gradient:

$$\nabla_\theta L_{\text{CE}} = -\frac{1}{\pi_\theta(y^\star | x)} \nabla_\theta \pi_\theta(y^\star | x)$$

Both share the same gradient direction but differ in scaling: cross-entropy amplifies updates for low-probability targets by factor $1/\pi$, while DFT applies a uniform factor of 1. This explains why DFT avoids the instability caused by excessively large gradients on unlikely expert tokens.

**From the RL perspective, the reward of corrected SFT (DFT) becomes uniformly 1 for all expert trajectories, akin to verification-based reward approaches like RLVR.** This uniform reward avoids over-concentration on specific low-probability reference tokens, leading to more stable updates and improved generalization without introducing any additional sampling or reward models. The method effectively transforms the sparse, inverse-probability-weighted implicit reward of SFT into a dense, uniformly-weighted reward that behaves closer to RL-style updates.

**The choice of token-level weighting over sentence-level weighting is motivated by numerical stability considerations, as confirmed by ablation studies in Table 5.** The authors evaluated two sentence-level variants: one using full sequence probability to scale the loss, and another using geometric-mean weighting inspired by GSPO. The full sequence probability approach produces values that are extremely small in practice, making the loss nearly uninformative and creating a highly skewed weight distribution that is difficult to tune. The geometric-mean variant is more stable but still provides a weak training signal with limited performance gains. Table 5 shows both sentence-level strategies lead to minimal changes over the base model (average accuracy 15.75 and 17.21 respectively), while token-level weighting delivers substantial improvements (average accuracy 31.58). This demonstrates that token-level weighting provides a more reliable optimization signal.

**DFT's loss formulation inverts the weighting philosophy of Focal Loss, reflecting a fundamental shift in challenges from the pre-LLM era to the current era.** Focal Loss is defined as $-(1-p)^\gamma \log(p)$ and deliberately downweights well-classified samples to emphasize underrepresented or hard cases, addressing underfitting. DFT takes the form $-p \log(p)$ and deliberately downweights poorly classified samples to encourage generalization, addressing overfitting and memorization. The authors note this inversion reflects that while underfitting was once a central challenge in machine learning, overfitting and memorization now dominate in the LLM era, demanding a rethinking of objective design.

**The design choice to modify only the loss function, without requiring external reward models or preference data, distinguishes DFT from concurrent methods like iw-SFT.** Appendix A.4 compares DFT with Importance-Weighted SFT (iw-SFT), which also uses importance weighting but requires a separate reference model to compute weights. DFT dynamically derives its own weighting directly from the token probabilities of the model being trained, resulting in a more efficient training procedure without additional model storage or computation. Empirically, DFT achieves higher average accuracy than iw-SFT on most model families (e.g., LLaMA-3.2-3B: +2.39 points, LLaMA-3.1-8B: +4.15 points) and consistently improves upon both base and SFT across nearly all datasets, whereas iw-SFT sometimes underperforms standard SFT on specific benchmarks.

**The implementation requires minimal changes to existing SFT pipelines, making it accessible without specialized RL infrastructure.** Training configurations match standard SFT hyperparameters: AdamW optimizer with learning rates of $5 \times 10^{-5}$ for most models (except LLaMA-3.1-8B-Base at $2 \times 10^{-5}$), mini-batch size of 256, maximum input length of 2048 tokens, and cosine decay learning rate schedule with warm-up ratio of 0.1. The only modification is multiplying each token's cross-entropy loss by its own probability before summing, with the probability detached from the computation graph. This simplicity contrasts sharply with RL methods like PPO and GRPO, which require reward models, large batch sizes, iterative sampling, and careful hyperparameter tuning.

**The theoretical framework explains why DFT improves generalization on reasoning tasks while potentially underperforming on factual knowledge acquisition, as shown in Section 4.5.** Because DFT reweights samples based on the model's own confidence, it tends to reinforce the model's existing beliefs. When the model lacks sufficient factual knowledge (as in Natural Questions), such reinforcement may hinder effective learning instead of facilitating it, reducing performance from 36.62% (SFT) to 30.14% (DFT). This limitation is not a bug but a consequence of the design: DFT is most effective when the task aligns well with the model's prior competence, such as logical reasoning or structured prediction, where the model already has relevant capabilities that need stabilization rather than new factual absorption. This nuanced understanding helps practitioners choose when to apply DFT versus standard SFT based on task characteristics.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper delivers contributions spanning theoretical analysis, practical methodology, and empirical understanding. Below, I distinguish between **fundamental innovations** (new conceptual frameworks or capabilities) and **incremental improvements** (refinements to existing approaches), explaining why each matters for the field.

---

### 4.1 Fundamental Innovation: Exact SFT-RL Gradient Equivalence with Identified Failure Mode

**What Prior Work Established:**
Previous theoretical efforts connected SFT and RL through approximate or conceptual frameworks. Du et al. (2025) reinterpreted RLHF as reward-weighted SFT but preserved reliance on explicit rewards. Wang et al. (2025a) showed SFT could be cast as RL with implicit reward but proposed only heuristic adjustments like smaller learning rates. GOLD (Pang & He, 2021) introduced offline RL with demonstrations but relied on unknown demonstration distributions and restrictive assumptions (Section 2).

**What This Paper Adds:**
The authors derive an **exact mathematical equivalence** between the SFT gradient and an on-policy policy gradient with importance sampling (Equation 5-6, Appendix A.2). Crucially, this derivation isolates the **inverse-probability weighting term** $1/\pi_\theta(y|x)$ as the specific mechanism causing SFT's instability — not just a vague "generalization gap."

**Why This Is Fundamental:**
This is not merely a theoretical curiosity. By pinpointing the exact mathematical term responsible for SFT's limitations, the work transforms an empirical observation ("SFT memorizes, RL generalizes") into a **diagnosable and fixable optimization problem**. Prior work established that SFT and RL behave differently; this paper explains precisely why at the gradient level.

**Significance:**
- Enables **targeted interventions** rather than heuristic tuning (e.g., "try smaller learning rates")
- Provides a **unified lens** for understanding diverse fine-tuning methods through their implicit reward structures
- Opens pathways for future work to modify other components of the gradient expression, not just the loss scaling

---

### 4.2 Fundamental Innovation: One-Line Loss Modification Achieving RL-Like Generalization Without RL Infrastructure

**What Prior Work Required:**
RL-based methods demand substantial infrastructure: reward models (InstructGPT), preference pairs (DPO), iterative sampling (PPO, GRPO), or reference models (iw-SFT). Even theoretically-motivated methods like MixCE (Zhang et al., 2023) require computing forward and reverse KL divergences, adding computational overhead.

**What This Paper Achieves:**
DFT requires **only a single-line change** to the standard cross-entropy loss: multiplying each token's loss by its own probability with stop-gradient. As derived in Section 3.3, this cancels the problematic $1/\pi_\theta$ term without introducing:
- External reward signals
- Preference data or negative samples
- Additional models (reference or reward)
- Iterative sampling or large batch sizes

**Why This Is Fundamental:**
The innovation lies in achieving **RL-style generalization benefits through purely supervised learning**. This collapses what was previously a two-stage pipeline (SFT → RL refinement) into a single stage. For practitioners without RL infrastructure — a common scenario in academic and resource-constrained settings — this removes a major barrier to accessing generalization improvements.

**Significance:**
- **Accessibility:** Any SFT pipeline can adopt DFT without architectural changes
- **Efficiency:** Table 2 shows DFT outperforms offline methods (DPO, RFT) and competes with online RL (PPO, GRPO) despite requiring none of their infrastructure
- **Scalability:** No reward model training or preference collection means the method scales with data availability alone

---

### 4.3 Incremental But Important: Token-Level vs. Sentence-Level Weighting Design Choice

**What Prior Work Did:**
Some methods apply weighting at the sequence level. GOLD (Pang & He, 2021) uses sequence-level importance weights. GSPO (Zheng et al., 2025) applies geometric-mean weighting across sequences. These approaches treat the entire response as a single unit for reweighting.

**What This Paper Tests:**
The authors explicitly compare token-level weighting (their final DFT) against two sentence-level variants: full sequence probability weighting and geometric-mean weighting (Table 5, Section 4.6).

**Results:**
| Weighting Strategy | Average Accuracy (Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B) |
|-------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Sentence-Level | 15.75 |
| Geometric-Mean | 17.21 |
| **Token-Level (DFT)** | **31.58** |

**Why This Matters:**
While the choice of token-level weighting might seem like an implementation detail, the ablation reveals it is **essential to the method's effectiveness**. Sentence-level probabilities become numerically tiny for long sequences, creating skewed weight distributions that provide weak training signals. Token-level weighting maintains stable, informative gradients throughout training.

**Significance:**
- Validates the design choice empirically rather than assuming it works
- Provides guidance for future methods considering similar reweighting schemes
- Demonstrates that numerical stability considerations directly impact final performance, not just training convergence

---

### 4.4 Fundamental Insight: Domain-Specific Effectiveness Boundary (Reasoning vs. Factual Knowledge)

**What Prior Work Assumed:**
Most fine-tuning methods are presented as universally beneficial, with evaluations focused on demonstrating improvements across benchmarks. Limitations are often acknowledged but not systematically characterized.

**What This Paper Reveals:**
Section 4.5 presents a critical finding: DFT **improves reasoning tasks but degrades factual knowledge acquisition**. On Natural Questions (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019):
- Base model: 31.24%
- SFT: 36.62% (+5.38 points)
- DFT: 30.14% (−1.10 points from base)

**Why This Is Fundamental:**
This is not a bug but a **consequence of the design**. Because DFT reweights samples based on the model's own confidence, it reinforces existing beliefs. When the model lacks factual knowledge, this reinforcement hinders learning new information. For reasoning tasks where the model has relevant prior competence, this same mechanism stabilizes optimization and improves generalization.

**Significance:**
- Provides **actionable guidance** for practitioners: use DFT for reasoning/code/multimodal tasks, stick with SFT for factual knowledge domains
- Demonstrates intellectual honesty by explicitly characterizing limitations rather than overstating claims
- Suggests future work could develop **hybrid strategies** that apply DFT selectively based on task type or token semantics

---

### 4.5 Mechanistic Insight: Bimodal Token Probability Distribution Explains Generalization

**What Prior Work Observed:**
Chu et al. (2024) summarized the SFT-RL distinction as "SFT memorizes, RL generalizes" but did not explain the mechanistic basis for this difference at the token level.

**What This Paper Shows:**
Figure 2 (Section 4.7) analyzes token probability distributions after training:
- **SFT:** Uniformly shifts all token probabilities toward higher confidence, including low-probability grammatical tokens
- **DFT:** Creates a **bimodal distribution** — high confidence on semantic content, low confidence on conjunctive words and punctuation ('the', 'let', ',', '.')
- **RL methods (DPO, PPO, GRPO):** Show similar bimodal trends but with milder scale than DFT

**Why This Matters:**
This reveals **what the model learns to ignore**. By deprioritizing tokens that serve grammatical functions rather than semantic content, DFT encourages the model to focus on substantive reasoning patterns. The authors analogize this to human pedagogy: students are taught to focus on concepts rather than perfecting connective words.

**Significance:**
- Provides **interpretability** into why DFT generalizes better — it learns which tokens matter for the task
- Suggests the bimodal distribution could serve as a **diagnostic metric** for training quality
- Opens questions about whether explicit token-type weighting (semantic vs. grammatical) could further improve performance

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Experimental Design Philosophy:**

The authors structure their evaluation across four distinct experimental groups, each testing a different aspect of DFT's capabilities:

1. **Main Experiment (Section 4.1):** Standard SFT setting on mathematical reasoning to establish core advantage over vanilla SFT
2. **Offline RL Setting (Section 4.2):** Testing DFT when dense rewards are available, comparing against established RL methods
3. **Code Generation (Section 4.3):** Cross-domain robustness beyond mathematical reasoning
4. **Multi-Modal Reasoning (Section 4.4):** Extension to vision-language tasks

This multi-pronged approach tests whether DFT's benefits generalize across tasks, modalities, and training paradigms — not just a narrow benchmark.

**Datasets and Training Configuration:**

| Experiment | Dataset | Sample Size | Models Tested |
|------------|---------|-------------|---------------|
| Math Reasoning | NuminaMath-CoT (LI et al., 2024) | 100,000 instances | Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B/7B, LLaMA-3.2-3B, LLaMA-3.1-8B, DeepSeekMath-7B |
| Offline RL | Self-generated (rejection sampling) | ~140,000 correct responses | Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B |
| Code Generation | UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2024) | 10,000 prompts | Qwen2.5-3B, Qwen2.5-Coder-3B/7B |
| Multi-Modal | WeThink (Yang et al., 2025) | Not specified | Qwen2.5-VL-3B |

**Training Hyperparameters (Section 4.1):**

### 5.6 Failure Cases and Limitations (Section 4.5)

**Factual Knowledge Domain (Natural Questions):**

The paper presents a critical limitation that practitioners must understand:

| Method | Natural Questions Accuracy |
|--------|---------------------------|
| Base Model | 31.24% |
| SFT | 36.62% (+5.38) |
| **DFT** | **30.14% (−1.10 from base)** |

**Why DFT Fails Here:**

> "Because it reweights samples based on the model's own confidence, it tends to reinforce the model's existing beliefs. When the model lacks sufficient factual knowledge, such reinforcement may hinder effective learning instead of facilitating it." (Section 4.5)

**Interpretation:** DFT's mechanism — downweighting tokens the model is uncertain about — is beneficial when the model has relevant prior competence (reasoning, code) but harmful when the task requires absorbing new factual information beyond the model's current knowledge. This is not a bug but a consequence of the design.

**Practical Guidance:**

| Task Type | Recommended Method | Rationale |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------|
| Mathematical Reasoning | DFT | Model has prior competence; DFT stabilizes optimization |
| Code Generation | DFT | Structured prediction aligns with model capabilities |
| Multi-Modal Reasoning | DFT | Leverages existing vision-language understanding |
| Factual Knowledge Acquisition | SFT | Requires learning new information; DFT's confidence-based weighting hinders this |

The authors explicitly state: "Our aim is not to assert that DFT universally outperforms SFT, but rather to offer a new perspective on objective design" (Section 5, Limitations).

### 5.8 Critical Assessment: Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

**Strengths of the Experimental Evidence:**

1. **Comprehensive Benchmark Coverage:** Five models, five math benchmarks, plus code and multi-modal tasks — results are not limited to a single narrow setting.

2. **Consistent Pattern Across Scales:** DFT's advantage holds across model sizes from 1.5B to 7B parameters, suggesting the method is not scale-dependent.

3. **Ablation Validation:** Token-level vs. sentence-level comparison (Table 5) confirms the design choice is essential, not incidental.

4. **Honest Limitation Reporting:** Section 4.5 explicitly documents where DFT fails (factual knowledge), preventing overgeneralization of claims.

5. **Learning Dynamics:** Figure 1 shows DFT converges faster, supporting the theoretical claim about gradient stability.

**Weaknesses and Open Questions:**

1. **Limited Model Scale:** All experiments use models up to 7B parameters. The paper acknowledges: "We have not yet assessed its performance on broader task categories or with larger-scale LLM" (Section 5, Limitations). It remains unclear whether DFT's benefits persist at 70B+ scale.

2. **Training Compute Not Reported:** While DFT avoids RL infrastructure, the paper does not explicitly compare training time or FLOPs between DFT and SFT. The one-line change may have computational costs not quantified.

3. **Single Random Seed?:** The paper does not specify whether results are averaged over multiple random seeds. Given the sensitivity of RL-style methods to initialization, this could affect reproducibility.

4. **Natural Questions Sample Size:** The factual knowledge experiment (Section 4.5) reports percentages but does not specify the test set size or whether this is a single run.

5. **Offline RL Setting Specificity:** Table 2's offline RL results use a specific data construction method (rejection sampling from base model). It's unclear whether DFT's advantage over RL methods holds with different data distributions.

**Overall Assessment:**

The experiments provide **strong support for the paper's core claims** within the tested domains (mathematical reasoning, code generation, multi-modal reasoning). The consistent pattern across models, benchmarks, and ablation studies makes it unlikely the results are artifacts of specific configurations. However, the explicit limitation on factual knowledge domains and the lack of large-scale model evaluation mean practitioners should apply DFT selectively, not universally.

The most convincing evidence is the **SFT degradation on challenging benchmarks** (Olympiad Bench, AIME 2024) contrasted with DFT's consistent improvement. This directly supports the theoretical claim that SFT's inverse-probability weighting causes instability on examples where the model is initially uncertain — precisely the examples that matter most for generalization.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the boundaries of DFT's effectiveness, the assumptions underlying its design, and the open questions that remain. While the paper demonstrates substantial improvements in specific domains, understanding where and why DFT fails is as important as understanding where it succeeds.

---

### 6.1 Fundamental Assumptions and Their Consequences

**Assumption 1: The Model Has Relevant Prior Competence**

DFT's core mechanism — reweighting tokens by the model's own confidence — assumes the model already possesses relevant capabilities that need stabilization rather than new knowledge acquisition. This assumption is explicitly validated by the Natural Questions experiment (Section 4.5):

| Method | Natural Questions Accuracy | Change from Base |
|--------|---------------------------|------------------|
| Base Model | 31.24% | — |
| SFT | 36.62% | **+5.38 points** |
| DFT | 30.14% | **−1.10 points** |

> "Because it reweights samples based on the model's own confidence, it tends to reinforce the model's existing beliefs. When the model lacks sufficient factual knowledge, such reinforcement may hinder effective learning instead of facilitating it." (Section 4.5)

**Trade-off:** DFT exchanges the ability to learn new factual information for improved stability on tasks where the model already has relevant competence. This is not a bug but a direct consequence of the $-p \log(p)$ loss formulation, which downweights tokens the model is uncertain about.

**Assumption 2: Token Probability Reflects Learning Difficulty**

The method assumes that low token probability indicates tokens that should receive reduced gradient weight. However, this conflates two distinct scenarios:

1. **Beneficial case:** Low probability due to model uncertainty on reasoning steps that should not be overfit
2. **Problematic case:** Low probability due to genuinely novel information the model needs to learn

The paper does not provide a mechanism to distinguish between these cases, which explains why DFT helps on mathematical reasoning (where the model has prior competence) but harms factual knowledge acquisition (where new information must be absorbed).

**Assumption 3: Token-Level Weighting Is Sufficient**

The authors acknowledge that "computing importance weights over the entire trajectory can induce numerical instability" (Section 3.3), motivating the token-level formulation. However, this introduces a new assumption: that token-level probabilities adequately capture the reward structure without sequence-level context.

Table 5 shows token-level weighting achieves 31.58 average accuracy versus 17.21 for geometric-mean sentence-level weighting. While token-level clearly wins empirically, the paper does not theoretically justify why token-level independence is valid for tasks where sequence-level coherence matters (e.g., multi-step reasoning chains).

---

### 6.2 Domain Boundaries: Where DFT Does Not Apply

**Factual Knowledge Domains:**

As documented in Section 4.5, DFT actively degrades performance on open-domain factual queries. The paper states:

> "In domains that primarily involve the acquisition of factual knowledge, conventional SFT still remains the most efficient approach." (Section 5, Limitations)

This represents a significant boundary condition. Many real-world fine-tuning applications involve knowledge injection (e.g., domain-specific terminology, updated information, proprietary data) rather than reasoning stabilization. For these use cases, DFT offers no advantage and may cause harm.

**Under-Represented Domains:**

The Limitations section explicitly notes:

> "DFT may also not be an ideal choice for hard examples or domains under-represented in the training data, since it assigns low initial probabilities to such samples, reducing their learning weight." (Section 5, Limitations)

This creates a paradox: the examples that most need learning signal (hard, under-represented cases) receive the least gradient weight under DFT. While this prevents overfitting to noisy hard examples, it may also prevent the model from learning genuinely challenging but important patterns.

**Tasks Requiring Uniform Token Fitting:**

The bimodal token probability distribution (Figure 2, Section 4.7) shows DFT actively suppresses confidence on grammatical tokens ('the', 'let', ',', '.'). While the authors frame this as beneficial for generalization, certain applications may require uniform token-level accuracy:

- **Translation tasks** where grammatical particles carry semantic weight
- **Code generation** where syntax errors (even in punctuation) cause compilation failures
- **Formal verification** where every token must be precisely correct

The paper does not evaluate DFT on these task types, leaving uncertainty about whether the bimodal distribution helps or harms in precision-critical domains.

---

### 6.3 Scalability and Computational Constraints

**Model Scale Limitation:**

All experiments use models ranging from 1.5B to 7B parameters. The Limitations section explicitly acknowledges:

> "We have not yet assessed its performance on broader task categories or with larger-scale LLM, which we leave for future exploration." (Section 5, Limitations)

This is a significant gap. Several phenomena scale non-linearly with model size:

- **Confidence calibration:** Larger models may have different probability distributions, affecting how the $p \log(p)$ weighting behaves
- **Gradient dynamics:** The inverse-probability instability may manifest differently at 70B+ scale
- **Memory overhead:** Computing and storing token probabilities with stop-gradient may have different memory characteristics at larger scales

Without evaluation at larger scales, practitioners cannot confidently deploy DFT on state-of-the-art models (e.g., 70B+ parameter LLMs).

**Training Compute Not Quantified:**

While the paper emphasizes DFT avoids RL infrastructure (reward models, iterative sampling), it does not explicitly compare:

- Training time between DFT and SFT
- FLOPs or memory overhead from computing token probabilities
- Whether the stop-gradient operation introduces any computational cost

The claim of "one-line change" suggests minimal overhead, but without explicit measurement, this remains an assumption. For large-scale training runs where even small overheads compound, this information matters.

**Batch Size and Learning Rate Sensitivity:**

Figure 3 (Appendix A.7) shows DFT and SFT have similar sensitivity to learning rate and batch size. While DFT consistently outperforms SFT across configurations, both methods degrade at extreme learning rates (2e-4 and 1e-5). This suggests DFT does not fundamentally relax hyperparameter tuning requirements — practitioners still need to find appropriate learning rates, though the optimal range appears similar to SFT.

---

**iw-SFT Outperforms DFT on Qwen2.5-Math-7B:**

Appendix A.4 (Table 6) shows that on Qwen2.5-Math-7B, iw-SFT achieves 39.60 average accuracy versus DFT's 37.15 — a **2.45 point advantage** for iw-SFT. The paper notes this improvement "is not consistent across datasets," but the existence of any case where iw-SFT wins suggests DFT is not universally superior to all importance-weighting approaches.

| Model | iw-SFT Avg | DFT Avg | Winner |
|-------|------------|---------|--------|
| LLaMA-3.2-3B | 2.26 | 4.65 | DFT (+2.39) |
| LLaMA-3.1-8B | 6.87 | 11.02 | DFT (+4.15) |
| DeepSeekMath-7B | 14.81 | 18.15 | DFT (+3.34) |

The key trade-off: iw-SFT requires a separate reference model (computational overhead) but may achieve better performance on certain model sizes. DFT is more efficient but may sacrifice some peak performance.

**Online RL Methods Still Competitive:**

Table 2 shows GRPO achieves 32.00 average accuracy versus DFT's 35.43 in the offline RL setting — DFT wins by 3.43 points. However, the paper notes these comparisons use "certain scale train set" (Section 4.2), and the advantage may not hold with:

- Larger training datasets
- Different reward signal qualities
- More extensive hyperparameter tuning for RL methods

The claim that DFT is a "streamlined alternative" to RL is supported within the tested configuration, but the boundary conditions for this claim remain unexplored.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Unresolved Issues

**Reproducibility Concerns:**

The paper does not specify:

- Whether results are averaged over multiple random seeds
- The variance across runs for key benchmarks
- Whether the 16 decoding runs per sample (Average@16) account for all sources of variance

Given that RL-style methods can be sensitive to initialization and sampling, the lack of multi-seed reporting leaves uncertainty about result stability. The Reproducibility Statement mentions "scripts for reproducing all reported results" will be released, but at time of publication, independent verification is not possible.

**Uniform Reward Assumption:**

The Limitations section identifies a key direction for future work:

> "An important future direction is to explore non-uniform or quality-aware reward assignments for demonstrations." (Section 5, Limitations)

The current DFT formulation treats all expert tokens equally (uniform reward of 1 after correction, Section 3.3). However, not all tokens in expert demonstrations are equally valuable:

- Key reasoning steps may deserve higher weight than transitional phrases
- Some demonstrations may be higher quality than others
- Certain token types (operators vs. variables in math) may have different importance

The uniform treatment simplifies implementation but may leave performance on the table. Future work could develop quality-aware weighting schemes that build on DFT's foundation.

**Interaction with Other Fine-Tuning Techniques:**

The paper evaluates DFT with full parameter tuning and LoRA (Appendix A.6), but does not explore:

- Interaction with other PEFT methods (prefix tuning, adapter layers)
- Combination with curriculum learning or progressive training
- Whether DFT benefits from multi-stage training (e.g., SFT → DFT refinement)

These combinations could potentially unlock additional gains but remain unexplored.

**Long-Sequence Behavior:**

All experiments use maximum input length of 2048 tokens (Section 4.1). The paper does not evaluate:

- Performance on sequences longer than 2048 tokens
- Whether token-level weighting accumulates errors over long sequences
- How DFT behaves on tasks requiring long-context reasoning (e.g., document QA, long-form generation)

Given that token probabilities can become extremely small over long sequences, the numerical stability advantages of token-level over sentence-level weighting may diminish at extreme lengths.

### 6.6 Summary: Practical Guidance Based on Limitations

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Mathematical reasoning with prior model competence | **DFT** | Consistent improvements across benchmarks (Table 1) |
| Code generation | **DFT** | Outperforms SFT on HumanEval, MultiPL-E (Table 3) |
| Multi-modal reasoning | **DFT** | Improves over SFT on MathVerse, MathVision (Table 4) |
| Factual knowledge acquisition | **SFT** | DFT degrades performance on Natural Questions (Section 4.5) |
| Under-represented domains | **SFT** | DFT reduces learning weight on low-probability samples (Section 5) |
| Models 7B+ parameters | **Uncertain** | Not evaluated; requires further research (Section 5) |
| Precision-critical tasks (formal code, translation) | **Uncertain** | Bimodal distribution may harm token-level accuracy (Section 4.7) |
| Resource-constrained settings | **DFT** | No reward model or reference model required (Section 3.3) |
| Maximum performance prioritized over efficiency | **Consider iw-SFT** | Outperforms DFT on Qwen2.5-Math-7B (Appendix A.4) |

The paper's intellectual honesty in documenting these limitations strengthens rather than weakens its contribution. By clearly articulating where DFT does and does not work, the authors provide actionable guidance for practitioners rather than overstating universal applicability.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how DFT reshapes our understanding of supervised fine-tuning, identifies concrete research pathways it opens, and provides actionable guidance for practitioners deciding when to adopt this method. The implications extend beyond the immediate performance gains — they challenge fundamental assumptions about how we design training objectives for language models.

---

### 7.1 Landscape Impact: Reframing SFT as Implicit RL

**Paradigm Shift in Objective Design:**

This work fundamentally changes how we should think about Supervised Fine-Tuning. Prior to this paper, the SFT-RL generalization gap was treated as an empirical observation — "SFT memorizes, RL generalizes" (Chu et al., 2024, cited in Section 2). This paper transforms that observation into a **diagnosable optimization problem** with a specific mathematical cause.

> "We mathematically establish LLM SFT as a special RL in policy gradient space, pinpoint the underlying reasons for the limited generalization of SFT, and derive a method to improve it." (Section 1, Contributions)

The key insight — that SFT gradients contain an implicit inverse-probability weighting term $1/\pi_\theta$ (Equation 6, Section 3.2) — means that **every SFT implementation is unknowingly applying a problematic reward structure**. This reframing has three cascading effects:

1. **Theoretical Unification:** SFT and RL are no longer separate paradigms but points on a continuum of reward-weighted policy gradients. This enables cross-pollination of techniques between the two fields.

2. **Debuggability:** Instead of heuristically tuning learning rates or batch sizes when SFT underperforms, practitioners can now diagnose whether the inverse-probability weighting is causing instability on specific examples.

3. **Objective Design Philosophy:** The inversion of Focal Loss philosophy (Section 2) signals a broader shift — in the LLM era, **overfitting and memorization dominate**, requiring loss functions that downweight uncertain tokens rather than emphasize them.

**Challenging the SFT → RL Pipeline Assumption:**

The standard post-training pipeline (SFT followed by RL refinement, as in InstructGPT) assumes SFT is merely an initialization step. DFT challenges this by demonstrating that **improved SFT can capture RL-like generalization without a second stage**.

Table 2 shows DFT in an offline setting achieves 35.43 average accuracy versus GRPO's 32.00 — a +3.43 point advantage without reward models or iterative sampling. This suggests:

- Many applications may not need RL refinement if SFT is properly designed
- The computational savings from eliminating the RL stage could be substantial
- Research focus should shift toward improving the SFT stage itself, not just the RL follow-up

**Democratization of Generalization Techniques:**

RL methods require infrastructure that many teams cannot access:

| Requirement | PPO/GRPO | DPO/RFT | **DFT** |
|-------------|----------|---------|---------|
| Reward Model | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ |
| Preference Pairs | ✗ | ✓ | ✗ |
| Reference Model | ✓ | ✓ | ✗ |
| Iterative Sampling | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ |
| Large Batch Sizes | ✓ | Moderate | Standard (256) |

By achieving competitive performance without these requirements, DFT **democratizes access to generalization improvements**. Academic labs, resource-constrained teams, and practitioners without RL expertise can now access benefits previously limited to well-funded organizations.

### 7.2 Research Directions Enabled by This Work

**Immediate Follow-Up Experiments:**

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions that warrant investigation:

1. **Scale Evaluation:**
   > "We have not yet assessed its performance on broader task categories or with larger-scale LLM, which we leave for future exploration." (Section 5, Limitations)
   
   Critical experiments needed:
   - DFT on 70B+ parameter models (e.g., LLaMA-3-70B, Qwen2.5-72B)
   - Whether the $1/\pi_\theta$ instability scales linearly or non-linearly with model size
   - Memory and compute overhead at larger scales

2. **Quality-Aware Reward Assignment:**
   > "An important future direction is to explore non-uniform or quality-aware reward assignments for demonstrations." (Section 5, Limitations)
   
   Current DFT treats all expert tokens equally (uniform reward of 1, Section 3.3). Research could explore:
   - Weighting tokens by their contribution to final answer correctness
   - Distinguishing reasoning steps from transitional phrases
   - Incorporating demonstration quality scores when available

3. **Long-Sequence Behavior:**
   
   All experiments use maximum input length of 2048 tokens (Section 4.1). Open questions:
   - Does token-level weighting accumulate errors over sequences >8192 tokens?
   - How does DFT behave on document QA or long-form generation?
   - Should weighting strategies adapt based on sequence position?

**Theoretical Extensions:**

The SFT-RL equivalence derivation (Section 3.2, Appendix A.2) opens several theoretical research paths:

1. **Generalized Importance Weighting:**
   
   DFT cancels the $1/\pi_\theta$ term completely. But what if partial cancellation is optimal?
   - Investigate loss forms like $-p^\alpha \log(p)$ where $\alpha \in [0, 1]$
   - Determine whether optimal $\alpha$ varies by task type or model scale
   - Connect to existing work on tempered softmax and label smoothing

2. **Token-Type Aware Weighting:**
   
   Figure 2 (Section 4.7) shows DFT creates bimodal distributions — high confidence on semantic content, low on grammatical tokens. This suggests:
   - Explicitly categorizing tokens by type (semantic vs. grammatical vs. structural)
   - Applying different weighting strategies per category
   - Potential integration with syntactic parsing or attention pattern analysis

3. **Connection to Confidence Calibration:**
   
   DFT's mechanism relies on token probabilities as confidence estimates. Research could explore:
   - Whether DFT improves or harms probability calibration
   - Integration with calibration techniques (temperature scaling, ensemble methods)
   - Whether miscalibrated models benefit more or less from DFT

**Methodological Combinations:**

The paper evaluates DFT in isolation, but combinations with other techniques remain unexplored:

1. **DFT + Curriculum Learning:**
   
   Start with standard SFT on easy examples, transition to DFT as model confidence increases. This could address the limitation that DFT reduces learning weight on hard examples (Section 5, Limitations).

2. **DFT + Preference Optimization:**
   
   When preference data is available, could DFT serve as the initialization before DPO? The complementary strengths might yield better final performance than either method alone.

3. **DFT + Multi-Stage Training:**
   
   The paper notes SFT remains indispensable as initialization prior to RL (Section 2, citing Chu et al., 2024). Research could test whether DFT initialization leads to better RL convergence than standard SFT initialization.

For practitioners integrating DFT:

1. **Loss Modification:**
   ```python
   # Standard SFT
   loss = -log_probs[target_tokens]
   
   # DFT (one-line change)
   loss = -stop_gradient(probs[target_tokens]) * log_probs[target_tokens]
   ```

   - Start with same learning rate as SFT (paper uses $5 \times 10^{-5}$ for most models, Section 4.1)

   - Consider task-type routing: DFT for reasoning, SFT for factual queries

### 7.5 Summary: A New Baseline for Supervised Fine-Tuning

This work establishes DFT as a **new default consideration** for supervised fine-tuning in reasoning-heavy domains. The combination of theoretical grounding (exact SFT-RL equivalence), practical simplicity (one-line change), and empirical validation (consistent improvements across models and tasks) makes it a compelling alternative to standard SFT.

However, the paper's intellectual honesty about limitations — particularly the factual knowledge degradation in Section 4.5 — means practitioners should adopt DFT **selectively, not universally**. The method is best viewed as expanding the SFT toolkit rather than replacing it entirely.

**Key Takeaways for the Field:**

1. **SFT is not fundamentally limited** — its generalization gap stems from a fixable optimization issue, not an inherent property of supervised learning.

2. **Theoretical analysis drives practical gains** — the exact mathematical characterization of SFT gradients enabled a targeted intervention that heuristic approaches missed.

3. **Simplicity can compete with complexity** — DFT achieves RL-like benefits without RL infrastructure, challenging the assumption that generalization requires complex pipelines.

4. **Domain awareness matters** — no fine-tuning method is universally optimal; task characteristics should drive method selection.

**Final Recommendation:**

For teams working on mathematical reasoning, code generation, or multi-modal reasoning tasks with positive-only demonstration data, **DFT should be the first method to try** — not as an exotic alternative, but as a sensible default that requires minimal implementation effort. For factual knowledge domains or precision-critical applications, standard SFT remains the safer choice until further research clarifies DFT's behavior in these settings.

The broader lesson is that **re-examining foundational assumptions** — in this case, the standard cross-entropy loss — can yield substantial improvements even in well-studied areas like supervised fine-tuning. This work invites the field to apply similar theoretical scrutiny to other standard practices, potentially uncovering additional opportunities for simple yet impactful improvements.
