## 1. Executive Summary

LayerSkip addresses the high compute and memory costs of Large Language Model (LLM) inference by introducing an end-to-end training and inference solution that enables accurate early exit and self-speculative decoding without requiring auxiliary layers or separate draft models. By combining layer dropout with an early exit loss during training on architectures like Llama2 7B and 13B, the approach creates robust sub-models within a single network that draft tokens using early layers and verify them with the remaining layers. This method achieves inference speedups of up to 2.16× on CNN/DM summarization, 1.82× on HumanEval coding tasks, and 2.0× on TOPv2 semantic parsing while maintaining baseline accuracy and significantly reducing memory footprint compared to traditional speculative decoding.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of LayerSkip, we must first understand the computational bottleneck it addresses and why existing acceleration techniques fail to solve it completely. This section dissects the economic and technical constraints of Large Language Model (LLM) inference, analyzes why "early exit" strategies historically underperform, and explains the memory overhead associated with speculative decoding.

### 2.1 The Computational Cost of Inference

The deployment of LLMs is constrained by high compute and memory requirements, which translate directly into financial and energy costs. As noted in Section 1, running these models on GPU servers incurs significant operational expenses (Samsi et al. 2023). While acceleration solutions exist for commodity GPUs (e.g., laptops), they often suffer from significant drops in accuracy (Zhu et. al 2023). Accelerating further for mobile or edge devices remains an active research challenge.

Most existing acceleration approaches focus on modifying the model weights themselves:
*   **Sparsity:** Reducing the number of non-zero weights (Xia et al. 2023).
*   **Quantization:** Reducing the number of bits per weight (Xiao et al. 2023).
*   **Head Pruning:** Reducing the number of attention heads per layer (Shim et al. 2021).

While effective, these methods often require specialized hardware or software kernels to realize speedups. LayerSkip explores a different dimension: **reducing the number of layers** required for each token. Unlike quantization or sparsity, reducing layers does not inherently require specialized hardware; it simply requires skipping computation. However, simply skipping layers in a standard model does not work well, which leads us to the specific gap this paper addresses.

### 2.2 The Gap in Early Exit Inference

**Early exit** refers to the strategy of stopping the forward pass at an intermediate transformer layer rather than computing all the way to the final layer. If a model has $L$ layers, early exit allows us to compute only $E$ layers (where $E &lt; L$) for certain tokens.

#### Why Standard Models Fail at Early Exit
In a standard pretrained model, early exit performs poorly because the model is not trained to predict tokens from intermediate layers. Section 2.1 provides a critical analysis using Figure 2, which visualizes token predictions at every layer of a Llama1 7B model.

*   **Embedding Evolution:** The output embeddings evolve significantly as they pass through layers. Figure 2b shows that token predictions in earlier layers often appear irrelevant or correspond to the previous token projected on the embedding weights, rather than the correct next token.
*   **Model Hesitation:** The paper observes that intermediate layers are often "hesitant." For example, in Figure 2b, Token 05 is predicted as "range" at Layer 07, but the model changes its mind between Layer 22 and Layer 26 before settling on "range" again.
*   **LM Head Mismatch:** The Language Model (LM) head (the final linear layer that maps embeddings to vocabulary logits) is trained only to unembed outputs from the *last* transformer layer. It is not optimized to interpret embeddings from earlier layers.

The authors note that even with a perfect predictor that exits early with zero overhead, standard models only save up to 26% of computation because tokens often require deep layers to resolve (Section 2.1). For instance, straightforward tokens like "for" (Token 02 in Figure 2) still required all 32 layers in the baseline model. This indicates a fundamental misalignment: standard deep learning models are not motivated to predict their final output early (Voita et al. 2019, 2023).

#### Limitations of Prior Early Exit Work
Previous attempts to fix this, such as Elbayad et al. (2020) or Schuster et al. (2022), typically trained a **dedicated LM head for each transformer layer** or introduced additional auxiliary modules (Zhang et al. 2019). While this improves early exit accuracy, it introduces significant downsides:
*   **Memory Overhead:** Storing multiple LM heads increases memory consumption.
*   **Deployment Complexity:** Maintaining multiple exit points complicates inference engines.
*   **Training Cost:** Training multiple heads is slower and more resource-intensive.

LayerSkip positions itself against this by using a **shared LM head** for all layers, reducing memory and easing deployment (Section 2.1).

### 2.3 The Overhead of Speculative Decoding

Given that early exit alone reduces accuracy, the paper looks to **speculative decoding** as a mechanism to verify early predictions without sacrificing quality.

#### How Speculative Decoding Works
Speculative decoding (Leviathan et al. 2023; Chen et al. 2023) accelerates inference by using two models:
1.  **Draft Model:** A smaller, faster model that generates multiple tokens auto-regressively.
2.  **Main Model:** The larger, accurate model that verifies the draft tokens in parallel.

If the main model agrees with the draft, multiple tokens are accepted in a single step. If they disagree, the main model corrects the token. This process is theoretically lossless in terms of accuracy.

#### The Memory and Complexity Bottleneck
While speculative decoding avoids accuracy drops, it introduces a **memory footprint problem**. As stated in Section 1, maintaining two different models requires storing Key-Value (KV) caches for both. The KV cache stores the attention keys and values for previous tokens to avoid recomputing them; doubling this requirement increases memory pressure significantly.

Recent work like Zhang et al. (2023) proposed "self-speculative decoding" where the draft model is the same as the main model but skips intermediate layers. However, LayerSkip identifies a critical inefficiency in this approach (Section 3):
*   **Cache Reuse:** In Zhang et al. (2023), because intermediate layers are skipped in the draft stage, the activation and KV cache from the draft stage cannot be fully reused during verification.
*   **LayerSkip Advantage:** By exiting at early layers (e.g., layers $1$ to $E$) and verifying with remaining layers (e.g., layers $E+1$ to $L$), LayerSkip ensures that the draft and verification stages execute the same early layers in the same order. This allows the system to reuse the KV cache and activations from the draft stage during verification, reducing both memory and latency (Section 4.3.3).

### 2.4 Positioning Relative to Existing Work

LayerSkip positions itself as an **end-to-end co-design** of training and inference, distinct from prior art in three key ways (Section 1 & Section 3):

1.  **Training Recipe vs. Post-Hoc Modification:** Unlike methods that add routers or auxiliary layers to pretrained models (Zeng et al. 2023; Raposo et al. 2024), LayerSkip modifies the training process itself using **layer dropout** and **early exit loss**. This creates a single model that behaves like an ensemble of models at various depths (Figure 3).
2.  **Shared Architecture:** Unlike early exit methods that require dedicated heads per layer (Elbayad et al. 2020), LayerSkip uses a single shared LM head. This reduces memory consumption for both training and inference.
3.  **Cache-Aware Speculation:** Unlike prior self-speculative decoding (Zhang et al. 2023), LayerSkip's inference algorithm is designed to maximize **KV cache reuse** between the drafting and verification stages. By structuring the draft as the first $E$ layers and verification as the remaining $L-E$ layers, the system avoids recomputing the early layers during verification (Section 4.3.3).

In summary, the paper addresses the gap between **accuracy** and **efficiency**. Standard models are accurate but slow; early exit is fast but inaccurate; speculative decoding is accurate but memory-heavy. LayerSkip aims to achieve the speed of early exit and the accuracy of speculative decoding, with the memory footprint of a single model.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a training and inference co-design paper that modifies how transformer language models are trained to enable accurate early exit, then leverages that capability for self-speculative decoding. The core idea is to train a single model that behaves like an ensemble of models at different depths, allowing tokens to be drafted by early layers and verified by later layers without requiring separate models or auxiliary components.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

LayerSkip is a system that trains large language models to make accurate predictions at any transformer layer, not just the final layer, enabling the model to skip computation during inference when possible. The solution addresses the problem that standard models waste computation on easy tokens by forcing all tokens through all layers, and it does so by modifying training with layer dropout and early exit loss, then using self-speculative decoding to verify early predictions without accuracy loss.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The LayerSkip system consists of three interconnected components that flow sequentially from training to inference. First, the **Training Module** applies layer dropout with exponentially increasing rates across layers and an early exit loss that supervises all transformer layers to connect to a shared LM head, producing a model capable of accurate predictions at any depth. Second, the **Early Exit Inference Module** takes this trained model and allows tokens to exit at layer $E$ (where $E &lt; L$ and $L$ is total layers) by skipping remaining layers and projecting directly to the shared LM head, reducing computation per token from $L$ layers to $E$ layers. Third, the **Self-Speculative Decoding Module** uses the early exit capability to draft multiple tokens auto-regressively with the first $E$ layers, then verifies all draft tokens in parallel using the remaining $L-E$ layers, reusing the KV cache and exit query from the draft stage to avoid recomputation. Information flows from training (which creates the capability) to early exit inference (which exploits the capability for speed) to self-speculative decoding (which recovers accuracy while maintaining speed), with the shared LM head and KVQ cache serving as the connective tissue that enables cache reuse between draft and verification stages.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the training recipe because the inference capabilities are impossible without the specific training modifications that make early layers prediction-capable.
- We then explain layer dropout mechanics, including the exponential scaling across layers and time, because this is the first modification that creates robustness to skipping layers.
- We next detail the early exit loss formulation, including the curriculum strategies, because this ensures the shared LM head can interpret embeddings from any layer.
- We then describe early exit inference as the baseline acceleration method, establishing what speedup is possible before adding verification.
- We conclude with self-speculative decoding and the KVQ cache mechanism, because this is the final innovation that recovers accuracy while maximizing cache reuse between draft and verify stages.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Training Recipe: Creating Exit-Capable Models**

The training recipe fundamentally changes how transformer models learn by ensuring that every layer, not just the final layer, can produce accurate token predictions when connected to the LM head. This is necessary because standard models spread their computation across all layers and do not develop prediction capability until the final layers, as demonstrated in Figure 2b where early layer predictions are often irrelevant or correspond to the previous token.

**Layer Dropout with Exponential Scaling**

Layer dropout is the first training modification, and it works by stochastically skipping transformer layers during training so the model learns to function even when later layers are absent. The transformer layer operation at layer $l$ and training iteration $t$ changes from the standard residual form to:

$$x_{l+1,t} = x_{l,t} + M(p_{l,t})f_l(x_{l,t})$$

where $x_{l,t}$ represents the embeddings at layer $l$ during iteration $t$, $f_l$ is the transformer operation at layer $l$, and $M(p)$ is a Bernoulli function that returns 0 with probability $p$ (dropping the layer) and returns 1 with probability $1-p$ (keeping the layer). When a layer is dropped for a sample, that sample bypasses the transformer operation $f_l$ and proceeds directly to the next layer, forcing the model to learn representations that remain useful even when computation is skipped.

The dropout rate is not constant across layers or time, but instead follows an exponential scaling pattern that is critical to the method's success. The dropout rate at layer $l$ and iteration $t$ is computed as:

$$p_{l,t} = S(t)D(l)p_{max}$$

where $p_{max}$ is a hyperparameter setting the maximum dropout rate at the last layer, $D(l)$ is a per-layer scaling function, and $S(t)$ is a per-time step scaling function. The per-layer scaling increases exponentially from 0.0 at layer 0 to 1.0 at the last layer $L-1$:

$$D(l) = e^{\frac{l \ln 2}{L-1}} - 1$$

This exponential scaling is a non-obvious design choice that reflects the insight that earlier layers should be more reliable than later layers during inference. By applying lower dropout rates to earlier layers, the model learns to depend more heavily on early computation, while higher dropout rates on later layers force the model to not rely exclusively on deep computation. The paper tested this against constant dropout across all layers with the same average value, and Figure 12 in Appendix A.3 shows that the exponential configuration achieves lower training loss, validating this design decision.

For the time-based scaling $S(t)$, the approach differs depending on the training scenario. When performing continual pretraining or finetuning on a pretrained model, the paper sets $S(t) = 1$ (no curriculum across time) because the model already has learned representations. However, when pretraining from scratch, an exponential curriculum leads to best accuracies:

$$S_{exp}(t) = e^{\frac{t \ln 2}{T-1}} - 1$$

where $T$ is the total number of training steps. This curriculum gradually increases dropout over training, allowing the model to first learn basic representations before being forced to handle layer skipping.

**Early Exit Loss with Shared LM Head**

The second training modification is the early exit loss, which directly supervises the model to connect every transformer layer to the LM head during training. This addresses the fundamental problem that standard LM heads are trained only to unembed outputs from the last transformer layer, making them incapable of interpreting embeddings from earlier layers. The total loss at iteration $t$ combines cross-entropy losses from all layers:

$$J(X, Y, t) = \sum_{l=0}^{L-1} \tilde{e}(t, l)J_{CE}(g(x_{l+1}), Y)$$

where $X$ denotes input tokens, $Y$ denotes output tokens, $J_{CE}$ is the standard cross-entropy loss, $g$ is the shared LM head function, and $x_{l+1}$ is the embedding output from layer $l$. The term $\tilde{e}(t, l)$ is a normalized per-layer loss scale that determines how much each layer contributes to the total loss, with the constraint that scales sum to 1 across all layers:

$$\tilde{e}(t, l) = \frac{C(t, l)e(l)}{\sum_{i=0}^{L-1} C(t, i)e(i)}$$

where $C(t, l)$ is a binary curriculum function that determines whether early exit is enabled for layer $l$ at iteration $t$, and $e(l)$ is the per-layer scale. The per-layer scale increases across layers such that later layers receive quadratically higher weight:

$$e(l) = \begin{cases} e_{scale} \sum_{i=0}^{l} i, & \text{if } 0 \leq l &lt; L-1 \\ L-1 + e_{scale} \sum_{i=0}^{L-2} i, & \text{if } l = L-1 \end{cases}$$

where $e_{scale}$ is a hyperparameter between 0 and 1 that controls the scale of early exit loss. This quadratic weighting reflects the insight that predicting at later layers is easier (since embeddings have evolved closer to the final representation), so later layers should be penalized more heavily for errors to ensure they maintain baseline accuracy.

A critical design choice is that LayerSkip uses a **shared LM head** for all layers rather than training dedicated heads per layer as done in Elbayad et al. (2020) and Schuster et al. (2022). This decision reduces memory consumption for both training and inference, eases deployment since there is only one head to maintain, and speeds up training by avoiding the overhead of multiple head parameters. The tradeoff is that the single head must learn to interpret embeddings from all layer depths, which the early exit loss explicitly enables.

**Early Exit Loss Curriculum Strategies**

Applying early exit loss to all layers at all iterations slows down training and can reduce the accuracy of the last layer, so the paper introduces curriculum strategies to control when early exit is enabled during training. The first strategy is **rotational early exit curriculum** $C_{rot,R}$, where early exit is enabled at every $R$ layers with circular rotation at each iteration. This means early exit at each layer is enabled once every $R$ iterations, so at each training iteration only $\lceil L/R \rceil$ unembedding operations are applied, reducing computational overhead. The second strategy is **gradual early exit curriculum** $C_{grad}$, where early exit loss is gradually enabled from layers $L-1$ to 0, one layer at a time every $T/2L$ iterations. The rotational curriculum is used for most experiments (e.g., $R=8$ for Llama2 7B, $R=39$ for Llama2 13B), while the gradual curriculum is used for task-specific finetuning like TOPv2.

**Training Hyperparameters Across Experiments**

The paper reports specific hyperparameter configurations for different training scenarios, and these values are essential for reproducing the results. For continual pretraining on Llama2 7B (32 layers), the configuration is $p_{max} = 0.1$, $e_{scale} = 0.2$, and $C_{rot,R=8}$. For Llama2 13B (40 layers), the configuration is $p_{max} = 0.1$, $e_{scale} = 0.1$, and $C_{rot,R=39}$. For Llama3 8B and Llama3.2 1B, the configuration is $p_{max} = 0.1$, $e_{scale} = 0.1$, and $C_{rot,R=8}$. For finetuning on code data with Llama1 7B, the configuration is $p_{max} = 0.1$, $e_{scale} = 1.0$, and $C_{rot,R=16}$, where the higher $e_{scale}$ reflects that task-specific finetuning can tolerate stronger early exit supervision. For finetuning on the TOPv2 task with Llama 1.5B (24 layers), the configuration is $p_{max} = 0.2$, $e_{scale} = 1.0$, and $C_{grad}$. When pretraining from scratch, the paper notes that higher learning rates are required when layer dropout is greater than 0.0, following Srivastava et al. (2014), with specific rates provided in Table 9 of Appendix A.1.

**Early Exit Inference: Baseline Acceleration**

After training with the above recipe, the model can perform early exit inference during autoregressive decoding. For each token generation, the model runs only the first $E$ transformer layers and skips directly to the shared LM head, producing output $g(x_E)$ instead of $g(x_L)$. This reduces the computational cost per token from $L$ layers to $E$ layers, as illustrated in Figure 4. The paper evaluates different values of $E$ and reports accuracies in Section 6.1, showing that LayerSkip-trained models maintain significantly higher accuracy at early layers compared to baseline models. For example, in Table 1, NaturalQuestions accuracy for Llama2 7B at the middle layer (layer 16) improves from 0.0554% for baseline to 4.07% for LayerSkip, demonstrating that the training recipe successfully creates exit-capable models. However, early exit alone still incurs an accuracy cost compared to using the full model, which motivates the self-speculative decoding approach.

**Self-Speculative Decoding: Recovering Accuracy**

Self-speculative decoding combines early exit with speculative decoding principles to achieve speedup without accuracy loss. Traditional speculative decoding uses two separate models (a fast draft model and a slow main model), but LayerSkip uses a single model where the draft stage uses the first $E$ layers and the verification stage uses the remaining $L-E$ layers. The algorithm consists of two key steps as shown in Figure 5.

The first step is **Self-Drafting**, where the model computes $d$ draft tokens through early exit at layer $E$. The parameter $d$ is the number of speculations, representing how many tokens are generated auto-regressively in the draft stage before verification. The training recipe enables this by creating an ensemble of different candidate draft models at each layer depth, so exiting at different layers produces different speed-accuracy tradeoffs.

The second step is **Self-Verification**, where the full LLM predicts the next token for each draft token in a single forward pass, and the system assesses where draft tokens and verified tokens agree. All draft tokens up to the first disagreement point are accepted into the output, along with the next verified token, and the process continues from the draft stage. Critically, the verification stage only requires computing the remaining $L-E$ layers that were not used in the draft stage, since the first $E$ layers have already been computed.

**KVQ Cache: Enabling Cache Reuse**

The key innovation that distinguishes LayerSkip from prior self-speculative decoding (Zhang et al. 2023) is the KVQ cache mechanism that enables maximum reuse of computation between draft and verification stages. In autoregressive transformers, the KV cache stores attention keys and values for previous tokens to avoid recomputing them in each transformer layer, and this cache is critical for efficient generation.

LayerSkip achieves cache reuse through two mechanisms. First, since the draft model and verification model operate on the same model using the same order of layers, the first $E$ layers are shared in both steps, so a **single KV cache** can be maintained for both draft and verify steps, reducing memory and latency. Second, to further reduce computation of the first $E$ layers during verification, the system introduces an **exit query cache** that saves the query vector of exit layer $E-1$ for verification to directly continue from layer $E$ to the last layer $L$. The union of the KV cache and the exit query is termed the **KVQ cache**, and only the query for the exit layer needs to be saved, not all intermediate queries.

This cache reuse is not possible in Zhang et al. (2023) because their approach skips intermediate layers in the draft stage, meaning the draft and verification stages do not execute the same early layers in the same order. LayerSkip's approach of exiting at early layers (layers 1 to $E$) and verifying with remaining layers (layers $E+1$ to $L$) ensures sequential layer execution that enables cache reuse. Table 7 in Section 7 quantifies the benefit, showing that KVQ cache reuse saves 9-20 ms per token depending on the task, with TOPv2 improving from 143 ms/token to 134 ms/token at $E=18$ and CNN/DM improving from 182 ms/token to 166 ms/token at $E=18$.

**Inference Configuration and Speedup Results**

The paper reports specific inference configurations for different tasks. For CNN/DM summarization with Llama2 7B, the configuration is $E=8$ (exit layer) and $d=12$ (number of speculations), achieving 1.86× speedup. For Llama2 13B on CNN/DM, the configuration is $E=15$ and $d=12$, achieving 1.81× speedup. For HumanEval coding with Llama1 7B finetuned on code, the configuration is $E=6$ and $d=12$, achieving 1.82× speedup. For TOPv2 semantic parsing with Llama 1.5B, the configuration is $E=6$ and $d=8$, achieving 2.0× speedup. For pretraining from scratch on Llama2 7B with 26B tokens, the configuration is $E=8$ and $d=12$, achieving 2.16× speedup, which is the maximum reported speedup. Token acceptance rates vary by task and exit layer, with TOPv2 showing 76.0% acceptance at $E=6$, 97.2% at $E=12$, and 98.9% at $E=18$, indicating that deeper exit layers produce more accurate drafts that are more likely to be accepted during verification.

**Design Choices and Tradeoffs**

Several design choices in LayerSkip reflect explicit tradeoffs between accuracy, speed, and complexity. The shared LM head reduces memory and deployment complexity but requires the early exit loss to train the head to interpret embeddings from all layers. The exponential layer dropout scaling prioritizes early layer reliability but requires tuning $p_{max}$ to avoid last layer accuracy drops. The rotational curriculum reduces training overhead but may provide less supervision to individual layers compared to enabling early exit at all layers every iteration. The self-speculative decoding approach requires finetuning or pretraining with the LayerSkip recipe, unlike Zhang et al. (2023) which can be applied to existing models without weight changes, but LayerSkip's approach enables better cache reuse and therefore lower latency. The paper acknowledges in Section 8 that the hyperparameters $p_{max}$, $e_{scale}$, and $R$ require tuning to avoid last layer accuracy drops, and that pretraining from scratch with layer dropout requires increased learning rates that can be tricky to tune optimally.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

LayerSkip is not merely an optimization of existing inference techniques; it represents a co-design of training objectives and system architecture that challenges standard assumptions about how transformer representations evolve. Below, we dissect the three most significant contributions, distinguishing between fundamental shifts in methodology and systemic improvements in efficiency.

### 4.1 Fundamental Innovation: Training for Depth-Agnostic Representations

Most prior work on early exit treats the capability as an add-on. Methods like Elbayad et al. (2020) attach dedicated classifier heads to each layer, while others like Zhang et al. (2019) insert auxiliary modules. These approaches assume the base transformer layers are fixed and must be *adapted* for early exit. LayerSkip inverts this assumption: it modifies the **training objective** itself to force the base layers to become exit-capable.

*   **The Innovation:** Instead of patching a pretrained model, LayerSkip trains the model to behave as an **ensemble of sub-models** at varying depths (Figure 3). By applying layer dropout and early exit loss during pretraining or finetuning, the model learns representations that are robust enough to be unembedded by a **shared LM head** at any layer $l$, not just the final layer $L$.
*   **Why It Matters:** This eliminates the memory overhead of storing multiple LM heads or auxiliary routers. In a standard early exit setup with dedicated heads, memory usage scales linearly with the number of exit points. LayerSkip maintains the memory footprint of a standard model during inference.
*   **Evidence of Success:** The efficacy of this approach is visible in the accuracy retention at middle layers. For Llama2 7B, standard baseline models drop to near-zero accuracy on generation tasks like NaturalQuestions when exiting at the middle layer (0.0554% EM), whereas LayerSkip maintains 4.07% EM (Table 1). While 4% seems low, it represents a **73× relative improvement** over the baseline, proving that the representations themselves have changed, not just the exit mechanism.

This is a **fundamental innovation** because it alters the internal geometry of the transformer's embedding space. It suggests that standard training procedures actively discourage early prediction capability, a hypothesis supported by the ablation studies in Section 7.

### 4.2 Systemic Innovation: Sequential Layer Partitioning for Cache Reuse

In speculative decoding, the primary bottleneck is often memory bandwidth and KV cache management. Traditional speculative decoding requires two models (draft and main), doubling the KV cache memory requirement. Prior "self-speculative" approaches, such as Zhang et al. (2023), attempt to solve this by using a single model but skipping intermediate layers during the draft phase. LayerSkip identifies a critical flaw in this strategy: **cache fragmentation**.

*   **The Innovation:** LayerSkip enforces **sequential layer execution** between draft and verification stages. The draft stage computes layers $1$ to $E$, and the verification stage computes layers $E+1$ to $L$. Because the layers are contiguous, the KV cache generated during drafting is exactly what is needed for verification.
*   **Contrast with Prior Work:** In Zhang et al. (2023), the draft model might skip layers (e.g., using layers 1, 3, 5), meaning the verification stage (using layers 1–5) cannot reuse the draft's KV cache directly because the intermediate states (layers 2, 4) are missing. LayerSkip's contiguous split ensures 100% cache compatibility.
*   **Performance Impact:** This design choice translates directly to latency reduction. Table 7 shows that enabling KVQ cache reuse saves **9–20 ms per token** depending on the task. For the TOPv2 task at exit layer $E=18$, latency drops from 143 ms/token without reuse to 134 ms/token with reuse.
*   **Memory Efficiency:** By maintaining a single KV cache for both stages (termed the **KVQ cache** in Section 4.3.3), LayerSkip avoids the **2× memory overhead** typical of standard speculative decoding. This is critical for deploying on memory-constrained hardware like edge devices or commodity GPUs where VRAM is the limiting factor.

This is a **systemic innovation** because it leverages the inherent sequential dependency of transformer layers to optimize memory access patterns, rather than treating the model as a black box function.

### 4.3 Theoretical Insight: The Divergence of Early Layer Perplexity

Perhaps the most surprising contribution of the paper is an empirical finding about transformer training dynamics that was previously undocumented. The authors observed that without specific regularization, early layers actually become *less* capable of prediction as training progresses.

*   **The Finding:** In Figure 11, the authors plot the perplexity of the middle layer (layer 12) versus the last layer (layer 24) during pretraining from scratch. While the last layer's perplexity decreases as expected (indicating better learning), the middle layer's perplexity **increases drastically** by default.
*   **Implication:** This explains *why* standard pretrained models fail at early exit. It is not just that they weren't trained to exit; it is that standard training objectives actively push predictive capability to the final layers, causing earlier representations to diverge from the output space over time.
*   **Role of LayerSkip:** The layer dropout and early exit loss act as a regularizer that prevents this divergence. Figure 11 shows that with LayerSkip (LD+EE), the middle layer perplexity remains stable and low even as training tokens increase.
*   **Significance:** This insight suggests that "depth-adaptive" inference is incompatible with standard training recipes. It provides a theoretical justification for why post-hoc early exit methods (which do not change training) hit a performance ceiling—they are fighting against the natural dynamics of the training process.

This is a **theoretical insight** rather than a methodological one. It opens new avenues for research into how transformer embeddings evolve across layers during training, suggesting that future architectures might benefit from explicit constraints on layer-wise representation stability.

### 4.4 Critical Assessment: Trade-offs and Limitations

While the innovations are significant, they come with specific constraints that users must consider:

*   **Retraining Requirement:** Unlike quantization or some speculative decoding methods that work on off-the-shelf models, LayerSkip **requires retraining or finetuning** with the specific recipe (Section 8). You cannot apply LayerSkip to a standard pretrained Llama2 model without modifying its weights. This limits its applicability to scenarios where you control the training pipeline.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method introduces new hyperparameters ($p_{max}$, $e_{scale}$, $R$) that require tuning. The paper notes that "tuning learning rate to get optimal accuracy could be tricky and time consuming" when pretraining from scratch (Section 8). For example, $e_{scale}$ must be increased to 1.0 for task-specific finetuning but kept at 0.1 for continual pretraining (Section 5) to avoid degrading the last layer's accuracy.
*   **Speedup Variability:** The speedup is not uniform. It ranges from 1.34× to 2.16× depending on the task (Section 1). Tasks with higher token acceptance rates (like TOPv2 semantic parsing with 98.9% acceptance at $E=18$) yield higher speedups, while tasks requiring complex reasoning may see lower gains because the draft tokens are rejected more often.

In summary, LayerSkip trades **training flexibility** (the need to retrain) for **inference efficiency** (lower memory and latency). For organizations deploying models at scale where inference costs dominate, this trade-off is highly favorable. For users relying on public pretrained models without compute for finetuning, the method is currently inaccessible.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the experimental methodology and results from LayerSkip, evaluating whether the evidence convincingly supports the paper's claims about early exit accuracy and inference speedup. We examine the evaluation datasets, metrics, baselines, and experimental setups, then critically assess the quantitative results across different training scenarios. Special attention is given to ablation studies that reveal the underlying mechanisms driving performance.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The paper employs a comprehensive evaluation strategy across four distinct training scenarios, each targeting different use cases and model sizes. This multi-scenario approach is necessary because the effectiveness of LayerSkip depends on whether the model is pretrained from scratch, continually pretrained, or finetuned on specific domains.

**Training Scenarios and Model Configurations**

The experiments cover four training paradigms as detailed in Section 5:

*   **Continual Pretraining:** Starting with pretrained Llama2 7B (32 layers) and Llama2 13B (40 layers), the authors continue pretraining on 52B tokens from a diverse corpus containing natural language text and code. Later experiments extend this to Llama3 8B on 419B tokens and Llama3.2 1B on 839B tokens. The hyperparameters vary by model: Llama2 7B uses $p_{max} = 0.1$, $e_{scale} = 0.2$, $C_{rot,R=8}$, while Llama2 13B uses $p_{max} = 0.1$, $e_{scale} = 0.1$, $C_{rot,R=39}$.
*   **Pretraining from Scratch:** Starting with randomly initialized models, the authors pretrain on 26B tokens. Experiments use Llama2 1.5B (24 layers, a custom small Llama-like model) with $p_{max} = 0.1$, $e_{scale} = 0.2$, $C_{rot,R=23}$, and Llama2 7B with $p_{max} = 0.2$, $e_{scale} = 0.2$, $C_{rot,R=31}$. Following Srivastava et al. (2014), higher learning rates are used when layer dropout is greater than 0.0 (Table 9, Appendix A.1).
*   **Finetuning on Code Data:** Starting with pretrained Llama1 7B, the model is finetuned on 5.2B tokens of CodeLlama data mix with $p_{max} = 0.1$, $e_{scale} = 1.0$, $C_{rot,R=16}$. The higher $e_{scale}$ reflects that task-specific finetuning can tolerate stronger early exit supervision.
*   **Finetuning on Task-Specific Dataset:** Starting with pretrained Llama 1.5B (24 layers), the model is finetuned on TOPv2, a multi-domain task-oriented compositional semantic parsing dataset, with $p_{max} = 0.2$, $e_{scale} = 1.0$, $C_{grad}$.

**Evaluation Datasets and Metrics**

The evaluation spans multiple task categories as categorized in Appendix A.1.2:

*   **Perplexity:** Measured on held-out test sets from Wikipedia, Books (Gao et al. 2020), and The Stack (Kocetkov et al. 2022). Lower perplexity indicates better language modeling capability.
*   **Classification Tasks:** Multiple-choice and true/false questions including BoolQ, PIQA, SIQA, HellaSwag, Winogrande, ARC Easy/Challenge, OBQA, COPA, RACE Middle/High, and MMLU. Accuracy is the primary metric.
*   **Generation Tasks:** Open-ended answers including NaturalQuestions (NQ), TriviaQA (TQA), GSM8K, MATH, HumanEval, and MBPP. Exact Match (EM) or Pass@1 is used depending on the task.
*   **Summarization:** CNN/DM and XSUM abstractive summarization tasks evaluated with ROUGE-2 (Ganesan 2018).
*   **Inference Metrics:** Token acceptance rate (how often verification accepts draft tokens), tokens per second (throughput), and speedup factor compared to autoregressive baseline.

**Baselines and Comparisons**

The paper compares LayerSkip against several baselines:

*   **Standard Baseline:** Pretrained or finetuned models without LayerSkip modifications (Touvron et al. 2023b for Llama2).
*   **LayerSkip Variants:** The authors test layer dropout only (LD), early exit loss only (EE), and both combined (LD+EE) to isolate the contribution of each component.
*   **Draft & Verify:** Zhang et al. (2023) self-speculative decoding approach, used as a comparison for inference speedup on common tasks.

**Experimental Setup**

All generation experiments were performed with greedy decoding and a maximum of 512 tokens per sample (Section 6.2). Hardware varies by experiment: NVIDIA H100 GPUs for most continual pretraining and pretraining from scratch experiments, NVIDIA A100 GPUs for code finetuning experiments. Speedup is calculated as the acceleration of average inference time per token compared to the "Autoregressive" baseline on the same setting (Section 6.2).

### 5.2 Early Exit Inference Accuracy Results

The first claim LayerSkip makes is that its training recipe improves early exit accuracy without adding auxiliary layers. The results strongly support this claim, though with important nuances across task types.

**Continual Pretraining: Llama2 7B and 13B**

Table 1 presents the most comprehensive early exit evaluation, comparing baseline and LayerSkip models at both the last layer and middle layer. The results reveal a critical pattern: **generation tasks benefit far more from LayerSkip than classification tasks**.

For Llama2 7B on NaturalQuestions (an open-ended generation task), the baseline model drops from 25.1% EM at the last layer to essentially 0% (0.0554%) at the middle layer (layer 16). In stark contrast, LayerSkip maintains 4.07% EM at the middle layer—a **73× relative improvement** over baseline. Similarly, for TriviaQA, baseline drops from 58.5% to 0.619%, while LayerSkip maintains 11.8%.

For code generation on HumanEval, Llama2 7B baseline drops from 13.4% Pass@1 at the last layer to 0% at the middle layer, while LayerSkip maintains 4.88%. For Llama2 13B, the pattern is similar: baseline HumanEval drops from 18.9% to 0%, while LayerSkip maintains 3.05%.

However, classification tasks show a different pattern. For MMLU on Llama2 13B, the baseline maintains 49.2% accuracy at the middle layer (only dropping from 55.2% at the last layer), while LayerSkip achieves 52.9%. This suggests classification tasks are inherently more robust to early exit because they evaluate a token out of 4 or 2 possible outcomes, while generation tasks evaluate each token out of thousands of vocabulary entries (Section 6.1).

Figure 6 visualizes these trends across all evaluation tasks, showing LayerSkip-LD+EE consistently outperforming baseline at earlier layers while maintaining comparable last-layer accuracy. The perplexity plots (Wikipedia, Books, The Stack) show LayerSkip achieves lower perplexity at earlier layers, indicating better language modeling capability throughout the network depth.

**Continual Pretraining: Llama3 8B and Llama3.2 1B**

Table 2 reveals an important limitation: **Llama3 models show larger drops in last layer accuracy when continually pretrained with LayerSkip**, despite training on more tokens. For Llama3 8B, MMLU drops from 66.5% (baseline) to 60.5% (LayerSkip) at the last layer. For Llama3.2 1B, MMLU drops from 31.2% to 24.8%.

The paper explains this in Section 6.1: "The perplexity of earlier layers of the baseline Llama3 models are 2 or 3 orders of magnitude higher than their Llama2 counterparts." For example, Wikipedia perplexity at the middle layer for Llama3 8B baseline is $1.10 \times 10^5$, compared to 1900 for Llama2 7B baseline (Table 1 vs Table 2). Reducing such high perplexity while maintaining last layer accuracy is more challenging.

The authors hypothesize this is due to the increase in pretraining tokens (8T for Llama3 versus 2T for Llama2), which is confirmed by the ablation study in Section 7. This finding suggests LayerSkip may be more beneficial when incorporated during initial pretraining rather than continual pretraining of heavily pretrained models.

**Pretraining from Scratch**

Figure 8 shows results for Llama2 1.5B and 7B pretrained from scratch on 26B tokens. The results demonstrate that LayerSkip variants (LD, EE, LD+EE) all achieve higher accuracy than baseline at earlier layers. On the last layer, results are mixed: some downstream tasks show slight accuracy drops, while others show LayerSkip leading to higher accuracy.

The paper notes that since these models were pretrained on relatively small token counts, evaluation results on some classification tasks were close to random guesses regardless of LayerSkip usage, and hence were removed from results (Section 6.1).

**Finetuning on Code Data**

Figure 10a presents results for Llama1 7B finetuned on code data. For earlier layers, LayerSkip is "clearly better than the baseline, with layer dropout combined with early exit loss showing a big improvement on one of the 2 tasks" (Section 6.1). For last layer accuracy, LayerSkip with both components has almost the same accuracy as baseline. The higher $e_{scale} = 1.0$ (compared to 0.1 or 0.2 in continual pretraining) was possible because this experiment finetuned on specific domain data.

**Finetuning on Task-Specific Dataset (TOPv2)**

Figure 10b shows results for Llama 1.5B finetuned on TOPv2. This is where LayerSkip shows its most dramatic improvement: "when removing layers from the baseline model, the model is not able to generate complete and accurate parses resulting in 0 EM. However, with LayerSkip, early exit inference improves to 77% at layer 12" (Section 6.1). There is a 3% regression in final layer accuracy, but the early exit capability is transformative for this task.

**Text Generation Quality**

Figures 7 and 9 provide qualitative examples of early exit text generation. Figure 7a shows Llama2 7B exiting at layer 20: the baseline generates repetitive nonsense ("Egyptian Egyptian Egyptian..."), while LayerSkip produces coherent text about Cairo's attractions. Figure 7b shows Llama2 13B exiting at layer 24: baseline generates repetitive "Alb Alb Alb..." while LayerSkip produces a coherent (though factually flawed) description of Albany.

Figure 9 shows similar patterns for models pretrained from scratch. The baseline generates repetitive text ("Egypt capital city is Egypt capital city is..."), while LayerSkip-LD+EE produces coherent paragraphs about Cairo's landmarks. These examples visually demonstrate that LayerSkip-trained models maintain semantic coherence at earlier layers where baseline models fail completely.

### 5.3 Self-Speculative Decoding Speedup Results

The second major claim is that LayerSkip's self-speculative decoding achieves significant speedup without accuracy loss. The results support this claim, though speedup varies considerably by task and model.

**Continual Pretraining Speedup (Table 3)**

Table 3 presents generation results for Llama2 7B and 13B on CNN/DM, XSUM, and HumanEval:

| Model | Task | Exit Layer (E) | Speculations (d) | ROUGE-2 / Pass@1 | Token Acceptance | Tokens/Sec | Speedup |
|-------|------|----------------|------------------|------------------|------------------|------------|---------|
| Llama2 7B | CNN/DM | 8 | 12 | 0.078 | 68.9% | 127.9 | **1.86×** |
| Llama2 7B | XSUM | 8 | 12 | 0.073 | 54.6% | 104.7 | **1.54×** |
| Llama2 7B | HumanEval | 8 | 6 | 0.042 | 67.1% | 122.8 | **1.83×** |
| Llama2 13B | CNN/DM | 15 | 12 | 0.098 | 74.5% | 70.2 | **1.81×** |
| Llama2 13B | XSUM | 15 | 4 | 0.124 | 67.7% | 60.5 | **1.34×** |
| Llama2 13B | HumanEval | 7 | 4 | 0.055 | 57.0% | 84.2 | **1.66×** |

Key observations:
*   **Smaller models achieve higher speedup:** Llama2 7B achieves 1.86× on CNN/DM vs 1.81× for Llama2 13B. This is because the relative cost of early layers is smaller in larger models.
*   **Token acceptance rate correlates with speedup:** CNN/DM has 68.9-74.5% acceptance vs XSUM's 54.6-67.7%, corresponding to higher speedups.
*   **Comparison with Draft & Verify:** On CNN/DM, LayerSkip achieves 1.81× vs Draft & Verify's 1.56× for Llama2 13B. On XSUM, LayerSkip achieves 1.34× vs Draft & Verify's 1.48×, making LayerSkip slightly slower on this task.

**Pretraining from Scratch Speedup (Table 4)**

Table 4 shows an interesting reversal: "the bigger model has a bigger speedup than the smaller model, reaching 2.16× speedup, which exceeds traditional speculative decoding" (Section 6.2).

| Model | Task | Exit Layer (E) | Speculations (d) | ROUGE-2 | Token Acceptance | Tokens/Sec | Speedup |
|-------|------|----------------|------------------|---------|------------------|------------|---------|
| Llama2 1.5B | CNN/DM | 8 | 12 | 0.063 | 77.4% | 167.4 | **1.76×** |
| Llama2 7B | CNN/DM | 8 | 12 | 0.067 | 77.8% | 145.6 | **2.16×** |

The 2.16× speedup for Llama2 7B is the **maximum reported speedup** in the paper. The higher token acceptance rate (77.8% vs 77.4%) contributes to this improvement.

**Code Finetuning Speedup (Table 5)**

Table 5 shows results for Llama1 7B finetuned on code, evaluated on HumanEval:

| Method | Exit Layer (E) | ROUGE-2 | Token Acceptance | Tokens/Sec | Speedup |
|--------|----------------|---------|------------------|------------|---------|
| Autoregressive | - | 0.0513 | - | 34 | 1.0× |
| Early Exit | 6 | 0.0035 | - | 170 | - |
| Self Speculative | 6 | 0.0513 | 45% | 62 | **1.82×** |

Notably, self-speculative decoding achieves **identical ROUGE-2 (0.0513)** as autoregressive baseline, confirming the lossless nature of speculative decoding. The token acceptance rate is relatively low at 45%, yet the speedup remains substantial at 1.82×.

**TOPv2 Semantic Parsing Speedup (Table 6)**

Table 6 presents the most impressive speedup results for Llama 1.5B finetuned on TOPv2:

| Method | Exit Layer (E) | EM | Token Acceptance | Time/Token (ms) | Speedup |
|--------|----------------|-----|------------------|-----------------|---------|
| Autoregressive | - | 85.9% | - | 36 | 1.00× |
| Early Exit | 18 | 83.3% | - | 28 | - |
| Early Exit | 12 | 79.4% | - | 19 | - |
| Early Exit | 6 | 62.9% | - | 10 | - |
| Self Speculative | 18 | 82.9% | 98.9% | 29 | 1.24× |
| Self Speculative | 12 | 82.9% | 97.6% | 22 | 1.64× |
| Self Speculative | 6 | 82.9% | 76.0% | 18 | **2.0×** |

Key observations:
*   **Very high token acceptance:** At $E=18$, acceptance reaches 98.9%, indicating the draft tokens are almost always correct.
*   **Speedup-accuracy tradeoff:** Early exit at $E=6$ drops EM from 85.9% to 62.9%, but self-speculative decoding at $E=6$ maintains 82.9% EM (only 3% drop from autoregressive) while achieving 2.0× speedup.
*   **Optimal configuration:** $E=6$ with $d=8$ speculations achieves the best speedup while maintaining near-baseline accuracy.

**CPU Inference (Appendix A.2.1)**

Table 11 in Appendix A.2.1 shows LayerSkip also achieves near 2× speedup on CPU inference for TOPv2:

| Method | Exit Layer (E) | EM | Acceptance | Time/Token (ms) |
|--------|----------------|-----|------------|-----------------|
| Autoregressive | - | 85.39% | - | 165 |
| Self Speculation | 6 | 82.9% | 76% | 87 |

This demonstrates LayerSkip's applicability beyond GPU servers to commodity hardware, addressing one of the motivations stated in Section 1.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies and Mechanism Validation

The paper includes several ablation studies that validate the underlying mechanisms driving LayerSkip's performance.

**KV Cache Reuse Impact (Table 7)**

Table 7 quantifies the benefit of the KVQ cache reuse mechanism introduced in Section 4.3.3:

| Generation | TOPv2 (ms/token) | CNN/DM (ms/token) |
|------------|------------------|-------------------|
| Self Speculation ($E=18$) | 134 | 166 |
| w.o KVQ Reuse | 143 | 182 |
| Self Speculation ($E=12$) | 104 | 165 |
| w.o KVQ Reuse | 110 | 185 |

The KVQ cache reuse saves **9-20 ms per token** depending on the task. For TOPv2 at $E=18$, latency drops from 143 ms/token to 134 ms/token (6.3% improvement). For CNN/DM at $E=18$, latency drops from 182 ms/token to 166 ms/token (8.8% improvement). This validates the claim in Section 4.3.3 that cache reuse is a meaningful optimization, though not the primary driver of speedup (which comes from skipping layers).

**Layer Dropout Configuration (Figure 12)**

Figure 12 in Appendix A.3 compares exponential layer dropout (increasing from 0 at first layer to 0.2 at last layer) against constant dropout (0.0889 on all layers, same average value). The exponential configuration achieves lower training loss throughout training, validating the design choice in Equation 3. This is a non-obvious finding: one might expect constant dropout to perform similarly given the same average dropout rate, but the exponential scaling explicitly prioritizes early layer reliability.

**Scaling with Pretraining Tokens (Figure 11)**

Figure 11 presents perhaps the most significant empirical finding: **middle layer perplexity increases drastically during standard training unless early exit loss is applied**. The experiment pretrained Llama 1.5B from scratch with different numbers of tokens (varying GPUs: 32, 64, 128 for 50,000 steps each).

*   **Last Layer (Layer 24):** Perplexity decreases as expected with more training tokens for all configurations.
*   **Middle Layer (Layer 12):** Perplexity **increases drastically** for baseline training, but remains stable for LayerSkip-LD+EE. Layer dropout alone (LD) reduces the increase, and early exit loss alone (EE) also helps, but the combination (LD+EE) is most effective.

This finding explains *why* standard pretrained models fail at early exit: standard training objectives actively push predictive capability to the final layers, causing earlier representations to diverge from the output space over time (Section 4.3). This is a theoretical insight that justifies why post-hoc early exit methods hit a performance ceiling.

**Component Ablation (Figures 8, 10)**

Figures 8 and 10 compare LayerSkip-LD (layer dropout only), LayerSkip-EE (early exit loss only), and LayerSkip-LD+EE (both combined). The results show:
*   **LD+EE consistently outperforms** either component alone at earlier layers.
*   **Early exit loss (EE) is more critical** for early layer accuracy than layer dropout alone.
*   **Last layer accuracy** is sometimes slightly better with LD only, suggesting early exit loss can marginally degrade final layer performance if not tuned carefully.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

**Claim 1: LayerSkip improves early exit accuracy without auxiliary layers.**

**Verdict: Strongly Supported.** The evidence is compelling across multiple models and tasks. Table 1 shows 73× relative improvement on NaturalQuestions for Llama2 7B middle layer. Figures 7 and 9 provide qualitative evidence of coherent text generation at early layers where baseline produces nonsense. The shared LM head design is validated by maintaining competitive last-layer accuracy while enabling early exit.

**Claim 2: Self-speculative decoding achieves speedup without accuracy loss.**

**Verdict: Supported with Conditions.** Table 5 shows identical ROUGE-2 (0.0513) for self-speculative vs autoregressive on HumanEval, confirming lossless acceleration. Table 6 shows EM drops from 85.9% to 82.9% (3% drop) for TOPv2, which is minimal given the 2.0× speedup. However, speedup varies significantly by task (1.34× to 2.16×), and token acceptance rates vary (45% to 98.9%). Tasks with higher acceptance rates yield higher speedups.

**Claim 3: KVQ cache reuse reduces latency compared to prior self-speculative decoding.**

**Verdict: Supported.** Table 7 shows 9-20 ms/token savings from KVQ reuse. The comparison with Zhang et al. (2023) in Table 3 shows LayerSkip achieves 1.81× vs 1.56× on CNN/DM for Llama2 13B, though LayerSkip is slightly slower on XSUM (1.34× vs 1.48×). The cache reuse mechanism is validated, though it is not the primary speedup driver.

**Claim 4: LayerSkip works across training scenarios.**

**Verdict: Mixed.** LayerSkip works well for pretraining from scratch (2.16× speedup) and finetuning (1.82× on HumanEval, 2.0× on TOPv2). However, continual pretraining of heavily pretrained models (Llama3) shows larger last-layer accuracy drops (Table 2), suggesting LayerSkip is more beneficial when incorporated during initial pretraining rather than as a post-hoc modification.

**Limitations and Failure Cases**

The paper acknowledges several limitations in Section 8:
*   **Retraining Requirement:** LayerSkip requires finetuning or pretraining with the specific recipe, unlike Zhang et al. (2023) which works on existing models.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** $p_{max}$, $e_{scale}$, and $R$ require tuning to avoid last layer accuracy drops. Learning rate tuning for pretraining from scratch is "tricky and time consuming."
*   **Speedup Variability:** Speedup ranges from 1.34× to 2.16× depending on task. Tasks with complex reasoning may see lower gains due to lower token acceptance rates.

**Unanswered Questions**

*   The paper does not report energy consumption or carbon footprint improvements, despite mentioning energy costs in Section 1.
*   The impact on multi-turn conversation or long-context inference is not evaluated; all experiments use maximum 512 tokens per sample.
*   The interaction with other acceleration techniques (quantization, sparsity) is not explored, though Section 9 mentions potential combination with LoRA.

### 5.6 Summary of Experimental Evidence

The experiments convincingly support LayerSkip's core claims, with important caveats:

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Key Supporting Data |
|-------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Early exit accuracy improvement | Strong | Table 1: 73× improvement on NQ; Figures 7, 9: qualitative examples |
| Self-speculative speedup | Strong | Tables 3-6: 1.34× to 2.16× speedup; Table 5: lossless acceleration |
| KVQ cache reuse benefit | Moderate | Table 7: 9-20 ms/token savings |
| Works across training scenarios | Mixed | Tables 1-2: Llama3 shows larger accuracy drops than Llama2 |
| Middle layer perplexity stability | Strong | Figure 11: baseline perplexity increases, LayerSkip remains stable |

The most compelling evidence is the middle layer perplexity ablation (Figure 11), which provides a theoretical justification for why LayerSkip works: standard training actively degrades early layer prediction capability, and LayerSkip's training recipe prevents this degradation. This insight elevates LayerSkip from an engineering optimization to a contribution with theoretical implications for transformer training dynamics.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

LayerSkip presents a compelling case for training-aware inference acceleration, but its practical deployment requires careful consideration of several constraints. This section examines the assumptions underlying the approach, identifies scenarios where it may underperform, and highlights open questions that remain unresolved.

### 6.1 Assumptions and Deployment Constraints

**Retraining Requirement**

The most significant constraint is that LayerSkip **cannot be applied to off-the-shelf pretrained models**. As explicitly stated in Section 8:

> "Our self-speculative decoding solution requires finetuning a model or pretraining it with our recipe, while the self-speculative decoding approach proposed in Zhang et al. (2023) does not require changing a model's weights."

This assumption fundamentally limits the addressable market for LayerSkip. Organizations that rely on public pretrained models (e.g., downloading Llama2 from HuggingFace) cannot benefit from LayerSkip without committing to a full finetuning or continual pretraining run. For a Llama2 7B model, the continual pretraining experiment required **64 A100 80 GB GPUs for 50,000 steps** (Table 8, Appendix A.1), representing a substantial computational investment that may exceed the expected inference savings for many users.

**Shared LM Head Assumption**

LayerSkip assumes that a single LM head can effectively unembed representations from all transformer layers. While the experimental results validate this for the tested models, this assumption may not hold universally. The paper notes that for Llama3 8B and Llama3.2 1B, there was a **larger drop in last layer accuracy** when continually pretrained with LayerSkip (Table 2). For Llama3 8B, MMLU accuracy dropped from 66.5% (baseline) to 60.5% (LayerSkip), a 6 percentage point degradation. The authors hypothesize this is because "the perplexity of earlier layers of the baseline Llama3 models are 2 or 3 orders of magnitude higher than their Llama2 counterparts," making it harder to reduce early layer perplexity while maintaining last layer accuracy.

This suggests the shared LM head assumption may break down for models trained on significantly larger corpora or with different architectural choices. Users adopting LayerSkip for newer model families should expect to validate last-layer accuracy carefully.

**Sequential Layer Execution**

The KVQ cache reuse mechanism (Section 4.3.3) assumes that draft and verification stages execute layers in sequential order (layers 1 to $E$ for drafting, layers $E+1$ to $L$ for verification). This design choice enables cache reuse but constrains the flexibility of the speculation strategy. Alternative speculation patterns—such as skipping intermediate layers during drafting (as in Zhang et al. 2023)—are incompatible with LayerSkip's cache optimization. This trade-off prioritizes memory efficiency over speculation flexibility.

### 6.2 Scenarios and Edge Cases Not Addressed

**Long-Context Inference**

All generation experiments in the paper used a **maximum of 512 tokens per sample** (Section 6.2). This is a critical limitation because the benefits of speculative decoding often scale with sequence length. Longer sequences provide more opportunities for draft token acceptance, potentially increasing speedup. Conversely, longer sequences also increase KV cache memory pressure, which could amplify LayerSkip's memory advantages—or expose bottlenecks in the KVQ cache management.

The paper does not evaluate:
*   Sequences longer than 512 tokens
*   Multi-turn conversations where context accumulates across turns
*   Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) scenarios with long retrieved documents

For applications requiring 4K, 8K, or 32K context windows (common in production LLM deployments), LayerSkip's performance characteristics remain unknown. The KVQ cache savings of 9–20 ms per token (Table 7) may become more significant at longer contexts, but this is not empirically validated.

**Dynamic Exit Conditions**

LayerSkip uses a **static exit layer** $E$ for all tokens during inference. Section 9 acknowledges this limitation:

> "In the future, we would like to increase the accuracy of early-exit layers in order to obtain better speedups during self-speculative decoding. We can also explore dynamic conditions to determine a different exit layer for each token (like Schuster et al. (2022)) and hence improve token acceptance rate of self-speculative decoding."

This is a meaningful gap. Not all tokens require the same computation:
*   Easy tokens (e.g., punctuation, common words) might exit at layer 4
*   Hard tokens (e.g., domain-specific terms, reasoning steps) might require layer 20+

A dynamic exit strategy could improve token acceptance rates beyond the 45%–98.9% range reported in Tables 5–6. However, implementing dynamic exit introduces overhead: the system must predict *which* layer to exit at for each token, potentially negating some speedup. LayerSkip leaves this optimization for future work.

**Streaming and Real-Time Applications**

The self-speculative decoding algorithm requires generating $d$ draft tokens before verification can begin (Section 4.3.1). For $d=12$ speculations (used in CNN/DM experiments, Table 3), this introduces **latency variance**: the system must buffer 12 tokens before verification, which may be unacceptable for real-time applications like chatbots or voice assistants where low time-to-first-token is critical.

The paper reports throughput (tokens per second) but does not report:
*   Time-to-first-token (TTFT)
*   Latency percentiles (p50, p95, p99)
*   Impact of speculation count $d$ on latency variance

For batched offline inference (e.g., summarization), this is less concerning. For interactive applications, the latency implications require further investigation.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

**Hyperparameter Sensitivity**

LayerSkip introduces three new hyperparameters that require careful tuning (Section 8):

| Hyperparameter | Description | Sensitivity |
|----------------|-------------|-------------|
| $p_{max}$ | Maximum dropout rate at last layer | High— affects last layer accuracy |
| $e_{scale}$ | Scale of early exit loss | High— task-dependent (0.1 for continual pretraining, 1.0 for finetuning) |
| $R$ | Dilation for rotational curriculum | Medium— affects training speed |

The paper notes that "tuning learning rate to get optimal accuracy could be tricky and time consuming" when pretraining from scratch (Section 8). Table 9 (Appendix A.1) shows that learning rates must be increased when layer dropout is applied (e.g., Llama2 7B from $3 \times 10^{-4}$ to $8 \times 10^{-4}$), but the optimal multiplier is not generalized—it must be determined empirically for each model size and training scenario.

This hyperparameter sensitivity creates a **deployment barrier**. Organizations without extensive ML engineering resources may struggle to tune these parameters effectively, risking degraded model quality.

**Speedup Variability by Task**

The reported speedups range from **1.34× to 2.16×** depending on the task (Section 1). This variability is driven by token acceptance rates, which depend on task complexity:

| Task | Token Acceptance | Speedup |
|------|------------------|---------|
| TOPv2 (semantic parsing) | 76.0%–98.9% | 2.0× |
| CNN/DM (summarization) | 68.9%–74.5% | 1.81×–1.86× |
| XSUM (summarization) | 54.6%–67.7% | 1.34×–1.54× |
| HumanEval (coding) | 45%–67.1% | 1.66×–1.83× |

Tasks requiring complex reasoning or open-ended generation (XSUM, HumanEval) show lower acceptance rates and thus lower speedups. This is expected—speculative decoding benefits most when draft tokens are predictable. However, the paper does not provide guidance on **predicting speedup for a new task** without running full benchmarks. Users must empirically measure acceptance rates for their specific use case, adding evaluation overhead.

**Model Size Scaling**

The speedup trends differ between continual pretraining and pretraining from scratch:
*   **Continual pretraining:** Smaller models achieve higher speedup (Llama2 7B: 1.86× vs Llama2 13B: 1.81× on CNN/DM, Table 3)
*   **Pretraining from scratch:** Larger models achieve higher speedup (Llama2 7B: 2.16× vs Llama2 1.5B: 1.76× on CNN/DM, Table 4)

The paper does not explain this reversal, leaving uncertainty about how LayerSkip scales to larger models (e.g., 70B parameters). If the trend from continual pretraining holds, speedups may diminish for very large models, reducing the economic incentive for adoption.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

**Accuracy-Speedup Trade-off Quantification**

While the paper claims self-speculative decoding is "lossless" in accuracy, Table 6 shows a **3% drop in Exact Match** for TOPv2 (85.9% → 82.9%) when using self-speculative decoding at $E=6$. Table 2 shows up to **6 percentage point drops** in MMLU for Llama3 8B. The paper frames these as acceptable trade-offs given the speedup, but does not provide a systematic analysis of the **Pareto frontier** between accuracy and speed.

For safety-critical applications (e.g., medical or legal text generation), even small accuracy degradations may be unacceptable. The paper does not discuss:
*   Error types introduced by early exit (hallucinations vs. grammatical errors)
*   Whether accuracy drops are consistent across demographic groups or domains
*   Calibration of confidence scores for early exit predictions

**Energy and Carbon Footprint**

Section 1 motivates LayerSkip partly on energy costs: "high compute and memory requirements lead to high financial and **energy costs** when deployed to GPU servers" (citing Samsi et al. 2023). However, the paper **does not report energy consumption or carbon footprint metrics** for any experiment.

Speedup does not directly translate to energy savings:
*   Higher throughput may encourage more inference requests (rebound effect)
*   GPU utilization patterns affect energy efficiency
*   Training LayerSkip models requires additional compute, offsetting inference savings

A complete environmental impact assessment would require measuring total energy consumption across the model lifecycle (training + inference). This remains an open question.

**Interaction with Other Acceleration Techniques**

Section 9 mentions potential combination with LoRA: "For finetuning, we hope that our proposed techniques may be combined with parameter efficient techniques suchs as LoRA" (Hu et al. 2021). However, the paper does not evaluate:
*   LayerSkip + Quantization (e.g., INT8, INT4)
*   LayerSkip + Sparsity (e.g., structured pruning)
*   LayerSkip + FlashAttention or other kernel optimizations

These combinations could compound speedups but may introduce unforeseen interactions. For example, quantization noise could degrade early layer predictions more than late layer predictions, undermining the early exit capability. This is an important direction for future work.

**Failure Mode Analysis**

The paper provides qualitative examples of baseline models generating repetitive nonsense at early layers (Figures 7, 9), but does not systematically analyze **failure modes** of LayerSkip-trained models:
*   Do early exit predictions fail more on certain token types (e.g., rare words, numbers)?
*   Are there domains where early exit consistently underperforms (e.g., code vs. natural language)?
*   Does early exit amplify biases present in the training data?

Understanding failure modes is critical for production deployment, where predictable degradation is preferable to unpredictable errors.

### 6.5 Summary of Limitations

| Category | Limitation | Severity | Mitigation |
|----------|------------|----------|------------|
| **Deployment** | Requires retraining/finetuning | High | Only viable for organizations with training infrastructure |
| **Accuracy** | Last layer accuracy drops for heavily pretrained models (Llama3) | Medium | Incorporate LayerSkip during initial pretraining |
| **Flexibility** | Static exit layer (no dynamic per-token exit) | Medium | Future work on dynamic exit conditions |
| **Latency** | Speculation buffer introduces latency variance | Medium | Tune speculation count $d$ for latency-sensitive applications |
| **Hyperparameters** | $p_{max}$, $e_{scale}$, $R$ require tuning | Medium | Provide default configurations per model size/task type |
| **Evaluation** | No long-context (>512 tokens) evaluation | Medium | Extend benchmarks to 4K+ context scenarios |
| **Metrics** | No energy/carbon footprint reporting | Low | Add energy metrics in future work |
| **Composability** | No evaluation with quantization/sparsity | Low | Explore combinations in future work |

LayerSkip represents a significant advance in training-aware inference acceleration, but its adoption requires organizations to weigh the **training investment** against the **inference savings**. For high-volume inference workloads where the same model serves millions of requests, the speedup (1.34×–2.16×) likely justifies the retraining cost. For low-volume or exploratory use cases, the retraining requirement may be prohibitive. The paper's most valuable contribution may be the theoretical insight (Figure 11) that standard training degrades early layer prediction capability—a finding that should inform future LLM training recipes regardless of whether LayerSkip itself is adopted.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

LayerSkip does more than offer a new inference acceleration technique—it challenges fundamental assumptions about how transformer models should be trained and deployed. This section examines how the work reshapes the research landscape, identifies concrete follow-up directions, outlines practical deployment scenarios, and provides actionable guidance for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this approach.

### 7.1 Paradigm Shift: Training-Aware Inference Acceleration

The most significant implication of LayerSkip is its demonstration that **inference efficiency cannot be decoupled from training objectives**. Prior work largely treated acceleration as a post-hoc optimization: quantize a pretrained model, prune its weights, or attach auxiliary exit heads. LayerSkip proves this approach leaves performance on the table.

**The Core Insight**

Figure 11 reveals a previously undocumented phenomenon: during standard pretraining, middle layer perplexity **increases drastically** even as last layer perplexity decreases. This means standard training objectives actively push predictive capability toward the final layers, causing earlier representations to diverge from the output space over time. As stated in Section 7:

> "This could open the door to more research on the dynamics of transformers and the evolution of embeddings in earlier layers to understand why embeddings across layers are close to each other early on in training but diverge drastically as training progresses."

This finding has three cascading implications:

*   **Post-hoc early exit methods hit a ceiling.** Techniques that add routers or auxiliary heads to pretrained models (Zeng et al. 2023; Raposo et al. 2024) are fighting against the natural dynamics of training. No amount of architectural patching can fully recover early layer prediction capability that was never developed.
*   **Training recipes must be co-designed with inference targets.** If an organization plans to deploy models with early exit or speculative decoding, the training objective should explicitly optimize for this capability from the start. LayerSkip's layer dropout and early exit loss (Section 4.1) should be considered part of the base training recipe, not an optional add-on.
*   **Model evaluation should include layer-wise metrics.** Standard benchmarks only evaluate the final layer output. LayerSkip suggests future model cards should report accuracy at intermediate layers (e.g., layer 8, 16, 24 for a 32-layer model) to characterize exit capability.

**Comparison to Prior Paradigms**

| Paradigm | Training Modification | Inference Flexibility | Memory Overhead |
|----------|----------------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Standard Pretraining | None | Limited (full model only) | 1× KV cache |
| Post-hoc Early Exit | Add auxiliary heads/routers | Moderate (fixed exit points) | 1× + auxiliary parameters |
| Speculative Decoding | None (two models) | High (any draft model) | 2× KV cache |
| **LayerSkip** | **Layer dropout + early exit loss** | **High (any exit layer)** | **1× KVQ cache** |

LayerSkip occupies a unique position: it requires training modification (like post-hoc early exit) but achieves the flexibility of speculative decoding with single-model memory footprint.

### 7.2 Open Research Questions and Follow-Up Directions

LayerSkip opens several concrete research avenues. Below we prioritize them by feasibility and potential impact.

**7.2.1 Dynamic Per-Token Exit Conditions**

The current implementation uses a **static exit layer** $E$ for all tokens. Section 9 explicitly identifies this as future work:

> "We can also explore dynamic conditions to determine a different exit layer for each token (like Schuster et al. (2022)) and hence improve token acceptance rate of self-speculative decoding."

*   **Research Question:** Can we predict, for each token, the minimum layer required for accurate prediction without computing all layers?
*   **Potential Approaches:**
    *   Train a lightweight classifier on early layer embeddings to predict exit depth
    *   Use confidence thresholds on early layer logits (similar to CALM Schuster et al. 2022)
    *   Learn a routing function during training that gates layer computation
*   **Expected Impact:** Token acceptance rates could improve beyond the 45%–98.9% range reported in Tables 5–6, particularly for tasks with heterogeneous token difficulty (e.g., code with mixed simple syntax and complex logic).
*   **Challenge:** The exit prediction mechanism must have negligible compute overhead, or it negates the speedup from skipping layers.

**7.2.2 Scaling to Larger Models (70B+ Parameters)**

All experiments in the paper use models up to 13B parameters (Llama2 13B) or 8B (Llama3 8B). The speedup trends show inconsistency:

*   **Continual pretraining:** Smaller models achieve higher speedup (Llama2 7B: 1.86× vs 13B: 1.81× on CNN/DM, Table 3)
*   **Pretraining from scratch:** Larger models achieve higher speedup (Llama2 7B: 2.16× vs 1.5B: 1.76× on CNN/DM, Table 4)

*   **Research Question:** How does LayerSkip scale to 70B+ parameter models, and does the speedup trend reverse again?
*   **Hypothesis:** For very large models, the relative cost of early layers becomes smaller, potentially reducing speedup. However, memory savings from single-model KV cache become more valuable at larger scales.
*   **Expected Impact:** If speedup diminishes for 70B+ models, LayerSkip's value proposition shifts from latency reduction to memory efficiency—critical for deploying large models on memory-constrained hardware.

**7.2.3 Combination with Other Acceleration Techniques**

Section 9 mentions potential combination with LoRA but does not evaluate it. Several combinations remain unexplored:

*   **LayerSkip + Quantization:** INT8 or INT4 quantization could compound speedups. However, quantization noise may degrade early layer predictions more than late layer predictions, undermining exit capability. Research needed on quantization-aware LayerSkip training.
*   **LayerSkip + Sparsity:** Structured pruning (e.g., head pruning Shim et al. 2021) could be applied to later layers while preserving early layer capacity for drafting.
*   **LayerSkip + LoRA:** For finetuning scenarios, LoRA adapters (Hu et al. 2021) could be applied only to later layers, reducing finetuning compute while preserving early exit capability.
*   **LayerSkip + FlashAttention:** Kernel-level optimizations are orthogonal to LayerSkip and should compound speedups. No interaction issues expected.

**7.2.4 Long-Context and Multi-Turn Inference**

All generation experiments used a **maximum of 512 tokens per sample** (Section 6.2). This is a critical gap:

*   **Research Question:** Does LayerSkip's speedup scale with sequence length, or does KV cache management overhead dominate for long contexts?
*   **Expected Behavior:** For 4K–32K context windows:
    *   **Benefit:** More tokens provide more opportunities for draft acceptance, potentially increasing speedup
    *   **Risk:** KVQ cache management overhead may increase, and memory savings from single cache become more valuable
*   **Evaluation Needed:** Benchmarks on long-context tasks (e.g., document QA, multi-turn conversation, RAG scenarios) with 4K+ token sequences.

**7.2.5 Energy and Carbon Footprint Measurement**

Section 1 motivates LayerSkip partly on energy costs, but **no energy metrics are reported** in any experiment. This is a significant omission:

*   **Research Question:** Does LayerSkip's speedup translate to proportional energy savings, or does higher GPU utilization offset the gains?
*   **Measurement Needed:**
    *   Total energy consumption (training + inference lifecycle)
    *   Carbon footprint per million tokens generated
    *   Comparison of energy efficiency vs. quantization and sparsity methods
*   **Expected Impact:** If LayerSkip reduces energy per token by 40%–50% (matching speedup), it becomes compelling for organizations with sustainability targets.

**7.2.6 Failure Mode Characterization**

The paper provides qualitative examples of baseline models generating repetitive nonsense at early layers (Figures 7, 9), but does not systematically analyze LayerSkip's failure modes:

*   **Research Question:** Do early exit predictions fail more on certain token types (rare words, numbers, domain-specific terms)?
*   **Evaluation Needed:**
    *   Error analysis by token category (punctuation, common words, rare words, numbers)
    *   Domain-specific performance (code vs. natural language vs. math)
    *   Bias amplification analysis (does early exit amplify demographic biases?)
*   **Expected Impact:** Understanding failure modes enables targeted mitigation (e.g., force full computation for certain token types).

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

LayerSkip's value proposition varies by deployment scenario. Below we identify use cases where the trade-offs are most favorable.

**7.3.1 High-Volume Inference Services**

*   **Scenario:** Organizations serving millions of inference requests daily (e.g., chatbots, summarization APIs, code completion services)
*   **Why LayerSkip Fits:**
    *   Speedup of 1.34×–2.16× (Section 1) directly reduces infrastructure costs
    *   Single-model memory footprint enables higher batch sizes on the same hardware
    *   Retraining cost is amortized over millions of inference requests
*   **Example Calculation:** For a service generating 1 billion tokens/month at $0.002/1K tokens revenue:
    *   **Baseline cost:** ~$2,000/month in compute
    *   **With 1.8× speedup:** ~$1,100/month in compute
    *   **Annual savings:** ~$10,800 per model
    *   **Retraining cost:** ~$5,000–$20,000 (one-time, depending on model size)
    *   **Break-even:** 1–2 months for high-volume services

**7.3.2 Edge and Mobile Deployment**

*   **Scenario:** Deploying LLMs on devices with limited VRAM (laptops, smartphones, edge servers)
*   **Why LayerSkip Fits:**
    *   Single KV cache reduces memory pressure compared to speculative decoding's 2× overhead
    *   CPU inference experiments (Table 11, Appendix A.2.1) show near 2× speedup on TOPv2
    *   No specialized kernels required (unlike quantization or sparsity)
*   **Constraint:** Retraining requirement may be prohibitive for small teams. Best suited for organizations that already finetune models for edge deployment.

**7.3.3 Domain-Specific Finetuning**

*   **Scenario:** Organizations finetuning models on proprietary data (e.g., legal documents, medical records, internal codebases)
*   **Why LayerSkip Fits:**
    *   Finetuning experiments show strong results: 1.82× on HumanEval (Table 5), 2.0× on TOPv2 (Table 6)
    *   Higher $e_{scale} = 1.0$ can be used for task-specific finetuning (Section 5), enabling stronger early exit supervision
    *   Retraining is already part of the workflow, so LayerSkip adds minimal overhead
*   **Recommendation:** Incorporate LayerSkip recipe into standard finetuning pipelines for domain-specific models.

**7.3.4 Scenarios Where LayerSkip Is Less Suitable**

*   **Off-the-Shelf Model Usage:** If you download pretrained models from HuggingFace without finetuning, LayerSkip is not applicable. Use standard speculative decoding (Leviathan et al. 2023) or quantization instead.
*   **Latency-Sensitive Real-Time Applications:** Self-speculative decoding requires buffering $d$ draft tokens before verification (Section 4.3.1). For $d=12$, this introduces latency variance that may be unacceptable for voice assistants or chatbots requiring low time-to-first-token.
*   **Safety-Critical Applications:** The 3% accuracy drop on TOPv2 (Table 6) and up to 6 percentage point drop on MMLU for Llama3 (Table 2) may be unacceptable for medical or legal text generation. Use full model inference or implement strict verification thresholds.

### 7.5 Summary: The Path Forward

LayerSkip demonstrates that **inference efficiency is a training objective, not just a deployment optimization**. The key takeaways for the field are:

1.  **Standard training degrades early layer capability.** Figure 11's finding that middle layer perplexity increases during standard training should inform all future LLM training recipes, regardless of whether early exit is planned.
2.  **Single-model speculative decoding is viable.** LayerSkip proves that draft and verification stages can share a single model and KV cache without sacrificing speedup, reducing memory pressure for deployment.
3.  **Retraining is the price of efficiency.** Organizations must weigh the one-time training cost against ongoing inference savings. For high-volume deployments, the economics are favorable.

**Immediate Next Steps for Researchers:**
*   Replicate Figure 11 on other model families (GPT, PaLM, Mistral) to confirm the middle layer perplexity phenomenon
*   Explore dynamic per-token exit conditions to improve token acceptance rates
*   Evaluate LayerSkip on 70B+ parameter models to understand scaling behavior
*   Measure energy consumption to quantify environmental impact

**Immediate Next Steps for Practitioners:**
*   If you control training: Incorporate LayerSkip recipe into finetuning pipelines
*   If you use off-the-shelf models: Monitor for LayerSkip-compatible model releases
*   For all deployments: Benchmark early exit accuracy on your specific tasks before committing to the approach

LayerSkip shifts the conversation from "how do we accelerate inference?" to "how should we train models to be inherently acceleratable?" This reframing may prove to be the paper's most lasting contribution, influencing LLM training recipes for years to come.
