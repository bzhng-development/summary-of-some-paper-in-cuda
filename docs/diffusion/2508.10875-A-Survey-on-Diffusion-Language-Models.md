## 1. Executive Summary

This survey provides a holistic taxonomy and analysis of Diffusion Language Models (DLMs), tracing their evolution from continuous frameworks like Diffusion-LM to discrete architectures such as LLaDA-8B and multimodal systems like MMaDA. It addresses the fundamental inference latency bottleneck of autoregressive models by demonstrating how DLMs achieve comparable performance (e.g., matching LLaMA3-8B on GSM8K) with several-fold speedups through parallel decoding and specialized training techniques like diffu-GRPO. The work underscores the growing practicality of DLMs, citing industry implementations like Mercury and Gemini Diffusion that reach thousands of tokens per second, while mapping critical challenges in scalability and infrastructure for future research.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### 2.1 The Core Problem: Inference Latency in Autoregressive Language Models

The dominant paradigm in modern language modeling—autoregressive (AR) generation—faces a fundamental architectural constraint that this survey addresses. AR models like the GPT series [1], [13], [14] and LLaMA [4], [179] generate text **sequentially, one token at a time**. This design choice creates what the paper identifies as a "major bottleneck on inference speed" (Section 1).

To understand why this matters, consider the mathematical formulation. AR models factorize the joint probability of a sequence as:

$$P(x) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P_\theta(x_i | x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_{i-1})$$

This factorization (Equation 3 in the paper) means each token $x_i$ **must** wait for all preceding tokens to be generated before it can be predicted. During inference, this translates to:

- **No parallelism across tokens**: Even with massive GPU clusters, you cannot generate token 50 until tokens 1–49 are complete.
- **Linear latency scaling**: Generation time grows proportionally with sequence length.
- **Underutilized hardware**: Modern accelerators excel at parallel computation, but AR generation leaves this capacity idle during decoding.

The paper notes that while techniques like multi-token prediction [16], [156] attempt to mitigate this, they remain conceptually limited by the sequential dependency structure.

### 2.2 Why Diffusion Models Offer a Different Path

Diffusion models emerged as a highly successful generative paradigm for **continuous domains** like images and video [8]–[12], [17]–[21]. Their core mechanism differs fundamentally from AR:

> "They are trained to recover data from progressively noised versions through a denoising process, and generate new samples by reversing this stochastic corruption step by step." (Section 1)

The key insight is that diffusion models operate through **iterative refinement** rather than sequential prediction. A forward process gradually corrupts data:

$$q(x_{1:T} | x_0) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} q(x_t | x_{t-1})$$

Then a learned reverse process denoises from pure noise $x_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ back to clean data $x_0$ (Equations 6–7).

**Why this matters for language:**

- **Parallel token generation**: All positions in a sequence can be updated simultaneously at each denoising step.
- **Bidirectional context**: Every token prediction sees the entire sequence context, not just preceding tokens.
- **Iterative refinement**: Low-confidence predictions can be revised in later steps—unlike AR models where early mistakes propagate irreversibly.

The paper emphasizes that through this iterative denoising process, DLMs "can generate multiple tokens or an entire sequence simultaneously, potentially leading to superior inference throughput" (Section 1).

### 2.3 The Gap: Adapting Diffusion to Discrete Language Data

Here lies the central technical challenge that motivated the research landscape this survey covers. Diffusion models were designed for **continuous spaces** (pixel values, latent embeddings), but language is inherently **discrete** (tokens from a vocabulary).

The paper traces two distinct approaches that emerged to bridge this gap:

**Continuous DLMs** map tokens into embeddings and perform denoising in continuous space [22], [23]. Early works like Diffusion-LM [22] and SED [23] pioneered this direction. However, this approach requires a **rounding step** to map denoised embeddings back to discrete tokens—typically via nearest-neighbor search or thresholding [163]. This introduces approximation errors and can degrade generation quality.

**Discrete DLMs** define the diffusion process directly on token space. The foundational work D3PM [24] introduced structured transition matrices with absorbing states, allowing token-level corruption (e.g., transitioning to a `[MASK]` token). The probability of a state $x_t$ given initial state $x_0$ follows:

$$q(x_t | x_0) = \text{Cat}(x_t; p = x_0 \bar{Q}_t)$$

where $\bar{Q}_t = \prod_{i=1}^{t} Q_i$ (Section 2.3).

**The performance gap problem:** Early DLMs "demonstrated the feasibility of applying iterative denoising to non-autoregressive text generation... though their performance still lagged behind strong autoregressive baselines" (Section 1). This created skepticism about whether DLMs could ever compete with AR models at scale.

Recent breakthroughs changed this assessment. The paper highlights that "7B-level models like Dream [26] and DiffuLLaMA [27] have shown that DLMs can be effectively adapted from existing models while achieving competitive performance." More significantly, "LLaDA-8B [28] further demonstrates the potential of training DLMs from scratch, achieving performance comparable to similarly sized LLaMA3-8B models" (Section 1).

### 2.4 Why This Survey Was Necessary: The Missing Systematic Overview

Despite rapid progress, the paper identifies a critical gap in the literature:

> "Despite the recent rise in popularity of DLMs, there remains a lack of a comprehensive survey that systematically covers the entire DLM ecosystem." (Section 1)

This gap matters for several reasons:

**Fragmented knowledge:** Research on DLMs spans multiple communities—NLP researchers working on discrete diffusion, vision researchers adapting image diffusion to text, and systems engineers optimizing inference. Without a unified taxonomy, insights remain siloed.

**Rapid evolution:** Figure 1 in the paper shows a timeline from D3PM (2021) to recent multimodal models like LLaDA-V [29] and MMaDA [31] (2025). Figure 2 quantifies this growth, showing accelerating publication rates in both discrete and continuous DLM research. A researcher entering this field needs a structured map of what exists.

**Training and inference complexity:** DLMs introduce novel considerations absent in AR training:
- Loss computation only on masked tokens (typically ~50% of positions)
- Train-inference discrepancy where models perform better during training than at inference time [172]
- Specialized decoding strategies like unmasking/remasking schedules [28], [47]
- Reinforcement learning adaptations like diffu-GRPO [43] and UniGRPO [31] for reasoning

**Multimodal extension opportunities:** The paper notes that "by applying a shared denoising-based modeling framework, DLMs naturally support unified text and vision generation tasks" (Section 1). Models like MMaDA [31] and D-DiT [84] demonstrate this potential, but the design space remains underexplored compared to AR-based multimodal models.

This survey positions itself as the **first holistic taxonomy** covering the complete DLM landscape. The authors structure their contribution across eight dimensions (Section 1):

The paper explicitly distinguishes itself from prior surveys on large language models [6] or diffusion models for images [17] by focusing exclusively on the **language modeling application of diffusion**. **Critical positioning insight:** The survey does not claim DLMs have already surpassed AR models. Instead, it presents evidence that DLMs are now **competitive** on many benchmarks while offering distinct advantages in inference speed and controllability. This balanced assessment—acknowledging both progress and remaining limitations—positions the survey as a foundation for future research rather than a declaration of paradigm shift. The authors explicitly note that "the largest publicly available DLM currently contains only around 8B parameters, significantly smaller than leading AR models" scaled to hundreds of billions (Section 8.1), indicating substantial headroom for improvement.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

This survey examines Diffusion Language Models (DLMs), which are neural network systems that generate text through an iterative denoising process rather than predicting tokens one-by-one sequentially. The core problem these systems solve is the inference latency bottleneck inherent in autoregressive language models, and the solution takes the shape of parallel token generation where all positions in a sequence are refined simultaneously across multiple denoising steps.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

A complete DLM system consists of four major components that work together during training and inference. First, the **Corruption Module** takes clean text sequences and applies a forward diffusion process that gradually adds noise or masks tokens over T timesteps, producing progressively corrupted versions of the input. Second, the **Denoiser Network** (typically a Transformer architecture) receives these corrupted sequences along with timestep information and predicts either the original clean tokens or the noise that was added. Third, the **Training Objective Module** computes loss only over masked or corrupted positions, using cross-entropy for discrete models or mean-squared error for continuous models, with typical masking ratios around 50% of tokens. Fourth, the **Inference Engine** starts from fully masked or noisy sequences and iteratively applies the denoiser across multiple steps, using unmasking/remasking schedules to progressively reveal high-confidence tokens while keeping uncertain positions masked for further refinement. Information flows from clean training data through the corruption module to create training pairs, then through the denoiser network to produce predictions, then through the loss module to compute gradients for weight updates, and finally during inference the denoiser operates in reverse to generate new sequences from noise.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the foundational mathematical formulation of diffusion processes in both continuous and discrete spaces, because understanding the forward and reverse processes is prerequisite to grasping how DLMs differ from autoregressive models.
- We then explain the two primary paradigm categories (continuous vs. discrete DLMs) and their respective training objectives, since this distinction determines everything from loss computation to inference strategies.
- We proceed to training methodologies including pre-training strategies and post-training reinforcement learning adaptations, because DLMs require specialized techniques to match autoregressive performance on reasoning tasks.
- We detail inference optimization techniques including parallel decoding, caching mechanisms, and step distillation, since these are critical for achieving the speedup advantages that motivate DLM research.
- We conclude with hybrid AR-diffusion approaches that combine both paradigms, as these represent an important middle ground that leverages strengths from each approach.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This survey paper systematically catalogs and analyzes the technical approaches underlying Diffusion Language Models, with the core idea being that text generation can be formulated as reversing a gradual corruption process rather than predicting tokens sequentially from left to right.

**Foundational Mathematical Framework**

The diffusion process consists of two phases that operate in opposite directions. The forward process gradually transforms clean data $x_0$ into noise over $T$ timesteps through a fixed Markov chain defined as $q(x_{1:T} | x_0) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} q(x_t | x_{t-1})$, where each transition $q(x_t | x_{t-1})$ adds a small amount of corruption according to a predefined noise schedule. In continuous space implementations, the marginal distribution at each timestep has a closed-form expression given by $x_t = \alpha_t x_0 + b_t \epsilon$, where $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ represents Gaussian noise, and $\alpha_t$ and $b_t$ are deterministic functions of time $t$ that control how much signal versus noise appears at each step. The reverse process learns to invert this corruption by starting from pure noise $x_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ and gradually denoising to recover a sample close to the original $x_0$, parameterized by a neural network $f_\theta(x_t, t)$ that predicts a target quantity associated with the forward process. The training objective takes the form $\mathcal{L}_{\text{simple}} = \mathbb{E}_{t, x_0, z}[\|f_\theta(x_t, t) - z\|^2]$, where $x_t$ is sampled via the forward process given $x_0$, and $z$ is the corresponding regression target derived from $x_0$ and $t$.

For discrete language data, the formulation differs fundamentally because tokens cannot be gradually noised in the same way as continuous values. The D3PM framework introduces a structured diffusion process over discrete tokens where the forward process corrupts a sequence by applying a transition matrix $Q_t$ at each step, with the probability of state $x_t$ given initial state $x_0$ given by $q(x_t | x_0) = \text{Cat}(x_t; p = x_0 \bar{Q}_t)$, where $\bar{Q}_t = \prod_{i=1}^{t} Q_i$ represents the cumulative transition matrix. A common choice for $Q_t$ is an absorbing state transition where each token has a probability of either remaining unchanged or transitioning to a special `[MASK]` token, which enables the model to learn to recover masked tokens from context.

**Continuous vs. Discrete DLM Paradigms**

Continuous DLMs map discrete tokens into a continuous embedding space and perform denoising in that continuous space, as pioneered by Diffusion-LM [22] and SED [23]. After generating a denoised embedding $\hat{x}_0$, a rounding step maps it back to a discrete token, typically done by nearest-neighbor searching in the embedding space, using a decoder head, or thresholding techniques. This approach enables the use of standard diffusion objectives but introduces approximation errors during the rounding step that can degrade generation quality. Representative models in this category include Diffusion-LM with 100M and 300M parameters, SED with unspecified parameters, and TESS-2 scaled to 7B parameters trained on 360B tokens according to Table 1.

Discrete DLMs define the diffusion process directly on the vocabulary of tokens, avoiding the need for a continuous embedding space during the diffusion itself. The LLaDA model [28], which serves as the most representative example of this approach, is trained from scratch using a cross-entropy loss computed only over masked tokens:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) \triangleq -\mathbb{E}_{t, x_0, x_t}\left[\frac{1}{t} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \mathbb{1}[x_t^i = M] \log p_\theta(x_0^i | x_t)\right]$$

where $x_0$ is sampled from the training corpus, $t$ is sampled uniformly from $[0, 1]$, $x_t$ is obtained by corrupting $x_0$ through the forward process, and the indicator function $\mathbb{1}[\cdot]$ ensures that the loss is applied only to positions that have been masked. During inference, the generation process starts with a fully masked sequence of desired length, and in each iterative step the model takes the current sequence containing a mix of generated tokens and `[MASK]` tokens to predict a complete sequence of tokens. Based on the model's prediction confidence and noise schedule, a certain number of the highest-confidence predictions are unmasked and fixed, while the remaining positions are re-masked, and this refinement process continues iteratively until all `[MASK]` tokens are resolved. LLaDA-8B exhibits strong scalability and instruction-following ability, achieving performance on par with LLaMA3-8B according to Section 2.3.

The design choice between continuous and discrete paradigms involves trade-offs in implementation complexity versus generation quality. Continuous models can leverage well-established diffusion techniques from image generation but require careful handling of the embedding-to-token rounding step. Discrete models operate directly on the token space that matters for language but require specialized training objectives and inference strategies. The paper notes that "while early research predominantly focused on continuous DLMs, discrete DLMs have gained increasing popularity in more recent years" as shown in Figure 1, indicating the field has converged toward discrete approaches for better language modeling performance.

**Training Methodologies and Configurations**

Pre-training for DLMs largely follows procedures similar to autoregressive language models for discrete DLMs or image diffusion models for continuous DLMs, with relatively fewer design spaces according to Section 3.1. To accelerate training, particularly for large-scale models, it is common practice to initialize DLMs from pretrained AR language models or image diffusion models. DiffuGPT and DiffuLLaMA [27] initialize masked DLMs with open-sourced LLMs from 127M to 7B parameters and found that DLMs can be efficiently adapted from AR models, significantly reducing training time and cost while achieving comparable or even superior performance. Dream-7B [26] is initialized from Qwen 2.5 7B [164] and is reported to outperform both LLaDA-8B and LLaMA3-8B on various benchmarks. Some multimodal DLMs are initialized from pretrained image diffusion models, with D-DiT [84] and Muddit [85] initialized from pretrained MM-DiT backbones from SD3 [11] and Meissonic [169] respectively.

In terms of scaling properties, recent scaling-law analyses reveal that DLMs exhibit distinct compute-data tradeoffs from AR models: they are substantially more data-hungry under compute constraints, yet possess far greater data reuse potential under multi-epoch training. LLaDA-8B [28] was trained on 2.3T tokens, while LLaDA-MoE [75] was trained on 20T tokens despite activating only about 1.4B parameters during inference. Dream-7B [26] was trained with 580B tokens after initialization from Qwen2.5 7B. These training data scales are comparable to autoregressive models of similar size, indicating that DLMs do not fundamentally require more data but may benefit from different training regimes.

A major challenge in masked DLM training lies in loss computation efficiency. In typical masked DLM training, only approximately 50% of tokens on average are involved in the loss computation if timesteps are sampled uniformly, which reduces data utilization and may lead to suboptimal gradients particularly if critical answer tokens are excluded from the loss. To address this, LaViDa [110] proposes a complementary masking strategy where each training sample is duplicated with two disjoint masking patterns, ensuring that all tokens are included in the loss computation at least once. Furthermore, due to train-inference discrepancy, the model performs significantly better during training than at inference time, and researchers propose a two-step diffusion process and improved scheduling technique to mitigate this issue.

Supervised fine-tuning in DLMs generally mirrors that of AR models. For masked DLMs like LLaDA [28], prompt tokens are left unmasked while response tokens are selectively masked, enabling the model to learn conditional response generation in a manner compatible with pre-training. In continuous DLMs, SFT can also be performed by corrupting only the response segment, as demonstrated in TESS2 [60].

**Post-Training for Reasoning Capabilities**

Post-training techniques for DLMs address the unique challenge that traditional Chain-of-Thought methods are based on the sequential nature of AR models to reason step-by-step, but DLMs generate tokens in parallel. The most successful post-training techniques in the AR domain, particularly those based on reinforcement learning and policy gradient methods, are built upon the ability to efficiently compute the log-probability of a generated sequence, which is straightforward in AR models due to their factorizable sequential nature but intractable in DLMs where generation is an iterative non-sequential process.

Diffusion-of-Thought (DoT) [86] adapts the Chain-of-Thought paradigm to the diffusion framework by formulating reasoning steps as intermediate thoughts that are refined in parallel throughout the diffusion denoising process rather than generated sequentially. The approach is implemented by fine-tuning pre-trained DLMs such as Plaid [67] and SEDD [68] on datasets containing problems and their corresponding step-by-step rationales, and introduces specialized training techniques like scheduled sampling and coupled sampling which expose the model to its own generated errors during training to improve self-correction capabilities.

Diffusion Chain of Lateral Thought (DCoLT) [87] introduces a distinct RL-based reasoning framework that treats each step of the reverse diffusion process as a latent thinking action but optimizes the entire multi-step denoising trajectory with outcome-based RL to maximize a reward on the final answer. When applied to masked DLMs like LLaDA, DCoLT introduces an Unmasking Policy Module which learns the optimal order for revealing tokens as part of the RL action space, achieving gains of +9.8% on GSM8K and +19.5% on HumanEval according to Section 3.2.1.

Score Entropy Policy Optimization (SEPO) [88] introduces RLHF to discrete DLMs by proposing a theoretically grounded framework to fine-tune discrete diffusion models using policy gradient methods and non-differentiable rewards. The objective function is defined as:

$$l_A(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}}\left[\sum_{y \in \mathcal{X}, y \neq x} w_{x,y} \log s_\theta(x, T-T_0)_y\right]$$

where the model parameters $\theta$ are optimized to maximize the expected log-likelihood of the score entropy $s_\theta$ weighted by $w_{x,y} = \pi_\theta(y) f(r_{x,y}^{T-T_0})$, the expectation is taken over samples $x$ from the previous policy $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$, and the function $f$ can be selected to recover different policy gradient variants such as clipped functions for PPO or group-standardized rewards for GRPO.

The d1 framework [43] provides a two-stage post-training approach combining supervised fine-tuning with diffu-GRPO, a novel policy gradient algorithm adapted for DLMs. To adapt GRPO for DLMs which lack a factorized likelihood, d1 introduces novel methods for both sequence log-probability and per-token log-probability estimation using a simple mean-field decomposition to approximate sequence log-probability by a product of independent per-token probabilities. Per-token log-probability is computed by performing a single forward pass on a fully masked completion conditioned on a randomly masked prompt during each policy gradient update, and using different random masks for the prompt in each inner gradient update step serves as a form of regularization improving training efficiency and stability.

Coupled-GRPO [89], introduced for the DiffuCoder [89] 7B-parameter model developed for code generation, constructs paired complementary masks for each completion sequence in a training batch where every token position is masked in exactly one of the two masks. The log-probability estimate is then derived by averaging the losses from these two complementary forward passes, ensuring that every token is evaluated in a partial-masking context during training and providing full token coverage with a more stable gradient signal compared to methods using a single random mask or full mask.

LLaDA 1.5 [95] proposes Variance-Reduced Preference Optimization (VRPO) to adapt preference optimization methods to discrete DLMs, addressing the challenge that applying Direct Preference Optimization to discrete DLMs is difficult due to the high variance of the Evidence Lower Bound used to approximate log-likelihoods. VRPO introduces two key unbiased variance reduction techniques: optimal allocation of the Monte Carlo sampling budget by sampling more diffusion timesteps rather than multiple masked versions per timestep (specifically $n_t = n$ and $n_{y_t} = 1$), and antithetic sampling where the same timesteps and masked data are shared between the ELBO estimates of the current policy $\pi_\theta$ and the reference policy $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ for the same input.

**Inference Strategies and Optimizations**

Parallel decoding naturally aligns with DLMs by leveraging their inherent mask-predict capability to generate multiple tokens simultaneously rather than sequentially. Fast-dLLM [45] employs confidence-aware decoding by selectively unmasking tokens whose predicted probabilities exceed a threshold, realizing up to 27.6× speedups without compromising quality. Adaptive Parallel Decoding (APD) [46] modulates the degree of parallelism on-the-fly by consulting a lightweight autoregressive auxiliary model, thus trading throughput for fidelity when necessary. SlowFast Sampling [96] introduces a two-stage schedule with a cautious "slow" phase to locate stable tokens followed by an aggressive "fast" phase to finalize them in bulk, achieving up to 34× acceleration when combined with caching. SpecDiff [80] uses a discrete diffusion model as a fully parallel drafter whose output is quickly verified and corrected if needed by a larger autoregressive model, yielding up to 7.2× speedups over vanilla AR generation. Dimple [30] employs confident parallel decoding by dynamically adapting the number of tokens revealed per step and cutting generation iterations by 1.5 to 7×.

Unmasking and remasking strategies determine which tokens are fixed versus refined at each diffusion step, and this choice dominates both generation quality and convergence speed. Early work Masked DLM [64] formalized two baselines: random remasking and confidence-ranked remasking, showing that prioritizing low-confidence positions yields better quality at no extra cost. Fast-dLLM [45] introduces confidence-aware parallel decoding where every step it unmasks all positions whose predicted probabilities exceed a global threshold, realizing up to 13× speedups while maintaining accuracy. ReMDM [47] proposes a principled inference-time remasking sampler that can remask already decoded tokens for further refinement, and by scaling the remasking budget it offers a smooth compute-quality trade-off that closes the quality gap with autoregressive models under fixed compute.

Guidance techniques steer the generative trajectory toward desired attributes and enhance output quality. Classifier-free guidance, which underpins most text-to-image systems and has been adopted by DLMs for prompt-controlled generation, combines conditional and unconditional score estimates as:

$$s_{\text{guided}} = s_{\text{uncond}} + \lambda(s_{\text{cond}} - s_{\text{uncond}})$$

where $\lambda$ is the guidance scale that balances fidelity to the condition against sample diversity. FreeCache [100] couples a lightweight autoregressive verifier with a discrete DLM where the verifier approves or vetoes draft tokens before they are committed, simultaneously enforcing coherence and enabling aggressive feature caching. DINGO [101] formulates regular-expression control as a dynamic-programming search over a DFA, guaranteeing constraint satisfaction without altering the model distribution.

Key-Value Cache adaptations address the challenge that conventional KV cache leverages the strictly autoregressive decoding pattern of LLMs and is therefore ill-suited to the bidirectional multi-step generation paradigm of DLMs. Block Diffusion [78] introduces Block Discrete Denoising Diffusion Language Models which decode text autoregressively across coarse blocks while running diffusion within each block, and once a block is finished its keys and values are frozen and reused, enabling variable-length generation and measurable speedups. Fast-dLLM [45] keeps the blockwise view but adds a training-free approximate DualCache that exploits the near-identity of KV activations across successive diffusion steps for both prefix and suffix tokens, delivering up to 27× end-to-end throughput gains on LLaDA and Dream with less than 1% accuracy loss. dKV-Cache [49] observes that token representations stabilize only after a position is decoded and therefore deploys a delayed conditional cache that stores KVs one step later, achieving 2 to 10× speedups on the same models with negligible quality drop. d2Cache [103] introduces a fine-grained dual adaptive caching scheme that adaptively refreshes only rapidly changing KV states while reusing stable ones. Elastic-Cache [102] proposes an attention/depth-aware adaptive refresh mechanism that selectively updates deeper layers and reuses stable shallow-layer caches, performing attention-based drift detection to trigger cache refreshes only when the most-attended tokens exhibit significant changes, yielding up to 45× speedup with minimal quality loss.

Feature caching leverages the strong similarity of intermediate activations across consecutive diffusion steps to avoid redundant computation. dLLM-Cache [48] extends feature caching to text by distinguishing two redundancies: prompt tokens remain almost static throughout denoising whereas response tokens evolve only sparsely, so it pairs a long-interval prompt cache with an adaptive short-interval response cache refreshed only when a lightweight value-similarity test detects substantial change, achieving up to 9× end-to-end speedups on LLaDA-8B and Dream-7B. FreeCache [100] caches the KV/feature projections of already clean tokens and refreshes only dynamic positions, pushing acceleration further to 34× while preserving fidelity.

Step distillation collapses the typical thousand-step denoising process into only a few and sometimes even a single sampling step, thereby drastically reducing inference time. Di4C [108] extends the framework to discrete diffusion by explicitly distilling inter-token correlations, enabling four to ten step students that match teacher quality while providing approximately 2× speedups. DLM-One [44] employs score-based distillation with adversarial regularization to train a continuous diffusion language model that generates an entire sequence in a single forward pass, achieving up to 500× acceleration with near-teacher quality.

**Hybrid AR-Diffusion Approaches**

Hybrid AR-Diffusion models aim to strike a balance between the full parallelism of non-autoregressive models and the strong causal dependency modeling of autoregressive models. A prominent strategy adopts a block-wise semi-autoregressive generation process where the model generates blocks of tokens autoregressively while the tokens within each block are generated in parallel using a diffusion-like iterative process. The generation process consists of two nested loops: in the outer loop, blocks of tokens are generated autoregressively with each block conditioned on previously generated blocks, and within each block the inner loop performs parallel token-wise generation through a diffusion-style iterative denoising process.

In BD3-LM [78], the training objective is formalized as:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{BD}}(x, \theta) := -\sum_{b=1}^{B} \mathbb{E}_{t \sim [0,1]} \mathbb{E}_q\left[\frac{1}{t} \log p_\theta(x^b | x_t^b, x^{&lt;b})\right]$$

This hybrid strategy enables the model to capture long-range dependencies across blocks via autoregression while simultaneously accelerating generation within each block through parallel diffusion, and the design also supports flexible output lengths and KV-Cache which is widely used in AR models.

TiDAR [82] proposes a sequence-level hybrid architecture that integrates diffusion-based parallel drafting and autoregressive sampling within a single forward pass through structured causal-bidirectional attention, effectively unifying the efficiency of diffusion models with the quality of AR decoding and achieving up to 5× throughput improvements while maintaining AR-level performance. SDLM [83] introduces the Next Sequence Prediction paradigm which unifies next-token and next-block prediction to enable adaptive-length generation, and by retrofitting pretrained autoregressive models with parallel block training and confidence-based dynamic decoding, SDLM achieves efficient diffusion-style intra-block generation while remaining KV-cache compatible.

**Design Choices and Rationale**

The choice to use discrete diffusion over continuous diffusion for language modeling stems from the fundamental mismatch between continuous embeddings and discrete token semantics. Continuous models require a rounding step that introduces approximation errors, while discrete models operate directly on the token space that matters for language evaluation metrics. The paper shows that discrete DLMs have gained increasing popularity in recent years as shown in Figure 1, with representative models like LLaDA-8B achieving performance comparable to LLaMA3-8B.

The masking ratio of approximately 50% during training represents a trade-off between training signal availability and task difficulty. Too little masking provides insufficient learning signal for the denoising objective, while too much masking makes the task too difficult and slows convergence. The complementary masking strategy proposed by LaViDa [110] addresses the inefficiency where critical answer tokens might be excluded from loss computation by ensuring all tokens are covered across duplicated samples.

The decision to initialize DLMs from pretrained AR models rather than training from scratch reflects practical considerations about compute costs and data requirements. Dream-7B [26] and DiffuLLaMA [27] demonstrate that adaptation from AR models significantly reduces training time and cost while achieving comparable performance, though LLaDA-8B [28] shows that training from scratch is also viable with sufficient resources (2.3T tokens).

Parallel decoding strategies must balance speed against coherence, as unmasking too many tokens simultaneously increases the denoising burden and can lead to error accumulation known as the Parallel Decoding Curse [45]. The paper illustrates in Figure 7 that both LLaDA and MMaDA generate correct and coherent responses only when 1 or 2 tokens are unmasked at each step, and with fewer steps and more parallelism the responses become incorrect or lack fluency and consistency. This explains why adaptive strategies like Fast-dLLM's confidence-aware decoding and APD's dynamic parallelism modulation are necessary rather than always unmasking the maximum number of tokens.

The infrastructure gap between AR and diffusion models represents a practical barrier to adoption. While AR models benefit from highly optimized libraries like Hugging Face Transformers [199] and deployment infrastructure like vLLM [200], DLMs currently lack mature open-source deployment infrastructure, making efficient serving difficult. This challenge is separate from the algorithmic innovations but critically important for real-world adoption.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the most significant conceptual and technical breakthroughs that distinguish DLMs from prior work. We separate fundamental innovations—those that enable new capabilities or resolve core limitations—from incremental improvements that refine existing approaches.

### 4.1 Fundamental Innovation: Discrete Diffusion Without Continuous Relaxation

**What changed:** Early DLMs like Diffusion-LM [22] operated in continuous embedding space, requiring a rounding step to map denoised vectors back to discrete tokens. This introduced approximation errors and degraded generation quality. The shift to **true discrete diffusion** (D3PM [24] → LLaDA [28]) eliminated this intermediate representation entirely.

**Why this is fundamental (not incremental):**

- **No information loss:** Continuous models must compress discrete token semantics into embeddings, then reconstruct them—a lossy process. Discrete DLMs operate directly on the vocabulary, preserving token-level precision throughout the diffusion process.
- **Training objective alignment:** The LLaDA loss function (Equation 10 in Section 2.3) computes cross-entropy only over masked positions, directly optimizing the metric that matters for language evaluation. Continuous models optimize reconstruction error in embedding space, which correlates imperfectly with downstream task performance.
- **Scalability proof:** LLaDA-8B [28] achieving performance "comparable to similarly sized LLaMA3-8B models" (Section 1) demonstrated that discrete diffusion could scale competitively—a claim early continuous DLMs could not make.

**Significance:** This resolved the core skepticism about whether diffusion could ever match AR quality on language tasks. Table 1 shows the field's trajectory: early works (2021–2023) were predominantly continuous, while recent large-scale models (LLaDA, Dream, MMaDA) are discrete. This paradigm shift enabled all subsequent advances in reasoning, multimodal integration, and inference optimization.

### 4.2 Fundamental Innovation: Reinforcement Learning Without Factorized Likelihood

**What changed:** AR models compute sequence log-probability as a sum of per-token probabilities due to their factorized structure (Equation 4 in Section 2.1.2). DLMs lack this property—their iterative, non-sequential generation makes log-likelihood **intractable**. This created a barrier to applying mature RL algorithms like GRPO [42] that depend on likelihood estimation.

**Why this is fundamental (not incremental):**

The solutions developed represent conceptual breakthroughs, not just engineering adaptations:

| Method | Core Innovation | Significance |
|--------|-----------------|--------------|
| **diffu-GRPO** [43] | Mean-field decomposition approximating sequence log-probability as product of independent per-token probabilities | First principled GRPO adaptation for DLMs |
| **coupled-GRPO** [89] | Complementary mask pairs ensuring every token is evaluated in partial-masking context | Reduces variance while maintaining training efficiency |
| **VRPO** [95] | Antithetic sampling sharing timesteps/masks between current and reference policy ELBO estimates | Addresses high variance in preference optimization |
| **UniGRPO** [31] | Structured noising strategy sampling masking ratio $p_i \in [0, 1]$ uniformly | Exposes model to all denoising stages, boosting multi-step capability utilization |

**Significance:** These methods enabled DLMs to acquire reasoning capabilities previously exclusive to AR models. DCoLT-reinforced LLaDA achieved "+9.8% on GSM8K and +19.5% on HumanEval" (Section 3.2.1)—gains that would be impossible without RL post-training. This innovation transformed DLMs from curiosity to competitive reasoning systems.

**Distinction from incremental work:** Methods like SEPO [88] that adapt existing policy gradient frameworks with importance sampling are valuable but incremental—they extend known techniques. In contrast, diffu-GRPO and VRPO required **new theoretical formulations** because the underlying likelihood computation does not exist in the same form for DLMs.

### 4.3 Fundamental Innovation: Unified Multimodal Generation Without Modality-Specific Decoders

**What changed:** AR-based multimodal models (e.g., LLaVA [176], Chameleon) typically use separate components: a vision encoder for understanding and an AR decoder for text generation. Image generation often requires entirely different architectures (e.g., Stable Diffusion [8]). DLMs enable **single-architecture unified modeling** where text and images share the same denoising process.

**Why this is fundamental (not incremental):**

- **True bidirectional interaction:** MMaDA-Parallel [115] enables "text and images to interact bidirectionally at every denoising step" (Section 5), unlike AR models where image tokens must precede or follow text tokens sequentially.
- **Joint inpainting capability:** UniDisc [112] demonstrates "joint image and text inpainting in a zero-shot manner, a feature not possible with previous AR or unified generative models" (Section 5). This emerges naturally from the diffusion formulation—any subset of tokens can be masked and refined.
- **No modality-specific heads:** MMaDA [31] "eliminates the need for an explicit vision encoder by tokenizing images into discrete codes using VQ-VAE, and modeling all modalities jointly with a modality-agnostic diffusion transformer" (Section 5). This architectural simplicity reduces engineering complexity and enables more flexible cross-modal reasoning.

**Significance:** Figure 6 shows MMaDA outperforming AR-based multimodal models on MME and MMMU benchmarks despite similar parameter counts. More importantly, unified DLMs enable capabilities like instruction-based image editing and interleaved reasoning-planning that require tight text-image coupling difficult to achieve with separate encoders and decoders.

**Distinction from incremental work:** Models like LLaDA-V [29] that add vision encoders to existing DLM backbones are incremental extensions. In contrast, MMaDA, D-DiT [84], and UniDisc represent **architectural innovations** that rethink how modalities are integrated at the modeling level.

### 4.4 Incremental but Critical: Inference Optimization Bridging the Latency Gap

**What changed:** While parallel generation is inherent to DLMs, naive implementation does not achieve the theoretical speedup due to the "Parallel Decoding Curse" [45]—unmasking too many tokens simultaneously degrades coherence. The innovations here are **engineering breakthroughs** that make DLMs practically deployable.

**Why this is incremental (but essential):**

These techniques adapt known optimization patterns (caching, distillation) to the DLM setting rather than introducing new theoretical frameworks:

| Technique | Adaptation Challenge | Achieved Speedup |
|-----------|---------------------|------------------|
| **KV-Cache variants** [45], [49], [102] | Bidirectional attention prevents standard AR caching; requires delayed or block-wise strategies | 2–45× |
| **Feature Cache** [48], [100] | Prompt tokens static, response tokens evolve sparsely; requires change detection | Up to 34× |
| **Step Distillation** [44], [108] | Discrete tokens resist standard distillation; requires correlation modeling | Up to 500× (single-step) |

**Significance:** Without these optimizations, DLMs would remain academically interesting but practically limited. Mercury [32] and Gemini Diffusion [33] achieving "thousands of tokens per second" (Section 1) depends entirely on these techniques. However, they do not expand DLM capabilities—they enable existing capabilities at production scale.

**Distinction from fundamental innovations:** Step distillation reducing 1000-step denoising to 1–10 steps [44], [108] is an efficiency improvement, not a capability expansion. The model can already generate coherent text; distillation just makes it faster. Contrast this with discrete diffusion formulation, which enabled DLMs to match AR quality in the first place.

### 4.5 Emerging Direction: Hybrid AR-Diffusion as Pragmatic Middle Ground

**What changed:** Rather than choosing between pure AR or pure diffusion, hybrid models like BD3-LM [78], TiDAR [82], and SDLM [83] combine both paradigms—autoregressive across blocks, diffusion within blocks.

**Why this is significant (status: promising but unproven at scale):**

- **KV-Cache compatibility:** Hybrid models "support flexible output lengths and KV-Cache which is widely used in AR models" (Section 2.4), addressing a major deployment concern for pure DLMs.
- **Throughput improvements:** TiDAR achieves "up to 5× throughput improvements while maintaining AR-level performance" (Section 2.4) by unifying diffusion drafting with AR verification in a single forward pass.
- **Dynamic length handling:** SDLM's Next Sequence Prediction paradigm enables "adaptive-length generation" (Section 2.4), partially addressing the fixed-length limitation of pure DLMs.

**Significance:** Hybrid approaches may represent the **pragmatic path to deployment** while pure DLM research continues. They leverage existing AR infrastructure while capturing diffusion's parallelism benefits. However, they do not resolve fundamental DLM challenges like scalability beyond 8B parameters (Section 8.1).

**Distinction from fundamental innovations:** Hybrid models are architectural compromises, not paradigm shifts. They acknowledge that pure diffusion still faces challenges (dynamic length, infrastructure) that AR handles well. This is valuable engineering but does not advance the theoretical understanding of diffusion for language.

---

**Summary of Innovation Classification:**

| Innovation | Type | Impact |
|------------|------|--------|
| Discrete diffusion formulation | Fundamental | Enabled competitive quality vs. AR |
| RL without factorized likelihood | Fundamental | Enabled reasoning capabilities |
| Unified multimodal architectures | Fundamental | Enabled new cross-modal capabilities |
| Inference optimizations | Incremental (critical) | Enabled production deployment |
| Hybrid AR-diffusion | Emerging direction | Pragmatic deployment path |

The first three innovations fundamentally expanded what DLMs can do. The fourth made them practically usable. The fifth represents an active research direction whose ultimate significance remains to be determined as models scale beyond current 8B parameter limits.

### 5.4 Failure Cases and Limitations

**Parallel Decoding Curse**

Figure 7 provides the most concrete failure case analysis in the survey. It shows generation results from LLaDA and MMaDA at different denoising step settings:

- **LLaDA (128 token generation)**:
  - 64 steps (~2 tokens unmasked per step): Correct, coherent response
  - 32 steps (~4 tokens per step): Minor errors appear
  - 8 steps (~16 tokens per step): Incorrect answer (473 instead of 165), incoherent reasoning

- **MMaDA (256 token generation)**:
  - 256 steps (~1 token per step): Correct response
  - 32 steps (~8 tokens per step): Degraded fluency
  - 8 steps (~32 tokens per step): Completely incoherent output with repeated tokens

The caption states:

> "Both models generate a correct and coherent response only when 1 or 2 tokens are unmasked at each step. With fewer steps and more parallelism, the responses are either incorrect or lack fluency and consistency." (Figure 7 caption)

This directly contradicts the theoretical advantage of parallel generation: maximum speedup requires unmasking many tokens simultaneously, but this degrades quality. The survey acknowledges this as the "Parallel Decoding Curse" (Section 8.1), noting:

> "When predicting multiple tokens at once, the model produces a distribution for each position and samples from them independently, failing to account for dependencies among positions." (Section 8.1)

The example given (training data has only "ABABAB" and "BABABA", but parallel sampling produces "AAABBA") illustrates why token interdependence breaks under aggressive parallelism.

**Train-Inference Discrepancy**

Section 3.1 notes a systematic gap:

> "Due to the train-inference discrepancy, as illustrated in [172], the model performs significantly better during training than at inference time."

The survey does not quantify this gap (e.g., "training perplexity 2.5 vs. inference perplexity 4.0"), but acknowledges it as a known issue requiring "two-step diffusion process and improved scheduling technique to mitigate."

**Long Sequence Limitations**

Section 8.1 identifies a critical constraint:

> "Most existing DLMs are limited to a maximum context length of 4,096 tokens, and widely used extrapolation techniques in AR models for longer sequences remain underexplored in the DLM setting."

The computational complexity compounds this:

> "Masked DLMs utilize full bidirectional attention at every denoising step, which incurs a computational cost of $O(N^2)$ per step, where $N$ is the sequence length. Assuming a fixed number of tokens are unmasked at each step, the total number of denoising steps scales linearly with $N$, leading to an overall inference complexity of $O(N^3)$."

This cubic scaling is a fundamental barrier that KV-cache adaptations only partially address. LongLLaDA [74] and UltraLLaDA [165] are cited as addressing this, but the survey does not provide benchmark results for 128K context performance.

**Scalability Gap**

The survey is explicit about DLMs' scale limitations:

> "The largest publicly available DLM currently contains only around 8B parameters, significantly smaller than leading AR models that have been scaled to hundreds of billions or even trillions, such as Llama-3.1-405B [179], DeepSeek-V3-671B-A37B MoE [16]..." (Section 8.1)

This is not a minor gap—AR models have scaled 50–100× beyond current DLMs. The survey notes that "the ability to further scale up DLMs still needs to be validated or explored," acknowledging uncertainty about whether DLMs follow the same scaling laws as AR models.

LaViDa's complementary masking strategy (duplicating samples with disjoint masks) is presented as an ablation-tested improvement, but the survey does not report the quantitative gain from this technique (e.g., "complementary masking improves GSM8K by X%").

The survey states "prioritizing low-confidence positions yields better quality at no extra cost," but does not provide the magnitude of improvement (e.g., confidence-ranked achieves X% better BLEU than random).

Table 2 compares multiple RL post-training methods, but most entries lack quantitative comparisons against each other. The table shows algorithm types and innovations but does not report head-to-head benchmarks (e.g., "diffu-GRPO outperforms SEPO by X% on GSM8K").

Section 5 describes multi-stage training for models like LLaDA-V (three stages) and LaViDa (two stages), but does not ablate the contribution of each stage. For example, it's unclear how much performance comes from vision-language alignment versus reasoning chain fine-tuning.

3. **Multimodal DLMs show promise**: MMaDA outperforming larger AR-based VLMs on MME (Figure 6) provides concrete evidence for this claim.

**Partially Supported Claims**

1. **DLMs excel at mathematical reasoning**: The GSM8K advantage (3–8 percentage points) is real but modest. The survey does not show DLM superiority across all math benchmarks (MATH, GPQA results are mentioned but not quantified in figures).

2. **RL post-training dramatically improves reasoning**: DCoLT's +19.5% HumanEval gain is impressive, but baseline scores are not provided, and other RL methods in Table 2 lack quantitative results.

**Weakly Supported Claims**

1. **Closed-source DLMs rival GPT-4o**: The statement about Gemini Diffusion and Mercury is not backed by benchmark numbers in the survey. This remains an unverified assertion.

2. **500× speedup from step distillation**: DLM-One's claim is extraordinary but lacks quality quantification ("near-teacher quality" is vague). Without knowing the quality degradation, the practical value is unclear.

3. **DLMs are more data-efficient under multi-epoch training**: Section 3.1 cites scaling-law analyses [170], [171] but does not present the actual data comparing DLM vs. AR data efficiency curves.

**Critical Gaps**

1. **No combined optimization results**: The survey does not report experiments combining parallel decoding + caching + distillation, which would reflect real-world deployment scenarios.

2. **Limited long-context evaluation**: Despite identifying long sequences as a challenge (Section 8.1), the survey provides no benchmark results for DLMs beyond 4K context.

3. **Missing compute-normalized comparisons**: DLMs may require different training compute than AR models for equivalent performance. The survey notes DLMs are "more data-hungry under compute constraints" (Section 3.1) but does not present compute-normalized performance curves.

4. **No failure mode taxonomy**: Figure 7 shows one failure case (parallelism degrading quality), but the survey does not systematically categorize when DLMs fail (e.g., specific task types, sequence lengths, or domain shifts).

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Diffusion Language Models (DLMs) offer compelling advantages in inference parallelism and bidirectional context, they are not a universal replacement for autoregressive (AR) models. This section critically examines the structural constraints, deployment barriers, and unresolved challenges that currently limit DLM adoption. We distinguish between **fundamental trade-offs** inherent to the diffusion formulation and **engineering gaps** that may be closed with future infrastructure work.

### 6.1 The Parallelism-Performance Trade-off (The "Parallel Decoding Curse")

The most significant limitation of DLMs is a fundamental tension between generation speed and output coherence. While DLMs *can* generate multiple tokens in parallel, doing so aggressively degrades quality—a phenomenon the paper terms the **Parallel Decoding Curse** [45].

**Why this happens:**
In autoregressive models, token dependencies are enforced strictly by architecture: token $x_t$ is conditioned on $x_{1:t-1}$. In DLMs, parallel decoding assumes conditional independence between positions during a single denoising step. When the model unmasking $k$ tokens simultaneously, it samples each position independently from its marginal distribution:

$$P(x_{i_1}, x_{i_2}, \ldots, x_{i_k} | \text{context}) \approx \prod_{j=1}^{k} P(x_{i_j} | \text{context})$$

This factorization ignores inter-token dependencies within the unmasked block. The paper illustrates this with a concrete example in Section 8.1: if training data contains only "ABABAB" and "BABABA", an AR model will correctly alternate tokens because each prediction sees the previous one. A DLM unmasking positions 1 and 2 in parallel might independently sample "A" for both, producing "AA...", which violates the learned pattern.

**Empirical Evidence:**
Figure 7 provides visual proof of this degradation. The figure compares LLaDA and MMaDA generation under different denoising step settings:
-   **LLaDA (128 tokens)**: With 64 steps (~2 tokens unmasked per step), the model produces a correct mathematical solution. With 8 steps (~16 tokens per step), the output becomes incoherent and calculates the wrong volume (473 vs. 165).
-   **MMaDA (256 tokens)**: At 8 steps (~32 tokens per step), the output collapses into repeated tokens and gibberish.

The caption explicitly states:
> "Both models generate a correct and coherent response only when 1 or 2 tokens are unmasked at each step. With fewer steps and more parallelism, the responses are either incorrect or lack fluency and consistency." (Figure 7)

**Implication:**
This trade-off means DLMs cannot simply "turn on" maximum parallelism to achieve theoretical speedups. Adaptive strategies like Fast-dLLM [45] or APD [46] are required to dynamically adjust the unmasking rate based on confidence, adding complexity to the inference engine. The theoretical $T \times$ speedup (where $T$ is the number of diffusion steps) is capped by the need to maintain sequential-like dependency modeling.

### 6.2 Infrastructure and Deployment Barriers

Beyond algorithmic challenges, DLMs face a significant **ecosystem gap** compared to autoregressive models. This is an engineering limitation rather than a theoretical one, but it critically impacts practical adoption.

**Library Support:**
The paper notes in Section 8.1 that while AR models benefit from "open-source, highly optimized libraries and frameworks (e.g., Hugging Face Transformers [199])", DLMs "still lag behind in this regard." Most major machine learning ecosystems offer "little to no native support for DLMs." This forces researchers to maintain custom codebases, slowing iteration and increasing the barrier to entry.

**Serving Infrastructure:**
For production deployment, AR models leverage mature serving engines like vLLM [200] that optimize KV-cache management, batched decoding, and continuous batching. The paper states:
> "During inference, DLMs lack mature, open-source deployment infrastructure akin to vLLM [200], making efficient serving of DLMs difficult." (Section 8.1)

While recent works like Fast-dLLM [45] and dKV-Cache [49] propose caching adaptations, these are research prototypes rather than production-ready systems. Until DLM-specific serving infrastructure matures, the inference speedups demonstrated in papers (e.g., 27.6× in Fast-dLLM) may not translate to real-world deployment without significant engineering effort.

### 6.3 Sequence Length and Dynamic Generation Constraints

DLMs face inherent difficulties with long contexts and variable-length generation, stemming from their training formulation.

**Fixed-Length Training:**
DLMs are typically "trained to denoise fixed-length sequences under a diffusion-based objective" (Section 8.1). This makes generalizing to longer sequences at inference time challenging. While AR models can extrapolate position embeddings beyond training length, DLMs must handle the compounding error of multiple denoising steps over longer sequences.

**Computational Complexity:**
The bidirectional attention mechanism in masked DLMs incurs a higher computational cost for long sequences compared to AR models with KV-cache. The paper breaks down the complexity in Section 8.1:
-   **Per-step cost**: Full bidirectional attention requires $O(N^2)$ computation, where $N$ is the sequence length.
-   **Total steps**: Assuming a fixed number of tokens are unmasked per step, the number of denoising steps scales linearly with $N$.
-   **Overall complexity**: This leads to an inference complexity of **$O(N^3)$**.

> "Without architectural optimizations such as KV-Cache, this cubic time complexity severely limits the scalability of DLMs for long-sequence generation in real-world applications." (Section 8.1)

**Partial Solutions:**
Recent works attempt to address this. LongLLaDA [74] provides a "training-free NTK-based RoPE extrapolation method" to extend context, and UltraLLaDA [165] enables "128K context windows" through diffusion-aware scaling. However, these are recent exceptions; "most existing DLMs are limited to a maximum context length of 4,096 tokens" (Section 8.1), which is insufficient for tasks requiring document-level understanding or complex multi-turn reasoning.

**Dynamic Length Handling:**
AR models naturally handle variable output lengths via the `[EOS]` token—generation stops when `[EOS]` is sampled. DLMs generally require the generation length to be "predetermined during inference" (Section 8.1). While DLMs can predict `[EOS]`, the entire sequence is still "fully updated throughout the denoising process, regardless of whether the generation has logically ended," leading to unnecessary computation. Training-based [201], [202] and training-free [203], [204] approaches are emerging to address this, but it remains a friction point compared to AR's native variable-length support.

### 6.4 Scalability Ceiling

Perhaps the most critical open question is whether DLMs can scale to the parameter counts achieved by modern AR models.

**Current State:**
The paper explicitly notes in Section 8.1:
> "The largest publicly available DLM currently contains only around 8B parameters, significantly smaller than leading AR models that have been scaled to hundreds of billions or even trillions..."

Table 1 confirms this: representative models like LLaDA-8B [28], Dream-7B [26], and MMaDA [31] cluster around the 7–8B range. In contrast, AR models like Llama-3.1-405B [179] and DeepSeek-V3-671B [16] operate at two orders of magnitude larger scale.

**Scaling Laws:**
While recent scaling-law analyses [170], [171] suggest DLMs have "far greater data reuse potential under multi-epoch training," the paper acknowledges that "the ability to further scale up DLMs still needs to be validated or explored" (Section 8.1). Closed-source models like Mercury [32] and Gemini Diffusion [33] reportedly rival GPT-4o, but without public parameter counts or training details, it is unclear if they achieve this through scale or architectural innovations.

**Implication:**
Until DLMs demonstrate competitive performance at 70B+ parameters, claims about surpassing AR models remain provisional. The current 8B ceiling means DLMs are competitive for edge deployment and mid-tier tasks but cannot yet tackle the most complex reasoning or knowledge-intensive tasks that require massive parameter capacity.

### 6.5 Training Efficiency and Data Utilization

DLMs exhibit distinct training inefficiencies compared to AR models, particularly in how they utilize data during loss computation.

**The 50% Loss Problem:**
In typical masked DLM training, timesteps are sampled uniformly, meaning "only ~50% of tokens (on average) are involved in the loss computation" (Section 3.1). This has two negative effects:
1.  **Reduced Data Utilization**: Half the tokens in a batch do not contribute to gradient updates at each step.
2.  **Gradient Variance**: If critical answer tokens are excluded from the mask (and thus the loss), the model receives no learning signal for those positions.

**Mitigation Strategies:**
LaViDa [110] proposes a "complementary masking strategy" where each sample is duplicated with disjoint masking patterns to ensure all tokens are covered. While effective, this doubles the memory footprint per batch. The paper notes in Section 8.2 that "Current DLMs generally exhibit lower training efficiency compared to AR models," suggesting this remains an open optimization area.

**Data Hunger:**
Scaling analyses indicate DLMs are "substantially more data-hungry under compute constraints" than AR models (Section 3.1). While they can reuse data more effectively across epochs, the initial data requirement to reach convergence may be higher. LLaDA-8B was trained on 2.3T tokens (Table 1), comparable to AR models of similar size, but LLaDA-MoE [75] required 20T tokens to train its sparse experts. This suggests that as DLMs scale, data requirements may grow faster than for AR counterparts.

### 6.6 Open Questions and Future Directions

The survey outlines several underexplored areas that represent both limitations of current systems and opportunities for future work (Section 8.2):

| Challenge | Current Status | Future Direction |
|-----------|---------------|------------------|
| **Quantization** | Largely unexplored for DLMs | Low-bit quantization (4-bit, 1-bit) to reduce memory and accelerate inference, as done extensively for AR models. |
| **Pruning & Distillation** | Step distillation exists [44], [108] | Structured pruning to reduce parameter count without quality loss; knowledge distillation from large AR teachers. |
| **Multimodal Reasoning** | Most models reason in single modality | Unified DLMs capable of complex reasoning across text and image simultaneously (e.g., visual math proofs). |
| **Agent Capabilities** | Underexplored | Leveraging bidirectional context and iterative refinement for dynamic environment interaction and tool use. |
| **Unified Architectures** | Emerging (MMaDA, D-DiT) | Truly modality-agnostic models that do not require separate vision encoders or tokenizers. |

**Critical Assessment:**
The paper positions these directions as "promising" but acknowledges they are "under-explored." For instance, while step distillation [44] achieves 500× speedup, it requires training a separate student model, adding to the training burden. Similarly, multimodal reasoning [31] shows promise but is currently limited to models around 8B parameters.

**Summary of Trade-offs:**

| Dimension | Autoregressive (AR) | Diffusion (DLM) | Trade-off Verdict |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Inference Speed** | Sequential (slow) | Parallel (fast, but coherence-limited) | DLM wins on throughput, AR wins on latency predictability. |
| **Generation Quality** | High (proven at scale) | Competitive at 8B, unproven at 70B+ | AR currently leads on absolute quality ceiling. |
| **Context Length** | Mature (128K+) | Limited (4K typical, 128K emerging) | AR has significant infrastructure advantage. |
| **Training Efficiency** | High (100% token usage) | Moderate (~50% token usage) | AR is more data-efficient per FLOP. |
| **Controllability** | Limited (prefix/prompt) | High (infilling, editing, guidance) | DLM offers unique editing capabilities. |
| **Infrastructure** | Mature (vLLM, Transformers) | Immature (custom codebases) | AR is production-ready; DLM is research-grade. |

In conclusion, DLMs represent a **viable alternative** to AR models for specific use cases—particularly where inference throughput, bidirectional context, or text editing are prioritized. However, they are not yet a **superior replacement** across all dimensions. The "Parallel Decoding Curse," infrastructure immaturity, and scalability ceiling mean that AR models will likely remain dominant for high-stakes, long-context, and massive-scale applications in the near term. The future landscape may be hybrid, leveraging DLMs for drafting and editing while relying on AR models for final verification and complex reasoning.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This survey does more than catalog existing Diffusion Language Models—it reveals a fundamental shift in how we think about language generation. By demonstrating that non-autoregressive diffusion can match autoregressive quality at comparable scales, the paper challenges the long-held assumption that sequential token prediction is necessary for high-quality text. This section examines how this work reshapes the research landscape, what new directions it opens, and when practitioners should consider DLMs over traditional autoregressive approaches.

### 7.1 Landscape-Level Implications: A Paradigm Shift in Language Modeling

The survey positions DLMs as the first **credible alternative** to autoregressive language modeling in over a decade. This has three cascading implications for the field:

**1. Breaking the Sequential Generation Monopoly**

For years, the GPT-style autoregressive paradigm dominated because no alternative could match its quality at scale. The survey documents how this changed:

> "LLaDA-8B [28] further demonstrates the potential of training DLMs from scratch, achieving performance comparable to similarly sized LLaMA3-8B models." (Section 1)

This is not an incremental improvement—it proves diffusion can scale competitively. Figure 6 shows DLMs matching or exceeding AR models on GSM8K (mathematical reasoning) and multimodal benchmarks (MME, MMMU) at the 7–8B parameter range. The implication is profound: **sequential generation is no longer the only path to quality**. This opens architectural design space that was previously closed.

**2. Unified Multimodal Modeling Becomes Architecturally Natural**

Autoregressive multimodal models require careful engineering to handle different modalities—vision encoders for images, text tokenizers for language, separate heads for generation versus understanding. DLMs offer something cleaner:

> "By applying a shared denoising-based modeling framework, DLMs naturally support unified text and vision generation tasks." (Section 1)

Models like MMaDA [31] and UniDisc [112] demonstrate this: they tokenize both images and text into a shared discrete space and apply the same diffusion process to both. The survey notes MMaDA "eliminates the need for an explicit vision encoder by tokenizing images into discrete codes using VQ-VAE, and modeling all modalities jointly with a modality-agnostic diffusion transformer" (Section 5). This architectural simplicity could accelerate progress on truly unified multimodal intelligence.

**3. Inference Speed Becomes a First-Class Optimization Target**

The survey documents inference speedups that were previously unachievable with AR models:

| Optimization | Speedup | Section Reference |
|--------------|---------|-------------------|
| Fast-dLLM parallel decoding | Up to 27.6× | Section 4.1 |
| Elastic-Cache KV optimization | Up to 45× | Section 4.4 |
| DLM-One step distillation | Up to 500× | Section 4.4 |
| Mercury (industry) | Thousands of tokens/second | Section 1 |

These are not theoretical ceilings—they are measured results from published systems. The implication: **throughput-optimized language generation is now feasible** without sacrificing quality. This matters for real-time applications (chatbots, coding assistants) where AR latency creates user experience friction.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions: What This Survey Makes Possible

The survey's taxonomy and analysis create a foundation for specific follow-up research. We identify five high-priority directions:

**Direction 1: Scaling DLMs Beyond 8B Parameters**

The survey is explicit about the current ceiling:

> "The largest publicly available DLM currently contains only around 8B parameters, significantly smaller than leading AR models that have been scaled to hundreds of billions or even trillions..." (Section 8.1)

This creates a clear research agenda:
- **Scaling law characterization**: Do DLMs follow the same power-law scaling as AR models? The survey cites analyses [170], [171] suggesting DLMs are "more data-hungry under compute constraints" but have "far greater data reuse potential under multi-epoch training." Systematic scaling studies are needed.
- **Sparse architectures**: LLaDA-MoE [75] demonstrates the first sparse Mixture-of-Experts DLM, activating only 1.4B of 7B parameters during inference. This could enable scaling without proportional compute increases.
- **Training stability at scale**: Larger models may face optimization challenges unique to diffusion objectives. The survey notes training-inference discrepancy [172] as an unresolved issue that could worsen at scale.

**Direction 2: Closing the Infrastructure Gap**

The survey identifies infrastructure as a critical bottleneck:

> "Currently, major machine learning ecosystems offer little to no native support for DLMs... DLMs lack mature, open-source deployment infrastructure akin to vLLM [200]." (Section 8.1)

This creates opportunities for systems research:
- **DLM-specific serving engines**: Adaptations of vLLM or TGI that handle bidirectional attention and multi-step denoising efficiently.
- **Kernel optimization**: Custom CUDA kernels for diffusion-specific operations (masking, remasking, timestep conditioning).
- **Batched inference**: AR models benefit from continuous batching; DLMs need analogous techniques for multi-request throughput.

**Direction 3: Long-Context DLMs**

The survey documents a critical limitation:

> "Most existing DLMs are limited to a maximum context length of 4,096 tokens... leading to an overall inference complexity of $O(N^3)$." (Section 8.1)

Recent work begins addressing this (LongLLaDA [74], UltraLLaDA [165] reaching 128K), but systematic solutions are needed:
- **Attention approximations**: Linear attention or sparse attention adapted for diffusion's bidirectional requirements.
- **KV-cache adaptations**: The survey documents dKV-Cache [49] and Elastic-Cache [102], but these need integration into production systems.
- **Dynamic-length generation**: AR models handle variable output lengths natively via `[EOS]`; DLMs require the sequence length to be "predetermined during inference" (Section 8.1). Training-free [203], [204] and training-based [201], [202] approaches are emerging but need validation.

**Direction 4: DLM-Based Agents**

The survey identifies agent capabilities as "largely underexplored" (Section 8.2). DLMs offer unique advantages for agent systems:
- **Iterative refinement**: Agents can revise plans across denoising steps, correcting mistakes before committing to actions.
- **Bidirectional context**: Full sequence visibility enables better tool selection and multi-step planning.
- **Infilling capability**: Agents can insert intermediate reasoning steps without regenerating entire sequences.

The survey cites robotics applications (LLaDA-VLA [146], dVLA [147], UD-VLA [148]) as early examples, but general-purpose agent frameworks remain undeveloped.

**Direction 5: Low-Bit and Compressed DLMs**

The survey notes quantization is "largely unexplored in DLMs" (Section 8.2):

> "While extensively studied in AR models, low-bit quantization and binarization remain largely unexplored in DLMs. Adapting these techniques to the diffusion paradigm could yield faster inference and reduced memory consumption." (Section 8.2)

This matters for edge deployment. AR models routinely run at 4-bit or even 1-bit precision; DLMs need analogous compression techniques that account for multi-step denoising dynamics.

### 7.3 Practical Applications: Where DLMs Excel Today

The survey documents diverse applications across domains. We categorize them by **fit**—identifying where DLMs offer clear advantages over AR models:

**High-Fit Applications (DLMs Strongly Preferred)**

| Application | Why DLMs Excel | Paper Evidence |
|-------------|----------------|----------------|
| **Text infilling and editing** | Bidirectional context enables arbitrary-position modification | Diffusion-LM [22] achieves "highly controllable text generation and infilling" (Section 2.2) |
| **Code completion with context** | Iterative refinement allows correcting earlier tokens based on later context | DiffuCoder [89] achieves competitive HumanEval; coupled-GRPO improves code generation (Section 3.2.2) |
| **Real-time chat and drafting** | Parallel decoding reduces latency for interactive applications | Mercury [32] achieves "thousands of tokens per second" (Section 1) |
| **Joint text-image generation** | Unified diffusion framework handles both modalities identically | MMaDA [31] outperforms AR-based VLMs on MME (Figure 6) |
| **Constrained generation** | Guidance techniques enforce structural constraints without altering model | DINGO [101] guarantees regex constraint satisfaction (Section 4.3) |

**Medium-Fit Applications (DLMs Competitive, Trade-offs Exist)**

| Application | Considerations | Paper Evidence |
|-------------|----------------|----------------|
| **Mathematical reasoning** | DLMs show advantage on GSM8K but require RL post-training | LLaDA + DCoLT achieves +9.8% on GSM8K (Section 3.2.1) |
| **Document summarization** | Iterative refinement helps, but long-context limitations apply | DiffuSum [120] and TermDiffuSum [121] show promise (Section 7.1) |
| **Machine translation** | Cross-lingual pretraining works, but AR still dominates production | XDLM [133] pioneers cross-lingual diffusion (Section 7.1) |

**Low-Fit Applications (AR Models Still Preferred)**

| Application | Why AR Wins | Paper Evidence |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| **Very long-context reasoning (100K+ tokens)** | DLMs face $O(N^3)$ complexity; AR has mature 128K+ support | "Most existing DLMs are limited to 4,096 tokens" (Section 8.1) |
| **Ultra-large-scale knowledge tasks** | DLMs capped at ~8B; AR models at 100B+ have more capacity | "Largest publicly available DLM... around 8B parameters" (Section 8.1) |
| **Production deployment today** | AR has vLLM, Transformers, cloud APIs; DLM infrastructure immature | "DLMs lack mature, open-source deployment infrastructure" (Section 8.1) |

**Emerging Scientific Applications**

The survey highlights non-NLP domains where DLMs show unique promise:

- **Protein design**: DPLM [140] and DPLM2 [145] generate protein sequences and structures jointly. The iterative refinement process mirrors how proteins fold—adjusting multiple residues simultaneously rather than sequentially.
- **Molecular optimization**: TransDLM [137] and TGM-DLM [138] guide molecular generation via textual property descriptions, avoiding error propagation in sequential SMILES generation.
- **Robotics control**: VLA models like LLaDA-VLA [146] and dVLA [147] use diffusion for action generation, enabling bidirectional planning where future goals inform current actions.

These applications leverage DLMs' **parallel refinement** capability—domains where multiple components must be coordinated simultaneously rather than generated left-to-right.

### 7.4 Integration Guidance: When to Choose DLMs Over AR Models

Based on the survey's evidence, we provide decision guidance for practitioners:

**Choose DLMs When:**

1. **Inference throughput is the primary constraint**: If your application requires high tokens-per-second (e.g., batch processing, real-time drafting), DLMs with parallel decoding offer 10–50× speedups (Section 4).

2. **You need bidirectional editing**: Applications requiring text infilling, mid-sequence revision, or constrained generation benefit from DLMs' bidirectional attention and guidance capabilities (Section 4.3).

3. **Multimodal unification is required**: If you need a single model for both text and image generation/understanding, DLMs like MMaDA [31] and D-DiT [84] offer cleaner architectures than AR + separate diffusion pipelines.

4. **Iterative refinement adds value**: Code generation, mathematical reasoning, and planning tasks benefit from DLMs' ability to revise earlier outputs across denoising steps (Section 3.2).

5. **Model size is 8B or below**: Current DLMs are competitive at this scale (Figure 6). Beyond 8B, AR models have proven scaling while DLM scaling remains unvalidated.

**Choose AR Models When:**

1. **Context length exceeds 4K tokens**: Until DLM long-context solutions mature, AR models offer reliable 128K+ support with optimized KV-cache.

2. **You need production-ready infrastructure**: vLLM, cloud APIs, and monitoring tools exist for AR models. DLM deployment requires custom engineering.

3. **Maximum quality is the priority**: For high-stakes applications where every percentage point matters, AR models at 70B+ parameters offer a quality ceiling DLMs have not yet reached.

4. **Dynamic output length is critical**: AR models handle variable-length generation natively; DLMs require the sequence length to be predetermined or use emerging workarounds.

5. **Training compute is constrained**: The survey notes DLMs are "more data-hungry under compute constraints" (Section 3.1). If your training budget is limited, AR models may converge faster.

**Hybrid Approach (Emerging Best Practice)**

The survey documents hybrid AR-diffusion models as a pragmatic middle ground:

> "Hybrid AR-Diffusion models aim to strike a balance between the full parallelism of non-autoregressive models and the strong causal dependency modeling of autoregressive models." (Section 2.4)

Models like BD3-LM [78], TiDAR [82], and SDLM [83] use diffusion for drafting within blocks and AR for block-level sequencing. This offers:
- **KV-cache compatibility**: Inherits AR infrastructure advantages
- **Parallel speedup**: 5× throughput improvements reported (TiDAR, Section 2.4)
- **Dynamic length**: AR block sequencing handles variable output naturally

For practitioners uncertain about committing to pure DLMs, hybrid approaches offer a lower-risk path to capturing diffusion benefits.

### 7.5 Reproducibility and Getting Started

The survey provides concrete resources for researchers and engineers:

**Code and Model Availability:**

- **GitHub Repository**: https://github.com/VILA-Lab/Awesome-DLMs (curated list of DLM implementations)
- **Open-source models**: LLaDA-8B [28], Dream-7B [26], DiffuCoder [89], MMaDA [31] all have public weights
- **Training code**: Most academic papers release training scripts; industry models (Mercury, Gemini Diffusion) remain closed-source

**Recommended Starting Points:**

| Goal | Recommended Model | Reason |
|------|-------------------|--------|
| Learn DLM fundamentals | LLaDA-8B [28] | Clean discrete diffusion implementation, well-documented |
| Code generation | DiffuCoder [89] | Specialized for code, coupled-GRPO post-training |
| Multimodal tasks | MMaDA [31] | Unified text-image, strong MME performance |
| Fast inference | Dream-7B [26] | Compatible with Fast-dLLM caching optimizations |
| Research baseline | Plaid [67] or SEDD [68] | Likelihood-based training, good for method comparison |

**Key Hyperparameters to Tune:**

The survey identifies critical inference parameters that affect the speed-quality trade-off:

- **Unmasking rate**: How many tokens to reveal per step. Figure 7 shows 1–2 tokens per step maintains quality; higher rates degrade coherence.
- **Total denoising steps**: More steps improve quality but reduce speed. Step distillation [44], [108] can reduce this to 1–10 steps.
- **Guidance scale $\lambda$**: Controls conditioning strength in classifier-free guidance (Equation 13, Section 4.3). Higher values increase prompt adherence but may reduce diversity.
- **Masking ratio during training**: Typically ~50%, but complementary masking [110] can improve gradient coverage.

**Common Pitfalls to Avoid:**

1. **Assuming AR infrastructure works**: KV-cache, batching, and serving engines need DLM-specific adaptations.
2. **Ignoring train-inference discrepancy**: Models perform better during training than at inference [172]; use improved scheduling techniques.
3. **Over-parallelizing**: Aggressive unmasking triggers the "Parallel Decoding Curse" (Section 8.1); use confidence-aware decoding [45].
4. **Expecting 100B+ scale today**: Current DLMs are validated at 8B; claims about larger scales remain speculative.

### 7.6 The Path Forward: A Balanced Outlook

This survey does not declare DLMs the winner over autoregressive models. Instead, it presents evidence for a **pluralistic future**:

> "We hope this survey serves as a comprehensive reference for researchers interested in diffusion-based language modeling, offering valuable insights about the current state of the field and its future prospects." (Section 9)

The data supports this balanced view:
- **At 7–8B scale**: DLMs are competitive on most benchmarks (Figure 6)
- **At 70B+ scale**: AR models remain unchallenged; DLM scaling is unproven
- **For throughput**: DLMs offer 10–50× speedups with optimizations (Section 4)
- **For infrastructure**: AR models have years of maturity; DLMs are research-grade

The most likely outcome is **coexistence**, not replacement. DLMs will dominate applications where parallelism, editing, or multimodal unification matter. AR models will remain preferred for long-context, ultra-large-scale, and production-critical deployments. Hybrid approaches may capture the best of both.

For researchers entering this field, the survey's taxonomy (Figure 3) and benchmark aggregation (Figure 6) provide a structured entry point. For practitioners, the integration guidance above offers a decision framework grounded in empirical evidence rather than hype. The field is young—most cited papers are from 2024–2025 (Figure 1)—meaning substantial opportunities remain for those willing to tackle the hard problems of scaling, infrastructure, and long-context generation.
