## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the lack of clear design principles in scaling reinforcement learning (RL) for agentic reasoning by systematically investigating data curation, algorithmic techniques, and reasoning modes. The authors introduce high-quality datasets (3k real end-to-end SFT, 30k diverse RL) and optimized recipes (e.g., `GRPO-TCR` with clip higher and token-level loss) to train `DemyAgent-4B`. These practices enable the 4B-parameter model to outperform significantly larger counterparts like `ReTool-32B` and `rStar2-Agent-14B` on challenging benchmarks including AIME2024/2025 and GPQA-Diamond (Table 2), establishing a strong baseline for efficient agentic RL.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Broader Landscape: From Static Reasoning to Agentic Systems

Large language models (LLMs) have evolved through distinct capability stages. After pre-training and supervised fine-tuning (SFT), reinforcement learning (RL) emerged as a critical "scaling axis" that aligns model behavior to incentivize reasoning fidelity through effective chain-of-thought (CoT) trajectories. Building on this foundation, **agentic reasoning** represents a paradigm shift: instead of relying solely on internal generation, LLMs are equipped to integrate external tools throughout the reasoning process. This shift has unlocked progress across mathematics, scientific discovery, and code generation.

However, a critical gap exists between the promise of agentic RL and its practical realization. While recent work demonstrates that RL *can* improve agentic reasoning ability, **the key design principles and optimal practices remain unclear** (Abstract). This uncertainty creates a barrier for researchers and practitioners attempting to build capable agentic systems.

### The Core Problem: Three Unresolved Dimensions

The paper identifies three interconnected dimensions where systematic understanding is lacking (Section 1, Introduction):

1. **Data Curation Gap**: Current agentic training pipelines predominantly rely on **stitch-style data synthesis** (Feng et al., 2025; Schick et al., 2023), where segments of internal reasoning are manually replaced with tool outputs. This patchwork approach overlooks the natural connectivity of reasoning and tool use, preventing data from faithfully mimicking real multi-turn trajectories that indicate *when* and *why* tools should be invoked. The fundamental question: do real end-to-end trajectories provide qualitatively richer learning signals?

2. **Algorithmic Uncertainty**: Despite rapid progress in GRPO-based variants, the optimal RL recipe for agentic reasoning remains undefined. Existing methods differ in optimization granularity (token-, sequence-, or trajectory-level) and impose distinct inductive biases—some encourage exploration through relaxed clipping or entropy management, while others suppress it through strong KL regularization. A principled understanding of *when* and *how* to deploy these algorithms is missing (Section 1, point 2).

3. **Reasoning Mode Ambiguity**: Open puzzles persist regarding the allocation of turn budgets, the trade-off between response length and tool-call efficiency, and the impact of long-CoT predispositions on multi-turn reasoning. These uncertainties often lead to either **overthinking** (long, inefficient loops) or **underthinking** (premature tool reliance) during agent reasoning (Section 1, point 3).

### Why This Problem Matters

The significance of resolving these gaps extends across both practical and theoretical dimensions:

**Practical Impact:**
- **Resource Efficiency**: Without clear design principles, practitioners waste computational resources on suboptimal training configurations. The paper demonstrates that with proper recipes, 4B-sized models can achieve superior agentic reasoning compared to 32B-sized models (Abstract, Table 2)—a potential 8× reduction in model size for equivalent performance.
- **Training Stability**: Direct application of policy optimization methods like GRPO often leads to **reward and entropy collapse**, unstable training dynamics, and inefficient on-policy rollout sampling (Introduction). Understanding how to prevent these failures enables more reliable deployment.
- **Tool Efficiency**: In real-world applications, tool calls incur latency and cost. The paper's finding that "fewer, more deliberate tool interactions often yield better results" (Abstract) has direct implications for production systems.

**Theoretical Significance:**
- **Exploration-Exploitation Dynamics**: Agentic RL fundamentally changes the classical RL trade-off. Unlike conventional RL where gains in `pass@k` come primarily from SFT and RL strengthens exploitation, agentic RL enables external tool interactions that can *jointly* improve both exploration and exploitation (Section 4.2). This challenges existing theoretical frameworks.
- **Entropy Mechanisms**: Prescriptions for entropy in RL diverge—some advocate minimization for deterministic policies, while others exploit high-entropy tokens for exploration. Agentic RL introduces tool-call steps that induce useful uncertainty, raising questions about optimal entropy regimes (Section 4.3).

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

**SFT-Based Tool-Integrated Reasoning (TIR):**
Previous TIR methods train models on datasets containing tool invocation demonstrations, teaching LLMs to follow predefined patterns (Section 7, Related Work). Examples include:
- `Toolformer` (Schick et al., 2023): Self-teaching tool use through synthetic data
- `ToRA` (Gou et al.): Tool-integrated reasoning for mathematical problems
- `ReAct` (Yao et al.): Interleaving reasoning with external tool calls

*Limitation*: These approaches compel models to use tools according to training data distribution, preventing development of **adaptive strategies** for deciding when to invoke tools, how often to call them, or how to balance tool use with internal reasoning (Section 7).

**Agent Reinforcement Learning:**
More recent work leverages RL to train more capable agents that optimize adaptive strategies through outcome-driven rewards (Section 7):
- `Search-R1` (Jin et al., 2025) and `R1-Searcher` (Song et al., 2025): Interleaving reasoning with search engine queries
- `ReTool` (Feng et al., 2025): Teaching models to decide when and how to call tools
- `ToRL` (Li et al., 2025d): Direct optimization of tool use at scale

*Limitation*: Current Agent RL methods are often **tied to specific workflows** and lack systematic understanding of how RL can more generally improve tool-use ability. Key issues such as algorithm design, tool-call efficiency, and reliance on synthetic trajectories remain underexplored (Section 7).

**Entropy-Aware RL:**
Recent work formalizes how entropy governs exploration and identifies collapse as a key bottleneck (Cui et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025b). Some incorporate entropy-aware objectives to balance exploration and exploitation in multi-turn reasoning (Cheng et al., 2025a; Dong et al., 2025b).

*Limitation*: These views largely arise from **conventional RL** settings. In agentic RL, tool-call steps induce unique uncertainty patterns that may require different entropy management strategies (Section 4.3).

### This Paper's Positioning

This work positions itself as a **systematic demystification** rather than a single-technique improvement. The authors explicitly state their goal: "to demystify reinforcement learning in agentic reasoning by systematically analyzing three dimensions: data, algorithm, and reasoning mode" (Section 2, Research Purpose).

**Key Differentiators:**

1. **Comprehensive Scope**: Unlike prior work focusing on isolated improvements, this paper conducts a "comprehensive and systematic investigation" across all three critical dimensions simultaneously (Abstract).

2. **Empirical Grounding**: The insights are derived from controlled experiments comparing real vs. synthetic data (Section 3), multiple RL recipes (Section 4), and reasoning modes (Section 5), rather than theoretical speculation.

3. **Practical Baseline**: Beyond empirical insights, the authors contribute tangible artifacts: a 3k high-quality end-to-end agentic SFT dataset, a 30k diverse RL dataset, and the `DemyAgent-4B` baseline model that validates the training insights (Section 6).

4. **Model-Aware Perspective**: The paper uniquely addresses **competence-difficulty mismatch**, where weaker models fail to improve despite identical algorithms and datasets due to inability to extract meaningful gradients (Section 3.3). This leads to model-aware dataset curation strategies absent from prior work.

5. **Long-CoT Integration Analysis**: The work critically examines whether Long-CoT models can be directly applied to agentic RL, finding they "over-rely on internal reasoning and avoid invoking tools when encountering reasoning tasks" (Section 5.2, Takeaway 5.2). This reveals a previously undocumented limitation in combining reasoning-optimized models with agentic workflows.

By addressing these gaps systematically, the paper establishes "a practical baseline for future agentic RL research" (Abstract), providing both conceptual clarity and actionable recipes for practitioners.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an empirical systems paper that investigates how to effectively train large language models for agentic reasoning using reinforcement learning. The core idea is that three interconnected design choices—data curation strategy, algorithmic configuration, and reasoning mode—determine whether agentic RL training succeeds or fails, and the authors systematically test each dimension to extract actionable recipes.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a reinforcement learning training pipeline that teaches small language models (4B–7B parameters) to autonomously decide when and how to call external tools like code interpreters during complex reasoning tasks. The solution addresses the problem that naive application of existing RL methods causes training instability, entropy collapse, and inefficient tool usage by replacing synthetic training data with real end-to-end trajectories, modifying the RL loss function to encourage exploration, and shaping rewards to penalize excessive response lengths.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The agentic RL system consists of four major components that operate in sequence. First, the **Data Curation Module** produces two datasets: a 3,000-sample supervised fine-tuning (SFT) dataset containing real end-to-end tool-use trajectories, and a 30,000-sample reinforcement learning dataset with diverse problem types (math, science, code). Second, the **SFT Initialization Stage** fine-tunes a base model (Qwen2.5-7B or Qwen3-4B) on the real trajectory dataset to establish coherent tool-calling behavior before RL begins. Third, the **RL Training Engine** implements a modified GRPO algorithm with three configurable techniques: loss aggregation granularity (token-level vs. sequence-level), clipping strategy (conservative vs. higher upper bounds), and reward shaping (outcome-only vs. outcome-plus-length penalty). Fourth, the **Tool Execution Environment** provides sandboxed code interpreter feedback during rollout generation, interleaving tool outputs with the model's reasoning tokens to form complete agentic trajectories. Information flows from curated data through SFT initialization into RL optimization, where each training step samples problems from the RL dataset, generates multiple rollouts with tool interactions, computes advantages based on outcome rewards and length penalties, and updates policy parameters to maximize expected reward while maintaining sufficient exploration entropy.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Type of Paper and Core Mechanism**

This is an empirical investigation paper that does not propose a novel algorithm but instead systematically compares existing techniques across three dimensions to identify which combinations produce stable, efficient agentic RL training. The core mechanism is a modified Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) algorithm that integrates tool-call feedback during rollout generation and applies exploration-friendly modifications to prevent entropy collapse.

**Mathematical Formulation of Agentic RL Objective**

The fundamental training objective maximizes expected reward while penalizing deviation from a reference policy. The paper formalizes this as:

$$\max_{\pi_\theta} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x; \mathcal{T})} \left[ r_\phi(x, y) \right] - \beta D_{\text{KL}} \left( \pi_\theta(y | x; \mathcal{T}) \parallel \pi_{\text{ref}}(y | x; \mathcal{T}) \right)$$

In this equation, $\pi_\theta$ represents the policy language model being trained, $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ is the reference model (typically the SFT-initialized checkpoint), $r_\phi$ is the reward function that evaluates output quality, $\mathcal{T}$ denotes the set of available tools, $x$ is the input problem sampled from dataset $\mathcal{D}$, $y$ is the model output that may interleave reasoning tokens with tool-call feedback, $\beta$ is the KL divergence coefficient set to 0.001, and $D_{\text{KL}}$ measures how much the policy diverges from the reference. The key distinction from conventional RL is that $y$ includes not just final answers but also tool invocation decisions and their outputs, making the action space substantially larger and more structured.

The rollout distribution factorizes into two components that reflect the agentic nature of the task:

$$P_\theta(R, y | x; \mathcal{T}) = \prod_{t=1}^{t_R} P_\theta(R_t | R_{&lt;t}, x; \mathcal{T}) \cdot \prod_{t=1}^{t_y} P_\theta(y_t | y_{&lt;t}, R, x; \mathcal{T})$$

Here $R$ represents the reasoning trajectory of length $t_R$ that is interleaved with tool-call feedback, and $y$ is the final answer with length $t_y$. The first product term captures **Agentic Reasoning** where the model decides whether to invoke tools and processes their outputs, while the second product term captures **Answer Generation** where the model produces the final solution. This factorization is critical because it means the model must learn two distinct skills: when to call tools and how to synthesize tool outputs into correct answers.

**GRPO-Based Algorithm with Three Configurable Techniques**

The paper uses GRPO as the baseline algorithm because it computes advantages by comparing rewards within a batch of rollouts rather than requiring a separate value function. The general GRPO objective is:

$$J_{\text{GRPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E} \left[ x \sim \mathcal{D}, \{R\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\text{ref}} \right] \text{Agg}(G, R) \left\{ \min \left[ r_{i,t}(\theta) \cdot \hat{A}_{i,t}, \text{clip} \left( r_{i,t}(\theta), 1 - \epsilon_{\text{low}}, 1 + \epsilon_{\text{high}} \right) \cdot \hat{A}_{i,t} \right] - \beta D_{\text{KL}}(\pi_\theta \parallel \pi_{\text{ref}}) \right\}$$

In this formulation, $G$ is the number of rollouts sampled per problem (the paper uses 32 rollouts during evaluation), $\text{Agg}(G, R)$ determines loss aggregation granularity, $r_{i,t}(\theta)$ is the importance ratio that depends on aggregation type, $\epsilon_{\text{low}}$ and $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ are clipping bounds that control how much the policy can change per update, and $\hat{A}_{i,t}$ is the normalized advantage computed as:

$$\hat{A}_{i,t} = \frac{r_\phi(x, y_t) - \text{mean}(\{r_\phi(R_1), \ldots, r_\phi(R_G)\})}{\text{std}(\{r_\phi(R_1), \ldots, r_\phi(R_G)\})}$$

The paper investigates three key improvement techniques that modify different parts of this objective.

**Technique 1: Loss Aggregation Granularity**

The first technique determines whether the loss is computed at the token level or sequence level. Token-level loss averages the objective across all tokens in all rollouts:

$$\text{AggTok}(G, R) = \frac{1}{\sum_{i=1}^G |R_i|} \sum_{i=1}^G \sum_{t=1}^{|R_i|}$$

Sequence-level loss treats each complete rollout as a single unit:

$$\text{AggSeq}(G) = \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^G$$

The corresponding importance ratios differ accordingly. For token-level loss, the importance ratio is:

$$r^{\text{Tok}}_{i,t}(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(R_{i,(t)} | R_{i,&lt;t}, \tau)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(R_{i,(t)} | R_{i,&lt;t}, \tau)}$$

For sequence-level loss, the importance ratio is:

$$r^{\text{Seq}}_{i,t}(\theta) = \left( \frac{\pi_\theta(R_i | x, \tau)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(R_i | x, \tau)} \right)^{\frac{1}{|R_i|}}$$

The design choice matters because token-level loss ensures each token contributes equally to the optimization signal, which leverages the model's exploratory capacity more effectively for models with better initial performance. The paper finds that for Qwen3-4B-RA-SFT (which has stronger exploration ability), token-level loss exceeds sequence-level loss by 3.95% on AIME2024 and 3.86% on AIME2025 under the same training budget. However, for weaker models like Qwen2.5-7B-RA-SFT, both aggregation methods achieve comparable performance, suggesting that aggregation granularity matters primarily when the model already has sufficient exploration capacity.

**Technique 2: Clipping Strategy**

The second technique modifies the clipping bounds $\epsilon_{\text{low}}$ and $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ that constrain how much the policy can change during each update. The standard GRPO implementation uses symmetric clipping with $\epsilon = 0.2$, meaning the importance ratio is clipped to $[0.8, 1.2]$. The paper introduces **clip higher**, which asymmetrically increases the upper bound to allow more aggressive policy updates when the advantage is positive. For the GRPO-TCR recipe, $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ is set to 0.28 and $\epsilon_{\text{low}}$ is set to 0.20, creating an asymmetric clip range of $[0.80, 1.28]$. For GRPO-SCR, the values are much more conservative at $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.0004$ and $\epsilon_{\text{low}} = 0.0003$. The design rationale is that higher $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ expands the exploration budget and improves short-horizon progress, but the paper observes a non-monotonic relationship: increasing $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ from 0.28 to 0.315 accelerates performance improvements (achieving equivalent performance 40% faster), but pushing further to 0.35 leads to worse training effectiveness despite faster initial lift because overly aggressive clipping introduces excessive entropy that destabilizes training.

**Technique 3: Reward Shaping**

The third technique modifies the reward function to provide denser learning signals. The baseline reward combines outcome accuracy with a tool-use bonus:

$$r_{\text{out+tool}}(x, y, n) = \begin{cases} 1 + 0.1n & \text{if match}(y_t, y) \\ \min(-1 + 0.1n) & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Here $n$ is the number of tool invocations, and the tool bonus is clipped to avoid degenerate "tool abuse" reward hacking where the model calls tools excessively without improving accuracy. The paper introduces **overlong reward shaping** that adds a length penalty to discourage verbose outputs:

$$r_{\text{length}}(y) = \begin{cases} 0, & |y| \leq L_{\text{max}} - L_{\text{cache}} \\ \frac{(L_{\text{max}} - L_{\text{cache}}) - |y|}{L_{\text{cache}}}, & L_{\text{max}} - L_{\text{cache}} &lt; |y| \leq L_{\text{max}} \\ -1, & L_{\text{max}} &lt; |y| \end{cases}$$

This function gives zero reward when output length is within a safe budget, applies a linear penalty as length approaches the maximum (from $L_{\text{max}} - L_{\text{cache}}$ to $L_{\text{max}}$), and assigns -1 if it exceeds $L_{\text{max}}$. The design preserves a smooth learning signal near the boundary while strongly discouraging overlong completions. The combined reward is $r_\phi = r_{\text{out+tool}} + r_{\text{length}}$ for recipes that incorporate overlong reward shaping.

**Three Experimental Recipes**

The paper constructs three distinct recipes to isolate the effect of each technique combination. **GRPO-T** adheres to the original GRPO implementation with token-level loss, symmetric clipping ($\epsilon = 0.20$), and outcome-only reward ($r_{\text{out+tool}}$). **GRPO-TCR** incorporates Token-level loss, Clip higher ($\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.28$), and overlong Reward shaping. **GRPO-SCR** incorporates Sequence-level loss, Clip higher ($\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.0004$), and overlong Reward shaping. These recipes enable controlled comparison: GRPO-TCR vs. GRPO-T isolates the effect of clip higher and reward shaping, while GRPO-TCR vs. GRPO-SCR isolates the effect of loss aggregation granularity.

**Data Curation Pipeline for SFT Initialization**

The paper argues that data quality at the SFT stage determines the initialization point for all subsequent RL training. The authors curate a 3,000-sample real end-to-end agentic SFT dataset that preserves complete reasoning behaviors. The data sources are: 1,000 samples from s1-1k (Muennighoff et al.), 1,000 samples from a self-curated LeetCode dataset, and 1,000 samples from a ReTool multi-turn SFT set. Trajectories are generated using Qwen3-Coder-30B-A3B as the teacher model with the open-source Qwen-Agent framework and SandboxFusion as the code interpreter. The LeetCode and ReTool subsets are scored using ReasonFlux-PRM to filter for high-quality data, retaining only the top trajectories. This contrasts with synthetic stitch-style trajectories where challenging long-CoT steps are substituted with tool invocations, which misses critical decision cues about when, why, and what to do next after tool calls.

The paper demonstrates the superiority of real trajectories in Table 1: Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 trained on real data achieves 29.79% on average@32, 75.66% on pass@32, and 51.64% on maj@32 on AIME2024, compared to 4.38%, 35.15%, and 0.01% respectively for synthetic trajectories. The real trajectories preserve four critical behaviors that synthetic data cannot replicate: (i) pre-call analysis that localizes which subproblems are efficiently solved via tools, (ii) guarded execution with intermediate checks, (iii) error recovery and strategy revision after failed attempts, and (iv) self-reflection and calibration before invoking tools.

**Data Curation Pipeline for RL Training**

The RL dataset contains 30,000 samples with higher diversity than prior work that focuses purely on mathematical problems. The composition is: 17,000 DAPO-Math samples, 4,902 math and 3,586 code samples from Skywork-or1, and 3,000 science problems from MegaScience. The design rationale is that dataset diversity sustains higher policy entropy during training, which directly incentivizes broader exploration. The paper shows in Figure 2 that training with the diverse dataset leads to significantly higher entropy gain during the early stage and sustains this entropy at a higher level throughout convergence, enabling the agent to achieve over 50% average@32 accuracy on AIME2025 within only 150 steps compared to 220 steps for the DAPO-Math baseline.

The paper further introduces **model-aware dataset curation** to address competence-difficulty mismatch. The procedure uses the SFT model to perform 8 rollouts per problem on the 30k RL dataset, taking the proportion of correct solutions as a proxy for problem difficulty. Problems with 0% or 100% accuracy are discarded because they provide no learning signal. The remainder are labeled with three difficulty levels: easy (accuracy ≥ 0.75), medium (0.75 > accuracy > 0.25), and hard (accuracy ≤ 0.25). The Qwen3 model's empirical difficulty histogram serves as the target distribution, and a model-aware dataset is curated for Qwen2.5 based on this distribution. This addresses the observation that Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct failed to improve despite identical algorithms and datasets because the base policy was too weak relative to the dataset to extract meaningful gradients.

**Complete Training Configuration**

The training setup uses Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 as base models. For SFT, models train for 5 epochs with batch size 32, AdamW optimizer with initial learning rate 5e-5, and maximum response length 32,768 tokens. For RL training, models train for 3 epochs with batch size 64 and learning rate 1e-6. The maximum prompt length is 2,560 tokens and maximum response length is 16,384 tokens. All experiments run on 8×Tesla-A100-80G GPUs using the VeRL framework. The KL loss coefficient $\beta$ is set to 0.001 for the GRPO baseline.

**Evaluation Metrics and Benchmarks**

The paper evaluates on four challenging benchmarks: AIME2024, AIME2025, GPQA-Diamond, and LiveCodeBench-v6. For AIME and GPQA, the paper samples 32 times per problem to compute three metrics: average@32 (overall agent performance), pass@32 (ability boundary), and maj@32 (performance stability via majority voting). For LiveCodeBench, the paper evaluates pass@1 and pass@5 according to official guidelines. By default, temperature is 1.0, top_p is 0.6, and maximum response length is 16,384 tokens.

**Reasoning Mode Analysis Framework**

The paper characterizes two distinct reasoning modes that emerge during training. **Reactive Mode** features short internal thinking with frequent tool calls, while **Deliberative Mode** features deliberate internal thinking with fewer but more targeted tool calls. The analysis visualizes the average number of tool calls and average response length per interaction round during training (Figure 7), then filters for correctly executed tool calls to calculate success rates (Figure 8). The finding is that Deliberative Mode agents achieve over 70% success in tool usage, while Reactive Mode agents exhibit substantially lower success rates because rapid, frequent calls often yield ineffective or erroneous results. This establishes a quality-over-quantity principle: agents that invest more inference tokens in deliberate reasoning before tool calls make fewer but more successful interactions.

**Long-CoT Integration Challenge**

The paper investigates whether Long-CoT models (like Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507) can be directly applied to agentic RL. The observation is that Long-CoT models achieve strong average@32 performance initially but rarely call tools, and as training progresses the average number of tool calls converges to zero. This occurs because Long-CoT models optimized for reasoning tasks over-rely on internal reasoning and avoid invoking tools when encountering reasoning-intensive tasks. To address this, the paper applies SFT initialization with multi-turn tool-use trajectories to guide Long-CoT models to balance deliberate internal reasoning with appropriate tool usage. However, even with SFT initialization, Long-CoT models ultimately achieve only comparable performance to instruction-based models because their ingrained internal reasoning patterns contradict agentic reasoning paradigms, forcing a scaling and pruning process where gains in agentic reasoning are offset by the need to suppress over-thinking behaviors.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the core conceptual advances of the paper, distinguishing between incremental hyperparameter tuning and fundamental shifts in how we understand agentic reinforcement learning (RL). The authors do not merely propose a new algorithm; they challenge existing assumptions about data synthesis, entropy management, and model architecture in the context of tool use. Below, we analyze the four most significant insights, explaining *why* they differ from prior work and quantifying their impact on performance and efficiency.

### 4.1 Fundamental Shift in Data Curation: Real Trajectories vs. Synthetic Stitching

**The Innovation:**
Prior agentic RL workflows predominantly relied on **stitch-style data synthesis**, where existing reasoning traces are artificially modified to insert tool calls (Section 1). This paper establishes that **real end-to-end trajectories** are not just "higher quality" but structurally necessary for learning *when* to invoke tools. This is a fundamental shift from treating data as a source of *syntax* (how to call a tool) to treating it as a source of *strategy* (when and why to call).

**Why It Differs from Prior Work:**
Synthetic stitching assumes that the decision point for tool use is interchangeable with internal reasoning steps. However, the authors demonstrate that real trajectories preserve critical decision cues that synthetic data inherently lacks (Section 3.1):
*   **Pre-call Analysis:** Real data shows the model localizing which subproblems are efficient for tools before calling.
*   **Error Recovery:** Real data includes instances where tools fail, teaching the agent to revise strategy—a behavior absent in "perfect" synthetic traces.
*   **Self-Reflection:** Real data captures the calibration step before invocation.

**Significance and Magnitude:**
The performance gap is not marginal; it represents a collapse of capability when using synthetic data. As shown in **Table 1**, for the Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 model on AIME2024:
*   **Synthetic Trajectories:** Yield only $35.15\%$ on `pass@32`.
*   **Real Trajectories:** Achieve $75.66\%$ on `pass@32`—a gain of **+40.51 percentage points**.

This magnitude suggests that synthetic data fails to provide a viable initialization point for RL, causing the model to learn brittle tool-use patterns that cannot generalize to complex reasoning tasks. The insight here is that **data fidelity dictates the ceiling of agentic capability**; no amount of algorithmic tuning can recover from the missing strategic signals in stitched data.

### 4.2 Entropy as a Driver of Efficiency, Not Noise

**The Innovation:**
Conventional RL wisdom often treats high policy entropy as a source of instability to be minimized via strong KL regularization or conservative clipping, aiming for deterministic policies. This paper inverts that perspective for agentic RL, demonstrating that **sustained high entropy is a prerequisite for effective exploration** in tool-augmented environments.

**Why It Differs from Prior Work:**
In standard reasoning RL (e.g., math-only), exploration is often limited to internal thought paths. In agentic RL, the action space includes external tool interactions, which introduce stochastic feedback. The paper shows that conservative clipping (e.g., standard GRPO) causes **entropy collapse**, where the model prematurely converges to a narrow set of tool-use patterns (Section 4.3). By contrast, **exploration-friendly techniques** (like `clip higher` with $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.28$) maintain sufficient uncertainty to allow the model to discover effective tool strategies.

**Significance and Magnitude:**
The relationship between entropy and performance is non-monotonic but critical. **Figure 5** illustrates that models trained with exploration-friendly recipes (GRPO-TCR) sustain higher entropy throughout training, correlating with superior performance.
*   **Training Speed:** With higher entropy maintenance, the agent achieves $>50\%$ `average@32` accuracy on AIME2025 in **150 steps**, whereas the baseline requires **220 steps** (Section 3.2, Figure 2).
*   **Optimal Range:** Pushing entropy too high destabilizes training. Increasing $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ from $0.28$ to $0.35$ leads to worse effectiveness despite faster initial lift (Section 4.3, Figure 6).

This insight reframes entropy not as a regularization penalty but as a **control signal for exploration breadth**. It suggests that agentic RL requires a distinct hyperparameter regime compared to non-agentic RL, specifically tailored to prevent premature convergence on suboptimal tool policies.

### 4.3 The Quality-Over-Quantity Principle in Reasoning Modes

**The Innovation:**
A common intuition in agent design is that more tool calls equate to greater capability (scaling tool use). This paper empirically disproves this, identifying a **Deliberative Mode** (fewer, targeted calls) that consistently outperforms a **Reactive Mode** (frequent, short-think calls).

**Why It Differs from Prior Work:**
Previous works often optimize for tool usage frequency or assume that interleaving reasoning and tools at every step is optimal. This analysis reveals that **internal computation is a substitute for external calls**, not just a precursor. Effective agents invest tokens in internal reasoning to verify necessity before incurring the cost of a tool call.

**Significance and Magnitude:**
The efficiency gain is quantifiable in terms of tool success rates. **Figure 8** shows that agents operating in Deliberative Mode achieve **>70% tool-use success rates**, whereas Reactive Mode agents exhibit substantially lower success rates due to ineffective or erroneous calls.
*   **Performance Correlation:** The strongest models (e.g., `DemyAgent-4B`) consistently adopt Deliberative Mode, while weaker models fall into Reactive Mode (Section 5.1).
*   **Cost Implication:** For production systems, this insight implies that optimizing for *fewer* calls may yield higher accuracy and lower latency/cost, contradicting the "scale tool calls" heuristic.

This finding establishes a new design principle: **agentic efficiency is measured by the success rate of invocations, not the frequency.** It suggests future algorithms should penalize unnecessary calls more aggressively than currently practiced.

### 4.4 The Incompatibility of Long-CoT Priors with Agentic Workflows

**The Innovation:**
Perhaps the most critical negative result is the finding that **Long-CoT (Chain-of-Thought) models are inherently misaligned with agentic RL** for reasoning tasks. Despite their strong internal reasoning capabilities, these models actively avoid tool use when fine-tuned for agentic tasks.

**Why It Differs from Prior Work:**
It is often assumed that stronger base models (like Long-CoT variants) will transfer better to agentic settings. The paper shows that **internal reasoning priors conflict with external tool reliance**. When faced with a reasoning problem, Long-CoT models prefer to "think longer" internally rather than delegate to tools, causing tool call rates to converge to zero during training (Section 5.2, **Figure 9**).

**Significance and Magnitude:**
This insight prevents wasted computational resources on ineffective model architectures.
*   **Behavioral Collapse:** Even with strong initial performance, Long-CoT models reduce tool calls to near zero as training progresses, effectively reverting to non-agentic reasoning.
*   **Remedy Cost:** Fixing this requires explicit SFT initialization with multi-turn tool trajectories to force a balance, yet even then, Long-CoT models only match (not surpass) instruction-based models (Section 5.3, **Figure 10**).

This distinguishes between **incremental improvements** (tuning clip ratios) and **architectural constraints**. It suggests that for agentic RL, **instruction-based models trained from scratch** on tool use are superior to adapting models optimized for pure internal reasoning. This is a vital guideline for model selection in future agentic system design.

### Summary of Innovations

| Insight | Prior Assumption | Paper's Finding | Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Data Curation** | Synthetic stitching is sufficient for scalability. | **Real end-to-end trajectories** are required for strategic initialization. | **+40.51%** gain in `pass@32` (Table 1). |
| **Entropy** | High entropy causes instability; minimize it. | **Sustained high entropy** drives exploration in tool spaces. | **40% faster** convergence to target accuracy (Section 3.2). |
| **Reasoning Mode** | More tool calls = better capability. | **Deliberative Mode** (fewer calls) yields higher success rates. | **>70%** tool success rate vs. low success for reactive (Figure 8). |
| **Model Architecture** | Stronger reasoning models (Long-CoT) transfer better. | **Long-CoT priors conflict** with tool use; instruction models preferred. | Prevents wasted training on models that avoid tools (Figure 9). |

These insights collectively move the field from heuristic-based agent training to a principled understanding of how data fidelity, exploration dynamics, and reasoning modes interact to produce capable agentic systems.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a rigorous audit of the experimental evidence supporting the paper's claims. While Section 4 distilled the conceptual insights, here we examine the **evaluation protocol**, **quantitative outcomes**, and **robustness checks** to determine whether the proposed recipes reliably improve agentic reasoning. We focus on the completeness of the baseline comparisons, the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters, and the boundaries of the claimed improvements.

### 5.1 Evaluation Protocol and Baseline Selection

The experimental design aims to isolate the impact of data, algorithm, and reasoning mode while controlling for model capacity. The evaluation setup is detailed in **Section 3.3** and **Appendix A.2**, but several critical design choices warrant specific attention here regarding their validity for agentic claims.

*   **Benchmark Difficulty:** The paper selects four high-difficulty benchmarks that stress different reasoning capabilities:
    *   **AIME2024/2025:** Competition-level mathematics requiring multi-step derivation.
    *   **GPQA-Diamond:** Graduate-level science questions (physics, chemistry, biology) testing knowledge retrieval and reasoning.
    *   **LiveCodeBench-v6:** Code generation tasks evaluating tool use (code interpreter) in a practical setting.
    *   *Significance:* Using AIME2025 (released after the model's training cutoff) ensures no data contamination, a common pitfall in reasoning evaluations.

*   **Sampling Robustness:** To account for the stochastic nature of RL-trained policies, the authors do not rely on single-run accuracy. For AIME and GPQA, they sample **32 times per problem** to compute:
    *   `average@32`: Mean accuracy across all samples (measures overall reliability).
    *   `pass@32`: Maximum accuracy achieved in any of the 32 samples (measures capability ceiling).
    *   `maj@32`: Accuracy via majority voting (measures consistency).
    *   *Critical Detail:* This high sample count ($N=32$) is computationally expensive but necessary to distinguish between a model that *can* solve a problem versus one that solves it *reliably*.

*   **Baseline Diversity:** The comparison spans three categories to validate the "small model, big performance" claim (**Table 2**):
    1.  **Instruction Baselines:** `Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct`, `Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507`.
    2.  **Proprietary/Large Open Models:** `DeepSeek-V3`, `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-32B`, `DeepSeek-R1-Zero (671B)`.
    3.  **Agentic Baselines:** `ReTool-32B`, `rStar2-Agent-14B`, `ARPO-7B`.
    *   *Validity Check:* Comparing a 4B agentic model against a 32B agentic model (`ReTool-32B`) isolates the efficiency gain of the training recipe. Comparing against 671B self-contained models (`DeepSeek-R1-Zero`) tests whether tool use can bridge the capacity gap.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results Across Benchmarks

The results are organized by the three investigative dimensions: data impact, algorithmic efficiency, and final performance ceilings.

#### 5.2.1 Data Curation Impact (SFT Initialization)
The first experimental block isolates the effect of **Real End-to-End Trajectories** versus **Synthetic Stitch-Style Trajectories** during the SFT cold-start phase. The results in **Table 1** demonstrate that data quality is not merely incremental but foundational.

*   **AIME2024 Performance (Qwen3-4B):**
    *   **Synthetic:** `average@32` = $4.38\%$, `pass@32` = $35.15\%$.
    *   **Real:** `average@32` = $33.23\%$, `pass@32` = $75.66\%$.
    *   **Gain:** A **+40.51 percentage point** increase in `pass@32`.
*   **AIME2025 Performance (Qwen3-4B):**
    *   **Synthetic:** `average@32` = $3.65\%$, `maj@32` = $0.10\%$.
    *   **Real:** `average@32` = $29.79\%$, `maj@32` = $45.82\%$.
    *   **Gain:** A **+45.72 percentage point** increase in stability (`maj@32`).

*   **Interpretation:** The near-zero `maj@32` for synthetic data indicates that while the model might occasionally guess correctly (high `pass@32` relative to `average`), it lacks consistent reasoning strategies. Real trajectories teach consistent tool-use policies, evidenced by the massive stability gain.

#### 5.2.2 Algorithmic Efficiency (Training Dynamics)
The second block compares the proposed `GRPO-TCR` recipe against the standard `GRPO-T` baseline. The metric here is **training efficiency** (steps to convergence) rather than just final accuracy.

*   **Convergence Speed (Figure 4 & Section 4.1):**
    *   **Target:** $54.7\%$ `average@32` on AIME2024 (the peak performance of `GRPO-T`).
    *   **GRPO-T:** Requires **450 steps** to reach its peak of $54.7\%$.
    *   **GRPO-TCR:** Reaches $54.7\%$ in only **100 steps**.
    *   **Efficiency Gain:** **77.8% reduction** in training steps to achieve the same performance level.
    *   **Final Performance:** `GRPO-TCR` continues to improve beyond this point, reaching $70.93\%$ at step 450, whereas `GRPO-T` plateaus.

*   **Entropy Maintenance (Figure 2 & 5):**
    *   Diverse datasets sustain policy entropy at a higher level throughout convergence.
    *   **Result:** Agents achieve $>50\%$ `average@32` on AIME2025 within **150 steps** using diverse data, compared to **220 steps** for the math-only baseline (DAPO-Math).
    *   **Interpretation:** Higher entropy prevents premature convergence to suboptimal tool-use policies, allowing the model to explore more effective reasoning paths early in training.

#### 5.2.3 Final Performance Ceilings (Benchmark Comparison)
The ultimate validation is whether the optimized 4B model (`DemyAgent-4B`) can outperform larger counterparts. **Table 2** provides the comprehensive comparison.

*   **Mathematics (AIME2025):**
    *   `DemyAgent-4B` (Agentic): **$70.0\%$**.
    *   `ReTool-32B` (Agentic): **$54.3\%$**.
    *   `DeepSeek-R1-Zero` (Self-Contained, 671B): **$53.5\%$**.
    *   `rStar2-Agent-14B` (Agentic): **$69.8\%$**.
    *   *Analysis:* The 4B model surpasses the 32B agentic baseline by **15.7 percentage points** and matches the 14B state-of-the-art despite having **3.5× fewer parameters**. It also exceeds the 671B self-contained model, validating that tool use compensates for parameter scale.

*   **Science (GPQA-Diamond):**
    *   `DemyAgent-4B`: **$58.5\%$**.
    *   `ARPO-7B`: **$53.0\%$**.
    *   `DeepSeek-R1-Zero`: **$59.6\%$**.
    *   *Analysis:* Performance is competitive with much larger models, though the gap to the 671B model is narrower here ($1.1\%$), suggesting science tasks may rely more on internal knowledge than tool computation.

*   **Code (LiveCodeBench-v6):**
    *   `DemyAgent-4B`: **$26.8\%$**.
    *   `Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507` (Base): **$23.0\%$**.
    *   `DeepSeek-V3`: **$16.1\%$**.
    *   *Analysis:* The agentic training provides a **$3.8\%$ absolute gain** over the base instruct model and significantly outperforms the larger `DeepSeek-V3`, indicating strong code interpreter integration.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Sensitivity Analysis

The paper includes several critical ablations that define the boundaries of the proposed methods. These experiments reveal where the recipes fail or require tuning.

#### 5.3.1 Clipping Strategy Sensitivity (Figure 6)
The `clip higher` technique ($\epsilon_{\text{high}} > \epsilon_{\text{low}}$) is central to the algorithm, but the optimal value is non-monotonic.
*   **Setup:** Varying $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ while keeping other settings constant on AIME2025.
*   **Results:**
    *   $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.28$: Baseline performance.
    *   $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.315$: **Optimal.** Achieves equivalent performance **40% faster** (step 60 vs. step 100).
    *   $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.35$: **Degraded.** Leads to worse training effectiveness despite faster initial lift.
*   **Failure Mode:** Excessive clipping ($\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.35$) introduces too much entropy, causing instability. This confirms that entropy must be *balanced*, not simply maximized.
*   **Model Dependency:** Weaker models (Qwen2.5-7B) benefit more from higher $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ to escape bottlenecks, while stronger models (Qwen3-4B) require tighter bounds to prevent over-exploration.

#### 5.3.2 Model-Aware Data Necessity (Figure 3)
The experiment addresses the **competence-difficulty mismatch** where weaker models fail to learn from standard datasets.
*   **Observation:** Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct initially stagnates with average reward $\approx 0$ on the full 30k dataset.
*   **Intervention:** Curating a subset based on model-specific difficulty (discarding 0% and 100% accuracy problems).
*   **Result:** Average reward rises substantially, providing valid gradient signals.
*   **Implication:** A single "universal" RL dataset is insufficient for heterogeneous model sizes. Effective agentic RL requires **dynamic data filtering** based on the policy's current capability.

#### 5.3.3 Long-CoT Integration Failure (Figure 9 & 10)
A critical negative result concerns the integration of Long-CoT models (e.g., `Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507`).
*   **Behavior:** During RL training, tool call rates converge to **zero**. The model reverts to internal reasoning despite reward incentives for tool use.
*   **Performance:** Even with SFT initialization to force tool use, Long-CoT models only match instruction-based models; they do not surpass them.
*   **Reasoning:** The internal reasoning prior conflicts with the agentic paradigm. The model must "unlearn" over-thinking to make room for tool orchestration, fragmenting learning efficiency.
*   **Conclusion:** For agentic RL, **instruction-based models trained from scratch** are superior to adapting reasoning-optimized models.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Claims

Based on the experimental evidence, we assess the validity and scope of the paper's contributions.

#### 5.4.1 Strengths of the Experimental Design
1.  **Contamination Control:** The use of AIME2025 (released post-training) provides strong evidence against data memorization, a common critique in reasoning benchmarks.
2.  **Multi-Metric Evaluation:** Reporting `average@k`, `pass@k`, and `maj@k` distinguishes between capability ceiling and reliability. The massive gap between `pass@32` and `maj@32` in synthetic data (Table 1) convincingly proves the instability of poor initialization.
3.  **Compute-Normalized Comparison:** Showing that `GRPO-TCR` reaches baseline performance in 25% of the steps (Section 4.1) validates the efficiency claim, not just the accuracy claim.

#### 5.4.2 Limitations and Boundary Conditions
The authors explicitly acknowledge limitations in **Section 9**, which temper the generalizability of the results:
1.  **Model Scale:** All experiments are conducted on **small-sized models (4B/7B)**. The paper notes that larger models may exhibit different sensitivities to reward signals and hyperparameters (Vattikonda et al., 2025). The "4B beats 32B" claim holds for *agentic* tasks but may not transfer to domains where internal knowledge dominates (e.g., GPQA gap is smaller).
2.  **Tool Scope:** The experiments focus primarily on **code interpreters**. While the authors suggest insights generalize to multi-tool environments (Section 8.3), there is no empirical validation for search engines or API calls in this work.
3.  **Data Cost:** While the RL dataset is 30k, the SFT dataset requires **real end-to-end trajectories**, which are "computationally costly to collect" (Section 8.1). The reliance on a 30B teacher model (`Qwen3-Coder-30B-A3B`) to generate SFT data (Appendix A.3) shifts the cost rather than eliminating it.

#### 5.4.3 Verdict on Claims
*   **"Real trajectories outperform synthetic":** **Strongly Supported.** The +40% `pass@32` gain in Table 1 is decisive.
*   **"Exploration-friendly RL improves efficiency":** **Supported.** The step-count reduction (450 vs. 100) is clear, though hyperparameter sensitivity (Figure 6) suggests careful tuning is required.
*   **"4B models outperform 32B models":** **Conditionally Supported.** True for agentic math tasks (AIME), but the gap narrows for science (GPQA) and depends on the specific baseline (matches `rStar2-14B` but does not vastly exceed it).
*   **"Long-CoT models are incompatible":** **Supported.** The convergence of tool calls to zero (Figure 9) is a robust negative finding that guides future model selection.

In summary, the experiments provide robust evidence for the proposed recipes within the **small-model, code-interpreter domain**. The methodological rigor (32-sample evaluation, contamination-free benchmarks) strengthens the credibility of the results, though the computational cost of data curation and the limitation to small models remain open challenges for broader scaling.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper establishes strong empirical foundations for agentic RL, several important limitations and trade-offs constrain the generalizability of its findings. Understanding these boundaries is critical for practitioners who wish to apply these recipes beyond the experimental setting. This section examines the assumptions underlying the approach, unaddressed scenarios, computational constraints, and open questions that remain.

### 6.1 Model Scale Assumptions and Uncertain Transferability

**The Core Limitation:**
All experiments in this paper are conducted on **small-sized models (4B and 7B parameters)**. The authors explicitly acknowledge this constraint in Section 9, noting that "larger models may demonstrate different sensitivities to reward signals, require different exploration strategies, or exhibit more robust reasoning patterns that interact differently with RL training dynamics."

**Why This Matters:**
The paper's central claim—that a 4B model can outperform 32B models—rests on the specific behavior of small models under agentic RL. However, recent work cited by the authors (Vattikonda et al., 2025) underscores that RL exhibits **extreme hyperparameter sensitivity**, especially for larger-sized models. This creates uncertainty about whether the proposed recipes will transfer:

| Aspect | Small Models (4B/7B) | Large Models (32B+) | Evidence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Entropy Requirements** | Benefit from higher $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ (0.315) to escape bottlenecks | May require tighter bounds to prevent instability | Figure 6 shows non-monotonic relationship |
| **Data Sensitivity** | Require model-aware dataset curation to avoid stagnation | May extract gradients from broader difficulty distributions | Figure 3 shows Qwen2.5 stagnation |
| **Tool-Use Behavior** | Learn tool invocation from scratch effectively | May have entrenched internal reasoning priors | Section 5.2 shows Long-CoT avoidance |

**Trade-off:**
The efficiency gains demonstrated (4B matching 32B) may not scale linearly. Larger models might:
1. Require different clipping strategies due to more stable internal representations
2. Benefit less from tool use on knowledge-intensive tasks where internal parameters already encode solutions
3. Exhibit different entropy dynamics that invalidate the "sustained high entropy" prescription

**Verdict:**
The paper's recipes are **validated only for the small-model regime**. Practitioners working with 14B+ models should treat the hyperparameters (especially $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$) as starting points requiring re-tuning rather than fixed prescriptions.

### 6.2 Tool Scope Constraints: Single Tool vs. Multi-Tool Environments

**The Core Limitation:**
The experiments focus primarily on **code interpreters** as the sole external tool. Section 8.3 acknowledges this: "In this work, we mainly focus on the code interpreter as a tool for agentic reasoning." While the authors suggest insights may generalize to multi-tool environments, **no empirical validation** is provided for search engines, API calls, or heterogeneous tool combinations.

**Why This Matters:**
Multi-tool environments introduce complexity that single-tool settings do not capture:

1. **Tool Selection Overhead:** In a multi-tool setting, the agent must decide *which* tool to invoke, not just *when*. This adds a discrete action space dimension that the current reward function ($r_{\text{out+tool}}$) does not explicitly optimize.

2. **Tool Combination Strategies:** Correct solutions may require different combinations of tools (Section 8.3). The current training does not teach agents to sequence heterogeneous tools (e.g., search → code → verify).

3. **Feedback Heterogeneity:** Code interpreters provide deterministic, structured outputs. Search engines return noisy, unstructured text. The entropy dynamics observed with code interpreters (Figure 5) may not hold when tool feedback itself is stochastic.

**Trade-off:**
The "Deliberative Mode" finding (fewer, more targeted calls) may not generalize when:
- Different tools have different costs (search is cheap, API calls are expensive)
- Some tools provide information that enables subsequent tool calls (cascading dependencies)
- Tool outputs vary in reliability (search results may be incorrect)

**Verdict:**
The paper establishes a **single-tool baseline**. Multi-tool generalization remains an open research question requiring new reward formulations and potentially different exploration strategies.

### 6.3 Data Curation Costs and Scalability

**The Core Limitation:**
While the paper advocates for **real end-to-end trajectories** over synthetic stitching, it acknowledges in Section 8.1 that such data is "computationally costly to collect." The SFT dataset curation process (Appendix A.3) reveals significant hidden costs:

- **Teacher Model:** Requires Qwen3-Coder-30B-A3B (a 30B parameter model) to generate trajectories
- **Filtering Pipeline:** Uses ReasonFlux-PRM to score and filter trajectories, retaining only top 1,000 from 3,000 LeetCode samples and 1,000 from 2,000 ReTool samples
- **Final Yield:** 3,000 high-quality trajectories from 6,000 initial problems (50% rejection rate)

**Why This Matters:**
The data efficiency claim ("4B beats 32B") shifts rather than eliminates computational cost:

| Cost Component | Synthetic Approach | Real Trajectory Approach |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Data Generation** | Cheap (LLM editing of existing traces) | Expensive (30B teacher model rollouts) |
| **Filtering** | Minimal | Requires PRM scoring infrastructure |
| **Final Dataset Size** | Can scale to 100k+ easily | Limited to 3k high-quality samples |
| **RL Dataset** | 30k diverse samples | 30k diverse samples (same) |

**Trade-off:**
The superior performance of real trajectories (Table 1: +40.51% `pass@32` gain) comes at the cost of:
1. **Upfront Investment:** Running a 30B model to generate SFT data
2. **Limited Scalability:** Cannot easily expand beyond 3k SFT samples without proportional cost increases
3. **Infrastructure Requirements:** Need for sandboxed execution environments (SandboxFusion) and process reward models (ReasonFlux-PRM)

**Verdict:**
The data curation recipe is **effective but expensive**. For resource-constrained practitioners, the synthetic baseline may remain the only viable option despite its performance limitations. The paper does not provide guidance on minimum viable data quality thresholds.

### 6.4 Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Tuning Burden

**The Core Limitation:**
The paper's recommended techniques exhibit **non-monotonic sensitivity** to hyperparameters, particularly the clipping upper bound $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$. Figure 6 demonstrates that performance does not improve monotonically with increased exploration budget:

- $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.28$: Baseline performance
- $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.315$: **Optimal** (40% faster convergence)
- $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.35$: **Degraded** (worse training effectiveness despite faster initial lift)

**Why This Matters:**
The optimal value is **model-dependent**:
- **Weaker models (Qwen2.5-7B):** Benefit from larger $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ to escape performance bottlenecks (Section 4.3, Takeaway 4.3)
- **Stronger models (Qwen3-4B):** Require tighter bounds to prevent over-exploration and instability

This creates a tuning burden:
1. Practitioners cannot simply apply $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.315$ universally
2. The optimal value may shift as the model improves during training
3. No automated method is provided for adaptive clipping adjustment

**Additional Sensitive Parameters:**
- **KL Coefficient ($\beta$):** Set to 0.001 throughout, but Section 4.2 notes that strong KL regularization can cause entropy collapse
- **Reward Shaping Thresholds ($L_{\text{max}}$, $L_{\text{cache}}$):** The overlong reward shaping function (Equation 9) requires setting length boundaries that are task-dependent
- **Difficulty Thresholds:** Model-aware data curation uses accuracy thresholds (0.25, 0.75) that may not transfer to other domains

**Trade-off:**
The "simple practices" claimed in the Abstract require **careful calibration**. The exploration-friendly techniques that drive efficiency gains (Section 4.1) can destabilize training if pushed too far (Section 4.3).

**Verdict:**
The recipes are **sensitive but navigable**. Practitioners should treat the reported hyperparameters as starting points and conduct small-scale ablations (varying $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ across 0.28–0.35) before full training runs.

### 6.5 Long-CoT Integration Challenges and Architectural Constraints

**The Core Limitation:**
Section 5.2 reveals a fundamental incompatibility: **Long-CoT models optimized for internal reasoning actively avoid tool use** when trained for agentic tasks. Figure 9 shows that tool call rates converge to zero during RL training, even when the model initially achieves strong performance.

**Why This Matters:**
This finding has significant implications for model selection:

1. **Cannot Directly Apply Strong Reasoning Models:** Practitioners cannot simply take a state-of-the-art Long-CoT model (e.g., DeepSeek-R1 variants) and apply agentic RL. The internal reasoning prior conflicts with external tool reliance.

2. **SFT Initialization Required:** Section 5.3 shows that Long-CoT models require explicit SFT initialization with multi-turn tool-use trajectories to acquire "effective tool-invocation priors before RL." This adds a training stage and data requirement.

3. **Performance Ceiling:** Even with SFT initialization, Long-CoT models only **match** instruction-based models; they do not surpass them (Figure 10). The paper attributes this to "conflicting objectives" where gains in agentic reasoning are offset by the need to suppress over-thinking behaviors.

**Trade-off:**
| Model Type | Advantage | Disadvantage for Agentic RL |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Instruction-Based** | No conflicting priors; learn tool use from scratch | Weaker initial reasoning capability |
| **Long-CoT** | Strong internal reasoning | Avoid tools; require unlearning; match (not exceed) instruction models |

**Verdict:**
For agentic RL, **instruction-based models trained from scratch** are superior to adapting reasoning-optimized models. This is a critical architectural constraint that limits the applicability of existing Long-CoT checkpoints to agentic workflows.

### 6.6 Domain-Specific Performance Variations

**The Core Limitation:**
The performance gains from agentic RL are **not uniform across domains**. Table 2 reveals significant variation:

| Benchmark | Domain | DemyAgent-4B vs. DeepSeek-R1-Zero (671B) | Gap |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **AIME2025** | Mathematics | 70.0% vs. 53.5% | **+16.5%** (agentic advantage) |
| **GPQA-Diamond** | Science | 58.5% vs. 59.6% | **-1.1%** (nearly equal) |
| **LiveCodeBench-v6** | Code | 26.8% vs. 16.1% | **+10.7%** (agentic advantage) |

**Why This Matters:**
The tool-use advantage is **domain-dependent**:
- **Mathematics:** Code interpreters provide exact computation, yielding large gains
- **Code:** Execution feedback directly validates correctness, yielding large gains
- **Science (GPQA):** Tasks may rely more on internal knowledge retrieval than computation, reducing the marginal benefit of tool use

**Trade-off:**
The "4B beats 32B" claim holds strongly for **computation-heavy tasks** (AIME, LiveCodeBench) but narrows for **knowledge-heavy tasks** (GPQA). Practitioners should expect:
1. Larger gains on problems where tools provide verifiable computation
2. Smaller gains on problems requiring external knowledge or conceptual reasoning
3. Potential need for different tools (search engines) for knowledge-intensive domains

**Verdict:**
The agentic RL recipes are **most effective for computation-verifiable domains**. Science and knowledge-intensive tasks may require different tool integrations and reward formulations to achieve comparable gains.

### 6.7 Summary of Limitations and Open Questions

| Limitation | Severity | Mitigation Strategy | Open Question |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Model Scale** | High | Treat hyperparameters as starting points; conduct ablations | Do recipes transfer to 14B+ models? |
| **Tool Scope** | Medium | Extend reward function for tool selection | How to optimize heterogeneous tool combinations? |
| **Data Cost** | High | Accept synthetic baseline if resources constrained | What is minimum viable SFT data quality? |
| **Hyperparameter Sensitivity** | Medium | Small-scale tuning before full training | Can adaptive clipping reduce tuning burden? |
| **Long-CoT Incompatibility** | High | Use instruction-based models for agentic RL | How to integrate reasoning priors without conflict? |
| **Domain Variation** | Medium | Match tool type to task domain | What tools maximize gains for knowledge tasks? |

**Final Assessment:**
The paper provides **robust, empirically validated recipes** for small-model agentic RL with code interpreters. However, the limitations above define clear boundaries:
- **Strongest Claims:** Real trajectories outperform synthetic; exploration-friendly RL improves efficiency; deliberative mode outperforms reactive mode (all validated within experimental scope)
- **Weakest Claims:** Generalizability to larger models, multi-tool environments, and knowledge-intensive domains (acknowledged as future work)

Practitioners should adopt the core insights (data fidelity, entropy maintenance, deliberative reasoning) while treating specific hyperparameters and architectural choices as **domain-dependent variables requiring validation** rather than universal constants.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work does more than optimize a specific training pipeline; it fundamentally reshapes how we approach the development of agentic systems. By demonstrating that **data fidelity**, **exploration dynamics**, and **reasoning modes** outweigh raw parameter count, the paper shifts the field's focus from "scaling laws" to "recipe engineering." Below, we analyze how these findings alter the research landscape, identify concrete avenues for follow-up work, outline practical deployment scenarios, and provide integration guidance for practitioners.

### 7.1 Shifting the Paradigm: From Scale to Strategy

The most profound implication of this work is the decoupling of **model size** from **agentic capability**. Traditionally, performance gains in reasoning tasks were pursued primarily through increasing parameter count (e.g., moving from 7B to 70B models). This paper challenges that assumption by showing that a **4B-parameter model** (`DemyAgent-4B`) can outperform a **32B-parameter model** (`ReTool-32B`) on agentic benchmarks (Table 2).

**Key Paradigm Shifts:**

1.  **Data Quality > Data Quantity:**
    The finding that **real end-to-end trajectories** yield a **+40.51 percentage point** gain in `pass@32` over synthetic stitching (Table 1, Section 3.1) implies that scaling synthetic data pipelines is a diminishing return. Future efforts should prioritize **curating high-fidelity interaction logs** over generating massive volumes of stitched examples. This shifts the bottleneck from computational generation cost to **data collection infrastructure** (e.g., sandboxed execution environments).

2.  **Entropy as a Resource, Not Noise:**
    Conventional RL wisdom often treats high policy entropy as instability to be minimized via strong KL regularization. This work inverts that view for agentic settings, showing that **sustained high entropy** is necessary to prevent premature convergence on suboptimal tool policies (Section 4.3). This suggests that **exploration-friendly algorithms** (like `GRPO-TCR` with $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.315$) should become the default starting point for agentic RL, replacing conservative clipping strategies.

3.  **Deliberative Reasoning > Reactive Tool Use:**
    The identification of the **Deliberative Mode** (fewer, targeted tool calls) as superior to the **Reactive Mode** (frequent, short-think calls) (Section 5.1) changes how we evaluate agent efficiency. Instead of optimizing for tool invocation frequency, systems should optimize for **tool success rates** (which exceed 70% in Deliberative Mode, Figure 8). This implies that **internal computation is a valid substitute for external calls**, and agents should be rewarded for *not* calling tools when internal reasoning suffices.

4.  **Architecture Matters: Instruction vs. Long-CoT:**
    The discovery that **Long-CoT models actively avoid tool use** (Section 5.2, Figure 9) warns against blindly applying strong reasoning checkpoints to agentic tasks. This implies a divergence in model specialization: **Instruction-based models** are better suited for learning tool orchestration from scratch, while **Long-CoT models** are better reserved for purely internal reasoning tasks where tools are unavailable.

### 7.2 Promising Research Avenues

The paper explicitly identifies several open puzzles in Section 8 (Discussion and Future Work) that define the next frontier of agentic RL research.

**1. Scalable Data Curation (Addressing Section 8.1)**
*   **Problem:** Real end-to-end trajectories are "computationally costly to collect" (Section 8.1), requiring large teacher models (e.g., `Qwen3-Coder-30B-A3B`) and sandboxed execution.
*   **Follow-up Direction:** Develop **distillation recipes** that transfer the strategic behaviors of real trajectories into smaller datasets without requiring massive teacher rollouts. Research could explore **self-improvement loops** where the agent generates its own high-quality trajectories after an initial SFT phase, reducing reliance on external teachers.
*   **Key Question:** What is the **minimum viable data quality threshold**? Can we identify specific trajectory features (e.g., presence of error recovery steps) that predict RL success, allowing for smarter filtering rather than brute-force generation?

**2. Adaptive Exploration Mechanisms (Addressing Section 4.3)**
*   **Problem:** The optimal clipping upper bound $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ is non-monotonic and model-dependent. For Qwen3-4B, $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.315$ is optimal, but $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.35$ causes instability (Figure 6).
*   **Follow-up Direction:** Design **adaptive clipping strategies** that adjust $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ dynamically based on real-time entropy measurements or reward variance. Instead of a fixed hyperparameter, the algorithm could monitor the **pass@k vs. average@k gap** (Section 4.2) and expand the exploration budget only when the gap widens.
*   **Key Question:** Can we automate the tuning of $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ to eliminate the need for manual ablation sweeps across different model sizes?

**3. Multi-Tool Orchestration (Addressing Section 8.3)**
*   **Problem:** Current experiments focus on a **single tool** (code interpreter). Real-world agents often need to coordinate search engines, calculators, and APIs simultaneously.
*   **Follow-up Direction:** Extend the **reward shaping** function (Equation 8, 9) to account for **tool selection costs** and **heterogeneous feedback**. For example, search results are noisy while code execution is deterministic; the RL objective should reflect this difference in reliability.
*   **Key Question:** Does the **Deliberative Mode** principle hold when tool calls are interdependent (e.g., search result A is needed to formulate code query B)?

**4. Resolving the Long-CoT Conflict (Addressing Section 5.3)**
*   **Problem:** Long-CoT models suffer from "conflicting objectives" where gains in agentic reasoning are offset by the need to suppress over-thinking (Section 5.3).
*   **Follow-up Direction:** Investigate **modular architectures** where internal reasoning and tool orchestration are handled by separate heads or adapters, preventing the gradients from interfering. Alternatively, explore **curriculum learning** that gradually introduces tool use to Long-CoT models without triggering the avoidance behavior observed in Figure 9.
*   **Key Question:** Is it possible to fine-tune a Long-CoT model to *choose* between internal reasoning and tool use optimally, rather than forcing it to always use tools?

### 7.3 Practical Deployment Scenarios

The insights from this paper translate directly into specific use cases where agentic RL provides maximum value.

**1. Resource-Constrained Edge Deployment**
*   **Context:** Deploying agents on local devices (laptops, mobile) where memory and compute are limited.
*   **Application:** Use the `DemyAgent-4B` recipe to train a **4B-parameter model** that matches 32B performance on math and code tasks (Table 2).
*   **Benefit:** Reduces inference latency and memory footprint by **8×** compared to larger models, enabling real-time agentic interaction without cloud dependency.

**2. Cost-Sensitive API Automation**
*   **Context:** Production systems where every tool call (e.g., code execution, API query) incurs monetary cost or latency.
*   **Application:** Implement the **Deliberative Mode** training objective to minimize unnecessary tool calls.
*   **Benefit:** By achieving **>70% tool success rates** (Figure 8) with fewer calls, systems reduce wasted compute cycles and API costs. The **overlong reward shaping** (Equation 9) further ensures responses remain concise, reducing token generation costs.

**3. High-Stakes Reasoning (Math/Science/Code)**
*   **Context:** Domains requiring verifiable correctness, such as automated grading, code generation, or scientific calculation.
*   **Application:** Leverage the **real end-to-end SFT initialization** (Section 3.1) to ensure the agent understands error recovery and verification steps.
*   **Benefit:** The **+40.51% gain in pass@32** (Table 1) translates to higher reliability in production, reducing the need for human-in-the-loop verification.

**4. Domain-Specific Agent Specialization**
*   **Context:** Tasks where general knowledge is insufficient (e.g., specialized engineering calculations).
*   **Application:** Use the **model-aware dataset curation** (Section 3.3) to tailor the RL dataset to the specific difficulty distribution of the target domain.
*   **Benefit:** Prevents the **competence-difficulty mismatch** where models stagnate because tasks are too hard or too easy, ensuring consistent gradient signals during training (Figure 3).

### 7.4 Integration Guidance

For practitioners looking to adopt these methods, the following guidelines summarize when and how to apply the paper's recipes.

**When to Use This Approach:**
*   **Tool Use is Required:** If your task benefits from external computation (code, search, calculators), this agentic RL pipeline is superior to standard SFT or self-contained reasoning.
*   **Small Model Constraints:** If you are limited to **4B–7B parameter models** due to hardware constraints, this recipe allows you to punch above your weight class (matching 32B performance on AIME, Table 2).
*   **Verifiable Rewards Available:** The method relies on outcome-based rewards (accuracy, code pass/fail). It is best suited for domains where correctness can be automatically verified.

**When to Avoid or Modify:**
*   **Knowledge-Heavy Tasks:** For tasks relying on internal knowledge (e.g., GPQA-Diamond), the agentic advantage narrows (Table 2 shows only a **1.1% gap** vs. 671B models). In these cases, larger self-contained models may be more efficient.
*   **Long-CoT Base Models:** Do **not** start from a Long-CoT checkpoint (e.g., `Qwen3-Thinking`) without explicit SFT initialization. As shown in Section 5.2, these models will converge to **zero tool calls**. Prefer **Instruction-based models** (e.g., `Qwen3-4B-Instruct`) for agentic RL.
*   **Multi-Tool Environments:** The current reward function ($r_{\text{out+tool}}$) does not explicitly optimize for tool selection. If using multiple tools, you must extend the reward signal to penalize incorrect tool choices, not just incorrect answers.

**Recommended Starting Configuration:**
Based on the ablation studies in Section 4, practitioners should begin with the following hyperparameters before tuning:
*   **Algorithm:** `GRPO-TCR` (Token-level loss, Clip higher, Reward shaping).
*   **Clipping:** Set $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.315$ and $\epsilon_{\text{low}} = 0.20$ (Section 4.3). *Note: Monitor for instability; if training diverges, reduce $\epsilon_{\text{high}}$ to 0.28.*
*   **Data:** Prioritize **real end-to-end trajectories** for SFT. If unavailable, use synthetic data but expect a **~40% performance ceiling reduction** (Table 1).
*   **Entropy:** Monitor policy entropy during training. If it collapses early (Figure 5), increase dataset diversity or relax clipping bounds.

**Final Takeaway:**
This work establishes that **agentic reasoning is a learnable skill distinct from internal reasoning**. By treating tool use as a strategic decision rather than a syntactic pattern, and by maintaining sufficient exploration entropy during training, we can build compact, efficient agents that outperform much larger counterparts. The path forward lies not in scaling parameters, but in **scaling data fidelity and algorithmic precision**.
