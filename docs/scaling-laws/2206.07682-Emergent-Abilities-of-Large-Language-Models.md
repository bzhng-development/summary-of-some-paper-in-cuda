## 1. Executive Summary

This paper defines and surveys "emergent abilities" in large language models—capabilities that are absent in smaller models but appear unpredictably at larger scales, characterized by performance jumping from random to above-random on benchmarks like BIG-Bench at thresholds such as $\sim 10^{23}$ training FLOPs. By analyzing scaling curves across model families including GPT-3, LaMDA, Gopher, and PaLM, the authors demonstrate that abilities like multi-step reasoning and multi-task language understanding cannot be extrapolated from smaller-scale performance trends. This finding matters because it implies that further scaling may unlock unforeseen capabilities and risks, challenging the predictability of standard scaling laws and signaling a shift toward general-purpose models in NLP.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Predictability Gap in Language Model Scaling

To understand what problem this paper addresses, we first need to establish what the research community *expected* from scaling language models before this work. Prior to 2022, the dominant framework for understanding language model improvement came from **scaling laws**—empirical relationships showing that performance metrics improve smoothly and predictably as models grow larger.

The foundational work by Kaplan et al. (2020) demonstrated that pre-training **cross-entropy loss** decreases as a power law function of three factors:
- Training compute (measured in FLOPs)
- Number of model parameters
- Training dataset size

These scaling curves span **seven orders of magnitude** and are highly predictable. If you know a model's scale, you can reliably forecast its loss. This predictability led many researchers to assume that *downstream task performance* would follow similar smooth trajectories.

However, this paper identifies a critical gap: **certain capabilities do not follow smooth scaling curves**. Instead, they exhibit what the authors call **emergent abilities**—capabilities that are essentially absent in smaller models but appear suddenly at larger scales. The paper states this formally in Section 2:

> An ability is emergent if it is not present in smaller models but is present in larger models. Emergent abilities would not have been directly predicted by extrapolating a scaling law (i.e. consistent performance improvements) from small-scale models.

This creates a fundamental unpredictability problem. If you only have access to models up to 10B parameters, you cannot extrapolate to know whether a 100B parameter model will suddenly be able to solve multi-digit arithmetic or follow natural language instructions. The performance curve shows a **phase transition**—near-random performance until a critical threshold, then a sharp jump to substantially above-random (Section 2).

### Why This Matters: Theoretical and Practical Stakes

The importance of this phenomenon extends across multiple dimensions:

**Theoretical Significance:** Emergent abilities challenge our mechanistic understanding of how neural networks learn. If performance improvements are smooth at the loss level but discontinuous at the task level, this suggests that:
- The relationship between internal representations and downstream capabilities is non-linear
- Certain cognitive-like abilities may require minimum capacity thresholds to manifest
- Standard scaling laws may be insufficient for forecasting real-world capabilities

The paper notes in Section 5.1 that "there are currently few compelling explanations for why such abilities emerge in the way they do." This represents a significant knowledge gap in our understanding of deep learning.

**Practical Deployment Concerns:** If capabilities emerge unpredictably, then **risks may also emerge unpredictably**. Section 5.4 explicitly connects emergent abilities to **emergent risks**:

> Importantly, similar to how emergent abilities have been observed in the few-shot prompting setting without explicitly being included in pre-training, risks could also emerge.

Examples include:
- **Truthfulness**: Larger GPT-3 models were more likely to mimic human falsehoods on TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2021)
- **Toxicity**: Askell et al. (2021) found larger models could produce more toxic responses
- **Memorization**: Carlini et al. (2021; 2022) showed larger models are more likely to memorize training data

If we cannot predict when capabilities emerge, we cannot predict when dangerous capabilities might emerge either. This has direct implications for AI safety and governance.

**Sociological Shift in NLP:** Section 5.5 describes how emergence has catalyzed a shift from **task-specific models** to **general-purpose models**. Historically, NLP focused on training separate models for separate tasks (Jurafsky & Martin, 2009). Emergent few-shot capabilities mean a single large model can now perform tasks it was never explicitly trained on. The paper cites examples where:
- GPT-3 175B achieved state-of-the-art on TriviaQA and PiQA via few-shot prompting
- PaLM 540B achieved state-of-the-art on arithmetic reasoning benchmarks
- Flamingo 80B achieved state-of-the-art on visual question answering

This represents a paradigm shift in how the community builds and deploys language models.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

Before this paper, several lines of work touched on related phenomena, but none provided a unified framework:

**Individual Capability Discoveries:** Various papers documented specific emergent-like behaviors:
- Brown et al. (2020) showed GPT-3's few-shot learning capabilities
- Wei et al. (2022b) demonstrated chain-of-thought prompting enables multi-step reasoning
- Rae et al. (2021) documented Gopher's scaling behavior across tasks

However, these were isolated findings. No work systematically categorized *which* abilities emerge, *at what scale*, and across *which model families*.

**Scaling Law Literature:** Kaplan et al. (2020) and Hoffmann et al. (2022) established smooth scaling for pre-training loss. But as this paper notes in Section 5.1, smooth loss scaling does not imply smooth downstream task performance:

> Cross-entropy loss improves even for small model scales where the downstream metrics (exact match, BLEU, and accuracy) are close to random and do not improve.

This means loss-based scaling laws cannot predict emergent task capabilities.

**Early Recognition of Unpredictability:** Ganguli et al. (2022) noted that "performance for certain downstream tasks counterintuitively does not appear to continuously improve as a function of scale, and such tasks cannot be predicted ahead of time." However, this observation lacked systematic documentation across tasks and models.

### How This Paper Positions Itself

This work positions itself as a **systematic survey and definition** rather than a methods paper. Key positioning choices:

**Definition-First Approach:** Section 2 provides an operational definition of emergence tied to scaling curves: performance must be near-random until a threshold, then jump substantially above-random. This distinguishes emergence from smooth improvements or capabilities that never emerge (flat curves).

**Cross-Model Synthesis:** Rather than focusing on one model family, the paper synthesizes evidence across five families: GPT-3, LaMDA, Gopher, Chinchilla, and PaLM. Table 1 catalogs emergent abilities with their emergence thresholds in both training FLOPs and parameters.

**Two Categories of Emergence:** The paper structures its survey around:
1. **Few-shot prompted tasks** (Section 3): Capabilities that emerge when models are given input-output examples in context
2. **Augmented prompting strategies** (Section 4): Techniques like chain-of-thought or instruction tuning that only become effective at certain scales

**Forward-Looking Questions:** Section 5 explicitly frames emergence as motivating future research directions:
- Why do abilities emerge at specific thresholds?
- Will more scaling unlock further emergent abilities?
- Can we lower the scale threshold through better architectures or data?

The paper acknowledges its limitations in Section 5.1: "this analysis does not explain why downstream metrics are emergent or enable us to predict the scale at which emergence occurs." Rather than claiming to solve emergence, the paper aims to **document the phenomenon clearly** so the community can investigate it systematically.

This positioning is crucial: the paper does not propose new methods but instead **creates a shared vocabulary and evidence base** for discussing emergence. As the authors state in the Broader Impact Statement: "we surveyed results in the existing literature, without proposing new methods or models." The contribution is synthesis and framing, enabling future work on prediction and explanation.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper is a systematic survey and definitional work that establishes an operational framework for identifying emergent abilities through scaling curve analysis across multiple language model families. The core idea is that emergence is detected when task performance remains near-random across several orders of magnitude of scale, then abruptly transitions to substantially above-random at a critical threshold—a pattern that cannot be predicted by extrapolating from smaller models.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The "system" being analyzed is not a new model architecture but rather a **measurement methodology** that plots language model task performance against training compute (FLOPs) or parameter count to identify discontinuous capability jumps. This approach solves the problem of distinguishing true emergence from smooth scaling by requiring evidence of a phase transition—near-random performance followed by a sharp increase—across multiple independent model families to rule out architecture-specific artifacts.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The analytical pipeline consists of four interconnected components that process evidence from prior work into a unified emergence framework. **Component 1 (Definition Layer)** establishes the operational criteria: an ability is emergent if performance is near-random until a critical scale threshold, then jumps substantially above-random, visualized as a phase transition in scaling curves (Section 2). **Component 2 (Data Aggregation Layer)** collects performance data from five language model families—GPT-3, LaMDA, Gopher, Chinchilla, and PaLM—spanning from $2.1 \times 10^6$ parameters (smallest LaMDA) to $540 \times 10^9$ parameters (largest PaLM), with training FLOPs ranging from $3.30 \times 10^{18}$ to $2.53 \times 10^{24}$ (Table 2, Appendix C). **Component 3 (Categorization Layer)** organizes emergent abilities into two distinct settings: few-shot prompted tasks where models receive input-output examples in context (Section 3), and augmented prompting strategies where techniques like chain-of-thought or instruction tuning only become effective at certain scales (Section 4). **Component 4 (Validation Layer)** cross-checks emergence claims using alternative metrics (cross-entropy loss instead of accuracy) and alternative x-axes (WikiText103 perplexity instead of FLOPs) to ensure the phenomenon is not an artifact of measurement choices (Section 5.1, Section 5.3, Appendix A). Information flows from Component 1's definition through Component 2's data collection, into Component 3's categorization, with Component 4 providing robustness checks that feed back to validate or refine the emergence claims.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Paper Type and Core Measurement Framework**

This is a survey and definitional paper that does not propose new models but instead creates a standardized methodology for detecting emergent abilities through scaling curve analysis. The fundamental measurement unit is a **scaling curve** where the x-axis represents model scale (either training FLOPs or parameter count) and the y-axis represents task performance (accuracy, BLEU, exact match, or similar metrics). The paper explicitly chooses training FLOPs as the primary x-axis because "language models trained with more compute tend to also have more parameters" and "most dense Transformer language model families have scaled training compute roughly proportionally with model parameters" (Section 2). However, the authors acknowledge this is not perfect: "there is not a single proxy that adequately captures all aspects of scale" because models like Chinchilla use different compute-to-parameter ratios than Gopher (Section 2).

**Operational Definition of Emergence**

The paper establishes a precise, testable definition rather than using emergence informally. An ability qualifies as emergent only when it satisfies this criterion: "performance is near-random until a certain critical threshold of scale is reached, after which performance increases to substantially above random" (Section 2). This definition explicitly excludes two other patterns: **smooth scaling** where performance improves gradually and predictably from small to large models, and **flat scaling** where no model achieves above-random performance regardless of scale. The definition is visualized as a **phase transition**—a term borrowed from physics describing "a dramatic change in overall behavior that would not have been foreseen by examining smaller-scale systems" (Section 2, citing Huberman & Hogg, 1987). This phase transition characterization is crucial because it means emergence cannot be predicted by extrapolating a trend line from smaller models; the curve must show a qualitative change in shape, not just a quantitative improvement.

**Model Families and Scale Coverage**

The paper's credibility depends on analyzing emergence across multiple independent model families to rule out architecture-specific artifacts. Table 2 in Appendix C provides complete specifications for all models analyzed. The **GPT-3 family** spans 8 model sizes from 125M to 175B parameters, all trained on 300B tokens, with training FLOPs ranging from $2.25 \times 10^{20}$ to $3.14 \times 10^{23}$. The **LaMDA family** spans 12 model sizes from 2.1M to 137B parameters, trained on 132B to 674B tokens, with training FLOPs from $3.30 \times 10^{18}$ to $5.54 \times 10^{23}$. The **Gopher family** includes 4 model sizes (417M to 280B parameters) trained on 300B to 325B tokens, with FLOPs from $7.51 \times 10^{20}$ to $5.46 \times 10^{23}$. The **Chinchilla family** includes 4 model sizes (417M to 70B parameters) but crucially uses 1.34T tokens for the 70B model—significantly more data than Gopher's 280B model despite similar compute ($5.63 \times 10^{23}$ FLOPs)—following the compute-optimal training findings of Hoffmann et al. (2022). The **PaLM family** spans 3 model sizes (8B to 540B parameters) trained on 780B tokens, with FLOPs from $3.74 \times 10^{22}$ to $2.53 \times 10^{24}$, making the largest PaLM roughly 8x the computational budget of GPT-3 175B (Appendix C). This breadth matters because if emergence appears at similar scales across different architectures and training procedures, it suggests the phenomenon is fundamental to scale rather than specific to one model family.

**Evaluation Metrics and Their Limitations**

The paper uses multiple evaluation metrics depending on task type, and explicitly investigates whether metric choice creates artificial emergence. For **generative tasks** (producing text outputs), the primary metrics are exact match (whether output exactly equals target), BLEU (n-gram overlap with reference), and sometimes ROUGE or BLEURT (Section 3, Appendix A.2). For **classification tasks** (selecting from options), the metric is accuracy (percentage of correct predictions). The paper acknowledges a critical limitation: "using exact string match as the evaluation metric for long-sequence targets may disguise compounding incremental improvements as emergence" because partial credit is not awarded (Section 5.1). To test whether this creates false emergence, Appendix A.2 evaluates three generative BIG-Bench tasks using seven different metrics including ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, ROUGE-L-Sum, BLEURT, BLEU, Sequence F1, and Multiple Choice Grade. The finding is that "for all three tasks, the emergent behavior appears to be independent of which evaluation metric is used" (Appendix A.2), suggesting emergence is not merely a metric artifact. Additionally, Appendix A.1 analyzes **cross-entropy loss** (the pre-training objective) for six emergent BIG-Bench tasks and finds that loss improves smoothly even when downstream metrics remain at random—showing that "improvements in the log-likelihood of the target sequence can be masked by such downstream metrics" (Section 5.1). This is critical evidence that emergence reflects a real phenomenon in downstream task performance, not a measurement illusion.

**Category 1: Few-Shot Prompted Emergent Abilities**

Section 3 documents eight specific emergent abilities in the few-shot prompting setting, where models receive input-output examples in context before answering unseen queries (illustrated in Figure 1). Figure 2 displays scaling curves for all eight tasks across multiple model families. **Task A (Modified Arithmetic)** tests 3-digit addition/subtraction and 2-digit multiplication from BIG-Bench; GPT-3 and LaMDA show "close-to-zero performance for several orders of magnitude of training compute, before performance jumps to sharply above random at $2 \cdot 10^{22}$ training FLOPs (13B parameters) for GPT-3, and $10^{23}$ training FLOPs (68B parameters) for LaMDA" (Section 3). **Task B (IPA Transliteration)** tests converting International Phonetic Alphabet to text, showing similar emergence around the same scale. **Task C (Word Unscramble)** tests recovering words from scrambled letters, also emerging at approximately $10^{23}$ FLOPs. **Task D (Persian QA)** tests question-answering in Persian, demonstrating that emergence occurs for multilingual tasks, not just English. **Task E (TruthfulQA)** is adversarially curated against GPT-3; "small Gopher models also do not perform above random until scaled up to the largest model of $5 \cdot 10^{23}$ training FLOPs (280B parameters), for which performance jumps to more than 20% above random" (Section 3). **Task F (Grounded Conceptual Mappings)** requires mapping textual grid world directions, emerging only at the largest GPT-3 scale. **Task G (MMLU)** aggregates 57 tests across math, history, law, and more; "models of $\sim 10^{22}$ training FLOPs ($\sim 10$B parameters) or smaller do not perform better than guessing on average over all the topics, scaling up to 3–5 $\cdot 10^{23}$ training FLOPs (70B–280B parameters) enables performance to substantially surpass random" (Section 3). **Task H (Word in Context/WiC)** is particularly instructive: "GPT-3 and Chinchilla fail to achieve one-shot performance of better than random, even when scaled to their largest model size of $\sim 5 \cdot 10^{23}$ FLOPs" but "above-random performance eventually emerged when PaLM was scaled to $2.5 \cdot 10^{24}$ FLOPs (540B parameters)" (Section 3). This last example demonstrates that emergence thresholds are not fixed—further scaling can unlock abilities that were flat at previous scales.

**Category 2: Augmented Prompting Strategy Emergence**

Section 4 documents a different type of emergence where **techniques themselves** only become effective at certain scales, rather than raw task capability. Figure 3 displays four examples. **Technique A (Chain-of-Thought Prompting)** guides models to produce intermediate reasoning steps before final answers; this "only surpasses standard prompting without intermediate steps when scaled to $10^{23}$ training FLOPs ($\sim 100$B parameters)" (Section 4). The mechanism is that smaller models cannot reliably generate correct intermediate steps, so the technique provides no benefit or even harms performance. **Technique B (Instruction Tuning)** finetunes models on tasks phrased as natural language instructions; Wei et al. (2022a) found this "hurts performance for models of $7 \cdot 10^{21}$ training FLOPs (8B parameters) or smaller, and only improves performance when scaled to $10^{23}$ training FLOPs ($\sim 100$B parameters)" (Section 4). **Technique C (Scratchpad Finetuning)** trains models to predict intermediate computation outputs for multi-step tasks; "on 8-digit addition, using a scratchpad only helps for models of $\sim 9 \cdot 10^{19}$ training FLOPs (40M parameters) or larger" (Section 4). **Technique D (Calibration via P(True))** measures whether models can predict which questions they will answer correctly using a True/False evaluation technique; "the superiority of the True/False technique only emerges when scaled to the largest model scale of $\sim 3 \cdot 10^{23}$ training FLOPs (52B parameters)" (Section 4). This category is conceptually distinct from Section 3 because it shows that not only do capabilities emerge, but the **effectiveness of methods for eliciting capabilities** also emerges at scale.

**Threshold Variability and Confounding Factors**

The paper explicitly acknowledges that emergence thresholds are not immutable properties of abilities. Section 2 states: "the scale at which an ability is first observed to emerge depends on a number of factors and is not an immutable property of the ability." Several factors can shift thresholds: **Data quality**—"emergence may occur with less training compute or fewer model parameters for models trained on higher-quality data" (Section 2). **Architecture differences**—Section 5.2 notes that "PaLM 62B in fact achieves above-random performance" on 14 BIG-Bench tasks where "LaMDA 137B and GPT-3 175B models perform at near-random, despite having fewer model parameters and training FLOPs." Potential reasons include "high-quality training data (e.g., more multilingual and code data than LaMDA) and architectural differences (e.g., split digit-encodings)" (Section 5.2). **Training objectives**—Tay et al. (2022c) showed "a computationally-efficient continued pre-training stage on a mixture-of-denoisers objective enabled emergent performance on several BIG-Bench tasks" at smaller scales (Section 5.2). **Model architecture type**—Sanh et al. (2022) induced instruction-following behavior in an 11B encoder-decoder T5 model, whereas Wei et al. (2022a) initially found it only worked for 68B+ decoder-only models (Section 5.2). This variability is important because it means emergence is a function of "many correlated variables" not just raw scale (Section 2).

**Alternative X-Axis: Perplexity as Scale Proxy**

Section 5.3 and Figure 4 explore whether emergence can be viewed through lenses other than FLOPs or parameters. The paper plots MMLU performance against **WikiText103 perplexity** (a standard language modeling benchmark measuring how well models predict held-out text). For Gopher and Chinchilla, "WikiText103 perplexity and training FLOPs happen to be highly correlated," so "the plots of emergent abilities look similar for both" (Section 5.3). However, the paper cautions that "this correlation between WikiText103 perplexity and scale may not hold in the future as new techniques beyond vanilla dense Transformer models are developed" such as retrieval-augmented models (Section 5.3). This analysis serves two purposes: it validates that emergence is not an artifact of using FLOPs specifically, and it opens the possibility that future work might predict emergence from perplexity or other capability-adjacent metrics rather than raw compute. Figure 4 shows six plots: three in the top row showing relationships between training FLOPs, model parameters, and WikiText103 perplexity; three in the bottom row showing MMLU accuracy as a function of each of these three x-axes (Section 5.3).

**Task-Type Analysis and Emergence Patterns**

Appendix A.3 provides systematic analysis of which task types are most likely to exhibit emergence. The authors "manually classified all 210 BIG-Bench tasks as thus far emergent or not" using the Section 3 definition (Appendix A.3). Figure 8 shows emergence rates by keyword tag. The keywords with the **highest percentage of emergent tasks** are "analogical reasoning, word sense disambiguation, truthfulness, social reasoning, and emotional understanding" (Appendix A.3). Surprisingly, "arithmetic and mathematics had relatively low percentage of emergent tasks, which was unexpected since some of the earliest examples of emergence were on arithmetic" (Appendix A.3). Conversely, keywords with the **largest fraction of flat scaling curves** (no model better than random) include "visual reasoning" (8/13 tasks), "non-language," "repeated interaction," "context length," "computer code," and "multi-step"—all targeting known weaknesses of language models (Appendix A.3). Appendix B further stratifies MMLU by category (Humanities, STEM, Social Science, Other) and finds that "Social Science and Humanities have the largest jump in performance from the second-largest to the largest model, and STEM has the smallest jump in performance" (Appendix B, Figure 9). This task-type analysis is important because it begins to answer the open question of "which types of tasks are more likely to be emergent" (Section 5.1).

**Design Choices and Their Rationale**

Several methodological choices warrant explanation. **Why FLOPs over parameters?** The paper uses training FLOPs as the primary x-axis because it better captures total computational investment, and "using training FLOPs or model parameters as the x-axis produces curves with similar shapes due to the fact that most dense Transformer language model families have scaled training compute roughly proportionally with model parameters" (Section 2). However, Appendix D provides analogous figures with parameter count on the x-axis (Figures 11, 12, 13) for completeness. **Why multiple model families?** Analyzing GPT-3, LaMDA, Gopher, Chinchilla, and PaLM together ensures emergence is not architecture-specific; if only one family showed emergence, it could be dismissed as a peculiarity of that architecture. **Why the phase transition definition?** The strict definition (near-random to above-random) distinguishes true emergence from smooth scaling, which is important because "scaling curves for cross-entropy loss have been shown to empirically span more than seven orders of magnitude" and are predictable (Section 1)—the paper's contribution is showing that downstream task performance does not always follow this pattern. **Why survey rather than new experiments?** The authors state their goal is "not to characterize or claim that a specific scale is required to observe emergent abilities, but rather, we aim to discuss examples of emergent behavior in prior work" (Section 2). This is appropriate for a phenomenon that requires massive computational resources to study—individual research groups cannot easily replicate 540B parameter model training, so synthesizing existing work is the feasible approach.

**Limitations and Unanswered Questions**

The paper is explicit about what it does not resolve. Section 5.1 states: "there are currently few compelling explanations for why such abilities emerge in the way they do" and "this analysis does not explain why downstream metrics are emergent or enable us to predict the scale at which emergence occurs." The cross-entropy loss analysis in Appendix A shows that "small models do improve in some ways that downstream metrics that EM/BLEU/Acc do not capture" but "these tasks are still considered emergent, and this analysis does not provide any straightforward indicators of how to predict such emergent behaviors" (Appendix A.1). Section 5.6 outlines open directions including understanding why emergence occurs, whether further scaling unlocks new abilities, and how to lower emergence thresholds through better architectures or data. The paper also acknowledges that "an ability could emerge and then plateau; in other words, there is no guarantee that scaling enables an ability to reach the desired level" (Section 5.2). These limitations are important because they frame emergence as an observed phenomenon requiring explanation, not a solved problem.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper's contributions are primarily conceptual and synthetic rather than methodological. The authors do not propose new models or training techniques; instead, they establish a framework for understanding a phenomenon that was previously discussed only anecdotally. Below are the most significant innovations, distinguished by whether they represent fundamental shifts in understanding or incremental refinements of existing ideas.

### 4.1 Formalizing Emergence as a Testable Phase Transition (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Changed:** Prior to this work, "emergence" in machine learning was used informally to describe any capability that appeared surprising or unexpected. Papers like Brown et al. (2020) documented specific instances (e.g., GPT-3's few-shot learning) but did not provide a unified criterion for what counts as emergence versus smooth improvement.

**The Innovation:** This paper introduces an **operational definition** tied to scaling curve morphology: emergence occurs when performance remains near-random across multiple orders of magnitude, then jumps substantially above-random at a critical threshold (Section 2). This transforms emergence from a vague qualitative claim into a **falsifiable empirical pattern** that can be verified or refuted by plotting performance against scale.

**Why It Matters:** This definition enables several critical distinctions:
- **Emergent vs. Smooth:** Tasks like TriviaQA show smooth scaling (Figure 13, Appendix D), while tasks like 3-digit arithmetic show phase transitions (Figure 2A). These require different theoretical explanations.
- **Emergent vs. Flat:** Some tasks never emerge regardless of scale (Appendix E.4 lists 40+ such tasks). Distinguishing "not yet emerged" from "will never emerge" is essential for forecasting.
- **Predictability Implications:** If emergence is a phase transition, then extrapolation from small models is fundamentally invalid for certain capabilities. This challenges the assumption that scaling laws for loss translate to scaling laws for capabilities.

**Significance:** This is a **fundamental innovation** because it creates shared vocabulary and evaluation criteria for the field. Without this definition, researchers could not systematically compare emergence claims across papers or model families.

### 4.2 Discovery That Prompting Techniques Themselves Emerge (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Changed:** Prior work treated prompting strategies (e.g., chain-of-thought, instruction tuning) as methods that should work uniformly across model sizes—if a technique is valid, it should help regardless of scale.

**The Innovation:** Section 4 reveals that **the effectiveness of interaction methods is itself emergent**. Chain-of-thought prompting provides no benefit (and can harm performance) for models below $\sim 10^{23}$ training FLOPs ($\sim 100$B parameters), only becoming advantageous above this threshold (Figure 3A). Similarly, instruction tuning actively degrades performance for models under 8B parameters before improving at larger scales (Figure 3B).

**Why It Matters:** This finding has profound implications for how we think about model capabilities:
- **Capability Elicitation Is Scale-Dependent:** You cannot assume a prompting technique discovered on a large model will work on smaller models. This affects reproducibility and accessibility—researchers without access to large models may conclude techniques are ineffective when they are simply scale-gated.
- **Technique Discovery Requires Scale:** New interaction paradigms may only become discoverable at certain scales. This suggests the space of usable prompting strategies expands with scale, not just the space of raw capabilities.
- **Safety Implications:** If dangerous capabilities can only be elicited via techniques that work at large scales, then smaller models may be inherently safer not just because they lack capabilities, but because the methods to activate certain behaviors do not function.

**Significance:** This is a **fundamental innovation** because it shifts the unit of analysis from "what can the model do" to "what can we do with the model." The interaction surface between humans and models is itself scale-dependent.

### 4.3 Validation That Emergence Is Not a Measurement Artifact (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Changed:** A natural skepticism about emergence is that it might reflect measurement choices rather than real capability shifts. For example, exact match metrics do not award partial credit, so gradual improvements in output quality could appear as sudden jumps when the output finally matches exactly.

**The Innovation:** Appendix A conducts systematic validation across multiple dimensions:
- **Alternative Metrics:** Three generative BIG-Bench tasks were evaluated using seven different metrics (BLEU, ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, ROUGE-L-Sum, BLEURT, Sequence F1, Multiple Choice Grade). Emergence appeared consistently across all metrics (Figure 7, Appendix A.2).
- **Cross-Entropy Loss Analysis:** For six emergent tasks, cross-entropy loss improves smoothly even when downstream metrics remain at random (Figures 5–6, Appendix A.1). This shows models *are* learning something at small scales, but this learning does not translate to task success until a threshold.
- **Alternative X-Axes:** Emergence patterns persist when plotting against WikiText103 perplexity instead of FLOPs or parameters (Figure 4, Section 5.3).

**Why It Matters:** This validation addresses the most serious critique of emergence claims. The finding that cross-entropy loss improves smoothly while downstream metrics remain flat suggests:
- **Representation vs. Utilization Gap:** Small models may develop relevant internal representations but lack the capacity to utilize them for task success.
- **Threshold Mechanisms:** Something qualitative changes at the threshold beyond gradual improvement—possibly related to reasoning depth, working memory, or compositional generalization.

**Significance:** This is a **fundamental innovation** because it establishes emergence as a real phenomenon requiring explanation, not a measurement illusion. This justification is prerequisite for all subsequent research on emergence.

### 4.4 Demonstration That Emergence Thresholds Are Malleable (Incremental but Important)

**What Changed:** The initial framing of emergence might suggest fixed thresholds—certain capabilities require certain minimum scales. The WiC benchmark example initially supported this: GPT-3 175B and Chinchilla 70B both failed at one-shot WiC, suggesting a threshold beyond 280B parameters.

**The Innovation:** Section 5.2 reveals that **PaLM 62B achieves above-random WiC performance despite having fewer parameters and less training compute than GPT-3 175B**. This demonstrates that emergence thresholds depend on factors beyond raw scale:
- **Data Quality:** PaLM used more multilingual and code data than LaMDA.
- **Architecture:** PaLM used split digit-encodings and other architectural differences.
- **Training Objectives:** Continued pre-training with mixture-of-denoisers objectives enabled emergence at smaller scales (Tay et al., 2022c).

**Why It Matters:** This finding reframes emergence from an inevitable consequence of scale to an **optimizable target**:
- **Democratization Potential:** If thresholds can be lowered through better data or architectures, smaller models could access emergent capabilities, making research more accessible.
- **Efficiency Implications:** Researchers should not assume more scale is the only path to new capabilities; architectural and data improvements may be more cost-effective.
- **Prediction Complexity:** Emergence cannot be predicted from scale alone; data composition and architecture matter.

**Significance:** This is an **incremental but important** contribution because it modifies the initial scale-centric framing. It does not invalidate the emergence phenomenon but shows it is more nuanced than raw compute alone.

### 4.5 Systematic Task-Type Analysis Identifying Emergence Patterns (Incremental Contribution)

**What Changed:** Before this work, emergence examples were cherry-picked from notable successes. There was no systematic understanding of which task types are more or less likely to exhibit emergence.

**The Innovation:** Appendix A.3 manually classifies all 210 BIG-Bench tasks as emergent, smooth, or flat, then analyzes emergence rates by keyword tags (Figure 8). Key findings:
- **Highest Emergence Rates:** Analogical reasoning, word sense disambiguation, truthfulness, social reasoning, and emotional understanding.
- **Lowest Emergence Rates:** Visual reasoning (8/13 tasks flat), non-language, repeated interaction, context length, computer code.
- **Surprising Result:** Arithmetic and mathematics had relatively low emergence percentages despite early examples like 3-digit addition being emergent.

Appendix B further stratifies MMLU by category, finding Social Science and Humanities show the largest emergence jumps while STEM shows the smallest (Figure 9).

**Why It Matters:** This analysis begins to answer the open question from Section 5.1: "which types of tasks are more likely to be emergent?" The findings suggest:
- **Reasoning Is Not Monolithic:** Some reasoning tasks (analogical, social) emerge more readily than others (mathematical, logical).
- **Modality Matters:** Non-language and visual tasks are less likely to emerge in text-only models, as expected.
- **Knowledge Domains Differ:** Humanities and social science knowledge may be more emergent than STEM knowledge, possibly due to how this knowledge is represented in training data.

**Significance:** This is an **incremental contribution** because it does not explain *why* these patterns exist, only that they do. However, it provides the first empirical basis for hypothesizing about emergence mechanisms and prioritizing future research directions.

### Summary: Incremental vs. Fundamental Contributions

| Contribution | Type | Reasoning |
|-------------|------|-----------|
| Operational definition of emergence | Fundamental | Creates testable criterion where none existed; enables field-wide comparison |
| Prompting techniques themselves emerge | Fundamental | Shifts unit of analysis from capabilities to interaction methods |
| Validation against measurement artifacts | Fundamental | Establishes emergence as real phenomenon requiring explanation |
| Thresholds are malleable | Incremental | Modifies scale-centric framing but does not invalidate core phenomenon |
| Task-type emergence patterns | Incremental | Descriptive analysis without mechanistic explanation |

The fundamental innovations collectively establish emergence as a legitimate scientific phenomenon worthy of systematic study. The incremental contributions refine understanding and point toward future research directions. Together, they transform emergence from anecdotal observations into a structured research agenda.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This paper does not conduct new experiments in the traditional sense. Instead, it synthesizes and re-analyzes results from prior work across five language model families. This section describes the evaluation methodology underlying the surveyed results, summarizes the quantitative findings with specific numbers, and assesses whether the evidence convincingly supports the emergence claims.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Model Families and Scale Coverage**

The paper aggregates data from five independent language model families, providing coverage across **eight orders of magnitude** in training compute. Table 2 (Appendix C) provides complete specifications:

| Model Family | Parameter Range | Training FLOPs Range | Training Tokens |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| GPT-3 | 125M to 175B | $2.25 \times 10^{20}$ to $3.14 \times 10^{23}$ | 300B (all sizes) |
| LaMDA | 2.1M to 137B | $3.30 \times 10^{18}$ to $5.54 \times 10^{23}$ | 132B to 674B |
| Gopher | 417M to 280B | $7.51 \times 10^{20}$ to $5.46 \times 10^{23}$ | 300B to 325B |
| Chinchilla | 417M to 70B | $7.86 \times 10^{20}$ to $5.63 \times 10^{23}$ | 314B to 1.34T |
| PaLM | 8B to 540B | $3.74 \times 10^{22}$ to $2.53 \times 10^{24}$ | 780B (all sizes) |
| Anthropic LM | 800M to 52B | $4.08 \times 10^{21}$ to $2.65 \times 10^{22}$ | 850B (all sizes) |

The largest model (PaLM 540B) represents roughly **8x the computational budget** of GPT-3 175B (Appendix C). This breadth is critical: if emergence appears at similar scales across different architectures and training procedures, it suggests the phenomenon is fundamental rather than architecture-specific.

**Primary X-Axis: Training FLOPs**

The paper uses **training FLOPs** as the primary x-axis for scaling curves (Section 2). The rationale is that "language models trained with more compute tend to also have more parameters" and "most dense Transformer language model families have scaled training compute roughly proportionally with model parameters." However, the authors acknowledge this is imperfect: Chinchilla uses one-fourth as many parameters as Gopher but similar training compute (Section 2).

For completeness, Appendix D provides analogous figures with **parameter count** on the x-axis (Figures 11, 12, 13). The paper notes these produce "curves with similar shapes" due to the correlation between parameters and compute in dense models.

**Benchmarks and Tasks**

The paper surveys results across multiple benchmark suites:

- **BIG-Bench**: A crowd-sourced suite of over 200 tasks covering diverse capabilities (BIG-Bench, 2022). The paper manually classified all 210 tasks as emergent, smooth, or flat (Appendix A.3).
- **MMLU**: Massive Multi-task Language Understanding with 57 topics spanning math, history, law, and more (Hendrycks et al., 2021a).
- **TruthfulQA**: Adversarially curated benchmark measuring ability to answer questions truthfully (Lin et al., 2021).
- **WiC**: Word in Context benchmark for semantic understanding (Pilehvar & Camacho-Collados, 2019).
- **GSM8K**: Grade school math word problems (used for chain-of-thought evaluation).

**Evaluation Metrics**

The paper uses task-appropriate metrics (Section 3, Appendix A):

- **Accuracy (%)**: For classification tasks (e.g., TruthfulQA, MMLU, WiC)
- **Exact Match (%)**: For generative tasks requiring precise outputs (e.g., arithmetic, word unscramble)
- **BLEU (%)**: For tasks with flexible text outputs (e.g., IPA transliteration)
- **Cross-entropy loss**: For validation analysis in Appendix A.1
- **WikiText103 perplexity**: For alternative scale proxy in Section 5.3

**Baseline Comparisons**

Each scaling curve includes a **random performance baseline** shown as a horizontal line in Figures 2, 3, 11, and 12. For example:
- 4-choice multiple choice tasks have 25% random baseline
- Binary tasks have 50% random baseline
- Generative tasks have near-0% random baseline for exact match

The emergence criterion requires performance to jump from "near-random" to "substantially above random" (Section 2).

For chain-of-thought (Figure 3A), the gap between "No chain of thought" and "Chain of thought" lines is negligible below $10^{22}$ FLOPs, then widens dramatically at $10^{23}$ FLOPs, reaching approximately **20 percentage points** on GSM8K accuracy.

For instruction tuning (Figure 3B), the "No instruction tuning" line actually **outperforms** the "Instruction tuning" line for models at $7 \cdot 10^{21}$ FLOPs (8B parameters), but this reverses at $10^{23}$ FLOPs where instruction tuning provides approximately **10–15 percentage point** improvement on the 10 NLU task average.

**Cross-Entropy Loss Validation (Appendix A.1)**

Appendix A.1 analyzes whether emergence is a measurement artifact by comparing downstream metrics against cross-entropy loss. For six emergent BIG-Bench tasks:

- **Generative tasks** (Figure 5): Modified arithmetic, IPA transliterate, and word unscramble all show cross-entropy loss improving smoothly from $10^{20}$ to $10^{24}$ FLOPs, even when exact match/BLEU remains near-0% until $10^{22}$–$10^{23}$ FLOPs.
- **Classification tasks** (Figure 6): Logical arguments, sports understanding, and figure of speech show similar patterns—cross-entropy loss decreases (improves) steadily while accuracy remains at random until a threshold.

The paper concludes: "cross-entropy loss improves even for small model scales where the downstream metrics (exact match, BLEU, and accuracy) are close to random and do not improve" (Section 5.1). This represents **Outcome 2** from the analysis framework: models improve in ways not captured by downstream metrics until a critical scale enables task success.

**Alternative Metric Validation (Appendix A.2)**

Appendix A.2 tests whether exact match metrics create artificial emergence by evaluating three generative BIG-Bench tasks with seven different metrics (Figure 7):

- Exact match
- ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, ROUGE-L-Sum
- BLEURT
- BLEU
- Sequence F1
- Multiple choice grade

For all three tasks (modified arithmetic, IPA transliterate, periodic elements), "emergent behavior appears to be independent of which evaluation metric is used" (Appendix A.2). The phase transition pattern persists across all metrics, suggesting emergence is not a metric artifact.

**Task-Type Analysis (Appendix A.3, Figure 8)**

Appendix A.3 manually classifies all 210 BIG-Bench tasks and analyzes emergence rates by keyword tag. Key findings:

**Highest percentage of emergent tasks:**
- Analogical reasoning
- Word sense disambiguation
- Truthfulness
- Social reasoning
- Emotional understanding

**Lowest percentage of emergent tasks (highest flat rate):**
- Visual reasoning: 8/13 tasks (62%) flat
- Non-language
- Repeated interaction
- Context length
- Computer code
- Multi-step

Surprisingly, "arithmetic and mathematics had relatively low percentage of emergent tasks, which was unexpected since some of the earliest examples of emergence were on arithmetic" (Appendix A.3).

**MMLU Category Analysis (Appendix B, Figures 9–10)**

Appendix B stratifies MMLU performance by four categories (Humanities, STEM, Social Science, Other) for Gopher and Chinchilla:

| Category | 7B Model Performance | Largest Model Performance | Jump Magnitude |
|----------|---------------------|--------------------------|----------------|
| Humanities | ~25% (random) | ~55–60% | ~30–35 points |
| Social Science | ~25% (random) | ~55–65% | ~30–40 points |
| Other | ~25% (random) | ~45–50% | ~20–25 points |
| STEM | ~25% (random) | ~40–45% | ~15–20 points |

Figure 9 shows "Social Science and Humanities have the largest jump in performance from the second-largest to the largest model, and STEM has the smallest jump in performance" (Appendix B). This suggests emergence is not uniform across knowledge domains.

**Perplexity as Alternative Scale Proxy (Section 5.3, Figure 4)**

Figure 4 plots MMLU performance against three x-axes: training FLOPs, model parameters, and WikiText103 perplexity. For Gopher and Chinchilla, "WikiText103 perplexity and training FLOPs happen to be highly correlated," so emergence patterns look similar across all three views. However, the paper cautions this correlation "may not hold in the future as new techniques beyond vanilla dense Transformer models are developed" (Section 5.3).

**Threshold Malleability Evidence (Section 5.2)**

Section 5.2 documents 14 BIG-Bench tasks where "PaLM 62B in fact achieves above-random performance" despite "LaMDA 137B and GPT-3 175B models perform at near-random, despite having fewer model parameters and training FLOPs" (Section 5.2). Appendix F lists these tasks: anachronisms, ascii word recognition, conceptual combinations, cryptonite, disambiguation qa, emoji movie, goal step wikihow, gre reading comprehension, linguistics puzzles, logic grid puzzle, metaphor boolean, metaphor understanding, odd one out, and parsinlu qa.

This demonstrates that emergence thresholds are not fixed properties of tasks but depend on data quality, architecture, and training procedures.

### 5.3 Assessment of Experimental Support

**Strengths of the Evidence**

The experimental evidence supporting emergence claims is **convincing** for several reasons:

1. **Cross-Model Replication**: Emergence appears at similar scales across five independent model families (GPT-3, LaMDA, Gopher, Chinchilla, PaLM) with different architectures, training data, and training procedures. If emergence were an artifact of one model family, it would not replicate across all five.

2. **Multiple Validation Analyses**: The paper conducts three independent validation checks:
   - Cross-entropy loss analysis shows smooth improvement even when downstream metrics are flat (Appendix A.1)
   - Seven alternative metrics all show emergence patterns (Appendix A.2)
   - Three alternative x-axes (FLOPs, parameters, perplexity) all show emergence (Figure 4)

3. **Large Scale Coverage**: The analysis spans eight orders of magnitude in training compute ($3.30 \times 10^{18}$ to $2.53 \times 10^{24}$ FLOPs), providing sufficient resolution to distinguish smooth scaling from phase transitions.

4. **Systematic Task Classification**: The manual classification of all 210 BIG-Bench tasks (Appendix A.3) provides a comprehensive view rather than cherry-picked examples.

**Limitations and Caveats**

However, several limitations temper the conclusions:

1. **No Mechanistic Explanation**: The paper explicitly states "there are currently few compelling explanations for why such abilities emerge in the way they do" (Section 5.1). The experiments document *that* emergence occurs but not *why*.

2. **Subjective Threshold Definition**: The emergence criterion ("near-random" to "substantially above random") involves subjective judgment. The paper acknowledges: "this definition is potentially subjective based on the definition of 'near-random'" (Appendix A.3). Two co-authors agreed on classifications, but this does not eliminate subjectivity.

3. **Confounding Variables**: As Section 5.2 notes, emergence thresholds vary based on data quality, architecture, and training objectives. PaLM 62B outperforms GPT-3 175B on 14 tasks despite smaller scale. This means emergence cannot be predicted from scale alone, complicating the core claim.

4. **No Controlled Ablations**: The paper synthesizes results from prior work rather than conducting controlled ablations. For example, there is no experiment holding architecture constant while varying only scale, or holding scale constant while varying only data quality. The computational cost would be prohibitive, but this limits causal claims.

5. **Flat Tasks Not Explained**: Appendix E.4 lists 40+ tasks where "no model performs better than random" regardless of scale. The paper does not explain why some tasks never emerge. Examples include: checkmate in one, chinese remainder theorem, sudoku, program synthesis, and semantic parsing tasks.

6. **Potential Plateau**: Section 5.2 acknowledges "an ability could emerge and then plateau; in other words, there is no guarantee that scaling enables an ability to reach the desired level." The experiments show emergence but not whether continued scaling leads to human-level performance.

**Mixed Results and Conditional Findings**

Some results are conditional rather than universal:

- **Instruction Tuning**: Works for 68B+ decoder-only models (Wei et al., 2022a) but Sanh et al. (2022) induced similar behavior in 11B encoder-decoder T5 models (Section 5.2). This suggests architecture matters as much as scale.

- **WiC Benchmark**: GPT-3 175B and Chinchilla 70B both fail at one-shot WiC, but PaLM 540B succeeds (Figure 2H). Brown et al. (2020) initially suggested architectural changes were needed, but "further scaling a decoder-only language model was actually enough" (Section 5). This shows emergence thresholds can shift with more scale.

- **Arithmetic Tasks**: Early emergence examples included arithmetic (Brown et al., 2020), but Appendix A.3 finds "arithmetic and mathematics had relatively low percentage of emergent tasks." This suggests simple arithmetic emerges early but complex mathematical reasoning may not.

- **Bias and Toxicity**: Section 5.4 reports mixed findings on risks: scaling improved performance on WinoGender (reducing bias) but BIG-Bench found "bias can increase with scaling for ambiguous contexts" on BBQ. Toxicity can increase with scale but is mitigatable with appropriate prompting (Askell et al., 2021).

### 5.4 Robustness Checks and Failure Cases

**Robustness Checks Conducted**

The paper includes several robustness checks that strengthen confidence in the emergence claims:

1. **Multiple Model Families**: As noted, emergence replicates across GPT-3, LaMDA, Gopher, Chinchilla, and PaLM (Figures 2, 11).

2. **Alternative Metrics**: Seven metrics all show emergence for generative tasks (Figure 7, Appendix A.2).

3. **Alternative X-Axes**: FLOPs, parameters, and perplexity all produce similar emergence curves (Figure 4).

4. **Cross-Entropy Loss**: Smooth loss improvement alongside flat downstream metrics confirms models are learning something at small scales (Figures 5–6, Appendix A.1).

5. **Parameter-Based Figures**: Appendix D provides Figures 11, 12, 13 showing emergence with parameter count on x-axis, confirming FLOPs-based figures are not artifacts.

**Failure Cases and Flat Tasks**

Appendix E.4 lists 40+ tasks where no model achieves above-random performance regardless of scale. These represent **failure cases** for the emergence hypothesis—capabilities that do not emerge even at maximum tested scale:

- **Reasoning Tasks**: Checkmate in one, chinese remainder theorem, mathematical induction, multistep arithmetic
- **Program Understanding**: Program synthesis, python programming challenge, semantic parsing spider
- **Visual Tasks**: Cifar10 classification, mnist ascii, color, visual reasoning tasks
- **Language Games**: Language games, twenty questions, web of lies, tracking shuffled objects
- **Self-Awareness**: Self awareness, authorship verification

The existence of flat tasks is important: it shows emergence is not inevitable with scale. Some capabilities may require architectural changes, retrieval augmentation, or may never emerge in autoregressive language models.

**Tasks That Get Worse With Scale**

Appendix E.5 lists five tasks where "model gets worse with scale": bbq lite, bias from probabilities, diverse social bias, movie recommendation, and unqover. This represents **inverse scaling**—performance degrades as models grow larger. The paper mentions the "Inverse Scaling Prize" in Section 5.4, acknowledging that not all capabilities improve with scale.

**Boundary Conditions**

The emergence phenomenon has clear boundary conditions:

1. **Dense Transformer Models**: The analysis focuses on "pre-trained Transformer language models" (Section 2, footnote 1). Sparse mixture-of-experts models, retrieval-augmented models, or non-Transformer architectures may show different patterns.

2. **Pre-Training Scale**: The analysis assumes models are trained to convergence. Undertrained models may not show emergence at the same thresholds.

3. **Prompting Setup**: The few-shot emergence results (Section 3) apply to the prompting paradigm. Fine-tuned models may show different scaling behavior.

4. **Evaluation Protocol**: The emergence definition requires specific evaluation protocols (e.g., few-shot prompting with specific numbers of examples). Different protocols may yield different thresholds.

### 5.5 Summary Assessment

The experimental evidence **convincingly demonstrates** that emergent abilities exist as an empirical phenomenon. The cross-model replication, multiple validation analyses, and large scale coverage provide strong support for the core claim that certain capabilities appear unpredictably at larger scales.

However, the evidence **does not explain** why emergence occurs or enable prediction of emergence thresholds. The paper explicitly acknowledges this limitation: "this analysis does not explain why downstream metrics are emergent or enable us to predict the scale at which emergence occurs" (Section 5.1).

The existence of flat tasks (Appendix E.4) and inverse scaling tasks (Appendix E.5) shows emergence is not universal. Some capabilities never emerge regardless of scale, and some degrade with scale. This nuance is important for setting realistic expectations about what scaling can achieve.

Overall, the experiments successfully establish emergence as a **real phenomenon requiring explanation** rather than a measurement artifact or anecdotal observation. This provides a foundation for future research on prediction, explanation, and mitigation of emergent capabilities and risks.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This paper establishes emergence as an empirical phenomenon but leaves significant questions unanswered. Understanding these limitations is crucial because they define the boundaries of what we currently know—and what we still need to discover—about emergent abilities. Below I analyze the key assumptions, unaddressed scenarios, and open questions that constrain the paper's conclusions.

### 6.1 Definitional Subjectivity and Measurement Constraints

**The "Near-Random" Threshold Is Subjective**

The operational definition of emergence requires performance to jump from "near-random" to "substantially above random" (Section 2). However, the paper explicitly acknowledges this involves judgment calls:

> Because this definition is potentially subjective based on the definition of "near-random" (and any heuristic we decide on would encode these subjective biases), two co-authors of the paper worked together and agreed with confidence on all the tasks labeled as emergent. (Appendix A.3)

This creates three problems:

- **Reproducibility**: Other researchers might classify the same scaling curves differently based on their threshold for "near-random." A task at 26% accuracy on a 25% random baseline might be classified as emergent by one team and smooth by another.
- **Boundary Cases**: Some tasks may show gradual improvement rather than sharp phase transitions. The binary emergent/not-emergent classification may not capture these intermediate cases.
- **Statistical Significance**: The paper does not report confidence intervals or statistical tests for whether performance jumps are significant versus noise. Figure 6 shows "Logical arguments only has 32 samples, which may contribute to noise" (Appendix A.1.2), yet this task is still classified.

**Metric Choice May Still Mask Nuance**

While Appendix A.2 validates emergence across seven different metrics (BLEU, ROUGE, BLEURT, etc.), the paper acknowledges a fundamental limitation:

> Using exact string match as the evaluation metric for long-sequence targets may disguise compounding incremental improvements as emergence. (Section 5.1)

The validation shows emergence persists across metrics, but this does not fully resolve the concern. Consider a model that gradually improves its reasoning steps but only occasionally produces the exact correct final answer. The cross-entropy loss analysis in Appendix A.1 confirms models improve at small scales, but the paper admits:

> This analysis does not explain why downstream metrics are emergent or enable us to predict the scale at which emergence occurs. (Section 5.1)

The measurement infrastructure documents *that* emergence occurs but cannot distinguish between:
- A true capability threshold (the model literally cannot perform the task below a certain scale)
- A utilization threshold (the model has the capacity but cannot reliably access it)
- A measurement threshold (the model can perform the task but evaluation metrics fail to capture it)

### 6.2 Explanatory Gap: Documenting Without Explaining

**No Mechanistic Theory of Emergence**

The most significant limitation is that the paper provides **zero mechanistic explanation** for why emergence occurs. Section 5.1 states plainly:

> Although there are dozens of examples of emergent abilities, there are currently few compelling explanations for why such abilities emerge in the way they do.

The paper offers intuitions—such as multi-step reasoning requiring $O(l)$ layers for $l$ computation steps, or knowledge tasks requiring sufficient parameters for memorization—but these are speculative. The paper does not:

- Identify which internal representations change at emergence thresholds
- Explain why some tasks emerge at $10^{22}$ FLOPs while others require $10^{24}$ FLOPs
- Provide a theoretical framework for predicting emergence from model architecture or training data

This is a critical gap because without explanation, we cannot:
- **Predict** which capabilities will emerge at future scales
- **Engineer** models to lower emergence thresholds
- **Anticipate** which risks might emerge alongside capabilities

**Cross-Entropy Loss Analysis Is Incomplete**

Appendix A.1 shows that cross-entropy loss improves smoothly even when downstream metrics remain flat. The paper interprets this as evidence that "small models do improve in some ways that downstream metrics... do not capture" (Appendix A.1). However, this raises a deeper question the paper does not answer: **why doesn't smooth loss improvement translate to smooth task performance?**

If the model's probability distribution over correct answers is gradually improving, there should be some gradual improvement in task success rates. The fact that there isn't suggests a **non-linear relationship between loss and capability** that the paper documents but does not explain. This represents a fundamental gap in our understanding of how pre-training objectives relate to downstream capabilities.

### 6.3 Scope Limitations: What the Analysis Does Not Cover

**Model Architecture Constraints**

The paper focuses primarily on **dense Transformer language models**. Section 2 footnote 1 states: "This survey focuses on pre-trained Transformer language models." This excludes several important model classes:

- **Sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)**: The paper mentions MoE models have "more parameters per training/inference compute than dense models" (Section 2) but does not analyze whether emergence patterns differ. MoE models like Switch Transformer (Fedus et al., 2021) or GLaM (Du et al., 2021) may show different emergence thresholds due to their sparse activation patterns.
- **Encoder-Decoder vs. Decoder-Only**: Section 5.2 notes that instruction following emerged at 68B for decoder-only models but at 11B for encoder-decoder T5 models (Sanh et al., 2022). This suggests architecture significantly affects emergence, but the paper does not systematically compare architectures.
- **Retrieval-Augmented Models**: Section 5.3 cautions that the correlation between WikiText103 perplexity and scale "may not hold in the future as new techniques beyond vanilla dense Transformer models are developed (e.g., retrieval-augmented models)" (Section 5.3). Retrieval models may bypass certain emergence thresholds by accessing external knowledge rather than storing it in parameters.

**Task Coverage Gaps**

Appendix E.4 lists 40+ BIG-Bench tasks where **no model performs better than random** regardless of scale. These include:

- **Complex Reasoning**: Checkmate in one, chinese remainder theorem, mathematical induction, multistep arithmetic
- **Program Understanding**: Program synthesis, python programming challenge, semantic parsing spider
- **Visual Tasks**: Cifar10 classification, mnist ascii, visual reasoning (8/13 tasks flat)
- **Self-Reference**: Self awareness, authorship verification

The paper does not explain **why these tasks never emerge**. Possible explanations the paper does not investigate:

- These tasks may require capabilities fundamentally absent from autoregressive language models (e.g., visual processing, formal verification)
- The tasks may be poorly specified or too difficult even for humans
- The evaluation protocols may be mismatched to how language models process information

Additionally, Appendix E.5 lists five tasks where **performance degrades with scale** (inverse scaling): bbq lite, bias from probabilities, diverse social bias, movie recommendation, and unqover. The paper mentions the "Inverse Scaling Prize" (Section 5.4) but does not analyze why some capabilities worsen with scale. This represents a significant blind spot: if some capabilities emerge and others degrade, what determines the direction?

**Training Data and Objective Limitations**

The paper acknowledges that emergence thresholds depend on data quality and training objectives (Section 5.2), but does not systematically analyze these factors:

- **Data Composition**: PaLM 62B outperforms GPT-3 175B on 14 tasks despite smaller scale, potentially due to "more multilingual and code data" (Section 5.2). However, the paper does not ablate data composition to determine which data types drive emergence.
- **Training Objectives**: Tay et al. (2022c) showed "a computationally-efficient continued pre-training stage on a mixture-of-denoisers objective enabled emergent performance on several BIG-Bench tasks" (Section 5.2). But the paper does not analyze which objectives enable which capabilities.
- **Token Counts**: Table 2 (Appendix C) shows training tokens vary from 132B (LaMDA small) to 1.34T (Chinchilla 70B). The paper does not analyze whether token count independently affects emergence thresholds beyond compute.

### 6.4 Computational and Practical Constraints

**No Controlled Ablations**

The paper synthesizes results from prior work rather than conducting controlled experiments. Section 5.2 explicitly acknowledges this limitation:

> While there is not an empirical study ablating every difference between PaLM 62B and prior models (the computational cost would be too high), potential reasons for the better performance of PaLM could include high-quality training data... and architectural differences.

This means the paper cannot make **causal claims** about what drives emergence. When PaLM 62B outperforms GPT-3 175B, we cannot determine whether this is due to:
- Architecture differences (split digit-encodings, attention mechanisms)
- Data quality (more code, more multilingual data)
- Training procedure (optimizer settings, learning rate schedules)
- Random seed effects

The computational cost of training multiple 100B+ parameter models with controlled variations is prohibitive for most research groups. This creates a **methodological bottleneck**: the phenomenon requires massive scale to study, but massive scale prevents controlled experimentation.

**Limited Access to Largest Models**

The analysis depends on results from models that are not publicly available. GPT-3, LaMDA, Gopher, Chinchilla, and PaLM are all restricted-access models. This creates several problems:

- **Verification**: Independent researchers cannot verify the scaling curves or emergence claims.
- **Extension**: Researchers cannot test new hypotheses about emergence without training new large models.
- **Democratization**: Section 5.2 notes that "lowering the scale threshold for emergent abilities will become more important for making research on such abilities available to the community more broadly." But the paper itself relies on results from models that most researchers cannot access.

**Evaluation Protocol Variability**

The paper synthesizes results from multiple independent studies (Brown et al., 2020; Rae et al., 2021; Chowdhery et al., 2022; etc.). These studies may use different:
- **Prompt formats**: Number of few-shot examples, prompt wording, answer formatting
- **Evaluation scripts**: Exact match criteria, normalization procedures
- **Model checkpoints**: Different training stages or fine-tuning procedures

While the paper attempts to standardize by plotting all results on common axes (FLOPs, parameters), underlying protocol differences may introduce noise or systematic biases. The paper does not quantify this variability.

### 6.5 Predictive and Forward-Looking Limitations

**Cannot Predict Future Emergence**

The paper's most consequential limitation is that it **cannot predict** which capabilities will emerge at future scales. Section 5.6 lists "Understanding emergence" as a future direction:

> Future research could potentially analyze emergence in new ways... Overall, understanding emergence is an important direction because it could potentially allow us to predict what abilities future models may have. (Section 5.6)

This admission is significant: the paper documents past emergence but provides no framework for forecasting. This has practical implications:

- **Safety Planning**: If we cannot predict which risks will emerge, we cannot proactively mitigate them.
- **Capability Forecasting**: Organizations cannot plan product roadmaps around capabilities that may or may not emerge.
- **Resource Allocation**: Researchers cannot prioritize which capabilities to study based on emergence likelihood.

**Plateau Uncertainty**

Section 5.2 acknowledges that emergence does not guarantee continued improvement:

> An ability could emerge and then plateau; in other words, there is no guarantee that scaling enables an ability to reach the desired level. (Section 5.2)

The paper shows capabilities emerging from random to above-random, but does not show whether they continue improving toward human-level performance. Figure 2 shows MMLU reaching ~50-60% accuracy at largest scales—still far from expert human performance. The paper does not address:

- Whether emergence is followed by smooth scaling or additional phase transitions
- Whether some capabilities have hard ceilings that scaling cannot overcome
- Whether different capabilities emerge at different rates (some plateauing while others continue improving)

**Risk Characterization Is Incomplete**

Section 5.4 discusses emergent risks but provides less systematic analysis than capabilities:

- **Limited Examples**: The section cites prior work on truthfulness, toxicity, bias, and memorization but does not provide scaling curves analogous to Figure 2 for risks.
- **Mixed Findings**: The paper reports contradictory results: scaling improves WinoGender performance (reducing bias) but BIG-Bench found "bias can increase with scaling for ambiguous contexts" on BBQ (Section 5.4). The paper does not resolve when risks increase versus decrease with scale.
- **Unknown Unknowns**: The paper acknowledges "emergent risks also include phenomena that might only exist in future language models or that have not yet been characterized" (Section 5.4). This is an unavoidable limitation but means the risk analysis is necessarily incomplete.

### 6.6 Trade-offs in the Paper's Approach

**Survey vs. Original Experiments**

The paper explicitly positions itself as a survey rather than proposing new methods (Broader Impact Statement). This choice involves trade-offs:

| Advantage | Disadvantage |
|-----------|--------------|
| Synthesizes evidence across multiple model families | Cannot conduct controlled ablations |
| Lower computational cost | Dependent on others' experimental designs |
| Broader coverage of tasks and models | Less depth on any single phenomenon |
| Establishes shared vocabulary | Cannot test new hypotheses directly |

This is an appropriate trade-off given the computational constraints, but readers should understand the paper's claims are limited to **documenting patterns** rather than **explaining mechanisms**.

**Scale-Centric Framing**

The paper focuses on scale (FLOPs, parameters) as the primary variable, while acknowledging other factors matter (Section 5.2). This framing involves trade-offs:

| Advantage | Disadvantage |
|-----------|--------------|
| Simple, quantifiable x-axis | Oversimplifies multi-factor reality |
| Enables cross-model comparison | May obscure architecture/data effects |
| Aligns with scaling law literature | May mislead readers into thinking scale is the only lever |

The paper does acknowledge that "emergent abilities should probably be viewed as a function of many correlated variables" (Section 5.3), but the primary figures and analysis remain scale-centric.

**Binary Classification of Emergence**

The paper classifies tasks as emergent, smooth, or flat (Appendix A.3). This simplification enables clear communication but may obscure nuance:

- Some tasks may show **partial emergence** (modest jumps rather than dramatic phase transitions)
- Some tasks may emerge differently across **different model families**
- The classification may change as **larger models are trained** (today's flat task may emerge tomorrow)

The paper acknowledges this by noting PaLM 62B shows emergence on tasks where GPT-3 175B was flat (Section 5.2), but the binary classification system does not easily capture this dynamism.

### 6.7 Summary of Key Limitations

| Limitation Category | Specific Issue | Impact on Conclusions |
|--------------------|----------------|---------------------|
| **Definitional** | Subjective "near-random" threshold | Classification may not be reproducible |
| **Explanatory** | No mechanistic theory | Cannot predict or engineer emergence |
| **Scope** | Dense Transformers only | May not generalize to other architectures |
| **Scope** | 40+ flat tasks unexplained | Cannot distinguish "not yet" from "never" |
| **Methodological** | No controlled ablations | Cannot make causal claims |
| **Methodological** | Dependent on restricted models | Independent verification impossible |
| **Predictive** | Cannot forecast future emergence | Limited utility for planning |
| **Predictive** | Plateau uncertainty | Unknown if scaling leads to human-level performance |
| **Risk Analysis** | Incomplete risk characterization | Safety implications unclear |

These limitations do not invalidate the paper's core contribution—establishing emergence as a real, cross-model phenomenon—but they define the boundaries of what we can conclude. The paper successfully demonstrates **that** emergence occurs but leaves open **why**, **when**, and **what next**. Future research must address these gaps to move from documentation to prediction and control.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than document a phenomenon—it fundamentally reshapes how the research community should think about language model development, deployment, and safety. The recognition that capabilities emerge unpredictably at scale creates both opportunities and obligations for researchers and practitioners. Below, I analyze how this work changes the field's landscape, what research directions it enables, and how practitioners should integrate these insights into real-world decisions.

### 7.1 Landscape Shifts: From Predictable Scaling to Unpredictable Capability

**The End of Simple Extrapolation**

Prior to this work, the dominant mental model for language model improvement came from scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022), which showed smooth, predictable improvements in pre-training loss across seven orders of magnitude. This paper demonstrates that **downstream task performance does not follow the same predictable patterns** (Section 1). The implication is profound: you cannot extrapolate from a 10B parameter model to predict what a 100B parameter model will be capable of.

This changes how the field should approach capability forecasting:

- **Before this work**: Researchers could fit power-law curves to small-model performance and predict large-model capabilities with reasonable confidence.
- **After this work**: Certain capabilities must be treated as **unknown until empirically tested** at the target scale. The phase transition pattern (near-random to above-random) means extrapolation is fundamentally invalid for emergent tasks (Section 2).

This shift is analogous to physics: knowing how water behaves at 50°C does not let you predict it will boil at 100°C without understanding phase transitions. Similarly, knowing a 10B model cannot do 3-digit arithmetic does not tell you a 100B model suddenly can (Figure 2A).

**General-Purpose Models as the New Paradigm**

Section 5.5 describes a **sociological shift** in NLP from task-specific models to general-purpose models. This shift is enabled by emergent few-shot capabilities:

> The ability for general-purpose models to perform unseen tasks given only a few examples has also led to many new applications of language models outside the NLP research community. (Section 5.5)

Concrete examples include:
- **Robotics**: Translating natural language instructions into robot actions (Ahn et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022)
- **Human-AI Collaboration**: Interactive writing tools like Wordcraft (Coenen et al., 2021) and CoAuthor (Lee et al., 2022b)
- **Multimodal Reasoning**: Visual question answering with Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022)
- **Developer Tools**: GitHub CoPilot deploying language models directly as services (Section 5.5)

This represents a fundamental change in how models are built and deployed. Instead of training separate models for separate tasks, organizations now train single large models that can handle diverse tasks via prompting. The economic implication is significant: the fixed cost of training a large model is amortized across many use cases, but the variable cost of inference and the risk of unexpected capabilities increase.

**Safety and Risk Assessment Must Evolve**

Section 5.4 explicitly connects emergent abilities to **emergent risks**:

> Importantly, similar to how emergent abilities have been observed in the few-shot prompting setting without explicitly being included in pre-training, risks could also emerge. (Section 5.4)

This creates a critical asymmetry: capabilities that enable useful applications may emerge alongside capabilities that enable harm. The paper documents several risk patterns:

| Risk Type | Scaling Relationship | Evidence |
|-----------|---------------------|----------|
| **Truthfulness** | Increases with scale (negative) | GPT-3 more likely to mimic human falsehoods as it grows (Lin et al., 2021) |
| **Toxicity** | Increases with scale, but mitigatable | Larger models produce more toxic responses, but "helpful, harmless, honest" prompting helps (Askell et al., 2021) |
| **Memorization** | Increases with scale | Larger models more likely to memorize training data (Carlini et al., 2021; 2022) |
| **Bias** | Mixed | WinoGender improves with scale, but BBQ bias increases for ambiguous contexts (Section 5.4) |

The implication for safety research is that **risk assessment cannot rely solely on small-model testing**. A model that appears safe at 10B parameters may develop dangerous capabilities at 100B parameters. This necessitates:
- **Scale-aware safety evaluations**: Testing safety properties across multiple model sizes, not just the deployment size
- **Proactive risk discovery**: Using techniques like red-teaming with language models (Perez et al., 2022) to discover risks before deployment
- **Governance frameworks**: Developing processes for deciding which capabilities are safe to release at which scales (Bender et al., 2021; Ganguli et al., 2022)

### 7.2 Research Directions Enabled by This Work

Section 5.6 outlines several concrete research directions. I expand on each with specific actionable questions and methodological considerations.

**Understanding Emergence Mechanisms**

The paper's most significant open question is **why** emergence occurs. Section 5.1 states: "there are currently few compelling explanations for why such abilities emerge in the way they do." Future research could investigate:

- **Internal Representation Analysis**: Do emergent capabilities correspond to specific structural changes in attention patterns or hidden representations? Recent work on "induction heads" (Olsson et al., 2022) suggests certain circuit-level mechanisms may enable in-context learning—similar analysis could identify emergence-triggering circuits.
- **Compositional Task Synthesis**: Section 5.6 suggests creating "a synthetic task that requires multiple compositional sub-tasks and evaluate how each of those sub-tasks improve with scale and unlock emergence when combined." This would allow controlled study of emergence without the cost of training massive models.
- **Loss-to-Capability Mapping**: Appendix A.1 shows cross-entropy loss improves smoothly while downstream metrics remain flat. Research could investigate what internal changes occur at the threshold where loss improvements finally translate to task success.

**Lowering Emergence Thresholds**

Section 5.2 demonstrates that emergence thresholds are **malleable**, not fixed. PaLM 62B achieves above-random performance on 14 BIG-Bench tasks where GPT-3 175B and LaMDA 137B fail (Appendix F). This opens several research avenues:

- **Data Quality Optimization**: PaLM's advantage may come from "more multilingual and code data than LaMDA" (Section 5.2). Systematic ablation of data composition could identify which data types drive which capabilities.
- **Architecture Innovations**: Section 5.6 mentions sparse mixture-of-experts architectures (Fedus et al., 2021; Zoph et al., 2022) that "scale up the number of parameters in a model while maintaining constant computational costs." Research could test whether MoE models show different emergence patterns than dense models.
- **Training Objective Modifications**: Tay et al. (2022c) showed "a computationally-efficient continued pre-training stage on a mixture-of-denoisers objective enabled emergent performance on several BIG-Bench tasks" (Section 5.2). Further work could identify which objectives enable which capabilities at lower scales.

**Frontier Task Investigation**

Appendix E.4 lists 40+ BIG-Bench tasks where **no model performs better than random** regardless of scale. These represent the current frontier of what language models cannot do:

- **Complex Reasoning**: Checkmate in one, chinese remainder theorem, mathematical induction, multistep arithmetic
- **Program Understanding**: Program synthesis, python programming challenge, semantic parsing spider
- **Visual Tasks**: Cifar10 classification, mnist ascii, visual reasoning (8/13 tasks flat)
- **Self-Reference**: Self awareness, authorship verification

Research directions include:
- **Why don't these emerge?** Are they fundamentally impossible for autoregressive language models, or do they require even larger scales?
- **Alternative Approaches**: Could retrieval augmentation (Guu et al., 2020; Borgeaud et al., 2021) or tool use enable these capabilities without requiring scale increases?
- **Multilingual Emergence**: Section 5.6 notes "results on multilingual BIG-Bench tasks indicate that both model scale and training data play a role in emergence" (referencing Figure 2D on Persian QA). Research could investigate whether multilingual capabilities have different emergence thresholds than monolingual ones.

**Predictive Frameworks for Emergence**

The paper explicitly states it "does not explain why downstream metrics are emergent or enable us to predict the scale at which emergence occurs" (Section 5.1). Building predictive frameworks is a critical open direction:

- **Early Warning Signals**: Are there indicators in smaller models (e.g., specific loss patterns, representation changes) that predict which capabilities will emerge at larger scales?
- **Task Feature Analysis**: Appendix A.3 finds that "analogical reasoning, word sense disambiguation, truthfulness, social reasoning, and emotional understanding" have the highest emergence rates (Figure 8). Research could identify what features these tasks share that make them prone to emergence.
- **Perplexity-Based Prediction**: Section 5.3 explores WikiText103 perplexity as an alternative to FLOPs for viewing emergence (Figure 4). Future work could investigate whether perplexity on specific domains predicts emergence on related tasks.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Deployment Guidance

**When to Use Large Models vs. Small Models**

The emergence framework provides concrete guidance for model selection:

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| **Task known to emerge** (e.g., multi-step reasoning, MMLU) | Use large models ($\geq 10^{23}$ FLOPs) | Small models will perform at random regardless of fine-tuning (Figure 2G, Figure 3A) |
| **Task known to scale smoothly** (e.g., TriviaQA, PiQA) | Small models may suffice | Performance improves predictably; large models may not be cost-effective (Figure 13, Appendix D) |
| **Task unknown** | Test across multiple scales | Cannot assume smooth scaling; emergence may occur at unexpected thresholds |
| **Safety-critical deployment** | Test at deployment scale | Risks may emerge alongside capabilities; small-model safety testing is insufficient (Section 5.4) |

**Prompting Strategy Selection**

Section 4 reveals that **prompting techniques themselves emerge** at certain scales. This has direct implications for prompting strategy:

- **Chain-of-Thought**: Only beneficial for models $\geq 10^{23}$ FLOPs ($\sim 100$B parameters) (Figure 3A). Using chain-of-thought on smaller models wastes inference compute without improving accuracy.
- **Instruction Tuning**: Actively harms models $\leq 7 \cdot 10^{21}$ FLOPs (8B parameters), only helps at $\geq 10^{23}$ FLOPs (Figure 3B). Do not instruction-tune small decoder-only models.
- **Scratchpad Finetuning**: Helps for multi-step computation at $\geq 9 \cdot 10^{19}$ FLOPs (40M parameters) (Figure 3C). This technique works at much smaller scales than chain-of-thought.
- **Calibration via P(True)**: Only superior at $\geq 3 \cdot 10^{23}$ FLOPs (52B parameters) (Figure 3D). Small models should use standard calibration methods.

**Risk Mitigation Strategies**

Section 5.4 and Section 5.6 suggest several practical approaches for managing emergent risks:

- **Data Filtering**: Deduplication methods can "simultaneously reduce memorization while improving performance" (Kandpal et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022a) (Section 5.4).
- **Prompt-Based Mitigation**: Toxicity can be reduced by giving models prompts with examples of being "helpful, harmless, and honest" (Askell et al., 2021) (Section 5.4).
- **Capability Monitoring**: Organizations should monitor for emergence of capabilities that were not explicitly trained, especially in deployment settings where models interact with users.
- **Gradual Scale Deployment**: Rather than deploying the largest available model, consider deploying at multiple scales to observe where capabilities and risks emerge.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

**Access Constraints and Verification**

A critical practical limitation is that **most models analyzed in this paper are not publicly available**. Table 2 (Appendix C) shows models ranging from GPT-3 (125M to 175B) to PaLM (8B to 540B), all of which have restricted access. This creates challenges:

- **Independent Verification**: Researchers cannot directly verify the scaling curves presented in Figures 2–4.
- **Extension Research**: Testing new hypotheses about emergence requires training new large models, which is computationally prohibitive for most groups.
- **Democratization**: Section 5.2 notes that "lowering the scale threshold for emergent abilities will become more important for making research on such abilities available to the community more broadly."

**Practical Workarounds for Researchers**

Given access constraints, researchers can:

1. **Use Open-Source Alternatives**: Models like those from EleutherAI (referenced in Table 1 for "Ask me anything prompting" at 6B parameters) provide more accessible platforms for emergence research.
2. **Focus on Smaller-Scale Emergence**: Some capabilities emerge at relatively small scales (e.g., scratchpad at 40M parameters, Figure 3C). These are more accessible for academic research.
3. **Synthetic Task Creation**: As suggested in Section 5.6, creating synthetic tasks that require compositional reasoning could enable emergence study without massive model training.
4. **Collaborate with Industry**: The paper's author list spans Google Research, Stanford, DeepMind, and UNC Chapel Hill, reflecting the industry-academic collaborations needed for this research.

**Integration Into Existing Workflows**

For practitioners integrating language models into applications:

- **Benchmark at Deployment Scale**: Do not assume capabilities observed at one scale transfer to another. Test your specific tasks at the scale you plan to deploy.
- **Monitor for Capability Drift**: As you scale models (e.g., from 7B to 70B parameters), re-evaluate all capabilities and risks—do not assume only improvement.
- **Document Emergence Observations**: Contribute to community knowledge by documenting which capabilities emerge at which scales for your use cases. This helps build the empirical base for future predictive frameworks.
- **Plan for Unexpected Capabilities**: Build monitoring and kill-switches into deployments to handle capabilities that emerge unexpectedly (Hendrycks et al., 2021b; Ganguli et al., 2022).

### 7.5 Summary: The Emergence Research Agenda

This paper transforms emergence from anecdotal observation into a structured research agenda. The key takeaways for the field are:

| Dimension | Before This Work | After This Work |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Capability Prediction** | Extrapolate from small models | Test empirically at target scale |
| **Safety Assessment** | Test safety at small scale | Test safety across scales; risks may emerge |
| **Model Selection** | Choose based on smooth scaling | Account for phase transitions in capabilities |
| **Research Focus** | Improve scaling laws | Understand emergence mechanisms |
| **Deployment Strategy** | Scale up confidently | Monitor for unexpected capabilities and risks |

The fundamental insight is that **scale is not just a quantitative change—it enables qualitative changes in behavior** (Section 1, citing Anderson, 1972). This recognition should permeate how the field approaches language model development, evaluation, and deployment. Future research must move from documenting emergence to explaining it, predicting it, and ultimately controlling it—both to unlock beneficial capabilities and to mitigate emergent risks.
