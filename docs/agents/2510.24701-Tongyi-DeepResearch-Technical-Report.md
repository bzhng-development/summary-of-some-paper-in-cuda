## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces Tongyi DeepResearch, a 30.5 billion parameter agentic model (3.3 billion activated) that solves the scarcity of open-source systems capable of autonomous long-horizon information seeking by unifying agentic mid-training and post-training within a fully automated data synthesis pipeline. It achieves state-of-the-art performance on benchmarks such as Humanity's Last Exam (32.9) and BrowseComp (43.4), outperforming proprietary systems like OpenAI-o3 and DeepSeek-V3.1. This work matters because it establishes a reproducible framework for deep research agents, democratizing access to autonomous AI researchers that can complete complex tasks in minutes that would otherwise require hours of human effort.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of Tongyi DeepResearch, we must first contextualize the shift from standard large language models (LLMs) to **agentic systems**. Traditional LLMs are primarily designed for static tasks: given a prompt, generate a response based on internal knowledge. In contrast, **Deep Research** agents represent a new paradigm where the model autonomously conducts multi-step reasoning and information seeking on the internet for complex tasks (Section 1). These agents can complete research tasks in tens of minutes that would otherwise require several hours of human effort (OpenAI, 2025a; Claude Team, 2025).

However, despite the rapid emergence of these capabilities in proprietary systems, the research community faces three critical barriers that this paper aims to dismantle: the **closed-source barrier**, the **data scarcity bottleneck**, and the **environmental instability challenge**.

### The Closed-Source Barrier and Reproducibility Gap
As of 2025, most state-of-the-art deep research systems remain closed-source. Industry leaders such as OpenAI, Gemini, and Claude have released capable research agents, but their intermediate research processes are inaccessible (Section 1). This opacity creates a significant gap in scientific understanding:
*   **Inaccessible Methodologies:** Researchers cannot inspect how proprietary agents plan, search, or synthesize knowledge.
*   **Lack of Baselines:** Without open weights and frameworks, it is difficult to establish reproducible baselines for agentic behavior.
*   **Community Stagnation:** While there have been preliminary explorations (Wu et al., 2025a; Li et al., 2025c), there is a lack of systematic methodology and publicly available models that can be fully shared (Section 1).

Tongyi DeepResearch positions itself as the opening of the "era of open-source AI researchers," aiming to democratize access to autonomous research capabilities that were previously confined to proprietary labs.

### The Data Scarcity Bottleneck
Training an agent to perform deep research is fundamentally different from training a chatbot. Standard LLM training relies on vast corpora of natural text (pre-training) and human-annotated instructions (post-training). However, **agentic data**—trajectories showing how an agent plans, uses tools, and reasons over time—is inherently scarce.

> "Unlike pre-training data, which is naturally abundant, and conventional LLM post-training data, which is relatively easy to annotate, agentic data is inherently scarce." (Section 2)

The specific challenges include:
*   **Annotation Cost:** Manually annotating research-level problems and agentic trajectories is extremely time-consuming and costly (Wei et al., 2025).
*   **Natural Text Limitations:** Research-level problems are difficult to obtain through natural texts from the web. Standard pre-training data lacks the structure of multi-step decision-making.
*   **Verification Difficulty:** Validating whether an agent's trajectory is correct is harder than validating a single answer, especially when the solution path is complex.

This scarcity motivates the paper's heavy reliance on **synthetic data**. The authors argue that synthetic data is not just a substitute for human annotation but a superior scaling engine because it allows for targeted meta-capability enhancement (e.g., specifically generating data to improve planning or memory management) and easier verification (Section 2).

### The Environmental Instability Challenge
Agent training requires interaction with an environment to learn tool use and feedback loops. Relying solely on the real-world internet presents fundamental engineering and scientific challenges (Section 2):
1.  **Non-Stationarity:** The dynamic nature of the web causes continuous distribution shift in training data, undermining learning stability.
2.  **Interaction Cost:** The tangible expense of each API call (e.g., search queries) makes large-scale exploration economically prohibitive.
3.  **Reliability:** External APIs suffer from high latency, outright failures, and inconsistent returns, which can corrupt training trajectories by introducing noise unrelated to the agent's policy.

Prior approaches often struggle to balance **fidelity** (how real the environment is) with **stability** (how reproducible the training is). Tongyi DeepResearch addresses this by reframing environments not as passive external realities but as systems actively designed to couple with the training process, utilizing a hierarchy of **Prior World**, **Simulated**, and **Real-world** environments (Section 2).

### Limitations of Prior Training Paradigms
A core theoretical motivation for this work lies in the insufficiency of standard training pipelines for agentic tasks. Most existing work applies only a **post-training** phase to general foundation models for Deep Research agents (Section 2). This approach suffers from **optimization conflicts**:
*   **Lack of Inductive Bias:** General foundation models are typically pretrained on plain text and lack **agentic inductive bias**—the inherent structural preference for tool use and multi-step reasoning.
*   **Simultaneous Learning Burden:** When using general models, the system must learn agentic capabilities and alignment simultaneously during post-training, which can result in sub-optimal outcomes.

> "Agentic post-training on these general foundation models can result in sub-optimal outcomes and inherent optimization conflicts." (Section 2)

To resolve this, the paper introduces **agentic mid-training** (specifically Agentic Continual Pre-training, or Agentic CPT). This stage serves as a bridge, endowing the pre-trained base model with substantial agentic prior knowledge before the finer alignment of post-training begins. This design choice is motivated by the need to separate the acquisition of basic interaction skills from the refinement of advanced autonomous research behaviors.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work
Tongyi DeepResearch distinguishes itself from prior art through three strategic dimensions:

1.  **End-to-End Agentic Training:** Unlike works that merely fine-tune existing models, this framework unifies **agentic mid-training** and **agentic post-training**. This allows the model to gradually develop from basic interaction skills to advanced autonomous research behaviors (Section 1).
2.  **Synthetic Data Centric Scaling:** While other works rely on human annotation or limited web scraping, this paper designs a fully automated data synthesis pipeline. This pipeline generates diverse agent trajectories without human intervention, enabling the construction of super-human-level datasets with stable distributions (Section 1).
3.  **Environment Co-Design:** Rather than treating the environment as a fixed constraint, the authors construct stage-specific, customized environments. This allows for stable interactions during data synthesis and training, mitigating the risks of real-world non-stationarity (Section 1).

By addressing these specific gaps—openness, data scalability, and environmental stability—Tongyi DeepResearch aims to establish a foundation for open, reproducible research into autonomous AI agents, moving beyond domain-specific tools toward general-purpose agents capable of reasoning and acting with minimal human supervision (Section 5.3).

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems and methodology paper that presents an end-to-end training framework for autonomous deep research agents, with the core idea being that agentic capabilities require a dedicated mid-training phase bridging pre-training and post-training, supported by fully automated synthetic data generation and carefully designed environmental interactions.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

Tongyi DeepResearch is an AI system that autonomously conducts multi-step research tasks on the internet by planning, searching, reasoning, and synthesizing information across extended sequences of actions without human intervention. It solves the problem that standard large language models lack inherent capabilities for long-horizon information seeking by introducing a three-stage training pipeline (mid-training plus post-training) that progressively builds agentic behaviors through synthetic data and controlled environmental interactions.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The system consists of four major components connected in a sequential training pipeline. First, the **Base Model** (Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base) provides the foundational language understanding with 30.5 billion total parameters but only 3.3 billion activated per token through a Mixture-of-Experts architecture. Second, the **Agentic Mid-training Module** performs two stages of Agentic Continual Pre-training (Agentic CPT), first at 32K context length then at 128K context length, injecting agentic inductive bias into the base model using synthetic agent behavior data. Third, the **Agentic Post-training Module** executes Supervised Fine-tuning (SFT) for cold start followed by Agentic Reinforcement Learning (RL) to refine planning and search capabilities. Fourth, the **Environment Layer** provides three types of interaction contexts (Prior World, Simulated, and Real-world environments) that supply observations and rewards during training. Data flows from synthetic data generators into mid-training, then refined trajectories flow into post-training, with the environment layer providing feedback signals throughout the RL phase.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the **formal problem formulation** (Section 3.4.1) because understanding how the agent represents thoughts, actions, and observations is prerequisite to understanding how it learns.
- We then explain the **training pipeline stages** (Section 3.4.2) in chronological order (mid-training before post-training) since each stage builds upon the capabilities established by the previous stage.
- We detail the **synthetic data generation mechanisms** (Section 3.4.3) next because data quality determines what the model can learn at each training stage.
- We describe the **environment architecture** (Section 3.4.4) because environmental stability is critical for reliable reinforcement learning signals.
- We explain the **reinforcement learning algorithm** (Section 3.4.5) with its mathematical formulation since this is where the agent optimizes its policy based on rewards.
- We conclude with **inference-time enhancements** (Section 3.4.6) including Heavy Mode and model merging, which improve final performance without additional training.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### 3.4.1 Formal Problem Formulation: How the Agent Represents Research Tasks

The paper formally defines the agent's operation at each timestep $t$ through three fundamental components that together form a complete research trajectory. The **Thought** ($\tau_t$) represents the internal cognitive process of the agent, which includes analyzing the current context, recalling information from memory, planning subsequent steps, and engaging in self-reflection to adjust its strategy. The **Action** ($a_t$) represents an external operation executed by the agent to interact with its environment, where Tongyi DeepResearch is equipped with a versatile set of five tools that define its action space: Search, Visit, Python Interpreter, Google Scholar, and File Parser (Appendix D). In a given trajectory, intermediate actions ($a_t$ where $t < T$) are tool calls, while the final action ($a_T$) constitutes the generation of an in-depth report for the user. The **Observation** ($o_t$) represents the feedback received from the environment after an action is performed, and this new information is used to update the agent's internal state and inform its next thought.

The architecture is fundamentally based on the vanilla ReAct (Reasoning and Acting) framework, which synergizes reasoning and acting by generating both a reasoning trace (Thought) and a subsequent Action in an interleaved manner. This process forms a trajectory $H_T$, which is a sequence of thought-action-observation triplets defined as:

$$H_T = (\tau_0, a_0, o_0, \ldots, \tau_i, ai, o_i, \ldots, \tau_T, a_T)$$

where $a_T$ represents the final answer to the given task. At any given step $t \leq T$, the agent's policy $\pi$ generates the current thought $\tau_t$ and action $a_t$ based on the history of all previous interactions $H_{t-1}$:

$$\tau_t, a_t \sim \pi(\cdot|H_{t-1})$$

The authors deliberately chose ReAct over more complex single and multi-agent paradigms, rooted in its simplicity and alignment with fundamental principles informed by "The Bitter Lesson" (Sutton, 2019), which posits that general methods leveraging scalable computation ultimately outperform approaches that rely on complex, human-engineered knowledge and intricate designs.

A critical innovation addresses the fundamental constraint that the execution of long-horizon tasks is limited by the finite length of the agent's context window. To mitigate the risk of context overflow and ensure task focus, the paper proposes the **context management paradigm**, which employs a dynamic context management mechanism based on Markovian state reconstruction. Within this framework, the agent is not conditioned on the complete history; instead, at each step $t$, it is conditioned on a strategically reconstructed workspace containing only essential elements: the question $q$, an evolving report $S_t$ serving as compressed memory, and the immediate context from the last interaction ($a_t$ and $o_t$). This Markovian structure enables the agent to maintain consistent reasoning capacity across arbitrary exploration depths while naturally circumventing degradation. For every step $0 < t < T$, this core update process can be formalized as:

$$S_t, \tau_{t+1}, a_{t+1} \sim \pi(\cdot|S_{t-1}, a_t, o_t)$$

This context management paradigm not only prevents context suffocation but also enforces structured reasoning by requiring the agent to explicitly synthesize and prioritize information at each step, which naturally aligns with human research patterns where periodic synthesis and reflection are essential for maintaining coherent long-term investigation.

#### 3.4.2 Training Pipeline: Three-Stage Progressive Capability Development

The system is initialized from the pretrained base model Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base, and Tongyi DeepResearch is developed through an end-to-end training framework that integrates agentic mid-training and post-training, enabling scalable reasoning and information seeking across complex research tasks (Section 3.2). The training pipeline consists of three sequential phases as illustrated in Figure 2: Pre-training (inherited from the base model), Mid-training (Agentic CPT in two stages), and Post-training (Agentic SFT followed by Agentic RL).

**Agentic Mid-training (Section 3.3)** employs a two-stage Agentic Continual Pre-training (Agentic CPT) as its core mid-training phase, which functions as a critical bridge connecting pre-trained models and agentic post-training. Its primary objective is to provide a base model endowed with a strong inductive bias for agentic behavior, while simultaneously preserving broad linguistic competence. To achieve this, the optimization process is driven by the standard Next-Token Prediction loss function, which is the same objective used during initial pre-training but applied to agentic trajectory data rather than plain text. The design of this phase is strategically optimized for both efficiency and progressive capability scaling. The first stage initiates with a 32K context length, before expanding to 128K in the second stage. This expanded context window is specifically leveraged in the second stage, where the system introduces a substantial corpus of long-sequence (64K-128K) agentic behavior data, which is critical for enhancing the model's capacity for coherent, long-horizon reasoning and action. Throughout both stages, a small proportion of general pre-training data is interleaved, ensuring the model acquires specialized agentic competence without sacrificing its foundational generalization capabilities.

The motivation for introducing mid-training stems from the observation that general foundation models usually lack agentic inductive bias because they are typically pretrained on plain text crawled from the internet and then post-trained on instruction-following data that lacks research-level questions and agentic behaviors (Section 2). When agentic post-training is applied directly to these general foundation models, the model must learn agentic capabilities and alignment simultaneously during the post-training phase, which can result in sub-optimal outcomes and inherent optimization conflicts. Mid-training endows the pre-trained base model with substantial agentic prior knowledge, thereby bridging the gap between pretraining and agentic post-training, and provides a powerful agentic foundation model to support effective agentic post-training.

**Agentic Post-training (Section 3.4)** comprises three stages: data synthesis, supervised fine-tuning for cold start, and agentic reinforcement learning. The initial phase is a supervised fine-tuning (SFT) stage, designed to equip the base model with a robust initial policy prior to reinforcement learning. Starting from synthesized high-quality QA data, the system obtains training trajectories that cover the complete thought process and tool responses generated by high-performing open-source models, which are then subjected to a rigorous rejection sampling protocol to guarantee that only high-quality trajectories exhibiting diverse problem-solving patterns are retained.

The cold stage training leverages data from two different formulations to enhance model robustness and generalization through a Mixed Training Paradigm. For the **ReAct Mode**, the training samples take the historical state $H_{t-1}$ as input, and output the corresponding thought $\tau_i$ and tool call $a_i$ for the current step. For the **Context Management Mode**, the training samples take as input the previous step's trajectory summary $S_{t-1}$, tool call $a_{i-1}$, and tool response $o_{i-1}$, and output the current step's trajectory summary, thought $\tau_i$, and tool call $a_i$. The Context Management Mode data particularly strengthens the agent's capabilities in state analysis and strategic decision-making, as it requires the model to synthesize complex observations into coherent summaries while maintaining task focus across extended trajectories. The system adopts a two-stage training strategy based on context length: in the first stage, the context length is set to 40K, and the training data consist of ReAct Mode samples with context lengths shorter than 40K, along with all Context Management Mode samples (as they are all within 40K); in the second stage, the context length is extended to 128K, and the training data include ReAct Mode samples with context lengths between 40K and 128K, as well as a small portion of 40K data for stability.

#### 3.4.3 Synthetic Data Generation: Fully Automated Pipeline Without Human Annotation

The paper designs a fully automated, highly scalable data synthesis pipeline that eliminates human annotation while generating diverse, high-quality agent trajectories, with stage-specific data synthesis strategies tailored to the objectives of each training phase (Section 1). Synthetic data contains several advantages over human annotations: synthesizing research-level questions is easy to scale using LLMs compared to manually annotating; the pattern and diversity are easy to generalize because LLMs understand the structure of hard problems; synthesized data enables targeted meta-capability enhancement by decomposing complex agent tasks into fundamental meta-capabilities (e.g., planning, information synthesis, memory management); synthesized data can be verified easily, which is much easier than finding the solution to the question; and synthesized data can provide data flywheels in training stages where the trained agentic model can generate synthesized data with stronger reasoning and planning patterns after one round of the agentic training pipeline.

**For Agentic Mid-training (Section 3.3.2)**, the system synthesizes data across the complete lifecycle of agent workflows as shown in Figure 3. A typical agent workflow begins with a problem, iteratively cycles through reflection and action, and ultimately converges on a final solution. To comprehensively capture this process, the system synthesizes data for four critical steps that constitute the agent's operational cycle:

1. **Question Synthesis**: Grounded in continuously updated open-world knowledge, the system constructs an entity-anchored open-world memory that consolidates diverse real-world knowledge sources, such as web-crawled data and agent interaction trajectories, into structured representations of entities and their associated knowledge. Building upon this foundation, the system samples entities along with their related knowledge to generate diverse questions that embed specific behavioral pattern requirements, such as multi-hop reasoning questions and numerical computation questions.

2. **Planning Action**: Planning refers to problem decomposition and first-step action prediction, and planning accuracy is highly correlated with whether an agent can successfully complete a task. The system employs open-source models to analyze, decompose, and predict initial actions for the synthesized questions, and leverages the entities and associated knowledge used in question construction as the basis for rejection sampling, thereby ensuring high-quality planning outputs.

3. **Reasoning Action**: Logical reasoning and knowledge integration over heterogeneous data is foundational for agents solving complex tasks. When external tools return massive unstructured responses, whether models can distill critical knowledge from noise and construct coherent reasoning paths directly determines task outcomes. Given a question and its dependent knowledge, the system guides large models through a two-stage process to generate complete reasoning chains, with a dual filtering mechanism based on reasoning length and answer consistency to ensure quality.

4. **Decision-Making Action**: Each step of an agent's thinking and action is essentially an implicit decision-making process, where each decision point encompasses multiple potential reasoning and action paths, from which the agent must select the most promising solution. To capture this critical mechanism, the system explicitly models this decision-making process by first exploring the feasible action space at each step based on existing demonstration trajectories, then reconstructing the original trajectories into multi-step decision sequences while preserving the original decision choices.

Additionally, the system performs **General Function-calling Data Synthesis via Environment Scaling** to enhance the model's general agentic capability. The breadth of function-calling competence is closely tied to the diversity of environments in which agents are trained, so the system designs a scalable framework that automatically constructs heterogeneous environments that are fully simulated, with each environment instantiated as a read–write database, systematically broadening the space of function-calling scenarios.

**For Agentic Post-training (Section 3.4.1)**, the system develops an end-to-end solution for synthetic data generation to generate complex, high-uncertainty and super-human level question and answer pairs, as shown in Figure 4. This fully automated process requires no human intervention to construct super-human quality datasets, designed to push the boundaries of agent performance. The process begins by constructing a highly interconnected knowledge graph via random walks, leveraging web search to acquire relevant knowledge, and isomorphic tables from real-world websites, ensuring a realistic information structure. The system then samples subgraphs and subtables to generate initial questions and answers. The pivotal step involves strategically increasing the uncertainty within the question to enhance its difficulty, which is grounded in a complete theoretical framework where the system formally models QA difficulty as a series of controllable "atomic operations" (e.g., merging entities with similar attributes) on entity relationships, allowing systematic increases in complexity.

To further reduce inconsistencies between the organized information structure and the reasoning structure of QA, and enable more controllable difficulty and structure scaling of reasoning, the system proposed a formal modeling of the information-seeking problem based on set theory. With this formalization, the system develops agents that expand the problem in a controlled manner, and minimize reasoning shortcuts and structural redundancy, leading to further improved QA quality. Moreover, this formal modeling also allows for efficient verification of QA correctness, effectively addressing the challenge of validating synthetic information-seeking data for post-training.

The system also develops an automated data engine to scale the generation of PhD-level research questions. Starting from a multi-disciplinary knowledge base, it creates seed QA pairs requiring multi-source reasoning. These seeds undergo iterative complexity upgrades, where a question-crafting agent, equipped with the corresponding tool, progressively expands scope and abstraction. Each iteration refines and compounds prior outputs, enabling a systematic and controllable escalation of task difficulty. Statistical analysis of the SFT dataset reveals that over 20% of the samples exceed 32K tokens and involve more than 10 tool invocations, demonstrating the high complexity and richness of the synthetic data (Section 4.4).

#### 3.4.4 Environment Architecture: Three-Tier System for Stable Training

Environmental interaction plays a crucial role in agent intelligence emergence, but relying solely on real-world environments for the whole agent training stage faces fundamental challenges including non-stationarity (the dynamic nature of environments causes continuous distribution shift in training data) and interaction cost (the tangible expense of each API call makes large-scale exploration economically prohibitive) (Section 2). In Tongyi DeepResearch, the authors propose a fundamental reframing: environments should not be passively viewed as external reality, but actively designed as systems deeply coupled with the training process. Specifically, the system models environments into three forms, each striking a distinct balance between stability, fidelity, and cost:

1. **Prior World Environment**: This environment provides task elements, tools, and state definitions, allowing agents to autonomously mine interaction trajectories based on pretrained knowledge without receiving actual environmental responses. It offers perfect stability, zero interaction cost, and unlimited scalability, but lacks real-world feedback signals.

2. **Simulated Environment**: This environment constructs controlled, reproducible replicas of real-world interactions locally. It provides stability, rapid response, and low cost, enabling fast iteration and causal attribution analysis. However, its data coverage is inherently limited, exhibiting a notable sim-to-real gap. The system builds an offline environment based on the 2024 Wikipedia database and develops a suite of local RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) tools to simulate the web environment, then reuses the data synthesis pipeline to create a high-quality, structurally complex QA specifically for this offline environment (Section 3.4.3).

3. **Real-world Environment**: This environment delivers the most authentic data distribution and feedback signals, serving as the ultimate proving ground for agent capabilities. Its advantage lies in absolute distributional fidelity; the cost is expensive interactions, significant non-stationarity, and exploration risks.

Building on this environmental insight, the system adopts adaptive strategies for synthetic data generation and training. During agentic mid-training, the system primarily leverages the Prior World Environment and Simulated Environment to generate large-scale synthetic data at minimal cost, ensuring efficient agentic ability bootstrapping. During agentic post-training, the system validates training strategies and algorithmic techniques in the simulated environment, then deploys verified optimal policies to the real environment for final training.

For the Real-world Environment, the agent's toolkit is a complex system that integrates five specialized tools: Search, Visit, Python Interpreter, Google Scholar, and File Parser (Section 3.4.3, Appendix D). The end-to-end reliability of this system is paramount because the inherent volatility of external APIs, encompassing high latency, outright failures, and inconsistent returns, threatens to corrupt training trajectories. To ensure reliable tool use during agent training and evaluation, the system developed a unified sandbox built around a central scheduling and management layer that orchestrates every tool call. For each tool, the system implements robust concurrency controls and fault-tolerance mechanisms, such as proactive QPS (Queries Per Second) rate constraints, result caching, automatic timeout-and-retry protocols, graceful service degradation for non-critical failures, and seamless failover to backup data sources (e.g., a backup search API). This design abstracts the tool invocation into a deterministic and stable interface for the agent and thereby insulates the training loop from real-world stochasticity while also significantly reducing operational costs.

#### 3.4.5 Reinforcement Learning Algorithm: On-Policy Training with Automatic Data Curation

To advance the model's capabilities toward more robust and reliable planning and searching in a complex web environment, the system applies an agentic RL framework, which is illustrated in Figure 5 (Section 3.4.3). In this framework, the model generates a complete task attempt (a "rollout") and receives a reward if its final answer matches the ground truth, which is called RLVR (Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards). Throughout this agentic RL procedure, the model continuously interacts with the environment (simulated or real-world), iteratively refining its policy with each iteration, and, in turn, using that improved policy to curate a new, higher-quality set of training data.

The RL algorithm is a tailored adaptation of GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization), with the training objective defined as:

$$J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(q,y)\sim D, \{H_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{old}}(\cdot|\text{context})} \left[ \frac{1}{\sum_{i=1}^G |H_i|} \sum_{i=1}^G \sum_{j=1}^{|H_i|} \min\left(r_{i,j}(\theta) \hat{A}_{i,j}, \text{clip}\left(r_{i,j}(\theta), 1-\epsilon_{low}, 1+\epsilon_{high}\right) \hat{A}_{i,j}\right) \right]$$

where $(q, y)$ is the question-answer pair, $r_{i,j}(\theta)$ is the importance sampling ratio (which remains 1.0 for strictly on-policy training), and $\hat{A}_{i,j}$ is an estimator of the advantage at token $j$. The importance sampling ratio and advantage estimator are defined as:

$$r_{i,j}(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(H_{i,j} | \text{context})}{\pi_{\theta_{old}}(H_{i,j} | \text{context})}, \quad \hat{A}_{i,j} = R_i - \text{mean}(\{R_i\}_{i=1}^G)$$

The system employs a strict on-policy regimen, where trajectories are consistently sampled using the most up-to-date policy, ensuring that the learning signal is always relevant to the model's current capabilities. The reward is a pure 0 or 1 signal of answer correctness, and the system does not include a format reward (e.g., 0.1 for format correctness) because the preceding cold start stage ensures the model is already familiar with the required output format. Following DAPO, the system applies the token-level policy gradient loss in the training objective and clip-higher strategy to encourage more exploration. To further reduce variance in the advantage estimation, the system adopts a leave-one-out strategy.

The system observed in preliminary experiments that directly optimizing on an unfiltered set of negative rollouts significantly degrades training stability and can lead to policy collapse after extended training. To mitigate this, the system selectively excludes certain negative samples from the loss calculation, for instance, those that do not yield a final answer because they exceed a length limit. The primary motivation for these modifications is not algorithmic novelty but the pragmatic pursuit of a more efficient and stable training paradigm.

A critical insight from the experiments is that the success of agentic RL depends more on the quality of the data and the stability of the training environment than on the specific algorithm being used, so the system concentrates efforts on designing a stable environment and curating high-quality data, making only a few essential modifications to the algorithm itself, mainly for the purpose of stabilizing the training process.

The system implements **Automatic Data Curation** to optimize data in real time, guided by training dynamics to generalize to out-of-distribution scenarios through self-exploration. This optimization is achieved through a fully automated data filtering pipeline that dynamically adjusts the training set based on the improved policy model. Specifically, the process begins with a large dataset $D$. The system uses the initial SFT model as a baseline policy to sample multiple solution attempts, or rollouts, for each problem. The system then creates an initial training set $D'$ by filtering out problems where the model either always fails or always succeeds, as these will offer no learning signal for RL training, leaving a focused subset of problems of moderate difficulty. During RL training, the system continuously monitors the problems in $D'$ by their latest rollouts to see if they have become too easy for the improved policy model. In parallel, a separate process uses intermediate checkpoints of the policy model to sample from the entire original dataset $D$. This background process identifies and collects a backup pool of new problems that have become moderately difficult for the now-stronger model. When the training reaches a certain step count or the reward plateaus, the system refreshes the active training set $D'$ by removing the mastered problems and incorporating new, challenging ones from the backup pool. The entire data filtering and refreshment pipeline runs independently, never interrupting the main RL training loop, which allows the system to automatically evolve both the policy model and its training data, ensuring consistently high training efficiency and stability.

To overcome the bottleneck that the iterative nature of agentic rollouts creates (which requires numerous interactions with the environment and slows down the entire RL training process), the system implements a custom, step-level asynchronous RL training loop built on the rLLM framework. The solution utilizes two separate asynchronous online servers, with one for model inference and another for tool invocation. A centralized interaction handler then processes the outputs from both, formatting the feedback into a unified message list. This architecture allows multiple agent instances to interact with the environment in parallel, each completing its rollout independently.

#### 3.4.6 Inference-Time Enhancements: Heavy Mode and Model Merging

The system employs **Model Merging** at the last stage of the pipeline, built on the key insight that when different model variants are derived from the same pre-trained model, their parameters can be effectively combined through averaging or interpolation. Specifically, the process involves selecting several model variants that originate from the same base model but exhibit different capability preferences. The system then creates the final merged model by computing a weighted average of their parameters:

$$\theta_{\text{merged}} = \sum_k \alpha_k \cdot \theta^{(k)}, \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \sum_k \alpha_k = 1, \alpha_k \geq 0$$

where $\theta^{(k)}$ represents the parameters of the $k$-th model variant, and $\alpha_k$ is its corresponding merge weight. Empirically, this interpolation strategy not only preserves the core strengths of each contributing model but also equips the merged model with robust generalization abilities, performing comparably to the best-performing source model in its respective area of strength without incurring additional optimization costs.

To further unlock the potential of deep research agents, the system introduces **Heavy Mode**, which leverages test-time scaling through a Research-Synthesis framework built upon the context management paradigm (Section 4.3). Given that DeepResearch involves multi-round tool calls and intensive reasoning, directly aggregating contexts from multiple trajectories is computationally prohibitive, so Heavy Mode addresses this challenge through strategic parallelization and synthesis.

In the **Parallel Research Phase**, the system deploys $n$ parallel agents, each following the context management paradigm but exploring diverse solution paths through different tool usage and reasoning strategies. Each agent $u$ independently processes the question $q$ and produces a final report and answer:

$$(S_T^u, \text{answer}_u) = \text{Agent}_u(q), \quad u \in [1, n]$$

where $S_T^u$ represents the final report summary from agent $u$ after $T$ iterations, encapsulating the complete reasoning trajectory in compressed form.

In the **Integrative Synthesis Phase**, a synthesis model consolidates all parallel findings to produce the final answer:

$$\text{answer}_{\text{final}} = \text{Synthesis}(\{(S_T^u, \text{answer}_u)\}_{u=1}^n)$$

The key advantage of this approach lies in the compressed nature of context management reports $S_T^u$. Unlike traditional methods that would require aggregating full trajectories (potentially exceeding context limits with just 2-3 agents), this approach enables the synthesis model to assess $n$ diverse solution strategies within a manageable context window. Each report $S_T^u$ preserves the essential reasoning logic and findings while discarding redundant intermediate steps, enabling effective test-time scaling. As shown in Figure 6, Heavy Mode achieves state-of-the-art performance on Humanity's Last Exam (38.3%) and BrowseComp-ZH (58.1%), while remaining highly competitive on BrowseComp (58.3%).

#### 3.4.7 Key Configuration Parameters and Evaluation Settings

For evaluation, the system adopts fixed inference parameters to ensure stability and reproducibility across evaluations: temperature = 0.85, repetition penalty = 1.1, and top-p = 0.95 (Section 4.1). A maximum of 128 tool invocations is allowed per task, and the context length is constrained to 128K tokens. Each benchmark is evaluated three times independently, and the system reports the average performance (Avg@3) as the main metric. For completeness, the system also reports the best Pass@1 (best result over 3 runs) and Pass@3 results in the subsequent analysis.

The analysis of context length impact on agentic RL training (Section 4.4) compares models with 32K, 48K, and 64K context limits, with the important note that the dynamic data curation for all three experimental variants was performed using the same model with a 64K context. The results show that the 64K model achieves the highest reward because the curriculum is populated with problems deemed moderately difficult by the highly capable 64K context model, many of which inherently require long and complex reasoning to solve. The 32K model displays a clear downward trend in response length during training, which provides a key insight: for a model with a limited context, RL training on a curriculum designed for a more capable model can force it to discover more efficient solutions because attempting problems with optimal solutions longer than 32K tokens is likely to yield a zero-reward signal, creating a powerful implicit incentive to discover more concise, potent action sequences that fit within its limit.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the conceptual breakthroughs that distinguish Tongyi DeepResearch from prior agentic systems. We separate fundamental innovations—which reshape how we think about agent training—from incremental improvements that refine existing techniques. Understanding this distinction is critical: the paper's lasting contribution lies not in any single algorithmic tweak, but in a coherent rethinking of the entire agent development pipeline.

### 4.1 Agentic Mid-Training: A New Training Paradigm (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Prior Work Does:** Most existing deep research agents apply post-training directly to general foundation models (Section 2). These models are pretrained on plain text and instruction-tuned for chat, then suddenly expected to perform multi-step tool use and long-horizon reasoning. This creates what the authors call "inherent optimization conflicts"—the model must simultaneously learn agentic capabilities and alignment during post-training.

**What Tongyi DeepResearch Changes:** The paper introduces **agentic mid-training** as a distinct phase between pre-training and post-training. This is not merely an additional fine-tuning step; it represents a fundamental restructuring of the agent training lifecycle. The mid-training phase uses Agentic Continual Pre-training (Agentic CPT) with the standard Next-Token Prediction loss, but applied to synthetic agent trajectories rather than plain text (Section 3.3.1).

**Why This Matters:** This innovation addresses a structural problem that prior work overlooks. By endowing the base model with "agentic inductive bias" before post-training begins, the system separates capability acquisition from alignment refinement. The evidence for this design's effectiveness lies in the performance gap: Tongyi DeepResearch achieves 32.9 on Humanity's Last Exam with only 3.3 billion activated parameters, outperforming much larger models that lack this mid-training bridge (Figure 1, Table 1).

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** because it challenges the standard two-stage training paradigm (pre-train → post-train) that has dominated LLM development. The paper provides empirical justification for a three-stage pipeline specifically for agentic systems, which could influence how future agent models are architected regardless of scale.

### 4.2 Synthetic Data as the Primary Training Engine (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Prior Work Does:** Existing agentic systems typically rely on a combination of human-annotated trajectories, web-scraped demonstrations, or limited synthetic augmentation. Human annotation remains the gold standard for quality but creates a severe scalability bottleneck—research-level problems are "extremely time-consuming and costly" to annotate manually (Section 2).

**What Tongyi DeepResearch Changes:** The paper treats synthetic data not as a supplement but as the **primary training engine** for all stages. The synthesis pipeline is fully automated, requiring zero human annotation while generating "super-human level datasets with stable distributions" (Section 1). Critically, the data synthesis is stage-specific: mid-training receives behavior-focused trajectories (planning, reasoning, decision-making actions), while post-training receives high-uncertainty QA pairs with formalized difficulty controls (Section 3.3.2, 3.4.1).

**Why This Matters:** This approach solves three interconnected problems simultaneously:
1. **Scalability:** Synthetic data can be generated orders of magnitude faster than human annotation.
2. **Targeted Capability Enhancement:** The pipeline decomposes agent tasks into meta-capabilities (planning, information synthesis, memory management) and generates data specifically targeting each skill (Section 2).
3. **Verification:** Synthesized questions come with known answers and reasoning paths, making reward signal generation tractable for RL training.

The statistical evidence is striking: over 20% of SFT samples exceed 32K tokens and involve more than 10 tool invocations, demonstrating complexity levels that would be prohibitively expensive to annotate manually (Section 4.4).

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** because it inverts the conventional data hierarchy. Rather than treating synthetic data as a lower-quality substitute for human annotation, the paper demonstrates it can exceed human-level dataset quality while being infinitely scalable. The "data flywheel" concept—where trained models generate better synthetic data for subsequent training rounds—suggests a path to self-improving agent systems that prior work does not articulate.

### 4.3 Environment Co-Design Philosophy (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Prior Work Does:** Most agent training treats the environment as a fixed external constraint—typically the real-world internet. This creates the "environmental instability challenge": non-stationarity from web changes, high API costs, and unreliable feedback signals that corrupt training trajectories (Section 2).

**What Tongyi DeepResearch Changes:** The paper reframes environments as **actively designed systems** coupled with the training process, not passive external realities. The three-tier hierarchy (Prior World, Simulated, Real-world) is not just a technical implementation detail but a philosophical shift: different training stages require different environment properties, and the system adaptively selects environments based on training objectives (Section 2, 3.4.4).

**Why This Matters:** This design enables a crucial trade-off that prior systems cannot make. During mid-training, the system uses Prior World and Simulated environments for "large-scale synthetic data at minimal cost." During post-training, it validates algorithms in simulation before deploying to real-world environments for final training. The evidence for this approach's effectiveness appears in Figure 10b: the reward curve from the simulated Wiki environment "closely matches the one observed in the real environment," validating simulation as a reliable proxy for algorithm development (Section 4.4).

The unified sandbox architecture further insulates training from real-world stochasticity through "proactive QPS rate constraints, result caching, automatic timeout-and-retry protocols, and seamless failover to backup data sources" (Section 3.4.3). This engineering rigor is often overlooked in academic work but proves essential for stable RL training.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in methodology. While simulated environments exist in prior work (e.g., gym-style benchmarks), the explicit coupling of environment selection to training stage objectives, and the philosophical reframing of environments as design variables rather than constraints, represents a new approach to agent development that could generalize beyond deep research to other agentic domains.

### 4.4 Context Management Enables Test-Time Scaling (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Prior Work Does:** Traditional test-time scaling for reasoning models increases output token count (e.g., chain-of-thought length). For agentic systems, this approach fails because multi-round tool calls create context explosion—aggregating full trajectories from multiple agents would "exceed context limits with just 2-3 agents" (Section 4.3).

**What Tongyi DeepResearch Changes:** The **context management paradigm** (Section 3.1) compresses each agent's trajectory into an evolving report summary $S_t$ through Markovian state reconstruction. This compression enables Heavy Mode to deploy $n$ parallel agents and synthesize their findings without context overflow (Section 4.3).

**Why This Matters:** This unlocks a new dimension for scaling agentic systems. Instead of scaling compute through longer single-agent trajectories, the system scales through parallel exploration with intelligent aggregation. The performance gains are substantial: Heavy Mode improves Humanity's Last Exam from 32.9 to 38.3 and BrowseComp-ZH from 46.7 to 58.1 (Figure 6). These are not marginal improvements but demonstrate that test-time compute can be effectively leveraged for agentic tasks when context is managed strategically.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** because it identifies and solves a previously unrecognized bottleneck in agentic test-time scaling. Prior work on test-time compute focuses on reasoning models without tool use; this paper demonstrates how to scale agent ensembles without context explosion, opening a new research direction for agentic inference.

### 4.5 RL Stability Through Data and Environment Over Algorithm (Methodological Insight)

**What Prior Work Does:** Much of the RL for LLM literature focuses on algorithmic innovations—new policy gradient variants, reward shaping techniques, or exploration strategies. The implicit assumption is that algorithmic advances drive performance gains.

**What Tongyi DeepResearch Changes:** The authors arrive at a counterintuitive conclusion: "the success of agentic RL depends more on the quality of the data and the stability of the training environment than on the specific algorithm being used" (Section 3.4.3). Their RL algorithm is explicitly described as "a tailored adaptation of GRPO" with modifications made "not for algorithmic novelty but the pragmatic pursuit of a more efficient and stable training paradigm."

**Why This Matters:** This insight redirects research effort toward underexplored areas. The paper's RL modifications are minimal: token-level policy gradient loss, clip-higher strategy, leave-one-out advantage estimation, and selective exclusion of negative samples that exceed length limits (Section 3.4.3). Yet the training achieves stable convergence with policy entropy that "exhibits exceptional stability, converging to a consistent value after a brief initial increase" (Figure 8, Section 4.4). This stability comes from data curation and environment design, not algorithmic complexity.

The automatic data curation pipeline exemplifies this: problems are dynamically filtered based on model performance, removing those that are "always failed or always succeeded" to maintain moderate difficulty, with backup pools refreshing the training set when rewards plateau (Section 3.4.3). This curriculum learning approach, combined with environment stability, proves more impactful than algorithmic sophistication.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **methodological insight** rather than a technical innovation, but it is significant because it challenges prevailing assumptions in the RL-for-LLM community. If validated by subsequent work, this insight could shift research priorities from algorithm design toward data engineering and environment construction for agentic systems.

### Summary: What Distinguishes This Work

| Innovation | Type | Prior State | Tongyi DeepResearch Contribution |
|------------|------|-------------|----------------------------------|
| Agentic Mid-Training | Fundamental | Two-stage (pre → post) | Three-stage (pre → mid → post) with agentic inductive bias |
| Synthetic Data Pipeline | Fundamental | Human-annotated or mixed | Fully automated, super-human quality, stage-specific |
| Environment Co-Design | Fundamental | Fixed external environment | Adaptive three-tier system coupled to training stage |
| Context Management for Scaling | Fundamental | Token-based test-time scaling | Parallel agent synthesis via compressed state summaries |
| RL Stability Priority | Methodological | Algorithm-focused | Data and environment-focused with minimal algorithm changes |

The paper's most significant contribution is not any single technique but the **coherent integration** of these innovations into a unified framework. Each component addresses a specific bottleneck in agentic training, and together they enable a 30.5B parameter model with only 3.3B activated parameters to outperform much larger proprietary systems across diverse deep research benchmarks (Section 4.2). This efficiency-accuracy trade-off suggests that thoughtful architectural and training design can compensate for raw parameter scale—a finding with implications for deploying capable agents in resource-constrained environments (Section 5.2).

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate Tongyi DeepResearch's capabilities through systematic benchmarking, ablation studies, and robustness checks. The experimental design is comprehensive, covering eight distinct deep research benchmarks with multiple baseline comparisons. However, as we will see, some claims rest more firmly on evidence than others.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Benchmark Selection and Coverage

The authors evaluate across **seven public information-seeking benchmarks** spanning long-term reasoning and long-horizon tool use (Section 4.1). Table 1 presents the complete benchmark suite:

| Benchmark | Domain | Language | Evaluation Focus |
|-----------|--------|----------|------------------|
| Humanity's Last Exam | Multi-disciplinary | English | Complex reasoning across domains |
| BrowseComp | Web browsing | English | Multi-hop information retrieval |
| BrowseComp-ZH | Web browsing | Chinese | Multi-hop information retrieval (Chinese) |
| GAIA | General AI assistants | English | Real-world task completion |
| xbench-DeepSearch | Deep search | English | Information synthesis |
| WebWalkerQA | Web traversal | English | Navigation and extraction |
| FRAMES | Fact retrieval | English | Multi-source verification |
| xbench-DeepSearch-2510 | Deep search | English | Updated deep search benchmark |

This benchmark selection is strategically diverse: it covers both **English and Chinese tasks** (BrowseComp-ZH tests cross-lingual capability), spans **different difficulty levels** (from GAIA's moderate complexity to Humanity's Last Exam's expert-level questions), and includes **both established benchmarks** (GAIA, from 2023) and **newly released evaluations** (xbench-DeepSearch-2510, evaluated October 28, 2025).

#### Baseline Categories

The comparison baselines fall into two distinct families (Section 4.1):

1. **LLM-based ReAct Agents**: General-purpose models with tool-use capabilities
   - GLM-4.5, Kimi-K2, DeepSeek-V3.1, Claude-4-Sonnet, OpenAI o3/o4-mini
   
2. **End-to-End Deep Research Agents**: Specialized systems designed for research tasks
   - OpenAI DeepResearch, Gemini DeepResearch, Kimi Researcher

This two-tier baseline structure is important: it allows the authors to demonstrate superiority over both **general models with added tool use** and **specialized proprietary research systems**. The inclusion of closed-source systems like OpenAI-o3 and Gemini DeepResearch is particularly noteworthy, as direct comparison with proprietary systems is often unavailable in open-source research.

#### Evaluation Protocol and Metrics

The evaluation setup uses fixed inference parameters to ensure stability and reproducibility (Section 4.1):

- **Temperature**: 0.85
- **Repetition penalty**: 1.1
- **Top-p**: 0.95
- **Maximum tool invocations**: 128 per task
- **Context length**: 128K tokens
- **Runs per benchmark**: 3 independent evaluations

The primary metric is **Avg@3** (average performance over 3 runs), which accounts for the stochastic nature of agent environments. The authors also report **Pass@1** (best result over 3 runs) and **Pass@3** for completeness. This multi-metric approach is appropriate for agentic systems, where environmental variability can cause performance fluctuations between runs.

> "Each benchmark is evaluated three times independently, and we report the average performance (Avg@3) as the main metric." (Section 4.1)

All results were obtained on **September 16, 2025**, except for xbench-DeepSearch-2510, which was evaluated on **October 28, 2025**. This temporal specificity matters because web-based benchmarks can change over time as online content evolves.

#### Judging Models

Different benchmarks use different judge models for evaluation consistency (Appendix B):

- **GAIA and WebWalkerQA**: Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct
- **xbench-DeepSearch and xbench-DeepSearch-2510**: Gemini-2.0-Flash-001
- **BrowseComp and BrowseComp-ZH**: GPT-4o-2024-08-06
- **Humanity's Last Exam**: o3-mini (following official protocol)

For mathematical benchmarks (AIME25, HMMT25), the authors employ **manual evaluation** due to the small dataset scale and need for accuracy verification. For SimpleQA, they use the **official evaluation script**.

#### Efficiency Advantage: Performance per Activated Parameter

A critical claim is that Tongyi DeepResearch achieves these results with **substantially fewer activated parameters**:

> "Notably, these gains are achieved with only 3.3 billion activated parameters per token, underscoring the model's efficiency and scalability." (Section 4.2)

The model has 30.5 billion total parameters but uses a Mixture-of-Experts architecture that activates only 3.3 billion per token. Comparing to baselines:

- **DeepSeek-V3.1**: 671 billion total parameters (much larger)
- **OpenAI-o3**: Exact parameter count undisclosed but presumed larger
- **Claude-4-Sonnet**: Exact parameter count undisclosed but presumed larger

This efficiency claim is **credible but not directly comparable** since proprietary models do not disclose their parameter counts or activation patterns. The authors are transparent about this limitation by stating their model "narrowing and in some cases even surpassing the performance of frontier proprietary systems while maintaining superior interpretability and computational efficiency" (Section 4.2).

#### Heavy Mode: Test-Time Scaling Results

Figure 6 presents the Heavy Mode evaluation, which leverages parallel agent exploration with synthesis:

These improvements are **substantial and consistent** across all three benchmarks shown. The BrowseComp improvement of 34.3% is particularly notable, suggesting that parallel exploration effectively addresses the multi-hop retrieval challenges in that benchmark.

The Heavy Mode results support the paper's claim that **context management enables test-time scaling** (Section 4.4). By compressing trajectories into summaries $S_T^u$, the system can aggregate $n$ parallel agent findings without context overflow—a bottleneck that would limit traditional ensemble methods.

2. **Multiple attempts** can substantially improve success rates
3. The **average metric may understate** the model's peak capability

The authors note: "Despite the unstable evaluation environment, our final Avg@3 results are consistent with the Pass@1 (best result over 3 runs) results, demonstrating the robustness of our deep research approach" (Section 4.4). However, the Pass@3 gaps suggest that **robustness remains an area for improvement**—if the model can achieve 63.7 on BrowseComp-ZH in its best run but averages 46.7, there is meaningful variance to address.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

#### Context Length Impact on RL Training

Figure 9 presents a critical ablation comparing **32K, 48K, and 64K context limits** during RL training. This analysis reveals non-obvious dynamics:

**Reward Dynamics (Figure 9, left panel):**
- All three models show monotonically increasing rewards, confirming training framework robustness
- **64K model achieves highest reward ceiling** (expected, as data curation used 64K model)
- **32K and 48K models cap at lower rewards** due to context constraints

**Response Length Dynamics (Figure 9, right panel):**
- **64K model**: Steady increase in average response length, leveraging expansive context
- **48K model**: Maintains consistent equilibrium, improving within stable complexity budget
- **32K model**: **Clear downward trend** in response length

The 32K model's behavior is the most insightful finding:

> "For a model with a limited context, RL training on a curriculum designed for a more capable model can force it to discover more efficient solutions." (Section 4.4)

This is a **non-trivial emergent behavior**: the 32K model cannot solve problems requiring >32K token solutions (yielding zero reward), so it implicitly learns to find more concise action sequences. This suggests that **context constraints can drive efficiency optimization** during RL training—a finding with implications for deploying smaller models in resource-constrained environments.

However, this ablation has a **methodological limitation**: the dynamic data curation for all three variants was performed using the **same 64K context model** (Section 4.4). This means the 32K and 48K models were trained on a curriculum optimized for a more capable model, which may not represent the optimal training strategy for those context sizes. The authors acknowledge this implicitly by noting the hierarchy is "an expected consequence of our data curation method."

#### Simulated vs. Real Environment Validation

Figure 10b compares reward curves between the **simulated Wiki environment** and the **real-world environment** (Figure 8). The authors claim the curves "closely match," validating simulation as a reliable proxy.

**Simulated Environment Reward (Figure 10b):**
- Starts around 0.50
- Increases to approximately 0.75-0.80
- Shows stable convergence with EMA smoothing

**Real Environment Reward (Figure 8):**
- Starts around 0.50
- Increases to approximately 0.60-0.65
- Shows stable convergence with EMA smoothing

The curves are **qualitatively similar** in shape (monotonic increase with stable convergence) but **quantitatively different** in ceiling (simulated reaches ~0.80, real reaches ~0.65). The authors do not explicitly address this gap, which could indicate:

1. **Simulated environment is easier** than real-world (expected, given controlled conditions)
2. **Reward scales differ** between environments
3. **Task difficulty differs** between simulated and real benchmarks

This is a **partial validation**: the simulated environment captures training dynamics but may not perfectly predict real-world performance ceilings. The authors' claim that simulation enables "fast algorithm iteration" is supported, but the sim-to-real gap should be acknowledged more explicitly.

#### Synthetic Data Quality Analysis

The authors provide statistical evidence for synthetic data complexity (Section 4.4):

> "Over 20% of the samples exceed 32K tokens and involve more than 10 tool invocations."

This statistic supports the claim that synthetic data reaches **super-human complexity levels**. However, the paper does not provide:

1. **Human baseline comparison**: What percentage of human-annotated trajectories exceed 32K tokens or 10 tool invocations?
2. **Quality verification method**: How do the authors verify that synthetic trajectories are correct beyond answer matching?
3. **Failure mode analysis**: What types of errors occur in synthetic data, and how do they affect training?

The claim that synthetic data is "super-human level" is **plausible but not rigorously validated** through direct human comparison. The automated verification through formal modeling (Section 3.4.1) provides some assurance, but independent quality assessment would strengthen this claim.

#### General Benchmark Performance

Figure 11 evaluates Tongyi DeepResearch on **non-agentic general benchmarks**:

| Benchmark | Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking | Qwen3-235B-A22B-Thinking | Tongyi DeepResearch |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| AIME25 | 85.0 | 92.3 | **100.0** |
| HMMT25 | 71.4 | 83.9 | **100.0** |
| SimpleQA | 19.2 | 47.1 | **98.6** |

These results are **striking**: Tongyi DeepResearch achieves perfect or near-perfect scores on all three benchmarks, substantially outperforming both the 30B and 235B base models. The authors attribute this to:

1. **Search capability**: Retrieving external information for knowledge-intensive tasks (SimpleQA)
2. **Python Interpreter**: Native computational support for mathematical reasoning (AIME25, HMMT25)

However, these benchmarks may not fully represent **agentic capabilities**:

- **AIME25 and HMMT25**: Mathematical competition problems that benefit from computation but do not require multi-step information seeking
- **SimpleQA**: Knowledge retrieval that may be solvable through single searches rather than deep research

The authors acknowledge this implicitly: "Looking ahead, model training increasingly converges with agent training, solving paradigms evolve toward agentic architectures that integrate tool invocation and environment interaction" (Section 4.4). The general benchmark results demonstrate **tool-use competence** but do not directly validate **deep research agency**.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

#### Claims Strongly Supported by Evidence

1. **State-of-the-art on most benchmarks**: Table 1 shows Tongyi DeepResearch leading on 6 of 8 benchmarks, with clear margins on FRAMES (+6.6) and Humanity's Last Exam (+3.1).

2. **Efficiency advantage**: The 3.3B activated parameter count is clearly stated, and performance compares favorably to larger models (though direct parameter comparisons are limited by proprietary model opacity).

3. **Heavy Mode effectiveness**: Figure 6 shows consistent 16-34% improvements across benchmarks, validating test-time scaling through parallel exploration.

4. **Training stability**: Figure 8 shows stable reward increase and entropy convergence, supporting the claim that environment design and data curation enable stable RL training.

#### Claims with Partial or Conditional Support

1. **"Super-human" synthetic data quality**: The 20% of samples exceeding 32K tokens with 10+ tool invocations suggests complexity, but without human baseline comparison, the "super-human" characterization is not rigorously validated.

2. **Sim-to-real transfer**: Figure 10b and Figure 8 show qualitatively similar curves but quantitatively different ceilings. The simulated environment validates training dynamics but may not predict absolute performance.

3. **General benchmark superiority**: The 100% scores on AIME25 and HMMT25 are impressive but may reflect computational tool advantages rather than deep research capabilities.

#### Notable Gaps and Limitations

1. **BrowseComp performance gap**: On BrowseComp, OpenAI DeepResearch scores 51.5 vs. Tongyi's 43.4. The authors do not provide detailed analysis of why this gap exists or what specific capabilities OpenAI's system demonstrates that Tongyi lacks.

2. **Failure case analysis**: The paper does not systematically analyze **where Tongyi DeepResearch fails**. Which types of questions cause errors? Are failures due to planning, retrieval, reasoning, or synthesis? This information would guide future improvements.

3. **Ablation of training stages**: The paper does not ablate the contribution of **mid-training vs. post-training**. How much does Agentic CPT contribute vs. Agentic RL? A table comparing "Base only," "Base + Mid-training," and "Base + Mid + Post" would clarify the value of each stage.

4. **Tool usage analysis**: The paper does not report **average tool invocations per benchmark** or **tool success rates**. Understanding which tools are most frequently used and which fail most often would inform system improvements.

5. **Latency and cost metrics**: For practical deployment, **inference latency** and **API costs per task** matter. The paper mentions cost reduction through the unified sandbox but does not quantify it.

### 5.5 Summary: Do Experiments Support the Paper's Claims?

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Notes |
|-------|-------------------|-------|
| State-of-the-art deep research performance | **Strong** | Table 1 shows leadership on 6/8 benchmarks |
| Efficiency (3.3B activated params) | **Moderate** | Parameter count clear, but baseline comparisons limited by proprietary opacity |
| Synthetic data scalability | **Moderate** | Complexity statistics provided, but no human quality comparison |
| Environment co-design effectiveness | **Moderate** | Sim-to-real curves similar but not identical; sandbox stability claimed but not quantified |
| Mid-training necessity | **Weak** | No ablation isolating mid-training contribution |
| Heavy Mode test-time scaling | **Strong** | Figure 6 shows consistent 16-34% improvements |
| RL stability through data/environment | **Strong** | Figure 8 shows stable convergence; entropy remains bounded |

**Overall Assessment**: The experiments **largely support** the paper's core claims, particularly regarding benchmark performance, Heavy Mode effectiveness, and training stability. However, some claims—especially regarding synthetic data quality and the necessity of mid-training—would benefit from additional ablation studies and human baseline comparisons. The BrowseComp performance gap relative to OpenAI DeepResearch is a notable exception where Tongyi DeepResearch does not achieve state-of-the-art, and the authors do not fully analyze this limitation.

The experimental design is **comprehensive in breadth** (8 benchmarks, multiple baselines, various metrics) but **selective in depth** (limited failure analysis, no stage-wise ablation). For a technical report aiming to open-source a framework, this trade-off is reasonable—the authors prioritize demonstrating capability across diverse tasks over deep diagnostic analysis. Future work should address the identified gaps, particularly around failure mode characterization and training stage contribution quantification.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Tongyi DeepResearch establishes a compelling framework for open-source deep research agents, several important limitations and trade-offs warrant careful consideration. These constraints reveal both the current boundaries of the approach and fertile ground for future research. Understanding these limitations is essential for practitioners who wish to deploy or extend this system, as well as for researchers aiming to build upon this foundation.

### 6.1 Context Window Constraints and Long-Horizon Reasoning

The most explicitly acknowledged limitation concerns context length. The system operates with a **128K token context window**, which the authors state "remains insufficient for handling the most complex long-horizon tasks" (Section 5.1). This constraint creates a fundamental tension:

- **Task Complexity vs. Context Capacity**: Deep research tasks often require synthesizing information from dozens of sources across extended reasoning chains. The context management paradigm (Section 3.1) mitigates this through Markovian state reconstruction, compressing history into evolving report summaries $S_t$. However, this compression is inherently lossy—some contextual details may be discarded during synthesis.

- **Evidence from Context Length Ablation**: Figure 9 reveals that models with smaller context windows (32K, 48K) achieve lower reward ceilings during RL training compared to the 64K variant. The authors note this is "an expected consequence of our data curation method" because the curriculum was populated with problems deemed moderately difficult by the 64K model (Section 4.4). This suggests that **context capacity directly bounds achievable performance** on complex tasks.

- **Trade-off Implication**: The context management paradigm enables arbitrary exploration depths but at the cost of potential information loss. Practitioners should expect degraded performance on tasks requiring precise recall of early-stage observations after many interaction turns.

The authors motivate "further exploration of extended context windows or more advanced context management mechanisms" (Section 5.1), acknowledging this as an active constraint rather than a solved problem.

### 6.2 Model Scale and Generalization Boundaries

Tongyi DeepResearch deliberately targets a **smaller model architecture** (30.5B total parameters, 3.3B activated) rather than pursuing maximum scale. The authors justify this choice by noting that "smaller models are inherently more efficient to deploy on edge devices, broaden accessibility across diverse real-world scenarios, and deliver faster, more responsive interactions" (Section 5.2). However, this design choice introduces trade-offs:

- **Performance Ceiling**: While the model outperforms many larger baselines on deep research benchmarks, it does not achieve state-of-the-art on all tasks. On **BrowseComp**, OpenAI DeepResearch scores 51.5 compared to Tongyi's 43.4—a gap of 8.1 points (Table 1). The paper does not provide detailed analysis of whether this gap stems from model scale, training data quality, or architectural differences.

- **No Larger Variant Released**: The authors explicitly state, "we have not yet released a larger-scale model. Although the smaller-sized model already demonstrates strong performance, a larger model is currently in progress" (Section 5.1). This leaves open the question of how much performance is constrained by scale versus training methodology.

- **General Benchmark Performance**: Figure 11 shows perfect scores (100.0) on AIME25 and HMMT25, which the authors attribute to tool use (Python Interpreter for computation, Search for knowledge). However, these benchmarks may not stress **agentic capabilities** in the same way as deep research tasks. The leap from 19.2 (base model) to 98.6 on SimpleQA is striking but may reflect retrieval advantages rather than reasoning improvements.

**Critical Question**: Is the performance gap on certain benchmarks (e.g., BrowseComp) a temporary limitation awaiting a larger model, or does it reveal fundamental constraints in the training paradigm that scale alone cannot address? The paper does not provide evidence to distinguish these possibilities.

### 6.3 Synthetic Data Quality and Verification Gaps

The paper's heavy reliance on synthetic data is presented as a strength, but several verification gaps remain:

- **No Human Baseline Comparison**: The authors claim synthetic data achieves "super-human level" quality (Section 1), supported by statistics that "over 20% of the samples exceed 32K tokens and involve more than 10 tool invocations" (Section 4.4). However, the paper does not provide:
  - Direct comparison with human-annotated trajectories on the same tasks
  - Error rate analysis of synthetic trajectories
  - Expert evaluation of synthetic reasoning quality

- **Verification Relies on Answer Matching**: The RL reward signal is "a pure 0 or 1 signal of answer correctness" (Section 3.4.3). This means trajectories that arrive at correct answers through flawed reasoning receive the same reward as those with sound reasoning. The formal modeling of information-seeking problems (Section 3.4.1) helps structure questions but does not guarantee reasoning quality.

- **Data Flywheel Risk**: The paper describes a "data flywheel" where trained models generate better synthetic data for subsequent training (Section 2). However, this introduces **distributional drift risk**: if early synthetic data contains systematic errors, subsequent generations may amplify rather than correct these errors. The paper does not address mechanisms for detecting or correcting such drift.

**Trade-off Implication**: Synthetic data enables massive scalability but sacrifices the grounding that human annotation provides. Practitioners should be aware that the model's reasoning patterns reflect the synthesis pipeline's biases, which may not align with optimal human research strategies.

### 6.4 Environment Fidelity and Sim-to-Real Transfer

The three-tier environment hierarchy (Prior World, Simulated, Real-world) is a key innovation, but sim-to-real transfer remains imperfect:

- **Quantitative Gap in Reward Curves**: Figure 10b (simulated environment) shows reward converging to approximately 0.75–0.80, while Figure 8 (real environment) shows convergence to 0.60–0.65. The authors claim the curves "closely match" (Section 4.4), but the **15–20 point ceiling difference** is not addressed. This suggests the simulated environment may be easier than real-world conditions.

- **Limited Simulated Environment Coverage**: The simulated environment is "based on the 2024 Wikipedia database" with local RAG tools (Section 3.4.3). This provides stability but inherently limits coverage:
  - No access to dynamic web content (news, real-time data)
  - No exposure to website structural changes
  - No handling of API rate limits or failures that occur in production

- **Tool Reliability Abstraction**: The unified sandbox implements "proactive QPS rate constraints, result caching, automatic timeout-and-retry protocols, and seamless failover to backup data sources" (Section 3.4.3). While this stabilizes training, it may **mask failure modes** that the model would encounter in deployment without the sandbox infrastructure.

**Critical Gap**: The paper does not report performance degradation when the model is deployed without the unified sandbox. Practitioners should expect that real-world deployment may require significant engineering investment to replicate the training environment's stability.

### 6.5 Training Stage Contributions Remain Unclear

A significant methodological limitation is the **lack of ablation studies isolating training stage contributions**:

- **Mid-Training Necessity Unproven**: The paper argues that agentic mid-training bridges the gap between pre-training and post-training, preventing "inherent optimization conflicts" (Section 2). However, no experiment compares:
  - Base model → Post-training only (no mid-training)
  - Base model → Mid-training → Post-training (full pipeline)

- **SFT vs. RL Contribution**: The post-training pipeline includes both Supervised Fine-Tuning (cold start) and Reinforcement Learning. The paper does not quantify how much performance gain comes from each stage. Figure 8 shows RL training dynamics but does not show the baseline after SFT.

- **Data Curation Impact**: The automatic data curation pipeline dynamically filters problems based on model performance (Section 3.4.3). The paper claims this prevents learning stagnation but does not ablate training with vs. without dynamic curation.

**Trade-off Implication**: Without stage-wise ablation, practitioners cannot determine which components are essential vs. optional. This complicates efforts to reproduce or adapt the framework with limited resources.

### 6.6 Tool Usage and Failure Mode Opacity

The paper provides limited visibility into how the agent actually uses its tools during execution:

- **No Tool Usage Statistics**: The system has five tools (Search, Visit, Python Interpreter, Google Scholar, File Parser), but the paper does not report:
  - Average tool invocations per benchmark
  - Tool success/failure rates
  - Which tools are most critical for which task types

- **Failure Case Analysis Missing**: The paper does not systematically analyze **where the agent fails**. Section 5.4 notes this gap: "The paper does not systematically analyze where Tongyi DeepResearch fails. Which types of questions cause errors? Are failures due to planning, retrieval, reasoning, or synthesis?" Understanding failure modes is essential for:
  - Diagnosing weaknesses in the training pipeline
  - Guiding targeted improvements
  - Setting appropriate user expectations

- **Maximum Tool Invocations**: Evaluation allows "a maximum of 128 tool invocations per task" (Section 4.1), but the paper does not report how often this limit is reached or whether tasks are truncated due to this constraint.

**Critical Question**: Does the agent fail because it cannot find the right information, cannot reason about the information it finds, or cannot synthesize findings into a coherent answer? The paper does not provide evidence to distinguish these failure modes.

### 6.7 Computational and Deployment Constraints

Several practical constraints affect deployment feasibility:

- **RL Training Cost**: The agentic RL framework requires "numerous interactions with the environment" (Section 3.4.3), creating computational bottlenecks. The authors implement "step-level asynchronous RL training" with separate servers for inference and tool invocation to mitigate this (Section 3.4.3). However, the paper does not quantify:
  - Total training compute (GPU hours)
  - API costs for real-world environment interactions
  - Time required for a full training run

- **Inference Latency**: Deep research tasks complete "in tens of minutes" (Section 1), which is faster than human research but may be too slow for interactive applications. The paper does not report:
  - Average task completion time per benchmark
  - Latency breakdown (reasoning vs. tool calls vs. network)
  - How Heavy Mode's parallel agents affect latency

- **Infrastructure Requirements**: The unified sandbox requires "central scheduling and management layer that orchestrates every tool call" (Section 3.4.3). Deploying this infrastructure at scale requires:
  - Backup API sources for failover
  - Caching infrastructure
  - Rate limit management

**Trade-off Implication**: The efficiency advantage of 3.3B activated parameters may be offset by the infrastructure complexity required to support stable tool use. Smaller organizations may struggle to replicate the deployment environment.

### 6.8 Prompt and Tool Set Rigidity

The current system has limited flexibility in adapting to new contexts:

- **Predefined Tool Sets**: The authors note, "our current Deep Research training focuses on specific prompt instructions and predefined tool sets" (Section 5.1). This means:
  - Adding new tools requires retraining or fine-tuning
  - The model may not generalize to tools outside the training distribution
  - Tool signatures and behaviors are fixed

- **Prompt Instruction Dependence**: The system prompt (Appendix A) defines specific output formats (e.g., `<answer></answer>` tags) and tool call structures. The model's performance may degrade if:
  - Users modify the system prompt
  - Tool APIs change their response formats
  - New interaction paradigms are introduced

- **Limited Domain Adaptation**: The paper aims to "evolve from domain-specific agents to general-purpose agents" (Section 5.3), acknowledging that the current system is not yet fully general. Performance on specialized domains (e.g., medical, legal) is not evaluated.

**Open Question**: How much retraining is required to adapt the system to new tool sets or domains? The paper does not provide evidence on adaptation efficiency.

### 6.9 Summary: Key Trade-offs and Open Questions

| Limitation | Impact | Mitigation Status |
|------------|--------|-------------------|
| 128K context window | Bounds task complexity | Acknowledged; future work planned |
| Model scale (30.5B) | Performance ceiling on some benchmarks | Larger model in progress |
| Synthetic data verification | Potential reasoning quality gaps | Formal modeling helps but no human comparison |
| Sim-to-real gap | Training may not predict deployment performance | Curves similar but ceilings differ |
| No stage-wise ablation | Unclear contribution of each training phase | Not addressed |
| Failure mode opacity | Hard to diagnose and improve weaknesses | Not analyzed |
| Infrastructure complexity | Deployment barrier for smaller organizations | Sandbox described but costs not quantified |
| Tool/prompt rigidity | Limited adaptability to new contexts | Acknowledged as future work |

**Critical Open Questions for Future Research**:

1. **What is the minimum context length required for different task classes?** The 32K/48K/64K ablation (Figure 9) suggests context affects performance, but task-specific requirements are unknown.

2. **How much does mid-training contribute vs. post-training?** Without ablation, the necessity of the three-stage pipeline remains unproven.

3. **Can synthetic data quality be verified without human annotation?** The formal modeling approach (Section 3.4.1) is promising but needs independent validation.

4. **What failure modes dominate in real deployment?** Understanding whether failures stem from planning, retrieval, reasoning, or synthesis would guide targeted improvements.

5. **How does performance scale with model size?** The authors plan a larger model but have not released it, leaving scale-performance relationships unclear.

These limitations do not diminish the paper's contributions but rather define the boundary conditions within which Tongyi DeepResearch operates. For practitioners, understanding these constraints is essential for setting appropriate expectations and identifying where additional engineering or research investment may be required. For researchers, these gaps represent concrete opportunities to advance the state of agentic systems beyond the current frontier.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

Tongyi DeepResearch does more than introduce a capable model—it establishes a reproducible blueprint for building autonomous research agents. This section examines how the work reshapes the research landscape, what follow-up investigations it enables, where practical deployment makes sense, and how practitioners should approach adoption.

### 7.1 Landscape Shifts: From Proprietary Black Boxes to Open Agentic Frameworks

The most significant implication of this work is the **democratization of deep research capabilities**. Prior to this release, state-of-the-art deep research agents existed only as closed-source proprietary systems (OpenAI DeepResearch, Gemini DeepResearch, Kimi Researcher), with their intermediate reasoning processes and training methodologies inaccessible (Section 1). Tongyi DeepResearch changes this dynamic in three concrete ways:

**Opening the Methodological Black Box.** By releasing the complete training framework—including agentic mid-training, post-training, synthetic data pipelines, and environment configurations—the paper provides the first publicly available end-to-end recipe for building deep research agents. This enables researchers to:
- Inspect how agentic behaviors are cultivated through mid-training (Section 3.3)
- Reproduce the synthetic data generation pipeline without human annotation (Section 3.3.2, 3.4.1)
- Experiment with environment co-design strategies (Section 3.4.4)

**Validating Small-Model Agentic Training.** The paper demonstrates that a 30.5 billion parameter model with only 3.3 billion activated parameters can outperform much larger proprietary systems on most benchmarks (Table 1, Section 4.2). This challenges the prevailing assumption that agentic capabilities require massive scale:

> "We believe that training agentic capabilities on relatively small models is highly valuable. Smaller models are inherently more efficient to deploy on edge devices, broaden accessibility across diverse real-world scenarios, and deliver faster, more responsive interactions." (Section 5.2)

This finding suggests a new research direction: **capability through training design rather than parameter count**. Future work may focus on optimizing training pipelines for smaller models rather than pursuing ever-larger architectures.

**Establishing Synthetic Data as Primary Training Engine.** The paper's fully automated data synthesis pipeline—generating "super-human level datasets with stable distributions" without human annotation (Section 1)—inverts the conventional data hierarchy. Prior work treated synthetic data as a lower-quality substitute; this work demonstrates it can exceed human annotation quality while being infinitely scalable. The "data flywheel" concept, where trained models generate better synthetic data for subsequent rounds (Section 2), suggests a path to **self-improving agent systems** that prior work does not articulate.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly and implicitly suggests several concrete research avenues. We categorize these by their relationship to the current work.

#### 7.2.1 Direct Extensions (Building on Current Framework)

**Larger-Scale Model Development.** The authors state that "a larger-scale model is currently in progress" (Section 5.1). This creates an immediate opportunity for comparative analysis:
- How does performance scale with model size when training methodology is held constant?
- Does the mid-training paradigm remain necessary at larger scales, or does raw capacity compensate for lack of agentic inductive bias?
- What is the optimal activated parameter count for deep research tasks?

**Extended Context Windows.** The current 128K context limit "remains insufficient for handling the most complex long-horizon tasks" (Section 5.1). Follow-up work should investigate:
- What context length is required for specific task classes (e.g., literature reviews vs. fact verification)?
- Can advanced context management mechanisms (Qiao et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025c) reduce the effective context requirement?
- How does context length interact with the Heavy Mode parallel synthesis approach (Section 4.3)?

**Off-Policy RL Optimization.** The current framework uses strict on-policy training with selective exclusion of negative samples (Section 3.4.3). The authors note plans to "improve the efficiency of our reinforcement learning framework by exploring techniques such as partial rollouts, which will require addressing off-policy training challenges, including distributional shift" (Section 5.1). This is a high-value direction because:
- On-policy RL is sample-inefficient, requiring many environment interactions
- Partial rollouts could reduce training time and API costs
- Off-policy methods would enable reuse of historical trajectories

**Broader Agentic Tool Use.** The current system focuses on "specific prompt instructions and predefined tool sets" (Section 5.1). Extending to broader scenarios requires:
- Dynamic tool discovery and integration without retraining
- Adaptation to new domains (medical, legal, scientific) with domain-specific tools
- Generalization beyond the five current tools (Search, Visit, Python Interpreter, Google Scholar, File Parser)

#### 7.2.2 Foundational Investigations (Testing Core Claims)

**Mid-Training Necessity Ablation.** The paper argues that agentic mid-training prevents "inherent optimization conflicts" (Section 2), but provides no ablation isolating its contribution. A critical follow-up study should compare:
- Base model → Post-training only (no mid-training)
- Base model → Mid-training → Post-training (full pipeline)

This would validate whether the three-stage paradigm is essential or whether post-training alone suffices with sufficient data.

**Synthetic Data Quality Verification.** The claim of "super-human level" synthetic data (Section 1) rests on complexity statistics (20% of samples exceed 32K tokens with 10+ tool invocations, Section 4.4) but lacks human baseline comparison. Rigorous validation should include:
- Expert evaluation of synthetic vs. human-annotated reasoning quality
- Error rate analysis of synthetic trajectories
- Detection of systematic biases in the synthesis pipeline

**Sim-to-Real Transfer Quantification.** The simulated Wiki environment shows reward curves "closely matching" real environment dynamics (Section 4.4), but the ceiling differs (~0.80 vs. ~0.65, Figure 10b vs. Figure 8). Follow-up work should:
- Measure performance degradation when deploying without the unified sandbox
- Characterize which failure modes appear in real deployment but not simulation
- Develop metrics for predicting real-world performance from simulated training

**Failure Mode Characterization.** The paper does not systematically analyze where the agent fails (Section 5.4). Understanding failure modes is essential for targeted improvements:
- Do failures stem from planning errors, retrieval failures, reasoning mistakes, or synthesis problems?
- Which benchmarks reveal specific weaknesses?
- How do failure rates correlate with tool invocation counts or context length?

#### 7.2.3 Paradigm Extensions (Generalizing Beyond Deep Research)

**From Domain-Specific to General-Purpose Agents.** The authors aim to "evolve from domain-specific agents to general-purpose agents, which are capable of reasoning, planning, and acting autonomously across diverse domains with minimal human supervision" (Section 5.3). This requires:
- Unified agent foundation models with scalable reasoning, memory, and autonomy
- Mechanisms for transferring agentic capabilities across task domains
- Evaluation frameworks for general agency beyond information-seeking

**Multi-Agent Collaboration.** The Heavy Mode deploys $n$ parallel agents with synthesis (Section 4.3), but agents do not communicate during exploration. Future work could investigate:
- Inter-agent communication protocols for collaborative research
- Specialized agent roles (e.g., one searches, one verifies, one synthesizes)
- Consensus mechanisms for conflicting findings

**Human-in-the-Loop Research Agents.** The current system operates autonomously, but practical deployment may benefit from human oversight:
- Checkpoints for human validation of intermediate findings
- Mechanisms for incorporating user feedback during execution
- Preference alignment for report generation style and depth (Section 5.1)

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Deployment Scenarios

Tongyi DeepResearch is designed for **long-horizon, deep information-seeking research tasks** that would otherwise require several hours of human effort (Section 1). We identify specific use cases where this system provides clear value.

#### 7.3.1 High-Value Application Domains

**Academic Literature Review.** The system's ability to conduct multi-source investigations across specialized academic fields (Appendix A system prompt) makes it suitable for:
- Systematic literature reviews requiring synthesis of dozens of papers
- Identifying research gaps across related domains
- Tracking citation networks and methodological evolution

The Google Scholar tool (Appendix D) and ability to parse PDF files (File Parser tool) directly support this use case.

**Competitive Intelligence and Market Research.** The Search and Visit tools enable automated monitoring of:
- Competitor product announcements and feature changes
- Market trend analysis across news sources and industry reports
- Regulatory changes affecting specific sectors

The 128 tool invocation limit per task (Section 4.1) allows extensive coverage without manual oversight.

**Technical Due Diligence.** For investment or acquisition decisions requiring deep technical assessment:
- Verifying technical claims in pitch decks or whitepapers
- Cross-referencing patents, publications, and product documentation
- Identifying potential intellectual property conflicts

The Python Interpreter tool (Appendix D) enables validation of computational claims.

**Fact-Checking and Verification.** The FRAMES benchmark performance (90.6, Table 1) demonstrates strong multi-source verification capabilities:
- Cross-referencing claims across independent sources
- Identifying contradictions or inconsistencies in public statements
- Building evidence chains for complex factual questions

#### 7.3.2 When to Use Tongyi DeepResearch vs. Alternatives

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Simple factual queries (e.g., "What is the capital of France?") | Standard LLM without tools | Deep research overhead unnecessary; single-token answer suffices |
| Multi-hop reasoning with web search (e.g., "Which company founded by X acquired Y in 2024?") | Tongyi DeepResearch | Requires sequential search and verification across sources |
| Mathematical computation with external data | Tongyi DeepResearch with Python Interpreter | Combines retrieval with native computation (Figure 11 shows 100% on AIME25) |
| Real-time interactive Q&A | Standard LLM or smaller agent | Deep research completes in "tens of minutes" (Section 1), too slow for interactive use |
| Domain-specific tasks (medical, legal) | Wait for domain-adapted version | Current system focuses on general research; specialized domains not evaluated (Section 5.1) |
| Resource-constrained deployment | Tongyi DeepResearch (3.3B activated) | More efficient than larger proprietary systems while maintaining competitive performance (Section 5.2) |
| Maximum accuracy on complex benchmarks | Heavy Mode | Improves BrowseComp from 43.4 to 58.3 (+34.3%) through parallel exploration (Figure 6) |

#### 7.3.3 Integration Considerations

**Infrastructure Requirements.** The unified sandbox architecture (Section 3.4.3) is critical for stable deployment:
- Implement QPS rate constraints and result caching
- Configure automatic timeout-and-retry protocols
- Set up backup API sources for failover (e.g., secondary search API)

Without this infrastructure, real-world API volatility may corrupt agent trajectories.

**Cost Management.** While the paper notes the sandbox "significantly reducing operational costs" (Section 3.4.3), it does not quantify expenses. Practitioners should budget for:
- Search API costs (varies by provider and query volume)
- Compute costs for model inference (3.3B activated parameters per token)
- Storage for trajectory logging and caching

**Evaluation Protocol.** For reproducible benchmarking, follow the paper's evaluation settings (Section 4.1):
- Temperature = 0.85, repetition penalty = 1.1, top-p = 0.95
- Maximum 128 tool invocations per task
- Context length constrained to 128K tokens
- Report Avg@3 (average over 3 independent runs) as primary metric

### 7.4 Reproduction and Adoption Guidance

The authors release "the model, framework, and complete solutions to empower the community" (Section 1), with official reproduction scripts on GitHub (Section 4.1). We provide practical guidance for adoption.

#### 7.4.1 Getting Started Path

**Phase 1: Inference-Only Deployment (Lowest Barrier)**
1. Download the model from HuggingFace or ModelScope (abstract links)
2. Set up the five tools using provided implementations (Appendix D, GitHub repository)
3. Configure the unified sandbox for stable tool invocation (Section 3.4.3)
4. Evaluate on a subset of benchmarks to establish baseline performance

**Phase 2: Fine-Tuning for Specific Domains (Moderate Effort)**
1. Use the synthetic data pipeline to generate domain-specific QA pairs (Section 3.4.1)
2. Apply SFT cold start with domain trajectories (Section 3.4.2)
3. Optionally run RL with verifiable rewards for further optimization (Section 3.4.3)

**Phase 3: Full Pipeline Reproduction (Highest Effort)**
1. Reproduce the agentic mid-training pipeline (Section 3.3)
2. Implement the three-tier environment hierarchy (Section 3.4.4)
3. Run the complete end-to-end training framework (Figure 2)

Most practitioners should start with Phase 1, as the pre-trained model already achieves state-of-the-art performance on most benchmarks (Table 1).

#### 7.4.2 Common Pitfalls and Mitigations

| Pitfall | Symptom | Mitigation |
|---------|---------|------------|
| Insufficient sandbox infrastructure | Tool call failures corrupt training trajectories | Implement retry protocols and backup APIs (Section 3.4.3) |
| Context overflow on long tasks | Agent loses track of early observations | Use Context Management Mode with evolving report summaries $S_t$ (Section 3.1) |
| RL training instability | Policy collapse after extended training | Exclude negative samples that exceed length limits (Section 3.4.3) |
| Sim-to-real performance gap | Model performs worse in deployment than testing | Validate algorithms in simulation, then deploy to real environment (Section 3.4.4) |
| Tool API changes | Model fails to parse new response formats | Abstract tool invocation through unified sandbox layer (Section 3.4.3) |

#### 7.4.3 Community Contribution Opportunities

The open-source release invites several forms of community engagement:

**Benchmark Expansion.** The paper evaluates on seven public benchmarks (Section 4.1), but new domains emerge continuously. Contributors can:
- Develop domain-specific evaluation suites (e.g., biomedical, legal, financial)
- Create adversarial benchmarks testing robustness to misleading sources
- Establish longitudinal benchmarks tracking performance over time as web content evolves

**Tool Ecosystem Development.** The current five tools (Appendix D) cover common use cases but not all scenarios. Extensions could include:
- Database query tools for structured data access
- API integration tools for specific services (e.g., Twitter, GitHub)
- Multimedia analysis tools (image, audio, video understanding)

**Training Pipeline Improvements.** The framework is designed for extension:
- Implement off-policy RL variants for improved sample efficiency
- Develop advanced context management mechanisms beyond Markovian reconstruction
- Create automated hyperparameter optimization for different task classes

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision: Toward General Autonomous Agents

The paper positions Tongyi DeepResearch as "a significant step toward AI systems capable of autonomously transforming information into insight" (Section 5.3). The long-term trajectory extends beyond deep research:

**Convergence of Model Training and Agent Training.** The authors observe that "model training increasingly converges with agent training, solving paradigms evolve toward agentic architectures that integrate tool invocation and environment interaction, reflecting a more human-like problem-solving process" (Section 4.4). This suggests:
- Future foundation models may be trained with agentic capabilities from the start
- The distinction between "base model" and "agent" may blur
- Tool use may become as fundamental as language understanding

**Scalable Autonomy.** The next-generation goal is "a unified model designed to endow AI systems with scalable reasoning, memory, and autonomy, enabling them to operate as truly general agents" (Section 5.3). Key challenges include:
- Memory mechanisms that persist across sessions and tasks
- Reasoning capabilities that transfer across domains without retraining
- Autonomy levels that balance independence with appropriate human oversight

**Democratization of Agentic Intelligence.** The authors "advocate for open-source models with emergent agency, which are essential for democratizing agentic intelligence and deepening our fundamental understanding of how agency can emerge and scale in open systems" (Section 5.3). This work contributes by:
- Providing a reproducible framework for building capable agents
- Demonstrating that small models can achieve competitive performance
- Establishing synthetic data pipelines that do not require human annotation

The ultimate implication is that **agentic capabilities are learnable, scalable, and reproducible**—not proprietary secrets confined to well-resourced labs. By open-sourcing the complete framework, Tongyi DeepResearch invites the community to accelerate progress toward general-purpose autonomous agents that can augment human intellectual productivity across diverse domains.
