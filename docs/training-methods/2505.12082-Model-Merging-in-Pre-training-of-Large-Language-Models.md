This paper introduces Pre-trained Model Average (PMA), a strategy that merges checkpoints from the constant learning rate phase of pre-training to achieve significant performance gains across Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models ranging from 0.7B/7B to 20B/200B parameters, such as improving the `Seed-MoE-10B/100B` HumanEval score from 54.3 to 61.6. By demonstrating that PMA can accurately simulate the performance of computationally expensive cosine-annealed models without requiring the decay phase, the work offers a low-cost method to accelerate validation cycles and reduce training expenses. Additionally, the authors propose `PMA-init`, which utilizes merged weights to stabilize downstream Continual Training (CT) and Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) by recovering from irrecoverable loss spikes and smoothing GradNorm curves.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Critical Bottlenecks in Large-Scale Pre-training
The development of modern Large Language Models (LLMs) is currently constrained by four interrelated challenges that escalate dramatically as model sizes reach hundreds of billions of parameters:
1.  **Prohibitive Computational Costs:** Training models from scratch requires massive clusters running for months, making experimentation slow and financially demanding.
2.  **Unpredictable Performance Scaling:** It is difficult to precisely predict a model's final capability before completing the entire training run, leading to inefficient resource allocation.
3.  **Training Instability:** Large-scale training is prone to "loss spikes"—sudden, irrecoverable divergences in the loss function often caused by gradient explosions—which can invalidate weeks of computation.
4.  **Inefficient Validation Cycles:** The standard practice to estimate final model performance involves a "Warmup-Stable-Decay" (WSD) schedule, where the model must undergo a lengthy cosine annealing (decay) phase. This phase is computationally expensive but necessary to verify if the model has converged to a good optimum.

The core problem this paper addresses is the lack of efficient mechanisms to mitigate these issues specifically during the **pre-training phase**. While the community has developed sophisticated techniques for the post-training stage, the pre-training phase remains a "black box" where developers often wait until the very end of training to assess quality or recover from failures.

### The Gap: Post-Training Success vs. Pre-Training Scarcity
To understand the significance of this work, one must distinguish between the two primary phases of LLM development and how model merging has been applied to them.

**Model Merging in Post-Training (The Established Paradigm)**
Prior to this work, model merging was predominantly a post-training technique. In this context, researchers take multiple models that have been fine-tuned on *different* downstream tasks (e.g., one model specialized in math, another in coding) and merge their weights to create a single, versatile model.
*   **Mechanism:** Techniques like **Task Arithmetic** [18], **Ties-Merging** [46], and **DARE** [51] operate by combining the "delta" parameters (the difference between the fine-tuned weights and the base pre-trained weights).
*   **Success Case:** The paper cites a striking example where merging `WizardLM` and `WizardMath` using DARE improved the GSM8K math benchmark score from **2.2 to 66.3**.
*   **Limitation:** These methods rely on the existence of distinct, task-specific fine-tuned models. They do not address how to improve the *base* pre-trained model itself or how to optimize the training trajectory before fine-tuning begins.

**Model Merging in Pre-Training (The Unexplored Frontier)**
In contrast, pre-training merging involves combining checkpoints from a *single* training trajectory at different points in time.
*   **Prior Attempts:** Existing research in this area is scarce and limited in scope.
    *   **LAWA** [23] explored merging to accelerate training but did not provide a comprehensive framework for large-scale performance gains.
    *   **Checkpoint Merging** [27] evaluated merging on the Baichuan2 model but lacked detailed methodological disclosure.
    *   **Industry Black Boxes:** Major players like DeepSeek [26] and LLaMA-3 [11] have hinted at using merging techniques, but their specific methodologies, hyperparameters, and theoretical justifications remain undisclosed.
*   **The Specific Gap:** There is no public, systematic study that scales model merging to models exceeding 100 billion parameters, nor is there a clear understanding of how merging interacts with different phases of the learning rate schedule (constant vs. decay). Crucially, the community lacks a proven method to use merging as a *predictor* of final performance, which would allow developers to skip the expensive annealing phase during validation.

### Positioning of This Work: From Heuristic to Systematic Framework
This paper positions itself as the first comprehensive investigation into **Pre-trained Model Average (PMA)**, shifting model merging from an ad-hoc acceleration trick to a fundamental component of the pre-training strategy.

The authors differentiate their approach from prior work through three key dimensions:

1.  **Scale and Architecture:** Unlike previous studies limited to smaller dense models, this work validates PMA across a massive scale range—from **411M to 70B** dense parameters and **0.7B/7B to 20B/200B** MoE (Mixture-of-Experts) parameters. This demonstrates that the benefits of merging are not artifacts of small models but hold true at the frontier of LLM development.
2.  **The "Simulator" Hypothesis:** The paper introduces a novel conceptual shift: using PMA not just to improve weights, but as a **low-cost simulator for annealing**.
    *   *Traditional Approach:* Train with Warmup $\to$ Stable $\to$ Decay. Wait for the end to evaluate.
    *   *PMA Approach:* Train with Warmup $\to$ Stable. Merge checkpoints from the stable phase to *predict* the performance of the decayed model.
    *   *Impact:* As shown in **Figure 3**, merging checkpoints from the constant learning rate phase yields performance comparable to models that have undergone full cosine annealing. This allows researchers to validate model quality early, potentially skipping the decay phase entirely for experimental runs, thereby saving significant compute.
3.  **Training Stabilization (PMA-init):** While prior work focused on final performance, this paper extends the utility of merging to **training dynamics**. By introducing `PMA-init`, the authors propose using merged weights to initialize downstream stages (Continual Training or SFT). This acts as a stabilization mechanism, smoothing `GradNorm` curves and enabling recovery from "irrecoverable" loss spikes (detailed in **Section 4.5**), a capability absent in standard checkpoint loading.

In summary, this work bridges the gap between the theoretical promise of weight averaging and the practical realities of training trillion-token models. It transforms model merging from a post-hoc optimization into a proactive strategy for cost reduction, performance prediction, and stability assurance in the pre-training lifecycle.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a systematic empirical study and framework for **Pre-trained Model Average (PMA)**, a technique that merges sequential checkpoints from the constant learning rate phase of Large Language Model (LLM) pre-training to enhance performance, predict final model quality without expensive annealing, and stabilize downstream training. The core idea is that averaging weights from different points in a stable training trajectory leverages the geometric diversity of the loss landscape to find a superior optimum that individual checkpoints cannot reach alone.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system is a training methodology that periodically saves model snapshots during the "stable" phase of pre-training and mathematically averages their weights to create a single, higher-performing model. It solves the problems of high computational costs for validation and training instability by replacing the need for a long "cool-down" (annealing) phase with a quick merging operation and by providing a robust mechanism to recover from training crashes.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The PMA framework operates as a post-processing and initialization layer on top of a standard LLM training pipeline, consisting of three primary components:
1.  **The Trajectory Generator:** A standard LLM training run using a Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD) scheduler, which outputs a sequence of $N$ checkpoint files ($M_1, M_2, \dots, M_N$) at fixed token intervals during the constant learning rate phase.
2.  **The Merger Engine:** A module that ingests these $N$ checkpoints, applies a specific weighting strategy (e.g., Simple Moving Average), and computes a new set of parameters $M_{avg}$ via linear interpolation.
3.  **The Downstream Initializer (`PMA-init`):** An interface that takes the merged $M_{avg}$ weights and injects them as the starting point for subsequent Continual Training (CT) or Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), replacing the standard practice of initializing from the single latest checkpoint.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Formalizing the Merging Operation:** We first define the mathematical notation for combining checkpoints and the specific weighting schemes (SMA, EMA, WMA) evaluated in the study.
*   **The Training Schedule Context:** We explain the specific "Warmup-Stable-Decay" learning rate schedule used, as PMA's effectiveness is tightly coupled to the "Stable" phase where checkpoints are harvested.
*   **Hyperparameter Scaling Laws:** We detail the critical design choices regarding *how many* checkpoints to merge ($N$) and *how far apart* they should be in terms of training tokens ($V$), including how these values scale with model size.
*   **The `PMA-init` Stabilization Mechanism:** We describe the novel application of using merged weights to initialize downstream tasks, specifically focusing on how this smooths gradient norms and recovers from loss spikes.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** We conclude with the mathematical derivation based on Taylor expansion that explains *why* averaging diverse checkpoints yields a lower loss than the average loss of the individual checkpoints.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Definition and Mathematical Formulation**
The fundamental operation of PMA is a weighted linear interpolation of model parameters drawn from a single training trajectory.
*   Let $M_i$ represent the parameter weights of the model saved at the $i$-th checkpoint, where $i$ ranges from $1$ to $N$.
*   The goal is to compute a merged model $M_{avg}$ using a set of weighting coefficients $w_i$ such that the sum of all weights equals 1.
*   The general equation for the merged model is defined as:
    $$M_{avg} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i M_i$$
*   The paper assumes that the data consumption (number of tokens seen) between consecutive checkpoints forms an arithmetic sequence with a common difference $V$, defined as:
    $$V = T_{i+1} - T_i$$
    where $T_i$ is the cumulative token count for the $i$-th model. This $V$ represents the **merging interval**, a critical hyperparameter determining how distinct the checkpoints are.

**Weighting Strategies (The Merger Engine)**
The authors evaluate three distinct strategies for assigning the coefficients $w_i$, each reflecting a different hypothesis about which parts of the training trajectory are most valuable.
*   **Simple Moving Average (SMA):** This approach treats all selected checkpoints equally, assigning a uniform weight of $w_i = 1/N$ to every model.
    *   The formula simplifies to:
        $$M_{avg} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} M_i$$
    *   The paper finds that while other methods show slight advantages early in training, SMA becomes the most robust and stable choice once the model enters the later stages of pre-training, leading the authors to adopt it as the default for most experiments.
*   **Exponential Moving Average (EMA):** This method prioritizes recent checkpoints by applying exponentially decaying weights, operating on the assumption that newer weights are closer to the optimal solution.
    *   EMA is computed recursively rather than as a single sum, using a smoothing factor $\alpha$ (where $0 < \alpha < 1$):
        $$M^{(i)}_{avg} = \alpha \cdot M_i + (1 - \alpha) \cdot M^{(i-1)}_{avg}$$
    *   Here, $M^{(i)}_{avg}$ is the accumulated average after considering the $i$-th model, and a higher $\alpha$ (e.g., 0.2 vs 0.1) places more emphasis on the current checkpoint $M_i$.
*   **Weighted Moving Average (WMA):** Similar to EMA, this strategy prioritizes recent models but uses a linearly increasing weight scheme (e.g., $w_i = i$) rather than an exponential decay.
    *   The weights are normalized by their sum $w_{sum}$ to ensure they form a valid probability distribution:
        $$M_{avg} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{w_i}{w_{sum}} M_i, \quad \text{where } w_{sum} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i$$
    *   Experiments in **Figure 4** indicate that WMA performs best in the early phases of training when model weights are changing rapidly, but its advantage diminishes as training stabilizes.

**The Training Context: Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD)**
The effectiveness of PMA is intrinsically linked to the specific learning rate schedule used during pre-training, which the paper identifies as the Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD) scheduler.
*   The schedule begins with a short **Warmup** phase where the learning rate increases linearly to a peak value.
*   This is followed by a long **Stable** phase where the learning rate remains constant; this is the critical window during which the authors collect the $N$ checkpoints for merging.
*   Finally, a **Decay** (annealing) phase reduces the learning rate, typically following a cosine schedule, to refine the weights into a sharp minimum.
*   The central innovation of PMA is the finding that merging checkpoints from the **Stable** phase can replicate the performance benefits of the **Decay** phase, effectively allowing researchers to skip the computationally expensive annealing step during validation cycles.
*   **Figure 3** demonstrates that a model trained with a constant learning rate and then merged via PMA achieves performance comparable to, or even exceeding, a model that underwent full cosine annealing.

**Hyperparameter Scaling: Interval ($V$) and Count ($N$)**
The paper conducts extensive ablation studies to determine the optimal values for the merging interval $V$ and the number of models $N$, revealing that these parameters must scale with the model size.
*   **Optimal Interval ($V$):** The distance in tokens between checkpoints must be large enough to ensure the models have explored sufficiently different regions of the loss landscape, but not so large that early, unstable weights are included.
    *   For small MoE models (0.7B/7B activated/total parameters), the optimal interval is approximately **4B tokens**.
    *   For medium MoE models (1.3B/13B), the optimal interval scales to **8B tokens**.
    *   For large MoE models (10B/100B), the interval increases significantly to approximately **80B tokens**.
    *   The paper notes that using intervals that are too large (e.g., 32B for small models) during early training can degrade performance because it inadvertently includes weights from the unstable initial phase of training.
*   **Optimal Count ($N$):** The number of checkpoints to merge involves a trade-off between computational cost and performance gain.
    *   Experiments show that increasing $N$ consistently improves performance once training is complete, with $N=15$ outperforming $N=3$ by nearly 1 point on comprehensive benchmarks.
    *   However, during the *early* stages of training, merging too many models ($N=15$) can introduce noise from unstable checkpoints, whereas a smaller $N$ is safer.
    *   To balance efficiency and performance, the authors standardize on **$N=10$** for their primary experiments.

**Downstream Stabilization: The `PMA-init` Mechanism**
Beyond improving pre-training metrics, the authors introduce `PMA-init`, a technique that uses merged weights to initialize downstream stages like Continual Training (CT) and Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT).
*   **Standard Practice:** Typically, downstream training initializes from the single latest checkpoint ($M_N$), which may reside in a sharp or unstable region of the loss landscape.
*   **PMA-init Approach:** Instead, the process initializes from $M_{avg}$, the merged weights of the preceding $N$ checkpoints.
*   **Effect on Dynamics:** As shown in **Figure 6** and **Figure 7**, initializing with $M_{avg}$ results in smoother **GradNorm** (gradient norm) curves during the early steps of downstream training.
*   **Recovery from Loss Spikes:** The paper demonstrates that if a training run suffers an "irrecoverable" loss spike (a divergence caused by gradient explosion), one can resume training by merging $N$ checkpoints saved *before* the spike occurred.
    *   In a reproduction of instability on a 330M/3.3B MoE model with a high learning rate ($6 \times 10^{-3}$), standard resumption failed, but resuming with `PMA-init` allowed the model to navigate past the spike and recover its original trajectory.
    *   This suggests that the averaging process acts as a regularizer, centering the initialization in a flatter, more stable basin of attraction.

**Theoretical Mechanism: Why Merging Works**
The paper provides a mathematical justification for PMA using a second-order Taylor expansion of the loss function $L(\theta)$ around an optimal parameter set $\theta^*$.
*   Assuming the gradient at the optimum is zero ($\nabla L(\theta^*) = 0$), the loss for any parameter set $\theta$ can be approximated by the curvature (Hessian matrix $H$):
    $$L(\theta) \approx L(\theta^*) + \frac{1}{2}(\theta - \theta^*)^T H (\theta - \theta^*)$$
*   If we define the deviation of the $i$-th model from the optimum as $\delta_i = \theta_i - \theta^*$, the average loss of $k$ individual models is proportional to the average of their individual quadratic deviations:
    $$\frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} L(\theta_i) \approx L(\theta^*) + \frac{1}{2k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} \delta_i^T H \delta_i$$
*   In contrast, the loss of the *merged* model (where parameters are averaged: $\theta_{avg} = \frac{1}{k}\sum \theta_i$) depends on the square of the average deviation:
    $$L(\theta_{avg}) \approx L(\theta^*) + \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} \delta_i \right)^T H \left( \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} \delta_i \right)$$
*   Expanding this term reveals that the merged model's loss includes "cross-terms" ($\delta_i^T H \delta_j$ for $i \neq j$).
*   **Key Insight:** The merged model achieves lower loss than the average individual model if these cross-terms are negative.
    *   Mathematically, the condition for improvement is:
        $$\sum_{i=1}^{k} \sum_{j \neq i} \delta_i^T H \delta_j < (k-1) \sum_{i=1}^{k} \delta_i^T H \delta_i$$
    *   Intuitively, this means the individual checkpoints must have explored **diverse directions** in the parameter space such that their errors cancel each other out when averaged.
    *   **Figure 8** visualizes this by plotting checkpoints on MMLU score contour lines; the individual models (black dots) are scattered around a valley, while the merged model (the average position) lands closer to the center of the valley (the global optimum) than most individual points.
    *   This also explains why merging offers diminishing returns after extensive annealing: as models converge tightly to a single point, the diversity ($\delta_i$) vanishes, and the cross-terms no longer provide a beneficial correction.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper moves beyond the incremental application of existing averaging techniques to establish fundamental shifts in how large-scale pre-training is conceptualized, executed, and stabilized. The following insights distinguish PMA from prior heuristics, transforming it from a simple post-processing step into a core strategic component of LLM development.

### 4.1 Merging as a "Zero-Cost" Annealing Simulator
**The Innovation:** The most profound conceptual shift in this work is redefining model merging not merely as a performance booster, but as a **high-fidelity simulator for the annealing phase**.
*   **Prior Paradigm:** Traditionally, estimating a model's final potential requires completing the full Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD) schedule. The "Decay" (annealing) phase is computationally expensive, often consuming 10–20% of total training tokens, yet it provides no new data exposure—only refinement of existing weights. Researchers must wait until this phase concludes to know if a training run was successful.
*   **The PMA Breakthrough:** The authors demonstrate that merging checkpoints taken *solely* from the constant learning rate ("Stable") phase yields performance metrics nearly identical to those of a model that has undergone full cosine annealing.
    *   **Evidence:** As shown in **Figure 3**, a `Seed-MoE-1.3B/13B` model merged during stable training matches or exceeds the performance of its annealed counterpart on benchmarks like HumanEval and GSM8K, despite never having seen a decaying learning rate.
    *   **Significance:** This decouples *performance validation* from the *annealing process*. Developers can now train only through the stable phase, merge checkpoints to predict final quality, and discard unsuccessful runs before wasting resources on decay. This transforms the decay phase from a mandatory validation step into an optional final polish, potentially reducing validation cycle costs by orders of magnitude.

### 4.2 Emergent Scaling Laws for Merging Hyperparameters
**The Innovation:** The paper establishes the first empirical **scaling laws for model merging hyperparameters**, proving that optimal merging strategies are not universal constants but functions of model size.
*   **Prior Assumption:** Previous studies (e.g., LAWA, Checkpoint Merging) often applied fixed intervals or counts across different model sizes, treating merging as a one-size-fits-all heuristic.
*   **The PMA Breakthrough:** Through rigorous ablation on models ranging from 411M to 200B parameters, the authors reveal a direct correlation between model scale and the optimal token interval ($V$) between checkpoints.
    *   **Specific Findings:** The optimal interval scales non-linearly:
        *   **0.7B/7B MoE:** Requires a tight interval of **~4B tokens**.
        *   **1.3B/13B MoE:** Optimal at **~8B tokens**.
        *   **10B/100B MoE:** Requires a massive interval of **~80B tokens**.
    *   **Reasoning:** The paper links this to the tendency of larger models to utilize larger batch sizes [31], implying that larger models traverse the loss landscape more slowly per token, requiring wider spacing to capture distinct geometric explorations (diverse $\delta_i$ vectors as described in Section 3.4).
    *   **Significance:** This provides the community with a prescriptive formula rather than a guess. Ignoring these scaling laws (e.g., using a small-model interval on a 100B model) would result in merging highly correlated checkpoints, yielding negligible gains. This insight elevates merging from a "trick" to a principled engineering constraint.

### 4.3 `PMA-init`: Turning Merging into a Stability Mechanism
**The Innovation:** While prior work focused on merging for *final accuracy*, this paper introduces **`PMA-init`**, a novel application where merging serves as a **regularizer for training dynamics** to prevent and recover from catastrophic failures.
*   **Prior Limitation:** Standard practice initializes downstream stages (Continual Training or SFT) from the single latest checkpoint ($M_N$). If this checkpoint sits in a sharp, unstable region of the loss landscape (common after long training), it can trigger gradient explosions ("loss spikes") that are typically irrecoverable, forcing a restart from scratch.
*   **The PMA Breakthrough:** By initializing downstream training with the averaged weights ($M_{avg}$) of the preceding $N$ checkpoints, the starting point is effectively "centered" in a flatter, more stable basin of attraction.
    *   **Evidence:** **Figure 7 (Right)** demonstrates a scenario where a 330M/3.3B MoE model suffers an irrecoverable loss spike due to a high learning rate ($6 \times 10^{-3}$). Standard resumption fails, but resuming with `PMA-init` (merging 3 pre-spike checkpoints) allows the training to bypass the instability and continue converging.
    *   **Significance:** This offers a "safety net" for trillion-token training runs. Instead of viewing a loss spike as a fatal error requiring weeks of recomputation, `PMA-init` provides a mathematically grounded recovery protocol. It changes the risk profile of large-scale training, allowing for more aggressive learning rate schedules with a built-in rollback mechanism.

### 4.4 Geometric Complementarity Over Simple Averaging
**The Innovation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical justification that merging works due to **geometric complementarity** in the loss landscape, not just noise reduction.
*   **Prior Understanding:** Weight averaging was often intuitively explained as "smoothing out noise" or "ensembling."
*   **The PMA Breakthrough:** Using a second-order Taylor expansion (Section 3.4, Eq. 15), the authors prove that merging reduces loss *if and only if* the deviation vectors of individual checkpoints ($\delta_i$) exhibit negative correlation with respect to the Hessian matrix ($H$).
    *   **Visual Proof:** **Figure 8** visualizes this by plotting checkpoints on MMLU score contours. The individual models (black dots) are scattered around the optimum, while the merged model lands precisely in the deeper valley center.
    *   **Critical Nuance:** This explains *why* merging fails after extensive annealing. As noted in the analysis of Figure 8, once models are annealed to a low learning rate, they converge tightly to the same local point. The diversity ($\delta_i$) vanishes, the cross-terms become zero, and merging offers no benefit.
    *   **Significance:** This theoretical insight dictates *when* to merge. It confirms that merging is most potent during the **stable phase** where the model is exploring diverse regions of a broad basin, and least effective after the model has already been forced into a narrow minimum by annealing. This validates the core strategy of merging *before* decay.

### 4.5 Convergence of Merging Strategies in the Stable Regime
**The Innovation:** The study reveals that the choice of weighting strategy (SMA vs. EMA vs. WMA) becomes **negligible** once the model enters the stable training phase, simplifying practical deployment.
*   **Prior Debate:** In time-series forecasting and finance, the choice between Simple, Exponential, and Weighted Moving Averages is critical, as recent data often holds significantly more signal. It was assumed this would hold for LLM checkpoints too.
*   **The PMA Finding:** While **Figure 4** shows that Weighted Moving Average (WMA) offers a slight edge in *early* training (where weights change rapidly), the performance gap between SMA, EMA, and WMA collapses as training progresses.
    *   **Observation:** By the 1607B token mark, the performance differences among all three methods are statistically insignificant.
    *   **Significance:** This is a crucial practical simplification. It implies that during the long stable phase of pre-training, the model's trajectory is sufficiently smooth and consistent that complex weighting schemes offer no advantage over the computationally simplest approach: **Simple Moving Average (SMA)**. This allows engineering teams to implement PMA with minimal code complexity and computational overhead, removing a major barrier to adoption.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence supporting the Pre-trained Model Average (PMA) framework. The authors conducted a massive-scale experimental campaign, training models from scratch to isolate the effects of merging from confounding variables like dataset differences or architecture changes. The analysis moves from establishing raw performance gains to validating the "simulator" hypothesis, optimizing hyperparameters, and finally stress-testing the stability mechanisms.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

To ensure the findings are robust across the spectrum of modern LLM development, the experimental design covers diverse architectures, scales, and evaluation protocols.

**Model Architectures and Scales**
The study avoids the common pitfall of testing only on small proxy models. Instead, it trains a comprehensive suite of models from scratch:
*   **Dense Models:** Ranging from **411M** to **70B** parameters. (Detailed results for dense models are provided in **Appendix A**, specifically **Figure 9**).
*   **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) Models:** The primary focus, spanning activated/total parameter counts from **0.7B/7B** up to **20B/200B**.
*   **Training Corpus:** All models are trained on an internal corpus comprising **trillions of tokens**. While the specific dataset composition is not public, the learning rates are tuned according to established scaling laws [4, 24] to ensure optimal baseline performance.

**Learning Rate Schedule**
All experiments utilize the **Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD)** scheduler [15]:
1.  **Warmup:** Linear increase to peak learning rate.
2.  **Stable:** Extended period at constant learning rate (the primary source of checkpoints for PMA).
3.  **Decay:** Cosine annealing to a minimal learning rate.
The core investigation compares models merged during the *Stable* phase against baselines that complete the full *Decay* phase.

**Benchmarks and Metrics**
Performance is evaluated using a weighted average score across **17 standard benchmarks** in both zero-shot and few-shot settings. This aggregate metric ensures that gains are not isolated to a single domain. The suite includes:
*   **Reasoning & Knowledge:** MMLU [14], MMLU-Pro [41], BBH [39], ARC-Challenge [6], GPQA [33], AGIEval [56].
*   **Math & Code:** GSM8K [7], MATH [55], HumanEval [5], MBPP [3], Ape210K [55].
*   **Language Understanding:** HellaSwag [54], WinoGrande [34], DROP [10], TriviaQA [22], C-Eval [16].

**Baselines**
*   **Standard Baseline:** The latest checkpoint from a training run that completes the full WSD schedule (including annealing).
*   **Constant LR Baseline:** A model trained with only Warmup and Stable phases, skipping decay entirely.
*   **Merging Variants:** Comparisons between Simple Moving Average (SMA), Exponential Moving Average (EMA), and Weighted Moving Average (WMA).

### 5.2 Primary Performance Gains: Merging vs. Baselines

The first critical question is whether PMA actually improves performance compared to standard training. The results demonstrate consistent, significant gains across all model sizes.

**MoE Model Improvements (Figure 1)**
When applying PMA to models trained in the stable phase, the performance uplift is substantial.
*   **Seed-MoE-1.3B/13B:** On the **HumanEval** coding benchmark, the score jumps from **31.1** (Baseline) to **36.6** (PMA), a relative improvement of **~17.7%**. On **GSM8K** (math), it rises from **42.7** to **46.3**.
*   **Seed-MoE-10B/100B:** The gains persist at scale. HumanEval improves from **54.3** to **61.6** (+7.3 points), and GSM8K increases from **54.3** to **59.8**.
*   **Saturation Effects:** The paper notes that on some benchmarks like BBH for the largest models, gains are less pronounced. This is attributed to metric saturation, where the baseline is already near the ceiling of the benchmark's difficulty.

**Dense Model Generalization (Figure 9, Appendix A)**
Crucially, PMA is not limited to MoE architectures.
*   **Seed-Dense-70B:** The largest dense model shows remarkable improvements. HumanEval scores increase from **50.6** to **57.9** (+7.3 points), and GSM8K rises from **85.9** to **91.3**.
*   **Small Models:** Even the **411M** model sees gains, with HumanEval moving from **4.9** to **8.1**, proving the technique works regardless of parameter count.

**Assessment:** These results convincingly support the claim that merging checkpoints from the stable phase yields a superior optimum compared to simply stopping training at the latest step. The consistency across architectures (Dense vs. MoE) and scales (411M to 200B) suggests the mechanism is fundamental to the loss landscape of transformers, not an artifact of a specific setup.

### 5.3 Validating the "Annealing Simulator" Hypothesis

The most transformative claim of the paper is that PMA can replace the computationally expensive annealing phase for validation purposes.

**PMA vs. Annealed Models (Figure 2 & Figure 3)**
*   **Early Annealing Equivalence (Figure 2):** When comparing PMA applied during the *early* stages of cosine decay against models at the *end* of decay, the performance is comparable. For the **Seed-MoE-10B/100B** model, the PMA curve (blue) tracks closely with the annealed curve (orange), and in some token counts, PMA even slightly exceeds the final annealed performance.
*   **Skipping Decay Entirely (Figure 3):** This is the definitive experiment. The authors forked a training run at **1.4T tokens** into two paths: one continuing with constant learning rate (to be merged) and one undergoing cosine annealing.
    *   **Result:** The "Constant + PMA" model (blue line) significantly outperforms the "Constant" baseline (gray line) and matches or exceeds the "Annealing" model (orange line) on benchmarks like **HumanEval**, **BBH**, **MMLU**, and **GSM8K**.
    *   **Implication:** At **1.6T tokens**, the merged constant-LR model achieves a HumanEval score of roughly **40**, while the pure constant model lags at **~25**, and the annealed model sits around **38-39**.

**Assessment:** This experiment strongly validates the "simulator" hypothesis. It demonstrates that the geometric benefits of averaging diverse checkpoints in the stable basin can replicate the refinement usually achieved by slowly lowering the learning rate. This allows practitioners to validate model quality after the stable phase, potentially saving the **10–20% of compute** typically reserved for annealing during experimental runs.

---

### 5.4 Hyperparameter Ablation Studies

The paper provides critical guidance on *how* to merge, revealing that optimal hyperparameters are not universal but scale with model size.

**Merging Strategies: SMA vs. EMA vs. WMA (Figure 4)**
The authors compared three weighting schemes on the **Seed-MoE-1.3/13B** model:
*   **Early Training:** At **204B tokens**, **Weighted Moving Average (WMA)** performs best (score **~60.8**), outperforming SMA (**~60.6**) and EMA ($\alpha=0.2$). This suggests that when weights are changing rapidly, prioritizing recent checkpoints is beneficial.
*   **Late Training:** As training progresses to **1607B tokens**, the performance gap vanishes. All three methods converge to nearly identical scores (**~47.0–47.9** range).
*   **Conclusion:** Due to its simplicity and stability in the late regime, the authors adopt **Simple Moving Average (SMA)** as the default strategy.

**Optimal Interval ($V$) and Count ($N$) (Figure 5)**
*   **Interval Scaling:** The distance between checkpoints ($V$) must scale with model size to ensure sufficient diversity ($\delta_i$).
    *   **0.7B/7B Model:** Optimal $V \approx 4\text{B}$ tokens.
    *   **1.3B/13B Model:** Optimal $V \approx 8\text{B}$ tokens.
    *   **10B/100B Model:** Optimal $V \approx 80\text{B}$ tokens.
    *   **Failure Mode:** Using an interval that is too large (e.g., $V=32\text{B}$ for a small model) during early training degrades performance because it includes unstable weights from the initial warmup/early phase.
*   **Checkpoint Count ($N$):**
    *   Increasing $N$ consistently improves final performance. Merging **15** models yields a score nearly **1 point higher** than merging only **3** models on the comprehensive benchmark.
    *   **Trade-off:** While larger $N$ is better for final quality, merging too many models *early* in training can introduce noise. The authors recommend **$N=10$** as a robust balance between cost and performance.

**Assessment:** These ablations are essential for reproducibility. They correct the misconception that merging parameters are fixed constants. The clear scaling relationship between model size and interval $V$ provides a prescriptive rule for future large-scale training.

---

### 5.5 Downstream Impact and Stability (PMA-init)

The study extends beyond pre-training metrics to examine how merged weights affect subsequent Continual Training (CT) and Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT).

**Continual Training (CT) Dynamics (Figure 6)**
*   **Loss Curves:** Initializing CT with PMA weights results in marginally lower loss in the early steps compared to the baseline (latest checkpoint).
*   **Convergence:** While PMA-init models start stronger, they eventually converge to similar final loss levels as the baseline across different learning rate schedules.
*   **Benchmark Performance:** On **MMLU**, PMA-init models show an early lead. Although the gap narrows, they maintain a slight edge or parity, confirming that PMA initialization does not harm downstream potential.

**Stabilization and Recovery from Loss Spikes (Figure 7)**
This is the most practical application of PMA for large-scale operations.
*   **GradNorm Smoothing:** In SFT experiments, models initialized with PMA exhibit significantly smoother **GradNorm** (gradient norm) curves compared to the baseline, indicating more stable optimization dynamics.
*   **Recovery Experiment:** The authors simulated a catastrophic failure on a **330M/3.3B MoE** model by using an excessively high learning rate ($6 \times 10^{-3}$), causing an irrecoverable loss spike.
    *   **Baseline Failure:** Attempting to resume from the checkpoint immediately before the spike failed; the model diverged again.
    *   **PMA-init Success:** By merging **3 checkpoints** saved *before* the spike and resuming from this averaged state, the training successfully navigated past the instability and returned to a converging trajectory.

**SFT Performance Nuances (Table 1, Appendix B)**
Results on SFT are mixed but promising:
*   **Significant Gains:** On the **Seed-MoE-15B/150B** model, PMA-init with a learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-5}$ improved **LiveBench** by **2.7 points** (50.5 $\to$ 53.2) and **AMC-2023** (math competition) by **4.5 points** (61.0 $\to$ 65.5) compared to the baseline. Instruction Following (IF) scores also rose from **36.3** to **38.8**.
*   **Inconsistency:** The authors note they could not replicate these specific magnitude gains on other model sizes, though PMA-init never degraded performance.
*   **Conclusion:** PMA-init is a low-risk, high-reward strategy for stabilization, even if the magnitude of performance boosts varies.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

**Strengths:**
1.  **Scale and Rigor:** The experiments cover an unprecedented range of model sizes (up to 200B parameters), moving beyond the "toy model" limitations of prior academic work.
2.  **Causal Isolation:** By training models from scratch and controlling the learning rate schedule, the authors successfully isolate the effect of merging from other variables.
3.  **Practical Utility:** The "simulator" experiment (Figure 3) and the "recovery" experiment (Figure 7) provide direct, actionable value for engineering teams, addressing cost and reliability simultaneously.
4.  **Theoretical Alignment:** The empirical results (e.g., diminishing returns after annealing) align perfectly with the theoretical derivation regarding geometric diversity (Section 3.4), creating a cohesive narrative.

**Limitations and Open Questions:**
1.  **Learning Rate Sensitivity:** The authors acknowledge in **Appendix C** that they did not extensively explore the interaction between *higher* base learning rates and merging. They hypothesize that merging might allow for even more aggressive learning rates (citing [35]), but this remains unquantified due to compute costs.
2.  **SFT Variability:** The gains in the SFT stage (Table 1) are impressive but inconsistent across model sizes. The conditions under which PMA-init yields massive gains versus marginal gains are not fully characterized.
3.  **RL Exclusion:** The study focuses on pre-training, CT, and SFT. It explicitly excludes Reinforcement Learning (RL) stages, leaving open the question of whether PMA could stabilize RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) trajectories, which are notoriously unstable.

**Conclusion on Evidence:**
The experiments convincingly support the paper's core claims. The data robustly demonstrates that PMA improves final performance, accurately simulates annealing, and stabilizes training dynamics. The scaling laws for hyperparameters provide a clear path for adoption, transforming model merging from a heuristic curiosity into a standardized component of the LLM pre-training toolkit.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the Pre-trained Model Average (PMA) framework offers significant advantages in performance and stability, it is not a universal panacea. The approach relies on specific architectural and training assumptions, faces distinct computational trade-offs, and leaves several critical domains unexplored. A rigorous understanding of these limitations is essential for practitioners attempting to deploy PMA in production environments.

### 6.1 Dependency on the "Stable" Training Regime
The efficacy of PMA is fundamentally contingent on the model operating within the **constant learning rate ("Stable") phase** of the Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD) schedule.
*   **The Diversity Requirement:** As established in the theoretical analysis (Section 3.4), PMA works because individual checkpoints explore diverse regions of the loss landscape ($\delta_i$), allowing their errors to cancel out via negative cross-terms in the Hessian.
*   **The Annealing Failure Mode:** Once a model enters the **Decay (annealing)** phase, the learning rate decreases, forcing the optimizer into a narrow, sharp minimum. As noted in the analysis of **Figure 8**, checkpoints taken during deep annealing become highly correlated; they occupy nearly identical positions in the parameter space.
    *   *Consequence:* Merging these highly correlated checkpoints yields negligible diversity ($\delta_i \approx \delta_j$), causing the cross-terms to vanish. The merged model offers no improvement over the individual checkpoints.
    *   *Constraint:* This means PMA cannot be used to "rescue" a model that has already been over-annealed or to further improve a model that has completed its full decay schedule. Its utility is strictly bounded to the pre-decay window.

### 6.2 Unquantified Interaction with Aggressive Learning Rates
A significant open question remains regarding the interplay between PMA and the base learning rate magnitude.
*   **The Hypothesis:** The authors explicitly state in **Appendix C** that they suspect training with a **higher base learning rate** combined with PMA could yield even superior models. This aligns with prior work [35] suggesting that high learning rates encourage broader exploration, which PMA could then average into a robust optimum.
*   **The Gap:** Due to the "extremely high expenses" of large-scale training, the authors **did not quantify** this effect. They defaulted to using optimal learning rates derived from standard scaling laws [4, 24] rather than pushing the boundaries of instability.
*   **Risk:** Without empirical data, it is unknown whether PMA can reliably stabilize training at learning rates that would otherwise cause immediate divergence, or if there is a hard ceiling beyond which averaging cannot compensate for optimization noise. Practitioners attempting to use PMA to enable "super-aggressive" schedules do so without the safety net of the paper's experimental validation.

### 6.3 Inconsistent Downstream Gains (SFT Variability)
While PMA consistently improves pre-training metrics, its impact on the subsequent **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)** stage is less deterministic.
*   **Mixed Results:** In **Appendix B (Table 1)**, the `Seed-MoE-15B/150B` model showed substantial gains from `PMA-init`, improving **LiveBench** by 2.7 points and **AMC-2023** by 4.5 points. However, the authors explicitly admit: *"we were unable to replicate such significant gains in subsequent experiments with other model sizes."*
*   **The "No-Harm" Baseline:** The only consistent finding for SFT is that `PMA-init` does **not harm** performance and provides smoother `GradNorm` curves.
*   **Implication:** For teams specifically targeting SFT performance boosts, PMA-init should be viewed as a **low-risk stabilization technique** rather than a guaranteed performance multiplier. The conditions under which large downstream gains occur (e.g., specific data distributions, task complexities, or model architectures) remain unidentified.

### 6.4 Computational and Storage Overhead
Although PMA reduces the cost of *validation* by potentially skipping the annealing phase, it introduces specific overheads during the training lifecycle that must be managed.
*   **Checkpoint Storage Burden:** To perform merging with an optimal count ($N=10$) and interval ($V$), the training infrastructure must save **10 full model snapshots** simultaneously.
    *   *Scale Impact:* For a **200B parameter** MoE model, storing 10 additional checkpoints requires massive disk I/O and storage capacity. While the paper treats this as manageable, for clusters operating at maximum storage density, this could necessitate offloading checkpoints to slower cold storage, increasing the latency of the merging operation.
*   **Merging Latency:** The linear interpolation of hundreds of billions of parameters is computationally non-trivial. While faster than training, it is not instantaneous. In a rapid iteration loop where hundreds of experiments are run, the cumulative time spent loading and averaging weights could become a bottleneck if not parallelized efficiently.

### 6.5 Exclusion of Reinforcement Learning (RL) Domains
The scope of this work is strictly limited to **pre-training, Continual Training (CT), and Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)**.
*   **The Missing Piece:** The paper explicitly excludes **Reinforcement Learning (RL)** stages (e.g., RLHF, DPO) in **Appendix C**.
*   **Why It Matters:** RL training is notoriously unstable, often suffering from severe reward hacking and collapse. Recent innovations in RL algorithms [37, 52, 53] have improved stability, but the potential for PMA to merge policy checkpoints or value networks remains completely untested.
*   **Open Question:** Given that RL trajectories often oscillate wildly, the "geometric complementarity" assumed by PMA might behave differently in the non-convex, reward-driven landscape of RL. Applying PMA to RLHF without further research carries unknown risks of averaging away critical policy capabilities.

### 6.6 Sensitivity to Hyperparameter Scaling
Finally, the success of PMA is not "plug-and-play"; it requires strict adherence to the **scaling laws** identified in Section 4.3.
*   **The Scaling Trap:** The optimal merging interval ($V$) scales non-linearly with model size (e.g., 4B tokens for 7B models vs. 80B tokens for 100B models).
*   **Failure Risk:** Applying a "small model" strategy (e.g., $V=4\text{B}$) to a large model (100B) would result in merging checkpoints that are too close in time. These checkpoints would lack the necessary geometric diversity, rendering the merge ineffective. Conversely, using a large interval on a small model risks including unstable early-training weights.
*   **Operational Constraint:** Teams must accurately estimate their model's "effective batch size" and traversal speed to calculate the correct $V$. Misestimation leads directly to suboptimal results, adding a layer of complexity to the training configuration that does not exist in standard single-checkpoint workflows.

In summary, while PMA transforms model merging from a heuristic into a principled strategy, it demands careful management of the training schedule (staying in the stable phase), significant storage resources, and precise hyperparameter tuning. Its benefits in downstream SFT and potential synergy with aggressive learning rates remain promising but empirically unproven frontiers.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of Pre-trained Model Average (PMA) fundamentally alters the operational economics and risk profile of large-scale language model development. By transitioning model merging from a post-hoc optimization trick to a core component of the pre-training lifecycle, this work provides a blueprint for more efficient, stable, and predictable AI systems. The implications extend beyond immediate performance gains, reshaping how researchers approach validation, resource allocation, and failure recovery.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Pre-Training Landscape: From "Wait-and-See" to Predictive Validation
The most profound shift introduced by this work is the decoupling of **performance validation** from the **annealing phase**.
*   **The Old Paradigm:** Historically, determining the quality of a pre-training run required completing the full Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD) schedule. The decay phase, often consuming 10–20% of total compute, acted as a mandatory "proof of convergence." If a model failed to converge after weeks of annealing, the resources were already spent.
*   **The New Paradigm:** PMA establishes that merging checkpoints from the **stable phase** serves as a high-fidelity proxy for the final annealed performance (as demonstrated in **Figure 3**).
    *   **Strategic Impact:** Teams can now treat the decay phase as an optional final polish rather than a diagnostic necessity. For experimental runs or hyperparameter sweeps, developers can train only through the stable phase, merge checkpoints to predict final quality, and terminate unsuccessful runs early.
    *   **Economic Consequence:** This capability effectively creates a "zero-cost" validation mechanism for the annealing phase, potentially reducing the computational budget for model development cycles by double-digit percentages without sacrificing insight into final model capabilities.

### 7.2 Practical Integration Guide: When and How to Deploy PMA
For engineering teams looking to adopt these findings, the paper provides clear decision boundaries based on training stage and objectives.

| Scenario | Recommended Strategy | Rationale & Configuration |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Standard Pre-Training** | **Apply PMA at end of Stable Phase** | Merge $N=10$ checkpoints from the constant learning rate phase to boost final performance before optional annealing. &lt;br> *Config:* Use **Simple Moving Average (SMA)**. Set interval $V$ based on model size (e.g., ~80B tokens for 100B models). |
| **Hyperparameter Search** | **Skip Decay; Use PMA as Simulator** | Do not run the cosine annealing phase. Train to the end of the stable phase, merge checkpoints, and evaluate. | Eliminates the cost of the decay phase for failed experiments. PMA scores correlate strongly with what the annealed score *would* have been. |
| **Training Instability / Loss Spikes** | **Deploy `PMA-init` for Recovery** | If a loss spike occurs, do not restart from scratch. Merge $N=3$ to $N=5$ checkpoints saved *prior* to the spike and resume training from this averaged state. | As shown in **Figure 7**, this centers the optimizer in a flatter basin, allowing it to navigate past the instability that caused the original divergence. |
| **Downstream Initialization (SFT/CT)** | **Use `PMA-init` for Stability** | Initialize Supervised Fine-Tuning or Continual Training with merged weights instead of the single latest checkpoint. | Smooths `GradNorm` curves and reduces the risk of early divergence. While performance gains vary (see **Table 1**), the stability benefit is consistent and risk-free. |
| **Post-Annealing Optimization** | **Do Not Apply PMA** | Avoid merging checkpoints taken after the model has undergone significant cosine annealing. | As explained in **Section 4.4**, annealed checkpoints lack geometric diversity ($\delta_i \approx 0$), rendering the merging operation ineffective. |

### 7.3 Enabling Follow-Up Research Frontiers
The empirical success of PMA opens several critical avenues for future investigation, moving the field from "does it work?" to "how far can we push it?"

*   **Aggressive Learning Rate Schedules:**
    The authors hypothesize in **Appendix C** that PMA could enable the use of significantly higher base learning rates. Since merging averages out the noise and instability associated with high learning rates, future work should explore training regimes that would traditionally diverge. If PMA acts as a stabilizer, it might allow models to traverse the loss landscape faster, potentially reducing the total token count required for convergence.
*   **Extension to Reinforcement Learning (RL):**
    The current study explicitly excludes RL stages (RLHF, DPO), which are notoriously unstable. Given PMA's success in stabilizing pre-training loss spikes, a logical next step is applying `PMA-init` or online merging to RL policy updates. Merging policy checkpoints could mitigate reward hacking and collapse, common failure modes in alignment training.
*   **Dynamic Interval Scaling:**
    While the paper establishes static scaling laws for the merging interval $V$ (e.g., 80B tokens for 100B models), future research could investigate **adaptive intervals**. As the model converges, the optimal distance between diverse checkpoints might shrink. An adaptive scheduler that adjusts $V$ based on real-time gradient variance or loss curvature could further optimize the trade-off between diversity and relevance.
*   **Cross-Architecture Merging:**
    This work focused on merging checkpoints within a single trajectory. Future studies could explore merging checkpoints from *different* training runs (e.g., models trained on different data mixtures but with the same architecture) during the stable phase. If the geometric complementarity holds across data distributions, this could enable rapid "data ensembling" without retraining.

### 7.4 Broader Impact on Open-Source and Resource-Constrained Development
Perhaps the most significant societal implication of this work is its potential to democratize large-scale model development.
*   **Lowering the Barrier to Entry:** The high cost of the annealing phase and the catastrophic risk of loss spikes have historically favored well-funded labs with massive redundant compute. By providing a low-cost validation simulator and a robust recovery mechanism, PMA reduces the financial risk of training large models.
*   **Reproducibility and Transparency:** The paper's release of specific hyperparameters (intervals, counts, weighting schemes) and the open-sourcing of the PMA framework provide a reproducible baseline. This contrasts with the "black box" disclosures from major industry players (e.g., DeepSeek, LLaMA), allowing the broader community to verify and build upon these techniques.
*   **Sustainability:** By enabling early termination of unsuccessful runs and reducing the need for redundant annealing phases, PMA directly contributes to lowering the carbon footprint of LLM training. Efficient validation means fewer wasted GPU hours on models that will not meet performance targets.

In conclusion, this work transforms model merging from a niche technique into a foundational pillar of modern pre-training. It offers a pragmatic path forward where efficiency, stability, and performance are no longer competing objectives but mutually reinforcing outcomes of a unified strategy. As the field scales toward trillion-parameter models, the principles of PMA will likely become standard operating procedure, ensuring that the next generation of AI is built not just bigger, but smarter and more resilient.
