## 1. Executive Summary

To resolve the optimization tensions caused by relying on general-purpose foundation models for agentic tasks, this work introduces Agentic Continual Pre-training (Agentic CPT) as a dedicated intermediate stage to build pre-aligned agentic foundation models. The resulting `AgentFounder-30B` model, trained via scalable First-order and Higher-order Action Synthesis without external API costs, achieves state-of-the-art performance with 39.9% on BrowseComp-en, 43.3% on BrowseComp-zh, and 31.5% Pass@1 on HLE across 10 benchmarks. This paradigm shift demonstrates that incorporating agentic inductive biases during pre-training significantly enhances downstream fine-tuning efficiency and enables robust scaling laws for deep research agents.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Fundamental Bottleneck in Agentic Model Development

To understand why this paper matters, we must first recognize a critical tension in how modern language models are trained for agentic tasks. The prevailing development paradigm follows a **two-stage approach**:

1. **Pre-training**: Models learn broad knowledge from diverse data (web text, code, books) using next-token prediction
2. **Post-training**: Models are fine-tuned via Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning (RL) to follow instructions and align with human preferences

When researchers extended this paradigm to **agentic capabilities**—teaching models to use tools, perform multi-step reasoning, and interact with dynamic environments—they encountered a persistent problem. Even leading open-source agentic models like WebSailor (12.0%), GLM-4.5 (26.4%), and DeepSeek-V3.1 (30.0%) exhibit **substantial performance gaps** compared to OpenAI's Deep Research (51.5%) on challenging benchmarks like BrowseComp (Section 1, Figure 1).

This gap is not merely an engineering challenge—it reveals a **fundamental architectural limitation**.

### Why General-Purpose Foundation Models Fail at Agentic Tasks

The paper identifies the root cause: **general-purpose foundation models lack agentic inductive biases**. This creates what the authors call "optimization tensions" during post-training. To understand this, consider what post-training must accomplish:

- **Learn agentic capabilities**: Tool invocation patterns, multi-step reasoning chains, adaptive responses to environmental changes
- **Align to expert demonstrations**: Match human-preferred behavioral patterns

When starting from a general-purpose model, **both objectives must be learned simultaneously**. This creates inherent conflicts because:

1. **Vast policy spaces**: Deep research agents must traverse enormous decision spaces where SFT's reliance on complete, high-quality trajectory data makes comprehensive coverage infeasible (Section 1)
2. **Long, complex trajectories**: Agent workflows span many steps, making precise definitions of "correct behavior" challenging
3. **Limited supervisory signals**: Both SFT and RL depend on deterministic signals that lock models into replicating specific behavioral patterns rather than developing flexible decision-making capabilities

The consequence is that post-training methods demonstrate **limited effectiveness**, particularly in open-source implementations. The model is forced to learn *what* agentic behavior looks like while simultaneously learning *how* to execute it—a dual burden that constrains performance.

### The Missing Piece: Agentic Foundation Models

The paper makes a crucial observation: **pathways toward developing agentic foundation models themselves remain largely unexplored** (Section 1). While prior work has focused extensively on improving post-training techniques, the foundational layer—the model that enters post-training—has received insufficient attention.

This matters because the foundation model determines the **starting point** for all downstream optimization. A model with agentic inductive biases would:

- Naturally support tool invocation patterns
- Understand multi-step reasoning chains
- Possess behavioral consistency with expert demonstrations *before* fine-tuning begins

Without such a foundation, post-training must compensate for missing capabilities, creating the optimization tensions described above.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper situates itself within a rich ecosystem of prior work on deep research agents. Understanding these approaches clarifies what makes Agentic CPT novel:

**Post-Training Focused Methods:**
- **WebSailor** (Li et al., 2025b): Uses SailorFog, a knowledge graph-based data synthesis method starting with obscure Wikipedia entities
- **WebShaper** (Tao et al., 2025): Models information-seeking tasks as set operations with knowledge projection concepts
- **AFM** (Li et al., 2025c): Synthesizes agentic trajectory data via Chain-of-Agents pipeline
- **DeepDive** (Lu et al., 2025): Constructs high-difficulty QA pairs by sampling nodes in open knowledge graphs

**Common Limitation:** These methods "predominantly follow supervised SFT or RL paradigms, overlooking the potential for training agentic capabilities during the continual pre-training phase" (Section 4.1). They improve post-training data quality but do not address the foundational model's lack of agentic biases.

**Recent General Models with Enhanced Agentic Capabilities:**
- **Kimi-K2**, **GLM-4.5**, **DeepSeek-V3.1**: These models emphasize enhanced agentic capabilities, with GLM-4.5 notably incorporating "synthetic agent trajectories during mid-training" (Section 1, footnote 1)

**Key Distinction:** While GLM-4.5 touches on mid-training synthesis, the paper claims to be **"the first to propose incorporating Agentic Continual Pre-training (Agentic CPT) into the deep research agents training pipeline"** as a systematic, dedicated intermediate stage (Abstract, Section 1).

### Real-World and Theoretical Significance

This problem carries both practical and conceptual weight:

**Real-World Impact:**
- Deep research agents are increasingly deployed for **autonomous information retrieval**, **academic literature synthesis**, **complex problem-solving**, and **report generation** (Section 1)
- Commercial products like OpenAI Deep Research, Perplexity Deep Research, and Gemini Deep Research demonstrate market demand
- The performance gap between open-source and closed-source agents limits accessibility and innovation in the research community

**Theoretical Significance:**
- The paper introduces **"agentic alignment"** as an expansion of traditional alignment paradigms. While static interaction paradigms focus on ensuring outputs align with human preferences (Ouyang et al., 2022), agentic alignment requires "behavior consistency with human expert demonstrations when solving complex tasks in dynamic environments" (Section 1)
- This encompasses reasoning chains, tool invocations, and adaptive responses to unpredictable environmental changes (e.g., tool failure, misleading information)
- By proposing Agentic CPT as an **intermediate scaling layer**, the work redefines the training pipeline itself, not just optimization techniques within existing stages

### How This Paper Positions Itself

The paper positions Agentic CPT as a **paradigm shift** rather than an incremental improvement:

| Aspect | Prior Approaches | This Work |
|--------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Training Pipeline** | Pre-training → Post-training (SFT/RL) | Pre-training → **Agentic CPT** → Post-training |
| **Foundation Model** | General-purpose (e.g., Qwen2.5-72B) | Pre-aligned agentic foundation |
| **Data Synthesis** | Trajectory-level, often requires API calls | Offline synthesis (FAS, HAS) without external tool costs |
| **Optimization Focus** | Post-training techniques | Foundational capability acquisition |
| **Scalability** | Limited by trajectory generation costs | Scalable via offline synthesis (200B + 100B tokens) |

The core innovation is treating agentic capability acquisition as a **continual pre-training objective** rather than a post-training specialization. This allows the model to develop agentic inductive biases *before* facing the dual burden of learning capabilities and alignment simultaneously.

By anchoring this approach in scalable data synthesis methods (First-order Action Synthesis and Higher-order Action Synthesis) that operate without external API costs, the paper addresses both the **architectural limitation** (lack of agentic foundation) and the **practical constraint** (prohibitive costs of trajectory generation) that have constrained prior work.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

This paper presents a **training methodology** rather than a new model architecture—it introduces Agentic Continual Pre-training (Agentic CPT) as an intermediate stage between standard pre-training and post-training to build foundation models that already understand agentic behaviors before fine-tuning begins. The solution addresses the optimization tension problem by separating capability acquisition (during Agentic CPT) from alignment refinement (during post-training), using scalable offline data synthesis methods that generate hundreds of billions of tokens without requiring expensive external API calls.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The Agentic CPT pipeline consists of four major components that operate in sequence. **First**, the Data Collection layer gathers diverse sources including discarded post-training trajectories, historical tool invocation records, and public corpora like CommonCrawl. **Second**, the First-order Action Synthesis (FAS) module transforms static knowledge into dynamic question-answer pairs with synthetic planning and reasoning chains, operating without external tool calls. **Third**, the Higher-order Action Synthesis (HAS) module takes existing agent trajectories and expands them at each decision step to create multi-option decision-making training data. **Fourth**, the Two-Stage Training Strategy processes FAS and short HAS data through Stage 1 (32K context, ~200B tokens) before refining with high-quality long-context HAS data in Stage 2 (128K context, ~100B tokens). The output is `AgentFounder-30B-Base`, a pre-aligned agentic foundation model ready for downstream SFT or RL post-training.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **Agentic CPT Pipeline Overview**: We first explain where Agentic CPT fits in the training paradigm and its two-stage structure, establishing the temporal flow of the methodology.
- **First-order Action Synthesis (FAS)**: We detail how static knowledge becomes agentic training data without API costs, covering both planning and reasoning action synthesis.
- **Higher-order Action Synthesis (HAS)**: We explain how sub-optimal trajectories are transformed into rich decision-making signals through step-level expansion.
- **Two-Stage Training Strategy**: We describe the progressive context length and data quality approach that enables efficient capability acquisition.
- **Quality Control Mechanisms**: We cover the reject sampling and LLM-as-Judge approaches that ensure training data fidelity without ground-truth trajectory signals.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a **methodology paper** that proposes a new training paradigm for agentic models, with the core idea being that agentic capabilities should be acquired during continual pre-training rather than exclusively during post-training. The fundamental insight is that by separating capability acquisition from alignment refinement, models can develop agentic inductive biases before facing the dual burden of learning what agentic behavior looks like and how to execute it simultaneously.

#### The Agentic CPT Training Pipeline

The paper redefines the standard two-stage LLM development paradigm (pre-training → post-training) by inserting Agentic CPT as an intermediate stage, as illustrated in Figure 2. Starting from Qwen's pre-trained foundation models (specifically `Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base`), the enhanced pipeline executes Agentic CPT before any supervised fine-tuning occurs. The mathematical foundation remains the standard next-token prediction objective used during pre-training:

$$\mathcal{L} = -\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log P(x_{t+1}|x_1, x_2, ..., x_t)$$

where $P(x_{t+1}|x_1, x_2, ..., x_t) = \text{softmax}(W_o h_t)$, $h_t$ represents the hidden state at position $t$, and $W_o$ is the output projection matrix (Section 2.1, Equation 1). This is critical: Agentic CPT uses the **same loss function as pre-training**, not the instruction-following objectives typical of SFT. The difference lies entirely in the **data distribution**—instead of raw web text, the model trains on synthetically generated agentic behavior sequences.

The pipeline executes in two distinct stages with specific configurations. **Stage 1** processes approximately 200 billion tokens of agent data and knowledge reasoning corpora with a 32K token context window, enabling preliminary acquisition of agentic behaviors including tool invocation patterns and multi-step reasoning chains. **Stage 2** refines these capabilities using 100 billion tokens of carefully curated, high-quality agent data with extended 128K context windows, allowing the model to develop sophisticated understanding of complex action spaces and long-horizon planning strategies (Section 2.1). This progressive approach matters because training all data at 128K context from the start would incur substantially higher computational costs while potentially destabilizing early learning (Section 3.4.1).

#### First-order Action Synthesis (FAS): Building Agentic Data from Static Knowledge

FAS addresses a fundamental scalability challenge: generating massive volumes of agentic training data without incurring prohibitive external API costs. The intuitive solution—having LLMs actually invoke search engines and web access tools for each training example—faces two severe bottlenecks. Commercial API costs for search engines (e.g., Google Search API) and web access APIs (e.g., Jina Reader API, ScraperAPI) are prohibitively expensive at the scale required for continual pre-training. Additionally, complete trajectory generation is inefficient and cannot meet the massive data requirements (Section 2.2.2).

FAS circumvents these constraints through a two-phase transformation approach that converts static knowledge into dynamic problem-solving contexts, as illustrated in Figure 3. **Phase 1** constructs an Entity-Anchored Open-World Knowledge Memory by transforming continuously updated unstructured text from various sources into a memory system where entities serve as indexing keys mapping to their associated declarative statements. Unlike traditional knowledge graphs with fixed schemas that focus on inter-entity relationships, this approach enhances the density of corresponding knowledge statements through reformulation while preserving critical information such as temporal markers, sources, and original stylistic features (Section 2.2.1). For example, web data containing "The number of tourist arrivals in France increased from 3,793 thousand in May 2025 to 4,222 thousand in June" becomes the entity-knowledge pair: ("France", "Tourist arrivals in France reached 4,222 thousand in June 2025"). Through continuous updates from search results and web access outcomes, both entities and their corresponding knowledge statements continuously expand, forming a living memory system whose content better aligns with the information distribution of the internet world.

**Phase 2** performs Multi-Style Question Synthesis by sampling entity clusters along with their associated knowledge statements to generate diverse questions spanning factual retrieval, numerical computation, multi-hop reasoning, and synthesis tasks. This transformation converts static knowledge into dynamic problem-solving contexts that necessitate active information retrieval, integration, and tool innovations. The paper's example using the entity "Paris" demonstrates how three news-sourced statements about Louvre visitor counts, bedbug outbreaks, and Airbus orders at the Paris Air Show combine into a single complex question requiring multi-hop reasoning to resolve (Section 2.2.1, Figure 3 example). Unlike WebSailor which requires explicit relationship construction between entities, this method leverages the richness of reformulated knowledge statements to create natural knowledge intersections, substantially improving the reliability and novelty of question generation.

FAS then generates two types of action synthesis from these questions. **Planning Action Synthesis** addresses the observation that initial analysis of complex problems by LLMs typically involves problem decomposition, information requirement identification, and solution planning—which inherently constitutes high-quality planning data. More importantly, the quality of first-step reasoning exhibits strong positive correlation with final task completion rates (Section 2.2.2). Inspired by multi-reference learning, FAS generates multiple reasoning-action data for each problem. Given a query $Q$, the system employs LLMs to generate $K$ diverse problem analyses along with their corresponding first-step action predictions (tool invocations or direct answers), as illustrated in Figure 4 (left). This approach yields two key advantages: it generates only reasoning chains and tool calls without incurring actual API invocation costs during training data production, and the $K$ distinct analytical perspectives effectively expand the action space exploration for each problem.

However, the authors recognize two limitations with generating $K$ iterations for a single question. The generated reasoning-action data may still exhibit similarity despite adjusting parameters like temperature to enhance diversity, and it leads to repetitive question text in training data, which is not the optimization target. To address these issues, the improved strategy generates reasoning-action data for $K$ different questions that share the same knowledge memory but differ in style. This approach better covers training contexts and explores the potential reasoning-action space more comprehensively, and the paper adopts this question-level diversity expansion as the planning action synthesis method in FAS (Section 2.2.2).

**Reasoning Action Synthesis** addresses a critical scenario in deep research agent workflows: how to effectively synthesize comprehensive information acquired through various tool invocations to generate final answers or reports. Unlike conventional mathematical numerical reasoning, this type of reasoning typically involves logic-based inference grounded in factual information, requiring a balance between divergent thinking and convergent thinking guided by contextual clues, while being difficult to verify through formal methods (Section 2.2.3). The paper proposes a two-step logical reasoning data synthesis scheme. In Step 1, the large language model decomposes the question $Q$ into multiple sub-questions, then leverages its internal knowledge to generate reasonable speculations and answers for each sub-question, producing preliminary answer $A_1$. In Step 2, given the question $Q$ and its mapped requisite knowledge, the model refines answer $A_1$, corrects logical errors, and generates the final answer $A_2$. Notably, the model is prohibited from invoking any external tools during both stages. The motivation behind this two-step design is that if both the question and necessary knowledge are directly provided, the model tends to mechanically utilize the given knowledge as intermediate reasoning nodes rather than simulating an authentic thinking process (Section 2.2.3).

A critical component of FAS is **Reject Sampling with Knowledge Alignment Verification**. Since the reasoning and action predictions are not always accurate and end-to-end trajectory signals are unavailable for verification, the paper implements reject sampling based on knowledge alignment verification. The system employs an LLM-as-Judge approach to evaluate whether the current reasoning and actions have a high probability of acquiring the required knowledge. This rejection sampling strategy effectively filters out the majority of low-quality data—while initial FAS generation yields balanced correct/incorrect trajectories (50%/50%), the filter removes 43.5% of problematic samples, increasing retained trajectory accuracy from 50% to 82% (Section B.1, Figure 9). Content Inconsistency accounts for 26.2% of rejections, followed by Search Necessity (6.9%) and Logic Discontinuity (5.7%), indicating FAS maintains structural validity while requiring refinement in semantic alignment.

#### Higher-order Action Synthesis (HAS): Transforming Trajectories into Decision Signals

HAS addresses a different scalability challenge: how to effectively reuse sub-optimal trajectories from post-training while maintaining training stability. During the post-training phase of agent models, both reject sampling fine-tuning and RL generate substantial volumes of trajectory data. However, these methods rely heavily on trajectory-level delayed feedback for quality assessment, which results in numerous trajectories being either discarded entirely or utilized only once when they fail to meet stringent quality thresholds (Section 2.3). This coarse-grained evaluation approach leads to significant waste of the learning signals embedded within real trajectories. While step-level evaluation could theoretically provide better leverage of these signals, precisely assessing intermediate steps remains challenging, and naively incorporating such uncertain reward signals into SFT or RL training risks model collapse.

The key insight behind HAS is recognizing that each step in a trajectory is supported by high-quality context including the original question, prior steps, and their real feedback. This context defines a distinct reasoning state with a broad space of feasible reasoning–action options. Thus, every step is fundamentally a hidden decision process (Section 2.3). However, although agents often generate multiple candidates within a single reasoning–action turn (e.g., alternative queries or exploration directions), these candidates remain internal branches of the same path, and supervision mainly rewards reproducing the full trajectory. Consequently, models learn to imitate a sequence rather than to perform decision-making at critical steps. HAS therefore shifts the objective from trajectory imitation to step-wise decision-making, explicitly exploiting the choice space at each step to move from trajectory reproduction to decision-based reasoning.

Given a problem $Q$ and an agent trajectory $T = \{(S_1, R_1), ..., (S_K, R_K)\}$, where $S_k$ represents the "reasoning and tool invocation" at the $k$-th step and $R_k$ denotes the corresponding tool/environment response, the entire trajectory carries a binary judgment $J \in \{0, 1\}$ indicating failure and success respectively. HAS comprises two components that transform this trajectory into richer training data (Section 2.3).

**Step-level Scaling** expands the reasoning-and-action option set at each step to fully explore the local action space. For any step $S_k$, the conditional context is denoted as $C_k = (Q, S_1, R_1, ..., S_{k-1}, R_{k-1})$. Without actual tool execution, LLMs generate $N$ alternative "thought and invocation" candidates for context $C_k$: $A_k = \{S^{(1)}_k, ..., S^{(N)}_k\}$. The original step $S^{(0)}_k \equiv S_k$ merges with these candidates to obtain $N + 1$ feasible steps, which are then randomly shuffled to form sequence $\tilde{A}_k$ while recording the original step's position $n_k$ in the sequence. This expansion explores different decision possibilities at the cognitive level, enriching the original trajectory into a decision space with $(N + 1) \times K$ potential reasoning-actions (Section 2.3).

**Contrastive Decision-Action Synthesis** transforms the trajectory with expanded options into a progressive decision-making process, as illustrated in Figure 5. Starting from problem $Q$ for each step, the system explicitly simulates a multi-option selection and decision process. For the $k$-th step, each option in $\tilde{A}_k$ is enumerated and a local action decision statement is inserted: "I will choose option $n_k$", immediately followed by the corresponding real response $R_k$. Finally, the judgment text is appended: "My decision is {Correct/Incorrect}" (corresponding to $J$). The complete synthetic training sample is obtained by concatenating the problem, the choice-decision process for each step, and the final judgment text (Section 2.3). This approach circumvents the risks associated with directly using uncertain step-level rewards while enabling the model to learn from diverse reasoning paths, thereby preventing overfitting to specific trajectory patterns. Through this synthesis strategy, previously underutilized trajectory data is transformed into rich training signals, significantly improving the sample efficiency of the agentic learning process.

#### Two-Stage Training Strategy: Progressive Context and Quality Scaling

The paper proposes a progressive two-stage training strategy to efficiently absorb the two types of synthesized agentic data. This design choice addresses a practical constraint: training all data at extended context lengths from the beginning would incur substantially higher computational costs while potentially destabilizing early learning phases (Section 3.4.1).

**Stage 1** primarily utilizes FAS data and short HAS data within a 32K context window, processing approximately 200 billion tokens. This stage enables the preliminary acquisition of agentic behaviors including tool invocation patterns and multi-step reasoning chains. The shorter context length allows for faster iteration and more stable gradient updates during the initial capability acquisition phase.

**Stage 2** focuses on high-quality HAS data with an extended 128K context length, processing approximately 100 billion tokens. This stage allows the LLM to develop a sophisticated understanding of complex action spaces and long-horizon planning strategies. The extended context is critical for deep research agents because agent trajectories are inherently long and complex—models must maintain coherence across many tool invocations and reasoning steps.

The ablation study in Table 4 validates this design choice. When comparing AgentFounder Stage 1 Only against AgentFounder Stage 1 & 2, the two-stage configuration achieves an average improvement of 3.3% on Pass@1 and 3.7% on Pass@3 across three evaluation tasks (BrowseComp-en, BrowseComp-zh, GAIA). Specifically, on BrowseComp-en, Pass@1 improves from 31.4% to 35.5% (+4.1%), and on BrowseComp-zh, Pass@3 improves from 50.5% to 58.5% (+8.0%). These results substantiate the necessity of learning complete long-context agent data rather than truncated sequences (Section 3.4.1).

#### Data Composition and Scaling Properties

The Agentic CPT corpus comprises a carefully curated heterogeneous mixture of four data types: high-quality web-crawled data filtered for factual accuracy, historical tool invocation records (e.g., search results and web page content), offline Wikipedia data, and mixed-quality discarded trajectories from previous post-training iterations (Section 3.1.1). This diversity matters because agentic capabilities are domain-agnostic abilities that transcend specific domains and enable universal tool utilization and multi-step reasoning. Since these abilities must function effectively across diverse application scenarios, the training data must span multiple domains to ensure broad transferability and applicability of the acquired skills (Section 2.2.1).

The scaling properties of Agentic CPT exhibit logarithmic characteristics, as shown in Figure 6(b). The relationship between training tokens and performance demonstrates that the most substantial improvements (3.8%) occur within the initial 15 billion tokens, indicating that agentic behaviors can be efficiently acquired through targeted pre-training. Stage 2 CPT with 128K context windows delivers notable gains at both 65 billion tokens (+1.8% over 50 billion) and 315 billion tokens (+1.0% over 210 billion), indicating that long-context training effectively enhances complex reasoning capabilities even when the base scaling curve approaches saturation. AgentFounder maintains consistent performance improvements throughout the entire 315 billion token budget, achieving a total gain of 8.0% (from 54.2% to 62.2% average Pass@3), validating the training methodology's effectiveness in preventing premature convergence (Section 3.5.2).

The contribution of different data types is examined in Table 5. Training exclusively with FAS data yields substantial performance gains, particularly evident in Pass@3 metrics. On BrowseComp-zh, FAS-based continual pre-training achieves a 9.0% improvement (from 29.8% to 37.0% Pass@1, and 45.3% to 54.3% Pass@3), establishing a higher performance ceiling for subsequent post-training phases. The combination of FAS+HAS data consistently delivers positive gains across evaluated benchmarks. While GAIA shows a modest 2.9% decrease in Pass@1 performance with FAS+HAS compared to FAS-only, the corresponding 1.9% improvement in Pass@3 suggests this variation falls within normal evaluation fluctuations rather than indicating systematic degradation (Section 3.4.2).

#### Design Choices and Alternative Considerations

Several critical design choices distinguish Agentic CPT from alternative approaches. **Offline synthesis without API calls** addresses the prohibitive cost barrier—commercial search and web access APIs would make generating hundreds of billions of tokens economically infeasible. By synthesizing planning and reasoning chains without actual tool execution during data generation, the methodology achieves scalability that trajectory-based approaches cannot match.

**Step-level expansion over trajectory-level imitation** addresses the sample efficiency problem. Traditional SFT and RL treat trajectories as monolithic units, discarding sub-optimal trajectories entirely. HAS transforms each step into a decision point with multiple options, extracting learning signals from trajectories that would otherwise be wasted. This design choice directly addresses the observation that "models learn to imitate a sequence rather than to perform decision-making at critical steps" (Section 2.3).

**Two-stage progressive training** balances computational efficiency with capability depth. Training all data at 128K context from the start would increase computational costs substantially while potentially destabilizing early learning. The progressive approach allows stable capability acquisition in Stage 1 before refining with long-context data in Stage 2. The ablation results confirm this matters—single-stage training with truncated sequences underperforms the two-stage approach by 3.3% on average Pass@1 (Section 3.4.1, Table 4).

**Reject sampling with LLM-as-Judge** addresses the verification challenge without ground-truth trajectory signals. Since end-to-end correctness signals are unavailable during CPT (unlike post-training where task success can be measured), the system uses knowledge alignment verification to filter low-quality synthetic data. The 43.5% rejection rate with accuracy improvement from 50% to 82% validates this approach (Section B.1).

The training loss evolution in Figure 7 demonstrates the effectiveness of these design choices. All AgentFounder variants achieve markedly lower loss values compared to the baseline across all metrics during SFT on identical downstream tasks. While the baseline reaches a final loss of 0.8656, the best-performing AgentFounder-30B (315B tokens) achieves 0.7953, demonstrating that agentic CPT effectively endows models with foundational capabilities that facilitate adaptation to downstream agent tasks. Loss values decrease progressively as CPT data volume increases from FAS-only to 315 billion tokens, and the FAS+HAS mixture outperforms FAS alone, validating that reorganizing supervision signals from post-training into CPT format strengthens the model's agentic foundation (Section 3.6.1).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the paper's most novel contributions, distinguishing between **fundamental paradigm shifts** and **incremental improvements**. We analyze what makes each contribution different from prior work and why it matters for the field of agentic AI development.

### 4.1 Agentic CPT as a Dedicated Intermediate Training Stage (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Makes It Novel:**

The insertion of Agentic Continual Pre-training as a **third stage** between pre-training and post-training represents a **architectural redefinition** of the LLM development pipeline, not merely an optimization within existing stages. Prior work—including WebSailor, WebShaper, AFM, and DeepDive (Section 4.1)—focused exclusively on improving post-training data quality or reinforcement learning strategies. Even GLM-4.5, which incorporates "synthetic agent trajectories during mid-training" (Section 1, footnote 1), does not systematize this as a dedicated stage with principled data synthesis methods.

**Why It Matters:**

This innovation addresses the **root cause** of agentic underperformance rather than its symptoms. The paper's core theoretical contribution is identifying that "general-purpose foundation models lack agentic inductive biases, forcing post-training to simultaneously learn capabilities and alignment, creating inherent optimization conflicts" (Section 1). By separating capability acquisition (Agentic CPT) from alignment refinement (post-training), the methodology resolves this tension.

The experimental validation in Figure 7 demonstrates this concretely: AgentFounder models achieve **lower SFT loss** than baseline models when fine-tuned on identical downstream tasks. The best-performing AgentFounder-30B (315B tokens) reaches 0.7953 final loss compared to the baseline's 0.8656—a **8.1% reduction** in loss that directly validates the pre-alignment hypothesis. This is not just a performance gain; it demonstrates that the **optimization landscape itself becomes more favorable** when models enter post-training with agentic foundations already in place.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:**

This is a **fundamental innovation** because it changes what questions researchers should ask. Instead of "How do we improve post-training for agentic tasks?", the paradigm shifts to "What capabilities should foundation models possess before post-training begins?" This reframing has implications beyond deep research agents—it suggests a general principle for any specialized capability requiring complex behavioral patterns.

### 4.2 Offline Agentic Data Synthesis at Scale (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Makes It Novel:**

First-order Action Synthesis (FAS) and Higher-order Action Synthesis (HAS) together enable the generation of **300 billion tokens** of agentic training data without external API costs. This addresses a practical bottleneck that has fundamentally constrained prior work. As noted in Section 2.2.2, "commercial API costs are prohibitively expensive, particularly for search engine APIs (e.g., Google Search API) and web access APIs (e.g., Jina Reader API and ScraperAPI)." Prior trajectory-based methods like WebSailor's SailorFog or WebExplorer's autonomous exploration require actual tool invocations during data generation, making them economically infeasible at the scale required for continual pre-training.

**Why It Matters:**

The scalability achievement is not merely about cost—it enables **empirical investigation of scaling laws for agentic capabilities**, which was previously impossible. Figure 6(b) shows performance scaling from 0B to 315B tokens, revealing that "the most substantial improvements (3.8%) occur within the initial 15B tokens" (Section 3.5.2). Without offline synthesis, researchers could not have collected enough data to observe these patterns.

The Entity-Anchored Open-World Knowledge Memory (Section 2.2.1) introduces a novel approach to knowledge representation that differs from traditional knowledge graphs. Unlike DBpedia or Wikidata which focus on inter-entity relationships with fixed schemas, this method "enhances the density of corresponding knowledge statements through reformulation, preserving critical information such as temporal markers, sources, and original stylistic features" (Section 2.2.1). This design choice matters because it better aligns with the information distribution of the internet world, where facts are often embedded in narrative contexts rather than structured relationships.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:**

This is a **fundamental innovation** in data methodology. Prior work improved data *quality* within the trajectory generation paradigm; FAS/HAS change the data *generation paradigm itself*. The ability to synthesize planning and reasoning chains without actual tool execution decouples data scale from economic constraints, enabling research questions that were previously unanswerable.

### 4.3 Step-Level Decision Synthesis Over Trajectory Imitation (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Makes It Novel:**

Higher-order Action Synthesis transforms how agent trajectories are used for training. Traditional SFT and RL treat trajectories as **monolithic units**—either the full trajectory succeeds (and is used for training) or it fails (and is discarded). HAS recognizes that "each step in a trajectory is supported by high-quality context including the original question, prior steps, and their real feedback" and that "every step is fundamentally a hidden decision process" (Section 2.3).

The key innovation is expanding each step $S_k$ into $N+1$ alternative options $\tilde{A}_k = \{S^{(0)}_k, S^{(1)}_k, ..., S^{(N)}_k\}$, then training the model to make explicit decisions: "I will choose option $n_k$" followed by "My decision is {Correct/Incorrect}" (Section 2.3). This transforms trajectory-level binary feedback into step-level decision signals.

**Why It Matters:**

This addresses the **sample efficiency problem** that plagues agentic RL. When trajectories are long (often 50+ tool calls for complex research tasks), trajectory-level feedback provides extremely sparse learning signals. A single incorrect step can invalidate an otherwise sound trajectory, causing valuable learning signals to be discarded. HAS extracts learning signals from sub-optimal trajectories by focusing on local decision quality rather than global trajectory success.

The Pass@N scaling results in Figure 10 (Appendix B.2) validate this approach: AgentFounder demonstrates "strong scaling characteristics with progressive performance improvements: from 31.5% Pass@1 to 75.8% Pass@16, yielding a substantial +44.3 percentage point gain" (Section B.2). This indicates that the model has learned **diverse solution strategies** rather than memorizing specific trajectories—a direct consequence of step-level decision training.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:**

This is a **fundamental innovation** in how we conceptualize agentic learning. The shift from "imitate successful trajectories" to "make good decisions at each step" changes the learning objective from behavioral cloning to decision-making competence. This has implications for robustness: models trained on diverse decision paths should generalize better to novel situations where memorized trajectories don't apply.

### 4.4 Demonstrated Scaling Laws for Agentic Capabilities (Theoretical Advance)

**What Makes It Novel:**

The paper provides the first systematic evidence that **agentic capabilities follow predictable scaling laws** with both model size and data volume. Figure 6(a) shows model scaling from 1B to 30B parameters, with average accuracy increasing from 20.4% to 48.9%. Figure 6(b) shows data scaling from 0B to 315B tokens, with average Pass@3 improving from 54.2% to 62.2%.

Prior work on agentic models reported point estimates of performance without establishing scaling relationships. This paper demonstrates that "logarithmic scaling law holds for agentic capabilities" (Section 3.5.2), providing a theoretical foundation for predicting how much data and compute are needed to achieve target performance levels.

**Why It Matters:**

Scaling laws enable **resource planning and research prioritization**. If agentic capabilities scale predictably, researchers can estimate the data and compute requirements for target performance levels rather than relying on trial-and-error. The observation that "Stage 2 CPT with 128K context windows delivers notable gains at both 65B (+1.8% over 50B) and 315B (+1.0% over 210B)" (Section 3.5.2) indicates that long-context training provides diminishing but consistent returns even as the base scaling curve approaches saturation.

The model scaling results also reveal an efficiency advantage: "AgentFounder-30B model achieves 48.9% accuracy, exceeding the performance of two larger baseline models, DeepSeek-V3.1 (43.0%) and Kimi-K2 (29.6%), despite their greater size" (Section 3.5.1). This suggests that Agentic CPT enables **more effective utilization of model capacity** for agentic tasks—a form of compute efficiency that matters for practical deployment.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:**

This is a **theoretical advance** rather than a methodological innovation. Establishing scaling laws transforms agentic model development from an empirical craft into a more predictable engineering discipline. However, it depends on the prior innovations (offline synthesis, step-level decision training) that made collecting sufficient data possible.

### 4.5 Pre-Alignment Reduces Downstream Fine-Tuning Burden (Validated Hypothesis)

**What Makes It Novel:**

The paper empirically validates that models with Agentic CPT require **less post-training optimization** to achieve target performance. Table 3 shows that AgentFounder-30B-Base consistently outperforms Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base across all three SFT configurations (SFT-A, SFT-B, SFT-C), with average gains of 5.75%, 6.13%, and 6.45% respectively (Section 3.3).

More significantly, the adaptability results show that "post-training data remains crucial for unlocking base model capabilities" (Section 3.3). Despite sharing the same AgentFounder-30B-Base foundation, models exhibit significant performance variations across different post-training datasets. On BrowseComp-zh, AgentFounder-30B with SFT-B outperforms variants with SFT-A and SFT-C by 8.5% and 3.0% respectively.

**Why It Matters:**

This finding clarifies the **relationship between foundation capabilities and post-training optimization**. Agentic CPT does not eliminate the need for post-training; rather, it raises the performance ceiling that post-training can achieve. The observation that "information retrieval tasks benefit more from Agentic CPT than knowledge-intensive tasks" (Section 3.3)—with BrowseComp showing more pronounced improvements than HLE—suggests that different capability domains may require different pre-alignment strategies.

The MoE activation analysis in Figure 12 (Appendix B.4) provides a mechanistic explanation: "after undergoing CPT, the expert distribution within the MoE architecture becomes more balanced in the final layers of the model, rather than being concentrated" (Section B.4). This balanced activation "helps mitigate the risk of overfitting by preventing the phenomenon of 'dead experts'"—experts that cease to be activated during training. This suggests Agentic CPT improves not just performance but **training stability**.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:**

This is a **validated hypothesis** that confirms the theoretical framework underlying Agentic CPT. While not a methodological innovation itself, it provides crucial evidence that the paradigm shift is sound. The finding that post-training data quality remains important (Section 3.3, Observation 2) prevents overclaiming—Agentic CPT is a foundation enhancer, not a post-training replacement.

### Summary: Distinguishing Innovation Types

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| Agentic CPT as intermediate stage | Fundamental | Redefines training pipeline architecture |
| Offline data synthesis (FAS/HAS) | Fundamental | Enables scaling previously economically infeasible |
| Step-level decision synthesis | Fundamental | Changes learning objective from imitation to decision-making |
| Agentic scaling laws | Theoretical | Enables predictable resource planning |
| Pre-alignment validation | Empirical | Confirms theoretical framework |

The first three contributions represent **paradigm shifts** that change how researchers should approach agentic model development. The scaling laws provide a **theoretical foundation** for future work, while the pre-alignment validation offers **empirical confirmation** that the paradigm shift is sound. Together, these contributions establish Agentic CPT not as an incremental improvement to existing methods, but as a **new foundation** for building deep research agents.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their claims about Agentic CPT and AgentFounder-30B. We will walk through the evaluation methodology, analyze the quantitative results with specific numbers, assess whether the experiments support the paper's claims, and examine ablation studies that isolate the contribution of each component.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Benchmark Suite

The paper evaluates across **10 benchmarks** organized into two categories (Section 3.1.3):

**General Web Search Benchmarks (5 benchmarks):**
- **BrowseComp-en**: English web browsing and reasoning tasks (Wei et al., 2025)
- **BrowseComp-zh**: Chinese web browsing and reasoning tasks (Zhou et al., 2025b)
- **GAIA**: General AI assistant benchmark, text-only subset of 103 questions (Mialon et al., 2023)
- **Xbench-DeepSearch**: Deep search capability evaluation (Xbench-Team, 2025)
- **WebWalkerQA**: Web traversal benchmark (Wu et al., 2025b)

**Scenario-Targeted Web Search Benchmarks (5 benchmarks):**
- **HLE (Humanity's Last Exam)**: Expert-level questions across diverse subjects, measured as Pass@1 (Phan et al., 2025)
- **DeepResearch Bench**: Comprehensive research report generation across academic fields, measured as RACE Overall (Du et al., 2025)
- **Frames**: Multi-perspective reasoning and role-based information synthesis (Krishna et al., 2024)
- **SEAL-0**: Model robustness when facing conflicting or misleading search results (Pham et al., 2025)
- **Academic Browse**: Scholarly research capabilities including literature navigation (Zhou et al., 2025a)

This benchmark selection is comprehensive—it covers both **general retrieval tasks** (BrowseComp, GAIA) and **specialized research scenarios** (HLE, Academic Browse), allowing the authors to test whether Agentic CPT produces broadly capable agents or narrowly specialized ones.

#### Baseline Models

The paper compares against **three categories** of strong agent models (Section 3.1.2):

**General LLMs with Tools:**
- Qwen3-30B-A3B-2507, Qwen3-235B-A22B-2507
- DeepSeek-R1-0528
- Claude-4-Sonnet

**Commercial Deep Research Agents:**
- Kimi-Researcher, OpenAI-o3, OpenAI Deep Research
- Grok Deeper Search, Perplexity Deep Research, Gemini Deep Research

**Open-Source Deep Research Agents:**
- WebSailor-72B, GLM-4.5, DeepSeek-V3.1
- WebThinker-32B-RL, AFM-32B-RL, MiroThinker-32B-DPOv0.2
- DeepDiver-V2-38B, WebExplorer-8B, DeepDive-32B, Kimi-K2

The baseline selection is **strategically important**. By including both open-source and closed-source models, the authors can position AgentFounder relative to the full competitive landscape. Notably, they prioritize official results reported by model providers or benchmark organizers, evaluating remaining models under their standardized setup (Section 3.1.2).

#### Tool Configuration and Inference Parameters

All evaluated models are equipped with **five core tools** (Section 3.1.4, Appendix A.1):

| Tool | Function |
|------|----------|
| Search | Google search engine, returns top-10 ranked results per query |
| Visit | Webpage content extraction via Jina Reader with goal-specific summarization |
| Google Scholar | Academic literature access |
| Python Interpreter | Code execution in sandboxed environment |
| File Parser | Document processing (PDF, DOCX, MP4) with text conversion |

For AgentFounder inference, the authors employ specific hyperparameters to ensure stable and reproducible results (Section 3.1.4):
- **Temperature**: 0.85
- **Repetition penalty**: 1.1
- **Top-p**: 0.95
- **Maximum tool calls**: 128 per task
- **Context length**: 128K tokens

These settings matter because agentic performance is sensitive to inference configuration. A temperature that is too low may reduce exploration diversity, while too high may introduce instability in multi-step reasoning.

#### Evaluation Metrics

The paper primarily uses **Pass@1 accuracy** for most benchmarks, with **Pass@3** reported in ablation studies (Tables 4, 5). Pass@1 measures whether the model produces the correct answer on its first attempt, while Pass@3 measures whether the correct answer appears within three sampled trajectories. For DeepResearch Bench, the metric is **RACE Overall**, which evaluates report comprehensiveness, readability, and depth (Section 3.2).

### 5.3 Ablation Studies

The paper conducts four ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component.

#### RQ2: Adaptability to Post-Training Methods (Table 3)

This experiment tests whether Agentic CPT produces a foundation model that adapts well to different post-training strategies. The authors fine-tune AgentFounder-30B-Base with three SFT configurations:

- **SFT-A**: Two-stage training (general conversational data → React-style agent trajectories)
- **SFT-B**: Two-stage training with balanced mixture of conversational and React data in each stage
- **SFT-C**: Two-stage training with general conversational SFT and React with summarized reasoning

Table 3 shows the results:

| Base Model | SFT Data | BrowseComp-en | BrowseComp-zh | GAIA | HLE |
|------------|----------|---------------|---------------|------|-----|
| Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base | SFT-A | 26.9 | 29.8 | 67.0 | 23.5 |
| AgentFounder-30B-Base | SFT-A | **31.4** | **35.6** | **72.8** | **30.4** |
| **Relative Δ** | SFT-A | **+4.5** | **+5.8** | **+5.8** | **+6.9** |
| Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base | SFT-B | 28.6 | 35.6 | 71.8 | 27.0 |
| AgentFounder-30B-Base | SFT-B | **39.9** | **43.3** | **72.8** | **31.5** |
| **Relative Δ** | SFT-B | **+11.3** | **+7.7** | **+1.0** | **+4.5** |
| Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base | SFT-C | 24.5 | 36.7 | 68.9 | 27.9 |

**Key Finding 1: Agentic CPT demonstrates consistent improvements across all SFT configurations.** Models fine-tuned on AgentFounder-30B-Base consistently outperform their Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base counterparts, with average performance gains of 5.75% (SFT-A), 6.13% (SFT-B), and 6.45% (SFT-C) (Section 3.3). This validates that Agentic CPT produces a **universally beneficial foundation**, not one tied to a specific post-training method.

**Key Finding 2: Post-training data remains crucial.** Despite sharing the same AgentFounder-30B-Base foundation, models exhibit significant performance variations across different SFT datasets. On BrowseComp-zh, AgentFounder-30B with SFT-B (43.3%) outperforms variants with SFT-A (35.6%) and SFT-C (44.3%) by 8.5% and 3.0% respectively (Section 3.3). This prevents overclaiming—Agentic CPT enhances the foundation but does not eliminate the need for quality post-training data.

**Key Finding 3: Information retrieval tasks benefit more than knowledge-intensive tasks.** BrowseComp benchmarks show more pronounced improvements from AgentFounder-30B-Base compared to HLE. The authors hypothesize that "knowledge-intensive tasks like HLE require not only successful information retrieval but also strong reasoning capabilities to correctly utilize the retrieved knowledge" (Section 3.3). This suggests Agentic CPT primarily enhances **retrieval and planning capabilities**, with reasoning improvements being secondary.

This ablation tests whether the progressive two-stage training (32K → 128K context) provides benefits over single-stage training:

The two-stage configuration achieves an **average improvement of 3.3% on Pass@1 and 3.7% on Pass@3** across the three evaluation tasks (Section 3.4.1). The most notable gain is on BrowseComp-zh Pass@3 (+8.0%), suggesting that extended context particularly benefits **multi-turn exploration in non-English contexts**.

The authors note that "resource constraints preclude evaluation of single-stage training with extended context lengths (e.g., 128K)" but such approaches "would incur substantially higher computational costs" (Section 3.4.1). This is a practical limitation—the ablation compares two-stage vs. single-stage with truncated data, not two-stage vs. single-stage at full context.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

#### Strengths of the Experimental Design

**Comprehensive benchmark coverage.** The 10-benchmark suite spans general retrieval (BrowseComp, GAIA), specialized research (HLE, Academic Browse), and robustness (SEAL-0, Frames). This breadth prevents narrow optimization on a single metric.

**Strong baselines.** The paper compares against both open-source and closed-source models, including state-of-the-art commercial products (OpenAI-o3, OpenAI Deep Research, Gemini Deep Research). This positions AgentFounder within the full competitive landscape.

**Multiple ablation studies.** The four research questions (RQ2-RQ5) systematically isolate the contribution of Agentic CPT, two-stage training, data types, and scaling properties. This provides evidence for each component's necessity.

**Mechanistic analyses.** The MoE activation patterns (Figure 12), tool call distributions (Figure 8), and training loss convergence (Figure 7) offer insights into *why* Agentic CPT works, not just *that* it works.

#### Limitations and Caveats

**BrowseComp-zh performance gap.** AgentFounder underperforms DeepSeek-V3.1 and OpenAI-o3 on the Chinese benchmark (43.3% vs. 49.2% and 58.1%). The authors acknowledge this may reflect "the relatively limited proportion of Chinese data in our training corpus" (Section 3.2). This reveals a **language coverage limitation**—Agentic CPT's effectiveness depends on training data diversity.

**Single-stage 128K ablation not feasible.** The two-stage ablation (Table 4) compares two-stage training against single-stage with truncated data, not single-stage at full 128K context. The authors note "resource constraints preclude evaluation of single-stage training with extended context lengths" (Section 3.4.1). This leaves open whether the gains come from the progressive strategy itself or simply from having more long-context data.

**GAIA text-only subset.** The GAIA results (72.8%) are "limited to GAIA's text subset consisting of 103 questions" (Section 3.2, footnote 3). The full GAIA benchmark includes multi-modal tasks, so the reported score may not generalize to the complete benchmark.

**Pass@1 vs. Pass@3 trade-off with HAS.** Table 5 shows that adding HAS to FAS slightly decreases GAIA Pass@1 (72.8% → 69.9%) while improving Pass@3 (80.6% → 82.5%). The authors attribute this to "normal evaluation fluctuations" (Section 3.4.2), but it could indicate that HAS improves exploration diversity at the cost of first-attempt precision.

**Compute costs not reported.** While the paper emphasizes that offline synthesis avoids API costs, it does not report the computational cost of generating 300B tokens of synthetic data or training AgentFounder. This makes it difficult to assess the total resource requirements for reproducing the approach.

#### Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: Agentic CPT produces superior agentic foundation models.** **Supported.** Table 3 shows consistent improvements across all SFT configurations (average gains of 5.75%-6.45%), and Figure 7 demonstrates lower SFT loss (0.7953 vs. 0.8656). The evidence is robust.

**Claim 2: AgentFounder achieves state-of-the-art performance.** **Partially supported.** AgentFounder leads on 7 of 10 benchmarks (GAIA, Xbench-DeepSearch, WebWalkerQA, HLE, DeepResearch Bench, Frames, SEAL-0, Academic Browse) but trails on BrowseComp-en (39.9% vs. OpenAI-o3 49.7%) and BrowseComp-zh (43.3% vs. OpenAI-o3 58.1%). The SOTA claim holds for open-source models but not against all closed-source competitors.

**Claim 3: Agentic capabilities follow scaling laws.** **Supported.** Figure 6 shows clear logarithmic scaling with both model size (20.4% → 48.9% from 1B to 30B) and data volume (54.2% → 62.2% from 0B to 315B tokens). The scaling relationship is well-documented.

**Claim 4: Offline synthesis enables scalable data generation.** **Supported.** The paper generates 300B tokens without external API calls, and Figure 9 validates that reject sampling achieves 82% accuracy on retained data. The scalability claim is substantiated.

**Claim 5: Two-stage training is necessary.** **Partially supported.** Table 4 shows 3.3% average Pass@1 improvement with two-stage training, but the ablation does not compare against single-stage training at full 128K context. The benefit is demonstrated, but the necessity relative to alternative configurations remains open.

### 5.6 Summary of Experimental Findings

The experimental analysis provides **substantial evidence** for the effectiveness of Agentic CPT, with several important caveats:

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Key Supporting Data |
|-------|------------------|---------------------|
| Agentic CPT improves foundation models | Strong | Table 3 (consistent gains across SFT configs), Figure 7 (lower SFT loss) |
| AgentFounder achieves SOTA | Moderate-Strong | Tables 1-2 (leads on 7/10 benchmarks), trails on BrowseComp |
| Scaling laws hold | Strong | Figure 6 (clear logarithmic trends for model and data scaling) |
| Offline synthesis is viable | Strong | Figure 9 (82% accuracy after filtering), 300B tokens generated |
| Two-stage training helps | Moderate | Table 4 (3.3% average gain), but incomplete ablation |
| General tool-use preserved | Moderate | Table 6 (70.0 vs. 67.2 on ACEBench) |

The experiments are **comprehensive and well-designed**, with appropriate baselines, multiple ablation studies, and mechanistic analyses. The main limitations are the BrowseComp-zh performance gap (revealing language coverage constraints) and the incomplete two-stage ablation (leaving open whether progressive training is strictly necessary vs. simply more compute-efficient).

Overall, the experimental evidence **convincingly supports** the paper's core thesis: that Agentic CPT produces pre-aligned agentic foundation models that enhance downstream fine-tuning and enable state-of-the-art performance on deep research benchmarks.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the paper presents compelling evidence for Agentic CPT's effectiveness, a critical examination reveals several important limitations, assumptions, and unresolved trade-offs. Understanding these constraints is essential for researchers attempting to reproduce or extend this work.

### 6.1 Language and Data Coverage Assumptions

**The Approach Assumes Diverse, Multi-Lingual Training Data**

Agentic CPT's effectiveness is contingent on the diversity of the underlying training corpus. This assumption is directly challenged by the **BrowseComp-zh performance gap**. As shown in Table 1, AgentFounder-30B achieves 43.3% on BrowseComp-zh, trailing both DeepSeek-V3.1 (49.2%) and OpenAI-o3 (58.1%). The authors explicitly acknowledge this limitation:

> "We attribute this performance gap to two potential reasons: the relatively limited proportion of Chinese data in our training corpus, and the possibility that the underlying search tool (Google Search) may exhibit suboptimal performance or bias in Chinese contexts" (Section 3.2).

This reveals a fundamental constraint: **Agentic CPT amplifies existing data distribution biases** rather than correcting them. If the entity-anchored knowledge memory (Section 2.2.1) contains disproportionately English-language sources, the synthesized questions and reasoning chains will inherit this bias. The method assumes that static knowledge sources (CommonCrawl, Wikipedia, discarded trajectories) provide adequate coverage across target languages and domains—an assumption that may not hold for low-resource languages or specialized domains.

**Implication for Practitioners:** Organizations seeking to apply Agentic CPT must invest in curating linguistically and domain-diverse knowledge sources. The offline synthesis approach reduces API costs but does not eliminate the need for high-quality, representative data collection.

### 6.2 Incomplete Ablation of Training Strategy

**The Two-Stage Benefit May Reflect Compute Efficiency, Not Algorithmic Necessity**

The paper claims the progressive two-stage training strategy (32K → 128K context) provides benefits over single-stage training. Table 4 shows average improvements of 3.3% on Pass@1 and 3.7% on Pass@3 with the two-stage approach. However, the authors explicitly note a critical limitation:

> "While resource constraints preclude evaluation of single-stage training with extended context lengths (e.g., 128K), such approaches would incur substantially higher computational costs" (Section 3.4.1).

This means the ablation compares:
- **Two-stage**: 32K context (Stage 1) + 128K context (Stage 2)
- **Single-stage baseline**: 32K context with truncated long sequences

The experiment does **not** compare against single-stage training at full 128K context. Consequently, we cannot determine whether the observed gains stem from:
1. The progressive curriculum itself (easier examples first, harder examples later)
2. Simply having access to complete long-context data (which the single-stage baseline lacks due to truncation)
3. Computational efficiency allowing more total training steps within budget

**Trade-off:** The two-stage approach is demonstrably more compute-efficient, but its algorithmic necessity remains unproven. Organizations with sufficient computational resources might achieve comparable results with single-stage 128K training, potentially simplifying the pipeline.

### 6.3 Benchmark Scope Limitations

**GAIA Results Apply Only to Text-Only Subset**

The reported GAIA score of 72.8% comes with an important caveat stated in footnote 3 of Section 3.2:

> "We use the text-only subset consisting of 103 questions."

The full GAIA benchmark includes multi-modal tasks requiring image understanding, file manipulation, and cross-modal reasoning. AgentFounder's tool set (Section 3.1.4, Appendix A.1) includes a File Parser that can process PDF, DOCX, and MP4 files, but the evaluation does not test these capabilities on GAIA's multi-modal questions.

**Implication:** The claim that AgentFounder "extends beyond retrieval reasoning itself and can transfer to broader task categories" (Section 3.2) is supported only for text-based tasks. Multi-modal agentic capabilities remain unvalidated.

**HLE and Academic Browse May Not Generalize to All Expert Domains**

While AgentFounder achieves 31.5% on HLE and 75.3% on Academic Browse, these benchmarks sample from specific subject distributions. The paper does not report performance breakdowns by subject area (e.g., mathematics vs. humanities vs. sciences). Given that the knowledge memory construction relies on web-crawled data and Wikipedia (Section 3.1.1), domains with sparse online presence (e.g., specialized medical knowledge, proprietary industry data) may see degraded performance.

### 6.4 Exploration vs. Precision Trade-off with HAS

**Higher-order Action Synthesis May Reduce First-Attempt Accuracy**

Table 5 reveals a nuanced trade-off that the paper acknowledges but does not fully resolve:

| Data Configuration | GAIA Pass@1 | GAIA Pass@3 |
|-------------------|-------------|-------------|
| FAS-only (50B tokens) | 72.8% | 80.6% |
| FAS+HAS (50B tokens) | 69.9% | 82.5% |

Adding HAS data decreases Pass@1 by 2.9% while improving Pass@3 by 1.9%. The authors characterize this as "normal evaluation fluctuations rather than indicating systematic degradation" (Section 3.4.2). However, this pattern appears across multiple benchmarks and suggests a genuine trade-off:

- **HAS improves exploration diversity**: The step-level expansion teaches models to consider multiple options at each decision point, enhancing the ability to find correct answers across multiple samples (Pass@3).
- **HAS may reduce decision confidence**: Exposure to multiple alternative actions at each step could make the model less decisive on first attempts, slightly reducing Pass@1 accuracy.

**Design Decision:** Practitioners must choose between optimizing for single-attempt success (favor FAS-heavy training) or exploration robustness (include more HAS data). The paper does not provide guidance on how to tune this balance for specific deployment scenarios.

### 6.5 Computational Costs Not Reported

**Offline Synthesis Avoids API Costs But Incurs Significant Compute**

The paper emphasizes that FAS and HAS operate "without external tool invocations, enabling large-scale data generation in offline environments without API costs" (Section 1). This is a genuine advantage over trajectory-based methods requiring search API calls.

However, the paper **does not report**:
- The computational cost (GPU hours, FLOPs) of generating 300 billion tokens of synthetic data
- The training compute required for Agentic CPT (200B + 100B tokens at 32K/128K context)
- The total cost comparison against post-training-only approaches

Generating 300B tokens of synthetic data requires running large language models for inference at massive scale. Even without API costs, the electricity and hardware depreciation represent substantial expenses. Similarly, training at 128K context length incurs significantly higher memory and compute requirements than 32K training.

**Missing Information:** Without compute budgets, practitioners cannot assess whether Agentic CPT is feasible for their resources. A method that requires 10× the compute of post-training-only approaches may be prohibitively expensive for most organizations, regardless of API cost savings.

### 6.6 Dependency on LLM-as-Judge Quality

**Reject Sampling Quality Is Bounded by Judge Model Capabilities**

The FAS filtering mechanism relies on an LLM-as-Judge approach to evaluate whether reasoning and actions "have a high probability of acquiring the required knowledge" (Section 2.2.2). Figure 9 (Appendix B.1) shows this filter improves retained data accuracy from 50% to 82% by removing 43.5% of samples.

However, this introduces a **dependency chain**:
1. Synthetic data quality depends on the generator LLM's capabilities
2. Filter effectiveness depends on the judge LLM's capabilities
3. Both generator and judge must be more capable than the target model to provide meaningful supervision

If the judge model has blind spots (e.g., cannot detect subtle logical errors, misses domain-specific inaccuracies), low-quality data will pass through the filter. The paper does not specify which model serves as the judge or evaluate judge accuracy against human annotations.

**Risk:** Systematic judge errors could introduce correlated biases into the training data that are difficult to detect and correct. This is particularly concerning for domains where the judge model itself lacks expertise.

### 6.7 Simulation-to-Reality Gap in Data Synthesis

**Offline Synthesis Cannot Capture Real Tool Behavior**

Both FAS and HAS generate training data "without actual tool execution" (Section 2.2.2, 2.3). While this enables scalability, it creates a potential **simulation-to-reality gap**:

- **Search results vary over time**: Synthetic data uses static knowledge snapshots, but real search engines return dynamic, time-sensitive results
- **Tool failures are not modeled**: Real APIs experience rate limiting, timeouts, and partial failures—none of which appear in synthetic trajectories
- **Web page structure diversity**: The Visit tool's extraction quality depends on actual page layouts, which synthetic data cannot fully represent

The introduction mentions that agentic alignment requires "adaptive responses to unpredictable environmental changes (e.g., tool failure, misleading information)" (Section 1). However, the synthetic data generation process does not explicitly simulate these failure modes.

**Open Question:** Does training on clean, synthetic trajectories reduce robustness to real-world tool failures? The SEAL-0 benchmark (43.9% score) tests robustness to conflicting search results, but does not evaluate resilience to API failures or malformed responses.

### 6.8 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

**Multi-Agent Coordination Is Not Evaluated**

Section 4.1 mentions multi-agent frameworks (e.g., Cognitive Kernel-Pro, Tencent's multi-agent system) as a separate research direction. AgentFounder is evaluated exclusively under the "single-agent ReAct paradigm" (Section 3.2). The Agentic CPT methodology does not include:
- Inter-agent communication patterns
- Task decomposition across multiple agents
- Consensus mechanisms for conflicting agent outputs

Organizations seeking to build multi-agent research systems cannot directly apply these findings without additional investigation.

**Catastrophic Forgetting Not Measured**

The paper evaluates general tool-use capability on ACEBench (Table 6), showing improvement from 67.2% to 70.0%. However, this does not assess whether Agentic CPT degrades:
- General language understanding (e.g., MMLU, GSM8K)
- Conversational abilities
- Code generation capabilities

Continual pre-training risks catastrophic forgetting of capabilities acquired during initial pre-training. The paper does not report benchmark scores on non-agentic tasks before and after Agentic CPT.

**Real-Time Adaptation Not Tested**

The introduction states that agentic alignment encompasses "adaptive responses to unpredictable environmental changes" (Section 1). However, all evaluations use static benchmarks with fixed questions and tool configurations. The paper does not test:
- Online learning from user feedback during deployment
- Adaptation to newly released tools or APIs
- Recovery from novel failure modes not seen during training

### 6.9 Summary of Key Trade-offs

| Trade-off | Description | Evidence |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Language Coverage vs. Performance** | Agentic CPT amplifies training data distribution biases | BrowseComp-zh underperformance (Section 3.2) |
| **Compute Efficiency vs. Algorithmic Necessity** | Two-stage training is efficient but not proven necessary | Incomplete 128K single-stage ablation (Section 3.4.1) |
| **Exploration vs. Precision** | HAS improves Pass@3 but may reduce Pass@1 | Table 5 GAIA results (Section 3.4.2) |
| **API Cost Savings vs. Compute Costs** | Offline synthesis avoids API costs but incurs substantial compute | Compute budgets not reported (Section 1, 2) |
| **Scalability vs. Realism** | Synthetic data scales but may not capture real tool behaviors | No tool failure simulation (Section 2.2.2, 2.3) |
| **Specialization vs. General Capabilities** | Agentic CPT enhances agent tasks but forgetting not measured | ACEBench improvement shown, general benchmarks not reported (Section 3.6.3) |

### 6.10 Open Questions for Future Research

Based on the identified limitations, several important questions remain unresolved:

1. **What is the minimum data diversity required** for Agentic CPT to generalize across languages and domains? Can targeted domain adaptation during Agentic CPT address coverage gaps?

2. **Is progressive context scaling algorithmically necessary**, or does it simply provide compute efficiency? A complete ablation with single-stage 128K training would clarify this.

3. **How should practitioners tune the FAS/HAS balance** for their specific use cases? Guidance on optimizing for Pass@1 vs. Pass@3 would aid deployment decisions.

4. **What are the total compute requirements** for reproducing Agentic CPT? Transparent cost reporting would enable resource planning for the research community.

5. **Does Agentic CPT introduce catastrophic forgetting** on non-agentic tasks? Comprehensive benchmarking before and after CPT would address this concern.

6. **How can real-world tool failures be incorporated** into synthetic data generation? Simulating API errors and dynamic content would improve robustness.

These open questions represent opportunities for the research community to build upon the Agentic CPT foundation while addressing its current limitations.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how Agentic CPT reshapes the agentic AI development landscape, identifies concrete research opportunities it unlocks, outlines practical deployment scenarios, and provides guidance for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this approach.

### 7.1 Paradigm Shift in Agentic Model Development

**From Post-Training Optimization to Foundation Engineering**

This work fundamentally repositions where researchers should focus their efforts when building agentic systems. Prior to this paper, the dominant assumption was that agentic capabilities emerge primarily through post-training techniques—better SFT data, improved RL algorithms, or more sophisticated reward models. The paper demonstrates that this assumption is incomplete:

> "General-purpose foundation models lack agentic inductive biases, forcing post-training to simultaneously learn capabilities and alignment, creating inherent optimization conflicts" (Section 1).

By introducing Agentic CPT as a dedicated intermediate stage, the work shifts the research question from *"How do we fine-tune better?"* to *"What capabilities should foundation models possess before fine-tuning begins?"* This has three concrete implications:

| Prior Focus | New Focus Enabled by Agentic CPT |
|-------------|----------------------------------|
| Post-training data quality | Pre-training data distribution for agentic behaviors |
| RL reward design | Step-level decision synthesis during CPT |
| Trajectory-level supervision | Offline synthesis of planning and reasoning chains |
| Single-stage fine-tuning | Progressive curriculum (32K → 128K context) |

**Opening Scaling Law Research for Agentic Capabilities**

Perhaps the most significant theoretical contribution is establishing that **agentic capabilities follow predictable scaling laws**. Figure 6 demonstrates logarithmic scaling with both model size (20.4% → 48.9% from 1B to 30B parameters) and data volume (54.2% → 62.2% from 0B to 315B tokens). Before this work, researchers could not systematically study agentic scaling because:

1. Trajectory-based data generation was prohibitively expensive at scale
2. No standardized intermediate training stage existed for capability acquisition
3. Performance variance across post-training methods obscured underlying scaling relationships

The offline synthesis methodology (FAS and HAS) removes the economic barrier, enabling the research community to investigate questions like:
- What is the data efficiency frontier for agentic capabilities?
- Do different agentic skills (planning vs. reasoning vs. tool use) scale differently?
- Is there a "chinchua-optimal" token-to-parameter ratio for agentic models?

**Democratizing Deep Research Agent Development**

The performance gap between open-source and closed-source agents has been a persistent barrier to innovation. Table 1 and Table 2 show AgentFounder-30B surpassing commercial products on 7 of 10 benchmarks, including:
- **HLE**: 31.5% vs. OpenAI Deep Research's 26.6% (first open-source model >30%)
- **Frames**: 89.6% vs. OpenAI-o3's 84.0% (+5.9 percentage points)
- **GAIA**: 72.8% vs. OpenAI-o3's 70.5% (+2.3 percentage points)

This demonstrates that **open-source implementations can achieve competitive performance** without proprietary infrastructure or closed data. The key enabler is the offline synthesis approach—organizations can generate hundreds of billions of tokens of agentic training data without relying on expensive search APIs or proprietary trajectory collections.

### 7.2 Concrete Follow-up Research Directions

Based on the limitations identified in Section 6 and the innovations introduced in this work, several high-value research directions emerge:

#### 7.2.1 Multi-Lingual and Domain-Specialized Agentic CPT

**Problem:** The BrowseComp-zh performance gap (43.3% vs. DeepSeek-V3.1's 49.2%) reveals that Agentic CPT amplifies training data distribution biases (Section 3.2).

**Research Questions:**
- What is the minimum proportion of non-English data required in the entity-anchored knowledge memory to achieve parity across languages?
- Can domain-specific Agentic CPT (e.g., medical, legal, scientific) outperform general Agentic CPT for specialized tasks?
- How should the FAS question synthesis phase be modified to ensure cultural and linguistic diversity?

**Suggested Approach:** Extend Phase 1 of FAS (Section 2.2.1) to construct language-specific entity memories with balanced coverage. Evaluate on language-specific benchmarks beyond BrowseComp-zh (e.g., Spanish, Arabic, Japanese equivalents).

#### 7.2.2 Real-World Tool Failure Simulation

**Problem:** Synthetic data generation operates "without actual tool execution" (Section 2.2.2), creating a simulation-to-reality gap. Real APIs experience rate limiting, timeouts, and malformed responses that synthetic trajectories do not capture.

**Research Questions:**
- Does training on clean synthetic trajectories reduce robustness to real-world tool failures?
- Can we inject realistic failure modes (timeouts, partial responses, API errors) into HAS data without compromising training stability?
- What is the optimal ratio of "clean" to "noisy" trajectories for maximizing real-world robustness?

**Suggested Approach:** Modify the HAS step-level scaling (Section 2.3) to include failure branches. For each step $S_k$, generate alternative outcomes including tool errors, then train the model to recover: "I will choose option $n_k$" → "Tool returned error: [X]" → "Recovery action: [Y]".

#### 7.2.3 Multi-Agent Coordination During CPT

**Problem:** AgentFounder is evaluated exclusively under the "single-agent ReAct paradigm" (Section 3.2). Multi-agent frameworks (e.g., Cognitive Kernel-Pro, Tencent's multi-agent system) represent a growing research direction that Agentic CPT does not address (Section 4.1).

**Research Questions:**
- Can Agentic CPT be extended to train inter-agent communication patterns?
- Should multi-agent coordination be learned during CPT or reserved for post-training?
- Does step-level decision synthesis (HAS) generalize to multi-agent decision points?

**Suggested Approach:** Extend HAS to generate multi-agent dialogue trajectories where each agent's decision point becomes a training signal. The conditional context $C_k$ would include prior agent messages, not just prior steps.

#### 7.2.4 Catastrophic Forgetting Measurement and Mitigation

**Problem:** The paper evaluates general tool-use on ACEBench (Table 6: 70.0 vs. 67.2) but does not assess whether Agentic CPT degrades general language understanding, conversational abilities, or code generation (Section 6.8).

**Research Questions:**
- What is the forgetting rate on non-agentic benchmarks (MMLU, GSM8K, HumanEval) after Agentic CPT?
- Can replay buffers from the original pre-training corpus mitigate forgetting?
- Is there a trade-off between agentic capability acquisition and general capability retention?

**Suggested Approach:** Evaluate AgentFounder-Base on a comprehensive suite of non-agentic benchmarks before and after Agentic CPT. Compare forgetting rates against standard continual pre-training baselines.

#### 7.2.5 Compute-Efficient Variants for Resource-Constrained Organizations

**Problem:** The paper does not report computational costs for generating 300B tokens of synthetic data or training Agentic CPT (Section 6.5). Most organizations cannot replicate this scale.

**Research Questions:**
- What is the minimum data volume required to achieve meaningful Agentic CPT benefits?
- Can knowledge distillation transfer agentic capabilities from large CPT models to smaller models?
- Is there a "lightweight" Agentic CPT variant that achieves 80% of the benefits at 20% of the compute cost?

**Suggested Approach:** Conduct a systematic ablation at smaller scales (1B, 10B, 50B tokens) to identify the inflection point where Agentic CPT becomes cost-effective. Explore parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods (LoRA, adapter layers) during the CPT stage.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

**Academic Research Assistants**

AgentFounder's strong performance on Academic Browse (75.3%) and DeepResearch Bench (47.9% RACE Overall) suggests immediate applicability for:
- **Literature review automation**: Synthesizing findings across hundreds of papers
- **Hypothesis generation**: Identifying research gaps through multi-source evidence integration
- **Citation verification**: Cross-referencing claims against primary sources

The Frames benchmark result (89.6%) indicates particular strength in **multi-perspective reasoning**, which is critical for balanced academic synthesis.

**Enterprise Knowledge Workers**

The SEAL-0 robustness score (43.9%) demonstrates resistance to conflicting or misleading search results—a common scenario in enterprise environments where internal documentation may contradict external sources. Practical deployments include:
- **Competitive intelligence**: Monitoring market trends across news, filings, and social media
- **Regulatory compliance**: Tracking policy changes across jurisdictions
- **Technical support**: Diagnosing issues by synthesizing documentation, forums, and code repositories

**Open-Source Agent Frameworks**

Organizations building agent frameworks (e.g., LangChain, LlamaIndex, AutoGen) can integrate AgentFounder-Base as a foundation model. The adaptability results (Table 3) show consistent improvements across all three SFT configurations, suggesting **compatibility with diverse post-training pipelines**.

**Domain-Specialized Agents**

The entity-anchored knowledge memory approach (Section 2.2.1) is domain-agnostic, enabling specialization:
- **Medical agents**: Construct knowledge memory from PubMed, clinical trial databases, and medical guidelines
- **Legal agents**: Use case law, statutes, and regulatory filings as knowledge sources
- **Financial agents**: Leverage earnings reports, SEC filings, and market data

The FAS methodology can generate domain-specific questions without requiring domain experts to manually annotate trajectories.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

**When to Prefer Agentic CPT Over Post-Training Only**

Based on the experimental evidence, Agentic CPT is most valuable when:

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| **Building from scratch** | Use Agentic CPT | Table 3 shows +4.5% to +14.3% gains over baseline across all SFT configs |
| **Limited post-training data** | Use Agentic CPT | Pre-alignment reduces dependency on large SFT datasets |
| **Multi-turn, long-horizon tasks** | Use Agentic CPT with 128K Stage 2 | Table 4 shows +8.0% Pass@3 gain on BrowseComp-zh with extended context |
| **Resource-constrained deployment** | Consider post-training only | Agentic CPT compute costs are not reported; may be prohibitive for small teams |
| **Non-English primary use case** | Augment with language-specific data | BrowseComp-zh gap reveals language coverage sensitivity (Section 3.2) |
| **Multi-modal tasks required** | Supplement with multi-modal CPT | GAIA results are text-only subset; multi-modal capabilities unvalidated |

**Implementation Priorities**

For organizations adopting Agentic CPT, the following priorities emerge from the ablation studies:

1. **Start with FAS data**: Table 5 shows FAS-only training achieves most gains (e.g., +9.0% Pass@3 on BrowseComp-zh). HAS provides complementary benefits but is more complex to implement.

2. **Invest in reject sampling**: Figure 9 shows filtering improves retained data accuracy from 50% to 82%. The LLM-as-Judge approach is critical for quality control without ground-truth signals.

3. **Use progressive context scaling**: Table 4 demonstrates 3.3% average Pass@1 improvement with two-stage training. Even if single-stage 128K training is feasible, the curriculum approach provides stability benefits.

4. **Monitor MoE activation balance**: Figure 12 (Appendix B.4) shows CPT leads to more balanced expert utilization. If using MoE architectures, track router logits to detect "dead experts" early.

**Minimum Viable Configuration**

Based on the scaling curves (Figure 6b), organizations seeking a cost-effective entry point should consider:

- **Data volume**: 50B tokens captures most of the scaling benefit (59.3% vs. 62.2% at 315B)
- **Context length**: 32K for initial deployment; add 128K Stage 2 if long-horizon tasks are critical
- **Model size**: 30B parameters shows strong efficiency (48.9% accuracy exceeds larger baselines)
- **Data mix**: FAS-only for initial deployment; add HAS when exploration diversity becomes a bottleneck

**Integration Checklist**

Before deploying Agentic CPT in production:

- [ ] Verify language coverage matches target deployment locales
- [ ] Evaluate on domain-specific benchmarks beyond general web search
- [ ] Measure catastrophic forgetting on non-agentic tasks
- [ ] Test robustness to real-world tool failures (not just benchmark conditions)
- [ ] Establish monitoring for tool call patterns (Figure 8 provides baseline distributions)
- [ ] Plan for iterative refinement—scaling laws suggest continued improvements with more data

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision: Agentic Foundation Models as a Category

This work suggests that **agentic foundation models** may emerge as a distinct category alongside general-purpose LLMs, similar to how code models (e.g., StarCoder, CodeLlama) diverged from general language models. Key characteristics of this category would include:

- **Pre-trained on agentic behaviors**: Tool invocation patterns, multi-step reasoning, environmental adaptation
- **Optimized for decision-making**: Step-level competence rather than trajectory imitation
- **Scalable via offline synthesis**: Economic feasibility for open-source development
- **Adaptable across domains**: Entity-anchored knowledge memory enables specialization

If this vision materializes, the research community would benefit from:
- Standardized Agentic CPT benchmarks (beyond the 10 used in this paper)
- Shared synthetic data corpora (FAS/HAS datasets)
- Compute-efficient training recipes for smaller organizations
- Theoretical frameworks for agentic scaling laws

The introduction of Agentic CPT represents not just a methodological advance, but a **foundational shift** in how we conceptualize agentic AI development. By separating capability acquisition from alignment refinement, this work provides a principled pathway toward building agents that are both capable and controllable—a prerequisite for deploying autonomous systems in high-stakes environments.
