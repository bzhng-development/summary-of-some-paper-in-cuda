## 1. Executive Summary

This paper completes the classification of two-dimensional normal affine algebraic monoids over an algebraically closed field of characteristic zero by extending Dzhunusov and Zaitseva's 2021 commutative results to the noncommutative case. The author proves that every two-dimensional affine algebraic monoid is toric (Theorem 3.2) and categorizes noncommutative structures into three distinct families ($G_n$, $X_{n}^{a,b}$, $Y_{n}^{a,b}$) determined by convex cones and pairs of Demazure roots (Theorem 3.10, Theorem 3.14). This resolution matters because it finalizes the structural theory of monoids on normal affine surfaces, providing explicit comultiplication formulas and characterizing key algebraic properties such as boundary divisors and opposite monoids.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of this work, we must first define the mathematical object under study and locate it within the broader landscape of algebraic geometry and group theory. This paper operates over an algebraically closed field $K$ of characteristic zero (Section 1).

### The Object of Study: Algebraic Monoids
An **algebraic monoid** is a geometric object that combines the structure of an algebraic variety with an algebraic multiplication rule. Specifically, it is an irreducible variety $X$ equipped with:
1.  A multiplication morphism $\mu: X \times X \to X$ that is associative.
2.  A unit element $e \in X$.

Unlike algebraic groups, not every element in a monoid needs to be invertible. The subset of invertible elements, denoted $G(X)$, forms an algebraic group that is open within $X$ (Proposition 2.3). The structure of $X$ is heavily constrained by the structure of this unit group $G(X)$.

The central problem addressed in this paper is the **classification of two-dimensional normal affine algebraic monoids**. In simpler terms, the author seeks to list all possible ways to define a multiplication rule on a specific class of 2D geometric surfaces (normal affine surfaces) such that the rules of algebra and geometry are preserved.

### The Classification Gap: Noncommutative Surfaces
While the theory of algebraic monoids was pioneered in the 1980s by Putcha and Renner (see monographs [8, 9]), a complete classification even in low dimensions remained elusive until recently. The difficulty lies in the diversity of possible unit groups $G(X)$.

*   **Reductive Case:** If $G(X)$ is a reductive group (like general linear groups), the classification was largely settled by previous works [13, 9].
*   **Unipotent Case:** If $G(X)$ is unipotent (essentially additive groups), the monoid $X$ must be the group itself, because orbits of unipotent actions on affine varieties are closed [12, Theorem 7.2.1].
*   **The Gap:** The intermediate cases—where $G(X)$ is solvable but not unipotent or reductive—were not fully classified for surfaces. Specifically, while Dzhunusov and Zaitseva classified **commutative** two-dimensional normal affine monoids in 2021 [4], the **noncommutative** case remained open.

This paper addresses that specific gap. As stated in the Introduction, "However, there were no attempts to classify general affine algebraic monoids even in low dimensions until recently." By solving the noncommutative case, this work "finishes the classification of monoid structures on normal affine surfaces" when combined with the results of [4].

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations
To appreciate the technical leap here, we must look at the tools used in prior work and where they fell short for the noncommutative setting.

1.  **Toric Geometry Connection:**
    A variety is called **toric** if it contains an algebraic torus $T$ as an open dense subset, such that the action of $T$ on itself extends to the whole variety. Toric varieties are powerful because they can be described combinatorially using convex cones.
    *   *Prior Work:* Dzhunusov and Zaitseva [4] showed that commutative normal affine monoids are toric. They provided explicit comultiplication formulas using **Demazure roots** (combinatorial data associated with the cone edges).
    *   *Limitation:* Their proof relied on the assumption of commutativity. It was unclear if noncommutative monoids would retain this convenient toric structure.

2.  **Affine Condition:**
    Rittatore proved that an algebraic monoid is affine if and only if its group of invertible elements $G(X)$ is affine [11].
    *   *Limitation:* This tells us *when* a monoid is affine, but not *what* the multiplication looks like structurally.

3.  **Group Structure:**
    For a two-dimensional noncommutative monoid, the unit group $G(X)$ cannot be a torus (which is commutative) nor purely unipotent.
    *   *Prior Knowledge:* It is known that such a group must be solvable.
    *   *Specific Structure:* This paper relies on the fact that any two-dimensional noncommutative connected linear algebraic group is isomorphic to a semidirect product $G_n = G_a \rtimes G_m$ (Proposition 2.5). Here, $G_m$ acts on $G_a$ by multiplication on the $n$-th power.
    *   *Challenge:* The classification must account for this parameter $n$ and the non-commutative interaction between the additive part ($G_a$) and multiplicative part ($G_m$).

### Strategic Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself as the direct noncommutative counterpart to [4], but it also strengthens the underlying theoretical foundations.

*   **Extending the Toric Property:** One of the most significant theoretical contributions is **Theorem 3.2**, which states: "Every two-dimensional (not necessarily normal) affine algebraic monoid admits a toric structure."
    *   *Why this matters:* This extends the result of [4] by dropping the assumptions of normality and commutativity for this specific claim. It implies that toric geometry is the correct language for *all* 2D affine monoids, not just the commutative ones. This allows the author to use convex cones to classify objects that are inherently noncommutative.

*   **Generalizing Comultiplication:** The paper adapts the Demazure root technique from [4]. In the commutative case, a single Demazure root suffices to define the multiplication. In this noncommutative setting, the author shows that structures are induced by a comultiplication formula involving a **pair of Demazure roots** $(e_1, e_2)$ (Theorem 3.14).
    $$ \chi^u \mapsto \chi^u \otimes \chi^u (1 \otimes \chi^{e_1} + \chi^{e_2} \otimes 1)^{\langle p, u \rangle} $$
    This formula (Equation 3) is a direct generalization of the commutative case, bridging the gap between the known theory and the new noncommutative results.

*   **Completing the Picture:** By categorizing the monoids into three families ($G_n$, $X_{n}^{a,b}$, $Y_{n}^{a,b}$) based on convex cones (Theorem 3.10), the paper provides a complete list. Together with [4], there are no remaining unclassified normal affine monoid structures on surfaces. This completeness is vital for researchers in representation theory and combinatorics who use these structures as building blocks for higher-dimensional theories.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a classification paper in algebraic geometry that uses toric geometry and combinatorial data (convex cones and Demazure roots) to systematically enumerate all possible noncommutative multiplication structures on two-dimensional normal affine surfaces.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The "system" being built is a complete mathematical catalog that takes a geometric surface as input and outputs all valid ways to define multiplication on it while preserving algebraic and geometric structure. The solution takes the form of three explicit families of monoids parameterized by integers $(n, a, b)$, each with a concrete comultiplication formula that can be written down and verified.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The classification pipeline consists of four major components connected in sequence. **Component 1 (Group Structure Analysis)** identifies what the invertible elements $G(X)$ must look like for any 2D noncommutative monoid, establishing that $G(X) \cong G_n = G_a \rtimes G_m$ for some positive integer $n$ (Proposition 2.5). **Component 2 (Toric Structure Proof)** demonstrates that every 2D affine monoid admits a toric structure, meaning it can be described using convex cones in a lattice (Theorem 3.2). **Component 3 (Cone Classification)** constructs two families of convex cones $\sigma^\vee(n, a, b)$ and $\bar{\sigma}^\vee(n, a, b)$ that satisfy the algebraic constraints imposed by the $G_a$ action (Construction 3.6, Lemma 3.5). **Component 4 (Comultiplication Formula)** translates the cone data into explicit multiplication rules using pairs of Demazure roots, providing the actual algebraic formulas for how elements multiply (Theorem 3.14). Information flows from abstract group theory through toric geometry to concrete algebraic formulas, with invariants $L_k(X)$ used to verify that different parameter choices produce genuinely distinct monoids (Lemma 3.8).

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- First, we establish the group structure of invertible elements $G(X)$ because the multiplication on the whole monoid must extend the group multiplication, so understanding $G(X)$ constrains everything that follows.
- Second, we prove the toric structure theorem because it allows us to replace geometric reasoning with combinatorial reasoning about convex cones, which is much more tractable for classification.
- Third, we derive the constraints on these cones from the requirement that locally nilpotent derivations (encoding the $G_a$ action) must preserve the algebra of functions on the monoid.
- Fourth, we construct the explicit cone families and prove they exhaust all possibilities, using invariants to show different parameters yield non-isomorphic monoids.
- Fifth, we translate the cone classification into comultiplication formulas using Demazure roots, providing the concrete algebraic description practitioners can actually use.
- Finally, we examine algebraic properties like opposite monoids and boundary divisors to complete the structural understanding of these objects.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Paper Type and Core Idea.** This is a structural classification theorem in algebraic geometry that proves every two-dimensional affine algebraic monoid is toric, then uses the combinatorial machinery of toric varieties (convex cones, lattices, Demazure roots) to enumerate all possible noncommutative multiplication structures explicitly.

**What Happens First: Identifying the Unit Group Structure.** The classification begins by determining what the group of invertible elements $G(X)$ must be, because $G(X)$ is an open dense subset of the monoid $X$ and the multiplication on $X$ must extend the group multiplication on $G(X)$ (Proposition 2.3). For a two-dimensional noncommutative connected linear algebraic group, the structure is completely determined: $G(X)$ must be isomorphic to $G_n = G_a \rtimes G_m$ for some positive integer $n$ (Proposition 2.5). Here $G_a$ denotes the additive group of the field $K$ and $G_m$ denotes the multiplicative group $K^\times$. The semidirect product structure means the group operation is explicitly given by:
$$(\alpha_1, \tau_1) \cdot (\alpha_2, \tau_2) = (\alpha_1 + \tau_1^n \alpha_2, \tau_1 \tau_2)$$
where $\alpha_i \in G_a$ and $\tau_i \in G_m$. The parameter $n$ is crucial: it determines how the multiplicative part acts on the additive part, and different values of $n$ produce non-isomorphic groups (Corollary 2.7). The center of $G_n$ is a cyclic group of order $n$, generated by $(0, \xi_n)$ where $\xi_n$ is a primitive $n$-th root of unity (Proposition 2.6). This group structure is the foundation because any monoid $X$ with $G(X) \cong G_n$ must contain $G_n$ as an open subset, and the toric structure on $G_n$ must extend to $X$.

**What Happens Second: Establishing the Toric Structure.** The key theoretical breakthrough is Theorem 3.2, which states that every two-dimensional affine algebraic monoid (not necessarily normal, not necessarily commutative) admits a toric structure. A toric structure means there exists an algebraic torus $T \cong G_m^2$ acting on $X$ with an open dense orbit, which allows $X$ to be described combinatorially via convex cones. The proof handles three cases separately. For commutative monoids where $G(X)$ is a two-dimensional torus, $X$ is toric by definition. For commutative monoids where $G(X) \cong G_a \times G_m$, the proof shows that the algebra of functions $K[X]$ is spanned by monomials, which defines a toric grading. For noncommutative monoids, the proof uses the fact that $G(X) \cong G_n$ and constructs a torus embedding $T \hookrightarrow \rho_n(G_n \times G_n)$ where $\rho_n(g_1, g_2)(g) = g_1 g g_2^{-1}$ describes the left-right action (Proposition 3.1). This torus action on $G_n$ extends uniquely to $X$ because automorphisms from $\rho_n(G_n \times G_n)$ extend to automorphisms of $X$. The grading on $K[G_n] = K[x, y, y^{-1}]$ is given by $\deg(x) = (1, 0)$ and $\deg(y) = (0, 1)$ in the character lattice $M \cong \mathbb{Z}^2$. This toric structure is essential because it reduces the classification problem from geometry to combinatorics: classifying monoids becomes classifying certain convex cones in the lattice $M$.

**What Happens Third: Deriving Cone Constraints from Group Actions.** Once we know $X$ is toric, we know $K[X]$ corresponds to a convex cone $\sigma_X^\vee \subset M_\mathbb{Q}$ containing lattice points (Section 2.2). However, not every cone works: the cone must satisfy constraints imposed by the $G_a$ subgroup action. The additive subgroup $G_a \subset G_n$ acts on $G_n$ by left and right multiplication, and these actions induce two locally nilpotent derivations on the function algebra. A **locally nilpotent derivation** $\delta$ on an algebra $A$ is a linear map satisfying the Leibniz rule $\delta(fg) = \delta(f)g + f\delta(g)$ such that for every $f \in A$, some power $\delta^k(f) = 0$. The left action induces $\delta_l = \partial_x$ and the right action induces $\delta_r = y^n \partial_x$ on $K[G_n] = K[x, y, y^{-1}]$ (Section 3.1, Notation). Since the $G_a$ action extends to all of $X$, the subalgebra $K[X] \subset K[G_n]$ must be invariant under both $\delta_l$ and $\delta_r$. Lemma 3.5 translates this invariance into a combinatorial condition on the cone: for every lattice point $(a, b)$ in $\sigma^\vee$, the points $(0, b)$ and $(0, b + na)$ must also lie in $\sigma^\vee$. This condition is the critical filter that eliminates most cones and leaves only the specific families that actually correspond to monoids. The intuition is that applying $\delta_l^a$ to $x^a y^b$ produces $a! y^b$, so $(0, b)$ must be in the cone, and applying $\delta_r^a$ produces $a! y^{b+na}$, so $(0, b+na)$ must be in the cone.

**What Happens Fourth: Constructing the Cone Families.** Construction 3.6 defines two explicit families of cones that satisfy the Lemma 3.5 constraints. The first family $\sigma^\vee(n, a, b)$ is the subcone of $\sigma_{G_n}^\vee$ generated by the rays $(0, 1)$ and $(a, b)$, where $n > 0$, $a > 0$, $b \geq 0$ are integers with $\gcd(a, b) = 1$. The corresponding surface is denoted $X_n^{a,b} = \text{Spec } K[\sigma^\vee(n, a, b) \cap M]$. The second family $\bar{\sigma}^\vee(n, a, b)$ is generated by rays $(0, -1)$ and $(a, -na - b)$ with the same parameter constraints, giving surfaces $Y_n^{a,b}$. Proposition 3.7 verifies these cones satisfy the Lemma 3.5 condition: for $X_n^{a,b}$, every point has $b' > 0$ and all points $(0, b'')$ with $b'' \geq 0$ lie in the cone; for $Y_n^{a,b}$, every point satisfies $b' \leq -na'$ so both $b'$ and $b' + na'$ are non-positive. Theorem 3.10 proves these families are exhaustive: any normal affine 2D noncommutative monoid is isomorphic to either $G_n$, $X_n^{a,b}$, or $Y_n^{a,b}$ for unique parameters. To prove different parameters give non-isomorphic monoids, the paper introduces invariants $L_k(X)$ defined as the codimension of the $k$-th image ideal of $\delta_l$:
$$L_k(X) = \dim \frac{\ker(\delta_l) \cap K[X]}{\ker(\delta_l) \cap \delta_l^k(K[X])}$$
Lemma 3.8 computes these explicitly: $L_k(X_n^{a,b}) = \lceil \frac{k}{ab} \rceil$ and $L_k(Y_n^{a,b}) = \lceil \frac{k}{a(b+na)} \rceil = \lceil \frac{k}{ab} \rceil + nk$, where $\lceil \cdot \rceil$ denotes the ceiling function. Proposition 3.9 uses these invariants to show that different $(a, b)$ pairs produce different $L_k$ values, hence non-isomorphic monoids.

**What Happens Fifth: Translating to Comultiplication Formulas.** The cone classification is combinatorial, but practitioners need explicit multiplication formulas. Theorem 3.14 provides these using Demazure roots. A **Demazure root** associated to a ray generator $p_i$ of a cone $\sigma$ is an element $e \in M$ satisfying $\langle e, p_i \rangle = -1$ and $\langle e, p_j \rangle \geq 0$ for $j \neq i$ (Definition 2.1). For a toric surface $Z_\sigma$ with ray generators $p_1, p_2$, a pair of Demazure roots $(e_1, e_2)$ associated to the same $p_i$ defines a comultiplication:
$$\chi^u \mapsto \chi^u \otimes \chi^u (1 \otimes \chi^{e_1} + \chi^{e_2} \otimes 1)^{\langle p_i, u \rangle}$$
where $\chi^u$ denotes the character corresponding to lattice point $u \in M$, and $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ is the natural pairing between $M$ and the dual lattice $N$. In plain language, this formula says: to multiply two functions, take their product and then apply a correction factor that depends on how the lattice point $u$ pairs with the cone edge $p_i$, with the correction built from the two Demazure roots. When $e_1 = e_2$, this reduces to the commutative Dzhunusov-Zaitseva comultiplication from [4]. When $e_1 \neq e_2$, we get noncommutative structures. The proof shows that in an appropriate basis, this formula transforms into the group comultiplication on $G_n$:
$$x^a y^b \mapsto (x \otimes 1 + y^n \otimes x)^a (y \otimes y)^b$$
which matches Equation (1) from Section 2.5. Corollary 3.11 gives the explicit multiplication on affine space $\mathbb{A}^2$ for the case $a=1$:
$$(x_1, y_1) \cdot (x_2, y_2) = (x_1 y_2^b + y_1^{b+n} x_2, y_1 y_2)$$
or the variant with $y_1^b x_2 + x_1 y_2^{b+n}$ in the first coordinate. Example 3.12 works through the case $X_n^{2, 2k+1}$ explicitly, showing it embeds as the quadric cone $\{(x, y, z) \in \mathbb{A}^3 : xz = y^2\}$ with a specific three-generator comultiplication.

**What Happens Sixth: Analyzing Algebraic Properties.** Section 4 examines structural properties that follow from the classification. The **opposite monoid** $X^{\text{op}}$ has the same underlying variety but reversed multiplication: $x^{\text{op}} y^{\text{op}} = (yx)^{\text{op}}$. Proposition 4.1 proves $Y_n^{a,b} \cong (X_n^{a,b})^{\text{op}}$ via an explicit lattice automorphism $\hat{\phi}: M \to M$ given by $\hat{\phi}(0, 1) = (0, -1)$ and $\hat{\phi}(1, 0) = (1, -n)$. This explains why there are two families: they are opposite to each other. For quotients, Proposition 4.2 shows that quotienting $X_{mn}^{a,b}$ by a central cyclic subgroup $C_m \subset Z(G_{mn})$ of order $m$ yields $X_n^{a', b'}$ where $a' = \frac{m}{\gcd(m, b)} a$ and $b' = \frac{1}{\gcd(m, b)} b$. This means every monoid is a quotient of a monoid on $\mathbb{A}^2$ (the $a=1$ case). The **boundary divisor** $R(X) = X \setminus G(X)$ consists of non-invertible elements. Theorem 4.3 proves that for $X_n^{a,b}$ with $a, b > 0$, the boundary $R$ is isomorphic to the affine line $\mathbb{A}^1$, contains a zero element (absorbing under multiplication), and the $G_n$ action on $R$ is given by $(\alpha, \tau) \cdot x = \tau^{b+an} x$ and $x \cdot (\alpha, \tau) = \tau^b x$. Corollary 4.4 concludes there are exactly three orbits under the $G_n \times G_n$ action: the group $G_n$ itself, $R \setminus \{0\}$, and $\{0\}$.

**Design Choices and Alternatives.** The paper makes several critical methodological choices. First, it uses toric geometry rather than direct algebraic manipulation because toric varieties have a combinatorial classification via cones, making exhaustive enumeration feasible. The alternative would be to work directly with comultiplication formulas on $K[X]$, but this becomes intractable without the cone framework. Second, the paper classifies via cones in $M$ (the character lattice) rather than cones in $N$ (the one-parameter subgroup lattice) because the function algebra $K[X]$ is naturally graded by $M$, and the invariance conditions from $\delta_l, \delta_r$ are easier to express in terms of which characters lie in $K[X]$. Third, the invariants $L_k(X)$ are chosen specifically because they are computable from the cone data and sensitive enough to distinguish all the constructed monoids; simpler invariants like dimension or Picard group would not distinguish the different $(a, b)$ parameters. Fourth, the Demazure root formulation in Theorem 3.14 is chosen to unify the commutative and noncommutative cases under one formula, showing the noncommutative theory is a natural extension rather than a separate phenomenon. The paper explicitly notes an inaccuracy in [4, Corollary 1] regarding commutative monoids on varieties with one-dimensional cones, which this work corrects by assuming full-dimensional cones in Theorem 3.14 (Remark 3.16).

**Key Parameters and Their Roles.** The classification depends on three integer parameters with specific constraints. The parameter $n \geq 1$ determines the group structure $G_n$ and appears in the comultiplication as the exponent in $y^n \otimes x$; it cannot be zero for noncommutative monoids (though $n=0$ recovers commutative cases as noted in Example 3.12). The parameter $a \geq 1$ controls the "slope" of the cone and affects the boundary divisor action exponents. The parameter $b \geq 0$ with $\gcd(a, b) = 1$ determines the second ray generator and distinguishes different monoids within the same $n$ family. The coprimality condition $\gcd(a, b) = 1$ ensures the cone is saturated (corresponding to a normal variety); without this condition, we would get non-normal surfaces outside the scope of this classification.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper does more than simply extend a classification list; it fundamentally alters how we understand the relationship between algebraic monoids and toric geometry in dimension two. While the **Technical Approach** section detailed the mechanical steps of the classification, this section highlights the conceptual breakthroughs that make the result robust and generalizable. We distinguish between incremental extensions of prior work [4] and fundamental innovations that change the theoretical landscape.

### 4.1 Universality of Toric Structures (Theorem 3.2)

The most profound theoretical contribution is the proof that **every** two-dimensional affine algebraic monoid admits a toric structure, regardless of commutativity or normality.

*   **Prior Limitation:** Previous work by Dzhunusov and Zaitseva [4] established this connection only for **commutative** and **normal** monoids. It was an open question whether noncommutative multiplication would disrupt the delicate combinatorial symmetry required for a toric structure.
*   **The Innovation:** Theorem 3.2 removes both assumptions. The proof demonstrates that the left and right actions of the unit group $G(X)$ naturally generate a torus action that extends to the whole variety.
*   **Significance:** This is a **fundamental innovation**, not merely an incremental extension. It implies that toric geometry is not just a convenient tool for "nice" cases, but the **native language** for all 2D affine monoids.
    > "Every two-dimensional (not necessarily normal) affine algebraic monoid admits a toric structure." (Theorem 3.2)

    This result justifies the entire methodological pipeline: researchers can now safely apply convex cone combinatorics to any 2D monoid problem without first verifying normality or commutativity.

### 4.2 The Demazure Pair Mechanism (Theorem 3.14)

The paper introduces a novel algebraic mechanism to encode noncommutativity within the toric framework using **pairs of Demazure roots**.

*   **Prior Approach:** In the commutative classification [4], a monoid structure is determined by a **single** Demazure root $e$. The comultiplication formula is symmetric with respect to the tensor factors.
*   **The Innovation:** Theorem 3.14 generalizes this to a **pair** $(e_1, e_2)$. The comultiplication formula becomes:
    $$ \chi^u \mapsto \chi^u \otimes \chi^u (1 \otimes \chi^{e_1} + \chi^{e_2} \otimes 1)^{\langle p, u \rangle} $$
    Noncommutativity arises precisely when $e_1 \neq e_2$. If $e_1 = e_2$, the formula collapses back to the commutative case.
*   **Significance:** This provides a **geometric diagnosis for noncommutativity**. Instead of viewing noncommutativity as an abstract algebraic property, we can now see it as an asymmetry in the combinatorial data (the roots) associated with the cone's boundary. This bridges the gap between group theory (noncommutative groups) and toric geometry (commutative actions) by showing how the latter can encode the former through root pairs.

### 4.3 Duality of Classification Families (Proposition 4.1)

The classification yields two seemingly distinct families of monoids, $X_n^{a,b}$ and $Y_n^{a,b}$ (Construction 3.6). A key insight is recognizing these not as unrelated objects, but as **opposite monoids**.

*   **Observation:** At first glance, the existence of two families suggests a doubling of cases to analyze.
*   **The Innovation:** Proposition 4.1 proves that $Y_n^{a,b} \cong (X_n^{a,b})^{\text{op}}$. The opposite monoid $X^{\text{op}}$ has the same underlying variety but reversed multiplication order ($x \cdot_{\text{op}} y = y \cdot x$).
*   **Significance:** This is a **structural simplification**. It means the classification list is not redundant; rather, it captures the inherent chirality (handedness) of the multiplication.
    > "The monoid $Y_n^{a,b}$ is isomorphic to the opposite of $X_n^{a,b}$." (Proposition 4.1)

    For applications, this implies that studying the algebraic properties of one family (e.g., ideals, representations) automatically yields results for the other via reversal of multiplication. It reduces the cognitive load for researchers using this classification.

### 4.4 Invariant-Based Distinction (Lemma 3.8)

Proving that different parameters $(n, a, b)$ yield non-isomorphic monoids requires robust invariants. The paper employs the codimension of image ideals of locally nilpotent derivations.

*   **Methodological Challenge:** Standard geometric invariants (like dimension or singularities) are often insufficient to distinguish between monoids that share the same underlying variety but differ in multiplication.
*   **The Innovation:** The author utilizes the sequence $L_k(X)$, defined as the codimension of the $k$-th image ideal of the derivation $\delta_l$:
    $$ L_k(X) = \dim \frac{\ker(\delta_l) \cap K[X]}{\ker(\delta_l) \cap \delta_l^k(K[X])} $$
    Lemma 3.8 provides explicit ceiling function formulas for these values based on $(a, b)$.
*   **Significance:** This is a **methodological advance** for classification proofs in this domain. The invariant $L_k(X)$ is sensitive enough to detect subtle differences in the cone slopes that other invariants miss.
    > "The monoids from Construction 3.6 are pairwise non-isomorphic." (Proposition 3.9, relying on Lemma 3.8)

    This technique ensures the classification is **rigorous and exhaustive**, preventing the over-counting of isomorphic structures—a common pitfall in algebraic classification problems.

### 4.5 Summary of Impact

| Innovation | Type | Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Toric Universality** (Thm 3.2) | Fundamental | Unifies 2D monoid theory under toric geometry; removes normality/commutativity barriers. |
| **Demazure Pairs** (Thm 3.14) | Structural | Provides a geometric mechanism for noncommutativity; generalizes prior commutative formulas. |

Together, these insights transform the paper from a simple catalog of examples into a robust theoretical framework. The shift from single to paired Demazure roots and the proof of universal toric structure provide tools that will likely influence higher-dimensional classification efforts beyond the surface case.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

It is critical to clarify at the outset that this paper is a work of **theoretical mathematics** (algebraic geometry and abstract algebra), not an empirical science or machine learning study. Consequently, it does not contain "experiments" in the traditional sense: there are no datasets, no training runs, no empirical metrics (like accuracy or loss), and no statistical baselines. Instead of empirical validation, the paper relies on **rigorous mathematical proof**, **combinatorial invariants**, and **explicit algebraic examples** to verify its claims.

In the context of this classification paper, the "experimental analysis" corresponds to the **verification of the classification scheme**. We assess whether the proposed families of monoids are distinct, exhaustive, and consistent with known results. The "quantitative results" are the specific integer parameters and invariant values derived in the lemmas and examples.

### 5.1 Verification Methodology

The author employs a three-pronged methodology to validate the classification, serving the role of experimental setup and metrics in this theoretical context.

*   **Mathematical Framework (Setup):** The analysis is constrained to **two-dimensional normal affine surfaces** over an algebraically closed field $K$ of **characteristic zero** (Section 2.1). This defines the "population" of objects being studied. The "baseline" is the prior classification of **commutative** monoids by Dzhunusov and Zaitseva [4], which this paper aims to extend to the noncommutative case.
*   **Invariants (Metrics):** To distinguish between different monoid structures (i.e., to prove they are not isomorphic), the paper defines a sequence of numerical invariants $L_k(X)$. These serve as the "metrics" for differentiation. Specifically, $L_k(X)$ measures the codimension of the $k$-th image ideal of a locally nilpotent derivation $\delta_l$ (Section 3.1, Notation).
    $$ L_k(X) = \dim \frac{\ker(\delta_l) \cap K[X]}{\ker(\delta_l) \cap \delta_l^k(K[X])} $$
    If two monoids have different $L_k$ values for any $k$, they are guaranteed to be non-isomorphic.
*   **Explicit Construction (Test Cases):** The paper constructs specific examples to ensure the theory holds in concrete cases. For instance, **Example 3.12** works through a specific monoid $X_n^{2, 2k+1}$ to show how the abstract cone classification translates into explicit polynomial multiplication rules.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results and Classification Families

The "results" of this paper are the explicit families of monoids and the formulas governing their invariants. Unlike empirical results which might vary with random seeds, these results are exact and deterministic.

#### 5.2.1 The Three Classification Families
**Theorem 3.10** establishes that every two-dimensional normal affine noncommutative algebraic monoid falls into exactly one of three families. This is the primary "output" of the classification:

1.  **The Group Case:** The group $G_n$ itself (where $n$ is a positive integer).
2.  **The $X$-Family:** Monoids $X_n^{a,b}$ determined by integers $n > 0$, $a > 0$, $b \geq 0$ with $\gcd(a, b) = 1$.
3.  **The $Y$-Family:** Monoids $Y_n^{a,b}$ determined by the same parameter constraints.

These parameters $(n, a, b)$ are the "hyperparameters" of the classification. The condition $\gcd(a, b) = 1$ is crucial; it ensures the underlying variety is **normal** (a specific geometric regularity condition).

#### 5.2.2 Invariant Values (Differentiation Metrics)
To prove that different parameters yield genuinely different monoids (and not just renamed versions of the same object), the paper computes the invariants $L_k(X)$ explicitly in **Lemma 3.8**. These formulas act as the "confusion matrix" for the classification, ensuring no two distinct parameter sets produce the same object.

*   For the family $X_n^{a,b}$:
    $$ L_k(X_n^{a,b}) = \left\lceil \frac{k}{ab} \right\rceil $$
*   For the family $Y_n^{a,b}$:
    $$ L_k(Y_n^{a,b}) = \left\lceil \frac{k}{a(b + na)} \right\rceil = \left\lceil \frac{k}{ab} \right\rceil + nk $$

Here, $\lceil \cdot \rceil$ denotes the ceiling function. The presence of the term $nk$ in the $Y$-family invariant is a key quantitative distinction that separates it from the $X$-family, even if $a$ and $b$ are similar. **Proposition 3.9** uses these specific values to prove that monoids with different $(a, b)$ pairs are pairwise non-isomorphic.

#### 5.2.3 Concrete Example: The Quadric Cone
**Example 3.12** provides a concrete "test case" to demonstrate the theory in action. It analyzes the monoid $X_n^{2, 2k+1}$ for integers $n > 0, k \geq 0$.
*   **Variety:** The underlying surface is isomorphic to the quadric cone in $\mathbb{A}^3$ defined by the equation $xz = y^2$.
*   **Generators:** The algebra is generated by three elements $f_1, f_2, f_3$ with degrees corresponding to the cone structure.
*   **Multiplication:** The example derives the explicit multiplication rule for elements $(x_1, y_1, z_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2, z_2)$:
    $$ (x_1, y_1, z_1) \cdot (x_2, y_2, z_2) = (x_1x_2, \dots, z_1x_2^{2k+1} + 2x_1^{n+k}y_1x_2^ky_2 + x_1^{n+2k+1}z_2) $$
    (Note: The middle term is abbreviated here for brevity; see **Example 3.12** for the full expansion).
*   **Consistency Check:** The example notes that if we set $n=0$, the formula recovers the commutative comultiplication from [4, Example 10]. This serves as a **baseline consistency check**, confirming that the new noncommutative theory reduces to the known commutative theory when the noncommutativity parameter $n$ vanishes.

### 5.3 Assessment of Claims and Robustness

The paper's claims are supported by deductive proof rather than statistical significance. However, we can assess the robustness of the classification based on the rigor of the verification steps.

*   **Exhaustiveness:** **Theorem 3.10** claims to list *all* such monoids. The proof relies on **Lemma 3.5**, which derives necessary conditions for the convex cones based on the action of the additive group $G_a$. By showing that only the constructed cones satisfy these conditions, the author argues exhaustiveness.
*   **Distinctness:** **Proposition 3.9** claims the listed monoids are non-isomorphic. This is supported by the invariant calculations in **Lemma 3.8**. Since the formulas for $L_k$ depend strictly on $(n, a, b)$, and these parameters are integers with specific coprimality constraints, the risk of "over-counting" (listing the same monoid twice) is mathematically ruled out.
*   **Duality Check:** **Proposition 4.1** provides a structural robustness check by showing that the two main families are opposites of each other ($Y_n^{a,b} \cong (X_n^{a,b})^{\text{op}}$). This explains why there are two families rather than one and ensures the classification respects the algebraic property of opposition.

### 5.4 Limitations and Boundary Conditions

While the mathematical proofs are rigorous within their domain, the "experimental" scope has strict boundary conditions that limit generalizability.

*   **Dimension Constraint:** The classification is strictly limited to **dimension two** (surfaces). The paper does not claim these results hold for three-dimensional varieties or higher. The proof techniques (specifically the classification of two-dimensional solvable groups in **Proposition 2.5**) rely heavily on low-dimensional group theory.
*   **Normality Assumption:** The main classification (**Theorem 3.10**) applies to **normal** affine surfaces. While **Theorem 3.2** proves that *any* 2D affine monoid is toric (even non-normal ones), the explicit cone classification assumes normality to ensure the cone is saturated (i.e., $\gcd(a, b)=1$). Non-normal cases would require handling non-saturated semigroups, which are not fully enumerated here.
*   **Field Characteristic:** The results assume the field $K$ has **characteristic zero** (Section 2.1). In positive characteristic (e.g., finite fields), the structure of algebraic groups and derivations can differ significantly (e.g., inseparable morphisms), so the classification may not hold.
*   **Missing Empirical Data:** As a theoretical paper, there are no computational performance metrics (e.g., time to compute the multiplication). For practical applications in computer algebra systems, the complexity of evaluating the comultiplication formulas (which involve powers like $y^n$) would depend on the magnitude of $n$, but this is not analyzed.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper successfully completes the classification of two-dimensional normal affine noncommutative monoids, the approach relies on specific mathematical constraints and methodological choices that limit its immediate generalizability. Understanding these limitations is crucial for researchers attempting to apply these results to higher dimensions, different fields, or computational settings.

### 6.1 Fundamental Mathematical Assumptions

The classification rests on three rigid pillars. Removing any one of them causes the proof structure to collapse, primarily because the underlying tools (group classification, toric geometry, derivations) behave differently outside these bounds.

*   **Characteristic Zero:** The paper explicitly states in Section 2.1 that $K$ is an algebraically closed field of **characteristic zero**.
    *   *Why this matters:* The theory of locally nilpotent derivations (LNDs) and the structure of algebraic groups used in Proposition 2.5 rely on properties that fail in positive characteristic. For instance, in characteristic $p > 0$, the exponential map relating derivations to group actions is not well-defined in the same way, and the classification of two-dimensional solvable groups becomes more complex due to inseparable isogenies.
    *   *Consequence:* The comultiplication formulas involving Demazure roots (Theorem 3.14) cannot be directly移植 (transplanted) to finite fields or modular arithmetic settings without significant modification.

*   **Dimension Two:** The entire classification is specific to **surfaces** (dimension two).
    *   *Why this matters:* The proof hinges on the classification of two-dimensional noncommutative connected linear algebraic groups (Proposition 2.5), which states they are all isomorphic to $G_n = G_a \rtimes G_m$. In dimension three or higher, the variety of possible solvable groups expands drastically, and the toric structure proof (Theorem 3.2) does not automatically generalize.
    *   *Consequence:* We cannot assume that three-dimensional affine monoids are toric. The combinatorial simplicity of convex cones in $\mathbb{Z}^2$ (which allows for the explicit parameterization by $(n, a, b)$) disappears in higher-rank lattices.

*   **Normality:** The explicit classification list (Theorem 3.10) applies only to **normal** affine surfaces.
    *   *Why this matters:* Normality ensures that the semigroup of characters $\sigma^\vee \cap M$ is saturated (i.e., if $k \cdot u \in \sigma^\vee \cap M$ for some integer $k > 0$, then $u \in \sigma^\vee \cap M$). This corresponds to the condition $\gcd(a, b) = 1$ in Construction 3.6.
    *   *Consequence:* While Theorem 3.2 proves that *non-normal* monoids are still toric, the paper does not provide the explicit classification list for them. Non-normal surfaces would correspond to non-saturated cones, requiring a more complex analysis of semigroup algebras that is outside the scope of this work.

### 6.2 Methodological Trade-offs

The author chooses specific mathematical frameworks to achieve the classification. These choices offer significant advantages in clarity and rigor but introduce trade-offs in terms of accessibility and generality.

*   **Toric Geometry vs. Direct Algebra:**
    *   *The Choice:* The paper translates the problem of classifying multiplication maps into classifying convex cones in a lattice (Section 3.1).
    *   *The Gain:* This reduces an infinite-dimensional algebraic problem to a finite-dimensional combinatorial one. It allows the use of powerful tools like Demazure roots to write down explicit comultiplication formulas (Theorem 3.14).
    *   *The Trade-off:* This requires the reader to be proficient in toric geometry (lattices, dual cones, pairings). A direct algebraic approach (working solely with polynomial rings $K[x,y]$) might be more accessible to algebraists unfamiliar with geometric combinatorics, but it would likely lack the structural insight provided by the cone visualization.

*   **Demazure Pairs vs. Single Roots:**
    *   *The Choice:* Noncommutative structures are encoded using a **pair** of Demazure roots $(e_1, e_2)$ rather than a single root (Theorem 3.14).
    *   *The Gain:* This unifies commutative and noncommutative cases under one formula (where $e_1 = e_2$ recovers the commutative case from [4]). It provides a geometric "diagnosis" for noncommutativity.
    *   *The Trade-off:* The formula becomes more complex:
        $$ \chi^u \mapsto \chi^u \otimes \chi^u (1 \otimes \chi^{e_1} + \chi^{e_2} \otimes 1)^{\langle p, u \rangle} $$
        Evaluating this requires computing pairings $\langle p, u \rangle$ and managing tensor products of characters, which is computationally heavier than standard polynomial multiplication.

*   **Invariant Selection ($L_k$):**
    *   *The Choice:* To prove non-isomorphism, the paper uses the codimension of image ideals of derivations $L_k(X)$ (Lemma 3.8).
    *   *The Gain:* These invariants are sensitive enough to distinguish between monoids that share the same underlying variety (e.g., different $b$ values on $\mathbb{A}^2$).
    *   *The Trade-off:* These invariants are abstract and difficult to compute without knowing the explicit cone structure. They are not "observable" from the multiplication table alone without first recovering the toric grading. This makes it hard to verify if an arbitrary given multiplication rule belongs to this classification without first performing a toric normalization.

### 6.3 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

Several specific scenarios fall outside the classification provided, representing open frontiers for future research.

*   **Non-Normal Surfaces:** As noted in Section 3.1, while Theorem 3.2 confirms non-normal monoids are toric, the explicit list in Theorem 3.10 excludes them.
    *   *Missing Detail:* There is no classification for cones where $\gcd(a, b) = d > 1$. These would correspond to surfaces with singularities that are not normal (e.g., certain quotient singularities). The behavior of the boundary divisor $R(X)$ in these cases is unknown.

*   **The Case $b=0$:** Theorem 4.3 explicitly excludes the case $X_n^{1,0}$ from its general statement about the boundary divisor.
    *   *Edge Case:* For $X_n^{1,0}$, the multiplication is $(x_1, y_1) \cdot (x_2, y_2) = (x_1 + y_1^n x_2, y_1 y_2)$. The boundary divisor multiplication behaves differently ($x \cdot y = x$ instead of $0$). This suggests that the "zero element" property of the boundary is not universal across all parameters, a subtlety that users of this classification must handle carefully.

*   **Correcting Prior Inaccuracies:** Remark 3.16 notes an inaccuracy in [4, Corollary 1] regarding commutative monoids on varieties with one-dimensional cones.
    *   *Implication:* This highlights that the boundary between "monoid structures" and "group structures" is delicate. The paper assumes full-dimensional cones in Theorem 3.14 to avoid cases where the variety degenerates to $A^1 \times K^\times$, where the monoid structure collapses to a group structure. Users must ensure their cones are full-dimensional to apply Theorem 3.14 safely.

### 6.4 Computational and Practical Constraints

For researchers intending to implement these structures (e.g., in computer algebra systems like Singular or Macaulay2), there are practical constraints to consider.

*   **Degree Growth:** The comultiplication formulas involve powers of $y$ determined by $n$ and $b$.
    *   *Constraint:* In the formula $(x \otimes 1 + y^n \otimes x)^a$, the degree of the resulting polynomials grows with $n \cdot a$. For large parameters, the multiplication rules become computationally expensive to evaluate.
    *   *Missing Analysis:* The paper does not analyze the computational complexity of multiplying two elements in $X_n^{a,b}$. There is no discussion of optimal bases for computation or whether sparse representations of the polynomials can mitigate the degree growth.

*   **Inverse Problem:** The paper provides a "forward" classification: given $(n, a, b)$, here is the monoid.
    *   *Missing Tool:* There is no algorithm provided for the "inverse" problem: given an arbitrary multiplication rule on a surface, how does one efficiently recover the parameters $(n, a, b)$? Solving this would require computing the toric grading and identifying the Demazure roots from the derivation structure, which is non-trivial without the theoretical framework provided here.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Future Directions

The completion of the 2D classification naturally points to several open questions that remain unresolved.

*   **Higher Dimensions:** The most significant open question is whether **Theorem 3.2** (every affine monoid is toric) holds in dimension three or higher.
    *   *Barrier:* The proof relies on the specific structure of 2D solvable groups. If a 3D noncommutative monoid exists that is *not* toric, the entire combinatorial approach would fail for higher dimensions.
*   **Representation Theory:** The paper classifies the varieties and multiplication rules but does not explore the **representation theory** of these monoids.
    *   *Opportunity:* Since these monoids are toric, their representations should be combinatorially describable. Understanding the module categories over these monoid algebras $K[X_n^{a,b}]$ is a natural next step.
*   **Quantum Deformations:** Given the explicit comultiplication formulas, a natural question is whether these structures admit **quantum deformations** (noncommutative geometry analogs).
    *   *Speculation:* The presence of the parameter $n$ in the term $y^n \otimes x$ suggests a potential link to quantum planes or skew-polynomial rings, but this connection is not explored in the text.

In summary, while this paper provides a definitive catalog for 2D normal affine noncommutative monoids, it does so by narrowing the scope to characteristic zero and normal varieties. The trade-off is a highly precise combinatorial description at the cost of generality. Researchers applying these results must verify that their specific problem setting aligns with these structural constraints.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than fill a gap in a classification list; it fundamentally reshapes the toolkit available for studying low-dimensional algebraic monoids. By proving that toric geometry is the universal language for two-dimensional affine monoids (Theorem 3.2) and providing explicit combinatorial data for their construction (Theorem 3.14), this work transforms abstract existence questions into concrete computational problems. This section outlines how this shift changes the field, what specific research avenues it opens, and how practitioners can integrate these results into their own work.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Theoretical Landscape

The most significant implication of this work is the **unification of commutative and noncommutative monoid theory** under the framework of toric geometry.

*   **Elimination of "Exotic" Cases:** Before this work, there was a possibility that noncommutative monoids might require entirely different geometric tools than their commutative counterparts. Theorem 3.2 shuts down this possibility for dimension two.
    > "Every two-dimensional (not necessarily normal) affine algebraic monoid admits a toric structure." (Theorem 3.2)

    This means researchers no longer need to hunt for non-toric examples in dimension two. The search space is now strictly combinatorial: classify the cones, and you classify the monoids.

*   **Demazure Roots as Noncommutative Encoders:** Prior to this paper, Demazure roots were primarily associated with additive group actions (locally nilpotent derivations) on toric varieties. This paper elevates them to **structural parameters for multiplication**.
    *   *Prior View:* A Demazure root $e$ defines a derivation $\delta_e$ (Section 2.3).
    *   *New View:* A **pair** of Demazure roots $(e_1, e_2)$ defines a comultiplication $\mu^*$ (Theorem 3.14).
    
    This conceptual shift allows geometricians to "read" noncommutativity directly from the lattice data. If $e_1 \neq e_2$, the monoid is noncommutative; if $e_1 = e_2$, it collapses to the commutative case studied in [4]. This provides a continuous deformation path between commutative and noncommutative structures within the same geometric family.

*   **Completion of the 2D Picture:** Combined with the commutative classification in [4], this paper finishes the classification of **normal** affine monoids on surfaces.
    > "Together with the result of Dzhunusov and Zaitseva it finishes the classification of monoid structures on normal affine surfaces." (Introduction)

    This completeness allows researchers to use these monoids as reliable building blocks for higher-dimensional constructions or counterexamples, knowing that the 2D foundation is solid.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Avenues

The explicit nature of the classification suggests several concrete directions for follow-up research. These are not merely vague suggestions but specific problems solvable using the tools developed in this paper.

#### 7.2.1 Higher-Dimensional Generalizations
The most immediate question is whether **Theorem 3.2** holds in dimension three or higher.
*   **The Challenge:** The proof of Theorem 3.2 relies on the specific structure of two-dimensional solvable groups ($G_n = G_a \rtimes G_m$) described in Proposition 2.5. In dimension three, the variety of solvable groups is much richer.
*   **Research Task:** Investigate whether three-dimensional affine monoids admit a toric structure. If they do, the combinatorial machinery of cones in $\mathbb{Z}^3$ can be applied. If not, this paper establishes dimension two as a unique "tame" regime where toric methods suffice.
*   **Clue from Paper:** The author notes in Section 6 (Limitations) that the proof techniques rely heavily on low-dimensional group theory. Extending this requires either a new proof of toric universality or a classification of 3D solvable groups compatible with toric embeddings.

#### 7.2.2 Non-Normal Surfaces
Theorem 3.2 states that **non-normal** monoids are also toric, but Theorem 3.10 only classifies the **normal** ones (where $\gcd(a, b) = 1$).
*   **The Gap:** Non-normal surfaces correspond to cones where the semigroup $\sigma^\vee \cap M$ is not saturated (i.e., $\gcd(a, b) = d > 1$).
*   **Research Task:** Extend Construction 3.6 to allow $\gcd(a, b) > 1$. The comultiplication formulas (Corollary 3.11) should still hold, but the geometric interpretation changes (the variety becomes a quotient singularity).
*   **Why it Matters:** Non-normal varieties appear frequently in moduli problems and degeneration limits. Having a monoid structure on them could provide new insights into compactifications of solvable groups.

#### 7.2.3 Representation Theory of Monoid Algebras
Now that the algebras $K[X_n^{a,b}]$ are explicitly known, their representation theory becomes accessible.
*   **The Opportunity:** Since $X_n^{a,b}$ is toric, the algebra $K[X_n^{a,b}]$ is graded by the character lattice $M$.
*   **Research Task:** Classify the finite-dimensional modules over $K[X_n^{a,b}]$. In the commutative case, these are often related to polyhedral geometry. In the noncommutative case (where $e_1 \neq e_2$), the multiplication twists the grading.
*   **Connection to Paper:** The boundary divisor $R(X)$ described in Theorem 4.3 is isomorphic to $\mathbb{A}^1$ and contains a zero element. This suggests that representations might decompose based on their support on $G(X)$ versus $R(X)$.

#### 7.2.4 Positive Characteristic
The paper assumes characteristic zero (Section 2.1).
*   **The Barrier:** In characteristic $p > 0$, locally nilpotent derivations behave differently (e.g., $\delta^p$ might not be zero in the same way, or exponentials are truncated).
*   **Research Task:** Determine if the Demazure root comultiplication formula (Equation 3) survives in characteristic $p$. The condition $\langle e, p_i \rangle = -1$ (Definition 2.1) is combinatorial and might hold, but the link to group actions (via derivations) needs verification.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

While this is a theoretical classification, the explicit formulas provided have practical utility in computational algebra and invariant theory.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For researchers intending to use these results, the following checklist ensures correct application and avoids common pitfalls identified in the paper.

#### 7.4.1 When to Use This Classification
*   **DO USE** when working with **two-dimensional** affine varieties equipped with an associative multiplication.
*   **DO USE** when the field $K$ has **characteristic zero**.
*   **DO USE** when the variety is **normal** (singularities are mild, e.g., quotient singularities).
*   **DO NOT USE** for dimension three or higher without additional proof of toric structure.
*   **DO NOT USE** for non-normal surfaces without adjusting the cone saturation condition ($\gcd(a, b)=1$).

#### 7.4.2 Identifying an Unknown Monoid
If you are given a multiplication rule on a surface and want to know if it fits this classification:
1.  **Check the Unit Group:** Compute the group of invertible elements $G(X)$. It must be isomorphic to $G_n = G_a \rtimes G_m$ for some $n$ (Proposition 2.5). If $G(X)$ is a torus $G_m^2$, you are in the commutative case [4].
2.  **Compute Invariants:** Calculate the invariants $L_k(X)$ defined in Lemma 3.8:
    $$ L_k(X) = \dim \frac{\ker(\delta_l) \cap K[X]}{\ker(\delta_l) \cap \delta_l^k(K[X])} $$
    where $\delta_l$ is the locally nilpotent derivation induced by left $G_a$ action.
3.  **Match Parameters:** Compare your computed $L_k(X)$ against the formulas:
    *   If $L_k \approx \lceil k/ab \rceil$, you likely have $X_n^{a,b}$.
    *   If $L_k \approx \lceil k/ab \rceil + nk$, you likely have $Y_n^{a,b}$.
4.  **Verify Demazure Roots:** If possible, identify the cone $\sigma^\vee$ and check if the comultiplication matches Equation (3) for some pair of Demazure roots $(e_1, e_2)$.

#### 7.4.3 Handling the Opposite Monoid
Be aware that the classification lists two families, $X_n^{a,b}$ and $Y_n^{a,b}$.
*   **Integration Tip:** You do not need to derive properties for both. **Proposition 4.1** states $Y_n^{a,b} \cong (X_n^{a,b})^{\text{op}}$.
*   **Practical Rule:** If you prove a property for $X_n^{a,b}$ that is invariant under reversing multiplication (e.g., dimension, singularities), it automatically holds for $Y_n^{a,b}$. If the property depends on order (e.g., left vs. right ideals), simply swap the family.

#### 7.4.4 Special Case: The Affine Plane $\mathbb{A}^2$
The most common use case will be monoids on $\mathbb{A}^2$.
*   **Condition:** This corresponds to $a=1$ in the classification (Corollary 3.11).
*   **Formula:** Use the explicit multiplication:
    $$ (x_1, y_1) \cdot (x_2, y_2) = (x_1 y_2^b + y_1^{b+n} x_2, y_1 y_2) $$
*   **Warning:** Ensure $n > 0$ for noncommutativity. If $n=0$, this reduces to the commutative case studied in [4].
