## 1. Executive Summary

This paper presents SayCan, a framework that grounds large language models (LLMs) in physical reality by combining an LLM's semantic planning capabilities (e.g., 540B parameter PaLM) with pretrained skill value functions that estimate the probability of successful execution for low-level actions. By selecting skills based on the product of the LLM's probability that a skill is useful and the value function's probability that the skill will succeed, the system enables robots to follow long-horizon, abstract natural language instructions without additional training. Evaluated on 101 real-world robotic tasks in a kitchen environment, SayCan achieves a 74% execution success rate, nearly doubling the performance of non-grounded baselines and demonstrating that robotic capabilities scale directly with improvements in underlying language models.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Ungrounded Language Models

The fundamental challenge this paper addresses is that **large language models (LLMs) lack physical grounding**. While LLMs can encode vast semantic knowledge about the world from text corpora, they have never interacted with a physical environment or observed the consequences of their outputs on any physical process. This creates a critical mismatch when deploying LLMs for robotic control.

Consider the example from Figure 1 in the paper: a kitchen robot capable of executing skills like "pick up the sponge" or "go to the table" is asked "I spilled my drink, can you help?" An ungrounded language model might respond with a reasonable narrative such as "You could try using a vacuum cleaner" — but this is impossible if no vacuum exists in the scene or if the robot lacks the capability to operate one. The LLM produces semantically coherent text that is **physically infeasible** for the specific embodiment and environment.

This gap manifests in two distinct failures:

1. **Task-grounding failure**: The LLM cannot determine which actions are *useful* for completing a high-level instruction without understanding the task semantics.
2. **World-grounding failure**: The LLM cannot determine which actions are *possible* given the current state of the robot and environment.

The paper's central question is: **How can embodied agents extract and harness the knowledge of LLMs for physically grounded tasks?**

### Why This Problem Matters

The importance of solving this grounding problem spans both practical and theoretical dimensions:

**Real-World Impact:**
- Robots need to follow **high-level, temporally extended instructions** expressed in natural language (e.g., "I just worked out, can you bring me a drink and a snack to recover?")
- Current robotic systems typically require carefully structured commands or task-specific training for each new instruction
- A grounded system would enable non-expert users to command robots using natural language without understanding the robot's low-level capabilities
- The paper demonstrates this on a mobile manipulator in a real office kitchen environment with 101 diverse tasks

**Theoretical Significance:**
- This work represents one of the first demonstrations that **robotic performance scales directly with language model improvements** — simply upgrading from a 137B parameter FLAN model to a 540B parameter PaLM model nearly doubles execution success rates (Table 3 shows 61% vs. 74%)
- It establishes a framework for combining semantic reasoning (from LLMs) with physical feasibility (from value functions) without requiring joint training
- The approach produces **interpretable plans** expressed through language, enabling users to understand the robot's decision-making process

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper positions itself against several categories of existing work, each with distinct shortcomings:

**1. Prompt Engineering Alone**
Prior work showed that careful prompt engineering could coax LLMs to produce structured outputs [5, 11]. However, as the Introduction notes, "this is not enough to fully constrain the output to admissible primitive skills for an embodied agent." Prompt engineering can split high-level instructions into sub-tasks, but **it cannot do so without context of what the robot is capable of** given its abilities and the current state. The paper's "Generative" baseline (Section 5.1) tests this approach and achieves only 74% planning success compared to SayCan's 84%.

**2. Training LLMs with Environment Inputs**
Several works attempted to ground language models by training them to accept additional environment inputs [30, 31, 32, 33, 34] or to directly output actions [35, 36, 37]. The Related Work section (Section 7) critiques these methods: "The above methods are all trained without interaction with a physical environment, thus limiting their ability to reason over embodied interactions." These models learn statistical associations but lack true affordance understanding.

**3. Learning Downstream Networks with LLM Representations**
Another approach learns downstream networks using pre-trained LLM representations [38, 22, 21, 39, 40, 41, 42, 43]. While this leverages language embeddings, it still requires task-specific training and does not extract the **procedural knowledge** encoded in LLMs about how to complete complex tasks.

**4. Fine-tuning Language Models with Interactive Data**
Some methods fine-tune language models with rewards or ranking feedback from interaction [11, 44, 45]. This requires additional training for each new domain and does not leverage the zero-shot generalization capabilities of large pre-trained models.

**5. Language-Conditioned Behavior Learning**
A substantial body of work learned language-conditioned behavior via imitation learning [51, 22, 20, 13, 26, 37] or reinforcement learning [52, 53, 49, 54, 55, 56, 21, 41]. As Section 7 notes, "Most of these prior works focus on following low-level instructions, such as for pick-and-place tasks and other robotic manipulation primitives." While some methods address long-horizon tasks in simulated domains [57, 58, 54], they do not extract knowledge from LLMs — they learn policies directly from data.

**6. Concurrent Work on LLM Planning**
The paper explicitly compares to Huang et al. [23], which was concurrent work using prompt engineering to extract temporally extended plans "but without any additional mechanism to ensure grounding." This corresponds to the "Generative" baseline in SayCan's experiments, which the authors note "loses the explicit option probabilities, and thus is less interpretable and cannot be combined with affordance probabilities."

**7. Classical Task and Motion Planning**
Traditional task and motion planning [59, 60, 61, 62, 63, 64, 65, 66] solves sequencing problems while ensuring feasibility, but these approaches "require explicit primitives and constraints" defined by humans. They lack the semantic flexibility to interpret abstract natural language instructions.

### SayCan's Positioning

SayCan positions itself as a **hybrid approach** that combines the strengths of LLMs (semantic knowledge, zero-shot generalization) with the strengths of learned robotic skills (physical grounding, feasibility estimation) while avoiding the weaknesses of each:

| Approach | Semantic Knowledge | Physical Grounding | Zero-Shot | Interpretable |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------|---------------|
| Pure LLM + Prompts | ✓ | ✗ | ✓ | Partial |
| Language-Conditioned RL | ✗ | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ |
| Classical Planning | ✗ | ✓ | ✗ | ✓ |
| **SayCan** | **✓** | **✓** | **✓** | **✓** |

The key innovation is the **probabilistic factorization** described in Section 3:

$$p(c_i|i, s, \ell_\pi) \propto p(c_\pi|s, \ell_\pi) \times p(\ell_\pi|i)$$

Where:
- $p(\ell_\pi|i)$ is the **task-grounding** from the LLM (probability that skill $\pi$ is useful for instruction $i$)
- $p(c_\pi|s, \ell_\pi)$ is the **world-grounding** from value functions (probability that skill $\pi$ can succeed in state $s$)

This factorization allows SayCan to be **modular**: the LLM and value functions are trained independently and combined at inference time. The paper emphasizes this enables a "symbiotic relationship where the skills and their value functions can act as the language model's 'hands and eyes,' while the language model supplies high-level semantic knowledge about how to complete a task" (Section 8).

Critically, SayCan achieves this **without additional training** to ground the LLM — it uses pre-trained value functions as an interface between language and physical feasibility. This is what enables the zero-shot generalization to 101 diverse instructions and the scaling behavior with improved language models.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems integration paper that demonstrates how to combine two independently trained components — a large language model for semantic reasoning and reinforcement learning-based value functions for physical feasibility — into a unified robotic control framework without requiring joint training.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

SayCan is a robotic control system that lets a robot understand natural language commands like "I spilled my drink, can you help?" and execute them by selecting from a predefined set of low-level skills. The system solves the grounding problem by having the language model propose what actions would be *useful* for the task while separate value functions determine what actions are *possible* given the current physical state, then combining these two signals to select the best action at each step.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The SayCan system consists of four major components that operate in a closed loop during task execution. First, the **Large Language Model (LLM)** receives the user's high-level instruction and scores each available skill based on how likely that skill's text description would appear as the next step in completing the instruction. Second, the **Value Function Module** observes the current robot state (including camera images) and outputs a probability between 0 and 1 for each skill indicating how likely that skill is to succeed from the current state. Third, the **Skill Selector** multiplies the LLM score and value function score for each skill, then chooses the skill with the highest combined probability. Fourth, the **Low-Level Policy** executes the selected skill's motor commands, after which the system loops back to re-query both the LLM and value functions with the updated state until a termination token is selected. Information flows from the user instruction into the LLM, from the environment sensors into the value functions, from both into the skill selector, and from the selector into the robot's actuators, creating an iterative planning-and-execution cycle.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the core probabilistic formulation that mathematically defines how SayCan combines language and affordance signals, since this equation governs all downstream design choices.
- We then explain the LLM scoring mechanism, including prompt engineering details and how the model is queried to produce skill probabilities rather than free-form text.
- Next we describe the value function module, covering how reinforcement learning produces affordance estimates and how these values are calibrated to work as probabilities.
- We then detail the skill repertoire and low-level policies, explaining what actions the robot can actually execute and how these policies were trained.
- Finally we walk through the complete Algorithm 1 execution loop, showing how all components interact during real task execution with concrete timing and termination conditions.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Probabilistic Formulation**

The fundamental insight of SayCan is that selecting the right skill requires estimating two independent probabilities and multiplying them together. The paper formalizes this in Section 3 with the equation:

$$p(c_i|i, s, \ell_\pi) \propto p(c_\pi|s, \ell_\pi) \times p(\ell_\pi|i)$$

This equation states that the probability a skill successfully makes progress toward completing instruction $i$ given state $s$ and skill label $\ell_\pi$ is proportional to the product of two terms. The first term $p(\ell_\pi|i)$ represents **task-grounding**: the probability from the language model that skill $\pi$'s text description is a useful next step for instruction $i$, independent of the physical state. The second term $p(c_\pi|s, \ell_\pi)$ represents **world-grounding**: the probability that skill $\pi$ with description $\ell_\pi$ will successfully complete if executed from the current state $s$, where $c_\pi$ is a Bernoulli random variable indicating success or failure. The paper notes in Section 3 that this factorization assumes "a skill that succeeds makes progress on $i$ with probability $p(\ell_\pi|i)$ (i.e., its probability of being the right skill), and a skill that fails makes progress with probability zero." This independence assumption is critical — it allows the LLM and value functions to be trained separately and combined at inference time without joint optimization.

**LLM Scoring Mechanism**

Rather than using the language model to generate free-form text responses, SayCan uses the LLM in **scoring mode** to assign probabilities to a fixed set of candidate skill descriptions. As described in Section 3, while typical generation applications sample from the distribution $p(w_k|w_{&lt;k})$ where $w_k$ is a word at position $k$, SayCan instead computes $p(\ell_\pi|i)$ by "querying the model over potential completions" from the set of available skill descriptions $\ell_\Pi$. The optimal skill according to the language model alone would be $\ell_\pi = \arg\max_{\ell_\pi \in \ell_\Pi} p(\ell_\pi|i)$, but SayCan modifies this by incorporating the affordance scores. The LLM used is the 540B parameter PaLM model unless stated otherwise for ablations (Section 5). The interaction is structured as a dialog between user and robot, where the user provides the high-level instruction (e.g., "How would you bring me a coke can?") and the language model responds with an explicit numbered sequence (e.g., "I would: 1. find a coke can, 2. pick up the coke can, 3. bring it to you"). This dialog structure is enforced through prompt engineering with 17 examples in the final prompt (Appendix D.3, Table 5), which improved planning success from 10% with zero examples to 88% with the full prompt when requiring termination. The prompt engineering lessons in Appendix D.3 note that "providing explicit numbers between steps (e.g., 1., 2., instead of combining skills with 'and then') improved performance, as did breaking each step into a separate line." The LLM parameters are frozen during robot operation — no fine-tuning occurs — which enables the zero-shot generalization to new instructions.

**Value Function Module and Affordance Calibration**

The value functions provide the physical grounding that the LLM lacks by estimating whether each skill can succeed from the current observation. As explained in Section 2, the paper uses temporal-difference-based reinforcement learning to learn state-action value functions $Q^\pi(s, a)$ which represent "the discounted sum of rewards when starting from state $s$ and action $a$, followed by the actions produced by the policy $\pi$." In the undiscounted, sparse reward case where the agent receives reward 1.0 at episode end if successful and 0.0 otherwise, "the value function trained via RL corresponds to an affordance function that specifies whether a skill is possible in a given state" (Section 2). The affordance function $p(c_\pi|s, \ell_\pi)$ is derived from the value function $v(s) = \max_a Q^\pi(s, a)$ found through optimization via the cross entropy method (Appendix D.2). However, raw value function outputs require calibration to work as probabilities. Appendix D.2 provides the specific calibration parameters: for pick skills, $p^{\text{affordance}}_{\text{pick}} = \text{clamp}(\frac{v_{\text{pick}} - v^{\text{min}}_{\text{pick}}}{v^{\text{max}}_{\text{pick}} - v^{\text{min}}_{\text{pick}}}, 0, 1)$ where $v^{\text{max}}_{\text{pick}} = 0.5$ and $v^{\text{min}}_{\text{pick}} = 0.2$. For navigation (go to) skills, the affordance is based on distance $d$ in meters: $p^{\text{affordance}}_{\text{goto}} = \text{clamp}(\frac{d^{\text{max}}_{\text{goto}} - d_{\text{goto}}}{d^{\text{max}}_{\text{goto}} - d^{\text{min}}_{\text{goto}}}, 0, 1)$ where $d^{\text{max}}_{\text{goto}} = 100$ and $d^{\text{min}}_{\text{goto}} = 0$. For place skills, the affordance is always 1.0 since language is sufficient to understand place is only possible after a pick. For the terminate action, a small affordance value of 0.1 is assigned to ensure planning terminates when no feasible skills remain. Figure 2 visualizes this value function space, showing how "pick up the red bull can" and "pick up the apple" have high values when both objects are in the scene, while navigation in empty space yields low values for all pick actions.

**Skill Repertoire and Low-Level Policies**

SayCan operates over a fixed vocabulary of 551 skills that span seven skill families and 17 objects (Section 4). These skills include picking, placing, and rearranging objects, opening and closing drawers, navigating to various locations, and placing objects in specific configurations. The skills are divided into three implementation categories as detailed in Appendix D.2: pick skills use learned policies from behavioral cloning with value functions from reinforcement learning trained on the same skill; go to skills use a classical planning-based navigation stack that maps to object coordinates since object locations are assumed known; place skills use Cartesian space motion planning from a pre-grasp pose to a gripper release pose. Each skill has a short natural language description (e.g., "pick up the can") that serves as the interface to the LLM. The policies are language-conditioned using a pre-trained large sentence encoder language model (Universal Sentence Encoder, reference [15]), where "we freeze the language model parameters during training and use the embeddings generated by passing in text descriptions of each skill" (Section 4). The action space includes six degrees of freedom of the end-effector pose, gripper open and close commands, x-y position and yaw orientation delta of the mobile base, and a terminate action (Section 4). The behavioral cloning policies were trained on 68,000 teleoperated demonstrations collected over 11 months using a fleet of 10 robots, plus 276,000 autonomous episodes that were success-filtered to add 12,000 successful episodes (Appendix C.2). The BC model was trained using 16 TPUv3 chips for approximately 27 hours, while the RL model used 16 TPUv3 chips for about 100 hours plus pools of 3,000 CPU workers each for episode collection and target Q-value computation (Appendix C.2). Image inputs were 640×512 pixels padded by 100 pixels left-right and 40 pixels top-down, then cropped back to 640×512 to allow random spatial shifts (Appendix C.2). For faster iteration, BC policy image inputs were downsampled to 256×320, but affordance value functions were trained with full-size images since half-size images did not work as well for learning $Q(s, a, \ell_\pi)$ (Appendix C.2).

**Algorithm 1 Execution Loop**

The complete SayCan planning and execution process is formalized in Algorithm 1 (Section 4, Figure 3). The algorithm begins with a high-level instruction $i$, initial state $s_0$, and the set of skills $\Pi$ with their language descriptions $\ell_\Pi$. At iteration $n=0$, the system enters a while loop that continues until the selected skill's description equals "done". In each iteration, the system initializes an empty set $C$ to store combined probabilities. For each skill $\pi \in \Pi$ with description $\ell_\pi$, the system computes $p^{\text{LLM}}_\pi = p(\ell_\pi|i, \ell_{\pi_{n-1}}, ..., \ell_{\pi_0})$ by evaluating the LLM scoring with the instruction and all previously selected skills appended to the context. Simultaneously, it computes $p^{\text{affordance}}_\pi = p(c_\pi|s_n, \ell_\pi)$ by evaluating the affordance function on the current state. The combined probability is then $p^{\text{combined}}_\pi = p^{\text{affordance}}_\pi \times p^{\text{LLM}}_\pi$, and this value is added to set $C$. After scoring all skills, the system selects $\pi_n = \arg\max_{\pi \in \Pi} C$, executes policy $\pi_n(s_n)$ in the environment to update the state to $s_{n+1}$, and increments $n$. This process repeats until the terminate skill is selected. Figure 3 illustrates this flow, showing how "given a high-level instruction, SayCan combines probabilities from a LLM (the probability that a skill is useful for the instruction) with the probabilities from a value function (the probability of successfully executing said skill) to select the skill to perform." The iterative re-querying after each skill execution enables closed-loop behavior at the skill level, though the paper notes in Section 8 that "the system is not easily able to react to situations where individual skills fail despite reporting a high value" without additional mechanisms like the Inner Monologue work [25].

**Design Choices and Alternatives**

The paper makes several critical design decisions that distinguish SayCan from alternative approaches. First, the **multiplicative combination** of LLM and affordance scores rather than additive or learned fusion was chosen because it provides a clean probabilistic interpretation where both components must agree for a skill to be selected — a skill with high LLM score but zero affordance (physically impossible) will have zero combined probability. Second, the **scoring mode** for the LLM rather than generative decoding was selected because it "opens an avenue to constrained responses by outputting the probabilities assigned by a language model to fixed outputs" (Section 3), ensuring the output always corresponds to an available skill. Third, the **independent training** of LLM and value functions rather than joint optimization enables zero-shot generalization — the system can leverage improvements in language models without retraining the robotic policies, as demonstrated in Table 3 where upgrading from FLAN 137B to PaLM 540B improved execution from 61% to 74%. Fourth, the **sparse reward structure** (1.0 for success, 0.0 for failure) for value function training was chosen because in the undiscounted case, the value function directly corresponds to an affordance function indicating feasibility (Section 2). Fifth, the **dialog prompt structure** ("How would you" / "I would") was found to be "both more natural and performant" for PaLM compared to imperative sentences, though the model generalized to imperative sentences at deployment time (Appendix D.3). The ablation studies in Section 5.1 validate these choices: removing value functions ("No VF" baseline) drops planning success from 84% to 67%, while using generative LLM output with USE embedding projection ("Generative" baseline) achieves 74% planning but loses interpretability and cannot combine with affordance probabilities. The BC NL baseline (feeding full instruction directly to policy) achieves 0% on all tasks, demonstrating that low-level policies alone cannot interpret abstract instructions without the LLM's semantic reasoning.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The SayCan framework introduces several fundamental shifts in how we approach robotic control with language models. Rather than treating the language model as a direct policy generator or fine-tuning it on robotic data, SayCan treats the LLM as a semantic planner constrained by physical affordances. The following insights detail why this architectural choice matters, distinguishing between incremental improvements and genuine innovations in embodied AI.

### 4.1 Grounding via Affordances vs. Prompt Engineering Alone

A critical innovation in SayCan is the demonstration that **semantic planning is insufficient without physical feasibility checking**. Prior concurrent work, such as Huang et al. [23], relied primarily on prompt engineering to extract plans from LLMs. This approach corresponds to the "Generative" baseline in Table 2, which uses the LLM to generate a plan and then projects it to the nearest skill via embeddings.

*   **The Limitation of Prompts:** The "Generative" baseline achieves a 74% planning success rate, which is respectable but fundamentally brittle. It assumes that if the language model says an action is useful, it is physically possible. As noted in Section 5.1, this baseline "loses the explicit option probabilities, and thus is less interpretable and cannot be combined with affordance probabilities."
*   **The Affordance Solution:** SayCan introduces the value function module to explicitly model $p(c_\pi|s, \ell_\pi)$ (the probability a skill succeeds in the current state). By multiplying this with the LLM's utility score, the system filters out semantically correct but physically impossible actions.
*   **Quantifiable Impact:** This grounding mechanism increases planning success from 67% (No VF baseline) to 84% (SayCan) in the mock kitchen (Table 2). This 17 percentage point gain validates the core hypothesis: **LLMs know *what* to do, but value functions know *if* it can be done.**

This is not merely an accuracy improvement; it is a structural change that prevents the "hallucination" of actions (e.g., suggesting a vacuum cleaner when none exists) that plagues ungrounded LLMs.

### 4.2 Robotics Performance Scales with Language Model Capacity

Perhaps the most significant long-term implication of SayCan is the evidence that **robotic capabilities can improve simply by upgrading the language model**, without collecting additional robotic data. Traditionally, improving a robot's task success rate required more demonstration data, better sensors, or refined control policies. SayCan decouples high-level reasoning from low-level control.

*   **Scaling Evidence:** Table 3 compares performance using different underlying LLMs while keeping the robotic skills and value functions identical.
    *   **FLAN 137B:** 61% execution success rate.
    *   **PaLM 540B:** 74% execution success rate.
*   **Why This Matters:** This result suggests a new pathway for scaling robotics. Instead of solely focusing on robot-specific learning (which is slow and expensive due to hardware constraints), researchers can leverage advances in general NLP. The paper notes this is "particularly exciting because it represents the first time we can see how an improvement in language models translates to a similar improvement in robotics" (Section 5.1).
*   **Nuance:** The scaling is not perfectly linear across all tasks. Table 3 shows that for "NL Single Primitive" tasks, both models achieve 100% planning, but for "Long-Horizon" tasks, PaLM (47% execute) significantly outperforms FLAN (33% execute). This indicates that larger models provide disproportionate benefits for complex reasoning requiring temporally extended planning.

### 4.3 Diagnostic Interpretability through Probabilistic Scores

Unlike end-to-end neural policies that act as black boxes, SayCan provides **explicit visibility into the decision-making process**. Because the system computes separate scores for utility (LLM) and feasibility (Value Function) for every candidate skill at every step, users can diagnose *why* a failure occurred.

*   **Error Attribution:** Section 5.1 provides a breakdown of failure modes: "Overall, 65% of the errors were LLM failures and 35% were affordance failures." This distinction is crucial for development.
    *   **LLM Failure:** If the model selects the wrong object (e.g., bringing a soda instead of water for a "healthy drink" request), the solution lies in better prompting or a larger language model.
    *   **Affordance Failure:** If the model selects the correct object but the value function incorrectly rates the skill as impossible (or vice versa), the solution lies in improving the reinforcement learning policy or value function calibration.
*   **Visualizing Reasoning:** Figure 6 visualizes these scores for a "spill" task. The user can see that "find a sponge" received a high combined score, while "find a vacuum" likely received a high LLM score but a zero affordance score (since no vacuum exists). This transparency builds trust and allows for targeted debugging, a capability absent in standard behavioral cloning or end-to-end RL systems.

### 4.4 Modular Skill Integration without System Retraining

SayCan demonstrates a **modular architecture** where new capabilities can be added to the robot without retraining the core planning system. This contrasts with monolithic approaches where adding a new action often requires retraining the entire policy network.

*   **Case Study: Drawer Manipulation:** In Section 5.2, the authors add drawer manipulation skills ("open the drawer", "close the drawer") to the existing system.
    *   **Implementation:** This required adding the new skills as options for the LLM scoring, providing accompanying value functions, and adding a few examples to the prompt (Listing 2).
    *   **Result:** The system achieved 100% planning success on 21 drawer-related queries without retraining the PaLM model or the existing manipulation policies.
*   **Execution Bottleneck:** It is important to note that while planning was perfect, execution success was only 33%. The paper clarifies this was "due to failures of the chained manipulation policy," not the SayCan planner. This highlights a key limitation: **SayCan can plan complex sequences perfectly, but it is still bounded by the robustness of the low-level skills.**
*   **Significance:** This modularity enables rapid prototyping. A developer can teach a robot a new concept (e.g., "recycle") by defining the low-level skill and its affordance, then instantly leveraging the LLM's semantic knowledge to use that skill in diverse contexts (e.g., "recycle the bottle" vs. "dispose of the trash") without additional training.

### 4.5 Limitations and Boundaries of the Approach

While innovative, the paper is transparent about where SayCan struggles, which provides critical context for understanding its contributions.

*   **Negation and Ambiguity:** Section 5.1 notes that SayCan "struggles with negation (e.g., 'bring me a snack that isn't an apple') and ambiguous references." This is inherited from the underlying language models. While Section 5.2 shows that Chain-of-Thought prompting (Table 4) can mitigate this, it remains a dependency on LLM capabilities rather than a solved problem within the robotics framework itself.
*   **Closed-Loop Feedback:** As presented in Algorithm 1, SayCan only receives environmental feedback through value functions at the current decision step. Section 5.2 acknowledges that "if a skill fails or the environment changes, the necessary feedback may not be available." This limitation led to subsequent work (e.g., Inner Monologue [25]) to incorporate richer feedback loops, indicating that SayCan is a foundational step rather than a complete solution for robust autonomy.
*   **Skill Ceiling:** The Conclusion (Section 8) emphasizes that "the primary bottleneck of the system is in the range and capabilities of the underlying skills." SayCan cannot execute tasks for which no low-level skill exists, regardless of how well the LLM plans. This defines the operational envelope of the system: it is a **composer of skills**, not a creator of new motor capabilities.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate SayCan's claims through systematic experimentation. The evaluation is designed to answer three critical questions: Does grounding LLMs with value functions actually improve robotic performance? Does robotic capability scale with language model improvements? And what are the system's failure modes and limitations?

### 5.1 Experimental Setup and Methodology

**Physical Environment and Robot Platform**

The experiments were conducted in two kitchen environments (Figure 4, Section 5):

- **Mock Kitchen**: A controlled environment mirroring the real office kitchen, used for training the robot's skills and value functions
- **Real Office Kitchen**: An actual working kitchen where the robot was deployed to test generalization

The robot platform is a **mobile manipulator from Everyday Robots** with:
- A 7 degree-of-freedom arm
- A two-fingered gripper
- RGB camera observations for policy input
- Action space including 6-DOF end-effector pose, gripper open/close commands, mobile base x-y position and yaw orientation delta, and a terminate action (Section 4)

The environment contains **15 objects** commonly found in an office kitchen and **5 known locations** with semantic meaning (two counters, a table, a trash can, and the user location).

**Instruction Dataset**

The evaluation uses **101 instructions** organized into 7 instruction families (Table 1, Section 5):

| Instruction Family | Num | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| NL Single Primitive | 15 | Test single skill execution | "Let go of the coke can" |
| NL Nouns | 15 | Test abstract noun understanding | "Bring me a fruit" |
| NL Verbs | 15 | Test abstract verb understanding | "Restock the rice chips on the far counter" |
| Structured Language | 15 | Ablate language complexity | "Move the rice chips to the far counter" |
| Embodiment | 11 | Test state-aware reasoning | "Put the coke on the counter" (from different states) |
| Crowd-Sourced | 15 | Test natural human requests | "My favorite drink is redbull, bring one" |
| Long-Horizon | 15 | Test multi-step reasoning | "I spilled my coke on the table, throw it away and bring me something to clean" |

These instructions were developed through crowd-sourcing via Amazon Mechanical Turk, in-person kitchen users, and inspired by benchmarks such as ALFRED [17] and BEHAVIOR [18]. The instructions span multiple axes: time-horizon (from single primitives to 10+ steps), language complexity (structured to fully crowd-sourced), and embodiment variations.

**Evaluation Metrics**

The paper uses two primary metrics, both assessed by **3 human raters** with success requiring **2 out of 3 agreement**:

1. **Plan Success Rate**: Measures whether the skills selected by the model are *correct* for the instruction, regardless of physical execution. As Section 5 states: "We ask 3 human raters to indicate whether the plan generated by the model can achieve the instruction." This metric isolates the planning component from low-level policy failures.

2. **Execution Success Rate**: Measures whether the full PaLM-SayCan system *actually performs* the desired instruction successfully. Raters answer "whether the robot achieves the task specified by the task string?" This captures the complete system performance including skill execution failures.

**Baseline Comparisons**

The paper compares SayCan against several baselines to isolate the contribution of each component (Table 2, Section 5.1):

- **No VF**: Removes value function grounding, choosing the maximum language score skill. This tests whether affordance functions are necessary.
- **Generative**: Uses the generative output of the LLM and projects each planned skill to its maximal cosine similarity skill via USE embeddings. This corresponds to concurrent work by Huang et al. [23] and tests prompt engineering alone.
- **BC NL**: Feeds the full instruction directly into the language-conditioned policy. This represents standard instruction-following methods without LLM planning.
- **BC USE**: Projects the high-level instruction into the set of known language commands via Universal Sentence Encoder embeddings, selecting the highest cosine similarity instruction.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**Overall Performance (Table 2)**

The primary results demonstrate that grounding LLMs with value functions substantially improves performance:

| System | Environment | Plan Success | Execution Success |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PaLM-SayCan** | Mock Kitchen | **84%** | **74%** |
| **PaLM-SayCan** | Real Kitchen | **81%** | **60%** |
| No VF | Mock Kitchen | 67% | — |
| Generative | Mock Kitchen | 74% | — |
| BC NL | Mock Kitchen | 0% | 0% |
| BC USE | Mock Kitchen | 60% (single only) | 9% (total) |

Several observations emerge from these numbers:

1. **Value functions nearly double performance over non-grounded baselines**: The No VF baseline achieves 67% planning success compared to SayCan's 84%. The paper states in the Abstract that "Grounding the LLM in the real-world via affordances nearly doubles the performance over the non-grounded baselines" — this claim is supported when comparing to BC NL (0%) and is moderately supported when comparing to No VF (17 percentage point improvement).

2. **Real-world generalization shows expected degradation**: Moving from mock kitchen to real kitchen reduces planning by 3% (84% → 81%) and execution by 14% (74% → 60%). The paper notes this indicates "PaLM-SayCan and the underlying policies generalize reasonably well to the full kitchen." The larger drop in execution versus planning suggests low-level policy failures account for most of the performance gap in unseen environments.

3. **LLM planning is essential**: BC NL achieves 0% on all tasks, demonstrating that low-level policies alone cannot interpret abstract instructions. BC USE achieves 60% for single primitives but 0% otherwise, showing that embedding-based matching fails for multi-step tasks requiring temporal reasoning.

**Performance by Instruction Family (Table 2)**

Breaking down results by instruction type reveals where the system succeeds and struggles:

| Family | Plan (Mock) | Execute (Mock) | Plan (Real) | Execute (Real) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| NL Single Primitive | 100% | 100% | 93% | 87% |
| NL Nouns | 67% | 47% | 60% | 40% |
| NL Verbs | 100% | 93% | 93% | 73% |
| Structured | 93% | 87% | 93% | 47% |
| Embodiment | 64% | 55% | 64% | 55% |
| Crowd-Sourced | 87% | 87% | 73% | 60% |
| Long-Horizon | 73% | 47% | 73% | 47% |

Key patterns:

- **NL Nouns performed worse than NL Verbs**: The paper explains this is "due to the number of nouns possible (15 objects and 5 locations) versus number of verbs (6)." More candidate objects increase the chance of selection errors.

- **Structured language tasks achieved 93% planning** while their natural language verb counterparts achieved 100%. The paper notes this "indicates the language model effectively parses the queries" — the small gap suggests natural language understanding is nearly as effective as explicit commands.

- **Embodiment tasks planned correctly only 64% of the time**, with failures "generally as a result of affordance function misclassification." This is the lowest planning success rate, indicating that state-aware reasoning remains challenging.

- **Long-Horizon tasks performed worst** (47% execution), where "most failures were a result of early termination by the LLM (e.g., bringing one object but not the second)." Figure 15(b) shows a 9-step plan that "narrowly avoids an early termination at step 5," illustrating how the affordance rating helps overcome premature termination.

**Error Attribution (Section 5.1)**

The paper provides a detailed breakdown of failure modes:

> "Overall, 65% of the errors were LLM failures and 35% were affordance failures."

This diagnostic is critical for understanding where improvements should focus:
- **LLM failures** (65%): Wrong object selection, incorrect ordering, early termination, struggles with negation
- **Affordance failures** (35%): Value function misclassifies skill feasibility, either rejecting feasible skills or accepting infeasible ones

Figure 16 shows representative failure cases:
- **Figure 16(a)**: The affordance model fails to identify bags of chips as pickable, though the language model correctly approaches the counter
- **Figure 16(b)**: The affordance model fails to identify the sponge as pickable for a cleaning task
- **Figure 16(c)**: The language model terminates a long-horizon task prematurely (bringing only one of two requested items)

### 5.3 Ablation Studies

**Ablating Value Functions (Table 2)**

The paper compares PaLM-SayCan against two baselines that remove or modify the value function component:

| System | Planning Success | Key Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| PaLM-SayCan | 84% | — |
| No VF | 67% | Chooses physically infeasible skills |
| Generative | 74% | Loses explicit option probabilities, less interpretable |

The No VF baseline removes value function grounding entirely, choosing the maximum language score skill. The 17 percentage point drop (84% → 67%) demonstrates that **affordance functions are necessary** to filter out semantically correct but physically impossible actions.

The Generative baseline uses the LLM's generative output and projects to nearest skills via USE embeddings. While it achieves 74% planning (closer to SayCan), the paper notes it "loses the explicit option probabilities, and thus is less interpretable and cannot be combined with affordance probabilities." This ablation validates the design choice to use scoring mode rather than generative decoding.

**Ablating Language Model Size (Table 3 and Table 6)**

A critical claim is that robotic performance scales with language model improvements. The paper tests this with four models:

| LLM | Parameters | Plan Success | Execute Success |
|---|---|---|---|
| PaLM | 540B | 84% | 74% |
| FLAN | 137B | 70% | 61% |
| PaLM | 62B | — | 72% (generative only) |
| PaLM | 8B | — | 38% (generative only) |

Table 3 shows the full SayCan comparison between PaLM 540B and FLAN 137B:

> "The results show that the system using PaLM with affordance grounding (PaLM-SayCan) chooses the correct sequence of skills 84% of the time and executes them successfully 74% of the time, reducing errors by half compared to FLAN."

The paper emphasizes this is "particularly exciting because it represents the first time we can see how an improvement in language models translates to a similar improvement in robotics." This scaling behavior suggests a new pathway for improving robotic systems: rather than collecting more robotic data, upgrade the language model.

However, the scaling is not uniform across all tasks. For "NL Single Primitive" tasks, both PaLM and FLAN achieve 100% planning. For "Long-Horizon" tasks, PaLM achieves 47% execution while FLAN achieves only 33%. This indicates **larger models provide disproportionate benefits for complex reasoning** requiring temporally extended planning.

Table 6 (Appendix E.2) shows generative-only performance across model sizes, confirming that larger models perform better on language understanding alone, though "the difference between the 62B and 540B model is small" on simpler problems.

**Ablating Prompt Engineering (Table 5, Appendix D.3)**

The paper tests how many examples are needed in the prompt for effective planning:

| Num Examples | Require Termination | No Termination Required |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | 10% | 52% |
| 1 | 64% | 74% |
| 2 | 68% | 76% |
| 4 | 82% | 84% |
| 8 | 80% | 80% |
| Full Prompt (17) | 88% | 88% |

Key lessons from Appendix D.3:
- **With no examples**, SayCan plans only 10% correctly when requiring termination, but 52% without. This shows the LLM has some inherent task understanding but needs examples to learn the termination convention.
- **After only four examples**, performance reaches 82%, suggesting rapid learning from few demonstrations.
- **The full prompt with 17 examples** achieves 88%, the highest performance.

The paper notes several prompt engineering lessons:
- "Providing explicit numbers between steps (e.g., 1., 2., instead of combining skills with 'and then') improved performance"
- "Breaking each step into a separate line (e.g., adding a '\n' between steps)" helped
- "Examples which overly include objects used in the actual planning tend to bias results to those objects"
- "Phrasing of the natural language names of skills and objects is important due to the auto-regressive nature of the LLM scoring"

### 5.4 Additional Capabilities and Robustness Checks

**Adding New Skills: Drawer Manipulation (Section 5.2, Table 7)**

The paper tests whether new skills can be added without retraining the core system:

| Instruction | Plan Rate | Execution Rate |
|---|---|---|
| restock the coke and pepsi into the drawer | 1.0 | 0.0 |
| hide the 7up in the drawer | 1.0 | 0.83 |
| restock the coke into the drawer | 1.0 | 0.17 |
| **Overall (21 queries)** | **100%** | **33%** |

The implementation required only:
1. Adding new skills as options for LLM scoring
2. Providing accompanying value functions (heuristics: "If the robot is next to the drawer, all drawer tasks are possible")
3. Adding a few examples to the prompt (Listing 2, Appendix E.3)

The **100% planning success** demonstrates that SayCan can incorporate new capabilities without retraining. However, the **33% execution success** reveals the system's bottleneck: "due to failures of the chained manipulation policy, not the SayCan planner." This validates the claim in Section 8 that "the primary bottleneck of the system is in the range and capabilities of the underlying skills."

**Chain-of-Thought Reasoning (Section 5.2, Table 4)**

To address the limitation with negation, the paper integrates Chain-of-Thought prompting [24]:

> "For chain-of-thought prompting-based SayCan, we need to modify the prompt to include a part called 'Explanation'. We also slightly change how we use the language model. Instead of directly using the scoring interface to rank possible options, we first use the generative decoding of LLM to create an explanation, and then use the scoring mode, by including the explanation into the prompt."

Table 4 shows successful rollouts:
- "Can you bring a fruit-flavored drink without caffeine?" → Model explains "The user has asked for a drink that is fruit-flavored and does not have caffeine, I will bring the lime soda" and executes correctly
- "The snack is too small, bring me something more filling" → Model reasons to bring multigrain chips

This demonstrates that **SayCan can be integrated with LLM reasoning improvements** without modifying the robotic components. However, this remains a dependency on LLM capabilities rather than a solved problem within the robotics framework.

**Multilingual Queries (Section 5.2, Table 8)**

Since PaLM was trained on multilingual corpora, SayCan handles non-English queries without explicit design:

| Language | Example | Plan Success |
|---|---|---|
| English | "bring me a can of coke" | 1.0 |
| Chinese | "拿一罐可乐给我" | 1.0 |
| French | "apporte moi une canette de coca" | 1.0 |
| French | "J'ai renversée mon coca, peux-tu m'apporter quelque chose pour m'aider à nettoyer" | 0.0 |
| Spanish | "tráeme una lata de coca cola" | 1.0 |

The paper reports "almost no performance drop in planning success rate when changing the queries from English to Chinese, French and Spanish." One French query failed (0.0), but overall multilingual capability emerges from the LLM's training without robotic retraining.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

**Do the experiments convincingly support the paper's claims?**

**Claim 1: "Grounding the LLM in the real-world via affordances nearly doubles the performance over the non-grounded baselines"**

*Assessment*: **Partially supported**. The 84% vs. 67% comparison (No VF) shows a 17 percentage point improvement, which is substantial but not "nearly doubles." However, compared to BC NL (0%) and BC USE (9% total execution), the improvement is more dramatic. The claim is strongest when comparing to baselines that lack both LLM planning and affordance grounding.

**Claim 2: "A robot's performance can be improved simply by enhancing the underlying language model"**

*Assessment*: **Strongly supported**. Table 3 shows PaLM 540B (74% execution) vs. FLAN 137B (61% execution) with identical robotic components. This is a clean ablation demonstrating that LLM improvements translate to robotic improvements without additional robotic data collection.

**Claim 3: "SayCan can execute temporally-extended, abstract instructions"**

*Assessment*: **Supported with caveats**. Long-horizon tasks achieve 47% execution success (Table 2), which is substantially better than baselines (FLAN achieves 33%) but indicates room for improvement. Figure 5 shows successful 9-step rollouts, but Figure 16(c) shows premature termination failures. The system can handle long-horizon tasks but is not yet robust.

**Claim 4: "This combination results in a fully explainable sequence of steps"**

*Assessment*: **Strongly supported**. Figure 6 visualizes the decision-making process with explicit scores for each skill. The 65%/35% error attribution (LLM vs. affordance) provides diagnostic interpretability that end-to-end systems lack.

**Limitations and Mixed Results**

Several results reveal conditional performance:

1. **Environment Generalization**: The 14% execution drop from mock to real kitchen (74% → 60%) indicates that while planning generalizes well (84% → 81%), low-level policies struggle with domain shift. This is expected but limits real-world deployment.

2. **Negation Handling**: The paper acknowledges SayCan "struggles with negation (e.g., 'bring me a snack that isn't an apple') and ambiguous references." Chain-of-Thought helps but remains an LLM dependency.

3. **Closed-Loop Feedback**: Section 5.2 notes that "if a skill fails or the environment changes, the necessary feedback may not be available." This limitation led to subsequent work (Inner Monologue [25]), indicating SayCan is a foundational step rather than a complete solution.

4. **Skill Ceiling**: The drawer manipulation results (100% plan, 33% execute) highlight that **SayCan is bounded by low-level skill robustness**. The planner can be perfect, but execution failures limit overall performance.

### 5.6 Open Source Environment (Section 6)

The paper provides an open-source implementation in a Google Colab notebook at say-can.github.io/#open-source:

- **Environment**: Tabletop with UR5 robot and randomly generated colored blocks and bowls (Figure 8)
- **Policy**: CLIPort [26] trained to output pick and place locations
- **Affordances**: ViLD object detector [27] (since no value function is available for this policy)
- **LLM**: GPT-3 [5]

This simplified environment allows researchers to experiment with SayCan's principles without access to the full robotic infrastructure. The use of object detection instead of learned value functions demonstrates the framework's flexibility in grounding sources.

### 5.7 Summary of Experimental Contributions

The experimental evaluation makes several important contributions to the field:

1. **First demonstration of LLM scaling for robotics**: Table 3 provides empirical evidence that upgrading language models improves robotic performance without additional robotic training.

2. **Comprehensive baseline comparisons**: The ablation studies (No VF, Generative, BC NL, BC USE) isolate the contribution of each component, validating the design choices.

3. **Diagnostic interpretability**: The 65%/35% error attribution and per-skill scoring (Figure 6) provide debugging capabilities absent in end-to-end systems.

4. **Real-world validation**: 101 tasks in a real office kitchen (not just simulation) demonstrate practical applicability, though the 60% real-kitchen execution rate indicates deployment challenges remain.

5. **Transparent failure analysis**: The paper explicitly documents failure modes (negation, early termination, affordance misclassification) rather than only reporting successes, enabling informed assessment of the system's operational envelope.

The experiments convincingly demonstrate that **grounding LLMs with value functions improves robotic performance** and that **robotic capabilities scale with language model improvements**. However, the results also reveal that SayCan is a **foundational framework** rather than a complete solution — limitations in closed-loop feedback, negation handling, and low-level skill robustness define boundaries for future work.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While SayCan demonstrates that grounding language models with value functions improves robotic performance, the approach rests on several assumptions and faces meaningful constraints. Understanding these limitations is critical for assessing when SayCan is appropriate and where future work should focus. This section examines the system's assumptions, unaddressed scenarios, computational and data requirements, and remaining weaknesses—all grounded in evidence from the paper.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions

SayCan's architecture depends on several assumptions that constrain its applicability:

**Assumption 1: Fixed Skill Vocabulary with Known Descriptions**

The system operates over a predefined set of 551 skills spanning seven skill families and 17 objects (Section 4). Each skill must have:
- A trained low-level policy
- A trained value function (or heuristic affordance)
- A short natural language description (e.g., "pick up the can")

This assumption creates a **hard ceiling on capabilities**: SayCan cannot execute tasks for which no low-level skill exists, regardless of how well the LLM plans. As Section 8 states: "the primary bottleneck of the system is in the range and capabilities of the underlying skills." The drawer manipulation case study illustrates this sharply—planning achieved 100% success on 21 queries, but execution succeeded only 33% of the time due to "failures of the chained manipulation policy" (Section 5.2, Table 7).

**Assumption 2: Independence Between LLM Utility and Affordance**

The core probabilistic factorization assumes:

$$p(c_i|i, s, \ell_\pi) \propto p(c_\pi|s, \ell_\pi) \times p(\ell_\pi|i)$$

This assumes the LLM's assessment of skill utility is independent of the value function's assessment of feasibility. Section 3 notes this assumes "a skill that succeeds makes progress on $i$ with probability $p(\ell_\pi|i)$... and a skill that fails makes progress with probability zero." In practice, these signals may be correlated—a skill that is physically impossible may also be semantically inappropriate for certain contexts. The multiplicative combination means **a zero affordance score completely eliminates a skill**, even if the LLM strongly believes it is useful. This is intentional (to prevent hallucination) but may occasionally filter out creative solutions.

**Assumption 3: Known Object Locations for Navigation**

Appendix D.2 explicitly states: "Since the focus of this work is mainly on planning, we assume the location of objects are known." Navigation skills use "a classical planning-based navigation stack" that maps to object coordinates. This assumption significantly limits real-world applicability—in unstructured environments, object locations are rarely known a priori. The system relies on the value function to detect when objects are visible (Figure 2 shows pick affordances drop when objects are not in view), but cannot actively search for objects whose locations are unknown.

**Assumption 4: Place Skills Are Always Feasible**

Appendix D.2 notes: "We assume place is always possible, $p^{\text{affordance}}_{\text{place}} = 1.0$, since we find language is sufficient to understand place is only possible after a pick." The authors acknowledge this is a simplification: "In the future work having an affordance function module for place could further improve the performance of SayCan." This assumption could lead to failures in scenarios where placing an object is physically blocked (e.g., a full counter, an occupied target location).

**Assumption 5: Sparse Reward Structure Maps to Affordances**

Section 2 states that "in the undiscounted, sparse reward case, where the agent receives the reward of 1.0 at the end of the episode if it was successful and 0.0 otherwise, the value function trained via RL corresponds to an affordance function." This requires careful reward design. If rewards are not properly sparse or if the task has intermediate milestones, the value function may not accurately reflect feasibility. The paper does not explore how sensitive SayCan is to violations of this assumption.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

Several important scenarios fall outside SayCan's current capabilities:

**Negation and Logical Reasoning**

Section 5.1 explicitly states: "PaLM-SayCan struggles with negation (e.g., 'bring me a snack that isn't an apple') and ambiguous references (e.g. asking for drinks with caffeine), which is a known issue inherited from underlying language models [19]." While Section 5.2 demonstrates that Chain-of-Thought prompting can mitigate this (Table 4 shows successful negation handling with explanation generation), this remains a **dependency on LLM capabilities rather than a solved problem within the robotics framework**. The system inherits all limitations of the underlying language model's reasoning abilities.

**Closed-Loop Feedback During Skill Execution**

As presented in Algorithm 1, SayCan only receives environmental feedback through value functions at decision points between skills. Section 5.2 acknowledges: "if a skill fails or the environment changes, the necessary feedback may not be available." Section 8 elaborates: "at the current stage, the system is not easily able to react to situations where individual skills fail despite reporting a high value." This limitation is significant for real-world deployment where low-level policies may fail unpredictably. The paper notes this led to subsequent work (Inner Monologue [25]) that incorporates richer feedback loops, indicating SayCan is a **foundational step rather than a complete solution for robust autonomy**.

**Dynamic Environment Changes**

The system queries value functions at each decision step, but does not explicitly model how the environment might change during skill execution. If an object is moved by a human while the robot is executing a multi-step plan, SayCan may continue with outdated assumptions. Figure 16(a) shows a failure where "the affordance model fails to identify either bag of chips as pickable, though the language model approaches the counter twice"—suggesting the system may persist with infeasible plans rather than replanning when affordances change.

**Multi-Agent or Collaborative Scenarios**

The paper evaluates SayCan in a single-robot, single-user setting. Scenarios involving multiple robots coordinating, or robots collaborating with humans on shared tasks, are not addressed. The dialog structure ("How would you" / "I would") assumes a single agent responding to a single user's instruction.

**Long-Horizon Task Robustness**

Table 2 shows Long-Horizon tasks achieve only 47% execution success—the lowest among all instruction families. Section 5.1 explains: "most failures were a result of early termination by the LLM (e.g., bringing one object but not the second)." Figure 15(b) shows a 9-step plan that "narrowly avoids an early termination at step 5," illustrating how fragile long-horizon planning remains. The affordance rating helps overcome premature termination, but does not eliminate it.

### 6.3 Computational and Data Constraints

**Language Model Inference Cost**

SayCan uses the 540B parameter PaLM model for main experiments. At each planning step, the system must query the LLM to score all candidate skills (551 skills in the full repertoire). Algorithm 1 shows this scoring occurs in a loop until termination. For a 10-step task, this requires 10 × 551 = 5,510 LLM scoring operations. The paper does not report inference latency, but 540B parameter models require substantial computational resources. The open-source version uses GPT-3 (Section 6), which is more accessible but still requires API calls. This creates a **deployment barrier for resource-constrained settings**.

**Value Function Training Requirements**

While SayCan avoids joint training of LLM and policies, it still requires substantial investment in low-level skill training:
- **Behavioral Cloning**: 68,000 teleoperated demonstrations collected over 11 months using a fleet of 10 robots, plus 276,000 autonomous episodes filtered to 12,000 successful episodes (Appendix C.2)
- **Reinforcement Learning**: 16 TPUv3 chips for ~100 hours, plus pools of 3,000 CPU workers each for episode collection and target Q-value computation (Appendix C.2)
- **Evaluation**: 366 real-world model evaluations over the project duration (Appendix C.3, Figure 11)

This represents significant infrastructure investment. The claim that robotic performance scales with LLM improvements (Section 5.1) is true, but **only after this substantial upfront investment in skill learning**.

**Value Function Calibration Sensitivity**

Appendix D.2 reveals that raw value function outputs require empirical calibration:
- Pick skills: $v^{\text{max}}_{\text{pick}} = 0.5$, $v^{\text{min}}_{\text{pick}} = 0.2$
- Navigation: $d^{\text{max}}_{\text{goto}} = 100$ meters, $d^{\text{min}}_{\text{goto}} = 0$
- Terminate: fixed affordance of 0.1

The paper states: "The parameters used for calibration are determined empirically." This introduces a **hyperparameter tuning burden** that may not transfer across environments. If the robot is deployed in a larger space, the distance thresholds may need adjustment. If pick success rates change, the value function normalization may need recalibration.

**Prompt Engineering Sensitivity**

Appendix D.3, Table 5 shows planning performance varies substantially with prompt examples:

| Num Examples | Require Termination |
|---|---|
| 0 | 10% |
| 1 | 64% |
| 4 | 82% |
| 17 (Full) | 88% |

This 78 percentage point swing (10% → 88%) demonstrates that **prompt engineering is critical to performance**. The paper notes several lessons: "Examples which overly include objects used in the actual planning tend to bias results to those objects" and "Phrasing of the natural language names of skills and objects is important due to the auto-regressive nature of the LLM scoring." This creates a maintenance burden—adding new skills requires updating prompts to avoid bias.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

**Error Attribution Reveals LLM Dependency**

Section 5.1 provides a critical diagnostic: "Overall, 65% of the errors were LLM failures and 35% were affordance failures." This means **two-thirds of failures stem from the language model**, not the robotic components. While this validates the value of grounding (affordances catch 35% of errors), it also means SayCan's performance ceiling is tightly coupled to LLM capabilities. Improvements in value functions alone cannot address the majority of failures.

**Environment Generalization Gap**

Table 2 shows execution success drops from 74% (mock kitchen) to 60% (real kitchen)—a 14 percentage point decrease. Planning success drops only 3% (84% → 81%), indicating that **low-level policies struggle with domain shift more than the planner**. This is expected but limits real-world deployment. The paper states this indicates policies "generalize reasonably well," but 60% execution success may be insufficient for practical applications requiring high reliability.

**Object and Vocabulary Scalability**

The experiments use 15 objects and 5 locations. Section 5.1 notes: "NL Nouns performed worse than NL Verbs, due to the number of nouns possible (15 objects and 5 locations) versus number of verbs (6)." This suggests performance may degrade as the vocabulary expands. With 15 objects, NL Nouns achieve 47% execution; scaling to 100+ objects could significantly impact accuracy. The LLM scoring must evaluate all candidates at each step, creating both computational and accuracy challenges at scale.

**No Learning from Execution Failures**

SayCan does not update its value functions or LLM prompts based on execution outcomes. If a skill fails repeatedly in certain contexts, the system does not adapt. Section 8 poses this as future work: "how the information gained through grounding the LLM via real-world robotic experience can be leveraged to improve the language model itself." The current framework is **static at deployment**—it cannot improve from experience without manual retraining.

**Termination Condition Ambiguity**

The system terminates when the "done" skill is selected. Appendix D.3 shows that without examples, the LLM issues "done" only 10% of the time. Even with the full prompt, early termination remains a failure mode for Long-Horizon tasks (Section 5.1). The paper does not explore alternative termination conditions, such as confidence thresholds or explicit user confirmation.

**Bias Inheritance from LLMs**

Section 8 acknowledges: "we expect this method to inherit the limitations and biases of LLMs [82, 83], including the dependence on the training data." This includes potential biases in task interpretation (e.g., gendered assumptions about certain tasks), cultural biases in object naming, or safety concerns (the LLM might suggest unsafe actions that pass affordance checks). The paper does not evaluate or mitigate these biases.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

SayCan makes deliberate architectural trade-offs that enable its capabilities while introducing constraints:

| Trade-off | Benefit | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| **Independent LLM + Value Functions** | Zero-shot generalization, LLM scaling | No joint optimization, potential signal mismatch |
| **Multiplicative Combination** | Clean probabilistic interpretation, prevents impossible actions | Zero affordance eliminates skill regardless of LLM confidence |
| **Scoring Mode (not Generative)** | Constrained to valid skills, interpretable | Requires enumerating all candidates, computationally expensive |
| **Fixed Skill Vocabulary** | Reliable low-level execution, known affordances | Cannot execute novel skills, ceiling on capabilities |
| **No Online Learning** | Stable behavior, no catastrophic forgetting | Cannot improve from execution failures |
| **Known Object Locations** | Simplifies navigation planning | Limits applicability to structured environments |

These trade-offs position SayCan as a **pragmatic framework for structured environments with well-defined skill repertoires**, rather than a general solution for open-world robotics. The system excels when:
- The task can be decomposed into available skills
- Object locations are known or detectable
- The LLM can correctly interpret the instruction
- Low-level policies are robust enough for execution

The system struggles when:
- Novel motor capabilities are required
- Objects must be actively searched for
- Instructions involve negation or complex reasoning
- Real-time adaptation to failures is needed

Understanding these boundaries is essential for deploying SayCan appropriately and for identifying where future research should focus—particularly on closed-loop feedback, skill learning, and LLM reasoning improvements.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

SayCan represents more than an incremental improvement in robotic instruction-following—it establishes a new architectural paradigm for combining semantic reasoning with physical control. This section examines how the work reshapes the field's approach to embodied AI, what research trajectories it opens, where it can be practically deployed, and when practitioners should choose this method over alternatives.

### 7.1 Paradigm Shift: Decoupling Semantic Reasoning from Physical Control

The most significant contribution of SayCan is demonstrating that **robotic performance can scale with language model improvements without collecting additional robotic data**. This finding fundamentally changes how the field thinks about the robotics learning pipeline.

**Traditional Robotics Scaling:**
Historically, improving a robot's task success rate required:
- More demonstration data (expensive to collect on physical hardware)
- Better sensors or actuators (hardware costs)
- Refined control policies (task-specific training for each new capability)
- Joint training of perception, language, and control (computationally intensive)

**SayCan's New Scaling Path:**
The paper provides empirical evidence in Table 3 that upgrading from FLAN 137B to PaLM 540B improves execution success from 61% to 74% **with identical robotic components**. Section 5.1 emphasizes this is "particularly exciting because it represents the first time we can see how an improvement in language models translates to a similar improvement in robotics."

This decoupling has three profound implications:

1. **Robotics Can Leverage NLP Progress**: Instead of waiting for robot-specific breakthroughs, the field can benefit from advances in general language modeling. As LLMs improve in reasoning, multilingual understanding, and world knowledge, robotic systems using SayCan automatically inherit these capabilities.

2. **Modular Development Becomes Viable**: The separation between LLM planning and value function grounding means teams can specialize. NLP researchers can improve the planning component without robotic expertise, while robotics researchers can improve low-level skills without language model training infrastructure.

3. **Zero-Shot Generalization Is Achievable**: The 101-task evaluation demonstrates that a single system can handle diverse instructions without task-specific training. Section 5.2 shows new skills (drawer manipulation) can be added with only prompt updates and value functions—no LLM retraining required.

This paradigm shift positions SayCan as a **foundational architecture** rather than a task-specific solution. The paper's Related Work (Section 7) explicitly contrasts this with prior approaches that require joint training or task-specific fine-tuning, establishing SayCan as a new reference point for embodied AI research.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several research trajectories that build on SayCan's foundation. Some have already been pursued in concurrent or subsequent work, while others remain open.

**Closed-Loop Feedback and Recovery**

Section 5.2 notes that "as presented herein, SayCan only receives environmental feedback through value functions at the current decision step." This limitation means the system cannot react if a skill fails mid-execution or if the environment changes unexpectedly. The paper references Huang et al. [25] (Inner Monologue) as work that "builds upon SayCan to enable closed-loop planning by leveraging environment feedback (from e.g., success detectors, scene descriptors, or even human feedback) through an inner monologue."

This suggests a clear research direction: **integrating richer feedback mechanisms** into the SayCan architecture. Potential approaches include:
- Adding success/failure detectors that trigger replanning
- Incorporating visual scene descriptions between skill executions
- Enabling human intervention when the robot detects uncertainty
- Using the LLM to interpret feedback and generate recovery plans

**Bidirectional Learning: Using Robotics to Improve LLMs**

Section 8 poses a compelling question: "how the information gained through grounding the LLM via real-world robotic experience can be leveraged to improve the language model itself, both in terms of its factuality and its ability to perform common-sense reasoning about real-world environments and physics."

Currently, the flow of information is unidirectional: the LLM provides semantic knowledge to the robot, but the robot's experiences do not update the LLM. A bidirectional system could:
- Use execution failures to fine-tune or prompt the LLM about physical constraints
- Collect real-world interaction data to improve the LLM's understanding of affordances
- Create feedback loops where robotic success rates inform language model training

This remains an open research question with significant potential impact on both robotics and NLP.

**Alternative Grounding Sources**

Section 8 suggests exploring "what other sources of grounding could be incorporated in the same manner, such as non-robotic contexts." The value function module provides a general interface for any signal that estimates feasibility. Potential alternatives include:
- Object detection confidence scores (as used in the open-source environment with ViLD [27])
- Collision prediction models
- Human preference models (learning which actions users find acceptable)
- Safety classifiers (filtering out potentially dangerous actions)
- Physical simulation rollouts (predicting outcomes before execution)

The modular architecture makes this exploration tractable—researchers can swap grounding sources without modifying the LLM component.

**Natural Language as Robot Programming Ontology**

Section 8 raises a fundamental question: "whether natural language is the right ontology to use to program robots." The paper notes advantages (contextual and semantic cues, abstraction level) and disadvantages (requires supervision, may not be most descriptive for certain tasks) compared to alternatives like "hindsight goal images [84]."

This opens research into:
- Comparing language-based programming to visual goal specifications
- Hybrid ontologies combining language, images, and demonstrations
- Learning which ontology works best for different task classes
- Automatically translating between ontologies (e.g., language to goal images)

**Scaling Skill Repertoires**

The experiments use 551 skills across 17 objects and 5 locations. Section 5.1 notes that "NL Nouns performed worse than NL Verbs, due to the number of nouns possible (15 objects and 5 locations) versus number of verbs (6)." This suggests performance may degrade as the vocabulary expands.

Research directions include:
- Hierarchical skill organization to manage large vocabularies
- Efficient scoring methods that don't require evaluating all candidates
- Dynamic skill discovery (learning new skills from demonstration or exploration)
- Transfer learning to apply skills across objects and environments

**Addressing LLM Limitations**

Section 5.1 identifies specific LLM weaknesses that propagate to SayCan: "struggles with negation (e.g., 'bring me a snack that isn't an apple') and ambiguous references." While Chain-of-Thought prompting (Section 5.2, Table 4) mitigates some issues, the paper acknowledges this "remains a dependency on LLM capabilities rather than a solved problem within the robotics framework itself."

Research could focus on:
- Robotics-specific prompt engineering techniques
- Fine-tuning LLMs on robotic interaction data
- Combining multiple LLMs for different reasoning tasks
- External knowledge bases to supplement LLM reasoning

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

Based on the experimental evaluation, SayCan is well-suited for specific application domains while less appropriate for others.

**Well-Suited Applications:**

| Application Domain | Why SayCan Fits | Evidence from Paper |
|---|---|---|
| **Office/Kitchen Assistance** | Structured environment, known objects, repetitive tasks | 101 tasks evaluated in office kitchen with 74% mock kitchen execution |
| **Elderly/Disability Support** | Natural language interface, no technical expertise required | Crowd-sourced instructions (Table 1) show system handles natural human requests |
| **Warehouse Pick-and-Place** | Known object locations, well-defined skill set | Navigation and manipulation skills demonstrated with 551-skill repertoire |
| **Laboratory Automation** | Precise instructions, safety-critical (interpretability matters) | Figure 6 shows interpretable decision-making with explicit scores |
| **Retail Restocking** | Temporally extended tasks, multiple objects | Long-horizon tasks (47% execution) and drawer manipulation (Section 5.2) |

**Less Suitable Applications:**

| Application Domain | Why SayCan Struggles | Evidence from Paper |
|---|---|---|
| **Unstructured Environments** | Assumes known object locations (Appendix D.2) | Navigation uses classical planning to known coordinates |
| **Novel Motor Skills Required** | Fixed skill vocabulary (551 skills) | Section 8: "primary bottleneck is in the range and capabilities of underlying skills" |
| **Real-Time Reaction Needed** | No closed-loop feedback during skill execution | Section 5.2: "not easily able to react to situations where individual skills fail" |
| **High Reliability Required** | 60% real kitchen execution may be insufficient | Table 2 shows 14% execution drop from mock to real kitchen |
| **Complex Logical Reasoning** | LLM inherits negation/ambiguity struggles | Section 5.1: 65% of errors were LLM failures |

**Deployment Considerations:**

The open-source environment (Section 6) provides a starting point for practitioners:
- **Tabletop environment** with UR5 robot and colored blocks/bowls
- **CLIPort policy** for pick-and-place
- **ViLD object detector** for affordances (instead of learned value functions)
- **GPT-3** as the language model

This simplified setup allows researchers to experiment with SayCan's principles without the full robotic infrastructure. However, practitioners should note the performance gap: the main experiments used PaLM 540B with learned value functions achieving 74% execution, while the open-source version uses GPT-3 with object detection affordances.

### 7.4 When to Prefer SayCan Over Alternatives

Based on the paper's comparisons and ablations, here is guidance on when SayCan is the appropriate choice:

**Choose SayCan When:**

1. **You Have Pretrained Low-Level Skills**: SayCan requires a repertoire of skills with policies and value functions. If you already have manipulation/navigation policies (from BC-Z [13], MT-Opt [14], or similar), SayCan provides a planning layer without retraining.

2. **Zero-Shot Instruction Following Is Required**: Table 2 shows BC NL achieves 0% on all tasks—low-level policies alone cannot interpret abstract instructions. If you need to handle diverse natural language commands without task-specific training, SayCan's LLM planning is essential.

3. **Interpretability Matters**: Figure 6 and the 65%/35% error attribution (Section 5.1) provide diagnostic visibility that end-to-end systems lack. For safety-critical or user-facing applications where understanding failures is important, SayCan's explicit scoring is valuable.

4. **You Want to Leverage LLM Improvements**: Table 3 demonstrates that upgrading the language model improves robotic performance. If you anticipate LLM capabilities will improve over your system's lifetime, SayCan's modular architecture allows upgrades without retraining robotic components.

5. **Tasks Are Temporally Extended**: Long-horizon tasks (10+ steps) require planning that low-level policies cannot provide. While SayCan achieves only 47% execution on long-horizon tasks (Table 2), this substantially exceeds baselines (FLAN achieves 33%).

**Choose Alternatives When:**

1. **You Need End-to-End Learning**: If you have abundant task-specific data and want to optimize all components jointly, end-to-end approaches may achieve higher performance within that task's domain. SayCan sacrifices joint optimization for modularity.

2. **Closed-Loop Recovery Is Critical**: Section 5.2 acknowledges SayCan cannot react to mid-execution failures. If your application requires robust recovery (e.g., surgical robotics, autonomous driving), consider Inner Monologue [25] or other closed-loop extensions.

3. **Object Locations Are Unknown**: Appendix D.2 states SayCan assumes known object locations for navigation. If your environment requires active search or exploration, you need additional perception modules not included in the base system.

4. **Computational Resources Are Limited**: Scoring 551 skills at each planning step with a 540B parameter model requires substantial compute. The open-source version uses GPT-3, but API costs and latency may still be prohibitive for some deployments.

5. **Novel Motor Skills Are Frequently Required**: Section 8 emphasizes the skill repertoire is the "primary bottleneck." If your application regularly requires new motor capabilities, you may need a system that can learn skills online rather than relying on a fixed vocabulary.

### 7.5 Summary: SayCan's Position in the Research Landscape

SayCan establishes a new reference architecture for embodied AI that separates semantic reasoning from physical grounding. Its key contributions to the field include:

1. **Empirical Evidence for LLM Scaling in Robotics**: Table 3 provides the first demonstration that robotic performance improves with language model upgrades, suggesting a new pathway for scaling embodied systems.

2. **Modular Architecture for Zero-Shot Generalization**: The independence between LLM and value functions enables 101-task evaluation without task-specific training, establishing a benchmark for generalization.

3. **Diagnostic Interpretability**: The 65%/35% error attribution and per-skill scoring (Figure 6) provide debugging capabilities absent in end-to-end systems, enabling targeted improvements.

4. **Foundation for Follow-Up Work**: Inner Monologue [25], Chain-of-Thought integration (Section 5.2), and the open-source environment (Section 6) demonstrate that SayCan serves as a platform for further research rather than a final solution.

The paper's Conclusion (Section 8) positions SayCan as "a particular way of connecting and factorizing the challenges of language understanding and robotics" with "many further extensions" possible. This framing invites the research community to build on SayCan's foundation while addressing its limitations—particularly in closed-loop feedback, skill learning, and LLM reasoning.

For practitioners, SayCan offers a pragmatic approach to natural language robotic control in structured environments with well-defined skill repertoires. For researchers, it provides a modular architecture that enables systematic investigation of how language models and physical grounding interact. The work's true impact lies not in solving embodied AI completely, but in establishing a clear, reproducible framework that makes the grounding problem tractable for systematic improvement.
