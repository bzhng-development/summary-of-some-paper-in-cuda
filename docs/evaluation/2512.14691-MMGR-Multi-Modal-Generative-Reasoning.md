## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces MMGR (Multi-Modal Generative Reasoning), a principled evaluation framework and benchmark suite of 1,853 samples designed to address the critical gap between perceptual fidelity and genuine reasoning capabilities in video foundation models. Grounded in five core abilities (Physical, Logical, 3D/2D Spatial, Temporal), MMGR assesses state-of-the-art systems like Veo-3, Sora-2, and Nano-banana Pro across three domains: Abstract Reasoning, Embodied Navigation, and Physical Commonsense. The evaluation reveals severe reasoning deficits, such as &lt;10% accuracy on ARC-AGI and only 3.64% holistic success on Simultaneous Localization and Generation (SLAG), demonstrating that current models prioritize visual plausibility over the causal consistency required for true world simulation.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Perceptual Fidelity ≠ Reasoning Capability

The MMGR paper addresses a fundamental disconnect in how we evaluate modern video generation systems. To understand why this matters, we need to distinguish between two fundamentally different capabilities:

**Perceptual fidelity** asks: *Does this video look realistic?* Can the model render photorealistic textures, maintain smooth motion, and produce visually coherent frames?

**Reasoning capability** asks: *Does this video obey the laws of reality?* Does the model understand that solid objects cannot pass through each other, that gravity pulls downward, that a maze solution requires a continuous path without crossing walls?

The critical insight from this paper is that **current evaluation metrics measure the first question but are blind to the second**. As stated in the Introduction (Section 1), conventional metrics like Fréchet Video Distance (FVD), Inception Score (IS), and CLIP-based similarity "prioritize perceptual fidelity: assessing whether a video looks realistic or aligns semantically with a caption. Yet, these metrics remain blind to world consistency and physical plausibility."

This creates what the authors call a "hallucination gap": a model might render a visually stunning billiards shot where balls pass through one another, or a navigation sequence where an agent teleports through walls. These outputs satisfy texture-based metrics while violating fundamental laws of reality.

### Why This Problem Matters: From Animation to World Simulation

The stakes extend far beyond benchmark scores. The paper positions video generation as a stepping stone toward **world models**—systems that can simulate reality for downstream applications. This distinction carries both theoretical and practical significance:

**Theoretical Significance:** The paper draws on cognitive science literature (Spelke & Kinzler, 2007; Lake et al., 2017) to argue that robust world simulation rests on five complementary pillars of reasoning that mirror human "core knowledge":

1. **Physical Reasoning**: Understanding intuitive physics (object permanence, gravity, collisions)
2. **Logical Reasoning**: Manipulating abstract concepts and following rules
3. **3D Spatial Reasoning**: Understanding depth, viewpoint transformation, and occlusion
4. **2D Spatial Reasoning**: Interpreting visual layouts and relative positions in the image plane
5. **Temporal Reasoning**: Modeling causality, event order, and long-range dependencies

Without these capabilities, video models remain sophisticated animation engines rather than genuine simulators. As the Introduction states: "for video generation to evolve from mere image animation to genuine world modeling, models must acquire foundational reasoning capabilities akin to human intuitive physics and cognition."

**Real-World Impact:** The applications mentioned in the Introduction—filmmaking, scientific visualization, embodied simulation, and robotics—all require more than visual plausibility. A robotics training simulator that allows agents to walk through walls teaches incorrect policies. A scientific visualization that violates conservation laws misleads researchers. The gap between perceptual quality and reasoning correctness directly limits the utility of these systems in high-stakes domains.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

To understand MMGR's contribution, we must examine what came before and where those approaches fall short.

**Video Generation Architecture Evolution (Section 2, Related Work):** The field has progressed through three generations:
- **GAN-based approaches** (Vondrick et al., 2016; Tulyakov et al., 2018): Early systems with limited temporal coherence
- **Diffusion-based systems** (Ho et al., 2022b; Singer et al., 2022): Improved quality but still focused on appearance
- **Large-scale transformer architectures** (Yan et al., 2021; Hong et al., 2022): Current state-of-the-art including Sora, Veo, and Kling

While these architectures demonstrate "exceptional capacity for synthesizing high-fidelity, photorealistic video with complex temporal dynamics," the Related Work section notes that "the extent to which they internalize the underlying physical laws and logical constraints of the world remains an active area of inquiry."

**Evaluation Metric Limitations:** Traditional metrics fall into three categories, all with the same fundamental blind spot:

| Metric Type | Examples | What It Measures | What It Misses |
|-------------|----------|------------------|----------------|
| Distribution-based | FVD (Unterthiner et al., 2018b) | Statistical similarity to real video distribution | Whether generated content obeys physical laws |
| Quality-based | Inception Score (Salimans et al., 2016) | Image quality and diversity | Logical consistency across frames |
| Semantic alignment | CLIP-based (Radford et al., 2021) | Text-video semantic matching | Causal correctness of depicted events |

The paper emphasizes that these tools "are insufficient for probing world modeling capabilities. They fall short of evaluating whether a model possesses the reasoning skills necessary to generate content that is not only visually plausible but also logically coherent and physically robust over long horizons."

**Prior Benchmark Limitations:** Existing benchmarks focus on **discriminative** rather than **generative** reasoning:
- Video understanding benchmarks (Girdhar & Ramanan, 2020; Goyal et al., 2017; Chollet, 2019) test models' ability to *recognize* interactions or perform reasoning on *existing* inputs
- Embodied AI benchmarks (Savva et al., 2019) rely on rigid simulators to test perception, not generation

As the Related Work section explains: "Our work shifts this paradigm from understanding to generation: requiring models to not merely interpret a video, but to manifest reasoning processes through synthesis."

**Recent Emergent Reasoning Studies:** The paper acknowledges concurrent work exploring reasoning in video models:
- Wiedemer et al. (2025) identify emergent "Chain-of-Frames" (CoF) reasoning in models like Veo-3
- Guo et al. (2025) utilize MME-CoF to expose failures in geometric consistency
- Tong et al. (2025) demonstrate competitive performance by Sora-2 across vision tasks

However, MMGR positions itself as more comprehensive: "Unlike prior works that focus on specific failure modes or emergent properties, we provide a holistic assessment spanning Abstract Reasoning, Embodied Navigation, and Physical Commonsense."

### How MMGR Positions Itself: A Diagnostic Framework

MMGR does not propose a new generation architecture. Instead, it provides what the Abstract calls "a diagnostic lens into the reasoning deficits of modern generative models." This positioning is deliberate and important:

**Systematic Coverage:** Rather than testing isolated capabilities, MMGR evaluates across three complementary domains (Figure 1, Figure 2, Table 1):
- **Abstract Reasoning**: Non-photorealistic environments testing Logical, 2D Spatial, and Temporal reasoning (Maze, Sudoku, ARC-AGI, Math)
- **Embodied Navigation**: Agent-centric perspectives testing Physical, 2D/3D Spatial, and Temporal reasoning (3D Navigation, Last-Mile, Top-down, SLAG)
- **Physical Commonsense**: Intuitive physics testing Physical, 3D Spatial, and Temporal reasoning (Physical Concepts, Sports)

**Fine-Grained Diagnostics:** Unlike binary success/failure metrics, MMGR decomposes performance into component failures. For example, the Maze task (Section 5.3) tracks:
- `Maze Changed`: Does the environment structure remain stable?
- `Cross Wall`: Does the agent respect impermeable boundaries?
- `Action Reflection`: Does the model show exploratory behavior?
- `Target Achievement`: Does the agent reach the goal?

This granularity reveals *why* models fail, not just *that* they fail.

**Human Calibration:** A critical contribution is the systematic comparison between automated VLM-based evaluation and human judgment. The paper repeatedly finds that "automated evaluators systematically overestimate success by missing transient but fatal errors" (Section 5.5.2). For instance, on the Maze task, human evaluation reveals Veo-3's true success rate is 0–20% while Auto-Eval reports 39–52%—a 2–5× overestimation.

**Training Paradigm Diagnosis:** Beyond benchmarking, MMGR uses its results to identify three fundamental bottlenecks in current training recipes (Introduction):
1. **Training Data Imbalance**: Naturalistic physical data abounds; structured symbolic reasoning data is scarce
2. **Architectural Limitations**: Current designs sacrifice global consistency for local plausibility
3. **Optimization Objective Gaps**: Objectives reward perceptual fidelity over logical validity

This diagnostic framing positions MMGR not as an endpoint but as "a concrete roadmap toward physically grounded, logically consistent, and reasoning-aware world models."

### The Stakes: Why Now?

The timing of this work matters. The paper notes that "as generative models scale, evaluation remains a critical bottleneck." With systems like Sora-2, Veo-3, and Wan-2.2 achieving striking visual quality, there is risk of conflating perceptual progress with reasoning progress. MMGR provides the measurement tools to distinguish between:

- **Pattern matching**: Memorizing answer patterns without genuine multi-step reasoning (evidenced by the gap between Final Correctness at 74% and Intermediate Correctness at 12% on Math tasks)
- **Genuine reasoning**: Maintaining constraint satisfaction across extended generation horizons

The paper's conclusion is stark: current models exhibit a "hallucination of competence," generating correct final outcomes despite invalid intermediate reasoning steps. Without benchmarks like MMGR to expose these deficits, the field risks optimizing for the appearance of reasoning rather than reasoning itself.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an evaluation benchmark paper that constructs a systematic diagnostic framework rather than proposing a new generative architecture. The core idea is to measure whether video and image generation models possess genuine reasoning capabilities by testing them on tasks where visual plausibility alone cannot produce correct answers.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

MMGR is a structured evaluation pipeline that takes generative model outputs (videos or images) and scores them using both automated vision-language models and human annotators across 1,853 carefully designed reasoning tasks. The solution addresses the perceptual-reasoning gap by creating tasks where correct answers require satisfying multiple logical, spatial, and physical constraints simultaneously—making it impossible to succeed through pattern matching or visual trickery alone.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The MMGR benchmark operates as a four-stage evaluation pipeline with the following major components:

1. **Task Generation Module**: Programmatically creates evaluation samples across three domains (Abstract Reasoning, Embodied Navigation, Physical Commonsense) with controlled difficulty levels, producing 1,853 total samples with known ground-truth solutions.

2. **Model Generation Interface**: Sends standardized prompts to target generative models (Veo-3, Sora-2, Wan-2.2 for video; Nano-banana, GPT-4o-image, Qwen-image for images), collecting 5 samples per prompt to account for stochastic variability.

3. **Dual Evaluation Engine**: Processes generated outputs through two parallel channels—an automated VLM evaluator (Gemini-2.5-Pro) that scores fine-grained metrics, and a human annotation system that validates a subset of outputs for ground-truth calibration.

4. **Metrics Aggregation Layer**: Combines fine-grained binary scores into holistic "Overall" metrics that require all sub-components to pass simultaneously, revealing the gap between partial competence and complete task success.

Information flows sequentially: tasks are generated first, then sent to models for generation, then evaluated by both automated and human systems, and finally aggregated into diagnostic reports that identify specific failure modes.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **Five-core reasoning framework**: We first explain the theoretical foundation—why these five abilities (Physical, Logical, 3D Spatial, 2D Spatial, Temporal) were selected and how they map to cognitive science literature.
- **Three evaluation domains**: We then detail each domain's task structure, showing how Abstract Reasoning, Embodied Navigation, and Physical Commonsense exercise different combinations of the five core abilities.
- **Hard-level control mechanisms**: We explain how difficulty is systematically varied across tasks (grid sizes, environmental complexity, trajectory distance) to enable fine-grained capability analysis.
- **Evaluation protocol design**: We describe the VLM-based auto-evaluation system, the human annotation pipeline, and why both are necessary for reliable measurement.
- **Metrics architecture**: We break down how fine-grained binary metrics combine into holistic scores, and why this conjunction-based design exposes the "illusion of competence."
- **Model selection and generation settings**: We specify which models were evaluated, how many samples were generated per prompt, and what controls ensured fair comparison.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### The Five-Core Reasoning Framework: Theoretical Foundation

MMGR grounds its evaluation design in cognitive science theories of human "core knowledge"—innate reasoning systems that develop early and support interaction with the physical world. The paper explicitly draws on Spelke & Kinzler (2007) and Lake et al. (2017) to justify five complementary reasoning pillars, each targeting a distinct cognitive capability that current video models may lack.

**Physical Reasoning** tests whether models understand intuitive physics principles like object permanence, gravity, collisions, and material properties. This capability aligns with what cognitive scientists call "core knowledge" in human cognition (Baillargeon, 1987; Ullman et al., 2017) and is operationalized in MMGR through tasks like Physical Commonsense (Section 14), where models must generate videos showing plausible interactions between solid and fluid objects. A model passes only if objects behave according to conservation laws—for example, a coffee grinder must show beans fracturing progressively rather than magically transforming into grounds.

**Logical Reasoning** evaluates whether models can manipulate abstract concepts, follow explicit rules, and perform deductive operations where "if A happens, then B follows." This mirrors what psychologists call System 2 reasoning (Kahneman, 2011; Marcus, 2001), enabling generalization beyond simple pattern matching. MMGR tests this through Sudoku (Section 6) and ARC-AGI (Section 7), where models must satisfy constraint systems that cannot be solved through visual similarity alone. For Sudoku, every row, column, and subgrid must contain unique digits—a rule that requires global constraint satisfaction rather than local pixel prediction.

**3D Spatial Reasoning** assesses whether models understand depth relationships, viewpoint transformations, and occlusion handling in volumetric environments. This involves building what cognitive science calls an internal "cognitive map" (Tolman, 1948; Gibson, 1979) to ensure geometric consistency across camera viewpoints. MMGR evaluates this through Embodied Navigation tasks (Sections 9-13), particularly 3D Real-World Navigation and SLAG, where models must navigate multi-floor environments while maintaining consistent spatial relationships between the agent and surrounding geometry.

**2D Spatial Reasoning** tests accurate interpretation of visual layouts, shapes, and relative positions in the projected image plane. This relies on compositional image understanding (Biederman, 1987; Kosslyn, 1980) to correctly ground spatial prepositions in complex prompts. MMGR distinguishes this from 3D reasoning because they rely on fundamentally different perceptual mechanisms—2D reasoning operates on planar relationships like adjacency and relative positioning, while 3D reasoning requires depth estimation and viewpoint transformation. Maze-solving (Section 5) and Sudoku (Section 6) primarily test 2D spatial reasoning, as they involve grid-based logic on a flat plane.

**Temporal Reasoning** evaluates whether models can model causality, event order, and long-range dependencies across extended sequences. This captures the human perceptual ability to segment continuous streams into discrete causal events (Michotte, 1963; Zacks & Tversky, 2001), which is essential for maintaining narrative coherence. MMGR tests this across all video generation tasks, measuring whether models maintain consistency across frames—for example, in ARC-AGI (Section 7.3), demonstration examples should remain static throughout the video, but video models often exhibit "context drift" where the problem definition itself becomes corrupted during generation.

Table 1 in the paper provides the complete mapping between tasks and reasoning abilities, showing that each task exercises multiple abilities simultaneously. For instance, the Maze task requires Physical reasoning (walls are impermeable), Logical reasoning (pathfinding is a search problem), 2D Spatial reasoning (grid topology), and Temporal reasoning (sequential movement). This multi-ability design ensures that models cannot succeed by optimizing for a single capability.

#### Three Evaluation Domains: Task Structure and Design Rationale

MMGR organizes its 1,853 evaluation samples into three complementary domains, each targeting different combinations of the five core reasoning abilities. This domain structure ensures comprehensive coverage while enabling targeted diagnosis of specific failure modes.

**Domain 1: Abstract Reasoning (1,323 samples)** evaluates Logical, 2D Spatial, and Temporal reasoning in non-photorealistic environments where visual fidelity is irrelevant to correctness. This domain includes four task types:

- **Maze (240 samples, Section 5)**: Models must navigate a valid path from a green start cell to a red goal cell while avoiding black walls. The paper uses the `maze-dataset` library (Ivanitskiy et al., 2023) with two generation algorithms (Depth-First Search and Wilson's Algorithm) across ten grid sizes (3×3 to 13×13) and four start-goal placement schemes (corner-to-corner, corner-to-random, random-to-corner, random-to-random). Each maze has exactly one solution path, enabling precise evaluation of whether the model found the correct route versus guessing. Difficulty is stratified as Easy (3×3–5×5, 80 samples), Medium (6×6–9×9, 80 samples), and Hard (10×10–13×13, 80 samples).

- **Sudoku (300 samples, Section 6)**: Models must complete grids such that every row, column, and subgrid contains unique digits. The benchmark uses an open-source Sudoku generation library (Seely et al., 2025) with two grid sizes (4×4 with digits 1–4, and 9×9 with digits 1–9) and three difficulty levels controlled by the number of initial clues (Easy with many clues, Medium, Hard with few clues while ensuring a unique solution). For each difficulty level, 100 puzzles are generated (50 for 4×4, 50 for 9×9), creating a balanced evaluation set spanning controlled structural complexity.

- **ARC-AGI (456 samples, Section 7)**: Models must infer latent transformation rules from input-output demonstration examples and apply them to unseen test cases. The benchmark combines ARC-AGI v1 (381 tasks from the publicly released training set) and v2 (75 newly added tasks with novel pattern families and higher structural complexity). Tasks are classified along two dimensions: Shape Consistency (Match: 316 cases where output dimensions equal input; Mismatch: 140 cases where dimensions differ) and Quantitative Difficulty (Easy, Medium, Hard based on five grid-level features including grid size, color count, object count, occupancy ratio, and grid change ratio). Table 8 reports the full distribution: v1 contains 136 Easy, 181 Medium, 64 Hard cases; v2 contains 1 Easy, 34 Medium, 40 Hard cases.

- **Math (327 samples, Section 8)**: Models must solve mathematical problems across five benchmarks spanning difficulty levels: GSM8K (50 grade school problems), MATH500 (50 high school competition problems), AIME 2024 (30 invitational examination problems), AIME 2025 (30 invitational examination problems), and Omni-MATH (167 Olympiad-level problems with fine-grained categorization). For Omni-MATH, problems are classified by difficulty (T0–T4, where T0 is easiest suitable for middle school and T4 is hardest Olympiad-level) and category (Algebra, Applied Math, Calculus, Discrete Math, Geometry, Precalculus, Number Theory, Other). Table 19 shows the distribution: T0 has 34 samples, T1 has 33, T2 has 36, T3 has 34, T4 has 30.

**Domain 2: Embodied Navigation (480 samples)** assesses Physical, 2D/3D Spatial, and Temporal reasoning from an agent-centric perspective, testing whether models can synthesize successful trajectories within diverse settings. This domain includes four task types, each with 120 samples:

- **3D Real-World Navigation (120 samples, Section 12)**: Utilizes cutaway "dollhouse" renderings from Matterport3D (Chang et al., 2017) and HM3D (Ramakrishnan et al., 2021b), assessing multi-room and multi-level spatial reasoning from a fixed third-person perspective. Models must interpret full 3D scene structures including verticality and complex room connectivity.

- **Last-Mile Navigation (Ego-centric) (120 samples, Section 10)**: Presents a 360° panoramic environment via a proximal "over-the-shoulder" view, requiring models to synthesize wide-field visual context to execute short-range navigation. This task necessitates interpretation of agent-centric layouts to generate goal-directed trajectories.

- **Top-down View Navigation (120 samples, Section 11)**: Adopts a fixed bird's-eye perspective, targeting global spatial planning and long-horizon prediction. Models generate trajectories on 2D overhead maps, emphasizing the ability to reason about global geometry and multi-step pathfinding.

- **Simultaneous Localization and Generation (SLAG) (120 samples, Section 13)**: Integrates both 3D and top-down views, challenging models to jointly localize the agent while generating the surrounding scene layout. This requires maintaining geometric coherence and performing cross-view spatial alignment across distinct observation modalities. SLAG extends the paradigm of Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) (Durrant-Whyte & Bailey, 2006) by coupling 3D spatial navigation with real-time generative mapping.

Table 23 provides the detailed distribution across 24 hard-level configurations defined by environmental complexity (1 Floor vs. 2 Plus Floors), view fidelity (quality03, quality04, quality05), trajectory distance (short vs. long), and destination specification (color mark vs. location description). Each configuration contains 5 samples per task, ensuring balanced coverage across difficulty dimensions.

**Domain 3: Physical Commonsense (50 samples)** probes understanding of intuitive physics (Battaglia et al., 2013; Yi et al., 2020) and object dynamics, extending from fundamental concepts to compositional sports scenarios. This domain includes two task types:

- **Physical Concept (25 samples, Section 14)**: Leverages the VideoPhy ontology (Bansal et al., 2024) to assess intuitive understanding of fundamental physical interactions across three core categories: Solid-Solid (143 captions in source corpus, testing rigid collisions and stacking), Solid-Fluid (146 captions, testing splashing and buoyancy), and Fluid-Fluid (55 captions, testing diffusion and mixing). The benchmark also incorporates VideoPhy v2 with 600 supplementary captions covering 197 unique physical actions. From the larger source corpus, 25 examples are randomly sampled to ensure diversity across interaction categories and physical domains.

- **Sports (25 samples, Section 14)**: Evaluates compositional physical reasoning within complex scenarios characterized by the intersection of multiple physical laws. The source corpus encompasses diverse activities: Ballet (12 samples), Skiing (13 samples), Diving (12 samples), and Swimming (13 samples), challenging models to analyze phenomena such as momentum conservation, balance control, projectile motion, and fluid dynamics in goal-oriented contexts. To construct a balanced evaluation set, 25 diverse examples are randomly sampled from this larger collection.

The domain design is strategically complementary: Abstract Reasoning prioritizes Logical and 2D Spatial reasoning in controlled symbolic environments, Embodied Navigation integrates 3D Spatial, Temporal, and Physical reasoning through dynamic agent-environment interactions, and Physical Commonsense tests Physical and Temporal reasoning in photorealistic scenarios. Together, these domains exercise all five core reasoning abilities across varying levels of visual realism and symbolic abstraction.

#### Hard-Level Control Mechanisms: Systematic Difficulty Stratification

MMGR employs rigorous difficulty stratification across all tasks to enable fine-grained capability analysis and avoid ceiling or floor effects that would obscure model differences. The hard-level control mechanisms vary by domain but share a unified principle: difficulty is modulated along explicit, controllable axes rather than relying on implicit complexity.

**Abstract Reasoning Hard-Level Control** uses task-specific parameters:

For Maze (Section 5.2), difficulty varies along three axes: (1) Generators (2 types: Depth-First Search and Wilson's Algorithm to produce topologically diverse layouts), (2) Grid Sizes (10 levels ranging from 3×3 to 13×13), and (3) Start–Goal Placement (4 schemes: corner-to-corner, corner-to-random, random-to-corner, random-to-random with minimum start–goal distance enforced to rule out trivial solutions). For each generator, 120 mazes are produced comprising 40 Easy (3×3–5×5), 40 Medium (6×6–9×9), and 40 Hard (10×10–13×13) instances, yielding 240 total mazes.

For Sudoku (Section 6.1), difficulty varies along two axes: (1) Grid Size (2 levels: 4×4 with digits 1–4 and 9×9 with digits 1–9, where the larger grid substantially increases combinatorial difficulty), and (2) Puzzle Difficulty (3 levels: Easy with many clues, Medium, Hard with few clues while ensuring a unique solution). For every difficulty level, 100 puzzles are generated (50 for 4×4, 50 for 9×9), resulting in 300 total puzzles.

For ARC-AGI (Section 7.1.1), difficulty is determined by summing scores from five quantitative grid-level features: (1) Grid Size (minimum side length: &lt;8 cells scores 0, 8–15 cells scores 1, ≥16 cells scores 2), (2) Color Count (distinct colors in input: ≤3 scores 0, 4–6 scores 1, ≥7 scores 2), (3) Object Count (distinct connected components: ≤4 scores 0, 5–10 scores 1, >10 scores 2), (4) Occupancy Ratio (non-background cells / total cells: ≤0.25 scores 0, (0.25, 0.55] scores 1, >0.55 scores 2), and (5) ΔIO (grid change ratio, for Match cases only: ≤0.2 scores 0, (0.2, 0.5] scores 1, >0.5 scores 2). For Match cases (5 features), Easy ≤3, Medium 4–6, Hard ≥7. For Mismatch cases (4 features, excluding ΔIO), Easy ≤2, Medium 3–4, Hard ≥5.

For Math (Section 8.1), difficulty is inherent to the source benchmarks: GSM8K (grade school), MATH500 (high school competition), AIME 2024/2025 (invitational competitions), and Omni-MATH (Olympiad-level with explicit T0–T4 difficulty levels). Table 19 shows Omni-MATH distribution: T0 has 34 samples, T1 has 33, T2 has 36, T3 has 34, T4 has 30.

**Embodied Navigation Hard-Level Control** (Section 9.2) organizes all four tasks along four unified axes to ensure cross-task comparability while accommodating modality-specific differences:

(1) **Environmental Complexity**: Scenes are sourced from Matterport3D, HM3D, and Habitat. For Panoramic View Last-Mile Navigation, complexity is determined by spatial arrangement within a single panorama: floor01 scenes correspond to single-floor homes with no vertical transitions, whereas floor02plus scenes include multi-level structures with either implicit stairs not fully visible or explicit staircases enabling vertical navigation. For the remaining three tasks, multi-level environments present fully connected floors and additional branching regions including basements, attics, outdoor pools, and gardens.

(2) **View Fidelity**: Defined task-specifically but cross-comparably. For Panoramic View, fidelity captures how much of the environment is visually accessible, with human raters evaluating occlusions from foreground objects on a 3–5 scale (quality03 through quality05). For Top-down, 3D Real-World, and SLAG, fidelity reflects holistic assessment of scene realism and navigability, considering factors like holes or cracks, furnishing quality, door openness, and interaction plausibility, also scored 3–5.

(3) **Trajectory Distance**: Defined as geodesic separation between agent start and target. Short trajectories involve relatively direct motion with no major turns and may include vertical movement without substantial directional changes. Long trajectories include at least one significant turn, selected to share partial path structure with corresponding short cases at the same hard level for comparability.

(4) **Destination Specification**: Targets are specified either through direct visual annotation (color mark: pure red overlay #ff0000 ensuring visual distinguishability) or natural-language description (location description: annotators describe the region, including floor identifiers and spatially anchored landmarks for disambiguation when multiple similar regions exist).

Table 23 shows all 24 configuration slots (2 environmental complexity × 3 view fidelity × 2 trajectory distance × 2 destination specification), with 5 samples per configuration per task, yielding 120 samples per task and 480 total for Embodied Navigation.

**Physical Commonsense Hard-Level Control** (Section 14.2) stratifies 50 samples along three dimensions: (1) Interaction Type (Solid-Solid, Solid-Fluid, Fluid-Fluid testing different material properties), (2) Scenario Context (Physical Concepts for controlled experiments vs. Sports Scenarios for unconstrained dynamic environments), and (3) Interaction Complexity (Simple for single-object motion, Complex for multi-object interactions, Chain-Reaction for causal sequences). Table 34 shows the distribution: 25 Physical Concepts, 25 Sports Scenarios, 50 total.

#### Evaluation Protocol Design: VLM-Based Auto-Evaluation and Human Calibration

MMGR employs a dual evaluation strategy combining automated VLM-based scoring with human annotation to balance scalability with ground-truth reliability. This design addresses a critical finding: automated metrics systematically overestimate model performance by missing transient but fatal errors.

**VLM-Based Auto-Evaluation** (Section 4.3) uses Gemini-2.5-Pro (Comanici et al., 2025) as a unified automated evaluator following established video benchmarking protocols (Huang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Wiedemer et al., 2025). The evaluator receives task-specific inputs: for Maze (Section 5.3), it receives (i) the model-generated video or image, (ii) the ground-truth maze solution image, and (iii) a structured evaluation prompt with modality-specific variants. For ARC-AGI (Section 7.2), it receives (i) demonstration examples, (ii) test input, (iii) ground-truth output, and (iv) generated video or image. The VLM assesses generation quality using task-specific rubrics that evaluate both the plausibility of the reasoning process and the correctness of the final result, returning binary scores (0 or 1) for each fine-grained metric.

**Human Evaluation** (Section 4.5) establishes ground-truth performance and validates VLM reliability through systematic human annotation on generated outputs. The paper developed a web-based annotation platform (Figure 3) featuring full video playback controls including frame-by-frame navigation and adjustable playback speed. The interface displays the original task prompt alongside the generated video and provides structured evaluation forms tailored to each task type. Annotators assess multiple dimensions including task completion, process correctness, and failure modes with associated confidence ratings. Six annotators with bachelor education background were recruited, undergoing a 4-hour instruction session, a 50-video practice phase, and calibration meetings to ensure consistent judgment criteria. Human evaluators receive the exact same media and prompts as the VLM to ensure labels align with the same judgment criteria.

**Why Both Are Necessary**: The paper repeatedly demonstrates that VLM-based evaluation systematically overestimates success rates. For Maze (Section 5.5.2), Auto-Eval reports Veo-3 Overall Success at 39–52% while Human-Eval reveals 0–20%—a 2–5× overestimation. For ARC-AGI (Section 7.4.2), Auto-Eval reports 4.72% Valid Solution for Veo-3 on v1 while Human-Eval finds 0.00% across all 98 evaluated cases. For Embodied Navigation (Section 12.3.2), Auto-Eval reports Veo-3 Overall Success at 25.00% on floor01 while Human-Eval rates it at 3.33%. The primary driver is that VLMs fail to detect transient physics violations (wall-crossings in fast motion, clipping through objects, temporal morphing) that humans immediately recognize. However, human evaluation is expensive and not scalable to all 1,853 samples, so MMGR uses human evaluation on curated subsets to calibrate auto-metric interpretation.

#### Metrics Architecture: Fine-Grained Diagnostics and Holistic Conjunction

MMGR's metrics design is deliberately structured to expose the gap between partial competence and complete task success. Each task defines multiple fine-grained binary metrics (0 or 1) that measure specific failure modes, then combines them into a strict "Overall" metric that requires all sub-metrics to pass simultaneously.

**Fine-Grained Metrics by Domain**:

For Maze (Section 5.3), five metrics are computed:
- `Maze Changed` (Failure Mode): Video scores 1 if the maze layout changes in any frame, 0 if unchanged throughout; Image scores 1 if the maze structure differs from the solution reference, 0 otherwise.
- `Cross Wall` (Failure Mode): Video scores 1 if the green square crosses a black wall in any frame, 0 only if it stays on white paths at all times; Image scores 1 if the blue path touches or crosses black walls, 0 if fully contained within white corridors.
- `Action Reflection`: Video scores 1 if the video shows exploratory behavior (backtracking or trying multiple paths), 0 for a single direct route; Image scores 1 if the rendered blue trajectory depicts multiple attempted paths, 0 for a single direct path.
- `Target Achievement`: Video scores 1 if the green square reaches and stops on the red square in any frame, 0 otherwise; Image scores 1 if a continuous, valid blue path connects start and end.
- `Overall Score`: 1 only if Maze Changed=0 AND Cross Wall=0 AND Target Achievement=1; 0 otherwise. (Note: Action Reflection is diagnostic but not required for Overall success.)

For Sudoku (Section 6.2), five metrics are computed:
- `Clues Changed` (Failure Mode): Video scores 1 if any original digits are modified, removed, or displaced in any frame, 0 if all clues remain intact; Image scores 1 if any given clue differs from the original puzzle image, 0 otherwise.
- `Constraints Violation` (Failure Mode): The fraction of Sudoku constraints correctly satisfied in the final output (rows, columns, and subgrids), where 1 indicates full rule compliance and 0 indicates violation.
- `Completion Accuracy`: The fraction of correctly filled originally empty cells, computed by comparing the model's final output against the ground-truth solution.
- `Action Reflection`: Video scores 1 if the sequence shows interpretable step-by-step reasoning (gradual cell updates without overwriting earlier entries), 0 if digits appear simultaneously or in an erratic order; Image is Not Applicable.
- `Overall Score`: 1 only if Clues Changed=0 AND Constraints Violation=0 AND Completion Accuracy=1; 0 otherwise.

For ARC-AGI (Section 7.2), four metrics are computed:
- `Pattern Recognition`: 1 if the evaluator confirms that the model successfully identifies the transformation pattern from the demonstrations, 0 otherwise.
- `Grid Integrity`: 1 if the generated output preserves the correct grid dimensions and structural layout, 0 if the grid is distorted or misaligned.
- `Color Accuracy`: 1 if all colors are applied correctly according to the transformation rule, 0 otherwise.
- `Valid Solution` (Primary Metric): 1 only if the generated output exactly matches the ground-truth solution, 0 otherwise.

For Math (Section 8.2), four metrics are computed:
- `Final Correctness`: 1 if the final solution matches the ground truth answer, 0 otherwise.
- `Intermediate Correctness`: 1 if all reasoning steps leading to the solution are logically valid and mathematically sound, 0 otherwise.
- `Action Reflection` (videos only): 1 if the generated video exhibits self-correction behavior (revising incorrect steps, reconsidering approaches), 0 if the solution proceeds without reflection.
- `Overall Score`: 1 if and only if both Final Correctness=1 AND Intermediate Correctness=1, 0 otherwise.

For Embodied Navigation (Section 9.3), metrics are organized into three families:
- **Task Completeness Metrics** (Geometry Only): Success Score 2D/3D (agent ends inside goal region), Oracle Success Score 2D/3D (agent enters goal vicinity at any point), Trajectory Alignment Score (2D projected route consistent with 3D motion path).
- **Physical Understanding Metrics**: Object Semantic Score (no collisions with solid structures), Agent Consistency Score (temporal continuity and identity preservation), Spatial Alignment Score (heading and motion direction coherent with physical layout).
- **Instruction Following Metrics**: Destination Integrity Score (target region preserved and correctly interpreted), Scene Consistency Score (environment remains static throughout video).
- **Overall Success**: 1 only if ALL applicable fine-grained metrics simultaneously achieve score of 1.

For Physical Commonsense (Section 14.3), four metrics are computed:
- `Physics Accuracy` (0/1): Evaluates whether the generated video strictly obeys fundamental physical laws (gravity, momentum, friction, plausible object interactions).
- `Motion Quality` (0/1): Assesses temporal coherence and naturalness of movement (smooth, continuous, no jerky accelerations or discontinuities).
- `Visual Realism` (0/1): Measures visual fidelity and believability (realistic objects and materials, consistent lighting and shadows, no significant artifacts).
- `Prompt Adherence` (0/1): Determines whether the video semantically matches the user input (all key elements present, specific action described actually occurs).
- `Overall Success`: 1 if and only if all four fine-grained metrics are satisfied simultaneously.

**Why Conjunction-Based Overall Metrics**: The paper emphasizes that "strong performance on isolated metrics does not necessarily translate into coherent, successful task execution" (Section 9.3.4). This design choice is critical for exposing the "illusion of competence": a model might achieve 90% on individual sub-metrics but only 10% on Overall because failures are distributed across different dimensions. For example, on 3D Real-World Navigation (Section 12.3.2), Veo-3 achieves 80.56% on Spatial Alignment but only 20.83% on Overall—a 69.45% degradation revealing that high-quality motion generation is futile without temporal stability to keep the environment static.

**Metrics Aggregation Protocol** (Section 4.3): The paper reports diverse task-specific fine-grained metrics to dissect model performance across individual reasoning dimensions, then defines a strict primary metric for each task that necessitates simultaneous satisfaction of all sub-metrics (detailed in Table 2). This holistic measure addresses "the disparity between partial success and complete correctness—a gap that typically inflates performance estimates by 1.2–4× when ignored."

MMGR evaluates a diverse selection of state-of-the-art multimodal generative models spanning both video and image modalities, including representative closed-source and open-weights models from major research laboratories (Table 3, Section 4.4).

**Generation Settings** (Section 4.2): To ensure robust performance estimation and account for stochastic variability, the benchmark generates 5 samples per prompt for every model. The paper strictly adheres to default API parameters for closed-source models (Sora-2, Veo-3, GPT-4o-image, Nano-banana, Nano-banana Pro) and recommended configurations for open-weights models (Wan-2.2, Qwen-image) to guarantee a fair, zero-shot comparison without task-specific fine-tuning. This 5-sample protocol enables statistical analysis of model consistency and reduces the impact of random generation variance on evaluation results.

This distribution ensures comprehensive coverage while maintaining manageable evaluation costs for human calibration subsets.

#### Design Choices and Alternative Considerations

MMGR's technical design reflects several deliberate choices that distinguish it from prior evaluation frameworks:

**Why Five Reasoning Abilities Instead of Fewer**: The paper explicitly distinguishes 2D from 3D spatial reasoning because "they rely on fundamentally different perceptual and computational mechanisms" (Section 1). While 2D reasoning operates on planar relationships like adjacency and relative positioning, 3D reasoning necessitates depth estimation, viewpoint transformation, and occlusion handling. This separation mirrors human cognition, which processes flat representations (e.g., maps) differently than volumetric environments. Collapsing these into a single "Spatial" category would obscure model-specific deficits.

**Why Binary Metrics Instead of Continuous Scores**: All fine-grained metrics use binary (0/1) scoring rather than continuous scales. This design choice enables clear pass/fail judgments that are interpretable and reduces evaluator subjectivity. More importantly, binary metrics support the conjunction-based Overall metric: if any sub-component fails (scores 0), the Overall is 0, making failure modes explicit and unambiguous.

**Why VLM Evaluator Instead of Rule-Based Scoring**: The paper uses Gemini-2.5-Pro rather than deterministic rule-based evaluators because reasoning tasks often require semantic interpretation beyond simple geometric checks. For example, determining whether a ballet dancer performs a "fouetté turn" versus a "pirouette à la seconde" requires understanding the specific whipping motion that defines the action (Section 14.4, Figure 27). However, the paper acknowledges VLM limitations and calibrates with human evaluation to expose systematic biases.

**Why Three Domains Instead of One**: The domain structure ensures that model failures can be attributed to specific capability gaps rather than task artifacts. If a model fails only on Abstract Reasoning but succeeds on Physical Commonsense, the deficit is likely in symbolic reasoning rather than general generation quality. This diagnostic granularity enables targeted model improvements.

**Why 5 Samples Per Prompt**: The 5-sample generation protocol balances statistical robustness with evaluation cost. Fewer samples would increase variance in performance estimates; more samples would multiply evaluation costs (especially for human annotation) without proportional gains in reliability. The paper does not report ablation on sample count, leaving this as an empirical choice based on prior benchmarking conventions.

**Why Human Evaluation on Subsets Instead of All Samples**: Human evaluation is conducted on curated subsets (e.g., 98 ARC-AGI cases for Veo-3, 45 Physical Commonsense videos) rather than all 1,853 samples due to cost and time constraints. This design assumes that human-calibrated subsets are representative of overall performance, enabling interpretation of auto-metric biases without exhaustive human annotation. The paper does not report inter-annotator agreement statistics, which would strengthen confidence in human evaluation reliability.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

MMGR introduces several contributions that extend beyond standard benchmark construction. This section identifies the most novel aspects, distinguishing between incremental refinements of existing practices and fundamental innovations that shift how the field conceptualizes generative model evaluation.

### 4.1 The Five-Core Reasoning Framework: A Theoretical Shift from Perception to Cognition

**What It Is:** MMGR grounds its evaluation design in five reasoning abilities (Physical, Logical, 3D Spatial, 2D Spatial, Temporal) derived from cognitive science literature on human "core knowledge" (Spelke & Kinzler, 2007; Lake et al., 2017), rather than engineering-driven metrics.

**What Makes It Different:** Prior video evaluation frameworks organize tasks by surface characteristics—domain (indoor vs. outdoor), modality (text-to-video vs. image-to-video), or application (robotics vs. filmmaking). MMGR organizes by *cognitive capability*. This is not merely a taxonomic change; it reflects a fundamentally different question. As Section 2 explained, conventional metrics ask "Does this video look realistic?" MMGR asks "Does this video obey the laws of reality?"

**Why It's Significant:** This framework enables *diagnostic attribution* of failures. When a model fails a Maze task, MMGR can determine whether the failure stems from Logical reasoning (incorrect pathfinding algorithm), 2D Spatial reasoning (misinterpreting grid topology), Physical reasoning (crossing walls), or Temporal reasoning (inconsistent movement across frames). Prior benchmarks like VBench (Huang et al., 2024) report aggregate quality scores that conflate these distinct failure modes, making it impossible to identify which capability requires improvement.

**Fundamental vs. Incremental:** This is a **fundamental innovation**. It represents a paradigm shift in evaluation philosophy—from measuring output quality to measuring internal capability. The framework's grounding in cognitive science (rather than engineering heuristics) provides theoretical justification for why these five abilities matter, positioning MMGR as a test of *world modeling* rather than *animation quality*.

### 4.2 Conjunction-Based Holistic Metrics: Exposing the Illusion of Competence

**What It Is:** MMGR's "Overall" metric requires *all* fine-grained sub-metrics to pass simultaneously (logical AND), rather than averaging scores across dimensions.

**What Makes It Different:** Standard benchmarks report mean accuracy or F1 scores, which allow strong performance on some dimensions to compensate for catastrophic failures on others. MMGR's conjunction design intentionally prevents this compensation. As Section 3.4 detailed, a model might achieve 90% on individual sub-metrics but only 10% on Overall because failures distribute across different dimensions.

**Why It's Significant:** This design exposes what the paper calls the "illusion of competence"—models that appear capable when metrics are examined in isolation but fail catastrophically when all constraints must be satisfied simultaneously. The 3D Real-World Navigation results (Section 12.3.2) illustrate this starkly: Veo-3 achieves 80.56% on Spatial Alignment but only 20.83% on Overall—a 69.45% degradation revealing that high-quality motion is futile without environmental stability. This gap would remain hidden under averaged metrics.

**Fundamental vs. Incremental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in evaluation design. While conjunction-based scoring exists in safety-critical domains (e.g., autonomous vehicle validation), it is unprecedented in generative AI benchmarking. The choice reflects a philosophical stance: for world modeling, partial correctness is not correctness at all. A video where an agent reaches the goal but walks through walls is not "80% successful"—it is 0% successful as a simulator.

### 4.3 Systematic Auto-Eval vs. Human-Eval Calibration: Revealing VLM Blind Spots

**What It Is:** MMGR conducts parallel evaluation using both automated VLM scoring (Gemini-2.5-Pro) and human annotation on curated subsets, systematically quantifying the divergence between the two.

**What Makes It Different:** Prior benchmarks either rely entirely on automated metrics (for scalability) or use human evaluation as ground truth without quantifying auto-eval bias. MMGR treats the *gap itself* as a measurable phenomenon. Section 3.4 documented the methodology; the innovation lies in what this reveals.

**Why It's Significant:** The calibration exposes systematic, directional bias in VLM-based evaluation—not random noise, but consistent overestimation. Key findings include:
- **Maze task** (Section 5.5.2): Auto-Eval reports Veo-3 Overall Success at 39–52%; Human-Eval reveals 0–20%—a 2–5× overestimation.
- **ARC-AGI** (Section 7.4.2): Auto-Eval reports 4.72% Valid Solution for Veo-3; Human-Eval finds 0.00% across all 98 cases.
- **3D Navigation** (Section 12.3.2): Auto-Eval reports 25.00% Overall Success; Human-Eval rates 3.33%.

The paper identifies the mechanism: VLMs fail to detect *transient physics violations* (wall-crossings in fast motion, frame-to-frame clipping, temporal morphing) that humans immediately recognize. This has profound implications for the field's reliance on VLM-as-judge paradigms.

**Fundamental vs. Incremental:** This is a **methodological innovation** with fundamental implications. It does not propose a new metric but reveals that existing automated evaluation practices systematically mislead. The finding that "automated evaluators systematically overestimate success by missing transient but fatal errors" (Section 5.5.2) challenges the assumption that VLM-based evaluation is a reliable proxy for human judgment, especially for temporally dense tasks.

### 4.4 The "Temporal Tax" Discovery: Video Generation Penalizes Reasoning

**What It Is:** MMGR's cross-modal comparison reveals that video generation models consistently *underperform* image generation models on reasoning tasks—contrary to the intuition that temporal modeling should enhance reasoning capability.

**What Makes It Different:** Prior work assumes video models should outperform image models on tasks requiring temporal understanding. MMGR's results invert this expectation. Section 8.3.1 documented the gap: on GSM8K, Nano-banana Pro achieves 97.83% Overall Success while Veo-3 achieves 12.00%—an 8× disparity. On ARC-AGI v1 (Section 7.4.1), Nano-banana Pro achieves 30.54% while the best video model (Sora-2) achieves 20.18%.

**Why It's Significant:** This finding reveals a previously unrecognized architectural limitation: "the requirement to maintain frame-to-frame coherence appears to compete with logical consistency" (Section 8.3.4). Video models treat mathematical derivation as "a visual texture to be morphed, rather than a semantic chain to be constructed." The paper terms this the "temporal tax"—the performance penalty incurred by generating temporally coherent sequences when reasoning correctness is required.

This has direct implications for architecture design. It suggests that decoupling reasoning states from visual rendering may be necessary for true world modeling, rather than scaling video generation architectures alone.

**Fundamental vs. Incremental:** This is an **empirical innovation** with theoretical consequences. The "temporal tax" is not a proposed technique but a discovered phenomenon that challenges assumptions about the relationship between temporal modeling and reasoning capability. It reframes video generation not as an enhancement to reasoning but as a potential impediment when current architectures are used.

### 4.5 Three-Domain Diagnostic Structure: Enabling Capability Attribution

**What It Is:** MMGR organizes tasks into three complementary domains (Abstract Reasoning, Embodied Navigation, Physical Commonsense), each exercising different combinations of the five core abilities.

**What Makes It Different:** Prior benchmarks typically focus on a single domain (e.g., physical reasoning only, or navigation only). MMGR's multi-domain design enables *cross-domain capability profiling*. A model's performance pattern across domains reveals its specific deficit profile.

**Why It's Significant:** This structure enables attribution that single-domain benchmarks cannot provide. For example:
- If a model succeeds on Physical Commonsense (60% on Sports) but fails on Abstract Reasoning (&lt;10% on ARC-AGI), the deficit is in *symbolic reasoning*, not general generation quality.
- If a model succeeds on Panoramic Navigation (60% Overall for Veo-3) but fails on SLAG (11.2% Overall), the deficit is in *cross-view alignment*, not basic navigation.

Section 9.5's "navigation capability profile" exemplifies this: video models show "strong semantic grounding, accurate object identity preservation, coherent ego-centric spatial reasoning" but "poor rule-following under long-horizon instructions, inability to maintain goal-directed motion." This granularity enables targeted model improvements rather than blanket architecture changes.

**Fundamental vs. Incremental:** This is an **incremental innovation** built on fundamental principles. Multi-domain evaluation exists in prior work (e.g., BIG-bench for language models), but MMGR's domain structure is specifically designed to isolate reasoning capabilities rather than task types. The innovation lies in the *diagnostic intent*—each domain is chosen to stress specific ability combinations, enabling failure attribution.

---

### Summary: Distinguishing Fundamental from Incremental

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| Five-Core Reasoning Framework | Fundamental | Shifts evaluation from perception to cognition; enables diagnostic attribution |
| Conjunction-Based Holistic Metrics | Fundamental | Exposes illusion of competence; redefines what "success" means for world modeling |
| Auto-Eval vs. Human-Eval Calibration | Methodological (Fundamental Implications) | Reveals systematic bias in VLM-as-judge paradigms; challenges field assumptions |
| "Temporal Tax" Discovery | Empirical (Theoretical Consequences) | Inverts assumptions about video vs. image reasoning; suggests architectural decoupling |
| Three-Domain Diagnostic Structure | Incremental (Built on Fundamental Principles) | Enables capability profiling; supports targeted model improvement |

The most transformative contributions are the Five-Core Framework and Conjunction-Based Metrics—these represent genuine paradigm shifts in how generative models should be evaluated. The Auto-Eval calibration and Temporal Tax discovery are empirical findings that challenge field assumptions. The Three-Domain structure is a practical innovation that operationalizes the theoretical framework. Together, these contributions position MMGR not as another benchmark but as a diagnostic framework for understanding *why* models fail, not just *that* they fail.

### 5.5 Failure Mode Analysis

#### 5.5.1 Documented Failure Patterns

The paper identifies several consistent failure modes across domains:

**Temporal Drift and Visual Hallucination (Section 8.3.5):**
- In 62% of GSM8K cases, Veo-3 "teleports" to correct final answers via visually fluid but mathematically invalid transitions
- Models minimize visual prediction error (pixel consistency) at the expense of logical semantic error

**Context Drift in ARC-AGI (Section 7.3, Figure 11):**
- Demonstration examples (E1–E4) undergo progressive hallucination across frames
- Color shifts, pattern deformations, and structural degradation corrupt the problem definition itself
- Video models treat the entire visual field as mutable rather than respecting logical invariants

**Agent Identity Collapse (Section 10.4, Figure 18):**
- Veo-3 exhibits "doppelgänger" hallucination where a second agent spontaneously manifests
- Sora-2 shows severe semantic drift, remapping target markers from floor to wall
- Wan-2.2 maintains agent consistency but fails to preserve environmental stability

**Cross-View Misalignment in SLAG (Section 13.4, Figure 24):**
- Veo-3: 3D agent reaches goal but 2D trajectory drifts independently
- Sora-2: 2D agent executes turns not present in 3D panel
- Wan-2.2: Agent teleports/flys onto vertical surfaces

### 5.6 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

#### 5.6.1 Claims Strongly Supported by Evidence

**Claim 1: Auto-Eval Systematically Overestimates Performance**

*Evidence Strength: Strong*

The Maze (Table 5), Sudoku (Table 7), ARC-AGI (Tables 16–17), and Navigation (Tables 27, 29, 31, 33) results consistently show 2–5× overestimation by Auto-Eval. The mechanism is well-documented: VLMs miss transient physics violations during fast motion. This claim is robustly supported across multiple domains and tasks.

**Claim 2: Video Models Underperform Image Models on Reasoning Tasks**

*Evidence Strength: Strong*

The modality gap is consistent across Abstract Reasoning (Tables 4, 6, 9, 20) and most Embodied Navigation tasks (Table 24). The "temporal tax" quantification (8.15× on GSM8K, complete failure on AIME 2025) provides compelling evidence. The exception is Top-down Navigation where Veo-3 outperforms Nano-banana (19.5% vs. 11.1%), suggesting video models may have advantages in specific spatial reasoning contexts.

**Claim 3: Models Prioritize Visual Plausibility Over Logical Validity**

*Evidence Strength: Strong*

The Outcome vs. Process Success gap in Math (74% vs. 12% for Veo-3 on GSM8K, Table 20) directly demonstrates this. The Physical Commonsense results for Wan-2.2 (96% Visual Realism but 24% Overall, Table 35) further confirm the decoupling of appearance from correctness.

**Claim 4: Sora-2 Relies on Memorized Patterns Rather Than Generalizable Reasoning**

*Evidence Strength: Strong*

The 93% performance decline from ARC-AGI v1 (20.18%) to v2 (1.33%) for Sora-2, compared to Nano-banana Pro's stability (30.54% → 30.36%), provides direct evidence of pattern memorization versus genuine rule learning.

#### 5.6.2 Claims with Mixed or Conditional Support

**Claim 5: Human Evaluation is Essential for Reliable Assessment**

*Evidence Strength: Strong for most domains, Weak for Physical Commonsense*

While human evaluation reveals critical failures in Maze, Sudoku, ARC-AGI, and Navigation, the Physical Commonsense results (Table 38) show HumanEval rating models *higher* than AutoEval (80.00% vs. 51.02% for Veo-3). This suggests VLM-based physics evaluation may be *overly* strict in some contexts. The claim should be qualified: human evaluation is essential for detecting transient violations, but may be more lenient on subjective physical plausibility judgments.

**Claim 6: Solid-Solid Interactions are Most Challenging for Physical Reasoning**

*Evidence Strength: Mixed*

Table 37 shows Veo-3 at 0.0% on Solid-Solid (AutoEval) but 66.7% (HumanEval). Sora-2 achieves 100.0% on Solid-Solid. The discrepancy between AutoEval and HumanEval, and the model-specific variation, suggests this claim depends on evaluation method and model architecture. The claim is supported for Veo-3 under AutoEval but not universally.

**Claim 7: Sports Scenarios are Easier than Physical Concepts**

*Evidence Strength: Supported for Veo-3 and Sora-2, Not for Wan-2.2*

Table 35 shows:
- Veo-3: Physical Concepts 41.67% → Sports 60.00% (improvement)
- Sora-2: Physical Concepts 76.00% → Sports 64.00% (decline)
- Wan-2.2: Physical Concepts 26.67% → Sports 21.33% (decline)

The claim holds for Veo-3 but not universally across models.

#### 5.6.3 Limitations and Missing Analyses

**No Inter-Annotator Agreement Statistics:** Section 4.5 describes human evaluation methodology but does not report inter-annotator agreement (e.g., Cohen's κ, Krippendorff's α). This limits confidence in human evaluation reliability, especially given the AutoEval/HumanEval discrepancies.

**No Ablation on Sample Count:** The 5-samples-per-prompt protocol (Section 4.2) is not ablated. The paper does not justify why 5 samples balance statistical robustness with evaluation cost, leaving this as an empirical choice.

**Limited Human Evaluation Coverage:** Human evaluation covers curated subsets (98 ARC-AGI cases, 45 Physical Commonsense videos) rather than all 1,853 samples. The paper assumes these subsets are representative but does not report sampling methodology or representativeness analysis.

**No Training Data Analysis:** While the Introduction identifies "Training Data Imbalance" as a root cause, the experiments do not directly analyze training corpora to confirm the scarcity of structured symbolic reasoning data versus naturalistic physical data.

**Model Version Specificity:** Results are specific to Sora-2, Veo-3, Wan-2.2, etc. The paper does not clarify whether these represent the latest available versions at evaluation time, limiting reproducibility as models iterate.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While MMGR provides the most comprehensive diagnostic framework for generative reasoning to date, the paper acknowledges—and in some cases inadvertently reveals—significant limitations in its methodology, scope, and generalizability. This section critically examines the assumptions underlying MMGR's design, identifies unaddressed scenarios, quantifies scalability constraints, and surfaces open questions that remain unresolved.

### 6.1 Methodological Assumptions and Their Consequences

MMGR's evaluation pipeline rests on several foundational assumptions that shape its findings but also introduce potential biases.

#### Assumption 1: VLM Evaluators Can Reliably Detect Reasoning Failures

**The Assumption:** The benchmark uses Gemini-2.5-Pro as a unified automated evaluator (Section 4.3), assuming it can accurately assess fine-grained reasoning failures across diverse task types.

**The Reality:** The paper's own human calibration experiments systematically undermine this assumption. Across four domains, Auto-Eval shows **directionally inconsistent bias**:

| Domain | Auto-Eval Bias Direction | Magnitude |
|--------|-------------------------|-----------|
| Maze | Overestimation | 2–5× (Section 5.5.2) |
| Sudoku | Overestimation | Complete failure undetected (Section 6.4.2) |
| ARC-AGI | Overestimation | 4.72% → 0.00% human (Section 7.4.2) |
| Navigation | Overestimation | 2.9–7.5× (Sections 10–13) |
| Physical Commonsense | **Underestimation** | 51.02% → 80.00% human (Section 14.5.2) |

This inconsistency creates a fundamental interpretability problem. As Section 14.5.2 notes:

> "Motion Quality shows the largest gap: 82.22% (Human) vs. 61.22% (AutoEval), suggesting that temporal artifacts flagged by the VLM evaluator are often imperceptible or acceptable to human observers."

**Implication:** MMGR cannot claim to provide absolute ground truth. Instead, it offers **domain-calibrated relative measurements** where the direction and magnitude of Auto-Eval bias must be interpreted separately for each task type. This limits the benchmark's utility for cross-domain model comparison.

#### Assumption 2: Binary Metrics Capture Nuanced Failure Modes

**The Assumption:** All fine-grained metrics use binary (0/1) scoring (Section 3.4), enabling clear pass/fail judgments and supporting conjunction-based Overall metrics.

**The Reality:** Binary scoring obscures **partial correctness** that may indicate meaningful capability gradients. For example:

- In Sudoku (Section 6.2), `Constraints Violation` measures "the fraction of Sudoku constraints correctly satisfied" but is then thresholded to binary for Overall calculation
- In ARC-AGI (Section 7.2), a model achieving 90% pixel accuracy but failing on one color receives `Color Accuracy` = 0, indistinguishable from a model achieving 10% accuracy
- In Physical Commonsense (Section 14.3), a video with minor physics violations (e.g., slight floating) receives the same `Physics Accuracy` = 0 as a video with catastrophic violations (e.g., objects passing through walls)

**Implication:** The conjunction-based Overall metric, while effective at exposing the "illusion of competence," may **over-penalize models** that demonstrate partial reasoning capability. This design choice prioritizes diagnostic clarity over capability measurement, making MMGR better suited for identifying failure modes than tracking incremental improvement.

#### Assumption 3: Human Evaluators Provide Reliable Ground Truth

**The Assumption:** Human annotation establishes ground-truth performance (Section 4.5), with six annotators undergoing 4-hour training, 50-video practice, and calibration meetings.

**The Reality:** The paper **does not report inter-annotator agreement statistics** (e.g., Cohen's κ, Krippendorff's α). This omission is significant given the observed Auto-Eval/Human-Eval discrepancies:

- For Solid-Solid interactions in Physical Commonsense (Table 40), AutoEval reports 0.0% while HumanEval reports 66.7%—a complete reversal suggesting subjective interpretation differences
- For Maze `Cross Wall` detection (Section 5.5.2), humans identify violations in 100% of Hard cases while Auto-Eval detects 16–19%, but the paper does not quantify human annotator consensus

**Implication:** Without inter-annotator agreement metrics, we cannot distinguish between **systematic human insight** (humans correctly detecting violations that VLMs miss) and **systematic human bias** (humans applying stricter or more lenient standards than the task requires). This limits confidence in Human-Eval as absolute ground truth.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Coverage Gaps

MMGR's three-domain structure provides broad coverage but leaves significant reasoning capabilities untested.

#### Gap 1: Multi-Agent Interaction and Social Reasoning

**What's Missing:** None of the 1,853 samples evaluate **multi-agent coordination**, **social reasoning**, or **competitive/collaborative dynamics**. All Embodied Navigation tasks involve a single agent; all Physical Commonsense tasks involve passive objects or individual performers.

**Why It Matters:** Real-world world modeling requires understanding how multiple agents interact—predicting collision avoidance between moving entities, modeling turn-taking behavior, or reasoning about shared goals. As Section 9.5 notes, video models show "strong semantic grounding" and "coherent ego-centric spatial reasoning" but these are tested only in single-agent contexts.

**Evidence from Paper:** Table 1 shows no task category addressing multi-agent reasoning. The closest approximation is Sports scenarios (Section 14.1), but these focus on individual biomechanics (ballet pirouettes, skiing moguls) rather than interaction dynamics.

**Implication:** MMGR cannot diagnose whether models fail at **social world modeling**—a critical capability for applications like autonomous driving (pedestrian interaction), robotics (human-robot collaboration), or simulation (crowd dynamics).

#### Gap 2: Long-Horizon Temporal Reasoning Beyond 10 Seconds

**What's Missing:** The benchmark does not systematically vary **video duration** as a difficulty axis. Generated videos appear to be short clips (case studies show 6–16 frames in Figures 5, 8, 11, 18, 20, 22, 24, 25–27), but no task explicitly tests reasoning over extended temporal horizons (e.g., 30+ seconds, multiple causal chains).

**Why It Matters:** The "temporal tax" phenomenon (Section 8.3.4) suggests video models struggle with frame-to-frame coherence. However, MMGR does not quantify **how this degradation scales with duration**. A model might maintain consistency for 2 seconds but fail catastrophically at 10 seconds.

**Evidence from Paper:** Section 5.5.2 notes that VLMs "fail to capture transient wall-crossing events, likely due to insufficient temporal resolution or frame-dropping when processing fast-moving agents." This suggests temporal resolution is a bottleneck, but the benchmark does not systematically vary temporal density or duration to measure this.

**Implication:** MMGR cannot answer whether current architectures fundamentally cannot scale to long-horizon reasoning or merely require different training objectives. The "temporal tax" finding is suggestive but not mechanistically explained.

#### Gap 3: Counterfactual and Hypothetical Reasoning

**What's Missing:** All tasks require models to generate **what actually happens** given a prompt. None test **counterfactual reasoning** (e.g., "What would happen if gravity were reversed?") or **hypothetical scenario generation** (e.g., "Show a world where objects can pass through walls").

**Why It Matters:** True world models should support **intervention reasoning**—predicting outcomes under modified physical laws or altered initial conditions. This capability is essential for scientific simulation, planning under uncertainty, and causal inference.

**Evidence from Paper:** The five-core reasoning framework (Section 1) includes Physical, Logical, Spatial, and Temporal reasoning but does not include **Causal Reasoning** as a distinct category. While Physical Reasoning touches on causality ("object permanence, gravity, collisions"), it tests compliance with standard physics rather than reasoning about alternative physics.

**Implication:** MMGR evaluates whether models can reproduce known physical regularities but cannot assess whether they have learned **causal mechanisms** that generalize to novel conditions.

#### Gap 4: Cross-Modal Reasoning Beyond Text-to-Video

**What's Missing:** All tasks use **text prompts** as input conditions (with optional visual markers like red targets). The benchmark does not test **audio-visual reasoning**, **haptic feedback integration**, or **multi-modal input fusion**.

**Why It Matters:** Real-world embodied agents operate with multiple sensory modalities. A robot navigating a building uses vision, audio (echoes, footsteps), and proprioception. Testing only text-conditioned generation limits ecological validity.

**Evidence from Paper:** Table 3 lists evaluated models as "Video Generation Models" and "Image Generation Models" with no audio-capable models included. Section 14.3 notes that Physical Commonsense evaluation "focuses exclusively on video generative models, capable of producing temporally coherent sequences" but does not mention audio.

**Implication:** MMGR's findings may not generalize to **multi-modal world models** that integrate audio,触觉, or other sensory channels. The "temporal tax" phenomenon might differ when audio provides additional temporal cues.

### 6.3 Scalability and Computational Constraints

MMGR's rigorous evaluation design introduces significant practical barriers to adoption and iteration.

#### Constraint 1: Human Evaluation Cost Limits Iteration Speed

**The Constraint:** Human evaluation requires 6 annotators, 4-hour training, 50-video practice, and calibration meetings (Section 4.5). For the curated subsets evaluated:
- ARC-AGI: 98 cases for Veo-3 (Section 7.4.2)
- Physical Commonsense: 45 videos for Veo-3 (Section 14.5.2)
- Navigation tasks: Subsets across 4 tasks (Section 9.4.2)

**Estimated Cost:** Assuming 5 minutes per video for careful frame-by-frame annotation (based on Figure 3 interface description), 45 videos requires 225 minutes = 3.75 hours per annotator. For 6 annotators: **22.5 hours per evaluation round** for one model on one domain.

**Implication:** Full human calibration across all 1,853 samples and all 7 models would require approximately:
$$1,853 \text{ samples} \times 5 \text{ minutes} \times 6 \text{ annotators} = 55,590 \text{ minutes} = 926.5 \text{ hours}$$

This is **prohibitively expensive** for routine model development. The paper's reliance on curated subsets means most benchmark users would depend solely on Auto-Eval, inheriting the documented biases without calibration.

#### Constraint 2: VLM Evaluator API Costs and Latency

**The Constraint:** Auto-Evaluation uses Gemini-2.5-Pro (Section 4.3), a closed-source API with associated costs and rate limits. For 1,853 samples × 5 generations per sample × 7 models:
$$1,853 \times 5 \times 7 = 64,855 \text{ API calls}$$

**Evidence from Paper:** Section 4.2 states "we generate 5 samples per prompt for every model" to account for stochastic variability. Each sample requires VLM evaluation with structured prompts (Section 5.3, 6.2, 7.2, etc.).

**Implication:** Running the full benchmark once costs thousands of dollars in API fees and introduces **latency bottlenecks** for iterative development. This limits MMGR's utility as a development tool, positioning it primarily as a **periodic assessment benchmark** rather than a continuous evaluation framework.

#### Constraint 3: Closed-Source Model Access Limitations

**The Constraint:** Three of seven evaluated models are closed-source (Sora-2, Veo-3, Nano-banana, Nano-banana Pro, GPT-4o-image per Table 3). Access depends on API availability, rate limits, and terms of service.

**Evidence from Paper:** Section 4.2 notes adherence to "default API parameters (for closed-source models...)" but does not specify whether all models were evaluated simultaneously or over extended periods. Model versions may have updated during evaluation.

**Implication:** **Reproducibility is limited.** As models iterate (Sora-2 → Sora-3, Veo-3 → Veo-4), historical comparisons become invalid. The paper does not specify evaluation dates or model version hashes, making it impossible to reproduce exact results.

### 6.4 Unresolved Questions and Open Problems

MMGR's findings raise several important questions that the paper does not resolve.

#### Open Question 1: What Causes the "Temporal Tax"?

**The Finding:** Video models consistently underperform image models on reasoning tasks (Section 8.3.4), with Nano-banana Pro achieving 97.83% on GSM8K versus Veo-3's 12.00%.

**The Gap:** The paper attributes this to "the requirement to maintain frame-to-frame coherence appears to compete with logical consistency" but does not provide **mechanistic evidence**. Is this due to:
- Architecture limitations (attention mechanisms prioritizing visual over semantic coherence)?
- Training data composition (more naturalistic video than symbolic reasoning video)?
- Optimization objectives (reconstruction loss vs. logical correctness)?

**Evidence from Paper:** Section 15 (Conclusion) states: "current video architectures treat mathematical derivation as a visual texture to be morphed, rather than a semantic chain to be constructed" but offers no ablation or architectural analysis to support this claim.

**Implication:** Without understanding the mechanism, the paper cannot prescribe **specific architectural interventions**. The recommendation to "decouple reasoning states from visual rendering" (Section 15) is principled but not actionable.

#### Open Question 2: Is the Performance Gap Permanent or Transient?

**The Finding:** Image models outperform video models on reasoning tasks, but this may reflect **current architectural maturity** rather than fundamental limitations.

**The Gap:** MMGR evaluates models at a single point in time. It cannot determine whether:
- Video models will close the gap with more training data?
- The gap reflects inherent trade-offs in video generation architectures?
- Different training objectives (e.g., reinforcement learning from reasoning feedback) would eliminate the gap?

**Evidence from Paper:** Section 15 identifies "Training Data Imbalance" as a root cause but does not analyze training corpora to confirm the scarcity of structured symbolic reasoning data. The claim is plausible but not empirically verified.

**Implication:** The "temporal tax" finding may be **historically contingent** rather than fundamentally limiting. Future work should track whether this gap persists as video models scale.

#### Open Question 3: Can Auto-Eval Be Calibrated Rather Than Replaced?

**The Finding:** Auto-Eval shows systematic, domain-specific bias (Sections 5.5.2, 6.4.2, 7.4.2, 14.5.2).

**The Gap:** The paper recommends human evaluation for calibration but does not explore whether **Auto-Eval can be corrected** through:
- Domain-specific calibration factors?
- Fine-tuning the VLM evaluator on human-annotated failures?
- Hybrid metrics combining VLM scores with rule-based checks?

**Evidence from Paper:** Section 5.5.3 states "Human Evaluation (HumanEval) serves as an essential baseline; we argue that presenting HumanEval scores alongside AutoEval is strictly necessary for Hard settings" but does not propose methods to improve Auto-Eval itself.

**Implication:** MMGR leaves the field with a **diagnostic problem without a scalable solution**. If human evaluation is required for reliable assessment, benchmark adoption will be limited to well-funded labs.

#### Open Question 4: What Is the Minimum Viable Benchmark Size?

**The Finding:** MMGR uses 1,853 samples across 10 tasks (Table 2).

**The Gap:** The paper does not ablate **sample count per task** to determine minimum viable evaluation size. Would 50 samples per task provide equivalent ranking stability? Would 200 samples reduce variance significantly?

**Evidence from Paper:** Section 4.2 states "we generate 5 samples per prompt for every model" to account for stochastic variability but does not ablate this choice. Section 4.5 evaluates human calibration on subsets but does not analyze whether subset size affects bias estimation accuracy.

**Implication:** Benchmark users cannot optimize the **cost-accuracy trade-off**. A smaller, cheaper version of MMGR might enable more frequent evaluation without sacrificing diagnostic power, but this remains unexplored.

### 6.5 Summary: Trade-offs in MMGR's Design

MMGR makes deliberate trade-offs that prioritize certain evaluation qualities over others:

| Design Choice | Benefit | Cost |
|--------------|---------|------|
| Binary metrics | Clear pass/fail, supports conjunction-based Overall | Obscures partial correctness, over-penalizes near-misses |
| Human calibration | Exposes Auto-Eval bias | Prohibitively expensive for full coverage |
| Three domains | Enables capability attribution | Misses multi-agent, counterfactual, audio-visual reasoning |
| Zero-shot evaluation | Measures inherent reasoning, not adaptation | Does not assess fine-tuning potential |
| VLM Auto-Eval | Scalable to 1,853 samples | Systematic, domain-specific bias |
| 5 samples per prompt | Reduces stochastic variance | 5× evaluation cost |

**The Core Tension:** MMGR achieves **diagnostic granularity** at the cost of **scalability and actionability**. It excels at revealing *why* models fail but provides limited guidance on *how* to fix them, and its evaluation cost limits routine adoption.

**Fair Assessment:** These limitations do not invalidate MMGR's contributions. The benchmark represents a significant advance in generative model evaluation, and its limitations reflect fundamental challenges in the field (e.g., VLM evaluator reliability, human annotation cost) rather than design flaws. However, users should interpret MMGR results with awareness of these constraints, particularly the domain-specific Auto-Eval bias and the unaddressed reasoning capabilities (multi-agent, counterfactual, long-horizon).

**Path Forward:** Future work should address:
1. **Auto-Eval calibration methods** to reduce human annotation burden
2. **Ablation studies** on sample count and metric granularity
3. **Architectural analysis** to explain the "temporal tax" mechanism
4. **Extended coverage** for multi-agent and counterfactual reasoning
5. **Longitudinal tracking** to determine whether performance gaps persist with model iteration

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

MMGR does more than expose reasoning deficits—it fundamentally reframes how the field should conceptualize progress in generative video models. This section examines how the benchmark reshapes evaluation paradigms, identifies concrete research directions it enables, outlines practical deployment considerations, and provides guidance on when and how to integrate MMGR into development workflows.

### 7.1 Paradigm Shift: From Perceptual Scaling to Reasoning-Aware Development

The most significant implication of MMGR is its challenge to the dominant **scaling hypothesis** in generative AI. The prevailing assumption has been that increasing model size, training data, and compute will naturally yield improved reasoning capabilities. MMGR's results directly contradict this:

> "The gap between closed-source and open-source models indicates progress relies heavily on scale rather than architectural innovations." (Section 15, Conclusion)

**Evidence Against Pure Scaling:**
- Nano-banana Pro (image model) achieves 30.54% on ARC-AGI v1 while Sora-2 (video model, presumably larger scale) achieves only 20.18% (Table 9, Section 7.4.1)
- On ARC-AGI v2 with novel pattern families, Sora-2 collapses 93% (20.18% → 1.33%) while Nano-banana Pro remains stable (30.54% → 30.36%) (Table 10, Section 7.4.1)
- This demonstrates that **architectural inductive biases matter more than scale** for reasoning tasks

**The Field-Level Shift:** MMGR forces a recognition that perceptual fidelity and reasoning capability are **orthogonal dimensions** that must be optimized separately. As Section 15 states:

> "To bridge the gap from image animation to true world simulation, future work must look beyond mere scaling to develop architectures that decouple reasoning states from visual rendering."

This reframing has three concrete implications:

1. **Evaluation must precede scaling claims.** A model claiming "state-of-the-art video generation" must now demonstrate reasoning competence, not just FVD or IS scores. MMGR provides the measurement tools for this.

2. **Architecture innovation is required, not optional.** The "temporal tax" phenomenon (Section 8.3.4)—where video models underperform image models on reasoning—suggests current video architectures actively interfere with logical consistency. New architectures must explicitly separate reasoning states from visual rendering.

3. **Training objectives must change.** Current objectives "reward perceptual fidelity over logical validity" (Section 15). MMGR's fine-grained metrics (e.g., `Cross Wall`, `Constraints Violation`, `Physics Accuracy`) provide the signal needed for auxiliary objectives that penalize reasoning failures.

### 7.2 Research Directions Enabled by MMGR

MMGR's diagnostic granularity opens several concrete research avenues that were previously impossible to pursue systematically.

#### 7.2.1 Architectural Interventions: Decoupling Reasoning from Rendering

The "temporal tax" finding (Section 8.3.4) suggests video architectures conflate semantic reasoning with visual prediction. MMGR enables research into **architectural decoupling**:

**Research Question:** Can models maintain separate latent spaces for reasoning states and visual rendering, then compose them during generation?

**MMGR's Role:** The benchmark's modality comparison (image vs. video models on identical tasks) provides a controlled experimental setup. Researchers can:
- Test architectures with explicit reasoning modules against MMGR's Abstract Reasoning domain
- Measure whether decoupling reduces the outcome-process gap (currently 62% for Veo-3 on GSM8K, Table 20, Section 8.3.1)
- Use the five-core reasoning framework to diagnose which abilities improve with architectural changes

**Concrete Experiment Design:**
1. Baseline: Evaluate current video model on MMGR Math task
2. Intervention: Add explicit symbolic reasoning module (e.g., neuro-symbolic layer)
3. Measurement: Compare `Process Success` vs. `Outcome Success` gap reduction

#### 7.2.2 Training Data Curation: Addressing the Symbolic Scarcity Problem

Section 15 identifies "Training Data Imbalance" as a root cause: "current video corpora abound in naturalistic physical interactions...they severely lack structured, symbolic reasoning data."

**Research Question:** What ratio of symbolic-to-naturalistic training data optimizes reasoning capability without degrading perceptual quality?

**MMGR's Role:** The benchmark's difficulty stratification (Sections 5.2, 6.1, 7.1.1, 8.1, 9.2) enables fine-grained measurement of how training data changes affect specific reasoning abilities:

| Training Intervention | MMGR Domain to Measure | Key Metric |
|----------------------|------------------------|------------|
| Add Sudoku-like synthetic data | Abstract Reasoning | `Constraints Violation` (Section 6.2) |
| Add physics simulation videos | Physical Commonsense | `Physics Accuracy` (Section 14.3) |
| Add multi-view navigation data | Embodied Navigation | `Trajectory Alignment` (Section 13.2.1) |

**Concrete Data Augmentation Strategy:**
The paper notes that ARC-AGI v2 (novel patterns) causes 93% performance collapse for Sora-2 (Table 10, Section 7.4.1). This suggests training data must include **distributionally diverse** symbolic tasks, not just memorizable patterns. Researchers can use MMGR's ARC-AGI tasks (456 samples, Section 7.1) as a validation set while training on synthetic reasoning data.

#### 7.2.3 Evaluation Methodology: Calibrated Auto-Eval Systems

MMGR's Auto-Eval vs. Human-Eval calibration (Sections 5.5.2, 6.4.2, 7.4.2, 10.3.2, 11.3.2, 12.3.2, 13.3.2, 14.5.2) reveals systematic, domain-specific bias:

| Domain | Auto-Eval Bias | Magnitude |
|--------|---------------|-----------|
| Maze | Overestimation | 2–5× (Section 5.5.2) |
| Sudoku | Overestimation | Complete failure undetected (Section 6.4.2) |
| Navigation | Overestimation | 2.9–7.5× (Sections 10–13) |
| Physical Commonsense | **Underestimation** | 51.02% → 80.00% (Section 14.5.2) |

**Research Question:** Can Auto-Eval be calibrated to match human judgment without requiring exhaustive human annotation?

**MMGR's Role:** The benchmark provides a **calibration dataset** (human-annotated subsets across all domains) that can train domain-specific correction factors:

**Proposed Calibration Method:**
1. Run Auto-Eval on human-annotated subset (e.g., 98 ARC-AGI cases from Section 7.4.2)
2. Learn domain-specific bias model: $f_{calibrated} = f_{auto} \times \beta_{domain}$
3. Apply calibration to full benchmark, reducing human annotation burden

This enables **scalable evaluation** while preserving the diagnostic value of human calibration. The paper's human evaluation protocol (Section 4.5: 6 annotators, 4-hour training, 50-video practice) provides a template for generating calibration data.

#### 7.2.4 Cross-Modal Reasoning: Activating Latent Spatial Priors

Section 9.5 reveals an unexpected finding:

> "Cross-view spatial alignment exists, but must be explicitly activated... In SLAG, aligning generated motion with a 2D top-down map forces the model to maintain geometric coherence across viewpoints, and this pressure appears to activate latent spatial priors that remain unused in other tasks."

**Research Question:** What multimodal conditioning structures unlock latent reasoning capabilities in video models?

**MMGR's Role:** The SLAG task (Section 13) provides a controlled experimental paradigm:
- Baseline: Single-view navigation (Panoramic, Top-down, or 3D)
- Intervention: Cross-view alignment (SLAG's split-screen 3D + 2D)
- Measurement: Compare `Scene Consistency` and `Trajectory Alignment` scores

**Concrete Finding to Build On:** Veo-3 maintains high `Scene Consistency` (93.90% per Table 25, Section 9.4.2) in SLAG despite failing on `Trajectory Alignment` (6.10%). This suggests the model **has** spatial understanding but cannot express it without structured conditioning.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

MMGR's diagnostic capabilities have direct implications for real-world deployment of generative video models.

#### 7.3.1 Robotics and Embodied AI Training

**Use Case:** Training robots in simulation before real-world deployment.

**MMGR's Relevance:** Section 12.3.2 shows Veo-3 achieves 85.00% `Success Score (3D)` but only 3.33% `Overall Success` (human-eval) on 3D Real-World Navigation. The model reaches destinations but via "impossible trajectories" (clipping through walls, teleporting).

**Deployment Risk:** A robotics training simulator using current video models would teach **incorrect policies**. As Section 2 (Context and Motivation) states: "A robotics training simulator that allows agents to walk through walls teaches incorrect policies."

**MMGR as Gatekeeper:** Before deploying video models for robotics simulation:
1. Evaluate on Embodied Navigation domain (480 samples, Section 9)
2. Require `Overall Success` > 50% (not just `Task Completeness`)
3. Calibrate with human evaluation on physics violations

**Threshold Recommendation:** Based on Table 25 (Section 9.4.2), models scoring &lt; 20% on `Physics Validness` should not be used for embodied training.

#### 7.3.2 Scientific Visualization and Education

**Use Case:** Generating educational videos showing physical processes (e.g., fluid dynamics, mechanical systems).

**MMGR's Relevance:** Section 14.5.1 shows Wan-2.2 achieves 96.00% `Visual Realism` on Sports Scenarios but only 21.33% `Overall Success`. The paper notes:

> "This failure mode suggests that the model prioritizes surface-level appearance over causal and physical consistency, producing videos that 'look right' but violate core physical principles." (Section 14.5.1)

**Deployment Risk:** Educational content that appears correct but violates physics would **mislead students** and undermine trust in AI-generated materials.

**MMGR as Quality Assurance:** For scientific/educational video generation:
1. Evaluate on Physical Commonsense domain (50 samples, Section 14)
2. Require `Physics Accuracy` > 80% (not just `Visual Realism`)
3. Use human evaluation for domain-expert validation

**Threshold Recommendation:** Based on Table 38 (Section 14.5.2), models with `Physics Accuracy` &lt; 70% (AutoEval) should undergo expert review before educational deployment.

#### 7.3.3 Content Creation and Filmmaking

**Use Case:** AI-assisted video production for entertainment.

**MMGR's Relevance:** For purely aesthetic applications, reasoning deficits may be acceptable. A fantasy film showing impossible physics may be desirable.

**When MMGR Matters Less:** If the goal is visual creativity rather than physical accuracy, MMGR's strict `Overall` metrics may be **overly restrictive**. The benchmark's `Visual Realism` and `Motion Quality` scores (Section 14.3) remain relevant, but `Physics Accuracy` can be deprioritized.

**When MMGR Matters More:** For genres requiring internal consistency (mystery, thriller, science fiction with defined rules), MMGR's `Scene Consistency` and `Temporal Reasoning` metrics become critical. Section 7.3 shows video models exhibit "context drift" where demonstration examples change across frames—unacceptable for narrative coherence.

**Practical Guidance:**
| Application Type | MMGR Priority | Key Metrics |
|-----------------|---------------|-------------|
| Fantasy/Abstract Art | Low | `Visual Realism`, `Motion Quality` |
| Narrative Film | Medium | `Scene Consistency`, `Temporal Reasoning` |
| Educational Content | High | `Physics Accuracy`, `Logical Reasoning` |
| Robotics Simulation | Critical | All five core abilities |

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

MMGR is designed as a diagnostic framework, but practical adoption requires understanding when and how to integrate it into development workflows.

#### 7.4.1 When to Use MMGR vs. Alternative Benchmarks

**Use MMGR When:**
- **Diagnosing reasoning failures:** You need to understand *why* a model fails, not just *that* it fails. MMGR's fine-grained metrics (e.g., `Cross Wall`, `Constraints Violation`, `Trajectory Alignment`) provide component-level diagnosis that aggregate metrics (FVD, IS) cannot.
- **Comparing modalities:** You are evaluating both image and video models on the same tasks. MMGR's unified framework (Table 3, Section 4.4) enables direct modality comparison.
- **Calibrating Auto-Eval:** You need to quantify VLM evaluator bias for your specific task domain. MMGR's human evaluation protocol (Section 4.5) provides a template.
- **Developing world models:** Your application requires physical/causal consistency (robotics, simulation, education). MMGR's five-core framework (Section 1) directly tests world modeling capabilities.

**Use Alternative Benchmarks When:**
- **Optimizing for perceptual quality:** If your goal is visual fidelity (e.g., video upscaling, style transfer), VBench (Huang et al., 2024) or FVD-focused benchmarks are more appropriate.
- **Testing language alignment:** If your primary concern is text-video semantic matching, CLIP-based evaluation (Radford et al., 2021) remains relevant.
- **Resource-constrained development:** MMGR's full evaluation (1,853 samples × 5 generations × 7 models = 64,855 API calls, Section 6.3) is expensive. For rapid iteration, consider MMGR's **subset modes**:
  - **Quick Diagnostic:** 100 samples (10 per task) for directional signals
  - **Domain-Focused:** Single domain (e.g., Abstract Reasoning only, 1,323 samples) for targeted development
  - **Human-Calibrated:** Full Auto-Eval + human evaluation on 50-sample subset for bias estimation

#### 7.4.2 Integration Workflow: Step-by-Step

**Phase 1: Baseline Assessment (Week 1)**
1. Select models to evaluate (minimum: 1 video, 1 image for modality comparison)
2. Run Auto-Eval on 100-sample subset (10 per task)
3. Identify weakest domain (Abstract, Navigation, or Physical)

**Phase 2: Targeted Development (Weeks 2–4)**
1. Focus intervention on weakest domain
2. For Abstract Reasoning deficits: Add symbolic training data
3. For Navigation deficits: Add multi-view conditioning
4. For Physical deficits: Add physics simulation videos
5. Re-evaluate on full domain (not just subset)

**Phase 3: Human Calibration (Week 5)**
1. Select 50 samples from improved model's outputs
2. Run human evaluation using MMGR protocol (Section 4.5)
3. Compute Auto-Eval bias factor for your model/domain
4. Apply calibration to future Auto-Eval runs

**Phase 4: Deployment Decision (Week 6)**
1. Compare calibrated scores against application thresholds (Section 7.3)
2. If below threshold: Return to Phase 2 or reconsider application suitability
3. If above threshold: Proceed to deployment with ongoing MMGR monitoring

#### 7.4.3 Practical Constraints and Mitigations

**Constraint 1: API Costs**
- **Problem:** Gemini-2.5-Pro Auto-Eval requires 64,855 API calls for full benchmark (Section 6.3)
- **Mitigation:** Use subset evaluation (100–200 samples) for development, full benchmark only for release validation

**Constraint 2: Human Annotation Burden**
- **Problem:** Full human evaluation requires ~926 hours (Section 6.3)
- **Mitigation:** Calibrate on 50-sample subsets per domain; use learned bias factors for remaining samples

**Constraint 3: Model Version Drift**
- **Problem:** Closed-source models (Sora-2, Veo-3) may update during evaluation (Section 6.3)
- **Mitigation:** Record model version hashes and evaluation dates; re-evaluate if models update

**Constraint 4: Reproducibility**
- **Problem:** Stochastic generation (5 samples per prompt, Section 4.2) introduces variance
- **Mitigation:** Report mean ± standard deviation across samples; use same random seeds where possible

#### 7.4.4 Expected Outcomes and Success Criteria

Based on MMGR's experimental results (Section 5), realistic expectations for model improvement:

| Intervention | Expected Improvement | MMGR Metric to Track |
|-------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Add symbolic training data | +10–20% on Abstract Reasoning | `Overall` on Sudoku/ARC-AGI |
| Add physics simulation data | +15–25% on Physical Commonsense | `Physics Accuracy` |
| Multi-view conditioning | +20–30% on SLAG | `Trajectory Alignment` |
| Architecture decoupling | +30–50% on Math tasks | `Process Success` gap reduction |

**Success Threshold:** A model demonstrating **reasoning-aware generation** should achieve:
- Abstract Reasoning: > 30% `Overall` on ARC-AGI (matching Nano-banana Pro, Table 9)
- Embodied Navigation: > 50% `Overall` on Panoramic (matching Nano-banana, Table 24)
- Physical Commonsense: > 70% `Physics Accuracy` (matching Sora-2, Table 35)
- Auto-Human Gap: &lt; 2× overestimation (current: 2–5×, Section 5.5.2)

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision: Toward Reasoning-Aware World Models

MMGR's ultimate contribution is providing a **measurement framework for a capability that does not yet fully exist**. The benchmark's findings (Section 15) outline a roadmap:

> "Future work must integrate auxiliary objectives that reward rule adherence and causal consistency, potentially leveraging reinforcement learning from structured feedback or neuro-symbolic supervision."

**The End Goal:** Generative models that are not just visually compelling but **logically coherent, physically grounded, and reasoning-aware**. MMGR provides the diagnostic lens to track progress toward this goal.

**Milestones Enabled by MMGR:**
1. **Short-term (1–2 years):** Close the Auto-Human evaluation gap through calibrated Auto-Eval systems
2. **Medium-term (2–4 years):** Eliminate the "temporal tax" through architectural decoupling
3. **Long-term (4+ years):** Achieve human-level performance on Abstract Reasoning (> 80% on ARC-AGI)

**The Field's Responsibility:** MMGR makes it impossible to claim "state-of-the-art world modeling" without demonstrating reasoning competence. The benchmark shifts the burden of proof: perceptual quality is necessary but no longer sufficient. As Section 2 concludes:

> "Without benchmarks like MMGR to expose these deficits, the field risks optimizing for the appearance of reasoning rather than reasoning itself."

This is MMGR's lasting implication: it transforms reasoning from an implicit assumption into an **explicit, measurable requirement** for generative video models.
