## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the massive computational cost of training frontier large language models (LLMs) by introducing a stable methodology for pretraining using the 4-bit `NVFP4` format, which overcomes historical challenges in convergence and stability at narrow precisions. The authors validate this approach by training a 12-billion-parameter hybrid Mamba-Transformer model on 10 trillion tokens, employing techniques like Random Hadamard transforms and 2D block scaling to achieve performance comparable to an FP8 baseline. With an MMLU-pro accuracy of 62.58% nearly matching the FP8 baseline's 62.62%, this work represents the first publicly documented successful 4-bit pretraining run at this scale, enabling significant improvements in computational speed and resource utilization for future model development.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Computational Imperative
The development of frontier Large Language Models (LLMs) is constrained primarily by computational cost. As models scale in size and training data quality, the required compute investment grows exponentially. The authors note that training a state-of-the-art model today demands "on the order of tens to hundreds of yottaflops" (Abstract). A yottaflop represents $10^{24}$ floating-point operations, indicating a massive investment in time, energy, and hardware resources. Consequently, improving pretraining efficiency is not merely an optimization task but a prerequisite for enabling the next generation of capable models. Without reductions in the computational burden, the pace of AI development risks hitting economic and energy consumption walls.

### The Precision Trade-off
One of the most effective levers for improving training efficiency is reducing numerical precision. In deep learning, model weights, activations, and gradients are traditionally stored in high-precision formats like 32-bit floating point (`FP32`) or 16-bit brain floating point (`BF16`). Lowering precision reduces memory usage and increases arithmetic throughput because hardware can process more low-precision numbers in parallel.

*   **FP8 Adoption:** 8-bit floating point (`FP8`) has become widely adopted as a standard for accelerated training (Micikevicius et al., 2022; DeepSeek-AI et al., 2024). It offers a balanced trade-off between efficiency and numerical stability.
*   **The FP4 Opportunity:** The industry is now transitioning to 4-bit floating point (`FP4`). According to the Introduction, moving from `FP8` to `FP4` delivers a "two- to three-fold boost in arithmetic performance and reducing memory usage by half." This is supported by new hardware architectures, such as NVIDIA Blackwell GPUs, which provide native Tensor Core support for 4-bit microscaling formats (Table 1).

However, simply halving the bit-width does not guarantee successful training. The core problem this paper addresses is not the *availability* of 4-bit hardware, but the **algorithmic stability** of using such narrow precision during the pretraining phase.

### The Stability Gap in 4-bit Training
While `FP4` offers theoretical efficiency gains, quantization at this level introduces severe numerical challenges that prior approaches have struggled to overcome at scale. The primary difficulties include:

1.  **Dynamic Range Limitations:** 4-bit formats can represent far fewer distinct values than 8-bit or 16-bit formats. For example, the `MXFP4` format (a prior 4-bit microscaling standard) encodes values using only 1 sign bit, 2 exponent bits, and 1 mantissa bit (`E2M1`). This limits representable values to a small set (e.g., $\pm0, \pm0.5, \pm1, \dots, \pm6$) (Section 2).
2.  **Outlier Sensitivity:** LLM tensors often contain "outliers"—values with magnitudes significantly larger than the average. In narrow precision, these outliers can cause saturation (clipping to the maximum representable value), destroying gradient information and halting learning (Section 4.2).
3.  **Quantization Bias:** Deterministic rounding (e.g., rounding to the nearest representable value) introduces systematic bias, particularly in gradients. Over long training horizons, this bias accumulates, leading to convergence failure or degraded accuracy (Section 4.4).
4.  **Chain Rule Violations:** During backpropagation, tensors are often transposed. If quantization scaling is applied inconsistently between the forward and backward passes (e.g., scaling along different dimensions), the mathematical chain rule is effectively broken, preventing proper gradient flow (Section 4.3).

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Before this work, the prevailing 4-bit standard was `MXFP4` (Microscaling FP4), defined by the Open Compute Project (2023). While `MXFP4` introduced block-wise scaling to manage dynamic range, it exhibits specific shortcomings that limit training stability:

*   **Coarse Block Scaling:** `MXFP4` groups 32 contiguous elements into a single block sharing one scale factor. The authors argue this is too coarse to capture local dynamic range variations effectively (Section 2).
*   **Power-of-Two Scales:** `MXFP4` stores scale factors in an unsigned `E8M0` format, which encodes only powers of two (e.g., $2^{-127}$ to $2^{127}$). This restriction means values cannot be scaled perfectly to fit the `FP4` range. As detailed in Appendix B.4, this can result in losing "up to one binade of dynamic range" and wasting representable values (e.g., unable to represent $\pm4$ and $\pm6$ in certain blocks).
*   **Lack of Methodological Integration:** Prior research on 4-bit training often focuses on the format itself without fully addressing the systemic training instabilities (e.g., outlier management, rounding bias) required for **long token horizons**. Many existing studies validate on smaller models or shorter training runs, leaving uncertainty about scalability (Abstract).

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself as the first comprehensive solution for **stable, large-scale 4-bit pretraining**. It distinguishes itself from prior work in three critical dimensions:

1.  **Format Enhancement (`NVFP4`):** The authors introduce `NVFP4`, which refines the microscaling approach by reducing block size from 32 to 16 elements and using `E4M3` (fractional) scale factors instead of power-of-two scales. This preserves more dynamic range and reduces quantization error compared to `MXFP4` (Section 2).
2.  **Algorithmic Methodology:** Rather than relying on the format alone, the paper proposes a suite of training techniques—including Random Hadamard Transforms (RHT) for outlier dispersion, 2D block scaling for consistency, and stochastic rounding for unbiased gradients (Section 4). These are presented as essential components, not optional optimizations.
3.  **Scale of Validation:** Crucially, the work validates these methods on a **12-billion-parameter model trained on 10 trillion tokens**. The authors emphasize this is "the longest publicly documented training run in 4-bit precision to date" (Abstract). This scale is necessary to prove that the methodology holds over the long horizons required for frontier model development, distinguishing it from prior works that may only demonstrate feasibility on smaller scales (e.g., 1.2B or 8B models used in ablations, Appendix A).

By addressing both the numerical format and the training algorithm, this work aims to bridge the gap between the theoretical efficiency of `FP4` and the practical requirements of stable LLM pretraining.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an algorithmic systems paper that presents a complete training methodology for stable 4-bit LLM pretraining. The core idea is that achieving convergence at 4-bit precision requires not just a better numerical format (`NVFP4`), but a coordinated suite of four techniques: mixed-precision layer selection, Random Hadamard transforms for outlier management, 2D block scaling for gradient consistency, and stochastic rounding for unbiased gradient estimation.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a complete pretraining pipeline that allows large language models to be trained using 4-bit numerical precision instead of the standard 8-bit or 16-bit formats, while maintaining comparable accuracy. The solution addresses the fundamental problem that 4-bit numbers cannot represent enough distinct values to capture the full range of weights, activations, and gradients in a neural network, which historically caused training to diverge or produce poor models.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The training pipeline consists of five major components that work together during each training step. First, the **NVFP4 Quantization Engine** converts high-precision tensors (weights, activations, gradients) into 4-bit format using a two-level scaling strategy with 16-element blocks and E4M3 scale factors. Second, the **Mixed-Precision Layer Selector** identifies which linear layers should remain in higher precision (BF16) rather than being quantized to FP4, specifically keeping the first two and last eight transformer blocks in BF16. Third, the **Random Hadamard Transform Module** applies 16×16 orthogonal rotations to weight gradient inputs before quantization to disperse outliers across more values. Fourth, the **2D Block Scaling Coordinator** ensures that weight tensors use 16×16 block scales that remain consistent between forward and backward passes, preventing chain rule violations. Fifth, the **Stochastic Rounding Unit** applies probabilistic rounding to gradient tensors during quantization to eliminate systematic bias that would accumulate over trillions of tokens.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the **NVFP4 numerical format** itself, because understanding how 4-bit values are represented and scaled is prerequisite to understanding why additional techniques are needed.
- We then explain the **two-level scaling procedure** with its mathematical formulation, since this is the foundation that makes 4-bit representation feasible without catastrophic information loss.
- Next, we detail the **four training methodology components** (mixed precision, Hadamard transforms, 2D scaling, stochastic rounding) in the order they are applied during a training step, showing how each addresses a specific failure mode.
- We then describe the **training configuration and hyperparameters** for the 12B model validation, providing the concrete numbers that define the experimental setup.
- Finally, we explain the **NVFP4 vs MXFP4 comparison methodology**, since this demonstrates why the format improvements matter in practice.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### The NVFP4 Numerical Format

This paper introduces `NVFP4` as an enhanced 4-bit floating-point format that improves upon the prior `MXFP4` microscaling standard through three specific design changes. First, `NVFP4` reduces the block size from 32 contiguous elements to 16 contiguous elements, which narrows the dynamic range within each block and allows values to fit more precisely into the limited 4-bit representable range (Section 2). Second, `NVFP4` stores block scale factors in `E4M3` format (4 exponent bits, 3 mantissa bits) rather than the `UE8M0` unsigned power-of-two format used by `MXFP4`, trading some exponent range for fractional precision that enables more accurate scaling (Section 2). Third, `NVFP4` employs a two-level scaling strategy that combines a fine-grained per-block `FP8` scale factor with a coarse per-tensor `FP32` scale, ensuring that at least 6.25% of values in each block (the maximum magnitude values) are encoded at near-`FP8` precision while the remaining values are stored in `FP4` (Section 2, Figure 1).

The underlying 4-bit element format uses `E2M1` encoding, meaning each element has 1 sign bit, 2 exponent bits, and 1 mantissa bit (Section 2, footnote 1). This encoding allows `NVFP4` to represent only the following discrete values: $\pm0, \pm0.5, \pm1, \pm1.5, \pm2, \pm3, \pm4, \text{and} \pm6$ (Section 2). Because original high-precision values (typically `FP32` or `BF16`) often exceed this narrow range, they must be scaled into the representable range during quantization using scale factors chosen so that the absolute maximum value within a block maps to the `FP4` maximum representable value of 6 (Section 2).

#### Two-Level Scaling Procedure

The quantization procedure applies scaling in two sequential stages to ensure values can be accurately recovered after matrix multiplication. First, a global tensor-level `FP32` scale factor remaps all values within an entire tensor into the representable range of a block, which is the product of `FP4` and `FP8` ranges (Appendix B.1). The global encode scale is computed using the formula:

$$s_{enc} = \frac{6 \cdot 4^{48}}{amax_x}$$

where $amax_x = \max_i(|x_i|)$ represents the absolute maximum value across the entire tensor $x$, the constant 6 represents the maximum representable magnitude in the `E2M1` format, and $4^{48}$ represents the maximum representable magnitude in the `E4M3` scale factor format (Appendix B.1, Equation 1). The corresponding decode scale $s_{dec} = 1/s_{enc}$ gets stored in `FP32` for decoding the resulting values after the `NVFP4` GEMM (general matrix multiplication) operation completes (Appendix B.1).

Second, a local block-level scale factor moves the values within each 16-element block into the `FP4` representable range (Appendix B.2). The local decode scales are chosen so the largest absolute value in each block normalizes to the `FP4` maximum representable value:

$$s_{dec,b} = \frac{amax_b}{6}$$

where $amax_b = \max_{i \in b}(|x_i|)$ represents the absolute maximum value within block $b$ (Appendix B.2, Equation 2). Since the local decode scales must be stored in `FP8` for Tensor Core hardware compatibility, they are first multiplied by the global encode scale before quantization:

$$s_{dec,b,e4m3} = \text{e4m3}(s_{dec,b} \cdot s_{enc})$$

where the $\text{e4m3}(\cdot)$ function quantizes the scale to the `E4M3` format using round-to-nearest-even (Appendix B.2, Equation 3). The real local encode scale factor is obtained by inverting the quantized local decode scale in higher precision and scaling it back to its original representable range: $s_{enc,b} = 1/(\text{fp32}(s_{dec,b,e4m3}) \cdot s_{dec})$ (Appendix B.2). This ensures that the original value can be recovered after scaling, since failing to do so impacts model accuracy (Appendix B.2).

During matrix multiplication, Tensor Cores read the local decode scales and apply them to partial dot-products computed over the 16 elements in each block (Appendix B.3). The computation follows:

$$s^x_{dec,b,e4m3} \cdot s^y_{dec,b,e4m3} \cdot \sum_{k \in b}(x_k \cdot y_k)$$

where $x$ and $y$ denote the two input operands to the matrix multiplication (Appendix B.3, Equation 5). After the GEMM operation completes, the global decode scales $s^x_{dec}$ and $s^y_{dec}$ are applied to the final output in a similar fashion (Appendix B.3).

This two-level approach provides a critical advantage over `MXFP4`, which uses power-of-two scale factors restricted to the `UE8M0` format (Appendix B.4). Because `MXFP4` scales can only represent powers of two, values cannot be scaled to fit perfectly into the `FP4` representable range, potentially causing the block maximum to either overflow and saturate or round down to a smaller `FP4` sample (Appendix B.4). In the worst case, `MXFP4` is unable to represent the samples at $\pm4$ and $\pm6$, reducing the dynamic range by nearly one binade where only $\log_2(3/0.5) = 2.58$ binades are utilized instead of the full $\log_2(6/0.5) = 3.58$ binades (Appendix B.4). `NVFP4` overcomes this limitation with the more precise `E4M3` block scale, which maps the block maximum much closer to the `FP4` maximum representable number, maximizing `FP4` sample utilization and preserving more dynamic range (Appendix B.4).

#### Four-Component Training Methodology

The paper identifies four essential techniques that must work together to enable stable 4-bit training at the 12B parameter scale over 10 trillion tokens (Section 4). Ablation studies in Figure 4 demonstrate that removing any single component leads to worse convergence, confirming that each addresses a distinct failure mode (Section 4).

**Component 1: Mixed-Precision Layer Selection.** Although linear layers are typically computed in narrower precisions, the authors observe that some linear layers are more sensitive to `FP4` quantization than others (Section 4.1). Specifically, training diverges when every linear layer is quantized to `FP4`, with the final few linear layers causing divergence because they require more dynamic range and mantissa precision than `FP4` provides (Section 4.1). Based on ablation studies in Appendix E.2, the recommendation is to leave a small fraction of the final layers (fewer than 15%) in `BF16` or `MXFP8` for better training convergence (Section 4.1). For the 12B model validation, the authors chose a conservative configuration keeping the first two blocks in addition to the final eight blocks (FFNs or Mamba-2, each with 2 linear layers) in `BF16`, representing 16% of the linear layers in the network in high precision (Section 4.1). However, Figure 4 indicates that convergence remains stable even when only the final four blocks are left in higher precision, suggesting that a larger portion of the model could have been safely trained in `FP4` (Section 4.1). Beyond linear layers, the original precision (`BF16` or `FP32`) is retained for embeddings, the output projection head, normalization layers, non-linearities, and attention components including softmax and the query-key and attention score-value batched GEMMs (Section 4.1). The main weights stored by the optimizer, weight gradients used for gradient accumulation across microbatches and data-parallel replicas, and optimizer states are also kept in `FP32`, while tensor parallel reductions are performed in `BF16` precision (Section 4.1).

**Component 2: Random Hadamard Transforms for Outlier Management.** While microscaling reduces the dynamic range needed to represent tensor values, outliers can still have a disproportionate impact on `FP4` formats, degrading model accuracy (Section 4.2). Random Hadamard transforms address this by redistributing outliers into an approximately Gaussian distribution, making them easier to represent in narrower formats (Section 4.2). The transforms are implemented as matrix multiplications between $d \times d$ Hadamard matrices and each tile of the tensor of equal size, where the Hadamard matrix is defined by normalized matrices of the form $H_d = (1/\sqrt{2})H_2 \otimes H_{d/2}$ with elements constrained to $\pm1$ (Appendix C). Random Hadamard transforms introduce randomness by multiplying with a random diagonal sign vector that flips the signs for entire rows or columns, reducing the chance that structured outliers aligned with the Hadamard basis survive the transform (Section 4.2).

The authors make a critical design choice to apply Hadamard transforms only to weight gradient (Wgrad) inputs, not to forward pass (Fprop) or activation gradient (Dgrad) inputs (Section 4.2). Empirically, transforming Wgrad inputs improves training for the 12B model, as Figure 4 shows that loss worsens after removing transformations from Wgrad (Section 4.2). On the other hand, Hadamard transforms show no measurable benefit for Fprop and Dgrad at smaller scales, likely because `FP4` already provides sufficient range for these tensors (Section 4.2). Additionally, Hadamard transforms are not applied to weight tensors themselves because transformed activations and gradients in weight-related GEMMs can no longer be inverted by transforming the weight tensor, preventing Fprop and Dgrad from benefiting from the transformation (Section 4.3).

The matrix size $d$ introduces a trade-off between accuracy and performance: larger matrices distribute outliers more effectively by spreading them over more values, but increase compute and memory costs (Section 4.2). At the 12B model scale, the authors observe diminishing gains from Hadamard matrices beyond moderate sizes, whereas having too few matrix entries affects convergence (Section 4.2). They therefore choose a matrix size of $d = 16$, which they find to have better convergence than $d = 4$ and similar results as $d = 128$ (Section 4.2). This is validated in Appendix E.4.2, where Figure 12 shows that $4 \times 4$ matrices induce an increase in loss and $128 \times 128$ matrices result in only minor benefit to model quality (Appendix E.4.2). For randomization, the authors use a single random sign vector that is shared across all linear layers throughout training, as studies show no measurable impact from increasing the number of random sign vectors (Section 4.2).

**Component 3: Two-Dimensional Block Scaling for Consistency.** During training, transform and scaling operations apply along the dot-product dimension, causing tensors to be transformed and scaled differently in the forward pass (along rows) and backward pass (along columns) (Section 4.3). This occurs because the backward pass transposes the tensors, which changes the dot-product dimension (Section 4.3). As a result, the same tensor can have two distinct quantized representations, effectively breaking the chain rule since backpropagation no longer differentiates the same function used in the forward pass (Section 4.3). More precisely, the backward update $\partial x = w^T_{bprop} \partial y$ computes a gradient for a different function $y_{bprop} = w_{bprop} x$ than used in the forward pass $y_{fprop} = w_{fprop} x$ when $w_{fprop} \neq w_{bprop}$ (Section 4.3). The authors hypothesize that chain rule violations in the weights contribute to reduced model accuracy (Section 4.3).

To mitigate this issue, the paper proposes a two-dimensional (2D) block scaling method that ensures consistent quantization in both forward and backward passes (Section 4.3). For weights, elements are grouped and scaled in $16 \times 16$ blocks (i.e., 16 input channels by 16 output channels), similar to DeepSeek-AI et al. (2024) (Section 4.3). The 2D block scales are replicated for each of the $1 \times 16$ blocks when being passed into Tensor Cores, and continue to leverage an `FP32` per-tensor scale (Section 4.3). Activations and gradients use the standard `NVFP4` scaling (i.e., $1 \times 16$ blocks), since finer-grained scaling improves quantization accuracy (Section 4.3). While activation quantization also presents a chain rule concern, the authors observe that training is less sensitive to inconsistencies in activation tensors than weight tensors (Section 4.3, Appendix E.5). Weights are also more tolerant to the scale granularity because they can adapt to the `FP4` values (Section 4.3). As illustrated in Figure 4, maintaining consistent quantized weights leads to improved training loss for the 12B model (Section 4.3).

**Component 4: Stochastic Rounding for Unbiased Gradients.** During quantization to `FP4`, deterministic rounding (e.g., round-to-nearest-even) can introduce bias, producing systematic errors due to mantissa distributions that favor rounding in a particular direction, values underflowing to zero, or values saturating to the largest representable number (Section 4.4). The effect of bias is typically more pronounced in gradient tensors, which can impact training convergence (Section 4.4). To address this bias, the authors adopt stochastic rounding during quantization of high-precision values to `FP4` (Section 4.4). Stochastic rounding rounds a value probabilistically to one of its two nearest representable numbers, with probabilities inversely proportional to their distances (Section 4.4). This prevents values from being consistently quantized in the same direction, thereby reducing bias (Section 4.4).

The authors observe that applying stochastic rounding to gradient tensors is essential for convergence in the 12B model, as illustrated in Figure 4 (Section 4.4). Other tensors in the backward pass do not benefit from stochastic rounding, reinforcing that gradients are the primary source of bias (Section 4.4, Appendix E.3). Moreover, applying stochastic rounding to the forward pass tensors is detrimental, as it amplifies quantization error relative to nearest rounding (Section 4.4, citing Castro et al., 2025). Figure 10 in Appendix E.3 shows that applying stochastic rounding to gradients leads to stable convergence of the training loss for the 1.2B model, whereas using it on activations or weights causes divergence (Appendix E.3). For the 12B model, stochastic rounding must be applied to gradients going into both Dgrad and Wgrad to achieve proper convergence (Appendix E.3).

#### Training Configuration and Hyperparameters

The validation experiment trains a 12-billion-parameter hybrid Mamba-Transformer model on 10 trillion tokens using the `NVFP4` methodology described above (Section 3, Abstract). The model architecture follows the Nemotron-H family, consisting of 62 blocks with 6 Self-Attention, 28 FFNs, and 28 Mamba-2 blocks, where each block has 2 linear layers (Appendix A.1, Table 3). The model has a hidden dimension of 5120, FFN dimension of 20480, 40 query heads, 8 key-value heads, and Mamba-2 blocks with 8 groups, state dimension of 128, head dimension of 64, expansion factor of 2, and convolution window size of 4 (Appendix A.1, Table 3). The model uses squared ReLU activations for FFN blocks, RMSNorm for normalization layers, and separate embedding and output layer weights, with no position embeddings, dropout, or biases for linear layers (Appendix A.1).

The pretraining dataset comprises 10 trillion tokens from a corpus of high-quality curated and synthetic data, including general web crawl data, Wikipedia, math, code, academic data, crawl++, multilingual, and synthetic SFT-style data (Appendix A.1). Pretraining uses a phased data-blending approach where the first phase covers 70% of training with a data mixture that promotes diversity, while the second and third phases primarily consist of high-quality datasets and span the last 20% and 10% of training, respectively (Appendix A.1).

The model is trained with a sequence length of 8192 and batch size of 736 (Appendix A.1). The learning rate schedule follows Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD), with a constant learning rate of $4.5 \times 10^{-4}$ through the first 80% of training, then decaying to $4.5 \times 10^{-6}$ over the last 20% (Appendix A.1). Adam optimizer parameters are $\beta_1 = 0.9$ and $\beta_2 = 0.95$, with weight decay set to 0.1 (Appendix A.1). For `NVFP4` precision, all linear layers are computed in `NVFP4` except the linear layers in the first two blocks and the last eight blocks (FFNs or Mamba-2), which are left in `BF16`, accounting for 16% of the total linear layers kept in high precision (Appendix A.1).

#### NVFP4 vs MXFP4 Comparison Methodology

To demonstrate the advantages of `NVFP4` over the prior `MXFP4` standard, the authors conduct a side-by-side comparison using an 8-billion-parameter hybrid Mamba-Transformer model trained on 1 trillion tokens (Section 5). The 8B model has 52 blocks: 4 Self-Attention, 24 FFNs, and 24 Mamba-2 blocks, with a hidden dimension of 4096 and FFN hidden dimension of 21504 (Appendix A.2, Table 4). The reference model is pretrained in `BF16`, while `FP4` pretraining follows the training methodology described in Section 4 with `MXFP4` and `NVFP4` as the respective data formats (Section 5).

For `MXFP4`, the authors adopt a Random Hadamard transform size of $d = 32$ for Wgrad inputs to align with the `MXFP4` block size of 32 elements (Section 5). In both settings, the last eight blocks (either FFNs or Mamba-2) are kept in `BF16`, comprising about 15% of the model (Section 5). Training consists of two phases of data-blending, between the first 60% and last 40% of training, with a batch size of 768, sequence length of 8192, and a WSD schedule using a constant learning rate of $8.0 \times 10^{-4}$ that decays to $8.0 \times 10^{-6}$ over the last 15% of training (Appendix A.2).

The comparison measures relative difference in training loss compared to the `BF16` baseline, as well as final validation loss across different token budgets (Section 5, Figure 6). Figure 6a demonstrates that `NVFP4` pretraining converges to a better loss than `MXFP4`, with `MXFP4` having a relative error of around 2.5% compared to 1.5% for `NVFP4` (Section 5). To close the gap with `NVFP4`, the authors extend `MXFP4` pretraining with additional tokens varying between 1T and 1.5T total tokens (Section 5). Figure 6b illustrates that `MXFP4` matches `NVFP4` loss when trained on 36% more tokens (i.e., using 1.36T instead of 1T tokens), which translates to a considerable increase in training time for `MXFP4` (Section 5).

#### Precision Switching Strategy for Loss Recovery

For scenarios where minimizing loss is critical, the paper identifies a strategy to reduce the gap between `NVFP4` and higher-precision baselines by transitioning to higher precision during the final stages of training (Section 3). In particular, changing precision from `NVFP4` to `BF16` (or potentially `MXFP8`) during the decay phase mitigates the loss gap (Section 3, Appendix D). Figure 7 in Appendix D shows that loss matches the `FP8` baseline when precisions are switched after 8.2T tokens (e.g., for 18% of training) and is only slightly worse when switched after 10T tokens (e.g., for less than 1% of training) (Appendix D).

The authors find that most of `FP4` training's loss gap arises from quantizing tensors in the forward pass (Appendix D, citing Castro et al., 2025). More specifically, most of the loss in the 12B model is recovered (from 1.5% to 0.5% relative error) by switching to higher precision for the forward pass starting at 8.2T tokens (Appendix D). In contrast to Chmiel et al. (2025), which reports loss recovery from switching precision in the backward pass, the authors observe no such improvement in their models (Appendix D). Focusing on the forward pass minimizes the overhead of switching precision, as only about 6% of the total computations (roughly one-third of the final 18% of training) are performed in higher precision (Appendix D). The recommendation is to switch to high precision shortly before the onset of learning rate decay for full loss recovery, or at the very end for notable loss improvements with minimal effect on training runtime (Appendix D).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper delivers several contributions that extend beyond incremental engineering optimizations. Below, I distinguish between fundamental innovations that enable new capabilities and refinements that improve existing approaches.

### 4.1 First Demonstrated Feasibility of 4-bit Pretraining at Frontier Scale

**What is novel:** Prior to this work, no publicly documented study had successfully trained a billion-parameter language model in 4-bit precision over a multi-trillion-token horizon. The 12B model trained on 10 trillion tokens represents a **scale breakthrough** that validates 4-bit training as viable for frontier model development, not just small-scale experiments.

**Why it matters:** This is a capability unlock, not merely an efficiency gain. The Abstract explicitly states this is "the longest publicly documented training run in 4-bit precision to date." The significance lies in proving that 4-bit training does not inherently diverge or degrade at scales required for competitive models. Figure 2 shows validation loss tracking FP8 within 1.5% relative error throughout training, and Table 2 confirms downstream accuracies remain comparable (e.g., MMLU-Pro at 62.58% vs. 62.62% for FP8).

**Distinction from prior work:** Previous 4-bit training research (Tseng et al., 2025b; Chmiel et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025) demonstrated feasibility on smaller models or shorter token horizons. This work establishes that the methodology **sustains stability** over the training durations necessary for frontier models, addressing the critical uncertainty about whether 4-bit training would accumulate errors catastrophically over trillions of tokens.

### 4.2 Two-Dimensional Block Scaling as a Chain Rule Preservation Mechanism

**What is novel:** The 2D block scaling approach (Section 4.3) addresses a **fundamental mathematical inconsistency** in quantized training that prior work largely overlooked. When tensors are transposed between forward and backward passes, standard 1D scaling produces different quantized representations for the same weight tensor, effectively breaking the chain rule: the backward pass computes gradients for a different function than the forward pass evaluated.

**Why it matters:** This is a theoretical insight with practical consequences. Section 4.3 formalizes the problem: when $w_{fprop} \neq w_{bprop}$ due to inconsistent quantization, the backward update $\partial x = w^T_{bprop} \partial y$ no longer correctly differentiates $y_{fprop} = w_{fprop} x$. Figure 4 shows that removing 2D scaling ("NVFP4 without 2D W") causes measurable degradation in training loss. Appendix E.5 (Figure 14) further demonstrates that weights are significantly more sensitive to this inconsistency than activations, revealing an asymmetry in how quantization errors propagate through backpropagation.

**Distinction from prior work:** While DeepSeek-AI et al. (2024) used similar 2D block scaling, this paper provides the **first explicit analysis** connecting 2D scaling to chain rule preservation in the context of 4-bit training. Prior work treated scaling granularity as an accuracy optimization; this work identifies it as a **correctness requirement** for stable gradient flow. This reframing elevates 2D scaling from a heuristic to a principled design choice.

### 4.3 Interdependent Methodology: Four Components as a Coherent System

**What is novel:** The paper demonstrates that the four training techniques (mixed precision, Random Hadamard transforms, 2D scaling, stochastic rounding) are **mutually necessary** rather than independently optional. Figure 4's ablation studies show that removing any single component degrades convergence, indicating these techniques address complementary failure modes that compound at scale.

**Why it matters:** This systems-level insight prevents practitioners from adopting partial solutions that appear promising in isolation but fail at frontier scales. For example:
- Stochastic rounding alone cannot prevent divergence if outliers saturate the 4-bit range (addressed by RHT).
- RHT alone cannot fix chain rule violations from inconsistent scaling (addressed by 2D blocks).
- 2D scaling alone cannot eliminate gradient bias accumulation (addressed by stochastic rounding).

The ablation in Figure 4 quantifies this interdependence: removing stochastic rounding ("NVFP4 without SR") or RHT ("NVFP4 without RHT") each produces distinct degradation patterns, confirming they target different numerical pathologies.

**Distinction from prior work:** Many prior 4-bit training papers present techniques as modular improvements that can be adopted incrementally. This work establishes that **holistic adoption** is required for stability at 12B/10T scale. Appendix E.1 explicitly states: "Our models diverge early in training when using this base method without any of the additional techniques... While techniques such as stochastic rounding can improve training stability, they eventually diverge when used in isolation."

### 4.4 NVFP4 Format Design: E4M3 Scales Over Power-of-Two

**What is novel:** The NVFP4 format's use of E4M3 scale factors (Section 2) instead of MXFP4's power-of-two UE8M0 scales provides **fractional precision** in scaling, preventing the dynamic range loss inherent to power-of-two rounding. Appendix B.4 quantifies this: MXFP4 can lose "up to one binade of dynamic range" because scale factors must round to powers of two, potentially wasting representable FP4 values (e.g., unable to represent $\pm4$ and $\pm6$ in certain blocks).

**Why it matters:** This is an incremental but measurable format improvement. Section 5 (Figure 6a) shows NVFP4 achieves ~1.5% relative error vs. BF16 compared to MXFP4's ~2.5% relative error. More significantly, Figure 6b demonstrates that MXFP4 requires **36% more tokens** (1.36T vs. 1T) to match NVFP4's final loss. This translates directly to training time and cost savings that compound at frontier scales.

**Distinction from prior work:** The microscaling concept originates from Rouhani et al. (2023) and the Open Compute Project (2023). NVFP4's contribution is the **specific scale factor design** (E4M3 with two-level scaling) that preserves more dynamic range. This is an incremental refinement of the MX format family rather than a fundamentally new format category, but the empirical token efficiency gains are significant for production training.

### 4.5 Asymmetric Precision Switching: Forward Pass Dominates Loss Recovery

**What is novel:** Appendix D reveals a counterintuitive finding: switching precision in the **forward pass** recovers most of the loss gap, while switching in the backward pass provides negligible benefit. Figure 7 shows that switching forward pass tensors to BF16 at 8.2T tokens reduces relative error from 1.5% to 0.5%, whereas backward-pass-only switching shows "no such improvement."

**Why it matters:** This insight challenges assumptions about where quantization error matters most. Prior work (e.g., Chmiel et al., 2025) suggested backward-pass precision was critical for gradient accuracy. This paper's results indicate that **activation quantization error** during the forward pass accumulates through layers and has greater impact on final loss than gradient quantization error. Practically, this enables efficient precision switching: only ~6% of total computations need higher precision (one-third of the final 18% of training) to recover most of the loss gap.

**Distinction from prior work:** This finding directly contradicts Chmiel et al. (2025), which reported loss recovery from switching precision in the backward pass. The paper acknowledges this discrepancy explicitly in Appendix D, suggesting model architecture or training horizon differences may explain the divergence. This highlights that 4-bit training insights are not universally transferable without empirical validation at scale.

---

### Summary: Incremental vs. Fundamental Contributions

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| 12B/10T 4-bit training feasibility | **Fundamental** | Proves 4-bit training scales to frontier model requirements |
| 2D scaling for chain rule preservation | **Fundamental** | Addresses mathematical correctness, not just accuracy |
| Interdependent 4-component methodology | **Fundamental** | Establishes system-level requirements vs. modular optimizations |
| NVFP4 E4M3 scale factors | Incremental | Format refinement with measurable token efficiency gains |
| Forward-pass precision switching | **Fundamental** | Counterintuitive insight challenging prior assumptions |

The most significant contributions are those that enable **new capabilities** (stable 4-bit training at scale) and correct **fundamental misunderstandings** (chain rule violations, asymmetric precision sensitivity). The format improvements, while valuable, build on existing microscaling concepts and represent evolutionary rather than revolutionary advances.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their NVFP4 training methodology. The experimental design is comprehensive, spanning multiple model scales, token horizons, and comparison baselines. However, the evaluation also reveals important conditions and trade-offs that practitioners must understand before adopting 4-bit pretraining.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Model Architectures and Scales

The paper evaluates three distinct model configurations to establish both scalability and component necessity (Appendix A):

| Model | Parameters | Architecture | Tokens | Purpose |
|-------|------------|--------------|--------|---------|
| 12B | 12 billion | Hybrid Mamba-Transformer | 10T | Primary validation of NVFP4 methodology |
| 8B | 8 billion | Hybrid Mamba-Transformer | 1T | NVFP4 vs MXFP4 format comparison |
| 1.2B | 1.2 billion | Standard Transformer | 1T | Ablation studies and component analysis |

The 12B model follows the Nemotron-H family architecture with 62 blocks: 6 Self-Attention, 28 FFNs, and 28 Mamba-2 blocks (Appendix A.1, Table 3). Each block contains 2 linear layers, yielding 124 total linear layers. The hidden dimension is 5120, with FFN dimension of 20480, 40 query heads, and 8 key-value heads. Notably, the model uses **no position embeddings, dropout, or biases** for linear layers—design choices that may affect quantization sensitivity.

#### Training Configuration

The primary 12B experiment uses the following hyperparameters (Appendix A.1):

- **Sequence length:** 8192 tokens
- **Batch size:** 736
- **Learning rate schedule:** Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD) with constant rate of $4.5 \times 10^{-4}$ for first 80% of training, decaying to $4.5 \times 10^{-6}$ over final 20%
- **Optimizer:** Adam with $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.95$, weight decay = 0.1
- **Data blending:** Three phases—70% diverse data, 20% high-quality, 10% high-quality (Appendix A.1)

The dataset comprises 10 trillion tokens from "high-quality curated and synthetic" sources including web crawl, Wikipedia, math, code, academic data, multilingual content, and synthetic SFT-style data (Appendix A.1). This phased approach is critical because the loss curve shows a noticeable jump at 9T tokens corresponding to the dataset blend change (Section 3, Figure 2).

#### Baselines and Metrics

The paper employs two primary baselines for comparison:

1. **FP8 Baseline (12B model):** Trained following DeepSeek-AI et al. (2024) and NVIDIA (2025b) methodology. All linear layers computed in E4M3 except first block and last two blocks kept in BF16 (Appendix A.1).
2. **BF16 Baseline (8B model):** Used for the NVFP4 vs MXFP4 format comparison in Section 5.

Evaluation metrics include:

- **Training loss:** Tracked throughout the 10T token horizon (Figure 2)
- **Validation loss:** Measured at regular intervals to assess convergence (Figure 2)
- **Downstream task accuracy:** Comprehensive benchmark suite including MMLU, MMLU-Pro, GSM8k, MATH, HumanEval+, MBPP+, ARC Challenge, HellaSwag, and others (Table 2, Figure 3)

All downstream evaluations are performed in **BF16 precision**, ensuring that accuracy differences reflect training quality rather than inference quantization effects (Table 2 caption).

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

#### Validation Loss Trajectory

Figure 2 presents the validation loss comparison between NVFP4 and FP8 pretraining over 10 trillion tokens. The key findings:

> "During the stable phase of training, the relative loss error of NVFP4 remains consistently below 1%, and widens to slightly above 1.5% as the learning rate is decayed towards the end of training." (Section 3)

This trajectory reveals two important patterns:

1. **Early stability:** NVFP4 tracks FP8 closely through the first 8T tokens (80% of training), indicating the methodology successfully prevents catastrophic divergence during the high-learning-rate phase.
2. **Late divergence:** The gap widens during learning rate decay (8T–10T tokens), suggesting that 4-bit quantization errors accumulate and become more pronounced when gradient updates are smaller.

The loss curve also shows a slope change at 8T tokens (learning rate decay onset) and a small jump at 9T tokens (dataset blend transition). Both formats exhibit these patterns, confirming that NVFP4 preserves training dynamics rather than introducing format-specific artifacts.

#### Downstream Task Accuracy

Table 2 provides the most comprehensive accuracy comparison across 16 benchmark tasks spanning general knowledge, mathematics, coding, multilingual capability, and commonsense reasoning:

| Category | Task | FP8 | NVFP4 | Difference |
|----------|------|-----|-------|------------|
| General | MMLU | 77.36 | 76.57 | -0.79 |
| General | MMLU-Pro 5-shot | 62.62 | 62.58 | **-0.04** |
| General | AGIEval English CoT | 67.01 | 70.31 | **+3.30** |
| Math | GSM8k CoT | 89.08 | 92.27 | **+3.19** |
| Math | MATH | 83.32 | 81.48 | -1.84 |
| Code | HumanEval+ | 59.93 | 57.43 | -2.50 |
| Code | MBPP+ | 59.11 | 55.91 | -3.20 |
| Commonsense | ARC Challenge | 91.81 | 91.81 | 0.00 |
| Commonsense | HellaSwag | 83.83 | 83.09 | -0.74 |

Several observations emerge from these numbers:

- **MMLU-Pro parity:** The headline result of 62.58% (NVFP4) vs 62.62% (FP8) represents near-perfect matching on this challenging reasoning benchmark. This is the paper's strongest evidence that 4-bit training can achieve frontier-model quality.
- **Math performance mixed:** NVFP4 exceeds FP8 on GSM8k (+3.19 points) but falls behind on MATH (-1.84 points). This suggests format effects may vary by task complexity—GSM8k involves simpler arithmetic while MATH requires multi-step reasoning.
- **Coding weakness:** Both HumanEval+ (-2.50) and MBPP+ (-3.20) show NVFP4 underperforming FP8. The authors attribute this to "noisy evaluations" noting that "MBPP+ accuracy drops on the very final checkpoint evaluation and choosing another checkpoint could potentially lead to better accuracy" (Section 3). This explanation is plausible but unverified.
- **Unexpected gains:** AGIEval English CoT (+3.30) and GSM8k (+3.19) show NVFP4 **exceeding** FP8. The paper does not hypothesize why 4-bit training might improve certain tasks, leaving open whether this reflects genuine benefits or evaluation variance.

Figure 3 tracks accuracy evolution throughout training across four representative benchmarks (MMLU-Pro, MMLU, MATH 500, MBPP+). The curves show NVFP4 and FP8 tracking closely throughout the 10T token horizon, with no catastrophic divergence at any point. This longitudinal evidence strengthens the claim that NVFP4 maintains stability over extended training.

#### NVFP4 vs MXFP4 Format Comparison

Section 5 presents a controlled comparison between NVFP4 and the prior MXFP4 standard using an 8B model trained on 1T tokens. Figure 6a shows relative training loss difference from BF16 baseline:

- **NVFP4:** ~1.5% relative error
- **MXFP4:** ~2.5% relative error

This 1 percentage point gap may seem small, but Figure 6b reveals its practical significance. To match NVFP4's final loss, MXFP4 requires **36% more tokens** (1.36T vs 1T). The authors state:

> "This translates to a considerable increase in training time for MXFP4, highlighting the benefits of NVFP4." (Section 5)

At frontier scales (10T+ tokens), a 36% token overhead would represent massive compute costs. This comparison validates the NVFP4 format design choices—specifically the E4M3 scale factors and 16-element blocks—over the power-of-two UE8M0 scales and 32-element blocks of MXFP4.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Component Necessity

Figure 4 presents the critical ablation study on the 12B model, systematically removing each methodology component after training up to 3.43T tokens:

| Configuration | Relative Difference from FP8 |
|--------------|------------------------------|
| Full NVFP4 methodology | ~0% to -1.5% |
| Without Stochastic Rounding (SR) | Worse convergence |
| Without Random Hadamard Transform (RHT) | Worse convergence |
| Without 2D Weight Scaling | Worse convergence |
| With only last 4 blocks in BF16 (vs 10 blocks) | Stable convergence |

The figure shows that removing any single component produces measurable degradation in training loss. This supports the paper's central claim that the four techniques are **interdependent** rather than modular improvements.

Appendix E provides additional ablation granularity on the 1.2B model:

- **Layer sensitivity (Figure 9, Appendix E.2):** Keeping only the last 4 blocks in BF16 achieves stable convergence, while keeping only the first 4 blocks does not. This confirms that **final layers are more sensitive** to FP4 quantization, likely because they require more dynamic range for output projection.
- **Stochastic rounding scope (Figure 10, Appendix E.3):** Applying SR to gradients produces stable convergence, but applying it to activations or weights causes divergence. This validates the design choice to restrict SR to gradient tensors only.
- **Hadamard transform scope (Figure 11, Appendix E.4.1):** Applying RHT to Wgrad inputs improves loss, but applying it to Fprop or Dgrad inputs **degrades** model quality. This counterintuitive finding suggests RHT introduces quantization error that offsets outlier benefits for certain tensor types.
- **Hadamard matrix size (Figure 12, Appendix E.4.2):** For the 12B model, 4×4 matrices increase loss, 16×16 achieves optimal balance, and 128×128 provides only marginal additional benefit. This validates the d=16 choice as cost-effective.
- **Randomization strategy (Figure 13, Appendix E.4.3):** A single fixed random sign vector shared across all layers performs equivalently to per-instance randomization. This simplifies implementation without accuracy cost.
- **Scaling consistency (Figure 14, Appendix E.5):** Weights with 16×16 scales (consistent forward/backward) outperform weights with 1×16 scales along different dimensions. Activations show less sensitivity to scaling consistency, supporting the decision to apply 2D scaling only to weights.

These ablations collectively demonstrate that the methodology is **over-constrained**—each component addresses a specific failure mode that becomes apparent only at scale. The 1.2B ablations sometimes show different sensitivities than the 12B model, reinforcing the paper's emphasis that small-scale experiments may not predict large-scale behavior.

### 5.4 Precision Switching for Loss Recovery

Appendix D explores whether transitioning to higher precision during final training stages can close the remaining loss gap. Figure 7 tests multiple switching strategies:

| Switching Strategy | Switch Point | Relative Error Recovery |
|-------------------|--------------|------------------------|
| NVFP4 throughout | N/A | ~1.5% |
| Switch Fprop+Bprop to BF16 | 8.2T tokens (18% remaining) | ~0% (matches FP8) |
| Switch Fprop only to BF16 | 8.2T tokens | ~0.5% |
| Switch Bprop only to BF16 | 8.2T tokens | No improvement |
| Switch Fprop+Bprop to BF16 | 10T tokens (<1% remaining) | Slight improvement |

The key insight is **asymmetric sensitivity**:

> "We find that most of FP4 training's loss gap arises from quantizing tensors in the forward pass... Most of the loss in the 12B model is recovered (from 1.5% to 0.5% relative error) by switching to higher precision for the forward pass starting at 8.2T tokens." (Appendix D)

This contradicts Chmiel et al. (2025), which reported loss recovery from switching precision in the backward pass. The authors acknowledge this discrepancy explicitly, suggesting model architecture or training horizon differences may explain the divergence.

Practically, forward-pass-only switching requires only ~6% of total computations in higher precision (one-third of the final 18% of training), making it a cost-effective loss recovery strategy.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

#### Strengths

1. **Unprecedented scale:** The 12B/10T experiment is genuinely novel—no prior public work demonstrates 4-bit pretraining at this token horizon. This addresses the critical uncertainty about whether quantization errors accumulate catastrophically over trillions of tokens.

2. **Comprehensive ablations:** The systematic component removal in Figure 4, combined with Appendix E's granular analysis, provides strong evidence for each technique's necessity. The ablations are not merely illustrative—they reveal genuine failure modes (divergence, degraded convergence).

3. **Multiple baselines:** Comparing against both FP8 (for the 12B model) and MXFP4 (for the 8B model) establishes both absolute quality and relative format advantages. The MXFP4 comparison is particularly valuable for practitioners choosing between 4-bit formats.

4. **Longitudinal tracking:** Figure 3's accuracy curves throughout training demonstrate that NVFP4 doesn't merely match FP8 at the final checkpoint—it maintains comparable quality throughout the entire training trajectory.

#### Limitations and Conditions

1. **Coding task weakness:** The consistent underperformance on HumanEval+ and MBPP+ (-2.5 to -3.2 points) is not fully explained. The "noisy evaluations" hypothesis is plausible but unverified. Practitioners targeting code generation should treat this as a known risk.

2. **Scale-dependent findings:** Several ablation results differ between 1.2B and 12B models (e.g., Hadamard matrix size sensitivity, randomization importance). The paper acknowledges this: "conclusions from smaller scales may not always hold for larger models" (Appendix E.4.2). This limits the transferability of ablation findings to other model sizes.

3. **Architecture specificity:** All experiments use hybrid Mamba-Transformer architectures. The Conclusion notes plans to evaluate "additional architectures such as mixture-of-experts," but current results may not generalize to pure Transformer or MoE models.

4. **No runtime efficiency measurements:** The paper explicitly states it is "primarily concerned with the underlying algorithms and methodology rather than with runtime efficiency or system-level optimizations" (Introduction). While Table 1 cites 4×–6× speedup vs BF16 for NVFP4 on Blackwell GPUs, no end-to-end training time comparisons are provided. Practitioners cannot directly infer wall-clock savings from these experiments.

5. **Evaluation variance:** Some accuracy differences (e.g., AGIEval +3.30, GSM8k +3.19) favor NVFP4, but the paper does not report confidence intervals or multiple-seed runs. It remains unclear whether these represent genuine improvements or evaluation noise.

6. **Precision switching overhead:** While Appendix D shows loss recovery via precision switching, the implementation complexity and memory overhead of maintaining dual-precision pipelines during training is not quantified.

#### Verdict on Claim Support

The experiments **convincingly support** the core claim that stable 4-bit pretraining is feasible at 12B/10T scale with comparable accuracy to FP8. The validation loss trajectories (Figure 2), downstream accuracies (Table 2), and ablation studies (Figure 4, Appendix E) provide robust evidence.

However, the experiments **partially support** the claim of universal applicability. The coding task weakness, architecture specificity, and scale-dependent ablation results indicate that NVFP4 training requires careful tuning for different use cases. The precision switching findings also suggest that "pure" 4-bit training may not achieve optimal loss—hybrid precision strategies may be necessary for frontier models where every loss point matters.

The NVFP4 vs MXFP4 comparison (Section 5) provides strong evidence for format superiority, but the 36% token overhead finding applies specifically to the 8B/1T configuration. Whether this ratio holds at 12B/10T scale remains untested.

### 5.6 Summary of Experimental Evidence

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Key Supporting Data |
|-------|-------------------|---------------------|
| 4-bit pretraining stable at 12B/10T scale | **Strong** | Figure 2 (loss trajectory), Table 2 (accuracies) |
| NVFP4 matches FP8 downstream accuracy | **Strong** | MMLU-Pro 62.58% vs 62.62%, most tasks within 1 point |
| All four methodology components necessary | **Strong** | Figure 4 ablations show degradation when any removed |
| NVFP4 more token-efficient than MXFP4 | **Strong** | Figure 6b: 36% fewer tokens to match loss |
| Forward pass dominates loss recovery | **Moderate** | Appendix D Figure 7, but contradicts prior work |
| Results generalize to all architectures | **Weak** | Only hybrid Mamba-Transformer tested |
| Coding performance matches FP8 | **Weak** | HumanEval+ and MBPP+ consistently lower |

The experimental design is rigorous for a technical report, with appropriate baselines, comprehensive metrics, and systematic ablations. The primary limitation is the narrow architectural scope and lack of runtime efficiency measurements. For practitioners, the results provide strong evidence that NVFP4 pretraining is viable—but with the caveat that task-specific performance (especially coding) and architecture-specific tuning may be required.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper demonstrates that stable 4-bit pretraining is feasible at significant scale, the approach relies on several assumptions and leaves important questions unresolved. This section critically examines the constraints, unaddressed scenarios, and open weaknesses that practitioners must consider before adopting NVFP4 training.

### 6.1 Architectural Assumptions and Generalization Gaps

**Hybrid Mamba-Transformer specificity.** All primary experiments use hybrid Mamba-Transformer architectures from the Nemotron-H family (Appendix A.1, A.2). The 12B validation model contains 6 Self-Attention blocks, 28 FFN blocks, and 28 Mamba-2 blocks (Table 3). The Conclusion explicitly acknowledges this limitation:

> "Future work will... evaluate it on larger models, longer token horizons, and additional architectures such as mixture-of-experts." (Section 6)

This statement implicitly admits that current results may not generalize to:
- **Pure Transformer architectures** (the dominant architecture for most existing LLMs)
- **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)** models (increasingly common for frontier systems)
- **Other emerging architectures** (e.g., state-space models without Transformer components)

The 1.2B ablation studies use a standard Transformer (Appendix A.3, Table 5), but this model is only used for component analysis, not for validating the full methodology at scale. Findings from 1.2B models do not always transfer to 12B models—for example, Hadamard matrix size sensitivity differs between scales (Appendix E.4.2, Figure 12). This creates uncertainty about whether the methodology will stabilize pure Transformer models at 12B+ scale.

**Layer sensitivity assumptions.** The mixed-precision strategy assumes that the **final layers** are most sensitive to FP4 quantization (Section 4.1, Appendix E.2). For the 12B model, 16% of linear layers (first two blocks + last eight blocks) remain in BF16. However, Figure 4 shows convergence remains stable with only the final four blocks in BF16, suggesting the 16% figure is conservative.

The paper does not explain **why** final layers are more sensitive. Appendix E.2 offers a hypothesis:

> "Based on tensor analysis, we observe the last layers tend to have larger quantization errors in the weight gradients." (Appendix E.2)

But this observation is not accompanied by quantitative tensor analysis or a principled method for identifying sensitive layers in new architectures. Practitioners adopting NVFP4 for different models must empirically determine which layers to keep in higher precision—a costly tuning process.

### 6.2 Task-Specific Performance Weaknesses

**Coding capability degradation.** Table 2 reveals consistent underperformance on coding benchmarks:

| Task | FP8 | NVFP4 | Difference |
|------|-----|-------|------------|
| HumanEval+ | 59.93 | 57.43 | **-2.50** |
| MBPP+ | 59.11 | 55.91 | **-3.20** |

The authors attribute this to evaluation noise:

> "We suspect the difference could be due to noisy evaluations: MBPP+ accuracy drops on the very final checkpoint evaluation and choosing another checkpoint could potentially lead to better accuracy for this task." (Section 3)

This explanation is **unverified**. The paper does not provide:
- Multiple checkpoint evaluations to assess variance
- Confidence intervals or standard deviations
- Analysis of whether coding tasks have different gradient distributions that interact poorly with FP4 quantization

For practitioners building code-generation models, this represents a **known risk** that is acknowledged but not resolved. The 3-point gap on MBPP+ is substantial for competitive coding models.

**Unexplained performance gains.** Conversely, NVFP4 **exceeds** FP8 on some tasks without explanation:

| Task | FP8 | NVFP4 | Difference |
|------|-----|-------|------------|
| AGIEval English CoT | 67.01 | 70.31 | **+3.30** |
| GSM8k CoT | 89.08 | 92.27 | **+3.19** |

The paper does not hypothesize why 4-bit training might improve certain reasoning tasks. This could indicate:
- Genuine benefits from quantization-induced regularization
- Evaluation variance (no multiple-seed runs reported)
- Dataset-specific interactions with the phased data-blending approach

Without confidence intervals or repeated evaluations, it is unclear whether these gains are reliable or artifacts of evaluation noise. This uncertainty makes it difficult to predict NVFP4's performance on unseen tasks.

### 6.3 Computational and Implementation Constraints

**No end-to-end runtime measurements.** The Introduction explicitly disclaims runtime analysis:

> "This report is primarily concerned with the underlying algorithms and methodology rather than with runtime efficiency or system-level optimizations." (Section 1)

While Table 1 cites theoretical speedups (4×–6× vs BF16 on Blackwell GPUs), the paper provides **no empirical training time comparisons**. Practitioners cannot directly infer:
- Actual wall-clock speedup after accounting for Hadamard transform overhead
- Memory savings in practice (vs theoretical 50% reduction)
- Communication overhead in distributed training scenarios

The Random Hadamard Transform alone introduces additional computation: a $d \times d$ matrix multiplication for each transformed tensor tile (Appendix C). For $d=16$, this is $16^2 = 256$ multiply-adds per tile, which may offset some FP4 arithmetic gains. The net efficiency benefit remains unquantified.

**Hardware dependency.** NVFP4 requires NVIDIA Blackwell GPUs with native Tensor Core support for 4-bit microscaling formats (Table 1). The format is not portable to:
- Previous-generation GPUs (Ampere, Hopper)
- Non-NVIDIA hardware (AMD, custom accelerators)
- CPU-based training infrastructure

This creates a **vendor lock-in** that limits adoption for organizations without Blackwell access. The paper does not discuss whether NVFP4 can be emulated efficiently on non-Blackwell hardware.

**Implementation complexity.** The methodology requires four coordinated techniques (Section 4):
1. Mixed-precision layer selection (identifying sensitive layers)
2. Random Hadamard transforms (with matrix size tuning)
3. 2D block scaling (different from standard NVFP4 scaling)
4. Stochastic rounding (gradient-specific)

Each component adds implementation overhead. The Transformer Engine support mentioned in the Abstract and Conclusion reduces this burden, but the paper does not quantify:
- Engineering effort required to integrate these techniques
- Debugging complexity when training diverges
- Memory overhead for storing additional scale factors and transform matrices

Appendix B describes a two-level scaling procedure requiring extra memory passes: "the global scale is computed dynamically across the entire tensor, it induces an extra pass through device memory" (Appendix B.1). This overhead is not quantified in terms of training throughput.

### 6.4 Precision Switching and "Pure" 4-bit Training

**Not truly end-to-end 4-bit.** While the paper demonstrates 4-bit pretraining, optimal loss recovery requires **precision switching** during final training stages (Appendix D). Figure 7 shows:

| Strategy | Switch Point | Relative Error |
|----------|--------------|----------------|
| NVFP4 throughout | N/A | ~1.5% |
| Switch Fprop to BF16 | 8.2T tokens | ~0.5% |
| Switch Fprop+Bprop to BF16 | 8.2T tokens | ~0% (matches FP8) |

To match FP8 loss completely, ~18% of training must occur in higher precision. The authors recommend:

> "Switching to high precision shortly before the onset of learning rate decay for full loss recovery, or at the very end for notable loss improvements with minimal effect on training runtime." (Appendix D)

This reveals that **pure 4-bit training does not achieve optimal loss**. For frontier models where every loss point matters, hybrid precision strategies are necessary. The paper does not quantify:
- Memory overhead of maintaining dual-precision pipelines
- Implementation complexity of dynamic precision switching
- Whether the 18% figure generalizes to other model sizes

**Contradiction with prior work.** Appendix D explicitly contradicts Chmiel et al. (2025) regarding which pass benefits from precision switching:

> "In contrast to Chmiel et al. (2025), which reports loss recovery from switching precision in the backward pass, we observe no such improvement in our models." (Appendix D)

The paper acknowledges this discrepancy but does not resolve it. This suggests that 4-bit training insights are **not universally transferable**—findings depend on model architecture, training horizon, or other uncontrolled variables. Practitioners cannot assume prior 4-bit training research applies without empirical validation.

### 6.5 Scale-Dependent Findings

**Ablation results vary by model size.** Several findings from 1.2B ablations do not hold at 12B scale:

| Finding | 1.2B Model | 12B Model |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| Hadamard matrix size sensitivity | No measurable difference (Appendix E.4.2) | 4×4 degrades, 16×16 optimal (Appendix E.4.2) |
| Randomization importance | No noticeable difference (Appendix E.4.3) | Benefits larger models (Section 4.2) |
| Stochastic rounding scope | Gradients only (Appendix E.3) | Gradients for both Dgrad and Wgrad (Appendix E.3) |

The paper explicitly warns:

> "It also highlights the need to experiment with larger models trained on longer token horizons, since conclusions from smaller scales may not always hold for larger models." (Appendix E.4.2)

This creates a **validation bottleneck**: practitioners cannot rely on small-scale ablations to tune NVFP4 for new models. Each architecture and scale combination may require fresh experimentation, increasing adoption costs.

**Token horizon uncertainty.** The 12B/10T experiment is the longest publicly documented 4-bit training run (Abstract). However, frontier models now train on **tens of trillions** of tokens. The paper does not address whether:
- Quantization errors continue to accumulate beyond 10T tokens
- The 1.5% loss gap widens further at longer horizons
- Precision switching requirements increase with token count

The Conclusion notes plans to "evaluate it on... longer token horizons" (Section 6), implicitly acknowledging this gap.

### 6.6 MXFP4 Comparison Limitations

**Token overhead ratio may not generalize.** Section 5 finds that MXFP4 requires 36% more tokens (1.36T vs 1T) to match NVFP4's loss on an 8B model (Figure 6b). However:
- This comparison uses an 8B model, not the 12B validation model
- The token horizon is 1T, not 10T
- The paper does not test whether the 36% ratio holds at larger scales

The Conclusion states:

> "Future studies should evaluate scaling laws for these formats on different parameter counts and token horizons." (Section 5)

This acknowledges that the token efficiency advantage is not yet characterized across scales. For practitioners choosing between NVFP4 and MXFP4, the 36% figure provides guidance but not certainty.

**MXFP4 configuration choices.** For the MXFP4 baseline, the authors use Hadamard transform size $d=32$ to align with MXFP4's 32-element block size (Section 5). This is a reasonable choice but not necessarily optimal. If MXFP4 could benefit from different transform sizes or other methodology adjustments, the comparison may underestimate MXFP4's potential. The paper does not explore MXFP4-specific methodology optimizations.

### 6.7 Summary of Key Limitations

| Limitation | Severity | Evidence |
|------------|----------|----------|
| Architecture specificity (hybrid Mamba-Transformer only) | **High** | Conclusion explicitly notes MoE/other architectures as future work |
| Coding task underperformance (-3 points on MBPP+) | **High** | Table 2, unexplained by authors |
| No runtime efficiency measurements | **High** | Introduction explicitly disclaims runtime analysis |
| Precision switching required for optimal loss | **Moderate** | Appendix D Figure 7, ~18% of training in higher precision |
| Scale-dependent ablation findings | **Moderate** | Appendix E.4.2, E.4.3 show 1.2B vs 12B differences |
| Hardware lock-in (Blackwell GPUs required) | **Moderate** | Table 1, native Tensor Core support |
| Implementation complexity (4 coordinated techniques) | **Moderate** | Section 4, Appendix B memory overhead noted |
| MXFP4 comparison at limited scale (8B/1T only) | **Low** | Section 5, Conclusion calls for more scaling studies |
| Evaluation variance (no confidence intervals) | **Low** | Table 2 shows unexplained gains without statistical analysis |

### 6.8 Open Questions for Future Research

The paper leaves several critical questions unresolved:

1. **Does NVFP4 stabilize pure Transformer architectures at 12B+ scale?** All large-scale experiments use hybrid Mamba-Transformer models.

2. **What is the actual wall-clock speedup?** Theoretical 4×–6× speedup (Table 1) is not empirically validated with end-to-end training times.

3. **How does coding performance degrade, and can it be fixed?** The MBPP+/HumanEval+ gap is acknowledged but not addressed.

4. **Does the 36% token overhead (NVFP4 vs MXFP4) hold at 10T+ token horizons?** The comparison is limited to 8B/1T.

5. **What is the memory overhead of two-level scaling and Hadamard transforms?** Appendix B notes extra memory passes but does not quantify costs.

6. **Can sensitive layers be identified algorithmically?** Currently, layer selection requires empirical tuning (Appendix E.2).

7. **Does quantization error accumulate beyond 10T tokens?** The longest run is 10T; frontier models train on more.

These open questions represent active research directions. For practitioners, the paper provides strong evidence that NVFP4 training is **feasible** at 12B/10T scale, but not that it is **optimal** for all use cases. The methodology requires careful validation for each target architecture, task domain, and scale combination.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than introduce a new numerical format; it fundamentally shifts the feasibility boundary for large-scale model training. By demonstrating stable 4-bit pretraining at the 12-billion-parameter scale over 10 trillion tokens, the authors move 4-bit training from a theoretical optimization to a production-ready capability. This section outlines how this work changes the research landscape, what specific avenues it opens for future inquiry, and how practitioners should integrate these findings into their own workflows.

### 7.1 Shifting the Landscape of Efficient Training

The primary implication of this work is the validation of **narrow-precision pretraining** as a viable path for frontier model development. Historically, quantization research has been heavily skewed toward *inference* (making models smaller after training) because training instability at low precision was considered a hard barrier. This paper dismantles that barrier.

*   **Redefining Efficiency Baselines:** Prior to this work, `FP8` was the practical lower bound for stable pretraining (Micikevicius et al., 2022). The Abstract notes that frontier training requires "tens to hundreds of yottaflops." By proving `NVFP4` achieves comparable loss to `FP8` (Figure 2) with half the memory footprint and potentially 4×–6× higher arithmetic throughput on Blackwell GPUs (Table 1), this work resets the efficiency baseline. Future models may be designed with 4-bit training as the default assumption rather than an exception.
*   **From Format to Methodology:** A critical shift in this paper is the recognition that format design alone is insufficient. The success of `NVFP4` is inextricably linked to the **four-component methodology** (Section 4). This changes how the field approaches efficiency: researchers can no longer focus solely on numerical formats (e.g., bit-width, exponent design) but must co-design training algorithms (e.g., scaling consistency, outlier management) alongside hardware support. The ablation studies in Figure 4 prove that removing any single component degrades convergence, establishing a new standard for "complete" low-precision training stacks.
*   **Validation at Scale:** Many prior 4-bit training claims were validated on small models (e.g., 1.2B) or short horizons. This paper's 12B/10T run (Abstract) provides the first public evidence that quantization errors do not accumulate catastrophically over multi-trillion-token horizons. This gives the community confidence to invest in 4-bit infrastructure for future frontier models, reducing the risk associated with adopting narrow precision.

### 7.2 Enabling Follow-up Research

This work establishes a foundation that enables several specific lines of inquiry. The authors explicitly highlight some of these in the Conclusion, but the data suggests additional avenues.

*   **Scaling Laws for 4-bit Training:** The paper compares `NVFP4` and `MXFP4` on an 8B model (Section 5), finding `NVFP4` requires 36% fewer tokens to match loss. However, the Conclusion notes: "Future studies should evaluate scaling laws for these formats on different parameter counts and token horizons." Research is needed to determine if the 36% token efficiency gap widens or narrows at 100B+ parameters. Does the advantage of `NVFP4`'s `E4M3` scales become more critical as model size increases outlier frequency?
*   **Architecture Generalization:** All large-scale experiments used hybrid Mamba-Transformer architectures (Appendix A.1). The Conclusion states plans to evaluate "additional architectures such as mixture-of-experts." Follow-up work must verify if the **2D block scaling** (Section 4.3) and **mixed-precision layer selection** (Section 4.1) hold for pure Transformer or MoE models. For instance, MoE models have sparse activation patterns that might interact differently with stochastic rounding (Section 4.4).
*   **Optimizing Precision Switching:** Appendix D demonstrates that switching to `BF16` for the final 18% of training recovers most of the loss gap (Figure 7). Future research could optimize this strategy:
    *   **Dynamic Switching:** Can layers switch precision individually based on gradient magnitude rather than a global schedule?
    *   **Earlier Switching:** Would switching at 50% of training yield better final loss, and what is the compute trade-off?
    *   **Backward Pass Nuances:** The paper contradicts Chmiel et al. (2025) regarding backward-pass switching (Appendix D). Resolving this discrepancy—whether it stems from architecture or horizon differences—is critical for standardizing precision switching protocols.
*   **Reducing High-Precision Overhead:** Currently, 16% of linear layers remain in `BF16` (Section 4.1). While Figure 4 suggests only the last four blocks might be sufficient, Appendix E.2 notes that layer sensitivity varies. Research into **automated layer selection**—using quantization error metrics to identify sensitive layers dynamically (Appendix E.2)—could reduce the high-precision footprint further, unlocking more memory savings.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

For practitioners, this work provides a clear decision framework for adopting 4-bit training. The choice depends on hardware availability, model architecture, and task requirements.

*   **Large-Scale Pretraining:** The primary use case is pretraining large models (10B+ parameters) where memory capacity and compute throughput are the limiting factors. Organizations with access to **NVIDIA Blackwell GPUs** (Table 1) should prioritize `NVFP4` integration to reduce training time and energy consumption. The 12B/10T results confirm this is safe for general-purpose models.
*   **Memory-Constrained Environments:** Since `FP4` reduces memory usage by approximately half compared to `FP8` (Introduction), `NVFP4` enables training larger models on fixed hardware clusters. For example, a cluster sized for an 8B `FP8` model might accommodate a 12B+ `NVFP4` model, assuming communication overheads scale similarly.
*   **Task-Specific Considerations:** Practitioners must weigh the accuracy trade-offs observed in Table 2:
    *   **Recommended:** General knowledge (MMLU), mathematics (GSM8k), and commonsense reasoning (ARC Challenge). `NVFP4` matched or exceeded `FP8` on these tasks.
    *   **Caution Advised:** Code generation (HumanEval+, MBPP+). `NVFP4` underperformed `FP8` by 2.5–3.2 points (Table 2). Until this gap is understood or mitigated, code-specific models might benefit from staying in `FP8` or using precision switching earlier in training.
*   **Inference Deployment:** While this paper focuses on training, models trained in `NVFP4` are naturally suited for `NVFP4` inference. This creates an end-to-end 4-bit pipeline, eliminating the need for post-training quantization (PTQ) which often degrades accuracy. The "near-FP8 precision" for block maximums (Section 2) suggests these models will retain high fidelity during deployment.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

Integrating `NVFP4` training requires specific hardware, software, and configuration choices. Based on the paper's methodology, the following checklist outlines the requirements for successful adoption.

*   **Hardware Requirements:**
    *   **GPU:** NVIDIA Blackwell architecture (e.g., GB200, GB300) is required for native `NVFP4` Tensor Core support (Table 1). Emulation on prior architectures (Hopper, Ampere) is not discussed and likely inefficient.
    *   **Memory:** Ensure sufficient HBM capacity to leverage the 50% memory reduction, potentially allowing for larger batch sizes or sequence lengths (e.g., the 8192 sequence length used in Appendix A.1).

*   **Software Stack:**
    *   **Transformer Engine:** The Conclusion states "`NVFP4` training on Blackwell is now fully supported via a recent update to Transformer Engine." Practitioners should use this library rather than implementing custom kernels, as it handles the complex two-level scaling (Appendix B) and 2D block coordination (Section 4.3).
    *   **Precision Management:** The software must support dynamic precision switching if adopting the loss recovery strategy from Appendix D.

*   **Configuration Checklist:**
    1.  **Mixed Precision:** Keep the final ~15% of linear layers (e.g., last 4–8 blocks) in `BF16` (Section 4.1). Do not quantize embeddings, normalization layers, or the output head (Section 4.1).
    2.  **Scaling:** Enable **2D block scaling** ($16 \times 16$) for weights to preserve chain rule consistency (Section 4.3). Use standard $1 \times 16$ scaling for activations and gradients.
    3.  **Outlier Management:** Apply **Random Hadamard Transforms** ($d=16$) to weight gradient (Wgrad) inputs only (Section 4.2). Do not apply to forward pass inputs unless validated for your specific architecture.
    4.  **Rounding:** Enable **stochastic rounding** for gradients (both Dgrad and Wgrad) but use round-to-nearest-even for weights and activations (Section 4.4, Appendix E.3).
    5.  **Loss Recovery (Optional):** For critical applications, schedule a switch to `BF16` for the forward pass during the learning rate decay phase (last 20% of training) to recover the final 1% loss gap (Appendix D).

*   **Validation Strategy:**
    *   Do not rely solely on training loss. Monitor downstream benchmarks throughout training (Figure 3) to detect task-specific degradation early.
    *   Run ablations on a smaller scale (e.g., 1.2B) to tune layer sensitivity before scaling to 12B+, but verify findings at scale as sensitivities may shift (Appendix E.4.2).

### 7.5 Summary of Impact

This paper marks a transition point in deep learning infrastructure. It demonstrates that **4-bit precision is no longer a compromise** but a viable standard for pretraining, provided it is accompanied by the correct algorithmic safeguards. The integration of `NVFP4` into the Transformer Engine suggests NVIDIA intends to standardize this format for future generations of models. For the research community, the challenge shifts from "Can we train in 4-bit?" to "How do we optimize the 4-bit pipeline for specific architectures and tasks?" The answers to these questions will define the efficiency frontier of AI development for the next decade.
