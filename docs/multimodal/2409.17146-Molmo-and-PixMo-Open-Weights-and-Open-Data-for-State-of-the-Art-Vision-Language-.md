## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces **Molmo**, a family of state-of-the-art open-weight vision-language models (VLMs), and **PixMo**, a suite of seven novel training datasets collected entirely without relying on synthetic data from proprietary VLMs. The core contribution is demonstrating that high-performance VLMs can be built from scratch using carefully curated human-annotated data—specifically **712k** detailed image captions, **162k** instruction-following pairs, and **2.3 million** 2D pointing annotations—rather than distilling closed models like GPT-4V. This approach matters because the resulting **Molmo-72B** model outperforms leading proprietary systems such as **Claude 3.5 Sonnet** and **Gemini 1.5 Pro** on academic benchmarks and ranks second only to **GPT-4o** in human preference evaluations, effectively closing the gap between open and closed multimodal AI while restoring full transparency to the training pipeline.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Transparency Gap in Vision-Language Models
The central problem this paper addresses is the **lack of foundational knowledge** regarding how to build state-of-the-art Vision-Language Models (VLMs) from scratch using fully open data. While VLMs like GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5 Pro, and Claude 3.5 Sonnet have achieved remarkable capabilities in describing images and answering complex visual questions, they remain **proprietary**. This means their model weights, training code, and crucially, their training data are closed off from the scientific community.

This opacity creates a significant bottleneck for research. Without access to the data that powers these models, researchers cannot rigorously study *why* certain architectures work, how data quality influences specific capabilities (like counting or OCR), or how to improve models without relying on trial-and-error black boxes. The field risks stagnating in a cycle where progress is only possible by accessing closed APIs, preventing independent verification and innovation.

### The "Distillation Trap" of Current Open Models
To bridge the performance gap, the research community has attempted to reproduce these capabilities in open-weight models. However, the paper identifies a critical flaw in recent approaches, which it terms the **"distillation trap."**

1.  **Early Open Efforts:** Initial open models, such as **LLaVA**, released both weights and training data. While fully transparent, these models now lag significantly behind the state-of-the-art in performance.
2.  **The Synthetic Data Shortcut:** More recent, higher-performing open-weight models (e.g., those trained on **ShareGPT4V**) have achieved better results by relying heavily on **synthetic data**. In this workflow, researchers use a proprietary VLM (like GPT-4V) to generate detailed image captions or question-answer pairs, which are then used to train the open model.
    *   **The Consequence:** As the authors note, these open models are effectively **distillations** of closed models. They inherit the capabilities—and potentially the biases and limitations—of the proprietary teacher model.
    *   **The Scientific Void:** Because the training data is generated by a closed system, the community still lacks understanding of how to curate high-quality *native* data. If the proprietary model changes its behavior or becomes unavailable, the foundation for these "open" models crumbles. We do not know how to build performant VLMs without leaning on closed-source giants.

### Why Native Data Collection Matters
The importance of solving this problem extends beyond mere academic curiosity; it is fundamental to the future of open AI:
*   **Reproducibility and Independence:** True openness requires that a model can be re-trained from scratch without dependency on external, closed APIs. If a model's training data relies on GPT-4V, it is not truly reproducible by the community.
*   **Understanding Capability Emergence:** By collecting data directly from humans rather than distilling it from another model, researchers can isolate which data characteristics drive specific skills. For instance, does detailed captioning improve reasoning? Does pointing data improve counting? These causal links are obscured when the data is pre-processed by another AI.
*   **Avoiding Model Collapse:** Relying on synthetic data generated by other models risks "model collapse," where errors and artifacts are amplified over generations of training. High-quality, human-verified data acts as a ground-truth anchor.

### Positioning Molmo and PixMo
This paper positions **Molmo** (the model family) and **PixMo** (the dataset suite) as a direct solution to the distillation trap. Unlike prior work that treats data as a commodity to be generated by stronger models, this work treats **data collection as a primary research contribution**.

The authors explicitly differentiate their approach from existing categories of VLMs:
*   **Vs. Proprietary Models:** Molmo offers competitive performance (surpassing Claude 3.5 Sonnet and Gemini 1.5 Pro on benchmarks) while releasing all weights, code, and data.
*   **Vs. Distilled Open Models:** Molmo is trained on **PixMo**, a dataset collected *without* using external VLMs. This includes:
    *   **PixMo-Cap:** 712k images with dense, human-spoken captions (averaging 196 words), avoiding the brevity of standard datasets like COCO (11 words).
    *   **PixMo-Points:** 2.3 million 2D pointing annotations, a novel modality for grounding and counting that is difficult to synthesize reliably.
    *   **PixMo-AskModelAnything:** 162k instruction-following pairs refined through human-in-the-loop editing.

By demonstrating that a model trained on this natively collected data can outperform models distilled from GPT-4V, the paper challenges the prevailing assumption that synthetic data is a prerequisite for state-of-the-art performance. It re-establishes the viability of human-centric data collection as the gold standard for building foundational multimodal models.

## 3. Technical Approach

This section details the construction of the Molmo family of models, arguing that state-of-the-art performance in vision-language tasks arises not from architectural novelty alone, but from the synergistic combination of a robust, standard architecture with high-fidelity, natively collected data and specific training optimizations. The core idea is that by replacing synthetic, distilled data with dense human annotations and introducing targeted mechanisms like overlapping crops and text-only dropout, one can train models that match or exceed proprietary systems without relying on their outputs.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The Molmo system is a multimodal artificial intelligence that accepts an image and a text prompt as input, processes the visual information into a sequence of tokens compatible with a language model, and generates a text response (which can include coordinate points for grounding) as output. It solves the problem of bridging the gap between raw pixel data and high-level language reasoning by using a specialized "connector" module to translate visual features into the language model's embedding space, augmented by a novel data pipeline that forces the model to learn fine-grained details through long-form captions and explicit pointing tasks rather than short, generic labels.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The Molmo architecture follows a standard decoder-only design composed of four sequential stages:
1.  **Image Pre-processor:** Takes a raw input image and decomposes it into a set of overlapping square crops plus one low-resolution global view, preserving aspect ratio and fine details.
2.  **Vision Encoder (ViT):** A frozen or fine-tuned Vision Transformer (specifically OpenAI CLIP ViT-L/14 or MetaCLIP) that independently processes each crop to extract patch-level feature vectors.
3.  **Vision-Language Connector:** A learnable module that pools local patch features (using attention) and projects them into the dimensionality of the Large Language Model's embedding space.
4.  **Decoder-Only LLM:** A pre-trained language model (ranging from OLMoE-1B to Qwen2-72B) that receives the sequence of projected visual tokens interleaved with text tokens and autoregressively generates the response.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
To fully understand how Molmo achieves its performance, we will proceed in the following logical order:
*   **Image Encoding Strategy:** We first explain the novel "overlapping multi-crop" mechanism, as this determines the quality and resolution of visual information entering the model, which is critical for tasks like OCR and counting.
*   **Feature Projection:** We then detail the connector architecture, specifically how it aggregates patch features using attention pooling rather than simple concatenation to create efficient visual tokens.
*   **Token Arrangement and Dropout:** We describe how visual and text tokens are sequenced for the LLM and the specific "text-only dropout" technique used during pre-training to prevent the model from ignoring the image.
*   **Training Pipeline Efficiency:** We explain the method for handling multiple annotations per image in a single forward pass, a key optimization that drastically reduces training time.
*   **Data-Model Interaction:** Finally, we connect these architectural choices to the specific PixMo datasets, showing how the model's output formats (like plain-text coordinates) are designed to leverage the unique pointing and captioning data.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Image Pre-processing: The Overlapping Multi-Crop Strategy
The fundamental challenge in vision-language modeling is that standard Vision Transformers (ViTs) require fixed-size square inputs (e.g., $336 \times 336$ pixels), which is often too low-resolution for fine-grained tasks like reading small text or counting distinct objects in a high-resolution image. To address this, Molmo employs a tiling strategy where the input image is divided into multiple square crops that collectively cover the entire image, alongside a single low-resolution version of the full image that provides global context. However, a naive tiling approach creates a "border effect" where patches at the edge of a crop lack the contextual information from adjacent patches, potentially breaking the understanding of objects that span crop boundaries.

Molmo introduces a novel **overlapping crop** mechanism to solve this context loss. Instead of tiling crops strictly edge-to-edge, the system shifts the crop windows so that they overlap by a fixed margin (specifically 4 patches or 56 pixels for a ViT with patch size 14). During encoding, the Vision Transformer processes these overlapping regions, allowing every patch to be computed with its full surrounding context. Crucially, after encoding, the features corresponding to the overlapping margins are discarded before being passed to the connector; only the features from the non-overlapping central regions of each crop are retained and arranged to perfectly tile the original high-resolution image. This design choice ensures that the model receives high-resolution features with full contextual awareness, albeit at the cost of slightly reducing the effective tiled resolution, a trade-off the authors found significantly improves performance on downstream benchmarks (Table 2d).

The pre-processor dynamically selects a grid configuration (e.g., $2 \times 2$, $3 \times 1$) that minimizes the amount of up-scaling or down-scaling required to fit the image while respecting a maximum crop limit (typically 12 high-resolution crops). The image is padded with black borders to fit the chosen grid exactly, and the model is trained to distinguish between natural black pixels and padding via learned embeddings.

#### Vision Encoder and Feature Extraction
Once the image is decomposed into crops, each crop is processed independently by a pre-trained Vision Transformer. The authors primarily utilize the **OpenAI CLIP ViT-L/14** model with an input resolution of $336 \times 336$ pixels, though they demonstrate that fully open alternatives like **MetaCLIP** and **SigLIP** yield comparable results. The encoder outputs a sequence of feature vectors, where each vector corresponds to a $14 \times 14$ pixel patch of the input crop.

To maximize the information extracted from the ViT, Molmo does not rely on the output of a single layer. Instead, it concatenates the feature maps from two specific layers: the **third-to-last layer** and the **tenth-from-last layer**. Empirical ablations (Table 15a) show that combining features from a layer near the end of the network (rich in semantic information) with a layer from the middle (rich in spatial and texture details) provides a slight but consistent performance boost over using either layer alone.

#### The Vision-Language Connector: Attention Pooling
The raw patch features from the ViT exist in a high-dimensional space (e.g., 1024 dimensions for ViT-L) and are too numerous to feed directly into an LLM without exploding the context window. The **connector** module serves to compress and project these features into the LLM's embedding space. Molmo departs from simple linear projection or flattening by employing a **multi-headed attention pooling** mechanism.

The connector operates on $2 \times 2$ windows of adjacent patch features. For each window, it computes a single pooled vector using a cross-attention mechanism where the **query** is the mean of the four patch vectors in that window, and the **keys and values** are the four patch vectors themselves. Mathematically, for a window of patches $P = \{p_1, p_2, p_3, p_4\}$, the query $q$ is defined as:
$$ q = \frac{1}{4} \sum_{i=1}^{4} p_i $$
The output of the pooling layer $z$ is then:
$$ z = \text{Attention}(q, P, P) $$
This attention-based pooling allows the model to dynamically weigh the importance of different patches within the local window, preserving salient details better than simple averaging or max-pooling. The resulting pooled vectors are then passed through a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) to project them into the dimensionality of the target LLM (e.g., 4096 for a 7B model). Ablation studies (Table 2f) confirm that this attention pooling strategy outperforms simple feature stacking, yielding higher captioning F1 scores and better benchmark averages.

#### Token Arrangement and Sequence Construction
After projection, the visual tokens must be arranged into a linear sequence that the autoregressive LLM can process. Molmo constructs this sequence in a specific order to preserve spatial structure:
1.  **Global Context:** The sequence begins with the tokens from the low-resolution full-image crop.
2.  **High-Resolution Tiles:** This is followed by the tokens from the high-resolution crops, arranged in row-major order (left-to-right, top-to-bottom).
3.  **Structural Tokens:** Special learned tokens are inserted to demarcate the structure. An `<image_start>` and `<image_end>` token surround the entire visual sequence. Additionally, `<row_end>` tokens are inserted between the rows of high-resolution patches to explicitly signal row transitions to the LLM.

This structured arrangement allows the language model to implicitly learn the 2D layout of the image, which is crucial for tasks requiring spatial reasoning, such as "point to the top-left corner" or reading text across lines.

#### Training Mechanisms: Text-Only Dropout and Multi-Annotation Efficiency
A common failure mode in VLM training is that the model learns to rely on language priors (statistical patterns in text) rather than the visual input, effectively hallucinating answers without looking at the image. To combat this during the pre-training phase on dense captions, Molmo employs a **text-only dropout** strategy. While residual dropout is applied to the LLM generally, during pre-training, dropout is applied **exclusively to the text tokens** of the target caption, while the vision tokens and prompt tokens remain intact. This forces the model to depend heavily on the visual information to predict the next token, as the linguistic context is frequently corrupted. The authors note that this specific configuration improves both captioning precision/recall and downstream task performance (Table 2c).

Furthermore, the PixMo datasets often contain multiple annotations for a single image (e.g., multiple questions for one image, or multiple spoken transcripts for one caption). Naively training on these would require encoding the same image multiple times, wasting significant compute. Molmo implements an efficient **multi-annotation training** scheme where all text annotations for a given image are concatenated into a single long sequence. The attention mask is modified such that:
*   Text tokens for a specific annotation can attend to the shared image tokens.
*   Text tokens can attend to other text tokens within the *same* annotation.
*   Text tokens **cannot** attend to text tokens from *different* annotations associated with the same image.

This setup is mathematically equivalent to training on individual image-text pairs but avoids redundant image encoding. The paper reports that this optimization reduces the number of processed images by two-thirds and shortens total training time by over 50%, with only a modest 25% increase in sequence length.

#### Output Formatting: Plain-Text Pointing
Unlike models that use special discrete tokens or segmentation masks for grounding, Molmo represents 2D points as **plain-text coordinates** normalized to a $0-100$ scale. When the model needs to point to an object, it generates an HTML-like tag in the text stream:
```html
<point x="63.5" y="44.5" alt="Mt. Rainier">Mt. Rainier</point>
```
For counting tasks, the model generates a sequence of such points, effectively "counting by pointing." The authors found that training with points in a predictable spatial order (top-down, left-to-right) significantly improves counting accuracy compared to unordered points (Table 4b). Surprisingly, representing these coordinates as plain text numbers outperforms adding 1,000 special location tokens to the vocabulary (Table 4d), likely because the LLM's existing arithmetic and sequence modeling capabilities are better suited to handling numerical strings than learning a new set of discrete spatial embeddings.

#### Hyperparameters and Scale
The Molmo family spans several scales, utilizing different backbones to balance efficiency and performance:
*   **MolmoE-1B:** Uses the OLMoE-1B-7B mixture-of-experts LLM (1.2B active parameters, 6.9B total).
*   **Molmo-7B-O/D:** Uses OLMo-7B or Qwen2-7B backbones.
*   **Molmo-72B:** Uses the Qwen2-72B backbone.

All models use a ViT-L/14 encoder. During pre-training, the learning rates are set to $2 \times 10^{-4}$ for the connector, $6 \times 10^{-6}$ for the ViT, and $2 \times 10^{-5}$ for the LLM, with a cosine decay schedule. The connector uses a short warmup of 200 steps, while the ViT and LLM use 2,000 steps. Notably, the authors found that a separate "connector-only" tuning stage, common in prior work (like LLaVA), is unnecessary when training on high-quality PixMo-Cap data; instead, they simply apply a higher learning rate to the connector at the start of joint training. This simplifies the pipeline and eliminates the need for noisy web-scale data typically used in that intermediate stage.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The success of Molmo is not driven by a single architectural breakthrough, but rather by a fundamental shift in philosophy regarding data collection and specific, targeted interventions that solve known failure modes in vision-language modeling. The following insights distinguish between incremental engineering improvements and the foundational innovations that allow Molmo to compete with proprietary giants without distillation.

### 4.1 The "Speech-to-Text" Modality Switch for Dense Captioning
**Innovation Type:** Fundamental Data Collection Methodology

Prior approaches to collecting detailed image captions faced a trilemma: annotators either wrote short, superficial descriptions (as seen in COCO captions, averaging 11 words), spent excessive time typing long paragraphs leading to low throughput, or cheated by copying outputs from proprietary VLMs (creating the distillation loop the field seeks to avoid).

Molmo introduces a novel **modality-switching trick** to break this deadlock. Instead of asking annotators to type, the pipeline instructs them to **speak** their description of the image for 60 to 90 seconds.
*   **Why it works:** Speaking is naturally faster and more fluid than typing, allowing annotators to produce significantly more detail (averaging **196 words** per caption) in less time. Crucially, the system records the **audio receipt** alongside the transcript. This audio file serves as cryptographic proof that the description was generated by a human in real-time, making it impossible to have copy-pasted text from a silent VLM API.
*   **Significance:** This method yielded **712k** high-fidelity, dense captions (PixMo-Cap) that are an order of magnitude more detailed than standard datasets. As shown in **Table 3a**, scaling this specific type of native human data from 0 to 712k images drives a massive jump in performance (from 74.9 to 76.9 on the 11-benchmark average), proving that *quality and density* of native data outweigh the quantity of noisy web-scale data. This validates that we do not need GPT-4V to teach models how to see details; we just need a better interface for humans to express what they see.

### 4.2 Counting as a Grounded Chain-of-Thought Process
**Innovation Type:** New Capability via Task Reformulation

Traditional VLMs treat counting as a direct regression or classification problem (e.g., outputting the token "5"), which often leads to hallucination because the model has no mechanism to verify its answer against the visual evidence. Molmo redefines counting as a **grounded chain-of-thought** task enabled by the **PixMo-Points** dataset.

*   **The Mechanism:** Rather than predicting a number directly, the model is trained to first generate a sequence of 2D coordinates pointing to each instance of the target object, and *then* output the total count based on the length of that sequence. As detailed in **Table 4a**, the "point-then-count" strategy achieves **86.3%** accuracy on the challenging PixMo-Count validation set, whereas "count-only" drops to **80.2%**.
*   **Why it's different:** This approach forces the model to perform explicit visual grounding before committing to a numerical answer. It transforms counting from a "guess" into a verifiable procedure. Furthermore, the data collection leveraged the efficiency of pointing (clicking) over drawing bounding boxes, enabling the collection of **2.3 million** grounding annotations—a scale previously unattainable for multi-object grounding.
*   **Significance:** This innovation propels Molmo to state-of-the-art performance on counting benchmarks (**Table 1**), surpassing both open and proprietary models. It demonstrates that complex visual reasoning can be unlocked not by larger models, but by structuring the output space to require intermediate verification steps (pointing) that align with human cognitive processes.

### 4.3 Overlapping Crops: Solving the Context-Boundary Trade-off
**Innovation Type:** Architectural Refinement with High Impact

While multi-crop strategies (tiling images) exist in prior work to handle high resolutions, they suffer from a critical flaw: patches at the border of a crop lack context from neighboring crops, causing objects spanning boundaries to be fragmented or misidentified.

Molmo introduces **overlapping crops** with a specific discard strategy. The system shifts crop windows to overlap by a fixed margin (4 patches), ensuring every patch is encoded with its full surrounding context. However, to maintain a consistent token budget and avoid duplicate information, the features from the overlapping margins are **discarded** before being passed to the connector; only the central, non-overlapping regions are retained to tile the image.
*   **Evidence of Impact:** As shown in **Table 2d**, moving from "multi-crop, no overlap" to "multi-crop, overlap" yields a significant gain in the 11-benchmark average (from **75.7** to **76.9**) and captioning F1 (from **53.4** to **54.1**).
*   **Significance:** This is a subtle but powerful design choice that maximizes the information density of every token sent to the LLM. It ensures that fine-grained details (like text characters or small objects) are never processed in isolation, directly addressing the resolution limitations that plague standard ViTs without inflating the context window with redundant data.

### 4.4 Text-Only Dropout: Forcing Visual Reliance
**Innovation Type:** Training Regularization Strategy

A pervasive issue in VLM training is "language priors," where the model ignores the image and predicts answers based solely on statistical patterns in the text (e.g., answering "yes" to "Is this a picture of...?" regardless of the image). Standard dropout applies noise to all tokens equally, which does not specifically penalize ignoring the vision input.

Molmo employs **text-only dropout** during the pre-training phase. In this scheme, dropout is applied exclusively to the *target* text tokens (the caption being generated), while the vision tokens and prompt remain untouched.
*   **The Logic:** By corrupting the linguistic context while keeping the visual signal pristine, the model is forced to rely on the image features to predict the next token. If it tries to rely on language priors, the corrupted context makes prediction impossible.
*   **Significance:** **Table 2c** shows that this specific regularization improves both the captioning metric (cap F1) and downstream benchmark performance compared to standard dropout or no dropout. It effectively acts as an anti-hallucination mechanism, ensuring the model learns to "look" before it "speaks," a critical requirement for the dense, detailed descriptions found in PixMo-Cap.

### 4.5 The Viability of Fully Open, Non-Distilled Pipelines
**Innovation Type:** Paradigm Shift

Perhaps the most profound insight of the paper is empirical rather than algorithmic: **distillation is not a prerequisite for state-of-the-art performance.**

For years, the prevailing assumption in the open-source community was that to match GPT-4V, one must train on data *generated* by GPT-4V. Molmo shatters this assumption.
*   **The Evidence:** **Table 3b** directly compares pre-training on PixMo-Cap (human data) versus ShareGPT4V (distilled data) versus images captioned by GPT-4o. The human-curated PixMo-Cap data performs on par with, and in some downstream metrics better than, the distilled data. Furthermore, **Table 1** shows Molmo-72B outperforming Claude 3.5 Sonnet and Gemini 1.5 Pro on academic benchmarks.
*   **Significance:** This validates the scientific premise that **native data collection** is a viable and superior path forward. It frees the community from dependency on closed APIs for data generation, preventing "model collapse" and allowing for true reproducibility. It proves that with careful design (overlapping crops, text-only dropout) and rigorous human-in-the-loop collection (speech-to-text, pointing), open models can lead the field.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

To validate the claim that natively collected data can produce state-of-the-art vision-language models without distillation, the authors employ a rigorous dual-evaluation strategy: **academic benchmarking** across 11 diverse tasks and **human preference evaluation** via pairwise comparisons. This section dissects the methodology, quantifies the results, and critically analyzes the ablation studies that isolate the specific contributions of the PixMo datasets and architectural choices.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The evaluation framework is designed to test both specific technical capabilities (via standardized benchmarks) and holistic user experience (via human judgment).

**Academic Benchmarks:**
The authors evaluate Molmo models on **11 distinct datasets** covering a broad spectrum of visual reasoning skills:
*   **General VQA:** `VQA v2.0` (open-ended), `OK-VQA` (external knowledge), `A-OKVQA` (multiple choice), `AI2D` (diagrams), and `RealWorldQA` (natural images).
*   **Document & Chart Understanding:** `DocVQA`, `InfographicVQA`, `ChartQA`, `TextVQA` (OCR-centric), `TabMWP`, `ST-VQA`, `PlotQA`, and `FigureQA`.
*   **Reasoning & Math:** `MMMU` (multidisciplinary expert level) and `MathVista` (visual math).
*   **Counting:** The authors introduce **`PixMo-Count`**, a new test set of natural images with counts ranging from 0 to 10, designed to be more challenging than the existing `CountBenchQA`.

*Crucial Methodological Detail:* To ensure fair comparison with models trained on specific dataset styles (e.g., `DocVQA` requiring verbatim text extraction), the authors use **style tags** during evaluation (e.g., prefixing questions with `vqa2:` or `docvqa:`). This prompts the model to adopt the specific answer format expected by the benchmark metric, preventing style mismatches from penalizing performance. For counting tasks, where pointing is integral, they strictly match the number of image crops used at training and test time to avoid resolution mismatches.

**Human Preference Evaluation (Elo Rating):**
Recognizing that academic benchmarks often favor short, specific answers over helpful, conversational responses, the authors conducted a large-scale human evaluation.
*   **Dataset:** They collected **15,000** diverse image-text prompt pairs across 10 categories (e.g., fine-grained QA, counting, documents, creativity).
*   **Protocol:** Using a pairwise comparison setup similar to Chatbot Arena, ~870 annotators evaluated over **325,000** model responses. Annotators chose between "Model A is better," "Model B is better," or ties (both good/bad).
*   **Metric:** Results are reported as an **Elo score** derived from the Bradley-Terry model, providing a global ranking of user preference.

**Baselines:**
The paper compares Molmo against three tiers of models:
1.  **Proprietary (API-only):** GPT-4o, GPT-4V, Gemini 1.5 Pro/Flash, Claude 3.5 Sonnet/Opus/Haiku.
2.  **Open Weights (Closed/Data-Distilled):** Qwen2-VL, InternVL2, Llama-3.2V, Pixtral.
3.  **Open Weights + Open Data (Distilled):** LLaVA-OneVision, Cambrian-1, xGen-MM (noted with a dagger † in tables to indicate reliance on synthetic data from proprietary VLMs).

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

The results demonstrate that Molmo not only leads the "fully open" category but competes directly with, and often surpasses, the strongest proprietary systems.

**Overall Performance (Table 1):**
*   **Molmo-72B** achieves the highest average score (**81.2**) across the 11 academic benchmarks, outperforming **GPT-4o** (78.5), **Gemini 1.5 Pro** (78.3), and **Claude 3.5 Sonnet** (76.7).
*   In human preference, Molmo-72B achieves an Elo score of **1077**, ranking **2nd** overall, just behind GPT-4o (1079) and ahead of Gemini 1.5 Pro (1074) and Claude 3.5 Sonnet (1069).
*   **Efficiency:** The smaller **MolmoE-1B** (based on a mixture-of-experts LLM) nearly matches **GPT-4V** on both benchmarks (68.6 vs. 71.1) and Elo (1032 vs. 1041), despite having significantly fewer active parameters.

**Domain-Specific Strengths:**
*   **Counting:** Molmo dominates counting tasks, a direct result of the `PixMo-Points` dataset. On `CountBenchQA`, Molmo-72B scores **91.2**, significantly higher than GPT-4o (87.9) and Qwen2-VL-72B (80.4). On the harder `PixMo-Count` test set, Molmo-72B achieves **85.2**, while GPT-4o drops to 59.6. This massive gap highlights the effectiveness of the "point-then-count" chain-of-thought strategy.
*   **Natural Images:** On `RealWorldQA`, Molmo-72B scores **75.2**, matching Llama-3.2V-90B (69.8) and exceeding GPT-4V (61.4).
*   **OCR & Documents:** Molmo performs strongly on OCR tasks (`TextVQA`: 83.1; `DocVQA`: 93.5), surpassing most open models and some proprietary ones, though it slightly trails **Qwen2-VL-72B** (96.5 on DocVQA), suggesting that specialized OCR pre-training in Qwen provides a marginal edge in pure text extraction.
*   **Reasoning Gap:** The results reveal a weakness in complex reasoning. On `MMMU` and `MathVista`, Molmo-72B scores **54.1** and **58.6** respectively, lagging behind GPT-4o (69.1/63.8) and Claude 3.5 Sonnet (68.3/67.7). The authors attribute this to the training mix lacking advanced reasoning-specific data, indicating a clear avenue for future improvement.

**Clock Reading (Table 10):**
In a specialized evaluation on analog clock reading, Molmo exhibits a startling advantage. While proprietary models like GPT-4o (2.7% accuracy) and Gemini 1.5 Pro (0.9%) struggle immensely, **Molmo-7B-D** achieves **68.2%** accuracy. This is driven entirely by the synthetic `PixMo-Clocks` dataset. Although it trails a specialized single-task model (78.9%), Molmo's performance is remarkable for a generalist VLM, proving that targeted synthetic data can instill specific, robust skills that general training misses.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies: Isolating the Drivers of Success

The paper provides extensive ablation studies (Tables 2, 3, 4, 5) that rigorously justify each design choice, moving beyond "it works" to explain *why* it works.

**Data Ablations (Table 3):**
*   **Scaling Native Captions:** Increasing `PixMo-Cap` from 0 to 712k images yields a steady increase in the 11-benchmark average from **74.9** to **76.9**. Crucially, removing `PixMo-Cap` entirely drops the human preference win rate against the default model to just **35%** (Table 5), proving that dense captions are the bedrock of the model's conversational quality.
*   **Human vs. Distilled Data:** In a direct comparison (Table 3b), pre-training on human-annotated `PixMo-Cap` (54.1 cap F1) outperforms pre-training on `ShareGPT4V` (36.3 cap F1), a popular distilled dataset. Even more telling, human data performs on par with using GPT-4o to caption the *same* images (52.9 cap F1), validating that the *source* of the data (diverse human speech) is as valuable as the *quality* of the teacher model.
*   **Fine-Tuning Mix:** Removing the proprietary-style academic datasets and training *only* on PixMo data still yields a strong model (76.9 avg), but adding academic datasets boosts specific benchmark scores (Table 3c). However, removing `PixMo-AskModelAnything` significantly hurts human preference (win rate drops to 40%), confirming that this dataset is critical for aligning the model with natural user interactions.

**Architectural Ablations (Table 2):**
*   **Overlapping Crops:** The impact of the overlapping crop strategy is undeniable. Moving from single-crop (62.8 avg) to multi-crop without overlap (75.7 avg) provides a huge boost, but adding the overlap pushes performance to **76.9**. This confirms that contextual continuity across crop boundaries is essential for fine-grained understanding.
*   **Text-Only Dropout:** Applying dropout only to text tokens during pre-training improves the captioning F1 from 53.7 (standard dropout) to **54.1**. While the absolute gain seems small, the authors note this correlates with reduced hallucination and better downstream performance.
*   **Pooling Strategy:** Replacing simple feature stacking with **attention pooling** in the connector improves the 11-benchmark average from 76.1 to **76.9**, demonstrating that dynamic weighting of local visual features aids the LLM's interpretation.

**Counting Mechanics (Table 4):**
The ablations on counting reveal the precise mechanics of Molmo's success:
*   **Order Matters:** Training with points in a predictable top-down, left-to-right order yields **86.3%** accuracy on PixMo-Count, whereas unordered points drop to **74.1%**. This suggests the LLM leverages its inherent sequence modeling capabilities to "count" the generated points.
*   **Plain Text vs. Special Tokens:** Representing coordinates as plain text numbers (e.g., `x="63.5"`) significantly outperforms adding 1,000 special location tokens to the vocabulary (86.3% vs. 80.9%). This implies that leveraging the LLM's existing numerical reasoning is more effective than forcing it to learn a new discrete spatial embedding space.
*   **Verification:** Simply generating extra tokens (random points) does not help; the points must be **correct** (86.3% vs. 76.3% for random points). This confirms the model is genuinely grounding its answer, not just padding the sequence.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment and Limitations

The experiments convincingly support the paper's central thesis: **high-quality, natively collected data combined with targeted architectural refinements can produce state-of-the-art VLMs without distillation.** The evidence is robust:
1.  **Direct Comparison:** Molmo beats distilled open models (like LLaVA-OneVision) and proprietary giants (Claude 3.5, Gemini 1.5) on aggregate benchmarks.
2.  **Ablation Proof:** The step-by-step removal of PixMo components or architectural features (overlap, dropout) consistently degrades performance, isolating the cause of success.
3.  **Human Alignment:** The high Elo score confirms that the model's outputs are not just benchmark-optimized but genuinely useful to humans.

**However, several nuances and limitations temper the conclusions:**
*   **The Reasoning Deficit:** The consistent underperformance on `MMMU` and `MathVista` indicates that while Molmo excels at perception, description, and grounded counting, it has not yet cracked the code on deep multi-step logical reasoning. The current data mix (heavy on captioning and pointing) does not sufficiently stimulate this capability.
*   **Resolution Sensitivity:** The model exhibits fragility regarding image resolution during inference. As noted in **Table 12**, simply increasing the number of crops at test time (from 12 to 36) without fine-tuning causes counting accuracy to collapse (from 85.2% to 73.9% on PixMo-Count). This requires a specific "high-resolution fine-tuning" step to resolve, adding complexity to deployment.
*   **Dependency on Closed LLMs for Curation:** While the *vision* data is native, the pipeline still relies on closed LLMs (like Claude 3.5 and GPT-4o-mini) for cleaning transcripts and generating synthetic QA pairs (Section F). The authors argue this is distinct from VLM distillation (as LLMs can be replaced by open ones like OLMo in the future), but it means the current pipeline is not 100% open-source reproducible *today*.
*   **Text-Only Degradation:** Fine-tuning on multimodal data causes a drop in pure text capabilities (Table 13). For instance, MMLU scores drop from 70.2 (base Qwen2-7B) to 64.6 (Molmo-7B-D). While adding back a small amount of text-only data (10% of Tulu 3) mitigates this, it highlights the inherent tension between specializing for vision and maintaining general language proficiency.

In summary, the experimental analysis provides a compelling blueprint for building open VLMs. It shifts the paradigm from "bigger models + distilled data" to "careful architecture + dense human data," successfully closing the gap with proprietary systems in perception and grounding, while clearly delineating the remaining frontier of complex reasoning.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Molmo and PixMo represent a significant leap forward for open vision-language models, the approach is not without constraints. The success of the system relies on specific architectural assumptions, incurs notable trade-offs in general language capability, and leaves certain high-level reasoning tasks unsolved. Understanding these limitations is critical for researchers attempting to replicate or extend this work.

### 6.1 Assumption: Resolution Consistency Between Training and Inference
A fundamental assumption of the Molmo architecture is that the **number of image crops used at inference must match the number used during training**, particularly for tasks involving counting and fine-grained grounding.

The model learns to associate the sequence length and spatial arrangement of visual tokens with specific tasks. When this distribution shifts, performance degrades sharply.
*   **Evidence:** As shown in **Table 12**, if a model trained with 12 crops is evaluated with 36 crops (a common strategy to boost resolution for other models), its accuracy on the `PixMo-Count` validation set collapses from **85.2%** to **73.9%**. Similarly, `CountBenchQA` drops from 88.5% to 87.7%.
*   **The Trade-off:** This creates a rigidity in deployment. Users cannot simply "zoom in" by increasing crop count at test time to get better results on difficult images unless they perform additional fine-tuning.
*   **The Mitigation Cost:** The authors demonstrate that this can be fixed via a brief "high-resolution fine-tuning" stage (3,000 additional steps), which restores performance to **87.4%** on `PixMo-Count` even with 36 crops. However, this adds an extra step to the training pipeline and implies that a single "universal" weight set may not optimally handle all resolution requirements without adaptation.

### 6.2 Trade-off: Multimodal Specialization vs. Text-Only Proficiency
A known phenomenon in multimodal learning is "catastrophic forgetting" or interference, where training on image-text pairs degrades the model's pure text reasoning capabilities. Molmo is no exception.

*   **The Degradation:** When the Qwen2-7B language model is fine-tuned to become Molmo-7B-D, its performance on standard text-only benchmarks drops significantly.
    *   **MMLU:** Drops from **70.2** (base LLM) to **64.6**.
    *   **MATH:** Plummets from **40.3** to **11.5**.
    *   **HumanEval (Coding):** Falls from **47.6** to **36.6**.
    *   (See **Table 13** for full metrics).
*   **The Cause:** The training mixture consists exclusively of multimodal data (images + text). The model adapts its weights to prioritize visual grounding and description, inadvertently overwriting some of the abstract reasoning patterns learned during pre-training.
*   **The Fix and Its Cost:** The authors show that mixing in text-only data (from the Tulu 3 dataset) can recover these losses. Interestingly, using only **10%** of the text data yields better results on both text benchmarks (MATH recovers to **27.5**) and multimodal benchmarks (11-avg rises to **77.1**) than using 100% of the text data.
*   **Implication:** To deploy Molmo as a general-purpose assistant, one cannot rely solely on the released multimodal weights; a hybrid training mix including text-only data is required to maintain competitive reasoning and coding skills. The "pure" multimodal model is specialized at the expense of generalist text abilities.

### 6.3 The Reasoning Gap: Perception vs. Logic
Despite outperforming proprietary models on perception-heavy tasks (counting, OCR, captioning), Molmo exhibits a clear deficit in **complex, multi-step logical reasoning**.

*   **The Evidence:** On the `MMMU` (Massive Multidiscipline Multimodal Understanding) and `MathVista` benchmarks, which require deep expert knowledge and logical deduction, Molmo-72B scores **54.1** and **58.6**, respectively.
    *   In contrast, **GPT-4o** scores **69.1** and **63.8**.
    *   **Claude 3.5 Sonnet** scores **68.3** and **67.7**.
    *   (Data from **Table 1**).
*   **Analysis:** The gap is not due to an inability to *see* the problem (perception is strong), but an inability to *solve* it. The PixMo data mix is heavily weighted towards description (`PixMo-Cap`), grounding (`PixMo-Points`), and direct QA (`AskModelAnything`). It lacks the dense, chain-of-thought reasoning data that likely powers the logical capabilities of closed models.
*   **Open Question:** This highlights a remaining challenge for the open community: How do we collect high-quality, native *reasoning* data without distilling it from closed models? While Molmo solves the "seeing" problem, the "thinking" problem remains partially dependent on proprietary data sources for state-of-the-art performance.

### 6.4 Dependency on Closed LLMs for Data Curation
While Molmo successfully eliminates dependency on proprietary *Vision-Language Models* (VLMs) for data generation, the pipeline is not yet 100% open-source reproducible due to its reliance on closed *Large Language Models* (LLMs).

*   **The Dependency:**
    *   **PixMo-Cap:** Raw audio transcripts are cleaned and summarized using a language-only LLM.
    *   **PixMo-Docs:** Synthetic documents and charts are generated using code written by **Claude-3.5 Sonnet**.
    *   **PixMo-CapQA:** Question-answer pairs are generated using **GPT-4o-mini**.
*   **The Authors' Defense:** The paper argues (Section H, "Bootstrapping from LLMs") that this is distinct from VLM distillation. Using a closed LLM to process text or generate code is a temporary constraint; as open LLMs (like OLMo) improve, they can be swapped in to recreate the dataset. In contrast, using a closed VLM (like GPT-4V) to generate image captions creates a circular dependency that can never be fully broken without access to the teacher's weights.
*   **The Reality:** Currently, a researcher cannot fully reproduce the PixMo datasets using *only* open tools. The quality of the data is tied to the capabilities of Claude and GPT-4o. If an open LLM of equivalent quality is not available, the reproduction fidelity suffers.

### 6.5 Computational and Data Scale Constraints
The approach demands significant resources, which may limit its accessibility to smaller research groups.

*   **Data Collection Cost:** Collecting **712k** dense captions via human speech and **2.3M** pointing annotations required a massive human-in-the-loop effort. While the "speech-to-text" trick improved efficiency, the cost of coordinating hundreds of annotators and verifying audio receipts is non-trivial and difficult to scale indefinitely compared to simply querying an API.
*   **Training Compute:** Training the largest model, **Molmo-72B**, required **256 H100 GPUs** running for **32.4 hours** just for the fine-tuning stage, plus **128 GPUs** for **33.3 hours** for pre-training (Table 8). The total compute cost runs into tens of thousands of GPU-hours. While this is efficient compared to training from scratch, it remains a high barrier to entry for independent researchers who wish to experiment with different data mixes or architectures at the 72B scale.

### 6.6 Edge Cases in Pointing and Counting
The novel pointing capability, while powerful, has specific boundaries defined by the training data.

*   **Count Limits:** The training pipeline explicitly excludes examples with more than **40 counts** to avoid memory errors from excessively long sequences (Section B.2). Consequently, the model's ability to count large crowds or dense textures is untested and likely to fail or hallucinate beyond this threshold.
*   **Spatial Order Sensitivity:** The model's counting accuracy is highly sensitive to the spatial ordering of points. Training with unordered points reduces accuracy on `PixMo-Count` from **86.3%** to **74.1%** (**Table 4b**). This implies the model relies on the LLM's inherent ability to process sequences in a specific order (top-down, left-to-right) to perform the counting logic. If a user prompt induces a chaotic pointing order, the counting reliability may degrade.

In summary, Molmo proves that open, non-distilled data can build world-class perception models, but it trades off some general reasoning ability, requires careful management of resolution constraints, and currently leans on closed LLMs for data curation. These are not fatal flaws, but rather clear signposts for the next generation of open research: improving open LLMs for data synthesis, developing better reasoning datasets, and creating more resolution-robust architectures.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The release of Molmo and PixMo represents a paradigm shift in how the research community approaches the development of Vision-Language Models (VLMs). By demonstrating that state-of-the-art performance can be achieved without distilling proprietary models, this work fundamentally alters the landscape of open AI, moving the field from a reliance on "black box" teachers to a focus on rigorous, human-centric data engineering.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Open-Source Landscape
**Breaking the Distillation Dependency:**
Prior to this work, the prevailing assumption was that high-performance open models *required* synthetic data generated by closed systems (e.g., GPT-4V) to compete. Molmo disproves this hypothesis. The success of **PixMo-Cap** (human-spoken captions) and **PixMo-Points** (human-clicked grounding) proves that **native data quality** supersedes **synthetic scale**.
*   **Impact:** This liberates the community from the "distillation trap." Researchers no longer need to wait for proprietary APIs to generate training data or fear that their models will inherit the specific biases and blind spots of a teacher model. It re-establishes **data collection methodology** as a primary research vector, equal in importance to architecture design.

**Restoring Scientific Reproducibility:**
By releasing weights, code, and the full dataset (including audio receipts), Molmo sets a new standard for transparency.
*   **Impact:** Unlike "open-weight" models trained on undisclosed or synthetic data, Molmo allows for true ablation studies. Researchers can now isolate exactly how specific data types (e.g., dense captions vs. pointing) affect specific capabilities (e.g., OCR vs. counting). This enables a causal understanding of model behavior that was previously impossible with distilled models.

### 7.2 Enabling New Research Frontiers
The specific innovations in Molmo open several distinct avenues for follow-up research:

**1. The "Pointing Agent" Paradigm:**
Molmo demonstrates that 2D pointing is not just a visualization tool but a robust mechanism for **grounded reasoning** and **counting**.
*   **Future Direction:** This paves the way for VLMs that act as autonomous agents in digital and physical environments. Instead of outputting abstract text commands, future agents could output sequences of coordinates to:
    *   Click buttons in a GUI (web automation).
    *   Identify grasp points for robotic arms.
    *   Navigate maps by selecting waypoints.
    *   *Hypothesis:* The "point-then-count" chain-of-thought could be extended to "point-then-reason," where models must visually verify every step of a logical deduction by grounding it in pixel space.

**2. Modality-Switching for Data Collection:**
The "speech-to-text" trick used for PixMo-Cap solves the bottleneck of obtaining long-form, detailed annotations.
*   **Future Direction:** This methodology can be applied to other domains requiring dense description, such as video captioning, medical imaging reports, or scientific diagram explanation. Researchers can now collect hours of detailed verbal description rather than minutes of typed text, potentially unlocking higher-fidelity training data for video-language models.

**3. Native Reasoning Data Collection:**
While Molmo excels at perception, it lags in complex reasoning (MMMU, MathVista).
*   **Future Direction:** The community must now apply Molmo's data-centric philosophy to **reasoning**. Just as Molmo avoided VLM distillation for captions, future work should focus on collecting native human chain-of-thought reasoning data for visual problems, rather than relying on closed models to generate "reasoning traces."

**4. Resolution-Robust Architectures:**
Molmo's sensitivity to crop counts (performance drops if test crops $\neq$ train crops) highlights a limitation in current tiling strategies.
*   **Future Direction:** Research into dynamic resolution adapters or position embeddings that are invariant to the number of crops could yield models that seamlessly zoom in/out at inference time without requiring fine-tuning.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases
Molmo's specific strengths make it uniquely suited for several real-world applications where transparency and grounding are critical:

*   **Automated Visual Inspection & Counting:**
    Due to its state-of-the-art counting capabilities (via pointing), Molmo is ideal for industrial or logistical tasks requiring accurate inventory counts (e.g., counting cells in microscopy, vehicles in traffic, or items on a shelf). The model's ability to output the specific locations of counted items provides an audit trail that pure text models cannot offer.
*   **Accessibility Tools for the Visually Impaired:**
    The dense, 200+ word captions generated by Molmo provide a level of detail far superior to standard alt-text generators. This allows for rich, narrative descriptions of complex scenes, enhancing screen readers and assistive technologies.
*   **Document Intelligence with Provenance:**
    In legal or financial domains, knowing *where* information was extracted is as important as the extraction itself. Molmo's ability to point to specific cells in a table or lines in a contract while answering questions offers built-in explainability, reducing the risk of hallucinated figures.
*   **Educational Tutors:**
    The model's strength in interpreting diagrams (AI2D) and charts, combined with its ability to point to specific elements while explaining them, makes it a powerful tool for STEM education, allowing it to act as a tutor that can "show and tell."

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance
For practitioners looking to adopt or extend this work, the following guidelines clarify when and how to use Molmo:

**When to Prefer Molmo:**
*   **Requirement for Grounding:** If your application requires the model to indicate *where* in the image it found the answer (e.g., bounding boxes, points, or segmentation hints), Molmo is currently the strongest open choice.
*   **Counting Tasks:** For any task involving enumerating objects, Molmo's "point-then-count" mechanism significantly outperforms standard VLMs.
*   **Data Sovereignty:** If your organization cannot rely on proprietary APIs for data generation or inference due to privacy or regulatory constraints, Molmo offers a fully auditable, self-hostable alternative that does not depend on closed-source teachers.
*   **Detailed Description:** When applications require long-form, nuanced image descriptions rather than short labels.

**When to Consider Alternatives:**
*   **Deep Logical Reasoning:** If the primary task involves complex multi-step math or expert-level logical deduction (e.g., solving novel physics problems), proprietary models like GPT-4o or Claude 3.5 Sonnet currently hold a significant advantage.
*   **Pure Text Performance:** If the model will be used in a mixed modality setting where pure text reasoning (coding, logic puzzles without images) is paramount, be aware that Molmo's multimodal fine-tuning degrades these skills. **Integration Tip:** As shown in **Table 13**, you should mix in approximately **10%** of high-quality text-only instruction data (e.g., from Tulu 3) during fine-tuning to recover text capabilities without sacrificing visual performance.

**Deployment Considerations:**
*   **Resolution Matching:** When deploying Molmo, ensure the number of image crops used at inference matches the training configuration (typically 12 crops) for counting and grounding tasks. If higher resolution is needed, perform a brief **high-resolution fine-tuning** stage (as described in Section D) rather than simply increasing crops at test time, which causes performance collapse.
*   **Output Parsing:** Since Molmo outputs points as plain-text coordinates (e.g., `<point x="..." y="...">`), ensure your downstream pipeline includes robust regex or XML parsers to extract these coordinates for visualization or agent actions.

In conclusion, Molmo and PixMo do not just provide a new model; they provide a **blueprint**. They demonstrate that the path to closing the gap with proprietary AI lies not in mimicking their outputs, but in innovating how we collect and structure human knowledge. This shifts the burden of progress back to the open community, empowering researchers to build the next generation of AI on a foundation of transparency and native understanding.
