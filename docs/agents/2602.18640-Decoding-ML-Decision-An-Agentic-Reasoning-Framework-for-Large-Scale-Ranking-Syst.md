## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces GEARS (Generative Engine for Agentic Ranking Systems), a framework that solves the "engineering context constraint" in large-scale ranking by reframing optimization as an autonomous discovery process using Specialized Agent Skills and Deterministic Lifecycle Governance. By translating high-level product intent into executable hypotheses while filtering brittle policies, GEARS achieves a Top-1 Accuracy of $0.86$ in offline policy selection, significantly outperforming the $0.68$ accuracy of the best baseline (Table 1). Validated across $20$ internal experiments (Section 4.1), the framework delivers consistent real-world metric improvements, such as a $0.37\%$ lift on Surface 8, while ensuring production stability through rigorous feature stability audits (Table 2, Table 3).

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of GEARS, we must first understand the specific bottleneck it targets. In modern large-scale ranking systems, the primary challenge is no longer purely algorithmic—it is operational. This section dissects the "engineering context constraint," explains why traditional machine learning approaches fail to address it, and positions GEARS within the landscape of prior work.

### The Engineering Context Constraint

Modern ranking systems operate at a scale that defies manual optimization. As described in Section 1, these systems orchestrate heterogeneous product surfaces for a **billion-scale global user base**. The architecture is intricate, involving numerous optimization layers that concurrently target diverse and often conflicting metrics (e.g., maximizing engagement while minimizing negative feedback).

Historically, progress in this domain was limited by **signal estimation**—the ability of a model to accurately predict user behavior. However, the paper argues that the bottleneck has shifted. The new constraint is the **engineering context constraint**: the arduous process of translating ambiguous product intent into reasonable, executable, and verifiable hypotheses.

Consider the workflow of a human operator today:
1.  **Intent:** A product manager wants to "increase long-term engagement without hurting community health."
2.  **Translation:** An engineer must translate this vague intent into specific feature thresholds, cohort definitions, and weightings.
3.  **Verification:** The hypothesis must be checked against infrastructure constraints (e.g., is the feature stable?) and business guardrails.

This manual dependency creates a **scalability barrier**. The combinatorial complexity of multi-objective trade-offs means that high-value policies often remain undiscovered within the search space. Furthermore, human translation is slow and prone to error, leaving the system suboptimal.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches

To solve this, one might turn to existing optimization techniques. However, the paper identifies critical gaps in three main categories of prior work: traditional uplift modeling, adaptive experimentation, and generic LLM agents.

#### 1. Uplift Modeling and Heterogeneous Treatment Effects (HTE)
Uplift modeling aims to identify user segments most responsive to specific interventions. As detailed in Section 2, these approaches fall into three categories:
*   **Meta-learner methods:** Such as S-learner and T-learner, which use existing prediction models to estimate Individual Treatment Effects (ITE).
*   **Tree-based methods:** Which partition the user population into subgroups based on sensitivity to treatments.
*   **Neural network-based methods:** Which leverage deep learning to model complex user responses.

**The Gap:** While these methods excel at minimizing prediction error, they are "blind" to the engineering context (Section 3). A standard HTE model might identify a statistically optimal cohort based on a transient feature, unaware that the feature is deprecated or unstable. They treat personalization as a **static model selection task**, ignoring operational constraints like feature stability or cannibalization across products. Consequently, optimal policies identified offline are often brittle or undeployable in production environments.

#### 2. Adaptive Experimentation (AE)
Adaptive Experimentation focuses on optimizing outcomes through sequential decision-making, using methodologies like Multi-Armed Bandits and Bayesian Optimization (Section 2). AE is designed to balance exploration and exploitation to identify optimal configurations dynamically.

**The Gap:** While AE automates parameter tuning, it does not solve the **hypothesis generation** problem. It assumes the space of interventions is well-defined. In ranking systems, the challenge is often defining *what* to test based on high-level intent, not just tuning the weights of an existing test. AE lacks the reasoning capability to interpret complex business "vibes" or navigate non-differentiable business constraints.

#### 3. Generic LLM Agents
Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) have enabled agents capable of tool use and reasoning (Section 2). Techniques like Tool-Integrated Reasoning (TIR) and self-refinement mechanisms (e.g., Self-Refine, Reflexion) allow models to interact with environments.

**The Gap:** Generic "LLM-as-Ranker" agents suffer from **hallucination** and **context rot** (Section 3).
*   **Hallucination:** The agent may generate policies that are semantically plausible but operationally brittle (e.g., referencing a feature that does not exist).
*   **Context Rot:** As the reasoning chain lengthens, the model loses fidelity to the original constraints or infrastructure rules.
Without specialized grounding, these agents cannot be trusted with production-level ranking decisions where stability is paramount.

### Positioning GEARS: Bridging the Deployment Gap

GEARS positions itself not as a replacement for ranking models, but as a **framework for autonomous discovery** that sits above the modeling layer. It addresses the **Deployment Gap**—the disconnect between offline optimality and online deployability.

The framework reframes ranking optimization from a one-shot inference task into an **autonomous discovery loop** (Section 3). This positioning is defined by three strategic shifts relative to prior work:

1.  **From Static Selection to Dynamic Discovery:**
    Unlike uplift modeling which selects a static policy, GEARS treats the experimentation ecosystem as an interactive environment. Agents navigate through executable operations to generate hypotheses and evaluate feasibility in real-time. This is visualized in **Figure 1**, which shows the end-to-end workflow from experiment link to auto-generated ranking configuration.

2.  **From Low-Level Tuning to Vibe Optimization:**
    Traditional systems require operators to tune low-level parameters. GEARS introduces **Vibe Optimization**, allowing operators to steer systems via high-level intent (e.g., "prioritize long-term engagement"). The system translates this qualitative intuition into explicit algorithmic constraints using **Specialized Agent Skills**. These skills encapsulate expert ranking knowledge into reusable reasoning capabilities, bridging the semantic gap between human intent and numerical objectives.

3.  **From Point-in-Time Metrics to Deterministic Governance:**
    To counter the instability of generic agents, GEARS incorporates **Deterministic Lifecycle Governance**. Rather than relying solely on aggregate signals which may overfit short-term noise, the framework registers validation hooks around every executable action. These hooks audit feature stability and statistical robustness over a **6-month window** (Section 3.3). This ensures that recommended configurations are durable, filtering out policies that rely on unstable feature distributions.

By integrating these components, GEARS moves beyond the limitations of pure algorithmic optimization. It acknowledges that in large-scale ranking systems, a policy is only "optimal" if it is stable, interpretable, and aligned with evolving business criteria. This holistic approach allows the system to discover near-Pareto-efficient policies that traditional methods would miss due to their inability to process engineering context.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems paper that proposes an agentic framework for autonomous ranking optimization, where the core idea is to replace manual hypothesis generation with an autonomous discovery loop that combines algorithmic policy search with expert knowledge encoding and production safety validation.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

GEARS is an autonomous agent system that sits on top of existing ranking infrastructure, taking high-level product goals as input and outputting production-ready ranking configurations after navigating a searchable policy space. The system solves the problem of translating vague business intent into deployable ranking policies by combining algorithmic candidate generation with expert knowledge modules and stability validation hooks that filter out brittle solutions before they reach production.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The GEARS framework consists of three major components arranged in a sequential pipeline with feedback loops. First, the **Intent-Conditioned Personalization Module** receives natural language product intent and generates a high-recall set of candidate policies using the GAS (Generative Agentic Search) algorithm, which performs heterogeneous treatment effect estimation across user segments. Second, the **Insight-Driven Policy Selection Module** takes these candidates and applies Specialized Agent Skills—modular, filesystem-based resources encoding expert ranking knowledge—to evaluate trade-offs, interpret features, and rank policies by deployment feasibility. Third, the **Deterministic Lifecycle Governance Module** registers validation hooks around every executable action to audit feature stability and statistical robustness over a 6-month window, rejecting policies that fail stability thresholds before they are recommended. Information flows from intent specification through candidate generation, then through skill-based reasoning, and finally through governance validation, with refinement loops triggering if any stage rejects a candidate policy.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with **Intent-Conditioned Personalization** because it defines the input space and candidate generation mechanism that all downstream components operate on.
- We then examine **Tolerance-Based Frontier Expansion** because it explains how GEARS improves upon standard Pareto optimization to capture operationally valuable non-convex policies.
- We proceed to **Specialized Agent Skills** because they represent the core innovation that enables the agent to reason with domain expertise rather than generic prompting.
- We analyze **Progressive Disclosure Strategy** because it addresses the critical engineering challenge of context management in long-horizon agent reasoning.
- We conclude with **Deterministic Lifecycle Governance** because it provides the production safety guarantees that distinguish GEARS from generic LLM agents.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Intent-Conditioned Personalization and Candidate Generation

The first stage of GEARS transforms ambiguous product intent into a structured search specification that can be systematically explored. When an operator provides a natural-language intent describing desired trade-offs, such as "prioritize long-term engagement while maintaining guardrails," the system constructs a search specification consisting of objectives, cohort constraints, and experiment parameters. This specification is not the final policy but rather a high-recall set of candidate policies that will be interpreted and validated by downstream modules. The candidate generation leverages GAS (Generative Agentic Search), a large-scale Heterogeneous Treatment Effect framework designed to match optimal interventions to specific user segments across conflicting objectives.

GAS operates by defining candidate policies through quantile-based cohort segmentation over user features. For a feature $X$, the system defines $N$ segments using quantile boundaries:

$$S^{\text{ind}}_i(X) = \left\{ u \mid Q\left(X, \frac{i-1}{N}\right) < X(u) \leq Q\left(X, \frac{i}{N}\right) \right\}$$

where $Q(X, p)$ denotes the $100p$-th percentile of feature $X$, and $u$ represents a user. This creates a combinatorial space of candidate policies across cohorts and treatments, where each policy is a mapping from segments to actions represented as a set of (segment, action) pairs. The system then identifies Pareto-optimal policies that jointly optimize multiple metrics using a weight-search procedure that defines a family of linear scalarized rewards over $K$ metrics using weight vectors $w \in \mathbb{R}^K$.

For each segment $S_b$ with associated action $a_b$, GAS evaluates the weighted average metric lift across users in that segment and selects policies by maximizing this scalarized objective. The resulting Pareto policy set is defined as:

$$\mathcal{P}_{\text{pareto}} := \left\{ \arg\max_{a} \sum_{b=1}^{B} \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k \frac{1}{|S_b|} \sum_{u_i \in S_b} \delta_k(u_i, a_b) \;\middle|\; w \in \mathcal{W} \right\}$$

where $\delta_k(u_i, a_b)$ represents the observed outcome of user $u_i$ under action $a_b$ on metric $k$, and $\mathcal{W}$ is the set of weight vectors sampled from the simplex. This mathematical formulation allows the system to identify policies representing different trade-offs rather than a single global optimum, which is critical when objectives conflict.

#### Tolerance-Based Frontier Expansion

Standard Pareto optimization has a critical limitation that GEARS addresses through tolerance-based frontier expansion. Conventional approaches using random weight search tend to emphasize convex regions of the trade-off space, potentially overlooking non-convex or near-optimal policies that may offer better stability or operational robustness. In real ranking systems, desirable operating points may lie in non-convex regions or may be intentionally chosen to balance stability, risk, or business constraints rather than purely maximizing metrics. GEARS introduces a tolerance-based Pareto filtering mechanism that admits both optimal and near-optimal candidates by defining a tolerance band proportional to uncertainty for each metric.

The tolerance-based filtering works through a two-stage algorithm outlined in Appendix C. In Step 1, the algorithm performs candidate collection via random-weight search by sampling $W$ weight vectors from the simplex $\Delta^{M-1}$ and retaining the Top-$K$ policies for each weight configuration. In Step 2, it applies tolerance-based Pareto filtering where instead of strict Pareto dominance, the algorithm defines a per-metric tolerance margin proportional to the estimate's uncertainty. A candidate policy $p$ is only discarded if it is strictly dominated by another policy $q$ beyond this tolerance threshold, where the tolerance margin is defined as:

$$\epsilon_m(p) \triangleq \tau \cdot \sigma_m(p)$$

where $\tau \geq 0$ is a tolerance hyperparameter and $\sigma_m(p)$ represents the uncertainty estimate for metric $m$ under policy $p$. The tolerance-dominance relation $q \succ_\tau p$ (for maximization) is defined as:

$$\left( \forall m, \mu_m(q) \geq \mu_m(p) - \epsilon_m(p) \right) \land \left( \exists m, \mu_m(q) > \mu_m(p) + \epsilon_m(p) \right)$$

where $\mu_m(p)$ is the estimated mean for metric $m$ under policy $p$. This approach effectively expands the candidate set to include valuable non-convex or near-frontier policies that might offer better real-world stability, as visualized in Figure 2 where tolerance-based expansion allows GEARS to surface both convex and near-frontier candidates.

#### Specialized Agent Skills Architecture

The second major component addresses the challenge that candidate generation algorithms identify policies that are near Pareto-efficient in offline metrics, but these candidates are frequently undeployable in production systems due to unobserved factors such as feature instability, infrastructure constraints, or misalignment with specific business guardrails. GEARS introduces an Insight Generator powered by Specialized Agent Skills, which are modular, filesystem-based resources that encode engineering expertise into executable decision procedures. Unlike conventional static prompt templates, these skills are structured as comprehensive tools comprising three components: lightweight metadata for efficient agent routing, structured step-by-step analytical instructions, and direct references to internal artifacts such as SQL scripts and codebases.

Each skill encapsulates expert ranking knowledge into reusable computational capabilities, enabling the agent to perform advanced analyses such as feature explanation, feature stability audits, and guardrail interpretation. The skills integrate structured instructions, operational procedures, and access to domain artifacts, allowing agents to autonomously execute complex, domain-specific tasks such as trade-off diagnosis and feature interpretation. This architecture enables what the paper terms Vibe Optimization, where operators guide ranking systems through high-level intent rather than low-level parameter tuning. By translating qualitative intuition into explicit algorithmic constraints, GEARS enables agents to autonomously generate policies that are interpretable, stable, and aligned with product goals.

To ground the agent's reasoning in platform-specific context, GEARS integrates a Domain Knowledge Brain, which is a curated reasoning backbone that supplies the LLM with high-fidelity historical data and domain expert learnings. This Knowledge Brain systematizes innovation by providing the critical contextual evidence behind past experiments, ensuring that the agent's policy recommendations are empirically valid and deeply integrated with complex system dynamics. Without this grounding, the execution of skills would remain limited because the agent would lack the platform-specific context needed to make valid deployment decisions.

#### Progressive Disclosure Strategy for Context Management

A critical engineering challenge in agentic systems is preventing context rot, which is the performance degradation LLMs experience with overly long prompts as the reasoning chain lengthens. GEARS employs a progressive disclosure strategy where information is dynamically loaded in three distinct stages to maintain a clean context window and prevent reasoning drift during long-horizon policy exploration. First, compact metadata is exposed for skill retrieval, allowing the agent to identify which skills are relevant without loading their full content. Second, structured instructions are injected only upon skill activation, ensuring that detailed procedural knowledge is only present in context when actively being used. Third, specific internal scripts are accessed purely during the execution phase, with subtasks delegated to subagents rather than keeping all artifacts in the main context window.

This staged design maintains a clean context window, externalizes expertise efficiently, and prevents reasoning drift during long-horizon policy exploration. The design choice to use progressive disclosure rather than loading all context upfront addresses a fundamental limitation of generic LLM agents that suffer from context rot when handling complex, multi-step reasoning tasks. By externalizing expertise into modular skills and loading them on-demand, GEARS can handle longer reasoning chains without the fidelity degradation that would occur if all knowledge were loaded into a single monolithic prompt.

#### Deterministic Lifecycle Governance

The third major component addresses the challenge that granting autonomy to agents in production environments introduces risks regarding long-term policy stability. In real-world systems, policies that appear statistically strong in short-term experiments often fail to generalize due to temporal shifts, sampling noise, or reliance on unstable feature distributions. Aggregate signals may produce apparent gains within limited observation windows yet degrade once exposed to broader cohorts or longer-term dynamics. GEARS introduces a Deterministic Lifecycle Governance mechanism that enforces long-term stability throughout the agent's decision process by systematically validating every intermediate and final decision against reproducible stability criteria.

The governance layer evaluates proposed actions using deterministic checks that assess statistical reliability, feature consistency, and robustness over a 6-month window. These checks ensure that metric improvements are not driven by noisy or transient features, and that policies maintain performance across temporal slices. If a candidate fails to meet these invariants, the system rejects the policy and provides structured feedback to the agent, triggering a refinement loop. This stability-first design grounds agent-driven exploration in long-term reliability rather than short-term gains.

To operationalize stability validation, GEARS establishes a quantitative benchmark for checking feature stability using the User-Cohort Shift Ratio ($R_{\text{shift}}$), defined as the percentage of users who migrate from their assigned cohort (bucket) to a different one over a 6-month window. The system benchmarked a perceived stable feature set $S$ using two cohort definitions: Quantile Cuts (4 equal buckets) and Binary Cuts (p25/p75 thresholds). As shown in Table 2, feature set $S$ shifted by 6% over 6 months under quantile cuts and 2% under binary cuts, establishing a lower bound for unavoidable natural drift. Based on these benchmarks, GEARS implements a validation hook with the following pre-search filter thresholds: features must exhibit $R_{\text{shift}} \leq 15\%$ (Binary) or $\leq 45\%$ (Quantile) to enter the search space.

This benchmarking process allows GEARS to automatically disqualify high-lift but unstable features that baseline methods would have erroneously selected. For example, Product Feature 4 showed high volatility with approximately 50% shift under quantile cuts, making it unsuitable for long-term policy targeting despite potentially strong offline metrics. The validation hooks register around every executable action to audit policy robustness, evaluating feature stability, cohort consistency, and performance persistence rather than relying on point-in-time metrics. By filtering policies that overfit transient signals, GEARS ensures that recommended configurations are durable and deployable.

#### Integration and Workflow Orchestration

The three components integrate into a cohesive autonomous discovery loop as illustrated in Figure 1. The workflow begins when an operator provides an experiment link and high-level intent, which flows into the Intent-Conditioned Personalization module for candidate generation. The GAS agent then searches over candidate policies to generate insights about trade-offs between topline metrics. These candidates flow into the Insight-Driven Policy Selection module where Specialized Agent Skills perform feature understanding, validation, and interpretability checks. Finally, candidates pass through Deterministic Lifecycle Governance where stability and guardrail compliance are verified before an auto-generated iterated ranking configuration is produced.

This workflow transforms ranking from a manual, disjointed process into a cohesive autonomous discovery loop. The system takes high-level product intent as input and navigates a programmable environment to output a production-ready configuration. The integration of these three components—intent translation, policy selection with skills, and deterministic governance—addresses the deployment gap that pure algorithmic optimization cannot solve. Unlike baselines that only solve for performance, GEARS explicitly models the process of experimentation, utilizing deterministic governance to filter out high-risk policies that purely statistical methods would erroneously promote.

The design choice to make this a framework rather than a single model is deliberate. GEARS is designed as a general framework for ranking optimization, with personalization serving as an illustrative application where the policy space is combinatorial and deployment feasibility depends on stability and infrastructure constraints beyond offline optimality. This modular architecture allows the framework to be adapted to different product surfaces while maintaining the core innovations of skill-based reasoning and deterministic governance.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

GEARS represents a paradigm shift in ranking optimization, moving from static model selection to an autonomous, agentic discovery process. While the technical components (Skills, Governance, GAS) are described in Section 3, their true innovation lies in how they fundamentally alter the trade-off between optimality and deployability. This section isolates the three most critical insights that distinguish GEARS from prior work in uplift modeling and generic agent systems, supported by empirical evidence from the paper's evaluation.

### 4.1 Externalizing Expertise: Skills vs. Prompt Engineering

A common misconception in applying LLMs to engineering tasks is that sufficient context in a prompt can replicate expert reasoning. GEARS demonstrates that this is insufficient for high-stakes ranking decisions. The innovation here is not merely using an LLM, but **externalizing expert knowledge into executable Specialized Agent Skills** (Section 3.2).

Unlike standard prompt engineering, which relies on the model's internal weights and static text instructions, GEARS skills are modular, filesystem-based resources that grant the agent access to internal artifacts (SQL scripts, codebases) and structured analytical procedures. This design choice addresses the "context rot" problem identified in Section 2, where long reasoning chains degrade model performance.

The significance of this architecture is quantified in the ablation study in **Table 1**. When the Skill module is removed (`GEARS w/o Skill`), the Top-1 Accuracy drops from $0.86$ to $0.77$. While this might appear modest compared to other components, it represents a **10.5% relative degradation** in the model's ability to select the optimal policy solely due to the lack of structured expertise. More importantly, the Skills enable **interpretability**: the agent does not just output a policy ID, but provides the rationale (e.g., feature stability audits) that justifies the selection. This transforms the system from a "black box" optimizer into a collaborative partner that can explain *why* a policy was chosen, a capability absent in traditional uplift modeling (Section 2).

### 4.2 Uncertainty-Aware Optimization: Beyond Strict Pareto Dominance

Traditional multi-objective optimization relies on strict Pareto dominance, where a policy is discarded if another policy performs better on all metrics. While mathematically sound, this approach is brittle in production environments where metric estimates have noise. GEARS introduces **Tolerance-Based Frontier Expansion** (Appendix C, Algorithm 1), which modifies the dominance condition to account for uncertainty.

The core insight is that a policy appearing slightly inferior might actually be superior when measurement noise is considered. GEARS defines a tolerance margin $\epsilon_m(p)$ for each metric $m$:

$$\epsilon_m(p) \triangleq \tau \cdot \sigma_m(p)$$

where $\sigma_m(p)$ is the uncertainty estimate and $\tau$ is a hyperparameter. A policy is only dominated if it exceeds this tolerance threshold. This allows the system to retain **near-frontier candidates** that lie in non-convex regions of the trade-off space (Figure 2).

**Why this matters:** In ranking systems, strictly optimal policies often rely on unstable features that maximize short-term metrics but fail over time. By expanding the frontier to include robust, near-optimal policies, GEARS increases the surface area for finding configurations that satisfy both performance and stability constraints. This is a theoretical refinement with practical consequences: it prevents the system from discarding a stable policy simply because it is marginally worse on a noisy metric than an unstable alternative.

### 4.3 Governance as a Signal Enhancer, Not Just a Safety Filter

Perhaps the most counter-intuitive finding in this paper is that **Deterministic Lifecycle Governance** improves selection accuracy, not just safety. One might assume that adding constraints (validation hooks) would restrict the search space and reduce performance. However, the ablation results in **Table 1** show that removing the bash-based filtering stage (`GEARS w/o Bash`) causes a catastrophic drop in Top-1 Accuracy from $0.86$ to $0.26$.

This indicates that the governance layer acts as a **signal enhancer**. By filtering out policies that rely on unstable features early in the process, the agent avoids being distracted by "false positive" lifts driven by noise. The paper benchmarks this using the User-Cohort Shift Ratio ($R_{\text{shift}}$) over a 6-month window (Table 2). For example, **Feature 4** showed a $\sim 50\%$ shift under quantile cuts, marking it as unstable. A standard optimizer might select a policy leveraging Feature 4 for its high offline lift, but GEARS rejects it via the pre-search filter ($R_{\text{shift}} \leq 45\%$ for Quantile).

**Significance:** This reframes governance from a compliance hurdle to a core optimization component. It ensures that the "optimal" policy found by the agent is durable. The backtest results in **Figure 4** confirm this: the selected policy maintains consistent metric improvement over one month, validating that the governance hooks successfully filtered out transient signals. This is a fundamental departure from prior work (Section 2), where stability checks are often post-hoc or manual.

### 4.4 Fundamental vs. Incremental Contributions

It is important to distinguish which aspects of GEARS are fundamental innovations versus incremental improvements:

*   **Fundamental Innovation:** The **Agentic Discovery Loop** itself. Reframing ranking optimization as an interactive process (Figure 1) rather than a static prediction task is a architectural shift. It enables the system to handle the "engineering context constraint" (Section 2) that pure models cannot solve.
*   **Fundamental Innovation:** **Deterministic Lifecycle Governance**. Integrating stability checks directly into the agent's decision loop (rather than as a separate validation phase) is a novel system design pattern for production ML.
*   **Incremental Improvement:** **Tolerance-Based Frontier Expansion**. While valuable, this builds upon existing Pareto optimization literature. The novelty lies in its specific application to ranking stability rather than the mathematical formulation itself.
*   **Incremental Improvement:** **Specialized Agent Skills**. While the implementation (filesystem-based, progressive disclosure) is novel, the concept of tool-augmented LLMs exists in prior work (Section 2). GEARS advances this by tailoring skills specifically for ranking infrastructure (SQL, feature audits).

In summary, GEARS does not merely improve metric lift; it solves the **deployability problem**. The $0.37\%$ lift on Surface 8 (Table 3) is significant, but the greater achievement is achieving this lift while maintaining guardrail compliance and stability over a 6-month window. This demonstrates that in large-scale ranking systems, **robustness is a prerequisite for optimality**, not a secondary constraint.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

To validate the claims made in the technical approach, the authors conduct a multi-layered evaluation strategy. This section dissects the experimental design, analyzing how the dataset was constructed, why specific baselines were chosen, and what the quantitative results reveal about the system's capabilities. We will walk through the offline policy selection benchmarks, the stability governance tests, and the real-world deployment metrics to understand not just *that* GEARS works, but *under what conditions* it succeeds.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Dataset Construction

A common pitfall in agentic system research is evaluating on toy datasets that do not reflect production complexity. To avoid this, the authors construct a benchmark derived from **20 internal experiments** at Meta (Section 4.1). This is not a static public dataset; it is a dynamic collection of real-world ranking experiments, ensuring the evaluation captures the noise and complexity of live systems.

#### Dataset Generation Process
The dataset creation process is rigorous and involves three steps:
1.  **Candidate Generation:** For each of the 20 experiments, the GAS algorithm (described in Section 3.4) generates hundreds of policy candidates along with their metric measurements and confidence intervals.
2.  **Instruction Synthesis:** For each experiment, the authors automatically synthesize **5 distinct instruction types** representing common policy selection scenarios faced by engineers:
    *   `Maximize Both`: Jointly optimize two metrics.
    *   `Maximize with Constraint`: Optimize a primary metric while ensuring a secondary metric does not regress.
    *   `Tradeoff Analysis`: Identify Pareto-optimal policies.
    *   `Efficiency Optimization`: Select policies with the highest composite efficiency score.
    *   `Single Metric`: Maximize a single target metric.
3.  **Ground Truth Definition:** For each of the $20 \times 5 = 100$ instructions, the ground truth is defined as the **top-5 policies** according to the specified optimization criteria. This allows the evaluation to measure not just exact matching, but ranking quality.

This setup yields a total of **100 instructions** used to evaluate performance in real-world settings. By defining ground truth as a set of top-5 policies rather than a single winner, the evaluation acknowledges that multiple valid solutions may exist in a multi-objective space.

#### Baselines and Metrics
To assess effectiveness, GEARS is benchmarked against five state-of-the-art prompting strategies (Section 4.1):
*   **Naive Prompting:** Direct query without reasoning guidance.
*   **Chain-of-Thought (CoT):** Encourages step-by-step reasoning.
*   **Self-Consistency:** Samples 5 responses with temperature $T=0.7$ and aggregates via Borda count voting.
*   **Self-Refine:** Generates initial recommendations, then critiques and refines them (1 iteration).
*   **Code-as-Action:** The LLM generates and executes code to solve the task, making outputs verifiable.

Performance is measured using standard information retrieval and decision-oriented metrics (Section 4.1):
*   **Precision@K** and **Recall@K**: Measure coverage and accuracy within the top-$K$ recommendations.
*   **NDCG@K** (Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain): Evaluates ranking quality, penalizing relevant policies found lower in the list:
    $$ \text{NDCG@K} = \frac{\text{DCG@K}}{\text{IDCG@K}}, \quad \text{DCG@K} = \sum_{i=1}^{K} \frac{2^{rel_i} - 1}{\log_2(i + 1)} $$
    where $rel_i \in \{0, 1\}$ indicates if the policy at position $i$ is in the ground-truth set.
*   **Top-1 Accuracy**: Whether the highest-ranked prediction exactly matches the best ground-truth policy.
*   **Ranking Correlation**: Spearman's $\rho$, capturing global ordering fidelity.

All experiments use **Claude Sonnet (ant)** as the backbone LLM. This specific model choice is critical; results may vary with different model families, a limitation we will revisit in the critical assessment.

### 5.2 Offline Policy Selection Performance

The core claim of GEARS is that it can select superior policies under multi-metric constraints compared to generic reasoning strategies. The results in **Table 1** provide a quantitative breakdown of this claim across five key dimensions.

#### Main Results Analysis
GEARS achieves a **Top-1 Accuracy of $0.86$**, significantly outperforming the strongest baseline, **Code-as-Action**, which achieves $0.68$. This $18$ percentage point gap is substantial in a production setting where selecting the wrong policy can lead to metric regression.

Looking deeper into the ranking quality metrics:
*   **NDCG@1**: GEARS scores **$0.94$** vs. Code-as-Action's **$0.77$**. This indicates that GEARS is not just guessing the right policy occasionally; it is consistently ranking the optimal policy at the very top.
*   **Recall@5**: GEARS achieves **$0.95$**, meaning it successfully includes valid ground-truth policies in its top-5 recommendations $95\%$ of the time. In contrast, Naive Prompting only reaches **$0.77$**.

The performance gap widens when comparing GEARS to **Naive Prompting** (Top-1 Acc: $0.44$) and **Self-Consistency** (Top-1 Acc: $0.37$). This suggests that simple sampling or direct querying is insufficient for the combinatorial complexity of ranking policies. Even **Self-Refine**, which attempts to correct its own errors, only reaches $0.61$ Top-1 Accuracy, indicating that self-critique alone cannot compensate for the lack of domain-specific tools.

#### Ablation Studies: Isolating Component Contribution
The most revealing data in **Table 1** comes from the ablation studies, where components of GEARS are removed to measure their individual impact.

1.  **Impact of Governance (`GEARS w/o Bash`):**
    When the bash-based filtering stage (Deterministic Lifecycle Governance) is removed, Top-1 Accuracy collapses from **$0.86$ to $0.26$**.
    *   **Interpretation:** This is a catastrophic drop. It implies that the LLM reasoning alone (even with Skills) is prone to selecting policies that *look* good offline but fail deterministic checks. The governance layer is not just a safety net; it is the primary filter preventing the agent from choosing "false positive" policies. This validates the claim in Section 4.3 that governance acts as a signal enhancer.

2.  **Impact of Skills (`GEARS w/o Skill`):**
    When the Specialized Agent Skills module is removed, Top-1 Accuracy drops from **$0.86$ to $0.77$**.
    *   **Interpretation:** While less severe than the governance drop, this $10.5\%$ relative degradation confirms that encoding expert knowledge into reusable skills provides significant value beyond simple filtering. The skills help the agent interpret *why* a policy is good, improving the ranking quality (NDCG@1 drops from $0.94$ to $0.87$).

These ablations demonstrate that GEARS is not a monolithic model improvement but a system where **reasoning (Skills)** and **verification (Governance)** are interdependent. Removing either compromises the system's integrity.

### 5.3 Stability and Governance Validation

A unique aspect of this paper is the rigorous benchmarking of feature stability, addressing the risk that policies overfit transient signals. The authors establish a quantitative benchmark for checking feature stability over a **6-month window** (Section 4.3).

#### Feature Stability Benchmarks (Table 2)
The authors define the **User-Cohort Shift Ratio ($R_{\text{shift}}$)** as the percentage of users who migrate from their assigned cohort to a different one over 6 months. This metric quantifies how stable a feature's segmentation is over time.

*   **Baseline Stability:** Even a perceived stable feature set $S$ exhibits drift: **$6\%$** shift under Quantile Cuts and **$2\%$** under Binary Cuts. This establishes a lower bound for unavoidable natural drift.
*   **Unstable Features:** **Feature 4** shows high volatility with approximately **$50\%$** shift under quantile cuts and **$20\%$** under binary cuts.
*   **Validation Thresholds:** Based on these benchmarks, GEARS implements a pre-search filter:
    *   Features must exhibit $R_{\text{shift}} \leq 15\%$ (Binary) or $\leq 45\%$ (Quantile).
    *   This automatically disqualifies high-lift but unstable features like Feature 4.

This experimental design is critical because it moves stability from a qualitative concern to a quantitative constraint. By setting thresholds based on empirical baselines (Table 2), the system avoids arbitrary rules.

#### Backtest Consistency (Figure 4)
To validate the efficacy of this governance, the authors conducted a controlled selection from a randomized experiment with four policy candidates. After filtering out high-variance candidates, the remaining policy ("Best for Metric 2") demonstrated consistent metric improvement over a **one-month period** (Figure 4).

*   **Significance:** This backtest confirms that the 6-month stability check predicts short-to-medium-term performance persistence. It bridges the gap between the long-term stability benchmark (Table 2) and immediate deployment value (Figure 4).

### 5.4 Real-World Deployment Impact

Offline metrics are necessary but insufficient; the ultimate test is production performance. The authors report results across **9 diverse product surfaces** (Table 3) and a detailed case study (Section 5.1).

#### Cross-Surface Performance (Table 3)
Table 3 shows metric lifts across various domains. The results are heterogeneous, reflecting the reality of production systems:
*   **Surface 8:** Achieves **$0.13\%$** lift on Metric 1 and **$0.37\%$** on Metric 2. This is the strongest result, demonstrating the framework's potential ceiling.
*   **Surface 6:** Shows modest gains across three metrics ($0.011\%$, $0.017\%$, $0.08\%$).
*   **Surface 5:** Shows a smaller lift of **$0.042\%$**.

**Critical Observation:** The variability in lifts (from $0.011\%$ to $0.37\%$) suggests that GEARS is not a "magic bullet" that guarantees massive gains everywhere. Instead, it consistently finds *some* improvement where manual tuning might have found none. The paper notes that a dash (—) indicates the metric was not applicable or not the primary target, highlighting that GEARS adapts to different surface constraints.

#### Case Study: Resolving Trade-offs (Table 4)
Section 5.1 presents an anonymized case study illustrating how GEARS handles conflicting objectives. **Table 4** shows the baseline treatment arms:
*   **Treatment 1:** Improves Metric 2 ($+0.282\%$) but hurts Metric 1 ($-0.049\%$).
*   **Treatment 2:** Improves Metric 1 ($+0.036\%$) but hurts Metric 2 ($-0.289\%$).

This is a classic zero-sum scenario where global uniform treatments fail. GEARS resolves this by discovering **cohort-specific policies**:
*   **Active Users:** Benefit more from Treatment 1.
*   **Less Active Users:** Prefer Treatment 2.

By deploying a cohort-targeted policy, the system yields a statistically significant lift on the prioritized metric while maintaining neutrality on the competing metric. This validates the **Vibe Optimization** claim (Section 3.1): the system successfully translated the high-level intent ("find the tradeoff") into a segmented execution strategy that manual analysis might have missed due to the complexity of the slice-and-dice space.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Validity

While the experiments are comprehensive, a critical reading reveals both strengths and limitations that a practitioner should consider.

#### Strengths
1.  **Real-World Data:** The use of 20 internal experiments and 9 production surfaces ensures the results are not artifacts of a clean academic dataset. The noise and constraints are authentic.
2.  **Strong Baselines:** Comparing against **Code-as-Action** and **Self-Consistency** sets a high bar. Many agentic papers compare only against naive prompting; here, GEARS proves it adds value over sophisticated reasoning strategies.
3.  **Ablation Rigor:** The `w/o Bash` and `w/o Skill` ablations clearly isolate the contribution of each system component, preventing the "black box" critique often leveled at LLM systems.

#### Limitations and Open Questions
1.  **Model Dependency:** All experiments use **Claude Sonnet (ant)**. The paper does not report results on open-source models or other proprietary families. It is unclear if the Specialized Agent Skills would perform equally well with a less capable backbone, or if the results are tightly coupled to this specific model's reasoning ability.
2.  **Ground Truth Definition:** The offline ground truth is defined as the top-5 policies according to specified criteria. While robust, this still relies on offline metrics. While the governance layer checks stability, the ultimate "ground truth" is online performance. The paper bridges this with Table 3, but the offline selection (Table 1) remains a proxy.
3.  **Scalability of Skills:** The Specialized Agent Skills rely on "filesystem-based resources" and "internal artifacts such as SQL scripts." The paper does not quantify the engineering effort required to maintain these skills. If every new product surface requires manual skill creation, the scalability benefit might be partially offset by maintenance overhead.
4.  **Stability Window:** The 6-month window for $R_{\text{shift}}$ is rigorous, but feature distributions can shift due to external events (e.g., holidays, product changes) that historical data doesn't capture. The backtest (Figure 4) only covers one month; longer-term online stability beyond the 6-month historical lookback is not explicitly measured.

#### Conclusion on Experiments
The experiments convincingly support the paper's central thesis: **autonomous discovery with governance outperforms static optimization.** The massive drop in performance when governance is removed (Table 1) is the most compelling evidence that stability checks are integral to optimization, not just compliance. However, the variability in production lifts (Table 3) suggests that GEARS is a tool for **consistent incremental improvement** rather than guaranteed breakthrough gains on every surface. The framework succeeds by making the optimization process robust and scalable, even if the magnitude of gain depends on the specific product context.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While GEARS demonstrates significant improvements in policy selection accuracy and deployment stability, its architecture introduces specific trade-offs that practitioners must consider. The system shifts complexity from model training to knowledge engineering and relies on assumptions about data stationarity that may not hold in all environments. This section critically examines the foundational assumptions, unaddressed edge cases, operational constraints, and remaining weaknesses of the framework.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions

The effectiveness of GEARS rests on two critical assumptions regarding data stability and model capability. If these assumptions are violated, the system's guarantees degrade.

*   **Stationarity of Feature Distributions:** The Deterministic Lifecycle Governance module (Section 3.3) assumes that historical feature stability predicts future behavior. The validation hooks rely on a **6-month window** to calculate the User-Cohort Shift Ratio ($R_{\text{shift}}$), as shown in **Table 2**.
    *   **The Risk:** This assumes relative stationarity in user behavior and feature definitions over half a year. In rapidly evolving product environments (e.g., during a major UI redesign or external market shock), historical stability metrics may become obsolete. A feature stable for 6 months might become volatile overnight due to factors not captured in the historical log.
    *   **Implication:** The pre-search filter thresholds ($R_{\text{shift}} \leq 15\%$ Binary) may incorrectly reject valid policies during periods of legitimate distribution shift, causing the system to miss opportunities during dynamic phases.

*   **LLM Reasoning Capability:** The Specialized Agent Skills (Section 3.2) assume the backbone LLM possesses sufficient reasoning capability to interpret structured instructions and execute complex tasks like SQL generation or trade-off diagnosis.
    *   **The Evidence:** All experiments in **Section 5.1** utilize **Claude Sonnet (ant)**. The ablation study in **Table 1** shows that without the Skill module, performance drops significantly, but this assumes the LLM can actually *use* those skills effectively.
    *   **The Risk:** Smaller or open-source models may lack the reasoning depth to navigate the progressive disclosure strategy (Section 3.4) without succumbing to context rot or hallucination. The framework's success is tightly coupled to the specific capabilities of high-end proprietary models, creating a dependency risk.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

GEARS is designed for large-scale ranking optimization where historical data is abundant. Several scenarios fall outside its current design scope.

*   **Cold Start Problems:** The governance mechanism requires extensive historical data to establish stability baselines (e.g., the 6% baseline drift in **Table 2**).
    *   **The Gap:** For new products, new features, or new user cohorts with less than 6 months of history, the $R_{\text{shift}}$ metric cannot be computed reliably. GEARS does not propose a fallback mechanism for these "cold start" scenarios.
    *   **Consequence:** In greenfield projects, the system may be unable to provide governance guarantees, forcing operators to revert to manual validation or accept higher risk, thereby reducing the framework's utility in innovation-heavy contexts.

*   **Real-Time Inference vs. Configuration Optimization:** It is crucial to distinguish between *ranking inference* and *ranking configuration*.
    *   **The Clarification:** GEARS operates as an offline configuration optimizer (Figure 1), generating policies that are then deployed to the ranking system. It does not perform real-time ranking decisions itself.
    *   **The Gap:** The agentic loop involves multiple steps (Candidate Generation -> Skill Reasoning -> Governance Validation). This latency is acceptable for weekly or daily policy updates but is incompatible with real-time decision-making. Users expecting an agentic system to handle live traffic routing will find the architecture unsuitable.

*   **Non-Quantifiable Business Constraints:** While "Vibe Optimization" (Section 3.1) allows for high-level intent, some business constraints are political or qualitative rather than metric-based.
    *   **The Gap:** Constraints such as "avoid controversy in region X" or "prioritize partner content Y" may not be easily encoded into the SQL scripts or guardrail metrics accessible to the Specialized Agent Skills.
    *   **Consequence:** The system optimizes for what is measurable. If a critical business requirement lacks a corresponding metric or feature audit, GEARS may optimize a policy that is mathematically optimal but politically untenable.

### 6.3 Operational and Scalability Constraints

Implementing GEARS introduces operational overheads that differ from traditional machine learning pipelines. The system trades model complexity for system complexity.

*   **Engineering Overhead for Skill Maintenance:** The Specialized Agent Skills are modular, filesystem-based resources containing structured instructions and internal artifacts (Section 3.2).
    *   **The Constraint:** These skills are not static; they require maintenance as infrastructure changes. If a SQL table schema changes or a feature is deprecated, the corresponding Skill must be updated.
    *   **Trade-off:** This shifts the burden from **model retraining** (traditional ML) to **knowledge engineering** (Agentic ML). Organizations must invest in maintaining this knowledge base. If skills become outdated, the agent's reasoning fidelity degrades, leading to the "context rot" the system aims to prevent. The paper does not quantify the engineering hours required to maintain this skill library across diverse product surfaces.

*   **Computational Cost of Agentic Loops:** The workflow in **Figure 1** involves multiple LLM calls: intent translation, candidate inspection, skill execution, and governance validation.
    *   **The Constraint:** Each step incurs token costs and latency. While **Table 1** demonstrates accuracy, it does not report the compute cost per policy recommendation.
    *   **Trade-off:** Compared to a single-shot uplift model inference, GEARS is significantly more expensive per decision. This is justified for high-impact ranking configurations but may be cost-prohibitive for low-traffic surfaces where the ROI does not cover the agentic overhead.

*   **Scalability of Governance Checks:** The Deterministic Lifecycle Governance registers hooks around every executable action (Section 3.3).
    *   **The Constraint:** As the number of candidate policies grows (hundreds per experiment in **Section 4.1**), the number of validation checks scales linearly.
    *   **Trade-off:** Rigorous governance slows down the discovery process. There is a tension between the depth of validation (e.g., checking 6-month history for every feature) and the speed of iteration. In fast-moving experimentation cycles, this thoroughness could become a bottleneck.

### 6.4 Remaining Weaknesses and Open Questions

Despite strong empirical results, several weaknesses and open questions remain regarding the long-term viability and generalizability of the approach.

*   **Conservative Bias in Optimization:** The combination of Tolerance-Based Frontier Expansion (Section 3.4) and Deterministic Governance (Section 3.3) inherently favors stability.
    *   **The Weakness:** While this prevents brittle policies, it may also filter out high-risk, high-reward innovations. The pre-search filter ($R_{\text{shift}} \leq 45\%$ Quantile) explicitly removes volatile features.
    *   **Open Question:** Does this stability focus lead to local optima? If all volatile features are banned, the system may converge on a "safe" plateau where no further significant gains are possible without accepting higher risk. The paper does not measure the **opportunity cost** of rejected policies—i.e., how much potential lift was sacrificed for stability.

*   **Generalizability Beyond Ranking:** The paper positions GEARS as a general framework but validates it primarily on ranking personalization (Section 3.1).
    *   **The Weakness:** Ranking systems have specific properties (clear metrics, abundant logs, established experimentation infrastructure) that facilitate agentic reasoning.
    *   **Open Question:** It is unclear how well the Specialized Agent Skills transfer to domains with less structured data or weaker feedback signals (e.g., generative content creation, strategic planning). The reliance on "internal artifacts such as SQL scripts" (Section 3.2) suggests a dependency on mature data infrastructure that may not exist in other domains.

*   **Long-Term Online Stability:** The backtest in **Figure 4** confirms consistency over **one month**, and the governance checks use a **6-month historical window** (Table 2).
    *   **The Weakness:** There is no data on policy performance beyond the initial deployment month. A policy stable for 6 months historically might degrade after 7 months due to concept drift.
    *   **Open Question:** Does the system require periodic re-validation of deployed policies? The current workflow focuses on *selection* (Figure 1), not continuous *monitoring* of deployed policies. Without a feedback loop that retrains the agent on post-deployment failures, the system risks recommending policies that eventually decay.

*   **Proprietary Model Lock-in:** The reliance on a specific proprietary model (Claude Sonnet) for the backbone LLM (Section 5.1) creates a vendor dependency.
    *   **The Weakness:** If the model API changes, costs increase, or performance degrades, the entire GEARS framework is impacted. The Specialized Agent Skills are tuned to the reasoning patterns of this specific model family.
    *   **Open Question:** Can the skills be easily ported to open-source models to reduce cost and risk? The paper does not explore cross-model compatibility, leaving uncertainty about the framework's portability.

In summary, GEARS trades **computational efficiency and simplicity** for **robustness and interpretability**. It is best suited for mature organizations with stable data infrastructure, high-value ranking surfaces, and the engineering bandwidth to maintain specialized agent skills. For fast-moving, data-scarce, or cost-sensitive environments, the operational overhead and conservative bias may outweigh the benefits of autonomous discovery.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of GEARS signals a maturation in how we approach machine learning operations for large-scale systems. It moves the field beyond the question of "which model predicts best?" to "which system discovers and deploys safely?" This section outlines how this work reshapes the research landscape, identifies critical avenues for follow-up study, and provides concrete guidance for practitioners considering integration.

### 7.1 Shifting the Paradigm: From Model-Centric to System-Centric AI

GEARS fundamentally alters the optimization landscape by treating the **engineering context** as a first-class citizen in the learning process. Historically, research focused on improving the signal estimator (e.g., better uplift models, as discussed in Section 2). GEARS demonstrates that in mature production environments, the bottleneck is not estimation accuracy, but **deployment feasibility**.

This shift has three profound implications for the field:

1.  **Governance as Performance:**
    The ablation study in **Table 1** shows that removing the governance layer (`GEARS w/o Bash`) collapses Top-1 Accuracy from $0.86$ to $0.26$. This proves that safety filters are not merely compliance hurdles; they are **signal enhancers** that prevent the optimizer from chasing noise. Future system design must integrate validation hooks directly into the optimization loop, rather than treating them as post-hoc checks.
2.  **Explicit Knowledge Encoding:**
    The success of **Specialized Agent Skills** (Section 3.2) over generic prompting suggests that implicit knowledge stored in LLM weights is insufficient for high-stakes engineering. The field must move toward **explicit knowledge bases** (filesystem-based skills, SQL scripts) that agents can query. This bridges the gap between the flexibility of LLMs and the precision required for infrastructure control.
3.  **Autonomous Discovery Loops:**
    By reframing optimization as an interactive process (**Figure 1**) rather than a static inference task, GEARS enables the discovery of non-convex policies via **Tolerance-Based Frontier Expansion** (Section 3.4). This suggests that future ranking systems should not just select weights, but actively **generate hypotheses** about user segments and feature interactions that human engineers might overlook due to cognitive load.

### 7.2 Critical Avenues for Follow-Up Research

While GEARS validates the agentic approach, several open questions remain that define the next frontier of research. Based on the limitations identified in Section 6 and the architecture in Section 3, we identify four key areas for investigation.

#### 1. Stability Estimation in Data-Scarce Environments
The current **Deterministic Lifecycle Governance** relies on a **6-month window** to calculate the User-Cohort Shift Ratio ($R_{\text{shift}}$), as detailed in **Table 2**. This creates a barrier for new products or features lacking historical depth.
*   **Research Question:** How can stability be estimated with limited data?
*   **Potential Direction:** Investigating **Bayesian priors** or **synthetic control methods** to bootstrap stability estimates for cold-start features. The goal would be to adapt the $R_{\text{shift}}$ threshold dynamically based on data availability rather than using a fixed 6-month benchmark.

#### 2. Skill Evolution and Self-Improvement
Currently, **Specialized Agent Skills** are static, filesystem-based resources (Section 3.2). They encode expert knowledge but do not learn from new experiments automatically.
*   **Research Question:** Can the agent update its own skills based on deployment outcomes?
*   **Potential Direction:** Developing a **meta-learning loop** where successful policy rationales are automatically converted into new Skill modules. This would reduce the manual engineering overhead noted in Section 6.3 and allow the system to accumulate institutional knowledge over time.

#### 3. Model Agnosticism and Efficiency
All experiments in **Section 5.1** utilize **Claude Sonnet (ant)**. The computational cost of the agentic loop (multiple reasoning steps, skill executions) is significant compared to single-shot models.
*   **Research Question:** Can smaller, open-source models achieve comparable performance with optimized prompting?
*   **Potential Direction:** Distilling the reasoning patterns of the **Insight-Driven Policy Selection** module into smaller models. This would lower the barrier to entry for organizations without access to top-tier proprietary APIs and reduce the latency of the discovery loop.

#### 4. Closing the Online Feedback Loop
The current workflow focuses on **offline selection** validated by historical stability (Section 3.3). While **Figure 4** shows one-month backtest consistency, there is no mechanism described for the agent to learn from **post-deployment online performance**.
*   **Research Question:** How do we safely incorporate online feedback into the agent's knowledge base?
*   **Potential Direction:** Implementing a **continuous monitoring agent** that compares predicted stability (from Governance) against actual online drift. If a policy degrades faster than expected, this signal should trigger a refinement of the Stability Benchmarks (Table 2) for future searches.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

Although validated on ranking systems, the core architecture of GEARS—**Intent Translation + Skill-Based Reasoning + Deterministic Governance**—is applicable to any domain where optimization must respect hard constraints.

*   **Marketing and Promotion Optimization:**
    Similar to ranking, marketing campaigns involve multi-objective trade-offs (e.g., conversion vs. brand sentiment). The **Vibe Optimization** capability (Section 3.1) allows marketers to specify high-level goals ("maximize holiday sales without discounting premium tiers") while the agent handles the segment-level targeting logic.
*   **Supply Chain and Logistics:**
    The **Governance Module** is highly relevant for logistics where constraints are physical and rigid (e.g., warehouse capacity, delivery windows). The **Specialized Agent Skills** could encode operational rules (e.g., "do not route through Region X during storm season") that pure optimization models might violate to save cost.
*   **Financial Portfolio Management:**
    In finance, **brittle policies** can lead to catastrophic loss. The **Tolerance-Based Frontier Expansion** (Section 3.4) is directly applicable to portfolio selection, where "near-optimal" portfolios with lower volatility are often preferred over strictly optimal ones with high risk. The $R_{\text{shift}}$ metric could be adapted to measure **asset correlation stability** over time.
*   **Legacy System Modernization:**
    Many organizations operate on older infrastructure accessible only via SQL or scripts. GEARS demonstrates how to wrap these legacy artifacts in **Agent Skills** (Section 3.2), allowing modern LLM interfaces to control older systems without requiring a full rewrite. This is a practical pathway for integrating AI into established engineering environments.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For practitioners considering adopting this framework, success depends on matching the system's strengths to your organizational context. Based on the experimental design in **Section 4** and limitations in **Section 6**, we offer the following integration guidelines.

#### When to Prefer GEARS
*   **High-Stakes Decisions:** Use GEARS when policy errors are costly (e.g., revenue loss, user trust). The **Governance Module** adds latency and cost but prevents brittle deployments.
*   **Multi-Objective Trade-offs:** Ideal for scenarios where metrics conflict (e.g., engagement vs. satisfaction), as shown in the **Table 4** case study. The agent excels at navigating non-convex Pareto frontiers.
*   **Mature Data Infrastructure:** The framework requires robust historical data to establish stability baselines (e.g., the **6-month window** in Table 2). If you lack this depth, the governance checks may be too conservative.

#### When to Avoid GEARS
*   **Cold Start Scenarios:** If you lack 6 months of historical data for feature stability, the $R_{\text{shift}}$ metric cannot be computed reliably. In these cases, simpler uplift models or manual exploration may be more effective.
*   **Real-Time Inference:** GEARS is an **offline configuration optimizer** (Figure 1). It is not designed for millisecond-level ranking decisions. Do not attempt to run the agentic loop in the inference path.
*   **Low-Budget Environments:** The agentic workflow involves multiple LLM calls (intent, skills, governance). If the expected lift (e.g., $0.011\%$ in **Table 3**) does not justify the compute cost, a simpler heuristic may suffice.

#### Integration Checklist
To replicate the core benefits of GEARS without necessarily adopting the full stack immediately:
1.  **Define Stability Metrics:** Adopt the **User-Cohort Shift Ratio ($R_{\text{shift}}$)** methodology from **Section 4.3**. Calculate shift rates for your key features over a 6-month window to establish your own baseline (like the $6\%$ baseline in **Table 2**).
2.  **Implement Pre-Search Filters:** Before running optimization, filter out features that exceed your stability threshold (e.g., $R_{\text{shift}} \leq 15\%$ Binary). This single step replicates the most critical value of the Governance module.
3.  **Encode Expert Rules:** Instead of relying on generic prompts, document your engineering constraints as executable checks (SQL queries, code scripts). Connect your optimizer to these scripts to prevent hallucinated policies.
4.  **Start with Hybrid Workflows:** You do not need full autonomy immediately. Use the agent to **generate candidates** (Section 3.1) but retain human approval for final selection. This captures the discovery benefit while mitigating risk during the learning phase.

By treating optimization as a **governed discovery process** rather than a static modeling task, practitioners can achieve the dual goals of innovation and stability. GEARS provides the architectural blueprint for this shift, demonstrating that in large-scale systems, **robustness is the foundation of optimality**.
