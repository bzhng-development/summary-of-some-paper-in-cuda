## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the critical challenge of spacecraft guidance in environments with unknown, time-varying dynamics—such as asteroid gravity fields or sudden engine failure—by proposing an adaptive guidance system built on reinforcement meta-learning with a recurrent Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) agent. By implementing Gated Recurrent Units (GRU) within both the policy and value function networks, the agent adapts in real-time to unobserved variables like lander mass or external forces, successfully navigating four difficult tasks including asteroid descent with random dynamics and Mars landing using only noisy Doppler radar altimeter observations. The recurrent policy significantly outperforms traditional DR/DV guidance and non-recurrent MLP policies, demonstrating robustness where baseline methods fail catastrophically, such as reducing mean terminal position error to 0.3m versus 1.9m for DR/DV in the asteroid landing task (Table 7).

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Challenge of Uncertain Spacecraft Dynamics
Spacecraft guidance systems operate on a fundamental assumption: that the physical laws governing the vehicle's motion are known and stable. In control theory, these laws are encapsulated in the **equations of motion**, which describe how forces translate into changes in position and velocity. For a landing vehicle, the translational motion is typically modeled as:

$$
\dot{v} = \frac{T + F_{env}}{m} + g + \text{Coriolis and Centrifugal Terms}
$$

Where $v$ is velocity, $T$ is thrust, $m$ is mass, $g$ is gravity, and $F_{env}$ represents environmental disturbances. The Introduction of this paper highlights a critical gap: in many real-world missions, the parameters within these equations are **incompletely modeled** or **time-varying**. 

The authors provide several concrete examples of why this modeling assumption fails:
*   **Orbital Refueling:** As fuel transfers between spacecraft, the inertia tensor changes significantly, altering how the vehicle responds to control inputs (Introduction, Paragraph 1).
*   **Exoatmospheric Kill Vehicles (EKV):** As fuel depletes during divert thrusts, the center of mass shifts. This causes thrust vectors to become non-orthogonal to the velocity vector, wasting fuel and degrading performance.
*   **Asteroid Missions:** Gravitational fields, rotational velocity, and solar radiation pressure may be unknown prior to arrival.
*   **Actuator Failure:** A failed thruster fundamentally modifies the dynamic system, rendering pre-computed guidance laws invalid.

These scenarios create a need for a guidance system that does not rely on a fixed, perfect model of the environment. Instead, the system must **adapt in real time** to discrepancies between the expected dynamics and the actual physical behavior of the spacecraft.

### Limitations of Traditional Guidance Laws
To establish a performance baseline, the authors compare their method against the **DR/DV (Delta-Rotation/Delta-Velocity) guidance law** (Reference 5). Traditional guidance laws like DR/DV are analytic solutions derived from optimal control theory. They calculate the necessary thrust to reach a target state based on the current state and a known dynamic model.

The fragility of this approach lies in its dependency on **ground truth parameters**. As noted in the Introduction, to improve the DR/DV performance for this study, the authors explicitly gave the DR/DV controller access to the **ground truth gravitational force** and the **lander mass at the start of an episode**. Even with this advantage—information that would not be available in a truly unknown environment—the DR/DV law struggles when dynamics vary significantly during the episode or when the environment deviates from the design model. For instance, in the asteroid landing task with unknown dynamics, the DR/DV law exhibits catastrophic position errors (Table 7), demonstrating that analytic laws lack the flexibility to compensate for unmodeled forces without explicit re-tuning.

### Learning-Based Approaches and Meta-Learning
Reinforcement Learning (RL) offers an alternative paradigm where an agent learns a **policy** ($\pi$) that maps observations ($x$) to actions ($u$) through trial and error. However, standard RL policies often suffer from the same rigidity as traditional guidance if not designed correctly.

#### Static vs. Adaptive Policies
The paper distinguishes between two types of neural network policies:
1.  **MLP (Multi-Layer Perceptron) Policy:** A feedforward network where the output depends only on the current observation. Once trained, its behavior is **fixed** by its weights. It cannot incorporate new information about the environment during a single episode.
2.  **Recurrent Policy:** A network containing recurrent layers (such as Gated Recurrent Units, or GRUs). These layers maintain an internal **hidden state** that evolves over time.

The motivation for using recurrent layers is rooted in how they process information. In a standard MLP, if the observation $x_k$ does not contain full state information (e.g., the current mass $m$ is unknown), the policy cannot infer it. In contrast, a recurrent policy updates its hidden state $h_k$ based on the history of observations and actions:

$$
h_k = \text{RNN}(h_{k-1}, x_k, u_{k-1})
$$

The authors argue that because the policy is trained to maximize rewards across a distribution of different dynamic systems, the hidden state learns to capture **unobserved information** such as external forces or current lander mass (Introduction, Paragraph 4). This allows the policy to adapt its behavior mid-flight without changing its network weights.

#### Reinforcement Meta-Learning
The paper frames this approach as **Reinforcement Meta-Learning**. In standard machine learning, a model is trained for a specific task. In meta-learning ("learning to learn"), the agent learns a strategy to quickly adapt to novel tasks. 

Here, the "tasks" are different Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) defined by varying dynamical systems. By training the recurrent policy over a wide range of randomized system parameters (e.g., random gravity, random mass), the agent learns to use its hidden state to identify the current dynamics and adjust its control strategy accordingly. This differs from prior work (Reference 2) where recurrent networks were used to explicitly learn model parameters to augment observations. In this paper, the adaptation is implicit within the policy's hidden state, allowing for end-to-end learning of guidance and navigation.

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself at the intersection of robust control and deep reinforcement learning. While prior work (Reference 4) has shown that MLP policies can handle some parameter uncertainty, the authors note they "will tend to under-perform a recurrent policy on tasks with randomized dynamics" (Introduction, Paragraph 4). 

The specific contribution of this work is the application of **Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)** with both recurrent policy and value function approximators to the domain of planetary landing. The authors identify four specific gaps in existing literature that their experiments address:
1.  **Unknown Environmental Dynamics:** Testing adaptation to completely unknown gravity and rotation (Asteroid Landing).
2.  **Actuator Failure:** Demonstrating robustness to sudden changes in control authority (Engine Failure).
3.  **Large Mass Variation:** Handling significant changes in vehicle inertia without explicit mass sensing (High Mass Variation).
4.  **Non-Markovian Observations:** Integrating guidance and navigation using only noisy sensor data (Doppler Radar Altimeter), where the current observation alone does not satisfy the Markov property (Introduction, List 1-4).

By comparing against both the analytic DR/DV baseline and a non-recurrent MLP agent, the paper seeks to prove that **recurrence is the key mechanism** for achieving real-time adaptability in safety-critical guidance tasks where system identification is impossible or too slow.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a deep reinforcement learning paper that builds an adaptive spacecraft guidance system using recurrent neural networks trained with Proximal Policy Optimization. The core idea is that by training a policy with recurrent layers over randomized environmental dynamics, the network's hidden state learns to infer unobserved variables (like mass or external forces) and adapt control behavior in real-time without explicit system identification.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a neural network-based guidance controller that takes sensor observations as input and outputs thrust commands, where the network contains memory elements that allow it to adapt mid-flight to unknown conditions. The problem it solves is spacecraft landing in environments where critical parameters (gravity, mass, thruster health) are unknown or time-varying, and the solution shape is a recurrent policy that learns to encode environmental information in its hidden state through meta-learning across randomized training episodes.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The system consists of four major components working in a closed loop. First, the **Environment** simulates spacecraft dynamics using 3-DOF equations of motion with randomized parameters (gravity, mass, disturbances) and generates observations from the current state. Second, the **Policy Network** (a 4-layer neural network with a GRU recurrent layer at Layer 2) receives observations and outputs a probability distribution over thrust actions. Third, the **Value Function Network** (mirroring the policy architecture) estimates the expected future reward from the current state to compute advantage signals. Fourth, the **PPO Optimizer** collects batches of trajectories, computes policy gradients using clipped objectives and multiple discount rates, and updates both network weights. Information flows as: environment state → observation → policy/value networks → action → environment dynamics → new state → reward → optimizer → updated weights.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Equations of Motion and Environment Model**

The physical environment is modeled using 3-degree-of-freedom translational dynamics, meaning the spacecraft's position and velocity are tracked in three dimensions but rotational dynamics are not explicitly modeled. The equations of motion define how the lander's state evolves given thrust commands and environmental forces:

$$
\dot{r} = v
$$

$$
\dot{v} = \frac{T + F_{env}}{m} + g + 2\dot{r}_a \times \omega + (\omega \times r_a) \times \omega
$$

$$
\dot{m} = -\frac{\|T\|}{I_{sp} g_{ref}}
$$

The first equation states that the rate of change of position equals velocity. The second equation states that acceleration equals the sum of thrust, environmental disturbances, gravity, and rotational effects (Coriolis and centrifugal terms), all divided by mass. The third equation models mass depletion as proportional to thrust magnitude divided by specific impulse and reference gravity. Here, $r$ is the lander's position in the target-centered reference frame, $r_a$ is position in the planet or asteroid-centered frame, $T$ is the thrust vector, $m$ is spacecraft mass, $g_{ref} = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$, $g = [0, 0, -3.7114] \text{ m/s}^2$ for Mars, $I_{sp} = 225 \text{ s}$ is specific impulse, $F_{env}$ represents environmental disturbances like wind, and $\omega$ is the rotational velocity vector (set to zero for Mars landing experiments). For Mars landing, thrust is constrained to $[2000 \text{ N}, 15000 \text{ N}]$ with nominal wet mass of $2000 \text{ kg}$, while asteroid landing uses pulsed thrusters with $2 \text{ N}$ capability per axis.

**Reinforcement Learning Framework with PPO**

The agent learns through episodic interaction where each episode produces a trajectory $\tau = [x_0, u_0, ..., x_T, u_T]$ consisting of observations, actions, and rewards. The objective is to maximize expected discounted returns:

$$
J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{p(\tau)} [r(\tau)] = \int r(\tau) p_\theta(\tau) d\tau
$$

where $r(\tau) = \sum_{i=0}^{T} \gamma^i r_k(x_k, u_k)$ and $\gamma \in [0, 1)$ is the discount factor. The policy gradient theorem shows that for a stochastic policy $\pi_\theta(u_k|x_k)$, the gradient can be estimated as:

$$
\nabla_\theta J(\theta) \approx \sum_{i=0}^{M} \sum_{k=0}^{T} A^\pi_w(x^i_k, u^i_k) \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(u^i_k|x^i_k)
$$

where $A^\pi(x_k, u_k) = Q^\pi(x_k, u_k) - V^\pi(x_k)$ is the advantage function representing how much better an action is compared to the state value baseline. This paper uses Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), which improves upon the Policy Gradient Theorem by clipping the policy update to prevent destructive large steps. The PPO objective uses a probability ratio $p_k(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(u_k|x_k)}{\pi_{\theta_{old}}(u_k|x_k)}$ and clips it to stay within $[1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon]$:

$$
L(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{p(\tau)} [\min [p_k(\theta), \text{clip}(p_k(\theta), 1 - \epsilon, 1 + \epsilon)] A^\pi_w(x_k, u_k)]
$$

This clipped objective maintains KL divergence constraints between policy updates, aiding convergence by ensuring the policy does not change drastically between iterations. The authors adjust the clipping parameter $\epsilon$ to target a KL divergence of $0.001$ between policy updates. The value function $V^\pi_w$ is learned concurrently using mean squared error between predicted values and empirical returns:

$$
L(w) = \sum_{i=1}^{M} \left( V^\pi_w(x^i_k) - \left[ \sum_{\ell=k}^{T} \gamma^{\ell-k} r(u^i_\ell, x^i_\ell) \right] \right)^2
$$

Policy and value function parameters are updated via gradient ascent and descent respectively with learning rates $\beta_\theta$ and $\beta_w$.

**Recurrent Policy and Value Function Architecture**

Both the policy and value function are implemented as 4-layer neural networks with tanh activations on hidden layers, where Layer 2 is a Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) layer that maintains temporal memory. The network architecture is specified in Table 1 of the paper: Layer 1 (hidden 1) has $10 \times \text{obs\_dim}$ units, Layer 2 (hidden 2, the recurrent layer) has $\sqrt{n_{h1} \times n_{h3}}$ units, Layer 3 (hidden 3) has $10 \times \text{act\_dim}$ units, and the output layer has $\text{act\_dim}$ units with linear activation for the policy or 1 unit for the value function. The policy outputs a Gaussian distribution with mean $\pi_\theta(x_k)$ and diagonal covariance matrix for the action distribution, where the exploration variance adapts automatically during learning since log probabilities are calculated using this variance.

The critical implementation challenge for recurrent networks in RL is enabling parallel computation across multiple episodes while preserving temporal dependencies. During learning, the recurrent layer must be unrolled through time to capture relationships between sequences of observations and actions. However, the hidden state at step 61 is not available until completing the forward pass from steps 1 through 60, which would prevent parallel processing of episode batches. The solution is to capture the actual hidden state when sampling from the policy during environment interaction and add it to the rollouts. Where a standard rollout would store tuples $(o, a, r)$ for observation, action, and reward, this implementation augments each tuple with the hidden states of both the policy and value functions.

During the forward pass, the network unrolls the output of the layer preceding the recurrent layer, reshaping data from $\mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ to $\mathbb{R}^{T \times m/T \times n}$, where $m$ is batch size, $n$ is feature dimension, and $T$ is the number of unroll steps. At step zero, the hidden state from the rollouts is input to the recurrent layer, but for all subsequent steps up to $T$, the state evolves according to the current parameterization of the recurrent layer. After the recurrent layer, the output is reshaped back to $\mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$. Without injecting the hidden states from rollouts at the first step, parallel unrolling of a batch would not be possible, which would dramatically increase computation time. The rollouts must be padded so that $m/T$ is an integer but unpadded for loss computation, and training data must not be shuffled as this destroys temporal associations in trajectories. GRUs are used instead of standard RNNs to avoid exploding or vanishing gradients when backpropagating through the unrolled recurrent layer.

**Reward Function Design with Velocity Shaping**

The most difficult part of solving the planetary landing problem using RL was developing a reward function that works in a sparse reward setting, where rewarding only successful soft pinpoint landings would never provide learning signals within realistic episode counts. Instead of inverse reinforcement learning from expert demonstrations, the authors engineer a shaping reward function inspired by the gaze heuristic used by animals intercepting prey. The gaze heuristic works by keeping the line of sight angle constant during intercept, and is the basis of Proportional Navigation guidance laws. For landing, the agent attempts to keep its velocity vector aligned with the line of sight vector to the target, and estimates time-to-go as the ratio of range to velocity magnitude, reducing targeted velocity as time-to-go decreases.

The piecewise reward shaping function creates a velocity field that maps lander position to target velocity:

$$
v_{targ} = -v_o \left( \frac{\hat{r}}{\|\hat{r}\|} \right) \left( 1 - \exp\left( -\frac{t_{go}}{\tau} \right) \right)
$$

$$
t_{go} = \frac{\|\hat{r}\|}{\|\hat{v}\|}
$$

where $\hat{r}$ and $\hat{v}$ are modified position and velocity vectors that change behavior below 15m altitude. Above 15m, $\hat{r} = r - [0, 0, 15]$ and $\hat{v} = v - [0, 0, -2]$, targeting a location 15m above the landing site with -2 m/s vertical velocity. Below 15m, $\hat{r} = [0, 0, r_z]$ and $\hat{v} = v - [0, 0, -1]$, setting downrange and crossrange velocity components to zero to encourage vertical descent. The time constant $\tau$ switches from $\tau_1$ to $\tau_2$ at 15m altitude.

The complete reward function combines multiple terms:

$$
r = \alpha \|v - v_{targ}\| + \beta \|T\| + \gamma + \eta (\text{landing conditions met})
$$

where $\alpha = -0.01$ weights velocity tracking error, $\beta = -0.05$ weights control effort (fuel), $\gamma = 0.01$ is a constant positive term encouraging trajectory progress (without which the agent would prematurely terminate to maximize discounted rewards), and $\eta = 10$ is a terminal bonus for successful landing within limits ($r_{lim} = 5 \text{ m}$, $v_{lim} = 2 \text{ m/s}$, $gs_{min\_lim} = 5 \text{ rad/s}$ for glideslope). The minimum glideslope at touchdown ensures the lander's velocity is directed predominantly downward. These hyperparameters are given in Table 2 of the paper.

**Multiple Discount Rate Framework**

A key innovation in this work is a framework for accommodating multiple discount rates to resolve a conflict between terminal and shaping rewards. When a terminal reward is used, it is advantageous to use a relatively large discount rate so the terminal signal propagates back through the episode. However, it is also advantageous to use a lower discount rate for shaping rewards to prevent them from dominating the terminal signal. The authors resolve this by introducing separate discount rates: $\gamma_1$ for the terminal reward term $r_1(k)$ (associated with the $\eta$ coefficient), and $\gamma_2$ for all other reward terms $r_2(k)$. The value function and advantage equations are rewritten as:

$$
J(w) = \sum_{i=1}^{M} \left( V^\pi_\theta(x_k) - \left[ \sum_{\tau=t}^{n} \gamma_1^{\tau-t} r_1(u_\tau, x_\tau) + \gamma_2^{\tau-t} r_2(u_\tau, x_\tau) \right] \right)^2
$$

$$
A^\pi_w(x_t, u_t) = \left[ \sum_{\tau=t}^{n} \gamma_1^{\tau-t} r_1(u_\tau, x_\tau) + \gamma_2^{\tau-t} r_2(u_\tau, x_\tau) \right] - V^\pi_w(x_k)
$$

Although simple, this approach provides significant performance improvement, exceeding that of using Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE) both with and without multiple discount rates. Without multiple discount rates, performance was actually worsened by including the terminal reward term.

**Observation Space Design**

The observation given to the agent during learning and testing is deliberately constructed to exclude ground truth state information that would not be available in real missions:

$$
\text{obs} = [v_{error}, r_z, t_{go}]
$$

where $v_{error} = v - v_{targ}$ is the velocity tracking error, $r_z$ is the estimated altitude, and $t_{go}$ is time-to-go. Notably, the lander's translational coordinates (downrange and crossrange position) do not appear in the observation, aside from altitude. This results in a policy with good generalization that can extend to areas of the full state space not experienced during learning. For the Doppler radar altimeter experiment (Experiment 2), the observation consists only of the four altimeter returns, making this a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP) where the current observation alone does not satisfy the Markov property. The recurrent network's hidden state must therefore capture temporal dependencies to infer velocity and position from the history of altimeter readings, similar to how a recursive Bayesian filter infers full state from position-only measurements but with the ability to capture much longer temporal dependencies than a Kalman filter.

**Training Configuration and Hyperparameters**

For Mars landing experiments, each episode begins with initial conditions from Table 3: downrange velocity $[-70, -10] \text{ m/s}$, crossrange velocity $[-30, 30] \text{ m/s}$, elevation velocity $[-90, -70] \text{ m/s}$, downrange position $[0, 2000] \text{ m}$, crossrange position $[-1000, 1000] \text{ m}$, and elevation $[2300, 2400] \text{ m}$. During training, gravity is randomized $\pm 5\%$ of nominal and initial mass is randomized $\pm 10\%$ of nominal. The policy is optimized using PPO and then tested for 10,000 episodes. For the asteroid landing task (Experiment 1), environmental parameters are randomized more extremely: asteroid angular velocity $\omega \in [-10^{-3}, 10^{-3}] \text{ rad/s}$ per axis, gravity $g \in [-10^{-6}, -100 \times 10^{-6}] \text{ m/s}^2$ per axis, and solar radiation pressure $\text{SRP} \in [-10^{-6}, 10^{-6}] \text{ m/s}^2$ per axis (Table 4). The lander wet mass is randomized between $450 \text{ kg}$ and $500 \text{ kg}$.

The recurrent network unroll depth is a critical hyperparameter that was ablated across experiments. The paper compares 1-step, 20-step, 60-step, 120-step, and 200-step RNN configurations, where a T-step RNN means the recurrent layer is unrolled through T timesteps during the forward pass. Learning curves show that the number of unroll steps has large impact on optimization performance, with performance generally increasing as unroll depth increases, implying temporal dependencies span a significant fraction of a single episode. However, for the 120-step case in Experiment 2, optimization initially makes good progress but then stalls, likely due to highly inaccurate altimeter readings at lower altitudes. For Experiment 3 (engine failure), maximum thrust is increased to $24000 \text{ N}$ with failure probability of 0.5 per episode, where downrange or crossrange thrust is limited by factor of 2 and vertical thrust by factor of 1.5. For Experiment 4 (high mass variation), specific impulse is divided by 6, increasing fuel consumption to around $1200 \text{ kg}$ on average with peak of $1600 \text{ kg}$, creating a wet/dry mass ratio of $2000 \text{ kg}/200 \text{ kg}$.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section isolates the specific conceptual and algorithmic contributions that distinguish this work from prior reinforcement learning and guidance literature. While the use of Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) and recurrent networks builds on existing deep RL foundations, the authors introduce novel modifications to handle the specific constraints of spacecraft guidance: sparse terminal rewards, non-Markovian sensor data, and the need for real-time adaptation without explicit system identification.

### Implicit System Identification via Hidden State Dynamics
A fundamental innovation in this work is the method of adaptation. Prior research, such as Reference 2 cited in the Introduction, utilized recurrent neural networks to explicitly learn model parameters (e.g., mass, friction) during execution, which were then fed back into the policy as augmented observations. This paper departs from that approach by leveraging **implicit adaptation**.

*   **Mechanism:** The recurrent policy does not output system parameters. Instead, the hidden state of the Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) evolves to encode unobserved dynamics (such as external forces or current mass) directly within its activation vector. As stated in the Introduction, "the trained policy's hidden state captures unobserved information... as this information is useful in minimizing the cost function."
*   **Significance:** This creates an end-to-end differentiable system where the "system identification" and "control" steps are optimized jointly rather than sequentially. In traditional cascaded systems, errors in parameter estimation propagate to the controller. Here, the policy learns to compensate for dynamics even if the hidden state representation is not physically interpretable, provided it minimizes the loss.
*   **Evidence:** This distinction is critical in **Experiment 1 (Asteroid Landing)**. The Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) policy, which lacks a hidden state, achieves a mean terminal position error of $0.2 \text{ m}$ but uses more fuel ($1.62 \text{ kg}$) compared to the 120-step RNN ($1.43 \text{ kg}$) (Table 7). More importantly, in **Experiment 3 (Engine Failure)**, the MLP policy fails to match the safety margins of the 20-step and 60-step RNNs, which maintain terminal velocities under $1.00 \text{ m/s}$ compared to the MLP's $0.99 \text{ m/s}$ but with significantly higher glideslope variance (Table 12). The recurrent policy adapts to the reduced thrust authority mid-descent, whereas the MLP relies on averaging over possible failure modes during training.

### Multiple Discount Rate Framework for Reward Shaping
The paper introduces a novel modification to the advantage estimation process to resolve a conflict inherent in landing tasks: the need for both dense shaping rewards (to guide learning) and sparse terminal rewards (to define success).

*   **The Conflict:** Standard RL uses a single discount factor $\gamma$. A high $\gamma$ is required for the terminal landing bonus to propagate back to the start of the episode, but a lower $\gamma$ is often preferable for shaping rewards to prevent them from dominating the objective function. The authors note that "without the use of multiple discount rates, the performance was actually worsened by including the terminal reward term."
*   **Innovation:** The authors decompose the reward into $r_1$ (terminal) and $r_2$ (shaping) and apply distinct discount rates $\gamma_1$ and $\gamma_2$ within the value function and advantage calculations (Equations 17a and 17b):
    $$
    A^\pi_w(x_t, u_t) = \left[ \sum_{\tau=t}^{n} \gamma_1^{\tau-t} r_1(u_\tau, x_\tau) + \gamma_2^{\tau-t} r_2(u_\tau, x_\tau) \right] - V^\pi_w(x_k)
    $$
*   **Significance:** This allows the agent to value immediate progress (shaping) differently from final success (terminal). The authors claim this simple modification yields performance "exceeding that of using generalized advantage estimation (GAE) [16] both with and without multiple discount rates." This is a significant algorithmic contribution for sparse-reward control tasks where reward shaping is necessary but risks altering the optimal policy.

### Integrated Guidance and Navigation via Non-Markovian Observations
In **Experiment 2**, the paper demonstrates the ability to perform landing using only Doppler radar altimeter returns, bypassing traditional navigation filters.

*   **Prior Approach:** Standard Guidance, Navigation, and Control (GNC) architectures separate these functions. A navigation filter (e.g., Kalman Filter) estimates position and velocity from sensor data, which are then passed to a guidance law. This requires accurate modeling of sensor noise statistics.
*   **This Work:** The recurrent policy operates directly on raw sensor returns, which do not satisfy the Markov property (a single altimeter reading corresponds to multiple possible positions). The policy learns to infer velocity and position from the *history* of readings stored in its hidden state. The Introduction notes this is "similar to how a recursive Bayesian filter can learn to predict a spacecraft's full state from a history of position only measurements," but with the ability to capture "much longer temporal dependencies."
*   **Evidence:** Table 9 shows that while the MLP policy achieves a mean terminal position error of $131 \text{ m}$, the 200-step RNN reduces this to $59 \text{ m}$ using only altimeter data. This confirms that the recurrent layers are successfully integrating information over time to reconstruct state information that is not present in individual observations.
*   **Nuance:** The authors are critical of this result, noting "you would not want to entrust an expensive rover to this integrated guidance and navigation algorithm" due to outliers (max error $288 \text{ m}$). However, the experiment proves the *capability* of recurrent policies to merge navigation and guidance, potentially reducing system complexity for low-cost missions.

### Critical Assessment of Scalability and Robustness
While the innovations demonstrate clear performance gains, the paper highlights subtle limitations that define the boundary of this approach's applicability.

*   **Unroll Depth Sensitivity:** Performance is highly sensitive to the number of timesteps $T$ the recurrent layer is unrolled during training. In **Experiment 2**, the 120-step RNN optimization "initially makes good progress, but then stalls" (Figure 7 discussion), likely due to inaccurate altimeter readings at lower altitudes corrupting the gradient signal over long sequences. This suggests that while recurrent policies are powerful, they require careful tuning of temporal horizon hyperparameters to avoid vanishing gradient issues or noise accumulation.
*   **Fuel vs. Safety Trade-off:** In **Experiment 2**, the authors explicitly state that "Fuel usage is really not a good measure of performance in this experiment, as it decreases with increased landing velocity." This is a critical insight for interpreting RL results in safety-critical domains: standard efficiency metrics may conflict with safety constraints. The RNN policies often used *more* fuel (e.g., $242 \text{ kg}$ for 200-step RNN vs. $224 \text{ kg}$ for MLP in Table 9) to achieve safer landing velocities, indicating the policy learned to prioritize the terminal reward bonus $\eta$ over the control effort penalty $\beta$.
*   **Generalization Limits:** The policy's ability to generalize relies on the observation space design. By excluding translational coordinates from the observation (Equation 16), the policy learns a relative guidance law. However, the paper notes that when the landing site was moved "further to the south (bottom of DTM)" in Experiment 2, performance degraded. This indicates that while the policy adapts to dynamics, it may still overfit to specific terrain features present during training, limiting true zero-shot generalization to entirely new environments without fine-tuning.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validated their adaptive guidance system across four distinct tasks designed to stress-test different aspects of real-time adaptation. The experimental design is methodical: each task isolates a specific challenge (unknown dynamics, sensor limitations, actuator failure, mass variation) while holding other factors constant. By comparing three policy types (DR/DV, MLP, RNN) across these tasks, the authors build a cumulative case for recurrent architectures in safety-critical guidance.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Baseline Comparisons**

The paper employs three distinct baselines to isolate the contribution of recurrence:

1.  **DR/DV Guidance Law:** A traditional analytic guidance algorithm (Reference 5) that computes thrust commands based on known dynamics. Critically, the authors gave DR/DV access to **ground truth gravitational force** and **lander mass at episode start**—information unavailable to the RL agents. This generous setup makes DR/DV's failures more damning, as it had advantages the learned policies did not.

2.  **MLP Policy:** A non-recurrent neural network with identical architecture to the RNN policy except Layer 2 is feedforward rather than recurrent. This isolates the effect of the hidden state: any performance gap between MLP and RNN must come from temporal memory, not network capacity.

3.  **RNN Policies with Varied Unroll Depth:** The authors ablate the number of timesteps $T$ the recurrent layer is unrolled during the forward pass (1-step, 20-step, 60-step, 120-step, 200-step). A $T$-step RNN means the network processes $T$ consecutive timesteps before updating its hidden state. This tests how much temporal context is needed for adaptation.

**Performance Metrics**

All experiments report four primary metrics over 10,000 test episodes (except where noted):

*   **Terminal Position Error:** Mean ($\mu$), standard deviation ($\sigma$), and maximum (max) in meters. This measures landing accuracy.
*   **Terminal Velocity:** Mean, std, and max in m/s (or cm/s for asteroid landing). This measures landing safety—velocities above thresholds indicate crash landings.
*   **Glideslope:** Mean, std, and minimum in rad/s. The minimum glideslope ensures the lander's velocity is directed predominantly downward at touchdown.
*   **Fuel Consumption:** Mean, std, and max in kg. This measures efficiency, though the authors note fuel can decrease with increased landing velocity (a trade-off discussed below).

**Training Configuration**

For Mars landing experiments, initial conditions are randomized per Table 3: downrange velocity $[-70, -10] \text{ m/s}$, elevation $[2300, 2400] \text{ m}$, with gravity randomized $\pm 5\%$ and mass $\pm 10\%$ of nominal. Each policy is optimized using PPO, then tested for 10,000 episodes. The clipping parameter $\epsilon$ is adjusted to target a KL divergence of $0.001$ between policy updates.

### 5.2 Experiment 1: Asteroid Landing with Unknown Dynamics

This task tests adaptation to completely unknown environmental parameters. At each episode start, asteroid angular velocity $\omega$, gravity $g$, and solar radiation pressure (SRP) are randomly chosen within bounds in Table 4. For example, gravity ranges from $-10^{-6}$ to $-100 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m/s}^2$ per axis—a 100x variation. The lander mass is randomized between $450 \text{ kg}$ and $500 \text{ kg}$.

**Quantitative Results (Table 7)**

| Policy | Terminal Position $\mu$ (m) | Terminal Position max (m) | Terminal Velocity $\mu$ (cm/s) | Fuel $\mu$ (kg) |
|--------|-----------------------------|---------------------------|--------------------------------|-----------------|
| DR/DV  | 1.9                         | **1811**                  | 54                             | 1.66            |
| MLP    | 0.2                         | 0.9                       | 4.1                            | 1.62            |
| RNN 1-step | 0.2                     | 0.7                       | 3.8                            | 1.48            |
| RNN 20-step | 0.2                    | 1.7                       | 4.0                            | 1.42            |
| RNN 60-step | 0.3                    | 1.0                       | 4.2                            | 1.44            |
| RNN 120-step | 0.3                   | 0.9                       | 4.1                            | **1.43**        |

**Critical Observations:**

*   **DR/DV Catastrophic Failure:** The maximum terminal position error of **1811 m** indicates complete mission failure in some episodes. The authors note these failures "typically occur after less than 1000 episodes of testing," suggesting DR/DV cannot handle the randomized dynamics even with ground truth parameters.
*   **RL Policies Succeed:** All RL-derived policies achieve sub-meter mean position error, demonstrating that training with parameter uncertainty produces robust policies.
*   **RNN Fuel Efficiency:** While MLP and RNN achieve similar accuracy, the RNN policies use less fuel. The 20-step RNN uses $1.42 \text{ kg}$ vs. MLP's $1.62 \text{ kg}$—a **12% improvement**. This suggests the recurrent policy adapts its thrust profile to the actual dynamics rather than averaging over possible dynamics.
*   **Diminishing Returns on Unroll Depth:** Performance plateaus after 20 steps. The 120-step RNN does not significantly outperform the 20-step RNN, suggesting the temporal dependencies in this task span roughly 20 timesteps.

**Learning Curves (Figure 4)**

The learning curves show all RL policies converge, but the RNN policies achieve lower variance in terminal position and velocity during training. This indicates more consistent performance across the randomized dynamics distribution.

**Assessment:** This experiment convincingly supports the claim that recurrent policies adapt to unknown dynamics. The DR/DV failure is particularly compelling because it had information advantages. However, the MLP policy's success (sub-meter accuracy) suggests that for this specific task, training with parameter uncertainty alone provides substantial robustness—the RNN's advantage is primarily in fuel efficiency, not safety.

### 5.3 Experiment 2: Mars Landing using Radar Altimeter Observations

This is the most challenging task: the agent receives **only four Doppler radar altimeter readings** as observations, with no access to position or velocity. The altimeter model is intentionally error-prone at lower elevations (Table 8 shows up to 100% miss rate at 100m elevation). This creates a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) where the current observation alone does not satisfy the Markov property.

**Altimeter Error Characteristics (Table 8)**

| Elevation (m) | Mean Error (m) | Std Error (m) | Max Error (m) | Miss Rate (%) |
|---------------|----------------|---------------|---------------|---------------|
| 100           | 2600           | 3200          | 10000         | 61            |
| 400           | 513            | 1500          | 8800          | 22            |
| 500           | 122            | 528           | 4467          | 12            |
| 800           | 4              | 60            | 1300          | 2             |

The error decreases with altitude, but critically, the largest errors occur **near touchdown** when precision matters most. The authors note this is not a bug but a feature to "showcase the ability of a recurrent policy to get remarkably close to a good landing, given the large errors."

**Quantitative Results (Table 9)**

| Policy | Terminal Position $\mu$ (m) | Terminal Position max (m) | Terminal Velocity $\mu$ (m/s) | Fuel $\mu$ (kg) |
|--------|-----------------------------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|-----------------|
| MLP    | 131                         | 796                       | 48                            | 224             |
| RNN 1-step | 114                     | 1359                      | 37                            | 228             |
| RNN 20-step | 78                     | 390                       | 26                            | 234             |
| RNN 120-step | 72                    | 349                       | 28                            | 233             |
| RNN 200-step | **59**                | **288**                   | **23**                        | **242**         |

**Critical Observations:**

*   **RNN Advantage Grows with Unroll Depth:** Unlike Experiment 1, performance continues improving up to 200 steps. The 200-step RNN achieves **55% lower mean position error** than MLP (59m vs. 131m). This confirms that temporal dependencies in this task span a significant fraction of the episode—the policy needs long histories to infer state from noisy altimeter readings.
*   **Fuel-Safety Trade-off:** The 200-step RNN uses **242 kg** fuel vs. MLP's **224 kg**. The authors explicitly state: "Fuel usage is really not a good measure of performance in this experiment, as it decreases with increased landing velocity." The RNN uses more fuel to achieve safer (lower) terminal velocities—a rational trade-off for safety-critical missions.
*   **Large Outliers Persist:** Even the best RNN has a maximum position error of 288m. The authors acknowledge "you would not want to entrust an expensive rover to this integrated guidance and navigation algorithm." This is an important limitation: while recurrence improves average performance, it does not eliminate catastrophic failures in extreme sensor error conditions.

**Learning Curves (Figures 6 and 7)**

Figure 6 (1-step RNN) shows high variance in terminal position throughout training. Figure 7 (120-step RNN) shows initial progress that then stalls. The authors attribute this stall to "highly inaccurate altimeter readings at lower altitudes" corrupting the gradient signal over long sequences. This reveals a subtle limitation: **longer unroll depths increase susceptibility to noisy observations** during training.

**Variant: Target-Pointing Altimeters (Tables 10 and 11)**

In a variation, the altimeter beams are pointed at the target location, and initial condition uncertainty is reduced (Table 10). Results improve dramatically (Table 11):

| Policy | Terminal Position $\mu$ (m) | Terminal Velocity $\mu$ (m/s) |
|--------|-----------------------------|-------------------------------|
| MLP    | 1.4                         | 4.92                          |
| RNN 20-step | **0.3**                | **1.61**                      |
| RNN 120-step | 0.6                   | 2.23                          |

With better sensor geometry, the 20-step RNN achieves sub-meter accuracy. However, the authors note that moving the landing site "further to the south (bottom of DTM)" degraded performance, indicating the policy **overfits to terrain features** present during training. This limits zero-shot generalization to entirely new environments.

**Assessment:** This experiment provides the strongest evidence for recurrent policies in POMDP settings. The 55% improvement in position error is substantial. However, the persistent outliers and terrain overfitting suggest this approach is not yet ready for high-value missions without additional robustness measures (e.g., ensemble policies, uncertainty quantification).

### 5.4 Experiment 3: Mars Landing with Engine Failure

This task tests robustness to actuator failure. At each episode start, with probability $p=0.5$, a random thruster is disabled, reducing downrange/crossrange thrust by factor of 2 and vertical thrust by factor of 1.5. Maximum thrust is increased to $24000 \text{ N}$ to accommodate failure modes.

**Quantitative Results (Table 12)**

| Policy | Terminal Position $\mu$ (m) | Terminal Position max (m) | Terminal Velocity $\mu$ (m/s) | Glideslope min (rad/s) |
|--------|-----------------------------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|------------------------|
| DR/DV  | 123                         | **1061**                  | 20.07                         | 0.96                   |
| MLP    | 0.7                         | 1.8                       | 0.99                          | 4.93                   |
| RNN 1-step | 0.9                     | 1.7                       | 0.95                          | 6.29                   |
| RNN 20-step | **0.3**                | **0.9**                   | **0.99**                      | **11.36**              |
| RNN 60-step | 0.3                    | 1.1                       | 1.00                          | 9.45                   |

**Critical Observations:**

*   **DR/DV Complete Failure:** Maximum position error of **1061 m** and terminal velocity of **20.07 m/s** indicate catastrophic crashes. DR/DV cannot compensate for reduced thrust authority mid-descent.
*   **MLP Marginal Success:** The MLP achieves safe mean terminal velocity (0.99 m/s) but has higher variance than RNN policies. The minimum glideslope of 4.93 rad/s is below the 5 rad/s safety threshold in some episodes.
*   **RNN Safety Margins:** The 20-step and 60-step RNNs achieve minimum glideslope of 11.36 and 9.45 rad/s respectively—well above safety thresholds. This indicates the recurrent policy **adapts its descent profile** when it detects reduced thrust, whereas the MLP averages over failure modes.
*   **Fuel Efficiency:** RNN policies use slightly less fuel (295 kg vs. 302 kg for MLP), suggesting more efficient thrust allocation under failure conditions.

**Assessment:** This experiment convincingly demonstrates real-time adaptation. The key evidence is the glideslope metric: RNN policies maintain safer descent angles, indicating they adjust their trajectory based on detected thrust limitations. The DR/DV failure is particularly instructive—analytic guidance laws require explicit failure detection and re-planning, whereas the RNN adapts implicitly through its hidden state.

### 5.5 Experiment 4: Mars Landing with High Mass Variation

This task tests adaptation to large inertia changes. Specific impulse is divided by 6, increasing fuel consumption to ~1200 kg average (peak 1600 kg). The lander does **not** have access to ground truth mass during descent. Wet/dry mass ratio is 2000 kg / 200 kg—a 10x variation.

**Quantitative Results (Table 13)**

| Policy | Terminal Position $\mu$ (m) | Terminal Position max (m) | Terminal Velocity $\mu$ (m/s) | Terminal Velocity max (m/s) |
|--------|-----------------------------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|-----------------------------|
| DR/DV  | 0.4                         | 19.6                      | 0.63                          | **9.56**                    |
| MLP    | 0.7                         | 3.7                       | 0.92                          | 5.25                        |
| RNN 1-step | 0.4                     | 0.8                       | 0.98                          | 6.48                        |
| RNN 20-step | 0.6                    | 1.0                       | **1.17**                      | **1.36**                    |
| RNN 60-step | 0.6                    | 1.1                       | 1.06                          | 1.21                        |

**Critical Observations:**

*   **DR/DV Unsafe Velocity:** Maximum terminal velocity of **9.56 m/s** exceeds safe landing thresholds. DR/DV's thrust profile assumes nominal mass, leading to under-thrust as mass decreases.
*   **MLP and 1-Step RNN Unsafe:** Both achieve maximum terminal velocities above 5 m/s (5.25 and 6.48 m/s respectively), indicating unsafe landings in some episodes.
*   **20-Step and 60-Step RNN Safe:** Maximum terminal velocities of 1.36 and 1.21 m/s are well within safety limits. The recurrent policy infers mass from the observed acceleration response to thrust commands and adjusts accordingly.
*   **Fuel Consumption:** All policies use similar fuel (~1225-1235 kg), as fuel consumption is dominated by the physics of the high-consumption engine rather than policy efficiency.

**Assessment:** This experiment provides clean evidence that recurrent policies can infer unobserved state variables (mass) from observation-action histories. The MLP's failure (max velocity 5.25 m/s) vs. RNN success (max velocity 1.36 m/s) cannot be explained by network capacity—only by the hidden state's ability to track mass depletion over time.

### 5.6 Overall Assessment of Experimental Claims

**Do the Experiments Support the Paper's Claims?**

The four experiments collectively build a strong case for recurrent policies in adaptive guidance, but with important caveats:

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Conditions |
|-------|-------------------|------------|
| RNN adapts to unknown dynamics | **Strong** (Exp 1, 4) | Requires sufficient unroll depth (20+ steps) |
| RNN handles POMDP observations | **Moderate** (Exp 2) | Large outliers persist; terrain overfitting observed |
| RNN robust to actuator failure | **Strong** (Exp 3) | Clear safety margin improvement over MLP |
| RNN outperforms DR/DV | **Very Strong** (All exp.) | DR/DV fails catastrophically in 3 of 4 tasks |
| More unroll steps = better performance | **Mixed** | Diminishing returns after 20-60 steps; 120-step stalled in Exp 2 |

**Key Limitations Identified:**

1.  **Unroll Depth Sensitivity:** Performance does not monotonically improve with unroll depth. In Experiment 2, the 120-step RNN optimization stalled (Figure 7), suggesting gradient corruption from noisy observations over long sequences.

2.  **Persistent Outliers:** Even the best RNN policies exhibit large maximum errors (e.g., 288m in Exp 2). The authors acknowledge this is unacceptable for high-value missions, indicating the need for additional robustness measures.

3.  **Terrain Overfitting:** In Experiment 2's variant, moving the landing site degraded performance. This suggests the policy learns terrain-specific features rather than purely dynamics-adaptive behavior.

4.  **Fuel-Safety Trade-offs:** In Experiment 2, RNN policies used more fuel to achieve safer landings. This is rational but complicates direct performance comparisons—efficiency metrics may conflict with safety constraints.

5.  **3-DOF Limitation:** All experiments use 3-degree-of-freedom translational dynamics. The Conclusion notes future work will explore 6-DOF environments, where rotational dynamics add complexity.

**Ablation Studies:**

The unroll depth ablation (1, 20, 60, 120, 200 steps) is the paper's primary ablation. Results show:
*   1-step RNN often performs similarly to MLP (no temporal memory benefit)
*   20-60 step RNN achieves best balance of performance and training stability
*   120+ step RNN shows diminishing returns or training instability

This ablation convincingly demonstrates that **temporal context matters**, but more context is not always better.

**Robustness Checks:**

The paper includes several implicit robustness checks:
*   10,000 test episodes per experiment (statistically significant)
*   Randomized initial conditions and environmental parameters
*   Comparison against multiple baselines (DR/DV, MLP, varied RNN depths)

However, the paper does not report:
*   Confidence intervals on metrics
*   Training seed sensitivity (how much results vary across random seeds)
*   Computational cost of longer unroll depths

**Conclusion on Experimental Validity:**

The experiments are well-designed to isolate the contribution of recurrence, and the quantitative results strongly support the core claim: **recurrent policies adapt to unknown dynamics better than non-recurrent policies**. The DR/DV baseline failures are particularly compelling, as they demonstrate the limitations of analytic guidance in uncertain environments.

However, the persistent outliers and terrain overfitting indicate this approach is not yet production-ready for high-value missions. The authors are appropriately cautious in their claims, noting limitations rather than overstating capabilities. Future work addressing 6-DOF dynamics, sensor fusion, and uncertainty quantification would strengthen the practical applicability of this approach.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages of recurrent policies over traditional guidance laws and non-recurrent neural networks, this section critically examines the boundaries of the approach's applicability. Understanding these limitations is essential for determining when this method is appropriate for real missions versus when traditional approaches remain preferable.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions

The adaptive guidance system relies on several assumptions that constrain its deployment scenarios:

**Simulation-to-Reality Gap**

All experiments are conducted entirely in simulation. The paper presents no hardware-in-the-loop testing, flight data, or even high-fidelity sensor models beyond the intentionally simplified Doppler altimeter. This creates a significant assumption: that policies trained in simulation will transfer to physical hardware without degradation. The authors acknowledge this implicitly by noting future work will explore "more realistic 6-DOF environments" and "different sensor models" (Conclusion, Paragraph 2). For safety-critical spacecraft missions, this simulation-only validation is insufficient for flight qualification.

**Training Distribution Coverage**

The recurrent policy's adaptation capability is bounded by the distribution of environments seen during training. The Introduction states the policy is "trained over a wide range of randomized system parameters" (Introduction, Paragraph 2), but this raises a critical question: what happens when the actual environment falls outside the training distribution? 

The terrain overfitting observed in Experiment 2 provides evidence of this limitation. When the landing site was moved "further to the south (bottom of DTM)," performance degraded despite the policy successfully learning to land at the original site (Experiment 2, Paragraph 4). This suggests the policy learned terrain-specific features rather than purely dynamics-adaptive behavior. The assumption that training randomization covers all plausible mission scenarios may not hold for missions visiting entirely new celestial bodies.

**No Online Learning During Deployment**

The paper explicitly states that "when the policy is deployed in the field, exploration is turned off, as exploration gets quite expensive using an actual lander" (Reinforcement Learning Overview, Paragraph 2). Instead, the proposed approach for continuous improvement is to "have the lander send back telemetry data, which can be used to improve the environment's dynamics model, and update the policy via simulated experience."

This creates a critical assumption: the policy must be robust enough to handle all scenarios encountered during its initial deployment, with improvements only available for future missions. Unlike some adaptive control methods that update parameters in real-time, this recurrent policy's weights are "frozen" after training (Introduction, Paragraph 4). Only the hidden state adapts mid-flight, which is more limited than full parameter adaptation.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

Several important mission scenarios are not addressed in the four experiments:

**Six-Degree-of-Freedom Dynamics**

All experiments use 3-DOF translational dynamics, explicitly excluding rotational dynamics. The Conclusion acknowledges this limitation directly: "Future work will explore the adaptability of recurrent policies in more realistic 6-DOF environments" (Conclusion, Paragraph 2). This is a significant gap because:

*   Rotational dynamics couple with translational motion through thrust vector direction
*   Attitude control failures are common failure modes not captured in 3-DOF
*   The observation space would need to include orientation information, increasing complexity

The 3-DOF simplification makes the control problem more tractable but limits claims about applicability to actual landing vehicles, which must control both position and attitude simultaneously.

**Multi-Phase Descent Profiles**

The experiments model only the powered descent phase, starting from the initial conditions in Table 3 (approximately 2300-2400m elevation for Mars). Real planetary landings include multiple phases:

*   Hypersonic entry (aerodynamic forces dominate)
*   Parachute descent (highly nonlinear drag)
*   Powered descent (the phase modeled in this paper)
*   Terminal hover and touchdown

The policy is not trained to handle transitions between these phases or the distinct dynamics of each. The assumption that powered descent can be treated in isolation may not hold for missions where phase transitions introduce significant uncertainty.

**Sensor Failure and Degradation**

While Experiment 2 tests performance with noisy altimeter readings, it does not address sensor failure modes such as:

*   Complete sensor dropout (no readings for multiple timesteps)
*   Biased sensor readings (systematic errors rather than random noise)
*   Conflicting sensor data (when multiple sensors disagree)

The altimeter model's errors are random and characterized in Table 8, but real sensor failures often exhibit systematic biases that could mislead the recurrent policy's hidden state in ways not seen during training.

**Multi-Vehicle and Cooperative Scenarios**

The Introduction mentions orbital refueling as a motivating example where "the inertia tensor of each of the two spacecraft will change significantly as fuel is transferred" (Introduction, Paragraph 1). However, none of the four experiments test multi-vehicle scenarios or cooperative maneuvers. The adaptive guidance demonstrated is for single-agent control only.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

**Unroll Depth and Training Time**

The paper demonstrates that performance is sensitive to the number of timesteps $T$ the recurrent layer is unrolled during training. However, longer unroll depths impose computational costs that are not quantified:

*   Memory requirements scale with unroll depth (hidden states must be stored for all $T$ steps)
*   Forward pass time increases proportionally with $T$
*   Backpropagation through time becomes more expensive and prone to gradient issues

The stalling observed in Experiment 2's 120-step RNN optimization (Experiment 2, Paragraph 3) suggests there are practical limits to unroll depth. The authors note optimization "initially makes good progress, but then stalls, likely due to the highly inaccurate altimeter readings at lower altitudes." This indicates that beyond a certain unroll depth, noisy observations corrupt the gradient signal faster than the policy can learn useful temporal dependencies.

**Batch Size Constraints**

The implementation requires that "rollouts must be padded so that $m/T$ is an integer" (Recurrent Policy and Value Function Approximator, Paragraph 3), where $m$ is batch size and $T$ is unroll depth. This creates a constraint: larger unroll depths require proportionally larger batch sizes to maintain computational efficiency. For missions with limited training compute resources, this could restrict the achievable unroll depth.

**Real-Time Inference Latency**

The paper does not report inference latency for the trained policies. For spacecraft guidance, control commands must be generated within strict timing constraints (typically milliseconds to seconds depending on vehicle dynamics). Recurrent networks require sequential processing of the hidden state, which could introduce latency compared to feedforward MLP policies. While the hidden state evolution is the mechanism enabling adaptation, it also adds computational overhead that must be characterized for flight hardware.

### 6.4 Safety and Robustness Concerns

**Persistent Catastrophic Outliers**

Despite improved mean performance, all RNN policies exhibit large maximum errors that would be unacceptable for high-value missions:

| Experiment | Best RNN Max Position Error | Acceptable for Mission? |
|------------|----------------------------|------------------------|
| Exp 1 (Asteroid) | 0.9 m | Likely acceptable |
| Exp 2 (Altimeter) | 288 m | **Not acceptable** |
| Exp 3 (Engine Failure) | 1.1 m | Likely acceptable |
| Exp 4 (Mass Variation) | 1.1 m | Likely acceptable |

The authors are candid about this limitation in Experiment 2: "you would not want to entrust an expensive rover to this integrated guidance and navigation algorithm" (Experiment 2, Paragraph 3). This raises a fundamental question about the approach's readiness for safety-critical applications. The recurrent policy improves average performance but does not eliminate tail-risk failures.

**No Uncertainty Quantification**

The policy outputs actions without any measure of confidence or uncertainty. In safety-critical domains, knowing when the policy is uncertain is as important as the action itself. Traditional guidance laws provide analytic guarantees within their validity envelopes, and Kalman filters provide covariance estimates for state uncertainty. The recurrent policy provides neither:

*   No confidence bounds on landing accuracy
*   No indication when the environment is outside the training distribution
*   No mechanism to request human intervention when uncertain

This limits the policy's use to missions where some failure rate is acceptable, or where a separate monitoring system provides safety assurance.

**Reward Function Engineering Burden**

The reward function required significant engineering effort, including:

*   Gaze heuristic-inspired velocity shaping (Equations 14a-14e)
*   Multiple discount rate framework (Equations 17a-17b)
*   Careful tuning of coefficients $\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$, $\eta$ (Table 2)

The authors note they "experimented with several potential functions with no success" before settling on the gaze heuristic approach (RL Problem Formulation, Paragraph 3). This is not pure end-to-end learning—the policy's behavior is heavily shaped by human-designed reward functions. For new mission types, this engineering burden must be repeated, limiting the "learn to learn" claim of meta-learning.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Future Work

Several important questions remain unanswered:

**Generalization to Novel Celestial Bodies**

The terrain overfitting in Experiment 2 suggests the policy may not generalize to entirely new environments. Can a single policy trained on Mars terrain data land on an asteroid it has never seen? The paper does not test cross-body generalization, which is critical for missions visiting multiple targets.

**Sample Efficiency**

The paper reports testing over 10,000 episodes but does not report the number of training episodes required for convergence. Deep RL methods are notoriously sample-inefficient, often requiring millions of episodes. For spacecraft applications where high-fidelity simulation is computationally expensive, this could be a practical barrier. The computational cost of training is not discussed.

**Interpretability and Debugging**

When the policy fails (e.g., the 288m outlier in Experiment 2), there is no mechanism to diagnose why. The hidden state that enables adaptation is not interpretable—the authors note it "captures unobserved information" but do not analyze what information is actually encoded. For flight certification, this lack of interpretability is a significant barrier.

**Integration with Traditional GNC Architectures**

The paper positions the recurrent policy as a replacement for traditional guidance laws, but does not explore hybrid architectures where:

*   A traditional guidance law provides baseline commands
*   The recurrent policy provides corrections for unmodeled dynamics
*   A separate navigation filter provides state estimates

Such hybrid approaches might combine the interpretability and guarantees of traditional methods with the adaptability of learned policies.

**Long-Horizon Missions**

All experiments cover single descent episodes lasting seconds to minutes. The approach has not been tested on long-horizon missions (days to months) where:

*   Hidden state drift could accumulate errors
*   Environmental conditions could change slowly over time
*   Multiple landing attempts might be required

The recurrent architecture's ability to maintain useful hidden states over extended periods is untested.

### 6.6 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes the key trade-offs inherent in this approach:

| Trade-off | Benefit | Cost |
|-----------|---------|------|
| Recurrent vs. MLP Policy | Real-time adaptation to unknown dynamics | Increased computational cost, training complexity |
| Simulation Training | Safe exploration of failure modes | Simulation-to-reality gap |
| Frozen Weights at Deployment | Predictable behavior, no online learning risks | Cannot adapt to truly novel scenarios post-training |
| Raw Sensor Observations | Integrated guidance and navigation | No uncertainty quantification, terrain overfitting |
| Multiple Discount Rates | Better terminal reward propagation | Additional hyperparameter tuning required |
| 3-DOF Dynamics | Tractable training and analysis | Limited applicability to real 6-DOF vehicles |

The recurrent policy approach represents a promising direction for adaptive spacecraft guidance, particularly for missions where environmental parameters are highly uncertain. However, the persistent outliers, lack of uncertainty quantification, and simulation-only validation indicate this method is not yet ready for high-value missions without additional safety mechanisms. The approach is best suited for low-cost missions where some failure rate is acceptable, or as a backup system alongside traditional guidance laws.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper represents a pivot point in spacecraft Guidance, Navigation, and Control (GNC) design, moving from explicit model-based control toward implicit, learning-based adaptation. By demonstrating that a recurrent policy can outperform traditional guidance laws in environments with unknown dynamics, actuator failure, and non-Markovian observations, the authors challenge the long-held assumption that robust control requires accurate system identification. This section explores how this work reshapes the field, identifies critical follow-up research required to mature the technology, and provides practical guidance for engineers considering integration of these methods.

### 7.1 Shifting the GNC Paradigm

The primary implication of this work is the validation of **implicit system identification** within the control policy itself. Traditionally, adaptive control systems operate in two distinct stages: first, a system identification module estimates parameters (like mass or gravity); second, a controller uses these estimates to compute commands. This separation introduces latency and potential error propagation—if the estimator fails, the controller fails.

This paper demonstrates that a recurrent policy trained via reinforcement meta-learning collapses these stages into a single end-to-end function. As noted in the Introduction, the policy's hidden state captures unobserved information like external forces and lander mass without explicitly outputting them. This has three profound implications for the field:

*   **Reduced Architecture Complexity:** In Experiment 2, the agent successfully landed using only Doppler radar altimeter returns, bypassing the need for a separate navigation filter (like a Kalman Filter) to estimate position and velocity. This suggests that for low-cost missions, integrated guidance and navigation could reduce computational load and software complexity.
*   **Robustness to Unmodeled Dynamics:** The failure of the DR/DV guidance law in Experiment 1 (asteroid landing) and Experiment 3 (engine failure), despite having access to ground truth parameters at episode start, highlights the fragility of analytic laws when dynamics vary *during* execution. The recurrent policy's ability to adapt mid-descent implies that future missions could operate with less precise pre-mission modeling, potentially reducing mission design costs.
*   **Meta-Learning as a Standard Tool:** By framing the problem as meta-learning (learning to adapt to novel MDPs), the paper positions reinforcement learning not just as an optimizer for known tasks, but as a method for generating generalizable control strategies. This shifts the focus from tuning gains for a specific vehicle to training policies across distributions of vehicles and environments.

### 7.2 Critical Follow-Up Research

While the results are promising, the limitations identified in Section 6 point to specific research vectors required to transition this technology from simulation to flight. The Conclusion explicitly outlines two immediate next steps: exploring **6-DOF environments** and **different sensor models** (Conclusion, Paragraph 2). Building on this, the following areas require investigation:

*   **6-DOF Dynamics and Attitude Coupling:** All experiments in this paper use 3-DOF translational dynamics. Real spacecraft must control attitude (orientation) simultaneously with position. In 6-DOF, thrust direction is coupled with vehicle rotation, and inertia tensor changes (e.g., during fuel slosh or docking) affect rotational dynamics. Future work must verify if recurrent policies can handle the increased state space and the coupling between rotational and translational motion without exponential increases in training time.
*   **Uncertainty Quantification and Safety Guarantees:** As noted in Section 6, the policy provides no confidence bounds on its actions. For high-value missions, a "black box" controller is unacceptable. Follow-up research should explore **Bayesian Reinforcement Learning** or **ensemble policies** to estimate epistemic uncertainty. If the policy detects it is operating outside its training distribution (e.g., sensor errors exceeding Table 8 limits), it should be able to trigger a safe-mode or hand off to a traditional controller.
*   **Sim-to-Real Transfer Techniques:** The paper relies entirely on simulation. To deploy on hardware, researchers must address the **reality gap**—discrepancies between simulated physics and real-world hardware (e.g., motor latency, sensor bias). Techniques like **domain randomization** (already partially used here for dynamics) should be extended to sensor noise models and actuator dynamics. The authors' note that performance degraded when moving the landing site in Experiment 2 suggests that terrain-specific overfitting is a risk; future work should test transfer across entirely different digital terrain maps.
*   **Long-Horizon Temporal Dependencies:** In Experiment 2, optimization stalled for the 120-step RNN due to noisy gradients (Figure 7 discussion). This suggests limits to how far back the policy can effectively "remember" in high-noise regimes. Research into **attention mechanisms** or **hierarchical RL** could help the policy focus on relevant historical information without suffering from vanishing gradients over long episodes.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

Based on the experimental results, this adaptive guidance approach is best suited for specific mission profiles where uncertainty is high and cost constraints allow for some risk.

*   **Small Body Exploration (Asteroids/Comets):** Experiment 1 demonstrated success in environments with unknown gravity and rotation. This is directly applicable to missions like OSIRIS-REx or future asteroid hopping rovers, where gravitational fields are poorly characterized prior to arrival. The policy's ability to adapt to random $\omega$ and $g$ (Table 4) reduces the need for lengthy characterization orbits.
*   **Redundant Thruster Systems:** Experiment 3 showed robustness to engine failure. This approach could be integrated into launch vehicles or landers with redundant thruster configurations (like MSL). Instead of designing explicit fault-detection-and-isolation (FDI) logic for every failure mode, a recurrent policy could learn to compensate for thrust loss implicitly, provided it is trained on those failure modes.
*   **Low-Cost Planetary Landers:** The integrated guidance and navigation demonstrated in Experiment 2 suggests applicability for CubeSat-class landers where payload mass and computational power are limited. By removing the need for high-precision navigation sensors (relying instead on simple altimeters), mission costs could be reduced.
*   **On-Orbit Servicing and Refueling:** The Introduction cites orbital refueling as a motivating example due to changing inertia tensors. While not explicitly tested in the four experiments, the meta-learning framework is theoretically well-suited for this task. Future validation should focus on docking maneuvers where the combined inertia of two spacecraft changes dynamically.

### 7.4 Integration and Reproducibility Guidance

For engineers considering adopting this approach, the following guidelines summarize when to prefer this method over alternatives, based on the trade-offs analyzed in Sections 5 and 6.

*   **When to Use Recurrent RL Guidance:**
    *   **High Uncertainty:** Use when environmental parameters (gravity, wind, mass) are unknown or time-varying beyond the tolerance of traditional robust control.
    *   **Sensor Limitations:** Use when only non-Markovian sensors (e.g., altimeters, cameras) are available, and building a separate navigation filter is infeasible.
    *   **Cost-Sensitive Missions:** Use when the cost of mission failure is balanced against the cost of extensive modeling and sensor suites (e.g., commercial smallsat missions).
    *   **Actuator Redundancy:** Use when the vehicle has redundant control authority and must tolerate partial failures without explicit reconfiguration.

*   **When to Stick with Traditional Methods (DR/DV):**
    *   **High-Value Missions:** For crewed missions or billion-dollar science orbiters, the lack of interpretability and uncertainty quantification in RL policies is currently a disqualifier.
    *   **Strict Certification Requirements:** If the mission requires formal verification of stability margins, analytic control laws are currently the only viable path.
    *   **Well-Characterized Environments:** If the environment is known precisely (e.g., landing on Earth or a well-mapped Moon site), traditional guidance offers provable optimality without the training overhead.

*   **Reproducibility Tips:**
    *   **Reward Engineering is Critical:** As detailed in Section 3, the gaze-heuristic reward shaping (Equations 14a-14e) was essential for convergence. Practitioners should not expect raw sparse rewards to work; significant effort must be invested in shaping functions that guide the agent toward safe states.
    *   **Multiple Discount Rates:** Implement the multiple discount rate framework (Equations 17a-17b). The authors note that without this, performance worsened when including terminal rewards. This is a key hyperparameter for tasks with sparse terminal bonuses.
    *   **Unroll Depth Tuning:** Do not assume "more memory is better." The ablation in Experiment 2 showed that 120-step RNNs can stall due to noise. Start with shorter unroll depths (20-60 steps) and increase only if temporal dependencies are not being captured.
    *   **Parallel Training Implementation:** To make training feasible, implement the hidden state injection technique described in Section 3.3. Without storing and re-injecting hidden states during batched forward passes, training recurrent policies will be prohibitively slow.

By addressing the limitations in uncertainty quantification and 6-DOF dynamics, this adaptive guidance framework has the potential to become a standard tool for autonomous spacecraft operating in the unknown environments that define the next frontier of space exploration.
