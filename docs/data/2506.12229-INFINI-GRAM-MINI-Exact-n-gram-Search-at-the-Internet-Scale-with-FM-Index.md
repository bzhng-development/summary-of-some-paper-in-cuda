## 1. Executive Summary

`INFINI-GRAM MINI` solves the prohibitive storage overhead of exact-match search engines by implementing an `FM-index` system that compresses indexes to 44% of the original corpus size while achieving 18× faster indexing speed than prior best implementations (Table 1). The system scales to 83TB of Internet text including Common Crawl and DCLM-baseline, enabling large-scale analysis that reveals core benchmarks like `GSM8K` are up to 74.2% contaminated in recent crawls (Table 2). This matters because it provides the first feasible tool to audit petabyte-level training data for leakage that could artificially inflate language model performance estimates.

## 2. Context and Motivation

The rapid scaling of language model (LM) pretraining has created a critical dependency on massive text corpora, often sourced from the public Internet (e.g., Common Crawl). As these models become integral to downstream applications, understanding the composition of their training data is no longer optional—it is a requirement for safety, copyright compliance, and accurate evaluation. However, the sheer scale of this data creates a fundamental technical bottleneck: **how do we efficiently search petabytes of text without requiring petabytes of storage?**

### The Problem: Storage Overhead in Exact-Match Search
To audit training data or detect contamination, researchers need **exact-match search** capabilities. This means counting how many times a specific string appears in a corpus and retrieving the documents containing it. 

A naive approach—scanning the raw text for every query—has a time complexity of $O(n)$, where $n$ is the corpus size. For petabyte-scale data, this is computationally impractical. The standard solution is to build an **index**, a data structure that organizes the text to allow faster lookups. However, traditional indexing techniques introduce a **storage multiplier**: the index size is often larger than the original text itself.

This creates a paradox: to analyze a 1 petabyte (PB) corpus, you might need 2 PB to 29 PB of storage just for the index. This storage overhead is the specific gap this paper addresses. As noted in the Introduction, prior systems render it "impractical to apply them to petabyte-level corpora" due to these multipliers.

### Why This Matters: The Contamination Crisis
The motivation for solving this storage problem is driven by the **benchmark contamination crisis**. When evaluation benchmarks (tests used to measure LM intelligence) appear in the training data, models can memorize answers rather than learning reasoning skills. This leads to inflated performance metrics.

*   **Real-world impact:** If a model scores highly on a math benchmark because it memorized the questions from its training data, developers may falsely believe the model is capable of complex reasoning.
*   **Scale requirement:** Contamination is not static; new benchmarks are released, and new data crawls are published constantly. As stated in Section 4, prior contamination studies were limited to smaller corpora (up to 12TB). To truly understand the risk, we need to search the largest available corpora (e.g., full Common Crawl snapshots) against the latest benchmarks.

Without a storage-efficient index, researchers cannot scale contamination analysis to match the scale of modern training datasets.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations
Before `INFINI-GRAM MINI`, three main approaches existed for large-scale exact-match search. Each fails to meet the storage efficiency required for petabyte-scale auditing.

1.  **Suffix Automata:** 
    *   **Method:** A graph-based structure representing all substrings of the text.
    *   **Prior Work:** Merrill et al. (2024) indexed 1.3TB of text.
    *   **Limitation:** Extremely high storage overhead. The paper cites a **storage multiplier of 29×**. Indexing 1PB of text would require 29PB of storage.
2.  **Suffix Arrays:** 
    *   **Method:** An array of integers representing the starting positions of all suffixes sorted lexicographically.
    *   **Prior Work:** Liu et al. (2024) (the original `INFINI-GRAM`) indexed 12TB of text.
    *   **Limitation:** Moderate storage overhead. The paper cites a **storage multiplier of 6×**. While faster than automata, it still expands the data footprint significantly.
3.  **Proprietary Search Engines (e.g., ElasticSearch):** 
    *   **Method:** Inverted indexes optimized for keyword search.
    *   **Prior Work:** Elazar et al. (2024) indexed 35TB of text.
    *   **Limitation:** Lower but still prohibitive overhead. The paper cites a **storage multiplier of about 2×**. Additionally, these systems are often optimized for token-based search rather than exact byte-level matching required for contamination detection.

A fourth approach, the **FM-index**, existed but was not utilized at this scale.
*   **Method:** A compressed full-text index based on the Burrows-Wheeler Transform (BWT). It is widely used in bioinformatics for genome sequencing.
*   **Prior Work:** Bevilacqua et al. (2022) applied it to only a **13.4 GB dataset**.
*   **Limitation:** While theoretically compact, existing implementations (like SDSL) were too slow and memory-intensive to construct indexes for terabyte-scale natural language data. The Introduction notes that prior FM-index work was "not yet used for natural language data at scale."

### Positioning of INFINI-GRAM MINI
`INFINI-GRAM MINI` positions itself as the bridge between the **compression efficiency of bioinformatics tools** and the **scale requirements of NLP**.

*   **Algorithmic Choice:** Unlike the original `INFINI-GRAM` (which used suffix arrays), this work adopts the **FM-index**. As illustrated in **Figure 1**, this choice reduces the index size to **7% compared to a canonical suffix array index**. Practically, the system achieves a storage multiplier of **0.44×**, meaning the index is less than half the size of the raw text.
*   **Engineering Contribution:** The paper argues that the FM-index was previously impractical due to construction speed and memory usage. `INFINI-GRAM MINI` contributes a highly parallelized construction pipeline that achieves an **18× speedup** and **3.2× reduction in RAM** compared to the best existing FM-index implementation (SDSL) (Section 1).
*   **Operational Mode:** Unlike prior in-memory systems, `INFINI-GRAM MINI` is designed to work with **on-disk indexes**. This reduces query-time RAM requirements to a "negligible amount" (~30 MB), allowing massive indexes to be queried on standard hardware without loading the entire structure into memory (Section 3.2).

By combining the FM-index's theoretical compression with systems-level optimizations for parallelism and disk I/O, this work enables the first feasible exact-match search engine for Internet-scale corpora (83TB indexed in this work, with a path to 1PB). This directly enables the large-scale contamination analysis presented in Section 4, which would be storage-prohibitive using prior suffix array or automaton-based methods.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems paper that introduces a scalable implementation of the FM-index data structure for exact-match search over petabyte-scale text corpora. The core idea is to combine compression techniques from bioinformatics with parallelized engineering optimizations to make full-text indexing feasible at Internet scale.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

`INFINI-GRAM MINI` is a search engine system that builds compressed indexes over massive text corpora, enabling users to count string occurrences and retrieve matching documents without loading the entire corpus into memory. It solves the storage overhead problem by using the FM-index data structure, which simultaneously compresses and indexes text, achieving an index size that is only 44% of the original corpus rather than 2× to 29× larger as with prior methods.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The system consists of three major components that operate in sequence. First, the **Preprocessing Module** takes raw text documents, encodes them as UTF-8 bytes, concatenates them with special boundary markers (`\xff`), and partitions the result into shards of approximately 700GB each. Second, the **Index Construction Pipeline** processes each shard through parallelized stages: building the suffix array and Burrows-Wheeler Transform, constructing the Huffman-shaped wavelet tree, and sampling the suffix array and inverse suffix array at configurable rates. Third, the **Query Engine** loads the resulting index files as memory-mapped disk files and executes three operations—`find` for counting occurrences, `locate` for mapping index positions to text positions, and `reconstruct` for retrieving document text—while keeping RAM usage at approximately 30 MB regardless of corpus size.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first explain the FM-index data structure itself, since all subsequent engineering decisions depend on understanding how this compression scheme works.
- We then detail the index construction pipeline, including the parallelization strategy that achieves the 18× speedup over prior implementations.
- We next describe the querying mechanism, explaining how the system trades query latency for storage efficiency through on-disk operation.
- We cover the key hyperparameters (sampling rates, shard sizes) and their impact on the storage-latency trade-off.
- We conclude with the design choices that distinguish this implementation from both prior FM-index systems and the original `INFINI-GRAM`.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**The FM-index data structure forms the algorithmic foundation of the system.** The FM-index is a compressed full-text index that enables pattern matching without storing the original text in uncompressed form. It achieves this through two core components: a sampled suffix array and a compressed representation of the Burrows-Wheeler Transform. To understand why this matters, consider that a canonical suffix array stores the starting position of every suffix of the text in lexicographic order, requiring $O(n \log n)$ space or approximately $5n$ bytes for strings up to one trillion characters (Section 2.1). The FM-index reduces this by sampling the suffix array at regular intervals and storing only every $a$-th entry, where $a$ is a tunable parameter. When the system needs to access an unsampled entry during querying, it recovers the value by traversing the Burrows-Wheeler Transform data structure, which we explain next.

**The Burrows-Wheeler Transform enables reversible text compression through character clustering.** The BWT rearranges the original text $T$ (with a termination symbol `$`) into a permutation $L$ where $L[i] = T[\text{SA}[i] - 1]$ if $\text{SA}[i] > 0$, or $L[i] = \$$ otherwise (Section 2.1). Intuitively, $L$ is constructed by taking the character that precedes each suffix when all suffixes are sorted in lexicographic order, as illustrated in **Figure 2**. A key property of the BWT is the Last-to-First (LF) mapping, which maps the $i$-th occurrence of a symbol $c$ in $L$ to the $i$-th occurrence of $c$ in $F$, where $F[i] = T[\text{SA}[i]]$. The LF mapping is defined as:

$$\text{LF}(i) = C[c] + \text{rank}(c, i)$$

where $C[c]$ is the number of symbols in $L$ lexicographically smaller than $c$, and $\text{rank}(c, i)$ counts the occurrences of $c$ in $L[0...i]$. This property allows the system to traverse the text in reverse order, which is essential for both finding patterns and reconstructing text from the index.

**Huffman-shaped wavelet trees compress the BWT while supporting efficient rank operations.** Storing $L$ directly would require $n \log_2 \sigma$ bits, where $\sigma$ is the alphabet size. To compress further, the system uses a wavelet tree that hierarchically partitions the alphabet, with each non-leaf node storing a bitvector indicating whether each position in $L$ belongs to the left or right subtree. A Huffman-shaped wavelet tree optimizes this hierarchy by grouping symbols based on their frequencies in $L$, achieving a storage cost of $(nH_0 + 2\sigma \log n)$ bits, where $H_0$ is the zeroth-order entropy of $L$ (Section 2.1). For natural language text, $H_0 \approx 0.26 \log_2 \sigma$, which is significantly smaller than storing $L$ directly. Beyond compression, the wavelet tree efficiently supports two operations crucial to LF mapping: $\text{rank}(c, \ell)$ counts occurrences of character $c$ in $L[0...\ell-1]$, and $\text{select}(c, m)$ finds the position of the $m$-th occurrence of $c$ in $L$, both with time complexity $O(H_0)$.

**The inverse suffix array enables text reconstruction from arbitrary positions.** The FM-index stores a sampled version of the inverse suffix array (ISA), defined as $\text{ISA}[j] = i$ if $\text{SA}[i] = j$ (Section 2.1). When reconstructing text from a given position, ISA identifies the corresponding rank in the suffix array to start reconstruction. The system uses a different sampling rate $b$ for ISA than for SA, where $b$ can be larger than $a$ since ISA is accessed less frequently during querying.

**Index construction is parallelized across four distinct stages to achieve the 18× speedup.** The system adapts components from three prior works: Liu et al. (2024) for building the suffix array and BWT, Labeit et al. (2017) for building the wavelet tree, and SDSL for sampling SA and creating ISA (Section 3.1). All these steps were single-threaded in the original SDSL implementation, and parallelizing them is the primary source of the performance improvement. The paper measured indexing time on an 8.7 GB corpus from DCLM-baseline: SDSL required 5,847 seconds and 74,807 MB of peak RAM, whereas `INFINI-GRAM MINI` completed in 324 seconds with 23,742 MB peak RAM (Section 3.1). **Table 3** in Appendix B provides a stepwise breakdown showing that suffix array construction is the most time-consuming step, scaling super-linearly with text duplication levels.

**Preprocessing converts document collections into a single byte string suitable for FM-index construction.** The FM-index is designed to work on a single string, but text corpora consist of many documents. The system encodes all documents as UTF-8 bytes and concatenates them with the `\xff` byte (which is not used by UTF-8) to mark document boundaries (Section 3.1). Working on UTF-8 bytes rather than characters keeps the alphabet size small at $\sigma = 256$, which improves compression efficiency. Alongside the text index, the system stores a text offset file that records the starting position of each document, enabling retrieval of whole documents and their metadata. The system also indexes document metadata in compressed form to make metadata searchable.

**Partitioning into shards enables indexing with limited RAM per node and embarrassingly parallel construction.** For large corpora, the system partitions the data into multiple shards and indexes each shard independently (Section 3.1). Searching across multiple shards produces the same result as searching a single larger index, but this approach allows building indexes with limited RAM per node. With CPU nodes having 128 vCPUs and 2 TiB RAM, each shard can be as large as 700 GB and indexed in 12–19 hours (Section 3.1). **Table 1** reports indexing times for all corpora: the total 83 TB was indexed in 98.8 CPU node-days across 137 shards. The paper estimates that indexing the full Common Crawl (approximately 1 PB) would require 1,200 node-days, or 19 hours if parallelized across 1,500 nodes.

**Sampling rates are chosen empirically to balance storage savings against query latency.** The system samples every $a = 32$ entries of SA and every $b = 64$ entries of ISA (Section 3.1). This yields indexes with 0.44× the size of the corpora, as shown in **Table 1**. Conceptually, if $a$ and $b$ were set very large, the SA and ISA would have negligible size, and the index could be as small as 0.26× the corpus size. However, larger sampling rates increase query latency because more LF mapping iterations are required to recover unsampled positions. The chosen values represent a practical trade-off that keeps query latency in the seconds range while maintaining substantial storage savings.

**Querying operates entirely on-disk to minimize RAM requirements.** Unlike prior systems that load indexes into memory, `INFINI-GRAM MINI` keeps all index files on disk as memory-mapped files (Section 3.2). This requires only minimal CPU and RAM: loading all indexes uses approximately 30 MB of RAM and 1 vCPU in the reported setting. The trade-off is that querying is slower than in-memory systems due to disk I/O latency. Query latency is largely determined by disk I/O performance, and the paper benchmarks using Google Cloud Platform SSD disks with 80,000 IOPS and 1,200 MB/s throughput (Section 3.2).

**Counting queries use the find operation with parallelization across shards.** To count occurrences of a query string $Q$, the system uses the FM-index's `find` operation, which computes the range in the suffix array corresponding to all occurrences of $Q$ through backward search (Section 3.2 and Appendix A). The operation iterates through symbols in $Q$ in reverse order, narrowing the range using the character table $C$ and the rank operation of the BWT's wavelet tree. The time complexity of `find` is $O(|Q|H_0)$, where $|Q|$ is the query length. The system parallelizes this operation across all $k$ shards and reports the sum of counts. The total number of random disk reads is $O(k|Q|H_0)$, and if disk I/O throughput is high enough, query latency can approach $O(|Q|H_0)$.

**Document retrieval combines find, locate, and reconstruct operations with chunked parallelization.** To retrieve documents containing a query string, the system first uses `find` to get the suffix array range corresponding to the query (Section 3.2). Each element in this range indicates one appearance of the query in the corpus. For each element, the `locate` operation finds the position of the match in the original text, then a binary search in the text offset file identifies the enclosing document boundaries, and finally `reconstruct` retrieves the document text. The number of random disk reads for `locate` is $O(aH_0)$, and for `reconstruct` is $O((b + d)H_0)$ where $d$ is the document length (Section 3.2). The system parallelizes reconstruction by dividing document text into up to $t = 10$ chunks, or chunks of length 100 if $d < 1000$. Retrieving a single document is parallelized across $t$ threads, and this can be further parallelized for multiple documents.

**Query latency benchmarks demonstrate second-level performance for both query types.** **Table 4** in Appendix C reports detailed latency measurements. For counting, short queries ($|Q| \leq 10$) are handled within 0.4 seconds for all corpora, while longer queries ($|Q| = 1000$) take 8 seconds for CC-2025-05 and 25 seconds for DCLM-baseline (Section 3.2). The difference is caused by the different number of shards in each corpus (15 vs. 25). For document retrieval, retrieving a snippet of $d = 100$ bytes takes within 2 seconds for all corpora, and retrieving $d = 3000$ bytes takes up to 4.5 seconds. **Table 5** in Appendix D compares retrieval latency with the original `INFINI-GRAM`: for $|Q| = 10$ bytes, `INFINI-GRAM MINI` takes 106 ms versus 13 ms for the original, reflecting the storage-latency trade-off.

**The system deliberately works on raw UTF-8 bytes rather than tokens, unlike the original `INFINI-GRAM`.** The original `INFINI-GRAM` tokenized text before indexing, which reduces index size but requires tokenization time and locks the index to a specific tokenizer (Section 3). `INFINI-GRAM MINI` constructs the index directly on raw UTF-8 bytes, which saves tokenization time and allows more flexibility when querying. Tokenization is a technique to reduce index size, but since FM-index is already a compressed index, tokenization would not provide additional benefit here. This design choice also enables exact byte-level matching, which is critical for contamination detection where minor textual discrepancies matter.

**Three fundamental limitations arise from the FM-index design choices.** First, document retrieval latency remains higher than canonical suffix arrays because the original text is not stored in contiguous blocks; reconstructing a 3,000-character document takes 1.8 seconds on Pile and 4.5 seconds on DCLM-baseline, compared to millisecond-level retrieval in systems like the original `INFINI-GRAM` (Limitations section). Second, identifying co-occurrences of multiple patterns is inefficient because mapping every match from the suffix array range back to its position in the original text takes very long time, making this operation impractical. Third, the system only supports exact-match searches, so the contamination analysis is limited to case-sensitive exact matching, which may fail to detect instances with minor textual discrepancies. These limitations are explicit trade-offs for achieving the 0.44× storage multiplier that enables petabyte-scale indexing.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section isolates the specific contributions that distinguish `INFINI-GRAM MINI` from prior art. We distinguish between **engineering optimizations** (making existing methods faster) and **fundamental innovations** (enabling capabilities that were previously impossible). The paper delivers both: it optimizes FM-index construction enough to cross a feasibility threshold, and it uses that threshold to reveal new scientific insights about data contamination.

### 4.1 Domain Transfer: Adapting FM-Index from Genomics to Web Text

The most fundamental algorithmic innovation is the successful transfer of the FM-index from bioinformatics to natural language processing at scale. While the FM-index was introduced in 2000 (Ferragina and Manzini, 2000), its application has historically been confined to genomic data.

*   **The Barrier:** Genomic sequences use a small alphabet ($\sigma = 4$ for A, C, G, T), resulting in low entropy and highly efficient compression. Natural language text uses UTF-8 bytes ($\sigma = 256$), which increases the complexity of the wavelet tree and the entropy $H_0$. Prior attempts to use FM-index for NLP were limited to tiny datasets; Bevilacqua et al. (2022) indexed only **13.4 GB**, whereas `INFINI-GRAM MINI` indexes **83 TB** (Table 1).
*   **The Innovation:** This work demonstrates that FM-index compression ratios hold even at high entropy scales. The system achieves an index size of **0.44×** the corpus size (Table 1), whereas prior NLP-specific indexes (suffix arrays) required **6×** storage (Liu et al., 2024). This is not merely an incremental improvement; it is a **order-of-magnitude shift in storage efficiency** that changes the economic feasibility of auditing training data.
*   **Why It Matters:** Without this domain adaptation, researchers would need to choose between **exact matching** (suffix arrays, high storage cost) or **approximate matching** (hash-based, lower fidelity). `INFINI-GRAM MINI` proves that exact matching is viable at petabyte scale without prohibitive storage costs, enabling audits that were previously financially impossible.

### 4.2 Scientific Insight: The Velocity and Texture of Contamination

While building the tool is an engineering feat, the paper's application of the tool yields a critical scientific insight: **benchmark contamination is accelerating over time.** Prior contamination studies (e.g., Elazar et al., 2024) provided static snapshots. `INFINI-GRAM MINI` enables temporal tracking across multiple Common Crawl snapshots (January to July 2025).

*   **Contamination Velocity:** The data reveals that benchmarks do not just "leak" once; they proliferate rapidly in newer crawls. For example, `GSM8K` shows **0.00%** contamination in `Pile-train` (2020 cutoff) and **5.00%** in `DCLM-baseline` (2022 cutoff), but spikes to **74.20%** in `CC-2025-21` (Table 2). This suggests that once a benchmark is released, it quickly permeates the web ecosystem via solution blogs, dataset mirrors, and educational sites.
*   **Contamination Texture:** Using the system's retrieval capabilities, the authors categorize *how* contamination occurs (Section 4.3, Figure 3). They find that **Type 1 contamination** (exact question and answer match) accounts for **72.5%** of dirty entries in `Pile-train` and **82.6%** in `DCLM-baseline`. However, in newer crawls like `CC-2025-05`, Type 1 drops to **58%**, while **Type 3** (question only, no answer) rises to **30.2%**.
*   **Why It Matters:** This nuance changes how we interpret model performance. A model trained on `CC-2025-21` might not simply be "memorizing answers" (Type 1); it might be learning to solve problems where only the question is present (Type 3), or it might be encountering conflicting answers in the wild. The ability to distinguish these types via document retrieval (Section 3.2) provides a diagnostic tool for evaluating *why* a model fails or succeeds, rather than just *that* it failed.

### 4.3 Architectural Innovation: Prioritizing Storage Over Latency for Accessibility

`INFINI-GRAM MINI` makes a deliberate architectural choice to prioritize **storage compression** over **query latency**, distinguishing it from prior high-performance search systems.

*   **The Trade-off:** The original `INFINI-GRAM` (Liu et al., 2024) achieved millisecond-level latency (13 ms for 10-byte queries) but required a **6× storage multiplier** and significant RAM. `INFINI-GRAM MINI` accepts higher latency (**106 ms** for the same query, Table 5) to achieve a **0.44× storage multiplier**.
*   **The Innovation:** By designing the query engine to operate entirely on **memory-mapped disk files** (Section 3.2), the system reduces RAM requirements to **~30 MB**. This allows the index to be queried on standard consumer hardware or small cloud instances, rather than requiring high-memory server nodes.
*   **Why It Matters:** This shifts the system from a **specialized research tool** to a **community utility**. The authors host a public web interface and API (Section 3.3), enabling researchers without access to massive compute clusters to audit data. This democratization is critical for the proposed "Benchmark Contamination Bulletin" (Section 4.4), which relies on community contribution and access. The innovation here is not just the compression algorithm, but the **system design philosophy** that enables open access to petabyte-scale search.

### 4.4 Limitations as Design Boundaries

It is important to recognize that some "limitations" identified in the paper are actually explicit design boundaries resulting from these innovations.

*   **Exact Match Only:** The system does not support semantic search or fuzzy matching (Limitations section). This is a consequence of using FM-index, which relies on exact byte sequences. While this limits flexibility, it ensures **precision** for contamination detection, where false positives (matching similar but distinct questions) could invalidate audit results.
*   **Reconstruction Latency:** Retrieving a 3,000-byte document takes **4.5 seconds** on `DCLM-baseline` (Limitations section), compared to milliseconds in suffix array systems. This is the direct cost of the **0.44× storage compression**. The system reconstructs text character-by-character from the BWT rather than reading contiguous blocks.
*   **Significance:** Acknowledging these boundaries clarifies the tool's intended use case: it is optimized for **audit and counting** (low latency) rather than **bulk data extraction** (high latency). Users seeking to download entire corpora should not use this tool; users seeking to verify the presence of specific strings should.

By combining these innovations, `INFINI-GRAM MINI` does not merely improve upon prior search engines; it redefines the feasible scale of data auditing. It transforms contamination analysis from a retrospective study on small datasets into a continuous, scalable monitoring process for the entire Internet-scale training ecosystem.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section evaluates `INFINI-GRAM MINI` across two dimensions: **system performance** (indexing speed, storage efficiency, query latency) and **scientific utility** (benchmark contamination detection at scale). The experiments are designed to validate three core claims: (1) the system achieves superior storage efficiency compared to prior indexing methods, (2) indexing and querying are feasible at petabyte scale, and (3) the tool reveals contamination patterns that prior smaller-scale studies could not detect.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Scale.** The authors index three major corpora representing different scales and content types (**Table 1**):

- **The Pile:** 1.3TB training set + 1.4GB validation set (Gao et al., 2020). This is a curated, diverse text corpus commonly used for LM pretraining.
- **DCLM-baseline:** 16.666TB (Li et al., 2024). A high-quality subset of Common Crawl spanning approximately a decade of web crawls.
- **Common Crawl (2025):** Seven monthly snapshots from January to July 2025 (`CC-2025-05` through `CC-2025-30`), totaling 65TB. These represent fresh, unfiltered web data.

The **total indexed corpus is 83.059TB**, partitioned into **137 shards** of approximately 700GB each. This scale is critical: prior contamination studies were limited to corpora up to 12TB (Section 5, Related Work), making this the largest open corpus analyzed for contamination to date.

**Indexing Hardware and Setup.** All indexes were constructed on CPU nodes with **128 vCPUs and 2 TiB RAM** (Section 3.1). Each shard was indexed independently, enabling embarrassingly parallel construction. The paper reports total indexing time as **98.8 CPU node-days** for the full 83TB corpus. For comparison, the authors estimate that indexing the full Common Crawl (~1PB) would require **1,200 node-days**, or **19 hours if parallelized across 1,500 nodes**.

**Querying Hardware and Setup.** Query benchmarks were conducted on Google Cloud Platform using **pd-balanced SSD disks** with **80,000 IOPS and 1,200 MB/s throughput** on an `n2-highcpu-64` node (Appendix C). This setup is important because query latency is **disk I/O-bound**, not CPU-bound, due to the on-disk index design (Section 3.2).

**Baselines for System Performance.** The paper compares against two categories of prior work:

1. **FM-index implementations:** The primary baseline is **SDSL** (Gog et al., 2014), the best existing FM-index library. The authors measured indexing time and RAM usage on an 8.7GB corpus from DCLM-baseline.
2. **Alternative indexing methods:** The Introduction cites storage multipliers for suffix automata (29×, Merrill et al., 2024), suffix arrays (6×, Liu et al., 2024), and ElasticSearch (~2×, Elazar et al., 2024). These are not re-benchmarked but serve as reference points for storage efficiency.

**Baselines for Contamination Analysis.** Prior contamination studies (e.g., Elazar et al., 2024; Llama Team, 2024) are referenced as baselines for contamination rates, but `INFINI-GRAM MINI` enables analysis on **larger and newer corpora** than these studies could access. The paper does not claim to improve contamination detection methodology itself; rather, it scales existing methods to previously infeasible corpus sizes.

**Contamination Detection Metrics.** The authors use a standard lexical overlap metric (Section 4.1):

- For each benchmark entry, extract all **50-character substrings** with a stride of one word.
- Compute contamination ratio $\eta$ as:

$$\eta = \frac{\sum_{s \in S} \mathbb{I}[\text{count}(s) > 0]}{|S|}$$

where $S$ is the set of substrings and $\mathbb{I}$ is the indicator function.

- Classify entries into three levels:
  - **Clean:** $\eta < 20\%$
  - **Suspicious:** $20\% \leq \eta < 80\%$
  - **Dirty:** $\eta \geq 80\%$

This thresholding follows Touvron et al. (2023) with renamed categories. The paper focuses primarily on **dirty rates** (percentage of entries classified as dirty) as the key metric for contamination severity.

**Benchmark Suite.** The analysis covers **24 benchmarks** across five categories (**Table 6**, Appendix F):

- **Knowledge and Reasoning (6 benchmarks):** MMLU, MMLU-Pro, BigBenchHard, AGIEval, GPQA, HLE
- **Math (4 benchmarks):** AIME-2024, GSM8K, MATH-500, MGSM
- **Code (5 benchmarks):** HumanEval, HumanEval+, LiveCodeBench, SWE-bench, MBPP
- **Commonsense Understanding (6 benchmarks):** ARC-Challenge, ARC-Easy, CSQA, HellaSwag, OpenbookQA, Social IQa, WinoGrande
- **Reading Comprehension (2 benchmarks):** CoQA, SQuAD

For benchmarks with test sets larger than 1,000 entries, the authors **downsample to 1,000 entries** for efficiency while maintaining representative distribution across subtasks (Section 4.1).

### 5.2 System Performance Results

**Indexing Speed and Memory Efficiency.** The most direct system benchmark compares `INFINI-GRAM MINI` against SDSL on an 8.7GB corpus (Section 3.1):

| Metric | SDSL | INFINI-GRAM MINI | Improvement |
|--------|------|------------------|-------------|
| Indexing Time | 5,847 seconds | 324 seconds | **18× speedup** |
| Peak RAM | 74,807 MB | 23,742 MB | **3.2× reduction** |

This is not a marginal improvement—it crosses a **feasibility threshold**. At this speed, indexing 83TB becomes tractable (99 days on a single node), whereas SDSL would require approximately **1,782 days** (18× longer) for the same corpus, assuming linear scaling.

**Stepwise Indexing Breakdown.** **Table 3** (Appendix B) provides a detailed breakdown of indexing time per step for representative shards:

- **Suffix Array + BWT construction** is the dominant cost: 41,710 seconds (11.6 hours) for a 653GB Pile-train shard, compared to 29,543 seconds (8.2 hours) for a similar-size DCLM-baseline shard.
- The paper notes that SA construction **scales super-linearly with text duplication**. The Pile has over 60% duplicate documents (Elazar et al., 2024), which explains why SA construction takes longer despite similar shard sizes.
- **Wavelet tree construction** and **sampling operations** are roughly linear and take 5,000–6,000 seconds each for 650GB shards.

This breakdown is important for practitioners: if indexing speed is a bottleneck, optimizing SA construction (e.g., through better deduplication preprocessing) would yield the largest gains.

**Storage Efficiency.** **Table 1** reports index sizes for all corpora. The key result is consistent across datasets:

- **Index size multiplier: 0.44×** (range: 0.44× to 0.46× across corpora)
- **Total index size: 36.884TB** for 83.059TB of text

This is the paper's most significant system claim. Compared to prior methods:

| Method | Storage Multiplier | Index Size for 83TB Corpus |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------------|
| Suffix Automaton | 29× | 2,407 TB |
| Suffix Array | 6× | 498 TB |
| ElasticSearch | ~2× | 166 TB |
| **INFINI-GRAM MINI** | **0.44×** | **36.9 TB** |

The storage savings are **order-of-magnitude**. Indexing 83TB with a suffix array would require nearly 500TB of storage; `INFINI-GRAM MINI` requires only 37TB. This is not just a cost saving—it makes the difference between **feasible and infeasible** for most research institutions.

**Theoretical vs. Practical Compression.** The paper notes that the index could theoretically reach **0.26×** corpus size if sampling rates $a$ and $b$ were set very large (Section 3.1). However, this would increase query latency because more LF mapping iterations are needed to recover unsampled positions. The chosen values ($a = 32$, $b = 64$) represent an **empirical trade-off** that keeps query latency in the seconds range while maintaining substantial storage savings.

**Query Latency.** **Table 4** (Appendix C) reports detailed latency benchmarks across query types and corpus sizes:

**Counting Queries:**
- Short queries ($|Q| \leq 10$ bytes): **0.004–0.4 seconds** across all corpora
- Medium queries ($|Q| = 100$ bytes): **0.696–2.857 seconds**
- Long queries ($|Q| = 1,000$ bytes): **4.808–25.47 seconds**

Latency scales with both query length and number of shards. For example, a 1,000-byte query takes **8 seconds** on `CC-2025-05` (15 shards) but **25 seconds** on `DCLM-baseline` (25 shards). This is because the system parallelizes across shards, and each shard requires independent disk reads.

**Document Retrieval Queries:**
- Small snippets ($d = 100$ bytes): **0.734–1.991 seconds**
- Medium snippets ($d = 1,000$ bytes): **0.94–2.385 seconds**
- Large snippets ($d = 3,000$ bytes): **1.858–4.456 seconds**

Retrieval latency is higher than counting because it requires the `locate` and `reconstruct` operations, which involve more random disk reads (**Table 4**, Appendix C).

**Comparison with Original INFINI-GRAM.** **Table 5** (Appendix D) directly compares query latency between `INFINI-GRAM MINI` (FM-index, on-disk) and the original `INFINI-GRAM` (suffix array, in-memory) on the Pile-train corpus:

| Query Length | INFINI-GRAM | INFINI-GRAM MINI | Slowdown |
|--------------|-------------|------------------|----------|
| 10 bytes | 13 ms | 106 ms | **8.2×** |
| 20 bytes | 14 ms | 182 ms | **13×** |
| 100 bytes | 13 ms | 696 ms | **53.5×** |

This is the **explicit trade-off** the system makes: `INFINI-GRAM MINI` is significantly slower per query but requires **0.44× storage** versus **6× storage** for the original. For contamination analysis—where queries are relatively infrequent but storage costs are prohibitive—this trade-off is justified. However, for applications requiring high-throughput querying (e.g., real-time search), the original `INFINI-GRAM` would be more appropriate.

**RAM Usage at Query Time.** A critical but often overlooked metric is query-time memory usage. `INFINI-GRAM MINI` requires only **~30 MB of RAM** and **1 vCPU** to load all indexes (Section 3.2). This is because indexes are memory-mapped from disk rather than loaded into RAM. In contrast, suffix array-based systems typically require the entire index to reside in memory, which for an 83TB corpus would be infeasible even with the 6× multiplier (498TB of RAM).

This design choice enables the **public web interface and API** (Section 3.3): the system can serve queries from modest cloud instances rather than requiring high-memory server nodes.

**Overall Contamination Rates.** **Table 2** reports dirty rates for all 24 benchmarks across the Pile, DCLM-baseline, and seven Common Crawl snapshots. Key findings:

- **ARC-Challenge:** 1.80% (Pile) → **34.10%** (DCLM) → 3.10–11.90% (CC 2025)
- **ARC-Easy:** 1.30% (Pile) → **31.70%** (DCLM) → 5.40–9.50% (CC 2025)
- **SQuAD:** 2.80% (Pile) → **40.10%** (DCLM) → 1.50–33.00% (CC 2025)
- **OpenbookQA:** 10.80% (Pile) → 15.60% (DCLM) → **30.20%** (CC-2025-08)

**Relatively Clean Benchmarks (Dirty Rate < 5% across all corpora):**
- **LiveCodeBench:** 0.00% across all corpora
- **WinoGrande:** 0.00% across all corpora
- **HellaSwag:** 0.00–0.10% across all corpora
- **HumanEval / HumanEval+:** 0.00–0.60% across all corpora

**Temporal Trends.** The paper's most striking finding is that contamination **increases over time** for newer benchmarks:

- **AIME-2024:** 0.00% (Pile, DCLM) → 10.00% (CC-2025-05) → **40.00%** (CC-2025-21)
- **GSM8K:** 0.00% (Pile) → 5.00% (DCLM) → 0.70–6.90% (early CC 2025) → **74.20%** (CC-2025-21)
- **MGSM:** 0.00% (Pile, DCLM) → 5.60% (CC-2025-05) → **72.80%** (CC-2025-21)

This pattern suggests that benchmarks do not leak once and stabilize; instead, they **proliferate rapidly** through the web ecosystem after release. The spike in CC-2025-21 (May 2025 crawl) for math benchmarks is particularly concerning, as it indicates that solution blogs, dataset mirrors, and educational sites quickly incorporate benchmark content.

**Domain-Specific Patterns.** Contamination varies systematically by benchmark category:

- **Commonsense and Reading Comprehension** benchmarks (ARC, SQuAD, CoQA, OpenbookQA) show consistently higher dirty rates across all corpora. These benchmarks have been available for years, giving more time for leakage.
- **Math benchmarks** (GSM8K, MATH-500, AIME) were initially clean but show rapid contamination in recent crawls. This aligns with the proliferation of math solution websites and LLM training data leaks.
- **Code benchmarks** (HumanEval, LiveCodeBench, SWE-bench) remain relatively clean, possibly because code-specific content is less likely to appear in general web crawls or because these benchmarks are newer.

**Contamination Type Analysis.** Section 4.3 categorizes dirty entries into four types using LLM-as-a-judge (**Figure 3**, Appendix H shows examples):

| Contamination Type | Pile-train | DCLM-baseline | CC-2025-05 |
|-------------------|------------|---------------|------------|
| Type 1: Question + Answer (exact) | 72.5% | 82.6% | 58.0% |
| Type 2: Question + Answer (paraphrased) | 4.5% | 2.1% | 6.2% |
| Type 3: Question only (no answer) | 18.1% | 10.9% | 30.2% |
| Type 4: False positives | 3.1% | 1.0% | 3.0% |

This breakdown reveals an important trend: **Type 1 contamination (exact question and answer match) dominates in older corpora** (Pile, DCLM), but **Type 3 contamination (question only) increases in newer crawls**. This suggests that as benchmarks become more widely known, the questions spread faster than the answers—possibly through discussion forums, solution attempts, or partial leaks.

**Implications for Model Evaluation.** The paper argues that high contamination rates, especially Type 1, indicate that models trained on these corpora may be **memorizing answers rather than learning reasoning**. For GSM8K with 74.2% contamination in CC-2025-21, a model trained on this data could achieve high scores through rote memorization of 3 out of 4 questions. This undermines the validity of GSM8K as a measure of mathematical reasoning for models trained on recent web data.

### 5.4 Assessment of Experimental Validity

**Strengths of the Experimental Design.**

1. **Scale is unprecedented.** The 83TB corpus is **7× larger** than the largest prior contamination study (35TB, Elazar et al., 2024) and **69× larger** than suffix array-based studies (12TB, Liu et al., 2024). This scale is not just a number—it enables detection of contamination patterns that smaller corpora would miss (e.g., the GSM8K spike in CC-2025-21).

2. **Temporal analysis is rigorous.** By indexing seven monthly Common Crawl snapshots, the paper provides **time-series evidence** of contamination growth rather than a single static measurement. This strengthens the claim that contamination is accelerating.

3. **System benchmarks are fair.** The comparison with SDSL uses the same hardware and corpus, and the stepwise breakdown (Table 3, Appendix B) allows readers to understand where improvements come from. The query latency comparison with original `INFINI-GRAM` (Table 5, Appendix D) is also direct and honest about the trade-off.

4. **Contamination methodology follows prior work.** The 50-character substring matching with 20%/80% thresholds is consistent with Touvron et al. (2023) and Llama Team (2024), making results comparable to prior studies.

**Limitations and Caveats.**

1. **Exact-match only.** The paper explicitly acknowledges that contamination analysis is limited to **case-sensitive exact matching** (Limitations section). This may miss contamination where benchmark text has been slightly modified (e.g., paraphrased questions, reformatted answers). The authors note this could lead to **underestimation** of true contamination rates.

2. **Downsampling for large benchmarks.** For benchmarks with more than 1,000 test entries, the authors downsample to 1,000 entries (Section 4.1). While they sample proportionally from subtasks, this introduces **sampling variance** that is not quantified. For benchmarks with highly heterogeneous subtasks, the reported dirty rates may not be fully representative.

3. **Query latency is hardware-dependent.** The reported latencies (Table 4, Appendix C) are benchmarked on GCP SSDs with 80,000 IOPS. On slower disks or with higher query concurrency, latencies would increase. The paper does not provide a model for predicting latency under different I/O conditions.

4. **No ablation on sampling rates.** The choice of $a = 32$ and $b = 64$ is described as empirical (Section 3.1), but the paper does not present an ablation study showing how different sampling rates affect the storage-latency trade-off. Readers must trust that these values are near-optimal without seeing the Pareto frontier.

5. **Contamination type classification relies on LLM judgment.** The categorization of dirty entries into Types 1–4 uses `gpt-4o-mini` as a judge (Appendix I). While the prompt is provided, the paper does not report inter-annotator agreement or validate the LLM's classifications against human labels. This introduces potential **classification noise** in the type breakdown percentages.

**Failure Cases and Edge Cases.**

The Limitations section explicitly identifies three failure modes:

1. **High-latency document retrieval.** Reconstructing a 3,000-character document takes **4.5 seconds** on DCLM-baseline, compared to milliseconds in suffix array systems. For applications requiring bulk document extraction, this is a significant bottleneck.

2. **Inefficient co-occurrence queries.** Identifying documents that contain **multiple patterns** is impractical because mapping every match from the suffix array range back to text positions requires excessive random disk reads. This limits the system's utility for complex queries (e.g., "find documents containing both X and Y").

3. **No fuzzy matching.** The system cannot detect contamination where benchmark text has been slightly modified (e.g., whitespace changes, synonym substitutions). This is a fundamental limitation of FM-index, not an implementation issue.

**Robustness Checks.**

The paper includes several robustness checks:

- **Multiple corpora:** Contamination is measured across three distinct corpora (Pile, DCLM, Common Crawl) with different knowledge cutoffs, reducing the risk that results are corpus-specific artifacts.
- **Multiple benchmarks:** 24 benchmarks across five categories are analyzed, reducing the risk that findings are driven by a few outlier benchmarks.
- **Temporal consistency:** The contamination trends (e.g., GSM8K increasing over time) are consistent across multiple math benchmarks (GSM8K, MATH-500, MGSM, AIME-2024), suggesting the pattern is real rather than noise.

However, the paper does not include:
- **Inter-rater reliability** for contamination type classification
- **Sensitivity analysis** for the 20%/80% contamination thresholds
- **False positive rate** estimation for the substring matching method

### 5.5 Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: "INFINI-GRAM MINI achieves 18× faster indexing and 3.2× less RAM than SDSL."**

**Supported.** The benchmark on 8.7GB corpus (Section 3.1) is direct and reproducible. The stepwise breakdown in Table 3 (Appendix B) explains where improvements come from (parallelization of previously single-threaded steps). This claim is well-substantiated.

**Claim 2: "Index size is 0.44× corpus size, enabling petabyte-scale indexing."**

**Supported.** Table 1 shows consistent 0.44× multiplier across all 83TB of indexed data. The comparison with prior methods (29× for suffix automata, 6× for suffix arrays) is based on cited literature, not re-benchmarked, but the multipliers are widely accepted in the field. The claim that this enables petabyte-scale indexing is plausible given the storage savings, though the paper does not actually index 1PB—it extrapolates from 83TB.

**Claim 3: "Query latency is in the seconds range with ~30 MB RAM usage."**

**Supported.** Table 4 (Appendix C) provides detailed latency measurements across query types and corpus sizes. The ~30 MB RAM figure is stated in Section 3.2 but not independently benchmarked; however, this is consistent with the on-disk, memory-mapped design.

**Claim 4: "Core benchmarks are heavily contaminated (up to 74.2% for GSM8K)."**

**Supported with caveats.** Table 2 provides specific dirty rates for all benchmarks. The 74.2% figure for GSM8K in CC-2025-21 is precise and reproducible. However, the claim that this "could lead to overestimating the capabilities of language models" is an **inference**, not a direct experimental result. The paper does not train models on contaminated vs. clean data and compare performance—it infers the risk from contamination rates alone. This is a reasonable inference given prior work on memorization, but it is not directly proven in this paper.

**Claim 5: "Contamination is increasing over time in newer crawls."**

**Strongly supported.** The temporal pattern across seven Common Crawl snapshots (Table 2) is clear and consistent for multiple benchmarks (GSM8K, AIME-2024, MGSM). This is one of the paper's most compelling findings because it demonstrates a **dynamic trend** rather than a static measurement.

**Overall Assessment.** The experiments convincingly support the **system performance claims** (indexing speed, storage efficiency, query latency) with direct benchmarks and comparisons. The **contamination findings** are well-measured and internally consistent, though the implications for model evaluation are inferred rather than directly demonstrated. The paper is transparent about limitations (exact-match only, no ablation on sampling rates, LLM-based type classification), which strengthens credibility.

The most significant contribution is **scale-enabled discovery**: the GSM8K contamination spike in CC-2025-21 would not have been detectable with prior 12TB-scale tools. This validates the core thesis that storage-efficient indexing enables new scientific insights about training data.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

Every systems design involves explicit trade-offs, and `INFINI-GRAM MINI` is no exception. The paper is unusually transparent about its limitations, dedicating a full section to them. However, some constraints are implicit in the design choices and warrant explicit discussion. This section categorizes limitations into four groups: **fundamental algorithmic trade-offs**, **query pattern constraints**, **detection methodology gaps**, and **evaluation uncertainties**. Understanding these boundaries is critical for determining when `INFINI-GRAM MINI` is the right tool—and when it is not.

### 6.1 Fundamental Algorithmic Trade-offs: Compression vs. Latency

The most significant limitation is inherent to the FM-index itself: **storage compression comes at the cost of query latency**. This is not an implementation bug—it is a mathematical consequence of how the data structure works.

**The Reconstruction Cost.** Unlike suffix arrays, which store text positions directly, the FM-index does not store the original text in contiguous blocks. To retrieve a document, the system must reconstruct it character-by-character by traversing the Burrows-Wheeler Transform using LF mapping. As stated in the Limitations section:

> "To retrieve a document, we need to reconstruct it character-by-character, leading to a large number of reads in random addresses. These process takes long time since the entire index is kept on disk at inference time."

The paper quantifies this trade-off in **Table 5** (Appendix D):

| Query Length | INFINI-GRAM (Suffix Array) | INFINI-GRAM MINI (FM-index) | Slowdown |
|--------------|---------------------------|----------------------------|----------|
| 10 bytes | 13 ms | 106 ms | **8.2×** |
| 100 bytes | 13 ms | 696 ms | **53.5×** |

For a 3,000-character document, retrieval takes **1.8 seconds on Pile** and **4.5 seconds on DCLM-baseline** (Limitations section), compared to millisecond-level retrieval in suffix array systems. This is the direct price of achieving a **0.44× storage multiplier** instead of **6×**.

**Why This Trade-off Exists.** The FM-index achieves compression by:
1. Sampling the suffix array at intervals ($a = 32$), requiring up to $a$ LF mapping iterations to recover unsampled positions.
2. Storing the BWT in compressed wavelet tree form, which requires $O(H_0)$ operations per character access.
3. Keeping the entire index on disk rather than in RAM to minimize memory requirements (~30 MB).

Each of these choices reduces storage but increases the number of random disk reads per query. The paper acknowledges this could "potentially be reduced using techniques like disk page prefetching" (Limitations section), but such optimizations are not implemented.

**When This Matters.** This limitation makes `INFINI-GRAM MINI` unsuitable for:
- **High-throughput querying:** Applications requiring thousands of queries per second would be bottlenecked by disk I/O.
- **Bulk document extraction:** Downloading large portions of the corpus would be prohibitively slow.
- **Real-time search:** User-facing search interfaces expecting sub-second responses may find 4.5-second latencies unacceptable.

**When This Does Not Matter.** For the paper's intended use case—**audit and contamination analysis**—this trade-off is justified. Contamination checks involve relatively infrequent queries (e.g., checking 1,000 benchmark entries), where storage cost is the primary constraint, not query throughput. As the authors note, the system enables analysis that would be "much more expensive if using other indexing methods" (Introduction).

### 6.2 Query Pattern Constraints: What the System Cannot Do

The FM-index supports a narrow set of query operations. Several common search patterns are either inefficient or impossible.

**Multi-Pattern Co-occurrence Queries Are Impractical.** The Limitations section explicitly states:

> "Identifying co-occurrences of multiple patterns is inefficient with the FM-index, since mapping every match from the suffix array range back to its position in original text takes very long time thus making this operation impractical."

This means queries like "find all documents containing both 'GSM8K' and 'solution'" require:
1. Finding the suffix array range for each pattern separately.
2. Mapping every match position back to document boundaries using `locate`.
3. Intersecting the document sets.

Step 2 is the bottleneck: each `locate` operation requires $O(aH_0)$ random disk reads. For patterns with thousands of matches, this becomes computationally prohibitive. In contrast, the original `INFINI-GRAM` (suffix array-based) "supports this operation efficiently" (Limitations section).

**Implication:** Researchers cannot easily analyze **contextual relationships** between terms. For example, studying whether benchmark questions appear alongside specific answer formats or discussion patterns would require impractical query times.

**Exact-Match Only: No Fuzzy or Semantic Search.** The system only supports **case-sensitive exact byte matching** (Limitations section). This has two consequences:

1. **Minor textual discrepancies are missed.** Contamination where benchmark text has been paraphrased, reformatted, or had whitespace changed will not be detected. The paper acknowledges this "may fail to detect contamination of instances with minor textual discrepancies."

2. **No semantic similarity.** Queries like "find documents discussing mathematical reasoning similar to GSM8K" are impossible. The system cannot understand meaning—only exact byte sequences.

**Why This Design Choice Was Made.** Exact matching ensures **precision** for contamination audits. False positives (matching similar but distinct questions) could invalidate audit results. However, this likely leads to **underestimation** of true contamination rates, as paraphrased or modified benchmark content would not be flagged.

**Alphabet and Encoding Constraints.** The system works on **UTF-8 bytes** with $\sigma = 256$ (Section 3.1). This keeps the alphabet small for efficient wavelet tree construction but introduces edge cases:

- **Non-UTF-8 text:** Binary files or text in non-UTF-8 encodings would not be indexed correctly.
- **Normalization issues:** Text that has been Unicode-normalized differently (e.g., composed vs. decomposed characters) would not match, even if semantically identical.

The paper does not discuss how these edge cases are handled, suggesting they are out of scope for the current implementation.

### 6.3 Detection Methodology Gaps: What Contamination Analysis Misses

The contamination analysis in Section 4 relies on specific methodological choices that introduce blind spots.

**Substring Matching Thresholds Are Arbitrary.** The contamination detection uses 50-character substrings with a stride of one word, classifying entries as "dirty" if $\eta \geq 80\%$ of substrings match (Section 4.1). These thresholds follow Touvron et al. (2023), but the paper provides no **sensitivity analysis**:

- What if the threshold were 70% or 90%?
- What if substring length were 30 or 100 characters?
- How many false positives does this method produce?

Without ablation studies, readers must trust that these thresholds are near-optimal. The paper does not report **false positive rates** or **inter-rater reliability** for the contamination classification.

**LLM-Based Contamination Type Classification Is Unvalidated.** The categorization of dirty entries into Types 1–4 uses `gpt-4o-mini` as a judge (Appendix I). While the prompt is provided, the paper does not:

- Report **inter-annotator agreement** between the LLM and human labelers.
- Validate classifications against a **human-annotated gold standard**.
- Quantify **classification error rates**.

This introduces potential **classification noise** in the type breakdown percentages (e.g., 72.5% Type 1 in Pile-train). If the LLM systematically misclassifies certain contamination types, the reported trends (e.g., Type 3 increasing in newer crawls) could be artifacts rather than real patterns.

**Downsampling Introduces Sampling Variance.** For benchmarks with more than 1,000 test entries, the authors **downsample to 1,000 entries** (Section 4.1). While they sample "proportionally from each subtask to maintain representative distribution," the paper does not:

- Report **confidence intervals** for dirty rate estimates.
- Quantify **sampling variance** across multiple random samples.
- Discuss whether certain subtasks are **underrepresented** after downsampling.

For benchmarks with highly heterogeneous subtasks (e.g., MMLU has 57 subjects), a 1,000-entry sample may not capture contamination patterns in rare subjects.

**Contamination Sources Are Not Systematically Traced.** Section 4.3 identifies four contamination sources (benchmark sourced from Internet, other benchmarks cite it, blogs/papers cite it, coincidence with online source), with examples in Appendix H (Figures 6–12). However:

- Only **7 examples** are shown out of thousands of dirty entries.
- No **quantitative breakdown** of contamination sources is provided.
- The **original webpages** responsible are identified for examples but not systematically cataloged.

This limits the ability to generalize about **where** contamination originates. For instance, is GSM8K contamination primarily from HuggingFace dataset mirrors (Figure 10) or from educational blogs? The paper suggests the former but does not quantify this.

### 6.4 Computational and Scalability Constraints

While the paper claims petabyte-scale feasibility, several practical constraints remain.

**Indexing Time Is Still Substantial.** The system indexed 83TB in **98.8 CPU node-days** (Table 1). While this is feasible, it is not trivial:

- **Single-node timeline:** 99 days on one node is impractical for most researchers.
- **Parallelization requirement:** To complete in 19 hours requires **137 nodes** (Introduction), which is a significant cloud computing cost.
- **Full Common Crawl estimate:** Indexing 1PB would require **1,200 node-days** or **1,500 nodes for 19 hours** (Section 3.1).

The paper does not provide **cost estimates** for cloud computing, which would help readers assess feasibility. At typical cloud rates (e.g., $0.05–0.10 per vCPU-hour), indexing 83TB could cost thousands of dollars.

**Disk I/O Performance Is a Bottleneck.** Query latency is "largely determined by the disk I/O performance" (Section 3.2). The benchmarks use GCP SSDs with **80,000 IOPS and 1,200 MB/s throughput** (Appendix C). On slower disks:

- Latencies would increase proportionally.
- The paper provides **no model for predicting latency** under different I/O conditions.
- Concurrent queries would contend for disk bandwidth, degrading performance.

This makes the system's performance **hardware-dependent**, limiting reproducibility on different infrastructure.

**No Ablation on Sampling Rates.** The sampling rates $a = 32$ and $b = 64$ are chosen "empirically to balance storage savings and query latency" (Section 3.1). However:

- No **Pareto frontier** is shown illustrating the storage-latency trade-off.
- No **sensitivity analysis** demonstrates how different rates affect performance.
- The theoretical minimum of **0.26×** index size is mentioned but not benchmarked.

Readers must trust these values are near-optimal without seeing the underlying data. Different use cases (e.g., more counting queries vs. more retrieval queries) might benefit from different sampling rates.

**RAM Constraints During Indexing.** While query-time RAM is minimal (~30 MB), **indexing-time RAM** is substantial:

- Peak RAM for indexing an 8.7GB corpus: **23,742 MB** (Section 3.1).
- Shard size limit: **700GB per shard** given 2 TiB RAM nodes (Section 3.1).

This constrains the **minimum hardware** required for index construction. Researchers without access to high-RAM nodes cannot build indexes, even if they can query them.

### 6.5 Ethical and Data Quality Concerns

The paper briefly acknowledges ethical limitations but does not deeply address them.

**Retrieved Content May Be Harmful.** The Limitations section notes:

> "As the text corpora may contain biased or toxic content, document retrieval output may contain content that can be perceived as ethically problematic, and may contain sensitive information."

The paper states that "output of INFINI-GRAM MINI does not reflect authors' views," but does not describe:

- **Content filtering mechanisms:** Are there safeguards against retrieving harmful content?
- **Access controls:** Who can query the public API, and are there rate limits or usage monitoring?
- **Privacy considerations:** Could the system inadvertently expose personally identifiable information (PII) from web crawls?

Given that the system enables searching 83TB of Internet text, these are non-trivial concerns. The public web interface (Section 3.3) makes this capability widely accessible, which amplifies potential misuse.

**Contamination Analysis May Be Misinterpreted.** The paper warns that contamination "could lead to overestimating the capabilities of language models" (Introduction). However:

- High contamination rates do not **prove** models memorized answers—only that the data was present.
- The paper does not **train models** on contaminated vs. clean data to demonstrate performance differences.
- The claim that contamination inflates benchmarks is an **inference** from prior work, not a direct experimental result.

This creates risk that readers may **overinterpret** contamination rates as definitive proof of evaluation invalidity, when the actual impact on model performance depends on many factors (e.g., model architecture, training objectives, deduplication).

### 6.6 Open Questions and Future Directions

Several important questions remain unanswered:

**1. What Is the True Contamination Rate?** Given exact-match only detection, the reported dirty rates (e.g., 74.2% for GSM8K) are likely **lower bounds**. How much higher would rates be with fuzzy matching or semantic similarity?

**2. How Does Contamination Actually Affect Model Performance?** The paper infers that contamination inflates benchmarks but does not demonstrate this empirically. Future work could train models on clean vs. contaminated subsets and measure performance differences.

**3. Can Prefetching or Caching Reduce Latency?** The paper suggests "disk page prefetching" as a potential optimization (Limitations section) but does not explore it. How much latency reduction is possible?

**4. What Is the Optimal Sampling Rate for Different Use Cases?** Without ablation studies, it is unclear whether $a = 32, b = 64$ is optimal for contamination analysis. Different applications (e.g., counting-heavy vs. retrieval-heavy) might benefit from different configurations.

**5. How Should the Community Respond to Contamination?** The paper hosts a "Benchmark Contamination Bulletin" (Section 4.4) but does not prescribe actions. Should contaminated benchmarks be retired? Should training data be deduplicated? These policy questions are left open.

### 6.7 Summary: When to Use (and Not Use) INFINI-GRAM MINI

Based on the limitations discussed, here is a practical guide:

**Use INFINI-GRAM MINI when:**
- You need to search **petabyte-scale corpora** with limited storage budget.
- Your queries are **infrequent** (audit-style, not real-time search).
- You need **exact byte-level matching** for precision (e.g., contamination detection).
- You have limited RAM available for querying (~30 MB is sufficient).
- You are willing to accept **seconds-level latency** for document retrieval.

**Do NOT use INFINI-GRAM MINI when:**
- You need **high-throughput querying** (thousands of queries per second).
- You need to search for **multiple co-occurring patterns** efficiently.
- You need **fuzzy or semantic matching** (paraphrases, synonyms).
- You need **millisecond-level latency** for user-facing applications.
- You need to **bulk extract** large portions of the corpus.
- You lack access to **high-RAM nodes** for index construction (2 TiB per shard).

Understanding these boundaries ensures that researchers apply the tool appropriately and interpret results within the system's constraints. The limitations are not flaws—they are explicit design choices that enable the system's core innovation: **storage-efficient indexing at Internet scale**.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how `INFINI-GRAM MINI` reshapes what is possible in language model research, what new questions it enables us to ask, and how practitioners should decide whether to adopt this system. The implications extend beyond contamination analysis to fundamental questions about data transparency, evaluation validity, and the economics of large-scale NLP research.

### 7.1 Transforming the Research Landscape

`INFINI-GRAM MINI` changes the field in three concrete ways: it makes petabyte-scale auditing **economically feasible**, it shifts contamination analysis from **retrospective to continuous**, and it **democratizes access** to tools previously limited to well-resourced institutions.

**Economic Feasibility Through Storage Efficiency.** The most immediate impact is economic. Prior to this work, indexing 83TB of text required prohibitive storage:

| Method | Storage for 83TB Corpus | Approximate Storage Cost* |
|--------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Suffix Automaton | 2,407 TB | ~$240,000/year |
| Suffix Array | 498 TB | ~$50,000/year |
| ElasticSearch | 166 TB | ~$17,000/year |
| **INFINI-GRAM MINI** | **37 TB** | **~$3,700/year** |

*Estimated at $0.023/GB/month (GCP standard disk rates)

The **0.44× storage multiplier** (Table 1) reduces infrastructure costs by an order of magnitude compared to even the most efficient prior method (ElasticSearch at ~2×). This is not merely a cost saving—it changes **who can do this research**. A university lab with modest cloud credits can now audit training data at a scale previously reserved for industry labs.

**From Retrospective to Continuous Monitoring.** Prior contamination studies provided static snapshots: Elazar et al. (2024) analyzed 35TB, Liu et al. (2024) analyzed 12TB. These were one-time analyses that quickly became outdated as new crawls were published. `INFINI-GRAM MINI` enables **continuous monitoring** through the Benchmark Contamination Bulletin (Section 4.4), which tracks contamination across seven Common Crawl snapshots from January to July 2025.

The temporal data reveals a critical insight: contamination is **accelerating**, not stable. For GSM8K, dirty rates jumped from 5.00% in DCLM-baseline (2022 cutoff) to **74.20% in CC-2025-21** (Table 2). This pattern—also seen in AIME-2024 (0.00% → 40.00%) and MGSM (0.00% → 72.80%)—suggests that benchmarks permeate the web ecosystem rapidly after release. Without continuous monitoring, researchers would miss these dynamics and make decisions based on outdated contamination estimates.

**Democratization Through Low-Memory Querying.** The system's design choice to operate with **~30 MB of RAM** at query time (Section 3.2) enables public access through a web interface and API endpoint (Section 3.3). This is a deliberate departure from prior systems that required loading entire indexes into memory. The original `INFINI-GRAM` achieved millisecond latency but required substantial RAM; `INFINI-GRAM MINI` accepts seconds-level latency to enable **community access**.

This democratization matters because contamination affects the entire research community, not just those who can afford large-scale infrastructure. The Bulletin invites community contributions of new benchmarks (Appendix J, Figure 13), creating a **collaborative monitoring system** rather than a closed analysis.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

The capabilities of `INFINI-GRAM MINI` open several research directions that were previously infeasible due to scale constraints.

**1. Causal Studies of Contamination Impact on Model Performance.** The paper identifies contamination rates but does not directly measure their impact on model evaluation. As noted in Section 6.5, the claim that contamination "could lead to overestimating the capabilities of language models" is an inference from prior work, not a direct experimental result.

`INFINI-GRAM MINI` enables the next step: **controlled training studies**. Researchers can now:
- Use the system to identify **clean subsets** of training corpora (documents without benchmark overlap)
- Train models on clean vs. contaminated data
- Measure performance differences on affected benchmarks

This would transform contamination from a **correlational concern** to a **causal understanding**. For example, if GSM8K has 74.2% contamination in CC-2025-21, does a model trained on this corpus actually perform better on GSM8K than a model trained on clean data? Quantifying this effect would inform whether contaminated benchmarks should be retired or reweighted.

**2. Systematic Analysis of Contamination Sources.** Section 4.3 identifies four contamination sources (benchmark sourced from Internet, other benchmarks cite it, blogs/papers cite it, coincidence with online source), with examples in Appendix H (Figures 6–12). However, only **7 examples** are shown out of thousands of dirty entries, and no quantitative breakdown is provided.

Future work could use `INFINI-GRAM MINI`'s document retrieval to:
- Catalog **all source URLs** for contaminated entries in a benchmark
- Classify sources by type (educational sites, dataset mirrors, discussion forums, papers)
- Track how contamination **propagates** through the web ecosystem over time

For GSM8K, Figure 10 shows contamination from a HuggingFace dataset mirror with erroneous "steps" fields. Systematically identifying such sources could inform **data filtering strategies** for training corpora.

**3. Optimization of FM-Index for NLP Workloads.** The paper acknowledges that query latency could "potentially be reduced using techniques like disk page prefetching" (Limitations section), but does not implement this. Several optimization directions remain:

- **Prefetching and caching:** Predict which BWT nodes will be accessed during reconstruction and preload them
- **Adaptive sampling:** Use different sampling rates ($a$, $b$) for different shards based on query patterns
- **Hybrid indexes:** Combine FM-index for counting with suffix arrays for frequent retrieval targets
- **GPU acceleration:** Offload wavelet tree operations to GPU for parallel rank/select computation

The paper reports that reconstructing a 3,000-character document takes **4.5 seconds on DCLM-baseline** (Limitations section). Even a 2× improvement would make the system viable for more interactive use cases.

**4. Expanded Contamination Detection Methodology.** The current analysis is limited to **case-sensitive exact matching** (Limitations section), which likely underestimates true contamination. Future work could:

- Combine `INFINI-GRAM MINI` with **fuzzy matching** techniques (e.g., edit distance, skip-grams)
- Use the exact-match results as **seeds** for finding paraphrased variants
- Develop **semantic contamination detection** using embedding similarity

The exact-match system provides a **high-precision baseline**; extending it with recall-oriented methods would give a more complete picture of contamination.

**5. Cross-Corpus Contamination Tracking.** The paper indexes three corpora (Pile, DCLM-baseline, Common Crawl) but analyzes them separately. Future work could:
- Track **which benchmarks appear in which corpora** over time
- Identify **upstream sources** of contamination (e.g., does contamination in Common Crawl originate from Pile?)
- Study how **data filtering** (DCLM is a filtered subset of crawls) affects contamination rates

Table 2 shows that DCLM-baseline has higher contamination than some CC snapshots for certain benchmarks (e.g., MMLU: 28.40% vs. 13.50% in CC-2025-05). Understanding why filtered data has higher contamination could inform data curation practices.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

Beyond contamination analysis, the paper identifies two additional use cases (Section 4):

**Task-Specific Dataset Construction.** Researchers building datasets for specific tasks can use `INFINI-GRAM MINI` to retrieve documents containing relevant terms or phrases. For example:
- A researcher studying legal reasoning could search for documents containing specific legal terminology
- A developer building a medical QA system could retrieve documents discussing specific conditions

The system's **exact byte-level matching** ensures precision: if you search for a specific phrase, you get exact matches, not approximate similarities. This is critical when the exact wording matters (e.g., legal citations, code snippets).

**Data Curation and Deduplication.** The system can assist in identifying and removing duplicate, low-quality, or sensitive text. The Limitations section notes that the system supports counting occurrences, which enables:
- **Duplicate detection:** Find documents that appear multiple times in a corpus
- **Quality filtering:** Identify documents containing known low-quality patterns
- **Privacy auditing:** Search for PII patterns (email addresses, phone numbers) across corpora

However, the **4.5-second retrieval latency** for 3,000-character documents (Limitations section) makes this impractical for bulk processing. The system is better suited for **auditing** (verifying presence of specific content) than **curation** (processing entire corpora).

**Benchmark Contamination Monitoring.** The Benchmark Contamination Bulletin (Section 4.4, Appendix J) is the most immediate practical application. The system enables:
- **Continuous tracking** of contamination rates for core benchmarks
- **Community contributions** of new benchmarks for monitoring
- **Temporal analysis** showing how contamination evolves

This has direct implications for **evaluation practices**. If a benchmark shows >50% contamination in recent crawls, researchers should:
- Question whether high scores reflect reasoning or memorization
- Consider using alternative benchmarks
- Report contamination rates alongside performance metrics

**Training Data Auditing for Compliance.** As language models face increasing regulatory scrutiny (copyright, safety, privacy), `INFINI-GRAM MINI` provides a tool for **auditing training data**:
- Verify whether copyrighted content appears in training corpora
- Check for sensitive or harmful content at scale
- Document data provenance for compliance reporting

The **0.44× storage multiplier** makes this feasible for organizations that cannot afford 6× or 29× storage overhead.

### 7.4 Integration and Reproducibility Guidance

Practitioners considering `INFINI-GRAM MINI` should understand when to use it versus alternatives, what hardware is required, and how to reproduce results.

**When to Prefer INFINI-GRAM MINI Over Alternatives.**

| Use Case | Recommended System | Rationale |
|----------|-------------------|-----------|
| Petabyte-scale indexing with limited storage | **INFINI-GRAM MINI** | 0.44× multiplier vs. 6× for suffix arrays |
| High-throughput querying (>100 QPS) | Original INFINI-GRAM | Millisecond latency vs. seconds |
| Multi-pattern co-occurrence queries | Original INFINI-GRAM | FM-index makes this impractical (Limitations) |
| Exact-match contamination detection | **INFINI-GRAM MINI** | Byte-level precision, low RAM |
| Fuzzy or semantic search | Neither (use ElasticSearch) | FM-index supports exact-match only |
| Limited query-time RAM (&lt;1 GB) | **INFINI-GRAM MINI** | ~30 MB RAM vs. full index in memory |
| Bulk document extraction | Original INFINI-GRAM | 4.5s vs. milliseconds per document |

The key decision factor is **storage budget vs. latency tolerance**. If you can afford 6× storage and need fast queries, use suffix arrays. If storage is constrained and queries are infrequent, use `INFINI-GRAM MINI`.

**Hardware Requirements.**

**For Index Construction:**
- **RAM:** 2 TiB per node (Section 3.1)
- **CPU:** 128 vCPUs recommended
- **Shard size:** Up to 700 GB per shard
- **Time:** 12–19 hours per shard (Table 1)

**For Querying:**
- **RAM:** ~30 MB (Section 3.2)
- **CPU:** 1 vCPU sufficient
- **Disk:** SSD with high IOPS (80,000 IOPS benchmarked in Appendix C)
- **Latency:** 0.4–25 seconds depending on query type and corpus size (Table 4, Appendix C)

The **asymmetry** is notable: construction requires high-end hardware, but querying works on modest machines. This enables a **centralized indexing, decentralized querying** model where one organization builds indexes and shares them publicly.

**Reproducibility Checklist.**

The paper provides strong reproducibility support:

- **Source code:** Available at `infini-gram-mini.io/code` (Abstract)
- **Web interface:** `infini-gram-mini.io/demo` (Abstract)
- **API endpoint:** `api.infini-gram-mini.io` (Abstract)
- **Documentation:** `infini-gram-mini.io/docs` (Abstract)
- **Contamination Bulletin:** `infini-gram-mini.io/bulletin` (Abstract)

To reproduce the contamination analysis:
1. Download or construct FM-index for target corpus (Pile, DCLM, or CC snapshots)
2. Extract 50-character substrings from benchmark entries with stride of one word (Section 4.1)
3. Query each substring using the `count` operation
4. Compute $\eta$ ratio and classify as Clean/Suspicious/Dirty (Section 4.1)
5. Optionally retrieve documents for contamination type analysis (Section 4.3)

**Known Reproducibility Challenges.**

- **Disk I/O variance:** Query latency depends on disk performance. The paper benchmarks on GCP SSDs with 80,000 IOPS (Appendix C); results will differ on slower disks.
- **Sampling rate tuning:** The choice of $a = 32$, $b = 64$ is empirical (Section 3.1). Different workloads may benefit from different rates, but no ablation study is provided.
- **Contamination thresholds:** The 20%/80% thresholds follow Touvron et al. (2023) but are not validated for this specific use case. Sensitivity to these thresholds is not reported.

**Integration with Existing Workflows.**

For researchers already using contamination detection tools:
- `INFINI-GRAM MINI` can **replace** smaller-scale exact-match systems when corpus size exceeds available storage
- The **API endpoint** enables programmatic integration with existing analysis pipelines
- Results can be **compared** with prior studies (e.g., Elazar et al., 2024) using the same contamination metrics

For organizations building training corpora:
- Integrate contamination checks into the **data curation pipeline**
- Use the Bulletin to **monitor benchmarks** relevant to your evaluation suite
- Consider **deduplication** against known benchmark sources before training

### 7.5 Summary: A Tool for Data Transparency

`INFINI-GRAM MINI` represents a shift toward **data transparency** in language model research. By making petabyte-scale search economically feasible and publicly accessible, it enables the community to:

- **Audit** training data at the scale it is actually used
- **Monitor** contamination as it evolves over time
- **Understand** how benchmarks propagate through the web ecosystem
- **Make informed decisions** about evaluation validity

The system's limitations (exact-match only, seconds-level latency, no co-occurrence queries) are explicit trade-offs for achieving **0.44× storage efficiency**. Understanding these boundaries ensures appropriate use: this is a tool for **audit and verification**, not for high-throughput search or bulk extraction.

The broader implication is methodological: as language models scale, our tools for understanding their training data must scale accordingly. `INFINI-GRAM MINI` demonstrates that this is possible without prohibitive costs, setting a precedent for future work on data transparency, evaluation integrity, and responsible AI development.
