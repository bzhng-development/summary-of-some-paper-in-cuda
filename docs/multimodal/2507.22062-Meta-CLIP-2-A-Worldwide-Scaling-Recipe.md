## 1. Executive Summary
Meta CLIP 2 solves the "curse of multilinguality" in Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining (`CLIP`), where scaling to worldwide web data typically degrades English performance due to inadequate curation and insufficient model capacity. This paper presents the first recipe to train `CLIP` from scratch on native worldwide image-text pairs across 300+ languages without machine translation, achieving a 0.8% improvement on `ImageNet` (81.3% vs 80.5%) over its English-only counterpart (Figure 1) and setting new state-of-the-art on multilingual benchmarks like `CVQA` (57.4%) and `Babel-ImageNet` (50.2%) (Table 1). The significance lies in demonstrating that English and non-English data become mutually beneficial when metadata, curation, and training capacity are scaled jointly, enabling full multilingual support without sacrificing English performance.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of Meta CLIP 2, we must first examine the current landscape of multimodal foundation models. While Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining (`CLIP`) has become a standard building block for vision systems, its development has been constrained by a reliance on English-centric data. This section details the specific technical gaps this paper addresses, the real-world implications of these limitations, and how prior attempts to solve them have fallen short.

### The English Bottleneck and the Curse of Multilinguality
`CLIP` models are typically trained on image-text pairs where the text is in English. This design choice discards a massive portion of available data: approximately $50.9\%$ of web content is non-English (Introduction). While one might assume that simply adding non-English data would improve model robustness, prior research has identified a phenomenon known as the **"curse of multilinguality."** In the context of `CLIP`, this refers to the observation that when a model is trained on worldwide (English + non-English) data, its performance on English benchmarks often degrades compared to a model trained on English data alone.

This performance trade-off is visually demonstrated in **Figure 1**, where a `ViT-L/14` model trained on worldwide data underperforms its English-only counterpart on `ImageNet`. The paper identifies two root causes for this phenomenon:
1.  **Lack of Curation:** There is no established method to curate non-English data at scale with the same rigor as English data (Challenge #1, Introduction).
2.  **Insufficient Capacity:** Existing models lack the capacity to learn from the increased diversity without sacrificing English proficiency (Challenge #2, Introduction).

This creates a dilemma for practitioners: optimize for English performance and ignore half the web, or accept degraded English capabilities to gain multilingual support. For instance, `mSigLIP` suffers a $1.5\%$ drop in `ImageNet` accuracy compared to its English-only counterpart, `SigLIP` (Introduction, Section 2.3).

### Limitations of Prior Multilingual Approaches
Before Meta CLIP 2, attempts to create multilingual `CLIP` models generally fell into two categories, both of which introduced significant drawbacks regarding data control and quality.

*   **Distillation from External Resources:** Many high-performing models rely on "distillation," where a student model learns from a teacher model's outputs rather than raw data. For example, `OpenCLIP` uses `LAION` data filtered by an existing `CLIP` model, and `DFN` uses a filter model trained on private data (Section 2.1). Similarly, `mSigLIP` and `SigLIP 2` rely on `WebLI`, a dataset derived from Google Image Search with a private processing pipeline (Section 2.1, Section 2.3).
    *   *The Shortcoming:* This approach yields an **untractable distribution**. The training data distribution is owned by the outsourcing party (e.g., the teacher model or private pipeline), making it impossible for researchers to understand or control what concepts the model is learning. It effectively black-boxes the data curation process.
*   **Machine Translation:** Some approaches attempt to multilingualize data by translating non-English captions into English, or vice versa (Section 2.3).
    *   *The Shortcoming:* This introduces **translation bias**. The model learns from synthetic text generated by machines rather than native speakers, which can distort semantic meaning and cultural context. Additionally, some models like `SigLIP 2` prioritize English performance by composing $90\%$ of their training data from English sources, which limits true multilingual capability (Section 2.3).

### Why Worldwide Scaling is Critical
The motivation to solve these problems extends beyond academic benchmarks; it addresses fundamental constraints in the future scaling of AI systems.

*   **Data Exhaustion:** High-quality English Internet data is a finite resource. Research suggests that English data sources may be exhausted soon, limiting the ability to scale models using English-only pipelines (Introduction, citing Villalobos et al., 2022). To continue scaling, models must effectively utilize the non-English $50.9\%$ of the web.
*   **Cultural Diversity and Bias:** English-centric training data inherits biases from North American and Western European content. This limits performance on region-specific tasks. For example, models trained without diverse data struggle with geo-localization and recognizing objects in different socioeconomic contexts (Section 4.2.3). Meta CLIP 2 aims to retain the entire global distribution of images to improve coverage in these areas (Introduction).
*   **Native-Language Supervision:** Learning directly from alt-texts written by native speakers provides a richer semantic signal than machine-translated text. This is crucial for applications requiring nuanced cultural understanding, such as multilingual visual question answering (Introduction).

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work
Meta CLIP 2 positions itself distinctly from prior "state-of-the-art" (SoTA) chasing systems. Many existing works achieve high performance by combining all available techniques, including private data and bespoke architecture changes, which introduces **confounding factors** (Introduction). It becomes unclear whether performance gains come from the data strategy or the system engineering.

In contrast, Meta CLIP 2 is designed as a **generalizable recipe**. It maximizes architectural overlap with the original OpenAI `CLIP` and English `Meta CLIP` (Introduction). By keeping the model architecture standard (e.g., `ViT-H/14`) and avoiding private data or translation, the authors isolate the variables of **metadata**, **curation**, and **training capacity**. This allows the findings to be attributed directly to the worldwide scaling strategy rather than system-level optimizations. The goal is not merely to set a benchmark record, but to provide a reproducible method for training `CLIP` from scratch on native worldwide image-text pairs, enabling the community to move beyond English-centric foundations without sacrificing performance (Conclusion).

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a **data curation and training recipe paper** that demonstrates how to scale Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining (`CLIP`) from English-only to worldwide multilingual data without degrading English performance. The core idea is that the "curse of multilinguality" is not an inherent limitation of multilingual training, but rather a consequence of insufficient metadata coverage, improper data balancing across languages, and inadequate model capacity—problems that can be solved through three coordinated innovations in metadata construction, curation algorithm design, and training framework scaling.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

Meta CLIP 2 is a complete training pipeline that transforms raw image-text pairs from the worldwide web into a balanced, multilingual training dataset and then trains a `CLIP` model on that data using scaled compute and model capacity. The solution addresses the worldwide scaling challenge through a three-stage recipe: (1) constructing metadata covering 300+ languages from Wikipedia and WordNet, (2) implementing per-language curation with language-specific balancing thresholds, and (3) scaling training batch size and model capacity proportionally to the increased data diversity.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The Meta CLIP 2 system consists of four major components that process data sequentially from raw internet scrapes to a trained multimodal model. **First**, the *Worldwide Metadata Construction* component takes Wikipedia dumps and WordNet synsets across 329 languages and produces language-specific concept dictionaries (Section 3.2). **Second**, the *Language Identification* module classifies each alt-text in the raw image-text pool into one of the supported languages using a language identification model (Section 3.3). **Third**, the *Per-Language Curation Algorithm* performs substring matching between alt-texts and their corresponding language metadata, computes global match counts per concept, and applies language-specific balancing thresholds to produce a curated dataset with controlled head-tail concept distributions (Section 3.3, Algorithm 1). **Fourth**, the *Worldwide Training Framework* takes the curated dataset and trains a `ViT-H/14` vision encoder with a multilingual text tokenizer using a 2.3× scaled batch size to ensure English data is not downsampled relative to English-only training (Section 3.4, Table 6). Information flows unidirectionally: raw data → language identification → metadata matching → balancing → curated dataset → model training.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the **worldwide metadata construction** because metadata defines what concepts the model can learn, and without proper multilingual metadata coverage, the curation algorithm cannot function correctly across languages.
- We then explain the **language identification and mapping** step since every image-text pair must be assigned to a language before language-specific curation can occur.
- We proceed to the **curation algorithm with per-language balancing** as this is the core innovation that replaces the English-only threshold with language-adaptive thresholds to maintain consistent head-tail concept distributions.
- We cover the **mathematical formulation of the balancing** to show precisely how sampling probabilities are computed from match counts and thresholds.
- We describe the **training framework modifications** including batch size scaling and model capacity requirements, as these determine whether the curated data can be effectively learned.
- We conclude with **implementation optimizations** that make worldwide-scale curation computationally feasible, since processing hundreds of languages introduces significant complexity.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Worldwide Metadata Construction

Meta CLIP 2 constructs metadata from the same four source types as English `Meta CLIP`, but extends coverage beyond English to 300+ languages. The metadata serves as a controlled vocabulary of visual concepts that the curation algorithm uses to identify and balance concepts in the training data. **First**, the system includes all synsets from Multilingual WordNet covering 31 languages, where each synset represents a distinct visual concept with language-specific lexical realizations. **Second**, the system processes Wikipedia unigrams and bigrams from Wikipedia dumps dated May 2024, which include corpora in 329 languages, using space and punctuation to tokenize text into words for most languages. **Third**, for languages without space separation (such as Chinese, Japanese, Thai, and other Asian languages), the system employs open-source tokenizers developed by local communities to properly split text into words while maintaining semantic integrity (Appendix Table 5). **Fourth**, the system extracts Wikipedia page titles from 40 random dates of Wikipedia snapshots and ranks these titles by click-through traffic for each language to prioritize high-salience concepts. The key design choice here is maintaining **independent metadata per language** rather than merging all languages into a single metadata set, because the same word can have different meanings across languages (e.g., "mit" means different things in English and German) and this design enables flexible addition of new languages in the future (Section 3.2). The ablation study in Table 2 confirms this design choice: merging all metadata without language separation yields worse English performance (62.1% on `ImageNet`) compared to language-isolated metadata (64.7% on `ImageNet`), demonstrating that language isolation is critical for maintaining English performance while building multilingual capability.

#### Language Identification and Mapping

Before curation can occur, each image-text pair in the raw data pool must be assigned to a language so that the correct language-specific metadata can be applied. The system uses a language identification (`LID`) model (Grave et al., 2018) to classify the language of each alt-text from an image-text pair. However, the set of languages covered by the `LID` model and the set of languages covered by metadata sources (e.g., Wikipedia) are usually different, requiring a mapping step. The system establishes a mapping between one language in `LID` to a unique set of languages in metadata entries, where languages in the metadata mapped to the same language in `LID` are merged into one group. This produces a dictionary representation of metadata $\mathcal{M}$ where the keys are each language in `LID` and the values are the combined metadata of each group of languages. The system also includes a key "other" for metadata of languages that cannot be associated with any language in `LID`, ensuring no data is discarded due to language classification failures. After language identification, each alt-text `text` in the data pool $\mathcal{D}$ is assigned a predicted language `text.lang`, which determines which subset of metadata $\mathcal{M}[\text{text.lang}]$ will be used for substring matching.

#### Per-Language Curation Algorithm with Adaptive Thresholds

The curation algorithm transforms the raw image-text pair distribution into a balanced distribution ready for training, and this is where Meta CLIP 2 introduces its most critical innovation: **language-specific balancing thresholds**. In English-only `CLIP` curation, a single threshold $t$ determines which concepts are "head" (frequent) versus "tail" (rare), where entries with match counts fewer than $t$ are tail entries and otherwise head. Image-text pairs from head concepts are downsampled by a sampling probability derived from $t$ to balance the training data distribution. OpenAI `CLIP` sets $t$ to 20k for 400M pairs, and English `Meta CLIP` raised $t$ to 170k for scaling to 2.5B English pairs while keeping the same ratio of 6% of matches from tail concepts (Section 3.1). However, for worldwide data, the data size and match counts differ greatly across languages, so applying a single threshold $t$ to all languages yields suboptimal performance—a larger $t$ for a language with fewer pairs may yield too many pairs of head concepts and dilute tail concepts in the curated data (Section 3.3).

To derive the threshold $t_{\text{lang}}$ for each language, Meta CLIP 2 leverages the **invariance assumption** adopted in the original `Meta CLIP` algorithm design: the percentage of tail matches should remain constant across languages. The paper fixes this percentage at 6%, matching what OpenAI `CLIP` used for its 400M English pairs. The threshold determination proceeds in two steps, implemented as the `t_to_p()` and `p_to_t()` functions in Algorithm 1. **Step 1 (From $t_{\text{en}}$ to $p$)**: The system calculates the global tail proportion $p$ for all languages based on matches of English tail entries decided by the English threshold $t_{\text{en}}$. Mathematically, this is computed as:

$$p = \frac{\sum_{e: \text{count}(e) &lt; t_{\text{en}}} \text{count}(e)}{\sum_{e} \text{count}(e)}$$

where $\text{count}(e)$ is the number of matches for entry $e$ in English, and $p$ represents the proportion of total matches that come from tail entries. **Step 2 (From $p$ to $t_{\text{lang}}$)**: For each non-English language, the system reversely finds the language-specific threshold $t_{\text{lang}}$ based on the calculated $p$ to ensure the same tail proportion across all languages. This is computed by sorting entry counts, computing cumulative sums, and finding the threshold where the cumulative proportion equals $p$:

$$t_{\text{lang}} = \text{count}_{(k)} \quad \text{where} \quad k = \arg\min_i \left| \frac{\sum_{j=1}^{i} \text{count}_{(j)}}{\sum_{j} \text{count}_{(j)}} - p \right|$$

where $\text{count}_{(j)}$ denotes the $j$-th smallest entry count. With $t_{\text{lang}}$ determined, entry counts are converted to entry probabilities $\text{entry\_prob}$ similarly as in English `Meta CLIP` but computed independently for each language.

#### Sampling Probability Computation and Dataset Construction

After thresholds are computed, the algorithm samples image-text pairs based on matched entries in the alt-text with probabilities $\text{entry\_prob}$. Pairs matched with tail entries (where $\text{count}(e) &lt; t_{\text{lang}}$) are always selected with probability 1.0, ensuring rare concepts are fully represented in the training data. Pairs with head entries have sampling probabilities computed as $t_{\text{lang}} / \text{count}(e)$, which downsample frequent concepts proportionally to their overrepresentation. The sampling process iterates through each image-text pair $(\text{image}, \text{text})$ in the raw pool $\mathcal{D}$, and for each matched entry ID in `text.matched_entry_ids`, the algorithm draws a random number and compares it against $\text{entry\_prob}[\text{text.lang}][\text{entry\_id}]$. If the random number is less than the probability, the pair is added to the curated dataset $\mathcal{D}^*$ and the algorithm breaks to avoid duplicate sampling of the same pair. This produces the final curated dataset $\mathcal{D}^*$ with balanced and diverse training pairs across all languages (Algorithm 1, Stage 3). The ablation study in Table 2 demonstrates the importance of language-specific thresholds: using the same $t_{\text{en}}$ for all languages yields 61.1% on `ImageNet` and 31.5% on `Babel-ImageNet`, while language-specific $t_{\text{lang}}$ improves these to 64.7% and 31.5% respectively, with further gains on multilingual retrieval benchmarks (Section 4.2.2).

#### Training Framework: Batch Size Scaling and Model Capacity

Adopting data prepared with worldwide curation in the current `CLIP` training framework addresses the first challenge (lack of curation), but the curse of multilinguality still exists as shown in Figure 1, requiring additional training framework modifications. Meta CLIP 2 follows OpenAI/Meta `CLIP`'s training setting and model architecture with three additions to make the framework and findings generalizable. **First**, the system uses a multilingual text tokenizer to support worldwide languages, with the ablation study in Table 3 showing that `XLM-V` with 900k vocabulary size yields the strongest performance in both English and non-English benchmarks compared to `mT5` (250k), `Gemma` (256k), and `XLM-Roberta` (250k). **Second**, the system scales seen training pairs proportionally to the growth of data size from non-English pairs to ensure the amount of English seen pairs remains unchanged during worldwide `CLIP` training. This is achieved by increasing the global training batch size, which encourages cross-lingual learning while keeping other training hyperparameters unchanged. The paper chooses a **2.3× scaling of global batch size** to reflect that English pairs constitute 44% of the training data (Section 3.4). **Third**, the system studies the minimal viable model capacity to enable learning on extra seen pairs and break the curse of multilinguality. As shown in Figure 1 and Table 1, a `ViT-L/14` (the largest model size used by OpenAI) still suffers from the curse due to deficient capacity even with 2.3× scaling, while `ViT-H/14` is the inflection point that breaks the curse with strong performance improvement in both English and non-English tasks.

The key hyperparameters are documented in Table 6 (Appendix B). The activation function remains `QuickGELU` matching OpenAI `CLIP` / English `Meta CLIP`. The seen pairs increase from 12.8B (English-only) to 29B (2.3×) for worldwide training. The batch size increases from 32,768 to 75,366 (2.3×) to accommodate the scaled data. The learning rate remains at $4.0 \times 10^{-4}$ for `ViT-H/14`, and warm-up steps remain at 2k. The ablation study in Table 1 demonstrates that these training framework modifications are essential: `Meta CLIP 2` with `ViT-H/14` and worldwide data at 2.3× scaling achieves 81.3% on `ImageNet`, surpassing the English-only counterpart at 80.5%, while `ViT-L/14` with worldwide data at 2.3× scaling only achieves 78.8% on `ImageNet`, still suffering from the curse (Section 4.2.1).

#### Implementation Optimizations for Worldwide-Scale Curation

Worldwide scaling of data curation significantly increases time and space complexity due to storing metadata across hundreds of languages, requiring several efficient implementations that English-only curation does not need. **First**, for efficient string matching, the system adopts the Aho-Corasick algorithm which utilizes prefix trees (tries) for rapid substring matching, achieving matching speed approximately 2,000 times faster than English `Meta CLIP`'s brute-force implementation and enabling matching with million-scale metadata (Appendix A.2). **Second**, for lazy metadata loading, the system pre-builds and stores the metadata into an Aho-Corasick automaton for each language separately, loading these automata dynamically and only when encountering a new language for alt-text during processing, thereby minimizing the total number of languages encountered for each shard of data and saving re-compiling time. **Third**, for memory management during probability computation, the system utilizes memory-mapped file loading (`mmap`) to efficiently access counts per entry across all languages, preventing out-of-memory errors caused by loading all counts from different languages simultaneously. These implementation choices ensure the worldwide data curation is computationally feasible and scalable to billions of image-text pairs from hundreds of languages. The curation algorithm runs in parallel with 800 jobs (each job has 40GB CPU memory) and takes 1 hour to substring match and count for all alt-text pairs (Appendix B).

#### Safety Filtering and Benchmark Deduplication

To ensure training data quality and prevent evaluation contamination, Meta CLIP 2 applies several filtering steps that are orthogonal to the language curation but essential for responsible deployment. The system runs a state-of-the-art safety classifier to remove NSFW contents (e.g., adult, sexual, violence) from training data. The system also applies a face detector to remove human biometrics and personally identifiable information from data. To avoid benchmark leakage, the system removes any overlap with `ImageNet` evaluation sets by performing deduplication using 64-bit hashes, where these hashes are generated by applying random projection to feature embeddings from a similarity search model, reducing them to 64 dimensions followed by sign-based quantization (Appendix A.2). These safety and deduplication steps ensure that performance gains reported in evaluation are not artifacts of training-test contamination or unsafe content learning.

#### Design Choice Rationale: Why This Approach Over Alternatives

The Meta CLIP 2 recipe makes several deliberate design choices that distinguish it from prior multilingual `CLIP` approaches, and each choice addresses a specific limitation identified in the motivation section. **Independent per-language metadata** is chosen over merged multilingual metadata because the ablation in Table 2 shows 2.6 percentage point improvement on `ImageNet` (64.7% vs 62.1%), demonstrating that language isolation prevents cross-lingual concept confusion. **Language-specific thresholds $t_{\text{lang}}$** are chosen over a single global threshold because different languages have vastly different data volumes in the raw web pool, and a single threshold would either over-represent head concepts in low-resource languages or under-represent tail concepts in high-resource languages. **2.3× batch size scaling** is chosen over keeping batch size constant because English pairs constitute only 44% of worldwide data, and constant batch size would downsample English training signal, directly causing the curse of multilinguality observed in Figure 1. **`ViT-H/14` capacity** is chosen over `ViT-L/14` because the ablation shows `ViT-L/14` still suffers the curse (78.8% `ImageNet`) while `ViT-H/14` breaks it (81.3% `ImageNet`), indicating that model expressivity must scale with data diversity. **Native alt-texts without translation** are chosen over machine-translated text because translation introduces synthetic bias and loses cultural nuance, while native supervision preserves the semantic richness that multilingual applications require. **No private data or distillation** is chosen over `WebLI`-based approaches because the goal is a reproducible, generalizable recipe that the community can adopt without relying on proprietary resources, enabling transparent understanding of what the model learns from the data distribution.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

Meta CLIP 2 introduces several conceptual shifts that distinguish it from prior multilingual vision-language models. While some components (like tokenizer swaps) are incremental engineering choices, the core contributions represent fundamental changes in how we understand data curation and scaling for multimodal models. Below, we analyze the four most critical innovations, distinguishing between what is merely improved and what is fundamentally rethought.

### 1. Reframing the "Curse of Multilinguality" as a Capacity Problem
Prior work treated the performance degradation on English tasks when adding non-English data as an inherent trade-off—a "curse" that practitioners must accept to gain multilingual capability. For example, `mSigLIP` accepts a $1.5\%$ drop in `ImageNet` accuracy to support multiple languages (Introduction). Meta CLIP 2 fundamentally challenges this assumption.

The paper demonstrates that the curse is not an intrinsic property of multilingual data, but rather a symptom of **insufficient model capacity** and **improper data balancing**. This is empirically proven in **Figure 1**, where a `ViT-L/14` model (the largest size from original OpenAI `CLIP`) still suffers from the curse when trained on worldwide data, but a `ViT-H/14` model breaks the curse entirely.
> "Although a ViT-L/14... still suffers the curse of multilinguality, ViT-H/14 breaks the curse. English accuracy rises from 80.5% to 81.3% on ImageNet." (Introduction)

This insight is significant because it shifts the solution space from "how do we protect English performance?" to "how do we scale capacity and curation to accommodate diversity?" It implies that future multilingual models do not need to sacrifice their primary language performance if compute and data engineering are scaled jointly.

### 2. Distributional Invariance Across Languages via Adaptive Thresholds
A major innovation in the curation algorithm is the assumption of **distributional invariance** across languages. Prior curation methods either applied no balancing to non-English data or used a single global threshold $t$ for all languages (Section 3.3). Meta CLIP 2 posits that the *proportion* of tail concepts (rare but important visual concepts) should remain constant across languages, even if the absolute data volume differs.

This is implemented via the language-specific threshold $t_{\text{lang}}$, derived to maintain a fixed tail proportion (approximately $6\%$, matching OpenAI `CLIP`'s original design). The significance of this is highlighted in **Table 2**, where using a single English threshold $t_{\text{en}}$ for all languages results in an `ImageNet` accuracy of $61.1\%$, whereas adaptive $t_{\text{lang}}$ recovers performance to $64.7\%$.
> "Applying a single threshold $t$ to all languages yields suboptimal performance, e.g., a larger $t$ for a language with fewer pairs may yield too many pairs of head concepts and dilutes tail concepts." (Section 3.3)

This is a **fundamental algorithmic innovation** rather than an incremental tweak. It ensures that low-resource languages are not overwhelmed by head concepts from high-resource languages, preserving the semantic diversity necessary for robust multilingual representation learning.

### 3. Mutual Benefit Through Proportional Scaling of Seen Pairs
A common misconception in multilingual training is that adding non-English data inevitably dilutes the English signal. Meta CLIP 2 shows that English and non-English data can be **mutually beneficial**, provided the training regime prevents English downsampling.

The key insight is that the total number of seen English pairs must remain constant compared to an English-only run. Since English data constitutes only $44\%$ of the worldwide pool, the global batch size must be scaled proportionally (by $2.3\times$) to ensure the model sees the same volume of English examples while also learning from non-English examples. **Table 1** confirms this synergy: the `Worldwide (2.3×)` run outperforms both the `English (1.0×)` and `Non-English (1.3×)` runs on their respective strong suits.
> "Meta CLIP 2 on ViT-H/14 with worldwide data and scaled seen pairs consistently outperforms its counterparts... on both English and multilingual tasks." (Section 4.2.1)

This finding is critical for the future of foundation models as English data sources approach exhaustion (Introduction). It validates the strategy of leveraging the remaining $50.9\%$ of non-English web data not just for multilingual support, but to enhance overall model robustness.

### 4. Native Supervision vs. Black-Box Distillation
While many high-performing multilingual models rely on distillation from teacher models or machine translation (Section 2.3), Meta CLIP 2 adheres to a **native supervision philosophy**. The model learns directly from alt-texts written by native speakers across 300+ languages, without translating text to English or filtering based on language.

This distinguishes Meta CLIP 2 from systems like `mSigLIP` or `SigLIP 2`, which rely on `WebLI`, a dataset with a private processing pipeline (Section 2.1). The significance here is **transparency and controllability**. By curating from scratch using public metadata (Wikipedia, WordNet), the training distribution is tractable and understood, rather than being an opaque output of a teacher model.
> "Meta CLIP 2 removes the last filter (i.e., whether the alt-text is in English) in pipeline, achieving better diversity and minimizing biases introduced by filters." (Introduction)

This is a **methodological innovation** that prioritizes data provenance. It enables researchers to audit what concepts are learned (via the metadata matching in Section 3.3) and reduces biases introduced by machine translation errors or teacher model blind spots. The result is a model that sets new state-of-the-art on cultural diversity benchmarks like `Dollar Street` and `GeoDE` (**Table 4**, **Figure 3**) without relying on private data resources.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their claims about breaking the "curse of multilinguality." The experimental design is notably rigorous: rather than simply reporting final performance numbers, the authors construct a series of controlled ablations that isolate each component of their recipe. This allows us to understand not just *that* Meta CLIP 2 works, but *why* each design choice matters. We will walk through the evaluation methodology, main results, ablation studies, and critical assessment of the evidence.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Benchmark Selection

The authors evaluate Meta CLIP 2 across two distinct categories of benchmarks, each serving a specific purpose in validating their claims.

**English-Only Benchmarks** test whether worldwide training degrades English performance—the core claim about breaking the curse of multilinguality:
- **ImageNet (`IN val`)**: Zero-shot classification on 1,000 classes, the standard benchmark for `CLIP` English performance (Russakovsky et al., 2015).
- **SLIP 26 tasks (`SLIP 26 avg.`)**: Average accuracy across 26 diverse vision tasks including object recognition, scene classification, and texture recognition (Mu et al., 2021).
- **DataComp 37 tasks (`DC 37 avg.`)**: Average performance across 37 downstream tasks from the DataComp benchmark suite (Gadre et al., 2023).

**Multilingual Benchmarks** test whether the model achieves genuine multilingual capability rather than just preserving English:
- **Babel-ImageNet (`Babel-IN`)**: Zero-shot classification on ImageNet with classes and prompts translated into 280 languages, reporting average accuracy (Geigle et al., 2024).
- **XM3600**: Multilingual image-text retrieval across 36 languages, reporting Recall@1 for both text-to-image (`T→I`) and image-to-text (`I→T`) directions (Thapliyal et al., 2022).
- **CVQA**: Culturally-diverse multilingual visual question answering with English (`EN`) and local (`LOCAL`) language subsets, reporting average answer accuracy (Mogrovejo et al., 2024).
- **Flickr30k-200**: Flickr30k test set translated into 200 languages, measuring retrieval Recall@1 (Visheratin, 2023).
- **XTD-10 and XTD-200**: Multilingual image-text retrieval on MSCOCO averaged over 7 and 200 languages respectively (Aggarwal and Kale, 2020; Visheratin, 2023).

**Baselines for Comparison** include both English-only and multilingual `CLIP` variants:
- **Meta CLIP (English-only)**: The direct predecessor, trained on 2.5B English pairs with `ViT-L/14` and `ViT-H/14` (Xu et al., 2024).
- **mSigLIP**: Multilingual `CLIP` trained on `WebLI` (12B pairs) with `ViT-B/16` and `ViT-SO400M` (Zhai et al., 2023).
- **SigLIP 2**: Recent multilingual model also trained on `WebLI` with English-centric design (90% English data) (Tschannen et al., 2025).
- **XLM-CLIP**: Earlier multilingual attempt trained on `LAION-5B` (Ilharco et al., 2021).

**Training Setup** follows OpenAI `CLIP` and English `Meta CLIP` with minimal changes to ensure generalizability (Appendix Table 6):
- Activation function: `QuickGELU` (unchanged from OpenAI `CLIP`)
- Learning rate: $4.0 \times 10^{-4}$ for `ViT-H/14`
- Warm-up steps: 2,000
- Seen pairs: 12.8B (English-only) → 29B (Worldwide 2.3×)
- Batch size: 32,768 (English-only) → 75,366 (Worldwide 2.3×)

The authors explicitly note that they grey out baselines that are "SoTA-aiming systems with confounding factors" in Table 1, acknowledging that `mSigLIP` and `SigLIP 2` use private data (`WebLI`) and system-level optimizations that make direct comparison unfair. This transparency is important for interpreting the results.

The central claim—that worldwide training can improve English performance while achieving state-of-the-art multilingual capability—is supported by **Table 1**. Let us examine the key comparisons systematically.

| mSigLIP | SO400M | WebLI (12B) | 40B (3.0×) | 80.6% |
| SigLIP 2 | SO400M | WebLI (12B) | 40B (3.0×) | 83.2% |

The critical observation is that **Meta CLIP 2 ViT-H/14 with worldwide data (81.3%) surpasses its English-only counterpart (80.5%) by 0.8 percentage points**. This directly contradicts the curse of multilinguality, which predicts English performance should degrade when non-English data is added. However, note the capacity dependency: `ViT-L/14` with worldwide data achieves only 78.8%, still suffering from the curse. This confirms the paper's claim that model capacity must scale with data diversity.

| Meta CLIP 2 (Worldwide 2.3×) | **50.2%** | **64.3%** | **57.4%** |

Meta CLIP 2 sets new state-of-the-art on all three multilingual benchmarks despite using:

- **Lower resolution**: 224px vs 256px (mSigLIP/SigLIP 2)
- **Comparable architecture**: `ViT-H/14` vs `ViT-SO400M`

The gains are substantial: **+3.8% on Babel-ImageNet**, **+1.5% on XM3600 I→T**, and **+7.6% on CVQA LOCAL**. The CVQA improvement is particularly notable because it measures culturally-diverse visual question answering, directly validating the cultural diversity claims.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies: Validating Each Component

The authors conduct three major ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each recipe component. These ablations are essential for understanding which design choices are necessary versus optional.

#### Metadata and Curation Ablation (Table 2)

This study uses `ViT-B/32` for efficiency and tests the transition from English-only to worldwide curation:

| Step | Metadata | Alt-texts | ImageNet | Babel-IN | XM3600 I→T |
|------|----------|-----------|----------|----------|------------|
| 1: English CLIP | English | English | 67.5% | - | - |
| 2: Remove English filter | English | All (1 set) | 66.9% | - | - |
| 3: No language isolation | All (1 set) | All (1 set) | 62.1% | 31.2% | 37.8% |
| 4: Language isolation, $t_{\text{lang}} = t_{\text{en}}$ | By lang. | By lang. | 61.1% | 31.5% | 37.9% |
| 5: Language-specific $t_{\text{lang}}$ | By lang. | By lang. | **64.7%** | 31.5% | **38.1%** |

**Key findings:**
- **Step 2** (removing English filter but using English metadata): 0.6% drop on ImageNet, indicating that simply including non-English text without proper metadata matching harms English performance.
- **Step 3** (merged multilingual metadata): Significant degradation to 62.1% on ImageNet, confirming that language isolation is critical. The same word having different meanings across languages (e.g., "mit" in English vs German) causes concept confusion.
- **Step 4** (language isolation with single threshold): Further drops to 61.1%, showing that applying English threshold $t_{\text{en}}$ to all languages is suboptimal for low-resource languages.
- **Step 5** (language-specific thresholds): Recovers to 64.7% on ImageNet and improves multilingual retrieval, validating the adaptive threshold design.

The authors note that "curse of multilinguality remains unresolved in ViT-B/32 until the main ablation described above" (Section 4.2.2), acknowledging that model capacity also matters—this ablation isolates curation effects but cannot break the curse without sufficient capacity.

**XLM-V** with 900k vocabulary yields the strongest performance across all benchmarks. The larger vocabulary likely better captures morphological diversity across 300+ languages. Notably, `mT5` (used by mSigLIP) performs competitively on English but lags on multilingual retrieval, suggesting vocabulary size matters more for cross-lingual alignment than English-only tasks.

**Meta CLIP 2 H/14 (WW 2.3×)** achieves competitive scores on both metrics compared to mSigLIP and SigLIP 2 variants. ### 5.4 Critical Assessment: Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

The experimental evidence strongly supports the paper's core claims, but with important conditions and limitations.

**Claim 1: "Curse of Multilinguality" Can Be Broken**
- **Supported**: Table 1 shows 81.3% (Worldwide) vs 80.5% (English-only) on ImageNet with ViT-H/14.
- **Condition**: Requires `ViT-H/14` capacity; `ViT-L/14` still suffers the curse (78.8%).
- **Condition**: Requires 2.3× batch size scaling; 1.0× scaling degrades English performance (79.5%).

**Claim 2: English and Non-English Data Are Mutually Beneficial**
- **Supported**: Worldwide 2.3× outperforms both English-only and Non-English-only on their respective strong suits (Table 1).
- **Mechanism validated**: Ablation shows scaling seen pairs prevents English downsampling (Section 4.2.1).

**Claim 3: State-of-the-Art Multilingual Performance**
- **Supported**: New SoTA on Babel-ImageNet (50.2%), XM3600 (64.3% I→T), CVQA (57.4%).
- **Caveat**: Baselines like SigLIP 2 use more seen pairs (40B vs 29B) and higher resolution (256px vs 224px), making direct comparison imperfect. The authors acknowledge this by greying out SoTA-aiming systems in Table 1.

**Claim 4: Improved Cultural Diversity**
- **Supported**: Table 4 and Figure 3 show consistent improvements on Dollar Street, GLDv2, and few-shot geo-localization.
- **Limitation**: Appendix C acknowledges that existing benchmarks are still biased toward North America and Western Europe, limiting comprehensive evaluation of worldwide representation quality.

**Potential Weaknesses and Open Questions:**

1. **Compute Requirements**: Breaking the curse requires `ViT-H/14` (632M parameters) rather than `ViT-L/14` (307M parameters), approximately doubling compute costs. This may limit accessibility for researchers with constrained resources.

2. **Benchmark Coverage**: Appendix C explicitly notes that "high-quality benchmarks... consist mainly of content scraped from North America and Western Europe." While Meta CLIP 2 improves on available diversity benchmarks, the evaluation itself may not fully capture worldwide representation quality.

3. **Holdout Data Contamination**: The alignment/uniformity analysis (Figure 4) uses 5k holdout pairs, but the authors cannot guarantee these pairs did not leak into baseline training data (Section 4.2.4). This introduces uncertainty in embedding quality comparisons.

4. **Language Coverage Details**: While the paper claims 300+ languages, Appendix Table 5 shows special tokenizers are only provided for 8 language groups (Tibetan, Japanese, Khmer, Lao, Myanmar, Thai, Chinese). The tokenization approach for other non-space-separated languages is not fully specified.

5. **Training Stability**: The paper does not report training convergence curves or loss trajectories, making it unclear whether worldwide training introduces optimization challenges beyond capacity and batch size scaling.

The experiments are well-designed to isolate the contribution of each recipe component. The ablation studies (Tables 2, 3) particularly strengthen the causal claims by showing performance degradation when individual components are removed or modified. However, readers should note the capacity requirement (`ViT-H/14`) and benchmark limitations (Appendix C) when interpreting the results for their own applications.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Meta CLIP 2 demonstrates that the "curse of multilinguality" can be broken through careful data curation and capacity scaling, the approach is not without significant constraints. This section examines the assumptions underlying the recipe, the scenarios where it may not generalize, and the practical trade-offs practitioners must consider when adopting this approach. Understanding these limitations is essential for determining whether Meta CLIP 2 is appropriate for a given use case.

### 6.1 Core Assumptions and Their Validity

The Meta CLIP 2 recipe relies on several foundational assumptions that enable its design choices. When these assumptions hold, the approach performs well; when they break down, performance may degrade unpredictably.

**Assumption 1: Distributional Invariance Across Languages**

The curation algorithm assumes that the proportion of tail concepts (rare but important visual concepts) should remain constant across all languages—specifically fixed at approximately $6\%$, matching OpenAI `CLIP`'s original English-only design (Section 3.3). This is implemented via the language-specific threshold $t_{\text{lang}}$, derived to maintain this tail proportion regardless of language.

*   **Why this matters:** This assumption enables the algorithm to compute $t_{\text{lang}}$ for low-resource languages without manual tuning. Without it, each language would require independent threshold calibration, making worldwide scaling intractable.
*   **Where it may fail:** Languages with fundamentally different concept distributions (e.g., languages with richer morphological structures or different cultural concept hierarchies) may not conform to the $6\%$ tail proportion. The paper does not validate this assumption per-language, instead treating it as a global invariant. If a language naturally has $15\%$ tail concepts, forcing it to $6\%$ could either oversample rare noise or undersample genuine rare concepts.
*   **Evidence:** The ablation in Table 2 shows that language-specific $t_{\text{lang}}$ improves over a single global $t_{\text{en}}$, but this validates the *adaptive threshold* design, not the specific $6\%$ proportion choice.

**Assumption 2: Language Identification Accuracy**

The curation pipeline depends on accurate language identification (`LID`) to assign each alt-text to the correct metadata subset (Section 3.3). Misclassification would apply the wrong language's metadata, corrupting the concept matching.

*   **Why this matters:** If an English alt-text is misclassified as German, the German metadata would be used for substring matching, likely finding few or incorrect concept matches.
*   **Where it may fail:** Code-switched text (mixing multiple languages in one sentence), low-resource languages not well-represented in the `LID` model, or short alt-texts with insufficient signal could all cause misclassification. The paper mentions an "other" key for languages that cannot be associated with any `LID` language (Section 3.3), but does not report what percentage of data falls into this category or how it affects training.
*   **Evidence:** The paper cites Grave et al. (2018) for `LID` but does not report `LID` accuracy metrics on their specific data distribution.

**Assumption 3: Metadata Coverage Reflects Visual Concept Distribution**

The metadata construction assumes that Wikipedia and WordNet synsets adequately represent the visual concepts present in worldwide web images (Section 3.2).

*   **Why this matters:** If a visual concept exists in images but has no corresponding metadata entry in a given language, the curation algorithm cannot identify or balance it.
*   **Where it may fail:** Region-specific objects, cultural practices, or emerging concepts not yet documented in Wikipedia/WordNet would be invisible to the curation process. This is particularly problematic for low-resource languages where Wikipedia coverage is sparse.
*   **Evidence:** Appendix C acknowledges that "high-quality benchmarks... consist mainly of content scraped from North America and Western Europe," suggesting that even evaluation datasets may not fully capture worldwide concept diversity. If benchmarks are biased, metadata derived from similar sources may inherit the same bias.

### 6.2 Computational and Scalability Constraints

Breaking the curse of multilinguality comes with substantial compute requirements that may limit accessibility for researchers and organizations with constrained resources.

**Model Capacity Requirement**

The paper explicitly demonstrates that model capacity is not optional—it is a prerequisite for breaking the curse. **Figure 1** and **Table 1** show that `ViT-L/14` (307M parameters) still suffers from the curse with worldwide data (78.8% `ImageNet`), while `ViT-H/14` (632M parameters) breaks it (81.3% `ImageNet`).

*   **Trade-off:** Practitioners cannot simply apply the Meta CLIP 2 curation recipe to a smaller model and expect the same benefits. The capacity requirement approximately doubles the parameter count and associated training compute compared to the original OpenAI `CLIP` largest model.
*   **Implication:** For resource-constrained settings where `ViT-L/14` is already at the budget limit, the worldwide scaling benefits may not be accessible without accepting English performance degradation.

**Batch Size Scaling**

The training framework requires a $2.3\times$ increase in global batch size (from 32,768 to 75,366) to ensure English pairs are not downsampled relative to English-only training (Section 3.4, Appendix Table 6).

*   **Trade-off:** Larger batch sizes require more GPU memory and may necessitate gradient accumulation or distributed training setups that increase communication overhead. The paper does not discuss whether this batch size scaling introduces optimization challenges (e.g., learning rate warmup adjustments, gradient noise reduction).
*   **Implication:** Organizations with limited GPU clusters may struggle to achieve the required batch size without sacrificing training efficiency or stability.

**Curation Infrastructure**

The worldwide curation process requires significant infrastructure: 800 parallel jobs, each with 40GB CPU memory, running for approximately 1 hour to process all alt-text pairs (Appendix B).

*   **Trade-off:** While the Aho-Corasick algorithm provides $2,000\times$ speedup over brute-force matching (Appendix A.2), the absolute compute requirement remains substantial. The lazy metadata loading and memory-mapped file optimizations are necessary but add implementation complexity.
*   **Implication:** Reproducing the curation pipeline requires non-trivial engineering effort beyond the algorithmic description. The paper provides code (Introduction), but the infrastructure requirements may be prohibitive for smaller teams.

### 6.3 Scenarios and Edge Cases Not Addressed

The paper focuses on zero-shot classification and retrieval benchmarks, leaving several important scenarios unexplored.

**Low-Resource Language Performance**

While the paper claims coverage of 300+ languages, the evaluation benchmarks do not uniformly cover all languages. **Babel-ImageNet** covers 280 languages, but **XM3600** covers only 36 languages, and **XTD-10** covers only 7 languages (Section 4.2.1).

*   **Gap:** Performance on the lowest-resource languages (those with minimal Wikipedia presence or no WordNet coverage) is not isolated in the results. The aggregate multilingual scores may be dominated by higher-resource languages like Spanish, French, or Chinese.
*   **Open Question:** Does Meta CLIP 2 actually improve performance for languages with fewer than 1M Wikipedia articles, or does it primarily benefit mid-resource languages?

**Code-Switched and Mixed-Language Text**

The curation algorithm assigns each alt-text to a single language via `LID` (Section 3.3). However, real-world web data often contains code-switched text (e.g., Hindi-English mixing, Spanglish).

*   **Gap:** The paper does not describe how code-switched text is handled. If `LID` picks one dominant language, metadata from the other language(s) in the text would be ignored, potentially losing concept matches.
*   **Implication:** In multilingual regions where code-switching is common (e.g., India, Southeast Asia, Latin America), the curation may be suboptimal.

**Downstream Task Generalization Beyond Benchmarks**

The paper mentions that `CLIP` models serve as vision encoders in multimodal large language models (`MLLMs`) (Introduction, Section 2.2), but does not evaluate Meta CLIP 2 in this capacity.

*   **Gap:** It is unclear whether the worldwide training improves `MLLM` performance on multilingual tasks, or whether the `ViT-H/14` capacity requirement creates bottlenecks for `MLLM` training (which often uses smaller vision encoders for efficiency).
*   **Open Question:** Does the improved cultural diversity (Table 4, Figure 3) translate to better `MLLM` reasoning on region-specific queries?

**Training Convergence and Stability**

The paper reports final accuracy metrics but does not include training loss curves, convergence rates, or stability analysis.

*   **Gap:** It is unknown whether worldwide training introduces optimization challenges (e.g., slower convergence, higher loss variance, sensitivity to learning rate). The $2.3\times$ batch size scaling may affect gradient noise and require learning rate adjustments not discussed in the paper.
*   **Implication:** Practitioners adopting this recipe may need to invest in hyperparameter tuning to achieve stable training, especially if their data distribution differs from the paper's.

### 6.4 Evaluation Limitations and Benchmark Bias

The authors are transparent about limitations in the evaluation itself, which is important for interpreting the results.

**Geographic Bias in Benchmarks**

Appendix C explicitly acknowledges that "high-quality benchmarks... consist mainly of content scraped from North America and Western Europe (NA+EU)." Even benchmarks designed for diversity, like `XM3600`, have been critiqued for Western bias (Appendix C, citing Pouget et al., 2024).

*   **Implication:** The reported improvements on cultural diversity benchmarks (Table 4, Figure 3) may underestimate the true gap between English-only and worldwide training. If benchmarks themselves are biased, they cannot fully measure worldwide representation quality.
*   **Open Question:** What would performance look like on benchmarks specifically designed for underrepresented regions (e.g., rural Africa, Southeast Asia, South America)?

**Holdout Data Contamination Risk**

The alignment and uniformity analysis (Figure 4) uses 5k holdout image-text pairs, but the authors explicitly state: "we have no control on whether these 5k pairs are leaked in other baselines" (Section 4.2.4).

*   **Implication:** Comparisons of embedding quality between Meta CLIP 2 and baselines like `mSigLIP` or `SigLIP 2` may be confounded by training-test contamination in the baselines. The Meta CLIP 2 scores are more trustworthy (since the holdout is from their own data), but baseline scores may be artificially inflated or deflated.
*   **Limitation:** This reduces confidence in the embedding quality comparisons, though it does not affect the zero-shot classification results which use standard public benchmarks.

**Incomplete Language Tokenizer Coverage**

Appendix Table 5 lists special tokenizers for only 8 language groups (Tibetan, Japanese, Khmer, Lao, Myanmar, Thai, Chinese). For other non-space-separated languages, the paper states "we use open-source tokenizers developed by local communities" (Section 3.2) but does not specify which ones.

*   **Gap:** It is unclear whether all 329 languages with Wikipedia dumps have appropriate tokenizers, or whether some languages are tokenized suboptimally (e.g., using space-based splitting on languages without spaces).
*   **Implication:** Performance for languages not covered by the listed tokenizers may be degraded due to poor text segmentation during metadata construction.

### 6.6 Open Questions for Future Work

Several important questions remain unanswered by this work:

1.  **What is the minimum viable capacity for different language subsets?** Could a mixture-of-experts approach allow smaller models to handle worldwide data efficiently?

2.  **How does the $6\%$ tail proportion assumption vary across language families?** Empirical validation per language family could refine the curation algorithm.

3.  **What is the impact on MLLM downstream tasks?** Evaluating Meta CLIP 2 as a vision encoder in multilingual `MLLMs` would validate its utility beyond standalone `CLIP` applications.

4.  **Can the curation pipeline be made more accessible?** The infrastructure requirements (800 jobs, Aho-Corasick automata) may be prohibitive for smaller teams; simplified variants could broaden adoption.

5.  **How does performance scale beyond 29B seen pairs?** The paper does not explore whether further scaling continues to improve both English and multilingual performance, or whether diminishing returns set in.

These limitations do not invalidate the core contribution—Meta CLIP 2 demonstrably breaks the curse of multilinguality with a reproducible recipe. However, they define the boundaries within which the approach is most effective and highlight areas where future research can build upon this foundation.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

Meta CLIP 2 does more than achieve a new benchmark score; it fundamentally alters the strategy for scaling multimodal foundation models. By demonstrating that non-English data is not a compromise but a catalyst for performance, this work shifts the field from an English-centric paradigm to a worldwide scaling paradigm. This section explores how this shift changes research priorities, enables new applications, and provides concrete guidance for practitioners integrating these models.

### 7.1 Shifting the Paradigm of Multimodal Scaling

The most profound implication of Meta CLIP 2 is the refutation of the "curse of multilinguality" as an inherent constraint. Prior to this work, the standard industry heuristic was to prioritize English data to maintain core performance, treating non-English data as secondary or noisy. For instance, `SigLIP 2` explicitly adopts an English-centric design with $90\%$ of its training data in English to protect English performance (Section 2.3). Meta CLIP 2 proves this heuristic is obsolete when curation and capacity are scaled correctly.

*   **From Protection to Integration:** The result in **Figure 1**—where `ViT-H/14` worldwide training *improves* English `ImageNet` accuracy from $80.5\%$ to $81.3\%$—implies that future scaling laws must account for linguistic diversity as a positive signal. As English internet data approaches exhaustion (Introduction, citing Villalobos et al., 2022), the non-English $50.9\%$ of the web is not just a reserve; it is a necessary engine for continued performance gains.
*   **Transparency as a Standard:** By relying on public metadata (Wikipedia, WordNet) and a transparent curation algorithm (Algorithm 1) rather than private distillation pipelines like `WebLI` (Section 2.1), Meta CLIP 2 sets a new standard for reproducibility. This shifts the field away from "black-box" performance gains toward auditable data engineering. Researchers can now trace performance changes back to specific metadata concepts rather than opaque teacher models.

### 7.2 Enabling New Research Avenues

The open-sourcing of the metadata, curation code, and training recipe (Introduction) unlocks several specific research directions that were previously blocked by data opacity or performance trade-offs.

*   **Auditable Concept Coverage:** Because the training distribution is defined by human-curated metadata (Section 3.2), researchers can now audit exactly which visual concepts are being upsampled or downsampled. This enables **concept-level debugging**. For example, if a model fails on a specific cultural artifact, researchers can check the metadata count for that concept in the relevant language and adjust the threshold $t_{\text{lang}}$ accordingly. This is impossible with distillation-based models where the data distribution is owned by an outsourcing party (Section 2.1).
*   **Multilingual MLLM Backbones:** `CLIP` models are frequently used as vision encoders for Multimodal Large Language Models (`MLLMs`) (Section 2.2). Prior multilingual `CLIP` variants often introduced translation bias or degraded English vision features, harming the `MLLM`'s reasoning. Meta CLIP 2 provides a **native multilingual vision encoder** that preserves English performance while adding global coverage. This enables `MLLMs` to process non-English visual queries without relying on error-prone machine translation pipelines.
*   **Synergy with Self-Supervised Learning (SSL):** The Introduction notes that Meta CLIP data benefits SSL efforts like `Web-DINO` (Fan et al., 2025). Since Meta CLIP 2 extends this curation to worldwide data, it enables **language-free visual representation learning** on a global scale. Researchers can now train SSL models on worldwide data without being constrained by English caption quality, potentially improving robustness in low-text regions.
*   **Cultural AI Benchmarking:** While Appendix C notes current benchmark biases, Meta CLIP 2 provides the model capacity to drive the creation of better benchmarks. The significant gains on `Dollar Street` and `GeoDE` (**Table 4**, **Figure 3**) demonstrate that models are now capable of learning geographic diversity; the bottleneck shifts to evaluation. This encourages the community to invest in non-Western evaluation datasets to match the model's new capabilities.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

For practitioners deploying vision-language systems, Meta CLIP 2 offers specific advantages in scenarios requiring global reach or fairness.

*   **Global Search and Retrieval:** The state-of-the-art results on `XM3600` ($64.3\%$ image-to-text retrieval) and `Flickr30k-200$ (**Table 1**) directly translate to improved cross-lingual image search. A practical application is **multilingual e-commerce**, where a user querying in Thai or Portuguese can retrieve relevant products indexed with native descriptions, without relying on query translation.
*   **Region-Specific Recognition:** The improvements in geo-localization (**Figure 3**) and cultural diversity benchmarks (**Table 4**) make this model suitable for **deployment in the Global South**. Applications in agriculture (crop identification), healthcare (region-specific symptoms), or infrastructure (local vehicle types) benefit from the model's exposure to non-Western visual distributions. The "no-filter philosophy" (Introduction) ensures these regions are not systematically excluded by English-based safety or quality filters.
*   **Bias Mitigation in Content Moderation:** Because the model retains the entire global distribution of images (Introduction), it is less likely to flag culturally normal content as anomalous. This is critical for **content moderation systems** operating across multiple countries, where Western-centric models often exhibit high false-positive rates on local cultural practices.
*   **Native-Language Visual Assistants:** For visual question answering (`CVQA`), the $+7.6\%$ improvement on local language subsets (**Table 1**) enables more accurate **visual assistants for non-English speakers**. Unlike models trained on machine-translated captions, Meta CLIP 2 understands native semantic nuances, reducing errors in tasks like reading local signage or interpreting cultural context in images.

### 7.4 Integration and Reproducibility Guidance

Adopting Meta CLIP 2 requires careful consideration of compute resources and deployment constraints. Based on the ablation studies and limitations analyzed in this paper, here is a decision framework for practitioners.

*   **When to Prefer Meta CLIP 2:**
    *   **Requirement:** You need high performance on *both* English and multilingual tasks with a single model.
    *   **Constraint:** You have sufficient compute to train or fine-tune a `ViT-H/14` model. The paper explicitly shows `ViT-L/14` fails to break the curse of multilinguality (**Figure 1**, **Table 1**).
    *   **Value:** You prioritize data transparency and want to avoid private data dependencies (e.g., `WebLI`).
    *   **Use Case:** Global deployment where cultural bias is a risk (e.g., public sector, international commerce).

*   **When to Stick with English-Only Meta CLIP:**
    *   **Requirement:** Your deployment is strictly English-only.
    *   **Constraint:** You are compute-constrained and cannot support `ViT-H/14` or the $2.3\times$ batch size scaling.
    *   **Reasoning:** If you cannot scale capacity, adding non-English data will degrade English performance due to the curse of multilinguality (**Section 4.2.1**). In this regime, English-only curation remains optimal.

*   **Critical Integration Checks:**
    *   **Tokenizer Selection:** Do not use the default English tokenizer. The ablation in **Table 3** shows `XLM-V` (900k vocab) significantly outperforms `mT5` and `Gemma` on multilingual tasks. Swapping the tokenizer is a minimal change with maximal impact.
    *   **Batch Size Scaling:** If fine-tuning on worldwide data, you must scale the batch size proportionally to prevent English downsampling. The paper uses a $2.3\times$ increase (from 32,768 to 75,366) to reflect the $44\%$ English data ratio (**Section 3.4**, **Appendix Table 6**). Keeping the batch size constant will reproduce the "curse" performance drop.
    *   **Curation Reproduction:** If curating your own worldwide data, you must implement language-specific thresholds $t_{\text{lang}}$. Using a single global threshold $t$ degrades performance (**Table 2**, Step 4 vs. Step 5). The provided code repository (Introduction) includes the Aho-Corasick implementation necessary for efficient matching at this scale.

*   **Known Integration Risks:**
    *   **Low-Resource Languages:** While 300+ languages are supported, special tokenizers are only explicitly listed for 8 language groups (**Appendix Table 5**). For other non-space-separated languages, verify tokenization quality before deployment.
    *   **Benchmark Bias:** Remember that evaluation benchmarks still skew North American/Western European (**Appendix C**). Do not assume state-of-the-art scores on `ImageNet` guarantee performance in underrepresented regions without local validation.

By following this recipe, practitioners can move beyond the English bottleneck without sacrificing core capabilities. Meta CLIP 2 demonstrates that with the right curation and capacity, the worldwide web is not a noise source to be filtered, but a signal to be scaled.
