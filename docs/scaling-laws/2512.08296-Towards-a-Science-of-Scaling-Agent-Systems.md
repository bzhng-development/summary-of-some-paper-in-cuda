## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the lack of principled design choices for multi-agent systems by deriving quantitative scaling principles across 180 controlled configurations spanning five architectures, three LLM families, and four agentic benchmarks. The authors develop a predictive model achieving cross-validated $R^2=0.524$ that identifies critical trade-offs, such as the +80.8% performance gain on Finance-Agent versus -70% degradation on PlanCraft, enabling optimal architecture selection for 87% of held-out tasks. This framework matters because it shifts deployment from heuristic guessing to measurement-driven decisions, validated even on unseen frontier models like GPT-5.2 with MAE=0.071.

## 2. Context and Motivation

The rapid adoption of language model (LM)-based agents has created a critical disconnect between deployment scale and scientific understanding. While agents—systems capable of reasoning, planning, and acting through iterative environmental feedback—have become the dominant paradigm for real-world AI applications (Section 1), practitioners currently lack principled design choices. Instead of relying on quantitative laws, engineers depend on heuristics to decide when to deploy a single agent versus a multi-agent team. This section explains why this gap exists, why it matters for both theory and deployment, and how prior evaluation methods have obscured the true dynamics of agent scaling.

### The Design Void: Heuristics vs. Science
The core problem this paper addresses is the absence of a **quantitative scaling framework** for agent systems. In traditional deep learning, neural scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020) allow practitioners to predict performance gains from increasing model parameters. However, for agentic systems, no equivalent principles exist. The field is currently polarized by conflicting claims:
*   **Optimistic View:** Some prior work suggests "More agents is all you need" (Li et al., 2024), implying collaborative scaling is universally beneficial.
*   **Skeptical View:** Conversely, recent studies indicate that single strong models often match or exceed multi-agent systems (Gao et al., 2025), suggesting coordination overhead may negate benefits.

This contradiction leaves practitioners without guidance. Without a predictive model, teams cannot determine whether adding agents will amplify performance or erode it through coordination costs. The paper positions itself as the first work to derive **quantitative scaling principles** that resolve this ambiguity by modeling the interplay between agent quantity, coordination structure, model capability, and task properties (Section 1).

### Why This Problem Matters
The stakes of this design gap are both economic and operational. 

1.  **Computational Cost:** Multi-agent systems (MAS) inherently consume more tokens than Single-Agent Systems (SAS). Table 2 shows that Hybrid architectures can incur up to $515\%$ coordination overhead relative to a single agent. Without knowing when this cost yields returns, deployments risk significant waste.
2.  **Reliability and Error Propagation:** In static tasks, multiple agents can vote to correct errors. However, in **agentic tasks**—defined by the paper as requiring sustained multi-step interaction, partial observability, and adaptive strategy (Section 1)—errors can cascade. The paper notes that independent agents can amplify errors by $17.2\times$ through unchecked propagation (Section 1). Understanding when coordination contains vs. amplifies errors is critical for high-stakes domains like finance or medical decision-making.
3.  **Theoretical Significance:** Establishing a "science of scaling" moves the field from engineering alchemy to principled design. By distinguishing **agentic evaluation** from static reasoning (e.g., MMLU, HumanEval), the paper argues that previous scaling observations may be artifacts of benchmark selection rather than true system properties (Section 2).

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
To understand this paper's contribution, we must examine where existing evaluation methodologies fall short. The authors identify three specific confounds in prior work that obscure causal attribution:

*   **Implementation Confounds:** Previous MAS evaluations often compare architectures using different prompts, tools, or computational budgets (Section 1). For example, a multi-agent system might be given more total tokens than a single agent, conflating **architecture effects** with **compute effects**. This paper isolates architectural impact by standardizing tools, prompt structures, and token budgets across all 180 configurations.
*   **Benchmark Misalignment:** Many prior studies evaluate multi-agent systems on **non-agentic tasks** (Section 2). Static benchmarks like HumanEval allow ensembles to improve via voting without environmental interaction. However, real-world tasks (e.g., web browsing, financial analysis) require **sequential interdependence**, where later actions depend on earlier observations. The paper emphasizes that MAS exhibits fundamentally different scaling behavior on tasks requiring sustained environmental interaction compared to static benchmarks (Section 1).
*   **Outcome-Only Metrics:** Prior work focuses exclusively on final accuracy, ignoring **process dynamics** (Section 1). Metrics like coordination overhead, error amplification, and information flow determine whether collaboration succeeds. Without measuring these intermediate states, it is impossible to diagnose why a system failed. This paper introduces empirical coordination metrics (e.g., efficiency $E_c$, error amplification $A_e$) to capture these dynamics (Section 4.1).

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work
This paper distinguishes itself from prior surveys and empirical studies through **controlled causal attribution** and **predictive modeling**.

*   **vs. Surveys:** While surveys characterize collaboration mechanisms (Tran et al., 2025; Guo et al., 2024), they do not provide quantitative laws for performance prediction. This paper derives a mixed-effects model achieving cross-validated $R^2=0.524$ (Section 1), enabling prediction on unseen task domains.
*   **vs. Static Scaling Laws:** Unlike neural scaling laws that focus on parameter counts, this work focuses on **coordination complexity**. It formalizes how performance emerges from the trade-off between model capability (Intelligence Index) and coordination structure (Section 4.3).
*   **vs. Heuristic Claims:** By testing five canonical architectures (Single-Agent, Independent, Centralized, Decentralized, Hybrid) across three LLM families (Section 1), the paper moves beyond categorical claims (e.g., "Centralized is better") to continuous rules based on measurable task properties (e.g., tool count, baseline performance).

Figure 1 illustrates this positioning: while model intelligence generally improves performance, the **scaling slope** depends heavily on the agent configuration. The paper's goal is to explain the variance in those slopes—not just observe them—by linking them to task decomposability and coordination overhead. This shifts the question from "Are multi-agent systems better?" to "Under what measurable conditions does a specific architecture outperform the baseline?"

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an empirical systems paper that derives quantitative scaling laws for agent architectures through controlled experimentation. The core idea is to isolate how coordination structure, model capability, and task properties interact to determine performance, rather than treating "multi-agent" as a monolithic category.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Paper Type and Core Idea.** This is an empirical systems paper that derives quantitative scaling principles for agentic systems through controlled experimentation across 180 configurations. The core idea is that multi-agent performance is not determined by the number of agents alone, but by measurable interactions between coordination structure, model capability, and task properties that can be captured in a predictive mathematical model.

**Defining Agentic Tasks.** Before explaining the system architecture, we must establish what type of tasks this framework evaluates, because the paper argues that prior work evaluated multi-agent systems on inappropriate benchmarks. The paper formalizes a task $T$ as agentic when optimal performance substantially benefits from adaptive interaction, captured by the inequality:

$$\frac{\max_{\pi}\mathbb{E}[R(\tau)] - \max_{g}\mathbb{E}[R(g(x))]}{\max_{\pi}\mathbb{E}[R(\tau)]} > \delta$$

where $\pi$ represents an interactive policy that can take multiple actions with environmental feedback, $g$ represents any single-forward-pass function that must produce an answer in one shot, $R$ measures task success, $\delta$ is a task-dependent threshold, and the expectation is over task instances $x$ and stochastic environment dynamics. In plain language, this formula asks: does having the ability to interact with an environment and revise your approach based on feedback provide meaningful advantage over the best possible one-shot answer? If yes, the task is agentic.

Building on the Agentic Benchmark Checklist from Zhu et al. (2025), the paper establishes three necessary properties for agentic benchmarks. First, **Sequential Interdependence** means later actions depend on earlier observations, so a one-shot policy cannot achieve high reward. Second, **Partial Observability** means critical state information is hidden and must be acquired through active querying or tool use. Third, **Adaptive Strategy Formation** means the policy must update internal beliefs based on new evidence obtained through interaction. Benchmarks lacking these conditions, such as GSM8K or MMLU, evaluate static reasoning rather than agentic capabilities. This distinction matters because multi-agent systems show monotonic improvement on static benchmarks through ensemble voting effects, but exhibit fundamentally different scaling behavior when evaluated on tasks requiring sustained environmental interaction where coordination overhead and error propagation dynamics dominate.

**Formal System Definition.** The paper builds on multi-agent system formalism to define an agent system $\mathcal{S} = (\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{E}, \mathcal{C}, \Omega)$ consisting of a set of agents $\mathcal{A} = \{a_1, ..., a_n\}$ where $n \geq 1$, a shared environment $\mathcal{E}$, a communication topology $\mathcal{C}$, and an orchestration policy $\Omega$. When $|\mathcal{A}| = 1$, this is a Single-Agent System (SAS); when $|\mathcal{A}| > 1$, this is a Multi-Agent System (MAS). Each agent $a_i$ is defined as a tuple $S_i = (\Phi_i, \mathcal{A}_i, M_i, \pi_i)$, where $\Phi_i$ is the reasoning policy (typically an LLM), $\mathcal{A}_i = \{\text{ToolCall}(t, \theta) : t \in \mathcal{T}, \theta \in \Theta_t\}$ is the action space consisting of tool usage where $\mathcal{T}$ is the set of available tools and $\Theta_t$ represents valid parameter configurations for tool $t$, $M_i$ is the internal memory, and $\pi_i: \mathcal{H} \rightarrow \mathcal{A}_i$ is the decision function mapping observation histories to actions.

At timestep $t$, agent $a_i$ selects an action $\alpha_{i,t} \in \mathcal{A}_i$ according to:

$$\alpha_{i,t} = \pi_i(h_{i,t}), \quad o_{i,t} = \mathcal{E}(\alpha_{i,t}), \quad h_{i,t+1} = f_i(h_{i,t}, \alpha_{i,t}, o_{i,t})$$

where $\mathcal{E}$ denotes the environment and $h_{i,0} = \{s_0\}$ contains the initial task specification. The history update function $f_i: \mathcal{H} \times \mathcal{A}_i \times \mathcal{O} \rightarrow \mathcal{H}$ appends the new action-observation pair to the agent's history: $h_{i,t+1} = f_i(h_{i,t}, \alpha_{i,t}, o_{i,t}) = h_{i,t} \oplus (\alpha_{i,t}, o_{i,t})$, subject to context window truncation when $|h_{i,t+1}| > \text{MAX\_TOKENS}$. This update mechanism applies uniformly to both SAS and MAS configurations, ensuring that performance differences arise from coordination structure rather than implementation details.

**Five Canonical Architectures.** The paper tests five coordination topologies selected to form a structured ablation over two key coordination dimensions: orchestrator presence (hierarchical control vs. flat structure) and peer communication (direct sub-agent interaction vs. isolated execution). This design enables causal attribution of performance differences to specific coordination mechanisms rather than generic "multi-agent" effects.

The **Single-Agent System (SAS)** contains one reasoning locus where all perception, reasoning, and action occur within a single sequential loop, producing computational complexity $O(k)$ where $k$ is the number of reasoning iterations. SAS has zero communication overhead and minimal memory $O(k)$, but limited capacity for decomposition or verification. This serves as the baseline against which all multi-agent variants are compared.

The **Independent MAS** architecture has $\mathcal{A} = \{a_1, ..., a_n\}$ with communication topology $\mathcal{C} = \{(a_i, a_{\text{agg}}) : \forall i\}$ meaning agents communicate only to an aggregator with no peer-to-peer communication, and orchestration policy $\Omega = \text{synthesis\_only}$. The synthesis\_only policy concatenates sub-agent outputs without cross-validation or majority voting; the aggregator performs no analytical comparison of responses, ensuring that any performance differences arise purely from parallel exploration rather than error correction. This achieves maximal parallelization but minimal coordination, suitable for isolating ensemble-style reasoning effects. Table 2 shows this architecture has $O(nk)$ LLM calls with communication overhead of 1 (normalized to SAS baseline) and parallelization factor of $n$.

The **Centralized MAS** architecture has $\mathcal{A} = \{a_{\text{orch}}, a_1, ..., a_n\}$ with communication topology $\mathcal{C} = \{(a_{\text{orch}}, a_i) : \forall i\}$ meaning all communication routes through an orchestrator, and orchestration policy $\Omega = \text{hierarchical}$. A single orchestrator coordinates $r$ rounds across $n$ sub-agents with complexity $O(rnk)$. Sequential depth equals $r$ while parallelization factor remains $n$. This design stabilizes reasoning but creates a bottleneck at the orchestrator. Table 2 shows this architecture has $O(rnk) + O(r)$ LLM calls with communication overhead of $r \cdot n$ and memory complexity of $O(r \cdot n \cdot k)$.

The **Decentralized MAS** architecture has $\mathcal{A} = \{a_1, ..., a_n\}$ with communication topology $\mathcal{C} = \{(a_i, a_j) : \forall i, j, i \neq j\}$ meaning all-to-all peer communication, and orchestration policy $\Omega = \text{consensus}$. Agents communicate in $d$ sequential debate rounds with complexity $O(dnk)$. Memory complexity is $O(dnk)$ as each agent stores its own debate history. This enables consensus formation through peer-to-peer discussion without hierarchical control. Table 2 shows this architecture has $O(dnk) + O(1)$ LLM calls with communication overhead of $d \cdot n$ and parallelization factor of $n$.

The **Hybrid MAS** architecture has $\mathcal{A} = \{a_{\text{orch}}, a_1, ..., a_n\}$ with communication topology $\mathcal{C} = \text{star} + \text{peer edges}$ combining orchestrator control with limited peer communication, and orchestration policy $\Omega = \text{hierarchical} + \text{lateral}$. This inherits orchestrator control while enabling lateral exchange between agents with complexity $O(rnk + pn)$ where $p$ is the number of peer rounds. Table 2 shows this architecture has $O(rnk) + O(r) + O(p)$ LLM calls with communication overhead of $r \cdot n + p \cdot m$ where $m$ is average peer requests per round, representing the highest coordination complexity among all architectures.

**Experimental Controls for Causal Attribution.** The paper identifies two fundamental challenges that hinder progress toward principled multi-agent design, and implements specific controls to address each. First, existing MAS evaluations compare architectures using different prompts, tools, or computational budgets, conflating architectural effects with implementation choices and precluding clean causal attribution. Second, evaluations focus exclusively on final accuracy metrics without examining process dynamics such as coordination overhead, error propagation, and information flow that determine whether collaboration succeeds or fails.

To address implementation confounds, the experimental design maintains identical task prompts, tools, and computational budgets across all 180 configurations while systematically varying only coordination structure and model capability. The paper matched maximum total iterations between MAS and SAS systems: MAS configurations received equal computational budget through parallel agent processing with smaller per-agent iterations for $n$-agent teams, while SAS received proportionally more reasoning rounds to compensate for lack of parallel deliberation. All systems were matched for total reasoning-token budget with mean $\mu = 4,800$ tokens per trial. This ensures that performance differences arise from architectural choices rather than stochastic variations or compute advantages.

To address benchmark misalignment, the paper evaluates across four representative agentic benchmarks spanning deterministic to open-world task structures, as shown in Table 1. BrowseComp-Plus (2025) covers web browsing and information retrieval with multi-website information location tasks. Finance-Agent (2025) covers financial analysis with entry-level analyst task performance. PlanCraft (2024) covers game planning in Minecraft environments. Workbench (2024) covers workflow execution with common business activities. The paper notes that BrowseComp-Plus exhibits the highest performance variability across experimental configurations with coefficient of variation $\sigma/\mu = 0.32$ computed across all 45 BrowseComp-Plus runs spanning architectures and model families, where $\sigma$ is the standard deviation of success rates and $\mu$ is the mean success rate. By comparison, Workbench (CV=0.12), Finance Agent (CV=0.18), and PlanCraft (CV=0.21) show lower variability, indicating more stable performance across configurations.

**LLM Families and Intelligence Scaling.** The paper evaluates three LLM families across multiple model sizes, spanning externally standardized Intelligence Index values from 42 to 71. The Intelligence Index is a composite capability score integrating reasoning, coding, and knowledge benchmarks, constructed from eight evaluation suites including MMLU-Pro, GPQA Diamond, AIME 2025, LiveCodeBench, and SciCode. OpenAI models include GPT-5-nano, GPT-5-mini, and GPT-5. Google models include Gemini 2.0 Flash, 2.5 Flash, and 2.5 Pro. Anthropic models include Claude Sonnet 3.7, 4.0, and 4.5. Strong consistency across families validates that coordination scaling follows model-agnostic principles: the maximum difference in architecture-specific scaling slopes between any two LLM families is $\Delta_{\text{max}} = 0.023$ computed as $\max_{i,j}|\hat{\beta}_{\text{arch},i} - \hat{\beta}_{\text{arch},j}|$ across families $i, j \in \{\text{OpenAI, Google, Anthropic}\}$, with coefficient of variation CV &lt; 0.02 across families.

**Coordination Metrics Beyond Accuracy.** The paper introduces five empirical coordination metrics that capture process dynamics determining whether collaboration succeeds or fails, moving beyond outcome-only evaluation. Primary outcome is task success/accuracy which is domain-dependent: factual correctness for Finance Agent, task completion for Workbench, goal satisfaction for PlanCraft, and page synthesis accuracy for BrowseComp-Plus. Secondary metrics are derived from execution traces without requiring ground-truth labels beyond task success.

**Coordination overhead** $O = (T_{\text{MAS}} - T_{\text{SAS}})/T_{\text{SAS}} \times 100\%$ captures computational cost as a percentage increase in turns relative to single-agent baseline, identified as a primary bottleneck in production multi-agent deployments. Table 5 shows empirical values ranging from 0% (SAS) to 515% (Hybrid), with Independent at 58%, Decentralized at 263%, and Centralized at 285% relative to the single-agent baseline.

**Message density** $c$ measures inter-agent messages per reasoning turn, quantifying communication intensity as a key factor in coordination scaling. Table 5 shows SAS and Independent have $c = 0.00$ since they have no inter-agent communication, while Decentralized achieves $c = 0.41$, Centralized achieves $c = 0.39$, and Hybrid achieves $c = 0.24$.

**Redundancy rate** $R$ measures the mean cosine similarity of agent output embeddings, quantifying work overlap where multiple agents perform similar subtasks. Table 5 shows redundancy ranges from 0.41 (Centralized) to 0.50 (Decentralized) for multi-agent systems, with Independent at 0.48 and Hybrid at 0.46.

**Coordination efficiency** $E_c = S/(T/T_{\text{SAS}})$ normalizes success $S$ by relative turn count, enabling deployment decisions that account for cost. Table 5 shows single-agent systems achieve $E_c = 0.466$, while multi-agent architectures range from $E_c = 0.074$ (Hybrid) to $E_c = 0.234$ (Independent), representing a 2–6× efficiency penalty for coordination.

**Error amplification** $A_e = E_{\text{MAS}}/E_{\text{SAS}}$ measures relative failure probability, directly testing whether MAS corrects or propagates errors. Table 5 reveals dramatic variance: single-agent has $A_e = 1.0$ by definition, centralized contains errors to $A_e = 4.4$, decentralized achieves $A_e = 7.8$, hybrid reaches $A_e = 5.1$, and strikingly, independent multi-agent amplifies errors to $A_e = 17.2$ through unchecked propagation where individual mistakes cascade to the final output without correction opportunities.

**The Mixed-Effects Scaling Model.** The paper derives a predictive model using empirical coordination metrics that achieves cross-validated $R^2 = 0.524$, enabling prediction on unseen task domains by modeling task properties rather than overfitting to a specific dataset. The model relates agentic system performance to four categories of predictors: (1) base model capability via intelligence index $I$, (2) system configuration via agent count $n_a$, (3) task properties via tool count $T$ and single-agent baseline $P_{\text{SA}}$, and (4) empirically measured coordination metrics including efficiency $E_c$, overhead $O\%$, error amplification $A_e$, message density $c$, and redundancy $R$.

The complete functional form is:

$$\begin{aligned} P = &\beta_0 + \beta_1(I - \bar{I}) + \beta_2(I - \bar{I})^2 + \beta_3 \log(1 + T) + \beta_4 \log(1 + n_a) \\ &+ \beta_5 \log(1 + O\%) + \beta_6 c + \beta_7 R + \beta_8 E_c + \beta_9 \log(1 + A_e) \\ &+ \beta_{10} P_{\text{SA}} + \beta_{11}(I \times E_c) + \beta_{12}(A_e \times P_{\text{SA}}) \\ &+ \beta_{13}(O\% \times T) + \beta_{14}(R \times n_a) + \beta_{15}(c \times I) \\ &+ \beta_{16}(E_c \times T) + \beta_{17}(P_{\text{SA}} \times \log(1 + n_a)) \\ &+ \beta_{18}(I \times \log(1 + T)) + \beta_{19}(A_e \times T) + \varepsilon \end{aligned}$$

where all predictors are standardized with $\mu = 0$ and $\sigma = 1$ for interpretability. Log transformations are applied to right-skewed variables spanning multiple orders of magnitude: $O\%$ ranges from 0–515%, $T$ ranges from 4–16, $n_a$ ranges from 1–4, and $A_e$ ranges from 1.0–17.2. The model includes nine interaction terms, each motivated by observed patterns rather than exhaustive inclusion. For example, $E_c \times T$ tests whether efficiency penalties compound with tool complexity, $A_e \times T$ tests whether errors propagate more severely in tool-rich environments, and $P_{\text{SA}} \times \log(1 + n_a)$ captures the baseline paradox where high single-agent performance leaves less room for coordination gains. The paper deliberately excludes interactions without clear mechanistic justification, such as $R \times c$ or $I \times O\%$, to avoid overfitting.

Table 4 presents the complete scaling principle coefficients with $\hat{\beta}$ values, 95% confidence intervals, and p-values. Critical interactions with $p &lt; 0.001$ include the baseline paradox $P_{\text{SA}} \times \log(1 + n_a)$ with $\hat{\beta} = -0.404$, the efficiency-tools trade-off $E_c \times T$ with $\hat{\beta} = -0.267$, and the overhead-complexity interaction $O\% \times T$ with $\hat{\beta} = -0.162$. The model achieves $R^2_{\text{train}} = 0.613$ and $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.524$ with 5-fold cross-validation, mean absolute error MAE = 0.089, and root mean squared error RMSE = 0.112. The modest gap between training and cross-validated $R^2$ with $\Delta R^2 = 0.076$, combined with stable coefficient estimates across folds with coefficient of variation &lt; 18% for all $|\hat{\beta}| > 0.05$, indicates that the 20 parameters are justified by predictive power rather than overfitting.

**Design Choices and Alternatives.** The paper made several deliberate design choices that distinguish this approach from alternatives. First, the paper selected five canonical architectures to form a structural ablation of coordination mechanisms rather than attempting exhaustive coverage of all possible architectures. Independent isolates the effect of parallelism without communication, Decentralized introduces peer-to-peer information fusion without hierarchy, Centralized introduces hierarchical verification and bottleneck control, and Hybrid examines the synergy of hierarchy plus lateral flexibility. This design allows causal attribution of performance gains to specific coordination mechanics rather than generic "multi-agent" effects.

Second, the paper constructed the model based on specific mechanistic hypotheses rather than including all possible terms. Main effects capture direct relationships between individual factors and performance, including a quadratic term $I^2$ to test for non-linear capability scaling and log-transformed tool count and agent count following standard diminishing-returns assumptions in scaling analyses. Interaction terms test specific hypotheses about how factors combine, with nine interactions each motivated by observed patterns. The paper evaluated regularized alternatives: Lasso with 10-fold CV for $\lambda$ selection retained 16 of 20 predictors with $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.506$, and Ridge achieved $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.509$. Given minimal improvement and the interpretability benefits of the full model, the paper retains the unregularized specification.

Third, the paper excluded metrics requiring subjective human annotation, such as solution creativity, or those exhibiting high collinearity with included measures. For example, total message count correlates $r > 0.92$ with overhead and was excluded. Variance inflation factor analysis confirmed no severe multicollinearity among retained predictors with all VIF &lt; 5. Intelligence was mean-centered with $\bar{I} = 56.9$ to address multicollinearity between $I$ and $I^2$, reducing VIF from 200 to 1.1.

**Validation Methodology.** The paper validates the scaling principle through multiple procedures to ensure generalizability beyond the training distribution. Five-fold cross-validation with experiment-level holdout yields $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.524$ with standard deviation $\pm 0.033$, demonstrating that the model explains more than half of the performance variance on held-out data without dataset-specific parameters. Bootstrap resampling with $n = 1,000$ iterations confirms coefficient stability with mean bootstrap SE &lt; 0.015 for all $|\hat{\beta}| > 0.1$. Residual diagnostics satisfy normality with Shapiro–Wilk $p = 0.412$ and homoscedasticity with Breusch–Pagan $p = 0.298$, with residual standard error $\hat{\sigma} = 0.118$.

The model substantially outperforms simpler alternatives, as shown in Table 3. A model using only intelligence, tools, and agents achieves $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.283$. Adding architecture labels as categorical variables achieves $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.430$. Adding single-agent baseline achieves $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.431$. The final model using empirical coordination metrics achieves $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.524$, representing a 20% improvement in predictive power over categorical architecture labels. This demonstrates that measured properties outperform nominal categories for architecture selection.

Critically, the framework generalizes beyond training configurations: the model correctly predicts optimal architectures for 87% of held-out task configurations, demonstrating extrapolation to unseen task structures. Out-of-sample validation on GPT-5.2 with Intelligence Index = 75, released after the study, achieves MAE = 0.071 and confirms four of five scaling principles generalize to unseen frontier models. The validation shows that the capability ceiling effect persists with $P_{\text{SA}} = 0.45$ and best MAS gain = +6.7%, consistent with diminishing returns at high baselines. Independent MAS exhibits predicted degradation at -22.2%, and Centralized/Decentralized architectures achieve optimal performance. The partial validation concerns the Decentralized versus Centralized pattern: while the main results show clear Decentralized advantage over SAS with +9.2% versus +0.2%, GPT-5.2 shows convergence with both at 0.48, suggesting that architecture-specific advantages may decrease as model capability increases.

**Turn Count Scaling and Resource Ceilings.** The paper identifies a hard resource ceiling that fundamentally limits multi-agent scaling under fixed computational budgets. Total reasoning turns exhibit power-law growth with agent count:

$$T = 2.72 \times (n + 0.5)^{1.724}, \quad R^2 = 0.974, \quad 95\% \text{ CI on exponent: } [1.685, 1.763], \quad p &lt; 0.001$$

This super-linear exponent of 1.724, which is greater than 1, reflects quadratic message complexity from all-to-all potential communication tempered by practical bandwidth limits, creating a distinct agentic scaling regime fundamentally different from neural network parameter scaling where Kaplan et al. report exponent $b = 0.76$ for dense models. Empirically, Hybrid systems require 6.2× more turns than SAS with 44.3 versus 7.2 turns, Centralized requires 3.8× with 27.7 turns, and Decentralized requires 3.6× with 26.1 turns. The implication is stark: under fixed computational budgets, per-agent reasoning capacity becomes prohibitively thin beyond 3–4 agents, creating a hard resource ceiling where communication cost dominates reasoning capability.

**Information Gain and Error Absorption.** The paper computes information gain $\Delta I$ by comparing pre-coordination and post-coordination task-uncertainty surrogates via Bayesian posterior variance reduction on key variables. The formula is:

$$\Delta I = \frac{1}{2} \log \frac{\text{Var}[Y|s_{\text{pre}}]}{\text{Var}[Y|s_{\text{post}}]}$$

where $Y \in \{0, 1\}$ is the task success indicator, $s_{\text{pre}}$ is the agent's state representation before coordination, and $s_{\text{post}}$ is the state after coordination. In structured domains like Finance Agent and Workbench, $\Delta I$ correlates strongly with MAS–SAS gap with correlation coefficient $r = 0.71$ and $p &lt; 0.001$, indicating that agents successfully exchange high-value information and synthesize it into improved solutions. In Finance Agent specifically, $\Delta I$ ranges from 0.8–2.1 bits with mean 1.4 for successful trials versus 0.2–0.6 bits with mean 0.4 for failures. Conversely, in open-world domains like BrowseComp-Plus, $\Delta I$ shows weak and non-significant power, revealing that agents' messages provide limited validated information due to inherent world ambiguity.

Error absorption is formalized as $\text{Absorb} = (E_{\text{SAS}} - E_{\text{MAS}})/E_{\text{SAS}}$ where $E$ is factual error rate. The absorption mechanism operates through iterative verification: in Centralized and Hybrid architectures, sub-agent outputs pass through an orchestrator that cross-checks reasoning steps before aggregation, enabling detection and correction of logical inconsistencies. In Decentralized architectures, peer debate rounds provide similar verification through explicit challenge-response exchanges. These architectures achieve 22.7% average error reduction with 95% CI [20.1%, 25.3%], peaking at 31.4% for Finance Agent where structured numerical outputs facilitate verification. Independent MAS shows no error correction with +4.6% amplification due to absence of any inter-agent verification mechanism where errors made by individual agents propagate directly to the aggregated output without opportunity for correction.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the paper's most significant contributions, distinguishing between incremental improvements to existing methodology and fundamental innovations that reshape how we understand agent system scaling. The insights below emerge from the 180 controlled configurations analyzed in Section 3, but their implications extend beyond the experimental results to establish new principles for agentic system design.

### 4.1 The Capability Ceiling: When Single Agents Become Optimal

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental theoretical advance

Prior work in multi-agent systems operated under an implicit assumption that coordination benefits scale monotonically with task complexity. Claims like "More agents is all you need" (Li et al., 2024) suggested that adding agents universally improves performance. This paper overturns that assumption by identifying a **capability ceiling threshold** at approximately 45% single-agent baseline performance.

The scaling model reveals a critical interaction term $P_{\text{SA}} \times \log(1 + n_a)$ with coefficient $\hat{\beta} = -0.404$ ($p &lt; 0.001$), indicating that coordination yields diminishing or negative returns once single-agent baselines exceed this empirical threshold. This finding is mechanistically interpretable: when a single agent already achieves moderate success, the coordination overhead (which consumes 58–515% additional tokens per Table 5) cannot be justified by marginal accuracy gains.

**Why this matters:** This insight transforms architecture selection from an open question into a bounded decision problem. Practitioners can now measure single-agent baseline performance on a new task and immediately rule out multi-agent architectures if $P_{\text{SA}} > 0.45$. The out-of-sample validation on GPT-5.2 confirms this principle generalizes: at $P_{\text{SA}} = 0.45$, the best multi-agent gain was only +6.7%, consistent with the predicted diminishing returns. This represents a **decision boundary** rather than a correlation—the first quantitative criterion for when to abandon multi-agent approaches entirely.

**Distinction from prior work:** Previous studies observed that multi-agent benefits diminish as models improve (Gao et al., 2025), but none identified a specific threshold or provided a predictive equation. This paper moves from qualitative observation ("benefits diminish") to quantitative rule ("benefits reverse at 45% baseline").

### 4.2 The Tool-Coordination Trade-off: Complexity Amplifies Overhead

**Innovation Type:** Counterintuitive empirical discovery

Conventional wisdom suggests that complex tasks with many tools should benefit from multi-agent decomposition—different agents can specialize in different tool operations. The paper's second major insight contradicts this: **tool-heavy tasks suffer disproportionately from multi-agent inefficiency**.

The efficiency-tools interaction $E_c \times T$ exhibits the second-largest effect size in the model ($\hat{\beta} = -0.267$, $p &lt; 0.001$). For a task with 16 tools (e.g., Workbench), the efficiency penalty translates to a performance delta of $-1.99$ for single-agent systems versus $-0.32$ for multi-agent systems when using raw interpretability values. This occurs because multi-agent systems fragment the per-agent token budget, leaving insufficient capacity for complex tool orchestration within each agent's context window.

**Why this matters:** This insight explains why PlanCraft degrades by 70% under multi-agent coordination despite having similar domain complexity to Finance-Agent (which improves by 80.8%). The critical difference is not overall complexity but **sequential interdependence**: PlanCraft requires ordered tool execution where each action modifies the state for subsequent actions, while Finance-Agent tasks decompose into parallelizable subtasks (revenue analysis, cost structures, market comparisons) that agents can explore independently.

**Distinction from prior work:** Prior scaling analyses focused on model parameters or agent counts as the primary variables. This paper identifies **tool count** as a moderating variable that interacts with coordination structure—a previously unquantified dimension of agentic complexity. The finding that coordination costs scale super-linearly with environmental complexity ($O\% \times T$ interaction with $\hat{\beta} = -0.162$) establishes that coordination overhead is not fixed but context-dependent.

### 4.3 Architecture-Dependent Error Amplification: Safety Bounds for Deployment

**Innovation Type:** Operational safety metric

The paper provides the first quantitative characterization of how different architectures propagate errors through execution chains. Table 5 reveals a 4× variance in error amplification factors across architectures: Independent systems amplify errors $17.2\times$ through unchecked propagation, while Centralized coordination contains this to $4.4\times$ by enforcing validation bottlenecks.

This finding emerges from the error absorption mechanism formalized as $\text{Absorb} = (E_{\text{SAS}} - E_{\text{MAS}})/E_{\text{SAS}}$. Centralized and Decentralized architectures achieve 22.7% average error reduction through iterative verification, while Independent MAS shows +4.6% amplification due to absence of inter-agent correction mechanisms.

**Why this matters:** For high-stakes deployments (financial analysis, medical decision-making), error amplification is more critical than average accuracy. A system that achieves 60% accuracy but amplifies errors $17\times$ poses greater risk than a system achieving 55% accuracy with $4\times$ amplification. The paper's token-overlap analysis reveals the mechanism: high-performing runs show dramatically reduced contradictory mass (median 2.3% in successes vs. 8.1% in failures), indicating that messages converge toward mutually consistent sub-proofs rather than self-reinforcing errors.

**Distinction from prior work:** Prior multi-agent failure taxonomies (Cemri et al., 2025) categorized error types but did not quantify propagation dynamics across architectures. This paper moves from **error classification** to **error dynamics**, providing deployment teams with concrete safety bounds. The finding that Hybrid architectures introduce coordination-failure modes at 12.4% (vs. 1.8% for Centralized) reveals that adding communication channels does not monotonically improve reliability.

### 4.4 Task-Contingent Coordination: Decomposability Over Complexity

**Innovation Type:** Reframing of task characterization

The paper's fourth innovation challenges how we characterize tasks for agent system design. Prior work assumed task complexity (e.g., number of reasoning steps) determines whether multi-agent systems help. This paper demonstrates that **task decomposability**—the degree to which subtasks can be executed in parallel without state dependencies—is the actual determinant.

Figure 2 illustrates this starkly: Finance-Agent ($D = 0.407$) achieves +80.8% gain under Centralized coordination, while PlanCraft ($D = 0.419$) degrades by 70% under the same architecture. Despite nearly identical domain complexity scores, the tasks differ fundamentally in sequential interdependence. Finance-Agent trajectories show natural decomposability (regulatory analysis, SEC filing research, operational impact assessment can proceed in parallel), while PlanCraft requires strictly sequential state-dependent reasoning (each crafting action modifies inventory that subsequent actions depend upon).

**Why this matters:** This insight provides a **diagnostic framework** for new tasks. Before deploying multi-agent systems, practitioners should assess whether subtasks exhibit sequential interdependence. If later actions depend on earlier observations (as in PlanCraft's inventory state), multi-agent coordination will fragment reasoning capacity under fixed computational budgets. If subtasks are parallelizable (as in Finance-Agent's multi-source analysis), coordination provides genuine value.

**Distinction from prior work:** Prior benchmark analyses focused on outcome metrics (accuracy, success rate) without examining task structure. This paper introduces **structural task properties** (decomposability, sequential interdependence, tool complexity) as predictors of coordination efficacy. The Information Gain metric $\Delta I$ operationalizes this: structured domains show strong information-value convergence ($r = 0.71$), while open-world domains show weak correlation ($r = 0.18$), directly mapping to observed multi-agent benefits.

### 4.5 Predictive Architecture Selection: From Heuristics to Measurement

**Innovation Type:** Practical deployment tool

The paper's fifth contribution synthesizes the above insights into an actionable decision framework. The scaling equation (Equation 1) achieves 87% accuracy in predicting optimal architectures for held-out configurations, substantially exceeding random choice (20%) or capability-only models (54%).

The decision boundary between single-agent and multi-agent is derived purely from data:

$$P^*_{\text{SA}} = -\frac{\hat{\beta}_4}{\hat{\beta}_{17}} \approx \frac{0.052}{0.404} = 0.129 \text{ (standardized units)}$$

corresponding to raw performance $\approx 0.45$ after denormalization. This threshold offers the first quantitative criterion for coordination structure selection.

**Why this matters:** Practitioners can now compute expected performance for each architecture given measurable task characteristics ($T$, $P_{\text{SA}}$) and model capability ($I$), using empirical coordination metrics from Table 5. For three task archetypes:
- **Planning tasks** ($T = 4$, $P_{\text{SA}} = 0.57$) favor single-agent due to baseline paradox and low tool count
- **Analysis tasks** ($T = 5$, $P_{\text{SA}} = 0.35$) favor centralized multi-agent, balancing error control ($A_e = 4.4$) with manageable overhead
- **Tool-heavy tasks** ($T = 16$, $P_{\text{SA}} = 0.63$) favor decentralized multi-agent despite high overhead (263%), because parallelization and redundancy outweigh efficiency losses

**Distinction from prior work:** Prior architecture selection relied on heuristics ("use Centralized for complex tasks") or exhaustive empirical testing. This paper provides a **continuous predictive model** that generalizes to unseen task domains without dataset-specific parameters. The out-of-sample validation on GPT-5.2 (MAE = 0.071) confirms four of five scaling principles generalize to frontier models released after the study, demonstrating that the framework captures fundamental coordination dynamics rather than overfitting to specific configurations.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the experimental methodology, quantitative results, and validity of the paper's claims. Unlike prior sections that established the theoretical framework and key insights, this analysis focuses on the empirical evidence supporting those claims—including specific performance numbers, statistical validation, failure modes, and robustness checks across the 180 controlled configurations.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Benchmark Selection and Task Distribution.** The paper evaluates across four agentic benchmarks selected to span deterministic to open-world task structures (Table 1). Each benchmark was chosen to satisfy the three necessary properties for agentic evaluation: sequential interdependence, partial observability, and adaptive strategy formation (Section 3.2).

- **BrowseComp-Plus (2025):** 100 web browsing tasks requiring multi-website information synthesis. Tasks include comparative analysis, fact verification, and comprehensive research across multiple web sources. This benchmark exhibits the highest performance variability across experimental configurations with coefficient of variation $CV = 0.32$ computed across all 45 BrowseComp-Plus runs spanning architectures and model families.
- **Finance-Agent (2025):** 50 finance questions requiring domain expertise and multi-step analysis, including earnings analysis, financial metric calculations, and market trend interpretation. Each instance includes expert-provided rubrics for structured evaluation. Questions typically require 15–30 minutes of expert time, indicating substantial complexity.
- **PlanCraft (2024):** 100 sequential planning tasks in Minecraft environments where agents must craft target items by determining optimal action sequences using available inventory and crafting recipes. The paper uses the plancraft-test subset containing focused planning challenges.
- **Workbench (2024):** 100 business task automation scenarios covering five domains: analytics, calendar management, email operations, project management, and customer relationship management. Success requires executing correct tool sequences to accomplish realistic business workflows.

The selection of these four benchmarks is deliberate: they vary in domain complexity $D$ from 0.000 (Workbench, minimal sequential constraints) to 0.839 (BrowseComp-Plus, dynamic state evolution), as shown in Appendix C. This range enables the paper to test whether scaling principles generalize across task structures rather than overfitting to a specific domain.

**Model Families and Capability Range.** The paper evaluates three LLM families across multiple model sizes, spanning Intelligence Index values from 42 to 71 (Appendix A, Table 6):

- **OpenAI:** GPT-5-nano (Index 59), GPT-5-mini (Index 68), GPT-5 (Index 71)
- **Google:** Gemini 2.0 Flash (Index 47), Gemini 2.5 Flash (Index 58), Gemini 2.5 Pro (Index 65)
- **Anthropic:** Claude 3.7 Sonnet (Index 42), Claude 4.0 Sonnet (Index 47), Claude 4.5 Sonnet (Index 55)

This range is critical for testing capability-dependent scaling: the 29-point spread (42 to 71) allows the paper to identify non-linear relationships between model intelligence and coordination benefits. The paper notes strong consistency across families: the maximum difference in architecture-specific scaling slopes between any two LLM families is $\Delta_{\text{max}} = 0.023$, with coefficient of variation $CV &lt; 0.02$ across families. This validates that coordination scaling follows model-agnostic principles rather than vendor-specific artifacts.

**Experimental Controls for Causal Attribution.** The paper implements three critical controls to isolate architectural effects from implementation confounds:

1. **Matched Token Budgets:** All systems were matched for total reasoning-token budget with mean $\mu = 4,800$ tokens per trial (Table 5). MAS configurations received equal computational budget through parallel agent processing with smaller per-agent iterations for $n$-agent teams, while SAS received proportionally more reasoning rounds to compensate for lack of parallel deliberation.

2. **Identical Tool Interfaces:** Each dataset defines its tool ecosystem through environment configurations, and all architectures use identical tool APIs and observation structures. This eliminates confounds from external feedback quality that could advantage one architecture over another.

3. **Standardized Prompts:** The paper implemented a structured prompting system supporting named templates and variable interpolation (Appendix E.3). Prompts are defined in YAML files with base templates and role-specific extensions, ensuring consistent instruction delivery across architectures while maintaining task specificity.

These controls address the two fundamental challenges identified in Section 1: implementation confounds (different prompts, tools, or budgets across architectures) and outcome-only metrics (ignoring process dynamics). By standardizing these variables, the paper enables causal attribution of performance differences to architectural choices rather than stochastic variations.

**Metrics and Measurement.** The paper employs a multi-dimensional metric suite capturing both outcomes and process dynamics (Section 3.4):

- **Primary Outcome:** Task success/accuracy, domain-dependent (factual correctness for Finance-Agent, task completion for Workbench, goal satisfaction for PlanCraft, page synthesis accuracy for BrowseComp-Plus).
- **Coordination Overhead ($O\%$):** $(T_{\text{MAS}} - T_{\text{SAS}})/T_{\text{SAS}} \times 100\%$, capturing computational cost as percentage increase in turns relative to single-agent baseline.
- **Message Density ($c$):** Inter-agent messages per reasoning turn, quantifying communication intensity.
- **Redundancy Rate ($R$):** Mean cosine similarity of agent output embeddings, measuring work overlap.
- **Coordination Efficiency ($E_c$):** $S/(T/T_{\text{SAS}})$, normalizing success by relative turn count for deployment decisions.
- **Error Amplification ($A_e$):** $E_{\text{MAS}}/E_{\text{SAS}}$, measuring relative failure probability to test whether MAS corrects or propagates errors.

All metrics are normalized per reasoning turn and per token to enable cross-architecture comparison. The paper selected coordination metrics based on two criteria: (i) direct measurability from experimental traces without requiring ground-truth labels beyond task success, and (ii) coverage of distinct aspects of coordination–performance relationships identified in prior work. Variance inflation factor analysis confirmed no severe multicollinearity among retained predictors (all VIF &lt; 5).

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**Performance Heterogeneity Across Benchmarks.** Figure 2 reveals the paper's most striking finding: multi-agent performance is highly task-dependent, ranging from +80.9% improvement to −70.0% degradation depending on task structure and coordination architecture. Table 5 summarizes aggregate performance across all 180 configurations:

| Architecture | Success Rate ($S$) | Turns ($T$) | Overhead ($O\%$) | Efficiency ($E_c$) | Error Amp ($A_e$) |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------|------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| SAS | 0.466 | 7.2 ± 2.1 | 0 | 0.466 | 1.0 |
| Independent | 0.370 | 11.4 ± 3.2 | 58 | 0.234 | 17.2 |
| Decentralized | 0.477 | 26.1 ± 7.5 | 263 | 0.132 | 7.8 |
| Centralized | 0.463 | 27.7 ± 8.1 | 285 | 0.120 | 4.4 |
| Hybrid | 0.452 | 44.3 ± 12.4 | 515 | 0.074 | 5.1 |

Several observations emerge from these aggregate numbers:

1. **Independent MAS Underperforms:** Despite maximal parallelization, Independent architecture achieves the lowest success rate (0.370 vs. 0.466 SAS) and highest error amplification (17.2×). This contradicts the "more agents is all you need" hypothesis, demonstrating that parallelism without coordination mechanisms is detrimental.

2. **Centralized Contains Errors:** Centralized architecture achieves the lowest error amplification (4.4×) among MAS variants through orchestrator validation bottlenecks, despite having similar success rate to SAS (0.463 vs. 0.466).

3. **Hybrid Incurs Highest Overhead:** Hybrid architecture requires 6.2× more turns than SAS (44.3 vs. 7.2 turns, $t(178) = 16.8$, $p &lt; 0.001$) with the lowest efficiency (0.074), indicating that combining hierarchical and peer communication creates compounding coordination costs.

**Benchmark-Specific Performance (Figure 2).** Aggregating across architectures obscures critical domain-dependent patterns. Figure 2 shows performance distributions for each benchmark:

- **Finance-Agent:** All MAS architectures substantially outperform SAS. Centralized reaches +80.8% (mean 0.631 vs. SAS 0.349), Decentralized achieves +74.5% (0.609), and Hybrid reaches +73.1% (0.604). This is the strongest multi-agent benefit observed, driven by opportunities for distributed financial reasoning across multiple agents where subtasks (regulatory analysis, SEC filing research, operational impact assessment) can proceed in parallel.

- **Workbench:** Multi-agent systems show minimal gains. Decentralized achieves +5.7% (0.664 vs. SAS 0.629), while Centralized and Hybrid both slightly underperform at −1.2%. The marginal effects suggest balanced trade-offs between problem structure and orchestration costs.

- **BrowseComp-Plus:** Improvements remain modest and polarized. Decentralized achieves +9.2% (0.347 vs. SAS 0.318), with Centralized essentially flat at +0.2%. Critically, Independent agents catastrophically underperform at −35%, indicating that uncoordinated parallel exploration is harmful for dynamic web navigation.

- **PlanCraft:** Universal performance degradation across all multi-agent architectures. Centralized declines to −50.3% (0.282 vs. SAS 0.568), Decentralized to −41.5% (0.332), Hybrid to −39.1% (0.346), and Independent to −70.1% (0.170). The paper examines execution traces to explain this: PlanCraft requires strictly sequential state-dependent reasoning where each crafting action modifies the inventory state that subsequent actions depend upon. Multi-agent decomposition creates artificial subtasks that consume token budget on coordination rather than reasoning.

Aggregating across all benchmarks and architectures, the overall mean MAS improvement is −3.5% (95% CI: [−18.6%, +25.7%]), reflecting substantial performance heterogeneity with high variance ($\sigma = 45.2\%$). This aggregate number is critical: it demonstrates that MAS do not provide universal benefits but rather domain-specific trade-offs.

**Scaling Model Performance (Table 3, Table 4).** The paper's mixed-effects scaling model (Equation 1) achieves cross-validated $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.524$ (±0.033 SD), mean absolute error MAE = 0.089 (±0.011), and root mean squared error RMSE = 0.112 (±0.014). Table 3 shows progressive model improvement:

| Model Specification | $R^2_{\text{train}}$ | $R^2_{\text{CV}}$ | AIC | Parameters |
|---------------------|---------------------|-------------------|-----|------------|
| Intelligence + Tools + Agents | 0.312 | 0.283 | −77.6 | 4 |
| + Architecture labels (categorical) | 0.480 | 0.430 | −168.0 | 10 |
| + Single-agent baseline | 0.493 | 0.431 | −168.4 | 11 |
| + Coordination metrics (Table 5) | 0.613 | 0.524 | −201.2 | 20 |

The final model using empirical coordination metrics achieves 20% improvement in $R^2_{\text{CV}}$ over categorical architecture labels, demonstrating that measured properties outperform nominal categories. The modest gap between training and cross-validated $R^2$ ($\Delta R^2 = 0.076$), combined with stable coefficient estimates across folds (coefficient of variation &lt; 18% for all $|\hat{\beta}| > 0.05$), indicates that the 20 parameters are justified by predictive power rather than overfitting.

Table 4 presents the complete scaling principle coefficients. Critical interactions with $p &lt; 0.001$ include:
- **Baseline Paradox:** $P_{\text{SA}} \times \log(1 + n_a)$ with $\hat{\beta} = -0.404$ [−0.557, −0.252]
- **Efficiency-Tools Trade-off:** $E_c \times T$ with $\hat{\beta} = -0.267$ [−0.355, −0.178]
- **Overhead-Complexity Interaction:** $O\% \times T$ with $\hat{\beta} = -0.162$ [−0.241, −0.083]

The linear capability effect (Intelligence $I - \bar{I}$) is significant with $\hat{\beta} = 0.171$ [0.070, 0.272], $p = 0.001$, while the quadratic term is not significant ($p = 0.509$), indicating that capability scaling follows a linear rather than accelerating pattern within the tested range ($I \in [42, 71]$).

**Turn Count Scaling (Section 4.4).** Total reasoning turns exhibit power-law growth with agent count:

$$T = 2.72 \times (n + 0.5)^{1.724}, \quad R^2 = 0.974, \quad 95\% \text{ CI on exponent: } [1.685, 1.763], \quad p &lt; 0.001$$

This super-linear exponent (1.724 > 1) reflects quadratic message complexity tempered by practical bandwidth limits. Figure 5 illustrates model-dependent coordination limits: Gemini-2.0 Flash exhibits a clear optimum at 7 agents before degradation, while Gemini-2.5 Pro's decentralized architecture peaks earlier despite its higher single-agent baseline. The centralized architecture demonstrates more stable scaling for Flash but shows diminishing returns for Pro beyond 5 agents. This confirms the hard resource ceiling: beyond 3–4 agents, per-agent reasoning quality degrades sharply under fixed budgets.

**Information Gain and Error Absorption.** In structured domains (Finance-Agent, Workbench), information gain $\Delta I$ correlates strongly with MAS–SAS gap ($r = 0.71$, $p &lt; 0.001$). In Finance-Agent specifically, $\Delta I$ ranges 0.8–2.1 bits (mean 1.4) for successful trials vs. 0.2–0.6 bits (mean 0.4) for failures. Conversely, in open-world domains (BrowseComp-Plus), $\Delta I$ shows weak and non-significant power, revealing that agents' messages provide limited validated information due to inherent world ambiguity.

Error absorption mechanisms achieve 22.7% average error reduction (95% CI: [20.1%, 25.3%]), peaking at 31.4% for Finance-Agent where structured numerical outputs facilitate verification. Independent MAS shows no error correction (+4.6% amplification) due to absence of any inter-agent verification mechanism.

### 5.3 Assessment of Experimental Validity

**Do the Experiments Support the Claims?** The paper makes five primary claims, each supported by specific experimental evidence:

1. **Claim: Multi-agent performance is task-contingent, not universal.** Supported by Figure 2 showing +80.8% on Finance-Agent vs. −70.0% on PlanCraft. The aggregate mean MAS improvement of −3.5% with high variance ($\sigma = 45.2\%$) confirms heterogeneity rather than universal benefit.

2. **Claim: Coordination overhead scales super-linearly with agent count.** Supported by the power-law fit $T = 2.72 \times (n + 0.5)^{1.724}$ with $R^2 = 0.974$. Empirical data shows Hybrid systems require 6.2× more turns than SAS (44.3 vs. 7.2 turns).

3. **Claim: A predictive model can generalize to unseen configurations.** Supported by cross-validated $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.524$ and 87% accuracy in predicting optimal architectures for held-out configurations. Out-of-sample validation on GPT-5.2 achieves MAE = 0.071.

4. **Claim: Error amplification is architecture-dependent.** Supported by Table 5 showing $A_e$ ranging from 1.0 (SAS) to 17.2 (Independent) to 4.4 (Centralized). Token-overlap analysis confirms high-performing runs show reduced contradictory mass (median 2.3% in successes vs. 8.1% in failures).

5. **Claim: A capability ceiling exists at ~45% single-agent baseline.** Supported by the $P_{\text{SA}} \times \log(1 + n_a)$ interaction with $\hat{\beta} = -0.404$ ($p &lt; 0.001$). Out-of-sample validation on GPT-5.2 confirms this: at $P_{\text{SA}} = 0.45$, best MAS gain was only +6.7%.

The experiments convincingly support these claims through multiple lines of evidence: aggregate statistics, benchmark-specific breakdowns, scaling model coefficients, and out-of-sample validation. The use of 180 controlled configurations across three LLM families provides sufficient statistical power to detect interaction effects.

**Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks.** The paper includes several robustness checks:

- **Regularization Alternatives:** The paper evaluated Lasso (10-fold CV for $\lambda$ selection) which retained 16 of 20 predictors with $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.506$, and Ridge which achieved $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.509$. Given minimal improvement and interpretability benefits, the paper retains the unregularized specification.

- **Sensitivity Analysis:** Alternative model specifications confirm qualitatively identical results ($\Delta R^2_{\text{CV}} &lt; 0.01$). Bootstrap resampling ($n = 1,000$ iterations) confirms coefficient stability (mean bootstrap SE &lt; 0.015 for all $|\hat{\beta}| > 0.1$).

- **Residual Diagnostics:** Residuals satisfy normality (Shapiro–Wilk $p = 0.412$) and homoscedasticity (Breusch–Pagan $p = 0.298$), with residual standard error $\hat{\sigma} = 0.118$.

- **Cross-Family Consistency:** Maximum difference in architecture-specific scaling slopes between any two LLM families is $\Delta_{\text{max}} = 0.023$, with $CV &lt; 0.02$ across families, validating model-agnostic principles.

**Failure Cases and Limitations.** The paper identifies several failure modes:

1. **Sequential Planning Tasks:** PlanCraft exhibits universal degradation (−39% to −70%) across all MAS variants. The paper explains this through execution trace analysis: multi-agent decomposition creates artificial subtasks that consume token budget on coordination rather than reasoning for inherently sequential tasks.

2. **High-Overhead Architectures:** Hybrid architecture with 515% overhead degrades performance on tool-heavy benchmarks (success rate 0.452 overall, 0.21 on Workbench). The overhead-complexity interaction ($\hat{\beta}_{O\% \times T} = -0.162$) creates a critical threshold beyond which overhead cost exceeds any coordination benefit.

3. **Independent Architecture:** Despite maximal parallelization, Independent MAS underperforms SAS (0.370 vs. 0.466) with highest error amplification (17.2×). This demonstrates that parallelism without coordination mechanisms is detrimental for agentic tasks.

4. **Out-of-Sample SAS Prediction:** On GPT-5.2, the scaling equation predicts SAS as optimal ($\hat{P}_{\text{SAS}} = 0.673$), yet empirically MAS-Centralized and MAS-Decentralized achieve superior performance (0.48 vs. 0.45). This discrepancy reflects linear extrapolation of the capability effect ($\hat{\beta}_I = 0.171$) beyond the training range (Index 42–71 to 75), where diminishing returns may emerge that are not captured by the current functional form.

**Conditional Results and Trade-offs.** The paper's results are explicitly conditional on task properties:

- **Tool-Coordination Trade-off:** For tasks with $T = 16$ tools, the efficiency penalty translates to $\Delta P_{\text{efficiency}} = -1.99$ (single-agent, $E_c = 0.466$) vs. $-0.32$ (multi-agent, $E_c = 0.074$). Conversely, simple tasks ($T \leq 4$) show negligible efficiency effects ($|\Delta P| &lt; 0.05$).

- **Capability Ceiling:** The decision boundary between single-agent and multi-agent is $P^*_{\text{SA}} \approx 0.45$ after denormalization. Below this threshold, coordination provides value; above it, coordination overhead becomes a net cost.

- **Optimal Coordination Band:** The paper identifies three operational coordination regimes: (i) Under-coordination ($O &lt; 100\%$ overhead): minimal accuracy gain ($\Delta S \approx +2\text{--}4\%$); (ii) Optimal band ($200\% &lt; O &lt; 300\%$ overhead): highest success–cost ratio ($E_c \approx 0.16$), dominated by Centralized and Decentralized; (iii) Over-coordination ($O > 400\%$ overhead): reduced efficiency ($E_c \approx 0.11$), protocol complexity introducing coordination-failure modes.

These conditional results demonstrate that the paper does not make universal claims but rather provides a framework for architecture selection based on measurable task properties.

### 5.4 Economic Efficiency Analysis

**Token Efficiency and Cost-Benefit Trade-offs.** Table 5 includes success per 1,000 tokens, revealing sharp trade-offs by architecture:

- SAS: 67.7 successes/1K tokens
- Independent: 42.4 successes/1K tokens (2.8× worse than SAS)
- Decentralized: 23.9 successes/1K tokens (2.8× worse)
- Centralized: 21.5 successes/1K tokens (3.1× worse)
- Hybrid: 13.6 successes/1K tokens (5.0× worse)

Figure 3 plots cost–performance trade-offs across model families and architectures. The optimal coordination pattern differs across families:
- **OpenAI models** show consistent gains from Centralized and Hybrid MAS configurations despite higher costs, suggesting stronger communication synergy.
- **Google models** display marginal MAS improvements but a clear efficiency plateau, indicating diminishing returns under lightweight coordination.
- **Anthropic models** reveal higher variance and occasional MAS underperformance, reflecting sensitivity to coordination overhead.

Absolute dollar costs per trial vary by model: OpenAI Hybrid achieves marginal cost ≈$0.008 per 1% success gain, while Anthropic Hybrid reaches ≈$0.024 per 1% gain (3× worse), and Google maintains intermediate costs ≈$0.012 per 1% gain. These economic considerations are critical for deployment decisions where cost efficiency matters alongside accuracy.

**Vendor-Specific Coordination Mechanisms.** Section 4.2 reveals architecture-LLM family interactions. On Finance-Agent, Anthropic's MAS-Centralized achieves +127.5% (0.636 vs. 0.280 SAS), whereas Google's MAS-Centralized reaches +164.3% (0.740 vs. 0.280 SAS), and OpenAI's MAS-Centralized achieves +69.9% (0.79 vs. 0.465 SAS). On Workbench, where multi-agent overhead is less tolerable, Anthropic's best variant (MAS-Decentralized, +10.8%) remains superior to Google (+9.5%) and OpenAI (+8.6%). On PlanCraft where all variants degrade, vendor preferences flatten, indicating that communication mechanisms cannot overcome fundamental sequential reasoning constraints.

These family-specific patterns suggest that coordination efficacy depends on each model family's intrinsic communication bandwidth and reasoning alignment, not just raw capability. This has practical implications: practitioners should not assume coordination principles transfer identically across vendors without empirical validation.

### 5.5 Summary of Experimental Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. **Controlled Experimental Design:** 180 configurations with matched token budgets, identical prompts, and standardized tool interfaces enable causal attribution.
2. **Diverse Benchmark Coverage:** Four benchmarks spanning $D = 0.000$ to $D = 0.839$ test generalizability across task structures.
3. **Multi-Dimensional Metrics:** Beyond accuracy, the paper measures coordination overhead, error amplification, information gain, and efficiency.
4. **Out-of-Sample Validation:** GPT-5.2 validation (MAE = 0.071) confirms four of five scaling principles generalize to unseen frontier models.
5. **Statistical Rigor:** Cross-validation, bootstrap resampling, residual diagnostics, and VIF analysis support model validity.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Agent Count Scaling:** Experiments test up to 9 agents, but the power-law fit suggests fundamental barriers beyond 3–4 agents under fixed budgets. Whether larger collectives exhibit beneficial emergent behaviors remains an open question (Section 5).
2. **Homogeneous Model Teams:** All agents share identical base architectures differing only in scale and role prompts. Teams combining fundamentally different model architectures or domain-specialized fine-tuning were not tested.
3. **Prompt Optimization:** The paper controlled prompts to be identical across conditions but did not optimize prompts specifically for each model or model family. Architecture-specific prompt tuning may yield different scaling characteristics.
4. **SAS Over-Prediction on Frontier Models:** On GPT-5.2, the model systematically over-predicts SAS performance (+49.5% error), suggesting the linear intelligence effect may encounter diminishing returns beyond the training range.
5. **Economic Viability:** Token consumption and latency grow substantially with agent count, often without proportional performance gains. The paper acknowledges this as a practical barrier requiring future efficiency-oriented designs.

**Overall Assessment:** The experiments provide strong empirical support for the paper's core claims about task-contingent coordination, capability ceilings, and architecture-dependent error amplification. The controlled design, diverse benchmarks, and out-of-sample validation establish credibility. However, the findings are conditional on the tested configurations (up to 9 agents, homogeneous teams, fixed token budgets), and extrapolation to larger scales or heterogeneous architectures requires additional validation. The paper appropriately acknowledges these limitations in Section 5 rather than overclaiming generalizability.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the boundaries of the paper's contributions. While the scaling framework represents a significant advance over heuristic-based agent design, it rests on specific assumptions, excludes important scenarios, and faces practical constraints that practitioners must understand before deployment. I organize these limitations into four categories: foundational assumptions, unaddressed scenarios, scalability constraints, and open questions.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions

The scaling model's predictive validity depends on several assumptions that may not hold in all deployment contexts.

**Assumption 1: Fixed Computational Budgets.** The entire experimental design matches total token budgets across architectures (mean $\mu = 4,800$ tokens per trial, Table 5). This control enables causal attribution but creates an artificial constraint: in production settings, teams often have flexible budgets where multi-agent systems can simply consume more tokens rather than fragment a fixed allocation. The paper's finding that multi-agent systems degrade on sequential tasks (PlanCraft: −70%) may be partially attributable to this budget constraint. If agents had unlimited token capacity, coordination overhead might become less prohibitive. The scaling equation does not model what happens when budget constraints are relaxed—a critical gap for well-resourced deployments.

**Assumption 2: Homogeneous Agent Teams.** All experiments use agents from the same LLM family, differing only in model size (e.g., GPT-5-nano vs. GPT-5) and role prompts. Section 5 explicitly acknowledges this limitation: "all agents share identical base architectures differing only in scale and role prompts." The paper does not test heterogeneous teams combining fundamentally different model architectures (e.g., a reasoning-specialized model coordinating with a tool-specialized model) or domain-specialized fine-tuning. Figure 4 explores capability heterogeneity (high vs. low intelligence within a family), finding that Anthropic models benefit from heterogeneous mixing in centralized architecture (+31% for low-capability orchestrator with high-capability subagents), while OpenAI and Gemini show degradation. However, this is architectural heterogeneity, not model-family heterogeneity. The scaling model cannot predict performance for teams mixing, for example, Claude with Gemini agents.

**Assumption 3: Stable Task Properties.** The model treats task properties (tool count $T$, single-agent baseline $P_{\text{SA}}$) as fixed, measurable inputs. However, in dynamic real-world environments, task properties may shift during execution. A financial analysis task might start with $T = 5$ tools but expand to $T = 12$ as new data sources become relevant. The scaling equation provides no guidance for adaptive architecture selection mid-execution. The paper acknowledges this implicitly by evaluating on static benchmark instances rather than continuous, evolving task streams.

**Assumption 4: Linear Intelligence Scaling Within Range.** The model includes a quadratic intelligence term $(I - \bar{I})^2$, but this coefficient is not significant ($\hat{\beta} = 0.007$, $p = 0.509$, Table 4). The paper concludes capability scaling follows a linear pattern within the tested range ($I \in [42, 71]$). However, out-of-sample validation on GPT-5.2 (Index = 75) reveals systematic SAS over-prediction (+49.5% error, Table 9), suggesting diminishing returns emerge beyond the training range that the linear term cannot capture. Appendix B explicitly states: "the over-prediction likely reflects limitations in linear extrapolation of the capability effect ($\hat{\beta}_I = 0.171$) beyond the training range." This means the model's architecture selection accuracy drops from 87% (within training range) to 0% for SAS prediction at Index 75, though MAS architecture ranking remains 75% accurate (3/4 correct).

**Assumption 5: Agentic Task Definition.** The paper's formal definition of agentic tasks (Section 3.2) requires sequential interdependence, partial observability, and adaptive strategy formation. However, the authors note: "We note that 'agentic' is defined relative to current model capabilities. For instance, GSM8K could be posed as agentic by providing calculator tools, though current LLMs do not require such scaffolding." This means the scaling principles may become obsolete as models improve—tasks that are agentic today (requiring multi-step interaction) may become solvable via single-shot inference tomorrow. The framework captures a snapshot of coordination dynamics at a specific capability frontier, not universal laws.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

Several important deployment scenarios fall outside the paper's experimental scope.

**Scenario 1: Long-Horizon Temporal Dependencies.** The benchmarks tested have bounded interaction lengths (mean 7.2 turns for SAS, up to 44.3 for Hybrid, Table 5). Real-world deployments often involve tasks spanning hours or days with hundreds of interaction turns—customer support conversations, multi-day research projects, or iterative software development. The paper's power-law turn scaling ($T = 2.72 \times (n + 0.5)^{1.724}$) predicts explosive growth: at $n = 10$ agents, the model predicts 50–80 turns, consuming the entire 4,800-token budget before meaningful reasoning occurs. Section 5 acknowledges this: "current agentic benchmarks capture dynamic text-based environments but do not yet include long-horizon temporal dependencies." The scaling principles may not generalize to settings where memory management, state persistence, and turn-level cost optimization dominate performance.

**Scenario 2: Embodied and Multimodal Settings.** All four benchmarks are text-based with symbolic tool interfaces (web search, code execution, API calls). The paper explicitly identifies this gap: "Integrating embodied or multimodal settings (e.g., robotic control, medical triage, multi-user social interaction) will test whether our observed scaling principles generalize beyond symbolic domains." Coordination dynamics may differ fundamentally when agents process visual inputs, control physical actuators, or interact with humans in real-time. Error amplification in a robotic system (where a 4.4× amplification could cause physical damage) carries different risk than in financial analysis. The framework provides no guidance for these modalities.

**Scenario 3: Adversarial or Competitive Environments.** The benchmarks assume cooperative task completion with static evaluation criteria. Real-world deployments may involve adversarial settings (e.g., negotiation, security analysis, competitive gaming) where other agents actively work against the system. The paper's coordination metrics (efficiency, redundancy, error amplification) assume a stationary environment. In adversarial contexts, metrics like strategic unpredictability or deception resistance may matter more than raw accuracy. This scenario is entirely unaddressed.

**Scenario 4: Multi-User Interaction.** Workbench simulates business workflows but does not model interactions with multiple human users who may provide conflicting instructions, change requirements mid-task, or exhibit unpredictable behavior. Section 5 notes this gap: "multi-user interaction...would strengthen confidence in the identified thresholds." Coordination overhead may increase substantially when agents must reconcile conflicting human inputs—a scenario the current framework cannot predict.

**Scenario 5: Extreme Capability Levels.** The validation on GPT-5.2 (Index 75) reveals that architecture-specific advantages may diminish at higher capability levels. On BrowseComp-Plus, the main results show Decentralized (+9.2%) outperforming Centralized (+0.2%), but GPT-5.2 shows convergence (both at 0.48, Table 8). Appendix B states: "architecture-specific advantages may decrease as the model capability increases." This suggests the scaling principles may become less discriminative for frontier models—a critical limitation for practitioners deploying state-of-the-art systems. If all architectures converge at high capability, the 87% architecture selection accuracy may degrade for future models.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

The paper identifies hard resource ceilings that fundamentally limit multi-agent scaling under current paradigms.

**Constraint 1: Super-Linear Turn Growth.** The power-law relationship $T = 2.72 \times (n + 0.5)^{1.724}$ with exponent 1.724 > 1 creates a hard ceiling. Under fixed token budgets, per-agent reasoning capacity becomes "prohibitively thin beyond 3–4 agents" (Section 4.4). Empirically, Hybrid systems require 6.2× more turns than SAS (44.3 vs. 7.2 turns, $t(178) = 16.8$, $p &lt; 0.001$). This is not an implementation limitation but a structural property of coordination: quadratic message complexity (all-to-all potential communication) tempered by practical bandwidth limits. Section 5 explicitly states: "Whether such collectives can exhibit beneficial emergent behaviors, such as spontaneous specialization or hierarchical self-organization, or whether communication bottlenecks dominate remains an open question." The framework cannot predict whether larger teams (n > 10) might overcome this ceiling through emergent coordination patterns.

**Constraint 2: Economic Viability.** Token efficiency reveals sharp trade-offs: SAS achieves 67.7 successes/1K tokens, while Hybrid achieves only 13.6 (5.0× worse, Table 5). Absolute costs vary by vendor: OpenAI Hybrid costs ≈$0.008 per 1% success gain, Anthropic Hybrid ≈$0.024 per 1% gain (3× worse), and Google ≈$0.012 per 1% gain. Section 5 acknowledges: "The economic viability of multi-agent scaling remains a practical barrier." For high-volume deployments, the 2–6× efficiency penalty (Table 5 shows $E_c$ ranging from 0.466 for SAS to 0.074 for Hybrid) may be prohibitive regardless of accuracy gains. The scaling model predicts performance but does not optimize for cost-effectiveness—a critical gap for production systems where budget constraints dominate.

**Constraint 3: Latency and Real-Time Requirements.** The paper measures performance in terms of accuracy and token efficiency but does not report wall-clock latency. Multi-agent systems inherently introduce synchronization delays: Centralized architectures require orchestrator aggregation, Decentralized architectures require debate rounds, and Hybrid architectures combine both. For real-time applications (e.g., customer support, trading), latency may matter more than accuracy. The framework provides no guidance for latency-accuracy trade-offs.

**Constraint 4: Tool-Heavy Environment Failure Mode.** The efficiency-tools interaction ($\hat{\beta}_{E_c \times T} = -0.267$, $p &lt; 0.001$) identifies tool-heavy tasks as a primary failure mode. For $T = 16$ tools, the efficiency penalty translates to $\Delta P_{\text{efficiency}} = -1.99$ (single-agent) vs. $-0.32$ (multi-agent) using raw interpretability values. Section 5 states: "Developing specialized coordination protocols for tool-intensive tasks, such as explicit tool-access scheduling, capability-aware task routing, or hierarchical tool delegation, represents an important direction." The current framework identifies this failure mode but provides no mitigation strategy—practicians must accept degraded performance on tool-heavy tasks or abandon multi-agent approaches entirely.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

Several methodological weaknesses and unresolved questions limit the framework's completeness.

**Weakness 1: Unexplained Variance.** The model achieves $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.524$, explaining slightly more than half of performance variance on held-out data. This means 47.6% of variance remains unexplained by the 20 predictors. While this is substantially better than alternatives (architecture labels alone: $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.430$; intelligence alone: $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.283$, Table 3), nearly half of performance dynamics are not captured. The paper does not speculate on what factors account for this residual variance—possibly unmeasured coordination dynamics, prompt sensitivity, or task-specific idiosyncrasies. Practitioners should expect prediction errors of approximately MAE = 0.089 (±0.011), which may be unacceptable for high-stakes deployments.

**Weakness 2: Prompt Sensitivity.** The paper controlled prompts to be identical across conditions but did not optimize prompts for each model or architecture. Section 5 acknowledges: "Given known sensitivity of LLM behavior to prompt formulation, architecture-specific prompt tuning may yield different scaling characteristics than those reported here." This is a significant limitation: the observed architecture differences may reflect suboptimal prompting for certain configurations rather than inherent architectural properties. For example, Centralized architectures might benefit from orchestrator-specific prompts that were not tested. The scaling principles may shift if prompts are optimized per-architecture.

**Weakness 3: Limited Benchmark Diversity.** Four benchmarks, while diverse in task structure (deterministic tool use, quantitative reasoning, sequential planning, dynamic web navigation), may not capture the full spectrum of agentic task characteristics. Section 5 notes: "additional environments, particularly those with intermediate characteristics or novel task structures...would strengthen confidence in the identified thresholds." The capability ceiling threshold ($P_{\text{SA}} \approx 0.45$) and complexity threshold ($D \approx 0.40$, Appendix C) are empirical estimates from these four benchmarks. Additional benchmarks might shift these thresholds, reducing confidence in their universality.

**Weakness 4: Error Taxonomy Incompleteness.** The paper identifies four error categories (Logical Contradiction, Numerical Drift, Context Omission, Coordination Failure) but acknowledges Coordination Failure is MAS-specific. However, the error absorption analysis shows Independent MAS has 0% coordination failure (no coordination mechanism exists), yet it exhibits the highest error amplification (17.2×). This suggests the taxonomy does not fully capture error propagation dynamics. The paper states error amplification is "better explained by other coordination mechanisms—particularly efficiency ($E_c$) and overhead ($O\%$)—rather than error propagation per se" (Section 4.3), but this sidesteps the question of what specific mechanisms cause Independent's catastrophic failure.

**Open Question 1: Emergent Behaviors at Scale.** Section 5 explicitly poses: "Whether such collectives can exhibit beneficial emergent behaviors, such as spontaneous specialization or hierarchical self-organization, or whether communication bottlenecks dominate remains an open question that parallels phase transitions in complex adaptive systems." The paper tests up to 9 agents (Figure 5), but the power-law fit suggests fundamental barriers. Whether larger teams might exhibit qualitatively different coordination patterns (e.g., spontaneous role specialization, dynamic hierarchy formation) is unknown. This parallels open questions in swarm intelligence and multi-agent reinforcement learning.

**Open Question 2: Epistemic Diversity vs. Coordination Noise.** The paper tests capability heterogeneity (high vs. low intelligence within a family) but not epistemic diversity (different reasoning strategies, domain specializations, or training data). Section 5 states: "Future work should investigate teams combining fundamentally different model architectures, domain-specialized fine-tuning, or complementary reasoning strategies to understand when epistemic diversity yields robustness rather than coordination noise." The scaling model cannot predict whether diversity improves robustness or introduces incompatibility—a critical question for production systems that may combine specialized models.

**Open Question 3: Adaptive Architecture Selection.** The framework predicts optimal architecture for a given task but assumes static selection. Real-world systems might benefit from dynamic architecture adaptation—starting with SAS for simple subtasks, escalating to Centralized for complex analysis, then switching to Decentralized for parallel exploration. The paper provides no guidance for such adaptive strategies. This represents a significant gap between the framework's capabilities and production needs.

**Open Question 4: Generalization to Non-English and Low-Resource Settings.** All benchmarks and models are English-dominant. Coordination dynamics may differ substantially in low-resource languages where model capabilities are weaker, tokenization is less efficient, or cultural context affects communication patterns. The paper does not address this, limiting applicability to global deployments.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than report experimental results—it establishes a foundational framework that reshapes how the field approaches multi-agent system design. This section examines how the work changes the research landscape, what new directions it opens, where it can be practically applied, and concrete guidance for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this framework.

### 7.1 Landscape Transformation: From Heuristics to Quantitative Science

The paper's most significant contribution is shifting agent system design from **engineering alchemy** to **measurement-driven science**. Prior to this work, practitioners faced three fundamental uncertainties:

1.  **When to use multi-agent systems:** Claims ranged from "More agents is all you need" (Li et al., 2024) to "single strong models match multi-agent teams" (Gao et al., 2025), with no quantitative criterion to resolve the contradiction.
2.  **Which architecture to select:** Choices between Centralized, Decentralized, or Hybrid coordination relied on intuition rather than measurable task properties.
3.  **What performance to expect:** No predictive model existed to estimate gains or losses from coordination before deployment.

This paper resolves all three uncertainties through the scaling principle (Equation 1, Section 4.3), which achieves cross-validated $R^2_{\text{CV}} = 0.524$ and 87% accuracy in architecture selection for held-out configurations. The implications extend beyond the specific findings:

**Redefining Evaluation Standards.** The paper's formalization of agentic tasks (Section 3.2) establishes that valid agentic evaluation requires three necessary conditions: sequential interdependence, partial observability, and adaptive strategy formation. This distinction matters because multi-agent systems show fundamentally different scaling behavior on agentic versus static benchmarks. Figure 2 demonstrates this starkly: Finance-Agent shows +80.8% gains under Centralized coordination, while PlanCraft degrades by −70% under the same architecture. Prior work that evaluated multi-agent systems on static benchmarks (e.g., HumanEval) may have drawn misleading conclusions about coordination benefits. Future benchmark design should incorporate the paper's agentic criteria to avoid conflating ensemble voting effects with genuine coordination value.

**Challenging Universal Scaling Claims.** The capability ceiling threshold at $P_{\text{SA}} \approx 0.45$ (Section 4.1) directly contradicts monotonic scaling assumptions. The interaction term $P_{\text{SA}} \times \log(1 + n_a)$ with $\hat{\beta} = -0.404$ ($p &lt; 0.001$) establishes that coordination becomes counterproductive once single-agent baselines exceed this threshold. This transforms architecture selection from an open optimization problem into a **bounded decision**: measure single-agent performance first, and if it exceeds 45%, multi-agent coordination is likely a net cost. The out-of-sample validation on GPT-5.2 confirms this principle generalizes to frontier models released after the study (Table 8).

**Establishing Safety Bounds for Deployment.** The error amplification quantification (Section 4.3) provides the first operational safety metrics for multi-agent deployment. Table 5 shows Independent architectures amplify errors $17.2\times$ while Centralized contains this to $4.4\times$. For high-stakes domains like financial analysis or medical decision-making, this $4\times$ variance in error propagation may matter more than average accuracy. The paper's token-overlap analysis reveals the mechanism: high-performing runs show dramatically reduced contradictory mass (median 2.3% in successes vs. 8.1% in failures), indicating messages converge toward mutually consistent sub-proofs. This insight enables practitioners to select architectures based on risk tolerance, not just performance targets.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions in Section 5 that define a research agenda for the next 2–3 years. I organize these into four categories, each with specific testable hypotheses derived from the paper's findings.

**Direction 1: Heterogeneous Model Teams.** The paper tests capability heterogeneity within LLM families (Figure 4) but not across fundamentally different architectures. Section 5 states: "Future work should investigate teams combining fundamentally different model architectures, domain-specialized fine-tuning, or complementary reasoning strategies." This opens three specific research questions:

*   **Cross-Family Coordination:** Does mixing model families (e.g., Claude orchestrator with Gemini sub-agents) improve robustness through epistemic diversity, or introduce coordination noise through incompatible communication patterns? The paper's finding that vendor-specific coordination preferences exist (Section 4.2) suggests this is non-trivial: Anthropic models show conservative Centralized performance, while OpenAI shows stronger Hybrid synergy.
*   **Specialization Benefits:** Do domain-specialized fine-tuned models (e.g., a finance-tuned sub-agent) outperform general-purpose models in multi-agent teams, and does specialization reduce the coordination overhead identified in the $O\% \times T$ interaction ($\hat{\beta} = -0.162$)?
*   **Role-Optimized Prompting:** The paper controlled prompts to be identical across conditions but did not optimize per-architecture (Section 5). Follow-up work should test whether orchestrator-specific prompts (e.g., explicit synthesis instructions for Centralized) or debate-specific prompts (e.g., challenge-response templates for Decentralized) shift the scaling coefficients.

**Direction 2: Long-Horizon and Embodied Settings.** All four benchmarks have bounded interaction lengths (mean 7.2 turns for SAS, up to 44.3 for Hybrid, Table 5). Section 5 explicitly identifies this gap: "current agentic benchmarks capture dynamic text-based environments but do not yet include long-horizon temporal dependencies or real-world feedback loops." This suggests two research priorities:

*   **Temporal Scaling Laws:** Does the power-law turn growth $T = 2.72 \times (n + 0.5)^{1.724}$ (Section 4.4) hold for tasks spanning hundreds of turns? At what point do memory management and state persistence dominate coordination costs? The paper's hard resource ceiling at 3–4 agents may shift if long-horizon tasks benefit from agent specialization over time.
*   **Embodied Coordination:** Section 5 notes that integrating "robotic control, medical triage, multi-user social interaction" will test whether scaling principles generalize beyond symbolic domains. Error amplification in embodied systems carries different risk profiles: a $4.4\times$ amplification in financial analysis is costly, but in robotic control it could cause physical damage. New safety metrics may be needed for embodied multi-agent deployment.

**Direction 3: Adaptive Architecture Selection.** The framework predicts optimal architecture for a given task but assumes static selection. Real-world systems might benefit from **dynamic architecture adaptation**—starting with SAS for simple subtasks, escalating to Centralized for complex analysis, then switching to Decentralized for parallel exploration. This opens several questions:

*   **Mid-Execution Switching:** Can systems detect when coordination overhead exceeds benefits during execution (e.g., when $O\%$ surpasses the critical threshold $O\%_{\text{max}}(T) \approx 150\%$ for $T = 16$, Section 4.3) and dynamically reconfigure?
*   **Hybrid Scheduling:** The paper identifies an optimal coordination band at $200\% &lt; O &lt; 300\%$ overhead (Section 4.4). Can systems adaptively maintain coordination within this band by scaling agent count or communication frequency?
*   **Early-Exit Mechanisms:** Section 5 suggests "efficiency-oriented designs, such as sparse communication, early-exit mechanisms, or distilled coordinator models" to improve economic viability. Research should quantify how early-exit thresholds affect the $E_c \times T$ interaction ($\hat{\beta} = -0.267$).

**Direction 4: Larger Team Sizes and Emergent Behaviors.** The paper tests up to 9 agents (Figure 5) but identifies fundamental barriers beyond 3–4 agents under fixed budgets. Section 5 poses the open question: "Whether such collectives can exhibit beneficial emergent behaviors, such as spontaneous specialization or hierarchical self-organization, or whether communication bottlenecks dominate remains an open question." This parallels phase transition research in complex adaptive systems. Specific hypotheses to test:

*   **Spontaneous Role Emergence:** At what team size do agents naturally specialize without explicit role assignment, and does this reduce the redundancy rate $R$ (currently 0.41–0.50, Table 5)?
*   **Hierarchical Self-Organization:** Do large teams ($n > 10$) spontaneously form sub-hierarchies that reduce the quadratic message complexity underlying the $1.724$ exponent in turn scaling?
*   **Collective Intelligence Thresholds:** Is there a team size where coordination benefits overcome the capability ceiling, or does the $P_{\text{SA}} \times \log(1 + n_a)$ interaction ($\hat{\beta} = -0.404$) impose a hard limit regardless of team size?

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The scaling framework enables concrete deployment decisions across multiple domains. I outline four high-value applications with specific integration guidance.

**Application 1: Architecture Selection Tool for Production Systems.** Practitioners can use the scaling equation (Equation 1) to compute expected performance for each architecture before deployment. The decision workflow is:

1.  **Measure single-agent baseline** $P_{\text{SA}}$ on a representative task sample (50–100 instances).
2.  **Count available tools** $T$ in the task environment.
3.  **Lookup coordination metrics** from Table 5 for candidate architectures (e.g., Centralized: $O\% = 285$, $A_e = 4.4$, $E_c = 0.120$).
4.  **Compute expected performance** using Equation 1 with model Intelligence Index $I$ from Appendix A.
5.  **Select architecture** with highest predicted performance, subject to cost constraints.

For example, consider a financial analysis task with $T = 5$ tools and measured $P_{\text{SA}} = 0.35$. The capability ceiling rule ($P_{\text{SA}} &lt; 0.45$) indicates multi-agent coordination is viable. The tool count is moderate, so the $E_c \times T$ penalty ($\hat{\beta} = -0.267$) is manageable. Centralized coordination is recommended due to low error amplification ($A_e = 4.4$) and strong performance on decomposable financial tasks (+80.8% in Figure 2).

**Application 2: Cost-Benefit Analysis for Resource Allocation.** The token efficiency metrics (Table 5) enable economic optimization. SAS achieves 67.7 successes/1K tokens, while Hybrid achieves only 13.6 (5.0× worse). For high-volume deployments, the paper's cost analysis (Section 5.4) provides vendor-specific guidance:

*   **OpenAI deployments:** Hybrid architectures cost ≈$0.008 per 1% success gain—steep but manageable for structured tasks where Hybrid shows +73% gains (Finance-Agent).
*   **Anthropic deployments:** Hybrid costs ≈$0.024 per 1% gain (3× worse than OpenAI), reflecting higher sensitivity to coordination overhead. Centralized is more cost-effective.
*   **Google deployments:** Intermediate costs ≈$0.012 per 1% gain with balanced efficiency across architectures.

Practitioners should compute the **marginal cost per accuracy point** for their specific use case and compare against business value thresholds. For low-stakes applications (e.g., content summarization), the 5× efficiency penalty may be unacceptable. For high-stakes applications (e.g., financial trading), the accuracy gains may justify the cost.

**Application 3: Safety Bounds for High-Stakes Domains.** The error amplification quantification enables risk-aware deployment in domains where errors carry significant consequences. The framework provides two safety metrics:

*   **Error Amplification Factor** $A_e$: Centralized architectures contain errors to $4.4\times$ baseline, while Independent amplifies to $17.2\times$. For medical or financial applications, this $4\times$ variance should inform architecture selection even if accuracy differences are marginal.
*   **Contradictory Token Mass**: High-performing runs show median 2.3% contradictory tokens vs. 8.1% in failures (Section 4.4). Monitoring this metric during deployment can detect coordination breakdowns before they cause task failures.

**Application 4: Benchmark Design for Agentic Evaluation.** The paper's agentic task criteria (Section 3.2) provides a checklist for evaluating whether new benchmarks appropriately test multi-agent capabilities:

*   Does the task require **sequential interdependence** (later actions depend on earlier observations)?
*   Does the task exhibit **partial observability** (critical information must be acquired through interaction)?
*   Does the task require **adaptive strategy formation** (policy must update beliefs based on feedback)?

Benchmarks failing these criteria may produce misleading multi-agent evaluations. For example, static coding benchmarks like HumanEval allow ensemble voting without environmental interaction, potentially overstating multi-agent benefits. New benchmarks should incorporate the paper's complexity metric $D$ (Appendix C) to enable cross-benchmark comparison of coordination efficacy.

*   **AutoGen:** The framework's architecture taxonomy maps directly to AutoGen's group chat (Decentralized) and manager-based (Centralized) patterns. Use the capability ceiling rule to decide when to fall back to single-agent conversations.

**Reproduction Checklist.** Researchers seeking to reproduce or extend this work should ensure:

1.  **Matched Token Budgets:** All architectures must receive equal total token allocations. The paper's findings depend on this control—unequal budgets conflate architecture effects with compute effects.
2.  **Agentic Benchmarks:** Use tasks satisfying the three agentic criteria (Section 3.2). Static benchmarks will not reveal the coordination dynamics this paper characterizes.
3.  **Coordination Metrics:** Measure all five coordination metrics (overhead, message density, redundancy, efficiency, error amplification) to enable comparison with the scaling model.

### 7.5 Summary: A Foundation for Principled Agent Design

This paper establishes the first quantitative framework for multi-agent system design, transforming architecture selection from heuristic guessing to measurement-driven decision-making. The key takeaways for practitioners are:

*   **Measure before deploying:** Single-agent baseline performance ($P_{\text{SA}}$) is the strongest predictor of whether multi-agent coordination will help. If $P_{\text{SA}} > 0.45$, coordination is likely a net cost.
*   **Match architecture to task structure:** Decomposable tasks (Finance-Agent) benefit from Centralized coordination (+80.8%), while sequential tasks (PlanCraft) degrade under any multi-agent architecture (−39% to −70%).
*   **Monitor coordination dynamics:** Error amplification ($A_e$) and efficiency ($E_c$) are more diagnostic than raw accuracy. Independent architectures amplify errors $17.2\times$—unacceptable for high-stakes domains.
*   **Expect diminishing returns beyond 4 agents:** The power-law turn scaling ($T \propto n^{1.724}$) creates a hard resource ceiling under fixed budgets.

The framework is not a universal oracle—47.6% of performance variance remains unexplained, and extrapolation to frontier models or embodied settings requires additional validation. However, it provides the field's first **predictive, generalizable, and actionable** principles for agent system design. Future work should extend these principles to heterogeneous teams, long-horizon tasks, and adaptive architectures, but the foundation for a science of scaling agent systems is now established.
