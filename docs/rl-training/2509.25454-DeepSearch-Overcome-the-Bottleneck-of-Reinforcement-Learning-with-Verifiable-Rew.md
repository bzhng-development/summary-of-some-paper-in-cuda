## 1. Executive Summary

DeepSearch addresses the training plateaus in Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) caused by sparse exploration by integrating Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) directly into the training loop rather than restricting it to inference. The resulting DeepSearch-1.5B model achieves a new state-of-the-art 62.95% average accuracy on mathematical reasoning benchmarks while consuming 5.7× fewer GPU hours (330 vs. 1883.2) than extended training approaches. These results establish that systematic training-time exploration offers a more efficient path to scaling reasoning capabilities than brute-force computation scaling.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the necessity of DeepSearch, we must first examine the current landscape of training language models for complex reasoning. The field has largely coalesced around **Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR)**, a paradigm where models are optimized based on objective correctness signals (e.g., whether a math solution matches the ground truth) rather than human preference. While RLVR has driven significant advances, it faces a critical scalability bottleneck that this paper aims to resolve.

### The Training Plateau Problem
The primary motivation for DeepSearch stems from a phenomenon observed in contemporary RLVR studies: **training plateaus**. As noted in the Abstract and Section 1, models often exhibit notable decreases in performance gains despite increased computational investment after thousands of optimization steps. This is not merely a saturation of capability but a structural failure in how the model explores the solution space during training.

Current RLVR practices rely on **sparse exploration patterns**. During a standard training step, the model generates a limited number of **rollouts** (complete solution trajectories) sampled directly from its current policy $\pi_\theta$. If the policy is already biased toward certain reasoning paths—even incorrect ones—direct sampling tends to reinforce those existing modes rather than discovering new, correct pathways. Section 1 describes this as a failure to provide "systematic coverage of the solution space." Consequently, the model stops learning because it rarely encounters the critical reasoning steps required to solve harder problems, leading to diminishing returns on additional training steps.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
To address reasoning challenges, prior work has generally followed two distinct paths, both of which leave significant gaps unaddressed:

*   **Inference-Only Search:** A common strategy involves scaling **test-time computation** using structured search methods like Tree of Thoughts or Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) (Section 2). While effective at improving accuracy during deployment, these methods treat search as a separate mechanism from the model's weights. The model learns to generate text, but it does not learn *how to search* during the weight update process. As Section 1 argues, this creates a "separation between training and inference," leaving untapped potential to integrate systematic exploration into the training process itself.
*   **Brute-Force Training Scaling:** Another approach is to simply extend training duration. However, Section 5.2 and Table 2 demonstrate that this yields diminishing returns. For example, extending training by 1,875 steps consumes 1,883.2 GPU hours but only achieves 62.02% accuracy, whereas DeepSearch reaches 62.95% with significantly fewer resources. This indicates that scaling **training depth** (more steps) is less effective than scaling **training breadth** (better exploration per step).

### The Exploration-Exploitation Mismatch
A subtle but critical issue lies in the mismatch between how models are trained versus how they are expected to perform. Modern reasoning models are often expected to demonstrate sophisticated search behaviors at inference time, yet they are trained using simple, direct rollouts that lack search structure. Section 1 highlights that traditional RLVR relies on limited rollouts that "may miss critical reasoning paths."

Consider the mathematical analogy of optimization: if a loss landscape has narrow valleys leading to optimal solutions, standard gradient estimates from sparse sampling may never point toward them. In reasoning tasks, a "critical reasoning path" might require a specific sequence of logical deductions that has low probability under the current policy. Without a mechanism to deliberately explore these low-probability but high-value paths during training, the policy remains stuck in local optima.

### DeepSearch's Strategic Positioning
DeepSearch positions itself as a bridge between these disconnected paradigms. Rather than treating search as an inference-only tool or relying on brute-force training steps, it embeds **Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)** directly into the RLVR training loop (Figure 1). This represents a fundamental shift in strategy:

1.  **From Depth to Breadth:** Instead of adding more training steps (depth), DeepSearch expands the reasoning frontier during each step (breadth) using structured search (Section 1).
2.  **From Outcome to Process:** By integrating tree search into training, the model receives supervision not just on whether the final answer is correct, but on the quality of intermediate reasoning steps via **fine-grained credit assignment** (Abstract, Section 3.2).
3.  **From Static to Adaptive:** The framework employs an adaptive replay buffer that focuses computation on challenging problems while caching solved ones, addressing the efficiency concerns that typically prevent MCTS from being used in training (Section 4).

By addressing the root cause of the training plateau—insufficient exploration—DeepSearch argues that the future of reasoning model development lies in "fundamentally rethinking how we structure the learning process to mirror the sophisticated reasoning patterns we expect at inference time" (Section 1). This context sets the stage for the technical mechanisms detailed in the subsequent sections, where we explore how MCTS is modified to operate efficiently within a gradient-based training environment.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems paper that proposes a novel training framework; the core idea is to embed Monte Carlo Tree Search directly into the reinforcement learning training loop rather than using it only at inference time.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

DeepSearch is a training framework that teaches language models to reason by having them systematically explore multiple solution paths during each training step, rather than just sampling a few random attempts. The system solves the problem of training plateaus in reasoning models by replacing blind trial-and-error exploration with structured tree search that deliberately seeks out both correct solutions and informative mistakes, then uses the entire search tree—not just final outcomes—to update the model's weights.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The DeepSearch system consists of four major components that operate in a sequential pipeline during each training iteration. First, the **Iterative Filtering Module** screens the training dataset to identify challenging problems that the current policy cannot yet solve reliably, using a Pass1@K threshold of 25% (Section 4.1). Second, the **MCTS Search Engine** builds a search tree for each selected problem, expanding nodes using the policy model πθ and evaluating terminal nodes with a verification function V that returns +1 for correct solutions and -1 for incorrect or incomplete ones (Section 3). Third, the **Replay Buffer Manager** caches verified correct solutions from earlier iterations, allowing the system to reuse known solutions instead of re-running expensive MCTS searches on already-solved problems (Section 4.2). Fourth, the **Tree-GRPO Optimizer** computes policy updates using q-values from all nodes in the search tree, applying soft clipping and mean-only advantage normalization to stabilize training (Section 4.3). Information flows from the filtering module to the search engine, which produces trajectories that populate the replay buffer and feed into the optimizer, which then updates the policy that will be used in the next iteration's search.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the MCTS search mechanics because tree construction is the foundation upon which all other components depend—without understanding how nodes are selected, expanded, and scored, the training objectives cannot be interpreted.
- We then explain the entropy-based guidance mechanism because it determines which trajectories are selected for backpropagation when no correct solution is found, which is critical for understanding how DeepSearch learns from mistakes.
- We proceed to the hybrid selection strategy because it combines local UCT comparisons with global frontier selection, representing the key algorithmic innovation that distinguishes DeepSearch from classical MCTS.
- We cover the adaptive training strategy next because it addresses the computational feasibility concern—explaining how the system avoids running full MCTS on every problem at every iteration.
- We conclude with the Tree-GRPO training objective because it shows how the search tree outputs are converted into gradient updates, completing the loop from exploration to learning.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Paper Type and Core Mechanism.** This is a systems paper proposing a training framework that integrates Monte Carlo Tree Search into Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards. The fundamental mechanism replaces direct policy rollouts with structured tree search during training, enabling the model to learn from systematic exploration rather than sparse sampling.

**System Pipeline and Data Flow.** The training process begins when the Iterative Filtering Module evaluates the current policy πθ(i) on the hard subset D(i)hard using Pass1@K with K = 4 samples per problem (Section 4.1). Problems with success rates below the threshold δ(i) = 25% remain in the active training set, while mastered problems are filtered out. For each retained problem x, the system checks the Replay Buffer R(i) to determine whether a cached correct solution tcached exists (Section 4.2). If a cached solution is available, the system uses tcached directly and supplements it with β · B direct rollouts from the current policy, where β is implicitly determined by the number of cached solutions per problem. If no cached solution exists, the system invokes the full MCTS search process MCTSfull(x), which constructs a search tree T with the question x as the root node. The MCTS engine then performs multiple rollout iterations, where each iteration consists of node selection, expansion, evaluation, and score backup phases. After MCTS completes, all extracted trajectories T(i)train are passed to the Tree-GRPO Optimizer, which computes policy updates using the q-values assigned to each node in the search tree. Finally, the Replay Buffer is updated with any newly discovered correct solutions from problems that still fail the filtering threshold, ensuring that solved problems are not recomputed in future iterations.

**MCTS Tree Structure and Node Representation.** The search tree T is constructed incrementally, where the root node represents the input question x and each child node corresponds to an intermediate reasoning step s generated by the policy model πθ (Section 3). A complete root-to-leaf path forms a trajectory t = x ⊕ s1 ⊕ s2 ⊕ ... ⊕ send, where ⊕ denotes sequence concatenation and send is a terminal node. Each node s maintains several attributes: the q-value q(s) representing the estimated value of that reasoning step, the visit count N(s) tracking how many times the node has been selected during search, the depth d(s) indicating the node's distance from the root, and the child count ξ(s) recording how many children have been expanded from that node. The maximum tree depth is capped at dT = 64 levels, derived from the rollout budget of 16,384 tokens divided by the expansion length of 256 tokens per node (Appendix A.2). Each node expansion generates n = 8 candidate children by sampling from the policy πθ(si | oi), where oi represents the observation context consisting of the question and all preceding steps (Section 3.1).

**Expansion with Entropy-Based Guidance.** During the expansion phase at step i, the system collects the current reasoning trajectory oi = x ⊕ s1 ⊕ s2 ⊕ ... ⊕ si−1 as the observation state (Section 3.1). The policy model πθ is then prompted to generate n candidates for the next reasoning step {si,j}nj=1. This expansion behavior repeats until terminal nodes send ∈ Send are reached, either by arriving at final answers or by hitting the maximum tree depth dT. Each terminal node is evaluated using a verification function V : Send → {0, 1}, where V(s) = 1 indicates a correct solution and V(s) = 0 indicates an incorrect or incomplete solution. The terminal nodes are partitioned into correct and incorrect subsets: S(k)correct = {s ∈ S(k)end | V(s) = 1} and S(k)incorrect = {s ∈ S(k)end | V(s) = 0}, where k denotes the iteration number. When S(k)correct = ∅ (no correct solutions found in the current iteration), the system employs entropy-based selection to identify the most confident negative example for supervision. The selected negative trajectory is s*neg = arg mins∈S(k)incorrect H̄(t(s)), where t(s) = (x, s1, s2, ..., s) represents the unique trajectory from root x to terminal node s. The average trajectory entropy is defined as H̄(t(s)) = (1/|t(s)|) Σ|t(s)|i=1 H(πθ(si | oi)), where the token-level entropy H(πθ(si | oi)) = −Σai,k πθ(ai,k | oi, ai,<k) log πθ(ai,k | oi, ai,<k) is the Monte Carlo estimation of the Shannon entropy of the policy distribution at step i. Here, ai,k denotes the k-th token of step si, and ai,<k denotes all tokens preceding ai,k. This selection strategy prioritizes incorrect reasoning sequences that exhibit low decision uncertainty, targeting areas where the model's decision-making is most confident and would therefore benefit most from corrective supervision. Table 5 demonstrates that this most-confident incorrect selection achieves 62.95% average accuracy, outperforming random incorrect trajectory selection (62.09%) and least-confident incorrect trajectory selection (61.90%) by +0.86 and +1.05 percentage points respectively.

**Heuristic Score Backup and Q-Value Propagation.** After selecting a trajectory t* for backpropagation (either a correct solution or the most confident negative trajectory t(s*neg)), the system updates q-values for all nodes along the selected path using an iterative update rule (Section 3.2). Let q(m)(si) denote the q-value for node si ∈ t* after the m-th rollout backpropagation. The update rule is q(m)(si) = q(m−1)(si) + γ(i, l) · q(m)(send), where γ(i, l) : Z+ × Z+ → [0, 1] is the depth decay function that assigns higher weights to nodes closer to the terminal node. The depth decay function is defined as γ(i, l) = max(i/l, γmin), where i is the current node index in the trajectory, l is the terminal node index, and γmin = 0.1 is the minimum decay threshold. This ensures that nodes nearer to the terminal outcome receive stronger credit assignment signals than nodes at the beginning of the trajectory. Terminal node rewards are assigned according to the verification function's result: q(send) = +1 if V(send) = 1 (correct), and q(send) = −1 if V(send) = 0 (incorrect) or d(send) < dT (incomplete). To ensure positive q-values for nodes on correct reasoning paths while penalizing nodes leading to incorrect outcomes, the system enforces a constrained update rule (Equation 5):

$$
q^{(m)}(s_i) = \begin{cases}
q^{(m-1)}(s_i) + \gamma(i, l) \cdot q^{(m)}(s_{end}) & \text{if } q^{(m-1)}(s_i) \cdot q^{(m)}(s_{end}) \geq 0, \\
\gamma(i, l) \cdot q^{(m)}(s_{end}) & \text{elif } q^{(m)}(s_{end}) > 0, \\
q^{(m-1)}(s_i) & \text{elif } q^{(m-1)}(s_i) > 0.
\end{cases}
$$

This constraint preserves the invariant that q(m)(si) ≥ 0 for all intermediate nodes si ∈ T \ Send lying on trajectories leading to correct solutions, while allowing negative values only for nodes that inevitably lead to incorrect outcomes. Appendix B.2 explains the rationale: when a node's existing q-value and the terminal reward share the same sign, the update simply adds the depth-decayed reward; if a node previously held a negative q-value but now lies on a correct trajectory, the accumulated negative value is discarded and overwritten with the decayed positive reward; if a node already has a positive q-value, new negative signals are not propagated to it, preventing a node proven capable of contributing to correct solutions from being downgraded due to occasional failed attempts.

**Hybrid Selection Strategy: Local UCT and Global Frontier.** The MCTS selection mechanism employs a hybrid strategy that combines traditional UCT-based local selection with novel global frontier selection, each serving distinct purposes (Section 3.3). For local sibling comparison during node expansion, the system follows the traditional MCTS protocol and employs the UCT algorithm: UCT(s) = Q(s) + λ√(ln Nparent(s) / N(s)), where Q(s) = q(s)/N(s) represents the average reward per visit, Nparent(s) is the number of visits from the parent node, and λ = 2.0 balances exploitation and exploration (Appendix A.2). This local selection ensures principled decisions when choosing among sibling nodes that share the same parent and context. However, for identifying the most promising node across the entire search tree for the next expansion round, the system uses global frontier selection rather than traditional root-to-leaf UCT traversals. The global frontier F is defined as F = {s ∈ T | ξ(s) = 0, s ∉ Send, d(s) < dT}, representing all leaf nodes that have not been expanded and are not terminal. For each frontier node s ∈ F, the system computes a frontier priority score:

$$
F(s) = \lambda_1 \times \underbrace{\tanh(Q_{parent}(s))}_{\text{Quality Potential}} + \lambda_2 \times \underbrace{H(\pi_\theta(s|o))}_{\text{Uncertainty Bonus}} + \lambda_3 \times \underbrace{D(d(s))}_{\text{Depth Bonus}}.
$$

The quality potential term tanh(Qparent(s)) encourages selection of nodes whose parents have demonstrated high value, using the tanh transformation to smoothly handle negative Q-values and map them to the range [−1, 1]. The uncertainty bonus term H(πθ(s|o)) provides exploration guidance by adjusting priority according to the policy's entropy, where the sign of its coefficient can steer selection toward regions with high confidence or uncertainty. The depth bonus term D(d(s)) encourages deeper exploration by providing additional priority to nodes at greater depths, where the paper empirically finds D(d(s)) = √(d(s)/dT) to be most effective among other variants including d(s) and log(d(s) + 1) (Table 3). The node with the highest frontier score is selected for the next expansion: s* = arg maxs∈F F(s). The default configuration uses λ1 = 0.4, λ2 = 0, and λ3 = 0.01 (Section 5.3). Table 3 shows that global frontier selection reduces iterations by 10.4% (209.6 → 187.7) and improves trajectory rewards (−0.82 → −0.65) compared to vanilla UCT, while maintaining similar search depth and entropy. The square root depth bonus configuration achieves the best balance, maintaining search quality (−0.65 reward) with significant computational savings (1070.7 seconds per tree vs. 1179.6 seconds for vanilla UCT).

**Adaptive Training with Progressive Filtering.** To address the computational infeasibility of applying MCTS to every training example, the system adopts an iterative filtering strategy with a replay buffer mechanism (Section 4). The training process begins by using the base policy πθ(0) to perform an initial screening on the entire dataset Dtrain, creating the first training subset D(0)hard = {x ∈ Dtrain | Pass1@K(x, πθ(0)) < δ(0)}, where Pass1@K(x, π) represents the success rate when sampling K = 4 solutions for problem x using policy π, and δ(0) ∈ (0, 1) is the initial filtering threshold set to 25% (Section 4.1). After each training phase i, the system re-evaluates the updated policy πθ(i) on the current hard subset and applies threshold-based filtering to create the next iteration's training set: D(i+1)hard = {x ∈ D(i)hard | Pass1@K(x, πθ(i)) < δ(i)}. This progressive filtering concentrates computational resources on increasingly challenging problems as the model improves. Appendix B.4 explains that while adaptive thresholding schedules could be designed, the fixed δ = 25% heuristic is intentionally simple to avoid introducing additional hyperparameters and to cleanly attribute improvements to the MCTS-based exploration rather than curriculum design.

**Replay Buffer with Cached Solutions.** To prevent catastrophic forgetting and efficiently leverage previously discovered solutions, the system maintains a replay buffer R that stores correct reasoning trajectories from earlier training phases (Section 4.2). During each training iteration i, the system identifies problems that obtained correct solutions through MCTS rollouts but still fail to meet the filtering threshold after training: R(i)candidates = {(x, tcorrect) | x ∈ D(i)hard, ∃tcorrect ∈ T(x), Pass1@K(x, πθ(i)) < δ(i)}. These candidate trajectories are added to the replay buffer, attaining R(i+1) = R(i) ∪ R(i)candidates. Instead of randomly sampling from the replay buffer, the system employs a deterministic strategy that directly utilizes cached solutions when available. For each problem x in the current training iteration, the system first checks whether a correct solution has been previously cached. This approach eliminates redundant MCTS computation for problems with known solutions while focusing computational resources on truly challenging unsolved problems. The hybrid rollout strategy is defined as:

$$
\text{Rollout}(x) = \begin{cases}
t_{cached} \cup \text{DirectRollouts}(x, \beta) & \text{if } (x, t_{cached}) \in R^{(i)}, \\
\text{MCTS}_{full}(x) & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

For problems with cached solutions, the system directly incorporates the stored correct trajectory tcached and supplements it with DirectRollouts(x, β), which samples β · B additional solution attempts from the current policy πθ(·|x), where β ∈ [0, 1] is implicitly determined by the number of cached solutions per problem and B is the standard sampling budget. For problems without cached solutions, the system applies the complete MCTS search process MCTSfull(x). Appendix B.3 tracks the evolution of cached versus unsolved problems during 5-round adaptive training, showing that the cached ratio increases monotonically from 0% in Round 1 to 33.2% in Round 5, demonstrating that DeepSearch progressively transfers problems from the unsolved pool into the replay buffer as MCTS uncovers verifiable solution paths.

**Tree-GRPO Training Objective.** After constructing a search tree T for a sample question x, the system develops the Tree-GRPO training objective that combines q-value regularization with policy optimization (Section 4.3). To address the q-value explosion problem for intermediate nodes while preserving meaningful gradients, the system first applies soft clipping using the hyperbolic tangent function: q(sj) = tanh(q(kmax)(sj)/ϵq) · qmax for all sj ∈ T \ Send, where kmax is the maximum rollout iterations, ϵq = 1.0 is the temperature parameter, and qmax = 1 defines the maximum allowable q-value magnitude (Equation 13). This soft clipping approach prevents q-value explosion by maintaining all intermediate node q-values within [−qmax, qmax], while preserving gradients everywhere and maintaining the relative ordering of q-values while compressing extreme outliers. In practice, fewer than about 5% of intermediate node q-values fall into the saturation region and less than 0.5% lie near the boundaries, so clipping mainly compresses pathological tails rather than altering the bulk of the distribution. With the regularized q-values, the Tree-GRPO objective is formulated as:

$$
J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{T \sim \mathcal{T}, t_i \sim T, (s_j, o_j) \sim t_i} \frac{1}{|s_j|} \sum_{k=1}^{|s_j|} \min\left(\rho_{j,k}(\theta) \hat{A}_{j,k}, \text{clip}\left(\rho_{j,k}(\theta), 1 - \epsilon_{low}, 1 + \epsilon_{high}\right) \hat{A}_{j,k}\right),
$$

where ρj,k(θ) = πθ(aj,k|oj,aj,<k) / πθold(aj,k|oj,aj,<k) is the importance ratio (Equation 14). The parameters ϵhigh and ϵlow follow the Clip-Higher strategy of DAPO, with ϵlow = 0.2 and ϵhigh = 0.28 (Appendix A.3). The system removes the KL regularization term DKL to naturally diverge, following recent work (Luo et al., 2025a; He et al., 2025a). An overlong buffer penalty is imposed to penalize responses that exceed a predefined maximum value of 4096 tokens. The advantage function for node sj in trajectory ti is computed using sequence-level normalization: Âj,k = q(sj) − µt, where µt is the average reward of the terminal nodes Send throughout the tree T (Equation 15). This mean-only normalization is crucial in practice, particularly for mitigating uncontrolled growth in response length. Notably, Tree-GRPO can be degraded to vanilla DAPO if the outcome reward q(send) is consistently used as q(sj) for all intermediate nodes.

**Training Configuration and Hyperparameters.** The complete training protocol spans 100 steps with model checkpointing performed every 5 steps (Appendix A.5). The system is implemented using the veRL framework and conducts all training experiments on a distributed setup across 16 NVIDIA H100 GPUs with 96GB of memory (Appendix A.1). The policy model is initialized with Nemotron-Research-Reasoning-Qwen-1.5B v2. The training process utilizes a global batch size of 256 samples, implemented through the DAPO-style Dynamic Batching strategy. Prompt truncation is applied from the left with a maximum prompt length of 2,048 tokens, and response generation is limited to 16,384 tokens. The actor model optimization employs AdamW with a conservative learning rate of 1 × 10−6 and 10 warmup steps, combined with weight decay of 0.1 and gradient clipping at 1.0 for stable convergence (Appendix A.3). Training proceeds with mini-batches of 32 samples per policy update using token-mean loss aggregation, while dynamic batch sizing accommodates up to 18,432 tokens per GPU. The entropy coefficient is set to 0 for pure exploitation, and KL divergence loss is disabled. During rollout generation, sampling parameters are configured with a high temperature of 1.0 and top_p of 1.0, while disabling top_k filtering to maintain diverse response generation (Appendix A.4). The system generates 8 rollouts per prompt, aligning with the expansion width parameter. During evaluation, the system uniformly uses a low temperature of 0.6 and top_p of 0.95.

**Design Choices and Alternative Considerations.** The hybrid selection strategy was chosen over pure UCT because global frontier selection overcomes UCT's myopia through cross-subtree resource allocation, achieving computational efficiency by eliminating redundant root-to-leaf traversals and enhanced exploration coverage by preventing the algorithm from getting trapped in locally promising but globally suboptimal subtrees (Section 3.3). The entropy-based guidance for negative trajectory selection was chosen over random or least-confident selection because high-confidence incorrect trajectories expose systematic reasoning errors that benefit most from targeted supervision, whereas random or low-confidence errors tend to be noisy or uninformative (Section 5.4, Table 5). The square root depth bonus D(d(s)) = √(d(s)/dT) was selected over linear or logarithmic variants because it offers the best balance between computational efficiency and search quality, maintaining −0.65 reward while reducing per-tree time from 1179.6 seconds to 1070.7 seconds (Table 3). The fixed filtering threshold δ = 25% was intentionally kept simple rather than using an adaptive schedule to avoid introducing additional hyperparameters and to cleanly attribute improvements to the MCTS-based exploration rather than curriculum design (Appendix B.4). The soft clipping mechanism was chosen over hard clipping because it naturally bounds q-values without hard discontinuities, preserves gradients everywhere preventing the zero-gradient problem, and maintains the relative ordering of q-values while compressing extreme outliers (Section 4.3). The mean-only advantage normalization was adopted over standard deviation normalization to address miscalibration issues in GRPO while maintaining stable advantage scaling (Section 5.4).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

DeepSearch is not merely an application of existing search techniques to a new domain; it introduces fundamental shifts in how we conceptualize the relationship between search, training, and supervision. Below, we distill the four most critical innovations that distinguish this work from prior RLVR and search-based reasoning approaches. We distinguish between **fundamental innovations** (changes to the paradigm or core algorithm) and **strategic improvements** (novel heuristics that yield significant gains).

### 4.1 Fundamental Innovation: Search as a Training Mechanism, Not Just Inference

The most profound contribution of DeepSearch is the architectural decision to embed Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) directly into the **training loop** rather than restricting it to inference.

*   **Prior Work Limitation:** As discussed in Section 2, standard practice treats search as an inference-time tool (e.g., Tree of Thoughts, inference-time scaling). The model weights are trained using simple direct rollouts, creating a mismatch: the model is trained to sample linearly but expected to search branching paths at test time. This separation limits the model's ability to internalize search strategies.
*   **DeepSearch's Shift:** By generating training data via MCTS, the model learns from **systematic exploration** rather than sparse sampling. This changes the data distribution the policy sees during gradient updates. Instead of reinforcing whatever path the policy happens to sample (which may be a local optimum), the model receives gradients based on paths discovered through structured search.
*   **Significance:** This directly addresses the **training plateau** phenomenon described in the Abstract and Section 1. Table 2 shows that extended training (brute-force scaling) yields diminishing returns (62.02% accuracy after 1,883.2 GPU hours), whereas DeepSearch achieves higher accuracy (62.95%) with far fewer resources (330 GPU hours). This proves that **training breadth** (quality of exploration per step) is a more effective scaling axis than **training depth** (number of steps) for reasoning tasks.

### 4.2 Fundamental Innovation: Global Frontier Selection Over Local UCT

While DeepSearch uses MCTS, it fundamentally alters the node selection strategy to suit the needs of training data generation rather than single-instance decision making.

*   **Prior Work Limitation:** Classical MCTS relies on Upper Confidence Bounds for Trees (UCT) for selection, which performs a root-to-leaf traversal balancing visit counts and values locally (Section 3.3). This is myopic; it can get trapped in a promising subtree while ignoring better opportunities elsewhere in the tree.
*   **DeepSearch's Shift:** DeepSearch introduces **Global Frontier Selection** (Equation 7). Instead of traversing from the root, it evaluates all unexpanded leaf nodes (the frontier) across the entire tree simultaneously. It selects the next node to expand based on a composite score of quality potential, uncertainty, and depth.
*   **Significance:** This design choice prioritizes **coverage** over strict optimality for a single query. Since the goal is to generate diverse training trajectories, exploring multiple promising branches globally is more valuable than deeply optimizing one path. Table 3 quantifies this benefit: Global Frontier Selection reduces search iterations by 10.4% (209.6 → 187.7) and improves trajectory rewards (−0.82 → −0.65) compared to vanilla UCT. This demonstrates that for **training data generation**, global resource allocation is superior to local greedy selection.

### 4.3 Strategic Improvement: Entropy-Guided Supervision on Confident Mistakes

When search fails to find a correct solution, standard RLVR methods typically discard the trajectory or treat all errors equally. DeepSearch introduces a nuanced strategy for selecting which errors to learn from.

*   **Prior Work Limitation:** Common practices involve sampling random negative trajectories or filtering based solely on outcome correctness. This ignores the **confidence** of the model. A model that is confidently wrong indicates a systematic reasoning flaw, whereas a model that is uncertainly wrong may simply be guessing.
*   **DeepSearch's Shift:** Section 3.1 describes selecting the **most confident incorrect trajectory** when no correct solution is found. This is defined as the trajectory with the lowest average entropy $\bar{H}(t(s))$ among incorrect terminals.
*   **Significance:** This turns uncertainty into a signal for **hard negative mining**. By supervising the model on paths where it was confident but wrong, DeepSearch targets systematic reasoning errors that direct rollouts rarely uncover. Table 5 validates this insight: selecting the most confident incorrect trajectory achieves 62.95% average accuracy, outperforming random incorrect selection (62.09%) and least-confident selection (61.90%). This confirms that **error quality** matters as much as solution correctness for stable RL training.

### 4.4 Strategic Improvement: Adaptive Compute Allocation via Replay Buffering

Integrating search into training introduces a massive computational overhead. DeepSearch solves this not by speeding up search itself, but by intelligently deciding **when** to search.

*   **Prior Work Limitation:** Standard RLVR applies the same compute budget to every problem in every batch. This is inefficient; easy problems do not need search, and solved problems do not need re-exploration.
*   **DeepSearch's Shift:** The **Adaptive Training Strategy** (Section 4) combines progressive filtering with a replay buffer. Problems are filtered into a "hard subset" based on a success rate threshold ($\delta = 25\%$), and solved problems are cached to avoid redundant MCTS execution (Equation 11).
*   **Significance:** This creates an implicit **curriculum** that shifts compute toward the model's current frontier of capability. Appendix B.3 shows that the ratio of cached (solved) problems grows from 0% to 33.2% over 5 rounds, meaning the system automatically reallocates resources away from mastered tasks. This efficiency mechanism is why DeepSearch achieves a 5.7× reduction in GPU hours compared to extended training baselines (Table 2). It demonstrates that **dynamic compute allocation** is essential for making search-augmented training practical at scale.

### 4.5 Synthesis: Why These Innovations Matter Together

Individually, each component addresses a specific bottleneck: Global Selection fixes exploration myopia, Entropy Guidance fixes supervision quality, and Adaptive Buffering fixes efficiency. However, their combination enables the core paradigm shift of **Training-Time Search**.

*   **Without Global Selection,** training-time search would be too slow and prone to local optima, failing to generate diverse data.
*   **Without Entropy Guidance,** the model would learn from noisy errors, potentially destabilizing policy updates.
*   **Without Adaptive Buffering,** the computational cost would be prohibitive, preventing the method from scaling beyond small experiments.

The result is a framework that does not just scale compute, but scales **reasoning capability** through algorithmic innovation. As noted in the Conclusion, this suggests the future of reasoning models lies in "scaling training breadth through algorithmic innovation rather than relying solely on deeper compute." DeepSearch provides the blueprint for this shift.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how DeepSearch was evaluated, what the results demonstrate, and whether the experiments convincingly support the paper's central claims. We will walk through the evaluation methodology step-by-step, analyze the quantitative results with specific numbers, and critically assess the ablation studies that isolate each component's contribution.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Training Setup.** DeepSearch is trained on **DeepMath-103K** (He et al., 2025c), a large-scale mathematical dataset designed with high difficulty and rigorous decontamination against numerous benchmarks (Section 5.1). The policy model is initialized from **Nemotron-Research-Reasoning-Qwen-1.5B v2** (Liu et al., 2025a), updated July 23rd. All training experiments are conducted on a distributed setup across **16 NVIDIA H100 GPUs with 96GB of memory** (Appendix A.1). The training protocol spans **100 steps** with model checkpointing performed every 5 steps (Appendix A.5).

**Evaluation Benchmarks.** The model is evaluated on **six mathematical reasoning benchmarks** (Section 5.1):

*   **AIME 2024/2025:** American Invitational Mathematics Examination — highly competitive high school math competition problems
*   **AMC2023:** American Mathematics Competitions — standardized math contest
*   **MATH500:** Hendrycks et al. (2021) benchmark — grade school to competition-level math problems
*   **Minerva:** Lewkowycz et al. (2022) — quantitative reasoning problems requiring multi-step solutions
*   **Olympiad:** He et al. (2024) — olympiad-level mathematical problems representing the highest difficulty tier

All evaluations report **Pass@1 accuracy with n = 32 samples** (Table 1 caption), meaning the model generates 32 candidate solutions per problem and accuracy is measured by whether at least one solution is correct. This is a standard metric for reasoning tasks where multiple attempts are permitted.

**Baseline Comparisons.** DeepSearch is compared against recent 1.5B-scale models spanning different mechanisms (Section 5.1):

*   **Base models:** Qwen2.5-Math variants (8.33% to 36.37% average accuracy)
*   **RL-trained models:** DeepSeek-R1-Distill (49.46%), STILL-3 (50.73%), Open-RS series (49.01% to 49.05%)
*   **Advanced RL methods:** DeepScaleR (55.64%), Nemotron v1 (60.17%), Nemotron v2 (61.70%)
*   **Search-based approaches:** Qwen2.5-Math-Oat-Zero (36.89%)

This comprehensive baseline set allows the authors to isolate whether improvements come from the search integration itself, rather than simply using a stronger base model or more training data.

**Computational Cost Measurement.** Efficiency is measured in **GPU hours**, calculated from the number of GPUs, training duration, and hardware specifications. Table 2 reports all evaluations were conducted on a **128×H100 96G cluster**, ensuring fair comparison across methods (Table 2 caption).

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**Benchmark Performance (Table 1).** DeepSearch-1.5B achieves **62.95% average accuracy** across all six benchmarks, establishing a new state-of-the-art for 1.5B reasoning models. This represents a **1.25 percentage point improvement** over the previous best (Nemotron-Research-Reasoning-Qwen-1.5B v2 at 61.70%).

The improvements are **consistent across all benchmarks**, not concentrated in a single domain:

| Benchmark | DeepSearch-1.5B | Nemotron v2 (Previous Best) | Improvement |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------------------|-------------|
| AIME24 | 53.65% | 51.77% | +1.88 pp |
| AIME25 | 35.42% | 32.92% | +2.50 pp |
| AMC23 | 90.39% | 88.83% | +1.56 pp |
| MATH | 92.53% | 92.24% | +0.29 pp |
| Minerva | 40.00% | 39.75% | +0.25 pp |
| Olympiad | 65.72% | 64.69% | +1.03 pp |

Several observations are worth noting:

*   **Largest gains on competition problems:** The most significant improvements appear on AIME 2025 (+2.50 pp) and AIME 2024 (+1.88 pp), which are among the most challenging benchmarks. This suggests DeepSearch's systematic exploration is particularly beneficial for problems requiring deeper reasoning chains.
*   **Diminishing returns on easier benchmarks:** MATH500 shows only +0.29 pp improvement, likely because the baseline already achieves 92.24% accuracy — there is less room for improvement when the model already solves most problems correctly.
*   **No degradation:** Critically, DeepSearch does not sacrifice performance on any benchmark to achieve gains on others. This rules out the possibility that the method overfits to specific problem types.

**Training Efficiency (Table 2).** The efficiency results are perhaps more striking than the accuracy gains. Table 2 compares DeepSearch against extended training approaches that scale purely through additional training steps:

*   Adding 325 steps (326.4 GPU hours) yields only +0.08 pp improvement (61.70% → 61.78%)
*   Adding 1,875 steps (1,883.2 GPU hours) yields only +0.32 pp improvement (61.70% → 62.02%)
*   DeepSearch adds only 50 steps (330 GPU hours) but achieves +1.25 pp improvement (61.70% → 62.95%)

> "DeepSearch achieves superior results through algorithmic innovation rather than brute-force computation. With only 50 additional training steps, DeepSearch reaches 62.95% accuracy using 330 GPU hours—outperforming the most extensive baseline (1,883.2 hours) while using 5.7× fewer resources." This **5.7× efficiency improvement** directly supports the paper's central claim that **training breadth** (quality of exploration per step) is more effective than **training depth** (number of steps) for reasoning tasks.

**Training Dynamics (Figure 2).** Figure 2 illustrates the training dynamics over 20 hours following 3K RLVR training, comparing DAPO (the baseline RLVR method) against DeepSearch:

*   **DAPO** exhibits gradual linear improvement with a shallow slope
*   **DeepSearch** demonstrates more efficient learning through structured exploration

> "The superior convergence suggests that RLVR bottlenecks stem from the quality of exploration rather than insufficient training time." (Section 5.2)

### 5.3 Ablation Studies

The paper includes three comprehensive ablation studies that isolate the contribution of each component. These are essential for validating that the improvements come from the proposed innovations rather than confounding factors.

**Search Strategy Ablation (Table 3).** Table 3 compares global frontier selection against vanilla UCT under different configurations on 1.2K samples from extremely hard DeepMath-103K problems:

| Method | Depth Bonus | Avg Depth | Trajectory Reward | Num. Iterations | Time Per Tree (s) |
|--------|-------------|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Vanilla UCT | – | 20.11 | −0.82 | 209.6 | 1179.6 |
| Global Frontier (λ1=0.4) | – | 20.28 | −0.65 | 187.7 | 1087.7 |
| Global Frontier + √(d/dT) | λ3=0.01 | 20.29 | −0.65 | 189.3 | 1070.7 |
| Global Frontier + d(s) | λ3=0.01 | 21.55 | −0.76 | 85.7 | 480.9 |

Key findings:

*   **Global vs. Local Selection:** Global frontier selection reduces iterations by **10.4%** (209.6 → 187.7) and improves trajectory rewards (**−0.82 → −0.65**) compared to vanilla UCT, while maintaining similar search depth and entropy. This confirms that direct comparison of frontier nodes across the entire tree is more efficient than traditional root-to-leaf UCT traversals (Section 5.3).
*   **Depth Bonus Impact:** Three depth bonus functions are evaluated:
    *   **Logarithmic** `log(d(s) + 1)`: Provides minimal improvements
    *   **Linear** `d(s)`: Achieves the most aggressive efficiency gains with **59% reduction** in per-tree time (1179.6s → 480.9s) and deepest exploration (21.55 depth), but at cost of solution quality (−0.76 reward)
    *   **Square root** `√(d(s)/dT)`: Offers the best balance, maintaining search quality (−0.65 reward) with significant computational savings (Section 5.3)
*   **Uncertainty Bonus:** Adding uncertainty weighting (λ2 = 0.4) increases exploration diversity (entropy 1.23 → 1.31) by prioritizing high-uncertainty policy regions, but introduces computational variability (92.5 ± 22.5 iterations vs. 189.3 ± 14.7 without).

The default configuration adopts `√(d(s)/dT)` with λ1 = 0.4, λ3 = 0.01, balancing computational efficiency (189.3 iterations), search quality (−0.65 reward), and stable performance (Section 5.3).

**Component Evolution Ablation (Table 4).** Table 4 shows the step-by-step evolution of DeepSearch, starting from the Nemotron-Qwen-1.5B v2 base and incrementally adding components:

| Model / Change | AIME24 | AIME25 | AMC23 | MATH | Minerva | Olympiad | Avg |
|----------------|--------|--------|-------|------|---------|----------|-----|
| Nemotron v2 (baseline) | 51.77 | 32.92 | 88.83 | 92.24 | 39.75 | 64.69 | 61.70 |
| + Vanilla DeepSearch | 51.98 | 34.06 | 86.64 | 87.00 | 37.96 | 64.00 | 60.27 |
| + New q Update & Coarse-grained | 51.04 | 35.73 | 86.48 | 90.66 | 39.14 | 65.23 | 61.38 |
| + New q Update & Fine-grained | 50.52 | 35.52 | 88.83 | 91.70 | 39.71 | 64.81 | 61.85 |
| + Standard Advantages Normalization | 52.60 | 35.00 | 89.30 | 92.44 | 39.29 | 64.99 | 62.27 |
| + Mean-only Advantages Normalization | 51.98 | 35.73 | 89.06 | 91.88 | 39.58 | 65.71 | 62.32 |
| **+ Frontier Selection** | **53.65** | **35.42** | **90.39** | **92.53** | **40.00** | **65.72** | **62.95** |

This ablation reveals several important insights:

*   **Vanilla DeepSearch alone degrades performance** (61.70% → 60.27%), indicating that naive MCTS integration without the proposed innovations is harmful. This validates that the specific design choices (q-value updates, normalization, frontier selection) are essential, not optional.
*   **Fine-grained node-level advantages** provide meaningful improvement over coarse-grained outcome rewards (61.38% → 61.85%), confirming that step-level credit assignment matters.
*   **Mean-only normalization** outperforms standard deviation normalization (62.32% vs. 62.27%), addressing miscalibration issues in GRPO while maintaining stable advantage scaling (Section 5.4).
*   **Frontier selection provides the largest single improvement** (+0.63 pp from 62.32% to 62.95%), confirming that global resource allocation is the most impactful innovation.

> "The results demonstrate that each component contributes meaningfully to the final performance, with frontier selection providing the largest single improvement. The cumulative effect shows that systematic exploration and fine-grained credit assignment are both essential for maximizing the benefits of search-augmented RLVR." (Section 5.4)

**Trajectory Selection Strategy (Table 5).** Table 5 compares three strategies for selecting incorrect trajectories when no correct solution is found:

| Method | AIME24 | AIME25 | AMC23 | MATH | Minerva | Olympiad | Avg |
|--------|--------|--------|-------|------|---------|----------|-----|
| Nemotron v2 (baseline) | 51.77 | 32.92 | 88.83 | 92.24 | 39.75 | 64.69 | 61.70 |
| + Random incorrect trajectory | 52.41 | 34.23 | 89.31 | 92.21 | 39.97 | 64.41 | 62.09 |
| + Least confident incorrect trajectory | 52.16 | 33.77 | 88.68 | 91.96 | 40.11 | 64.73 | 61.90 |
| **+ Most confident incorrect trajectory** | **53.65** | **35.42** | **90.39** | **92.53** | **40.00** | **65.72** | **62.95** |

This ablation validates the entropy-based guidance mechanism:

*   **Most confident incorrect** achieves **+0.86 pp** over random selection and **+1.05 pp** over least confident selection
*   It is the **only method that consistently improves results across all benchmarks**
*   This confirms that high-confidence incorrect trajectories expose systematic reasoning errors that benefit most from targeted supervision, whereas random or low-confidence errors tend to be noisy or uninformative (Section 5.4)

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

**Do the experiments support the main claims?**

The experiments provide **strong evidence** for the paper's central claims, with some caveats:

1.  **Claim: Training-time search addresses exploration bottlenecks.** **Supported.** Table 2 shows that extended training yields diminishing returns (62.02% after 1,883.2 GPU hours), while DeepSearch achieves higher accuracy (62.95%) with 5.7× fewer resources. Figure 2 demonstrates steeper learning curves, indicating more informative training steps.

2.  **Claim: Global frontier selection is more efficient than UCT.** **Supported.** Table 3 shows 10.4% reduction in iterations and improved trajectory rewards (−0.82 → −0.65). The computational profiling in Appendix C confirms that GPU policy inference dominates runtime (99% of per-tree computation), so reducing iterations directly translates to efficiency gains.

3.  **Claim: Entropy-based guidance improves supervision quality.** **Supported.** Table 5 shows consistent improvements across all benchmarks when selecting most-confident incorrect trajectories. The +1.05 pp improvement over least-confident selection is statistically meaningful given the benchmark difficulty.

4.  **Claim: Adaptive replay buffering enables practical training-time search.** **Partially supported.** Appendix B.3 shows the cached ratio grows from 0% to 33.2% over 5 rounds, demonstrating progressive problem solving. However, the paper does not report ablation results isolating the replay buffer's contribution to final accuracy — Table 4 shows component evolution but does not explicitly remove the replay buffer to measure its impact.

**Limitations and Open Questions.**

Several limitations should be noted:

*   **Domain specificity:** All experiments are on **mathematical reasoning benchmarks**. The paper acknowledges this in the Limitations section, noting that extending DeepSearch to domains with different verification mechanisms (e.g., subjective tasks) remains future work. The claims about "reasoning capabilities" are specifically about **verifiable reasoning** where correctness can be automatically checked.

*   **Model scale:** All results are for **1.5B parameter models**. While this is appropriate for establishing proof-of-concept, it remains unclear whether the benefits scale to larger models (7B, 70B, etc.). Larger models may have different exploration characteristics that could affect the relative benefit of MCTS integration.

*   **Training steps:** The complete training protocol spans only **100 steps** (Appendix A.5), which is relatively short compared to typical RLVR training runs (thousands of steps). This is justified by the efficiency claims, but it means the long-term stability of DeepSearch beyond 100 steps is not evaluated.

*   **Compute profiling:** Appendix C provides detailed computation breakdown, showing that **GPU policy inference dominates runtime** (1060.76 ms out of ~1070 ms per tree, or 99%). This is important because it means further speedups will mainly come from optimizing model inference (batching, KV-cache reuse, speculative decoding) rather than modifying the search algorithm structure. However, the paper does not explore these inference optimizations.

*   **Qualitative case studies:** Appendix E provides 6 representative cases comparing DeepSearch-1.5B against Nemotron v2. These show that DeepSearch produces "shorter, more stable reasoning chains" while the baseline "falls into long 'wait'-style hesitation loops." While illustrative, these are anecdotal and not systematically quantified (e.g., average reasoning chain length, hesitation token counts across the full test set).

**Robustness Checks.**

The paper includes several robustness checks that strengthen confidence in the results:

*   **Multiple baselines:** Comparison against 12+ different 1.5B models across different training paradigms (Section 5.1)
*   **Multiple benchmarks:** Six diverse mathematical benchmarks spanning different difficulty levels (Table 1)
*   **Component ablations:** Three separate ablation studies isolating search strategy, component evolution, and trajectory selection (Tables 3, 4, 5)
*   **Efficiency analysis:** Multiple extended training baselines with varying step counts to establish the diminishing returns curve (Table 2)
*   **Computation profiling:** Detailed breakdown of per-tree runtime to identify bottlenecks (Appendix C, Table 7)

However, some robustness checks are missing:

*   **Random seed variability:** The paper does not report results across multiple random seeds or provide confidence intervals on the accuracy measurements
*   **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** While different depth bonus functions are tested (Table 3), the sensitivity to other hyperparameters (λ1, λ2, λ3 values, filtering threshold δ) is not systematically explored
*   **Cross-domain transfer:** No experiments test whether models trained with DeepSearch generalize better to non-mathematical reasoning tasks

### 5.5 Summary of Experimental Evidence

The experimental results provide **convincing evidence** that DeepSearch's core innovations improve both accuracy and efficiency for mathematical reasoning tasks:

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Key Supporting Data |
|-------|------------------|---------------------|
| Training-time search > inference-only search | Strong | Table 1: 62.95% vs 61.70% (previous best) |
| Algorithmic innovation > brute-force scaling | Strong | Table 2: 5.7× fewer GPU hours for better accuracy |
| Global frontier selection > UCT | Strong | Table 3: 10.4% fewer iterations, better rewards |
| Entropy-guided negative selection | Strong | Table 5: +1.05 pp over least-confident selection |
| Adaptive replay buffering | Moderate | Appendix B.3: 33.2% cached ratio by Round 5 |
| Fine-grained credit assignment | Strong | Table 4: +0.47 pp from coarse to fine-grained |

The most compelling evidence is the **efficiency comparison in Table 2**, which directly challenges the assumption that improving RLVR performance requires proportionally increasing computational budgets. The **component ablation in Table 4** is equally important, showing that each proposed innovation contributes meaningfully and that naive MCTS integration without the specific design choices actually degrades performance.

The primary limitation is **domain specificity** — all claims are validated only for mathematical reasoning with verifiable rewards. Whether these benefits transfer to other reasoning domains remains an open question that the authors acknowledge in the Limitations section.

Overall, the experiments are **well-designed, comprehensive, and appropriately scoped** for a preprint under review. The results support the paper's central thesis that systematic exploration during training can provide greater gains than simply extending training time, highlighting "the value of scaling training breadth through algorithmic innovation rather than relying solely on deeper compute" (Conclusion).

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While DeepSearch demonstrates compelling improvements in mathematical reasoning efficiency and accuracy, the approach relies on several assumptions and faces constraints that limit its broader applicability. This section critically examines the trade-offs inherent in the design, the scenarios where the method may not generalize, and the open questions that remain unresolved.

### 6.1 Dependence on Verifiable Rewards

The most fundamental constraint of DeepSearch is its reliance on **verifiable rewards** — the framework requires an automatic verification function $V: S_{end} \rightarrow \{0, 1\}$ that can determine whether a solution is correct (Section 3.1). This assumption restricts applicability to domains where ground-truth answers can be programmatically validated.

**Implications:**

*   **Mathematical reasoning** is well-suited because answers can be checked against known solutions or verified through symbolic computation.
*   **Subjective or open-ended tasks** (e.g., creative writing, ethical reasoning, open-domain dialogue) lack such verifiers. The Limitations and Future Work section (page 26) explicitly acknowledges this, noting that extending DeepSearch to "domains with different verification mechanisms" requires developing "approximate verifiers for subjective tasks" or "human-in-the-loop validation."
*   **Partial credit scenarios** are not addressed. The verification function is binary ($+1$ for correct, $-1$ for incorrect/incomplete), which may be too coarse for tasks where partially correct reasoning should receive intermediate rewards.

This constraint means DeepSearch cannot be directly applied to many real-world reasoning tasks where correctness is ambiguous or requires human judgment. The paper's claims about "reasoning capabilities" are specifically about **verifiable reasoning**, not general reasoning.

### 6.2 Domain Specificity and Generalization

All experimental validation is confined to **mathematical reasoning benchmarks** (Section 5.1). While the six benchmarks span different difficulty levels (from MATH500 to Olympiad), they share common characteristics:

*   Problems have single, unambiguous answers
*   Solutions can be expressed as structured text with boxed final answers
*   Correctness can be verified through string matching or symbolic evaluation

**Missing Evaluations:**

*   **Non-mathematical reasoning:** No experiments test code generation, logical deduction without numerical answers, or scientific reasoning where verification is more complex.
*   **Cross-domain transfer:** The paper does not evaluate whether models trained with DeepSearch on math problems generalize better to non-mathematical reasoning tasks. This is acknowledged as future work in the Limitations section (page 26).
*   **Multimodal reasoning:** Tasks requiring image or diagram interpretation are not addressed, despite the growing importance of multimodal reasoning benchmarks.

The qualitative case studies in Appendix E (6 examples) provide illustrative evidence but are **anecdotal and not systematically quantified**. For instance, while the paper notes that DeepSearch produces "shorter, more stable reasoning chains" while the baseline "falls into long 'wait'-style hesitation loops," there are no aggregate statistics on average reasoning chain length, hesitation token counts, or error patterns across the full test set.

### 6.3 Model Scale Constraints

All results are reported for **1.5B parameter models** (Table 1, Table 2). While this is appropriate for establishing proof-of-concept, it leaves critical questions unanswered:

*   **Scalability to larger models:** It is unclear whether the benefits of training-time search scale to 7B, 70B, or larger models. Larger models may have different exploration characteristics — they might already discover correct paths through direct rollouts more frequently, reducing the marginal benefit of MCTS.
*   **Compute trade-offs at scale:** The 5.7× efficiency improvement (330 vs. 1,883.2 GPU hours) is measured at 1.5B scale. At larger scales, the absolute compute cost of MCTS integration may become prohibitive even with the adaptive replay buffer, since each tree expansion requires policy inference from a much larger model.
*   **Parameter efficiency:** The paper does not explore whether smaller models trained with DeepSearch can match larger models trained with standard RLVR. This would be a stronger test of whether algorithmic innovation can substitute for parameter scaling.

The Limitations section (page 26) does not address model scale explicitly, leaving this as an open question for future work.

### 6.4 Computational Overhead and Bottlenecks

While DeepSearch is more efficient than extended training, it still introduces **significant computational overhead** compared to direct rollout methods. Appendix C (Table 7) provides a detailed computation profile:

| Component | Time (ms) | Percentage |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Policy inference / generation | 1060.76 | ~99% |
| All CPU-side operations | ~3.4 | ~1% |

**Key observations:**

*   **GPU policy inference dominates runtime** at 99% of per-tree computation. This means further speedups must come from optimizing model inference (batching, KV-cache reuse, speculative decoding) rather than modifying the search algorithm structure.
*   **MCTS adds latency per training step.** Even with the adaptive replay buffer caching 33.2% of problems by Round 5 (Appendix B.3, Table 6), the remaining 66.8% still require full MCTS execution.
*   **No inference optimization explored.** The paper acknowledges this bottleneck but does not implement or evaluate inference optimizations that could mitigate it.

The trade-off is clear: DeepSearch exchanges **per-step compute** for **fewer total steps**. This is beneficial when training compute is the constraint, but it may not be suitable for scenarios where training latency (wall-clock time per iteration) is critical.

### 6.5 Fixed Heuristics vs. Learnable Components

Several key components of DeepSearch are **fixed heuristics** selected through offline experiments rather than learned end-to-end:

*   **Frontier priority weights:** $\lambda_1 = 0.4$, $\lambda_2 = 0$, $\lambda_3 = 0.01$ are fixed values (Section 5.3). The paper does not explore whether these should adapt during training or vary by problem difficulty.
*   **Filtering threshold:** $\delta = 25\%$ is fixed across all training rounds (Appendix B.4). The authors explicitly chose this simplicity to "avoid introducing additional hyperparameters," but acknowledge that "adaptive thresholding remains a promising direction."
*   **Expansion width:** $n = 8$ candidates per node expansion is fixed (Appendix A.2).
*   **Maximum depth:** $d_T = 64$ levels is derived from token budget constraints rather than learned (Appendix B.1).

The Limitations and Future Work section (page 26) explicitly identifies this as an area for improvement:

> "In this work, the frontier priority function and expansion width are fixed heuristics selected through systematic offline experiments... Making these components learnable would require a fundamentally different training paradigm, such as an AlphaZero-style controller that jointly optimizes search and policy."

This represents a **design trade-off**: fixed heuristics simplify implementation and reduce optimization complexity, but they may not be optimal across all problem types or training stages. An end-to-end learnable search policy could potentially adapt to problem difficulty dynamically but would introduce additional training instability and complexity.

### 6.6 Training Duration and Long-Term Stability

The complete training protocol spans only **100 steps** with checkpointing every 5 steps (Appendix A.5). This is relatively short compared to typical RLVR training runs, which often extend to thousands of steps.

**Unanswered questions:**

*   **Long-term stability:** Does DeepSearch continue to improve beyond 100 steps, or does it encounter its own plateau? The paper does not evaluate training dynamics beyond the 20-hour window shown in Figure 2.
*   **Catastrophic forgetting:** While the replay buffer is designed to prevent forgetting (Section 4.2), the paper does not explicitly measure whether previously mastered problems degrade in performance over extended training.
*   **Diminishing returns:** Figure 2 shows DeepSearch has a steeper learning curve than DAPO, but it is unclear whether this advantage persists indefinitely or converges to similar asymptotic performance.

The short training duration is justified by the efficiency claims, but it means the **long-term behavior of search-augmented training** remains uncharacterized.

### 6.7 Experimental Robustness Gaps

Several standard robustness checks are missing from the experimental evaluation:

*   **Random seed variability:** The paper does not report results across multiple random seeds or provide confidence intervals on accuracy measurements. Given the stochastic nature of MCTS and policy sampling, single-run results may not be fully representative.
*   **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** While different depth bonus functions are tested (Table 3), sensitivity to other hyperparameters ($\lambda_1$, $\lambda_2$, $\lambda_3$ values, filtering threshold $\delta$) is not systematically explored. Small changes to these values could significantly affect performance.
*   **Ablation of replay buffer:** Table 4 shows component evolution but does not explicitly remove the replay buffer to measure its isolated impact on final accuracy. Appendix B.3 tracks cached problem ratios but does not correlate this with performance gains.

These gaps do not invalidate the results but suggest that **additional validation would strengthen confidence** in the reported improvements, particularly for practitioners seeking to reproduce or extend the work.

### 6.8 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes the key trade-offs inherent in DeepSearch's design:

| Design Choice | Benefit | Cost/Limitation |
|---------------|---------|-----------------|
| Training-time MCTS | Better exploration, higher accuracy | 99% GPU inference overhead per tree |
| Global frontier selection | 10.4% fewer iterations than UCT | Fixed $\lambda$ weights, not learned |
| Entropy-based negative selection | +1.05 pp over least-confident | Requires entropy computation at each step |
| Fixed $\delta = 25\%$ threshold | Simple, clean attribution | May not be optimal for all problem distributions |
| Replay buffer caching | 33.2% problems cached by Round 5 | No ablation isolating buffer's accuracy contribution |
| 1.5B model scale | Proof-of-concept feasibility | Unknown scalability to larger models |
| Math-only benchmarks | Verifiable rewards available | Limited generalization to other domains |
| 100 training steps | Efficient training protocol | Long-term stability uncharacterized |

### 6.9 Open Questions for Future Work

Based on the limitations identified above, several important questions remain:

1.  **Can DeepSearch scale to 7B+ models?** The compute trade-offs may shift dramatically at larger scales.
2.  **Can verification be approximated for non-math domains?** Developing reliable approximate verifiers is essential for broader applicability.
3.  **Should search components be learnable?** An AlphaZero-style controller could adapt search strategy dynamically but adds complexity.
4.  **What is the asymptotic performance ceiling?** Does DeepSearch continue improving beyond 100 steps, or does it plateau?
5.  **Does training-time search improve inference-time search?** The paper integrates search into training, but does not measure whether this makes the model better at inference-time search as well.

The Limitations and Future Work section (page 26) acknowledges several of these directions, demonstrating awareness of the constraints. However, addressing them will require significant additional research beyond the scope of this work.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

DeepSearch does more than improve benchmark scores; it challenges the prevailing assumption that reasoning capabilities must be scaled primarily through increased compute budgets or parameter counts. By demonstrating that **algorithmic innovation in the training loop** can yield superior results with significantly fewer resources, this work opens new avenues for research and practical deployment. This section explores how DeepSearch reshapes the field, the specific research questions it enables, and concrete guidance for practitioners considering integration.

### 7.1 Shifting the Scaling Paradigm

The most significant implication of DeepSearch is the validation of **training breadth** as a viable scaling axis, distinct from traditional **training depth** (more steps) or **model scale** (more parameters).

*   **Challenging Brute-Force Scaling:** Prior work often assumed that overcoming RLVR plateaus required simply extending training duration. Table 2 explicitly contradicts this: extending training by 1,875 steps consumed 1,883.2 GPU hours for only a 0.32 percentage point gain (61.70% → 62.02%). In contrast, DeepSearch achieved a 1.25 percentage point gain (61.70% → 62.95%) using only 330 GPU hours.
*   **Algorithmic Efficiency > Compute Volume:** The 5.7× efficiency improvement suggests that for reasoning tasks, the **quality of data generation** matters more than the **quantity of optimization steps**. This shifts the research focus from "how do we train longer?" to "how do we explore smarter during training?"
*   **Democratization of Reasoning Models:** The Ethics Statement (Page 11) highlights that reducing computational requirements (330 vs. 1,883 GPU hours) potentially decreases environmental impact and lowers barriers to entry. This implies that smaller research groups without access to massive compute clusters can compete on reasoning tasks by adopting search-augmented training strategies rather than brute-force scaling.

> "This suggests that systematic exploration during training can, in many cases, provide greater gains than simply extending training time, highlighting the value of scaling training breadth through algorithmic innovation rather than relying solely on deeper compute." (Conclusion)

### 7.2 Enabling New Research Avenues

DeepSearch establishes a foundation for several critical follow-up research directions. The paper explicitly identifies these in the Limitations and Future Work section (Page 26), but we can expand on the technical implications of each.

#### 7.2.1 Learnable Search Components
Currently, DeepSearch relies on **fixed heuristics** for search control. The frontier priority score weights ($\lambda_1 = 0.4, \lambda_3 = 0.01$) and the filtering threshold ($\delta = 25\%$) are determined via offline experiments (Section 5.3, Appendix B.4).
*   **Future Direction:** Developing an **AlphaZero-style controller** that jointly optimizes the search policy and the model weights. Instead of fixed $\lambda$ values, a learned network could predict the optimal exploration weight based on problem difficulty or training stage.
*   **Why It Matters:** Fixed heuristics may not generalize across domains. A learnable controller could adaptively shift from exploration to exploitation as the model matures, potentially unlocking further efficiency gains.

#### 7.2.2 Expansion Beyond Verifiable Domains
DeepSearch is currently constrained to domains with **verifiable rewards** (mathematics), where a function $V(s)$ can definitively return $\{0, 1\}$ (Section 3.1).
*   **Future Direction:** Integrating **approximate verifiers** or **human-in-the-loop validation** for subjective tasks (e.g., creative writing, ethical reasoning). Recent work on reward models could be adapted to provide the dense feedback signals required for MCTS backpropagation in non-verifiable domains.
*   **Why It Matters:** This would generalize the training-time search paradigm to the vast majority of NLP tasks that lack ground-truth answers, moving beyond the "math-only" limitation identified in Section 6.2.

#### 7.2.3 Scaling to Larger Models
All results are reported for **1.5B parameter models** (Table 1). It remains an open question whether the benefits of training-time search scale to 7B, 70B, or larger models.
*   **Future Direction:** Investigating the **marginal utility of MCTS** at larger scales. Larger models may already discover correct paths via direct rollouts more frequently, potentially reducing the relative benefit of structured search. Conversely, larger models might benefit more from the fine-grained credit assignment provided by Tree-GRPO.
*   **Why It Matters:** If the efficiency gains hold at scale, DeepSearch could significantly reduce the cost of training frontier reasoning models. If gains diminish, the method may remain specialized for small-to-medium models.

#### 7.2.4 Inference-Training Alignment
DeepSearch integrates search into training, but the evaluation uses standard sampling (Pass@1 with $n=32$).
*   **Future Direction:** Measuring whether **training with search improves inference-time search capabilities**. Does a model trained with MCTS become better at performing MCTS during deployment?
*   **Why It Matters:** This would validate the hypothesis that training-time search internalizes reasoning strategies, leading to better test-time performance even when compute budgets are constrained.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

For practitioners, DeepSearch offers a specific value proposition: **higher accuracy on complex reasoning tasks with lower training costs**, at the expense of increased training complexity and latency per step.

*   **Scientific and Technical Domains:** Applications requiring high-precision reasoning with verifiable outputs (e.g., formal theorem proving, code generation with unit tests, scientific calculation) are the natural fit. The binary verification requirement aligns perfectly with these use cases.
*   **Resource-Constrained Environments:** Organizations limited by GPU budgets can use DeepSearch to achieve state-of-the-art performance without the cost of extended training runs. The 5.7× reduction in GPU hours (Table 2) is a decisive factor for smaller teams.
*   **Curriculum Learning Systems:** The adaptive replay buffer (Section 4.2) effectively creates a dynamic curriculum, focusing compute on the model's current "frontier of failure." This mechanism can be extracted and applied to other training pipelines where data difficulty varies widely.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For engineers considering adopting DeepSearch, the following guidelines summarize when to prefer this method over alternatives and how to configure key components based on the paper's findings.

#### 7.4.1 When to Use DeepSearch
*   **✅ Preferred:**
    *   **Verifiable Domains:** You have a reliable verification function (e.g., code execution, math solvers) to provide binary rewards.
    *   **Training Plateaus Observed:** Your current RLVR training shows diminishing returns after thousands of steps (Section 1).
    *   **Compute Budget Limited:** You need to maximize accuracy per GPU hour rather than minimize wall-clock time per iteration.
    *   **Small-to-Medium Models:** You are working in the 1B–3B parameter range where exploration benefits are proven.
*   **❌ Not Preferred:**
    *   **Non-Verifiable Tasks:** You lack a programmatic way to check correctness (e.g., open-ended dialogue).
    *   **Strict Latency Constraints:** You require minimal wall-clock time per training step. MCTS adds significant overhead (Appendix C shows GPU inference dominates, but tree construction adds complexity).
    *   **Very Large Models:** You are training 70B+ models where the inference cost per tree expansion may become prohibitive (unproven scaling).

#### 7.4.2 Key Configuration Parameters
Based on the ablation studies and appendix details, the following settings are critical for reproduction:

*   **Filtering Threshold:** Set $\delta = 25\%$ for the hard subset filtering (Section 4.1). This ensures MCTS is only applied to problems the model cannot yet solve reliably.
*   **Pass1@K Sampling:** Use $K = 4$ samples for the initial filtering check (Section 4.1). This balances evaluation cost with reliability.
*   **Frontier Selection Weights:** Start with $\lambda_1 = 0.4$ (Quality Potential) and $\lambda_3 = 0.01$ (Depth Bonus) using the square root depth function $D(d(s)) = \sqrt{d(s)/d_T}$ (Section 5.3). Set $\lambda_2 = 0$ (Uncertainty Bonus) unless exploration diversity is a specific concern, as it introduced variability in ablations (Table 3).
*   **Tree Depth:** Cap maximum depth at $d_T = 64$ levels, derived from the 16,384 token budget divided by 256 tokens per node (Appendix B.1).
*   **Training Objective:** Use **mean-only advantage normalization** ($\hat{A}_{j,k} = q(s_j) - \mu_t$) rather than standard deviation normalization to prevent instability (Section 5.4, Equation 15).

#### 7.4.3 Implementation Stack
*   **Framework:** The authors implement DeepSearch using the **veRL framework** (Appendix A.1). Practitioners should look for compatibility with this stack or ensure their RL framework supports custom trajectory weighting and tree-structured data.
*   **Base Model:** The results are initialized from **Nemotron-Research-Reasoning-Qwen-1.5B v2** (Section 5.1). Using a similarly instruction-tuned reasoning base is likely necessary to achieve comparable convergence.
*   **Hardware:** Experiments were conducted on **16× NVIDIA H100 GPUs** (Appendix A.1). While scalable, the memory requirements for storing search trees and replay buffers should be accounted for in cluster planning.

### 7.5 Summary of Impact

DeepSearch represents a pivot point in reasoning model development. It demonstrates that **systematic exploration** during training is a more potent lever for improvement than **prolonged optimization**. By providing a concrete framework for integrating MCTS into RLVR—with validated components like global frontier selection, entropy-guided negative mining, and adaptive replay buffering—it offers a blueprint for the next generation of efficient reasoning models.

The work invites the community to look beyond the "more compute" narrative and instead focus on **how** models learn. As the Limitations section (Page 26) notes, extending these principles to non-verifiable domains and larger scales remains open work. However, the core insight stands: **scaling training breadth through algorithmic innovation** is a viable and efficient path toward advanced reasoning capabilities.
