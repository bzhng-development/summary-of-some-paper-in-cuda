## 1. Executive Summary
This paper introduces Performers, Transformer architectures that estimate regular softmax attention with provable accuracy using only linear ($O(L)$) space and time complexity instead of the standard quadratic ($O(L^2)$) scaling, thereby solving the computational bottleneck for long input sequences. By leveraging a novel Fast Attention Via positive Orthogonal Random features (FAVOR+) mechanism, Performers achieve unbiased or nearly-unbiased estimation of the attention matrix without relying on sparsity or low-rank priors. The authors demonstrate competitive results on large-scale tasks including protein sequence modeling (TrEMBL), pixel-prediction (ImageNet64 with $L=12288$), and text modeling (PG-19), enabling applications like proteome-scale protein interaction prediction that are infeasible for regular Transformers.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Computational Bottleneck

Transformers have become the dominant architecture across machine learning domains — from natural language processing and neural machine translation to image generation and bioinformatics. However, all standard Transformer variants share a fundamental limitation: the attention mechanism scales **quadratically** with the input sequence length $L$. 

To understand why this matters, consider the regular dot-product attention mechanism (Equation 1 in the paper). Given query, key, and value matrices $Q, K, V \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}$, the attention output requires computing an attention matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times L}$ where:

$$A = \exp(QK^\top / \sqrt{d})$$

This matrix $A$ must be **stored explicitly** during computation, resulting in:
- **Time complexity:** $O(L^2 d)$ 
- **Space complexity:** $O(L^2 + Ld)$

For moderate sequence lengths (e.g., $L = 4096$), this becomes prohibitively expensive. For long sequences (e.g., $L = 12288$ for ImageNet64 pixel prediction, or $L = 8192$ for concatenated protein sequences), regular Transformers simply **run out of memory** even at batch size 1 per chip (Section 4.5). This quadratic scaling precludes Transformer usage in settings with limited computational resources and blocks applications requiring very long sequences.

### Why This Problem Matters

The importance of solving this bottleneck is both **theoretical** and **practical**:

**Real-World Impact:**
- **Bioinformatics:** Protein sequences can be extremely long, and modeling interactions across the proteome scale requires capturing dependencies across thousands of tokens. Existing methods require large evolutionary sequence alignments — a bottleneck for mammalian genomes (Section 6). The authors explicitly state this was "one of the main motivations of this work."
- **Document Processing:** Long documents, books, and scientific papers exceed typical Transformer length limits.
- **High-Resolution Images:** Treating images as sequences of pixels (as in ImageNet64) creates sequences with $L = 12288$, infeasible for regular Transformers.
- **Environmental Cost:** Regular Transformers require very large computational resources, translating to higher CO₂ emissions and energy consumption (Section 6 cites Strubell et al., 2019; You et al., 2020).

**Theoretical Significance:**
- There was no method capable of **provably accurate** approximation of regular softmax attention with linear complexity.
- Existing efficient methods either changed the attention mechanism fundamentally (losing compatibility) or lacked rigorous guarantees about approximation quality.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper identifies several categories of prior work attempting to address Transformer scalability. Each has meaningful shortcomings that Performers aim to overcome:

#### 1. Sparse Attention Methods

These approaches restrict which tokens can attend to which other tokens:
- **Local neighborhoods:** Parmar et al. (2018) restrict attention to nearby tokens
- **Sparsity patterns:** Child et al. (2019) use fixed sparse structures
- **Sliding windows:** Beltagy et al. (2020) limit attention to a window around each token
- **LSH-based clustering:** Kitaev et al. (2020) use locality-sensitive hashing to group similar tokens (Reformer)
- **Routing/clustering:** Roy et al. (2020) apply k-means to learn dynamic sparse attention regions

**Critical Limitation:** These methods "do not aim to approximate regular attention, but rather propose simpler and more tractable attention mechanisms" (Section 1). They incorporate structural priors (sparsity, locality) that may not match the actual dependency structure of the data. Furthermore, "there is a lack of rigorous guarantees for the representation power produced by such methods, and sometimes the validity of sparsity patterns can only be verified empirically through trial and error" (Section 1). Some require custom GPU operations (C++ CUDA kernels or TVMs) to implement efficiently.

#### 2. Low-Rank Kernel Substitutions

These methods replace softmax with kernels that admit efficient computation:
- Katharopoulos et al. (2020), Shen et al. (2018) use low-rank kernels substituting softmax

**Critical Limitation:** These "critically rely on kernels admitting explicit representations as dot-products of finite positive-feature vectors" (Section 1). They do not approximate the original softmax attention — they replace it with a different kernel entirely, losing backward compatibility with pretrained models.

#### 3. Memory Reduction Techniques

These approaches reduce memory without changing attention complexity:
- **Reversible residual layers:** Kitaev et al. (2020) allow one-time activation storage in training
- **Shared attention weights:** Xiao et al. (2019) reuse attention parameters across layers

**Critical Limitation:** "These constraints may impede application to long-sequence problems, where approximations of the attention mechanism are not sufficient" (Section 1). They address memory but not the fundamental $O(L^2)$ computation.

#### 4. Truncated Back-Propagation

- Dai et al. (2019) propagate gradients only within a localized window

**Critical Limitation:** "Unable to capture long-distance correlations since the gradients are only propagated inside a localized window" (Section 1). This defeats the purpose of attention for long-range dependency modeling.

#### 5. Biased Attention Estimation

- Wang et al. (2020) (Linformer) propose biased estimation of regular attention

**Critical Limitation:** Works "only in the non-causal setting and with large mean squared error" (Section 1). The bias and error limit accuracy and applicability.

### How Performers Position Themselves

The paper positions Performers as addressing a **specific gap** left by all prior approaches:

> "We introduce the first Transformer architectures, Performers, capable of provably accurate and practical estimation of regular (softmax) full-rank attention, but of only linear space and time complexity and not relying on any priors such as sparsity or low-rankness." (Section 1)

Key differentiators:

| Aspect | Prior Efficient Methods | Performers |
|--------|------------------------|------------|
| **Approximates softmax?** | No (replace with different mechanism) | Yes (provably accurate estimation) |
| **Theoretical guarantees** | Empirical or limited | Unbiased/nearly-unbiased, uniform convergence, low variance |
| **Requires sparsity priors?** | Yes | No |
| **Requires low-rank priors?** | Often | No |
| **Backward compatible?** | Generally no | Yes (via small fine-tuning) |
| **Works for causal & non-causal?** | Often restricted | Both (unidirectional and bidirectional) |

The paper emphasizes that Performers are "the first linear architectures fully compatible (via small amounts of fine-tuning) with regular Transformers" (Section 1). This backward compatibility is positioned as "a very important additional feature... particularly attractive for practitioners" (Section 6) — you can take a pretrained Transformer and convert it to a Performer without retraining from scratch.

Furthermore, the FAVOR+ mechanism enables something previously impossible: "accurately compare softmax with other kernels for the first time on large-scale tasks, beyond the reach of regular Transformers, and investigate optimal attention-kernels" (Section 1). This opens new research directions in understanding which attention kernels work best for different tasks.

In essence, Performers occupy a unique position: they maintain the **representational power** of regular Transformers (full-rank softmax attention) while achieving the **computational efficiency** of linear-time methods, backed by **theoretical guarantees** that prior efficient methods lacked.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

Performers are a drop-in replacement for the attention mechanism in standard Transformers that achieves linear-time computation while provably approximating the original softmax attention. The solution works by transforming the attention computation from an $O(L^2)$ matrix multiplication problem into an $O(L)$ sequence of feature map transformations using carefully designed random projections that preserve the mathematical properties of softmax.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The Performer architecture consists of three major computational components that replace only the attention module while keeping all other Transformer components identical. First, the **Query/Key Feature Mapper** takes the original query and key matrices ($Q, K \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}$) and projects them through a randomized feature map $\phi$ to produce transformed matrices ($Q', K' \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times r}$) where $r$ is the number of random features (typically $r \ll L$). Second, the **Linear Attention Core** computes the attention output through a reordered matrix multiplication sequence: instead of computing the $L \times L$ attention matrix explicitly, it first computes $(K')^\top V$ (an $r \times d$ matrix), then multiplies by $Q'$ to get the final output (an $L \times d$ matrix). Third, the **Normalization Estimator** computes the diagonal normalization matrix $\hat{D}$ using the same feature map approach to ensure the attention weights sum to one per token. For unidirectional (causal) attention, a fourth component called the **Prefix-Sum Accumulator** replaces the standard matrix multiplication with a cumulative sum operation that respects the causal mask without explicitly storing it.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first establish the mathematical foundation by reviewing how regular attention works and why it requires $O(L^2)$ computation, so you understand what we're approximating.
- We then introduce the generalized kernelizable attention framework that enables linear-time computation through feature maps, explaining the key insight that allows reordering of matrix multiplications.
- We explain why naive approaches using trigonometric random features fail catastrophically for softmax attention, which motivates the need for positive random features.
- We detail the Positive Random Features (PRF) mechanism that provides unbiased estimation with variance that decreases as attention values approach zero.
- We describe Orthogonal Random Features (ORF) that further reduce estimation variance by enforcing orthogonality among random samples.
- We conclude with the complete FAVOR+ algorithm, complexity analysis, and key hyperparameters needed for implementation.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a methods paper that introduces a novel attention approximation mechanism called FAVOR+ (Fast Attention Via positive Orthogonal Random features) which enables Transformers to achieve linear space and time complexity while maintaining provable accuracy guarantees for softmax attention estimation.

#### The Regular Attention Mechanism: What We're Approximating

To understand what Performers approximate, we must first examine the standard dot-product attention mechanism that appears in all Transformers. For an input sequence of $L$ tokens with hidden dimension $d$, the attention mechanism accepts three matrices: $Q, K, V \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}$, where each row represents a query, key, or value vector for a single token position. The bidirectional (non-causal) attention output is computed as:

$$\text{Att}_{\leftrightarrow}(Q, K, V) = D^{-1} A V$$

where $A \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times L}$ is the attention matrix defined as $A = \exp(QK^\top / \sqrt{d})$, and $D = \text{diag}(A \mathbf{1}_L)$ is a diagonal normalization matrix ensuring each row of attention weights sums to one (Equation 1 in the paper). The exponential function $\exp(\cdot)$ is applied elementwise to every entry of the $L \times L$ matrix $QK^\top / \sqrt{d}$. This formulation creates the computational bottleneck: computing $A$ requires $O(L^2 d)$ time because we must compute $L^2$ pairwise dot products between queries and keys, and storing $A$ explicitly requires $O(L^2)$ space. For unidirectional (causal) attention used in generative modeling, the attention matrix is masked to be lower-triangular: $\tilde{A} = \text{tril}(A)$, where $\text{tril}(\cdot)$ returns only the lower-triangular part including the diagonal (Equation 2).

#### The Key Insight: Generalized Kernelizable Attention

The breakthrough enabling linear-time attention comes from recognizing that the attention matrix can be expressed through a kernel function that admits a special mathematical structure. Instead of computing $A(i, j) = \exp(q_i^\top k_j / \sqrt{d})$ directly for all $L^2$ pairs, FAVOR+ uses a kernel function $\mathcal{K}: \mathbb{R}^d \times \mathbb{R}^d \rightarrow \mathbb{R}_+$ that can be written as an expected dot product of random feature maps:

$$\mathcal{K}(x, y) = \mathbb{E}[\phi(x)^\top \phi(y)]$$

where $\phi: \mathbb{R}^d \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^r_+$ is a randomized mapping from $d$-dimensional input vectors to $r$-dimensional non-negative feature vectors (Equation 3). This is the critical insight: if we can express the softmax kernel this way, we can approximate the attention computation without ever forming the $L \times L$ matrix. We construct transformed query and key matrices $Q', K' \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times r}$ where each row $i$ is given by $\phi(q_i^\top)^\top$ and $\phi(k_i^\top)^\top$ respectively. The approximate attention then becomes:

$$\widehat{\text{Att}}_{\leftrightarrow}(Q, K, V) = \hat{D}^{-1} (Q' ((K')^\top V))$$

where $\hat{D} = \text{diag}(Q' ((K')^\top \mathbf{1}_L))$ (Equation 4). The brackets indicate the order of computation: we first compute $(K')^\top V$ which is an $r \times d$ matrix, then multiply by $Q'$ to get an $L \times d$ output. This reordering is the computational trick that reduces complexity from $O(L^2 d)$ to $O(Lrd)$, since we never explicitly form the $L \times L$ intermediate matrix. The space complexity becomes $O(Lr + Ld + rd)$ instead of $O(L^2 + Ld)$, which is linear in $L$ when $r \ll L$ (Section 2.2, Figure 1).

#### Why Trigonometric Random Features Fail for Softmax

Before explaining what works, we must understand why the obvious approach fails. A natural choice for approximating the softmax kernel $\text{SM}(x, y) = \exp(x^\top y)$ comes from the relationship between softmax and Gaussian kernels. Since $\text{SM}(x, y) = \exp(\|x\|^2/2) \mathcal{K}_{\text{gauss}}(x, y) \exp(\|y\|^2/2)$, we can use trigonometric random features with $h(x) = \exp(\|x\|^2/2)$, $l=2$, $f_1 = \sin$, $f_2 = \cos$ to obtain an unbiased estimator called $\widehat{\text{SM}}^{\text{trig}}_m$ (Section 2.3). However, this approach produces **negative values** in the feature map dimensions because sine and cosine oscillate between -1 and 1. This creates a catastrophic problem for attention: the attention mechanism constructs convex combinations of value vectors with coefficients given by renormalized kernel scores, which requires all kernel scores to be **non-negative**. When trigonometric features produce negative estimates for kernel values near zero (which occurs for many low-relevance token pairs), the normalization matrix $D^{-1}$ can have negative diagonal values, leading to completely unstable training behavior.

The paper provides both empirical and theoretical evidence for this failure. Figure 2 (left) shows that for critical regions where the angle $\phi$ between input vectors is large (corresponding to small softmax-kernel values), the mean squared error (MSE) of trigonometric features becomes arbitrarily worse than positive features. Lemma 2 provides the mathematical proof: as $\text{SM}(x, y) \rightarrow 0$, we have $\text{MSE}(\widehat{\text{SM}}^{\text{trig}}_m(x, y)) \rightarrow \infty$ while $\text{MSE}(\widehat{\text{SM}}^{+}_m(x, y)) \rightarrow 0$ (Equation 8). This is the fundamental reason why "the robust random feature map mechanism for approximating regular softmax attention was never proposed" before this work (Section 2.3). The variance explodes precisely where we need accurate estimation most: for low-attention token pairs that should receive near-zero weight.

#### Positive Random Features (PRF): The R+ Component

The solution to the negativity problem comes from a novel random feature construction that guarantees non-negative outputs. Lemma 1 establishes that the softmax kernel admits a positive random feature map through the identity:

$$\text{SM}(x, y) = \mathbb{E}_{\omega \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_d)} \left[ \exp\left(\omega^\top x - \frac{\|x\|^2}{2}\right) \exp\left(\omega^\top y - \frac{\|y\|^2}{2}\right) \right]$$

where $\omega$ is sampled from a standard Gaussian distribution (Equation 7). This can be rewritten using hyperbolic cosine as $\Lambda \mathbb{E}_{\omega \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_d)} \cosh(\omega^\top z)$ where $z = x + y$ and $\Lambda = \exp(-(\|x\|^2 + \|y\|^2)/2)$. The key design choice is setting $h(x) = \exp(-\|x\|^2/2)$, $l=1$, $f_1 = \exp$ in the general feature map form (Equation 5), which produces the estimator $\widehat{\text{SM}}^{+}_m$. An alternative formulation uses $h(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \exp(-\|x\|^2/2)$ with $l=2$, $f_1(u) = \exp(u)$, $f_2(u) = \exp(-u)$ for further variance reduction, called $\widehat{\text{SM}}^{\text{hyp}+}_m$.

The critical advantage of positive features is that the variance **decreases** as the approximated kernel value approaches zero. From Lemma 2, we have $\text{MSE}(\widehat{\text{SM}}^{+}_m(x, y)) = \frac{1}{m} \exp(\|x+y\|^2) \text{SM}^2(x, y) (1 - \exp(-\|x+y\|^2))$, which clearly approaches 0 as $\text{SM}(x, y) \rightarrow 0$. This is the opposite behavior from trigonometric features and is essential for stable attention computation. Figure 2 (right upper corner) empirically demonstrates that in low softmax-kernel value regions, positive features achieve dramatically lower MSE than trigonometric features. The paper notes that this positive feature mechanism "may be of independent interest for scalable kernel methods" beyond the Transformer application (Abstract).

#### Orthogonal Random Features (ORF): The O Component

While positive features solve the negativity problem, we can further reduce estimation variance by changing how we sample the random vectors $\omega_1, \ldots, \omega_m$. Instead of sampling each $\omega_i$ independently from $\mathcal{N}(0, I_d)$, the ORF mechanism enforces that all $m$ samples are **exactly orthogonal** to each other: $\omega_i^\top \omega_j = 0$ for all $i \neq j$. This is achieved through the standard Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization procedure applied to $m$ initial Gaussian samples (Section 2.4). The orthogonality constraint maintains unbiasedness whenever the distribution $D$ is isotropic (which includes the Gaussian distribution used here), because the marginal distribution of each individual $\omega_i$ remains unchanged.

Theoretical analysis shows that orthogonality provides strictly better concentration bounds than independent sampling. Theorem 2 quantifies the MSE improvement: for any dimension $d > 0$ and $m \leq d$,

$$\text{MSE}(\widehat{\text{SM}}^{\text{ort}+}_m(x, y)) \leq \text{MSE}(\widehat{\text{SM}}^{+}_m(x, y)) - \frac{2(m-1)}{m(d+2)} \left( \text{SM}(x, y) - \exp\left(-\frac{\|x\|^2 + \|y\|^2}{2}\right) \right)^2$$

(Equation 10). This shows the MSE reduction is proportional to $(m-1)/(d+2)$, meaning orthogonality provides the most benefit when $m$ is large relative to $d$. Theorem 3 provides even stronger guarantees for the regularized softmax kernel $\text{SM}_{\text{REG}}$, showing "exponentially small bounds for probabilities of estimators' tails that are strictly better than for non-orthogonal variants for every $d > 0$" (Section 3). This is a novel theoretical contribution: previous ORF methods only showed asymptotic improvements for large $d$, but this work proves benefits for any dimensionality.

The regularized softmax kernel $\text{SM}_{\text{REG}}$ is obtained by replacing $\omega$ with $\sqrt{d} \omega / \|\omega\|$ in Equation 7, which changes the sampling distribution from $\mathcal{N}(0, I_d)$ to $\text{Unif}(\sqrt{d} S^{d-1})$, the uniform distribution on a sphere of radius $\sqrt{d}$ (Section 2.3). Theorem 1 shows this regularized kernel is a universal lower bound for the standard softmax kernel with ratio approaching 1 as $d$ increases: $\inf_{i,j} A_{\text{reg}}(i, j) / A(i, j) \geq 1 - 2d^{-1/3} + o(d^{-1/3})$ (Equation 9).

#### The Complete FAVOR+ Algorithm

The FAVOR+ mechanism combines Positive Random Features with Orthogonal Random Features to produce the final attention approximation. Algorithm 1 in Appendix B provides the complete pseudocode. For bidirectional attention, the algorithm proceeds as follows:

1. **Feature Map Computation:** Compute $Q'$ and $K'$ by applying the positive orthogonal random feature map $\phi$ to each row of $Q$ and $K$. The default configuration uses $m = 256$ random features (Appendix A.3), with $\text{ortho\_features} = \text{True}$ and $\text{ortho\_scaling} = 0.0$.

2. **Buffer Construction:** Form the augmented value matrix $C = [V \;\; \mathbf{1}_L] \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times (d+1)}$ by concatenating $V$ with an all-ones column for normalization computation.

3. **First Multiplication:** Compute $\text{Buf}_1 = (K')^\top C \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times (d+1)}$. This is the critical $O(Lmd)$ operation that aggregates information across all sequence positions without forming the $L \times L$ attention matrix.

4. **Second Multiplication:** Compute $\text{Buf}_2 = Q' \text{Buf}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times (d+1)}$. This produces the unnormalized attention output combined with normalization factors.

5. **Split and Normalize:** Split $\text{Buf}_2$ into $[\text{Buf}_3 \;\; \text{buf}_4]$ where $\text{Buf}_3 \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}$ contains the attention output and $\text{buf}_4 \in \mathbb{R}^L$ contains the normalization denominators. Return $\text{diag}(\text{buf}_4)^{-1} \text{Buf}_3$.

For unidirectional (causal) attention, the algorithm uses a **prefix-sum mechanism** instead of direct matrix multiplication (Appendix B.1, Figure 8). The goal is to compute $\text{tril}(Q' (K')^\top) C$ without explicitly constructing the lower-triangular mask. The algorithm maintains a running sum $\text{GPS}_{i,:,:} = \sum_{j=1}^i G_{j,:,:}$ where $G_{j,:,:} = K'_j C_j^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times (d+1)}$. At each position $i$, the output is computed as $\text{GPS}_{i,:,:} \times Q'_i$. An efficient parallel prefix-sum algorithm takes $O(L)$ total steps and $O(\log L)$ time when computed in parallel (citing Ladner & Fischer, 1980; Cormen et al., 2009).

#### Complexity Analysis and Hyperparameters

The time and space complexity of FAVOR+ depends critically on the relationship between sequence length $L$, hidden dimension $d$, and number of random features $m$. For bidirectional FAVOR+ with independent or regular orthogonal random features (R-ORFs), the space complexity is $O(md + Ld + mL)$ compared to $\Theta(L^2 + Ld)$ for standard attention (Appendix B.3). The time complexity is $O(Lmd)$ compared to $O(L^2 d)$ for the baseline. When $L \gg m$, this provides substantial savings: for example, with $L = 8192$ and $m = 256$, we achieve roughly $32\times$ reduction in the dominant term.

The R-ORF mechanism requires one-time $O(md^2)$ preprocessing for Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization, but this is negligible when $L \gg d$. Alternative ORF variants can reduce this cost: Hadamard ORFs (H-ORFs) encode the feature matrix in $O(m)$ space with $O(m \log d)$ computation time, while Givens ORFs (G-ORFs) use $O(m \log d)$ space (Appendix B.2). However, H/G-ORFs introduce small bias that tends to 0 as $d \rightarrow \infty$, whereas R-ORFs provide unbiased estimation.

Key hyperparameters from Appendix A.3 for approximate softmax attention:
- `renormalize_attention = True`: Apply the $D^{-1}$ normalization operator
- `numerical_stabilizer = 10^{-6}`: Add small constant to prevent division by zero
- `number_of_features = 256`: The value of $m$ (random feature dimensions)
- `ortho_features = True`: Use orthogonal rather than independent random samples
- `ortho_scaling = 0.0`: Scaling factor for orthogonal features

For generalized attention (Appendix A.4), the default kernel is ReLU with `kernel_epsilon = 10^{-3}`. The paper notes that "Performers are using the same training hyperparameters as Transformers, yet achieving competitive results - this shows that FAVOR can act as a simple drop-in without needing much tuning" (Appendix A.2).

#### Design Choices and Trade-offs

Several critical design decisions distinguish FAVOR+ from prior efficient attention methods. First, the choice of **positive features over trigonometric features** is non-negotiable for stable training: Section 4.3 and Appendix D.2 show that trigonometric features lead to "highly unstable" training on PG-19 and can even produce NaN values (Appendix D.3, Figure 15 right). Second, **feature redrawing** is essential for multi-layer training: Figure 5 (right) shows that without periodically redrawing random features, training plateaus at suboptimal perplexity, while redrawing allows the model to "correct itself" when unlucky feature samples occur (Appendix D.2, Figure 15 left). The paper notes this is "a cheap procedure, but can be further optimized" (Section 4.2).

Third, the **regularized softmax kernel** $\text{SM}_{\text{REG}}$ enables faster convergence than standard softmax approximation while maintaining accuracy (Figure 5 right, Section 4.3). Fourth, for protein modeling tasks, **generalized attention with ReLU kernel** actually outperforms softmax approximation (Figure 6), demonstrating that FAVOR+ enables exploration of optimal attention kernels beyond softmax on large-scale tasks. The default configuration uses generalized ReLU attention unless explicitly specified as "Performer-SOFTMAX" (Appendix A).

The number of random features $m$ allows explicit trade-off between computational cost and approximation accuracy. Theorem 4 shows that for uniform convergence with error $\epsilon$, we need $m = \Theta(\frac{d}{\delta^2} \log(\frac{4d^{3/4}R}{\delta}))$ where $\delta = \epsilon / (h^*)^2$ and $h^* = \exp(l^2/2)$ with $l = R d^{-1/4}$ (Section 3). Critically, $m$ depends on $d$ and the radius $R$ of the ball containing queries/keys, but **does not depend on sequence length $L$**. This means the approximation quality remains constant as we scale to longer sequences, which is essential for the method's utility. However, Appendix F.6 notes a limitation: "FAVOR cannot approximate hard attention on sequences of unlimited length with a fixed $m$" because query/key norms cannot grow unbounded while maintaining approximation quality.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the fundamental contributions that distinguish Performers from prior efficient attention methods. We distinguish between **incremental improvements** (building on existing techniques) and **fundamental innovations** (enabling capabilities previously impossible).

---

### 4.1 Positive Random Features for Softmax: A Theoretical Breakthrough

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental

**What Prior Work Could Not Do:**

Before this paper, no method existed for approximating softmax attention with random features that was both **unbiased** and **stable**. As explained in Section 3.4, trigonometric random features (the standard approach for kernel approximation since Rahimi & Recht, 2007) produce negative values that cause catastrophic instability when attention scores approach zero. The paper explicitly states this is "one of the main reasons why the robust random feature map mechanism for approximating regular softmax attention was never proposed" (Section 2.3).

**What Performers Introduce:**

Lemma 1 provides a novel mathematical identity showing that the softmax kernel admits a **positive** random feature representation through exponential functions rather than trigonometric functions. This is not a minor modification — it is a fundamentally different feature map construction that had not been discovered for softmax kernels before.

**Why This Matters:**

The significance extends beyond just making training stable. Lemma 2 proves that the Mean Squared Error (MSE) of positive features **approaches zero** as the softmax kernel value approaches zero, while trigonometric feature MSE **approaches infinity** in the same regime (Equation 8). This is critical because:

- Low-attention token pairs (those with small softmax values) are the most numerous in any sequence
- These pairs need accurate estimation to avoid corrupting the normalization matrix $D^{-1}$
- Prior methods either ignored this regime or accepted high variance as unavoidable

**Evidence of Impact:**

Figure 15 (right) in Appendix D.2 shows trigonometric features cause "very unstable training behaviors" that can produce NaN values, while positive features train smoothly. On PG-19, positive features with redrawing match regular Transformer perplexity, while trigonometric features plateau at significantly worse performance (Figure 5, Section 4.3).

**Assessment:**

This is a **fundamental innovation**, not an incremental improvement. It solves a theoretical obstacle that prevented random feature methods from being applied to softmax attention at all.

---

### 4.2 Orthogonal Random Features with Non-Asymptotic Guarantees

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental Theoretical Advance

**What Prior Work Could Not Do:**

Orthogonal Random Features (ORF) were known before this paper (Yu et al., 2016; Choromanski et al., 2017), but existing theoretical results only proved benefits **asymptotically for large dimensionality $d$**. Practitioners had no guarantee that ORF would help for the actual dimensions used in Transformers (typically $d = 512$ or $d = 1024$).

**What Performers Introduce:**

Theorem 2 and Theorem 3 provide the **first proofs** that orthogonal features reduce variance for **any dimensionality $d > 0$**, not just in the limit. Theorem 2 explicitly quantifies the MSE gap between orthogonal and independent features (Equation 10), showing the improvement is proportional to $(m-1)/(d+2)$. Theorem 3 provides "exponentially small bounds for probabilities of estimators' tails that are strictly better than for non-orthogonal variants for every $d > 0$" (Section 3).

**Why This Matters:**

This theoretical advance has direct practical implications:

- Practitioners can confidently use ORF without worrying that their embedding dimension is "too small" for the benefits to materialize
- The explicit MSE gap formula allows tuning $m$ (number of random features) based on desired accuracy
- The tail bounds guarantee that extreme estimation errors are exponentially unlikely, which is critical for stable training across many layers

**Evidence of Impact:**

Figure 4 (Section 4.2) empirically validates the theory: orthogonal features produce lower approximation error than independent (IID) features across all tested values of $m$. The gap is consistent and measurable, not just asymptotic.

**Assessment:**

This is a **fundamental theoretical advance** that strengthens the mathematical foundation of ORF methods. While ORF itself is not new, the non-asymptotic guarantees are novel and directly enable reliable use in Transformer architectures.

### 4.3 First Provable Linear-Time Softmax Attention Approximation

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental

**What Prior Work Could Not Do:**

As detailed in Section 2, prior efficient attention methods fall into two categories:

| Method Category | Approximates Softmax? | Theoretical Guarantees |
|-----------------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Sparse Attention (Reformer, Longformer) | No — replaces with sparse mechanism | Empirical only |
| Low-Rank Kernels (Linformer, Linear Transformer) | No — replaces with different kernel | Limited or biased |
| Memory Reduction (Reversible Layers) | Yes, but still $O(L^2)$ compute | N/A |

No prior method could claim to **provably approximate regular softmax attention** with **linear complexity** and **unbiased estimation** simultaneously.

**What Performers Introduce:**

Performers are "the first Transformer architectures... capable of provably accurate and practical estimation of regular (softmax) full-rank attention, but of only linear space and time complexity and not relying on any priors such as sparsity or low-rankness" (Section 1). The key theoretical guarantees are:

- **Unbiased or nearly-unbiased estimation** of the attention matrix (Abstract)
- **Uniform convergence** with error bounds independent of sequence length $L$ (Theorem 4)
- **Low estimation variance** with explicit MSE formulas (Lemma 2, Theorem 2)

**Why This Matters:**

This capability enables three things previously impossible:

1. **Direct comparison of softmax vs. alternative kernels** on large-scale tasks. Section 4.4 shows Performer-RELU outperforms softmax on protein modeling — a comparison that "regular Transformers" could not support at the required sequence lengths.

2. **Backward compatibility with pretrained models**. Since Performers approximate (rather than replace) softmax, pretrained Transformer weights can be transferred with only fine-tuning (Figure 5, Section 4.3). This is positioned as "a very important additional feature... particularly attractive for practitioners" (Section 6).

3. **Confidence in long-sequence extrapolation**. Theorem 4 proves approximation error does not grow with $L$, so performance on short sequences predicts performance on long sequences. Prior methods had no such guarantee.

**Evidence of Impact:**

- On ImageNet64 with $L = 12288$ (infeasible for regular Transformers), Performer/12-layers matches Reformer/24-layers performance (Figure 7, Section 4.5)
- On concatenated TrEMBL with $L = 8192$, regular Transformer "overloads memory even at a batch size of 1 per chip" while Performer trains at batch size 8 (Section 4.5)
- Performer achieves "nearly optimal speedup" approaching the theoretical limit where attention returns $V$ directly (Figure 3, Section 4.1)

**Assessment:**

This is a **fundamental innovation** that fills a gap no prior method addressed. It enables new research questions (optimal kernels, long-sequence modeling) that were previously blocked by computational constraints.

---

### 4.4 Generalized Attention Framework for Kernel Discovery

**Innovation Type:** Incremental Innovation with Significant Implications

**What Prior Work Could Not Do:**

Prior efficient attention methods were typically designed for **one specific kernel** (usually softmax or a fixed alternative). This made it impossible to systematically compare different attention kernels on the same large-scale tasks, because regular Transformers could not handle the required sequence lengths.

**What Performers Introduce:**

FAVOR+ works for **any kernelizable attention mechanism** where $\mathcal{K}(x, y) = \mathbb{E}[\phi(x)^\top \phi(y)]$ (Section 2.2). This "representational power is crucial to accurately compare softmax with other kernels for the first time on large-scale tasks, beyond the reach of regular Transformers, and investigate optimal attention-kernels" (Section 1).

**Why This Matters:**

This transforms attention kernel selection from an **architectural constraint** into a **tunable hyperparameter**. The paper demonstrates this by showing:

- **ReLU kernel outperforms softmax** on protein sequence modeling (Figure 6, Section 4.4)
- **Regularized softmax (SM$_{\text{REG}}$)** enables faster convergence than standard softmax approximation (Figure 5, Section 4.3)
- Different kernels may be optimal for different domains (text vs. proteins vs. images)

**Evidence of Impact:**

Appendix D.3 (Figures 16-17) shows extensive ablation studies across multiple kernels (sigmoid, exponential, ReLU, absolute, GeLU, cosine, tanh, identity) on TrEMBL. The authors note that "the effective batch size slightly affects the rankings" and they "by default use the generalized ReLU kernel with other default hyperparameters... as we observed that they are empirically optimal for large batch size runs" (Appendix D.3).

**Assessment:**

This is an **incremental innovation** built on the fundamental PRF+ORF contributions, but it has **significant implications** for future research. It opens a new research direction: systematic kernel discovery for attention mechanisms across different domains.

---

### 4.5 Practical Backward Compatibility Without Architectural Changes

**Innovation Type:** Incremental but Practically Significant

**What Prior Work Could Not Do:**

Most efficient Transformer variants require **training from scratch** with their specific architecture. Reformer, Longformer, Linformer, and others cannot directly use weights from pretrained BERT or GPT models because they fundamentally change the attention computation.

**What Performers Introduce:**

Because Performers **approximate** rather than **replace** softmax attention, pretrained Transformer weights can be transferred with only "small amounts of fine-tuning" (Abstract). Section 6 emphasizes: "Our Performer can be used on the top of a regular pre-trained Transformer as opposed to other Transformer variants."

**Why This Matters:**

This has three practical benefits:

1. **Reduced compute cost for adoption**. Organizations with existing pretrained models can convert to Performers without retraining from scratch.
2. **Fast inference deployment**. "Even if up-training is not required, FAVOR+ can still be used for fast inference with no loss of accuracy" (Section 6).
3. **Research continuity**. Findings from standard Transformer research remain applicable to Performers.

**Evidence of Impact:**

Figure 5 (left) shows a Performer initialized with pretrained Transformer weights starts at "0.07 accuracy" but "quickly recovers accuracy in a small fraction of the original number of gradient steps" on LM1B. Appendix D.1 (Figure 14) analyzes error propagation through layers, showing that approximation errors compound but remain manageable with fine-tuning.

**Assessment:**

This is an **incremental improvement** in terms of technical novelty (it follows from the approximation property), but it is **practically significant** for adoption. It lowers the barrier for practitioners to benefit from linear-time attention.

---

### Summary: Innovation Hierarchy

| Contribution | Innovation Type | Significance |
|--------------|-----------------|--------------|
| Positive Random Features for Softmax | Fundamental | Enables stable softmax approximation (previously impossible) |
| ORF with Non-Asymptotic Guarantees | Fundamental Theoretical | Proves benefits for any dimension, not just asymptotically |
| Provable Linear-Time Softmax Approximation | Fundamental | First method with rigorous guarantees for full-rank softmax |
| Generalized Attention Framework | Incremental with Impact | Enables kernel comparison and discovery at scale |
| Backward Compatibility | Incremental but Practical | Lowers adoption barrier for practitioners |

The **core innovations** (PRF, ORF guarantees, provable approximation) are what make Performers unique. The **practical innovations** (generalized framework, backward compatibility) amplify the impact by enabling real-world adoption and new research directions.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their theoretical claims through empirical evaluation. We analyze the experimental methodology, dissect the quantitative results with specific numbers, and critically assess whether the evidence supports the paper's core claims about linear-time softmax attention approximation.

---

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Datasets and Task Diversity

The authors test Performers across **three distinct domains** to demonstrate generalizability beyond any single application area:

| Domain | Dataset | Sequence Length (L) | Task Type | Significance |
|--------|---------|---------------------|-----------|--------------|
| **Protein Modeling** | TrEMBL (Jan 2019) | 1,024 (single), 8,192 (concatenated) | Next-token prediction (U & B) | Tests biological sequence understanding; L=8,192 infeasible for regular Transformers |
| **Text Modeling** | PG-19 | Variable (long-range) | Language modeling (U & B) | Challenging long-range text from Project Gutenberg books (pre-1919) |
| **Text Modeling** | LM1B | Standard | Language modeling (B) | Tests backward compatibility with pretrained models |
| **Image Modeling** | ImageNet64 | 12,288 | Pixel prediction (U) | Treats images as pixel sequences; L=12,288 exceeds regular Transformer capacity |

The TrEMBL dataset contains **139,394,261 protein sequences** (106,030,080 unique), representing approximately **99.5% of total sequences in UniProtKB** (Appendix C.1). For the long-sequence protein interaction task, sequences are concatenated to **L = 8,192** with end-of-sequence tokens separating individual proteins (Table 1, Appendix C.1).

#### Metrics

The paper uses three evaluation metrics depending on task type (Appendix A.1):

1. **Accuracy**: For unidirectional models, measured on next-token prediction averaged across all sequence positions. For bidirectional models, tokens are masked with **15% probability** (following BERT protocol) and accuracy is measured across masked positions.

2. **Perplexity**: Computed as average log-likelihood per token. For PG-19, perplexities are adjusted by the ratio of SentencePiece tokenization to original token count (multipliers: train=1.5634, valid=1.5487, test=1.5359) (Appendix A.1.1).

3. **Bits Per Dimension/Character (BPD/BPC)**: Calculated as loss divided by ln(2), used for image modeling tasks.

#### Baselines

The authors compare against multiple efficient Transformer variants, each with specific limitations:

| Baseline | Reference | Directional Constraint | Key Limitation |
|----------|-----------|----------------------|----------------|
| **Regular Transformer** | Vaswani et al. (2017) | Both | O(L²) complexity, memory overflow at large L |
| **Reformer** | Kitaev et al. (2020) | Unidirectional (U) only | LSH-based sparse attention, no softmax approximation |
| **Linformer** | Wang et al. (2020) | Bidirectional (B) only | Biased estimation, large MSE (Section 1) |
| **Linear Transformer** | Katharopoulos et al. (2020) | Both | Uses ELU+1 feature map, numerically unstable (Appendix D.4) |

For fairness, the authors note that their Linformer implementation uses **k = 600** projection dimensions, which is "more than twice than the default k = 256 from the paper, and also twice than our default m = 256 number of features" (Appendix A.6). They also use feature redrawing for Linformer to avoid "unlucky" projections.

#### Hardware and Training Setup

Experiments were conducted on **TPU-v2** and **V100 GPU** hardware with the following configurations:

- **36-layer protein experiments**: 16×16 TPU-v2 chips (8GB per chip) (Appendix A.2)
- **ImageNet64**: Varied TPU configurations (2×2, 4×4, 8×8, 16×16)
- **GPU memory tests**: V100 GPU with 16GB memory (Figure 3)
- **Default architecture**: (nheads, nlayers, dff, d) = (8, 6, 2048, 512) unless specified
- **Training hyperparameters**: 0.5 gradient clip, 0.1 weight decay, 0.1 dropout, 10⁻³ fixed learning rate with Adam (β₁ = 0.9, β₂ = 0.98, ε = 10⁻⁹) (Appendix A.2)

The authors emphasize that "Performers are using the same training hyperparameters as Transformers, yet achieving competitive results - this shows that FAVOR can act as a simple drop-in without needing much tuning" (Appendix A.2).

### 5.2 Computational Efficiency Results

#### Memory and Speed Scaling (Figure 3, Section 4.1)

Figure 3 presents the most direct evidence of Performer's linear complexity advantage. The authors measured forward and backward pass speed and maximum sequence length L allowed before out-of-memory errors on a **V100 GPU with 16GB**.

**Key Findings:**

- The Performer achieves **"nearly optimal speedup"** approaching the theoretical limit depicted by the "X"(OPT) line, which represents the maximum possible speedup when attention simply returns the V-matrix (identity function)
- The Performer reaches **L = 2¹⁵ = 32,768** as the maximum power-of-2 length allowed on the V100 GPU using regular dimensions (Appendix E, Figure 21)
- Regular Transformers produce **out-of-memory errors** at much lower sequence lengths (the exact threshold is not explicitly stated in the main text, but Figure 3 shows the Transformer curve terminating well before L = 32,768)
- The Performer demonstrates **sub-quadratic memory consumption** since the explicit O(L²) attention matrix is not stored

**Interpretation:**

This result directly validates the theoretical complexity claims. The "nearly optimal" characterization is significant: it means the attention computation overhead is minimal compared to the unavoidable cost of processing the value matrix V itself. For practitioners, this translates to the ability to train with **larger batch sizes** and **lower wall clock time per gradient step** for large L (Section 4.1).

#### Layer Scaling (Appendix E, Figure 20)

The authors also tested Performer scaling with varying numbers of layers:

- Performer can scale up to **20 layers** without computational issues
- The "Small" architecture variant (nheads, nlayers, dff, d) = (1, 6, 64, 64) was tested to isolate attention computation from feed-forward layer bottlenecks
- Even in the "Small" configuration where attention dominates computation, Performer maintains its efficiency advantage

### 5.3 Approximation Quality Results

#### Orthogonal vs. Independent Features (Figure 4, Section 4.2)

Figure 4 directly tests the theoretical claim that orthogonal random features reduce approximation error. The experiment used **L = 4,096, d = 16**, and varied the number of random samples m.

**Quantitative Results:**

- **Orthogonal features consistently produce lower MSE** than independent (IID) features across all tested values of m
- **Positive features produce lower MSE** than trigonometric sin/cos features
- Standard deviations shown across **15 samples** of appropriately normalized random matrix input data

**Interpretation:**

This empirically validates Theorem 2 (orthogonality reduces MSE) and Lemma 2 (positive features have better MSE properties than trigonometric). The fact that both improvements are measurable at practical parameter settings (not just asymptotically) supports the paper's claim of "non-asymptotic guarantees."

#### Softmax Approximation on Transformers (Figure 5, Section 4.3)

Figure 5 tests whether FAVOR+'s softmax approximation can maintain performance when integrated into full Transformer architectures. Two datasets were used:

**LM1B Dataset (Figure 5, left):**

- A pretrained Transformer's weights were transferred into the Performer
- Initial accuracy: **0.07** (dotted orange line)
- The Performer "quickly recovers accuracy in a small fraction of the original number of gradient steps"
- This demonstrates **backward compatibility** even with trigonometric features (though positive features are recommended)

**PG-19 Dataset (Figure 5, right):**

- **Positive (POS) softmax features with redrawing** become "crucial for achieving performance matching regular Transformers"
- **Trigonometric (TRIG) softmax approximation becomes highly unstable** (full curve in Appendix D.2)
- **Linformer** (even with redrawn projections) and **positive features without redrawing** "plateau at the same perplexity"
- **SMREG (regularized softmax)** allows "faster convergence" than standard softmax approximation

**Critical Detail from Appendix D.2 (Figure 15):**

The appendix reveals important nuance about feature redrawing:

- Without redrawing, an "unlucky" set of random features may cause training degradation (Seed 1 curve early-stops)
- A "lucky" set may cause no issue (Seed 2 curve performs well)
- **Redrawing allows training to correct itself** when unlucky samples occur (shown at the black vertical line in Figure 15, left)

**Interpretation:**

This result is conditional: positive features alone are insufficient for large-scale tasks. **Feature redrawing is essential** for matching regular Transformer performance on challenging datasets like PG-19. This is a practical requirement that practitioners must account for.

The protein modeling experiments provide the most comprehensive comparison across multiple attention mechanisms.

**36-Layer Model Results (Figure 6):**

#### Linear Transformer Instability (Appendix D.4, Figure 18)

The authors tested the Linear Transformer (Katharopoulos et al., 2020) under identical conditions to ensure fair comparison:

- Used the same training hyperparameters as Performer-RELU
- Used ReLU nonlinearity for MLPs (instead of GeLU) to prevent confounding results
- Ran **3 seeds** for the unidirectional 36-ProGen setting

**Results:**

- **All 3 seeds produced exploding gradients very early**, stopping the training run (Figure 18, left)
- In the bidirectional setting, Linear Transformer "produced an exploding gradient in the middle of training, near 125K steps" (Figure 18, right)
- "Exploding gradients can be evidenced by the sharp drop in train accuracy right before a NaN error"

**Interpretation:**

This is a significant negative result for a competing method. The authors attribute this to numerical instability in the Linear Transformer's feature map (ELU+1). This strengthens Performer's positioning as a **stable** alternative, though it should be noted that the Linear Transformer authors may use different stabilization techniques in their official implementation.

#### ImageNet64 Pixel Prediction (Figure 7, Section 4.5)

This experiment tests Performer at sequence lengths **infeasible for regular Transformers**.

**Setup:**

- **L = 12,288** (64×64×3 pixels flattened)
- All models used standard (nheads, dff, d) = (8, 2048, 512) but varying nlayers
- Regular Transformer cannot run at this length (memory overflow)

**Results:**

| Model | Layers | Performance Comparison |
|-------|--------|----------------------|
| **Performer** | 6 | Matches Reformer/12-layers |
| **Performer** | 12 | Matches Reformer/24-layers |
| **Reformer** | 12 | Baseline for Performer/6 |
| **Reformer** | 24 | Baseline for Performer/12 |

**Key Finding:**

"Performer/6-layers matches the Reformer/12-layers, while the Performer/12-layers matches the Reformer/24-layers." This suggests Performer achieves **approximately 2× efficiency** in terms of layers needed for equivalent performance.

**Additional Observation:**

"Depending on hardware (TPU or GPU), we also found that the Performer can be **2× faster** than the Reformer via Jax optimizations for the (U) setting" (Section 4.5).

#### Concatenated Protein Sequences (Figure 7, Section 4.5)

This experiment tests protein interaction modeling at **L = 8,192**.

**Setup:**

- Protein sequences concatenated to L = 8,192 from TrEMBL
- Regular Transformer "overloads memory even at a batch size of 1 per chip, by a wide margin"
- Baseline Transformer reduced to (nheads, nlayers, dff, d) = (8, {1, 2, 3}, 256, 256)
- Performer trains at batch size 8 per chip using standard (8, 6, 2048, 512) architecture

**Results:**

- **Smaller Transformer (nlayer = 3)**: "Quickly bounded at ≈19%" accuracy
- **Performer**: "Able to train continuously to ≈24%" accuracy

**Interpretation:**

This demonstrates Performer's ability to handle sequence lengths that force competitors to use **severely reduced architectures**. The 5 percentage point accuracy gap (24% vs 19%) is substantial for this task, and it's achieved while using a **much larger model** (standard vs. reduced architecture) at **8× higher batch size**.

### 5.5 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

#### Kernel Comparison (Appendix D.3, Figures 16-17)

The authors conducted extensive ablation studies across multiple kernel functions on TrEMBL with L = 512.

**Kernels Tested:**

- Sigmoid, Exponential, ReLU, Absolute, GeLU, Cosine (original softmax approximation), Tanh, Identity

**Key Findings:**

- Some kernels produced **NaN values** causing runs to stop early (visible in Figure 16's log-scale plot)
- **ReLU kernel** was observed to be "empirically optimal for large batch size runs (i.e. 8×8 or 16×16 TPU's)" (Appendix D.3)
- **Effective batch size affects kernel rankings**: difference observed between 2×2 and 4×4 TPU runs
- **Renormalization** (applying D⁻¹ operator) was tested on/off; authors "noticed that disabling it does not necessarily hurt accuracy"

**Default Configuration:**

Based on these ablations, the default Performer configuration uses **generalized ReLU attention** unless explicitly specified as "Performer-SOFTMAX" (Appendix A).

#### Error Propagation Through Layers (Appendix D.1, Figure 14)

This study addresses a critical concern: do small approximation errors compound across multiple Transformer layers?

**Finding:**

"Even if the approximation of the attention mechanism is tight, small errors can easily propagate throughout multiple Transformer layers (e.g. MLPs, multiple heads)" (Section 4.3).

Figure 14 shows output approximation errors between a vanilla Transformer and Performer (with orthogonal features) for varying numbers of layers. The error **increases with depth**, which explains why:

- Pretrained Transformer weights cannot be immediately used for Performer inference without fine-tuning
- "Very tight approximations may sometimes be needed" for deep models (Section 4.3)

**Implication:**

This is a **limitation** of the approach. While Performers are backward compatible, they require fine-tuning to compensate for error propagation. The authors are transparent about this requirement.

#### Trigonometric Feature Instability (Appendix D.2, Figure 15 Right)

The appendix provides the "full view of the unstable training curve when using Trigonometric softmax."

**Observation:**

- Trigonometric features cause "very unstable training behaviors"
- Can produce **NaN values** during training (Appendix D.3 mentions NaN issues with certain kernels)
- This validates the theoretical analysis in Lemma 2 showing MSE → ∞ as softmax values → 0

**Implication:**

This is not merely a theoretical concern — it manifests as training failures in practice. Practitioners **must use positive features** for stable training.

### 5.7 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

#### Strengths

1. **Comprehensive Domain Coverage**: Testing across proteins, text, and images demonstrates generalizability beyond any single application area.

2. **Direct Complexity Validation**: Figure 3 provides clear evidence of linear vs. quadratic scaling, with specific sequence length limits (L = 32,768 achievable).

3. **Ablation Rigor**: The kernel comparison (Appendix D.3) and feature type comparison (Figure 4) isolate the contribution of each FAVOR+ component.

4. **Transparency About Limitations**: The authors openly report:
   - Trigonometric feature instability (Figure 15, Appendix D.2)
   - Error propagation requiring fine-tuning (Figure 14, Appendix D.1)
   - Linear Transformer comparison showing numerical instability (Figure 18)

5. **Fair Baseline Configuration**: The Linformer implementation uses stronger hyperparameters (k = 600 vs. default 256) than the original paper, reducing the risk of unfair comparison.

#### Weaknesses and Open Questions

1. **PG-19 Perplexity Numbers Not Explicit**: While Figure 5 shows curves, the exact perplexity values for Performer vs. Transformer on PG-19 are not provided in tabular form. The claim of "matching" performance is visual rather than quantitative.

2. **ImageNet64 BPD Values**: Figure 7 shows curves but does not provide final BPD numbers for direct comparison. The claim "Performer/12-layers matches Reformer/24-layers" is qualitative.

3. **Limited Comparison to Some Baselines**: Sparse attention methods like Longformer (Beltagy et al., 2020) and Big Bird are mentioned in Appendix D.5 but not directly compared in the main experiments.

4. **Feature Redrawing Overhead Not Quantified**: The paper states redrawing is "a cheap procedure" (Section 4.2) but does not provide timing data on the computational cost of periodic feature regeneration.

5. **Maximum L for Regular Transformer Not Specified**: Figure 3 shows the Transformer curve terminating before L = 32,768, but the exact failure point is not stated, making it difficult to quantify the improvement factor.

6. **OOD Performance Degradation**: Table 2 shows Performer (generalized) underperforms Transformer on OOD data for unidirectional modeling (18.44% vs. 19.70% accuracy). This suggests potential generalization concerns that are not discussed.

#### Conditional Results and Trade-offs

The experimental evidence reveals several **conditional requirements** for Performer success:

| Condition | Requirement | Consequence if Not Met |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Feature Type** | Must use positive (not trigonometric) features | Training instability, NaN errors (Figure 15) |
| **Feature Redrawing** | Must periodically redraw features for large datasets | Performance plateaus at suboptimal perplexity (Figure 5) |
| **Fine-tuning** | Required when transferring from pretrained Transformer | Initial accuracy ~0.07, requires recovery steps (Figure 5) |
| **Kernel Selection** | ReLU may outperform softmax for some domains | Softmax not universally optimal (Figure 6) |
| **Sequence Length** | Query/key norms must remain bounded | Approximation quality degrades if norms grow unbounded (Appendix F.6) |

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the constraints, assumptions, and open questions surrounding the Performer architecture. While the paper presents compelling evidence for FAVOR+'s effectiveness, several important limitations merit careful consideration before adoption. We distinguish between **fundamental constraints** (inherent to the mathematical approach), **practical trade-offs** (engineering decisions with costs), and **open questions** (areas requiring further research).

---

### 6.1 Fundamental Mathematical Constraints

#### Bounded Query/Key Norms Required

The approximation guarantees provided by FAVOR+ depend critically on the assumption that query and key vectors remain within a bounded region of the embedding space. Theorem 4 (Section 3) explicitly states that the number of random features $m$ required for uniform convergence depends on $R$, the radius of the ball containing all queries and keys:

$$m = \Theta\left(\frac{d}{\delta^2} \log\left(\frac{4d^{3/4}R}{\delta}\right)\right)$$

where $\delta = \epsilon / (h^*)^2$ and $h^* = \exp(l^2/2)$ with $l = R d^{-1/4}$.

**Critical Implication:** Appendix F.6 acknowledges this limitation directly:

> "FAVOR cannot approximate hard attention on sequences of unlimited length with a fixed $m$. When the sequence length increases, even the standard attention requires longer and longer vectors to make the softmax concentrated enough to pick single elements."

This means that if query/key norms grow unbounded during training (which can happen with certain initialization schemes or optimization dynamics), the approximation quality will degrade unless $m$ is increased proportionally. The paper does not provide explicit bounds on how large $R$ can grow in practice before approximation quality becomes unacceptable.

**Practical Impact:** Practitioners must monitor query/key norm distributions during training. If norms drift significantly, they may need to:
- Increase $m$ (number of random features), raising computational cost
- Add normalization constraints to keep embeddings bounded
- Accept degraded approximation quality for very long sequences

#### Orthogonal Features Require $m \leq d$

Section 2.4 states: "The ORF mechanism requires $m \leq d$, but this will be the case in all our experiments." This constraint arises because orthogonal random features are constructed via Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization, which requires at least as many dimensions as samples.

**Critical Implication:** For models with small embedding dimensions (e.g., $d = 64$ or $d = 128$), the maximum number of random features is limited. This constrains the approximation accuracy achievable through orthogonality alone.

**Workaround:** Appendix B.2 notes that "if we need $m > d$, ORFs still can be used locally within each $d \times d$ block of $W$" (citing Yu et al., 2016). However, this adds implementation complexity and the paper does not evaluate whether block-wise orthogonality provides the same variance reduction benefits as full orthogonality.

#### Regularized Softmax is a Lower Bound, Not Exact

Theorem 1 (Section 3) proves that the regularized softmax kernel $\text{SM}_{\text{REG}}$ is a **universal lower bound** for the standard softmax kernel:

$$\sup_{i,j} \frac{A_{\text{reg}}(i, j)}{A(i, j)} \leq 1$$

Furthermore, the lower bound ratio approaches 1 only asymptotically:

$$\inf_{i,j} \frac{A_{\text{reg}}(i, j)}{A(i, j)} \geq 1 - 2d^{-1/3} + o(d^{-1/3})$$

**Critical Implication:** When using $\text{SM}_{\text{REG}}$ for faster convergence (as recommended in Figure 5, Section 4.3), there is **inherent approximation bias** that cannot be eliminated by increasing $m$. For $d = 512$, this bound suggests potential underestimation of up to approximately $2 \times 512^{-1/3} \approx 25\%$ in the worst case, though the average case is likely much better.

**Trade-off:** Practitioners choosing $\text{SM}_{\text{REG}}$ for faster training must accept this systematic bias. The paper does not quantify how this bias affects downstream task performance across different domains.

---

### 6.2 Practical Engineering Trade-offs

#### Feature Redrawing Overhead Not Quantified

Section 4.2 states that "random samples should be periodically redrawn" to improve approximation across multiple Transformer layers, and calls this "a cheap procedure, but can be further optimized" (referencing Appendix B.2). Appendix D.2 (Figure 15, left) demonstrates that without redrawing, training can plateau at suboptimal perplexity due to "unlucky" random feature samples.

**Critical Gap:** The paper does **not** provide:
- Recommended redrawing frequency (every step? every epoch?)
- Computational cost of redrawing in wall-clock time
- Memory overhead for storing multiple feature sets
- Whether redrawing affects reproducibility (different random seeds produce different results)

**Practical Impact:** Without guidance on redrawing frequency, practitioners must tune this hyperparameter empirically. Too frequent redrawing wastes computation; too infrequent risks training instability. The claim that redrawing is "cheap" is not substantiated with timing data.

#### Fine-Tuning Required for Pretrained Model Transfer

Section 4.3 and Appendix D.1 (Figure 14) demonstrate that approximation errors **compound across layers**, requiring fine-tuning when transferring weights from a pretrained Transformer to a Performer.

**Quantitative Evidence:** Figure 5 (left) shows that a Performer initialized with pretrained Transformer weights starts at only **0.07 accuracy** on LM1B before recovering through fine-tuning. Appendix D.1 states: "error propagation due to non-attention components of the Transformer is one of the primary reasons that pretrained Transformer weights cannot be immediately used for inference on the corresponding Performer."

**Critical Implication:** While the paper emphasizes backward compatibility as a key advantage (Section 6), this compatibility is **conditional**:
- Fine-tuning is **required**, not optional
- The amount of fine-tuning needed scales with model depth (Figure 14 shows error increasing with layers)
- For very deep models, extensive fine-tuning may approach the cost of training from scratch

**Trade-off:** Organizations with large pretrained models can convert to Performers, but must budget compute resources for the fine-tuning phase. This is still cheaper than full retraining, but not the "drop-in replacement" that the abstract might suggest.

#### R-ORF Preprocessing Cost

Appendix B.2 and B.3 detail the computational cost of different ORF variants:

| ORF Variant | Preprocessing Cost | Space Complexity | Bias |
|-------------|-------------------|------------------|------|
| **R-ORF (Regular)** | $O(md^2)$ one-time | $O(md)$ | Unbiased |
| **H-ORF (Hadamard)** | None | $O(m)$ | Small bias ($\rightarrow 0$ as $d \rightarrow \infty$) |
| **G-ORF (Givens)** | None | $O(m \log d)$ | Small bias ($\rightarrow 0$ as $d \rightarrow \infty$) |

**Critical Implication:** The unbiased R-ORF variant (used in most experiments) requires $O(md^2)$ preprocessing. For large $d$ (e.g., $d = 1024$) and $m = 256$, this is approximately $256 \times 1024^2 \approx 2.7 \times 10^8$ operations — non-negligible for models that are frequently reinitialized.

**Trade-off:** Practitioners must choose between:
- **R-ORF**: Unbiased but higher preprocessing cost
- **H/G-ORF**: Lower cost but introduces bias (though the paper claims this bias "tends to 0 with $d \rightarrow \infty$")

The paper does not provide empirical comparison of these variants on downstream tasks, so the practical impact of H/G-ORF bias is unknown.

#### Kernel Selection Becomes a New Hyperparameter

Section 4.4 and Appendix D.3 demonstrate that different attention kernels perform differently across domains:
- **ReLU kernel** outperforms softmax on protein modeling (Figure 6)
- **Softmax** remains competitive on text and image tasks
- Some kernels (e.g., cosine/trigonometric) produce **NaN values** during training (Appendix D.3, Figure 16)

**Critical Implication:** FAVOR+'s generalized attention framework transforms kernel selection from an **architectural constraint** into a **tunable hyperparameter**. While this enables optimization, it also adds complexity:

- Practitioners must now select a kernel function (ReLU, softmax, exponential, etc.)
- Kernel performance may vary with batch size (Appendix D.3 notes "effective batch size slightly affects the rankings")
- No theoretical guidance exists for which kernel to choose for a given domain

**Trade-off:** The flexibility to explore kernels comes at the cost of additional hyperparameter tuning. The paper's default configuration uses **generalized ReLU attention** (Appendix A.4), but this may not be optimal for all tasks.

---

### 6.3 Scalability and Resource Constraints

#### Maximum Sequence Length Not Infinite

While Performers achieve linear complexity, there are still **practical limits** on maximum sequence length:

**Evidence from Experiments:**
- Figure 3 shows Performer reaches $L = 2^{15} = 32,768$ on a V100 GPU with 16GB (Appendix E)
- This is the "maximum possible power of 2 length allowed" — suggesting hardware memory still constrains very long sequences
- The $O(Lmd)$ time complexity means computation still grows linearly with $L$, which can be prohibitive for extremely long sequences (e.g., $L > 100,000$)

**Critical Implication:** Performers enable **much longer** sequences than regular Transformers, but not **arbitrarily long** sequences. For applications requiring millions of tokens (e.g., full-book modeling, genome-scale analysis), additional techniques beyond FAVOR+ may be needed.

#### Memory Complexity Still Depends on $m$ and $d$

Appendix B.3 provides the space complexity for bidirectional FAVOR+:

$$O(md + Ld + mL)$$

**Critical Implication:** While this is linear in $L$, the $mL$ term can become significant for very long sequences. For example:
- $L = 32,768$, $m = 256$: $mL \approx 8.4 \times 10^6$ elements
- At 4 bytes per element (float32): ~32 MB just for this term
- For $L = 100,000$: ~95 MB

This is manageable for current hardware, but the linear growth means memory will eventually become a bottleneck for extreme sequence lengths.

#### TPU/GPU Optimization Dependencies

Section 4.5 notes: "Depending on hardware (TPU or GPU), we also found that the Performer can be 2× faster than the Reformer via Jax optimizations for the (U) setting."

**Critical Implication:** Performer's efficiency gains are **partially dependent** on hardware-specific optimizations (e.g., Jax JIT compilation, TPU matrix multiplication kernels). Performance on other hardware platforms (e.g., CPUs, different GPU architectures) is not evaluated.

**Open Question:** How portable are the performance gains across different deep learning frameworks (PyTorch, TensorFlow, Jax) and hardware platforms?

---

### 6.4 Empirical Gaps and Open Questions

#### Out-of-Distribution Generalization Concerns

Table 2 (Appendix C.3) reveals a notable performance gap on out-of-distribution (OOD) protein sequences:

| Model | OOD Accuracy (UNI) | OOD Accuracy (BID) |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Transformer** | 19.70% | 25.07% |
| **Performer (generalized)** | 18.44% | 24.10% |
| **Performer (softmax)** | N/A | 23.48% |

**Critical Observation:** Performer (generalized) **underperforms** the regular Transformer on OOD data for unidirectional modeling (18.44% vs. 19.70%). The paper does not discuss or analyze this gap.

**Open Questions:**
- Does approximation error reduce model robustness to distribution shift?
- Is this specific to protein sequences, or does it generalize to other domains?
- Can this gap be closed with more random features ($m$) or different kernels?

#### Limited Direct Comparison to Some Baselines

Appendix D.5 references the Long Range Arena (LRA) benchmark (Tay et al., 2021) and states Performers achieve "the largest LRA score among all tested scalable Transformers methods." However:

- These results are **from the LRA paper itself**, not new experiments by the Performer authors
- Some efficient attention methods (Longformer, Big Bird, Sparse Transformer) are mentioned but **not directly compared** in the main experiments
- The Linformer comparison uses a **stronger configuration** ($k = 600$ vs. default $k = 256$), which could be seen as either fair (giving baseline every advantage) or unfair (not comparing to optimally tuned baseline)

**Open Question:** How does Performer compare to Longformer or Big Bird on identical tasks with identical hyperparameter tuning budgets?

#### Feature Redrawing and Reproducibility

The requirement for periodic feature redrawing (Section 4.2, Appendix D.2) introduces **non-determinism** into training:

- Different random feature samples produce different gradients
- Results may vary between runs even with the same random seed for other components
- The paper does not specify whether feature redrawing seeds are controlled or truly random

**Open Question:** How much does feature redrawing affect result reproducibility? Should practitioners fix the redrawing seed for reproducibility, or allow true randomness for better averaging?

#### Numerical Stability Edge Cases

Appendix D.3 (Figures 16-17) shows that certain kernel functions produce **NaN values** during training, causing runs to stop early. While the authors identify ReLU as stable, this raises questions:

- What numerical properties make a kernel stable vs. unstable?
- Are there edge cases where even ReLU kernel could produce NaN (e.g., very large inputs)?
- The default `numerical_stabilizer = 10^{-6}` (Appendix A.3) suggests division-by-zero is a concern — under what conditions does this trigger?

**Open Question:** What are the numerical stability boundaries for FAVOR+? Are there input distributions or training regimes where instability is likely?

### 6.5 Summary: Limitations Hierarchy

| Limitation Category | Severity | Mitigation |
|--------------------|----------|------------|
| **Bounded query/key norms** | High (fundamental) | Monitor norms; increase $m$ if needed |
| **Feature redrawing overhead** | Medium (practical) | Tune frequency empirically; optimize implementation |
| **Fine-tuning for weight transfer** | Medium (practical) | Budget compute for fine-tuning phase |
| **OOD generalization gap** | Medium (empirical) | Requires further investigation; may be domain-specific |
| **$m \leq d$ for ORF** | Low (workaround exists) | Use block-wise orthogonality for $m > d$ |
| **Kernel selection complexity** | Low (flexibility trade-off) | Start with ReLU default; tune if needed |
| **Hardware optimization dependencies** | Low (portability) | Test on target hardware before deployment |

### 6.6 Fair Assessment

The limitations identified above should be weighed against the **substantial advantages** Performers provide:

**What Performers Enable That Was Previously Impossible:**
- Linear-time softmax attention with theoretical guarantees
- Sequence lengths up to $L = 32,768+$ on commodity hardware
- Direct comparison of attention kernels at scale
- Backward compatibility with pretrained models (with fine-tuning)

**What Performers Do Not Solve:**
- Arbitrarily long sequences (still linear growth in compute/memory)
- Elimination of all hyperparameter tuning (kernel selection, $m$, redrawing frequency)
- Guaranteed OOD generalization parity with regular Transformers
- Framework/hardware-independent performance guarantees

**Bottom Line for Practitioners:**

Performers represent a **significant advance** in efficient attention mechanisms, particularly for applications requiring sequence lengths beyond $L = 4,096$. However, they are not a universal panacea. Successful adoption requires:

1. **Understanding the approximation trade-offs**: Positive features are mandatory; trigonometric features will fail.
2. **Budgeting for fine-tuning**: Pretrained model transfer requires additional training steps.
3. **Monitoring approximation quality**: Track query/key norms and consider increasing $m$ if performance degrades at long sequences.
4. **Domain-specific kernel tuning**: ReLU may outperform softmax for some tasks (e.g., proteins), but this requires empirical validation.

The paper is **transparent about most limitations** (e.g., fine-tuning requirements, feature redrawing necessity), which strengthens confidence in the reported results. The main gaps are in **quantifying overhead costs** (redrawing time, preprocessing cost) and **exploring failure modes** (OOD generalization, numerical stability boundaries). These are natural directions for follow-up research rather than fundamental flaws in the approach.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how Performers reshape the efficient attention landscape, identifies concrete research directions enabled by the FAVOR+ framework, and provides practical guidance for practitioners deciding when to adopt this approach. We distinguish between **immediate impacts** (changes observable now) and **long-term implications** (research trajectories opened for the field).

---

### 7.1 Landscape Shifts: How Performers Change Efficient Attention Research

#### From Structural Priors to Approximation Guarantees

Before Performers, efficient attention research was dominated by **structural prior assumptions**:

| Pre-Performer Approach | Core Assumption | Limitation |
|-----------------------|-----------------|------------|
| Sparse Attention (Child et al., 2019) | Attention is inherently sparse | May miss long-range dependencies |
| LSH-based (Kitaev et al., 2020) | Similar tokens cluster together | Hash collisions lose information |
| Sliding Window (Beltagy et al., 2020) | Local context dominates | Cannot capture global dependencies |
| Low-Rank (Wang et al., 2020) | Attention matrix is low-rank | Biased estimation with large MSE (Section 1) |

Performers introduce a **paradigm shift**: instead of assuming structure in the attention pattern, they provide **provable approximation guarantees** for full-rank softmax attention. As stated in Section 1:

> "We introduce the first Transformer architectures, Performers, capable of provably accurate and practical estimation of regular (softmax) full-rank attention, but of only linear space and time complexity and not relying on any priors such as sparsity or low-rankness."

**Immediate Impact:** This shifts the research question from "What structure can we assume?" to "How accurately can we approximate without assumptions?" This is fundamentally different because it preserves the **representational capacity** of standard Transformers while achieving efficiency.

#### Kernel Selection Becomes a Research Variable

Section 4.4 and Appendix D.3 demonstrate that FAVOR+ enables systematic comparison of attention kernels at sequence lengths previously infeasible. The paper shows **ReLU kernel outperforms softmax** on protein modeling (Figure 6), while softmax remains competitive on text and images.

**Long-term Implication:** Attention kernel selection transforms from an **architectural constraint** into a **tunable hyperparameter**. This opens a new research direction: **domain-specific kernel discovery**. Future work could:
- Develop automated kernel search methods for different domains
- Theoretically characterize which kernels suit which data distributions
- Create kernel ensembles that adapt during training

As the authors note in Section 1, this representational power is "crucial to accurately compare softmax with other kernels for the first time on large-scale tasks, beyond the reach of regular Transformers, and investigate optimal attention-kernels."

#### Theoretical Rigor Becomes Expected

Prior efficient methods often lacked rigorous guarantees. Section 1 states: "there is a lack of rigorous guarantees for the representation power produced by such methods, and sometimes the validity of sparsity patterns can only be verified empirically through trial and error."

Performers establish a new standard with:
- **Unbiased or nearly-unbiased estimation** (Abstract)
- **Uniform convergence bounds** independent of sequence length $L$ (Theorem 4, Section 3)
- **Explicit MSE formulas** for different feature types (Lemma 2, Equation 8)
- **Non-asymptotic ORF guarantees** for any dimension $d > 0$ (Theorem 2, Section 3)

**Field-wide Impact:** Future efficient attention papers will likely be expected to provide similar theoretical guarantees, raising the bar for methodological rigor in the field.

### 7.2 Follow-up Research Directions

#### Theoretical Extensions

Several theoretical questions remain open and warrant investigation:

**1. Tighter Convergence Bounds**

Theorem 4 provides uniform convergence bounds, but the dependence on $R$ (query/key norm radius) suggests approximation quality degrades if norms grow unbounded. Appendix F.6 acknowledges:

> "FAVOR cannot approximate hard attention on sequences of unlimited length with a fixed $m$."

**Research Direction:** Develop adaptive $m$ selection strategies that scale with observed query/key norms during training, or prove norm bounds for common optimization procedures.

**2. Optimal Feature Count $m$**

The default configuration uses $m = 256$ random features (Appendix A.3), but Theorem 4 shows $m = \Theta(d \log d)$ suffices for uniform convergence. The exact relationship between $m$, task difficulty, and final performance is not fully characterized.

**Research Direction:** Develop theory or empirical rules for selecting $m$ based on:
- Task complexity (e.g., classification vs. generation)
- Sequence length distribution
- Model depth (since errors compound across layers per Appendix D.1, Figure 14)

**3. Generalized Attention Kernel Theory**

While the paper demonstrates ReLU outperforms softmax on proteins (Figure 6), there is no theoretical framework for **predicting** which kernel suits which domain.

**Research Direction:** Characterize kernel properties (e.g., smoothness, boundedness, positivity) that correlate with performance on different data types (text, images, sequences, graphs).

#### Empirical Extensions

**1. Comprehensive Benchmarking**

Appendix D.5 references Long Range Arena (LRA) results but notes these are from Tay et al. (2021), not new experiments. Direct comparison to all efficient methods on identical tasks is incomplete.

**Research Direction:** Systematic benchmark across:
- All major efficient attention methods (Performer, Reformer, Longformer, Big Bird, Linear Transformer)
- Multiple sequence length regimes ($L = 1K, 4K, 16K, 64K+$)
- Diverse domains (NLP, vision, biology, audio, time series)

**2. Feature Redrawing Optimization**

Section 4.2 states redrawing is "a cheap procedure, but can be further optimized" (referencing Appendix B.2). However, no quantitative analysis of redrawing frequency vs. performance trade-offs is provided.

**Research Direction:** Empirical study of:
- Optimal redrawing frequency (per step? per epoch? adaptive?)
- Computational overhead in wall-clock time
- Impact on reproducibility and result variance

**3. Very Deep Model Behavior**

Appendix D.1 (Figure 14) shows approximation errors compound across layers, requiring fine-tuning for pretrained model transfer. The scaling behavior for models with 100+ layers is unexplored.

**Research Direction:** Investigate whether error accumulation limits maximum viable depth for Performers, or whether techniques like residual connections or layer normalization mitigate compounding errors.

#### Architectural Extensions

**1. Hybrid Attention Mechanisms**

Section 1 notes: "in principle, FAVOR+ can also be combined with other techniques, such as reversible layers (Kitaev et al., 2020) or cluster-based attention (Roy et al., 2020)."

**Research Direction:** Explore combinations such as:
- Performer + Reversible Layers for memory efficiency
- Performer + Sparse Attention for hybrid local/global modeling
- Performer + Memory Networks for infinite context

**2. Cross-Modal Applications**

Section 6 mentions FAVOR+ applicability beyond Transformers: "hierarchical attention networks (HANS), graph attention networks, image processing, and reinforcement learning/robotics."

**Research Direction:** Adapt FAVOR+ to:
- **Graph Attention Networks** (Velickovic et al., 2018): Handle large graphs with $O(N^2)$ attention
- **Vision Transformers**: Enable higher-resolution image processing
- **Multi-modal Models**: Scale cross-attention between modalities (e.g., image-text)

**3. Causal Modeling Improvements**

The unidirectional case uses prefix-sum computation (Appendix B.1, Figure 8), which has $O(L)$ complexity but may have different constant factors than bidirectional attention.

**Research Direction:** Optimize causal attention specifically for autoregressive generation, potentially leveraging the structure of sequential decoding.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

#### When to Prefer Performer Over Alternatives

Based on the experimental evidence and limitations identified, here is concrete guidance for practitioners:

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| **$L &lt; 2,048$** | Regular Transformer | Quadratic cost is manageable; no approximation needed |
| **$L = 2,048 - 8,192$** | **Performer** | Sweet spot for linear-time benefits; strong empirical results |
| **$L > 8,192$** | **Performer** or Longformer | Performer for full-rank attention; Longformer if sparsity is acceptable |
| **Pretrained model available** | Performer with fine-tuning | Backward compatibility (Section 4.3, Figure 5) |
| **Protein/biological sequences** | Performer with **ReLU kernel** | Figure 6 shows ReLU outperforms softmax |
| **Text generation (causal)** | Performer with positive features + redrawing | Section 4.3 shows trigonometric features fail |
| **Memory-constrained deployment** | Performer with H-ORF or G-ORF | Appendix B.2 shows lower space complexity |
| **Maximum accuracy required** | Performer with R-ORF + high $m$ | Unbiased estimation (Appendix B.2) |

#### Critical Configuration Requirements

Based on experimental failures documented in the paper, certain configurations are **mandatory** for stable training:

**Must-Have Settings (from Appendix A.3-A.4):**
```
renormalize_attention = True      # Apply D⁻¹ normalization
numerical_stabilizer = 1e-6       # Prevent division by zero
number_of_features = 256          # Default m value
ortho_features = True             # Use orthogonal (not IID) features
```

**Feature Type Selection:**
- **Positive features:** Required for stable training (Section 4.3, Appendix D.2)
- **Trigonometric features:** Will cause NaN errors (Appendix D.3, Figure 16)
- **Feature redrawing:** Required for large datasets like PG-19 (Figure 5, Appendix D.2)

**Kernel Selection:**
- **Default:** Generalized ReLU attention (Appendix A.4)
- **Softmax approximation:** Use when backward compatibility with pretrained models is needed
- **SM_REG:** Use for faster convergence (Figure 5, Section 4.3)

#### Known Failure Modes to Monitor

Based on limitations identified in Section 6 and experimental results:

| Failure Mode | Symptoms | Mitigation |
|--------------|----------|------------|
| **Query/key norm growth** | Approximation quality degrades at long $L$ | Monitor norms; increase $m$ if needed |
| **Insufficient redrawing** | Training plateaus at suboptimal perplexity | Increase redrawing frequency |
| **Wrong feature type** | NaN errors, training instability | Switch to positive features immediately |
| **OOD generalization gap** | Performance drop on distribution shift (Table 2) | Consider regular Transformer for critical OOD tasks |
| **Deep model error accumulation** | Fine-tuning required for weight transfer (Figure 14) | Budget compute for fine-tuning phase |

#### Reproducibility Checklist

For researchers attempting to reproduce or build upon this work:

- [ ] **Use positive random features** (not trigonometric) — Appendix D.2 shows trigonometric fails
- [ ] **Enable orthogonal features** — Figure 4 shows consistent MSE improvement
- [ ] **Implement feature redrawing** — Section 4.2 shows it's necessary for large datasets
- [ ] **Monitor query/key norms** — Appendix F.6 warns about unbounded norm issues
- [ ] **Test multiple kernels** — Appendix D.3 shows kernel performance varies by domain
- [ ] **Fine-tune after weight transfer** — Appendix D.1 shows errors compound across layers
- [ ] **Use default hyperparameters from Appendix A** — Authors note these are empirically optimal

The paper provides:

- **Protein LM code:** `github.com/google-research/google-research/tree/master/protein_lm` (footnote, page 1)

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision: Where This Leads

#### Democratizing Long-Sequence Modeling

By reducing sequence length constraints from $O(L^2)$ to $O(L)$, Performers make long-sequence modeling accessible to:
- **Researchers with limited compute:** No need for massive GPU clusters
- **Production deployments:** Lower inference costs enable real-time applications
- **New domains:** Fields previously blocked by sequence length (genomics, legal, scientific literature) can now apply Transformers

#### Enabling New Model Classes

The generalized attention framework opens possibilities beyond standard Transformers:

**1. Infinite-Context Models**

Combined with memory mechanisms, FAVOR+ could enable models that process streaming data with theoretically unbounded context, limited only by available computation time rather than memory.

**2. Multi-Scale Attention**

Different attention kernels could operate at different scales simultaneously (local ReLU, global softmax), potentially capturing both fine-grained and coarse-grained dependencies more effectively than single-kernel approaches.

**3. Adaptive Attention**

The kernel selection flexibility could enable models that **learn** which attention kernel to use for different inputs or layers, rather than fixing it architecturally.

#### Broader Methodological Impact

Beyond attention mechanisms, the FAVOR+ approach demonstrates a general principle: **random feature approximation with theoretical guarantees** can replace expensive exact computations in neural networks. This could extend to:

- **Kernel methods** beyond attention (Section 2.3 notes FAVOR+ "may be of independent interest for scalable kernel methods")
- **Similarity search** in large-scale retrieval systems
- **Graph neural networks** with expensive pairwise computations

As the authors state in Section 6:

> "We believe that our results can shape research on efficient Transformers architectures, guiding the field towards methods with strong mathematical foundations."

Performers occupy a **unique position** in the efficient attention landscape:

| Dimension | Performer | Sparse Methods | Low-Rank Methods |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|------------------|

- Sequence lengths beyond $L = 4,096$

- Studying attention mechanisms without computational constraints

- Building on rigorous theoretical guarantees rather than empirical heuristics

The work represents not just an incremental improvement, but a **foundational shift** in how we think about efficient attention: from imposing structural constraints to providing approximation guarantees. This shift enables both immediate practical benefits and long-term research directions that were previously blocked by computational limitations.
