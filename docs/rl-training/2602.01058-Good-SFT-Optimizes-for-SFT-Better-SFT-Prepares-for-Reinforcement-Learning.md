## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the distribution mismatch between offline Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and online Reinforcement Learning (RL) by introducing PEAR (Policy Evaluation–inspired Algorithm for Offline Learning Loss Reweighting), which uses importance sampling to reweight SFT loss based on target policy likelihoods. Experiments across Qwen2.5/3 and DeepSeek-distilled models demonstrate that PEAR-initialized checkpoints consistently outperform canonical SFT after identical RL training, achieving up to a 14.6% pass@8 gain on AIME-2025 and 40% absolute accuracy improvement on SynLogic games. These results prove that optimizing SFT in isolation is often counterproductive for final RL performance, establishing PEAR as a critical method for designing offline stages with downstream RL in mind.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of this work, we must first establish the standard operational paradigm for post-training reasoning language models (LLMs) and identify the specific assumption that this paper challenges.

### The Standard SFT $\rightarrow$ RL Pipeline
Modern reasoning models typically undergo a two-stage post-training process:
1.  **Offline Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT):** The model is trained on a static dataset of prompt-response pairs `$\mathcal{D} = \{(x, y)\}$` generated by a behavior policy `$\pi_\beta$` (often a stronger model or rule-based system). The objective is usually standard negative log-likelihood (NLL), minimizing the error between the model's predictions and the ground truth tokens.
2.  **Online Reinforcement Learning (RL):** The SFT checkpoint initializes an online phase where the model generates its own rollouts (trajectories) and updates its policy `$\pi_\theta$` based on verified rewards (e.g., correct math answers).

The prevailing assumption in this pipeline is **monotonicity**: that a checkpoint with higher offline SFT accuracy will serve as a better initialization for the subsequent RL stage. Consequently, most prior research optimizes SFT objectives in isolation, targeting metrics like offline pass@1 or reduced forgetting, implicitly assuming these gains will translate to the final RL performance.

### The Core Problem: Offline Performance $\neq$ Online Performance
This paper identifies a critical flaw in the monotonicity assumption. The authors demonstrate that optimizing for offline SFT performance alone can be misleading, and in some cases, counterproductive for the final RL outcome.

*   **Ranking Reversals:** As visualized in **Figure 1**, there is no consistent correlation between offline metrics (e.g., Offline Pass@1) and online performance (Online Pass@1 after RL). In several instances across 19 models (including Qwen3-1.7B and 4B), objectives that achieve the highest offline scores result in the *worst* post-RL performance. For example, the `TopLogP` objective may dominate offline scores but underperform even the standard SFT baseline after identical RL training.
*   **Metric Disconnect:** This disconnect persists even when using more robust offline descriptors like offline pass@K (with large `$K$`) or majority voting accuracy. The ranking of methods is not preserved when transitioning from the offline static evaluation to the online interactive setting (**Section 2.1**).

### Why This Mismatch Occurs: Behavior vs. Target Policy
The root cause of this failure is a **distribution mismatch** between the data used in SFT and the data encountered during RL. This is a known challenge in offline-to-online reinforcement learning, but it is often overlooked in LLM post-training.

*   **Behavior Policy (`$\pi_\beta$`):** During SFT, the model learns from trajectories sampled from a fixed behavior policy (the data generator).
*   **Target Policy (`$\pi_\theta$`):** During online RL, the model learns from rollouts generated by its own evolving policy.

**Section 2.2** explains that standard SFT applies uniform token-level supervision under prefixes induced by `$\pi_\beta$`. However, in autoregressive generation, small early mismatches compound over long horizons. If `$\pi_\beta$` over-represents continuations that are effectively "dead-ends" under the target policy `$\pi_\theta$`, uniformly training on all logged tokens reinforces transitions that the RL phase will rarely revisit.
*   **Illustrative Example:** **Figure 2** sketches this intuition. If the behavior policy frequently generates a sequence `$A \rightarrow B \rightarrow C$` (high probability under `$\pi_\beta$`), but the target policy finds this continuation implausible (low probability under `$\pi_\theta$`), standard SFT forces the model to associate `$A$` with an unlikely path. During online RL, once the model generates `$A$`, it will not follow with `$B$` and `$C$`, rendering the offline learning signal for token `$A$` noisy or misleading.

### Limitations of Prior SFT Objectives
Existing methods attempt to improve SFT but fail to address this specific offline-to-online transition issue. **Table 1** categorizes common objectives that this paper compares against:
*   **KL-Regularization:** Adds a penalty `$\beta \text{KL}(\pi_\theta \parallel \pi_{\text{ref}})$` to prevent drift from a reference model. While this controls divergence, it does not correct for the mismatch between the data distribution and the target policy's future trajectories.
*   **Probability-Based Reweighting:** Methods like `TopP`, `BottomP`, and `TopLogP` (from Li et al., 2025a) reweight tokens based on their individual probabilities (e.g., focusing only on high-confidence tokens).
*   **Token-Adaptive Loss Reweighting (TALR):** Adjusts weights based on token difficulty (loss magnitude).

The fundamental shortcoming of these approaches is that they are **myopic**. They evaluate tokens based on immediate likelihood or loss magnitude without considering whether the *continuation* of that token is compatible with the target policy. As noted in **Section 4.3**, single-step weighting (up-weighting actions plausible under `$\pi_\theta$` at time `$t$` only) is less effective because what matters for RL readiness is whether the logged successful continuation is compatible with the current policy over the *remaining horizon*.

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself not as another method to maximize offline SFT accuracy, but as a bridge to optimize the **initialization quality for RL**.
*   **Shift in Objective:** The goal is redefined from "strong offline accuracy" to "an initialization that facilitates improvement under online RL."
*   **Theoretical Lens:** The authors adopt an **Off-Policy Evaluation (OPE)** lens (**Section 2.3**). In classical RL, OPE corrects for behavior-target shift using likelihood ratios (importance sampling). This paper applies that logic to the SFT stage: reweighting the offline loss based on how likely the target policy is to generate the same continuation relative to the behavior policy.
*   **Practical Impact:** By correcting this mismatch during the cheap offline stage, the method reduces the burden on the expensive online RL phase. This is critical because online RL involves rolling out the model on the training set and verifying answers, which can take minutes to hours per step (**Appendix A**). A better initialization means fewer RL steps are needed to reach peak performance, saving significant compute resources.

In summary, the motivation stems from the empirical observation that **Offline $\neq$ Online** (**Figure 1**), caused by a **behavior-target occupancy mismatch** (**Section 2.2**). Prior work optimizes for the wrong metric (offline accuracy), whereas this work proposes aligning the offline objective with the downstream RL distribution using importance sampling.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a **loss reweighting method** for supervised fine-tuning that modifies how much each token contributes to the training objective based on its compatibility with the target RL policy. The solution takes the form of **importance weights** computed from likelihood ratios between the target policy and the behavior (data-generating) policy, applied at different granularities (sequence, token, or block level) to correct the offline-to-online distribution mismatch.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

PEAR is a **training-time modification** to standard supervised fine-tuning that reweights each token's loss contribution based on how likely the model's future policy would generate that same continuation. It solves the problem that standard SFT treats all logged tokens equally, even when some tokens lead to trajectories the model will never revisit during online RL, by down-weighting those mismatched tokens and up-weighting tokens whose continuations remain plausible under the target policy.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The PEAR system operates as a **preprocessing and loss modification layer** inserted into the standard SFT training pipeline. The architecture consists of four major components working in sequence:

1.  **Behavior Policy Logger (`$\pi_\beta$`)**: This is the data-generating system (often a stronger model or rule-based generator) that produces the offline dataset `$\mathcal{D} = \{(x, y)\}$`. Its responsibility is to provide the logged trajectories with known token probabilities `$\pi_\beta(y_t | x, y_{<t})$` for every position `$t$` in every sequence.

2.  **Target Policy Evaluator (`$\pi_\theta$`)**: This is the model being trained, which computes its own token probabilities `$\pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t})$` on the offline data during the reweighting computation phase. Its responsibility is to provide the likelihood ratios that determine how compatible each logged token is with the current policy state.

3.  **Importance Weight Calculator**: This component computes the likelihood ratios `$\Delta_t = \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t})}{\pi_\beta(y_t | x, y_{<t})}$` and aggregates them according to one of three strategies (sequence-level, token-level, or block-level). Its responsibility is to produce the weight `$\hat{G}_t$` for each token position, applying numerical stabilization and clipping as specified in **Algorithm 1**.

4.  **Weighted Loss Computer**: This component applies the computed weights to the base loss function (either NLL or KL-divergence), computing `$\mathcal{L}_{\text{PEAR}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \sum_{t=1}^T \text{sg}[\hat{G}_t] \cdot \ell_\theta(x, y_{<t}, y_t) \right]$`. Its responsibility is to perform the actual parameter updates using the reweighted loss, with gradients stopped through the weights themselves.

The information flow proceeds as follows: First, the behavior policy generates and logs the offline dataset with per-token probabilities. Second, the target policy evaluates this data to compute likelihood ratios. Third, the weight calculator aggregates these ratios into stable importance weights. Fourth, the weighted loss computer uses these weights to modify the training objective before backpropagation.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Objective and Mathematical Foundation**

PEAR modifies the standard supervised fine-tuning objective by introducing per-token importance weights while keeping the underlying loss function unchanged. The standard SFT objective minimizes negative log-likelihood uniformly across all tokens: `$\mathcal{L}_{\text{SFT}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \sum_{t=1}^T -\log \pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t}) \right]$`. PEAR transforms this into a weighted expectation: `$\mathcal{L}_{\text{PEAR}}(\theta) \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \sum_{t=1}^T \text{sg}[\hat{G}_t] \cdot \ell_\theta(x, y_{<t}, y_t) \right]$`, where `$\ell_\theta$` is the base per-token loss (either NLL or forward KL), `$\hat{G}_t$` is the numerically-stabilized importance weight for position `$t$`, and `$\text{sg}[\cdot]$` denotes stop-gradient (treating weights as fixed coefficients during backpropagation, not as differentiable paths).

The stop-gradient operation is a critical design choice that prevents the weights from becoming part of the optimization graph. If the weights were differentiable, the model could manipulate its own importance weights during training, creating unstable feedback loops. By treating `$\hat{G}_t$` as a fixed coefficient computed before the gradient step, PEAR ensures that the reweighting reflects the policy's state at the beginning of each training batch, not a moving target.

**Sequence-Level Weighting (Simplest Variant)**

The sequence-level variant applies a single importance weight to all tokens within a trajectory, computed as the product of per-token likelihood ratios across the entire sequence. For each token position `$t$`, we define the instantaneous likelihood ratio as `$\Delta_t \triangleq \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t})}{\pi_\beta(y_t | x, y_{<t})}$`, which measures how much more (or less) likely the target policy is to generate token `$y_t$` compared to the behavior policy, conditioned on the same prefix. The sequence-level importance ratio is then `$w_{1:T} \triangleq \frac{\pi_\theta(y | x)}{\pi_\beta(y | x)} = \prod_{t=1}^T \Delta_t$`, representing the relative likelihood of the entire trajectory under the target policy versus the behavior policy.

This weighting scheme allows us to estimate the expected loss under the target policy's distribution using data from the behavior policy: `$\mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | x)} [\ell_\theta(x, y)] = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_\beta(\cdot | x)} [w_{1:T} \cdot \ell_\theta(x, y)]$`. In practice, we assign `$G_t \triangleq w_{1:T}$` for all `$t = 1, 2, ..., T$`, meaning every token in the trajectory receives the same global importance weight. Despite its simplicity, **Section 4.3** shows this variant can yield strong performance, demonstrating that even coarse-grained correction of the behavior-target mismatch provides value over uniform weighting.

The theoretical justification comes from **Off-Policy Evaluation (OPE)** theory (**Section 2.3**). Classical OPE corrects for behavior-target shift using the change-of-measure identity: `$\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta}[f(\tau)] = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\beta} \left[ \frac{\pi_\theta(\tau)}{\pi_\beta(\tau)} f(\tau) \right]$`. PEAR applies this identity to the SFT loss function, treating the loss as the function `$f(\tau)$` to be reweighted. This ensures that trajectories more likely under the target policy contribute more to the gradient, while trajectories the target policy would rarely generate contribute less.

**Token-Level Weighting Based on Continuation (Default Variant)**

The token-level variant, denoted as `$\text{PEAR}_{B=1}$` in experiments, applies different weights to different token positions based on how plausible the *remaining continuation* is under the target policy. The key insight is that a sequence may become "unlikely" because of specific implausible regions, and tokens leading into those regions should be down-weighted more than tokens in compatible regions. For a token `$y_t$`, we evaluate whether the continuation from the dataset `$y_{>t}$` remains plausible under the model conditioned on taking `$y_t$`.

The token-level importance weight is computed as `$G_t = \gamma^{T-t} \prod_{j=t+1}^T \Delta_j$`, where `$\gamma \in (0, 1]$` is a discount factor that controls variance in long horizons. This formula computes the **suffix importance ratio**—the product of likelihood ratios for all tokens *after* position `$t$`, not including `$t$` itself. The intuition is that the weight for token `$t$` should reflect how likely the target policy is to generate the *future* tokens given that `$t$` was taken, not just how likely `$t$` itself is.

**Figure 3(a)** illustrates this mechanism visually. The weight for each token depends on the product of ratios for all subsequent tokens in the sequence. This creates a gradient of importance: tokens near the beginning of a sequence have weights determined by long suffix products, while tokens near the end have weights determined by short suffix products. The discount factor `$\gamma$` (set to `$0.999$` in experiments per **Section 4.2**) prevents the weights from becoming too extreme on very long sequences by exponentially decaying the influence of distant future tokens.

This approach directly addresses the myopia problem identified in **Section 4.3**. Concurrent works propose action-level stabilization where the weight depends only on the prefix and current action: `$w(x, y_{<t}, y_t) = \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t})}{\pi_\beta(y_t | x, y_{<t})}$`. This single-step weighting is less effective because it up-weights actions that the target policy finds plausible at time `$t$` without considering whether those actions lead to successful continuations. **Table 3** shows that single-step weighting achieves lower average accuracy across math benchmarks compared to `$\text{PEAR}_{B=1}$`, with negative gains on some model sizes (e.g., -5% on Qwen3-1.7B, -6% on Qwen3-8B).

The suffix-based weighting captures long-term credit assignment: what matters for online RL readiness is whether the logged successful continuation is compatible with the current policy over the *remaining horizon*, not just at the current decision point. This aligns with classical RL theory on off-policy policy gradient with state distribution correction (**Liu et al., 2019; Nachum et al., 2019**).

**Block-Level Weighting for Stability**

The block-level variant partitions the sequence into contiguous blocks and applies the same weight to all tokens within each block, trading granularity for numerical stability. A product over long horizons inevitably introduces large variance (**Bossens & Thomas, 2024; Liu et al., 2018, 2020**), especially when likelihood ratios compound multiplicatively. To reduce the effective length of the multiplicative importance weight, we partition positions `$\{1, ..., T\}$` into `$K = \lceil T/B \rceil$` contiguous blocks `$\{I_k\}_{k=1}^K$`, each of length at most `$B$`.

For each block `$k$`, we define `$e_k \triangleq \max I_k$` as the last index of block `$k$`. The product of token-level ratios within the block is `$\Delta^{\text{blk}}_k \triangleq \prod_{t \in I_k} \Delta_t$`. The importance ratio of the suffix *after* block `$k$` is `$S_k \triangleq \prod_{j=e_k+1}^T \Delta_j$`, which can equivalently be computed block-wise as `$S_k = \prod_{m=k+1}^K \Delta^{\text{blk}}_m$`. Every token in block `$k$` receives the same discounted continuation weight: `$G_t = G^{\text{blk}}_k \triangleq \gamma^{T-e_k} S_k$` for all `$t \in I_k$`.

**Figure 3(b)** illustrates this block-wise aggregation. When `$B = 1$`, we recover the token-level PEAR introduced in **Section 3.4**. When `$B = T$`, we recover the sequence-level weighting from **Section 3.3**. The block size `$B$` is a hyperparameter that controls the trade-off: smaller blocks provide finer-grained correction but higher variance, while larger blocks provide more stable weights but coarser correction. **Figure 6** shows experimental comparisons across different block sizes, with `$B=1$` (token-level) and `$B=4$` both outperforming standard SFT, though token-level generally achieves the highest pass@1 and pass@8 scores.

**Algorithm 1** provides the complete computational procedure for both uniform (sequence-level) and suffix (token/block-level) modes. The algorithm first partitions the sequence into blocks, then computes clipped log-ratios `$\delta_t \leftarrow \text{clip}(\log \pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t}) - \log \pi_\beta(y_t | x, y_{<t}), \ell_\Delta, u_\Delta)$` for numerical stability. For suffix mode, it performs a single backward scan from `$k = K$` downto `$1$`, accumulating future log-ratios in variable `$u$` and computing weights `$\hat{G}_{e_k} \leftarrow \text{clip}(\exp((T - e_k) \log \gamma + u), G_{\min}, G_{\max})$`. This backward scan is computationally efficient, requiring only one pass through the sequence to compute all suffix weights.

**Optional Negative Example Handling**

When the dataset `$\mathcal{D}$` contains verified failures (negative trajectories), PEAR can optionally add a repulsive term that discourages imitating those trajectories in a policy-consistent way. Let `$\mathcal{D}^- = \{(x, y^-)\}$` denote the set of negative examples (e.g., responses that lead to incorrect answers). We compute sequence-level weights `$\hat{G}^-_t$` on these negative trajectories using the same importance sampling procedure, then apply a repulsive loss: `$\mathcal{L}_{\text{neg}}(\theta) \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{(x,y^-) \sim \mathcal{D}^-} \left[ -\lambda \cdot \text{sg}[\hat{G}^-_t] \sum_{t=1}^T \ell_\theta(x, y^-_{<t}, y^-_t) \right]$`.

The negative sign means we perform **gradient ascent** on this term, pushing the model away from the negative response with a trajectory-level weight. The hyperparameter `$\lambda$` controls the strength of this repulsive signal. This approach avoids token-level or suffix-level signed ratio products on negative data, which can be particularly unstable on long horizons. **Figure 4(a)** shows that mixing negative trajectories for stabilization brings significant additional gains to RL over positive-only PEAR initialization, with the "Ours_token w. Negative+GRPO" bar achieving higher pass@1 than "Ours_token+GRPO" across all model sizes (1.7B, 4B, 8B).

The design choice to use sequence-level weights for negative examples (rather than token-level) is deliberate. Negative trajectories should be discouraged as complete units—if any part of the trajectory leads to failure, the entire trajectory is undesirable. Token-level weighting on negative data could create ambiguous signals where some tokens in a failing trajectory are up-weighted (if their local continuation looks plausible) even though the overall trajectory leads to failure.

**Numerical Stabilization Techniques**

PEAR employs multiple stabilization techniques to prevent variance explosion and training instability, all documented in **Section 3.7** and **Algorithm 1**. First, importance weights are computed in **log-space** to avoid direct products of ratios over long sequences. Instead of computing `$\prod_{t=1}^T \Delta_t$` directly, we compute `$\sum_{t=1}^T \log \Delta_t$` and then exponentiate: `$\exp(\sum_{t=1}^T \log \Delta_t)$`. This prevents underflow (products becoming too small) and overflow (products becoming too large) that would occur with direct multiplication of many ratios.

Second, we apply **clipping** at two levels. Per-decision log-ratios are clipped: `$\delta_t \leftarrow \text{clip}(\log \Delta_t, \ell_\Delta, u_\Delta)$` with default bounds `$[-0.08, 0.3]$` per **Section 4.2**. This prevents any single token from having an extreme likelihood ratio that would dominate the weight computation. Final weights are also clipped: `$\hat{G}_t \leftarrow \text{clip}(\hat{G}_t, G_{\min}, G_{\max})$` with default bounds `$[-10, 5]$` in log-space. These clipping bounds are hyperparameters that should be tuned based on sequence length and task difficulty—longer sequences generally require tighter clipping to prevent variance accumulation.

Third, the **discount factor** `$\gamma \in (0, 1]$` controls how much future tokens influence the current weight. Setting `$\gamma < 1$` (e.g., `$\gamma = 0.999$`) exponentially decays the influence of distant future tokens, reducing the effective horizon of the importance weight computation. This is analogous to discounting in reinforcement learning (**Sutton & Barto, 2018; Jiang & Li, 2016**) and helps stabilize training on long reasoning traces where very long suffix products would otherwise have extreme variance.

**Figure 5** visualizes how PEAR's weights distribute across tokens with different KL divergence levels from the base model. The y-axis shows the weight (clipped), and we see that token distribution is more heavily driven on important tokens that drive success probability. Tokens with higher KL divergence (more changed from the base model) tend to receive higher weights, indicating that PEAR concentrates learning on positions where the policy has meaningfully diverged from the behavior policy—exactly the positions where correction is most needed.

**Compatibility with Different Base Objectives**

PEAR is designed as a **plug-in reweighting approach** that works with multiple base objectives, not just standard NLL. **Figure 4(b)** demonstrates that PEAR's weighting scheme works with KL-based knowledge distillation, where the base loss is `$\ell_\theta = \text{KL}(\pi_\beta(\cdot | x, y_{<t}) \parallel \pi_\theta(\cdot | x, y_{<t}))$`. The importance weights are computed using the same suffix likelihood ratio procedure, adding minimal overhead to the KL-based KD baseline because the probabilities needed for weight computation are already computed during the KD forward pass.

This generality is important because different base objectives have different properties. NLL directly maximizes the likelihood of ground-truth tokens, while KL-divergence minimizes the divergence from a reference policy (often useful for preventing catastrophic forgetting). PEAR's reweighting mechanism is orthogonal to the choice of base loss—it modifies *how much* each token contributes to the objective, not *what* the objective measures. **Table 2** shows PEAR applied to vanilla NLL loss across multiple model families (Qwen2.5-1.5B-Math, DeepSeek-Distill-Qwen-1.5B, Qwen3-4B-Base, Qwen3-8B-Base), consistently improving post-GRPO performance.

**Computational Overhead and Efficiency**

The additional training overhead of PEAR is minimal once the behavior policy probabilities are collected. The weight computation requires one forward pass through the target model to compute `$\pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t})$` for all tokens in the offline dataset, plus the log-ratio aggregation described in **Algorithm 1**. This is a one-time cost per training epoch (or can be done less frequently if weights are cached). The backward pass complexity is identical to standard SFT because the weights are stopped-gradient—the computational graph for backpropagation is the same, just with different scalar coefficients on each token's loss.

**Section 4.2** specifies the training details: offline training runs for 1 epoch with learning rate `$3 \times 10^{-5}$` on games and `$1 \times 10^{-5}$` on math. For PEAR specifically, the hyperparameters are `$\gamma = 0.999$`, clip log `$\hat{G}_t$` to `$[-10, 5]$`, and clip per-decision log `$\Delta_t$` to `$[-0.08, 0.3]$`. These values were tuned on the SynLogic games dataset and transferred effectively to math reasoning tasks without re-tuning, suggesting the stabilization parameters are relatively robust across domains.

**Why This Approach Over Alternatives**

The importance sampling approach was chosen over alternative mismatch-correction methods for several reasons grounded in both theory and practicality. First, importance sampling has a solid theoretical foundation in off-policy evaluation (**Precup et al., 2000; Thomas & Brunskill, 2016; Jiang & Li, 2016**), providing unbiased estimates of the target policy's expected loss when the behavior policy has support over the target policy's distribution. Second, it is computationally efficient—unlike methods that require additional environment interaction or rollout generation during the offline phase, PEAR only requires logged probabilities from the behavior policy, which are already available in standard SFT datasets.

Third, the suffix-based weighting specifically addresses the **compounding error problem** in autoregressive generation (**Ross et al., 2011; Ross & Bagnell, 2014**). Small early mismatches shift the prefix distribution and propagate forward, compounding over long horizons. By weighting tokens based on their future continuation compatibility (not just their immediate likelihood), PEAR focuses learning on prefixes whose continuations from the dataset are compatible with the current target policy. This is especially acute for long-form reasoning (**Guo et al., 2025**), where traces often involve implicit search, trial, backtracking, and self-correction.

Fourth, the method is **orthogonal to other SFT improvements**. PEAR can be combined with KL-regularization, probability-based objectives, or other loss modifications because it operates at the weighting level, not the loss function level. This modularity makes it easy to integrate into existing training pipelines without replacing the entire objective.

**Limitations and Design Trade-offs**

Several design trade-offs are worth noting for practitioners implementing PEAR. The suffix-based weighting requires access to behavior policy probabilities `$\pi_\beta(y_t | x, y_{<t})$` for all tokens in the dataset. If the offline data was generated by a system that did not log these probabilities (e.g., simple API calls without log-prob output), they must be recomputed by running the behavior policy on the dataset, which adds computational cost. The paper assumes these probabilities are available as part of the offline buffer construction (**Section 4.1.1**).

The clipping bounds `$[\ell_\Delta, u_\Delta]$` and `$[G_{\min}, G_{\max}]$` are hyperparameters that require tuning. Too tight clipping reduces the correction effect (weights become nearly uniform), while too loose clipping allows variance explosion. The paper's default values (`$[-0.08, 0.3]$` for log-ratios, `$[-10, 5]$` for log-weights) work well for sequences up to a few thousand tokens, but may need adjustment for significantly longer or shorter sequences.

The discount factor `$\gamma$` controls the effective horizon of credit assignment. Setting `$\gamma = 1$` gives full weight to the entire suffix, which is theoretically correct but can have high variance on long sequences. Setting `$\gamma < 1$` (e.g., `$0.999$`) reduces variance but introduces bias by discounting distant future tokens. The optimal value depends on the typical sequence length and the degree to which early tokens influence final outcomes in the target task.

Finally, PEAR assumes the behavior policy has **support** over the target policy's distribution (i.e., `$\pi_\beta(y_t | x, y_{<t}) > 0$` whenever `$\pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t}) > 0$`). If the behavior policy never generates certain tokens that the target policy would generate, the likelihood ratio becomes undefined (division by zero). In practice, this is mitigated by clipping and by using behavior policies that are sufficiently diverse (e.g., sampling with temperature > 0 rather than greedy decoding).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper introduces both a fundamental shift in how we evaluate post-training objectives and specific algorithmic innovations to address the identified mismatch. Below, we distinguish between the paradigmatic changes in research focus and the technical mechanisms that enable them.

### 4.1 Paradigm Shift: Evaluating SFT by RL Readiness, Not Offline Accuracy
The most significant contribution of this work is conceptual rather than purely algorithmic: it challenges the **monotonicity assumption** that dominates LLM post-training research. Prior work implicitly assumes that improvements in offline metrics (e.g., SFT loss, offline pass@1) will translate linearly to improvements after online RL. This paper provides empirical evidence that this assumption is false, and often dangerously misleading.

*   **Ranking Reversals:** As demonstrated in **Figure 1**, there is no consistent correlation between offline and online performance. In some cases, the relationship is negative. For instance, on Qwen3-1.7B-Base, the `TopLogP` objective achieves superior offline scores but results in the **worst** post-RL performance among all tested methods, even underperforming standard SFT.
*   **Metric Disconnect:** This disconnect persists across different offline descriptors. **Figure 1** shows that even robust metrics like offline pass@128 or majority voting accuracy fail to preserve ranking when compared to online pass@1. This implies that optimizing for these offline metrics is optimizing for the wrong target.
*   **Significance:** This finding forces a reevaluation of how SFT methods are benchmarked. A method that claims "state-of-the-art SFT performance" may actually be detrimental to the final pipeline if it increases the distribution mismatch with the online RL phase. The paper argues that the correct evaluation metric for an offline stage is **post-RL performance**, not offline accuracy.

### 4.2 Algorithmic Innovation: Suffix-Based Weighting vs. Myopic Single-Step Correction
While importance sampling is a known technique in reinforcement learning, applying it to SFT via **suffix-based weighting** is a distinct algorithmic contribution that differentiates PEAR from concurrent works. Many recent methods propose action-level stabilization where weights depend only on the current token's likelihood ratio (e.g., $w_t = \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t)}{\pi_\beta(y_t)}$). PEAR demonstrates that this **myopic** approach is insufficient for long-horizon reasoning.

*   **Long-Horizon Credit Assignment:** The core insight is that online RL cares about **trajectories**, not individual tokens. A token might be plausible under the target policy $\pi_\theta$ in isolation, but if it leads to a continuation that $\pi_\theta$ would never generate, training on it is wasted compute. PEAR's token-level variant weights tokens based on the likelihood of the *remaining suffix*: $G_t \propto \prod_{j=t+1}^T \frac{\pi_\theta(y_j)}{\pi_\beta(y_j)}$.
*   **Empirical Validation:** **Table 3** provides a direct ablation comparing PEAR against "Single Step IS" (importance sampling on the token only). On Qwen3-1.7B-Base, Single Step IS achieves **18%** accuracy after RL, which is a **5% decrease** compared to standard SFT+GRPO (23%). In contrast, PEAR (suffix-based) achieves **26%**, a **3% gain**. This proves that correcting for the future trajectory (suffix) is critical, whereas correcting only for the current action can be harmful.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:** This aligns with off-policy evaluation theory where the value of a state-action pair depends on the expected return of the subsequent trajectory (**Appendix C**). By weighting based on the suffix, PEAR approximates an off-policy estimate of the token's contribution to the final return, rather than just its immediate likelihood.

### 4.3 Practical Innovation: Block-Level Weighting for Variance Control
A known limitation of importance sampling is variance explosion over long horizons, especially when ratios compound multiplicatively. PEAR introduces a **block-level weighting** variant (**Section 3.5**) that offers a tunable trade-off between granularity and stability, making the method robust for long reasoning traces (e.g., math proofs).

*   **Mechanism:** Instead of computing a unique weight for every token (high variance) or one weight for the whole sequence (low granularity), the sequence is partitioned into blocks of size $B$. All tokens in a block share the same weight based on the suffix *after* the block.
*   **Performance Trade-off:** **Figure 6** compares different variants on SynLogic games. While token-level ($B=1$) generally performs best, block-level ($B=4$) remains highly competitive and significantly outperforms standard SFT. This suggests that for very long contexts where token-level variance might destabilize training, block-level weighting offers a safe fallback without sacrificing most of the benefit.
*   **Significance:** This makes PEAR applicable to tasks with varying sequence lengths. For short logic games, token-level precision is feasible. For long-form math reasoning (where sequences can exceed 1000 tokens), block-level weighting prevents the importance weights from becoming too extreme, ensuring stable gradient updates during the offline phase.

### 4.4 Extension: Policy-Consistent Repulsion via Negative Examples
Standard SFT typically ignores negative data (failed trajectories) or treats them uniformly. PEAR introduces a mechanism to leverage verified failures as a **repulsive force**, weighted by their importance under the target policy (**Section 3.6**).

*   **Asymmetric Learning:** For positive data, PEAR attracts the model toward high-likelihood continuations. For negative data $\mathcal{D}^-$, it applies a repulsive loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{neg}}$ with gradient ascent, pushing the model away from trajectories that lead to failure. Crucially, these negative trajectories are also weighted by their sequence-level importance, ensuring the model prioritizes avoiding failures that it is currently likely to generate.
*   **Stability Choice:** The paper deliberately uses **sequence-level weights** for negative examples rather than token-level suffix weights. The reasoning is that a negative trajectory is undesirable as a whole; decomposing it into token-level weights might inadvertently up-weight early tokens in a failing trajectory if their immediate suffix looks plausible.
*   **Result:** **Figure 4(a)** shows that adding negative examples ("Ours_token w. Negative+GRPO") yields higher pass@1 scores than positive-only PEAR across all model sizes (1.7B, 4B, 8B). This demonstrates that verified failures are a valuable resource for reducing distribution mismatch, provided they are incorporated in a policy-consistent manner.

### 4.5 Summary of Contributions
| Contribution | Type | Key Differentiator | Evidence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Offline $\neq$ Online** | Fundamental | Proves offline accuracy is a flawed proxy for RL readiness. | **Figure 1** (Ranking reversals) |
| **Suffix Weighting** | Algorithmic | Corrects for trajectory compatibility, not just token likelihood. | **Table 3** (vs. Single Step IS) |
| **Block-Level Stability** | Practical | Controls variance for long-horizon reasoning tasks. | **Figure 6** (Variant comparison) |
| **Negative Repulsion** | Extension | Leverages failures as weighted repulsive signals. | **Figure 4(a)** (Negative data gain) |

These innovations collectively establish PEAR not just as a new loss function, but as a framework for designing offline stages that are explicitly aligned with the dynamics of online reinforcement learning.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate PEAR's effectiveness through controlled experiments. We will walk through the evaluation methodology, analyze the quantitative results with specific numbers, and assess whether the evidence convincingly supports the paper's central claims about offline-to-online alignment.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The authors design a **controlled, contamination-free experimental setup** to isolate the effect of different SFT objectives on downstream RL performance. The key principle is to vary *only* the SFT-stage objectives while keeping all other factors constant (same RL algorithm, same RL hyperparameters, same evaluation benchmarks).

#### Datasets and Task Domains

The experiments span two distinct reasoning domains to test generalization:

**Logic Games (SynLogic and Enigmata):**
- **SynLogic** (Liu et al., 2025): Synthetic, verifiable puzzles procedurally generated from diverse environments. The offline buffer contains approximately **100,000 correct trajectories** sampled from Qwen3-8B and verified with final answers.
- **Enigmata** (Chen et al., 2025b): Another synthetic reasoning environment with verifiable puzzle instances. Used for transfer experiments (**Section 4.4**).
- **Evaluation metric:** Pass@{1, 8} on held-out puzzle sets with original verifiers.

**Math Reasoning:**
- **Offline training data:** Subset of SYNTHETIC-2 dataset (Prime Intellect, 2025) — **33,400 unique instructions**, sampled to **100,000 question-response pairs** from Qwen3-8B.
- **Online RL data:** DAPO-17k dataset (Yu et al., 2025).
- **Evaluation benchmarks:** MATH-500 (Hendrycks et al., 2021), MINERVA (Lewkowycz et al., 2022), AIME-2024, AIME-2025, and AMC-2023 (Balunović et al., 2026).
- **Evaluation metric:** Average accuracy across **64 samples** to reduce variance, plus Pass@K metrics.

#### Model Families and Sizes

The experiments cover **6 different models** across varying scales to test robustness:

| Model Family | Sizes Tested |
|--------------|--------------|
| Qwen3-Base | 0.6B, 1.7B, 4B, 8B |
| Qwen2.5-Math | 1.5B |
| DeepSeek-Distill-Qwen | 1.5B |

This range allows the authors to verify whether PEAR's benefits scale with model size or are limited to specific architectures.

#### Baselines and Comparators

**Table 1** catalogs the full spectrum of SFT objectives tested. The baselines cover the "loss-strength" spectrum from drift control to hard masking:

- **Standard SFT (NLL):** `$\ell(p) = -\log p$` — the canonical baseline.
- **SFT+KL:** `$\ell(p) = -\log p + \beta \text{KL}$` with `$\beta \in \{0.03, 0.1, 0.3, 1\}$` — tests whether KL regularization alone addresses the mismatch.
- **GeneralFamily-α:** `$\ell_\alpha(p) = \frac{1 - p^\alpha}{\alpha}$` — a parametric family where `$\alpha \to 0$` recovers NLL.
- **Threshold-based methods:** TopP-q, BottomP-q, TopLogP-q, BottomLogP-q — hard masking toward high- or low-confidence tokens.
- **TALR:** Token-Adaptive Loss Reweighting with adaptive weights `$w_t \propto p_t^{1/\tau}$`.
- **Single Step IS:** A concurrent approach that weights tokens based only on immediate likelihood ratio `$\frac{\pi_\theta(y_t)}{\pi_\beta(y_t)}$` without suffix consideration.

#### Training Configuration

**Section 4.2** specifies the training details to ensure reproducibility:

**Offline Training:**
- **Epochs:** 1 epoch for all methods.
- **Learning rates:** `$3 \times 10^{-5}$` on logic games, `$1 \times 10^{-5}$` on math.
- **PEAR hyperparameters:** `$\gamma = 0.999$`, clip log `$\hat{G}_t$` to `[-10, 5]`, clip per-decision log `$\Delta_t$` to `[-0.08, 0.3]`.

**Online RL (GRPO):**
- **Learning rate:** `$10^{-6}$`.
- **Batch size:** 128.
- **KL coefficient:** 0.01.
- **Procedure:** Starting from each offline checkpoint `$\pi_0$`, the same online RL procedure produces `$\pi_{\text{RL}}$` for fair comparison.

This controlled setup ensures that any performance differences after RL can be attributed to the SFT-stage objective, not RL hyperparameter tuning.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

The experiments provide strong empirical support for the paper's central hypothesis: **PEAR-initialized checkpoints consistently outperform canonical SFT after identical RL training**.

#### Logic Games: SynLogic Results

**Figure 4(a)** presents the most direct comparison on SynLogic games across three model sizes (1.7B, 4B, 8B). The bars reflect Pass@1 and dots mark Pass@8. Key findings:

- **1.7B Model:** PEAR$_{B=1}$+GRPO achieves **26%** Pass@1 compared to SFT+GRPO's **23%** (+3% absolute gain). With negative examples, performance reaches even higher.
- **4B Model:** PEAR$_{B=1}$+GRPO achieves **44%** Pass@1 vs. SFT+GRPO's **39%** (+5% absolute gain).
- **8B Model:** PEAR$_{B=1}$+GRPO achieves **41%** Pass@1 vs. SFT+GRPO's **36%** (+5% absolute gain).

The paper notes a striking result in **Section 4.3**:

> "Comparing checkpoints finetuned from Qwen3-1.7B-Base using PEAR versus canonical SFT on the same data, the former outperform the latter by **40% absolute accuracy** on logic games."

This 40% figure refers to a specific comparison scenario (likely comparing best PEAR variant with negative examples against a weaker SFT baseline), demonstrating the magnitude of improvement possible.

**Figure 4(b)** shows PEAR applied to KL-based knowledge distillation. The "Ours+KD+GRPO" bar outperforms "KD+GRPO" alone, proving PEAR's weighting scheme is **orthogonal to the base objective** — it improves both NLL and KL-based training.

#### Transfer to Different RL Task Distributions

**Section 4.4** tests whether PEAR's benefits transfer when the offline training domain differs from the online RL domain. **Table 4** shows results when initializing RL from PEAR checkpoints and training on Enigmata tasks:

The **25.2% gain on Qwen3-1.7B-Base** is particularly striking. This demonstrates that PEAR's benefits are **not overfit to the offline domain** — the method produces an initialization that transfers better to shifted online RL distributions under identical RL compute.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Variant Comparisons

The paper includes several ablations to isolate which components of PEAR drive the improvements.

#### PEAR Variants: Sequence vs. Token vs. Block Level

**Figure 6** compares different PEAR variants on SynLogic games for 1.7B and 4B models:

- **Sequence-Level (Ours$_{\text{seq}}$+GRPO):** Applies one weight to the entire trajectory. Still outperforms SFT but is less effective than token-level.
- **Token-Level (Ours$_{B=1}$+GRPO):** The default variant with per-token suffix weighting. Achieves the highest Pass@1 and Pass@8 scores.
- **Block-Level (Ours$_{B=4}$+GRPO):** Partitions sequences into blocks of 4 tokens. Performance is competitive with token-level, offering a stability-granularity trade-off.

For the 4B model specifically:
- SFT+GRPO: **45.0%** Pass@1
- Ours$_{\text{seq}}$+GRPO: **57.5%** Pass@1
- Ours$_{B=4}$+GRPO: **65.0%** Pass@1
- Ours$_{B=1}$+GRPO: **63.7%** Pass@1

The token-level and block-level variants are close, suggesting practitioners can use block-level for very long sequences without sacrificing much performance.

#### Negative Example Integration

**Figure 4(a)** includes a bar for "Ours$_{\text{token}}$ w. Negative+GRPO" which incorporates the repulsive loss from **Section 3.6**. The setup uses **50K positive and 50K negative data** from the same behavior policy on the same instructions.

Results show consistent gains from adding negative examples:
- **1.7B:** Negative variant outperforms positive-only PEAR.
- **4B:** Negative variant achieves **70.0%** Pass@1 vs. **63.7%** for positive-only.
- **8B:** Negative variant achieves **82.5%** Pass@1 vs. **76.2%** for positive-only.

This confirms that verified failures are a valuable resource when incorporated with policy-consistent weighting.

#### Gradient Alignment Analysis

**Figure 7** measures the **average principal angle** between offline PEAR gradients and online GRPO gradients. Smaller angles indicate better alignment between offline and online learning directions.

- **PEAR (Ours):** ~51.27°
- **SFT:** ~52.53°
- **SFT+KL (β=0.03):** ~55.16°
- **SFT+KL (β=0.3):** ~55.94°
- **SFT+KL (β=1):** ~56.25°

The **smaller angle for PEAR** (51.27° vs. 52.53° for SFT) provides mechanistic evidence that PEAR's correction makes offline updates more consistent with online GRPO learning direction. Interestingly, **stronger KL constraints create greater mismatch** (larger angles), even though they better preserve closeness to the base model. This supports the paper's argument that minimizing drift is not the same as optimizing for RL readiness.

#### Parameter Drift Analysis

**Figure 8** analyzes parameter drift using **Normalized Spectrum Shift (NSS)**, which measures the relative drift of a singular-value spectrum after training:

- **Figure 8(a):** NSS between offline and base model. PEAR shows **greater offline drift** than SFT, meaning more learning happens during the offline stage.
- **Figure 8(b):** NSS between online and offline model. PEAR shows **smaller online drift** than SFT, meaning less correction is needed during RL.

The paper interprets this in **Section 4.6**:

> "We observe online RL training after PEAR [has the] smallest drift measured by average NSS... between online and offline checkpoints compared with other initializations, whereas the heavy-lifting happened in the offline stage."

This is critical evidence that PEAR **shifts the learning burden to the cheaper offline stage**, reducing the parameter updates needed during expensive online RL.

### 5.4 Assessment of Experimental Validity

#### Strengths of the Experimental Design

1. **Controlled Comparison:** The experiments vary only the SFT objective while keeping RL hyperparameters, data, and evaluation identical. This isolates the causal effect of PEAR.

2. **Multiple Model Scales:** Testing across 0.6B to 8B models demonstrates the method is not limited to a specific scale.

3. **Multiple Domains:** Logic games and math reasoning are sufficiently different to test generalization. The transfer experiment (**Table 4**) further validates cross-domain robustness.

4. **Mechanistic Analysis:** The gradient alignment (**Figure 7**) and parameter drift (**Figure 8**) analyses provide *why* PEAR works, not just *that* it works.

5. **Ablation of Variants:** Comparing sequence, token, and block-level weighting (**Figure 6**) clarifies which design choices matter most.

#### Potential Limitations and Open Questions

1. **Behavior Policy Probability Requirement:** PEAR requires access to `$\pi_\beta(y_t | x, y_{<t})$` for all tokens. If offline data was generated without logging probabilities, they must be recomputed, adding computational cost. The paper assumes these are available (**Section 4.1.1**) but does not quantify the overhead.

2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The clipping bounds (`[-0.08, 0.3]` for log-ratios, `[-10, 5]` for log-weights) and discount factor (`$\gamma = 0.999$`) are tuned on SynLogic and transferred to math. The paper does not include a sensitivity analysis showing how performance degrades if these are mis-specified.

3. **Very Long Sequences:** While block-level weighting addresses variance, the paper does not test sequences exceeding several thousand tokens (e.g., complex code generation or long-form writing). The effectiveness on such tasks remains unverified.

4. **Comparison to Advanced RL Initialization Methods:** The baselines are primarily SFT variants. The paper does not compare against methods that explicitly co-train SFT and RL or use iterative DPO-style approaches, which might also address the offline-online mismatch differently.

5. **Compute Efficiency Claims:** The paper argues PEAR saves RL compute by producing better initializations (**Appendix A** notes RLVR can take "minutes to hours per step"). However, there is no explicit experiment measuring **RL steps to convergence** comparing PEAR vs. SFT initialization. The parameter drift analysis (**Figure 8**) is suggestive but not a direct compute comparison.

#### Mixed Results and Trade-offs

Not all results are uniformly positive:

- **AIME-2025 Pass@8 for Qwen3-8B-Base:** **Table 2** shows 35% for both SFT+GRPO and PEAR+GRPO (0% gain). This suggests PEAR's benefits may diminish at larger model sizes for certain benchmarks, possibly because larger models already have sufficient capacity to correct the mismatch during RL.

- **AMC-2023 Pass@8 for Qwen3-1.7B-Math:** SFT+GRPO achieves 80% while PEAR+GRPO achieves 78% (-2%). This is one of the few cases where PEAR underperforms, though the Pass@1 and Pass@64 metrics still show gains.

- **Offline Performance:** **Section 4.3** explicitly notes: "PEAR's performance does not surface in terms of its out-of-the-box offline performance, and even may not beat SFT." This is expected (PEAR optimizes for RL readiness, not offline accuracy) but means practitioners cannot use offline metrics to select PEAR hyperparameters.

### 5.5 Summary of Experimental Evidence

The experiments provide **convincing support** for the paper's central claims:

| Claim | Evidence | Strength |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Offline ≠ Online | **Figure 1** shows ranking reversals across 19 models | Strong |
| PEAR improves post-RL performance | **Table 2**, **Figure 4** show consistent gains across models | Strong |
| Suffix weighting > Single-step | **Table 3** shows Single Step IS has negative gains on some models | Strong |
| Benefits transfer across domains | **Table 4** shows gains when RL domain differs from offline | Strong |
| PEAR reduces online RL burden | **Figure 8** shows smaller online parameter drift | Moderate (indirect) |
| Negative examples help | **Figure 4(a)** shows gains from repulsive loss | Strong |

The most compelling evidence is the **consistent pattern across model sizes and domains**. If PEAR's benefits were due to overfitting or chance, we would not see systematic improvements across 0.6B to 8B models on both logic games and math benchmarks.

The **mechanistic analyses** (gradient alignment, parameter drift) strengthen the causal story by showing *how* PEAR achieves these gains — not just that it does. This moves the work beyond empirical observation toward principled understanding.

However, the lack of explicit **compute efficiency measurements** (RL steps to convergence) is a gap. The paper argues PEAR saves RL compute but does not directly quantify this. Future work should measure wall-clock time or RL steps needed to reach a target performance level, comparing PEAR-initialized vs. SFT-initialized models.

Overall, the experimental design is rigorous, the results are reproducible (hyperparameters are specified), and the conclusions are well-supported by the data. The few mixed results (e.g., AMC-2023 Pass@8 for 1.7B) are honestly reported and do not undermine the broader pattern of improvement.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While PEAR demonstrates consistent improvements in post-RL performance, practitioners must account for several structural assumptions, data requirements, and unresolved trade-offs before deploying it in production pipelines. This section critically examines the constraints inherent to the method, distinguishing between limitations acknowledged by the authors and gaps remaining in the experimental validation.

### 6.1 Data and Logging Requirements
The most significant practical barrier to adopting PEAR is its dependency on **behavior policy probabilities**. Unlike standard SFT, which only requires prompt-response pairs `$(x, y)$`, PEAR requires the logged probability `$\pi_\beta(y_t | x, y_{<t})$` for every token `$t$` in the offline dataset (**Section 3.1**).

*   **Recomputation Cost:** If the offline dataset was generated by a third party or an API that did not export log-probabilities, practitioners must replay the entire dataset through the behavior policy `$\pi_\beta$` to recover these values. For large datasets (e.g., the **100,000 trajectories** used in **Section 4.1.1**), this adds a non-trivial one-time computational overhead before training can begin.
*   **Support Assumption:** The importance sampling formulation theoretically requires that the behavior policy has support over the target policy's distribution (i.e., `$\pi_\beta(y_t | \dots) > 0$` whenever `$\pi_\theta(y_t | \dots) > 0$`). **Appendix B** notes this assumption explicitly. In practice, if `$\pi_\beta$` assigns zero probability to a token that `$\pi_\theta$` considers likely, the likelihood ratio becomes undefined. While **Section 3.7** employs clipping (e.g., clipping per-decision log `$\Delta_t$` to `[-0.08, 0.3]`) to mitigate extreme values, it does not fully resolve the zero-probability case. This restricts PEAR to behavior policies that are sufficiently diverse (e.g., sampled with temperature `$> 0$`) rather than greedy decoders.

### 6.2 Task Domain Constraints
The experimental validation is strictly limited to **verifiable reasoning tasks**. The paper evaluates exclusively on synthetic logic games (SynLogic, Enigmata) and mathematical benchmarks (AIME, MATH-500, AMC) where answers can be programmatically verified (**Section 4.1**).

*   **Reward Signal Dependency:** PEAR is designed to prepare models for online RL with dense or verifiable rewards (e.g., correct/incorrect math answers). It is unclear whether the suffix-based weighting translates effectively to domains with sparse or subjective rewards, such as helpfulness/harmlessness alignment (RLHF), where the "continuation compatibility" is harder to define without a clear verifier.
*   **Negative Data Granularity:** For negative examples (verified failures), PEAR uses **sequence-level weights** rather than token-level suffix weights (**Section 3.6**). The authors justify this by stating negative trajectories should be discouraged as complete units. However, this is a design trade-off: it prevents the model from learning *which specific token* caused the failure within a long trajectory. In tasks where errors occur early but propagate (e.g., a wrong variable definition in code), sequence-level repulsion might be less sample-efficient than token-level correction.

### 6.3 Scalability and Sequence Length
Importance sampling over long horizons is prone to variance explosion, a problem known as the "curse of horizon" (**Section 3.5**). PEAR addresses this with block-level weighting and discounting, but limitations remain.

*   **Block Size Sensitivity:** The block-level variant partitions sequences into blocks of size `$B$` to stabilize weights. Experiments in **Figure 6** test `$B=1$` (token-level) and `$B=4$`. While `$B=4$` performs competitively, the paper does not explore larger block sizes (e.g., `$B=64$` or `$B=256$`) that might be necessary for very long contexts (e.g., `$>4096$` tokens). The optimal `$B$` likely scales with sequence length, but no heuristic is provided for selecting `$B$` based on context window size.
*   **Discount Factor Bias:** The discount factor `$\gamma = 0.999$` (**Section 4.2**) reduces variance by exponentially decaying the influence of distant future tokens. However, this introduces bias: tokens at the beginning of a 2000-token sequence receive weights that largely ignore the final outcome. For tasks where early decisions are critical (e.g., planning steps), this discounting might attenuate important learning signals.

### 6.4 Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Tuning
PEAR introduces new hyperparameters that require tuning, yet the paper provides limited guidance on their sensitivity.

*   **Clipping Bounds:** The method relies on clipping log-ratios to `[-0.08, 0.3]` and log-weights to `[-10, 5]` (**Section 4.2**). These values were tuned on SynLogic games and transferred to math tasks. There is no ablation study showing how performance degrades if these bounds are mis-specified for a different domain (e.g., code generation). Too tight clipping renders PEAR equivalent to uniform SFT; too loose clipping risks instability.
*   **Offline Metric Disconnect:** A critical operational challenge is that **PEAR does not improve offline metrics**. **Section 4.3** states: *"PEAR's performance does not surface in terms of its out-of-the-box offline performance, and even may not beat SFT."* This means practitioners cannot use standard offline validation sets (e.g., offline pass@1) to tune PEAR's hyperparameters. Instead, they must rely on expensive online RL rollouts to verify improvements, which contradicts the goal of saving compute during the offline stage.

### 6.5 Compute Efficiency Claims
The paper argues that PEAR saves compute by reducing the burden on the online RL phase (**Appendix A** notes RLVR can take "minutes to hours per step"). However, the evidence for this is indirect.

*   **Parameter Drift vs. Steps to Convergence:** **Figure 8** shows that PEAR-initialized models undergo less parameter drift (NSS) during online RL compared to SFT-initialized models. While this suggests the model is "closer" to the final solution offline, the paper does not report **steps to convergence**. It is possible that while PEAR reduces drift per step, it requires the same number of RL steps to reach peak performance. Without a direct comparison of wall-clock time or RL steps required to hit a target accuracy (e.g., 50% on AIME), the compute savings remain hypothetical.
*   **Forward Pass Overhead:** Computing weights requires a forward pass through the target model `$\pi_\theta$` on the offline data (**Section 3.7**). For large models (e.g., 8B+), this adds latency to the offline training loop, especially if weights are recomputed every epoch rather than cached. The paper states overhead is "little" once probabilities are collected but does not quantify this in GPU-hours.

### 6.6 Diminishing Returns at Scale
While PEAR consistently improves performance across model sizes, there is evidence of diminishing returns at larger scales.

*   **8B Model Saturation:** In **Table 2**, for the Qwen3-8B-Base model on AIME-2025 Pass@8, both SFT+GRPO and PEAR+GRPO achieve **35%** (0% gain). Similarly, on MATH-500 Pass@8, the gain is only **+3%** (90% to 93%). This suggests that larger models may have sufficient capacity to correct the behavior-target mismatch during the RL phase on their own, reducing the marginal utility of PEAR.
*   **Benchmark Specificity:** On AMC-2023 Pass@8 for Qwen2.5-1.5B-Math, PEAR actually underperforms SFT (**78%** vs. **80%**, a **-2%** drop in **Table 2**). While aggregate metrics favor PEAR, this indicates that for certain easier benchmarks or specific model architectures, the reweighting might introduce noise that hinders optimization.

### 6.7 Summary of Open Questions
The following questions remain unresolved by the current work:
*   **Non-Verifiable Domains:** Can PEAR be adapted for RLHF where rewards are learned from preference models rather than verified answers?
*   **Dynamic Weighting:** The weights `$\hat{G}_t$` are treated as fixed coefficients (stop-gradient) per batch (**Section 3.2**). Would updating weights more frequently (e.g., every step) improve convergence, or would it destabilize training?
*   **Negative Data Optimization:** Why limit negative examples to sequence-level weights? Could a stable token-level repulsive signal yield better sample efficiency on failure data?
*   **Explicit Compute Metrics:** Future work should measure RL steps-to-convergence to validate the claim that PEAR reduces online training costs.

In summary, PEAR is a robust method for verifiable reasoning tasks where behavior policy probabilities are available, but its benefits must be weighed against the logistical cost of data logging and the inability to tune hyperparameters using offline metrics.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work fundamentally alters how we should conceptualize the offline stage of LLM post-training. By demonstrating that **offline accuracy is a flawed proxy for RL readiness**, the paper forces a shift in evaluation metrics and experimental design. The implications extend beyond the specific PEAR algorithm to the broader methodology of building reasoning models.

### 7.1 Shifting the Post-Training Paradigm

The most profound impact of this work is conceptual: it decouples the objectives of the offline stage from the metrics used to evaluate it.

*   **From "Best SFT" to "Best Initialization":** Prior research optimizes SFT objectives to maximize offline metrics (e.g., pass@1 on a validation set). **Figure 1** proves this is dangerous: methods like `TopLogP` dominate offline scores but collapse after RL. The new paradigm requires evaluating offline methods **only after downstream RL**. A method that yields lower offline accuracy but higher post-RL performance (like PEAR) is superior.
*   **Reevaluating Prior Work:** Many existing SFT improvements (e.g., KL-regularization, probability-based reweighting from **Table 1**) were benchmarked solely on offline performance. This paper suggests their rankings may reverse in an SFT$\rightarrow$RL pipeline. Future benchmarks must include the RL stage to be valid for reasoning tasks.
*   **The "Offline $\neq$ Online" Principle:** Practitioners must accept that **Section 2.1**'s finding is likely general: static evaluation does not predict interactive performance. This validates the need for online RL but suggests the offline stage should be redesigned to support it, not replace it.

### 7.2 Enabling More Efficient RL Pipelines

Online RL is computationally expensive. **Appendix A** notes that RLVR (Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards) can take "minutes to hours per step" due to rollouts and verification. PEAR offers a pathway to reduce this cost, though indirectly.

*   **Reducing Parameter Drift:** **Figure 8** shows that PEAR-initialized models undergo significantly less parameter drift (measured by Normalized Spectrum Shift) during online RL compared to SFT-initialized models.
    *   **Interpretation:** The offline stage is doing more of the "heavy lifting." If the model is already aligned with the target policy's distribution offline, the online phase requires fewer updates to converge.
    *   **Potential Savings:** While the paper does not explicitly measure **steps-to-convergence**, the reduced drift implies that fewer RL steps may be needed to reach peak performance. For large-scale training runs, even a 10-20% reduction in RL steps translates to massive GPU-hour savings.
*   **Stabilizing Online Training:** By correcting the behavior-target mismatch offline, PEAR reduces the risk of the online RL phase encountering out-of-distribution trajectories early in training. This can prevent instability or collapse that sometimes occurs when RL starts from a poorly aligned checkpoint.

### 7.3 Suggested Follow-Up Research

The paper opens several concrete avenues for future investigation, ranging from theoretical extensions to practical engineering.

*   **Extension to Non-Verifiable Domains (RLHF):** PEAR is currently validated on verifiable reasoning tasks (Math, Logic) where rewards are binary (correct/incorrect).
    *   **Challenge:** In RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback), rewards are learned from a reward model and are noisy.
    *   **Question:** Can suffix-based weighting work when the "continuation compatibility" is defined by a reward model rather than a verifier? The importance sampling logic holds, but the variance might be higher due to reward noise.
*   **Dynamic Weighting Strategies:** Currently, PEAR computes weights `$\hat{G}_t$` using a stop-gradient operation (**Section 3.2**), treating them as fixed coefficients per batch.
    *   **Hypothesis:** Updating weights more frequently (e.g., every step rather than every epoch) might better track the evolving target policy `$\pi_\theta$`.
    *   **Risk:** This could introduce instability if the weights change too rapidly. Future work should explore the trade-off between weight freshness and training stability.
*   **Token-Level Negative Repulsion:** **Section 3.6** uses sequence-level weights for negative examples to avoid instability.
    *   **Opportunity:** A stable token-level repulsive signal could teach the model *where* a trajectory fails, not just *that* it fails. This might improve sample efficiency on failure data, especially for long trajectories where errors occur early.
*   **Explicit Compute Metrics:** Future experiments should directly measure **wall-clock time** or **RL steps** required to reach a target accuracy (e.g., 50% on AIME). This would quantify the compute savings hinted at by the parameter drift analysis in **Figure 8**.

### 7.4 Practical Integration Guidance

For practitioners considering PEAR, the decision depends on pipeline constraints and task characteristics. Below is a guide on when to adopt this method.

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **SFT $\rightarrow$ RL Pipeline** | **Use PEAR** | The method is designed specifically to improve the initialization for RL. **Table 2** shows consistent gains across models. |
| **SFT Only (No RL)** | **Do Not Use** | **Section 4.3** notes PEAR may not improve (and might slightly hurt) offline metrics. If there is no downstream RL, standard SFT is preferable. |
| **Verifiable Rewards** | **Strong Fit** | Math, code, logic games where answers are checkable. The method relies on the RL phase having clear signal to optimize. |
| **Subjective Rewards (RLHF)** | **Experimental** | Theoretically applicable, but untested in the paper. Variance from reward models might require tighter clipping bounds. |
| **Behavior Log-Probs Available** | **Required** | You must have `$\pi_\beta(y_t \| x, y_{<t})$` for all tokens. If your data lacks this, you must replay the behavior policy to generate them. |
| **Long Context (>4k tokens)** | **Use Block-Level** | Use `$\text{PEAR}_{B=4}$` or larger blocks. **Figure 6** shows block-level is competitive with token-level and more stable for long horizons. |

#### Integration Checklist
1.  **Data Preparation:** Ensure your offline dataset includes log-probabilities from the behavior policy. If not, run inference with the behavior model to collect them.
2.  **Hyperparameters:** Start with the paper's defaults: `$\gamma = 0.999$`, log-ratio clip `[-0.08, 0.3]`, log-weight clip `[-10, 5]`.
3.  **Validation:** **Do not tune hyperparameters on offline accuracy.** Use a small pilot RL run to evaluate PEAR configurations, as offline metrics are misleading (**Figure 1**).
4.  **Negative Data:** If you have verified failures, include them with the repulsive loss (**Section 3.6**). **Figure 4(a)** shows this adds significant value.

### 7.5 Conclusion

The paper establishes a critical principle: **the offline stage should prioritize reproducible successes under the target policy that will be optimized online.** PEAR provides a mechanistic solution to this problem using importance sampling, but its broader contribution is the realization that **SFT and RL cannot be optimized in isolation**.

By aligning the offline objective with the online distribution, we move toward a more holistic post-training framework. This reduces the burden on expensive online RL, stabilizes training dynamics, and ultimately yields models that reason more effectively. As the field scales to larger models and more complex tasks, methods like PEAR that bridge the offline-online gap will become essential infrastructure for efficient model development.
