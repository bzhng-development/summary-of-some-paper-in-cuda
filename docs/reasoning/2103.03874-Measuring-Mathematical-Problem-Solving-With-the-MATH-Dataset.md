## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces `MATH`, a new dataset of 12,500 challenging competition mathematics problems with step-by-step solutions, alongside `AMPS`, a large auxiliary pretraining corpus containing over 100,000 Khan Academy problems and 5 million Mathematica-generated problems. Experiments show that even enormous Transformer models like GPT-3 (175B) achieve low accuracy ranging from 3.0% to 6.9% on `MATH`, indicating that current scaling trends are insufficient for strong mathematical reasoning. The authors conclude that achieving human-level performance would require impractical parameter counts (around $10^{35}$), necessitating new algorithmic advancements beyond simply increasing model size.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Measuring Genuine Mathematical Reasoning

This paper addresses a fundamental gap in machine learning evaluation: **existing benchmarks do not adequately measure mathematical problem-solving ability**. While machines have become proficient at calculations and pattern matching, they still lack the capacity to analyze problems, select appropriate heuristics from many possibilities, and chain reasoning steps together to produce answers. The authors distinguish this from "plug-and-chug" calculations—mechanical procedures that ML models can already perform reliably.

The specific gap is twofold:

1. **Prior datasets focus on either formal theorem proving or trivial calculations**, neither of which captures how humans actually do mathematics in practice.
2. **Existing text-based benchmarks are becoming saturated** by simply scaling up Transformer models, making it difficult to distinguish genuine reasoning improvements from memorization or pattern matching.

### Why Mathematical Problem Solving Matters

The importance of this problem extends beyond academic benchmarking. The Introduction emphasizes that mathematics is "unreasonably effective" (Wigner, 1960) across intellectual domains because it provides:

- **Consistency and logic-based reasoning** that can be relied upon
- **Precise encoding of phenomena** ranging from planetary orbits to atomic motion to signal frequencies
- **Domain-generality** that transfers across scientific disciplines

From a machine learning perspective, mathematics serves as an ideal testbed for problem-solving ability because it requires:
- Analyzing a problem structure
- Selecting appropriate heuristics from a large set of possibilities
- Chaining these heuristics together coherently

This contrasts sharply with visual or linguistic reasoning tasks like image classification, where problem-solving is not the primary focus. Success on mathematical reasoning would indicate capabilities that transfer to broader intellectual endeavors.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper identifies three categories of prior work, each with distinct shortcomings (Section 2, Related Work):

#### 1. Neural Theorem Provers

Work in automated theorem proving uses formal verification environments:

| Benchmark | Approach | Limitation |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| Coq (Huang et al., 2019) | 1,602 theorems and lemmas | Requires formal language |
| HOList (Bansal et al., 2019) | Formal language for automatic evaluation | Manually converting advanced math is "extremely time-consuming" |
| Metamath (Polu and Sutskever, 2020) | Formalization language | Same formal language barrier |

The critical limitation, as noted by Szegedy (2020), is that **working on formal theorem provers alone is an impractical path towards world-class mathematical reasoning**. Humans write mathematics in natural language with commonplace notation (LaTeX), not in proof assistant languages. Converting between these representations creates a bottleneck that limits scalability.

#### 2. Neural Calculators and Plug-and-Chug Tasks

These benchmarks test computational ability rather than reasoning:

- **DeepMind Mathematics Dataset** (Saxton et al., 2019): Algorithmically generated problems like "Divide 1136975704 by -142121963" or "Sort 2, 4, 0, 6" (shown in Figure 1)
- **Symbolic Integration** (Lample and Charton, 2020): Achieves 95%+ accuracy on integration problems

The limitation is explicit in the paper: **Henighan et al. (2020) show that the vast majority of DeepMind Mathematics problems can be straightforwardly solved with large Transformers** (excluding problems with astronomically large numbers). These tasks test calculation, not problem-solving heuristics.

#### 3. General Language Benchmarks

Other NLP benchmarks are becoming saturated:

- **SuperGLUE** (Wang et al., 2019): Solved by training enormous Transformers (He et al., 2020)
- **HumanEval** (Chen et al., 2021): Code generation that should be solved with continued scaling
- **LogiQA** (Liu et al., 2020): Logical reasoning that "will soon be straightforwardly solved by enormous Transformers" (shown in Figure 6, Appendix A.2)
- **MMLU** (Hendrycks et al., 2021): Multiple-choice benchmark with only "a few hundred questions about mathematics"

The pattern is clear: **scaling Transformers automatically solves most existing text-based tasks**, raising the question of whether NLP can be solved by simply increasing compute and funding (Kaplan et al., 2020; Henighan et al., 2020).

### How This Paper Positions Itself

The MATH dataset positions itself as a **deliberately unsolvable benchmark under current scaling trends**. The authors make several strategic design choices:

**1. Natural Language Mathematics, Not Formal Proofs**

Unlike theorem proving benchmarks, MATH uses mathematics "written in natural language and commonplace mathematical notation" (Section 2). This means:
- Answers can be assessed "without the need for a cumbersome theorem proving environment"
- Models must understand how humans actually write mathematics
- The evaluation framework is more accessible to the broader research community

**2. Competition-Level Problems, Not Calculations**

Figure 1 contrasts MATH with prior work. While DeepMind Mathematics asks "Solve 4 - 4 - 4 = 188*m for m" (answer: -1/47), MATH asks problems like:

> "The equation $x^2 + 2x = i$ has two complex solutions. Determine the product of their real parts."

This requires completing the square, working with complex exponentials, and trigonometric identities—multiple reasoning steps rather than a single calculation.

**3. Step-by-Step Solutions for Training**

Every MATH problem includes a full step-by-step solution in LaTeX and natural language. This enables:
- Models to learn derivation patterns, not just answer mapping
- Interpretability of model outputs
- Training on "scratch space" where models generate intermediate computations

**4. Demonstrated Resistance to Scaling**

Figure 2 shows state-of-the-art accuracy across mathematics datasets. MATH sits at the bottom with accuracy far below other benchmarks. The paper explicitly states (Section 1):

> "While enormous Transformers pretrained on massive datasets can now solve most existing text-based tasks, this low accuracy indicates that our MATH dataset is distinctly harder."

The scaling analysis is particularly striking: **assuming log-linear scaling trends, models would need around $10^{35}$ parameters to achieve 40% accuracy on MATH** (Section 1). This is physically impractical, meaning progress requires algorithmic innovation rather than just more compute.

**5. Fine-Grained Assessment**

MATH enables detailed analysis through:
- **Seven subjects**: Prealgebra, Algebra, Number Theory, Counting and Probability, Geometry, Intermediate Algebra, and Precalculus (Section 3.1)
- **Five difficulty levels** (1-5): Following AoPS conventions, where AMC 8 early problems are level 1 and AIME problems are level 5
- **Geometry with diagrams**: Figures encoded in Asymptote language rather than raster images, allowing pure language models to process graphics (Section 3.1)

This structure allows researchers to identify specific weaknesses rather than treating mathematical ability as monolithic.

### The Stakes

The Conclusion frames the significance clearly: "Given the broad reach and applicability of mathematics, solving the MATH dataset with machine learning would be of profound practical and intellectual significance." 

The paper positions MATH not as another benchmark to be solved through scaling, but as a **signal that current approaches are fundamentally limited**. If Transformers cannot make substantial progress on MATH despite enormous parameter counts, this indicates that mathematical reasoning requires capabilities beyond pattern matching and memorization—capabilities that the research community has not yet discovered how to build.

This framing makes MATH both a benchmark and a research agenda: it measures current limitations while pointing toward the need for "new algorithmic advancements from the broader research community" (Abstract).

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper is fundamentally a **dataset and benchmark construction paper** that introduces two complementary resources (MATH for evaluation, AMPS for pretraining) and systematically evaluates how current Transformer architectures perform on them. The core idea is that by creating a challenging, competition-level mathematics dataset with step-by-step solutions, researchers can measure genuine problem-solving ability rather than simple calculation or pattern matching, and the accompanying pretraining corpus enables models to learn mathematical fundamentals before tackling advanced problems.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The *system* being built is a two-dataset pipeline: MATH serves as a challenging test set of 12,500 competition mathematics problems with verified answers, while AMPS serves as a massive training corpus of 5+ million simpler mathematics problems with step-by-step solutions. The *solution shape* is to pretrain language models on AMPS to learn mathematical fundamentals, then fine-tune on MATH to learn competition-level problem-solving, with evaluation based on exact match of boxed final answers.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The technical pipeline consists of four major components connected in sequence:

1. **MATH Dataset Construction** (Section 3.1): Collects 12,500 competition problems from sources like AMC 10, AMC 12, and AIME, each with a step-by-step LaTeX solution and a boxed final answer. This component outputs the benchmark test set.

2. **AMPS Pretraining Corpus** (Section 3.2): Combines 100,000+ Khan Academy problems with 5 million Mathematica-generated problems (from 100 hand-designed scripts), totaling 23GB of mathematics problems with solutions. This component outputs the pretraining data.

3. **Model Training Pipeline** (Section 4.1): Takes autoregressive language models (GPT-2, GPT-3, BART), pretrained on AMPS for one epoch, then fine-tuned on MATH with a mix of answer-only and solution-full sequences. This component outputs trained models.

4. **Evaluation Framework** (Sections 3.1, 4.2): Parses model outputs to extract boxed answers, normalizes them according to formatting rules, and compares against ground truth using exact match. This component outputs accuracy metrics across subjects and difficulty levels.

Information flows from dataset construction → pretraining → fine-tuning → evaluation, with ablation studies testing variations at each stage (e.g., with/without AMPS pretraining, with/without step-by-step solutions during training).

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### MATH Dataset Construction and Structure

The MATH dataset contains exactly 12,500 mathematics problems split into 7,500 training problems and 5,000 test problems, sourced from high school mathematics competitions including AMC 10, AMC 12, AIME, and other contests collected from aops.com/community/c3158_usa_contests. Each problem in MATH has two required components: a problem statement written in LaTeX and natural language, and a full step-by-step solution that concludes with a boxed final answer using the `\boxed{}` command. The dataset spans seven subject categories: Prealgebra, Algebra, Number Theory, Counting and Probability, Geometry, Intermediate Algebra, and Precalculus (Table 1 in the paper shows related topics covered in AMPS, but MATH uses these seven subjects). Within each subject, problems are tagged with difficulty levels from 1 to 5 following Art of Problem Solving (AoPS) conventions, where level 1 represents the easiest problems in that subject (such as early AMC 8 problems) and level 5 represents the hardest (such as AIME problems). This difficulty tagging enables fine-grained analysis of model performance across problem complexity, and Section 4.2 shows that GPT-2 (1.5B) achieves approximately 15% accuracy on level 1 problems but only 4% on level 5 problems.

Geometry problems in MATH include diagrams specified using the Asymptote vector graphics language rather than raster images, which is a critical design choice that allows pure language models to process geometric information without requiring multi-modal architectures. Figure 7 in Appendix A.3 shows an example of Asymptote code alongside the figure it produces, demonstrating that visual mathematics problems can be concisely specified with code. The Asymptote commands are embedded directly in the LaTeX source, so models trained on MATH learn to interpret commands like `draw((1,0)--(1,-2))` as geometric constructions rather than treating them as images requiring computer vision.

The answer format in MATH is designed for automatic evaluation through exact match rather than heuristic metrics like BLEU. All final answers are wrapped in `\boxed{}` delimiters, which allows the evaluation script to parse model outputs and extract only the final answer regardless of how much intermediate reasoning the model generates. The paper specifies detailed normalization rules to handle equivalent answer representations: probabilities must be expressed as simplified fractions, matrix entry fractions use `x/y` encoding while other fractions use `\frac{x}{y}`, coefficients are written without multiplication symbols (e.g., `5x` not `5*x`), expressions with multiple variables are entered in alphabetical order, and polynomials are expressed in decreasing degree order. The evaluation treats different fraction encodings as equivalent (such as `\frac{x}{y}` and `\dfrac{x}{y}` and `x/y`), different parenthesis encodings as equivalent (such as `\left(` and `(`), allows units to be included or omitted, ignores spaces, and treats common equivalent number representations as the same (e.g., `0.5` and `1/2`, or `0.1` and `.1`). For factorized polynomials, different orderings of factors are permitted so that `4(x + 1)(x − 1)` is equivalent to `4(x − 1)(x + 1)`. These normalization rules cover nearly all practical cases where generated and ground truth solutions could be equivalent, enabling reliable automatic scoring.

#### AMPS Pretraining Corpus Design

The Auxiliary Mathematics Problems and Solutions (AMPS) dataset addresses a fundamental limitation: mathematics comprises only a small fraction of online text, so general pretrained language models lack sufficient exposure to mathematical notation and reasoning patterns. AMPS contains over 23GB of mathematics problems and solutions, which is larger than the 16GB of natural language used to train BERT (Devlin et al., 2019). The corpus has two components: Khan Academy data and Mathematica-generated data.

The Khan Academy subset contains 693 exercise types with over 100,000 problems and full step-by-step solutions typeset in LaTeX. These exercises span from elementary mathematics (basic addition) to advanced topics (Stokes' Theorem) and are the same exercises used to teach actual K-12 students. The exercises can be regenerated using code from github.com/Khan/khan-exercises/, and Appendix Figures 12-15 list all 693 module names, ranging from "2 step equations" to "z scores 3" to "washer method: revolving around x- or y-axis." All 693 Khan Academy modules include full step-by-step solutions, not just final answers.

The Mathematica subset contains approximately 5 million problems generated from 100 hand-designed scripts, with around 50,000 exercises produced from each script. Table 1 lists representative topics covered by these scripts, including conic sections, polynomial GCD, De Moivre's theorem, function inverses, arclength, Jacobian, Laplacian, divergence, curl, gradients, integrals, expectation, geometric mean, harmonic mean, KL divergence, variance, triangle area, triangle inradius, polygon angles, polyhedron diameter, characteristic polynomials, eigenvalues, reduced row echelon form, modular inverse, Euler's totient function, and Chinese remainder theorem. Of the 100 scripts, 37 generate full step-by-step LaTeX solutions in addition to final answers, while the remaining 63 generate only problems and answers. The use of Mathematica's computer algebra system allows the generation to manipulate fractions, transcendental numbers, and analytic functions correctly, unlike prior approaches that algorithmically generate mathematics problems without symbolic computation support. Figure 8 in Appendix A.3 shows example Mathematica code that generates polynomial GCD problems, demonstrating how the scripts randomly construct polynomials with controlled degrees and coefficients, then use Mathematica's built-in `PolynomialGCD` function to compute correct answers.

During pretraining, the authors upsample Khan Academy data by a factor of 5 and downsample Mathematica data by a factor of 2 to account for the large difference in dataset sizes (100,000 vs 5 million problems). This balancing ensures models receive adequate exposure to both human-curated problems (Khan Academy) and algorithmically generated problems (Mathematica) without being overwhelmed by the sheer volume of Mathematica data.

#### Model Training Pipeline and Hyperparameters

The paper uses autoregressive language models because MATH answers must be generated as sequences rather than selected from multiple choices. The primary architectures tested are GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2016) and GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020), which are decoder-only Transformer models pretrained on natural language text. The GPT-2 models tokenize numbers so that one digit is processed at a time rather than treating multi-digit numbers as single tokens, following Henighan et al. (2020). The authors tested T5 (Raffel et al., 2020) but found its tokenizer removes many LaTeX symbols, and after a two-week hyperparameter sweep, T5 performance was not competitive. BART (Lewis et al., 2020) was also evaluated as it has a bidirectional encoder and unidirectional decoder without removing LaTeX symbols, but BART-Large (0.4B) achieved only 4.9% on MATH's test set, slightly worse than the smallest GPT-2 model (Appendix A.5).

Pretraining on AMPS uses the standard autoregressive language modeling objective for one epoch with AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov and Hutter, 2019), batch size of 128, and weight decay of 0.05. Models are trained on 8 A100 GPUs, with each model requiring less than a day to train. During fine-tuning on MATH, models are trained to predict both final answers and full solutions using an equal mix of two sequence formats: `⟨P⟩Final Answer: <Answer>` and `⟨P⟩Full Solution: <Step-by-Step Solution>`, where `⟨P⟩` represents the problem statement. This mixed training enables models to both generate full solutions and output just the final answer, providing flexibility at inference time. Fine-tuning uses the same batch size (128) and weight decay (0.05) as pretraining.

For inference, the default evaluation prompts models with `⟨P⟩Final Answer:` so they directly generate the final answer without producing step-by-step solutions. When generating only the final answer, beam search uses a beam size of 20. When generating full step-by-step solutions, beam search uses a beam size of 10. Unless otherwise specified, GPT-2 models use default HuggingFace (Wolf et al., 2020) generation parameters except for the beam search configuration.

GPT-3 evaluation uses the OpenAI API with two model variants: 'Curie' (approximately 13 billion parameters) and 'Davinci' (approximately 175 billion parameters). GPT-3 models are evaluated both with fine-tuning and in a few-shot setting. For few-shot evaluation, the prompt is constructed by prepending 8 problems with correct answers (but not step-by-step solutions due to space constraints). Using temperature 0, models output up to 20 tokens for the final answer. The OpenAI API allows fine-tuning models up to 13B parameters at the time of writing, but does not support pretraining on datasets as large as AMPS, which is why GPT-3 models in Table 2 are marked with '*' for few-shot and do not pretrain on AMPS due to API limits.

#### Evaluation Methodology and Metrics

The primary evaluation metric is exact match accuracy on the boxed final answers. After a model generates its output, the evaluation script parses the content inside the `\boxed{}` command and compares it against the ground truth answer using the normalization rules described earlier. This approach works even when models generate step-by-step solutions before the final answer, because the box delimiter provides a well-defined location for the answer.

For error detection analysis, the paper defines model confidence as the average prediction probability of the tokens that make up a generated answer. The authors compute the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (AUROC) to measure how well confidence distinguishes correct from incorrect answers. An AUROC of 100% corresponds to perfect detection (all correct answers have higher confidence than all incorrect answers), while 50% corresponds to random chance. For GPT-2 (1.5B), the AUROC is 68.8%, indicating substantial overlap between correct and incorrect answer confidences and suggesting significant room for improvement in detecting model errors. The paper notes that GPT-2 (1.5B) is highly overconfident, with confidences often around 100% regardless of correctness.

Human-level performance is assessed by giving 20 randomly sampled MATH test problems to six university students studying computer science, with a one-hour time limit and no calculator use. One participant who does not like mathematics scored 8/20 (40%), one ambivalent participant scored 13/20, two participants who like mathematics scored 14/20 and 15/20, one participant with a perfect AMC 10 score and USAMO attendance scored 18/20, and a three-time IMO gold medalist scored 18/20 (90%) with missed questions due exclusively to small arithmetic errors. Expert-level performance is theoretically 100% given enough time, and the paper notes that even 40% accuracy for a machine learning model would be impressive but would have ramifications for cheating on homework.

#### Key Ablation Experiments and Design Choices

The paper conducts several ablation studies to isolate the effects of different design choices:

**AMPS Pretraining Ablation:** Without pretraining on AMPS, a GPT-3 (13B) model fine-tuned on MATH attains 5.2% accuracy. In contrast, a GPT-2 (0.1B) model both pretrained on AMPS and fine-tuned on MATH attains 5.4% accuracy. This demonstrates that AMPS pretraining increases accuracy about as much as a 130× increase in parameters. The authors also tested pretraining on Math StackExchange (approximately 3GB of real-world mathematics text) in addition to AMPS, but a GPT-2 (0.3B) model achieved only 6.0% accuracy, less than the 6.2% from AMPS alone. This shows that the curated AMPS dataset is more useful for pretraining than diverse real-world mathematics data.

**Model Size Scaling:** Table 2 shows accuracies across subjects for GPT-2 models ranging from 0.1B to 1.5B parameters. The smallest model (0.1B) achieves 5.4% average accuracy, while the largest GPT-2 (1.5B, which is 15× more parameters) achieves 6.9% average accuracy, representing a 28% relative improvement. However, absolute accuracy remains far from the ceiling and increases only slowly with model size, unlike most other text-based tasks. GPT-3 (13B) few-shot achieves 3.0% without AMPS pretraining, and GPT-3 (175B) few-shot achieves 5.2%, showing that even enormous models struggle on MATH.

**Step-by-Step Solutions at Training vs. Test Time:** The paper makes a critical distinction between using step-by-step solutions during training versus during inference. Training on step-by-step solutions (the `⟨P⟩Full Solution: <Step-by-Step Solution>` format) increases relative accuracy by 10% compared to training on questions and answers directly. Specifically, when fine-tuning with only final answers (no step-by-step solutions), GPT-2 (1.5B) accuracy decreases by 0.6% to 6.3%. However, having models *generate* their own step-by-step solutions at test time actually decreases accuracy. When prompted with `⟨P⟩Full Solution:` to generate a full solution along with a boxed answer, GPT-2 (1.5B) accuracy drops to 5.3% from 6.9% when generating only the final answer. The authors hypothesize this drop arises from a "snowballing effect" where partially generated solutions with mistakes derail subsequent generated text. This reveals an important gap: models can learn from step-by-step solutions provided to them during training, but cannot yet effectively use their own generated solutions as scratch space during inference.

**Partial Solution Hints:** The paper tests whether providing partial ground truth step-by-step solutions during inference can improve accuracy. Models are prompted with `⟨P⟩<Partial Step-by-Step Solution without Final Answer> Final Answer:` during both fine-tuning and evaluation, with varying fractions of the solution provided. Figure 5 shows results for GPT-2 (0.7B): when models see 0% of the solution (the default setting), accuracy is around 6-7%. When models see 99% of the solution (all text before the final answer), accuracy reaches approximately 40%. This demonstrates that models can understand and complete solutions when given substantial hints, but still struggle even with 99% of the solution provided, indicating room for improvement in mathematical reasoning.

**Problem Difficulty and Subject Analysis:** Figure 9 in Appendix A.4 shows that problems more difficult for humans are also more difficult for GPT-2, with accuracy decreasing monotonically from difficulty level 1 to level 5. Figure 10 shows accuracy per subject per difficulty level, revealing substantial variation: for example, Geometry shows higher accuracy than Number Theory at most difficulty levels for GPT-2 (1.5B). Figures 11a and 11b analyze the relationship between accuracy and problem/solution length, finding that problems with long questions or ground truth solutions tend to be more difficult than problems with short questions or solutions.

#### Scaling Analysis and Extrapolation

The paper's most striking technical claim is the extrapolation of scaling trends to estimate the parameters needed for strong MATH performance. Based on the observed log-linear scaling trend from 0.1B to 175B parameters, the authors estimate that models would need around $10^{35}$ parameters to achieve 40% accuracy on MATH. This number is physically impractical (far exceeding any conceivable computational resource), leading to the conclusion that scaling Transformers alone will not solve mathematical reasoning. The Abstract states: "Moreover, we find that simply increasing budgets and model parameter counts will be impractical for achieving strong mathematical reasoning if scaling trends continue."

This scaling analysis contrasts sharply with other benchmarks. Appendix A.2 shows that LogiQA (a difficult logical reasoning task) will soon be solved by making models larger, assuming trends continue (Figure 6). The C-Test (a pattern completion test correlated with human IQ) shows GPT-3 (175B) achieving approximately 40% on the hardest examples after converting letters to numbers, while average humans attain around 20% on the same examples. These results demonstrate that enormous Transformers perform well on other logic and intelligence tests, making MATH's resistance to scaling particularly notable.

#### Design Choice Rationale

Several design choices in this paper warrant explanation regarding why alternatives were rejected:

**Why exact match instead of BLEU or other heuristic metrics?** The authors chose exact match with normalization because mathematical answers have unique correct values after normalization, unlike natural language generation where multiple phrasings can be correct. BLEU would penalize mathematically equivalent but textually different answers (e.g., `1/2` vs `0.5`), while exact match with proper normalization correctly identifies equivalence.

**Why competition problems instead of textbook problems?** Competition problems require selecting heuristics from many possibilities and chaining them together, whereas textbook problems often follow predictable patterns tied to specific chapters. The Introduction states: "Unlike most prior work, most problems in MATH cannot be solved with a straightforward application of standard K-12 mathematics tools. Instead, humans often solve such problem by applying problem solving techniques and 'heuristics' (Pólya, 1945)."

**Why Asymptote code instead of images for geometry?** Using code allows pure language models to process geometric information without requiring multi-modal architectures. The Introduction notes: "This enables pure language models to process figures, diagrams, and graphics, making it possible to assess these models on subjects such as geometry for the first time."

**Why not use formal theorem proving environments?** Section 2 explains that manually converting advanced mathematics to proof generation languages is "extremely time-consuming," and Szegedy (2020) argues that "working on formal theorem provers alone will be an impractical path towards world-class mathematical reasoning." MATH uses natural language and LaTeX so models learn to understand mathematics as humans write it.

**Why include AMPS instead of just evaluating on MATH?** Pretraining data greatly influences performance (Hernandez et al., 2021; Gururangan et al., 2020), and mathematics is a small fraction of online text. Without AMPS, models lack sufficient exposure to mathematical notation and reasoning patterns. The AMPS corpus enables a 0.1B parameter model to perform comparably to a fine-tuned model that is 130× larger, demonstrating its value for learning mathematical fundamentals before tackling competition problems.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the paper's most significant contributions into four core insights. These are not merely empirical results but fundamental observations about the current state of machine learning reasoning, the value of data curation, and the limitations of scaling laws. We distinguish between **incremental improvements** (better performance on existing tasks) and **fundamental innovations** (new ways of measuring or understanding model capabilities).

### 4.1 The "Scratch Space" Paradox: Training vs. Inference Discrepancy

One of the most counter-intuitive findings in this work is the divergence between how models *learn* from step-by-step solutions and how they *use* them during generation. This reveals a critical limitation in current autoregressive reasoning mechanisms.

*   **The Innovation:** The paper disentangles the utility of step-by-step solutions at **training time** versus **test time**.
    *   **Training Benefit:** When models are fine-tuned on sequences containing full solutions (`⟨P⟩Full Solution: <Step-by-Step Solution>`), relative accuracy increases by approximately 10% compared to training on answers alone (Section 4.3). This shows models can internalize reasoning patterns from data.
    *   **Inference Cost:** Conversely, when models are prompted to *generate* their own step-by-step solutions before the final answer (`⟨P⟩Full Solution:`), accuracy **decreases**. For GPT-2 (1.5B), accuracy drops from 6.9% (answer only) to 5.3% (with solution generation) (Section 4.3).

*   **Why This Happens (The Mechanism):** The authors hypothesize a **"snowballing effect"**. Because autoregressive models generate tokens sequentially, an early logical error in the generated solution corrupts the context for all subsequent tokens. Unlike humans, who can backtrack or verify intermediate steps, the model commits to the error and propagates it. Figure 3 illustrates this: a model might generate coherent LaTeX and relevant-looking steps that are logically flawed, leading to a wrong boxed answer.
    > "We hypothesize that the drop in accuracy from using scratch space arises from a snowballing effect, in which partially generated 'solutions' with mistakes can derail subsequent generated text." (Section 4.3)

*   **Significance:** This is a **fundamental insight** into model reliability. It suggests that current Transformers lack an internal verification mechanism. They can recognize correct reasoning when seen (training) but cannot reliably construct it without self-sabotage (inference). Bridging this gap—enabling models to use their own generated text as reliable scratch space—is identified as a key direction for future research (Section 1).

### 4.2 The Scaling Wall for Mathematical Reasoning

While scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020) predict performance improvements based on parameter count and compute, this paper provides evidence that mathematical reasoning may hit a hard wall where scaling becomes impractical.

*   **The Innovation:** The paper quantifies the **impracticality of scaling** for this specific task. By observing the log-linear scaling trend from 0.1B to 175B parameters, the authors extrapolate the compute required to reach human-competitive performance.
    *   **The Projection:** To achieve just 40% accuracy on MATH (which is considered impressive but below expert level), models would require approximately $10^{35}$ parameters (Abstract, Section 1).
    *   **The Contrast:** This contrasts sharply with other benchmarks. For example, Figure 6 (Appendix A.2) shows that on LogiQA (logical reasoning), accuracy is rising steeply with model size, suggesting human-level performance is imminent via scaling alone.

*   **Why This Matters:** This distinguishes MATH from tasks like SuperGLUE or code generation (HumanEval), where scaling is "automatically solving most other text-based tasks" (Abstract). The $10^{35}$ figure is physically unreachable, implying that **algorithmic advancements** are necessary rather than just larger models.
    > "While scaling Transformers is automatically solving most other text-based tasks, scaling is not currently solving MATH." (Abstract)

*   **Significance:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in benchmark design. Most benchmarks are created to be solved; MATH is designed to signal when current approaches are insufficient. It serves as a "stop sign" for pure scaling strategies, directing the community toward new architectures or reasoning algorithms (e.g., neural-symbolic hybrids, verification steps) rather than simply increasing budget.

### 4.3 Data Quality as a Force Multiplier (AMPS Efficiency)

The paper demonstrates that curated domain-specific pretraining data can substitute for massive increases in model parameters. This challenges the notion that parameter count is the primary driver of capability.

*   **The Innovation:** The **AMPS pretraining corpus** acts as a force multiplier. The ablation study in Section 4.2 compares a small model with AMPS pretraining against a large model without it.
    *   **The Result:** A GPT-2 model with 0.1 billion parameters pretrained on AMPS achieves 5.4% accuracy. A GPT-3 model with 13 billion parameters (130× larger) fine-tuned on MATH *without* AMPS achieves only 5.2% accuracy (Section 4.2).
    *   **The Comparison:** Even pretraining on real-world math data (Math StackExchange, ~3 GB) yielded lower accuracy (6.0%) than AMPS alone (6.2%) for a 0.3B model, indicating that **curriculum quality** matters more than raw data diversity.

*   **Why This Works:** General web text contains very little mathematics, and what exists is often informal. AMPS provides dense exposure to LaTeX notation, mathematical syntax, and derivation structures. This builds "mathematical fluency" (understanding the language of math) which is a prerequisite for "mathematical reasoning" (solving the problems).
    > "Pretraining on AMPS enables a 0.1 billion parameter model to perform comparably to a fine-tuned model that is 130× larger." (Abstract)

*   **Significance:** This is a **practical innovation** for resource-constrained research. It shows that investing in high-quality synthetic data (like the Mathematica scripts in AMPS) can yield efficiency gains equivalent to orders-of-magnitude increases in compute. It suggests that for specialized domains, data curation is a more efficient lever than model scaling.

### 4.4 Rigorous Automatic Evaluation via LaTeX and Asymptote

Designing a benchmark for mathematical generation is difficult because correct answers can be expressed in infinitely many ways (e.g., `$\frac{1}{2}$` vs. `$0.5$`). This paper introduces a robust evaluation framework that enables reliable exact match scoring without human intervention.

*   **The Innovation:** The use of **canonical normalization rules** and **code-based geometry**.
    *   **Normalization:** The paper defines strict equivalence rules for answers. For instance, `$\frac{x}{y}$`, `\dfrac{x}{y}`, and `x/y` are treated as equivalent; polynomials must be in decreasing degree order; variables in alphabetical order (Section 3.1).
    *   **Geometry:** Instead of raster images, geometric figures are encoded in **Asymptote** vector graphics language (e.g., `draw((1,0)--(1,-2))`). This allows pure language models to process diagrams as text sequences (Section 3.1, Figure 7).

*   **Why This is Necessary:** Standard NLP metrics like BLEU are inappropriate for math because they penalize semantically equivalent but textually different answers. Human evaluation is too slow for large-scale benchmarking. The `\boxed{}` delimiter allows the evaluation script to locate the final answer automatically, even if the model generates hundreds of tokens of reasoning beforehand (Section 3.1).
    > "These answers are unique after normalization, allowing MATH to be scored with exact match rather than with heuristic metrics such as BLEU." (Section 1)

*   **Significance:** This is a **methodological innovation** for benchmark construction. It solves the "evaluation bottleneck" for mathematical generation. By making geometry accessible to text-only models via Asymptote, it also opens up a new sub-domain (visual math reasoning) without requiring multi-modal architectures. This design choice ensures that performance gains are due to reasoning improvements, not metric gaming.

### 4.5 Summary of Contributions

| Contribution | Type | Key Finding | Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Scratch Space Paradox** | Fundamental Insight | Training on solutions helps; generating them hurts. | Models lack error correction/verification during generation. |
| **Scaling Wall** | Fundamental Insight | $10^{35}$ parameters needed for 40% accuracy. | Scaling alone is insufficient; new algorithms needed. |
| **AMPS Pretraining** | Practical Innovation | 0.1B + AMPS ≈ 13B without AMPS. | Domain-specific data curation > Raw parameter count. |
| **LaTeX/Asymptote Eval** | Methodological Innovation | Exact match via normalization & code-based diagrams. | Enables reliable, scalable scoring for math generation. |

Together, these insights reframe mathematical problem solving not as a task to be solved by brute force, but as a complex capability requiring specific algorithmic breakthroughs in verification, data efficiency, and reasoning structure.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the experimental methodology, quantitative results, and critical assessment of whether the evidence supports the paper's central claims. We analyze the evaluation framework, present specific numerical findings from all major experiments, and identify both strengths and limitations in the experimental design.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Experimental Setup

#### Datasets and Splits

The evaluation uses two distinct datasets with different purposes:

| Dataset | Purpose | Size | Split |
|---------|---------|------|-------|
| **MATH** | Benchmark evaluation | 12,500 problems | 7,500 training / 5,000 test |
| **AMPS** | Pretraining corpus | 23GB (5M+ problems) | Pretraining only (no test split) |

MATH problems are sourced from mathematics competitions (AMC 10, AMC 12, AIME) collected from aops.com/community/c3158_usa_contests. Each problem includes a LaTeX-formatted statement, step-by-step solution, and boxed final answer. AMPS combines 100,000+ Khan Academy problems (693 exercise types) with 5 million Mathematica-generated problems (100 scripts, 50,000 exercises each).

#### Models and Architectures

The paper evaluates multiple model families to test generality of findings:

- **GPT-2 variants**: 0.1B, 0.3B, 0.7B, 1.5B parameters (all pretrained on AMPS)
- **GPT-3 variants**: 13B (Curie), 175B (Davinci) via OpenAI API (cannot pretrain on AMPS due to API limits)
- **BART-Large**: 0.4B parameters (Appendix A.5)

All GPT-2 models tokenize numbers digit-by-digit following Henighan et al. (2020). T5 was tested but rejected after a two-week hyperparameter sweep because its tokenizer removes many LaTeX symbols, making performance non-competitive.

#### Training Configuration

Pretraining on AMPS uses:
- **Objective**: Standard autoregressive language modeling
- **Optimizer**: AdamW with weight decay of 0.05
- **Batch size**: 128
- **Epochs**: 1
- **Hardware**: 8 A100 GPUs (less than one day per model)
- **Data balancing**: Khan Academy upsampled 5×, Mathematica downsampled 2×

Fine-tuning on MATH uses an equal mix of two sequence formats:
1. `⟨P⟩Final Answer: <Answer>`
2. `⟨P⟩Full Solution: <Step-by-Step Solution>`

This mixed training enables models to output either format at inference time.

#### Inference Configuration

- **Default evaluation**: Prompt with `⟨P⟩Final Answer:` (direct answer generation)
- **Beam search**: Beam size 20 for answer-only, beam size 10 for full solutions
- **GPT-3 few-shot**: 8 example problems prepended (answers only, no solutions due to space)
- **Temperature**: 0 for GPT-3
- **Max tokens**: 20 for final answer

#### Primary Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Purpose |
|--------|------------|---------|
| **Exact Match Accuracy** | Normalized answer matches ground truth | Primary performance metric |
| **AUROC** | Area under ROC curve for confidence vs. correctness | Error detection capability |
| **Relative Improvement** | Percentage change over 0.1B baseline | Scaling analysis |

The exact match metric uses extensive normalization rules (Section 3.1): fractions like `\frac{x}{y}`, `\dfrac{x}{y}`, and `x/y` are equivalent; `0.5` equals `1/2`; polynomial factor orderings are permitted; units can be included or omitted.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

#### Overall Accuracy by Model Size (Table 2)

Table 2 presents the core scaling results across all subjects:

| Model | Parameters | Average Accuracy | Relative Improvement |
|-------|------------|------------------|---------------------|
| GPT-2 | 0.1B | 5.4% | baseline (+0%) |
| GPT-2 | 0.3B | 6.2% | +15% |
| GPT-2 | 0.7B | 6.4% | +19% |
| GPT-2 | 1.5B | 6.9% | +28% |
| GPT-3* | 13B | 3.0% | −44% |
| GPT-3 | 13B | 5.6% | +4% |
| GPT-3* | 175B | 5.2% | −4% |

*Few-shot models (cannot pretrain on AMPS due to API limits)

**Critical observation**: The 15× parameter increase from GPT-2 0.1B to 1.5B yields only a 1.5 percentage point absolute improvement (5.4% → 6.9%). This represents diminishing returns far steeper than typical NLP tasks where scaling often produces dramatic gains.

#### Accuracy by Subject (Table 2)

Performance varies substantially across the seven subject categories:

| Subject | GPT-2 0.1B | GPT-2 1.5B | GPT-3 175B* |
|---------|------------|------------|-------------|
| Prealgebra | 5.2% | 8.3% | 7.7% |
| Algebra | 5.1% | 6.2% | 6.0% |
| Number Theory | 5.0% | 4.8% | 4.4% |
| Counting & Probability | 2.8% | 5.4% | 4.7% |

| Precalculus | 7.3% | 8.8% | 4.0% |

**Notable patterns**:
- **Geometry** shows the largest gap between GPT-2 1.5B (8.7%) and GPT-3 175B few-shot (3.1%), suggesting AMPS pretraining matters more than raw parameter count for spatial reasoning
- **Number Theory** is the only subject where the 1.5B model (4.8%) performs worse than the 0.1B model (5.0%)
- **Precalculus** has the highest absolute accuracy (8.8% for GPT-2 1.5B) despite being the most advanced subject

#### Accuracy by Difficulty Level (Figure 9, Appendix A.4)

Figure 9 shows monotonic degradation with increasing difficulty:

| Difficulty Level | GPT-2 0.1B | GPT-2 1.5B |
|------------------|------------|------------|
| Level 1 (easiest) | ~10% | ~15% |
| Level 5 (hardest) | ~3% | ~4% |

The paper states in Section 4.2: "The accuracy of GPT-2 (1.5B) is around 15% for level 1 (easy) and around 4% for level 5 (hard)." This 4× accuracy gap between easiest and hardest problems demonstrates that MATH successfully differentiates problem complexity, and models struggle proportionally more as heuristic chaining requirements increase.

#### Human Performance Baseline (Section 3.1)

Six university students were evaluated on 20 randomly sampled MATH test problems with 1 hour time limit and no calculator:

| Participant Profile | Score | Percentage |
|---------------------|-------|------------|
| CS PhD (doesn't like math) | 8/20 | 40% |
| Ambivalent toward math | 13/20 | 65% |
| Likes mathematics (2 participants) | 14/20, 15/20 | 70%, 75% |
| Perfect AMC 10 + USAMO attendee | 18/20 | 90% |
| Three-time IMO gold medalist | 18/20 | 90% |

The IMO gold medalist's missed questions were "exclusively due to small errors of arithmetic." Expert-level performance is theoretically 100% given sufficient time. The paper notes that "even 40% accuracy for a machine learning model would be impressive but have ramifications for cheating on homework."

**Gap analysis**: The best model (GPT-2 1.5B at 6.9%) achieves less than one-fifth the accuracy of a mathematics-disinterested CS PhD student (40%), and approximately one-thirteenth of expert performance (90%).

### 5.3 Ablation Studies

#### AMPS Pretraining Effect (Section 4.2)

The most striking ablation isolates the value of domain-specific pretraining:

| Model | Parameters | AMPS Pretrained | Accuracy |
|-------|------------|-----------------|----------|
| GPT-3 | 13B | No | 5.2% |
| GPT-2 | 0.1B | Yes | 5.4% |

**Interpretation**: AMPS pretraining provides an accuracy boost equivalent to a 130× increase in parameters. This is the paper's strongest evidence that data quality can substitute for model scale in specialized domains.

A secondary ablation tested adding Math StackExchange data (~3GB of real-world mathematics text):

| Pretraining Data | Model | Accuracy |
|------------------|-------|----------|
| AMPS only | GPT-2 0.3B | 6.2% |
| AMPS + StackExchange | GPT-2 0.3B | 6.0% |

**Surprising finding**: Adding diverse real-world mathematics data *decreased* accuracy by 0.2 percentage points. The authors conclude that curated, clean data (AMPS) is more valuable than noisy, diverse data (StackExchange) for mathematical reasoning.

#### Step-by-Step Solutions: Training vs. Inference (Section 4.3)

This ablation reveals the "scratch space paradox" discussed in Section 4.1:

| Training Format | Inference Format | GPT-2 1.5B Accuracy |
|-----------------|------------------|---------------------|
| Answers only | Answer only | 6.3% |
| Answers + Solutions | Answer only | 6.9% |
| Answers + Solutions | Generate solutions | 5.3% |

**Key findings**:
1. Training with solutions provides +0.6% absolute improvement (+10% relative) over training with answers only
2. Generating solutions at inference time *decreases* accuracy by 1.6% absolute (6.9% → 5.3%)
3. The snowballing effect hypothesis: early errors in generated solutions corrupt subsequent reasoning

#### Partial Solution Hints at Inference (Figure 5)

Figure 5 tests whether providing ground truth partial solutions helps models complete the reasoning:

| Fraction of Solution Provided | GPT-2 0.7B Accuracy |
|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| 0% (default) | ~6-7% |
| 50% | ~20% |
| 90% | ~30% |
| 99% (all but final answer) | ~40% |

**Critical insight**: Even with 99% of the solution provided, models only achieve 40% accuracy—matching a CS PhD student who dislikes mathematics, but far below the 90% expert level. This suggests models struggle with even the final inference step when given near-complete reasoning chains.

#### Architecture Comparison (Appendix A.5)

BART-Large (0.4B) was tested to determine if encoder-decoder architectures help:

| Model | Parameters | Architecture | Accuracy |
|-------|------------|--------------|----------|
| GPT-2 | 0.1B | Decoder-only | 5.4% |
| BART-Large | 0.4B | Encoder-Decoder | 4.9% |

BART performs slightly worse despite having 4× more parameters and bidirectional encoding. This suggests the decoder-only autoregressive architecture is not the primary bottleneck for MATH performance.

#### Error Detection Capability (Section 4.2)

The paper evaluates whether model confidence correlates with correctness:

| Metric | GPT-2 1.5B Value | Interpretation |
|--------|------------------|----------------|
| Average confidence on answers | ~100% | Highly overconfident |
| AUROC (correct vs. incorrect) | 68.8% | Poor error detection |

An AUROC of 68.8% means there is substantial overlap between confidence distributions for correct and incorrect answers. For comparison, random guessing would yield 50%, and perfect detection would yield 100%. This indicates models cannot reliably identify when they have made mistakes—a critical limitation for deployment.

### 5.4 Scaling Extrapolation Analysis

The paper's most provocative claim is the extrapolation of scaling trends to estimate parameters needed for human-competitive performance.

**Observed scaling** (GPT-2 family, AMPS-pretrained):
- 0.1B → 5.4%
- 1.5B → 6.9%
- 15× parameters yields 1.5 percentage point improvement

**Extrapolation** (Abstract, Section 1):
- Target: 40% accuracy (CS PhD level, not expert)
- Estimated parameters needed: $10^{35}$
- Comparison: This exceeds any physically realizable compute

**Contrast with other benchmarks** (Appendix A.2, Figure 6):
- LogiQA accuracy increases steeply with model size
- C-Test: GPT-3 175B achieves ~40% on hardest examples vs. 20% for average humans
- These tasks appear solvable via scaling alone

**Interpretation**: MATH is uniquely resistant to scaling compared to other reasoning benchmarks. The $10^{35}$ figure is not a precise prediction but a rhetorical device emphasizing that current scaling laws cannot practically achieve strong mathematical reasoning.

### 5.5 Failure Cases and Qualitative Analysis

#### Generated Solution Errors (Figures 3, 4)

Figure 3 shows a representative failure case:

**Problem**: Find minimum value of $\frac{a^2+b^2}{a-b}$ given $a > b$ and $ab = 8$.

**Generated solution**: Applies QM-AM inequality incorrectly, derives $a^2 + b^2 \geq \frac{s^2}{2}$ where $s = a+b$, then makes algebraic errors.

**Ground truth**: Uses AM-GM on $(a-b) + \frac{16}{a-b} \geq 8$, achieving the correct minimum of 8.

**Observation**: The model generates coherent LaTeX and relevant-looking steps but applies wrong inequalities and makes algebraic mistakes. This supports the snowballing hypothesis.

Figure 4 shows a geometry problem where the model generates Asymptote code but computes the wrong probability (1/2 vs. correct 1/128), demonstrating that code generation capability does not guarantee correct reasoning.

#### Correct Answer, Wrong Reasoning (Figure 3)

Some generated solutions arrive at correct answers through flawed reasoning. This is particularly concerning because exact match evaluation cannot detect this. The paper acknowledges this limitation but argues that correct answers still indicate some mathematical capability, even if the derivation is unreliable.

#### Subject-Specific Weaknesses (Table 2)

Number Theory shows the lowest absolute performance across all model sizes (4.4% to 5.0%), suggesting modular arithmetic, divisibility, and number-theoretic heuristics are particularly challenging. Counting & Probability also underperforms (2.8% to 5.4%), indicating difficulty with combinatorial reasoning.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment of Experimental Validity

#### Strengths

1. **Comprehensive ablation design**: The paper isolates AMPS pretraining, model size, solution generation, and partial hints in separate experiments, enabling causal attribution of performance differences.

2. **Multiple model families**: Testing GPT-2, GPT-3, and BART reduces the risk that findings are architecture-specific artifacts.

3. **Human baselines**: Including six human participants with varying mathematical backgrounds provides meaningful context for model performance (40% to 90% range vs. 3% to 7% for models).

4. **Fine-grained metrics**: Subject and difficulty breakdowns (Table 2, Figures 9-10) enable targeted analysis rather than treating mathematical ability as monolithic.

5. **Transparent normalization rules**: Section 3.1 specifies exact equivalence rules for answer evaluation, enabling reproducibility.

#### Limitations

1. **GPT-3 API constraints**: GPT-3 models cannot pretrain on AMPS due to API limits, confounding the comparison between GPT-2 (AMPS-pretrained) and GPT-3 (not pretrained). The 13B GPT-3 fine-tuned model (5.6%) outperforms the few-shot version (3.0%), but we cannot isolate whether this is due to fine-tuning alone or fine-tuning + implicit mathematical knowledge from GPT-3's broader pretraining.

2. **Small human sample**: Only six human participants were evaluated, all university CS students. This limits statistical confidence in human baselines, though the range (40% to 90%) is informative.

3. **Single random seed**: The paper does not report variance across multiple training runs. Given the small absolute accuracy differences (e.g., 6.2% vs. 6.4%), some results may be within noise.

4. **Beam search sensitivity**: Results depend on beam size (20 for answers, 10 for solutions), but no ablation tests alternative beam sizes or sampling strategies.

5. **No ensemble results**: The paper does not explore whether ensembling multiple models improves accuracy, which is standard practice in competition mathematics.

6. **Time-constrained human evaluation**: Humans had 1 hour for 20 problems (3 minutes per problem), which may underestimate true human capability. The IMO gold medalist missed questions due to "small arithmetic errors" under time pressure.

#### Mixed Results and Trade-offs

| Finding | Condition | Trade-off |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Step-by-step solutions help | Training time only | Cannot use at inference without accuracy drop |
| AMPS pretraining helps | Small models (0.1B) | GPT-3 cannot access AMPS due to API limits |
| Partial hints help | 99% of solution provided | Still only 40% accuracy, far from expert level |
| Larger models help | Within GPT-2 family | Diminishing returns (15× params → 28% relative gain) |
| Geometry via Asymptote works | Text-only models | Requires learning code-to-geometry mapping |

The most significant trade-off is the **training-inference discrepancy** for step-by-step solutions. Models learn reasoning patterns from solutions during training but cannot reliably construct them during inference. This suggests current autoregressive architectures lack verification mechanisms that humans use when solving mathematics problems.

### 5.7 Do Experiments Support the Paper's Claims?

#### Claim 1: "MATH is distinctly harder than existing benchmarks"

**Supported**: Table 2 shows 3-7% accuracy for best models vs. 95%+ on DeepMind Mathematics (Henighan et al., 2020) and 80%+ on symbolic integration (Lample & Charton, 2020). Figure 2 visually confirms MATH has the lowest accuracy among mathematics benchmarks.

#### Claim 2: "Scaling alone will not solve MATH"

**Partially supported**: The $10^{35}$ parameter extrapolation is speculative but grounded in observed log-linear trends. The 15× parameter increase yielding only 28% relative improvement (vs. often 100%+ on other tasks) supports diminishing returns. However, the extrapolation assumes trends continue unchanged, which may not hold with architectural innovations.

#### Claim 3: "AMPS pretraining is valuable"

**Strongly supported**: The 0.1B + AMPS vs. 13B without AMPS comparison (5.4% vs. 5.2%) is compelling evidence. The StackExchange ablation (6.0% vs. 6.2%) further supports that curated data matters more than raw diversity.

#### Claim 4: "Models cannot use their own generated solutions as scratch space"

**Supported**: The 6.9% → 5.3% accuracy drop when generating solutions at inference is clear evidence. However, the 40% accuracy with 99% of ground truth solution provided (Figure 5) suggests the limitation is in *generating* correct intermediate steps, not in *using* them.

#### Claim 5: "New algorithmic advancements are needed"

**Reasonably supported**: Given the scaling extrapolation and training-inference discrepancy, the conclusion that algorithmic innovation (not just scaling) is necessary follows logically. However, the paper does not propose specific algorithmic directions, leaving this as an open research agenda.

### 5.8 Summary of Experimental Findings

| Experiment | Key Result | Implication |
|------------|------------|-------------|
| Model scaling (Table 2) | 0.1B→1.5B: 5.4%→6.9% | Diminishing returns on MATH |
| AMPS pretraining | 0.1B+AMPS ≈ 13B without | Data quality > parameter count |
| Solution generation | 6.9%→5.3% when generating | Snowballing effect at inference |
| Partial hints (Fig 5) | 99% solution → 40% accuracy | Models can complete but not construct |
| Error detection | AUROC 68.8% | Poor confidence calibration |
| Human baseline | 40%-90% range | Large gap to model performance |
| Scaling extrapolation | $10^{35}$ params for 40% | Scaling alone is impractical |

The experiments collectively paint a coherent picture: MATH successfully measures mathematical reasoning capabilities that current Transformers cannot master through scaling alone. The ablation studies isolate specific bottlenecks (scratch space usage, error detection, data efficiency) that point toward concrete research directions rather than simply increasing compute budgets.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the assumptions, constraints, and unresolved weaknesses in the MATH benchmark and experimental design. While the paper makes substantial contributions, several limitations affect how we should interpret the results and what conclusions we can draw about mathematical reasoning capabilities.

### 6.1 Assumptions Underlying the Approach

#### Exact Match Evaluation Assumes Answer Uniqueness

The evaluation framework relies on a critical assumption: **that normalized exact match accurately reflects mathematical reasoning ability**. Section 3.1 describes extensive normalization rules to handle equivalent answer representations (e.g., `$\frac{1}{2}$` equals `$0.5$`, polynomial factor orderings are permitted). However, this approach cannot distinguish between:

- **Correct reasoning leading to correct answers** (ideal case)
- **Incorrect reasoning leading to correct answers** (lucky guesses or compensating errors)
- **Correct reasoning leading to incorrect answers** (arithmetic slips in otherwise sound derivations)

Figure 3 explicitly shows a case where the model generates a "misleading and wrong explanation" yet arrives at the right boxed answer. The paper acknowledges this but proceeds with exact match scoring anyway. This creates an **evaluation blind spot**: a model could theoretically achieve higher accuracy through pattern matching or memorization rather than genuine reasoning, and the benchmark would not detect it.

#### Log-Linear Scaling Extrapolation Assumes Trend Continuity

The claim that `$10^{35}$` parameters would be needed for 40% accuracy (Abstract, Section 1) rests on a strong assumption: **that log-linear scaling trends observed from 0.1B to 175B parameters will continue unchanged**. This extrapolation spans over 30 orders of magnitude beyond the largest model tested. The paper itself notes this is "assuming scaling trends continue" (Abstract), but does not deeply examine whether:

- Scaling laws might exhibit phase transitions at larger sizes
- Architectural innovations could change the scaling curve
- The relationship between parameters and reasoning ability might be non-monotonic

While the rhetorical point (scaling alone is impractical) is valid, the specific `$10^{35}$` figure should be treated as illustrative rather than predictive.

#### Autoregressive Generation Assumes Sequential Reasoning is Sufficient

The entire experimental setup assumes that **autoregressive token generation is an appropriate paradigm for mathematical problem solving**. Section 4.1 states: "Because MATH answers must be generated, we use autoregressive language models." However, this conflates two distinct capabilities:

1. **Expressing** a solution sequentially (which autoregressive models do)
2. **Constructing** a solution through iterative reasoning (which may require non-sequential operations)

Human mathematicians often work backward from the desired answer, revise intermediate steps, or maintain multiple reasoning branches simultaneously. The autoregressive constraint forces models to commit to each token without backtracking, which may fundamentally limit reasoning capability regardless of parameter count. The paper does not explore alternative generation paradigms (e.g., draft-and-revise, tree search, or verification loops).

#### Competition Problems Assume Representative Difficulty Distribution

MATH sources problems from AMC 10, AMC 12, AIME, and other competitions (Section 3.1). The assumption is that **competition mathematics adequately represents general mathematical reasoning ability**. However:

- Competition problems are specifically designed to be solvable within time limits using specific heuristics
- They may emphasize clever tricks over systematic reasoning
- Real-world mathematical problems (e.g., in science or engineering) often have different structures

The benchmark measures competition math performance, which is valuable but not necessarily equivalent to broader mathematical reasoning capability.

### 6.2 Scenarios and Edge Cases Not Addressed

#### Multi-Modal Reasoning Beyond Asymptote Code

While the paper innovates by encoding geometry in Asymptote code (Section 3.1, Figure 7), this approach has limitations:

- **Only text-based diagram specification is supported**: Actual raster images, hand-drawn diagrams, or complex visual representations are not included
- **Models must learn Asymptote syntax**: This adds a secondary language learning burden unrelated to mathematical reasoning
- **Limited diagram complexity**: The examples shown (Figure 7) are relatively simple geometric constructions

The paper claims this "enables pure language models to process figures, diagrams, and graphics" (Section 3.1), but this is only true for diagrams that can be concisely specified in code. Many real-world mathematical visualizations (e.g., complex function plots, statistical graphs, 3D surfaces) would be difficult to encode this way.

#### Interactive and Iterative Problem Solving

The evaluation framework assumes **single-pass generation**: the model receives a problem and produces an answer (with optional intermediate steps). Several important scenarios are not addressed:

- **Clarification requests**: What if the problem statement is ambiguous?
- **Tool use**: Could models benefit from external calculators or computer algebra systems?
- **Multiple attempts**: Competition mathematicians often try multiple approaches; the benchmark evaluates only the first generated answer
- **Feedback loops**: Could models improve if given hints about where their reasoning went wrong?

Section 4.3 shows that providing 99% of a ground truth solution yields only 40% accuracy (Figure 5), suggesting models struggle even with near-complete guidance. However, the paper does not explore whether iterative refinement or interactive hinting could bridge this gap.

#### Verification and Error Correction Mechanisms

A critical missing capability is **self-verification**. The "snowballing effect" hypothesis (Section 4.3) suggests that early errors corrupt subsequent generation, but the paper does not test:

- Whether models can detect errors in their own generated solutions
- Whether prompting models to "check your work" improves accuracy
- Whether training on error-correction examples helps

The AUROC of 68.8% for confidence-based error detection (Section 4.2) shows models cannot reliably identify their own mistakes. However, no experiments test whether explicit verification training could improve this.

#### Ensemble and Multiple-Strategy Approaches

Competition mathematicians often try multiple solution strategies. The benchmark evaluates **single-model, single-pass performance**:

- No ensemble results are reported (combining multiple model outputs)
- No majority voting or consensus mechanisms are tested
- No exploration of whether different models excel at different subjects

Table 2 shows substantial subject variation (e.g., Geometry 8.7% vs. Number Theory 4.8% for GPT-2 1.5B), suggesting specialized models or ensembles could improve performance. This remains unexplored.

### 6.3 Computational, Data, and Scalability Constraints

#### GPT-3 API Limits Confound Model Comparisons

A significant methodological limitation affects the GPT-3 results in Table 2: **GPT-3 models cannot pretrain on AMPS due to API limits** (Section 4.1). This creates a confound:

| Model | Parameters | AMPS Pretrained | Accuracy |
|-------|------------|-----------------|----------|
| GPT-2 | 0.1B | Yes | 5.4% |
| GPT-3 | 13B | No | 3.0% (few-shot) / 5.6% (fine-tuned) |
| GPT-3 | 175B | No | 5.2% (few-shot) |

The paper states: "GPT-3 models do not pretrain on AMPS due to API limits" (Table 2 caption). This means the comparison between GPT-2 and GPT-3 conflates **architecture/pretraining differences** with **parameter count differences**. We cannot determine whether GPT-3's relatively poor performance (175B at 5.2% vs. GPT-2 1.5B at 6.9%) is due to:

- Lack of AMPS pretraining
- Architectural differences
- True scaling limitations

The AMPS ablation (0.1B + AMPS ≈ 13B without AMPS) is compelling, but the GPT-3 comparison is weakened by this constraint.

#### AMPS Distributional Differences from MATH

AMPS contains 5 million Mathematica-generated problems and 100,000 Khan Academy exercises (Section 3.2), while MATH contains 12,500 competition problems. There are **distributional mismatches**:

- **Problem style**: AMPS problems are more formulaic and routine; MATH problems require heuristic selection
- **Difficulty**: AMPS covers K-12 fundamentals; MATH covers competition-level challenges
- **Solution structure**: Only 37 of 100 Mathematica scripts generate step-by-step solutions (Table 1)

The paper assumes that pretraining on AMPS fundamentals transfers to MATH competition problems. While the ablation shows AMPS helps (Section 4.2), the **transfer gap** between routine exercises and competition heuristics is not quantified. Models may learn mathematical syntax without learning problem-solving strategies.

#### Resource Requirements Limit Accessibility

The experimental setup requires significant computational resources:

- **8 A100 GPUs** for training (Section 4.1)
- **23GB of pretraining data** (AMPS)
- **Less than one day per model** for training

While the paper states this is feasible, it still represents a barrier for researchers without access to high-end GPU clusters. The GPT-3 API constraints further limit who can reproduce certain experiments. The paper notes code is available at github.com/hendrycks/apps (Appendix B), but full reproducibility requires substantial infrastructure.

#### Mathematica Script Coverage May Be Incomplete

The 100 Mathematica scripts cover specific topics (Table 1), but the paper does not claim comprehensive coverage of all mathematical fundamentals. Appendix Figures 12-15 list 693 Khan Academy modules, but only 100 Mathematica scripts supplement these. **Topic gaps** in AMPS could limit model learning in certain areas, potentially explaining subject-specific performance variations in Table 2 (e.g., Number Theory underperformance).

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

#### Why Does Inference-Time Solution Generation Hurt Performance?

The paper identifies that generating step-by-step solutions at inference **decreases accuracy** (6.9% → 5.3% for GPT-2 1.5B, Section 4.3), but the explanation remains speculative:

> "We hypothesize that the drop in accuracy from using scratch space arises from a snowballing effect, in which partially generated 'solutions' with mistakes can derail subsequent generated text." (Section 4.3)

This is not experimentally verified. Alternative explanations are not explored:

- Could the prompt format (`⟨P⟩Full Solution:`) confuse models trained on mixed formats?
- Do models allocate attention differently when generating solutions vs. answers?
- Is the beam size difference (10 for solutions vs. 20 for answers) a confounding factor?

The paper identifies an important phenomenon but does not fully explain the mechanism, leaving this as an **open research question**.

#### What Specific Algorithmic Advancements Are Needed?

The conclusion states: "we will need new algorithmic advancements from the broader research community" (Abstract, Section 1). However, the paper **does not propose specific directions**:

- Should models incorporate symbolic reasoning modules?
- Should verification steps be added to the generation process?
- Should architectures support non-autoregressive operations?
- Should training include explicit error-correction objectives?

This is a reasonable position for a benchmark paper, but it leaves practitioners without concrete guidance on how to improve beyond scaling. The paper signals a problem without suggesting solutions.

#### Small Human Baseline Sample

The human evaluation includes only **six participants**, all university computer science students (Section 3.1, Appendix A.6):

| Participant | Score |
|-------------|-------|
| CS PhD (dislikes math) | 40% |
| Ambivalent | 65% |
| Likes math (2 participants) | 70%, 75% |
| AMC 10 perfect + USAMO | 90% |
| IMO gold medalist (3×) | 90% |

While the range (40% to 90%) is informative, the small sample size limits statistical confidence. Additionally:

- All participants are from similar backgrounds (CS students)
- Time constraints (1 hour for 20 problems) may underestimate true capability
- The IMO gold medalist missed questions "exclusively due to small errors of arithmetic" under time pressure

The paper acknowledges this is "a rough but informative comparison" (Section 3.1), but readers should treat human baselines as approximate rather than definitive.

#### No Variance Reporting Across Training Runs

Table 2 reports single accuracy values for each model configuration. The paper **does not report variance** across multiple training runs with different random seeds. Given the small absolute differences between models (e.g., 6.2% vs. 6.4% for GPT-2 0.3B vs. 0.7B), some results may be within the noise range. Without variance reporting, it is difficult to determine whether observed differences are statistically significant.

#### Correct-Answer-Wrong-Reasoning Cannot Be Detected

As noted in Section 6.1, the exact match evaluation cannot distinguish correct answers derived from flawed reasoning. This creates a **measurement validity concern**:

- A model could memorize answer patterns for common problem types
- A model could guess correctly through partial reasoning
- The benchmark would credit these as successful reasoning

The step-by-step solutions enable qualitative analysis (Figures 3, 4), but the primary metric (exact match accuracy) does not penalize incorrect derivations. This could inflate apparent performance relative to genuine reasoning capability.

#### Limited Exploration of Alternative Architectures

The paper tests GPT-2, GPT-3, and BART (Appendix A.5), but does not explore:

- **Neural-symbolic hybrids** that combine neural networks with symbolic computation
- **Retrieval-augmented models** that could access mathematical formula databases
- **Chain-of-thought variants** with explicit verification steps
- **Non-Transformer architectures** (e.g., neural Turing machines, differentiable computers)

The conclusion that "new algorithmic advancements" are needed (Abstract) is supported, but the paper does not test whether existing alternative architectures might already offer improvements over standard Transformers.

### 6.5 Summary of Limitations

| Category | Limitation | Impact |
|----------|------------|--------|
| **Evaluation** | Exact match cannot detect wrong reasoning | May overestimate reasoning capability |
| **Scaling** | `$10^{35}$` extrapolation assumes trend continuity | Specific number is illustrative, not predictive |
| **Architecture** | Autoregressive generation prevents backtracking | May fundamentally limit reasoning |
| **Data** | GPT-3 cannot pretrain on AMPS | Confounds model comparisons |
| **Data** | AMPS-MATH distributional mismatch | Transfer gap not quantified |
| **Human Baseline** | Only 6 participants | Limited statistical confidence |
| **Variance** | No multiple-run reporting | Uncertain significance of small differences |
| **Mechanism** | Snowballing effect not experimentally verified | Explanation remains speculative |
| **Guidance** | No specific algorithmic directions proposed | Leaves practitioners without concrete next steps |
| **Scope** | Competition math may not represent all reasoning | Benchmark scope is narrower than claimed |

These limitations do not invalidate the paper's contributions but should inform how the research community interprets and builds upon this work. The MATH benchmark successfully identifies current limitations in mathematical reasoning, but addressing those limitations will require careful consideration of the constraints and assumptions outlined above.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than introduce a new benchmark—it fundamentally reframes how the machine learning community should think about mathematical reasoning and the limits of scaling. This section examines how the MATH dataset changes the research landscape, what specific follow-up work it enables, practical applications beyond benchmarking, and guidance for researchers who want to build on this foundation.

### 7.1 How This Work Changes the Field

#### Shifting from Scaling to Algorithmic Innovation

The most significant landscape change is the **explicit challenge to scaling-only strategies**. Before MATH, the prevailing assumption (supported by Kaplan et al., 2020; Henighan et al., 2020) was that most NLP tasks would eventually be solved by increasing model size and compute. The paper's extrapolation that $10^{35}$ parameters would be needed for 40% accuracy (Abstract, Section 1) serves as a concrete signal that this assumption does not hold for mathematical reasoning.

This creates a **new research paradigm**:

| Before MATH | After MATH |
|-------------|------------|
| Focus on parameter count and compute budgets | Focus on reasoning architectures and verification |
| Benchmarks expected to be solved via scaling | Benchmarks designed to resist scaling |
| Data diversity prioritized over curation | Domain-specific curation shown to outperform raw scale |
| Inference-time generation assumed beneficial | Training-inference discrepancy revealed as critical gap |

The paper positions MATH not as a task to be conquered through brute force, but as a **diagnostic tool** that reveals specific capability gaps. This is a fundamental shift in benchmark philosophy—most benchmarks are created to measure progress toward solving them, while MATH is designed to measure how far current approaches fall short.

#### Establishing Data Curation as a First-Class Research Direction

The AMPS ablation study (Section 4.2) demonstrates that a 0.1B parameter model with AMPS pretraining matches a 13B model without it. This finding elevates **data curation** from a preprocessing step to a core research contribution. The implication is clear:

> "Pretraining on AMPS enables a 0.1 billion parameter model to perform comparably to a fine-tuned model that is 130× larger." (Abstract)

For resource-constrained researchers, this opens a viable path to competitive performance without requiring massive compute budgets. The field can now investigate:
- What makes mathematical data "high quality" versus "noisy"?
- How do step-by-step solutions specifically contribute to learning?
- Can similar curation strategies work for other specialized domains (law, medicine, science)?

The StackExchange ablation (6.0% with StackExchange vs. 6.2% without) further reinforces that **more data is not always better**—curated, clean data outperforms diverse, real-world data for this task.

#### Revealing the Training-Inference Gap as a Fundamental Limitation

The "scratch space paradox" (Section 4.1, 4.3) is perhaps the most surprising finding: models learn from step-by-step solutions during training but cannot use their own generated solutions at inference. This reveals a **fundamental asymmetry** in current autoregressive architectures:

- **Training**: Models see correct reasoning chains and learn to associate them with answers
- **Inference**: Models must construct reasoning chains from scratch, and early errors propagate

This gap had not been systematically measured before MATH because prior benchmarks either:
1. Did not include step-by-step solutions (DeepMind Mathematics)
2. Used formal languages where errors are caught immediately (theorem provers)
3. Were easy enough that direct answer generation sufficed

MATH makes this gap visible and measurable, creating a new research target: **enabling models to use their own generated text as reliable scratch space**.

### 7.2 Specific Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions. Below we expand these into concrete research programs with specific hypotheses and experimental designs.

#### Direction 1: Verification and Error Correction Mechanisms

**Problem**: The AUROC of 68.8% for confidence-based error detection (Section 4.2) shows models cannot reliably identify their own mistakes. The "snowballing effect" hypothesis suggests early errors corrupt subsequent generation.

**Research Questions**:
- Can models be trained to verify their own intermediate steps before proceeding?
- Does explicit error-correction training improve inference-time accuracy?
- Can confidence calibration be improved through specialized training objectives?

**Suggested Experiments**:
1. Train models on pairs of (incorrect solution, corrected solution) to learn error detection
2. Add a verification prompt at intermediate steps: "Check if this step is correct before continuing"
3. Compare beam search vs. sampling strategies for reducing error propagation
4. Measure AUROC improvements after verification training

**Why This Matters**: If models can detect and correct their own errors, the inference-time accuracy drop from generating solutions (6.9% → 5.3%) could be reversed. This would unlock the benefit of scratch space without the snowballing cost.

#### Direction 2: Non-Autoregressive and Iterative Reasoning

**Problem**: The autoregressive constraint forces models to commit to each token without backtracking (Section 6.1). Human mathematicians revise, work backward, and maintain multiple reasoning branches.

**Research Questions**:
- Do draft-and-revise architectures improve mathematical reasoning?
- Can tree search methods (like AlphaZero-style search) be applied to solution generation?
- Would models benefit from generating multiple solution attempts and selecting the best?

**Suggested Experiments**:
1. Implement a two-stage pipeline: generate multiple solutions, then vote or verify
2. Test models with explicit backtracking capabilities (e.g., scratchpad with edit operations)
3. Compare single-pass vs. multi-pass generation on MATH
4. Explore reinforcement learning with reward signals for correct intermediate steps

**Why This Matters**: The $10^{35}$ parameter extrapolation assumes current architectures. If architectural changes improve the scaling curve, the extrapolation becomes obsolete. This direction tests whether the limitation is fundamental or architectural.

#### Direction 3: Neural-Symbolic Hybrids

**Problem**: Pure neural models struggle with exact symbolic manipulation (e.g., algebraic simplification, modular arithmetic). Number Theory is the lowest-performing subject across all model sizes (Table 2: 4.4% to 5.0%).

**Research Questions**:
- Can external computer algebra systems be integrated into the generation process?
- Would neural models benefit from calling symbolic tools for specific subtasks?
- Can symbolic constraints be enforced during generation (e.g., type checking for mathematical expressions)?

**Suggested Experiments**:
1. Train models to generate tool calls (e.g., `simplify(expression)`, `solve(equation)`)
2. Compare pure neural vs. neural+tool architectures on MATH
3. Test whether symbolic verification of generated steps improves accuracy
4. Measure performance on Number Theory specifically (where symbolic reasoning is most critical)

**Why This Matters**: The AMPS corpus includes Mathematica-generated problems, suggesting symbolic computation is valuable. Integrating actual symbolic tools could bridge the gap between neural pattern matching and exact mathematical reasoning.

#### Direction 4: Improved Pretraining Strategies

**Problem**: AMPS helps significantly, but there remains a large gap between AMPS-pretrained models and human performance. The transfer from routine exercises (AMPS) to competition heuristics (MATH) is incomplete.

**Research Questions**:
- What pretraining curriculum best prepares models for competition mathematics?
- Should pretraining include more problem-solving heuristics vs. routine calculations?
- Can curriculum learning (easy → hard problems) improve transfer?

**Suggested Experiments**:
1. Create a graded pretraining corpus with explicit difficulty progression
2. Add more competition-style problems to pretraining (not just K-12 exercises)
3. Test whether pretraining on proof explanations improves heuristic selection
4. Measure subject-specific pretraining effects (e.g., Number Theory-focused pretraining)

**Why This Matters**: The 130× parameter equivalence from AMPS pretraining shows data matters. Optimizing pretraining could yield further gains without architectural changes.

#### Direction 5: Cross-Domain Reasoning Transfer

**Problem**: MATH measures mathematical reasoning specifically. It is unclear whether improvements on MATH transfer to other reasoning domains (logic, science, programming).

**Research Questions**:
- Do models that improve on MATH also improve on LogiQA, GSM8K, or code generation?
- Is mathematical reasoning a bottleneck for general reasoning capability?
- Can techniques developed for MATH be applied to other domains?

**Suggested Experiments**:
1. Evaluate MATH-improved models on other reasoning benchmarks
2. Test whether MATH pretraining helps downstream tasks in science or engineering
3. Compare subject-specific improvements (does Geometry improvement transfer to spatial reasoning tasks?)
4. Measure correlation between MATH accuracy and real-world mathematical task performance

**Why This Matters**: If MATH improvements generalize, it validates mathematics as a proxy for general reasoning. If not, MATH remains a specialized benchmark with limited broader implications.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

Beyond benchmarking, the MATH dataset and AMPS corpus enable several practical applications:

#### Educational Technology and Tutoring Systems

The step-by-step solutions in MATH and AMPS are directly applicable to **automated tutoring systems**:

- **Problem Generation**: AMPS's Mathematica scripts can generate unlimited practice problems with verified solutions
- **Solution Explanation**: Models trained on MATH solutions can explain reasoning to students
- **Error Diagnosis**: Models could identify where students make mistakes in their derivations
- **Adaptive Difficulty**: The 1-5 difficulty tagging enables personalized problem selection

**Integration Note**: The paper explicitly states: "We do not intend for this dataset to train models that help students cheat on mathematics exams" (Appendix B). Researchers building educational tools should implement safeguards (e.g., not providing direct answers on homework problems).

#### Mathematical Software Assistance

Professional mathematicians and scientists could benefit from **reasoning assistance tools**:

- **Proof Sketching**: Models could suggest intermediate steps in proofs
- **Formula Verification**: Check whether derivations are mathematically sound
- **Literature Search**: Connect problems to relevant theorems or techniques
- **Computation Verification**: Cross-check symbolic computations

**Current Limitation**: The 6.9% accuracy on MATH means such tools are not yet reliable for professional use. However, the AMPS pretraining strategy could be applied to domain-specific corpora (e.g., physics, engineering) for more narrow, reliable applications.

#### Benchmarking for Specialized Domains

The MATH/AMPS construction methodology can be **replicated for other domains**:

| Domain | Evaluation Benchmark | Pretraining Corpus |
|--------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Law | Case analysis problems | Legal textbooks + statutes |
| Medicine | Diagnostic reasoning | Medical literature + case studies |
| Science | Problem-solving questions | Textbooks + research papers |
| Programming | Algorithm challenges | Code repositories + explanations |

**Key Design Principles** (from MATH):
1. Include step-by-step solutions for training
2. Use exact-match evaluation where possible (avoid BLEU for structured outputs)
3. Tag problems by difficulty and subject for fine-grained analysis
4. Create large pretraining corpora specific to the domain
5. Design problems that require heuristic selection, not just pattern matching

#### Data Curation as a Service

The AMPS construction demonstrates that **curated synthetic data** can outperform raw web data. This has commercial implications:

- Companies could offer domain-specific pretraining corpora
- Data quality assessment could become a distinct service
- Synthetic data generation (like the Mathematica scripts) could be productized

**Practical Note**: The AMPS code is available at github.com/hendrycks/apps (Appendix B) under MIT license, enabling researchers to regenerate the corpus or adapt the Mathematica scripts for new domains.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

For researchers who want to use MATH and AMPS in their own work, we provide the following practical guidance:

#### When to Use MATH vs. Other Benchmarks

| Use MATH When | Use Other Benchmarks When |
|---------------|--------------------------|
| Measuring genuine problem-solving ability | Measuring calculation or pattern matching |
| Testing resistance to scaling | Testing scaling improvements |
| Evaluating step-by-step reasoning | Evaluating direct answer generation |
| Researching mathematical reasoning | Researching general NLP capabilities |
| Domain-specific pretraining studies | General language understanding studies |

**Recommendation**: Use MATH as a **complementary benchmark** alongside easier tasks (e.g., GSM8K for grade-school math, DeepMind Mathematics for calculations). MATH's difficulty means it should not be the only benchmark, but it provides unique signal about reasoning capabilities that easier benchmarks cannot.

#### How to Incorporate AMPS Pretraining

The paper's pretraining setup (Section 4.1) provides a template:

```
Pretraining Configuration:
- Objective: Autoregressive language modeling
- Optimizer: AdamW, weight decay 0.05
- Batch size: 128
- Epochs: 1
- Data balancing: Khan Academy upsampled 5×, Mathematica downsampled 2×
- Hardware: 8 A100 GPUs, <1 day per model
```

**Practical Tips**:
1. **Tokenization**: Use a tokenizer that preserves LaTeX symbols (T5's tokenizer removes them, per Section 4.1)
2. **Number Tokenization**: Tokenize digits individually rather than as whole numbers (following Henighan et al., 2020)
3. **Fine-tuning Mix**: Use equal mix of answer-only and solution-full sequences for flexibility
4. **Beam Search**: Use beam size 20 for answer-only, beam size 10 for solution generation

**When AMPS Pretraining May Not Help**:
- If your model is already pretrained on large mathematics corpora
- If your task does not involve LaTeX or mathematical notation
- If you are using API-based models (like GPT-3) that cannot accept custom pretraining

#### Evaluation Best Practices

The paper's evaluation framework (Section 3.1) should be followed for comparability:

1. **Extract boxed answers**: Parse content inside `\boxed{}` commands
2. **Apply normalization rules**: Handle equivalent representations (fractions, polynomials, etc.)
3. **Use exact match**: Do not use BLEU or other heuristic metrics for mathematical answers
4. **Report subject and difficulty breakdowns**: Average accuracy masks important variation (Table 2, Figure 9)

**Common Pitfalls to Avoid**:
- Do not evaluate on the MATH training set (7,500 problems)—use the 5,000 test problems
- Do not skip normalization—equivalent answers will be marked incorrect
- Do not compare to GPT-3 few-shot results without noting the AMPS pretraining confound
- Do not interpret small accuracy differences (&lt;1%) as significant without variance reporting

#### Integration with Existing Frameworks

The MATH dataset is compatible with standard NLP libraries:

- **HuggingFace Transformers**: Models can be fine-tuned using standard `Trainer` classes
- **Datasets Library**: MATH and AMPS can be loaded via the `datasets` library (available at github.com/hendrycks/apps)
- **Evaluation Scripts**: The paper provides evaluation code that implements the normalization rules

**Recommended Workflow**:
1. Download MATH and AMPS from the repository
2. Pretrain on AMPS (or use AMPS-pretrained checkpoints if available)
3. Fine-tune on MATH training set with mixed answer/solution format
4. Evaluate on MATH test set using provided evaluation scripts
5. Report results with subject and difficulty breakdowns for comparability

### 7.5 Summary: A Research Agenda for Mathematical Reasoning

The MATH paper does not claim to solve mathematical reasoning—it claims to **measure it properly** and **identify what is missing**. The implications extend beyond this specific benchmark:

| Insight | Research Direction | Expected Outcome |
|---------|-------------------|------------------|
| Scaling is insufficient | Develop new reasoning architectures | Models that improve faster than scaling predicts |
| Scratch space paradox | Build verification mechanisms | Models that can use generated solutions reliably |
| AMPS efficiency | Optimize domain-specific pretraining | Smaller models with specialized capabilities |
| Error detection gap | Train confidence calibration | Models that know when they are wrong |
| Symbolic manipulation weakness | Integrate neural-symbolic hybrids | Better performance on Number Theory and algebra |

The paper's conclusion is explicit: "To have more traction on mathematical problem solving we will likely need new algorithmic advancements from the broader research community" (Abstract). This is not a limitation of the paper—it is an **invitation**.

For researchers entering this space, the path forward is clear:

1. **Use MATH as your benchmark**—it measures what matters (reasoning, not calculation)
2. **Leverage AMPS for pretraining**—it provides mathematical fluency efficiently
3. **Focus on the training-inference gap**—this is where current models fail most clearly
4. **Report fine-grained results**—subject and difficulty breakdowns reveal specific weaknesses
5. **Consider non-scaling solutions**—architectural innovation, not just parameter count

The MATH dataset represents a **inflection point** in how the field approaches mathematical reasoning. Before MATH, the question was "How large must models be to solve math?" After MATH, the question is "What capabilities must models have to solve math?" This shift from quantification to qualification is the paper's most enduring contribution.
