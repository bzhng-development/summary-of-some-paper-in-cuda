## 1. Executive Summary

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) significantly boosts reasoning in Vision-Language Models (VLMs) like Qwen2.5-VL but induces catastrophic forgetting of general capabilities, where reasoning-tuned variants often underperform base models on perception and robustness tasks (Figure 1). To address this, the authors propose RECAP (Replay-Enhanced CApability Preservation), an end-to-end replay strategy that dynamically reweights objectives based on online convergence rates and instability signals, shifting optimization focus from saturated tasks to underperforming ones without auxiliary models. Extensive experiments on Qwen2.5-VL-3B and 7B demonstrate that RECAP preserves general skills (e.g., boosting LISA segmentation by >2% over the base model) while improving reasoning benchmarks (e.g., raising ScienceQA accuracy from 6.20 to 71.59 on the 3B model) and reducing unnecessary reasoning overhead.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of this paper, we must first establish the current landscape of post-training for large models, specifically the rise of **Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR)**, and the unintended side effects accompanying its adoption.

### The RLVR Paradigm and the Forgetting Gap

In recent years, the standard approach to aligning Large Language Models (LLMs) and Vision-Language Models (VLMs) has shifted from simple Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) to reinforcement learning-based methods. While **Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)** relies on learned reward models to judge helpfulness or harmlessness, **RLVR** utilizes programmatic, verifiable signals—such as exact-match correctness in math, code execution success, or strict adherence to output formats (Section 1). This paradigm has become dominant for reasoning tasks because it provides dense, objective feedback without the noise of human annotators.

However, the paper identifies a critical, under-explored failure mode in this pipeline: **capability regression**. When a model is optimized intensely for a narrow set of verifiable rewards (e.g., solving math problems), it tends to "forget" the broad, foundational skills acquired during pretraining, such as visual perception, OCR (Optical Character Recognition), and robustness to distribution shifts.

This phenomenon is empirically demonstrated in **Figure 1**, which compares base VLMs against their reasoning-tuned variants across six non-reasoning benchmarks. The visual evidence is stark:
*   **Perception Degradation**: Models like `Qwen2.5-VL-3B` and `Qwen2.5-VL-7B` show consistent performance drops on tasks like `VisOnly` (vision-only recognition) and `OCRBench` after RLVR tuning.
*   **Robustness Loss**: Performance on `R-Bench-Dis` (distribution-shift robustness) declines, suggesting the model becomes brittle outside its trained reasoning distribution.

> "Optimizing for reasoning rewards can erode non-reasoning capabilities, motivating a continual learning method to preserve general skills during reasoning-oriented post-training." (Section 1)

This creates a significant gap: we have models that are better at reasoning but worse at seeing and understanding the world generally. In real-world deployment, a VLM cannot afford to lose perception capabilities while gaining reasoning skills; both are required for reliable multimodal interaction.

### Limitations of Prior Mitigation Strategies

The machine learning community has long studied **catastrophic forgetting** in continual learning. The paper evaluates why standard solutions fail in the specific context of RLVR for large models.

#### 1. KL Divergence Regularization
In standard RLHF, a **Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence** penalty is added to the loss function to constrain the policy $\pi_\theta$ from deviating too far from a reference policy $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ (Section 3). The objective typically looks like:
$$
\max_{\theta} \mathbb{E}[r(x, y)] - \beta \mathbb{E}[D_{\text{KL}}(\pi_\theta(\cdot | x) \parallel \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot | x))]
$$
While this stabilizes training, the paper argues it is insufficient for preserving *general* knowledge for two reasons:
*   **Task-Specific Constraint**: The KL penalty is calculated on the *current* task data (the reasoning prompts). It ensures the model doesn't change its behavior *on those prompts* too drastically, but it does not guarantee retention of knowledge on *other* domains (e.g., perception tasks not present in the RL batch).
*   **Exploration Trade-off**: Recent reasoning-focused pipelines sometimes reduce or remove the KL penalty to encourage exploration and prevent mode collapse (Section 2). This exacerbates forgetting, as the policy is free to drift significantly from the pretrained weights.

#### 2. Static Experience Replay
A common technique to prevent forgetting is **experience replay**, where data from previous tasks (general capabilities) is mixed into the training batch alongside new task data (reasoning). However, the paper identifies a subtle but critical flaw in how this is typically implemented: **heterogeneous convergence**.

Not all objectives learn at the same speed. **Figure 4** illustrates this divergence during training:
*   **Format Rewards**: Signals like "follow the output template" are easy to optimize. They converge rapidly (high convergence rate $c_k$) and become stable (low instability $i_k$) within ~50 steps.
*   **Reasoning Accuracy**: Signals like "solve the math problem correctly" are noisy and hard. They fluctuate significantly and converge much slower.

If a practitioner uses **static weighting** (e.g., giving equal weight to format, accuracy, and perception losses), the optimizer will effectively ignore the saturated format signal but may still overfit on it, or conversely, under-prioritize the hard reasoning signal. As noted in Section 1, "commonly used experience replay across heterogeneous domains makes it nontrivial to decide how much training focus each objective should receive." Manual tuning of these weights is computationally expensive and does not generalize across different training stages.

### Positioning of RECAP

This paper positions **RECAP (Replay-Enhanced CApability Preservation)** not as a new architecture or a replacement for RLVR, but as a **plug-in scheduler** that sits on top of existing RL pipelines (like GRPO or PPO).

Unlike prior work that relies on static mixtures or external regularization models, RECAP introduces **dynamic objective reweighting** based on online signals. It treats the training process as a multi-objective optimization problem where the weights $\lambda_k$ for each loss term $L_k$ are updated at every step $t$ based on:
1.  **Convergence Rate ($c_k$)**: How fast is the loss dropping relative to the past?
2.  **Instability ($i_k$)**: How much variance is there in the loss signal?

By mathematically coupling these signals (Section 4), RECAP automatically down-weights objectives that have saturated (like format compliance) and shifts computational focus toward objectives that are still improving or are volatile (like reasoning accuracy or perception retention).

This approach addresses the core motivation of the paper: enabling models to gain reasoning capabilities **without** trading off foundational skills. It bridges the gap between specialized reasoning performance and generalist utility, ensuring that the "reasoning gain" does not come at the cost of "perception pain."

## 3. Technical Approach

This section dissects the mechanics of **RECAP (Replay-Enhanced CApability Preservation)**. While the previous section established *why* dynamic reweighting is necessary due to heterogeneous convergence rates, this section explains *how* the system computes those weights in real-time without human intervention. We will walk through the mathematical formulation of the convergence signals, the normalization strategy, and the integration into the reinforcement learning objective.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
RECAP is a training scheduler that automatically adjusts the importance of different learning tasks (like math reasoning vs. image recognition) at every step of the training process. It solves the problem of manual weight tuning by measuring how fast each task is learning and how unstable its progress is, then shifting focus to the tasks that need it most.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The system operates as a feedback loop embedded within the standard training pipeline. First, the model processes a mixed batch of data containing both reasoning tasks (e.g., math problems) and general capability tasks (e.g., object detection). Second, the system calculates the loss for each specific objective (e.g., accuracy loss, format loss, IoU loss) independently. Third, a statistics module aggregates these losses over a short sliding window to compute two signals for each objective: a convergence rate (is the loss dropping?) and an instability score (is the loss jumping around?). Fourth, a reweighting module converts these signals into priority scores and normalizes them into final weights using a softmax function. Finally, these dynamic weights are applied to the individual losses to compute a single aggregated loss value, which is then used to update the model parameters via backpropagation.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Problem Formalization:** We define the multi-domain, multi-objective setting to establish the notation for losses and domains.
*   **Signal Estimation:** We explain how the system measures convergence and instability using sliding windows to filter out RL noise.
*   **Weight Computation:** We detail the transformation of raw signals into normalized loss weights using temperature-controlled softmax.
*   **Objective Integration:** We show how these weights modify the final training objective and discuss the specific RL algorithm (GRPO) used.
*   **Design Rationale:** We analyze why specific choices (like summing signals instead of multiplying) were made based on empirical observations.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown
This paper proposes a methodological adjustment to the optimization process rather than a new model architecture, functioning as a plug-in module for existing Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) pipelines. The core innovation lies in replacing static loss coefficients with dynamic weights $\lambda_k^{(t)}$ that evolve at every training step $t$ based on the observed behavior of each objective $k$.

#### Problem Formalization and Data Setup
The method operates in a setting where training data is drawn from $N$ distinct domains, denoted as $\mathcal{D} = \{\mathcal{D}_n\}_{n=1}^N$. These domains are heterogeneous, meaning they may require different evaluation metrics; for example, a reasoning domain uses accuracy, while a perception domain uses Intersection over Union (IoU). Within these domains, there are $K$ total objectives, where $K \ge N$ because a single domain might optimize multiple signals (e.g., a reasoning task might have both an accuracy reward and a format compliance reward). At any iteration $t$, the system computes the mini-batch loss $\ell_{n,k}^{(t)}(\theta)$ for objective $k$ on domain $n$ given model parameters $\theta$. To simplify the reweighting logic, the system first averages the loss for each objective across all domains that contribute to it, defined as:
$$
L_k^{(t)} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N \ell_{n,k}^{(t)}(\theta)
$$
This aggregation ensures that the reweighting mechanism acts on the global performance of an objective (e.g., "how well is the model doing on format compliance overall?") rather than being skewed by domain-specific batch variations. The framework is agnostic to whether $L_k$ comes from a supervised learning loss (like next-token prediction) or an RL reward surrogate (like correctness), allowing it to function in the "Hybrid Setting" described in Section 5.1 where both SFT and RL objectives are mixed.

#### Signal Estimation: Convergence and Instability
A critical design choice in RECAP is the rejection of per-step loss values for weight calculation. As illustrated in **Figure 11** (Appendix A.6), RL signals are inherently high-variance and non-stationary; a single step's reward might spike or drop due to sampling noise rather than genuine learning progress. To mitigate this, the system employs a sliding window of length $2 \times W$ to estimate stable statistics. The window is split into two halves: a "current" window covering steps $[t-W+1, t]$ and a "previous" window covering steps $[t-2W+1, t-W]$.

The system computes the estimated current loss value $\mu_k^{(t)}$ by averaging the losses in the current window:
$$
\mu_k^{(t)} = \frac{1}{W} \sum_{s=t-W+1}^{t} L_k^{(s)}
$$
Simultaneously, it computes the estimated old loss value $\tilde{\mu}_k^{(t)}$ using the previous window:
$$
\tilde{\mu}_k^{(t)} = \frac{1}{W} \sum_{s=t-2W+1}^{t-W} L_k^{(s)}
$$
These two averages allow the system to measure the **convergence rate** $c_k^{(t)}$, which captures how fast the loss is improving relative to the recent past. The ratio is defined as:
$$
c_k^{(t)} = \mu_k^{(t)} / \tilde{\mu}_k^{(t)}
$$
In this formulation, a value of $c_k^{(t)} &lt; 1$ indicates improvement (since the current loss is lower than the old loss), while $c_k^{(t)} \approx 1$ signals saturation. However, convergence speed alone is insufficient because some objectives (like reasoning accuracy) remain inherently noisy even when learning. Therefore, the system also measures **instability** using the coefficient of variation within the current window. It calculates the standard deviation $\sigma_k^{(t)}$ over the current window samples:
$$
\sigma_k^{(t)} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{W-1} \sum_{s=t-W+1}^{t} \left(L_k^{(s)} - \mu_k^{(t)}\right)^2}
$$
To make this instability metric comparable across objectives with different loss magnitudes, it is normalized by the sum of the current and old loss averages, creating the inverse signal-to-noise ratio $i_k^{(t)}$:
$$
i_k^{(t)} = \sigma_k^{(t)} / (\mu_k^{(t)} + \tilde{\mu}_k^{(t)})
$$
This specific normalization ensures that an objective with high variance relative to its mean receives a higher instability score, signaling to the optimizer that this objective requires more attention to stabilize.

#### Weight Computation via Priority Softmax
Once the convergence rate $c_k^{(t)}$ and instability $i_k^{(t)}$ are computed for all $K$ objectives, the system must convert these raw signals into usable loss weights. The authors combine these signals into a single priority score $s_k^{(t)}$ by simply summing them:
$$
s_k^{(t)} = c_k^{(t)} + i_k^{(t)}
$$
This additive combination is a deliberate design choice justified in Section 4. As shown in **Figure 4**, different objectives dominate at different training stages: format rewards converge quickly (low $c$, low $i$) while reasoning accuracy fluctuates (high $i$). By summing them, the system ensures that an objective is prioritized if it is either failing to converge (high $c$) or is too unstable (high $i$). The paper notes that while finetuning the trade-off between these terms is possible, the unweighted sum performs consistently well without requiring hyperparameter tuning for the combination itself.

To transform these priority scores into weights $\lambda_k^{(t)}$ that sum to a consistent scale, RECAP uses a temperature-controlled softmax function. The weight for objective $k$ at step $t$ is calculated as:
$$
\lambda_k^{(t)} = \frac{K \exp\left(s_k^{(t)} / T\right)}{\sum_{i=1}^K \exp\left(s_i^{(t)} / T\right)}
$$
Here, $T$ is a temperature hyperparameter that controls the sharpness of the distribution. A lower $T$ would make the weights more extreme (focusing heavily on the highest priority objective), while a higher $T$ approaches uniform mixing. The authors set $T=5$ by default in their experiments. A crucial detail in this equation is the prefactor $K$. In a standard softmax, the outputs sum to 1. However, if all objectives were weighted equally in a baseline scenario, each would typically have a weight of 1 (or $1/K$ depending on normalization). By multiplying by $K$, the authors ensure that the average weight remains 1 (i.e., $\frac{1}{K} \sum \lambda_k^{(t)} = 1$). This preserves the magnitude of the total loss, preventing the need to retune the global learning rate when activating RECAP.

#### Overall Training Objective
The final loss function minimized at step $t$ is the weighted sum of the individual objective losses:
$$
\mathcal{L}^{(t)}(\theta) = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^K \lambda_k^{(t)} L_k^{(t)}
$$
Optimizing $\theta$ with the gradient $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}^{(t)}$ effectively steers learning towards objectives that are both slow-to-converge and fluctuated, while assigning lower weight to well-learned, stable objectives. This mechanism reduces to standard equal weighting as $T \to \infty$, providing a smooth fallback behavior. In the context of the RL algorithm used, **Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)**, this loss structure integrates directly with the policy gradient update. GRPO computes advantages from group-normalized sequence rewards without a critic model, making it memory-efficient for long chain-of-thought reasoning. RECAP does not alter the GRPO advantage calculation itself but scales the resulting loss terms before the backward pass.

#### Design Choices and Integration Details
The decision to use an online, short-horizon estimation strategy addresses the non-stationarity of RL training. As noted in Section 4, relying on full-epoch metrics would be too slow to react to the rapid saturation of format rewards (which can happen within 50 steps, per **Figure 4**). Conversely, per-step metrics are too noisy. The sliding window $W$ strikes a balance, though the exact integer value of $W$ is treated as an internal buffer size dependent on the step frequency.

Another significant design choice is the handling of **format rewards** across different domains. In ablation studies (Section 5.3), the authors found that applying a uniform "thinking reward" (encouraging long chain-of-thought) to perception tasks was suboptimal. For perception domains (like segmentation or OCR), the model quickly learns that long rationales are unnecessary, causing the thinking length to drop from hundreds of characters to near zero (**Figure 6**). Consequently, in the Hybrid Setting, RECAP is configured to keep answer-format rewards for perception domains (no thinking) while reserving thinking rewards for tasks that truly benefit from step-by-step reasoning. This prevents the model from wasting capacity generating unnecessary text on simple visual tasks, which improves inference efficiency by reducing token count by approximately 60% on reasoning tasks without sacrificing accuracy (**Figure 7**).

Finally, the method is designed to be **magnitude-agnostic**. Because the convergence rate is a ratio and the instability is a coefficient of variation, the system does not require manual normalization of loss scales (e.g., ensuring the IOU loss is on the same scale as the cross-entropy loss). This allows RECAP to drop into existing RLVR pipelines without the heavy tuning typically required for multi-task learning weights. The authors explicitly disable the reference-KL penalty in their main experiments to disentangle the effectiveness of RECAP from standard regularization, demonstrating that the replay and reweighting mechanism alone is sufficient to prevent forgetting.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the conceptual breakthroughs that distinguish RECAP from prior continual learning and multi-task optimization approaches. While Section 3 detailed *how* the mechanism operates, we now examine *why* these design choices represent meaningful advances over existing methods and what they reveal about RLVR training dynamics.

### 4.1 Optimization by Learning Dynamics, Not Loss Magnitude

**Prior Approach:** Traditional multi-task learning and loss reweighting schemes typically rely on static coefficients tuned via grid search, or dynamic methods that weight objectives by their loss magnitude (e.g., uncertainty weighting) or gradient norm (e.g., GradNorm). These approaches assume that larger losses or gradients indicate greater need for attention.

**RECAP's Innovation:** RECAP fundamentally shifts the weighting criterion from *loss scale* to *learning dynamics*. Instead of asking "how large is this loss?", it asks "how is this loss behaving over time?" The convergence rate $c_k^{(t)}$ and instability $i_k^{(t)}$ signals (Section 3) capture the *trajectory* of learning rather than its instantaneous state.

**Why This Matters:**
*   **Magnitude Agnosticism:** As noted in Section 3, the ratio-based convergence and coefficient-of-variation instability are inherently scale-invariant. This eliminates the need for practitioners to manually balance loss scales across heterogeneous objectives (e.g., ensuring IoU loss is comparable to cross-entropy loss). Prior methods often require careful loss normalization or learnable scale parameters.
*   **Automatic Saturation Detection:** The system naturally identifies when an objective has plateaued ($c_k \approx 1$) and reduces its weight without explicit threshold tuning. This is critical in RLVR where format rewards saturate within ~50 steps (**Figure 4**), but static weighting would continue allocating capacity to them.
*   **Instability as a Priority Signal:** Perhaps counterintuitively, RECAP *increases* weight for volatile objectives. Prior methods often treat high variance as a problem to suppress. RECAP treats it as a signal that the objective is still learning and requires more optimization focus. This aligns with the observation that reasoning accuracy remains the most fluctuating signal throughout training (**Figure 4**).

**Classification:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in optimization strategy. It reframes multi-objective weighting as a meta-learning problem about training dynamics rather than a static loss balancing problem.

### 4.2 Empirical Discovery: Heterogeneous Convergence Across Objectives

**Prior Assumption:** Many RLVR pipelines implicitly assume that all reward signals progress at similar rates, or they do not explicitly monitor per-objective convergence behavior during training. Static mixture approaches (e.g., MoDoMoDo) optimize mixing ratios but do not adapt weights *during* training based on convergence.

**RECAP's Discovery:** The paper provides the first systematic characterization of how different RLVR objectives evolve over training time. **Figure 4** reveals that:
*   Format rewards converge up to **3× faster** than reasoning accuracy (Section 1).
*   Format rewards become stable ($i \approx 0$) after ~50 steps, while accuracy remains volatile ($i \approx 0.3$) throughout.
*   Perception objectives (IoU) exhibit intermediate behavior.

**Why This Matters:**
*   **Explains Static Weighting Failure:** This empirical finding directly explains why manually tuned or static weighting schemes underperform. A weight configuration that is optimal at step 50 (when format is still learning) becomes suboptimal at step 200 (when format has saturated).
*   **Justifies Dynamic Adaptation:** The heterogeneity is not minor—it is substantial enough that a single static weight configuration cannot be optimal across the training trajectory. This validates the core premise of RECAP.
*   **Generalizes Beyond This Paper:** This insight likely applies to any multi-objective RL setting with verifiable rewards of varying difficulty. It suggests that monitoring convergence rates should become standard practice in RLVR debugging.

**Classification:** This is a **fundamental empirical contribution**. While the RECAP mechanism itself is novel, the discovery of heterogeneous convergence patterns provides independent value to the community for understanding RLVR training dynamics.

### 4.3 Task-Appropriate Reasoning Depth (Not All Tasks Need Chain-of-Thought)

**Prior Trend:** Recent reasoning-focused VLMs often apply uniform chain-of-thought prompting and thinking rewards across *all* tasks, under the assumption that more reasoning is universally beneficial. Models are encouraged to generate long rationales regardless of task type.

**RECAP's Finding:** The ablation studies in Section 5.3 reveal that this assumption is incorrect. **Figure 6** shows that when a uniform "thinking reward" is applied to perception tasks (e.g., segmentation), the model quickly learns to suppress its reasoning trace—thinking length drops from several hundred characters to near zero. **Figure 7** further shows that RECAP's selective approach reduces thinking length on reasoning tasks by **60%** (67 → 27 words) while preserving accuracy.

**Why This Matters:**
*   **Efficiency Gains:** Shorter rationales directly reduce inference latency and compute cost. A 60% reduction in generated tokens is significant for deployment-scale systems.
*   **Capability Preservation:** Forcing unnecessary reasoning on perception tasks appears to interfere with those capabilities. The qualitative examples in **Figure 9** (Appendix A.3) show that reasoning-only models generate verbose, sometimes hallucinated rationales for simple visual questions, while RECAP-trained models produce concise, direct answers.
*   **Challenges "More Reasoning is Better":** This finding contradicts the implicit assumption in many recent reasoning model papers. It suggests that reasoning should be *selectively* applied based on task requirements, not universally enforced.

**Classification:** This is a **fundamental insight** about VLM architecture and training. It challenges a prevailing design pattern in the reasoning model community and has direct implications for how future models should be trained.

### 4.4 Forgetting is Systematic and Predictable, Not Random

**Prior Understanding:** Catastrophic forgetting in neural networks has been well-studied, but its manifestation in large-scale RLVR pipelines for VLMs was not systematically characterized. Some prior work assumed forgetting was an unavoidable trade-off or focused on specific capabilities (e.g., safety alignment).

**RECAP's Characterization:** **Figure 1** and **Tables 1–2** provide comprehensive evidence that forgetting follows predictable patterns:
*   **Perception Degrades Consistently:** Both Qwen2.5-VL-3B and 7B families show drops on `VisOnly`, `OCRBench`, and `AesBench` after RLVR tuning.
*   **Robustness Suffers:** Performance on `R-Bench-Dis` (distribution-shift robustness) declines, indicating models become brittle outside their training distribution.
*   **Reasoning-Only Training is Harmful:** The "Reasoning-only" baseline in **Table 2** achieves strong reasoning scores but drops LISA segmentation from 65.13 to 57.58—a **7.5-point degradation** on a core perception task.

**Why This Matters:**
*   **Quantifies the Trade-off:** The paper provides concrete numbers on the cost of reasoning-focused training. Practitioners can now make informed decisions about whether the reasoning gains justify the perception losses.
*   **Identifies Vulnerable Capabilities:** Not all capabilities forget equally. Perception and robustness are most vulnerable, while some reasoning benchmarks improve. This helps prioritize which capabilities need replay protection.
*   **Validates Replay Necessity:** The systematic nature of forgetting confirms that it is not a bug in specific implementations but an inherent property of narrow RLVR optimization. This justifies the replay-based approach over simpler regularization.

**Classification:** This is a **fundamental empirical contribution**. The comprehensive benchmarking across six non-reasoning tasks (**Figure 1**) and nine hybrid benchmarks (**Table 2**) establishes a new baseline for evaluating RLVR side effects.

### 4.5 Plug-In Design Without Auxiliary Models

**Prior Approaches:** Many continual learning and regularization methods require training additional models (e.g., distillation targets, auxiliary reward models, or proxy models for mixture optimization like MoDoMoDo). MoDoMoDo, cited as a strong baseline, trains multiple proxy models of the same size as the baseline to learn test performance as a function of mixing ratio (Section 5.2).

**RECAP's Design:** RECAP requires **no auxiliary models**, no additional training passes, and no external reward models. It operates entirely on the loss signals already computed during standard RLVR training. The reweighting computation (Section 3) adds negligible overhead—essentially sliding window statistics and a softmax operation.

**Why This Matters:**
*   **Compute Efficiency:** Avoiding auxiliary models reduces memory footprint and training time. For large-scale VLM training, even a 10% overhead can translate to significant cost.
*   **Deployment Simplicity:** The method "drops into RLVR pipelines without overhead" (Section 1). Practitioners do not need to manage additional model checkpoints or coordinate training between multiple models.
*   **Scalability:** Methods requiring auxiliary models often do not scale well to billion-parameter models due to memory constraints. RECAP's lightweight design makes it applicable at any scale.

**Classification:** This is an **incremental but practically significant improvement**. The core algorithmic innovation lies in the dynamic weighting, but the plug-in design ensures the innovation is *usable* in real-world training pipelines. Many theoretically sound methods fail due to impractical overhead; RECAP avoids this pitfall.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the experimental methodology and results that validate RECAP's effectiveness. We will walk through the evaluation setup, analyze the quantitative outcomes with specific numbers, assess the strength of the evidence supporting the paper's claims, and examine the ablation studies that isolate the contribution of each component.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Experimental Setup

The authors design their experiments to test RECAP under two complementary scenarios that reflect different real-world training paradigms. This dual-setup approach is critical because it demonstrates that RECAP works not only in pure RLVR settings but also in the more common hybrid training regimes used in large-scale model development.

#### Model Selection and Training Configuration

The experiments use two base models from the Qwen2.5-VL family:
- **Qwen2.5-VL-3B**: Used in the RLVR-Only Setting (smaller scale)
- **Qwen2.5-VL-7B**: Used in the Hybrid Setting (larger scale)

Both models are trained on **8 GPUs using data parallelism**, but the configurations differ meaningfully between setups:

| Setting | Model | Batch Size | Rollouts | Training Steps | Key Characteristic |
|---------|-------|------------|----------|----------------|-------------------|
| RLVR-Only | 3B | 2 per device | 4 per prompt | Until data exhausted | Pure RLVR, follows Liang et al. (2025) |
| Hybrid | 7B | 1 per device (16 effective) | 4 per prompt (64 per optimizer step) | 500 steps | Mixes RLVR + SFT objectives |

The Hybrid Setting more closely resembles production training pipelines, combining reinforcement learning objectives with supervised fine-tuning losses. This is important because many real-world VLM training runs do not use pure RLVR.

#### Datasets and Benchmarks

The evaluation spans **15 distinct benchmarks** across both settings, covering reasoning, perception, OCR, and robustness:

**RLVR-Only Setting (6 benchmarks):**
- `SAT` (Spatial Aptitude Training)
- `ScienceQA` (Science Visual Question Answering)
- `MathVista (mini)` (Mathematical Reasoning in Visual Contexts)
- `ChartQA` (Chart Question Answering)
- `InfoVQA` (Infographic VQA)
- `MMMU` (Massive Multi-discipline Multimodal Understanding)

**Hybrid Setting (9 benchmarks):**
- `LISA` (Reasoning Segmentation)
- `MMMU-Pro` (Robust Multi-discipline Understanding)
- `AI2D` (Diagram Understanding)
- `MathVista`, `MathVision`, `MathVerse` (Math Reasoning variants)
- `MMBench` (General Multimodal Understanding)
- `VizWiz` (Accessibility/Vision for Blind Users)
- `OCRBench v2` (Text Recognition)

This breadth is notable—many papers evaluate on 2-3 benchmarks, but this work tests across **perception** (LISA, VizWiz), **reasoning** (MathVista, MathVerse), **OCR** (OCRBench), and **robustness** (MMMU-Pro, R-Bench-Dis in Figure 1).

#### Baseline Comparisons

The paper includes **six distinct baselines** to isolate the effect of replay and reweighting:

1. **Reasoning-only**: Trains solely on target reasoning tasks with fixed reward weights (no replay). This is the most direct test of whether forgetting occurs.
2. **PropMix**: General data included during finetuning, sampled proportionally to source size. Losses are not reweighted.
3. **Uniform**: Data sampled uniformly across domains. Losses are not reweighted. This is the most direct comparison to RECAP.
4. **Coreset**: Replays a size-limited subset of general data (half the reasoning-data volume). Aligns with standard continual learning replay methods.
5. **LwF (Learning without Forgetting)**: Uniform sampling with KL regularization coefficient β=0.01.
6. **MoDoMoDo**: A strong upper-bound baseline that trains multiple proxy models to learn optimal mixing ratios. This is computationally expensive but represents the best static mixture approach.

Additionally, the paper includes **open-source reasoning models** (e.g., VLAA-Thinker-3B, Vision-R1-7B) for context, though the authors explicitly note they are not aiming to outperform these models as they undergo more complex training pipelines.

#### Critical Implementation Detail: KL Penalty Disabled

A crucial methodological choice is that **the reference-KL penalty is disabled by default** in RECAP experiments. The authors state: "Unless otherwise noted, we disable the reference-KL penalty to disentangle the effectiveness of regularization approaches and our replay mechanism." This is important because it isolates whether RECAP's gains come from the replay/reweighting itself, not from standard RLHF regularization. The LwF baseline serves as the comparison point for KL-based approaches.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

The experimental results provide strong quantitative evidence for RECAP's effectiveness. We will analyze both settings separately, then synthesize the findings.

#### RLVR-Only Setting Results (Table 1)

**Table 1** presents accuracy scores across six benchmarks for the 3B model. The most striking result is the dramatic improvement on ScienceQA:

> "Especially on SCIENCEQA, RL lifts the performance of Qwen2.5-VL-3B from 6.20 to 60. On this benchmark, our proposed method even outperforms comparative open-source reasoning models." (Section 5.2)

The specific numbers tell a compelling story:

Key observations:
- **RECAP outperforms MoDoMoDo** on 5 of 6 benchmarks, despite MoDoMoDo being described as the "upper-bound approach of static data mixture approaches" with access to target task performance during finetuning.
- **ScienceQA improvement**: RECAP achieves 71.59 vs. MoDoMoDo's 65.74—a **5.85 point gain** over the strongest static baseline.
- **MMMU improvement**: RECAP reaches 42.44 vs. base 38.67, showing reasoning gains do not come at the cost of general understanding.
- **Consistent wins**: RECAP is bolded (best) on SAT, ScienceQA, MathVista, InfoVQA, and MMMU.

The authors note that MoDoMoDo requires "training multiple proxy models of the same size as the baseline models to learn the test performance as a function of mixing ratio, which is computationally expensive." RECAP achieves better results without this overhead.

**Table 2** is arguably more important because it tests RECAP in the more realistic hybrid training regime. The results here directly address the forgetting problem identified in Figure 1.

> "Compared with the base model, naively finetuning on the reasoning domain causes significant forgetting, especially on tasks not requiring extensive thinking. For example, finetuning on ThinkLite-VL-70k reduces the segmentation ability from 65.13 to 57.58 on LISA." (Section 5.2)

This is a **7.55 point degradation** (65.13 → 57.58) from reasoning-only training. RECAP not only recovers this loss but **exceeds the base model by 2.11 points** (65.13 → 67.24). - **MMMU-Pro**: RECAP achieves 34.15, the highest among all Qwen2.5-VL-7B variants (Reasoning-only: 33.87, Uniform: 31.91).
- **MathVision**: RECAP reaches 25.11, outperforming all baselines including Coreset (23.36) and Reasoning-only (24.87).
- **MathVerse**: RECAP achieves 40.83, matching Reasoning-only (40.74) while preserving perception capabilities.
- **VizWiz**: RECAP scores 61.97, which is lower than Coreset (63.76) and Reasoning-only (62.45). The authors acknowledge this: "RECAP achieves the best or runner-up performance across different datasets, except for VizWiz where we still improve the base model performance by more than 10%."

The VizWiz result is worth examining—it's the only benchmark where RECAP does not achieve the best score within the Qwen family. However, it still improves over the base model (50.82 → 61.97, an **11.15 point gain**), so the method does not cause degradation, just does not maximize this particular capability.

Combining results from both tables, we can assess whether RECAP achieves its dual goal of **preserving general capabilities while improving reasoning**:

1. **General Capability Preservation**: 
   - LISA (perception): Base 65.13 → RECAP 67.24 (**+2.11**)
   - OCRBench v2: Base 39.49 → RECAP 39.72 (**+0.23**, preserved)
   - MMMU: Base 38.67 → RECAP 42.44 (**+3.77** in RLVR-only)

2. **Reasoning Improvement**:
   - ScienceQA: Base 6.20 → RECAP 71.59 (**+65.39** in RLVR-only)
   - MathVista: Base 23.6 → RECAP 33.2 (**+9.6** in RLVR-only)
   - MathVerse: Base 26.29 → RECAP 40.83 (**+14.54** in Hybrid)

3. **Comparison to Reasoning-only**:
   - Reasoning-only achieves strong reasoning scores (MathVerse 40.74 vs. RECAP 40.83) but catastrophically forgets perception (LISA 57.58 vs. RECAP 67.24).
   - RECAP matches reasoning performance while **avoiding the 7.55 point perception drop**.

This pattern strongly supports the paper's central claim: RECAP enables reasoning gains without the typical perception tax.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Mechanism Validation

The ablation studies in Section 5.3 are critical because they isolate *why* RECAP works, not just *that* it works. The authors conduct three key ablations.

#### Ablation 1: Uniform Baseline vs. RECAP (Figure 5)

This ablation compares RECAP against a **Uniform baseline** that uses identical hyperparameters, data sampling, and training pipeline—differing **only in the loss reweighting mechanism** (λk = 1/K for Uniform).

**Figure 5** shows training curves for format and accuracy rewards:

> "In the early phase, the baseline climbs format faster—consistent with format being a low-variance, easy-to-optimize signal—yet a crossover soon appears and our method surpasses it as training progresses (step 40). In contrast, for accuracy, our method opens a growing lead over time." (Section 5.3)

The specific dynamics:
- **Format Reward**: Uniform baseline is initially better (steps 0-40), but RECAP surpasses it after step 40. This aligns with RECAP's design: once format converges (c ≈ 1, i ≈ 0), its weight is down-regulated.
- **Accuracy Reward**: RECAP maintains a consistent lead throughout training, with the gap widening over time.

This directly validates the convergence-based reweighting hypothesis: by shifting capacity away from saturated format objectives toward harder accuracy objectives, RECAP improves task correctness without sacrificing format compliance.

#### Ablation 2: Thinking Length on Perception Tasks (Figure 6)

This ablation examines what happens when a uniform "thinking reward" is applied across all domains, including perception tasks.

**Figure 6** plots thinking length (characters) on a segmentation task during training:

> "When a uniform 'thinking reward' is applied to all domains, the model quickly learns that long chains of thought are unnecessary for segmentation. The average response length drops from several hundred characters at the start of training to tens (often near zero) later on." (Section 5.3 caption)

The key insight: **explicit reasoning is unnecessary for perception tasks**, and encouraging long rationales can be detrimental. This motivated the design choice in the Hybrid Setting to "keep answer-format rewards for perception domains (no thinking) and reserve thinking rewards for tasks that truly benefit from step-by-step reasoning."

#### Ablation 3: Thinking Length on Reasoning Tasks (Figure 7)

This ablation compares thinking length on reasoning tasks between Reasoning-only training and RECAP.

**Figure 7** shows:
- **Reasoning-only**: Maintains long chains of thought with high variability (~67.3 words average)
- **RECAP**: Progressively reduces thinking length, converging to ~27.3 words

This is a **59.4% reduction** (67.3 → 27.3 words). The authors note: "This shorter reasoning directly improves inference efficiency—fewer generated tokens reduce latency and compute cost—without sacrificing problem-solving quality."

This is a significant secondary benefit: RECAP not only preserves capabilities but also produces **more efficient inference** by learning to generate only as much reasoning as the task requires.

#### Appendix Ablations: Reward Dynamics (Figures 10-11)

**Figure 10** (Appendix A.5) shows final performance across metrics:
- Think Format: Baseline 95.69 → RECAP 95.76 (+0.07)
- Answer Format: Baseline 95.36 → RECAP 95.79 (+0.43)
- Reasoning Acc.: Baseline 63.96 → RECAP 65.97 (+2.01)
- IoU: Baseline 74.04 → RECAP 75.15 (+1.11)
- Mean Token Acc.: Baseline 83.44 → RECAP 84.84 (+1.40)

The pattern is clear: **format adherence is maintained** (near-parity), while **correctness-oriented metrics improve** (Reasoning Acc, IoU, Token Acc). This aligns with the design goal of down-weighting saturated format rewards to shift capacity to harder objectives.

**Figure 11** (Appendix A.6) shows reward dynamics during training, confirming the high variance of RL signals that motivated the sliding window approach. The Total Reward Standard Deviation peaks near 0.9 around step ~20, justifying the need for short-horizon statistics rather than per-iteration magnitudes.

### 5.4 Assessment of Experimental Convincingness

The experiments provide **strong but not complete** evidence for the paper's claims. We assess each claim separately:

#### Claim 1: RLVR Causes Systematic Forgetting of General Capabilities

**Evidence Strength: Strong**

- **Figure 1** shows consistent degradation across six non-reasoning benchmarks for both 3B and 7B model families.
- **Table 2** quantifies this: Reasoning-only training drops LISA from 65.13 to 57.58 (7.55 points).
- The pattern is consistent across model sizes and benchmark types (perception, OCR, robustness).

This claim is well-supported. The forgetting is not random—it systematically affects perception and robustness tasks while reasoning benchmarks improve.

#### Claim 2: RECAP Preserves General Capabilities

**Evidence Strength: Strong**

- **Table 2**: RECAP achieves 67.24 on LISA vs. base 65.13 (+2.11 points).
- **Table 1**: RECAP maintains or improves MMMU (42.44 vs. base 38.67).
- RECAP outperforms all replay baselines (Uniform, PropMix, Coreset, LwF) on perception tasks.

The evidence is compelling. RECAP does not just prevent forgetting—it sometimes **improves** over the base model, suggesting the replay provides beneficial regularization.

#### Claim 3: RECAP Improves Reasoning Performance

**Evidence Strength: Moderate to Strong**

- **Table 1**: RECAP outperforms MoDoMoDo on 5 of 6 benchmarks.
- **Table 2**: RECAP matches or exceeds Reasoning-only on most reasoning benchmarks (MathVerse 40.83 vs. 40.74).
- **Figure 5**: Accuracy reward shows consistent improvement over Uniform baseline.

The evidence is strong, but there is a nuance: RECAP's reasoning gains are sometimes marginal compared to Reasoning-only (e.g., MathVerse +0.09 points). The primary benefit is **preserving perception while matching reasoning**, not necessarily exceeding reasoning-only performance. The paper is honest about this: "RECAP preserves or improves general capabilities while matching or exceeding the reasoning performance of reasoning-only finetuning."

#### Claim 4: Dynamic Reweighting is Superior to Static Weighting

**Evidence Strength: Strong**

- **RECAP vs. Uniform**: RECAP consistently outperforms Uniform across both settings, isolating the reweighting effect.
- **Figure 5**: The crossover at step 40 shows dynamic adaptation matters—static weighting cannot capture this.
- **RECAP vs. MoDoMoDo**: RECAP outperforms the computationally expensive static mixture optimizer.

This is one of the strongest findings. The ablation against Uniform (same data, different weighting) directly validates the reweighting mechanism.

#### Claim 5: Task-Appropriate Reasoning Depth Improves Efficiency

**Evidence Strength: Strong**

- **Figure 7**: 59.4% reduction in thinking length (67.3 → 27.3 words).
- **Figure 6**: Perception tasks naturally suppress reasoning when not rewarded for it.
- **Figure 9** (Appendix): Qualitative examples show RECAP produces concise answers on simple visual questions.

This claim is well-supported and has practical significance for deployment efficiency.

### 5.5 Limitations and Conditional Results

The experiments have some limitations and conditional findings that warrant acknowledgment:

#### VizWiz Performance

As noted, RECAP scores 61.97 on VizWiz vs. Coreset's 63.76. The authors acknowledge this but note it still improves over the base model (50.82 → 61.97). This suggests RECAP may not optimize all capabilities equally—some tasks (like accessibility-focused VizWiz) may benefit more from coreset-style replay than dynamic reweighting.

#### KL Penalty Disentanglement

The authors disable KL penalty by default to isolate RECAP's effect. While this is methodologically sound, it means the results do not directly show how RECAP interacts with standard RLHF regularization. The LwF baseline (β=0.01) provides some comparison, but a full ablation on KL + RECAP combinations is not presented.

#### Window Size Hyperparameter

The sliding window size $W$ is mentioned but not ablated. The paper states "the exact integer value of $W$ is treated as an internal buffer size dependent on the step frequency," but does not report sensitivity analysis. This is a minor gap—practitioners would benefit from guidance on tuning $W$ for different training scales.

#### Generalization Beyond RLVR

The Limitations section (Section 6) acknowledges: "Our empirical evaluation focuses on RLVR and standard SFT settings. We expect RECAP to yield similar gains over uniform or manually tuned baselines with any heterogeneous objective sets, but we leave a comprehensive evaluation across non-RL objectives to future work." This is an honest acknowledgment that the method's generality beyond RLVR/SFT is not yet empirically validated.

### 5.6 Summary of Experimental Findings

| Claim | Supported? | Key Evidence |
|-------|-----------|--------------|
| RLVR causes forgetting | Yes | Figure 1, Table 2 (LISA 65.13 → 57.58) |
| RECAP preserves capabilities | Yes | Table 2 (LISA 67.24, +2.11 over base) |
| RECAP improves reasoning | Yes (with nuance) | Table 1 (ScienceQA 71.59), Table 2 (MathVerse 40.83) |
| Dynamic > Static weighting | Yes | RECAP vs. Uniform, RECAP vs. MoDoMoDo |
| Efficiency gains | Yes | Figure 7 (59.4% thinking length reduction) |

The experiments are comprehensive, well-controlled, and directly address the paper's core claims. The ablation studies isolate the contribution of each component, and the multi-benchmark evaluation provides robust evidence across capability types. The main limitation is that some claims (generalization beyond RLVR, KL interaction) are not fully tested, but the authors are transparent about this in the Limitations section.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

No method is without constraints, and RECAP is no exception. While the paper presents compelling evidence for dynamic reweighting in RLVR pipelines, a critical examination reveals several assumptions, unaddressed scenarios, and open questions that practitioners should consider before adoption. This section dissects these limitations with the same rigor applied to the method's strengths, grounding each point in specific evidence from the paper.

### 6.1 Assumptions Underlying the Approach

RECAP's effectiveness relies on several implicit assumptions about the training environment and objective structure. Understanding these is essential for determining whether the method applies to your specific use case.

#### Assumption 1: Objectives Exhibit Heterogeneous Convergence Rates

The core premise of RECAP—that different objectives converge at different rates—is empirically validated in **Figure 4**, which shows format rewards converging ~3× faster than reasoning accuracy. However, this assumption may not hold in all training scenarios.

> "Some rewards converge up to three times faster than others, which should therefore not be emphasized later in training when the model is already able to obtain the corresponding skills." (Section 1)

**Critical Question**: What if all objectives converge at similar rates? In such cases, the dynamic reweighting mechanism would provide minimal benefit over uniform weighting, while still incurring the computational overhead of computing sliding window statistics. The paper does not characterize scenarios where convergence heterogeneity is low, leaving practitioners without guidance on when RECAP is most valuable.

#### Assumption 2: Sliding Window Statistics Are Representative

The method assumes that short-horizon statistics (convergence rate $c_k^{(t)}$ and instability $i_k^{(t)}$) computed over a window of length $2 \times W$ accurately reflect learning progress. This works well for the RLVR setting where signals fluctuate rapidly (**Figure 11**, Appendix A.6), but may fail in scenarios with:

- **Delayed reward signals**: If an objective's true progress only manifests after many steps beyond the window size, the reweighting may prematurely deprioritize it.
- **Non-stationary environments**: If the data distribution shifts during training (e.g., curriculum learning), past window statistics may not predict future learning dynamics.

The paper does not specify the exact integer value of $W$, stating only that it is "treated as an internal buffer size dependent on the step frequency" (Section 3.4). This lack of transparency makes it difficult to tune the method for different training scales.

#### Assumption 3: General Capability Data Is Available and Representative

RECAP requires replaying general-capability data alongside reasoning data to preserve foundational skills. This assumes:

- **Data availability**: Practitioners have access to high-quality general capability datasets (e.g., RefCOCO, LLaVA-OneVision-OCR) that are compatible with their reasoning tasks.
- **Representativeness**: The replay data adequately covers the capabilities at risk of forgetting. If the replay set is too narrow, the model may preserve those specific skills while forgetting others.

**Table 3** (Appendix A.1) shows the specific datasets used (RefCOCO, LLaVA-OneVision-OCR, etc.), but the paper does not explore what happens when replay data is limited or mismatched to the forgetting patterns.

### 6.2 Scenarios and Problem Settings Not Addressed

The paper's evaluation, while comprehensive within its scope, leaves several important scenarios untested.

#### Non-RL Objectives and Preference-Based Training

The Limitations section (Section 6) explicitly acknowledges this gap:

> "Our empirical evaluation focuses on RLVR and standard SFT settings. We expect RECAP to yield similar gains over uniform or manually tuned baselines with any heterogeneous objective sets, but we leave a comprehensive evaluation across non-RL objectives to future work."

This is a significant limitation because many production pipelines use **Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)** or **Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)** with learned reward models rather than verifiable rewards. The method's behavior with:

- **Learned reward models** (which may have different noise characteristics than verifiable rewards)
- **Preference-based losses** (which operate on pairwise comparisons rather than absolute scores)
- **Alignment objectives** (e.g., safety, helpfulness)

...remains unvalidated. While the authors claim the method is "magnitude-agnostic" and should generalize, this is an assumption, not an empirical finding.

#### Larger Model Scales

All experiments use **Qwen2.5-VL-3B** and **Qwen2.5-VL-7B** models. While these are respectable sizes, they do not reflect the scale of frontier models (70B+ parameters) where:

- **Memory constraints** may make sliding window statistics more expensive to store
- **Training dynamics** may differ significantly (e.g., slower convergence, different instability patterns)
- **Compute overhead** becomes more consequential at scale

The claim that RECAP adds "negligible overhead" (Section 1) is not quantified in terms of wall-clock time or memory usage, making it difficult to assess scalability.

#### Domain-Specific Forgetting Patterns

**Figure 1** shows that forgetting is systematic—affecting perception and robustness tasks consistently. However, the paper does not explore whether different reasoning domains (e.g., math vs. code vs. logic) cause different forgetting patterns. A practitioner training on code generation may face different preservation challenges than one training on mathematical reasoning, but RECAP provides no domain-specific guidance.

### 6.3 Computational, Data, and Scalability Constraints

While RECAP is designed to be lightweight, several practical constraints warrant consideration.

#### Computational Overhead

The paper claims RECAP is "end-to-end and readily applicable to existing RLVR pipelines without training additional models or heavy tuning" (Abstract). This is true in the sense that no auxiliary models are required (unlike MoDoMoDo, which trains proxy models). However, the method does introduce:

- **Sliding window storage**: Maintaining $2 \times W$ steps of loss history for $K$ objectives requires memory proportional to $O(K \times W)$.
- **Per-step computation**: Computing convergence rates, instability scores, and softmax weights at every training step adds computational overhead.

The paper does not quantify this overhead in terms of:
- Additional GPU memory usage
- Training time increase (e.g., "% slowdown per step")
- Communication overhead in distributed training settings

For small-scale experiments on 8 GPUs (**Section 5.1**), this may be negligible. At larger scales with hundreds of GPUs, even small per-step overheads compound significantly.

#### Data Requirements

RECAP requires **mixed-domain data** to function. The Hybrid Setting uses:
- **ThinkLite-VL-70k** for reasoning (69,997 samples)
- **RefCOCO** for perception (321,327 samples)
- **LLaVA-OneVision-OCR** for text recognition (66,468 samples)

**Table 3** (Appendix A.1) provides full statistics. This raises practical questions:

- **What if general data is scarce?** The Coreset baseline tests limited replay (half the reasoning-data volume), but RECAP's performance under severe data constraints is not explored.
- **What is the optimal replay ratio?** The paper uniformly samples across data sources by default (Section 5.1), but does not ablate different replay ratios.
- **Data quality sensitivity**: If the replay data is noisy or mislabeled, does RECAP's instability signal amplify or suppress its influence? This is not addressed.

#### Temperature Hyperparameter Sensitivity

The temperature $T$ in the softmax weighting controls how sharply priorities are differentiated:

$$
\lambda_k^{(t)} = \frac{K \exp\left(s_k^{(t)} / T\right)}{\sum_{i=1}^K \exp\left(s_i^{(t)} / T\right)}
$$

The paper sets $T=5$ by default (Section 3.4) but provides no ablation study on this hyperparameter. Questions remain:

- How sensitive is performance to $T$?
- Should $T$ be tuned differently for different model sizes or objective counts?
- What happens as $T \to 0$ (extreme focusing) or $T \to \infty$ (uniform weighting)?

The claim that the method requires "no heavy tuning" (Abstract) is somewhat undermined by the presence of this un-ablated hyperparameter.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

Several weaknesses and open questions emerge from a critical reading of the experimental results.

#### VizWiz Performance Anomaly

**Table 2** shows that RECAP scores **61.97** on VizWiz, while the Coreset baseline achieves **63.76**. This is the only benchmark where RECAP does not achieve the best score within the Qwen2.5-VL-7B family. The authors acknowledge this:

> "RECAP achieves the best or runner-up performance across different datasets, except for VizWiz where we still improve the base model performance by more than 10%." (Section 5.2)

While RECAP still improves over the base model (50.82 → 61.97, an **11.15 point gain**), the fact that Coreset outperforms it suggests that dynamic reweighting may not be optimal for all capability types. VizWiz focuses on accessibility and vision for blind users—a domain that may benefit more from consistent replay (Coreset) than dynamic prioritization. This raises an open question: **Are there capability categories where static replay is superior to dynamic reweighting?**

#### KL Penalty Interaction Not Fully Explored

The paper disables the reference-KL penalty by default to "disentangle the effectiveness of regularization approaches and our replay mechanism" (Section 5.1). While methodologically sound for isolating RECAP's effect, this leaves a critical question unanswered: **How does RECAP interact with standard KL regularization?**

The LwF baseline (β=0.01) provides one data point, showing LwF achieves similar scene understanding to Uniform but lower reasoning scores (e.g., 29.59 vs. 31.91 on MMMU-Pro, **Table 2**). However, a full ablation combining RECAP + KL is not presented. In production RLHF pipelines, KL penalties are standard—practitioners need to know whether RECAP complements or conflicts with this regularization.

#### Unweighted Sum of Convergence and Instability

The priority score combines convergence and instability via simple addition:

$$
s_k^{(t)} = c_k^{(t)} + i_k^{(t)}
$$

The paper acknowledges this is a simplification:

> "Finetuning the trade-off between those two terms offers finer-grained control and potentially improves the performance. However, we simply take their unweighted sum s = c + i for simplicity, which performs consistently well in our experiments." (Section 4)

This is a reasonable design choice for a first method, but it leaves open whether:
- Different objectives should weight convergence vs. instability differently
- The relative importance of these signals changes during training
- A learned combination (e.g., via a small meta-network) could outperform the fixed sum

The claim that it "performs consistently well" is supported by experiments, but the potential ceiling of this design is unknown.

#### Reasoning Gains Are Sometimes Marginal

While RECAP preserves general capabilities impressively, its reasoning improvements over baselines are sometimes marginal. For example, in **Table 2**:

| Model | MathVerse |
|-------|-----------|
| Reasoning-only | 40.74 |
| RECAP | 40.83 |

This is a **0.09 point gain**—statistically negligible. The paper is honest about this:

> "RECAP preserves or improves general capabilities while matching or exceeding the reasoning performance of reasoning-only finetuning." (Section 1, Contributions)

The key word is "matching." RECAP's primary benefit is **avoiding the perception tax** (7.55 point LISA drop in Reasoning-only), not necessarily exceeding reasoning-only performance. Practitioners seeking maximum reasoning gains may need to accept some capability regression, while those prioritizing generalist performance will benefit from RECAP. This trade-off should be made explicit.

#### Thinking Length Reduction: Benefit or Limitation?

**Figure 7** shows RECAP reduces thinking length on reasoning tasks by **59.4%** (67.3 → 27.3 words). The paper frames this as an efficiency gain:

> "This shorter reasoning directly improves inference efficiency—fewer generated tokens reduce latency and compute cost—without sacrificing problem-solving quality." (Section 5.3)

However, this raises an open question: **Does shorter reasoning always indicate better efficiency, or could it indicate under-reasoning on complex problems?** The paper evaluates accuracy but does not analyze whether the reduced thinking length correlates with performance on harder vs. easier problems within each benchmark. If RECAP causes the model to "give up" on hard problems by generating shorter (but incorrect) rationales, this would be a concerning failure mode not captured by aggregate accuracy metrics.

### 6.6 Practical Guidance for Practitioners

Based on the limitations identified, here is guidance for practitioners considering RECAP:

1. **Verify convergence heterogeneity**: Before applying RECAP, monitor your objectives' convergence rates (as in **Figure 4**). If all objectives converge at similar rates, uniform weighting may suffice.

2. **Ensure replay data quality**: The method assumes replay data is representative of capabilities you want to preserve. Invest in curating diverse, high-quality general capability datasets.

3. **Test KL + RECAP combination**: Since the paper disables KL by default, run your own ablation combining RECAP with standard KL regularization to understand the interaction in your pipeline.

4. **Monitor per-capability performance**: Don't rely solely on aggregate benchmarks. Track individual capabilities (like VizWiz) where dynamic reweighting may underperform static replay.

5. **Validate reasoning depth**: If inference efficiency is critical, verify that reduced thinking length (**Figure 7**) does not correlate with performance drops on hard problems within your domain.

6. **Budget for hyperparameter tuning**: While claimed to require "no heavy tuning" (Abstract), the temperature $T$ and window size $W$ may need adjustment for your specific training setup.

---

The limitations identified here do not invalidate RECAP's contributions—they contextualize them. The method represents a meaningful advance in addressing RLVR forgetting, but like all methods, it operates within a specific design space. Understanding these boundaries is essential for effective deployment and for guiding future research that addresses the open questions identified above.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section synthesizes how RECAP reshapes our understanding of RLVR training, identifies concrete research pathways it opens, and provides actionable guidance for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this approach. We move beyond evaluating *whether* the method works to examining *what it enables* and *where it should be applied*.

### 7.1 Shifting the RLVR Training Paradigm

RECAP represents more than an incremental improvement—it signals a fundamental shift in how the community should approach post-training for reasoning-capable models. Three paradigm changes emerge from this work:

#### From Single-Objective to Multi-Objective Dynamics

Prior RLVR pipelines often treated training as optimizing a single composite reward (e.g., "solve the math problem correctly"). RECAP demonstrates that **monitoring per-objective learning dynamics** is essential for stable, capable models. The discovery that format rewards converge 3× faster than reasoning accuracy (**Figure 4**) should become standard diagnostic practice, not an exception.

> "Some rewards converge up to three times faster than others, which should therefore not be emphasized later in training when the model is already able to obtain the corresponding skills." (Section 1)

This reframes multi-task optimization from a *loss balancing* problem to a *learning dynamics* problem. Future RLVR pipelines should routinely track convergence rates and instability metrics across all objectives, using these signals not just for reweighting but for debugging training pathologies.

#### From "More Reasoning is Better" to Task-Appropriate Reasoning

The finding that RECAP reduces thinking length by **59.4%** (67.3 → 27.3 words, **Figure 7**) while maintaining accuracy challenges a prevailing assumption in the reasoning model community. Many recent works implicitly treat longer chain-of-thought as universally beneficial, but this paper demonstrates that:

- **Perception tasks do not benefit from extended reasoning** (**Figure 6** shows thinking length collapsing to near-zero on segmentation tasks)
- **Reasoning tasks can be solved with shorter, more focused rationales** when the model is not over-optimized on format rewards
- **Inference efficiency improves directly** through reduced token generation

This suggests future model training should **selectively apply reasoning rewards** based on task type, rather than enforcing uniform chain-of-thought across all domains. The qualitative examples in **Figure 9** (Appendix A.3) illustrate this clearly: RECAP-trained models produce concise, direct answers on simple visual questions while maintaining detailed reasoning on math problems.

#### From Accepting Forgetting to Preventing It

Before this work, capability regression in RLVR was often treated as an unavoidable trade-off: gain reasoning, lose perception. **Figure 1** and **Table 2** demonstrate this is false—RECAP achieves **both** reasoning gains (ScienceQA: 6.20 → 71.59) **and** perception improvements (LISA: 65.13 → 67.24).

This shifts the burden of proof: future RLVR papers should demonstrate that their methods do *not* cause systematic forgetting on non-target benchmarks, not just that they improve target reasoning scores. The six-benchmark evaluation in **Figure 1** (A-OKVQA, AesBench, VStar, VisOnly, OCRBench, R-Bench-Dis) provides a template for comprehensive capability assessment.

### 7.2 Concrete Research Pathways

RECAP opens several specific research directions that build directly on its findings and limitations:

#### 1. Scaling to Frontier Model Sizes

All experiments use Qwen2.5-VL-3B and 7B models. Critical questions remain:

- Do convergence heterogeneity patterns hold at 70B+ scale?
- Does the sliding window overhead become prohibitive in distributed training across hundreds of GPUs?
- Do larger models exhibit different forgetting patterns (e.g., more resistant to perception degradation)?

**Suggested experiment**: Apply RECAP to a 70B-class VLM training run, measuring wall-clock overhead and capability preservation across the **Figure 1** benchmark suite.

#### 2. Extension to Non-RL Objectives

The Limitations section (Section 6) explicitly notes RECAP is untested on:

- **Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)**: Where losses come from pairwise comparisons rather than verifiable rewards
- **RLHF with learned reward models**: Where reward noise characteristics differ from programmatic verifiers
- **Safety and alignment objectives**: Where "convergence" may have different semantics (e.g., avoiding harmful outputs vs. maximizing correctness)

**Suggested experiment**: Integrate RECAP into a DPO pipeline for instruction tuning, using convergence/instability signals across helpfulness, harmlessness, and honesty objectives.

#### 3. Learned Signal Combination

RECAP uses an unweighted sum $s_k^{(t)} = c_k^{(t)} + i_k^{(t)}$ for priority scoring. The paper acknowledges this is a simplification:

> "Finetuning the trade-off between those two terms offers finer-grained control and potentially improves the performance." (Section 4)

**Suggested experiment**: Train a lightweight meta-network (e.g., 2-layer MLP) to predict optimal weights from convergence and instability signals, using validation performance as the learning signal. Compare against the fixed sum baseline.

#### 4. Domain-Specific Forgetting Characterization

**Figure 1** shows systematic forgetting on perception and robustness tasks, but the paper does not explore whether different *reasoning domains* cause different forgetting patterns:

- Does math reasoning training degrade OCR more than logic reasoning?
- Does code generation training affect different capabilities than spatial reasoning?

**Suggested experiment**: Train separate models on math-only, code-only, and logic-only RLVR, then evaluate forgetting patterns across the full **Figure 1** benchmark suite to identify domain-specific vulnerability profiles.

#### 5. KL + RECAP Interaction

The paper disables KL penalty by default to isolate RECAP's effect (Section 5.1). However, production RLHF pipelines typically include KL regularization. The interaction between these mechanisms is unexplored:

- Does KL + RECAP provide complementary protection against forgetting?
- Does KL reduce the need for aggressive replay, allowing smaller replay buffers?
- Do the two mechanisms conflict (e.g., KL constrains policy while RECAP encourages exploration on volatile objectives)?

**Suggested experiment**: Full factorial ablation across KL coefficients (β ∈ {0, 0.01, 0.1}) and RECAP (enabled/disabled), measuring both reasoning and perception benchmarks.

#### 6. Window Size and Temperature Sensitivity

Two hyperparameters are not ablated:

- **Window size $W$**: Only described as "dependent on step frequency" (Section 3.4)
- **Temperature $T$**: Set to 5 by default with no sensitivity analysis

**Suggested experiment**: Sweep $W \in \{10, 50, 100, 200\}$ and $T \in \{1, 5, 10, 20\}$ on the Hybrid Setting, measuring final benchmark performance and training stability.

#### 7. Application to Other Modalities

RECAP's design is modality-agnostic—it operates on loss signals regardless of whether inputs are text, images, or audio. This suggests applicability to:

- **Speech-language models**: Balancing transcription accuracy with reasoning capabilities
- **Robotics policies**: Balancing task success with safety constraints
- **Code models**: Balancing correctness with code style and documentation quality

**Suggested experiment**: Apply RECAP to a code generation RLVR pipeline, tracking both execution success rates and code quality metrics (readability, documentation, style adherence).

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

RECAP is designed for deployment in real-world training pipelines. Here are specific scenarios where it provides clear value:

#### Production VLM Training

**When to use**: Training multimodal models that must handle both reasoning tasks (math, science, logic) and perception tasks (OCR, object detection, segmentation) in production.

**Why RECAP**: Prevents the perception degradation shown in **Figure 1** while achieving reasoning gains. The **Table 2** results demonstrate this directly: LISA segmentation improves 2.11 points over base while MathVerse improves 14.54 points.

**Integration**: Drop into existing GRPO or PPO pipelines with minimal modification—only the loss aggregation step changes.

#### Continual Learning Scenarios

**When to use**: Incrementally adding new capabilities to deployed models without retraining from scratch.

**Why RECAP**: The replay mechanism preserves previously acquired skills while the dynamic reweighting prevents new objectives from dominating training. Outperforms Coreset baseline on most benchmarks (**Table 2**).

**Integration**: Maintain a replay buffer of representative data from previous capabilities, mix with new task data, and let RECAP handle the weighting.

#### Inference-Efficient Model Training

**When to use**: Scenarios where inference latency and token costs are critical (e.g., high-volume API deployments, edge devices).

**Why RECAP**: The **59.4% reduction in thinking length** (**Figure 7**) directly translates to lower inference costs without accuracy sacrifice. Models learn to generate only as much reasoning as the task requires.

**Integration**: Configure thinking rewards selectively—apply only to tasks that benefit from extended reasoning, not to perception or simple QA tasks.

#### Multi-Task Training with Heterogeneous Objectives

**When to use**: Any training scenario with multiple loss terms that converge at different rates (e.g., mixing SFT with RL, combining accuracy with format compliance).

**Why RECAP**: Eliminates manual weight tuning. The magnitude-agnostic design (**Section 3.4**) handles objectives with different scales automatically.

**Integration**: Replace static loss weights with RECAP's dynamic weighting module. Monitor convergence rates to verify heterogeneity exists (if all objectives converge similarly, uniform weighting may suffice).

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

Based on the paper's methodology and limitations, here is practical guidance for practitioners:

#### When to Prefer RECAP Over Alternatives

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Heterogeneous convergence rates confirmed | **RECAP** | Dynamic reweighting adapts to saturation (**Figure 4**) |
| All objectives converge similarly | **Uniform** | RECAP provides minimal benefit, adds overhead |
| General capability data scarce | **Coreset** | RECAP requires representative replay data (**Table 3**) |
| KL regularization required | **RECAP + KL** (untested, recommended) | RECAP handles replay, KL handles policy constraint |
| Compute budget extremely limited | **Uniform** or **PropMix** | RECAP adds sliding window computation overhead |
| Maximum reasoning performance needed | **Reasoning-only** (accept forgetting) | RECAP matches but rarely exceeds reasoning-only (**Table 2**) |
| General capability preservation critical | **RECAP** | Only method that improves over base on perception (**Table 2**, LISA +2.11) |

#### Minimum Requirements for Deployment

1. **Mixed-domain data**: Must have access to both target task data (reasoning) and general capability data (perception, OCR, etc.) as shown in **Table 3**.

2. **Per-objective loss tracking**: Must be able to compute and store loss values for each objective separately (not just aggregate loss).

3. **Sliding window buffer**: Must allocate memory for $2 \times W$ steps of loss history per objective. For $K=5$ objectives and $W=50$, this is 500 floating-point values—negligible for most setups.

4. **GRPO or PPO pipeline**: RECAP integrates with policy gradient methods. Integration with DPO or other preference-based methods is untested.

#### Hyperparameter Starting Points

Based on the paper's configuration:

| Hyperparameter | Default | Tuning Guidance |
|----------------|---------|-----------------|
| Temperature $T$ | 5 | Increase for smoother weights, decrease for sharper focusing |
| Window size $W$ | Not specified | Start with 50 steps, adjust based on training step frequency |
| Replay ratio | Uniform (1:1) | Can adjust based on capability importance |
| KL coefficient $β$ | 0 (disabled) | If using KL, start with 0.01 and ablate |

#### Monitoring During Training

To verify RECAP is functioning correctly, track:

1. **Per-objective convergence rates** $c_k^{(t)}$: Should show heterogeneity (some objectives saturate faster than others, as in **Figure 4**).

2. **Weight evolution** $\lambda_k^{(t)}$: Should shift over training, down-weighting saturated objectives.

3. **Capability-specific benchmarks**: Evaluate on both target (reasoning) and non-target (perception) benchmarks periodically, not just at training end.

4. **Thinking length**: Monitor response length on different task types to verify task-appropriate reasoning depth (**Figures 6-7**).

#### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Applying uniform thinking rewards**: As **Figure 6** demonstrates, this causes models to suppress reasoning on perception tasks. Use task-specific format rewards.

2. **Insufficient replay data**: If general capability data is too limited, RECAP cannot preserve those skills. Ensure replay datasets are representative (**Table 3**).

3. **Ignoring convergence diagnostics**: If all objectives converge at similar rates, RECAP provides minimal benefit. Verify heterogeneity exists before deployment.

4. **Skipping non-target evaluation**: Only evaluating on reasoning benchmarks misses the primary benefit (capability preservation). Use a suite like **Figure 1**'s six benchmarks.

### 7.5 Broader Impact on Model Development

RECAP's implications extend beyond the specific method:

#### Benchmarking Standards

Future RLVR papers should report performance on **both** target reasoning benchmarks **and** non-target capability benchmarks. The **Figure 1** evaluation framework (A-OKVQA, AesBench, VStar, VisOnly, OCRBench, R-Bench-Dis) provides a template for comprehensive assessment that detects systematic forgetting.

#### Training Transparency

The paper's detailed convergence analysis (**Figures 4, 5, 11**) should become standard reporting practice. Understanding *how* objectives evolve during training is as important as final performance numbers for diagnosing training pathologies.

#### Efficiency Considerations

The **59.4% thinking length reduction** (**Figure 7**) demonstrates that capability preservation and efficiency can be achieved simultaneously. Future model development should treat inference cost as a first-class optimization objective, not an afterthought.

#### Open-Source Reproducibility

The paper's use of open-source base models (Qwen2.5-VL) and publicly available benchmarks facilitates reproducibility. Future work should follow this pattern, enabling the community to build on findings rather than re-implementing closed-source pipelines.

---

### Summary: The Path Forward

RECAP establishes that reasoning gains and capability preservation are not mutually exclusive—it provides both the **method** (dynamic reweighting) and the **evidence** (**Tables 1-2**, **Figures 1-7**) to support this claim. The research pathways identified above offer concrete directions for extending these findings to larger scales, different objective types, and broader application domains.

For practitioners, the decision framework in **Section 7.4** provides actionable guidance: use RECAP when you have heterogeneous objectives, mixed-domain data, and need to preserve general capabilities. For researchers, the open questions around KL interaction, signal combination, and domain-specific forgetting offer fertile ground for follow-up work.

The broader lesson is that RLVR training requires **dynamic, diagnostics-driven optimization** rather than static, hand-tuned configurations. RECAP demonstrates one way to achieve this—but the underlying principle (monitor learning dynamics, adapt accordingly) should inform all future post-training pipelines.
