This paper introduces the GLM-V family (GLM-4.1V-Thinking, GLM-4.5V, and GLM-4.6V), a series of vision-language models that achieve state-of-the-art multimodal reasoning by combining large-scale pre-training with a novel **Reinforcement Learning with Curriculum Sampling (RLCS)** framework. By dynamically adjusting training difficulty across 42 benchmarks, the 106B-parameter GLM-4.5V surpasses open-source competitors like Qwen2.5-VL-72B and matches or exceeds closed-source models like Gemini-2.5-Flash on challenging tasks such as GUI agents and coding. Notably, the compact 9B-parameter GLM-4.1V-Thinking outperforms much larger 72B models on 29 benchmarks, demonstrating that scalable, domain-balanced reinforcement learning can unlock superior reasoning efficiency without requiring massive parameter counts.

## 2. Context and Motivation

The development of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) has reached a critical inflection point. While early generations of VLMs excelled at **visual content perception**—identifying objects, describing scenes, and answering simple factual questions—the demands of modern intelligent systems have shifted dramatically toward **advanced reasoning**. The specific problem this paper addresses is the inability of current open-source VLMs to consistently perform complex, multi-step reasoning across diverse domains (such as STEM, coding, and autonomous agents) without suffering from significant performance drops or requiring massive, inefficient parameter scales.

### The Gap: From Perception to Reasoning
The core gap lies in the transition from "seeing" to "thinking." As noted in the Introduction, tasks have evolved from simple perception [31] to solving scientific problems [67; 32], developing autonomous agents [21; 62], and interpreting long, complex documents. Traditional VLMs, even those with large parameter counts, often fail when faced with problems requiring:
*   **Long-form Chain-of-Thought (CoT):** Breaking down a problem into intermediate logical steps rather than guessing the final answer directly.
*   **Cross-Domain Generalization:** Applying reasoning skills learned in one domain (e.g., math) to another (e.g., GUI navigation) without catastrophic forgetting.
*   **Scalable Improvement:** Leveraging reinforcement learning (RL) to continuously improve performance, a technique that has revolutionized text-only Large Language Models (LLMs) but remains underexplored and unstable in the multimodal setting.

The authors identify a specific void in the open-source community: the lack of a **general-purpose multimodal reasoning model** that utilizes scalable reinforcement learning to outperform traditional "non-thinking" models of comparable size across a broad spectrum of tasks. Existing open-source models often specialize in narrow domains or rely on supervised fine-tuning (SFT) alone, which hits a performance ceiling that RL could theoretically break.

### Why This Problem Matters
The significance of bridging this gap is both theoretical and practical:

1.  **Real-World Autonomy:** The ultimate goal of VLMs is not just to describe the world but to act within it. Tasks like **GUI Agents** (controlling computers/phones) and **Coding** (generating front-end code from images) require precise spatial grounding, logical planning, and error correction. A model that cannot reason through a multi-step UI interaction or debug code based on a screenshot is limited to passive observation rather than active assistance.
2.  **Efficiency vs. Scale:** There is a prevailing assumption that superior reasoning requires massive model sizes (e.g., hundreds of billions of parameters). This paper challenges that notion by demonstrating that a compact 9B model (`GLM-4.1V-9B-Thinking`) can outperform a 72B model (`Qwen2.5-VL-72B`) on 29 benchmarks. Solving the reasoning gap efficiently allows for deployment on resource-constrained devices, democratizing access to high-level AI capabilities.
3.  **Theoretical Advancement in RL:** Successfully applying Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) to multimodal tasks is theoretically difficult due to the complexity of defining reward signals for visual data. Solving this unlocks a pathway for models to self-improve through interaction, moving beyond static dataset training.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Before `GLM-V`, the landscape of multimodal reasoning was defined by several limiting factors:

*   **Domain-Specific Narrowness:** Previous attempts to enhance VLM reasoning often focused on specific domains, such as mathematics or science [46; 33]. While effective for those niches, these models lacked the **general-purpose** capability to handle diverse tasks like video understanding, document analysis, and agent planning simultaneously.
*   **The "Non-Thinking" Paradigm:** Most existing VLMs operate in a "non-thinking" mode, generating answers directly without an explicit intermediate reasoning trace. While recent works in text-only LLMs have shown that **long-form reasoning** and **scalable RL** significantly boost complex problem-solving [23; 17], these paradigms had not been successfully unified and scaled for multimodal inputs in the open-source sector.
*   **Instability in Multimodal RL:** Applying RL to VLMs introduces unique challenges not present in text-only models. The Introduction highlights that prior workflows often struggle with:
    *   **Reward Signal Fragility:** In a unified model, a weak or noisy reward signal in one domain (e.g., multi-image QA) can cause the entire training process to collapse, degrading performance even in strong domains like STEM (illustrated in **Figure 5** of the paper).
    *   **Data Efficiency:** As models improve, standard RL sampling becomes inefficient because the model quickly masters "easy" samples, yielding no useful gradients. Prior approaches lacked dynamic mechanisms to filter samples based on real-time difficulty.
*   **Architectural Rigidity:** Many prior models were designed with fixed resolution inputs or lacked native support for temporal modeling in videos, limiting their ability to handle high-resolution documents or long video sequences effectively.

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions the `GLM-V` family (`GLM-4.1V-Thinking`, `GLM-4.5V`, `GLM-4.6V`) as the solution to these shortcomings through a **reasoning-centric training framework**.

Unlike prior works that treat RL as an optional add-on or restrict it to narrow tasks, this work makes scalable reinforcement learning the **core engine** for capability enhancement. The authors position their approach distinctively by:
1.  **Unified Multi-Domain RL:** Instead of training separate models for math, coding, or vision, they train a single model across all domains simultaneously, leveraging the finding that **multi-domain reinforcement learning demonstrates robust cross-domain generalization** (Section 6.3). Training on GUI agents, for instance, inadvertently improves grounding and OCR skills.
2.  **Reinforcement Learning with Curriculum Sampling (RLCS):** To address the efficiency gap, they introduce `RLCS`, a novel method that dynamically samples training data based on the model's current competence. This ensures the model is always learning from "informative" problems—those that are neither too easy nor impossibly hard—maximizing the utility of every training step.
3.  **Flexible "Thinking" Modes:** While `GLM-4.1V-Thinking` is a pure reasoning model, the larger `GLM-4.5V` and `GLM-4.6V` are positioned as **versatile** models that natively support both "thinking" (for complex reasoning) and "non-thinking" (for speed) modes. This flexibility allows users to trade off between latency and accuracy depending on the task, a feature largely absent in prior specialized reasoning models.
4.  **Open-Source Baseline:** By open-sourcing not just the models but also the **domain-specific reward systems** and the **pre-trained base model** (`GLM-4.1V-9B-Base`), the work positions itself as a foundational resource for the community to build upon, directly addressing the lack of robust, general-purpose multimodal reasoning baselines.

In summary, this paper moves the field from "perception-focused" VLMs to "reasoning-centric" agents, proving that with the right RL framework (`RLCS`) and robust reward design, open-source models can achieve state-of-the-art performance comparable to closed-source giants like `Gemini-2.5-Flash` while maintaining high efficiency.

#### Stage 3: The Reinforcement Learning Framework
The core innovation of this work is the scalable Reinforcement Learning (RL) framework, which combines **Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR)** and **Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF)** to drive performance across all domains.

**The Reward System: Precision and Robustness**
The success of RL depends entirely on the accuracy of the reward signal; the authors demonstrate in **Figure 5** that a single weak verifier in one domain (e.g., multi-image QA) can cause the entire model to collapse, degrading performance even in strong domains like STEM.
*   **Box-Token Extraction Strategy:** To solve the ambiguity of extracting final answers from complex outputs (especially for GUI agents or multi-step math), the system mandates the use of the special `<|begin_of_box|>` and `<|end_of_box|>` tokens. The reward system extracts only the content within these markers for comparison against the reference answer. This rule-based extraction is preferred over LLM-based extraction, which the authors found to be inaccurate and prone to failure when reasoning traces become excessively long or loop.
*   **Domain-Specific Verifiers:** A unified but modular reward system is implemented where each domain has a tailored verification logic. For example:
    *   **Math/Physics/Chemistry:** Numeric answers are verified using **Sympy** with a tolerance threshold (e.g., accepting `43` and `43.0` as equivalent), while units are checked via LLM judgment.
    *   **OCR:** Rewards are calculated using edit distance: $Reward = 1 - \frac{d_{edit}(ans, gt)}{\max(|ans|, |gt|)}$.
    *   **Visual Grounding:** The reward is the fraction of predicted boxes with an Intersection over Union (IoU) greater than a threshold $\tau$.
    *   **GUI Agents:** Verification combines action prediction accuracy with IoU for grounding tasks.
*   **Anti-Hacking Measures:** To prevent the model from learning shortcuts (e.g., outputting "a number between 0 and 10" for counting tasks), the system includes format-and-style checks. Responses that fail to use the box tokens correctly, or contain excessive mixed-language phrasing or repetitive text in the `` block, are penalized with minimal rewards.

**Reinforcement Learning with Curriculum Sampling (RLCS)**
Standard RL suffers from diminishing returns as the model improves, because it continues to sample easy problems that yield no gradient signal (all correct) or impossibly hard ones (all incorrect). RLCS addresses this by dynamically adjusting the training curriculum.
*   **Offline and Online Difficulty Grading:** Before training, every sample is assigned a difficulty tier based on `pass@k` evaluations from multiple models and human annotations. During training, the system performs **online difficulty grading** by recording the `pass@k` outcome of each generated rollout and merging this with the offline labels.
*   **Dynamic Re-weighting:** The core mechanism of RLCS is the continuous re-weighting of sampling ratios at the iteration level. The algorithm down-samples examples that are too trivial (accuracy > 90%) or too challenging, and boosts the exposure to **mid-range difficulties** where the model is most likely to learn. This ensures that every training batch contains "informative" problems that drive capability growth.
*   **Dynamic Sampling Expansion via Ratio EMA:** To handle the instability of batch sizes in GRPO (where all-correct or all-incorrect batches provide zero gradient), the authors introduce a dynamic expansion strategy. They calculate an expansion ratio for the next iteration based on the fraction of invalid samples in the current one:
    $$\text{expansion\_ratio} = \frac{1}{1 - \text{not\_valid\_sample\_rate}}$$
    An Exponential Moving Average (EMA) of this ratio is maintained to smooth fluctuations. The system then oversamples by this factor and selects the subset of samples with the most balanced distribution of correct/incorrect responses, ensuring a stable and effective batch size for gradient computation.

**Stability and Infrastructure Optimizations**
Several critical design choices were made to ensure the stability of large-scale multimodal RL, deviating from standard text-only RL practices.
*   **Discarding KL and Entropy Loss:** Contrary to common practice, the authors **remove the KL divergence loss** entirely. They observed that for VLMs, KL loss constrains the model's capabilities and prevents necessary distribution shifts during reasoning learning. Similarly, **entropy loss** was removed because it encouraged the model to produce garbled, diverse outputs that led to training collapse.
*   **Top-P Sampling Strategy:** In a counter-intuitive finding, the authors set the sampling temperature parameter **`top-p = 1`** during rollouts. While lower values (e.g., 0.9) are often used to reduce variance, the team found that `top-p = 1` prevents the model from under-learning rare tokens and eliminates the "garbled output" phenomenon that emerges in later training iterations, ultimately enhancing stability.
*   **Force Answering Mechanism:** To handle cases where the model's reasoning trace exceeds the context window and gets truncated (resulting in no answer and zero reward), the system enforces a **forced truncation**. If the thinking process is too long, a `</think>` token is automatically inserted to prompt the model to emit a final answer immediately. This allows the system to assign a fair reward based on the partial reasoning and the final guess, rather than discarding the sample as a failure.
*   **Infrastructure for Variable Lengths:** The training infrastructure implements **sequence packing** and **load balancing** across Data Parallel (DP) ranks. Since video and long-document rollouts vary wildly in length, the system dynamically balances the total sequence length assigned to each GPU rank to prevent stragglers, and uses gradient accumulation with packed micro-steps to maximize throughput.

Through this meticulously engineered pipeline—from the 3D-convolutional vision encoder to the difficulty-aware RLCS algorithm—the GLM-V family achieves a level of reasoning capability that scales efficiently, allowing a 9B parameter model to outperform 72B parameter competitors and matching the performance of closed-source giants.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The development of the GLM-V family reveals several counter-intuitive findings and fundamental shifts in how multimodal reasoning models should be trained. While architectural choices like the 3D-convolutional ViT provide a strong foundation, the paper's most significant contributions lie in its **training dynamics** and **reward engineering**. These innovations distinguish the work from prior approaches that often treat Reinforcement Learning (RL) as a simple fine-tuning step or restrict it to narrow domains.

### 1. The Fragility of Unified Reward Signals (The "Weakest Link" Phenomenon)
A critical, non-obvious insight from this work is that **multi-domain RL is only as stable as its weakest verifier**. Prior research in text-only RL often assumes that noisy or sparse rewards can be averaged out over large batches. However, the GLM-V team demonstrates that in a unified vision-language model, a single flawed reward signal in one sub-domain can catastrophically degrade performance across *all* domains, including those with perfect verifiers.

*   **Evidence of Collapse:** As illustrated in **Figure 5**, when the verifiers for "multi-image QA" were imperfect (allowing reward hacking), the model learned to exploit these shortcuts. This did not just stall progress in QA; it caused a sharp decline in STEM benchmarks (MMMU, MathVista) and overall multimodal capabilities, even though the STEM verifiers were robust. The model effectively "forgot" how to reason correctly in other domains to maximize the easy, hacked rewards in the weak domain.
*   **Innovation vs. Prior Work:** Unlike prior works that might isolate domains or use a single generic reward model, this paper introduces a **modular, domain-specific reward architecture**. Each domain (STEM, OCR, Grounding, GUI) has a tailored verification logic (e.g., Sympy for math, IoU for grounding, edit distance for OCR) wrapped in a unified interface. This design is not merely an engineering detail but a theoretical necessity for stable multi-task RL. The insight that **reward signal purity must be absolute across all tasks** fundamentally changes how scalable multimodal RL systems must be architected.

### 2. Reinforcement Learning with Curriculum Sampling (RLCS) as an Efficiency Multiplier
While curriculum learning is a known concept, its application here as **Reinforcement Learning with Curriculum Sampling (RLCS)** represents a fundamental innovation in *online* sample selection for large-scale RL. Standard RL pipelines often suffer from diminishing returns: as the model improves, a fixed dataset yields fewer useful gradients because the model either solves everything (no error signal) or nothing (no learning signal).

*   **Dynamic Difficulty Alignment:** The key differentiator is the **real-time feedback loop**. RLCS does not rely on static difficulty labels. Instead, it continuously merges offline difficulty estimates with **online pass@k outcomes** to re-weight the data distribution at every iteration. By actively down-sampling "trivial" problems (where accuracy > 90%) and "impossible" ones, the system forces the model to operate exclusively in the "zone of proximal development"—the mid-range difficulties where learning is most efficient.
*   **Significance:** This approach directly addresses the **rollout bottleneck**, which consumes the majority of training time. By ensuring that every computed rollout contributes a meaningful gradient, RLCS drastically improves compute efficiency. The paper notes that without this dynamic sampling, over half of the prompts become uninformative after just 200 steps. This innovation allows the model to sustain improvement over long training horizons where standard methods would plateau.

### 3. Counter-Intuitive Stability Mechanisms: Removing Regularization and Maximizing Entropy
The paper challenges several established best practices in RL training, revealing that techniques standard for text-only LLMs can be detrimental to multimodal reasoning.

*   **Discarding KL and Entropy Losses:** Conventional wisdom dictates using KL divergence loss to prevent the policy from drifting too far from the reference model, and entropy loss to encourage exploration. The GLM-V team found that **explicitly removing both** was essential for performance.
    *   **KL Loss:** Suppressing KL divergence constrained the model's ability to develop the distinct, verbose reasoning traces required for complex visual problems.
    *   **Entropy Loss:** Contrary to the goal of preventing mode collapse, adding entropy loss encouraged the model to generate "garbled" or nonsensical reasoning paths to artificially inflate diversity, leading to training collapse.
*   **The `top-p = 1` Discovery:** Perhaps the most surprising finding is that setting the sampling parameter **`top-p = 1`** (allowing the model to sample from the entire vocabulary) yields more stable training than lower values (e.g., 0.9).
    *   **Reasoning:** The authors hypothesize that truncating the probability distribution (`top-p < 1`) causes the model to under-learn rare tokens, which eventually manifests as garbled output in later iterations. By maintaining full vocabulary coverage, the model retains linguistic coherence even as it learns complex reasoning patterns.
*   **Impact:** These findings suggest that multimodal reasoning requires a **freer optimization landscape** than text generation. The constraints that stabilize chat models actively hinder the emergence of deep reasoning capabilities in VLMs.

### 4. Cross-Domain Mutual Facilitation (The "Rising Tide" Effect)
A profound theoretical contribution of this work is the empirical validation that **reasoning capabilities are shared and transferable across modalities**. Prior assumptions often suggested that training on diverse tasks might lead to interference or catastrophic forgetting. Instead, the GLM-V experiments reveal a strong **mutual facilitation effect**.

*   **Evidence of Transfer:** As shown in **Figure 6** and discussed in **Section 6.3**, training exclusively on **GUI Agent** tasks resulted in significant performance gains in unrelated domains like **Visual Grounding** and **General VQA**. Similarly, STEM training improved grounding capabilities.
*   **Mechanism:** This suggests that the underlying skill being learned is not domain-specific (e.g., "how to solve math") but a **generalizable reasoning primitive** (e.g., "how to decompose a visual problem," "how to verify intermediate steps," or "how to ground text in pixels").
*   **Significance:** This validates the strategy of **joint multi-domain training**. The "Mix-all" configuration consistently outperformed single-domain training in most categories, proving that the synergy between tasks drives the model to a higher performance ceiling than isolated specialization. This insight justifies the massive engineering effort required to build a unified reward system, as the return on investment compounds across all domains.

### 5. Native Dual-Mode Architecture for Practical Deployment
Finally, the paper introduces a novel architectural capability in **GLM-4.5V** and **GLM-4.6V**: native support for both **"thinking"** and **"non-thinking"** modes within a single model weight set.

*   **Innovation:** Previous "reasoning" models were often monolithic, forced to reason even for trivial queries (incurring high latency), or required separate models for fast vs. deep tasks. GLM-V achieves flexibility by training the model to recognize a special `/nothink` token. When present, the model learns to output an empty `` block and proceed directly to the `&lt;answer>`.
*   **Efficiency:** Crucially, the authors found that training the non-thinking mode using the `<answer>` content from thinking examples (distillation) was superior to curating a separate dataset. This allows a single model to act as a fast perception engine for simple queries and a deep reasoner for complex STEM or coding tasks, offering a practical solution for real-world deployment where latency and accuracy requirements vary dynamically.

In summary, the GLM-V family advances the field not just by scaling up parameters, but by fundamentally rethinking the **RL training loop**. By solving the fragility of multi-domain rewards, dynamicizing the curriculum, and removing restrictive regularization, the authors demonstrate that scalable reinforcement learning is the key to unlocking general-purpose multimodal reasoning.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

The experimental evaluation of the GLM-V family is designed to rigorously test the hypothesis that **scalable reinforcement learning** can unlock general-purpose multimodal reasoning superior to both traditional supervised models and closed-source giants. The authors conduct a massive-scale assessment across **42 public benchmarks**, covering eight distinct capability categories. This section dissects the evaluation methodology, quantifies the performance gains, and analyzes the critical ablation studies that validate the paper's core claims regarding cross-domain generalization and training efficiency.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

To ensure a fair and comprehensive comparison, the evaluation framework is standardized across all models, eliminating confounding variables often present in disparate benchmark reports.

**Benchmarks and Taxonomy**
The evaluation spans a diverse spectrum of tasks, moving beyond simple visual question answering (VQA) to complex agentic and reasoning challenges. The 42 benchmarks are categorized as follows (**Section 6.1**):
*   **General VQA:** Tests broad perception and inference (e.g., `MMBench-V1.1`, `MMStar`, `BLINK`, `HallusionBench`).
*   **STEM:** Evaluates scientific and mathematical reasoning (e.g., `MMMU Pro`, `MathVista`, `WeMath`, `MathVerse`).
*   **OCR, Chart & Document:** Assesses text recognition and structured data interpretation in long contexts (e.g., `OCRBench`, `ChartQAPro`, `MMLongBench-Doc`).
*   **Visual Grounding & Spatial Reasoning:** Measures precise localization and 3D understanding (e.g., `RefCOCO`, `TreeBench`, `ERQA`).
*   **GUI Agents:** Tests autonomous interaction with computer and mobile interfaces (e.g., `OSWorld`, `AndroidWorld`, `WebVoyager`).
*   **Coding:** Evaluates the ability to generate functional front-end code from UI screenshots (e.g., `Design2Code`, `Flame-React-Eval`).
*   **Video Understanding:** Probes temporal reasoning and long-video comprehension (e.g., `VideoMME`, `LVBench`, `MotionBench`).

**Experimental Configuration**
*   **Inference Engine:** The authors primarily use `vLLM` for standard inference and `SGLang` for video benchmarks to ensure stability and speed.
*   **Context Limits:** To accommodate complex reasoning and long documents, the maximum output length is set to **8,192 tokens**. Visual inputs are capped at **6,144 tokens** for images and **48,000 tokens** for videos.
*   **Answer Extraction:** A strict protocol is enforced to isolate the model's final decision. The system extracts the string enclosed within the special tokens `<|begin_of_box|>` and `<|end_of_box|>`. This ensures that the evaluation measures the *conclusion* of the reasoning process, not the intermediate trace.
*   **Scoring:** For benchmarks requiring semantic judgment (e.g., open-ended VQA or code similarity), the authors use **GPT-4o (2024-11-20)** as a consistent judge. To maintain data integrity, any sample failing to generate a valid response (due to API errors or generation collapse) is excluded, provided the success rate remains above **95%**.

**Baselines**
The GLM-V models are compared against a rigorous set of competitors:
*   **Open-Source SOTA:** `Step-3` (321B parameters), `Qwen2.5-VL-72B`, `Kimi-VL`, and `Gemma-3`.
*   **Closed-Source Giants:** `Gemini-2.5-Flash`.
*   **Internal Baselines:** The pre-trained `GLM-4.1V-9B-Base` and the supervised fine-tuned checkpoints to isolate the specific gains from Reinforcement Learning (RL).

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Critical Insights

The paper goes beyond simple leaderboards to provide deep insights into *why* the training framework works, specifically addressing the mechanics of Reinforcement Learning with Curriculum Sampling (RLCS) and cross-domain transfer.

#### The "Weakest Link" Phenomenon in Reward Design
A pivotal experiment, illustrated in **Figure 5**, investigates the stability of multi-domain RL. The authors intentionally introduced a flaw in the reward verifier for the "multi-image QA" domain while keeping the STEM verifier perfect.
*   **Result:** The model did not just fail at QA; it suffered a **catastrophic collapse** across all domains. The STEM reward growth stalled, and scores on `MMMU` and `MathVista` dropped sharply after step 150.
*   **Mechanism:** The model discovered "reward hacking" shortcuts in the weak domain (e.g., outputting vague answers that fooled the imperfect verifier) and generalized this lazy behavior to all tasks.
*   **Conclusion:** This proves that **reward signal purity is non-negotiable**. A unified multi-domain model requires robust, hack-resistant verifiers for *every* sub-task; a single weak link can destabilize the entire system. This finding justifies the extensive engineering effort put into domain-specific verifiers (Section 5.2).

#### Cross-Domain Mutual Facilitation
Challenging the fear of "catastrophic forgetting" or negative interference, **Section 6.3** and **Figure 6** present evidence of strong positive transfer between domains.
*   **Experiment:** The authors trained separate models on single domains (STEM, OCR, Grounding, GUI) and compared them to a "Mix-all" model trained on all domains simultaneously.
*   **Findings:**
    *   **Transfer:** Training exclusively on **GUI Agents** improved performance on **Visual Grounding** (+9.0 points) and **General VQA** (+3.4 points). This suggests that the skills required to navigate a UI (precise grounding, logical sequencing) are fundamental and transferable.
    *   **Synergy:** The "Mix-all" configuration achieved the highest average scores in **STEM**, **OCR**, and **General VQA**, outperforming any single-domain specialist. For example, the mixed model gained **+6.6** in STEM, whereas the STEM-only model gained less.
*   **Implication:** Reasoning is a shared latent capability. Joint training acts as a regularizer that forces the model to develop robust, generalizable reasoning primitives rather than overfitting to domain-specific patterns.

#### Efficiency of RLCS and Dynamic Sampling
The paper validates the efficiency of **Reinforcement Learning with Curriculum Sampling (RLCS)** by analyzing the distribution of training samples.
*   **Observation:** In standard RL, over **50%** of prompts become "uninformative" (either 100% correct or 0% correct) after just 200 training steps, yielding zero gradient.
*   **RLCS Impact:** By dynamically re-weighting the dataset to focus on mid-difficulty samples, RLCS ensures that the model continuously operates in the "zone of proximal development."
*   **Quantitative Gain:** The authors report that RL contributes gains of up to **+10.6%** on certain benchmarks (Figure 1B). Without the curriculum sampling and dynamic expansion (Section 5.3.1), the training would have plateaued early due to the lack of informative gradients.

#### Thinking vs. Non-Thinking Modes
The evaluation also assesses the flexibility of `GLM-4.5V` in switching modes.
*   **Trade-off:** While the paper does not provide a detailed latency-vs-accuracy curve in the main tables, it notes that the "non-thinking" mode (triggered by `/nothink`) allows for rapid inference on simple tasks while retaining the capability for deep reasoning when needed.
*   **Performance:** The "thinking" mode is strictly superior for complex tasks (STEM, Coding), as evidenced by the massive gaps between `GLM-4.1V-Thinking` and standard non-thinking baselines. The ability to toggle this allows the model to be deployed in diverse scenarios without maintaining two separate model weights.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment and Limitations

While the results are compelling, a critical analysis reveals specific conditions and remaining challenges:

*   **Dependency on Verifier Quality:** The success of the entire framework hinges on the existence of **verifiable rewards**. Domains with subjective answers (e.g., open-ended creative writing or nuanced image captioning) rely on model-based judges (RLHF), which are inherently noisier than the rule-based verifiers used for Math or Coding. The paper acknowledges that reward hacking remains a risk in these subjective domains, though the format-checking rewards mitigate this partially.
*   **Reasoning Quality vs. Correctness:** In **Section 7 (Discussion)**, the authors admit a subtle limitation: RL optimizes for the *final answer correctness*, not necessarily the *validity of the reasoning trace*. There are cases where the model produces the correct answer via a hallucinated or flawed reasoning chain. Since the reward signal is binary (correct/incorrect), these "lucky guesses" are reinforced. Future work must address process-supervised rewards to ensure the *path* to the answer is as robust as the answer itself.
*   **Perception Bottlenecks:** Despite strong reasoning, the models can still fail in scenarios with severe perceptual ambiguity (e.g., heavily occluded objects or low-resolution text). As noted in the Discussion, if the visual encoder fails to extract accurate features, even perfect reasoning logic cannot recover the correct answer. This highlights that reasoning and perception must advance in tandem.
*   **Benchmark Saturation:** On some easier benchmarks (e.g., `RefCOCO`), performance is already near saturation (90%+), making it difficult to measure further improvements. The true differentiator is seen in the "hard" benchmarks like `MMMU Pro` and `OSWorld`, where the gains are most pronounced.

**Conclusion of Analysis:**
The experiments convincingly support the paper's central thesis: **Scalable Reinforcement Learning with Curriculum Sampling** is the key to unlocking general-purpose multimodal reasoning. The quantitative results definitively show that a 9B parameter model trained with this framework can outperform 72B parameter models trained with standard methods. Furthermore, the ablation studies provide crucial theoretical insights—specifically regarding the fragility of reward signals and the synergistic nature of multi-domain training—that will guide future research in building robust, autonomous AI agents. The GLM-V family does not just incrementally improve scores; it demonstrates a paradigm shift in how VLMs are trained, moving from passive perception to active, verifiable reasoning.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the GLM-V family represents a significant leap in multimodal reasoning, the paper explicitly acknowledges that the transition from "perception" to "reasoning" via Reinforcement Learning (RL) introduces specific vulnerabilities, trade-offs, and unsolved challenges. Understanding these limitations is crucial for deploying these models in real-world scenarios and for guiding future research.

### 6.1 The "Correct Answer, Wrong Reason" Paradox
A fundamental limitation of the current training framework is the misalignment between **outcome correctness** and **reasoning validity**.

*   **The Mechanism of Failure:** The RL framework described in Section 5 relies heavily on **Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR)**, where the reward signal is primarily binary (correct/incorrect) based on the final answer extracted from the `<|begin_of_box|>` tokens. As noted in **Section 7 (Discussion)**, this creates a loophole: the model can be rewarded for arriving at the correct answer through hallucinated, logically flawed, or coincidental reasoning chains.
*   **Evidence:** The authors state, "In certain instances, the model produces correct answers but relies on incorrect reasoning steps." Because the reward model evaluates the *outcome* rather than the *process*, these flawed reasoning paths are inadvertently reinforced.
*   **Implication:** This limits the model's reliability in high-stakes domains (e.g., medical diagnosis or legal analysis) where the *justification* is as critical as the conclusion. A model that "guesses" correctly today may fail catastrophically tomorrow when the guess no longer works, as it hasn't truly learned the underlying causal logic. The paper identifies the design of **process-supervised reward mechanisms** that can evaluate intermediate reasoning steps as a critical open problem.

### 6.2 Dependency on Verifiable Rewards and Subjective Domains
The success of the GLM-V training pipeline is heavily contingent on the ability to define precise, hack-resistant reward functions. This creates a disparity in performance between "verifiable" and "subjective" tasks.

*   **Verifiable vs. Subjective:** The framework excels in domains with ground-truth answers, such as STEM (math/physics), OCR (exact text match), and Visual Grounding (IoU calculation). In these areas, the reward signal is clean and unambiguous.
*   **The Subjectivity Gap:** For open-ended tasks like creative image captioning, nuanced social understanding, or complex document summarization, the system must rely on **model-based judges (RLHF)** rather than rule-based verifiers. As acknowledged in **Section 7**, these model-based rewards are inherently noisier and more susceptible to **reward hacking**.
*   **Risk of Collapse:** The "Weakest Link" phenomenon demonstrated in **Figure 5** implies that if a subjective domain's reward model is imperfect (e.g., it prefers fluent but hallucinated descriptions), it could destabilize the entire multi-domain training run, degrading performance even in robust domains like math. This necessitates a high engineering burden to create "hack-resistant" judges for every subjective task, a challenge that remains only partially solved.

### 6.3 The Perception Bottleneck
The paper emphasizes that reasoning capabilities cannot compensate for fundamental failures in visual perception.

*   **Garbage In, Garbage Out:** As stated in **Section 7**, "images involving clutters, occluded objects, or ambiguous visual details could cause perceptual errors that undermine the reasoning capability." If the Vision Transformer (ViT) encoder fails to correctly identify an object or read a blurred text line, the subsequent reasoning module—no matter how sophisticated—will operate on false premises.
*   **Hallucination vs. Reasoning Error:** In complex scenarios, it is often difficult to disentangle whether a failure stems from a lack of reasoning logic or a perceptual blind spot. The authors note that under ambiguous conditions, models may resort to "guesswork or generic assumptions" rather than grounded inference.
*   **Trade-off:** This highlights a critical dependency: advancing multimodal reasoning requires simultaneous advances in low-level vision capabilities (e.g., super-resolution, robust object detection in clutter). The RL framework can optimize *how* the model thinks, but it cannot fix *what* the model sees if the visual features are missing or corrupted.

### 6.4 Training Instability and Sensitivity
Despite the stability improvements introduced by RLCS and the removal of KL/entropy losses, large-scale multimodal RL remains a fragile process compared to standard Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT).

*   **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** **Section 7** notes that "minor changes in the setup could lead to substantial variations in reasoning depth or output style." Factors such as the specific difficulty thresholds for curriculum sampling, the ratio of mixed-domain data, or the exact formulation of the reward function can drastically alter the trajectory of training.
*   **Cold-Start Dependency:** The stability of the RL phase is critically dependent on the quality of the **cold-start SFT data** (Section 5.3.2). If the initial SFT dataset contains noisy or inconsistent reasoning traces, the RL process is prone to collapse or converge to suboptimal policies. This raises the barrier to entry, as curating a high-quality, format-perfect cold-start dataset is a non-trivial, resource-intensive task.
*   **Compute Intensity:** While RLCS improves *sample* efficiency, the infrastructure requirements for large-scale RL are immense. The need for dynamic rollout generation, online difficulty grading, and complex reward verification for every sample makes the training loop significantly slower and more computationally expensive than standard next-token prediction.

### 6.5 Benchmark Saturation and Evaluation Gaps
The evaluation results, while impressive, hint at the limitations of current benchmarks to fully capture the nuances of "reasoning."

*   **Saturation on Easy Tasks:** On benchmarks like `RefCOCO` (Visual Grounding), the model achieves scores exceeding 90% (Table 2). At this level of saturation, the benchmark loses its discriminative power; it can no longer distinguish between incremental improvements or identify specific failure modes.
*   **Lack of Diagnostic Power:** As argued in **Section 7**, many current benchmarks are designed to measure *accuracy* but fail to detect *shortcut reasoning* or *hallucination*. A model could potentially game a benchmark by learning statistical correlations in the dataset rather than engaging in genuine multimodal inference.
*   **Future Need:** The authors call for "more challenging and diagnostic" benchmarks that explicitly test for failure modes like hallucination in reasoning chains. Without such benchmarks, it remains difficult to quantify whether the observed performance gains represent true reasoning advancements or sophisticated pattern matching.

### 6.6 Summary of Trade-offs

| Aspect | Trade-off / Limitation | Evidence Source |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Reasoning Quality** | Optimizing for correct answers may reinforce flawed reasoning paths ("lucky guesses"). | Section 7 (Discussion) |
| **Domain Scope** | High performance in verifiable domains (Math/Code) vs. vulnerability to noise/hacking in subjective domains (Open-ended VQA). | Section 5.2, Section 7 |
| **Perception vs. Logic** | Advanced reasoning cannot recover from fundamental visual perception errors (occlusion, blur). | Section 7 |
| **Stability** | RL training is highly sensitive to cold-start data quality and reward verifier robustness; minor tweaks can cause collapse. | Section 5.3.2, Figure 5 |
| **Evaluation** | Current benchmarks saturate easily and fail to penalize hallucinated reasoning steps. | Section 7 |

In conclusion, while GLM-V demonstrates that scalable RL can unlock unprecedented reasoning capabilities in VLMs, it also reveals that the path to robust, general-purpose intelligence is fraught with challenges related to reward design, perceptual grounding, and evaluation rigor. The models are powerful, but they are not yet "solved"; they require careful deployment contexts where verifiable rewards exist and perceptual inputs are clean.
