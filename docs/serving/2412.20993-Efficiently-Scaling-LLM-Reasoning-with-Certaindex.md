## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the critical inefficiency in Large Language Model (LLM) reasoning, where algorithms like Chain-of-Thought and Self-Consistency often waste up to 3× more tokens than necessary due to "self-doubt" and a lack of early termination mechanisms. The authors introduce `Certaindex`, an algorithm-agnostic metric that quantifies answer stabilization to signal when further computation is unlikely to change the result, and implement it within `Dynasor`, a reasoning-aware serving system. By leveraging `Certaindex` for dynamic token allocation and gang scheduling, the system achieves up to **50% compute savings** in batch workloads and **3.3× higher throughput** in online serving across models like **DeepSeek-R1** and **Qwen2.5** on datasets including **MATH500** and **AIME24**, all without sacrificing accuracy.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Efficiency Crisis in Test-Time Scaling
The central problem this paper addresses is the **systematic token inefficiency** inherent in modern "test-time scaling" algorithms. As Large Language Models (LLMs) have evolved, the community has shifted from simply scaling model parameters to scaling *inference compute*. Techniques such as **Chain-of-Thought (CoT)**, **Self-Consistency (SC)**, and **Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)** allow models to solve complex reasoning tasks by generating more tokens—essentially "thinking" longer before answering. While effective, the paper identifies a critical flaw: these algorithms lack a mechanism to detect **diminishing returns**.

The authors observe that reasoning models frequently engage in **token overuse**, generating vast amounts of text that do not improve the final answer. This phenomenon is quantified in **Figure 1**, which compares a standard instruct model against a reasoning-optimized model (Qwen2.5 7B) on the MATH-500 dataset. The graph reveals that while the reasoning model eventually achieves higher peak accuracy, it requires up to **4.5× more tokens** to reach the *same* accuracy level as the non-reasoning model. In extreme cases, such as with **DeepSeek-R1** on the AMC23 dataset (**Figure 2**), the model generates a median of **2.7K tokens**, whereas the correct answer could have been derived with a median of only **830 tokens**. This represents a massive waste of computational resources, energy, and latency in production environments.

### The Phenomenon of "Self-Doubt" and Answer Stabilization
To understand *why* this inefficiency occurs, the authors conduct a deep dive into the generation behavior of reasoning models, identifying a pattern they term **"self-doubt."** As illustrated in **Figure 3**, a reasoning model often arrives at the correct solution early in its generation process (e.g., within the first 300 tokens) but fails to stop. Instead, it continues to generate tokens to:
*   Re-evaluate already correct steps.
*   Check for completeness unnecessarily.
*   Engage in "perspective bridging" or re-verification loops.
*   Build artificial confidence through repetitive phrasing.

Crucially, the paper posits that despite this verbose "chatter," the model's internal state regarding the *final answer* often stabilizes. The authors introduce the concept of **answer stabilization**: the observation that intermediate solutions cease to change after a certain point, regardless of whether the model continues to generate text. If a model outputs the same intermediate answer across several consecutive reasoning steps, it signals that the model has "settled," and further computation is unlikely to alter the outcome. The gap in current technology is the inability to detect this stabilization signal in real-time to trigger an **early exit**.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Before `Certaindex`, efforts to optimize LLM inference focused primarily on system-level mechanics rather than the *semantic progress* of the reasoning itself. Existing approaches fall into three categories, each with significant shortcomings regarding reasoning efficiency:

1.  **System-Level Optimizations (Batching & Memory Management):**
    Prior systems like **vLLM** (paged attention), **SGLang**, and **ParrotServe** optimize how requests are batched, how KV-cache memory is paged, and how prefixes are shared.
    *   *Shortcoming:* These systems treat requests as independent, static units of work. They optimize *how* tokens are generated (throughput/memory) but not *whether* those tokens need to be generated in the first place. They lack visibility into the logical progress of a reasoning chain.

2.  **Static Resource Allocation:**
    Current deployments of reasoning algorithms typically use fixed budgets (e.g., "generate 5 samples for Self-Consistency" or "allow 4096 tokens for CoT").
    *   *Shortcoming:* This "one-size-fits-all" approach is inefficient. Easy problems receive the same massive compute budget as hard problems, leading to the token overuse described above. Conversely, hard problems might be cut off arbitrarily before a solution is found if the static budget is too low.

3.  **Model-Specific Uncertainty Estimation:**
    Academic research has explored uncertainty estimation via **log-probability entropy**, **semantic metrics**, or analyzing **hidden states**.
    *   *Shortcoming:* Many of these methods are either computationally expensive (requiring additional forward passes or complex hidden state analysis) or are tightly coupled to specific model architectures. Furthermore, they often measure *confidence* in a general sense rather than *stability* of the specific reasoning trajectory. They do not provide a unified, lightweight interface that can be plugged directly into a serving engine to control dynamic token allocation across diverse algorithms (CoT, SC, MCTS, Rebase).

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself at the intersection of **algorithmic reasoning** and **systems scheduling**. Unlike prior work that treats the reasoning algorithm as a black box to be executed, this work opens the black box to monitor its internal convergence.

*   **From Static to Dynamic:** The paper moves the paradigm from static token budgets to **dynamic, data-driven allocation**. By introducing `Certaindex`, the authors provide a universal metric that quantifies "evolving stability."
*   **Algorithm-Agnostic Design:** While previous optimizations were often specific to CoT or specific to search algorithms, `Certaindex` is designed to work across the spectrum. It normalizes confidence signals whether they come from answer consistency in CoT, semantic entropy in Self-Consistency, or reward scores in MCTS/Rebase.
*   **System Integration:** The work distinguishes itself by not just proposing a metric, but implementing it as a core scheduler (`Dynasor`) within a real serving engine (SGLang). It demonstrates that monitoring reasoning progress can directly drive **gang scheduling** (grouping related sub-queries) and **early exiting**, achieving theoretical efficiency gains in practical, deployed systems.

In summary, the paper argues that the next frontier of LLM efficiency is not just faster matrix multiplication or better memory management, but **smarter termination**. By recognizing that LLMs signal when they "know" the answer, we can reclaim the up to **50% of wasted compute** currently spent on unnecessary "self-doubt."

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a systems-algorithm co-design that introduces `Certaindex`, a unified metric for quantifying reasoning stability, and implements it within `Dynasor`, a reasoning-aware serving scheduler that dynamically allocates compute resources based on real-time convergence signals. The core idea is to treat the "settling" of an LLM's answer not as a passive outcome, but as an active control signal to terminate generation early or reprioritize batched requests, thereby eliminating the token waste caused by "self-doubt."

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system being built is a smart traffic controller for LLM reasoning tasks that sits between the user's request and the GPU, constantly monitoring how "sure" the model is of its answer to decide whether to let it keep thinking or stop it immediately. It solves the problem of wasted computation by replacing fixed, rigid token budgets with a dynamic, feedback-driven loop that detects when a model's reasoning has stabilized, allowing the system to cut off unnecessary processing steps without losing accuracy.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The `Dynasor` architecture consists of three logically distinct layers that interact in a tight feedback loop: the **Reasoning Program Abstraction**, the **Application Runtime**, and the **System Runtime**.
*   **Reasoning Program Abstraction:** This is the interface layer where diverse reasoning algorithms (like Self-Consistency or MCTS) are wrapped into a standard format; its responsibility is to expose two critical functions: `execute()` to run a step of reasoning and `update_certaindex()` to calculate the current stability score.
*   **Application Runtime:** Acting as the brain of the operation, this component hosts the **Intra-Program Scheduler**, which continuously polls the `Certaindex` value from the abstraction layer to make per-query decisions on whether to allocate more tokens, reduce the budget, or trigger an early exit.
*   **System Runtime:** This is the muscle that manages the physical GPU resources; it contains the **Inter-Program Scheduler** which uses the stability signals to perform "gang scheduling" (grouping related sub-requests) and implements prefix caching to minimize memory overhead across the batch.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   First, we define the mathematical foundation of `Certaindex`, explaining how it normalizes different types of uncertainty signals (entropy, reward scores) into a single 0-to-1 scale.
*   Second, we detail the "Probe-In-The-Middle" mechanism for Chain-of-Thought models, which forces intermediate answer extraction to measure consistency over time.
*   Third, we explain the **Intra-Program Scheduler**, describing the thresholding logic that translates a `Certaindex` score into a concrete action (stop or continue).
*   Fourth, we analyze the **Inter-Program Scheduler**, focusing on how "gang scheduling" and Shortest-Job-First (SJF) approximations leverage these signals to optimize batch throughput.
*   Finally, we provide the theoretical justification, outlining the proof that answer stability implies convergence to the stationary distribution, ensuring that early exiting does not degrade accuracy.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### The Certaindex Metric: Unifying Stability Signals
The fundamental innovation of this work is `Certaindex`, a scalar value between 0 and 1 that quantifies the stability of a reasoning process, where a value of 1 indicates the model has fully converged on an answer and 0 indicates maximum uncertainty. The authors recognize that different reasoning algorithms produce different types of confidence signals, so `Certaindex` provides specific instantiation formulas for two primary categories of algorithms: those generating multiple paths and those utilizing reward models.

For algorithms that generate multiple reasoning paths to aggregate a final answer, such as **Self-Consistency (SC)**, **MCTS**, and **Rebase**, the system measures certainty using **semantic entropy**. The process begins by generating $n$ independent reasoning paths for a given question and clustering their final answers into $m$ distinct groups, denoted as $C_1, C_2, \dots, C_m$, where $|C_i|$ represents the count of paths falling into group $i$. The system calculates the empirical entropy $H$ of this distribution using the standard formula:
$$ H = -\sum_{i=1}^{m} \frac{|C_i|}{n} \log \left( \frac{|C_i|}{n} \right) $$
High entropy implies the answers are scattered across many different groups (high uncertainty), while low entropy implies they are concentrated in one group (high certainty). To create a normalized metric where higher is better, the system computes `Certaindex` ($\tilde{H}$) by subtracting the observed entropy from the maximum possible entropy ($\log n$) and dividing by that maximum:
$$ \tilde{H} = \frac{\log n - H}{\log n} $$
This ensures that if all $n$ paths yield unique answers, $\tilde{H} = 0$, and if all paths yield the exact same answer, $\tilde{H} = 1$. For tasks with flexible outputs like code or complex math expressions where exact string matching fails, the system employs a lightweight embedding model (e.g., 100M parameters) to compute pairwise semantic similarities before clustering, adding negligible overhead compared to the LLM inference itself.

For algorithms that rely on a **reward model** to guide search, such as **MCTS** and **Rebase**, `Certaindex` simplifies to the normalized output of the reward model itself. Since these algorithms already produce a score $R \in [0, 1]$ for each reasoning path during normal execution, the system aggregates these scores to form the metric: for MCTS, it takes the average reward across all explored paths, while for Rebase, it takes the maximum reward found. This design choice leverages existing computation without requiring additional forward passes, adhering to the principle of zero-overhead monitoring.

#### Probe-In-The-Middle: Detecting Convergence in Chain-of-Thought
For internalized Chain-of-Thought (CoT) models that generate a single long sequence of tokens, the system cannot rely on multiple path aggregation, so it employs a technique called **"Probe-In-The-Middle"** to artificially create a sequence of intermediate answers for consistency checking. The mechanism works by interrupting the model's generation at fixed token intervals (e.g., every 64 tokens) and appending a specific prompt: `"Oh, I suddenly got the answer to the whole problem. Final Answer: boxed{"`. This prompt forces the model to prematurely output its current best guess, which the system records as an intermediate answer $y_k$ before discarding the probe tokens and resuming the original reasoning chain.

Once a sequence of $m$ probed answers $\{y_1, y_2, \dots, y_m\}$ is collected, the system assesses certainty by measuring the consistency of these answers over a sliding window of width $w$. The consistency score $C_k$ at step $k$ is calculated as the fraction of answers in the window that match the current answer $y_k$:
$$ C_k = \frac{1}{w} \sum_{j=k-w+1}^{k} \mathbb{I}[y_j = y_k] $$
where $\mathbb{I}[\cdot]$ is the indicator function that returns 1 if the condition is true and 0 otherwise. If this consistency score $C_k$ exceeds a predefined threshold $\tau$ (where $\tau \in (0, 1]$), the system deems the model sufficiently certain and triggers an early exit. To further robustness, the system includes a **Post-Generation Validation** step that scans for linguistic markers of uncertainty (e.g., "wait", "hmm", "let me double-check"); if these are detected in a probed answer, that specific response is omitted from the consistency calculation to prevent false positives where a model confidently repeats a wrong answer while verbally doubting it.

#### Dynasor Application Runtime: Intra-Program Scheduling
The **Application Runtime** serves as the decision-making engine that translates `Certaindex` values into resource allocation actions via the **Intra-Program Scheduler**. When a reasoning program arrives, the scheduler initializes it with a maximum resource cap (e.g., a maximum number of branches for SC or iterations for MCTS) and a specific detection step, known as `Detect@knob`, which determines when to first evaluate the metric. For example, in the Self-Consistency experiments on GSM8K, the `Detect@knob` is set to 5, meaning the system evaluates certainty after the first 5 branches are generated.

The scheduler operates on a continuous loop: as the program executes, it requests resources for the next step, and the scheduler checks the current `Certaindex` against a calibrated threshold. If the metric exceeds the threshold (e.g., $\tilde{H} > 0.7$ for SC on MATH), the scheduler denies further resource allocation, signaling the program to terminate and aggregate its current results. The paper emphasizes that threshold selection is critical; for instance, on the SC-GSM8K workload, an entropy threshold of 0.5 was chosen because higher thresholds led to aggressive termination and accuracy drops, while lower thresholds failed to save sufficient tokens. These thresholds are not hardcoded but are determined via a **profiler-guided calibration** process where the system runs a labeled dataset to find the optimal cutoff that maximizes token savings while maintaining a target accuracy level.

#### Dynasor System Runtime: Inter-Program Scheduling and Gang Execution
Beyond managing individual queries, the **System Runtime** optimizes the execution of multiple concurrent reasoning programs through an **Inter-Program Scheduler** that implements **Gang Scheduling** and an approximate **Shortest-Job-First (SJF)** policy. Gang Scheduling addresses the inefficiency of interleaving requests from different reasoning programs; instead, it groups all sub-requests belonging to the same logical program (e.g., all 20 branches of a Single-Consistency query) into the same batch. This design choice maximizes **KV-cache reuse**, as all requests in the gang share the same prompt prefix, significantly reducing memory bandwidth pressure and latency.

The scheduler further enhances throughput by approximating SJF to mitigate head-of-line blocking, prioritizing programs that are predicted to finish soonest. Since the exact length of a reasoning program is unknown beforehand, the system estimates the total execution time by combining the remaining compute requirements (controlled by `Certaindex`) with the historical average token length per iteration. If a program's `Certaindex` indicates high stability, the scheduler predicts a short remaining life and prioritizes it to clear the queue faster. To prevent starvation of long-running complex queries, the system implements a **priority escalation mechanism**: if a program waits longer than a fairness threshold, its priority is artificially boosted, ensuring that the finish-time fairness metric $\phi = T_{shared} / \#\text{output tokens}$ remains balanced across all users.

#### Theoretical Grounding for Early Exiting
The validity of terminating generation based on `Certaindex` rests on a theoretical proof that answer stability implies convergence to the model's stationary distribution. The authors formalize the reasoning process as a sequence of next-token distributions $P_t(Y_{t+1} | x, Y_{1:t})$ and assume that for any solvable problem, this sequence eventually converges to a stationary distribution $P^*(Y|x)$. The core argument is that if the empirical mixture distributions of the generated answers remain stable over a sufficiently large window of steps $k$, then the underlying true distributions must also be close to each other within a small error bound $\epsilon$.

Specifically, the paper defines the mixture distribution $\bar{P}_i^{i+k}$ as the average of the distributions from step $i$ to $i+k$. Using concentration bounds (Lemma 1), the authors show that if the number of probe steps $k$ satisfies $k = \tilde{\Omega}\left(\frac{M + \log(1/\delta)}{\epsilon^2}\right)$, where $M$ is the number of disjoint output groups, then the empirical estimate $\hat{P}$ is $\epsilon/3$-close to the true mixture $\bar{P}$ with probability $1-\delta$. Consequently, if the Total Variation (TV) distance between consecutive empirical mixture distributions is below a threshold $\epsilon'$, the triangle inequality guarantees that the true underlying distributions are $\epsilon$-close. This mathematical guarantee justifies the engineering decision: observing stability in the "Probe-In-The-Middle" outputs is a statistically sound proxy for knowing that further computation will not significantly alter the final answer distribution, thus allowing safe early termination.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The paper's primary contribution lies not merely in optimizing a specific algorithm, but in reframing LLM reasoning as a **dynamic, observable control process** rather than a static execution task. The following innovations distinguish this work from prior efforts in efficiency and serving systems.

### 4.1 The Paradigm Shift: From "Compute Budgeting" to "Convergence Detection"
Prior approaches to LLM efficiency operate on a **static budgeting** model: system administrators assign a fixed token limit (e.g., 4,096 tokens) or a fixed number of samples (e.g., $N=10$ for Self-Consistency) regardless of the query's difficulty. This "one-size-fits-all" approach inevitably leads to the token overuse documented in **Figure 1**, where easy problems consume resources intended for hard ones.

**Certaindex** introduces a fundamental innovation by shifting the control logic from **pre-defined limits** to **real-time convergence detection**.
*   **Why it is different:** Unlike previous uncertainty estimation methods that rely on hidden states or log-probabilities (which measure *confidence* in a single step), `Certaindex` measures **trajectory stability**. It answers the question: "Has the model's answer stopped changing?" rather than "How likely is this specific token?"
*   **Significance:** This enables **adaptive compute allocation**. As shown in **Figure 6**, this shift allows the system to slash token usage by **11–29%** on average (and up to **81%** for the top 1% of easiest problems on MATH500) while maintaining identical accuracy. It transforms the reasoning process from a blind march toward a token limit into a targeted search that terminates the moment the solution stabilizes.

### 4.2 Algorithm-Agnostic Unification of Reasoning Signals
A major barrier to deploying efficient reasoning systems is the heterogeneity of algorithms: Chain-of-Thought (CoT) relies on sequential consistency, Self-Consistency (SC) on majority voting, and MCTS/Rebase on reward scores. Prior optimizations typically require custom engineering for each algorithm.

**Certaindex** provides a **unified interface** that normalizes these disparate signals into a single $[0, 1]$ metric.
*   **Why it is different:** The paper demonstrates that semantic entropy (for SC), reward aggregation (for MCTS/Rebase), and sliding-window consistency (for CoT via "Probe-In-The-Middle") all exhibit the same underlying property: **diminishing variance as compute increases**. By abstracting these into a single `Certaindex` value, the system decouples the *scheduling policy* from the *reasoning algorithm*.
*   **Significance:** This generality allows `Dynasor` to apply the same scheduling logic across diverse workloads without code changes to the core engine. **Figure 7** validates this, showing consistent token savings of **9–52%** across SC, MCTS, and Rebase. This suggests a new standard for LLM serving: reasoning algorithms should expose a "stability signal" as a first-class citizen, enabling universal optimization layers.

### 4.3 Reasoning-Aware Gang Scheduling
Traditional LLM schedulers (like those in vLLM or standard SGLang) treat individual generation requests as independent units, optimizing for memory paging and prefix caching but ignoring logical dependencies between sub-requests.

**Dynasor** introduces **Gang Scheduling** specifically tailored for "reasoning programs" (collections of related sub-queries).
*   **Why it is different:** Instead of interleaving branches from different problems (which fragments the KV-cache and increases context switching), the inter-program scheduler explicitly groups all sub-requests belonging to the same reasoning program into a single batch. Furthermore, it leverages `Certaindex` to approximate **Shortest-Job-First (SJF)** scheduling: programs with high certainty (predicted short remaining life) are prioritized to clear the queue.
*   **Significance:** This architectural change yields massive throughput gains in online settings. As detailed in **Figure 8**, Dynasor achieves **1.6–3.3× higher sustainable request rates** and supports **1.3–4.7× tighter latency SLOs** compared to baselines like SGLang and Parrot. Crucially, **Figure 20** shows that this prioritization also improves **finish-time fairness**, proving that intelligent scheduling can benefit both system throughput and user experience simultaneously.

### 4.4 Theoretical Guarantees for Early Exiting
A common critique of early-exit mechanisms is the risk of "premature termination"—stopping before the model has fully reasoned, leading to accuracy drops. Many prior heuristic approaches lack formal bounds on this risk.

This paper provides a **theoretical proof** (Section 3.4 and Appendix E) that links empirical stability to distributional convergence.
*   **Why it is different:** The authors prove that if the empirical mixture distributions of answers remain stable over a window $k$ (determined by concentration bounds involving the number of output groups $M$ and error tolerance $\epsilon$), then the underlying true distributions are $\epsilon$-close to the stationary distribution $P^*$.
*   **Significance:** This transforms early exiting from a heuristic gamble into a **statistically grounded strategy**. It assures practitioners that observing stability via `Certaindex` is a mathematically valid proxy for knowing that further computation yields diminishing returns. This theoretical backing is essential for deploying such systems in high-stakes environments where accuracy guarantees are non-negotiable.

### 4.5 Non-Invasive System Integration
Finally, the implementation of `Dynasor` represents a significant engineering innovation in its **non-invasive design**.
*   **Why it is different:** Many efficiency techniques require model fine-tuning, architectural changes, or complex external validators. In contrast, `Dynasor` acts as a lightweight scheduler layer (~500 lines of core code changes to SGLang) that requires **no modification to model weights** or the underlying execution backend.
*   **Significance:** This low barrier to entry means the benefits of `Certaindex` can be realized on existing, pre-trained reasoning models (like DeepSeek-R1 or Qwen-QWQ) immediately. It demonstrates that substantial efficiency gains (up to **50% compute savings**) can be achieved purely through smarter system-level orchestration, making it a highly practical solution for immediate deployment in production LLM clouds.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

The authors conduct a rigorous, multi-faceted evaluation to validate `Certaindex` and the `Dynasor` system. The experiments are designed to answer two distinct questions: (1) Can `Certaindex` reduce token consumption in **batch inference** without sacrificing accuracy? and (2) Can `Dynasor` improve throughput and latency SLO attainment in **online serving** scenarios? The evaluation spans diverse reasoning algorithms (CoT, SC, MCTS, Rebase), model scales (7B to 34B+), and datasets ranging from grade-school math to competitive programming.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

The experimental design carefully separates **offline batch workloads** (focusing on token-efficiency curves) from **online serving workloads** (focusing on latency and throughput under load).

**Datasets and Models:**
The evaluation covers a broad spectrum of difficulty and domains:
*   **Mathematical Reasoning:** `MATH500` (general competition math), `AIME24` (high-difficulty American Invitational Mathematics Examination), `AMC23` (American Mathematics Competitions), `GSM8K` (grade school math), and `ASDiv` (arithmetic).
*   **Code Generation:** `LiveCodeBench` (holistic code evaluation).
*   **Models:** The authors test distilled reasoning models based on **Qwen2.5** (7B, 14B, 32B) and the large-scale **DeepSeek-R1**. For search-based algorithms (SC, MCTS, Rebase), they utilize **Llama3.1-8B**, **Llama2-7B**, and **Llemma** (7B/34B) as base generators, often paired with reward models like **Skywork-7B** or **Llemma-34B**.

**Baselines:**
To ensure fair comparison, the authors establish strong baselines representing current state-of-the-art practices:
1.  **Baseline-Even:** Allocates resources uniformly across all queries (e.g., generating exactly $N=20$ samples for every Self-Consistency query). This represents the standard "static budget" approach.
2.  **Baseline-Length:** Uses cumulative token count at a specific step (`Detect@knob`) as a proxy for progress. If a query exceeds a length threshold, it continues; otherwise, it stops. This represents heuristics based on output length rather than semantic stability.
3.  **System Baselines:** For online serving, `Dynasor` is compared against **SGLang** (optimized with longest-prefix matching and prefix caching) and **Parrot** (which implements gang scheduling but lacks adaptive compute allocation).

**Metrics:**
*   **Batch Efficiency:** The primary metric is the **Token-to-Accuracy curve**, measuring total tokens consumed to achieve a specific accuracy level.
*   **Online Performance:** The key metric is **P90 Deadline Attainment** (the percentage of queries completing within their Service Level Objective). The authors also measure **Sustainable Request Rate** (queries per second) and **SLO Scale** (how tight the deadline can be while maintaining attainment).
*   **Fairness:** Measured via **Finish-Time Fairness** ($\phi = T_{shared} / \#\text{output tokens}$), ensuring that efficiency gains do not come at the cost of starving long-running queries.

**Hyperparameter Calibration:**
Crucially, the `Certaindex` thresholds are not arbitrary. As detailed in **Table 3**, thresholds are calibrated using a profiler-guided approach on labeled data. For example, for Self-Consistency on `MATH`, the entropy threshold $\tilde{H}_\tau$ is set to **0.7**, while for MCTS on `ASDiv`, the reward score threshold $R_\tau$ is set to **0.4**. The detection step (`Detect@knob`) varies by algorithm (e.g., step 5 for SC, step 16 for Rebase) to allow sufficient initial exploration before making termination decisions.

### 5.2 Batch Inference: Token Efficiency vs. Accuracy

The batch experiments decisively demonstrate that `Certaindex` enables significant compute savings without accuracy degradation.

**Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Results:**
**Figure 6** presents the token-accuracy trade-off for DeepSeek-Qwen distilled models (7B, 14B, 32B) across `AIME24`, `AMC23`, and `MATH500`.
*   **Average Savings:** Across all configurations, `Dynasor` reduces token usage by **11% to 29%** while maintaining the same accuracy as the baseline. For instance, on the **32B model** solving `MATH500`, the system achieves a **29% reduction** in tokens.
*   **Extreme Cases:** The savings are even more pronounced for problems where the model converges quickly. For the top **10%** of problems with the highest token reduction, savings reach **34% on AIME** and **53% on MATH500**. For the top **1%** of easiest problems, the system slashes token usage by **53% on AIME** and a staggering **81% on MATH500**.
*   **Scalability:** These gains hold for larger models as well. **Appendix G.1 (Figure 17)** shows that applying `Dynasor` to the massive **DeepSeek-R1** model yields **12% token savings on AIME** and **24% on AMC**, proving the method scales to state-of-the-art reasoning models.

**Search-Based Algorithms (SC, MCTS, Rebase):**
**Figure 7** extends these findings to multi-path algorithms. The authors compare `Dynasor` against `baseline-even` (uniform allocation) and `baseline-length` (length-based pruning).
*   **Consistent Gains:** `Certaindex` reduces token usage by **9% to 52%** across all workloads compared to both baselines.
*   **Specific Benchmarks:** On **Self-Consistency (SC) with GSM8K**, the system achieves over **47% token savings**. On **Rebase with Math**, savings exceed **50%**.
*   **Superiority over Length Heuristics:** The `baseline-length` approach often leads to accuracy degradation even with conservative pruning, as token count is a poor proxy for reasoning progress. In contrast, `Certaindex` maintains baseline accuracy while aggressively cutting compute, validating that *stability* is a far better signal than *length*.

### 5.3 Online Serving: Throughput and Latency SLOs

In online settings, the goal shifts from pure token saving to maximizing throughput and meeting strict latency deadlines. **Figure 8** illustrates `Dynasor`'s performance against SGLang and Parrot across three workloads (SC-Math, MCTS-ASDiv, Rebase-GSM8K).

**Sustainable Request Rate:**
**Figure 8(a)** plots the Program Arrival Rate against P90 SLO attainment.
*   `Dynasor` sustains **1.6× to 3.3× higher request rates** compared to SGLang and **1.6× to 3.2× higher** than Parrot while maintaining the same P90 attainment.
*   **Mechanism:** This gain stems from two factors: (1) The intra-program scheduler early-terminates high-certainty programs, freeing GPU resources faster; (2) The inter-program scheduler's gang scheduling reduces context switching and improves KV-cache utilization.

**Tighter SLO Scales:**
**Figure 8(b)** examines how tightly a service provider can configure deadlines.
*   At fixed request rates, `Dynasor` supports **1.3× to 4.7× tighter SLO scales** than SGLang and **1.7× to 3.3× tighter** than Parrot.
*   For example, in the **Rebase-GSM8K** workload, `Dynasor` allows the system to operate with significantly stricter deadlines while still achieving near-100% attainment, a regime where baselines fail completely.

**Accuracy vs. SLO Trade-off:**
**Figure 8(c)** reveals a counter-intuitive finding: `Dynasor` achieves **0.7% to 2.0% higher accuracy** than baselines at the same SLO attainment level.
*   **Reasoning:** By dynamically reallocating compute from "easy" queries (which terminate early) to "hard" queries (which need more steps), `Dynasor` effectively solves more difficult problems within the same latency budget. Baselines, constrained by static budgets, either waste time on easy problems or cut off hard ones prematurely.

**Throughput Note:**
The authors note that average throughput (tokens per second) remains equal to baselines in saturated memory-bound settings. This confirms that the scheduler introduces **negligible overhead** (~500 lines of code) and that the gains come purely from better resource *allocation*, not faster raw generation.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

The paper includes critical ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component and verify the robustness of `Certaindex`.

**Choice of Signal (Figure 10 & Appendix B.3):**
Is `Certaindex` actually the best signal? The authors compare it against:
1.  **Reasoning Path Length:** Often correlates with difficulty but is noisy.
2.  **Mean Normalized Log Probability:** A standard confidence metric.
3.  **Linear Combinations:** Complex mixes of the above.
**Figure 10** shows that `Certaindex` (specifically the entropy measure $H$) achieves the strongest correlation with ground-truth compute requirements, with a **Pearson Correlation of 0.68** and **Kendall's Tau of 0.61**. This outperforms length (0.40 Pearson) and log-prob (0.40 Pearson), confirming that *answer stability* is a superior predictor of remaining work than *confidence* or *length*.

**Threshold Sensitivity (Figure 9):**
How sensitive is the system to the threshold choice?
*   For **SC on GSM8K**, an entropy threshold of **0.5** is optimal. Higher thresholds (e.g., 0.75, 1.0) cause aggressive early exiting and accuracy drops. Lower thresholds waste tokens.
*   For **MCTS on ASDiv**, a reward score threshold of **0.4** balances the trade-off.
*   **Takeaway:** While threshold selection matters, the "sweet spot" is broad enough for practical calibration using the profiler-guided approach described in Section D.2.2.

**Component Contributions (Figures 18 & 19):**
The authors decompose the performance gains into three parts: **Gang Scheduling**, **SJF (Shortest-Job-First)**, and **Certaindex-based Allocation**.
*   **GSM8K Workload:** `Certaindex` allocation is the dominant factor, reducing mean latency by up to **60%** primarily through token savings (up to 50%). Gang scheduling provides modest additional gains.
*   **MATH Workload:** Here, token savings are smaller (~10%), so `Certaindex` alone yields a **1.2×** rate increase. However, adding **SJF** scheduling boosts the peak rate to **1.9×** by effectively mitigating head-of-line blocking for short jobs.
*   **Conclusion:** The full benefit requires the co-design of both intra-program (Certaindex) and inter-program (Gang/SJF) scheduling.

**Fairness Analysis (Figure 20):**
A common fear with priority scheduling is starvation. The authors measure **Finish-Time Fairness** and find that `Dynasor` actually *improves* fairness compared to baselines.
*   **Gang Scheduling** consistently improves fairness by preventing fragmentation.
*   **SJF** maintains fairness comparable to even allocation, thanks to the priority escalation mechanism that boosts waiting long jobs.
*   This disproves the notion that efficiency optimizations must sacrifice fairness; `Dynasor` achieves both.

**Fine-Grained vs. Static Allocation (Table 4):**
The authors explore whether checking `Certaindex` at every step (Dynamic Curve Fitting) is better than a single static check.
*   **Result:** Dynamic checking yields marginal extra savings (**~1.7% to 2.8%** additional reduction over static thresholding).
*   **Trade-off:** However, frequent checking forces sequential execution of parallel branches (e.g., in SC, you cannot run all 20 branches at once if you need to check certainty after every 5), increasing mean latency from **289s to 366s**.
*   **Decision:** The authors rightly choose the **static threshold** approach for the main system, prioritizing latency and parallelism over negligible extra token savings.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment

The experimental evidence strongly supports the paper's claims. The results are convincing for several reasons:
1.  **Consistency Across Algorithms:** The gains are not limited to one specific method; they hold for CoT, SC, MCTS, and Rebase, validating the "algorithm-agnostic" claim of `Certaindex`.
2.  **Real-World Relevance:** The online serving experiments (Figure 8) demonstrate tangible benefits for production systems (higher QPS, tighter SLOs), moving beyond theoretical token counts.
3.  **Robustness:** The ablation studies confirm that the gains come from the specific design choices (stability metric, gang scheduling) rather than incidental factors. The fairness analysis further strengthens the case for deployment.

**Limitations and Conditions:**
*   **Calibration Dependency:** The system relies on a calibration phase to set thresholds (Table 3). While the paper shows this is feasible, performance might degrade if the data distribution shifts significantly without re-calibration.
*   **Overhead of Probing:** For CoT, the "Probe-In-The-Middle" technique requires interrupting generation and appending prompts. While described as lightweight, in extremely latency-sensitive real-time applications, the slight disruption to the decoding stream could be a concern, though the paper argues the token savings outweigh this.
*   **Unsolvable Queries:** The method excels at cutting off "easy" or "converged" queries. For truly unsolvable queries where the model never stabilizes, the system will run until the maximum budget is reached, offering no savings for that specific subset (though it prevents *infinite* wandering if a hard cap exists).

In summary, the experiments provide a comprehensive validation that monitoring reasoning stability via `Certaindex` is a highly effective strategy for optimizing LLM inference, delivering up to **50% compute savings** and **3.3× throughput gains** without compromising accuracy or fairness.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While `Dynasor` and `Certaindex` demonstrate substantial efficiency gains, the approach is not a universal panacea. Its effectiveness relies on specific behavioral assumptions about LLMs, introduces non-trivial engineering trade-offs between parallelism and monitoring granularity, and leaves several critical scenarios unaddressed. A critical examination of the paper reveals the following constraints and open questions.

### 6.1 Reliance on the "Answer Stabilization" Assumption
The fundamental premise of `Certaindex` is that **answer stability implies correctness** (or at least, that further computation will not change the answer). The system assumes that if a model's intermediate outputs converge to a single value, the reasoning process has reached a stationary distribution $P^*$ (Section 3.4).

*   **The Risk of Early Convergence to Wrong Answers:** The theoretical proof in **Appendix E** guarantees that the *distribution* of answers has stabilized, but it does not guarantee that the stabilized answer is *correct*. If a model confidently converges on an incorrect reasoning path early (a phenomenon known as "hallucinated confidence"), `Certaindex` will detect this stability and trigger an early exit, effectively locking in the error.
    *   *Evidence:* The paper acknowledges this implicitly by noting that `Certaindex` measures stability "regardless of whether the answer is ultimately correct or not" (Section 1). While the empirical results show no accuracy drop on average, this suggests that for the specific datasets tested (mostly math/code with deterministic answers), early stabilization correlates strongly with correctness. However, in open-ended domains (e.g., creative writing, complex legal reasoning) where "correctness" is subjective or requires deep deliberation to uncover subtle flaws, high stability might merely indicate the model has settled on a plausible but superficial answer.
*   **The "Unsolvable" Query Problem:** The method excels at truncating queries that converge quickly. However, for genuinely difficult or unsolvable problems where the model *never* stabilizes (i.e., it keeps oscillating between different incorrect answers), `Certaindex` provides no mechanism to stop early. In these cases, the system defaults to running until the maximum resource cap is reached.
    *   *Evidence:* In **Figure 12** (Appendix B), unsolvable queries are depicted as gray dots that often remain low in `Certaindex` even at high step counts. For these queries, the system incurs the full computational cost, offering zero savings compared to a static budget. The approach optimizes the "easy" and "medium" tail of the distribution but does not solve the inefficiency of the "hard/unsolvable" head.

### 6.2 The Parallelism vs. Monitoring Granularity Trade-off
A significant systems-level trade-off exists between how frequently the system checks for certainty and how efficiently it can utilize GPU parallelism.

*   **Loss of Parallelism with Fine-Grained Checking:** Ideally, one might want to check `Certaindex` after every single reasoning step (e.g., after every branch in Self-Consistency) to maximize token savings. However, doing so forces the system to serialize execution.
    *   *Evidence:* In **Appendix G.4**, the authors explicitly test "Dynamic Curve Fitting" (checking certainty at every step) versus a "Static Threshold" (checking once). They find that while dynamic checking yields marginally better token savings (an additional **1.7%–2.8%** reduction), it drastically increases latency. For Self-Consistency, checking frequently forces the system to process branches in small sequential batches (e.g., 4 batches of 5) rather than launching all 20 branches concurrently. This increased mean latency from **289s to 366s** led the authors to reject fine-grained monitoring for the main system.
    *   *Implication:* Users must accept a sub-optimal token saving (the static threshold result) to maintain high throughput. The "optimal" efficiency curve is theoretically reachable but practically inaccessible in high-throughput serving environments due to hardware constraints.

### 6.3 Dependency on Calibration and Domain Shifts
`Dynasor` is not a "plug-and-play" solution that works out-of-the-box for any new dataset or model without configuration. It relies heavily on a **profiler-guided calibration phase** to set critical hyperparameters.

*   **Threshold Sensitivity:** The performance of the scheduler is highly sensitive to the chosen `Certaindex` threshold ($\tau$). As shown in **Figure 9**, setting the entropy threshold too high (e.g., 0.75 instead of 0.5 for SC-GSM8K) causes aggressive early termination and significant accuracy degradation. Setting it too low wastes tokens.
*   **Distribution Shift Vulnerability:** The optimal thresholds and `Detect@knob` steps (Table 3) are calibrated on specific datasets (e.g., GSM8K, MATH). If the system is deployed on a dataset with a different difficulty distribution or a new model architecture that exhibits different "self-doubt" patterns, the pre-calibrated thresholds may become invalid.
    *   *Evidence:* The paper states in **Section D.2.2** that the profiler must be "periodically executed in real-world serving scenarios to meet the dynamics of data distribution shifts." This introduces an operational overhead: the system requires a continuous feedback loop of labeled data to re-tune its scheduling policies, which may not be available in all deployment scenarios (e.g., few-shot learning on novel domains).

### 6.4 Unaddressed Scenarios and Architectural Constraints
The current implementation of `Dynasor` leaves several advanced serving techniques and problem types unexplored.

*   **Incompatibility with Advanced Serving Primitives:** The paper explicitly lists in **Section 6 (Limitations)** that it did not explore integration with **PD Disaggregation** (separating prefill and decode phases) or **Chunked Prefill**.
    *   *Implication:* Modern high-performance serving systems increasingly rely on these techniques to minimize latency. Integrating `Certaindex` logic into a disaggregated architecture is non-trivial; for instance, deciding to early-exit during the decode phase might leave prefill resources stranded or complicate the hand-off between prefill and decode servers. The current gains might not translate directly to next-generation disaggregated clusters without significant re-engineering.
*   **Limited Scope of "Reasoning":** The evaluation is heavily skewed towards **mathematical reasoning** and **code generation** (MATH, GSM8K, LiveCodeBench), where answers are exact and easy to cluster for entropy calculation.
    *   *Gap:* The paper does not provide evidence for how `Certaindex` performs on open-ended generative tasks (e.g., summarization, dialogue, story writing) where "answer stabilization" is ill-defined. In these domains, a model might stabilize on a generic, low-quality response, and the semantic entropy metric might fail to distinguish between "confidently correct" and "confidently generic."
*   **Security and Adversarial Risks:** The authors briefly mention in **Section 6** the risk of **side-channel attacks** and **manipulation**.
    *   *Risk:* Since `Certaindex` relies on the model's internal signals to make scheduling decisions, a malicious user could potentially craft prompts that force the model to exhibit high stability early (e.g., by repeating a wrong answer confidently) to trigger an early exit, thereby causing the system to return an incorrect result while consuming minimal resources (a Denial-of-Service via accuracy degradation). The paper identifies this risk but offers no mitigation strategy.

### 6.5 Summary of Open Questions
Despite the strong empirical results, several questions remain for future work:
1.  **Generalizability to Non-Deterministic Tasks:** Can `Certaindex` be adapted for tasks where there is no single "correct" answer, and stability might indicate mode collapse rather than solution quality?
2.  **Dynamic Re-calibration:** How robust is the system to rapid domain shifts without access to ground-truth labels for re-calibration?
3.  **Adversarial Robustness:** What mechanisms can be built to prevent users from exploiting the early-exit logic to degrade service quality?
4.  **Integration with Disaggregated Architectures:** How does the latency benefit of `Dynasor` hold up when prefill and decode are physically separated on different GPU nodes?

In conclusion, while `Dynasor` represents a significant leap forward in reasoning-aware scheduling, it is best viewed as a powerful optimization for **well-defined, deterministic reasoning workloads** where calibration data is available. Its reliance on stability as a proxy for correctness and the inherent trade-off between monitoring frequency and parallelism define the boundaries of its current applicability.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of `Certaindex` and the `Dynasor` system marks a pivotal shift in how the research community and industry practitioners approach LLM inference efficiency. By moving from static, blind resource allocation to dynamic, signal-driven scheduling, this work fundamentally changes the landscape of test-time scaling, suggesting that the next frontier of performance gains lies not in faster hardware or larger models, but in **smarter termination**.

### 7.1 Changing the Landscape: From "More Compute" to "Smarter Compute"
Historically, the trajectory for improving LLM reasoning has been linear: to solve harder problems, simply allocate more tokens (test-time scaling). This paper disrupts that paradigm by demonstrating that **token volume is a poor proxy for reasoning quality**. The discovery that models exhibit "self-doubt" and answer stabilization implies that a significant portion of current inference compute—up to **50%** in batch settings—is effectively wasted on redundant verification loops.

This work forces a re-evaluation of how reasoning algorithms are designed and deployed:
*   **Algorithm Design:** Future reasoning algorithms (beyond CoT, SC, and MCTS) will likely need to be designed with **observability** in mind. Just as modern neural networks are trained to output confidence scores, future reasoning models may be optimized to expose explicit stability signals earlier in their generation process, making them natively compatible with schedulers like `Dynasor`.
*   **Serving Architecture:** The success of `Dynasor` challenges the assumption that serving systems should treat requests as stateless, independent units. It establishes **reasoning-aware scheduling** as a new standard, where the scheduler maintains state about the logical progress of a "reasoning program" (a collection of related sub-queries) to optimize KV-cache reuse and gang execution. This shifts the focus of systems research from purely memory-level optimizations (like paged attention) to **semantic-level optimizations**.

### 7.2 Enabling Follow-Up Research
The framework established by `Certaindex` opens several concrete avenues for future investigation:

*   **Stability in Open-Ended Domains:** Current validation relies heavily on deterministic tasks (math, code) where answer clustering is straightforward via string matching or simple embeddings. A critical next step is adapting `Certaindex` for **open-ended generation** (e.g., creative writing, long-form QA, legal analysis). Research is needed to define "stability" in domains without a single ground truth—perhaps measuring the stabilization of *themes*, *argument structures*, or *semantic intent* rather than exact string matches.
*   **Adversarial Robustness and Security:** As noted in the limitations, `Certaindex` relies on the model's internal signals, creating a potential attack surface. Future work must explore **adversarial prompting** strategies that could manipulate a model into exhibiting false stability (e.g., confidently repeating a hallucination) to trigger early exits and degrade service quality. Developing robust detectors that distinguish between "genuine convergence" and "mode collapse" will be essential for secure deployment.
*   **Integration with Disaggregated Architectures:** The paper explicitly leaves open the integration with **Prefill-Decode (PD) Disaggregation** and **Chunked Prefill**. Follow-up research should investigate how `Certaindex` signals can be propagated across disaggregated nodes. For instance, can a decode node signal a prefill node to stop generating context for a branch that has already stabilized? Solving this could unlock efficiency gains in next-generation cloud architectures where prefill and decode are physically separated.
*   **Learning-to-Schedule Policies:** While `Dynasor` uses profiler-guided static thresholds, future systems could employ **Reinforcement Learning (RL)** to learn optimal scheduling policies dynamically. An RL agent could observe real-time cluster load, query difficulty distributions, and `Certaindex` trajectories to make fine-grained decisions on token allocation, potentially surpassing the marginal gains of static thresholding without sacrificing parallelism.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases
The immediate practical impact of this work is most profound in high-volume, cost-sensitive, and latency-critical deployments:

*   **Cost Reduction for Reasoning-Heavy APIs:** For cloud providers offering access to large reasoning models (e.g., DeepSeek-R1, o1-like models), integrating `Certaindex`-based early exiting can directly reduce inference costs by **30–50%** for a significant portion of queries. This allows providers to either lower prices to gain market share or improve margins without retraining models.
*   **Real-Time Interactive Agents:** In applications like coding assistants or interactive tutoring, latency is paramount. `Dynasor`'s ability to support **1.3–4.7× tighter SLOs** means that complex reasoning agents can respond faster to user inputs. By cutting off "self-doubt" loops, the system delivers the answer sooner, improving the user experience in real-time collaboration tools.
*   **High-Throughput Batch Processing:** For offline tasks like large-scale data analysis, scientific simulation, or automated grading, the **3.3× throughput gain** enables organizations to process significantly larger datasets within the same time window and hardware budget. This democratizes access to powerful reasoning models for researchers and enterprises with limited GPU resources.
*   **Green AI Initiatives:** By eliminating unnecessary token generation, `Dynasor` directly reduces the energy consumption of LLM inference. This aligns with broader sustainability goals in AI, offering a software-level mechanism to reduce the carbon footprint of large-scale model deployment.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance
For practitioners considering the adoption of `Certaindex` and `Dynasor`, the following guidance outlines when and how to integrate these techniques:

*   **When to Prefer This Method:**
    *   **Ideal Scenario:** Your workload consists of **deterministic reasoning tasks** (math, logic, code generation, multiple-choice QA) where answers can be easily clustered or verified. You are using reasoning algorithms that generate multiple paths (SC, MCTS) or long CoT sequences.
    *   **Avoid If:** Your tasks are highly open-ended with subjective answers, or if your latency requirements are so strict that even the minimal overhead of "Probe-In-The-Middle" interruptions (for CoT) is unacceptable. Also, if you lack a calibration dataset to tune thresholds, the static defaults may not yield optimal results.

*   **Integration Steps:**
    1.  **Instrumentation:** Wrap your existing reasoning logic (SC, MCTS, CoT) in the `Dynasor` **Reasoning Program Abstraction**. This requires implementing two functions: `execute()` (your existing logic) and `update_certaindex()` (calculating entropy or reward aggregation). As noted in Section D.4, this adds only **40–150 lines of code** per algorithm.
    2.  **Calibration:** Run a **profiler-guided calibration** on a representative subset of your data (Section D.2.2). Do not guess the thresholds; use labeled data to find the `Certaindex` cutoff that maximizes token savings while maintaining your target accuracy (e.g., &lt;0.5% drop).
    3.  **Deployment:** Deploy the modified scheduler (based on SGLang) in a staging environment. Start with **Gang Scheduling** enabled to realize immediate latency benefits from KV-cache reuse, then gradually enable **Intra-Program Early Exiting** once thresholds are validated.
    4.  **Monitoring:** Continuously monitor the distribution of `Certaindex` values and early-exit rates. If the distribution shifts (indicating a change in query difficulty or model behavior), trigger a re-calibration routine.

*   **Code Availability:** The authors have open-sourced the implementation at `https://github.com/hao-ai-lab/Dynasor.git`. Practitioners should start by replicating the **SC-GSM8K** or **CoT-MATH500** benchmarks provided in the repository to validate the ~50% token savings on their own hardware before moving to production workloads.

In summary, `Certaindex` provides a pragmatic, theoretically grounded, and immediately deployable path to making LLM reasoning significantly more efficient. It transforms the "black box" of reasoning into an observable, controllable process, paving the way for a new generation of intelligent, adaptive inference systems.
