## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the emerging bottleneck of high-quality token scarcity in large language model scaling by introducing an efficient Triton kernel implementation of the 2-simplicial Transformer, which generalizes standard dot-product attention to trilinear functions. Experiments with MoE models ranging from 1B to 3.5B active parameters demonstrate that 2-simplicial attention improves the scaling law exponent $\alpha$ by up to 20.2% on reasoning benchmarks like MMLU-pro and GSM8k compared to standard Transformers (Table 3). This shift implies that 2-simplicial architectures achieve superior token efficiency, allowing model size to scale faster than data volume while maintaining performance gains in math, coding, and logic tasks.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of this work, we must first establish the current paradigm governing large language model (LLM) development: neural scaling laws. These laws empirically describe how model performance improves as we increase resources. Specifically, Hoffmann et al. (2022) propose that the training loss $L(N, D)$ decays as a power law dependent on the number of model parameters $N$ and the number of training tokens $D$. As formalized in Section 3, this relationship is often expressed as:

$$
L(N, D) = E + \frac{A}{N^\alpha} + \frac{B}{D^\beta}
$$

Here, $E$ represents the irreducible loss (the entropy of the data), while the terms involving $N$ and $D$ capture how quickly the model approaches that limit. The exponents $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are critical: they determine the *rate* of improvement. A higher exponent means the model learns more efficiently per unit of parameter or data added.

### The Shift from Compute-Bound to Data-Bound

Historically, scaling laws assumed an infinite supply of data, implying that models were **compute-bound**—limited only by how much processing power was available to train them. Under this assumption, the optimal strategy (often called "Chinchilla scaling") is to scale model size and data volume proportionally. However, Section 1 highlights a shifting landscape: as models consume massive internet-scale datasets, the supply of high-quality tokens is becoming a finite resource. We are transitioning to a **data-bound** or **token-constrained** regime.

This shift creates a specific problem: if we cannot indefinitely increase $D$, we must architectures that maximize performance under a fixed token budget. This property is referred to as **token efficiency**. In a token-constrained setting, an architecture that learns faster per token (higher $\beta$) or requires fewer tokens to reach a specific loss level is significantly more valuable than one that simply scales larger.

### The Architectural Gap: Offset vs. Exponent

A central motivation for this paper is a limitation observed in prior architectural research. As noted in Section 1, most modifications to the Transformer architecture—whether through optimizer tweaks or structural changes—merely shift the error offset (changing $E$ or the coefficients $A, B$) without fundamentally altering the scaling exponents $\alpha$ or $\beta$. Citing Everett (2025) and Kaplan et al. (2020), the authors emphasize that shifting the offset provides diminishing returns; the model still requires the same proportion of data to improve further.

The only known method to change the exponent previously involved altering the data distribution itself (Sorscher et al., 2022; Bahri et al., 2024). This paper positions itself to challenge the assumption that *architecture* cannot change the exponent. The goal is to find an architectural inductive bias that allows the model to extract more information per token, effectively steepening the learning curve.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

Several lines of work have attempted to improve Transformer efficiency or expressiveness, but each faces specific trade-offs:

*   **Linear Attention and State Space Models:** Works like Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) reduce the computational complexity of attention from quadratic $O(n^2)$ to linear $O(n)$ with respect to sequence length. While efficient, Section 2 notes that these methods have seen less widespread adoption due to "worse quality compared to Transformers in practice." They sacrifice the expressive power of standard attention for speed.
*   **Looped Transformers:** Architectures like Universal Transformers (Dehghani et al., 2018) reuse layers to compute more expressive functions per parameter. While effective for logical reasoning, Section 2 highlights a trainability barrier: looping $k$ times increases effective depth by a factor of $k$, exacerbating optimization difficulties in large models.
*   **Higher-Order Attention:** The theoretical foundation for this paper rests on **2-simplicial attention**, originally proposed by Clift et al. (2019). Standard attention is bilinear (involving pairs of tokens, query and key), analogous to a 1-simplex (an edge). 2-simplicial attention is **trilinear**, involving triplets of tokens (query, key, and a second key), analogous to a 2-simplex (a triangle).
    *   *Theoretical Advantage:* Section 2 cites Sanford et al. (2023), who proved that 2-simplicial Transformers can solve classes of problems (specifically "Match3" tasks) that require exponentially many layers for standard dot-product attention to solve.
    *   *Practical Limitation:* Despite this expressiveness, prior implementations were impractical. The computational complexity scales as $O(n^3)$ with sequence length, making it prohibitive for long contexts. Additionally, standard positional encodings like RoPE (Su et al., 2024) rely on rotation invariance properties of dot products that do not naturally extend to trilinear forms.

### Positioning of This Work

This paper positions itself as the bridge between the theoretical expressiveness of 2-simplicial attention and practical large-scale training. It addresses the prior limitations through three key interventions:

1.  **Efficient Implementation:** By developing a custom **Triton kernel** (Section 7), the authors reduce the practical overhead of trilinear operations, achieving performance rivaling optimized Flash Attention v3 implementations (Figure 3).
2.  **Sliding Window Mechanism:** To mitigate the $O(n^3)$ complexity, the architecture parametrizes attention over localized sliding windows $w_1$ and $w_2$ rather than the full sequence (Section 6). This reduces complexity to $O(n \times w_1 \times w_2)$, making it comparable to standard attention at specific context lengths.
3.  **Rotation Invariant Forms:** The paper derives a determinant-based trilinear form (Section 5) that preserves the rotation invariance required for positional encodings like RoPE, solving a key compatibility issue with modern Transformer stacks.

Unlike prior architectural tweaks that only shift the loss curve, this work claims to fundamentally alter the scaling exponent $\alpha$ under token constraints (Section 8). By revisiting a previously impractical higher-order attention mechanism and making it computationally viable, the paper argues for a new direction in architecture design—one prioritizing token efficiency over raw compute scaling.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems and architecture paper that demonstrates how to make a theoretically expressive but computationally prohibitive attention mechanism practical for large-scale language model training. The core idea is to generalize standard dot-product attention—which computes relationships between pairs of tokens—to trilinear attention that computes relationships between triplets of tokens, while using custom kernel optimizations and sliding window constraints to make this computationally feasible.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a modified Transformer architecture that replaces some standard attention layers with 2-simplicial attention layers, which process three tokens simultaneously instead of two. This solves the problem of token inefficiency in large language models by enabling the architecture to extract more information per training token through higher-order token interactions, while custom Triton kernels and sliding window constraints keep the computational cost manageable.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The architecture consists of four major components that work together in sequence. First, the **Input Projection Layer** takes the input sequence and produces three distinct projection matrices: query vectors $Q$, and two sets of key-value pairs $(K, V)$ and $(K', V')$, whereas standard Transformers only produce one key-value pair. Second, the **2-Simplicial Attention Kernel** computes trilinear attention logits by taking the element-wise product of query, key, and second-key vectors across a sliding window, then applies softmax normalization to produce attention weights. Third, the **Value Aggregation Module** combines the two value matrices $V$ and $V'$ using element-wise multiplication before weighting them by the attention scores to produce the output. Fourth, the **Interleaved Layer Structure** places 2-simplicial attention in every fourth layer of a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) Transformer, with standard dot-product attention filling the remaining layers to balance expressiveness with training stability.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the mathematical foundation of 2-simplicial attention to establish what makes it different from standard attention at the equation level.
- We then explain the sliding window mechanism that reduces computational complexity from cubic to manageable levels, including the specific window sizes chosen.
- We examine the rotation-invariant determinant form that enables compatibility with positional encodings like RoPE.
- We detail the Triton kernel implementation including the forward pass tiling strategy and the split backward pass design.
- We conclude with the full model configuration including MoE structure, training hyperparameters, and layer interleaving strategy.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Mathematical Foundation of 2-Simplicial Attention**

Standard Transformer attention computes a bilinear form between query and key vectors, producing an attention logit matrix where each entry $A_{ij}$ represents the compatibility between token $i$ and token $j$. The 2-simplicial Transformer generalizes this to a trilinear form where each attention logit $A^{(2s)}_{ijk}$ represents the compatibility between a query token $i$ and two key tokens $j$ and $k$ simultaneously. The core equation for 2-simplicial attention logits is:

$$
A^{(2s)}_{ijk} = \frac{\langle q_i, k_j, k'_k \rangle}{\sqrt{d}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}} \sum_{l=1}^d Q_{il} K_{jl} K'_{kl}
$$

where $q_i$ is the query vector for position $i$, $k_j$ is the first key vector for position $j$, $k'_k$ is the second key vector for position $k$, and $d$ is the hidden dimension. This differs from standard attention which only has $A_{ij} = \langle q_i, k_j \rangle / \sqrt{d}$ with two indices instead of three. The attention weights are then computed by applying softmax normalization across both key dimensions:

$$
S^{(2s)}_{ijk} = \frac{\exp(A^{(2s)}_{ijk})}{\sum_{j,k} \exp(A^{(2s)}_{ijk})}
$$

The final output for token $i$ is computed as a weighted sum of the Hadamard (element-wise) product of the two value vectors:

$$
\tilde{v}^{(2s)}(i) = \sum_{j,k=1}^n S^{(2s)}_{ijk} (v_j \circ v'_k)
$$

where $v_j \circ v'_k$ denotes the element-wise product between two vectors in $\mathbb{R}^d$. This design choice—multiplying the value vectors element-wise before weighting—allows the model to capture interactions between the two key positions in the output representation, rather than simply attending to one value vector.

**Sliding Window Constraint for Computational Feasibility**

A naive implementation of 2-simplicial attention over a full sequence of length $n$ would have computational complexity $O(n^3)$ because the attention tensor has three sequence dimensions. This is prohibitive for long contexts, so the authors introduce a sliding window constraint that limits which key positions each query can attend to. Instead of attending to all $n$ positions for both key matrices, each query vector $Q_i$ attends to a localized region of $w_1$ positions for the first key matrix $K$ and $w_2$ positions for the second key matrix $K'$. This reduces the computational complexity from $O(n^3)$ to $O(n \times w_1 \times w_2)$, which is linear in sequence length when the window sizes are fixed.

The paper systematically evaluates different window size configurations to find the optimal trade-off between latency and model quality, as shown in Table 1. For example, a configuration with $w_1 = 1024$ and $w_2 = 32$ at 32k sequence length achieves 104.1 ms latency, while $w_1 = 512$ and $w_2 = 32$ at 16k sequence length achieves 55.1 ms latency. The authors ultimately choose a window size of $(512, 32)$ as their default configuration, which balances latency and quality while making the computational complexity of 2-simplicial attention comparable to dot-product attention at 48k context length. The complexity formula for 2-simplicial attention with sliding windows is:

$$
O(A^{(2s)}) = 3 \cdot 2 n w_1 w_2 = 6 n w_1 w_2
$$

This increase in complexity compared to standard attention arises from the trilinear einsum operation, which necessitates an additional multiplication compared to standard dot-product attention. For comparison, causal dot-product attention has complexity $O(A) = 2n^2$, involving two matrix multiplications (one for $QK^\top$ and one for the attention-output multiplication), each requiring two floating-point operations per element, with the causal mask allowing us to skip half of these computations.

**Rotation-Invariant Determinant Form for Positional Encodings**

A critical challenge in adapting 2-simplicial attention for modern language models is compatibility with Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE), which are now standard in most Transformer architectures. RoPE works by applying position-dependent rotations to query and key vectors such that their dot product $\langle q_i, k_j \rangle$ becomes a function of the relative distance $i - j$. This relies on a key property: dot products are invariant to orthogonal transformations, meaning $\langle q_i, k_j \rangle = \langle R q_i, R k_j \rangle$ for any rotation matrix $R$.

However, the standard trilinear form defined in Equation 5 is not rotation-invariant. Applying the same rotation to $q_i$, $k_j$, and $k'_k$ does not preserve the inner product: $\langle q_i, k_j, k'_k \rangle = \sum_{l=1}^d q_{il} k_{jl} k'_{kl} \neq \langle R q_i, R k_j, R k'_k \rangle$. This breaks RoPE's mechanism, which expects the attention score between tokens at the same position to remain unchanged after applying position-based rotations.

To solve this, the authors derive a determinant-based trilinear form that is rotation-invariant. The key insight is that determinants of matrices formed from vector components are invariant to rotations. For 2D vectors, the determinant $\hat{f}_2(a, b) = a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1$ measures the signed area of the parallelogram spanned by vectors $a$ and $b$. For 3D vectors, the determinant $\hat{f}_3(a, b, c)$ measures the signed volume of the parallelotope spanned by three vectors. Using Sarrus' rule, this 3D determinant can be expressed as:

$$
\hat{f}_3(a, b, c) = a_1 b_2 c_3 + a_2 b_3 c_1 + a_3 b_1 c_2 - a_1 b_3 c_2 - a_2 b_1 c_3 - a_3 b_2 c_1
$$

This can be rewritten as the difference of two trilinear dot products, which means it can be computed using two einsum operations instead of one. The rotation-invariant attention logits are then defined as:

$$
A^{(\text{det})}_{ij_1j_2} = \sum_{l=1}^p \det([q^{(l)}_i, k^{(l)}_{j_1}, k'^{(l)}_{j_2}])
$$

where $q^{(l)}$ denotes the $l$-th chunk of size 3 from the query vector. Theorem 5.1 proves that this sum-of-determinants form is expressive enough to solve the Match3 problem (finding three tokens that sum to zero modulo $M$) with a single attention head of dimension $d = 7$. However, since the determinant form involves 6 terms compared to the simpler trilinear form's 1 term, the authors use the simpler form (Equation 5) for their backward pass computations without loss of generality, noting that the determinant form would simply require 2 einsums instead of 1 in the forward pass.

**Triton Kernel Implementation and Optimization**

The authors implement 2-simplicial attention using Triton, a Python-like language for writing GPU kernels, building on techniques from Flash Attention (Dao et al., 2022) including online softmax for numerical stability. For the trilinear operations, they perform 2D tiling by merging one of the inputs via element-wise multiplication and executing matrix multiplication on the product. This allows them to overlap both $QK$ and $VV'$ operations on CUDA Cores with $(QK)K'$ and $P(VV')$ operations on Tensor Cores, maximizing hardware utilization.

The forward pass kernel (Listing 1) processes queries in blocks of size `BLOCK_SIZE_Q` (default 64) and key-value pairs in blocks of size `BLOCK_SIZE_KV` (default 32). The kernel loads query tiles, then iterates over key positions within the sliding window, computing element-wise products $Q \odot K_1$ on CUDA cores before performing the final matrix multiplication with $K_2$ on Tensor Cores. The online softmax algorithm maintains running maximum and sum values ($m_i$ and $l_i$) to compute softmax without storing the full attention matrix, reducing memory usage from $O(n^2)$ to $O(n)$.

The backward pass presents a unique challenge because aggregating gradients across three different dimension orderings introduces significant overhead from atomic operations (which are needed when multiple threads write to the same memory location). To mitigate this, the authors decompose the backward pass into two distinct kernels rather than using a single fused kernel. The first kernel (Listing 2, `two_simplicial_attn_bwd_kv1_kernel`) computes gradients with respect to $K$, $V$, $K'$, $V'$, and $Q$ by iterating over key-value positions. The second kernel (Listing 3, `two_simplicial_attn_bwd_kv2q_kernel`) is optimized for small $w_2$ and uses a two-stage approach to compute $dQ$ jointly with $dK'$ and $dV'$ without atomics.

The two-stage backward approach divides the query sequence along the sequence dimension into tiles of size $[w_2, \text{dim}]$. In the first stage, the kernel iterates over even tiles, storing intermediate gradients for $dQ$, $dK$, $dK'$, $dV$, and $dV'$. In the second stage, it iterates over odd tiles, storing $dQ$ directly while accumulating into $dK$, $dK'$, $dV$, and $dV'$. Although this approach incurs additional overhead from recomputing the output $O$ and attention weights $dS$, the authors find it is better than the extra overhead from atomics needed for a single fused kernel. They note this may be a limitation of Triton's coarser-grained pipeline control making it difficult to hide the overhead from atomics, and suggest that further optimization could be achieved with a lower-level language like CUTLASS for finer-grained tuning.

The implementation achieves 520 TFLOPS, rivaling the fastest Flash Attention v3 Triton implementations. Despite using Triton rather than CUTLASS, the authors achieve competitive performance compared to CUTLASS FAv3 for large sequence lengths, as shown in Figure 3.

**Model Architecture and Training Configuration**

The authors train a series of Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models ranging from 1 billion active parameters and 57 billion total parameters to 3.5 billion active parameters and 176 billion total parameters. They use interleaved sliding-window 2-simplicial attention, where every fourth layer is a 2-simplicial attention layer rather than standard dot-product attention. The choice of this particular ordering (every fourth layer) is to distribute the load in attention computation when using pipeline parallelism, since 2-simplicial attention and global attention are the most compute-intensive operations in a single pipeline stage and have comparable FLOPs.

The models use Grouped Query Attention (GQA) with a ratio of 64, meaning 64 query heads share the same key and value heads. This high GQA ratio was inspired by Native Sparse Attention (Yuan et al., 2025) and enables efficient tiling along query heads, ensuring dense computation and eliminating the need for costly element-wise masking. A naive sliding window 2-simplicial attention implementation would have each $Q_i$ vector attending to $w_1 + w_2 - 1$ different $KK'$ vectors, which leads to poor compute throughput when tiling queries like in Flash Attention.

Training uses the AdamW optimizer with a peak learning rate of $4 \times 10^{-3}$ and weight decay of 0.0125. The learning rate schedule uses a warmup of 4000 steps followed by cosine decay decreasing the learning rate to 0.01× of the peak learning rate. The authors report negative log-likelihood on GSM8k, MMLU, MMLU-pro, and MBPP benchmarks, since these benchmarks most strongly test math, reasoning, and coding skills in pre-training. For MMLU and MMLU-pro, they measure the negative log-likelihood of the choice together with the entire answer, and for GSM8k they use 5-shot evaluation.

The interleaved architecture design choice reflects a practical consideration: 2-simplicial attention is more expressive but also more computationally expensive than standard attention. By placing it in only 25% of layers (every fourth layer), the model gains the expressiveness benefits of higher-order attention while maintaining training stability and keeping overall compute costs manageable. This is particularly important given that the backward pass requires two separate kernels and the forward pass has higher complexity than standard attention.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the core novelties of the work, distinguishing between incremental engineering improvements and fundamental shifts in architectural design. While Section 3 detailed the mechanical implementation, here we analyze why these choices matter for the broader landscape of large language model development.

### 4.1 Fundamental Shift in Scaling Laws (Exponent vs. Offset)

The most significant theoretical contribution of this work is the empirical demonstration that architectural changes can alter the scaling exponent $\alpha$, not just the loss offset. Prior literature, including Kaplan et al. (2020) and discussions by Everett (2025), suggests that most architectural modifications merely shift the error curve (changing coefficients $A$ or $B$ in the scaling law) without changing the rate of improvement. This implies diminishing returns: no matter how clever the architecture, you still need proportional increases in data to improve.

This paper challenges that assumption. As quantified in **Table 3**, the 2-simplicial Transformer exhibits a steeper scaling exponent $\alpha$ compared to standard dot-product attention across reasoning benchmarks. For instance, on GSM8k, the exponent increases from $0.1420$ to $0.1683$, a relative improvement of **18.5%**. On MMLU-pro, the increase is **20.2%**.

*   **Why this is innovative:** In a **token-constrained regime** (where high-quality data is scarce), improving the exponent is vastly more valuable than shifting the offset. A better exponent means the model continues to learn efficiently even as data runs out, whereas an offset shift provides a one-time gain that saturates quickly.
*   **Implication:** This suggests that "Chinchilla scaling" (Hoffmann et al., 2022), which advocates balancing parameters and tokens, may need revision for higher-order architectures. The authors note in Section 1 that for 2-simplicial Transformers, it may be possible to increase tokens at a *slower rate* than parameters while still achieving performance gains.

### 4.2 Operationalizing Higher-Order Interactions

While 2-simplicial attention was theoretically proposed by Clift et al. (2019) and shown to solve strictly harder problem classes (e.g., Match3 tasks) than standard Transformers (Sanford et al., 2023), it remained practically unusable due to its $O(n^3)$ complexity. This work bridges the gap between theoretical expressiveness and practical deployment.

*   **The Innovation:** The key insight is not just the attention mechanism itself, but the **systems co-design** that makes it viable. By combining the sliding window constraint (Section 6) with a custom Triton kernel (Section 7), the authors reduce the effective complexity to $O(n \times w_1 \times w_2)$.
*   **Significance:** This transforms 2-simplicial attention from a theoretical curiosity into a drop-in replacement for standard attention layers. As shown in **Table 1**, specific window configurations (e.g., $w_1=512, w_2=32$) achieve latencies comparable to standard attention at 48k context length. This enables models to capture **trilinear dependencies** (interactions between triplets of tokens) which are impossible for standard bilinear attention to represent efficiently, potentially unlocking better performance on tasks requiring multi-step reasoning or logical consistency.

### 4.3 Compatibility with Modern Positional Encodings

A subtle but critical barrier to adopting higher-order attention is incompatibility with Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE), the industry standard for positional encoding. Standard trilinear forms break the rotation invariance property that RoPE relies on, which would normally force developers to revert to less efficient learned absolute positions.

*   **The Innovation:** Section 5 introduces a **determinant-based trilinear form** that preserves rotation invariance. By constructing attention logits using the determinant of vector chunks (essentially measuring the signed volume spanned by query and key vectors), the mechanism remains invariant to orthogonal transformations.
*   **Significance:** This allows 2-simplicial attention to integrate seamlessly into existing Transformer stacks that rely on RoPE (such as Llama or Gemma variants) without requiring fundamental changes to how position is encoded. Theorem 5.1 proves this form retains sufficient expressiveness to solve modular arithmetic tasks, ensuring that the fix for compatibility does not come at the cost of capability.

### 4.4 Strategic Interleaving for Trainability and Load Balancing

Rather than replacing all attention layers with 2-simplicial layers, the authors adopt a hybrid strategy: interleaving 2-simplicial attention in **every fourth layer** (Section 8). This is a pragmatic architectural insight that balances expressiveness with stability.

*   **Trainability:** Deep networks with complex higher-order operations can suffer from optimization instability. By limiting the density of 2-simplicial layers, the model retains the gradient flow properties of standard Transformers for most of its depth while injecting higher-order reasoning capabilities at regular intervals.
*   **Pipeline Parallelism:** The authors explicitly note in Section 8 that this ordering helps distribute computational load during pipeline parallelism. Since 2-simplicial layers are more compute-intensive than standard layers, spacing them out prevents "bubbles" (idle time) in the pipeline where some stages wait for others to complete heavy attention computations.
*   **Distinction:** This differs from prior "Universal Transformer" approaches (Dehghani et al., 2018) which looped *all* layers, often exacerbating training difficulties. The interleaved approach captures the benefit of increased expressiveness per parameter without the full cost of a deep higher-order network.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence provided in the paper to validate the claims regarding token efficiency, scaling laws, and system performance. We will walk through the evaluation methodology, analyze the crossover behavior in model performance, and scrutinize the scaling law fits that form the core theoretical contribution of this work.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

To fairly assess whether 2-simplicial attention offers a fundamental advantage over standard dot-product attention, the authors design a controlled comparison across model scales and reasoning benchmarks.

*   **Model Architecture:** The experiments utilize Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models. The scale ranges from **1 billion active parameters** (57 billion total) to **3.5 billion active parameters** (176 billion total). Crucially, the architecture uses **interleaved 2-simplicial layers**: every fourth layer uses 2-simplicial attention, while the remaining three use standard dot-product attention. This hybrid design balances the computational cost of trilinear attention with the stability of standard Transformers.
*   **Baselines:** For each model size (1B, 2B, 3.5B active parameters), a standard Transformer baseline is trained with the exact same parameter count and token budget. This ensures that any performance difference is attributable to the attention mechanism, not model capacity.
*   **Datasets and Metrics:** Performance is measured using **Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL)** on four specific benchmarks chosen for their demand on reasoning and coding skills:
    *   **GSM8k:** Grade school math word problems.
    *   **MMLU & MMLU-pro:** Multi-task language understanding (academic and professional domains).
    *   **MBPP:** Python programming tasks.
    *   *Note on Metric:* NLL is a loss metric where **lower values indicate better performance**. The paper reports 5-shot evaluation for GSM8k and includes the entire answer string for MMLU likelihood calculations.
*   **Training Configuration:** All models are trained using the **AdamW** optimizer with a peak learning rate of $4 \times 10^{-3}$, weight decay of $0.0125$, and a cosine decay schedule following 4,000 warmup steps. This uniformity isolates the architectural variable.

### 5.2 Primary Results: The Performance Crossover

The most critical empirical finding is found in **Table 2**, which compares the NLL of Transformer baselines against 2-simplicial models. A casual glance might miss the nuance, but a detailed look reveals a **scaling crossover**: the 2-simplicial architecture starts slightly behind at small scales but surpasses the Transformer as model size increases.

*   **1 Billion Active Parameters:** At the smallest scale, the standard Transformer actually performs better. For GSM8k, the Transformer achieves an NLL of **0.3277**, while the 2-simplicial model scores **0.3302**. The $\Delta(\%)$ row shows **+0.79%**, indicating the 2-simplicial model has higher loss (worse performance). The text explicitly notes: "on models smaller than 2.0 billion (active) parameters, we see no gains from using 2-simplicial attention."
*   **2 Billion Active Parameters:** The trend reverses. The 2-simplicial model achieves an NLL of **0.2942** on GSM8k, beating the Transformer's **0.2987**. The $\Delta(\%)$ is **-1.51%**, where the negative sign now indicates a reduction in loss (improvement).
*   **3.5 Billion Active Parameters:** The gap widens in favor of 2-simplicial attention. On GSM8k, the 2-simplicial model reaches **0.2718** compared to the Transformer's **0.2781** ($\Delta = -2.27\%$). Similar improvements are seen on MBPP (**-0.45%**) and MMLU (**-1.06%**).

**Why this matters:** This crossover behavior is the smoking gun for a changed scaling exponent. If 2-simplicial attention merely shifted the offset (as most architectural tweaks do), we would expect it to be either consistently better or consistently worse across all scales. The fact that it *improves relative to the baseline as size increases* suggests it learns more efficiently per parameter when given enough capacity.

### 5.3 Scaling Law Analysis

To quantify the observation above, the authors fit the experimental results to a power law scaling function. Since the token budget $D$ is fixed across compared models, they simplify the standard scaling law (Equation 1) to depend primarily on parameters $N$:

$$
L(N) = E' + \frac{A}{N^\alpha}
$$

Taking the logarithm allows them to estimate the exponent $\alpha$ via linear regression (Equation 20). The results are presented in **Table 3**.

*   **Higher Scaling Exponents ($\alpha$):** Across all four benchmarks, the 2-simplicial Transformer exhibits a higher $\alpha$ than the standard Transformer.
    *   **GSM8k:** $\alpha$ increases from **0.1420** (Transformer) to **0.1683** (2-simplicial), an **18.5%** relative improvement.
    *   **MMLU-pro:** $\alpha$ increases from **0.0901** to **0.1083**, a **20.2%** improvement.
    *   **MBPP:** $\alpha$ increases from **0.1720** to **0.1837**.
*   **Goodness of Fit:** **Table 4** confirms that these power laws are not artifacts of noisy data. The $R^2$ values are exceptionally high, ranging from **0.9962** to **0.9999** across models and benchmarks. The residuals are minimal (e.g., $2.8 \times 10^{-6}$ for Transformer on GSM8k), indicating the power law model accurately describes the empirical loss trajectory.

**Interpretation:** A higher $\alpha$ means the loss $L(N)$ decreases more rapidly as $N$ grows. In practical terms, this validates the paper's central thesis: under token constraints, increasing model size yields diminishing returns for standard Transformers faster than it does for 2-simplicial Transformers. This supports the claim that 2-simplicial attention is more **token-efficient**.

### 5.4 System Performance and Latency

Theoretical gains are useless if the model cannot be trained efficiently. The paper provides detailed system metrics to prove the viability of their Triton kernel implementation.

*   **Sliding Window Trade-offs (Table 1):** Since full 2-simplicial attention is $O(n^3)$, the sliding window parameters $w_1$ and $w_2$ are critical. The authors evaluate various configurations. For instance, at 32k sequence length, a window of $w_1=1024, w_2=32$ results in **104.1 ms** latency. Reducing the sequence length to 16k with $w_1=512, w_2=32$ drops latency to **55.1 ms**. The chosen configuration of **(512, 32)** balances quality and speed, making complexity comparable to dot-product attention at 48k context.
*   **Throughput (Figure 3):** The custom Triton kernel achieves **520 TFLOPS**. The paper states this rivals the fastest Flash Attention v3 (FAv3) Triton implementations. While Figure 3 is referenced for FLOPs and latencies comparison, the text notes that despite using a higher-level language (Triton) rather than CUTLASS, performance remains competitive for large sequence lengths.
*   **Backward Pass Optimization:** A key engineering detail is the split backward pass (Algorithm 2). By decomposing the gradient computation into two kernels to avoid atomic operation overhead, the authors trade recomputation overhead for reduced memory contention. This design choice was necessary to make training stable and efficient within the Triton framework.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment and Limitations

While the results are promising, a critical educator must highlight the conditions and limitations inherent in the experimental design.

*   **Scale Dependency:** The benefits are **not universal** at small scales. As seen in Table 2, the 1B active parameter model performs *worse* with 2-simplicial attention. This implies a "minimum viable scale" exists where the expressiveness of trilinear attention outweighs the optimization difficulty or capacity constraints. Practitioners cannot simply apply this to small models and expect gains.
*   **Interleaving Density:** The experiments use a fixed interleaving ratio (every 4th layer). The paper does not provide an ablation study on this ratio (e.g., every 2nd vs. every 8th layer). It remains an open question whether a different density could further optimize the trade-off between compute cost and scaling exponent.
*   **Token Budget Fixed:** The scaling law analysis holds the token count constant to isolate the parameter exponent $\alpha$. While this proves token efficiency, it does not explicitly show the behavior if tokens were scaled alongside parameters (the Chinchilla regime). The claim that "tokens can increase at a slower rate than parameters" is an inference from the exponent change, not a direct experimental result of varying token counts.
*   **Implementation Maturity:** The authors candidly state in Section 9 that the Triton kernel is "efficient for prototyping" but "far away from being used in production." They suggest that co-designing for specific hardware accelerators (potentially using CUTLASS) is needed for full deployment. The 520 TFLOPS figure is impressive for Triton but may have headroom for improvement.

**Conclusion on Experiments:** The experiments convincingly support the claim that 2-simplicial attention alters the scaling exponent $\alpha$ for reasoning tasks, provided the model is sufficiently large (>2B active parameters). The crossover from worse performance at 1B to better performance at 3.5B is strong evidence that the architecture unlocks capabilities that standard attention cannot access efficiently, validating the theoretical motivation regarding higher-order interactions.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the 2-simplicial Transformer demonstrates compelling improvements to scaling exponents under token constraints, the approach rests on several assumptions and incurs non-trivial trade-offs. This section critically examines the conditions under which the method succeeds, the scenarios it does not address, and the open questions that remain for future work.

### 6.1 Minimum Viable Scale Requirement

A critical assumption underlying this approach is that models must exceed a certain capacity threshold before 2-simplicial attention provides benefits. The experimental results in **Table 2** reveal a clear scale dependency:

*   At **1 billion active parameters**, the 2-simplicial model performs *worse* than the standard Transformer across all benchmarks (e.g., GSM8k NLL of 0.3302 vs. 0.3277, a **+0.79%** degradation).
*   Benefits only emerge at **2 billion active parameters** and increase at **3.5 billion**.

This implies a **minimum viable scale** exists where the expressiveness gains from trilinear attention outweigh the optimization difficulty or capacity constraints introduced by the more complex mechanism. For practitioners working with smaller models (sub-2B active parameters), this architecture may not be suitable. The paper does not provide a theoretical explanation for this threshold, leaving it as an empirical observation that requires further investigation.

### 6.2 Implementation Maturity and Hardware Co-Design

The authors are candid about the production-readiness of their implementation. In **Section 9 (Discussion)**, they state:

> "Our Triton kernel while efficient for prototyping is still far away from being used in production. More work in co-designing the implementation of 2-simplicial attention tailored to the specific hardware accelerator is needed in the future."

This acknowledgment reveals several practical constraints:

*   **Triton vs. CUTLASS:** While the kernel achieves **520 TFLOPS** (Section 7), the authors note that "further optimization could be achieved with a lower-level language like CUTLASS for finer grained tuning and optimizations." Triton's higher-level abstractions may limit the ability to fully exploit hardware-specific features.
*   **Backward Pass Overhead:** The split backward pass design (Algorithm 2) was necessitated by "Triton's coarser grained pipeline control making it difficult to hide the overhead from atomics." This architectural workaround incurs recomputation overhead for outputs $O$ and attention gradients $dS$, which could be eliminated with more granular control over memory and compute scheduling.
*   **Hardware Specificity:** The current implementation is optimized for NVIDIA GPUs with Tensor Cores. Deployment on alternative accelerators (TPUs, custom AI chips) would require significant re-engineering, as the tiling strategy and kernel fusion decisions are tightly coupled to CUDA architecture.

### 6.3 Computational Complexity Trade-offs

Even with sliding window constraints, 2-simplicial attention remains more computationally expensive than standard attention. **Section 6** provides the complexity formulas:

*   **Causal dot-product attention:** $O(A) = 2n^2$
*   **2-simplicial attention (sliding window):** $O(A^{(2s)}) = 6nw_1w_2$

While the sliding window reduces complexity from cubic $O(n^3)$ to linear in sequence length, the constant factors are significant. The trilinear einsum operation "necessitates an additional multiplication compared to standard dot product attention." This has several implications:

*   **Energy Efficiency:** Higher FLOP counts per token translate to increased energy consumption during training and inference. For organizations with strict carbon or cost budgets, this overhead may offset the token efficiency gains.
*   **Inference Latency:** While **Table 1** shows competitive latencies for specific window configurations (e.g., 55.1 ms for $w_1=512, w_2=32$ at 16k), these measurements are for the attention kernel in isolation. End-to-end inference latency including feed-forward layers and communication overhead in distributed settings is not reported.
*   **Memory Bandwidth:** The trilinear operation requires loading three key/value matrices ($K, V, K', V'$) instead of two, increasing memory bandwidth pressure. This could become a bottleneck on memory-constrained hardware even if compute throughput is sufficient.

### 6.4 Unexplored Architectural Design Choices

Several architectural hyperparameters were fixed without ablation, leaving open questions about optimal configuration:

*   **Interleaving Ratio:** The models use 2-simplicial attention in **every fourth layer** (Section 8). The paper does not test alternative ratios (e.g., every 2nd, every 8th, or only in early/late layers). It remains unknown whether a different density could further optimize the trade-off between compute cost and scaling exponent improvement.
*   **Window Size Selection:** The chosen configuration of $(w_1, w_2) = (512, 32)$ balances latency and quality, but **Table 1** shows significant variation across configurations. For instance, $w_1=1024, w_2=16$ achieves the same 55.1 ms latency as $w_1=512, w_2=32$ at 16k sequence length. The paper does not explore whether different tasks benefit from asymmetric window configurations (e.g., larger $w_2$ for tasks requiring broader secondary key context).
*   **GQA Ratio:** The models use a Grouped Query Attention ratio of **64** (Section 6), inspired by Native Sparse Attention (Yuan et al., 2025). This high ratio was chosen to "enable efficient tiling along query heads," but the interaction between GQA ratio and 2-simplicial attention expressiveness is not studied. Lower GQA ratios might improve quality at the cost of compute efficiency.

### 6.5 Rotation Invariance Not Validated in Experiments

A notable gap exists between the theoretical contributions in **Section 5** and the experimental validation in **Section 8**. The paper derives a determinant-based trilinear form (Equation 9) that preserves rotation invariance, enabling compatibility with RoPE positional encodings. **Theorem 5.1** proves this form can solve Match3 tasks with dimension $d=7$.

However, the authors explicitly state:

> "Since Equation 8 has 2 dot product terms due to Sarrus rule, it would modify Algorithm 1 to use 2 einsums instead of 1 in line 2. [...] in the following sections where we compute the backwards function for 2-simplicial attention, we will use the simpler trilinear form of Equation 5 without loss of generality."

This means the **experiments in Table 2 and Table 3 use the non-rotation-invariant form** (Equation 5), not the determinant form (Equation 9). The practical impact of this choice is unclear:

*   Did the models use RoPE at all? The paper does not explicitly state whether positional encodings were applied during training.
*   If RoPE was used with the non-invariant form, positional information may not be correctly captured, potentially limiting performance on tasks requiring positional reasoning.
*   The determinant form requires 2 einsum operations instead of 1, which would increase compute cost. The trade-off between rotation invariance and efficiency was not empirically evaluated.

### 6.6 Limited Benchmark Scope

The evaluation focuses exclusively on four benchmarks: GSM8k, MMLU, MMLU-pro, and MBPP (**Section 8**). The authors justify this by noting these "most strongly test math, reasoning and coding skills in pre-training." However, this narrow scope leaves several questions unanswered:

*   **Language Modeling Perplexity:** Standard next-token prediction perplexity on corpora like C4 or Pile is not reported. This is the canonical metric for comparing Transformer architectures, and its absence makes it difficult to assess whether 2-simplicial attention improves general language understanding or only specialized reasoning tasks.
*   **Long-Context Tasks:** Despite the sliding window mechanism enabling longer sequences, the paper does not evaluate tasks that specifically require long-context reasoning (e.g., needle-in-haystack retrieval, document QA). The window sizes $(512, 32)$ limit effective context to local neighborhoods, which may not capture long-range dependencies.
*   **Multimodal or Non-Text Domains:** The theoretical motivation cites AlphaFold's triangle attention (Section 2), suggesting potential applications beyond text. However, no experiments on protein structure prediction, image generation, or other modalities are included.

### 6.7 Token Scaling Not Directly Tested

The paper's central claim is that 2-simplicial attention enables **token-efficient scaling**—that tokens can "increase at a slower rate than parameters" compared to Chinchilla scaling (Section 1). However, the experimental design holds the token budget **fixed** across all compared models (**Section 8**):

> "Since we train both the models on the same fixed number of tokens, we may ignore the third term [in the scaling law] and simply write the loss as: $L(N) = E' + \frac{A}{N^\alpha}$"

The inference that tokens can scale more slowly is derived from the exponent $\alpha$ change, not from direct experimentation with varying token counts. This leaves open the possibility that:

*   The exponent improvement might diminish when tokens are scaled alongside parameters (the Chinchilla regime).
*   The benefits observed under token constraints might not transfer to compute-optimal training where both $N$ and $D$ increase proportionally.

A complete validation would require training curves where token counts vary independently of parameter counts, which is computationally prohibitive but would strengthen the token efficiency claim.

### 6.8 Summary of Trade-offs

| Trade-off Dimension | Standard Transformer | 2-Simplicial Transformer | Implication |
|--------------------|---------------------|-------------------------|-------------|
| **Minimum Scale** | Benefits at all scales | Requires 2B+ active params | Not suitable for small models |
| **Compute per Token** | $2n^2$ FLOPs | $6nw_1w_2$ FLOPs | Higher training/inference cost |
| **Implementation** | Mature (FAv3, CUTLASS) | Prototype (Triton) | Production deployment risk |
| **Positional Encoding** | RoPE compatible | Determinant form needed (not tested) | Potential position modeling gap |
| **Scaling Regime** | Chinchilla-optimal | Token-constrained optimal | Benefits depend on data availability |

These trade-offs suggest that 2-simplicial attention is not a universal replacement for standard attention, but rather a specialized tool for specific regimes: large-scale models (>2B active parameters) operating under token constraints where reasoning and coding performance are prioritized over raw throughput or deployment maturity.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work represents a potential inflection point in how we approach large language model architecture design, shifting the focus from merely optimizing compute efficiency to maximizing information extraction per token. By demonstrating that architectural changes can alter scaling exponents—not just loss offsets—the paper challenges the prevailing assumption that Transformer improvements are subject to diminishing returns governed by fixed power laws. This section outlines how this work reshapes the research landscape, identifies critical follow-up studies required to validate these findings, and provides practical guidance for integrating 2-simplicial attention into real-world systems.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Architectural Landscape

The primary implication of this work is the validation of **higher-order attention** as a viable path for scaling in data-constrained regimes. For several years, the field has bifurcated into two camps: standard Transformers (high quality, quadratic complexity) and linear attention mechanisms like State Space Models (e.g., Mamba) (lower quality, linear complexity). This paper proposes a third path: **efficient higher-order attention**.

*   **From Compute-Bound to Data-Bound Design:** As noted in Section 1, the industry is transitioning from a compute-bound regime (where data is infinite) to a data-bound regime (where high-quality tokens are scarce). Standard scaling laws (Hoffmann et al., 2022) dictate balancing parameters and tokens. However, the results in Table 3 show that 2-simplicial attention increases the parameter scaling exponent $\alpha$ by up to **20.2%** on MMLU-pro. This implies that in a data-scarce environment, architects can prioritize model capacity over data volume more aggressively than previously thought safe.
*   **Architecture as a Lever for Exponents:** Prior work (Kaplan et al., 2020; Everett, 2025) suggested architecture tweaks only shift the error offset. This paper provides empirical evidence (Table 3) that specific inductive biases—here, trilinear interactions—can steepen the learning curve itself. This invites a renewed search for other architectural modifications that might similarly affect scaling exponents, moving beyond the "offset-only" paradigm.
*   **Bridging Theory and Systems:** Theoretical works like Clift et al. (2019) and Sanford et al. (2023) established the expressiveness of 2-simplicial attention (e.g., solving Match3 tasks), but lacked practical implementations. By delivering a Triton kernel that rivals Flash Attention v3 in throughput (Section 7), this work demonstrates that theoretical expressiveness need not be sacrificed for systems efficiency. It sets a precedent for **systems-aware architecture design**, where new layers are developed alongside their custom kernels rather than as afterthoughts.

### 7.2 Critical Follow-Up Research

While the results are promising, several gaps identified in Section 6 and Section 9 require targeted research to fully realize the potential of 2-simplicial Transformers.

*   **Empirical Validation of Rotation Invariance:**
    *   *The Gap:* Section 5 derives a determinant-based trilinear form (Equation 9) to ensure compatibility with Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE). However, the experiments in Section 8 use the simpler, non-invariant form (Equation 5).
    *   *The Research:* Future work must train models using the determinant form to verify if rotation invariance improves performance on tasks requiring precise positional reasoning (e.g., code indentation, syntactic structure). Researchers should measure whether the compute cost of the extra einsum operation (2 vs 1) is justified by perplexity gains.
*   **Token Scaling Laws (Varying $D$):**
    *   *The Gap:* The experiments in Section 8 hold the token budget $D$ fixed to isolate the parameter exponent $\alpha$. The claim that "tokens can increase at a slower rate than parameters" is an inference, not a direct measurement.
    *   *The Research:* A comprehensive scaling study should vary $D$ independently of $N$. Specifically, researchers should test if a 2-simplicial model trained on **0.5× the tokens** of a Chinchilla-optimal Transformer achieves comparable loss. This would directly validate the token efficiency claim.
*   **Interleaving Strategy Ablations:**
    *   *The Gap:* The models use 2-simplicial attention in **every fourth layer** (Section 8). It is unknown if this is the optimal density for balancing compute cost and expressiveness.
    *   *The Research:* Systematic ablations should test ratios such as every 2nd, every 8th, or clustering 2-simplicial layers in early vs. late stages. Early layers might benefit more from local token interactions, while late layers might require global context.
*   **Hardware Co-Design (CUTLASS vs. Triton):**
    *   *The Gap:* Section 9 acknowledges the Triton kernel is "far away from being used in production" and suggests CUTLASS for finer-grained tuning.
    *   *The Research:* Engineering efforts should focus on porting the kernel to lower-level languages (C++/CUDA) to reduce the backward pass overhead caused by atomic operations (Section 7). Optimizing for specific hardware (e.g., H100 vs. A100) could close the latency gap with standard attention further.
*   **Cross-Modal Applications:**
    *   *The Gap:* Section 2 mentions AlphaFold's use of triangle attention for protein geometry, but this paper focuses solely on text.
    *   *The Research:* Applying 2-simplicial attention to multimodal tasks (e.g., vision-language models) could leverage its ability to model triplet relationships (e.g., object-object-context interactions). The sliding window mechanism might be particularly effective for high-resolution image tokens.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

Based on the experimental results and limitations, 2-simplicial attention is not a universal replacement for standard attention but a specialized tool for specific domains.

*   **Reasoning and Code Specialists:** The largest scaling exponent gains occur on **GSM8k** (math) and **MBPP** (coding) (Table 3). Organizations building models specifically for logical reasoning, mathematical problem solving, or code generation should prioritize this architecture. The trilinear form likely better captures the dependency structures inherent in logical chains and syntax trees.
*   **Data-Scarce Enterprise Domains:** In verticals where high-quality data is limited (e.g., legal, medical, specialized engineering), the token efficiency of 2-simplicial attention offers a strategic advantage. If an organization cannot access trillions of tokens, this architecture allows them to extract more signal from a smaller corpus by increasing model capacity instead.
*   **Long-Context Local Reasoning:** The sliding window mechanism (Section 6) makes this architecture suitable for tasks requiring dense local reasoning over long sequences, such as document analysis where relationships between nearby entities are critical. However, for tasks requiring global attention (e.g., retrieving a single fact from a 100k token document), the sliding window may be a limitation unless combined with global attention layers.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For practitioners considering adopting this approach, the following guidelines summarize when to prefer 2-simplicial attention over standard Transformers or linear alternatives.

*   **When to Prefer 2-Simplicial Attention:**
    *   **Model Scale:** Your model exceeds **2 billion active parameters**. Table 2 shows performance degradation at 1B active parameters; the architecture requires sufficient capacity to leverage trilinear interactions.
    *   **Data Constraints:** You are operating in a **token-constrained regime** where acquiring more data is costlier than increasing compute.
    *   **Task Focus:** Your primary metrics are **reasoning, math, or coding** rather than general language modeling perplexity.
    *   **Infrastructure:** You have the engineering capacity to maintain custom Triton kernels or invest in CUTLASS optimization.

*   **When to Avoid:**
    *   **Small Models:** For models under 1B active parameters, standard dot-product attention remains superior (Table 2).
    *   **Latency-Critical Production:** If you require production-grade latency on diverse hardware, the current Triton implementation (Section 9) may not be mature enough compared to optimized Flash Attention v3 libraries.
    *   **General Perplexity:** If your goal is next-token prediction on broad corpora (e.g., C4, Pile), the benefits are unproven as the paper only evaluates reasoning benchmarks (Section 8).

*   **Integration Checklist:**
    1.  **Kernel Implementation:** Start with the provided Triton kernels (Listings 1–3) but budget engineering time for optimization. Expect higher memory bandwidth usage due to loading $K, V, K', V'$.
    2.  **Layer Configuration:** Begin with the **interleaved strategy** (every 4th layer) to ensure training stability. Do not replace all layers initially.
    3.  **Window Tuning:** Use the $(w_1, w_2) = (512, 32)$ configuration as a baseline (Section 6), but profile latency on your specific hardware sequence lengths using Table 1 as a reference.
    4.  **Positional Encodings:** Be aware that the experiments did not use the rotation-invariant determinant form. If using RoPE, monitor for positional degradation or implement the determinant form (Equation 9) despite the compute cost.

By treating 2-simplicial attention as a specialized component rather than a wholesale replacement, practitioners can harness its superior scaling properties for reasoning tasks while mitigating the computational and engineering costs identified in this work.
