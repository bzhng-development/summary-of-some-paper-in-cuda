## 1. Executive Summary
This paper introduces Iterative Group Relative Policy Optimization (iGRPO), a two-stage reinforcement learning algorithm that addresses the lack of self-reflection in existing methods like Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) by enabling models to iteratively refine their own best drafts during training. iGRPO operates by sampling multiple exploratory drafts in Stage 1, selecting the highest-reward output, and conditioning Stage 2 refinements on this self-generated feedback without increasing the total rollout budget. This approach achieves new state-of-the-art results on mathematical reasoning benchmarks, specifically reaching 85.62% on AIME24 and 79.64% on AIME25 using OpenReasoning-Nemotron-7B trained on AceReason-Math, while consistently outperforming standard GRPO across 7B, 8B, and 14B model scales.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Missing Self-Reflection in RL Training

Large Language Models trained with Reinforcement Learning have made significant progress on mathematical reasoning, yet they share a fundamental limitation: **they solve problems in a single pass without reflecting on their own outputs**. This paper identifies a critical gap between how humans approach complex problems and how LLMs are typically trained.

Consider how humans solve nontrivial mathematical problems. We rarely produce a correct solution on the first attempt. Instead, we:
- Write an initial draft
- Identify mistakes or gaps in reasoning
- Refine the solution based on internal feedback

This iterative process is well-documented in cognitive science literature (Flower & Hayes, 1981; Simon, 2012; Polya, 2014), yet **existing RL frameworks do not capitalize on this iterative refinement process** (Introduction, paragraph 2). The model generates once, receives a scalar reward, and updates — but it never learns to look back at its own attempt and improve upon it.

### Why This Gap Matters

The absence of self-feedback has both practical and theoretical consequences:

**Practical Impact:** Complex mathematical reasoning tasks like AIME (American Invitational Mathematics Examination) problems require multi-step derivations where a single error late in the chain invalidates the entire solution. The paper notes that gains from iGRPO "concentrate on multi-step benchmarks where a mostly-correct attempt can fail due to a small late error" (Section 4.2). Without a mechanism to identify and correct these near-miss failures, models leave performance on the table.

**Theoretical Significance:** The gap represents a missed opportunity for **bootstrapped learning** — where the model's own improving capabilities generate increasingly useful training signals. As the paper states in Section 3.2.1, standard approaches treat each generation as independent, "ignoring potentially valuable information from the model's own generation process."

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

To understand iGRPO's contribution, we need to examine what came before and where each approach falls short.

#### Reinforcement Learning for Reasoning

**RLHF and Preference-Based Training:** Early work like InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022) and ChatGPT (Achiam et al., 2023) demonstrated that incorporating preference-based rewards improves usability and correctness. However, these methods rely on human feedback signals rather than leveraging the model's own outputs for self-improvement.

**DeepSeek-R1 and Zero-Configuration RL:** DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025) introduced a "zero configuration" approach where RL directly enhances the base model without intermediate supervised fine-tuning. This breakthrough sparked numerous efforts to replicate or refine its underlying RL mechanisms (Zeng et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025). However, these methods still operate in a single-shot generation paradigm.

#### GRPO and Its Variants

**Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO):** The foundation for iGRPO is GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), a value-function-free alternative to Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO). GRPO's key innovation is computing advantages through group-relative reward normalization rather than training a separate critic model.

For a prompt $q$, GRPO samples $G$ candidate outputs $\{o_1, o_2, \ldots, o_G\}$ and computes the advantage for each output $o_i$ as:

$$\hat{A}_{i,t} = \frac{R_i - \text{mean}(\{R_1, \ldots, R_G\})}{\text{std}(\{R_1, \ldots, R_G\})}$$

where $R_i$ is the reward for output $o_i$ (Section 3.1). All tokens $t$ in $o_i$ share the same advantage $\hat{A}_i$, reflecting a single scalar reward per completion.

**GRPO Variants:** Several works have extended GRPO to address specific limitations:
- **Dr. GRPO** (Liu et al., 2025) analyzes bias in token-level objectives and proposes removing divisions by sequence length
- **DAPO** (Yu et al., 2025) targets long chain-of-thought training through dynamic sampling and reward shaping
- **GSPO** (Zheng et al., 2025) operates at the sequence level to improve stability in Mixture-of-Experts settings

However, as the paper notes in Section 2, "these approaches primarily focus on stabilizing or correcting the underlying optimization objective." They do not address the fundamental single-pass generation limitation.

#### Self-Learning and Self-Improvement Methods

A parallel line of work explores having models learn from their own outputs:

**Self-Verification** (Zhang et al., 2025a) unifies problem-solving and generative verification within a single RL framework, using the model's own verification scores to reweight sampled solutions. **Critique-GRPO** (Zhang et al., 2025b) augments GRPO with natural-language critiques by generating critique-conditioned refinements.

These methods have a critical limitation: **they blur the roles of reward provider and reward recipient** (Section 2, Related Work). The model must allocate capacity to generating critiques or verification rationales — behaviors that are "only indirectly optimized by the outcome reward" (Section 4.2). This introduces complexity and potential failure modes if the model's self-evaluation is unreliable.

**In-Context Learning (ICL):** Another relevant approach conditions generation on fixed demonstrations. However, ICL suffers from a "fundamental limitation: the conditioning signal remains fixed throughout training and does not adapt to the evolving policy's capabilities" (Section 3.2.1).

### How iGRPO Positions Itself

iGRPO occupies a distinct niche relative to these prior approaches. The key differentiators are:

**1. Dynamic Self-Conditioning vs. Static Demonstrations:** Unlike ICL's fixed examples, iGRPO's conditioning signal is "generated by the policy itself and co-evolves with learning" (Section 3.2.1). As the policy improves, the quality of drafts it generates improves, creating a bootstrapping effect captured in Proposition 3.1:

$$\mathbb{E}[R_\varphi(\hat{d}_\theta(q))] = 1 - (1 - V_\theta(q))^N$$

where $V_\theta(q)$ is the expected reward under policy $\pi_\theta$ and $N$ is the number of drafts sampled. This shows the expected quality of the selected draft increases monotonically with the policy's success probability.

**2. Clear Separation vs. Blurred Roles:** Unlike Self-Verification or Critique-GRPO, iGRPO "uses externally evaluated best-prior drafts as an in-context guide" (Section 2). The reward signal comes from an external verifier (typically a rule-based answer checker), not from the model's own critique. This "maintains a clearer separation between the model's generation process and the reward signal" while still leveraging self-improvement.

**3. Orthogonal to Optimization Stability:** While Dr. GRPO, DAPO, and GSPO focus on stabilizing the optimization objective itself, iGRPO is "orthogonal: it introduces a two-stage mechanism that uses externally evaluated best drafts as additional training context, shaping the data distribution seen by the optimizer" (Section 2). This means iGRPO can potentially be combined with these stability improvements.

**4. Matched Compute Budget:** Crucially, iGRPO achieves its gains without increasing the total rollout budget. The paper explicitly keeps $N + G = G_{\text{GRPO}}$, redistributing the same number of completions across Stage 1 (draft generation) and Stage 2 (refinement) rather than increasing them (Section 3.2.5). This makes the comparison fair and the improvements attributable to the algorithmic design rather than additional compute.

In summary, iGRPO addresses a specific, well-motivated gap — the lack of iterative self-reflection in RL training — through a mechanism that is both theoretically grounded (via the bootstrapping analysis) and practically efficient (via matched rollout budgets). It positions itself not as a replacement for GRPO, but as an extension that adds a self-feedback layer while preserving GRPO's efficiency advantages over PPO.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a reinforcement learning algorithm paper that introduces a two-stage training mechanism enabling language models to learn from their own best attempts during optimization. The core idea is simple but powerful: instead of generating once and updating, the model first explores multiple draft solutions, picks the strongest one, then learns to refine beyond that draft in a second generation pass — all within a single training step and without increasing total compute budget.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a modified reinforcement learning training loop that teaches language models to iteratively improve their own reasoning outputs by conditioning on their best prior attempts. The problem it solves is that standard RL training treats each generation as independent, missing the opportunity for the model to learn refinement behaviors that humans naturally use when solving complex problems. The solution takes the shape of a two-stage pipeline embedded within each training iteration: first generate and select, then condition and refine.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

Think of iGRPO as a training pipeline with four major components that operate in sequence during each optimization step. **Component 1 (Draft Sampler)** takes an input prompt and generates $N$ candidate draft solutions from the current policy snapshot. **Component 2 (Draft Selector)** evaluates all $N$ drafts using a reward function and identifies the highest-scoring draft as the self-feedback signal. **Component 3 (Prompt Augmenter)** concatenates the original prompt with the selected best draft to create an augmented training context. **Component 4 (Refinement Trainer)** samples $G$ new completions conditioned on this augmented prompt and applies GRPO-style policy updates only to these Stage 2 outputs. Information flows linearly: prompts enter Component 1, drafts flow to Component 2, the winning draft flows to Component 3, and the augmented prompt flows to Component 4 where gradients are computed and the policy is updated. Critically, Stage 1 drafts never receive direct gradient updates — they serve only to shape the training distribution for Stage 2.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Paper Type and Core Mechanism.** This is a reinforcement learning algorithm paper that extends Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) by inserting a self-feedback loop within each training iteration. The fundamental insight is that by conditioning the policy on its own best prior attempt, the model learns a refinement function that maps draft solutions to improved solutions, rather than learning only single-shot generation.

**Background: GRPO Advantage Computation.** To understand iGRPO, you must first understand GRPO's value-function-free advantage estimation, which iGRPO preserves in Stage 2. Given a prompt $q$, GRPO samples a group of $G$ candidate outputs $\{o_1, o_2, \ldots, o_G\}$ from the old policy $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$. A reward model evaluates each output, producing scores $\{R_1, \ldots, R_G\}$. GRPO then normalizes these scores within the group to compute the advantage $\hat{A}_{i,t}$ at each token index $t$ of output $o_i$ using the formula:

$$\hat{A}_{i,t} = \frac{R_i - \text{mean}(\{R_1, \ldots, R_G\})}{\text{std}(\{R_1, \ldots, R_G\})}$$

where $\text{mean}(\cdot)$ and $\text{std}(\cdot)$ denote the sample mean and sample standard deviation of the group's reward scores. All tokens $t$ in completion $o_i$ share the same advantage $\hat{A}_{i,t} = \hat{A}_i$, reflecting that a single scalar reward is assigned to each complete output. If the standard deviation equals zero (all outputs receive identical rewards), the normalized advantages are set to 0, or equivalently a small constant $\delta$ may be added to the denominator — iGRPO applies the same convention. The GRPO objective then maximizes a clipped surrogate similar to PPO, but without requiring a separate critic model to estimate value functions.

**Stage 1: Exploratory Draft Generation.** In iGRPO's first stage, the algorithm samples $N$ candidate drafts from the current policy snapshot for each input prompt $q$. Formally, each draft $d_i$ is sampled as $d_i \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot \mid q)$ for $i = 1, \ldots, N$. Each draft is evaluated using the same reward function $R_\varphi$ that will be used for Stage 2 optimization. The algorithm then identifies the highest-scoring draft using the selection rule:

$$\hat{d} = \arg\max_{i \in \{1,\ldots,N\}} R_\varphi(d_i)$$

This stage performs what the paper calls "implicit curriculum generation" because early in training the selected draft $\hat{d}$ may be weak, but as the policy improves, $\hat{d}$ increasingly represents a high-quality attempt that approaches optimal performance. Importantly, Stage 1 is not differentiated through — no gradients flow back through the draft selection process within a single iteration. However, across iterations, the distribution of selected drafts shifts as $\theta$ evolves, which means the self-conditioned prompts seen in Stage 2 co-evolve with the policy.

**Stage 2: Conditioned Refinement.** The second stage creates an augmented prompt by appending the best draft immediately after the original prompt using concatenation:

$$q' = \text{Concat}(q, \hat{d})$$

In practice, this concatenation uses a fixed prompt template provided in the supplementary materials (Appendix C), which instructs the model to treat the draft as guidance rather than ground truth and to produce a strictly improved final answer. The algorithm then samples a group of $G$ completions from the old policy conditioned on this augmented prompt: $o_j \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot \mid q')$ for $j = 1, \ldots, G$. These completions are scored using the reward function, and GRPO-style advantage estimation is applied exclusively to these Stage 2 outputs. The advantage for each Stage 2 completion $o_j$ is computed as:

$$\hat{A}_j = \frac{R_\varphi(o_j) - \text{mean}(\{R_\varphi(o_1), \ldots, R_\varphi(o_G)\})}{\text{std}(\{R_\varphi(o_1), \ldots, R_\varphi(o_G)\})}$$

All tokens $t$ in $o_j$ share the same advantage $\hat{A}_{j,t} = \hat{A}_j$, consistent with the GRPO formulation. Policy updates are applied only to Stage 2 tokens — Stage 1 drafts serve purely as an adaptive exploration mechanism that shapes the optimization landscape without receiving direct gradient updates.

**Complete iGRPO Objective Function.** The full iGRPO objective combines both stages into a single expectation over prompts, drafts, and refinements. Let $\pi_\theta$ denote the policy being optimized, with $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ representing the policy snapshot at the start of each iteration for importance sampling. The complete objective is:

$$\mathcal{J}_{\text{iGRPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{q \sim P(Q)} \mathbb{E}_{\substack{\{d_i\}_{i=1}^N \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot \mid q) \\ \hat{d} = \arg\max_i R_\varphi(d_i) \\ q' = \text{Concat}(q, \hat{d}) \\ \{o_j\}_{j=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot \mid q')}} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{j=1}^G \frac{1}{|o_j|} \sum_{t=1}^{|o_j|} \left( \min\left(r_{j,t}(\theta) \hat{A}_j, \text{clip}\left(r_{j,t}(\theta), 1 - \epsilon, 1 + \epsilon\right) \hat{A}_j\right) - \beta \hat{D}_{\text{KL}}^{(j,t)} \right) \right]$$

where the importance sampling ratio is computed with respect to the augmented prompt:

$$r_{j,t}(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(o_{j,t} \mid q', o_{j,<t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(o_{j,t} \mid q', o_{j,<t})}$$

and the KL penalty uses the same non-negative per-token estimator as GRPO, computed under the augmented prompt:

$$\hat{D}_{\text{KL}}^{(j,t)} = \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(o_{j,t} \mid q', o_{j,<t})}{\pi_\theta(o_{j,t} \mid q', o_{j,<t})} - \log \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(o_{j,t} \mid q', o_{j,<t})}{\pi_\theta(o_{j,t} \mid q', o_{j,<t})} - 1$$

The key structural difference from vanilla GRPO is that all Stage 2 completions are conditioned on the augmented prompt $q'$, formed by appending the best Stage 1 draft immediately after the original prompt $q$. This provides a self-feedback signal that encourages the policy to refine its solutions beyond its strongest initial attempt.

**Reward Function Design.** Following standard practice for verifiable reasoning tasks, iGRPO employs a rule-based reward function that checks whether the extracted final answer matches the ground truth. Formally:

$$R_\varphi(o) = \mathbb{1}[\text{extract}(o) = a]$$

where $\text{extract}(\cdot)$ parses the final answer from the completion and $a$ is the ground-truth reference answer from the training instance $(q, a) \in \mathcal{B}$. This binary reward, combined with group normalization, provides sufficient signal for distinguishing solution quality within each prompt group. However, the ablation studies show that iGRPO can also work with a generative judge — replacing the rule-based checker with GPT-5 scoring each solution in $[0, 1]$ improves average Pass@1 from 69.87% to 70.81% on DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B (Table 3). The largest gains appear on AIME24/25 and Minerva, which is consistent with partial credit for near-miss reasoning traces that can survive Stage 1 selection and be refined into correct answers in Stage 2.

**Matched Rollout Budget and Computational Cost.** A critical design choice in iGRPO is maintaining the same total sampling budget as standard GRPO to ensure fair comparison. Let $C_{\text{gen}}$ denote the cost of producing one sampled completion (including prompt encoding, autoregressive decoding, and reward evaluation). Standard GRPO samples $G_{\text{GRPO}}$ completions per prompt, giving per-prompt rollout cost $C_{\text{GRPO}} \approx G_{\text{GRPO}} C_{\text{gen}}$. iGRPO samples $N$ Stage 1 drafts and $G$ Stage 2 refinements per prompt, so $C_{\text{iGRPO}} \approx (N + G) C_{\text{gen}}$. The paper keeps the sampling budget fixed by setting $N + G = G_{\text{GRPO}}$, meaning iGRPO redistributes the same number of rollouts across Stage 1 and Stage 2 rather than increasing them. For example, with $G_{\text{GRPO}} = 16$ in GRPO, the experiments use $N = 8$ and $G = 8$ in iGRPO, yielding $C_{\text{iGRPO}} \approx (N + G) C_{\text{gen}} = G_{\text{GRPO}} C_{\text{gen}} \approx C_{\text{GRPO}}$. Empirical measurements confirm this: peak memory usage is nearly identical (54.9349 GB for iGRPO vs 54.9286 GB for GRPO, a difference of roughly 0.0063 GB), and throughput shows only a mild slowdown (0.34 samples/s for iGRPO vs 0.41 samples/s for GRPO) due to the sequential Stage 1 plus Stage 2 decoding (Table S.3). Total GPU hours increase by approximately 13% (94.1 vs 83.3), which the authors view as a favorable tradeoff given the several-point gains on AIME benchmarks.

**Theoretical Bootstrapping Analysis.** The paper provides a theoretical characterization of why iGRPO should work through Proposition 3.1, which analyzes the bootstrapping effect under binary rewards. Assume the reward is binary ($R_\varphi(o) \in \{0, 1\}$) and Stage 1 drafts $\{d_i\}_{i=1}^N$ are sampled i.i.d. from $\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid q)$. Let $V_\theta(q) = \mathbb{E}_{o \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid q)}[R_\varphi(o)]$ denote the expected reward under policy $\pi_\theta$, which equals the success probability $p_\theta(q) = \text{Pr}[R_\varphi(o) = 1]$ in the binary case. Then the expected reward of the selected best draft $\hat{d}_\theta(q) = \arg\max_i R_\varphi(d_i)$ satisfies:

$$\mathbb{E}[R_\varphi(\hat{d}_\theta(q))] = 1 - (1 - V_\theta(q))^N$$

This quantity is monotonically increasing in $V_\theta(q)$. The proof relies on the observation that for binary rewards, $R_\varphi(\hat{d}_\theta(q)) = 1$ if and only if at least one of the $N$ sampled drafts achieves reward 1, which occurs with probability $1 - \text{Pr}[\forall i, R_\varphi(d_i) = 0] = 1 - (1 - V_\theta(q))^N$ under i.i.d. sampling. The function $1 - (1 - x)^N$ is increasing in $x \in [0, 1]$, establishing monotonicity. Consequently, if optimization increases $V_\theta(q)$, then $\mathbb{E}[R_\varphi(\hat{d}_\theta(q))]$ also increases, improving the conditioning quality for subsequent iterations in expectation. This captures the bootstrapping effect: better policies generate better drafts, which provide more informative conditioning, which enables learning better policies. The model does not merely learn to copy the conditioning — it learns a refinement function that maps draft attempts to improved solutions.

**Dynamic Self-Conditioning vs. Static In-Context Learning.** The paper distinguishes iGRPO's approach from standard in-context learning (ICL), which conditions on fixed demonstrations. In ICL, the objective is $\mathcal{J}_{\text{ICL}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{q \sim P(Q)}[\mathbb{E}_{o \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid q, e)}[R_\varphi(o)]]$ where $e$ is a static example. However, ICL suffers from a fundamental limitation: the conditioning signal $e$ remains fixed throughout training and does not adapt to the evolving policy's capabilities. iGRPO introduces dynamic self-conditioning where the conditioning signal is generated by the policy itself and co-evolves with learning:

$$\mathcal{J}_{\text{iGRPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{q \sim P(Q)}[\mathbb{E}_{o \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid q'_\theta(q))}[R_\varphi(o)]]$$

where $q'_\theta(q) = \text{Concat}(q, \hat{d}_\theta(q))$ and $\hat{d}_\theta(q)$ is the best draft generated by the current policy. This creates a coupled dynamical system where the constructed prompt $q'_\theta(q)$ changes as $\theta$ changes, since $\hat{d}_\theta(q)$ is generated by $\pi_\theta$. Although the expressions are written in terms of $\pi_\theta$ for clarity, iGRPO follows the PPO/GRPO convention for stable optimization: at each iteration, a snapshot $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ is taken, both Stage 1 drafts and Stage 2 completions are sampled from $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$, and $\theta$ is updated using importance ratios computed relative to $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$. Dynamic self-conditioning is used only during training — at inference time, the trained policy generates directly from the original prompt $q$ without any draft generation, conditioning, or specialized selection scheme.

**Key Hyperparameters and Training Configuration.** The main experiments use specific configurations that are important to understand for reproducibility. All models are trained for one epoch with a KL divergence loss coefficient of 0 and no entropy regularization. The learning rate is $1 \times 10^{-6}$ with a cosine schedule. The paper generates eight completions per prompt in standard GRPO, which is halved in each stage for iGRPO (so $N = 4$ and $G = 4$ in the main 7B/14B experiments, though Section 3.2.5 mentions $N = 8, G = 8$ for the $G_{\text{GRPO}} = 16$ example). The maximum prompt length is 1,024 tokens across all datasets, and the completion length is capped at 4,096 tokens. The batch size is 1024 for evaluation. For training, the global batch size is 128 with per-device batch size of 16 for 7B models (Table S.2). The temperature is set to 0.7 for generation during training, and evaluation uses temperature 0.6 with top-p of 0.95 and generation length of 65,000 tokens. Two reward functions (accuracy and format) are each weighted at 1.0. The KL coefficient ablation (Table S.4) shows that $\beta = 0$ achieves 69.87% while $\beta = 0.0001$ achieves 70.23%, but the difference is marginal, so $\beta = 0$ is used to reduce overhead.

**Why This Design Over Alternatives.** The paper makes several deliberate design choices that distinguish iGRPO from alternative self-improvement approaches. First, iGRPO uses externally evaluated drafts rather than model-generated critiques, which maintains a clearer separation between the generation process and the reward signal compared to methods like Self-Verification or Critique-GRPO that blur the roles of reward provider and reward recipient. Second, the two-stage structure is orthogonal to optimization stability improvements like Dr. GRPO, DAPO, or GSPO, meaning iGRPO can potentially be combined with these methods. Third, the matched rollout budget ensures that performance gains are attributable to the algorithmic design rather than additional compute. Fourth, the refinement wrapper generalizes beyond GRPO — Table 2 shows that adding the same self-feedback layer to DAPO and GSPO yields consistent +1.1 to +1.2 macro-average improvements, indicating the gains primarily stem from the refinement interface rather than GRPO-specific details. Fifth, the entropy analysis (Figure 3) shows that iGRPO delays premature mode collapse compared to GRPO: both methods start at 2.45 nats, but GRPO collapses rapidly (0.60 at 10%, 0.42 by 30%) while iGRPO decays more gradually (0.80 at 15%, 0.48 at 30%) and remains slightly higher through mid-training (0.46 at 60%) before converging near GRPO (0.44 vs 0.42 at 100%). This suggests iGRPO preserves alternative continuations long enough to recover from near-miss reasoning traces, which explains part of why the method works.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the conceptual breakthroughs that make iGRPO work, separating fundamental innovations from incremental refinements. Understanding these insights matters because they reveal *why* the method succeeds and what principles might transfer to other domains beyond mathematical reasoning.

### 4.1 Dynamic Self-Conditioning as a Bootstrapped Curriculum

**What makes this novel:** Prior self-improvement methods either use static demonstrations (In-Context Learning) or require the model to generate its own evaluation signal (Self-Verification, Critique-GRPO). iGRPO introduces a third path: the conditioning signal is *generated by the policy itself* but *evaluated externally*, creating a co-evolving training distribution without conflating generation and evaluation roles.

**Why this is a fundamental innovation:** The bootstrapping analysis in Proposition 3.1 is not merely a theoretical curiosity — it establishes that iGRPO creates an **implicit curriculum** that adapts to the model's current capabilities. Early in training, selected drafts are weak, so Stage 2 learns basic refinement. As the policy improves, drafts become stronger, and Stage 2 learns to fix increasingly subtle errors. This is fundamentally different from static curricula (which may be too easy or too hard) or fixed demonstrations (which don't adapt).

**Significance beyond performance numbers:** This insight changes how we think about RL training data distribution. Instead of treating training examples as fixed, iGRPO shows that **the training context itself can be a learned variable** that evolves with the policy. The paper demonstrates this works for mathematical reasoning, but the principle — that self-generated conditioning signals can create adaptive curricula — could apply to code generation, scientific reasoning, or any domain where iterative refinement is valuable.

**What distinguishes this from incremental work:** Many RL papers tweak optimization objectives (clipping thresholds, advantage normalization, KL penalties). iGRPO instead changes *what data the optimizer sees* at each step. This is orthogonal to objective-level improvements and explains why Table 2 shows the refinement wrapper improves DAPO and GSPO — the innovation is in the training loop structure, not the gradient computation.

### 4.2 Clean Separation of Generation and Evaluation Roles

**What makes this novel:** Methods like Self-Verification and Critique-GRPO ask the model to both generate solutions *and* evaluate them (or generate critiques). This blurs roles and requires the model to allocate capacity to behaviors that are "only indirectly optimized by the outcome reward" (Section 4.2). iGRPO maintains a crisp separation: the model generates, an external verifier scores, and the best generation becomes context for refinement.

**Why this is architecturally significant:** This separation has three practical consequences that prior work overlooked:

1. **Reward reliability:** External verifiers (rule-based answer checkers) provide consistent signals. Model-generated critiques can be unreliable, especially early in training when the model is weak. iGRPO avoids training on potentially noisy self-evaluations.

2. **Capacity allocation:** The model doesn't need to learn critique generation — it only learns refinement. This focuses optimization on the core task (producing correct answers) rather than auxiliary behaviors (explaining why answers are right/wrong).

3. **Training stability:** Because Stage 1 drafts are selected by external reward, not self-evaluation, the quality of conditioning signals is grounded in objective performance. This prevents the feedback loops that can occur when models learn from their own potentially flawed critiques.

**What distinguishes this from incremental work:** This is a design principle, not a hyperparameter tuning choice. It reflects a philosophical stance on how RL should incorporate self-improvement: leverage the model's generation capabilities while keeping evaluation grounded externally. The ablation in Table 3 shows this principle is flexible — iGRPO *can* use a generative judge (GPT-5) when rule-based verification isn't available, but the default design prioritizes external grounding.

### 4.3 Matched Budget Efficiency as a Design Philosophy

**What makes this novel:** Many RL improvements come from scaling — more samples, larger models, longer training. iGRPO explicitly constrains itself to the same rollout budget as baseline GRPO ($N + G = G_{\text{GRPO}}$), forcing the gains to come from algorithmic efficiency rather than compute scaling.

**Why this matters practically:** The matched budget design has two important implications:

1. **Attribution clarity:** When iGRPO outperforms GRPO in Table 1, we know the improvement comes from the two-stage mechanism, not from sampling more candidates. This makes the results scientifically cleaner and more reproducible.

2. **Deployment feasibility:** The empirical measurements in Table S.3 show peak memory is virtually identical (54.9349 GB vs 54.9286 GB) and throughput decreases only modestly (0.34 vs 0.41 samples/s). The 13% increase in total GPU hours (94.1 vs 83.3) is a predictable overhead from sequential Stage 1 + Stage 2 decoding, not a fundamental scaling barrier.

**What distinguishes this from incremental work:** Most RL papers report improvements under whatever compute budget is available, making it hard to disentangle algorithmic gains from scaling effects. iGRPO's explicit budget matching is a methodological contribution — it establishes a standard for fair comparison that future work should emulate. The fact that gains persist under this constraint demonstrates the mechanism itself is valuable, not just the additional sampling.

### 4.4 Entropy Dynamics Reveal the Learning Mechanism

**What makes this novel:** Many RL papers report final performance but don't analyze *how* training dynamics differ. Figure 3 provides this analysis: iGRPO maintains higher mid-training entropy (0.46 nats at 60% vs 0.42 for GRPO) before converging to similar final values (0.44 vs 0.42 at 100%).

**Why this insight matters:** The entropy trajectory explains *why* iGRPO works, not just *that* it works:

- **Delayed mode collapse:** GRPO's rapid entropy collapse (0.60 at 10%, 0.42 by 30%) suggests the policy quickly commits to specific solution patterns. iGRPO's gradual decay preserves exploration longer.

- **Recovery from near-misses:** The paper notes that conditioning on the best draft "encourages refinement around a strong scaffold while preserving alternative continuations long enough to recover from near-miss reasoning traces." This explains why gains concentrate on AIME benchmarks where "a mostly-correct attempt can fail due to a small late error" (Section 4.2).

- **Not just more randomness:** Final entropies are close, so the gains aren't from higher randomness at convergence. Instead, iGRPO sustains exploration *during* learning, allowing the policy to discover better solutions before committing.

**What distinguishes this from incremental work:** This is diagnostic insight that informs future method design. If entropy preservation matters, then methods that explicitly regularize entropy (or prevent premature collapse) might complement iGRPO. The paper doesn't explore this, but the analysis opens the door. It also suggests that monitoring training dynamics — not just final metrics — should be standard practice in RL for reasoning.

### 4.5 Summary: What's Fundamental vs. Incremental

| Innovation | Type | Why It Matters |
|------------|------|----------------|
| Dynamic self-conditioning with external evaluation | **Fundamental** | Creates adaptive curricula without blurring generation/evaluation roles |
| Two-stage refinement wrapper | **Fundamental** | Orthogonal to optimizer; works with GRPO, DAPO, GSPO (Table 2) |
| Matched budget design | **Methodological** | Establishes fair comparison standard; proves gains are algorithmic |
| Entropy dynamics analysis | **Analytical** | Explains mechanism, not just performance; guides future work |
| Rule-based vs. generative judge flexibility | **Incremental** | Shows robustness, but not core to the method's success |

The first two innovations represent genuine conceptual advances in how RL training loops can incorporate self-improvement. The matched budget design is a methodological contribution that strengthens the empirical claims. The entropy analysis provides mechanistic understanding that should inform future research. Together, these insights suggest iGRPO's core principle — iterative refinement with external grounding — may transfer beyond mathematical reasoning to other domains where multi-pass problem solving is valuable.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines whether iGRPO's theoretical advantages translate into measurable improvements across diverse models, datasets, and reasoning benchmarks. The experiments are designed to answer three critical questions: Does the two-stage refinement mechanism actually improve performance? Do gains persist across model scales and families? And what computational trade-offs does iGRPO introduce?

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Training Datasets.** The paper evaluates iGRPO across two primary training distributions (Section 4.1):

- **MATH dataset** (Hendrycks et al., 2021): 7,500 step-by-step competition problems used for the main controlled comparisons in Table 1.
- **AceReason-Math** (Chen et al., 2025b): 9,400 problems used for the stronger base model experiments in Figure 2.
- **OpenR1-Math-220k** (R1, 2024): A subset of 94,000 examples used for the 14B scaling study in Appendix B.

All models are trained for **one epoch** with a KL divergence loss coefficient of **0** and no entropy regularization. The learning rate is $1 \times 10^{-6}$ with a cosine schedule.

**Evaluation Benchmarks.** Performance is measured on six mathematical reasoning benchmarks plus two general reasoning tasks (Section 4.1):

| Benchmark | Domain | Difficulty |
|-----------|--------|------------|
| AIME24 / AIME25 | Competition math | Highest (US invitational exam) |
| MATH500 | Competition math | High |
| AMC23 | Competition math | High |
| GSM8K | Grade school math | Medium |
| Minerva Math | Graduate-level math | Highest |
| GPQA | Graduate reasoning | Highest |
| MMLU-Pro | General knowledge | Medium-High |

**Metrics and Robustness.** The primary metric is **Pass@1 accuracy**. However, the paper applies different averaging strategies to ensure robustness (Section 4.1):

- **AIME24/AIME25**: Averaged over **64 runs** due to high variance in competition math performance.
- **Other benchmarks**: Averaged over **8 runs**.
- **Evaluation decoding**: Temperature of 0.6, top-p of 0.95, and generation length of 65,000 tokens using the NeMo-Skills framework.

**Baselines.** The controlled study compares iGRPO against three categories of methods (Table 1):

1. **Vanilla GRPO**: The direct baseline to isolate the effect of the two-stage mechanism.
2. **Self-Verification** (Zhang et al., 2025a): A method that unifies problem-solving and generative verification within a single RL framework.
3. **Critique-GRPO** (Zhang et al., 2025b): Augments GRPO with natural-language critiques for refinement.

For the ablation studies, the paper also tests the refinement wrapper on **DAPO** and **GSPO** (Table 2) to demonstrate generalization beyond GRPO.

**Matched Compute Budget.** A critical experimental design choice is keeping the total sampling budget fixed across all methods (Section 4.2). For the main experiments, all methods use **eight completions per prompt**. iGRPO redistributes this budget across stages (e.g., $N = 4$ drafts in Stage 1, $G = 4$ refinements in Stage 2) rather than increasing total rollouts. This ensures that performance differences reflect algorithmic improvements, not additional compute.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**Controlled Study Across Model Scales (Table 1).** The paper evaluates iGRPO on 7B, 8B, and 14B models from two families: Nemotron and DeepSeek-R1 Distilled. The results show consistent improvements across all scales:

| Model Family | Scale | GRPO Avg | iGRPO Avg | Gain |
|--------------|-------|----------|-----------|------|
| Nemotron-H-8B-Base-8K | 8B | 41.08% | **45.04%** | +3.96 |
| DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen | 7B | 68.29% | **69.87%** | +1.58 |
| OpenMath-Nemotron | 7B | 75.02% | **76.07%** | +1.05 |
| DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen | 14B | 71.29% | **73.02%** | +1.73 |
| OpenMath-Nemotron | 14B | 76.73% | **78.00%** | +1.27 |

Several patterns emerge from Table 1:

- **Largest gains on weaker base models**: The Nemotron-H-8B-Base-8K model shows the largest absolute improvement (+3.96 points), suggesting iGRPO provides the most benefit when the base policy has more room for improvement.
- **Consistent gains on hard benchmarks**: Improvements concentrate on AIME24/25 and Minerva Math — benchmarks that "most strongly penalize near-miss reasoning" (Section 4.2). For example, on Nemotron-H-8B, AIME25 improves from 7.78% (GRPO) to 9.17% (iGRPO).
- **Diminishing but persistent gains on stronger models**: Even on OpenMath-Nemotron-7B, which starts at 74.83% average, iGRPO adds +1.05 points. This indicates the refinement mechanism extracts value even when the base model is already strong.

**Competition Against Critique-Style Methods.** Table 1 also compares iGRPO to Self-Verification and Critique-GRPO. On the DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B model:

- Self-Verification: 69.08% average
- Critique-GRPO: 69.14% average
- **iGRPO: 69.87% average**

iGRPO outperforms both baselines despite not requiring the model to generate critiques or verification rationales. The paper attributes this to iGRPO's "tightly aligned" training loop that keeps optimization focused on the verifiable outcome reward rather than auxiliary behaviors (Section 4.2).

**State-of-the-Art Results (Figure 2).** When applied to OpenReasoning-Nemotron-7B trained on the harder AceReason-Math dataset, iGRPO achieves new best results:

> "iGRPO improves performance across all benchmarks, with the strongest gains on AIME24/25 (+1.52/+1.78) and clear transfer beyond math to GPQA (+1.84) and MMLU-Pro (+0.91), yielding a +1.23 overall average improvement." (Section 4.3)

The specific AIME scores are:
- **AIME24: 85.62%** (new state-of-the-art)
- **AIME25: 79.64%** (new state-of-the-art)

Critically, these gains transfer beyond mathematics to general reasoning benchmarks (GPQA, MMLU-Pro), indicating that "iterative self-feedback improves broadly useful refinement behaviors rather than only math-specific patterns" (Section 4.3).

**Scaling to 14B Parameters (Table 1 and Appendix B).** The benefits persist at larger scales:

- DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-14B: AIME24 improves from 60.26% (GRPO) to **64.06%** (iGRPO) — a +3.80 point gain on the hardest benchmark.
- OpenMath-Nemotron-14B: AIME25 improves from 64.53% (GRPO) to **65.57%** (iGRPO).

Appendix B presents an additional scaling study on OpenMath-Nemotron-14B trained on 94,000 examples from OpenR1-Math-220k (Table S.1):

| Model | AIME25 | AIME24 | MATH500 | AMC | GSM8K | Minerva | Avg |
|-------|--------|--------|---------|-----|-------|---------|-----|
| OpenMath-Nemotron-14B | 61.18% | 73.28% | 95.55% | 95.00% | 94.01% | 33.46% | 75.41% |
| + iGRPO | **66.04%** | **76.61%** | **96.90%** | **97.50%** | **94.16%** | **38.24%** | **78.24%** |

This represents a +2.83 point average improvement, with particularly large gains on Minerva Math (+4.78 points), suggesting iGRPO helps larger models correct "residual errors that survive strong pretraining and standard RL fine-tuning" (Section 4.2).

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

**Beyond GRPO: The Refinement Wrapper Generalizes (Table 2).** A critical ablation tests whether iGRPO's two-stage mechanism is specific to GRPO or can improve other group-based PPO variants. The paper applies the same self-feedback refinement layer to DAPO and GSPO under matched rollout budgets:

| Base Method | Base Avg | + iGRPO Wrapper | Δ |
|-------------|----------|-----------------|---|
| DAPO | 69.74% | 70.93% | +1.19 |
| GSPO | 69.20% | 70.31% | +1.11 |

This demonstrates that "the gains primarily stem from the refinement interface rather than GRPO-specific details" (Section 5). The wrapper is orthogonal to the underlying optimization objective and can be layered on top of other methods.

**Generative Judge vs. Rule-Based Reward (Table 3).** The default reward function is a binary rule-based checker (Eq. 6), but iGRPO only requires a scalar reward to rank drafts and compute advantages. The paper tests replacing this with a GPT-5 judge that scores solutions in $[0, 1]$:

| Benchmark | Rule-based | GPT-5 Judge | Δ |
|-----------|------------|-------------|---|
| AIME25 | 40.16% | 41.12% | +0.96 |
| AIME24 | 56.30% | 57.45% | +1.15 |
| MATH500 | 93.80% | 94.20% | +0.40 |
| AMC | 95.00% | 96.25% | +1.25 |
| GSM8K | 92.42% | 92.95% | +0.53 |
| Minerva | 41.54% | 42.88% | +1.34 |
| **Average** | **69.87%** | **70.81%** | **+0.94** |

The largest gains appear on AIME24/25 and Minerva, which is "consistent with partial credit for near-miss traces, which lets them survive Stage 1 selection and be refined into correct answers in Stage 2" (Section 5). This shows iGRPO is flexible enough to incorporate richer reward signals when available.

**KL Divergence Coefficient (Table S.4).** The paper varies $\beta \in \{0, 0.0001, 0.001, 0.01\}$ to examine regularization strength:

| $\beta$ | Score (%) |
|---------|-----------|
| 0 | 69.87 |
| 0.0001 | **70.23** |
| 0.001 | 69.31 |
| 0.01 | 69.91 |

While $\beta = 0.0001$ achieves the highest score, the differences are marginal. The paper opts for $\beta = 0$ in main experiments to "reduce overhead and maintain efficiency" (Appendix E). This suggests the self-feedback mechanism itself provides sufficient regularization, reducing dependence on explicit KL penalties.

**Number of Completions (Table S.4).** The paper studies how total completions (split evenly across stages) affect performance:

| Total Completions | Score (%) |
|-------------------|-----------|
| 4 | 67.79 |
| 8 | 69.87 |
| 16 | 70.17 |
| 32 | 70.33 |

Increasing from 4 to 8 completions gives a clear +2.08 point improvement, but gains beyond 8 are modest (+0.46 from 8 to 32). The paper notes that "larger budgets also increase training time and inference latency for minimal returns" (Appendix E), justifying the choice of 8 completions for main experiments.

**Entropy Dynamics (Figure 3).** To understand *how* iGRPO alters learning, the paper tracks per-token Shannon entropy during training:

> "Both methods start at 2.45 nats, but GRPO collapses rapidly (0.60 at 10%, 0.42 by 30%) and then stays flat through the end. In contrast, iGRPO decays more gradually (0.80 at 15%, 0.48 at 30%) and remains slightly higher through mid-training (0.46 at 60%) before converging near GRPO (0.44 vs. 0.42 at 100%)." (Section 5)

This entropy trajectory supports the hypothesis that iGRPO "delays premature mode collapse" by conditioning on the best draft while "preserving alternative continuations long enough to recover from near-miss reasoning traces" (Section 5). The fact that final entropies are close indicates gains come from sustained mid-training exploration, not higher randomness at convergence.

**Response Length Analysis (Figure S.2).** A potential concern is that two-stage generation might produce verbose outputs. However, Figure S.2(b) shows "both methods exhibit nearly identical lengths, with GRPO producing slightly longer outputs on average." This indicates "iGRPO's two-stage process does not manifest in lengthy completions but instead appears to refine solutions within a similar token budget" (Appendix E). Gains arise from improved response quality, not verbosity.

### 5.4 Computational Overhead and Practical Feasibility

**Memory and Throughput (Table S.3).** Despite the two-stage structure, iGRPO's resource usage is nearly identical to GRPO:

| Method | Peak Memory (GB) | Throughput (Samples/sec) | Total GPU Hours |
|--------|------------------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| GRPO | 54.9286 | 0.41 | 83.3 |
| iGRPO | 54.9349 | 0.34 | 94.1 |

Key observations:
- **Peak memory**: Difference of only 0.0063 GB — "practically negligible" (Appendix D.2).
- **Throughput**: Mild slowdown from 0.41 to 0.34 samples/s due to sequential Stage 1 + Stage 2 decoding.
- **Total GPU hours**: 13% increase (94.1 vs 83.3), which the authors view as "a favorable and practical value proposition" given the several-point gains on AIME benchmarks.

**Pass@N Analysis (Figure S.1).** Appendix B.1 examines how accuracy scales with multiple sampling attempts ($N$) on AIME benchmarks for the 14B model:

- **AIME25**: Improves from 66.04% (Pass@1) to 86.67% (Pass@8) to 96.67% (Pass@256).
- **AIME24**: Converges to 93.33% by $N = 16$, with no further improvement at higher $N$.

This reveals that "certain problem distributions, especially in AIME25, may benefit from a larger number of sampled attempts, whereas others, such as those in AIME24, can be adequately solved with fewer solution attempts" (Appendix B.1). The iGRPO-trained model capitalizes on multi-sample scenarios better than the base SFT model, demonstrating improved solution diversity.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment: Do Experiments Support the Claims?

**Strengths of the Experimental Design.**

1. **Fair comparison through matched budgets**: By keeping $N + G = G_{\text{GRPO}}$, the paper isolates algorithmic improvements from compute scaling. This is methodologically rigorous and makes the results more convincing.

2. **Diverse model families and scales**: Testing on Nemotron, DeepSeek-R1, and OpenMath models across 7B, 8B, and 14B scales demonstrates the method's generality rather than overfitting to a specific architecture.

3. **Multiple ablation dimensions**: The paper tests the refinement wrapper on other optimizers (Table 2), swaps reward functions (Table 3), varies hyperparameters (Table S.4), and analyzes training dynamics (Figure 3). This comprehensive ablation strengthens causal claims about what drives improvements.

4. **Transfer beyond math**: Figure 2 shows gains on GPQA and MMLU-Pro, indicating the refinement behavior generalizes beyond the training domain.

**Limitations and Open Questions.**

1. **Single-epoch training**: All models are trained for one epoch (Section 4.1). It's unclear whether iGRPO's advantages persist, diminish, or grow with longer training schedules. The entropy analysis suggests iGRPO delays mode collapse, but long-term dynamics remain unexplored.

2. **Limited failure mode analysis**: The paper doesn't systematically examine cases where iGRPO underperforms GRPO. Table 1 shows iGRPO wins on all benchmarks, but understanding when the two-stage mechanism fails would inform future improvements.

3. **Inference-time overhead not discussed**: While training overhead is quantified (13% more GPU hours), the paper notes that "at inference time, we use the trained policy in the standard single-shot manner" (Section 3.2.1). This is a strength — no inference cost — but it also means the refinement behavior must be internalized during training rather than deployed at test time.

4. **Reward function dependence**: The generative judge ablation (Table 3) shows iGRPO works with both rule-based and learned rewards, but all experiments use verifiable tasks. It's unclear how iGRPO would perform on open-ended tasks without clear ground truth.

5. **Draft selection sensitivity**: The paper uses $\arg\max$ to select the best draft (Eq. 2), but doesn't explore alternative selection strategies (e.g., top-k sampling, reward-weighted averaging). The theoretical analysis assumes binary rewards (Proposition 3.1), but real rewards may be noisier.

**Overall Assessment.** The experiments convincingly support the paper's core claims:

- ✅ iGRPO consistently outperforms GRPO across model scales and families (Table 1).
- ✅ Gains concentrate on hard, multi-step benchmarks where refinement matters most (AIME, Minerva).
- ✅ The refinement wrapper generalizes beyond GRPO to DAPO and GSPO (Table 2).
- ✅ Computational overhead is modest (13% more GPU hours, negligible memory increase).
- ✅ Training dynamics differ in theoretically predicted ways (delayed entropy collapse in Figure 3).

The main limitation is that all experiments focus on mathematical reasoning with verifiable rewards. Whether iGRPO's self-feedback mechanism transfers to open-ended generation tasks remains an open question. However, the GPQA and MMLU-Pro gains in Figure 2 provide preliminary evidence of broader applicability.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While iGRPO demonstrates consistent improvements over GRPO across multiple benchmarks, the paper reveals several important constraints and open questions. Understanding these limitations is critical for practitioners considering adoption and for researchers building on this work.

### 6.1 Training-Only Mechanism: No Inference-Time Refinement

**The constraint:** iGRPO's two-stage refinement operates exclusively during training. As the paper states in Section 3.2.1:

> "Dynamic self-conditioning is used only during training. At inference time, we use the trained policy in the standard single-shot manner, generating directly from the original prompt $q$ without any draft generation, conditioning, or specialized selection scheme."

**Why this matters:** This design choice has two competing implications:

| Aspect | Implication |
|--------|-------------|
| **Deployment cost** | ✅ No inference overhead — the trained model generates in one pass |
| **Capability ceiling** | ⚠️ Refinement behavior must be fully internalized during training; the model cannot iteratively improve at test time |

**Trade-off analysis:** Methods like Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023) or Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023) allow iterative refinement at inference, potentially extracting more performance from the same model. iGRPO sacrifices this flexibility to keep inference simple. The benefit is operational efficiency; the cost is that any refinement capability not learned during training is permanently unavailable.

**Evidence from the paper:** The Pass@N analysis in Figure S.1 shows the trained model *can* generate diverse solutions across multiple samples (AIME25 reaches 96.67% at Pass@256), but this requires external sampling — the model itself doesn't iteratively refine a single output at inference.

**Open question:** Would combining iGRPO's training-time refinement with inference-time iterative decoding yield compounding benefits, or would the two mechanisms interfere?

### 6.2 Dependence on Verifiable Rewards

**The constraint:** All main experiments use rule-based reward functions that check exact answer matching (Eq. 6):

$$R_\varphi(o) = \mathbb{1}[\text{extract}(o) = a]$$

This requires tasks with unambiguous ground truth.

**Why this matters:** The binary reward structure limits applicability to domains where:
- Final answers can be programmatically extracted
- Correctness is objectively verifiable (math, code execution, formal logic)
- Partial credit is either unavailable or must be approximated

**Evidence from the paper:** The generative judge ablation (Table 3) shows iGRPO *can* work with a GPT-5 judge scoring solutions in $[0, 1]$, improving average Pass@1 from 69.87% to 70.81%. However, this experiment still uses mathematical reasoning tasks — the judge evaluates math solutions, not open-ended generation.

**Unaddressed scenarios:**
- **Creative writing or summarization:** No clear ground truth exists for evaluating quality.
- **Code generation without test suites:** Functional correctness requires execution environments.
- **Scientific hypothesis generation:** Correctness may be unverifiable or time-delayed.
- **Dialogue or instruction following:** Quality is subjective and context-dependent.

**Trade-off analysis:** iGRPO's reliance on external evaluation (rather than self-critique) provides reward reliability but constrains the method to verifiable domains. Methods like Self-Verification or Critique-GRPO, which generate their own evaluation signals, could theoretically apply more broadly — though at the cost of reward noise.

### 6.3 Computational Overhead: The 13% Training Tax

**The constraint:** While iGRPO matches GRPO's rollout budget ($N + G = G_{\text{GRPO}}$), the sequential Stage 1 + Stage 2 decoding introduces wall-clock overhead. Table S.3 quantifies this:

| Method | Peak Memory (GB) | Throughput (Samples/sec) | Total GPU Hours |
|--------|------------------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| GRPO | 54.9286 | 0.41 | 83.3 |
| iGRPO | 54.9349 | 0.34 | 94.1 |

**The overhead breakdown:**
- **Memory:** Negligible difference (0.0063 GB) — the two-stage mechanism doesn't increase peak memory because Stage 1 drafts are discarded after selection.
- **Throughput:** 17% reduction (0.41 → 0.34 samples/sec) — sequential decoding prevents parallelization of Stage 1 and Stage 2.
- **Total training time:** 13% increase (83.3 → 94.1 GPU hours) — the cumulative effect of reduced throughput over a full training run.

**Trade-off analysis:** The paper frames this as "a favorable and practical value proposition" (Appendix D.2) given the several-point gains on AIME benchmarks. However, this calculus depends on the use case:

| Scenario | Is 13% overhead acceptable? |
|----------|----------------------------|
| Research / SOTA pursuit | ✅ Yes — performance gains justify cost |
| Production fine-tuning | ⚠️ Depends on budget constraints |
| Resource-constrained environments | ❌ May be prohibitive |
| Large-scale pretraining | ⚠️ 13% at scale is substantial |

**Unaddressed question:** The paper doesn't explore whether the overhead could be reduced through parallelization strategies (e.g., pipelining Stage 1 of batch $k+1$ with Stage 2 of batch $k$).

### 6.4 Single-Epoch Training: Unknown Long-Term Dynamics

**The constraint:** All models are trained for exactly one epoch (Section 4.1, Appendix C). The paper does not report results for multi-epoch training or extended schedules.

**Why this matters:** The entropy analysis (Figure 3) shows iGRPO delays mode collapse during mid-training (0.46 nats at 60% vs 0.42 for GRPO), but both methods converge to similar final entropies (0.44 vs 0.42 at 100%). This raises questions:

- Does iGRPO's advantage persist, diminish, or reverse with longer training?
- Does the bootstrapping effect saturate once the policy reaches a certain capability threshold?
- Could extended training cause the self-conditioned prompts to become too homogeneous, reducing exploration?

**Evidence gap:** The paper provides no ablation on training duration. Table S.4 shows hyperparameter sensitivity (KL coefficient, completion count), but not epoch count.

**Risk:** If iGRPO's gains are primarily early-training phenomena (helping the policy escape poor local optima), then the method may offer limited value for already well-trained models. Conversely, if the bootstrapping effect compounds, multi-epoch training could yield larger gains — but this remains untested.

### 6.5 Draft Selection Strategy: Argmax Without Alternatives

**The constraint:** Stage 1 draft selection uses deterministic argmax (Eq. 2):

$$\hat{d} = \arg\max_{i \in \{1,\ldots,N\}} R_\varphi(d_i)$$

The paper does not explore alternative selection strategies.

**Unexplored alternatives:**
- **Top-k sampling:** Select from the top $k$ drafts randomly to preserve diversity.
- **Reward-weighted sampling:** Sample drafts proportionally to their rewards rather than taking the maximum.
- **Diversity-aware selection:** Penalize drafts that are too similar to previously selected drafts.
- **Uncertainty-aware selection:** Prefer drafts where the reward model is confident.

**Why this matters:** The theoretical analysis (Proposition 3.1) assumes binary rewards and i.i.d. sampling. In practice:
- Rewards may be noisy (especially with generative judges).
- The argmax draft may be an outlier rather than a representative strong solution.
- Always selecting the maximum could reduce diversity in Stage 2 conditioning signals.

**Evidence from the paper:** The completion count ablation (Table S.4) shows diminishing returns beyond 8 completions (70.33% at 32 vs 69.87% at 8). This suggests that simply sampling more drafts and taking argmax has limited value — but alternative selection strategies might extract more signal from the same samples.

### 6.6 Domain Specificity: Math-Centric Evaluation

**The constraint:** All training datasets are mathematical reasoning benchmarks (MATH, AceReason-Math, OpenR1-Math-220k). While Figure 2 shows transfer to GPQA (+1.84) and MMLU-Pro (+0.91), these are still reasoning-heavy tasks.

**Unaddressed domains:**
- **Code generation:** Requires execution-based rewards, not answer extraction.
- **Scientific reasoning:** May involve multi-modal inputs (figures, equations).
- **Multi-turn dialogue:** Requires evaluating coherence across turns, not single completions.
- **Tool use / agent tasks:** Rewards may be delayed or sparse.

**Risk:** The refinement behavior iGRPO learns may be domain-specific. The model learns to "fix math errors" rather than learning general refinement capabilities. The GPQA and MMLU-Pro gains are encouraging but modest (+0.91 to +1.84) compared to AIME gains (+1.52 to +3.80).

**Open question:** Would training on diverse task types (math + code + reasoning) produce more generalizable refinement behaviors, or does iGRPO inherently favor domains with verifiable rewards?

### 6.7 Limited Failure Mode Analysis

**The constraint:** Table 1 shows iGRPO outperforming GRPO on all benchmarks across all model scales. The paper does not systematically analyze cases where iGRPO underperforms or fails.

**Missing analysis:**
- **Per-prompt failure cases:** Are there specific problem types where two-stage refinement harms performance?
- **Draft quality threshold:** At what draft quality does conditioning become unhelpful or misleading?
- **Negative refinement:** Does the model ever learn to "over-correct" and introduce new errors while fixing old ones?
- **Prompt length sensitivity:** Does augmenting prompts with drafts cause context window issues for longer problems?

**Evidence gap:** Figure S.2 shows average training rewards and response lengths, but doesn't break down performance by problem difficulty, topic, or error type.

**Risk:** Without understanding failure modes, practitioners cannot anticipate when iGRPO might degrade performance. The consistent wins in Table 1 could reflect benchmark selection rather than universal superiority.

### 6.8 Hyperparameter Sensitivity: Unexplored Dimensions

**The constraint:** The paper explores KL coefficient (Table S.4) and completion count (Table S.4), but leaves other hyperparameters fixed or underexplored.

**Underexplored parameters:**
| Parameter | Tested Values | Default |
|-----------|---------------|---------|
| KL coefficient $\beta$ | 0, 0.0001, 0.001, 0.01 | 0 |
| Total completions | 4, 8, 16, 32 | 8 |
| Stage 1 / Stage 2 split | Not varied | $N = G$ (equal) |
| Temperature (training) | Not varied | 0.7 |
| Temperature (eval) | Not varied | 0.6 |
| Clip parameter $\epsilon$ | Not reported | Standard PPO default |

**Critical gap:** The Stage 1 / Stage 2 split ($N$ vs $G$) is never ablated. The paper uses equal splits (e.g., $N=4, G=4$ for 8 total completions), but it's unclear whether:
- More Stage 1 drafts (better selection) would help.
- More Stage 2 refinements (better gradient signal) would help.
- The optimal split depends on model scale or task difficulty.

**Risk:** Default hyperparameters may not be optimal for all use cases. Without ablation guidance, practitioners must tune blindly.

### 6.9 Summary: Key Trade-offs

| Trade-off | iGRPO Choice | Implication |
|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Inference complexity** | Single-shot inference | ✅ Simple deployment, ⚠️ refinement must be learned |
| **Reward type** | External verification | ✅ Reliable signals, ⚠️ limited to verifiable tasks |
| **Compute budget** | Matched rollouts, +13% time | ✅ Fair comparison, ⚠️ slower training |
| **Training duration** | One epoch | ✅ Efficient, ⚠️ long-term dynamics unknown |
| **Draft selection** | Argmax | ✅ Simple, ⚠️ no diversity preservation |
| **Domain focus** | Mathematical reasoning | ✅ Strong results, ⚠️ generalization unproven |

**Bottom line:** iGRPO makes deliberate engineering trade-offs that favor training-time simplicity and inference-time efficiency. These choices are well-suited for mathematical reasoning tasks with verifiable rewards and moderate training budgets. However, the method's applicability to open-ended tasks, its behavior under extended training, and its sensitivity to hyperparameter choices remain open questions that future work should address.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how iGRPO reshapes the reinforcement learning landscape for language model reasoning, identifies concrete research directions it opens, and provides practical guidance for practitioners considering adoption. The goal is to translate the paper's technical contributions into actionable insights for both researchers and engineers.

### 7.1 How This Work Changes the Field

iGRPO introduces three paradigm shifts that extend beyond its immediate performance gains on mathematical benchmarks.

**Shift 1: Training Data Distribution as a Learned Variable.** Prior RL methods treat training examples as fixed — the prompt $q$ stays constant, and only the policy $\pi_\theta$ changes. iGRPO demonstrates that **the training context itself can co-evolve with the policy** through dynamic self-conditioning (Section 3.2.1). The augmented prompt $q'_\theta(q) = \text{Concat}(q, \hat{d}_\theta(q))$ changes as $\theta$ improves, creating what the paper calls a "coupled dynamical system." This reframes RL training from optimizing over a static data distribution to optimizing over a distribution that adapts to the model's current capabilities. Future methods may explore other ways to make training contexts adaptive — for example, conditioning on retrieved examples, tool outputs, or multi-agent interactions that evolve during training.

**Shift 2: Orthogonality of Refinement and Optimization.** Table 2 shows the iGRPO refinement wrapper improves both DAPO (+1.19 points) and GSPO (+1.11 points), demonstrating that **the two-stage refinement mechanism is orthogonal to the underlying optimization objective**. This separates two previously conflated questions: "How do we stabilize policy gradients?" (addressed by Dr. GRPO, DAPO, GSPO) and "How do we structure the training loop to enable self-improvement?" (addressed by iGRPO). This separation enables modular research — teams can improve optimization stability and refinement mechanisms independently, then combine them. The field should expect more "wrapper" methods that layer additional training structure on top of existing optimizers.

**Shift 3: Internalized Refinement Without Inference Overhead.** Unlike inference-time methods like Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023) or Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023), iGRPO internalizes refinement behavior during training so that "at inference time, we use the trained policy in the standard single-shot manner" (Section 3.2.1). This achieves the benefits of iterative reasoning without deployment complexity. The entropy analysis in Figure 3 suggests the model learns to preserve exploration during training, then converges to confident single-pass generation. This trade-off — training complexity for inference simplicity — may become a standard design principle for production RL systems where latency and cost matter.

**Shift 4: Matched Budget Methodology as a Standard.** Section 3.2.5 explicitly constrains $N + G = G_{\text{GRPO}}$ to ensure fair comparison. This methodological rigor — isolating algorithmic improvements from compute scaling — should become standard practice in RL for LLMs. Many prior improvements conflate better algorithms with more samples; iGRPO demonstrates how to disentangle these factors. Future papers should report whether gains persist under matched rollout budgets.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper's limitations (Section 6) and unexplored design spaces suggest concrete research agendas.

#### 7.2.1 Domain Generalization Beyond Mathematics

**Open Question:** Does iGRPO's refinement behavior transfer to domains without verifiable rewards?

**Why This Matters:** All experiments use rule-based answer checkers (Eq. 6) or, in one ablation, a GPT-5 judge on math problems (Table 3). The method has not been tested on:
- **Code generation** with execution-based rewards
- **Scientific reasoning** with multi-modal inputs
- **Dialogue systems** with subjective quality metrics
- **Tool-use agents** with sparse, delayed rewards

**Concrete Experiments to Run:**
1. Train on HumanEval or MBPP with test-case execution as the reward function. Measure whether iGRPO improves Pass@1 compared to GRPO under matched budgets.
2. Apply to multi-turn dialogue with a learned reward model. Test whether conditioning on best prior turns improves coherence.
3. Evaluate on tool-use benchmarks (e.g., API call sequences) where rewards are only available after multiple steps.

**Hypothesis:** iGRPO should help most in domains where "near-miss" solutions are common — code that almost passes tests, reasoning that is mostly correct but has one flawed step. Domains with binary success/failure (no partial credit) may see smaller gains.

#### 7.2.2 Training Dynamics and Multi-Epoch Behavior

**Open Question:** How does iGRPO behave under extended training schedules?

**Why This Matters:** All experiments use one epoch (Section 4.1, Appendix C). Figure 3 shows iGRPO delays entropy collapse during mid-training, but both methods converge to similar final entropies (0.44 vs. 0.42 at 100%). It's unknown whether:
- Gains persist, diminish, or reverse with multiple epochs
- The bootstrapping effect saturates at some capability threshold
- Extended training causes self-conditioned prompts to become homogeneous

**Concrete Experiments to Run:**
1. Train for 3–5 epochs and plot performance vs. epoch for both GRPO and iGRPO.
2. Measure draft quality distribution over time — do selected drafts become more homogeneous?
3. Track per-prompt improvement: for each prompt, does Stage 2 consistently outperform Stage 1 across epochs?

**Hypothesis:** Early epochs should show the largest iGRPO gains (escaping poor local optima), with diminishing returns as the policy converges. However, if the bootstrapping effect compounds (Proposition 3.1), later epochs might show sustained advantages.

#### 7.2.3 Draft Selection Strategy Optimization

**Open Question:** Is $\arg\max$ the optimal draft selection strategy?

**Why This Matters:** Section 6.5 notes the paper uses deterministic argmax selection (Eq. 2) without exploring alternatives. The theoretical analysis (Proposition 3.1) assumes binary rewards and i.i.d. sampling, but real rewards may be noisy.

**Concrete Experiments to Run:**
1. **Top-k sampling:** Select randomly from the top $k$ drafts instead of always taking the maximum. Measure whether this preserves diversity in Stage 2 conditioning.
2. **Reward-weighted sampling:** Sample drafts proportionally to $R_\varphi(d_i)^\alpha$ for temperature $\alpha$.
3. **Diversity-aware selection:** Penalize drafts similar to previously selected drafts using token-level similarity metrics.
4. **Uncertainty-aware selection:** If using a generative judge, prefer drafts where the judge is confident (low variance across multiple judge samples).

**Hypothesis:** Argmax should work well when rewards are reliable (rule-based checkers), but stochastic selection may help when rewards are noisy (generative judges) or when diversity in conditioning signals matters.

#### 7.2.4 Stage 1 / Stage 2 Split Optimization

**Open Question:** What is the optimal allocation of completions between draft generation and refinement?

**Why This Matters:** Section 6.8 identifies this as a critical unexplored hyperparameter. The paper uses equal splits ($N = G$) but never ablates this choice.

**Concrete Experiments to Run:**
1. Fix total completions at 8, vary $N \in \{2, 4, 6\}$ with $G = 8 - N$.
2. Measure whether more Stage 1 drafts (better selection) or more Stage 2 refinements (better gradient signal) matters more.
3. Test whether optimal split depends on model scale or task difficulty.

**Hypothesis:** For weaker models, more Stage 1 drafts may help (harder to find good drafts). For stronger models, more Stage 2 refinements may help (drafts are already good, need better gradient signal).

#### 7.2.5 Combining Training-Time and Inference-Time Refinement

**Open Question:** Can iGRPO's internalized refinement be combined with inference-time iterative decoding?

**Why This Matters:** Section 6.1 raises this as an open question. iGRPO learns refinement during training but generates single-shot at inference. Methods like Self-Refine do the opposite — no training refinement, but iterative inference.

**Concrete Approach:**
1. Train with iGRPO to internalize refinement behavior.
2. At inference, generate multiple samples and apply a lightweight refinement pass (using the same prompt template as training).
3. Compare to: (a) iGRPO training + single-shot inference, (b) GRPO training + iterative inference.

**Hypothesis:** The combination should yield compounding benefits — the model has learned refinement during training, and inference-time iteration provides additional correction capacity. However, there may be diminishing returns if the model has already internalized most refinement capability.

#### 7.2.6 Entropy-Regularized Variants

**Open Question:** Can explicit entropy regularization amplify iGRPO's delayed collapse effect?

**Why This Matters:** Figure 3 shows iGRPO naturally maintains higher mid-training entropy (0.46 nats at 60% vs. 0.42 for GRPO). The paper uses $\beta = 0$ for KL regularization (Table S.4), but explicit entropy bonuses might extend this effect.

**Concrete Experiments to Run:**
1. Add an entropy bonus term to the iGRPO objective: $\mathcal{J} + \lambda \mathbb{E}[\mathcal{H}(\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid q'))]$.
2. Vary $\lambda$ and measure whether sustained exploration improves final performance.
3. Compare to methods that explicitly prevent mode collapse (e.g., diversity-promoting objectives).

**Hypothesis:** Moderate entropy regularization should complement iGRPO's natural exploration preservation, particularly on tasks where multiple solution paths exist.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

iGRPO is best suited for specific application profiles. Understanding where it fits helps practitioners decide whether to adopt it.

#### 7.3.1 Ideal Application Profiles

**Use iGRPO when:**
- **Tasks have verifiable rewards:** Math problems, code with test suites, formal logic, constraint satisfaction.
- **Near-miss errors are common:** Multi-step reasoning where a single late error invalidates the solution (AIME-style problems).
- **Inference latency matters:** You want refinement benefits without multi-pass inference overhead.
- **Training budget allows 13% overhead:** Table S.3 shows 94.1 vs. 83.3 GPU hours — acceptable for SOTA pursuit or production fine-tuning where accuracy is critical.
- **Model scale is 7B–14B:** Table 1 shows consistent gains across this range; smaller models may not have capacity to learn refinement, larger models may have diminishing returns.

**Avoid iGRPO when:**
- **Tasks lack verifiable rewards:** Open-ended generation, creative writing, subjective quality tasks (unless you have a reliable generative judge).
- **Training budget is extremely constrained:** The 13% time overhead may be prohibitive at large scale.
- **Inference-time refinement is acceptable:** If you can afford multi-pass generation at inference, methods like Self-Refine may extract more performance without training changes.
- **Single-epoch training is insufficient:** If your use case requires multi-epoch fine-tuning, iGRPO's long-term dynamics are untested.

#### 7.3.2 Specific Downstream Applications

**Mathematical Reasoning Systems:**
- Competition math tutoring (AIME, AMC, Olympiad problems)
- Automated theorem proving assistance
- STEM education platforms requiring step-by-step solutions

**Code Generation and Debugging:**
- Generate code with test-case verification as the reward
- The refinement mechanism can learn to fix compilation errors or failing tests
- Particularly useful for complex algorithms where first attempts often have bugs

**Scientific and Technical Reasoning:**
- Physics problem solving with equation verification
- Chemistry reaction prediction with validity checks
- Engineering design with constraint satisfaction

**Data Processing Pipelines:**
- ETL workflows where outputs can be validated against schemas
- Data cleaning tasks with rule-based quality checks
- Any pipeline where "almost correct" outputs are common and fixable

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For practitioners wanting to implement or integrate iGRPO, here are concrete recommendations based on the paper's specifications.

- **14B models:** 5 nodes × 8 × NVIDIA A100 GPUs (one node for vLLM)

**Key Infrastructure Components:**

- **Reward computation:** Rule-based answer extraction must be fast — bottlenecks here will dominate training time
- **Prompt templating:** Appendix C provides the exact template instructing models to treat drafts as guidance, not ground truth

#### 7.4.2 Hyperparameter Starting Points

**From Table S.2 and Section 4.1:**

**Reward Function Design:**
- Start with rule-based answer extraction (Eq. 6) if available
- If using a generative judge, expect ~1% additional gains (Table 3) but higher compute cost
- Ensure reward computation is deterministic — noisy rewards may undermine draft selection

#### 7.4.3 Integration with Existing Frameworks

**Compatible Optimizers:**
- **GRPO:** Direct replacement (Table 1)
- **DAPO:** Add iGRPO wrapper for +1.19 point gains (Table 2)
- **GSPO:** Add iGRPO wrapper for +1.11 point gains (Table 2)

**Implementation Checklist:**
1. **Stage 1 sampling:** Generate $N$ drafts from $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot \mid q)$
2. **Draft selection:** Compute rewards, select $\hat{d} = \arg\max_i R_\varphi(d_i)$
3. **Prompt augmentation:** Concatenate using the template from Appendix C
4. **Stage 2 sampling:** Generate $G$ refinements from $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot \mid q')$
5. **Advantage computation:** Apply GRPO-style group normalization (Eq. 4)
6. **Policy update:** Apply clipped surrogate objective (Eq. 5) only to Stage 2 tokens

**Critical Implementation Detail:** Stage 1 is not differentiated through within an iteration (Section 3.2.4). The distribution of selected drafts shifts across iterations as $\theta$ evolves, but no gradients flow through the argmax selection in a single step.

#### 7.4.4 When to Prefer iGRPO Over Alternatives

**iGRPO vs. Standard GRPO:**
- Choose iGRPO when you can afford 13% more training time for several-point accuracy gains
- Particularly valuable on hard benchmarks (AIME, Minerva) where near-miss errors dominate

**iGRPO vs. Critique-GRPO:**
- Choose iGRPO when you have reliable external rewards (rule-based checkers)
- Choose Critique-GRPO when you need natural-language explanations or lack verifiable rewards

**iGRPO vs. Self-Verification:**
- Choose iGRPO for simpler training loops with clearer separation of generation and evaluation
- Choose Self-Verification when you want the model to learn explicit verification behavior

**iGRPO vs. Inference-Time Refinement:**
- Choose iGRPO when inference latency is critical (single-shot generation)
- Choose inference-time methods when you can afford multi-pass generation and want maximum accuracy

### 7.5 Summary: The Path Forward

iGRPO establishes that **iterative self-feedback during training** is a viable and effective paradigm for improving language model reasoning. The method's key contributions — dynamic self-conditioning, orthogonal refinement wrappers, and matched budget methodology — provide a foundation for future research rather than a final solution.

**Immediate Next Steps for Researchers:**
1. Test iGRPO on code generation benchmarks (HumanEval, MBPP) with execution-based rewards
2. Ablate Stage 1 / Stage 2 split ratios to find optimal configurations
3. Explore multi-epoch training dynamics and saturation points
4. Combine with explicit entropy regularization to amplify exploration preservation

**Immediate Next Steps for Practitioners:**
1. Start with the hyperparameters in Table S.2 as a baseline
2. Use rule-based rewards when available; consider generative judges only if necessary
3. Monitor training entropy (Figure 3) to detect premature mode collapse
4. Expect 13% training overhead but no inference overhead

**Long-Term Vision:** The principle of dynamic self-conditioning — making training contexts adaptive rather than static — may extend beyond RL for reasoning to any domain where iterative refinement is valuable. Code generation, scientific discovery, multi-agent coordination, and tool use all involve multi-pass problem solving. iGRPO demonstrates one way to internalize this capability during training; future work will explore others.

The field should expect more methods that treat **training data distribution as a learned variable** rather than a fixed input. iGRPO is an early example of this paradigm, and its success on mathematical reasoning suggests the approach has broad applicability. The question is no longer whether self-feedback improves reasoning — Table 1 and Figure 2 answer that definitively. The question now is how far this principle can be extended, and what other training-time mechanisms can internalize capabilities that previously required inference-time computation.
