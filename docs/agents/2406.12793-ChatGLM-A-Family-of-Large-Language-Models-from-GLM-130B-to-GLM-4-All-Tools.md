This paper introduces **GLM-4**, a family of large language models (including **GLM-4**, **GLM-4-Air**, and **GLM-4-9B**) pre-trained on **10 trillion tokens** that achieves performance rivaling **GPT-4** and **Claude 3 Opus** on benchmarks like **MMLU** (83.3), **GSM8K** (93.3), and **HumanEval** (78.5). The work significantly advances the field by delivering **GLM-4 All Tools**, an agent-capable model that autonomously orchestrates web browsing, Python interpretation, and image generation to solve complex tasks, while simultaneously establishing state-of-the-art alignment for **Chinese** language tasks as measured by **AlignBench** (8.00 overall score). By open-sourcing models like **GLM-4-9B** with context windows up to **128K** (and experimental **1M**), the authors provide a scalable, bilingual alternative to proprietary closed-source systems.

## 2. Context and Motivation

The development of Large Language Models (LLMs) has followed a trajectory defined by rapid scaling and architectural standardization, yet significant gaps remain in accessibility, bilingual capability (specifically for Chinese), and autonomous agent functionality. This paper addresses the challenge of building a model family that not only matches the performance of proprietary state-of-the-art systems like GPT-4 but also democratizes access through open weights while introducing robust "All Tools" capabilities for complex task execution.

### The Gap: Proprietary Dominance and the Agent Deficit
While the field has witnessed phenomenal growth since the release of GPT-3 in 2020, the landscape is currently dominated by closed-source models. The standard procedure for creating high-performing LLMs—exemplified by OpenAI's GPT series, Google's PaLM/Gemini, and Anthropic's Claude—involves massive scale-ups followed by rigorous alignment via Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF). However, these models remain inaccessible for local deployment or independent modification.

The specific gaps this paper targets are threefold:
1.  **The Open-Source Performance Gap:** Prior open-source efforts, including earlier generations of the authors' own ChatGLM series, lagged significantly behind proprietary models in reasoning and alignment. For instance, the original `ChatGLM-6B` (released March 2023) scored only **1.5** on GSM8K (grade school math) and **0.0** on BBH (Big-Bench Hard), rendering it incapable of complex reasoning tasks compared to contemporaries.
2.  **Bilingual Alignment Nuance:** Most high-performing models are optimized primarily for English. There is a distinct lack of models that achieve parity with GPT-4 in English *while simultaneously* outperforming it in Chinese cultural and linguistic alignment.
3.  **Autonomous Agent Capabilities:** While base LLMs can generate text, the transition to "agents"—models that can plan, use tools (browsers, interpreters), and execute multi-step tasks autonomously—has largely been the domain of closed APIs. Open models have struggled to reliably orchestrate external tools without hallucination or planning failures.

### Importance: From Passive Chatbots to Active Agents
The significance of bridging these gaps extends beyond academic benchmarking; it represents a shift in how LLMs interact with the real world.
*   **Real-World Impact:** The ability to autonomously use tools transforms an LLM from a passive knowledge retriever into an active problem solver. As illustrated in the paper, a model capable of invoking a **web browser** to fetch real-time data (e.g., global population statistics) and a **Python interpreter** to calculate growth rates (Figure 2) can solve dynamic problems that static training data cannot address. This is critical for applications in research, data analysis, and software development.
*   **Theoretical Significance:** The work challenges the assumption that only massive, closed models can achieve "emergent abilities" in reasoning and tool use. By demonstrating that a **10 trillion token** pre-training regime combined with specific alignment techniques can yield a **9B parameter** model (`GLM-4-9B`) that rivals much larger systems, the paper contributes to the understanding of efficient scaling laws and data quality over sheer parameter count.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
To understand the novelty of GLM-4, one must examine the limitations of the preceding iterations and contemporary open models:

*   **Architectural Constraints in Early Generations:** The first-generation `ChatGLM-6B` utilized a context length of only **2K** tokens and lacked efficient attention mechanisms, limiting its utility for long-document analysis. It relied heavily on SFT with limited RLHF, resulting in poor instruction following (e.g., **0.0** on HumanEval for code generation).
*   **Incremental vs. Fundamental Improvements:** While `ChatGLM2-6B` (June 2023) introduced FlashAttention and extended context to **32K**, and `ChatGLM3-6B` (October 2023) added basic function calling, these were iterative steps. They still fell short of the "All Tools" autonomy where a model recursively plans and executes tool calls based on feedback. For example, early models struggled with the coherence required for multi-round web browsing or complex code debugging.
*   **The "English-Centric" Bias:** Many open models trained on predominantly English corpora fail to capture the nuances of Chinese logic, idioms, and professional knowledge. Prior benchmarks showed significant drops in performance when these models were evaluated on Chinese-specific tasks like `C-Eval` or `AlignBench`.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work
The GLM-4 family positions itself as a direct competitor to the closed-source elite while maintaining the openness of the LLaMA or Mistral families.

*   **Performance Parity:** The paper explicitly positions `GLM-4 (0520)` against **GPT-4 Turbo** and **Claude 3 Opus**. On the **MMLU** benchmark (measuring general knowledge), GLM-4 achieves **83.3**, closely rivaling GPT-4 Turbo's **86.7** and surpassing many intermediate open models. Crucially, on **AlignBench** (a Chinese alignment benchmark), GLM-4 scores **8.00**, outperforming GPT-4 Turbo (**7.90**) and Claude 3 Opus (**7.53**), establishing a new state-of-the-art for Chinese language understanding.
*   **The "All Tools" Differentiator:** Unlike standard open models that require external frameworks to handle tool use, `GLM-4 All Tools` is natively aligned to understand user intent and autonomously decide *when* and *which* tool to use. The paper claims this implementation "matches and even surpasses GPT-4 All Tools" in practical applications like web browsing and math solving via Python.
*   **Scalability and Accessibility:** By releasing `GLM-4-9B`, the authors provide a model that fits on consumer hardware yet supports a **128K** context window (with an experimental **1M** version). This contrasts sharply with the resource requirements of 100B+ parameter models, effectively democratizing long-context and agent capabilities.

In summary, this work does not merely present another incremental update to the GLM family; it represents a convergence of scale, efficient architecture, and advanced alignment to produce an open model family that competes directly with the most capable proprietary systems, specifically closing the gap in bilingual proficiency and autonomous agent behavior.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The GLM-4 system is a family of large language models built on a refined Transformer architecture, trained on a massive 10-trillion-token corpus, and aligned through a multi-stage process to function not just as chatbots but as autonomous agents capable of planning and using external tools. It solves the problem of bridging the performance gap between open-source and proprietary models by combining high-quality bilingual data, efficient architectural modifications for long-context handling, and a specialized "All Tools" alignment that enables the model to recursively break down tasks and execute code or web searches to find answers.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The GLM-4 ecosystem operates as a sequential pipeline starting with **Pre-Training Data Processing**, where raw multilingual text is deduplicated, filtered, and tokenized into a unified 150,000-token vocabulary. This data feeds into the **Base Model Architecture**, a Transformer variant utilizing Group Query Attention (GQA) and Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE) to efficiently process contexts up to 128K (or experimentally 1M) tokens. The output of pre-training is then passed to the **Alignment Pipeline**, which applies Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) with authentic human dialogues followed by Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) to refine safety and instruction following. Finally, for the **GLM-4 All Tools** variant, an additional agent-specific alignment layer enables the model to output structured function calls, interact with a **Tool Execution Environment** (Python interpreter, Web Browser, Image Generator), and ingest the results back into the context window for iterative problem solving.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Data Foundation:** We first examine the pre-training corpus construction and tokenization strategy, as the quality and scale of the 10 trillion tokens directly determine the model's base knowledge and bilingual fluency.
*   **Architectural Innovations:** We then dissect the specific modifications to the Transformer backbone (such as GQA and bias removal) that enable faster training and efficient handling of extremely long contexts (128K+).
*   **Alignment Methodology:** Next, we detail the transition from base pre-training to human alignment, explaining why authentic human prompts and specific RLHF techniques are critical for reducing hallucinations and improving safety.
*   **Agent and Tool Integration:** Finally, we explain the "All Tools" mechanism, describing how the model learns to plan, invoke external APIs, and process feedback loops to solve complex, multi-step tasks autonomously.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This paper presents a comprehensive engineering report on the iterative development of the ChatGLM family, culminating in GLM-4, with a core idea that combining massive-scale bilingual pre-training with precise, multi-stage alignment and native tool-use capabilities can yield open models that rival closed-source state-of-the-art systems.

**Pre-Training Data Pipeline and Tokenization**
The foundation of GLM-4 is a pre-training corpus consisting of approximately **10 trillion tokens**, derived from a diverse mixture of webpages, Wikipedia, books, code, and research papers, with a primary focus on Chinese and English supplemented by 24 other languages.
*   The data processing pipeline executes three distinct stages: **deduplication** (using both exact and fuzzy matching to remove redundant documents and increase diversity), **filtering** (removing noisy content like offensive language, placeholder text, and low-quality source code), and **tokenization**.
*   To optimize training speed and vocabulary efficiency, the authors employ a **byte-level Byte Pair Encoding (BPE)** algorithm that separately learns Chinese and multilingual tokens before merging them with the `cl100k_base` tokenizer (from `tiktoken`) into a unified vocabulary of size **150,000**.
*   In the final dataset construction, different data sources are **re-weighted** to increase the importance of high-quality educational sources such as books and Wikipedia, reflecting the finding that data quality and diversity are more crucial than sheer volume for effective LLMs.
*   Despite these empirical successes, the authors explicitly note that they have yet to identify a fundamental theoretical principle that could fully guide the processes of data collection and cleaning, leaving this as an open research direction.

**Model Architecture and Efficiency Mechanisms**
The GLM-4 architecture builds upon the standard Transformer but introduces several specific design choices to enhance training speed, inference efficiency, and context length capacity.
*   **Bias Removal:** To increase training speed and slightly improve length extrapolation, the model removes all bias terms from the neural network layers except for the biases in the Query, Key, and Value (QKV) matrices of the attention layers.
*   **Normalization and Activation:** The architecture replaces the standard LayerNorm with **RMSNorm** (Root Mean Square Layer Normalization) and substitutes the ReLU activation function with **SwiGLU** (Swish Gated Linear Unit), both of which are chosen for their ability to stabilize training and improve overall model performance.
*   **Positional Encoding:** The model utilizes **Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE)**, extended to a **two-dimensional form** to accommodate the specific 2D positional encoding requirements inherent in the GLM architecture, allowing it to better understand token relationships in long sequences.
*   **Group Query Attention (GQA):** A critical efficiency upgrade is the replacement of Multi-Head Attention (MHA) with **Group Query Attention (GQA)**, which significantly reduces the size of the Key-Value (KV) cache during inference by sharing key and value heads across multiple query heads.
*   To maintain the total parameter count despite GQA using fewer parameters for attention, the authors increase the dimension of the Feed-Forward Network (FFN) by setting the intermediate hidden size ($d_{ffn}$) to **$10/3$** of the model's main hidden size.
*   **Context Extension:** The context window has been progressively expanded from **2K** in early generations to **32K** in ChatGLM2/3, and finally to **128K** (with an experimental **1M** version) in GLM-4.
*   This extension is achieved not merely by changing position encodings but through a combination of **positional interpolation**, **continual training** on long texts, and a specialized **long context alignment** process (detailed in the "LongAlign" technique) that ensures the model can effectively retrieve and reason over information spanning hundreds of thousands of tokens.

**Alignment and Post-Training Strategy**
While pre-training provides the foundational knowledge, the alignment phase refines the model to follow instructions, engage in multi-turn dialogue, and adhere to safety guidelines through a multi-stage process involving Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF).
*   **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT):** The authors emphasize that using **authentic human prompts and interactions** is vital for high-quality alignment, rejecting template-based or purely model-generated responses which can degrade performance.
*   For early models (ChatGLM-6B/130B), prompt-response pairs were annotated by developers, but for GLM-4, the data is a combination of **in-house annotation** and **proprietary third-party data**, subjected to strict quality control.
*   Annotators score model responses across multiple dimensions including **safety, factuality, relevance, helpfulness, and human preferences**, creating a robust dataset for teaching the model desired behaviors.
*   **Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF):** Following SFT, RLHF is applied to mitigate specific issues such as response rejection, safety violations, the mixing of bilingual tokens in inappropriate contexts, and inconsistencies in multi-turn coherence.
*   The team employs techniques like **Self-Contrast**, a feedback-free alignment strategy where the target LLM generates its own massive set of negative samples to train against, reducing the reliance on expensive human preference data.
*   Specific practices for applying **PPO** (Proximal Policy Optimization) and **DPO** (Direct Preference Optimization) are utilized to align the model with human feedback, as detailed in their "ChatGLM-RLHF" technical report.

**GLM-4 All Tools: Autonomous Agent Capabilities**
The "All Tools" variant represents a specialized alignment where the model is trained to function as an intelligent agent capable of autonomous planning and tool execution.
*   **Intent Understanding and Planning:** When presented with a complex user request, the model is trained to first **analyze the task** and **plan a step-by-step problem-solving process** rather than attempting to answer immediately from internal knowledge.
*   **Tool Selection and Invocation:** If the model determines it cannot complete the task independently, it autonomously decides **when** and **which** tool(s) to call from a available set including a **web browser**, **Python interpreter**, **text-to-image model** (e.g., CogView3), and **user-defined functions**.
*   **Execution Loop:** The system operates in a recursive loop: the model generates a tool call, the external environment executes the tool (e.g., running Python code or fetching a webpage), and the **intermediate feedback and results** are fed back into the model's context window.
*   **Iterative Refinement:** The model uses this feedback to refine its understanding and generate subsequent steps, allowing it to handle multi-round interactions such as browsing multiple websites to gather data before performing a calculation.
*   **Customizable Agents (GLMs):** Built on this core capability, the platform allows users to create customized agents ("GLMs") that can integrate specific **APIs**, **external knowledge bases**, and **user-defined functions** to address niche domain needs.
*   This architecture enables the model to surpass static knowledge limitations, effectively solving dynamic problems like calculating the average annual growth rate of the global population by first searching for current and historical data and then executing the mathematical formula in Python.

**Mathematical and Algorithmic Specifics**
The technical report highlights several algorithmic choices that underpin these capabilities, particularly regarding how the model handles sequence length and optimization.
*   The extension of context length relies on modifying the positional encoding mechanism, often represented mathematically in RoPE as rotating the query and key vectors by an angle dependent on their position $m$ and dimension $i$:
    $$
    \begin{pmatrix} q_m^{(i)} \\ q_m^{(i+1)} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos(m\theta_i) & -\sin(m\theta_i) \\ \sin(m\theta_i) & \cos(m\theta_i) \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} q_0^{(i)} \\ q_0^{(i+1)} \end{pmatrix}
    $$
    where $\theta_i$ is a frequency parameter. For GLM-4, this is adapted to support the 128K and 1M token windows through interpolation and continual training.
*   The **GQA** mechanism reduces the memory complexity of the KV cache from $O(N \cdot H)$ in standard MHA to $O(N \cdot G)$, where $N$ is the sequence length, $H$ is the number of heads, and $G$ is the number of groups ($G < H$), enabling feasible inference on long sequences with limited hardware memory.
*   The **Self-Contrast** alignment strategy utilizes the model's own probability distribution to generate negative samples $y^-$ that are plausible but incorrect, optimizing the policy $\pi_\theta$ to maximize the margin between the preferred response $y^+$ and the self-generated negative response:
    $$
    \mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\log \sigma \left( \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y^+|x)}{\pi_{ref}(y^+|x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y^-|x)}{\pi_{ref}(y^-|x)} \right)
    $$
    where $\pi_{ref}$ is the reference model and $\beta$ is a temperature parameter, allowing for alignment without explicit human ranking data for every sample.

**Design Choices and Rationale**
The authors' design choices reflect a prioritization of efficiency, bilingual balance, and practical utility over raw parameter scaling alone.
*   The decision to use **GQA** instead of MHA was driven by the need to support **128K context lengths** on practical hardware; without reducing the KV cache size, inference memory requirements would be prohibitive for such long sequences.
*   The choice to train on a **10 trillion token** corpus with a heavy emphasis on **Chinese and English** addresses the specific gap in high-quality bilingual models, ensuring that the model does not sacrifice Chinese cultural nuance for English performance.
*   The **multi-stage post-training** (SFT followed by RLHF) is chosen because SFT alone, while effective for basic instruction following, often fails to resolve subtle issues like safety refusals or multi-turn coherence, which RLHF specifically targets through preference optimization.
*   The **All Tools** approach is designed to be **native** rather than plugin-based; by integrating tool use directly into the model's alignment phase, GLM-4 learns to treat tool calls as a natural part of the reasoning process, reducing the hallucination rates often seen in models where tool use is an afterthought.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The GLM-4 report distinguishes itself not merely through incremental scaling, but by identifying and solving specific bottlenecks that have historically separated open-source models from proprietary leaders. The following insights represent the core conceptual and practical innovations that enable GLM-4 to rival GPT-4 and Claude 3 Opus while maintaining openness and bilingual proficiency.

### 4.1 The "Native Agent" Paradigm: From Tool-Use as a Feature to Tool-Use as a Behavior
Prior approaches to equipping LLMs with tool capabilities often treated function calling as an add-on feature, typically implemented via few-shot prompting or external orchestration frameworks (e.g., LangChain) that sit *outside* the model's weights. In these setups, the base model generates text, and a separate parser attempts to extract tool calls, leading to brittle pipelines where the model might hallucinate function arguments or fail to interpret execution errors.

**GLM-4 All Tools** introduces a fundamental shift by embedding agent capabilities directly into the model's alignment phase.
*   **The Innovation:** Instead of relying on prompt engineering to teach tool use, the authors perform a specialized alignment where the model learns to **autonomously plan, execute, and iterate** based on tool feedback. As illustrated in **Figure 2**, the model does not just output a single function call; it engages in a recursive loop: *Analyze Task $\rightarrow$ Plan Steps $\rightarrow$ Call Tool (e.g., Web Browser) $\rightarrow$ Ingest Feedback $\rightarrow$ Refine Plan $\rightarrow$ Call Tool (e.g., Python)*.
*   **Why It Matters:** This native integration transforms the model from a passive text generator into an active problem solver. The significance is quantified in **Table 9**, where GLM-4 All Tools achieves **91.59** on GSM8K using a Python interpreter, closely matching GPT-4's **92.72**, and surpasses it in information seeking (**78.08** vs. **67.12**). This demonstrates that when tool use is treated as a core behavioral trait learned during RLHF rather than a syntactic output format, the model gains robustness in handling multi-step, real-world tasks that require dynamic information retrieval and computation.

### 4.2 Breaking the "English-First" Alignment Ceiling
A persistent limitation in the LLM landscape is the "English-first" bias, where models trained on predominantly English corpora achieve state-of-the-art reasoning in English but degrade significantly when switched to other languages, particularly Chinese. Prior open models often treated Chinese alignment as a translation task or a secondary fine-tuning step, resulting in a loss of cultural nuance and logical coherence.

**GLM-4** challenges this by demonstrating that **bilingual parity** is achievable without sacrificing performance in either language.
*   **The Innovation:** The team employs a balanced pre-training corpus (10 trillion tokens) heavily weighted toward high-quality Chinese and English sources, followed by an alignment process specifically calibrated for Chinese logic and cultural context. Crucially, they utilize **AlignBench**, a rigorous benchmark for Chinese alignment, to drive improvements rather than relying solely on translated English benchmarks.
*   **Why It Matters:** The results in **Table 4** provide definitive evidence of this breakthrough. GLM-4 (0520) achieves an overall score of **8.00** on AlignBench, **outperforming** GPT-4 Turbo (2024-04-09) at **8.00** (effectively matching the latest version) and significantly surpassing GPT-4 (0613) at **7.46** and Claude 3 Opus at **7.53**. More strikingly, in specific dimensions like **Logic Reasoning** (7.95 vs. 7.67 for GPT-4 Turbo) and **Language Understanding**, GLM-4 sets a new state-of-the-art. This proves that open models can exceed proprietary systems in non-English domains if the alignment data and evaluation metrics are natively designed for that language, rather than adapted from English.

### 4.3 Efficient Scaling via Architectural Rebalancing (GQA + Expanded FFN)
As context windows expand to **128K** and experimentally to **1M**, the memory cost of the Key-Value (KV) cache in standard Multi-Head Attention (MHA) becomes prohibitive, often forcing a trade-off between context length and model size. Previous solutions often simply reduced the number of attention heads, which could degrade representation quality.

**GLM-4** introduces a nuanced architectural rebalancing that maintains model capacity while drastically improving inference efficiency.
*   **The Innovation:** The authors replace MHA with **Group Query Attention (GQA)** to compress the KV cache. However, recognizing that GQA reduces the total parameter count in the attention layers, they explicitly **compensate** by expanding the Feed-Forward Network (FFN) dimension. Specifically, they set the intermediate hidden size ($d_{ffn}$) to **$10/3$** of the main hidden size. Additionally, they remove bias terms from all layers except the QKV matrices to accelerate training and improve length extrapolation.
*   **Why It Matters:** This design choice allows GLM-4 to support massive context windows (up to **1M tokens** in the experimental `GLM-4-9B-Chat-1M`) without the exponential memory blowup typical of MHA. The effectiveness is visible in **Table 5**, where GLM-4 (0520) scores **87.3** on English long-context tasks and **84.0** on Chinese tasks in LongBench-Chat, matching or exceeding GPT-4 Turbo and Claude 3 Opus. This validates the hypothesis that parameter efficiency can be preserved in long-context models by strategically shifting capacity from attention mechanisms to feed-forward networks.

### 4.4 Data-Centric Emergence: The Threshold of Reasoning
While many works attribute reasoning capabilities purely to parameter scale, the GLM team provides empirical evidence supporting a **loss-centric view of emergence**, suggesting that data quality and training loss thresholds are the true drivers of complex abilities.

**The Innovation:** Building on their prior work on "Emergent Abilities of LLMs" [12], the GLM-4 training strategy focuses intensely on **data re-weighting** (prioritizing books and Wikipedia) and **deduplication** to drive down pre-training loss efficiently. They observe that capabilities like those measured in **MMLU** and **GSM8K** do not emerge linearly but appear sharply once the pre-training loss falls below a specific threshold.
*   **Why It Matters:** This insight explains how the smaller **GLM-4-9B** model can achieve **74.7** on MMLU and **84.0** on GSM8K (**Table 1**), outperforming much larger predecessors and rivaling models with significantly more parameters. It shifts the field's focus from "bigger is better" to "cleaner and denser is better," offering a roadmap for building capable models with reduced computational budgets. The dramatic jump from ChatGLM3-6B (MMLU: 61.4) to GLM-4-9B (MMLU: 74.7) underscores that optimized data curation can yield disproportionate gains in general intelligence.

### 4.5 Democratizing Long-Context and Agent Capabilities
Perhaps the most significant societal innovation is the decision to open-source these advanced capabilities. Historically, features like **128K context windows** and **autonomous agent planning** have been exclusive to closed APIs (e.g., GPT-4 Turbo, Claude 3).

**The Innovation:** By releasing **GLM-4-9B** with native support for **128K** context (and an experimental **1M** version) and full **All Tools** functionality, the team breaks the monopoly of proprietary providers on high-end LLM features.
*   **Why It Matters:** This allows researchers and developers to deploy sophisticated agents locally or on private clouds, ensuring data privacy and customization that closed APIs cannot offer. The fact that GLM-4-9B outperforms **Llama-3-8B** and supports the full agent pipeline (Table 7 shows GLM-4-9B-Chat scoring **81.00** on function calling vs. Llama-3-8B's **58.88**) means that the barrier to entry for building complex AI applications has been significantly lowered. This move accelerates the ecosystem's ability to innovate on top of agent architectures without being gated by API costs or usage limits.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

The evaluation of the GLM-4 family is designed to be exhaustive, moving beyond standard academic benchmarks to stress-test the model's capabilities in bilingual alignment, long-context reasoning, autonomous agent behavior, and real-world coding scenarios. The authors employ a rigorous methodology comparing GLM-4 variants against the strongest available proprietary baselines, including **GPT-4** (various versions), **Claude 3 Opus**, **Gemini 1.5 Pro**, and open-source competitors like **Llama-3-8B**.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

The experimental setup prioritizes fairness and reproducibility. Unless otherwise noted, GLM-4 models are evaluated using **BFloat16** precision. The baselines are sourced either from official technical reports or tested directly via public APIs to ensure consistent prompting strategies.

The evaluation spans seven distinct dimensions:
1.  **Academic Benchmarks:** Standardized tests for knowledge, math, reasoning, and coding (MMLU, GSM8K, MATH, BBH, GPQA, HumanEval).
2.  **Instruction Following:** Measured using **IFEval**, which verifies adherence to explicit constraints (e.g., "end with P.S.") via string matching.
3.  **Chinese Alignment:** Assessed via **AlignBench**, a specialized benchmark using an LLM-as-Judge approach calibrated on human preferences across eight categories.
4.  **Long Context:** Evaluated on **LongBench-Chat**, covering contexts from 10k to 100k tokens in both English and Chinese.
5.  **Real-World Coding:** Tested on **NaturalCodeBench (NCB)**, which uses complex, real-user prompts rather than introductory algorithmic problems.
6.  **Function Calling & Agents:** Measured on the **Berkeley Function Call Leaderboard** and **AgentBench**, testing the ability to invoke APIs and navigate environments.
7.  **Safety:** Audited using **SafetyBench** across seven dimensions of risk.

### 5.2 General Capabilities: Academic Benchmarks

The primary claim of GLM-4 is its parity with GPT-4 on general intelligence tasks. The results in **Table 2** substantiate this, showing GLM-4 (0520) closing the gap significantly.

*   **Knowledge & Reasoning (MMLU, BBH, GPQA):** On **MMLU** (Massive Multitask Language Understanding), GLM-4 (0520) scores **83.3**, achieving **96.3%** of the original GPT-4's score (86.4) and trailing the latest GPT-4 Turbo (2024-04-09) by only 3.4 points. In **BBH** (Big-Bench Hard), GLM-4 scores **84.7**, surpassing GPT-4 (83.1) and approaching GPT-4 Turbo (88.2). For the challenging graduate-level **GPQA** benchmark, GLM-4 achieves **39.9**, narrowing the gap with Claude 3 Opus (50.4) and GPT-4 Turbo (49.3).
*   **Mathematics (GSM8K, MATH):** The model demonstrates strong mathematical reasoning. On **GSM8K**, GLM-4 (0520) reaches **93.3**, outperforming the original GPT-4 (92.0) and sitting just behind GPT-4 Turbo (95.6). On the harder **MATH** dataset, it scores **61.3**, a substantial improvement over earlier generations but still lagging behind GPT-4 Turbo's **73.4**. The authors explicitly note in Section 3.3 that the remaining gap in mathematics is a focus for future iterations using self-critique techniques.
*   **Coding (HumanEval):** For code generation, GLM-4 (0520) achieves **78.5** on HumanEval. While this is a massive leap from the 0.0 score of the original ChatGLM-6B, it remains below GPT-4 Turbo (88.2) and Claude 3 Opus (84.9).

**The Open-Source Leap:** The smaller **GLM-4-9B-Chat** model also delivers remarkable efficiency. As shown in **Table 1**, it scores **74.7** on MMLU and **84.0** on GSM8K. This outperforms the much larger **Llama-3-8B** (which typically scores ~68 on MMLU) and even surpasses the previous generation's largest models, validating the efficacy of the 10-trillion-token training regime.

A critical differentiator for GLM-4 is its performance in Chinese contexts, where it claims to surpass proprietary models.

**Instruction Following (IFEval):**
Using the **IFEval** benchmark (**Table 3**), the authors measure strict and loose adherence to instructions.

*   In **Chinese**, the model maintains robustness with a loose instruction-level score of **84.2**, nearly identical to GPT-4 Turbo (84.2). This confirms that the bilingual training did not dilute instruction-following capabilities in either language.

**Chinese Alignment (AlignBench):**
The most striking results appear in **Table 4**, which evaluates alignment on Chinese tasks.
*   **Overall Performance:** GLM-4 (0520) achieves an overall score of **8.00**. This matches the latest GPT-4 Turbo (2024-04-09) at **8.00** and decisively outperforms the original GPT-4 (7.46) and Claude 3 Opus (7.53).
*   **Domain Specifics:** The model excels in **Logic Reasoning** (7.95 vs. 7.67 for GPT-4 Turbo) and **Language Understanding** (8.00 vs. 7.60).
*   **The Math Gap:** The only dimension where GLM-4 (7.89) trails GPT-4 Turbo (8.32) is Mathematics, consistent with the findings in the academic benchmarks.
*   **Significance:** These results support the claim that GLM-4 is currently the state-of-the-art for Chinese language alignment among open and closed models, effectively breaking the "English-first" ceiling.

### 5.4 Long-Context Capabilities

To validate the architectural changes (GQA, RoPE extension), the authors evaluate performance on **LongBench-Chat** (**Table 5**), which tests comprehension over 10k–100k token contexts.

*   **English Context:** GLM-4 (0520) scores **87.3**, virtually tying with GPT-4 Turbo (87.2) and Claude 3 Opus (87.7).
*   **Chinese Context:** Here, GLM-4 demonstrates a clear advantage, scoring **84.0**. This surpasses GPT-4 Turbo (82.1) and Claude 3 Opus (82.7).
*   **Scale:** The paper notes the existence of an experimental **GLM-4-9B-Chat-1M** model supporting 1 million tokens, though specific benchmark numbers for the 1M variant are not provided in the tables. The success at 128K suggests the "LongAlign" recipe effectively preserves attention mechanisms over extreme distances.

### 5.5 Real-World Coding and Agent Abilities

Moving beyond synthetic benchmarks, the paper evaluates GLM-4 on tasks that mirror actual user behavior.

**Real-World Coding (NaturalCodeBench):**
**Table 6** presents results on NCB, a dataset of complex, real-user coding prompts.
*   GLM-4 (0520) achieves an overall score of **47.1**. While this trails GPT-4 Turbo (53.8), it is highly competitive with Claude 3 Opus (48.3) and significantly ahead of Gemini 1.5 Pro (42.3).
*   Notably, the model shows strong bilingual coding ability, scoring **45.4** on Chinese Python prompts, outperforming GPT-4 Turbo (53.1) is false; actually, it trails GPT-4 Turbo (53.1) but beats Claude 3 Opus (45.0). *Correction based on Table 6:* GLM-4 scores **45.4** on Python (zh), while GPT-4 Turbo (2024-04-09) scores **53.1**. However, GLM-4 outperforms Claude 3 Opus (45.0) in this specific category. The gap to GPT-4 suggests that while general coding is strong, handling highly complex, ambiguous real-world requests remains an area for improvement.

**Function Calling and Agents:**
The "All Tools" capability is quantified in **Table 7** (Function Calling) and **Table 8** (AgentBench).
*   **Function Calling:** On the Berkeley Leaderboard, GLM-4 (0520) achieves an overall accuracy of **81.76**, aligning closely with GPT-4 Turbo (81.24). Interestingly, the smaller **GLM-4-9B-Chat** scores **81.00**, significantly outperforming **Llama-3-8B-Instruct** (58.88). The authors note a non-obvious trend: overall accuracy does not strictly scale with model size (GLM-4-9B beats GLM-4-Air in some metrics), though execution summary accuracy does improve with scale.
*   **Agent Tasks:** In **AgentBench** (**Table 8**), GLM-4 (0520) achieves an overall score of **3.79**, slightly edging out GPT-4 Turbo (3.77) and Claude 3 Opus (3.62).
    *   **Strengths:** The model excels in **House Holding** (82.0) and **Web Shopping** (68.3), surpassing GPT-4 Turbo in both.
    *   **Weaknesses:** It lags in **Operating System** tasks (36.8 vs. 41.0 for GPT-4 Turbo) and **Lateral Thinking Puzzles** (15.3 vs. 19.4), indicating that highly interactive or abstract reasoning agents still have room to grow.

### 5.6 The "All Tools" Autonomous Performance

The paper provides a specific evaluation of the **GLM-4 All Tools** system in **Table 9**, comparing it directly against GPT-4 with web browsing enabled.
*   **Math via Python:** On **GSM8K**, GLM-4 All Tools achieves **91.59** accuracy using the Python interpreter, nearly matching GPT-4's **92.72**. On the **MATH** dataset, it scores **63.60** vs. GPT-4's **65.00**.
*   **Information Seeking:** In a surprising result, GLM-4 All Tools **surpasses** GPT-4 in web-based information seeking, scoring **78.08** compared to GPT-4's **67.12**.
*   **Interpretation:** This suggests that the native alignment for tool use (planning, executing, feedback loops) allows GLM-4 to navigate the web more effectively than GPT-4 in certain scenarios, possibly due to better handling of multi-round browsing or summarization of retrieved content.

### 5.7 Safety and Robustness

Safety is evaluated on **SafetyBench** (**Table 10**).
*   **Overall Safety:** GLM-4 (0520) scores **87.2**, which is comparable to Claude 3 Opus (87.5) and slightly behind the GPT-4 family (~88-89).
*   **Specific Risks:** The model performs well on **Mental Health** (93.3) and **Ethics** (92.3). However, it shows a weakness in **Physical Health** (92.3 vs. 96.7 for GPT-4 0613) and **Unfairness & Bias** (66.0, matching Claude 3 Opus but trailing GPT-4's 73.3-75.0).
*   **Mitigation:** The authors attribute the Physical Health gap to a need for more robust common sense knowledge about the physical world and note ongoing red-teaming efforts to address these specific failure modes.

### 5.8 Critical Assessment of Results

The experimental analysis convincingly supports the paper's central claims:
1.  **Parity with Proprietary Models:** The data in Tables 2, 3, and 5 confirms that GLM-4 is no longer a "distant follower" but a direct competitor to GPT-4 Turbo and Claude 3 Opus across most general metrics.
2.  **Chinese Superiority:** The AlignBench results (Table 4) provide strong evidence that GLM-4 has achieved state-of-the-art performance in Chinese alignment, fulfilling the goal of bilingual parity.
3.  **Effective Agent Integration:** The results in Table 9 are particularly compelling. Surpassing GPT-4 in information seeking suggests that the "All Tools" architecture is not just a marketing feature but a functional advantage in dynamic tasks.

**Limitations and Mixed Results:**
*   **Mathematical Reasoning:** Despite strong GSM8K scores, the gap on **MATH** (61.3 vs 73.4) and the **Math** dimension of AlignBench indicates that complex, competition-level mathematical reasoning remains a challenge.
*   **Real-World Coding:** While HumanEval scores are high, the drop in performance on **NaturalCodeBench** (47.1 vs 53.8 for GPT-4 Turbo) highlights the difficulty of translating synthetic coding skills to messy, real-world user requests.
*   **Agent Consistency:** The AgentBench results show a mixed bag; while excellent at shopping and house-holding tasks, the model struggles with OS-level interactions, suggesting that agent reliability is still context-dependent.

In conclusion, the experiments demonstrate that GLM-4 represents a significant maturation of the ChatGLM family. By combining massive scale (10T tokens), architectural efficiency (GQA, 128K context), and native agent alignment, it delivers performance that validates the authors' claim: open-source models can now rival the best closed systems, particularly when optimized for specific linguistic and functional niches.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the GLM-4 family represents a significant leap in open-source capabilities, achieving parity with proprietary models in many domains, the paper explicitly acknowledges several critical limitations, trade-offs, and unresolved challenges. These constraints highlight the boundaries of the current approach and point toward necessary future research directions.

### 6.1 The Persistent Mathematics Gap
Despite strong performance on grade-school math benchmarks like GSM8K (where GLM-4 scores **93.3**, surpassing GPT-4's **92.0**), the model exhibits a notable deficit in advanced, competition-level mathematics.
*   **Evidence:** On the **MATH** benchmark, which features high-school and competition-level problems, GLM-4 (0520) achieves a score of **61.3**. This lags significantly behind **GPT-4 Turbo (2024-04-09)**, which scores **73.4**, and **Claude 3 Opus** at **60.1** (though GLM-4 is close to Opus, the gap to the latest GPT-4 Turbo is substantial).
*   **Alignment Correlation:** This weakness is mirrored in the **AlignBench** results (**Table 4**), where GLM-4 scores **7.89** in the Mathematics dimension, falling short of GPT-4 Turbo's **8.32**.
*   **Root Cause & Mitigation:** The authors attribute this gap to the inherent difficulty of complex reasoning tasks that require multi-step logical deduction beyond standard pattern matching. They note that while techniques like **self-critique** (introduced in *ChatGLM-Math* [48]) are being employed to enhance these capabilities, the current iteration has not yet fully closed the gap with the state-of-the-art. This suggests that simply scaling data or parameters is insufficient for elite mathematical reasoning without specialized architectural or training interventions.

### 6.2 Real-World Coding vs. Synthetic Benchmarks
A critical trade-off exists between performance on clean, synthetic coding benchmarks and messy, real-world user requests.
*   **The Discrepancy:** GLM-4 performs admirably on **HumanEval** (**78.5**), a standard benchmark for introductory algorithmic problems. However, its performance drops considerably on **NaturalCodeBench (NCB)**, a dataset derived from actual user prompts involving complex, ambiguous requirements.
*   **Evidence:** On NCB, GLM-4 (0520) achieves an overall score of **47.1**, whereas **GPT-4 Turbo (2024-04-09)** scores **53.8**. The gap is particularly evident in Python coding for English prompts (**51.6** vs. **57.5**).
*   **Implication:** This indicates that while the model has mastered the syntax and logic of standard coding interview questions, it struggles with the nuance, context interpretation, and error handling required for practical software engineering tasks. The authors concede that "there is quite much potential to improve its performance on NCB via better training strategies and data curation," admitting that current training data may not fully reflect the complexity of real-world development workflows.

### 6.3 Agent Capability Inconsistencies
The "All Tools" agent capabilities, while impressive, are not uniformly robust across all environments. The model's effectiveness as an agent is highly context-dependent.
*   **Environment Variance:** In **AgentBench** (**Table 8**), GLM-4 excels in structured, goal-oriented environments like **House Holding** (**82.0**, beating GPT-4 Turbo's **72.0**) and **Web Shopping** (**68.3**). However, it underperforms in environments requiring low-level system interaction or abstract lateral thinking.
*   **Specific Weaknesses:**
    *   **Operating System Tasks:** GLM-4 scores **36.8**, trailing GPT-4 Turbo (**41.0**) and Claude 3 Opus (**55.0**). This suggests difficulties in navigating file systems, executing shell commands, or managing process states reliably.
    *   **Lateral Thinking Puzzles:** The model scores only **15.3**, significantly behind GPT-4 Turbo (**19.4**) and Claude 3 Opus (**20.0**). This reveals a limitation in the model's ability to break out of standard reasoning patterns when faced with non-linear or trick-based problems.
*   **Trade-off:** The native alignment for tool use improves general autonomy but does not guarantee universal competence. The model appears to rely heavily on the clarity of the environment's feedback loop; when the environment is noisy or requires deep common sense (as in OS tasks or puzzles), performance degrades.

### 6.4 Safety Vulnerabilities in Physical Domains
While GLM-4 demonstrates competitive safety overall, it exhibits specific vulnerabilities in domains requiring robust physical common sense.
*   **Evidence:** On **SafetyBench** (**Table 10**), GLM-4 (0520) scores **92.3** in the **Physical Health** dimension. While respectable, this is notably lower than **GPT-4 (0613)**, which scores **96.7**, and **GPT-4 Turbo**, which scores **92.0** (GLM-4 is roughly on par with the *older* Turbo but behind the original GPT-4). More critically, in **Unfairness & Bias**, GLM-4 scores **66.0**, matching Claude 3 Opus but trailing the GPT-4 family (which ranges from **73.3** to **75.0**).
*   **Analysis:** The authors explicitly state that the Physical Health gap "demands robust common sense knowledge about the physical world to avoid potential risks." This implies that the model's training corpus, despite its size, may lack sufficient grounded physical reasoning data or that the alignment process has not adequately penalized physically dangerous suggestions. This remains an open risk for real-world deployment in safety-critical applications.

### 6.5 Data Curation: Empirical Success vs. Theoretical Void
A fundamental limitation of the GLM-4 approach is the lack of a guiding theoretical framework for data selection.
*   **The Admission:** In Section 2, under "Pre-Training Data," the authors make a candid admission: "Despite the empirical lessons and insights gained, we have to date yet to identify a fundamental principle that could guide the processes of data collection, cleaning, and selection."
*   **Implication:** The success of GLM-4 relies heavily on iterative experimentation and heuristic re-weighting of data sources (e.g., boosting books and Wikipedia). This "trial-and-error" methodology limits reproducibility and scalability. Without a theoretical understanding of *why* certain data mixes yield better emergent abilities, future improvements may require increasingly expensive brute-force exploration rather than targeted optimization.

### 6.6 Computational and Scalability Constraints
While the architectural choices (GQA, bias removal) improve efficiency, significant computational barriers remain, particularly for the largest context windows.
*   **Context Length Trade-offs:** The paper mentions an experimental **GLM-4-9B-Chat-1M** model supporting 1 million tokens. However, no benchmark results are provided for this 1M variant in **Table 5** (which only covers up to 100k). This omission suggests that while the architecture *supports* 1M tokens, the quality of reasoning or the computational cost at this scale may not yet be production-ready or comparable to the 128K version.
*   **Training Cost:** The pre-training corpus of **10 trillion tokens** represents a massive computational investment. While the resulting 9B model is efficient to *run*, the barrier to *training* such a model from scratch remains prohibitively high for most academic or independent groups. The "democratization" offered is primarily in inference and fine-tuning, not in the ability to replicate the base model training.

### 6.7 Language Scope Limitations
Finally, despite being described as "multilingual," the model's capabilities are heavily skewed.
*   **Focus:** The abstract and Section 2 clarify that the model is pre-trained "mostly in Chinese and English, along with a small set of corpus from 24 languages."
*   **Constraint:** The alignment and evaluation are "primarily for Chinese and English usage." There is no evidence or benchmark data provided for the model's performance in the other 24 languages. Users expecting high-quality reasoning or alignment in languages like Spanish, Arabic, or Hindi may find the model's performance degrades significantly compared to its bilingual core, a common trade-off in models optimized for specific linguistic markets.

In summary, while GLM-4 closes the gap with proprietary models in general knowledge and Chinese alignment, it trades off performance in advanced mathematics, real-world coding complexity, and specific agent environments. The reliance on empirical data curation without theoretical grounding and the remaining safety gaps in physical reasoning represent the primary frontiers for future iteration.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The release of the GLM-4 family represents a inflection point in the large language model landscape, shifting the narrative from "open models as distant followers" to "open models as direct competitors" in specific, high-value domains. By demonstrating that a carefully curated 10-trillion-token training regime combined with native agent alignment can yield performance rivaling GPT-4 Turbo and Claude 3 Opus, this work fundamentally alters the strategic calculus for researchers and developers. The implications extend beyond mere benchmark scores, reshaping expectations for bilingual capability, autonomous agency, and the accessibility of long-context reasoning.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Open-Source Landscape: The End of the "Good Enough" Compromise
Historically, the open-source community has operated under a compromise: accept lower performance in exchange for accessibility, privacy, and customizability. GLM-4 challenges this dichotomy.
*   **Bilingual Parity as a New Standard:** The most immediate impact is the establishment of a new baseline for Chinese-language AI. With an **AlignBench** score of **8.00**, surpassing GPT-4 (0613) and matching the latest GPT-4 Turbo, GLM-4 proves that open models can lead in non-English domains. This forces proprietary vendors to reconsider their alignment strategies for Chinese markets, where cultural nuance and logical coherence are now measurable advantages for open weights.
*   **Democratization of Agent Capabilities:** Prior to GLM-4, robust "All Tools" functionality—autonomous planning, web browsing, and code execution—was largely gated behind closed APIs. By open-sourcing a model that natively integrates these capabilities (scoring **81.76** on the Berkeley Function Call Leaderboard), the authors lower the barrier to entry for building complex AI agents. Developers can now deploy autonomous agents on private infrastructure without relying on external API calls, enabling use cases in sensitive domains like healthcare, finance, and legal research where data sovereignty is paramount.
*   **Efficiency Over Scale:** The success of the **GLM-4-9B** model (scoring **74.7** on MMLU) validates the hypothesis that data quality and architectural efficiency (GQA, SwiGLU) can outperform raw parameter scaling. This shifts the field's focus toward "dense" training regimes and optimized data curation, suggesting that future breakthroughs may come from smarter data pipelines rather than merely larger clusters.

### 7.2 Enabling Follow-Up Research Directions
The technical choices and empirical findings in this report open several concrete avenues for future investigation:

*   **Theoretical Foundations of Data Curation:** The authors explicitly admit in Section 2 that they lack a "fundamental principle" guiding data collection, relying instead on empirical re-weighting. This admission invites rigorous research into **data influence functions** and **loss landscape analysis** to theoretically explain *why* certain mixes of books, code, and web data trigger emergent reasoning abilities. Future work could aim to replace heuristic re-weighting with algorithmic data selection strategies that predict performance gains before training begins.
*   **Native Agent Alignment Mechanisms:** GLM-4's success with "All Tools" suggests that treating tool use as a behavioral trait learned during RLHF is superior to prompt-engineering approaches. This invites research into **multi-modal RLHF**, where reward models evaluate not just text quality but the *efficacy* of tool execution (e.g., did the Python script run? did the browser find the answer?). Researchers could explore extending this to physical robotics, where the "tools" are actuators and sensors, leveraging the same planning-and-feedback loop.
*   **Extreme Long-Context Reasoning:** With the experimental **1M context** model, the field is pushed toward understanding how attention mechanisms degrade (or persist) over millions of tokens. Follow-up research could focus on **hierarchical retrieval mechanisms** within the transformer or hybrid architectures that combine dense attention with sparse retrieval to maintain coherence at the 1M scale, addressing the current lack of benchmark data for such extreme lengths.
*   **Cross-Lingual Transfer Limits:** While GLM-4 excels in Chinese and English, its performance in the other 24 supported languages remains unquantified. This creates an opportunity for research into **cross-lingual alignment transfer**: can the strong reasoning capabilities learned in Chinese/English be efficiently transferred to low-resource languages via lightweight adapter tuning, or does each language require its own high-quality alignment corpus?

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases
The specific strengths of GLM-4 make it uniquely suited for several high-impact applications:

*   **Autonomous Research Assistants:** The combination of **128K context** and **web browsing** capabilities enables the creation of agents that can read entire technical reports, search for corroborating evidence online, and synthesize findings. Unlike standard chatbots, GLM-4 All Tools can iteratively refine its search queries based on initial results, making it ideal for literature reviews, market analysis, and competitive intelligence.
*   **Bilingual Customer Support and Legal Tech:** Given its state-of-the-art performance on **AlignBench**, GLM-4 is particularly well-suited for customer service and legal consultation in Chinese-speaking regions. Its ability to understand complex logic and cultural nuances allows it to handle escalated tickets or draft legal documents with a level of fidelity that previous open models could not achieve.
*   **Educational Coding Tutors:** With strong **HumanEval** scores (**78.5**) and native Python interpreter access, GLM-4 can serve as an interactive coding tutor that not only explains concepts but also executes student code, identifies runtime errors, and suggests fixes in real-time. The **NaturalCodeBench** results suggest it is particularly effective at handling the ambiguous, multi-step problems often found in real-world development.
*   **Private Enterprise Agents:** Organizations can deploy **GLM-4-9B** locally to create custom agents connected to internal APIs and knowledge bases. Because the model supports **user-defined functions** natively, enterprises can build secure, domain-specific agents for tasks like inventory management, HR onboarding, or IT troubleshooting without exposing sensitive data to third-party APIs.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance
For practitioners considering adopting GLM-4, the following guidance outlines when and how to integrate these models effectively:

*   **When to Prefer GLM-4 Over Alternatives:**
    *   **Choose GLM-4 (or GLM-4-9B)** if your application requires **strong Chinese language understanding**, **long-context analysis (up to 128K)**, or **autonomous tool use** within a private deployment. It is the superior choice over Llama-3-8B for tasks involving function calling (scoring **81.00** vs. **58.88**) or Chinese alignment.
    *   **Choose GPT-4 Turbo or Claude 3 Opus** if your primary workload involves **advanced competition-level mathematics** (MATH benchmark) or **complex, ambiguous real-world coding tasks** where the ~6-7 point gap in NaturalCodeBench is critical. Also, prefer proprietary models if you need robust performance in languages other than Chinese or English.
    *   **Choose GLM-4-9B** specifically for edge or cost-constrained deployments where a **9B parameter** model fitting on consumer GPUs (with INT4 quantization) is required, but you still need 128K context and agent capabilities.

*   **Integration Best Practices:**
    *   **Leverage Native Tool Use:** Do not treat GLM-4 All Tools as a standard text generator. To maximize its potential, integrate it with an execution environment that supports the feedback loop described in **Figure 4**. Ensure your system can capture tool outputs (stdout, web page text) and feed them back into the context window for iterative reasoning.
    *   **Prompting for Long Context:** While the model supports 128K, performance may vary based on information density. For critical retrieval tasks, structure prompts to explicitly direct the model's attention to relevant sections of the long context, leveraging the **LongAlign** training it received.
    *   **Safety Guardrails:** Given the identified gaps in **Physical Health** safety (scoring **92.3** vs. GPT-4's **96.7**), deploy additional moderation layers or rule-based filters for applications involving medical advice or physical safety instructions. Do not rely solely on the model's internal alignment for high-stakes safety decisions.

*   **Access and Deployment:**
    *   The models are available via the **Zhipu AI API** (`bigmodel.cn`) for immediate integration.
    *   Open weights for **GLM-4-9B** and its variants (including the 1M context experimental model) can be accessed through **Hugging Face** (`THUDM`) and **GitHub**. This allows for local fine-tuning using frameworks like LoRA or QLoRA to adapt the model to specific domain vocabularies or tasks.

In conclusion, GLM-4 does not merely add another entry to the list of available LLMs; it redefines the ceiling for what open-source models can achieve in bilingual alignment and autonomous agency. By providing a viable, high-performance alternative to closed systems, it empowers a new wave of innovation where developers can build sophisticated, private, and culturally attuned AI agents without being constrained by API limits or linguistic biases.
