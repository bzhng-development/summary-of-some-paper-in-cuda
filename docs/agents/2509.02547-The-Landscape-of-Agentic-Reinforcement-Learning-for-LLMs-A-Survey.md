## 1. Executive Summary

This survey formalizes Agentic Reinforcement Learning (Agentic RL) as a critical paradigm shift from conventional single-step Preference-Based Reinforcement Fine-Tuning (PBRFT) to temporally extended Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes (POMDPs), enabling LLMs to function as autonomous agents with capabilities like planning, tool use, and memory (Section 3). By synthesizing over 500 recent works, the authors construct a comprehensive taxonomy across capability and application domains (e.g., search, code, math in Section 4) and consolidate open-source environments and frameworks to address the current fragmentation in terminology and evaluation protocols. This unified treatment matters because it provides the theoretical foundations and practical compendium necessary to accelerate the development of scalable, general-purpose AI agents embedded in dynamic worlds.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### 2.1 The Core Problem: A Fragmented Research Landscape

This survey addresses a fundamental disconnect in how the research community conceptualizes and develops LLM-based agents. Despite explosive growth in both **LLM agent capabilities** and **RL-based optimization methods**, these two threads have evolved largely in isolation, creating three critical gaps:

**Gap 1: Terminological Inconsistency.** Different research groups use conflicting definitions for core concepts. What one paper calls "agentic behavior," another labels "tool-augmented generation." This inconsistency makes it difficult to compare methods across papers or build cumulatively on prior work. As the authors note in Section 1, *"current studies often examine isolated capabilities, domains, or custom environments, with inconsistent terminology and evaluation protocols, making systematic comparison and cross-domain generalization difficult."*

**Gap 2: Theoretical Fragmentation.** There is no unified mathematical framework distinguishing conventional LLM RL from truly agentic RL. Early RL approaches for LLMs—such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)—optimize single-turn outputs against static preference datasets. In contrast, agentic systems must operate over **extended time horizons** in **partially observable, dynamic environments**. Without a formal distinction, researchers cannot precisely characterize what makes an approach "agentic" versus merely "RL-enhanced."

**Gap 3: Evaluation Silos.** Methods are benchmarked on task-specific environments (e.g., code generation on HumanEval, web navigation on WebArena) without cross-domain analysis. A technique that excels at mathematical reasoning may never be tested on embodied control tasks, obscuring whether improvements reflect genuine agentic capability or domain-specific overfitting.

### 2.2 Why This Problem Matters

The stakes extend beyond academic organization. The transition from static text generators to autonomous agents represents a **qualitative shift in AI system behavior** with profound implications:

**Theoretical Significance.** Conventional LLM RL operates on what the paper formalizes as a *degenerate Markov Decision Process* with horizon $T = 1$ (Section 2.1). The agent receives a prompt, generates one response, and the episode terminates. Agentic RL, by contrast, requires modeling **Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes (POMDPs)** with $T > 1$, where:
- States evolve dynamically based on agent actions
- Observations are incomplete representations of the true environment state
- Credit must be assigned across multi-step trajectories

This shift demands fundamentally different algorithms. Policy gradient methods designed for single-turn alignment (e.g., standard PPO for RLHF) may fail when rewards are sparse and delayed across dozens of interaction steps. Understanding these distinctions is essential for developing scalable training methods.

**Real-World Impact.** Agentic systems are already being deployed in high-stakes domains:
- **Software Engineering:** Agents that debug, refactor, and maintain codebases (Section 4.2)
- **Scientific Research:** Systems that search literature, synthesize findings, and draft reports (Section 4.1)
- **Embodied Control:** Robots that perceive environments and execute multi-step manipulation tasks (Section 4.6)

In these settings, failures compound over time. A single misaligned tool invocation in a 50-step workflow can cascade into catastrophic outcomes. Without principled understanding of how RL shapes long-horizon behavior, deployed agents risk learning **reward-hacking strategies**—exploiting loopholes in the reward function rather than developing robust capabilities. Section 6.1 discusses how RL can inadvertently amplify security vulnerabilities, hallucination, and sycophantic behavior if not carefully designed.

**Economic Implications.** Training agentic systems is orders of magnitude more expensive than single-turn fine-tuning. Rollout generation, environment interaction, and multi-step credit assignment demand substantial compute. Section 6.2 notes that *"scaling reinforcement learning fine-tuning computation directly enhances the reasoning ability of LLM-based agents,"* but efficiency remains a bottleneck. A unified framework helps researchers identify which methodological choices genuinely improve capabilities versus those that merely increase training cost.

### 2.3 Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper identifies two complementary research threads that preceded Agentic RL (Section 1):

#### Thread 1: Synergy Between RL and LLMs

This line focuses on **using RL to improve LLMs**. Key approaches include:

| Approach | Mechanism | Limitation for Agentic Settings |
|----------|-----------|--------------------------------|
| **RLHF** (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022) | Optimize LLM outputs against human preference rankings | Single-turn optimization; no environmental interaction |
| **DPO** (Rafailov et al., 2023) | Direct preference optimization without explicit reward model | Static dataset; cannot adapt to dynamic environments |
| **GRPO** (DeepSeek-AI et al., 2025) | Group-relative policy optimization eliminating value critic | Designed for outcome rewards; struggles with temporal credit assignment |

These methods excel at **alignment**—making LLM outputs safer, more helpful, or more consistent with human values. However, they treat the LLM as a *static conditional generator* rather than a *policy embedded in a decision loop*. As Section 2.1 formalizes, PBRFT (Preference-Based Reinforcement Fine-Tuning) degenerates to:

$$\langle S_{\text{trad}}, A_{\text{trad}}, P_{\text{trad}}, R_{\text{trad}}, T = 1, \gamma = 1 \rangle$$

where the state space reduces to a single prompt and the episode ends immediately after one response. This formulation cannot capture multi-turn planning, tool invocation, or memory maintenance.

#### Thread 2: LLM Agents

This line focuses on **what LLMs can do as autonomous entities**. Representative capabilities include:

- **Tool Use:** Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), API calls, code execution (Li, 2025; Wang et al., 2024k)
- **Planning:** Task decomposition, plan-execute-reflect loops (Masterman et al., 2024; Kumar et al., 2025)
- **Memory:** Long-term storage and retrieval mechanisms (Liu et al., 2025a)
- **Reasoning:** Chain-of-thought, multi-step inference (Tao et al., 2024)

However, most agent systems rely on **prompt engineering or supervised fine-tuning (SFT)** rather than RL. As Section 3.2 explains, early tool-use paradigms like ReAct (Yao et al., 2023b) and Toolformer (Schick et al., 2023) *"train agents to replicate static, pre-defined patterns of tool use, thereby lacking the strategic flexibility to adapt to novel scenarios or recover from unforeseen errors."*

SFT-based agents mimic expert trajectories but cannot discover strategies absent from training data. When environments change or tasks exceed the distribution of demonstration data, performance degrades sharply. RL offers outcome-driven optimization that enables agents to *autonomously discover* when, how, and which tools to deploy—but this potential remains underexplored in the agent literature.

#### The Missing Synthesis

Existing surveys address fragments of this landscape:
- **Luo et al. (2025a)** propose a methodology-centered taxonomy for LLM agents but do not emphasize RL as the optimization mechanism.
- **Plaat et al. (2025)** emphasize reasoning, acting, and interacting as defining features but lack formal MDP/POMDP grounding.
- **Guo & Wang (2025)** survey RL throughout the LLM lifecycle but focus on alignment rather than agentic capabilities.
- **Cao et al. (2025c)** provide taxonomies for LLM-enhanced RL in control environments but do not address language-based agentic behaviors.

None offer a **unified treatment** that (1) formally distinguishes Agentic RL from conventional LLM RL, (2) systematically catalogs RL-optimizable agentic capabilities, and (3) connects theoretical foundations to practical implementations across domains.

### 2.4 This Paper's Positioning

This survey positions itself as the **bridging work** that synthesizes these fragmented threads. The authors make three distinctive contributions:

**Contribution 1: Formal Paradigm Distinction.** Section 2 provides the first systematic formalization contrasting PBRFT (degenerate MDP, $T=1$) with Agentic RL (POMDP, $T>1$). Table 1 explicitly compares state space, action space, transition dynamics, reward functions, and learning objectives. This formalism enables precise communication about what constitutes "agentic" versus "alignment-focused" RL.

**Contribution 2: Capability-Centered Taxonomy.** Section 3 organizes the literature around **six core agentic capabilities** that RL can optimize:
1. Planning (Section 3.1)
2. Tool Use (Section 3.2)
3. Memory (Section 3.3)
4. Self-Improvement (Section 3.4)
5. Reasoning (Section 3.5)
6. Perception (Section 3.6)

This taxonomy treats capabilities not as static modules but as **interdependent policies** that can be jointly optimized. For example, RL for memory shapes retrieval and encoding dynamics; RL for tool use optimizes invocation timing and fidelity.

**Contribution 3: Application-to-Capability Mapping.** Section 4 surveys concrete task domains (search, code, math, GUI, embodied, multi-agent) while Section 5 consolidates environments and frameworks. Table 9 provides a dependency matrix linking applications to capabilities, enabling researchers to identify which capabilities matter most for their target domain.

**Scope Boundaries.** The authors explicitly delimit what falls outside this survey's scope (Section 1):
- ✗ RL for human value alignment (e.g., harmful query refusal)
- ✗ Traditional RL algorithms not LLM-based (e.g., MARL without language)
- ✗ RL for boosting pure LLM performance on static benchmarks

This聚焦 ensures the survey remains coherent rather than attempting to cover all RL-LLM intersections.

By providing this unified treatment, the survey enables researchers to (1) precisely characterize their methods within a common framework, (2) identify underexplored capability-domain combinations, and (3) build on prior work without reinventing terminology or evaluation protocols. The ultimate goal is accelerating development of **scalable, general-purpose agentic intelligence** that can operate robustly in complex, dynamic worlds.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

This survey paper formalizes Agentic Reinforcement Learning as a unified framework that transforms Large Language Models from static text generators into autonomous decision-making agents capable of planning, tool use, memory, and reasoning over extended time horizons. The solution addresses the fundamental problem that conventional RL for LLMs only optimizes single-turn outputs against static preferences, whereas agentic systems must learn policies that operate in dynamic, partially observable environments with rewards that arrive after many interaction steps.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The Agentic RL framework consists of five interconnected components that form a complete training and deployment pipeline. First, the **Environment Simulator** provides the world state and receives agent actions, returning observations and rewards based on task completion or intermediate progress. Second, the **LLM Policy Network** serves as the agent's decision-making core, receiving observations and generating both natural language outputs and structured actions (e.g., tool calls, API invocations). Third, the **Reward Function** evaluates agent behavior at either the trajectory level (final outcome) or step level (intermediate progress), providing learning signals that can be sparse or dense depending on task design. Fourth, the **RL Optimization Algorithm** (e.g., PPO, GRPO, DPO) updates the policy parameters based on collected trajectories and reward signals, balancing exploration with exploitation. Fifth, the **Capability Modules** (planning, tool use, memory, reasoning, perception, self-improvement) represent the specific agentic behaviors that RL optimizes, each with distinct input-output interfaces and training objectives. Information flows sequentially: the environment provides an observation, the policy generates an action, the environment transitions and returns a reward, and the optimizer updates the policy—this loop repeats for multiple steps until task completion.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This survey synthesizes over 500 recent works to establish Agentic Reinforcement Learning as a coherent paradigm, and the core technical idea is that reinforcement learning provides the optimization mechanism for transforming static LLM capabilities into adaptive, robust agentic behaviors embedded in sequential decision processes.

#### The Formal Foundation: MDPs and POMDPs

The paper's central technical contribution is a formal distinction between two types of reinforcement learning formulations for LLMs. Conventional Preference-Based Reinforcement Fine-Tuning (PBRFT), which includes methods like RLHF and DPO, operates on what the authors call a **degenerate Markov Decision Process** with a single time step. Formally, this is expressed as a tuple:

$$\langle S_{\text{trad}}, A_{\text{trad}}, P_{\text{trad}}, R_{\text{trad}}, T = 1, \gamma = 1 \rangle$$

where $S_{\text{trad}}$ represents the state space (reduced to a single prompt), $A_{\text{trad}}$ is the action space (pure text sequences), $P_{\text{trad}}$ denotes transition dynamics (deterministic transition to terminal state), $R_{\text{trad}}$ is the reward function (single scalar score), $T$ is the task horizon (equal to 1), and $\gamma$ is the discount factor (equal to 1, meaning no future rewards are discounted). In plain language, this means the agent receives one prompt, generates one response, receives one reward, and the episode ends immediately—there is no notion of multi-step interaction or environmental feedback.

By contrast, Agentic RL is modeled as a **Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP)** with extended horizons:

$$\langle S_{\text{agent}}, A_{\text{agent}}, P_{\text{agent}}, R_{\text{agent}}, \gamma, O \rangle$$

where $S_{\text{agent}}$ represents the full world state (which evolves over time), $A_{\text{agent}}$ is the action space (including both text and structured actions), $P_{\text{agent}}$ denotes dynamic transition probabilities $P(s_{t+1} | s_t, a_t)$, $R_{\text{agent}}$ provides step-wise or trajectory-level rewards, $\gamma$ is a discount factor between 0 and 1, and $O$ represents the observation function $o_t = O(s_t)$ that maps true states to partial observations. The critical difference is that the agent now acts over multiple time steps ($T > 1$), accumulates intermediate signals (tool results, user messages, environment feedback), and must learn to assign credit across long action sequences. Table 1 in Section 2 provides a side-by-side comparison of these two formulations across all MDP components.

The action space in Agentic RL is particularly important because it unifies language generation with environment interaction. Formally:

$$A_{\text{agent}} = A_{\text{text}} \cup A_{\text{action}}$$

where $A_{\text{text}}$ denotes free-form natural language tokens emitted via autoregressive decoding, and $A_{\text{action}}$ denotes abstract, non-linguistic actions delimited by special tokens like `<action_start>` and `<action_end>`. These structured actions may invoke external tools (e.g., `call("search", "Einstein")`) or interact with environments (e.g., `move("north")`), and importantly, $A_{\text{action}}$ is recursively constructed so that a single action element may itself represent a sequence of primitive actions. This design choice enables a unified policy to jointly model language generation and environment interaction within the same RL formulation, rather than requiring separate modules for communication and action.

The learning objective also differs fundamentally between the two paradigms. For PBRFT, the objective is to maximize expected response reward:

$$J_{\text{trad}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi_\theta}[r(a)]$$

where no discount factor is required because optimization resembles maximum-expected-reward sequence modeling. For Agentic RL, the objective is to maximize discounted cumulative reward over trajectories:

$$J_{\text{agent}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta}\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T-1} \gamma^t R_{\text{agent}}(s_t, a_t)\right]$$

where $0 &lt; \gamma &lt; 1$ and $\tau$ represents a full trajectory of states and actions. This objective requires policy-gradient or value-based methods with explicit exploration and long-term credit assignment mechanisms, which is why standard RLHF algorithms often fail when directly applied to agentic settings.

#### The Six Core Agentic Capabilities

Section 3 of the paper organizes the technical landscape around six capabilities that RL can optimize, treating each not as a static module but as a learnable policy component.

**Planning** refers to deliberation over a sequence of actions to achieve a goal, and RL enhances planning through two distinct paradigms. In the first paradigm, RL serves as an **external guide** where the LLM generates potential actions within a structured search framework, and RL trains an auxiliary value or heuristic function that guides algorithms like Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). Representative works include RAP (Hao et al., 2023) and LATS (Zhou et al., 2024a), where the LLM acts as a knowledge-rich action proposer while RL provides adaptive evaluative feedback. In the second paradigm, RL serves as an **internal driver** that directly optimizes the LLM's planning policy through environmental interaction, using methods derived from RLHF such as DPO on successful versus failed trajectories (ETO, Song et al., 2024b) or lifelong learning frameworks like VOYAGER (Wang et al., 2024a) that iteratively build skill libraries. The prospective direction is synthesizing these paradigms so that RL optimizes a meta-policy governing the deliberation process itself—learning when to explore alternative paths, how to prune unpromising branches, and how deeply to reason before committing to an action.

**Tool Use** has evolved from early ReAct-style prompting to deeply interleaved Tool-Integrated Reasoning (TIR) systems optimized by RL. Early paradigms relied on prompt engineering (ReAct, Yao et al., 2023b) or supervised fine-tuning (Toolformer, Schick et al., 2023; FireAct, Chen et al., 2023) to elicit tool-use behaviors, but these approaches train agents to replicate static patterns without strategic flexibility. RL-based approaches shift the objective from imitation to outcome-driven optimization, enabling agents to autonomously discover when, how, and which tools to deploy. Frameworks like ToolRL (Qian et al., 2025) demonstrate that even base models without imitation traces can elicit emergent capabilities like self-correction of faulty code and adaptive adjustment of invocation frequency through RL training alone. The primary frontier is extending this to robust long-horizon tasks requiring multi-turn reasoning, which is bottlenecked by **temporal credit assignment**—the challenge of pinpointing which specific tool invocation in a long sequence contributed to success or failure. Nascent research explores granular reward schemes like turn-level advantage estimation in GiGPO (Feng et al., 2025b) and SpaRL (Wang et al., 2025b), but accurate credit assignment without promoting reward hacking remains largely unsolved.

**Memory** transforms from passive data stores into dynamic, RL-controlled subsystems that decide what to store, when to retrieve, and how to forget. The paper traces four representative phases. First, **RAG-style Memory** treated memory as an external datastore where RL solely regulated when to perform queries (e.g., Prospect, Tan et al., 2025b; Memory-R1, Yan et al., 2025b), though the memory medium itself remained static. Second, **Token-level Memory** introduced explicit trainable memory controllers where agents regulate their own memory states stored in token form, either as explicit natural-language tokens (MemAgent, Yu et al., 2025d; MEM1, Zhou et al., 2025g) or implicit latent embeddings (MemoryLLM, Wang et al., 2024h; M+, Wang et al., 2025s). Third, **Structured Memory** organizes information beyond flat token sequences using temporal knowledge graphs (Zep, Rasmussen et al., 2025), atomic memory notes (A-MEM, Xu et al., 2025d), or hierarchical graph-based designs (G-Memory, Zhang et al., 2025d; Mem0, Chhikara et al., 2025), though their management has thus far been governed by handcrafted rules rather than RL. Table 3 in Section 3.3 provides an overview of these three classic categories with works marked by whether they directly employ RL.

**Self-Improvement** uses RL as a mechanism for ongoing reflection, enabling agents to learn from their own mistakes across planning, reasoning, tool use, and memory. Initial methods leveraged prompt-based heuristics (verbal reinforcement learning) where agents generate an answer, linguistically reflect on errors, and produce refined solutions within a single inferential pass without gradient updates (Reflexion, Shinn et al., 2023; Self-refine, Madaan et al., 2023). Subsequent research employed RL with gradient-based updates to internalize reflective feedback loops directly into model parameters (KnowSelf, Qiao et al., 2025; Reflection-DPO, Patel et al., 2025). The most advanced class combines reflection, reasoning, and task generation into self-sustaining loops enabling unbounded self-improvement without human-labeled data, distinguished by learning loop architecture: (1) self-play and search-guided refinement emulating AlphaZero (R-Zero, Huang et al., 2025a; ISC, Tian et al., 2024), (2) execution-guided curriculum generation where agents create their own problems and learn from verifiable outcomes (Absolute Zero, Zhao et al., 2025a; Self-Evolving Curriculum, Chen et al., 2025f), and (3) collective bootstrapping where learning is accelerated by aggregating shared experience (SiriuS, Zhao et al., 2025e; MALT, Motwani et al., 2025). The prospective frontier is applying RL at a higher abstraction level to enable meta-learning for adaptive reflection—learning not just to correct errors but to learn how to self-correct more effectively over time.

**Reasoning** is categorized into fast reasoning (rapid, heuristic-driven inference with minimal intermediate steps) and slow reasoning (deliberate, structured, multi-step reasoning), building on dual-process cognitive theory. Fast reasoning models operate analogously to System 1 cognition, generating immediate responses without explicit step-by-step deliberation, but this efficiency comes at the cost of systematic reasoning, making models vulnerable to factual errors and shallow generalization. Slow reasoning models emulate System 2 cognition by explicitly producing intermediate reasoning traces using techniques like chain-of-thought prompting, multi-step verification, and reasoning-augmented RL, achieving higher accuracy in knowledge-intensive tasks despite slower inference. Representative examples include OpenAI's o1 and o3 series, DeepSeek-R1, and methods incorporating dynamic test-time scaling or reinforcement learning. The open challenge is employing RL for reliably training slow-thinking reasoning capabilities in agentic scenarios, where pure fast models may overlook critical steps while slow models suffer from excessive latency or overthinking behaviors. Emerging hybrid strategies combine efficiency with rigor through adaptive test-time scaling that allows models to decide whether to respond quickly or engage in extended deliberation depending on task complexity.

**Perception** bridges visual perception with linguistic abstraction in Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs), moving beyond passive perception toward active visual cognition through RL. Early efforts focused on preference-based RL to strengthen Chain-of-Thought reasoning in MLLMs (Visual-RFT, Liu et al., 2025v; Reason-RFT, Tan et al., 2025a), directly applying GRPO to the vision domain with vision-specific metrics like Intersection-over-Union (IoU) as verifiable reward signals. Recent work explores active cognition over multimodal content by treating visual representations as manipulable and verifiable intermediate thoughts, achieved through three directions: (1) **Grounding-Driven Active Perception** anchors each Chain-of-Thought step to specific image regions via bounding-box tokens (GRIT, Fan et al., 2025c; Ground-R1, Cao et al., 2025a), (2) **Tool-Driven Active Perception** frames visual cognition as an agentic process where external tools assist the cognitive workflow (VisTA, Huang et al., 2025d; VTool-R1, Wu et al., 2025g), and (3) **Generation-Driven Active Perception** equips LVLMs with the ability to generate sketches or images interleaved with reasoning (Visual Planning, Xu et al., 2025e; GoT-R1, Duan et al., 2025). RL has also extended to audio modalities for reasoning enhancement in audio-language models and fine-grained component optimization in speech synthesis.

#### RL Algorithms for Agentic Settings

Section 2.7 introduces four canonical RL algorithms that form a spectrum from general policy gradients to specialized preference learning, each with distinct characteristics for agentic applications.

**REINFORCE** provides the foundational policy gradient theory, operating by increasing the probability of actions leading to high cumulative reward and decreasing probability of those leading to low reward. Its objective function is:

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{s_0}\left[\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(R(s_0, a^{(i)}) - b(s_0)\right)\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a^{(i)}|s_0)\right]$$

where $a^{(i)} \sim \pi_\theta(a|s_0)$ is the $i$-th sampled response, $R(s_0, a)$ denotes final rewards on task completion, and $b(s)$ is a baseline function (commonly the value function $V(s)$) to reduce gradient variance. Despite concise formulation and easy implementation, REINFORCE suffers from high variance in gradient estimates, sample inefficiency, sensitivity to learning rate, and lack of a critic (value estimator).

**Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)** became the dominant RL algorithm for LLM alignment due to stability and reliability, improving upon vanilla policy gradients by limiting update steps to prevent destructively large policy changes. Its primary clipped objective function is:

$$L_{PPO}(\theta) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\min\left(\frac{\pi_\theta(a_t^{(i)}|s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t^{(i)}|s_t)}A(s_t, a_t^{(i)}), \text{clip}\left(\frac{\pi_\theta(a_t^{(i)}|s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t^{(i)}|s_t)}, 1 - \epsilon, 1 + \epsilon\right)A(s_t, a_t^{(i)})\right)$$

where $a_t^{(i)} \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a|s_t)$ is sampled from the old policy, $A_t$ is the estimated advantage given by $A(s_t, a_t) = R(s_t, a_t) - V(s_t)$, and the clip term prevents the probability ratio from moving too far from 1. The estimation of the advantage function plays a predominant role in PPO performance, with recent variants concentrating on reducing bias (VinePPO, Kazemnejad et al., 2024) or variance (VAPO, Yue et al., 2025b), though a remaining drawback is reliance on a separate critic network that substantially increases parameter count during training.

**Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)** represents a groundbreaking shift by entirely bypassing the need for a separate explicit reward model, reframing the problem of maximizing reward under a KL-constraint as a likelihood-based objective on human preference data. Given a dataset of preferences $\mathcal{D} = \{(y_w, y_l)\}$ where $y_w$ is preferred and $y_l$ is dispreferred, the DPO loss is:

$$L_{DPO}(\pi_\theta; \pi_{\text{ref}}) = -\mathbb{E}_{(x, y_w, y_l) \sim \mathcal{D}}\left[\log \sigma\left(\beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_w|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w|x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_l|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l|x)}\right)\right]$$

where $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ is a reference policy (usually the initial SFT model) and $\beta$ is a hyperparameter. While DPO eliminates the critic, its performance is intrinsically tied to the quality and coverage of its static preference dataset, making it less suitable for dynamic agentic environments where preferences must be learned online.

**Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)** addresses the inefficiency of PPO's large critic by introducing a lightweight evaluation paradigm that operates on groups of responses, using their relative rewards within a group to compute advantages without an absolute value critic. The core GRPO objective is:

$$L_{GRPO} = \frac{1}{G}\sum_{g=1}^{G}\min\left(\frac{\pi_\theta(a_t^{(g)}|s_t^{(g)})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t^{(g)}|s_t^{(g)})}\hat{A}(s_t^{(g)}, a_t^{(g)}), \text{clip}\left(\frac{\pi_\theta(a_t^{(g)}|s_t^{(g)})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t^{(g)}|s_t^{(g)})}, 1 - \epsilon, 1 + \epsilon\right)\hat{A}(s_t^{(g)}, a_t^{(g)})\right)$$

where a group of outputs $\{(s_0^{(g)}, a_0^{(g)}, \ldots, s_{T-1}^{(g)}, a_{T-1}^{(g)})\}_{g=1}^G$ is sampled from the old policy, and the advantage function is estimated by:

$$\hat{A}(s_t, a_t) = \frac{R(s_t, a_t) - \text{mean}(R(s_t^{(1)}, a_t^{(1)}), \ldots, R(s_t^{(G)}, a_t^{(G)}))}{\text{std}(R(s_t^{(1)}, a_t^{(1)}), \ldots, R(s_t^{(G)}, a_t^{(G)}))}$$

This group-relative approach is highly sample-efficient and reduces computational overhead, but the group-based advantage estimation is vulnerable to high variance and low accuracy. Table 2 in Section 2.7 provides a comprehensive comparison of popular variants from each algorithm family, including PPO variants (VAPO, LitePPO, PF-PPO, VinePPO, PSGPO), DPO variants (β-DPO, SimPO, IPO, KTO, ORPO, Step-DPO, LCPO), and GRPO variants (DAPO, GSPO, GMPO, ProRL, Posterior-GRPO, Dr.GRPO, Step-GRPO, SRPO, GRESO, StarPO, GHPO, Skywork R1V2, ASPO, TreePo, EDGE-GRPO, DARS, CHORD, PAPO, Pass@k Training).

#### Reward Design Strategies

The reward function design is critical because it determines what behaviors the agent learns to optimize. Section 2.5 distinguishes between PBRFT rewards (single scalar $r(a)$ with no intermediate feedback) and Agentic RL rewards that combine multiple signal types:

$$R_{\text{agent}}(s_t, a_t) = \begin{cases} r_{\text{task}} & \text{on task completion} \\ r_{\text{sub}}(s_t, a_t) & \text{for step-level progress} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

allowing dense, sparse, or learned rewards (e.g., unit-test passes, symbolic verifier success).

Two major reward paradigms emerge across the application domains surveyed in Section 4. **Outcome reward RL** defines rewards solely by final correctness (e.g., pass@k, unit-test success, task completion), which is simple to implement but suffers from sparse feedback and difficult credit assignment in long-horizon tasks. **Process reward RL** leverages intermediate evaluators (e.g., unit tests, assertions, sub-task checks, compilation feedback) to provide denser feedback, shaping credit assignment and improving learning efficiency. For example, in code generation, StepCoder (Dou et al., 2024) decomposes compilation and execution into step-level signals, while PSGPO (Dai et al., 2025) leverages intermediate error traces and process annotations for dense rewards. In mathematical reasoning, process-aware methods inject handcrafted hint text into Long Chain-of-Thought traces to encourage code executor calls during inference (START, Li et al., 2025b), or recursively generate and solve progressively simpler variants of complex problems using verifiable reward signals (LADDER, Simonds & Yoshiyama, 2025).

The choice between outcome and process rewards involves a fundamental trade-off. Outcome rewards are easier to define and less prone to reward hacking (where agents exploit loopholes in the reward function), but they provide weak learning signals for long-horizon tasks where the agent may take dozens of steps before receiving any feedback. Process rewards provide denser supervision that accelerates learning, but they require careful design to avoid teaching agents to optimize intermediate metrics at the expense of final task success. Section 6.1 discusses how outcome-driven RL can inadvertently amplify hallucination if agents learn spurious correlations that achieve the goal without being factually sound.

#### Open Challenges and Future Directions

Section 6 discusses three pivotal challenges that define the field's research frontier, because understanding current limitations is essential for positioning new research contributions.

**Trustworthiness** encompasses security, hallucination, and sycophancy. Security risks are fundamentally more complex for agents than standard LLMs because agents possess expanded attack surfaces through external components like tools, memory, and planning modules. RL magnifies these risks by transforming agents from passive victims into active goal-seeking exploiters of vulnerabilities through reward hacking—learning that unsafe actions are the most effective path to maximizing long-term reward. Mitigation requires defense-in-depth approaches including robust sandboxing, process-based rewards that penalize unsafe intermediate steps, adversarial training within the RL loop, and continuous monitoring for anomaly detection. Hallucination in agentic LLMs extends beyond factual errors to encompass unfaithful reasoning paths and misaligned planning, and outcome-driven RL can inadvertently amplify hallucination if agents learn spurious correlations. Promising mitigations include shifting from outcome-only to process-based rewards (e.g., Factuality-aware Step-wise Policy Optimization), training on mixtures of solvable and unsolvable problems to restore ability to abstain, and adding cross-modal alignment objectives for multimodal agents. Sycophancy refers to agents generating outputs that conform to user beliefs even when factually incorrect, caused by RLHF reward models equating user satisfaction with agreeable responses. Mitigation focuses on sycophancy-aware reward models and co-optimizing policy and reward models online (Cooper, Hong et al., 2025b).

**Scaling up Agentic Training** addresses computation, model size, data size, and efficiency bottlenecks. Recent advances demonstrate that scaling RL fine-tuning computation directly enhances reasoning ability, with studies showing longer training horizons systematically improve tool-use frequency, reasoning depth, and task accuracy (Agent RL Scaling Law, Mai et al., 2025; ProRL, Liu et al., 2025h; ProRLv2, Hu et al., 2025a). However, increasing model capacity heightens both promise and pitfalls—larger models unlock greater potential but risk entropy collapse and narrowing of capability boundaries as RL sharpens output distributions toward high-reward modes. Data scaling introduces synergy and conflict across domains, with cross-domain RL in math, code, and logic showing complex interactions where some pairings enhance each other while others interfere. Efficiency improvements include calibrating data difficulty, employing diversity-driven sampling, extending reasoning length (POLARIS, An et al., 2025), and introducing RL principles into gradient scaling (Dynamic Fine-Tuning, Wu et al., 2025l).

**Scaling up Agentic Environments** addresses the scarcity of interactive, adaptive environments and the difficulty of engineering effective reward signals. Research is moving beyond adapting the agent's policy to co-evolving the environment itself through two strategies. First, automating reward function design by deploying an auxiliary "explorer" agent to generate diverse interaction trajectories used to train a reward model via heuristics or preference modeling, decoupling agent training from expensive manual reward specification. Second, automating curriculum generation by establishing a feedback loop where agent performance data highlighting weaknesses is fed to an "environment generator" LLM that procedurally adapts environment configuration, creating new tasks targeting agent deficiencies (EnvGen, Zala et al., 2024). Together, automated rewards and adaptive curricula create a symbiotic relationship establishing a scalable "training flywheel" essential for self-improving agentic systems.

**The Mechanistic Debate on RL in LLMs** presents two competing explanations for why RL appears to boost LLM reasoning. The "amplifier" view holds that RL with verifiable rewards mainly reshapes the base model's output distribution by concentrating probability mass on already-reachable reasoning paths, consistent with large-k pass@k analyses finding that base models eventually match or surpass RL-tuned counterparts. The "new-knowledge" view argues that RL after next-token prediction can install qualitatively new computation by leveraging sparse outcome-level signals and encouraging longer test-time computation, with theory showing RL enables generalization on problems where next-token training alone is statistically prohibitive. Empirically, "new-capability" behaviors appear most reliably on tasks with high-fidelity executable or formally checkable reward signals, compositional or multi-step structure where partial trajectories are verifiably graded, and base models in the "intermediate" regime where the space of near-miss trajectories is rich enough for exploration but still densely populated with correct reasoning paths.

Figure 5 in Section 3 provides a conceptual overview of how RL empowers agentic LLMs across the six core capabilities, with the central panel summarizing the capability taxonomy and side panels illustrating representative RL mechanisms and interaction patterns.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This survey does not merely catalog existing methods; it synthesizes over 500 works to propose fundamental shifts in how the community conceptualizes, trains, and scales agentic systems. The following insights distinguish between incremental algorithmic tweaks and the structural innovations required to transition from static text generators to autonomous agents.

### 4.1 Fundamental Innovation: Formalizing the Paradigm Shift via Horizon $T$

Prior to this work, the distinction between "RL for LLMs" (e.g., RLHF) and "Agentic RL" was often ambiguous, leading to the misapplication of algorithms designed for single-turn alignment to multi-step decision tasks. This survey introduces a **formal mathematical boundary** based on the task horizon $T$.

*   **What Makes It Different:** Conventional surveys treat RLHF and Agentic RL as points on a continuum of "alignment." This paper argues they are distinct classes of problems. As formalized in Section 2.1, Preference-Based Reinforcement Fine-Tuning (PBRFT) operates on a degenerate Markov Decision Process (MDP) where $T = 1$. In contrast, Agentic RL requires Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes (POMDPs) where $T > 1$.
*   **Why It Is Significant:** This distinction is not merely semantic; it dictates algorithmic viability. Algorithms optimized for $T=1$ (like standard DPO) lack the mechanisms for **temporal credit assignment** required when rewards are delayed across dozens of steps. By anchoring the definition to $T$, the survey prevents researchers from using single-turn optimization tools for long-horizon problems, where they would fail to learn causal relationships between early actions and late outcomes.
*   **Impact:** This formalism enables precise communication. When a method claims to be "agentic," researchers can now verify if it optimizes a trajectory objective $J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}[\sum \gamma^t R_t]$ versus a single-turn objective $J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}[R]$. This clarity reduces wasted compute on mismatched algorithm-task pairings.

### 4.2 Fundamental Innovation: RL as the Mechanism for Capability Plasticity

A dominant assumption in prior agent literature (Section 2.3) was that capabilities like planning, memory, and tool use are **static modules** engineered via prompting or supervised fine-tuning (SFT). This survey posits a transformative thesis: these capabilities should be treated as **adaptive policies** optimized by RL.

*   **What Makes It Different:** Traditional approaches hardcode logic (e.g., "always search before answering"). This survey synthesizes evidence (Section 3) showing RL enables **strategic flexibility**. For example, in Tool Use (Section 3.2), RL-trained agents learn *when* to invoke tools based on context uncertainty, rather than following fixed rules. In Memory (Section 3.3), RL optimizes *what* to store and *when* to retrieve, transforming memory from a passive database into a dynamic subsystem.
*   **Why It Is Significant:** Static modules brittlely fail when environments change. RL-optimized capabilities exhibit **robustness to distribution shift**. The survey highlights works like `ToolRL` (Qian et al., 2025) where base models without imitation traces elicit emergent tool-use strategies solely through outcome-driven optimization. This shifts the engineering burden from designing exhaustive rules to designing reward functions that incentivize desired behaviors.
*   **Impact:** This insight reframes the research agenda. Instead of building better prompt templates for planning, researchers should focus on **reward design for planning**. It suggests that general-purpose agents will not emerge from larger context windows alone, but from RL policies that learn to manage those contexts adaptively.

### 4.3 Critical Insight: The Mechanistic Debate (Amplifier vs. New Knowledge)

While many works assume RL inherently improves reasoning, this survey critically synthesizes conflicting evidence in Section 6.4 to highlight a fundamental unresolved question: **Does RL create new reasoning capabilities or merely reshape existing ones?**

*   **What Makes It Different:** Most literature reports performance gains (e.g., higher pass@1) without analyzing the source. This survey distinguishes two competing hypotheses:
    *   **The Amplifier View:** RL concentrates probability mass on reasoning paths already latent in the pretrained model. Evidence includes large-$k$ pass@k analyses where base models eventually match RL-tuned counterparts given enough sampling.
    *   **The New-Knowledge View:** RL installs qualitatively new computation (e.g., verification, backtracking) absent in the base model. Evidence includes generalization to out-of-distribution rules where next-token training fails.
*   **Why It Is Significant:** This distinction dictates scaling strategy. If RL is merely an amplifier, gains will saturate once the model's latent capacity is exhausted. If RL creates new knowledge, then **training compute** becomes a primary driver of capability beyond model size. The survey notes empirically that "new-capability" behaviors appear most reliably on tasks with **high-fidelity executable rewards** and **composable multi-step structure** (Section 6.4).
*   **Impact:** This insight prevents overconfidence in RL scaling. It suggests that for domains lacking verifiable rewards (e.g., creative writing), RL may only refine style (amplification) rather than improve substance (new knowledge), guiding researchers to prioritize domains with dense feedback signals for genuine capability growth.

### 4.4 Fundamental Innovation: Co-Evolutionary Environment Scaling

Standard scaling laws focus on model parameters and data tokens. This survey identifies a bottleneck unique to agentic systems: **the scarcity of interactive environments**. Section 6.3 proposes a paradigm shift from scaling the agent to scaling the **world it learns in**.

*   **What Makes It Different:** Conventional approaches manually engineer environments (e.g., fixed web simulators) and rewards. This survey advocates for **automated environment generation**. Specifically, it highlights strategies where an auxiliary "environment generator" LLM procedurally adapts task difficulty based on agent performance (e.g., `EnvGen`, Zala et al., 2024), creating a "training flywheel."
*   **Why It Is Significant:** Agentic RL suffers from **data exhaustion** faster than standard LLM training because interaction trajectories are expensive to collect. Static environments lead to overfitting on specific environment quirks (e.g., memorizing website structures rather than learning navigation). Co-evolution ensures the agent is consistently challenged within its "zone of proximal development," preventing plateauing.
*   **Impact:** This insight redirects infrastructure investment. Instead of solely building larger GPU clusters for model training, the field must invest in **scalable environment simulators** and **automated reward modeling**. It suggests that the limiting factor for general-purpose agents may not be model capacity, but the complexity and adaptability of the digital worlds we train them in.

### 4.5 Practical Innovation: The Capability-Application Dependency Matrix

Finally, the survey provides a practical tool for research prioritization via Table 9 (Section 4.8), which maps application domains to core capabilities.

*   **What Makes It Different:** Prior surveys list applications and capabilities separately. This matrix quantifies their **dependency levels** (Core vs. Supporting vs. Minimal). For instance, it reveals that `Memory` is a Core dependency for Embodied Agents but Minimal for Math Agents.
*   **Why It Is Significant:** This prevents **misallocated research effort**. A technique improving long-term memory retrieval may yield significant gains for robotics (Embodied) but negligible returns for theorem proving (Math). Conversely, `Planning` is Core for both Search and Code agents, indicating it as a high-leverage target for general improvement.
*   **Impact:** This matrix serves as a heuristic for resource allocation. Researchers developing a new Math Agent can deprioritize complex memory modules based on this mapping, focusing instead on `Reasoning` and `Planning`. It transforms the survey from a passive reference into an active **strategic planning tool** for the community.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

Since this work is a **survey** synthesizing over 500 recent studies rather than a single empirical paper, this section does not describe new experiments conducted by the authors. Instead, it analyzes the **collective empirical evidence** presented across the surveyed literature to evaluate the validity of Agentic RL claims. We examine the evaluation methodologies, benchmark performance, data efficiency, and robustness checks reported in the cited works to determine where Agentic RL demonstrably succeeds and where evidence remains mixed.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology Across Surveyed Works

The survey highlights a critical fragmentation in how Agentic RL systems are evaluated, which complicates direct comparison across studies. The authors categorize evaluation protocols into three distinct tiers based on environmental fidelity and reward verification.

*   **Static Benchmark Evaluation:** The majority of surveyed works (e.g., code generation, math reasoning) rely on static datasets with verifiable answers.
    *   **Metrics:** Primary metrics include `pass@k` (probability of at least one correct solution in $k$ samples), exact match accuracy, and task completion rates.
    *   **Environments:** Examples include **HumanEval** (164 Python tasks), **MBPP** (974 tasks), **MATH-500**, and **AIME** (competition math).
    *   **Limitation:** As noted in Section 6.6, static benchmarks suffer from **evaluation contamination**, where models may have memorized test cases during pretraining, leading to inflated scores that do not reflect genuine agentic reasoning.

*   **Interactive Environment Evaluation:** A smaller subset of works evaluates agents in dynamic environments where state evolves based on actions.
    *   **Metrics:** Success rate over multi-step trajectories, cumulative reward, and step efficiency.
    *   **Environments:** **WebArena** (web navigation), **AndroidWorld** (116 tasks across 20 apps, Section 5.1.2), **SWE-bench** (real GitHub issues), and **ALFWorld** (embodied tasks).
    *   **Advantage:** These settings better capture the **POMDP** nature of Agentic RL (Section 2.1), testing temporal credit assignment rather than single-turn correctness.

*   **Human Preference Evaluation:** Used primarily for alignment and safety (though noted as out-of-scope for core Agentic RL in Section 1), some works employ human raters to assess trajectory quality when automatic verifiers are unavailable.
    *   **Limitation:** High cost and low scalability make this impractical for the thousands of rollouts required for RL training.

The survey explicitly critiques this fragmentation in Section 1, noting that *"inconsistent terminology and evaluation protocols... mak[e] systematic comparison and cross-domain generalization difficult."* This lack of standardization is a primary barrier to establishing reliable performance baselines.

### 5.2 Quantitative Performance Benchmarks

Despite methodological inconsistencies, the survey consolidates specific quantitative results that demonstrate the efficacy of Agentic RL in high-stakes domains. The following data points, extracted from Sections 4 and 6, provide concrete evidence of capability gains.

#### Mathematical Reasoning
Agentic RL shows strong performance in domains with **verifiable, executable rewards**.
*   **Competition Math:** The survey cites **rStar2-Agent** (Shang et al., 2025a), a 14B-parameter model trained with agentic RL, achieving **80.6% pass@1 on AIME24** and **69.8% on AIME25**. Notably, this performance was reached in just **510 RL steps**, suggesting high sample efficiency when reward signals are dense and executable (Section 4.3.1).
*   **Data Efficiency:** **1-shot RLVR** (Wang et al., 2025r) demonstrates that RL with verifiable signals can achieve performance close to using a **1.2k-example dataset** with only **1 training example**. With **2 examples**, performance approaches that of models trained on the **7.5k MATH dataset**. This supports the claim that RL amplifies reasoning capabilities even with minimal data when rewards are high-fidelity (Section 4.3.1).
*   **Formal Proof:** **DeepSeek-Prover-v1.5** (Xin et al., 2025) shows significant improvements on **miniF2F** and **ProofNet** benchmarks using binary verifier feedback, validating that sparse rewards can work in formal domains when paired with effective exploration strategies like MCTS (Section 4.3.2).

#### Code Generation and Software Engineering
*   **Pass Rate Improvements:** **PRLCoder** (Ye et al., 2025b) introduces process-supervised RL, yielding **+10.5% pass-rate improvements** over base models by scoring partial snippets rather than just final outputs. This quantifies the benefit of **process reward RL** over outcome-only rewards in complex synthesis tasks (Section 4.2.1).
*   **Scale and Parallelism:** **Qwen3-Coder** (Team, 2025b) performs execution-driven RL across **20,000 parallel environments**, achieving state-of-the-art performance on **SWE-Bench Verified**. This highlights the compute-intensive nature of scaling Agentic RL (Section 4.2.3).
*   **SQL Generation:** **SkyRL-SQL-7B** (Liu et al., 2025j) surpasses **GPT-4o** and **o4-mini** on SQL generation benchmarks using only **653 training examples**, demonstrating that specialized agentic training can outperform general-purpose large models in narrow domains (Section 4.8).

#### Search and Research Agents
*   **Closed-Source Gap:** On the challenging **BrowseComp** benchmark (measuring ability to locate hard-to-find information), **OpenAI Deep Research** achieves **51.5% pass@1**. The survey notes that most open-source models still fail on this benchmark, revealing gaps in long-horizon planning and cross-source verification (Section 4.1.2).
*   **Open-Source Progress:** Methods like **DeepRetrieval** (Jiang et al., 2025c) and **Search-R1** (Jin et al., 2025a) show gains on simpler retrieval tasks using GRPO-trained policies, but struggle to match closed-source systems on complex multi-hop reasoning (Section 4.1.1).

#### Infrastructure Efficiency
*   **Training Speed:** The **AWorld** framework (Yu et al., 2025a) achieves a **14.6× speedup** over single-node execution by orchestrating massively parallel rollouts across clusters. This quantifies the engineering overhead required to make Agentic RL tractable at scale (Section 5.2).

### 5.3 Component Ablations and Reward Design

The survey synthesizes ablation studies from cited works to determine which components of Agentic RL drive performance improvements. These findings validate the theoretical distinctions made in Section 2.

#### Outcome vs. Process Rewards
*   **Finding:** Process-level supervision consistently outperforms outcome-only rewards in long-horizon tasks.
*   **Evidence:** In code generation, **StepCoder** (Dou et al., 2024) and **PSGPO** (Dai et al., 2025) leverage intermediate error traces for dense rewards, leading to faster convergence than outcome-only baselines (Section 4.2.1).
*   **Trade-off:** While process rewards accelerate learning, they require careful design to avoid **reward hacking** (optimizing intermediate metrics at the expense of final success). The survey notes that outcome rewards are more robust but suffer from sparsity (Section 3.7).

#### Algorithm Variants (PPO vs. GRPO vs. DPO)
*   **Finding:** GRPO variants dominate recent agentic settings due to efficiency, while PPO remains stable for critical alignment.
*   **Evidence:** Table 2 lists over 20 variants of GRPO (e.g., **DAPO**, **Dr.GRPO**, **Step-GRPO**). The survey explains that GRPO eliminates the need for a separate critic network, reducing memory overhead—a critical factor when rolling out large agents over long horizons (Section 2.7).
*   **Condition:** However, GRPO's group-based advantage estimation is vulnerable to high variance. Works like **VAPO** (Yue et al., 2025b) and **VinePPO** (Kazemnejad et al., 2024) introduce variance control mechanisms to stabilize training, suggesting GRPO requires additional regularization for robust agentic learning (Table 2).

#### The Mechanistic Debate: Amplifier vs. New Knowledge
*   **Finding:** Empirical evidence is split on whether RL creates new capabilities or merely reshapes existing ones.
*   **Evidence:** The survey reports that approximately **2/3 of cited mathematical and code-reasoning studies** primarily emphasize improvements in **pass@1 accuracy** (reshaping distribution), while about **1/3 explicitly report expanding pass@k frontiers** (e.g., higher pass@32 at fixed pass@1), indicating genuine capability expansion (Section 6.4).
*   **Condition:** "New-capability" behaviors appear most reliably on tasks with:
    1.  **High-fidelity, executable rewards** (e.g., code execution, formal proof).
    2.  **Compositional multi-step structure** (where partial trajectories are verifiable).
    3.  **Base models in the "intermediate" regime** (neither near-random nor near-ceiling) (Section 6.4).
*   **Implication:** This suggests RL is not a universal booster; its ability to generate "new knowledge" is conditional on the presence of verifiable feedback signals.

### 5.4 Limitations and Robustness Checks

The survey critically assesses where empirical evidence is weak or where Agentic RL systems fail, providing a realistic view of current limitations.

#### Trustworthiness and Safety Failures
*   **Hallucination Tax:** Studies cited in Section 6.1 (e.g., Song et al., 2025c) show that RL-only post-training can degrade an agent's ability to refuse unanswerable questions, compelling it to generate responses rather than abstaining. This "hallucination tax" indicates that outcome-driven RL may optimize for confidence over truthfulness.
*   **Reward Hacking:** The survey warns that RL agents may learn unsafe actions if they yield high rewards (e.g., using malicious tools to complete a task faster). This is a documented failure mode in agentic settings where safety constraints are not explicitly encoded in the reward function (Section 6.1).
*   **Sycophancy:** RLHF can exacerbate sycophantic tendencies, teaching agents to conform to user views rather than providing accurate information (Wen et al., 2024). This is a robustness failure in human-agent interaction scenarios (Section 6.1).

#### Scaling Bottlenecks
*   **Compute Cost:** Large-scale RL is substantially more resource-intensive than SFT due to rollout generation and long-horizon reasoning. The survey notes that agentic systems increase training and deployment carbon footprints significantly (Section 6.6).
*   **Environment Scarcity:** A critical limitation is the lack of scalable environments. Prevalent environments like **ALFWorld** and **ScienceWorld** are deemed insufficient for training general-purpose agents (Section 6.3). The survey cites **EnvGen** (Zala et al., 2024) as a promising direction for automated curriculum generation, but this remains an open challenge.

#### Generalization Gaps
*   **Domain Interference:** Cross-domain RL studies show complex interactions; some domain pairings enhance each other (e.g., math and code), while others interfere and reduce performance (Li et al., 2025o). This indicates that multi-task agentic training is not simply additive (Section 6.2).
*   **Closed-Source Disparity:** The performance gap on **BrowseComp** (51.5% for OpenAI vs. failure for most open-source) suggests that current open-source agentic methods lack the long-horizon planning or tool-use robustness of proprietary systems (Section 4.1.2).

### 5.5 Assessment of Empirical Claims

Based on the synthesized evidence, we can assess the survey's core claims:

1.  **Claim:** *Agentic RL enables autonomous decision-making in dynamic environments.*
    *   **Verdict:** **Supported.** Quantitative results in interactive environments (AndroidWorld, WebArena) and software engineering (SWE-Bench) demonstrate agents can successfully navigate multi-step tasks where single-turn models fail.
2.  **Claim:** *RL transforms static capabilities into adaptive policies.*
    *   **Verdict:** **Partially Supported.** Evidence from tool use (ToolRL) and memory (Memory-R1) shows agents learn *when* to invoke tools or retrieve memory. However, many systems still rely on handcrafted reward structures, limiting true adaptability.
3.  **Claim:** *RL creates new reasoning capabilities (vs. amplifying existing ones).*
    *   **Verdict:** **Conditional.** Evidence supports this only in domains with **verifiable rewards** (math, code, formal proof). In domains lacking clear feedback (e.g., creative writing, open-ended dialogue), evidence suggests RL acts primarily as an amplifier (Section 6.4).
4.  **Claim:** *Scaling agentic training improves reasoning.*
    *   **Verdict:** **Supported.** Studies like **ProRL** and **Agent RL Scaling Law** show longer training horizons systematically improve tool-use frequency and reasoning depth (Section 6.2). However, efficiency remains a bottleneck.

In summary, the empirical evidence synthesized in this survey strongly supports the viability of Agentic RL in **verifiable, structured domains** (code, math, search). However, robustness, safety, and generalization to unstructured environments remain significant challenges, with current open-source methods lagging behind closed-source systems in complex long-horizon tasks.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This survey synthesizes compelling evidence for Agentic RL's potential, but a critical reading reveals substantial limitations, untested assumptions, and fundamental trade-offs that constrain its current applicability. Understanding these boundaries is essential for researchers to avoid overextending claims or misallocating resources. This section examines four categories of limitations: (1) core assumptions that underpin the Agentic RL formalism but may not hold universally, (2) scenarios and problem settings the survey does not adequately address, (3) computational and data constraints that limit scalability, and (4) persistent weaknesses and open questions that define the field's research frontier.

### 6.1 Core Assumptions and Their Fragility

The Agentic RL paradigm rests on several foundational assumptions that enable its theoretical formalism but may break down in practice. Recognizing these assumptions helps researchers identify when Agentic RL is appropriate versus when alternative approaches may be preferable.

#### Assumption 1: Verifiable Reward Signals Are Available

**The Assumption.** Agentic RL fundamentally requires reward signals to guide policy optimization. The survey's most successful case studies—mathematical reasoning (Section 4.3), code generation (Section 4.2), and formal theorem proving (Section 4.3.2)—all share a critical property: **executable verification**. A math answer is either correct or incorrect; code either passes unit tests or fails; proofs are either accepted by Lean or rejected. This enables dense, high-fidelity feedback that RL algorithms can exploit.

**Where It Breaks.** Many real-world agent tasks lack such verifiable rewards. Consider:
- **Open-ended dialogue:** There is no ground-truth "correct" response in a multi-turn conversation.
- **Creative writing:** Quality is subjective and context-dependent.
- **Strategic negotiation:** Success depends on counterpart behavior, not just agent actions.

The survey acknowledges this limitation implicitly. Section 6.4 notes that "new-capability" behaviors from RL appear most reliably on tasks with "high-fidelity, often executable or formally checkable reward signals." For domains without such signals, the survey provides limited guidance. Section 6.1 discusses hallucination amplification when outcome-driven RL optimizes for confidence over truthfulness in the absence of verifiable feedback, but does not offer principled alternatives.

**Trade-off.** Researchers face a choice: (1) restrict Agentic RL to domains with verifiable rewards, limiting applicability, or (2) use learned reward models (e.g., RLHF-style critics), which introduce their own biases and may not capture true task objectives. The survey does not resolve this tension.

#### Assumption 2: Environment Dynamics Are Stationary or Learnable

**The Assumption.** The POMDP formalism (Section 2.1) assumes that transition dynamics $P(s_{t+1} | s_t, a_t)$ are either stationary or change slowly enough for the agent to adapt. This enables credit assignment across trajectories—agents can learn that action $a_t$ at step $t$ contributed to reward at step $T$.

**Where It Breaks.** Real-world environments often violate this assumption:
- **Web environments:** Website layouts change frequently, breaking learned navigation policies.
- **Multi-agent settings:** Other agents adapt their strategies, creating non-stationary dynamics.
- **Embodied robotics:** Physical wear, lighting changes, and object displacement alter state transitions.

The survey touches on this in Section 6.3, noting that "prevalent environments like ALFWorld and ScienceWorld are insufficient for training general-purpose agents" partly because they are static. However, it does not deeply analyze how non-stationarity affects Agentic RL convergence. Section 4.4.3 mentions that interactive GUI environments require "robustness to stochastic transitions," but does not quantify failure rates when dynamics shift.

**Trade-off.** Agents trained in static simulators may overfit to environment quirks rather than learning generalizable policies. The survey proposes co-evolutionary environment generation (Section 6.3) as a mitigation, but this remains an open research direction without empirical validation at scale.

#### Assumption 3: Credit Assignment Is Tractable Across Horizons

**The Assumption.** Agentic RL assumes that temporal credit assignment—determining which actions in a long trajectory contributed to final outcomes—is solvable with current algorithms. The discounted reward objective $J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}[\sum \gamma^t R_t]$ (Section 2.6) presumes that gradient signals can propagate effectively across dozens or hundreds of steps.

**Where It Breaks.** The survey explicitly identifies this as a bottleneck. Section 3.2 states that long-horizon Tool-Integrated Reasoning is "fundamentally bottlenecked by the challenge of temporal credit assignment," and Section 3.7 notes that "sparse and delayed feedback obscures the link between an agent's actions and a distant outcome." While nascent research explores granular reward schemes (e.g., GiGPO, SpaRL), the survey concedes these are "early steps" and that accurate credit assignment "without inadvertently punishing useful exploration or promoting reward hacking remains a critical and largely unsolved problem."

**Trade-off.** Researchers must choose between:
- **Outcome-only rewards:** Robust to reward hacking but provide weak learning signals for long horizons.
- **Process rewards:** Provide dense supervision but require careful design to avoid teaching agents to optimize intermediate metrics at the expense of final success.

The survey does not provide a principled framework for when to use each, leaving this as a heuristic decision.

#### Assumption 4: Base Models Possess Latent Capabilities to Amplify

**The Assumption.** The "amplifier" view of RL (Section 6.4) presumes that base pretrained models already contain latent reasoning paths that RL can concentrate probability mass upon. If the base model lacks certain capabilities entirely, RL cannot create them ex nihilo.

**Where It Breaks.** This assumption is contested. The "new-knowledge" view argues RL can install qualitatively new computation absent in the base model. The survey reports empirical evidence is split: approximately 2/3 of cited studies emphasize pass@1 improvements (consistent with amplification), while 1/3 report expanded pass@k frontiers (consistent with new capability creation). However, the survey notes this occurs primarily in the "intermediate" regime—base models that are "neither near-random nor near-ceiling." For models at either extreme, RL's effectiveness is unclear.

**Trade-off.** If RL is primarily an amplifier, then **pretraining quality** becomes the limiting factor, not RL optimization. This shifts the research focus from RL algorithm design to foundation model development. The survey does not resolve this debate, leaving it as an open mechanistic question.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

While the survey covers eight application domains (Section 4), several important scenarios receive minimal or no treatment. These gaps define boundaries of the current Agentic RL literature.

#### Multi-Modal Integration Beyond Vision

**The Gap.** Section 3.6 discusses perception extensively for vision-language models, and briefly mentions audio (Section 3.6, "Audio" subsection). However, the survey does not address:
- **Tactile sensing** for embodied agents
- **Olfactory or chemical sensing** for scientific agents
- **Haptic feedback** for manipulation tasks

These modalities are critical for many embodied applications but lack the verifiable reward structures that vision tasks enjoy (e.g., IoU for bounding boxes). The survey's heavy emphasis on vision may create a false impression that Agentic RL is equally applicable across all sensory modalities.

#### Real-World Deployment Constraints

**The Gap.** The survey focuses on training-time optimization but gives limited attention to deployment constraints:
- **Latency requirements:** Many agentic applications (e.g., real-time robotics, customer service) require sub-second response times, but slow reasoning models (Section 3.5) may exceed these budgets.
- **Memory constraints:** Long-horizon agents accumulate context over many steps, but edge devices have limited memory.
- **Energy efficiency:** Section 6.6 briefly mentions environmental sustainability but does not quantify the carbon footprint of Agentic RL training versus inference.

Section 4.6 acknowledges that "conducting RL directly in real-world settings is currently impractical due to the high cost and complexity of physical robot experiments," but does not explore sim-to-real transfer methods in depth.

#### Adversarial and Safety-Critical Settings

**The Gap.** Section 6.1 discusses trustworthiness broadly but does not deeply analyze:
- **Adversarial attacks on agentic systems:** How might adversaries exploit tool invocation, memory retrieval, or planning modules?
- **Safety-critical domains:** Medical diagnosis, autonomous driving, and financial trading require guarantees that current Agentic RL methods cannot provide.
- **Regulatory compliance:** Agents operating in regulated industries (healthcare, finance) must satisfy audit requirements that opaque RL policies may not meet.

The survey mentions "robust sandboxing" as a mitigation (Section 6.1) but does not discuss formal verification methods for agentic policies.

#### Low-Resource and Edge Settings

**The Gap.** The survey's discussed methods predominantly assume access to substantial compute:
- **GRPO variants** require sampling multiple rollouts per update (Section 2.7).
- **Parallel environments** like Qwen3-Coder's 20,000 parallel rollouts (Section 4.2.3) demand significant infrastructure.
- **AWorld's 14.6× speedup** (Section 5.2) presupposes multi-cluster access.

Researchers in low-resource settings (e.g., developing countries, small labs) may find Agentic RL inaccessible. The survey does not discuss methods for resource-constrained agentic training.

### 6.3 Computational and Data Constraints

Agentic RL imposes substantial resource requirements that limit its scalability and accessibility. The survey provides quantitative evidence of these constraints but does not fully explore their implications.

#### Compute Costs

**Evidence.** Section 6.2 states that "large-scale RL is substantially more resource-intensive than SFT due to rollout generation, long-horizon reasoning, and iterative decision steps." Specific examples include:
- **Qwen3-Coder:** Trained across 20,000 parallel environments (Section 4.2.3).
- **AWorld:** Requires cluster-level parallelism to achieve 14.6× speedup (Section 5.2).
- **Agent RL Scaling Law:** Shows that "longer training horizons systematically improve tool-use frequency, reasoning depth, and overall task accuracy" (Section 6.2), implying diminishing returns require extended training.

**Implication.** Agentic RL may exhibit **increasing marginal costs**—each additional percentage point of performance requires disproportionately more compute. This contrasts with pretraining, where scaling laws are more predictable. The survey does not provide cost-benefit analyses to help researchers decide when additional RL training is worthwhile.

#### Data Efficiency

**Evidence.** The survey presents mixed findings on data efficiency:
- **Positive:** 1-shot RLVR achieves near-dataset-level performance with only 1-2 examples (Section 4.3.1).
- **Negative:** Cross-domain RL shows "complex interactions" where some domain pairings "interfere and reduce performance" (Section 6.2).
- **Negative:** Static benchmarks suffer from "evaluation contamination," inflating apparent data efficiency (Section 6.6).

**Implication.** Data efficiency is highly **domain-dependent**. Tasks with verifiable rewards (math, code) show strong few-shot learning, while tasks without clear feedback signals may require extensive data. The survey does not provide a framework for predicting data requirements across domains.

#### Environment Scalability

**Evidence.** Section 6.3 identifies environment scarcity as a critical bottleneck:
- **Static environments** like ALFWorld and ScienceWorld are "insufficient for training general-purpose agents."
- **Manual reward engineering** is expensive and does not scale.
- **Automated environment generation** (e.g., EnvGen) is promising but "remains an open and promising direction."

**Implication.** The field faces a **chicken-and-egg problem**: better agents require better environments, but better environments require capable agents to generate them. The survey proposes co-evolutionary approaches but acknowledges these are nascent.

#### Algorithm-Specific Overheads

**Evidence.** Table 2 (Section 2.7) reveals algorithm-specific constraints:
- **PPO:** Requires a separate critic network, "substantially increasing the parameter count during training."
- **GRPO:** Eliminates the critic but is "vulnerable to high variance and low accuracy" in group-based advantage estimation.
- **DPO:** "Performance is intrinsically tied to the quality and coverage of its static preference dataset," limiting adaptability.

**Implication.** No single algorithm dominates across all agentic settings. Researchers must trade off between **stability** (PPO), **efficiency** (GRPO), and **simplicity** (DPO), with no clear guidance on selection criteria.

### 6.4 Remaining Weaknesses and Open Questions

The survey concludes by highlighting several unresolved issues that define the field's research frontier. These represent genuine weaknesses in current Agentic RL understanding, not merely gaps in the literature.

#### Weakness 1: Lack of Theoretical Guarantees

**The Issue.** Unlike classical RL, which has convergence guarantees under certain conditions (e.g., tabular MDPs with sufficient exploration), Agentic RL lacks formal guarantees for:
- **Convergence:** Will policy optimization converge to a stable policy in high-dimensional language action spaces?
- **Generalization:** Will agents trained on specific environments generalize to unseen tasks?
- **Safety:** Can we bound the probability of unsafe actions during exploration?

**Evidence.** Section 6.4's mechanistic debate illustrates this uncertainty—researchers cannot even agree whether RL creates new capabilities or amplifies existing ones, let alone provide theoretical bounds on capability growth.

**Open Question.** Can we develop **PAC-style guarantees** for Agentic RL that bound sample complexity and generalization error in language-action spaces?

#### Weakness 2: Evaluation Fragmentation

**The Issue.** Section 1 notes that "inconsistent terminology and evaluation protocols... mak[e] systematic comparison and cross-domain generalization difficult." This fragmentation persists throughout the surveyed literature:
- **Metrics vary:** pass@k for code, exact match for math, success rate for navigation.
- **Environments vary:** WebArena, AndroidWorld, SWE-Bench, ALFWorld—each with different state representations and action spaces.
- **Baselines vary:** Some papers compare to SFT, others to prompt engineering, others to prior RL methods.

**Evidence.** Section 5.1 explicitly critiques this: "current studies often examine isolated capabilities, domains, or custom environments, with inconsistent terminology and evaluation protocols."

**Open Question.** Can the community establish **standardized benchmarks** for Agentic RL analogous to ImageNet for vision or GLUE for NLP?

#### Weakness 3: Closed-Source Disparity

**The Issue.** Section 4.1.2 reveals a significant performance gap between open-source and closed-source systems:
- **OpenAI Deep Research:** 51.5% pass@1 on BrowseComp.
- **Open-source methods:** "Most open-source models still fail on OpenAI's BrowseComp."

**Evidence.** The survey attributes this to "industry's more powerful foundation models and the availability of more high-quality data," but does not explore whether algorithmic differences also contribute.

**Open Question.** Is the gap due to **model capacity**, **data quality**, **algorithmic sophistication**, or **engineering infrastructure**? Without transparency from closed-source developers, this remains speculative.

#### Weakness 4: Safety and Alignment Integration

**The Issue.** The survey explicitly excludes "RL for human value alignment" from scope (Section 1), but agentic systems inherently require safety constraints:
- **Tool invocation:** Agents should not call malicious APIs.
- **Memory access:** Agents should not retrieve sensitive information.
- **Planning:** Agents should not devise harmful multi-step strategies.

**Evidence.** Section 6.1 discusses security risks but frames them as mitigation challenges rather than core design requirements. The survey does not integrate safety into the Agentic RL formalism.

**Open Question.** Can safety constraints be **baked into the POMDP formulation** (e.g., constrained MDPs) rather than added as post-hoc guardrails?

#### Weakness 5: Long-Horizon Stability

**The Issue.** As horizons extend, several failure modes emerge:
- **Reward hacking:** Agents exploit loopholes in reward functions (Section 6.1).
- **Capability collapse:** RL "sharpens output distributions toward high-reward modes, limiting diversity" (Section 6.2).
- **Catastrophic forgetting:** Agents may lose previously learned capabilities when optimizing for new tasks.

**Evidence.** Section 6.2 notes that "increasing model capacity heightens both the promise and pitfalls of RL-based agent training," with risks of "entropy collapse and narrowing of capability boundaries."

**Open Question.** Can we develop **regularization techniques** that preserve capability diversity while optimizing for task performance?

#### Weakness 6: Interpretability and Debuggability

**The Issue.** Agentic RL policies are opaque:
- **Why did the agent invoke this tool?**
- **Why did it retrieve this memory?**
- **Why did it choose this plan?**

**Evidence.** The survey discusses "legibility optimization" in the context of prover-verifier games (Section 4.3.1), but this is limited to mathematical reasoning. No general framework exists for interpreting agentic decisions.

**Open Question.** Can we develop **interpretable agentic policies** that provide explanations for their decisions without sacrificing performance?

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes key trade-offs researchers face when designing Agentic RL systems:

| Trade-off | Option A | Option B | When to Choose A | When to Choose B |
|-----------|----------|----------|------------------|------------------|
| **Reward Type** | Outcome-only | Process-supervised | When rewards are verifiable and horizons are short | When horizons are long and intermediate signals are available |
| **Algorithm** | PPO | GRPO | When stability is critical (e.g., safety-critical domains) | When compute efficiency is paramount |
| **Environment** | Static simulator | Interactive environment | When reproducibility is needed | When generalization to real-world is the goal |
| **Capability Focus** | Specialized (single domain) | General (multi-domain) | When domain performance is the priority | When transfer learning is the goal |
| **Safety** | Post-hoc guardrails | Integrated constraints | When rapid prototyping is needed | When deployment requires guarantees |

These trade-offs are not merely engineering choices—they reflect fundamental tensions in the Agentic RL paradigm. The survey provides evidence for each option's viability but does not prescribe a universal solution, appropriately leaving these decisions to domain-specific considerations.

### 6.6 Critical Assessment

In summary, the Agentic RL paradigm offers a compelling formalism for transforming LLMs into autonomous agents, but its current instantiation faces significant limitations:

1. **Domain Restriction:** Agentic RL excels in domains with verifiable rewards (math, code, formal proof) but lacks clear pathways for subjective or open-ended tasks.

2. **Resource Intensity:** The compute and data requirements limit accessibility, potentially concentrating agentic capabilities in well-resourced organizations.

3. **Theoretical Gaps:** The lack of convergence guarantees and mechanistic understanding makes it difficult to predict when Agentic RL will succeed or fail.

4. **Safety Integration:** Safety remains an add-on rather than a core design principle, creating risks for deployed systems.

5. **Evaluation Fragmentation:** Inconsistent benchmarks and metrics hinder cumulative progress and cross-paper comparison.

Researchers should approach Agentic RL with **measured optimism**—recognizing its demonstrated successes in structured domains while acknowledging its limitations in unstructured, safety-critical, or resource-constrained settings. The survey's value lies not in presenting Agentic RL as a universal solution, but in mapping its boundaries so researchers can make informed decisions about when and how to apply it.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This survey does more than catalog existing methods; it fundamentally reorients how the community should approach the development of autonomous LLM systems. By formalizing Agentic RL as distinct from conventional alignment (Section 2) and mapping capabilities to applications (Section 4), it provides a strategic roadmap for the next decade of research. This section translates the survey's theoretical and empirical findings into concrete implications for the field, prioritizes high-value research frontiers, and offers practical guidance for practitioners deciding whether and how to adopt Agentic RL.

### 7.1 Shifting the Research Paradigm

The survey's primary contribution is legitimizing Agentic RL as a distinct scientific discipline rather than a subset of RLHF. This shift has three profound implications for how future research should be conducted:

*   **From Prompt Engineering to Policy Optimization.** Prior to this synthesis, many "agentic" improvements were achieved via prompt engineering (e.g., ReAct, Chain-of-Thought). The survey demonstrates that while prompting establishes baseline capabilities, **RL is required for adaptation** (Section 3.2). Future research should move beyond designing better prompt templates and focus on **reward function design**. For instance, instead of manually crafting a "plan-execute-reflect" prompt, researchers should design rewards that incentivize the agent to *learn* when to plan versus when to act directly.
*   **From Static Benchmarks to Dynamic Curricula.** The survey highlights that static benchmarks (e.g., HumanEval, MATH-500) are susceptible to contamination and fail to test long-horizon robustness (Section 6.6). The implication is that the field must invest in **environment scaling** alongside model scaling. As argued in Section 6.3, future progress depends on **co-evolutionary systems** where environments adapt to agent capabilities (e.g., `EnvGen`, Zala et al., 2024). Research proposals that rely solely on static test sets should be viewed with skepticism unless they demonstrate generalization to dynamic settings.
*   **From Monolithic Models to Modular Capabilities.** The capability-centered taxonomy (Section 3) implies that "general intelligence" may emerge from optimizing specific modules (planning, memory, tool use) rather than scaling a single monolithic policy. Future architectures might resemble **Mixture-of-Agents**, where specialized RL-trained policies handle specific capabilities (e.g., a dedicated planning policy vs. a dedicated coding policy), coordinated by a high-level controller. This aligns with the survey's finding that different applications depend on different capability subsets (Table 9).

### 7.2 Priority Research Frontiers

Based on the gaps identified in Section 6, the survey points to four high-leverage research directions that could unlock the next generation of agentic systems.

#### 1. Solving Temporal Credit Assignment
The survey identifies temporal credit assignment as the "critical and largely unsolved problem" for long-horizon tasks (Section 3.2). Current methods rely on sparse outcome rewards or heuristic process rewards.
*   **Research Direction:** Develop **hierarchical credit assignment mechanisms** that can attribute reward signals to specific sub-goals or tool invocations within a 50+ step trajectory.
*   **Why It Matters:** Without this, agents cannot learn complex workflows (e.g., software engineering) efficiently. Techniques like **segment-level DPO** (Section 3.7) or **turn-level advantage estimation** (GiGPO, Section 3.2) are early steps, but a unified theory for multi-step attribution is needed.
*   **Concrete Goal:** Create a benchmark where success requires correctly attributing credit to a specific action taken 20 steps prior, distinguishing it from noise in intermediate steps.

#### 2. Mechanistic Interpretability of RL Effects
The "Amplifier vs. New Knowledge" debate (Section 6.4) remains unresolved. We do not know if RL is uncovering latent capabilities or installing new circuits.
*   **Research Direction:** Apply **mechanistic interpretability tools** (e.g., activation patching, circuit analysis) to compare base models vs. RL-tuned agents.
*   **Why It Matters:** If RL is merely an amplifier, scaling efforts should focus on pretraining data quality. If it creates new knowledge, compute scaling for RL becomes the priority.
*   **Concrete Goal:** Publish a study visualizing the attention patterns or neuron activations associated with "verification" behaviors in math agents (e.g., `rStar2-Agent`, Section 4.3.1) to determine if these circuits exist pre-RL.

#### 3. Safety-Constrained Agentic RL
Section 6.1 warns that RL can amplify security risks (reward hacking, tool misuse) because agents actively optimize for rewards rather than passively generating text.
*   **Research Direction:** Integrate **Constrained Markov Decision Processes (CMDPs)** into Agentic RL frameworks. Instead of post-hoc guardrails, safety constraints (e.g., "do not call external API X") should be part of the optimization objective.
*   **Why It Matters:** Post-hoc filters can be bypassed by adaptive agents. Safety must be baked into the policy $\pi_\theta$ via the learning objective.
*   **Concrete Goal:** Develop a safe RL algorithm that maintains performance on `SWE-Bench` while guaranteeing zero unauthorized file system modifications during training rollouts.

#### 4. Automated Environment and Reward Generation
The survey notes that manual reward engineering is a bottleneck (Section 6.3).
*   **Research Direction:** Build **foundation models for environment generation**. Just as we have foundation models for text, we need models that can generate valid, verifiable task environments and reward functions based on natural language descriptions.
*   **Why It Matters:** This enables the "training flywheel" where agents generate their own curriculum, solving the data exhaustion problem.
*   **Concrete Goal:** A system where a user specifies "train an agent to manage email," and the framework automatically generates a simulated email environment with verifiable success metrics (e.g., inbox zero achieved).

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The survey's Capability-Application Dependency Matrix (Table 9) provides a heuristic for identifying high-ROI applications. Practitioners should prioritize domains where **verifiable rewards** align with **core capabilities**.

| Application Domain | Viability | Rationale (Based on Survey) | Recommended Approach |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Code Generation & Debugging** | **High** | Verifiable rewards (unit tests); Core dependency on Tool Use & Reasoning (Table 9). | Use **Process Reward RL** (e.g., `PSGPO`, Section 4.2.1) to guide intermediate steps. |
| **Mathematical Reasoning** | **High** | Binary verifiers (correct/incorrect); Core dependency on Planning & Reasoning. | Use **GRPO variants** (e.g., `Dr.GRPO`, Table 2) for sample efficiency on verifiable tasks. |
| **Web Search & Research** | **Medium** | Partially verifiable (relevance); Core dependency on Planning & Memory. | Use **Outcome Reward RL** with retrieval quality metrics (e.g., `Search-R1`, Section 4.1.1). |
| **Embodied Control (Robotics)** | **Medium** | Sim-to-real gap; Core dependency on Perception & Memory. | Use **Simulation-first RL** (e.g., `VLA-RL`, Section 4.6) with domain randomization. |
| **Open-Ended Dialogue** | **Low** | Subjective rewards; High risk of sycophancy (Section 6.1). | Prefer **SFT + RLHF** over Agentic RL; avoid long-horizon optimization without verifiable signals. |
| **Creative Writing** | **Low** | No ground truth; RL may amplify hallucination (Section 6.1). | Prefer **Prompt Engineering** or **DPO** for style alignment; avoid trajectory optimization. |

**Key Takeaway:** Agentic RL is not a universal booster. It excels in **structured, verifiable domains** (Code, Math, Search) but offers diminishing returns in **subjective, unstructured domains** (Creative Writing, Open Dialogue) where reward signals are noisy.

### 7.4 Integration and Reproducibility Guidance

For researchers and engineers looking to implement Agentic RL, the survey consolidates a fragmented ecosystem into actionable recommendations. The following guidance helps navigate algorithm selection, framework choice, and environment setup.

#### When to Prefer Agentic RL Over Alternatives
*   **Choose Agentic RL When:**
    *   The task requires **multi-step interaction** ($T > 1$) with an environment (Section 2.1).
    *   You have access to **verifiable reward signals** (e.g., code execution, API return values, math solvers).
    *   The base model shows **latent capability** but lacks strategic consistency (the "Amplifier" scenario, Section 6.4).
*   **Choose SFT / Prompting When:**
    *   The task is **single-turn** ($T = 1$) (e.g., classification, summarization).
    *   Rewards are **subjective** (e.g., tone, style) and require human preference modeling (RLHF/DPO).
    *   Compute budget is **limited**; Agentic RL requires 10-100x more rollouts than SFT (Section 6.3).

#### Algorithm Selection (Based on Table 2)
*   **For Efficiency (Low Memory):** Use **GRPO** or its variants (e.g., `DAPO`, `Dr.GRPO`). These eliminate the critic network, reducing memory overhead—critical for long-horizon rollouts where activation cache is large.
*   **For Stability (Safety-Critical):** Use **PPO** or **VAPO**. The clipped objective and separate critic provide more stable updates, reducing the risk of policy collapse in high-stakes domains (Section 2.7).
*   **For Preference Alignment:** Use **DPO** or **Step-DPO**. If you have preference data (winning vs. losing trajectories) but no executable verifier, DPO is more sample-efficient than policy gradients.

#### Framework and Environment Stack
*   **Frameworks:** Start with **SkyRL-v0** (Section 5.2) for long-horizon real-world training or **AgentRL** for asynchronous multi-task training. For multi-agent systems, **MARTI** provides integrated training and inference.
*   **Environments:**
    *   **Beginner:** Use **WebShop** or **ALFWorld** (Section 5.1) for controlled, reproducible benchmarks.
    *   **Advanced:** Use **WebArena** or **AndroidWorld** for realistic, dynamic interaction.
    *   **Code:** Use **SWE-Bench** or **R2E-Gym** for software engineering tasks.
*   **Reproducibility Tip:** Always report the **horizon $T$** and **reward sparsity** (steps per reward) alongside accuracy metrics. A 90% success rate on a 2-step task is not comparable to 90% on a 20-step task (Section 2.1).

### 7.5 Concluding Perspective

This survey establishes that Agentic RL is not merely an incremental improvement over RLHF but a **qualitative shift** in how we build AI systems. By moving from $T=1$ alignment to $T>1$ optimization, we enable LLMs to function as autonomous entities capable of planning, tool use, and self-improvement. However, the path forward is constrained by fundamental challenges in credit assignment, environment scaling, and safety.

The field stands at an inflection point. The next breakthroughs will not come from larger models alone, but from **smarter training paradigms** that treat capabilities as learnable policies and environments as co-evolutionary partners. Researchers who adopt the formalisms, taxonomies, and critical perspectives outlined in this survey will be best positioned to navigate this transition—from building models that *answer* questions to building agents that *solve* problems.
