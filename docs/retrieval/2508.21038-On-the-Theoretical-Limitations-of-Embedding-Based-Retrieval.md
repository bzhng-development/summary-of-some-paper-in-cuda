## 1. Executive Summary

This paper proves that single-vector embedding models face fundamental theoretical limitations governed by embedding dimension $d$ and sign-rank, meaning they cannot represent all possible top-$k$ document combinations regardless of training data. The authors validate this with the `LIMIT` dataset, where state-of-the-art models like `E5-Mistral 7B` and `Gemini Embed` score less than 20% Recall@100 on trivial tasks, whereas sparse models like `BM25` succeed. This demonstrates that dense retrieval cannot scale to arbitrary instruction-following requirements, necessitating a shift toward more expressive architectures like multi-vector models or cross-encoders.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Growing Expectation Gap in Dense Retrieval

Over the last two decades, information retrieval has undergone a fundamental architectural shift. The field moved from sparse techniques like `BM25` [Robertson et al., 1995], which rely on exact term matching, to neural language models that produce dense vector embeddings [Lee et al., 2019, Craswell et al., 2020]. These **dense retrieval** models compress entire queries and documents into single vectors (typically 512 to 4096 dimensions), then compute relevance via dot product similarity. This approach enabled remarkable generalization across domains and became the dominant paradigm for large-scale search systems.

However, the community's expectations for what these models should accomplish have grown far beyond their original design scope. Modern benchmarks now task embedding models with:

- **Instruction-following retrieval**: Representing any relevance definition provided in natural language [Weller et al., 2025a,b, Su et al., 2024]
- **Logical reasoning**: Combining concepts with operators like "OR" (e.g., "Moths or Insects or Arthropods of Guadeloupe") [Malaviya et al., 2023]
- **Complex problem matching**: Finding Leetcode problems that share sub-tasks like dynamic programming [Su et al., 2024]

The implicit assumption underlying these benchmarks is that **any retrieval task can be solved** with sufficiently large models and better training data. As stated in the paper:

> "While prior works have pointed out theoretical limitations of vector embeddings, there is a common assumption that these difficulties are exclusively due to unrealistic queries, and those that are not can be overcome with better training data and larger models."

This paper challenges that assumption directly. It asks: *Are there retrieval tasks that single-vector embeddings fundamentally cannot represent, regardless of training quality or model size?*

### Why This Problem Matters: Both Theoretical and Practical

The significance of this question operates on two levels:

**Theoretical Significance.** Vector embeddings exist in geometric space, which means their representational capacity is governed by well-studied mathematical principles from communication complexity theory and linear algebra [Papadimitriou and Sipser, 1982]. If embedding dimension $d$ imposes a hard ceiling on how many distinct document combinations can be retrieved, this is not an engineering problem—it is a mathematical constraint. Understanding this boundary helps the community distinguish between tasks that are *difficult but solvable* versus tasks that are *impossible under the current paradigm*.

**Real-World Impact.** As retrieval systems are increasingly deployed for instruction-based agents and complex query understanding, practitioners need to know when dense embeddings will fail. The paper notes:

> "As the tasks given to embedding models require returning ever-increasing combinations of top-$k$ relevant documents (e.g., through instructions connecting previously unrelated documents with logical operators), we will reach a limit of combinations they cannot represent."

This has direct implications for system architecture decisions. If a use case requires representing arbitrary combinations of documents (e.g., "find all documents matching condition A OR condition B OR condition C"), a single-vector retriever may be fundamentally unsuitable regardless of how much it is fine-tuned.

### Prior Approaches and Their Shortcomings

**Empirical Benchmarks Push Boundaries Without Explaining Limits.** Datasets like QUEST [Malaviya et al., 2023] and BRIGHT [Su et al., 2024] were designed to stress-test embedding models with complex queries. However, these benchmarks operate empirically—they measure *what models currently fail at* rather than *why they fail*. The paper points out a critical sampling issue:

> "The QUEST dataset has 325k documents and queries with 20 relevant documents per query, with a total of 3357 queries. The number of unique top-20 document sets that could be returned with the QUEST corpus would be $\binom{325k}{20}$ which is equal to 7.1e+91... Thus, the 3k queries in QUEST can only cover an infinitesimally small part of the qrel combination space."

In other words, existing benchmarks test a vanishingly small fraction of possible query-document combinations. A model might pass these benchmarks while still being incapable of representing many valid retrieval patterns.

**Previous Theoretical Work Lacks Practical Bounds.** The mathematical study of nearest-neighbor regions dates back to Voronoi diagrams (formalized in 1908 [Voronoi, 1908]). The order-$k$ Voronoi diagram—which depicts regions where a particular set of $k$ points are the nearest neighbors—is a direct analog to top-$k$ retrieval. However, as the paper notes:

> "Proofs placing a bound on the count of the number of regions in the order-$k$ Voronoi problem are notoriously difficult to bound tightly and do not provide much practical insight for IR [Bohler et al., 2015, Lee, 1982, Chen et al., 2023]."

This theoretical machinery exists but does not translate cleanly to embedding dimension constraints that practitioners can use.

**Prior Empirical Limitation Studies Lack Theoretical Grounding.** Some previous work identified dimension-related issues empirically:
- Reimers and Gurevych [2020] showed smaller dimension models have more false positives with large corpora
- Ormazabal et al. [2019] demonstrated limitations in cross-lingual settings
- Yin and Shen [2018] explored how dimensions relate to bias-variance tradeoffs

However, these works did not connect empirical observations to formal theoretical bounds. They showed *that* limitations exist, but not *why* they must exist or *where* the hard boundary lies.

### How This Paper Positions Itself

This work occupies a unique position by **bridging theoretical bounds with empirical validation** in three distinct ways:

**1. Theoretical Foundation via Sign-Rank.** Rather than using Voronoi geometry, the paper connects embedding capacity to the **sign-rank** of the query-relevance matrix (the "qrel" matrix where entry $A_{ij} = 1$ if document $j$ is relevant to query $i$). The sign-rank provides a lower bound on the embedding dimension needed to represent a given set of retrieval relationships. This draws from communication complexity theory [Hatami et al., 2022, Alon et al., 2014], providing tighter and more interpretable bounds than prior geometric approaches.

**2. Best-Case Empirical Validation.** The paper introduces "free embedding" optimization, where query and document vectors are directly optimized via gradient descent without any natural language constraints. This establishes an **upper bound on what any embedding model could possibly achieve**. If free embeddings cannot solve a task, no real retrieval model (which must encode meaning through language) can solve it either. This separates architectural limitations from training data or optimization issues.

**3. Real-World Instantiation via LIMIT.** The authors create a natural language dataset called `LIMIT` that instantiates the theoretically-hard combinations in a simple, interpretable format (e.g., "Who likes Apples?" where documents describe user preferences). Despite the trivial nature of the queries, state-of-the-art models fail dramatically. This demonstrates that the theoretical limits are not abstract—they manifest in realistic settings.

The paper's positioning is summarized in its three contributions:

> "(1) a theoretical basis for the fundamental limitations of embedding models, (2) a best-case empirical analysis showing that this proof holds for any dataset instantiation (by free embedding optimization), and (3) a simple real-world natural language instantiation called LIMIT that even state-of-the-art embedding models cannot solve."

This positions the work not as another benchmark proposal, but as a **diagnostic framework** for understanding when and why dense retrieval will fail—enabling the community to make informed architecture choices (e.g., cross-encoders, multi-vector models, or sparse methods) for tasks that exceed single-vector capacity.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a theoretical analysis combined with empirical validation to demonstrate fundamental limitations of single-vector embedding models. The core idea is that the embedding dimension $d$ imposes a hard mathematical ceiling on how many distinct top-$k$ document combinations can be represented, regardless of training data quality or model architecture. The authors prove this using sign-rank theory from communication complexity, validate it through direct vector optimization experiments, and instantiate it in a real-world dataset called `LIMIT`.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Type of Paper and Core Idea.** This is a theoretical analysis paper with empirical validation that proves single-vector embedding models cannot represent all possible top-$k$ document combinations when the number of combinations exceeds what the embedding dimension $d$ can encode. The core idea is that retrieval capacity is fundamentally bounded by linear algebra: if you have $m$ queries and $n$ documents, the minimum dimension needed to perfectly separate relevant from non-relevant documents is determined by the sign-rank of the query-relevance matrix, and this dimension can exceed practical embedding sizes even for simple tasks.

**System and Data Pipeline.** The technical pipeline operates in three sequential stages. First, the theoretical framework takes a binary relevance matrix $A \in \{0, 1\}^{m \times n}$ as input and outputs lower and upper bounds on the required embedding dimension using sign-rank analysis. Second, the free embedding optimization takes a target relevance matrix and directly optimizes query vectors $U \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times m}$ and document vectors $V \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times n}$ using gradient descent to find the maximum number of documents $n$ that can be handled for a given dimension $d$. Third, the LIMIT dataset takes the theoretically-determined hard combinations and instantiates them as natural language queries and documents, then evaluates pre-trained embedding models to measure actual performance degradation. What happens first is the mathematical proof establishing the bounds, what happens second is the controlled optimization experiment testing if the bounds are achievable, and what happens third is the real-world evaluation showing state-of-the-art models fail on the instantiated task.

**Formal Definitions of Embedding Capacity.** The paper introduces three distinct but related definitions of the minimum dimension needed for a vector embedding model to correctly retrieve documents. The first is **row-wise order-preserving rank**, denoted $\text{rank}_{\text{rop}} A$, which is defined as the smallest integer $d$ such that there exists a rank-$d$ matrix $B$ that preserves the relative order of entries in each row of $A$. In plain language, this means if document $j$ is more relevant than document $k$ for query $i$ in the ground truth matrix $A$, then the score $B_{ij}$ must be greater than $B_{ik}$ in the embedding score matrix. The second is **row-wise thresholdable rank**, denoted $\text{rank}_{\text{rt}} A$, which is the minimum rank of a matrix $B$ for which there exist row-specific thresholds $\{\tau_i\}_{i=1}^m$ such that $B_{ij} > \tau_i$ if $A_{ij} = 1$ and $B_{ij} &lt; \tau_i$ if $A_{ij} = 0$. This definition is slightly stronger because it requires a clean separation between relevant and non-relevant documents via a threshold, rather than just correct ordering. The third is **globally thresholdable rank**, denoted $\text{rank}_{\text{gt}} A$, which is the minimum rank of a matrix $B$ for which there exists a single threshold $\tau$ (the same for all queries) such that $B_{ij} > \tau$ if $A_{ij} = 1$ and $B_{ij} &lt; \tau$ if $A_{ij} = 0$. These three definitions form a hierarchy of constraints, with globally thresholdable being the most restrictive.

**Sign-Rank and Its Connection to Embedding Dimension.** The paper connects these embedding capacity definitions to **sign-rank**, a well-studied concept from communication complexity theory. The sign-rank of a matrix $M \in \{-1, 1\}^{m \times n}$, denoted $\text{rank}_{\pm} M$, is defined as the smallest integer $d$ such that there exists a rank-$d$ matrix $B \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ whose entries have the same sign as those of $M$. In other words, $\text{sign}(B_{ij}) = M_{ij}$ for all $i, j$. To connect this to binary relevance matrices, the paper transforms $A \in \{0, 1\}^{m \times n}$ to $\{-1, 1\}^{m \times n}$ via the transformation $2A - \mathbf{1}_{m \times n}$, where $\mathbf{1}_{m \times n}$ is an $m \times n$ matrix of ones. The key theoretical result is captured in Proposition 2, which establishes the following inequality chain:

$$\text{rank}_{\pm}(2A - \mathbf{1}_{m \times n}) - 1 \leq \text{rank}_{\text{rop}} A = \text{rank}_{\text{rt}} A \leq \text{rank}_{\text{gt}} A \leq \text{rank}_{\pm}(2A - \mathbf{1}_{m \times n})$$

This inequality has profound practical implications. It means that for any binary relevance matrix $A$, the minimum embedding dimension needed to capture the retrieval relationships exactly is bounded below by $\text{rank}_{\pm}(2A - \mathbf{1}_{m \times n}) - 1$ and bounded above by $\text{rank}_{\pm}(2A - \mathbf{1}_{m \times n})$. Since there exist matrices with arbitrarily high sign-rank, this proves that for any fixed embedding dimension $d$, there exists a retrieval task that cannot be solved by $d$-dimensional embeddings.

**Why Sign-Rank Over Voronoi Geometry.** The paper explicitly chooses sign-rank analysis over the more intuitive order-$k$ Voronoi diagram approach for two critical reasons. First, while Voronoi regions directly correspond to top-$k$ retrieval sets (each region represents the set of points where a particular $k$ documents are the nearest neighbors), proofs placing tight bounds on the number of Voronoi regions are notoriously difficult and do not provide practical insight for information retrieval systems. Second, sign-rank has well-established lower bound techniques from communication complexity theory, with known results for specific matrix families that can be directly applied. The paper cites prior work that has already proven sign-rank bounds for various matrix structures [Hatami et al., 2022, Alon et al., 2014, Chierichetti et al., 2017], allowing the authors to leverage existing mathematical machinery rather than deriving new bounds from scratch.

**Free Embedding Optimization Experimental Design.** To empirically validate the theoretical bounds, the paper designs a "best-case" optimization experiment where the vectors themselves are directly optimizable via gradient descent, without any natural language encoding constraints. The experiment creates a random document matrix of size $n$ and a random query matrix with all possible top-$k$ combinations (of size $m = \binom{n}{k}$), both initialized as unit vectors. The optimization uses the Adam optimizer [Kingma and Ba, 2014] with a learning rate that is not explicitly specified in the main text but is standard for such experiments. Each gradient update processes a full batch containing all correct query-document triples from the dataset, using the InfoNCE loss function [Oord et al., 2018]. The InfoNCE loss is defined as:

$$L_{\text{total}} = -\frac{1}{M} \sum_{i=1}^{M} \log \frac{\sum_{d_r \in R_i} \exp(\text{sim}(q_i, d_r)/\tau)}{\sum_{d_k \in D} \exp(\text{sim}(q_i, d_k)/\tau)}$$

where $d_r$ are the relevant documents for query $q_i$, $d_k$ are all documents in the batch (serving as negatives), and $\tau$ is a temperature parameter. The paper notes in footnote 7 that InfoNCE performed best in preliminary experiments, beating both MSE and Margin losses. After each gradient update, vectors are normalized to unit length, matching the practice of nearly all production embedding models. Early stopping is triggered when there is no improvement in the loss for 1000 iterations.

**Critical-n Determination and Polynomial Fitting.** The experiment systematically increases the number of documents $n$ (and thus the combinatorial number of queries $m = \binom{n}{k}$) until the optimization can no longer achieve 100% accuracy on the retrieval task. This failure point is called the **critical-n** value for a given embedding dimension $d$. The paper focuses on $k=2$ (each query has exactly 2 relevant documents) and increases $n$ by one for each $d$ value until optimization breaks. Due to the combinatorial explosion of combinations with larger document values (e.g., 50k documents with top-$k$ of 100 gives $7.7 \times 10^{311}$ combinations), the experiments use relatively small sizes for $n$, $k$, and $d$. The critical-n values are then fit to a polynomial regression line to enable extrapolation to larger dimensions. The fitted 3rd degree polynomial has the formula:

$$y = -10.5322 + 4.0309d + 0.0520d^2 + 0.0037d^3$$

with an $r^2$ value of 0.999, indicating an excellent fit. Using this formula, the paper extrapolates critical-n values for common embedding dimensions: 500k documents for $d=512$, 1.7m documents for $d=768$, 4m documents for $d=1024$, 107m documents for $d=3072$, and 250m documents for $d=4096$. These numbers represent the absolute best case, as real embedding models cannot directly optimize vectors to match the test qrel matrix and are constrained by the need to encode natural language meaning.

**LIMIT Dataset Construction Methodology.** The LIMIT dataset instantiates the theoretically-hard combinations as a natural language retrieval task. The construction process begins with the decision to use 50k documents and 1000 queries, with $k=2$ relevant documents per query (mirroring datasets like Natural Questions and HotpotQA). To map combinations to natural language, the authors use a "user preferences" schema where documents describe what attributes a person likes (e.g., "Jon Durben likes Quokkas and Apples"). This schema was chosen because preferences are plentiful, independent (one can like Hawaiian pizza but dislike pepperoni), and all combinations are semantically valid. The attribute list was generated by prompting Gemini 2.5 Pro and then cleaned to 1850 unique items by iteratively removing duplicates and hypernyms, while also checking top BM25 failures to ensure no lexical overlap that would make the task trivial. The qrel matrix is chosen to maximize difficulty: the authors select 46 documents because $\binom{46}{2} = 1035$, which is the smallest number of documents that produces more than 1000 unique top-2 combinations (matching the 1000 query budget). Each query is assigned a random natural language attribute, and this attribute is added to the two relevant documents for that query. Each document is given a random first and last name from open-source lists, and additional random attributes are sampled until all documents have the same number of attributes. A "small" version of LIMIT is also created with only the 46 relevant documents (removing the 49.95k non-relevant documents) to test whether the failure is due to corpus size or the combination structure itself.

**Model Evaluation Configuration.** The paper evaluates state-of-the-art embedding models spanning a range of dimensions and training styles. The evaluated single-vector models include: GritLM (7B parameters, dimensions not explicitly specified in main text but typically 1024), Qwen3 Embeddings (dimensions up to 4096), Promptriever Llama3 8B (dimensions up to 4096), Gemini Embeddings (dimensions up to 3072), Snowflake Arctic Embed Large v2.0 (dimensions up to 1024), and E5-Mistral Instruct (7B parameters, dimensions up to 1024). The paper also evaluates three non-single-vector models for comparison: BM25 (sparse lexical retrieval), GTE-ModernColBERT (multi-vector model using MaxSim operator), and a token-wise TF-IDF baseline (which achieves 100% on all tasks by reverse-engineering the dataset construction). Models are evaluated at their full embedding dimension and also with truncated dimensions (typically 32 to 4096) to show the impact of dimensionality. For models not trained with Matryoshka Representation Learning (MRL) [Kusupati et al., 2022], truncation results in sub-par scores, so MRL-trained models are indicated with stars in the result plots. The evaluation metrics are Recall@2, Recall@10, Recall@20, and Recall@100, measuring whether the relevant documents appear in the top-$k$ retrieved results.

**Ablation Study: Training on LIMIT.** To test whether the poor performance is due to domain shift (i.e., models not seeing similar queries during pre-training), the paper fine-tunes an off-the-shelf embedding model (lightonai/modernbert-embed-large) on either a training set of similar examples or the official LIMIT test set. The fine-tuning uses the SentenceTransformers library with MultipleNegativesRankingLoss, a full dataset batch size, and a no-duplicates sampler to ensure in-batch negatives are not duplicates of positive documents. The learning rate is 5e-5, training runs for 5 epochs, and the training set is limited to 2k examples (matching the test set size, reduced from 2.5k). The model projects the hidden layer down to specified dimensions (32 to 1024) during training rather than using MRL. Results show that training on the training set provides minimal improvement (near zero Recall@10 to at most 2.8 Recall@10), while training on the test set allows the model to overfit to the specific tokens in the test queries. This confirms the issue is not domain shift but intrinsic task difficulty. Notably, even with 64 dimensions, the real embedding model cannot completely solve the $N=46$ version, whereas free embeddings can solve it with only 12 dimensions, indicating real models are multiple times more limited than the theoretical best case.

**Ablation Study: Qrel Pattern Variations.** To confirm that the difficulty comes from testing many combinations (high qrel matrix density) rather than other factors, the paper instantiates LIMIT with four different qrel patterns. The **random** pattern samples queries randomly from all possible combinations. The **cycle** pattern creates a cycle-based setup where the next query is relevant to one document from the previous query and the next new document. The **disjoint** pattern ensures each query is relevant to two entirely new documents (no overlap). The **dense** pattern (the standard LIMIT setup) maximizes the number of connections by using $\binom{n}{k}$ for the largest $n$ that fits in the query budget. All configurations use the same setup: 50k documents, 1k queries, $k=2$. Results show that all patterns except dense have relatively similar performance across models. However, the dense pattern shows strikingly lower scores: GritLM drops 50 absolute points in Recall@100, and E5-Mistral has an almost 10x reduction (40.4 vs 4.8 Recall@100). This confirms that testing more combinations is the key factor driving failure, not other dataset characteristics.

**Why This Approach Over Alternatives.** The paper's three-pronged approach (theory, free optimization, real dataset) is designed to rule out alternative explanations for model failure. The theoretical proof establishes that limitations must exist mathematically, not just empirically. The free embedding optimization shows these limitations hold even with unlimited training freedom and direct vector access, ruling out optimization or training data issues. The LIMIT dataset shows the limitations manifest in realistic natural language settings, ruling out the claim that the theory only applies to artificial constructs. This layered validation is stronger than any single approach alone: theory without empirical validation could be dismissed as impractical, empirical results without theory could be dismissed as fixable with better training, and artificial datasets without natural language instantiation could be dismissed as irrelevant to real use cases.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper does not merely propose a new benchmark; it establishes a diagnostic framework for understanding the hard limits of dense retrieval. The following innovations distinguish this work from prior empirical studies and theoretical analyses, shifting the community's focus from "how to train better" to "what is representable."

### 1. Algebraic Bounds Replace Geometric Intuition
Prior theoretical work on retrieval limits relied on **order-$k$ Voronoi diagrams** to model the regions of space where a specific set of $k$ documents are the nearest neighbors (Section 2.3). While geometrically intuitive, these diagrams are notoriously difficult to bound tightly for high-dimensional spaces, offering little practical guidance for model design [Bohler et al., 2015, Lee, 1982].

This paper innovates by shifting the theoretical lens from geometry to **algebra via sign-rank**. By connecting the query-relevance matrix to communication complexity theory, the authors derive a concrete inequality chain (Section 3.2) that bounds the required embedding dimension $d$:

$$\text{rank}_{\pm}(2A - \mathbf{1}_{m \times n}) - 1 \leq \text{rank}_{\text{rop}} A \leq \text{rank}_{\pm}(2A - \mathbf{1}_{m \times n})$$

**Why this is significant:**
*   **Actionable Extrapolation:** Unlike Voronoi bounds, the sign-rank approach allows the authors to fit a polynomial regression to empirical data (Figure 2). This enables them to extrapolate limits for realistic embedding dimensions (e.g., predicting failure at 1.7m documents for $d=768$), providing practitioners with a quantitative tool to estimate capacity.
*   **Universality:** The bound applies to *any* matrix $A$, meaning it covers all possible retrieval tasks, not just specific geometric configurations. This proves the limitation is fundamental to the vector space itself, not an artifact of specific data distributions.

### 2. Free Embedding Optimization as an Architectural Diagnostic
Standard empirical evaluations test pre-trained models, conflating **architectural limits** (can the geometry hold this structure?) with **training limits** (did the model see enough data?). Prior limitation studies [Reimers and Gurevych, 2020] observed performance drops but could not definitively attribute them to dimensionality constraints versus optimization failures.

This paper introduces **free embedding optimization** (Section 4) as a methodological innovation. By directly optimizing query and document vectors via gradient descent without language modeling constraints, the authors establish a **theoretical upper bound** on performance for a given dimension $d$.

**Why this is significant:**
*   **Isolating the Variable:** If free embeddings fail to solve a task, no real model (which faces additional constraints from language encoding) can solve it. This conclusively proves that failures on the `LIMIT` dataset are due to geometric capacity, not lack of pre-training data.
*   **Quantifying the Gap:** The results show real models are "multiple times more limited than free-embeddings" (Section 5.3). For example, free embeddings solve the $N=46$ case with 12 dimensions, while real models struggle even with 64 dimensions. This quantifies the "tax" imposed by natural language encoding on top of the geometric limit.

### 3. Combinatorial Density vs. Semantic Complexity
Existing stress-test benchmarks like QUEST [Malaviya et al., 2023] or BRIGHT [Su et al., 2024] focus on **semantic complexity** (e.g., reasoning, logical operators). The implicit assumption is that models fail because the queries are too hard to understand.

This paper reveals that **combinatorial density** is a distinct and orthogonal failure mode. The `LIMIT` dataset uses trivially simple queries (e.g., "Who likes Apples?") but constructs a query-relevance graph with exceptionally high density. Table 1 shows `LIMIT` has a **Graph Density** of 0.085481, compared to 0.000037 for HotpotQA and 0.025641 for FollowIR Core17.

**Why this is significant:**
*   **Ruling Out Semantics:** Figure 6 demonstrates that when the qrel pattern is changed from "dense" to "disjoint" or "cycle" (reducing the number of combinations tested), performance recovers significantly (e.g., E5-Mistral jumps from 4.8 to 36.6 Recall@100). This proves the failure is driven by the number of connections, not the semantic difficulty of the query.
*   **Blind Spot in Current Benchmarks:** Figure 7 shows no correlation between BEIR scores and `LIMIT` performance. A model can be state-of-the-art on standard benchmarks while being fundamentally incapable of handling high-density retrieval tasks. This warns the community that high MTEB scores do not guarantee robustness against combinatorial explosion.

### 4. Architectural Escape Hatches (Sparse and Multi-Vector)
Many papers identifying limitations stop at the diagnosis. This paper provides a clear **architectural path forward** by evaluating alternative retrieval paradigms in Section 5.6. It demonstrates that the limitation is specific to the **single-vector paradigm**, not retrieval as a whole.

**Why this is significant:**
*   **Sparse Models Work:** `BM25` achieves near-perfect scores on `LIMIT` (Figure 3) because its effective dimensionality equals the vocabulary size (often tens of thousands), far exceeding the 1024–4096 dimensions of dense models. This validates the theoretical claim that dimension $d$ is the bottleneck.
*   **Multi-Vector Viability:** `GTE-ModernColBERT` significantly outperforms single-vector models (e.g., 23.1 vs 1.3 Recall@2 on full `LIMIT` at default dimensions, Table 5) by using multiple vectors per document. This suggests that **late interaction** mechanisms can bypass the single-vector sign-rank bound, offering a concrete recommendation for system designers facing complex instruction-following requirements.
*   **Cross-Encoder Confirmation:** The paper notes that a cross-encoder (Gemini-2.5-Pro) solves the small `LIMIT` task with 100% accuracy. This reinforces that the limitation is in the **representation** (compressing to one vector), not the **scoring** function.

Together, these innovations move the field beyond empirical trial-and-error. They provide a mathematical basis for predicting failure, a diagnostic tool for isolating causes, and a clear set of architectural alternatives for tasks that exceed single-vector capacity.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section evaluates the empirical evidence provided to support the paper's central claim: that single-vector embedding models face fundamental capacity limits dictated by embedding dimension $d$ and sign-rank, independent of training data quality. The experiments are structured in three tiers of increasing realism: (1) **Free Embedding Optimization** (theoretical best-case), (2) **LIMIT Dataset Evaluation** (realistic models on synthetic tasks), and (3) **Ablation Studies** (ruling out alternative explanations). We analyze whether the quantitative results convincingly isolate dimensionality as the bottleneck.

### 5.1 Evaluation Strategy Overview

The experimental design prioritizes **isolating variables** to distinguish between architectural limits and training deficiencies. As detailed in Section 3, the methodology employs a tiered approach:

*   **Tier 1 (Best-Case):** Directly optimizing vectors via gradient descent without language constraints. This establishes an upper bound on what is geometrically possible for a given dimension $d$.
*   **Tier 2 (Real-World):** Evaluating pre-trained state-of-the-art (SOTA) models on the `LIMIT` dataset. This tests if theoretical limits manifest in practice.
*   **Tier 3 (Control):** Ablation studies varying training data (in-domain vs. out-of-domain) and query-relevance (qrel) patterns (dense vs. sparse). This rules out domain shift or semantic complexity as primary failure causes.

This structure is critical because standard benchmarks (e.g., MTEB) conflate these factors. By separating them, the paper attempts to prove that failure persists even when training data is perfect (Tier 1) or semantics are trivial (Tier 2).

### 5.2 Validating the Theoretical Bound (Free Embedding)

The first claim to verify is whether embedding dimension $d$ strictly limits the number of representable document combinations. The **Free Embedding** experiment (Section 4) provides the cleanest test of this hypothesis.

**Critical-n Scaling.** The authors determine the **critical-n** value: the maximum number of documents $n$ for which all $\binom{n}{2}$ top-2 combinations can be perfectly retrieved for a given dimension $d$. The results in **Figure 2** and **Table 6** show a clear polynomial relationship. The fitted curve is:

$$y = -10.5322 + 4.0309d + 0.0520d^2 + 0.0037d^3$$

with an $r^2=0.999$, indicating an extremely tight fit. This empirical validation supports the theoretical sign-rank bounds derived in Section 3.2.

**Extrapolated Limits.** Using this formula, the paper extrapolates capacity limits for common embedding dimensions:
*   $d=512$: ~500k documents
*   $d=768$: ~1.7m documents
*   $d=1024$: ~4m documents
*   $d=3072$: ~107m documents
*   $d=4096$: ~250m documents

**Analysis:** These numbers represent the **absolute ceiling**. If free vectors cannot represent more combinations than this, no neural model can. The fact that the curve grows polynomially rather than exponentially suggests that doubling the dimension does not double the capacity for combinations; it increases it by a factor governed by the cubic term. This validates the claim that web-scale retrieval (billions of documents) with arbitrary combination requirements may exceed the capacity of even large embedding dimensions (e.g., 4096).

### 5.3 Real-Model Performance on LIMIT

The second claim is that SOTA models fail on tasks that exceed this capacity, even when the semantics are trivial. The `LIMIT` dataset results (**Figure 3**, **Figure 4**, **Table 5**, **Table 4**) provide the evidence.

**Full Corpus Performance (50k Documents).**
In the full `LIMIT` setting, models perform poorly despite the queries being simple natural language (e.g., "Who likes Apples?").
*   **Single-Vector Models:** Even the largest models struggle. For instance, `E5-Mistral 7B` at 4096 dimensions achieves only **8.3% Recall@100** (**Table 5**). `Gemini Embed` at 3072 dimensions scores **10.0% Recall@100**.
*   **Dimensionality Dependence:** Performance scales with dimension, confirming the theoretical bottleneck. `E5-Mistral` improves from **0.5% Recall@100** at 32 dimensions to **8.3%** at 4096 dimensions. However, even the highest dimension is insufficient for near-perfect retrieval.
*   **Sparse/Multi-Vector Success:** In contrast, `BM25` achieves **93.6% Recall@100**, and `GTE-ModernColBERT` (multi-vector) achieves **54.8% Recall@100** (**Table 5**). This divergence isolates the failure to the **single-vector representation**, not the retrieval task itself.

**Small Corpus Performance (46 Documents).**
To rule out corpus size as the confounder, a "Small" version with only the 46 relevant documents was tested (**Figure 4**, **Table 4**).
*   **Persistent Failure:** Models still fail to perfectly solve the task. `E5-Mistral 7B` at 4096 dimensions reaches only **85.2% Recall@20**. Since there are only 46 documents, Recall@20 should be trivial if the combinations were representable.
*   **Comparison to Free Embeddings:** Free embeddings can solve the $N=46$ case with only **12 dimensions** (Section 5.3 text). Real models require significantly higher dimensions and still underperform. This indicates that **natural language encoding imposes an additional "tax"** on top of the geometric limit, reducing effective capacity further.

**Analysis:** The results convincingly show that single-vector models cannot handle high-density qrel matrices. The success of `BM25` (high dimensionality via vocabulary) and `ColBERT` (multiple vectors per document) confirms that increasing representational capacity resolves the issue, supporting the hypothesis that dimension $d$ is the primary constraint.

### 5.4 Ruling Out Confounding Factors (Ablations)

A critical part of experimental analysis is ensuring the observed failure is due to the proposed cause (dimensionality) and not artifacts (domain shift, semantic difficulty). The paper provides three key ablations.

**1. Domain Shift vs. Intrinsic Difficulty.**
To test if models fail because they haven't seen similar queries during pre-training, the authors fine-tuned `modernbert-embed-large` on either a `LIMIT` training set or the `LIMIT` test set (**Figure 5**, **Table 2**).
*   **Train on Train:** Performance remains near zero. At 1024 dimensions, Recall@2 is only **1.0%**.
*   **Train on Test:** Performance jumps significantly. At 1024 dimensions, Recall@2 reaches **96.5%**.
*   **Implication:** The task is learnable (proving it's not semantically impossible), but models cannot generalize the combination logic to unseen queries. This supports the claim that the limitation is **representational capacity**, not optimization difficulty. If the model could generalize, training on the train set would help; instead, it must overfit to the test set tokens.

**2. Qrel Pattern Density.**
To confirm that **combinatorial density** drives failure (rather than random noise), the authors tested four qrel patterns: Random, Dense, Cycle, and Disjoint (**Figure 6**, **Table 3**).
*   **Dense Pattern:** This is the standard `LIMIT` setup (maximizing combinations). `E5-Mistral` scores **4.8% Recall@100** at 4096 dimensions.
*   **Other Patterns:** Performance recovers significantly when density is reduced. `E5-Mistral` scores **40.4% (Random)**, **36.6% (Cycle)**, and **38.8% (Disjoint)** at 4096 dimensions.
*   **Implication:** The drop from ~40% to ~5% solely due to changing the connection pattern proves that **graph density** is the failure mode. This validates the theoretical focus on sign-rank (which measures matrix complexity) rather than semantic complexity.

**3. Correlation with Standard Benchmarks.**
The paper compares `LIMIT` performance against BEIR (a standard IR benchmark used in MTEB) (**Figure 7**, **Table 7**).
*   **No Correlation:** Models with high BEIR scores (e.g., `Qwen3 Embed` at 62.76) perform poorly on `LIMIT` (4.8% Recall@100). `Promptriever` scores lower on BEIR (56.40) but higher on `LIMIT` (18.9% Recall@100).
*   **Implication:** High performance on existing benchmarks does not guarantee robustness against combinatorial limits. This suggests current evaluations may be under-testing the representational capacity of models, focusing on semantic matching rather than combination handling.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Design

While the experiments strongly support the paper's claims, several limitations and conditions warrant attention.

**Strengths:**
*   **Isolation of Variables:** The use of free embedding optimization is a particularly strong design choice. It decouples geometric capacity from language modeling, providing a clean upper bound that is rarely established in IR literature.
*   **Controlled Complexity:** The `LIMIT` dataset uses trivial semantics ("Who likes X?") to ensure failure is not attributed to reasoning deficits. This forces the conclusion back to architectural limits.
*   **Architectural Comparison:** Including `BM25` and `ColBERT` provides a necessary counterfactual. If all models failed, the task might be impossible. Since sparse/multi-vector models succeed, the single-vector paradigm is correctly identified as the bottleneck.

**Limitations and Conditions:**
*   **Top-$k$ Constraint:** The experiments focus primarily on **$k=2$** (Section 4). While the theory holds for any $k$, the empirical extrapolation relies on $k=2$ data. Real-world retrieval often requires $k=10$ or $k=100$. The combinatorial explosion for higher $k$ is significantly worse ($\binom{n}{k}$ grows rapidly), suggesting the actual limits might be even stricter than reported.
*   **Synthetic Data:** The `LIMIT` dataset is synthetically constructed. While the natural language instantiation is realistic, the underlying qrel matrix is designed to be maximally difficult (dense). Real-world user queries may naturally cluster in ways that reduce effective sign-rank. The paper acknowledges this in Section 6 ("Limitations"), noting they cannot prove *which* combinations models will fail on, only that *some* exist.
*   **Scale of Free Embedding:** The free embedding experiments are limited to small $n$ (up to ~600 documents for high $d$, **Table 6**) due to combinatorial explosion. The extrapolation to millions of documents relies on the polynomial fit. While $r^2=0.999$ is compelling, extrapolating beyond the observed data range always carries risk.
*   **Training Compute:** The fine-tuning experiments (Section 5.3) use a specific model (`modernbert-embed-large`) and limited epochs (5). It is possible that more extensive training regimes could slightly improve generalization, though the theoretical bound suggests they cannot overcome the dimension limit entirely.

**Conclusion on Evidence:**
The experiments convincingly demonstrate that **single-vector embeddings have a hard capacity limit** governed by dimension $d$. The divergence between free embeddings (theoretical max) and real models (actual performance), combined with the success of alternative architectures (`BM25`, `ColBERT`), isolates the single-vector constraint as the root cause. While the exact failure point in real-world scenarios may vary due to data distribution, the existence of the limit is empirically validated.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding embedding capacity, it operates within specific boundaries. Recognizing these limitations is crucial for practitioners who might otherwise overgeneralize the results. The constraints fall into three categories: **architectural scope** (what models are covered), **theoretical strictness** (what kinds of retrieval are analyzed), and **practical trade-offs** (what alternatives exist and their costs).

### 6.1 Architectural Scope: Single-Vector Focus

The primary limitation of the theoretical proof is its focus on the **single-vector paradigm**. The sign-rank bounds derived in Section 3.2 apply specifically to models where each query and document is represented by a single vector $u_i, v_j \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and relevance is scored via dot product $u_i^T v_j$.

*   **Multi-Vector Models:** The paper explicitly states in Section 6 ("Limitations") that their results "do not hold necessarily for other architectures, such as multi-vector models." Empirical results in Section 5.6 show that `GTE-ModernColBERT` (a multi-vector model using the MaxSim operator) significantly outperforms single-vector models on `LIMIT` (e.g., **23.1% Recall@2** vs **1.3% Recall@2** for best single-vector models in **Table 5**). However, the authors note that multi-vector techniques are "not generally used for instruction-following or reasoning-based tasks," leaving it an open question whether they can fully resolve the limitation for complex queries.
*   **Sparse Models:** Sparse models like `BM25` avoid the dimension bottleneck because their effective dimensionality equals the vocabulary size (often tens of thousands), far exceeding the $d=1024$ to $d=4096$ of dense models. However, Section 5.6 highlights a functional trade-off: "it is less clear how to apply sparse models to instruction-following and reasoning-based tasks where there is no lexical or even paraphrase-like overlap." Thus, while sparse models pass the `LIMIT` test, they may fail on semantic reasoning tasks that dense models handle well.

### 6.2 Theoretical Strictness: Perfect vs. Approximate Retrieval

The theoretical bounds assume a requirement for **exact representation** of the relevance matrix. This creates a gap between the theory and practical deployment scenarios where approximate retrieval is acceptable.

*   **Zero-Error Assumption:** The sign-rank bounds determine the dimension needed to perfectly separate relevant from non-relevant documents (Section 3.2). The paper acknowledges in Section 6 ("Limitations") that they "did not show theoretical results for the setting where the user allows some mistakes, e.g. capturing only the majority of the combinations." In practice, retrieval systems often tolerate some error if the most relevant documents are found. The paper invites readers to examine works like Ben-David et al. [2002] for bounds on approximate scenarios, but does not provide them itself.
*   **Unpredictable Failure Modes:** While the paper proves that *some* combinations cannot be represented, it cannot predict *which* ones. As stated in Section 6: "we cannot prove apriori which types of combinations they will fail on." This means a model might perform perfectly on a specific real-world dataset simply because that dataset happens to avoid the high-sign-rank combinations, even if the dataset is large. This makes it difficult for practitioners to diagnose failure without explicit stress testing like `LIMIT`.

### 6.3 Experimental Constraints and Extrapolation

The empirical validation, while robust, relies on extrapolation due to computational constraints. Understanding the scale of these experiments is vital for interpreting the results.

*   **Combinatorial Explosion:** The "free embedding" experiments (Section 4) must test all combinations of top-$k$ documents. The paper notes that "50k docs with top-$k$ of 100 gives $7.7 \times 10^{311}$ combinations," which is computationally infeasible. Consequently, experiments focus on relatively small sizes for $n$ (documents) and $k=2$ (relevant documents per query).
*   **Polynomial Extrapolation:** To estimate limits for web-scale corpora, the authors fit a 3rd-degree polynomial to the critical-$n$ values (Figure 2):
    $$y = -10.5322 + 4.0309d + 0.0520d^2 + 0.0037d^3$$
    While the fit is excellent ($r^2=0.999$), extrapolating from $n \approx 600$ (Table 6) to $n \approx 1.7 \text{ million}$ (for $d=768$) carries inherent risk. The true capacity curve could deviate at larger scales, though the theoretical bound suggests the polynomial trend is grounded in sign-rank properties.
*   **Fixed $k=2$:** The `LIMIT` dataset and free embedding experiments primarily use $k=2$ (Section 5.2). Real-world retrieval often requires $k=10$ or $k=100$. Since the number of combinations grows as $\binom{n}{k}$, the capacity limits would be reached much faster for higher $k$. The paper does not empirically validate the bounds for $k > 2$, though the theory implies the constraints would be stricter.

### 6.4 Trade-offs of Proposed Alternatives

The paper suggests alternative architectures to bypass single-vector limits, but each introduces significant trade-offs that practitioners must weigh.

*   **Cross-Encoders (High Accuracy, High Cost):** The paper shows that a cross-encoder (`Gemini-2.5-Pro`) solves the small `LIMIT` task with **100% accuracy** (Section 5.6). However, cross-encoders compute attention between every query-document pair, making them "more computationally expensive than embedding models and thus cannot be used for first stage retrieval when there are large numbers of documents." They are viable for reranking top-100 results, but not for scanning millions of documents.
*   **Multi-Vector Models (Medium Cost, Untested for Instructions):** Models like `ColBERT` store multiple vectors per document, increasing storage and compute requirements compared to single-vector models. Additionally, as noted in Section 5.6, their effectiveness for "instruction-following or reasoning-based tasks" remains an open question, as most current implementations focus on semantic search rather than complex logic.
*   **Fine-Tuning Limits:** The ablation study in Section 5.3 shows that fine-tuning on the `LIMIT` training set provides minimal improvement (e.g., **1.0% Recall@2** at 1024 dimensions, **Table 2**). This suggests that simply training longer or on more data will not resolve the fundamental capacity issue. The paper used specific training constraints (5 epochs, learning rate 5e-5, Appendix 9); while more extensive training might yield marginal gains, the theoretical bound suggests it cannot overcome the dimension limit entirely.

### 6.5 Summary of Open Questions

The paper concludes by highlighting several areas requiring future research, which serve as a roadmap for understanding where the current analysis ends:

*   **Extending Theory to Multi-Vector:** Can sign-rank bounds be adapted for late-interaction models where relevance is $\max_{i,j} u_i^T v_j$ rather than a single dot product?
*   **Approximate Bounds:** What are the theoretical limits when allowing for $\epsilon$-error in retrieval? This would provide a more practical bound for real-world systems.
*   **Instruction-Following Transfer:** Can multi-vector models be successfully trained for instruction-following tasks without losing their capacity advantages?
*   **Sparse Semantic Models:** How can sparse models be adapted to handle semantic reasoning where lexical overlap is minimal?

By acknowledging these limitations, the paper avoids claiming that dense retrieval is "broken," but rather that it is **bounded**. For tasks within those bounds (low combinatorial density), dense embeddings remain efficient and effective. For tasks exceeding them (high-density instruction following), the community must adopt more expressive architectures despite their higher costs.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than identify a limitation—it reframes how the community should think about retrieval architecture selection. The implications extend across theoretical research, benchmark design, and practical system deployment. This section outlines how the work changes the field's landscape, what research directions it opens, and how practitioners should integrate these insights into real-world systems.

### 7.1 Shifting the Field's Mental Model

The most significant contribution of this work is a **paradigm shift** in how we evaluate and select retrieval architectures. Prior to this paper, the dominant assumption was:

> "If a model fails on a retrieval task, the solution is more training data, better instructions, or larger models."

This paper replaces that assumption with a more nuanced understanding:

> "Some retrieval tasks are **geometrically impossible** for single-vector embeddings, regardless of training quality."

This shift has three concrete consequences for the field:

**1. From Empirical to Theoretical Benchmarking.** Current benchmarks like MTEB and BEIR measure *what models currently achieve* on specific query sets. This paper demonstrates that high scores on these benchmarks do not guarantee robustness against combinatorial complexity. Figure 7 shows **no correlation** between BEIR scores and LIMIT performance—`Qwen3 Embed` scores 62.76 on BEIR but only 4.8% Recall@100 on LIMIT. Future benchmarks should incorporate **combinatorial density metrics** (like the Graph Density and Average Query Strength from Table 1) to stress-test representational capacity, not just semantic matching.

**2. Architecture Selection Becomes a Capacity Question.** System designers can no longer assume dense embeddings are universally applicable. The polynomial extrapolation from Figure 2 provides a quantitative tool:

$$y = -10.5322 + 4.0309d + 0.0520d^2 + 0.0037d^3$$

For a given embedding dimension $d$, practitioners can estimate the maximum number of documents $n$ for which all top-$k$ combinations are representable. If the use case exceeds this bound (e.g., instruction-following agents that dynamically combine documents with logical operators), alternative architectures become necessary.

**3. Training Diminishing Returns Are Explained.** The fine-tuning ablation in Section 5.3 shows that training on in-domain LIMIT data provides minimal improvement (1.0% Recall@2 at 1024 dimensions, Table 2), while training on the test set allows overfitting (96.5% Recall@2). This explains why some retrieval tasks resist improvement despite extensive fine-tuning: the limitation is **representational**, not optimization-based. This saves practitioners from wasting compute on doomed training runs.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions in Section 6 ("Limitations"), but the work enables additional research trajectories beyond those stated.

**Extending Sign-Rank Theory to Alternative Architectures.** The current bounds apply only to single-vector dot-product scoring. Key open questions include:
*   **Multi-Vector Models:** Can sign-rank bounds be adapted for late-interaction models where relevance is computed as $\max_{i,j} u_i^T v_j$ (as in ColBERT)? The empirical results show `GTE-ModernColBERT` achieves 23.1% Recall@2 on full LIMIT versus 1.3% for best single-vector models (Table 5), but no theoretical framework explains this gap.
*   **Sparse Models:** What are the capacity bounds when dimensionality equals vocabulary size (tens of thousands)? BM25 achieves 93.6% Recall@100 on LIMIT (Table 5), suggesting sparse representations bypass the dense bottleneck, but the theoretical characterization remains open.
*   **Cross-Encoders:** Since cross-encoders solve the small LIMIT task with 100% accuracy (Section 5.6), what is their effective "dimension" in terms of representational capacity? This could inform when to deploy cross-encoders versus bi-encoders.

**Approximate Retrieval Bounds.** The current theory assumes **zero-error** retrieval—all relevant documents must be ranked above all non-relevant ones. Practical systems tolerate some errors. Follow-up work should address:
*   What dimension $d$ is needed to capture 90% of combinations correctly?
*   Can we bound the **expected error rate** for a given $d$ and sign-rank?
*   The paper cites Ben-David et al. [2002] as a starting point, but IR-specific bounds would be more actionable.

**Dynamic Capacity Estimation.** The paper provides static bounds, but real retrieval systems face **dynamic query distributions**. Research directions include:
*   Can we estimate the sign-rank of a query stream online and trigger architecture switches when capacity is exceeded?
*   What features of a query (e.g., number of logical operators, document interconnections) predict sign-rank without computing it explicitly?
*   Could hybrid systems use single-vector retrieval for low-sign-rank queries and fall back to multi-vector for high-sign-rank queries?

**Training Methods for Dimension Utilization.** The gap between free embeddings (12 dimensions for $N=46$) and real models (struggling at 64+ dimensions, Section 5.3) suggests current training methods underutilize available capacity. Prompts for investigation:
*   Do instruction-tuned models (like Promptriever, which performs better on LIMIT) use embedding dimensions more efficiently?
*   Can regularization techniques encourage models to spread information across all dimensions rather than collapsing into lower-dimensional manifolds?
*   Does Matryoshka Representation Learning (MRL) help or hurt capacity? The paper notes MRL-trained models are marked with stars in Figure 3, but does not analyze whether MRL affects LIMIT performance systematically.

**Benchmark Design Reform.** The LIMIT dataset itself is a prototype for a new class of benchmarks. Future work should:
*   Create **combinatorial stress tests** for other modalities (image retrieval, audio retrieval, multi-modal retrieval).
*   Develop **sign-rank estimation tools** that practitioners can run on their own qrel matrices before deployment.
*   Integrate combinatorial density metrics into MTEB or similar evaluation frameworks to prevent models from being rated highly while having fundamental capacity gaps.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and System Design

The theoretical insights from this paper translate directly into actionable guidance for building retrieval systems. The following recommendations help practitioners avoid architectural mismatches.

**When to Use Single-Vector Embeddings.** Single-vector models remain appropriate when:
*   **Combinatorial density is low:** Queries retrieve documents independently (e.g., "find documents about topic X" without logical operators combining topics). The disjoint and cycle qrel patterns in Figure 6 show ~10x better performance than dense patterns.
*   **Efficiency is critical:** First-stage retrieval over millions of documents requires the speed of approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search, which single-vector models support natively.
*   **Semantic matching dominates:** Tasks where paraphrase detection and semantic similarity are the primary relevance signals (e.g., duplicate question detection, semantic search).
*   **Embedding dimension is sufficient:** Use the polynomial formula to estimate capacity. For a corpus of 10M documents with arbitrary top-2 combinations, even $d=4096$ may be insufficient (extrapolated critical-n is 250M for $d=4096$, but this assumes all combinations must be representable).

**When to Use Multi-Vector or Sparse Models.** Alternative architectures become necessary when:
*   **Instruction-following is required:** Queries that dynamically combine concepts (e.g., "documents about X OR Y but not Z") create high-sign-rank qrel matrices. Table 3 shows multi-vector models (GTE-ModernColBERT) achieve 61.8% Recall@2 on dense patterns versus 1.3-4.8% for single-vector models.
*   **Lexical overlap exists:** If queries and documents share terminology, sparse models like BM25 achieve near-perfect scores (93.6% Recall@100 on LIMIT, Table 5) due to high effective dimensionality.
*   **Storage/compute budget allows:** Multi-vector models store multiple vectors per document, increasing storage by 10-100x compared to single-vector. This trade-off is acceptable when retrieval quality is paramount.

**When to Use Cross-Encoders.** Cross-encoders should be deployed when:
*   **Candidate sets are small:** Section 5.6 shows Gemini-2.5-Pro solves the small LIMIT task with 100% accuracy, but cross-encoders cannot scale to first-stage retrieval over millions of documents.
*   **Reranking is feasible:** Use single-vector or sparse models for first-stage retrieval (top-1000), then apply cross-encoders for final ranking (top-100 to top-10).
*   **Accuracy outweighs latency:** Applications like legal document retrieval or medical literature search where missing relevant documents has high cost.

**Hybrid Architecture Recommendations.** For production systems facing uncertain query patterns:
1.  **Tier 1 (First Stage):** Use sparse retrieval (BM25 or neural sparse) for broad coverage. This handles high-sign-rank queries that dense models cannot represent.
2.  **Tier 2 (Re-ranking):** Apply single-vector dense embeddings for semantic refinement. This captures paraphrases and semantic similarity that sparse models miss.
3.  **Tier 3 (Final Ranking):** Use cross-encoders for the top-100 candidates when latency permits. This resolves edge cases where both sparse and dense signals are ambiguous.

This hybrid approach leverages the strengths of each architecture while mitigating their individual limitations.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

The paper provides open-source code and data at `https://github.com/google-deepmind/limit`, enabling practitioners to reproduce results and test their own systems. The following guidance helps integrate these tools into existing workflows.

**Running LIMIT Evaluations.** To evaluate your own embedding models on LIMIT:
1.  Download the dataset from the repository (includes both full 50k document and small 46 document versions).
2.  Use the MTEB evaluation framework with the provided task definition.
3.  Test at multiple embedding dimensions (32, 128, 512, 1024, etc.) to observe the dimensionality scaling curve.
4.  Compare Recall@2, Recall@10, and Recall@100 against the baselines in Table 5 and Table 4.

**Estimating Sign-Rank for Custom Tasks.** While computing exact sign-rank is NP-hard, practitioners can:
1.  **Sample combinations:** Create a subset of queries that test different document combinations from your corpus.
2.  **Run free embedding optimization:** Use the paper's methodology (Section 4) to directly optimize vectors on your qrel matrix. If optimization fails to achieve 100% accuracy, the task exceeds your dimension capacity.
3.  **Monitor qrel graph density:** Compute the Graph Density metric from Appendix 10. Values above 0.05 suggest high combinatorial complexity that may challenge single-vector models.

**Integration Checklist for Production Systems.** Before deploying a single-vector retrieval system, verify:
- [ ] **Query patterns are low-density:** Queries do not frequently combine unrelated documents via logical operators.
- [ ] **Corpus size is within capacity:** Use the polynomial formula to estimate whether your document count exceeds the critical-n for your embedding dimension.
- [ ] **Fallback architecture exists:** If queries can be high-sign-rank (e.g., agent-generated queries with dynamic conditions), have a multi-vector or sparse fallback ready.
- [ ] **Evaluation includes combinatorial tests:** Standard benchmarks (BEIR, MTEB) do not test sign-rank limits. Add custom evaluations that stress combination handling.

**Common Pitfalls to Avoid.**
*   **Assuming MTEB Scores Guarantee Robustness:** Figure 7 shows no correlation between BEIR and LIMIT. A model scoring 60+ on MTEB may still fail on high-density tasks.
*   **Over-Fine-Tuning:** Section 5.3 demonstrates that fine-tuning on in-domain data provides minimal improvement for capacity-limited tasks. Recognize when the issue is architectural, not training-related.
*   **Ignoring Dimension Truncation:** Many models use Matryoshka Representation Learning (MRL) to support variable dimensions. Truncating dimensions below the model's trained capacity (e.g., using 128 dimensions from a 1024-dimensional model) can severely degrade performance on complex tasks, as shown in Figure 3.

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision for Retrieval Research

This paper marks a transition point for the retrieval community. The next phase of research should focus on:

**1. Expressive Yet Efficient Architectures.** The ideal retrieval system combines the efficiency of single-vector embeddings with the capacity of multi-vector or cross-encoder models. Research into **adaptive computation** (e.g., using more vectors only for complex queries) could bridge this gap.

**2. Theoretical-Practical Feedback Loops.** Future work should create tighter connections between theoretical bounds and empirical benchmarks. For example, deriving sign-rank estimates from query logs could predict when a system will fail before deployment.

**3. Instruction-Following Retrieval Standards.** As retrieval systems increasingly serve AI agents that generate dynamic queries, the community needs standardized benchmarks for instruction-following capacity. LIMIT is a starting point, but broader benchmarks covering diverse instruction types (logical operators, temporal constraints, negation) are needed.

**4. Cross-Modal Capacity Analysis.** The theoretical framework applies to any modality using single-vector embeddings (images, audio, video). Extending LIMIT to multi-modal retrieval could reveal whether the same dimension bottlenecks exist across modalities.

By adopting these directions, the field can move beyond empirical trial-and-error toward principled architecture selection grounded in mathematical understanding. This paper provides the foundation; the community must build the tools, benchmarks, and systems that operationalize these insights.
