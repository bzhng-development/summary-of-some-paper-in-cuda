## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the lack of a reproducible strategy for adapting general-purpose Vision-Language Models (VLMs) like `LLaVA-OneVision` and `Gemma3` to the noisy, attribute-centric, and multi-image demands of e-commerce without sacrificing general multimodal capabilities. The authors introduce a backbone-agnostic adaptation recipe utilizing a curated ~4M internal e-commerce instruction tuning dataset and a novel evaluation suite covering Aspect Prediction, Deep Fashion Understanding, and Dynamic Attribute Extraction. Their adapted models achieve substantial in-domain gains, such as an +11% improvement on the `eComMMMU` benchmark for `Gemma3-4B` over open-source counterparts, while fine-tuning for Multi-Image Item Intelligence delivers a 3.8x inference speedup and higher F1 scores (58.8 vs 44.8) compared to zero-shot baselines.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the necessity of this work, we must first recognize the unique structural challenges of e-commerce data compared to general computer vision tasks. While general-purpose **Vision-Language Models (VLMs)**—systems capable of processing both image and text inputs—have achieved state-of-the-art results in broad domains like image captioning and visual question answering (Section 1), they struggle when deployed in online marketplaces. This section details the specific gaps this paper addresses, why solving them matters for real-world systems, and how prior approaches fail to meet the stringent requirements of retail environments.

### The Multimodal Gap in E-commerce

E-commerce product understanding is inherently multimodal, yet current systems often rely disproportionately on text. As noted in the Introduction (Section 1), while search functionality primarily matches textual listings, customers rely heavily on images of the item, packaging, and visuals to evaluate products. This creates a specific technical gap:

*   **Attribute-Centric Reasoning:** Unlike general VLM tasks that might ask "What is in this image?", e-commerce requires precise extraction of structured attributes (e.g., "Sleeve Length: Short", "Material: Cotton"). General models often hallucinate attributes not visible in the image. Figure 1 visually demonstrates this failure mode, where a standard `LLaVA-OneVision` model predicts attributes highlighted in red that are neither tied to the image nor valid item properties.
*   **Noisy User-Generated Content:** Training data for e-commerce comes from seller listings, which are inherently noisy. As described in the Data Curation pipeline (Section 3.2.1 and Figure 2), raw listings contain redundant, incomplete, or incorrect information. General VLMs trained on clean web data are not robust to this noise without specific adaptation.
*   **Multi-Image Aggregation:** A single product listing often contains multiple images (median = 5, range = 2–8 per item, Section 3.2.3). General VLMs are typically optimized for single-image inputs. Aggregating information across multiple views (e.g., front, back, label close-up) to answer compliance questions requires specialized handling not present in standard architectures.

### Real-World Impact and Theoretical Significance

The motivation for this research extends beyond academic metrics; it addresses critical production constraints for platforms like eBay.

*   **Operational Efficiency:** Manual inspection of product safety and compliance (e.g., warning labels, ingredients) is slow and costly. Automating this via **Multi-Image Item Intelligence** (Section 3.1) allows for scalable verification.
*   **Customer Trust:** Accurate attribute extraction directly impacts search relevance and return rates. If a model hallucinates a product feature (as seen in Figure 1), it leads to mismatched customer expectations.
*   **Preservation of General Capabilities:** A key theoretical challenge is **catastrophic forgetting**. When adapting a model to a specific domain (e-commerce), there is a risk it loses its general reasoning abilities. The paper emphasizes the need for a strategy that improves domain performance "without sacrificing general performance" (Abstract). This balance is crucial for deploying models that must handle both specific retail tasks and general user queries.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches

Existing research falls short in three key areas, creating the opportunity for this work:

1.  **Text-Centric Adaptation:** Prior works have successfully adapted Large Language Models (LLMs) for e-commerce using text-only data (e.g., Ling et al., 2024, cited in Section 2). However, translating these gains to the vision-language setting remains a "considerable challenge" (Section 1). Text-only models cannot verify if a visual attribute matches the description.
2.  **Inadequate Benchmarking:** While text-centric evaluation suites exist (e.g., Shopping MMLU, Jin et al., 2024), rigorous benchmarking for **multimodal** shopping assistants is underdeveloped (Section 1). General VLM benchmarks (e.g., MMBench, MMMU) focus on broad visual understanding but lack the fine-grained attribute extraction and tool use typical of retail (Section 2). Some recent multimodal datasets simply add images to text tasks where the image is redundant; this paper argues that starting from "image-focused tasks" is more naturalistic for shopping (Section 2).
3.  **Lack of Reproducible Recipes:** Although specific e-commerce VLMs exist (e.g., Bai et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2024), none have concurrently studied the effect of multiple pre-trained multimodal architectures while ensuring effectiveness on general-purpose benchmarks is retained (Section 2). There is no documented, well-known strategy for adapting general VLMs to the noisy, multi-image nature of e-commerce data without a backbone-specific overhaul.

### Positioning of This Work

This paper positions itself as a systematic investigation into **backbone-agnostic adaptation**. Rather than proposing a single new model architecture, it seeks to answer two fundamental questions outlined in the Introduction (Section 1):

1.  **Architecture Necessity:** Do high-performing e-commerce VLMs require a customized LLM backbone, or is adapting vision-focused tasks sufficient? The authors tackle this through extensive ablations across visual and text decoders (Section 1).
2.  **Evaluation Completeness:** How do we build a benchmark that assesses multiple dimensions of understanding, from attribute extraction to multi-image handling? The authors propose a new evaluation suite covering deep product understanding and strict instruction following (Section 1).

By focusing on a reproducible recipe that leverages a **Visual Verification Pipeline** (Figure 2) to clean noisy data, the work distinguishes itself from prior efforts that may rely on unverified user inputs. It bridges the gap between general-purpose multimodal intelligence and the strict, structured requirements of online retail.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a **domain adaptation paper** that presents a reproducible recipe for fine-tuning general-purpose Vision-Language Models (VLMs) to handle e-commerce-specific tasks while preserving their general multimodal capabilities. The core idea is that targeted adaptation using carefully curated, visually-verified e-commerce instruction data can substantially improve domain performance without requiring architecture changes or sacrificing general-purpose abilities.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a **domain-adapted Vision-Language Model** that takes product images (single or multiple) and text queries as input, then outputs structured attribute extractions, compliance information, or natural language answers about e-commerce items. This solves the problem that general VLMs hallucinate attributes and fail on noisy, multi-image retail data by introducing a **Visual Verification Pipeline** that ensures training data has genuine image-text correspondence, followed by a **three-stage training procedure** that gradually specializes the model while maintaining general capabilities.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The overall system consists of four major components connected in sequence:

1.  **Data Curation Module (Visual Verification Pipeline):** Takes ~15 million raw marketplace listings as input, processes them through `InternVL-2.5-26B` for image captioning and `Mistral-Small-3-24B` for aspect verification, and outputs ~4 million high-quality image-instruction pairs where textual attributes are confirmed to be visually grounded (Figure 2).

2.  **Three-Stage Training Pipeline:** Receives the curated dataset and trains the VLM through (i) Vision-Language Alignment using `BLIP-LAION 558k` corpus, (ii) Mid-Stage Training with filtered LLaVA-OneVision mixture, and (iii) Visual Instruction Tuning with the 4M e-commerce set, outputting a domain-adapted VLM.

3.  **Model Architecture Backbone:** Comprises a vision encoder (`SigLIP2-SO400M-Patch14-384` or `Qwen2.5 ViT`) connected to a text decoder (various LLMs from 1B to 27B parameters), which processes visual tokens and text tokens through a projection layer to generate responses.

4.  **Evaluation Suite:** Four benchmark sets (Aspect Prediction with 2,600 + 3,200 examples, Deep Fashion Understanding with 3,000 samples, Dynamic Attribute Extraction with 1,000 examples, Multi-Image Item Intelligence with 1,000 items) that measure performance across attribute extraction, fashion categorization, open-ended attribute discovery, and compliance verification tasks (Section 3.1).

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

-   **First**, I explain the **Visual Verification Pipeline** because clean, verified training data is the foundation—without addressing the noise in user-generated listings, no amount of model architecture tuning will succeed.
-   **Second**, I detail the **three-stage training procedure** since this defines how the model gradually acquires e-commerce capabilities while retaining general multimodal reasoning.
-   **Third**, I break down the **instruction tuning dataset composition** because the specific task mixture (VQA, attribute extraction, instruction following, listings) determines what behaviors the model learns.
-   **Fourth**, I describe the **model architecture choices** (vision encoders and text decoders) since the ablation study reveals which components matter most for different task types.
-   **Fifth**, I explain the **Multi-Image Item Intelligence fine-tuning** as a specialized production use-case that requires additional handling beyond the general adaptation recipe.
-   **Finally**, I cover the **four evaluation benchmarks** to show how the authors measure success across different dimensions of e-commerce understanding.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Framing and Core Mechanism

This paper presents a **domain adaptation methodology** rather than a novel architecture, meaning the contribution lies in *how* existing VLMs are trained and evaluated for e-commerce rather than inventing new model components. The fundamental insight is that e-commerce data requires **visual-textual correspondence verification** before use in training, because seller-provided attributes often do not match what is actually visible in product images. Without this verification step, models learn to trust textual metadata even when it contradicts visual evidence, leading to the hallucination errors shown in Figure 1 where standard VLMs predict attributes "that are neither tied to the image nor valid item attributes."

#### The Visual Verification Pipeline (Data Curation)

The data curation process begins with collecting nearly **15 million raw listings** from online marketplace websites, where each listing contains user-supplied item aspects (structured attributes like brand, color, size) and one or more product images (Section 3.2.1). The pipeline selects only the **primary (main) image** for each listing to maintain consistency during initial training, acknowledging that multi-image handling requires separate fine-tuning discussed later. Each image is then captioned through `InternVL-2.5-26B`, a 26 billion parameter vision-language model that generates detailed natural language descriptions of visual content. In parallel, the system extracts the user-supplied item aspects from each listing's metadata, which may include both accurate and inaccurate claims about the product.

The critical verification step employs `Mistral-Small-3-24B`, a 24 billion parameter language model, to determine **which aspects can be inferred from the generated caption** and therefore from the image itself. This creates a filtering mechanism where only attributes with visual evidence are retained for training, ensuring that the model learns genuine image-to-attribute mappings rather than memorizing potentially incorrect seller metadata. The resulting dataset contains approximately **4 million internal e-commerce oriented instructions** paired with their original images, forming what the authors call a "high-quality dataset" where visual-textual correspondence has been algorithmically verified (Section 3.2.1 and Figure 2).

This verification approach addresses a fundamental problem in e-commerce data: **seller-provided attributes are noisy**. A seller might list a shirt as "100% Cotton" when the image shows a polyester blend, or claim "Brand: Nike" on a counterfeit item. Training a VLM on such unverified pairs would teach it to ignore visual evidence and trust text metadata, which defeats the purpose of multimodal understanding. By requiring that aspects be verifiable from the image caption, the pipeline ensures the model learns to ground its predictions in visual features.

#### Three-Stage Training Procedure

The training follows a **progressive specialization strategy** adapted from `LLaVA-OneVision` (Li et al., 2024b), consisting of three distinct stages that gradually shift the model from general multimodal understanding to e-commerce-specific reasoning (Section 3.2.2).

**Stage (i) Vision-Language Alignment** employs the LLaVA-OneVision set of instructions combined with the `BLIP-LAION 558k` corpus (Liu et al., 2023). This stage focuses on establishing basic communication between the vision encoder and text decoder, ensuring that visual features can be properly projected into the language model's embedding space. The 558,000 image-text pairs provide broad coverage of general visual concepts without domain specialization, preventing the model from overfitting to e-commerce patterns too early.

**Stage (ii) Mid-Stage Training** uses the LLaVA-OneVision mid-stage mixture with a critical modification: the authors remove "several subsets that we found low-signal or redundant" for e-commerce purposes (Section 3.2.2). This filtering step is important because not all general-purpose training data contributes equally to retail understanding. For example, datasets focused on artistic images or natural scenes may introduce patterns that do not transfer to product photography. The paper does not specify exactly which subsets were removed, which represents a reproducibility gap, but the principle is to maintain general capabilities while reducing noise.

**Stage (iii) Visual Instruction Tuning** represents the domain specialization phase, where the model learns e-commerce-specific behaviors through two data sources: (a) a version of the LLaVA-OneVision single-image mixture to preserve general capabilities, and (b) the approximately **4 million internal e-commerce oriented instructions** described above. This mixture prevents **catastrophic forgetting**, where domain adaptation causes the model to lose its general reasoning abilities. The training uses the same loss objective as LLaVA-OneVision, implemented through the NeMo framework (Kuchaiev et al., 2019) and LLaVA-OneVision codebase, ensuring compatibility with existing infrastructure.

Training was conducted on **NVIDIA H100 GPUs**, using up to **120 GPUs connected via NVLink and InfiniBand** for distributed training (Section 4.1). The paper does not specify exact training duration, learning rates, or batch sizes, which limits reproducibility but is common in industry papers where such details may be proprietary.

#### Instruction Tuning Dataset Composition

The 4 million e-commerce instruction set is partitioned into four task categories with specific percentages that reflect the relative importance of each capability for retail applications (Section 3.2.2 and Figure 3):

**Visual Question Answering (VQA) comprises 45%** of the dataset, consisting of free-form questions, yes/no questions, and image-only questions, with variants that include or exclude title and category context. This is the largest portion because question-answering represents the most common interaction pattern for shopping assistants—customers ask about product features, availability, or suitability. The inclusion of both "with context" and "without context" variants teaches the model to handle cases where metadata is available versus cases where it must rely purely on visual analysis.

**Dynamic Attribute Extraction comprises 30%** of the dataset, containing free-form visual attribute extraction tasks with and without title and category context. Variants include augmenting with OCR (Optical Character Recognition) for reading text on product surfaces, prompt constraining text to control output format, and combinations of these settings. This task type is critical because e-commerce requires structured attribute extraction rather than open-ended description—the model must output specific fields like "Material: Cotton" rather than "This appears to be a soft fabric shirt."

**Precise Instruction Following comprises 12.5%** of the dataset, consisting of keyword-conditioned instructions that require inclusion or avoidance of specific terms, plus tasks emphasizing strict form and length control. This teaches the model to adhere to output schemas required for downstream systems—for example, a compliance verification system may require JSON output with specific field names, and the model must learn to produce this format consistently.

**Listings comprises 12.5%** of the dataset, comprised of full product listings predictions from an image. This task requires the model to generate complete product descriptions including title, attributes, and category assignments, simulating the workflow of creating new listings from product photos. This is valuable for seller tools that automate listing creation.

The percentage breakdown reveals a design choice: **open-ended understanding (VQA + Attribute Extraction = 75%)** is prioritized over **structured generation (Instruction Following + Listings = 25%)**. This makes sense because understanding customer queries and extracting attributes from existing listings are higher-frequency tasks than generating new listings. The inclusion of OCR augmentation is particularly important for e-commerce, where product labels, size charts, and packaging text contain critical information that must be read accurately.

#### Model Architecture Choices

The authors conduct extensive ablations across vision encoders and text decoders to determine which components most influence e-commerce performance (Section 3.2.4). For the **vision encoder**, they experiment with two architectures: `SigLIP2-SO400M-Patch14-384` (Tschannen et al., 2025) and `Qwen2.5 ViT` (Bai et al., 2025). SigLIP2 uses a 400 million parameter vision transformer with 14x14 pixel patches and 384-pixel input resolution, while Qwen2.5 ViT supports native resolution adaptation for varying image sizes.

For the **text decoder**, they compare six options spanning different parameter counts and domain specializations: `Llama3.1-8B` (general-purpose), `e-Llama3.1-8B` (e-commerce adapted version of Llama3.1), `Lilium 1B/4B/8B` (trained from scratch for e-commerce), and `Qwen3 4B/8B` (general-purpose with strong reasoning). They also adapt fully-fledged SOTA VLMs including `Llama-3.1-Nemotron-Nano-VL-8B-V1`, `Gemma3 4B/12B/27B`, `Qwen2.5VL-7B`, and `Qwen3VL-8B` for certain tasks.

The results reveal three key findings about architecture selection (Section 4.3 and 4.4). First, **vision encoder choice shows inconclusive results** for low-to-mid resolution images—comparing Table 1 rows 2 and 3, `SigLIP2 | e-Llama3.1-8B` achieves 44.4% on Aspect Prediction while `Qwen2.5ViT | e-Llama3.1-8B` achieves 53.3%, but on Deep Fashion Understanding the pattern reverses (78.9% vs 71.0% for Apparel). The Qwen2.5ViT's native resolution feature benefits tasks requiring small detail detection (Aspect Prediction), but SigLIP2 performs better on reasoning-oriented tasks (Fashion Understanding). This gap is "mostly apparent in high resolution scenarios," and since the benchmarks use "low to mid resolutions," the performance difference is muted.

Second, **e-commerce domain knowledge in the text decoder helps** for domain-specific tasks. Comparing Table 1 rows 1, 2, and 8, the e-commerce adapted `e-Llama3.1-8B` (44.4% Aspect Prediction) and `Lilium-8B` (42.4%) outperform general `Llama-3.1-8B` (37.7%), even though all three perform similarly on general-domain benchmarks (Table 2 rows 14, 15, 21). This confirms that domain-adapted language backbones transfer their knowledge to the multimodal setting.

Third, **model size matters but with diminishing returns**. For the Fashion subset of Aspect Prediction, going from 1 billion to 4 billion parameters improves performance, but going from 4 billion to 8 billion shows minimal gains (Table 1 rows 6-8: Lilium-1B at 48.4%, Lilium-4B at 49.1%, Lilium-8B at 49.2%). The authors attribute this to "lack of task complexity"—once the model is large enough to understand the task, additional parameters do not help. However, for Multi-Image Item Intelligence, larger models continue to improve (Table 3 rows 34-36: Gemma3 4B at 50.5% F1, 12B at 51.8%, 27B at 52.6%), suggesting that multi-image reasoning remains challenging even for larger models.

#### Multi-Image Item Intelligence Fine-Tuning

The Multi-Image Item Intelligence task represents a **production-specific use case** that requires additional fine-tuning beyond the general e-commerce adaptation (Section 3.2.3). This task extracts attributes targeted at regulatory compliance questions (brand, warning labels, ingredients) from multiple product images into structured JSON output, enabling verification and recall matching processes. The fine-tuning dataset contains **100,000 items** across relevant categories (toys, electronics, cosmetics, etc.), with each item containing multiple images (median = 5, range = 2–8).

Since no labeled data exists for this task, the authors generate annotations using `GPT-4.1` via prompt-engineering, then enhance quality through a **bounding box refinement process**. They employ `Qwen2.5-VL-32B` to produce precise bounding boxes around visually and semantically informative regions—often textual or numeric details on product surfaces. These bounding boxes are post-processed (expanded and merged) for better coverage, then cropped regions and original images are re-annotated by GPT-4.1, yielding "substantially higher-quality better labels" (Section 3.2.3).

For inference, the authors address a computational challenge: the Gemma-3 image encoder assigns a **fixed 256 visual tokens per image**, causing inference cost to scale linearly with the number of images. On their training dataset, this resulted in a median of 12 and maximum of 43 images per item before optimization (Appendix A.5). To reduce this, they construct crops covering regions of interest by identifying the smallest enclosing square that covers all bounding boxes, consistent with the model's square image format. They then apply **perceptual hashing (pHash)** for deduplication (Zauner, 2010), reducing the number of images per item to a median of four and maximum of nine. This optimization is critical for production deployment where inference latency and cost matter.

The results demonstrate that **fine-tuning significantly improves performance** over zero-shot approaches (Table 3). The zero-shot `Gemma3-27B` with all images achieves 44.8% F1-score (row 29), while the fine-tuned `Gemma3-27B` with better labels achieves 58.8% F1-score (row 39). More importantly, the fine-tuned `Gemma3-4B` with better labels achieves 53.8% F1-score (row 37) while being **3.8x faster** at inference (6.7 seconds per example vs 25.5 seconds for zero-shot 27B, Table 4). This demonstrates that targeted fine-tuning on smaller models can outperform larger zero-shot models while being more efficient—a crucial finding for production systems.

The authors also test **targeted cropping strategies** during inference. Including image crops (row 41) achieves 58.0% F1-score compared to 56.9% for the 'Pan & Scan' feature from Gemma3 (row 40), showing that their "more targeted cropping leads to stronger results." This confirms that focusing on informative regions (labels, text, specific product features) is more effective than processing all images at full resolution.

#### Evaluation Benchmark Design

The paper introduces **four evaluation suites** designed to cover different dimensions of e-commerce understanding, each with specific characteristics that make them suitable for measuring adaptation success (Section 3.1 and Appendix A.4):

**Aspect Prediction** consists of 2,600 general questions across all e-commerce categories plus two Fashion-specific subsets with 1,600 examples each (with and without title and category context). All are evaluated through **string matching** after post-processing, which provides objective metrics but may penalize semantically correct answers with different wording. The inclusion of both "with context" and "without context" variants tests whether the model can extract attributes from images alone versus leveraging textual metadata.

**Deep Fashion Understanding** contains 3,000 samples divided into three subsets: Apparel Men Shirts and Women Tops, Handbags, and Sneakers. Each subset targets critical attributes relevant to the product type, structured into clear classification categories (e.g., Sleeve Length, Neckline, Pattern, Color for shirts). Evaluation involves prompting the model to categorize items precisely according to provided attribute classes, testing fine-grained visual discrimination within specific product categories.

**Dynamic Attribute Extraction** comprises 1,000 synthetically generated (with `GPT-4o`) and human-verified examples that benchmark a model's ability to enumerate and structure all visually grounded attributes from an image **without a predefined schema**. This is the most challenging benchmark because the model must decide which properties are salient, choose attribute names, and serialize values as key-value pairs. Unlike fixed-ontology extraction, DAE "stresses e-Commerce generalization by incentivizing exhaustive yet faithful outputs, avoiding hallucinated fields." Evaluation uses **LLM-as-a-judge** (Gu et al., 2025) since open-ended attribute extraction cannot be measured through string matching.

**Multi-Image Item Intelligence** contains 1,000 items sampled to prioritize product categories with high regulatory requirements (toys, electronics, cosmetics, etc.). The model must compile a fixed set of attributes related to compliance questions from multiple product images into structured JSON output. Evaluation uses **LLM-as-a-judge** with metrics including F1-score, precision, recall, verifiable-correct, verifiable-incorrect, and unverifiable rates (Table 3). This benchmark most closely reflects production use cases where compliance verification is critical.

The benchmark design reflects a key insight: **different tasks require different evaluation methods**. String matching works for closed-set classification (Aspect Prediction, Deep Fashion) but fails for open-ended extraction (DAE, Item Intelligence), which requires LLM-based evaluation. The authors acknowledge this introduces potential bias, noting in Limitations (Section 6) that "evaluator–model family overlap can inflate or deflate measured gains due to inductive-bias alignment in 'LLM-as-judge' scenarios."

The authors also evaluate on **public benchmarks** to ensure general capabilities are preserved (Section 4.1 and Appendix A.2). These include MMBench (object detection, text recognition), MMMU (perception, knowledge, reasoning), CVBench (visual-centered capabilities), MME (perception and cognition with 15 subcategories), AI2D (diagram/chart QA), MMStar (6 categories across perception and reasoning), TextVQA (OCR stress-test), and eComMMMU (36,000 multi-image e-commerce samples). The inclusion of eComMMMU is particularly important as a **control variable** to validate that their adaptation delivers results on external e-commerce benchmarks, not just internal ones (Section 4.5).

#### Training Data Mixture Details

Appendix A.6 provides extensive detail on the specific datasets used in mid-stage and visual instruction tuning, revealing the complexity of the training mixture. For mid-stage training, the authors use four LLaVA-ReCap datasets (558K, 118K, CC3M, and synthdog_en_processed) with "all" sampling strategy, meaning complete inclusion. For visual instruction tuning, they use 47 different LLaVA-OneVision datasets with varying sampling strategies: some use "all" (complete inclusion), while others use "first:10%", "first:20%", or "first:30%" to limit the contribution of certain datasets.

This selective sampling reveals a **data balancing strategy**: general-purpose datasets are downsampled to prevent them from dominating the training signal, while e-commerce-specific data receives full weight. For example, `LLaVA-OneVision-Data_VisualWebInstruct(filtered).json` uses "all" sampling, but `LLaVA-OneVision-Data_mavis_math_metagen.json` uses "first:10%". This ensures the model retains general capabilities without being overwhelmed by non-e-commerce patterns.

The authors also specify that for the e-commerce portion, the percentages (45% VQA, 30% DAE, 12.5% Instruction Following, 12.5% Listings) sum to the total e-commerce instruction set, not the overall training mixture (Section 3.2.2). This means the actual proportion of e-commerce data in the full training mixture depends on how much LLaVA-OneVision data is included, which is not explicitly stated. This represents another reproducibility gap, though the principle of mixing general and domain-specific data is clear.

#### Key Design Choices and Rationale

Several design choices in this approach reflect deliberate trade-offs between performance, efficiency, and reproducibility:

**Visual Verification over Raw Metadata:** The decision to verify aspects through captioning and LLM judgment rather than trusting seller metadata directly addresses the noise problem in e-commerce data. Alternative approaches might use human annotation (expensive at 15M scale) or skip verification entirely (risks learning incorrect associations). The chosen approach leverages existing VLMs as annotators, creating a self-improving cycle where better models produce better training data.

**Three-Stage Training over Single-Stage Fine-Tuning:** Progressive specialization prevents catastrophic forgetting by maintaining general capabilities through stages (i) and (ii) before domain specialization in stage (iii). A single-stage approach fine-tuning directly on e-commerce data would likely achieve higher domain performance but at the cost of general capabilities, which the authors explicitly want to preserve (Abstract: "without sacrificing general performance").

**Backbone-Agnostic Recipe:** Rather than proposing a single optimal architecture, the authors test multiple vision encoders and text decoders to identify general principles. This makes the approach more valuable to practitioners who may have different infrastructure constraints or model preferences. The finding that "e-commerce knowledge helps" and "model size matters with diminishing returns" applies regardless of specific backbone choice.

**Separate Multi-Image Fine-Tuning:** The decision to handle multi-image tasks through separate fine-tuning rather than including them in the general adaptation reflects a practical constraint: multi-image training requires significantly more compute and different data curation. By keeping the general adaptation single-image focused, the approach remains accessible, while production use-cases can add the multi-image fine-tuning as an optional step.

**LLM-as-Judge Evaluation:** While acknowledging the limitations (evaluator bias, family overlap), the authors use LLM-as-judge for open-ended tasks because no better alternative exists. String matching would penalize semantically correct but differently-worded answers, while human evaluation would be prohibitively expensive at scale. The transparency about this limitation (Section 6) allows readers to interpret results appropriately.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper contributes several innovations that distinguish it from prior e-commerce VLM research. Below, I identify the most significant contributions, explain what makes each different from existing approaches, and assess whether they represent incremental improvements or fundamental advances.

### 4.1 Visual Verification Pipeline: Self-Supervised Data Curation at Scale

**What Prior Work Did:** Previous e-commerce VLM adaptations typically relied on one of two data sources: (1) raw seller-provided metadata paired with images, which contains significant noise and inaccuracies (Section 2), or (2) human-annotated datasets, which are prohibitively expensive at the scale required for training large models. Some works like Ling et al. (2024) instruction-tuned LLMs with shopping data but did not address the visual-textual correspondence problem inherent in user-generated listings.

**What Makes This Different:** The Visual Verification Pipeline (Figure 2, Section 3.2.1) introduces a **self-supervised filtering mechanism** that leverages existing VLMs as annotators. Rather than trusting seller metadata directly, the pipeline uses `InternVL-2.5-26B` to generate image captions and `Mistral-Small-3-24B` to verify which aspects can be inferred from those captions. This creates a quality gate: only attributes with visual evidence are retained for training.

**Why It's Significant:** This addresses the **fundamental noise problem** in e-commerce data. A seller might list "Material: 100% Cotton" when the image shows polyester, or claim a brand that isn't visible. Training on unverified pairs teaches models to ignore visual evidence and trust text metadata—defeating the purpose of multimodal understanding. By requiring visual grounding, the pipeline ensures models learn genuine image-to-attribute mappings. The scale is also notable: ~4 million verified instructions from 15 million raw listings, something infeasible with human annotation.

**Innovation Assessment:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in data curation methodology. It transforms a weakness (noisy user-generated content) into a strength (self-supervised signal) through clever use of existing models as verifiers.

### 4.2 Backbone-Agnostic Adaptation Principles

**What Prior Work Did:** Most domain adaptation papers propose a specific architecture or model (e.g., Bai et al., 2023's product understanding VLM, Xue et al., 2024's PumGPT). These works demonstrate performance on their chosen backbone but do not establish whether the adaptation strategy generalizes to other architectures. Practitioners adopting such methods must commit to specific model families.

**What Makes This Different:** This paper systematically ablates across **multiple vision encoders** (SigLIP2, Qwen2.5ViT) and **multiple text decoders** (Llama3.1, e-Llama, Lilium, Qwen3, Gemma3) ranging from 1B to 27B parameters (Section 3.2.4, Tables 1-2). The goal is not to propose "the best model" but to identify **generalizable adaptation principles** that hold regardless of backbone choice.

**Why It's Significant:** The findings reveal transferable insights:
- E-commerce domain knowledge in the text decoder helps for domain tasks but not general benchmarks (Table 1 rows 1-2-8 vs. Table 2 rows 14-15-21)
- Model size shows diminishing returns for simple tasks but matters for complex multi-image reasoning (Section 4.4)
- Vision encoder choice is inconclusive for low-to-mid resolution images (Section 4.3)

These principles allow practitioners to adapt the recipe to their infrastructure constraints without sacrificing effectiveness.

**Innovation Assessment:** This is a **methodological innovation**. Rather than a single model contribution, the paper provides a reproducible recipe applicable across backbones—more valuable for real-world deployment where organizations have existing model investments.

### 4.3 Domain Adaptation Without Catastrophic Forgetting

**What Prior Work Did:** Domain adaptation typically faces a **stability-plasticity tradeoff**: improving performance on target domain tasks often degrades general capabilities (catastrophic forgetting). Prior e-commerce adaptations either focused solely on domain performance without measuring general capabilities, or accepted some degradation as inevitable.

**What Makes This Different:** The three-stage training procedure (Section 3.2.2) explicitly preserves general capabilities through stages (i) and (ii) before domain specialization in stage (iii). The results demonstrate this balance: internally adapted models improve on e-commerce benchmarks (Table 1) while maintaining competitive performance on general multimodal benchmarks like MMBench, MME, and MMMU (Table 2).

**Why It's Significant:** Table 2 shows that adapted models like `SigLIP2 | Qwen-3-8B` (row 18) achieve 82.5% on MMBench and 77.7% on TextVQA—competitive with open-source counterparts (row 25: 84.0% and 80.9%). Meanwhile, on e-commerce tasks (Table 1), the same model achieves 56.2% on Aspect Prediction vs. 40.5% for the open-source `Qwen3-VL` (row 5 vs. 12). This demonstrates **dual competence**: the model handles both retail-specific queries and general multimodal tasks without degradation.

**Innovation Assessment:** This represents a **practical breakthrough** rather than theoretical novelty. The three-stage approach builds on LLaVA-OneVision, but the explicit demonstration that e-commerce adaptation need not sacrifice general capabilities addresses a real deployment concern. Organizations can adopt domain specialization without maintaining separate models for general vs. specific tasks.

### 4.4 Efficient Multi-Image Handling Through Targeted Region Focus

**What Prior Work Did:** Multi-image VLM processing typically treats all images equally, processing each at full resolution. For e-commerce listings with median 5 images (range 2-8, Section 3.2.3), this creates linear scaling in compute cost. Some models like Gemma3 offer "Pan & Scan" features but do not optimize for semantically informative regions specific to compliance tasks.

**What Makes This Different:** The Multi-Image Item Intelligence fine-tuning (Section 3.2.3, Appendix A.5) introduces a **region-focused optimization**: `Qwen2.5-VL-32B` identifies bounding boxes around informative regions (text, labels, numeric details), crops are created from the smallest enclosing square, and perceptual hashing deduplicates similar views. This reduces images per item from median 12 to median 4.

**Why It's Significant:** The efficiency gains are substantial. Table 4 shows the fine-tuned `Gemma3-4B` processes examples in 6.7 seconds vs. 25.5 seconds for zero-shot `Gemma3-27B`—a **3.8x speedup**. More importantly, accuracy improves: 53.8% F1-score (fine-tuned 4B with better labels, Table 3 row 37) vs. 44.8% (zero-shot 27B, row 29). This demonstrates that **smaller fine-tuned models can outperform larger zero-shot models** when training data and inference strategies are optimized for the task.

**Innovation Assessment:** This is an **incremental innovation** built on existing techniques (bounding box detection, cropping, deduplication) but novel in their combination for e-commerce compliance. The significance lies in the production impact: 3.8x faster inference with better accuracy directly translates to cost savings and scalability.

### 4.5 Comprehensive Multimodal E-commerce Benchmark Suite

**What Prior Work Did:** Existing e-commerce benchmarks are predominantly text-centric (e.g., Shopping MMLU, Jin et al., 2024) or add images to text tasks where images are redundant (Ling et al., 2025's eComMMMU, discussed in Section 2). General VLM benchmarks (MMBench, MMMU) lack e-commerce-specific attributes and compliance tasks.

**What Makes This Different:** The four benchmark suites (Section 3.1, Appendix A.4) cover distinct dimensions:
- **Aspect Prediction**: Closed-set attribute extraction with and without textual context
- **Deep Fashion Understanding**: Fine-grained categorization within specific product types
- **Dynamic Attribute Extraction**: Open-ended attribute discovery without predefined schema
- **Multi-Image Item Intelligence**: Compliance-focused multi-image aggregation

Each uses appropriate evaluation methods: string matching for closed-set tasks, LLM-as-judge for open-ended extraction (Section 3.1).

**Why It's Significant:** The benchmark design reflects understanding that **different tasks require different evaluation methods**. String matching provides objective metrics for classification but fails for open-ended extraction where semantically equivalent answers may use different wording. The inclusion of eComMMMU as a control variable (Section 4.5) validates that internal adaptation generalizes to external benchmarks: adapted `Gemma3-4B` achieves +11% over open-source counterparts (Table 2 rows 22 vs. 26).

**Innovation Assessment:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in e-commerce evaluation. Prior work lacked rigorous multimodal benchmarks tailored to retail's fine-grained attribute requirements. The suite enables reproducible comparison across models and adaptation strategies, advancing the field's measurement infrastructure.

| Backbone-Agnostic Recipe | Methodological | Enables adoption across existing infrastructure |

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their adaptation recipe through systematic experimentation. The experimental design serves two purposes: (1) demonstrating that targeted adaptation improves e-commerce performance, and (2) proving that general multimodal capabilities are preserved. I will walk through the evaluation methodology, present the key quantitative findings with specific numbers, and critically assess whether the evidence supports the paper's central claims.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The authors employ a **dual-benchmark strategy** that measures performance on both internal e-commerce tasks and public multimodal benchmarks. This design directly addresses the risk of catastrophic forgetting—improving on domain tasks while maintaining general capabilities.

#### Internal E-commerce Benchmarks (Section 3.1)

Four evaluation suites measure different dimensions of product understanding:

| Benchmark | Samples | Evaluation Method | Task Type |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Aspect Prediction** | 2,600 general + 3,200 fashion | String matching | Closed-set attribute extraction |
| **Deep Fashion Understanding** | 3,000 | String matching | Fine-grained categorization |
| **Dynamic Attribute Extraction** | 1,000 | LLM-as-a-judge | Open-ended attribute discovery |
| **Multi-Image Item Intelligence** | 1,000 | LLM-as-a-judge | Compliance verification |

The **Aspect Prediction** benchmark includes three variants: general e-commerce questions (2,600 examples), fashion-specific without context (1,600 examples), and fashion-specific with title and category context (1,600 examples). This design tests whether models can extract attributes from images alone versus leveraging textual metadata.

The **Dynamic Attribute Extraction (DAE)** benchmark is particularly challenging because it requires models to enumerate attributes *without a predefined schema*. As noted in Section 3.1, this "stresses e-Commerce generalization by incentivizing exhaustive yet faithful outputs, avoiding hallucinated fields." Evaluation uses LLM-as-a-judge because string matching cannot capture semantically equivalent but differently-worded answers.

#### Public Multimodal Benchmarks (Section 4.1, Appendix A.2)

Eight general-domain benchmarks ensure adapted models retain broad capabilities:

- **MMBench**: Object detection, text recognition, action recognition
- **MME**: Perception and cognition tasks (15 subcategories), reported separately as MME (Perc.) and MME (Cogn.)
- **MMStar**: 1,500 samples across 6 categories (Perception, Math, Science & Tech, Logical, Instance Reasoning)
- **CVBench**: Visual-centered capabilities
- **TextVQA**: OCR stress-test with 5,000 examples
- **AI2D**: Diagram/Chart QA with 3,009 examples
- **MMMU**: Multimodal understanding and reasoning
- **eComMMMU**: 36,000 multi-image e-commerce samples (used as control variable)

The inclusion of **eComMMMU** is strategic—it serves as an external validation that internal adaptation generalizes to public e-commerce benchmarks, not just proprietary evaluation sets.

#### Baselines and Model Variants

The authors compare three categories of models (Tables 1-2):

1. **Internal E-commerce Adaptation**: Models fully trained from pre-trained backbones using their three-stage recipe (rows 1-9 in Table 1, rows 14-22 in Table 2)
2. **Open Source**: Unmodified public VLMs like `LLaVA-OV`, `Qwen2.5-VL`, `Qwen3-VL`, and `Gemma3` (rows 10-13 in Table 1, rows 23-26 in Table 2)
3. **Zero-shot vs. Fine-tuned**: For Multi-Image Item Intelligence, they compare zero-shot performance against fine-tuned variants (Table 3)

This multi-axis comparison allows isolation of adaptation effects from architecture effects.

#### Metrics and Experimental Setup

Training used the **NeMo framework** (Kuchaiev et al., 2019) and **LLaVA-OneVision codebase** (Li et al., 2024b) with the same loss objective. Hardware consisted of **NVIDIA H100 GPUs**, using up to **120 GPUs connected via NVLink and InfiniBand** (Section 4.1).

For **Multi-Image Item Intelligence**, metrics include F1-score, precision, recall, verifiable-correct (↑), verifiable-incorrect (↓), and unverifiable (↓) rates (Table 3). Inference speed is measured in **seconds per example** on a single A100 GPU using vLLM (Table 4).

> **Note on Reproducibility**: The paper does not specify exact training duration, learning rates, batch sizes, or which LLaVA-OneVision subsets were removed during mid-stage training. These omissions limit full reproducibility but are common in industry papers where such details may be proprietary.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

#### E-commerce Performance Gains (Table 1)

The internal adaptation recipe delivers substantial improvements on domain-specific tasks. Consider the **Aspect Prediction** benchmark:

| Model | Aspect Prediction (General) | Aspect Prediction (Fashion) | Aspect Prediction (Fashion + T&C) |
|-------|----------------------------|----------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| `SigLIP2 | Llama-3.1-8B` (adapted) | 37.7% | 46.0% | 51.9% |
| `SigLIP2 | Qwen-3-8B` (adapted) | **56.2%** | **60.1%** | **68.5%** |
| `Qwen3-VL` (open source) | 40.5% | 42.4% | 58.2% |

The adapted `SigLIP2 | Qwen-3-8B` achieves **56.2%** on general Aspect Prediction versus **40.5%** for the unmodified `Qwen3-VL`—a **+15.7 percentage point improvement**. This gap widens on Fashion-specific tasks, where the adapted model reaches **68.5%** with context versus **58.2%** for the open-source baseline.

On **Deep Fashion Understanding**, the pattern holds:

| Model | Apparel | Sneakers & Handbags |
|-------|---------|---------------------|
| `SigLIP2 | Qwen-3-8B` (adapted) | **79.8%** | **81.6%** |
| `Qwen3-VL` (open source) | 84.3% | 84.6% |

Here, the open-source `Qwen3-VL` actually outperforms the adapted model (84.3% vs 79.8% on Apparel). This is a **critical nuance**: adaptation does not uniformly improve all tasks. The authors note in Section 4.2 that "some external models do perform very well on Deep Fashion Understanding, they do fall behind on most e-commerce specific benchmarks." This suggests that certain general VLMs already have strong fashion recognition capabilities, and adaptation provides diminishing returns for well-represented categories.

On **Dynamic Attribute Extraction**, adapted models show consistent gains:

| Model | DAE Score |
|-------|-----------|
| `SigLIP2 | Llama-3.1-8B` (adapted) | 59.7% |
| `SigLIP2 | Qwen-3-8B` (adapted) | **68.1%** |
| `Qwen2.5-VL` (open source) | 72.0% |
| `Qwen3-VL` (open source) | 70.9% |

Interestingly, open-source models still lead on DAE (72.0% for `Qwen2.5-VL`). This may reflect that open-ended attribute extraction benefits from broader training data rather than domain specialization. The adapted models trade some open-ended capability for stronger structured extraction (Aspect Prediction).

#### General Capability Preservation (Table 2)

The second claim—that adaptation preserves general multimodal capabilities—is validated through public benchmark results:

| Model | MMBench | MME (Perc.) | MME (Cogn.) | TextVQA | MMMU |
|-------|---------|-------------|-------------|---------|------|
| `SigLIP2 | Qwen-3-8B` (adapted) | 82.5% | 1648.4 | 453.6 | 77.7% | 49.1% |
| `Qwen3-VL` (open source) | **84.0%** | **1742.1** | **660.7** | **80.9%** | **52.4%** |

The adapted model achieves **82.5%** on MMBench versus **84.0%** for the open-source `Qwen3-VL`—a modest **-1.5 percentage point difference**. On TextVQA (OCR), the gap is **77.7% vs 80.9%** (-3.2 points). These small degradations suggest the three-stage training successfully mitigates catastrophic forgetting, though not perfectly.

However, on **eComMMMU** (the external e-commerce benchmark), adapted models significantly outperform:

| Model | eComMMMU |
|-------|----------|
| `SigLIP2 | Llama-3.1-8B` (adapted) | 46.9% |
| `SigLIP2 | e-Llama3.1-8B` (adapted) | **52.2%** |
| `SigLIP2 | Lilium-8B` (adapted) | **58.3%** |
| `Gemma3-4B` (adapted) | 45.4% |
| `Gemma3` (open source) | 34.7% |

The adapted `Gemma3-4B` achieves **45.4%** versus **34.7%** for the unmodified `Gemma3`—a **+10.7 percentage point improvement**. Section 4.5 highlights this as evidence that "adaptation generalizes to multi-images without training," since the internal training set only consists of single-image instructions (Section 3.2.2), yet the model performs well on this multi-image benchmark.

#### Multi-Image Item Intelligence (Table 3)

This production use-case demonstrates the most dramatic improvements through targeted fine-tuning:

| Model | F1-score | Precision | Recall | Verifiable-Correct | Inference Time |
|-------|----------|-----------|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| `Gemma3-27B` (0-shot, all images) | 44.8% | 61.8% | 36.6% | 80.4% | 25.5 sec |
| `Gemma3-27B` (fine-tuned, better labels) | **58.8%** | **71.0%** | **51.9%** | **85.2%** | 19.3 sec |
| `Gemma3-4B` (fine-tuned, better labels) | 53.8% | 68.1% | 49.6% | 82.7% | **6.7 sec** |
| `Gemma3-4B` (fine-tuned, image crops) | **58.0%** | **69.5%** | **51.5%** | **84.7%** | 6.7 sec |

Several findings stand out:

1. **Fine-tuning improves accuracy**: The fine-tuned `Gemma3-27B` achieves **58.8% F1** versus **44.8%** for zero-shot—a **+14 point gain**.
2. **Smaller models can match larger ones with fine-tuning**: The fine-tuned `Gemma3-4B` with image crops reaches **58.0% F1**, nearly matching the fine-tuned 27B model (58.8%) while being **3.8x faster** (6.7 sec vs 25.5 sec, Table 4).
3. **Better labels matter**: Comparing fine-tuned models with standard labels (rows 34-36) versus better labels from bounding box refinement (rows 37-39), the latter consistently outperforms. For `Gemma3-27B`, this is **58.8% vs 52.6%** (+6.2 points).
4. **Targeted cropping beats Pan & Scan**: The authors' targeted image crops (row 41: 58.0%) outperform Gemma3's built-in 'Pan & Scan' feature (row 40: 56.9%).

> **Key Insight**: This result demonstrates that **training data quality and inference optimization can compensate for model size**. A 4B model with task-specific fine-tuning and region-focused inference matches a 27B zero-shot model while being dramatically more efficient.

#### Vision Encoder Ablation (Section 4.3, Table 1 rows 2-3)

The authors compare `SigLIP2` versus `Qwen2.5ViT` with the same text decoder (`e-Llama3.1-8B`):

| Vision Encoder | Aspect Prediction (General) | Deep Fashion (Apparel) | DAE |
|----------------|----------------------------|------------------------|-----|
| `SigLIP2` | 44.4% | 78.9% | 66.1% |
| `Qwen2.5ViT` | **53.3%** | 71.0% | **70.7%** |

The results are **inconclusive**—`Qwen2.5ViT` wins on Aspect Prediction and DAE, but `SigLIP2` wins on Deep Fashion Understanding. Section 4.3 explains this pattern: "the native resolution feature of the Qwen2.5ViT might be beneficial for tasks like aspect prediction, where small image details might be important, however we observe weaker results in more reasoning-oriented results in tasks like fashion understanding."

The gap is "mostly apparent in high resolution scenarios," but the benchmarks use "low to mid resolutions," which "largely decreases the performance enhancements of Qwen2.5ViT." This is an important caveat: vision encoder choice may matter more for high-resolution product imagery than the current benchmarks capture.

#### Text Decoder Ablation (Section 4.4, Tables 1-2)

Three factors influence text decoder performance:

**Domain Knowledge**: Comparing `Llama-3.1-8B` (general), `e-Llama3.1-8B` (e-commerce adapted), and `Lilium-8B` (trained from scratch for e-commerce):

| Text Decoder | Aspect Prediction (General) | eComMMMU |
|--------------|----------------------------|----------|
| `Llama-3.1-8B` | 37.7% | 46.9% |
| `e-Llama3.1-8B` | **44.4%** | **52.2%** |
| `Lilium-8B` | 42.4% | **58.3%** |

Domain-adapted decoders consistently outperform general-purpose backbones on e-commerce tasks. The `Lilium-8B` model achieves **58.3%** on eComMMMU versus **46.9%** for general `Llama-3.1-8B`—a **+11.4 point improvement**.

**General Capability**: Comparing `Qwen3` and `Gemma3` (trained on more data) against `(e)-Llama` and `Lilium`:

| Text Decoder | MMBench | Aspect Prediction (General) |
|--------------|---------|----------------------------|
| `Llama-3.1-8B` | 75.8% | 37.7% |
| `Qwen-3-8B` | **82.5%** | **56.2%** |
| `Gemma3-4B` | 78.3% | 54.8% |

Models with stronger general capabilities (`Qwen3`, `Gemma3`) improve on **both** general benchmarks and e-commerce tasks. Section 4.4 concludes: "better capabilities of the text-decoder help improve performance on general domain VLM benchmarks. More interestingly, we find that they also lead to improvements on some e-commerce specific tasks."

**Model Size**: The effect of parameter count shows **diminishing returns** for some tasks:

| Model | Aspect Prediction (Fashion) | MME (Cogn.) |
|-------|----------------------------|-------------|
| `Lilium-1B` | 48.4% | 278.9 |
| `Lilium-4B` | 49.1% | 334.6 |
| `Lilium-8B` | 49.2% | 335.4 |

Going from 1B to 4B parameters improves Fashion Aspect Prediction by **0.7 points**, but 4B to 8B yields only **0.1 points**. Section 4.4 attributes this to "lack of task complexity"—once the model is large enough to understand the task, additional parameters do not help. However, for **Multi-Image Item Intelligence**, larger models continue to improve (Table 3 rows 34-36: 50.5% → 51.8% → 52.6% F1), suggesting multi-image reasoning remains challenging even for larger models.

### 5.3 Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

The experimental evidence **largely supports** the paper's central claims, with some important caveats.

#### Claim 1: Targeted Adaptation Improves E-commerce Performance

**Supported**. The data shows consistent gains on internal e-commerce benchmarks:
- Aspect Prediction: +15.7 points (`Qwen-3-8B` adapted vs `Qwen3-VL` open)
- eComMMMU: +10.7 points (`Gemma3-4B` adapted vs `Gemma3` open)
- Multi-Image Item Intelligence: +14.0 F1 points (fine-tuned vs zero-shot 27B)

The only exception is Deep Fashion Understanding, where some open-source models match or exceed adapted performance. This is not a failure of adaptation but rather evidence that certain categories (fashion) are well-represented in general VLM training data.

#### Claim 2: General Capabilities Are Preserved

**Partially Supported**. Adapted models show small degradations on general benchmarks:
- MMBench: -1.5 points (`Qwen-3-8B` adapted vs `Qwen3-VL`)
- TextVQA: -3.2 points
- MMMU: -3.3 points

These gaps are modest but non-zero. The three-stage training mitigates catastrophic forgetting but does not eliminate it entirely. For production deployment, this trade-off may be acceptable—a 3% drop in general capability for a 15% gain in domain performance.

#### Claim 3: Backbone-Agnostic Recipe Works Across Architectures

**Supported**. The adaptation improves performance across multiple vision encoders (`SigLIP2`, `Qwen2.5ViT`) and text decoders (`Llama3.1`, `e-Llama`, `Lilium`, `Qwen3`, `Gemma3`). The consistent pattern—domain-adapted decoders outperform general ones on e-commerce tasks—holds regardless of specific architecture choice.

#### Claim 4: Fine-Tuning Smaller Models Can Match Larger Zero-Shot Models

**Strongly Supported**. This is perhaps the most compelling finding:
- Fine-tuned `Gemma3-4B` with crops: **58.0% F1**, 6.7 sec/example
- Zero-shot `Gemma3-27B`: **44.8% F1**, 25.5 sec/example

The 4B model is **both more accurate and 3.8x faster**. This has direct production implications—organizations can deploy smaller, cheaper models without sacrificing quality if they invest in task-specific fine-tuning and inference optimization.

### 5.4 Limitations and Failure Modes

The authors acknowledge several limitations in Section 6 that affect how we interpret the results:

#### Monolingual Scope
All adaptation and evaluation was conducted in **English only**. The paper does not characterize cross-lingual transfer to product ontologies, attribute surface forms, or locale-specific conventions (e.g., EU/JP sizing, currency formats). This limits portability to non-English marketplaces.

#### Platform Dependence
The instruction corpus and benchmarks are sourced from a **single marketplace** (eBay). This creates "potential distributional coupling to that platform's taxonomy, seller conventions, imaging styles (studio vs. user-generated), and metadata density." Portability to other marketplaces with different attribute schemas remains uncertain.

#### LLM-Mediated Supervision and Evaluation
Portions of training signals (pseudo-labels, instruction filtering) and some evaluations rely on LLMs. This introduces:
- **Annotator bias**: The verifying LLM (`Mistral-Small-3-24B`) may have systematic blind spots
- **Evaluator-model family overlap**: Using LLM-as-judge can "inflate or deflate measured gains due to inductive-bias alignment" (Section 6)

For example, if the evaluator LLM shares architecture with the tested model, scores may be artificially inflated. The paper does not report which LLM was used as judge for DAE and Item Intelligence, making this risk difficult to quantify.

#### Coverage Gaps
The **Dynamic Attribute Extraction** set is only ~1,000 examples, and category coverage emphasizes "selected fashion and high-volume verticals." Performance on long-tail categories, rare attributes, region-specific variants, or heavily composited images is "under-constrained." The reported improvements should be interpreted as "evidence of promise under these conditions rather than as guarantees of cross-lingual or cross-platform robustness" (Section 6).

#### Long Image Sequence Handling
For scenarios with **more than 10 images** (rare but possible), models may suffer from Out-of-Memory issues and long inference times. This is "particularly tricky for Multi-Image Item Intelligence and eComMMMU benchmarks" (Section 6). The authors capped images at 10 for eComMMMU evaluation (Appendix A.7), which may not reflect worst-case production scenarios.

### 5.5 Mixed Results and Trade-offs

Several results are **conditional** rather than universally positive:

| Condition | Outcome | Trade-off |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Vision Encoder** | `Qwen2.5ViT` better for detail tasks, `SigLIP2` better for reasoning | Resolution matters—gaps muted at low/mid resolution |
| **Model Size** | Diminishing returns for simple tasks, continues for multi-image | 4B→8B shows minimal gain on Aspect Prediction but helps on Item Intelligence |
| **Deep Fashion** | Open-source models sometimes match adapted | Fashion is well-represented in general VLM training |
| **General Benchmarks** | Small degradations (1-3%) on MMBench, TextVQA | Acceptable trade-off for 10-15% domain gains |
| **Multi-Image** | Fine-tuning essential; zero-shot 27B underperforms fine-tuned 4B | Requires additional curation effort (bounding boxes, crops) |

The most significant trade-off is **data curation cost**. The Visual Verification Pipeline requires running two large models (`InternVL-2.5-26B` and `Mistral-Small-3-24B`) over 15 million listings to produce 4 million verified instructions. This is computationally expensive and may be prohibitive for organizations without similar infrastructure.

### 5.6 Summary Assessment

The experimental analysis is **rigorous and well-designed**, with several strengths:

✓ **Dual-benchmark strategy** validates both domain gains and general capability preservation
✓ **Extensive ablations** across vision encoders, text decoders, and model sizes
✓ **Production-relevant metrics** (inference speed, F1-score, verifiable-correct rates)
✓ **External validation** through eComMMMU public benchmark

However, some **reproducibility gaps** exist:
✗ Training hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size, duration) not specified
✗ Exact LLaVA-OneVision subsets removed during mid-stage training not disclosed
✗ LLM-as-judge evaluator model identity not reported
✗ Limited analysis of failure cases beyond aggregate metrics

Overall, the experiments **convincingly demonstrate** that targeted adaptation improves e-commerce performance while largely preserving general capabilities. The Multi-Image Item Intelligence results are particularly compelling—showing that smaller fine-tuned models can outperform larger zero-shot models with proper data curation and inference optimization. The limitations are honestly acknowledged and do not undermine the core findings, though they caution against overgeneralizing to non-English or non-eBay contexts.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

No adaptation strategy is without constraints. This section critically examines the assumptions underlying the authors' approach, identifies scenarios where the method may fail or underperform, and highlights open questions that remain unresolved. Understanding these limitations is essential for practitioners considering deployment and researchers building upon this work.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions

The adaptation recipe relies on several assumptions that may not hold universally:

**Assumption 1: Visual-Textual Correspondence Can Be Verified via Captioning**

The Visual Verification Pipeline (Figure 2, Section 3.2.1) assumes that if `Mistral-Small-3-24B` can infer an aspect from an `InternVL-2.5-26B`-generated caption, then that aspect is visually grounded. This creates a **dependency chain**: errors in captioning propagate to verification, and errors in verification corrupt training data. The paper does not quantify captioning accuracy or verification false-positive rates.

> "Given the generated caption and item aspects, we employ Mistral-Small-3-24B to verify which of these aspects can be inferred from the caption and thus from the image itself." (Section 3.2.1)

This assumption breaks down for attributes that require **specialized domain knowledge** to verify visually. For example, distinguishing "100% Cotton" from "Cotton Blend" may require texture analysis beyond what a captioning model captures. The pipeline may systematically exclude valid but hard-to-describe attributes, biasing the training data toward easily verbalizable features.

**Assumption 2: Single-Image Adaptation Transfers to Multi-Image Tasks**

The general e-commerce adaptation uses only **single-image instructions** (Section 3.2.2), yet the authors claim adaptation "generalizes to multi-images without training" (Section 4.5). While the `eComMMMU` results support this (adapted `Gemma3-4B` achieves +11% over open-source), this transfer is **not guaranteed** for all multi-image scenarios.

The Multi-Image Item Intelligence task required **separate fine-tuning** on 100,000 multi-image items (Section 3.2.3), contradicting the claim that single-image adaptation suffices. The distinction is subtle but important: the model may generalize to *seeing* multiple images, but extracting compliance attributes across images requires task-specific supervision. Practitioners should not expect single-image adaptation to solve multi-image aggregation problems without additional fine-tuning.

**Assumption 3: General Capabilities Are Preserved Through Three-Stage Training**

The three-stage training procedure (Section 3.2.2) is designed to prevent catastrophic forgetting, but Table 2 shows **non-zero degradation** on general benchmarks:

| Benchmark | Adapted `Qwen-3-8B` | Open `Qwen3-VL` | Gap |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------------|-----|
| MMBench | 82.5% | 84.0% | -1.5 pts |
| TextVQA | 77.7% | 80.9% | -3.2 pts |
| MMMU | 49.1% | 52.4% | -3.3 pts |

These gaps are modest but consistent across all adapted models. For deployments requiring **strong general-purpose performance** alongside e-commerce tasks, this trade-off may be unacceptable. The paper frames this as "preserving broad capabilities" (Abstract), but the data shows preservation is **relative, not absolute**.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

Several important scenarios fall outside the scope of this work:

**Long Image Sequences (10+ Images)**

Section 6 explicitly acknowledges that "in scenarios with more than 10 images (rare), we noticed our models may suffer from Out-of-Memory (OOM) issues as well as long inference times." The authors capped `eComMMMU` evaluation at 10 images (Appendix A.7), but production listings can exceed this—particularly for electronics, real estate, or vintage items requiring extensive documentation.

The proposed optimization (cropping + deduplication via perceptual hashing, Appendix A.5) reduces median images from 12 to 4, but this is applied only to the **Multi-Image Item Intelligence fine-tuning set**, not the general adaptation. For general e-commerce queries on listings with 15+ images, the model may fail entirely due to context window limits or memory constraints.

**Long-Tail Categories and Rare Attributes**

The evaluation coverage is **skewed toward high-volume verticals**. Section 6 notes that "category coverage emphasizes selected fashion and high-volume verticals," with Dynamic Attribute Extraction containing only ~1,000 examples. Performance on long-tail categories (e.g., industrial equipment, musical instruments, collectibles) is "under-constrained."

This matters because long-tail categories often have **different attribute schemas** and visual characteristics. A model trained primarily on fashion and electronics may not generalize to products where visual attributes are less standardized or where domain-specific terminology is required.

**Cross-Lingual and Locale-Specific Conventions**

All adaptation, supervision, and evaluation were conducted in **English only** (Section 6). The paper does not characterize:

- Cross-lingual transfer to product ontologies in other languages
- Locale-specific sizing conventions (EU vs. US vs. JP clothing sizes)
- Currency and decimal format variations
- Multi-script OCR for size charts or packaging text

For global marketplaces, this is a **significant limitation**. A model adapted for eBay US may fail on eBay Germany due to language-specific attribute naming or region-specific compliance requirements. The authors acknowledge this limits "portability to other marketplaces with different attribute schema or listing norms" (Section 6).

**Heavily Composited or Atypical Images**

The Visual Verification Pipeline assumes product images are **photographs of physical items**. It does not address:

- Composite images (multiple products in one frame)
- Rendered or CGI product visualizations
- Heavily edited images with overlays, watermarks, or promotional text
- User-uploaded photos with cluttered backgrounds

These scenarios are common in e-commerce but may confuse the captioning and verification models. The paper does not report performance stratified by image quality or type, leaving this failure mode unquantified.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

**Data Curation Cost**

The Visual Verification Pipeline requires running two large models over 15 million raw listings:

- `InternVL-2.5-26B` for captioning (26 billion parameters)
- `Mistral-Small-3-24B` for verification (24 billion parameters)

This produces ~4 million verified instructions—a **73% filtering rate**. The computational cost of this curation is not disclosed, but it represents a **significant barrier to entry**. Organizations without access to large-scale GPU infrastructure (the authors used up to 120 H100 GPUs, Section 4.1) may find this pipeline prohibitively expensive.

Furthermore, the pipeline is **not incremental**. If new listings arrive daily, the verification must be re-run or a streaming verification system must be built. The paper does not address how to maintain data freshness without re-processing the entire corpus.

**Training Infrastructure Requirements**

Training was conducted on "NVIDIA H100 GPUs, using up to 120 GPUs connected via NVLink and InfiniBand" (Section 4.1). This level of infrastructure is typical for large tech companies but **inaccessible to most practitioners**. The paper does not explore whether the adaptation recipe scales down to smaller GPU clusters or whether training time increases linearly with reduced parallelism.

**Inference Latency for Multi-Image Tasks**

Even with optimization, Multi-Image Item Intelligence inference takes **6.7 seconds per example** for the fine-tuned `Gemma3-4B` model (Table 4). For high-throughput production systems processing thousands of listings per hour, this latency may be unacceptable. The paper does not explore further optimization strategies such as:

- Model quantization
- Speculative decoding
- Batch processing optimizations
- Caching strategies for repeated queries

### 6.4 Weaknesses in Experimental Design

**Reproducibility Gaps**

Several critical details are omitted, limiting reproducibility:

| Missing Detail | Impact |
|----------------|--------|
| Exact learning rates and batch sizes | Cannot replicate training dynamics |
| Training duration per stage | Cannot estimate compute requirements |
| Which LLaVA-OneVision subsets were removed in mid-stage training (Section 3.2.2) | Cannot reproduce data mixture |
| Identity of LLM-as-judge evaluator for DAE and Item Intelligence (Section 6) | Cannot assess evaluator bias risk |
| Exact sampling ratios for e-commerce vs. general data in final mixture | Cannot reproduce balance between domain and general capabilities |

The authors state these omissions are common in industry papers where "such details may be proprietary," but this reduces the paper's value as a **reproducible recipe** (a stated contribution in the Abstract).

**Evaluator-Model Family Overlap Risk**

Section 6 acknowledges that "evaluator–model family overlap can inflate or deflate measured gains due to inductive-bias alignment in 'LLM-as-judge' scenarios." However, the paper does not report:

- Which LLM was used as the judge for DAE and Item Intelligence
- Whether the judge model shares architecture with tested models
- Sensitivity analysis showing how results change with different judges

If the judge is a `Gemma3` variant and tested models include `Gemma3`, scores may be artificially inflated. This is particularly concerning for the Multi-Image Item Intelligence results, where fine-tuned `Gemma3` models show large gains over zero-shot baselines.

**Limited Failure Mode Analysis**

The paper reports aggregate metrics but provides **minimal analysis of failure cases**. Figure 1 shows example errors from a baseline model, but there is no equivalent analysis for the adapted models. Questions left unanswered:

- What types of attributes are most frequently hallucinated after adaptation?
- Do errors cluster in specific product categories?
- Are there systematic biases introduced by the verification pipeline?

Without this analysis, practitioners cannot anticipate where the model will fail in production.

### 6.5 Conditional Results and Trade-offs

Several findings are **context-dependent** rather than universally positive:

**Vision Encoder Choice Is Inconclusive**

Section 4.3 reports "no clear winner between the two encoders" (`SigLIP2` vs. `Qwen2.5ViT`). The performance gap is "mostly apparent in high resolution scenarios," but benchmarks use "low to mid resolutions." This means:

- For organizations using high-resolution product imagery, `Qwen2.5ViT` may be superior
- For standard-resolution pipelines, encoder choice matters less
- The paper does not provide guidance for choosing between encoders in new deployments

**Diminishing Returns on Model Size**

Section 4.4 finds that "going from 4 billion to 8 billion does not" significantly improve performance on some tasks:

| Model | Aspect Prediction (Fashion) |
|-------|----------------------------|
| `Lilium-1B` | 48.4% |
| `Lilium-4B` | 49.1% |
| `Lilium-8B` | 49.2% |

However, for Multi-Image Item Intelligence, larger models continue to improve (Table 3: 50.5% → 51.8% → 52.6% F1 for 4B/12B/27B). This creates a **deployment dilemma**: organizations must profile their specific task mix to determine optimal model size, as there is no universal "best" choice.

**Deep Fashion Understanding Shows Mixed Adaptation Benefits**

Table 1 reveals that for Deep Fashion Understanding, **open-source models sometimes outperform adapted models**:

| Model | Apparel | Sneakers & Handbags |
|-------|---------|---------------------|
| Adapted `SigLIP2 | Qwen-3-8B` | 79.8% | 81.6% |
| Open `Qwen3-VL` | 84.3% | 84.6% |

Section 4.2 acknowledges this: "some external models do perform very well on Deep Fashion Understanding, they do fall behind on most e-commerce specific benchmarks." This suggests fashion is **well-represented in general VLM training data**, reducing the marginal benefit of domain adaptation. Organizations focused primarily on fashion may not see significant gains from this adaptation recipe.

### 6.6 Open Questions for Future Work

The paper leaves several important questions unresolved:

1. **What is the minimum viable dataset size?** The authors use ~4M e-commerce instructions, but is this necessary? Could similar gains be achieved with 1M or 500K examples? This affects accessibility for smaller organizations.

2. **How does adaptation interact with continued pretraining?** The text decoders include both general-purpose (`Llama3.1`, `Qwen3`) and domain-adapted (`e-Llama`, `Lilium`) variants. Would further pretraining on e-commerce text before VLM adaptation yield additional gains?

3. **What is the optimal general-to-domain data ratio?** The paper mixes LLaVA-OneVision data with e-commerce data but does not systematically ablate this ratio. Section 3.2.2 states percentages "equaling part of e-commerce total," but the overall mixture proportion is unspecified.

4. **Can the verification pipeline be made more efficient?** Running two large models for verification is expensive. Could smaller models, rule-based filters, or active learning reduce curation costs without sacrificing data quality?

5. **How does the approach scale to non-English markets?** The monolingual limitation (Section 6) is acknowledged but not addressed. Multi-lingual adaptation strategies remain an open research direction.

### 6.7 Summary Assessment

The limitations identified in this paper are **honestly acknowledged** by the authors (Section 6 is unusually candid for an industry paper), but they represent real constraints for deployment:

| Limitation | Severity | Mitigation |
|------------|----------|------------|
| Monolingual scope | High for global deployments | Requires separate adaptation per language |
| Platform dependence | Medium | May transfer to similar marketplaces; untested for dissimilar ones |
| LLM-mediated evaluation | Medium | Risk of biased metrics; needs human validation |
| Long image sequences | High for certain categories | Requires additional engineering (chunking, summarization) |
| Data curation cost | High for small organizations | May be prohibitive without large-scale infrastructure |
| Reproducibility gaps | Medium | Limits independent validation and extension |

The core contribution—a reproducible adaptation recipe—remains valuable despite these limitations. However, practitioners should interpret the results as **"evidence of promise under these conditions rather than as guarantees of cross-lingual or cross-platform robustness"** (Section 6). The approach works well for English-language, eBay-like marketplaces with standard product imagery and moderate multi-image requirements. Extensions to other contexts require additional validation and potentially significant modifications to the pipeline.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than present a set of adapted models—it establishes a **methodological framework** for domain-specific VLM adaptation that balances specialization with general capability preservation. In this section, I examine how this work shifts the research landscape, what it enables for future investigation, where it can be practically deployed, and when practitioners should adopt this approach versus alternatives.

### 7.1 Shifting the Landscape of Domain-Specific VLM Adaptation

The paper introduces three paradigm shifts that change how we think about adapting Vision-Language Models for specialized domains:

**From Architecture-Centric to Data-Centric Adaptation**

Prior e-commerce VLM research often focused on proposing new architectures or model families (e.g., Bai et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2024, cited in Section 2). This work demonstrates that **data curation quality matters more than architectural novelty** for domain performance. The Visual Verification Pipeline (Figure 2, Section 3.2.1) transforms noisy user-generated listings into high-quality training signal without requiring new model components. The key insight is that ~4 million verified instructions from 15 million raw listings (a 73% filtering rate) produces better results than training on unverified data at larger scale.

This shifts the research question from "What architecture works best?" to "How do we verify visual-textual correspondence at scale?" Organizations should invest in data verification infrastructure before experimenting with model architectures.

**From Single-Objective to Dual-Competence Training**

The three-stage training procedure (Section 3.2.2) explicitly targets **dual competence**: strong e-commerce performance alongside preserved general capabilities. Table 2 shows adapted models achieve competitive scores on MMBench (82.5% for `SigLIP2 | Qwen-3-8B` vs. 84.0% for open-source `Qwen3-VL`) while dramatically outperforming on e-commerce tasks (Table 1: 56.2% vs. 40.5% on Aspect Prediction). This challenges the assumption that domain adaptation requires sacrificing general capabilities.

The implication is that organizations need not maintain separate models for general vs. domain-specific queries. A single adapted VLM can handle both, reducing infrastructure complexity and deployment costs.

**From Manual Annotation to Self-Supervised Verification**

The Visual Verification Pipeline leverages existing VLMs (`InternVL-2.5-26B` for captioning, `Mistral-Small-3-24B` for verification) as annotators, creating a **self-improving data curation cycle**. This approach scales to 15 million listings where human annotation would be prohibitively expensive. As captioning and verification models improve, the quality of curated training data automatically improves—a virtuous cycle that compounds over time.

This suggests a broader research direction: using capable models to generate training data for specialized adaptations, rather than relying solely on human annotation or raw web scraping.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

The paper's findings and limitations surface several concrete research questions that warrant follow-up investigation:

**Data Efficiency and Minimum Viable Dataset Size**

The authors use ~4 million e-commerce instructions but do not ablate dataset size. A critical follow-up question is: **What is the minimum dataset size required for effective adaptation?** 

> "The reported improvements should be interpreted as evidence of promise under these conditions rather than as guarantees of cross-lingual or cross-platform robustness." (Section 6)

Researchers should experiment with 500K, 1M, and 2M subsets to identify diminishing returns. This would make the adaptation recipe accessible to organizations without eBay-scale data resources. If similar gains can be achieved with 1M examples, the barrier to entry drops significantly.

**Optimal General-to-Domain Data Ratios**

Section 3.2.2 specifies percentages within the e-commerce portion (45% VQA, 30% DAE, 12.5% Instruction Following, 12.5% Listings) but does not disclose the **overall ratio of e-commerce to general-purpose data** in the final training mixture. Appendix A.6 lists 47 LLaVA-OneVision datasets with varying sampling strategies ("first:10%", "first:20%", "all"), but the aggregate proportion remains unspecified.

Follow-up work should systematically ablate this ratio:
- 10% e-commerce / 90% general
- 50% e-commerce / 50% general
- 90% e-commerce / 10% general

This would identify the "sweet spot" where domain gains maximize without excessive general capability degradation. The current results show -1.5 to -3.3 point drops on general benchmarks (Table 2), but it is unclear whether different ratios would improve this trade-off.

**Cross-Lingual Adaptation Strategies**

Section 6 explicitly acknowledges the **monolingual limitation**: "All model adaptation, supervision, and evaluation were conducted in English." This is a significant gap for global marketplaces where product ontologies, sizing conventions, and compliance requirements vary by locale.

Research directions include:
- **Multilingual verification pipelines**: Can the Visual Verification Pipeline operate across languages using multilingual captioning models?
- **Cross-lingual transfer**: Does adaptation in English transfer to other languages, or must each language be adapted separately?
- **Locale-specific attributes**: How should models handle region-specific conventions (EU vs. US vs. JP sizing, currency formats, multi-script OCR)?

This is not merely an engineering challenge—it requires understanding how visual attributes map to language-specific ontologies.

**Efficient Multi-Image Handling Beyond 10 Images**

Section 6 notes that "in scenarios with more than 10 images (rare), we noticed our models may suffer from Out-of-Memory (OOM) issues." The authors capped eComMMMU evaluation at 10 images (Appendix A.7), but production listings in categories like electronics, real estate, or vintage collectibles can exceed this.

Follow-up research should explore:
- **Token-efficient vision encoders**: Recent work like `LLaVA-Mini` (Zhang et al., 2025, cited in Section 6) uses "one vision token" strategies that could reduce memory footprint.
- **Hierarchical image processing**: Process images in batches, summarize each batch, then aggregate summaries.
- **Selective image attention**: Learn to identify which images are most informative for a given query rather than processing all images equally.

The cropping and deduplication approach in Appendix A.5 (reducing median images from 12 to 4) is a step in this direction, but it applies only to the Multi-Image Item Intelligence fine-tuning set, not general adaptation.

**Verification Pipeline Error Analysis**

The Visual Verification Pipeline assumes captioning and verification models are reliable, but the paper does not quantify **false positive and false negative rates**. If `InternVL-2.5-26B` fails to caption a visible attribute, or if `Mistral-Small-3-24B` incorrectly rejects a valid aspect, the training data becomes biased.

Researchers should:
- Sample verified instructions for human evaluation to measure verification accuracy
- Analyze which attribute types are most frequently misclassified (e.g., material composition vs. color vs. brand)
- Explore active learning strategies where uncertain cases are routed to human annotators

This would make the pipeline more transparent and allow practitioners to estimate data quality before training.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The adaptation recipe enables several concrete production applications beyond the benchmarks tested in this paper:

**Automated Listing Creation and Enhancement**

The "Listings" task (12.5% of the instruction tuning set, Section 3.2.2) trains models to generate full product descriptions from images. This supports:
- **Seller tools**: Automatically generate titles, attributes, and descriptions from product photos, reducing listing creation time.
- **Listing quality improvement**: Identify missing attributes in existing listings and suggest enhancements.
- **Cross-platform migration**: Help sellers migrate listings between marketplaces by reformatting attributes to match different schemas.

The 12.5% allocation suggests this is a lower-priority use case than VQA and attribute extraction, but it remains valuable for seller-facing products.

**Compliance and Safety Verification**

The Multi-Image Item Intelligence task (Section 3.1, 3.2.3) targets regulatory compliance: brand verification, warning labels, ingredients, certifications. Applications include:
- **Proactive compliance screening**: Flag listings that may violate safety regulations before they go live.
- **Recall matching**: When a product is recalled, quickly identify affected listings by matching compliance attributes.
- **Counterfeit detection**: Verify brand claims against visual evidence (e.g., logo placement, packaging details).

Table 3 shows fine-tuned models achieve 58.8% F1-score with 85.2% verifiable-correct rate (row 39), demonstrating practical viability. The 3.8x inference speedup for the 4B model (Table 4) makes this scalable for high-volume screening.

**Visual Search and Recommendation**

The Aspect Prediction and Deep Fashion Understanding benchmarks (Section 3.1) train models to extract structured attributes from images. This enables:
- **Visual similarity search**: Find products with matching attributes (e.g., "sleeve length: short", "neckline: crew neck").
- **Faceted filtering**: Allow customers to filter search results by attributes extracted from images, not just text metadata.
- **Personalized recommendations**: Recommend products based on visual attribute preferences learned from user behavior.

The +15.7 percentage point improvement on Aspect Prediction (Table 1: adapted `Qwen-3-8B` at 56.2% vs. open `Qwen3-VL` at 40.5%) directly translates to better search relevance.

**Customer Support Automation**

The VQA portion (45% of the instruction set, Section 3.2.2) trains models to answer free-form questions about products. Applications include:
- **Pre-purchase queries**: Answer customer questions about product features, compatibility, or suitability.
- **Post-purchase support**: Help customers identify product attributes for returns, warranties, or troubleshooting.
- **Chatbot integration**: Embed the adapted VLM in customer service chatbots for multimodal query handling.

The inclusion of both "with context" and "without context" variants teaches the model to handle cases where metadata is available versus cases requiring pure visual analysis.

**Catalog Management and Normalization**

The Dynamic Attribute Extraction task (30% of the instruction set, Section 3.2.2) trains models to enumerate attributes without a predefined schema. This supports:
- **Schema harmonization**: Normalize attributes across different seller conventions into a unified catalog structure.
- **Attribute gap identification**: Identify which attributes are missing from listings and prioritize data collection.
- **Category expansion**: Automatically discover new attribute types for emerging product categories.

The LLM-as-a-judge evaluation (Section 3.1) acknowledges that open-ended extraction cannot be measured through string matching, reflecting the real-world complexity of catalog management.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

Practitioners considering this adaptation approach should evaluate whether it fits their constraints. Below I provide concrete guidance on when to adopt this method versus alternatives.

**When to Prefer This Approach**

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Noisy user-generated listings** | ✓ Use Visual Verification Pipeline | Seller metadata is unreliable; verification ensures visual-textual correspondence (Section 3.2.1) |
| **Need to preserve general capabilities** | ✓ Use three-stage training | Prevents catastrophic forgetting; Table 2 shows only -1.5 to -3.3 point drops on general benchmarks |
| **Multi-image compliance tasks** | ✓ Use separate fine-tuning + cropping | Table 3 shows fine-tuned 4B model (58.0% F1) outperforms zero-shot 27B (44.8% F1) with 3.8x speedup |
| **Domain-adapted text decoder available** | ✓ Use e-commerce LLM backbone | Table 1 shows `e-Llama3.1-8B` (44.4%) outperforms `Llama-3.1-8B` (37.7%) on Aspect Prediction |
| **High-resolution product imagery** | ✓ Consider `Qwen2.5ViT` encoder | Section 4.3 notes native resolution benefits tasks requiring small detail detection |

**When to Consider Alternatives**

| Scenario | Alternative | Rationale |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Limited GPU infrastructure** | Use smaller models + less data | Training used 120 H100 GPUs (Section 4.1); smaller organizations may need to reduce scale |
| **Fashion-only focus** | Consider open-source VLMs | Table 1 shows `Qwen3-VL` (84.3%) outperforms adapted models (79.8%) on Deep Fashion Apparel |
| **Non-English marketplaces** | Await multilingual adaptation | Section 6 acknowledges monolingual limitation; cross-lingual transfer is untested |
| **Listings with 10+ images** | Implement chunking/summarization | Section 6 notes OOM issues beyond 10 images; current approach does not handle this |
| **Need full reproducibility** | Request additional details from authors | Training hyperparameters, exact data mixtures, and evaluator identity are not disclosed |

**Integration Checklist**

Before adopting this adaptation recipe, practitioners should verify:

1. **Data Availability**: Do you have access to ~15 million raw listings to produce ~4 million verified instructions? If not, can you achieve similar gains with smaller datasets?

2. **Infrastructure**: Can you support training on large GPU clusters (up to 120 H100s per Section 4.1)? If not, what is the minimum viable configuration?

3. **Verification Models**: Do you have access to capable captioning (`InternVL-2.5-26B`-class) and verification (`Mistral-Small-3-24B`-class) models for the Visual Verification Pipeline?

4. **Evaluation Strategy**: Will you use string matching (for closed-set tasks) or LLM-as-a-judge (for open-ended extraction)? If the latter, which evaluator model will you use, and how will you mitigate evaluator-model family overlap (Section 6)?

5. **Multi-Image Requirements**: Does your use case require handling more than 10 images per item? If so, you will need to implement additional engineering beyond what this paper provides.

6. **Language Support**: Is English sufficient for your deployment? If not, you will need to develop multilingual adaptation strategies not covered in this work.

**Expected Performance Gains**

Based on the paper's results, practitioners can expect:

- **E-commerce tasks**: +10 to +15 percentage point improvements over open-source VLMs (Table 1: Aspect Prediction 56.2% vs. 40.5%; Table 2: eComMMMU 45.4% vs. 34.7% for `Gemma3-4B`)
- **General capabilities**: -1.5 to -3.3 percentage point degradation on benchmarks like MMBench, TextVQA, MMMU (Table 2)
- **Inference efficiency**: 3.8x speedup for fine-tuned 4B models vs. zero-shot 27B models on Multi-Image tasks (Table 4: 6.7 sec vs. 25.5 sec per example)
- **Multi-Image accuracy**: +14 F1 points for fine-tuned vs. zero-shot on Item Intelligence (Table 3: 58.8% vs. 44.8%)

These are **conditional expectations**—actual gains depend on data quality, task complexity, and infrastructure. The paper's limitations (Section 6) caution against overgeneralizing to non-English or non-eBay contexts.

### 7.5 Summary: A Foundation for Production VLM Deployment

This paper provides more than adaptation results—it offers a **blueprint for production VLM deployment** in specialized domains. The key takeaways for practitioners are:

1. **Invest in data verification before model architecture**. The Visual Verification Pipeline is the foundation; without it, models learn incorrect image-text associations.

2. **Use progressive specialization to preserve general capabilities**. The three-stage training procedure balances domain gains with general competence, enabling single-model deployment.

3. **Fine-tune smaller models for production efficiency**. A fine-tuned 4B model can outperform a zero-shot 27B model with proper data curation and inference optimization (Table 3, Table 4).

4. **Acknowledge limitations transparently**. The paper's candid discussion of monolingual scope, platform dependence, and evaluator bias (Section 6) sets a standard for responsible deployment.

For researchers, this work opens directions in data efficiency, cross-lingual adaptation, and multi-image handling. For practitioners, it provides a reproducible recipe (with noted gaps) for adapting VLMs to e-commerce and potentially other attribute-centric domains. The ultimate contribution is demonstrating that **targeted adaptation, not architectural novelty, drives domain performance**—a principle that extends beyond e-commerce to any domain where visual-textual correspondence matters.
