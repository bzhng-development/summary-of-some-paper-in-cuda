## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the high computational cost and lack of systematic understanding in developing Video Large Multimodal Models (video-LMMs) by identifying "Scaling Consistency," a phenomenon where design decisions made on moderately sized models (2–4B parameters) and datasets (~500K samples) reliably transfer to larger architectures with $R^2 > 0.9$. Leveraging these insights to optimize video sampling, encoder selection, and training schedules, the authors introduce **Apollo**, a family of state-of-the-art models where **Apollo-3B** outperforms most existing 7B models (scoring 55.1 on LongVideoBench) and **Apollo-7B** achieves superior results (70.9 on MLVU) that rival 30B-parameter systems. Additionally, the work curates **ApolloBench**, a streamlined evaluation suite that reduces assessment time by 41× while maintaining high correlation with existing benchmarks, effectively democratizing efficient video-LMM research.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: A Design Space Shrouded in Uncertainty
Despite the rapid proliferation of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) capable of processing images and text, the extension of these capabilities to **video** remains fragmented and inefficient. The central problem this paper addresses is the **lack of systematic understanding** regarding the design choices that drive video-LMM performance.

Currently, the field operates in a "black box" manner where critical architectural and training decisions—such as how to sample frames from a video, which vision encoders to use, how to compress visual tokens, and how to structure training schedules—are often made without empirical justification. As noted in the introduction, "many design decisions in this domain are made without proper justification or analysis." This uncertainty is exacerbated by the **prohibitive computational cost** of experimentation. Training and evaluating video-LMMs requires processing massive amounts of temporal data, often necessitating hundreds of GPU hours for a single run. Consequently, researchers cannot afford to perform the exhaustive ablation studies required to isolate effective strategies, leading to a stagnation where new models are built on unverified assumptions rather than grounded principles.

### Why This Matters: The Unique Challenge of Video
The significance of this problem extends beyond mere efficiency; it strikes at the heart of what makes video distinct from static images. Videos provide a rich, dynamic information source containing **nuanced temporal and spatial features** that static images cannot capture, such as motion dynamics, causality, and event progression.

However, integrating this temporal dimension introduces a significantly broader and more complex design space compared to image-based LMMs:
*   **Temporal Sampling:** Unlike images, videos must be sampled. Should the model see 8 frames or 64? Should they be spread uniformly across the duration, or sampled at a constant frame rate (fps)?
*   **Token Explosion:** A video generates orders of magnitude more visual tokens than an image. Without effective resampling strategies, the context window of the language model is quickly overwhelmed.
*   **Encoder Suitability:** It is unclear whether encoders trained on static images (which have high spatial resolution) or specialized video encoders (which capture motion but may have weaker spatial details) are superior for downstream reasoning.

Without answering these fundamental questions, the development of video-LMMs risks lagging behind their image-based counterparts, failing to unlock the potential of hour-long video understanding required for real-world applications like surveillance analysis, educational content summarization, and autonomous agent navigation.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Existing research has attempted to bridge the gap between image and video LMMs, but these approaches suffer from specific shortcomings that this paper seeks to rectify:

1.  **Naive Extensions of Image Models:** Early works primarily extended image-LMMs by simply feeding multiple frames into the model or applying video-specific fine-tuning without re-architecting the core components. These methods often fail to effectively model long-range temporal dependencies.
2.  **Ad-hoc Design Choices:** Recent methods have introduced diverse but unverified strategies, such as extending context windows, mixing modalities in complex ways, or employing agent workflows. While innovative, the impact of these specific design decisions on performance is "poorly understood." For instance, many models default to **uniform frame sampling** (picking $N$ frames evenly across a video) for simplicity, despite the fact that this alters the effective "playback speed" presented to the model depending on the video's original duration.
3.  **Inefficient Evaluation Protocols:** The field relies on a proliferation of heavy benchmarks (e.g., Video-MME, MLVU, LongVideoBench). Evaluating a single 3B-parameter model across these suites can require **184 A100 GPU hours**. Furthermore, the authors reveal that many of these benchmarks contain significant redundancy and, critically, can often be solved using only text or a single static frame, meaning they do not actually measure video perception capabilities. This makes iterative research slow and potentially misleading.
4.  **Rigid Scaling Laws:** Traditional scaling laws predict performance based on model size but typically require training multiple models from scratch across different scales to derive trends. In the context of LMMs, which integrate multiple pre-trained components (vision encoders, LLMs, connectors), applying traditional scaling laws is impractical due to the immense cost and the lack of available pre-trained components at every intermediate scale.

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself as a **foundational systematic exploration** intended to democratize and accelerate video-LMM research. Rather than proposing a single incremental architectural tweak, the authors conduct a comprehensive dissection of the entire video-LMM pipeline.

The work distinguishes itself through three key strategic shifts:
*   **From Scaling Laws to Scaling Consistency:** Instead of demanding expensive multi-scale training runs, the paper introduces and validates **Scaling Consistency**. This phenomenon demonstrates that design decisions optimized on moderately sized models ($\sim$2–4B parameters) and datasets ($\sim$500K samples) transfer reliably to much larger models ($R^2 > 0.9$). This insight fundamentally changes the research methodology, allowing for rapid, cost-effective prototyping on smaller hardware.
*   **From Assumption to Evidence:** The paper rigorously tests long-held assumptions. For example, it provides empirical evidence that **frames-per-second (fps) sampling** is vastly superior to uniform sampling for preserving temporal dynamics, and that combining a strong image encoder (SigLIP) with a video encoder (InternVideo2) yields better results than either alone.
*   **From Redundant to Efficient Evaluation:** By analyzing the correlation and modality reliance of existing benchmarks, the authors curate **ApolloBench**, a streamlined evaluation suite. This tool reduces evaluation time by **41×** while filtering out questions that do not require video perception, ensuring that future progress is measured on genuine temporal understanding rather than language priors or static image recognition.

In essence, this work serves as a "guidebook" for the field, replacing guesswork with data-driven principles to enable the development of efficient, high-performance video-LMMs like the **Apollo** family, which achieves state-of-the-art results without relying on brute-force scaling.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper is a systematic empirical study that replaces guesswork in Video Large Multimodal Model (video-LMM) design with data-driven principles, culminating in the **Apollo** family of models which achieve state-of-the-art performance through optimized sampling, encoding, and training strategies rather than brute-force scaling. The core idea is that by rigorously isolating variables—such as frame sampling rates, encoder combinations, and data mixtures—on moderately sized models, one can derive universal design rules that transfer efficiently to larger systems.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system being built is a **Video Large Multimodal Model (video-LMM)** capable of ingesting hour-long videos and answering complex questions about their temporal and spatial content. It solves the problem of "temporal blindness" and computational inefficiency in existing models by enforcing a constant frame-rate sampling strategy and using a dual-encoder architecture that merges high-quality static image features with motion-aware video features.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The Apollo architecture functions as a sequential pipeline where raw video is first chopped into fixed-duration clips, then processed simultaneously by two distinct vision encoders (one for spatial detail, one for temporal dynamics). The resulting feature maps are interpolated to match dimensions, concatenated, and passed through a **Perceiver Resampler** that compresses the visual information into a fixed, small number of tokens per clip. These compressed tokens are then injected into a Large Language Model (LLM) alongside text prompts, where the LLM performs autoregressive generation to produce answers.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Scaling Consistency Methodology:** We first explain the experimental framework that validates using small models (2–4B) as reliable proxies for large models, which justifies the efficiency of the subsequent ablation studies.
*   **Video Sampling and Tokenization:** We detail the critical shift from uniform sampling to frames-per-second (fps) sampling and how this preserves temporal dynamics for the model.
*   **Dual-Encoder Representation:** We analyze the specific combination of `SigLIP` and `InternVideo2` encoders and why their fusion outperforms single-encoder approaches.
*   **Token Resampling and Integration:** We describe the mechanism of the Perceiver Resampler and the specific textual formatting used to inject video tokens into the LLM context.
*   **Training Protocol and Data Composition:** We outline the three-stage training schedule and the precise mixture of text, image, and video data required to prevent catastrophic forgetting while maximizing video understanding.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### The Principle of Scaling Consistency
The foundation of this work is the discovery of **Scaling Consistency**, a phenomenon where design decisions optimized on moderately sized models reliably predict performance on larger models, thereby bypassing the need for expensive full-scale ablation studies.
*   The authors trained 84 distinct model variations by crossing 21 different design configurations (covering architecture, sampling, and data) with four different Large Language Model (LLM) backbones: `Qwen2-0.5B`, `Qwen2-1.5B`, `Qwen1.5-4B`, and `Qwen2-7B`.
*   By calculating the coefficient of determination ($R^2$) between the performance rankings of these models, they found that design choices made on models with approximately 2–4 billion parameters correlate with 7B models at an $R^2 > 0.9$.
*   Specifically, the correlation between the 4B and 7B variants was measured at $R^2 = 0.938$, indicating that a design choice improving the 4B model will almost certainly improve the 7B model.
*   This consistency extends to dataset size; the study reveals that training on a subset of approximately 500,000 samples is sufficient to identify optimal design patterns, as the correlation with larger datasets plateaus beyond this point.
*   This finding fundamentally alters the research methodology, allowing the authors to perform exhaustive hyperparameter searches on 3B models using 750K samples, confident that the results will transfer to the final Apollo-7B system.

#### Video Sampling: Preserving Temporal Dynamics
A critical failure mode in prior video-LMMs is the use of **uniform frame sampling**, where $N$ frames are picked evenly across a video regardless of its duration, which distorts the perceived speed of events.
*   In uniform sampling, if a model samples 16 frames from a 10-second video, it sees 1.6 frames per second (fps), but if it samples 16 frames from a 100-second video, it sees only 0.16 fps, effectively presenting the model with inconsistent "playback speeds" during training.
*   The authors propose and validate **fps sampling**, where frames are selected based on a constant temporal rate (e.g., 2 fps) rather than a fixed count, ensuring the model always observes motion at a consistent speed.
*   Experiments demonstrate that models trained with uniform sampling consistently underperform those trained with fps sampling, even when tested with the same number of frames, proving the deficit arises from the training distribution mismatch.
*   The optimal configuration identified involves a trade-off between **tokens per second (tps)** and **frames per second (fps)**, with the study finding that maintaining **8 to 32 tokens per frame** yields the strongest performance across diverse tasks.
*   For the final Apollo models, the system samples video clips at a constant **2 fps**, and if a video exceeds the maximum token limit, the system spaces out the *clips* uniformly rather than altering the frame rate within the clips.

#### Dual-Encoder Visual Representation
To capture both high-fidelity spatial details and coherent temporal motion, Apollo employs a **dual-encoder architecture** that fuses features from a specialized image encoder and a specialized video encoder.
*   The study evaluated seven distinct encoders, including self-supervised models like `DINOv2` and `VideoMAE`, and language-supervised models like `SigLIP` and `InternVideo2`.
*   Results showed that language-supervised encoders consistently outperform self-supervised ones, with **`SigLIP-SO400M`** emerging as the superior single encoder for overall performance due to its strong spatial representation.
*   However, `SigLIP` lacks explicit temporal modeling, while video-specific encoders like **`InternVideo2`** excel at temporal perception but may lack spatial resolution; thus, the optimal strategy is to combine them.
*   The architecture processes video clips (typically 4 frames for `InternVideo2`) through both encoders in parallel.
*   The output features from both encoders are interpolated to ensure matching spatial dimensions and then **concatenated along the channel dimension**, creating a unified feature map that contains both static detail and motion context.
*   This combined representation yields a ~7% improvement on ApolloBench compared to single-encoder setups, specifically boosting performance on tasks requiring fine-grained temporal reasoning.

#### Token Resampling and Integration
Because encoding every frame at full resolution produces too many tokens for the LLM's context window, the system employs a **Perceiver Resampler** to compress visual information into a fixed, manageable budget.
*   Unlike simple average pooling or MLP projection, the Perceiver Resampler uses a set of learnable latent queries to attend to the visual features, effectively extracting the most relevant information into a fixed number of output tokens.
*   The study determines that resampling each video clip into **32 tokens** provides the optimal balance between information density and computational efficiency.
*   This approach allows the model to process hour-long videos by treating them as a sequence of independent clips, each represented by a compact 32-token vector.
*   For **token integration**, the authors reject naive concatenation in favor of inserting explicit structural tokens between video segments to help the LLM distinguish temporal boundaries.
*   The specific format used is a textual timestamp prompt: `clip from {MM:SS}-{MM:SS}: <vid_token>`, which informs the LLM of the exact time range covered by the subsequent visual tokens.
*   This formatting strategy alone contributes a 2–3% performance gain across benchmarks by providing the language model with explicit temporal grounding.

#### Training Schedule and Data Composition
The training of Apollo is executed in a rigorous **three-stage schedule** designed to progressively align visual and linguistic representations while preventing catastrophic forgetting of language capabilities.
*   **Stage 1 (Alignment):** The vision encoders are frozen, and only the connector (Perceiver Resampler) is trained on a 198,000-sample dataset consisting of a 50/50 mixture of image and video captions to align visual features with the LLM's embedding space.
*   **Stage 2 (Vision Pretraining):** The vision encoders are unfrozen and fine-tuned exclusively on **video-only data** (396,000 samples), while the LLM remains frozen; this step is crucial for adapting the pre-trained encoders to the specific demands of video-LMM tasks, particularly improving egocentric reasoning.
*   **Stage 3 (Supervised Fine-Tuning):** All components (LLM, encoders, connector) are trained together on a massive 3.2 million sample mixture.
*   The data composition in Stage 3 is critical: the authors found that including **10–14% pure text data** is mandatory to maintain the LLM's general reasoning abilities, while the remaining visual data should be slightly **video-heavy** to prioritize temporal understanding.
*   Deviating from this mixture—either by removing text data or making the dataset too image-heavy—results in significant performance degradation, confirming that a balanced multimodal diet is essential for robust video understanding.

#### Apollo Model Specifications
Leveraging these findings, the authors instantiate the **Apollo** family using the `Qwen2.5` series of LLMs as backbones, specifically creating 1.5B, 3B, and 7B parameter variants.
*   The visual backbone strictly adheres to the `SigLIP-SO400M` + `InternVideo2` dual-encoder configuration.
*   During inference, the models can perceive hour-long videos efficiently by processing them as a sequence of 2 fps clips, each compressed to 32 tokens.
*   The training utilizes 128 NVIDIA A100 GPUs with ZeRO-2 optimization, a batch size of 256, and a cosine learning rate schedule with a warm-up ratio of 0.03.
*   The learning rates are scaled by model size, with the connector module receiving a higher learning rate ($1 \times 10^{-4}$) compared to the LLM ($5 \times 10^{-5}$ for 1.5B, down to $1 \times 10^{-5}$ for 7B) to ensure rapid adaptation of the visual interface.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper moves beyond incremental architectural tweaks to provide fundamental shifts in how the research community approaches Video Large Multimodal Model (video-LMM) development. The following insights distinguish between mere performance optimizations and paradigm-changing methodologies that redefine the cost-benefit landscape of multimodal research.

### 4.1 Scaling Consistency: A Methodological Paradigm Shift
The most profound contribution of this work is not a specific model architecture, but the discovery of **Scaling Consistency**, a phenomenon that fundamentally alters the economics of LMM research.

*   **Distinction from Prior Work:** Traditional scaling laws (e.g., Hoffmann et al., 2022) describe how performance scales with compute *when training from scratch*, requiring researchers to train multiple models of varying sizes to predict the optimal configuration for a large model. This is computationally prohibitive for LMMs, which rely on distinct, pre-trained components (vision encoders, LLMs, connectors) that cannot be easily scaled in isolation. Prior approaches often guessed design choices for large models or relied on expensive, full-scale ablation studies.
*   **The Innovation:** The authors demonstrate that design decisions optimized on **moderately sized models (2–4B parameters)** and **smaller datasets (~500K samples)** transfer with near-perfect fidelity ($R^2 > 0.9$) to much larger architectures (7B+). As shown in **Figure 3**, while 0.5B models fail to predict larger model behavior ($R^2 &lt; 0.8$), the 4B model acts as a reliable proxy for the 7B model ($R^2 = 0.938$).
*   **Significance:** This transforms video-LMM research from a "high-stakes gamble" requiring hundreds of GPU hours per experiment into an accessible iterative process. It allows researchers to validate complex hypotheses—such as sampling strategies or data mixtures—on commodity hardware before committing resources to final training. This is a **fundamental innovation in research methodology** rather than a mere model improvement, effectively democratizing access to state-of-the-art video understanding research.

### 4.2 Temporal Fidelity via FPS Sampling vs. Uniform Sampling
The paper identifies and rectifies a critical, widespread flaw in how video data is presented to LMMs: the distortion of temporal dynamics through uniform sampling.

*   **Distinction from Prior Work:** Most existing video-LMMs employ **uniform frame sampling**, selecting $N$ frames evenly distributed across a video's duration. As detailed in **Section 4.1**, this approach inadvertently alters the "playback speed" perceived by the model: a 10-second video sampled at 16 frames presents 1.6 fps, while a 100-second video sampled at 16 frames presents 0.16 fps. This inconsistency forces the model to learn a mapping where the same visual motion corresponds to different temporal intervals depending on the video length, hampering its ability to reason about speed and causality.
*   **The Innovation:** The authors prove that **frames-per-second (fps) sampling** is strictly superior. By maintaining a constant temporal rate (e.g., 2 fps) regardless of video duration, the model receives a consistent representation of time. **Figure 4** illustrates that models trained with uniform sampling cannot recover performance even when tested with fps sampling, confirming the damage occurs during the learning phase.
*   **Significance:** This is a **corrective innovation** that addresses a foundational data processing error in the field. The shift to fps sampling, combined with the finding that **8–32 tokens per frame** is the optimal density, enables models to genuinely understand temporal dynamics (e.g., "how fast is the car moving?") rather than just recognizing static sequences. This directly contributes to Apollo's ability to handle hour-long videos without losing temporal coherence.

### 4.3 The Necessity of Dual-Encoder Fusion for Spatio-Temporal Reasoning
The study resolves the debate between using specialized video encoders versus high-quality image encoders by demonstrating that neither is sufficient alone; their fusion is required for robust performance.

*   **Distinction from Prior Work:** Previous works typically chose one path: either leveraging powerful image encoders (like `SigLIP` or `CLIP`) for their rich spatial features, assuming the LLM could infer motion, or using dedicated video encoders (like `VideoMAE` or `InternVideo`) to capture temporal dynamics, often at the cost of spatial resolution. **Figure 5** shows that while `SigLIP-SO400M` is the best *single* encoder, it underperforms on tasks requiring fine-grained temporal perception. Conversely, video encoders alone lack the spatial detail needed for tasks like OCR or object identification.
*   **The Innovation:** Apollo implements a **dual-encoder architecture** that concatenates features from `SigLIP-SO400M` (spatial) and `InternVideo2` (temporal) after interpolating them to match dimensions. Crucially, the paper finds that **language-supervised encoders** significantly outperform self-supervised ones, and that combining them yields a **~7% improvement** on ApolloBench compared to single-encoder baselines.
*   **Significance:** This validates the hypothesis that current LLMs struggle to implicitly reconstruct missing modalities (motion from static images or high-res details from compressed video tokens). By explicitly providing both signals, the model achieves a level of **spatio-temporal alignment** that single-encoder approaches cannot reach, enabling it to excel in diverse tasks ranging from reading text in a moving video to understanding complex action sequences.

### 4.4 ApolloBench: Filtering Signal from Noise in Evaluation
The introduction of **ApolloBench** represents a critical innovation in evaluation rigor, addressing the "false progress" caused by benchmarks that do not actually measure video understanding.

*   **Distinction from Prior Work:** The field relies on heavy, redundant benchmarks (e.g., Video-MME, MLVU) where evaluation can take **184 A100 GPU hours** for a single 3B model. More critically, **Figure 2** reveals that many existing benchmarks can be solved with high accuracy using only text inputs or a single static frame, meaning they measure language priors or image recognition rather than video perception. This leads to inflated scores that do not reflect true temporal reasoning capabilities.
*   **The Innovation:** ApolloBench is curated by systematically filtering out questions that >50% of models can answer with text or image-only inputs. It retains only the top 400 questions that exhibit high entropy (discriminative power) and require genuine temporal perception, categorized into five dimensions: Temporal OCR, Egocentric, Spatial, Perception, and Reasoning.
*   **Significance:** This results in an evaluation suite that is **41× faster** to run while being **more correlated** with the full suite of existing benchmarks (**Figure 2, Right**). By removing "easy" questions that confound image/text capabilities with video understanding, ApolloBench ensures that future performance gains reflect actual improvements in temporal reasoning. This shifts the community's focus from maximizing scores on flawed benchmarks to solving genuinely hard video problems.

### 4.5 Data Composition as a Regularizer Against Catastrophic Forgetting
The paper provides precise, counter-intuitive guidelines on data mixing that challenge the assumption that "more video data is always better" for video models.

*   **Distinction from Prior Work:** A common intuition in domain adaptation is to maximize the proportion of target-domain data (video) during fine-tuning. However, the authors show in **Section 5.3** and **Figure 6** that removing text data or skewing the mixture too heavily toward images leads to catastrophic forgetting of the LLM's general reasoning abilities.
*   **The Innovation:** The optimal recipe requires a "Goldilocks" zone: including **10–14% pure text data** is mandatory to preserve language capabilities, while the remaining visual data should be **slightly video-heavy**. Furthermore, **Section 5.2** reveals that fine-tuning the vision encoders *exclusively* on video data (while freezing the LLM) significantly boosts egocentric reasoning, a nuance often missed in mixed-data training stages.
*   **Significance:** These findings provide a **prescriptive framework** for training stable video-LMMs. They demonstrate that video understanding is not just about exposure to video tokens, but about maintaining the delicate balance between the LLM's abstract reasoning (sustained by text) and its perceptual grounding (enhanced by video). This insight prevents the degradation of general intelligence often seen in overly specialized multimodal models.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence supporting the paper's claims, moving from the validation of the "Scaling Consistency" methodology to the final performance of the Apollo models. The authors employ a rigorous ablation strategy, isolating variables across sampling, architecture, and data composition before synthesizing these findings into the final model family.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Benchmark Critique

Before presenting results, the authors fundamentally critique the evaluation landscape, arguing that existing benchmarks are inefficient and often measure the wrong capabilities.

**The Problem with Existing Benchmarks:**
The study evaluates ten open-source LMMs across major benchmarks: `Video-MME`, `MLVU`, `LongVideoBench`, `TempCompass`, `NExT-QA`, and `PerceptionTest`. The methodology involves testing each model under three distinct input modalities:
1.  **Video:** Full video input (standard sampling).
2.  **Image:** Only the center frame of the video.
3.  **Text:** The question only, with no visual input.

**Key Findings on Benchmark Quality (Figure 2, Left):**
*   **Text-Only Solvability:** A significant portion of questions in benchmarks like `NExT-QA` and `PerceptionTest` can be answered correctly using *only* text inputs. For instance, in `NExT-QA`, the performance gap between Video and Text inputs is negligible, indicating the benchmark measures language priors rather than video perception.
*   **Single-Frame Solvability:** Many "video" benchmarks are solvable with a single static image. The yellow shaded area in **Figure 2 (Left)** quantifies the benefit of temporal information; for `NExT-QA` and `PerceptionTest`, this area is minimal, confirming they are effectively image QA tasks.
*   **Duration Bias:** As video duration increases (e.g., `Video-MME` Short vs. Long), the reliance on actual video perception *decreases*. Models rely more on text comprehension for longer videos, suggesting current long-video benchmarks fail to enforce temporal reasoning.

**Redundancy Analysis (Figure 2, Right):**
The authors compute the correlation matrix of model performances across benchmarks.
*   **High Redundancy:** There is a block-diagonal structure in the correlation matrix, indicating that many benchmarks measure identical capabilities. For example, performance on short vs. long videos within `Video-MME` shows an $R^2 = 0.94$. Similarly, different question formats (Multiple Choice vs. Yes/No) in `TempCompass` correlate at $R^2 > 0.8$.
*   **Implication:** Running all these benchmarks is computationally wasteful. Evaluating a single 3B model takes **184 A100 GPU hours** across the full suite, yet much of this data is redundant.

**ApolloBench Construction:**
To address these inefficiencies, the authors curate **ApolloBench**:
*   **Filtering Criteria:** Questions where >50% of models could answer correctly with Text-only or Image-only inputs were discarded.
*   **Selection:** The top 400 questions with the highest entropy (most discriminative between models) were selected across five categories: Temporal OCR, Egocentric, Spatial, Perception, and Reasoning.
*   **Efficiency Gain:** Evaluating on ApolloBench is **41× faster** than the full suite while maintaining high correlation with existing benchmarks (**Figure 2, Right**).

### 5.2 Validation of Scaling Consistency

The core methodological claim is that design decisions made on smaller models transfer to larger ones. This was tested by training **84 models** (21 design variations $\times$ 4 LLM sizes: 0.5B, 1.5B, 4B, 7B).

**Quantitative Results (Figure 3, Table 14 & 15):**
*   **Critical Size Threshold:** Design decisions on very small models (0.5B) do *not* reliably predict larger model performance ($R^2 &lt; 0.8$). However, once the model size reaches **2–4 billion parameters**, the correlation becomes extremely strong.
*   **Specific Correlation:** The correlation between the **4B** model variants and the **7B** model variants is **$R^2 = 0.938$** (**Figure 3, Left**). This implies that if a specific sampling strategy or encoder combination improves the 4B model, it will almost certainly improve the 7B model.
*   **Dataset Size Plateau:** The study also varied dataset sizes from 75K to 1M samples. The correlation of smaller models (4B) to the large model (7B) plateaus at approximately **500K samples** (**Figure 3, Right**). Training on larger datasets yields diminishing returns for *identifying* good design choices, though it is still necessary for final model performance.

**Conclusion:** This experiment convincingly supports the claim that researchers can use ~3B models and ~500K data subsets to rapidly prototype designs, saving orders of magnitude in compute before scaling up.

### 5.3 Architectural and Training Ablations

Using the validated "small-model proxy" approach (using Qwen2.5-3B and 750K samples), the authors conducted exhaustive ablations on video-specific design choices.

#### 5.3.1 Video Sampling Strategies (Figure 4, Table 10 & 11)
The study compares **Uniform Sampling** (fixed $N$ frames) vs. **FPS Sampling** (constant frame rate).
*   **Performance Gap:** Models trained with uniform sampling consistently underperform those trained with FPS sampling.
    *   Training with 64 uniform frames yields an overall ApolloBench score of **55.1**.
    *   Training with FPS sampling (2 fps) yields significantly higher scores, with the optimal configuration reaching **58.6** (**Table 10**, Row 17).
*   **Mechanism Verification:** To prove the deficit is due to training dynamics (inconsistent playback speed) rather than test-time inference, models trained on uniform sampling were *tested* with FPS sampling. Performance did not recover (**Figure 4, Middle**), confirming the damage occurs during learning.
*   **Token Density Trade-off:** There is a trade-off between Tokens Per Second (tps) and Frames Per Second (fps). The sweet spot is **8–32 tokens per frame**. Too few tokens per frame (&lt;4) causes a collapse in OCR and Spatial tasks (**Table 10**, Rows 21–28), while too many wastes context window capacity.

#### 5.3.2 Vision Encoder Selection (Figure 5, Table 9)
The authors tested 7 encoders individually and in pairs.
*   **Single Encoder Winner:** **`SigLIP-SO400M`** is the best single encoder (Overall Score: **52.7**), outperforming video-specific encoders like `InternVideo2` (52.0) and `LanguageBind-Video` (48.7). This confirms that strong spatial features are currently more critical than temporal ones for general performance.
*   **Temporal Deficit:** However, `SigLIP` underperforms on strictly temporal tasks. Video encoders like `InternVideo2` excel in "Perception" and "Reasoning" categories but lag in OCR.
*   **Dual-Encoder Synergy:** Combining **`SigLIP-SO400M` + `InternVideo2`** yields the best overall performance (**57.9**), a **~7% improvement** over the best single encoder (**Table 9**, Row 19). This fusion provides both high-resolution spatial detail and robust temporal motion cues.

#### 5.3.3 Token Resampling and Integration (Table 1 & 2)
*   **Resampler:** The **Perceiver Resampler** significantly outperforms average pooling or simple MLP projection.
    *   Perceiver Resampler Overall Score: **55.5**.
    *   2-layer MLP + Average Pooling: **53.2**.
    *   2D Conv + Average Pooling: **44.7**.
    *   *Reasoning:* The Perceiver effectively compresses information without the "informational waste" of naive up-projection.
*   **Integration Format:** Adding explicit structural tokens between video clips improves performance by **2–3%**.
    *   Naive concatenation (`<vid_token>`): **55.5**.
    *   Textual Timestamps (`clip from {MM:SS}-{MM:SS}: <vid_token>`): **56.8** (**Table 2**).
    *   This explicit temporal grounding helps the LLM distinguish between different segments of the video.

#### 5.3.4 Training Schedule and Data Composition (Table 3, Figure 6, Table 12 & 13)
*   **Training Stages:** A **3-stage training schedule** is superior to 1-stage or 2-stage approaches.
    *   **Stage 1:** Align connector (frozen encoders/LLM) on image/video captions.
    *   **Stage 2:** Fine-tune *only* vision encoders on **video-only data**. This step is crucial, boosting Egocentric reasoning significantly (from ~48 to ~64 in some metrics, **Table 3**).
    *   **Stage 3:** Full fine-tuning on mixed data.
    *   The best 3-stage configuration achieves an overall score of **59.2**, compared to **48.7** for a naive 1-stage approach (**Table 3**).
*   **Data Mixture:**
    *   **Text Data Requirement:** Including **10–14% pure text data** is mandatory. Removing text data (0%) causes a catastrophic drop in performance (Overall score drops from **59.0** to **41.8**, **Table 13**, Row 8).
    *   **Video Heaviness:** Among visual data, a slightly **video-heavy** mixture performs best. A mix of 15% Text, 25% Image, 20% Multi-Image, and 40% Video achieved the highest score (**59.0**, **Table 13**, Row 2).

### 5.4 Final Model Performance: The Apollo Family

Leveraging these insights, the authors trained the **Apollo** family (1.5B, 3B, 7B) using `Qwen2.5` backbones, `SigLIP`+`InternVideo2` encoders, and the optimized 3-stage protocol.

3.  **Temporal Reasoning:** On `ApolloBench` (which filters out non-video questions), Apollo models show massive gains, particularly in **Egocentric** and **Reasoning** categories, confirming the success of the dual-encoder and FPS sampling strategies.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment and Limitations

**Strengths of the Experimental Design:**
*   **Systematic Isolation:** The use of Scaling Consistency allowed for a depth of ablation (testing 21+ variations) that would be computationally impossible on 7B models directly.
*   **Benchmark Rigor:** By introducing ApolloBench and demonstrating the flaws in existing benchmarks (text/image solvability), the paper ensures its results reflect genuine video understanding.
*   **Reproducibility:** The paper provides extensive hyperparameters (learning rates, batch sizes, data mixes) in the Appendix (Tables 7, 14, 15), facilitating reproduction.

**Limitations and Conditional Results:**
*   **Active Sampling Excluded:** The study explicitly notes it did not explore **active frame selection** strategies (where the query guides sampling, e.g., Q-Former based pooling). The authors hypothesize these may not generalize well to multi-turn conversations, but this remains an unverified assumption (**Section 4.1**).
*   **Unified vs. Split Architecture:** While the paper adopts a **unified architecture** (using the video encoder for both images and videos by duplicating frames), Appendix Table 8 shows the performance difference between Unified (56.8) and Split (56.2) is marginal. The choice for unity was driven by simplicity and parameter efficiency, not a overwhelming performance gap.
*   **Conversational Evaluation:** The benchmarks used are primarily multiple-choice. The authors acknowledge in the "Future Work" section that this inadequately assesses open-ended conversational abilities, and a dedicated conversational benchmark is needed.
*   **Dependency on Pre-trained Encoders:** The success of the dual-encoder approach relies heavily on the quality of existing pre-trained models (`SigLIP`, `InternVideo2`). If these encoders have biases or limitations, the LMM inherits them. The paper does not explore training video encoders from scratch.

**Conclusion on Claims:**
The experiments convincingly support the paper's central thesis: **design matters more than scale** in the current regime of video-LMMs. The quantitative evidence—specifically the $R^2=0.938$ correlation for Scaling Consistency, the ~7% gain from dual encoders, and the 41× efficiency of ApolloBench—provides a robust foundation for the claimed advancements. The Apollo models' ability to outperform 30B parameter systems with only 7B parameters serves as the ultimate empirical validation of these design principles.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the Apollo framework establishes a robust set of design principles for video-LMMs, the approach relies on specific assumptions and leaves several critical challenges unaddressed. Understanding these limitations is essential for contextualizing the results and identifying future research directions.

### 6.1 Architectural Assumptions: The Unified vs. Split Trade-off
A core design choice in Apollo is the **unified architecture**, where a single video encoder processes both video clips and static images (by duplicating the image $N$ times to match the clip length).
*   **The Trade-off:** The authors explicitly acknowledge that this design prioritizes **simplicity and parameter efficiency** over potential performance gains. In **Appendix Table 8**, the comparison between the Unified and Split architectures shows a marginal difference in overall performance (56.8 vs. 56.2 on ApolloBench).
*   **The Limitation:** While the unified approach is "good enough," it assumes that forcing an image through a video encoder (which expects temporal dynamics) does not introduce significant representational noise. The paper notes in **Appendix Section A** that exploring separated architectures—where images are processed by a dedicated image encoder and videos by a dual-encoder stream—remains an open question. It is possible that a split architecture could yield larger gains for static image tasks or specific video domains, but the authors opted against it to reduce complexity. Furthermore, the study did not investigate training both encoders on mixed image/video data within a unified setup, leaving the optimal data strategy for such a configuration unknown.

### 6.2 Exclusion of Active and Query-Guided Sampling
The paper rigorously validates **frames-per-second (fps) sampling** as superior to uniform sampling but explicitly excludes **active frame selection** strategies from its scope.
*   **The Assumption:** The authors assume that a constant temporal rate (fps) is the most robust baseline for general video understanding. They deliberately omit methods that use the initial user query to guide frame sampling (e.g., using a Q-Former to pool tokens based on text relevance), as seen in works like *Video-LLaVA* or *Goldfish*.
*   **The Weakness:** The paper hypothesizes in **Section 4.1** and **Appendix Section A** that active, query-guided sampling "may not generalize well to multi-turn conversations." If the model selects frames based on the first question, those frames might be irrelevant for a follow-up question, requiring expensive re-sampling at every conversational turn. However, this remains an **unverified assumption**. The exclusion of these methods means Apollo may be suboptimal for tasks requiring extreme compression of hour-long videos where only a few seconds are relevant to the query. The model currently processes all clips uniformly, which, while consistent, is computationally heavier than skipping irrelevant segments entirely.

### 6.3 Evaluation Constraints: The Multiple-Choice Bias
Despite the introduction of the more rigorous **ApolloBench**, the evaluation landscape covered in this work is still heavily skewed towards **multiple-choice questions (MCQ)**.
*   **The Constraint:** As noted in **Section 2.3** and **Appendix Section B.1**, the authors filtered benchmarks to focus on MCQ to eliminate the need for external LLM judges (like GPT-4) and avoid the noise associated with subjective scoring of open-ended answers.
*   **The Gap:** The authors admit in **Appendix Section A** that "current benchmarks primarily use academic multiple-choice formats, which inadequately assess conversational abilities." While ApolloBench filters out questions solvable by text or single frames, it does not necessarily test the model's ability to generate coherent, long-form narratives or engage in complex, multi-turn dialogues about video content. The "Future Work" section explicitly calls for a dedicated conversational evaluation benchmark, acknowledging that high MCQ scores do not guarantee success in real-world agent applications where open-ended generation is required.

### 6.4 Dependency on Pre-trained Encoder Quality
The success of the **dual-encoder strategy** (`SigLIP` + `InternVideo2`) is contingent upon the availability and quality of existing pre-trained models.
*   **The Constraint:** The paper does not explore training video encoders from scratch. Instead, it relies on the hypothesis that combining a strong language-supervised image encoder with a capable video encoder is superior to training a monolithic model.
*   **The Risk:** This approach inherits any biases or limitations present in the pre-trained weights of `SigLIP-SO400M` and `InternVideo2`. If these encoders fail to capture specific types of motion or spatial details due to their pre-training data distributions, the Apollo model cannot recover this information. The study confirms in **Section 4.2** that self-supervised encoders (like `DINOv2` or `VideoMAE`) underperform compared to language-supervised ones, suggesting that the field is currently bottlenecked by the scarcity of high-quality, language-aligned video encoders. The ~7% gain from dual encoders might be an upper bound limited by the weaker of the two components.

### 6.5 Scalability and Computational Barriers
While **Scaling Consistency** significantly lowers the barrier for *design exploration*, the final training and inference of Apollo models still face substantial computational hurdles.
*   **Training Cost:** Although design choices were validated on 3B models with 750K samples, training the final **Apollo-7B** model required **128 NVIDIA A100 GPUs** (**Appendix Section C.4**). This remains prohibitively expensive for many research groups, meaning that while the *methodology* is democratized, the *execution* of state-of-the-art models is not.
*   **Inference Latency:** The dual-encoder architecture inherently doubles the visual computation cost compared to single-encoder models. Processing every frame through both `SigLIP` and `InternVideo2`, followed by interpolation and concatenation, introduces latency. For real-time applications (e.g., robotics or live video analysis), this overhead could be a critical bottleneck. The paper does not provide detailed latency benchmarks or optimization strategies (such as quantization or distillation) to mitigate this inference cost.
*   **Context Window Limits:** While Apollo can handle "hour-long" videos by spacing out clips, there is a hard limit on the number of clips that can be processed before the LLM's context window is exhausted. The paper mentions spacing out clips when the maximum is reached (**Section 4.1**), but this effectively reduces the temporal resolution for very long videos, potentially missing short-duration events in hour-long inputs. The trade-off between video duration and temporal granularity remains an unresolved constraint.

### 6.6 Data Licensing and Composition Restrictions
The composition of the training data was influenced by external licensing constraints, which may limit the model's potential performance.
*   **The Constraint:** In **Section 6** and **Appendix Section C.3**, the authors state they omitted "non-permissive sources (e.g., those reliant on ChatGPT)" due to licensing issues. This excluded some commonly used large-scale datasets.
*   **The Impact:** The authors explicitly note that "performance could be further improved without such restrictions and by training on larger datasets like those introduced in LLaVA-OneVision." This suggests that the reported state-of-the-art results are achieved *despite* a potentially suboptimal training corpus. The reliance on a specific mixture of publicly available data means that Apollo's performance ceiling might be lower than what could be achieved with a fully unrestricted, massive-scale dataset, raising questions about how much of the gain is due to architectural innovation versus the specific (and possibly limited) data curation.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The publication of **Apollo** and the accompanying systematic study represents a inflection point for Video Large Multimodal Model (video-LMM) research. By shifting the focus from brute-force scaling to principled design, this work fundamentally alters the economic and methodological landscape of the field. The implications extend beyond the specific performance metrics of the Apollo models, offering a roadmap for efficient, high-fidelity video understanding.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Research Landscape: From "Scale at All Costs" to "Design First"
The most immediate impact of this work is the validation of **Scaling Consistency** as a viable alternative to traditional scaling laws.
*   **Democratization of Research:** Previously, optimizing a video-LMM required access to massive compute clusters to run ablation studies on 7B+ parameter models, effectively gating progress to well-funded labs. The finding that **2–4B parameter models** act as reliable proxies ($R^2 > 0.9$) for larger architectures means that rigorous architectural search can now be conducted on single-node or small-cluster setups. This lowers the barrier to entry, allowing a broader range of researchers to contribute to state-of-the-art video understanding.
*   **Methodological Shift:** The field is encouraged to move away from the "train and hope" approach, where design choices (like uniform sampling or single encoders) are adopted due to convention rather than evidence. Apollo demonstrates that **data-driven design choices**—specifically **fps sampling**, **dual-encoder fusion**, and **structured token integration**—can yield models that outperform systems 5× their size. This suggests that future performance gains will likely come from refining these architectural priors rather than simply increasing parameter counts.
*   **Evaluation Rigor:** The introduction of **ApolloBench** challenges the community to abandon redundant, noisy benchmarks. By proving that many existing benchmarks measure language priors or static image recognition rather than video perception, this work forces a reckoning with "false progress." Future papers will be expected to demonstrate gains on metrics that strictly require temporal reasoning, raising the bar for what constitutes a valid improvement in the field.

### 7.2 Enabling Follow-Up Research Directions
The findings in this paper open several specific, high-value avenues for immediate follow-up research:

*   **Optimizing Active Sampling Strategies:** While Apollo validates constant **fps sampling** as a robust baseline, it explicitly excludes **query-guided active sampling** (e.g., using a Q-Former to select frames based on the user's question) due to concerns about multi-turn consistency (**Section 4.1**). A critical next step is to develop **dynamic sampling mechanisms** that can adapt frame selection per conversational turn without losing temporal coherence. Research could explore lightweight "scouting" agents that identify relevant clips before the main LMM processes them, potentially enabling the handling of day-long videos rather than just hour-long ones.
*   **Specialized Encoder Training:** The success of the **dual-encoder approach** (`SigLIP` + `InternVideo2`) highlights a bottleneck: the reliance on off-the-shelf encoders that were not jointly optimized for this specific fusion. Future work could focus on **co-training video and image encoders** specifically for LMM integration, perhaps using the **Scaling Consistency** framework to rapidly iterate on encoder architectures at the 2–4B scale before full deployment. There is also room to explore **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)** encoders that dynamically route static frames to image specialists and motion-heavy segments to video specialists.
*   **Conversational Video Benchmarks:** The authors explicitly note in **Appendix Section A** that current benchmarks, including ApolloBench, rely heavily on multiple-choice questions, which fail to assess open-ended generative capabilities. This creates a clear mandate for the community to build **conversation-centric video benchmarks** that evaluate long-form narrative generation, causal explanation, and multi-turn dialogue grounding. Such a benchmark would need to avoid the pitfalls of LLM-as-a-judge noise while capturing the nuance of human-video interaction.
*   **Unified vs. Split Architecture Deep Dive:** While Apollo adopts a **unified architecture** for simplicity, the marginal performance gap observed in **Appendix Table 8** suggests that a **split architecture** (dedicated paths for images and videos) might offer untapped potential, particularly for tasks requiring high-frequency static image analysis alongside temporal reasoning. Future research could investigate hybrid models that switch encoding strategies based on input modality characteristics.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases
The efficiency and temporal fidelity of the Apollo family make it uniquely suited for real-world applications where previous models struggled due to latency or "temporal blindness."

*   **Long-Form Content Analysis:** Apollo's ability to process **hour-long videos** efficiently enables applications in **educational technology** (summarizing lecture recordings, answering student questions about specific moments), **legal discovery** (analyzing bodycam or surveillance footage for specific events), and **media indexing** (generating detailed, timestamped chapters for podcasts or streams). The **textual timestamp integration** (`clip from {MM:SS}-{MM:SS}`) ensures that answers are not just correct but precisely grounded in time.
*   **Egocentric and Robotics Vision:** The significant gains in **egocentric reasoning** (driven by the dual-encoder and video-only fine-tuning stage) position Apollo as a strong candidate for **robotics and embodied AI**. Robots operating in dynamic environments need to understand not just *what* objects are present, but *how* they are moving and interacting over time. The model's robustness to varying video speeds (via fps sampling) makes it more reliable for processing robot-collected data, which often varies in frame rate and duration.
*   **Edge and Resource-Constrained Deployment:** The **Apollo-3B** model's ability to outperform existing 7B models suggests that high-quality video understanding can be deployed on **edge devices** or in latency-sensitive cloud environments. This opens doors for **real-time video assistance** on mobile devices, where bandwidth and compute are limited, but the need for temporal understanding (e.g., "did the package arrive yet?") is high.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance
For practitioners looking to adopt or build upon this work, the paper provides clear guidelines on when and how to apply these techniques.

*   **When to Use Apollo vs. Alternatives:**
    *   **Prefer Apollo** when the task involves **long-context video understanding** (>1 minute), requires **fine-grained temporal reasoning** (speed, causality, sequence of events), or operates under **compute constraints** where a 3B–7B model is preferred over 30B+ alternatives.
    *   **Consider Alternatives** if the application is strictly **single-image analysis** (where a dedicated image-LMM might be slightly more efficient) or if the use case involves **extreme compression** of day-long videos where active, query-based frame skipping is mandatory (a capability Apollo currently lacks).

*   **Integration Best Practices:**
    *   **Adopt FPS Sampling:** If building a custom video pipeline, strictly avoid uniform frame sampling. Implement a sampler that maintains a constant **frames-per-second (fps)** rate (e.g., 2 fps) regardless of video duration to preserve temporal dynamics.
    *   **Dual-Encoder Setup:** For highest accuracy, replicate the **SigLIP-SO400M** (spatial) + **InternVideo2** (temporal) fusion. If compute is extremely limited, **SigLIP-SO400M** alone is the best single-encoder fallback, but expect a ~7% drop in temporal reasoning tasks.
    *   **Data Mixture Discipline:** When fine-tuning, do not purge text data. Maintain a **10–14% pure text data** mixture to prevent catastrophic forgetting of language capabilities. Ensure the visual data is slightly **video-heavy** to prioritize temporal learning.
    *   **Token Formatting:** When injecting video tokens into the LLM context, use explicit **textual timestamps** (e.g., `clip from 00:15-00:30: <tokens>`) rather than naive concatenation. This simple formatting change provides a free 2–3% performance boost by grounding the LLM in time.

*   **Reproducibility Resources:** The authors have released extensive details in the Appendix, including the **ApolloBench** curation process (**Appendix B**), exact hyperparameters for all three training stages (**Appendix Table 7**), and raw ablation results (**Appendix Tables 9–15**). Researchers can leverage the **Scaling Consistency** finding to reproduce the *design search* phase on modest hardware (e.g., training a 3B model on 500K samples) before committing to full-scale training.

In summary, **Apollo** does not merely present a new model; it provides a **verified blueprint** for building efficient, temporally aware multimodal systems. By adhering to the principles of Scaling Consistency, fps sampling, and dual-encoder fusion, the community can accelerate the development of video-LMMs that are not only smarter but also significantly more accessible and practical for real-world deployment.
