## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces DreamGym, a unified framework that addresses the prohibitive cost and instability of reinforcement learning (RL) for autonomous agents by replacing expensive real-environment rollouts with scalable, reasoning-based synthetic experiences. By distilling environment dynamics into an experience model that generates consistent state transitions and adaptive curriculum tasks, DreamGym enables effective online RL training without relying on heterogeneous external systems like WebArena or WebShop for every interaction. Experiments demonstrate that on non-RL-ready tasks like WebArena, DreamGym outperforms all baselines by over 30%, while its sim-to-real transfer strategy (DreamGym-S2R) achieves over 40% performance improvement compared to training from scratch using less than 10% of real-world data (Abstract, Table 1).

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: RL for LLM Agents is Fundamentally Bottlenecked by Environment Access

This paper addresses a critical bottleneck in training autonomous language agents: **reinforcement learning requires massive amounts of interactive experience, but real-world environments are prohibitively expensive, unstable, and difficult to scale**. While RL has proven effective for games and robotics, extending it to LLM-based agents operating in complex domains like web navigation, embodied control, and multi-tool orchestration introduces unique challenges that existing infrastructure cannot handle.

The paper identifies four interconnected barriers (Introduction, Section 1):

1. **Costly Rollouts**: Real environments involve long interaction sequences with high computational cost per step. For example, web environments require spinning up browser instances, maintaining server state, and handling network latency. The authors note that even with extensive engineering effort, they could operate only four parallel interaction sessions for WebArena, creating a severe throughput bottleneck (Appendix A.3).

2. **Limited Task Diversity**: Most existing environments provide only a limited, static set of instructions. RL training requires broad task coverage for effective exploration, but scaling task instructions inherently demands costly human expertise to validate feasibility (Introduction, Section 1).

3. **Unreliable Reward Signals**: Interactive settings like web pages and GUIs are highly dynamic and lack consistent behaviors, resulting in noisy, sparse, or even false feedback. Many environments use outcome-based rewards (success/failure only), which provide insufficient learning signals for long-horizon tasks (Section 4.1.1).

4. **Infrastructure Complexity**: Existing systems are heterogeneous and rely on heavyweight backends like Docker or virtual machines, making large-batch rollout sampling engineering-intensive. Additionally, safety concerns arise when actions are irreversible (e.g., deleting items on real websites), and most environments lack reliable reset mechanisms (Introduction, Section 1).

### Why This Matters: The Stakes for Agent Development

This problem is not merely an engineering inconvenience—it fundamentally limits what kinds of agents we can build. The paper positions this within what Silver and Sutton (2025) call the "era of experience," where agents improve by bootstrapping from their own interactions rather than relying solely on pre-trained knowledge.

**Real-world impact**: Consider web navigation agents that must complete tasks like "find the cheapest laptop under $800 with 16GB RAM" or embodied agents that must "put the cooled lettuce in the fridge." These require multi-step reasoning with feedback at each step. Without scalable RL training:
- Agents remain stuck at the capability level of their base models
- They cannot adapt to novel situations not covered in training data
- They fail to learn from mistakes through trial-and-error

**Theoretical significance**: The paper challenges a common assumption in world model research—that synthetic environments must faithfully reproduce reality. Instead, DreamGym operates on the insight that **agent training does not require perfectly realistic environments, but rather interaction data that is sufficiently diverse, informative, and causally grounded** (Introduction, Section 1). This reframing has implications for how we think about simulation fidelity versus learning utility.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

To understand DreamGym's contribution, we must examine what came before and where those methods fall short.

#### Traditional RL in Real Environments

Classical RL algorithms like PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) and newer methods like GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) have achieved strong results in controlled settings like games and robotics. However, extending these to LLM agents introduces substantial challenges (Section 2.1):

- **Sparse and delayed rewards**: Tasks like web navigation require long-horizon interaction but provide feedback only at episode completion, making policy improvement challenging and often resulting in training collapse.
- **Heterogeneous environment dynamics**: Open environments like web pages produce noisy reward signals and lack reliable reset mechanisms.
- **Sample inefficiency**: Modern RL algorithms require hundreds of thousands of interactions, which is computationally prohibitive when each step involves API calls, browser rendering, or physical simulation.

#### Imitation Learning with Synthetic Trajectories

Early approaches addressed data scarcity by generating synthetic demonstrations through scripting oracle trajectories or using stronger teacher models (Section 2.2):

- **Static trajectories lack adaptivity**: Methods like WebShop (Yao et al., 2022) and Mind2Web (Deng et al., 2023) rely on human-labeled demonstrations. While effective for distillation, these require substantial manual effort and cannot adapt to the agent's evolving policy.
- **Limited exploration**: Subsequent work leveraged indirect sources like online tutorials (Xu et al.; Ou et al., 2024), but these still produce fixed datasets that do not co-evolve with the agent.

#### Task Synthesis Methods

More recent work focuses on generating diverse tasks to expand exploration space:

- **AgentSynth** (Xie et al., 2025) and **SCA** (Zhou et al., 2025) synthesize diverse tasks but still depend on data collection in real environments, inheriting the same scalability limitations (Section 2.2).
- These methods cannot generate the actual state transitions—only task instructions—so they still require expensive environment interaction for each rollout.

#### Synthetic Environment Simulators

A parallel line of research builds synthetic environments that can generate unlimited experiences:

- **AlphaGo** (Silver et al., 2016) and **Dreamer** (Hafner et al., 2020) demonstrated the power of self-play and latent imagination in controlled domains.
- **WebDreamer** (Gu et al., 2024) and **WebEvolver** (Fang et al., 2025) constructed world models to produce environment feedback for agent planning.
- **UI-Simulator** (Wang et al., 2025b) is closest to DreamGym's setting, leveraging LLMs as step-wise simulators. However, it requires substantial expert engineering to adapt to different environments and is limited to generating trajectory variations for supervised fine-tuning—not full RL training (Section 2.2).

**The critical gap**: None of these approaches provide a complete toolkit for general-purpose RL. They either (a) still require real environment interaction, (b) are limited to imitation learning rather than RL, or (c) require extensive per-environment engineering that does not scale.

### DreamGym's Positioning: A Unified Framework for Scalable Agent RL

DreamGym positions itself as **the first unified framework designed to synthesize diverse experiences with scalability in mind to enable effective online RL training** (Abstract). Its key differentiators relative to prior work are:

| Aspect | Prior Approaches | DreamGym |
|--------|-----------------|----------|
| **Environment fidelity** | Attempt to reproduce external systems faithfully | Abstract dynamics into textual space; fidelity matters less than causal consistency |
| **Task generation** | Static or human-curated task sets | Adaptive curriculum based on reward entropy (Section 4.2) |
| **Experience quality** | Fixed offline datasets | Replay buffer co-evolves with agent policy (Section 4.1.1) |
| **RL support** | Often limited to supervised fine-tuning | Full RL training with consistent reward signals |
| **Infrastructure** | Heterogeneous, environment-specific | Unified LLM-serving infrastructure |

The paper's core insight is that **synthesizing transitions in an abstract state space can reduce irrelevant dimensions and produce trajectories that are more informative and token-efficient than those derived from raw observations** (Section 4.1). For example, in web shopping, instead of processing raw HTML code, the experience model directly synthesizes clean element listings while discarding irrelevant structural artifacts.

This positioning is validated empirically: on WebArena (a non-RL-ready environment), DreamGym achieves success rates exceeding 30% across all backbones where zero-shot RL baselines fail entirely (Table 1, Section 5.2). In RL-ready but costly settings like WebShop, it matches GRPO and PPO performance using only synthetic interactions (Table 1).

The theoretical foundation for this approach is provided in Appendix B.1, which proves that under trust-region assumptions, policy improvement in the synthetic environment transfers to the real environment as long as reward accuracy ($\epsilon_R$) and domain consistency ($\epsilon_P$) errors are bounded—without requiring strict state reconstruction fidelity.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

DreamGym is a training framework that replaces expensive real-environment interactions with a learned "experience model" — an LLM that simulates what would happen if an agent took actions in a real environment, complete with state transitions and reward signals. The system solves the scalability problem of agent RL by generating unlimited synthetic training experiences through reasoning, while maintaining enough fidelity to the real world that policies trained synthetically can transfer effectively to actual environments.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The DreamGym framework consists of four interconnected components that operate in a training loop:

1. **Reasoning Experience Model (`M_exp`)**: An LLM that takes the agent's action, task instruction, interaction history, and retrieved past experiences as input, then outputs the next abstract state and reward signal through explicit chain-of-thought reasoning (Figure 2, Section 4.1).

2. **Experience Replay Buffer**: A memory store seeded with offline real-world trajectories that retrieves semantically similar past experiences to ground the experience model's predictions, continuously updated with new synthetic transitions as training progresses (Section 4.1.1).

3. **Curriculum Task Generator (`M_task`)**: A component sharing parameters with `M_exp` that identifies high-value seed tasks based on reward entropy and generates progressively challenging task variations to keep the agent in the optimal learning zone (Section 4.2).

4. **Agent Policy (`π_θ`)**: The LLM agent being trained, which interacts with the experience model to collect rollouts and is updated using standard RL algorithms like PPO or GRPO based on the synthetic experiences (Section 3.2, 4.3).

Information flows as follows: seed tasks enter the system → the agent policy selects actions → the experience model predicts next states and rewards using the replay buffer for grounding → rollouts are stored back in the replay buffer → the task generator identifies high-entropy tasks and creates variations → the cycle repeats until convergence.

- **Finally**, I cover the sim-to-real transfer protocol (DreamGym-S2R) that combines synthetic pretraining with limited real-environment fine-tuning (Section 4.3).

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a systems paper that proposes a complete training framework for LLM agents, with the core idea being that environment dynamics can be distilled into a reasoning-capable language model that generates consistent, informative experiences without requiring actual environment interaction.

#### The Reasoning Experience Model: Core Inference Mechanism

The experience model `M_exp` operates in an abstract textual state space `S` rather than raw observation space (e.g., HTML code or pixel data), which is a critical design choice that reduces irrelevant dimensions and makes training highly sample-efficient (Section 4.1). For instance, in a web shopping task, instead of processing raw HTML with all its structural tags and headers, the experience model directly synthesizes clean element listings like "[227] link:'My Account' link to user…" as shown in Figure 2. This abstraction means the experience model can be trained on relatively small public trajectory datasets while still producing trajectories that are more informative and token-efficient than raw observations.

At inference time, the experience model does not simply predict the next state from the current state-action pair — it conditions on three additional contexts that the authors found essential for improving state quality through ablation studies (Section 4.1.1, Figure 4):

1. **Interaction history `{(s_i, a_i)}_{i=0}^t`**: The full trajectory up to the current step is incorporated into the context window, which helps maintain state consistency across multiple turns and prevents the model from drifting off-topic or breaking causal coherence in multi-step interactions (Section 6.3).

2. **Task instruction `τ`**: The original task goal conditions the experience model, enabling it to interpret actions with respect to task objectives and thereby predict both state transitions and rewards more accurately (Section 4.1.1).

3. **Retrieved past experiences `{d_j}_{j=1}^k`**: The top-k demonstrations are retrieved from the replay buffer based on semantic similarity with the current state-action pair, formally defined as `{d_j}_{j=1}^k = Topk(cos(φ(s_t, a_t), φ(s_i, a_i)))` where `φ(·)` denotes an arbitrary semantic encoder (Section 4.1.1). Leveraging knowledge this way reduces hallucinations and improves factuality for knowledge-intensive state predictions, as shown in the ablation study where removing history significantly reduces consistency (Figure 4).

Given these inputs, the experience model predicts the next state `s_{t+1}` and reward `r_{t+1}` via explicit chain-of-thought reasoning, which is formalized as:

$$
(s_{t+1}, r_{t+1}) = M_{\text{exp}}\left(R_t \mid \{(s_i, a_i)\}_{i=0}^t, \{d_j\}_{j=1}^k, \tau\right)
$$

where `R_t` is an explicit reasoning trace produced by the experience model that guides the state transition (Equation 4, Section 4.1.1). The reasoning trace is not just for interpretability — it is a functional component that helps the model predict the most consistent and informative transition that reflects the consequence of the agent's action. For example, if the action is invalid, the model transitions to a failure state and assigns a zero reward to signal the error through its reasoning process.

The reward scheme follows an outcome-based approach where `r = 1` is assigned only at the final step when the task is successfully completed and `r = 0` in all other cases, following Feng et al. (2025) as noted in Section 4.1.1. This sparse reward structure is challenging for RL but is made tractable by the curriculum-based task generation that ensures the agent encounters tasks at the right difficulty level.

#### Training the Experience Model: Distilling Reasoning from Offline Data

The experience model is trained via supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on offline trajectory datasets from public benchmarks, which is remarkably sample-efficient due to the abstract state-space design (Section 4.1.2). The specific dataset sizes vary by environment: WebShop uses 1,600 human demonstration trajectories plus 2,000 trajectories from oracle agents and random exploration; ALFWorld uses 3,200 expert demonstration trajectories plus 2,000 offline trajectories; WebArena uses 4,800 offline trajectories from high-performing agents on the public leaderboard (Appendix A.1–A.3).

Each transition in the training dataset is annotated with an explicit reasoning trace `R*_t` generated by a strong teacher LLM using prompts provided in Appendix C.1, which explains why the action `a_t` taken in state `s_t` consequently leads to the next state `s_{t+1}` and reward `r_{t+1}` given the available contexts. The experience model is trained with a joint objective over reasoning generation and next-state prediction:

$$
L_{\text{SFT}} = \mathbb{E}_{(s_t, a_t, s_{t+1}, R^*_t) \sim \mathcal{D}}\left[-\log P_\theta(R^*_t \mid s_t, a_t, H_t, D_k) - \log P_\theta(s_{t+1} \mid s_t, a_t, R^*_t, H_t, D_k)\right]
$$

where `H_t` denotes the interaction history, `D_k` denotes the retrieved top-k demonstrations, and `θ` denotes the parameters of `M_exp` (Equation 5, Section 4.1.2). This objective ensures two critical capabilities: first, the model learns to generate faithful reasoning traces that explain the causal effect of an action; second, it leverages these traces to predict consistent and informative next states. By training on reasoning traces rather than just state transitions, the experience model acquires the ability to generalize reasoning for novel rollouts during RL training rather than merely imitating expert trajectories.

The experience model backbone is Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct for all main results (Section 5.1), though ablation studies in Figure 5 show that smaller backbones like Llama-3.2-3B remain viable — it underperforms the 8B model but improves steadily as more data becomes available, reaching about 55% success on WebShop with 20k samples. This data efficiency is a key finding: even with very limited offline samples (2k–10k steps), the experience model already reaches competitive performance, indicating that large-scale offline datasets are not strictly necessary for effective experience synthesis (Section 6.4).

#### Experience Replay Buffer: Co-evolving Memory for Stable Training

The experience replay buffer serves two critical functions that distinguish DreamGym from prior synthetic environment approaches: it grounds the experience model's predictions in real-world knowledge, and it co-evolves with the agent policy to ensure produced rollouts remain aligned with the agent's updated capabilities (Section 4.1.1, Figure 2).

The buffer is initialized with offline real-world data, which provides essential context about actual environment dynamics that the experience model might not capture from reasoning alone. During training, the buffer is continuously enriched with fresh synthetic interactions generated on-the-fly, creating a feedback loop where the experience model and agent policy co-evolve. This design addresses a subtle but important problem: if the experience model is trained only on static offline data while the agent policy improves, the synthetic experiences may become misaligned with the agent's current capability level, leading to unstable training.

At each inference step, the experience model retrieves top-k similar trajectories from the replay buffer based on semantic similarity with the current state-action pair (Section 4.1.1). This retrieval mechanism serves as a form of in-context learning that reduces hallucinations — the ablation study in Figure 4 shows that removing access to past trajectory history ("w/o History") significantly reduces consistency because without awareness of prior turns, the model often drifts off-topic and breaks causal coherence in multi-step interactions.

The replay buffer also enables the curriculum task generator to identify high-value tasks, as described in the next section. By maintaining a diverse set of trajectories with varying success rates, the buffer provides the raw material for computing reward entropy and selecting tasks that are challenging but feasible for the current agent policy.

#### Curriculum-Based Task Generation: Automatic Difficulty Scaling

The curriculum task generator addresses the problem that scaling task collections is costly, as it typically requires significant human effort to verify the feasibility of each task in the target environment (Section 4.2). DreamGym alleviates this burden by having the experience model actively generate new tasks as variations of a set of `m` seed tasks:

$$
\tau_t = M_{\text{task}}(\{\tau^i_{t-1}\}_{i=1}^m)
$$

where `M_task` shares parameters with `M_exp` (Equation 6, Section 4.2). Parameter sharing is an important efficiency choice — rather than training a separate model for task generation, the same reasoning capabilities that enable state prediction also enable task variation generation.

The seed tasks are chosen based on two criteria: they must be sufficiently challenging for the current agent policy to maximize information gain, and they must be well-defined so that unrealistic or malformed tasks can be discarded (Section 4.2). To satisfy both conditions, DreamGym introduces a group-based reward entropy metric for selecting high-quality and challenging tasks. Formally, for a task `τ`, its value is defined as:

$$
V_\tau = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (r_i - \bar{r})^2, \quad \text{where} \quad \bar{r} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n r_i
$$

where `r_i` are the outcome rewards from `n` rollouts of task `τ` within the group `G` (Equation 7, Section 4.2). For GRPO, `G` can simply be the training group, while for PPO, tasks can be first clustered using a semantic embedder and each cluster forms a group from which task variations can be generated.

The key insight behind this metric is that a non-zero variance in `G` indicates that the agent observes both successes and failures on the task, signaling that the task is feasible yet challenging (Section 4.2). A task reaches maximum entropy when successes and failures are evenly balanced in `G`, providing the greatest information gain for credit assignment. This observation is consistent with recent findings that LLMs learn most effectively from tasks of intermediate difficulty (Gao et al., 2025). By feeding such high-entropy tasks into `M_task`, the system generates progressively more challenging variations to enhance agent exploration and knowledge acquisition.

To stabilize training, a hyperparameter `λ` bounds the proportion of synthetic tasks sampled per iteration (Section 4.2). This preserves sufficient coverage of the original task distribution while directing exploration toward the current policy's weakness regions for curriculum-based improvement. The ablation study in Table 2 shows that removing the task generator leads to a 6.6% drop in success rate on WebShop and a 6.0% drop on WebArena, confirming its importance — without adaptive task generation, the replay buffer saturates with low-entropy, repetitive trajectories, which limits experience diversity and stalls exploration (Section 6.2).

#### Learning from Synthetic Experiences: RL Training Pipeline

The policy training process follows a cyclical pattern as illustrated in Figure 2 (Section 4.3). DreamGym begins with a seed task set and generates multi-turn rollouts for each task by alternating between the agent policy, which selects actions from states, and the experience model, which predicts next states conditioned on the agent action, history, and task context. The collected rollouts are then used with standard RL algorithms to update the policy.

For PPO, the advantage function is computed using Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE):

$$
\hat{A}^{\text{PPO}}_t = \sum_{l=0}^{K-1} (\gamma\lambda)^l \left[r_{t+l} + \gamma V(s_{t+l+1}) - V(s_{t+l})\right]
$$

where `V(·)` is a value function approximated by an LLM, and `λ` controls the bias-variance tradeoff (Equation 2, Section 3.2). For GRPO, the value function is discarded and advantages are normalized within each group of responses `G` sampled for the same task instruction:

$$
\hat{A}^{\text{GRPO}}_t = (r_t - \text{mean}_{i \in G}(r_i)) / \text{std}_{i \in G}(r_i)
$$

where `r_t` is the reward for output `o_t` (Equation 3, Section 3.2). DreamGym is orthogonal to specific RL algorithms — it focuses on scaling the synthesis of diverse, informative experiences, thereby amplifying the effectiveness of whichever RL algorithm is used.

After each training iteration, the experience model augments the task set by generating variations of challenging tasks with high reward entropy (Section 4.3). This cycle of interaction, training, and curriculum expansion continues until convergence or a predefined training budget is reached. The training curve analysis in Figure 3 (Right) shows that success rate improves much more rapidly within the first 40 steps compared to baselines, demonstrating that synthesized trajectories offer more informative gradients than sparse real rollouts (Section 6.1). Additionally, DreamGym curves exhibit reduced variance in learning dynamics — baseline curves show larger oscillations caused by sparse or unstable rewards, while DreamGym curves remain smoother across runs, suggesting that synthesized trajectories provide not only denser but also more consistent feedback (Section 6.1).

#### Theoretical Guarantees: Why Synthetic Training Transfers to Real Environments

A critical question is whether policies trained purely on synthetic experiences can perform well in real environments. Appendix B.1 provides a theoretical analysis showing that under standard trust-region policy update assumptions, a policy optimized in the synthetic environment `M̂` is guaranteed to also achieve policy improvement in the real environment `M`.

Theorem 1 establishes a lower bound on policy improvement in the real environment:

$$
J_M(\pi') - J_M(\pi) \geq \underbrace{\frac{1}{1-\gamma} \mathbb{E}_{s \sim d^\pi_{\hat{M}}, a \sim \pi'(\cdot|s)}\left[A^\pi_{\hat{M}}(s, a)\right]}_{\text{synthetic surrogate gain in } M_{\text{exp}}} - \underbrace{\frac{4\gamma}{(1-\gamma)^2} V_{\text{max}} \delta}_{\text{trust-region penalty}} - \underbrace{2\left(\frac{\epsilon_R}{1-\gamma} + \frac{2\gamma R_{\text{max}}}{(1-\gamma)^2} \epsilon_P\right)}_{\text{experience model error}}
$$

where `γ ∈ (0, 1)` is the discount factor, rewards are bounded `R, R̂ ∈ [0, R_max]` with `V_max := R_max / (1 - γ)`, `δ` is the per-state KL radius enforced by PPO or GRPO, `ε_R = sup_{s,a} |R(s,a) - R̂(s,a)|` measures reward accuracy error, and `ε_P = sup_{s,a} TV(P(·|s,a), P̂(·|s,a))` measures domain consistency error in state transitions (Equation 9, Appendix B.1).

The key insight from this theorem is that policy improvement in the real environment depends on three terms: the synthetic surrogate gain (how much the policy improves in the synthetic environment), the trust-region penalty (constrained by the KL divergence between old and new policies), and the experience model error (how well the synthetic environment preserves learning-relevant signals) (Appendix B.1). Crucially, the experience model error depends only on reward accuracy `ε_R` and domain consistency `ε_P`, not on strict fidelity metrics like state reconstruction error. This theoretical result validates the paper's core design choice: the synthetic environment need only provide domain-consistent transitions and correct, retrospective learning signals, without having to clone the original environment at the raw state level (Appendix B.1).

In practice, both `ε_R` and `ε_P` can be made very small even when `M_exp` is trained with minimal trajectory data annotated with explicit reasoning traces, which explains why DreamGym achieves strong sim-to-real transfer despite operating in an abstract state space.

#### Sim-to-Real Transfer Protocol (DreamGym-S2R)

DreamGym-S2R extends the framework to a sim-to-real setting where the agent policy is first trained with synthetic experiences and then transferred to RL in real environments (Section 4.3). Pretraining in synthetic environments expands exploration coverage across diverse tasks and allows the agent to acquire broad knowledge at low cost, providing a strong initialization that makes subsequent real-environment learning more sample-efficient.

To enable seamless transfer, consistency of the state space between synthetic and real environments is ensured by applying the same rule-based mapping function or a lightweight fine-tuned model (Section 4.3). This is critical because if the state representations differ too much between synthetic and real environments, the policy learned in simulation may not generalize.

The empirical results validate this approach: DreamGym-S2R consistently outperforms both GRPO and PPO baselines trained from scratch in real environments while using only 5K real interactions compared to 80K for traditional baselines (Table 1, Section 5.2). On WebArena, DreamGym-S2R achieves 13.9% success rate with Llama-3.1-8B compared to 6.1% for GRPO trained from scratch — over 40% relative improvement using less than 10% of the external data (Table 1). This validates the hypothesis that synthetic training can serve as an efficient warm-start strategy that establishes a strong foundation for more sample-efficient RL in the real environment.

The generalization analysis in Figure 3 (Middle) further shows that policies trained in DreamGym exhibit strong cross-domain transferability — when trained on WebShop, the policy generalizes to WebArena and surpasses SFT models directly trained there, and vice versa (Section 5.2). This suggests that DreamGym learns within an abstract meta-representation space, enabling the agent to learn domain-agnostic behavioral priors rather than memorizing task-specific patterns. However, when the domain gap becomes too large, such as transferring from web-based environments to ALFWorld (embodied control), performance drops significantly, indicating the limits of current meta-representations (Section 5.2).

#### Design Choices and Trade-offs

Several non-obvious design choices warrant explanation, as they reflect important trade-offs in the system architecture:

**Abstract state space vs. raw observations**: The decision to operate in textual state space rather than raw HTML or pixel data is motivated by sample efficiency — the experience model can be trained on small public datasets because it does not need to learn to parse irrelevant structural artifacts (Section 4.1). However, this abstraction limits transfer to domains with very different state representations, as seen in the WebShop-to-ALFWorld transfer gap (Section 5.2).

**Reasoning traces vs. direct prediction**: Training the experience model to generate explicit reasoning traces before predicting states adds computational overhead but significantly improves consistency and reduces hallucination, as shown in the ablation study where "w/o Reasoning" hurts informativeness and increases factual errors (Figure 4, Section 6.3).

**Reward entropy vs. other difficulty metrics**: The choice of reward entropy for task selection is grounded in the observation that maximum information gain occurs when successes and failures are balanced — tasks that are too easy (all successes) or too hard (all failures) provide little learning signal (Section 4.2). Alternative metrics like task length or action complexity would not directly measure whether the task is at the right difficulty level for the current policy.

**Parameter sharing between `M_exp` and `M_task`**: Rather than training separate models for experience generation and task generation, DreamGym shares parameters between them (Section 4.2). This is an efficiency choice that leverages the same reasoning capabilities for both functions, though it means task generation quality is tied to experience model quality.

**Synthetic-only vs. hybrid training**: The paper evaluates both pure synthetic training (DreamGym) and hybrid sim-to-real training (DreamGym-S2R), finding that hybrid training achieves the best results (Table 1). This suggests that synthetic experiences are most valuable as a warm-start strategy that reduces the amount of expensive real-environment interaction needed, rather than as a complete replacement for real data.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the fundamental conceptual contributions that distinguish DreamGym from prior work. Rather than incremental engineering improvements, these insights represent shifts in how we think about environment modeling, curriculum design, and the relationship between simulation fidelity and learning utility.

---

### 4.1 Innovation 1: Fidelity Is Not the Goal — Causal Consistency Is

**What prior work assumed:** World model research has historically pursued environmental fidelity as the primary objective. Approaches like Dreamer (Hafner et al., 2020) and WebDreamer (Gu et al., 2024) attempt to reconstruct raw observations (pixels, HTML) with high accuracy, operating under the assumption that better reconstruction leads to better policy transfer.

**What DreamGym changes:** The paper makes a foundational conceptual shift: **agent training does not require perfectly realistic environments, but rather interaction data that is sufficiently diverse, informative, and causally grounded** (Introduction, Section 1). This reframes the objective from "clone the environment" to "preserve learning-relevant signals."

**Why this matters:** This insight has three significant implications:

1. **Sample efficiency gains:** By operating in an abstract textual state space rather than raw observations, the experience model requires far less training data. Figure 5 shows competitive performance with only 2k–10k offline samples, whereas fidelity-focused world models typically require orders of magnitude more data to learn accurate reconstruction.

2. **Token efficiency:** Abstract states discard irrelevant structural artifacts (HTML tags, headers, visual noise), producing trajectories that are more token-efficient during RL training. This directly reduces computational cost per rollout.

3. **Theoretical validation:** Appendix B.1 proves that policy improvement transfers as long as reward accuracy ($\epsilon_R$) and domain consistency ($\epsilon_P$) are bounded — strict state reconstruction error does not appear in the bound. This provides mathematical justification for abandoning fidelity as a primary objective.

**Incremental or fundamental?** This is a **fundamental innovation**. It challenges a core assumption in world model research and opens a new design space where abstraction is a feature, not a compromise. Prior work like UI-Simulator (Wang et al., 2025b) still operates with substantial engineering per environment; DreamGym demonstrates that abstract reasoning alone can suffice.

---

### 4.2 Innovation 2: Co-Evolving Replay Buffer for Policy-Aligned Experience

**What prior work assumed:** Synthetic environment approaches typically use static offline datasets or fixed demonstration collections. The experience generator does not adapt as the agent policy improves, leading to a distributional mismatch where synthetic experiences become misaligned with the agent's current capability level.

**What DreamGym changes:** The experience replay buffer is not a passive memory store — it **co-evolves with the agent policy** by continuously enriching with fresh synthetic interactions generated on-the-fly (Section 4.1.1, Figure 2). At each inference step, the experience model retrieves top-k similar trajectories from this buffer to ground its predictions.

**Why this matters:** This design addresses a subtle but critical stability problem that prior work overlooks:

| Aspect | Static Buffer (Prior) | Co-Evolving Buffer (DreamGym) |
|--------|----------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Distribution match** | Fixed to offline data distribution | Adapts to current policy distribution |
| **Hallucination risk** | Increases as agent improves beyond training data | Mitigated through fresh grounding |
| **Curriculum support** | Cannot identify policy weaknesses | Enables reward entropy computation for task selection |

The ablation study in Figure 4 provides direct evidence: removing access to past trajectory history ("w/o History") significantly reduces consistency because the model drifts off-topic and breaks causal coherence in multi-step interactions (Section 6.3). Table 2 shows that removing the replay buffer entirely causes a 4.7% drop on WebShop and a 3.6% drop on WebArena.

**Incremental or fundamental?** This is a **fundamental innovation** in how synthetic environments interact with agent training. Prior world models treat experience generation as a one-way process (model → agent); DreamGym establishes a feedback loop where the agent's own trajectories improve the experience model, which then generates better experiences for the agent. This co-evolution mechanism is analogous to self-play in AlphaGo but applied to environment modeling rather than opponent modeling.

---

### 4.3 Innovation 3: Reward Entropy as Automatic Curriculum Signal

**What prior work assumed:** Curriculum learning for agents typically requires human-designed task progressions or heuristic difficulty metrics (e.g., task length, action complexity). Methods like AgentSynth (Xie et al., 2025) and SCA (Zhou et al., 2025) synthesize diverse tasks but cannot automatically identify which tasks are at the right difficulty level for the current policy.

**What DreamGym changes:** The paper introduces **group-based reward entropy** as a principled, automatic signal for identifying challenging-but-feasible tasks (Section 4.2). Formally, task value is defined as the variance of outcomes across rollouts:

$$
V_\tau = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (r_i - \bar{r})^2
$$

A task reaches maximum entropy when successes and failures are evenly balanced, indicating it is neither too easy nor too hard for the current policy.

**Why this matters:** This insight connects curriculum learning to information theory in a way that prior work does not:

1. **No human annotation required:** The system automatically identifies which tasks provide maximum information gain without requiring humans to label difficulty levels or verify feasibility.

2. **Policy-adaptive:** As the agent improves, previously challenging tasks become low-entropy (mostly successes), and the system naturally shifts to harder variations. This creates an automatic curriculum that tracks the agent's capability.

3. **Theoretical grounding:** The observation that LLMs learn most effectively from tasks of intermediate difficulty (Gao et al., 2025) is operationalized through entropy rather than heuristics.

The ablation study in Table 2 confirms the importance: removing task generation causes a 6.6% drop on WebShop and 6.0% on WebArena (Section 6.2). Figure 3 (Right) shows that without the task generator, agents plateau quickly because the replay buffer saturates with low-entropy, repetitive trajectories.

**Incremental or fundamental?** This is a **fundamental innovation** in curriculum design for LLM agents. Prior work uses proxy metrics (task length, number of steps) that do not directly measure whether a task is learnable. Reward entropy directly measures the learning signal available from a task, making it a more principled foundation for automatic curriculum generation.

---

### 4.4 Innovation 4: Making RL Feasible in Non-RL-Ready Environments

**What prior work assumed:** RL training requires environments with reliable reset mechanisms, consistent reward signals, and scalable infrastructure. Environments like WebArena were considered unsuitable for RL due to these limitations, forcing researchers to use imitation learning or supervised fine-tuning instead.

**What DreamGym changes:** By synthesizing experiences through the reasoning model, DreamGym **makes RL training feasible in environments that previously could not support it**. Table 1 shows that on WebArena (a non-RL-ready benchmark), DreamGym achieves success rates exceeding 30% across all backbones where zero-shot RL baselines fail entirely (Section 5.2).

**Why this matters:** This is not merely a performance improvement — it is a **new capability**:

| Environment Type | Prior RL Approaches | DreamGym |
|-----------------|---------------------|----------|
| **RL-ready (WebShop, ALFWorld)** | Expensive but feasible | Matches performance with 0 real interactions |
| **Non-RL-ready (WebArena)** | Not feasible | First viable RL approach, 30%+ success |
| **Sim-to-real transfer** | Requires substantial real data | 40% improvement with &lt;10% real data |

The efficiency gain is substantial: Figure 3 (Left) shows DreamGym reduces training effort to roughly one-third or one-fifth of RL baselines in real environments while achieving higher performance (Section 5.2). This arises from dense feedback offered by curriculum-based rollout synthesis and lightweight abstract state transitions that avoid heterogeneous environment bottlenecks.

**Incremental or fundamental?** This is a **fundamental innovation** in what environments can support RL training. Prior work accepts environment limitations as fixed constraints; DreamGym treats the environment itself as a design variable that can be synthesized to overcome those constraints. This expands the set of problems where RL can be applied.

### 4.5 Summary: What Distinguishes DreamGym from Incremental Improvements

The table below distinguishes DreamGym's fundamental innovations from what would constitute incremental improvements:

| Aspect | Incremental Improvement | DreamGym's Fundamental Innovation |
|--------|------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Environment modeling** | Better reconstruction accuracy | Abstract state space with causal consistency guarantees |
| **Experience quality** | Larger offline datasets | Co-evolving replay buffer that adapts to policy |
| **Task diversity** | More human-curated tasks | Automatic curriculum via reward entropy |
| **RL applicability** | Faster training in existing environments | RL made feasible in previously non-RL-ready environments |
| **Sim-to-real transfer** | Better domain adaptation techniques | Theoretical guarantee without state reconstruction fidelity |

**The unifying insight:** DreamGym treats environments not as fixed constraints to be endured, but as **generators of structured, reasoning-rich experiences** that can be designed to optimize learning (Conclusion, Section 7). This reframing enables all four innovations above and suggests a new direction for agent training research where the boundary between "environment" and "training signal" becomes fluid rather than fixed.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate DreamGym's claims through systematic experimentation. Rather than simply reporting numbers, I will walk through the experimental design choices, explain what each result tells us about the system's behavior, and critically assess whether the evidence supports the paper's core assertions.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: What Was Tested and Why

The experimental design addresses three distinct questions that map to DreamGym's key claims:

1. **Can DreamGym enable RL in environments where it was previously infeasible?** (Non-RL-ready environments)
2. **Can DreamGym match traditional RL while eliminating real-environment costs?** (RL-ready but costly environments)
3. **Can synthetic pretraining improve sample efficiency for real-environment fine-tuning?** (Sim-to-real transfer)

To answer these questions comprehensively, the authors test across three environments with different characteristics (Section 5.1):

| Environment | Domain | RL-Ready? | Key Challenge |
|-------------|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **WebShop** | E-commerce navigation | Yes | Product search, query refinement, multi-page browsing |
| **ALFWorld** | Embodied household control | Yes | Physical affordances, object manipulation, spatial reasoning |
| **WebArena-Lite** | Realistic web interaction | **No** | Long-horizon tasks, no reliable reset, high computational cost |

**Why WebArena-Lite matters:** The authors explicitly note that WebArena is "not RL-ready" because it lacks scalable data collection and environment reset mechanisms while incurring high computational costs (Section 5.1). This is the critical test case — if DreamGym works here, it demonstrates the framework's ability to overcome fundamental environment limitations, not just reduce costs.

**Agent backbones:** Three model families are evaluated to test generalizability (Section 5.1):
- Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct (smaller, efficient)
- Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (medium, used for main results)
- Qwen-2.5-7B-Instruct (different architecture family)

**Baseline categories (Section 5.1):**
1. **Offline Imitation Learning:** SFT (20K samples), DPO (40K samples) — represents the traditional approach when RL is infeasible
2. **Traditional Online RL:** GRPO and PPO with 80K real interactions — represents the gold standard when RL is feasible
3. **DreamGym:** Same RL algorithms but with 0 real interactions (purely synthetic)
4. **DreamGym-S2R:** Synthetic pretraining followed by 5K real interactions — tests the hybrid warm-start strategy

**Metric:** Success rate (%) — the percentage of tasks completed successfully. This is an outcome-based metric where `r = 1` only at episode completion and `r = 0` otherwise (Section 4.1.1). While sparse, this metric is standard for agent benchmarks and directly measures task completion capability.

**Computational setup:** All experiments use 8 nodes with A100 GPUs and 4 nodes with H100 GPUs (Appendix A.1–A.3). This is important context — DreamGym's efficiency gains are measured against baselines running on identical hardware, ensuring fair comparison.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results: What the Numbers Tell Us

#### Claim 1: DreamGym Enables RL in Non-RL-Ready Environments

**Table 1 (WebArena columns)** provides the most compelling evidence for this claim. Consider the Llama-3.1-8B backbone:

| Method | Real Data | Success Rate |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| SFT | 20K | 5.5% |
| DPO | 40K | 4.8% |
| GRPO (Traditional) | 80K | 6.1% |
| **DreamGym** | **0** | **9.1%** |
| **DreamGym-S2R** | **5K** | **9.7%** |

**Key observations:**

1. **Traditional RL fails:** GRPO with 80K real interactions achieves only 6.1% — barely above SFT with 20K samples. The authors explain this in Section 5.2: "zero-shot RL baselines suffer from limited exploration diversity and sparse reward signals in the original environments." The environment's instability and lack of reset mechanisms prevent effective policy improvement regardless of how much real data is collected.

2. **DreamGym succeeds where RL cannot:** With 0 real interactions, DreamGym achieves 9.1% — a **49% relative improvement** over traditional GRPO. This is not a marginal gain; it demonstrates that synthetic experiences can provide learning signals that real environment interactions cannot, when the real environment is fundamentally broken for RL.

3. **The paper's "30% improvement" claim:** The Abstract states DreamGym "outperforms all baselines by over 30%." Looking at Table 1, this claim holds: 9.1% vs 6.1% is a 49% relative improvement for L3.1-8B; 13.3% vs 7.3% is an 82% relative improvement for L3.2-3B. The claim is conservative.

**Why this works:** Section 5.2 explains that DreamGym is "not merely a surrogate for costly rollouts, but a mechanism that makes RL training feasible in domains that were previously intractable." The reasoning-based experience model provides consistent state transitions and reward signals that the real WebArena environment cannot guarantee due to its dynamic, unstable nature.

#### Claim 2: DreamGym Matches Traditional RL Without Real Interactions

**Table 1 (WebShop and ALFWorld columns)** tests whether DreamGym can match traditional RL in environments where RL is feasible but expensive.

**WebShop results (Llama-3.1-8B):**
- GRPO (80K real): 65.0%
- **DreamGym (0 real): 63.9%**
- Difference: **1.1 percentage points** (not statistically significant per the paper)

**ALFWorld results (Llama-3.1-8B):**
- GRPO (80K real): 70.9%
- **DreamGym (0 real): 66.3%**
- Difference: **4.6 percentage points**

**Interpretation:** On WebShop, DreamGym essentially matches traditional RL performance while eliminating 80K real interactions entirely. On ALFWorld, there is a modest gap, but DreamGym still achieves 93.5% of the traditional RL performance with zero real data.

**Why the ALFWorld gap?** Section 5.2's generalization analysis provides insight: "when the domain gap becomes too large, such as transferring from web-based environments (WebShop/WebArena) to ALFWorld (embodied control), the performance drops significantly, indicating the limits of current meta-representations." ALFWorld requires physical affordance reasoning (e.g., "can I put this object here?") that may be harder to capture in abstract textual states than web navigation patterns.

#### Claim 3: DreamGym-S2R Provides Efficient Warm-Start for Real RL

This is where DreamGym shows its strongest empirical results. **Table 1** compares DreamGym-S2R (5K real interactions after synthetic pretraining) against traditional RL (80K real interactions from scratch):

**WebShop (Llama-3.1-8B):**
- Traditional GRPO (80K): 65.0%
- **DreamGym-S2R (5K): 75.0%**
- Improvement: **+10 percentage points with 93.75% less real data**

**ALFWorld (Llama-3.1-8B):**
- Traditional GRPO (80K): 70.9%
- **DreamGym-S2R (5K): 75.9%**
- Improvement: **+5 percentage points with 93.75% less real data**

**WebArena (Llama-3.1-8B):**
- Traditional GRPO (80K): 6.1%
- **DreamGym-S2R (5K): 9.7%**
- Improvement: **+3.6 percentage points (59% relative)**

**The "40% improvement" claim:** The Abstract states DreamGym-S2R "yields over 40% performance improvement compared to training from scratch in real-world environments while using less than 10% of the external data." Looking at WebShop L3.2-3B: 70.5% (DreamGym-S2R) vs 62.1% (Traditional) = 13.5% relative improvement. For L3.1-8B: 75.0% vs 65.0% = 15.4% relative. The 40% figure appears to reference specific backbone/environment combinations (possibly Qwen-2.5-7B on ALFWorld: 82.4% vs 79.8% = 3.3%, which doesn't match either). **This claim appears inconsistent with Table 1 data** — the actual improvements range from 13-59% depending on the specific configuration, not a uniform 40%.

**Sample efficiency visualization (Figure 3 Left):** The training curve shows DreamGym achieves substantial performance gains on WebArena while "reducing training effort (including both rollouts sampling time and GPU hours) to roughly one-third or even one-fifth of RL baselines" (Section 5.2). This efficiency arises from two factors:
1. Dense feedback from curriculum-based rollout synthesis (vs sparse real rewards)
2. Lightweight abstract state transitions (vs heavyweight browser instances)

### 5.3 Ablation Studies: What Each Component Contributes

The authors conduct systematic ablation studies to validate that each architectural component serves a distinct purpose. **Table 2** shows average success rates when removing individual components:

| Configuration | WebShop | WebArena |
|--------------|---------|----------|
| **Full DreamGym** | **63.9%** | **13.3%** |
| w/o Experience Replay | 59.2% | 9.7% |
| w/o Experience Reasoning | 55.8% | 7.3% |
| w/o Task Generation | 57.3% | 7.3% |

**Interpretation:**

1. **Experience Replay Buffer (-4.7% WebShop, -3.6% WebArena):** Section 6.2 explains that without the replay buffer, "the replay buffer may saturate with low-entropy, repetitive trajectories, which limits the diversity of experiences and stalls exploration." The buffer's co-evolution mechanism (Section 4.1.1) ensures synthetic experiences remain aligned with the agent's improving policy.

2. **Experience Reasoning (-8.1% WebShop, -6.0% WebArena):** This is the largest single component drop. Section 6.3's Figure 4 analysis shows that removing reasoning "mainly hurts informativeness of the states and increases hallucination." Without explicit chain-of-thought traces, the experience model produces shallow, less factually grounded predictions.

3. **Task Generation (-6.6% WebShop, -6.0% WebArena):** Section 6.2 notes that removing task generation causes agents to "make some initial progress but then plateau more quickly." The curriculum mechanism (Section 4.2) continually produces challenging tasks that push the agent beyond its current capability, preventing stagnation.

**Figure 4 (Experience Model Quality):** The authors evaluate four variants using GPT-4o as a judge across four criteria (Consistency, Diversity, Informativeness, Hallucination), with scores in {0, 1, 2}:

| Variant | Consistency | Diversity | Informativeness | Hallucination |
|---------|-------------|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| Traditional (Real Env) | ~1.8 | ~1.5 | ~1.6 | ~1.9 |
| **DreamGym (Full)** | **~1.9** | **~1.7** | **~1.8** | **~1.9** |
| w/o History | ~1.3 | ~1.6 | ~1.5 | ~1.7 |
| w/o Reasoning | ~1.5 | ~1.5 | ~1.2 | ~1.4 |

**Key insight:** Removing history ("w/o History") significantly reduces consistency — "without awareness of prior turns, the model often drifts off-topic and breaks causal coherence in multi-step interactions" (Section 6.3). Removing reasoning ("w/o Reasoning") mainly hurts informativeness and increases hallucination — "without reasoning capabilities, the generated experiences tend to become shallow and less factually grounded" (Section 6.3).

**Figure 5 (Data Efficiency):** This figure investigates how experience model performance varies with offline training data size:

- **Llama-3.1-8B on WebShop:** Exceeds 50% success rate with only 10K samples
- **Llama-3.2-3B on WebShop:** Reaches ~55% with 20K samples
- **WebDreamer comparison:** Initially outperforms Llama-3.1-8B at small data scales (due to web-specific pretraining) but converges as offline samples increase

**Implication:** "Large-scale offline datasets are not strictly necessary for effective experience synthesis" (Section 6.4). This is crucial for practical adoption — organizations need not collect massive demonstration datasets before deploying DreamGym.

### 5.4 Training Dynamics: Why DreamGym Converges Faster

**Figure 3 (Right)** compares training curves on WebShop across different setups. Two notable patterns emerge:

1. **Rapid initial improvement:** "The success rate improves much more rapidly within the first 40 steps, showing that synthesized trajectories offer more informative gradients than sparse real rollouts" (Section 6.1). This validates the curriculum mechanism — by exposing the agent to high-entropy tasks (balanced success/failure), DreamGym provides denser learning signals than outcome-only rewards from real environments.

2. **Reduced variance:** "Baseline curves exhibit larger oscillations caused by sparse or unstable rewards, while DreamGym curves remain smoother across runs" (Section 6.1). This stability is critical for practical training — unstable learning dynamics often require extensive hyperparameter tuning and can lead to training collapse.

**Why synthesized trajectories provide better gradients:** The experience model's reasoning traces (Section 4.1.1) create implicit intermediate supervision. Even though the final reward is sparse (r=1 only at completion), the reasoning process provides step-by-step feedback about whether actions are valid and how states should transition. This is analogous to dense reward shaping but learned automatically through the experience model's reasoning capabilities.

### 5.5 Generalization and Transfer Limits

**Figure 3 (Middle)** tests cross-domain transferability — can a policy trained on one environment perform well on another?

**Successful transfers:**
- WebShop → WebArena: Surpasses SFT models directly trained on WebArena
- WebArena → WebShop: Similarly outperforms SFT baselines

**Failed transfer:**
- WebShop/WebArena → ALFWorld: "Performance drops significantly, indicating the limits of current meta-representations" (Section 5.2)

**Interpretation:** Section 5.2 explains that "DreamGym learns within an abstract meta-representation space, enabling the agent to learn domain-agnostic behavioral priors rather than memorizing task-specific patterns." Web navigation tasks (WebShop, WebArena) share similar abstract structures — clicking elements, filling forms, navigating pages — which transfer well. ALFWorld's embodied control requires different abstractions (physical affordances, spatial relationships) that the current experience model does not capture.

**This reveals a boundary condition:** DreamGym's abstraction is powerful but not universal. The framework works best when source and target environments share similar action spaces and state representations. Cross-domain transfer to fundamentally different interaction paradigms (web → embodied) remains challenging.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment: Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

#### Strengths of the Experimental Design

1. **Three-tier validation:** Testing on non-RL-ready (WebArena), RL-ready but costly (WebShop, ALFWorld), and sim-to-real scenarios provides comprehensive coverage of DreamGym's intended use cases.

2. **Multiple backbones:** Evaluating three different model families (Llama-3.2-3B, Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen-2.5-7B) demonstrates that results are not architecture-specific.

3. **Systematic ablations:** Table 2 and Figures 4-5 isolate the contribution of each component, preventing the "kitchen sink" problem where it's unclear what drives improvements.

4. **Training dynamics analysis:** Figure 3 (Right) goes beyond final performance to show how learning unfolds over time, providing insight into why DreamGym works (faster convergence, reduced variance).

#### Limitations and Open Questions

1. **The "40% improvement" claim inconsistency:** As noted in Section 5.2, the Abstract's claim of "over 40% performance improvement" does not uniformly match Table 1 data. The actual improvements range from 13-59% depending on configuration. This suggests either (a) the claim references specific unpublished configurations, or (b) the claim is imprecisely stated.

2. **ALFWorld performance gap:** DreamGym achieves 66.3% vs GRPO's 70.9% on ALFWorld with L3.1-8B — a 4.6 point gap that the paper attributes to domain representation limits but does not deeply investigate. Understanding this gap better would clarify DreamGym's boundaries.

3. **Single-environment focus:** The Limitations section (Section 7) acknowledges that DreamGym is evaluated on individual environments rather than a unified multi-environment setting. The paper does not test whether a single experience model can handle multiple domains simultaneously.

4. **Reward scheme simplicity:** All experiments use outcome-based rewards (r=1 at completion, r=0 otherwise) per Section 4.1.1. While this is standard, it leaves open whether DreamGym could leverage denser reward signals if available.

5. **Compute costs not fully quantified:** While Figure 3 (Left) mentions "one-third or one-fifth" training effort reduction, the paper does not provide detailed GPU-hour comparisons. The experience model itself requires LLM inference at each step, which has non-trivial compute costs that are not fully accounted for.

#### Overall Assessment

**The experiments convincingly support the core claims:**

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Key Supporting Result |
|-------|------------------|----------------------|
| RL feasible in non-RL-ready environments | **Strong** | WebArena: 9.1% (DreamGym) vs 6.1% (Traditional GRPO) |
| Matches traditional RL without real data | **Strong** | WebShop: 63.9% (DreamGym) vs 65.0% (GRPO 80K) |
| Efficient warm-start for real RL | **Strong** | WebShop: 75.0% (S2R 5K) vs 65.0% (GRPO 80K) |
| Each component contributes | **Strong** | Table 2 ablations show 4-8% drops per component |
| Cross-domain transfer works (within domain) | **Moderate** | Web↔Web transfer succeeds; Web→Embodied fails |

**The most significant finding:** DreamGym makes RL training **possible** in environments where it was previously infeasible (WebArena), not just more efficient. This is a capability expansion, not merely an optimization. Prior work accepted environment limitations as fixed constraints; DreamGym demonstrates that synthesizing experiences through reasoning can overcome those constraints.

**The most important caveat:** DreamGym's abstraction has limits. Cross-domain transfer to fundamentally different interaction paradigms (web → embodied) shows significant performance drops, indicating that the meta-representation space is not universal. Future work on unified multi-environment experience models (mentioned in Limitations, Section 7) would address this boundary.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While DreamGym demonstrates compelling results across multiple environments, a critical understanding of its limitations is essential for researchers considering adoption or extension. This section examines the assumptions underlying the approach, scenarios where it may struggle, computational trade-offs, and open questions that remain unresolved.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions: What DreamGym Requires to Work

DreamGym's effectiveness depends on several non-trivial assumptions that may not hold in all settings. Understanding these prerequisites helps identify where the framework will succeed versus where it may fail.

#### Assumption 1: Abstract State Representations Capture Learning-Relevant Signals

The core design choice to operate in an abstract textual state space rather than raw observations (Section 4.1) assumes that **domain-consistent transitions and correct learning signals matter more than state reconstruction fidelity**. This is theoretically justified in Appendix B.1, which shows policy improvement depends on reward accuracy ($\epsilon_R$) and domain consistency ($\epsilon_P$), not state reconstruction error.

**Where this breaks down:** The cross-domain transfer results in Figure 3 (Middle) reveal the boundary of this assumption. When transferring from web-based environments (WebShop/WebArena) to ALFWorld (embodied control), "performance drops significantly, indicating the limits of current meta-representations" (Section 5.2). 

**Why this happens:** Web navigation tasks share similar abstract structures—clicking elements, filling forms, navigating pages—which transfer well through textual abstractions. ALFWorld requires physical affordance reasoning (e.g., "can I put this object here?", spatial relationships, object permanence) that the current experience model does not capture effectively. The abstract state space that works for web tasks does not naturally encode physical constraints.

**Practical implication:** DreamGym works best when source and target environments share similar action spaces and state representations. Researchers should not expect seamless transfer across fundamentally different interaction paradigms without additional adaptation.

#### Assumption 2: Offline Trajectory Data Exists for Experience Model Training

Despite DreamGym's sample efficiency, the experience model still requires offline trajectory data for initial training. Section 6.4 and Figure 5 show competitive performance with only 2k–10k offline samples, but **zero offline data is not feasible**.

**Data requirements by environment (Appendix A.1–A.3):**
| Environment | Offline Trajectories | Source |
|-------------|---------------------|--------|
| WebShop | 3,600 (1,600 human + 2,000 oracle/random) | Official repository + oracle agent |
| ALFWorld | 5,200 (3,200 expert + 2,000 oracle/random) | Training split + oracle/random policies |
| WebArena | 4,800 | High-performing agents from public leaderboard |

**Where this breaks down:** For novel environments without any existing demonstration data or public benchmarks, researchers must first collect offline trajectories before DreamGym can be deployed. This could involve:
- Running existing agents (possibly suboptimal) to collect initial trajectories
- Human demonstration collection (costly)
- Oracle agent development (requires environment access and reward logic)

**The chicken-and-egg problem:** If an environment is truly non-RL-ready (no reset mechanism, unstable dynamics), collecting even offline trajectories may be challenging. DreamGym reduces the amount of real-environment interaction needed, but does not eliminate it entirely for initial bootstrapping.

#### Assumption 3: Reasoning Capabilities Suffice for Environment Dynamics

The experience model relies on chain-of-thought reasoning to predict state transitions (Section 4.1.1). This assumes that **environment dynamics can be captured through linguistic reasoning rather than requiring specialized world knowledge or physical simulation**.

**Where this breaks down:** The ablation study in Figure 4 shows that removing reasoning ("w/o Reasoning") causes the largest performance drop (-8.1% on WebShop, -6.0% on WebArena in Table 2). However, even with reasoning, the experience model cannot perfectly capture all environment dynamics.

**Evidence of reasoning limits:** Section 6.3 notes that removing reasoning "mainly hurts informativeness of the states and increases hallucination." The full DreamGym model still exhibits some hallucination (score ~1.9/2.0 in Figure 4), indicating that reasoning alone cannot eliminate factual errors. For domains requiring precise numerical computation, temporal reasoning, or external knowledge retrieval, the experience model may produce plausible-sounding but incorrect state transitions.

**Practical implication:** DreamGym is best suited for environments where state transitions can be reasoned about linguistically. Domains requiring precise calculation (e.g., financial trading with exact numerical outcomes) or real-time physical dynamics (e.g., robotics with continuous control) may require additional mechanisms beyond reasoning-based prediction.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios: Where DreamGym Does Not Apply

The paper explicitly acknowledges several scenarios that remain outside the current framework's scope.

#### Single-Environment Focus

**What the paper states:** "Our work primarily investigates single-environment learning setups, where DreamGym is applied to individual agentic scenarios" (Limitations and Future Work, Section 7).

**What this means:** Each DreamGym deployment trains a separate experience model for a specific environment (WebShop, ALFWorld, or WebArena). The framework does not currently support:
- A unified experience model that handles multiple environments simultaneously
- Knowledge transfer between different environment types during training
- Multi-task learning where an agent must master several environments in parallel

**Why this matters:** A general-purpose agent should ideally learn across diverse environments without requiring separate experience models for each. The Limitations section suggests future work could "build a universal world model that unifies multiple environment models, enabling knowledge transfer across environments" (Section 7), but this remains unimplemented.

**Practical implication:** Organizations seeking to train agents that operate across multiple domains (e.g., both web navigation and embodied control) would need to maintain separate DreamGym instances, increasing infrastructure complexity and preventing cross-domain knowledge accumulation.

#### Dense Reward Settings

**What the paper uses:** All experiments employ outcome-based rewards where `r = 1` only at task completion and `r = 0` otherwise (Section 4.1.1), following Feng et al. (2025).

**What this excludes:** The paper does not evaluate DreamGym in environments with:
- Dense intermediate rewards (e.g., partial credit for subtask completion)
- Shaped reward functions that provide step-by-step feedback
- Multi-objective rewards with trade-offs between competing goals

**Why this matters:** Many real-world tasks benefit from dense reward signals. For example, a web navigation task might reward successful search query formulation, correct product identification, and proper checkout completion separately. DreamGym's current design assumes sparse rewards, and it is unclear how the experience model would handle dense reward prediction or whether the curriculum mechanism (based on outcome variance) would remain effective.

**Open question:** Could DreamGym's experience model learn to generate meaningful dense rewards, or would this require additional training objectives beyond the current SFT formulation in Equation 5?

#### Multi-Agent and Adversarial Settings

**What the paper addresses:** DreamGym trains a single agent policy interacting with a static experience model.

**What this excludes:** The framework does not support:
- Multi-agent scenarios where multiple agents interact (cooperative or competitive)
- Adversarial environments where the environment itself adapts to the agent's policy
- Self-play scenarios where the agent competes against versions of itself

**Why this matters:** Many important agent applications involve multi-agent dynamics—negotiation, auction bidding, collaborative task completion, or competitive gaming. DreamGym's current design assumes a single-agent MDP formulation (Section 3.1), which does not capture game-theoretic considerations or opponent modeling.

**Practical implication:** Researchers working on multi-agent RL would need to extend DreamGym's experience model to simulate multiple agents or opponent policies, which is not addressed in the current work.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

While DreamGym reduces real-environment interaction costs, it introduces new computational considerations that warrant careful examination.

#### Experience Model Inference Overhead

**The hidden cost:** DreamGym eliminates expensive real-environment rollouts (browser instances, server maintenance, API calls), but each synthetic rollout requires **LLM inference for the experience model** at every step.

**Scale of inference:** For a typical trajectory with ~10 steps (noted in Table 1 caption: "a trajectory often has ~10 steps"), training with 80K transitions requires approximately 8,000 trajectory rollouts. Each rollout step involves:
- Experience model forward pass (Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct for main results, Section 5.1)
- Retrieval of top-k demonstrations from replay buffer
- Chain-of-thought reasoning generation
- State and reward prediction

**Compute comparison:** The paper states DreamGym reduces "training effort (including both rollouts sampling time and GPU hours) to roughly one-third or even one-fifth of RL baselines" (Section 5.2, Figure 3 Left). However, this comparison is against real-environment RL baselines that also require substantial infrastructure (AWS servers for WebArena, browser instances for WebShop).

**What's missing:** The paper does not provide:
- Absolute GPU-hour numbers for DreamGym training
- Breakdown of compute costs between experience model inference and agent policy training
- Comparison against pure offline training methods (SFT, DPO) which have no online inference cost

**Practical implication:** For organizations without access to scalable LLM serving infrastructure, the experience model inference overhead may still be substantial. The efficiency gain is relative to real-environment RL, not necessarily to all training paradigms.

#### Replay Buffer Scaling

**Current design:** The experience replay buffer stores trajectories and retrieves top-k similar experiences based on semantic similarity (Section 4.1.1). The retrieval uses an arbitrary semantic encoder `φ(·)` with cosine similarity.

**Scaling concern:** As training progresses and the buffer accumulates more trajectories:
- Retrieval latency may increase with buffer size
- Semantic embedding computation for each new state-action pair adds overhead
- Buffer management (eviction policies, deduplication) is not discussed

**What the paper says:** The buffer is "continuously enriched with fresh interactions generated on-the-fly" (Section 4.1.1), but no maximum buffer size or eviction strategy is specified in the implementation details (Appendix A).

**Open question:** At what trajectory count does retrieval become a bottleneck? Does the buffer require periodic pruning or compression for long training runs?

#### Experience Model Training Data Requirements

**Sample efficiency claim:** Figure 5 shows the experience model reaches competitive performance with 2k–10k offline samples, and "large-scale offline datasets are not strictly necessary" (Section 6.4).

**The caveat:** While 2k–10k samples is modest compared to typical RL datasets, this still requires:
- Access to existing agent trajectories (from leaderboard agents or oracle policies)
- Annotation of reasoning traces using a strong teacher LLM (Section 4.1.2, Appendix C.1)
- Quality control to ensure trajectory correctness

**Cost of reasoning annotation:** Each transition requires a reasoning trace `R*_t` generated by a teacher LLM. For 10k transitions with ~10 steps per trajectory, this means 100k reasoning annotations. The computational cost of generating these annotations is not quantified in the paper.

**Practical implication:** For novel environments without existing trajectory datasets, the upfront cost of collecting and annotating offline data may be non-trivial, even if it is amortized over extensive RL training.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

Several weaknesses emerge from the experimental results that warrant acknowledgment, along with open questions for future research.

#### The ALFWorld Performance Gap

**The observation:** On ALFWorld with Llama-3.1-8B, DreamGym achieves 66.3% success rate compared to GRPO's 70.9% with real interactions (Table 1). This 4.6 percentage point gap is the largest among all environments tested.

**The paper's explanation:** Section 5.2 attributes this to domain representation limits: "when the domain gap becomes too large, such as transferring from web-based environments to ALFWorld (embodied control), the performance drops significantly."

**What remains unclear:**
- Is the gap due to the experience model's inability to capture physical affordances?
- Would training the experience model on more ALFWorld-specific data close the gap?
- Does the abstract textual state space fundamentally limit embodied reasoning?

**Open question:** Can DreamGym's state representation be extended to better capture physical dynamics, or is a different abstraction needed for embodied domains?

#### Inconsistent Performance Improvement Claims

**The discrepancy:** The Abstract claims DreamGym-S2R "yields over 40% performance improvement compared to training from scratch in real-world environments." However, Table 1 data shows variable improvements:

| Configuration | Traditional | DreamGym-S2R | Relative Improvement |
|--------------|-------------|--------------|---------------------|
| WebShop L3.2-3B | 62.1% | 70.5% | +13.5% |
| WebShop L3.1-8B | 65.0% | 75.0% | +15.4% |
| ALFWorld L3.1-8B | 70.9% | 75.9% | +7.0% |
| WebArena L3.2-3B | 7.3% | 13.9% | +90.4% |
| WebArena L3.1-8B | 6.1% | 9.7% | +59.0% |

**Analysis:** The 40% figure appears to reference specific configurations (possibly averaging across backbones or referencing WebArena results), but is not uniformly accurate across all settings. This imprecision makes it difficult to set accurate expectations for practitioners.

**Recommendation:** Readers should interpret the "40% improvement" claim as an upper bound observed in specific configurations rather than a guaranteed outcome.

#### Reward Scheme Limitations

**Current approach:** Outcome-based rewards (`r = 1` at completion, `r = 0` otherwise) are used throughout all experiments (Section 4.1.1).

**Potential weakness:** Sparse rewards are known to be challenging for RL, requiring many samples for effective credit assignment. While DreamGym's curriculum mechanism (Section 4.2) helps by selecting high-entropy tasks, the fundamental sparsity remains.

**Open question:** Could DreamGym benefit from learned dense rewards? The experience model already generates reasoning traces that implicitly evaluate action quality—could these be leveraged to produce intermediate reward signals without requiring manual reward engineering?

#### Hallucination in Experience Model

**The evidence:** Figure 4 shows the full DreamGym experience model scores ~1.9/2.0 on the hallucination metric, indicating some factual errors remain even with reasoning traces.

**The risk:** Hallucinated state transitions could teach the agent incorrect environment dynamics, potentially leading to policies that fail when deployed in real environments. The sim-to-real results (Table 1) suggest this risk is manageable, but the failure modes are not systematically analyzed.

**Open question:** What types of hallucinations occur most frequently? Are they concentrated in specific state types or action categories? Understanding this could inform targeted improvements to the experience model.

#### Lack of Multi-Environment Evaluation

**The gap:** As noted in Section 7, DreamGym is evaluated on individual environments rather than a unified multi-environment setting.

**Why this matters:** A truly general agent framework should demonstrate:
- Ability to train on multiple environments simultaneously
- Knowledge transfer between environments during training
- Scalability to many environments without linear cost increase

**Open question:** Can a single experience model handle multiple environments, or does each environment require a dedicated experience model? If the latter, how does the infrastructure cost scale with the number of environments?

### 6.5 Summary: When to Use DreamGym (and When Not To)

Based on the limitations identified above, here is a practical guide for researchers considering DreamGym:

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| **Web navigation with limited real access** | ✅ Strong fit | DreamGym's primary success case (WebArena results) |
| **Embodied control with physical dynamics** | ⚠️ Use with caution | ALFWorld gap suggests representation limits |
| **Novel environment with no offline data** | ⚠️ Requires bootstrapping | Need initial trajectory collection before deployment |
| **Multi-agent or adversarial settings** | ❌ Not supported | Single-agent MDP formulation only |
| **Dense reward environments** | ⚠️ Untested | Current design assumes sparse outcome rewards |
| **Cross-domain generalist agents** | ⚠️ Partial support | Web↔Web transfer works; Web→Embodied fails |
| **Resource-constrained deployment** | ⚠️ Consider inference costs | Experience model requires LLM serving infrastructure |

**The key takeaway:** DreamGym is not a universal solution for all agent RL challenges. It excels in environments where (1) abstract textual state representations capture relevant dynamics, (2) some offline trajectory data exists for experience model training, and (3) the cost of real-environment interaction is prohibitive. For domains outside these boundaries, the framework may require significant adaptation or may not be applicable.

**The research opportunity:** Many of the limitations identified—multi-environment support, dense reward handling, hallucination reduction, embodied domain adaptation—represent fertile ground for future work. The paper's Limitations section (Section 7) explicitly invites extension toward "a universal world model that unifies multiple environment models," suggesting the authors view DreamGym as a foundation rather than a final solution.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how DreamGym reshapes the research landscape for agent reinforcement learning, identifies concrete follow-up directions enabled by this work, outlines practical deployment scenarios, and provides guidance on when to adopt this framework versus alternatives.

---

### 7.1 Landscape Impact: Reframing Environment Modeling for Agent RL

DreamGym represents a paradigm shift in how we conceptualize environments for RL training. Rather than treating environments as fixed constraints that must be endured or faithfully replicated, the paper establishes that **environments can be designed as learning signal generators** optimized for agent improvement. This reframing has three cascading implications for the field.

#### From Fidelity to Learning Utility

The theoretical result in Appendix B.1 fundamentally challenges the world model research community's historical focus on reconstruction accuracy. Theorem 1 proves that policy improvement in real environments depends on:

$$
J_M(\pi') - J_M(\pi) \geq \text{synthetic surrogate gain} - \text{trust-region penalty} - 2\left(\frac{\epsilon_R}{1-\gamma} + \frac{2\gamma R_{\text{max}}}{(1-\gamma)^2} \epsilon_P\right)
$$

Critically, **state reconstruction error does not appear in this bound** — only reward accuracy ($\epsilon_R$) and domain consistency ($\epsilon_P$) matter. This mathematical justification validates abandoning pixel-level or HTML-level fidelity in favor of abstract, reasoning-based state representations.

**Field-wide implication:** Future world model research should prioritize measuring $\epsilon_R$ and $\epsilon_P$ directly rather than using reconstruction metrics (e.g., MSE, BLEU) as proxies for environment quality. This shifts evaluation methodology from "how realistic does the simulation look?" to "how well does the simulation preserve learning-relevant signals?"

#### Making RL Accessible in Previously Intractable Domains

Table 1 demonstrates that DreamGym enables RL training in WebArena — an environment the authors explicitly classify as "not RL-ready" due to lack of reset mechanisms, unstable dynamics, and high computational costs (Section 5.1). Traditional GRPO with 80K real interactions achieves only 6.1% success rate on Llama-3.1-8B, while DreamGym with 0 real interactions achieves 9.1% — a **49% relative improvement** where RL was previously considered infeasible.

**Field-wide implication:** The set of problems where RL can be applied is no longer bounded by environment infrastructure. Researchers working on domains with expensive simulators (robotics, scientific discovery, clinical decision-making) can now consider RL training even when real-environment interaction is prohibitively costly or unsafe.

#### Co-Evolution as a Design Principle

The experience replay buffer's co-evolution mechanism (Section 4.1.1) — where synthetic experiences continuously update to match the agent's improving policy — introduces a new architectural pattern for synthetic environment research. Prior work like UI-Simulator (Wang et al., 2025b) uses static experience generation; DreamGym demonstrates that **bidirectional feedback between agent and environment model** improves stability and prevents distributional mismatch.

**Field-wide implication:** Future synthetic environment frameworks should incorporate mechanisms for the environment model to adapt based on agent trajectories, not just generate experiences unidirectionally. This parallels self-play in AlphaGo but applies it to environment modeling rather than opponent modeling.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper's findings and acknowledged limitations suggest several concrete research directions. I organize these by proximity to the current work — from immediate extensions to longer-term foundational questions.

#### Immediate Extensions (1–2 Years)

| Direction | Motivation | Specific Approach |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| **Multi-environment experience models** | Paper acknowledges single-environment focus (Section 7, Limitations) | Train a unified `M_exp` with environment tokens or adapters, similar to multi-task LLM training |
| **Dense reward synthesis** | All experiments use outcome-based rewards (Section 4.1.1) | Leverage reasoning traces `R_t` to generate intermediate rewards; train `M_exp` with auxiliary reward prediction head |
| **Hallucination reduction** | Figure 4 shows ~1.9/2.0 hallucination score remains | Add verification steps where `M_exp` cross-checks predictions against retrieved buffer examples before committing |
| **Embodied domain adaptation** | ALFWorld shows 4.6-point gap vs traditional RL (Table 1) | Extend abstract state space to include physical affordance primitives (e.g., "graspable", "containable") |

#### Medium-Term Directions (2–4 Years)

**Universal Experience Models.** The Limitations section explicitly suggests building "a universal world model that unifies multiple environment models, enabling knowledge transfer across environments" (Section 7). This would require:

- A shared abstract state representation that captures commonalities across web, embodied, and tool-use domains
- Environment-specific adapters that handle domain-unique dynamics
- Curriculum mechanisms that balance exploration across environments rather than within a single environment

**Adversarial Experience Generation.** Current DreamGym uses a cooperative experience model that helps the agent learn. An adversarial variant could:

- Train `M_exp` to generate challenging states that expose agent weaknesses (beyond reward entropy)
- Implement minimax training where the agent tries to succeed while the experience model tries to find failure modes
- This could accelerate robustness learning for safety-critical applications

**Theoretical Refinements.** Appendix B.1 provides a lower bound under trust-region assumptions. Follow-up work could:

- Derive tighter bounds that account for curriculum effects (the current bound assumes fixed task distribution)
- Analyze how replay buffer size affects $\epsilon_P$ (domain consistency error)
- Extend the theory to multi-agent settings where environment dynamics include other learning agents

#### Long-Term Foundational Questions (4+ Years)

**Can Synthetic-Only Training Reach Superhuman Performance?** DreamGym-S2R still requires 5K real interactions for best results (Table 1). A foundational question is whether purely synthetic training can ever match or exceed real-environment RL without any sim-to-real transfer. This depends on:

- Whether $\epsilon_R$ and $\epsilon_P$ can be driven arbitrarily close to zero through better experience models
- Whether there exist environment dynamics that fundamentally cannot be captured through linguistic reasoning
- Whether the abstract state space can be made rich enough to encode all learning-relevant information

**What Is the Minimal State Abstraction?** The paper shows abstract textual states work for web navigation but struggle with embodied control (Section 5.2, Figure 3 Middle). A deeper question is: what is the minimal state representation that supports transfer across all agent domains? Candidates include:

- Graph-structured representations capturing object relationships
- Affordance-based representations encoding action possibilities
- Hybrid symbolic-neural representations combining reasoning with learned embeddings

**Automated Environment Design.** DreamGym manually designs the abstract state space for each environment. Long-term, could the system automatically discover optimal abstractions? This connects to meta-learning and neural architecture search, but applied to environment representation rather than model architecture.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

DreamGym's capabilities map to several high-value application scenarios where real-environment RL is currently impractical.

#### Enterprise Agent Deployment

**Scenario:** Companies want to deploy LLM agents for customer service, IT operations, or internal workflow automation, but cannot risk agents making irreversible mistakes in production systems.

**DreamGym fit:** The framework enables extensive RL training in synthetic environments before any real-system access. The sim-to-real results (Table 1) show that 5K real interactions after synthetic pretraining outperforms 80K interactions from scratch — meaning production risk is minimized while achieving superior performance.

**Specific use cases:**
- Database query agents that learn optimal query patterns without risking production database load
- IT automation agents that learn system navigation without risking service disruptions
- Compliance-checking agents that learn regulatory requirements through synthetic scenarios

#### Scientific Discovery Agents

**Scenario:** Scientific environments (laboratory equipment, simulation software, data analysis pipelines) are expensive to access and often lack reset mechanisms.

**DreamGym fit:** The "non-RL-ready environment" results on WebArena (Section 5.2) demonstrate that DreamGym enables RL where traditional approaches fail. Scientific domains share these characteristics — expensive equipment, irreversible actions, limited access windows.

**Specific use cases:**
- Laboratory automation agents that learn experimental protocols through synthetic lab simulations
- Data analysis agents that learn optimal exploration strategies for scientific datasets
- Simulation control agents that learn to configure complex physics or chemistry simulators

#### Safety-Critical Agent Training

**Scenario:** Agents operating in healthcare, finance, or autonomous systems must be highly reliable, but real-environment training carries unacceptable risk.

**DreamGym fit:** The co-evolving replay buffer (Section 4.1.1) ensures synthetic experiences remain aligned with the agent's improving policy, reducing the risk of learning incorrect dynamics. The theoretical guarantees (Appendix B.1) provide bounds on sim-to-real transfer quality.

**Specific use cases:**
- Medical diagnosis agents that learn diagnostic reasoning through synthetic patient cases
- Financial trading agents that learn market interaction patterns without risking capital
- Autonomous vehicle agents that learn edge-case handling through synthetic traffic scenarios

#### Education and Training Systems

**Scenario:** Intelligent tutoring systems need to adapt to individual student needs, but collecting real student interaction data is slow and privacy-sensitive.

**DreamGym fit:** The curriculum task generator (Section 4.2) automatically creates progressively challenging tasks based on reward entropy — this mechanism can be adapted to create personalized learning paths based on student performance variance.

**Specific use cases:**
- Programming tutors that generate increasingly challenging coding exercises
- Language learning agents that adapt conversation difficulty to learner proficiency
- Math tutoring systems that identify and target specific knowledge gaps

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

Based on the paper's implementation details and experimental results, here is practical guidance for researchers considering DreamGym adoption.

#### When to Prefer DreamGym Over Alternatives

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| **Environment has no reset mechanism** | DreamGym | Traditional RL infeasible; DreamGym makes RL possible (WebArena results, Table 1) |
| **Real interaction cost > $10/step** | DreamGym | Synthetic rollouts eliminate per-step costs; 5K real interactions sufficient for S2R |
| **Safety concerns with exploration** | DreamGym | Synthetic training isolates risky exploration from production systems |
| **Limited offline data (< 5K trajectories)** | DreamGym | Figure 5 shows competitive performance with 2K–10K samples |
| **Web or GUI-based domains** | DreamGym | Strongest results on WebShop/WebArena; abstract state space well-suited |
| **Embodied/physical domains** | Traditional RL or DreamGym-S2R | ALFWorld gap (4.6 points) suggests pure synthetic training has limits |
| **Abundant real interaction data** | Traditional RL | If 80K+ real interactions are affordable, traditional RL matches DreamGym (Table 1) |
| **Multi-agent settings** | Traditional RL | DreamGym supports single-agent MDP only (Section 3.1) |

#### Minimum Viable Setup for Reproduction

Based on Appendix A, a minimal DreamGym deployment requires:

**Offline Data Collection:**
- 3,000–5,000 trajectories from the target environment (can be suboptimal agents)
- Each trajectory annotated with reasoning traces using a teacher LLM (prompts in Appendix C)
- Estimated annotation cost: ~100K reasoning traces for 10K trajectories

**Model Infrastructure:**
- Experience model: Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct or equivalent (Section 5.1)
- Agent policy: Same family as experience model for best transfer
- Replay buffer: Semantic encoder for trajectory retrieval (any embedding model)
- GPU requirements: 8× A100 or 4× H100 for parallel training (Appendix A)

**Training Configuration:**
- RL algorithm: PPO or GRPO (both supported; GRPO more scalable per Section 3.2)
- Curriculum hyperparameter: λ bounds synthetic task proportion (Section 4.2; value not specified in main text)
- Replay buffer retrieval: top-k demonstrations with cosine similarity (Section 4.1.1; k not specified)

**Missing Implementation Details:** The paper does not specify several hyperparameters needed for exact reproduction:
- Retrieval buffer size `k` for similar experience lookup
- Maximum replay buffer capacity and eviction policy
- Curriculum mixing parameter `λ` value
- Learning rates and batch sizes for agent policy training

Researchers should contact the authors (zhaorun@uchicago.edu, {jase, dathuynh}@meta.com) for these details or treat them as hyperparameters to tune.

#### Integration Checklist

Before deploying DreamGym, verify:

- [ ] **Offline trajectories available:** At least 2K–5K trajectories exist for experience model training (Figure 5)
- [ ] **Abstract state space definable:** Domain dynamics can be represented textually without losing critical information
- [ ] **Reward logic specified:** Even if sparse, the task completion criterion must be clearly defined
- [ ] **LLM serving infrastructure:** Experience model requires scalable inference (8B model at ~10 steps/trajectory)
- [ ] **Evaluation protocol:** Hold out test tasks separate from training to avoid contamination (Appendix A.1)

#### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**Pitfall 1: Insufficient offline data diversity.** Figure 5 shows performance improves with more diverse offline samples. Collecting 10K trajectories from a single agent policy may not provide enough variation for robust experience modeling.

**Pitfall 2: Skipping reasoning annotation.** Table 2 shows removing experience reasoning causes an 8.1% drop on WebShop — the largest single component loss. Do not omit the reasoning trace annotation step (Appendix C.1).

**Pitfall 3: Ignoring curriculum mixing.** Section 4.2 notes that hyperparameter `λ` bounds synthetic task proportion to preserve original task distribution coverage. Setting `λ = 1.0` (all synthetic tasks) may cause distributional shift.

**Pitfall 4: Cross-domain transfer without validation.** Figure 3 (Middle) shows Web→Embodied transfer fails significantly. Do not assume policies trained on web environments will transfer to physical domains without empirical validation.

### 7.5 Concluding Perspective

DreamGym establishes that **the bottleneck in agent RL is not algorithmic — it is environmental**. By treating environments as designable components rather than fixed constraints, the framework unlocks RL training in domains previously considered intractable. The theoretical guarantees (Appendix B.1), empirical results (Table 1, Figures 3–5), and ablation studies (Table 2) collectively validate this approach.

However, the work is a foundation, not a final solution. The limitations identified in Section 7 — single-environment focus, sparse rewards only, hallucination remaining, embodied domain gaps — represent active research frontiers. The most impactful follow-up work will address these boundaries while preserving DreamGym's core insight: that **synthetic experiences optimized for learning utility can outperform real experiences optimized for fidelity**.

For practitioners, the decision framework is clear: if real-environment interaction is costly, unsafe, or infeasible, DreamGym provides a viable path to RL training that was previously unavailable. For researchers, the open questions around universal experience models, dense reward synthesis, and minimal state abstractions offer rich directions for advancing the field beyond the current state of the art.
