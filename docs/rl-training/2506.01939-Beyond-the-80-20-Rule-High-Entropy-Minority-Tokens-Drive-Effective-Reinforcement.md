## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the opaque mechanisms of Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) by demonstrating that reasoning performance is driven primarily by high-entropy "forking tokens" rather than the full token sequence. By restricting policy gradient updates to only the top 20% of highest-entropy tokens, the authors achieve significant improvements over full-gradient updates on Qwen3 models, specifically gaining +11.04 points on AIME'25 and +7.71 points on AIME'24 for the Qwen3-32B base model. These findings challenge standard full-token training practices, revealing that low-entropy majority tokens contribute minimally to reasoning optimization and enabling more efficient RLVR strategies that scale effectively with model size.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this work, we must first establish the current landscape of Large Language Model (LLM) reasoning, the standard techniques used to improve it, and the specific blind spots this paper aims to illuminate.

### 2.1 The Rise of Reasoning Models and RLVR
Recent advancements in LLMs have shown substantial progress in complex domains like mathematics and programming. Models such as OpenAI o1, Claude 3.7, DeepSeek R1, and Qwen3 have demonstrated enhanced reasoning capabilities, largely propelled by **test-time scaling** methodologies (Section 1). A pivotal technique driving these improvements is **Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR)**.

In RLVR, models optimize their outputs through RL objectives tied to **automated correctness verification**. Unlike human feedback, which is subjective and expensive, verifiable rewards provide a clear signal (e.g., "is the math answer correct?") that allows the model to learn from trial and error. While recent RLVR advancements have stemmed from algorithmic innovations (e.g., DAPO, GRPO) and cross-domain applications, existing implementations typically **train over all tokens** in a generated sequence (Section 1).

This standard practice assumes that every token in a reasoning trajectory contributes equally to the learning process. However, this assumption neglects the **heterogeneous functional roles** tokens play. For instance, some tokens might be critical decision points, while others are merely completing a sentence structure. By failing to prioritize critical decision points, current methods may hinder further performance gains or compute efficiency.

### 2.2 The Black Box of Token-Level Learning
The core problem this paper addresses is the **lack of understanding regarding which tokens actually facilitate reasoning** during RLVR training.

*   **The Mechanism Gap:** While RLVR is known to work, its internal mechanisms are not well understood (Abstract). We know the model gets better at reasoning, but we do not know *where* in the sequence the learning is happening.
*   **The Efficiency Gap:** Standard policy gradient algorithms update the model weights based on every single token generated. If a large portion of these tokens contributes little to reasoning improvement, this represents a significant waste of computational resources.
*   **The Entropy Gap:** Prior works have highlighted the importance of generation entropy in chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning, but they typically analyze the entropy of **all tokens collectively** (Section 3). There is a lack of granular, token-level analysis examining how entropy patterns evolve *during* RLVR training.

### 2.3 Prior Approaches and Their Limitations
To position this work, we must look at both the algorithmic baselines and the prior analytical work surrounding RLVR.

#### 2.3.1 Algorithmic Baselines
The paper builds upon state-of-the-art RLVR algorithms that operate without a value network. Key predecessors include:
*   **Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO):** A widely adopted policy gradient algorithm that restricts updates to remain within a proximal region of the old policy using a clipped surrogate objective (Section 2.2).
*   **Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO):** Discards the value network by estimating advantages using the average reward within a group of sampled responses (Section 2.2).
*   **Dynamic sAmpling Policy Optimization (DAPO):** The baseline used in this work. It builds on GRPO by removing the KL penalty, introducing a `clip-higher` mechanism, and applying a token-level policy gradient loss (Section 2.2).

**Limitation:** All these algorithms, in their standard forms, apply policy gradient updates uniformly across the generated sequence. They do not differentiate between tokens based on their informational content or uncertainty.

#### 2.3.2 Prior Analytical Work
Several studies have attempted to analyze RLVR characteristics:
*   **Reasoning Behaviors vs. Correctness:** Gandhi et al. (2025) found that the presence of reasoning behaviors, rather than answer correctness, drives performance improvements (Section 7).
*   **Structure vs. Content:** Li et al. (2025) showed that the structure of long CoTs is critical, while the content of individual steps has minimal impact (Section 7).
*   **Critical Tokens:** Vassoyan et al. (2025) and Lin et al. (2024) identified "critical tokens" where models are prone to errors, proposing modifications to encourage exploration around them (Section 7).

**Limitation:** Prior work often judges token importance based on the **correctness of the output** or error rates. This paper argues that **token entropy** may be a more accurate criterion for reflecting the underlying mechanisms of LLMs (Section 7). Furthermore, prior entropy analyses lacked the token-level resolution to distinguish between "forking" decisions and "following" executions.

### 2.4 Positioning This Work
This paper positions itself as a **mechanistic exploration** of RLVR through the novel perspective of **token entropy patterns**. It diverges from prior work in three key ways:

1.  **Token-Level Granularity:** Instead of analyzing average entropy over a whole sequence, it examines entropy at each token step `$t$`, defined as:
    ```math
    H_t := -\sum_{j=1}^{V} p_{t,j} \log p_{t,j}
    ```
    where `$p_{t,j}$` is the probability distribution over the vocabulary `$V$` at step `$t$` (Section 2.1).
2.  **Functional Distinction:** It categorizes tokens into **high-entropy "forking tokens"** (critical decision points) and **low-entropy "majority tokens"** (execution steps), as depicted in **Figure 1(a)** (Section 1).
3.  **Selective Optimization:** Based on the finding that RLVR primarily adjusts the entropy of high-entropy tokens (Section 4), the paper proposes restricting policy gradient updates to only the top 20% highest-entropy tokens (Section 5).

By shifting the focus from "all tokens" to "high-entropy minority tokens," this work challenges the standard 80/20 rule in training efficiency. It suggests that the efficacy of RLVR arises primarily from optimizing the specific tokens that decide reasoning directions, rather than the tokens that simply follow the path. This positioning not only aims to improve performance but also to provide a clearer theoretical understanding of *why* RLVR generalizes better than Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) (Section 6).

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a **mechanistic analysis and optimization method** for Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) in Large Language Models, with the core idea that restricting policy gradient updates to only the top 20% highest-entropy tokens can match or exceed full-token training performance while revealing that reasoning improvements originate from specific "forking" decision points rather than uniform sequence optimization.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a **modified reinforcement learning training pipeline** that selectively updates model weights using only high-uncertainty tokens identified during reasoning generation, rather than updating on every token in the generated sequence. This approach solves the problem of inefficient and potentially counterproductive RLVR training by recognizing that low-entropy tokens (which simply complete linguistic patterns) contribute minimally to reasoning learning, while high-entropy tokens (which represent branching decision points) drive nearly all performance gains.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The technical pipeline consists of four major components that operate in sequence. **First**, the **Token Entropy Calculator** computes entropy values for each token position in generated reasoning sequences using the model's probability distribution over the vocabulary at each step. **Second**, the **Entropy Pattern Analyzer** examines the distribution of these entropy values across tokens to identify which positions exhibit high versus low uncertainty, establishing the 80/20 split between majority low-entropy tokens and minority high-entropy tokens. **Third**, the **Selective Gradient Mask** filters the policy gradient loss computation to include only tokens whose entropy exceeds a batch-specific threshold corresponding to the top 20% highest-entropy positions. **Fourth**, the **RLVR Optimizer** applies the masked gradients to update model parameters using the DAPO algorithm with clip-higher mechanisms, completing one training iteration. Information flows from sequence generation through entropy computation, then through gradient masking, and finally to parameter updates, with the key innovation being the insertion of the entropy-based filter between gradient computation and parameter optimization.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with **token entropy calculation** because understanding how uncertainty is measured at each token position is foundational to all subsequent analysis and optimization decisions.
- We then examine **entropy patterns in Chain-of-Thought reasoning** to establish the empirical observation that motivates the entire approach—namely, that high-entropy tokens are rare but functionally distinct from low-entropy tokens.
- We proceed to **entropy pattern evolution during RLVR training** to demonstrate that the base model's entropy structure persists through training, justifying why selective optimization can work without disrupting learned patterns.
- We detail the **selective policy gradient formulation** that mathematically implements the core contribution, showing exactly how low-entropy tokens are excluded from loss computation.
- We conclude with **training configurations and hyperparameters** to provide complete reproducibility details and explain why specific values were chosen over alternatives.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Type of paper and core mechanism.** This is a **mechanistic analysis and algorithmic modification paper** that combines empirical observation with targeted intervention in the RLVR training process. The core mechanism operates by computing token-level entropy during sequence generation, ranking tokens within each training batch by their entropy values, and then masking the policy gradient loss so that only the top 20% highest-entropy tokens contribute to weight updates.

**Token entropy calculation foundation.** The paper defines token-level generation entropy (referred to as `token entropy` for brevity) for each token position $t$ using the standard Shannon entropy formula applied to the model's output probability distribution. The mathematical definition is:

$$H_t := -\sum_{j=1}^{V} p_{t,j} \log p_{t,j}$$

where $V$ represents the vocabulary size, $p_{t,j}$ denotes the probability assigned to vocabulary item $j$ at position $t$, and the probability distribution $(p_{t,1}, \cdots, p_{t,V}) = p_t = \pi_\theta(\cdot | q, o_{&lt;t})$ is computed as $\text{Softmax}(z_t / T)$. Here, $\pi_\theta$ denotes the LLM parameterized by $\theta$, $q$ is the input query, $o_{&lt;t} = (o_1, o_2, \cdots, o_{t-1})$ represents all previously generated tokens up to position $t-1$, $z_t \in \mathbb{R}^V$ denotes the pre-softmax logits at time step $t$, and $T \in \mathbb{R}$ is the decoding temperature. A critical clarification the paper emphasizes is that "token entropy" corresponds to the **token generation distribution at position $t$**, not to any specific token identity—meaning that if the same token string appears at two different positions $t$ and $t'$, their entropies $H_t$ and $H_{t'}$ are not necessarily equal because they depend on the context-dependent probability distribution at each position, not the token string itself.

**Baseline RLVR algorithm selection.** The paper uses **Dynamic sAmpling Policy Optimization (DAPO)** as its baseline RLVR algorithm because DAPO represents one of the state-of-the-art RLVR methods that operates without a value network, making it suitable for isolating the effects of token selection from value estimation artifacts. DAPO builds upon Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) by removing the KL penalty term, introducing a clip-higher mechanism that asymmetrically allows larger positive policy ratios, incorporating dynamic sampling strategies, applying token-level policy gradient loss, and adopting overlong reward shaping for extended reasoning sequences. The DAPO objective function that the paper modifies is:

$$J_{DAPO}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{B \sim D, (q,a) \sim B, \{o_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{old}}(\cdot|q)} \left[ \frac{1}{\sum_{i=1}^G |o_i|} \sum_{i=1}^G \sum_{t=1}^{|o_i|} \min\left(r_t^i(\theta) \hat{A}_t^i, \text{clip}\left(r_t^i(\theta), 1-\epsilon_{low}, 1+\epsilon_{high}\right) \hat{A}_t^i\right) \right]$$

subject to the constraint that $0 < |\{o_i | \text{is\_equivalent}(a, o_i)\}| < G$, ensuring that not all sampled responses are correct or incorrect within each group. In this equation, $r_t^i(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(o_t^i | q, o_{&lt;t}^i)}{\pi_{\theta_{old}}(o_t^i | q, o_{&lt;t}^i)}$ represents the importance ratio between the current policy and the old policy, $\hat{A}_t^i$ is the estimated advantage computed using group-relative rewards, $\epsilon_{low} = 0.2$ and $\epsilon_{high} = 0.28$ are the clipping bounds that asymmetrically allow larger positive updates, $G$ is the group size for advantage estimation, and $|o_i|$ denotes the length of response $i$.

**Entropy pattern discovery in Chain-of-Thought reasoning.** The paper's analysis begins by examining token entropy distributions in Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning sequences generated by Qwen3-8B on AIME'24 and AIME'25 benchmarks using a decoding temperature of $T = 1.0$. The researchers collected over $10^6$ response tokens and computed entropy for each token according to Equation 1, revealing two critical patterns that motivate the entire approach. **Entropy Pattern 1** states that typically only a minority of tokens are generated with high entropy while a majority of tokens are outputted with low entropy—specifically, the entropy of over half the tokens (approximately 50.64%) is below $10^{-2}$, while only 20% of tokens have entropy greater than 0.672, establishing the 80/20 split that the optimization leverages. **Entropy Pattern 2** states that tokens with the highest entropy typically serve to bridge the logical connection between two consecutive parts of reasoning (acting as "forks"), while tokens with the lowest entropy tend to complete the current part of a sentence or finish constructing a word (acting as "followers"). The paper identifies high-entropy tokens as logical connectors such as "wait," "however," "unless," "thus," "also," "since," "because," "suppose," "assume," "given," and "define," whereas low-entropy tokens are often word suffixes, source code fragments, or mathematical expression components that exhibit high determinism.

**Experimental validation of forking token importance.** To quantitatively confirm the role of high-entropy tokens as reasoning forks, the paper conducts controlled experiments by manually modulating the decoding temperature assigned to forking tokens versus other tokens during evaluation. The temperature modulation follows this rule:

$$T'_t = \begin{cases} T_{high} & \text{if } H_t > h_{threshold} \\ T_{low} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

where $z_t \in \mathbb{R}^V$ denotes the pre-softmax logits for token $t$, $T'_t \in \mathbb{R}$ represents the adjusted temperature for token $t$, $h_{threshold} = 0.672$ is the entropy threshold used to distinguish forking tokens (estimated by calculating the 80th percentile among the sampled $10^6$ tokens), $T_{high} \in \mathbb{R}$ corresponds to the temperature for forking tokens, and $T_{low} \in \mathbb{R}$ corresponds to the temperature for other tokens. The modified probability distribution is then computed as $p'_t = \text{Softmax}(z_t / T'_t)$. Results from this experiment (presented in **Figure 3**) show that lowering $T_{high}$ significantly degrades performance compared to lowering $T_{low}$, while increasing $T_{high}$ results in substantially better performance than increasing $T_{low}$, confirming that forking tokens benefit from operating at even higher entropy levels than they naturally exhibit.

**Entropy pattern preservation during RLVR training.** Before proposing selective optimization, the paper establishes that RLVR training largely preserves the base model's entropy patterns rather than fundamentally restructuring them. Using DAPO applied to the Qwen3-14B base model, the researchers generated 16 responses per question across six benchmarks and computed the overlap ratio (the fraction of shared top 20% high-entropy positions) between each intermediate training checkpoint and both the base model and final RLVR model. **Table 1** shows that although overlap with the base model gradually decreases during training, the base model's overlap still remains above 86% at convergence (step 1360), suggesting that RLVR largely retains the base model's entropy patterns regarding which tokens exhibit high or low uncertainty. Additionally, the paper computes the average entropy change after RLVR for each 5% entropy percentile range of the base model, revealing that tokens with higher initial entropy in the base model tend to exhibit larger increases in entropy after RLVR, while the entropy of low-entropy tokens varies only within a small range throughout training. This finding is critical because it justifies why selective optimization can work—if RLVR primarily adjusts high-entropy tokens anyway, then explicitly masking low-entropy tokens from gradient computation should not disrupt the learning signal.

**Selective policy gradient formulation.** Building on the DAPO objective in Equation 4, the paper introduces a modified objective that discards policy gradients from low-entropy tokens and trains the model using only the policy gradients of high-entropy tokens. For each batch $B$ sampled from the dataset $D$, the maximum objective is:

$$J^{B}_{HighEnt}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{B \sim D, (q,a) \sim B, \{o_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{old}}(\cdot|q)} \left[ \frac{1}{\sum_{i=1}^G \sum_{t=1}^{|o_i|} \mathbb{I}\left[H_t^i \geq \tau^B_\rho\right]} \sum_{i=1}^G \sum_{t=1}^{|o_i|} \mathbb{I}\left[H_t^i \geq \tau^B_\rho\right] \cdot \min\left(r_t^i(\theta) \hat{A}_t^i, \text{clip}\left(r_t^i(\theta), 1-\epsilon_{low}, 1+\epsilon_{high}\right) \hat{A}_t^i\right) \right]$$

subject to the same constraint $0 < |\{o_i | \text{is\_equivalent}(a, o_i)\}| < G$. In this equation, $H_t^i$ denotes the entropy of token $t$ in response $i$, $\mathbb{I}[\cdot]$ is the indicator function that evaluates to 1 if the condition inside holds and 0 otherwise, $\rho \in (0, 1]$ is a predefined ratio specifying the top proportion of high-entropy tokens to be selected within a batch, and $\tau^B_\rho$ is the corresponding entropy threshold within batch $B$ such that only tokens with $H_t^i \geq \tau^B_\rho$, comprising the top-$\rho$ fraction of all tokens in the batch, are used to compute the gradient. Comparing this equation with the original DAPO objective, there are exactly two differences: **(i)** the advantage term is multiplied by $\mathbb{I}\left[H_t^i \geq \tau^B_\rho\right]$, ensuring that within each (micro-)batch $B$, only tokens $o_t^i$ whose corresponding entropy $H_t^i \geq \tau^B_\rho$ are involved in the policy gradient loss calculation, and **(ii)** the normalization term for the number of tokens is adjusted to $\sum_{i=1}^G \sum_{t=1}^{|o_i|} \mathbb{I}\left[H_t^i \geq \tau^B_\rho\right]$, meaning that only tokens whose entropy is at least the threshold $\tau^B_\rho$ are considered in the denominator. This formulation ensures that the gradient magnitude remains appropriately scaled even though only a subset of tokens contributes to the loss.

**Training configuration and hyperparameters.** The paper adapts its training codebase from verl (Sheng et al., 2024) and follows the training recipe of DAPO with specific configurations that apply to both the vanilla DAPO baseline (using all tokens) and the selective high-entropy token approach. Both configurations employ clip-higher with $\epsilon_{high} = 0.28$ and $\epsilon_{low} = 0.2$, overlong reward shaping with a maximum response length of 20480 tokens and a cache length of 4096 tokens, a training batch size of 512, and a mini-batch size of 32 in verl's configuration, resulting in 16 gradient steps per training batch. The learning rate is set to $10^{-6}$ with no learning rate warmup or scheduling, and importantly, the training process **excludes both KL divergence loss and entropy loss**, which distinguishes this setup from traditional PPO implementations that typically include entropy bonuses to encourage exploration. For the main results, the paper sets $\rho = 20\%$ in the selective gradient equation, meaning that the policy is updated using only the gradients of the top 20% highest-entropy tokens within each batch. ## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper delivers several contributions that range from fundamental mechanistic discoveries to practical algorithmic improvements. Below, we distinguish between the **foundational insights** (which change how we understand RLVR) and the **algorithmic innovations** (which directly improve training efficiency and performance).

---

### 4.1 Fundamental Insight: Reasoning Performance is Driven by High-Entropy "Forking Tokens"

**What makes this novel:** Prior work on RLVR analyzed generation entropy at the **sequence level** or treated all tokens as functionally equivalent during policy gradient updates. This paper is the first to demonstrate that token entropy follows a **highly skewed distribution** in Chain-of-Thought reasoning, where approximately 80% of tokens have entropy below $10^{-2}$ while only 20% exceed 0.672 (Section 3). More critically, it establishes a **functional distinction**: high-entropy tokens act as logical "forks" that determine reasoning direction, while low-entropy tokens merely execute along established paths.

**Why this matters:** This insight fundamentally challenges the assumption underlying all standard RLVR implementations—that every token contributes equally to learning. The paper's controlled temperature modulation experiment (**Figure 3**) provides causal evidence: lowering the temperature of forking tokens significantly degrades performance, while lowering the temperature of non-forking tokens has minimal effect. This is not merely a correlation; it demonstrates that **maintaining high entropy at decision points is causally necessary for reasoning performance**.

**Common misconception:** One might assume that high-entropy tokens represent model uncertainty or confusion that should be reduced during training. The paper shows the opposite: **high entropy at forking tokens is desirable** because it enables exploration across diverse reasoning pathways. Reducing this entropy (as SFT tends to do) locks the model into rigid reasoning patterns.

---

### 4.2 Fundamental Insight: RLVR Preserves Base Model Entropy Patterns Rather Than Restructuring Them

**What makes this novel:** A reasonable expectation might be that RLVR training fundamentally restructures how the model distributes uncertainty across tokens—perhaps creating new high-entropy positions or shifting entropy patterns entirely. Instead, the paper reveals that **RLVR largely preserves the base model's entropy structure**. **Table 1** shows that even at convergence (step 1360), the overlap between the base model's top 20% high-entropy positions and the trained model's remains above 86%.

**Why this matters:** This finding has two critical implications:

1. **Justifies selective optimization:** If RLVR primarily adjusts the entropy of tokens that were already high-entropy in the base model, then explicitly masking low-entropy tokens from gradient computation should not disrupt the learning signal. This provides the theoretical foundation for the algorithmic contribution in Section 4.3.

2. **Explains generalization differences:** The paper hypothesizes in **Discussion 1 (Section 6)** that this entropy preservation is why RL generalizes better than Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). SFT pushes output logits toward one-hot distributions, reducing entropy at forking tokens and losing reasoning path flexibility. RLVR maintains this flexibility by preserving high entropy at decision points.

**Non-obvious design choice:** The paper excludes both KL divergence loss and entropy loss from training (Section 3.4). This is deliberate: adding an entropy bonus would increase entropy uniformly across all tokens, including the low-entropy majority, which the paper argues is **suboptimal for CoT reasoning** (Discussion 3, Section 6). The `clip-higher` mechanism in DAPO achieves a more targeted effect by moderately raising $\epsilon_{high}$ to promote entropy primarily in high-entropy tokens.

---

### 4.3 Algorithmic Innovation: Selective Policy Gradient Updates on Top 20% High-Entropy Tokens

**What makes this novel:** Building on the insights above, the paper introduces a **masked policy gradient formulation** (Equation 6 in Section 3) that restricts updates to only the top $\rho = 20\%$ highest-entropy tokens within each batch. This differs from prior "critical token" approaches (Vassoyan et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2024) that identify important tokens based on **output correctness or error rates**. Instead, this method uses **token entropy as the selection criterion**, which the paper argues more accurately reflects the underlying mechanisms of LLM reasoning (Section 7).

**Why this matters:** The performance results are striking and counterintuitive. As shown in **Table 2**:

| Model | Benchmark | Full-Token DAPO | Forking-Token DAPO | Improvement |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Qwen3-32B | AIME'24 | 55.83 | 63.54 | **+7.71** |
| Qwen3-32B | AIME'25 | 45.63 | 56.67 | **+11.04** |
| Qwen3-14B | AIME'24 | 45.21 | 50.42 | **+5.21** |
| Qwen3-14B | AIME'25 | 38.13 | 42.92 | **+4.79** |
| Qwen3-8B | AIME'24 | 33.33 | 34.58 | +1.25 |

> "Utilizing only 20% of the tokens while maintaining performance comparable to full-gradient updates on the Qwen3-8B base model and significantly surpassing full-gradient updates on the Qwen3-32B and Qwen3-14B base models." (Abstract)

This is not merely an efficiency gain—it is a **performance improvement** despite using only one-fifth of the training signal. Conversely, training exclusively on the 80% lowest-entropy tokens leads to **severe performance degradation** (**Figure 7**), confirming that low-entropy tokens contribute minimally or may even hinder reasoning optimization.

**Incremental vs. fundamental:** While the mathematical modification to the DAPO objective is technically incremental (adding an indicator function and adjusting normalization), the **implication is fundamental**: it demonstrates that standard full-token training is not just inefficient but potentially **suboptimal** because low-entropy tokens may introduce noise that interferes with learning at critical decision points.

---

### 4.4 Scaling Insight: Selective Optimization Benefits Increase with Model Size

**What makes this novel:** The paper reveals a **strong scaling trend** that is not obvious from the method itself. As shown in **Figure 8**, the performance gain from using only forking tokens increases with model size: the 32B model shows the largest improvement (+7.71 on AIME'24), followed by the 14B model (+5.21), and the smallest gain for the 8B model (+1.25).

**Why this matters:** This scaling behavior suggests that **larger models have greater capacity to benefit from enhanced exploration** at forking tokens. The paper hypothesizes that smaller models may have insufficient capacity to fully leverage the increased exploration that selective optimization enables (Section 1, last paragraph). This has important practical implications:

1. **The technique becomes more valuable as models scale**, making it particularly relevant for frontier models.
2. **Compute efficiency improves disproportionately** for larger models, since the same 80% gradient reduction yields larger performance gains.

**Ablation evidence:** **Figure 7** shows that deviating from the 20% threshold in either direction reduces performance. Reducing to 10% removes useful forking tokens and weakens exploration. Increasing to 50% or 100% adds low-entropy tokens that reduce exploration effectiveness. This suggests the 20% threshold represents an **optimal balance between exploration and training stability**, though the paper acknowledges this value may need adjustment in different RLVR settings (Limitations, Section 8).

---

### 4.5 Broader Implication: Token Entropy as a Lens for Understanding RLVR Generalization

**What makes this novel:** Beyond the immediate algorithmic contribution, the paper proposes **token entropy patterns as a theoretical framework** for understanding why RLVR generalizes better than SFT. This addresses a fundamental question in the field that prior work has not fully explained.

**Why this matters:** The paper's analysis suggests that:

- **SFT memorizes** because it pushes all tokens (including forking tokens) toward low-entropy, deterministic outputs.
- **RL generalizes** because it preserves high entropy at forking tokens, maintaining flexibility in reasoning paths.

This framework could inform future work beyond RLVR, including supervised fine-tuning strategies, distillation methods, and inference-time techniques. The paper explicitly identifies this as a direction for future research (Future Directions, Section 8).

**Caveat:** The paper acknowledges limitations in generalizability. Experiments on Llama-3.1-8B required cold-start SFT with Qwen3-32B generated data to achieve meaningful performance, and results were less convincing than on Qwen3 models (Section 5.4). Additionally, the 20% threshold may need adjustment for different domains beyond mathematics (Limitations, Section 8). These caveats are important for readers evaluating the broader applicability of these insights.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence supporting the paper's central claim: that restricting RLVR updates to high-entropy "forking tokens" improves reasoning performance. We walk through the evaluation setup, analyze the main quantitative results, examine ablation studies that validate the design choices, and assess the robustness of these findings across model sizes and domains.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

To rigorously test the hypothesis that high-entropy tokens drive RLVR performance, the authors design experiments that compare their **Selective Gradient Method** (updating only top-20% entropy tokens) against a **Vanilla DAPO Baseline** (updating all tokens).

#### 5.1.1 Models and Training Data
The experiments primarily focus on the **Qwen3 family** of models to ensure consistent architectural baselines. The specific base models evaluated are:
*   **Qwen3-32B**: The largest model tested, used to establish state-of-the-art (SoTA) performance.
*   **Qwen3-14B**: A mid-scale model used to verify scaling trends.
*   **Qwen3-8B**: A smaller model used to test the lower bound of the method's effectiveness.
*   **Llama-3.1-8B**: Tested in a separate experiment (Section 5.4) to check generalization beyond the Qwen family, though it required significant modification (cold-start SFT) to train effectively.

The training dataset is **DAPO-Math-17K** (Yu et al., 2025), a mathematics-focused dataset containing 17,000 queries. This choice isolates reasoning capabilities in a domain with verifiable rewards (exact match or equivalence checking).

#### 5.1.2 Benchmarks and Metrics
Performance is evaluated on six standard mathematical reasoning benchmarks in a **zero-shot setting**:
*   **AIME'24 & AIME'25**: High-difficulty competition math problems (American Invitational Mathematics Examination).
*   **AMC'23**: American Mathematics Competitions.
*   **MATH500**: A standard benchmark for grade school to competition math.
*   **Minerva**: STEM problems requiring multi-step reasoning.
*   **OlympiadBench**: High-difficulty olympiad-level problems.

The primary metric is **Acc@16**, defined as the average accuracy over 16 independent response generations per question at a decoding temperature of $T=1.0$. The secondary metric is **Len@16**, the average number of tokens per response, which serves as a proxy for reasoning depth and computational cost during inference.

#### 5.1.3 Baselines and Hyperparameters
The baseline is **Vanilla DAPO** (Dynamic sAmpling Policy Optimization), which applies policy gradient updates to every token in the generated sequence. The proposed method modifies DAPO by setting the retention ratio $\rho = 20\%$ in Equation 6 (Section 3.4), meaning only the top 20% of tokens with the highest entropy in each batch contribute to the loss.

Key hyperparameters are kept identical for fair comparison:
*   **Clip-Higher**: $\epsilon_{high} = 0.28$, $\epsilon_{low} = 0.2$.
*   **Max Response Length**: 20,480 tokens (unless specified otherwise).
*   **Batch Size**: 512 total, with mini-batch size 32.
*   **Learning Rate**: $10^{-6}$ (no warmup or scheduling).
*   **Regularization**: Both KL divergence loss and entropy loss are **excluded** to isolate the effect of token selection.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

The core evidence for the paper's claim lies in **Table 2** and **Figure 6**, which compare the Selective Gradient Method against Vanilla DAPO across all three Qwen3 model sizes.

#### 5.2.1 Performance Gains on Qwen3 Models
The results demonstrate that discarding 80% of the tokens (the low-entropy majority) does not degrade performance; instead, it significantly **improves** it for larger models.

*   **Qwen3-32B**: The selective method achieves **63.54** on AIME'24 and **56.67** on AIME'25. This represents a massive improvement over the vanilla baseline, which scored **55.83** (+7.71) and **45.63** (+11.04), respectively.
*   **Qwen3-14B**: Similar gains are observed, with AIME'24 improving from **45.21** to **50.42** (+5.21) and AIME'25 from **38.13** to **42.92** (+4.79).
*   **Qwen3-8B**: The gains are marginal but positive. AIME'24 improves from **33.33** to **34.58** (+1.25). On AMC'23, there is a slight decrease (-0.625), suggesting the method's benefit may be capacity-dependent.

> "Utilizing only 20% of the tokens while maintaining performance comparable to full-gradient updates on the Qwen3-8B base model and significantly surpassing full-gradient updates on the Qwen3-32B and Qwen3-14B base models." (Abstract)

**Why this is surprising:** Standard intuition suggests that using less data (fewer gradient updates) should hurt performance. Here, removing "noisy" low-entropy tokens acts as a regularizer, allowing the model to focus optimization signal on the tokens that actually matter for reasoning direction.

#### 5.2.2 Response Length and Reasoning Depth
A critical side effect of the method is an increase in response length, as shown in **Table 2** and **Figure 6(b, d, f)**.
*   For **Qwen3-32B**, the average response length on AIME'24 increases from **9,644** to **12,197** tokens (+2,553).
*   For **Qwen3-14B**, the increase is even more pronounced proportionally, jumping from **7,945** to **11,814** tokens on AIME'24.

**Interpretation:** Longer responses indicate that the model is engaging in more extended Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning rather than collapsing to short, heuristic answers. This aligns with the paper's hypothesis that preserving high entropy at forking tokens encourages **exploration** of deeper reasoning paths.

#### 5.2.3 Training Stability
**Figure 6(a, c, e)** displays the training curves for AIME'24 scores. The Selective Gradient Method (top 20%) shows **more stable convergence** compared to Vanilla DAPO, particularly for the 32B model. The vanilla curve exhibits more variance, while the selective method climbs steadily. This suggests that low-entropy tokens may introduce gradient noise that destabilizes training in large models.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies: Validating the 20% Threshold

To ensure the 20% threshold is not arbitrary, the authors conduct ablation studies varying the proportion of tokens used for gradient updates ($\rho$). The results are presented in **Figure 7**.

#### 5.3.1 The "Sweet Spot" at 20%
**Figure 7(a, c, e)** compares performance across $\rho \in \{10\%, 20\%, 50\%, 100\%\}$ and a "Bottom 80%" condition (using only low-entropy tokens).
*   **Top 20%**: Consistently yields the highest or near-highest scores across all model sizes.
*   **Top 10%**: Performance drops slightly compared to 20%. This indicates that cutting too deep removes some useful forking tokens, weakening exploration.
*   **Top 50% & 100% (Vanilla)**: Performance declines as more low-entropy tokens are included. For the 32B model, the drop from 20% to 100% is significant (e.g., ~7 points on AIME'24).
*   **Bottom 80%**: This condition leads to **severe performance degradation**. Despite using 80% of the total tokens, the model fails to learn effective reasoning.

**Key Takeaway:** This ablation confirms that **token quality (entropy) matters more than quantity**. The bottom 80% of tokens are not just neutral; in large models, they appear to be actively detrimental to the optimization process, likely by diluting the gradient signal from critical decision points.

#### 5.3.2 Entropy Evolution
**Figure 7(b, d, f)** tracks the **overall entropy** of the model during training.
*   Models trained with **Top 20%** maintain higher overall entropy throughout training.
*   Models trained with **Bottom 80%** or **100%** show lower overall entropy, converging faster to deterministic outputs.

This supports the mechanistic claim in **Section 4.2**: RLVR works by preserving exploration capability. By masking low-entropy tokens, we prevent the model from over-optimizing on "filler" tokens, thereby keeping the forking tokens flexible.

### 5.4 Scaling and Generalization Checks

The paper goes beyond standard benchmarks to test whether these findings hold under different conditions, such as out-of-distribution (OOD) data, extended context, and different model families.

#### 5.4.1 Scaling Trend (Figure 8)
**Figure 8** plots the performance gain (Selective vs. Vanilla) against model size. There is a clear **positive correlation**:
*   **8B Model**: Minimal gain (+1.25 on AIME'24).
*   **14B Model**: Moderate gain (+5.21 on AIME'24).
*   **32B Model**: Largest gain (+7.71 on AIME'24).

**Implication:** The method scales favorably. Larger models have the capacity to utilize the enhanced exploration provided by selective gradients, whereas smaller models may be capacity-constrained. This suggests the technique is particularly valuable for frontier-scale models.

#### 5.4.2 Out-of-Distribution Generalization (Figure 9)
To test if the method overfits to math, the authors evaluate on **LiveCodeBench** (a coding benchmark) after training solely on math data (DAPO-Math-17K).
*   **Result**: The Selective Gradient Method (Top 10% and Top 20%) **significantly outperforms** Vanilla DAPO (100%) on this OOD task (**Figure 9**).
*   **Significance**: This suggests that focusing on forking tokens improves **general reasoning capabilities** that transfer across domains, rather than just memorizing math patterns.

#### 5.4.3 Extended Context Length (Figure 10)
The authors test if the method benefits from longer reasoning traces. They extend the maximum response length for the Qwen3-32B model from 20,480 to **29,696 tokens**.
*   **Result**: The AIME'24 score improves from **63.54** to **68.12**.
*   **Significance**: This sets a new SoTA for models under 600B parameters trained directly from base models. It implies that the selective gradient method effectively utilizes the additional context window, whereas vanilla training might struggle to optimize such long sequences efficiently.

#### 5.4.4 Cross-Architecture Robustness (Figure 11)
A critical stress test involves applying the method to **Llama-3.1-8B**.
*   **Challenge**: Direct RLVR on Llama-3.1-8B failed (accuracy ~1%, repetitive outputs). The authors had to use a **cold-start SFT** phase using data generated by Qwen3-32B.
*   **Result**: Even after cold-start, the Selective Gradient Method outperforms Vanilla DAPO on AIME'24 (**Figure 11**).
*   **Caveat**: The absolute performance is low compared to Qwen3 models. The authors note these results are "less convincing" (Section 5.4), highlighting that the method's success depends on a base model that already possesses some reasoning capability.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

The experiments provide strong, multi-faceted support for the paper's claims, though with specific boundary conditions.

#### 5.5.1 Strengths of the Evidence
1.  **Causal Validation**: The ablation studies (Figure 7) rule out the possibility that the gains are due to random hyperparameter tuning. The clear degradation when using "Bottom 80%" tokens proves that **entropy is the distinguishing factor**, not just token count.
2.  **Scaling Consistency**: The monotonic improvement with model size (Figure 8) strengthens the argument that this is a fundamental property of RLVR in large models, not a fluke of a specific checkpoint.
3.  **Mechanistic Alignment**: The entropy tracking (Figure 5, Figure 7) aligns perfectly with the performance metrics. Higher maintained entropy correlates with higher accuracy, validating the theoretical link between exploration and reasoning.

#### 5.5.2 Limitations and Trade-offs
1.  **Inference Cost**: While *training* is more efficient (fewer gradient updates), *inference* becomes more expensive. The response lengths increase significantly (Table 2), meaning generating answers takes more compute and time. This is a trade-off: **training efficiency vs. inference cost**.
2.  **Base Model Dependency**: The failure of direct RLVR on Llama-3.1-8B indicates the method requires a base model with inherent reasoning structure. It is an **optimizer for reasoning models**, not a creator of reasoning from scratch.
3.  **Threshold Sensitivity**: While 20% works well here, the "Limitations" section (Section 8) acknowledges this threshold may need adjustment for different domains (e.g., coding vs. math) or different RLVR algorithms. It is not necessarily a universal constant.

#### 5.5.3 Conclusion on Experimental Validity
The experiments convincingly demonstrate that **high-entropy minority tokens drive RLVR performance**. The consistency across model sizes, the robustness in OOD settings, and the clear ablation results leave little doubt that the standard practice of updating all tokens is suboptimal for reasoning tasks. The primary remaining question is not *if* the method works, but *how best to tune it* for non-math domains and how to manage the increased inference length in practical deployments.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper presents compelling evidence that high-entropy minority tokens drive RLVR performance, the approach relies on specific assumptions and exhibits trade-offs that constrain its applicability. Below, we critically examine the methodological limitations, unaddressed scenarios, computational constraints, and open questions that remain.

---

### 6.1 Core Assumptions Underlying the Approach

The selective gradient method depends on several foundational assumptions that may not hold universally across all RLVR settings.

#### 6.1.1 Entropy as a Proxy for Token Importance

The entire approach rests on the assumption that **token entropy accurately reflects functional importance** in reasoning trajectories. The paper argues that high-entropy tokens correspond to "forking" decision points while low-entropy tokens correspond to "following" execution steps (Section 3). However, this mapping is empirically observed rather than theoretically proven.

**Evidence from the paper:**
> "Tokens with the highest entropy typically serve to bridge the logical connection between two consecutive parts of reasoning, while tokens with the lowest entropy tend to complete the current part of a sentence." (Section 3, Entropy Pattern 2)

**Potential weakness:** Entropy measures uncertainty in the probability distribution, not semantic importance. A token could have high entropy due to genuine ambiguity in the training data rather than representing a critical reasoning fork. Conversely, some critical decision points might have low entropy if the model has already learned a deterministic correct path. The paper does not provide a formal proof that entropy correlates with causal importance for reasoning performance.

#### 6.1.2 Stability of Entropy Patterns Across Training

The method assumes that **entropy patterns remain relatively stable** during RLVR training, justifying the use of batch-level entropy thresholds. **Table 1** shows that the overlap between base model and trained model high-entropy positions remains above 86% at convergence.

**Evidence from the paper:**
> "Although overlap with the base model gradually decreases and overlap with the final RLVR model increases, the base model's overlap still remains above 86% at convergence (step 1360)." (Section 4, Table 1 analysis)

**Potential weakness:** This stability was observed only for Qwen3-14B on mathematical benchmarks. If entropy patterns shift more dramatically in other domains (e.g., coding, multi-modal reasoning) or with different base models, the batch-level threshold $\tau^B_\rho$ may become misaligned with actual forking tokens during training, degrading performance.

#### 6.1.3 Optimal Threshold at Approximately 20%

The paper identifies $\rho = 20\%$ as the optimal retention ratio for high-entropy tokens. **Figure 7** shows performance degradation when deviating to 10%, 50%, or 100%.

**Evidence from the paper:**
> "Retaining approximately 20% of the highest-entropy tokens optimally balances exploration and performance, while deviating from 20% reduces the overall entropy with diminished exploration and incurs worse performance." (Section 1, last paragraph)

**Potential weakness:** The paper explicitly acknowledges in the Limitations section that this threshold **may not be universal**:
> "In a different RLVR setting, the effective proportion of 20% observed in our experiments may need to be adjusted to a different value to achieve optimal results." (Section 8, Limitations)

The 20% threshold was determined empirically on mathematical reasoning tasks with Qwen3 models. Different domains (e.g., code generation, scientific reasoning) may have different entropy distributions, requiring re-tuning of $\rho$. This reduces the method's plug-and-play applicability.

---

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

Several important scenarios remain unexplored or inadequately addressed in the paper.

#### 6.2.1 Non-Mathematical Domains

All primary experiments use **DAPO-Math-17K**, a mathematics-focused dataset. While the paper tests generalization to LiveCodeBench (Figure 9), this is an out-of-distribution evaluation after math training, not direct training on code.

**Evidence from the paper:**
> "The scope of our dataset could be expanded to encompass domains beyond mathematics, such as programming or more complex tasks like ARC-AGI." (Section 8, Limitations)

**Implication:** The entropy patterns observed in mathematical CoT may not generalize to other reasoning domains. For example:
- **Code generation** may have different entropy distributions due to syntax constraints.
- **Multi-modal reasoning** (vision-language) may exhibit entirely different token-level uncertainty patterns.
- **Open-ended tasks** without verifiable rewards may not benefit from the same selective optimization strategy.

The paper does not provide evidence that the 80/20 entropy split holds outside mathematical reasoning.

#### 6.2.2 Models Without Inherent Reasoning Capability

The method requires a base model that already possesses some reasoning structure. The **Llama-3.1-8B experiment** reveals a critical boundary condition.

**Evidence from the paper:**
> "When DAPO is applied to the Llama-3.1-8B base model, we observe that it achieves very low accuracy (approximately 1%) on the training dataset and often generates responses with repetitive words early in the RL training process." (Section 5.4)

To make Llama-3.1-8B trainable, the authors had to:
1. Use Qwen3-32B as a teacher to generate responses.
2. Perform **cold-start SFT** on 10,000 correct samples.
3. Reserve only 7,398 queries for RL after cold-start.

**Implication:** The selective gradient method is an **optimizer for reasoning-capable models**, not a creator of reasoning from scratch. Models without inherent CoT structure may not exhibit the entropy patterns required for the method to function. This limits applicability to base models that have already been pretrained with reasoning capabilities.

#### 6.2.3 Very Small or Very Large Model Scales

The experiments cover 8B, 14B, and 32B models. The paper does not address:
- **Models below 8B:** May lack capacity to benefit from enhanced exploration (as suggested by the smaller gains on 8B).
- **Models above 32B:** The scaling trend in **Figure 8** suggests increasing benefits, but no empirical data exists for 70B+ models.

**Evidence from the paper:**
> "This may be due to the insufficient capacity of the smaller model, which restricts its ability to benefit from increased exploration." (Section 1, last paragraph)

**Implication:** The method's effectiveness may be **capacity-dependent**. Very small models may not have enough representational capacity to leverage selective gradients, while very large models may exhibit different entropy dynamics that require threshold adjustment.

#### 6.2.4 Alternative RLVR Algorithms

The paper uses **DAPO** as the sole baseline algorithm. While DAPO is state-of-the-art, the method's compatibility with other RLVR algorithms remains untested.

**Evidence from the paper:**
> "In this work, we use DAPO as the baseline for our RLVR experiments." (Section 2.2)

**Implication:** The selective gradient formulation (Equation 6) is derived from DAPO's objective. Algorithms with different structures (e.g., PPO with value networks, offline preference optimization like DPO) may require different masking strategies. The paper does not address whether entropy-based selection works with:
- Value-network-based methods (PPO)
- Offline methods (DPO, SimPO)
- Methods with explicit entropy bonuses

---

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

The method introduces trade-offs between training efficiency and inference cost that must be carefully considered.

#### 6.3.1 Training Efficiency vs. Inference Cost

While the selective gradient method reduces **training compute** (fewer tokens contribute to gradients), it significantly increases **inference cost** due to longer response lengths.

**Evidence from Table 2:**
| Model | Method | AIME'24 Acc@16 | AIME'24 Len@16 |
|-------|--------|----------------|----------------|
| Qwen3-32B | Vanilla DAPO | 55.83 | 9,644 |
| Qwen3-32B | Forking Tokens | 63.54 | 12,197 |
| Qwen3-14B | Vanilla DAPO | 45.21 | 7,945 |
| Qwen3-14B | Forking Tokens | 50.42 | 11,814 |

**Trade-off analysis:**
- **Training:** ~80% reduction in gradient computations per batch (only 20% of tokens used).
- **Inference:** ~26-49% increase in response length, directly increasing inference time and cost.

**Implication:** The method is beneficial for **training-limited scenarios** but may be detrimental for **inference-limited deployments**. Organizations with abundant training compute but strict inference latency requirements may not benefit from this approach.

#### 6.3.2 Entropy Computation Overhead

The method requires computing token entropy for every token in every batch to determine the threshold $\tau^B_\rho$. This adds computational overhead during training.

**Technical detail:** Entropy computation requires:
1. Access to the full probability distribution $p_t \in \mathbb{R}^V$ at each token position.
2. Computing $H_t = -\sum_{j=1}^{V} p_{t,j} \log p_{t,j}$ for each token.
3. Sorting tokens within each batch to identify the top-$\rho$ percentile.

**Implication:** While gradient computation is reduced, the **entropy calculation and sorting overhead** partially offsets this gain. The paper does not quantify this overhead, making it unclear whether the net training efficiency improvement matches the theoretical 80% reduction.

#### 6.3.3 Memory Requirements

Storing probability distributions for entropy computation may increase memory usage during training, particularly for large vocabulary sizes ($V \approx 150,000$ for Qwen3).

**Implication:** For models already memory-constrained during RLVR training, the additional memory required for entropy computation and threshold determination may limit batch sizes or require gradient checkpointing, potentially negating efficiency gains.

---

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

Several weaknesses and unresolved questions remain that warrant future investigation.

#### 6.4.1 Lack of Theoretical Foundation

The paper provides strong empirical evidence but lacks a **theoretical explanation** for why 20% is optimal or why entropy correlates with reasoning importance.

**Open questions:**
- Is there a theoretical bound on the optimal $\rho$ given model capacity and task complexity?
- Can entropy be formally linked to exploration efficiency in the RL objective?
- Why does the bottom 80% of tokens actively hinder performance in large models (Figure 7) rather than simply being neutral?

**Evidence from the paper:**
> "One possible explanation is that, prior to performance convergence, a subset (∼20% in our experiments) of high-entropy tokens facilitates exploration, while low-entropy tokens offer minimal benefit or may even impede it." (Section 1, Key Takeaways)

The phrase "one possible explanation" indicates this remains a hypothesis rather than a proven mechanism.

#### 6.4.2 Threshold Determination Strategy

The paper uses a **batch-level dynamic threshold** $\tau^B_\rho$ but does not explore alternative thresholding strategies.

**Unexplored alternatives:**
- **Global threshold:** Fixed entropy threshold across all batches (may be more stable).
- **Layer-specific thresholds:** Different $\rho$ values for different transformer layers.
- **Adaptive thresholds:** Dynamically adjusting $\rho$ during training based on convergence signals.

**Implication:** The current method requires manual tuning of $\rho$ for each new domain or model. An adaptive strategy could improve robustness and reduce hyperparameter sensitivity.

#### 6.4.3 Interaction with Other RLVR Techniques

The paper excludes KL divergence and entropy loss to isolate the token selection effect. However, in practice, these regularizers are often used.

**Open questions:**
- How does selective gradient masking interact with **KL penalties**?
- Does the method complement or conflict with **entropy bonuses**?
- Can the method be combined with **curriculum learning** or **difficulty-based sampling**?

**Evidence from the paper:**
> "In RLVR, entropy bonus may be suboptimal, as it increases the entropy of low-entropy majority tokens. In contrast, clip-higher effectively promotes entropy in high-entropy minority tokens." (Section 6, Discussion 3)

This suggests potential conflict with standard entropy regularization, but the paper does not experimentally validate combinations of techniques.

#### 6.4.4 Long-Term Training Dynamics

All experiments appear to run for a fixed number of training steps (e.g., step 1360 in Table 1). The paper does not address:
- **Convergence behavior:** Does selective training converge faster or slower than vanilla training?
- **Overfitting risk:** Does focusing on 20% of tokens increase susceptibility to overfitting on the training distribution?
- **Catastrophic forgetting:** Does masking low-entropy tokens affect the model's ability to retain previously learned capabilities?

**Implication:** Without understanding long-term dynamics, it is unclear whether the method remains beneficial throughout extended training or only during early exploration phases.

#### 6.4.5 Reproducibility Across Implementations

The paper uses a modified version of the **verl** codebase with specific configurations. It is unclear whether the results generalize to other RLVR implementations.

**Evidence from the paper:**
> "We adapt our training codebase from verl (Sheng et al., 2024) and follow the training recipe of DAPO." (Section 5.2)

**Implication:** Implementation-specific details (e.g., gradient accumulation, mixed precision, communication patterns in distributed training) may affect the entropy computation and threshold determination. Independent replication is needed to confirm robustness across frameworks.

### 6.5 Summary of Limitations

| Category | Limitation | Severity | Mitigation |
|----------|------------|----------|------------|
| **Assumptions** | Entropy ≈ Importance | Medium | Theoretical analysis needed |
| **Assumptions** | 20% threshold is universal | Medium | Domain-specific tuning required |
| **Domains** | Only math tested primarily | High | Extend to code, science, multi-modal |
| **Models** | Requires reasoning-capable base | High | Cold-start SFT needed for weaker models |
| **Compute** | Increased inference cost | Medium | Trade-off acceptable for training-limited scenarios |
| **Compute** | Entropy computation overhead | Low | Not quantified in paper |
| **Theory** | No formal explanation for 20% | Medium | Open research direction |
| **Integration** | Untested with KL/entropy bonuses | Medium | Requires ablation studies |

**Bottom line:** The selective gradient method is a **powerful but context-dependent optimization**. It excels for training large, reasoning-capable models on mathematical tasks where training compute is the bottleneck. However, practitioners must carefully evaluate the inference cost trade-off, tune the threshold for their domain, and ensure their base model exhibits the required entropy patterns before deployment.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work fundamentally alters how we conceptualize optimization in Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR). By demonstrating that **only 20% of tokens drive performance**, the paper shifts the field from a paradigm of uniform sequence optimization to **selective, entropy-aware training**. Below, we outline how this changes the research landscape, what specific follow-up work it enables, and how practitioners should decide whether to adopt this method.

### 7.1 Transforming the RLVR Training Paradigm

The most immediate implication is the obsolescence of the assumption that **all tokens contribute equally to reasoning learning**. Prior RLVR implementations (e.g., standard PPO, GRPO, vanilla DAPO) compute policy gradients over every token in a generated trajectory. This paper proves that such uniformity is not just inefficient but potentially **suboptimal** for large models.

*   **Efficiency Redefinition:** Traditionally, efficiency in RLVR was measured by sample efficiency (how many queries needed) or wall-clock time. This work introduces **token-level gradient efficiency**. By masking the bottom 80% of low-entropy tokens, we reduce the number of gradient updates per batch by approximately 80% without sacrificing performance (Section 5.3).
*   **Theoretical Shift:** The findings suggest that RLVR's success is not due to reinforcing correct reasoning steps uniformly, but specifically due to **preserving exploration at decision points**. As discussed in **Section 6, Discussion 1**, this explains why RL generalizes better than Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT): SFT collapses entropy at forking tokens (memorization), while RLVR maintains it (generalization).
*   **Algorithmic Baseline Update:** Future RLVR papers should now treat **full-token gradient updates** as a baseline to be beaten, rather than the standard. The **Selective Policy Gradient** formulation (Equation 6) should be considered a core component of state-of-the-art reasoning training stacks.

### 7.2 Catalyst for Follow-Up Research

This work opens several concrete avenues for future investigation, ranging from algorithmic design to cross-domain applications.

#### 7.2.1 Entropy-Aware Algorithm Design
The current method modifies DAPO by adding a mask (Equation 6). Future work could design algorithms **natively built around entropy patterns**.
*   **Dynamic Thresholding:** Instead of a fixed $\rho = 20\%$, research could explore **adaptive thresholds** that change during training. For instance, early training might require more exploration (higher $\rho$), while later stages might focus on refinement.
*   **Layer-Wise Entropy:** The paper analyzes entropy at the output layer. Future work could investigate if specific transformer layers exhibit distinct entropy patterns, allowing for **layer-specific gradient masking**.
*   **Alternative Selection Criteria:** While entropy is the proxy used here, future research might identify **semantic proxies** for forking tokens (e.g., specific logical connectors) that are cheaper to compute than full vocabulary entropy.

#### 7.2.2 Beyond Mathematical Reasoning
The primary experiments rely on **DAPO-Math-17K** and math benchmarks (AIME, MATH500). The paper explicitly identifies expanding domains as a key future direction (Section 8, Limitations).
*   **Code Generation:** Coding tasks have strict syntax constraints that might alter entropy distributions. The preliminary LiveCodeBench result (**Figure 9**) suggests transferability, but direct training on code datasets is needed to verify if the 80/20 rule holds when syntax dictates low entropy for many tokens.
*   **Multi-Modal Reasoning:** In vision-language models, "tokens" include visual patches or embedded features. It is an open question whether **visual tokens** exhibit similar forking behavior or if the entropy pattern is unique to text-based Chain-of-Thought.
*   **Open-Ended Tasks:** RLVR relies on verifiable rewards (correct/incorrect). Applying this to tasks without clear rewards (e.g., creative writing) would require new reward models, but the **entropy masking principle** might still apply to improve coherence.

#### 7.2.3 Impact on SFT and Distillation
The paper hypothesizes that entropy preservation is key to generalization (**Section 6, Discussion 1**). This suggests applications beyond RLVR:
*   **Entropy-Regularized SFT:** Standard SFT minimizes negative log-likelihood, pushing all tokens toward low entropy. A modified SFT loss that **preserves high entropy at forking tokens** could yield models that generalize better without requiring RL.
*   **Distillation:** When distilling a large reasoning model into a smaller one, we might prioritize distilling the **high-entropy tokens** to ensure the student model learns the decision logic rather than just mimicking the surface form.

#### 7.2.4 Inference-Time Strategies
If high-entropy tokens are critical decision points, this knowledge can optimize inference:
*   **Compute Allocation:** During test-time scaling (e.g., Best-of-N or Tree-of-Thoughts), we could allocate more compute resources to branches diverging at **high-entropy positions**, as these are the points that matter most.
*   **Early Stopping:** If a model's entropy at a forking token drops below a threshold prematurely, it might indicate collapse into a heuristic path, signaling the need for intervention or regeneration.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Deployment

For practitioners deciding whether to implement this method, the decision hinges on the trade-off between **training efficiency** and **inference cost**.

#### 7.3.1 When to Use This Method
*   **Training Compute is the Bottleneck:** If you are limited by GPU hours for training, this method offers a theoretical **~80% reduction in gradient computation** per batch. This allows for larger batch sizes or more training steps within the same budget.
*   **Large Model Scale:** The scaling trend in **Figure 8** shows that benefits increase with model size. For models **≥14B parameters**, the performance gains are substantial (+5 to +11 points on AIME). For smaller models (e.g., 8B), gains are marginal (+1 point), suggesting the method is best suited for mid-to-large scale reasoning models.
*   **Base Model Has Reasoning Capability:** The failure on **Llama-3.1-8B** without cold-start SFT (**Section 5.4**) indicates this method optimizes existing reasoning structures. It is not a tool to instill reasoning in models that lack it entirely.

#### 7.3.2 When to Avoid or Caution
*   **Inference Latency is Critical:** The method produces longer responses. **Table 2** shows average response length on AIME'24 for Qwen3-32B increased from **9,644** to **12,197 tokens** (+26%). If your application has strict latency constraints or token budget limits, this trade-off may be unacceptable.
*   **Memory Constrained Training:** Calculating entropy requires accessing the full probability distribution $p_t \in \mathbb{R}^V$ at each step. For models with large vocabularies (e.g., $V \approx 150,000$), this adds memory overhead that might limit batch size on smaller GPU clusters.
*   **Non-Math Domains:** Until verified on code or science tasks, deploying this in production for non-math reasoning carries risk. The **20% threshold** may need tuning (e.g., code might have more low-entropy syntax tokens, shifting the balance).

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For researchers aiming to reproduce or integrate these findings, the following checklist outlines the critical components based on the paper's methodology.

#### 7.4.1 Core Implementation Requirements
*   **Baseline Algorithm:** Start with **DAPO** (Dynamic sAmpling Policy Optimization) as described in **Section 2.2**. The method relies on DAPO's token-level loss structure.
*   **Entropy Calculation:** Implement on-the-fly entropy computation for each token position $t$:
    ```math
    H_t := -\sum_{j=1}^{V} p_{t,j} \log p_{t,j}
    ```
    Ensure this uses the **current policy's probability distribution**, not the sampled token ID.
*   **Gradient Masking:** Modify the loss function to include the indicator function $\mathbb{I}\left[H_t^i \geq \tau^B_\rho\right]$ as shown in **Equation 6**.
    *   **Threshold $\tau^B_\rho$:** Compute this dynamically per batch to select the top $\rho$ fraction of tokens.
    *   **Normalization:** Adjust the loss denominator to count only the selected tokens, ensuring gradient magnitude remains stable.

#### 7.4.2 Recommended Hyperparameters
Based on the successful configurations in **Section 5.2**:
*   **Retention Ratio:** Start with `$\rho = 20\%$`. Be prepared to ablate this (e.g., try 10%, 30%) if performance plateaus.
*   **Clip-Higher:** Use `$\epsilon_{high} = 0.28$` and `$\epsilon_{low} = 0.2$`. The paper notes that `clip-higher` complements entropy masking by promoting entropy in high-importance tokens (**Section 6, Discussion 3**).
*   **Regularization:** **Exclude KL divergence and entropy loss**. The paper found these unnecessary and potentially counterproductive when using selective gradients.
*   **Learning Rate:** `$10^{-6}$` with no warmup was effective for Qwen3 models.

#### 7.4.3 Monitoring and Debugging
*   **Track Entropy Percentiles:** Plot the evolution of entropy percentiles during training (similar to **Figure 5**). If the top 20% entropy drops too quickly, the model may be collapsing into memorization.
*   **Check Overlap:** Periodically measure the overlap of high-entropy positions with the base model (similar to **Table 1**). A sudden drop might indicate the model is learning unnatural reasoning patterns.
*   **Response Length:** Monitor average response length closely. If it grows uncontrollably without accuracy gains, the model might be "over-thinking" due to excessive exploration pressure.

### 7.5 Summary of Strategic Impact

This paper provides a **mechanistic lens** for understanding RLVR that extends beyond simple performance tuning. By identifying **high-entropy minority tokens** as the primary drivers of reasoning improvement, it offers a path to more efficient training and better generalization. However, it comes with the cost of increased inference length and dependency on capable base models.

For the field, the key takeaway is that **not all tokens are created equal**. Future advancements in LLM reasoning will likely come from **strategic allocation of optimization signal** rather than brute-force scaling of training data. Researchers should prioritize understanding **where** learning happens in a sequence, not just **how much** data is processed.
