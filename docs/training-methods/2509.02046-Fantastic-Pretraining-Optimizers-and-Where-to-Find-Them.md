## 1. Executive Summary
This study addresses the discrepancy between claimed 1.4×–2× speedups of novel pretraining optimizers and their limited adoption by benchmarking eleven optimizers across four model scales (0.1B–1.2B parameters) and data-to-model ratios (1–8× Chinchilla optimum) on the C4-EN dataset with rigorous hyperparameter tuning. The authors demonstrate that proper baseline tuning alone can yield up to 2× improvements, reducing the actual advantage of matrix-based optimizers like Muon and Soap to merely 1.1× for 1.2B parameter models (Figure 1, Figure 4), indicating that many reported efficiency gains stem from methodological shortcomings rather than algorithmic superiority. These findings establish that fair comparisons require end-of-training evaluations across scaling regimes, as early-stage loss curves often mislead rankings and speedup benefits diminish significantly as model size increases.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Stakes of Pretraining Efficiency

Large language model pretraining represents the dominant computational expense in modern AI development. The paper notes that pretraining accounts for **over 95% of the total cost** in systems like DeepSeek V3, with subsequent reinforcement learning phases contributing comparatively little. At this scale, even marginal improvements in training efficiency translate to enormous resource savings. A claimed 2× speedup in optimizer performance could theoretically halve the computational budget required for training frontier models — representing millions of dollars in reduced compute costs and significantly faster iteration cycles for research teams.

This economic reality explains why the optimizer research community has produced a steady stream of new methods promising substantial gains over the established baseline. However, the paper identifies a critical disconnect: despite numerous claims of 1.4× to 2× speedups, these novel optimizers have **not been widely adopted** in production pretraining pipelines. The notable exception is Kimi K2, which uses a variant of the Muon optimizer. This adoption gap raises a fundamental question: are these methods genuinely superior but underutilized, or do the claimed benefits not hold up under rigorous scrutiny?

### The Methodological Gap in Optimizer Evaluation

The authors pinpoint two specific methodological shortcomings that have obscured fair comparisons and hindered practical adoption of new optimizers:

**First, unequal hyperparameter tuning.** Many prior studies compare proposed optimizers against AdamW baselines that use suboptimal or transferred hyperparameters. The paper demonstrates this dramatically in Figure 1 (top left): simply tuning the learning rate in the standard GPT-3 recipe for a 130M model can yield up to a **2× speedup** over the commonly used baseline configuration. When prior works fix hyperparameters like learning rate and weight decay across all optimizers (e.g., Liu et al. [2025a]), they inadvertently create an unfair comparison — optimal settings for one optimizer may be severely suboptimal for another. For instance, Figure 1 (top right) shows that Lion's optimal weight decay is approximately 0.6, while AdamW's is approximately 0.1. Using a shared value disadvantages one or both methods.

**Second, limited and misleading evaluation setups.** Previous optimizer studies typically evaluate at a single model scale and data-to-model ratio, leaving unanswered questions about how methods perform across realistic training regimes. The Chinchilla optimal data-to-model ratio (approximately 20 tokens per parameter based on Hoffmann et al. [2022]) varies significantly in practice — models may be trained at 1× to over 50× this ratio depending on compute budgets and objectives. Additionally, many studies judge optimizer performance using **intermediate checkpoints** before training completes. The paper shows in Figure 5 that loss curves can cross multiple times during learning rate decay, meaning early-stage rankings may completely reverse by the end of training.

### The Landscape of Prior Optimizer Research

The optimizer development landscape spans several decades of research, which the paper organizes into distinct lineages:

| Optimizer Class | Representative Methods | Key Mechanism |
|-----------------|----------------------|---------------|
| Adaptive Learning Rate | Adam, AdamW, RMSProp | Per-parameter learning rates based on gradient moments |
| Variance-Reduced Adam Variants | NAdamW, Mars, Cautious | Nesterov lookahead, gradient variance reduction |
| Memory-Efficient | Lion, Adam-mini | Reduced auxiliary state (e.g., first-order momentum only) |
| Matrix-Based Preconditioners | Muon, Soap, Kron, Scion | Matrix multiplication rather than scalar operations on gradients |
| Hessian-Approximation | Sophia | Second-order curvature information via Hessian-vector products |

AdamW (Adam with decoupled weight decay) emerged as the default baseline following Kingma and Ba [2017] and Loshchilov and Hutter [2019]. Subsequent improvements targeted various limitations: convergence guarantees on convex losses, interpolation between Adam and SGD for better generalization, memory reduction, and support for larger batch sizes.

More recently, a wave of papers has claimed substantial speedups specifically for LLM pretraining:
- **Sophia** (Liu et al. [2024a]): 2× speedup via stochastic second-order optimization
- **Soap** (Vyas et al. [2025]): 1.4× speedup using Shampoo-style preconditioning with Adam
- **Muon** (Jordan et al. [2024], Liu et al. [2025a]): 2× speedup via Newton-Schulz orthogonalization
- **MARS** (Yuan et al. [2025]): 2× speedup through variance reduction
- **Cautious AdamW** (Liang et al. [2025]): 2× speedup with minimal code changes

The paper notes that Sophia is among the few works that conducted independent hyperparameter sweeping for their baseline, and indeed reports speedups closest to what this study observes. This observation reinforces the central thesis: tuning methodology fundamentally shapes reported results.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Evaluation Work

This paper situates itself within a tradition of rigorous re-evaluation in deep learning. Schmidt et al. [2021] previously benchmarked optimizers and concluded that (i) optimal optimizer choice is problem-specific, and (ii) unequal hyperparameter tuning accounts for most claimed speedups. However, that work predates the current generation of LLM-specific optimizers and did not focus on pretraining at scale.

More recent evaluation efforts include:
- **Zhao et al. [2025]**: Examines optimizer performance on pretraining but focuses on understanding loss structure; tested optimizers showed slower convergence than AdamW, unlike the modest improvements observed here.
- **Kaddour et al. [2023]**: Compared optimizers including Sophia on BERT and T5-scale models, observing that optimizer rankings can flip during learning rate decay and AdamW remains competitive when properly tuned.
- **Semenov et al. [2025]**: Concurrent work investigating the same topic. The authors note key differences: Semenov et al. use smaller batch sizes (0.02M–0.1M tokens vs. ≥0.4M tokens here), which advantages variance-reduced methods like Mars in noise-dominated regimes. Additionally, this paper conducts more extensive learning rate sweeps and separately tunes embedding layer learning rates for Muon, improving its performance.

The **Algoperf competition** (Kasimbeg et al. [2025]) also evaluates optimizers across settings and reaches similar high-level conclusions: matrix-based optimizers and denoising methods like Nesterov momentum provide speedups over AdamW. This paper differentiates itself by focusing specifically on the pretraining setting and systematically investigating how optimizer performance scales with both model size and data budget.

### Why This Evaluation Matters Now

The timing of this study is significant. The field has reached a point where pretraining costs dominate AI development budgets, and claims of efficiency improvements carry substantial weight in guiding resource allocation decisions. If claimed 2× speedups are artifacts of methodological shortcomings rather than genuine algorithmic advances, the community risks:

1. **Misallocated research effort** — pursuing optimization directions that don't deliver at scale
2. **Premature adoption** — switching production pipelines to methods that offer marginal or no benefit
3. **Confusion about scaling laws** — drawing incorrect conclusions about how optimizer performance changes with model size

By establishing a rigorous benchmarking protocol with coordinate-descent hyperparameter tuning, evaluation across multiple model scales (130M to 1.2B parameters), and assessment at multiple data-to-model ratios (1× to 8× Chinchilla, with additional 16× experiments), this paper provides a methodological foundation for future optimizer development. The findings — that speedups diminish with model scale and that matrix-based methods consistently outperform scalar-based methods only for smaller models — offer concrete guidance for both researchers designing new optimizers and practitioners selecting methods for production use.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

This paper builds a systematic benchmarking framework for evaluating deep learning optimizers during language model pretraining, treating optimizer comparison as a controlled scientific experiment rather than a single-run demonstration. The system solves the problem of unfair optimizer comparisons by implementing a three-phase hyperparameter tuning protocol that ensures each optimizer receives individually optimized settings before speedup claims are measured, revealing that many reported gains disappear when baselines are properly tuned.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The benchmarking system consists of four interconnected components that flow sequentially: First, the **Experimental Infrastructure** layer establishes the foundation with four Llama 2 model scales (130M, 300M, 520M, 1.2B parameters), three pretraining datasets (DCLM-baseline, StarCoder V2, ProofPile 2), and TPU v5 hardware with mixed-precision training. Second, the **Optimizer Catalog** contains eleven distinct optimization algorithms grouped into five classes (baseline AdamW, variance-reduced variants, memory-efficient methods, matrix-based preconditioners, and Hessian-approximation methods), each with algorithm-specific hyperparameter spaces. Third, the **Three-Phase Tuning Pipeline** performs iterative coordinate-descent sweeps: Phase I identifies near-optimal configurations across six model/data regimes, Phase II isolates scaling-sensitive hyperparameters that change with model size, and Phase III fits mathematical scaling laws to extrapolate optimal settings to 1.2B models. Fourth, the **Evaluation and Speedup Calculation** module measures final validation loss on C4-EN, fits a power-law function to AdamW's loss-versus-data relationship, and computes speedup ratios by determining how much data AdamW would need to match each optimizer's final loss.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the experimental foundation (datasets, model architectures, hardware) because understanding the training environment is prerequisite to interpreting optimizer behavior.
- We then detail the eleven optimizers under study, explaining their update rules and grouping rationale, since algorithmic differences drive the performance variations we measure.
- We proceed to the three-phase hyperparameter tuning methodology, which is the paper's core methodological contribution and the reason its findings differ from prior work.
- We explain the speedup calculation procedure, which transforms raw loss measurements into comparable efficiency metrics across different data budgets.
- We conclude with the evaluation metrics and benchmark suite, establishing how the paper validates that loss improvements translate to downstream task performance.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is an empirical benchmarking paper whose core idea is that fair optimizer comparison requires exhaustive, optimizer-specific hyperparameter tuning across multiple model scales and data-to-model ratios before any speedup claims can be trusted. The authors construct a controlled experimental pipeline where every optimizer undergoes identical tuning effort, eliminating the confounding variable of unequal optimization effort that plagues prior work.

**Experimental Foundation: Datasets, Models, and Hardware**

The training corpus combines three publicly available datasets tokenized with the Llama 3 tokenizer, creating a diverse pretraining mixture similar to OLMo 2's data composition. The DCLM-baseline dataset contributes 3.8 trillion tokens, StarCoder V2 Data adds 0.25 trillion tokens focused on code, and ProofPile 2 provides 55 billion tokens of mathematical proofs, ensuring the model encounters varied linguistic and symbolic patterns during training. The paper evaluates four model scales derived from the Llama 2 architecture, with approximately 130 million, 300 million, 520 million, and 1.2 billion parameters respectively, as detailed in Table 2 of the paper. Each model variant maintains a fixed sequence length of 4,096 tokens and 32 transformer layers, differing only in hidden dimension (ranging from 512 to 1536), intermediate dimension (2048 to 6144), and number of attention heads (8 to 24), which isolates model capacity as the primary variable while holding architectural depth constant. Training is implemented in JAX and executed on TPU v5 hardware using a mixed-precision scheme where parameters are stored in 32-bit floating point (fp32) for numerical stability while activations use 16-bit brain floating point (bf16) to reduce memory footprint and increase throughput. For each model size, the authors compute the Chinchilla optimal data-to-model ratio by multiplying the non-embedding parameter count by 20, following Hoffmann et al. [2022]'s finding that compute-optimal training uses approximately 20 tokens per parameter. The paper uses the notation "n× Chinchilla" to represent training for n times the Chinchilla-optimal number of tokens, enabling systematic comparison across undertrained (1×), optimally trained (1×), and overtrained (4×, 8×, 16×) regimes.

**Optimizer Catalog: Eleven Algorithms Across Five Classes**

The study evaluates eleven optimizers listed in Table 1, selected according to three guiding principles: include widely adopted baselines like AdamW and Lion, cover recently proposed methods with claimed speedups, and when multiple methods share similar update rules, choose representative algorithms to ensure both breadth and depth. The optimizers group into five classes based on their algorithmic mechanisms. The baseline class contains only AdamW, which uses first-order momentum $m_t$ and second-order momentum $v_t$ of gradients with the update rule $w_{t+1} = w_t - \eta \frac{\hat{m}_t}{\sqrt{\hat{v}_t} + \epsilon} - \eta \lambda w_t$, where $\eta$ is the learning rate, $\lambda$ is weight decay, and $\epsilon$ provides numerical stability. The variance-reduced Adam variants class includes NAdamW, Mars, and Cautious, all motivated by reducing the variance of gradient estimates to improve convergence stability. NAdamW incorporates Nesterov lookahead to estimate gradients more accurately, modifying the momentum calculation to $\tilde{m}_t = \beta_1 m_t + (1 - \beta_1) g_t$ before applying bias correction. Mars introduces a variance reduction term $\gamma$ that adjusts gradients based on the difference between consecutive gradient steps: $c_t = g_t + \gamma \frac{\beta_1}{1 - \beta_1}(g_t - g_{t-1})$. Cautious adds a sign-consistency check that only applies updates when the momentum direction agrees with the current gradient, computed as $s_t = \mathbb{I}(u_t \cdot \hat{g}_t > 0)$ where $\mathbb{I}$ is an indicator function. The memory-efficient class contains Lion and Adam-mini, which reduce auxiliary state requirements compared to AdamW. Lion keeps only first-order momentum and uses the sign of the momentum for updates: $w_{t+1} = w_t - \eta \cdot \text{sign}(\hat{m}_t) - \eta \lambda w_t$, eliminating the second-moment buffer entirely. Adam-mini partitions parameters into blocks and uses a single scalar second-moment value per block rather than per-parameter, reducing memory overhead while maintaining adaptive learning rate benefits. The matrix-based preconditioners class includes Muon, Scion, Kron, and Soap, which leverage the matrix structure of neural network weight tensors by preconditioning gradients through matrix multiplication rather than entry-wise scalar operations. Muon applies Newton-Schulz orthogonalization to the momentum matrix, computing $\text{NS}^{(5)}(M) \approx \arg\max_{\|O\|_{\text{op}}=1} \text{Tr}(O^\top M)$ when $\|M\|_{\text{op}} < 1$, where the superscript (5) indicates five iterations of the Newton-Schulz iteration. This operation is applied to all weight matrices except the token classification head and embeddings, which use standard AdamW updates. Soap combines Shampoo-style preconditioning with Adam, maintaining left and right preconditioner matrices $Q_A$ and $Q_B$ that are updated via QR decomposition every $k$ steps: $Q_A = \text{QR}(G_A Q_A)$ where $G_A$ accumulates gradient outer products. Kron uses Kronecker-factored preconditioners that approximate curvature information with lower computational cost than full matrix methods. The Hessian-approximation class contains Sophia, which uses Hessian-vector products to approximate the diagonalized Hessian matrix, computing curvature estimates periodically (every $k$ steps) via random probe vectors: $v = g_t \cdot r$ where $r \sim \{\pm 1\}^d$, then $u = \nabla_\theta v$ to estimate second-order information.

**Three-Phase Hyperparameter Tuning Pipeline**

The paper's methodological core is a three-phase hyperparameter tuning framework designed to ensure each optimizer receives fair, near-optimal settings before comparison, addressing the unequal tuning problem identified in prior work. Phase I performs fine-grained coordinate-descent sweeps across multiple model sizes and data-to-model ratios to identify scaling-sensitive parameters that require re-tuning as scale changes. For each optimizer, the authors define a discrete grid for every hyperparameter—for AdamW this includes learning rate, weight decay, warmup steps, $\beta_1$, $\beta_2$, $\epsilon$, gradient-norm clipping, and batch size. Starting from an initial configuration similar to the hyperparameters provided in the original paper proposing each optimizer, the procedure holds all but one hyperparameter fixed at current best values, searches the whole grid for that parameter, and accepts the new value if validation loss improves by more than $\Delta_1 = 3 \times 10^{-3}$. This process repeats in passes until no parameter update yields further significant gain, ensuring coordinate-wise local optimality. Phase I sweeping occurs across six different settings: 130M, 300M, and 520M models at 1× Chinchilla, plus 130M models at 2×, 4×, and 8× Chinchilla, providing coverage of both model scale and data budget dimensions. Table 3 illustrates an exemplary hyperparameter optimization procedure for AdamW on a 300M model at 1× Chinchilla, showing how warmup steps increased from 1000 to 2000, batch size decreased from 256 to 128, and maximum gradient norm increased from 1 to 2 across tuning rounds, ultimately reducing validation loss from 3.298 to 3.263. Phase II refines the tuning by identifying which hyperparameters are scaling-sensitive versus scaling-insensitive, reducing the search space for larger-scale experiments where exhaustive tuning becomes computationally prohibitive. The authors empirically observe two crucial properties: first, losses are sensitive to only a subset of hyperparameters while many have little effect on performance when perturbed from optimal values; second, among sensitive hyperparameters, optimal settings for most remain stable across scales, meaning tuning is needed only at smaller scales. To formalize this, the paper defines the approximate-optimal configuration for a given regime $r$ as the set of all hyperparameter tuples whose final loss lies within $\Delta_2 = 6.4 \times 10^{-3}$ of the regime's best-observed loss $L^*_r$. A hyperparameter $c_h$ is called scaling-insensitive if there exists a single value $v_h$ such that for every regime $r$ in Phase I, there exists a configuration $c$ in the approximate-optimal set $C_r$ where $c_h = v_h$. Otherwise, $c_h$ is scaling-sensitive, meaning its optimal value shifts depending on model size or data budget. Table 4 lists the scaling-sensitive hyperparameters identified for each optimizer: AdamW requires tuning of learning rate, warmup, weight decay, and batch size; Lion requires only learning rate and $\beta_2$; Muon requires only learning rate; Soap requires learning rate, warmup, and block size. Phase II then performs coordinate-descent sweeps on only these scaling-sensitive parameters across six additional settings: 300M and 520M models at 2×, 4×, and 8× Chinchilla. Combined with Phase I results, this produces near-optimal hyperparameters and corresponding losses for 12 different settings covering 130M, 300M, 520M models at 1×, 2×, 4×, 8× Chinchilla ratios. Phase III fits a smooth scaling law that predicts the optimal value of each scaling-sensitive hyperparameter as a function of model size $N$ and data budget $D$, enabling extrapolation to the 1.2B parameter regime where exhaustive tuning would be prohibitively expensive. The authors model the optimal value for each scaling-sensitive hyperparameter $h$ as:

$$h(N, D) = \alpha N^{-A} D^{-B} + \beta$$

where $A$, $B$, $\alpha$, and $\beta$ are learned coefficients estimated via non-linear least-squares on the 12 observed $(N, D, h)$ triples for each optimizer. To validate the scaling law's predictive quality, the authors ran a full Phase I sweep at $N = 1.2\text{B}$ and Chinchilla = 1× for AdamW, comparing the identified optimum against the fitted hyperparameters. The predicted hyperparameters yield a final loss within $3 \times 10^{-3}$ of the optimal configuration, demonstrating that the hyperparameter scaling law can effectively predict optimal settings without exhaustive search at the largest scale. Phase III then performs two case studies using the extrapolated hyperparameters: first, training 1.2B models using AdamW, Nesterov AdamW, and Muon on 1× to 8× Chinchilla ratios to test how speedup scales with model size; second, training 130M and 300M models using AdamW, Nesterov AdamW, Muon, and Soap on 16× Chinchilla ratio to test optimizer behavior in extreme overtraining regimes not covered in prior work.

**Speedup Calculation and Evaluation Metrics**

The paper quantifies optimizer speedup by comparing the data efficiency of each method relative to the tuned AdamW baseline, rather than comparing wall-clock time which confounds algorithmic efficiency with implementation details. To calculate speedup, the authors first fit how AdamW's loss scales with data budget $D$ for each model size $N$ using the functional form:

$$\hat{L}_N(D) = \alpha_N D^{-B_N} + \beta_N$$

where $\alpha_N$, $B_N$, and $\beta_N$ are fitted coefficients specific to each model size. Suppose an alternative optimizer achieves loss $L_{\text{optimizer}}$ at data budget $D_{\text{Optimizer}}$. The corresponding data budget needed for AdamW to achieve this same loss, denoted $D_{\text{AdamW}}$, is found by solving the equation $\hat{L}_N(D_{\text{AdamW}}) = L_{\text{optimizer}}$ for $D_{\text{AdamW}}$. The estimated speedup ratio is then calculated as $D_{\text{AdamW}} / D_{\text{Optimizer}}$, representing how many times more data AdamW would need to match the optimizer's performance. This methodology, described in Section 3.3 of the paper, ensures speedup reflects genuine algorithmic improvements rather than implementation artifacts or transient training dynamics. The primary evaluation metric is language modeling loss on the English split of the C4 dataset, which has been shown to be a strong proxy for downstream performance according to Bhagia et al. [2024]. The paper also tracks downstream accuracy on a comprehensive benchmark suite including ARC (Easy and Challenge), BoolQ, COPA, CommonsenseQA, HellaSwag, LAMBADA, OpenBookQA, PIQA, WSC273, and Winogrande, providing validation that loss improvements translate to task performance gains. Table 5 presents benchmark performance for 1.2B models at 8× Chinchilla, showing that while Muon and NAdamW achieve slightly lower loss than AdamW, the downstream accuracy differences are minimal (average accuracy of 66.70–67.15%), indicating diminishing returns at larger scales. The paper explicitly notes that step-wise computation overhead for matrix-based optimizers can be reduced to under 10% through proper implementation, citing Vyas et al. [2025] and Liu et al. [2025a], which justifies focusing comparison on tokens-to-target-loss rather than wall-clock time.

**Design Choices and Methodological Rationale**

The authors' methodological choices reflect deliberate responses to identified weaknesses in prior optimizer evaluation literature. The decision to use coordinate-descent tuning rather than grid search or random search balances computational feasibility with tuning thoroughness—grid search across all hyperparameter combinations would be exponentially expensive, while random search might miss optimal regions. The threshold $\Delta_1 = 3 \times 10^{-3}$ for accepting hyperparameter improvements was chosen to be large enough to exceed measurement noise but small enough to capture meaningful loss differences, based on empirical observation of validation loss variance across runs. The choice to evaluate at end-of-training rather than intermediate checkpoints directly addresses the finding in Figure 5 that loss curves can cross multiple times during learning rate decay, making early-stage rankings potentially misleading. The paper's focus on C4-EN loss as the primary metric, supplemented by downstream benchmarks, follows Bhagia et al. [2024]'s validation that pretraining loss correlates strongly with downstream task performance, enabling efficient evaluation without running full benchmark suites at every checkpoint. The decision to test up to 1.2B parameters rather than frontier-scale models (7B+) reflects a practical constraint—the authors note this limitation in Section 5—but argue that evaluating at scales comparable to prior optimizer studies (which typically test up to 0.6B) still reveals the critical finding that speedups diminish with model size. The use of Llama 2 architecture rather than newer variants ensures comparability with prior work while providing a well-understood, stable training baseline. The mixed-precision scheme (fp32 parameters, bf16 activations) matches common production practice, ensuring findings transfer to real-world pretraining pipelines rather than reflecting artifacts of full-precision research setups.

**Critical Methodological Distinctions from Prior Work**

The paper's methodology differs from prior optimizer evaluations in several specific ways that explain its divergent findings. Unlike Liu et al. [2025a] and similar works that fix hyperparameters across optimizers, this study performs optimizer-specific tuning, revealing that shared hyperparameter values disadvantage methods with different optimal settings. Unlike Sophia (Liu et al. [2024a]) which used a data loader with correlated shuffling that limited achievable learning rates, this study uses fully randomized data ordering enabling higher peak learning rates that benefit AdamW. Unlike Semenov et al. [2025] which used batch sizes of 0.02M–0.1M tokens, this study uses tuned batch sizes not smaller than 0.4M tokens, which changes the relative advantage of variance-reduced methods that perform better in noise-dominated small-batch regimes. Unlike many prior works that evaluate at 1× Chinchilla only, this study tests 1×, 2×, 4×, 8×, and 16× Chinchilla ratios, revealing that optimizer rankings shift with data budget—for example, Muon is best at 1–4× Chinchilla but is overtaken by Soap and Kron at 8× and above. Unlike studies that judge performance at intermediate checkpoints, this study evaluates only at target training budgets, avoiding the ranking reversals shown in Figure 5 where optimizers with rapid early descent plateau while others continue improving during learning rate decay. The separate tuning of embedding layer learning rates for Muon, not performed in some prior Muon evaluations, improves its performance and ensures fair comparison with AdamW which also benefits from embedding-specific tuning.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper delivers several contributions that extend beyond incremental benchmarking results. Below, we distinguish between **fundamental innovations** (methodological or conceptual advances that change how the field should approach optimizer evaluation) and **empirical findings** (specific observations about optimizer behavior that inform future development).

---

### 4.1 Fundamental Innovation: The Three-Phase Hyperparameter Tuning Protocol

**What makes it different:** Prior optimizer papers typically employ one of two flawed approaches: (i) transfer hyperparameters from AdamW without optimizer-specific tuning, or (ii) perform limited manual sweeps that may not reach true optima. This paper introduces a **systematic coordinate-descent framework** with explicit convergence criteria ($\Delta_1 = 3 \times 10^{-3}$ for Phase I, $\Delta_2 = 6.4 \times 10^{-3}$ for Phase II) and a novel concept of **scaling-sensitive hyperparameters** that identifies which knobs require re-tuning as model size changes.

**Why it's significant:** This protocol exposes that many claimed speedups are artifacts of unfair baselines. The dramatic example in Figure 1 (top left) shows that tuning only the learning rate in the standard GPT-3 recipe yields up to **2× improvement** over the commonly used baseline configuration—essentially eliminating the claimed advantage of many proposed optimizers. This is not merely a tuning improvement; it represents a **methodological correction** that should become standard practice in optimizer research. The scaling law formulation $h(N, D) = \alpha N^{-A} D^{-B} + \beta$ for extrapolating hyperparameters to larger scales is itself a reusable contribution for future benchmarking efforts.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in evaluation methodology. While coordinate descent itself is not new, its systematic application to optimizer benchmarking with explicit scaling-law extrapolation and the identification of scaling-sensitive parameters represents a conceptual advance in how the field should validate efficiency claims.

---

### 4.2 Fundamental Finding: Speedup Diminishes with Model Scale

**What makes it different:** Prior optimizer papers typically report speedups at a single model scale (often ≤0.6B parameters) and implicitly assume these gains persist at larger scales. This paper systematically tests four model scales (130M to 1.2B) and discovers that **speedup is inversely proportional to model size**: matrix-based optimizers show 1.4× speedup at 0.1B but only 1.1× at 1.2B (Figure 1, bottom left; Figure 4, mid).

**Why it's significant:** This finding fundamentally challenges the economic justification for adopting novel optimizers in production pretraining. A 1.4× speedup at small scale might justify switching costs, but a 1.1× speedup at frontier scales (where pretraining costs dominate budgets) may not warrant the engineering effort, stability risks, or memory overhead of new methods. The paper's scaling law analysis in Appendix B.1 even predicts that **Muon will result in higher loss than AdamW at 7B parameters and 1× Chinchilla**, suggesting the trend may continue beyond the tested range. This insight redirects optimizer research toward methods whose efficiency remains stable under scaling rather than those that excel only at small scales.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental empirical finding** with implications for resource allocation in AI development. It contradicts the implicit assumption in prior work that optimizer advantages scale favorably, and it provides concrete evidence that evaluation at small scales cannot predict performance at production scales.

---

### 4.3 Design Insight: Matrix Preconditioning as the Distinguishing Factor

**What makes it different:** The paper identifies a clear structural pattern: **all optimizers that consistently outperform AdamW use matrix preconditioners** (Muon, Soap, Kron, Scion) rather than entry-wise scalar operations (AdamW, Lion, Mars). Figure 1 (bottom right) shows matrix-based optimizers (solid lines) consistently achieve lower loss than scalar-based optimizers (dashed lines) across all Chinchilla ratios for models under 520M.

**Why it's significant:** This observation provides a **design principle for future optimizer development**. Rather than pursuing incremental modifications to AdamW (e.g., variance reduction, momentum tweaks), the evidence suggests that leveraging the matrix structure of neural network parameters is the most promising direction. However, the paper also notes the caveat that this advantage diminishes with scale, suggesting that future work should focus on making matrix preconditioning **scale-stable** rather than simply improving small-scale performance. The paper also notes that step-wise computation overhead for matrix-based methods can be reduced to under 10% through proper implementation (citing Vyas et al. [2025]), addressing a common practical objection.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is an **incremental synthesis** of existing optimizer designs rather than a novel algorithmic contribution. However, as a **design insight**, it is significant because it provides empirical validation for a specific architectural choice (matrix vs. scalar preconditioning) that can guide future research directions.

---

### 4.4 Methodological Insight: Data-to-Model Ratio Shifts Optimizer Rankings

**What makes it different:** Prior optimizer studies typically evaluate at a single data-to-model ratio (usually 1× Chinchilla). This paper tests 1×, 2×, 4×, 8×, and 16× Chinchilla ratios, revealing that **optimizer rankings are not stable across training regimes**. Specifically, Muon is consistently best at 1–4× Chinchilla but is overtaken by Soap and Kron at 8× and above (Figure 4, right; Figure 3).

**Why it's significant:** This finding has practical implications for optimizer selection. A method that excels in compute-optimal training (1× Chinchilla) may be suboptimal for overtrained models (common in production when teams maximize data usage within fixed compute budgets). The paper conjectures that **second-order momentum maintained by Soap and Kron becomes more effective when the data-to-model ratio increases**, suggesting adaptivity to parameter direction heterogeneity matters more in overtrained regimes. This insight means optimizer benchmarking should report performance across multiple data budgets, not just at a single point.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental empirical finding** about optimizer behavior that was previously unexplored. It challenges the assumption that optimizer performance is intrinsic and stable, showing instead that it depends on the training regime.

---

### 4.5 Cautionary Insight: Early Training Curves Can Mislead Rankings

**What makes it different:** Many prior optimizer papers evaluate performance using intermediate checkpoints or early stopping criteria. This paper demonstrates in Figure 5 that **loss curves cross multiple times during learning rate decay**, meaning an optimizer that appears superior at 50% of training may be inferior at completion.

**Why it's significant:** This finding invalidates a common evaluation practice in optimizer research. The paper explicitly recommends evaluating optimizers **only on final checkpoints** rather than intermediate points, as early-stage loss improvements may reflect rapid initial descent followed by plateauing rather than genuine efficiency gains. This insight applies beyond optimizers to any method evaluated on partial training trajectories (e.g., architecture search, data pruning).

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is an **incremental validation** of concerns raised by Kaddour et al. [2023], but with stronger empirical evidence across multiple optimizers and scales. It is significant as a **methodological correction** that should change evaluation standards in the field.

| **Fundamental Innovation** | Three-phase tuning protocol with scaling-law extrapolation | Establishes new standard for fair optimizer evaluation |
| **Fundamental Finding** | Speedup diminishes from 1.4× to 1.1× as models scale from 0.1B to 1.2B | Challenges economic justification for adopting novel optimizers at frontier scales |
| **Design Insight** | Matrix preconditioning distinguishes high-performing optimizers | Guides future optimizer development toward scale-stable matrix methods |
| **Methodological Insight** | Optimizer rankings shift with data-to-model ratio | Requires multi-regime benchmarking rather than single-point evaluation |
| **Cautionary Insight** | Early training curves can reverse final rankings | Invalidates intermediate-checkpoint evaluation practices |

The paper's most significant contribution is not any single optimizer comparison, but rather the **methodological framework** that reveals how prior claims were inflated by unfair baselines and limited evaluation regimes. This reframes the optimizer research landscape: the question is no longer "which optimizer is best?" but rather "under what conditions does each optimizer provide meaningful gains, and do those gains persist at production scales?"

## 5. Experimental Analysis

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Training Corpus.** The paper constructs a pretraining corpus from three publicly available datasets tokenized with the Llama 3 tokenizer (Section 3.1). The DCLM-baseline dataset contributes 3.8 trillion tokens, StarCoder V2 Data adds 0.25 trillion tokens of code, and ProofPile 2 provides 55 billion tokens of mathematical proofs. This mixture mirrors OLMo 2's data composition, ensuring diversity in linguistic and symbolic patterns.

**Model Architectures.** Four Llama 2-derived model scales are evaluated (Table 2):
| Model | Parameters | Hidden Dim | Intermediate Dim | Attention Heads |
|-------|------------|------------|------------------|-----------------|
| Llama-130M | 130M | 512 | 2048 | 8 |
| Llama-300M | 300M | 768 | 3072 | 12 |
| Llama-520M | 520M | 1024 | 4096 | 16 |
| Llama-1.2B | 1.2B | 1536 | 6144 | 24 |

All models use a fixed sequence length of 4,096 tokens and 32 transformer layers, isolating model capacity as the primary variable. Training uses mixed-precision (fp32 parameters, bf16 activations) on TPU v5 hardware.

**Training Regimes.** The paper evaluates across multiple data-to-model ratios based on the Chinchilla optimum (approximately 20 tokens per parameter per Hoffmann et al. [2022]). Main experiments cover 1×, 2×, 4×, and 8× Chinchilla ratios. Additional case studies extend to 16× Chinchilla for overtraining analysis (Section 3.4, Appendix F).

**Primary Metrics.** Language modeling loss on the English split of the C4 dataset serves as the primary evaluation metric, validated by Bhagia et al. [2024] as a strong proxy for downstream performance. The paper also tracks accuracy on ten downstream benchmarks: ARC (Easy and Challenge), BoolQ, COPA, CommonsenseQA, HellaSwag, LAMBADA, OpenBookQA, PIQA, WSC273, and Winogrande.

**Speedup Calculation.** Rather than comparing wall-clock time (which confounds algorithmic efficiency with implementation details), the paper computes speedup as a data efficiency ratio (Section 3.3). For each model size $N$, AdamW's loss scaling is fitted as $\hat{L}_N(D) = \alpha_N D^{-B_N} + \beta_N$. When an optimizer achieves loss $L_{\text{optimizer}}$ at data budget $D_{\text{Optimizer}}$, the speedup is calculated as $D_{\text{AdamW}} / D_{\text{Optimizer}}$, where $D_{\text{AdamW}}$ solves $\hat{L}_N(D_{\text{AdamW}}) = L_{\text{optimizer}}$.

**Baselines.** Eleven optimizers are benchmarked (Table 1), grouped into five classes: AdamW (baseline), variance-reduced Adam variants (NAdamW, Mars, Cautious), memory-efficient methods (Lion, Adam-mini), matrix-based preconditioners (Muon, Scion, Kron, Soap), and Hessian-approximation (Sophia). All optimizers undergo identical three-phase hyperparameter tuning to ensure fair comparison.

---

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

#### 5.2.1 Hyperparameter Tuning Impact

The paper's first critical finding demonstrates that baseline tuning alone can eliminate claimed speedups. Figure 1 (top left) shows that tuning only the learning rate in the standard GPT-3 recipe for a 130M model yields **up to 2× improvement** over the commonly used baseline configuration (learning rate 6e-4 vs. optimized 8e-3). This single adjustment produces loss curves that match or exceed many proposed optimizers running with their original hyperparameters.

Figure 1 (top right) further illustrates that optimal hyperparameters vary significantly between optimizers even within the same class. Lion's optimal weight decay is approximately **0.6**, while AdamW's is approximately **0.1**. Using a shared weight decay value (as done in prior work like Liu et al. [2025a]) disadvantages one or both methods, invalidating fair comparison.

#### 5.2.2 Speedup Across Model Scales (0.1B–0.5B)

Figure 2 presents validation loss curves for 130M, 300M, and 520M models across 1× to 8× Chinchilla ratios. Three patterns emerge:

**Pattern 1: Matrix-based methods outperform scalar-based methods.** For every model size, matrix-based optimizers (Soap, Muon, Kron, Scion—solid curves in Figure 1 bottom right) consistently achieve lower validation loss than scalar-based counterparts (AdamW, NAdamW, Mars, Lion—dashed curves). At 1× Chinchilla compute, Muon performs best; at 8× Chinchilla, the advantage shifts to Soap and Kron.

**Pattern 2: Speedup decreases with model size.** Figure 3 quantifies speedup ratios across scales:
- **130M models:** Matrix-based optimizers achieve up to **1.4×** speedup over AdamW
- **300M models:** Speedup decreases to approximately **1.3×**
- **520M models:** Speedup further decreases to approximately **1.2×**

The highest observed speedup across all configurations is capped at **1.4×** (Figure 3), substantially lower than the 2× claims in prior literature.

**Pattern 3: Variance-reduction provides modest gains.** Within the scalar-based family, all variance-reduced Adam variants (NAdamW, Mars, Cautious) consistently surpass vanilla AdamW, but with speedup ratios **below 1.2×** on average. This contrasts sharply with claimed 2× speedups in papers like Yuan et al. [2025] (MARS) and Liang et al. [2025] (Cautious).

**Pattern 4: Memory-efficient variants track AdamW closely.** Lion and Adam-mini show speedup ratios within **5% of AdamW** (sometimes slightly better, sometimes slightly worse). Interestingly, Lion's disadvantage relative to AdamW widens with model size, while Adam-mini's disadvantage narrows.

#### 5.2.3 Speedup at 1.2B Scale

Figure 4 (Left and Mid) presents results for 1.2B parameter models—the largest scale tested. The speedup trend continues to diminish:

| Optimizer | Speedup at 1.2B (8× Chinchilla) |
|-----------|--------------------------------|
| NAdamW | ~1.1× |
| Muon | ~1.1× |
| Soap | ~1.1× |

All three optimizers still deliver speedup over AdamW, but the advantage is marginal. Table 5 shows downstream benchmark performance for 1.2B models at 8× Chinchilla:

| Optimizer | Average Accuracy (10 benchmarks) |
|-----------|----------------------------------|
| AdamW | 67.15% |
| NAdamW | 66.70% |
| Muon | 66.98% |

Despite achieving slightly lower training loss, Muon and NAdamW **do not lead to downstream improvements** over AdamW at this scale. The paper's scaling law analysis in Appendix B.1 even predicts that **Muon will result in higher loss than AdamW at 7B parameters and 1× Chinchilla**, suggesting the trend may continue beyond the tested range.

#### 5.2.4 High Data-to-Model Ratio Regimes

Figure 4 (Right) and Figure 8 (Appendix B.3) test optimizer behavior at 16× Chinchilla ratio—an overtraining regime not covered in prior optimizer studies. Results show:

- **130M models at 16×:** Soap outperforms Muon
- **300M models at 16×:** Soap outperforms Muon; NAdamW also surpasses Muon

The paper conjectures that **second-order momentum maintained by Soap and Kron becomes more effective when the data-to-model ratio increases**. In long training runs, adaptivity to heterogeneity in parameter directions may lead to larger speedup, shifting the optimal optimizer choice based on training budget.

#### 5.2.5 Sophia Performance (Failure Case)

Appendix B.2 presents Sophia experiments, revealing a notable failure case. Figure 7 shows that Sophia **underperforms AdamW in smaller compute regimes** and only slightly outperforms AdamW when either model size or data size increases. The paper attributes this to the data loader implementation in Sophia's original codebase, which did not fully randomize data order. This correlated shuffling limited the optimal peak learning rate for AdamW in that setup; with fully randomized data (as used in this paper), AdamW can use higher learning rates, eliminating Sophia's advantage.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

#### 5.3.1 Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis

Appendix C (Tables 36–99) and Appendix D (Tables 100–159) present exhaustive hyperparameter ablation tables for Phase I and Phase II. Key findings:

**Learning rate sensitivity.** For AdamW on 130M at 1× Chinchilla (Table 36), changing learning rate from optimal 0.008 to 0.032 increases loss from 3.529 to 7.781—a **catastrophic failure**. Similarly, reducing to 0.004 increases loss to 3.550. This demonstrates extreme sensitivity to learning rate selection.

**Weight decay sensitivity.** For Lion on 130M at 1× Chinchilla (Table 47), optimal weight decay is 0.7 with loss 3.552. Using weight decay 0.0 increases loss to 3.570; using 1.0 gives 3.557. The optimal range is narrow but not as catastrophic as learning rate.

**Batch size effects.** For AdamW on 520M at 1× Chinchilla (Table 41), batch size 256 gives loss 3.110, but batch size 128 causes divergence (loss 7.630). This indicates batch size interacts critically with learning rate and must be tuned jointly.

#### 5.3.2 Early vs. End-of-Training Evaluation

Figure 5 (Right) demonstrates that **loss curves cross multiple times during learning rate decay**. An optimizer may appear superior at 50% of training but be inferior at completion. For example, on 520M models at 8× Chinchilla, Muon shows faster early descent than Mars, but Mars eventually achieves lower final loss when both are properly tuned.

Figure 5 (Left) further shows that **2× the optimal learning rate diminishes Soap's advantage over Mars** on 520M 8× experiments. This confirms that hyperparameter sensitivity can flip optimizer rankings if tuning is not rigorous.

#### 5.3.3 Scaling Law Validation

Appendix B.1 validates the hyperparameter scaling law $h(N, D) = \alpha N^{-A} D^{-B} + \beta$ by running a full Phase I sweep at 1.2B and 1× Chinchilla for AdamW. The predicted hyperparameters yield final loss **within $3 \times 10^{-3}$ of the optimal configuration**, demonstrating the scaling law can effectively predict optimal settings without exhaustive search at the largest scale.

Fitted scaling laws for loss (not hyperparameters) predict:
- **AdamW:** $\alpha = 21.4289, A = 0.1555, \beta = 276.4235, B = 0.2804, \gamma = 1.7324$ (RMS error $3 \times 10^{-3}$)
- **Muon:** $\alpha = 32.7458, A = 0.1864, \beta = 59.0221, B = 0.2074, \gamma = 1.8063$ (RMS error $5 \times 10^{-3}$)

The higher $\alpha$ and $\gamma$ values for Muon suggest it starts with higher loss but may converge differently at extreme scales—supporting the prediction that Muon will underperform AdamW at 7B parameters.

#### 5.3.4 Downstream Benchmark Correlation

Figure 2 (Bottom) shows HellaSwag accuracy improvements closely mirror validation loss gains across 130M, 300M, and 520M models. This validates the use of C4-EN loss as a proxy metric. However, at 1.2B scale (Table 5), loss improvements **no longer translate to downstream gains**, suggesting diminishing returns where loss differences become too small to affect benchmark performance.

---

### 5.4 Assessment: Do Experiments Support the Claims?

#### 5.4.1 Claim 1: Fair Comparisons Require Rigorous Hyperparameter Tuning

**Strongly supported.** The 2× improvement from tuning only the learning rate (Figure 1, top left) is dramatic and unambiguous. The hyperparameter ablation tables (Appendices C–F) provide exhaustive evidence that small deviations from optimal settings cause large loss increases, often enough to flip optimizer rankings (Figure 5, Mid). This claim is the paper's most robust finding.

#### 5.4.2 Claim 2: Speedup Diminishes with Model Scale

**Well-supported within tested range.** The paper demonstrates a clear trend from 1.4× at 130M to 1.1× at 1.2B (Figure 1 bottom left, Figure 4 Mid). However, the paper **does not test beyond 1.2B parameters**, and the 7B prediction is based on extrapolation (Appendix B.1). While the trend is consistent across four model scales, readers should note this is an extrapolation, not a direct measurement. The claim is convincing for the tested range but remains uncertain at frontier scales (7B+).

#### 5.4.3 Claim 3: Matrix-Based Optimizers Consistently Outperform Scalar-Based

**Supported for models under 520M.** Figure 1 (bottom right) and Figure 3 show matrix-based optimizers (Muon, Soap, Kron, Scion) consistently achieve lower loss than scalar-based methods across all Chinchilla ratios for 130M–520M models. However, at 1.2B, the advantage narrows significantly (Figure 4), and the paper does not claim matrix-based methods remain superior at larger scales. This claim is **conditional on model size**.

#### 5.4.4 Claim 4: Early Training Curves Can Mislead

**Strongly supported.** Figure 5 provides clear visual evidence of loss curve crossings during learning rate decay. The paper's recommendation to evaluate only at end-of-training is well-justified by this data. This finding aligns with prior work (Kaddour et al. [2023]) but is demonstrated here across more optimizers and scales.

#### 5.4.5 Claim 5: Data-to-Model Ratio Shifts Optimizer Rankings

**Supported within tested regimes.** Figure 4 (Right) and Figure 8 show Muon is overtaken by Soap and Kron at 8× and 16× Chinchilla. However, this is demonstrated only on 130M and 300M models at extreme overtraining. The paper does not test whether this ranking shift occurs at 1.2B with high Chinchilla ratios. The claim is **valid but limited to smaller models in overtraining regimes**.

---

### 5.5 Limitations and Conditional Findings

**Scale limitation.** The paper's most significant constraint is the 1.2B parameter ceiling. While this exceeds prior optimizer studies (typically ≤0.6B), it remains far from frontier models (7B–70B+). The scaling law extrapolation to 7B is informative but not definitive.

**Hardware regime specificity.** The comparison with Semenov et al. [2025] in Section 2 reveals that batch size differences (≥0.4M tokens here vs. 0.02M–0.1M tokens there) affect relative optimizer performance. Variance-reduced methods like Mars advantage in noise-dominated small-batch regimes, while matrix-based methods excel in larger-batch settings. Findings may not transfer across hardware regimes.

**Architecture specificity.** All experiments use Llama 2 architecture. While this ensures comparability with prior work, results may not generalize to other architectures (e.g., MoE, decoder-only variants with different normalization schemes).

**Implementation overhead not measured.** The paper notes that matrix-based optimizers' step-wise computation overhead can be reduced to under 10% through proper implementation (citing Vyas et al. [2025], Liu et al. [2025a]), but does not measure actual wall-clock speedup. A 1.1× data efficiency gain could be negated by 20% computational overhead in practice.

**Sophia implementation differences.** The Sophia results differ from the original paper due to data loader randomization differences. While this reveals a methodological issue in the original Sophia evaluation, it means this paper's Sophia results are not directly comparable to the published Sophia claims.

### 5.6 Summary of Experimental Evidence

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Key Supporting Data |
|-------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Hyperparameter tuning critical | **Strong** | Figure 1 (top left): 2× from LR tuning; Tables 36–99: ablation sensitivity |
| Speedup diminishes with scale | **Moderate-Strong** | Figure 1 (bottom left), Figure 4: 1.4×→1.1× from 0.1B→1.2B |
| Matrix-based > scalar-based | **Conditional** | Figure 1 (bottom right): true for ≤520M; narrows at 1.2B |
| Early curves mislead | **Strong** | Figure 5: loss curve crossings during LR decay |
| Data ratio shifts rankings | **Conditional** | Figure 4 (Right), Figure 8: Muon→Soap at 8×–16× Chinchilla (130M–300M only) |

The experiments convincingly support the paper's core methodological claims (tuning importance, evaluation timing) and provide strong evidence for scale-dependent speedup within the tested range. Claims about optimizer rankings at frontier scales or across all architectures remain extrapolations requiring further validation.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper establishes a rigorous benchmarking framework that corrects methodological flaws in prior optimizer evaluation, its findings come with important constraints. Understanding these limitations is critical for interpreting the results appropriately and for designing future evaluation efforts. We organize these limitations into four categories: **scale extrapolation uncertainty**, **hardware and implementation dependencies**, **architectural and data specificity**, and **unmeasured practical factors**.

---

### 6.1 Scale Extrapolation Uncertainty

**The 1.2B Parameter Ceiling.** The most significant limitation acknowledged by the authors is the maximum model scale tested. Section 5 (Conclusion and Limitations) explicitly states:

> "This benchmarking study has the limitation that it does we haven't scaled to models larger than 1.2B parameters."

While 1.2B parameters exceeds prior optimizer studies (typically ≤0.6B per Vyas et al. [2025], Liu et al. [2025a]), it remains far from frontier production models (7B–70B+). The paper's claim that speedup diminishes with scale is well-supported within the tested range (1.4× at 130M → 1.1× at 1.2B per Figure 1 and Figure 4), but the trend beyond 1.2B is **extrapolated, not measured**.

Appendix B.1 presents a scaling law fit predicting that **Muon will result in higher loss than AdamW at 7B parameters and 1× Chinchilla**. However, this prediction rests on:
- A functional form $L(N, D) = \alpha N^{-A} + \beta D^{-B} + \gamma$ fitted to only 16 runs
- RMS error of $5 \times 10^{-3}$ for Muon (higher than AdamW's $3 \times 10^{-3}$)
- No validation at intermediate scales (e.g., 3B, 5B) to confirm the trend continues

**Implication:** Practitioners should treat the 7B prediction as a hypothesis requiring validation, not an established fact. The diminishing speedup trend is robust within the tested range but uncertain at frontier scales where pretraining costs are most consequential.

**Limited Chinchilla Ratio Coverage at Large Scale.** The 1.2B experiments cover 1× to 8× Chinchilla ratios, but the 16× Chinchilla experiments (Section 3.4, Appendix F) are restricted to 130M and 300M models. This creates a gap: we know Muon is overtaken by Soap at 16× Chinchilla for small models, but **we do not know if this ranking shift occurs at 1.2B or larger scales**. The interaction between model size and data budget remains partially unexplored.

---

### 6.2 Hardware and Implementation Dependencies

**Batch Size Regime Specificity.** The comparison with Semenov et al. [2025] in Section 2 reveals a critical dependency: this paper uses batch sizes **not smaller than 0.4M tokens**, while Semenov et al. use 0.02M–0.1M tokens. The authors note:

> "Since Mars and AdEMAMix both perform gradient averaging and variance reduction, these methods are advantageous in their noise-dominated small-batch regime, whereas in our larger-batch setting these benefits diminish and matrix-level optimizers become more competitive."

This finding has two implications:
1. **Variance-reduced methods (Mars, Cautious, NAdamW) may underperform in this study relative to small-batch settings** where their noise reduction provides greater benefit.
2. **Results may not transfer across hardware regimes.** Teams training on fewer GPUs (where smaller batches are necessary) may observe different optimizer rankings than reported here.

**Step-Wise Overhead Not Directly Measured.** The paper focuses on **data efficiency** (tokens to target loss) rather than **wall-clock time**. Section 3.1 states:

> "As previous works Vyas et al. [2025], Liu et al. [2025a] show that the step-wise computation overhead of matrix-based optimizers can be reduced to under 10% through proper implementation, we primarily compare algorithms by the number of tokens needed to reach a given loss."

This is a reasonable methodological choice for isolating algorithmic efficiency, but it introduces a **practical gap**:
- A 1.1× data efficiency gain at 1.2B scale could be **negated by 20% computational overhead** in real implementations.
- Matrix-based optimizers (Muon, Soap, Kron) require additional matrix operations (Newton-Schulz iterations, QR decompositions) that may have different scaling characteristics on different hardware (TPU vs. GPU vs. custom accelerators).
- Memory overhead for matrix preconditioners is not quantified in this study, though Section 3.1 notes Soap uses parameter blocking (Anil et al. [2021]) to reduce memory.

**Implication:** A practitioner deciding whether to adopt Muon for a 1.2B model cannot rely solely on the 1.1× speedup figure—they must measure actual wall-clock time and memory usage in their specific deployment environment.

**Data Loader Randomization.** The Sophia results (Appendix B.2, Figure 7) differ from the original Liu et al. [2024a] paper due to data loader implementation:

> "As the data loader in the codebase used did not fully randomize the order of the data, the optimal peak learning rate in that code base for AdamW is significantly smaller than the optimal peak learning rate for a fully randomized data loader setting."

This reveals that **optimizer performance can be confounded by data pipeline details** unrelated to the optimization algorithm itself. While this paper's fully randomized shuffling is methodologically superior, it means:
- Sophia's performance in this study is not directly comparable to published Sophia claims.
- Other optimizers may similarly be sensitive to data ordering, though this was not systematically tested.

---

### 6.3 Architectural and Data Specificity

**Single Architecture Family.** All experiments use the **Llama 2 architecture** (Touvron et al. [2023b], Grattafiori et al. [2024]) with 32 transformer layers and varying hidden dimensions (Table 2). While this ensures comparability with prior work, results may not generalize to:
- **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures**, which have different gradient sparsity patterns and may interact differently with matrix preconditioners.
- **Alternative normalization schemes** (e.g., RMSNorm vs. LayerNorm, pre-norm vs. post-norm).
- **Different depth-to-width ratios**, which could affect how matrix structure manifests in gradients.

The paper notes in Section 5 that Kimi K2 uses Muon-clip (a Muon variant), suggesting some production adoption, but this is a single data point rather than systematic evidence of architectural generality.

**Data Mixture Specificity.** The training corpus combines DCLM-baseline (3.8T tokens), StarCoder V2 (0.25T tokens), and ProofPile 2 (55B tokens) per Section 3.1. This OLMo 2-like mixture is diverse but **may not represent all pretraining scenarios**:
- Models trained primarily on code may exhibit different optimizer behavior (code has different token distributions and syntactic structure).
- Domain-specific pretraining (e.g., biomedical, legal) with smaller, specialized corpora may have different optimal data-to-model ratios.
- The 20 tokens-per-parameter Chinchilla optimum (Hoffmann et al. [2022]) is based on specific data compositions and may shift for different mixtures.

**Learning Rate Schedule Constraints.** All experiments use a **warmup-stable-decay (WSD)** learning rate schedule (referenced in Table 4 for Scion's scaling-sensitive hyperparameters). The paper does not test:
- Constant learning rate schedules (used in SPlus per Frans et al. [2025], which claims 2× speedup in that atypical setup).
- Cosine decay schedules (common in many production pipelines).
- Cyclic or multi-phase schedules.

Optimizer performance can be schedule-dependent. For example, Figure 5 shows loss curves crossing during learning rate decay, suggesting that **the decay phase is where optimizer differences manifest most clearly**. A different schedule could amplify or suppress these differences.

---

### 6.4 Unmeasured Practical Factors

**Training Stability and Failure Modes.** The paper reports final validation loss but does not systematically quantify:
- **Gradient explosion/implosion rates** across optimizers.
- **Divergence frequency** during hyperparameter sweeps (some ablation tables show loss >10 or NaN, e.g., Table 56 row 4 for Mars, Table 81 row 16 for Soap, but these are not aggregated into stability metrics).
- **Sensitivity to initialization** or random seed variance.

Table 3 and the Phase I methodology show that hyperparameter tuning accepts improvements only if loss decreases by $\Delta_1 = 3 \times 10^{-3}$, but this does not capture how often runs fail entirely. Practitioners care not only about final performance but also about **reliability**—an optimizer that achieves 1.1× speedup but diverges 5% of the time may be less valuable than a stable baseline.

**Downstream Task Saturation.** Table 5 shows that at 1.2B scale and 8× Chinchilla, loss improvements **do not translate to downstream accuracy gains**:

| Optimizer | Average Accuracy (10 benchmarks) |
|-----------|----------------------------------|
| AdamW | 67.15% |
| NAdamW | 66.70% |
| Muon | 66.98% |

This suggests **diminishing returns where loss differences become too small to affect benchmark performance**. The paper does not explore whether this saturation occurs at all scales or only at 1.2B. If loss improvements cease to matter for downstream tasks beyond a certain threshold, the economic value of optimizer speedups decreases accordingly.

**Fine-Tuning and Adaptation Behavior.** All experiments measure **pretraining loss only**. The paper does not address:
- Do optimizers that achieve lower pretraining loss also fine-tune better?
- Do matrix-based preconditioners affect **transfer learning** differently than scalar methods?
- How do optimizers interact with **instruction tuning** or **RLHF** phases that follow pretraining?

Given that pretraining is only one component of the full model development pipeline (even if it accounts for 95% of cost per Section 1), optimizer effects on downstream adaptation remain an open question.

**Optimizer State Memory Overhead.** While the paper includes memory-efficient optimizers (Lion, Adam-mini) in the benchmark, it does not report **absolute memory consumption** for each optimizer. Matrix-based methods (Muon, Soap, Kron) maintain additional state (preconditioner matrices, momentum buffers) that could:
- Limit maximum batch size on memory-constrained hardware.
- Increase communication overhead in distributed training.
- Affect activation checkpointing strategies.

Section 3.1 notes Soap uses parameter blocking to reduce memory, but the trade-off between memory savings and performance is not quantified.

---

### 6.5 Open Questions for Future Research

The paper's limitations point to several unresolved questions:

| Question | Why It Matters | How to Address |
|----------|---------------|----------------|
| **Does speedup continue diminishing beyond 1.2B?** | Determines whether novel optimizers are worth adopting at frontier scales. | Run benchmark at 3B, 7B, 13B parameters with same tuning protocol. |
| **Do matrix-based methods excel in small-batch regimes?** | Affects optimizer choice for teams with limited hardware. | Replicate study with batch sizes 0.02M–0.1M tokens (Semenov et al. regime). |
| **How do optimizers interact with MoE architectures?** | MoE models are increasingly common in production. | Extend benchmark to Llama-style MoE variants. |
| **What is the wall-clock speedup including implementation overhead?** | Data efficiency ≠ training time in practice. | Measure actual training time on fixed hardware with optimized implementations. |
| **Do optimizer differences persist through fine-tuning?** | Pretraining is a means to downstream performance, not an end. | Fine-tune pretrained checkpoints on standard task suites and compare. |
| **How sensitive are results to data mixture composition?** | Different domains may have different optimizer optima. | Test on code-only, math-only, and domain-specific corpora. |

---

### 6.6 Summary: Interpreting the Findings Appropriately

This paper's limitations do not invalidate its core contributions—the methodological framework for fair optimizer comparison remains robust, and the finding that hyperparameter tuning dramatically affects reported speedups is unambiguous. However, readers should interpret the quantitative results with the following caveats:

1. **Speedup figures (1.1×–1.4×) apply to the tested regime only** (130M–1.2B parameters, ≥0.4M token batches, Llama 2 architecture, OLMo 2-like data). Extrapolation beyond this range is uncertain.

2. **Data efficiency ≠ wall-clock time.** A 1.1× speedup in tokens-to-loss may not translate to 1.1× faster training if implementation overhead is significant.

3. **Loss improvements may not matter at scale.** Table 5 shows downstream accuracy saturation at 1.2B, suggesting that beyond a certain point, optimizer choice has minimal impact on final model capability.

4. **Optimizer rankings are regime-dependent.** Muon is best at 1–4× Chinchilla but overtaken by Soap at 8×+ (Figure 4). Practitioners should match optimizer choice to their specific data budget, not adopt a single method universally.

The paper's most enduring contribution is not any specific speedup number, but the **methodological standard** it establishes: rigorous, optimizer-specific hyperparameter tuning across multiple scales and data budgets, with evaluation at end-of-training. Future optimizer research that adopts this framework will produce more reliable, actionable findings—even if the specific numbers differ from those reported here.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper's findings extend beyond a single benchmarking study—they reshape how the field should approach optimizer development, evaluation, and adoption. Below, we outline the cascading implications for research methodology, practical deployment, and future investigation.

---

### 7.1 Reshaping the Optimizer Research Landscape

**From Speedup Claims to Scale Stability.** Prior optimizer papers implicitly assumed that advantages discovered at small scales (≤0.6B parameters) would persist or improve at larger scales. This paper overturns that assumption with empirical evidence: speedup is **inversely proportional to model size**, dropping from 1.4× at 130M to 1.1× at 1.2B (Figure 1, bottom left; Figure 4, mid). This finding redirects the research question from "which optimizer is fastest?" to "**which optimizer maintains efficiency under scaling?**"

Future optimizer papers should be expected to report performance across multiple model scales as a standard requirement, not an optional ablation. A method that excels only at 130M parameters but offers marginal gains at 1.2B should be framed as a small-scale optimization, not a general pretraining improvement.

**Methodological Standards for Fair Comparison.** The three-phase hyperparameter tuning protocol (Section 3.2–3.4) establishes a new baseline for optimizer evaluation. The dramatic 2× improvement from tuning only the learning rate in the GPT-3 recipe (Figure 1, top left) demonstrates that **unequal tuning effort can fully explain claimed speedups**. This has three concrete implications:

1. **Optimizer-specific tuning is mandatory.** Transferring AdamW hyperparameters to new optimizers (as done in Liu et al. [2025a]) is no longer defensible. Each optimizer must receive comparable tuning effort.

2. **Scaling-sensitive hyperparameters must be identified.** The paper's framework for distinguishing scaling-sensitive from scaling-insensitive parameters (Table 4) provides a reusable template. Future work should report which hyperparameters shift with scale and which remain stable.

3. **End-of-training evaluation is required.** Figure 5's demonstration of loss curve crossings during learning rate decay invalidates intermediate-checkpoint evaluation. Papers reporting speedups based on partial training trajectories should be treated with skepticism.

**Reframing the Matrix Preconditioning Narrative.** The paper identifies matrix preconditioning as the distinguishing factor between high-performing and baseline optimizers (Figure 1, bottom right), but also reveals that this advantage diminishes with scale. This creates a nuanced design principle: **matrix preconditioning is necessary but not sufficient** for meaningful speedups at frontier scales. Future work should focus on:
- Making matrix operations **scale-stable** rather than simply improving small-scale performance
- Reducing the computational overhead of matrix preconditioners below the 10% threshold cited from Vyas et al. [2025]
- Understanding why matrix advantages decay—whether due to gradient structure changes, optimization landscape shifts, or implementation limitations

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper's limitations (Section 6) naturally suggest concrete follow-up investigations. We organize these by priority and feasibility.

#### 7.2.1 High-Priority: Scale Extrapolation Validation

**Testing at 3B–7B Parameters.** The paper's scaling law predicts Muon will underperform AdamW at 7B parameters and 1× Chinchilla (Appendix B.1), but this is extrapolation from 16 runs, not direct measurement. A follow-up study should:
- Train 3B and 7B models using the same three-phase tuning protocol
- Test AdamW, Muon, and Soap at 1×, 4×, and 8× Chinchilla ratios
- Validate whether the 1.4×→1.1× trend continues to 1.0× or below

This research is computationally expensive but essential for determining whether novel optimizers warrant adoption at production scales where pretraining costs are most consequential.

**Wall-Clock Time Measurements.** This paper measures **data efficiency** (tokens to target loss) rather than **wall-clock time**. A practical follow-up should:
- Implement optimized versions of Muon, Soap, and Kron on TPU v5 and H100 GPU clusters
- Measure actual training time including matrix operation overhead
- Quantify memory overhead for preconditioner state (not reported in this study)
- Determine whether 1.1× data efficiency translates to meaningful wall-clock savings after overhead

A 1.1× speedup with 15% computational overhead yields only ~1.04× real-world improvement—potentially insufficient to justify engineering effort and stability risks.

#### 7.2.2 Medium-Priority: Regime-Specific Investigations

**Small-Batch Regime Replication.** The comparison with Semenov et al. [2025] reveals that batch size fundamentally affects optimizer rankings. Variance-reduced methods (Mars, Cautious) excel in noise-dominated small-batch regimes (0.02M–0.1M tokens), while matrix-based methods dominate at larger batches (≥0.4M tokens). A systematic study should:
- Test all eleven optimizers across batch sizes from 0.02M to 1M tokens
- Map the "crossover point" where matrix methods overtake variance-reduced methods
- Provide hardware-specific guidance (e.g., "use Mars on 1–8 H100 GPUs, use Muon on 128+ TPU cores")

This would resolve the apparent contradiction between this paper's findings and Semenov et al.'s observation that Mars outperforms Muon.

**Architecture Generalization.** All experiments use Llama 2 architecture. Follow-up work should test whether findings generalize to:
- **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)** models, which have sparse gradient patterns that may interact differently with matrix preconditioners
- **Alternative normalization schemes** (RMSNorm vs. LayerNorm, pre-norm vs. post-norm)
- **Different depth-to-width ratios**, which could affect how matrix structure manifests in gradients

The paper notes Kimi K2 uses Muon-clip in production (Section 1), but this is a single data point. Systematic architecture testing would determine whether matrix preconditioning benefits are architecture-specific or universal.

**Data Mixture Sensitivity.** The training corpus combines DCLM-baseline, StarCoder V2, and ProofPile 2 (Section 3.1). Follow-up research should test whether optimizer rankings shift with:
- **Code-heavy mixtures** (e.g., 50%+ code tokens), where syntactic structure differs from natural language
- **Domain-specific corpora** (biomedical, legal, scientific), which may have different optimal data-to-model ratios
- **Lower-quality data**, which may affect optimal learning rates and stability

The Sophia results (Appendix B.2) already show that data loader randomization affects achievable learning rates. Data composition may similarly confound optimizer comparisons.

#### 7.2.3 Lower-Priority: Downstream Behavior

**Fine-Tuning and Adaptation.** This paper measures pretraining loss only. A valuable extension would:
- Fine-tune pretrained checkpoints on standard task suites (GLUE, SuperGLUE, etc.)
- Measure whether pretraining loss differences translate to fine-tuning performance
- Test instruction tuning and RLHF phases to see if optimizer choice affects alignment behavior

Given that pretraining is a means to downstream capability (not an end), optimizer effects on adaptation matter for practical adoption decisions.

**Training Stability Metrics.** The paper reports final loss but does not systematically quantify:
- Divergence rates during hyperparameter sweeps (some ablation tables show loss >10 or NaN)
- Gradient norm stability across training
- Sensitivity to random seed variance

A stability-focused study would help practitioners assess risk-reward tradeoffs: is a 1.1× speedup worth a 2% divergence rate?

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Deployment Guidance

For practitioners deciding whether to adopt novel optimizers in production pretraining, this paper provides actionable guidance conditioned on specific constraints.

#### 7.3.1 When to Use Matrix-Based Optimizers

**Recommended scenarios:**
- **Model size ≤520M parameters:** Matrix-based optimizers (Muon, Soap, Kron) show consistent 1.2×–1.4× speedups (Figure 3). The engineering effort to integrate these methods is justified by meaningful compute savings.
- **Large-batch training (≥0.4M tokens):** Matrix methods outperform variance-reduced methods in this regime (Section 2 comparison with Semenov et al.).
- **Data budget ≥4× Chinchilla:** Soap and Kron overtake Muon at high data-to-model ratios (Figure 4, right). If overtraining within a fixed compute budget, prefer Soap or Kron.

**Not recommended:**
- **Model size ≥1.2B parameters:** Speedup diminishes to ~1.1× (Figure 4, mid). At frontier scales, the marginal gain may not justify integration complexity, stability risks, or memory overhead.
- **Small-batch training (<0.1M tokens):** Variance-reduced methods (Mars, Cautious) may outperform matrix methods in noise-dominated regimes.
- **Stability-critical applications:** If training failures are costly, AdamW's proven reliability may outweigh modest speedup from novel methods.

#### 7.3.2 When to Use Variance-Reduced Adam Variants

**Recommended scenarios:**
- **Small-batch regimes (0.02M–0.1M tokens):** Mars and Cautious show advantages in noise-dominated settings per Semenov et al. [2025] comparison.
- **Minimal engineering overhead:** Cautious AdamW requires "one line of code" changes (Liang et al. [2025]), making it attractive for teams unable to integrate complex matrix operations.
- **Model size 300M–1.2B:** NAdamW shows consistent ~1.1× speedup with lower implementation complexity than matrix methods (Figure 4, mid).

**Not recommended:**
- **Large-batch training (≥0.4M tokens):** Variance reduction benefits diminish as gradient noise decreases.
- **Claims of 2× speedup:** This paper shows variance-reduced methods achieve <1.2× speedup with proper baseline tuning (Figure 3).

#### 7.3.3 Hyperparameter Tuning Checklist

Based on the paper's methodology (Section 3.2–3.4), practitioners should:

1. **Never transfer hyperparameters across optimizers.** Optimal learning rates and weight decay values differ significantly (Figure 1, top right: Lion WD≈0.6 vs. AdamW WD≈0.1).

2. **Tune scaling-sensitive parameters first.** Table 4 identifies which hyperparameters shift with scale for each optimizer. For AdamW, prioritize learning rate, warmup, weight decay, and batch size.

3. **Use coordinate descent with convergence criteria.** Accept hyperparameter changes only if validation loss improves by at least $3 \times 10^{-3}$ (Phase I threshold).

4. **Evaluate at end-of-training.** Do not judge optimizer performance at intermediate checkpoints—loss curves cross during learning rate decay (Figure 5).

5. **Test multiple data-to-model ratios.** An optimizer that wins at 1× Chinchilla may lose at 8× (Figure 4, right). Match optimizer choice to your actual training budget.

| **Implementation complexity** | Low (standard) | High (matrix ops) | Low–Medium | Low |
| **Memory overhead** | Baseline | +10–30% (preconditioners) | Baseline | −30–50% |
| **Stability track record** | Extensive | Limited (≤1.2B) | Moderate | Moderate |

**Hyperparameter Tables.** Appendices C–F (Tables 36–183) provide exhaustive hyperparameter ablation results for all optimizers across all tested regimes. This is unusually thorough—most optimizer papers report only final configurations. These tables enable:
- Warm-start tuning for practitioners (use reported optima as initialization)
- Sensitivity analysis (see how loss changes when deviating from optimal values)
- Meta-analysis of hyperparameter trends across optimizer classes

**Recommended Reproduction Protocol.** Teams seeking to replicate or extend this work should:
1. Use the same Llama 2 architecture variants (Table 2) for comparability
2. Apply the three-phase tuning protocol rather than single-pass tuning
3. Report speedup at multiple model scales, not just one
4. Evaluate at end-of-training, not intermediate checkpoints
5. Disclose batch size and data loader randomization details (critical per Sophia analysis in Appendix B.2)

### 7.5 The Path Forward: A New Optimizer Research Paradigm

This paper's most enduring contribution is not any specific speedup number, but the **methodological framework** it establishes. The field should move toward a new paradigm where:

1. **Optimizer papers include scale-ablation as standard.** Claims of speedup must be validated at multiple model sizes, with explicit discussion of whether advantages persist at frontier scales.

2. **Baseline tuning effort is matched.** Proposed optimizers and baselines must receive comparable hyperparameter optimization effort. Papers should report tuning budgets (number of runs, search space size) for transparency.

3. **Evaluation regimes are explicit.** Papers should specify data-to-model ratios, batch sizes, and architecture families tested, with clear statements about where findings may not generalize.

4. **Wall-clock time is reported alongside data efficiency.** Data efficiency alone is insufficient for deployment decisions—implementation overhead matters.

5. **Negative results are published.** This paper's finding that speedups diminish with scale is itself valuable—it prevents wasted effort pursuing methods that don't deliver at production scales. The field needs more honest assessments of where methods fail, not just where they succeed.

**Final Takeaway.** The question is no longer "which optimizer is best?" but rather "**under what conditions does each optimizer provide meaningful gains, and do those gains persist at production scales?**" This paper provides the methodological tools to answer that question rigorously. Future optimizer research that adopts this framework will produce more reliable, actionable findings—even if the specific numbers differ from those reported here.
