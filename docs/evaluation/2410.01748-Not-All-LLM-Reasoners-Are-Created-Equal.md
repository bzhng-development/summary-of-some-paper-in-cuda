## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces **Compositional GSM**, a diagnostic benchmark that chains two Grade-School Math (GSM8K) problems such that the answer to the first question ($Q_1$) becomes a variable in the second ($Q_2$), revealing a critical "reasoning gap" where models fail to combine known concepts despite high individual accuracy. The study demonstrates that while frontier models like **GPT-4o** maintain robustness, smaller and cost-efficient models (e.g., **GPT-4o mini**, **Phi-3-mini**) exhibit reasoning gaps up to **12× larger** than their expensive counterparts, and math-specialized models like **Qwen2.5-MATH-7B-IT** score below **60%** on these compositional tasks despite exceeding **80%** on harder standalone benchmarks. These findings matter because they prove that high scores on standard benchmarks like GSM8K often mask superficial pattern matching and distraction vulnerabilities, particularly in the smaller, widely deployed models relied upon for practical applications.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Illusion of Mathematical Mastery
The primary motivation for this work stems from a growing disconnect between the reported performance of Large Language Models (LLMs) on standard benchmarks and their actual reasoning capabilities. In recent years, models have achieved near-perfect scores on grade-school math datasets like **GSM8K** (Cobbe et al., 2021), leading to a widespread belief that LLMs have "mastered" elementary arithmetic and logic. However, the authors argue that this mastery may be illusory. The central problem addressed is whether these models truly grasp underlying mathematical concepts or if they are merely engaging in **superficial pattern recognition**—memorizing the structure of common word problems without understanding the logical dependencies required to solve novel variations.

If an LLM has genuinely learned the concept of "finding a variable and substituting it," it should seamlessly handle a problem that chains two known concepts together. The paper posits that if models are truly reasoning, their performance on a composite task should be predictable based on their performance on the individual components. The failure to meet this expectation reveals a fundamental brittleness in current reasoning architectures.

### The Real-World Stakes: Reliability of Cost-Efficient Models
This investigation is not merely theoretical; it has significant practical implications for the deployment of AI. The industry is rapidly shifting toward **cost-efficient, smaller models** (e.g., `GPT-4o mini`, `Phi-3`, `Gemma`) for widespread application due to their lower latency and inference costs. These models often match frontier models on standard benchmarks through techniques like knowledge distillation and optimized data mixtures.

However, if these smaller models rely heavily on pattern matching rather than robust reasoning, they pose a reliability risk in real-world scenarios where problems rarely appear in the exact format seen during training. For instance, in automated tutoring systems or financial analysis tools, a model might correctly solve isolated steps but fail catastrophically when those steps are interdependent. The paper highlights that this "reasoning gap" is disproportionately large in smaller, cost-efficient models, suggesting that current optimization strategies may be sacrificing generalization for benchmark scores.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Before this work, researchers attempted to probe the robustness of LLM reasoning through several methods, each with specific shortcomings:

*   **Test-Set Leakage Checks:** Recent studies (e.g., Zhang et al., 2024) examined "held-out" private datasets to check if models were simply memorizing the GSM8K test set. While some open-weight models showed signs of overfitting, frontier models generally performed well, leaving the question of *why* they fail on slightly harder tasks unanswered.
*   **Adversarial Perturbations:** Other works introduced adversarial examples or semantic substitutions (changing names or numbers slightly) to test robustness. While effective at breaking models, these approaches often create "unnatural" problems that do not reflect how humans compose logic.
*   **Synthetic Compositional Tasks:** Previous research on compositional generalization often relied on synthetic, formal languages (e.g., logical puzzles or arithmetic expressions) rather than natural language word problems. These settings, while controlled, lack the linguistic complexity and ambiguity of real-world grade-school math problems.

The critical gap in prior work is the lack of a **naturalistic, multi-hop diagnostic** that isolates reasoning depth without changing the difficulty level of the individual steps. Existing benchmarks either test single-step recall or introduce entirely new, harder domains (like high-school competition math), making it difficult to pinpoint whether a failure is due to a lack of knowledge or a lack of compositional ability.

### Positioning: Compositional GSM as a Diagnostic Tool
This paper positions itself not as the creator of a new "leaderboard" to be maximized, but as a **case study** to stress-test the nature of reasoning. The authors introduce **Compositional GSM**, a novel evaluation framework that constructs problems by chaining two existing GSM8K test questions ($Q_1$ and $Q_2$) such that the answer to $Q_1$ becomes a variable $X$ in $Q_2$ (see Figure 2).

This approach offers three distinct advantages over prior methods:
1.  **Controlled Difficulty:** Since both $Q_1$ and $Q_2$ are drawn from the standard GSM8K test set, the individual cognitive load remains at the grade-school level. Any performance drop cannot be attributed to the complexity of the math itself.
2.  **Quantifiable Expectation:** The paper defines a clear baseline for success. If a model solves $Q_1$ with accuracy $S_1$ and $Q_2$ with accuracy $S_2$, the expected accuracy on the compositional task is simply the product $S_1 \times S_2$. The **Reasoning Gap** ($\Delta$) is defined as the difference between the actual compositional accuracy and this expected value:
    $$ \Delta = S_{comp} - (S_1 \times S_2) $$
    This metric allows for a precise measurement of how much the "chaining" process degrades performance.
3.  **Isolation of Failure Modes:** By comparing performance on independent vs. compositional tasks, the authors can distinguish between **distraction** (failing $Q_1$ because $Q_2$ is present in the context) and **poor second-hop reasoning** (solving $Q_1$ correctly but failing to substitute the result into $Q_2$).

Unlike works that aim to "solve" a benchmark, this study uses Compositional GSM to reveal that high scores on standard metrics like GSM8K can obscure systematic flaws, particularly in the smaller models that are increasingly becoming the backbone of deployed AI systems.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a diagnostic evaluation framework rather than a new model architecture, utilizing a "stress-test" methodology where the core idea is to construct multi-hop reasoning problems by chaining two existing grade-school math questions to measure the deviation between expected and actual performance.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The "system" described here is not a neural network being trained, but a rigorous data construction and evaluation pipeline designed to generate **Compositional GSM** problems by linking two independent math word problems into a single dependent chain. This approach solves the problem of distinguishing between genuine reasoning and superficial pattern matching by creating a test where the answer to the first question becomes a mandatory variable for the second, forcing the model to perform explicit intermediate computation rather than relying on memorized problem templates.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The evaluation pipeline consists of four distinct logical components that flow sequentially:
1.  **Source Data Selector**: Extracts pairs of questions ($Q_1$ and $Q_2$) from the standard GSM8K test set, ensuring they are solvable independently.
2.  **Compositional Constructor**: Modifies the text of $Q_2$ to replace a specific numerical constant with the variable $X$ (the answer to $Q_1$) and algorithmically recalculates the correct final answer for the new composite problem.
3.  **Prompt Engine**: Formats the chained questions into specific 8-shot prompt templates (either natural language or code-based) that instruct the model to solve $Q_1$, extract $X$, and then solve $Q_2$.
4.  **Metric Calculator**: Computes the **Reasoning Gap** ($\Delta$) by comparing the model's actual accuracy on the composite task against the theoretical probability of success derived from its independent performance on $Q_1$ and $Q_2$.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
To fully understand how this diagnostic works, we will proceed in the following order:
*   First, we define the mathematical formalism of the **Reasoning Gap**, establishing the baseline expectation for a perfect reasoner.
*   Second, we detail the **Data Construction Pipeline**, explaining exactly how questions are paired, modified, and validated to ensure logical consistency.
*   Third, we describe the **Evaluation Protocols**, including the specific 8-shot prompting strategies and the distinction between natural language and code-generation modes.
*   Finally, we outline the **Control Experiments**, such as the "modified GSM" test, which are critical for ruling out test-set leakage as a confounding factor.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Mathematical Formalism: Defining the Reasoning Gap**
The central metric of this study is the **Reasoning Gap** ($\Delta$), which quantifies the extent to which a model fails to compose two known skills.
*   Let $S_1$ represent the model's accuracy on the set of first questions ($Q_1$) when presented independently.
*   Let $S_2$ represent the model's accuracy on the set of second questions ($Q_2$) when presented independently (with the variable $X$ already substituted or the original number present).
*   If a model possesses true compositional reasoning abilities, the probability of it solving the chained problem ($Q_1 \rightarrow Q_2$) should be the product of its probabilities of solving each step individually, assuming independence.
*   Therefore, the **expected compositional accuracy** is defined as $S_{expected} = S_1 \times S_2$.
*   The paper defines the actual measured accuracy on the compositional test set as $S_{comp}$.
*   The **Reasoning Gap** is then calculated using the equation:
    $$ \Delta = S_{comp} - (S_1 \times S_2) $$
*   In this formulation, a value of $\Delta = 0$ indicates perfect compositional consistency (the model performs exactly as well as its individual components predict), while a negative $\Delta$ indicates a systematic failure to chain reasoning steps.
*   The authors note that in Figure 1, the x-axis represents the geometric mean $\sqrt{S_1 \times S_2}$ (labeled "GSM8K Accuracy" for simplicity), and the trend line $y = x^2$ represents the theoretical expectation $S_1 \times S_2$ assuming $S_1 \approx S_2$.

**Data Construction Pipeline: Creating Compositional GSM**
The creation of the **Compositional GSM** dataset involves a precise, multi-step algorithmic process to ensure that the resulting problems are logically sound and statistically comparable to the original benchmark.
*   **Source Selection**: The authors select pairs of questions from a subset of 1,200 examples from the original GSM8K test split.
*   **Variable Substitution**: For each pair, the answer to the first question ($Q_1$) is designated as a variable $X$. The text of the second question ($Q_2$) is then modified by replacing a specific numerical constant with the symbol $X$.
*   **Constraint Enforcement**: The choice of which number to replace in $Q_2$ is not random; it is selected such that the new final answer of $Q_2$ remains a **positive integer** and is "not too far" in magnitude from the original answer of $Q_2$. This constraint ensures that the difficulty level of the arithmetic does not artificially inflate due to unwieldy numbers.
*   **Answer Recalculation**: To obtain the ground truth for the new composite problem, the authors do not rely on human re-solving. Instead, they utilize **code-form solutions** (Python scripts) derived from prior work (Gao et al., 2023). They programmatically replace the constant in the code solution of $Q_2$ with the value $X$ and execute the code to generate the new correct final answer automatically.
*   **Distribution Matching**: The authors verified that this modification process preserves the statistical properties of the original dataset. As shown in **Figure A.1** (Appendix A), the distribution of final answer magnitudes (on a log scale) for the Compositional GSM test set closely mirrors that of the original GSM8K test set, confirming that the task difficulty has not shifted in terms of numerical scale.
*   **Logical Validation**: To ensure the modified questions remain sensible in natural language (e.g., avoiding scenarios where substituting $X$ creates a physical impossibility), the authors employed a filtering step. They generated 16 candidate solutions per modified question using **GPT-4o** and **Gemini 1.5 Pro**. If fewer than 4 out of the 16 models agreed with the code-executed final answer, the question was flagged for manual review and modification. Approximately **25%** of the generated questions required this manual intervention to ensure logical coherence.

**Evaluation Protocols: Prompting and Inference**
The evaluation strategy is designed to test models under standardized conditions while probing different reasoning modalities (natural language vs. code).
*   **Test Sets**: Each model is evaluated on three distinct sets, each containing **1,200 examples**:
    1.  The original GSM8K test split (to measure $S_1$).
    2.  The modified GSM8K test split where $X$ is substituted but $Q_1$ is not present (to measure $S_2$).
    3.  The full Compositional GSM test set containing the chained $Q_1 \rightarrow Q_2$ structure (to measure $S_{comp}$).
*   **Prompting Strategy**: The authors utilize an **8-shot prompting** format for all evaluations, following the methodology of Zhang et al. (2024).
    *   For the original and modified splits, a standard 8-shot prompt (see **Appendix D**) is used, providing examples of questions and step-by-step solutions ending with "The final answer is ANSWER."
    *   For the Compositional GSM split, a specialized 8-shot prompt (see **Appendix E**) is constructed. This prompt explicitly instructs the model: "Let X be the answer to Q1... Solve it and use the value of X to solve Q2." The few-shot examples demonstrate the required output format: first solving $Q_1$, stating "The Q1 answer is ANSWER1," and then proceeding to solve $Q_2$ using that value.
*   **Inference Configuration**: All models are sampled with a **temperature of 0** (greedy decoding) to ensure deterministic outputs for accurate accuracy measurement. Performance is reported using the **pass@1** metric, meaning the model must produce the correct final answer on its first attempt.
*   **Code Generation Mode**: In addition to natural language Chain-of-Thought (CoT), the authors evaluate a **code-generation** mode (Section 3.5).
    *   In this mode, the prompt (see **Appendix F**) instructs the model to write two Python functions: `solve_q1()` and `solution()`.
    *   The `solution()` function must explicitly begin with the line `X = solve_q1()`, enforcing the dependency structure in code.
    *   This approach leverages the precision of code execution to verify if the model can structurally enforce the reasoning chain even if its natural language generation is flawed.

**Control Experiments: Isolating Failure Modes**
A critical component of the technical approach is the series of control experiments designed to rule out alternative explanations for poor performance, specifically **test-set leakage** and **context distraction**.
*   **Leakage Control (Modified GSM)**: To test if models are simply memorizing the GSM8K test set, the authors evaluate models on the "Modified GSM" set (the $Q_2$ questions with $X$ substituted but without the $Q_1$ context).
    *   As shown in **Figure 9**, most models perform nearly identically on the Original GSM8K and the Modified GSM8K (points lie close to the $x=y$ line).
    *   This result technically demonstrates that the drop in performance on Compositional GSM is **not** due to the models failing to recognize the modified $Q_2$ because of memorization; they can solve the modified math when presented in isolation.
*   **Distraction Control (Q1 in Context)**: To determine if the presence of $Q_2$ distracts the model from solving $Q_1$, the authors compare the accuracy of $Q_1$ when solved alone versus when solved as part of the compositional pair.
    *   **Figure 10** plots "% Q1 Solved in Non-Compositional format" against "% Q1 Solved in Compositional format."
    *   Models falling below the diagonal indicate **distraction**: they fail to solve $Q_1$ correctly simply because $Q_2$ is present in the prompt, even though $Q_1$'s solution does not depend on $Q_2$.
*   **Second-Hop Reasoning Control**: To isolate failures in the second step, the authors analyze the conditional accuracy: "Given that $Q_1$ is solved correctly, how often is $Q_2$ solved correctly?"
    *   **Figure 11** compares the independent accuracy of $Q_2$ against this conditional accuracy.
    *   A gap here indicates **poor second-hop reasoning**: the model successfully computes $X$ but fails to substitute it or reason through the second step in the chained context, suggesting a breakdown in maintaining the logical state across the two hops.
*   **Independence Control**: Finally, **Figure 12** tests models on a scenario where $Q_2$ is present in the context but is **independent** of $Q_1$ (i.e., $Q_2$ does not use $X$).
    *   The results show that distraction from $Q_1$ is minimal when $Q_2$ is independent, confirming that the primary failure mode is specifically the **dependency** on the intermediate answer $X$, not merely the increased context length.

**Design Choices and Rationale**
The authors made several non-obvious design choices to ensure the validity of their findings.
*   **Why Chain Existing Questions?** Instead of generating entirely new synthetic problems, the authors chose to chain existing GSM8K questions. This ensures that the linguistic complexity and mathematical concepts remain within the "grade-school" domain, isolating the **compositional** variable as the sole source of increased difficulty.
*   **Why Code-Form Solutions for Ground Truth?** By using code execution to determine the new final answers, the authors eliminate human error in answer key generation and ensure that the "correct" answer is mathematically rigorous regardless of how complex the substitution makes the arithmetic.
*   **Why Temperature 0?** Using greedy decoding (temperature 0) removes stochastic variance, ensuring that the measured "Reasoning Gap" reflects a systematic inability to reason compositionally rather than random sampling noise.
*   **Why Not Just Measure Final Accuracy?** Reporting only $S_{comp}$ would be misleading because a model with low base competence ($S_1, S_2$) would naturally have a low $S_{comp}$. The $\Delta$ metric normalizes for base competence, allowing for a fair comparison between a massive model (high $S_1$) and a small model (low $S_1$) regarding their specific ability to **chain** thoughts.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper moves beyond simply reporting a new benchmark score; it fundamentally challenges the assumption that high performance on standard math benchmarks equates to robust reasoning. The following insights represent the core conceptual shifts driven by the Compositional GSM framework.

### 4.1 The "Cost-Efficiency Paradox": Smaller Models Trade Reasoning for Pattern Matching
The most striking and practically significant finding is the inverse relationship between model efficiency and compositional robustness. While the industry has successfully optimized smaller models (e.g., `GPT-4o mini`, `Phi-3-mini`, `Gemma2-9B`) to match frontier models on single-step benchmarks like GSM8K, this optimization appears to come at the cost of deep reasoning capabilities.

*   **The Innovation:** Prior work often assumed that scaling laws or knowledge distillation would preserve reasoning capabilities proportionally. This paper reveals a **discontinuity**: smaller models exhibit reasoning gaps up to **12× larger** than their larger counterparts despite having nearly identical scores on the original GSM8K test. For instance, **Figure 4** shows that while `GPT-4o mini` achieves ~90% accuracy on standard GSM8K (comparable to `GPT-4o`), its reasoning gap is **14.2%**, whereas `GPT-4o`'s gap is only **1.1%**.
*   **Why It Matters:** This suggests that current techniques for creating cost-efficient models (such as distillation from larger teachers or training on synthetic data) may be teaching models to recognize **superficial statistical patterns** specific to the GSM8K distribution rather than the underlying logical operators required for multi-hop inference.
*   **Distinction from Prior Work:** Previous studies on model scaling (e.g., Kaplan et al., 2020) generally showed smooth improvements in capability as size increased. This work identifies a specific **failure mode** in small models that is invisible to standard benchmarks: they are "good enough" to solve isolated problems but brittle when those problems are chained. This is a fundamental warning for deploying small models in complex, real-world workflows where tasks are rarely isolated.

### 4.2 Instruction Tuning as a Double-Edged Sword for Generalization
The paper uncovers a counter-intuitive phenomenon regarding instruction tuning (IT): while IT drastically improves performance on standard benchmarks for small models, it actively harms their ability to generalize to compositional tasks compared to their pretrained (PT) counterparts.

*   **The Innovation:** The analysis in **Figure 5** demonstrates a divergence in learning dynamics based on model size. For small models (e.g., `Mistral-7B`, `LLAMA3-8B`), instruction tuning yields massive gains on original GSM8K (+14.1% to +25.1%) but negligible or minimal gains on Compositional GSM (+3.1% to +4.8%). In contrast, larger models (e.g., `LLAMA3-70B`) show consistent improvements across both settings.
*   **Why It Matters:** This implies that standard instruction-tuning recipes for smaller models may be **over-optimizing** for the specific format and distribution of common benchmarks (like GSM8K), effectively "memorizing" the solution style rather than learning the abstract reasoning process. The model becomes a specialist in answering *known* question types but loses the flexibility to adapt when the context changes (i.e., when a second question is appended).
*   **Distinction from Prior Work:** Conventionally, instruction tuning is viewed as an unalloyed good that aligns models with human intent and improves follow-through. This paper provides evidence that for smaller parameter counts, IT can induce **task-specific overfitting**, creating a false sense of competence that disappears immediately upon encountering a novel compositional structure.

### 4.3 Math Specialization Does Not Equate to Compositional Robustness
A common belief in the field is that models specifically fine-tuned on mathematical datasets (Math-Specialized LLMs) possess superior reasoning architectures. This paper debunks that assumption, showing that specialization often leads to rigid overfitting.

*   **The Innovation:** The evaluation of state-of-the-art math models like `Qwen2.5-MATH-7B-IT` and `NuminaMath-7B-CoT` reveals that they suffer from reasoning gaps comparable to, or even worse than, general-purpose models of the same size. **Figure 6** highlights that `Qwen2.5-MATH-7B-IT`, despite scoring >80% on the difficult MATH benchmark (high-school competition level), solves **less than 60%** of compositional grade-school problems.
*   **Why It Matters:** This indicates that "math capability" as measured by hard, standalone problems is orthogonal to "compositional reasoning." These specialized models appear to have learned to recognize complex problem templates and apply specific solution heuristics, but they lack the **fluid logical chaining** required to combine simple concepts. A model can be an expert at solving hard integrals but fail to chain two simple arithmetic steps if the presentation deviates from its training distribution.
*   **Distinction from Prior Work:** Previous evaluations of math models focused almost exclusively on accuracy against harder datasets (like MATH or GSM-Hard). This work introduces a new dimension of evaluation: **structural flexibility**. It proves that being "smart" (solving hard problems) is not the same as being "robust" (solving chained simple problems).

### 4.4 Code Generation as a Structural Crutch for Small Models
The paper identifies a unique interaction between reasoning modality (natural language vs. code) and model size, revealing that code generation acts as a compensatory mechanism for weaker reasoning in smaller models.

*   **The Innovation:** While generating Python code generally improves performance across all models, the relative benefit is disproportionately high for smaller models. **Figure 8** shows that for `Mistral-7B-IT`, switching from natural language Chain-of-Thought (CoT) to code generation yields a **149%** relative improvement on Compositional GSM, compared to only a **71%** improvement for the larger `Mixtral-8x7B-IT`.
*   **Why It Matters:** This suggests that smaller models struggle to maintain the logical state ($X$) in the unstructured space of natural language, where they are prone to distraction and hallucination. Code provides a **rigid syntactic structure** that forces the model to explicitly define the dependency (`X = solve_q1()`), effectively offloading the "memory" of the intermediate step to the code interpreter.
*   **Distinction from Prior Work:** Prior research (e.g., Gao et al., 2023) established that code aids reasoning. This paper refines that understanding by showing that code is not just a general booster but a **critical scaffold** for smaller models that lack the internal capacity to manage multi-hop dependencies in natural language. It highlights a fundamental difference in *how* small and large models reason: large models can hold the chain in context, while small models require external structural enforcement.

### 4.5 Diagnosing the Root Cause: Distraction vs. Second-Hop Failure
Rather than attributing failures to a vague lack of "intelligence," the paper precisely dissects the failure into two distinct mechanistic causes: **context distraction** and **second-hop reasoning collapse**.

*   **The Innovation:** Through controlled ablation studies (Figures 10, 11, and 12), the authors isolate the failure points:
    1.  **Distraction:** Models often fail $Q_1$ simply because $Q_2$ is present in the prompt, even though $Q_1$ is independent (Figure 10). This contradicts the assumption that extra context is neutral.
    2.  **Second-Hop Collapse:** Even when $Q_1$ is solved correctly, models frequently fail to substitute the result into $Q_2$ (Figure 11). The gap between independent $Q_2$ accuracy and conditional $Q_2$ accuracy (given correct $Q_1$) proves that the failure is not due to the difficulty of $Q_2$, but the **integration step**.
*   **Why It Matters:** This granular diagnosis rules out **test-set leakage** (confirmed by Figure 9, where models solve modified $Q_2$ perfectly in isolation) as the primary culprit. Instead, it points to architectural limitations in **attention mechanisms** or **state tracking** within the transformer, where the presence of a dependent second task disrupts the processing of the first, or the hand-off between steps is lost.
*   **Distinction from Prior Work:** Many prior robustness studies attribute failures to data contamination or adversarial perturbations. This work demonstrates that even with clean, non-adversarial data, the mere act of **composing** two valid reasoning steps creates a cognitive load that current LLMs (especially smaller ones) cannot reliably manage.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence provided in the paper, moving from the high-level trends to the specific numerical breakdowns that validate the core claims. The authors employ a rigorous comparative methodology, evaluating a diverse suite of models across three distinct test conditions to isolate the "reasoning gap."

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

**Model Portfolio and Diversity**
The study evaluates a broad spectrum of **23 distinct models**, ensuring the findings are not artifacts of a single architecture or training regime. The portfolio includes:
*   **Frontier Closed-Source Models:** `GPT-4o`, `GPT-4o mini`, `Gemini 1.5 Pro`, `Gemini 1.5 Flash`, and `Gemini 1.0 Pro`.
*   **Open-Weight General Models:** The `LLAMA3` family (8B and 70B, both Pretrained/PT and Instruction-Tuned/IT), `Gemma2` (9B and 27B, PT and IT), `Mistral-7B`, `Mixtral-8x7B`, and `Phi` (Phi-2 and Phi-3-mini).
*   **Math-Specialized Models:** `NuminaMath-7B-CoT`, `Mathstral-7B`, and `Qwen2.5-MATH` (7B and 72B).

This selection allows for direct comparisons across **size** (e.g., 7B vs. 70B), **training objective** (PT vs. IT), and **domain specialization** (General vs. Math).

**Datasets and Test Conditions**
As detailed in Section 3, the evaluation relies on three carefully constructed datasets, each containing **1,200 examples**:
1.  **Original GSM8K:** The standard test split to establish baseline competence ($S_1$).
2.  **Modified GSM8K:** Questions from the test split where a specific number is replaced by a variable $X$, but presented *without* the preceding $Q_1$. This measures the model's ability to handle the modified syntax in isolation ($S_2$).
3.  **Compositional GSM:** The chained format where the model must solve $Q_1$ to derive $X$, then use $X$ to solve $Q_2$. This measures actual compositional performance ($S_{comp}$).

**Metrics and Inference Protocol**
The primary metric is **accuracy** (pass@1), measured with **temperature 0** (greedy decoding) to eliminate stochastic variance. The core analytical metric is the **Reasoning Gap** ($\Delta$), defined in Equation 1:
$$ \Delta = S_{comp} - (S_1 \times S_2) $$
A negative $\Delta$ indicates the model performs worse on the chained task than probability theory would predict based on its individual step capabilities. The authors also utilize **relative improvement** percentages when comparing prompting strategies (e.g., Code vs. Natural Language).

### 5.2 Quantitative Results: The Reasoning Gap

The central finding of the paper is visualized in **Figure 1** and **Figure 3**, which plot Compositional GSM accuracy against standard GSM8K accuracy. The trend line $y = x^2$ represents the expected performance if models were perfect compositional reasoners (assuming $S_1 \approx S_2$).

**The Universal Gap**
Nearly every model evaluated falls below the $y=x^2$ trend line, indicating a systematic failure to compose reasoning steps.
*   **Frontier Models:** Even the most capable models exhibit a gap. `GPT-4o` and `Gemini 1.5 Pro` sit closest to the ideal line, but still show deviations.
*   **The Scale of Failure:** **Figure 3** quantifies this gap ($\Delta$) explicitly. While `GPT-4o` has a gap of only **1.1%**, smaller or cost-efficient models suffer significantly more. For instance, `GPT-4o mini` has a gap of **14.2%**, and `Phi-3-mini-4k-IT` exhibits a gap of roughly **20-25%** (visually estimated from Figure 3 relative to others).

**The Cost-Efficiency Paradox (Figure 4)**
The paper provides a stark numerical comparison between "high-cost" and "low-cost" variants within the same model families. This data directly supports the claim that efficiency optimizations may compromise reasoning depth.
*   **GPT Family:** `GPT-4o` achieves a reasoning gap of **1.1%**. Its cheaper counterpart, `GPT-4o mini`, while matching it on standard GSM8K (~90% accuracy), sees its gap explode to **14.2%**. This is a **~13x increase** in the reasoning error rate.
*   **Gemini Family:** `Gemini 1.5 Pro` has a gap of **5.8%**, whereas `Gemini 1.5 Flash` (the efficient version) has a gap of **11.3%**, nearly double that of the Pro model.
*   **LLAMA3 Family:** The 70B instruction-tuned model (`LLAMA3-70B-IT`) has a gap of **4.9%**, while the 8B version (`LLAMA3-8B-IT`) suffers a gap of **27.5%**. This represents a **5.6x degradation** in compositional reliability despite the 8B model being highly competent on single-step tasks.
*   **Gemma2 Family:** The disparity is even more pronounced here. `Gemma2-27B-IT` has a gap of **18%**, while the smaller `Gemma2-9B-IT` has a massive gap of **37.3%**.

These numbers confirm that smaller models are not just "slightly worse"; they are fundamentally less reliable when tasks require chaining logic, with error rates on compositional tasks that are an order of magnitude higher than their larger counterparts.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Deep Dives

The authors conduct several critical ablation studies to rule out alternative explanations and pinpoint the mechanisms of failure.

#### 5.3.1 Ruling Out Test-Set Leakage (Figure 9)
A common critique of benchmark failures is that models have memorized the test set. If this were true, modifying the numbers in $Q_2$ should cause a massive drop in performance even in isolation.
*   **Experiment:** Compare accuracy on **Original GSM8K** vs. **Modified GSM8K** (where numbers are changed but no $Q_1$ is present).
*   **Result:** **Figure 9** shows that almost all models lie extremely close to the $x=y$ diagonal. For example, `GPT-4o`, `LLAMA3-70B`, and `Gemma2-27B` show virtually identical performance on both sets.
*   **Conclusion:** The drop in Compositional GSM performance is **not** due to an inability to handle modified numbers or test-set leakage. The models *can* solve the modified math; they fail only when it is chained to a previous step.

#### 5.3.2 The Impact of Instruction Tuning (Figure 5)
This ablation compares Pretrained (PT) vs. Instruction-Tuned (IT) versions of the same models to see how tuning affects generalization.
*   **Small Models (Top Row of Figure 5):** Instruction tuning provides massive gains on standard GSM8K but fails to translate to compositional tasks.
    *   `Mistral-7B`: IT improves GSM8K by **+14.1%** but Compositional GSM by only **+4.3%**.
    *   `LLAMA3-8B`: IT improves GSM8K by **+25.1%** but Compositional GSM by only **+12.6%**.
    *   `Gemma2-9B`: IT improves GSM8K by **+22.8%** but Compositional GSM by a mere **+4.8%**.
*   **Large Models (Bottom Row of Figure 5):** The trend reverses or stabilizes.
    *   `LLAMA3-70B`: IT improves GSM8K by **+8.6%** and Compositional GSM by **+19.0%**. Here, tuning *helps* compositional reasoning significantly more than single-step reasoning.
    *   `Gemma2-27B`: IT improves GSM8K by **+15.2%** and Compositional GSM by **+17.2%**.
*   **Implication:** For small models, standard instruction tuning appears to overfit to the *format* of single-step benchmarks, actively harming their ability to generalize to multi-hop structures. Large models, conversely, use instruction tuning to better align with complex reasoning directives.

#### 5.3.3 Math Specialization vs. Generalization (Figure 6)
Does training specifically on hard math problems fix this? The results suggest the opposite.
*   **Qwen2.5-MATH-7B-IT:** Despite scoring >80% on the difficult MATH benchmark, it achieves a reasoning gap of **21.9%**.
*   **NuminaMath-7B-CoT:** Shows a gap of **12.1%**.
*   **Comparison:** These specialized models perform similarly to or worse than general-purpose models of the same size (e.g., `LLAMA3-8B-IT` gap of 27.5%).
*   **Conclusion:** Specialization on hard, standalone problems does not teach compositional chaining. In fact, it may induce rigidity, where the model expects a specific "hard problem" template and fails when presented with a chain of "easy" problems.

#### 5.3.4 Overfitting via Supervised Fine-Tuning (Figure 7)
The authors performed a controlled fine-tuning experiment on `Gemma2-27B-PT` using both human and synthetic GSM8K solutions.
*   **Setup:** Training for 50, 100, and 400 steps.
*   **Result:**
    *   At **100 steps**, performance on both GSM8K and Compositional GSM improved.
    *   At **400 steps**, GSM8K accuracy continued to rise, but **Compositional GSM accuracy dropped**.
*   **Interpretation:** This is a classic signature of **task overfitting**. The model learns to maximize the reward signal on the specific training distribution (single-step GSM8K) at the expense of generalization to the compositional format. This warns against the industry trend of "over-training" small models on massive synthetic datasets derived from standard benchmarks.

#### 5.3.5 Code Generation as a Remediation Strategy (Figure 8)
Can changing the output modality help? The paper compares Natural Language Chain-of-Thought (CoT) vs. Python Code Generation.
*   **General Trend:** Code generation improves performance for all models.
*   **Disproportionate Benefit for Small Models:**
    *   `Mistral-7B-IT`: Code yields a **149%** relative improvement over CoT on Compositional GSM.
    *   `Mixtral-8x7B-IT` (Larger): Code yields only a **71%** relative improvement.
    *   `LLAMA3-8B-IT`: Code yields a **69%** improvement, while `LLAMA3-70B-IT` sees only **2%**.
*   **Mechanism:** Code provides a rigid syntactic scaffold (`X = solve_q1()`) that forces the model to explicitly store and retrieve the intermediate state. Small models, which struggle to maintain this state in the unstructured flow of natural language, benefit immensely from this external structure. Large models, which can already manage the state internally, see diminishing returns.

### 5.4 Diagnostic Analysis: Distraction vs. Second-Hop Failure

To understand *why* the gap exists, the authors decompose the failure modes using **Figures 10, 11, and 12**.

**1. Distraction by Context (Figure 10)**
*   **Hypothesis:** Does the mere presence of $Q_2$ confuse the model when solving $Q_1$?
*   **Metric:** Compare % $Q_1$ solved alone vs. % $Q_1$ solved in the compositional pair.
*   **Finding:** Many models fall below the $x=y$ line. For example, `Phi-3-mini` and `Mistral-7B` show noticeable drops.
*   **Qualitative Insight:** The authors note that models often miss details in $Q_1$ (e.g., "per month" vs. "total") when $Q_2$ is present, suggesting that the additional context acts as a **distractor**, degrading attention on the first step.

**2. Second-Hop Reasoning Collapse (Figure 11)**
*   **Hypothesis:** If the model solves $Q_1$ correctly, can it solve $Q_2$?
*   **Metric:** Compare % $Q_2$ solved independently vs. % $Q_2$ solved *given* $Q_1$ was correct.
*   **Finding:** Significant deviations below the diagonal are observed across almost all models.
*   **Implication:** Even when the prerequisite ($X$) is successfully computed, models frequently fail to **substitute** it correctly or execute the second logic step. This indicates a failure in **state tracking** or **variable binding** rather than a lack of mathematical knowledge.

**3. Dependency vs. Independence (Figure 12)**
*   **Hypothesis:** Is the failure due to the *dependency* or just the *length* of the context?
*   **Experiment:** Compare solving $Q_2$ when it is independent of $Q_1$ (just present in context) vs. when it depends on $Q_1$.
*   **Result:** Models perform well when $Q_2$ is independent (bars for "Q2 w/ Q1 in context" are high). Performance crashes only when $Q_2$ *depends* on $Q_1$ ("Q2 | Q1 Solved").
*   **Conclusion:** The bottleneck is specifically the **multi-hop dependency**. The models have the capacity to process two questions simultaneously, but they cannot reliably pass the output of the first as the input to the second.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Validity

**Strengths:**
*   **Isolation of Variables:** By keeping the mathematical difficulty constant (grade-school level) and only changing the *structure* (chaining), the experiments convincingly isolate **compositional reasoning** as the variable of interest.
*   **Robust Controls:** The "Modified GSM" control (Figure 9) effectively rules out test-set leakage, a major confounder in LLM evaluation. The separation of distraction (Fig 10) and second-hop failure (Fig 11) provides a granular diagnosis that prior work lacked.
*   **Diverse Model Coverage:** The inclusion of math-specialized models and the PT/IT ablation adds significant depth, showing that neither specialization nor standard alignment fixes the core issue.

**Limitations and Conditions:**
*   **Scope of Math:** The experiments are limited to **grade-school arithmetic**. While this is a feature for isolation, it remains an open question whether these findings scale to more complex domains (e.g., calculus or physics) where the "steps" are inherently harder.
*   **Prompt Sensitivity:** The results rely on specific 8-shot prompts. While the authors tested preambles (Appendix B), it is possible that more advanced prompting techniques (e.g., Tree-of-Thoughts or automated verification loops) could mitigate the gap, particularly for smaller models. However, the paper's goal is to test *standard* inference capabilities, where these gaps are indeed prevalent.
*   **Code Dependency:** The finding that code helps small models (Figure 8) is conditional on the model's ability to generate syntactically correct Python. For models with weak coding capabilities, this remediation strategy might fail.

**Final Verdict:**
The experiments provide **convincing and robust evidence** that current LLMs, particularly smaller and cost-efficient ones, lack true compositional reasoning capabilities. The data clearly demonstrates that high scores on standard benchmarks like GSM8K are poor predictors of performance on multi-hop tasks. The systematic nature of the failure—evident across families, sizes, and training objectives—suggests this is a fundamental architectural or training dynamic limitation, not a mere artifact of data contamination or prompt engineering.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the Compositional GSM framework provides a rigorous diagnostic for multi-hop reasoning, it is not without constraints. The study's design choices, while necessary to isolate specific failure modes, introduce assumptions and blind spots that limit the generalizability of the findings to broader real-world applications. Furthermore, the proposed mitigations (such as code generation) come with their own computational and functional trade-offs.

### 6.1 Assumptions in Experimental Design

**The Independence Assumption in Probability Modeling**
The core metric of the paper, the **Reasoning Gap** ($\Delta = S_{comp} - S_1 \times S_2$), relies on the probabilistic assumption that solving $Q_1$ and $Q_2$ are independent events for a competent reasoner.
*   **The Constraint:** This assumes that if a model knows how to solve $Q_1$ and $Q_2$ individually, the joint probability of solving them together should strictly be the product of their individual accuracies.
*   **The Limitation:** In reality, cognitive load is not perfectly additive. Even for humans, solving two problems in sequence within a single context window introduces a "working memory" cost that might slightly degrade performance below the theoretical product $S_1 \times S_2$, even without a fundamental reasoning failure. By treating any deviation from $S_1 \times S_2$ as a "gap," the metric may slightly overestimate the severity of the failure for models that are simply sensitive to context length rather than logically brittle. However, given the magnitude of the gaps observed (e.g., **37.3%** for `Gemma2-9B-IT` in **Figure 3**), this statistical nuance does not invalidate the primary conclusion of systematic failure.

**Grade-School Math as a Proxy for General Reasoning**
The study assumes that the ability to chain two grade-school arithmetic problems is a sufficient proxy for general multi-hop reasoning capabilities.
*   **The Constraint:** The mathematical operations involved are limited to basic arithmetic (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division) and simple fractions, as defined by the **GSM8K** dataset.
*   **The Limitation:** It remains an open question whether the "reasoning gap" scales linearly with problem complexity. A model might fail at chaining simple arithmetic due to attention distraction but succeed at chaining complex logical deductions if the semantic cues are stronger. Conversely, the gap might widen exponentially in domains like physics or formal logic where intermediate variables are abstract symbols rather than concrete numbers. The paper explicitly acknowledges this scope limitation in **Section 5**, noting that future work must extend this framework to benchmarks like **MATH** (high-school competition level) to verify if the "cost-efficiency paradox" holds for higher-order reasoning.

**Validity of the "Modified" Control Set**
To rule out test-set leakage, the authors compare performance on Original GSM8K vs. Modified GSM8K (where a number is swapped for $X$).
*   **The Constraint:** This control assumes that substituting a number with a variable $X$ (and recalculating the answer) preserves the *semantic* difficulty of the problem perfectly.
*   **The Limitation:** While **Figure A.1** confirms that the *magnitude* of the final answers remains consistent, the *linguistic* structure of the modified questions changes. For example, replacing "5 bagels" with "X bagels" alters the syntactic flow. If a model is sensitive to specific syntactic patterns rather than just memorizing numbers, the "Modified" set might inadvertently introduce a new confounding variable. The authors mitigated this by manually reviewing **25%** of the questions (**Section 2**), but the possibility remains that some failure attributed to "compositional reasoning" is partially driven by sensitivity to these specific syntactic modifications.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

**Non-Linear and Branched Reasoning Chains**
The Compositional GSM benchmark strictly tests **linear, two-hop reasoning** ($Q_1 \rightarrow Q_2$).
*   **The Gap:** Real-world reasoning often involves **branched** dependencies (e.g., solving $Q_1$ and $Q_2$ independently to answer $Q_3$) or **iterative** loops (where the output of $Q_2$ feeds back into a modified $Q_1$).
*   **Implication:** The paper does not address whether the observed reasoning gaps are specific to linear chains or if they compound catastrophically in deeper trees. A model with a **14.2%** gap on a two-hop task (like `GPT-4o mini` in **Figure 4**) might exhibit near-zero accuracy on a three-hop task, rendering it useless for complex workflow automation. The current study provides a lower bound on reasoning fragility but does not map the decay curve for deeper reasoning depths.

**Multimodal and Code-Heavy Contexts**
The evaluation is confined to text-only mathematical word problems.
*   **The Gap:** Many practical applications of cost-efficient models involve **multimodal inputs** (charts, diagrams) or heavy **codebases** as context.
*   **Implication:** It is unknown if the "distraction" effect observed in **Figure 10** (where the presence of $Q_2$ degrades $Q_1$ performance) is exacerbated by non-textual noise. If a small model is easily distracted by a second text question, it may be completely incapable of filtering irrelevant visual data while performing multi-step logical deduction. The paper suggests extending the framework to multimodal problems in **Section 5**, but currently, these scenarios remain unaddressed.

**Adversarial vs. Naturalistic Distractions**
The "distraction" analyzed in this paper is **structural** (the presence of a dependent second question), not **adversarial**.
*   **The Gap:** The study does not test how these models perform when the second question contains actively misleading information or contradictory premises (adversarial attacks).
*   **Implication:** The results show that models fail even with *helpful* context (a solvable $Q_2$). It is highly probable that under adversarial conditions, the reasoning gap for small models would widen significantly further. The current findings represent a "best-case" scenario for compositional reasoning; real-world robustness is likely worse.

### 6.3 Computational and Data Constraints

**The Cost of Code-Generation Remediation**
The paper identifies **code generation** as a highly effective mitigator for small models, yielding up to **149%** relative improvement for `Mistral-7B-IT` (**Figure 8**). However, this solution introduces significant trade-offs:
*   **Inference Latency & Cost:** Generating executable Python code typically requires more tokens and complex syntactic planning than natural language Chain-of-Thought (CoT). For cost-efficient models deployed in latency-sensitive applications (e.g., real-time tutoring), the overhead of generating and executing code may negate the economic benefits of using a smaller model in the first place.
*   **Execution Environment Dependency:** Unlike natural language reasoning, code generation requires a secure, sandboxed execution environment. This adds engineering complexity and security risks (e.g., code injection) that do not exist with pure text generation.
*   **Model Capability Threshold:** This remediation only works if the model has been trained on code. A small, math-specialized model with no code training (like some versions of `NuminaMath`) cannot leverage this scaffold, leaving it vulnerable to the reasoning gap.

**Data Generation and Validation Overhead**
Creating the Compositional GSM dataset was not trivial, highlighting a scalability constraint for future benchmarking.
*   **The Constraint:** The authors had to programmatically modify questions, recalculate answers via code execution, and then perform a **manual review of ~25%** of the generated questions to ensure logical sensibility (**Section 2**).
*   **Implication:** Scaling this diagnostic to other domains (e.g., legal reasoning, medical diagnosis) or larger datasets would require significant human-in-the-loop validation to ensure that the "chained" problems remain logically coherent. Automated generation alone risks creating nonsensical compound problems that confound the evaluation.

### 6.4 Open Questions and Weaknesses

**The "Black Box" of Instruction Tuning Dynamics**
While the paper conclusively shows that instruction tuning (IT) harms compositional generalization in small models (**Figure 5**), it does not explain *why*.
*   **The Open Question:** Is this due to the specific data mixture used in IT (e.g., an overrepresentation of single-turn Q&A pairs)? Or is it an inherent limitation of optimizing smaller parameter counts for alignment?
*   **Weakness:** Without identifying the specific training signals that cause this divergence, it is difficult for practitioners to adjust their fine-tuning recipes to fix the issue. The paper diagnoses the symptom (overfitting to single-step formats) but leaves the cure (modified training objectives) as future work.

**Generalizability Beyond "Positive Integer" Constraints**
The data construction pipeline enforced a constraint that all final answers must be **positive integers** not too far from the original magnitudes (**Section 2**).
*   **The Open Question:** Does the reasoning gap persist when intermediate variables ($X$) are floating-point numbers, negative values, or large magnitudes?
*   **Weakness:** Small models often struggle with numerical precision. It is possible that the observed gap is partially driven by the models' inability to handle the specific numerical properties of the substituted $X$, rather than the logical chaining itself. If $X$ were a decimal, the arithmetic error rate might increase, conflating "reasoning failure" with "calculation error."

**Lack of Internal State Analysis**
The study relies entirely on **input-output behavior** (accuracy metrics).
*   **The Weakness:** It does not analyze the internal attention maps or hidden states of the models to see *where* the information about $X$ is lost. Does the model attend to $Q_1$ correctly but fail to bind the value to $X$? Or does it simply ignore $Q_1$ entirely when $Q_2$ appears?
*   **Implication:** Without mechanistic interpretability, the distinction between "distraction" and "second-hop failure" remains a behavioral hypothesis. Understanding the internal mechanism would be crucial for designing architectural fixes (e.g., improved attention heads for variable binding) rather than just prompting workarounds.

In summary, while **Compositional GSM** effectively exposes a critical fragility in current LLMs, particularly smaller ones, it operates within a constrained domain of grade-school math and linear chains. The proposed solution of code generation offers a path forward but introduces latency and engineering trade-offs that may limit its practicality for the very "cost-efficient" use cases these models target. Future research must address whether these findings hold for deeper reasoning chains, non-numeric domains, and whether modified training objectives can close the gap without sacrificing single-step performance.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The findings from **Compositional GSM** extend far beyond a critique of grade-school math benchmarks; they fundamentally alter how the field must approach the evaluation, deployment, and training of Large Language Models (LLMs). By demonstrating that high scores on standard benchmarks can mask severe fragility in multi-hop reasoning—particularly in the smaller, cost-efficient models driving industry adoption—this work forces a paradigm shift from measuring **static competence** to measuring **compositional robustness**.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Evaluation Landscape: From Static to Dynamic Benchmarks

The most immediate impact of this work is the obsolescence of single-step benchmarks (like standard GSM8K) as sufficient proxies for reasoning capability. The paper proves that a model can achieve >90% accuracy on isolated problems while failing catastrophically (>50% error rate relative to expectation) when those same problems are chained.

*   **The End of "Benchmark Saturation":** The field has largely considered GSM8K "solved" by frontier models. This paper re-opens the problem by showing that "solving" a benchmark via pattern matching is distinct from mastering the underlying logic. Future evaluations must adopt **dynamic, generative benchmarks** where test questions are algorithmically constructed at inference time (e.g., chaining $N$ steps) to prevent memorization and measure true generalization.
*   **New Metrics for Reliability:** The **Reasoning Gap** ($\Delta$) introduced in Equation 1 should become a standard metric alongside accuracy. Just as "calibration" measures how well a model's confidence matches its accuracy, the Reasoning Gap measures how well a model's compositional performance matches its component capabilities. A model with high accuracy but a large $\Delta$ should be flagged as unreliable for complex workflows, regardless of its leaderboard ranking.
*   **Stress-Testing Small Models:** The industry trend toward distilling large models into smaller, cheaper variants (e.g., `GPT-4o mini`, `Phi-3`) relies on the assumption that reasoning capabilities scale smoothly. Figure 4 and Figure 5 shatter this assumption, revealing a **discontinuity** where small models lose compositional abilities disproportionately. Future model cards and technical reports must include compositional stress tests to verify that efficiency gains have not come at the cost of logical brittleness.

### 7.2 Directions for Follow-Up Research

This case study opens several critical avenues for future inquiry, moving from diagnosis to mechanism and remedy.

**A. Mechanistic Interpretability of Variable Binding**
The paper identifies **second-hop reasoning collapse** (Figure 11) and **context distraction** (Figure 10) as primary failure modes, but the internal mechanisms remain a black box.
*   **Research Question:** Do smaller models lack specific attention heads dedicated to "variable binding" (linking the output of $Q_1$ to the input slot $X$ in $Q_2$)? Or does the attention mechanism simply dilute the signal of $Q_1$ when $Q_2$ is present?
*   **Proposed Approach:** Future work should apply mechanistic interpretability tools (e.g., activation patching, attention rollouts) to trace the flow of the intermediate value $X$ through the network layers. Identifying the specific circuit failures could lead to architectural modifications, such as dedicated memory slots or modified attention biases, to enforce state persistence across hops.

**B. The "Depth Decay" Curve**
Compositional GSM tests a chain of length two ($Q_1 \rightarrow Q_2$). Real-world tasks often require chains of length $N$ (e.g., coding agents, multi-step data analysis).
*   **Research Question:** How does the reasoning gap scale with chain depth? If a model has a gap of $\Delta$ at depth 2, does performance decay linearly, exponentially, or collapse entirely at depth 3 or 4?
*   **Proposed Approach:** Construct **Compositional GSM-N**, where $Q_1 \rightarrow Q_2 \rightarrow \dots \rightarrow Q_N$. Mapping this decay curve is essential for determining the maximum reliable autonomy of current models. It is hypothesized that smaller models, which already struggle at depth 2, will hit a "reasoning wall" almost immediately, necessitating external orchestration frameworks even for simple tasks.

**C. Instruction Tuning Recipes for Generalization**
Figure 5 reveals that standard instruction tuning (IT) harms compositional generalization in small models while helping large ones. This suggests a misalignment in current training data mixtures.
*   **Research Question:** Can we design "Compositional Instruction Tuning" datasets that explicitly teach models to chain reasoning steps?
*   **Proposed Approach:** Instead of training on isolated Q&A pairs, future IT datasets should include a significant proportion of multi-hop problems where the solution requires explicit intermediate variable extraction and substitution. The goal is to shift the model's inductive bias from "pattern match the final answer" to "execute the procedural chain."

**D. Beyond Arithmetic: Domain Generalization**
While this paper focuses on grade-school math, the underlying failure mode (distraction and state loss) is likely domain-agnostic.
*   **Research Question:** Does the reasoning gap persist in legal reasoning (chaining precedents), medical diagnosis (symptom $\rightarrow$ differential $\rightarrow$ test), or code debugging (error $\rightarrow$ cause $\rightarrow$ fix)?
*   **Proposed Approach:** Extend the Compositional GSM framework to domains like **MATH** (higher-level math), **LegalBench**, or **MedQA**. If the gap persists, it confirms a fundamental architectural limitation in transformers regarding long-range dependency management, not just a quirk of arithmetic word problems.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

For practitioners deploying LLMs, these findings offer immediate guidance on risk management and system design.

**1. Risk Assessment for Autonomous Agents**
Many modern applications rely on **agentic workflows** where an LLM plans and executes a sequence of actions (e.g., "Search for X, then calculate Y, then email Z").
*   **Implication:** This paper provides a theoretical upper bound on the reliability of such agents. If a model has a 20% reasoning gap on a simple two-step math problem, its reliability in a 5-step autonomous workflow will be negligible without external verification.
*   **Actionable Advice:** Before deploying a small/cost-efficient model as an agent, run a **Compositional GSM-style diagnostic** relevant to your domain. If the model exhibits a large gap, it should not be trusted with multi-step autonomy. Instead, it should be restricted to single-step tasks with human or code-based orchestration managing the chain.

**2. Model Selection: Cost vs. Robustness Trade-off**
The paper quantifies the hidden cost of "cheap" models. While `GPT-4o mini` is 25–35× cheaper than `GPT-4o` (Section 3.1), its 14.2% reasoning gap (vs. 1.1% for `GPT-4o`) implies a much higher rate of silent failures in complex tasks.
*   **Implication:** In high-stakes domains (finance, healthcare, legal), the "savings" from using a smaller model may be erased by the cost of error correction, hallucination monitoring, or failed task completion.
*   **Actionable Advice:** Use frontier models for **orchestration and multi-hop planning**, even if smaller models are used for **execution of simple sub-tasks**. Do not assume a small model can reliably plan its own multi-step solution.

**3. Code Generation as a Reliability Scaffold**
Figure 8 demonstrates that forcing small models to generate code can improve compositional accuracy by up to 149%.
*   **Implication:** For applications where latency allows, switching the output modality from natural language to code can act as a "reasoning crutch" for smaller models.
*   **Actionable Advice:** In system design, implement a **hybrid mode**: prompt smaller models to output Python functions for multi-step logic (leveraging the interpreter for state tracking) while reserving natural language CoT for larger, more robust models. This allows the use of cost-efficient models in complex workflows, provided the infrastructure supports secure code execution.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

For researchers and engineers wishing to adopt this diagnostic framework, the following integration path is recommended:

*   **When to Prefer This Method:** Use Compositional GSM diagnostics when:
    *   Evaluating models for **agentic** or **multi-step** tasks.
    *   Selecting between **cost-efficient** models where benchmark scores are similar.
    *   Debugging why a model performs well on unit tests but fails in end-to-end integration.
    *   *Do not* use this as a standalone metric for single-turn chatbots or simple classification tasks where compositional depth is not required.

*   **Integration Steps:**
    1.  **Dataset Generation:** Follow the pipeline in Section 2: Select pairs from your domain's standard test set, substitute the answer of $Q_1$ as variable $X$ in $Q_2$, and verify logical consistency (ensure positive integer answers if applicable).
    2.  **Baseline Measurement:** Measure $S_1$ (accuracy on $Q_1$) and $S_2$ (accuracy on modified $Q_2$) independently.
    3.  **Compositional Measurement:** Measure $S_{comp}$ using the chained prompt format (Appendix E).
    4.  **Gap Calculation:** Compute $\Delta = S_{comp} - (S_1 \times S_2)$.
    5.  **Remediation Test:** If $\Delta$ is large, re-evaluate using the **Code Generation** prompt format (Appendix F) to see if structural scaffolding recovers performance.

*   **Available Resources:** The authors provide the specific 8-shot prompts for both natural language (Appendix E) and code (Appendix F) modes. Replicating the study requires only the GSM8K test set and the ability to programmatically modify the question text and recalculate answers (as described in Section 2).

In conclusion, **Compositional GSM** serves as a critical wake-up call: the era of evaluating LLMs solely on their ability to answer isolated questions is over. True reasoning is compositional, and until models can reliably chain simple thoughts without distraction or state loss, their "mastery" of complex domains remains an illusion. This work provides the tools to pierce that illusion and build more robust, trustworthy AI systems.
