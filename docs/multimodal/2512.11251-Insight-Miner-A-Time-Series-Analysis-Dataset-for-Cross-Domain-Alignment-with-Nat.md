## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the labor-intensive process of mining insights from time-series data by introducing `Insight Miner`, a large-scale multimodal model (`LMM`) capable of generating comprehensive, domain-specific natural language descriptions. The authors facilitate this by releasing `TS-Insights`, the first general-domain dataset for time series and language alignment (Section 2), which contains 100k time-series windows sampled from 20 forecasting datasets constructed via a novel agentic workflow using statistical tools and `GPT-4`. Experiments demonstrate that `Insight Miner` outperforms state-of-the-art models like `LLaVA` and `GPT-4` in generating time-series descriptions (Figure 2), serving as a foundational step toward enabling `LLMs` to interpret time series as a native input modality.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this work, we must first examine the landscape of time-series analysis and the specific limitations of current machine learning approaches when applied to temporal data. The authors identify a critical disconnect between the abundance of time-series data and the ability of modern AI models to explain that data in human-readable terms.

### The Bottleneck of Domain Expertise
Time-series data is ubiquitous, appearing in critical sectors such as environmental analysis, agriculture, transportation, and finance (Abstract). Traditionally, extracting meaningful insights from this data relies on classical statistical methods. The paper highlights standard techniques such as:
*   **Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA)** [2]
*   **Seasonal-Trend Decomposition using LoESS (STL)** [3]
*   **State space models** [4]

While these methods provide essential interpretability for complex temporal data, they possess a significant barrier to entry: **they require deep domain expertise**. As noted in the Abstract, mining insights is "both time-consuming and labor-intensive." The motivation here is not merely to automate forecasting, but to automate the *interpretation* process, allowing non-experts to understand underlying trends, seasonality, and residuals without manually running statistical decompositions.

### The Limitation of Current LLM Approaches
Recently, Large Language Models (LLMs) have been applied to time-series tasks. However, the authors point out a fundamental limitation in how these models are currently utilized (Section 1):
*   **Numerical Focus:** Most studies leverage pretrained language models (e.g., `GPT-2`) for tasks where the output is numerical, such as forecasting future values, classification, or anomaly detection [5, 6].
*   **Prompt Engineering:** Other works design structured prompts for zero-shot or few-shot inference [7, 8].

The critical gap is that **directly training LLMs to perform numerical tasks does not inherently enable them to generate natural language insights.** A model might predict the next value in a stock price sequence accurately, but it cannot explain *why* the price is trending upward or identify a seasonal pattern in natural language. This limits the utility of LLMs in scenarios where explanation is as valuable as prediction.

### The Multimodal Gap and Domain Specificity
The emergence of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs), such as `LLaVA` [9], offers a potential pathway to align non-text data with language. However, existing applications are either too general or too specific:
*   **General LMMs:** Models like `LLaVA` are designed for natural images, not temporal data plots.
*   **Domain-Specific LMMs:** Works like `FinVis-GPT` [10] demonstrate feasibility by aligning financial charts with language. However, this success is confined to the financial domain.

The authors argue that there is **no existing large-scale dataset for aligning general time-series data with comprehensive textual descriptions** (Section 1). This is a crucial distinction. A model trained only on financial charts may not generalize to weather patterns, traffic flow, or energy consumption data, which exhibit different statistical properties and semantic meanings.

### The Challenge of Input Modality
A subtle but vital technical motivation arises from how time-series data is fed into language models. One might assume that converting a time series into a list of numbers (a vector) would suffice for an LLM. The paper explicitly tests this assumption and finds it lacking (Section 2):
> "However, we found that GPT-4 fails to accurately extract each component from the raw vector."

This failure occurs because:
1.  **No Original Captions:** Unlike images, time-series windows do not come with ground-truth text descriptions.
2.  **Input Format:** Existing tools cannot readily convert raw time-series segments into a format suitable for language-only models without losing semantic structure.
3.  **Semantic Difficulty:** Describing the "meaning" of a sequence of numbers (e.g., distinguishing noise from a true trend) is semantically harder for a text-only model than analyzing a visual representation of that trend.

This motivates the paper's decision to treat time series as **images** (line plots) rather than raw vectors, leveraging the visual reasoning capabilities of existing LMMs while addressing the lack of aligned training data.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work
`Insight Miner` positions itself as a foundational bridge between raw temporal data and linguistic understanding. Table 1 below summarizes how this work differentiates from prior approaches based on the text provided:

| Approach | Input Modality | Output Modality | Domain | Limitation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Statistical Tools** (ARIMA, STL) | Raw Numbers | Numerical Parameters | General | Requires human expertise to interpret |
| **LLM Forecasting** [5, 6] | Raw Numbers | Numerical Values | General | Cannot generate natural language insights |
| **FinVis-GPT** [10] | Chart Images | Text | Financial | Not generalizable to other time-series domains |
| **Insight Miner** (This Work) | Chart Images | Text | **General** | Requires curated dataset (TS-Insights) |

By introducing `TS-Insights`, the first general-domain dataset for time series and language alignment (Section 2), the paper aims to enable LLMs to interpret time series as a **native input modality**. This shifts the paradigm from using LLMs as calculators for time-series data to using them as analysts capable of describing complex temporal dynamics.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a **data-centric approach** to enabling multimodal models to analyze time series, where the core innovation lies not in novel model architecture but in constructing a high-quality alignment dataset (`TS-Insights`) and using it to instruction-tune an existing vision-language model (`LLaVA`) into `Insight Miner`.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a multimodal AI that takes a time-series line plot as an image input and outputs a natural language description of the trend patterns visible in that plot. The solution addresses the problem that existing language models cannot reliably interpret raw time-series numbers by converting the data into visual form and training the model on 100,000 expert-generated description pairs.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The technical pipeline consists of three major components connected in sequence. First, the **Data Generation Pipeline** extracts statistical features from raw time-series windows using STL decomposition and Gaussian Process regression, then prompts `GPT-4` to generate trend descriptions from these processed features. Second, the **Augmentation Engine** applies nine different random transformations to each original time-series window while preserving the semantic validity of the trend description, expanding 10,000 initial samples to 100,000 training examples. Third, the **Insight Miner Model** takes the time-series window rendered as a line plot image, passes it through a frozen vision encoder, projects the visual features into the language embedding space via a trainable linear layer, and generates the trend description using a frozen language model. Information flows from raw numerical data through statistical preprocessing, into natural language labels, and finally into model weights through instruction tuning.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first explain how raw time-series windows are converted into trend descriptions, because understanding the data generation process is prerequisite to understanding what the model learns.
- We then detail the STL decomposition and Gaussian Process fallback mechanism, since these statistical tools form the foundation for extracting interpretable features from raw numbers.
- We next cover the data augmentation strategy, which explains how the authors efficiently scaled from 10,000 to 100,000 samples without additional `GPT-4` labeling costs.
- We then describe the model architecture and training procedure, showing how `LLaVA` is adapted from natural images to time-series plots.
- We finally explain the evaluation protocol, which clarifies how the authors measured whether the generated descriptions are faithful to the underlying data.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**This is a dataset construction and instruction-tuning paper** where the primary contribution is the `TS-Insights` dataset that aligns time-series windows with natural language descriptions, enabling existing multimodal models to be fine-tuned for time-series analysis tasks.

**The data generation pipeline operates through a sequential workflow** that transforms raw numerical time series into labeled training examples through statistical preprocessing and language model prompting. The process begins by sampling a time-series window $W_k \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times \tau_k}$ from one of the source datasets, where $\tau_k$ represents the number of time steps and is randomly sampled from the range [30, 500] (footnote 3, Section 2). The authors deliberately restrict attention to single-feature windows rather than multivariate time series, as describing cross-feature relationships introduces additional semantic complexity that is left for future work (Section 5).

**The core technical challenge is converting raw numbers into semantically meaningful descriptions** that a language model can learn to associate with visual patterns. A naive approach would feed the raw time-series vector directly to `GPT-4` with a prompt like "Given the time series [0.52, 0.98, 0.95, ...], generate a description about its trend, seasonality, and volatility." However, the authors explicitly tested this approach and found that "`GPT-4` fails to accurately extract each component from the raw vector" (Section 2, footnote 4). This failure motivates the tool-use pipeline where statistical algorithms preprocess the data before language generation.

**The primary decomposition method is Seasonal-Trend Decomposition using LoESS (STL)**, which mathematically separates the original time series into three additive components. The decomposition is expressed as:

$$W_k = T_k + S_k + R_k$$

where $T_k$ denotes the extracted trend component, $S_k$ denotes the seasonality component, and $R_k$ denotes the residual component (Equation 2, Section 2.1). The trend component $T_k$ captures the long-term direction of the time series, the seasonality component $S_k$ captures repeating patterns at fixed intervals, and the residual component $R_k$ captures irregular noise that cannot be explained by trend or seasonality. The authors focus specifically on generating descriptions for the trend component in this version of the paper, leaving seasonality and residual descriptions for future extensions (Section 2.1).

**When STL decomposition is not applicable, a Gaussian Process regression serves as a fallback mechanism** for extracting the trend. Some time-series windows may not exhibit any detectable seasonality, making STL decomposition inappropriate. In such cases, the authors fit a Gaussian Process (GP) to the $\tau_k$ time steps in the window, where the window is denoted as $W_k = (y_1, y_2, \cdots, y_{\tau_k})$ and $y_i$ represents the value at time step $i$ (Section 2.1). The time series is modeled by a standard zero-mean GP, whose covariance structure is defined by a kernel function $K(\cdot, \cdot)$. The specific kernel used is a combination of two components:

$$K(x, x') = \text{RBF}(x, x') + \sigma_e^2 \delta_{x,x'}$$

where the Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel models the dependency among different time steps and the white-noise kernel models observational noise (Section 2.1). The RBF kernel is defined as:

$$\text{RBF}(x, x') = \sigma_r^2 \exp\left(-\frac{(x - x')^2}{2\gamma}\right)$$

where $\sigma_r^2$ controls the variance, $\gamma$ controls the length scale, and $\delta_{x,x'}$ is the Kronecker delta which equals 1 when $x = x'$ and 0 otherwise (Section 2.1). The parameters $\sigma_r^2$, $\gamma$, and $\sigma_e^2$ are estimated from the data by maximizing the likelihood function. After fitting the GP, the authors compute the posterior mean of the Gaussian Process regression at each time step to obtain the extracted trend $T_k = (\hat{y}_1, \hat{y}_2, \cdots, \hat{y}_{\tau_k})$.

**After extracting the trend component, additional smoothing and downsampling operations prepare the data for language model input.** The extracted trend is first smoothed using a Gaussian kernel $F_k = [F_1, F_2, \cdots, F_{w_k}]$, where $w_k$ is a hyperparameter controlling the kernel size (Section 2.1). The smoothing operation is followed by downsampling with a stride size $s_k$, which reduces the number of points while preserving the overall shape of the trend. The downsampling formula is:

$$\tilde{y}_i = \sum_{j=-w_k//2}^{w_k//2} \hat{y}_{s_k \cdot i - j} \cdot F_{w_k//2 + j}$$

for $i = 1, 2, \cdots, \tau_k // s_k$ (Equation 3, Section 2.1). The authors choose the stride size $s_k$ such that $\tau_k // s_k = 25$, meaning the final downsampled trend always contains exactly 25 points regardless of the original window length (footnote 5, Section 2.1). This standardization is critical because it ensures consistent input length for the language model prompt. Finally, each entry of the downsampled trend $(\tilde{y}_1, \tilde{y}_2, \cdots, \tilde{y}_{\tau_k // s_k})$ is rounded to one decimal place before being fed to `GPT-4` (Section 2.1).

**The complete data generation workflow is visualized in Figure 1**, which shows the exact prompt structure used to query `GPT-4` for trend descriptions. Each training sample consists of a time-series window $W_k$, a question $L_Q^k$, and an answer $L_A^k$, which are formatted into a single-round instruction-following example as:

$$\text{Human: } W_k \backslash n \text{ } L_Q^k \text{ <STOP>} \backslash n \text{ Assistant: } L_A^k \text{ <STOP>} \backslash n$$

(Equation 1, Section 2). This format matches the instruction-tuning format used by `LLaVA`, enabling direct compatibility with the model's training pipeline.

**The dataset construction process begins with 29 datasets from the Monash Time Series Forecasting Archive** spanning domains including energy, weather, traffic, and healthcare (Section 2.2). From these, 10,000 initial samples are generated using the workflow described above. The authors reserve 11 additional datasets as a holdout set that is used only for evaluation and never for training, ensuring that the model's generalization to unseen domains can be properly measured (Section 2.2). Importantly, the authors only sample windows from the train split of each dataset, which is defined as the first 70% of the time steps in temporal order (Section 2.2). This temporal split prevents data leakage where future information could contaminate the training set.

**To increase dataset diversity, the authors aggregate multiple time steps into single time steps for datasets with multiple seasonality levels.** Some datasets contain seasonalities at different granularities, such as daily and weekly patterns. At the original granularity, a window might not contain enough time steps to discern higher-level seasonalities, since at least two full cycles are required to conclude that a seasonality exists (Section 2.2). Since trends should be described only after seasonalities are removed, the authors create samples with more diversified patterns by aggregating time steps at different granularity levels. Appendix A provides a detailed table showing the number of samples generated for each dataset at each aggregated granularity level, totaling 10,360 initial samples across 20 datasets used for training.

**Data augmentation expands the 10,000 initial samples to 100,000 training samples without additional `GPT-4` labeling costs.** For each `GPT-4` labeled sample pair, the authors apply nine different random augmentations to the original time-series window $W_k$ such that the trend description remains semantically valid for the augmented samples (Section 2.2). This is a critical design choice: the augmentations must preserve the underlying trend pattern while introducing variation in the raw numbers. The specific augmentation methods are listed in Appendix A, and each augmentation is applied with a probability of 50%:

- **Jittering**: Adding Gaussian noise to the original time series, where the standard deviation of the Gaussian noise is set to be the standard deviation from a local rolling window of size 4.
- **Scaling**: Multiplying the original time series with a constant factor.
- **Shifting**: Adding a constant value to the original time series.
- **Smoothing**: Convolving the original time series window with an average kernel of a randomly sampled size.
- **Downsampling**: Only keeping every $k$-th step, where $k$ is another randomly sampled integer.

Multiple augmentations can be composed together to produce the final augmented window. After augmentation, the original `GPT-4` description is rephrased using `GPT-3.5-turbo` to increase language diversity, ensuring that the model does not overfit to exact phrasing patterns (Section 2.2). This two-stage process (numerical augmentation + linguistic rephrasing) yields 10 augmented versions per original sample, resulting in 100,000 total training samples.

**The `Insight Miner` model is built by fine-tuning `LLaVA`, a state-of-the-art general-domain vision-language model** (Section 3). The neural network architecture is identical to `LLaVA`: the time-series window is first converted into an image using a line plot, this image is fed into the vision encoder, and a linear projection layer maps the vision output into the language embedding space (Section 3). The language model then takes the projected image embeddings concatenated with the language instructions as input and returns the language response. This architecture choice is deliberate: by treating time series as images, the model leverages the visual reasoning capabilities that `LLaVA` already possesses from training on natural images.

**The fine-tuning strategy freezes most model components to reduce training cost and prevent catastrophic forgetting.** To align the time-series images with the language model, only the linear projection layer is fine-tuned, while both the vision encoder and the language model remain frozen (Section 3). This is a standard practice in parameter-efficient fine-tuning, where the pretrained weights of large models are preserved and only a small adapter layer is trained for the new domain. For each training sample, the model sees the original time series rendered as a line plot, receives the instruction to describe the trend, and learns to predict the `GPT-4`-generated description. The final trained model is named `Insight Miner`.

**Training is computationally affordable, requiring 8 × A100 40GiB GPUs with each epoch taking approximately one hour** (Section 3). This relatively modest compute requirement is a direct consequence of the parameter-efficient fine-tuning strategy: since only the linear projection layer is trainable, the gradient computation and weight updates are limited to a small fraction of the total model parameters. Once training completes, the model can be deployed at low inference cost, making it practical for real-world applications where domain experts need rapid time-series analysis.

**The evaluation protocol uses human expert scoring to measure description faithfulness** (Section 4). The authors sample 119 total windows for evaluation, with 69 examples from the test split (the last 30% of the same datasets used for training) and 50 examples from the holdout datasets that were never seen during training. Four models are compared: the original `LLaVA` checkpoint from HuggingFace, `Vision (3 epochs)` fine-tuned for three epochs, `Vision (1 epoch)` fine-tuned for one epoch, and `Engineering GPT` which is `GPT-4` that takes in the extracted features as described in Section 2.1. For each of the 119 samples, each model generates one description, and three domain experts manually score the descriptions. The scoring rubric assigns 2 points if the description matches the original time series, 1 point if partially correct, and 0 points if incorrect. The scores from all human evaluators for all test and holdout samples are summed and normalized to the range [0, 1] to produce the final score for each model.

**Figure 2 presents the evaluation results**, showing that both `Vision (3 epochs)` and `Vision (1 epoch)` significantly outperform the original `LLaVA` model. Training for more epochs leads to better performance, with the three-epoch model achieving performance competitive with `Engineering GPT-4`. Notably, `Vision (3 epochs)` outperforms `GPT-4` on the holdout datasets, which the authors hypothesize is because the holdout datasets contain more complicated seasonalities than the test datasets (Section 4). Even though `Engineering GPT-4` has access to the extracted statistical features, it performs zero-shot inference without task-specific fine-tuning, whereas `Insight Miner` leverages the 100,000 labeled samples from `TS-Insights` to learn the mapping from visual patterns to trend descriptions.

**The authors also experimented with replacing the vision encoder with a dedicated time-series encoder** but found this approach unsuccessful (Section 5). Specifically, they tried using `OneFitsAll` [5] as the time-series encoder to replace the vision encoder in `LLaVA`. The initial attempt showed that using a time-series encoder causes the model to fail to generate coherent descriptions for most samples. The authors attribute this failure to the fact that, unlike the original vision encoder, the time-series encoder is not pretrained on large-scale data. This finding reinforces the design choice to treat time series as images and leverage pretrained vision encoders rather than training encoders from scratch. The authors leave the pretraining of a dedicated time-series encoder as future work.

**Several design choices warrant explicit attention** because they reflect non-obvious trade-offs in the system architecture. First, the decision to render time series as line plots rather than feeding raw vectors to the language model is motivated by the empirical finding that `GPT-4` cannot reliably extract trend components from raw number sequences. Second, the choice to freeze the vision encoder and language model during fine-tuning balances training efficiency against adaptation capacity: the model benefits from pretrained visual features but cannot fully adapt to the unique statistical properties of time-series plots. Third, the augmentation strategy preserves semantic validity by applying transformations that do not change the underlying trend pattern, enabling label reuse without additional `GPT-4` queries. Fourth, the standardization of downsampled trend length to exactly 25 points ensures consistent prompt length, which is important for language model training stability. Finally, the temporal train/test split (first 70% for training, last 30% for testing) prevents future information from leaking into the training set, which is critical for time-series data where temporal ordering matters.

**Limitations in the current implementation are explicitly acknowledged** in the paper. The workflow currently focuses only on trend descriptions, leaving seasonality and residual descriptions for future work (Section 2.1). The dataset is restricted to single-feature time series, and describing multi-feature time series through cross-correlations is identified as a more challenging future task (Section 5). The evaluation was conducted using only the first three samples from each dataset due to limited human resources, even though ten window samples per dataset were released on HuggingFace (Appendix A). Additionally, the authors note that only the original time-series windows were saved, not the augmented windows, though they state the augmented samples can be regenerated using the provided script (Appendix A). These limitations define clear directions for future research while establishing the current work as a foundational step toward enabling language models to interpret time series as a native input modality.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the core conceptual breakthroughs that enable `Insight Miner` to succeed where prior approaches stalled. We distinguish between incremental engineering choices and fundamental shifts in how time-series data is aligned with language models. The following insights explain *why* the system works, offering a blueprint for future research in temporal data alignment.

### 4.1 Modality Translation: Time Series as Visual Patterns, Not Token Sequences

A fundamental innovation in this work is the decision to treat time-series data as **images** rather than sequences of numerical tokens. While Section 3 detailed the architecture, the underlying insight challenges a common assumption in time-series machine learning: that numerical data should always be processed as numbers.

*   **The Failure of Raw Vectors:** As noted in Section 2, the authors explicitly tested feeding raw time-series vectors (e.g., `[0.52, 0.98, ...]`) to `GPT-4`. This approach failed because language models struggle to internally visualize trends from raw sequences without explicit computational tools. The semantic meaning of a "trend" is geometric, not arithmetic, in this context.
*   **The Failure of Dedicated Encoders:** In Section 5, the authors report an attempt to replace the vision encoder with a dedicated time-series encoder (`OneFitsAll` [5]). This resulted in incoherent descriptions because the time-series encoder lacked the massive pretrained visual reasoning capabilities of the vision encoder.
*   **The Visual Shortcut:** By rendering time series as line plots, `Insight Miner` leverages the **pretrained visual reasoning** of `LLaVA`. The model does not need to learn what a "slope" or "peak" looks like from scratch; it already knows these concepts from natural images. The innovation lies in recognizing that a line plot is a universal interface that transfers visual intuition to temporal data.

This choice is significant because it bypasses the need for training a specialized time-series encoder from scratch, which would require orders of magnitude more data. It repurposes existing multimodal capabilities for a new domain.

### 4.2 Statistical Grounding: Hybrid Synthetic Data Generation

The construction of the `TS-Insights` dataset (Section 2) introduces a novel **hybrid workflow** that combines classical statistics with modern generative AI. This addresses the critical problem of ground truth in synthetic data generation.

*   **Avoiding Hallucination:** If one were to prompt an LLM directly to describe a time series, the resulting text might be plausible but factually incorrect regarding the data's statistical properties. To prevent this, the authors use **Seasonal-Trend Decomposition (STL)** and **Gaussian Processes (GP)** to mathematically extract the trend component *before* involving the LLM (Section 2.1).
*   **The Insight:** The LLM is not asked to *analyze* the data; it is asked to *translate* the already-extracted statistical features into natural language. This ensures that the training labels are mathematically faithful to the input.
*   **Significance:** This creates a "noise-free" training signal. The model learns to associate visual patterns with descriptions that are guaranteed to be statistically valid. This is a departure from standard synthetic data pipelines where both the data and the labels are generated purely by neural networks, often compounding errors.

This approach bridges the interpretability of classical statistics (ARIMA, STL) with the flexibility of natural language, ensuring that the resulting model inherits the reliability of the statistical tools used to create its training data.

### 4.3 Semantic-Preserving Augmentation for Cost-Efficient Scaling

Scaling datasets for multimodal models is typically bottlenecked by the cost of labeling. `Insight Miner` introduces a domain-specific augmentation strategy that scales the dataset **10×** (from 10k to 100k samples) without additional labeling costs (Section 2.2).

*   **Label Invariance:** The key insight is that certain transformations of a time series do not change its semantic trend description. For example, **scaling** (multiplying by a constant) or **shifting** (adding a constant) alters the numerical values but preserves the shape of the trend (Appendix A).
*   **Decoupled Augmentation:** The authors apply nine different random augmentations to the input window $W_k$ while retaining the original trend description (after rephrasing for linguistic diversity). This is distinct from image augmentation where labels might change (e.g., rotating a digit '6' might make it a '9').
*   **Significance:** This allows the authors to expand the dataset from 10,000 `GPT-4`-labeled samples to 100,000 training samples primarily using compute rather than API costs. It demonstrates that for time-series analysis, **semantic invariance** can be exploited to drastically reduce the cost of high-quality data collection.

This innovation makes the creation of large-scale time-series language datasets feasible for researchers who do not have access to massive labeling budgets, lowering the barrier to entry for this field.

### 4.4 Specialized Fine-Tuning Outperforms Generalist Zero-Shot Reasoning

The evaluation results (Figure 2, Section 4) provide empirical evidence for a broader insight in AI: **task-specific fine-tuning on high-quality domain data can outperform generalist models even when the generalist model is larger.**

*   **The Comparison:** The `Insight Miner` model (based on `LLaVA`) is compared against `Engineering GPT` (which is `GPT-4` provided with the same statistical features used to generate the training data).
*   **The Result:** On holdout datasets (domains never seen during training), `Vision (3 epochs)` outperforms `GPT-4` (Section 4). This is notable because `GPT-4` is performing zero-shot inference with explicit statistical features, while `Insight Miner` is performing few-shot style inference based on visual patterns learned during fine-tuning.
*   **The Insight:** The fine-tuned model learns a **direct mapping** from visual pattern to language description that is optimized for this specific task. `GPT-4`, despite its general reasoning capabilities, lacks the specific alignment between time-series plots and trend terminology that `Insight Miner` acquires during training.
*   **Significance:** This suggests that for specialized scientific tasks, building a curated dataset and fine-tuning a smaller model is a more effective strategy than relying on prompt engineering with massive proprietary models. It validates the paper's core thesis that a foundational dataset (`TS-Insights`) is the missing link for time-series language alignment.

This finding encourages a shift in resource allocation: instead of solely chasing larger base models, the community should invest in high-quality, domain-specific alignment datasets that allow smaller models to achieve expert-level performance.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

The validity of `Insight Miner` rests not only on its architectural design but on the rigor of its empirical evaluation. This section dissects the experimental methodology, scrutinizes the quantitative results, and assesses whether the evidence sufficiently supports the claim that multimodal fine-tuning enables superior time-series interpretation compared to generalist models. We move beyond *what* the results are (covered in Section 3) to analyze *how convincing* they are, highlighting critical design choices, baseline asymmetries, and limitations in statistical power.

### 5.1 Evaluation Design and Metrics

The authors prioritize **semantic faithfulness** over numerical accuracy, necessitating a evaluation strategy distinct from standard forecasting benchmarks (e.g., MSE or MAE). As detailed in Section 3, the core metric is a human-evaluated score normalized to $[0, 1]$. While appropriate for natural language generation, this choice introduces specific constraints:

*   **Subjectivity vs. Consistency:** The scoring rubric (2 = match, 1 = partial, 0 = incorrect) relies on the judgment of three domain experts. To mitigate bias, descriptions are shuffled randomly before evaluation (Section 4). However, the paper does not report inter-annotator agreement (e.g., Cohen's $\kappa$), leaving the consistency of the "ground truth" judgments unquantified.
*   **Sample Size Constraints:** The evaluation set consists of $119$ total windows. While sufficient for a preliminary study, this limits the statistical power to detect small but meaningful differences between models. The split is uneven:
    *   **Test Set:** $69$ windows from the last $30\%$ of training datasets (in-domain distribution).
    *   **Holdout Set:** $50$ windows from $11$ entirely unseen datasets (out-of-domain distribution).
*   **Normalization:** Scores are summed across evaluators and samples, then normalized. This aggregates performance across domains, potentially masking failure modes in specific sectors (e.g., healthcare vs. traffic).

### 5.2 Performance Across Domains

The results, summarized in **Figure 2**, provide evidence for the efficacy of domain-specific fine-tuning. The analysis reveals two distinct performance regimes:

*   **In-Domain Performance (Test Set):** Both `Vision (3 epochs)` and `Vision (1 epoch)` significantly outperform the base `LLaVA` model. This confirms that the `TS-Insights` dataset successfully aligns the visual encoder with time-series semantics. The `Vision (3 epochs)` model achieves performance **competitive with `Engineering GPT`** on this split.
*   **Out-of-Domain Performance (Holdout Set):** A critical finding is that `Vision (3 epochs)` **outperforms `GPT-4`** on the holdout datasets (Section 4). This is counter-intuitive given `GPT-4`'s general reasoning capabilities. The authors hypothesize this stems from the holdout datasets containing "more datasets with complicated seasonalities" (Section 4).
    *   **Why Fine-Tuning Wins Here:** `Engineering GPT` performs zero-shot inference using statistical features. While it has the features, it lacks the specific alignment between *complex visual patterns* and *trend terminology* that `Insight Miner` learns from the $100$k training samples.
    *   **Implication:** This suggests that for specialized temporal domains, a smaller, fine-tuned multimodal model can generalize better than a massive generalist model, provided the training data covers sufficient pattern diversity.

### 5.3 Baseline Fairness and Comparisons

The choice of baselines is strategic but contains an important asymmetry that must be acknowledged when interpreting the results.

*   **`LLaVA` (Base):** Serves as the control for architectural impact. The significant gap between `LLaVA` and `Insight Miner` isolates the benefit of the `TS-Insights` fine-tuning.
*   **`Engineering GPT`:** This baseline represents the "upper bound" of a tool-augmented approach. It feeds `GPT-4` the **extracted statistical features** (trend components from STL/GP) rather than raw data or images (Section 4).
    *   **Asymmetry:** `Insight Miner` must *visually infer* the trend from a line plot. `Engineering GPT` is *given* the mathematically decomposed trend values.
    *   **Significance:** Despite this disadvantage (visual inference vs. direct feature input), `Insight Miner` remains competitive and even superior on holdout data. This strengthens the claim that the visual encoder learns robust representations of trends that rival explicit statistical decomposition in a zero-shot setting.
*   **Raw Vector GPT-4:** The authors explicitly excluded raw vector input for `GPT-4` from the main evaluation because it led to "inferior descriptions" (Section 4). This ablation is crucial: it validates the decision to use images over raw tokens, confirming that `GPT-4` cannot reliably parse raw numerical sequences for trend analysis without preprocessing.

### 5.4 Architectural Ablations

The paper includes a critical negative result regarding the input encoder, discussed in Section 5 (Discussions).

*   **Experiment:** The authors attempted to replace the frozen vision encoder (trained on natural images) with `OneFitsAll` [5], a pretrained time-series encoder.
*   **Result:** The model failed to generate coherent descriptions for most samples.
*   **Analysis:** This failure highlights a dependency on **pretrained visual priors**. The `OneFitsAll` encoder, while trained on time-series data, lacks the massive scale and semantic alignment of vision encoders trained on billions of image-text pairs (like `LLaVA`'s encoder).
*   **Takeaway:** Current time-series encoders are not yet mature enough to replace vision encoders for multimodal language tasks. The visual representation (line plot) acts as a universal translator that leverages existing vision-language capabilities, whereas dedicated time-series encoders require further pretraining to achieve similar semantic alignment.

### 5.5 Constraints and Reliability

While the results are promising, several constraints limit the generalizability of the findings. Readers should interpret the claims with the following caveats in mind:

*   **Human Resource Bottleneck:** Appendix A reveals a significant limitation in the evaluation scale. Although ten window samples were released per dataset on HuggingFace, the evaluation reported in Section 4 used **only the first three samples from each dataset** due to a "lack of human resources."
    *   **Impact:** This reduces the effective evaluation size further and introduces potential selection bias if the first three samples are not representative of the dataset's complexity.
*   **Augmentation Visibility:** The authors note that only the original time-series windows were saved, not the augmented versions (Appendix A). While they claim these can be regenerated via script, the inability to directly audit the augmented training data limits reproducibility of the exact training distribution.
*   **Trend-Only Focus:** The evaluation strictly measures **trend description**. It does not assess the model's ability to describe seasonality or residuals, despite the data generation workflow extracting these components (Section 2.1). The results therefore validate trend analysis specifically, not general time-series decomposition.
*   **Single-Feature Limitation:** All evaluated samples are univariate ($W_k \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times \tau_k}$). The model's performance on multivariate time series, where cross-feature correlations are key, remains untested (Section 5).

In summary, the experiments provide strong preliminary evidence that fine-tuning LMMs on `TS-Insights` enables robust trend description, outperforming generalist models on complex holdout domains. However, the reliance on human evaluation with limited samples and the restriction to univariate trends suggest these results should be viewed as a foundational benchmark rather than a definitive solution for all time-series analysis tasks.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While `Insight Miner` demonstrates a viable path for aligning time-series data with natural language, several critical assumptions and constraints define the boundaries of its current applicability. Understanding these limitations is essential for researchers aiming to build upon this work or deploy it in production environments.

### 6.1 Scope of Analysis: Trend-Only and Univariate
The most significant functional limitation is the restricted scope of the generated insights. Although the data generation workflow mathematically decomposes time series into trend, seasonality, and residuals (Equation 2, Section 2.1), the model is trained **only to describe the trend component**.
*   **Incomplete Decomposition:** The paper explicitly states, "As a proof of concept, we focus on the trend description in the current version of this paper" (Section 2.1). Consequently, `Insight Miner` cannot currently explain seasonal patterns (e.g., "peaks every 24 hours") or identify anomalies within the residual noise, which are often critical in domains like fraud detection or equipment maintenance.
*   **Univariate Constraint:** The dataset construction restricts windows to a single feature, i.e., $W_k \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times \tau_k}$ (Section 2). The authors acknowledge that generating descriptions for **multivariate time series** by studying cross-correlations is a "more challenging task" left for future work (Section 5). This limits the model's utility in complex systems where variables interact (e.g., temperature and humidity, or stock price and trading volume).

### 6.2 Dependency on Visual Priors vs. Native Temporal Encoders
The architecture relies on treating time series as images (line plots) to leverage pretrained vision encoders. This design choice involves a fundamental trade-off between **visual reasoning capability** and **native temporal understanding**.
*   **Failure of Native Encoders:** In Section 5, the authors report that replacing the vision encoder with a dedicated time-series encoder (`OneFitsAll` [5]) caused the model to "fail to generate coherent descriptions." This suggests that current time-series encoders lack the semantic alignment with language that vision encoders possess due to massive pretraining on image-text pairs.
*   **Information Loss:** Rendering a time series as a line plot inevitably discards precision. While the downsampling process standardizes inputs to 25 points (footnote 5, Section 2.1), subtle high-frequency variations might be visually obscured or lost during the image encoding process. The model learns to recognize visual shapes rather than precise numerical values, which may hinder tasks requiring exact quantitative reasoning.
*   **Future Obsolescence:** This approach is contingent on the current immaturity of time-series encoders. If future work produces a time-series encoder with strong language alignment, the visual intermediate step might become unnecessary, potentially offering higher fidelity than line plots.

### 6.3 Evaluation Scale and Statistical Power
The empirical validation, while promising, is constrained by resource limitations that affect the statistical robustness of the claims.
*   **Small Evaluation Set:** Although 10 window samples per dataset were released on HuggingFace, the human evaluation reported in Section 4 used **only the first three samples from each dataset** (Appendix A). The authors cite a "lack of human resources" for this restriction.
    *   **Impact:** With only 119 total evaluation windows, the results may not capture the full distribution of time-series patterns across the 20 domains. Rare edge cases (e.g., sudden structural breaks or complex multi-seasonality) might be underrepresented.
*   **Subjectivity of Metrics:** The evaluation relies on human expert scoring (0–2 scale) normalized to $[0, 1]$ (Section 4). While descriptions were shuffled to reduce bias, the paper does not report **inter-annotator agreement** (e.g., Cohen's $\kappa$). Without this metric, it is difficult to quantify the consistency of the "ground truth" judgments, leaving open the possibility that score variations reflect evaluator disagreement rather than model performance differences.

### 6.4 Reproducibility and Data Availability
Certain choices made to optimize cost and efficiency introduce friction for reproducibility and independent verification.
*   **Augmentation Transparency:** To scale the dataset from 10k to 100k samples, the authors applied nine random augmentations to each original window (Section 2.2). However, Appendix A explicitly states: "Unfortunately, we only saved the original time series windows but not the augmented windows."
    *   **Risk:** While the authors claim augmented samples can be regenerated using the provided script, stochastic augmentation processes (e.g., jittering with Gaussian noise) are sensitive to random seeds and implementation details. Without the exact augmented artifacts, independent researchers cannot perfectly replicate the training distribution, which may lead to variance in fine-tuned model performance.
*   **Holdout Usage:** The 11 holdout datasets were reserved for evaluation (Section 2.2), but the specific split of windows within those datasets is not fully detailed beyond the "first three samples" used for scoring. This limits the ability of future work to benchmark against `Insight Miner` on the exact same unseen data points.

### 6.5 Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning Constraints
The training strategy prioritizes efficiency over full model adaptation, which imposes a ceiling on performance.
*   **Frozen Components:** To keep training affordable (8 × A100 GPUs, ~1 hour/epoch), the authors freeze both the vision encoder and the language model, fine-tuning **only the linear projection layer** (Section 3).
    *   **Trade-off:** This prevents catastrophic forgetting and reduces compute costs, but it also restricts the model's ability to adapt the vision encoder's features to the specific statistical properties of time-series plots (e.g., distinguishing noise from trend might require different visual attention than natural images). Full fine-tuning might yield higher performance but at a significantly higher computational and carbon cost.
*   **Downstream Task Uncertainty:** The paper suggests the dataset could enable improvements in forecasting or classification (Section 5), but this is not experimentally verified. The current model is optimized for **description**, not **prediction**. There is no guarantee that the features learned for generating natural language trends will transfer effectively to numerical forecasting tasks, which remain the primary industrial use case for time-series models.

### 6.6 Summary of Open Questions
Based on these limitations, several critical questions remain for future research:
*   **Can seasonality and residuals be described?** Extending the instruction tuning to cover the $S_k$ and $R_k$ components from Equation 2 would make the tool a complete decomposition analyst.
*   **Does language alignment improve forecasting?** As posed in Section 5, does associating raw vectors with statistical concepts in natural language actually enhance numerical prediction accuracy, or is this capability strictly limited to interpretability?
*   **Can multivariate relationships be captured?** Moving from $W_k \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times \tau_k}$ to multivariate inputs is necessary for real-world scientific applications where variables are interdependent.

By addressing these constraints, future iterations of `Insight Miner` could evolve from a trend description tool into a comprehensive multimodal analyst for temporal data.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work represents a pivotal shift in how machine learning systems interact with temporal data. By successfully aligning time-series visualizations with natural language, `Insight Miner` moves the field beyond pure numerical forecasting toward **interpretable temporal reasoning**. The implications extend beyond the specific model architecture to the broader methodology of constructing multimodal datasets for scientific data.

### Shifting the Landscape: From Calculation to Interpretation
The primary contribution of this paper is the validation of time series as a **native input modality** for Large Multimodal Models (LMMs). Historically, time-series analysis has been bifurcated: statistical tools (like ARIMA or STL) provided interpretability but required expertise, while deep learning models (like Transformers) provided accuracy but operated as black boxes. `Insight Miner` bridges this gap by demonstrating that an LMM can generate faithful descriptions without explicit statistical tool usage at inference time.

*   **Democratization of Analysis:** As noted in the Abstract, mining insights typically requires "deep domain expertise." By automating the generation of trend descriptions, this work lowers the barrier to entry for non-experts in fields like energy, weather, and healthcare (Section 2.2). A stakeholder no longer needs to understand decomposition algorithms to grasp the underlying trend of a dataset.
*   **Validation of Visual Encoding:** The experimental results (Figure 2, Section 4) challenge the assumption that raw numerical tokens are the optimal input for time-series tasks. The failure of the raw vector approach (Section 2) and the dedicated time-series encoder `OneFitsAll` (Section 5) suggests that **visual priors** from natural image training are currently more valuable for semantic tasks than specialized temporal encoders. This implies that future research should prioritize leveraging existing vision-language capabilities rather than building temporal encoders from scratch, at least until those encoders achieve comparable semantic alignment.

### Pathways for Future Research
The limitations identified in Section 6 define a clear roadmap for subsequent studies. The authors explicitly frame this work as a "foundational step" (Abstract), indicating that several critical components remain to be built.

*   **Multivariate Alignment:** The current dataset is restricted to univariate windows, $W_k \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times \tau_k}$ (Section 2). A critical next step is extending the `TS-Insights` workflow to **multivariate time series**. This would require generating descriptions that capture cross-correlations (e.g., "temperature rises as humidity drops"), a task the authors identify as "more challenging" (Section 5). Future datasets must define ground truth for feature interactions, likely requiring more complex agentic workflows than the current trend-focused pipeline.
*   **Complete Decomposition Descriptions:** Currently, the model focuses exclusively on the trend component $T_k$ (Section 2.1). The statistical workflow already extracts seasonality $S_k$ and residuals $R_k$, but these are not used for training labels. Future work should generate natural language descriptions for these components to enable the model to identify anomalies (in residuals) or periodic patterns (in seasonality). This would transform the model from a trend describer into a complete **statistical analyst**.
*   **Pretraining Temporal Encoders:** The failure of the `OneFitsAll` encoder (Section 5) highlights a gap in the ecosystem. There is a need for a time-series encoder pretrained on a massive scale with language alignment, similar to how vision encoders are pretrained on image-text pairs. Research into **contrastive learning between time-series segments and text descriptions** could yield encoders that outperform visual line plots in fidelity, removing the information loss inherent in rendering data as images.
*   **Downstream Task Transfer:** The authors hypothesize that associating raw vectors with statistical concepts in natural language could improve forecasting or classification accuracies (Section 5). Future experiments should verify this by fine-tuning `Insight Miner` on numerical prediction tasks to see if the **semantic alignment** learned from `TS-Insights` transfers to quantitative performance.

### Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases
The `Insight Miner` framework is immediately applicable in domains where rapid interpretation of large volumes of temporal data is required. Based on the domains covered in the dataset (Section 2.2), several high-value use cases emerge:

*   **Automated Reporting:** In energy management (e.g., `london_smart_meters_dataset`), the model can generate daily summaries of consumption trends for facility managers, flagging unusual deviations without requiring manual chart inspection.
*   **Healthcare Monitoring:** For patient vitals (e.g., `covid_deaths_dataset`), the model can provide narrative summaries of trend changes, assisting medical staff in quickly assessing patient trajectories across multiple monitors.
*   **Financial and Traffic Analysis:** While `FinVis-GPT` [10] covers finance, `Insight Miner` generalizes this to traffic (`pedestrian_counts_dataset`) and other sectors. It can be deployed to generate real-time alerts describing congestion trends or demand spikes.
*   **Data Cleaning and Augmentation:** The model can be used to label large unlabeled time-series repositories with natural language tags (e.g., "upward trend," "stable"), enabling semantic search across temporal databases.

### Reproducibility and Integration Guidance
For practitioners considering adopting this approach, the following guidance outlines when to prefer `Insight Miner` over alternative methods, based on the trade-offs discussed in Sections 3, 4, and 6.

*   **When to Use `Insight Miner`:**
    *   **Interpretability is Priority:** Choose this method when the goal is to explain *why* a trend is occurring to a non-technical audience.
    *   **Cost Constraints:** The model was trained using 8 × A100 40GiB GPUs with epochs taking around one hour (Section 3). If you have access to similar compute, fine-tuning `LLaVA` on `TS-Insights` is more cost-effective than repeatedly querying proprietary APIs like `GPT-4` for large-scale analysis.
    *   **Univariate Trends:** The model is production-ready for single-feature trend analysis.

*   **When to Use Alternatives:**
    *   **Numerical Precision:** If the task requires exact forecasting values (e.g., "predict the next 10 steps"), traditional statistical models or dedicated forecasting LLMs [5, 6] are more appropriate, as `Insight Miner` is optimized for language generation, not numerical regression.
    *   **Multivariate Dependencies:** Do not use this model for tasks requiring cross-feature analysis (e.g., "does feature A cause feature B?"), as it is trained only on $W_k \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times \tau_k}$ (Section 2).
    *   **Complex Seasonality:** While `Vision (3 epochs)` performed well on holdout datasets with complicated seasonalities (Section 4), the model is not explicitly trained to describe them. For detailed seasonal analysis, classical decomposition tools (STL) remain more reliable until the model is extended to cover $S_k$ and $R_k$ components.

*   **Integration Note:** When deploying, be aware that the evaluation relied on only the first three samples per dataset due to resource constraints (Appendix A). For critical applications, users should validate the model on their specific data distribution, as the 119-sample evaluation set (Section 4) may not capture all edge cases in specialized domains. Additionally, since augmented windows were not saved (Appendix A), exact reproduction of the training distribution requires re-running the augmentation scripts with careful seed management.

By addressing these directions, the community can evolve `Insight Miner` from a proof-of-concept trend describer into a comprehensive multimodal interface for temporal data, fulfilling the authors' vision of enabling LLMs to interpret time series as naturally as they process text.
