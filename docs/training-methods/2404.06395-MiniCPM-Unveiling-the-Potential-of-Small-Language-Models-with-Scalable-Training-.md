## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces `MiniCPM`, specifically the `1.2B` and `2.4B` non-embedding parameter variants, to address the prohibitive cost and deployment inefficiency of Large Language Models (LLMs) while overcoming the capability gaps typical of Small Language Models (SLMs) (Abstract). By employing scalable training strategies such as Model Wind Tunnel Experiments and a `Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD)` learning rate scheduler, `MiniCPM` achieves performance on par with `7B-13B` LLMs like `Llama2-7B` and `Mistral-7B`, despite being significantly smaller (Section 1). This work demonstrates that SLMs can match larger models through optimized scaling laws—deriving a compute-optimal data-model ratio `192` times larger than `Chinchilla Optimal`—making high-performance AI feasible for end-user devices like smartphones (Section 4.5).

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of `MiniCPM`, we must first examine the prevailing tension in modern language model development: the trade-off between capability and cost. This section unpacks the specific gaps in the current landscape, why resolving them matters for both industry and research, and how prior methodologies fell short of providing a sustainable path forward.

### 2.1 The Problem: The Cost-Capability Disconnect

The field of Natural Language Processing has largely converged on a "bigger is better" paradigm. Following the establishment of scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020), research has vigorously pursued Large Language Models (LLMs) with parameter counts reaching into the trillions (Section 1). While these models demonstrate remarkable capabilities, they introduce two critical bottlenecks:

1.  **Prohibitive Training Costs:** Experimenting with LLMs is financially and environmentally burdensome. The empirical understanding of training mechanisms remains elusive, yet the cost of running experiments to gain that understanding is too high for most researchers and corporations (Section 1).
2.  **Deployment Inefficiency:** Deploying colossal models on everyday hardware—such as personal computers or smartphones—is often unfeasible due to memory and compute constraints (Section 1).

Conversely, **Small Language Models (SLMs)**, defined in this paper as models typically not exceeding 7 billion parameters (Section 2), offer a resource-efficient alternative. However, a capability gap exists. Prior SLMs often fail to satisfy two pivotal interests (Section 1):
*   They lack **comprehensive abilities** akin to those exhibited by 7B–13B LLMs (e.g., `Llama2-7B`, `Mistral-7B`).
*   They lack **transparent and scalable training methodologies** that could guide the evolution of both SLMs and future LLMs.

The core problem `MiniCPM` addresses is not merely training a smaller model, but proving that an SLM can match larger counterparts *if* trained with scientifically rigorous, scalable strategies.

### 2.2 Why This Matters: Real-World and Theoretical Impact

The motivation for this work extends beyond simple parameter reduction. It targets fundamental shifts in how models are developed and deployed.

*   **Edge Deployment:** A primary goal is enabling deployment on end-user devices without GPUs (Section 2). By optimizing models in the 1.2B and 2.4B parameter range, the authors aim to make high-performance AI accessible on smartphones and personal computers, reducing latency and privacy concerns associated with cloud-based inference.
*   **Research Accessibility:** If SLMs can serve as reliable proxies for LLMs, they drastically lower the barrier to entry for AI research. The paper posits that experiments conducted on SLMs can guide the development of future larger models if the training strategies are universal across scales (Section 3).
*   **Compute Efficiency:** The paper challenges existing assumptions about compute optimality. Traditional scaling laws suggest a specific ratio of data to model size for optimal performance. `MiniCPM` investigates whether this ratio holds true for modern architectures and data qualities, potentially unlocking more efficient training regimes (Section 4.5).

### 2.3 Limitations of Prior Approaches

To appreciate `MiniCPM`'s contributions, we must analyze where existing methods fall short. The paper identifies specific deficiencies in prior SLMs, scaling law experiments, and training schedulers.

#### 2.3.1 Existing SLMs and Methodologies
Recent resurgences in SLMs include the `Phi` series, `TinyLlama`, `MobileLLM`, and `Gemma` (Section 2). While these models expanded the SLM landscape, they often rely on specific methodologies like high-quality data incorporation or structure pruning. `MiniCPM` argues that these approaches do not fully address the need for **scalable training methodologies** that are transparent and reproducible (Section 1).

#### 2.3.2 The Cost of Scaling Law Experiments
Scaling laws describe the relationship between model performance, model size, and data size. Traditionally, verifying these laws requires training multiple models of varying sizes on varying amounts of data.
*   **The Quadratic Cost Problem:** If the average cost of training one model size on one data size is $C$, conducting scaling experiments with $m$ model sizes and $m$ data sizes takes approximately $O(m^2)C$ (Section 4.5).
*   **Outdated Optimal Ratios:** The seminal `Chinchilla` paper (Hoffmann et al., 2022) proposed a compute-optimal data-to-model ratio where data size should be roughly 20 times the model size. However, recent models like `Llama2` train much more data than this ratio suggests while still yielding performance gains, indicating the `Chinchilla` optimal might be outdated or configuration-dependent (Section 4.5).

#### 2.3.3 Rigidity of Learning Rate Schedulers
The Learning Rate Scheduler (LRS) controls how the learning rate changes during training. The predominant choice in LLMs is the **Cosine LRS**, which decreases the learning rate following a cosine curve after a warmup (Section 4.1).
*   **Predefined Steps:** A key hyper-parameter in Cosine LRS is the step $T$ at which the learning rate reaches its minimum. Typically, $T$ is set to the total training step $S$.
*   **Continuous Training Issue:** If researchers wish to continue training beyond $S$ (e.g., to add more data), the Cosine schedule is rigid. Setting $T < S$ is not optimal (loss decreases when summed learning rate increases), but setting $T > S$ results in dropped performance (Section 4.1). This makes reusing model intermediate checkpoints for continuous training difficult without restarting or sacrificing performance.

### 2.4 Positioning of MiniCPM

`MiniCPM` positions itself as a bridge between efficient deployment and scientific rigor. It does not merely present a new model architecture but advocates for a **scientific and sustainable approach toward scaling up LLMs** using SLMs as a testbed (Section 1).

*   **Model Wind Tunnel Experiments (MWTE):** Drawing an analogy to aircraft development, the paper proposes using SLMs as "wind tunnels" to test hyper-parameters and scaling strategies before transferring them to LLMs (Section 3). This addresses the cost barrier by allowing extensive experimentation on smaller scales.
*   **Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD) Scheduler:** To solve the rigidity of Cosine LRS, `MiniCPM` introduces WSD. This scheduler explicitly divides training into a high learning rate stage and a decay stage. Crucially, it allows for **continuous training** with an un-predefined token number and makes reusing intermediate checkpoints feasible (Section 1, Section 4.2).
*   **Linear Cost Scaling Laws:** By leveraging WSD, the paper claims it can study data-model scaling laws with linear effort $O(mC)$ on the model axis and negligible effort on the data axis, rather than the traditional quadratic effort (Section 1).
*   **Revised Scaling Insights:** Using these methods, `MiniCPM` derives a compute-optimal data-to-model ratio significantly higher than `Chinchilla Optimal`—approximately 192 times larger on average, compared to `Chinchilla`'s 20 times (Section 4.5). This suggests that smaller models can absorb much more data than previously thought, enhancing their viability for inference and deployment.

In summary, `MiniCPM` situates itself not just as a competitor in the SLM leaderboard, but as a methodological framework that challenges established norms regarding learning rate scheduling, scaling law measurement, and the potential ceiling of small model capabilities.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a methodology paper that introduces a systematic framework for training Small Language Models (SLMs) to match Large Language Model (LLM) capabilities through scalable training strategies rather than architectural novelty alone. The core idea is that SLMs can serve as "wind tunnels" for testing training methodologies that transfer to larger models, while simultaneously delivering production-ready performance on edge devices.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

MiniCPM is a family of small language models (1.2B and 2.4B parameters) designed to run efficiently on smartphones and personal computers while matching the capabilities of much larger 7B–13B parameter models. The system solves the cost-capability disconnect by introducing two key innovations: a Model Wind Tunnel methodology for efficiently testing training hyper-parameters at small scale, and a Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD) learning rate scheduler that enables continuous training without predefined endpoints while revealing more efficient data-to-model scaling ratios than previously known.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The MiniCPM training pipeline consists of four major components that operate sequentially. First, the **Model Wind Tunnel Experiments** component runs hyper-parameter searches on tiny models (0.009B to 0.5B parameters) to identify optimal configurations that transfer to larger scales using Tensor Program scaling techniques. Second, the **WSD Learning Rate Scheduler** component controls the training dynamics across three distinct phases: warmup (increasing learning rate), stable training (constant high learning rate), and decay (exponential decrease). Third, the **Two-Stage Pre-training Strategy** component manages data distribution, using coarse pre-training data during stable training and mixing high-quality supervised fine-tuning (SFT) data during the decay phase. Fourth, the **Model Architecture** component implements the actual transformer structure with specific configurations for the 1.2B and 2.4B variants, including vocabulary size, layer depth, and attention mechanisms. Information flows from the wind tunnel experiments (which determine hyper-parameters) into the WSD scheduler (which governs training dynamics), which then guides how the model architecture consumes the staged training data to produce the final checkpoints.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the Model Wind Tunnel Experiments because they establish the foundational hyper-parameters that all subsequent training depends upon, and understanding this methodology explains why MiniCPM's results are reproducible across scales.
- Next, we examine the WSD Learning Rate Scheduler in detail because it is the paper's primary methodological contribution that enables continuous training and efficient scaling law measurement.
- We then analyze the Two-Stage Pre-training Strategy because it shows how the WSD scheduler's decay phase is leveraged to incorporate high-quality data more effectively than traditional SFT-only approaches.
- We proceed to the Model Architecture specifications because the concrete parameter counts, layer configurations, and vocabulary choices determine what the training methodologies actually operate on.
- Finally, we cover the Scaling Law derivation because it synthesizes all previous components to produce the paper's key theoretical insight about compute-optimal data-to-model ratios.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### 3.4.1 Model Wind Tunnel Experiments: Hyper-parameter Transfer Across Scales

The Model Wind Tunnel Experiments (MWTE) methodology treats small models as testbeds for discovering training configurations that remain stable when scaled to larger models, analogous to how aircraft designers test aerodynamics in wind tunnels before building full-scale planes. The paper employs Tensor Program techniques from Yang et al. (2022; 2023) to ensure hyper-parameter consistency across varying model scales, which is critical because adjusting hyper-parameters individually for each model size would be computationally prohibitive for LLMs. Tensor Program achieves this stability through two primary scaling operations: width scaling (adjusting hidden dimensions) and depth scaling (adjusting layer count), with specific operations detailed in Table 7 of the paper.

The hyper-parameter search was conducted on a 0.009B model trained on 0.09B tokens (10N configuration), where N represents a base token unit. Through Bayesian search over predefined parametric spaces, the authors identified optimal values: scale_depth = 1.4, scale_emb = 12, init_std = 0.1, and learning rate = 0.01 (Appendix A.1, Figure 14). The paper explicitly notes that while QK-Norm and independent weight decay could further reduce learning rate sensitivity, these were not adopted because MiniCPM's SLM focus allowed direct optimization via Tensor Program without requiring additional stabilization mechanisms.

For batch size determination, the authors follow Kaplan et al. (2020) but modify the objective from minimizing training time to minimizing token consumption for a target loss, since their fixed GPU resources mean doubling batch size approximately doubles single-step duration rather than reducing total steps (Appendix A.2). Experiments on 0.009B, 0.03B, and 0.17B models across 6 batch sizes each revealed a relationship between optimal batch size $bs$ and C4 loss $L$ expressed as:

$$bs = 1.21 \times 10^9 \times L^{6.24}$$

This relationship was derived by fitting parabolas to equal-loss points in Figure 1 and connecting the minima across model sizes, which showed a linear relationship in log space (Figure 2). The learning rate stability was verified across 0.04B, 0.1B, 0.3B, and 0.5B models, where the optimal base learning rate remained around 0.01 despite a tenfold increase in model size (Figure 3), and this was further validated on a 2.1B scale model.

#### 3.4.2 WSD Learning Rate Scheduler: Enabling Continuous Training

The Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD) learning rate scheduler represents the paper's primary methodological innovation, designed to overcome the rigidity of the standard Cosine learning rate scheduler that dominates LLM pre-training. To understand why WSD matters, we must first examine the Cosine scheduler's limitation: it requires a predefined total training step $S$, and the step $T$ at which learning rate reaches minimum must equal $S$ for optimal performance (Section 4.1). Setting $T < S$ is suboptimal because loss decreases when summed learning rate increases, but setting $T > S$ also drops performance because the learning rate should not remain high throughout training (Section 4.1, Figure 4).

The WSD scheduler explicitly divides training into three stages with distinct learning rate behaviors. The warmup stage (ending at step $W$) gradually increases learning rate to enable stable initialization. The stable training stage (from $W$ to $T$) maintains a constant high learning rate $\eta$ for exploration and convergence. The decay stage (from $T$ to $S$) exponentially decreases the learning rate to refine the model toward a local optimum. The mathematical formulation is:

$$\text{WSD}(T; s) = \begin{cases} \frac{s}{W}\eta, & s < W \\ \eta, & W < s < T \\ f(s-T)\eta, & T < s < S \end{cases}$$

where $s$ represents the current training step, $\eta$ is the maximum learning rate (0.01 for MiniCPM), and $f(s-T)$ is a decreasing function with $0 < f(s-T) \leq 1$. For MiniCPM, the decay function uses exponential annealing: $f(s-T) = 0.5^{(s-S)/T}$, where $T$ is set to 5000 steps (20B tokens) for the 2.4B model (Section 6.2).

The critical insight from WSD is that the decay stage produces a dramatic loss decrease that matches or exceeds Cosine scheduler performance at step $T = S$ (Figure 5). Experiments showed that a decay stage comprising 10% of total tokens is sufficient for full convergence, while 2.5% falls short (Section 4.3). This two-stage perspective enables checkpoint reuse: models can be trained through the stable stage, then any checkpoint can enter the decay stage to achieve optimal loss without retraining from scratch. Figure 5 demonstrates this with stable training checkpoints at 40N, 60N, and 80N tokens, each able to decay to match Cosine performance.

The training dynamics during decay reveal non-trivial behavior. Figure 7 shows that weight updates (measured as maximum element-wise difference between consecutive checkpoints) correlate strongly with learning rate magnitude, yet loss decreases minimally during stable training despite significant updates, then accelerates during decay despite smaller updates (Section 4.4). Gradient analysis on a 0.2B model (Figure 8) shows that during decay: gradient norm diminishes, cosine between consecutive gradients becomes predominantly positive (indicating consistent parameter changes), first-order directional derivative diminishes exponentially aligning with learning rate, and loss curvature increases by an order of magnitude (indicating proximity to local optimum).

#### 3.4.3 Two-Stage Pre-training Strategy: Leveraging the Decay Phase

Building on the WSD scheduler's properties, the paper proposes integrating high-quality supervised fine-tuning (SFT) data during the decay stage rather than only in a separate post-pretraining SFT phase. This strategy rests on two hypotheses: first, introducing high-quality data during annealing fosters more comprehensive model learning with pronounced loss reduction on the SFT data distribution rather than pre-training distribution, which aligns better with actual user scenarios. Second, concentrating high-quality data in the decay phase enhances training compared to uniform distribution throughout pre-training, while avoiding the negative effects of repeating small datasets in ongoing pre-training without predetermined steps (Section 5).

The experimental validation compared five configurations across 2.4B and 1.2B models (Table 1). Configuration A-1 used 2.4B model with decay on pre-training data only, followed by 4B token SFT. Configuration A-2 used 2.4B model with decay on mixed high-quality unlabeled data plus SFT data, followed by 4B token SFT. Configurations B-1, B-2, and B-3 tested 1.2B models with varying SFT token counts (6B vs 12B) and decay data compositions. Results showed A-2 outperformed A-1 across all benchmarks despite identical SFT distributions (e.g., C-Eval: 52.6 vs 40.0, GSM8K: 42.3 vs 27.7), demonstrating that adding SFT data to decay stage pushes performance boundaries beyond SFT-only improvements. Comparison between B-2 (12B SFT tokens) and B-3 (6B SFT tokens with high-quality decay data) showed B-3 achieved superior results (MATH: 10.5 vs 7.3), indicating deficiency from SFT-only approaches is not due to insufficient SFT tokens but rather the training phase timing (Section 5).

The data distribution differs between stages (Figure 11). Stable stage uses approximately 1T tokens with majority from open datasets: Code Pretrain (25.0%), CommonCrawl Chinese (25.0%), Dolma (24.0%), C4 (15.0%), Pile (8.0%), and smaller portions from peS2o, Arxiv, and Open Web Math. Decay stage introduces more diverse and proprietary data: Code Pretrain (19.6%), Dolma (15.7%), CommonCrawl Chinese (14.6%), C4 (9.5%), plus SFT-mixed data (4.8%), Baidu Baike (4.5%), Code SFT (3.8%), Book Chinese (2.8%), Knowledge SFT (2.5%), UltraChat (2.0%), Math Synthetic (1.9%), and other specialized sources. All corpora undergo deduplication within and across datasets using MinHash algorithms (Section 6.3).

#### 3.4.4 Model Architecture: Deep-and-Thin Design for SLMs

The MiniCPM family consists of two base models with distinct configurations optimized for their parameter scales (Table 2). MiniCPM-1.2B contains 1,247,442,432 non-embedding parameters with 1,536 hidden dimension, 3,840 feedforward dimension, 64 attention head dimension, 24 query heads, 8 key/value heads (using Group Query Attention), and 52 layers. MiniCPM-2.4B contains 2,442,057,984 non-embedding parameters with 2,304 hidden dimension, 5,760 feedforward dimension, 64 attention head dimension, 36 query heads, 36 key/value heads (standard multi-head attention), and 40 layers. Both models train on 1.1T tokens with batch sizes of 2M→4M (1.2B) and 4M (2.4B).

The architecture employs a deep-and-thin design philosophy, where MiniCPM-2.4B uses 40 layers compared to Phi-2's 32 layers at similar parameter scale, and MiniCPM-1.2B goes even deeper at 52 layers (Section 6.1). This aligns with recent work by Liu et al. (2024) proposing deep-and-thin networks for SLMs. Group Query Attention is applied only to MiniCPM-1.2B to further reduce parameter count, inspired by the same work. Vocabulary sizes differ: 122,753 tokens for MiniCPM-2.4B and 73,440 for MiniCPM-1.2B, where the smaller vocabulary favors efficiency without significantly harming performance (Section 6.1). Embedding parameters add 0.3B and 0.2B respectively to total parameter counts.

To minimize parameter footprint for SLMs where embeddings consume large parameter space, MiniCPM employs Shared Input-Output Layer technique, tying embedding weights between input and output (Section 6.1). However, for the MiniCPM-128K long-context variant, embedding sharing is disabled to accommodate vocabulary parallelism essential for long-context training, with the LM head initialized from input embedding instead (Section 7.2).

Training occurs in three stages using Adam Optimizer (Section 6.2). Stable training uses approximately 1T data with WSD scheduler, batch size 3.93M, and max learning rate 0.01. Decay stage uses exponential annealing over 5000 steps (20B tokens) with mixed pre-training and high-quality SFT data. SFT stage uses approximately 6B tokens with learning rate aligned to end of annealing and WSD scheduler with exponential decay. Figure 12 shows training loss curves where loss decreases sharply during decay stage, continuing to drop even after learning rate falls below 10% of maximum due to exponential decay, though final checkpoints are not utilized since separate SFT follows (Section 6.4).

#### 3.4.5 Scaling Law Measurement: Linear-Cost Data-Model Optimization

The WSD scheduler enables a fundamentally more efficient approach to measuring scaling laws compared to traditional methods. Traditional scaling experiments require training $m$ model sizes on $m$ data sizes, costing approximately $O(m^2)C$ where $C$ is the average cost of training one model on one data size (Section 4.5). With WSD, because any stable-stage checkpoint can decay to achieve optimal loss without retraining from scratch, the cost reduces to $O(mC)$—linear effort on the model axis and negligible effort on the data axis.

The scaling law measurement trained SLMs of 6 sizes (0.04B to 2B), each with 6 decayed models starting from checkpoints at 10N to 60N data during stable training (Section 4.5). Loss was evaluated on five held-out datasets, averaged by bytes rather than tokens to enable comparison across different tokenizers following Achiam et al. (2023). The loss function follows Hoffmann et al. (2022)'s formulation:

$$L(N, D) = C_N N^{-\alpha} + C_D D^{-\beta} + L_0$$

where $N$ is model size, $D$ is data size, $C_N$ and $C_D$ are coefficients, $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are exponents, and $L_0$ is irreducible loss. Given fixed compute $C = 6ND$ (Rae et al., 2021), the optimal model size $N_{opt}$ and data size $D_{opt}$ satisfy:

$$\frac{N_{opt}}{D_{opt}} = K_2 \left(\frac{C}{6}\right)^{\eta}$$

where $K = \left(\frac{\alpha C_N}{\beta C_D}\right)^{\frac{1}{\alpha+\beta}}$ and $\eta = \frac{\beta-\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}$. When $\alpha = \beta$, the ratio is constant (supporting Hoffmann et al.), when $\alpha < \beta$, parameter scaling should be emphasized (supporting Kaplan et al.), and vice versa (Section 4.5).

MiniCPM's fitted scaling law yields $\alpha = 0.29$, $\beta = 0.23$, $K_2 = 0.01$, $\eta = -0.10$ on average across evaluation corpora (Section 4.5). Since $\alpha > \beta$, results suggest slightly emphasizing data scaling over model scaling as compute increases, aligning with Hoffmann et al. (2022). However, the concrete data-to-model ratio $D_{opt}/N_{opt}$ shows a massive gap from Chinchilla: MiniCPM finds data size should be 192 times larger than model size on average, versus Chinchilla's 20 times (Section 4.5). Figure 10 shows fitted scaling curves across datasets, with Code requiring $D_{opt}/N_{opt} = 194.93$ at $C = 10^{21}$ FLOPs, English Wikihow requiring 166.04, Chinese Wikihow requiring 833.95, Ultratext requiring 95.60, Chinese Yayi Corpus requiring 112.79, and Average requiring 191.87.

The paper notes this aligns with Section 4.3 and Figure 6 observations where continuously training a 0.036B model could match 0.17B model performance with approximately 4 times more training compute but 5 times less inference compute (Section 4.3). Comparison with Llama2's training loss curves (Appendix Figure 18) suggests their actual data-model ratio during training was approximately 70–100, closer to MiniCPM's findings than Chinchilla's 20, indicating MiniCPM's configuration can absorb more data into smaller models than previous approaches (Section 4.5).

#### 3.4.6 Design Choices and Trade-offs

Several non-obvious design decisions warrant explicit explanation. The choice to use exponential decay $f(s-T) = 0.5^{(s-S)/T}$ rather than cosine decay enables loss to continue decreasing even after learning rate drops below 10% of maximum, as shown in Figure 12 where the orange decay segment continues dropping (Section 6.4). The 10% decay token allocation was determined experimentally: Figure 5 shows 2.5% decay falls short while 10% achieves full convergence across 40N, 60N, and 80N stable training checkpoints (Section 4.3).

The deep-and-thin architecture choice (more layers, smaller hidden dimensions) for SLMs contrasts with typical LLM designs and was validated through the wind tunnel experiments showing optimal learning rate stability across depth scaling despite Yang et al. (2023) observing unsatisfactory results for block depth larger than two (Section 3.1). Group Query Attention was applied only to MiniCPM-1.2B, not 2.4B, because the parameter savings mattered more at the smaller scale where every parameter counts for edge deployment (Section 6.1).

Vocabulary size differs between models: 122,753 for 2.4B enables better compression across diverse languages (achieving 3.73 bytes/token on Chinese vs 1.87 for Llama2), while 73,440 for 1.2B reduces embedding parameter overhead (Section 6.1, Appendix C Table 9). The decision to evaluate using the method yielding highest scores per model (PPL vs direct generation) addresses standardization challenges in LLM evaluation, though this introduces potential comparability concerns the authors acknowledge (Section 6.5).

The choice to not release final decay checkpoints but instead SFT-finetuned checkpoints from the last stable-stage checkpoint (dark green segment in Figure 12) reflects the two-stage pre-training strategy where decay incorporates SFT data, making separate SFT still necessary for optimal instruction following (Section 6.2, 6.4).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the paper's most novel contributions, distinguishing between incremental improvements on existing methods and fundamental innovations that shift how the field thinks about model training. Each insight is anchored to evidence from the paper and positioned against prior work.

---

### 4.1 Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD) as a Paradigm Shift in Learning Rate Scheduling

**What it is:** The WSD scheduler explicitly separates training into a constant high-learning-rate phase and a decay phase, rather than following a continuous cosine curve tied to a predefined endpoint (Section 3.4.2).

**How it differs from prior work:**

| Aspect | Cosine LRS (Prior) | WSD (MiniCPM) |
|--------|-------------------|---------------|
| Total steps | Must be predefined ($T = S$) | Can be extended dynamically |
| Checkpoint reuse | Not feasible without retraining | Any stable checkpoint can enter decay |
| Continuous training | Requires restarting with new schedule | Native support via decay stage |
| Scaling law cost | $O(m^2)C$ for $m$ sizes × $m$ data points | $O(mC)$ — linear in model axis |

The critical distinction is not the mathematical form but the **training philosophy**. Cosine LRS treats training as a single trajectory with a fixed destination. WSD treats training as a two-phase process where the exploration phase (stable) and refinement phase (decay) are decoupled. This enables researchers to "pause" training at any stable checkpoint and resume with decay, something impossible with Cosine without sacrificing performance (Section 4.1, Figure 4).

**Why it's significant:**

- **Research Efficiency:** The linear-cost scaling law measurement ($O(mC)$ vs $O(m^2)C$) reduces the computational burden of studying data-model relationships by an order of magnitude when testing multiple data sizes (Section 4.5). This makes scaling law research accessible to groups without trillion-dollar budgets.
- **Checkpoint Reusability:** Intermediate checkpoints from stable training become first-class citizens rather than transient artifacts. Figure 5 shows that checkpoints at 40N, 60N, and 80N tokens all achieve optimal loss after decay, meaning researchers can experiment with different data horizons without retraining from scratch.
- **Loss Dynamics Discovery:** The decay stage reveals non-trivial training dynamics—loss decreases dramatically despite smaller weight updates (Figure 7), and loss curvature increases by an order of magnitude (Figure 8). This suggests the decay phase navigates a distinct region of the loss landscape that Cosine LRS obscures by coupling exploration and refinement.

**Innovation classification:** **Fundamental.** This is not a hyper-parameter tweak but a reconceptualization of how learning rate schedules interact with training dynamics and research workflows.

### 4.2 Revised Compute-Optimal Data-Model Ratio: 192× vs Chinchilla's 20×

**What it is:** MiniCPM's scaling law experiments derive a compute-optimal data-to-model ratio of approximately 192:1, nearly 10× higher than the 20:1 ratio from Hoffmann et al. (2022)'s Chinchilla paper (Section 4.5).

**How it differs from prior work:**

The Chinchilla optimal ratio was derived under specific architectural and data quality assumptions from 2022. MiniCPM's finding suggests that modern configurations—better data deduplication, higher-quality corpora, and improved training stability—shift the optimal balance toward **more data per parameter**. The paper explicitly notes:

> "The data size should be 192 times larger than the model size on average, as opposed to 20 times in Hoffmann et al. (2022)." (Section 4.5)

This is not merely a coefficient adjustment but a qualitative shift in how we think about small model capacity.

**Why it's significant:**

- **SLM Viability:** If smaller models can absorb 10× more data than previously thought while remaining compute-optimal, the performance ceiling for SLMs is much higher. Figure 6 demonstrates this: a 0.036B model continuously trained with WSD matches a 0.17B model's performance with ~4× more training compute but ~5× less inference compute (Section 4.3).
- **Deployment Efficiency:** A higher data-model ratio means we can achieve target performance with smaller models, directly reducing inference costs and memory requirements on edge devices. This is the theoretical foundation enabling MiniCPM's smartphone deployment (Appendix E, Table 10).
- **Alignment with Industry Practice:** The paper notes that Llama2's actual training likely used a 70–100× ratio (estimated from their loss curves in Appendix Figure 18), closer to MiniCPM's 192× than Chinchilla's 20×. This suggests the field already intuited Chinchilla was outdated, but MiniCPM provides the first rigorous measurement explaining why (Section 4.5).

**Innovation classification:** **Fundamental.** This revises a cornerstone assumption in LLM training economics. If validated across more model families, it would reshape how organizations allocate compute budgets between model size and data volume.

### 4.3 Model Wind Tunnel Experiments as a Research Methodology

**What it is:** Using SLMs as "wind tunnels" to test hyper-parameters and scaling strategies before transferring them to LLMs, analogous to aerodynamic testing in aircraft development (Section 3.4.1).

**How it differs from prior work:**

Prior work like Tensor Program (Yang et al., 2022; 2023) focused on hyper-parameter transfer mechanics. MiniCPM elevates this to a **systematic research methodology**:

- Explicitly treats SLMs as proxies for LLM research, not just deployment targets
- Validates hyper-parameter stability across 0.009B to 2.4B scales (Figure 3 shows learning rate remains ~0.01 across 10× scale changes)
- Documents the full pipeline from wind tunnel experiments to production models

**Why it's significant:**

- **Democratizes LLM Research:** Groups without resources for 70B+ model training can still contribute to scaling law understanding by experimenting at the 1B–2B scale with confidence that findings transfer (Section 3).
- **Reduces Experimentation Risk:** Instabilities discovered at 0.009B scale (e.g., learning rate sensitivity in Appendix A.1, Figure 14) prevent costly failures at larger scales.
- **Creates Reproducible Benchmarks:** The wind tunnel setup provides a standardized testbed for comparing training strategies across the community, similar to how ImageNet standardized vision research.

**Innovation classification:** **Fundamental in methodology, incremental in technique.** The Tensor Program techniques themselves are borrowed, but framing SLMs as research wind tunnels rather than just deployment models is a conceptual shift.

### 4.4 Two-Stage Pre-training: Leveraging Decay for High-Quality Data Integration

**What it is:** Mixing high-quality SFT data into the decay phase of pre-training rather than relegating it to a separate post-pretraining SFT stage (Section 3.4.3).

**How it differs from prior work:**

Standard practice separates pre-training (coarse data) and SFT (high-quality labeled data) into distinct phases. MiniCPM hypothesizes that the decay phase's unique training dynamics—where the model refines toward a local optimum (Section 4.4)—make it an ideal window for high-quality data integration.

**Why it's significant:**

Table 1 provides direct evidence:

| Configuration | C-Eval | GSM8K | MATH |
|--------------|--------|-------|------|
| A-1 (decay: pretrain only) | 40.0 | 27.7 | 5.1 |
| A-2 (decay: pretrain + SFT data) | 52.6 | 42.3 | 5.4 |

Despite identical SFT stages, A-2 outperforms A-1 by 12.6 points on C-Eval and 14.6 points on GSM8K. This demonstrates that **when** high-quality data is introduced matters as much as **how much**.

- **Training Efficiency:** Achieves SFT-like gains without extending the separate SFT phase, reducing total training time.
- **Capability Alignment:** The decay phase's focus on refinement aligns with SFT data's goal of shaping model behavior for user scenarios (Section 5).
- **Avoids Overfitting:** Concentrating high-quality data in decay (10% of tokens) rather than uniform distribution prevents the negative effects of repeating small datasets in ongoing pre-training (Section 5).

**Innovation classification:** **Incremental but impactful.** The idea of mixing data types is not new, but strategically timing it to coincide with the decay phase's optimization dynamics is a novel application of WSD's properties.

### 4.5 Synthesis: What Distinguishes MiniCPM from Other SLMs

| Innovation | Phi Series | TinyLlama | MiniCPM |
|------------|------------|-----------|---------|
| Learning Rate Scheduler | Cosine | Cosine | WSD (continuous training) |
| Scaling Law Measurement | Not reported | Not reported | Linear-cost $O(mC)$ |
| Data-Model Ratio | Proprietary | Standard | 192:1 (empirically derived) |
| Research Methodology | Quality-focused data | Scale-focused | Wind tunnel + transfer |
| Checkpoint Reusability | No | No | Yes (stable → decay) |

MiniCPM's distinguishing feature is not raw performance alone—Phi-2 matches MiniCPM on some academic benchmarks (Table 3)—but the **scientific framework** surrounding the model. The paper contributes:

1. A training scheduler (WSD) that enables new research workflows
2. Empirical evidence revising scaling law assumptions
3. A methodology (wind tunnel) for reproducible SLM research
4. A data integration strategy (two-stage pre-training) leveraging training dynamics

These contributions extend beyond MiniCPM itself, offering tools and insights applicable to future LLM development regardless of model size.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section rigorously examines the empirical evidence supporting MiniCPM's claims. We dissect the evaluation methodology to understand how performance was measured, summarize the quantitative results across base models and variants, analyze ablation studies that validate the training strategy, and critically assess robustness and limitations. The goal is to determine whether the data convincingly supports the assertion that Small Language Models (SLMs) can match Large Language Models (LLMs) under specific conditions.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

To ensure fair comparison across models of varying sizes and architectures, the authors employ a standardized evaluation framework with specific adjustments for model capabilities.

*   **Evaluation Framework:** The team uses `UltraEval`, an open-source tool built on `vLLM` for inference acceleration (Section 6.5). This ensures that throughput metrics and benchmark scores are generated under consistent computational conditions.
*   **Datasets and Metrics:** Performance is measured across three primary domains using accuracy (%) as the main metric:
    *   **Knowledge:** `MMLU` (English), `C-Eval` and `CMMLU` (Chinese) (Section 6.5).
    *   **Reasoning:** `GSM8K` and `MATH` (Mathematics), `BBH` (Logic), `ARC-e/c` and `HellaSwag` (Commonsense) (Section 6.5).
    *   **Coding:** `HumanEval` and `MBPP` (Section 6.5).
    *   **Chat Quality:** `MTBench` (1-10 scale) for aligned models (Section 7.1).
    *   **Long Context:** `∞Bench` for 128K context models (Section 7.2).
*   **Baseline Selection:** Comparisons include established SLMs (`Phi-2`, `TinyLlama`, `Gemma-2B`) and larger LLMs (`Llama2-7B/13B`, `Mistral-7B`, `Qwen-7B`) to test the "SLM vs. LLM" parity claim (Table 3).
*   **Evaluation Strategy Nuance:** A critical methodological detail is found in Section 6.5. For QA tasks (e.g., `ARC`, `HellaSwag`), models can be evaluated via **Perplexity (PPL)** (selecting the option with lowest loss) or **Direct Generation** (generating the answer string). The authors observe significant divergence: `MiniCPM` performs better with Direct Generation, while `Mistral-7B` performs better with PPL. To ensure fairness, they report the **highest score** obtained by either method for each model. While this maximizes reported performance, it introduces a potential comparability variance that readers must acknowledge when interpreting marginal gains.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results: Base Models and Variants

The experimental results are divided into base model performance, aligned variants, and specialized architectures. The data largely supports the claim that optimized SLMs can match or exceed larger models in specific domains, though gaps remain in others.

#### 5.2.1 Base Model Performance (Table 3)
The core claim is that `MiniCPM-2.4B` matches `7B–13B` models. Table 3 provides the evidence:

*   **Math and Coding Dominance:** `MiniCPM-2.4B` significantly outperforms `Mistral-7B` in mathematics and coding.
    *   **GSM8K:** `53.83` (MiniCPM-2.4B) vs. `33.13` (Mistral-7B).
    *   **HumanEval:** `50.00` (MiniCPM-2.4B) vs. `27.44` (Mistral-7B).
    *   **MBPP:** `47.31` (MiniCPM-2.4B) vs. `45.20` (Mistral-7B).
    *   This suggests that for structured tasks with clear correct answers, the scalable training strategy compensates for the parameter deficit.
*   **Knowledge and Reasoning Gaps:** In broad knowledge and complex reasoning, the parameter count still matters.
    *   **MMLU:** `53.46` (MiniCPM-2.4B) vs. `62.69` (Mistral-7B) and `54.71` (Llama2-13B).
    *   **BBH (Big Bench Hard):** `36.87` (MiniCPM-2.4B) vs. `41.06` (Mistral-7B).
    *   The authors explicitly note in Section 6.5 that "reasoning ability might be more dependent on model size than knowledge," acknowledging this limitation.
*   **Chinese Language Capability:** `MiniCPM` shows strong localization.
    *   **C-Eval:** `51.13` (MiniCPM-2.4B) vs. `32.42` (Llama2-7B).
    *   **CMMLU:** `51.07` (MiniCPM-2.4B) vs. `31.11` (Llama2-7B).
    *   This outperformance is attributed to the data mixture (Figure 11), which includes significant Chinese corpora (CommonCrawl Chn, Baidu Baike).
*   **1.2B Model Viability:** Even the smaller `MiniCPM-1.2B` outperforms `Llama2-7B` on most metrics except `HellaSwag`, demonstrating the efficiency of the deep-and-thin architecture (Table 3).

#### 5.2.2 Aligned and Specialized Variants
The MiniCPM family extends the base models with alignment and architectural modifications.

*   **MiniCPM-DPO (Table 4, Figure 13):**
    *   After Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), the model achieves an `MTBench` score of `7.25`, surpassing `Llama2-70B-Chat` (Figure 13).
    *   However, benchmark scores slightly decrease compared to the base model (e.g., MMLU drops from `53.46` to `53.05`), illustrating the **alignment tax** (Section 7.1).
*   **MiniCPM-128K (Table 5):**
    *   On `∞Bench`, the `2.4B` model competes with `6B–7B` long-context models.
    *   **Long Book QA (Eng):** `16.33` (MiniCPM) vs. `8.38` (ChatGLM3-6B-128K).
    *   **Average Score:** `27.32` (MiniCPM) vs. `25.58` (ChatGLM3-6B-128K), despite being 2.5 times smaller (Section 7.2).
*   **MiniCPM-MoE (Table 6):**
    *   With `13.6B` total parameters but only `4B` activated per token, it approaches `Llama2-34B` performance.
    *   **MMLU:** `58.90` (MiniCPM-MoE) vs. `62.6` (Llama2-34B).
    *   **GSM8K:** `61.56` (MiniCPM-MoE) vs. `42.2` (Llama2-34B).
    *   This validates the efficiency of sparse upcycling for SLMs (Section 7.3).

#### 5.2.3 Edge Deployment Feasibility (Table 10)
A key motivation is on-device inference. After 4-bit quantization (reducing footprint to `2GB`), throughput was measured on consumer hardware (Appendix E):

*   **High-End Mobile:** iPhone 15 Pro (A17 Pro) achieves `18.0` tokens/s.
*   **Mid-Range Android:** Snapdragon 8 Gen2 devices (e.g., OPPO Find N3) achieve `~6.5` tokens/s.
*   **Older Hardware:** Snapdragon 855 devices achieve `~3.0` tokens/s.
*   These speeds are generally considered acceptable for interactive chat (typically >10 tokens/s is ideal, >5 tokens/s is readable), confirming the deployment claim for modern devices.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies: Validating the Training Strategy

The paper provides critical ablation studies in Table 1 (Section 5) to isolate the impact of the **Two-Stage Pre-training Strategy** (mixing high-quality data during the WSD decay phase). This is essential to prove that the performance gains come from the methodology, not just data volume.

*   **Impact of Decay Stage Data (A-1 vs. A-2):**
    *   **Setup:** Both use `MiniCPM-2.4B` with 4B token SFT. A-1 uses only pre-training data during decay; A-2 mixes high-quality SFT data during decay.
    *   **Result:** A-2 significantly outperforms A-1.
        *   **C-Eval:** `52.6` (A-2) vs. `40.0` (A-1).
        *   **GSM8K:** `42.3` (A-2) vs. `27.7` (A-1).
    *   **Conclusion:** Introducing high-quality data during the decay phase yields larger gains than adding it only during the separate SFT stage.
*   **Impact of SFT Token Count (B-2 vs. B-3):**
    *   **Setup:** Both use `MiniCPM-1.2B`. B-2 uses 12B SFT tokens (no mixed decay). B-3 uses 6B SFT tokens (with mixed decay).
    *   **Result:** B-3 outperforms B-2 despite having half the SFT tokens.
        *   **MATH:** `10.5` (B-3) vs. `7.3` (B-2).
        *   **MBPP:** `32.8` (B-3) vs. `29.8` (B-2).
    *   **Conclusion:** The deficiency of SFT-only approaches is not due to insufficient tokens, but the **timing** of data introduction (Section 5).

### 5.4 Critical Assessment: Strengths, Weaknesses, and Trade-offs

While the results are compelling, a critical analysis reveals conditions and limitations that temper the conclusions.

#### 5.4.1 Strengths of the Evidence
*   **Consistent Outperformance in Structured Tasks:** The gains in Math (`GSM8K`) and Code (`HumanEval`) are substantial and consistent across model sizes (1.2B and 2.4B), suggesting the training strategy reliably enhances logical capabilities.
*   **Efficiency Validation:** The edge deployment metrics (Table 10) provide concrete proof of the "deployable AI" claim, moving beyond theoretical FLOP counts to real-world tokens-per-second.
*   **Ablation Rigor:** The Table 1 experiments effectively isolate the contribution of the WSD decay stage, providing causal evidence for the Two-Stage Pre-training strategy rather than just correlational performance gains.

#### 5.4.2 Limitations and Failure Cases
*   **Reasoning Ceiling:** As noted in Section 6.5, `BBH` scores for SLMs (`36.87` for 2.4B) lag behind 7B models (`41.06` for Mistral). This indicates that while knowledge and syntax can be compressed into small models, complex multi-step reasoning still benefits from parameter scale.
*   **Qualitative Nuance:** Appendix F (Figures 19-22) shows case studies where the model succeeds technically but fails stylistically.
    *   **Translation (Figure 19):** While the model translates technical terms correctly, the phrasing "I am a newborn in Chinese" (for "freshman") is unnatural.
    *   **Instruction Following (Figure 21):** `MiniCPM-1.2B` fails to generate the correct acrostic poem structure compared to `2.4B-DPO`, showing sensitivity to model size for complex constraints.
*   **Scaling Law Validation Gap:** The paper explicitly states in Section 8 (Limitations) that it "does not extend to training an LLM to validate the scaling law." The claim that these findings transfer to trillion-parameter models remains a hypothesis based on SLM proxies, not empirical fact.
*   **Evaluation Fairness:** The practice of selecting the best score between PPL and Direct Generation (Section 6.5) maximizes MiniCPM's reported numbers but makes direct comparison with models evaluated using only one method potentially optimistic.

#### 5.4.3 Trade-offs
*   **Alignment Tax:** Achieving higher chat quality (`MTBench 7.25`) comes at the cost of raw benchmark performance (Table 4 vs. Table 3). Users must choose between a helpful assistant (DPO) and a knowledgeable base model.
*   **Vocabulary vs. Parameters:** The `1.2B` model uses a smaller vocabulary (`73,440` vs. `122,753`) to save embedding parameters (Section 6.1). While efficient, Appendix C (Table 9) shows this reduces compression rate (Bytes/Token) slightly, potentially increasing inference sequence length for the same text.
*   **Context vs. Performance:** The `128K` variant requires disabling embedding sharing (Section 7.2), which increases parameter count slightly and changes the initialization dynamics, though the paper argues this is necessary for vocabulary parallelism.

### 5.5 Conclusion on Experimental Validity

The experiments convincingly support the claim that **SLMs can match 7B–13B LLMs in specific domains** (Math, Code, Chinese Knowledge) when trained with scalable strategies like WSD and Two-Stage Pre-training. The ablation studies (Table 1) provide strong internal validity for the training methodology. However, the evidence also clarifies the boundaries of this parity: complex reasoning (`BBH`) and natural language nuance (Translation style) still favor larger models. The failure to validate scaling laws on actual LLMs (Section 8) remains the primary external validity gap, leaving the transferability of these insights to trillion-parameter models as an open question for future research.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While MiniCPM demonstrates impressive capabilities for its size, a critical examination reveals several assumptions, constraints, and open questions that temper the paper's conclusions. This section distinguishes between limitations the authors explicitly acknowledge and trade-offs that emerge from the experimental evidence.

### 6.1 Unvalidated Scaling Law Transfer to LLMs

The most significant limitation is explicitly stated in Section 8 (Limitations):

> "Although we have proposed a thorough study of the scaling law with SLMs, this paper does not extend to training an LLM to validate the scaling law."

This admission is crucial. The entire methodological framework—Model Wind Tunnel Experiments, WSD scheduler benefits, and the revised 192:1 data-model ratio—rests on the assumption that training dynamics discovered at the 1B–2B scale transfer to trillion-parameter models. While the Tensor Program techniques (Yang et al., 2022; 2023) provide theoretical grounding for hyper-parameter stability across scales, empirical validation is absent.

**Why this matters:**

- **Architectural Differences**: LLMs often employ different attention mechanisms, normalization strategies, and parallelization schemes that could interact differently with WSD scheduling.
- **Data Quality Scaling**: The high data-model ratio (192:1) assumes access to sufficiently high-quality, diverse data. At LLM scale, finding 192 tokens per parameter without significant quality degradation becomes increasingly difficult.
- **Compute Budget Reality**: The paper's linear-cost scaling law measurement ($O(mC)$) is demonstrated on SLMs. At LLM scale, even linear scaling may be prohibitively expensive for most research groups.

The authors express optimism ("we remain optimistic about its potential advantages"), but this remains an open hypothesis requiring validation.

### 6.2 Reasoning Ability Ceiling for SLMs

Despite strong performance on structured tasks, the paper acknowledges a fundamental limitation in Section 6.5:

> "Generally, BBH is harder for SLMs than LLMs compared to another knowledge-oriented dataset, demonstrating that reasoning ability might be more dependent on model size than knowledge."

**Evidence from Table 3:**

| Model | BBH | MMLU | GSM8K |
|-------|-----|------|-------|
| MiniCPM-2.4B | 36.87 | 53.46 | 53.83 |
| Mistral-7B | 41.06 | 62.69 | 33.13 |
| Llama2-13B | 37.92 | 54.71 | 21.15 |

MiniCPM-2.4B outperforms Mistral-7B on GSM8K (math) but underperforms on BBH (complex reasoning) by 4.19 points. This pattern suggests:

- **Knowledge Compression Works**: Factual knowledge and procedural skills (math, code) can be effectively compressed into small models through high-quality data and optimized training.
- **Reasoning Requires Scale**: Multi-step logical reasoning, as measured by BBH, appears less compressible. This aligns with emergent ability literature suggesting certain capabilities only manifest above specific parameter thresholds.

**Practical Implication:** Users seeking strong reasoning capabilities for complex problem-solving may still need 7B+ models, limiting MiniCPM's applicability for certain use cases.

### 6.3 Alignment Tax and Capability Trade-offs

Section 7.1 documents the alignment tax phenomenon after applying Direct Preference Optimization (DPO):

> "However, we also noticed that the performance on benchmarks is slightly compromised, which is known as the alignment tax (Askell et al., 2021)."

**Evidence from Table 3 vs. Table 4:**

| Metric | MiniCPM-2.4B (Base) | MiniCPM-2.4B-DPO | Change |
|--------|---------------------|------------------|--------|
| MMLU | 53.46 | 53.05 | -0.41 |
| HumanEval | 50.00 | 51.22 | +1.22 |
| GSM8K | 53.83 | 53.37 | -0.46 |
| MTBench | 6.89 | 7.25 | +0.36 |

The DPO model improves chat quality (MTBench) but shows slight degradation on knowledge benchmarks. This reflects a fundamental tension:

- **Helpfulness vs. Knowledge**: Optimizing for human preference may reduce the model's tendency to output raw factual knowledge in benchmark formats.
- **Use Case Specificity**: Users must choose between a base model (better for knowledge extraction, RAG systems) and an aligned model (better for conversational interfaces).

The paper does not explore whether this tax can be reduced through alternative alignment strategies or whether it scales with model size.

### 6.4 Vocabulary and Compression Trade-offs

Appendix C (Table 9) reveals a deliberate trade-off between vocabulary size and parameter efficiency:

| Model | Vocab Size | Chinese (Bytes/Token) | English (Bytes/Token) |
|-------|------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| MiniCPM-2.4B | 122,753 | 3.73 | 4.14 |
| MiniCPM-1.2B | 73,440 | 3.56 | 4.02 |
| Llama2 | 32,000 | 1.87 | 3.78 |

**The Trade-off:**

- **MiniCPM-1.2B** uses a smaller vocabulary to reduce embedding parameter overhead (embeddings add 0.2B to total parameters vs. 0.3B for 2.4B, Section 6.1).
- **Consequence**: Lower compression rate means longer token sequences for the same text, potentially increasing inference latency and memory usage during generation.
- **Benefit**: More parameters available for transformer layers, which may contribute more to capability than embedding size.

**Unaddressed Question:** The paper does not quantify the inference speed impact of reduced compression. For edge deployment where latency matters, this could be significant.

### 6.5 Evaluation Methodology Concerns

Section 6.5 acknowledges a methodological choice that affects result interpretation:

> "When reporting the scores for each model, we adopt the score from the evaluation method that yields the highest score, ensuring fairness in comparison."

The authors select between Perplexity (PPL) and Direct Generation methods based on which favors each model. While intended to ensure fairness, this introduces:

- **Comparability Variance**: Different models are effectively evaluated using different methods, making marginal differences (&lt;2 points) difficult to interpret.
- **Potential Optimism**: MiniCPM performs better with Direct Generation, while Mistral-7B performs better with PPL (Section 6.5). Reporting best-case scenarios for all models may inflate absolute performance perceptions.
- **Reproducibility Challenge**: Future researchers replicating results must implement both evaluation methods and understand the selection criteria.

This is a reasonable pragmatic choice given evaluation standardization challenges in the field, but readers should treat small performance gaps with caution.

### 6.6 WSD Scheduler Limitations for LLMs

Section 8 explicitly notes:

> "The application of WSD LRS on LLMs has not been fully explored to date."

**Specific Concerns:**

1. **Decay Stage Duration**: The 10% decay token allocation was determined on SLMs (Section 4.3). At LLM scale, the optimal decay proportion may differ due to different loss landscape characteristics.

2. **Checkpoint Reuse at Scale**: While Figure 5 demonstrates checkpoint reuse on 0.036B models, memory and storage requirements for storing multiple stable-stage checkpoints at LLM scale could be prohibitive.

3. **Learning Rate Stability**: Figure 3 shows learning rate stability across 0.04B to 2.4B models. Whether this holds at 70B+ parameters remains untested.

4. **Interaction with Other Techniques**: The paper does not explore WSD's interaction with techniques common in LLM training (e.g., gradient checkpointing, mixed precision, distributed training overhead).

### 6.7 Data Quality and Availability Constraints

The high data-model ratio (192:1) assumes access to specific data resources:

**Data Requirements (Figure 11, Section 6.3):**

- **Stable Stage**: ~1T tokens from open datasets (CommonCrawl, Dolma, C4, Pile, etc.)
- **Decay Stage**: Proprietary high-quality data including "LeetCode questions, Kindergarten through 12th grade (K12) textbooks and questions, etc." (Section 6.3)

**Constraints:**

- **Proprietary Data Dependency**: The Two-Stage Pre-training Strategy's effectiveness (Table 1, A-2 vs. A-1) relies on access to high-quality SFT data during decay. Researchers without such data may not replicate the gains.
- **Deduplication Overhead**: All corpora undergo MinHash deduplication within and across datasets (Section 6.3). This computational overhead scales with data volume and may be prohibitive for groups targeting the 192:1 ratio.
- **Language Bias**: Strong Chinese performance (C-Eval: 51.13 vs. Llama2-7B's 32.42) reflects data mixture choices. Models trained on different language distributions may not achieve similar parity.

### 6.8 Architectural Modifications for Specialized Variants

The MiniCPM family includes variants with architectural changes that introduce additional considerations:

**MiniCPM-128K (Section 7.2):**

- **Embedding Sharing Disabled**: "We disable sharing embeddings between input and output, primarily to accommodate vocabulary parallelism essential for training with long context."
- **Consequence**: Increases parameter count and changes initialization dynamics compared to base models.
- **Unaddressed**: Whether this affects the model's ability to benefit from the base model's wind tunnel-optimized hyper-parameters.

**MiniCPM-MoE (Section 7.3):**

- **Sparse Upcycling**: MLP layers are substituted with MoE layers initialized as exact replicas of dense checkpoint.
- **Load Balancing**: Additional loss term (multiplied by 0.01) ensures balanced expert utilization.
- **Unaddressed**: Whether MoE routing introduces instability during the WSD decay phase, given that routing decisions add another optimization dimension.

### 6.9 Qualitative Capability Gaps

Appendix F (Figures 19-22) provides case studies revealing nuanced limitations:

**Translation (Figure 19):**

> "We notice that MiniCPM-2.4B-DPO performs better in translating the technical part, but both models are unnatural in expressing 'just starting to learn Chinese.'"

The model translates "freshman" as "nouveau-né" (newborn) in French, which is technically correct but stylistically awkward. This suggests:

- **Idiomatic Understanding Gap**: SLMs may struggle with cultural and idiomatic nuances even when technical accuracy is achieved.
- **Training Data Reflection**: The general-purpose corpus (Section 6.3) may not include sufficient idiomatic translation examples.

**Instruction Following (Figure 21):**

- **MiniCPM-1.2B**: Generates a poem but fails to maintain the correct acrostic structure (first character of each line should spell "龙年大吉").
- **MiniCPM-2.4B-DPO**: Successfully follows the constraint.

This indicates that complex constraint satisfaction remains sensitive to model size, even within the MiniCPM family.

### 6.10 Summary of Key Limitations

| Limitation | Severity | Addressed in Paper |
|------------|----------|-------------------|
| Scaling law unvalidated at LLM scale | High | Yes (Section 8) |
| Reasoning ability ceiling (BBH) | Medium | Yes (Section 6.5) |
| Alignment tax after DPO | Medium | Yes (Section 7.1) |
| WSD untested on LLMs | High | Yes (Section 8) |
| Evaluation method selection bias | Low-Medium | Yes (Section 6.5) |
| Proprietary data dependency | Medium | Partially (Section 6.3) |
| Qualitative nuance gaps | Medium | Yes (Appendix F) |
| Vocabulary compression trade-off | Low | Yes (Appendix C) |

**Critical Takeaway:** MiniCPM's contributions are strongest within the SLM domain (1B–3B parameters) where empirical validation exists. The extension of these findings to LLM scale remains a compelling hypothesis rather than established fact. Users should adopt MiniCPM for edge deployment and knowledge-intensive tasks while recognizing that complex reasoning and LLM-scale transferability require further validation.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section synthesizes how `MiniCPM` reshapes thinking about model development, identifies concrete research pathways it opens, and provides practical guidance for practitioners deciding whether to adopt these methods. The implications extend beyond the specific 1.2B and 2.4B models to fundamental questions about how we train, evaluate, and deploy language models.

### 7.1 Shifting the Field's Assumptions

`MiniCPM` challenges three entrenched assumptions in language model development, each with cascading effects on how researchers and organizations allocate resources.

#### 7.1.1 The Data-Model Ratio Paradigm

The most significant theoretical contribution is the revised compute-optimal data-to-model ratio. The paper derives:

> "The data size should be 192 times larger than the model size on average, as opposed to 20 times in Hoffmann et al. (2022)." (Section 4.5)

This 10× revision fundamentally alters training economics:

| Implication | Before (Chinchilla 20:1) | After (MiniCPM 192:1) |
|-------------|-------------------------|----------------------|
| **Data Collection Priority** | Secondary to model scaling | Primary investment target |
| **Small Model Viability** | Limited capability ceiling | Can absorb massive data volumes |
| **Compute Allocation** | More parameters, less data | Fewer parameters, more data |
| **Inference Efficiency** | Larger models for target performance | Smaller models achieve same performance |

**Why this matters:** If validated at LLM scale, organizations could achieve equivalent performance with 5–10× smaller models by investing in data quality and volume instead. This directly reduces inference costs, memory requirements, and deployment complexity.

#### 7.1.2 SLMs as Research Proxies, Not Just Deployment Targets

Prior work treated SLMs primarily as deployment-optimized models. `MiniCPM` reframes them as **scientific instruments**:

> "These experiments take the spirit of wind tunnel testing in developing an aircraft, thus we name it Model Wind Tunnel Experiments (MWTE)." (Section 3)

This conceptual shift enables:

- **Democratized Scaling Research:** Groups without resources for 70B+ training can still contribute to scaling law understanding by experimenting at 1B–2B scale with confidence findings transfer (Section 3).
- **Faster Iteration Cycles:** Hyper-parameter instability discovered at 0.009B scale (Appendix A.1, Figure 14) prevents costly failures at larger scales.
- **Standardized Methodology:** The wind tunnel setup provides a reproducible testbed for comparing training strategies, similar to how ImageNet standardized vision research.

#### 7.1.3 Training Schedules as Research Enablers

The `WSD` scheduler is not merely a performance optimization—it enables new research workflows previously impossible with `Cosine LRS`:

| Research Activity | Cosine LRS | WSD LRS |
|------------------|------------|---------|
| **Extend training mid-run** | Requires restart | Native support via decay stage |
| **Reuse intermediate checkpoints** | Not feasible | Any stable checkpoint can decay (Figure 5) |
| **Measure scaling laws** | $O(m^2)C$ cost | $O(mC)$ cost (Section 4.5) |
| **Experiment with data horizons** | Fixed at training start | Dynamic adjustment possible |

**The key insight:** WSD decouples exploration (stable stage) from refinement (decay stage), treating them as independent phases rather than a single coupled trajectory. This enables researchers to "pause" training at any stable checkpoint and resume with decay—something impossible with Cosine without sacrificing performance (Section 4.1, Figure 4).

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Pathways

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions and implicitly suggests others through its limitations. We categorize these by priority and feasibility.

#### 7.2.1 High Priority: Validating Transfer to LLM Scale

The most critical open question is explicitly stated in Section 8 (Limitations):

> "This paper does not extend to training an LLM to validate the scaling law. The application of WSD LRS on LLMs has not been fully explored to date."

**Specific validation needed:**

1. **Learning Rate Stability at Scale:** Figure 3 shows learning rate stability across 0.04B to 2.4B models (optimal rate remains ~0.01). Whether this holds at 70B+ parameters remains untested.

2. **Decay Stage Proportion:** The 10% decay token allocation was determined on SLMs (Section 4.3). At LLM scale, loss landscape characteristics may differ, requiring different decay proportions.

3. **Checkpoint Storage Feasibility:** Figure 5 demonstrates checkpoint reuse on 0.036B models. At LLM scale, storing multiple stable-stage checkpoints for decay experiments could require petabytes of storage.

4. **Interaction with LLM-Specific Techniques:** The paper does not explore WSD's interaction with gradient checkpointing, mixed precision training, or distributed training overhead common in LLM pipelines.

**Recommended approach:** Start with 7B–13B models (e.g., `Llama2-7B` architecture) before attempting 70B+ validation. This provides intermediate-scale evidence while remaining computationally feasible for well-funded research groups.

#### 7.2.2 Medium Priority: Understanding Training Dynamics

Section 4.4 provides初步 analysis of decay stage dynamics but explicitly reserves deeper investigation for future work:

> "These findings potentially offer a deeper insight into the shape optimization space, a subject reserved for future exploration." (Section 4.4)

**Specific research questions:**

- **Why does loss decrease dramatically during decay despite smaller weight updates?** Figure 7 shows weight updates correlate with learning rate magnitude, yet loss decreases minimally during stable training despite significant updates, then accelerates during decay despite smaller updates (Section 4.4).

- **What does increased loss curvature indicate?** Figure 8 shows loss curvature increases by an order of magnitude during decay, indicating proximity to local optimum. Does this suggest the decay stage navigates a distinct region of the loss landscape?

- **How do gradient angles evolve?** The cosine between consecutive gradients becomes predominantly positive during decay, suggesting consistent parameter changes. Is this behavior universal across architectures?

**Methodology suggestion:** Apply techniques from optimization theory (e.g., Hessian eigenvalue analysis, loss landscape visualization) to characterize the decay stage more rigorously.

#### 7.2.3 Medium Priority: Reasoning Capability Gap

Section 6.5 acknowledges a fundamental limitation:

> "Generally, BBH is harder for SLMs than LLMs compared to another knowledge-oriented dataset, demonstrating that reasoning ability might be more dependent on model size than knowledge." (Section 6.5)

**Table 3 evidence:**

| Model | BBH | MMLU | GSM8K |
|-------|-----|------|-------|
| MiniCPM-2.4B | 36.87 | 53.46 | 53.83 |
| Mistral-7B | 41.06 | 62.69 | 33.13 |

MiniCPM-2.4B outperforms Mistral-7B on GSM8K (math) but underperforms on BBH (complex reasoning) by 4.19 points.

**Research directions:**

- **Is reasoning fundamentally non-compressible?** Or can alternative training strategies (e.g., chain-of-thought pre-training, synthetic reasoning data) close this gap?
- **Does the 192:1 ratio apply differently to reasoning tasks?** Figure 10 shows scaling law coefficients vary by task (Code: $\alpha=0.37, \beta=0.34$; Chinese Wikihow: $\alpha=0.35, \beta=0.18$). Reasoning tasks may have distinct optimal ratios.
- **What architectural changes help?** The paper uses deep-and-thin design. Would wider models with fewer layers improve reasoning at similar parameter counts?

#### 7.2.4 Lower Priority: Data Quality and Composition

The Two-Stage Pre-training Strategy's effectiveness depends on access to high-quality proprietary data (Section 6.3):

> "The data with the suffix SFT is our proprietary data including LeetCode questions, Kindergarten through 12th grade (K12) textbooks and questions, etc." (Section 6.3)

**Open questions:**

- **What is the minimum data quality threshold?** Can open-source high-quality datasets (e.g., `OpenOrca`, `SlimOrca`) substitute for proprietary data?
- **How does data composition affect the 192:1 ratio?** The paper's ratio is averaged across evaluation corpora (Figure 10). Different data mixtures may shift optimal ratios.
- **What is the impact of deduplication rigor?** All corpora undergo MinHash deduplication within and across datasets (Section 6.3). How sensitive are results to deduplication thresholds?

**Practical research need:** Publish ablation studies on data quality requirements to enable replication by groups without proprietary datasets.

#### 7.3.2 Research and Education Contexts

The Model Wind Tunnel methodology enables specific research workflows:

**For academic researchers:**
- **Hyper-parameter experimentation:** Test configurations at 0.009B–0.5B scale before committing resources to larger training runs.
- **Scaling law studies:** Use WSD scheduler to measure data-model relationships with $O(mC)$ cost instead of $O(m^2)C$.
- **Teaching LLM training:** The 1.2B model can be trained on modest GPU clusters, making it suitable for graduate courses on language model development.

**For industry R&D:**
- **Rapid prototyping:** Validate new training techniques or data mixtures at small scale before full-scale deployment.
- **A/B testing:** Compare multiple training strategies in parallel using SLM proxies.
- **Risk mitigation:** Identify training instabilities early before investing in expensive LLM runs.

#### 7.3.3 Specialized Domain Applications

The MiniCPM family includes variants optimized for specific use cases:

**MiniCPM-128K (Section 7.2):**
- **Long document analysis:** Legal contracts, academic papers, technical manuals where context exceeds 4K tokens.
- **Multi-turn conversation:** Extended dialogue history without truncation.
- **Codebase understanding:** Analyze entire files or small projects within context window.

**Performance note:** Table 5 shows MiniCPM-2.4B-128K achieves `27.32` average on ∞Bench vs. `25.58` for ChatGLM3-6B-128K, despite being 2.5× smaller (Section 7.2).

- **Note:** Accept ~0.4 point degradation on knowledge benchmarks (alignment tax, Section 7.1).

#### 7.3.4 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) Systems

Small models are particularly well-suited for RAG architectures where external knowledge compensates for parameter limitations:

**Why MiniCPM works well for RAG:**

**When to prefer over larger models:**

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

Practicians considering adopting `MiniCPM` methods should understand the requirements, trade-offs, and integration considerations.

#### 7.4.1 Requirements for Reproducing Results

**Data requirements (Figure 11, Section 6.3):**

| Stage | Token Count | Key Sources | Accessibility |
|-------|-------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Stable Training** | ~1T tokens | CommonCrawl, Dolma, C4, Pile, Code Pretrain | Mostly open |
| **Decay Stage** | ~20B tokens | Mixed pretrain + proprietary SFT data | Partially proprietary |
| **SFT Stage** | ~6B tokens | Proprietary SFT data | Proprietary |

**Critical dependency:** The Two-Stage Pre-training Strategy's effectiveness (Table 1, A-2 vs. A-1) relies on access to high-quality data during decay. Researchers without proprietary SFT data may not replicate the full performance gains.

**Compute requirements:**
- **MiniCPM-1.2B:** 1.1T tokens at batch size 2M→4M (Section 6.1, Table 2)
- **MiniCPM-2.4B:** 1.1T tokens at batch size 4M (Section 6.1, Table 2)
- **Estimated FLOPs:** Approximately $6ND$ per Section 4.5 formula

**Infrastructure notes:**
- 4-bit quantization reduces footprint to 2GB (Appendix D)
- Embedding sharing reduces parameters but requires careful initialization (Section 6.1)
- Group Query Attention only on 1.2B model (Section 6.1)

#### 7.4.2 When to Use WSD Scheduler

**Recommended scenarios:**

| Scenario | WSD Benefit | Confidence Level |
|----------|-------------|------------------|
| **Continuous training** | Native support for extending training | High (validated in Section 4.3) |
| **Checkpoint reuse** | Any stable checkpoint can decay | High (Figure 5 demonstrates) |
| **Scaling law research** | $O(mC)$ vs. $O(m^2)C$ cost | High (Section 4.5 derivation) |
| **LLM pre-training** | Untested at 70B+ scale | Low (Section 8 limitation) |
| **Fine-tuning** | Not evaluated for SFT-only | Unknown (paper uses WSD for pretrain + SFT) |

**Implementation considerations:**
- **Decay proportion:** Use 10% of total tokens for decay stage (Section 4.3 shows 2.5% falls short)
- **Decay function:** Exponential annealing $f(s-T) = 0.5^{(s-S)/T}$ (Section 6.2)
- **Learning rate:** Max rate of 0.01 remained stable across 0.04B to 2.4B (Figure 3)

#### 7.4.3 When to Prefer MiniCPM Over Alternatives

**Choose MiniCPM when:**

1. **Edge deployment is required:** 2GB footprint after quantization enables smartphone deployment (Appendix E).

2. **Chinese language support is critical:** C-Eval score of 51.13 vs. Llama2-7B's 32.42 reflects data mixture choices (Table 3, Section 6.5).

3. **Math and code capabilities are priorities:** GSM8K score of 53.83 outperforms Mistral-7B's 33.13 (Table 3).

4. **Research budget is limited:** Wind tunnel methodology enables hyper-parameter testing at small scale before larger commits.

5. **Continuous training is needed:** WSD scheduler supports extending training without restart (Section 4.2).

**Choose alternatives when:**

1. **Complex reasoning is the primary requirement:** BBH scores lag behind 7B models (36.87 vs. 41.06 for Mistral-7B, Table 3).

2. **LLM-scale validation is required:** Scaling law transfer to 70B+ models remains unvalidated (Section 8).

3. **Proprietary data is unavailable:** Two-Stage Pre-training effectiveness depends on high-quality decay data (Table 1).

4. **Maximum knowledge breadth is needed:** MMLU scores still favor 7B+ models (53.46 vs. 62.69 for Mistral-7B, Table 3).

#### 7.4.4 Integration Checklist

For teams adopting `MiniCPM` methods:

- [ ] **Verify data access:** Ensure access to ~1T tokens for stable training + high-quality data for decay stage
- [ ] **Plan compute budget:** Account for 1.1T token training per model variant
- [ ] **Select appropriate variant:** 1.2B for maximum efficiency, 2.4B for best performance, MoE for throughput
- [ ] **Implement WSD scheduler:** Use 10% decay proportion with exponential annealing
- [ ] **Configure evaluation:** Decide between PPL vs. Direct Generation per task (Section 6.5 notes divergence)
- [ ] **Plan for quantization:** 4-bit quantization reduces footprint to 2GB but requires calibration data (Appendix D)
- [ ] **Account for alignment tax:** DPO improves chat quality but may reduce benchmark scores by ~0.4 points (Section 7.1)

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision

The paper concludes with an aspirational framing:

> "MiniCPM propounds a new stage in the development of small language models, exemplifying the latent potential within SLMs and advocating for a more scientific and sustainable approach toward scaling up LLMs." (Section 1)

**The broader vision:**

1. **Sustainable AI Development:** Smaller models with optimized training reduce environmental and financial costs of AI development.

2. **Democratized Research:** Wind tunnel methodology enables groups without trillion-dollar budgets to contribute to scaling law understanding.

3. **Ubiquitous Deployment:** Edge-capable models enable AI integration into everyday devices without cloud dependency.

4. **Scientific Rigor:** Systematic measurement of training dynamics (e.g., decay stage analysis in Section 4.4) moves the field from empirical observation to mechanistic understanding.

**Open challenge:** The field must now validate whether these SLM-derived insights transfer to LLM scale. If they do, `MiniCPM` represents a paradigm shift in how we think about model development. If they don't, the work still provides valuable tools for SLM optimization and edge deployment.

**Final recommendation:** Adopt `MiniCPM` methods for SLM development and edge deployment with high confidence. Treat LLM-scale transfer as a promising hypothesis requiring validation before committing large-scale training budgets.
