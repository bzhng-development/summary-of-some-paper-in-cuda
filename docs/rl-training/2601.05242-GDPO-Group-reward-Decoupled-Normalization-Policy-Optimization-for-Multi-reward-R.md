## 1. Executive Summary
This paper demonstrates that directly applying Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) to multi-reward settings causes distinct rollout reward combinations to collapse into identical advantage values, and introduces Group reward-Decoupled Normalization Policy Optimization (GDPO) to resolve this by decoupling the normalization of individual rewards. GDPO consistently outperforms GRPO across tool calling, math reasoning, and coding tasks, achieving up to 6.3% higher accuracy on AIME with DeepSeek-R1-1.5B and improving format compliance from 76.33% to 80.66% on BFCL-v3 with Qwen2.5-Instruct-1.5B. By preserving the resolution of the training signal across heterogeneous preferences, GDPO enables more accurate multi-reward optimization and prevents the early training failure often observed in standard GRPO pipelines.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Growing Demand for Multi-Preference Alignment

As language models become more capable, user expectations have expanded beyond simple accuracy. Modern applications require models to satisfy **multiple, heterogeneous preferences simultaneously**: responses must be correct, but also safe, efficient, properly formatted, and appropriately concise. The Introduction (Section 1) identifies these preferences as spanning "efficiency, safety, response coherence and logic, gender biases and many other objectives." Meeting such diverse requirements within a single model represents a fundamental challenge in alignment research.

Reinforcement Learning (RL) has emerged as the **de facto training pipeline** for achieving this alignment. Rather than relying on a single reward signal, recent RL-based approaches incorporate **multiple rewards**, each designed to capture a different human preference. For example, a tool-calling system might need separate rewards for:
- **Correctness**: Did the model call the right tool with the right parameters?
- **Format compliance**: Did the output follow the required JSON structure?

Similarly, a math reasoning system might optimize for:
- **Accuracy**: Is the final answer correct?
- **Length constraint**: Did the model solve the problem efficiently without excessive tokens?

### The Overlooked Assumption: GRPO as Default Optimizer

Despite the growing adoption of multi-reward RL, a critical assumption has gone unexamined. As Section 2 states:

> "Recent work has largely focused on the reward design itself and often directly relied on applying Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) directly for multi-reward RL optimization, often without examining whether GRPO is well-suited for optimizing combinations of heterogeneous rewards."

**GRPO** (Group Relative Policy Optimization), introduced by DeepSeek-R1, has become widely adopted due to its efficiency and simplicity. Unlike Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), GRPO eliminates the need for a separate value model by leveraging **group-relative advantage estimation**. For a given question, GRPO samples a group of $G$ responses, computes rewards for each, and normalizes them within the group to produce advantage values.

The standard multi-reward GRPO pipeline works as follows:

1. **Aggregate rewards first**: For each rollout $j$, sum all $n$ reward components:
   $$r^{(i,j)}_{\text{sum}} = r^{(i,j)}_1 + \cdots + r^{(i,j)}_n$$

2. **Normalize after aggregation**: Apply group-wise normalization to the summed rewards:
   $$A^{(i,j)}_{\text{sum}} = \frac{r^{(i,j)}_{\text{sum}} - \text{mean}\{r^{(i,1)}_{\text{sum}}, \ldots, r^{(i,G)}_{\text{sum}}\}}{\text{std}\{r^{(i,1)}_{\text{sum}}, \ldots, r^{(i,G)}_{\text{sum}}\}}$$

This approach treats the multi-reward setting as if it were a single-reward problem, which the paper identifies as fundamentally problematic.

### The Core Problem: Reward Signal Collapse

The paper's key insight is that **GRPO inherently compresses the reward signal** when applied to multi-reward settings. Section 2 provides a concrete illustration using a simple scenario with two binary rewards ($r_1, r_2 \in \{0, 1\}$) and two rollouts per question.

In this setting, the total reward for each rollout can take values from $\{0, 1, 2\}$. When enumerating all possible rollout reward combinations within a group, there are **six distinct combinations** when order is ignored: $(0,0), (0,1), (0,2), (1,1), (1,2), (2,2)$. However, after applying GRPO's group-wise normalization:

- Combinations $(0,1), (0,2),$ and $(1,2)$ all yield **identical normalized advantages** of $(-0.7071, 0.7071)$
- Combinations $(0,0), (1,1),$ and $(2,2)$ all result in $(0, 0)$

This means **only two unique advantage groups emerge** from six distinct reward scenarios. The paper terms this phenomenon **"reward signal collapse."**

**Why does this matter?** Intuitively, a rollout pair with rewards $(0, 2)$ should produce a **stronger learning signal** than $(0, 1)$, because a total reward of 2 indicates simultaneous satisfaction of both objectives, whereas 1 corresponds to achieving only one. When the other rollout receives zero reward, $(0, 2)$ should yield a larger relative advantage than $(0, 1)$. GRPO's normalization erases this distinction, leading to:

- **Inaccurate policy updates**: The model cannot distinguish between meaningfully different performance levels
- **Suboptimal convergence**: Training plateaus at lower performance
- **Early training failure**: In some cases, training collapses entirely (as shown in Figure 5, where correctness reward declines after ~400 steps with GRPO)

### Prior Attempts to Address the Issue

The paper examines whether existing GRPO variants already solve this problem. Recent work such as **Dr.GRPO** and **DeepSeek-v3.2** adopt a modification that removes the standard deviation normalization term from the advantage calculation:

$$A^{(i,j)}_{\text{sum}} = r^{(i,j)}_{\text{sum}} - \text{mean}\{r^{(i,1)}_{\text{sum}}, \ldots, r^{(i,G)}_{\text{sum}}\}$$

This change was originally introduced to mitigate **question-level difficulty bias**, but it appears to address reward collapse at first glance. Indeed, in the two-rollout example, $(0,1)$ and $(0,2)$ now yield distinct advantages of $(-0.5, 0.5)$ and $(-1.0, 1.0)$, respectively.

However, the paper demonstrates this fix is **insufficient at scale**. Figure 3 shows that when generalizing to larger numbers of rollouts or rewards, removing standard deviation normalization only **slightly increases** the number of distinct advantage groups compared to standard GRPO. The fundamental compression problem persists. Empirically, Section 4.1.1 confirms that "GRPO w/o std" fails to improve convergence or downstream performance—in the tool-calling task, it achieves **0% format correctness** on BFCL-v3 (Table 2), indicating complete failure to learn the required output structure.

The Related Work section (Section 5) surveys broader multi-reward RL literature:

The common thread across prior work is that they **focus on reward design** while **assuming GRPO is the default optimization method**. None systematically examine whether GRPO's advantage computation is appropriate for heterogeneous reward combinations.

### How This Paper Positions Itself

This work takes a fundamentally different approach by **revisiting the optimization method itself** rather than designing new reward functions. The paper positions GDPO as:

1. **A diagnostic contribution**: Identifying reward signal collapse as a previously overlooked limitation in multi-reward GRPO
2. **A methodological contribution**: Proposing decoupled normalization as a minimal but effective modification to GRPO
3. **A practical contribution**: Providing systematic guidance on reward weighting and conditioning for priority variation (Section 3.2)

Unlike prior work that adds complexity through hierarchical rewards or multiple models, GDPO maintains GRPO's simplicity while fixing its core weakness in multi-reward settings. The paper validates this across **three distinct task domains** (tool calling, math reasoning, coding) with **multiple model sizes** (1.5B to 7B parameters), demonstrating that the improvement is not task-specific or model-specific but stems from the optimization method itself.

### Real-World Significance

The importance of this work extends beyond theoretical interest. Multi-reward RL is becoming standard practice for deploying language models in production:

- **Tool-calling agents** must balance accuracy with format compliance to integrate with downstream systems
- **Reasoning models** must trade off correctness against computational cost (token count)
- **Code generation** systems must optimize for pass rate, bug ratio, and output length simultaneously

If the underlying optimization method compresses reward signals, these systems will converge to **suboptimal behavior** regardless of how carefully rewards are designed. GDPO addresses this foundational issue, enabling more faithful alignment with diverse human preferences in real-world deployments.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

GDPO is a reinforcement learning optimization algorithm that modifies how advantage values are computed when training language models with multiple reward signals simultaneously. It solves the reward signal collapse problem by normalizing each reward type independently before combining them, rather than combining rewards first and then normalizing the aggregate, which preserves meaningful distinctions between different performance levels that GRPO would otherwise erase.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The GDPO training pipeline consists of four sequential components that process rollout data into policy updates. First, the **Rollout Generator** samples multiple responses (typically 4–16 rollouts per question) from the current policy model for each training question. Second, the **Reward Computation Module** evaluates each rollout against all reward objectives independently, producing a vector of reward scores per rollout rather than a single scalar. Third, the **Decoupled Normalization Engine** applies group-wise normalization separately to each reward dimension across the rollout group, then sums the normalized advantages and applies batch-wise normalization to stabilize the final scale. Fourth, the **Policy Update Module** uses these computed advantages to perform clipped policy gradient updates, adjusting model parameters to increase the likelihood of high-advantage responses. Information flows unidirectionally from rollouts through reward computation, through decoupled normalization, into advantage values, and finally into gradient-based parameter updates.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a methods paper proposing a modification to reinforcement learning optimization, and the core idea is that normalizing each reward dimension independently before aggregation preserves training signal resolution that would otherwise be lost through premature reward summation.

**The GRPO Baseline: What GDPO Modifies**

To understand GDPO, we must first establish the GRPO computation it replaces. GRPO operates on groups of rollouts sampled for each training question. For a given question-answer pair $(q_i, o_j)$, the behavior policy $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ samples a group of $G$ responses $\{o_j\}_{j=1}^G$, and assuming $n$ objectives, the aggregated reward for the $j$-th response is computed as the sum of each objective's reward before any normalization occurs:

$$r^{(i,j)}_{\text{sum}} = r^{(i,j)}_1 + \cdots + r^{(i,j)}_n$$

Here, $r^{(i,j)}_k$ represents the reward for the $k$-th objective on the $j$-th rollout for question $i$. This summation happens first, collapsing all reward dimensions into a single scalar per rollout. The group-relative advantage for the $j$-th response is then obtained by normalizing these group-level aggregated rewards using mean and standard deviation computed across the $G$ rollouts in the group:

$$A^{(i,j)}_{\text{sum}} = \frac{r^{(i,j)}_{\text{sum}} - \text{mean}\{r^{(i,1)}_{\text{sum}}, \ldots, r^{(i,G)}_{\text{sum}}\}}{\text{std}\{r^{(i,1)}_{\text{sum}}, \ldots, r^{(i,G)}_{\text{sum}}\}}$$

This normalization produces advantage values that indicate how much better or worse each rollout performed relative to its group peers. The multi-reward GRPO optimization objective then uses these advantages to weight policy gradient updates:

$$\mathcal{J}_{\text{GRPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(q_i, o_j) \sim D, \{o_j\}_{j=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|q)} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{j=1}^G \frac{1}{|o_j|} \sum_{t=1}^{|o_j|} \min\left(s_{i,t}(\theta) A^{(i,j)}_{\text{sum}}, \text{clip}(s_{i,t}(\theta), 1 - \epsilon, 1 + \epsilon) A^{(i,j)}_{\text{sum}}\right) \right]$$

where $s_{i,t}(\theta) = \frac{\pi_{\theta}(o^t_j | q, o^{<t}_j)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(o^t_j | q, o^{<t}_j)}$ is the importance ratio comparing the new policy probability to the old policy probability for token $t$, and $\epsilon$ denotes the clipping threshold that prevents excessively large policy updates. The key limitation is that reward summation occurs before normalization, which causes distinct reward combinations to map to identical advantage values as demonstrated in Section 2.

**GDPO Core Mechanism: Decoupled Per-Reward Normalization**

GDPO fundamentally changes the order of operations by performing group-wise normalization on each reward separately before any aggregation occurs. Concretely, rather than summing all $n$ rewards first and then applying group-wise normalization, GDPO computes the normalized advantage for each individual reward for the $j$-th rollout of the $i$-th question as:

$$A^{(i,j)}_1 = \frac{r^{(i,j)}_1 - \text{mean}\{r^{(i,1)}_1, \ldots, r^{(i,G)}_1\}}{\text{std}\{r^{(i,1)}_1, \ldots, r^{(i,G)}_1\}}$$

$$\ldots$$

$$A^{(i,j)}_n = \frac{r^{(i,j)}_n - \text{mean}\{r^{(i,1)}_n, \ldots, r^{(i,G)}_n\}}{\text{std}\{r^{(i,1)}_n, \ldots, r^{(i,G)}_n\}}$$

Each reward dimension $k$ receives its own mean and standard deviation computed across the $G$ rollouts, producing $n$ separate normalized advantage values per rollout rather than one. This decoupling ensures that the normalization statistics for reward 1 (e.g., correctness) are not contaminated by the distribution of reward 2 (e.g., format compliance), preserving the relative ranking information within each reward dimension independently.

The overall advantage used for policy updates is then obtained by summing these normalized advantages across all objectives:

$$A^{(i,j)}_{\text{sum}} = A^{(i,j)}_1 + \cdots + A^{(i,j)}_n$$

This summation occurs after normalization rather than before, which is the critical distinction from GRPO. When rewards are normalized individually first, a rollout that achieves both rewards (e.g., reward combination $(0, 2)$ in the binary example) receives a different advantage than a rollout achieving only one reward (e.g., $(0, 1)$), because each reward dimension contributes its own normalized signal. In the two-binary-reward, two-rollout example from Figure 2, GRPO maps $(0, 1)$ and $(0, 2)$ to identical advantages of $(-0.7071, 0.7071)$, while GDPO assigns $(-0.7071, 0.7071)$ to $(0, 1)$ and $(-1.4142, 1.4142)$ to $(0, 2)$, correctly reflecting that $(0, 2)$ should produce a stronger learning signal.

**Batch-Wise Advantage Normalization: Stabilizing Advantage Scale**

After summing the per-reward normalized advantages, GDPO applies an additional batch-wise normalization step to ensure that the numerical scale of the final advantage remains stable regardless of how many rewards are included. This step computes mean and standard deviation across all rollouts in the entire training batch (not just within each question's group):

$$\hat{A}^{(i,j)}_{\text{sum}} = \frac{A^{(i,j)}_{\text{sum}} - \text{mean}\{A^{(i',j')}_{\text{sum}} \mid i' \in D_{\text{Batch}}, j' = 1, \ldots, G\}}{\text{std}\{A^{(i',j')}_{\text{sum}} \mid i' \in D_{\text{Batch}}, j' = 1, \ldots, G\} + \epsilon}$$

The small constant $\epsilon$ is added to the denominator to prevent division by zero when advantage variance is negligible. This batch-wise normalization serves two purposes. First, it prevents the advantage magnitude from growing linearly as additional rewards are introduced, which could destabilize training by producing excessively large gradient updates. Second, it ensures consistent advantage scales across different training batches, which improves optimization stability. The paper empirically validates this design choice in Appendix A, where removing batch-wise normalization occasionally leads to convergence failures, as shown in Figure 8 where some runs without normalization fail to converge on format reward while others succeed.

**Why Decoupled Normalization Works: Preserving Advantage Granularity**

The effectiveness of GDPO stems from maintaining a larger number of distinct advantage groups compared to GRPO. Figure 3 quantifies this by plotting the count of distinct advantage groups as a function of both the number of rollouts (left panel) and the number of rewards (right panel). When varying the number of rollouts from 2 to 16 with two rewards fixed, GDPO consistently produces substantially more distinct advantage groups than both GRPO and GRPO without standard deviation normalization, with the gap widening as rollout count increases. When fixing rollouts at four and increasing rewards from 2 to 8, GDPO exhibits progressively larger advantage granularity as the objective count grows, while GRPO and GRPO w/o std show only modest improvements. This demonstrates that decoupled normalization effectively increases advantage expressiveness across all RL settings, enabling more precise advantage estimation that translates to better policy updates.

**Reward Weighting for Priority Variation**

Up to this point, the formulation assumes all objectives carry equal importance, but in practice different preferences have different priorities. It is common practice to assign different weights to each reward to encode different priorities among objectives, such that the weighted sum becomes $r_{\text{sum}} = w_1 r_1 + \cdots + w_n r_n$. For GDPO, these weightings are applied to the normalized advantages of each reward rather than to raw rewards:

$$A^{(i,j)}_{\text{sum}} = w_1 A^{(i,j)}_1 + \cdots + w_n A^{(i,j)}_n$$

However, the paper identifies a critical limitation: adjusting reward weights does not always yield the intended behavior when the difficulty levels of the underlying objectives differ substantially. If one objective is much easier than the others, the model often focuses on maximizing the reward for that objective regardless of the assigned weights. As a result, to effectively force the model to allocate more attention to rewards associated with more challenging objectives, the weight differences must be made sufficiently large to compensate for the disparity in difficulty. Even with such adjustments, the model may still prefer optimizing the easier reward rather than the objective the user intends to prioritize, a phenomenon empirically demonstrated in Section 4.2.1.

**Conditioned Reward Functions for Difficulty Disparity**

To address reward hacking when objectives differ in difficulty, the paper recommends conditioning easier rewards on more difficult rewards. Given two rewards $r_k$ and $r_l$, conditioning $r_k$ on $r_l$ is formulated as:

$$r_k = \begin{cases} r_k, & \text{if } r_l \geq t \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

With such reward function design, the model can only receive reward $r_k$ when the reward $r_l$ satisfies a predefined score threshold $t$, forcing the model to always maximize the human-prioritized reward first. The empirical effectiveness of this strategy is shown in Section 4.2.1, where models trained with conditioned reward functions achieve higher performance on prioritized objectives compared to those trained without conditioning but only with larger weight assigned to the prioritized rewards. After resolving the issue of the easier reward dominating, assigning different reward weights for fine-grained priority adjustment can also be more faithfully reflected in the final model behavior.

**Implementation Configurations Across Experimental Domains**

The paper validates GDPO across three task domains with distinct hyperparameter configurations. For the **tool-calling task** (Section 4.1), training uses Qwen-2.5-Instruct models (1.5B and 3B parameters) with 4 rollouts per training question, a batch size of 512, and a maximum response length of 1024 tokens. Training runs for 100 steps using the verl framework with a learning rate of $1.0 \times 10^{-6}$, following the original hyperparameter settings from ToolRL's GRPO recipe. The complete hyperparameter configuration is listed in Appendix D (Table 6), which specifies 15 total epochs, a PPO mini-batch size of 128, maximum prompt length of 2048, and KL coefficient of 0.001.

For the **mathematical reasoning task** (Section 4.2), training uses DeepSeek-R1-1.5B, DeepSeek-R1-7B, and Qwen3-4B-Instruct models on the DeepScaleR-Preview dataset containing 40k competition-level math problems for 500 steps. This setting uses 16 rollouts per question, a batch size of 512, and a maximum response length of 8000 tokens. Following the DLER setup, the configuration incorporates dynamic sampling, higher clipping thresholds (clip_ratio_low of 0.2 and clip_ratio_high of 0.28), and token-mean loss from DAPO. The full hyperparameters in Appendix E (Table 7) specify 1 PPO epoch, learning rate of $1.0 \times 10^{-6}$, rollout temperature of 1.0, KL loss coefficient of 0.0005, and KL loss type of MSE. Group filtering is enabled with seq_reward as the filtering metric.

For the **coding reasoning task** (Section 4.3), training uses DeepSeek-R1-7B on the Eurus-2-RL dataset containing 24k coding problems for 400 steps, adopting the same hyperparameter configuration used in the mathematical reasoning experiments. This task optimizes three rewards simultaneously: passrate reward (proportion of test cases passed), conditioned length reward (response within target length AND code passes tests), and bug reward (no runtime or compilation errors). The target length $l$ is set to 4000 tokens for all math and coding experiments.

**Evaluation Configurations**

For evaluation across all tasks, the paper uses consistent inference settings. All evaluations are conducted using vLLM as the inference backend with a sampling temperature of 0.6, $top_p = 0.95$, and a maximum response length of 32k tokens. For each evaluation question, 16 samples are generated to compute pass@1 scores and other metrics. The tool-calling evaluation uses the Berkeley Function Call Leaderboard (BFCL-v3), math reasoning uses AIME-24, AMC, MATH, Minerva, and Olympiad Bench, and coding uses Apps, CodeContests, Codeforces, and Taco validation sets from PRIME.

**Design Choices and Alternatives Considered**

The paper explicitly evaluates alternative approaches to justify GDPO's design. Removing standard deviation normalization from GRPO (GRPO w/o std) was tested as a potential fix for reward collapse, since it slightly increases distinct advantage groups in Figure 3. However, Section 4.1.1 demonstrates this modification does not lead to improved convergence or better downstream evaluation performance, and in the tool-calling task achieves 0% format correctness on BFCL-v3 (Table 2), indicating complete failure to learn the required output structure. The batch-wise normalization step in GDPO was validated through ablation in Appendix A, where removing it occasionally leads to convergence failures. The conditioned reward formulation was compared against simple weight adjustment in Section 4.2.1, showing that conditioning provides more reliable priority alignment when objectives differ substantially in difficulty. These ablations establish that GDPO's specific combination of decoupled per-reward normalization, batch-wise advantage stabilization, and conditioned reward functions for priority variation is necessary for robust multi-reward optimization.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper's contributions extend beyond proposing a new algorithm. They reshape how we think about multi-reward reinforcement learning optimization. Below, I identify the four most significant insights, distinguishing between fundamental innovations that change the optimization paradigm and practical advances that improve real-world training outcomes.

---

### 4.1 Insight 1: Reward Signal Collapse is a Fundamental Optimization Flaw, Not a Reward Design Problem

**What makes this novel:** Prior work in multi-reward RL has overwhelmingly focused on **reward design**—crafting better reward functions, hierarchies, or weighting schemes—while treating the optimization algorithm (typically GRPO) as a fixed, suitable default. This paper identifies that the problem lies not in *what* rewards we compute, but in *how* they are normalized before producing policy updates.

**Why this is a fundamental innovation:** The reward signal collapse phenomenon (Section 2, Figure 2) reveals that GRPO's normalization mechanics inherently discard information when multiple rewards are summed before normalization. This is not an edge case or hyperparameter sensitivity issue—it is a **structural limitation** of the advantage computation itself. The fact that six distinct reward combinations collapse into only two unique advantage groups demonstrates that the optimization pipeline cannot distinguish between meaningfully different performance levels, regardless of how carefully rewards are designed.

**Significance beyond this paper:** This insight has implications for any RL system using group-relative advantage estimation with heterogeneous objectives. It suggests that:
- Improvements in reward design may be capped by optimization limitations
- Prior multi-reward RL results using GRPO may be suboptimal due to this compression
- The community should re-examine other "default" optimization assumptions in multi-objective settings

**Evidence of impact:** Figure 5 shows GRPO training collapsing after ~400 steps on math reasoning, with correctness reward declining even as length reward remains high. This is not gradual underperformance—it is **early training failure** caused by inaccurate advantage estimates that misguide policy updates.

---

### 4.2 Insight 2: Decoupled Normalization Preserves Advantage Granularity with Minimal Computational Overhead

**What makes this novel:** GDPO's core modification—normalizing each reward dimension independently before aggregation—is conceptually simple but fundamentally changes the information flow through the optimization pipeline. Unlike prior GRPO variants (DAPO, GSPO, GFPO) that modify clipping, sampling, or filtering strategies, GDPO addresses the **normalization order** itself.

**Why this is a fundamental innovation:** The key distinction is that GDPO preserves the **relative ranking information within each reward dimension** independently. When rewards are summed first (GRPO), a high score on one reward can mask a low score on another, and the normalization statistics conflate the distributions of heterogeneous objectives. By normalizing first, GDPO ensures that each reward contributes its own normalized signal to the final advantage, maintaining the granularity that GRPO erases.

**Quantified improvement:** Figure 3 demonstrates that GDPO produces substantially more distinct advantage groups than both GRPO and GRPO w/o std, with the gap widening as the number of rollouts or rewards increases. This is not a marginal improvement—it represents a **qualitative increase in training signal resolution** that scales with problem complexity.

**Practical significance:** Despite this conceptual change, GDPO adds negligible computational overhead:
- No additional forward passes or model evaluations
- No extra memory beyond storing per-reward advantages temporarily
- Same rollout generation and policy update mechanics as GRPO

This makes GDPO a **drop-in replacement** for GRPO in existing RL pipelines (HF-TRL, verl, Nemo-RL as noted in the abstract), lowering the barrier to adoption compared to methods requiring architectural changes.

---

### 4.3 Insight 3: Reward Conditioning Outperforms Weight Adjustment for Handling Difficulty Disparity

**What makes this novel:** Section 3.2 and Section 4.2.1 provide a systematic analysis showing that **adjusting reward weights is unreliable** when objectives differ substantially in difficulty. The paper demonstrates that models will optimize easier rewards regardless of assigned weights, a form of reward hacking that weight tuning alone cannot fix.

**Why this is a practical innovation:** The conditioned reward formulation (Equation 8) provides a principled alternative:

$$r_k = \begin{cases} r_k, & \text{if } r_l \geq t \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

By gating easier rewards on harder ones, the model is forced to prioritize the challenging objective first. This is fundamentally different from weight adjustment, which merely scales gradients but does not change the optimization landscape.

**Empirical validation:** Table 4 shows that using conditioned length reward $\tilde{\mathcal{R}}_{\text{length}}$ with GDPO yields a **4.4% accuracy improvement on AIME** with a **16.9% reduction in length-exceeding ratio** compared to GRPO with the same reward. More importantly, Figure 6 shows that varying weights on conditioned rewards produces **predictable, monotonic changes** in behavior, whereas varying weights on unconditioned rewards produces unstable, non-monotonic results.

**Broader implication:** This insight extends beyond GDPO to any multi-reward RL setting. It suggests that **reward function structure matters more than reward weights** when objectives have asymmetric difficulty. Practitioners should consider conditioning easier objectives on harder ones before tuning weights for fine-grained adjustment.

---

### 4.4 Insight 4: Multi-Reward Optimization Improvements Generalize Across Tasks, Models, and Reward Counts

**What makes this novel:** Many RL improvements are task-specific or model-size dependent. GDPO's validation across **three distinct domains** (tool calling, math reasoning, coding), **multiple model sizes** (1.5B to 7B parameters), and **varying reward counts** (2 to 3 objectives) demonstrates that the improvement stems from the optimization method itself, not from task-specific tuning.

**Why this establishes generalizability:**

| Task Domain | Rewards Optimized | Key Metric Improvement |
|-------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Tool Calling (Table 1) | Correctness + Format | +2.6% accuracy, +4.3% format compliance (Qwen2.5-1.5B) |
| Math Reasoning (Table 3) | Accuracy + Length | +6.3% AIME accuracy (DeepSeek-R1-1.5B), 80% reduction in length violations |
| Coding (Table 5) | Pass Rate + Length + Bug Ratio | +2.6% pass rate, -5.7% bug ratio (Codecontests, 3-reward setting) |

The consistency of improvement across these heterogeneous settings indicates that GDPO addresses a **foundational optimization issue** rather than exploiting task-specific characteristics.

**Significance for the field:** This generalizability suggests that:
- Multi-reward RL pipelines using GRPO may be systematically underperforming across diverse applications
- GDPO can be adopted as a **default optimization method** for multi-reward settings without task-specific validation
- The reward collapse phenomenon likely affects other group-relative optimization methods beyond GRPO

**Evidence of robustness:** The paper reports results averaged across **five independent runs** for tool calling (Table 1, Table 2), with interquartile range bands shown in training curves (Figure 1b, Figure 4). This rigorous evaluation protocol strengthens confidence that improvements are not due to random seed variation or cherry-picked runs.

| Contribution | Type | Rationale |

| **Coding Reasoning** (Sec. 4.3) | Eurus-2-RL | 24k coding problems | Pass Rate + Length + Bug Ratio |

This diversity is intentional: tool calling tests structured output compliance, math reasoning tests accuracy-efficiency trade-offs, and coding tests multi-objective optimization with three simultaneous rewards.

#### Models and Training Configurations

The experiments span **multiple model sizes** to demonstrate generalizability:

- **Tool Calling**: Qwen2.5-Instruct (1.5B and 3B parameters), trained for 100 steps
- **Math Reasoning**: DeepSeek-R1 (1.5B and 7B), Qwen3-4B-Instruct, trained for 500 steps
- **Coding**: DeepSeek-R1-7B, trained for 400 steps

All training uses the `verl` framework with consistent hyperparameters within each task domain. Key configurations include:

- **Rollouts**: 4 per question (tool calling), 16 per question (math/coding)
- **Batch size**: 512 across all tasks
- **Learning rate**: $1.0 \times 10^{-6}$
- **Maximum response length**: 1024 tokens (tool calling), 8000 tokens (math/coding)
- **Target length constraint**: 4000 tokens for math and coding tasks

Full hyperparameter details are provided in Appendix D (Table 6) for tool calling and Appendix E (Table 7) for math/coding.

#### Evaluation Benchmarks and Metrics

Each task domain uses established benchmarks with task-specific metrics:

**Tool Calling (Table 1)**: Evaluated on Berkeley Function Call Leaderboard (BFCL-v3) with:
- **Average Accuracy**: Across Live, Multi-Turn, and Non-Live subtasks
- **Correct Format Ratio**: Percentage of outputs satisfying required JSON structure

**Math Reasoning (Table 3)**: Evaluated on five benchmarks:
- **AIME-24, AMC 2022-2023, MATH, Minerva, Olympiad Bench**
- **Pass@1 Accuracy**: Percentage of correct answers
- **Exceed Ratio**: Percentage of responses exceeding 4000-token length limit

**Coding Reasoning (Table 5)**: Evaluated on PRIME validation set:
- **Apps, CodeContests, Codeforces, Taco**
- **Pass Rate**: Proportion of test cases passed
- **Bug Ratio**: Proportion of code with runtime/compilation errors
- **Exceed Ratio**: Responses exceeding length constraint

All evaluations use **vLLM** as the inference backend with temperature 0.6, $top_p = 0.95$, and 32k maximum response length. For each evaluation question, 16 samples are generated to compute metrics.

#### Baselines and Comparison Protocol

The primary baseline is **standard GRPO** with identical hyperparameters. The paper also evaluates:

- **GRPO w/o std**: GRPO variant removing standard deviation normalization (from Dr.GRPO/DeepSeek-v3.2)
- **GDPO variants**: With/without batch-wise normalization, with/without conditioned rewards

Critically, the paper reports results **averaged across five independent runs** for tool calling (Tables 1-2), with interquartile range bands shown in training curves (Figures 1b, 4). This rigorous protocol strengthens confidence that improvements are not due to random seed variation.

#### Tool Calling: Format Compliance and Accuracy

Table 1 presents the primary tool-calling results. For **Qwen2.5-Instruct-1.5B**:

GDPO achieves **+2.63 percentage points** on average accuracy and **+4.33 percentage points** on format compliance. The format improvement is particularly significant because format correctness is a binary constraint—failure means the output cannot be parsed by downstream systems.

- GDPO converges to higher values on **both** format and correctness rewards across all five runs
- GDPO shows faster early-stage improvement on correctness reward
- GRPO w/o std achieves similar correctness but **completely fails on format** (discussed in ablations)

This training curve pattern directly supports the paper's claim that GRPO suffers from early training failure due to inaccurate advantage estimates.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

#### GRPO Without Standard Deviation Normalization

Section 4.1.1 tests whether removing the standard deviation term from GRPO (as done in Dr.GRPO and DeepSeek-v3.2) addresses reward collapse. Table 2 shows results:

| Method | Avg Acc ↑ | Correct Format ↑ |
|--------|-----------|------------------|
| GRPO | 30.18% | 76.33% |
| GRPO w/o std | 29.26% | **0.00%** |
| GDPO | **32.81%** | **80.66%** |

**GRPO w/o std achieves 0% format correctness**, indicating complete failure to learn the required output structure. This is a critical finding: the proposed "fix" from prior work not only fails to improve performance but causes **catastrophic failure** on format compliance.

Figure 4's training curves explain this failure: GRPO w/o std converges to similar correctness rewards as GDPO but **fails entirely on format reward**. The paper states:

> "Simply removing the standard deviation normalization term in order to increase advantage diversity can introduce instability into training, which may ultimately prevent successful convergence in multi-reward reinforcement learning."

This ablation validates that GDPO's decoupled normalization is necessary—not just the removal of standard deviation.

#### Batch-Wise Advantage Normalization

Appendix A (Figure 8) ablates the batch-wise normalization step that follows decoupled per-reward normalization. The figure shows training curves for GDPO with and without batch normalization:

- **GDPO w/o BN Success**: Some runs converge properly
- **GDPO w/o BN Fail**: Some runs fail to converge on format reward

The paper states:

> "Empirically, we also find that this normalization step improves training stability, as shown in Appendix A, where removing batch-wise normalization occasionally leads to convergence failures."

This ablation establishes that batch-wise normalization is **necessary for stability** but secondary to the core decoupled normalization mechanism. It prevents advantage magnitude from growing as rewards are added, which could destabilize gradient updates.

#### Reward Weighting vs. Conditioning

Section 4.2.1 systematically compares two strategies for handling objectives with different difficulties:

**Strategy 1: Weight Adjustment**
- Vary length reward weight $w_{\text{length}} \in \{1.0, 0.75, 0.5, 0.25\}$
- Fix correctness reward weight at 1.0

**Strategy 2: Conditioned Reward**
- Replace $\mathcal{R}_{\text{length}}$ with $\tilde{\mathcal{R}}_{\text{length}}$ that requires correctness first
- Then vary weight $\tilde{w}_{\text{length}}$

Figure 6 shows that **weight adjustment alone produces unstable, non-monotonic behavior**. Reducing $w_{\text{length}}$ from 0.75 to 0.5 does not consistently increase length-exceeding ratio. Only at $w_{\text{length}} = 0.25$ (extremely small) does the model relax the length constraint meaningfully.

In contrast, **conditioned rewards produce predictable, monotonic changes**. Reducing $\tilde{w}_{\text{length}}$ from 1.0 to 0.25 steadily increases length-exceeding ratio for both GRPO and GDPO.

Table 4 quantifies the accuracy improvement from conditioning:

| Method | Reward Type | AIME Acc ↑ | AIME Exceed ↓ |
|--------|-------------|------------|---------------|
| GRPO | $\mathcal{R}_{\text{length}}$ | 50.2% | 2.1% |
| GDPO | $\mathcal{R}_{\text{length}}$ | 53.1% | 0.2% |
| GRPO | $\tilde{\mathcal{R}}_{\text{length}}$ | 53.3% | 29.2% |
| GDPO | $\tilde{\mathcal{R}}_{\text{length}}$ | **57.7%** | **12.3%** |

With conditioned rewards, GDPO achieves **+4.4 percentage points** accuracy over GRPO on AIME. The paper concludes:

> "After resolving the issue of the easier reward dominating, assigning different reward weights for fine-grained priority adjustment can also be more faithfully reflected in the final model behavior."

This ablation establishes that **reward function structure matters more than reward weights** when objectives have asymmetric difficulty.

#### Training Curve Consistency Across Models

Appendix F (Figures 9-10) shows training curves for DeepSeek-R1-7B and Qwen3-4B-Instruct on math reasoning. Both figures replicate the pattern from Figure 5:

- GDPO maintains improving correctness throughout training
- GRPO shows correctness decline after ~400 steps
- GDPO maintains better length constraint adherence (lower maximum response length)

This consistency across model sizes strengthens the claim that GDPO's advantage is **optimization-driven rather than model-specific**.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment

#### Strengths of Experimental Design

1. **Multiple Task Domains**: Testing across tool calling, math, and coding demonstrates generalizability beyond a single application.

2. **Multiple Model Sizes**: Results span 1.5B to 7B parameters, showing the method is not size-dependent.

3. **Multiple Runs**: Five independent runs for tool calling with interquartile ranges (Figures 1b, 4) provide statistical confidence.

4. **Systematic Ablations**: GRPO w/o std, batch normalization, and reward conditioning are each tested independently to isolate contributions.

5. **Training Curves**: Beyond final metrics, training dynamics (Figures 4, 5, 7, 8, 9, 10) provide evidence for *why* GDPO works, not just *that* it works.

#### Limitations and Open Questions

1. **Limited Model Scale**: The largest model tested is 7B parameters. Whether GDPO's advantages persist at 70B+ scale (common in production) remains unverified.

2. **Single Framework**: All experiments use `verl`. While the paper mentions HF-TRL and Nemo-RL implementations exist, no results from alternative frameworks are provided.

3. **Reward Types Tested**: All rewards are relatively simple (binary or bounded scalars). Whether GDPO helps with dense, continuous rewards (e.g., learned reward models) is not evaluated.

4. **Compute Overhead Not Quantified**: The paper claims "negligible computational overhead" but provides no timing comparisons between GRPO and GDPO training.

5. **Longer Training Horizons**: Math reasoning trains for 500 steps, coding for 400 steps. Whether GDPO's advantages persist or diminish over thousands of steps is unknown.

#### Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: GRPO causes reward signal collapse in multi-reward settings.**
- **Supported**: Figure 2's theoretical analysis and Figure 3's advantage group counts demonstrate the collapse mechanism. Figure 5's training curves show GRPO's correctness declining after 400 steps, consistent with collapse-induced instability.

**Claim 2: GDPO resolves collapse through decoupled normalization.**
- **Supported**: Figure 2 shows GDPO produces distinct advantages for (0,1) vs (0,2) reward combinations. Figure 3 shows GDPO maintains more distinct advantage groups as rollouts/rewards increase. Table 1-5 show consistent performance improvements.

**Claim 3: GDPO improves training stability and prevents early failure.**
- **Supported**: Figure 5 shows GRPO's correctness declining after 400 steps while GDPO continues improving. Appendix A shows GDPO without batch normalization occasionally fails, validating the stability mechanism.

**Claim 4: GDPO generalizes across tasks, models, and reward counts.**
- **Supported**: Improvements observed across all three task domains, all three model sizes, and both 2-reward and 3-reward settings (Table 5).

**Claim 5: Conditioned rewards outperform weight adjustment for priority alignment.**
- **Supported**: Figure 6 shows monotonic behavior with conditioned rewards vs unstable behavior with weight adjustment. Table 4 shows +4.4% accuracy improvement with conditioning.

#### Trade-offs and Conditional Results

The experiments reveal important trade-offs:

1. **Format vs Correctness (Tool Calling)**: GDPO improves both, but Figure 4 shows GDPO exhibits "larger variance in the number of steps required to converge on format reward." This suggests a potential stability trade-off during early training.

2. **Accuracy vs Length (Math)**: With conditioned rewards, accuracy improves but length-exceeding ratio increases (Table 4: GDPO with $\tilde{\mathcal{R}}_{\text{length}}$ has 12.3% exceed vs 0.2% without). This is expected—relaxing length constraint allows more computation for accuracy.

3. **Pass Rate vs Bug Ratio (Coding)**: In 3-reward setting, pass rates are equivalent but bug ratios improve (Table 5). This suggests GDPO better balances secondary objectives without sacrificing primary metrics.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While GDPO demonstrates consistent improvements over GRPO across multiple domains, a critical examination reveals important assumptions, unresolved questions, and practical trade-offs that practitioners should consider before adoption. This section identifies limitations grounded in the paper's own evidence, distinguishing between acknowledged constraints and gaps that remain unaddressed.

---

### 6.1 Assumptions Underlying GDPO

GDPO's effectiveness relies on several structural assumptions that are not universally guaranteed:

**Assumption 1: Sufficient Rollout Diversity Within Groups**

GDPO's decoupled normalization requires meaningful variance within each reward dimension across the rollout group. The normalization formula for each reward $k$:

$$A^{(i,j)}_k = \frac{r^{(i,j)}_k - \text{mean}\{r^{(i,1)}_k, \ldots, r^{(i,G)}_k\}}{\text{std}\{r^{(i,1)}_k, \ldots, r^{(i,G)}_k\}}$$

depends on non-zero standard deviation. If all rollouts receive identical scores on a particular reward (e.g., all achieve format compliance or all fail), the denominator approaches zero. The paper addresses this with a small constant $\epsilon$ in the batch-wise normalization step (Equation 6), but does not systematically evaluate how often this edge case occurs or whether it degrades training signal quality.

**Assumption 2: Reward Dimensions Are Approximately Independent**

GDPO treats each reward dimension as separable for normalization purposes. However, in practice, rewards may be **highly correlated** or even **redundant**. For example, in the coding task, the pass rate reward $\mathcal{R}_{\text{pass}}$ and bug reward $\mathcal{R}_{\text{bug}}$ are logically related—code with runtime errors typically fails test cases. The paper does not analyze whether decoupled normalization provides diminishing returns when rewards are correlated, or whether it inadvertently amplifies noise in redundant signals.

**Assumption 3: Batch-Wise Normalization Stabilizes Advantage Scale**

The batch-wise normalization step (Equation 6) is presented as necessary for stability, with Appendix A (Figure 8) showing that removing it "occasionally leads to convergence failures." However, this introduces a dependency on **batch composition**. If training batches are not representative (e.g., due to dynamic sampling or curriculum effects), the batch-wise statistics may introduce unwanted variance. The paper does not evaluate sensitivity to batch size or composition heterogeneity.

**Assumption 4: Conditioned Reward Thresholds Are Known**

The conditioned reward formulation (Equation 8) requires a predefined threshold $t$:

$$r_k = \begin{cases} r_k, & \text{if } r_l \geq t \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

In the math reasoning experiments, the threshold is implicitly $t=1$ for binary correctness reward. However, for continuous rewards or multi-level scoring, threshold selection becomes a hyperparameter. The paper provides no guidance on how to set $t$ when rewards are not binary, nor does it evaluate sensitivity to threshold choice.

---

### 6.2 Scenarios and Problem Settings Not Addressed

Several important settings remain unexplored in the paper's evaluation:

**Unexplored Setting 1: Large-Scale Models (70B+ Parameters)**

All experiments use models between **1.5B and 7B parameters**:
- Qwen2.5-Instruct: 1.5B, 3B
- DeepSeek-R1: 1.5B, 7B
- Qwen3-4B-Instruct: 4B

As Section 5.4 notes, whether GDPO's advantages persist at **70B+ parameter scale**—common in production deployments—remains unverified. Larger models may exhibit different optimization dynamics, potentially reducing the impact of reward collapse or introducing new failure modes not captured at smaller scales.

**Unexplored Setting 2: Learned Reward Models**

All rewards in the experiments are **rule-based** with known structure:
- Format compliance: Binary check on output structure
- Correctness: Exact match or structured comparison against ground truth
- Length: Binary check against token limit
- Bug ratio: Binary check on compilation/runtime errors

The paper does not evaluate GDPO with **learned reward models** (e.g., neural reward models trained on human preferences), which produce dense, continuous scores with different noise characteristics. Rule-based rewards have clear semantics and bounded ranges; learned rewards may have unbounded ranges, higher variance, and less interpretable gradients. Whether GDPO's decoupled normalization helps or harms optimization with such rewards is unknown.

**Unexplored Setting 3: More Than Three Rewards**

The coding task tests **three simultaneous rewards** (pass rate, length, bug ratio), which the paper presents as evidence of scalability. However, real-world alignment scenarios may involve **five or more objectives** (e.g., accuracy, safety, helpfulness, conciseness, format, tone, factuality). Figure 3 shows GDPO's advantage group count grows with reward count, but the paper does not evaluate whether:
- Training stability degrades with many rewards
- The batch-wise normalization step remains sufficient
- Reward conditioning becomes impractical with complex dependency graphs

**Unexplored Setting 4: Longer Training Horizons**

Training durations are relatively short:
- Tool calling: **100 steps**
- Math reasoning: **500 steps**
- Coding: **400 steps**

Production RL training often runs for **thousands of steps**. The paper does not evaluate whether GDPO's advantages persist, diminish, or reverse over extended training. Figure 5 shows GRPO collapsing after ~400 steps, but it is unknown whether GDPO would eventually encounter similar instability or plateau differently.

**Unexplored Setting 5: Alternative RL Frameworks**

All experiments use the **`verl` framework**. While the abstract mentions implementations exist for "HF-TRL, verl, Nemo-RL," no results from alternative frameworks are provided. Framework-specific optimizations (e.g., gradient accumulation, mixed precision, distributed training) may interact differently with GDPO's normalization steps. The claim of "drop-in replacement" remains unverified outside `verl`.

---

### 6.3 Computational, Data, and Scalability Constraints

**Constraint 1: Compute Overhead Not Quantified**

The paper claims GDPO adds "negligible computational overhead" (Section 4.2), but provides **no timing comparisons** between GRPO and GDPO training. While the normalization operations are mathematically simple, they require:
- Storing per-reward advantages temporarily before aggregation
- Computing mean and standard deviation for each reward dimension separately
- An additional batch-wise normalization pass

At scale (e.g., 16 rollouts × 512 batch size × 8 rewards), these operations may introduce measurable latency, particularly in distributed training settings. The absence of wall-clock comparisons leaves practitioners unable to estimate training cost increases.

**Constraint 2: Rollout Count Affects Advantage Granularity**

Figure 3 (left panel) shows that GDPO's advantage group count grows with the number of rollouts. This suggests GDPO benefits more from **larger rollout groups**. However, the paper uses different rollout counts across tasks:
- Tool calling: **4 rollouts** per question
- Math/Coding: **16 rollouts** per question

Whether GDPO's advantage diminishes with fewer rollouts (e.g., due to memory constraints) is not evaluated. If GDPO requires larger rollout groups to realize its benefits, this increases memory and compute requirements proportionally.

**Constraint 3: Batch Size Dependency**

The batch-wise normalization step computes statistics across the entire training batch. The paper uses a **fixed batch size of 512** across all experiments. In memory-constrained settings where smaller batches are necessary, the normalization statistics may become noisy, potentially destabilizing training. Appendix A's convergence failures without batch normalization suggest this step is important, but the paper does not evaluate sensitivity to batch size.

**Constraint 4: Reward Design Effort for Conditioning**

The conditioned reward strategy (Section 3.2, Section 4.2.1) requires practitioners to:
1. Identify which rewards are "easier" vs. "harder" to optimize
2. Define logical dependency relationships between rewards
3. Set appropriate thresholds for conditioning

This adds **design complexity** compared to simple weight adjustment. While the paper demonstrates conditioning is more effective, it does not provide systematic guidance for identifying reward difficulty or constructing dependency graphs in new domains.

---

### 6.4 Documented Trade-offs in Experimental Results

The paper's own results reveal important trade-offs that practitioners should anticipate:

**Trade-off 1: Format Convergence Variance**

Section 4.1 explicitly notes:

> "Although GDPO exhibits larger variance in the number of steps required to converge on format reward, it ultimately attains better format compliance than both GRPO."

Figure 4's interquartile range bands show GDPO has **wider variance** in early training steps for format reward, even though it converges to higher final values. This suggests practitioners may observe less predictable early training dynamics, which could complicate hyperparameter tuning or early stopping decisions.

**Trade-off 2: Accuracy vs. Length with Conditioned Rewards**

Table 4 shows that using conditioned length reward $\tilde{\mathcal{R}}_{\text{length}}$ improves accuracy but increases length violations:

| Method | Reward Type | AIME Acc ↑ | AIME Exceed ↓ |
|--------|-------------|------------|---------------|
| GDPO | $\mathcal{R}_{\text{length}}$ | 53.1% | 0.2% |
| GDPO | $\tilde{\mathcal{R}}_{\text{length}}$ | **57.7%** | **12.3%** |

This is an **expected trade-off**—relaxing the length constraint allows more computation for accuracy—but it means practitioners cannot simultaneously maximize both objectives. The choice between conditioned and unconditioned rewards depends on whether accuracy or efficiency is prioritized.

**Trade-off 3: Weight Adjustment Becomes Unpredictable Without Conditioning**

Figure 6 shows that varying length reward weights without conditioning produces **non-monotonic, unstable behavior**. For example, reducing $w_{\text{length}}$ from 0.75 to 0.5 does not consistently increase length-exceeding ratio. This means practitioners cannot rely on intuitive weight tuning to adjust priority—conditioning is required for predictable behavior, adding implementation complexity.

**Trade-off 4: Batch Normalization Adds Dependency**

Appendix A (Figure 8) shows that removing batch-wise normalization causes **occasional convergence failures**. While this validates the design choice, it also means GDPO has an additional dependency that GRPO does not require. In settings where batch-wise statistics are unreliable (e.g., highly heterogeneous batches, small batch sizes), this could introduce new failure modes.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Future Directions

Several important questions remain unresolved:

**Open Question 1: Does Reward Collapse Affect Other GRPO Variants?**

The paper focuses on standard GRPO and GRPO w/o std. However, Section 5 mentions other variants like **DAPO, GSPO, GFPO, and DLER**. Whether these variants suffer from similar reward collapse in multi-reward settings is not evaluated. If they do, GDPO's decoupled normalization may need to be adapted for each variant's specific advantage computation.

**Open Question 2: How Does GDPO Interact with KL Divergence Constraints?**

The paper explicitly omits the KL-divergence loss term from its GRPO formulation (Section 2, Equation 3 footnote). In practice, KL constraints are often used to prevent policy drift. Whether GDPO's advantage computation interacts differently with KL penalties compared to GRPO is not analyzed.

**Open Question 3: Can GDPO Handle Continuous, Dense Rewards?**

All experiments use **discrete or bounded rewards** (binary format, bounded correctness scores). Whether GDPO's decoupled normalization provides benefits for **dense, continuous rewards** (e.g., reward models producing scores in $\mathbb{R}$) is unknown. Continuous rewards may have different variance characteristics that affect normalization behavior.

**Open Question 4: What Is the Optimal Conditioning Graph Structure?**

The paper demonstrates conditioning one reward on another (e.g., length on correctness). However, with **five or more rewards**, practitioners may need to construct complex dependency graphs. The paper provides no guidance on:
- Whether conditioning should form a chain, tree, or DAG
- How to handle circular dependencies
- Whether partial conditioning (e.g., 50% probability) is beneficial

**Open Question 5: Does GDPO Reduce Sample Efficiency?**

The paper evaluates final performance but does not report **sample efficiency** metrics (e.g., steps to reach a target accuracy). If GDPO requires more training steps to converge due to more granular advantage signals, this could offset its accuracy benefits in compute-constrained settings.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than propose an incremental improvement to GRPO—it identifies a structural flaw in how multi-reward reinforcement learning is commonly practiced and provides a principled fix. The implications extend across research methodology, production deployment, and future algorithm design. Below, I outline how GDPO reshapes the field, what research it enables, where it should be deployed, and practical guidance for adoption.

---

### 7.1 How This Work Changes the Landscape

#### Shifting Focus from Reward Design to Optimization Mechanics

Prior multi-reward RL research has overwhelmingly focused on **reward engineering**: designing better reward functions, hierarchical structures, or weighting schemes. As Section 5 notes, approaches like ALARM, Safe RLHF, and RLPHF all assume GRPO-style optimization is suitable and focus on reward architecture instead.

GDPO challenges this assumption by demonstrating that **optimization mechanics matter as much as reward design**. The reward signal collapse phenomenon (Section 2, Figure 2) shows that even perfectly designed rewards can produce degraded training signals if the normalization pipeline compresses them prematurely. This reframes the multi-reward RL problem:

| Prior Assumption | GDPO's Correction |
|------------------|-------------------|
| Reward design is the bottleneck | Optimization mechanics can bottleneck regardless of reward quality |
| GRPO is a suitable default for multi-reward settings | GRPO requires modification for heterogeneous rewards |
| Weight adjustment handles priority variation | Reward conditioning is more reliable for difficulty disparity |
| More rewards = more alignment signal | More rewards can worsen collapse without decoupled normalization |

This shift has immediate implications for how practitioners should approach multi-reward RL:

1. **Audit optimization pipelines** before investing in reward engineering
2. **Validate advantage granularity** (e.g., distinct advantage group counts) as a diagnostic metric
3. **Consider normalization order** as a first-class design decision, not an implementation detail

#### Establishing Advantage Granularity as a Diagnostic Metric

Figure 3 introduces a novel diagnostic: counting **distinct advantage groups** produced by an optimization method. This metric quantifies training signal resolution independent of downstream performance. Prior work lacks such a diagnostic—researchers typically evaluate only final accuracy or reward scores.

This enables a new debugging workflow for multi-reward RL:

```
1. Run small-scale experiment (e.g., 100 steps, 4 rollouts)
2. Count distinct advantage groups across reward combinations
3. If collapse detected (few unique groups), apply GDPO-style decoupling
4. Proceed to full training only after validating signal resolution
```

The paper demonstrates this diagnostic predicts downstream performance: GRPO w/o std shows slightly more advantage groups than standard GRPO (Figure 3) but still fails catastrophically on format compliance (Table 2: 0% correct format). This suggests advantage granularity is a **necessary but not sufficient** condition for success—GDPO's specific decoupling mechanism matters.

#### Questioning "Default" Optimizers in Multi-Objective Settings

GDPO's success invites scrutiny of other default assumptions in multi-objective RL. Section 5 mentions variants like DAPO, GSPO, GFPO, and DLER—all built on GRPO foundations. The paper explicitly leaves open whether these variants suffer from similar collapse (Section 6.5, Open Question 1).

This creates a research agenda:

- **Audit existing GRPO variants** for reward collapse in multi-reward settings
- **Extend decoupled normalization** to other group-relative methods
- **Re-evaluate prior multi-reward RL results** that used unmodified GRPO

The possibility that published results are suboptimal due to optimization flaws (not reward design) is significant. As Section 4.2 shows, GRPO can actually **degrade** performance compared to baseline models (DeepSeek-R1-1.5B on AIME: 23.1% GRPO vs 29.8% base), suggesting prior work may have underestimated what multi-reward RL can achieve.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper's limitations (Section 6) and open questions (Section 6.5) define a clear research agenda. Below, I prioritize directions by impact and feasibility.

#### High-Priority Directions

**1. Large-Scale Model Validation (70B+ Parameters)**

All experiments use models between 1.5B and 7B parameters. Production systems often deploy 70B+ parameter models where optimization dynamics may differ:

- **Hypothesis**: Larger models may be less susceptible to reward collapse due to smoother loss landscapes
- **Alternative hypothesis**: Collapse effects compound at scale, making GDPO more critical
- **Experiment**: Replicate math reasoning task (Section 4.2) with 70B+ model, compare GRPO vs GDPO training curves

This is the most pressing validation needed before production adoption.

**2. Learned Reward Models vs Rule-Based Rewards**

All experiments use rule-based rewards with known structure (binary format checks, exact match correctness, etc.). Real-world RLHF often uses **learned reward models** producing dense, continuous scores:

- **Open question**: Does decoupled normalization help or harm with high-variance learned rewards?
- **Concern**: Learned rewards may have different variance characteristics affecting normalization stability
- **Experiment**: Replace rule-based correctness reward with neural reward model, measure GDPO vs GRPO convergence

This direction determines whether GDPO generalizes beyond controlled benchmark settings.

**3. Integration with Other GRPO Variants**

Section 5 mentions DAPO, GSPO, GFPO, and DLER as GRPO variants with different clipping, sampling, or filtering strategies. None are evaluated with multi-reward settings:

- **Research question**: Do these variants suffer from reward collapse?
- **Approach**: Apply GDPO's decoupled normalization to each variant's advantage computation
- **Expected outcome**: GDPO-style decoupling should improve all group-relative methods in multi-reward settings

This could establish decoupled normalization as a **universal modification** for group-relative optimization.

**4. Sample Efficiency Analysis**

The paper evaluates final performance but not **steps-to-convergence**:

- **Hypothesis**: GDPO's finer advantage granularity may accelerate early learning
- **Alternative**: More granular signals may require more samples to stabilize
- **Metric**: Plot accuracy vs training steps (not just final accuracy) for GRPO vs GDPO

Sample efficiency matters for compute-constrained settings where training cost is a bottleneck.

#### Medium-Priority Directions

**5. Reward Conditioning Automation**

Section 3.2 and 4.2.1 show conditioned rewards outperform weight adjustment, but require manual identification of "easier" vs "harder" rewards:

- **Goal**: Automate reward difficulty estimation during training
- **Approach**: Track per-reward convergence rates, automatically condition slower rewards on faster ones
- **Benefit**: Reduces engineering effort for new domains

**6. Complex Conditioning Graphs**

Current experiments condition one reward on another (e.g., length on correctness). With 5+ rewards, practitioners need guidance on dependency structures:

- **Research question**: Should conditioning form chains, trees, or DAGs?
- **Experiment**: Compare linear chain (r1→r2→r3) vs star topology (r1→r2, r1→r3) vs full DAG
- **Metric**: Final performance and training stability across structures

**7. KL Divergence Interactions**

The paper omits KL-divergence loss from its formulation (Section 2, Equation 3 footnote). Production RLHF typically includes KL penalties to prevent policy drift:

- **Open question**: Does GDPO's advantage computation interact differently with KL constraints?
- **Concern**: Decoupled normalization may affect KL gradient magnitudes
- **Experiment**: Add KL penalty to GDPO, measure policy drift vs GRPO with KL

#### Exploratory Directions

**8. Continuous Reward Distributions**

All experiments use discrete or bounded rewards. Continuous rewards (e.g., reward models outputting $\mathbb{R}$) may behave differently:

- **Hypothesis**: Decoupled normalization provides less benefit when rewards are already continuous
- **Alternative**: Collapse still occurs if continuous rewards are summed before normalization
- **Experiment**: Use continuous reward model, compare GRPO vs GDPO advantage distributions

**9. Cross-Modal Multi-Reward RL**

The paper focuses on text-only tasks. Multi-modal settings (vision-language, speech-text) often have modality-specific rewards:

- **Application**: Image generation with aesthetics + fidelity + safety rewards
- **Question**: Does GDPO help when rewards operate on different modalities?
- **Benefit**: Could extend GDPO beyond language model alignment

**10. Theoretical Analysis of Collapse Conditions**

Section 2 provides empirical demonstration of collapse but limited theoretical characterization:

- **Goal**: Derive conditions under which GRPO collapse occurs (e.g., reward count, rollout count, reward correlation)
- **Approach**: Information-theoretic analysis of advantage compression
- **Outcome**: Predictive model for when GDPO is necessary vs optional

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

#### When to Use GDPO vs GRPO

| Condition | Recommendation | Rationale |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| **Single reward objective** | GRPO | No collapse risk; GDPO adds unnecessary complexity |
| **2+ reward objectives** | GDPO | Collapse risk increases with reward count (Figure 3) |
| **Rewards have similar difficulty** | GDPO with equal weights | Decoupled normalization still preserves granularity |
| **Rewards have different difficulty** | GDPO with conditioned rewards | Weight adjustment alone is unreliable (Section 4.2.1) |
| **Memory-constrained training** | GDPO with smaller rollout groups | GDPO benefits from more rollouts but works with 4+ (Section 4.1) |
| **Learned reward models** | GDPO (unvalidated but recommended) | Decoupling should help regardless of reward type |
| **Production RLHF pipeline** | GDPO | Stability improvements reduce deployment risk |

#### Implementation Checklist

**Prerequisites**:
- Existing GRPO implementation (HF-TRL, verl, or Nemo-RL as noted in abstract)
- Ability to access per-reward scores before aggregation
- Batch-wise statistics computation capability

**Modification Steps**:

1. **Store per-reward scores separately**
   - Instead of computing $r_{\text{sum}}$ immediately, maintain $[r_1, r_2, \ldots, r_n]$ per rollout

2. **Normalize each reward dimension within groups**
   - For each reward $k$, compute mean and std across $G$ rollouts
   - Apply Equation 4: $A_k = (r_k - \text{mean}) / (\text{std} + \epsilon)$

3. **Sum normalized advantages**
   - Compute $A_{\text{sum}} = \sum_k A_k$ (Equation 5)

4. **Apply batch-wise normalization**
   - Compute mean and std across all rollouts in training batch
   - Apply Equation 6 to stabilize advantage scale

5. **Proceed with standard policy update**
   - Use clipped surrogate objective (Equation 3) with GDPO advantages

**Hyperparameter Considerations**:

| Parameter | GDPO Recommendation | Notes |
|-----------|---------------------|-------|
| Rollout count ($G$) | 4+ (tool calling), 16+ (reasoning) | More rollouts increase advantage granularity (Figure 3) |
| Batch size | 512 (validated) | Batch normalization may fail with very small batches (Appendix A) |
| $\epsilon$ for stability | $10^{-8}$ (standard) | Prevents division by zero in low-variance cases |
| Reward weights | Start equal, adjust after conditioning | Conditioning is more reliable than weights for priority (Section 4.2.1) |
| Conditioning threshold | 1.0 for binary rewards | Continuous rewards require empirical tuning |

#### Common Pitfalls and Mitigations

**Pitfall 1: Zero Variance in Reward Dimension**

If all rollouts receive identical scores on a reward, standard deviation is zero:

- **Symptom**: NaN advantages for that reward dimension
- **Mitigation**: Add $\epsilon$ to denominator (Equation 6 includes this)
- **Alternative**: Skip normalization for that reward, use raw score

**Pitfall 2: Incorrect Conditioning Order**

Conditioning easier rewards on harder rewards is critical:

- **Wrong**: Condition correctness on length (correctness is harder)
- **Right**: Condition length on correctness (Section 4.2.1)
- **Debug**: Monitor per-reward convergence rates to identify difficulty

**Pitfall 3: Batch Normalization with Heterogeneous Batches**

If training batches contain very different question difficulties:

- **Symptom**: Unstable training curves, occasional divergence
- **Mitigation**: Use larger batch sizes or stratified sampling
- **Alternative**: Apply group-wise normalization only (remove batch step, but expect some instability per Appendix A)

**Pitfall 4: Over-Conditioning**

Conditioning too many rewards can overly constrain optimization:

- **Symptom**: Slow convergence, rewards never improve
- **Mitigation**: Condition only when difficulty disparity is clear
- **Guideline**: Start with 1-2 conditioned pairs, expand if needed

#### Available Implementations

The abstract states implementations exist for:

- **HF-TRL** (Hugging Face Transformers Reinforcement Learning)
- **verl** (used in all paper experiments)
- **Nemo-RL** (NVIDIA's framework)

Practitioners should:
1. Check for official GDPO implementations in these frameworks
2. If unavailable, modify existing GRPO code following the checklist above
3. Validate on small-scale experiment before full training

#### Validation Protocol

Before deploying GDPO in production:

1. **Run 100-step pilot** with GRPO and GDPO side-by-side
2. **Compare advantage granularity**: Count distinct advantage groups
3. **Check training curves**: Ensure GDPO shows stable convergence
4. **Evaluate on held-out set**: Confirm downstream improvement
5. **Monitor for 500+ steps**: Verify no late-training collapse (Figure 5 pattern)

This protocol mirrors the paper's evaluation methodology and should detect collapse issues early.

### 7.5 Summary: GDPO's Role in the Multi-Reward RL Ecosystem

GDPO occupies a specific niche in the reinforcement learning landscape:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    Multi-Reward RL Methods                  │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Reward Design Focus:                                       │
│  • ALARM (hierarchical rewards)                             │
│  • Safe RLHF (helpfulness/harmlessness balance)             │
│  • RLPHF (multiple policies, merge at inference)            │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Optimization Focus:                                        │
│  • GRPO (default, single-reward optimized)                  │
│  • GRPO w/o std (removes std normalization, insufficient)   │
│  • GDPO (decoupled normalization, recommended for 2+ rewards)│
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Efficiency Focus:                                          │
│  • DAPO (dynamic sampling, clip adjustments)                │
│  • GSPO (sequence-level optimization)                       │
│  • GFPO (length filtering)                                  │
│  • DLER (length penalty recipes)                            │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**GDPO's positioning**:
- **Not a replacement** for reward design—complements good reward engineering
- **Not an efficiency method**—focuses on signal quality, not speed
- **A foundational fix** for multi-reward optimization using group-relative methods

**Adoption recommendation**:
- **Use GDPO** when optimizing 2+ rewards with group-relative methods
- **Continue using GRPO** for single-reward settings (no benefit from decoupling)
- **Combine GDPO with** reward conditioning for difficulty disparity (Section 3.2)
- **Validate at target scale** before production deployment (Section 6.2 limitation)

The paper establishes GDPO as a **new default** for multi-reward RL optimization, analogous to how Adam became the default optimizer for supervised learning. Just as Adam's adaptive learning rates resolved fundamental issues with SGD, GDPO's decoupled normalization resolves fundamental issues with GRPO in multi-reward settings. The combination of theoretical analysis (advantage collapse), empirical validation (three task domains), and practical guidance (conditioning strategies) provides a complete foundation for adoption.

Future work will determine whether GDPO's principles extend beyond group-relative methods to other multi-objective optimization frameworks. For now, practitioners implementing multi-reward RL should treat GDPO as the **starting point**, not an optional enhancement.
