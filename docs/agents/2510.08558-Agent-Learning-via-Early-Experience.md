## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the scalability bottleneck in training language agents, where Reinforcement Learning lacks verifiable rewards and Supervised Fine-Tuning suffers from limited expert data, by introducing the "early experience" paradigm that leverages agent-generated future states as reward-free supervision. Evaluated across eight diverse environments (including WebShop, ALFWorld, and TravelPlanner) using models like Llama-3.2-3B and Qwen-2.5-7B, the proposed Implicit World Modeling and Self-Reflection strategies consistently outperform imitation learning baselines, achieving up to +18.4% higher success rates on WebShop. Furthermore, this approach reduces dependency on human data—surpassing full-data imitation learning with only 1/8 of the expert demonstrations on WebShop—and establishes a robust foundation for subsequent reinforcement learning warm-starts.

## 2. Context and Motivation

The central ambition of artificial intelligence is to create autonomous agents capable of perceiving, acting, and learning in complex environments without human intervention. With the rise of large language models (LLMs), "language agents" have emerged as a promising vehicle for this goal, capable of navigating websites, controlling tools, and conducting scientific experiments. However, a fundamental bottleneck exists in how these agents are trained. The field is currently trapped between two dominant paradigms, neither of which scales effectively to the open-ended, reward-sparse reality of real-world tasks.

### The Binary Trap: Imitation vs. Reinforcement

To understand the gap this paper addresses, we must first examine the two standard approaches to agent training and why they fail in complementary ways.

**Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) / Imitation Learning**
The most common approach today is Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), often referred to as imitation learning in reinforcement learning literature. In this paradigm, the agent learns by mimicking expert demonstrations. Mathematically, the goal is to minimize the negative log-likelihood of expert actions given states:

$$
L_{IL}(\theta) = -\sum_{i=1}^{N} \log \pi_\theta(a_i | s_i)
$$

where $\pi_\theta$ is the policy parameterized by $\theta$, $s_i$ is the state, and $a_i$ is the expert action (Equation 1).

While SFT is stable and does not require reward signals, it suffers from a critical limitation: **passivity**. The agent only observes successful trajectories curated by humans or stronger models. It never sees the consequences of its own mistakes. This leads to **distribution shift**: during deployment, the agent inevitably deviates from the expert path, entering states it never saw during training. Because it has no experience with these "off-path" states, errors compound rapidly (Section 1). Furthermore, scaling SFT is expensive; high-quality human demonstrations are difficult and costly to produce in sufficient quantities.

**Reinforcement Learning (RL)**
The theoretical ideal for agents is Reinforcement Learning (RL), where an agent optimizes for cumulative reward through trial and error. This approach powered breakthroughs like AlphaGo in domains with clear rules and reward structures (e.g., board games). However, applying RL to language agents in real-world environments is currently impractical for two reasons (Section 1):
1.  **Lack of Verifiable Rewards:** Many environments, such as websites or multi-turn tool use, do not provide ground-truth feedback. A form submission might appear successful visually, but the environment provides no scalar reward signal indicating whether the data was correct.
2.  **Inefficient Long-Horizon Rollouts:** Tasks often require long sequences of actions (e.g., planning a travel itinerary). Without dense rewards, credit assignment (determining which action led to success) becomes unstable and computationally expensive.

### The Missing Middle: Reward-Free Interaction

This dichotomy creates a specific gap in the research landscape. Real-world environments often possess **state dynamics** (the world changes in response to actions) even if they lack **reward functions** (the world doesn't explicitly say if the change was "good").

*   **SFT** ignores dynamics; it treats data as static pairs.
*   **RL** requires rewards; it cannot function without a signal to optimize.

Figure 1 visualizes this progression. The "Era of Human Data" (Left) relies on static demonstrations. The envisioned "Era of Experience" (Right) relies on verifiable rewards. The paper argues that we are not yet ready for the Era of Experience, but we have moved past the limitations of pure Human Data. There is a "middle-ground" where agents can interact with the environment to collect **future states** without needing rewards.

### Why This Problem Matters

Addressing this gap is critical for both theoretical and practical reasons:

*   **Scalability:** Relying solely on human data caps agent performance at the level of the data providers. To surpass human performance, agents must learn from their own interactions.
*   **Robustness:** Agents trained only on expert data are brittle. They fail when encountering novel situations (out-of-domain scenarios). Learning from the consequences of actions—even suboptimal ones—builds a model of the environment that generalizes better.
*   **Infrastructure Readiness:** As noted in Section 2.1, the infrastructure for large-scale RL in language agent environments (simulators, reset mechanisms, reward models) is underdeveloped. A paradigm that improves agents *before* RL infrastructure is fully mature allows for immediate progress while bridging toward future RL capabilities.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

The paper positions "Early Experience" not as a replacement for RL, but as a **practical bridge** (Section 1). It distinguishes itself from prior work in several key ways:

*   **Vs. World Models:** Traditional world models (Section 2.2) are often separate simulators used for planning. This paper integrates world modeling *implicitly* into the policy itself. The agent learns to predict future states as an auxiliary task, internalizing dynamics without a standalone simulator.
*   **Vs. Self-Reflection:** Prior self-reflection methods (Section 2.2) often operate at inference time (prompting the model to check its work) or rely on external feedback (rewards) to validate reflections. This paper trains the agent to reflect on *state transitions* caused by its own actions, using the environment's response as the ground truth for the reflection, even without a reward signal.
*   **Vs. Synthetic Data:** While synthetic data increases volume, it is often static. Early Experience is **interactive**; the data is generated by the agent proposing actions and observing the actual environment's response, ensuring the supervision is grounded in reality.

By formalizing this middle ground, the paper argues that agents can convert their own "early" interactions—actions taken before they are experts—into scalable supervision. This shifts the training paradigm from "learning what to do" (imitation) to "learning what happens when I do this" (experience), laying the groundwork for the eventual transition to full reinforcement learning.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a methods paper that introduces a training paradigm called "early experience," where language agents learn from the consequences of their own actions by predicting future states or reflecting on action comparisons, all without requiring external reward signals. The core idea is to convert agent-generated interaction traces into supervision signals by treating environment responses (next states) as implicit feedback about action quality.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a training pipeline that augments standard supervised fine-tuning with agent-generated experience data, where the agent proposes alternative actions at each state, executes them in the environment, and learns from the resulting state transitions. The problem it solves is the scarcity of expert demonstrations and the absence of reward signals in real-world environments, and the solution takes the shape of two complementary training objectives: one that teaches the agent to predict what happens after any action (implicit world modeling), and another that teaches the agent to explain why expert actions outperform alternatives (self-reflection).

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The training pipeline consists of four major components arranged in a sequential data flow. First, the **Expert Data Collector** gathers human or model-generated demonstration trajectories, producing state-action pairs that form the baseline training set $D_{expert}$. Second, the **Action Proposer** takes each expert state $s_i$ and samples $K$ alternative actions from the current policy $\pi_\theta(\cdot | s_i)$, excluding the expert action $a_i$. Third, the **Environment Executor** runs each proposed action $a^j_i$ in the actual environment to observe the resulting next state $s^j_i$, creating rollout triples $(s_i, a^j_i, s^j_i)$. Fourth, the **Training Objective Generator** processes these triples into one of two formats: either next-state prediction targets for Implicit World Modeling, or comparison-based reasoning explanations for Self-Reflection. Finally, the **Policy Trainer** fine-tunes the language model using standard next-token prediction loss on the augmented dataset. Information flows unidirectionally from expert data through action proposal, environment interaction, objective construction, and into parameter updates, with no reward signals required at any stage.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Formal Foundation: Markov Decision Process and Early Experience Notation**

The paper formalizes language agent decision-making as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), which is a mathematical framework for modeling sequential decision problems where outcomes depend partly on chance and partly on agent choices. An MDP is defined by the tuple $\mathcal{M} = (\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{T}, \mathcal{R}, \gamma, \rho_0)$, where $\mathcal{S}$ denotes the state space (e.g., webpage contents, tool outputs), $\mathcal{A}$ represents the action space (e.g., clicking elements, invoking tools), $\mathcal{T}: \mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{A} \rightarrow \Delta(\mathcal{S})$ governs state dynamics (the probability distribution over next states given current state and action), $\mathcal{R}: \mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{A} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ provides feedback signals when available (though this function may be unknown or unverifiable), $\gamma \in [0, 1]$ is the discount factor, and $\rho_0 \in \Delta(\mathcal{S})$ specifies the initial state distribution (Section 3). The agent maintains a policy $\pi_\theta: \mathcal{S} \rightarrow \Delta(\mathcal{A})$, parameterized by $\theta$, which maps states to action distributions.

The starting point for early experience is a dataset of expert demonstrations $D_{expert} = \{(s_i, a_i)\}_{i=1}^N$, where $a_i$ denotes the expert action at state $s_i$, and $N$ is the number of state-action pairs (Section 4.1). For each expert state $s_i$ in this dataset, the method defines a candidate action set $A_i = \{a^1_i, a^2_i, ..., a^K_i\}$, where $K$ alternative actions are sampled from the initial policy $\pi_\theta(\cdot | s_i)$ (the number of alternatives is a hyperparameter that varies by environment, as shown in Figure 2). The expert action $a_i$ is also included in the analysis, but the key innovation is that for each alternative action $a^j_i \in A_i$, executing it in the environment leads to a next state $s^j_i$ sampled from the transition function $\mathcal{T}(s_i, a^j_i)$. These next states capture immediate consequences of taking action $a^j_i$ at state $s_i$, reflecting changes such as updated DOM structures, new tool outputs, error messages, or task progression.

All these interactions are collected into a rollout dataset defined as $D_{rollout} = \{(s_i, a^j_i, s^j_i) | i \in [N], j \in [K]\}$, where each triple represents a state, an alternative action taken at that state, and the resulting next state (Equation 2). Critically, all actions $a^j_i$ differ from the expert action $a_i$, allowing the agent to experience diverse state transitions from its own proposed actions. This rollout dataset provides rich supervision signals without requiring explicit rewards, because the next states $\{s^j_i | j \in [K]\}$ encode implicit feedback about action quality through environment responses. The paper leverages both $D_{expert}$ and $D_{rollout}$ to develop two different training approaches under the same early experience principle, which I now explain in detail.

**Method 1: Implicit World Modeling (IWM)**

Implicit World Modeling formulates world modeling as an auxiliary prediction task that helps the agent internalize environment dynamics from its own early experience, treating next-state prediction as a standard next-token prediction objective compatible with language model fine-tuning (Section 4.2). The key insight is that states are represented entirely in natural language (e.g., webpage text, tool outputs), allowing the model to predict next states using the same architecture and parameters it uses for action prediction. For each rollout triple $(s_i, a^j_i, s^j_i) \in D_{rollout}$, the method constructs a prediction task where the model takes the state-action pair $(s_i, a^j_i)$ as input and learns to predict the resulting next state $s^j_i$.

The training objective is defined as a next-token prediction loss:

$$
L_{IWM} = -\sum_{(s_i, a^j_i, s^j_i) \in D_{rollout}} \log p_\theta(s^j_i | s_i, a^j_i)
$$

where $p_\theta$ denotes the language model's output distribution (Equation 3). The paper uses the same model parameters $\theta$ for both state prediction (during world modeling) and action prediction (during policy execution), allowing the policy to internalize environment dynamics directly without a separate world model module. This design choice removes the need for a standalone simulator and fits naturally within the LLM fine-tuning paradigm, unlike traditional world models that treat prediction and policy as separate components (Section 2.2).

The training pipeline follows a two-stage approach: first, the model trains with $L_{IWM}$ for one epoch to internalize coarse environment dynamics, then it continues with supervised fine-tuning on $D_{expert}$ using the imitation learning loss $L_{IL}$ (Section 4.2). The total number of optimization steps is fixed to match the imitation learning baseline budget, ensuring fair comparison without extra compute. In practice, the rollout data are often an order of magnitude larger than $D_{expert}$; for example, in ALFWorld, the expert dataset contains 21,031 state-action pairs, but the augmented world modeling dataset contains 189,279 triplets (21,031 × 9, where 8 alternative actions are sampled per state plus the expert action, as detailed in Appendix B.1). This data amplification is possible because alternative actions are generated automatically by the policy itself, requiring no human annotation.

The method encourages the model to capture regularities in environment behavior, including common transitions, side effects, and invalid action outcomes. For instance, in WebShop, when the model enters an invalid date during flight booking, it learns to predict the textual error message as a natural-language representation of the next state (Section 4.2). Unlike inference-time world models used for planning, this implicit formulation integrates predictive signals directly into policy learning, serving as a lightweight warm-up before supervised learning or downstream optimization. The approach exposes the agent to diverse, non-expert behaviors, improving robustness to distribution shifts and reducing dependence on brittle expert trajectories.

**Method 2: Self-Reflection (SR)**

Self-Reflection formulates a mechanism for agents to learn from their own exploratory outcomes by comparing expert actions with alternatives sampled from the policy, using the resulting next states to generate natural language explanations of why the expert choice is preferable (Section 4.3). Rather than relying solely on expert state-action pairs, the agent generates chain-of-thought reasoning that highlights potential limitations or inefficiencies in alternative actions, grounded in the actual state transitions observed. This approach leverages the LLM's strength in processing language to internalize decision principles that generalize across tasks, moving beyond rote imitation to develop more generalizable decision criteria.

The data construction process proceeds as follows: for each expert state $s_i$, the method first executes the expert action $a_i$ to obtain the expert next state $s_{i+1}$. For each alternative action $a^j_i$ (where $j \in \{1, ..., K\}$), it obtains the corresponding next state $s^j_i$ by executing that action in the environment. The method then prompts a language model to generate a chain-of-thought $c^j_i$ explaining why the expert action $a_i$ is preferable to the alternative $a^j_i$ based on the differences between their resulting states $s_{i+1}$ and $s^j_i$. The prompt template (shown in Section 4.3) instructs the model to analyze the situation and goal, compare possible actions, justify why the expert action is most suitable grounded in the expected outcome, and highlight relevant clues, constraints, or consequences.

The resulting triplets $(s_i, a^j_i, c^j_i)$ are collected into a dataset $D_{refl}$. The training objective jointly predicts the chain-of-thought and the expert action conditioned on the state $s_i$, using a next-token prediction loss over the concatenated target sequence $c^j_i \circ a_i$:

$$
L_{SR} = -\sum_{(s_i, a^j_i, c^j_i) \in D_{refl}} \log p_\theta(c^j_i, a_i | s_i)
$$

where $p_\theta$ denotes the language model's output distribution, aligned with the agent's policy $\pi_\theta$ (Equation 4). In practice, the self-reflection data $D_{refl}$ is mixed with the expert dataset $D_{expert}$ and trained using standard next-token prediction loss. Chain-of-thought reasoning is generated only for the self-reflection training data, and the original chain-of-thought reasoning in $D_{expert}$ is retained whenever provided by the expert trajectories.

The paper provides a concrete example from WebShop: when the expert action is "click on the \$15 blue shirt," an alternative might be "click on the \$30 red shirt," and the generated reflection could be: "While the red shirt matches the color preference, it exceeds the \$20 budget constraint specified in the query. The blue shirt satisfies both the style requirement and budget limit" (Section 4.3). This teaches the model to prioritize constraints, a lesson that generalizes beyond this specific item. The joint training setup balances grounded decision-making from demonstrations with contrastive insights from exploratory outcomes, encouraging the model to develop transferable decision criteria rather than memorizing specific action sequences.

**Data Construction Hyperparameters and Environment-Specific Configurations**

The branching factor $K$ (number of alternative actions per state) is a critical hyperparameter that varies by environment based on action space complexity and compute budget. In ALFWorld, the method samples 8 non-expert actions uniformly without replacement from the admissible action list at each state, yielding 21,031 × 9 = 189,279 triplets for implicit world modeling (Appendix B.1). In WebShop, the method lets the policy propose actions at temperatures {0.5, 0.8, 0.9} and additionally samples up to five admissible actions uniformly at random per state, merging these with the expert action to yield 122,954 triplets (Appendix B.2). In BFCLv3, the target model samples ten alternative actions in addition to the expert action, producing 11,904 samples (Appendix B.3). In TravelPlanner, the method performs exhaustive augmentation by executing ALL available valid actions at each state (not just sampling), generating over 70,000 state-transition samples (Appendix B.7). In WebArena-Lite, the target model proposes 5 non-expert actions using free-form generation per state, resulting in 7,044 × 6 = 42,264 triplets (Appendix B.8).

For self-reflection data construction, the number of alternatives is typically smaller to maintain reasoning quality. In ALFWorld, the policy proposes up to 3 alternative actions with temperature 1.0, and proposed actions are canonicalized to keep only unique ones (Appendix B.1). In WebShop, the method retains 3 distinct alternatives per state after canonicalization and deduplication, intentionally including admissible but unhelpful actions, empty responses, and occasional invalid actions to help the model learn sharper decision boundaries (Appendix B.2). In TravelPlanner, the method explores up to 30 alternative valid actions per state-action pair and generates reasoning using temperature 0.9 with 8-way tensor parallelism (Appendix B.7). In SearchQA, the method randomly samples 2 alternative actions for each state, resulting in 7,691 training instances containing detailed reasoning processes (Appendix B.5).

Quality filtering is applied selectively depending on the environment. In WebShop, a simple quality filter retains only actions from trajectories whose tasks can be completed within fewer than 15 steps, resulting in 6,235 reflection examples (Appendix B.2). In BFCLv3, a small number of low-quality samples where the concluded action did not match the expert action are filtered out, leaving 1,200 training examples (Appendix B.3). In Tau-Bench, filtering removes low-quality reflection samples, resulting in 5,233 training instances (Appendix B.4). In WebArena-Lite, the method filters out low-quality generations where the explanation incorrectly supports a non-expert action, leaving 3,190 high-quality self-reflection examples (Appendix B.8).

**Training Configurations Across Environments**

Training hyperparameters are tuned per environment based on data size and task complexity, but all experiments use consistent prompt formatting and decoding strategies. In ALFWorld, training uses a batch size of 16 and a learning rate of 1e-5 for 2 epochs using LlamaFactory (Appendix B.1). In WebShop, the batch size is 4 with a learning rate of 1e-5 (Appendix B.2). In BFCLv3, training uses a batch size of 16 and a learning rate of 1e-5 (Appendix B.3). In Tau-Bench, imitation learning trains for 6 epochs with a learning rate of 1e-5, while implicit world model learning trains for 1 epoch with a learning rate of 5e-6, and self-reflection conducts 6 epochs of SFT with a learning rate of 1e-5, all with batch size 16 (Appendix B.4). In SearchQA, imitation learning and self-reflection train for 3 epochs with a learning rate of 1 × 10^-5, a context window of 8192 tokens, and a batch size of 2 per GPU with gradient accumulation steps of 16 (Appendix B.5). In ScienceWorld, training uses a batch size of 32 and a learning rate of 5e-6 for 1 epoch (Appendix B.6). In TravelPlanner, models train for 5 epochs with a learning rate of 1e-5 and cosine scheduler, using 32K context windows with batch size 16 per GPU (Appendix B.7). In WebArena-Lite, all models train for 2 epochs with a learning rate of 1e-5 and cosine scheduler (Appendix B.8).

For Implicit World Modeling, the pipeline begins with one epoch of the world modeling objective and then continues with supervised updates so that the total updates equal the imitation learning budget without extra steps (Section 5.1). For Self-Reflection, training runs for the same number of epochs as imitation learning. All experiments use at most 8 H100 GPUs for training and evaluation. For evaluation, the paper reports each benchmark's main native metric and follows official validators. For models at 70B scale (Llama-3.3-70B), fine-tuning uses parameter-efficient LoRA with the same rank and update steps across all methods, and for IWM, the same adapters are continued in the second stage so that total tunable parameters and compute match imitation learning (Section 6.4).

**Design Choices and Rationale**

The paper makes several deliberate design choices that distinguish early experience from alternative approaches. First, using the same model parameters $\theta$ for both state prediction and action prediction (rather than separate world model and policy networks) reduces architectural complexity and allows the policy to internalize dynamics directly, avoiding the planning overhead required by explicit simulators (Section 2.2, Section 4.2). Second, generating alternative actions from the current policy itself (rather than random sampling or human-curated alternatives) ensures that the exploration is relevant to the agent's current capabilities and failure modes, making the supervision more targeted. Third, treating next states as natural language sequences (rather than structured representations) leverages the LLM's existing next-token prediction capability without requiring architectural modifications. Fourth, mixing self-reflection data with expert data during training (rather than sequential training) balances grounded decision-making from demonstrations with contrastive insights from exploratory outcomes, preventing the model from overfitting to either source alone (Section 4.3).

The two-stage pipeline for Implicit World Modeling (world modeling first, then expert fine-tuning) is motivated by the intuition that internalizing environment dynamics provides a better initialization for policy learning, similar to mid-training approaches (Section 2.2). However, the total compute budget is kept identical to the imitation learning baseline, ensuring that performance gains are attributable to data quality rather than increased training steps. For Self-Reflection, generating chain-of-thought explanations only for self-reflection data (while retaining original reasoning in expert data when available) preserves the benefits of expert rationales while adding contrastive reasoning signals where expert data lacks them.

The branching factor $K$ is tuned per environment based on action space characteristics: larger $K$ benefits Implicit World Modeling (which improves steadily as $K$ increases, as shown in Figure 4b), while Self-Reflection works best with modest $K$ (e.g., 2-4) because comparing many alternatives occasionally includes other success-leading actions, reducing contrast with the expert, and current models have limited capacity to reason over many alternatives and outcomes in a single context (Section 6.3). This ablation reveals that the two methods have different data efficiency profiles, with IWM favoring quantity of transitions and SR favoring quality of comparisons.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper introduces several contributions that extend beyond incremental improvements to existing agent training methods. Below, I distinguish between the fundamental conceptual shifts and the practical innovations that enable them, explaining why each matters for the field's progression.

### 4.1 The Early Experience Paradigm: A Conceptual Reframing of "Experience"

The most fundamental contribution is not either specific method (IWM or SR), but the **paradigm itself**. Prior work treated agent training as a binary choice: either learn from static human demonstrations (SFT) or learn from reward signals (RL). This paper identifies and formalizes a third category that had been largely overlooked: **reward-free interaction data**.

What makes this conceptually novel:

*   **Redefining Supervision:** The paper argues that future states $s^j_i$ resulting from an agent's own actions constitute valid supervision signals, even without scalar rewards. This reframes "experience" from "reward-labeled trajectories" to "state transitions caused by my actions." As stated in Section 1: *"the agent's own actions and the resulting future states can still constitute experience that serves as a direct source of supervision."*

*   **Bridging Two Eras:** Figure 1 positions early experience as a bridge between the "Era of Human Data" and the "Era of Experience." This is not merely a methodological tweak—it is a **training philosophy** that acknowledges current infrastructure limitations while enabling progress toward fully experience-driven agents. The paradigm is designed to be immediately usable (no rewards needed) while remaining compatible with future RL pipelines.

*   **Scalability Without Rewards:** The key insight is that state dynamics exist independently of reward functions. A website will change its DOM structure when you click a button regardless of whether that click was "correct." Early experience harvests this ubiquitous signal. Table 2 demonstrates the practical impact: on WebShop with Llama-3.1-8B, Implicit World Modeling achieves 58.6% success versus 47.3% for imitation learning—a **+11.3% gain** without any reward signals.

This is a **fundamental innovation**, not an incremental improvement. It changes what researchers should consider as "trainable data" for agents, expanding the solution space for environments where rewards are unavailable or sparse.

### 4.2 Implicit World Modeling: Internalizing Dynamics Without a Separate Simulator

Traditional world models (Section 2.2) are typically separate neural networks trained to predict environment states, used primarily for planning or rollouts during inference. Examples include Dreamer (Hafner et al., 2020) or recent LLM-based world models (Gu et al., 2025) that treat the world model as a distinct simulator.

The innovation here is architectural integration:

*   **Single Model, Dual Capability:** The same parameters $\theta$ are used for both action prediction (policy) and state prediction (world modeling). As Equation 3 shows, the loss $L_{IWM}$ trains the policy directly on next-state prediction. This eliminates the need for a standalone world model module, reducing architectural complexity and avoiding the "planning overhead" that explicit simulators require (Section 2.2).

*   **Auxiliary Task as Warm-Up:** The two-stage pipeline (world modeling first, then expert fine-tuning) treats dynamics learning as a **preparatory task** rather than a deployment-time tool. This is distinct from model-based RL, where world models are used for speculative planning during execution. Here, the world model serves to ground the policy before it ever sees expert actions.

*   **Data Amplification:** Appendix B.1 shows that in ALFWorld, the rollout dataset contains 189,279 triplets—**9× larger** than the 21,031 expert state-action pairs. This amplification is automatic and reward-free. The significance is clear in Figure 4(a): on WebShop, using only **1/8 of expert trajectories** with early experience surpasses imitation learning trained on the **full dataset**. This directly addresses the scalability bottleneck identified in Section 1.

This is a **methodological innovation** with practical significance. It demonstrates that world modeling need not be a separate, computationally expensive component—it can be an auxiliary objective that strengthens the policy directly.

### 4.3 Self-Reflection Grounded in State Transitions (Not Rewards)

Prior self-reflection work falls into two categories, both with limitations that early experience addresses:

| Prior Approach | Limitation | Early Experience Solution |
|----------------|------------|---------------------------|
| Inference-time reflection (Shinn et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023) | No parameter updates; relies on prompting | Trains parameters directly on reflection data |
| Reward-dependent reflection (Zelikman et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023) | Requires verifiable rewards to validate reflections | Uses state transitions as ground truth, no rewards needed |

The key innovation is **grounding reflection in observed outcomes** rather than abstract reasoning or external feedback:

*   **Contrastive Learning from Actions:** Section 4.3 explains that the model generates chain-of-thought $c^j_i$ explaining why expert action $a_i$ is preferable to alternative $a^j_i$ based on differences between resulting states $s_{i+1}$ and $s^j_i$. The environment's response (the next state) validates the reflection, not a reward signal.

*   **Transferable Decision Criteria:** The WebShop example in Section 4.3 illustrates this: the model learns *"prioritize constraints"* as a general principle, not just *"click the blue shirt."* Table 3 confirms this transfers to out-of-domain settings: on SearchQA OOD, Self-Reflection achieves 50.7% F1 versus 47.4% for imitation learning (+3.3% gain).

*   **Training-Time, Not Inference-Time:** Unlike Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023) which operates during deployment, this approach bakes reflection capabilities into the model weights. This is critical for efficiency—reflection becomes part of the policy's forward pass rather than an expensive multi-turn dialogue.

This is a **methodological innovation** that extends self-reflection to reward-scarce environments. The significance lies in generalization: Section 5.3 shows SR excels when distribution shifts affect tool availability or arguments (e.g., BFCLv3 OOD), indicating the model learns reasoning principles rather than memorizing trajectories.

### 4.4 Early Experience as a Reinforcement Learning Warm-Start

Perhaps the most forward-looking contribution is demonstrating that early experience checkpoints serve as **superior initialization for subsequent RL**. This positions the paradigm not as a competitor to RL, but as a **practical bridge** toward the "Era of Experience."

Figure 3 provides the critical evidence. On three environments with verifiable rewards (WebShop, ALFWorld, SearchQA), the authors append GRPO-based RL to models trained with different initializations:

*   **Higher Post-RL Ceilings:** Checkpoints from early experience methods (IWM, SR) consistently achieve higher final performance after RL than imitation learning starts. On ALFWorld with Llama-3.1-8B, IWM+GRPO reaches 97.7% success versus 93.8% for IL+GRPO—a **+3.9% advantage** that persists after identical RL training.

*   **Advantages Amplified During RL:** In some cases, the gap grows during RL training (e.g., ALFWorld), suggesting early experience provides a better optimization landscape. The paper notes: *"the performance gap grows during RL training... even when reward optimization is applied for the same number of steps, IL starts rarely match the final performance of early-experience starts"* (Section 5.4).

*   **Necessity of Initialization:** Running GRPO directly from the raw pretrained model (without any supervised stage) performs worst and shows unstable training dynamics. This confirms that some supervised initialization is necessary, and early experience provides a **better** initialization than imitation learning alone.

This is a **practical innovation** with strategic significance. It means organizations can deploy early experience methods *now* in environments without rewards, then seamlessly transition to RL when reward infrastructure becomes available—without retraining from scratch. The paradigm is **future-proof** by design.

### 4.5 Distinguishing Incremental vs. Fundamental Contributions

To be precise about the novelty hierarchy:

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| Early Experience Paradigm | **Fundamental** | Redefines what counts as trainable experience; expands solution space for reward-scarce environments |
| Implicit World Modeling (Integrated) | **Methodological** | Eliminates separate world model architecture; enables data amplification without rewards |
| Self-Reflection (Reward-Free) | **Methodological** | Extends reflection training to environments without verifiable feedback |
| RL Warm-Start Bridge | **Practical** | Enables seamless transition from imitation to RL; preserves gains across training stages |
| Performance Gains (Table 2) | **Empirical** | Validates the paradigm across 8 environments; demonstrates scalability (Figure 4) |

The **paradigm itself** is the core innovation—the methods (IWM, SR) are instantiations of it. The RL warm-start result is the strategic validation that early experience is not a dead-end but a stepping stone. Together, these contributions shift how researchers should think about agent training: from "collect expert data or wait for rewards" to "harvest state transitions from your own actions now, optimize for rewards later."

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence supporting the early experience paradigm. The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation designed to answer three specific questions: (1) Does early experience improve task performance compared to standard imitation learning? (2) Does it enhance robustness to unseen environments (out-of-domain generalization)? (3) Does it provide a superior foundation for subsequent reinforcement learning? To answer these, the study spans eight diverse environments, three model families, and multiple ablation conditions. I will walk through the experimental design, the quantitative results, and what they imply for the viability of this paradigm.

### 5.1 Evaluation Framework and Baselines

To ensure the results are not artifacts of a specific domain or model size, the evaluation framework is constructed with significant diversity in mind.

**Environments and Tasks**
The experiments cover eight distinct environments, categorized into three major domains (Table 1):
*   **Embodied and Scientific Simulation:** Includes `ALFWorld` (household tasks), `ScienceWorld` (chemistry/physics experiments), and `TravelPlanner` (long-horizon itinerary planning). These environments feature structured, often discrete action spaces.
*   **Multi-Turn Tool-Use:** Includes `BFCLv3` (API function calling), `Tau-Bench` (customer service with policy constraints), and `SearchQA` (multi-hop question answering with search tools). These require precise argument generation and reasoning over tool outputs.
*   **Web Navigation:** Includes `WebShop` (e-commerce product search) and `WebArena-Lite` (general web tasks like CMS or GitLab management). These present high-dimensional, noisy observation spaces (e.g., DOM trees).

The scale of expert data varies significantly, from 45 trajectories in `TravelPlanner` to 3,553 in `ALFWorld` (Table 1). This variance allows the authors to test data efficiency across regimes where expert data is either scarce or relatively abundant.

**Models and Training Budget**
The primary evaluation uses three instruction-tuned models to test scalability across parameter counts: `Llama-3.2-3B`, `Qwen-2.5-7B`, and `Llama-3.1-8B` (Section 5.1). Crucially, the experimental design controls for compute to ensure fair comparison. For Implicit World Modeling (IWM), the training begins with one epoch of the world modeling objective, followed by supervised updates such that the **total optimization steps equal the imitation learning baseline budget** (Section 5.1). This means any performance gain cannot be attributed to "more training," but must result from the *quality* of the early experience data. For Self-Reflection (SR), the epoch count matches imitation learning exactly.

**Baselines**
The primary baseline is **Imitation Learning (IL)**, trained solely on expert demonstrations $D_{expert}$ using the standard loss $L_{IL}$. The authors also compare against the raw instruction-tuned model ("Prompt") to establish the zero-shot floor. In Section 6.1, they introduce two critical ablation baselines to isolate the source of gains:
1.  **Long CoT:** Extending reasoning length at inference time without parameter updates.
2.  **STaR-style Data:** Training on rationales generated for expert actions *without* executing alternative actions or observing resulting states (Zelikman et al., 2022).

**Metrics**
Performance is measured using each benchmark's native metric, primarily **Success Rate (%)** for task completion (e.g., did the agent buy the correct item?) or **F1 Score** for question answering (Table 2). For Reinforcement Learning (RL) experiments, the same metrics are used after fine-tuning with the GRPO algorithm (Shao et al., 2024).

### 5.2 Primary Effectiveness Results

The core claim of the paper is that early experience improves effectiveness over imitation learning alone. Table 2 provides the evidence across all eight environments and three model sizes.

**Quantitative Gains**
The results show consistent improvements. Early experience methods (IWM and SR) outperform Imitation Learning in nearly every setting.
*   **Web Navigation:** On `WebShop` with `Llama-3.2-3B`, Implicit World Modeling achieves a **60.2%** success rate, compared to **41.8%** for Imitation Learning—a gain of **+18.4%** (Table 2). This is the largest absolute gain observed, suggesting that understanding state dynamics (e.g., what happens when I click "filter"?) is critical in high-dimensional web environments.
*   **Long-Horizon Planning:** On `TravelPlanner`, Self-Reflection yields substantial gains. With `Llama-3.1-8B`, SR reaches **32.2%** success versus **17.2%** for Imitation Learning (**+15.0%**). This supports the hypothesis that contrasting expert actions with suboptimal alternatives helps the agent learn constraint satisfaction (e.g., budget limits) better than rote memorization.
*   **Structured Simulation:** On `ALFWorld`, gains are steady but smaller in magnitude (e.g., **+5.5%** for SR with `Llama-3.2-3B`). This environment has a closed, finite action space, making it easier for standard imitation learning to achieve high performance, leaving less room for improvement.

**Why Methods Differ by Environment**
The paper provides a nuanced analysis of *why* one method might outperform the other depending on the environment's structure (Section 5.2):
*   **Implicit World Modeling (IWM)** excels in environments with **stable, predictable dynamics**. In `WebShop`, the consequence of clicking a button is consistent and observable in the DOM. IWM helps the agent internalize these transitions, leading to the +18.4% gain mentioned above.
*   **Self-Reflection (SR)** shines in environments requiring **reasoning and constraint satisfaction**. In `TravelPlanner` and `ScienceWorld`, failures often stem from logical errors (e.g., violating a budget) rather than misunderstanding the interface. SR's contrastive reasoning ("Why is expert action A better than B?") explicitly teaches these constraints, yielding larger gains (+15.0% in TravelPlanner) than IWM.
*   **Open Action Spaces:** In `SearchQA` or `WebArena`, where the number of possible actions is vast (e.g., any search query), gains are smaller in absolute terms (e.g., **+0.6% to +3.3%** in SearchQA) but remain consistent. This indicates that while early experience helps, the complexity of open-ended action generation remains a challenge.

**Baseline Comparisons (Table 4)**
To ensure the gains come from *experience* (interacting with the environment) rather than just *more reasoning*, the authors compare against Long CoT and STaR-style baselines (Table 4).
*   **Long CoT:** On `WebShop`, adding Long CoT to the imitation-trained model actually **degrades performance to 0.0%** (from 47.3%). The authors explain that models fine-tuned only on expert trajectories lack the ability to sustain coherent long-form reasoning when they deviate from the expert path; the reasoning drifts or collapses.
*   **STaR:** Training on ungrounded rationales (without executing alternative actions) yields **25.0%** on `WebShop`, significantly lower than Early Experience (**58.6%**). This confirms that **grounding reflection in actual state transitions** (observing what *actually* happens when you take action B) is superior to generating plausible-sounding rationales in a vacuum.

### 5.3 Out-of-Domain Generalization

A critical weakness of Imitation Learning is distribution shift: the agent fails when it encounters states not present in the expert data. Section 5.3 tests whether early experience mitigates this by evaluating on Out-of-Domain (OOD) splits.

**OOD Results (Table 3)**
The evaluation uses predefined OOD splits for `ALFWorld`, `BFCLv3`, and `SearchQA`. For example, in `BFCLv3`, the OOD setting includes tasks with missing functions or arguments that were not seen during training.
*   **Recovering the Gap:** While OOD scores naturally drop compared to in-domain performance, early experience recovers a substantial portion of the gap. On `ALFWorld` with `Llama-3.1-8B`, IWM achieves **78.1%** OOD success, compared to **63.3%** for Imitation Learning—a **+14.8%** improvement (Table 3).
*   **Relative Gains:** In several cases, the *relative* improvement on OOD data is larger than on in-domain data. For `SearchQA` with `Llama-3.2-3B`, IWM improves OOD F1 from **40.5%** to **45.4%** (+4.9%), whereas the in-domain gain was only +1.0% (Table 2 vs Table 3). This suggests that experiencing diverse state transitions during training prepares the policy for novel states it will encounter at test time.
*   **Method Specificity:** IWM excels when dynamics are stable across domains (e.g., `ALFWorld` navigation mechanics remain similar). SR excels when the shift affects tool availability or arguments (e.g., `BFCLv3`), as the reasoning process generalizes better than specific action patterns.

**Significance**
These results validate the claim that early experience builds a more robust internal model of the environment. By training on alternative actions (e.g., "what if I clicked the wrong button?"), the agent learns recovery strategies and boundary conditions that pure expert imitation never exposes it to.

### 5.4 Reinforcement Learning Compatibility

The paper positions early experience not as a replacement for RL, but as a bridge to it. Section 5.4 tests whether checkpoints trained with early experience serve as better warm-starts for RL than standard imitation learning checkpoints.

**RL Warm-Start Results (Figure 3)**
The authors apply the GRPO reinforcement learning algorithm to models initialized with IWM, SR, or IL. The environments used (`WebShop`, `ALFWorld`, `SearchQA`) have verifiable rewards, allowing for standard RL optimization.
*   **Higher Ceilings:** Checkpoints from early experience consistently lead to higher post-RL performance. On `ALFWorld` with `Llama-3.1-8B`, the IWM-initialized model reaches **97.7%** success after RL, whereas the IL-initialized model reaches only **93.8%** (Figure 3b). This **+3.9%** advantage persists even though both models undergo the *same* RL training steps.
*   **Amplified Advantages:** In some cases, the gap widens during RL. On `ALFWorld`, the pre-RL gap between IWM and IL is roughly 5.4% (85.9% vs 80.5% in Table 2), but post-RL, the gap becomes 3.9% (97.7% vs 93.8%). Wait, looking closely at Figure 3 and Appendix Table 5: The IL+GRPO reaches 93.8%, while IWM+GRPO reaches 97.7%. The pre-RL gap was 85.9% - 80.5% = 5.4%. The post-RL gap is 97.7% - 93.8% = 3.9%. The gap narrows slightly in percentage points, but the absolute ceiling is higher. In `WebShop` (Figure 3a), the IWM+GRPO reaches **91.4%** vs IL+GRPO **80.5%** (Appendix Table 6), a massive **+10.9%** difference post-RL.
*   **Necessity of Warm-Start:** Running GRPO directly from the raw pretrained model (without any supervised stage) performs worst and shows unstable training dynamics (Section 5.4). This confirms that a strong initialization is required for RL to work effectively in these environments, and early experience provides a *better* initialization than imitation learning.

**Implication**
This result is strategically vital. It means organizations can deploy early experience methods *now* in environments without rewards to build a strong policy. Later, if reward infrastructure is added, they can fine-tune that same policy with RL to unlock further gains, without needing to retrain from scratch. The early experience stage effectively "shapes" the policy landscape to make subsequent RL optimization more stable and effective.

### 5.5 Ablation Studies and Scaling

To understand the limits and resource requirements of early experience, the paper conducts ablations on data efficiency, branching factor, and model scale.

**Data Efficiency (Figure 4a)**
How much expert data is actually needed if we use early experience? Figure 4(a) varies the fraction of expert trajectories used to seed the rollout generation.
*   **Surpassing Full Data with Less:** On `WebShop`, using only **1/8 of the expert demonstrations** with Implicit World Modeling yields a success rate of **58.6%** (approximate from chart, consistent with Table 2 full data IWM). This surpasses Imitation Learning trained on the **full dataset** (47.3%).
*   **Diminishing Returns:** Both IWM and SR improve with more expert data, but the margin over imitation learning remains large even at low data fractions. This confirms that the *rollout data* (generated by the agent) provides a significant portion of the supervision signal, reducing reliance on expensive human annotations.

**Branching Factor (Figure 4b)**
The branching factor $K$ (number of alternative actions sampled per state) controls the volume of rollout data.
*   **IWM Prefers Quantity:** Implicit World Modeling improves steadily as $K$ increases. This makes sense: predicting more diverse state transitions helps the model build a richer internal dynamics model.
*   **SR Prefers Quality:** Self-Reflection improves at small to moderate $K$ (e.g., 2–4) but can become non-monotonic at very large $K$. The authors explain that comparing too many alternatives occasionally includes other successful actions, reducing the contrast with the expert. Additionally, current models have limited context capacity to reason over many alternatives in a single prompt (Section 6.3).
*   **Trade-off:** This reveals a resource trade-off. IWM scales well with compute (more rollouts = better), while SR requires careful tuning to ensure the contrastive examples remain informative.

**Model Scaling (Figure 5)**
Does early experience work for larger models, or does it only help small models "catch up"? The authors evaluate `Llama-3.3-70B` on `WebArena-Lite` using LoRA fine-tuning.
*   **Persistent Gains:** Early experience outperforms imitation learning at every scale. For the 70B model, IWM achieves **16.4%** success versus **13.3%** for Imitation Learning (Table 10).
*   **Complementary:** Absolute performance rises with scale (70B > 8B > 3B), and early experience checkpoints consistently occupy the top curve. This indicates that the supervision provided by early experience complements model capacity rather than substituting for it. Even with parameter-efficient tuning (LoRA), the gains persist, demonstrating effectiveness under constrained compute budgets.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment of Evidence

The experimental section provides robust support for the paper's claims, but a critical reader should note specific conditions and limitations.

**Strengths of the Evidence**
*   **Diversity:** The use of eight environments across three distinct domains (web, tools, simulation) makes it difficult to attribute gains to a single benchmark's quirks.
*   **Compute Control:** By matching the total optimization steps between early experience and imitation learning baselines (Section 5.1), the authors rule out "more training" as the cause of improvement.
*   **Ablation Depth:** The comparison against STaR and Long CoT (Table 4) effectively isolates the value of *environment interaction*. It proves that the gains come from observing state transitions, not just generating more text.
*   **RL Bridge:** The warm-start experiments (Figure 3) are particularly convincing for long-term viability. They show that early experience is not a dead-end but a compatible precursor to RL.

**Limitations and Nuances**
*   **Absolute Performance in Hard Domains:** While relative gains are consistent, absolute performance remains low in complex web environments. On `WebArena-Lite`, even the best method (IWM with 70B) only reaches **16.4%** success (Table 10). This indicates that while early experience helps, it does not "solve" the difficulty of open-world web navigation.
*   **Rollout Generation Cost:** The paper notes that rollout data can be an order of magnitude larger than expert data (Section 4.2). Generating this data requires executing the agent in the environment $K$ times per state. In real-world settings (e.g., live websites), this interaction cost could be prohibitive or risky (e.g., making real purchases). The experiments use simulators or sandboxes; deploying this in production requires careful safety constraints.
*   **Short-Horizon Focus:** The Limitations section (Section 7) acknowledges that current approaches focus on short-horizon traces. Extending early experience to address long-horizon credit assignment without explicit rewards remains an open challenge. The gains in `TravelPlanner` are significant but absolute scores (32.2%) suggest long-horizon planning is still difficult.
*   **Reward-Free Assumption:** The paradigm relies on the environment providing *state feedback*. In some real-world scenarios, state feedback might be delayed or opaque (e.g., an email is sent, but you don't know if it was read until days later). The current methods assume immediate state transitions are observable.

**Conclusion on Experiments**
The experiments convincingly demonstrate that **reward-free interaction data is a valuable supervision signal**. The consistency of gains across models, domains, and data regimes supports the core thesis: agents can learn from their own "early" mistakes and observations before they are experts. While absolute performance in the hardest domains remains a challenge, the paradigm successfully bridges the gap between static imitation learning and dynamic reinforcement learning, offering a practical path forward for agent development in reward-scarce environments.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the early experience paradigm demonstrates consistent improvements across eight environments, a critical examination reveals important assumptions, constraints, and open challenges. Understanding these limitations is essential for researchers considering adoption and for identifying directions for future work. I will analyze the approach's foundational assumptions, scenarios it does not address, computational and data trade-offs, and remaining weaknesses grounded in evidence from the paper.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions

The early experience paradigm relies on several assumptions that may not hold in all real-world deployment scenarios.

**Assumption 1: Immediate State Feedback is Observable**

The core mechanism of early experience requires that when an agent takes an action $a^j_i$ at state $s_i$, the environment returns an observable next state $s^j_i$ that can be used as supervision (Section 4.1). This assumption holds in simulated environments like ALFWorld, WebShop, and ScienceWorld, where state transitions are immediate and fully observable.

However, many real-world agent tasks involve **delayed or opaque feedback**. For example:
*   An agent sends an email on behalf of a user, but whether the recipient read or acted on it may not be known for days.
*   An agent submits a form to a third-party service, but confirmation of successful processing may arrive via a separate channel (e.g., email confirmation) rather than as an immediate state change.
*   An agent makes a reservation, but the validity of that reservation may only be discovered at check-in time.

The paper acknowledges this limitation implicitly in Section 7 (Limitations and Future Work), noting that extending early experience to address **long-horizon credit assignment without explicit rewards remains an open challenge**. When state feedback is delayed, the rollout dataset $D_{rollout} = \{(s_i, a^j_i, s^j_i)\}$ cannot be constructed immediately, breaking the training pipeline.

**Assumption 2: Environment Supports Safe Exploration**

The paradigm requires executing $K$ alternative actions at each expert state to collect rollout data (Section 4.1). In the experiments, all environments are **simulators or sandboxes** where exploration carries no real-world cost. Appendix B details show that WebShop uses a simulated e-commerce site, ALFWorld uses a text-based household simulator, and TravelPlanner uses a gym-based environment with synthetic data.

In production settings, this assumption becomes problematic:
*   **Financial risk**: An agent exploring alternative actions on a real shopping site might place actual orders, incurring costs.
*   **Data integrity**: An agent modifying a real database (e.g., CRM systems in Tau-Bench scenarios) could corrupt production data.
*   **User experience**: An agent interacting with real users (as in Tau-Bench's customer service scenarios) might provide incorrect information during exploration.

The paper does not address **safe exploration mechanisms** such as action masking, simulation-to-real transfer, or rollback capabilities. This limits immediate applicability to high-stakes domains.

**Assumption 3: State Representations are Language-Compatible**

Both Implicit World Modeling and Self-Reflection treat states as natural language sequences that can be predicted using next-token prediction (Section 4.2). This works well for environments where states are already textual (webpage HTML, tool outputs, task descriptions).

However, environments with **high-dimensional or non-linguistic state representations** pose challenges:
*   Visual states (e.g., screenshots in GUI agents) require additional encoding before they can be used in the next-state prediction objective.
*   Structured states (e.g., database schemas, API response objects) may need serialization that preserves semantic meaning.
*   Partially observable states (where the agent cannot see the full environment configuration) complicate the definition of $s^j_i$.

The paper's environments all provide text-based observations (Table 1), so this assumption was not stress-tested.

### 6.2 Scenarios and Edge Cases Not Addressed

Several important problem settings fall outside the scope of the current evaluation.

**Long-Horizon Tasks with Sparse State Changes**

While TravelPlanner involves long-horizon planning (multi-day itineraries), the state representation includes explicit progress tracking (budget spent, days planned), making state transitions relatively dense. Tasks where **state changes are sparse or subtle** are not evaluated:
*   Multi-day workflows where most actions produce no visible state change until the final step.
*   Tasks requiring memory of events from many steps ago (e.g., "remember the user's preference mentioned 20 turns ago").
*   Environments where the relevant state is distributed across multiple systems (e.g., checking email, calendar, and CRM simultaneously).

The Limitations section (Section 7) explicitly acknowledges this: *"our current approaches, implicit world modeling and self-reflection, focus on short-horizon traces."* The branching factor ablation (Figure 4b) also reveals that Self-Reflection becomes non-monotonic at large $K$ partly because *"current models have limited capacity to reason over many alternatives and outcomes in a single context"* (Section 6.3). This context window limitation compounds in long-horizon settings.

**Multi-Agent or Adversarial Environments**

All eight environments are **single-agent, cooperative settings** where the environment responds deterministically (or stochastically but not adversarially) to agent actions. Scenarios not addressed include:
*   **Multi-agent coordination**: Tasks requiring collaboration or negotiation with other agents.
*   **Adversarial environments**: Settings where the environment actively tries to thwart the agent (e.g., security testing, competitive games).
*   **Human-in-the-loop feedback**: Scenarios where humans provide intermittent feedback that is neither a full reward signal nor a state transition.

The MDP formulation in Section 3 assumes a single agent interacting with a static environment transition function $\mathcal{T}$. Extending early experience to multi-agent or adversarial settings would require rethinking how rollout data is collected and interpreted.

**Continual Learning and Distribution Shift Over Time**

The experiments train on a fixed dataset $D_{expert}$ augmented with rollouts, then evaluate on a held-out test set. This does not address **continual learning** scenarios where:
*   The environment changes over time (e.g., website layouts update, APIs deprecate).
*   New tasks are introduced incrementally without retraining from scratch.
*   The agent must balance exploration for learning with exploitation for task performance during deployment.

Section 7 mentions future work on *"integrating it with reward-based fine-tuning in a continual learning setting,"* implying this is not addressed in the current work. The paradigm as presented is a **batch training approach**, not an online learning algorithm.

**Cross-Environment Transfer**

Each environment is trained and evaluated independently. The paper does not investigate whether early experience learned in one environment (e.g., WebShop navigation) transfers to another (e.g., WebArena CMS tasks). Section 7 notes future work on *"leveraging cross-environment transfer,"* confirming this is an open question.

This limitation is significant because one motivation for early experience is scalability—if agents could learn general interaction principles from one domain and apply them to another, the value of the paradigm would increase substantially. Table 3 shows out-of-domain generalization within a single environment (e.g., SearchQA trained on Musique, tested on HotpotQA), but not cross-environment transfer.

### 6.3 Computational and Data Constraints

The paradigm introduces specific resource requirements that may limit adoption in resource-constrained settings.

**Rollout Generation Overhead**

The rollout dataset $D_{rollout}$ is often **an order of magnitude larger** than the expert dataset $D_{expert}$ (Section 4.2). This amplification comes at a cost:

| Environment | Expert Pairs ($D_{expert}$) | Rollout Triples ($D_{rollout}$) | Amplification |
|-------------|----------------------------|--------------------------------|---------------|
| ALFWorld | 21,031 | 189,279 | 9× (Appendix B.1) |
| WebShop | 15,464 | 122,954 | 8× (Appendix B.2) |
| WebArena-Lite | 7,044 | 42,264 | 6× (Appendix B.8) |
| TravelPlanner | 1,395 | 70,000+ | 50× (Appendix B.7) |

Generating this data requires:
1.  **Environment interactions**: Each alternative action must be executed in the environment, which may be slow (e.g., web page loads, API calls).
2.  **Model inference**: The policy must propose $K$ alternative actions at each state, requiring $K$ forward passes per state.
3.  **Storage**: Rollout data must be stored and processed during training, increasing memory requirements.

In Appendix B, the authors note using **up to 8 H100 GPUs** for training and evaluation. For the 70B model experiments, parameter-efficient LoRA fine-tuning was required due to compute constraints (Section 6.4). Organizations with limited compute may find the rollout generation phase prohibitive.

**Branching Factor Trade-offs**

Figure 4(b) reveals that the two methods have **different scaling profiles** with respect to the branching factor $K$:

*   **Implicit World Modeling**: Improves steadily as $K$ increases. More diverse state transitions help the model build a richer dynamics model.
*   **Self-Reflection**: Improves at small to moderate $K$ (e.g., 2–4) but can become **non-monotonic** at very large $K$.

The paper explains this divergence in Section 6.3: *"comparing many alternatives occasionally includes other success-leading actions, reducing contrast with the expert, and current models have limited capacity to reason over many alternatives and outcomes in a single context."*

This creates a practical trade-off:
*   IWM scales well with compute (more rollouts = better performance) but requires more environment interactions.
*   SR is more data-efficient per rollout but requires careful tuning of $K$ and may not benefit from massive data amplification.

Organizations must choose based on their constraints: if environment interactions are cheap but training compute is limited, IWM may be preferable. If environment interactions are expensive (e.g., real-world APIs with rate limits), SR with modest $K$ may be more practical.

**Quality Filtering Requirements**

Self-Reflection data requires **quality filtering** to remove low-quality samples. Appendix B.8 notes that for WebArena-Lite, the authors *"filter out low-quality generations where the explanation incorrectly supports a non-expert action, leaving 3,190 high-quality self-reflection examples"* from an initially larger set. Appendix B.2 mentions filtering WebShop trajectories to retain only those completable within 15 steps.

This filtering introduces:
*   **Additional compute**: Running a quality checker (often the same model) on all generated reflections.
*   **Data loss**: Some generated samples are discarded, reducing the effective amplification factor.
*   **Hyperparameter tuning**: Determining filtering thresholds requires validation data or heuristics.

Implicit World Modeling does not require this filtering (all state transitions are valid training signals), giving it an implementation advantage despite higher data volume.

### 6.4 Performance Limitations in Challenging Domains

While early experience consistently outperforms imitation learning, **absolute performance remains low** in the most challenging environments.

**Web Navigation Ceiling**

Table 10 shows results on WebArena-Lite, a subset of 165 challenging web tasks:
*   **Llama-3.1-8B with IWM**: 8.5% success rate
*   **Llama-3.3-70B with IWM**: 16.4% success rate

Even with a 70B model and early experience augmentation, the agent succeeds on fewer than 1 in 6 tasks. This indicates that while early experience improves policies, it does not **solve** the fundamental challenges of open-world web navigation:
*   Complex DOM structures with hundreds of elements.
*   Ambiguous task specifications.
*   Long-horizon dependencies across multiple pages.

The +3.6% gain from IWM over imitation learning on WebArena-Lite (Table 2) is meaningful but modest in absolute terms. Researchers should not expect early experience to bridge the gap from research benchmarks to production-ready web agents without additional innovations.

**Long-Horizon Planning Challenges**

TravelPlanner tests multi-day itinerary planning with budget and constraint satisfaction. Table 9 shows:
*   **Llama-3.1-8B with SR**: 32.2% pass rate (best result)
*   **Imitation Learning baseline**: 17.2% pass rate

The +15.0% relative gain is substantial, but a 32.2% pass rate means the agent fails on **two-thirds of tasks**. Section 5.2 notes that SR excels in environments requiring constraint satisfaction, yet absolute performance suggests that **long-horizon credit assignment without rewards** remains difficult. The Limitations section (Section 7) explicitly identifies this as an open challenge.

**Open Action Space Difficulty**

Environments with open action spaces (e.g., SearchQA with free-form search queries, WebArena with arbitrary web interactions) show smaller absolute gains than closed action space environments:
*   **SearchQA**: +0.6% to +3.3% gains (Table 2)
*   **ALFWorld** (closed actions): +3.9% to +7.8% gains (Table 2)

Section 5.2 explains: *"Open action sets... allow a vast number of possible actions, often combinatorial in nature. These are the hardest regimes; nevertheless, early experience still yields reliable gains."* The phrasing acknowledges that while gains are consistent, the **magnitude** is limited by action space complexity.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Future Directions

The paper identifies several unresolved questions that warrant future investigation.

**Integration with Reward-Based Learning**

Section 5.4 demonstrates that early experience checkpoints serve as superior warm-starts for RL, but the **optimal integration strategy** remains unclear:
*   Should early experience training be interleaved with RL updates, or kept as a separate pre-training phase?
*   How should the rollout data be weighted relative to reward-labeled trajectories during combined training?
*   Can early experience continue to provide value *during* RL training (e.g., by generating additional rollouts on-policy)?

Section 7 mentions future work on *"integrating it with reward-based fine-tuning in a continual learning setting,"* confirming this is not addressed.

**Safety and Alignment During Exploration**

The paradigm encourages agents to explore alternative actions, including suboptimal ones. In production settings, this raises **safety concerns**:
*   How can exploration be constrained to prevent harmful actions (e.g., deleting data, making unauthorized purchases)?
*   Can the agent learn to distinguish between "safe to explore" and "unsafe to explore" states?
*   Should exploration be limited to simulation environments, with policies transferred to production only after validation?

The paper does not address safety mechanisms, assuming all experiments occur in controlled sandbox environments.

**Generalization Across Model Families**

The experiments cover Llama and Qwen model families, with additional Gemma results in SearchQA (Appendix B.5). However, several questions remain:
*   Do the gains persist for **smaller models** (e.g., <3B parameters) where reasoning capacity may be more limited?
*   How does early experience interact with **mixture-of-experts** architectures or other recent model innovations?
*   Are there model-specific hyperparameters (e.g., optimal $K$ for SR) that must be tuned per architecture?

Figure 5 shows gains persist at 70B scale, but the lower bound of model size effectiveness is not explored.

**Theoretical Understanding of Why It Works**

The paper provides empirical evidence that early experience improves performance, but the **theoretical mechanisms** are not fully elucidated:
*   Does IWM primarily reduce distribution shift by exposing the agent to off-path states, or does it improve the policy's internal representations?
*   Does SR teach generalizable reasoning principles, or does it simply provide more diverse training examples?
*   What is the relationship between rollout data diversity and downstream performance?

Section 5.2 offers hypotheses (e.g., *"IWM helps the policy internalize transition regularities"*), but formal analysis is limited. Future work could investigate these questions through representational analysis or information-theoretic measures.

### 6.6 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes the key trade-offs practitioners should consider when adopting early experience:

| Dimension | Implicit World Modeling | Self-Reflection |
|-----------|------------------------|-----------------|
| **Data Volume** | High (benefits from large $K$) | Moderate (optimal at $K=2$–4) |
| **Environment Interactions** | Many (one per alternative action) | Many (same as IWM) |
| **Quality Filtering** | Not required | Required (remove incorrect rationales) |
| **Best For** | Stable dynamics (WebShop, ALFWorld) | Reasoning/constraints (TravelPlanner, ScienceWorld) |
| **Compute Profile** | Training-heavy (large dataset) | Inference-heavy (rationale generation) |
| **Absolute Gains** | Larger in predictable environments | Larger in reasoning-heavy tasks |

**Key Takeaway**: Early experience is not a universal solution but a **pragmatic bridge** between imitation learning and reinforcement learning. It offers consistent improvements across diverse settings while operating under realistic constraints (no rewards required). However, practitioners should expect modest absolute gains in the most challenging domains, invest in safe exploration infrastructure for production deployment, and plan for eventual integration with reward-based learning as infrastructure matures.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work does more than propose two new training objectives; it fundamentally alters the roadmap for developing autonomous language agents. By validating the "early experience" paradigm, the paper shifts the field's focus from waiting for perfect reward infrastructure to leveraging immediate, reward-free interaction data. This section outlines how this work changes the research landscape, the specific avenues it opens for future investigation, practical use cases where this paradigm excels, and concrete guidance for integrating these methods into existing pipelines.

### 7.1 Shifting the Research Landscape: From Binary to Continuum

Historically, agent training has been viewed as a binary choice: either rely on static human demonstrations (Supervised Fine-Tuning) or wait for verifiable reward signals (Reinforcement Learning). Figure 1 visualizes this progression, positioning "Early Experience" as a critical middle ground between the "Era of Human Data" and the "Era of Experience."

This work changes the landscape in three specific ways:

*   **Redefining Trainable Data:** The paper demonstrates that state transitions $(s_i, a^j_i, s^j_i)$ constitute valid supervision signals independent of rewards (Section 4.1). This expands the solution space for researchers working in environments where rewards are ambiguous or sparse (e.g., web navigation, customer service). Instead of treating non-expert trajectories as "failed data" to be discarded, they are reclassified as "experience data" to be mined for dynamics and reasoning lessons.
*   **Decoupling Dynamics from Rewards:** By showing that Implicit World Modeling (IWM) improves performance without rewards (Table 2), the paper proves that learning *how the environment works* is a distinct and valuable objective from learning *what is good*. This encourages future architectures to explicitly model environment dynamics as an auxiliary task, even in purely supervised settings.
*   **Lowering the Barrier for RL:** The results in Figure 3 show that early experience checkpoints serve as superior warm-starts for Reinforcement Learning. This implies that RL is no longer an "all-or-nothing" proposition. Researchers can deploy agents using early experience immediately, then layer on RL later when reward infrastructure matures, without discarding prior training investments.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Avenues

The validation of this paradigm opens several specific lines of inquiry that were previously impractical or overlooked. The paper's Limitations and Future Work section (Section 7) explicitly highlights some of these, while the experimental results suggest others.

*   **Continual and Online Learning:** The current implementation uses a batch training approach (collect rollouts, then train). A natural extension is **online early experience**, where the agent collects rollouts during deployment and updates its policy continually. Section 7 notes future work on *"integrating it with reward-based fine-tuning in a continual learning setting."* This would allow agents to adapt to changing environments (e.g., website layout updates) without full retraining.
*   **Cross-Environment Transfer:** Currently, models are trained and evaluated within single environments (Table 1). The paradigm suggests that environment dynamics (e.g., "clicking a button changes the page state") might be transferable across domains. Section 7 identifies *"leveraging cross-environment transfer"* as a key direction. If an agent learns general interaction principles in WebShop, can it apply them to WebArena without new expert data? This remains an open empirical question.
*   **Safety and Alignment via Negative Experience:** The rollout dataset $D_{rollout}$ inherently contains suboptimal actions and their consequences (e.g., error messages, budget violations). Future work could explicitly train agents to recognize and avoid these "negative states" as a form of safety alignment. This is particularly relevant given the safety concerns raised in Section 6.1 regarding real-world exploration.
*   **Hybrid Objective Optimization:** Section 5.4 demonstrates sequential training (Early Experience $\rightarrow$ RL). Future research could explore **joint optimization**, where the world modeling loss $L_{IWM}$ and reflection loss $L_{SR}$ are combined with RL loss during the same training phase. This could stabilize RL training by maintaining the dynamics grounding provided by early experience.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The experimental results across eight environments (Table 2) provide clear signals for where practitioners should prioritize this paradigm.

*   **Enterprise Automation (Tool Use & Customer Service):**
    *   **Context:** Environments like `Tau-Bench` involve interacting with internal APIs and adhering to policy documents. Rewards are often hard to define programmatically (e.g., "was the customer satisfied?"), but state feedback (API responses, policy checks) is available.
    *   **Application:** Use Self-Reflection (SR) to train agents on why certain API calls violate policy. Table 2 shows SR improves `Tau-Bench` success by **+5.6%** (Qwen-2.5-7B) over imitation learning. This is ideal for scaling customer support agents where expert data is scarce but interaction logs are abundant.
*   **Web Navigation in Sandboxed Environments:**
    *   **Context:** Tasks like `WebShop` involve high-dimensional observation spaces (DOM trees) where understanding state changes is critical.
    *   **Application:** Use Implicit World Modeling (IWM) to help agents predict page changes. Table 2 shows IWM improves `WebShop` success by **+18.4%** (Llama-3.2-3B). This is highly applicable for internal workflow automation (e.g., filling forms, navigating intranets) where simulations exist but rewards do not.
*   **Data-Scarce Domains:**
    *   **Context:** In specialized domains (e.g., scientific experimentation in `ScienceWorld`), expert demonstrations are expensive to collect.
    *   **Application:** Leverage the data efficiency shown in Figure 4(a). On `WebShop`, using only **1/8 of expert trajectories** with early experience surpasses imitation learning trained on the **full dataset**. This allows organizations to deploy capable agents with minimal human annotation budget.
*   **RL Preparation Pipeline:**
    *   **Context:** For teams planning to use Reinforcement Learning eventually (e.g., for optimization), but lacking stable training dynamics.
    *   **Application:** Mandate an early experience pre-training phase before RL. Figure 3 shows that early experience warm-starts achieve higher post-RL ceilings (e.g., **97.7%** vs **93.8%** on `ALFWorld`). This reduces the risk of RL instability and improves final performance.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For researchers and engineers looking to implement early experience, the paper provides specific hyperparameters and decision criteria. The following guidance synthesizes the experimental configurations (Appendix B) and ablation studies (Section 6).

**When to Prefer Implicit World Modeling (IWM) vs. Self-Reflection (SR)**

The choice between methods depends on the environment's dynamics and your compute constraints.

| Feature | Implicit World Modeling (IWM) | Self-Reflection (SR) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Best Environment Type** | Stable, predictable dynamics (e.g., `WebShop`, `ALFWorld`). | Reasoning-heavy, constraint satisfaction (e.g., `TravelPlanner`, `ScienceWorld`). |
| **Branching Factor ($K$)** | Scale up $K$. Performance improves steadily with more alternatives (Figure 4b). | Keep $K$ modest (2–4). Large $K$ reduces contrast and exceeds context limits (Section 6.3). |
| **Data Volume** | High. Rollout data is often **8–9× larger** than expert data (Appendix B.1, B.2). | Moderate. Requires quality filtering (e.g., **3,190** examples from larger set in WebArena, Appendix B.8). |
| **Compute Profile** | Training-heavy (processing large datasets). | Inference-heavy (generating rationales for filtering). |
| **Filtering Needed?** | No. All state transitions are valid training signals. | Yes. Must filter rationales that incorrectly support non-expert actions. |

**Critical Hyperparameters and Infrastructure**

*   **Branching Factor ($K$):** This is the most sensitive hyperparameter. For IWM, the paper uses $K=8$ in `ALFWorld` (Appendix B.1) and exhaustive augmentation in `TravelPlanner` (Appendix B.7). For SR, $K=3$ is common (Appendix B.1, B.2). Do not exceed $K=4$ for SR without testing context window limits.
*   **Compute Budget:** Experiments used at most **8 H100 GPUs** (Section 5.1). For large models (e.g., `Llama-3.3-70B`), parameter-efficient LoRA fine-tuning is required to match compute budgets (Section 6.4). If resources are limited, prioritize SR with a smaller $K$ to reduce data storage and training time.
*   **Environment Safety:** The paper assumes a **sandboxed environment** where exploration is safe (Section 6.1). Do not deploy rollout generation on live production systems (e.g., real banking APIs) without action masking or simulation layers. The cost of generating rollouts involves $K$ environment interactions per state; ensure your environment can handle this load (e.g., `TravelPlanner` generated **70,000+** samples from 1,395 expert pairs, Appendix B.7).
*   **Training Schedule:** For IWM, use a two-stage pipeline: **1 epoch** of world modeling followed by supervised fine-tuning to match the total imitation learning budget (Section 5.1). For SR, mix reflection data with expert data and train for the same number of epochs as imitation learning.

**Missing Details for Practitioners**

While the paper provides extensive appendices, a few implementation details require inference or external tooling:
*   **Prompt Templates:** The Self-Reflection prompt template is provided in Section 4.3, but environment-specific state formatting (e.g., how to serialize a DOM tree vs. a JSON API response) is left to the implementer (Appendix B examples show varied formats).
*   **Rollout Generation Latency:** The paper does not report the wall-clock time required to generate $D_{rollout}$. In slow environments (e.g., real web browsing), this could be a bottleneck. Practitioners should benchmark environment step time before committing to large $K$.
*   **Quality Filter Implementation:** For SR, the paper mentions filtering "low-quality samples" (Appendix B.8) but does not specify the exact classifier or heuristic used beyond checking if the explanation supports the expert action. Implementers may need to develop their own verification logic.

By treating early experience as a modular layer between data collection and policy optimization, practitioners can achieve immediate gains in agent robustness and data efficiency, while positioning their systems for future reinforcement learning integration.
