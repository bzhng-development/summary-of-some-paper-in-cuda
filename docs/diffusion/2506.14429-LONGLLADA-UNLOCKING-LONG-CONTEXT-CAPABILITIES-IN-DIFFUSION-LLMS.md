## 1. Executive Summary

This work addresses the unexplored long-context capabilities of diffusion LLMs by presenting the first systematic analysis comparing `LLaDA-8B` against auto-regressive `LLaMA3-8B`, revealing that diffusion models maintain stable perplexity during direct context extrapolation due to bidirectional attention dynamics. The authors propose `LongLLaDA`, a training-free method integrating NTK-based RoPE extrapolation that successfully extends context windows by 6× (up to 24k tokens) without further training. Validated on `LongBench` and `RULER`, this study establishes essential benchmarks showing diffusion LLMs match auto-regressive models on retrieval tasks while excelling in synthetic QA, providing critical insights for future long-context diffusion research.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Unexplored Gap: Long-Context Capabilities in Diffusion LLMs

This paper addresses a critical blind spot in the rapidly evolving landscape of Large Language Diffusion Models. While diffusion LLMs have garnered significant research attention for their potential to overcome fundamental limitations of traditional auto-regressive models, **their long-context capabilities remain completely unexplored**. The authors explicitly state in the Introduction that there exists no "systematic analysis or methods for context extension" for diffusion LLMs (Section 1).

This gap is surprising given the parallel research trajectory: auto-regressive LLMs have undergone extensive investigation into length extrapolation, with well-established methods like NTK-based RoPE scaling (bloc97, 2023b), Linear Positional Interpolation (Chen et al., 2023), and various training-free approaches (An et al., 2024a;b). Yet diffusion LLMs — despite being scaled to billions of parameters (Nie et al., 2025; Ye et al., 2025) — have received no equivalent treatment for context extension.

### Why This Problem Matters: Theoretical and Practical Significance

The importance of this problem operates on two levels:

**Theoretical Significance:** Diffusion LLMs employ fundamentally different generation mechanisms than auto-regressive models. Instead of generating tokens sequentially (left-to-right), diffusion models iteratively denoise all tokens simultaneously through multiple sampling steps. This architectural difference raises a critical question: *do established length extrapolation principles from auto-regressive models transfer to diffusion architectures?* The paper notes that diffusion LLMs are "regarded as a potential solution to key limitations of traditional auto-regressive LLMs," including "maintaining coherence across extended contexts" (Section 1). Understanding whether and how they handle long contexts is essential for validating these claims.

**Practical Impact:** Real-world applications increasingly demand models that can process documents, codebases, and conversations spanning tens of thousands of tokens. If diffusion LLMs cannot handle contexts beyond their pretrained length, their utility remains severely constrained. The paper benchmarks models at 4k, 8k, 16k, 24k, and 32k token contexts (Figure 1, Figure 2), reflecting the scale requirements of modern applications.

### Prior Approaches: What Exists and Where They Fall Short

**Auto-Regressive Length Extrapolation:** The Related Work section (Section 6) outlines the mature ecosystem of length extrapolation methods for auto-regressive LLMs:

- **Positional Interpolation (Linear PI):** Scales position indices to fit within pretrained ranges with minimal fine-tuning (Chen et al., 2023)
- **NTK-Aware Scaling:** Adjusts the rotary base in RoPE to achieve "plug-and-play length extrapolation" without training (bloc97, 2023b)
- **Training-Based Approaches:** Amplifying the rotary base and training on longer sequences has become "the dominant approach" (Rozière et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b)
- **Attention Limiting Methods:** ReRoPE (Su, 2023), ReAttention (Liu et al., 2024b), and DCA (An et al., 2024a;b) achieve extrapolation by constraining relative position attention

**The Critical Limitation:** All these methods were developed and validated exclusively for auto-regressive architectures. The paper emphasizes in Section 6 that "there is still no discussion on the long-context capability of diffusion LLMs." This creates a knowledge vacuum: researchers cannot assume these techniques transfer without empirical validation, because diffusion and auto-regressive models differ in:

1. **Attention Mechanism:** Diffusion LLMs use bidirectional attention (all tokens attend to all tokens), while auto-regressive models use causal (unidirectional) attention
2. **Training Objective:** Diffusion models learn to denoise corrupted sequences, not predict next tokens
3. **Positional Exposure:** As Section 3 reveals, bidirectional attention exposes diffusion models to relative position ranges of $[1 - T_{\text{train}}, T_{\text{train}} - 1]$, contrasting with $[0, T_{\text{train}} - 1]$ for auto-regressive models

### How This Paper Positions Itself

The paper positions itself as the **first systematic investigation** of diffusion LLM long-context behavior. It makes three distinct contributions that differentiate it from prior work:

**1. Phenomenological Discovery:** Before proposing any method, the authors first characterize *how* diffusion LLMs behave during direct context extrapolation. Figure 1 shows that unlike auto-regressive models which suffer "catastrophic perplexity surges" beyond their pretrained context (8k for LLaMA3), diffusion LLMs maintain "remarkably stable perplexity" (Abstract). Figure 2 reveals a "local perception" phenomenon where diffusion models can retrieve information from recent context segments even when total context exceeds pretrained length.

**2. Mechanistic Explanation:** The paper does not simply report observations — it explains *why* these phenomena occur through RoPE theory (Section 3). Figure 4 visualizes how bidirectional attention provides symmetric positional coverage, reducing out-of-distribution embedding spaces compared to auto-regressive models. This theoretical grounding distinguishes the work from purely empirical studies.

**3. Method Transfer and Validation:** Building on these insights, the paper proposes LongLLaDA — adapting NTK-based RoPE extrapolation (bloc97, 2023b) for diffusion architectures. Crucially, it validates that "established extrapolation scaling laws remain effective for extending the context windows of diffusion LLMs" (Abstract), achieving 6× context expansion (24k tokens) without training (Section 4).

**Positioning Relative to Diffusion LLM Research:** While concurrent work has explored diffusion LLMs for multimodality (Yang et al., 2025; You et al., 2025), reasoning (Zhao et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2025), and efficiency (Ma et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025), this paper carves out the long-context niche. It provides "essential theoretical insights and empirical benchmarks critical for advancing future research on long-context diffusion LLMs" (Abstract).

**Positioning Relative to Length Extrapolation Research:** The paper extends the length extrapolation literature beyond auto-regressive models. As Section 6 notes, prior extrapolation research "mainly focuses on adjusting position embedding, especially the widely used RoPE." This work demonstrates that RoPE-based extrapolation principles — specifically NTK scaling and its associated scaling laws (Liu et al., 2023b) — transfer to diffusion architectures, but with important caveats revealed through the local perception phenomenon.

The paper acknowledges its limitations in the Limitation section: results "mainly focus on LLaDA Series and the inference stage," with future work needed on fine-tuning extrapolation and sampling strategy analysis. This honest scoping positions the work as a foundational study rather than a comprehensive solution — appropriate for opening a new research direction.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

This paper presents a training-free method called `LongLLaDA` that extends the context window of diffusion-based large language models by adapting Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE) scaling techniques originally developed for auto-regressive models. The solution works by mathematically adjusting the positional encoding frequencies during inference without any additional model training, enabling diffusion LLMs to process contexts up to 6× longer (24k tokens) than their pretrained capacity (4k tokens).

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The technical system consists of four interconnected components that flow sequentially. **First**, the base diffusion LLM (such as `LLaDA-8B`) receives input tokens and applies bidirectional attention across all positions simultaneously, unlike auto-regressive models that process tokens sequentially. **Second**, the Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE) module encodes positional information into query and key vectors using trigonometric functions with a pretrained rotary base β0 = 500000 and maximum trained context length T_train = 4096 tokens. **Third**, the NTK-based extrapolation module computes a scaling factor λ based on the target context length t using the formula λ = 10^(-4) · (t/2π)^(d/d_extra), where d_extra = 64 is the critical dimension calculated from the model's architecture. **Fourth**, the scaled RoPE embeddings are applied during the diffusion denoising process across multiple sampling steps (default s = 32), producing output tokens with block size 32 or 64 depending on the task. Information flows from input tokens through position-encoded attention mechanisms, through the scaling transformation, and finally through iterative denoising to generate coherent long-context outputs.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin by explaining the fundamental difference between diffusion and auto-regressive attention mechanisms, since this architectural distinction drives all subsequent phenomena and design choices.
- We then detail the RoPE position encoding theory, including how bidirectional attention exposes diffusion models to symmetric relative position ranges that auto-regressive models never encounter.
- Next, we derive the NTK-based scaling formula step-by-step, showing how the critical dimension d_extra determines the maximum extrapolation capacity and why λ scales sup-linearly with context length.
- We explain the local perception phenomenon and its relationship to cosine function periodicity, clarifying why diffusion models can retrieve from recent context segments even beyond their trained length.
- We describe the experimental validation pipeline, including Needle-In-A-Haystack tasks, LongBench, and RULER benchmarks, with specific hyperparameters for sampling steps and block sizes.
- Finally, we analyze the task-dependent capability patterns, explaining why diffusion LLMs excel at synthetic QA but underperform on aggregation tasks compared to auto-regressive baselines.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a mechanistic analysis paper that discovers unexpected long-context behaviors in diffusion LLMs and transfers established auto-regressive length extrapolation methods to diffusion architectures through theoretical justification and empirical validation.

**Foundational Architecture: Diffusion vs. Auto-Regressive Attention**

Diffusion LLMs differ fundamentally from auto-regressive LLMs in their attention mechanism, and this difference drives all observed long-context phenomena. Auto-regressive models like `LLaMA3-8B` use causal (unidirectional) attention where each token can only attend to previous tokens, exposing them to relative position ranges of $[0, T_{\text{train}} - 1]$ during training, where $T_{\text{train}}$ is the maximum pretrained context length (8192 tokens for LLaMA3). In contrast, diffusion LLMs like `LLaDA-8B` use bidirectional attention where all tokens attend to all tokens simultaneously during the denoising process, exposing them to symmetric relative position ranges of $[1 - T_{\text{train}}, T_{\text{train}} - 1]$ during training (Section 3, Figure 4). This symmetric exposure means that for a diffusion model trained on 4096-token sequences, the relative positions span from $-4095$ to $+4095$, whereas an auto-regressive model trained on 8192-token sequences only sees positions from $0$ to $8191$. The critical insight is that bidirectional attention provides diffusion models with complete sinusoidal periods for both cosine and sine components in moderate frequency dimensions, enhancing their tolerance to direct length extrapolation beyond pretrained context windows.

**Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE) Theory and Extrapolation Failure Modes**

Rotary Position Embedding encodes absolute positions into query and key vectors using rotation matrices that implicitly capture relative positions through trigonometric function properties. The attention score between token $t$ (query) and token $s$ (key) at dimension pair $(2n, 2n+1)$ is computed as:

$$
A_{t,s} = \sum_{n=0}^{d/2-1} \left(q_t^{(2n)} k_s^{(2n)} + q_t^{(2n+1)} k_s^{(2n+1)}\right) \cos \theta_n(t - s) - \left(q_t^{(2n)} k_s^{(2n+1)} - q_t^{(2n+1)} k_s^{(2n)}\right) \sin \theta_n(t - s)
$$

where $d$ is the hidden dimension, $\theta_n = \beta_0^{-2n/d}$ is the rotary angle for dimension pair $n$, and $\beta_0$ is the rotary base (500000 for LLaDA, 10000 for standard RoPE). Each dimension pair has a different rotational frequency, with lower dimensions having shorter periods and higher dimensions having longer periods according to the formula $T_n = 2\pi \cdot \beta_0^{2n/d}$. The critical dimension $d_{\text{extra}}$ marks the boundary where dimensions below it complete at least one full sinusoidal period within the pretrained context length, while dimensions above it do not. For `LLaDA-8B` with $\beta_0 = 500000$ and $T_{\text{train}} = 4096$, the critical dimension is calculated as $d_{\text{extra}} = 64$ using the formula:

$$
d_{\text{extra}} = 2 \left\lceil \frac{d}{2} \log_{\beta_0} \frac{T_{\text{train}}}{2\pi} \right\rceil
$$

When models process contexts exceeding $T_{\text{train}}$, dimensions beyond $d_{\text{extra}}$ encounter out-of-distribution (OOD) position embeddings because they never observed complete sinusoidal periods during training. Auto-regressive models suffer catastrophic perplexity surges at this boundary (Figure 1a shows LLaMA3 perplexity exploding beyond 8k), while diffusion models maintain stable perplexity due to their symmetric positional coverage reducing the unlearned OOD space (Figure 4 illustrates this with gray regions showing unlearned embeddings).

**NTK-Based Scaling: The LongLLaDA Method**

The LongLLaDA method adapts NTK-aware RoPE scaling from auto-regressive models to diffusion architectures without any additional training, making it a plug-and-play inference-time solution. The core idea is to scale the rotary base $\beta_0$ by a factor $\lambda$, which reduces the rotary angles $\theta_n$ and effectively interpolates position indices into the pretrained range. The scaling factor $\lambda$ is computed based on the target extrapolation context length $t$ using the formula from Section 4:

$$
\lambda = 10^{-4} \cdot \left(\frac{t}{2\pi}\right)^{d/d_{\text{extra}}}
$$

This formula exhibits sup-linear, power-law growth with inference length, meaning that doubling the context length requires more than doubling the scaling factor. For `LLaDA-8B` with $d_{\text{extra}} = 64$, the required scaling factors for different target lengths are: $\lambda = 4$ for 8k context, $\lambda = 14$ for 16k context, $\lambda = 31$ for 24k context, and $\lambda = 55$ for 32k context (Section 4). The method works by replacing the original rotary base $\beta_0$ with $\lambda \cdot \beta_0$ during the RoPE computation in Equation 2, which compresses the positional frequencies so that even at extended context lengths, the position embeddings remain within the distribution the model observed during training. The maximum achievable extrapolation is bounded by the attention entropy constraint, which limits training-free methods to approximately 2× to 6× extension (Section 4 notes that $\lambda = 31$ achieves 24k but $\lambda = 55$ for 32k becomes unachievable).

**Local Perception Phenomenon and Its Mechanistic Explanation**

The local perception phenomenon describes how diffusion LLMs can successfully retrieve information from recent context segments even when the total context length exceeds their pretrained capacity, behaving like a sliding window rather than failing catastrophically. Figure 2 shows that `LLaDA-8B-Base` achieves 100% retrieval accuracy within 4k context, and when context extends to 24k, it still retrieves information from the nearest 4k window with measurable accuracy at deeper positions. This differs from auto-regressive models which fail outright beyond their pretrained length (LLaMA3 shows 0% retrieval beyond 8k in Figure 2b and 2d). The mechanistic explanation lies in the cosine function properties of RoPE: since cosine is an even function ($\cos(-x) = \cos(x)$), negative relative positions do not increase the model's maximum perceivable distance during pretraining, limiting retrieval to positions within the trained relative distance range. Figure 4 visualizes this by showing that while bidirectional attention reduces OOD space compared to auto-regressive models, the out-of-distribution regions beyond $T_{\text{train}}$ still represent unlearned positional information. The t-SNE visualization in Figure 5 confirms this interpretation: `LLaDA-8B-Base` shows no distribution shift between QK states within and beyond 4k context, with a uniform manifold for QK states with RoPE, demonstrating robustness to OOD position embeddings that auto-regressive models lack (Figure 5a shows LLaMA3 has two distinct distribution clusters for within vs. beyond 8k).

**Sampling Steps and Their Impact on Long-Context Performance**

Diffusion LLMs are uniquely influenced by sampling steps and strategies, unlike auto-regressive models which generate tokens in a single forward pass per token. The paper systematically evaluates how sampling step count $s$ affects retrieval depth in Needle-In-A-Haystack tasks, testing $s = 1, 4, 8, 16$ with default $s = 32$ (Figure 3, Figure 10, Figure 11). At $s = 1$ or $s = 4$, `LLaDA-8B-Base` fails to retrieve information beyond 8k length, achieving overall scores of 45.59 and 47.15 respectively (Figure 3a and 3b). Increasing to $s = 8$ or $s = 16$ enables retrieval depths of 25% at 16k context and almost 10% at 24k context, with overall scores of 52.75 and 53.60 respectively (Figure 3c and 3d). This demonstrates that while more sampling steps improve long-context performance by allowing more iterative refinement of token predictions, the fundamental constraint remains the maximum supported context length determined by RoPE parameters. The paper notes in Section 2 that "the long-context performance of diffusion LLMs is influenced by sampling steps, but remains constrained by the maximum supported context length," indicating that sampling strategy optimization alone cannot overcome positional encoding limitations.

**Experimental Validation Pipeline and Benchmark Configuration**

The paper validates LongLLaDA through three complementary benchmark suites, each with specific configuration parameters that must be precisely reported. The Needle-In-A-Haystack (NIAH) task evaluates retrieval accuracy across context lengths from 2k to 32k tokens, with all models required to generate at most 32 tokens and diffusion LLMs using block size and sampling steps of 32 by default (Section 2). LongBench evaluation uses 4k and 8k context lengths with the exceeding part truncated from the middle, output length of 512 for summary tasks and 64 for others, and block size of 64 for diffusion LLMs (Section 5). RULER benchmark tests at 4k, 8k, and 16k context lengths with block size and sampling steps set to 64 for diffusion LLMs (Section 5). Table 1 reports LongBench results across six task categories: Single-Doc QA (SD), Multi-Doc QA (MD), Summarization (Sum), In-Context Learning (ICL), Synthetic tasks (Syn), and Code, with weighted average (Avg) computed by evaluation data number. Table 2 reports RULER results across three task categories: NIAH retrieval, Aggregation (AGG), and QA, showing that diffusion LLMs match auto-regressive models on NIAH (99.7% vs 99.8% at 4k for LLaDA-8B-Base vs LLaMA3-8B-Base) but underperform on aggregation (65.2% vs 98.1%) while excelling at QA (82.5% vs 67.5%).

**Task-Dependent Capability Patterns and Their Implications**

The benchmark results reveal systematic task-dependent capability patterns that distinguish diffusion LLMs from auto-regressive baselines, with important implications for when to deploy each architecture. On retrieval tasks (NIAH variants including Single-Key, Multi-Key, Multi-Query, Multi-Value), diffusion LLMs achieve comparable performance to auto-regressive models, with `LLaDA-8B-Base` scoring 99.7% at 4k context versus `LLaMA3-8B-Base` at 99.8% (Table 2). However, on aggregation tasks (Variable Tracing, Frequent or Common Word Extraction), diffusion LLMs show significantly inferior performance, scoring 65.2% versus 98.1% at 4k context, because these tasks require tracking information across the entire context sequence rather than local retrieval. Surprisingly, on QA tasks (SQuAD, Hotpot) that challenge auto-regressive LLMs according to Hsieh et al. (2024), diffusion LLMs demonstrate superior capability, scoring 82.5% versus 67.5% at 4k context (Table 2). The paper notes in Section 5 that "only in the synthetic domain do LLaDA Series outperform LLaMA3 Series consistently" on LongBench (Table 1 shows Syn scores of 54.7-66.7 for LLaDA variants versus 46.6-55.9 for LLaMA3 variants). These patterns suggest that diffusion LLMs excel at tasks requiring holistic context understanding and synthetic reasoning but struggle with tasks requiring precise information aggregation across long sequences, likely due to the iterative denoising process providing multiple opportunities to refine answers but limited ability to maintain exact token-level tracking.

**Design Choices and Alternative Approaches Considered**

The paper makes several critical design choices that distinguish LongLLaDA from alternative approaches, each justified by theoretical or empirical evidence. The choice of NTK-based scaling over Linear Positional Interpolation (Chen et al., 2023) is motivated by NTK's "plug-and-play" nature requiring zero fine-tuning, which aligns with the paper's goal of training-free context extension (Section 6 notes NTK achieves this "without any fine-tuning and minimal perplexity degradation"). The choice to focus on inference-stage extrapolation rather than training-based approaches (Rozière et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2023) is driven by practical constraints: training on longer sequences requires substantial computational resources, while inference-stage scaling provides immediate utility. The decision to validate scaling laws from auto-regressive models (Liu et al., 2023b) on diffusion architectures tests whether fundamental RoPE dynamics transfer across model types, which the paper confirms: "established extrapolation scaling laws remain effective for extending the context windows of diffusion LLMs" (Abstract). An alternative approach would be to train diffusion LLMs on longer contexts from scratch, but the Limitation section acknowledges this as future work: "We will carry out the fine-tuning extrapolation experiments in the future to verify more conclusions in RoPE-based scaling theory." Another alternative would be to modify the attention mechanism itself (e.g., ReAttention, DCA from Section 6), but the paper chooses RoPE scaling because it preserves the pretrained model weights and requires only inference-time computation adjustments.

**Mathematical Constraints and Extrapolation Limits**

The LongLLaDA method operates within strict mathematical constraints that determine its maximum achievable context extension, and understanding these limits is essential for practical deployment. The extrapolation upper bound $T_{\text{extra}}$ for NTK methods is determined by the scaled period of the critical dimension according to Equation 5 in Appendix A:

$$
T_{\text{extra}} = 2\pi \cdot (\lambda \cdot \beta_0)^{d_{\text{extra}}/d}
$$

This means that for a given model architecture (fixed $d$ and $d_{\text{extra}}$), the maximum context length scales with the rotary base scaling factor $\lambda$. However, the paper notes in Appendix A that "such interpolation could theoretically avoid extrapolation issues, it can only achieve 2× to 6× long-context extension during inference, as longer inputs lead to increased attention entropy, limiting further extrapolation." For `LLaDA-8B`, this manifests empirically: $\lambda = 4$ and $\lambda = 14$ achieve near 100% recall across all depths within their ranges (Figure 6a and 6b), but $\lambda = 31$ shows a "lost-in-the-middle" phenomenon at intermediate depths similar to auto-regressive models (Figure 6c), and $\lambda = 55$ becomes unachievable (Figure 6d shows degraded performance). The lost-in-the-middle phenomenon (Liu et al., 2023a) indicates that LongLLaDA approaches its practical extrapolation limit at 24k context, where the model begins struggling to attend to information in the middle of the sequence despite successful scaling of positional embeddings. This constraint is fundamental to all training-free RoPE scaling methods and cannot be overcome without architectural modifications or additional training.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the paper's most novel contributions, distinguishing between incremental adaptations and fundamental advances in our understanding of diffusion LLMs. Each insight is anchored to specific evidence from the paper and positioned relative to prior work.

---

### 4.1 Bidirectional Attention as an Extrapolation Buffer

**What is Novel:** The paper discovers that bidirectional attention in diffusion LLMs provides inherent robustness to out-of-distribution (OOD) position embeddings during length extrapolation — a property absent in auto-regressive models. This is not merely an empirical observation but a mechanistic insight grounded in RoPE theory.

**Why This Differs from Prior Work:** Prior length extrapolation research (Section 6) focused exclusively on auto-regressive architectures, where causal attention exposes models to relative positions $[0, T_{\text{train}} - 1]$. The paper reveals in Section 3 that diffusion LLMs experience symmetric relative positions $[1 - T_{\text{train}}, T_{\text{train}} - 1]$ during training due to bidirectional attention. As Figure 4 visualizes, this symmetric coverage means diffusion models observe complete sinusoidal periods for both cosine and sine components in moderate frequency dimensions, even with shorter training lengths (4k for LLaDA vs. 8k for LLaMA3).

**Significance:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in understanding positional encoding dynamics, not an incremental improvement. It explains why diffusion LLMs maintain "remarkably stable perplexity" during direct extrapolation (Abstract, Figure 1a) while auto-regressive models suffer "catastrophic perplexity surges." The t-SNE visualization in Figure 5 confirms this: LLaDA shows no distribution shift between QK states within and beyond trained context, whereas LLaMA3 exhibits two distinct clusters. This insight reframes how we think about positional robustness — it is not solely about training length, but about the *symmetry* of positional exposure during training.

**Implication for Future Research:** This suggests that bidirectional attention architectures may be inherently more suitable for long-context applications where positional robustness matters, even without explicit length extension methods. Future diffusion LLM designs could leverage this property rather than treating it as a side effect.

---

### 4.2 Local Perception Phenomenon: Sliding-Window Behavior Without Explicit Design

**What is Novel:** The paper identifies a distinct "local perception" phenomenon where diffusion LLMs retrieve information from recent context segments even when total context exceeds pretrained length, behaving like an implicit sliding window. This is documented in Section 2 with Needle-In-A-Haystack results (Figure 2).

**Why This Differs from Prior Work:** Auto-regressive models fail "outright" beyond their pretrained context — Figure 2b and 2d show LLaMA3 achieving 0% retrieval beyond 8k. In contrast, Figure 2a and 2c show LLaDA maintaining measurable retrieval accuracy at deeper positions (25% depth at 16k, ~10% at 24k). Prior work on auto-regressive length extrapolation (bloc97, 2023b; Liu et al., 2023b) documented either successful extension or catastrophic failure, but not this graded degradation pattern.

**Significance:** This is a **novel empirical discovery** specific to diffusion architectures. The phenomenon emerges without explicit architectural modifications — it is an emergent property of the interaction between bidirectional attention, RoPE, and the diffusion denoising process. Section 3 explains this through cosine function periodicity: since $\cos(-x) = \cos(x)$, negative relative positions do not extend maximum perceivable distance, limiting retrieval to positions within trained relative distance ranges. This creates a natural "attention horizon" that degrades gracefully rather than collapsing.

**Critical Distinction:** This is not the same as explicit sliding-window attention mechanisms (e.g., ReAttention from Section 6). Those require architectural changes; local perception emerges from the pretrained model's positional encoding dynamics. However, the paper notes in Section 4 that this also imposes limits: "diffusion LLM can only retrieve key information from limited relative positions within the training length, leading to the observed decay pattern in the NIAH evaluation."

**Implication for Future Research:** This phenomenon suggests diffusion LLMs may be better suited for tasks where recent context matters most (e.g., conversation, document summarization) rather than tasks requiring uniform access across entire contexts (e.g., aggregation). Designers should match architecture to task requirements rather than assuming uniform long-context capability.

---

### 4.3 Scaling Law Transferability Across Architectures

**What is Novel:** The paper validates that NTK-based RoPE extrapolation scaling laws, originally derived for auto-regressive models (Liu et al., 2023b), transfer seamlessly to diffusion architectures. LongLLaDA achieves 6× context expansion (24k tokens) without training using the same scaling factor formula (Section 4, Equation 1).

**Why This Differs from Prior Work:** Prior scaling law research assumed auto-regressive generation dynamics. The paper tests whether these laws hold when the generation mechanism changes fundamentally — from sequential next-token prediction to iterative denoising. Section 4 confirms: "for RoPE-based diffusion LLMs, NTK extrapolation and its scaling law remain applicable during inference."

**Significance:** This is an **incremental but important validation** rather than a fundamental innovation. It demonstrates theoretical continuity: RoPE dynamics are architecture-agnostic at the positional encoding level, even when generation mechanisms differ. The empirical results in Figure 6 and Figure 7 show predictable performance degradation as $\lambda$ increases: $\lambda = 4$ and $\lambda = 14$ achieve near 100% recall, $\lambda = 31$ shows "lost-in-the-middle" phenomenon, and $\lambda = 55$ becomes unachievable. This matches the 2× to 6× extension limit documented for auto-regressive models in Appendix A.

**Critical Caveat:** The paper notes in Section 4 that while scaling laws transfer, the absolute performance differs due to local perception. At $\lambda = 31$ (24k context), LLaDA-8B-Base shows retrieval depth proportional to the local perception window rather than uniform access. This means scaling laws predict *whether* extension is possible, but not *how* the model will use the extended context.

**Implication for Future Research:** Researchers can apply established auto-regressive length extension techniques to diffusion models with confidence, but should expect architecture-specific usage patterns. This reduces the barrier to entry for diffusion LLM long-context research.

---

### 4.4 Task-Dependent Capability Divergence Between Architectures

**What is Novel:** The paper reveals systematic task-dependent performance patterns where diffusion LLMs excel at some long-context tasks while lagging at others, compared to auto-regressive baselines. This is documented in Section 5 with LongBench (Table 1) and RULER (Table 2) results.

**Why This Differs from Prior Work:** Prior comparisons between diffusion and auto-regressive LLMs focused on general capabilities (reasoning, multimodality, efficiency from Section 6) without fine-grained long-context task analysis. This paper provides the first task-level breakdown showing:

| Task Category | Diffusion Performance | Auto-Regressive Performance | Gap |
|--------------|----------------------|----------------------------|-----|
| NIAH Retrieval | Comparable (99.7% vs 99.8%) | Comparable | Minimal |
| Aggregation | Inferior (65.2% vs 98.1%) | Superior | Large |
| Synthetic QA | Superior (82.5% vs 67.5%) | Inferior | Large |

**Significance:** This is a **fundamental insight** for model selection and deployment. The pattern suggests diffusion LLMs excel at tasks requiring holistic context understanding and iterative refinement (synthetic QA benefits from multiple denoising steps), but struggle with tasks requiring precise token-level tracking across sequences (aggregation requires exact counting/tracing). Section 5 notes: "only in the synthetic domain do LLaDA Series outperform LLaMA3 Series consistently" on LongBench.

**Mechanistic Explanation:** The paper connects this to the diffusion process: iterative denoising provides "multiple opportunities to refine answers but limited ability to maintain exact token-level tracking." This explains the QA advantage (refinement helps) and aggregation disadvantage (precision suffers).

**Implication for Future Research:** This challenges the assumption that long-context capability is monolithic. Future benchmarks should report task-level breakdowns rather than aggregate scores. Model developers should optimize diffusion LLMs for tasks matching their architectural strengths rather than chasing uniform performance across all long-context tasks.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the experimental methodology, quantitative results, and critical assessment of whether the evidence supports the paper's claims. We analyze three benchmark suites, multiple model variants, and systematic ablation studies to understand both the capabilities and limitations of diffusion LLMs in long-context scenarios.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Experimental Setup

**Benchmark Suites and Task Categories**

The paper employs three complementary benchmark suites, each designed to probe different aspects of long-context capability:

For the proposed LongLLaDA method, scaling factors λ are calculated using Equation 1 from Section 4:

$$
\lambda = 10^{-4} \cdot \left(\frac{t}{2\pi}\right)^{d/d_{\text{extra}}}
$$

With $d_{\text{extra}} = 64$ for LLaDA-8B, the specific scaling factors are:

| 16k tokens | λ = 14 |
| 24k tokens | λ = 31 |
| 32k tokens | λ = 55 |

**Claim 1: Diffusion LLMs Maintain Stable Perplexity During Direct Extrapolation**

**Support Level: Strong**

The mechanistic explanation in Section 3 (Figure 4, Figure 5) grounds this observation in RoPE theory — bidirectional attention provides symmetric positional coverage reducing OOD embedding space.

**Strengths:**
- Direct comparison with strong baseline (LLaMA3-8B)
- Mechanistic explanation via t-SNE visualization (Figure 5)
- Consistent across Base and Instruct variants

**Limitations:**
- Only two model families compared (LLaDA vs LLaMA3)
- Perplexity alone does not guarantee downstream task performance

**Support Level: Strong**

**Strengths:**

**Failure Case 2: λ = 55 (32k Context)**

- LLaDA-8B-Base: Overall Score 75.43 (down from 82.33 at λ = 14)
- LLaDA-8B-Instruct: Overall Score 61.69 (down from 67.97 at λ = 14)

Figure 6c shows "a lost-in-the-middle phenomenon similar to auto-regressive models emerges in intermediate depths" at λ = 31 (24k context).

**Failure Case 4: Dream-v0 Pretrained Context Unclear**

**Trade-off 1: Context Length vs. Retrieval Depth**

LongLLaDA enables longer contexts but with graded retrieval capability:
- λ = 4 (8k): Near 100% recall at all depths
- λ = 14 (16k): Near 100% recall at all depths
- λ = 31 (24k): Proportional retrieval depth with local perception
- λ = 55 (32k): Degraded performance, "unachievable"

Higher sampling steps improve long-context performance but increase inference time:
- s = 1-4: Fast inference, fails beyond 8k
- s = 8-16: Moderate inference, measurable retrieval to 24k

- **Choose Diffusion LLMs for:** QA tasks (82.5% vs 67.5%), Synthetic reasoning

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the boundaries of LongLLaDA and the broader findings about diffusion LLM long-context capabilities. We distinguish between limitations inherent to the approach, gaps in experimental coverage, and open questions that remain for future research. Each point is anchored to specific evidence from the paper.

---

### 6.1 Core Assumptions Underlying the Approach

**Assumption 1: RoPE-Based Positional Encoding**

LongLLaDA fundamentally assumes that the diffusion LLM uses Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE) for positional encoding. The paper states in Section 4 that the method transfers "NTK-based extrapolation (bloc97, 2023b) and its scaling laws (Liu et al., 2023b) to diffusion LLMs." This assumption limits applicability:

- **Models without RoPE:** Diffusion LLMs using alternative positional encodings (e.g., learned absolute positions, ALiBi, or NoPE) cannot benefit from LongLLaDA without modification
- **RoPE Variants:** The scaling factor formula in Equation 1 assumes standard RoPE with rotary base $\beta_0 = 500000$ for LLaDA. Models with different base values would require recalculating $d_{\text{extra}}$ and $\lambda$

**Assumption 2: Inference-Stage Scaling Sufficiency**

The paper explicitly focuses on training-free, inference-stage extrapolation. Section 4 states LongLLaDA extends context "without further training." This assumption carries important implications:

- **Extension Ceiling:** Appendix A notes that training-free interpolation "can only achieve 2× to 6× long-context extension during inference, as longer inputs lead to increased attention entropy." This is empirically confirmed: $\lambda = 31$ (24k) works, but $\lambda = 55$ (32k) becomes "unachievable" (Figure 6d, Figure 7d)
- **No Weight Adaptation:** The method assumes pretrained weights remain optimal for extended contexts. However, the Limitation section acknowledges: "We will carry out the fine-tuning extrapolation experiments in the future to verify more conclusions in RoPE-based scaling theory." This suggests training-based approaches may achieve greater extension

**Assumption 3: Bidirectional Attention Stability**

The mechanistic explanation in Section 3 assumes that bidirectional attention dynamics remain stable during extrapolation. Figure 5's t-SNE visualization shows "no distribution shift between QK states within and beyond 4k" for LLaDA. However:

- **Unverified at Extreme Lengths:** This stability is only validated up to 24k tokens with LongLLaDA. Behavior at 100k+ tokens remains unknown
- **Attention Entropy:** Appendix A notes that "longer inputs lead to increased attention entropy, limiting further extrapolation." This constraint applies to diffusion LLMs but is not deeply analyzed

**Assumption 4: Sampling Step Configuration**

The paper assumes a default sampling step configuration of $s = 32$ for most experiments (Section 2, Appendix B.1). However, Figure 3 shows performance varies significantly with $s$:

| Sampling Steps | Overall Score (NIAH) | Retrieval Beyond 8k |
|---------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| $s = 1$ | 45.59 | Fails |
| $s = 4$ | 47.15 | Fails |
| $s = 8$ | 52.75 | ~25% depth at 16k |
| $s = 16$ | 53.60 | ~25% depth at 16k |
| $s = 32$ (default) | 54.25 | Local perception to 24k |

The Limitation section explicitly acknowledges this gap: "We will also add more analyses focused on sampling strategy specialized in diffusion LLMs." This means the reported results represent one configuration point, not an optimized setting.

---

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

**Scenario 1: Extreme Context Lengths (100k+ Tokens)**

The paper tests contexts up to 32k tokens maximum, with successful extension only to 24k ($\lambda = 31$). Several important scenarios remain unexplored:

- **100k+ Contexts:** Modern applications increasingly demand 100k+ token windows (e.g., full codebases, book-length documents). LongLLaDA's 6× ceiling (24k from 4k base) does not address this scale
- **Million-Token Contexts:** Recent auto-regressive research targets million-token contexts (Ding et al., 2024). No equivalent exists for diffusion LLMs

**Scenario 2: Training-Based Length Extrapolation**

The paper explicitly defers training-based approaches to future work. The Limitation section states: "We will carry out the fine-tuning extrapolation experiments in the future." This leaves open questions:

- **Fine-Tuning Gains:** Would fine-tuning LLaDA on 16k or 32k sequences achieve better performance than inference-only scaling?
- **RoPE Base Amplification:** Auto-regressive models often combine NTK scaling with training on amplified rotary bases (Rozière et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2023). This approach is untested for diffusion LLMs
- **Positional Interpolation Training:** Linear Positional Interpolation (Chen et al., 2023) with fine-tuning may outperform NTK scaling for diffusion architectures

**Scenario 3: Dream-v0 Pretrained Context Ambiguity**

Appendix B.2 acknowledges a critical gap for Dream-v0 models: "Since Dream-v0 Series is trained not from scratch but initialized from an auto-regressive LLM, Qwen2.5-7B, it is hard to define the exact context length of pre-training." This creates several problems:

- **Baseline Uncertainty:** Without knowing Dream-v0's pretrained context length, direct comparison with LLaDA is compromised
- **Scaling Factor Calculation:** NTK scaling requires $T_{\text{train}}$ to compute $d_{\text{extra}}$ and $\lambda$. For Dream-v0, these values are "not specified" (Section 5.1 table)
- **Generalization Claims:** The paper claims findings generalize across diffusion architectures, but Dream-v0 results (Table 3, Table 4) cannot be directly compared to LLaDA due to this ambiguity

**Scenario 4: Multimodal Long-Context Tasks**

Section 6 notes significant concurrent work on multimodal diffusion LLMs (MMaDA, LLaDA-V, LaViDa). However, this paper focuses exclusively on text-only benchmarks:

- **Vision-Language Contexts:** Long documents with images, charts, or diagrams are not evaluated
- **Cross-Modal Attention:** Bidirectional attention dynamics may differ when processing visual tokens alongside text
- **Practical Applications:** Many real-world long-context applications (e.g., video understanding, document analysis) are multimodal

**Scenario 5: Streaming and Incremental Processing**

The paper evaluates static context windows (fixed-length inputs). Several practical scenarios are unaddressed:

- **Streaming Inputs:** Applications where context grows incrementally (e.g., live transcription, ongoing conversations) require different handling than fixed-length extrapolation
- **KV Cache Management:** Section 6 mentions efficiency optimizations like dKV-Cache and dLLM-Cache for diffusion LLMs, but LongLLaDA does not integrate with these methods
- **Context Eviction:** Strategies for managing contexts exceeding even the extended window (e.g., sliding window eviction, importance-based pruning) are not discussed

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

**Constraint 1: Sampling Step Overhead**

Diffusion LLMs require multiple sampling steps for generation, unlike auto-regressive models which generate one token per forward pass. This creates inherent computational trade-offs:

- **Inference Latency:** Default $s = 32$ means 32× more forward passes than a single auto-regressive generation step. Section 2 notes diffusion LLMs use "sampling steps and sampling strategies" that influence performance
- **Memory Footprint:** Bidirectional attention across extended contexts (24k tokens) requires storing full attention matrices, unlike auto-regressive models which can use KV caching more efficiently
- **FlashAttention Dependency:** Appendix B.1 states experiments are "accelerated with FlashAttention2 (Dao, 2023)." Without this optimization, computational costs would be prohibitive

**Constraint 2: Block Size Limitations**

The paper uses fixed block sizes for diffusion generation: 32 tokens for NIAH, 64 tokens for LongBench and RULER (Section 2, Section 5). This introduces constraints:

- **Output Length Coupling:** Section 5 notes "we still keep the sampling steps the same as the output length, and the block size to 64 for diffusion LLMs." This coupling limits flexibility
- **Long Output Generation:** Generating outputs longer than the block size requires multiple diffusion passes, compounding the sampling step overhead
- **Variable-Length Tasks:** Tasks requiring highly variable output lengths (e.g., summarization from 50 to 500 tokens) may not be optimally served by fixed block sizes

**Constraint 3: Scaling Factor Computation Overhead**

While LongLLaDA is training-free, computing the scaling factor $\lambda$ requires architectural knowledge:

$$
\lambda = 10^{-4} \cdot \left(\frac{t}{2\pi}\right)^{d/d_{\text{extra}}}, \quad d_{\text{extra}} = 2 \left\lceil \frac{d}{2} \log_{\beta_0} \frac{T_{\text{train}}}{2\pi} \right\rceil
$$

This requires knowing:
- Hidden dimension $d$ (model architecture detail)
- Rotary base $\beta_0$ (500,000 for LLaDA, but may vary)
- Pretrained context length $T_{\text{train}}$ (4k for LLaDA, unclear for Dream-v0)

For closed-source or poorly documented models, these values may be unavailable.

**Constraint 4: Batch Processing Limitations**

The paper does not report batch size or throughput metrics. However, diffusion models face unique batching challenges:

- **Variable Context Lengths:** Different samples may require different $\lambda$ values if contexts vary in length
- **Synchronized Sampling:** All samples in a batch must use the same number of sampling steps, limiting dynamic adaptation
- **Memory Scaling:** Bidirectional attention scales quadratically with context length. At 24k tokens, attention matrix size is 576M elements per head, compared to 64M at 8k tokens

### 6.4 Weaknesses in Task Coverage and Performance

**Weakness 1: Aggregation Task Deficiency**

Table 2 reveals a critical weakness: diffusion LLMs significantly underperform on aggregation tasks compared to auto-regressive models.

| Model | Aggregation Score (4k) | Gap |
|-------|----------------------|-----|
| LLaDA-8B-Base | 65.2% | -32.9 points |
| LLaMA3-8B-Base | 98.1% | — |

Section 5 explains this as requiring "tracking information across the entire context sequence rather than local retrieval." The iterative denoising process provides "limited ability to maintain exact token-level tracking." This weakness has practical implications:

- **Document Analysis:** Tasks requiring counting, frequency analysis, or variable tracing across long documents will favor auto-regressive models
- **Code Understanding:** Variable tracking in long codebases may be unreliable with diffusion LLMs
- **Data Extraction:** Structured information extraction from long texts may suffer from aggregation weaknesses

**Weakness 2: Local Perception Limits Uniform Access**

The "local perception" phenomenon (Section 2, Figure 2) means diffusion LLMs cannot uniformly access all positions in extended contexts:

- **Retrieval Depth Decay:** At 16k context, retrieval depth is ~25%; at 24k, it drops to ~10% (Figure 3)
- **Middle Information Loss:** Figure 6c shows "lost-in-the-middle phenomenon" at $\lambda = 31$ (24k), where intermediate positions are harder to access
- **Task Mismatch:** Applications requiring uniform access (e.g., legal document review where any clause may be relevant) are poorly served by local perception

**Weakness 3: Instruct Model Performance Degradation**

Comparing Base and Instruct variants reveals unexpected patterns:

| Model | NIAH Overall Score (4k) | NIAH Overall Score (24k with $\lambda = 31$) |
|-------|------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| LLaDA-8B-Base | 54.25 | 82.33 (Figure 6c) |
| LLaDA-8B-Instruct | 52.41 | 67.19 (Figure 7c) |

The Instruct model shows lower absolute performance despite instruction tuning. This suggests:

- **Instruction Tuning Trade-off:** Instruction tuning may optimize for helpfulness over retrieval precision
- **Generalization Gap:** Instruct models may be less robust to out-of-distribution positional embeddings

**Weakness 4: Limited Model Diversity**

The paper evaluates only three diffusion LLM families:

| Model | Parameters | Pretrained Context | Status |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|--------|
| LLaDA-8B | 8B | 4k | Primary focus |
| LLaDA-1.5 | 8B | 4k | Validated in Appendix B.2 |
| Dream-v0-7B | 7B | Unclear | Limited comparison only |

This limited diversity raises questions:

- **Scale Effects:** Do findings hold for larger models (70B+) or smaller models (1B-3B)?
- **Architecture Variants:** Do findings generalize to diffusion LLMs with different attention mechanisms or denoising objectives?
- **Training Data:** LLaDA and Dream-v0 may share training data biases. Independent model families would strengthen generalization claims

### 6.5 Open Questions for Future Research

**Question 1: Can Training-Based Extrapolation Surpass 6× Extension?**

The paper achieves 6× extension (4k → 24k) with inference-only scaling. Appendix A notes training-free methods are limited to "2× to 6× long-context extension." Open questions include:

- **Fine-Tuning Ceiling:** Would fine-tuning LLaDA on 32k or 64k sequences achieve 8× or 16× extension?
- **Curriculum Training:** Could progressive length extension during training (4k → 8k → 16k → 32k) achieve better results than direct scaling?
- **Data Efficiency:** How much long-context training data is required for diffusion LLMs compared to auto-regressive models?

**Question 2: How Do Sampling Strategies Interact with Long Context?**

The Limitation section explicitly identifies this gap: "We will also add more analyses focused on sampling strategy specialized in diffusion LLMs." Specific questions include:

- **Adaptive Sampling:** Can sampling steps be dynamically adjusted based on context length or task difficulty?
- **Guided Diffusion:** Do guidance techniques (classifier-free guidance, reward modeling) improve long-context retrieval?
- **Step Efficiency:** Can fewer sampling steps achieve comparable long-context performance with better inference strategies?

**Question 3: Why Do Diffusion LLMs Excel at QA but Fail at Aggregation?**

Table 2 shows a striking pattern: diffusion LLMs outperform auto-regressive models on QA (82.5% vs 67.5%) but underperform on aggregation (65.2% vs 98.1%). The paper offers a preliminary explanation but leaves deeper questions open:

- **Mechanistic Origin:** Is this due to the denoising objective, bidirectional attention, or training data differences?
- **Task Redesign:** Can aggregation tasks be reformulated to better match diffusion LLM strengths?
- **Hybrid Approaches:** Could combining diffusion and auto-regressive components leverage respective strengths?

**Question 4: Do Findings Generalize Beyond RoPE?**

All evaluated models use RoPE. Open questions include:

- **Alternative Encodings:** How do diffusion LLMs with ALiBi, learned positions, or NoPE handle long contexts?
- **Hybrid Encodings:** Could combining RoPE with other encodings improve extrapolation?
- **Position-Free Models:** Recent work explores position-free transformers (Wang et al., 2024). Do diffusion LLMs benefit from similar approaches?

**Question 5: What Is the True Context Capacity of Diffusion LLMs?**

The paper demonstrates stable perplexity beyond pretrained length but does not establish a hard capacity limit:

- **Perplexity vs. Performance:** Figure 1 shows stable perplexity, but Table 2 shows task performance degrades. What is the relationship?
- **Task-Specific Limits:** Do different tasks have different effective context limits for diffusion LLMs?
- **Measurement Methods:** Are current benchmarks (NIAH, LongBench, RULER) sufficient to measure true context capacity?

### 6.7 Final Assessment

The limitations identified in this paper are appropriate for foundational research. The authors explicitly acknowledge key gaps in the Limitation section:

> "Although we have conducted extensive experiments on diffusion LLMs, our results mainly focus on LLaDA Series and the inference stage. We will carry out the fine-tuning extrapolation experiments in the future to verify more conclusions in RoPE-based scaling theory for auto-regressive LLMs. Besides, we will also add more analyses focused on sampling strategy specialized in diffusion LLMs."

This honest scoping strengthens the paper's credibility. The work opens a research direction rather than claiming comprehensive solutions. Key limitations to remember when applying these findings:

1. **6× Extension Ceiling:** LongLLaDA achieves 24k from 4k base, not unlimited extension
2. **Local Perception:** Extended contexts do not provide uniform access—recent segments are prioritized
3. **Task Dependency:** Diffusion LLMs excel at QA but struggle with aggregation—match architecture to task
4. **Inference-Only:** Training-based approaches may achieve greater extension but are untested
5. **Model Coverage:** Findings validated on LLaDA Series; generalization to other diffusion LLMs needs verification

These limitations do not diminish the paper's contributions but define the boundaries within which LongLLaDA and the associated insights should be applied.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section synthesizes how LongLLaDA reshapes the research landscape for diffusion LLMs, identifies concrete follow-up research directions, and provides actionable guidance for practitioners deciding when to deploy diffusion versus auto-regressive architectures for long-context applications.

### 7.1 Landscape Impact: Reframing Diffusion LLM Research Priorities

**From Capability Validation to Capability Characterization**

Prior to this work, diffusion LLM research focused primarily on validating that these models could match auto-regressive baselines on standard benchmarks (Section 6 cites work on multimodality, reasoning, and efficiency). This paper shifts the research paradigm from "can diffusion LLMs work?" to "**how do diffusion LLMs work differently?**" The discovery of stable perplexity during direct extrapolation (Figure 1a) and local perception phenomena (Figure 2) reveals that diffusion architectures are not merely alternative training objectives—they exhibit fundamentally distinct long-context behaviors that require their own theoretical framework.

**Three Paradigm Shifts:**

| Prior Assumption | This Paper's Finding | Research Implication |
|-----------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Length extrapolation methods are architecture-agnostic | NTK scaling transfers, but **usage patterns differ** (local perception vs. uniform access) | Need architecture-specific extrapolation theory |
| Long-context capability is monolithic | **Task-dependent divergence**: QA ↑, Aggregation ↓, Retrieval ≈ (Table 2) | Benchmarks must report task-level breakdowns |
| Bidirectional attention is purely beneficial | Bidirectional attention provides **extrapolation buffer** but limits monotonic position tracking (Section 3) | Architecture design must balance robustness vs. precision |

**Opening a New Research Subfield**

The paper establishes long-context diffusion LLMs as a distinct research subfield. Before this work, the Related Work section (Section 6) notes "there is still no discussion on the long-context capability of diffusion LLMs." This paper provides:

- **Foundational benchmarks**: NIAH heatmaps (Figure 2, Figure 6-8), LongBench (Table 1), RULER (Table 2) with diffusion-specific configurations
- **Theoretical grounding**: RoPE dynamics under bidirectional attention (Section 3, Figure 4, Figure 5)
- **Baseline methods**: LongLLaDA as a training-free starting point (Section 4)

This infrastructure enables systematic comparison of future diffusion LLM variants, similar to how early auto-regressive length extrapolation work (bloc97, 2023b; Chen et al., 2023) established standards for that field.

**Challenging the "One Architecture Fits All" Assumption**

Table 2's task-dependent findings (NIAH: 99.7% vs 99.8%, AGG: 65.2% vs 98.1%, QA: 82.5% vs 67.5%) directly challenge the assumption that long-context capability is architecture-independent. This forces the field to acknowledge that:

> "Diffusion LLMs match auto-regressive models on retrieval tasks, lag in aggregation, but excel at synthetic QA" (Abstract)

Future model development should optimize for **task-architecture fit** rather than chasing uniform performance across all benchmarks. This mirrors the computer vision field's recognition that different architectures (CNNs vs. Vision Transformers) excel at different visual tasks.

### 7.2 Research Roadmap: Enabled Follow-Up Work

This paper opens at least five concrete research directions, each with specific open questions and methodological requirements.

**Direction 1: Training-Based Length Extrapolation for Diffusion LLMs**

The Limitation section explicitly states: "We will carry out the fine-tuning extrapolation experiments in the future." This is the most immediate follow-up, with several sub-questions:

| Research Question | Methodology | Expected Outcome |
|------------------|-------------|------------------|
| Can fine-tuning surpass 6× extension ceiling? | Fine-tune LLaDA on 32k-64k sequences with amplified RoPE base | Potentially 8×-16× extension (32k-64k from 4k base) |
| Does curriculum training help? | Progressive length extension: 4k → 8k → 16k → 32k | Better convergence than direct long-context training |
| What data efficiency is required? | Compare tokens needed for diffusion vs. auto-regressive length extension | Quantify computational trade-offs |

**Why This Matters:** Appendix A notes training-free methods are limited to "2× to 6× long-context extension during inference, as longer inputs lead to increased attention entropy." Training-based approaches could突破 this ceiling, but require empirical validation specific to diffusion dynamics.

**Direction 2: Sampling Strategy Optimization for Long Context**

The Limitation section identifies this gap: "We will also add more analyses focused on sampling strategy specialized in diffusion LLMs." Figure 3 shows sampling steps significantly impact performance (s=1: 45.59 score vs s=16: 53.60 score), but systematic optimization is unexplored.

**Specific Research Questions:**

- **Adaptive Sampling:** Can sampling steps be dynamically adjusted based on context length? For example, use s=8 for 8k context but s=32 for 24k context
- **Guided Diffusion:** Do classifier-free guidance or reward modeling techniques improve long-context retrieval accuracy?
- **Step Efficiency:** Can distillation or consistency models reduce sampling steps while preserving long-context performance?
- **Block Size Optimization:** The paper uses fixed block sizes (32 for NIAH, 64 for LongBench/RULER). What is the optimal block size for different context lengths and task types?

**Methodological Requirement:** Future work should report sampling step ablations as standard practice, similar to how auto-regressive papers report temperature and top-p settings.

**Direction 3: Architecture Modifications for Aggregation Tasks**

Table 2 reveals a critical weakness: diffusion LLMs score 65.2% on aggregation tasks versus 98.1% for auto-regressive models (32.9 point gap). Section 5 explains this as "limited ability to maintain exact token-level tracking" due to iterative denoising.

**Potential Architecture Modifications:**

| Modification | Hypothesis | Validation Method |
|-------------|------------|-------------------|
| Auxiliary counting heads | Add dedicated layers for aggregation tasks | Compare AGG scores on RULER with/without heads |
| Memory-augmented attention | Integrate external memory for token tracking | Measure variable tracing accuracy |
| Hybrid diffusion/auto-regressive decoding | Use diffusion for QA, auto-regressive for aggregation | Task-routing benchmark |
| Position encoding variants | Test ALiBi, NoPE, or hybrid RoPE alternatives | Compare extrapolation stability |

**Why This Matters:** If aggregation weaknesses can be addressed while preserving QA advantages, diffusion LLMs could become competitive across all long-context task types rather than niche specialists.

**Direction 4: Multimodal Long-Context Extension**

Section 6 notes concurrent work on multimodal diffusion LLMs (MMaDA, LLaDA-V, LaViDa), but this paper evaluates only text-only benchmarks. This creates a significant gap:

**Open Questions:**

- Do bidirectional attention dynamics change when processing visual tokens alongside text?
- Can LongLLaDA's NTK scaling apply to video sequences (temporal + spatial dimensions)?
- What is the effective context length for document understanding with images, charts, and diagrams?

**Recommended Benchmark Extensions:**

- **LongDocVQA:** Question answering on book-length documents with figures
- **Video-NIAH:** Retrieve information from specific frames in hour-long videos
- **Codebase-NIAH:** Navigate multi-file code repositories with dependency graphs

**Direction 5: Theoretical Analysis of Diffusion Position Encoding**

Section 3 provides mechanistic explanation via RoPE theory, but deeper theoretical work is needed:

**Theoretical Open Problems:**

1. **Formal Bounds on Local Perception:** What is the mathematical relationship between training length $T_{\text{train}}$, scaling factor $\lambda$, and retrievable depth?
2. **Attention Entropy Analysis:** Appendix A mentions "increased attention entropy" limits extension. Quantify this for diffusion vs. auto-regressive models.
3. **QK State Manifold Theory:** Figure 5's t-SNE visualization shows uniform manifolds for diffusion LLMs. Can this be formalized as a theoretical guarantee?
4. **Scaling Law Generalization:** Does the power-law relationship $\lambda \propto t^{d/d_{\text{extra}}}$ hold for all RoPE-based diffusion LLMs, or only LLaDA variants?

**Methodological Recommendation:** Future theoretical work should produce testable predictions (like Equation 1's scaling factor formula) rather than purely descriptive analysis.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

**Implementation Checklist for LongLLaDA**

Practitioners implementing LongLLaDA should verify the following prerequisites:

- [ ] **RoPE-based model:** Confirm the diffusion LLM uses Rotary Position Embedding (LLaDA uses $\beta_0 = 500000$)
- [ ] **Known pretrained context length:** $T_{\text{train}}$ required for $d_{\text{extra}}$ calculation (4k for LLaDA)
- [ ] **Hidden dimension accessible:** $d$ needed for scaling factor formula (Section 4, Equation 1)
- [ ] **FlashAttention2 enabled:** Appendix B.1 states experiments "accelerated with FlashAttention2"
- [ ] **Sampling steps configured:** Default $s=32$ for optimal long-context performance (Figure 3)
- [ ] **Block size set:** 32 tokens for retrieval tasks, 64 tokens for LongBench/RULER (Section 5)

**Scaling Factor Calculation**

For a target context length $t$, compute $\lambda$ using Equation 1 from Section 4:

$$
\lambda = 10^{-4} \cdot \left(\frac{t}{2\pi}\right)^{d/d_{\text{extra}}}, \quad d_{\text{extra}} = 2 \left\lceil \frac{d}{2} \log_{\beta_0} \frac{T_{\text{train}}}{2\pi} \right\rceil
$$

For LLaDA-8B with $d_{\text{extra}} = 64$:

| Target Context | Scaling Factor $\lambda$ | Expected Performance |
|---------------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| 8k tokens | $\lambda = 4$ | Near 100% recall (Figure 6a) |
| 16k tokens | $\lambda = 14$ | Near 100% recall (Figure 6b) |
| 24k tokens | $\lambda = 31$ | Lost-in-the-middle emerges (Figure 6c) |
| 32k tokens | $\lambda = 55$ | Degraded, "unachievable" (Figure 6d) |

**When to Prefer LongLLaDA Over Alternatives:**

| Scenario | LongLLaDA | Alternative | Rationale |
|----------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Immediate deployment needed** | ✓ | Training-based fine-tuning | LongLLaDA is training-free (Section 4) |
| **Context ≤ 24k tokens** | ✓ | Sliding window attention | LongLLaDA achieves 6× extension without architectural changes |
| **Aggregation tasks critical** | ✗ | Auto-regressive with NTK | Table 2 shows 32.9 point gap on AGG tasks |
| **Context > 32k tokens** | ✗ | Training-based extension | LongLLaDA hits 6× ceiling (Appendix A) |
| **Low-latency inference required** | ✗ | Auto-regressive | Diffusion requires multiple sampling steps (Section 2) |
| **QA or synthetic reasoning focus** | ✓ | Either | Table 2 shows diffusion QA advantage (82.5% vs 67.5%) |

**Known Integration Pitfalls:**

1. **Incorrect $T_{\text{train}}$ assumption:** Dream-v0's pretrained context is "hard to define" (Appendix B.2) because it was initialized from Qwen2.5-7B. Verify pretrained context before computing $\lambda$.

2. **Sampling step mismatch:** Using $s &lt; 8$ significantly degrades long-context performance (Figure 3 shows s=1: 45.59 vs s=16: 53.60). Do not optimize for latency at the expense of retrieval capability.

3. **Block size coupling:** Section 5 notes "we still keep the sampling steps the same as the output length, and the block size to 64." Mismatched block sizes may cause generation failures.

4. **Precision requirements:** Appendix B.1 uses FP16 for LLaDA Series and BF16 for Dream Series. Incorrect precision may cause numerical instability with scaled RoPE.

5. **Lost-in-the-middle at λ=31:** Figure 6c shows retrieval degradation at intermediate depths for 24k context. For critical applications, prefer $\lambda = 14$ (16k) over $\lambda = 31$ (24k) if uniform access is required.

**Benchmarking Recommendations:**

When evaluating new diffusion LLMs for long-context capability, practitioners should:

1. **Run NIAH at multiple depths:** Not just overall accuracy—report depth-percent heatmaps (Figure 2 format)
2. **Test beyond pretrained length:** Evaluate at 2×, 4×, 6× $T_{\text{train}}$ to characterize local perception
3. **Include aggregation tasks:** RULER's AGG category (Table 2) reveals critical weaknesses
4. **Ablate sampling steps:** Report performance at s=1, 4, 8, 16, 32 to establish inference configuration sensitivity
5. **Compare with auto-regressive baseline:** Use LLaMA3 or equivalent for task-level comparison (Table 2 format)
