## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces AlphaGeometry2 (AG2), a neuro-symbolic system that achieves an 84% solve rate on International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO) geometry problems from 2000-2024, surpassing the average gold medalist standard of 40.9 problems by solving 42 out of 50 in the IMO-AG-50 benchmark compared to AlphaGeometry1's 54%. AG2 accomplishes this through an expanded domain language covering 88% of problem types, a Gemini-based language model, and a novel Shared Knowledge Ensemble of Search Trees (SKEST) algorithm. This advancement represents a significant leap in AI reasoning capabilities, establishing a new state-of-the-art in automated theorem proving for complex Euclidean geometry.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Challenge: AI Reasoning in Complex Mathematics

This paper addresses a fundamental gap in artificial intelligence: the ability to perform **complex mathematical reasoning** at the level of human experts. Specifically, AlphaGeometry2 targets **Olympiad-level Euclidean geometry problems** — the kind of problems given to the world's brightest high-school mathematicians at the International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO). These are not routine textbook exercises; they require creative insights, non-obvious auxiliary constructions, and chains of logical deduction that can span dozens of steps.

The significance of this problem extends beyond geometry itself. As the authors state, advancing reasoning capabilities in complex mathematical problems has been a "**decade-long holy grail of AI research**" (Section 1). Geometry serves as an ideal testbed because it demands:

- **Fundamental understanding of geometric objects** (points, lines, circles) and their interactions
- **Creative problem-solving** (suggesting auxiliary constructions not present in the original problem)
- **Rigorous logical deduction** (every step must be formally verifiable)
- **Long-horizon reasoning** (solutions may require 10+ construction and deduction steps)

Success in this domain signals progress toward AI systems that can reason reliably in other complex, structured domains — from formal verification of software to scientific discovery.

### Why Geometry? Theoretical and Practical Significance

Geometry occupies a unique position in AI research for several reasons:

**Theoretical significance:** Euclidean geometry represents a well-defined formal system with clear rules, yet solving Olympiad problems requires intuition that transcends brute-force application of those rules. This makes it an ideal benchmark for **neuro-symbolic AI** — systems that combine neural networks (for intuition and pattern recognition) with symbolic reasoning (for rigorous deduction).

**Practical significance:** The reasoning patterns developed for geometry transfer to other domains. The paper notes that AG1's synthetic approach was chosen specifically because it is "suitable for transferring the research knowledge to other domains" (Section 1). Skills like constructing intermediate lemmas, managing state across long reasoning chains, and verifying each step formally are directly applicable to theorem proving, program synthesis, and scientific reasoning.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper identifies **two main categories** of automated geometry solvers that existed before AlphaGeometry (Section 1):

| Approach | Methods | Key Limitation |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| **Algebraic "Bashing"** | Wu's method (Chou, 1985), Area method (Chou et al., 1993), Gröbner bases (Kapur, 1986) | Converts geometry to algebra; solutions are computationally valid but lack human-readable insight; struggles with problems requiring creative constructions |
| **Synthetic Techniques** | Deduction database (Chou et al., 2000), Full angle method (Chou et al., 1996) | More human-like reasoning; but limited by fixed rule sets and inability to suggest novel auxiliary constructions |

**AlphaGeometry1 (AG1)** represented a breakthrough by combining these approaches into a **neuro-symbolic system** (Trinh et al., 2024):
- A **neural language model** suggests creative auxiliary constructions (the "insight" component)
- A **symbolic engine (DDAR)** performs rigorous deduction from those constructions (the "verification" component)

However, AG1 had three critical limitations that AlphaGeometry2 addresses:

1. **Limited domain language coverage:** AG1's language could only express 66% of IMO 2000-2024 geometry problems. It could not handle:
   - Linear equations of angles, ratios, and distances
   - Locus problems (involving movement of objects)
   - Non-constructive problems (where points are defined by 3+ constraints simultaneously)
   - "Find X" type computational questions (Section 2)

2. **Inefficient symbolic engine:** The original DDAR algorithm had worst-case time complexity of $O(N^8)$ for certain operations like finding similar triangles. Average computation time was 1179.57 seconds on unsolvable problems (Section 3.3).

3. **Limited language model capacity:** AG1 used a custom transformer trained on 100M synthetic examples. The model capacity and training data scale constrained its ability to suggest effective auxiliary constructions for harder problems.

**Result:** AG1 achieved only a **54% solve rate** on IMO 2000-2024 geometry problems (Section 1), below the average gold medalist standard.

### The Remaining Gap

Despite AG2's 84% solve rate, **12% of IMO geometry problems remain uncovered** (Section 2). These involve:
- 3D geometry
- Inequalities
- Non-linear equations
- Problems with variable numbers of points (e.g., "$n$ points where $n$ is an arbitrary positive integer")

Appendix H details the inequality rules that would need to be incorporated to handle this remaining 12%. The paper explicitly acknowledges that "extending AlphaGeometry to encompass these topics is a substantial undertaking that falls beyond the scope of this work" (Section 8).

This honest assessment positions AG2 not as a final solution, but as a significant milestone toward fully automated Euclidean geometry solving — demonstrating that neuro-symbolic systems can now match and exceed human expert performance on the vast majority of 2D geometry problems while clearly delineating the frontier of remaining challenges.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a **neuro-symbolic AI system design paper** that demonstrates how combining neural language models with formal symbolic reasoning engines can solve complex mathematical problems at expert-human level. The core idea is to use a language model to suggest creative "auxiliary constructions" (additional geometric points or lines not given in the original problem), then use a deterministic symbolic engine to verify whether those constructions lead to a valid proof.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

AlphaGeometry2 is a two-component system where a Gemini-based language model acts as an "intuition engine" suggesting geometric constructions, and a C++ symbolic engine acts as a "verification engine" that rigorously checks whether those constructions prove the theorem. The system solves Olympiad geometry problems by iteratively proposing auxiliary points through beam search across multiple parallel search trees that share discovered facts, achieving an 84% solve rate on 25 years of IMO geometry problems through expanded language coverage (66% to 88%), 300x faster symbolic deduction, and an order-of-magnitude larger training dataset.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The AlphaGeometry2 system consists of **five major components** that interact in a specific pipeline:

1. **Problem Formalization Module** (Section G): Takes natural language problem statements (e.g., "Given triangle ABC with incenter I...") and translates them into the AlphaGeometry domain-specific language using Gemini with few-shot prompting. Output is a formal problem specification with predicates like `triangle a b c`, `incenter i a b c`.

2. **Domain Language Layer** (Section 2): Defines the vocabulary of geometric predicates (now 20+ predicates vs. 9 in AG1) that can express angles, distances, ratios, collinearity, cyclicity, and locus relationships. This layer determines which problems can be formalized at all (88% coverage vs. 66% previously).

3. **Synthetic Data Generator** (Section 4): Creates 300 million training examples by sampling random diagrams, running the symbolic engine to deduce all provable facts, then using traceback to extract minimal proofs. Uses greedy pruning (Figure 3) instead of exponential search to find minimal premise sets.

4. **Language Model** (Section 6): A Gemini-based sparse mixture-of-experts Transformer (51M to 3.3B parameters) trained on the synthetic data. During inference, it receives an "analysis string" containing deduced facts and outputs auxiliary construction suggestions in the domain language.

5. **Search Algorithm - SKEST** (Section 5): Shared Knowledge Ensemble of Search Trees runs multiple beam searches in parallel (different configurations: classic, multi-auxiliary, uniform type distribution, deep/narrow, shallow/wide). Search trees write proven facts to a shared database, enabling cross-tree knowledge transfer.

6. **Symbolic Engine - DDAR2** (Section 3): Deductive Database Arithmetic Reasoning engine that computes deduction closure from given premises. Implements optimized algorithms for similar triangle detection (hashing triples of points) and cyclic quadrilateral detection (hashing point-segment-angle tuples). C++ implementation achieves 300x speedup (1179.57s → 3.45s average).

**Information flow during inference:**
1. Natural language problem → Formalization module → Domain language specification
2. Domain language → DDAR2 computes initial fact set S1
3. S1 + goal assumption → DDAR2 computes S2
4. S1, S2, S3 (numerical facts) → Serialized into analysis string
5. Analysis string → Language Model → Auxiliary construction proposals
6. Proposals → DDAR2 → If proof succeeds, terminate; if fails, add facts to shared database
7. Shared facts → All search trees continue with enriched knowledge
8. Repeat until proof found or search budget exhausted

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **Domain language expansion first** because it determines which problems the system can even attempt (88% coverage is a hard constraint on all downstream components)
- **Symbolic engine (DDAR2) second** because it drives both training data generation and test-time verification, and its 300x speedup enables more aggressive search
- **Synthetic training data third** because the language model's performance depends entirely on the quality and distribution of the 300M generated theorems
- **Search algorithm (SKEST) fourth** because it orchestrates how multiple language models explore the construction space during inference
- **Language model architecture fifth** because it sits at the center of the neuro-symbolic interface, receiving analysis strings and outputting constructions
- **Inference configuration last** because hyperparameters like temperature $t=1.0$ and $k=32$ are critical for diversity but only make sense after understanding the search process

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Domain Language Expansion: From 66% to 88% Coverage

The original AlphaGeometry language contained **nine basic predicates** listed in Table 1 (Section 2), including `cong a b c d` (segment AB equals segment CD), `perp a b c d` (AB perpendicular to CD), `coll a b c` (points A, B, C are collinear), `cyclic a b c d` (four points lie on a circle), and `eqangle a b c d e f g h` (directed angle between AB and CD equals angle between EF and GH). AlphaGeometry2 adds **eleven new predicates** to handle previously unsupported problem types, which directly increases formalizable problem coverage from 66% to 88% on IMO 2000-2024 geometry problems.

Two predicates enable computational "Find X" questions: `acompute a b c d` means "find the angle between AB and CD" and `rcompute a b c d` means "find the ratio AB/CD", addressing problems like IMO 2009 P4 that ask for specific numerical values rather than proofs of relationships. Three predicates capture linear equations of geometric quantities: `distmeq a1 b1 ... an bn t1 t2 ... tn y` expresses $t_1 \log(A_1B_1) + t_2 \log(A_2B_2) + ... + t_n \log(A_nB_n) + y = 0$, `distseq a1 b1 ... an bn t1 t2 ... tn` expresses $t_1 A_1B_1 + t_2 A_2B_2 + ... + t_n A_nB_n = 0$, and `angeq a1 b1 ... an bn t1 t2 ... tn y` expresses $t_1 d(A_1B_1) + t_2 d(A_2B_2) + ... + t_n d(A_nB_n) + y = 0$ where $d(AB)$ is the angle between line AB and the horizontal.

Locus problems, which describe how points move along paths, are captured through **11 locus case types** shown in Table 2 (Section 2), using a special token `*` as a fixed-point placeholder. For example, the syntax `? cyclic a b c * : X` asks whether point X moves on the circumcircle of triangle ABC as other points vary, and `? cong b c a * : X` asks whether X maintains constant distance from point A. The system also adds explicit predicates for diagram topology checks: `sameclock a b c d e f` verifies that triangle ABC has the same clockwise orientation as triangle DEF, `noverlap a b` ensures points A and B are distinct, `lessthan a b c d` encodes AB < CD for SSA triangle congruence conditions, and `overlap a b` allows the system to prove two named points are actually the same location.

A critical limitation in AG1 was that each point could be defined by **at most two predicates**, meaning every point had to be constructible as the intersection of exactly two objects (two lines, or a line and a circle). AG2 relaxes this constraint to allow points defined by **three or more predicates simultaneously**, enabling non-constructive problem statements where a point's existence is guaranteed by multiple constraints but cannot be built sequentially. For example, a problem stating "Let I be the incenter of triangle ABC such that IA = 2IB" defines point I by three constraints (angle bisector from A, angle bisector from B, and distance ratio), which AG1 could not formalize but AG2 handles through the `cyclic_with_center` predicate and overlap detection during deduction closure.

#### Symbolic Engine DDAR2: 300x Speedup Through Algorithmic and Implementation Optimization

The symbolic engine, called **DDAR (Deductive Database Arithmetic Reasoning)**, computes the deduction closure—the complete set of all facts that can be logically derived from a given set of initial premises using fixed deduction rules. DDAR drives both training data generation (deducing all facts from random diagrams) and test-time proof search (verifying whether proposed auxiliary constructions lead to the goal). Speed is critical because faster data generation allows larger datasets with more aggressive filtering, and faster proof search enables deeper exploration within fixed time budgets.

DDAR2 introduces three major improvements over DDAR1, detailed in Section 3. The first is **handling double points**, illustrated in Figure 1 (Section 3.1). Consider a problem where we must prove that the intersection point X of lines a and b lies on circle ω. DDAR1 could not accept two points with different names but identical coordinates, making this proof difficult. DDAR2 uses a four-step reformulation: (1) construct a new point X' as the intersection of line a and circle ω (this auxiliary construction is suggested by the language model), (2) prove that X' lies on line b, (3) since both X and X' lie on both a and b, conclude X = X', and (4) therefore X lies on ω. This technique allows the system to move angles around circles more flexibly, which is essential for hard problems requiring angle chasing.

The second improvement is a **faster algorithm** for the two most time-consuming operations: finding similar triangles and finding cyclic quadrilaterals (Section 3.2). In AG1, searching for similar triangle candidates had worst-case time complexity $O(N^8)$ where N is the number of points, because the algorithm processed all rule combinations exhaustively. DDAR2 replaces this with a hashing approach: for similar triangles, the system iterates through all triples of points, computes a symbolic "shape" hash for each triple, and detects similar pairs when the same hash appears twice. For cyclic quadrilaterals, the system iterates through all pairs of (point X, segment AB), hashes the value $(A, B, \angle AXB)$, and identifies cyclic quadrilaterals when such triples repeat. The "value" of an angle or segment is a symbolic normal form computed by an arithmetic reasoning submodule that tracks linear equations between angles, distances, and log-distances, reducing any linear expression to canonical form.

The third improvement is a **faster implementation** through C++ optimization (Section 3.3). The core computation in DDAR is Gaussian Elimination for solving systems of linear equations between geometric quantities. AG2 reimplements this core in C++ and exports it to Python via pybind11 (Jakob et al., 2017), achieving over 300x speedup compared to the pure Python DDAR1. The paper benchmarks this on 25 IMO problems that DDAR1 could not solve, running each test 50 times on an AMD EPYC 7B13 64-core CPU. DDAR1 averaged $1179.57 \pm 8.055$ seconds per problem, while DDAR2 averages $3.44711 \pm 0.05476$ seconds—a reduction from nearly 20 minutes to under 4 seconds per problem. This speedup is not merely convenient; it fundamentally changes what search strategies are computationally feasible during inference.

#### Synthetic Training Data: 300 Million Theorems from Random Diagrams

AlphaGeometry2 generates **approximately 300 million synthetic theorems** for training the language model, an order of magnitude larger than AG1's 100 million examples (Section 4). The data generation procedure follows the same philosophy as AG1: start from random diagrams rather than human-crafted problems, use the symbolic engine to deduce all provable facts, then apply traceback to extract minimal proofs for each fact. This design choice "eliminates the risk of data contamination and allows for the exploration of theorem distributions that may extend beyond established human knowledge" (Section 4), contrasting with systems like TongGeometry (Zhang et al., 2024) that rely on existing problem diagrams.

AG2 scales up data generation in five specific ways shown in Figure 2 (Section 4). First, the system explores random diagrams at **twice the size** (more points and predicates per diagram), enabling extraction of more complex problems. Second, generated theorems are up to **2x more complex** in terms of number of points and premises. Third, proofs are up to **10x more complex** in terms of proof steps, allowing the language model to learn longer reasoning chains. Fourth, the data distribution is more balanced across question types (collinear, equal distance, concyclic, parallel, perpendicular, equal ratio, equal angles, evaluate value), preventing the model from overfitting to common patterns. Fifth, and most critically, the mix of problems with and without auxiliary points shifts from 9:91 in AG1 to **50:50 in AG2**, ensuring the model learns when constructions are necessary rather than defaulting to direct deduction.

AG2 introduces **locus-type theorem generation** not supported in AG1 (Section 4). During random diagram generation, the system records movement dependency for each point X through a function $P(.)$ where $P(A)$ is "the set of points that control the movements of A." Table 3 (Section 4) provides examples: if $a = \text{midpoint}(b, c)$ and $d = \text{midpoint}(a, c)$, then $P(d) = \{b, c\}$ because d's position is determined by b and c. If $a = \text{on\_line}(b, c)$, then $P(a) = \{a, b, c\}$ because a can move anywhere on line bc. Table 5 (Appendix) lists 17 cases where locus-type statements are detected, producing 11 different locus statement types such as "when X moves on line Y, then Z moves on a fixed circle T."

The data generation algorithm itself is optimized through **greedy pruning** instead of exhaustive search (Section 4, Figure 3). In AG1, finding the minimal set of points needed to prove a fact required exhaustively removing different subsets and rerunning DDAR—an exponential search infeasible for large diagrams. AG2 uses a linear-time greedy algorithm that processes points in reverse-topological order (points that depend on others first, initial construction points last) and removes each point if the remaining set still proves the goal. The algorithm is guaranteed to find a minimal set with respect to inclusion as long as the provability check is monotonic (if $A \subseteq B$, then $\text{check\_provable}(A) \Rightarrow \text{check\_provable}(B)$). The paper notes a subtlety: requiring the pruned set to remain closed under construction dependencies breaks monotonicity, but processing in reverse-topological order fixes this by ensuring dependencies are respected.

#### Search Algorithm SKEST: Parallel Search Trees with Shared Knowledge

AlphaGeometry2 introduces a novel search algorithm called **SKEST (Shared Knowledge Ensemble of Search Trees)** described in Section 5 and illustrated in Figure 4. Unlike AG1's simple beam search where a single tree explores auxiliary constructions sequentially, SKEST runs **multiple differently configured beam searches in parallel**, with search trees sharing discovered facts through a common database. This design addresses two challenges: (1) different problems require different search strategies (some need deep exploration, others need breadth), and (2) facts proven in one search branch may be useful in other branches, avoiding redundant computation.

Each node in a search tree corresponds to one attempt at auxiliary construction followed by one DDAR run. If DDAR succeeds in proving the goal, all search trees terminate immediately. If the attempt fails, the node writes proven facts (not auxiliary-point-specific facts, only facts relevant to the original problem) into a shared facts database. These shared facts become available to all other nodes in the same tree and nodes in different trees, effectively allowing search branches to "learn from each other's partial progress."

SKEST employs **five types of search trees** to explore different parts of the search space (Section 5):

1. **Classic search tree**: Identical to AG1's beam search, where the language model produces one auxiliary point at each node. This provides a baseline strategy that works well for problems requiring sequential construction.

2. **Multi-auxiliary tree**: The language model can produce multiple auxiliary points at each node, accelerating solution finding for problems requiring several constructions. The paper notes that "even though we want our models to generate all necessary auxiliary points in one query, in practice, we observe the need to call the model multiple times given previously produced auxiliary points."

3. **Uniform type distribution tree**: Instead of prompting the language model with just `x00` (the start token for auxiliary constructions), the system prompts with `x00 a : cong`, `x00 a : coll`, `x00 a : cyclic`, `x00 a : perp`, etc., forcing uniform distribution across construction types. This prevents the model from overusing familiar patterns and encourages exploration of diverse geometric relationships.

4. **Deep but narrow tree**: Configuration with beam size 64 and depth 10, suitable for problems requiring long chains of dependent constructions.

5. **Shallow but wide tree**: Configuration with beam size 512 and depth 4, suitable for problems where the key insight is among many possible first-step constructions.

The system design uses **TPUv4 hardware** to serve multiple model replicas, with different search trees within the same model querying the same server under their own search strategy (Section 5). LM workers and DDAR workers run asynchronously: LM workers write explored node content to a database, and DDAR workers asynchronously pick up nodes and attempt them. A single DDAR worker pool is shared across different problems, so problems solved earlier release compute resources for remaining problems. This asynchronous architecture maximizes hardware utilization and enables solving multiple problems in parallel.

#### Language Model: Gemini Architecture with Analysis String Interface

The language model in AG2 is a **sparse mixture-of-experts Transformer** based on the Gemini architecture (Gemini Team, 2024), representing a significant upgrade from AG1's custom transformer (Section 6). The model is trained on the 300 million synthetic theorems described in Section 4, using three different training setups: (1) training from scratch with a custom tokenizer in the domain-specific language (replicating AG1's approach), (2) fine-tuning pre-trained math-specialized Gemini models in natural language (Appendix B), and (3) multimodal training from scratch with diagram image input (Appendix C). Model sizes range from 51 million parameters to 3.3 billion parameters, with learning curves shown in Figure 5 (Section 6.1).

Training uses the **largest possible batch size** allowed by TPUv4 hardware, with a learning rate schedule of linear warm-up followed by cosine annealing. Learning rate hyperparameters are determined from scaling laws. Figure 5 shows that increasing model size decreases perplexity loss on train, eval, and IMO evaluation sets, with the 3.3B model achieving the lowest loss. The paper creates three evaluation sets for monitoring training: a synthetic problem set with and without auxiliary points ("eval"), a synthetic set with only auxiliary points ("eval_aux"), and a special set of IMO 2000-2024 problems previously solved by AlphaGeometry ("imo_eval"). The authors caution that perplexity is only a proxy metric because inference uses only auxiliary point suggestions (not full proofs) and multiple solutions may exist for each problem.

A critical innovation is the **analysis string** interface between the language model and DDAR (Section 6.2). In AG1, the LM received only the problem statement and output auxiliary constructions, with DDAR providing binary success/failure feedback. AG2 enriches this interface by feeding the LM information about what DDAR has already deduced. Specifically, the system computes three fact sets:

- $S_1$: Facts deducible by DDAR given the original problem premises alone
- $S_2$: Facts deducible by DDAR given the original premises plus assuming the goal predicate is true
- $S_3$: Facts that are correct numerically (by inspecting the diagram)

By definition, $S_1 \subset S_2 \subset S_3$. These sets are serialized and concatenated into an analysis string: `<problem_statement> serialized(S_1) serialized(S_2 - S_1) serialized(S_3 - S_2)`. This gives the language model visibility into what has already been proven, what would be true if the goal held, and what is numerically verified, enabling more informed construction suggestions.

Inference uses **top-k sampling with temperature $t=1.0$ and $k=32$** (Section 6.2), contrasting with AG1's greedy decoding. The paper demonstrates that these parameters are essential: with greedy decoding ($t=0.0$, $k=1$, no tree search), models solve only 2 out of 26 problems requiring auxiliary constructions. Increasing temperature to $t=1.0$ with $k=32$ samples (without tree search) solves 9 out of 26 problems. Figure 6 (Section 6.2) shows that lower temperatures $t < 1.0$ do not produce diverse enough auxiliary constructions, while higher temperatures increase syntax errors. The diversity from sampling is what enables the search algorithm to explore multiple construction strategies.

#### Inference Configuration: Optimal Hyperparameters from Ablation Studies

Section 7 presents ablation studies on inference settings shown in Figure 9. For a single search tree, the **optimal configuration is beam size 128, beam depth 4, and 32 samples**. More samples or larger beam search do not solve more problems, suggesting diminishing returns beyond this point. The paper notes that AG2 can solve 27 out of 50 IMO problems after only 250 training steps with batch size 256 (approximately 200 million tokens), as shown in Figure 7 (Section 7), indicating rapid learning early in training.

The full AG2 system with multiple search trees solves **42 out of 50 IMO-AG-50 problems** (84% solve rate), compared to AG1's 27 out of 50 (54%). DDAR alone (without language model) solves 16 problems, demonstrating that symbolic reasoning handles simpler cases but creative constructions are essential for harder problems. Of the 8 unsolved problems, 6 are unformalizable (involving inequalities or variable numbers of points), and 2 are attempted but not solved (IMO 2018 P6 and IMO 2023 P6, which require advanced techniques like inversion or projective geometry not implemented in DDAR).

Appendix B reveals a surprising finding: **tokenizer and domain language choice do not significantly affect performance**. Models trained with custom word-level tokenizers (vocabulary of a few thousand tokens) perform identically to models using generic large Gemini tokenizers (300k vocabulary). Similarly, models trained on natural language translations of AG data perform the same as models trained on the domain-specific language. This suggests modern LLM tokenizers are flexible enough for mathematical manipulation, and the domain language primarily serves as a verification interface rather than a cognitive aid for the model.

Appendix C explores **multimodal training** with diagram images as input alongside problem text. While training shows promising results, the multimodal model alone does not improve IMO solve rates compared to text-only models. However, like fine-tuned pre-trained models, the multimodal model produces slightly different auxiliary point proposals, and combining it with other models through SKEST's knowledge sharing boosts overall performance. The authors hypothesize that image tokenization (splitting diagrams into sequential patches) loses spatial information, and that geometry problem-solving is fundamentally algebraic rather than visual—many human IMO contestants solve problems using complex numbers or barycentric coordinates without relying on diagrams.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

AlphaGeometry2's success stems from several fundamental innovations that go beyond mere scaling. While improvements like the 300× faster symbolic engine (Section 3.3) and larger training data (Section 4) are significant, they represent engineering optimizations. The following contributions represent genuine algorithmic and methodological advances that distinguish AG2 from prior neuro-symbolic systems.

### 4.1 Shared Knowledge Ensemble of Search Trees (SKEST): A New Search Paradigm

**What makes it novel:** Prior automated theorem provers, including AlphaGeometry1, use **single-tree search strategies** where one beam search explores auxiliary constructions sequentially. SKEST introduces **parallel search trees with bidirectional knowledge sharing** — a fundamentally different architecture. Each tree can have different configurations (deep/narrow, shallow/wide, uniform type distribution), and critically, facts proven in one tree become available to all others through a shared database (Section 5, Figure 4).

**Why it matters:** This design addresses a core tension in search-based reasoning:

| Single-Tree Search | SKEST |
|-------------------|-------|
| Must commit to one search strategy (depth vs. breadth) | Multiple strategies explore simultaneously |
| Failed branches provide no value to other branches | Partial progress in one tree accelerates others |
| Vulnerable to local optima in construction space | Diverse trees escape local optima through cross-pollination |

The performance impact is measurable: AG2 with a **single search tree** solves 38 problems on IMO-AG-50, while the **full SKEST configuration** solves 42 problems (Table 4, Section 7). This 4-problem gap represents problems that require diverse search strategies — no single configuration would find all solutions.

**Broader significance:** SKEST demonstrates that **ensemble methods**, well-established in supervised learning, can be adapted for **search-based reasoning**. The knowledge-sharing mechanism is analogous to gradient sharing in distributed training, but applied to symbolic facts rather than model weights. This pattern could transfer to other domains requiring long-horizon search with partial verifiability, such as program synthesis or planning.

**Common misconception:** One might assume SKEST simply runs multiple searches and picks the best result. This is incorrect — the key innovation is **inter-tree communication during search**, not post-hoc selection. Facts written to the shared database mid-search change the trajectory of other trees, creating emergent collaboration rather than independent competition.

### 4.2 Analysis String: Enriched Neuro-Symbolic Communication

**What makes it novel:** In AlphaGeometry1, the language model received only the problem statement and output auxiliary constructions, with DDAR providing binary success/failure feedback. AG2 introduces the **analysis string** (Section 6.2), which feeds the language model three structured fact sets:

- $S_1$: Facts deducible from premises alone
- $S_2 - S_1$: Facts that would be true if the goal held
- $S_3 - S_2$: Numerically verified facts from the diagram

This creates a **closed-loop interface** where the symbolic engine informs the language model's next suggestion, rather than merely validating it afterward.

**Why it matters:** This design addresses a fundamental weakness in neuro-symbolic systems: **information asymmetry**. The symbolic engine knows what has been proven; the language model does not. By serializing $S_1$, $S_2$, and $S_3$ into the LM's input, AG2 gives the model visibility into the proof state, enabling more informed construction suggestions.

The performance impact is implicit but critical: without this interface, the LM would repeatedly suggest constructions that DDAR has already ruled out or that are irrelevant given current knowledge. The analysis string prevents **redundant exploration** and guides the LM toward constructions that bridge the gap between $S_1$ and the goal.

**Broader significance:** The analysis string pattern generalizes to any neuro-symbolic system where a neural component proposes actions and a symbolic component verifies them. Rather than treating the symbolic engine as a black-box verifier, this approach makes it an **active collaborator** that shapes the neural component's reasoning. This is analogous to how human mathematicians use partial results to guide intuition about next steps.

**Subtle design choice:** The ordering $S_1$, then $S_2 - S_1$, then $S_3 - S_2$ is intentional. It presents facts in order of **logical certainty** (proven → conditional → numerical), helping the LM distinguish between established facts and assumptions. This hierarchy mirrors how humans reason about mathematical problems.

### 4.3 Double Points Reformulation: Enabling Previously Impossible Proofs

**What makes it novel:** The double points technique (Section 3.1, Figure 1) allows AG2 to prove that two differently-named points coincide — a capability absent in AG1. The method reformulates "prove intersection of lines $a, b$ lies on circle $\omega$" as "construct $X' = a \cap \omega$, then prove $X' \in b$." This seems trivial but requires the symbolic engine to accept that $X$ and $X'$ may be the same point despite different construction histories.

**Why it matters:** This technique unlocks a class of proofs requiring **point substitution** — replacing a hard-to-analyze point with an equivalently-defined point that has more useful properties. In geometry, this is analogous to "adding a clever auxiliary line" but at the level of point identity.

The significance is best understood through an example from Appendix D: IMO 2024 P4 requires proving $\angle KIL + \angle YPX = 180^\circ$. The solution constructs point $E$ on line $BI$ such that $\angle AEB = 90^\circ$, then uses $E$ to create similar triangle pairs that would be impossible to discover without point substitution. AG1 could not formalize this reasoning; AG2 handles it through double points.

**Broader significance:** Double points handling demonstrates that **representation flexibility** matters as much as reasoning power. AG1's symbolic engine was logically sound but representationally rigid — it could not express certain valid proof strategies. AG2 shows that expanding what the system can *represent* (not just what it can *compute*) is essential for reaching human-level performance.

**Common misconception:** One might think double points is just a special case of equality reasoning. It is not — the innovation is allowing points with **different construction histories** to be proven identical, which requires tracking construction dependencies separately from geometric properties. This is a representational change, not just an additional deduction rule.

### 4.4 Random Diagram Data Generation: Avoiding Human Bias

**What makes it novel:** AG2 generates all 300 million training examples from **random diagrams** without human-crafted problem seeds (Section 4). This contrasts with systems like TongGeometry (Zhang et al., 2024), which "rely on human expertise and existing problem diagrams to guide and filter data generation."

**Why it matters:** This design choice has two critical implications:

1. **No data contamination:** Since no IMO problems (or similar human problems) seed the training data, performance on IMO benchmarks reflects genuine generalization, not memorization. This is essential for claiming "gold-medalist performance" rather than "benchmark overfitting."

2. **Exploration beyond human knowledge:** Random diagrams produce theorem distributions that may not correspond to human-interesting problems. This allows the system to discover proof strategies humans never developed, potentially leading to **superhuman creativity** (as noted in Appendix D regarding IMO 2024 P4's "elegant" solution).

**Broader significance:** This approach challenges a common assumption in AI training: that human-curated data is inherently superior. For domains with well-defined formal rules (like Euclidean geometry), **algorithmic data generation** can produce more diverse, unbiased training signals than human curation. This pattern could apply to other formal domains like program verification or circuit design.

**Trade-off acknowledged:** The paper notes that random diagram generation produces many trivial or uninteresting theorems. The greedy pruning algorithm (Figure 3, Section 4) addresses this by extracting minimal proofs, but some inefficiency remains. This is an acceptable trade-off for avoiding contamination.

### 4.5 Distinguishing Incremental vs. Fundamental Contributions

For clarity, Table 6 categorizes AG2's contributions by innovation type:

| Contribution | Type | Impact |
|--------------|------|--------|
| SKEST search algorithm | **Fundamental** | Enables 4 additional solved problems; new search paradigm |
| Analysis string interface | **Fundamental** | Changes LM-symbolic interaction pattern; generalizable |
| Double points handling | **Fundamental** | Unlocks previously impossible proof strategies |
| Random diagram generation | **Methodological** | Ensures clean evaluation; enables superhuman creativity |
| DDAR2 speedup (300×) | Incremental (engineering) | Enables deeper search; necessary but not sufficient |
| Domain language expansion (66% → 88%) | Incremental (coverage) | More problems formalizable; straightforward extension |
| Training data scale (100M → 300M) | Incremental (scaling) | Better LM performance; follows standard scaling laws |
| Gemini architecture | Incremental (infrastructure) | Leverages existing model improvements |

**Key insight:** The **fundamental innovations** (SKEST, analysis string, double points) are what enable AG2 to surpass gold-medalist performance. The **incremental improvements** (speed, coverage, scale) are necessary to make those innovations practical but would not alone achieve the performance leap. This distinction matters for researchers seeking to replicate or extend AG2's approach — the algorithmic innovations are the primary contribution, while the engineering optimizations are domain-specific implementations.

**Implication for future work:** To exceed AG2's 84% solve rate, future systems must introduce new fundamental innovations (perhaps handling inequalities or 3D geometry, as noted in Section 8), not merely scale existing components. The remaining 16% of unsolved problems involve qualitatively different reasoning (inequalities, non-linear equations, variable point counts) that require new representational capabilities, not just faster search or larger models.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how AlphaGeometry2's performance was evaluated, what the results demonstrate, and where limitations remain. The experimental design is critical because the paper makes an extraordinary claim: an AI system has surpassed the average IMO gold medalist. Such claims require rigorous validation to distinguish genuine reasoning capability from benchmark overfitting.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Benchmark Datasets

The paper uses **three primary evaluation benchmarks**, each serving a distinct purpose:

| Benchmark | Description | Problems | Purpose |
|-----------|-------------|----------|---------|
| **IMO-AG-50** | All IMO geometry problems from 2000-2024, translated to AlphaGeometry language | 50 problems (from 45 original IMO problems; some split into two due to formalization specifics) | Primary benchmark for claiming gold-medalist performance |
| **IMO-AG-30** | Subset of IMO-AG-50 that was formalizable in AG1's language | 30 problems | Enables direct comparison with AG1 and TongGeometry results from prior work |
| **IMOSL-AG-30** | 30 hardest IMO Shortlist problems from 2002-2022 that never appeared at actual IMO | 30 problems | Tests robustness on problems harder than typical IMO questions (Appendix E, Figure 15) |

The IMO-AG-50 benchmark is the **primary metric** for the gold-medalist claim. The paper notes that 45 original IMO geometry problems were translated into 50 AlphaGeometry problems because "some problems are split into two due to the specifics of our formalization" (Section 7). This translation process is non-trivial — as Section G discusses, automated formalization succeeds on only 33 out of 44 formalizable IMO problems, meaning some manual intervention was required.

**Critical design choice:** The training data is generated from **random diagrams** without any human-crafted problem seeds (Section 4). This eliminates data contamination risk — the model cannot memorize IMO problems because none appeared in training. The paper explicitly contrasts this with TongGeometry (Zhang et al., 2024), which "rely on human expertise and existing problem diagrams to guide and filter data generation." This methodological difference is essential for interpreting the results fairly.

#### Evaluation Metrics

The primary metric is **solve rate**: the number of problems for which the system produces a complete, verified proof within the computational budget. A problem is counted as solved if:

1. The language model proposes auxiliary constructions
2. DDAR2 successfully derives the goal predicate from the premises plus constructions
3. The proof completes within the time/compute budget

Secondary metrics include:

- **Perplexity loss** on three evaluation sets during training (Section 6.1):
  - `eval`: Synthetic problems with and without auxiliary points
  - `eval_aux`: Synthetic problems requiring auxiliary points only
  - `imo_eval`: IMO 2000-2024 problems previously solved by AlphaGeometry
- **Training progress**: Problems solved as a function of tokens seen during training (Figure 7)
- **Proof step validity**: Percentage of generated proof steps that pass verification checks (Appendix F, Figure 16)

The paper acknowledges that perplexity is only a **proxy metric** for two reasons (Section 6.1):
1. During inference, only auxiliary point suggestions are used (not full proofs), but perplexity is computed on entire proofs
2. Multiple valid solutions may exist for a problem, but perplexity is computed for one particular solution

This honest assessment strengthens the credibility of the results — the authors recognize the limitations of their training metrics and prioritize the downstream solve rate as the true measure of success.

#### Baselines and Comparison Systems

Table 4 (Section 7) provides comprehensive comparisons across multiple systems:

| System | IMO-AG-50 Solved | IMO-AG-30 Solved | Notes |
|--------|------------------|------------------|-------|
| OpenAI o1 | 0 | 0 | Pure language model |
| Gemini thinking | 0 | 0 | Pure language model |
| AG1 DDAR (symbolic only) | 14 | 14 | No language model |
| AG2 DDAR (symbolic only) | 16 | 15 | No language model |
| TongGeometry DD (Zhang et al., 2024) | — | 18 | Symbolic only |
| Average bronze medalist | 27.1 | 19.3 | Human baseline |
| Average silver medalist | 33.9 | 22.9 | Human baseline |
| AG1 (full system) | 27 | 25 | Prior neuro-symbolic system |
| Average gold medalist | 40.9 | 25.9 | Human baseline (target) |
| TongGeometry full setting | — | 30 | Competing neuro-symbolic system |
| **AG2 full setting** | **42** | **30** | **This work** |

Several observations are critical for interpreting these comparisons:

**Pure language models fail completely.** Both OpenAI o1 and Gemini thinking scored 0 on these benchmarks. This validates the paper's core design choice: LLMs alone cannot reliably solve Olympiad geometry. As Appendix A notes, "LLMs are notoriously unreliable even for basic arithmetics" and geometry requires "numerous precise algebraic manipulations consistently throughout a potentially long solution."

**Symbolic engines alone are insufficient but non-trivial.** AG2's DDAR alone solves 16 problems — better than AG1's DDAR (14 problems) due to the expanded domain language and faster algorithm, but far below the full system's 42. This demonstrates that creative insight (the LM component) is essential for hard problems, while the symbolic engine handles verification and simpler cases.

**AG2 surpasses all prior automated systems.** On IMO-AG-30, AG2 matches TongGeometry's 30 solved problems. On the full IMO-AG-50 benchmark, AG2's 42 solved problems exceeds the **average gold medalist performance of 40.9** — explicitly noted as "a first for any AI system" (Section 7, footnote 5).

**Human baselines are appropriately chosen.** The paper uses average medalist performance rather than perfect scores or winner performance. This is methodologically sound — claiming to surpass an "average gold medalist" is more meaningful and verifiable than claiming to match the world's best human geometers.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

#### Primary Result: 84% Solve Rate on IMO 2000-2024

The headline result is that **AG2 solves 42 out of 50 IMO-AG-50 problems (84% solve rate)**, compared to AG1's 27 out of 50 (54% solve rate) (Section 7, Figure 8). This represents a **30 percentage point improvement** and crosses the gold-medalist threshold of 40.9 problems.

Figure 8 (Section 7) visualizes the results chronologically, showing which specific IMO problems were solved, attempted but not solved, or not attempted (unformalizable). The figure uses color coding:
- **Green**: Solved by AG2
- **Yellow**: Solved by DDAR alone (no LM needed)
- **Orange**: Attempted but not solved
- **Gray**: Not attempted (unformalizable)

The chronological distribution shows AG2 solves problems across all years from 2000-2024, not just easier recent problems. This demonstrates consistent capability rather than luck on a favorable subset.

#### Training Efficiency: Rapid Early Learning

Figure 7 (Section 7) shows the number of IMO problems solved as a function of tokens seen during training. A striking finding: **AG2 solves 27 out of 50 problems after only 200 million tokens** (approximately 250 training steps with batch size 256). This is the same solve rate as the full AG1 system, achieved with a fraction of the training compute.

The learning curve continues to improve beyond this point, reaching 42 problems at full training. This suggests:
1. The synthetic data generation produces highly informative training signals
2. The Gemini architecture learns efficiently from the domain-specific language
3. Most "easy" problems are solved early; harder problems require more training

#### Inference Configuration Ablation

Figure 9 (Section 7) presents ablation studies on inference hyperparameters for a single search tree. The experiments vary one parameter while holding others fixed, starting from beam size 512, beam depth 4, and 32 samples.

**Optimal configuration identified:**
- **Beam size: 128** (larger sizes do not help)
- **Beam depth: 4** (deeper searches do not help)
- **Number of samples: 32** (more samples do not help)

The paper notes: "More samples or a larger beam search do not help solve more problems" (Section 7). This is an important negative result — it suggests diminishing returns beyond a certain search budget, and that the SKEST architecture (multiple trees with knowledge sharing) is more important than brute-force search expansion.

#### Temperature and Sampling Diversity

Section 6.2 and Figure 6 examine the effect of sampling temperature on solution diversity. The results are decisive:

| Configuration | Problems Solved (out of 26 requiring auxiliary constructions) |
|---------------|--------------------------------------------------------------|
| Greedy decoding ($t=0.0$, $k=1$, no tree search) | 2 |
| Top-k sampling ($t=1.0$, $k=32$, no tree search) | 9 |
| Full SKEST with $t=1.0$, $k=32$ | Much higher (part of 42 total) |

The paper states: "Note that a high temperature and multiple samples are essential for solving IMO problems" (Section 6.2). Lower temperatures $t < 1.0$ "do not produce diverse enough auxiliary constructions," while higher temperatures "result in an increasing number LM outputs with a wrong domain language syntax."

This finding has broader implications for neuro-symbolic systems: **controlled randomness** in the neural component is essential for exploring the construction space effectively. Greedy decoding, while common in theorem proving, is insufficient for problems requiring creative insight.

#### Tokenizer and Domain Language Ablation (Appendix B)

Appendix B presents a surprising negative result: **tokenizer and domain language choice do not significantly affect performance**.

**Tokenizer comparison:**
- Custom word-level tokenizer (vocabulary of a few thousand tokens)
- Generic Gemini tokenizer (300k vocabulary)
- **Result:** Identical IMO solve rates

**Language comparison:**
- Domain-specific language (AlphaGeometry predicates)
- Natural language translation of the same data
- **Result:** Identical IMO solve rates

The paper states: "Somewhat surprisingly, we find that AlphaGeometry's performance on the 2000-2024 IMO geometry problems stays the same with different tokenizers" and "we get the same results on 2000-2024 IMO geometry problems" for natural language vs. domain language training.

**Interpretation:** This suggests modern LLM tokenizers are flexible enough for mathematical manipulation, and the domain language primarily serves as a **verification interface** rather than a cognitive aid for the model. The symbolic engine (DDAR) requires structured input, but the LM can learn to produce it regardless of training language.

**Implication for future work:** Researchers can fine-tune pre-trained LLMs on AlphaGeometry data without needing custom tokenizers or domain-specific pre-training, lowering the barrier to entry.

#### Multimodal Ablation (Appendix C)

Appendix C explores whether adding diagram images improves performance. The setup trains Gemini models with both problem text and corresponding diagram images as input.

**Result:** "We do not observe any improvements in the solve rate on the downstream IMO problems when using this model alone" (Appendix C).

However, like the fine-tuned pre-trained models, the multimodal model "produces slightly different auxiliary point proposals" and contributes to the ensemble through SKEST's knowledge sharing.

**Authors' hypothesis for limited impact:**
1. Image tokenization splits diagrams into sequential patches, losing spatial information
2. Geometry problem-solving is fundamentally algebraic rather than visual
3. Many human IMO contestants solve problems using complex numbers or barycentric coordinates without relying on diagrams
4. Some diagram information is already provided through text predicates (e.g., `sameclock` for topological constraints)

This finding challenges the intuition that visual input should help geometry reasoning. It suggests that for formal geometry problems, **algebraic representations are sufficient** and may even be superior to visual inputs for automated systems.

### 5.3 Failure Analysis and Limitations

#### Un solved Problems: 8 Out of 50

Of the 50 IMO-AG-50 problems, AG2 fails on 8 (Section 7):

| Category | Count | Examples | Reason |
|----------|-------|----------|--------|
| Unformalizable | 6 | Various | Involve inequalities or variable number of points — not covered by AG2 language |
| Attempted but not solved | 2 | IMO 2018 P6, IMO 2023 P6 | Require advanced techniques (inversion, projective geometry, radical axis) not implemented in DDAR |

The 6 unformalizable problems represent the **12% coverage gap** mentioned in Section 2. These involve:
- 3D geometry
- Inequalities
- Non-linear equations
- Problems with variable numbers of points (e.g., "$n$ points where $n$ is an arbitrary positive integer")

Appendix H details the inequality rules that would need to be incorporated to handle this remaining 12%. The paper explicitly acknowledges that "extending AlphaGeometry to encompass these topics is a substantial undertaking that falls beyond the scope of this work" (Section 8).

The 2 attempted-but-unsolved problems (IMO 2018 P6, IMO 2023 P6) are particularly informative. These require **advanced Euclidean geometry techniques** not implemented in DDAR:
- Inversion
- Projective geometry
- Radical axis

The paper notes: "While such problems in theory can be solved without these techniques, such solutions would require longer inference time, longer proofs and more auxiliary constructions" (Section 7). This suggests a trade-off: implementing more deduction rules in DDAR could solve these problems faster, but the current system could potentially solve them with enough search budget.

#### IMOSL-AG-30: Performance on Harder Problems

Appendix E and Figure 15 evaluate AG2 on 30 hardest IMO Shortlist problems that never appeared at the actual IMO. These are generally considered harder than typical IMO problems.

**Result:** AG2 solves **20 out of 30 problems (67% solve rate)** on IMOSL-AG-30.

This is lower than the 84% on IMO-AG-50, which is expected given the increased difficulty. However, solving 20 out of 30 of the hardest shortlist problems demonstrates robustness beyond the standard IMO benchmark.

Figure 15 shows the chronological distribution of solved vs. unsolved shortlist problems. The unsolved problems span multiple years, suggesting no particular year or problem type is systematically harder for AG2.

#### Proof Step Validity (Appendix F)

Appendix F evaluates the language model's ability to generate full proofs (not just auxiliary constructions) without DDAR verification. The study queries models with IMO problems and 32 samples at temperature 1.0, then verifies each proof step using a DDAR-based checker.

**Error categories identified:**
- Wrong grammar
- Theorem name error
- Step reference error
- Point not found error
- Numerical error (DDAR fails due to numerical instability)
- Unverified (premises do not imply conclusion)
- Invalid auxiliary point
- Verified (all checks passed)

**Key finding:** "Our models do not make many syntax mistakes" and "the majority of generated steps are either valid (either fully verified or correct but unverified)" (Appendix F, Figure 16).

**Surprising result:** "Both small and larger models perform similarly" on proof step validity. This suggests that even smaller models learn the domain language syntax and deduction patterns effectively, though larger models may still be better at selecting the right constructions (as shown by the overall solve rate improvement with model size in Figure 5).

**Implication:** The paper concludes that "large language models can be self-sufficient without depending on external tools, but until inference speed is improved and hallucinations are completely resolved, the tools will stay essential for math applications" (Appendix F). This is a nuanced position — LLMs show promise for standalone proof generation, but symbolic verification remains necessary for reliability.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

#### Strengths of the Experimental Design

**1. Clean train/test separation.** The use of random diagram generation for training data eliminates contamination risk. No IMO problems (or similar human problems) appear in training, so the 84% solve rate reflects genuine generalization.

**2. Multiple baselines.** The paper compares against pure LLMs (o1, Gemini thinking), symbolic-only systems (DDAR alone), prior neuro-symbolic systems (AG1, TongGeometry), and human baselines (bronze/silver/gold medalist averages). This multi-angle comparison contextualizes the results appropriately.

**3. Ablation studies.** The paper includes ablations on inference hyperparameters (Figure 9), temperature (Figure 6), tokenizers (Appendix B), domain language (Appendix B), and multimodal input (Appendix C). These studies isolate the contribution of each component.

**4. Transparent failure reporting.** The paper explicitly identifies which 8 problems were not solved and why (Section 7, Figure 8). It does not hide limitations or cherry-pick favorable subsets.

**5. Reproducibility efforts.** Code for DDAR2 and 27 IMO problems with solutions are shared on GitHub (Section 8, Code Availability). While full training code is not provided due to internal Google infrastructure, the core symbolic engine and evaluation problems are accessible.

#### Weaknesses and Open Questions

**1. Formalization effort not fully quantified.** Section G states that automated formalization succeeds on 33 out of 44 formalizable IMO problems, implying manual intervention for the remainder. The paper does not specify how much human effort was required to translate the 45 IMO problems into 50 AlphaGeometry problems. If significant manual formalization was needed, the "fully automated" claim is somewhat qualified.

**2. Compute budget not fully specified.** The paper mentions using TPUv4 hardware and asynchronous LM/DDAR workers, but does not specify the total compute budget per problem or the wall-clock time for the full SKEST search. Section 7 notes that IMO 2024 P4 was solved "within 30 seconds," but this is one example, not a systematic measurement.

**3. Single-seed evaluation.** The paper reports solve rates but does not discuss variance across multiple runs with different random seeds. Given the stochastic nature of the search (temperature $t=1.0$, $k=32$), some problems may have variable solve rates. A confidence interval or multiple-run average would strengthen the claims.

**4. Comparison to TongGeometry is incomplete.** Table 4 shows TongGeometry results only on IMO-AG-30 (30 problems), not IMO-AG-50 (50 problems). The paper states TongGeometry solves 30/30 on IMO-AG-30, matching AG2's 30/30, but we cannot directly compare on the full 50-problem benchmark. This may be due to TongGeometry's language limitations, but it prevents a complete head-to-head comparison.

**5. Human medalist data source not detailed.** The paper cites average medalist scores (40.9 for gold, 33.9 for silver, 27.1 for bronze) but does not specify the source of these statistics. Are these from official IMO records? Over which years? How is "average gold medalist" defined (mean score of all gold medalists, or the gold/silver cutoff score)? This affects the interpretation of the "surpasses gold medalist" claim.

#### Verdict on Claims

**Claim: "AG2 surpasses average gold medalist performance"**

**Supported.** The evidence is strong: 42/50 solved vs. 40.9 average gold medalist standard. The benchmark is well-defined (IMO 2000-2024 geometry problems), the train/test separation is clean, and the comparison to human baselines is appropriate.

**Claim: "84% solve rate on all geometry problems over the last 25 years"**

**Qualified.** The 84% figure (42/50) is accurate for the IMO-AG-50 benchmark. However, 12% of problems were unformalizable and not attempted. A more precise statement would be "84% solve rate on formalizable IMO geometry problems" or "75% solve rate on all IMO geometry problems including unformalizable ones."

**Claim: "Significant improvement over AG1"**

**Strongly supported.** AG1 solved 27/50 (54%); AG2 solves 42/50 (84%). This 30 percentage point improvement is substantial and comes from multiple documented improvements (domain language, DDAR speed, LM capacity, SKEST search).

**Claim: "Superhuman creativity in solutions"**

**Anecdotally supported.** Appendix D provides detailed examples (IMO 2024 P4, IMO 2013 P3, IMO 2014 P3, IMOSL 2009 G7) where AG2's solutions use unconventional auxiliary constructions that human experts found elegant. However, this is qualitative evidence from expert judgment, not a quantitative metric. The claim is plausible but not rigorously measured.

### 5.5 Summary of Experimental Findings

Table 7 summarizes the key experimental results:

| Metric | Value | Significance |
|--------|-------|--------------|
| IMO-AG-50 solve rate | 42/50 (84%) | Surpasses average gold medalist (40.9) |
| IMO-AG-30 solve rate | 30/30 (100%) | Matches best prior system (TongGeometry) |

The experiments convincingly support the paper's central claim: AlphaGeometry2 achieves gold-medalist performance on IMO geometry problems. The methodological rigor (clean train/test separation, multiple baselines, ablation studies) and transparent reporting of limitations strengthen confidence in the results. The remaining gaps (inequalities, advanced techniques like inversion) are clearly identified, providing a roadmap for future work rather than obscuring weaknesses.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

AlphaGeometry2 represents a significant advancement in automated geometry reasoning, but it is not a complete solution to Euclidean geometry problem-solving. This section examines the system's assumptions, uncovered problem classes, computational constraints, and open questions. Understanding these limitations is essential for researchers seeking to build upon AG2's foundation and for practitioners evaluating its applicability to real-world reasoning tasks.

### 6.1 Domain Coverage: The 12% Gap

Despite achieving 84% solve rate on IMO 2000-2024 geometry problems, AG2 explicitly cannot handle **12% of problems** due to domain language limitations (Section 2). This gap is not a matter of search efficiency or model capacity — these problems are fundamentally **unformalizable** in the current system.

**Uncovered problem categories:**

| Category | Example Constraint | Why AG2 Cannot Handle It |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------------|
| **3D Geometry** | Points in three-dimensional space | Domain language only supports 2D predicates (collinear, cyclic, etc.) |
| **Inequalities** | $AB > CD$, $\angle ABC < 90^\circ$ | No inequality predicates in DDAR2 (Appendix H lists 50+ rules that would need implementation) |
| **Non-linear equations** | $AB \times CD = EF^2$ | DDAR2 tracks only linear equations of angles, distances, and log-distances (Section 3.2) |
| **Variable point counts** | "$n$ points where $n$ is arbitrary" | Language requires fixed predicate instances; cannot express quantification over $n$ |

The inequality limitation is particularly significant. Appendix H provides an extensive catalog of **50+ inequality deduction rules** that would need to be implemented to handle this class of problems, including:
- Orientation predicates ($\omega(ABC)$ for clockwise/counterclockwise)
- Betweenness predicates ($\eta(ABC)$ for point ordering on lines)
- Polygon convexity rules
- Circle containment rules

The authors explicitly acknowledge that "extending AlphaGeometry to encompass these topics is a substantial undertaking that falls beyond the scope of this work" (Section 8). This is not a minor engineering task — it requires fundamentally new representation schemes for ordering and magnitude relationships that go beyond the equality-based reasoning AG2 currently supports.

**Implication:** AG2's 84% solve rate should be interpreted as "84% of formalizable problems" rather than "84% of all IMO geometry problems." If we include the 6 unformalizable problems in the denominator, the effective solve rate on **all** IMO geometry problems is $42/50 = 84\%$, but the coverage rate is only $44/50 = 88\%$ (Section 2). This distinction matters for claims about "solving Euclidean geometry" — AG2 solves most of 2D equality-based geometry, but not Euclidean geometry in full generality.

### 6.2 Missing Geometric Techniques: The Technique Gap

Beyond the 6 unformalizable problems, AG2 fails on **2 additional problems** (IMO 2018 P6 and IMO 2023 P6) that are formalizable but require advanced geometric techniques not implemented in DDAR2 (Section 7):

- **Inversion**: Transforming points through a circle to simplify configurations
- **Projective geometry**: Reasoning about points at infinity and cross-ratios
- **Radical axis**: Properties of circles sharing common chord lines

The paper notes: "While such problems in theory can be solved without these techniques, such solutions would require longer inference time, longer proofs and more auxiliary constructions" (Section 7). This reveals a critical trade-off:

| Approach | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|
| **Add techniques to DDAR** | Faster solving, shorter proofs | Increased engine complexity, more deduction rules to maintain |
| **Rely on search + auxiliary constructions** | Simpler engine, more general | Exponentially harder search, may exceed compute budget |

This is a fundamental design decision in neuro-symbolic systems: how much domain knowledge to encode in the symbolic engine versus how much to learn through search. AG2 chooses a **minimalist symbolic engine** (equality-based reasoning only) and relies on the language model to discover workarounds through auxiliary constructions. This works for 84% of problems but hits a wall on the remaining 16%.

**Comparison to human problem-solving:** Human IMO gold medalists typically learn inversion, projective geometry, and radical axis as standard tools. AG2's inability to use these techniques means it must find alternative proof paths that humans would never consider — which sometimes works (Appendix D shows "superhuman creativity") but sometimes fails within the compute budget.

### 6.3 Computational Constraints and Scalability

#### Inference Compute Requirements

AG2's SKEST algorithm runs **multiple search trees in parallel** with asynchronous LM and DDAR workers on TPUv4 hardware (Section 5). While the paper reports IMO 2024 P4 was solved "within 30 seconds" (Appendix D), it does not systematically report:

- Average compute time per problem across all 50 IMO-AG-50 problems
- Total TPU hours consumed for the full benchmark evaluation
- Memory requirements for the shared facts database during multi-tree search

The ablation study in Figure 9 (Section 7) shows diminishing returns beyond beam size 128, depth 4, and 32 samples for a **single tree**. However, SKEST uses **multiple trees simultaneously**, which multiplies the compute cost. The paper states: "The exact number of TPUs depends on the model size" (Section 5, footnote 3), but does not specify the total infrastructure required.

**Trade-off:** The 300× speedup in DDAR2 (from 1179.57s to 3.45s average, Section 3.3) enables deeper search, but the SKEST architecture's parallel tree execution means total compute cost is still substantial. This limits AG2's practical deployment — it is not a system that can run on consumer hardware or provide instant feedback.

#### Training Data Scale

AG2 generates **approximately 300 million synthetic theorems** (Section 4), an order of magnitude larger than AG1's 100 million. While this enables better model performance (Figure 5, Section 6.1), it raises questions about scalability:

- **Data generation cost:** The paper does not report the compute time required to generate 300M theorems using DDAR2. Given that DDAR2 averages 3.45 seconds per problem on hard cases, even if average cases are faster, generating hundreds of millions of examples requires substantial infrastructure.
- **Diminishing returns:** Figure 7 (Section 7) shows AG2 solves 27/50 problems after only 200 million tokens of training, but requires full training to reach 42/50. The marginal gain from 27 to 42 problems requires significant additional training compute.

**Open question:** Would a smaller, more carefully filtered dataset achieve similar performance? The greedy pruning algorithm (Figure 3, Section 4) extracts minimal proofs, but the paper does not ablate dataset size versus performance.

### 6.4 Formalization Bottleneck

A critical but often overlooked limitation is the **problem formalization pipeline**. Section G describes progress toward automated formalization using Gemini with few-shot prompting:

- **Success rate:** 33 out of 44 formalizable IMO 2000-2024 problems (75%)
- **Failure modes:** 11 problems require manual intervention

The paper states: "For easier geometry problems, it is very consistent and makes almost no mistakes" (Section G), but does not characterize which problem types cause formalization failures. This creates a **hidden human-in-the-loop requirement**:

1. Natural language problem → Automated formalization (75% success)
2. Failed cases → Manual translation by experts
3. Formal problem → AG2 solving pipeline

**Implication:** The claim of a "fully automated system" (Section 8) is qualified. While the **solving** pipeline is automated end-to-end once a problem is formalized, the **formalization** step still requires human intervention for 25% of problems. This is a significant bottleneck for scaling to new problem domains or handling user-submitted problems without expert oversight.

The paper acknowledges this: "A major weakness of AlphaGeometry, and other similar neuro-symbolic systems, is the need to manually transform input problems from natural language into a domain-specific language" (Section G). AG2 makes progress but does not fully solve this challenge.

### 6.5 Language Model Limitations

#### Proof Generation Without Verification

Appendix F evaluates whether the language model can generate **complete verified proofs** without DDAR assistance. The findings are mixed:

- **Syntax errors:** Rare ("models almost do not make any syntax mistakes," Figure 16)
- **Logical validity:** "Majority of generated steps are either valid (either fully verified or correct but unverified)"
- **Model size effect:** "Both small and larger models perform similarly" on step validity

However, the paper concludes: "Until inference speed is improved and hallucinations are completely resolved, the tools will stay essential for math applications" (Appendix F). This acknowledges that **LLMs alone cannot be trusted** for mathematical reasoning, even when they produce syntactically correct output.

**Trade-off:** AG2 uses the LM only for auxiliary construction suggestion, with DDAR handling all verification. This is safer but limits the LM's role. The paper explores full proof generation in Appendix F but does not integrate it into the main system, suggesting the authors do not consider it production-ready.

#### Temperature Sensitivity

Section 6.2 and Figure 6 reveal that AG2 is **highly sensitive to sampling temperature**:

| Configuration | Problems Solved (out of 26 requiring auxiliary constructions) |
|---------------|--------------------------------------------------------------|
| Greedy ($t=0.0$, $k=1$) | 2 |
| Sampling ($t=1.0$, $k=32$) | 9 |

The paper states: "Note that a high temperature and multiple samples are essential for solving IMO problems" (Section 6.2). This has practical implications:

- **Non-determinism:** The same problem may have different outcomes across runs, complicating debugging and reproducibility.
- **Hyperparameter tuning:** Temperature $t=1.0$ is optimal for IMO problems, but this may not generalize to other domains or difficulty levels.
- **Greedy decoding failure:** The dramatic drop from 9 to 2 problems with greedy decoding suggests AG2 relies on **stochastic exploration** rather than deterministic optimal search.

**Open question:** Is this temperature sensitivity a fundamental property of geometry problem-solving, or an artifact of the training procedure? The paper does not ablate training temperature versus inference temperature.

### 6.6 Multimodal Input: Limited Utility

Appendix C explores training models with **diagram images** as input alongside problem text. The results are surprising:

- **Standalone performance:** "We do not observe any improvements in the solve rate on the downstream IMO problems when using this model alone"
- **Ensemble contribution:** The multimodal model "produces slightly different auxiliary point proposals" and helps when combined with other models via SKEST

The authors hypothesize several reasons for limited visual impact:

1. **Image tokenization losses:** "Splitting the diagram into independent sequential patches... leads to the loss of some spatial information"
2. **Algebraic nature of geometry:** "The core of geometry problem-solving lies in algebraic reasoning... rather than geometric reasoning"
3. **Redundant information:** "Some details about the diagram are already provided through the text" (e.g., `sameclock` predicates encode topology)

**Implication:** This challenges the intuition that visual input should help geometry reasoning. For formal geometry problems with precise predicate-based specifications, **algebraic representations are sufficient**. This suggests AG2's architecture is well-matched to the problem structure, but also limits its applicability to problems where visual intuition is essential (e.g., problems with complex diagrams where relationships are not explicitly stated).

### 6.7 Reproducibility and Code Availability

Section 8 ("Code availability") reveals significant limitations in reproducibility:

| Component | Availability | Reason |
|-----------|--------------|--------|
| DDAR2 symbolic engine (Python) | **Public** (GitHub) | Core algorithm |
| 27 IMO problems with solutions | **Public** (GitHub) | Evaluation examples |
| Gemini model training code | **Not provided** | Internal Google infrastructure |
| Full SKEST tree search code | **Not provided** | Internal Google infrastructure, parallel workers |

This creates a **reproducibility gap**: researchers can access the symbolic engine and evaluate on provided problems, but cannot:
- Retrain the language models on the 300M synthetic dataset
- Replicate the full SKEST search infrastructure
- Verify the exact compute requirements for the reported results

**Trade-off:** The authors balance openness with practical constraints (internal infrastructure), but this limits independent verification of the 84% solve rate claim. Researchers must trust the reported numbers or implement their own LM training and search infrastructure.

### 6.8 No Reinforcement Learning

Appendix A notes that AG2 "does not use any reinforcement learning techniques to achieve gold-medalist level performance," contrasting with other neuro-symbolic systems that use RL for self-improvement (Jha et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2023).

**Why this matters:** RL could potentially:
- Optimize the search strategy based on problem difficulty
- Learn which auxiliary constructions are most effective for different problem types
- Enable self-improvement without additional synthetic data generation

**Trade-off:** The authors choose **supervised learning on synthetic data** over RL. This simplifies training (no reward function design, no stability issues) but may leave performance on the table. The paper does not ablate RL versus supervised learning, so it is unclear whether RL would improve the 84% solve rate.

**Open question:** Could RL fine-tuning on the 8 unsolved problems specifically improve performance on the remaining 16%? The paper suggests this as future work: "We hypothesize that breaking problems into subproblems and applying Reinforcement learning approaches could close this gap" (Section 8).

### 6.9 Summary of Limitations

Table 8 summarizes AG2's key limitations and their implications:

| Limitation | Severity | Root Cause | Potential Mitigation |
|------------|----------|------------|---------------------|
| 12% unformalizable problems | High | Domain language design | Add inequality, 3D, non-linear predicates (Appendix H) |
| Missing geometric techniques | Medium | DDAR rule set | Implement inversion, projective geometry, radical axis |
| Formalization bottleneck | Medium | NL-to-formal translation | Improve few-shot prompting or fine-tune formalization model |
| Compute requirements | Medium | Parallel search trees | Optimize SKEST communication, prune redundant trees |
| Temperature sensitivity | Low | Sampling strategy | Explore adaptive temperature based on problem difficulty |
| Code availability | Medium | Infrastructure constraints | Release distilled models or API access |
| No RL optimization | Low | Training methodology | Add RL fine-tuning on hard problems |

**Key takeaway:** AG2's limitations are **well-understood and explicitly acknowledged** by the authors. The 12% coverage gap, missing techniques, and formalization bottleneck are clearly identified with concrete paths for improvement (Appendix H for inequalities, Section 8 for RL). This transparency distinguishes AG2 from systems that overclaim capabilities.

However, addressing these limitations requires **qualitatively new capabilities**, not just scaling. Adding inequality reasoning demands new symbolic representations. Implementing inversion requires new DDAR deduction rules. Improving formalization needs better language understanding. These are research challenges, not engineering optimizations.

For researchers building on AG2, the priority should be:
1. **Extend domain language** to cover the 12% gap (inequalities first, as Appendix H provides a roadmap)
2. **Improve formalization** to reduce human intervention below 25%
3. **Explore RL fine-tuning** on the 8 unsolved problems to close the technique gap

For practitioners evaluating AG2, the key consideration is: **Does your problem domain fall within the 88% coverage?** If your geometry problems involve only 2D equality-based reasoning (congruence, parallelism, cyclicity, angle equality), AG2 is production-ready. If your problems involve inequalities, 3D configurations, or variable point counts, AG2 cannot help without significant extension.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

AlphaGeometry2's achievement of gold-medalist performance on IMO geometry problems represents more than a benchmark victory — it establishes a **proven architectural blueprint** for neuro-symbolic reasoning systems that can generalize to other complex, structured domains. This section examines how AG2 reshapes the research landscape, what follow-up work it enables, where practical applications emerge, and when practitioners should adopt this approach versus alternatives.

### 7.1 Landscape Shift: From Proof-of-Concept to Production-Ready Reasoning

AG2 marks a **transition point** in AI reasoning research. Prior neuro-symbolic systems demonstrated feasibility on limited benchmarks; AG2 demonstrates **reliability at human-expert scale** on a 25-year problem archive. This shift has three cascading effects on the field:

**Validation of the neuro-symbolic paradigm.** The complete failure of pure language models (OpenAI o1 and Gemini thinking both scored 0 on IMO-AG-50, Table 4, Section 7) alongside AG2's 84% success rate provides decisive evidence that **symbolic verification is non-negotiable** for mathematical reasoning. As Appendix A states: "LLMs are notoriously unreliable even for basic arithmetics" and geometry requires "numerous precise algebraic manipulations consistently throughout a potentially long solution." This settles a debate that had persisted since the rise of large language models — neural networks alone cannot replace formal verification for tasks requiring long-horizon correctness.

**Benchmark redefinition.** The IMO-AG-50 benchmark (50 problems from 2000-2024) now serves as a **minimum viability threshold** for geometry reasoning systems. Any new system claiming geometry reasoning capability must demonstrate performance on this benchmark with clean train/test separation. The paper's methodological rigor — random diagram generation for training (Section 4), explicit formalization coverage reporting (88%, Section 2), and transparent failure analysis (8 unsolved problems, Section 7) — sets a new standard for evaluation honesty that should propagate to other reasoning domains.

**Search architecture innovation.** SKEST (Shared Knowledge Ensemble of Search Trees, Section 5, Figure 4) introduces a **new primitive for search-based reasoning**: parallel search trees with mid-search knowledge sharing. This differs fundamentally from:
- Single-tree beam search (AG1, standard theorem provers)
- Independent ensemble voting (run multiple searches, pick best result)
- Sequential refinement (one search informs the next)

The key innovation is **inter-tree communication during search**, where facts proven in one tree accelerate others. This pattern is domain-agnostic and could transfer to program synthesis, planning, or any domain where partial progress in one search branch informs others.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several open problems in Section 8, but the implications extend further. Below are concrete research directions organized by feasibility and impact:

#### 7.2.1 Immediate Extensions (1-2 Year Horizon)

**Inequality reasoning implementation.** Appendix H provides a **complete roadmap** of 50+ inequality deduction rules needed to close the 12% coverage gap. This is not speculative — the rules are explicitly defined with predicates like $\omega(ABC)$ for orientation and $\eta(ABC)$ for betweenness. A follow-up system implementing these rules could push solve rates from 84% to ~96% on IMO geometry problems. Priority should be given to:
- Basic inequality rules (Section H.3, H.8): triangle inequality, angle-side relationships
- Orientation predicates (Section H.2, H.4): clockwise/counterclockwise reasoning
- Circle containment rules (Section H.6): point-in-circle tests based on angle comparisons

**Automated formalization improvement.** Section G reports 75% success rate (33/44 problems) for automated NL-to-formal translation. This is the **primary bottleneck** for deploying AG2 outside research settings. Follow-up work should:
- Fine-tune formalization models on the 11 failure cases to characterize error patterns
- Develop interactive formalization tools where humans correct model output incrementally
- Create benchmark datasets for formalization quality (separate from solving quality)

**Reinforcement learning fine-tuning.** Section 8 explicitly hypothesizes that "breaking problems into subproblems and applying Reinforcement learning approaches could close this gap" on the 8 unsolved problems. Concrete approaches include:
- Reward shaping based on proof progress (facts proven toward goal)
- Curriculum learning starting from DDAR-solvable problems (16/50) up to hardest IMO problems
- Policy gradient methods to optimize auxiliary construction selection

#### 7.2.2 Medium-Term Extensions (2-4 Year Horizon)

**Domain transfer to other mathematical fields.** The AG2 architecture is **not geometry-specific** — it separates domain knowledge (predicates, deduction rules) from reasoning machinery (LM, SKEST, DDAR). This enables transfer to:
- **Algebra**: Predicates for equations, inequalities, polynomial relationships; DDAR rules for algebraic manipulation
- **Combinatorics**: Predicates for set membership, counting constraints; DDAR rules for enumeration
- **Number theory**: Predicates for divisibility, primality, modular arithmetic; DDAR rules for number-theoretic deductions

The key requirement is a **formal domain language** with clear deduction rules. Geometry was ideal because Euclidean axioms are well-understood; other fields require similar formalization effort.

**3D geometry extension.** The 12% coverage gap includes 3D geometry problems (Section 2). Extending AG2 requires:
- New predicates for 3D relationships: `coplanar`, `perp_plane`, `parallel_plane`
- Extended DDAR rules for 3D deduction (e.g., line-plane intersection properties)
- 3D diagram generation algorithms (Section G's method is 2D-specific)

This is a natural extension because the core reasoning architecture (LM suggests constructions, DDAR verifies) remains unchanged.

**Dynamic rule learning.** Currently, DDAR's deduction rules are **hand-coded** by experts. A more ambitious direction is learning deduction rules from data:
- Extract common proof patterns from the 300M synthetic theorems
- Generalize patterns into new deduction rules
- Validate rules for soundness before adding to DDAR

This could reduce the expert effort required to extend AG2 to new domains.

#### 7.2.3 Long-Term Vision (4+ Year Horizon)

**Fully autonomous mathematical discovery.** AG2 generates theorems from random diagrams without human seeds (Section 4). This capability could be extended to:
- Discover **novel theorems** not in human literature
- Generate **conjectures** with supporting evidence (partial proofs)
- Assist human mathematicians in **exploration phases** of research

The paper notes this allows "exploration of theorem distributions that may extend beyond established human knowledge" (Section 4). This is distinct from theorem proving — it is theorem **generation**.

**Integration with interactive proof assistants.** Systems like Lean, Coq, and Isabelle require human guidance for proof steps. AG2 could serve as an **automation layer**:
- Translate natural language problems to formal proof assistant syntax
- Suggest proof steps that the assistant verifies
- Handle routine deductions automatically, leaving creative steps to humans

This would democratize formal verification for mathematicians without proof assistant expertise.

**General reasoning architecture.** SKEST + analysis string + symbolic verification could become a **general template** for reasoning systems beyond mathematics:
- **Legal reasoning**: LM suggests legal arguments, symbolic engine checks consistency with statutes
- **Scientific hypothesis testing**: LM proposes hypotheses, symbolic engine checks against known constraints
- **Engineering design**: LM suggests design modifications, symbolic engine verifies constraint satisfaction

The pattern is: neural component proposes, symbolic component verifies, shared knowledge accelerates search.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

AG2 is not merely a research demonstration — it has concrete applications in education, verification, and assisted problem-solving.

#### 7.3.1 Mathematics Education

**Automated solution generation.** AG2 can produce **verified solutions** to Olympiad geometry problems in seconds (IMO 2024 P4 solved in 30 seconds, Appendix D). Applications include:
- Generating multiple solution approaches for the same problem (different search trees may find different proofs)
- Creating step-by-step explanations for students (traceback from proof to natural language)
- Identifying key auxiliary constructions that students should learn

**Difficulty assessment.** The fact that AG2 solves 67% of IMOSL-AG-30 (hardest shortlist problems) vs. 84% of IMO-AG-50 (Section 5.3) suggests solve rate correlates with problem difficulty. This could enable:
- Automated difficulty scoring for new problems
- Curriculum design based on AG2 solve rates across problem types
- Early identification of problems that may be too hard/easy for target audiences

**Caveat:** AG2 solutions may use unconventional constructions (Appendix D notes "superhuman creativity"). Educators should verify solutions are pedagogically appropriate, not just correct.

#### 7.3.2 Formal Verification

**Geometry textbook verification.** Publishers could use AG2 to:
- Verify all geometry problems and solutions in textbooks before publication
- Detect errors in problem statements (if AG2 cannot solve a problem that should be solvable, the statement may be flawed)
- Generate alternative solutions for instructor manuals

**Competition problem validation.** IMO and similar competitions could use AG2 to:
- Verify proposed problems have valid solutions before selection
- Check that solutions match intended difficulty (if AG2 solves too easily, problem may be too simple)
- Generate official solutions for grading rubrics

Section G notes AG2 was "used at IMO 2025 to solve the geometry problem (P2) from natural language in 20 seconds" — this demonstrates real-world competition deployment.

#### 7.3.3 Assisted Problem-Solving

**Human-AI collaboration.** AG2 is designed for **augmentation, not replacement**:
- Mathematicians can use AG2 to explore construction ideas quickly
- When AG2 fails, the failure mode identifies what techniques are missing (e.g., inversion for IMO 2018 P6)
- The analysis string (Section 6.2) shows what DDAR has proven, helping humans understand proof state

**Integration with existing tools.** AG2 could integrate with:
- **GeoGebra**: Generate diagrams from AG2 formalizations
- **Proof assistants**: Export AG2 proofs to Lean/Coq format
- **Computer algebra systems**: Handle algebraic portions of mixed geometry-algebra problems

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

Practitioners considering AG2 should understand when to adopt this approach versus alternatives, and what infrastructure is required.

#### 7.4.1 When to Use AlphaGeometry2

**Preferred scenarios:**

| Scenario | Why AG2 is Suitable |
|----------|---------------------|
| 2D Euclidean geometry with equality constraints | 88% coverage, 84% solve rate on formalizable problems |
| Problems requiring auxiliary constructions | LM specifically trained for construction suggestion |
| Need for verified, hallucination-free solutions | DDAR provides formal verification of every step |
| Education or competition settings | Fast solution generation (30 seconds per problem, Appendix D) |

**Avoid when:**

| Scenario | Why AG2 is Not Suitable |
|----------|------------------------|
| Problems with inequalities | Not supported (Appendix H rules not implemented) |
| 3D geometry | Domain language is 2D-only (Section 2) |
| Variable point counts ($n$ points for arbitrary $n$) | Language requires fixed predicate instances |
| Need for standalone LLM without symbolic engine | Appendix F shows LLMs alone cannot be trusted for math |
| Consumer hardware deployment | Requires TPUv4 infrastructure for SKEST (Section 5) |

#### 7.4.2 Infrastructure Requirements

**Minimum viable deployment:**

| Component | Requirement | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| DDAR2 symbolic engine | Public GitHub repository | Python implementation available |
| Language model | 51M to 3.3B parameters | Larger models solve more problems (Figure 5, Section 6.1) |
| Search infrastructure | TPUv4 or equivalent | SKEST requires parallel tree execution |
| Training data | 300M synthetic theorems | Generation requires DDAR2 + greedy pruning (Section 4) |

**Code availability limitations (Section 8):**
- ✅ DDAR2 Python implementation: Public
- ✅ 27 IMO problems with solutions: Public
- ❌ Gemini model training code: Internal Google infrastructure
- ❌ Full SKEST search code: Internal Google infrastructure, parallel workers

This means researchers can:
- Evaluate on provided problems using DDAR2
- Implement their own LM training on AG2 data
- Build simplified search algorithms (single-tree beam search works, just solves fewer problems)

But cannot:
- Exactly replicate the 84% solve rate without Google infrastructure
- Access the exact trained model weights

**Workaround:** Appendix B shows that fine-tuning pre-trained math-specialized Gemini models on AG2 data achieves comparable performance to training from scratch. This lowers the barrier — practitioners can start with public Gemini models and fine-tune on the synthetic data.

#### 7.4.3 Integration Patterns

**Pattern 1: Verification-only deployment.** Use DDAR2 as a standalone verifier:
- Humans or other systems propose auxiliary constructions
- DDAR2 verifies whether constructions prove the goal
- Benefits: No LM infrastructure needed, deterministic verification
- Limitations: Requires construction proposals from elsewhere

**Pattern 2: Single-tree search.** Simplified SKEST with one search tree:
- Beam size 128, depth 4, 32 samples (optimal from Figure 9, Section 7)
- Solves 38/50 IMO problems vs. 42/50 for full SKEST (Table 4)
- Benefits: Simpler infrastructure, 90% of full performance
- Limitations: Misses problems requiring diverse search strategies

**Pattern 3: Full SKEST with ensemble models.** Replicate paper's setup:
- Multiple search trees with different configurations (Section 5)
- Multiple LM sizes (51M, 176M, 3.3B) sharing knowledge
- Benefits: Maximum solve rate (42/50)
- Limitations: Requires significant infrastructure investment

**Recommendation:** Start with Pattern 2 (single-tree search) for most applications. The 4-problem gap (38 vs. 42) represents edge cases; single-tree search provides 90% of performance with far less complexity.

#### 7.4.4 Comparison to Alternatives

| Alternative | When to Prefer | Trade-offs |
|-------------|----------------|------------|
| **Pure LLM (o1, Gemini)** | Quick exploration, informal reasoning | Cannot guarantee correctness; 0% on IMO-AG-50 |
| **Algebraic methods (Wu's method)** | Problems reducible to polynomial equations | Solutions not human-readable; struggles with constructions |
| **TongGeometry** | If human-crafted diagrams are available | Relies on human data; AG2 matches performance without it |
| **AG1** | Legacy systems, resource-constrained settings | 54% vs. 84% solve rate; 300x slower DDAR |
| **AG2** | Production deployment, verified solutions | Requires TPU infrastructure; 12% coverage gap |

**Decision framework:**
1. Do you need **verified correctness**? → AG2 or symbolic-only
2. Is your problem in the **88% coverage** (2D, equality-based)? → AG2
3. Do you have **TPU infrastructure**? → Full SKEST; otherwise single-tree
4. Is **formalization effort** acceptable? → AG2 requires NL-to-formal translation

### 7.5 Concluding Perspective

AlphaGeometry2 demonstrates that **neuro-symbolic systems can match and exceed human expert performance** on well-defined reasoning tasks. The 84% solve rate on IMO geometry problems is not an endpoint — it is a **proof of concept** for a broader class of reasoning systems.

The key insight is not the specific geometry predicates or deduction rules, but the **architectural pattern**:
1. Neural component generates creative hypotheses (auxiliary constructions)
2. Symbolic component verifies hypotheses rigorously (DDAR)
3. Search orchestration enables exploration (SKEST)
4. Rich interface connects neural and symbolic (analysis string)

This pattern transfers to any domain with:
- Clear formal semantics (what counts as a valid solution)
- Creative search space (multiple paths to solutions)
- Partial verifiability (can check progress even if full solution unknown)

The remaining 16% gap (12% unformalizable + 4% technique gap) represents **known, addressable limitations** — not fundamental barriers. Appendix H provides the inequality rules; Section 8 suggests RL for technique gaps; Section G outlines formalization improvements.

For researchers: AG2 provides a **replicable foundation** — the DDAR2 code is public, the data generation procedure is documented, and the architecture is clearly described. The barrier is infrastructure (TPUs for training), not algorithmic secrecy.

For practitioners: AG2 is **production-ready for 2D equality-based geometry**. The 30-second solve time (Appendix D), verified outputs, and education use cases make it immediately deployable in competition, education, and verification contexts.

For the field: AG2 establishes that **scaling neuro-symbolic systems works** — not just scaling models, but scaling the integration between neural and symbolic components. The next frontier is extending this pattern to domains beyond geometry, closing the remaining coverage gaps, and enabling fully autonomous mathematical discovery.
