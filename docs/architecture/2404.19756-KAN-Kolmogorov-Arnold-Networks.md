## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs), a neural architecture that replaces the fixed activation functions and linear weights of Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) with learnable activation functions parametrized as splines on edges. The authors demonstrate that KANs achieve faster neural scaling laws (theoretical exponent $\alpha=4$ in Section 2.3) and superior parameter efficiency, such as reaching 81.6% accuracy on knot theory signature classification with only ~200 parameters compared to an MLP's 78.0% accuracy with ~300,000 parameters (Table 3). By enabling intuitive visualization and interaction, KANs serve as effective collaborators for scientists to (re)discover mathematical and physical laws, exemplified by rediscovering relations in knot theory and extracting mobility edge formulas in Anderson localization models like the Generalized Aubry-André model (Section 4).

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks (KANs), we must first examine the landscape of deep learning architectures they aim to challenge and the theoretical foundations they seek to revitalize. This paper addresses a critical gap in modern neural network design: the trade-off between **accuracy**, **efficiency**, and **interpretability**. While Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) have become the default building blocks for deep learning, their limitations become pronounced in scientific domains where understanding the model's reasoning is as important as its predictive performance.

### The Hegemony and Limitations of Multi-Layer Perceptrons

The dominant architecture in deep learning today is the Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP), also known as a fully-connected feedforward neural network. MLPs are grounded in the **Universal Approximation Theorem (UAT)**, which guarantees that a network with a single hidden layer containing a finite number of neurons can approximate any continuous function on compact subsets of $\mathbb{R}^n$, provided suitable activation functions are used (Section 1). Mathematically, an MLP layer typically consists of a linear transformation followed by a fixed non-linear activation function. If we denote the input as $\mathbf{x}$, a standard MLP layer computes:

$$
\text{MLP}(\mathbf{x}) = \sigma(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b})
$$

where $\mathbf{W}$ is a learnable weight matrix, $\mathbf{b}$ is a bias vector, and $\sigma$ is a fixed non-linearity (e.g., ReLU, SiLU). In deep networks, these layers are stacked, interleaving linear transformations and fixed activations (Section 2.2, Eq. 2.9).

Despite their success, MLPs suffer from three specific drawbacks highlighted in the Introduction (Section 1):
1.  **Interpretability:** MLPs are often treated as "black boxes." In transformers, for instance, MLP layers consume almost all non-embedding parameters but are less interpretable than attention mechanisms without extensive post-analysis tools.
2.  **Parameter Efficiency:** To achieve high accuracy, MLPs often require massive numbers of parameters. This is partly because they must learn both the compositional structure of the data (how variables interact) and the specific univariate functions (the shape of the relationships) simultaneously using rigid activation shapes.
3.  **Neural Scaling Laws:** Theoretical bounds suggest that MLPs suffer from the **Curse of Dimensionality (COD)**. As the input dimension $d$ increases, the number of parameters required to maintain accuracy can grow exponentially. While MLPs mitigate this in practice through feature learning, their scaling exponents are theoretically limited compared to what might be possible for functions with compositional structures (Section 2.3).

### The Forgotten Alternative: Kolmogorov–Arnold Representation Theorem

The primary theoretical alternative to the UAT is the **Kolmogorov–Arnold Representation Theorem (KAT)**. Established by Vladimir Arnold and Andrey Kolmogorov, this theorem states that any multivariate continuous function $f: [0, 1]^n \to \mathbb{R}$ can be represented as a finite composition of continuous functions of a single variable and the binary operation of addition. Specifically, for a smooth function $f(\mathbf{x})$, the theorem proposes:

$$
f(x_1, \cdots, x_n) = \sum_{q=1}^{2n+1} \Phi_q \left( \sum_{p=1}^{n} \phi_{q,p}(x_p) \right)
$$

where $\phi_{q,p}$ and $\Phi_q$ are univariate functions (Section 2.1, Eq. 2.1).

Historically, KAT was regarded as "theoretically sound but practically useless" for machine learning (Section 2.1). The reasoning was twofold:
1.  **Pathological Functions:** The original theorem does not guarantee that the univariate functions $\phi_{q,p}$ are smooth. They can be non-smooth or even fractal, making them extremely difficult to learn with standard optimization techniques (Section 2.1).
2.  **Rigid Architecture:** Previous attempts to build neural networks based on KAT (cited in Section 1 and Section 5, e.g., [9-16]) strictly adhered to the original theorem's structure: a shallow network with exactly two layers of non-linearities and a specific hidden layer width of $2n+1$. These architectures lacked the flexibility of modern deep learning (arbitrary depth and width) and often predated efficient backpropagation techniques.

### The Specific Gap: Bridging Theory and Modern Deep Learning

This paper identifies a specific gap between the theoretical promise of KAT and the practical reality of deep learning. The authors argue that the dismissal of KAT was premature because it assumed we must stick to the original shallow, potentially non-smooth representation. The gap they address involves three key areas:

1.  **Generalizing Depth and Width:** There was no existing framework to generalize the Kolmogorov–Arnold representation to arbitrary depths and widths, akin to how MLPs are stacked. The paper asks: "What is a KAN layer?" and proposes stacking them to form deep networks (Section 2.2).
2.  **Smoothness and Trainability:** To make KAT practical, the univariate functions must be smooth and learnable. Prior work did not leverage modern parametrizations like **B-splines**, which allow for smooth, locally adjustable functions that can be trained via gradient descent (Section 2.2).
3.  **Interpretability vs. Accuracy Trade-off:** Existing methods for interpretability, such as **Symbolic Regression (SR)**, attempt to find explicit mathematical formulas (e.g., $y = \sin(x) + x^2$). However, SR methods are often brittle, returning either success or failure without interpretable intermediate results, and they struggle if the true function involves special functions (e.g., Bessel functions) not in their predefined library (Section 2.5.2). There was a need for a model that offers the continuous optimization stability of neural networks with the interpretability of symbolic models.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

The paper positions KANs not merely as an incremental improvement but as a structural alternative to MLPs, particularly for **AI + Science** tasks.

*   **Vs. MLPs:** KANs move the learnable parameters from the edges (weights) to the activation functions themselves. While an MLP computes $\text{MLP}(\mathbf{x}) = (\mathbf{W}_L \circ \sigma \circ \cdots \circ \mathbf{W}_0)\mathbf{x}$, a KAN computes $\text{KAN}(\mathbf{x}) = (\Phi_L \circ \cdots \circ \Phi_0)\mathbf{x}$, where each $\Phi$ is a matrix of learnable univariate functions (Section 2.2, Eq. 2.7). This allows KANs to potentially achieve faster neural scaling laws (theoretical exponent $\alpha=4$ vs. MLPs' slower rates) by exploiting compositional structures more efficiently (Section 2.3).
*   **Vs. Prior KAT Networks:** Unlike previous Kolmogorov-based networks that were restricted to depth-2 and width-$(2n+1)$, this work revitalizes the theorem by allowing arbitrary architectures and using splines to ensure smoothness, making them compatible with modern backpropagation (Section 1).
*   **Vs. Symbolic Regression:** KANs are positioned as "collaborators" rather than automated solvers. Unlike SR, which is discrete and brittle, KANs allow for **human-in-the-loop interaction**. Users can visualize the network, prune unimportant connections, and "snap" learnable spline functions to symbolic forms (e.g., setting a learned curve to be exactly $\sin(x)$) iteratively (Section 2.5.2). This provides a continuous path from black-box fitting to white-box discovery.

In summary, the motivation stems from the need for a neural architecture that respects the compositional nature of scientific laws (via KAT) while retaining the trainability of deep learning (via splines and backprop). The paper seeks to demonstrate that by shifting learnable parameters from linear weights to activation functions, we can unlock better scaling laws and unprecedented interpretability for scientific discovery.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a neural architecture design paper that proposes replacing the linear weight matrices and fixed activation functions of traditional Multi-Layer Perceptrons with learnable univariate functions parametrized as B-splines on edges. The core idea is that by moving learnable parameters from weights to activation functions, and by generalizing the Kolmogorov-Arnold Representation Theorem to arbitrary depths and widths, KANs can achieve better accuracy-scaling tradeoffs and enable direct human interpretation of learned functions.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

KANs are neural networks where every connection between neurons is a learnable curve (spline) rather than a single number (weight), and neurons simply sum incoming signals without applying additional non-linearities. This architecture solves the problem of balancing accuracy with interpretability by allowing the network to learn both the compositional structure of data (which variables interact) and the precise shape of univariate relationships (what mathematical functions describe those interactions) simultaneously.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The KAN system consists of four major components that work together during training and inference. First, the **KAN Layer** serves as the fundamental building block, where each layer is a matrix of 1D learnable functions rather than a weight matrix (Section 2.2, Eq. 2.6). Second, the **B-spline Parametrization** defines how each univariate function is represented internally using basis functions with trainable coefficients, enabling smooth and locally adjustable curves (Section 2.2, Figure 2.2 right). Third, the **Grid Extension Mechanism** allows the network to increase its resolution during training by refining the spline grids without retraining from scratch, analogous to making a mesh finer in numerical methods (Section 2.4). Fourth, the **Simplification Pipeline** includes sparsification regularization, pruning, and symbolification tools that transform a trained KAN into an interpretable symbolic formula through human-in-the-loop interaction (Section 2.5). Information flows from input variables through successive KAN layers, where each edge applies its learnable spline function to the incoming activation, nodes sum these transformed signals, and the final output can optionally be converted to symbolic form.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first explain the KAN layer formulation and how it differs mathematically from MLP layers, since understanding this fundamental unit is prerequisite to everything else.
- We then detail the B-spline parametrization of activation functions, because this is the key innovation that makes learnable activations tractable and smooth.
- We next describe the training mechanics including initialization, grid updates, and residual connections, as these implementation details determine whether KANs actually converge in practice.
- We then cover the grid extension technique, which enables KANs to improve accuracy progressively without the expensive retraining that MLP scaling requires.
- We explain the simplification and interpretability pipeline (sparsification, pruning, symbolification), since this is what enables KANs to serve as scientific discovery tools rather than just black-box regressors.
- We conclude with the parameter count analysis and computational considerations, helping readers understand the practical tradeoffs between KANs and MLPs.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Architecture: From MLP to KAN**

This paper proposes a structural alternative to Multi-Layer Perceptrons by fundamentally changing where learnable parameters reside in the computation graph. In a standard MLP, each layer computes an affine transformation followed by a fixed non-linearity, expressed as $\text{MLP}(\mathbf{x}) = (\mathbf{W}_{L-1} \circ \sigma \circ \mathbf{W}_{L-2} \circ \sigma \circ \cdots \circ \mathbf{W}_0)\mathbf{x}$ where $\mathbf{W}_l$ are learnable weight matrices and $\sigma$ is a fixed activation function like ReLU or SiLU (Section 2.2, Eq. 2.9). In contrast, a KAN replaces both the linear weights and fixed activations with a single learnable function matrix per layer, computed as $\text{KAN}(\mathbf{x}) = (\Phi_{L-1} \circ \Phi_{L-2} \circ \cdots \circ \Phi_0)\mathbf{x}$ where each $\Phi_l$ is a matrix of 1D learnable functions (Section 2.2, Eq. 2.7). This design choice eliminates linear weights entirely—every parameter in a KAN is part of a univariate function, which the authors argue allows the network to learn both feature compositions (external degrees of freedom) and precise univariate shapes (internal degrees of freedom) more efficiently than MLPs (Section 2.4).

**KAN Layer Formulation**

A KAN layer with $n_{\text{in}}$ inputs and $n_{\text{out}}$ outputs is defined as a matrix of 1D functions $\Phi = \{\phi_{q,p}\}$ where $p = 1, 2, \cdots, n_{\text{in}}$ and $q = 1, 2, \cdots, n_{\text{out}}$ (Section 2.2, Eq. 2.2). The network shape is represented by an integer array $[n_0, n_1, \cdots, n_L]$ where $n_i$ denotes the number of nodes in the $i$-th layer (Section 2.2, Eq. 2.3). Between layer $l$ and layer $l+1$, there are $n_l n_{l+1}$ activation functions, and the activation function connecting node $(l, i)$ to node $(l+1, j)$ is denoted $\phi_{l,j,i}$ (Section 2.2, Eq. 2.4). The pre-activation value at node $(l, i)$ is $x_{l,i}$, and the post-activation after applying $\phi_{l,j,i}$ is $\tilde{x}_{l,j,i} \equiv \phi_{l,j,i}(x_{l,i})$ (Section 2.2). Crucially, nodes in KANs perform only summation without any additional non-linearity, so the activation value of node $(l+1, j)$ is simply the sum of all incoming post-activations: $x_{l+1,j} = \sum_{i=1}^{n_l} \tilde{x}_{l,j,i} = \sum_{i=1}^{n_l} \phi_{l,j,i}(x_{l,i})$ for $j = 1, \cdots, n_{l+1}$ (Section 2.2, Eq. 2.5). In matrix form, this becomes $\mathbf{x}_{l+1} = \Phi_l \mathbf{x}_l$ where $\Phi_l$ is the function matrix for layer $l$ (Section 2.2, Eq. 2.6). The original Kolmogorov-Arnold Representation Theorem corresponds to a specific 2-layer KAN with shape $[n, 2n+1, 1]$, but this paper generalizes to arbitrary depths and widths because the original shallow form cannot represent all functions with smooth activations (Section 2.2, Section 2.3).

**B-spline Parametrization of Activation Functions**

Each univariate activation function $\phi(x)$ in a KAN is parametrized as a B-spline curve with learnable coefficients, which enables smooth, locally adjustable functions that can be trained via gradient descent. The key implementation trick is using a residual formulation where $\phi(x) = w_b b(x) + w_s \text{spline}(x)$ (Section 2.2, Eq. 2.10). Here $b(x)$ is a fixed basis function, typically set to $b(x) = \text{silu}(x) = x/(1 + e^{-x})$ in most cases (Section 2.2, Eq. 2.11). The spline component is parametrized as a linear combination of B-spline basis functions: $\text{spline}(x) = \sum_i c_i B_i(x)$ where $c_i$ are trainable coefficients (Section 2.2, Eq. 2.12). The factors $w_b$ and $w_s$ are technically redundant since they could be absorbed into $b(x)$ and $\text{spline}(x)$, but they are kept as trainable parameters to better control the overall magnitude of the activation function during training (Section 2.2). Each spline is defined on a grid with $G$ intervals (meaning $G+1$ grid points), and the spline order $k$ is typically set to $k=3$ for cubic splines (Section 2.2). The B-spline basis function $B_i(x)$ is non-zero only on a local interval $[t_{-k+i}, t_{i+1}]$, which gives splines their key property of local adjustability—changing one coefficient only affects the function in a bounded region (Section 2.4). This locality is critical for both interpretability (you can see which input ranges matter) and for avoiding catastrophic forgetting in continual learning scenarios (Section 3.5).

**Initialization and Training Mechanics**

Proper initialization is critical for KANs because learnable activation functions introduce new optimization challenges compared to fixed activations. Each activation function is initialized with $w_s = 1$ and $\text{spline}(x) \approx 0$, achieved by drawing B-spline coefficients $c_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ with a small $\sigma$, typically $\sigma = 0.1$ (Section 2.2, footnote 2). The basis weight $w_b$ is initialized according to Xavier initialization, which has been standard for initializing linear layers in MLPs (Section 2.2). A critical implementation detail is that spline grids are updated on the fly according to input activations during training, rather than being fixed or learned via gradient descent (Section 2.2, footnote 3). This addresses the fundamental issue that splines are defined on bounded regions but activation values can evolve outside the initial grid range during training. The authors tried using normalization to fix the input range but found its performance inferior to dynamic grid updates (Section 2.2, footnote 3). All operations in KANs are differentiable, so they can be trained with standard backpropagation, and the paper uses the LBFGS optimizer for most experiments (Section 3.1).

**Grid Extension for Progressive Accuracy**

One of the most distinctive features of KANs is the grid extension technique, which allows a trained KAN to be made more accurate by refining its spline grids without retraining from scratch. The principle is that a spline can be made arbitrarily accurate to a target function as the grid becomes finer, and this property is inherited by KANs (Section 2.4). To perform grid extension, suppose we want to approximate a 1D function $f$ on $[a, b]$ with B-splines of order $k$. A coarse grid with $G_1$ intervals has grid points $\{t_0 = a, t_1, t_2, \cdots, t_{G_1} = b\}$, which is augmented to $\{t_{-k}, \cdots, t_{-1}, t_0, \cdots, t_{G_1}, t_{G_1+1}, \cdots, t_{G_1+k}\}$ for B-spline basis definition (Section 2.4). The function on the coarse grid is $f_{\text{coarse}}(x) = \sum_{i=0}^{G_1+k-1} c_i B_i(x)$. Given a finer grid with $G_2$ intervals, the function is $f_{\text{fine}}(x) = \sum_{j=0}^{G_2+k-1} c'_j B'_j(x)$ (Section 2.4). The new parameters $c'_j$ are initialized from the old parameters $c_i$ by minimizing the distance between the fine and coarse splines: $\{c'_j\} = \arg\min_{\{c'_j\}} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p(x)} \left( \sum_{j=0}^{G_2+k-1} c'_j B'_j(x) - \sum_{i=0}^{G_1+k-1} c_i B_i(x) \right)^2$, which is implemented via least squares (Section 2.4, Eq. 2.16). Grid extension is performed for all splines in a KAN independently. In practice, the number of grid points starts at $G=3$ and increases to higher values every 200 LBFGS steps, ending at $G=1000$ in the toy experiments (Section 2.4). This produces staircase-like loss curves where loss suddenly drops after each grid refinement then plateaus (Section 2.4, Figure 2.3 top). The training time scales favorably with grid size $G$ up to a point, though at $G=1000$ training becomes significantly slower due to LBFGS line search issues on bad loss landscapes (Section 2.4, footnote 4).

**Sparsification and Regularization**

To make KANs interpretable, the paper introduces sparsification techniques that prune unimportant connections, analogous to L1 regularization in MLPs but adapted for function-valued parameters. Since KANs have no linear weights, the L1 norm must be defined on activation functions themselves. The L1 norm of an activation function $\phi$ is defined as its average magnitude over $N_p$ inputs: $|\phi|_1 \equiv \frac{1}{N_p} \sum_{s=1}^{N_p} |\phi(x^{(s)})|$ (Section 2.5.1, Eq. 2.17). For a KAN layer $\Phi$ with $n_{\text{in}}$ inputs and $n_{\text{out}}$ outputs, the L1 norm is the sum over all activation functions: $|\Phi|_1 \equiv \sum_{i=1}^{n_{\text{in}}} \sum_{j=1}^{n_{\text{out}}} |\phi_{i,j}|_1$ (Section 2.5.1, Eq. 2.18). The authors found L1 regularization alone insufficient for KAN sparsification, so they add an entropy regularization term defined as $S(\Phi) \equiv -\sum_{i=1}^{n_{\text{in}}} \sum_{j=1}^{n_{\text{out}}} \frac{|\phi_{i,j}|_1}{|\Phi|_1} \log\left(\frac{|\phi_{i,j}|_1}{|\Phi|_1}\right)$ (Section 2.5.1, Eq. 2.19). The total training objective combines prediction loss with both regularization terms: $\ell_{\text{total}} = \ell_{\text{pred}} + \lambda \left( \mu_1 \sum_{l=0}^{L-1} |\Phi_l|_1 + \mu_2 \sum_{l=0}^{L-1} S(\Phi_l) \right)$ where $\mu_1, \mu_2$ are relative magnitudes usually set to $\mu_1 = \mu_2 = 1$, and $\lambda$ controls overall regularization magnitude (Section 2.5.1, Eq. 2.20). For visualization, the transparency of each activation function $\phi_{l,i,j}$ is set proportional to $\tanh(\beta A_{l,i,j})$ where $\beta = 3$, so functions with small magnitude appear faded (Section 2.5.1).

**Pruning and Symbolification**

After training with sparsification penalties, KANs can be pruned to smaller subnetworks for improved interpretability. Pruning is performed at the node level rather than the edge level, which is a design choice that preserves the structural integrity of the computation graph. For each node (the $i$-th neuron in layer $l$), an incoming score is defined as $I_{l,i} = \max_k(|\phi_{l-1,i,k}|_1)$ and an outgoing score as $O_{l,i} = \max_j(|\phi_{l+1,j,i}|_1)$ (Section 2.5.1, Eq. 2.21). A node is considered important if both incoming and outgoing scores exceed a threshold hyperparameter $\theta = 10^{-2}$ by default, and all unimportant neurons are pruned (Section 2.5.1). After pruning, users can interact with the KAN through symbolification, where suspected symbolic activation functions (e.g., $\sin$, $\log$, $\exp$) are set to exact symbolic forms using the `fix_symbolic(l,i,j,f)` interface (Section 2.5.1). However, simply setting an activation to a symbolic formula is insufficient because inputs and outputs may have shifts and scalings, so the system fits affine parameters $(a, b, c, d)$ such that $y \approx c f(ax + b) + d$ using iterative grid search for $a, b$ and linear regression (Section 2.5.1). If users lack domain knowledge about which symbolic functions to use, a `suggest_symbolic` function provides candidates based on matching the numerical values of learned activations (Section 2.5.2, Table 6 in Appendix A). After symbolifying all activations, only the affine parameters remain trainable, and continued training should drive loss to machine precision if the correct symbolic form was identified (Section 2.5.2).

**Parameter Count and Computational Considerations**

Understanding the parameter efficiency of KANs requires careful analysis because the parameterization differs fundamentally from MLPs. For a KAN with depth $L$, equal layer widths $n_0 = n_1 = \cdots = n_L = N$, spline order $k$ (usually $k=3$), and $G$ intervals per spline, the total parameter count is $O(N^2 L (G + k)) \sim O(N^2 L G)$ (Section 2.2). In contrast, an MLP with the same depth and width requires only $O(N^2 L)$ parameters, which appears more efficient at first glance (Section 2.2). However, KANs typically require much smaller widths $N$ than MLPs to achieve comparable accuracy, which not only saves parameters but also achieves better generalization and facilitates interpretability (Section 2.2). For 1D problems, one can take $N = L = 1$ and the KAN reduces to a simple spline approximation (Section 2.2). The paper acknowledges that KAN implementations are typically 10x slower to train than MLPs given the same number of parameters, primarily because different activation functions cannot leverage batch computation as effectively as MLPs where the same activation is applied everywhere (Section 3.4, Section 6). This is framed as an engineering problem rather than a fundamental limitation, and the authors suggest future work could explore grouping activation functions into "multi-head" configurations where members within a group share the same function (Section 6).

**Theoretical Guarantees: Approximation and Scaling Laws**

The paper provides theoretical justification for why KANs should outperform MLPs on functions with compositional structure. Theorem 2.1 (Approximation theory, KAT) states that if a function $f(\mathbf{x})$ admits a KAN representation where each $\Phi_{l,i,j}$ is $(k+1)$-times continuously differentiable, then there exist $k$-th order B-spline functions such that the approximation error in $C^m$-norm scales as $\|f - (\Phi^G_{L-1} \circ \cdots \circ \Phi^G_0)\mathbf{x}\|_{C^m} \leq C G^{-k-1+m}$ where $G$ is the grid size (Section 2.3, Eq. 2.15). For $m=0$ (the $L^\infty$ norm), this gives a scaling exponent of $k+1$, and with cubic splines ($k=3$), the theoretical exponent is $\alpha = 4$ (Section 2.3). This is compared against neural scaling law theories for MLPs: Sharma & Kaplan suggest $\alpha = (k+1)/d$ which suffers from the curse of dimensionality, while Michaud et al. found $\alpha = (k+1)/d^* = (k+1)/2$ where $d^*=2$ is the maximum arity of operations (Section 2.3). The KAN bound of $\alpha = k+1 = 4$ is the largest among these theories, and the paper claims this bound can be achieved empirically on synthetic datasets while MLPs struggle to saturate even slower bounds (Section 2.3, Section 3.1). The key insight is that KANs decompose high-dimensional functions into 1D functions, avoiding the curse of dimensionality as long as smooth Kolmogorov-Arnold representations exist (Section 2.3). However, the constant $C$ in the bound depends on the representation and thus on dimension, which the authors leave for future work (Section 2.3).

**External vs. Internal Degrees of Freedom**

A conceptual contribution of this paper is distinguishing between external and internal degrees of freedom in neural architectures. External degrees of freedom refer to the computational graph structure—how nodes are connected—which is responsible for learning compositional structures of multiple variables (Section 2.4). Internal degrees of freedom refer to the grid points inside activation functions, which are responsible for learning univariate functions accurately (Section 2.4). KANs benefit from having both: external degrees of freedom (which MLPs have but pure splines do not) enable feature learning and compositional structure discovery, while internal degrees of freedom (which splines have but MLPs do not) enable accurate approximation of univariate functions (Section 2.4). This distinction explains why KANs can outperform both pure splines (which fail on high-dimensional problems due to curse of dimensionality) and MLPs (which are inefficient at approximating specific univariate functions like $\sin$ or $\exp$ with ReLU activations) (Section 1, Section 2.4).

**Continual Learning via Locality**

The paper demonstrates that KANs can avoid catastrophic forgetting in continual learning scenarios, leveraging the locality of spline basis functions as the key mechanism. When a spline basis is local, a training sample only affects a few nearby spline coefficients, leaving far-away coefficients intact (Section 3.5). This is contrasted with MLPs that use global activations like ReLU, Tanh, or SiLU, where any local change may propagate uncontrollably to regions far away, destroying previously stored information (Section 3.5). In a toy 1D regression task with 5 Gaussian peaks presented sequentially, KANs remodel only regions where current-phase data is present while leaving previous regions unchanged, whereas MLPs remodel the whole region after seeing new data (Section 3.5, Figure 3.4). The authors note this is a preliminary result on a simple example, and it remains unclear whether the method generalizes to high-dimensional cases where defining "locality" is less straightforward (Section 3.5).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the paper's most significant contributions, distinguishing between fundamental architectural shifts and methodological innovations. While Section 3 detailed the mechanics of KANs, here we analyze *why* these design choices matter for the broader landscape of machine learning and scientific discovery. We identify four core insights that separate KANs from prior art.

### 4.1 Fundamental Innovation: Learnable Activations on Edges (No Linear Weights)

The most radical departure in this work is the complete removal of linear weight matrices. In 70 years of neural network research, the standard paradigm has been $\text{output} = \sigma(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{x})$, where $\mathbf{W}$ is learnable and $\sigma$ is fixed. KANs invert this: $\text{output} = \sum \phi(\mathbf{x})$, where $\phi$ is learnable and there is no $\mathbf{W}$ (Figure 0.1).

*   **Difference from Prior Work:** Previous attempts to use learnable activations (e.g., Parametric ReLU, Swish) kept them on *nodes* and retained linear weights on edges. Prior Kolmogorov-Arnold networks (Section 5) were restricted to shallow, fixed-width architectures that could not be trained with modern backpropagation. KANs are the first to successfully stack learnable activation functions into deep networks without linear projections.
*   **Significance:** This design decouples **structure learning** from **function learning**.
    *   **External Degrees of Freedom:** The network topology (which nodes connect) learns the compositional structure of the data (e.g., that $x_1$ interacts with $x_2$).
    *   **Internal Degrees of Freedom:** The spline grids learn the precise shape of the univariate relationships (e.g., that the interaction is sinusoidal).
    *   **Implication:** This separation allows KANs to be simultaneously accurate (via splines) and interpretable (via graph structure), whereas MLPs conflate these tasks into dense weight matrices that are hard to disentangle. This is not an incremental tweak; it is a new primitive for neural architecture design.

### 4.2 Methodological Innovation: Grid Extension as Progressive Resolution

KANs introduce a unique training dynamic called **grid extension**, where a model's accuracy is improved by refining the resolution of its spline grids during training, rather than by changing width or depth (Section 2.4).

*   **Difference from Prior Work:** In MLPs, improving accuracy typically requires training a new, larger model from scratch (e.g., doubling the width). This is computationally expensive and discontinuous. KANs allow a single model to evolve from coarse to fine resolution.
*   **Significance:** This transforms model scaling into a **curriculum learning** problem.
    *   **Optimization Landscape:** By starting with coarse grids (few parameters), the model finds the global structure of the function easily. As grids refine, it focuses on local details. This explains the "staircase" loss curves observed in Figure 2.3, where loss drops sharply after each extension.
    *   **Efficiency:** It decouples architecture search from resolution search. A user can train a small KAN and extend it only if needed, rather than guessing the correct width $N$ upfront. This is particularly valuable for scientific tasks where the underlying function might be smooth but requires high precision in specific regions.

### 4.3 Capability Innovation: Human-in-the-Loop Symbolic Discovery

The paper positions KANs not as automated solvers but as **collaborative tools** for scientific discovery. This is exemplified by the interpretability pipeline (Section 2.5) which allows users to prune, snap, and refine activation functions into symbolic forms.

*   **Difference from Prior Work:** Traditional **Symbolic Regression (SR)** methods (e.g., AI Feynman, PySR) are brittle: they either find the exact formula or fail completely, offering no intermediate insight. They also struggle if the true function involves special functions (e.g., Bessel functions) not in their predefined library. KANs, however, can approximate *any* function numerically via splines first, then allow humans to guide the symbolic conversion.
*   **Significance:** This enables discovery in regimes where automation fails.
    *   **Case Study:** In the Modified Aubry-André Model (MAAM) experiment (Section 4.4), automated symbolic regression produced a complex, unreadable formula (Table 5, "KAN auto"). However, by manually guiding the KAN (hypothesizing that certain activations should be $\cosh$), the user derived a simpler, physically meaningful formula with comparable accuracy (Table 5, "KAN man").
    *   **Paradigm Shift:** This moves AI from "Oracle" (input data, output answer) to "Collaborator" (input data, output hypothesis + visualization). The user can "stare at the brain" of the network (Figure 4.6) and perform surgery, a level of control unavailable in black-box MLPs or rigid SR tools.

### 4.4 Theoretical Insight: Scaling Laws for Compositional Science

The paper provides a theoretical justification for why KANs are specifically suited for **AI + Science**, based on neural scaling laws (Section 2.3).

*   **Difference from Prior Work:** Standard neural scaling laws for MLPs suggest the test loss exponent $\alpha$ depends on input dimension $d$ (e.g., $\alpha \approx (k+1)/d$), implying a Curse of Dimensionality. KANs theoretically achieve $\alpha = k+1$ (e.g., $\alpha=4$ for cubic splines), independent of $d$, *provided* the function admits a smooth Kolmogorov-Arnold representation.
*   **Significance:** This validates the hypothesis that **scientific laws are compositionally sparse**.
    *   **Empirical Evidence:** In Figure 3.1, KANs saturate the $\alpha=4$ bound on synthetic scientific functions, while MLPs plateau at much slower rates.
    *   **Implication:** This suggests KANs are not necessarily better for *all* machine learning tasks (e.g., unstructured image data where KAT representations may not exist), but are fundamentally superior for tasks involving physical laws, differential equations, and mathematical relations where compositional structure is inherent. It reframes the choice of architecture as a choice about the underlying data manifold: use MLPs for unstructured data, use KANs for structured scientific laws.

### 4.5 Summary of Contributions

| Contribution | Type | Prior State | KAN Innovation | Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Edge Activations** | Fundamental | Fixed activations on nodes; linear weights on edges. | Learnable spline activations on edges; no linear weights. | Enables simultaneous structure and function learning; improves interpretability. |
| **Grid Extension** | Methodological | Retrain larger models from scratch to scale. | Refine spline grids on trained model. | Coarse-to-fine optimization; efficient accuracy scaling (Figure 2.3). |
| **Interactive Discovery** | Capability | Brittle Symbolic Regression; Black-box MLPs. | Prune $\to$ Snap $\to$ Refine workflow. | Human-in-the-loop discovery; handles special functions (Table 5). |
| **Scaling Laws** | Theoretical | $\alpha$ depends on dimension $d$ (MLP). | $\alpha = k+1$ independent of $d$ (for compositional functions). | Justifies KANs for Science; beats Curse of Dimensionality (Figure 3.1). |

In summary, KANs are not merely a new activation function; they represent a rethinking of the neural network primitive itself. By aligning the architecture with the mathematical structure of scientific laws (compositional, smooth univariate functions), they unlock capabilities in interpretability and efficiency that MLPs cannot match, albeit at the cost of training speed on current hardware.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the experimental methodology and results presented in the paper. We analyze whether the empirical evidence convincingly supports the theoretical claims about KANs' accuracy, scaling laws, and interpretability. The experiments span seven distinct task categories, each designed to probe different aspects of KAN performance relative to MLP baselines.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Task Categories**

The paper evaluates KANs across seven distinct experimental categories, each targeting specific claims about the architecture:

1.  **Toy Function Fitting (Section 3.1, Figure 3.1):** Five synthetic functions with known Kolmogorov-Arnold representations:
    *   $f(x) = J_0(20x)$ (Bessel function, 1D)
    *   $f(x, y) = \exp(\sin(\pi x) + y^2)$ (2D compositional)
    *   $f(x, y) = xy$ (multiplication, 2D)
    *   $f(x_1, \cdots, x_{100}) = \exp\left(\frac{1}{100}\sum_{i=1}^{100} \sin^2\left(\frac{\pi x_i}{2}\right)\right)$ (100D high-dimensional)
    *   $f(x_1, x_2, x_3, x_4) = \exp\left(\frac{1}{2}(\sin(\pi(x_1^2 + x_2^2)) + \sin(\pi(x_3^2 + x_4^2)))\right)$ (4D compositional)

2.  **Special Functions (Section 3.2, Figure 3.2, Table 1):** Fifteen special functions from `scipy.special` including Jacobian elliptic functions (`ellipj`), incomplete elliptic integrals (`ellipkinc`, `ellipeinc`), Bessel functions (`jv`, `yv`, `kv`, `iv`), Associated Legendre functions (`lpmv`), and spherical harmonics (`sph_harm`). These test whether KANs can discover compact representations for mathematically complex functions without prior knowledge of their structure.

3.  **Feynman Dataset (Section 3.3, Table 2, Figure D.1):** Fifty-four physics equations from Feynman's textbooks with at least 2 variables. Examples include relativistic velocity addition $f(u, v) = \frac{u+v}{1+uv}$ and gravitational force laws. This tests KANs' ability to rediscover known physical laws from data alone.

4.  **PDE Solving (Section 3.4, Figure 3.3):** A Poisson equation $u_{xx} + u_{yy} = f$ on $\Omega = [-1, 1]^2$ with zero Dirichlet boundary conditions. The ground truth solution is $u = \sin(\pi x)\sin(\pi y^2)$. This evaluates KANs in the Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) framework.

5.  **Continual Learning (Section 3.5, Figure 3.4):** A 1D regression task with 5 Gaussian peaks where data around each peak is presented sequentially. This tests whether KANs avoid catastrophic forgetting via spline locality.

6.  **Knot Theory (Section 4.3, Table 3, Table 4, Figure 4.3, Figure 4.4):** Classification of knot signatures using 17 knot invariants as inputs. This is a direct comparison with DeepMind's prior work [45] on AI-assisted mathematical discovery.

7.  **Anderson Localization (Section 4.4, Table 5, Figure 4.5, Figure 4.6):** Three quasiperiodic tight-binding models (Mosaic Model, Generalized Aubry-André Model, Modified Aubry-André Model) where the task is to extract mobility edge formulas from numerical data.

**Baselines and Comparators**

The paper uses MLPs as the primary baseline across all experiments:
*   **MLP depths:** Swept over $\{2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$ layers depending on the task
*   **MLP widths:** Fixed at 5, 10, 100, or 300 depending on the experiment
*   **MLP activations:** ReLU, Tanh, and SiLU are tested (Section 3.3)
*   **DeepMind's MLP:** For knot theory, a 4-layer width-300 MLP with ~$3 \times 10^5$ parameters serves as the baseline (Table 3)

For symbolic discovery tasks, the paper implicitly compares against Symbolic Regression (SR) methods like AI Feynman [36, 37] and PySR [96], though direct numerical comparisons are not provided. The authors argue KANs offer better human-in-the-loop interaction rather than claiming superior automated discovery.

**Metrics**

*   **Test RMSE:** Primary metric for regression tasks (function fitting, PDE solving)
*   **Classification Accuracy:** Used for knot signature prediction (Table 3)
*   **Parameter Count:** Critical for comparing efficiency (Pareto frontiers in Figure 3.1, 3.2, D.1)
*   **Training Time:** Reported in seconds per step (Figure 2.3 bottom right)
*   **L2 and H1 Error:** For PDE solving, measuring solution accuracy and energy norm (Figure 3.3)

**Training Setup**

*   **Optimizer:** LBFGS for most experiments (Section 3.1, 3.2, 3.3), with Adam used for image fitting (Appendix B.3)
*   **Grid Extension:** Grid points start at $G=3$ and increase every 200 LBFGS steps to $G \in \{3, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100, 200, 500, 1000\}$ (Section 2.4, 3.1)
*   **Regularization:** Sparsification with $\lambda \in \{10^{-2}, 10^{-3}\}$, $\mu_1 = \mu_2 = 1$ for interpretability tasks (Section 3.2, 3.3)
*   **Random Seeds:** 3 seeds for most experiments; 100 seeds for unsupervised knot theory (Section 4.3)
*   **Hardware:** Not explicitly specified, but training time comparisons imply similar compute environments for KAN/MLP comparisons

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**Function Fitting: Scaling Laws (Figure 3.1)**

The most critical claim is that KANs achieve faster neural scaling laws than MLPs. Figure 3.1 plots test RMSE against number of parameters for five functions:

| Function | KAN Scaling Exponent | MLP Scaling Exponent | Theory (KAN) | Theory (MLP) |
|----------|---------------------|---------------------|--------------|--------------|
| $J_0(20x)$ (1D) | $\approx 4$ | $\approx 2$ | $\alpha = 4$ | $\alpha = 4/d = 4$ |
| $\exp(\sin(\pi x) + y^2)$ (2D) | $\approx 4$ | $\approx 2$ | $\alpha = 4$ | $\alpha = 4/d = 2$ |
| $xy$ (2D) | $\approx 4$ | $\approx 2$ | $\alpha = 4$ | $\alpha = 4/d = 2$ |
| 100D compositional | $\approx 4$ | Plateaus quickly | $\alpha = 4$ | $\alpha = 4/100 = 0.04$ |
| 4D compositional (3-layer KAN) | $\approx 4$ | $\approx 1$ | $\alpha = 4$ | $\alpha = 4/4 = 1$ |

Key observations from Figure 3.1:
*   KANs consistently achieve test RMSE $\propto N^{-4}$ (red dashed line), saturating the theoretical bound from Theorem 2.1
*   MLPs struggle to converge even at the slower black-dashed lines ($\alpha = 4/d$) and plateau quickly
*   For the 100D function, a [100, 1, 1] KAN outperforms all MLP depths (2-5 layers) by 2-3 orders of magnitude in RMSE at comparable parameter counts
*   The 3-layer KAN [4, 2, 2, 1] significantly outperforms the 2-layer KAN [4, 9, 1] on the 4D compositional function, demonstrating deeper KANs have greater expressive power

**Special Functions: Pareto Efficiency (Figure 3.2, Table 1)**

Table 1 reports minimal KAN shapes and test RMSE for 15 special functions. Key results:

Notable findings:
*   Auto-discovered KAN shapes are **smaller** than human constructions in most cases (e.g., relativistic velocity addition: human assumed 6 layers, KAN discovered 2 layers)
*   For I.29.16 and I.30.3, pruned KANs achieve **100x lower loss** than human-constructed KANs, suggesting humans may not find optimal KA representations
*   MLPs perform comparably on average to KANs for Feynman equations, which the authors attribute to these functions being "too simple" with smooth/monotonic variable dependence (Section 3.3)

**PDE Solving: Accuracy and Efficiency (Figure 3.3)**

Figure 3.3 compares KANs and MLPs on the Poisson equation:

| Model | Parameters | L2 Error Squared | H1 Error Squared |
|-------|------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| KAN [2,10,1] | ~$10^2$ | $10^{-7}$ | $10^{-4}$ |
| MLP [2,100,100,100,1] | ~$10^4$ | $10^{-5}$ | $10^{-2}$ |

Key results:
*   A 2-layer width-10 KAN is **100x more accurate** than a 4-layer width-100 MLP ($10^{-7}$ vs. $10^{-5}$ MSE)
*   The KAN uses **100x fewer parameters** ($10^2$ vs. $10^4$)
*   KANs achieve steeper scaling laws ($N^{-4}$) compared to MLPs which plateau

However, the authors note KANs are **10x slower to train** than MLPs given the same parameter count (Section 3.4, Section 6).

**Knot Theory: Accuracy with Minimal Parameters (Table 3)**

This is one of the most compelling results for AI + Science applications:

| Method | Architecture | Parameters | Accuracy |
|--------|-------------|------------|----------|
| DeepMind's MLP | 4-layer, width-300 | $3 \times 10^5$ | 78.0% |
| KAN | 2-layer [17, 1, 14] | $2 \times 10^2$ | **81.6%** |

The KAN achieves **3.6% higher accuracy** with **1500x fewer parameters**. This is extraordinary parameter efficiency. Additionally:
*   Using only $\mu_r$ (real part of meridinal distance) achieves 65.0% accuracy
*   Using only $\mu_i$ (imaginary part) achieves 43.8% accuracy
*   This contradicts DeepMind's finding that $\mu_i$ was more important, suggesting KANs may reveal different insights (Section 4.3)

Table 4 shows symbolic formulas discovered by KANs. Formula D ([3, 2, 1] KAN) achieves 84.0% accuracy with $R^2 = 0.947$ correlation with signature, nearly matching DeepMind's human-discovered formula A ($R^2 = 0.946$).

Table 5 shows mobility edge formulas for GAAM and MAAM models:

| System | Method | Formula Complexity | Accuracy |
|--------|--------|-------------------|----------|
| GAAM | Theory | $\alpha E + 2\lambda - 2 = 0$ | 99.2% |
| GAAM | KAN auto | Complex polynomial | 99.0% |
| MAAM | Theory | $E + \exp(p) - \lambda \cosh p = 0$ | 98.6% |
| MAAM | KAN auto | Very complex (see Table 5) | 97.1% |

The key insight is the **trade-off knob**: more manual intervention produces simpler formulas with slight accuracy sacrifice. This demonstrates the "collaborator" paradigm — the human provides inductive biases (e.g., "activation should be $\cosh$"), and KAN tests hypotheses quickly.

**Continual Learning: Catastrophic Forgetting (Figure 3.4)**

Figure 3.4 shows a qualitative but compelling result:
*   KANs perfectly preserve previously learned Gaussian peaks when training on new peaks
*   MLPs exhibit severe catastrophic forgetting, remodeling the entire function after each phase

This is attributed to spline locality: training samples only affect nearby spline coefficients (Section 3.5). However, the authors explicitly note this is a **preliminary result on an extremely simple 1D example**, and generalization to high-dimensional cases is unclear.

### 5.3 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

**Claims Strongly Supported**

1.  **Faster Scaling Laws for Compositional Functions:** Figure 3.1 provides convincing evidence that KANs achieve $\alpha \approx 4$ scaling on functions with known KA representations, while MLPs plateau at slower rates. This is consistent across all five toy functions.

2.  **Parameter Efficiency:** Table 3 (knot theory) and Figure 3.3 (PDE) show dramatic parameter efficiency gains. The 1500x parameter reduction with 3.6% accuracy improvement on knot signatures is particularly compelling.

3.  **Interpretability via Visualization:** Figures 4.1, 4.3, 4.4, and 4.6 demonstrate that KANs can be visualized and pruned to reveal computational structure. The knot theory unsupervised mode (Figure 4.4) rediscovered three known mathematical relations without manual input/output partitioning.

4.  **Human-in-the-Loop Discovery:** Table 5 shows that manual intervention can simplify KAN-discovered formulas while maintaining acceptable accuracy. This validates the "collaborator" paradigm over fully automated symbolic regression.

**Claims with Mixed or Conditional Support**

1.  **General Superiority Over MLPs:** The Feynman dataset results (Table 2) show MLPs and KANs perform **comparably on average**. The authors conjecture these functions are "too simple" with smooth/monotonic dependence (Section 3.3). This suggests KANs' advantages are **task-dependent** — they excel on compositional, oscillatory functions (special functions, PDEs) but may not dominate on simpler regression tasks.

2.  **Curse of Dimensionality:** While the 100D function in Figure 3.1 shows KANs outperforming MLPs, the authors acknowledge the constant $C$ in Theorem 2.1 depends on dimension (Section 2.3). The theory guarantees scaling exponent independence from $d$, but not constant independence. High-dimensional empirical validation beyond the 100D synthetic function is limited.

3.  **Continual Learning:** Figure 3.4 is qualitative and on a 1D task. The authors explicitly state "it remains unclear whether our method can generalize to more realistic setups, especially in high-dimensional cases where it is unclear how to define 'locality'" (Section 3.5). This claim needs further validation.

**Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks**

The paper includes several ablation-style analyses:

1.  **Grid Size Effects (Figure 2.3):** Test RMSE follows a U-shape with grid size $G$, with optimal performance at the interpolation threshold ($G \approx 50$ for 1000 samples). This validates the bias-variance tradeoff in grid extension.

2.  **Depth Comparison (Figure 3.1, rightmost plot):** A 3-layer KAN [4, 2, 2, 1] significantly outperforms a 2-layer KAN [4, 9, 1] on the 4D compositional function, demonstrating deeper KANs have greater expressive power.

3.  **Hyperparameter Dependence (Appendix C, Figure C.1):** Entropy penalty is necessary for sparsification; without it, many duplicate activation functions remain. Random seeds affect pruned network size, and overall penalty strength $\lambda$ controls sparsity.

4.  **KAN vs. LAN (Appendix B):** Learnable Activation Networks (LANs) have learnable activations on **nodes** (like MLPs) rather than edges. LANs are less interpretable and less accurate than KANs, suggesting placing learnable activations on **edges** (not just making them learnable) is critical for KAN's advantages (Appendix B.2).

**Failure Cases and Limitations**

1.  **Training Speed:** KANs are **10x slower to train** than MLPs given the same parameter count (Section 3.4, Section 6). This is attributed to inability to batch different activation functions efficiently. The authors frame this as an engineering problem, but it is a significant practical limitation.

2.  **Grid Size Limits:** At $G=1000$, training becomes significantly slower due to LBFGS line search issues on bad loss landscapes (Section 2.4, footnote 4). This suggests grid extension has practical limits.

3.  **Boundary Effects:** The observed scaling exponent is $\approx 3$ rather than the theoretical $\alpha = 4$, attributed to boundary effects where a few samples have significantly larger errors (Section 2.4). This indicates optimization challenges remain.

4.  **Unsupervised Discovery Limitations:** In knot theory unsupervised mode, KANs rediscovered **known** relations but "have not discovered anything new yet" (Section 4.3). The good news is reliability; the bad news is no novel discoveries.

5.  **Feynman Dataset Simplicity:** The authors acknowledge Feynman equations may be too simple to showcase KAN advantages, as variable dependence is usually smooth/monotonic unlike the oscillatory behavior of special functions (Section 3.3).

### 5.4 Summary of Experimental Evidence

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Key Supporting Result | Caveats |
|-------|------------------|----------------------|---------|
| Faster scaling laws ($\alpha = 4$) | **Strong** | Figure 3.1: KANs saturate $\alpha = 4$ bound on 5 synthetic functions | Only for functions with smooth KA representations |
| Parameter efficiency | **Strong** | Table 3: 81.6% accuracy with 200 params vs. 78.0% with 300K params | Knot theory is a specific classification task |
| Interpretability | **Moderate-Strong** | Figures 4.1, 4.3, 4.4: Visual pruning reveals structure | Requires human intervention for symbolification |
| Human-KAN collaboration | **Moderate** | Table 5: Manual simplification trades 2-3% accuracy for simpler formulas | Demonstrated on physics models, not general tasks |
| Continual learning | **Weak-Preliminary** | Figure 3.4: No forgetting on 1D Gaussian peaks | Only 1D; high-dimensional generalization unclear |
| General superiority over MLPs | **Conditional** | Figure 3.2: KANs dominate on special functions; Table 2: Comparable on Feynman | Advantages depend on function complexity/structure |

**Overall Assessment:** The experiments convincingly support KANs' advantages for **AI + Science tasks** involving compositional, oscillatory, or mathematically structured functions (special functions, PDEs, physical laws). The evidence is less compelling for general machine learning tasks, and the authors are appropriately cautious about claims beyond small-scale scientific applications. The 10x training slowdown is a significant practical limitation that the paper acknowledges but does not fully address. The interpretability claims are well-supported through visualization and interaction examples, though the "discovery" of truly novel mathematical relations remains future work.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While KANs demonstrate compelling advantages for specific AI + Science tasks, the paper presents several important limitations and trade-offs that practitioners must consider before adopting this architecture. This section critically examines the assumptions underlying KANs, the scenarios where they may not provide benefits, and the open questions that remain.

### 6.1 Computational and Efficiency Constraints

**Training Speed Overhead**

The most significant practical limitation acknowledged in the paper is training speed. The authors state explicitly in Section 6:

> "Currently, the biggest bottleneck of KANs lies in its slow training. KANs are usually 10x slower than MLPs, given the same number of parameters."

This slowdown is attributed to a fundamental architectural difference: MLPs apply the **same** activation function across all neurons in a layer, enabling efficient batch computation on modern hardware (GPUs/TPUs). In contrast, KANs have **different** learnable activation functions on each edge, which cannot leverage vectorized operations as effectively (Section 6, Algorithmic aspects point 2).

The paper frames this as an "engineering problem to be improved in the future rather than a fundamental limitation," but this distinction matters for practitioners. Even if the slowdown is engineering-related, it represents a real barrier to adoption for time-sensitive applications or large-scale deployments. The authors suggest potential mitigations like grouping activation functions into "multi-head" configurations where members within a group share the same function, but this remains untested future work.

**Grid Size Practical Limits**

While grid extension theoretically allows arbitrary accuracy improvement, there are practical limits. Section 2.4, footnote 4 notes:

> "When $G = 1000$, training becomes significantly slower, which is specific to the use of the LBFGS optimizer with line search. We conjecture that the loss landscape becomes bad for $G = 1000$."

This suggests that the coarse-to-fine training curriculum has diminishing returns. The staircase loss curves in Figure 2.3 show that beyond a certain grid size, optimization becomes unstable rather than progressively improving. This limits the maximum achievable precision for a single KAN model without architectural changes.

**Memory and Parameter Overhead**

While KANs often require fewer parameters than MLPs for comparable accuracy, the parameter structure is less efficient for storage and inference. A KAN with width $N$, depth $L$, and grid size $G$ has $O(N^2 L G)$ parameters compared to MLP's $O(N^2 L)$ (Section 2.2). For large $G$, this can exceed MLP parameter counts even if $N$ is smaller. The paper does not provide detailed memory footprint comparisons or inference latency benchmarks, leaving uncertainty about deployment costs.

### 6.2 Theoretical Assumptions and Their Implications

**Dependence on Smooth Kolmogorov-Arnold Representations**

The theoretical guarantees for KANs rest on a critical assumption: the target function must admit a **smooth** Kolmogorov-Arnold representation. Section 2.3 states:

> "The constant $C$ is dependent on the representation; hence it will depend on the dimension. We will leave the discussion of the dependence of the constant on the dimension as a future work."

This is a significant caveat. Theorem 2.1 guarantees a scaling exponent $\alpha = k+1 = 4$ independent of input dimension $d$, **but only if** a smooth KA representation exists. The original Kolmogorov-Arnold theorem does not guarantee smoothness—the univariate functions can be non-smooth or even fractal (Section 2.1). The paper's optimism rests on the empirical observation that "most functions in science and daily life are often smooth and have sparse compositional structures" (Section 2.1), but this is an assumption, not a proven fact.

For tasks where smooth KA representations do not exist or are extremely complex, KANs may not achieve the promised scaling advantages. The paper provides no theoretical bounds on **which** function classes admit efficient smooth KA representations, leaving practitioners without guidance on when to expect KAN benefits.

**Constant Factor Dependence on Dimension**

Even if the scaling exponent $\alpha$ is dimension-independent, the constant factor $C$ in the approximation bound $\|f - \text{KAN}\| \leq C G^{-k-1}$ may still grow with dimension. Section 2.3 acknowledges this explicitly but defers analysis to future work. In practice, this means that while KANs may scale better asymptotically, the crossover point where they outperform MLPs could shift to impractically large model sizes for high-dimensional problems.

The 100-dimensional synthetic function in Figure 3.1 shows KAN superiority, but this is a carefully constructed compositional function. Real-world high-dimensional data (e.g., images, text embeddings) may not have the same structure, and the paper provides no empirical evidence for such domains.

### 6.3 Task-Specific Limitations

**Limited Advantage on Simple Functions**

The Feynman dataset results reveal that KANs do not universally outperform MLPs. Section 3.3 notes:

> "We find that MLPs and KANs behave comparably on average. We conjecture that the Feynman datasets are too simple to let KANs make further improvements, in the sense that variable dependence is usually smooth or monotonic, which is in contrast to the complexity of special functions which often demonstrate oscillatory behavior."

This is a critical insight: KANs excel on **complex, oscillatory, compositional** functions (special functions, PDE solutions) but show marginal gains on simpler regression tasks. Table 2 shows many Feynman equations where MLP test RMSE is comparable to or even better than KAN test RMSE. Practitioners working on standard regression or classification tasks without known compositional structure may not see significant benefits.

**No Novel Scientific Discoveries Yet**

Despite positioning KANs as tools for scientific discovery, the paper acknowledges in Section 4.3 (unsupervised knot theory):

> "The good news is that the results discovered by KANs are probably reliable; the bad news is that we have not discovered anything new yet."

KANs successfully **rediscovered** known mathematical relations in knot theory and **approximated** known mobility edge formulas in Anderson localization models. However, they have not yet led to genuinely novel mathematical or physical insights. This is not necessarily a failure—the authors frame KANs as "collaborators" rather than autonomous discoverers—but it temper expectations about immediate scientific breakthroughs.

**Continual Learning Generalization Unclear**

The continual learning results in Section 3.5 and Figure 3.4 are compelling but limited. The experiment uses a 1D regression task with 5 Gaussian peaks, where KANs avoid catastrophic forgetting while MLPs do not. However, the authors explicitly state:

> "It remains unclear whether our method can generalize to more realistic setups, especially in high-dimensional cases where it is unclear how to define 'locality'."

Spline locality is well-defined in 1D, but in high dimensions, the notion of "nearby" spline coefficients becomes ambiguous. Without evidence that the locality benefit transfers to realistic continual learning benchmarks (e.g., sequential CIFAR, language task sequences), this claimed advantage remains speculative.

### 6.4 Methodological and Experimental Gaps

**Limited Baseline Comparisons**

The paper primarily compares KANs against standard MLPs with ReLU, Tanh, and SiLU activations. However, several relevant baselines are missing:

1.  **Advanced MLP Variants:** The authors note in Section 3.1: "We have adopted the vanilla setup where both KANs and MLPs are trained with LBFGS without advanced techniques, e.g., switching between Adam and LBFGS, or boosting." Modern MLP training often uses sophisticated optimization schedules, normalization layers, and regularization techniques not tested here.

2.  **Other Interpretable Models:** For interpretability claims, comparisons with Neural Additive Models (NAMs) [70], Explainable Boosting Machines (EBMs), or other intrinsically interpretable architectures would strengthen the argument. The paper mentions these in Related Work (Section 5) but does not compare empirically.

3.  **Symbolic Regression Benchmarks:** While the paper argues KANs offer better human-in-the-loop interaction than Symbolic Regression (SR), no direct comparison on SR benchmarks (e.g., Feynman-SR, Penn Machine Learning Benchmarks) is provided. Table 5 shows KAN-derived formulas, but without SR baselines on the same tasks, the relative advantage is unclear.

**Small-Scale Focus**

All experiments in the paper are **small-scale** by modern deep learning standards:
- Maximum 100 input dimensions (synthetic function)
- Maximum ~300K parameters (DeepMind MLP baseline in knot theory)
- No large-scale language modeling, image classification, or reinforcement learning tasks

Section 6 acknowledges this: "We would also like to apply KANs to machine-learning-related tasks, which would require integrating KANs into current architectures, e.g., transformers." Until KANs are tested at scale (millions to billions of parameters) on standard ML benchmarks, their viability as general-purpose neural architectures remains unproven.

**Optimizer Dependence**

Most experiments use the LBFGS optimizer, which is uncommon in large-scale deep learning. LBFGS is a second-order method that requires storing Hessian approximations, making it memory-intensive for large models. Section 2.4, footnote 4 notes LBFGS line search issues at large grid sizes. The paper does not systematically compare KAN training with first-order optimizers (Adam, AdamW) that dominate modern deep learning, leaving uncertainty about optimizer compatibility.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Future Directions

The paper concludes Section 6 with several open questions that highlight remaining uncertainties:

**Mathematical Foundations**
- Does empirical success with deeper KANs imply a generalized Kolmogorov-Arnold theorem beyond depth-2 compositions?
- Can "Kolmogorov-Arnold depth" characterize function classes, analogous to how VC dimension or Rademacher complexity characterizes learnability?
- What is the precise dependence of the approximation constant $C$ on input dimension $d$?

**Algorithmic Improvements**
- Can activation functions be grouped ("multi-head") to recover batch computation efficiency without sacrificing expressivity?
- Are there alternative parametrizations (radial basis functions, other local kernels) that outperform B-splines?
- Can adaptive grid strategies (multigrid methods, domain-dependent basis functions) improve both accuracy and efficiency?

**Application Scope**
- Do smooth KA representations exist for language modeling, image recognition, or reinforcement learning tasks?
- Can KANs be integrated into transformers ("kansformers") effectively, and do they improve upon MLP-based transformers?
- What is the boundary between tasks where KANs excel (scientific laws, PDEs) and tasks where MLPs remain superior (unstructured data)?

**Interpretability Boundaries**
- How much human intervention is required for meaningful symbolic discovery? Can the process be further automated?
- What is the cognitive load on users interacting with KAN visualizations? Are there usability studies on scientist-KAN collaboration?
- Can KANs discover relations that are **not** expressible in standard symbolic form (e.g., special functions not in libraries)?

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks (KANs) represents more than just a new neural architecture; it proposes a structural shift in how we design models for scientific discovery. By moving learnable parameters from linear weights to activation functions on edges, KANs challenge the 70-year hegemony of the Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) primitive. This section analyzes how this work changes the landscape of AI + Science, outlines the specific research directions it enables, and provides concrete guidance on when to integrate KANs into existing workflows.

### 7.1 Shifting the Paradigm: From Oracle to Collaborator

The most profound implication of this work is a redefinition of the role of AI in scientific discovery. Traditionally, machine learning models act as **Oracles**: users input data, and the model outputs a prediction or a classification, often without解释nable reasoning. Symbolic Regression (SR) tools attempt to be **Automated Scientists**, searching for formulas autonomously, but they are often brittle—returning either success or failure without intermediate insight (Section 2.5.2).

KANs introduce a third paradigm: the **AI Collaborator**. As stated in Section 6, "The language of science is functions. KANs are composed of interpretable functions, so when a human user stares at a KAN, it is like communicating with it using the language of functions." This shifts the workflow from fully automated discovery to **Human-in-the-Loop Interaction**:

*   **Transparency:** Users can visualize the "brain" of the network (e.g., Figure 4.6) to see which variables interact and what functional forms they take.
*   **Intervention:** Users can perform "surgery" on the model—pruning nodes, snapping activations to symbolic forms (e.g., $\sin$, $\cosh$), and retraining only affine parameters (Section 2.5.2).
*   **Hypothesis Testing:** Instead of guessing a formula and testing it manually, a scientist can hypothesize a functional form (e.g., "this activation looks like $\cosh$"), set it in the KAN, and let the model verify if accuracy is maintained (Table 5, MAAM example).

This paradigm acknowledges that **human preferences for interpretability are domain-specific** and hard to codify into a loss function. By making the model interpretable by design, KANs allow scientists to inject their own inductive biases directly into the architecture during the discovery process.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

The paper identifies three primary categories of future research that KANs enable or require. These directions address the current limitations (Section 6) and expand the scope of applications.

#### 7.2.1 Mathematical Foundations: Generalizing the Theorem
The current Kolmogorov–Arnold Representation Theorem (KAT) corresponds to a specific 2-layer network with width $2n+1$ (Section 2.1). However, empirical results show that **deeper KANs** (e.g., 3-layer or 4-layer) often perform better than shallow ones (Figure 3.1, rightmost plot). This gap suggests a need for new mathematical theory:
*   **Generalized KAT:** Is there a theoretical theorem that justifies deep Kolmogorov–Arnold representations beyond depth-2 compositions?
*   **Smoothness vs. Depth:** Can we relate the smoothness of activation functions to network depth? The paper hypothesizes that some functions cannot be represented smoothly in depth-2 KAT but might be smoothly represented in depth-3 or beyond (Section 6).
*   **Function Classes:** Can we define a notion of "Kolmogorov–Arnold depth" to characterize function classes, similar to how VC dimension characterizes learnability?

#### 7.2.2 Algorithmic Improvements: Efficiency and Hybridization
The most significant bottleneck for KANs is training speed—they are typically **10x slower** than MLPs given the same parameter count (Section 6). This is due to the inability to batch different activation functions efficiently. Future work suggested in Section 6 includes:
*   **Multi-Head Activations:** Grouping activation functions into clusters where members within a group share the same function. This would interpolate between MLPs (all same) and KANs (all different), potentially recovering batch computation efficiency.
*   **Hybrid Architectures:** Investigating models that combine KAN and MLP properties. For example, Learnable Activation Networks (LANs) place learnable activations on nodes (like MLPs) rather than edges (Appendix B). Preliminary results suggest LANs are less interpretable than KANs, but further exploration of hybrid layers could yield efficiency gains.
*   **Adaptive Grids:** Using multigrid methods or domain-dependent basis functions to improve accuracy and efficiency dynamically during training (Section 6).

#### 7.2.3 Application Scope: Beyond Small-Scale Science
Currently, KANs are validated on small-scale AI + Science tasks (max ~100 input dimensions, ~300K parameters). The paper explicitly calls for integration into larger architectures:
*   **"Kansformers":** Replacing the MLP blocks in Transformer architectures with KAN layers. Since MLPs consume almost all non-embedding parameters in transformers (Section 1), replacing them with KANs could potentially improve interpretability and parameter efficiency in language models, provided the efficiency bottleneck is solved.
*   **Complex PDEs:** Extending beyond the Poisson equation (Section 3.4) to solve Navier-Stokes equations, Density Functional Theory (DFT), or other high-dimensional physics problems.
*   **Continual Learning:** Validating the preliminary continual learning results (Section 3.5) on high-dimensional benchmarks. The locality of splines suggests potential for avoiding catastrophic forgetting, but defining "locality" in high dimensions remains an open challenge.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

Based on the experimental evidence in Sections 3 and 4, KANs are particularly well-suited for specific downstream use cases where their strengths align with task requirements.

| Application Domain | Specific Use Case | Why KANs? | Evidence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Scientific Discovery** | Rediscovering physical laws (e.g., mobility edges) | Human-in-the-loop symbolification allows trading accuracy for simplicity. | Table 5 (MAAM), Section 4.4 |
| **Mathematics** | Finding relations between invariants (e.g., Knot Theory) | Unsupervised mode can discover relations without manual input/output splitting. | Figure 4.4, Section 4.3 |
| **PDE Solving** | Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) | Higher accuracy with fewer parameters; better scaling laws ($\alpha=4$). | Figure 3.3, Section 3.4 |
| **Model Reduction** | Replacing lookup tables for special functions | Compact KA representations can break high-dimensional tables into 1D functions. | Table 1, Section 3.2 |
| **Continual Learning** | Sequential task learning (low-dimensional) | Spline locality prevents catastrophic forgetting of previous tasks. | Figure 3.4, Section 3.5 |

**Note on Limitations:** For large-scale machine learning tasks (e.g., image classification on ImageNet, large language modeling), MLPs remain the preferred choice due to training speed and lack of evidence for KAN scaling in unstructured data domains (Section 6.6).

### 7.4 Integration Guidance: When to Prefer KANs

The paper provides a decision framework (Figure 6.1) to help practitioners decide between KANs and MLPs. We synthesize this into concrete integration guidance based on the trade-offs identified in Section 6.

**Prefer KANs when:**
1.  **Interpretability is Paramount:** You need to understand *why* the model made a prediction, not just the prediction itself. The ability to visualize and prune the network (Section 2.5) is unique to KANs.
2.  **Accuracy on Compositional Functions:** The task involves fitting functions with known or suspected compositional structure (e.g., physical laws, special functions). KANs saturate the $\alpha=4$ scaling law while MLPs plateau (Figure 3.1).
3.  **Parameter Efficiency is Critical:** You are working in memory-constrained environments where a 1000x reduction in parameters (e.g., 200 vs. 300,000 in Knot Theory, Table 3) outweighs training time costs.
4.  **Small-Scale Science Tasks:** The problem size is manageable (e.g., <100 dimensions, regression/PDE solving), and training time (10x slower) is acceptable for the gain in insight.

**Prefer MLPs when:**
1.  **Training Speed is Critical:** You need fast iteration cycles or are training on massive datasets where the 10x slowdown is prohibitive.
2.  **Unstructured Data:** The task involves images, text, or audio where compositional structure is less clear, and large-scale benchmarks favor MLP-based architectures (e.g., Transformers).
3.  **Simple Regression/Classification:** The relationships are smooth and monotonic (like many Feynman equations), where MLPs perform comparably to KANs but train faster (Table 2, Section 3.3).
4.  **Production Deployment:** Inference latency and batch processing efficiency are prioritized over model interpretability.

**Integration Strategy:**
For teams interested in adopting KANs, the paper suggests a **hybrid workflow**:
1.  **Start Small:** Use KANs for exploratory data analysis on small subsets of scientific data to discover potential symbolic relations (Section 4.1).
2.  **Verify:** Once a symbolic formula is discovered via KAN interaction, verify it using traditional numerical methods or simpler models.
3.  **Deploy Efficiently:** If the discovered formula is simple, hard-code it for inference. If a neural network is needed, consider distilling the KAN into a smaller MLP or using a LAN (Appendix B) for faster inference if interpretability is less critical at deployment.

In summary, KANs open a new avenue for **AI-Scientist Collaboration**, prioritizing interpretability and accuracy on structured tasks over raw training speed. While they are not a universal replacement for MLPs, they offer a powerful specialized tool for domains where understanding the model's reasoning is as valuable as its output.
