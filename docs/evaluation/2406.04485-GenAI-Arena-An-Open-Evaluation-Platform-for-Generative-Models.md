## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces GenAI-Arena, an open evaluation platform that addresses the lack of trustworthy metrics for generative AI by leveraging over 9000 community votes to rank 35 models across text-to-image, text-to-video, and image editing tasks using Elo ratings. The authors release GenAI-Bench, a preference dataset revealing that even advanced multimodal models like GPT-4o achieve only 49.19% accuracy in mimicking human judgments, while identifying state-of-the-art systems such as PlayGround V2.5 and StableVideoDiffusion. This work establishes a democratic framework for assessing model performance where automatic metrics like FID and CLIP often fail to capture user satisfaction.

## 2. Context and Motivation

The rapid advancement of generative artificial intelligence has created a paradox: while model capabilities are exploding, our ability to measure them reliably is stagnating. This section dissects the specific evaluation gap this paper addresses, explains why existing solutions are insufficient, and positions GenAI-Arena within the broader landscape of AI benchmarking.

### The Evaluation Gap in Generative AI

The core problem identified by the authors is the **absence of trustworthy evaluation metrics** for generative models, particularly in visual domains like image and video generation (Abstract). While algorithmic innovations drive progress, the tools used to verify that progress have not kept pace. 

This gap is critical for two reasons:
1.  **Subjectivity vs. Objectivity:** Generative content is inherently subjective. Qualities like "aesthetics," "naturalness," and "user satisfaction" are difficult to quantify with deterministic formulas. The Introduction notes that while traditional metrics offer specific insights, they "often fall short in providing a comprehensive assessment of overall model performance, especially when considering subjective qualities" (Section 1).
2.  **Multi-Faceted Complexity:** Visual generation is not a single-dimensional task. An output must be judged on visual quality, consistency, text alignment, absence of artifacts, and more. The authors argue that this "multi-faceted nature makes the evaluation intrinsically difficult" (Introduction). A model might score well on pixel-level similarity but fail to capture the semantic meaning of a prompt, or vice versa.

Without reliable evaluation, researchers and practitioners cannot confidently navigate the "multitude of available models," hindering both development and deployment (Section 1).

### Limitations of Prior Approaches

To understand the necessity of GenAI-Arena, we must examine why existing evaluation methods fail to solve this problem. The paper categorizes prior work into automatic metrics and evaluation platforms, both of which have significant shortcomings.

#### 1. Automatic Metrics
Standard automatic metrics rely on mathematical distances between data distributions or embedding similarities. Common examples include:
*   **FID (Fréchet Inception Distance) [20]:** Measures the distance between feature distributions of real and generated images.
*   **CLIPScore [19]:** Computes cosine similarity between image and text embeddings to measure alignment.
*   **FVD (Fréchet Video Distance) [72]:** Extends FID concepts to video coherence.

While valuable for specific technical aspects, these metrics suffer from **low correlation with human preferences** (Section 2.1). For instance, a model might optimize for FID by producing blurry but statistically average images, which humans would rate poorly. The paper highlights that "these automatic metrics still lag compared with human preferences, achieving low correlation and thus giving doubts to their reliability" (Section 2.1). Furthermore, supervised data for training better auto-metrics is "relatively scarce on the web" (Introduction), limiting the development of learned evaluators.

#### 2. Existing Benchmark Platforms
Several benchmark suites exist to systematically rank models, such as `T2I-CompBench` [24], `HEIM` [38], `VBench` [25], and `EvalCrafter` [50]. However, these platforms share a critical flaw: they rely on the **model-based evaluation metrics** described above (Section 2.2). 

Table 1 provides a direct comparison of these platforms against GenAI-Arena. Key differentiators include:
*   **Human Label Transparency:** Most existing platforms (e.g., `T2I-CompBench`, `VBench`) do not utilize human labels or make them transparent.
*   **Open/Public Voting Process:** Prior platforms typically run static benchmarks. They do not allow for an "Open/Public Voting Process" where the community actively participates (Table 1).
*   **Task Coverage:** Many platforms are siloed. `T2I-CompBench` focuses only on text-to-image, while `VBench` focuses on video. GenAI-Arena is positioned as the first to cover **text-to-image, text-guided image editing, and text-to-video** simultaneously with human voting (Table 1).

#### 3. The Arena Precedent
The concept of an "arena" where models compete via human voting is not new. `Chatbot Arena` by LMSys [13] pioneered this for large language models (LLMs), setting a standard for evaluation based on pairwise human preferences (Section 2.2). Subsequent arenas emerged for vision-language models [78] and text-to-speech models [53]. 

However, the authors note a specific void: **"There is no existing arena for generative AI models"** that handle image and video synthesis (Section 2.2). Existing arenas focus on understanding (VLMs) or speech, not the complex generative processes involved in creating visual content from scratch or editing existing media.

### Positioning GenAI-Arena

GenAI-Arena positions itself as a **complementary solution** that bridges the gap between automatic metrics and human judgment. It does not seek to replace automatic metrics entirely but offers a "democratic and accurate measure of model performance" grounded in actual user experience (Abstract).

The platform distinguishes itself through three strategic design choices relative to prior work:
1.  **Human-in-the-Loop Ranking:** Unlike `VBench` or `HEIM`, which compute scores algorithmically, GenAI-Arena utilizes collective user feedback to calculate Elo ratings. This directly addresses the correlation gap identified in Section 2.1.
2.  **Transparency:** The platform ensures "labeling transparency" through a public voting process (Section 1). Users can see the prompts and outputs, unlike closed benchmark suites where evaluation data might be private.
3.  **Benchmarking the Judges:** Uniquely, the paper leverages the collected human data to evaluate the evaluators themselves. By releasing `GenAI-Bench`, the authors test whether Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) can mimic human voting. This turns the platform into a tool for improving automatic metrics, not just ranking generative models (Figure 1, Section 1).

In summary, the motivation stems from a crisis of confidence in generative AI evaluation. Existing metrics are too abstract, and existing benchmarks are too automated. GenAI-Arena proposes a return to human preference as the ground truth, structured within a scalable, open-source arena framework that covers the full spectrum of modern generative tasks.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
This system is a crowdsourced evaluation platform that replaces abstract automatic scores with direct human pairwise comparisons to rank generative AI models. It solves the problem of unreliable metrics by collecting user votes on side-by-side model outputs and processing them through a statistical ranking engine to produce a stable, democratic leaderboard.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The platform operates as a closed-loop data pipeline consisting of four major components.
*   **Model Integration Layer:** This component hosts 35 diverse generative models across three tasks (text-to-image, image editing, text-to-video), standardizing their inference parameters to ensure fair comparison.
*   **User Interaction Interface:** This is the front-end where users submit prompts and view anonymous side-by-side outputs from two randomly selected models to cast votes.
*   **Statistical Ranking Engine:** This backend service ingests the raw vote data and applies the Bradley-Terry model to calculate Elo ratings and confidence intervals for each model.
*   **GenAI-Museum & Bench:** This storage layer caches pre-computed model outputs to reduce latency and exports cleaned preference data to evaluate Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) as automated judges.

Information flows from the User Interface (prompts) to the Model Integration Layer (generation), then back to the Interface (display), then to the Ranking Engine (votes), and finally to the GenAI-Bench dataset (analysis).

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Model Standardization:** We first explain how the authors ensure fair comparisons across 35 different models with varying architectures and inference requirements.
*   **Voting Mechanism:** We detail the user interface design and the specific voting options that capture nuanced human preferences.
*   **Ranking Mathematics:** We dissect the Elo rating system and the Bradley-Terry model, explaining why the latter is necessary for stability in large-scale data.
*   **Infrastructure Optimization:** We describe the "GenAI-Museum" caching strategy used to mitigate the high latency of diffusion model inference.
*   **Benchmark Construction:** We outline the data cleaning and MLLM evaluation pipeline that transforms user votes into the GenAI-Bench dataset.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown
This is a systems and methodology paper that constructs a live evaluation platform to rank generative models using human preference data processed through statistical models.

**System Pipeline and Data Flow**
The operational lifecycle of the platform begins when a user inputs a text prompt into the web interface.
*   The system first checks the prompt against safety filters before routing it to the inference engine.
*   The inference engine selects two models from the same task category (e.g., two text-to-image models) and generates outputs using standardized hyperparameters.
*   These two outputs are presented side-by-side to the user without revealing model identities (anonymous battle).
*   The user selects one of four voting options, which is recorded as a pairwise preference data point.
*   The backend aggregates these votes and periodically recomputes the leaderboard using the Bradley-Terry model.
*   Finally, valid votes are cleaned and released as the GenAI-Bench dataset for external research.

**Model Integration and Standardization**
To ensure that the ranking reflects model quality rather than inference tricks, the authors enforce strict standardization across all 35 integrated models.
*   The platform covers three distinct tasks: text-to-image generation (15 models), text-guided image editing (9 models), and text-to-video generation (9 models), as detailed in Tables 2, 3, and 4.
*   For text-to-image, the system includes diverse architectures such as Latent Diffusion Models (e.g., `SDXL`), Diffusion Transformers (e.g., `PixArt-α`), and Flow Matching models (e.g., `FLUX.1-dev`).
*   The authors ported highly dispersed codebases into a unified format to prevent per-instance prompt or hyper-parameter tuning that could bias results.
*   Inference hyperparameters are fixed; for example, `SDXL` runs at 50 steps with 1K×1K resolution, while `SDXL-Turbo` runs at 1 step with the same resolution.
*   For video generation, the authors built a new library called `VideoGenHub` to standardize inference procedures similar to `ImagenHub` for images.
*   Seeds are set to 42 for fair comparison in the video library, and the best hyper-parameters are selected for each model to ensure highest performance before evaluation.
*   This standardization is critical because without it, a model could artificially inflate its score by using higher resolution or more inference steps than its competitors.

**Voting Mechanism and Interface**
The core data collection mechanism is the "Anonymous Battle" interface, designed to eliminate brand bias.
*   Users are presented with two images or videos labeled only as "Model A" and "Model B" alongside the generating prompt.
*   The system provides four distinct voting options to capture nuanced judgment: "Left is better," "Right is better," "Tie," and "Both are bad."
*   These four options are explicitly mapped to numerical values for the ranking algorithm, where a win is 1, a tie is 0.5, and a loss is 0.
*   To prevent users from guessing model identities based on speed or artifacts, the system ensures model identity remains anonymous throughout the interaction.
*   Votes are discarded if the model identity is revealed during the interaction, maintaining the integrity of the blind test.
*   The platform also includes a "Direct Generation" tab and a "Random Sample" button, but only the anonymous battle votes contribute to the Elo ranking.

**Ranking Mathematics: Elo and Bradley-Terry**
The platform uses a two-stage statistical approach to convert raw votes into a stable leaderboard, moving from online updates to batch estimation.

*Stage 1: Online Elo Rating*
The authors initially consider the standard Elo rating system, which models the probability of one player beating another based on their current ratings.
*   Intuitively, if a highly-rated model beats a low-rated model, it gains few points, but if it loses, it loses many points.
*   The probability of player $i$ winning against player $j$ is calculated using a logistic function based on the rating difference.
*   Mathematically, the probability $P(Y_{ij} = 1)$ is defined as:
    $$P(Y_{ij} = 1) = \frac{1}{1 + 10^{(R_j - R_i)/\alpha}}$$
    where $R_i$ and $R_j$ are the current ratings of players $i$ and $j$, and $\alpha$ is a scaling factor set to 400.
*   After each match, the rating $R_i$ is updated using the formula:
    $$R'_i = R_i + K \times (S(i, j) - E(i, j))$$
    where $S(i, j)$ is the actual outcome (1 for win, 0.5 for tie, 0 for loss) and $E(i, j)$ is the expected probability calculated above.
*   The authors note a critical limitation here: in an online system, the order of matches significantly affects the final rating because ratings update dynamically after every vote.
*   Since the arena collects thousands of votes with varying quality, this order-dependence introduces instability.

*Stage 2: Bradley-Terry Model Estimation*
To overcome the instability of online Elo, the authors adopt the Bradley-Terry (BT) model for the final leaderboard.
*   The BT model treats the problem as a logistic regression task that estimates ratings by maximizing the likelihood of all observed pairwise comparisons simultaneously.
*   This approach is order-consistent, meaning the final ratings do not depend on the sequence in which votes were collected.
*   The log-likelihood function $L(R)$ for all pairwise comparisons is written as:
    $$L(R) = \sum_{i,j \in N, i \neq j} \left( W_{ij} \log \frac{1}{1 + 10^{(R_j - R_i)/\alpha}} \right)$$
    where $W_{ij}$ is the number of times player $i$ has won against player $j$, and $R$ represents the set of all Elo ratings.
*   The authors solve this optimization using the `LogisticRegression` model from `sklearn`.
*   A specific design choice is required for handling ties, as the standard BT model assumes binary outcomes.
*   The authors duplicate all votes containing a "tie" result, allocating half to the scenario where model $i$ wins ($Y_{ij} = 1$) and the other half to where model $j$ wins ($Y_{ij} = 0$).
*   This approximation allows the binary logistic regression solver to incorporate tie data without violating model assumptions.

**Confidence Intervals and Stability**
To quantify the uncertainty in the estimated ratings, the system computes confidence intervals using a bootstrap-like method.
*   The authors record the estimated Elo rating based on the same number of battles sampled from the previous round.
*   This sampling process continues for 100 rounds to generate a distribution of possible ratings.
*   They select the lowest sampled Elo rating as the lower bound and the highest as the upper bound of the 95% confidence interval.
*   This method, described as using "sandwich" standard errors, helps users understand if the difference between two models on the leaderboard is statistically significant or due to variance.

**Infrastructure Optimization: GenAI-Museum**
A major technical challenge is the inference latency of generative models compared to language models.
*   While auto-regressive language models can generate responses in less than a second using tools like `vLLM`, diffusion models typically take 5 to 120 seconds per inference (Table 3).
*   To prevent users from waiting excessively during battles, the authors implement "GenAI-Museum," a pre-computed data pool.
*   This museum stores various inputs from existing datasets along with each model's corresponding output generated offline.
*   When a user clicks the "Random Sample" button, the system sends a request to the museum to retrieve input and two random model outputs instantly.
*   This design saves GPU computation time, enables instant comparisons, and balances the votes for each unique input over time.
*   The input prompts are sampled from `ImagenHub` and `VBench`, and every prompt is filtered via an NSFW detector before addition.
*   The system periodically updates the input prompts with the latest collected real-world human votes to prevent bias in the prompt distribution.

**GenAI-Bench Construction and MLLM Evaluation**
The collected vote data is processed into a benchmark dataset to evaluate automated judges.
*   The authors apply `Llama Guard` as an NSFW filter to ensure the released data is appropriate for a wide audience.
*   In the text-to-image task, 4.3k anonymous votes were collected, but only 1.7k remained after filtering, with 85.6% of abandoned data removed due to sexual content.
*   For image editing, 1.1k votes were collected, resulting in 0.9k released votes, with 87.5% of unsafe inputs containing violent crimes.
*   For text-to-video, 1.2k votes were collected, resulting in 1.1k released votes, with all unsafe data due to sexual content.
*   To evaluate MLLMs, the authors prompt models like `GPT-4o` and `Gemini-1.5-Pro` to output preference labels matching the human voting options.
*   The prompt templates require the MLLM to output one of four specific labels: `[[A>B]]`, `[[B>A]]`, `[[A=B=GOOD]]`, and `[[A=B=BAD]]`.
*   For video tasks, videos are extracted into image frames and fed as an image sequence unless the model has a specific video processing unit.
*   The accuracy is computed by comparing the model's output label with the actual human preference label using exact match.
*   This setup reveals that even the best model, `GPT-4o`, achieves only 49.19% accuracy across the three tasks, highlighting the gap between automated and human evaluation.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section moves beyond the system architecture to examine the novel contributions and empirical discoveries enabled by GenAI-Arena. While building the platform is an engineering feat, the true value lies in the shifts in evaluation philosophy and the critical data-driven insights revealed about both generative models and their evaluators. We distinguish between fundamental innovations—changes in how the field approaches evaluation—and critical empirical findings that correct common misconceptions.

### 4.1 Paradigm Shift: From Automatic Metrics to Democratic Preference

The most fundamental innovation of this work is the transition from **automatic metric-based benchmarking** to **human preference-based ranking** for generative visual models. Prior platforms like `VBench` [25] or `HEIM` [38] rely on algorithmic scores (e.g., FID, CLIPScore) that, as noted in Section 2, often correlate poorly with human satisfaction.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** As detailed in **Table 1**, existing platforms lack "Human Label Transparency" and an "Open/Public Voting Process." They operate as closed boxes where models are scored against static datasets. GenAI-Arena inverts this by making the community the evaluator.
*   **Significance:** This shift addresses the "evaluation gap" identified in the Introduction. By leveraging over 9000 community votes, the platform provides a "democratic and accurate measure of model performance" (Abstract).
*   **Why It Matters:** Automatic metrics optimize for statistical similarity, not aesthetic quality. A model could achieve a low FID score by producing blurry, average images that statistically match the training distribution but are visually unappealing. Human voting captures nuanced qualities like "aesthetics" and "user satisfaction" that formulas miss. This establishes a new standard where **human preference is the ground truth**, forcing model developers to optimize for what users actually want, not just what metrics measure.

### 4.2 The "Judge Evaluation" Paradox (GenAI-Bench)

A critical, non-obvious contribution is the use of the collected human data to evaluate the evaluators themselves. While many recent papers propose using Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) as automated judges to save costs, GenAI-Arena tests this assumption rigorously via **GenAI-Bench**.

*   **The Finding:** The results challenge the growing reliance on MLLMs for evaluation. Even the most advanced closed-source model, `GPT-4o`, achieves an average accuracy of only **49.19%** across the three generative tasks when compared to human voting (**Table 7**).
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** This is barely better than random guessing (25% for 4 choices). Open-source models perform even worse; for instance, `Idefics1` achieves only **2.22%** average accuracy (**Table 7**).
*   **Why It Matters:** This is a warning to the research community. Many studies currently use MLLMs to scale up evaluation because human labeling is expensive. This paper demonstrates that **current MLLMs are not ready to replace human judges** for complex visual generation tasks. The authors attribute this to a "lack of instruction-following and reasoning ability in complex vision scenarios" (Abstract).
*   **Innovation Type:** This is a **critical empirical finding** rather than a system feature. It transforms the platform from a simple leaderboard into a diagnostic tool that quantifies the limitations of automated evaluation, preventing researchers from drawing false conclusions based on unreliable auto-metrics.

### 4.3 Structural Bias in Elo Ratings for Generative Models

While the Elo rating system (and the Bradley-Terry model discussed in Section 3) is standard in competitive gaming, applying it to generative AI reveals specific structural biases that are non-obvious without large-scale data.

*   **The Insight:** The authors discover that Elo ratings can be skewed by an imbalance between "easy games" and "hard games." **Figure 3** (Winning Fraction Heatmap) shows that while Elo generally correlates with win rates, anomalies exist. For example, `PlayGround V2.5` has the highest Elo but only achieves a **0.58** winning fraction against `PixArt-σ`, which is below 60% (**Section 4.2**).
*   **Cause of Bias:** **Figure 4** (Battle Count Heatmap) reveals that some models fight each other far more often than others. For instance, `PlayGround V2.5` battled `SDXL-Turbo` **93** times, but only around **50** times against other models.
*   **Significance:** This highlights a vulnerability in crowd-sourced ranking: if a top model frequently faces weak opponents ("easy games"), its Elo inflates faster than if it faces strong opponents. The paper notes that "a reliable and robust Elo rating requires a large amount of voting data" to mitigate this (**Section 4.2**).
*   **Innovation Type:** This is a **methodological insight**. It informs future arena designers that random pairing (the current method) is insufficient for stability. It suggests the need for "vote-aware selection systems" that balance match difficulties, a direction the authors plan to explore in future work (**Section 3.3**).

### 4.4 Unified Standardization Across Heterogeneous Tasks

Finally, the platform achieves a systems-level innovation by unifying three distinct generative tasks—text-to-image, image editing, and text-to-video—under a single evaluation framework.

*   **Differentiation:** Prior arenas are typically siloed. `Chatbot Arena` focuses on text [13], while `VBench` focuses solely on video [25]. GenAI-Arena is the "first evaluation platform with comprehensive evaluation capabilities across multiple properties" (**Section 1**).
*   **Technical Enabler:** This was only possible through the standardization efforts described in Section 3, such as the creation of `VideoGenHub` to handle diverse video model architectures (e.g., `AnimateDiff` vs. `OpenSora`).
*   **Significance:** This allows for cross-task analysis of model capabilities. For example, the leaderboards (**Table 5**) reveal that different tasks have different "winning margins." In text-to-video, the top model (`StableVideoDiffusion`) has a significantly higher Elo (**1148**) compared to the bottom models than seen in image editing. This suggests that the video generation field may have clearer performance hierarchies than the more saturated image generation market.
*   **Innovation Type:** This is an **infrastructure innovation**. By solving the interoperability problem across diverse model types (Diffusion, DiT, Flow Matching), the platform creates a sustainable ecosystem where new models can be added without rebuilding the evaluation pipeline.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence gathered through the GenAI-Arena platform. While Section 3 detailed the system architecture and Section 4 discussed the high-level insights, here we rigorously examine the experimental methodology, statistical validity, and quantitative outcomes. We assess whether the collected data supports the paper's claims regarding model ranking reliability and the limitations of automated judges.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Data Composition

The core experiment relies on pairwise human preference data collected over a seven-month period. Understanding the composition of this dataset is critical for interpreting the leaderboards.

*   **Data Collection Timeline:** Voting commenced on **February 11th, 2024**, and the analysis cutoff for this paper is **October 24th, 2024** (Abstract, Section 1).
*   **Total Volume:** The platform amassed over **9000 votes** across three tasks (Abstract).
*   **Task Distribution:** The votes are not evenly distributed, which impacts statistical power per task:
    *   **Text-to-Image:** **6300 votes** (70% of total). This task has the highest statistical confidence.
    *   **Text-to-Video:** **2024 votes** (22.5% of total).
    *   **Image Editing:** **1154 votes** (12.8% of total). This task has the lowest statistical power (Section 4.1).
*   **Safety Filtering:** To create the public `GenAI-Bench` dataset, the authors applied `Llama Guard` [29] to filter unsafe content. The filtering rates reveal significant skew in user prompts:
    *   **Text-to-Image:** **4.3k** collected $\rightarrow$ **1.7k** released. **85.6%** of abandoned data was removed due to sexual content (Section 5.1).
    *   **Image Editing:** **1.1k** collected $\rightarrow$ **0.9k** released. **87.5%** of unsafe inputs contained violent crimes (Section 5.1).
    *   **Text-to-Video:** **1.2k** collected $\rightarrow$ **1.1k** released. **100%** of unsafe data was sexual content (Section 5.1).

**Critical Observation:** The high filtration rate in text-to-image (85.6%) suggests that a significant portion of community engagement involves unsafe prompts. While necessary for safety, this reduces the effective sample size for the public benchmark by more than half, potentially introducing selection bias where the released `GenAI-Bench` represents only "safe" user intents.

### 5.2 Generative Model Leaderboard Statistics

The primary output of the arena is the Elo-ranked leaderboard (Table 5). We analyze the statistical separation between models to determine if the rankings are meaningful.

*   **Rating Scale:** The Elo system uses a scaling factor $\alpha = 400$ (Section 3.3). A difference of 400 points implies a win probability of roughly $\approx 0.91$ for the higher-rated model.
*   **Confidence Intervals (CI):** The authors compute 95% CIs using a bootstrap method with 100 rounds of sampling (Section 3.3).
    *   **Text-to-Image Top Gap:** The top model, `PlayGround V2.5` (Elo **1122**, CI **+19/-20**), overlaps with the second place, `FLUX.1-dev` (Elo **1114**, CI **+45/-42**).
    *   **Interpretation:** Since the lower bound of `PlayGround V2.5` ($1122 - 20 = 1102$) is below the upper bound of `FLUX.1-dev` ($1114 + 45 = 1159$), the difference is **not statistically significant** at the 95% level. This nuance is crucial: while `PlayGround V2.5` is ranked first, the data does not conclusively prove it is superior to `FLUX.1-dev`.
    *   **Text-to-Video Separation:** `StableVideoDiffusion` (Elo **1148**, CI **+31/-28**) shows a clearer separation from the third place `T2V-Turbo` (Elo **1085**). The gap of 63 points exceeds the CI margins, suggesting a more robust performance lead in video generation compared to image generation.

**Baseline Performance:**
*   **Average Win Rate:** Figure 5 shows the average win rate of top models against all others. None exceed **80%**.
*   **Implication:** This indicates a lack of dominance. In a mature field, a SOTA model might consistently defeat older models (>90% win rate). The <80% cap suggests that model performance is still volatile and prompt-dependent, reinforcing the claim that the community is awaiting a "ChatGPT moment" (Section 4.2).

### 5.3 Human Annotation Quality Verification

To validate the ground truth used for ranking, the authors conducted an expert review of sampled votes. This experiment assesses the noise level in the human preference data.

*   **Sampling Method:** **350 items** were randomly sampled from `GenAI-Bench` for expert review (Section 4.2).
*   **Validity Rate:** **303 votes** (86.57%) were valid. **4.86%** were flagged as NSFW, and **8.57%** had technical issues (Table 6a).
*   **Quality Labels:** Valid votes were categorized by reasonableness (Table 6b):
    *   **Clearly Reasonable:** **231 votes** (76.24%).
    *   **Vague:** **51 votes** (16.83%).
    *   **Wrong:** **21 votes** (6.93%).
*   **Aggregate Reliability:** **93.07%** of votes were either clearly reasonable or vague (i.e., defensible).

**Experimental Implication:** The **6.93%** "Wrong Vote" rate establishes a **noise floor** for the evaluation system. Even with perfect statistical modeling, the leaderboard cannot exceed ~93% accuracy relative to "expert truth" because the underlying data contains inherent human error or disagreement. This limits the precision of the Elo ratings; small differences (<7%) between models may be attributable to annotation noise rather than true performance gaps.

### 5.4 Automated Judge Benchmarking (GenAI-Bench)

The paper treats Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) as experimental subjects to test their capability as automated evaluators. This serves as a control experiment to validate the necessity of human voting.

*   **Evaluation Metric:** Accuracy is computed via **exact match** between the MLLM's output label and the human preference label (Section 5.2).
*   **Label Space:** MLLMs must choose from 4 options: `[[A>B]]`, `[[B>A]]`, `[[A=B=GOOD]]`, `[[A=B=BAD]]` (Section 5.2).
*   **Random Baseline:** With 4 choices, random guessing yields **25%**. Table 7 shows the random baseline accuracy across tasks averages **25.47%**.
*   **Model Performance:**
    *   **Top Performer:** `GPT-4o` achieved an average accuracy of **49.19%**.
        *   Breakdown: Image Gen (**45.59%**), Editing (**53.54%**), Video (**48.46%**).
    *   **Open-Source Lag:** Open-source models like `Idefics1` scored as low as **2.22%** average accuracy, barely functioning above zero (Table 7).
    *   **Closed-Source Ceiling:** Even the best model, `GPT-4o`, fails to reach **50%** agreement with humans.

**Statistical Significance:**
While `GPT-4o`'s **49.19%** is nearly double the random baseline (**25.47%**), it remains below majority agreement. This suggests that while MLLMs capture *some* signal of quality, they lack the reasoning consistency to replace human judges. The gap between **49.19%** (best machine) and **93.07%** (human reasonableness) quantifies the current "evaluation gap" numerically.

### 5.5 Statistical Robustness and Bias Analysis

The authors performed diagnostic experiments to check for systemic biases in the Elo calculation, revealing vulnerabilities in the crowd-sourced design.

*   **Battle Distribution Bias (Figure 4):**
    *   The heatmap shows uneven pairing frequencies.
    *   **Specific Case:** `PlayGround V2.5` battled `SDXL-Turbo` **93 times**, whereas battles against other models averaged around **50 times** (Section 4.2).
    *   **Consequence:** Over-representation of specific matchups can skew Elo ratings if one model is disproportionately matched against weaker or stronger opponents.
*   **Winning Fraction Anomalies (Figure 3):**
    *   Ideally, a higher Elo should correlate with a higher winning fraction against all opponents.
    *   **Observation:** `PlayGround V2.5` (Top Elo) has a winning fraction of only **0.58** against `PixArt-σ` (Section 4.2).
    *   **Interpretation:** A 0.58 win rate is low for a top-ranked model, suggesting the Elo rating may be inflated by "easy games" against lower-ranked models rather than consistent dominance over peers.
*   **Confidence Interval Width:**
    *   In Table 5, `CogVideoX-2B` has a CI of **+71/-71**, which is significantly wider than `StableVideoDiffusion` (**+31/-28**).
    *   **Reason:** This indicates higher variance in `CogVideoX-2B`'s performance or fewer consistent battles, making its ranking less reliable despite being second place.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment of Experimental Rigor

The experiments provide strong evidence for the platform's utility but reveal specific limitations that condition the results.

*   **Support for Claims:**
    *   **Claim:** "Human preference is needed because metrics fail."
    *   **Evidence:** The **49.19%** accuracy of `GPT-4o` (Table 7) strongly supports this. If automated judges cannot agree with humans half the time, they cannot serve as ground truth.
    *   **Claim:** "The platform identifies SOTA models."
    *   **Evidence:** The leaderboards (Table 5) align with community consensus (e.g., `PlayGround V2.5` and `FLUX` are widely recognized as strong). However, the overlapping Confidence Intervals suggest the *ranking order* is less certain than the *tiering* of models.
*   **Limitations and Trade-offs:**
    *   **Sample Size:** **9000 votes** sounds large, but distributed across **35 models**, the average pairwise comparison count is low. For 35 models, there are $\binom{35}{2} = 595$ possible pairs. 9000 votes / 595 pairs $\approx$ **15 votes per pair** on average. This is sparse for robust Elo estimation, explaining the wide confidence intervals.
    *   **Voting Bias:** The "Battle Count Heatmap" (Figure 4) confirms that random pairing is not perfectly uniform. Without a "vote-aware selection system" (Section 3.3), popular models may accumulate votes faster, skewing the data distribution.
    *   **Ground Truth Noise:** The **6.93%** wrong vote rate (Table 6) imposes a hard ceiling on evaluation precision. The system cannot distinguish model differences smaller than this noise floor.

In summary, the experiments convincingly demonstrate that human preference data reveals performance hierarchies that automatic metrics miss (Section 5.4). However, the statistical analysis (Section 5.2, 5.5) cautions against over-interpreting small Elo differences. The data supports the platform as a tool for identifying *tiers* of model performance rather than precise ordinal rankings.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

No evaluation framework is without constraints, and GenAI-Arena is no exception. While the platform represents a significant advance over automatic metrics, it introduces its own set of assumptions, vulnerabilities, and practical trade-offs. This section critically examines the limitations acknowledged by the authors, identifies constraints implicit in the design, and highlights open questions that remain unresolved.

### 6.1 Assumptions About Human Preference as Ground Truth

The entire GenAI-Arena framework rests on a foundational assumption: **human voting reflects reliable ground truth** for model quality. While the expert review in Table 6 shows 93.07% of votes are "clearly reasonable" or "vague," this still leaves critical gaps.

*   **Noise Floor:** The **6.93% "Wrong Vote" rate** (Table 6b) establishes an irreducible error floor. Even with perfect statistical modeling, the leaderboard cannot distinguish performance differences smaller than this noise level. For models with Elo differences within this margin, the ranking order may reflect annotation noise rather than true capability gaps.
*   **Expertise Variance:** The authors acknowledge in Appendix A.2 that "individual biases and varying levels of expertise among users can skew the results." A casual user may prioritize aesthetic appeal, while a researcher may value prompt adherence. The platform aggregates these without weighting, assuming all votes carry equal validity.
*   **Cultural and Demographic Bias:** Appendix A.2 explicitly states that "the diversity and representativeness of the user base... may not fully encapsulate the broader population's preferences." The 9000+ votes come from self-selected participants who discovered the platform, likely skewing toward technically-minded users from specific regions. Preferences for artistic style, realism, or content appropriateness vary across cultures, but the platform does not stratify or balance for these factors.

**Trade-off:** The platform trades **evaluation objectivity** for **evaluation accessibility**. By opening voting to anyone, it gains scale and transparency but sacrifices demographic representativeness and expertise consistency.

### 6.2 Statistical Limitations in Elo Estimation

The Bradley-Terry model provides more stable ratings than online Elo, but several statistical vulnerabilities remain unaddressed.

*   **Sparse Pairwise Coverage:** With **35 models** across three tasks, there are $\binom{35}{2} = 595$ possible model pairs per task. Distributing **9000 total votes** across these pairs yields approximately **15 votes per pair on average** (Section 5.6). This is sparse for robust Elo estimation, particularly for models that receive fewer battles.
*   **Battle Distribution Imbalance:** Figure 4 (Battle Count Heatmap) reveals that pairing is not uniform. For example, `PlayGround V2.5` battled `SDXL-Turbo` **93 times**, but only around **50 times** against other models (Section 4.2). This imbalance means some models' ratings are estimated with higher confidence than others.
*   **Easy vs. Hard Game Bias:** The authors note that Elo can be "biased by imbalances between 'easy games' and 'hard games'" (Section 4.2). A top model facing many weak opponents inflates its rating faster than one facing peers. Figure 3 shows `PlayGround V2.5` has only a **0.58** winning fraction against `PixArt-σ`, suggesting its high Elo may reflect favorable matchups rather than universal dominance.
*   **Confidence Interval Width:** Table 5 shows wide confidence intervals for some models. `CogVideoX-2B` has a CI of **+71/-71**, compared to `StableVideoDiffusion`'s **+31/-28**. This variance indicates that lower-ranked or less-battled models have highly uncertain positions on the leaderboard.

**Open Question:** The authors mention planning to explore a "vote-aware selection system that increases the probability of selecting less-played models" (Section 3.3), but this remains unimplemented. Without active matchmaking, the platform relies on passive crowd behavior to balance comparisons.

### 6.3 Data Collection and Safety Filtering Constraints

The NSFW filtering process, while necessary, introduces significant data loss and potential selection bias.

*   **High Filtration Rates:** Section 5.1 reports that **85.6%** of text-to-image prompts were filtered due to sexual content, reducing the released dataset from 4.3k to 1.7k votes. For image editing, **87.5%** of unsafe inputs contained violent crimes.
*   **Selection Bias:** The filtered `GenAI-Bench` dataset represents only "safe" user intents. This may skew the benchmark toward benign, everyday prompts rather than edge cases or creative challenges that push model boundaries. Models optimized for safety may perform disproportionately well on the filtered benchmark.
*   **Filter Accuracy:** The paper uses `Llama Guard` [29] for filtering but does not report the filter's false positive or false negative rates. Overly aggressive filtering could remove valid creative prompts, while under-filtering could expose benchmark users to harmful content.

**Trade-off:** The platform trades **dataset comprehensiveness** for **safety and accessibility**. The released `GenAI-Bench` is suitable for public research but does not reflect the full distribution of real-world user prompts.

### 6.4 Infrastructure and Scalability Constraints

The technical architecture introduces practical limitations on platform growth and responsiveness.

*   **Inference Latency:** Diffusion models require **5 to 120 seconds** per inference (Table 3), compared to language models that generate responses in **less than a second** using tools like `vLLM` [34] or `SGLang` [85] (Section 3.4). This latency fundamentally limits the voting throughput.
*   **Pre-computation Dependency:** The `GenAI-Museum` caching system mitigates latency but introduces a new constraint: users can only vote on pre-computed outputs for the "Random Sample" feature. This reduces the freshness of comparisons and may not reflect model updates or new prompt types.
*   **GPU Resource Costs:** The platform runs on the Hugging Face Zero GPU system [27], which provides limited free compute. Scaling to more models or higher vote volumes requires proportional increases in GPU resources, creating a cost barrier to expansion.
*   **Model Update Frequency:** The leaderboard is static as of **October 24th, 2024** (Table 5). New models released after this date are not included until manually integrated. The paper notes plans to "continue collecting human votes to update the leaderboard" (Section 6), but the update cadence is not specified.

**Open Question:** As generative models grow in size (e.g., `FLUX.1-dev` at **12B parameters**, Table 2), inference costs will increase. The platform must balance including state-of-the-art large models against maintaining reasonable voting latency and GPU budgets.

### 6.5 Task Coverage Gaps

While GenAI-Arena covers three major generative tasks, significant areas of generative AI remain unevaluated.

*   **Excluded Modalities:** The platform does not cover **text-to-3D**, **text-to-audio**, **text-to-music**, or **multimodal generation** (e.g., generating images with accompanying text). These are rapidly advancing fields with similar evaluation challenges.
*   **Editing Limitations:** The image editing task focuses on instruction-guided editing but does not include **subject-driven editing** (e.g., DreamEdit [44]), **inpainting**, or **outpainting** as separate categories. Different editing paradigms may have different quality criteria.
*   **Video Duration and Resolution:** Table 4 shows all video models generate clips of **2 to 2.5 seconds** at resolutions ranging from **256×256** to **576×1024**. Longer-form video generation (e.g., 30+ seconds) and higher resolutions (e.g., 1080p) are not evaluated, despite being active research areas.

**Trade-off:** The platform trades **task breadth** for **evaluation depth**. By focusing on three core tasks, it achieves meaningful coverage within each but cannot claim to evaluate "generative AI" comprehensively.

### 6.6 MLLM Judge Evaluation Limitations

The GenAI-Bench results revealing MLLM limitations are valuable, but the evaluation methodology has constraints.

*   **Exact Match Metric:** Accuracy is computed via **exact match** between MLLM output and human label (Section 5.2). This is a strict metric; an MLLM that provides nuanced reasoning but selects a slightly different label (e.g., "Tie" vs. "A is slightly better") is counted as entirely wrong.
*   **Prompt Template Dependence:** The MLLM performance depends on the prompt templates in Appendix A.6. Different prompting strategies (e.g., chain-of-thought, few-shot examples) might improve accuracy. The paper does not explore prompt optimization for MLLM judges.
*   **Video Frame Extraction:** For video evaluation, videos are "extracted into image frames and fed into them as an image sequence" unless the model has a specific video processing unit (Appendix A.6). This may lose temporal information critical for evaluating video coherence and motion quality.
*   **Single Benchmark Snapshot:** The MLLM evaluation is performed once on the released `GenAI-Bench` dataset. As the dataset grows and diversifies, MLLM accuracy may change. The **49.19%** figure for `GPT-4o` is a point-in-time measurement, not a stable characteristic.

**Open Question:** The authors state they "plan to develop a more robust MLLM to better approximate human ratings in GenAI-Bench" (Section 6). However, it remains unclear whether this means fine-tuning existing models on the preference data or training new evaluators from scratch.

### 6.7 Summary of Key Limitations

| Limitation Category | Specific Issue | Evidence | Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Human Ground Truth** | 6.93% wrong vote rate | Table 6b | Sets noise floor for ranking precision |
| **Statistical Robustness** | ~15 votes per model pair | Section 5.6 calculation | Wide confidence intervals for some models |
| **Battle Distribution** | Uneven pairing (93 vs. 50 battles) | Figure 4, Section 4.2 | Elo bias from easy/hard game imbalance |
| **Data Filtering** | 85.6% T2I prompts filtered | Section 5.1 | Selection bias toward safe prompts |
| **Infrastructure** | 5-120s inference latency | Table 3, Section 3.4 | Limits voting throughput and model freshness |
| **Task Coverage** | No 3D, audio, or long video | Table 2-4 | Incomplete coverage of generative AI |
| **MLLM Evaluation** | 49.19% max accuracy | Table 7 | Automated judges cannot replace humans |

### 6.8 Fair Assessment

Despite these limitations, GenAI-Arena represents a meaningful advance over prior evaluation approaches. The limitations are largely **inherent to crowd-sourced evaluation** rather than design flaws. The authors transparently acknowledge most constraints (Appendix A.2, Section 4.2, Section 6) and propose concrete directions for improvement (vote-aware selection, MLLM development, continuous data collection).

The critical trade-off is clear: **human preference provides more reliable ground truth than automatic metrics, but at significantly higher cost, lower scalability, and introduced noise.** For researchers seeking to understand model performance as users experience it, GenAI-Arena offers invaluable data. For rapid iteration or large-scale benchmarking, automatic metrics remain necessary despite their limitations.

The platform's true value may lie not in providing definitive rankings, but in establishing a **reference standard** against which faster, cheaper evaluation methods can be calibrated. The GenAI-Bench results showing MLLM limitations (Table 7) exemplify this: the arena exposes where automated evaluation fails, guiding future research toward closing that gap.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work does more than rank models; it fundamentally alters how the research community validates progress in generative AI. By establishing human preference as the primary ground truth, GenAI-Arena shifts the optimization target from statistical metrics (like FID) to user satisfaction. This section outlines how this shift reshapes the field, identifies specific research avenues opened by the platform and dataset, details practical applications for practitioners, and provides guidance on integrating these methods into future workflows.

### 7.1 Transforming the Evaluation Landscape

The most significant implication of this work is the **deprecation of automatic metrics as standalone ground truth** for generative visual models. For years, researchers have optimized models to minimize Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) or maximize CLIPScore, assuming these correlate with quality. GenAI-Arena provides empirical evidence that this assumption is flawed.

*   **The Metric-Preference Gap:** The results in `Table 7` show that even the most advanced Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs), such as `GPT-4o`, achieve only **49.19%** accuracy when mimicking human votes. If state-of-the-art AI judges cannot agree with humans half the time, automatic metrics derived from similar architectures (like CLIP-based scores) are inherently unreliable for final evaluation.
*   **Democratization of Benchmarking:** By making the voting process public and open (Table 1), the platform shifts evaluation power from closed benchmark suites to the community. This reduces the risk of "benchmark overfitting," where models are tuned specifically to pass static tests like `VBench` [25] without improving actual user experience.
*   **Standardization Precedent:** The creation of `VideoGenHub` (Section 3.2) sets a new standard for inference reproducibility. Future papers releasing video models will likely need to provide compatible inference scripts to be included in such arenas, raising the bar for code quality and reproducibility in the field.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Avenues

The release of GenAI-Arena and GenAI-Bench unlocks several concrete research directions. These are not just abstract suggestions but specific problems defined by the data collected in this study.

#### 1. Training Better Automated Judges
The **49.19%** accuracy ceiling for `GPT-4o` (Table 7) represents a clear target for improvement. Researchers can now use the released GenAI-Bench preference data to fine-tune MLLMs specifically for evaluation tasks.
*   **Research Question:** Can a model fine-tuned on the **1.7k** safe text-to-image preference pairs (Section 5.1) surpass general-purpose models like `GPT-4o`?
*   **Methodology:** Future work should explore Reward Modeling techniques where the MLLM is trained to predict the human vote (Left/Right/Tie) rather than generating free-text critiques. The prompt templates in `Appendix A.6` provide a baseline for input formatting.
*   **Challenge:** Addressing the **6.93%** "Wrong Vote" noise floor (Table 6b). Models trained on this data must be robust to human annotation errors, possibly using loss functions that account for label uncertainty.

#### 2. Active Matchmaking Algorithms
The analysis in `Section 4.2` reveals that random pairing leads to biased Elo ratings. For instance, `PlayGround V2.5` battled `SDXL-Turbo` **93 times** but only around **50 times** against other models (Figure 4).
*   **Research Question:** How can we optimize pair selection to minimize Elo confidence intervals with fewer votes?
*   **Proposed Solution:** Implement a "vote-aware selection system" as suggested in `Section 3.3`. This system would prioritize matching models with similar current Elo ratings (to maximize information gain per vote) or under-sampled models (to reduce variance).
*   **Mathematical Goal:** Minimize the variance of the Bradley-Terry estimator (Equation 3) by strategically selecting pairs $(i, j)$ that maximize the Fisher Information Matrix, rather than selecting uniformly at random.

#### 3. Expanding Task Coverage
Currently, the platform covers text-to-image, image editing, and text-to-video (Table 1). Significant generative domains remain unevaluated.
*   **Text-to-3D and Audio:** The architecture designed for `VideoGenHub` (Section 3.2) can be adapted for 3D asset generation or audio synthesis. The core challenge will be defining appropriate viewing conditions (e.g., multiple angles for 3D) within the voting interface.
*   **Long-Form Video:** Current video models are limited to **2 to 2.5 seconds** (Table 4). Future arenas must handle longer contexts, which introduces challenges in memory management and temporal consistency evaluation beyond simple frame coherence.

#### 4. User Expertise Weighting
The current system treats all votes equally, despite the **6.93%** error rate in human judgments (Table 6b).
*   **Research Question:** Can we identify "expert voters" based on consistency or alignment with ground-truth cases?
*   **Implementation:** Introduce a reliability score for each user. Votes from users with high consistency could be weighted higher in the Bradley-Terry model (Equation 3), potentially reducing the noise floor and tightening Confidence Intervals (Table 5).

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

Beyond academic research, GenAI-Arena offers tangible utility for industry practitioners and developers.

*   **Model Selection for Deployment:** Practitioners building applications (e.g., marketing tools, game assets) can use the Leaderboard (Table 5) to select models. For example, if latency is critical, `SDXL-Lightning` (4 steps) offers a competitive Elo (**1020**) compared to slower models, making it a viable choice for real-time applications despite not being the absolute top performer.
*   **Safety and Alignment Auditing:** The filtering statistics in `Section 5.1` provide insight into user behavior. The fact that **85.6%** of abandoned text-to-image prompts were sexual content indicates a high demand for safety filters. Developers can use the filtered "unsafe" distribution (without the content itself) to train better NSFW detectors that align with community standards.
*   **Feedback Loops for Developers:** Model creators can submit new versions to the arena to gauge community reception before official release. The anonymous battle format ensures feedback is based on output quality rather than brand reputation, providing unbiased validation of architectural changes (e.g., switching from Diffusion to Flow Matching).
*   **Calibration of Automatic Metrics:** Companies running internal evaluations can use GenAI-Bench to calibrate their private automatic metrics. If an internal metric correlates poorly with GenAI-Bench human labels, it signals a need to adjust the internal evaluation pipeline.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

For researchers and engineers looking to adopt or build upon this work, the following guidance outlines when and how to integrate these methods.

#### When to Use Human Arena vs. Automatic Metrics
*   **Use Automatic Metrics (FID, CLIPScore):** During early-stage training and hyperparameter tuning. These metrics are fast, cheap, and differentiable, allowing for rapid iteration.
*   **Use GenAI-Arena (Human Votes):** For final model selection, release validation, and comparing distinct architectures. Do not rely on automatic metrics for final claims of superiority, as they may not correlate with user satisfaction (Section 2.1).
*   **Use MLLM Judges (GenAI-Bench):** Only as a preliminary filter. Given the **49.19%** accuracy cap (Table 7), MLLM judges should never replace human evaluation for critical decisions but can be used to filter out obviously bad outputs before human review.

#### Integrating GenAI-Bench
The dataset is released on HuggingFace with an **MIT license** (Section 5.1), allowing commercial and research reuse.
*   **Data Loading:** The dataset includes prompts, model outputs, and human preference labels. Be aware of the filtering bias: the text-to-image subset represents only **39.5%** (1.7k/4.3k) of original collected votes due to safety filtering (Section 5.1).
*   **Evaluation Protocol:** When benchmarking new MLLM judges, adhere to the exact match metric used in `Table 7`. Use the prompt templates provided in `Appendix A.6` to ensure comparability. For video tasks, note that videos are processed as image frames unless the model supports native video input (Appendix A.6).

#### Building a Custom Arena
Researchers wishing to replicate this platform for niche domains (e.g., medical imaging generation) should follow the architectural patterns in `Section 3`:
1.  **Standardize Inference:** Create a hub similar to `VideoGenHub` (Section 3.2) to enforce fixed seeds and hyperparameters. Without this, comparison is invalid.
2.  **Implement Caching:** Use a "Museum" approach (Section 3.4) to pre-compute outputs. Diffusion models take **5 to 120 seconds** per inference (Table 3); real-time generation will bottleneck user participation.
3.  **Adopt Bradley-Terry:** Do not use simple win-rate sorting. Implement the Bradley-Terry model (Equation 3) to handle transitive relationships (e.g., A beats B, B beats C, but C beats A) and provide confidence intervals.
4.  **Safety First:** Integrate filters like `Llama Guard` [29] at the input stage. The high rate of unsafe prompts (Section 5.1) demonstrates that open platforms will attract adversarial inputs without safeguards.

#### Critical Caveats for Adoption
*   **Statistical Power:** Ensure sufficient vote density. With **35 models**, the current **9000 votes** yield sparse coverage (~15 votes per pair). For custom arenas with fewer models, ensure at least **30-50 votes per pair** to achieve narrow confidence intervals.
*   **Bias Monitoring:** Regularly check battle distribution heatmaps (like Figure 4). If certain models are over-sampled, adjust the selection algorithm to rebalance.
*   **Temporal Validity:** Leaderboards decay as new models emerge. The results in `Table 5` are valid as of **October 24th, 2024**. Continuous data collection is required to maintain relevance (Section 6).

By treating human preference as a measurable signal rather than an abstract concept, GenAI-Arena provides the infrastructure needed to align generative AI development with actual user needs. The path forward involves refining the statistical machinery behind the rankings and closing the gap between automated judges and human perception, ensuring that progress in generative AI is both measurable and meaningful.
