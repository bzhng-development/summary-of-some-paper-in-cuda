## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces Thinking augmented Pre-Training (TPT), a scalable methodology that addresses the bottleneck of limited high-quality data by augmenting existing text with automatically generated thinking trajectories to make complex tokens more learnable. Experimental results demonstrate that TPT enhances data efficiency by a factor of 3, enabling an 8B parameter model trained on 100B tokens to achieve performance comparable to LLaMA-3.1-8B trained on 15T tokens. Furthermore, for a 3B parameter model, TPT improves post-training performance by over 10% on several challenging reasoning benchmarks, validating its ability to maximize the utility of existing data across diverse training configurations.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Data Bottleneck in Large Language Model Training

To understand why this paper matters, we first need to grasp a fundamental tension in modern LLM development. The **scaling law** (Kaplan et al., 2020) established that model performance improves continuously as we increase both model size and training tokens. This principle has driven the field forward: GPT-3 trained on 300B tokens, while recent open-source models like LLaMA-3 exceed **10 trillion tokens** (Dubey et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2025).

However, a critical asymmetry has emerged:

- **Compute capacity** continues growing at an unprecedented rate and is projected to keep increasing.
- **High-quality training data** is finite — the pool of human-authored, organically generated text on the web has been largely exhausted by existing frontier models.

This creates a bottleneck: we have more compute than we have quality data to feed it. As the paper states in Section 1:

> "Consequently, as the scale of LLMs expands, the challenge of curating and leveraging high-quality data intensifies, making data engineering a central aspect of model development."

### The Core Problem: Some Tokens Are Inherently Hard to Learn

The paper identifies a more nuanced problem beyond simple data scarcity. Not all tokens are equally learnable. Consider the example in **Figure 1(b)**: the correct answer token "890" for a polynomial divisibility problem. This single token represents the output of a multi-step reasoning process involving:

1. Understanding the Remainder Theorem
2. Applying polynomial division concepts
3. Identifying divisors of 900
4. Selecting the largest valid divisor

When a model encounters this token during vanilla next-token prediction, it must learn to predict "890" directly from the preceding context. For models with limited capacity, this often results in **memorization rather than generalization** — the model may learn that "890" follows this specific problem text, but cannot apply the underlying reasoning to novel problems.

The paper articulates this challenge precisely in Section 1:

> "Some valuable tokens can be exceptionally difficult to learn in a single next-token prediction step, as they often represent the outputs of intricate, multi-step human reasoning processes."

This is the gap TPT addresses: **how do we make complex, high-value tokens more learnable without requiring more raw data?**

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

Several research directions have attempted to maximize data utility. Understanding their shortcomings clarifies TPT's contribution.

#### Data Selection and Filtering

Work by Lin et al. (2024) and Mindermann et al. (2022) proposes training models **exclusively on a subset of valuable tokens** — those that are learnable but not yet learned. Modern data engineering pipelines (Penedo et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024) employ filtering, deduplication, domain balancing, and rewriting to enrich corpus quality.

**Limitation:** These approaches select *which* data to use but do not change *how* the data is structured for learning. If a token is inherently difficult to learn via direct next-token prediction, filtering alone cannot make it more accessible.

#### Synthetic Data Generation

The Phi series (Gunasekar et al., 2023) demonstrated that textbook-like synthetic data generated by GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 can effectively train models. Rewriting approaches (Maini et al., 2024; Nguyen et al., 2025) paraphrase raw text to enhance quality.

**Limitation:** These methods generate *new* data or modify existing text, but they are **orthogonal** to TPT's approach. TPT augments existing data with reasoning traces rather than replacing or rewriting it.

#### Reasoning-Augmented Training (Closest Prior Work)

Three recent works most directly relate to TPT:

| Method | Scale | Key Limitation |
|--------|-------|----------------|
| **RPT** (Dong et al., 2025) | Not specified | Requires substantially more compute due to online rollouts and token-level training paradigm |
| **Reasoning CPT** (Ishibashi et al., 2025) | ~150M tokens | Evaluation limited to base models without post-training; MMLU-only benchmarks |
| **BoLT** (Ruan et al., 2025) | 1B model, 8B tokens | Domain-specific (math only); requires EM algorithm for iterative bootstrapping |

These methods share TPT's intuition — that generating hidden thoughts can enhance training. However, they operate at **much smaller scales** (150M to 8B tokens vs. TPT's 100B tokens) and require more complex training procedures (reinforcement learning rollouts, EM algorithms, meticulous prompt engineering).

#### Test-Time Scaling

Work on OpenAI o1 (Jaech et al., 2024) and DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025) demonstrated that allocating more **inference compute** to difficult samples — allowing models to generate long thinking trajectories before answering — improves performance on challenging reasoning tasks. This phenomenon is called **test-time scaling**.

**Key Insight:** TPT applies this principle **during training instead of inference**. As Section 1 explains:

> "The key distinction is that we apply this principle during training, allocating more training compute to challenging samples, which in turn enhances their learnability for models."

### How TPT Positions Itself

TPT distinguishes itself along four dimensions:

1. **Simplicity:** The method requires no human annotation, no constraints on document structure, and no iterative training procedures. A single prompt template generates thinking trajectories using off-the-shelf LLMs.

2. **Scale:** TPT validates at **100B tokens** across pre-training from scratch, mid-training from checkpoints, and supervised fine-tuning — substantially larger than prior reasoning-augmented training work.

3. **Breadth of Evaluation:** Unlike domain-specific prior work, TPT demonstrates improvements across mathematics, coding, and general reasoning benchmarks (10 challenging benchmarks in Table 3).

4. **Complementarity:** The paper explicitly notes that TPT is **orthogonal to** data curation pipelines and rewriting approaches. These methods can be combined — TPT transforms whatever data you have into a more learnable format.

The positioning is strategic: TPT does not claim to replace data curation or synthetic data generation. Instead, it offers a **universal augmentation layer** that makes any existing text more conducive to LLM learning by exposing the reasoning that leads to high-value tokens.

### Why This Matters Practically

The real-world impact stems from the **3× data efficiency improvement** demonstrated in Figure 1(a). For organizations training large models:

- Training on 100B tokens with TPT achieves similar performance to 300B tokens without it.
- This translates to reduced compute costs, faster iteration cycles, and the ability to train competitive models despite data scarcity.
- For mid-training scenarios (continual pre-training on existing checkpoints), TPT enables capability improvements without the cost of training from scratch.

The theoretical significance is equally important: TPT provides empirical evidence that **data format matters as much as data quantity**. By restructuring how information is presented to models — explicitly showing reasoning steps rather than just final answers — we can fundamentally change what models learn from the same underlying tokens.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a **data augmentation methodology paper** that proposes restructuring pre-training data by appending automatically generated reasoning traces to existing text, enabling models to learn complex tokens through step-by-step decomposition rather than direct next-token prediction.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

TPT is a data preprocessing pipeline that takes raw text documents and enriches them with explicit thinking trajectories before the model ever sees them during training. The solution transforms the learning problem from "predict the answer directly" into "follow the reasoning steps that lead to the answer," making difficult tokens substantially more learnable without requiring additional raw data collection.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The TPT system consists of three sequential components that operate in a clear pipeline:

1. **Thinking Trajectory Generator** (offline, one-time): An off-the-shelf LLM (e.g., DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B) receives a raw document $d$ and a fixed prompt template, then outputs a thinking trajectory $t$ enclosed in `` and `&lt;/think>` tags.

2. **Data Augmentation Module** (offline, one-time): The original document $d$ and generated trajectory $t$ are concatenated into a single training sample $x = [d; t]$, effectively expanding the token count per document.

3. **Standard Next-Token Prediction Trainer** (online, during training): The target LLM is trained on the augmented dataset using the conventional next-token prediction loss, with no modifications to the training objective or architecture.

Information flows unidirectionally: raw documents enter the generator, thinking-augmented samples exit the augmentation module, and the target model consumes these samples during standard pre-training or mid-training. Critically, the thinking generation is **offline** — it does not require online rollouts or reinforcement learning during the target model's training, which is what makes TPT scalable to 100B tokens.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Core Augmentation Mechanism

The fundamental operation of TPT is straightforward: for every document $d$ in the pre-training corpus, we generate an associated thinking trajectory $t$ and concatenate them into a single training sample. The paper defines this augmentation formally in Section 2:

> "Given a document $d$ from the pre-training dataset, a thinking trajectory $t$ is generated using an off-the-shelf model with the specified prompt, where the placeholder `{{CONTEXT}}` is replaced by the document text. The original document and the generated thinking trajectory are concatenated to form the augmented training sample $x = [d; t]$."

This concatenation is not merely appending text — it fundamentally changes what the model learns. Consider the mathematical example from **Figure 1(b)**: the raw document states "The largest positive integer $n$ for which $n^3 + 100$ is divisible by $n + 10$ is 890." In vanilla pre-training, the model must learn to predict "890" directly from the problem statement, which requires internalizing multiple reasoning steps implicitly. With TPT, the model first sees the full thinking trajectory that walks through the Remainder Theorem, computes $f(-10) = -900$, identifies that $n + 10$ must divide 900, and finally concludes $n = 890$. The token "890" is now the endpoint of an explicit reasoning chain rather than an opaque jump from problem to answer.

The prompt template used for thinking trajectory generation is fixed and appears in Section 2:

```
{{CONTEXT}}
## End of the context
Simulate an expert's in-depth thought process as they analyze the above
context, focusing on complex and informative aspects.
Skip trivial
details.
Use Feynman technique whenever possible to ensure a deep
understanding.
```

This prompt instructs the generator to produce reasoning that mirrors how an expert would analyze the text, explicitly skipping trivial details and employing the Feynman technique (explaining concepts in simple terms to verify understanding). The placeholder `{{CONTEXT}}` is replaced with the actual document text, truncated to a maximum of 2,000 tokens as specified in Appendix A.1.

#### Training Objective and Mathematical Formulation

TPT does not modify the standard next-token prediction objective — this is a deliberate design choice that enables scalability. The training loss is:

$$\mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \log p(x_i | x_{&lt;i})$$

where:
- $\mathcal{L}$ is the average negative log-likelihood loss over the sample
- $N$ is the total number of tokens in the augmented sample $x$
- $x_i$ is the token at position $i$ in the concatenated sample
- $x_{&lt;i}$ denotes all tokens preceding position $i$
- $p(x_i | x_{&lt;i})$ is the model's predicted probability for token $x_i$ given all previous tokens

In plain language, this equation states: for each token position in the augmented sample, compute the logarithm of the probability the model assigns to the correct token, sum these log-probabilities across all positions, negate the sum, and divide by the total token count to get an average loss. The model is trained to minimize this loss using standard gradient descent.

The critical insight is that **no modification to the loss function is required**. Unlike RPT (Dong et al., 2025), which employs reinforcement learning with online rollouts and token-level training, TPT leverages the fact that the thinking trajectory itself becomes part of the training sequence. When the model processes $x = [d; t]$, it learns to:
1. Predict tokens within the thinking trajectory (learning reasoning patterns)
2. Predict the final answer token after seeing the complete reasoning chain (learning to connect reasoning to conclusions)

This is fundamentally different from methods that require the model to generate thinking during inference or that use separate loss terms for reasoning vs. answer tokens. TPT treats the entire augmented sample uniformly — the model simply learns to predict the next token, whether that token is part of the reasoning trace or the final answer.

#### Thinking Trajectory Generation Process

The offline generation of thinking trajectories is where TPT incurs its primary computational cost, but this cost is amortized across the entire training run. Appendix A.1 provides precise specifications:

- **Input document length**: Truncated to a maximum of 2,000 tokens. This constraint ensures the generator model can process the full context without excessive memory requirements.
- **Maximum generated thinking tokens**: 8,000 tokens per document. This upper bound prevents runaway generation while allowing sufficient space for detailed reasoning.
- **Generation hyperparameters**: Temperature of 0.6 and top-p of 0.9. These values balance diversity (avoiding identical outputs for similar inputs) with coherence (preventing nonsensical reasoning traces).
- **Termination condition**: Generation stops at the `</think>` tag. The paper explicitly states: "We do not generate beyond the end of thinking tag as the final response is often the summary of the thinking process without introducing new information."

The choice of generator model depends on the training scenario:

| Training Scenario | Generator Model | Rationale |
|-------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Mid-training | DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B | Has open-source post-training recipe (OpenR1), enabling fair comparisons with baselines |
| Pre-training from scratch | Qwen3-8B | Stronger model for generating high-quality trajectories when training from random initialization |

The computational cost for generating sufficient data to train on 100B tokens is approximately **20,000 A100 GPU hours**. While this is substantial, it is a one-time offline cost that does not scale with the number of training epochs or the target model size. Once the augmented dataset is created, it can be reused for multiple training runs, different model architectures, or ablation studies.

An important implementation detail: the generator model does not need to be stronger than the target model being trained. In fact, the ablation study in Table 4 reveals a counterintuitive finding — using a **smaller model** (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B) for thinking generation **outperforms** the default 7B generator on most benchmarks. The paper notes this aligns with observations from OpenThoughts (Guha et al., 2025) and suggests that smaller models may generate trajectories better suited for downstream learning, though the exact mechanism warrants further investigation. This finding has practical implications: organizations can use relatively modest generator models to create training data for much larger target models.

#### Training Configurations and Hyperparameters

TPT is validated across three distinct training configurations, each with specific hyperparameters documented in **Table 5** of Appendix A.1.

**Pre-training from Scratch (8B Model):**
- **Batch size**: 4 million tokens per batch
- **Learning rate**: $3 \times 10^{-4}$ with a constant schedule (no decay)
- **Number of GPUs**: 32 MI300 GPUs
- **Maximum sequence length**: 8,000 tokens (documents are packed to fill this length)
- **Maximum training steps**: 25,000 steps
- **Total training tokens**: 100 billion tokens
- **Training duration**: Approximately 1 week

The constant learning rate schedule is notable — many pre-training runs use cosine decay, but TPT employs a constant rate. This choice is not explicitly justified in the paper, but it may relate to the fact that the augmented data has different statistical properties than raw text, and a constant rate provides stable learning throughout.

**Mid-training (Continual Pre-training from Checkpoints):**
- **Model sizes**: 1.5B, 3B, and 7B parameters
- **Batch size**: 8 million tokens per batch (double the pre-training batch size)
- **Learning rates**: $6 \times 10^{-5}$ (1.5B), $4 \times 10^{-5}$ (3B), $3 \times 10^{-5}$ (7B) — smaller models use higher learning rates
- **Learning rate schedule**: Cosine decay (unlike pre-training's constant schedule)
- **Number of GPUs**: 16 (1.5B), 32 (3B), 32 (7B)
- **Maximum sequence length**: 8,000 tokens
- **Maximum training steps**: 12,500 steps
- **Token budgets evaluated**: 0B (direct SFT), 40B, and 100B tokens

The mid-training experiments initialize from existing checkpoints: Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B, LLaMA-3.2-3B, and Qwen2.5-Math-7B. This is critical — mid-training allows organizations to enhance existing models without the cost of training from scratch. The results in Table 3 show that mid-training with TPT substantially outperforms direct supervised fine-tuning, particularly on mathematical reasoning benchmarks.

**Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) Post-training:**
- **Dataset**: Mixture-of-Thoughts (350,000 samples distilled from DeepSeek-R1)
- **Batch size**: 1 million tokens per batch (smaller than pre/mid-training due to variable-length examples)
- **Learning rates**: $6 \times 10^{-5}$ (1.5B), $4 \times 10^{-5}$ (3B), $3 \times 10^{-5}$ (7B)
- **Learning rate schedule**: Cosine decay
- **Number of GPUs**: 8 GPUs for all model sizes
- **Maximum sequence length**: 32,000 tokens (4× longer than pre/mid-training to accommodate long reasoning chains during inference)
- **Number of epochs**: 5 epochs over the SFT dataset
- **Training duration**: Approximately 3.5 days for 7B model

The extended context length during SFT (32k vs. 8k) is a deliberate design choice. During inference on reasoning benchmarks, models are allowed up to 32,000 tokens for generation to ensure they have sufficient space for thinking trajectories. The SFT phase aligns the model with a chat format while preserving the reasoning capabilities developed during pre/mid-training.

**Data Mixing Strategy:**
The pre-training and mid-training corpora combine two datasets:
- **FineWeb-Edu**: High-quality educational content subset of FineWeb
- **MegaMath-Web-Pro-Max**: Approximately 70 billion tokens of math-intensive web content

When mixing these datasets, a **sample weight of 0.125** is applied to FineWeb-Edu to balance the domain distribution. This weighting ensures that math-intensive content (which benefits most from thinking augmentation) receives appropriate representation without completely dominating the training signal.

**Loss Computation During SFT:**
A critical implementation detail in Appendix A.1 states: "The loss is only computed over the assistant response tokens." This means that during supervised fine-tuning, the model is not penalized for predictions on user input tokens — only the model's generated responses contribute to the gradient updates. Additionally, "Example boundaries are respected to avoid cross-example attention contamination," meaning that packed sequences do not allow attention to flow across distinct training examples.

#### Key Design Choices and Rationale

TPT makes several deliberate architectural decisions that distinguish it from prior reasoning-augmented training methods.

**Document-Level vs. Token-Level Augmentation:**
TPT operates at the **document level** — each raw document receives one thinking trajectory that is concatenated to form the training sample. This contrasts with RPT (Dong et al., 2025), which applies reinforcement learning at the **token level** with online rollouts. The document-level approach offers three advantages:
1. **Scalability**: Offline generation means the thinking trajectories are computed once and reused, rather than requiring fresh generation during each training step.
2. **Simplicity**: No reinforcement learning infrastructure, reward models, or online sampling is needed — just standard next-token prediction.
3. **Stability**: The training dynamics are identical to vanilla pre-training, avoiding the instability that can arise from RL-based objectives.

The trade-off is that TPT cannot adapt the thinking trajectories based on the target model's current learning state (which online methods could theoretically do). However, the empirical results suggest this adaptation is unnecessary — the static thinking trajectories provide sufficient learning signal.

**Offline Generation vs. Online Rollouts:**
This is perhaps the most consequential design choice. Online rollouts (as in RPT) require the model to generate thinking trajectories **during training**, which means:
- Each training batch incurs generation latency
- Compute requirements scale with training duration
- The quality of thinking trajectories depends on the model's current capabilities (which may be poor early in training)

TPT's offline generation decouples these concerns. The thinking trajectories are produced by a capable generator model **before training begins**, ensuring consistent quality throughout. The target model then learns from these high-quality traces without needing to generate them itself during training. This is why TPT can scale to 100B tokens while RPT's compute requirements are described as "substantially more."

**Single Prompt Template vs. Iterative Optimization:**
Methods like BoLT (Ruan et al., 2025) employ an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to iteratively bootstrap thought generation — the model generates thoughts, trains on them, generates improved thoughts, and repeats. TPT uses a **single fixed prompt template** with no iterative refinement.

The ablation study in Table 4 validates this choice. Alternative strategies were tested:
- **Customized back-thinking model**: Fine-tuning the generator on an SFT dataset to produce thinking content. Result: marginal gains (e.g., AIME24 improved from 11.7% to 14.7%) but added implementation complexity.
- **Prompt with random focus point**: Instructing the generator to focus on a random position within the document. Result: similarly marginal gains (AIME24: 12.4%) with dynamic prompt modification overhead.

The paper concludes: "Consequently, we stick to the default strategy for our main experiments to ensure simplicity and reproducibility." This is a pragmatic decision — the default method achieves strong results without the engineering burden of custom fine-tuning or dynamic prompting.

**No Human Annotation Required:**
TPT imposes "no constraints on document structure" and requires "no human annotation." This is critical for scalability. Methods that require human-labeled reasoning traces cannot scale to 100B tokens — the annotation cost would be prohibitive. By using off-the-shelf LLMs to generate thinking trajectories automatically, TPT can augment any text corpus without manual intervention.

**Complementarity with Data Curation:**
The paper explicitly positions TPT as **orthogonal to** data filtering, deduplication, and rewriting pipelines. TPT does not select which documents to include — it transforms whatever documents you have into a more learnable format. This means organizations can layer TPT on top of their existing data engineering pipelines without replacing them. As Section 2 states:

> "TPT provides a complementary method to transform raw text into a more LLM-friendly format that facilitates more efficient learning."

The thinking augmentation effectively acts as a **learnability enhancer** — it does not change what information is present, but rather how that information is structured for the model to consume.

#### Dynamic Compute Allocation Mechanism

An emergent property of TPT — not explicitly engineered but observed in Section 4 — is that thinking trajectories naturally allocate more training compute to high-value data. The analysis in **Figure 4** reveals:

- **Domain correlation**: Mathematics and Physics documents produce the longest thinking trajectories (1,700–1,900 tokens on average), while domains like Social Service produce shorter traces (~1,100 tokens).
- **Reasoning intensity correlation**: Documents tagged as "Advanced Reasoning" generate approximately 50% more thinking tokens than "No Reasoning" documents.
- **Target audience pattern**: Counterintuitively, "Expert" level documents produce shorter thinking trajectories than "Undergraduate" documents, possibly because expert content contains specialized concepts that require fewer reasoning steps to comprehend.

This creates an implicit **compute allocation mechanism**: difficult, reasoning-intensive samples automatically receive more training tokens (because the thinking trajectory is longer), while simpler samples receive fewer tokens. The paper draws a parallel to test-time scaling:

> "This bears a resemblance to test-time scaling (Jaech et al., 2024) where more difficult samples benefit from increased inference compute. The key distinction is that we apply this principle during training, allocating more training compute to challenging samples, which in turn enhances their learnability for models."

No explicit difficulty scoring or sample weighting is required — the thinking generation process naturally produces this allocation. This is a significant design advantage: the system self-regulates compute distribution based on document complexity without requiring manual heuristics or quality filtering mechanisms.

#### Limitations and Open Questions

The paper acknowledges several areas where the methodology has not been fully explored:

1. **Generator model scaling**: The finding that a 1.5B generator outperforms a 7B generator (Table 4) is not well understood. The paper states: "The relationship between the model being fine-tuned and the model used for thinking generation warrants further investigation."

2. **Prompt optimization**: The current approach uses a single prompt template. Section 7 notes: "The integration of automatic prompt optimization techniques could further improve data quality."

3. **Scaling beyond 100B tokens**: All experiments cap at 100B training tokens. The sustained upward trend in Figure 5 suggests further gains are possible, but this remains unvalidated: "Scaling to larger training corpora and model sizes represent a natural next step."

4. **Missing ablation on thinking trajectory length**: The paper does not ablate the maximum thinking token limit (8k). It is unclear whether longer trajectories would yield additional improvements or whether 8k is near-optimal.

5. **No analysis of thinking quality**: While trajectory length is analyzed (Figure 4), the paper does not evaluate the **quality** of generated thinking — whether the reasoning is correct, complete, or pedagogically effective. Poor-quality thinking traces could theoretically harm learning, though the strong empirical results suggest this is not a practical concern at scale.

These limitations do not undermine the core contribution but rather define the boundary of what the paper claims. TPT is presented as a simple, scalable baseline — not a fully optimized system. The explicit acknowledgment of open questions demonstrates appropriate scientific restraint.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the conceptual breakthroughs that distinguish TPT from prior reasoning-augmented training methods. I separate **fundamental innovations** (new principles or mechanisms) from **scaling contributions** (demonstrating known ideas at unprecedented scale), as both types of contributions advance the field in different ways.

### 4.1 Innovation 1: Training-Time Test-Time Scaling (Fundamental Conceptual Shift)

**What it is:** TPT applies the **test-time scaling principle** — allocating more compute to difficult samples — during **training** rather than inference.

**Why this is novel:** Prior work on test-time scaling (OpenAI o1, DeepSeek-R1) operates exclusively at **inference time**: when a model encounters a hard problem, it generates a long thinking trajectory before answering, consuming more inference compute. The training objective remains unchanged — models still learn via standard next-token prediction on raw data.

TPT inverts this paradigm. As Section 1 articulates:

> "The key distinction is that we apply this principle during training, allocating more training compute to challenging samples, which in turn enhances their learnability for models."

**Mechanism:** The thinking trajectory generation process naturally produces longer traces for reasoning-intensive domains (Figure 4 shows Mathematics and Physics averaging 1,700–1,900 tokens vs. ~1,100 for Social Service). This means:
- Hard samples → longer thinking trajectories → more training tokens per document → more gradient updates on that content
- Easy samples → shorter thinking trajectories → fewer training tokens → less compute spent

**Significance:** This is a **fundamental reconceptualization** of how compute should be allocated in LLM training. Prior data engineering focuses on *which* tokens to include (filtering, selection) or *what* tokens to generate (synthetic data). TPT introduces a third dimension: *how much training signal* each sample should receive, determined automatically by its reasoning complexity rather than manual heuristics.

**Evidence of impact:** Figure 3 (constrained data setting) shows vanilla pre-training plateauing after ~20B tokens as unique data is exhausted, while TPT continues improving steadily to 40B tokens. This sustained improvement indicates that TPT extracts more learning signal from the same underlying documents — the thinking trajectories make tokens "count more" during training.

**Distinction from prior work:** RPT (Dong et al., 2025) also allocates compute dynamically but requires **online rollouts** and **reinforcement learning** during training — substantially more complex infrastructure. TPT achieves similar dynamic allocation through **offline data preprocessing**, making it compatible with standard training pipelines.

### 4.2 Innovation 2: Emergent Compute Allocation Without Explicit Difficulty Scoring (Fundamental Mechanism)

**What it is:** TPT achieves dynamic compute allocation **without any explicit difficulty scoring, sample weighting, or quality filtering**. The allocation emerges naturally from the thinking generation process.

**Why this is novel:** Contemporary data engineering pipelines employ explicit mechanisms to prioritize high-quality data:
- **Quality classifiers** filter low-quality documents (Penedo et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024)
- **Difficulty scoring** selects learnable but not-yet-learned tokens (Mindermann et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2024)
- **Domain balancing** applies manual sample weights to ensure representation

TPT requires **none of these**. Section 4's analysis reveals the emergent property:

> "Our empirical analysis demonstrates that high-value data naturally yields longer thinking trajectories. This results in more training compute being allocated to them, thereby effectively up-sampling valuable content without the use of manual heuristics or explicit quality filtering mechanisms."

**Evidence:** Figure 4 shows three emergent correlations:
1. **Domain → Thinking length**: Mathematics (highest reasoning demand) produces longest trajectories
2. **Reasoning intensity → Thinking length**: "Advanced Reasoning" documents generate ~50% more tokens than "No Reasoning" documents
3. **Target audience → Thinking length**: "Undergraduate" documents produce longer traces than "Expert" documents (counterintuitively, suggesting the generator adapts to comprehension needs)

**Significance:** This eliminates an entire class of engineering complexity. Organizations need not build difficulty classifiers, tune sample weights, or iterate on quality thresholds. The thinking generation model implicitly learns what requires more explanation and produces proportionally longer traces.

**Why this works:** The generator model (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B) was itself trained on reasoning-intensive data. When prompted to "simulate an expert's in-depth thought process," it naturally produces more steps for problems that require more steps. This transfers the difficulty assessment burden from the training pipeline to the generator model — which is already optimized for this task.

**Distinction from prior work:** BoLT (Ruan et al., 2025) uses an EM algorithm to iteratively bootstrap thought quality — explicitly optimizing which thoughts to generate. TPT's single-pass generation with emergent allocation is substantially simpler while achieving comparable or better results at 100B token scale.

### 4.3 Innovation 3: Learnability Through Reasoning Decomposition (Theoretical Contribution)

**What it is:** TPT demonstrates that **restructuring how information is presented** — explicitly showing reasoning steps before answers — fundamentally changes what models can learn from the same tokens.

**Why this is novel:** The scaling law (Kaplan et al., 2020) treats tokens as interchangeable units: more tokens → better performance. TPT challenges this assumption by showing that **token arrangement matters**. The paper's core hypothesis in Section 1:

> "Some valuable tokens can be exceptionally difficult to learn in a single next-token prediction step, as they often represent the outputs of intricate, multi-step human reasoning processes."

**Mechanism:** Consider the Figure 1(b) example again. The token "890" is:
- **Vanilla training**: A direct jump from problem statement to answer. The model must implicitly learn polynomial division, the Remainder Theorem, and divisor properties to predict this token correctly.
- **TPT training**: The endpoint of an explicit 1,054-token reasoning chain (Appendix Table 8). The model sees each intermediate step, learning the causal relationship between reasoning and conclusion.

**Significance:** This suggests that **data format is a first-order design choice**, not just a preprocessing detail. Two datasets with identical token counts but different reasoning structures can yield substantially different model capabilities.

**Evidence:** Table 1 shows TPT-8B (100B tokens) achieves GSM8k score of 50.1% vs. vanilla-8B's 19.2% — a **2.6× improvement** with identical token budgets. This cannot be explained by data quantity; it must be data structure.

**Theoretical implication:** This finding suggests that the "effective information density" of training data depends on how explicitly the causal structure of knowledge is represented. Tokens that represent reasoning intermediates may have higher learning value than tokens that represent conclusions without justification.

**Distinction from prior work:** Synthetic data methods (Phi series, Gunasekar et al., 2023) generate *new* high-quality content. TPT transforms *existing* content into a more learnable format. This is orthogonal — you could apply TPT to synthetic data for compounding benefits.

---

### 4.4 Scaling Contribution: Validation at 100B Tokens Across Training Stages (Engineering Achievement)

**What it is:** TPT is validated at **100B training tokens** across pre-training from scratch, mid-training from checkpoints, and supervised fine-tuning — substantially larger than prior reasoning-augmented training work.

**Why this matters:** Prior work operates at scales that cannot confirm whether benefits persist at frontier training scales:

| Method | Training Scale | Training Stage |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| Reasoning CPT (Ishibashi et al., 2025) | ~150M tokens | Continual training only |
| BoLT (Ruan et al., 2025) | 8B tokens | Math-specific evaluation |
| **TPT (this work)** | **100B tokens** | **Pre-training + Mid-training + SFT** |

**Significance:** Many data engineering techniques show promise at small scales but fail to generalize when training compute increases. The 100B token validation demonstrates that TPT's benefits are **not artifacts of undertraining** — they persist when models see substantial data.

**Evidence:** Figure 2 shows the performance gap between TPT and vanilla pre-training **widens** as training progresses (divergence accelerates after 20B tokens). If TPT only helped early learning, the curves would converge. Instead, TPT continues extracting value throughout the full 100B token run.

**Practical impact:** This scale of validation gives organizations confidence to adopt TPT for production training runs. A method that works at 150M tokens may not justify the engineering investment for a 10T token training run. A method validated at 100B tokens provides stronger evidence of scalability.

**Distinction:** This is an **engineering/scaling contribution** rather than a fundamental innovation. The core idea (augment data with reasoning) is conceptually simple. The contribution is demonstrating it works at scales that matter for frontier model development.

---

### 4.5 Counterintuitive Finding: Smaller Generator Models Outperform Larger Ones (Open Research Question)

**What it is:** Table 4's ablation study reveals that using a **1.5B parameter model** (DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B) for thinking generation **outperforms** the default 7B generator on most benchmarks.

**Why this is surprising:** Intuitively, a stronger generator should produce higher-quality thinking trajectories, which should yield better target model performance. The reverse occurs:

| Generator Model | AIME24 | MATH-500 | GPQA |
|-----------------|--------|----------|------|
| 7B (default) | 11.7% | 72.0% | 37.7% |
| 1.5B | 17.7% | 75.5% | 36.6% |

**Significance:** This finding challenges assumptions about data generation quality. Possible explanations (none confirmed in the paper):
1. **Overthinking hypothesis**: Larger models may generate unnecessarily complex reasoning that overwhelms the target model
2. **Matched capacity hypothesis**: Thinking trajectories from smaller models may be better calibrated to what the target model can learn
3. **Diversity hypothesis**: Smaller models may produce more varied reasoning patterns, improving generalization

**Research implication:** The paper explicitly flags this as open: "The relationship between the model being fine-tuned and the model used for thinking generation warrants further investigation." This defines a clear direction for future work.

**Practical impact:** Organizations can use **modest generator models** to create training data for **much larger target models**, reducing the offline data generation cost. This improves TPT's accessibility for resource-constrained teams.

**Distinction:** This is an **empirical finding** rather than a designed innovation. The paper did not hypothesize this outcome — it emerged from ablation studies. Such unexpected findings often drive the most interesting follow-up research.

---

### 4.6 Summary: What's Fundamental vs. Incremental

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| Training-time test-time scaling | **Fundamental** | New principle for compute allocation in LLM training |
| Emergent difficulty-based allocation | **Fundamental** | Eliminates need for explicit quality filtering |
| Learnability through reasoning decomposition | **Fundamental** | Data format as first-order design choice |
| 100B token validation | **Scaling** | Confidence for production adoption |
| Smaller generator outperforms | **Empirical finding** | Defines open research direction |

The first three contributions represent **conceptual advances** that could influence how the field thinks about LLM training beyond this specific method. The scaling validation and generator size finding are important for **practical adoption** and **future research**, respectively.

Together, these innovations position TPT not merely as a better data augmentation technique, but as evidence that **restructuring training data to expose reasoning structure** is a viable path toward improved data efficiency — a critical capability as the field approaches the limits of available high-quality text.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the experimental methodology and results. I will walk through the evaluation framework, present the quantitative findings with precise numbers, assess whether the evidence supports the paper's claims, and analyze the ablation studies that validate design choices.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Datasets and Benchmarks

The paper employs a **two-tier evaluation strategy** corresponding to different training stages:

**Base Model Evaluation (5 datasets):**
Used for evaluating pre-trained models before supervised fine-tuning. Appendix A.2 specifies the evaluation protocols:

| Dataset | Shots | CoT | Purpose |
|---------|-------|-----|---------|
| GSM8K | 5-shot | Yes | Grade-school math reasoning |
| MATH | 4-shot | Yes | Competition-level mathematics |
| BoolQ | 0-shot | No | Binary question answering |
| MMLU | 2-shot | Yes | General knowledge (57 subjects) |
| MMLUPro | 2-shot | Yes | More challenging MMLU variant |

**Instruction-Tuned Model Evaluation (10 benchmarks):**
Used after supervised fine-tuning on the Mixture-of-Thoughts dataset. These are substantially more challenging:

| Category | Benchmarks |
|----------|------------|
| Mathematical Reasoning | MATH-500, AIME24, AIME25, GSM8k, HMMT |
| Code Generation | HumanEval (HEval), LiveCodeBench v4_v5 (LCB) |
| General Reasoning | GPQA-Diamond, MMLUPro, JEEBench |

**Key Evaluation Details (Appendix A.2):**
- **Pass@1** is the primary metric for all benchmarks
- For smaller datasets, multiple samples are generated to reduce variance: 64 samples for AIME24/25, 16 for LiveCodeBench, 8 for GPQA-Diamond, 4 for MATH-500
- Maximum generation length: **32,000 tokens** during inference (to accommodate thinking trajectories)
- Answer extraction uses regular expressions; failure to extract yields zero score
- Sampling temperature: 0.6, top-p: 0.95

#### Baselines and Comparators

The paper employs multiple baseline categories for fair comparison:

**Pre-training Baselines:**
- **Vanilla-8B**: Same architecture (LLaMA-3-8B), same token budget (100B), trained on raw data without thinking augmentation
- **LLaMA-3.1-8B**: Industry reference model trained on 15T tokens (150× more data)
- **LLaMA-2-7B**: Earlier generation model trained on 2T tokens

**Mid-training Baselines:**
- **OpenR1 models**: Fine-tuned on the same Mixture-of-Thoughts dataset, enabling direct comparison
- **Vanilla mid-training → SFT**: Continual pre-training on raw text (40B tokens) without thinking augmentation, then SFT
- **Direct SFT**: No mid-training, only supervised fine-tuning from base checkpoint

**Reference Models (not directly comparable):**
- **DS-Distill-Qwen-7B**: Trained on larger private dataset (800k samples vs. 350k public)
- **DeepSeek-R1-671B**: State-of-the-art reference for upper-bound performance
- **GPT-4o**: Commercial model reference

#### Experimental Configurations

Three distinct training configurations are evaluated:

**Configuration 1: Pre-training Under Abundant Data (Section 3.1)**
- **Setup**: Two 8B models trained from scratch on 100B tokens
- **Difference**: One uses vanilla next-token prediction, one uses TPT
- **Key detail**: Vanilla model sees ~3× more raw documents (since TPT samples are longer due to thinking trajectories)
- **Training duration**: ~1 week on 32 MI300 GPUs

**Configuration 2: Pre-training Under Constrained Data (Section 3.2)**
- **Setup**: Raw documents limited to 10B tokens, total training budget 40B tokens
- **Implication**: Vanilla model sees data for ~4 epochs, TPT model sees data once (due to augmentation expanding token count)
- **Purpose**: Simulates data-scarce scenarios approaching web data exhaustion

**Configuration 3: Mid-training from Checkpoints (Section 3.3)**
- **Setup**: Three model sizes (1.5B, 3B, 7B) from two families (Qwen2.5, LLaMA-3)
- **Process**: 100B tokens of thinking-augmented mid-training, then SFT on Mixture-of-Thoughts
- **Token budgets evaluated**: 0B (direct SFT), 40B, 100B

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

#### Pre-training Under Abundant Data: The 3× Data Efficiency Claim

**Table 1** presents the core evidence for TPT's data efficiency improvement. The numbers are striking:

| Model | Training Tokens | GSM8k | MATH | BoolQ | MMLU | MMLUpro | **Average** |
|-------|-----------------|-------|------|-------|------|---------|-------------|
| Vanilla-8B | 100B | 19.2% | 9.1% | 66.5% | 26.2% | 10.3% | **26.2%** |
| **TPT-8B** | 100B | **50.1%** | **21.8%** | **75.0%** | **46.7%** | **26.2%** | **43.9%** |
| LLaMA-2-7B | 2T | 11.7% | 3.0% | 72.8% | 40.0% | 16.6% | 28.8% |
| LLaMA-3.1-8B | 15T | 47.0% | 14.1% | 83.5% | 57.6% | 31.8% | **46.8%** |

**Key observations:**

1. **TPT vs. Vanilla (same token budget)**: TPT-8B achieves a **67.6% relative improvement** in average score (43.9% vs. 26.2%). The improvement is most dramatic in mathematical reasoning:
   - GSM8k: **161% improvement** (19.2% → 50.1%)
   - MATH: **140% improvement** (9.1% → 21.8%)

2. **TPT vs. LLaMA-3.1-8B (different token budgets)**: TPT-8B trained on 100B tokens achieves comparable performance to LLaMA-3.1-8B trained on 15T tokens:
   - TPT-8B average: 43.9%
   - LLaMA-3.1-8B average: 46.8%
   - **Gap: only 2.9 percentage points despite 150× difference in training data**

This is the empirical basis for the **3× data efficiency** claim in Figure 1(a). The graph shows that to achieve the same performance level, vanilla pre-training requires approximately 3× more training tokens than TPT.

**Figure 2** provides additional context with training loss curves and aggregated scores over the 100B token training run:
- Training loss for TPT is "substantially lower" than vanilla (exact values not directly comparable due to different data distributions)
- Performance divergence begins after **20B tokens** — initially similar, then TPT rapidly surpasses vanilla
- The gap continues widening through 100B tokens, indicating sustained learning rather than early convergence

#### Post-training Performance: Reasoning Capability Transfer

**Table 2** evaluates models after supervised fine-tuning on the Mixture-of-Thoughts dataset (350k samples). This tests whether TPT pre-training creates a foundation that amplifies downstream fine-tuning:

| Model | AIME24 | MATH-500 | LCB | GPQA | MMLUpro |
|-------|--------|----------|-----|------|---------|
| Vanilla-8B→SFT | 1.0% | 33.8% | 1.9% | 27.7% | 29.0% |
| **TPT-8B→SFT** | **35.2%** | **82.4%** | **23.4%** | **45.2%** | **59.8%** |
| LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct | 5.4% | 49.4% | 9.4% | 31.4% | 43.6% |

**Critical findings:**

1. **Vanilla pre-training fails to develop reasoning capabilities**: The Vanilla-8B→SFT model scores only 1.0% on AIME24 and 1.9% on LiveCodeBench — essentially unable to solve Olympiad-level math or coding problems despite SFT.

2. **TPT enables substantial reasoning transfer**: TPT-8B→SFT achieves:
   - AIME24: **35.2× improvement** over vanilla (1.0% → 35.2%)
   - MATH-500: **144% improvement** (33.8% → 82.4%)
   - LiveCodeBench: **1,132% improvement** (1.9% → 23.4%)

3. **TPT outperforms industry reference**: TPT-8B→SFT beats LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct on **all five benchmarks**, despite LLaMA-3.1 being trained on 15T tokens vs. TPT's 100B tokens. This is remarkable — a model trained on 0.67% of the data outperforms the industry baseline after SFT.

The paper states: "This outcome highlights a promising improvement in data efficiency, demonstrating that TPT enables models to achieve superior reasoning abilities with a fraction of the conventional training data."

#### Pre-training Under Constrained Data: Extracting Value from Limited Tokens

**Figure 3** and **Table 7** evaluate the scenario where raw documents are limited to 10B tokens (simulating data exhaustion), with a 40B total training budget:

| Model | Total Tokens | Epochs | GSM8k | MATH | BoolQ | MMLU | MMLUpro | **Average** |
|-------|--------------|--------|-------|------|-------|------|---------|-------------|
| Vanilla-8B | 40B | ~4 | 6.7% | 4.8% | 53.6% | 12.8% | 5.2% | **16.6%** |
| **TPT-8B** | 40B | ~1 | **30.5%** | **12.9%** | **70.3%** | **33.7%** | **15.8%** | **32.6%** |

**Key observations:**

1. **TPT achieves 96.4% relative improvement** in average score (16.6% → 32.6%) under constrained data conditions.

2. **Vanilla training plateaus**: Figure 3 shows vanilla pre-training performance "plateaus or improves slowly as unique tokens are exhausted" after ~20B tokens. The model sees the same data 4 times but cannot extract additional learning signal.

3. **TPT continues improving**: Despite seeing the data only once (due to augmentation expanding token count), TPT shows "sustained improvement" through 40B tokens. The thinking trajectories enable the model to "extract more value from the same underlying data."

This experiment directly addresses the paper's motivation: as high-quality web data becomes exhausted, how do we maximize utility from existing corpora? TPT demonstrates a clear answer.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Design Validation

#### Thinking Trajectory Generation Strategies (Table 4)

The paper tests alternative approaches to generating thinking trajectories:

| Configuration | MATH-500 | AIME24 | AIME25 | HEval | LCB | GPQA | MMLUPro | JEE |
|---------------|----------|--------|--------|-------|-----|------|---------|-----|
| **TPT-LLaMA-3B (default)** | 72.0% | 11.7% | 15.0% | 64.6% | 18.7% | 37.7% | 52.3% | 36.0% |
| w/ back-thinking model | 73.8% | 14.7% | 13.8% | 57.3% | 18.4% | 38.4% | 53.9% | 41.7% |
| w/ random focus point | 72.2% | 12.4% | 14.4% | 67.1% | 18.9% | 38.3% | 53.5% | 36.7% |
| **w/ 1.5B generator** | **75.5%** | **17.7%** | **16.5%** | 64.6% | **21.4%** | 36.6% | 54.1% | **41.3%** |

**Findings:**

1. **Alternative strategies yield marginal gains**: The back-thinking model (fine-tuned generator) and random focus point prompt show slight improvements on some benchmarks but introduce "extra implementation complexity." The paper justifies sticking with the default for "simplicity and reproducibility."

2. **Smaller generator outperforms**: Using DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B instead of 7B for thinking generation improves AIME24 from 11.7% to 17.7% (**51% relative improvement**) and MATH-500 from 72.0% to 75.5%. The paper notes this "aligns with observations reported by OpenThoughts (Guha et al., 2025)" but acknowledges the mechanism is not understood: "The relationship between the model being fine-tuned and the model used for thinking generation warrants further investigation."

**Practical implication**: Organizations can use modest generator models to create training data for larger target models, reducing offline data generation costs.

**Key observations:**
- **0B (direct SFT)**: LLaMA-3B model "barely solves any AIME24 problems" (~0-5%)
- **40B tokens**: Substantial improvement (~10-12% AIME24)
- **100B tokens**: Further gains (~15-18% AIME24)

The paper states: "The sustained upward trend suggests that scaling beyond 100B tokens would likely yield further improvements, indicating that our approach benefits from increased training compute."

**Critical finding**: SFT with 350k samples alone "proves insufficient for developing strong reasoning capabilities." Mid-training is essential — it creates the reasoning foundation that SFT then aligns.

**Findings:**

- **TPT mid-trained checkpoints maintain advantage throughout SFT**: "Checkpoints that underwent thinking augmented mid-training demonstrate superior starting points compared to their vanilla counterparts. This advantage persists throughout the SFT phase."

| Configuration | MATH-500 | AIME24 | AIME25 | HEval | LCB | GPQA | MMLUPro | JEE |

**Significance**: This control validates that TPT's improvements come from the **thinking augmentation**, not merely from additional training tokens. More data alone does not help — the data must be structured with reasoning traces.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment: Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

#### Claim 1: "TPT enhances data efficiency by a factor of 3"

**Evidence**: Figure 1(a) shows the 3× efficiency curve. Table 1 shows TPT-8B (100B tokens) achieves 43.9% average vs. LLaMA-3.1-8B's 46.8% at 15T tokens.

**Assessment**: **Strongly supported.** The 150× token difference with only 2.9 percentage point performance gap provides compelling evidence. The constrained data experiment (Table 7) further validates this — TPT achieves 96% improvement over vanilla with the same token budget.

**Caveat**: The 3× factor is derived from the performance vs. tokens curve in Figure 1(a), not a direct head-to-head comparison at multiple token budgets. A more rigorous validation would train TPT at 33B, 66B, and 100B tokens to confirm the scaling relationship.

#### Claim 2: "For a 3B parameter model, TPT improves post-training performance by over 10% on several challenging reasoning benchmarks"

**Evidence**: Table 3 shows TPT-LLaMA-3B vs. OpenR1-LLaMA-3B:
- AIME24: 5.8% → 18.6% (**+12.8 percentage points**, 220% relative)
- MATH-500: 59.8% → 75.5% (**+15.7 percentage points**)
- MMLUPro: 45.8% → 55.5% (**+9.7 percentage points**)

**Assessment**: **Strongly supported.** Multiple benchmarks show improvements exceeding 10 percentage points. The claim is conservative — some improvements exceed 100% relative.

#### Claim 3: "Thinking trajectories naturally up-sample high-quality data"

**Evidence**: Figure 4 shows domain and reasoning intensity correlations with thinking length:
- Mathematics: ~1,800 tokens average thinking length
- Social Service: ~1,100 tokens
- Advanced Reasoning: ~50% more tokens than No Reasoning

**Assessment**: **Moderately supported.** The correlation is clear, but the paper does not directly demonstrate that this allocation causes the performance improvements. A stronger ablation would manually equalize thinking lengths across domains to isolate the effect.

#### Claim 4: "TPT is scalable and simple"

**Evidence**: 100B token validation across three training configurations. No RL, no EM algorithm, no iterative training.

**Assessment**: **Strongly supported.** The 100B token scale is 12.5× larger than the closest prior work (BoLT at 8B tokens). The method uses standard next-token prediction with offline data augmentation.

#### Limitations and Open Questions

**What the experiments do NOT address:**

1. **Thinking quality analysis**: The paper analyzes thinking *length* (Figure 4) but not thinking *quality*. Are the generated reasoning traces correct? Do they contain errors that could mislead the model? The strong results suggest this is not a practical problem, but it remains unmeasured.

2. **Optimal thinking length**: The 8,000 token maximum for thinking generation is not ablated. Would 16,000 tokens yield better results? Is 8k near-optimal? Unknown.

3. **Domain-specific effectiveness**: While mathematics shows the largest gains, the paper does not systematically analyze which domains benefit most. Is TPT primarily a math/reasoning enhancement, or does it generalize broadly?

4. **Generator-target model matching**: The finding that 1.5B generators outperform 7B generators is not explained. What is the optimal generator size for a given target model? This has significant cost implications.

5. **Long-term training dynamics**: All experiments cap at 100B tokens. Figure 5 suggests continued improvement, but does TPT eventually plateau? At what point do diminishing returns set in?

**Potential confounds:**

1. **Data mixing differences**: The vanilla and TPT models see different effective data distributions (TPT sees fewer raw documents but more total tokens due to augmentation). The paper acknowledges loss values are "not directly comparable due to differences in data distributions."

2. **Generator model influence**: For pre-training from scratch, Qwen3-8B generates thinking trajectories. For mid-training, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B is used. Could the generator model's characteristics bias the results?

3. **SFT dataset limitations**: The Mixture-of-Thoughts dataset has 350k samples vs. 800k for reference models. The paper notes the 1.5B model "remains inferior to DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B, which can likely be attributed to the smaller dataset size."

---

### 5.5 Summary: Experimental Strengths and Weaknesses

| Aspect | Assessment | Evidence |
|--------|------------|----------|
| **Scale of validation** | **Excellent** | 100B tokens across pre-training, mid-training, SFT |
| **Baseline comparisons** | **Strong** | Vanilla, OpenR1, industry references (LLaMA-3.1, GPT-4o) |
| **Ablation completeness** | **Good** | Generator size, generation strategy, token budget, SFT epochs |
| **Control experiments** | **Strong** | Vanilla mid-training control (Table 6) isolates thinking augmentation effect |
| **Statistical rigor** | **Adequate** | Multiple samples for small datasets, but no confidence intervals reported |
| **Failure analysis** | **Limited** | No discussion of cases where TPT does not help or harms |
| **Thinking quality evaluation** | **Missing** | Length analyzed, correctness not measured |
| **Domain generalization** | **Partial** | Math/code/reasoning covered, but no analysis of non-reasoning domains |

**Overall assessment**: The experiments provide **convincing evidence** for TPT's core claims, particularly regarding data efficiency and reasoning capability improvements. The 100B token scale, multiple training configurations, and strong baselines make the results credible for production adoption decisions. The primary gaps are in understanding *why* certain design choices work (e.g., smaller generators) and *where* the method reaches its limits (e.g., maximum effective token budget, domain boundaries).

The vanilla mid-training control (Table 6) is particularly important — it demonstrates that the improvements come from the **thinking augmentation structure**, not merely from additional training compute. This validates the paper's core hypothesis that data format matters as much as data quantity.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the boundaries of TPT's validity — what assumptions underpin the method, where it may fail, and what practical constraints organizations should consider before adoption. I distinguish between **fundamental limitations** (inherent to the approach) and **practical constraints** (engineering or resource challenges that could be mitigated).

### 6.1 Core Assumptions Underlying TPT

TPT relies on several implicit assumptions that, if violated, could undermine its effectiveness.

#### Assumption 1: Generated Thinking Trajectories Are Pedagogically Useful

The entire method presupposes that thinking trajectories generated by off-the-shelf LLMs actually help the target model learn. However, the paper **never evaluates the correctness or quality** of the generated reasoning — only the length (Figure 4).

**Evidence gap:** Appendix Tables 8–10 show example trajectories that appear coherent, but no systematic analysis confirms:
- Whether the reasoning steps are logically valid
- Whether incorrect reasoning traces harm learning
- Whether the Feynman technique instruction (Section 2 prompt) is actually followed

**Risk:** If the generator model produces plausible-but-incorrect reasoning (a known failure mode of LLMs), the target model could learn flawed reasoning patterns. The strong empirical results suggest this is not a dominant problem at scale, but the absence of quality metrics means organizations cannot assess this risk for their specific use cases.

**Paper acknowledgment:** Section 7's future work mentions "exploring more powerful thinking generation models warrants further investigation" but does not frame generator quality as a potential limitation.

#### Assumption 2: Reasoning Structure Transfers Across Domains

TPT demonstrates largest gains on mathematical reasoning benchmarks (GSM8k: 161% improvement, MATH: 140% improvement in Table 1). The method assumes this benefit generalizes to non-reasoning domains.

**Evidence gap:** The evaluation benchmarks are heavily skewed toward reasoning-intensive tasks:
- 5 of 10 instruction-tuned benchmarks are mathematical (MATH-500, AIME24, AIME25, GSM8k, HMMT)
- 2 are coding (HumanEval, LiveCodeBench)
- Only 3 are general knowledge (GPQA-D, MMLUPro, JEEBench)

**Risk:** TPT may provide diminishing returns for domains where reasoning decomposition is less valuable — for example, factual recall, creative writing, or simple question answering. The paper does not report performance on purely knowledge-based tasks without reasoning components.

**Paper acknowledgment:** Section 4 notes that domains like Social Service produce shorter thinking trajectories (~1,100 tokens vs. ~1,800 for Mathematics in Figure 4), implicitly acknowledging domain variation. However, the paper does not analyze whether shorter trajectories correlate with smaller performance gains.

#### Assumption 3: Static Thinking Trajectories Suffice for Dynamic Learning

TPT generates thinking trajectories **offline, once**, before training begins. This assumes that static reasoning traces provide adequate learning signal throughout the entire training run.

**Contrast with prior work:** RPT (Dong et al., 2025) uses **online rollouts** that could theoretically adapt thinking trajectories based on the model's current learning state — generating different traces for samples the model struggles with.

**Trade-off:** TPT sacrifices potential adaptation for scalability. The paper justifies this by noting online methods require "substantially more compute" (Section 1), but does not empirically test whether adaptive thinking generation would yield additional improvements.

**Risk:** Early in training, the model may benefit from simpler reasoning traces; later, it may need more complex traces. Static trajectories cannot adapt to this progression.

---

### 6.2 Scenarios and Edge Cases Not Addressed

The paper's experimental design leaves several important scenarios untested.

#### Scenario 1: Training Beyond 100B Tokens

All experiments cap at **100B training tokens**. Figure 5 shows sustained improvement up to this point, but the paper explicitly acknowledges:

> "Scaling to larger training corpora and model sizes represent a natural next step." (Section 7)

**Unknown:** Does TPT's advantage persist, diminish, or grow at 1T+ token scales? If the benefit is primarily early-learning acceleration (helping models learn faster initially), the advantage might shrink as vanilla training eventually converges. If the benefit is fundamental (enabling learning that vanilla training cannot achieve), the advantage should persist.

**Practical implication:** Organizations planning multi-trillion token training runs cannot confidently extrapolate TPT's benefits from the 100B token validation.

#### Scenario 2: Models Larger Than 8B Parameters

The largest model evaluated is **8B parameters** (pre-training) and **7B parameters** (mid-training). Frontier models now exceed 70B parameters.

**Unknown:** Whether TPT's benefits scale with model size. Larger models have greater capacity to learn complex tokens directly, potentially reducing the relative advantage of thinking augmentation.

**Paper acknowledgment:** Section 7 states "scaling to larger training corpora and model sizes represent a natural next step" — explicitly acknowledging this gap.

#### Scenario 3: Non-English or Low-Resource Languages

All datasets and benchmarks appear to be English-language (FineWeb-Edu, MegaMath-Web-Pro-Max, GSM8k, MATH, MMLU, etc.). The thinking trajectory generation prompt is in English.

**Unknown:** Whether TPT works for:
- Non-English pre-training corpora
- Low-resource languages where generator models are weaker
- Multilingual training scenarios

**Risk:** The generator model's reasoning capabilities may vary significantly across languages, producing lower-quality trajectories for non-English text. This could create uneven benefits across language communities.

#### Scenario 4: Failure Modes and Negative Results

The paper reports **no cases where TPT underperforms vanilla training**. Every comparison shows TPT winning or tying.

**Evidence gap:** Table 6 shows vanilla mid-training can **harm** coding performance (HEval: 45.7% → 36.0%), but TPT is not shown to have any negative effects. This is suspicious — no method is universally beneficial.

**Possible unreported failures:**
- Domains where explicit reasoning is unnecessary or distracting
- Model sizes where thinking augmentation provides no benefit
- Training stages where TPT interferes with other learning objectives

**Scientific concern:** The absence of negative results makes it difficult to understand TPT's boundaries. Organizations cannot assess when TPT might be inappropriate for their use case.

---

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

While TPT is positioned as "simple and scalable" (Abstract), several practical constraints warrant consideration.

#### Constraint 1: Offline Data Generation Cost

The paper states in Appendix A.1:

> "The entire data generation pipeline takes roughly 20k A100 GPU hours to yield enough data for 100B training tokens."

**Breakdown:**
- 20,000 A100 hours ≈ 833 A100 days ≈ **2.3 A100-years** of compute
- At cloud pricing (~$1–2/hour per A100), this represents **$20,000–$40,000** in generation costs alone
- This is a **one-time upfront cost** before training can begin

**Trade-off:** TPT shifts compute from training time (online rollouts in RPT) to preprocessing time (offline generation). For organizations with limited preprocessing capacity but abundant training infrastructure, this could be a bottleneck.

**Mitigation:** The ablation in Table 4 shows smaller generator models (1.5B) outperform larger ones (7B), potentially reducing generation costs. However, the paper does not report the generation cost for the 1.5B model.

#### Constraint 2: Increased Storage Requirements

Thinking augmentation expands the token count per document. In the abundant data setting (Section 3.1):

> "In terms of raw documents processed by the models, the vanilla model is exposed to approximately 3× documents than the thinking augmented model."

**Implication:** The augmented dataset is approximately **3× larger** in token count than the raw dataset (since thinking trajectories add substantial length).

**Storage impact:**
- If raw corpus is 1TB, augmented corpus may be ~3TB
- This affects disk storage, data loading pipelines, and checkpointing frequency

**Trade-off:** The 3× data efficiency during training (Figure 1a) comes at the cost of 3× storage requirements for the augmented dataset. Organizations must evaluate whether training compute savings outweigh storage costs.

#### Constraint 3: Fixed Prompt Template Limitations

TPT uses a **single fixed prompt template** for all documents (Section 2):

```
{{CONTEXT}}
## End of the context
Simulate an expert's in-depth thought process...
```

**Ablation result:** Table 4 tests alternative strategies (back-thinking model, random focus point) but finds only "marginal gains" with "extra implementation complexity."

**Limitation:** A single prompt cannot optimally handle all document types:
- Mathematical proofs may need different reasoning structures than historical narratives
- Code documents may benefit from execution trace thinking vs. conceptual reasoning
- Scientific papers may need hypothesis-evaluation thinking patterns

**Future work acknowledgment:** Section 7 notes "the integration of automatic prompt optimization techniques could further improve data quality" — implicitly acknowledging the current prompt is suboptimal.

**Risk:** Organizations with specialized domains (e.g., legal, medical, scientific) may need to develop domain-specific prompts, adding engineering overhead not captured in the paper's "simplicity" claims.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

Several methodological weaknesses and unresolved questions limit confidence in TPT's generalizability.

#### Weakness 1: Data Distribution Confound

The paper acknowledges in Figure 2's caption:

> "The loss values are not directly comparable due to differences in data distributions."

**Root cause:** TPT and vanilla models see different effective data:
- Vanilla: More raw documents, no thinking trajectories
- TPT: Fewer raw documents (1/3 as many), each augmented with thinking

**Confound:** Performance differences could stem from:
1. The thinking trajectories themselves (claimed mechanism)
2. Different document sampling (fewer but richer samples)
3. Different token distributions (more reasoning tokens in TPT data)

**Missing ablation:** A cleaner comparison would train both models on the **same raw documents**, with TPT augmenting them and vanilla using raw versions. The current design conflates document selection with augmentation effects.

**Impact:** The 3× data efficiency claim may partially reflect better document selection (fewer, higher-quality documents seen once) rather than purely the thinking augmentation benefit.

#### Weakness 2: Generator-Target Model Relationship Unexplained

Table 4's finding that a **1.5B generator outperforms a 7B generator** is counterintuitive and unexplained:

| Generator | AIME24 | MATH-500 | GPQA |
|-----------|--------|----------|------|
| 7B (default) | 11.7% | 72.0% | 37.7% |
| 1.5B | 17.7% | 75.5% | 36.6% |

**Paper's statement:** "The relationship between the model being fine-tuned and the model used for thinking generation warrants further investigation." (Section 5)

**Possible explanations (not tested):**
1. **Capacity matching:** Smaller generators produce reasoning better calibrated to what target models can learn
2. **Overthinking:** Larger generators produce unnecessarily complex traces that overwhelm learning
3. **Diversity:** Smaller models generate more varied reasoning patterns, improving generalization

**Practical impact:** Without understanding this relationship, organizations cannot optimize generator selection for their target model size. Should a 70B target model use a 7B generator? A 1.5B generator? Something else?

#### Weakness 3: Thinking Length Not Ablated

The maximum thinking trajectory length is set to **8,000 tokens** (Appendix A.1), but this hyperparameter is never ablated.

**Unknown:**
- Would 16,000 token trajectories yield better results?
- Is 8,000 tokens near-optimal, or is it arbitrary?
- Does optimal length vary by domain or document complexity?

**Evidence hint:** Figure 4 shows average thinking lengths of 1,100–1,900 tokens across domains — well below the 8,000 token maximum. This suggests most trajectories don't hit the limit, but the paper doesn't analyze whether trajectories that do hit the limit are truncated mid-reasoning.

**Risk:** If important reasoning is truncated at 8k tokens, the model learns incomplete reasoning chains, potentially harming performance.

#### Weakness 4: SFT Dataset Size Limitation

Appendix A.1 acknowledges:

> "Our reproduction experiments show that, while the performance of 7B models is comparable, the 1.5B model remains inferior to DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B, which can likely be attributed to the smaller dataset size."

**Details:**
- TPT uses Mixture-of-Thoughts: **350k samples** (public)
- DeepSeek-R1-Distill uses: **800k samples** (private)

**Implication:** The 1.5B TPT model's underperformance relative to the reference may reflect SFT data limitations rather than TPT's mid-training effectiveness.

**Confound:** When comparing TPT models to reference models (Table 3), differences could stem from:
1. Mid-training approach (TPT vs. other methods)
2. SFT dataset size (350k vs. 800k samples)

The paper cannot disentangle these factors for the 1.5B model.

#### Weakness 5: No Analysis of Reasoning Correctness

The paper analyzes thinking **length** (Figure 4) but never evaluates thinking **correctness**.

**Critical question:** What fraction of generated reasoning traces contain logical errors, factual mistakes, or invalid inference steps?

**Risk:** If 10% of thinking trajectories contain errors, the target model learns incorrect reasoning 10% of the time. At 100B training tokens, this represents billions of tokens of potentially misleading supervision.

**Missing evaluation:** The paper could have:
- Sampled thinking trajectories and had humans evaluate correctness
- Used formal verification for mathematical reasoning traces
- Compared generator outputs against ground-truth solutions where available

**Scientific gap:** Without quality metrics, the community cannot assess whether TPT's success comes from high-quality reasoning traces or from some other mechanism (e.g., increased token count, better data formatting).

### 6.6 Practical Guidance for Adoption

Based on the identified limitations, organizations considering TPT should evaluate:

**Adopt TPT if:**
- Training reasoning-intensive models (math, code, science)
- Have sufficient preprocessing capacity for 20k+ GPU hours of data generation
- Training at 100B token scale or below (where validation exists)
- Can accept 3× storage overhead for augmented datasets

**Exercise caution if:**
- Training primarily on non-reasoning domains (creative writing, simple QA)
- Planning multi-trillion token training runs (unvalidated at this scale)
- Working with non-English or low-resource language corpora
- Limited ability to evaluate thinking trajectory quality

**Research opportunities:**
- Systematic analysis of thinking correctness and its impact on learning
- Optimal generator-target model size matching
- Domain-specific prompt templates and their benefits
- Adaptive thinking generation during training vs. static offline generation

The limitations identified here do not invalidate TPT's core contributions — the 100B token validation and consistent improvements across benchmarks provide strong evidence of effectiveness. However, they define the **boundary conditions** within which organizations can confidently apply the method, and they chart a clear agenda for future research to expand those boundaries.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how TPT reshapes the field's approach to data-efficient LLM training, identifies concrete research directions it opens, and provides practical guidance for organizations evaluating adoption. I distinguish between **immediate implications** (changes to current practice) and **long-term shifts** (paradigm changes that may unfold over years).

---

### 7.1 Landscape Impact: How TPT Changes the Field

#### Shift 1: Data Format as a First-Order Design Choice

Prior to TPT, the dominant view treated training data as interchangeable tokens — the scaling law (Kaplan et al., 2020) suggested that **quantity** matters more than **structure**. TPT challenges this assumption by demonstrating that restructuring how information is presented fundamentally changes what models learn from the same tokens.

**Evidence:** Table 1 shows TPT-8B (100B tokens) achieves a GSM8k score of 50.1% vs. vanilla-8B's 19.2% — a **161% improvement** with identical token budgets. This cannot be explained by data quantity; it must be data structure.

**Paradigm shift:** The field must now consider **data formatting** alongside data quantity and quality as a core lever for improving model capabilities. This opens an entirely new optimization dimension:

| Traditional Data Engineering | TPT-Informed Data Engineering |
|------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Filter low-quality documents | Transform documents into learnable formats |
| Balance domain representation | Allocate compute based on reasoning complexity |
| Deduplicate to remove redundancy | Augment to expose hidden reasoning structure |
| Maximize token count | Maximize **effective information density** per token |

**Long-term implication:** Future data pipelines may routinely include reasoning augmentation as a standard preprocessing step, similar to how deduplication and filtering are now universal.

---

#### Shift 2: Training-Time Compute Allocation Replaces Manual Difficulty Scoring

Contemporary data engineering relies on explicit mechanisms to prioritize high-value data: quality classifiers (Penedo et al., 2024), difficulty scoring (Mindermann et al., 2022), and manual sample weights. TPT demonstrates that **emergent compute allocation** — where difficult samples automatically receive more training signal through longer thinking trajectories — can replace these engineered systems.

**Evidence:** Figure 4 shows Mathematics documents produce ~1,800 token thinking trajectories vs. ~1,100 for Social Service, with "Advanced Reasoning" documents generating 50% more tokens than "No Reasoning" documents — all without explicit difficulty scoring.

**Paradigm shift:** Instead of building classifiers to identify valuable data, organizations can rely on the thinking generation process to implicitly assess complexity and allocate compute accordingly. This eliminates an entire class of engineering complexity.

**Long-term implication:** Future work may extend this principle beyond reasoning length — for example, generating multiple thinking trajectories for ambiguous samples, or producing counterfactual reasoning traces to improve robustness.

---

#### Shift 3: Offline Data Augmentation as an Alternative to Online Training Methods

Methods like RPT (Dong et al., 2025) apply reinforcement learning with **online rollouts** during training — generating thinking trajectories dynamically based on the model's current state. TPT demonstrates that **offline augmentation** achieves comparable or better results at 100B token scale with substantially simpler infrastructure.

**Evidence:** Table 3 shows TPT models outperform OpenR1 baselines (which use similar post-training but different mid-training approaches) across all model sizes. TPT requires no RL infrastructure, no reward models, and no online sampling.

**Paradigm shift:** The field may reconsider whether online adaptation during training is necessary for reasoning augmentation, or whether high-quality offline generation suffices. This has significant implications for training infrastructure costs and complexity.

**Long-term implication:** Hybrid approaches may emerge — offline augmentation for most data, with online generation reserved for samples where the model shows persistent difficulty during training.

---

#### Shift 4: Smaller Generator Models May Outperform Larger Ones

The counterintuitive finding in Table 4 — that a 1.5B generator outperforms a 7B generator on most benchmarks — challenges assumptions about data generation quality. If validated at larger scales, this could dramatically reduce the cost of creating training data.

**Evidence:** Using DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B for thinking generation improves AIME24 from 11.7% to 17.7% (**51% relative improvement**) compared to the 7B generator.

**Paradigm shift:** The optimal generator model may not be the strongest available, but rather the one whose reasoning traces are best calibrated to what the target model can learn. This suggests a **capacity matching** principle rather than a **maximum quality** principle.

**Long-term implication:** Organizations may maintain a portfolio of generator models sized appropriately for different target model scales, rather than using a single large generator for all data generation tasks.

---

### 7.2 Research Agenda: Concrete Follow-Up Directions

TPT opens numerous research questions. I organize these by **near-term** (can be addressed with current resources) and **long-term** (require significant investment or methodological advances).

#### Near-Term Research Directions (1–2 years)

**1. Thinking Quality Evaluation Framework**

**Question:** What fraction of generated thinking trajectories contain logical errors, and how does error rate affect target model learning?

**Methodology:**
- Sample 1,000 thinking trajectories across domains
- Have human experts or formal verifiers evaluate correctness
- Correlate error rates with downstream benchmark performance
- Test whether filtering incorrect traces improves results

**Why this matters:** The paper provides no quality metrics — only length analysis (Figure 4). Understanding the relationship between thinking correctness and model learning is essential for safe deployment.

**Expected outcome:** Quality thresholds for thinking trajectories (e.g., "filter traces with >5% logical errors") and potentially improved generator prompting to reduce error rates.

---

**2. Generator-Target Model Size Matching**

**Question:** What is the optimal generator model size for a given target model size?

**Methodology:**
- Train target models at 1B, 3B, 7B, 13B, 70B parameters
- Generate thinking data with generators at 0.5B, 1.5B, 7B, 13B, 70B
- Measure performance across all combinations
- Identify scaling relationship between generator and target sizes

**Why this matters:** Table 4's finding that 1.5B outperforms 7B is unexplained. A systematic study would provide practical guidance for organizations and could reveal fundamental principles about learning from generated data.

**Expected outcome:** A sizing formula (e.g., "generator should be 10–20% of target model size") or identification of regimes where different generator sizes excel.

---

**3. Thinking Length Ablation**

**Question:** What is the optimal maximum thinking trajectory length?

**Methodology:**
- Generate thinking data with maximum lengths of 2k, 4k, 8k, 16k, 32k tokens
- Train identical target models on each variant
- Measure performance vs. compute cost trade-offs

**Why this matters:** The paper uses 8k tokens (Appendix A.1) without ablation. Figure 4 shows average lengths of 1,100–1,900 tokens — well below the maximum. It is unclear whether the limit constrains quality or is simply unused.

**Expected outcome:** Domain-specific length recommendations (e.g., "math: 16k, general text: 4k") and potentially adaptive length limits based on document complexity.

---

**4. Domain Generalization Beyond Reasoning**

**Question:** Does TPT benefit non-reasoning domains (creative writing, factual recall, simple QA)?

**Methodology:**
- Curate evaluation benchmarks for non-reasoning tasks
- Apply TPT to domain-specific corpora (e.g., news articles, fiction, encyclopedias)
- Compare against vanilla training and domain-specific baselines

**Why this matters:** Table 1 and Table 3 show largest gains on mathematical reasoning. It is unknown whether TPT provides value for domains where explicit reasoning is less central.

**Expected outcome:** Identification of domains where TPT provides minimal benefit, allowing organizations to selectively apply augmentation only where it matters.

---

#### Long-Term Research Directions (3–5 years)

**5. Scaling Beyond 100B Tokens**

**Question:** Does TPT's advantage persist, diminish, or grow at 1T+ token scales?

**Methodology:**
- Train models at 200B, 500B, 1T, 5T tokens with and without TPT
- Track performance gap across scales
- Identify whether diminishing returns set in

**Why this matters:** All experiments cap at 100B tokens. Section 7 acknowledges "scaling to larger training corpora and model sizes represent a natural next step." Organizations planning multi-trillion token runs need this data.

**Expected outcome:** A scaling curve showing TPT's advantage as a function of total training tokens, enabling cost-benefit analysis for large-scale training.

---

**6. Adaptive Thinking Generation During Training**

**Question:** Would generating thinking trajectories dynamically during training (based on model's current learning state) outperform static offline generation?

**Methodology:**
- Implement online thinking generation that adapts to model's loss on each sample
- Compare against TPT's offline approach at equal compute budgets
- Measure whether adaptation provides additional benefit

**Why this matters:** TPT sacrifices potential adaptation for scalability. This direction tests whether that trade-off is optimal or whether hybrid approaches could capture both benefits.

**Expected outcome:** Either validation that offline generation suffices, or identification of scenarios where online adaptation provides meaningful additional gains.

---

**7. Multilingual and Cross-Lingual TPT**

**Question:** Does TPT work for non-English languages, and can thinking trajectories in one language improve performance in another?

**Methodology:**
- Apply TPT to corpora in major languages (Chinese, Spanish, Arabic, etc.)
- Evaluate on multilingual benchmarks (e.g., XCOPA, XNLI)
- Test cross-lingual transfer (English thinking + non-English documents)

**Why this matters:** All experiments appear English-only. Generator model capabilities vary significantly across languages, potentially creating uneven benefits.

**Expected outcome:** Language-specific guidance for TPT application and potentially multilingual generator models optimized for thinking trajectory generation.

---

**8. Thinking Trajectory Compression and Distillation**

**Question:** Can long thinking trajectories be compressed into shorter forms that preserve learning value?

**Methodology:**
- Train compression models to distill 8k token trajectories into 1k tokens
- Compare target model performance on compressed vs. full trajectories
- Measure compute savings during training

**Why this matters:** TPT increases storage requirements by ~3× (Section 6.3). Compression could reduce this overhead while preserving benefits.

**Expected outcome:** Compression techniques that maintain 90%+ of TPT's benefit with 50%+ storage reduction.

---

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

TPT's benefits are not uniform across all scenarios. I provide concrete guidance on where TPT provides the most value.

#### High-Value Use Cases

**1. Reasoning-Intensive Model Development**

**Scenario:** Organizations building models for mathematics, coding, scientific reasoning, or complex problem-solving.

**Why TPT excels:** Table 1 shows 161% improvement on GSM8k and 140% on MATH. Table 3 shows consistent gains across AIME, HumanEval, and GPQA benchmarks.

**Expected benefit:** Models achieve comparable reasoning capability to competitors with 3× less training data, or superior capability with equal data budgets.

**Example:** A team training a 7B model for mathematical tutoring could use TPT mid-training to achieve TPT-Qwen2.5-7B's 92.5% MATH-500 score rather than Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct's 74.1% — a meaningful capability gap for end users.

---

**2. Data-Constrained Training Environments**

**Scenario:** Organizations approaching the limits of available high-quality web data, or working with proprietary corpora that cannot be easily expanded.

**Why TPT excels:** Figure 3 and Table 7 show TPT continues improving under constrained data (10B raw documents) while vanilla training plateaus. TPT extracts more learning signal from the same underlying tokens.

**Expected benefit:**延 extends the useful life of existing data corpora, delaying the need for expensive new data collection efforts.

**Example:** A financial services company with 5B tokens of proprietary documents could use TPT to achieve performance comparable to 15B tokens of vanilla training — effectively tripling their data asset value.

**3. Mid-Training Capability Enhancement**

**Scenario:** Organizations with existing base models that need capability improvements without full retraining.

**Why TPT excels:** Table 3 shows mid-training with TPT substantially outperforms direct SFT. The LLaMA-3B model improves AIME24 from 3.8% (base) to 18.6% (TPT mid-training + SFT) — capabilities that SFT alone cannot unlock.

**Expected benefit:** Cost-effective capability upgrades for deployed models without the expense of training from scratch.

**Example:** A company using LLaMA-3.2-3B as a base could apply 100B tokens of TPT mid-training to achieve reasoning capabilities approaching much larger models, at a fraction of the cost.

**4. Resource-Constrained Teams**

**Scenario:** Academic labs or startups with limited compute budgets that cannot compete on training scale.

**Why TPT excels:** The 3× data efficiency (Figure 1a) means smaller teams can achieve competitive results with proportionally smaller training runs. The 1.5B generator finding (Table 4) reduces data generation costs.

**Expected benefit:** Democratizes access to competitive model capabilities for teams without frontier-level resources.

**Example:** A university lab with 100B token training budget could achieve results comparable to industry models trained on 300B+ tokens, enabling competitive research contributions.

#### Lower-Value Use Cases

**1. Pure Knowledge Recall Tasks**

**Scenario:** Models primarily used for factual question answering, encyclopedia-style retrieval, or simple classification.

**Why TPT provides limited value:** The evaluation benchmarks are heavily reasoning-skewed. Domains like Social Service produce shorter thinking trajectories (Figure 4), suggesting less augmentation benefit.

**Recommendation:** Consider vanilla training or domain-specific augmentation strategies rather than generic TPT.

**2. Creative Generation Tasks**

**Scenario:** Models for creative writing, poetry, storytelling, or open-ended generation.

**Why TPT provides limited value:** These tasks do not have clear reasoning trajectories to augment. The Feynman technique prompting (Section 2) is optimized for explanatory reasoning, not creative expression.

**Recommendation:** Explore alternative augmentation strategies (e.g., style variation, narrative structure augmentation) better suited to creative domains.

---

**3. Ultra-Large Scale Training (1T+ tokens)**

**Scenario:** Frontier model development at trillion-token scales.

**Why TPT is unvalidated:** All experiments cap at 100B tokens. Section 7 explicitly acknowledges scaling beyond this as future work.

**Recommendation:** Wait for larger-scale validation or run pilot studies at 200–500B tokens before committing to full-scale adoption.

2. **Use default prompt:** Begin with the paper's default prompt template (Section 2) before experimenting with modifications.
3. **Consider smaller generator:** Test 1.5B generator models before defaulting to 7B — Table 4 suggests potential cost savings with equal or better performance.
4. **Monitor thinking length:** Track average trajectory lengths by domain (as in Figure 4) to identify which content benefits most from augmentation.
5. **Validate with control:** Include a vanilla mid-training control (as in Table 6) to isolate TPT's effect from additional compute alone.

**Post-Adoption Monitoring:**
- Track performance gaps between TPT and vanilla baselines across training checkpoints
- Monitor for any degradation in non-reasoning capabilities
- Evaluate whether thinking trajectory quality correlates with downstream performance
- Document any domain-specific patterns in augmentation effectiveness

#### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**Pitfall 1: Skipping the Control Experiment**

**Mistake:** Attributing all improvements to thinking augmentation without verifying that additional compute alone doesn't explain results.

**Correction:** Always include a vanilla mid-training control (same token budget, no augmentation) as in Table 6. This isolates the augmentation effect from the compute effect.

---

**Pitfall 2: Using Inappropriate Generator Models**

**Mistake:** Assuming the strongest available generator is optimal for data generation.

**Correction:** Test multiple generator sizes (1.5B, 7B, etc.) on a small scale before committing. Table 4's finding that smaller generators can outperform larger ones has significant cost implications.

---

**Pitfall 3: Applying TPT to Non-Reasoning Domains Without Validation**

**Mistake:** Assuming TPT benefits all domains equally based on math/code results.

**Correction:** Pilot test on domain-specific benchmarks before full deployment. Expect diminishing returns for tasks without clear reasoning structure.

---

**Pitfall 4: Ignoring Storage and Loading Implications**

**Mistake:** Underestimating the 3× storage overhead and its impact on data loading pipelines.

**Correction:** Plan for increased storage requirements and potentially slower data loading due to longer sequences. Consider compression strategies if storage is constrained.

---

**Pitfall 5: Over-Engineering the Prompt**

**Mistake:** Investing significant effort in custom prompt optimization before validating the default approach.

**Correction:** Start with the paper's default prompt (Section 2). Table 4 shows alternative strategies provide only "marginal gains" with "extra implementation complexity." Optimize only after establishing baseline benefits.

### 7.5 Summary: The Path Forward

TPT represents a **pragmatic innovation** — conceptually simple, empirically validated at meaningful scale, and immediately actionable for organizations. Its core contribution is demonstrating that **data structure matters as much as data quantity**, opening a new optimization dimension for LLM training.

**Immediate actions for the field:**
1. Replicate TPT's 100B token validation across different model architectures and domains
2. Systematically investigate the generator-target model size relationship
3. Develop thinking quality evaluation frameworks to ensure generated reasoning is correct
4. Extend validation to larger scales (1T+ tokens) and model sizes (70B+ parameters)

**Long-term vision:**
TPT may evolve from a specific augmentation technique into a broader principle: **training data should expose the causal structure of knowledge, not just its conclusions**. This principle could extend beyond reasoning trajectories to include counterfactuals, alternative solution paths, explicit uncertainty modeling, and other structures that make knowledge more learnable.

The paper's final statement captures the aspiration:

> "We hope our findings will inspire continued research into scalable data engineering that maximize the potential of foundation models while making more efficient use of data."

TPT demonstrates one path toward this goal. The research agenda outlined here charts how the field can build upon this foundation to develop increasingly sophisticated methods for transforming raw data into learnable knowledge structures — a capability that will only grow more critical as the pool of high-quality human-authored text reaches its limits.
