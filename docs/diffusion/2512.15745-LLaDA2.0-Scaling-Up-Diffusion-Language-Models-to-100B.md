## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces LLaDA2.0, a suite of discrete diffusion large language models scaling up to 100B parameters, which solves the challenge of bridging the performance and scale gap between diffusion models and dominant auto-regressive (AR) architectures through a novel Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD) conversion strategy rather than costly training from scratch. By systematically converting pre-trained AR checkpoints into diffusion models, the flagship LLaDA2.0-flash (100B) achieves parity with state-of-the-art AR models like Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 on general benchmarks while surpassing them in structured tasks such as coding (HumanEval 94.51) and agent capabilities (BFCL v3 75.43). This work establishes a new paradigm for frontier-scale deployment, validating that diffusion models can deliver superior performance and efficiency via parallel decoding without sacrificing the knowledge inheritance of existing AR foundations.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of LLaDA2.0, we must first establish the current landscape of large language model (LLM) architectures and the specific bottlenecks preventing wider adoption of alternative paradigms. This section dissects the fundamental trade-offs between the dominant Auto-Regressive (AR) approach and the emerging Diffusion paradigm, explaining why bridging the gap between them is both theoretically compelling and practically urgent.

### The Auto-Regressive Bottleneck

The prevailing architecture for modern LLMs is the Auto-Regressive (AR) model. In this paradigm, the model generates text sequentially, predicting one token at a time based on all previous tokens. Mathematically, the probability of a sequence $x = (x_1, x_2, \dots, x_L)$ is factorized as:

$$
p(x) = \prod_{t=1}^{L} p(x_t | x_{<t})
$$

While this aligns naturally with the sequential structure of language and enables stable training via next-token likelihood maximization, it imposes a hard constraint on inference: **generation cannot be parallelized**. Each token $x_t$ depends strictly on the completion of $x_{t-1}$. As model size scales to hundreds of billions of parameters, this sequential dependency creates severe latency bottlenecks, making real-time deployment costly and slow (Section 1).

Furthermore, the rigid causal structure (left-to-right attention) limits the model's ability to perform bidirectional reasoning. For tasks requiring holistic understanding—such as editing existing text, solving constraints where future context matters, or complex planning—the AR paradigm is suboptimal because it cannot "look ahead" during generation (Section 1).

### The Diffusion Promise and the Scale Gap

Discrete Masked Diffusion Language Models (MDLM) offer a compelling alternative. Instead of generating tokens sequentially, diffusion models start with a fully masked (noised) sequence and iteratively denoise it to reconstruct the original text. This process inherently supports **parallel decoding**, as multiple masked tokens can be predicted simultaneously given the surrounding context. Additionally, diffusion models leverage full bidirectional context at every step, potentially enabling superior reasoning capabilities (Section 1).

However, despite these theoretical advantages, diffusion models have not yet displaced AR models in frontier applications. The primary reason is a massive **scale gap**. As noted in Section 2.1, most existing diffusion models, including recent advancements like Block Diffusion Language Models (BDLMs), operate at scales ≤8B parameters. In contrast, leading AR models routinely scale into tens or hundreds of billions of parameters. This disparity matters because model performance typically scales with parameter count and data volume. A 8B diffusion model, even with efficient decoding, cannot compete with a 100B AR model on complex reasoning or knowledge-intensive tasks simply due to capacity limitations (Section 2.1).

### The Challenge of Conversion vs. Training from Scratch

To close this scale gap, researchers face a critical design choice: train diffusion models from scratch, or convert existing pre-trained AR models.

1.  **Training from Scratch:** Approaches like LLaDA (Nie et al., 2025) and LLaDA-MoE (Zhu et al., 2025) demonstrate that diffusion models can be trained independently. However, this path is computationally prohibitive. Training a 100B model from scratch requires immense data volumes and infrastructure maturity that currently favors AR pipelines. Consequently, scratch-trained diffusion models lag behind state-of-the-art AR models in overall performance due to disparities in training data volume and infrastructure optimization (Section 2.1).
2.  **Converting AR Models:** A more efficient route is to initialize a diffusion model from a pre-trained AR checkpoint, leveraging the linguistic knowledge already encoded in the AR weights. This is known as **knowledge inheritance**. However, this conversion is non-trivial. AR models are optimized for causal, left-to-right prediction, while diffusion models require bidirectional denoising. This creates a **data distribution gap**. Directly switching the objective often leads to unstable optimization and "catastrophic forgetting," where the model loses its pretrained capabilities (Section 2.2, Section 4).

### Limitations of Prior Conversion Strategies

Several prior works have attempted to bridge this AR-to-Diffusion gap, but they fall short when scaling to frontier sizes:

*   **Mask Annealing:** Methods like DiffusionLLaMA (Gong et al., 2025) and Dream-7B (Ye et al., 2025) gradually transition from causal to bidirectional attention during training. While effective for smaller models, these strategies often struggle with stability at larger scales.
*   **Direct Conversion:** Approaches like RND1 (Keshigeyan et al., 2025) immediately convert causal attention to bidirectional. While direct, this risks severe performance degradation without careful constraint on dense layer updates to prevent forgetting (Section 2.2).
*   **Block Diffusion (BDLM):** This hybrid paradigm generates text block-by-block (diffusion within blocks, AR between blocks). While BDLMs like SDAR (Cheng et al., 2025) can be initialized from AR models, they suffer from **low data utilization**. The block-wise structure limits the effective exploitation of large-scale corpora, hindering training efficiency for large-size models (Section 2.2).

Critically, existing methods are restricted to model scales ranging from 7B to 30B parameters. Whether initialization strategies can generalize to models beyond 30B remains an open question prior to this work (Section 2.2).

### Positioning LLaDA2.0

LLaDA2.0 positions itself as the first systematic framework to scale discrete diffusion models to **100B parameters** by resolving the conversion instability and efficiency issues described above. Rather than treating AR-to-Diffusion conversion as a single-step finetune, the paper proposes a **progressive adaptation** strategy.

The core innovation lies in recognizing that the model's receptive field must be expanded gradually. The paper introduces the **Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD)** training scheme (Section 1, Section 4):
*   **Warmup:** Progressively increases block size to gently shift the model from AR behavior (block size $L_B=1$) to full sequence diffusion.
*   **Stable:** Trains on large-scale corpora with full sequence masking to deepen diffusion understanding.
*   **Decay:** Reverts to compact block sizes to optimize inference efficiency without losing the global context learned during the stable phase.

This approach addresses the **efficiency-aware design** principle missing in prior work. By preserving the advantages of parallel decoding while inheriting the stability of AR pre-training, LLaDA2.0 aims to deliver a model that is not only competitive in performance but also optimized for practical deployment latency (Section 1). The work thus serves as a practical recipe for the community to leverage AR stability while achieving diffusion parallelism at a scale previously unexplored (Section 1).

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems and methods paper that presents a complete training recipe for converting auto-regressive language models into diffusion language models at unprecedented scale. The core idea is that instead of training diffusion models from scratch, we can systematically transform pre-trained AR checkpoints through a carefully staged process that preserves linguistic knowledge while introducing bidirectional denoising capabilities.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

LLaDA2.0 is a training framework that transforms existing auto-regressive language models into diffusion models through a three-phase conversion process, enabling 100B-parameter models with parallel decoding capabilities. The system solves the fundamental problem that AR models and diffusion models have incompatible training objectives by gradually morphing the model's behavior through block size scheduling, attention mask design, and specialized loss functions.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The LLaDA2.0 training pipeline consists of three major stages connected sequentially. First, the **Continual Pre-training (CPT) stage** takes a pre-trained AR base model (either Ling-mini-2.0 or Ling-flash-2.0) and applies the Warmup-Stable-Decay schedule to convert it into a Masked Diffusion Language Model, outputting a diffusion-capable base model. Second, the **Block Diffusion Pre-training stage** refines the model to operate efficiently with smaller block sizes while retaining global contextual knowledge from the stable phase. Third, the **Post-training stage** applies Supervised Fine-Tuning with complementary masking, Confidence-Aware Parallel training, and Direct Preference Optimization to produce instruction-tuned variants (LLaDA2.0-mini at 16B or LLaDA2.0-flash at 100B). Information flows from the AR checkpoint through CPT (where block size expands then contracts), through post-training alignment (where masking strategies and confidence losses are applied), and finally to deployment with optimized inference engines supporting threshold-based parallel decoding.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the Warmup-Stable-Decay conversion strategy because it is the foundational innovation that enables stable AR-to-diffusion transformation at scale.
- We then explain the document-level attention mask because it addresses a critical training stability issue that arises when packing heterogeneous documents.
- We cover the training objectives (BDLM and MDLM loss functions) next because they define what the model actually optimizes during each phase.
- We proceed to post-training techniques (SFT, CAP, DPO) because they build upon the diffusion foundation to produce usable instruction-following models.
- We conclude with inference mechanisms because they determine how the trained model generates text in practice, including the confidence threshold strategy.
- We finish with infrastructure details because they explain how 100B-parameter training is made computationally feasible.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Type of paper and core framing.** This is a methods paper that introduces a systematic conversion pipeline for transforming auto-regressive language models into discrete diffusion language models at the 100B parameter scale. The fundamental insight is that AR models can be viewed as a special case of Block Diffusion Language Models with block size $L_B=1$, which allows for a gradual transition rather than an abrupt objective switch that would cause catastrophic forgetting.

**The three-stage training pipeline.** Figure 2 illustrates the complete training framework, which progresses through three distinct stages. Stage 1 performs Continual Pre-Training using the Warmup-Stable-Decay strategy to convert the AR base model into a capable diffusion model. Stage 2.1 applies Block Diffusion Supervised Fine-Tuning to align the model with instruction-following tasks. Stage 2.2 optionally applies Confidence-Aware Parallel SFT to sharpen predictions for efficient decoding. Stage 2.3 applies Direct Preference Optimization to align outputs with human preferences. The right panel of Figure 2 shows the document-level block diffusion attention mask, which constructs a single input sequence from multiple noisy and clean examples like $[x_{noisy}^1, \ldots, x_{clean}^1, \ldots]$ and employs block-diagonal ($M_{BD}$), offset block-causal ($M_{OBC}$), and block-causal ($M_{BC}$) masks.

**Warmup-Stable-Decay conversion strategy mechanics.** The WSD strategy decomposes the AR-to-dLLM conversion into three coordinated phases that progressively adjust the model's receptive field. In Phase 1 (Warmup), the block size $L_B$ starts at 1 (equivalent to AR behavior) and incrementally scales through 4, 32, 64, and ultimately reaches 4096, at which point the entire sequence is treated as one single block equivalent to a standard Masked Diffusion Language Model. Each block-size transition trains on moderate-scale data to ensure smooth adaptation, allowing the model to gradually expand its internal representations to handle larger contextual spans and more complex masking patterns. In Phase 2 (Stable), once the block size reaches 4096, the model operates under the full MDLM paradigm where the "clean" part of the attention computation no longer needs to be maintained, significantly reducing computational cost and allowing data to be processed far more efficiently. This phase focuses on deepening the model's understanding of diffusion dynamics through extensive training on large-scale corpora with block size fixed at 4096. In Phase 3 (Decay), after large-scale MDLM training completes, the block size gradually reduces from 4096 back to a small value like 32, converting the model back into an efficient Block Diffusion Language Model. This decay process distills the global contextual knowledge learned during MDLM into a compact blockwise structure, and by decreasing block size step-by-step (for example, from 4096 to 2048) rather than abruptly, the model smoothly adapts from global to local conditioning while preserving semantic understanding and regaining practical benefits like KV-cache reuse and fast variable-length generation.

**Training objective equations and their interpretation.** The Block Diffusion Language Model optimization objective enables the model to accurately reconstruct original, uncorrupted tokens within designated masked blocks using standard cross-entropy loss. During warmup and decay phases (Phase 1 and Phase 3), the training loss under the BDLM paradigm is defined as:

$$
L_{BDLM}(\theta) = -E_{t,x_0,x_t}\left[\frac{\alpha'_t}{1-\alpha_t} \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{i=1}^{L_B} 1[x_{t,k}^i = [MASK]] \log p_\theta(x_{0,k}^i|x_{0,<k}, x_{t,k})\right]
$$

In this equation, the expectation is over timestep $t$, the clean sequence $x_0$, and its corrupted version $x_t$ where tokens are masked with probability $1-\alpha_t$. The indicator function $1[\cdot]$ ensures predictions are made only for masked tokens, and $-\alpha'_t/(1-\alpha_t)$ is the diffusion-derived time weight that scales the loss based on the noise schedule. Here $K = L_{total}/L_B$ represents the number of blocks, $L_B$ is the block size, $x_{t,k}^i$ is the $i$-th token in block $k$, $x_{0,<k}$ represents the preceding clean blocks, and $x_{t,k}$ is the noisy version of the current block. During stable training (Phase 2) of MDLM where $K=1$, the objective simplifies to:

$$
L_{MDLM}(\theta) = -E_{t,x_0,x_t}\left[\frac{\alpha'_t}{1-\alpha_t} \sum_{i=1}^{L} 1[x_t^i = [MASK]] \log p_\theta(x_0^i|x_t)\right]
$$

This simplified form removes the block structure and operates over the full sequence length $L$, treating the entire input as a single diffusion unit.

**Document-level attention mask design rationale.** Training sequences are formed by packing heterogeneous documents into fixed-length segments to maximize throughput, but this introduces artificial long-range dependencies across semantically unrelated texts. Without careful handling, standard attention would incorrectly attend across document boundaries, leading to contextual confusion and significantly hindering the model's ability to perform robust bidirectional modeling crucial for denoising. To mitigate this challenge, the paper redefines the attention mechanism with a specialized block-wise document-level attention mask that ensures attention operates strictly within document boundaries. For a concatenated sequence $x_{full}$ of length $2L$ (comprising $x_t$ followed by $x_0$), and assuming tokens $i$ and $j$ are already confined to the same document segment, the attention mask $M \in \{0, 1\}^{2L \times 2L}$ is constructed by dividing each sequence into contiguous blocks where $b(k) = \lfloor k/L_B \rfloor$ denotes the block index for token $k$ given block size $L_B$. The mask is defined as:

$$
M_{ij} = \begin{cases}
1_{b(i)=b(j)} & \text{if } i \in x_t \text{ and } j \in x_t \\
1_{b(i)>b(j-L)} & \text{if } i \in x_t \text{ and } j \in x_0 \\
1_{b(i-L) \geq b(j-L)} & \text{if } i \in x_0 \text{ and } j \in x_0 \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$

The first condition implements block-diagonal attention within the noisy sequence $x_t$. The second condition enables cross-attention from $x_t$ to $x_0$, but only from blocks in $x_t$ to earlier blocks in $x_0$. The third condition imposes a causal block attention pattern within the clean sequence $x_0$, allowing a block to attend to itself and all preceding blocks. The "otherwise" condition corresponds to a zero matrix, explicitly preventing attention from queries in $x_0$ to keys in $x_t$. For MDLM, this simplifies to $M \in \{0, 1\}^{L \times L}$ where $M_{ij} = 1$ if $i, j$ belong to the same document and $0$ otherwise. The paper notes that during exploration, other techniques like random-length masking and CART were experimented with, but the document-level attention mask proved more fundamental in CPT training and consistently achieved superior performance.

**Top-k checkpoint merging for generalization.** Upon completion of BDLM pre-training, the top $k$ best-performing model checkpoints are identified, typically selected based on validation metrics like perplexity. The parameters (weights and biases) of these $k$ checkpoints are then arithmetically averaged to form a single, unified BDLM. Based on the WSM scheduler, this merge strategy effectively ensembles diverse "knowledge" captured by the model at various optimal or near-optimal training states, which smooths the parameter landscape, mitigates overfitting, and yields a more stable and generalizable model. A key advantage of this approach is its optimizer-agnostic nature, allowing seamless integration without altering the underlying training pipeline. Crucially, this post-training Top-k Merge fundamentally differs from Exponential Moving Average because EMA is an in-training technique that continuously smooths parameters, whereas merging is an offline procedure that explicitly selects and averages distinct, high-performing model states, consolidating their strengths rather than merely smoothing the final training step.

**Supervised Fine-Tuning with block diffusion.** Following pre-training, the model aligns to follow user instructions through supervised fine-tuning by adapting the diffusion training objective to be conditional on an input prompt $c$. The model trains to generate the desired response $x_0$ by minimizing:

$$
L_{SFT}(\theta) = -E_{t,(c,x_0),x_t}\left[\frac{\alpha'_t}{1-\alpha_t} \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{i=1}^{L_B} 1[x_{t,k}^i = [MASK]] \log p_\theta(x_{0,k}^i|c, x_{0,<k}, x_{t,k})\right]
$$

Here the model $p_\theta$ learns to predict original tokens $x_{0,k}^i$ of a clean response from a noisy version $x_t$, with loss computed only on masked tokens within the current noisy block $x_{t,k}$. The prediction conditions on the prompt $c$, auto-regressive context from prior clean blocks $x_{0,<k}$, and the current noisy block $x_{t,k}$ that must be denoised.

**Padding strategies and mask ratio bandwidth.** To ensure compatibility with the block-wise attention mask, each sequence's length is quantized by rounding up to the nearest multiple of the block size $b$, defining an "effective length" that guarantees boundaries align perfectly with block boundaries required by the attention mechanism. To optimize training dynamics, a "mask ratio bandwidth" strategy clips the noise schedule, constraining sampling of mask rates to a bounded interval $[\alpha_{min}, \alpha_{max}]$ rather than the full unit interval $U[0, 1]$. Standard discrete diffusion processes typically sample mask probabilities across the full unit interval, but extreme masking rates induce high gradient variance while offering minimal learning signal: near-zero masking renders reconstruction trivial, while near-total masking reduces the objective to simply learning data marginals. This bandwidth restriction focuses the training objective on noise regimes that provide the most informative gradients, thereby stabilizing convergence and improving the model's generative perplexity.

**Complementary masking for data efficiency.** Complementary Masking is a training optimization that enhances the data efficiency of the MDLM objective by generating two antithetical training instances from a single source sequence $x_0$. A primary noised sequence $x_t$ is formed using a random mask, while a complementary sequence $x'_t$ is simultaneously produced using that mask's logical inverse. By incorporating both $x_t$ and $x'_t$ into the same training batch, this method provides a deterministic guarantee that every token position across the sequence length $L$ is presented to the model in its uncorrupted state exactly once within the pair. This not only doubles the effective data utilization from each sample, thereby accelerating convergence, but also entirely eliminates token-level sampling bias. The paper notes in footnote 1 that complementary masking was tried in CPT but only worked well on corpus less than 100B tokens, showing no advantages with more training data, so it is adopted only in post-training.

**Data recipe curation for SFT.** A balanced, high-quality SFT dataset underpins the model's capabilities through strategic composition spanning three principal pillars: Reasoning, General, and Industrial. The Reasoning pillar hones analytical and logical faculties through mathematics and code generation tasks. The General pillar cultivates linguistic richness and social intelligence via creative and dialogic tasks. The Industrial pillar embeds domain-specific expertise by simulating end-to-end workflows under real-world constraints. This integrated methodology ensures a holistic skill profile, preventing capability skew and enabling fluid shifts between abstract reasoning and applied problem-solving.

**Confidence-Aware Parallel training mechanism.** To enhance the model's predictive confidence, which is crucial for efficient parallel decoding, the paper proposes Confidence-Aware Parallel Training by incorporating an auxiliary confidence loss $L_{conf}$ inspired by dParallel. The primary objective $L_{SFT}$ ensures correctness but provides diminishing incentive to sharpen the predictive distribution for tokens that are already correctly predicted. The confidence loss addresses this by selectively minimizing the entropy of the model's output distribution $p_\theta(x_0|x_t, c)$, but only for the subset of tokens that are correctly predicted in a given step, which compels the model to increase its certainty on correct predictions. The final training objective is a weighted combination:

$$
L(\theta) = L_{SFT}(\theta) + \lambda L_{conf}(\theta)
$$

where $\lambda$ is a hyperparameter that balances the two objectives. As illustrated in Figure 3, CAP training effectively improves the decoding efficiency of LLaDA2.0-flash while maintaining competitive performance, demonstrating a favorable trade-off between generation quality and inference speed. The figure shows LLaDA2.0-flash-CAP achieves 535 tokens per forward compared to 383 for standard LLaDA2.0-flash, and 535 tokens per second compared to 256 and 237 for AR baselines.

**DPO adaptation for diffusion models.** Building upon the SFT stage, the policy model $\pi_\theta$ aligns with human intent using Direct Preference Optimization with a comprehensive dataset comprising 1.5 million preference pairs across diverse domains including general knowledge, mathematics, and instruction following. To ensure stable transition in optimization, the learning rate for the DPO stage initializes consistently with the final learning rate of the preceding SFT phase. Since the policy model $\pi_\theta$ trains to reconstruct clean tokens $x_0$ from noisy blocks $x_t$ conditioned on context $c$, the standard DPO formulation requiring exact log-likelihoods is intractable. Following established practices for diffusion models, conditional log-likelihoods are substituted with their Evidence Lower Bound. The conditional Block Diffusion ELBO $B_{BDLM}(\theta, x|c)$ for a response $x$ mirrors the inner objective of the SFT loss and is estimated via a single Monte Carlo sample over timesteps and noise:

$$
B_{BDLM}(\theta, x|c) = E_{t,x_t}\left[\frac{\alpha'_t}{1-\alpha_t} \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{i=1}^{L_B} 1[x_{t,k}^i = [MASK]] \log p_\theta(x_k^i|c, x_{<k}, x_{t,k})\right]
$$

Given a preference pair $(x_w, x_l)$ where $x_w$ is the preferred response and $x_l$ is the dispreferred response, the DPO objective maximizes the margin between the ELBO estimates of the policy $\pi_\theta$ and the frozen reference model $\pi_{\theta_{ref}}$ (initialized from the post-SFT model). The final loss function is:

$$
L_{DPO}(\theta) = -E_{(c,x_w,x_l) \sim D}\left[\log \sigma\left(\beta \left[\Delta B(x_w|c) - \Delta B(x_l|c)\right]\right)\right]
$$

where $\Delta B(x|c) = B_{BDLM}(\theta, x|c) - B_{BDLM}(\theta_{ref}, x|c)$ represents the ELBO advantage of the policy over the reference model, and $\beta$ is a hyperparameter set to 0.1 that controls deviation from the reference policy.

**Inference sampling strategy.** Generation samples one block at a diffusion step, conditioned on previously sampled blocks $p_\theta(x_s^b|c, x_{<t}^b)$. The generation of each block is itself a multi-step iterative refinement process where, at each step, candidate tokens are sampled for all remaining unfilled positions within the block. A hybrid acceptance strategy is employed: first, all tokens whose sampling probability exceeds a predefined confidence threshold are accepted. If an insufficient number of tokens meet this criterion, a low-confidence fallback triggers where instead a fixed number of the most probable tokens are accepted regardless of their absolute confidence. This dual mechanism ensures steady generation progress. For all LLaDA2.0 models in evaluation, the configuration uses temperature of 0.0, block size of 32, and decoding threshold of 0.95.

**Inference hyperparameter analysis.** Figure 4 presents analysis of denoising threshold and block size trade-offs on the LLaDA2.0-mini model. For denoising threshold with block size fixed at 32, a threshold of 0.95 achieved the highest quality score (70.15) at the cost of the lowest inference speed (2.55 tokens per forward). Lowering the threshold to 0.85 boosted speed to its peak (3.31 TPF) but led to unacceptable quality degradation with score dropping to 67.90. For block size with threshold set to 0.95, block size 16 yielded the highest score (70.26) but slowest inference (2.44 TPF), while increasing to 32 substantially improved speed to 2.55 TPF with only marginal quality drop to 70.15. Further increasing to 64 proved suboptimal, degrading both score and speed relative to size-32. Therefore, block size 32 emerges as the optimal choice, offering significant speed-up for negligible performance cost.

**Context length capabilities.** Figure 5 shows performance on the RULER benchmark across context lengths. Both models demonstrate strong performance and stability within context length of 32k tokens. The LLaDA2.0-flash model is particularly robust, maintaining a score above 93 across all lengths from 4k to 32k. The LLaDA2.0-mini model achieves high scores starting at 93.29 for 4k but shows degradation to 83.94 at 32k. To test extrapolation capabilities, context length extended to 64k using dynamic RoPE scaling during inference, specifically the YaRN method with scaling factor of 2.0. However, this extension resulted in performance degradation for both models, demonstrating a clear trade-off between context length extension and task accuracy.

**Pretraining infrastructure.** The system adopts Megatron-LM as the pretraining backend to enable efficient training of a 100B-parameter model with long sequences, leveraging data parallelism, pipeline parallelism, tensor parallelism, context parallelism, and expert parallelism as Figure 6 shows. To ensure consistency of masked tokens, masked tokens generate on a single model-parallel rank (that is, TP and PP) and then broadcast to all other ranks within the MP ranks. For flexible support of arbitrary block diffusion attention mask, cuDNN serves as the backend for the attention mechanism, achieving more than 1.3x end-to-end speedup and over 90% memory savings in the attention layer compared to the unfused attention implementation in TransformerEngine when training LLaDA2.0-mini. A zig-zag partitioning strategy applies to the block diffusion attention mask to achieve effective load balancing across the CP group.

**Numerical stability during AR-to-diffusion transition.** During the transition from AR to diffusion models, training can suffer from gradient explosion, especially at high mask ratios within a document. This issue stems from the fact that masked token embeddings are set to zero during AR training, as these tokens are never observed, leading their corresponding weights to gradually decay to zero. A straightforward fix of randomly reinitializing the masked token embeddings upon loading the AR model may disrupt other well-trained parameters, potentially causing catastrophic forgetting. To mitigate this while preserving pre-trained knowledge, independent Gaussian noise adds to the output of the embedding layer for each masked token during the initial iterations of training. This ensures that the L2 norm of the masked token's embedding remains significant to avoid gradient explosion, thereby stabilizing the training process.

**Post-training infrastructure.** For the post-training phase, the system leverages dFactory, a repository providing efficient training recipes for dLLMs built upon the VeOmni distributed training framework. The setup for fine-tuning LLaDA2.0 combines Data Parallelism and Expert Parallelism to ensure scalable and stable training. To further enhance data throughput and hardware utilization, a data packing strategy analogous to those used in continued pre-training concatenates multiple short sequences into a single longer sequence.

**Inference engine optimization.** The system adapts dInfer, originally built for high-performance diffusion LLM inference, to efficiently support block diffusion inference. This requires the inference engine to leverage optimization techniques traditionally designed for AR models. For instance, the framework can now effectively exploit KV-cache reuse to substantially reduce prefill computation. As block diffusion inference closely resembles auto-regressive generation in the execution pattern, block diffusion inference support was also incorporated into SGLang, allowing it to benefit from the same class of system-level optimizations designed for AR models. Figure 3 compares average inference throughput of optimized LLaDA2.0-flash models against state-of-the-art AR models of similar scale on four reasoning and code-generation benchmarks (HumanEval, MBPP, GSM8K, and CRUXEval). For diffusion-based models, a threshold decoder with threshold of 0.95 is adopted. AR baselines deploy using SGLang while diffusion models serve with dInfer, ensuring fair performance comparison. LLaDA2.0-flash-CAP reaches 535 tokens per second, outperforming standard LLaDA2.0-flash at 383 TPS and providing up to 2.1× speed-up over AR baselines at 256 and 237 TPS.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the conceptual breakthroughs that distinguish LLaDA2.0 from prior diffusion language model work. Rather than re-explaining the mechanics covered in Section 3, we focus on **why** these innovations matter, what prior assumptions they challenge, and their broader implications for the field.

### 4.1 Warmup-Stable-Decay as a Curriculum for Architectural Conversion

**Prior assumption:** Converting an AR model to diffusion requires either immediate objective switching (RND1) or gradual attention mask annealing (DiffusionLLaMA, Dream-7B). Both approaches treat the conversion as a **single-dimensional transition**—either you change the attention pattern or you change the masking schedule.

**LLaDA2.0's insight:** The AR-to-diffusion gap is fundamentally a **receptive field mismatch**, not just an attention or masking issue. An AR model with block size $L_B=1$ has learned to condition on exactly one token of future context (itself). A full diffusion model with $L_B=4096$ must condition on arbitrary subsets of a 4096-token span. Jumping directly between these regimes causes optimization instability because the model's internal representations are not prepared for the sudden expansion in contextual dependency.

**What makes WSD fundamentally different:**

- **Block size as a curriculum variable:** Prior work annealed attention masks or noise schedules. WSD treats block size $L_B$ itself as the primary scheduling parameter. This is conceptually distinct because block size directly controls the **granularity of joint denoising**—smaller blocks preserve more AR-like locality, while larger blocks force global bidirectional reasoning.
  
- **Three-phase structure is non-negotiable:** The Warmup phase ($L_B: 1 \rightarrow 4096$) expands the receptive field. The Stable phase ($L_B=4096$) trains on large-scale data efficiently under the MDLM paradigm. The Decay phase ($L_B: 4096 \rightarrow 32$) distills global knowledge back into an efficient blockwise structure. Omitting any phase degrades performance. The Decay phase is particularly novel—it recognizes that the optimal training configuration (full-sequence MDLM) is **not** the optimal inference configuration (compact BDLM).

**Significance:** This is the first demonstrated pathway to convert AR models beyond 30B parameters. Prior conversion methods capped at 7B–30B scales (Section 2.2). WSD enables the 100B LLaDA2.0-flash, validating that knowledge inheritance scales to frontier model sizes. The performance parity with Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 (Table 2: 73.18 vs. 73.60 average) while using a fundamentally different generation paradigm is evidence that the conversion preserves linguistic knowledge without catastrophic forgetting.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation**. Prior work treated AR-to-diffusion as a finetuning problem. WSD reframes it as a **progressive architectural morphing** problem, which is a conceptual shift in how we think about model conversion.

### 4.2 Document-Level Attention Mask: Solving a Hidden Stability Bottleneck

**Prior assumption:** Packed training sequences (concatenating multiple documents into fixed-length segments) are standard for throughput optimization in AR models. The same packing strategies were assumed to transfer directly to diffusion training.

**LLaDA2.0's insight:** Packed training introduces **spurious cross-document dependencies** that are far more damaging for diffusion models than for AR models. In AR training, causal masking naturally prevents tokens from attending to future documents. In bidirectional diffusion training, without explicit document boundaries, the model can form attention connections between semantically unrelated texts, leading to contextual confusion during denoising.

**What makes this mask fundamentally different:**

- **Block-wise document confinement:** The mask (Equation 3, Section 4.2) enforces attention constraints at **two levels simultaneously**: within blocks (block-diagonal structure) and within documents (document boundaries). Prior BDLM work applied block masks but did not explicitly prevent cross-document attention in packed sequences.

- **Asymmetric attention between noisy and clean sequences:** The mask treats $x_t$ (noisy) and $x_0$ (clean) differently. Queries in $x_0$ cannot attend to keys in $x_t$ (the "otherwise" condition in Equation 3), preventing the clean sequence from leaking information about the noise pattern. This asymmetry is crucial for maintaining the integrity of the denoising objective.

**Significance:** The paper explicitly states that during exploration, techniques like random-length masking and CART were experimented with, but "the document-level attention mask is more fundamental in CPT training compared to these techniques, and it consistently achieves superior performance" (Section 4.2). This suggests that without this mask, stable 100B-scale diffusion training is not feasible—the model would learn spurious correlations that degrade generalization.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** for diffusion training at scale. While attention masking is standard, the specific design for packed heterogeneous documents in bidirectional diffusion is novel and addresses a stability issue that only emerges at large scales with packed training.

---

### 4.3 Confidence-Aware Parallel Training: Training-Time Optimization for Inference Speed

**Prior assumption:** Diffusion model inference speed is primarily a **sampling algorithm problem**. Prior acceleration methods like DPad (Chen et al., 2025a) and D2F (Wang et al., 2025c) focus on post-training optimizations—reducing the number of denoising steps or enabling parallel block generation through algorithmic tricks.

**LLaDA2.0's insight:** The root cause of slow diffusion inference is not just the number of steps, but the **model's lack of predictive confidence**. If the model is uncertain about its predictions, the decoder must proceed conservatively, accepting fewer tokens per step. By training the model to be "sharper" (lower entropy on correct predictions), we enable more aggressive parallel decoding **without changing the sampling algorithm**.

**What makes CAP fundamentally different:**

- **Auxiliary loss during training, not post-hoc:** CAP adds $L_{conf}$ to the SFT objective (Equation 6, Section 5.2), directly optimizing the model's output distribution sharpness. Prior methods modify the decoder; CAP modifies the **model itself**.

- **Selective entropy minimization:** The confidence loss only applies to tokens that are already correctly predicted. This prevents the model from becoming overconfident on wrong predictions—a critical distinction from naive entropy regularization.

**Significance:** Figure 3 shows LLaDA2.0-flash-CAP achieves 535 tokens per second, a **2.1× speed-up** over AR baselines (256 and 237 TPS). This is the first demonstration that a diffusion model can **surpass AR inference speed** at the 100B scale while maintaining quality. The speed gain comes from training, not from a faster decoder—meaning the improvement is model-intrinsic and transfers across inference engines.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation**. Prior work treated inference speed as a systems problem. CAP reframes it as a **training objective problem**, shifting the optimization burden from inference-time algorithms to training-time model shaping.

---

### 4.4 Diffusion Models May Have Inherent Advantages in Structured Tasks

**Prior assumption:** Diffusion models are primarily valuable for their **parallel decoding efficiency**. Performance-wise, they were expected to lag behind AR models, with the trade-off being speed for quality.

**LLaDA2.0's insight:** At 100B scale, diffusion models do not just match AR models—they **surpass them in specific domains**. Table 2 shows LLaDA2.0-flash outperforming Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 on HumanEval (94.51 vs. 93.29), MBPP (88.29 vs. 86.65), and BFCL v3 (75.43 vs. 73.19). This pattern is consistent: coding, agent capabilities, and advanced mathematics (AIME 2025: 60.00 vs. 61.88, nearly parity) show diffusion advantages or parity.

**Why this might happen (hypothesis from the paper):**

- **Bidirectional context during generation:** Coding and constraint-satisfaction tasks often require reasoning about the entire structure simultaneously (e.g., ensuring variable names are consistent throughout a function, or satisfying multiple constraints in a math proof). AR models must commit to tokens left-to-right without seeing future context. Diffusion models can revise any position given full bidirectional context at each denoising step.

- **Structured generation benefits from parallelism:** Code has strong local dependencies (indentation, brackets, variable scope) that can be resolved in parallel within a block. The BDLM paradigm with $L_B=32$ allows the model to generate coherent code blocks in fewer steps than AR's token-by-token approach.

**Significance:** This challenges the prevailing narrative that diffusion models are a **speed-focused alternative** to AR. Instead, LLaDA2.0 suggests diffusion may be a **capability-focused alternative** for specific task classes. The paper states: "This suggests that as diffusion models scale, their inherent strengths in structured generation and tool use become increasingly apparent" (Section 6.2).

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental empirical insight**. Prior diffusion work at ≤8B scale could not test this hypothesis because capacity limitations masked architectural advantages. LLaDA2.0 is the first to demonstrate domain-specific superiority at frontier scale.

---

### 4.5 Knowledge Inheritance Without Catastrophic Forgetting at 100B Scale

**Prior assumption:** Converting AR models to diffusion inevitably involves **knowledge loss**. RND1 (Keshigeyan et al., 2025) observed that preserving knowledge-intensive capabilities requires constraining updates to dense layers to prevent catastrophic forgetting. This implies a hard trade-off: you can have diffusion capabilities or AR knowledge, but not both at full strength.

**LLaDA2.0's insight:** Catastrophic forgetting is not inevitable—it is a symptom of **abrupt objective switching**. The WSD curriculum (Section 4.1) combined with numerical stability techniques (Section 7.1: adding Gaussian noise to masked token embeddings rather than reinitializing them) enables the model to retain AR knowledge while acquiring diffusion capabilities.

**Evidence of successful inheritance:**

- **Knowledge benchmarks:** LLaDA2.0-flash scores 87.69 on MMLU and 73.36 on MMLU-Pro (Table 2), matching Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 (87.13 and 74.23). These are knowledge-intensive tasks where forgetting would be immediately apparent.

- **No dense layer freezing:** Unlike RND1, LLaDA2.0 does not constrain dense layer updates. The gradual WSD schedule alone prevents forgetting, suggesting that **optimization dynamics**, not parameter freezing, is the key to knowledge preservation.

**Significance:** This validates knowledge inheritance as a **viable scaling strategy** for diffusion models. Training a 100B diffusion model from scratch would require computational resources and data volumes that are currently prohibitive. By inheriting AR checkpoints, LLaDA2.0 achieves frontier performance with a fraction of the training cost.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in conversion methodology. Prior work assumed knowledge preservation required architectural constraints (freezing layers). LLaDA2.0 shows that **training schedule design** alone can preserve knowledge, which is more flexible and scalable.

| Warmup-Stable-Decay | Fundamental | Single-step conversion or mask annealing | Block size as curriculum variable with three-phase structure |
| Document-Level Mask | Fundamental | Standard packed training without document boundaries | Block-wise document confinement with asymmetric attention |

The work demonstrates that diffusion models can inherit AR stability, match AR performance on general benchmarks, and exceed AR capabilities in structured domains—all while delivering 2.1× inference speed-ups through parallel decoding.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section critically examines the empirical evidence provided in the paper. We move beyond the mechanical descriptions of the training pipeline to assess **how** the authors validated their claims, **what** the benchmarks reveal about the model's true capabilities, and **where** the limitations lie. The evaluation is designed to answer three core questions: Does the conversion preserve knowledge? Does the diffusion paradigm offer tangible benefits in specific domains? And is the efficiency gain real-world viable?

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Baseline Selection

The authors employ a comprehensive evaluation suite comprising **47 benchmarks** categorized into five distinct dimensions (Section 6.1). This breadth is critical because diffusion models might excel in structured tasks (coding) while lagging in open-ended knowledge retrieval. The categories are:

*   **Knowledge:** Standard academic benchmarks like `MMLU`, `MMLU-Pro`, `CMMLU`, and `C-EVAL`. These test factual retention and general world knowledge.
*   **Reasoning:** Tasks requiring logical deduction, such as `DROP`, `SQuAD 2.0`, `BIG-Bench Hard`, and `ZebraLogic`.
*   **Coding:** Synthesis and debugging tasks including `HumanEval`, `MBPP`, `LiveCodeBench`, and `Spider` (text-to-SQL).
*   **Math:** Grade school to Olympiad level problems (`GSM8K`, `MATH`, `AIME 2025`).
*   **Agent & Alignment:** Function calling and instruction following (`BFCL`, `IFEval`, `Nexus FC`).

**Baseline Selection Strategy:**
The choice of baselines is deliberate and stratified by model size to ensure fair comparison:
1.  **For LLaDA2.0-mini (16B):** The primary baseline is `Ling-mini-2.0`, the direct AR parent model. This isolates the effect of the **conversion process** itself. A secondary baseline is `Qwen3-8B`, a strong open-source AR model, to gauge competitiveness against external peers.
2.  **For LLaDA2.0-flash (100B):** The primary baseline is `Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507`. Note the parameter disparity: LLaDA2.0-flash has **100B total parameters** (likely sparse MoE given the "Flash" naming convention and comparison to A3B) compared to Qwen3's 30B. However, the comparison focuses on **performance parity** rather than parameter-matched efficiency, aiming to prove diffusion can reach frontier performance levels. `Ling-flash-2.0` serves as the AR parent baseline.

**Inference Configuration:**
To ensure reproducibility, the authors fix key inference hyperparameters across all main evaluations (Section 6.1):
*   **Temperature:** $0.0$ (greedy decoding) to minimize variance in scoring.
*   **Block Size:** $32$ tokens per block.
*   **Decoding Threshold:** $0.95$ confidence threshold for token acceptance.

This fixed setup is crucial because diffusion inference is sensitive to these parameters (as shown later in Section 5.3). By fixing them, the authors ensure that performance differences reflect model capability rather than tuning artifacts.

### 5.2 Performance Across Capability Dimensions

The results are presented in **Table 1** (Mini) and **Table 2** (Flash). A granular analysis reveals a nuanced picture: diffusion does not uniformly beat AR, but it shows distinct strengths in structured domains.

#### Knowledge Retention (The "Forgetting" Test)
A primary risk in AR-to-Diffusion conversion is catastrophic forgetting. The knowledge benchmarks serve as a control group to verify knowledge inheritance.
*   **LLaDA2.0-mini:** Achieves an average score of **64.34**, closely approaching its AR parent `Ling-mini-2.0` (**65.77**). On `MMLU`, it scores **80.53** vs. **82.15** for the AR base. This ~1.6 point drop is minimal given the architectural shift, suggesting the **Warmup-Stable-Decay** strategy successfully preserves priors.
*   **LLaDA2.0-flash:** Scores **73.18** average, nearly identical to `Qwen3-30B` (**73.60**). On `MMLU-Pro`, it scores **73.36** vs. **74.23**.
*   **Analysis:** The data supports the claim of **knowledge inheritance**. The diffusion models do not exhibit significant degradation in static knowledge tasks, validating that the WSD curriculum prevents the model from "unlearning" its AR foundations during the bidirectional adaptation phase.

#### Structured Generation (Coding and Agents)
This is where the diffusion hypothesis is most strongly tested. The paper claims bidirectional context aids structured tasks.
*   **Coding:** LLaDA2.0-flash achieves **94.51** on `HumanEval`, surpassing `Qwen3-30B` (**93.29**). On `MBPP`, it scores **88.29** vs. **86.65**. The gap widens on `MultiPL-E` (**74.87** vs. **70.67**).
*   **Agents:** On `BFCL v3` (function calling), LLaDA2.0-flash scores **75.43**, leading `Qwen3-30B` (**73.19**).
*   **Analysis:** These results are statistically significant enough to suggest an architectural advantage. Coding tasks often require consistency across a file (e.g., variable naming, bracket matching). The ability to denoise a block of code with full bidirectional context ($L_B=32$) likely allows the model to resolve dependencies that an AR model must guess sequentially.

#### Complex Reasoning and Math (The Limitations)
While coding shows gains, complex reasoning shows mixed results.
*   **Reasoning:** On `BIG-Bench Extra Hard`, LLaDA2.0-flash scores **27.86**, significantly trailing `Qwen3-30B` (**37.80**). On `ZebraLogic`, it scores **82.30** vs. **90.97**.
*   **Math:** On `AIME 2025` (competition math), LLaDA2.0-flash scores **60.00**, slightly below `Qwen3-30B` (**61.88**). On `HARDMath2`, both models struggle, scoring **4.27**.
*   **Analysis:** This reveals a boundary condition for the diffusion advantage. While bidirectional context helps with *structure* (code), it does not necessarily enhance *multi-step logical deduction* where chain-of-thought (CoT) length matters. AR models generate CoT sequentially, which may align better with certain reasoning workflows than block-wise denoising. The deficit in `BIG-Bench Extra Hard` suggests that for pure reasoning, the diffusion paradigm has not yet surpassed optimized AR architectures.

### 5.3 Efficiency and Inference Trade-offs

Performance is only half the story; the other half is efficiency. The paper provides detailed ablation on inference parameters in **Figure 3** and **Figure 4**.

#### Throughput vs. Quality (Figure 3)
The most striking efficiency claim is the tokens-per-second (TPS) comparison.
*   **LLaDA2.0-flash-CAP:** Achieves **535 TPS**.
*   **LLaDA2.0-flash (Standard):** Achieves **383 TPS**.
*   **AR Baselines:** `Ling-flash-2.0` and `Qwen3-30B` achieve **256 TPS** and **237 TPS** respectively.
*   **Analysis:** The **2.1× speed-up** over AR baselines is the strongest empirical validation of the diffusion promise. Crucially, this speed-up is achieved *without* sacrificing quality (as seen in Table 2). The **Confidence-Aware Parallel (CAP)** training adds an additional **40% throughput gain** (383 → 535 TPS) over the standard diffusion model, proving that training the model to be "sharper" directly translates to system-level latency reductions.

#### Hyperparameter Sensitivity (Figure 4)
The authors analyze the trade-off between the **Denoising Threshold** and **Block Size** using LLaDA2.0-mini.
*   **Threshold:** A threshold of $0.95$ yields the best quality score (**70.15**) but slower speed (**2.55 Tokens Per Forward**). Lowering to $0.85$ increases speed to **3.31 TPF** but drops quality to **67.90**.
*   **Block Size:** A block size of $32$ is identified as the "sweet spot." Size $16$ is slightly higher quality (**70.26**) but slower (**2.44 TPF**). Size $64$ degrades both quality and speed.
*   **Analysis:** This ablation justifies the main evaluation setup (Threshold $0.95$, Block Size $32$). It demonstrates that the efficiency gains are not free; they require careful tuning. The drop in performance at Block Size $64$ suggests that the model's denoising capability degrades if the block becomes too large for the current training regime, reinforcing the design choice of $L_B=32$ for inference.

### 5.4 Robustness and Context Length Limits

A critical stress test for any LLM is long-context handling. The paper uses the **RULER benchmark** to evaluate context lengths up to 64k tokens (**Figure 5**).

*   **Native Context (32k):** LLaDA2.0-flash is highly robust, maintaining a score **above 93** across all lengths from 4k to 32k. LLaDA2.0-mini shows more degradation, dropping from **93.29** (4k) to **83.94** (32k).
*   **Extrapolated Context (64k):** The authors attempt to extend context using **YaRN** (dynamic RoPE scaling) with a factor of $2.0$.
*   **Result:** Both models show **performance degradation** at 64k.
*   **Analysis:** This is a crucial failure case to acknowledge. While the models are robust within their native training window (32k), they do not naturally extrapolate beyond it without cost. The degradation confirms that the attention mechanisms (even with document-level masking) are optimized for the trained context length. Users requiring >32k context must accept a accuracy penalty, a trade-off common in AR models but important to highlight for diffusion deployments.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Validity

Do the experiments convincingly support the paper's claims?

**Strengths:**
1.  **Holistic Benchmarking:** Using 47 benchmarks prevents "cherry-picking" results. The admission of deficits in `BIG-Bench Extra Hard` adds credibility to the claims of success in Coding.
2.  **Infrastructure-Aware Evaluation:** The speed measurements (TPS) are conducted using optimized inference engines (`dInfer` vs. `SGLang`), ensuring the comparison reflects real-world deployment rather than theoretical FLOPs.
3.  **Ablation of Conversion:** By comparing against the AR parent (`Ling`), the authors isolate the effect of the diffusion conversion from the effect of model scaling.

**Weaknesses and Open Questions:**
1.  **Parameter Mismatch in Flash Comparison:** LLaDA2.0-flash (100B) is compared against Qwen3-30B. While LLaDA2.0 achieves parity, it uses **3.3× more parameters** to do so. The efficiency gain comes from parallel decoding, not parameter efficiency. A comparison against a 100B AR model would be ideal to confirm if diffusion is truly more compute-efficient at the same scale, though such models are rarely open-sourced.
2.  **Reasoning Gap:** The consistent underperformance on `BIG-Bench Extra Hard` and `ZebraLogic` suggests diffusion models may still struggle with tasks requiring long, sequential logical chains. The paper does not propose a solution for this specific deficit.
3.  **Context Extrapolation:** The drop at 64k indicates that diffusion models inherit the context limitations of their AR parents (RoPE-based) without necessarily improving extrapolation capabilities.

**Conclusion on Experiments:**
The experimental design is rigorous and supports the primary claim: **Diffusion models can be scaled to 100B parameters via conversion to match AR performance while offering superior inference speed.** However, the results also clarify that this is not a universal upgrade; it is a **specialized advantage** for structured tasks (coding, agents) and latency-sensitive deployments, rather than a blanket replacement for AR models in all reasoning domains.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While LLaDA2.0 demonstrates that diffusion language models can scale to 100B parameters and compete with auto-regressive architectures, the approach relies on several assumptions and introduces non-trivial trade-offs. This section critically examines where the method succeeds, where it struggles, and what constraints practitioners must accept when adopting this paradigm.

### 6.1 Parameter Efficiency Trade-off

**The 100B vs. 30B Disparity**

A critical limitation emerges when examining the parameter counts in the main comparisons. LLaDA2.0-flash contains **100B total parameters**, yet its primary baseline in Table 2 is `Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507` with only **30B parameters**. To achieve performance parity (73.18 vs. 73.60 average score), the diffusion model requires **3.3× more parameters**.

> "LLaDA2.0-flash achieves parity with the powerful Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 and establishes a lead in the critical Coding and Agent domains." (Section 6.2)

This raises an important question: **Is diffusion inherently less parameter-efficient than auto-regression?** The paper does not provide a comparison against a 100B AR model, which would be necessary to determine whether the performance gap closes at equal scale or whether diffusion requires more parameters to match AR capabilities. The efficiency gains come from **inference parallelism** (2.1× speed-up in tokens per second), not from **parameter efficiency**.

**Practical Implication:** Organizations with strict memory or deployment constraints may find the 100B parameter requirement prohibitive, even if inference is faster. The trade-off is **latency for capacity**—you get faster generation but need more GPU memory to load the model.

### 6.2 Reasoning Capability Gaps

**Structured Tasks vs. Logical Deduction**

The experimental results reveal a clear boundary condition for diffusion advantages. While LLaDA2.0-flash excels in structured generation tasks (coding, agents), it consistently **underperforms on pure reasoning benchmarks**:

| Benchmark | LLaDA2.0-flash | Qwen3-30B | Gap |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|-----|
| BIG-Bench Extra Hard | 27.86 | 37.80 | **-9.94** |
| ZebraLogic | 82.30 | 90.97 | **-8.67** |
| AIME 2025 | 60.00 | 61.88 | -1.88 |
| HARDMath2 | 4.27 | 4.27 | 0.00 |

(Table 2, Section 6.2)

**Why This Matters:** The diffusion paradigm's bidirectional context helps with **structural consistency** (e.g., matching brackets in code, maintaining variable names) but does not inherently enhance **multi-step logical deduction**. Tasks requiring long chains of reasoning (BIG-Bench Extra Hard, ZebraLogic) may actually benefit from the sequential nature of AR generation, which naturally aligns with chain-of-thought workflows.

**Open Question:** The paper acknowledges this gap but does not propose a solution. Future work may need to explore hybrid approaches (e.g., "think in AR, generate in diffusion") or specialized training objectives for reasoning tasks.

### 6.3 Context Length Extrapolation Limits

**Native Window vs. Extended Context**

Figure 5 presents the RULER benchmark results across context lengths. The models show strong performance within their **native 32k training window**:

- LLaDA2.0-flash maintains scores **above 93** from 4k to 32k tokens.
- LLaDA2.0-mini degrades from **93.29** (4k) to **83.94** (32k).

However, when extended to **64k tokens** using YaRN scaling (factor of 2.0), both models exhibit **performance degradation**:

> "However, this extension resulted in a performance degradation for both models, demonstrating a clear trade-off between context length extension and task accuracy." (Section 6.3)

**Root Cause:** The document-level attention mask (Equation 3, Section 4.2) is optimized for the trained context length. While it prevents cross-document contamination effectively at 32k, the attention patterns do not naturally extrapolate to longer sequences. This is not unique to diffusion models—AR models with RoPE also struggle with extrapolation—but it means diffusion models **inherit the context limitations of their AR parents** without necessarily improving upon them.

**Practical Implication:** Applications requiring >32k context (e.g., long document analysis, extended codebase understanding) must accept an accuracy penalty or explore alternative scaling methods not covered in this work.

### 6.4 Training Complexity and Stability Requirements

**Multi-Phase Scheduling Overhead**

The Warmup-Stable-Decay strategy, while effective, introduces significant **training complexity** compared to standard AR or diffusion pipelines:

1. **Three distinct phases** with different block sizes ($L_B: 1 \rightarrow 4096 \rightarrow 32$).
2. **Moderate-scale data** required for each block-size transition to ensure smooth adaptation (Section 4.1).
3. **Specialized attention masks** that must be recomputed as block sizes change (Equation 3, Section 4.2).

**Numerical Stability Fragility:** Section 7.1 reveals that the AR-to-diffusion transition is prone to **gradient explosion**, especially at high mask ratios:

> "This issue stems from the fact that masked token embeddings are set to zero during AR training, as these tokens are never observed, leading their corresponding weights to gradually decay to zero." (Section 7.1)

The fix—adding **independent Gaussian noise** to masked token embeddings during initial iterations—is a non-trivial intervention that adds another hyperparameter to tune. A simpler approach (randomly reinitializing embeddings) was rejected due to catastrophic forgetting risks.

**Practical Implication:** Reproducing LLaDA2.0's training requires careful orchestration of multiple moving parts. Small deviations in the WSD schedule or numerical stability handling could lead to training instability or performance degradation.

### 6.5 Inference Hyperparameter Sensitivity

**Threshold and Block Size Dependence**

Figure 4 demonstrates that diffusion inference is **highly sensitive** to hyperparameter choices:

- **Denoising Threshold:** Lowering from 0.95 to 0.85 increases speed from 2.55 to 3.31 TPF but drops quality from **70.15 to 67.90** (a 2.25 point drop).
- **Block Size:** Increasing from 32 to 64 **degrades both speed and quality**, making it strictly suboptimal.

> "Further increasing the block size to 64 proved suboptimal, as it degraded both score and speed relative to the size-32 setting." (Section 6.3)

**Contrast with AR Models:** Auto-regressive inference has fewer critical hyperparameters (primarily temperature and top-p). Diffusion models require tuning **threshold**, **block size**, and potentially the **number of denoising steps**, making deployment more complex.

**Practical Implication:** Production deployments must carefully validate hyperparameter choices for their specific use case. A threshold optimized for coding tasks may not generalize to creative writing or reasoning tasks.

### 6.6 Complementary Masking Data Efficiency Limits

**Corpus Size Dependency**

Footnote 1 in Section 5.1 reveals an important limitation of the Complementary Masking technique:

> "We also try complementary masking in CPT and find it only works fine on corpus less than 100B tokens, while it does not show advantages with more training data, so that we only adopt it in post-training." (Footnote 1, Section 5)

**Interpretation:** Complementary Masking doubles data utilization by presenting each token in both masked and unmasked states within a training pair. However, this benefit **diminishes at scale**. With sufficient training data (>100B tokens), the marginal gain from complementary masking becomes negligible, suggesting that **data volume can substitute for data efficiency tricks**.

**Practical Implication:** For organizations training on massive corpora, complementary masking may not justify its implementation complexity. It is most valuable for resource-constrained settings with limited data.

### 6.7 Infrastructure and Ecosystem Dependencies

**Specialized Training and Inference Stack**

LLaDA2.0 relies on a **custom infrastructure stack** that may not be widely available:

| Component | Purpose | Availability |
|-----------|---------|--------------|
| Megatron-LM | Pretraining backend | Open-source |
| dFactory | Post-training recipes | GitHub (InclusionAI) |
| dInfer | Diffusion inference engine | GitHub (InclusionAI) |
| cuDNN backend | Attention optimization | NVIDIA proprietary |
| SGLang integration | AR-style optimizations | Emerging support |

(Section 7.1–7.3)

**Contrast with AR Ecosystem:** Auto-regressive models benefit from mature, widely-adopted inference frameworks (vLLM, TGI, SGLang). Diffusion LLM inference is still nascent, with fewer optimization options and less community support.

**Practical Implication:** Adopting LLaDA2.0 requires commitment to a less mature ecosystem. Organizations may face integration challenges, limited debugging tools, and slower iteration cycles compared to AR deployments.

### 6.8 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

**Scenarios Not Evaluated**

The paper's evaluation, while comprehensive (47 benchmarks), leaves several important scenarios unaddressed:

1. **Streaming Generation:** Diffusion models generate in blocks, not token-by-token. Applications requiring **true streaming** (e.g., real-time translation, live captioning) may find block-wise generation introduces perceptible latency bursts.

2. **Incremental Editing:** One theoretical advantage of diffusion is the ability to edit existing text by re-denoising specific spans. The paper does not evaluate this capability, leaving it unclear whether LLaDA2.0 supports **partial regeneration** efficiently.

3. **Multilingual Performance:** While CMMLU and C-Eval are included, the evaluation is heavily skewed toward English benchmarks. Performance on low-resource languages or non-Latin scripts is not explicitly analyzed.

4. **Safety and Alignment Robustness:** The DPO stage uses 1.5M preference pairs (Section 5.3), but the paper does not report on **adversarial robustness**, jailbreak resistance, or harmful output rates—critical metrics for deployment.

5. **Long-Chain-of-Thought Reasoning:** Section 2.3 mentions TraDo series as the "first dLLM capable of long-chain-of-thought reasoning," but LLaDA2.0 does not explicitly evaluate or optimize for this capability. The reasoning deficits in Table 2 suggest this remains an open challenge.

### 6.9 Open Questions and Future Directions

The Conclusion (Section 8) explicitly acknowledges several unresolved areas:

> "Future work may point to further scaling of the parameter volume, RL/thinking paradigm and extending the decoding speed to its extreme." (Section 8)

**Key Open Questions:**

1. **Can diffusion models exceed 100B parameters?** The paper validates scaling to 100B but does not explore whether the WSD strategy continues to work at 500B or 1T scales.

2. **How does RL integration work for diffusion?** The paper mentions RL as future work but does not address how policy gradient methods (which rely on log-likelihoods) adapt to diffusion's intractable likelihoods. Section 2.3 notes SPG and TraceRL as early attempts, but LLaDA2.0 does not incorporate these.

3. **What is the optimal sparse MoE configuration?** Both LLaDA2.0-mini and flash are described as MoE variants, but the paper does not detail the **expert count**, **activation sparsity**, or **load balancing** strategies. This limits reproducibility for practitioners wanting to replicate the architecture.

4. **Can the reasoning gap be closed?** The consistent underperformance on BIG-Bench Extra Hard and ZebraLogic suggests a fundamental limitation. Whether this is addressable through training objectives, architecture changes, or hybrid approaches remains unknown.

### 6.10 Summary of Trade-offs

| Dimension | Advantage | Trade-off |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Inference Speed** | 2.1× faster than AR (535 vs. 256 TPS) | Requires 3.3× more parameters for parity |
| **Structured Tasks** | Superior on coding (HumanEval 94.51) and agents (BFCL 75.43) | Inferior on pure reasoning (BIG-Bench Extra Hard -9.94) |
| **Context Length** | Robust up to 32k native window | Degrades at 64k extrapolation |
| **Training** | Inherits AR knowledge without from-scratch cost | Requires complex 3-phase WSD scheduling |
| **Deployment** | Parallel decoding reduces latency | Sensitive to threshold/block size hyperparameters |
| **Ecosystem** | Custom optimized stack (dInfer, dFactory) | Less mature than AR inference frameworks |

**Bottom Line:** LLaDA2.0 is not a universal replacement for auto-regressive models. It is a **specialized alternative** optimized for **latency-sensitive, structured generation tasks** where parallel decoding provides tangible benefits. Organizations should adopt it when inference speed and structured output quality are priorities, but should carefully evaluate whether the parameter requirements and reasoning gaps align with their use case.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

LLaDA2.0 represents a inflection point for diffusion language models (dLLMs). Prior to this work, diffusion architectures were largely viewed as promising but immature alternatives to auto-regressive (AR) models, constrained by small scale (≤8B) and performance gaps. By demonstrating that a 100B-parameter diffusion model can match AR performance on general benchmarks while surpassing it in inference speed and structured tasks, this work shifts the field's focus from **feasibility** to **optimization**. This section outlines how this paradigm shift reshapes the research landscape, identifies concrete avenues for follow-up work, and provides guidance for practitioners considering adoption.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Landscape: From Niche to Frontier

The most significant implication of LLaDA2.0 is the validation of **knowledge inheritance** at frontier scale. Previously, the community faced a dichotomy: train diffusion models from scratch (costly, limited scale) or convert AR models (risk of catastrophic forgetting). LLaDA2.0 resolves this via the Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD) strategy, proving that AR checkpoints can serve as robust foundations for diffusion capabilities without freezing layers or sacrificing performance.

> "This work establishes a new paradigm for frontier-scale deployment... validating that diffusion models can deliver superior performance and efficiency via parallel decoding without sacrificing the knowledge inheritance of existing AR foundations." (Abstract)

This changes the strategic calculus for model developers in three ways:

1.  **Inference Speed is Now a Training Objective:** Prior acceleration methods (e.g., DPad, D2F) focused on algorithmic tricks at inference time. LLaDA2.0 demonstrates via Confidence-Aware Parallel (CAP) training that inference speed can be optimized **during pre-training and fine-tuning**. The 2.1× speed-up over AR baselines (535 vs. 256 tokens per second in Figure 3) is not just a system optimization—it is a model capability learned via the auxiliary confidence loss $L_{conf}$.
2.  **Structured Tasks as a Diffusion Stronghold:** The results in Table 2 suggest diffusion models may have an architectural advantage in domains requiring bidirectional consistency, such as code generation (HumanEval 94.51 vs. 93.29) and agent tool use (BFCL v3 75.43 vs. 73.19). This challenges the assumption that AR is universally superior for all generative tasks.
3.  **Scale Laws Apply to Diffusion:** By successfully scaling to 100B parameters, LLaDA2.0 confirms that diffusion models follow similar scaling trajectories to AR models when provided with appropriate training curricula. This invites renewed investment in diffusion-specific scaling laws, which were previously obscured by capacity limitations.

### 7.2 Critical Avenues for Follow-up Research

While LLaDA2.0 closes several gaps, it explicitly leaves others open. The paper's limitations (Section 6) and conclusion (Section 8) point to specific high-value research directions.

#### 7.2.1 Bridging the Reasoning Gap
LLaDA2.0-flash underperforms on complex reasoning benchmarks like `BIG-Bench Extra Hard` (27.86 vs. 37.80) and `ZebraLogic` (82.30 vs. 90.97) compared to AR baselines (Table 2). This suggests that bidirectional context aids **structural consistency** but does not inherently improve **sequential logical deduction**.
*   **Research Question:** Can hybrid architectures combine AR's strength in chain-of-thought (CoT) with diffusion's parallel generation?
*   **Potential Direction:** Investigate "Think in AR, Generate in Diffusion" pipelines, where the reasoning trace is generated auto-regressively to ensure logical coherence, followed by block-diffusion decoding for the final output to maximize speed. Related work like LiDAR (Liu et al., 2025) mentioned in Section 2.3 explores this, but integration at 100B scale remains untested.

#### 7.2.2 Reinforcement Learning for Diffusion
The paper identifies RL as a key future direction (Section 8). Standard policy gradient methods rely on tractable log-likelihoods, which are intractable for diffusion models.
*   **Research Question:** How do we scale RL techniques like SPG (Sandwich Policy Gradient) or TraceRL (mentioned in Section 2.3) to 100B parameters?
*   **Potential Direction:** Adapt Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) variants that better approximate the true likelihood gap. The current DPO implementation uses an Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) substitute (Equation 8, Section 5.3). Future work could explore tighter bounds or reward modeling techniques specifically tuned for the iterative denoising trajectory.

#### 7.2.3 Context Extrapolation Mechanisms
Performance degrades when extending context from 32k to 64k using YaRN (Figure 5). This indicates that the document-level attention mask (Equation 3) and positional embeddings are optimized for the native training window.
*   **Research Question:** Can diffusion-specific positional encodings or attention mechanisms improve extrapolation beyond trained context lengths?
*   **Potential Direction:** Explore RoPE variants designed for bidirectional attention, or train with curriculum-based context lengths that gradually expand beyond 32k during the Stable phase of WSD.

#### 7.2.4 Sparse MoE Architecture Details
Both LLaDA2.0-mini and flash are described as Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) variants (Abstract), yet the paper does not detail the expert count, activation sparsity, or load balancing losses.
*   **Research Question:** What is the optimal MoE configuration for diffusion models?
*   **Potential Direction:** Systematic ablation of expert counts and routing algorithms. Diffusion models may benefit from different sparsity patterns than AR models due to their bidirectional context usage.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

For practitioners, LLaDA2.0 is not a universal replacement for AR models but a specialized tool optimized for specific deployment scenarios. Based on the evaluation in Section 6, we can categorize suitable use cases:

| Use Case | Recommendation | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Code Generation APIs** | **Strong Fit** | Superior HumanEval/MBPP scores (Table 2) + 2.1× speed-up (Figure 3) reduces latency and cost per token. |
| **Agent Tool Use** | **Strong Fit** | Leading BFCL v3 scores (75.43) suggest better function calling reliability. Parallel decoding speeds up tool execution loops. |
| **Real-time Chat** | **Conditional Fit** | High throughput is beneficial, but block-wise generation may introduce perceptible latency bursts compared to token streaming. |
| **Long-Context Analysis** | **Poor Fit** | Performance degrades beyond 32k (Figure 5). AR models with established long-context techniques are currently more robust. |
| **Complex Reasoning** | **Poor Fit** | Deficits in `BIG-Bench Extra Hard` suggest AR models are safer for math/logic-heavy tasks requiring long CoT. |

**Deployment Strategy:**
For high-throughput backend services (e.g., code completion, data extraction), LLaDA2.0-flash offers a compelling value proposition: **lower latency per request** despite higher parameter count. The memory cost of loading 100B parameters is offset by the reduced compute time per token generated. However, for edge devices or memory-constrained environments, the 100B parameter requirement remains a barrier, making LLaDA2.0-mini (16B) the more viable option despite its lower absolute performance.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For teams aiming to replicate or integrate LLaDA2.0, several practical considerations arise from the infrastructure and hyperparameter analysis in Section 7 and Section 6.3.

#### 7.4.1 Infrastructure Requirements
LLaDA2.0 relies on a specialized stack that is less mature than the AR ecosystem:
*   **Training:** Requires `Megatron-LM` with custom modifications for block diffusion attention (Section 7.1). The cuDNN backend for attention is critical for achieving the reported 1.3× speedup and 90% memory savings.
*   **Inference:** Standard AR engines (vLLM, TGI) do not natively support block diffusion. Practitioners must use `dInfer` (adapted for this work) or the emerging `SGLang` integration mentioned in Section 7.3.
*   **Guidance:** Before adoption, verify that your deployment environment supports the required inference engine. Migrating from an AR-optimized stack to `dInfer` may require engineering effort.

#### 7.4.2 Critical Hyperparameters
The ablation study in Figure 4 identifies specific settings that are non-negotiable for optimal performance:
*   **Denoising Threshold:** Set to $0.95$. Lowering this to $0.85$ increases speed but causes unacceptable quality degradation (score drops from 70.15 to 67.90).
*   **Block Size:** Set to $32$. This balances speed (2.55 Tokens Per Forward) and quality. Increasing to $64$ degrades both.
*   **Temperature:** Set to $0.0$ (greedy) for benchmarking. For production, temperature tuning may be needed to balance diversity and correctness, but the impact on diffusion stability is less documented than for AR models.

#### 7.4.3 Converting Custom AR Models
Teams wishing to apply the WSD strategy to their own AR checkpoints should note:
*   **Numerical Stability:** Do not simply reinitialize masked token embeddings. As noted in Section 7.1, this causes catastrophic forgetting. Instead, add **independent Gaussian noise** to masked token embeddings during initial iterations to maintain L2 norm stability.
*   **Schedule Adherence:** The three-phase WSD schedule (Warmup $L_B: 1 \rightarrow 4096$, Stable $L_B=4096$, Decay $L_B: 4096 \rightarrow 32$) is critical. Skipping the Decay phase sacrifices inference efficiency; skipping the Warmup phase risks training instability.
*   **Data Packing:** Implement the document-level attention mask (Equation 3). Standard packed training without document boundaries will lead to spurious cross-document dependencies and degraded performance.

### 7.5 Conclusion on Impact

LLaDA2.0 demonstrates that diffusion language models are no longer a theoretical alternative but a **practical competitor** to auto-regressive architectures at the frontier scale. By solving the conversion stability problem via WSD and optimizing for inference speed via CAP training, it provides a viable path for organizations to leverage existing AR investments while unlocking parallel decoding benefits.

However, this is not a blanket victory. The trade-offs are clear: **gain speed and structured generation capability, but accept higher parameter counts and potential reasoning deficits.** Future work must address the reasoning gap and context extrapolation limits to make diffusion a universal standard. Until then, LLaDA2.0 stands as a powerful specialized tool for the next generation of high-throughput, structured language applications.
