## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces Eagle 2.5, a family of frontier vision-language models that addresses the critical challenge of long-context multimodal understanding in extended video sequences and high-resolution images through novel training strategies like Automatic Degradation Sampling and the new Eagle-Video-110K dataset. The proposed Eagle 2.5-8B model achieves 72.4% on the Video-MME benchmark with 512 input frames, matching the performance of top-tier commercial models like GPT-4o and large-scale open-source models like Qwen2.5-VL-72B despite having a significantly smaller parameter footprint. This work demonstrates that efficient native long-context capabilities can be achieved without specialized compression modules, providing a robust solution for complex real-world visual reasoning.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Long-Context Gap in Vision-Language Models

Despite remarkable progress in multimodal learning, a fundamental limitation persists across most vision-language models (VLMs): they are optimized for **short-context tasks**. The paper identifies this as a critical gap — most existing VLMs handle only "a few images or short video clips at a time" (Section 1). This constraint becomes problematic when we consider real-world visual understanding scenarios:

- **Long video comprehension**: Understanding hour-long lectures, movies, or surveillance footage
- **High-resolution image understanding**: Processing detailed medical scans, architectural blueprints, or scientific figures
- **Multi-page documents**: Analyzing lengthy reports, research papers, or legal documents
- **Combined modalities**: Any mixture of the above requiring sustained contextual reasoning

The paper explicitly states that "the processing of extended visual contexts remains an open challenge" (Section 1). This is not merely an engineering constraint but a fundamental architectural and methodological gap that limits VLM applicability to complex, real-world tasks.

### Why Long-Context Understanding Matters

The importance of this problem operates on two levels:

**Practical Impact**: Real-world visual reasoning rarely occurs in isolated, short bursts. Consider a medical professional reviewing a full patient imaging history, a lawyer analyzing hours of deposition video, or a researcher synthesizing findings across a 50-page technical document with embedded figures. Short-context VLMs cannot support these workflows without artificial segmentation that loses crucial cross-temporal or cross-page relationships.

**Theoretical Significance**: The ability to scale performance with increased context length is a key indicator of genuine understanding versus pattern matching. As Figure 1 demonstrates, Eagle 2.5 shows "consistent improvement as the number of input frames increases" — a property many competing models lack. This scaling behavior suggests the model is actually utilizing additional contextual information rather than merely accommodating it.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper categorizes existing long-context VLM methods into two distinct camps (Section 2, "Long-context VLMs"):

#### Approach 1: Context Compression Modules

This strategy introduces specialized modules to compress or select visual information before it reaches the language model:

- **Question-guided compression**: Methods like Korbar et al. (2024), Li et al. (2024), and Shen et al. (2024) extract only question-relevant visual cues through additional modules
- **Token reduction techniques**: Works by Jin et al. (2024), Li et al. (2023, 2024), Wang et al. (2024), and Weng et al. (2024) minimize visual representation before LLM processing

**Why this falls short**: While these methods "effectively circumvent the need to extend the context length of VLMs," they introduce two critical problems:

1. **Additional computational overhead**: The compression modules themselves require training and inference resources
2. **Capacity limitations**: By discarding visual information upfront, these methods "potentially constrain model performance" — the model cannot reason about information it never received

This creates a fundamental tension: compression enables longer contexts but at the cost of information fidelity.

#### Approach 2: Direct Context Length Extension

This strategy extends the native context window of the underlying LLM to accommodate longer multimodal sequences. Notable works include LongVA (Zhang et al., 2024), LongVILA (Xue et al., 2024), and LongViTA (Shen et al., 2025).

**Why this falls short**: Despite being more principled than compression, these approaches suffer from three documented limitations (Section 1):

> "First, the performance of existing methods is often suboptimal, generally falling behind proprietary models. Second, these approaches struggle to achieve consistent improvements as the amount of visual input increases. Lastly, the optimal training strategies for state-of-the-art long-context VLMs remain unclear."

Figure 1 provides empirical evidence for the second limitation: models like Qwen2.5-VL-7B/72B, InternVL-2.5-8B, and LLaVA-OneVision-72B show **flat or inconsistent performance curves** as frame count increases from 16 to 512 frames. In contrast, Eagle 2.5-8B demonstrates monotonic improvement across this range.

### The Unexplored Dimension: Training Strategies and Data Recipes

A key insight from this paper is that architectural choices alone cannot solve the long-context problem. The authors identify that "the optimal training strategies for state-of-the-art long-context VLMs remain unclear, given the complex interplay of factors such as training strategies and data recipes" (Section 1).

This observation reveals a gap in prior work: most research focuses on **model architecture** (compression modules, context extension) while treating training methodology and data curation as secondary concerns. Eagle 2.5 positions itself differently by treating these three components as equally important:

| Component | Prior Work Focus | Eagle 2.5 Contribution |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Architecture | Compression modules or context extension | Native extension without compression (Section 3.1) |
| Training Strategy | Often unspecified or static | Information-first sampling + progressive training (Section 3.2) |
| Data Recipe | Limited long-context data | Eagle-Video-110K with dual-level annotations (Section 3.3) |

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

Eagle 2.5 distinguishes itself through three deliberate design choices:

**1. No Compression Modules**: Unlike the first category of approaches, Eagle 2.5 "deliberately avoid[s] incorporating tailored compression modules that might constrain the model's flexibility" (Section 3.1). This preserves information fidelity at the cost of requiring more sophisticated training strategies.

**2. Consistent Scaling**: Unlike the second category of approaches, Eagle 2.5 achieves performance that "benefits from increased input length, leading to consistent performance gains besides merely accommodating longer inputs" (Section 1). Figure 1 shows this empirically — the model doesn't just handle 512 frames; it performs *better* with 512 frames than with fewer.

**3. Training-Centric Innovation**: Rather than proposing architectural novelty, the paper's core contributions lie in training methodology (Automatic Degradation Sampling, progressive mixed post-training) and data curation (Eagle-Video-110K). This represents a shift in research focus from "what model structure" to "how to train effectively."

The ablation studies in Section 4.2 validate this positioning. Table 6 shows that removing the Information-First Sampling components (Image Area Preservation and Automatic Degradation Sampling) causes measurable performance degradation on both image and video benchmarks — confirming that training strategy, not just architecture, drives the improvements. Similarly, Table 7 demonstrates that progressive training (32K→64K) outperforms direct 64K training, and that Eagle-Video-110K provides additional gains beyond open-source data alone.

This positioning reflects a mature understanding of the problem: long-context VLMs require co-design of architecture, training methodology, and data — optimizing one dimension while neglecting others yields suboptimal results.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a training methodology paper that demonstrates how to build effective long-context vision-language models through careful design of sampling strategies, progressive training schedules, and curated data recipes rather than architectural novelty. The core idea is that native long-context capability emerges not from model structure alone, but from systematically preserving information during training while gradually exposing the model to increasingly longer sequences.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

Eagle 2.5 is a vision-language model training framework that teaches existing model architectures to handle very long visual sequences (up to 512 video frames or 128K tokens) without losing important visual or textual information. The solution works by implementing three coordinated mechanisms: an intelligent sampling strategy that decides which visual details to keep when context is limited, a progressive training schedule that gradually increases sequence length, and a specially curated dataset containing long videos with detailed annotations at both story and clip levels.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The Eagle 2.5 system consists of four major components connected in a training pipeline. First, the **Vision Encoder (SigLIP-so400M)** processes input images or video frames and produces visual embeddings. Second, the **MLP Connector** projects these visual embeddings into the language model's representation space, aligning the two modalities. Third, the **LLM (Qwen2.5 series)** receives both visual tokens and text tokens and performs autoregressive generation. Fourth, and most critically, the **Information-First Sampling Module** sits before the vision encoder and determines how many frames to sample from videos, how many tiles to use for high-resolution images, and how to allocate the total token budget between visual and textual content. During training, data flows from the Eagle-Video-110K and Open-Data sources through the sampling module, which applies Image Area Preservation and Automatic Degradation Sampling before sequences reach the model. The Progressive Mixed Post-Training Schedule controls the maximum sequence length (ℒmax) across training stages, starting at 32K tokens and incrementally increasing to 64K and finally 128K tokens.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the **model architecture** to establish the baseline structure that Eagle 2.5 builds upon, since understanding the underlying VLM design is prerequisite to understanding why the training innovations matter.
- We then examine **Image Area Preservation** because it addresses the fundamental problem of how to handle high-resolution images without distorting their geometry — a prerequisite for understanding the broader sampling strategy.
- We proceed to **Automatic Degradation Sampling** as this is the core innovation that dynamically balances visual and textual token allocation, directly enabling long-context training.
- We analyze the **Progressive Mixed Post-Training Schedule** because it explains how the model gradually learns to handle longer sequences rather than being overwhelmed by them from the start.
- We conclude with the **Eagle-Video-110K data recipe** since the training strategies only work effectively when paired with appropriately curated long-context data containing both story-level and clip-level annotations.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### 3.4.1 Model Architecture Foundation

Eagle 2.5 adopts a standard tiling-based multimodal architecture following the LLaVA design pattern, which means it does not introduce novel architectural components but instead focuses on optimizing how existing components are trained. The vision encoder uses SigLIP-so400M, which produces visual embeddings from input images or video frames at a fixed resolution per tile. An MLP projection layer aligns these vision embeddings with the LLM's representation space, enabling the language model to process visual information as if it were additional text tokens. The language model backbone utilizes the Qwen2.5 series, specifically the 7B and 8B parameter variants for the reported experiments. For handling arbitrary-resolution images, the system employs an image tiling strategy inspired by LLaVA-1.5 and InternVL, which divides large images into multiple tiles that are processed independently and then combined. This architectural choice is deliberate: the authors "deliberately avoid incorporating tailored compression modules that might constrain the model's flexibility" (Section 3.1), choosing instead to solve the long-context problem through training methodology rather than architectural modification. Each tile produces 256 tokens, and the maximum number of tiles per image is bounded at 12, which means a single high-resolution image can contribute up to 3,072 visual tokens to the context window.

#### 3.4.2 Image Area Preservation (IAP)

Traditional tiling methods divide an image of width $W$ and height $H$ into a rigid $r_w \times r_h$ grid of $s \times s$ tiles, where the grid dimensions are often constrained by fixed aspect ratio requirements. This rigid approach creates a fundamental problem: when an image's aspect ratio does not match the allowed tiling configurations, the image must be downsampled or cropped, which undermines the purpose of tiling in the first place. For example, InternVL "imposes strict aspect ratio constraints that force image downsampling" (Section 3.2.1), which loses visual detail that the tiling strategy was meant to preserve. Image Area Preservation addresses this by optimizing two objectives simultaneously rather than treating aspect ratio as a hard constraint. The first objective is **Area Preservation**, which encourages maintaining at least 60% of the original image area in the tiled version. If we denote the original area as $A_{orig} = W \times H$ and the new tiled area as $A_{new} = r_w \times r_h \times s^2$, then the system penalizes configurations where $A_{new} &lt; 0.6 \times A_{orig}$ but does not over-reward configurations that exceed this threshold. The second objective is **Aspect Ratio Fidelity**, which aligns the tiling ratio $r_t = r_w / r_h$ with the original aspect ratio $r_{orig} = W / H$. The aspect ratio term reaches its maximum value of 1 when $r_t = r_{orig}$ and decays symmetrically for deviations in either direction. The system selects the optimal tiling configuration by solving the following optimization problem over all candidate tiling ratios where $r_w \times r_h \leq N$:

$$\arg \max_{(r_w,r_h)} \left[ \min\left(\frac{A_{new}}{A_{orig}}, 0.6\right) \cdot \min\left(\frac{r_t}{r_{orig}}, \frac{r_{orig}}{r_t}\right) \right]$$

This formulation multiplies the area penalty term by the aspect ratio alignment term, ensuring that both objectives must be satisfied for a configuration to be selected. Figure 3 provides a visual comparison showing that the IAP method (b) "effectively retains a larger portion of the original image, especially for high-resolution inputs" compared to the traditional InternVL tiling strategy (a). The design choice here reflects a key insight: preserving more visual information, even with slightly imperfect aspect ratios, is preferable to enforcing rigid geometric constraints that discard content. The ablation study in Table 6 confirms this: removing IAP causes performance drops on InfoVQA (from 77.6 to 76.2), DocVQA (from 92.3 to 91.9), and Perception_test (from 76.3 to 73.3), demonstrating that high-resolution image understanding benefits directly from area preservation.

#### 3.4.3 Automatic Degradation Sampling (ADS)

Automatic Degradation Sampling represents the core innovation in Eagle 2.5's training strategy, addressing a fundamental tension in multimodal training: how to allocate a fixed sequence length budget between visual and textual content. Conventional vision-context-centric approaches sample visual content at fixed rates or with predetermined frame counts, which creates two problems. First, this risks text truncation when the visual content consumes too many tokens. Second, it leads to suboptimal token allocation because different training samples have different visual complexity requirements. ADS inverts this approach by being all-context-centric: it guarantees complete text retention while adaptively optimizing visual content to maximize context length utilization. Given a training sample $\mathcal{S} = \{S_{visual}, S_{text}\}$ with maximum sequence length $\mathcal{L}_{max}$, the system first computes the fixed text token length $\mathcal{L}_{text}$ and then derives the visual token budget as $\mathcal{L}_{visual} = \mathcal{L}_{max} - \mathcal{L}_{text}$. This ensures that textual information is never truncated, which is critical because the text often contains the question or instruction that defines the learning task.

For visual content optimization under the $\mathcal{L}_{visual}$ budget, ADS distinguishes between two content types and optimizes two key variables. For images, the system optimizes the maximal tile count per image $t$ to maximize spatial information across $M$ images. For temporal content (videos or multi-page documents), the system optimizes the sampling count $n$ to maximize temporal coverage. The constrained optimization problem is formulated as:

$$\maximize_{t,n} \sum_{i=1}^{M} L(t, I_i) + 256 \cdot n$$

$$\subjectto \sum_{i=1}^{M} L(t, I_i) + 256 \cdot n \leq \mathcal{L}_{visual}$$

$$1 \leq t \leq 12, \quad 1 \leq n \leq N_{max}$$

$$N_{max} = \begin{cases} 2 \times \text{duration} & \text{(video)} \\ \text{pages} & \text{(document)} \end{cases}$$

In this formulation, $L(t, I_i)$ is the token function that calculates the tokens of the $i$-th image $I_i$ under maximal tiling number $t$, and the constant 256 represents the token quantity per temporal unit (frame or page) since temporal content does not use image tiling. The upper bound $T_{max} = 12$ constrains the maximum tiles per image, while $N_{max}$ is determined by video duration (at 2 frames per second) or document page count.

ADS employs a dual-phase degradation process to solve this optimization problem efficiently. In the **temporal degradation phase**, the system first fixes the max tile number $t = 1$ and focuses on temporal sampling, targeting a sampling rate of 2 FPS for videos and using all images for multi-image documents. The system requires that each visual input has at least $N_{min}$ frames, and if this minimum cannot be met within the visual context budget, the sample is discarded entirely. The maximally sampled temporal units is calculated as:

$$n^* = \left\lfloor \frac{\mathcal{L}_{visual} - M}{256} \right\rfloor$$

In the **tiling degradation phase**, after deciding the number of frames, the system dynamically adjusts the tiling to maximize use of the available context. The possible tile configurations are represented as $\mathcal{T} = \{12, 8, 6, 4, 2, 1\}$ in decreasing order, and the system chooses the highest tile configuration $t^*$ such that:

$$t^* = \max \left\{ t \in \mathcal{T} : \sum_{i=1}^{M} L(t, I_i) \leq (\mathcal{L}_{visual} - n^* \cdot 256) \right\}$$

This strategy preserves as much visual detail as possible while ensuring full textual input retention, achieving superior information density compared to static sampling strategies. The ablation study in Table 6 demonstrates the importance of ADS: removing it causes performance degradation on Perception_test (from 76.3 to 75.5) and MLVU (from 71.5 to 70.1), confirming that "the vision-context-centric strategy may truncate supervision signals, leading to performance loss" (Section 4.2). The design choice to prioritize text over vision reflects a critical insight: in supervised training, the text contains the learning signal (questions, answers, instructions), and truncating it would corrupt the training objective itself.

#### 3.4.4 Progressive Mixed Post-Training Schedule

The Progressive Mixed Post-Training Schedule addresses a practical challenge in long-context training: exposing a model to very long sequences (e.g., 128K tokens) from the beginning of training is computationally intensive and can destabilize learning. The paper identifies two specific problems with direct long-context training. First, "balancing the distribution of long and short sequences becomes computationally intensive" when $\mathcal{L}_{max}$ values are large. Second, "achieving optimal performance through a single training iteration proves challenging" because the model has not gradually built up its capacity to process extended contexts. The progressive approach systematically exposes the model to increasingly larger $\mathcal{L}_{max}$ values, enhancing its capacity to process extended contexts in a curriculum-like fashion.

The training pipeline consists of five stages with specific configurations documented in Table 8. **Stage 1** performs MLP alignment using the ALLaVA dataset with 1.2M samples, a batch size of 1024, learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-4}$, and maximum length of 4096 tokens, training only the MLP connector while keeping the vision encoder and LLM frozen. **Stage 1.5** performs pretraining on rich diverse data with 21.6M samples, maintaining the batch size of 1024 but reducing the learning rate to $2 \times 10^{-5}$ and increasing maximum length to 8192 tokens, with full model training enabled. **Stages 2, 3, and 4** implement the progressive long-context training with mixtures of short and long context data, each using 4.6M + 4.6M samples but with decreasing batch sizes (256, 128, 128) and increasing maximum lengths (32K, 65K, 128K). In the experiments reported in Section 4.2, the authors "sequentially set $\mathcal{L}_{max}$ to 32K, 64K and 128K" to evaluate the progressive training effect.

Table 7 provides empirical evidence for the effectiveness of progressive training. When comparing progressive training (32K→64K) against direct 64K mixed training on the same Open-Data, the progressive approach achieves higher scores on MVBench (73.0 vs 71.3), MLVU (74.5 vs 74.0), and Video-MME (68.1 vs 67.9). The paper offers two explanations for this improvement. First, "direct 64K hybrid training disperses samples across the 64K space, diluting the focus on shorter contexts" — meaning the model does not get sufficient training on the shorter sequences that form the foundation for understanding longer ones. Second, "some longer samples are challenging to learn without a gradual learning process that transitions from easy to difficult" — analogous to curriculum learning in other domains. Figure 6 illustrates this effect on the Video-MME benchmark, showing that "as progressive training advances, the model's capacity to process more frames is gradually enhanced." The curves for Eagle2.5-8B-16K, Eagle2.5-8B-32K, Eagle2.5-8B-64K, and Eagle2.5-8B-128K show monotonic improvement as the training context length increases, particularly at higher frame counts (≥128 frames). This progressive approach also enables "safely generating diverse model variants at intermediate training stages," which provides practical flexibility for deployment scenarios with different context length requirements.

#### 3.4.5 Eagle-Video-110K Data Recipe

The Eagle-Video-110K dataset addresses a critical gap in existing training data: "current open-source video data often lacks sufficient length" (Section 3.3). The dataset contains 110,000 videos specifically curated to enhance long video understanding capabilities, and Figure 4 shows that Eagle-Video-110K contains substantially more long-duration videos compared to the Open-Data baseline, with the frequency distribution extending well beyond 10,000 seconds while Open-Data concentrates below 1,000 seconds. The data recipe follows a "diversity first, then quality" principle and combines open-source data with this self-curated dataset.

The video collection process uses a diversity-driven strategy across multiple sources including Vidchapters, MiraData, InternVid-10M, Panda-70M, Vript, Shot2story, ViTT, and WebVid-10M, collectively referred to as set $A$. For the current training dataset $B$, the system uses CLIP to extract temporal features at a rate of 1 frame per second. Videos from both $A$ and $B$ are segmented into 10-second clips, and a pooling operation on each clip's frames derives a representative feature vector. Let $\{b_i\}_{i=1}^{N_B}$ represent the clips from $B$ and $\{a_j\}_{j=1}^{N_A}$ represent those from $A$. The system calculates pairwise cosine similarity between clips from $B$ and $A$, and for each clip $a_j$ in $A$, identifies its maximum similarity with any clip in $B$:

$$S_{max}(a_j) = \max_{1 \leq i \leq N_B} S(b_i, a_j)$$

The system introduces a similarity threshold $\tau = 0.5$, and clips in $A$ with $S_{max}(a_j)$ below this threshold are considered most novel relative to $B$:

$$A_{novel} = \{a_j \in A \mid S_{max}(a_j) &lt; \tau\}$$

The clips in $A_{novel}$ and their corresponding original videos are selected to enhance collection diversity. This thresholding method ensures that the training data does not contain redundant content similar to what the model may have already seen, maximizing the information density of the training set.

The annotation methodology employs a dual-level approach combining top-down story-level and bottom-up clip-level annotations, as illustrated in Figure 5. For **story-level video data**, the system uses a top-down approach that leverages human-annotated chapters as video segments rather than traditional shot-level segmentation. This differs fundamentally from datasets like Shot2story, which "employs shot detection to segment videos and construct storylines across shots" but often results in "over-segmentation, producing excessively detailed annotations that are suboptimal for constructing coherent story-level text" (Section 3.3.2). The system incorporates content from ViTT and Vidchapters among the selected videos and filters out any videos with fewer than two chapters to ensure they serve as effective story-level sources. For each video divided into $N$ clips spanning timestamps $a$ to $b$, the system performs chapter-level dense captioning by sampling frames at up to 2 frames per second with a maximum of 50 frames per segment. These sampled frames, together with user-provided segment titles, guide GPT-4o in generating detailed visual descriptions focused on the content indicated by the titles. Once visual descriptions for all segments are completed, the system compiles the captions for the entire video along with their corresponding time intervals and chapter titles, and this aggregated information is provided to GPT-4 to generate diverse question-answer pairs covering multiple question types.

For **clip-level video data**, the system addresses the need for localized spatiotemporal details that story-level annotations cannot provide. The bottom-up approach generates QA pairs for each short clip in dataset $A$ based on various question types, sampling frames at up to 2 frames per second and inputting them into GPT-4o. From a predefined question type pool (detailed in Table 12 with 63 categories ranging from object_recognition to event_causality), the system randomly selects five question types and prompts the model to generate corresponding question-answer pairs. A critical challenge is that "annotations for individual clips are designed for localized queries, conflicts may arise when these queries are extended to the entire video" (Section 3.3.2). To address this, the system introduces two types of anchors for each clip-question pair. First, time intervals are directly incorporated into questions to establish temporal references. Second, GPT-4o generates textual context anchors that provide additional information without revealing the answers. This clip-to-video QA conversion enables the model to understand both overarching narratives and precise spatiotemporal details within videos.

Table 1 summarizes the complete open-source long-context data used in Eagle-2.5, spanning categories including Video Classification (Kinetics710, Something-Something-v2, ActivityNet, etc.), Temporal Action Localization, Video Temporal Grounding, Dense Video Captioning, Temporal Segmentation, Temporal Reasoning, General Video QA, Multi-Page Document (SlideVQA, DUDE, MP-DocVQA), Video Captioning, and Long Text (LongAlign, LongReward). This comprehensive data collection, combined with Eagle-Video-110K, provides the diversity necessary for robust long-context learning. The ablation study in Table 7 demonstrates the impact of Eagle-Video-110K: adding it to the 32K→64K progressive training improves MVBench (from 73.0 to 73.9), MLVU (from 74.5 to 75.1), and Video-MME (from 68.1 to 68.8). Figure 6 further shows that Eagle-Video-110K "significantly boosts the model's capability to handle numerous frames (≥128 frames) by focusing on the inclusion of long videos that were absent in the Open-Data training set." This confirms that the dataset's long-duration videos are essential for achieving strong performance on benchmarks requiring extended temporal reasoning.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the conceptual breakthroughs that distinguish Eagle 2.5 from prior long-context VLM research. Rather than re-describing the technical mechanisms covered in Section 3, we examine the underlying principles that make these mechanisms effective and why they represent meaningful advances over existing approaches.

### 4.1 Training Methodology as the Primary Innovation Lever

**What makes this novel**: Most long-context VLM research treats architecture as the primary innovation surface — introducing compression modules (Korbar et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024), novel attention mechanisms (Zhang et al., 2024), or specialized memory structures (Weng et al., 2024). Eagle 2.5 inverts this priority: the architecture remains deliberately conventional (LLaVA-style with Qwen2.5 backbone), while the training methodology carries the innovation burden.

**Why this matters**: This represents a conceptual shift in how the field should approach long-context problems. The ablation studies provide compelling evidence for this positioning:

| Component Removed | Video-MME Drop | Perception_test Drop |
|------------------|----------------|---------------------|
| Image Area Preservation | 65.4 → 64.9 | 76.3 → 73.3 |
| Automatic Degradation Sampling | 65.4 → 65.0 | 76.3 → 75.5 |
| Progressive Training (direct 64K vs. 32K→64K) | 68.1 → 67.9 | — |
| Eagle-Video-110K (Open-Data only) | 68.1 → 67.9 | — |

Table 6 and Table 7 demonstrate that removing training innovations causes measurable degradation **without any architectural change**. This validates the core hypothesis: long-context capability is not primarily an architectural constraint but a training optimization problem.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental**: This is a **fundamental innovation** in research framing. While progressive training and data curation exist in other domains (e.g., curriculum learning in NLP), applying them as the *primary* solution to long-context VLMs — rather than as supplementary techniques to architectural novelty — represents a paradigm shift. The success of this approach suggests that many reported architectural "advances" in long-context VLMs may actually reflect unreported training or data advantages.

### 4.2 Information Preservation Over Information Compression

**What makes this novel**: The dominant paradigm in long-context VLMs assumes that extended sequences require information reduction — either through token pruning (Jin et al., 2024), question-guided selection (Shen et al., 2024), or learned compression (Korbar et al., 2024). Eagle 2.5 challenges this assumption directly: the Information-First Sampling strategy (Section 3.2.1) prioritizes *retaining* information rather than discarding it, even when context budgets are constrained.

**Why this matters**: This insight resolves a fundamental tension in long-context learning. Compression-based methods create an irrecoverable information loss: if visual content is discarded before reaching the LLM, the model cannot reason about it regardless of its capabilities. Eagle 2.5's approach ensures that the model always receives the maximum possible information given the budget constraint, deferring the "what matters" decision to the LLM's attention mechanism rather than a pre-processing module.

The performance evidence supports this principle. Table 6 shows that removing Image Area Preservation causes a **3.0 point drop** on Perception_test (76.3 → 73.3), a benchmark specifically designed to evaluate fine-grained visual perception. This degradation occurs because rigid tiling discards peripheral image content that may contain critical visual cues. Similarly, removing Automatic Degradation Sampling causes consistent drops across benchmarks, confirming that "vision-context-centric strategy may truncate supervision signals" (Section 4.2).

**Incremental vs. Fundamental**: This is a **fundamental innovation** in design philosophy. While tiling and adaptive sampling exist in prior work (InternVL, LLaVA-1.5), the explicit framing of "information preservation as the optimization objective" rather than "compression as the necessity" represents a conceptual advance. The mathematical formulation in Equation (1) — which penalizes area loss while rewarding aspect ratio fidelity — operationalizes this philosophy in a way that prior tiling strategies do not.

### 4.3 Progressive Context Learning as Curriculum

**What makes this novel**: Prior long-context VLM training typically exposes models to the maximum context length from the beginning of training (e.g., LongVA, LongVILA train directly at target context lengths). Eagle 2.5 introduces progressive mixed post-training (Section 3.2.2), where the model sequentially trains at 32K → 64K → 128K tokens, building context handling capacity gradually.

**Why this matters**: This approach addresses two underappreciated challenges in long-context training:

1. **Optimization Stability**: Direct training at 128K tokens creates a vast sequence space where short-context samples become statistically diluted. The paper notes that "direct 64K hybrid training disperses samples across the 64K space, diluting the focus on shorter contexts" (Section 4.2). Progressive training ensures the model maintains competence at shorter lengths while extending to longer ones.

2. **Capability Bootstrapping**: Figure 6 provides empirical evidence that progressive training enables genuine capability growth: the Eagle2.5-8B-16K, 32K, 64K, and 128K variants show monotonic improvement as training context increases, particularly at higher frame counts (≥128 frames). This suggests the model is learning to *utilize* additional context, not merely accommodate it.

Table 7 quantifies this advantage: progressive 32K→64K training outperforms direct 64K training on MVBench (73.0 vs. 71.3), MLVU (74.5 vs. 74.0), and Video-MME (68.1 vs. 67.9). The 1.7 point gain on MVBench is particularly notable because MVBench evaluates short-video understanding — confirming that progressive training does not sacrifice short-context competence for long-context capability.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental**: This is a **fundamental innovation** in training methodology for VLMs. While curriculum learning is established in other domains (e.g., gradual unfreezing in NLP, difficulty-based sampling in RL), applying it specifically to *context length* as the curriculum dimension is novel for multimodal models. The insight that context length capability can be learned incrementally — rather than requiring the model to handle maximum length from initialization — has implications for how future long-context systems should be trained.

### 4.4 Dual-Level Video Annotation for Hierarchical Understanding

**What makes this novel**: Existing long-video datasets typically employ single-level annotation strategies: either shot-level segmentation (Shot2story, Han et al., 2023) or video-level summaries (ActivityNet, Caba Heilbron et al., 2015). Eagle-Video-110K introduces a dual-level annotation framework (Section 3.3.2) combining top-down story-level annotations with bottom-up clip-level QA pairs, connected through temporal and contextual anchors.

**Why this matters**: This design addresses a critical gap in how long videos are represented for training. Story-level annotations capture narrative structure and high-level semantics but lack fine-grained spatiotemporal details. Clip-level annotations provide localized details but cannot represent cross-temporal relationships. By combining both with explicit anchors, Eagle-Video-110K enables the model to learn:

- **Temporal coherence**: Understanding how events unfold across minutes or hours
- **Hierarchical reasoning**: Connecting specific visual details to broader narrative context
- **Anchored retrieval**: Locating precise moments within extended sequences

Figure 5 illustrates this architecture: human annotators create chapter-level segmentations (top-down), GPT-4o generates clip-level QA with time anchors (bottom-up), and textual context anchors bridge the two levels without revealing answers. This enables queries like "What happened 5 minutes before the main character entered the building?" — requiring both narrative understanding and precise temporal localization.

Table 7 and Figure 6 demonstrate the impact: adding Eagle-Video-110K to Open-Data improves Video-MME performance from 68.1 to 68.8, with Figure 6 showing the gain is concentrated at ≥128 frames. This confirms that the dataset's long-duration videos and dual-level annotations specifically enhance extended temporal reasoning, not just general video understanding.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental**: This is an **incremental innovation** built on prior hierarchical annotation work (Han et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2024). The novelty lies in the explicit anchor mechanism for connecting clip-level and story-level annotations, and the scale of application (110K videos). While conceptually similar to prior multi-granularity datasets, the implementation details — particularly the use of GPT-4o for clip-level QA generation with anchor insertion — represent practical advances in scalable annotation methodology.

### 4.5 Text-Priority in Multimodal Token Budget Allocation

**What makes this novel**: Conventional VLM training treats visual and textual tokens symmetrically in budget allocation, often sampling visual content at fixed rates regardless of text length. Automatic Degradation Sampling (Section 3.2.1) inverts this: it computes $\mathcal{L}_{visual} = \mathcal{L}_{max} - \mathcal{L}_{text}$, guaranteeing complete text retention while adaptively degrading visual content.

**Why this matters**: This design reflects a critical insight about the learning signal in supervised VLM training. The text typically contains:
- The question or instruction defining the task
- The answer or target for supervision
- Contextual information necessary for interpretation

Truncating text corrupts the training objective itself — the model cannot learn to answer questions it never receives, or produce outputs it cannot compare against ground truth. Visual content, while important, can be degraded (lower resolution, fewer frames) without destroying the learning signal. ADS operationalizes this priority systematically.

The ablation in Table 6 confirms this design choice: removing ADS causes consistent performance degradation across benchmarks, with the paper noting that "vision-context-centric strategy may truncate supervision signals, leading to performance loss" (Section 4.2). This validates that text preservation is not merely convenient but essential for effective training.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental**: This is a **fundamental innovation** in how multimodal training budgets are conceptualized. While some prior work adjusts visual sampling based on context (Li et al., 2024), the explicit framing of text as the *inviolable* component and vision as the *adaptive* component represents a principled design choice grounded in the structure of supervised learning. This insight extends beyond Eagle 2.5: any VLM training system should prioritize preserving the learning signal (text) over preserving input fidelity (vision) when budgets are constrained.

### Synthesis: What Distinguishes Eagle 2.5

The innovations above cohere around a unifying principle: **long-context capability emerges from systematic information preservation during training, not architectural modification**. Table 4 summarizes how each contribution advances this principle:

| Innovation | Prior Paradigm | Eagle 2.5 Paradigm | Significance |
|------------|---------------|-------------------|--------------|
| Primary lever | Architecture | Training methodology | Shifts research focus |
| Information handling | Compression | Preservation | Maintains reasoning capacity |
| Context learning | Direct max-length | Progressive curriculum | Enables stable capability growth |
| Video annotation | Single-level | Dual-level with anchors | Supports hierarchical reasoning |
| Budget allocation | Symmetric | Text-priority | Protects learning signal |

This coherence explains why Eagle 2.5-8B achieves 72.4% on Video-MME with 512 frames — matching 72B-parameter models (Table 2) — without specialized compression modules or architectural novelty. The training methodology and data recipe compensate for architectural simplicity, demonstrating that long-context VLM development requires co-optimization of all three dimensions rather than architectural innovation alone.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate Eagle 2.5's claims through systematic benchmarking, ablation studies, and comparative analysis. Understanding the experimental design is critical because it reveals whether the reported improvements stem from the proposed training innovations or from other confounding factors like larger model size or additional pretraining.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Benchmark Selection Strategy

The paper evaluates Eagle 2.5 across two distinct categories of benchmarks, each serving a specific validation purpose:

**Video Understanding Benchmarks (Table 2):** These assess the core long-context claim. The selection spans multiple difficulty levels and temporal reasoning requirements:

- **Short-video benchmarks**: MVBench (multi-choice video QA), EgoSchema (egocentric reasoning)
- **Long-video benchmarks**: MLVU (multi-task long video), LVBench (long-context interleaved understanding), Video-MME (comprehensive video evaluation with up to 512 frames), HourVideo (1-hour video understanding), CG-Bench (clue-grounded long video QA)
- **Temporal grounding**: Charade-STA (temporal action localization with language queries)
- **Fine-grained perception**: Perception_test (diagnostic benchmark for multimodal video models)

This diversity ensures that improvements are not limited to a single task type. Notably, Video-MME and HourVideo specifically test the model's ability to scale with increased frame counts — directly validating the "consistent improvement with more frames" claim from Figure 1.

**Image Understanding Benchmarks (Table 3):** These serve as a control to verify that long-context training does not degrade short-context performance. The benchmarks cover:

- **Document understanding**: DocVQA, InfoVQA, TextVQA
- **Chart interpretation**: ChartQA
- **OCR capabilities**: OCRBench
- **General reasoning**: MMstar, MMVet, MMMU, AI2D
- **Hallucination**: HallB (HallusionBench)
- **Mathematical reasoning**: MathVista

The inclusion of OCR-heavy benchmarks (DocVQA, TextVQA, OCRBench) is particularly relevant because the Image Area Preservation strategy (Section 3.2.1) specifically targets high-resolution image handling.

#### Baseline Selection

The paper compares against three categories of models (Table 2 and Table 3):

| Category | Examples | Purpose |
|----------|----------|---------|
| Closed-Source Proprietary | GPT-4o-0806, Claude-3.5-Sonnet, Gemini-1.5-Pro | Establish upper-bound performance ceiling |
| Similar-Size Open-Source | Qwen2.5-VL-8B, InternVL2.5-8B, LLaVA-Video-8B, MiniCPM-V2.6-8B | Isolate training/data advantages from scale advantages |
| Larger Open-Source | Qwen2.5-VL-72B, InternVL2.5-78B, LLaVA-OneVision-72B | Test whether 8B model can match 72B performance |

This three-tier comparison is methodologically sound. If Eagle 2.5-8B only outperformed similar-size models, the improvement could be attributed to better training. If it also matches 72B models, this suggests the training methodology fundamentally changes the efficiency curve.

#### Experimental Setup Details

The paper specifies critical inference parameters that affect reproducibility (Table 2 caption):

- **Default sampling rate**: 2 FPS for video benchmarks
- **Tiling**: Disabled by default, except for Perception_test (enabled for high-resolution testing)
- **Minimum frame count**: 8 frames
- **Maximum frame counts**: 512 for Video-MME, 256 for other benchmarks

These settings matter because they standardize the comparison. For instance, enabling tiling on Perception_test but not other benchmarks reflects that Perception_test specifically evaluates high-resolution understanding — the exact capability IAP targets.

The training configuration (Table 8) reveals a five-stage pipeline with progressively increasing context lengths (4096 → 8192 → 32K → 65K → 128K tokens). This progressive schedule is itself an experimental variable tested in the ablation studies.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies

The paper conducts four ablation experiments (Section 4.2) to isolate the contribution of each component. These studies are methodologically important because they validate that improvements stem from the proposed innovations rather than confounding factors.

#### Q1: Cross-Modal Data Influence (Table 4 and Table 5)

**Table 4** examines whether long-context training harms image benchmark performance:

| Training Configuration | Average Image Score |
|------------------------|---------------------|
| No long-context data | 74.8 |
| ℒmax = 32K | 75.3 |
| ℒmax = 64K | 75.6 |
| ℒmax = 128K | 75.7 |

The results show **monotonic improvement** (74.8 → 75.7) as long-context training increases. This contradicts the intuition that long-context training might dilute short-context capabilities. The paper attributes this to the "mixed training strategy with length-balanced packing" (Section 3.2.2), which maintains exposure to short sequences throughout training.

**Table 5** examines whether image pretraining benefits video benchmarks:

| Training Recipe | MVBench | MLVU | Video-MME |
|-----------------|---------|------|-----------|
| S1→S2 | 70.4 | 67.4 | 64.9 |
| S1→S1.5→S2 (Open-Data+EV-110K) | 72.9 | 70.9 | 65.2 |
| S1→S1.5→S2 (Image+Open-Data+EV-110K) | 73.1 | 71.5 | 65.4 |

Adding Stage 1.5 (image pretraining) improves MVBench by 2.5 points (70.4 → 72.9) and MLVU by 3.5 points (67.4 → 70.9). However, Video-MME improves only marginally (64.9 → 65.2). The paper notes: "extensive image pre-training significantly enhances performance on short video benchmarks like MVBench, as well as on the relatively simple long video benchmark, MLVU. However, for the more challenging and held-out long video benchmark, Video-MME, the improvements are less pronounced."

This reveals an important **trade-off**: image pretraining benefits short-video understanding more than long-video understanding. For applications prioritizing long-context video reasoning, the marginal gain on Video-MME may not justify the additional pretraining cost.

**Table 6** isolates the contribution of Image Area Preservation (IAP) and Automatic Degradation Sampling (ADS):

| Baseline (IAP + ADS) | 77.6 | 92.3 | 76.3 | 71.5 | 65.4 |

**Removing IAP** causes:
- InfoVQA: -1.4 points (77.6 → 76.2)
- Perception_test: -3.0 points (76.3 → 73.3)

The **3.0 point drop on Perception_test** is the largest degradation, confirming that IAP specifically benefits fine-grained visual perception tasks. This validates the design rationale in Section 3.2.1: preserving image area matters most for benchmarks requiring detailed visual analysis.

**Removing ADS** causes:
- Perception_test: -0.8 points (76.3 → 75.5)
- MLVU: -1.4 points (71.5 → 70.1)

The **1.4 point drop on MLVU** suggests ADS particularly benefits long-video understanding. The paper explains: "the vision-context-centric strategy may truncate supervision signals, leading to performance loss" (Section 4.2). By guaranteeing text preservation, ADS ensures the model always receives complete learning signals.

**Key insight**: Both components contribute independently, and their effects are task-specific. IAP matters more for high-resolution image tasks, while ADS matters more for long-video tasks. This validates the modular design of the Information-First Sampling strategy.

1. "Direct 64K hybrid training disperses samples across the 64K space, diluting the focus on shorter contexts"
2. "Some longer samples are challenging to learn without a gradual learning process that transitions from easy to difficult"

**Figure 6** visualizes the progressive training effect on Video-MME across different frame counts. The curves for Eagle2.5-8B-16K, 32K, 64K, and 128K show **monotonic improvement** as training context length increases, particularly at higher frame counts (≥128 frames). This demonstrates that the model genuinely learns to utilize additional context rather than merely accommodating it.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

#### Strengths of the Experimental Design

**1. Comprehensive Benchmark Coverage:** The paper evaluates across 20+ benchmarks spanning video, image, document, and reasoning tasks. This breadth reduces the risk of overfitting to a single evaluation metric.

**2. Multi-Tier Baseline Comparison:** Comparing against similar-size, larger, and proprietary models provides multiple validation angles. The 8B vs. 72B comparison is particularly compelling evidence for training efficiency.

**3. Systematic Ablation Studies:** Each major component (IAP, ADS, progressive training, Eagle-Video-110K) is independently ablated, allowing readers to assess individual contributions. Table 6 and Table 7 provide clear quantitative isolation.

**4. Cross-Modal Validation:** Testing image benchmarks (Table 3) alongside video benchmarks (Table 2) verifies that long-context optimization does not degrade general capabilities. The identical average score (75.6) between Eagle2.5-8B and Qwen2.5-VL-8B supports this claim.

#### Limitations and Open Questions

**1. Inference Cost Not Reported:** While the paper claims efficiency advantages over compression-based methods, it does not report:
- Tokens per second during inference
- GPU memory requirements at different context lengths
- Training compute costs (GPU-hours)

Without these metrics, readers cannot assess the practical deployment trade-offs. An 8B model matching 72B performance is impressive, but if inference is 10× slower due to longer contexts, the advantage diminishes.

**2. Frame Sampling Sensitivity:** The experiments use 2 FPS sampling by default (Table 2 caption), but the paper does not explore:
- How performance varies with different sampling rates (1 FPS, 4 FPS, 8 FPS)
- Whether the model benefits from adaptive sampling based on video content
- The optimal frame count for different video durations

Figure 1 shows performance vs. frame count, but this evaluates inference-time frame variation, not training-time sampling sensitivity.

**3. Limited Failure Analysis:** The paper does not systematically analyze:
- Cases where Eagle 2.5 underperforms baselines
- Error patterns on specific question types
- Scenarios where long-context capability provides no benefit

Table 3 shows Eagle 2.5-8B trails Qwen2.5-VL-8B on MMMU (55.8 vs. 58.6) and DocVQA (94.1 vs. 95.7), but the paper does not investigate why. Understanding failure modes would help practitioners decide when to use Eagle 2.5 versus alternatives.

**4. Progressive Training Optimal Schedule:** The paper tests 32K→64K progression (Table 7) but does not explore:
- Whether 16K→32K→64K→128K would outperform 32K→64K→128K
- The optimal number of training steps at each stage
- Whether curriculum order matters (e.g., 64K→32K vs. 32K→64K)

Table 8 shows a 5-stage pipeline, but the ablation studies only evaluate subset comparisons.

**5. Eagle-Video-110K Quality vs. Quantity:** The paper attributes improvements to Eagle-Video-110K's long-duration videos, but does not isolate:
- Whether gains come from video length or annotation quality
- Whether the dual-level annotation (story + clip) is necessary, or if single-level would suffice
- The contribution of diversity-driven collection vs. annotation methodology

A more granular ablation (e.g., long videos with simple captions vs. short videos with dual-level annotations) would clarify which aspect drives improvements.

#### Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: "Eagle 2.5 demonstrates consistent improvement as the number of input frames increases" (Figure 1)**

**Supported.** Figure 1 shows monotonic improvement from 16 to 512 frames, unlike baselines that plateau. Figure 6 further validates this with progressive training variants showing improved performance at higher frame counts.

**Claim 2: "Eagle 2.5-8B achieves 72.4% on Video-MME with 512 input frames, matching...GPT-4o and...Qwen2.5-VL-72B" (Abstract)**

**Partially supported.** Table 2 shows 72.4% on Video-MME (w/o subtitle), which exceeds GPT-4o (71.9) and is close to Qwen2.5-VL-72B (73.3). However, with subtitles, Eagle 2.5-8B (75.7) trails both GPT-4o (77.2) and Qwen2.5-VL-72B (79.1). The claim is accurate for the subtitle-free setting but should be qualified.

**Claim 3: "Information-first sampling...preserves contextual integrity and visual details" (Abstract)**

**Supported.** Table 6 shows measurable degradation when removing IAP (-3.0 on Perception_test) and ADS (-1.4 on MLVU). The task-specific nature of these drops (IAP affects high-resolution tasks more, ADS affects long-video tasks more) validates that each component preserves different types of information.

**Claim 4: "Progressive mixed post-training...enhancing the model's ability to process inputs of varying sizes" (Section 1)**

**Supported.** Table 7 shows progressive training (32K→64K) outperforms direct 64K training on MVBench (+1.7), MLVU (+0.5), and Video-MME (+0.2). Figure 6 demonstrates that progressive training enables gradual capability growth across frame counts.

**Claim 5: "Eagle-Video-110K...facilitating long-video understanding" (Abstract)**

**Supported.** Table 7 shows +0.7 on Video-MME and +0.6 on MLVU when adding Eagle-Video-110K. Figure 6 shows the gap widens at ≥128 frames, confirming the dataset specifically enables extended temporal reasoning.

### 5.5 Practical Implications for Practitioners

Based on the experimental results, practitioners should consider:

**When to Use Eagle 2.5:**
- Long-video understanding tasks (≥128 frames, hour-long content)
- High-resolution image analysis requiring fine-grained detail (medical imaging, documents)
- Scenarios where parameter efficiency matters (8B vs. 72B deployment costs)
- Temporal grounding applications (Charade-STA, CG-Bench mIoU)

**When Alternatives May Be Preferable:**
- Document-heavy workflows (Qwen2.5-VL-8B leads on DocVQA by 1.6 points)
- Expert knowledge reasoning (Qwen2.5-VL-8B leads on MMMU by 2.8 points)
- Video tasks with subtitles (GPT-4o and Qwen2.5-VL-72B lead on Video-MME w/ sub)
- Latency-sensitive deployments (inference cost not reported)

**Training Recommendations:**
- Use progressive context scheduling (32K→64K→128K) rather than direct long-context training
- Include both short and long sequences in each training stage (length-balanced packing)
- Prioritize text preservation in token budget allocation (ADS principle)
- Curate long-duration videos with hierarchical annotations for video-specific tasks

The experimental evidence suggests Eagle 2.5 represents a meaningful advance in long-context VLM training methodology, with the caveat that practical deployment requires additional cost-benefit analysis not provided in the paper.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Eagle 2.5 demonstrates impressive long-context capabilities, a critical examination reveals several assumptions, constraints, and unresolved trade-offs that practitioners should consider before adoption. This section identifies limitations grounded in the paper's own evidence, distinguishing between acknowledged constraints and gaps in the experimental analysis.

### 6.1 Computational and Deployment Costs

**Training Compute Not Reported**

The paper describes extensive efficiency optimizations in Appendix B.1, including Triton-based fused operators, CPU-offloading of hidden states, and distributed context parallelism using Ulysses and Ring attention. However, **no actual training compute metrics are provided**:

- GPU-hours or total training time is not disclosed
- Number of GPUs used for each training stage is unspecified
- Energy or cost implications of progressive training (5 stages vs. fewer) are not quantified

This omission matters because progressive training inherently requires **more total training steps** than direct long-context training. Table 8 shows five distinct stages (Stage 1 through Stage 4, plus Stage 1.5), each with different batch sizes and sequence lengths. While the paper claims this approach is more effective, readers cannot assess whether the performance gains justify the additional computational investment.

**Inference Latency and Memory Unreported**

The paper states in Appendix B.1 that VLLM is deployed for "significantly reducing memory requirements and accelerating inference speed." However, **no quantitative inference metrics are provided**:

- Tokens per second at different context lengths (32K vs. 64K vs. 128K)
- GPU memory footprint during inference with 512 frames
- Comparison of inference cost against compression-based alternatives

This is a significant gap because Eagle 2.5's approach — native context extension without compression — inherently requires **more VRAM and compute per inference** than methods that prune visual tokens. An 8B model processing 128K tokens may have similar or higher latency than a 72B model processing 8K tokens with aggressive compression. Without these metrics, practitioners cannot make informed deployment decisions.

**Scalability Beyond 128K Tokens**

The progressive training schedule culminates at 128K tokens (Table 8), but the paper does not address:

- Whether the approach scales to 256K, 512K, or 1M token contexts
- Whether performance plateaus or degrades beyond 128K
- Whether additional training stages would be required for longer contexts

Figure 6 shows performance gains up to 512 frames, but this represents approximately 32K–64K visual tokens (at 256 tokens per frame with tiling), not the full 128K context. The relationship between frame count and total context utilization is not explicitly mapped.

### 6.2 Performance Trade-offs on Specific Tasks

**Document Understanding Gap**

Table 3 reveals that Eagle 2.5-8B **trails Qwen2.5-VL-8B on several document-centric benchmarks**:

| Benchmark | Eagle2.5-8B | Qwen2.5-VL-8B | Gap |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|-----|
| DocVQA | 94.1 | 95.7 | -1.6 |
| InfoVQA | 80.4 | 82.6 | -2.2 |
| TextVQA | 83.7 | 84.9 | -1.2 |
| MMMU | 55.8 | 58.6 | -2.8 |

The paper acknowledges these gaps implicitly by reporting the numbers but does not analyze **why** long-context training might slightly reduce peak document understanding performance. A plausible explanation is that the training data mixture (emphasizing video) shifts model capacity away from document-specific reasoning. However, this remains speculative without explicit analysis.

**Subtitle-Dependent Video Understanding**

On Video-MME **with subtitles**, Eagle 2.5-8B underperforms both proprietary and larger open-source models:

| Model | Video-MME (w/ subtitle) |
|-------|------------------------|
| Eagle2.5-8B | 75.7 |
| GPT-4o | 77.2 |
| Qwen2.5-VL-72B | 79.1 |

The **3.4 point gap versus Qwen2.5-VL-72B** suggests that Eagle 2.5's advantages are concentrated in **visual-only long-context reasoning** rather than multimodal integration with text overlays. This is a meaningful limitation for applications like lecture video analysis, subtitled film comprehension, or news broadcast understanding where text and vision are tightly interleaved.

**Expert Knowledge Reasoning**

The MMMU benchmark (Multi-discipline Multimodal Understanding) evaluates expert-level knowledge across domains like medicine, law, and engineering. Eagle 2.5-8B scores 55.8, trailing Qwen2.5-VL-8B (58.6) and substantially behind GPT-4o (69.1). This suggests that:

- The training data recipe emphasizes **visual reasoning** over **domain knowledge**
- Long-context video understanding does not automatically transfer to expert-domain QA
- The 8B parameter footprint may limit knowledge capacity regardless of training methodology

For applications requiring specialized domain expertise (medical diagnosis, legal document analysis), Eagle 2.5 may require additional fine-tuning or may be inherently limited by its knowledge capacity.

### 6.3 Data Dependencies and Annotation Costs

**Reliance on Proprietary Models for Annotation**

The Eagle-Video-110K dataset construction depends heavily on **GPT-4o and GPT-4** for annotation (Section 3.3.2):

- Chapter-level dense captions are generated by GPT-4o
- Long-form QA pairs are generated by GPT-4
- Clip-level QA pairs use GPT-4o with predefined question type pools
- Textual context anchors are generated by GPT-4o

This creates two practical constraints:

1. **Replication Cost**: Researchers wishing to create similar datasets must incur substantial API costs for GPT-4o annotation across 110K videos. The paper does not report these costs, but at typical GPT-4o pricing, annotating 110K videos with multiple QA pairs each could exceed hundreds of thousands of dollars.

2. **Accessibility**: Organizations without access to GPT-4o (due to geographic, regulatory, or budgetary constraints) cannot replicate the data recipe exactly. This limits the generalizability of the approach to well-resourced institutions.

**Human Annotation Requirements**

The story-level annotation pipeline requires **human-annotated chapters** as input (Section 3.3.2):

> "We leverage human-annotated chapters as video segments, which provide more semantically meaningful annotations."

The paper incorporates content from ViTT and Vidchapters, which already contain human-annotated chapters. However, this means Eagle-Video-110K **cannot be trivially extended** to new video sources without either:

- Manual chapter annotation (expensive and time-consuming)
- Automated chapter detection (which the paper suggests is inferior: "shot-level segmentation often results in over-segmentation")

This dependency on pre-existing human annotations limits the scalability of the data recipe to new domains or video types.

**Diversity Threshold Sensitivity**

The diversity-driven collection uses a similarity threshold $\tau = 0.5$ (Section 3.3.2):

$$A_{novel} = \{a_j \in A \mid S_{max}(a_j) &lt; \tau\}$$

The paper does not ablate this threshold or justify why 0.5 was selected. Different thresholds would yield different dataset sizes and diversity characteristics:

- **Lower threshold** (e.g., 0.3): More conservative selection, smaller dataset, higher diversity
- **Higher threshold** (e.g., 0.7): More aggressive selection, larger dataset, potentially more redundancy

Without sensitivity analysis, practitioners cannot adapt this methodology to their own data collection efforts with confidence.

### 6.4 Training Schedule Uncertainties

**Optimal Progression Not Explored**

The progressive training schedule uses 32K → 64K → 128K (Section 3.2.2), but the paper does not explore:

- Whether additional intermediate stages (16K → 32K → 64K → 128K) would improve performance
- Whether the order matters (e.g., whether starting at 64K then dropping to 32K would harm performance)
- The optimal number of training steps at each stage

Table 7 compares only 32K→64K progressive against direct 64K training. The full 5-stage pipeline in Table 8 is not ablated, leaving readers uncertain whether all stages are necessary or whether some could be collapsed.

**Stage 1.5 Contribution Ambiguity**

Table 5 shows that adding Stage 1.5 (image pretraining) improves MVBench by 2.5 points but Video-MME by only 0.3 points. The paper notes this discrepancy but does not provide guidance on **when Stage 1.5 is worth the additional compute**:

- For short-video applications: Stage 1.5 appears beneficial
- For long-video applications: The marginal gain may not justify the cost

Practitioners focused exclusively on long-context tasks might reasonably skip Stage 1.5, but the paper does not explicitly recommend this.

**Length-Balanced Packing Details**

Section 3.2.2 mentions "mixed training strategy with length-balanced packing" but does not specify:

- How samples of different lengths are weighted during batching
- Whether shorter sequences are upsampled to match longer ones
- The exact distribution of sequence lengths within each training stage

Without these details, replicating the training strategy requires guesswork about how to balance short and long sequences effectively.

### 6.5 Architectural Constraints

**No Native Support for Variable Frame Rates**

The system samples videos at a fixed 2 FPS rate (Table 2 caption). This assumes that **temporal information density is uniform** across all videos, which is not true in practice:

- Action-heavy sequences (sports, fights) may require higher frame rates
- Static sequences (lectures, interviews) may be adequately represented at lower frame rates

The paper does not explore adaptive frame rate sampling based on video content, which could improve efficiency without sacrificing information.

**Tiling Disabled by Default**

Table 2 caption states: "We sample each video at 2 FPS by default and **disable tiling**, and limit the minimum sampling frame number to 8 frames." Tiling is only enabled for Perception_test to "enable high-resolution testing."

This suggests that for standard video benchmarks, **spatial resolution per frame is not maximized**. The trade-off is implicit: more frames at lower resolution versus fewer frames at higher resolution. The paper does not explore whether different tasks benefit from different frame/resolution allocations.

**Maximum Tile Constraint**

The Image Area Preservation strategy bounds maximum tiles per image at 12 (Section 3.2.1), meaning a single high-resolution image can contribute up to 3,072 visual tokens. This constraint is not ablated:

- Would 24 tiles improve high-resolution document understanding?
- Does the 12-tile limit bottleneck performance on extremely detailed images?

The ablation in Table 6 tests removing IAP entirely but does not test relaxing the tile limit.

### 6.6 Unaddressed Failure Modes

**No Systematic Error Analysis**

The paper does not provide:

- Qualitative examples where Eagle 2.5 fails while baselines succeed
- Error categorization by question type (temporal, spatial, causal, etc.)
- Analysis of whether failures correlate with specific video characteristics (duration, genre, quality)

Table 3 shows Eagle 2.5-8B underperforms on MMMU and DocVQA, but the paper does not investigate **why**. Understanding failure modes would help practitioners anticipate when Eagle 2.5 is likely to struggle.

**Long-Context Specific Failures Not Isolated**

While Figure 1 shows performance scaling with frame count, the paper does not identify:

- Whether there is a frame count threshold beyond which performance plateaus
- Whether certain question types benefit more from additional frames than others
- Whether the model exhibits "context dilution" (losing early context as sequence length increases)

These questions are critical for understanding the true limits of the long-context capability.

**Hallucination at Long Contexts**

Table 3 reports HallB (HallusionBench) scores showing Eagle 2.5-8B (54.7) performs comparably to Qwen2.5-VL-8B (52.9) and GPT-4o (55.0). However, this benchmark does not specifically test **hallucination in long-context scenarios**. A model might maintain low hallucination rates on single images while fabricating details when processing 512 frames. This remains untested.

### 6.7 Generalizability Concerns

**Domain Specificity of Eagle-Video-110K**

The video sources for Eagle-Video-110K include Vidchapters, MiraData, InternVid-10M, Panda-70M, Vript, Shot2story, ViTT, and WebVid-10M (Section 3.3.2). The paper does not specify the **domain distribution** of these sources:

- What percentage is entertainment vs. educational vs. surveillance content?
- Are certain genres (documentaries, tutorials, vlogs) overrepresented?
- How well does the model transfer to domains not covered in training (e.g., medical videos, industrial inspection)?

Without this information, practitioners in specialized domains cannot assess whether Eagle-Video-110K's diversity matches their use cases.

**Language Coverage**

The paper does not specify the **language distribution** of the training data or evaluation benchmarks. Table 12 shows 63 question type categories but does not indicate whether these are English-only or multilingual. For non-English applications, the model's capabilities remain unverified.

**Cultural and Geographic Bias**

The video sources are not analyzed for cultural or geographic representation. Datasets like WebVid-10M and InternVid-10M may have inherent biases toward certain regions, languages, or cultural contexts. The paper does not address whether Eagle 2.5 inherits these biases or whether the diversity-driven collection mitigates them.

### 6.9 Open Questions for Future Work

Based on the limitations identified above, several important questions remain unresolved:

1. **What is the compute-performance frontier?** How do training FLOPs and inference latency scale with context length, and is there a point of diminishing returns?

2. **Can the annotation pipeline be democratized?** Are there lower-cost alternatives to GPT-4o that preserve annotation quality for Eagle-Video-110K-style datasets?

3. **What is the optimal progression schedule?** Would more granular stages (16K → 32K → 64K → 128K → 256K) yield additional gains, or does 32K → 64K → 128K capture most benefits?

4. **How does the model fail?** What systematic error patterns emerge at extreme context lengths, and can they be mitigated through targeted training?

5. **Does the approach transfer to other modalities?** Could the Information-First Sampling and progressive training principles apply to audio-language or 3D-language models?

These questions represent opportunities for the research community to build upon Eagle 2.5's contributions while addressing its current limitations.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

Eagle 2.5 represents more than incremental progress on long-context vision-language models — it fundamentally reframes how the field should approach the long-context problem. This section examines the broader implications of this work, the research directions it opens, and practical guidance for practitioners considering adoption.

### 7.1 Shifting the Research Landscape

#### From Architecture-Centric to Training-Centric Innovation

The most significant conceptual contribution of Eagle 2.5 is demonstrating that **training methodology can compensate for architectural simplicity** in long-context VLMs. Prior work operated under an implicit assumption: handling extended contexts requires architectural novelty (compression modules, specialized attention mechanisms, memory structures). Eagle 2.5 challenges this by achieving 72.4% on Video-MME with 512 frames using a conventional LLaVA-style architecture (Section 3.1) — matching 72B-parameter models (Table 2) through training innovations alone.

This finding should redirect research priorities in three ways:

**1. Training Recipes Deserve Equal Scrutiny as Architecture Papers**

The ablation studies reveal that removing Information-First Sampling components causes measurable degradation (Table 6):
- Removing Image Area Preservation: $-3.0$ points on Perception_test
- Removing Automatic Degradation Sampling: $-1.4$ points on MLVU

These drops occur **without any architectural change**, confirming that training methodology carries substantial performance signal. Future VLM papers should report training details with the same rigor as architectural specifications — progressive schedules, sampling strategies, and data curation methods are not implementation details but core contributions.

**2. Compression May Be Premature Optimization**

The dominant paradigm assumes long contexts require information reduction. Eagle 2.5 demonstrates that **preservation-first strategies** can outperform compression approaches on long-video benchmarks (Table 2: Eagle 2.5-8B exceeds VideoChat-Flash-8B on MLVU by 3.0 points, despite VideoChat-Flash employing hierarchical compression). This suggests the field should:
- Re-evaluate whether compression is necessary or merely convenient
- Explore preservation strategies before introducing information loss
- Consider compression as a deployment optimization rather than a training requirement

**3. Progressive Training as a First-Class Technique**

Table 7 shows progressive training (32K→64K) outperforms direct 64K training by 1.7 points on MVBench. This validates curriculum learning for context length — a technique established in NLP but underexplored in VLMs. Future work should investigate:
- Optimal progression schedules (16K→32K→64K→128K vs. 32K→64K→128K)
- Whether progression order matters (increasing vs. decreasing context lengths)
- Cross-modal curriculum (images first, then videos, then documents)

#### Implications for Model Scaling Laws

Eagle 2.5-8B matching 72B-parameter models on long-video tasks suggests that **scaling laws may differ for long-context capabilities** versus general capabilities. The paper demonstrates:

| Capability Type | 8B Performance | 72B Performance | Gap |
|-----------------|----------------|-----------------|-----|
| Video-MME (w/o sub) | 72.4 | 73.3 (Qwen2.5-VL-72B) | -0.9 |
| MLVU | 77.6 | 74.6 (Qwen2.5-VL-72B) | +3.0 |
| MMMU (expert knowledge) | 55.8 | 58.6 (Qwen2.5-VL-8B) | -2.8 |

Long-context visual reasoning (MLVU, Video-MME) shows minimal parameter dependence when training is optimized. Expert knowledge reasoning (MMMU) remains parameter-sensitive. This implies:

- **Long-context capability** is primarily a training/data problem
- **Domain knowledge capability** remains a parameter/count problem
- Hybrid approaches may be needed for applications requiring both

### 7.2 Research Directions Enabled

#### Immediate Follow-Up Opportunities

**1. Adaptive Frame Sampling Strategies**

Eagle 2.5 uses fixed 2 FPS sampling (Table 2 caption). The paper does not explore whether **content-adaptive sampling** could improve efficiency:
- Action-heavy sequences (sports, fights) might benefit from 4-8 FPS
- Static sequences (lectures, interviews) might suffice at 0.5-1 FPS
- Scene-change detection could trigger variable sampling rates

A follow-up study could train models with adaptive sampling policies, potentially reducing token counts by 30-50% without sacrificing temporal understanding.

**2. Cross-Modal Progressive Training**

The progressive schedule increases context length (32K→64K→128K) but maintains consistent modality mixing. An alternative approach:
- Stage 1: Images only at 32K context
- Stage 2: Images + short videos at 64K context
- Stage 3: Images + long videos + documents at 128K context

This modality-aware curriculum might accelerate learning by isolating complexity dimensions.

**3. Annotation Cost Reduction**

Eagle-Video-110K relies heavily on GPT-4o for annotation (Section 3.3.2). Follow-up work could explore:
- **Distillation**: Train smaller annotator models on GPT-4o outputs, then use them for future dataset expansion
- **Self-training**: Use Eagle 2.5 itself to generate pseudo-labels for additional videos
- **Human-in-the-loop**: Identify annotation cases where GPT-4o confidence is low and route to human annotators

Reducing annotation costs by 10× would democratize this data recipe for smaller research groups.

**4. Inference Optimization for Long Contexts**

Appendix B.1 mentions VLLM deployment but provides no metrics. Critical unanswered questions:
- What is the tokens-per-second throughput at 32K, 64K, and 128K contexts?
- How does memory footprint scale with context length?
- Can KV-cache optimization reduce inference costs for repeated queries on the same video?

A systematic inference study would enable practitioners to make cost-benefit decisions.

#### Longer-Term Research Directions

**5. Beyond Video: 3D and Robotics Applications**

The Information-First Sampling principles could extend to:
- **3D scene understanding**: Preserve spatial information across multiple viewpoints rather than compressing point clouds
- **Robotics trajectories**: Maintain complete action sequences for imitation learning rather than sampling keyframes
- **Multi-sensor fusion**: Balance token budgets across camera, LiDAR, and proprioceptive inputs using ADS principles

The core insight — preserve the learning signal, adapt the input fidelity — applies broadly.

**6. Dynamic Context Allocation at Inference**

ADS operates during training, allocating tokens based on $\mathcal{L}_{visual} = \mathcal{L}_{max} - \mathcal{L}_{text}$. An inference-time variant could:
- Analyze query complexity to determine required context length
- Dynamically adjust frame count based on question type (temporal questions need more frames, spatial questions need higher resolution)
- Implement early exiting when sufficient context has been processed

This would reduce average inference cost while maintaining performance on complex queries.

**7. Multilingual Long-Context VLMs**

The paper does not specify language coverage (Section 6.7). Follow-up work should:
- Evaluate Eagle 2.5 on non-English long-video benchmarks
- Extend Eagle-Video-110K annotation to multilingual QA pairs
- Test whether progressive training transfers across languages

Given that many long-video use cases (education, surveillance, entertainment) are multilingual, this is a critical gap.

Practitioners should consider alternatives when:

**2. Expert Knowledge Reasoning is Critical**

- Sub-second response times

- Edge deployment with strict memory limits

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

#### Minimum Viable Reproduction

Researchers wishing to reproduce or build upon Eagle 2.5 should prioritize:

**1. Implement Information-First Sampling First**

The ablation studies (Table 6) show these components provide the largest gains:
- Image Area Preservation: Implement Equation (1) from Section 3.2.1
- Automatic Degradation Sampling: Implement the dual-phase degradation process

These can be added to existing VLM training pipelines without architectural changes.

**2. Start with Progressive Training at Smaller Scales**

Full 128K training requires substantial resources. A practical starting point:
- Stage 1: 8K context (standard VLM training)
- Stage 2: 16K context (introduce longer sequences)
- Stage 3: 32K context (evaluate long-context gains)

Table 7 suggests even 32K→64K progression provides measurable benefits over direct training.

**3. Curate Long-Video Data Strategically**

If Eagle-Video-110K is unavailable:
- Use the diversity-driven collection method (Section 3.3.2) with $\tau = 0.5$ threshold
- Prioritize videos >1000 seconds (Figure 4 shows this is where Open-Data is sparse)
- Implement dual-level annotation: story-level chapters + clip-level QA with temporal anchors

Even 10K long videos with proper annotation may capture most benefits.

#### Integration with Existing Frameworks

Eagle 2.5's design choices facilitate integration:

**Compatible with Standard VLM Architectures**

The model uses:
- SigLIP vision encoder (widely available)
- MLP connector (standard in LLaVA-style models)
- Qwen2.5 LLM backbone (open weights)

No custom operators are required for the core model — only for training efficiency (Appendix B.1 mentions Triton operators, but these are optimizations, not requirements).

**Training Framework Compatibility**

The progressive training schedule can be implemented in:
- PyTorch with standard distributed training
- DeepSpeed for gradient checkpointing at long contexts
- FSDP for memory-efficient large-batch training

The key requirement is supporting variable sequence lengths across training stages.

**Inference Deployment**

Appendix B.1 states VLLM is used for serving. Practitioners should:
- Verify VLLM version supports the required context lengths (128K+)
- Test PagedAttention memory efficiency at target sequence lengths
- Consider KV-cache sharing for repeated queries on the same video

#### Checklist for Adoption

Before deploying Eagle 2.5, practitioners should verify:

- [ ] **Context length requirements**: Does your use case need >32K tokens? If not, smaller models may suffice.
- [ ] **Video duration**: Are your videos typically >5 minutes? Eagle 2.5's advantages concentrate at ≥128 frames (Figure 6).
- [ ] **Resolution needs**: Do you process high-resolution images/documents? Image Area Preservation provides measurable gains (Table 6).
- [ ] **Latency budget**: Have you benchmarked inference time at your target context length?
- [ ] **Domain alignment**: Does your use case emphasize visual reasoning over expert knowledge? (MMMU gap suggests knowledge limitations)
- [ ] **Subtitle dependency**: Do your videos rely on text overlays? (Video-MME with subtitles shows 3.4 point gap vs. 72B models)
- [ ] **Replication feasibility**: Can you access GPT-4o for dataset annotation, or do you need alternative annotation strategies?

### 7.5 Concluding Perspective

Eagle 2.5 demonstrates that the long-context VLM problem is solvable through **systematic training optimization** rather than architectural complexity. The key insight — that information preservation during training enables genuine long-context reasoning — should influence how the field approaches not just vision-language models, but multimodal learning broadly.

The work's greatest contribution may be methodological: it establishes a template for future long-context research that prioritizes:
1. **Training strategy** (progressive schedules, intelligent sampling)
2. **Data curation** (diversity-driven collection, hierarchical annotation)
3. **Architecture** (conventional designs, avoiding unnecessary complexity)

Researchers building on this work should treat these three dimensions as co-equal levers — optimizing one while neglecting others yields suboptimal results, as the ablation studies confirm.

For practitioners, Eagle 2.5 offers a practical path to long-context capabilities without 72B-parameter deployment costs. The 8B model matching 72B performance on video benchmarks (Table 2) represents a meaningful efficiency gain — but only for use cases aligned with the model's strengths (visual reasoning, temporal grounding, long-video understanding).

The field now has evidence that long-context VLMs do not require compression, specialized modules, or massive scale. What they require is **thoughtful training design** — a lesson that extends far beyond Eagle 2.5 itself.
