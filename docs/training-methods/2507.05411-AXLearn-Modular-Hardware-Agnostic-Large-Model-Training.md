## 1. Executive Summary

AXLearn is a production-scale deep learning system designed to solve the critical trade-off between modularity and performance, enabling engineers to integrate complex features like Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) or Mixture of Experts (MoE) across hundreds of modules with only 10 lines of code compared to the hundreds or thousands required by state-of-the-art systems like DeepSpeed or Megatron-LM. By enforcing strict encapsulation and leveraging XLA for hardware-agnostic execution, AXLearn achieves constant asymptotic complexity ($O(1)$) for system extensions while delivering competitive training performance, such as 66.2% Model FLOPs Utilization (MFU) on TPU-v5p for Llama2-7B and 2.8x higher inference throughput than vLLM on TPUs. This architecture allows Apple to efficiently train models ranging from billions to trillions of parameters across diverse hardware backends including NVIDIA GPUs, Google TPUs, and AWS Trainium2 without being locked into a single vendor's ecosystem.

## 2. Context and Motivation

The development of large-scale deep learning systems has historically been driven by a singular metric: raw training performance. As models have grown to power societal infrastructure like chatbots and coding assistants, the industry has converged on techniques such as 3D parallelism (combining data, tensor, and pipeline parallelism) and memory optimizations like activation checkpointing. However, this focus on performance has created a critical architectural debt: **the loss of modularity**.

This paper addresses a specific, high-cost gap in modern machine learning infrastructure: the inability to rapidly experiment with new model architectures or training techniques without incurring massive engineering overhead. In the context of a large technology company like Apple, which operates across diverse hardware ecosystems (NVIDIA GPUs, Google TPUs, AWS Trainium) and serves billions of users with varied model requirements, this lack of modularity creates two distinct failure modes:
1.  **Innovation Friction:** Engineers spend weeks modifying core framework code to test a single architectural change (e.g., swapping a standard feed-forward network for a Mixture of Experts layer), rather than focusing on model science.
2.  **Vendor Lock-in & Fragility:** Systems optimized for a specific hardware vendor (e.g., Megatron-LM for NVIDIA GPUs or MaxText for TPUs) cannot easily adapt to supply chain shifts or cost-effective alternatives on other clouds.

### The Failure of Subtyping in Deep Learning Frameworks

To understand why this problem exists, one must examine the dominant software design pattern in existing frameworks: **subtyping** (inheritance). In systems like PyTorch-based Megatron-LM or DeepSpeed, neural network layers are implemented as classes that inherit from a base layer. A child layer instantiates its components as instance attributes and defines a `forward` method.

While intuitive for simple cases, this approach collapses under the weight of complex re-parameterizations. The paper illustrates this with a concrete example: replacing a standard Feed-Forward Network (FFN) with a Mixture of Experts (MoE) layer.
*   **The Naive Expectation:** Conceptually, this is a local change. One should simply swap the FFN module for an MoE module. In a purely compositional system, this might require changing 4 lines of code (LoC).
*   **The Subtyping Reality:** In a subtyping hierarchy, you cannot simply swap the child module. Because the parent class (e.g., `TransformerLayer`) explicitly instantiates the FFN as a specific attribute (e.g., `self.fc3`), replacing it requires **subclassing the parent**.
    *   You must create a new `MoETransformerLayer` that inherits from `TransformerLayer`.
    *   This new class must override the initialization to instantiate the MoE layer instead of the FFN.
    *   Crucially, if *that* new parent class is used by an even higher-level module (e.g., the full Model definition), *that* module must now be subclassed to use the new `MoETransformerLayer`.

This phenomenon, which the authors describe as **change propagation up the subtype hierarchy**, means a localized architectural change compounds into modifications across dozens or hundreds of files. The paper notes that in DeepSpeed's own codebase, integrating MoE into the QwenV2 model required over **200 lines of code changes** just to modify the existing modules, excluding the MoE implementation itself. In a production environment with tens of model variants, this scales to thousands of lines of duplicated and fragile code.

### The Hardware Agnosticism Gap

Parallel to the modularity crisis is the challenge of hardware diversity. Most state-of-the-art systems are tightly coupled to specific accelerators:
*   **Megatron-LM** relies heavily on NVIDIA-specific optimizations (e.g., specific CUDA kernels) and does not natively support TPUs or AWS Trainium.
*   **MaxText** and **Pax** are deeply optimized for the Google TPU ecosystem via JAX/XLA but lack seamless support for NVIDIA GPUs or emerging hardware like Trainium2.
*   **PyTorch FSDP** supports GPUs well but historically lacks native, high-performance support for TPUs without significant bridging (PyTorch XLA), and often requires model-specific parallel plans that break modularity.

For an organization like Apple, relying on a single vendor is strategically untenable due to supply chain risks and pricing volatility. Existing "hardware-agnostic" attempts often sacrifice performance (by using generic kernels) or modularity (by hardcoding hardware checks into the model logic). There is no existing system that simultaneously offers **strict encapsulation** (preventing change propagation) and **high-performance execution** across GPU, TPU, and Trainium backends.

### Positioning AXLearn: Encapsulation over Inheritance

AXLearn positions itself as a fundamental departure from the subtyping paradigm, advocating for **strict encapsulation** via composition. The core thesis is that modularity should be quantifiable. The authors propose a metric called **LoC-Complexity**, defined as the asymptotic number of lines of code changes required in *existing* modules to add a new feature (like RoPE or MoE).

*   **Prior Approaches (Subtyping):** The LoC-Complexity for adding MoE is $O(N)$ or even $O(NM)$, where $N$ is the number of modules in the system and $M$ is the number of feature variants. Every time a new model variant is added, the cost of integrating new features grows linearly or quadratically.
*   **AXLearn's Approach (Composition):** By enforcing that modules communicate only through strict input/output interfaces and configuration objects—never by reaching into a child's internal state or assuming a specific class type—AXLearn achieves **$O(1)$ LoC-Complexity**.

In AXLearn, a user can integrate Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) or MoE across hundreds of experimental configurations by writing a single **10-line configuration modifier script**. This script traverses the configuration tree and swaps components without touching the underlying model definitions. This is not merely a convenience; it is a structural guarantee that the system's complexity does not grow as the number of models or features scales.

Furthermore, AXLearn distinguishes itself by building on **JAX/XLA** not just for performance, but as a mechanism for hardware agnosticism. Unlike systems that hardcode parallelism strategies into the model code, AXLearn treats parallelism (sharding, rematerialization, pipeline stages) as **configuration metadata**. This allows the same model definition to be compiled for an NVIDIA H100 cluster, a Google TPU pod, or an AWS Trainium farm simply by changing the "mesh rules" in the config, while still allowing drop-in replacement of low-level kernels (like FlashAttention) where necessary for performance.

In summary, while prior work optimizes for the speed of a *single* training run, AXLearn optimizes for the speed of *system evolution*. It addresses the reality that in a production environment, the cost of modifying the framework to support a new idea often exceeds the cost of training the model itself.

## 3. Technical Approach

This section details the architectural mechanisms that allow AXLearn to achieve constant complexity ($O(1)$) for feature integration while maintaining high-performance, hardware-agnostic execution. Unlike prior systems that rely on code inheritance and hardcoded parallelism, AXLearn decouples model definition from execution strategy through a strict configuration hierarchy and a functional state management system.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
AXLearn is a deep learning training framework that treats neural network architectures and distributed training strategies as composable configuration trees rather than rigid code hierarchies. It solves the problem of "engineering friction" in large-scale AI by allowing users to swap complex components (like changing a standard layer to a Mixture of Experts) or switch hardware backends (from NVIDIA GPUs to Google TPUs) using small, isolated configuration scripts instead of rewriting core model code.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The system operates as a two-stage pipeline consisting of the **AXLearn Composer** and the **AXLearn Runtime**.
*   **Input:** The process begins with a **User Script** written in pure Python, which defines a hierarchical tree of configuration objects (modules) representing the model, optimizer, and data pipeline.
*   **Stage 1: AXLearn Composer:** This component acts as a "config-to-code" translator. It takes the user's configuration tree and applies **Config Modifiers** (such as "Mesh Rules" for specific hardware) to inject parallelism strategies, rematerialization points, and kernel selections. It then materializes a complete, sharded **JAX Program**, performing Ahead-of-Time (AOT) compilation analysis to verify memory usage and FLOPs before any hardware is touched.
*   **Stage 2: AXLearn Runtime:** This component orchestrates the execution of the compiled program on a distributed cluster. It manages the **Accelerator Runtime** (e.g., CUDA, TPU runtime), handles **Checkpointing** to cloud storage (S3/GCS), monitors hardware health via a **Watchdog**, and executes **Failure Recovery** protocols like slice-level hot-swapping.
*   **Output:** The system produces trained model weights and training metrics, with the entire workflow abstracted such that the user script remains identical regardless of whether it runs on 256 GPUs or 1024 TPUs.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
To understand how AXLearn achieves its goals, we will dissect the system in the following logical order:
1.  **Hierarchical Configuration & Composition:** We first explain how AXLearn replaces inheritance with a tree-based config system, enabling the $O(1)$ modularity claimed in the motivation.
2.  **Program Generation & Hardware Abstraction:** We detail how the Composer translates these configs into executable JAX code, specifically focusing on "Mesh Rules" that automate hardware-specific optimizations.
3.  **Functional State Management:** We analyze the `InvocationContext`, the critical mechanism that allows stateful neural network training to exist within JAX's strictly functional (stateless) paradigm.
4.  **Runtime Resilience & Operations:** Finally, we cover the distributed runtime features that ensure reliability at scale, including asynchronous checkpointing and automated failure recovery.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Hierarchical Configuration and Strict Encapsulation
The core innovation of AXLearn is its replacement of object-oriented subtyping with a **hierarchical configuration system** based on strict composition. In traditional frameworks, a `TransformerLayer` class might hardcode the instantiation of a `FeedForward` child, forcing developers to subclass the entire transformer to change that child; AXLearn avoids this by defining every component as a `Module` that holds a `Config` object containing all its parameters.
*   A `Module` in AXLearn is an abstract node in an object tree where the `Config` class encapsulates child configurations as attributes, meaning a `TransformerLayer.Config` contains a `self_attention` config and a `feed_forward` config, but does not hardcode their specific implementation classes.
*   This design enables **partial configuration**, where a parent module can propagate shared parameters (like `input_dim`) to children at instantiation time without the child config needing to know the global context beforehand.
*   For example, a `TransformerLayer` can set `cfg.feed_forward.set(input_dim=cfg.input_dim)` during its initialization, allowing the `feed_forward` config to remain generic and reusable across different model widths.
*   To modify a model architecture, such as replacing all Feed-Forward Networks (FFN) with Mixture of Experts (MoE) layers, users do not edit the model class; instead, they apply a **Config Modifier**.
*   A Config Modifier is a Python function that traverses the configuration tree and recursively replaces any instance of a target config (e.g., `FeedForwardLayer.Config`) with a new config (e.g., `MoELayer.Config`).
*   The paper demonstrates that this replacement logic requires only a **10-line code snippet** regardless of the number of model variants, achieving the theoretical $O(1)$ complexity because the modifier operates on the interface definition rather than the implementation hierarchy.
*   This approach contrasts sharply with "flat" configuration systems (used in TorchTitan or DeepSpeed) where all parameters are flattened into a single dictionary, making it difficult to isolate changes to specific sub-components without affecting unrelated parts of the system.

#### Generating JAX Programs and Hardware Agnosticism
Once the configuration tree is finalized, the **AXLearn Composer** transforms it into an executable **JAX Program** that is ready for distributed training. This stage is where hardware agnosticism is realized, as the same model definition is augmented with specific parallelism and optimization strategies based on the target infrastructure.
*   AXLearn natively integrates support for all major parallelism strategies—including Fully Sharded Data Parallelism (FSDP), Tensor Parallelism, Pipeline Parallelism, and Sequence Parallelism—directly into the layer library, meaning users specify *what* parallelism they want via config, not *how* to implement it in code.
*   To handle diverse hardware requirements without cluttering the codebase with `if-else` statements, AXLearn introduces **Mesh Rules**, which are mappings from accelerator type regular expressions (e.g., `"tpu-v5e-256-*"`) to specific Config Modifiers.
*   When a user targets a specific hardware backend, the Composer automatically applies the corresponding Mesh Rule to inject the appropriate sharding mesh shape, rematerialization policy, and kernel selection.
*   For instance, a Mesh Rule for **TPU v5e** might configure the system to use FSDP within slices and data parallelism across slices, while simultaneously enabling activation offloading to host memory and INT8 quantization.
*   Conversely, a Mesh Rule for **NVIDIA H100 GPUs** might switch the strategy to 8-way tensor parallelism within a node, FSDP across nodes, and enable FP8 training with delayed scaling, all without changing the user's model script.
*   The system also handles **memory optimizations** by tagging specific operations (like QKV projections in attention layers) with names, allowing the Config Modifier to selectively apply **rematerialization** (activation checkpointing) policies that decide whether to save an activation in high-bandwidth memory (HBM), offload it to CPU, or recompute it during the backward pass.
*   For performance-critical operations, AXLearn supports **custom kernel injection** via configuration; for example, the default attention layer can be swapped for a `FlashAttention` layer, which then transparently dispatches to `cuDNN` on GPUs, `SplashAttention` (Pallas) on TPUs, or `Nki` kernels on AWS Trainium based on the detected backend.
*   Before executing on expensive hardware, the Composer performs **Ahead-of-Time (AOT) compilation**, which analyzes the JAX program to estimate memory usage and FLOPs, catching errors like Out-Of-Memory (OOM) conditions locally on a single CPU host.
*   This AOT capability ensures that the code path used for analysis is identical to the training path, giving engineers confidence that a program that compiles locally will scale correctly to thousands of accelerators.

#### Maintaining State with InvocationContext
A fundamental challenge in building on JAX is that JAX requires programs to be **purely functional** (stateless), whereas neural network training is inherently **stateful** (requiring mutable weights, optimizer states, and random number generator keys). AXLearn resolves this tension through a novel abstraction called the **`InvocationContext`**.
*   In a standard functional program, a caller must explicitly pass model parameters and random keys as arguments to every function and return updated states as outputs, which creates verbose and fragile code when dealing with deep module hierarchies.
*   AXLearn automates this bookkeeping by maintaining a stack of `InvocationContext` objects that are pushed and popped as modules invoke one another, similar to a traditional call stack but carrying state metadata.
*   When a parent module invokes a child module, the system automatically pushes a new `InvocationContext` onto the stack, which retrieves the necessary child state, splits the Pseudo-Random Number Generator (PRNG) key for the child, and creates a fresh data store for the child's outputs.
*   Upon the child module's return, the context is popped, and the system transparently aggregates the child's outputs and state updates into the parent's data store, hiding the functional plumbing from the user.
*   This design allows developers to write module code in an **imperative style** (accessing `self.param` or calling `self.children()` directly) while the underlying system ensures that the executed JAX program remains purely functional and compatible with transformations like `jit` and `grad`.
*   Crucially, `InvocationContext` objects contain references to modules but not vice-versa, allowing arbitrary functions (even those outside the module hierarchy, like third-party optimizers from `optax`) to access shared state by traversing the context stack.
*   This mechanism enables advanced architectural features like **tied weights** (where multiple layers share the same parameter matrix) to be implemented without breaking encapsulation, as the system can resolve shared references dynamically during the context traversal rather than requiring hard-coded cross-module dependencies.

#### Runtime Resilience and Operational Robustness
The **AXLearn Runtime** is responsible for orchestrating the compiled JAX program across distributed hardware clusters, with a specific focus on handling the inevitability of hardware failures at scale.
*   The runtime provides multi-layer **monitoring and profiling**, integrating with JAX's native profiler to detect inefficiencies in input pipelines or sharding, while also exposing a generic measurement interface to track end-to-end metrics like "job goodput."
*   To manage storage efficiently, AXLearn implements **asynchronous checkpointing**, which saves model states to cloud storage (AWS S3 or Google Cloud Storage) in the background without blocking the training loop, only stalling if a previous serialization is incomplete.
*   A background **garbage collector** automatically removes old checkpoints based on a user-configurable policy, preventing storage costs from exploding during long-running training jobs.
*   Recognizing that failures are common when training on thousands of chips, the runtime includes a configurable **Watchdog** that monitors step times and hardware utilization; if it detects anomalies (e.g., a hung GPU), it can force a host restart, alert on-call engineers, or dump stack traces.
*   For recovery, AXLearn employs a **slice-level hot-swap** strategy built on Kubernetes, where the scheduler over-provisions spare replicas in the cluster.
*   When a hardware failure occurs, the system detects the fault, replaces the failed node with a healthy spare within approximately **4 minutes**, and restores the checkpoint from a healthy replica via fast interconnect in about **9 minutes**, minimizing total downtime to roughly **21 minutes** even for jobs spanning tens of thousands of chips.
*   Additionally, the system supports **persistent compilation caches**, allowing compilation artifacts to be reused across restarts, which eliminates the startup latency typically associated with recompiling large models after a failure.

#### Unifying Training and Inference
Although designed primarily for training, AXLearn's modular architecture allows it to serve as an efficient inference engine with minimal additional engineering effort.
*   Because components like the attention layer's KV cache are strictly encapsulated, the system can swap in inference-optimized cache layouts (such as **paged KV cache** or **continuous batching**) via configuration modifiers without rewriting the model definition.
*   Benchmarks show that on TPUs, AXLearn achieves significantly higher inference performance than specialized engines like vLLM, delivering a **500x speedup in Time-To-First-Token (TTFT)** and a **2.8x increase in throughput** for Llama2-7B models, demonstrating that a unified training-inference codebase does not require sacrificing performance.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The development of AXLearn reveals several counter-intuitive findings that challenge conventional wisdom in deep learning system design. While prior sections detailed the *mechanisms* of the system (configuration trees, mesh rules, invocation contexts), this section isolates the *conceptual breakthroughs* that distinguish AXLearn from incremental improvements in existing frameworks.

### 4.1 Quantifiable Modularity via Asymptotic LoC-Complexity

The most fundamental theoretical contribution of this work is the shift from treating modularity as a qualitative "best practice" to a **quantifiable metric**. Prior systems often claim modularity based on code reuse or object-oriented principles, yet they fail to prevent "change propagation" when introducing new features.

*   **The Innovation:** The authors introduce **LoC-Complexity**, defined as the asymptotic number of lines of code changes required in *existing* modules to integrate a new feature (e.g., RoPE or MoE).
    *   In subtyping-based systems (Megatron-LM, DeepSpeed, TorchTitan), the complexity is **$O(N)$ or $O(NM)$**, where $N$ is the number of modules and $M$ is the number of feature variants. This is because adding a feature often requires modifying parent classes to accept new arguments or instantiate new child types, forcing changes up the inheritance hierarchy.
    *   In AXLearn, strict encapsulation via composition achieves **$O(1)$ LoC-Complexity**. The integration cost is constant regardless of system size because features are injected via config modifiers that operate on interfaces, not implementations.
*   **Why It Matters:** This metric exposes a hidden scaling law in ML engineering: **technical debt in framework code grows linearly or quadratically with the number of model variants in traditional systems.**
    *   **Evidence:** Table 2 demonstrates this starkly. Integrating MoE into a production codebase with 20 model variants requires **~4,000 LoC** changes in DeepSpeed versus **0 LoC** changes to existing interfaces in AXLearn (using a single 10-line script).
    *   **Significance:** This is not merely a convenience; it fundamentally alters the velocity of research. It decouples the cost of experimentation from the scale of the codebase, allowing hundreds of engineers to run thousands of diverse experiments (the paper notes **>1,000 experiments** using the same MoE snippet) without merging conflicting changes to core model files.

### 4.2 The "Compiler-First" Bet for Hardware Agnosticism

While many systems claim hardware agnosticism, they typically achieve it by writing lowest-common-denominator code (sacrificing performance) or by maintaining separate code paths for each backend (sacrificing maintainability). AXLearn introduces a third path: **delegating hardware specificity to the compiler and configuration, not the model code.**

*   **The Innovation:** AXLearn treats the **XLA compiler** not just as an optimization pass, but as the primary abstraction layer for hardware diversity. The system relies on the insight that **sharding and parallelism strategies are properties of the execution environment, not the model architecture.**
    *   Unlike Megatron-LM, which hardcodes CUDA-specific tensor parallelism into the model logic, or MaxText, which is deeply entangled with TPU-specific primitives, AXLearn keeps the model definition purely logical.
    *   Hardware-specific optimizations (e.g., using `cuDNN` on GPUs, `SplashAttention` on TPUs, or `Nki` on Trainium) are injected via **Mesh Rules** (Section 3.4) as configuration modifiers. The model code remains unaware of the underlying accelerator.
*   **Why It Matters:** This approach future-proofs the codebase against hardware volatility.
    *   **Evidence:** The paper highlights that AXLearn supported **AWS Trainium2** at scale shortly after its release, despite the hardware not existing when development began. This was possible because the integration required only a new Mesh Rule and kernel dispatch logic, not a rewrite of the transformer layers.
    *   **Significance:** It solves the **vendor lock-in dilemma**. Organizations can shift workloads between NVIDIA, Google, and AWS based on pricing or supply chain availability without refactoring their model zoo. The trade-off—accepting slightly lower peak performance on GPUs compared to highly tuned, GPU-specific frameworks like Megatron-LM (Table 3 shows ~40% vs ~47% MFU on H100s)—is presented as a strategic choice favoring flexibility over marginal gains on a single platform.

### 4.3 Decoupling State from Logic via InvocationContext

Building on JAX typically forces a choice between **functional purity** (passing state explicitly through every function call, which destroys modularity) and **global state** (which breaks reproducibility and composability). AXLearn's `InvocationContext` offers a novel synthesis that preserves both.

*   **The Innovation:** The `InvocationContext` acts as a **dynamic, scoped side-channel** for state management that is invisible to the function signature.
    *   Traditional JAX patterns require "threading" parameters and RNG keys through every layer: `output, new_state = layer(inputs, params, rng_key)`. This forces every module to know the internal state structure of its children, violating encapsulation.
    *   AXLearn pushes state onto a stack upon module entry and pops it upon exit (Figure 3). Modules access state implicitly via the context, allowing them to remain **imperative in style** (`self.param`) while remaining **functional in execution**.
*   **Why It Matters:** This enables **deep composability with third-party libraries** that are not JAX-native or aware of AXLearn's state system.
    *   **Evidence:** The paper notes that optimizers from `optax` (which expect standard functional signatures) can be integrated seamlessly because the `InvocationContext` allows the system to intercept and manage state updates outside the module hierarchy.
    *   **Significance:** It removes the "functional tax" on code readability and modularity. Engineers can write code that looks like standard PyTorch (imperative, stateful) but compiles to efficient, distributed JAX. This lowers the barrier to entry for researchers accustomed to imperative frameworks while retaining the scalability of XLA.

### 4.4 Unified Training and Inference via Encapsulation

A surprising emergent property of AXLearn's strict modularity is its ability to serve as a high-performance inference engine without specialized rewriting.

*   **The Innovation:** Because components like the **KV Cache** are strictly encapsulated modules, inference optimizations (e.g., paged attention, continuous batching) can be swapped in via configuration rather than code forks.
    *   In many systems, training and inference codebases diverge significantly because inference requires distinct memory layouts and caching strategies that break training assumptions.
    *   In AXLearn, the attention layer's interface remains constant; only the internal implementation of the cache module changes.
*   **Why It Matters:** It challenges the industry trend of maintaining separate "training frameworks" and "inference engines."
    *   **Evidence:** Table 4 shows AXLearn achieving **500x faster Time-To-First-Token (TTFT)** and **2.8x higher throughput** than vLLM (a state-of-the-art dedicated inference engine) on TPUs for Llama2-7B.
    *   **Significance:** This demonstrates that **modularity does not compromise performance**; in fact, it can enhance it by allowing rapid integration of cutting-edge inference techniques (like paged KV cache) into the training codebase directly. It reduces the operational burden of maintaining two distinct code paths for the same model architecture.

### 4.5 Shifting Debugging Left with AOT Compilation

AXLearn redefines the development workflow by moving error detection from the **distributed cluster** to the **local developer machine**.

*   **The Innovation:** By leveraging JAX's Ahead-of-Time (AOT) compilation capabilities, AXLearn allows users to verify memory usage, FLOPs, and sharding correctness **without executing a single step on hardware**.
    *   In traditional systems, errors like Out-Of-Memory (OOM) due to poor sharding strategies are often discovered only after launching expensive jobs on hundreds of GPUs, wasting significant resources and time.
    *   AXLearn's AOT path uses the exact same code as the training path, ensuring that a successful local compilation guarantees scalability.
*   **Why It Matters:** This drastically improves **developer goodput** (the rate of successful experiments per unit time).
    *   **Evidence:** The paper describes how this capability allowed development to scale to hundreds of engineers despite limited TPU capacity, as most debugging occurred on CPU-only local environments.
    *   **Significance:** It transforms resource contention from a bottleneck into a non-issue for development. By catching configuration errors locally, the system prevents "noisy neighbor" problems on shared clusters and accelerates the iteration cycle for complex distributed strategies.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

The authors validate AXLearn through a rigorous evaluation strategy that targets the system's three core promises: **constant-complexity modularity**, **competitive hardware-agnostic performance**, and **operational resilience at scale**. Unlike typical systems papers that focus solely on throughput, this evaluation explicitly quantifies the *engineering cost* of extending the system, treating Lines of Code (LoC) as a primary metric alongside standard performance indicators like Model FLOPs Utilization (MFU).

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The experimental design is split into four distinct pillars, each addressing a specific claim from the motivation section.

**1. Modularity Quantification (LoC-Complexity)**
To test the claim that AXLearn achieves $O(1)$ complexity for feature integration, the authors compare the code changes required to add **Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE)** and **Mixture of Experts (MoE)** across seven state-of-the-art systems: Megatron-LM, DeepSpeed, TorchTitan, Flax, Praxis (Pax's layer library), MaxText, and AXLearn.
*   **Metric:** The authors define **LoC-Complexity** as the asymptotic number of lines of code changes required in *existing* modules (excluding the new feature implementation itself) to support a new feature.
*   **Scenario:** They simulate a realistic production environment with **20 model variants** (e.g., different GPT sizes/architectures) and **10 variants of attention mechanisms** (different kernels, KV cache strategies).
*   **Measurement:** They count the specific LoC changes needed to propagate configuration parameters (like `rotary_base` or `moe_enabled`) through the inheritance hierarchy or config trees of each system.

**2. Training Performance Benchmarks**
To verify that modularity does not sacrifice speed, the authors benchmark training throughput against specialized, hardware-optimized baselines.
*   **Models:** Dense models (Llama2-7B, Llama2-70B) and a sparse MoE model (Qwen-3 30B-A3B).
*   **Hardware Backends:** The evaluation covers four distinct hardware clusters to test agnosticism:
    *   **NVIDIA GPUs:** 256/512 H100s (AWS P5d) and 512 B200s (AWS P6).
    *   **Google TPUs:** TPU-v5p-512 and TPU-v5p-1024.
    *   **AWS Trainium:** 1024 Trainium2 chips.
*   **Baselines:**
    *   *GPU:* PyTorch FSDP, Megatron-LM.
    *   *TPU:* PyTorch XLA FSDP, MaxText.
    *   *Trainium:* No direct baseline exists (AXLearn is the first to support it at scale), so standalone metrics are reported.
*   **Metrics:** Iteration time (seconds), MFU (%), and Throughput (tokens/second). All runs use a global batch size of 1024.

**3. Inference Performance**
To demonstrate the "unified training and inference" capability, the authors compare AXLearn against **vLLM**, a state-of-the-art dedicated inference engine.
*   **Setup:** Llama2-7B and 70B models on Google Cloud TPUs (v5p-8 and v6e-8).
*   **Dataset:** Prompts sampled from the **ShareGPT** dataset.
*   **Metrics:** Time-To-First-Token (TTFT), Time-Per-Output-Token (TPOT), and total throughput (tokens/s).

**4. Scalability and Resilience**
*   **Weak Scaling:** Two production models (Model A: 70B, Model B: 150B) are scaled from hundreds to tens of thousands of chips to measure MFU degradation.
*   **Failure Recovery:** A controlled fault injection test on a 32,768 TPU job measures the time to detect failure, hot-swap hardware, and restore checkpoints.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results

#### 5.2.1 The Cost of Modularity: $O(1)$ vs. $O(NM)$
The most striking results appear in **Table 2**, which quantifies the engineering debt of subtyping versus composition.

*   **AXLearn:** Achieves **0 LoC** changes to existing interfaces for both RoPE and MoE. The integration is handled entirely by a reusable **10-line config modifier script** that traverses the config tree. This confirms the theoretical **$O(1)$** complexity.
*   **DeepSpeed:** Requires an estimated **4,000 LoC** changes to integrate MoE across 20 model variants. This is due to the need to subclass base models and propagate MoE-specific arguments up the inheritance chain. The complexity scales as **$O(NM)$**.
*   **Megatron-LM:** Requires **400 LoC** for RoPE and **20 LoC** for MoE in the baseline count, but the authors note that in a production setting with multiple variants, the MoE cost scales linearly ($O(N)$) because every module composing a linear layer must be updated to handle the `is_expert` flag.
*   **TorchTitan & Flax:** Both exhibit **$O(NM)$** complexity. TorchTitan requires **400 LoC** for MoE and **240 LoC** for RoPE, as its "flattened" config approach forces every model implementation to explicitly handle new configuration fields.

> **Key Insight:** The data validates the authors' hypothesis that subtyping creates a "combinatorial explosion" of code changes. In a production environment with hundreds of experiments, AXLearn's approach saves thousands of lines of fragile, duplicated code per feature added.

#### 5.2.2 Training Performance: Competitive Across Heterogeneous Hardware
**Table 3** presents the training performance comparison. AXLearn demonstrates that hardware agnosticism does not require sacrificing peak performance, though trade-offs exist depending on the backend.

*   **TPU Performance (State-of-the-Art):**
    *   On **Llama2-7B (TPU-v5p-512)**, AXLearn achieves **66.2% MFU** and **1.7M tokens/s**, outperforming both PyTorch XLA FSDP (46.7% MFU) and MaxText (61.6% MFU).
    *   On **Llama2-70B (TPU-v5p-1024)**, AXLearn reaches **68.0% MFU**, significantly higher than MaxText's 64.4%. Notably, PyTorch XLA FSDP fails entirely with **Out-Of-Memory (OOM)** errors on this configuration.
    *   For the **Qwen-3 30B-A3B (MoE)** model, AXLearn (31.58% MFU) slightly edges out MaxText (31.31% MFU).

*   **GPU Performance (The Flexibility Trade-off):**
    *   On **H100 GPUs (Llama2-7B)**, AXLearn achieves **54.2% MFU**, matching MaxText (54.7%) and significantly beating PyTorch FSDP (29.9%).
    *   However, on the larger **Llama2-70B (H100)**, **Megatron-LM** leads with **47.2% MFU**, while AXLearn achieves **40.0% MFU**.
    *   **Analysis:** The ~7% MFU gap on GPUs is attributed to PyTorch's finer-grained scheduling capabilities compared to XLA. The authors explicitly frame this as a strategic trade-off: accepting slightly lower peak performance on NVIDIA GPUs to gain seamless support for TPUs and Trainium without code forks.

*   **Trainium2 Support:**
    *   AXLearn is the only system reported to run on **Trainium2**. It achieves **24.2% MFU** on Llama2-7B and **25.0% MFU** on Llama2-70B. While lower than GPU/TPU numbers (reflecting the maturity of the hardware and compiler stack), it proves the system's ability to target emerging hardware immediately upon release.

#### 5.2.3 Scalability and Efficiency
**Figure 4** illustrates the weak-scaling behavior of AXLearn on TPUs.
*   **Model A (70B):** Scaling from 256 to 4,096 chips reduces MFU from **63.0% to 52.4%**.
*   **Model B (150B):** Scaling from 8,192 to 32,768 chips reduces MFU from **40.6% to 37.6%**.
*   **Interpretation:** The relatively flat degradation curve demonstrates near-linear scaling. The lower absolute MFU for the 150B model is due to constraints on global batch size required for training convergence at that scale, not system inefficiency.

#### 5.2.4 Inference: Surpassing Specialized Engines
**Table 4** reveals a surprising result: AXLearn's training codebase, when configured for inference, outperforms **vLLM**, a dedicated inference engine, on TPUs.
*   **Llama2-7B:** AXLearn achieves a **TTFT of 40.1ms** vs. vLLM's **538.6ms** (a **13x speedup**, though the text claims "500x" likely referring to specific edge cases or initialization overheads not fully detailed in the table summary, the table clearly shows massive superiority). Throughput is **3125 tokens/s** for AXLearn vs. **1117 tokens/s** for vLLM (**2.8x faster**).
*   **Llama2-70B:** AXLearn achieves a **TTFT of 150.5ms** vs. vLLM's **80s** (a **~500x speedup**). Throughput is **1139 tokens/s** vs. **705 tokens/s** (**1.6x faster**).
*   **Context:** The authors note that vLLM's TPU support was experimental at the time of benchmarking, which contributes to the gap. However, the result validates that modular encapsulation allows AXLearn to swap in optimized KV cache layouts (like paged attention) as easily as it swaps training layers.

#### 5.2.5 Failure Recovery Latency
**Figure 5** details a real-world failure recovery event on a 32,768 TPU job.
*   **Timeline:**
    1.  **Failure Detection:** Immediate upon hardware fault.
    2.  **Slice-Level Hot-Swap:** Completed in **4 minutes**. The Kubernetes scheduler replaced the failed node with a spare.
    3.  **Checkpoint Restoration:** Completed in **9 minutes** via broadcast from a healthy replica.
*   **Total Downtime:** The total lost training time was **21 minutes**. This includes the recovery time plus the loss of progress since the last asynchronous checkpoint.
*   **Significance:** This demonstrates that at extreme scales (32k chips), where failures are frequent, automated hot-swapping is critical for maintaining high "job goodput."

### 5.3 Critical Assessment

#### Do the experiments support the claims?
**Yes, convincingly.**
1.  **Modularity Claim:** The LoC analysis in Table 2 is definitive. The difference between **0 LoC** (AXLearn) and **4,000 LoC** (DeepSpeed) for the same feature integration provides empirical proof that strict encapsulation eliminates change propagation. The use of a single 10-line script across 1,000+ internal experiments further validates the practical utility of this design.
2.  **Performance Claim:** The results show AXLearn is **state-of-the-art on TPUs** and **competitive on GPUs**. While it trails Megatron-LM on H100s by ~7% MFU, it surpasses PyTorch FSDP significantly. Given that Megatron-LM cannot run on TPUs or Trainium without major rewrites, AXLearn's "good enough" performance on GPUs combined with its universal hardware support validates the "hardware-agnostic" value proposition.
3.  **Resilience Claim:** The 21-minute recovery time on a 32k-chip cluster is a strong operational metric, proving the system can handle the inevitable hardware faults of massive clusters without manual intervention.

#### Limitations and Trade-offs
*   **GPU Peak Performance:** The most notable limitation is the performance gap on NVIDIA GPUs compared to highly specialized frameworks like Megatron-LM. The authors attribute this to XLA's compilation model versus PyTorch's eager/graph hybrid execution. For teams whose *only* requirement is maximum speed on NVIDIA hardware, AXLearn might not be the optimal choice yet.
*   **Trainium Maturity:** The MFU on Trainium2 (~24-25%) is significantly lower than on TPUs or GPUs. This reflects the early stage of the Trainium software stack rather than a flaw in AXLearn, but it highlights that "hardware agnostic" does not mean "equally optimized on day one" for all backends.
*   **Inference Baseline Context:** The massive inference speedup over vLLM is partly due to vLLM's immature TPU backend. While AXLearn's architecture *allows* for high performance, the comparison is somewhat asymmetric. A comparison against a mature TPU inference engine (like MaxText serving) would provide a more balanced view, though the paper implies MaxText's training performance is slightly lower than AXLearn's.

#### Ablation and Robustness
The paper does not present traditional ablation studies (e.g., removing specific components to measure impact) because the system's benefits are architectural rather than parametric. Instead, the **LoC-Complexity analysis** serves as a structural ablation, comparing the "composition" architecture against the "subtyping" architecture found in other systems. The robustness is demonstrated through the **scaling study** (Figure 4), which shows stable MFU even as chip counts increase by orders of magnitude, and the **failure recovery test** (Figure 5), which proves the system's ability to self-heal.

In conclusion, the experimental analysis successfully demonstrates that AXLearn breaks the traditional trade-off between modularity and performance. It achieves this not by being the absolute fastest on every single metric, but by providing a **stable, high-performance baseline across all major hardware platforms** while reducing the engineering cost of innovation to near zero.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While AXLearn successfully demonstrates that strict encapsulation and hardware agnosticism can coexist with high performance, the paper explicitly acknowledges several critical trade-offs. The system's design choices prioritize **flexibility, maintainability, and cross-platform portability** over achieving absolute peak performance on any single specific hardware backend. Understanding these limitations is essential for determining whether AXLearn is the appropriate tool for a given workload.

### 6.1 The Performance Penalty of Compiler Abstraction

The most significant quantitative trade-off identified in the paper is the **performance gap on NVIDIA GPUs** compared to highly specialized, GPU-native frameworks.

*   **The Evidence:** In **Table 3**, when training the **Llama2-70B** model on **64 × H100-8** clusters, AXLearn achieves **40.0% Model FLOPs Utilization (MFU)**. In contrast, **Megatron-LM**, a framework deeply optimized for NVIDIA GPUs, achieves **47.2% MFU** on the identical hardware. This represents a **~7.2 percentage point deficit** (approximately 15% relative difference in efficiency).
*   **The Root Cause:** The authors attribute this gap to the underlying execution model. AXLearn relies on **XLA (Accelerated Linear Algebra)** for compilation and execution to maintain hardware agnosticism. While XLA is powerful, the paper notes that "PyTorch currently [has] finer-grained scheduling capability over XLA."
    *   Megatron-LM leverages PyTorch's eager execution and custom CUDA kernels to manually orchestrate communication and computation overlap with extreme precision.
    *   AXLearn delegates this orchestration to the XLA compiler. While XLA can fuse operations effectively, it may not yet match the hand-tuned scheduling strategies that GPU-specific frameworks have refined over years.
*   **The Strategic Choice:** The authors frame this not as a failure, but as a deliberate engineering decision: *"This is a trade-off we are willing to take."* For an organization like Apple that must run on GPUs, TPUs, and Trainium simultaneously, accepting a ~7% performance penalty on GPUs is the cost of avoiding the maintenance burden of maintaining three separate codebases (one for each hardware type). If a user's *only* requirement is maximum speed on NVIDIA hardware, Megatron-LM remains the superior choice.

### 6.2 Hardware Maturity and "Day-One" Optimization

AXLearn's claim of being "hardware-agnostic" means it can target new accelerators immediately, but it does not guarantee **peak efficiency** on those accelerators from day one.

*   **The Evidence:** On the emerging **AWS Trainium2** platform, AXLearn achieves an MFU of only **24.2%** for Llama2-7B and **25.0%** for Llama2-70B (**Table 3**). This is roughly half the efficiency observed on TPU-v5p (~66%) and significantly lower than H100 performance.
*   **The Constraint:** The paper clarifies that this low efficiency reflects the **immaturity of the Trainium software stack and compiler**, not a flaw in AXLearn's architecture. Because Trainium2 was new, the underlying compilers and kernels (like the AWS Neuron Toolkit) had not yet been fully optimized for large transformer workloads.
*   **The Implication:** While AXLearn allows users to *run* on new hardware immediately (a capability no other baseline system possessed for Trainium2 in the study), users must accept sub-optimal utilization until the hardware vendor's toolchain matures. AXLearn mitigates this by allowing **custom kernel injection** (e.g., swapping in `Nki` kernels), but writing these high-performance kernels requires specialized expertise and time, meaning "hardware agnostic" does not equal "instantly optimal."

### 6.3 Dependency on the JAX/XLA Ecosystem

AXLearn's entire architecture is predicated on the capabilities and limitations of the **JAX and XLA ecosystem**. This introduces specific constraints that do not affect PyTorch-based systems.

*   **Functional Programming Paradigm:** Although AXLearn's `InvocationContext` (Section 4.3) masks the functional nature of JAX, allowing users to write imperative-style code, the system is still bound by JAX's requirement for **pure functions**.
    *   **Limitation:** This can complicate integration with third-party libraries that rely heavily on mutable global state or side effects that are not compatible with JAX's transformation rules (`jit`, `grad`, `pmap`). While the paper claims successful integration with `optax`, complex legacy codebases or libraries with opaque state management may require significant refactoring to fit into AXLearn's functional model.
*   **Compilation Latency:** The reliance on Ahead-of-Time (AOT) compilation (Section 4.2) shifts errors to the local machine (a benefit), but it also introduces **compilation overhead**.
    *   For very large models or complex sharding strategies, the XLA compilation time can be substantial. While the paper mentions **persistent compilation caches** to mitigate this during recovery, the initial compilation step remains a barrier to rapid, interactive debugging that eager frameworks (like standard PyTorch) offer.
*   **GPU Support History:** The paper notes that "JAX/XLA did not initially support GPUs," implying that AXLearn's GPU capabilities are a relatively recent development compared to its TPU roots. While performance is now "competitive," the ecosystem maturity for GPU-specific debugging and profiling tools in JAX may still lag behind the vast PyTorch ecosystem.

### 6.4 Scope Limitations: Inference and Edge Cases

While the paper highlights AXLearn's surprising inference capabilities, there are boundaries to its current scope.

*   **Inference is a "Bonus," Not a Primary Goal:** The authors state, *"Though inference performance is not our design goal..."* The impressive results against vLLM (Table 4) were achieved on TPUs where vLLM's support was "experimental."
    *   **Open Question:** The paper does not provide benchmarks comparing AXLearn inference against mature, production-grade inference engines on **GPUs** (e.g., TensorRT-LLM) or against **MaxText serving** on TPUs. It is unclear if the "unified codebase" approach can match the extreme latency optimizations (e.g., specific kernel fusion for decoding, aggressive quantization) of dedicated inference engines across *all* backends.
*   **Lack of Dynamic Graph Support:** Like most XLA-based systems, AXLearn operates on static graphs derived from the configuration.
    *   **Constraint:** This makes it less suitable for research scenarios requiring highly dynamic computation graphs where the model structure changes unpredictably at every step (e.g., certain types of reinforcement learning or neural architecture search with dynamic growth). The "config-first" approach assumes a relatively stable architecture that can be fully defined before compilation.
*   **Small-Scale Overhead:** The rigorous encapsulation, hierarchical config traversal, and AOT compilation pipeline introduce software overhead that is negligible at scale but might be disproportionate for **small-scale experiments** (e.g., training a tiny model on a single GPU). For researchers working on small prototypes without distributed needs, the complexity of setting up AXLearn's config hierarchy might be overkill compared to a simple PyTorch script.

### 6.5 Operational Complexity of Custom Kernels

Finally, while AXLearn simplifies *integrating* custom kernels via config modifiers, it does not eliminate the difficulty of *creating* them.

*   **The Burden of Expertise:** To close the performance gap on non-TPU backends (like the GPU MFU gap or Trainium inefficiency), engineers must write custom kernels (e.g., using Pallas for TPUs or Nki for Trainium).
*   **Maintenance Cost:** The paper admits that *"achieving peak hardware utilization on each backend requires backend-specific kernel implementations, which demands specialized expertise and ongoing maintenance as hardware evolves."*
*   **The Reality:** AXLearn reduces the *integration* cost from $O(N)$ to $O(1)$, but the *development* cost of high-performance kernels remains high. An organization adopting AXLearn must still invest in low-level kernel engineering teams for each hardware platform they wish to optimize, even if the framework makes deploying those kernels easier.

In summary, AXLearn is not a "silver bullet" that outperforms every specialized system on every metric. Its value proposition is **systemic efficiency**: it trades ~7% peak GPU performance and immediate Trainium optimality for a massive reduction in engineering debt, the ability to switch hardware vendors instantly, and a unified codebase for training and inference. For teams prioritizing raw speed on a single, mature hardware platform, specialized frameworks may still hold an edge; for teams navigating a multi-vendor, rapidly evolving hardware landscape, AXLearn's trade-offs are strategically advantageous.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of AXLearn represents a paradigm shift in how large-scale deep learning systems are architected, moving the industry focus from **raw single-run performance** to **systemic evolvability**. By mathematically demonstrating that strict encapsulation can reduce the complexity of feature integration from $O(NM)$ to $O(1)$, this work challenges the long-held assumption that modularity and high performance are mutually exclusive. The implications extend beyond Apple's internal infrastructure, offering a blueprint for the next generation of ML frameworks in an era of hardware fragmentation and rapid architectural innovation.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Landscape: From "Fastest Run" to "Fastest Evolution"

Historically, the "winner" of deep learning systems benchmarks has been the framework achieving the highest Model FLOPs Utilization (MFU) on a specific hardware cluster (typically NVIDIA GPUs). AXLearn reframes this metric. It argues that in a production environment where models iterate daily and hardware supply chains are volatile, the true cost metric is **engineering latency**—the time required to integrate a new architecture (like MoE) or migrate to a new accelerator (like Trainium2).

*   **The End of Monolithic Frameworks:** AXLearn demonstrates that monolithic, subtyping-heavy frameworks (like early versions of Megatron-LM or DeepSpeed) face a combinatorial explosion of technical debt as model variants grow. The paper's data showing **4,000 lines of code** required to add MoE in DeepSpeed versus **0 lines** in AXLearn suggests that future frameworks must adopt composition-based config systems to remain maintainable at scale.
*   **Hardware Agnosticism as a Standard:** By proving that a single codebase can achieve state-of-the-art performance on TPUs (66.2% MFU) while remaining competitive on GPUs (40.0% MFU) and functional on emerging hardware (Trainium2), AXLearn sets a new standard. It implies that future systems cannot afford to be vendor-locked; the ability to shift workloads based on cost and availability without code refactoring will become a primary requirement for enterprise AI infrastructure.
*   **Convergence of Training and Inference:** The surprising result that AXLearn's training code achieves **2.8x higher throughput** than vLLM (a dedicated inference engine) on TPUs suggests a future where the distinction between "training frameworks" and "inference engines" blurs. If strict encapsulation allows swapping KV cache strategies via config, the need for separate codebases for training and serving diminishes, potentially reducing the operational overhead of deploying LLMs by half.

### 7.2 Enabling Follow-Up Research

AXLearn's architecture opens several specific avenues for future research in systems and machine learning:

*   **Automated Architecture Search (NAS) at Scale:** Because AXLearn treats model architectures as composable config trees, it is uniquely suited for Neural Architecture Search. Researchers can programmatically generate thousands of variant configs (swapping attention heads, FFN types, or layer depths) and submit them to the cluster without modifying core code. This enables large-scale NAS experiments that were previously prohibitive due to the engineering effort required to instantiate each variant.
*   **Compiler-Guided Parallelism Optimization:** AXLearn's reliance on XLA and "Mesh Rules" invites research into **automated mesh shape selection**. Instead of manually defining sharding strategies for each hardware type, future work could leverage the AOT compilation phase to profile memory and communication costs, automatically generating the optimal Mesh Rule for a given model-hardware pair.
*   **Dynamic Reconfiguration and Elastic Training:** The decoupling of state (via `InvocationContext`) and logic suggests possibilities for **elastic training**, where the cluster size or topology changes mid-job. Since the runtime already supports slice-level hot-swapping for failure recovery, extending this to dynamically scale resources up or down based on cloud spot pricing becomes a tractable engineering problem rather than a fundamental architectural hurdle.
*   **Formal Verification of ML Systems:** The use of hierarchical configs and strict interfaces makes AXLearn more amenable to formal verification than imperative, stateful frameworks. Future research could explore using the config tree structure to mathematically prove properties about memory safety, gradient correctness, or parallelism validity before compilation, further leveraging the AOT capabilities described in Section 4.2.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The design choices in AXLearn directly address pain points in several real-world scenarios:

*   **Multi-Cloud Strategy for Enterprises:** Large organizations can deploy AXLearn to run training jobs on the most cost-effective cloud provider at any given time. For example, an organization could pre-train a base model on AWS Trainium2 instances (leveraging lower costs) and fine-tune on Google TPUs (leveraging higher MFU) using the exact same codebase, avoiding the vendor lock-in that plagues current PyTorch or JAX-native solutions.
*   **Rapid Prototyping for Research Labs:** Academic and industrial research labs can use AXLearn to test novel architectural ideas (e.g., hybrid attention mechanisms or new gating functions) in hours rather than weeks. The "10-line script" capability means a researcher can propose a structural change, implement it as a config modifier, and launch experiments across hundreds of nodes without waiting for framework engineers to refactor the core library.
*   **Unified MLOps Pipelines:** DevOps teams can simplify their infrastructure by maintaining a single repository for both training and inference. The ability to swap in inference-optimized kernels (like paged attention) via config means the same artifact used for training can be promoted to production serving with minimal modification, reducing the risk of "training-serving skew" where models behave differently in production than during training.
*   **Long-Term Model Maintenance:** For models that require years of maintenance and iterative fine-tuning (e.g., foundation models for healthcare or finance), AXLearn's $O(1)$ complexity ensures that the codebase does not become unmaintainable as new techniques (like quantization-aware training or new positional embeddings) are added over time.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

For practitioners considering adopting AXLearn or integrating its principles, the following guidance clarifies when this approach is preferable to alternatives:

*   **When to Prefer AXLearn:**
    *   **Heterogeneous Hardware Environments:** If your organization utilizes or plans to utilize a mix of NVIDIA GPUs, Google TPUs, and/or AWS Trainium, AXLearn is currently the only viable option that offers high performance across all three without maintaining separate code forks.
    *   **High-Variance Model Zoos:** If you manage dozens of model variants (e.g., different sizes, sparse/dense mixes, multimodal extensions), AXLearn's compositional config system will significantly reduce code duplication and merge conflicts compared to subtyping-based frameworks.
    *   **Large-Scale Distributed Training:** If you are training models with billions to trillions of parameters where failure recovery and checkpointing efficiency are critical, AXLearn's built-in slice-level hot-swap and asynchronous checkpointing provide production-grade resilience out of the box.

*   **When to Consider Alternatives:**
    *   **Single-Vendor GPU Optimization:** If your sole requirement is squeezing the absolute maximum performance (MFU) out of NVIDIA H100 clusters and you have no need for TPUs or Trainium, **Megatron-LM** or highly tuned **PyTorch FSDP** implementations may still offer a 5–10% performance advantage due to finer-grained control over CUDA kernels and scheduling.
    *   **Dynamic Graph Research:** If your research involves highly dynamic computation graphs that change structure at every step (common in certain reinforcement learning or neuro-symbolic AI tasks), the static graph nature of XLA and AXLearn's config-first approach may introduce friction compared to eager-execution frameworks like standard PyTorch.
    *   **Small-Scale Prototyping:** For single-GPU experiments or small models, the overhead of setting up AXLearn's hierarchical config and AOT compilation pipeline may be unnecessary. Simpler frameworks offer a faster "hello world" experience for lightweight tasks.

*   **Integration Path:**
    *   Teams migrating from PyTorch should anticipate a learning curve related to the **functional programming paradigm** of JAX, although AXLearn's `InvocationContext` mitigates this by allowing imperative-style coding.
    *   Successful adoption requires investing in **config management practices**. Since the system relies heavily on configuration trees, teams should adopt the "golden configuration" testing strategy mentioned in the paper, serializing key configs to version control to prevent regression in experimental setups.
    *   To achieve peak performance on non-TPU backends, organizations should be prepared to invest in **custom kernel development** (e.g., writing Pallas or Nki kernels), as the generic XLA kernels may not match the efficiency of vendor-specific libraries immediately.

In conclusion, AXLearn does not merely offer another training framework; it proposes a new software engineering discipline for machine learning. By prioritizing modularity and hardware agnosticism, it equips the field to handle the increasing complexity of model architectures and the volatility of the hardware landscape, ensuring that the pace of AI innovation is limited by ideas, not by infrastructure debt.
