## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the context length bottleneck in Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) for ultra-hard mathematics by introducing `Intern-S1-MO`, a multi-agent system that leverages lemma-based memory management to enable multi-round hierarchical reasoning using approximately 512K tokens per problem. Trained with the `OREAL-H` reinforcement learning framework, the agent achieves state-of-the-art performance by scoring 26 out of 35 points on IMO2025 non-geometry problems (matching silver medalists) and 102 out of 126 points in the official CMO2025 competition (gold medal level). These results surpass advanced LRMs like Gemini 2.5 Pro on benchmarks such as AIME2025 and HMMT2025, demonstrating a breakthrough in solving Olympiad-level tasks that exceed standard 64k context limits.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Context Length Bottleneck in Mathematical Reasoning

The central problem this paper addresses is a fundamental architectural limitation in Large Reasoning Models (LRMs): **context length constraints prevent adequate exploration of solution spaces for ultra-hard mathematical problems**. While LRMs have made substantial progress on AIME-level problems through Chain-of-Thought (CoT) techniques and Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR), their performance remains heavily dependent on extended reasoning context length (Abstract, Introduction paragraph 2).

The mismatch becomes critical at the International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO) level. As illustrated in **Figure 1(a)**, both average human thinking time and model token consumption per problem grow exponentially with problem difficulty. State-of-the-art reasoning models typically support a maximum context length of only 64k or 128k tokens (Introduction, paragraph 3), which the authors identify as insufficient for IMO-level problems that may require approximately 512K tokens to solve effectively. This is not merely a matter of allocating more compute—the paper notes that hardware and data limitations make unlimited scaling of context length infeasible (Introduction, paragraph 3).

The core gap is that **existing LRMs cannot accumulate and reuse intermediate reasoning progress across multiple exploration rounds**. When a model exhausts its context budget without reaching a solution, it cannot preserve validated intermediate conclusions (lemmas) for subsequent attempts. Instead, it must restart reasoning from scratch, losing all partial progress. This fundamentally limits the depth of logical deduction achievable in a single forward pass.

### Why This Problem Matters

This limitation carries both theoretical and practical significance:

**Theoretical Significance:** Reasoning is regarded as a significant milestone towards Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) (Introduction, paragraph 1), requiring integration of deductive logic, pattern recognition, and creative problem decomposition. IMO problems represent the frontier of mathematical reasoning challenges—they cannot be solved through pattern matching or heuristic retrieval from pre-training data alone. Instead, they demand construction of novel proof paths and synthesis of auxiliary lemmas (Section 5.2). Successfully solving these problems demonstrates a model's capacity for sustained, multi-step deduction—a capability essential for broader reasoning tasks beyond mathematics.

**Practical Impact:** The context length bottleneck directly limits what problems LRMs can solve. As **Figure 1(b)** shows, performance on challenging mathematical benchmarks correlates with available reasoning budget. Without a mechanism to extend effective reasoning depth, LRMs remain confined to problems solvable within their native context window. This constrains their utility in research, education, and any domain requiring complex logical derivation.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper systematically identifies several categories of prior work, each with specific shortcomings for IMO-level reasoning:

**1. Single-Cycle Multi-Round Methods:** Some studies explored multi-round interaction (Motwani et al. [21]) or parallel decoding (Zhang et al. [41]) to perform long logical deduction. Huang & Yang [12] introduced self-reflective prompt engineering allowing models to identify flaws in intermediate steps. However, these approaches **still confine problem-solving to a single reasoning cycle** (even with internal iterations) rather than building cumulatively upon prior reasoning trajectories (Introduction, paragraph 4). They lack mechanisms to leverage historical explorations for further in-depth deduction.

**2. Formal Language-Based Search:** Approaches using formal proof systems (Chen et al. [4], Ren et al. [27], Zhou et al. [46]) maintain structured repositories to store and reuse intermediate results, reducing reliance on model context length. However, they introduce three critical costs:
- Proof verification and state traversal demand extensive iterations, leading to high computational and search overhead
- Formal systems require translating informal problem descriptions into formal logic, introducing additional translation costs
- The formal representation hinders interaction between AI and humans (Introduction, paragraph 4)

**3. Tree Search Methods:** Tree-of-Thoughts (Yao et al. [37]) and Monte Carlo Tree Search (Zhang et al. [41]) facilitate parallel search during inference. While these methods broaden the search landscape, they **often lack depth and struggle to effectively decompose complex problems** (Section 2.1). They expand breadth rather than enabling sustained deep deduction.

**4. Tool-Augmented Reasoning:** Research augmenting LLMs with external tools (Gou et al. [8], Huang et al. [11], Shao et al. [29]) grounds reasoning in computation or verified knowledge. Yet these tools typically **serve to enhance the existing reasoning process rather than fundamentally restructure it** (Section 2.1).

**5. Outcome-Based Reinforcement Learning:** RL for mathematical reasoning has primarily focused on outcome rewards where feedback is based solely on final answer correctness (Section 2.2). Methods like ARTIST [42], ToRL [16], and rStar2-Agent [28] exhibit emergent agentic behaviors but remain limited: their decisions are mostly confined to choosing when to retry within a fixed reasoning template rather than engaging in strategic planning or deep exploration. Critically, they **lack summarization and cross-episode awareness**—they operate within isolated reasoning episodes and do not support cumulative knowledge transfer across inferences (Section 2.2).

**6. Proprietary Systems:** Proprietary LRMs (Google DeepMind [7], OpenAI [25]) have reported impressive results on IMO2025 problems, yet **the research community lacks access to their methodologies and models** (Introduction, paragraph 5). This limits reproducibility and prevents the community from building upon these advances.

### Positioning of This Work

Intern-S1-MO positions itself as an **open, multi-agent framework that fundamentally restructures the reasoning process** rather than enhancing existing single-pass approaches. The paper makes three key positioning claims:

**1. Hierarchical Decomposition Over Single-Pass Reasoning:** Unlike methods that attempt to solve problems in one continuous reasoning trace, Intern-S1-MO conducts multi-round hierarchical reasoning composed of reasoning, summary, and verification agents (Abstract). This strategy closely aligns with human problem-solving patterns where complex problems are decomposed into manageable sub-lemmas (Section 3).

**2. Lemma-Based Memory Over Context Scaling:** Rather than scaling context length (which faces hardware limits), the system maintains a compact memory in the form of lemmas. After each single-round reasoning, the agent compresses its current reasoning history into concise sub-lemmas with a structured memory repository, enabling recovery of historical exploration outcomes in subsequent steps (Introduction, paragraph 5). This effectively extends the 64K constraints of LRMs by about 8 times (Conclusion).

**3. Process-Verified RL Over Outcome-Only Training:** The OREAL-H framework exploits additional reward signals from an outcome process verifier (OPV) that provides continuous feedback, modified for Hierarchical Markov Decision Process formulation to suit the multi-agent setting (Introduction, paragraph 6). This addresses the sparse reward problem in outcome-based RL while avoiding the noise issues in direct process verification.

**4. Open Methodology Over Proprietary Systems:** Unlike proprietary LRMs that achieved gold-medal standards at IMO2025 (DeepMind [7]), this work provides full access to methodologies and models, enabling community reproduction and extension.

The ablation study in **Table 2** validates this positioning empirically: starting from "Single-round with Agents" (70.8 on HMMT2025), each architectural component adds measurable value, with the full system reaching 95.0. This demonstrates that performance gains stem from architectural sophistication rather than model scale alone.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems architecture paper that introduces a multi-agent reasoning framework with a novel reinforcement learning training methodology. The core idea is to replace single-pass reasoning with iterative lemma-based exploration, where intermediate mathematical conclusions are compressed, verified, and stored in memory for reuse across multiple reasoning rounds.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

Intern-S1-MO is a multi-agent system where three specialized models (reasoner, summarizer, and verifier) work together across multiple rounds to solve complex math problems by building up a library of proven lemmas. It solves the context length bottleneck by compressing long reasoning traces into compact lemma statements that can be reused, allowing the system to effectively reason with ~512K tokens despite individual model context limits of 64K tokens.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The system consists of four major components arranged in a hierarchical pipeline. First, the **Reasoner Agent** takes the original problem statement plus any previously verified lemmas from memory and generates a reasoning trace attempting to make progress. Second, the **Summarizer Agent** reads the reasoner's output and extracts any new valid lemmas, compressing the verbose reasoning into concise mathematical statements. Third, the **Theorem Verifier Agent** checks each extracted lemma for logical correctness using parallel sampling to produce confidence scores. Fourth, the **Process Verifier** evaluates the final solution's logical rigor and provides feedback for iterative revision. Information flows cyclically: the reasoner produces traces, the summarizer compresses them, the verifier validates them, and verified lemmas return to memory for the next reasoning round. After multiple rounds, the process verifier evaluates the final answer and triggers revision loops if needed.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### The Multi-Agent Inference Workflow

The inference process operates as a hierarchical multi-round loop as illustrated in **Figure 2**. In each reasoning round, the reasoner agent receives the original problem statement concatenated with all previously verified lemmas from the memory repository, and it attempts to generate new mathematical progress in the form of partial proofs or intermediate conclusions. After the reasoner completes its output, the summarizer agent reads the full reasoning trace and extracts only the essential validated intermediate lemmas, discarding redundant exploratory content and trial-and-error attempts that add little value to subsequent deductive steps. The theorem verifier agent then evaluates each extracted lemma independently using parallel sampling, performing $n=4$ verification attempts per lemma as specified in Appendix B.1, and computes a confidence score as the proportion of successful verifications. Only lemmas passing the verification threshold are added to the structured lemma library, which prevents error propagation from flawed intermediate conclusions that could mislead subsequent deductive directions. This verification step is computationally necessary because summarizing compelling exploration is as complex as the exploration process itself, requiring independent distillation and logical validity checking. After multiple reasoning rounds (up to 8 rounds maximum for reasoner and summarizer per Appendix B.1), the reasoner generates a final solution that undergoes evaluation by the process verifier from OPV [34], which achieves an F1-score greater than 85% on ProcessBench [44] according to Section 3. If the process verifier identifies logical fallacies, the system enters a modification loop where the reasoner revises the solution based on verifier feedback, repeating up to 8 revision rounds maximum until verification passes or the loop limit is reached. The system maintains a maximum output length of 64k tokens per agent turn (Appendix B.1), but through lemma compression and multi-round accumulation, the effective reasoning budget reaches approximately 512K tokens per problem as stated in the Abstract and Conclusion.

#### Lemma-Based Memory Management

The lemma memory system addresses the fundamental observation that models exhibit reasonable decomposition capability for mathematical problems but suffer from premature conclusion bias when reasoning budgets are exhausted. When context limits are reached, models tend to rush toward incomplete or incorrect final answers instead of acknowledging partial progress, so the system refines model behavior via prompt engineering and targeted training to explicitly produce partial deductive progress in single-turn attempts. The memory repository stores validated intermediate lemmas in a structured format that encourages the agent to reuse historical conclusions during new exploration rounds, allowing for deeper deductions based on prior lemmas rather than reprocessing redundant information. Each lemma entry contains the formal mathematical statement, a complete proof with step-by-step justification, and a confidence score from the theorem verifier. The system distinguishes between proven lemmas (propositions with complete or core proofs) and unproven lemmas (conjectures without full justification), storing them in separate boxed environments as specified in the system prompts in Appendix A.1. When the reasoner agent begins a new round, it receives the lemma library as input alongside the original problem, enabling it to build upon previously established results without re-deriving them. This design choice fundamentally restructures reasoning from single-path generation to cumulative problem solving, where each round extends the logical state rather than restarting from scratch. The lemma format requires precise LaTeX mathematical notation with explicit variable definitions, ensuring that lemmas are self-contained and can be understood independently of the reasoning trace that produced them.

#### Hierarchical Markov Decision Process Formulation

The authors model the agentic mathematical reasoning process as a Hierarchical Markov Decision Process denoted $\mathcal{M} = \langle \mathcal{S}, \mathcal{U}, \mathcal{V}, r, R, \gamma \rangle$ where each component serves a specific role in the training framework. The state space $\mathcal{S}$ contains the problem context, reasoning trace, and verification feedback at each timestep. The high-level meta-action space $\mathcal{U}$ includes actions like "extract lemmas", "invoke verification", and "commit answer" that govern the reasoning strategy. The low-level token vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$ represents the actual text generation at the token level. The agent alternates between high-level decisions and low-level generation: at each round $t$, it executes a reasoning action $u_t$ with token sequence $v_t = (v_{t,1}, \ldots, v_{t,T_t}) \sim \pi^L_\theta(\cdot | s_t)$ to produce a reasoning segment. This output is summarized and verified by external modules, yielding natural language feedback which induces an intermediate proxy reward $r_t \in \mathcal{R}$. Upon termination after several rounds, a sparse final reward $R$ indicates correctness of the solution. The training objective is to maximize expected final reward:

$$J(\theta, \varphi) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi^H_\varphi, \pi^L_\theta}[R]$$

where $\theta$ parameterizes the low-level policy and $\varphi$ parameterizes the high-level policy. Leveraging the conditional structure of the hierarchical policy, the per-round advantage can be estimated via a high-level critic $V(s_t)$ updated to satisfy:

$$V(s_t) \leftarrow \mathbb{E}[r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1})]$$

where $s_{t+1}$ is the state after applying action $u_t$ and $\gamma$ is the discount factor. The advantage for round $t$ is then $A_t = r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)$. At the low level, the system performs online policy gradient conditioned on this advantage, aggregating token-level log-likelihoods within the round:

$$\nabla_\theta J = \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t=1}^{K} A_t \cdot \sum_{\tau=1}^{T_t} \nabla_\theta \log \pi^L_\theta(v_{t,\tau} | s_t, v_{t,&lt;\tau})\right]$$

This formulation enables credit assignment across reasoning rounds while maintaining token-level optimization granularity.

#### OREAL-H Reinforcement Learning Framework

The OREAL-H framework introduces two critical adaptations to the base OREAL [18] formulation tailored for the Hierarchical MDP setting. First, credit assignment across high-level reasoning actions is non-trivial due to delayed rewards, requiring intermediate progress signals. Second, the Process Verifier introduces a continuous, noisy reward signal that deviates from the binary outcome supervision assumed in standard RLVR settings.

**Progress-Conditioned Advantage via Lemma Dependency Graphs:** Existing RLVR training predominantly targets outcome verification (final answer correctness), which proves insufficient for complex mathematical tasks requiring high process supervision. To align optimization with granular reasoning fidelity, the system assigns sparse reward signals across reasoning rounds, akin to performing round-level temporal differencing to minimize advantage estimation variance. The system introduces a lemma dependency graph by aggregating reasoning states across multiple rollouts of the same problem, where this graph structure captures the probabilistic contribution of specific lemmas to the final proof. This mechanism functions as a computationally efficient surrogate to Monte Carlo Tree Search, providing high-quality value estimation without the prohibitive overhead of extensive search. Within this topology, the value of a specific lemma $l$ is not isolated but structurally coupled with the proof's progression, defined recursively as the expected value of its subsequent derived lemmas:

$$v(l) = \mathbb{E}_{l' \in \text{Succ}(l)}[v(l')]$$

where $\text{Succ}(l)$ denotes the set of valid lemmas derived directly from $l$ in the dependency graph. For policy optimization, the system anchors credit to rounds that yield verifiable advances. Specifically, for a reasoning round $t$ that generates a set of candidate lemmas $\mathcal{L}_t$, the system adopts an optimistic value estimation strategy where the state value of round $t$ is the maximum value among its generated candidates:

$$V(s_t) = \max_{l \in \mathcal{L}_t} v(l)$$

The round-level advantage is then computed via the temporal difference error between the best potential of the current round and the next:

$$A_t = r_t + \gamma \max_{l' \in \mathcal{L}_{t+1}} v(l') - \max_{l \in \mathcal{L}_t} v(l)$$

where $r_t$ represents the immediate step reward (e.g., syntactic validity or solving a sub-goal). For intermediate rounds yielding no new lemmas, the advantage is effectively masked, ensuring that gradient estimation is driven by the most promising reasoning path discovered at each step and filtering out noise from suboptimal branches.

**Conjugate Reward Modeling for Noisy Process Verification:** Process Verification offers valuable insight into internal logical consistency but introduces inherent noise because a solution passing $k$ out of $n$ verification rounds does not guarantee superior reasoning quality—passes may arise from lucky sampling or superficial plausibility rather than deep correctness. Directly using the empirical ratio $k/n$ as a reward signal risks amplifying this noise, leading to unstable or misguided policy updates that overfit to verification artifacts. To address this, the system adopts a Bayesian perspective and models the latent reasoning quality $p \in [0, 1]$ as a random variable with a uniform prior $p \sim \text{Beta}(1, 1)$, encoding no initial assumption about solution validity. After observing $k$ successful verifications in $n$ independent PV trials, the conjugate Beta-Bernoulli update yields the posterior:

$$p | (k, n) \sim \text{Beta}(k + 1, n - k + 1)$$

Instead of using point estimates like the posterior mean, the system defines the reward as the probability that this solution is strictly better than a canonical "completely invalid" baseline that fails all $n$ checks ($k=0$). Let $p_1 \sim \text{Beta}(k + 1, n - k + 1)$ represent the quality of the current solution and $p_0 \sim \text{Beta}(1, n + 1)$ represent the baseline. The reward is then:

$$R(k, n) = P(p_1 > p_0) = \int_0^1 \int_0^1 \mathbb{I}(p_1 > p_0) \cdot f_{\text{Beta}(k+1,n-k+1)}(p_1) \cdot f_{\text{Beta}(1,n+1)}(p_0) \, dp_1 \, dp_0$$

This formulation provides a principled, probabilistically calibrated reward that accounts for uncertainty in the verification process, naturally suppressing spurious signals from low-pass outcomes while preserving strong gradients for high-confidence valid solutions. In practice, the system fixes $n=4$, balancing verification cost and signal fidelity as stated in Section 4.3.2. Under this setting, $R(4, 4) \approx 5.5$, corresponding to a 99.5% dominance probability over the $R(0, 4) = 0$ baseline, with smoothly interpolated rewards for intermediate cases ($k=1, 2, 3$). By grounding the reward in a relative, distributional comparison rather than raw counts, the conjugate reward model effectively denoises PV feedback, ensuring that policy optimization aligns with latent reasoning quality rather than stochastic verification artifacts.

#### Cold-Start Initialization via Behavioral Cloning

To prime the agent's adherence to structured reasoning formats and internalize the iterative agentic workflow, the system initializes policies via behavioral cloning on filtered trajectories before RL training begins. The training data $\mathcal{D}_{\text{init}} = \{(s_t, v_t)\}$ retains only rounds $t$ where the output admits a well-formed lemma summary that is syntactically valid, non-empty, and logically segmented. The token-level pretraining objective is:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{RFT}}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{(s_t, v_t) \sim \mathcal{D}_{\text{init}}}\left[\sum_{\tau=1}^{T_t} \log \pi^L_\theta(v_{t,\tau} | s_t, v_{t,&lt;\tau})\right]$$

The system continuously augments $\mathcal{D}_{\text{init}}$ with question-answer pairs that are filtered by outcome-based scoring without previous thinking traces. The authors observe that the model exhibits emergent generalization where patterns learned from these simplified trajectories boost agentic solving of the same problems, thereby improving the efficiency of positive trajectory discovery during online RL. This cold-start phase is critical because the RL training requires a reasonably structured policy to begin optimization—without it, the agent would struggle to discover valid lemma structures through exploration alone.

#### Implementation Hyperparameters and Configurations

The training configuration uses batches of 64 questions with 16 rollouts per question as specified in Appendix B.2. The maximum length of each rollout trajectory is set to 65536 tokens, and the correctness of each response is averaged to calculate the pass rate, with questions having an overall pass rate of 0 or 1 discarded from the training batch. For optimization, the policy model is trained with a learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-7}$ using the AdamW optimizer. Both models employ a cosine annealing learning rate schedule, decaying to 1/5 of the initial learning rate over time. The KL coefficient $\beta$ is set to 0.01 to regularize policy updates and prevent divergence from the reference model. During inference, the maximum number of rounds for the reasoner and summarizer agents is 8, the number of parallel verifications for the theorem verifier is 4 per lemma, and the maximum number of rounds for final iterative revision based on the process verifier is 8 (Appendix B.1). The reasoner and summarizer agents have a maximum output length of 64k tokens per turn. For CMO2025 evaluation, the system performed 256-shot parallel search over up to 12 rounds per problem, with intermediate lemmas receiving 8-shot verification feedback and candidate solutions undergoing 8-shot refinement comprising 24 revision rounds (Section 5.4).

#### Design Choices and Alternative Considerations

The choice of lemma-based memory over direct context scaling reflects the hardware reality that state-of-the-art reasoning models typically support maximum context lengths of only 64k or 128k tokens (Introduction, paragraph 3), making unlimited scaling infeasible. The multi-agent architecture separates concerns: the reasoner focuses on exploration, the summarizer on compression, and the verifier on validation, which prevents any single model from being overloaded with multiple conflicting objectives. The conjugate reward modeling choice over raw verification ratios addresses the fundamental noise problem in process verification—passes may arise from lucky sampling rather than genuine correctness—by modeling latent quality as a distribution rather than a point estimate. The lemma dependency graph advantage estimation serves as a computationally efficient surrogate to Monte Carlo Tree Search, providing high-quality value estimation without the prohibitive overhead of extensive search that would be required for full MCTS on Olympiad problems. The cold-start behavioral cloning phase is necessary because RL training from random initialization would struggle to discover valid lemma structures through exploration alone, given the sparse reward signal and complex action space. The system's adaptive control of reasoning budget—initiating multi-round exploration only for challenging tasks—ensures efficient resource allocation rather than uniformly applying maximum compute to all problems.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the fundamental conceptual breakthroughs in Intern-S1-MO, distinguishing between architectural innovations that enable new capabilities versus incremental improvements on existing methods. The paper's contributions fall into three categories: (1) restructuring how reasoning state is maintained across time, (2) reformulating how reinforcement learning signals are extracted from noisy verification, and (3) establishing a new evaluation paradigm for mathematical AI systems.

### 4.1 Lemma-Based Memory as a Context Extension Mechanism

**What Makes This Novel:** Prior approaches to extending reasoning depth fell into two camps: either scale context length directly (hitting hardware limits at 64k–128k tokens per Introduction paragraph 3) or use formal proof systems (requiring costly informal-to-formal translation per Introduction paragraph 4). Intern-S1-MO introduces a third path: **compressing reasoning traces into informal mathematical lemmas that persist across rounds**. This is fundamentally different from tree search methods like Tree-of-Thoughts [37] because lemmas are not just branching points—they are validated conclusions that can be reused without re-derivation.

**Why This Matters:** The innovation enables an 8× effective context extension (from 64k to ~512k tokens per Conclusion) without requiring hardware changes. More importantly, it changes the *structure* of reasoning from linear accumulation to hierarchical composition. As the ablation study in **Table 2** shows, adding multi-round reasoning alone improves HMMT2025 scores from 70.8 to 85.4, but adding the theorem verifier (which gates lemma storage) adds another 0.9 points. This reveals that **unverified memory is dangerous**—the system's ability to reject flawed lemmas is as important as its ability to store valid ones.

**Distinction from Prior Work:** Unlike formal systems (Seed-Prover [4], DeepSeek-Prover-V2 [27]) that require translating problems into formal logic, lemma memory operates entirely in natural language with LaTeX notation. This preserves human readability while still enabling structured reuse. Unlike outcome-based RL agents (ARTIST [42], ToRL [16]) that lack cross-episode awareness, the lemma library creates persistent state across reasoning rounds, enabling cumulative knowledge transfer that Section 2.2 identifies as a critical gap in existing math agents.

### 4.2 Conjugate Reward Modeling for Noisy Process Supervision

**What Makes This Novel:** Standard RLVR treats process verification as a binary or ratio signal: if a verifier passes $k$ out of $n$ checks, the reward is $k/n$. The paper identifies a subtle but critical flaw: **verification passes may arise from lucky sampling rather than genuine correctness** (Section 4.3.2). The conjugate reward model treats latent reasoning quality as a Beta distribution and computes the probability that the current solution dominates a completely invalid baseline. This is the first work to apply Bayesian calibration to process verification rewards in mathematical reasoning.

**Why This Matters:** The practical impact is visible in **Table 2**: adding the process verifier improves CNMO2025 scores from 203.0 to 215.2, and adding OREAL-H training (which uses conjugate rewards) pushes it to 232.4. Without conjugate modeling, noisy verification signals would destabilize policy updates—the paper notes that $R(4,4) \approx 5.5$ corresponds to 99.5% dominance probability over the baseline, providing smoothly interpolated rewards for intermediate cases ($k=1,2,3$) rather than abrupt jumps. This enables stable RL training even with imperfect verifiers.

**Distinction from Prior Work:** Prior process-aware RL (Prover-Verifier Games [14], ProcessBench [44]) used predefined rules or code execution for intermediate supervision, which are not well-suited for complex informal reasoning (Section 2.2). OPV [34] provides the verifier but does not address reward calibration. The conjugate reward formulation is a principled solution to the noise problem that prior work either ignored or handled through ad-hoc thresholding.

### 4.3 Lemma Dependency Graphs for Progress-Conditioned Advantage Estimation

**What Makes This Novel:** Standard RL assigns credit based on final outcomes, creating sparse rewards that make training inefficient for long-horizon tasks. The paper introduces **lemma dependency graphs** that backpropagate success probability from final answers to intermediate lemmas, defining lemma value recursively as $v(l) = \mathbb{E}_{l' \in \text{Succ}(l)}[v(l')]$ (Equation 5). This functions as a "computationally efficient surrogate to Monte Carlo Tree Search" (Section 4.3.1) without requiring exhaustive search.

**Why This Matters:** The advantage estimation formula $A_t = r_t + \gamma \max_{l' \in \mathcal{L}_{t+1}} v(l') - \max_{l \in \mathcal{L}_t} v(l)$ (Equation 6) ensures that gradient updates are driven by the most promising reasoning path at each step, decoupling optimization intensity from trajectory length. This is critical for IMO-level problems where valid solutions may require 10+ reasoning rounds—without progress-conditioned advantages, early rounds would receive vanishing gradients. The ablation study validates this: adding OREAL-H (which uses lemma dependency graphs) provides the largest single improvement in **Table 2**, adding 17.2 points on CNMO2025 over the process verifier baseline.

**Distinction from Prior Work:** Prior RLVR methods (OREAL [18], ZeroTIR [19]) focused on outcome rewards or simple process bonuses. Tree search methods (REST-MCTS* [41]) provide intermediate supervision but require extensive search overhead. Lemma dependency graphs offer a middle ground: they capture structural relationships between reasoning steps without the computational cost of full MCTS, enabling scalable training on Olympiad problems where exhaustive search is infeasible.

### 4.4 Real-World Competition as Evaluation Paradigm

**What Makes This Novel:** Most mathematical AI evaluations use static benchmarks with automated grading. This paper **officially participates in CMO2025** under the same time limits and grading standards as human contestants, with solutions scored by human experts (Section 5.4). This is the first documented case of an AI system competing in a national mathematics olympiad under official conditions.

**Why This Matters:** The score of 102/126 (exceeding the 78-point gold medal threshold) provides evidence that the system's reasoning generalizes beyond benchmark overfitting. **Table 3** shows the system achieved full marks on 4 of 6 problems, with partial credit on the remaining two—this fine-grained breakdown reveals where the system succeeds ( Problems 1, 2, 4, 5) and where it struggles (Problems 3, 6, each scoring 9/21). Such diagnostic information is unavailable from pass/fail benchmarks like AIME.

**Distinction from Prior Work:** Proprietary systems (Google DeepMind [7], OpenAI [25]) reported IMO2025 results but did not participate in official competitions under human judging. Academic benchmarks (MathArena [3], AIME [20]) use automated scoring that may miss nuanced proof errors. The CMO2025 participation establishes a new standard for evaluating mathematical AI: real competition under human expert judgment rather than automated answer matching.

### 4.5 Synthesis: What Separates Incremental from Fundamental

The table below distinguishes between incremental improvements and fundamental innovations in this work:

| Contribution | Incremental Aspect | Fundamental Innovation |
|--------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Multi-agent architecture | Agent orchestration patterns exist in prior work [21, 38] | Lemma memory enables cumulative reasoning across rounds, not just parallel exploration |
| Process verification | OPV [34] provides the verifier | Conjugate reward modeling transforms noisy verification into stable RL signals |
| RL training | OREAL [18] provides base framework | Lemma dependency graphs enable progress-conditioned credit assignment for long-horizon tasks |
| Evaluation | Math benchmarks exist [3, 10, 20] | Official competition participation under human judging establishes new evaluation paradigm |

The fundamental innovations are those that **restructure the reasoning process itself** rather than optimizing within existing paradigms. Lemma-based memory changes how state is maintained. Conjugate rewards change how verification signals are interpreted. Lemma dependency graphs change how credit is assigned across time. Together, these enable capabilities—solving IMO-level problems with 512k effective context—that prior architectures could not achieve regardless of model scale.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their claims about Intern-S1-MO's capabilities. The experimental design serves three purposes: (1) demonstrate state-of-the-art performance across multiple difficulty levels, (2) isolate the contribution of each architectural component through ablation, and (3) establish real-world validity through official competition participation. Understanding these experiments is critical because they reveal not just *that* the system works, but *under what conditions* and *why* certain components matter.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Datasets and Problem Selection

The authors evaluate across five distinct mathematical benchmarks, each serving a specific purpose in the evaluation hierarchy:

**Standard Competition Benchmarks (AIME2025, HMMT2025):** These represent the "upper intermediate" difficulty tier. The American Invitational Mathematics Examination (AIME) and Harvard-MIT Mathematics Tournament (HMMT) are well-established benchmarks in the LLM reasoning literature. The authors use the 2025 editions to ensure uncontaminated evaluation—problems not present in training data. These benchmarks test whether the system maintains competency on problems that advanced LRMs can already solve, establishing a performance floor.

**Olympiad-Level Benchmarks (IMO2025, CNMO2025):** These represent the "expert" difficulty tier where context length constraints become critical. The International Mathematical Olympiad 2025 (IMO2025) contains 6 problems total, but the authors evaluate only on the 5 non-geometry problems worth 35 points total (7 points each). The Chinese National High School Mathematics Olympiad 2025 (CNMO2025) serves as the preliminary round for CMO2025, comprising 14 high-school math competition problems (excluding geometry) worth 260 points total. These benchmarks test whether the system can handle problems requiring novel proof construction rather than pattern matching.

**Official Competition (CMO2025):** This represents the "real-world validation" tier. The Chinese Mathematics Olympiad 2025 (CMO2025) is an official competition with 6 problems worth 126 points total (21 points each). The authors participated under identical conditions to human contestants: same time limits (4.5 hours per day for 2 days), same submission requirements, and same human expert grading standards. This is not a benchmark evaluation but an actual competition entry—the first documented case of an AI system competing in a national mathematics olympiad under official conditions.

The exclusion of geometry problems across IMO2025, CNMO2025, and CMO2025 is a deliberate methodological choice. Geometry problems often require diagrammatic reasoning and spatial visualization that the current system architecture does not explicitly support. By focusing on non-geometry problems (algebra, number theory, combinatorics), the authors isolate the system's symbolic reasoning capabilities without confounding factors from visual-spatial processing.

#### Metrics and Scoring Protocols

The evaluation uses different metrics depending on problem type, reflecting the fundamental difference between answer-only and proof-based problems:

**Final Answer Accuracy (AIME2025, HMMT2025):** For datasets focused on numerical answers, the authors use binary scoring: a response receives full score if and only if the extracted final answer matches the ground truth exactly; otherwise, it receives zero. This is standard practice for these benchmarks and enables direct comparison with prior work.

**Fine-Grained Proof Scoring (IMO2025, CNMO2025):** For Olympiad-level proof problems, the authors adopt a rubric-based scoring logic inspired by MathArena [3], with critical modifications to ensure rigor. The key innovation is explicitly coupling sub-propositions with their corresponding conclusion requirements. As shown in **Appendix D**, the grading scheme for IMO2025 P1 specifies exact point allocations for each proof obligation (e.g., "1 point should be given for rigorously describing a construction for $n=3$"). This prevents false positives where LLM judges might award points for mentioning a concept without actually proving it.

To mitigate the inherent stochasticity of LLM judges, the authors implement an ensemble evaluation protocol. Each generated solution is evaluated in parallel across $N=8$ independent runs. For grading points worth more than 1 point, the model is awarded partial credit if it provides a valid partial proof. The final score for a solution is calculated as the arithmetic mean of the total scores obtained across the eight evaluation runs. This reduces variance from judge inconsistency.

**Human Expert Grading (CMO2025):** For the official competition, solutions were scored by human mathematics experts using the same standards applied to human contestants. This eliminates any ambiguity about evaluation quality—human experts can detect subtle logical gaps that automated judges might miss. The score of 102/126 is not an automated estimate but an official competition result.

#### Experimental Setup

**Training Configuration (Appendix B.2):** During training iterations, each batch consists of 64 questions with 16 rollouts per question. The maximum length of each rollout trajectory is set to 65536 tokens. Questions with an overall pass rate of 0 or 1 are discarded from the training batch—this filters out problems that are either too easy (providing no learning signal) or too hard (providing no positive examples). The policy model is trained with a learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-7}$ using the AdamW optimizer. Both models employ a cosine annealing learning rate schedule, decaying to 1/5 of the initial learning rate over time. The KL coefficient $\beta$ is set to 0.01 to regularize policy updates and prevent divergence from the reference model.

**Inference Configuration (Appendix B.1):** The system uses adaptive inference budgets based on problem difficulty. For standard benchmarks, the maximum number of inference rounds for the reasoner and summarizer agents is 8, the number of parallel verifications for the theorem verifier is 4 per lemma, and the maximum number of rounds for final iterative revision based on the process verifier is 8. The reasoner and summarizer agents have a maximum output length of 64k tokens per turn. For CMO2025 evaluation, the system used an extended search budget: 256-shot parallel search over up to 12 rounds per problem, with intermediate lemmas receiving 8-shot verification feedback and candidate solutions undergoing 8-shot refinement comprising 24 revision rounds (Section 5.4).

**Cold-Start Data (Section 5.1):** The authors collect problems from Art of Problem Solving (AoPS) and in-house datasets as cold-start data, spanning middle school, university, and competition-level difficulty, including both solution-based and proof-based questions. Candidate trajectories are generated using a variant of Intern-S1 [2], then filtered using CompassVerifier [17] for solution-based questions and OPV [34] for proof-based questions. A portion of challenging problems is selected as RL data based on the pass rate of Intern-S1 on those problems—this ensures the RL training focuses on problems where improvement is possible rather than problems that are trivially easy or impossibly hard.

### 5.3 Ablation Study Analysis

The ablation study in **Table 2** is critical for validating that performance gains stem from architectural sophistication rather than model scale or training data alone. The authors incrementally build up the full agent system from a simplified baseline, isolating the contribution of each component.

**Table 2: Ablation study results**

| Model Configuration | HMMT2025 | AIME2025 | CNMO2025 |
|---------------------|----------|----------|----------|
| Single-round with Agents | 70.8 | 81.9 | 178.0 |
| + Multi-round Reasoning | 85.4 | 91.0 | 201.7 |
| + Theorem Verifier | 86.3 | 93.3 | 203.0 |
| + Process Verifier | 89.1 | 94.0 | 215.2 |
| + OReal-H | 95.0 | 96.6 | 232.4 |

#### Component-by-Component Analysis

**Single-round with Agents (Baseline):** This configuration uses the multi-agent architecture (reasoner, summarizer, verifier) but performs only one round of inference—the left part of **Figure 2** without the memory loop. Scores of 70.8 on HMMT2025 and 178.0 on CNMO2025 establish that the agent architecture alone provides some benefit over a raw LRM, likely due to structured prompting and verification. However, the single-round constraint limits the system to problems solvable within one context window.

**+ Multi-round Reasoning:** Adding multi-round reasoning with lemma memory (left and middle parts of **Figure 2**) improves HMMT2025 from 70.8 to 85.4 (+14.6 points) and CNMO2025 from 178.0 to 201.7 (+23.7 points). This is the largest single improvement in the ablation sequence, validating the core claim that lemma-based memory enables deeper reasoning. The larger gain on CNMO2025 than HMMT2025 supports the hypothesis that memory benefits scale with problem difficulty.

**+ Theorem Verifier:** Adding confidence scores for intermediate lemmas provides modest additional gains: HMMT2025 from 85.4 to 86.3 (+0.9) and CNMO2025 from 201.7 to 203.0 (+1.3). While numerically small, this improvement is significant because it demonstrates that **unverified memory is dangerous**. Without verification, flawed lemmas would propagate errors through subsequent rounds. The theorem verifier gates lemma storage, ensuring only validated conclusions enter the memory repository.

**+ Process Verifier:** Adding the process verifier for final solution evaluation (complete inference workflow) improves HMMT2025 from 86.3 to 89.1 (+2.8) and CNMO2025 from 203.0 to 215.2 (+12.2). The larger gain on CNMO2025 reflects that Olympiad problems benefit more from iterative revision—the process verifier identifies logical gaps that the reasoner can then address in revision rounds. This component enables the modification loop described in **Figure 2** where the solution is refined until verification passes or the maximum loop rounds are reached.

**+ OReal-H:** Adding RL training with the OREAL-H framework provides the second-largest improvement: HMMT2025 from 89.1 to 95.0 (+5.9) and CNMO2025 from 215.2 to 232.4 (+17.2). This validates that the conjugate reward modeling and lemma dependency graph advantage estimation (Section 4.3) effectively optimize the multi-round reasoning policy. The 17.2-point gain on CNMO2025 is particularly notable—it exceeds the gain from adding multi-round reasoning itself, suggesting that training is as important as architecture.

#### Why Ablation Matters

The ablation study addresses a critical alternative explanation: perhaps Intern-S1-MO's performance comes from better training data or larger model scale rather than architectural innovations. The incremental build-up from "Single-round with Agents" to the full system demonstrates that each component contributes non-redundant value. The total improvement from baseline to full system is 24.2 points on HMMT2025 and 54.4 points on CNMO2025—gains that cannot be explained by model scale alone since all ablation variants use the same underlying LRM.

One limitation of the ablation study is that it does not include IMO2025 results. The authors explicitly note in Section 5.3 that "due to the limited number of problems in IMO2025 (only five), which brings the volatile results, we compare the evaluation results on HMMT2025, AIME2025, and CNMO2025." This is a methodologically sound choice—ablation studies require stable metrics to detect component contributions, and 5 problems provide insufficient statistical power. However, it means the ablation conclusions are extrapolated to IMO2025 rather than directly measured.

### 5.4 Failure Cases and Limitations

The paper includes a failure case in **Appendix F** that provides important insight into the system's limitations before and after RL training.

#### Pre-RL Failure: Incorrect Convergence

Before RL training, the system attempted an isosceles triangle problem (AMO-Bench P1) asking for the smallest integer $n$ such that any choice of $n$ points from 15 lattice points necessarily contains three points forming an isosceles triangle. The pre-RL system incorrectly concluded $n=9$, constructing an isosceles-free set of size 8 and claiming no isosceles-free set of size 9 exists.

The error stemmed from flawed lemma accumulation. In Round 1, the system generated Lemma 2 with a confidence score of 0.0 (indicating verification failure), yet continued to build upon it in subsequent rounds. Lemmas 10, 11, 12, and 13 all received confidence scores of 0.0, indicating systematic verification failures that the pre-RL policy did not adequately address. The final answer of $n=9$ was incorrect—the correct answer is $n=6$.

#### Post-RL Success: Correct Convergence

After RL training with OREAL-H, the system correctly solved the same problem with answer $n=6$. The key differences in the post-RL solution include:

1. **Proper Interior Point Analysis:** The system correctly identified that any six-point set containing an interior point necessarily contains an isosceles triangle via the Pigeonhole Principle (interior points have exactly four distinct squared distances to other lattice points, so five other points must produce a duplicate distance).

2. **Complete Boundary Case Analysis:** The system exhaustively analyzed all six possible distributions of boundary points across the three sides—(5,1,0), (4,2,0), (4,1,1), (3,3,0), (3,2,1), (2,2,2)—proving that each distribution necessarily produces an isosceles triangle.

3. **Sharpness Argument:** The system correctly demonstrated that five points on a single side are collinear and contain no non-degenerate triangle, establishing that $n=6$ is the minimum.

This failure case illustrates two important points. First, the architecture alone (multi-round reasoning with lemma memory) is insufficient—the RL training with OREAL-H is critical for learning to properly weight verified vs. unverified lemmas. Second, the system's weakness lies not in generating lemmas but in **evaluating lemma quality**—the pre-RL system accumulated low-confidence lemmas without appropriately discounting them, while the post-RL system learned to prioritize high-confidence reasoning paths.

#### Systematic Limitations

Beyond individual failure cases, the paper acknowledges several systematic limitations:

**1. Geometry Problems Excluded:** As noted in Section 5.1, geometry problems are excluded from evaluation across IMO2025, CNMO2025, and CMO2025. The current architecture does not explicitly support diagrammatic reasoning or spatial visualization. This is a significant limitation given that geometry comprises approximately 25% of typical Olympiad problem sets.

**2. "Spark-of-Insight" Problems:** The authors note in Section 5.2 that the remaining deficit on IMO2025 (9 points out of 35) "largely stems from problems requiring highly idiosyncratic transformations or 'spark-of-insight' constructions that elude systematic search." Lemma-based memory enables sustained deduction but does not replicate the creative insight that human mathematicians employ for non-obvious conceptual leaps. This suggests a fundamental limitation of the approach: it excels at problems where progress can be made incrementally but struggles with problems requiring discontinuous insight.

**3. Compute Efficiency:** The CMO2025 evaluation used 256-shot parallel search over 12 rounds with 24 revision rounds—substantially more compute than standard benchmark evaluation. While the Abstract claims the system uses "about 512K tokens to solve a single problem," the actual compute expenditure varies significantly based on problem difficulty and desired confidence level. The adaptive budget control (initiating multi-round exploration only for challenging tasks) helps but does not eliminate the compute-performance trade-off.

**4. Verifier Dependence:** The system's performance depends critically on verifier quality. The process verifier from OPV [34] achieves an F1-score greater than 85% on ProcessBench [44] (Section 3), but this still leaves 15% error rate. The conjugate reward modeling (Section 4.3.2) mitigates but does not eliminate verifier noise. If verifier quality degrades on out-of-distribution problems, the system's performance would correspondingly degrade.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

The experiments provide strong but not conclusive support for the paper's claims. Here is a balanced assessment:

#### Claims Well-Supported

**"Intern-S1-MO achieves state-of-the-art performance on mathematical benchmarks":** Strongly supported. **Table 1** shows Intern-S1-MO outperforming all baselines on all four benchmarks (HMMT2025, AIME2025, CNMO2025, IMO2025). The margins are substantial on Olympiad-level problems (47.5% improvement on CNMO2025 over Gemini2.5-pro).

**"Lemma-based memory enables multi-round reasoning beyond context limits":** Well-supported. The ablation study in **Table 2** shows that adding multi-round reasoning improves CNMO2025 scores from 178.0 to 201.7 (+23.7 points). The failure case in **Appendix F** demonstrates that pre-RL systems accumulate lemmas across rounds, and post-RL systems do so more effectively.

**"OREAL-H training improves multi-round performance":** Well-supported. Adding OREAL-H provides the second-largest ablation gain (+17.2 points on CNMO2025). The conjugate reward modeling and lemma dependency graph advantage estimation are novel contributions validated by this improvement.

**"System achieves gold medal level in official competition":** Strongly supported. The CMO2025 score of 102/126 exceeds the 78-point gold medal threshold by 24 points. This is not a benchmark evaluation but an official competition result with human expert grading.

#### Claims Requiring Qualification

**"Breaking through context constraints for IMO-level math problems":** Partially supported. The system effectively extends reasoning depth through lemma memory, but the 512K token figure (Abstract, Conclusion) is an estimate based on accumulated lemma storage rather than a direct measurement. The actual token consumption varies by problem, and the system still cannot solve all IMO problems (26/35, not 35/35).

**"Matching the performance of silver medalists":** Supported with caveats. The 26/35 score on IMO2025 non-geometry problems exceeds the 21-point silver medalist threshold. However, this is on 5 of 6 problems (geometry excluded), and the comparison is to human silver medalist performance on the same problems, not overall IMO ranking.

**"Surpassing current advanced LRMs like Gemini 2.5 Pro":** Supported for evaluated benchmarks. However, the proprietary models' scores come from technical reports or MathArena, not direct evaluation under identical conditions. There may be configuration differences affecting comparability.

#### Missing or Unclear Information

**Training Data Composition:** The paper states cold-start data comes from "Art of Problem Solving (AoPS) and in-house datasets" (Section 5.1) but does not specify the exact size, difficulty distribution, or overlap with evaluation benchmarks. This makes it difficult to assess whether performance gains stem from better training data rather than architectural innovations.

**Compute Budget Details:** While the paper mentions "512K tokens" for effective reasoning (Abstract, Conclusion), it does not provide a breakdown of token consumption by component (reasoner, summarizer, verifier) or by reasoning round. This makes it difficult to assess the efficiency of the approach compared to alternatives.

**Geometry Problem Performance:** The systematic exclusion of geometry problems from evaluation leaves open the question of whether the architecture can be extended to handle spatial reasoning. Given that geometry comprises a significant portion of Olympiad problems, this is a notable gap.

**Statistical Significance:** The paper does not report confidence intervals or statistical significance tests for the performance differences. While the margins are large enough that significance is likely, explicit statistical analysis would strengthen the claims.

#### Overall Assessment

The experiments provide convincing evidence that Intern-S1-MO's architectural innovations enable superior performance on ultra-hard mathematical reasoning problems. The ablation study isolates component contributions, the failure cases illustrate learning dynamics, and the official competition participation establishes real-world validity. However, some claims require qualification regarding the scope of evaluation (non-geometry only) and the compute-performance trade-off. The work represents a significant advance in mathematical reasoning systems, but it is not a complete solution to Olympiad-level problem solving—the 26/35 IMO2025 score and geometry exclusion indicate remaining challenges.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the assumptions, constraints, and unresolved weaknesses in Intern-S1-MO. While the system achieves state-of-the-art performance on non-geometry Olympiad problems, understanding its limitations is essential for assessing where the approach generalizes and where it may fail. I organize these limitations into four categories: architectural assumptions, unaddressed problem domains, computational trade-offs, and open research questions.

### 6.1 Architectural Assumptions and Their Consequences

The Intern-S1-MO framework relies on several foundational assumptions that enable its design but also constrain its applicability.

**Assumption 1: Mathematical Progress Can Be Decomposed into Verifiable Lemmas**

The entire lemma-based memory system presupposes that complex mathematical reasoning can be meaningfully decomposed into intermediate conclusions that are (a) expressible in natural language with LaTeX notation, (b) independently verifiable, and (c) reusable in subsequent reasoning rounds. This assumption holds well for algebra, number theory, and combinatorics problems where intermediate inequalities, identities, or structural properties can be isolated as lemmas.

However, this assumption breaks down for problems requiring **holistic insight** where the solution path cannot be cleanly segmented. Section 5.2 acknowledges that the 9-point deficit on IMO2025 "largely stems from problems requiring highly idiosyncratic transformations or 'spark-of-insight' constructions that elude systematic search." These problems resist lemma-based decomposition because the key insight is not an intermediate conclusion but a **reframing of the problem itself**. For example, a problem might require recognizing that a combinatorial counting problem is equivalent to a graph-theoretic property—a transformation that cannot be expressed as a lemma derived from prior steps.

**Assumption 2: Verifiers Can Reliably Assess Lemma Quality**

The system depends critically on verifier accuracy. Section 3 states that the process verifier from OPV [34] achieves an F1-score greater than 85% on ProcessBench [44]. While this is strong performance, it implies a **15% error rate** that compounds across multiple reasoning rounds. The conjugate reward modeling in Section 4.3.2 mitigates but does not eliminate this noise—it models uncertainty rather than removing it.

The failure case in Appendix F illustrates the consequence: before RL training, the system accumulated lemmas with confidence scores of 0.0 (indicating verification failure) yet continued building upon them. Lemma 2, Lemma 10, Lemma 11, Lemma 12, and Lemma 13 all received 0.0 confidence, yet the pre-RL policy did not appropriately discount these flawed conclusions. This reveals a critical vulnerability: **the architecture enables error propagation if the policy does not learn to trust verification signals**. The RL training with OREAL-H addresses this, but it means the system's reliability is contingent on successful policy optimization rather than architectural guarantees.

**Assumption 3: Multi-Round Reasoning Improves Over Single-Pass**

The ablation study in Table 2 validates that multi-round reasoning improves performance (+23.7 points on CNMO2025). However, this improvement assumes that the reasoner agent can meaningfully build upon prior lemmas rather than merely re-deriving them. The paper does not report metrics on **lemma reuse rates**—how often does the reasoner actually invoke a stored lemma versus rediscovering it? If reuse is low, the memory system adds overhead without commensurate benefit.

Furthermore, the adaptive budget control (initiating multi-round exploration only for challenging tasks, per Introduction paragraph 5) implies that **not all problems benefit from multi-round reasoning**. For problems solvable within a single context window, the lemma compression and verification overhead may reduce efficiency without improving accuracy. The paper does not provide data on what fraction of problems trigger multi-round exploration versus single-pass solving.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Problem Domains

The evaluation design systematically excludes certain problem types, leaving open questions about the system's generalizability.

**Geometry Problems: A Significant Gap**

Section 5.1 explicitly states that geometry problems are excluded from IMO2025, CNMO2025, and CMO2025 evaluation. This is a deliberate methodological choice because "geometry problems often require diagrammatic reasoning and spatial visualization that the current system architecture does not explicitly support." However, geometry comprises approximately **25% of typical Olympiad problem sets** (often 1-2 of 6 problems per competition day). The exclusion means the reported IMO2025 score of 26/35 is on 5 of 6 problems, and the CMO2025 score of 102/126 is on an unknown subset excluding geometry.

This limitation is not merely a gap in evaluation—it reflects a **fundamental architectural constraint**. The lemma-based memory system operates on symbolic mathematical statements in LaTeX. Geometry problems often require reasoning about spatial relationships, auxiliary constructions, and visual patterns that are difficult to express purely symbolically. Extending the system to geometry would likely require integrating diagram understanding or formal geometry proof systems, which introduces the translation costs the paper identifies as a drawback of formal approaches (Introduction paragraph 4).

**Problems Requiring Creative Reframing**

As noted in Section 5.2, the system struggles with problems requiring "spark-of-insight" constructions. This is not a minor edge case—many IMO problems are specifically designed to reward creative insight over systematic deduction. The paper's error analysis suggests the system excels at problems where "progress can be made through incremental lemma accumulation" but falters when "non-obvious conceptual leaps" are required.

This limitation reveals a deeper issue: **the system optimizes for verifiable progress, not creative discovery**. The lemma dependency graph backpropagates value from successful conclusions to intermediate steps (Equation 5), but this mechanism cannot assign value to reasoning paths that initially appear unproductive but later prove essential. Human mathematicians often pursue seemingly tangential directions that later become crucial—the system's progress-conditioned advantage estimation may prematurely prune such paths.

**Proof-Based vs. Computation-Based Problems**

The evaluation includes both proof-based problems (IMO, CNMO) and answer-only problems (AIME, HMMT). Table 1 shows stronger relative performance on proof-based benchmarks (232.4/260 on CNMO2025, 26/35 on IMO2025) compared to answer-only benchmarks (96.6% on AIME2025, 95% on HMMT2025). While both are state-of-the-art, the pattern suggests the system's strengths align more closely with problems requiring sustained logical derivation rather than computational tricks or closed-form solutions.

This is not necessarily a weakness—it reflects the system's design for long-horizon reasoning. However, it means the system may be **less competitive on benchmarks emphasizing computational speed or algebraic manipulation** where single-pass models with strong pattern recognition can excel.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

The system's performance comes with significant computational costs that the paper acknowledges but does not fully quantify.

**Token Consumption Estimates Are Approximate**

The Abstract and Conclusion state the system uses "about 512K tokens to solve a single problem," extending the 64K context constraint by about 8 times. However, Section 5.4 reveals that CMO2025 evaluation used "256-shot parallel search over up to 12 rounds" with "8-shot refinement procedure comprising 24 rounds." This suggests the 512K figure is a **rough estimate for standard benchmark evaluation**, not a fixed budget.

The paper does not provide a breakdown of token consumption by component (reasoner, summarizer, verifier) or by reasoning round. Without this data, it is difficult to assess:
- How much overhead does lemma compression and verification add?
- What fraction of tokens are spent on productive reasoning versus exploratory dead ends?
- How does token consumption correlate with problem difficulty?

Appendix B.1 states the maximum output length per agent turn is 64k tokens, with maximum 8 rounds for reasoner/summarizer and 8 rounds for revision. This implies a theoretical maximum of $64k \times (8 + 8) = 1.024M$ tokens, but the actual consumption depends on early termination and verification outcomes. The lack of empirical token statistics makes it difficult to evaluate the efficiency of the approach.

**Parallel Search Multiply Compute Requirements**

The CMO2025 evaluation used 256-shot parallel search (Section 5.4), which is **16 times more rollouts** than the standard 16-rollout evaluation protocol (Section 5.1). This reflects a practical trade-off: competition settings justify higher compute expenditure for maximum performance, while benchmark evaluation prioritizes efficiency for fair comparison.

However, this raises questions about **practical deployability**. If achieving gold-medal performance requires 256 parallel rollouts with 12 reasoning rounds and 24 revision rounds, the compute cost per problem is substantial. The paper does not report wall-clock time or GPU-hour requirements, making it difficult to assess whether this approach is feasible for real-world applications beyond competition settings.

**Training Data Requirements Are Underspecified**

Section 5.1 states that cold-start data comes from "Art of Problem Solving (AoPS) and in-house datasets" spanning "middle school, university, and competition-level" problems. However, the paper does not specify:
- The total number of problems in the training dataset
- The difficulty distribution across problem types
- The overlap between training data and evaluation benchmarks
- The fraction of problems that required human annotation versus automated generation

This omission makes it difficult to assess whether performance gains stem from architectural innovations or from **superior training data curation**. The ablation study in Table 2 controls for model architecture but not for training data quality—if the OREAL-H training uses carefully filtered trajectories (Section 4.2 mentions retaining only rounds with "well-formed lemma summary"), the improvement may reflect data quality rather than algorithmic advances.

### 6.4 Open Research Questions

Several unresolved questions remain about the Intern-S1-MO approach:

**Question 1: Can Lemma Memory Generalize Beyond Mathematics?**

The paper positions Intern-S1-MO as a step toward AGI reasoning capabilities (Introduction paragraph 1), but all evaluation is confined to mathematical reasoning. The lemma-based memory mechanism relies on the existence of **verifiable intermediate conclusions**—a property that holds for mathematics but may not generalize to domains like scientific reasoning, legal argumentation, or creative writing where "lemmas" are less well-defined.

Extending the approach to other domains would require answering: What constitutes a reusable intermediate conclusion in non-mathematical reasoning? How can verification be performed without ground-truth answers? The paper does not address these questions, leaving open whether the architectural innovations are domain-specific or broadly applicable.

**Question 2: How Does the System Handle Contradictory Lemmas?**

The lemma memory system stores verified conclusions, but the paper does not discuss how the system handles cases where **newly generated lemmas contradict previously stored lemmas**. This could occur if:
- A lemma was verified incorrectly (15% verifier error rate)
- The problem context changes between rounds
- Multiple reasoning paths produce incompatible intermediate conclusions

The system prompts in Appendix A describe lemma extraction and verification but do not specify conflict resolution mechanisms. This is a potential source of inconsistency that could undermine the reliability of long-horizon reasoning.

**Question 3: What Is the Optimal Balance Between Exploration and Verification?**

The system allocates computational resources across reasoning rounds, lemma verification (4 parallel checks per Appendix B.1), and solution revision (up to 8 rounds). However, the paper does not analyze whether this allocation is optimal. For example:
- Would more verification rounds per lemma reduce error propagation?
- Would fewer reasoning rounds with deeper search per round improve efficiency?
- How should the system adapt its budget allocation based on problem difficulty?

The adaptive budget control mentioned in Introduction paragraph 5 suggests the system initiates multi-round exploration "only for challenging tasks," but the paper does not specify the decision criterion for what constitutes a challenging task. This represents an open optimization problem for future work.

**Question 4: Can the System Learn From Failed Reasoning Paths?**

The OREAL-H framework uses lemma dependency graphs to backpropagate value from successful conclusions (Equation 5). However, the paper does not discuss whether the system learns from **failed reasoning paths**—lemmas that were generated but did not contribute to successful solutions. Learning from negative examples could improve efficiency by pruning unproductive reasoning patterns, but the current formulation focuses on reinforcing successful paths rather than penalizing unsuccessful ones.

**Question 5: How Does Performance Scale With Model Size?**

The paper includes Intern-S1-mini-MO, a distilled variant that scores 176.3 on CNMO2025 compared to 232.4 for the full Intern-S1-MO (Table 1). This 56.1-point gap suggests model scale matters, but the paper does not systematically analyze the **interaction between model capacity and architectural innovations**. Is the lemma memory system equally effective with smaller models, or does it require a minimum capability threshold to function? Answering this question is critical for assessing whether the approach can be deployed in resource-constrained settings.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

The table below summarizes the key trade-offs inherent in the Intern-S1-MO design:

| Trade-off | Benefit | Cost |
|-----------|---------|------|
| Lemma-based memory | Extends effective context to ~512K tokens | Adds overhead for compression and verification |
| Multi-round reasoning | Enables cumulative progress on hard problems | Increases latency and compute per problem |
| Parallel verification (4 shots) | Reduces verifier error propagation | 4× verification cost per lemma |
| Conjugate reward modeling | Denoises process verification signals | Adds computational complexity to reward calculation |
| 256-shot parallel search (CMO2025) | Maximizes competition performance | Substantially higher compute than benchmark evaluation |
| Geometry exclusion | Focuses evaluation on symbolically tractable problems | Limits generalizability to ~25% of Olympiad content |

These trade-offs reflect deliberate design choices rather than oversights. The authors prioritize **reasoning depth over breadth**, **verification over speed**, and **proof quality over computational efficiency**. For competition settings where correctness is paramount, these trade-offs are justified. For applications requiring real-time reasoning or broad domain coverage, the costs may be prohibitive.

### 6.6 Fair Assessment

Despite these limitations, Intern-S1-MO represents a significant advance in mathematical reasoning systems. The key question is not whether the system has weaknesses—all systems do—but whether the weaknesses are **fundamental to the approach** or **addressable through future work**.

The geometry exclusion and spark-of-insight limitations appear fundamental to the lemma-based architecture, which relies on decomposable, verifiable intermediate conclusions. These would require architectural extensions (e.g., integrating visual reasoning or creative search mechanisms) rather than incremental improvements.

In contrast, the computational efficiency, training data transparency, and verifier reliability issues are addressable through engineering refinements. Better token accounting, more detailed dataset documentation, and improved verifier models could mitigate these concerns without changing the core approach.

The open research questions identify fertile directions for future work. If the lemma memory mechanism can generalize beyond mathematics, if contradictory lemma handling can be formalized, and if exploration-verification balance can be optimized, the Intern-S1-MO framework could serve as a foundation for broader reasoning systems beyond mathematical competition problems.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how Intern-S1-MO reshapes the mathematical reasoning landscape, what research pathways it opens, and where practitioners should consider adopting (or avoiding) this approach. The implications extend beyond Olympiad mathematics—they touch on fundamental questions about how AI systems should accumulate, verify, and reuse knowledge across extended reasoning horizons.

### 7.1 Landscape Shift: From Context Scaling to Memory Architecture

Intern-S1-MO represents a **paradigm shift in how we think about extending reasoning depth**. Prior work assumed the solution was straightforward: scale context length until problems fit. This paper demonstrates that architectural innovation can outperform raw context scaling.

**The Core Insight:** The effective reasoning budget is not determined by context window size alone, but by **how efficiently intermediate conclusions are compressed and reused**. As shown in **Table 2**, adding multi-round reasoning with lemma memory improved CNMO2025 scores by 23.7 points—without any change to the underlying model's context limit. This challenges the prevailing assumption that 128k, 256k, or 1M token contexts are necessary for ultra-hard reasoning tasks.

**Implications for Model Development:**

| Prior Assumption | Intern-S1-MO's Challenge |
|-----------------|-------------------------|
| Longer contexts enable harder problems | Lemma memory enables harder problems with fixed contexts |
| Single-pass reasoning is sufficient | Multi-round cumulative reasoning outperforms single-pass |
| Outcome rewards are adequate for RL | Process-verified conjugate rewards stabilize training |
| Proprietary systems lead on hard math | Open methodology can match/exceed closed systems |

This shift has three concrete consequences for the field:

**1. Architecture Matters More Than Context Length:** Model developers should prioritize reasoning architectures (lemma memory, verification loops, hierarchical policies) over单纯 context window expansion. A 64k model with lemma memory can outperform a 256k model without it on problems requiring sustained deduction.

**2. Verification Is a First-Class Component:** The ablation study in **Table 2** shows that adding the theorem verifier (+0.9 on HMMT2025, +1.3 on CNMO2025) and process verifier (+2.8 on HMMT2025, +12.2 on CNMO2025) provides measurable gains. Verification is not optional overhead—it is integral to preventing error propagation in multi-round reasoning.

**3. Training Methodology Determines Ceiling:** The OREAL-H framework contributed +17.2 points on CNMO2025 in the ablation study—more than the theorem verifier and process verifier combined. This suggests that **how you train a reasoning agent matters as much as how you architect it**. Future work should treat RL framework design as a core research direction, not an implementation detail.

**Broader Impact on AGI Reasoning:** The Introduction positions reasoning as "a significant milestone towards Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)" (Introduction, paragraph 1). If lemma-based memory enables sustained deduction on IMO problems, similar mechanisms could extend to:
- Scientific hypothesis generation (accumulating experimental findings as "lemmas")
- Legal argumentation (building case precedents as reusable conclusions)
- Software debugging (tracking verified code properties across iterations)

The key requirement is that the domain supports **verifiable intermediate conclusions**—a property that holds for mathematics but requires adaptation for less formal domains.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper leaves several fertile questions unanswered. Here are the highest-priority research directions it enables:

#### 7.2.1 Extending Lemma Memory to Non-Mathematical Domains

**Research Question:** Can lemma-based memory generalize to domains without ground-truth verification?

**Why This Matters:** Mathematics has a unique advantage—answers are verifiable. In domains like scientific reasoning or policy analysis, "lemmas" (intermediate conclusions) lack binary correctness. Extending the approach requires:
- Defining what constitutes a reusable intermediate conclusion in ambiguous domains
- Developing verification mechanisms without ground truth (e.g., expert consensus, cross-model agreement)
- Handling contradictory lemmas when multiple valid reasoning paths exist

**Concrete Approach:** Start with domains that have partial verifiability:
- **Code generation:** Unit tests provide outcome verification; intermediate functions can serve as lemmas
- **Formal proofs:** Lean/Coq provide deterministic verification; natural language lemmas map to formal statements
- **Scientific claims:** Reproducibility criteria provide delayed verification; prior literature serves as lemma repository

**Expected Challenge:** The conjugate reward model (Section 4.3.2) depends on counting verification passes ($k$ out of $n$). Without binary verification, this formulation breaks. Alternative reward models—perhaps based on expert agreement or consistency checks—would be needed.

#### 7.2.2 Improving Verifier Reliability

**Research Question:** Can verifier accuracy exceed the 85% F1-score ceiling reported for OPV [34]?

**Why This Matters:** Section 6.1 identifies verifier error as a critical vulnerability. With 15% error rate and multiple reasoning rounds, error compounds. The failure case in **Appendix F** shows the pre-RL system accumulated lemmas with 0.0 confidence scores yet continued building upon them.

**Concrete Approaches:**
- **Ensemble verification:** Use multiple verifier models with different architectures; require consensus before accepting lemmas
- **Adversarial verification:** Train a verifier to specifically find flaws in lemmas (similar to Prover-Verifier Games [14])
- **Human-in-the-loop:** For high-stakes applications, route low-confidence lemmas to human review
- **Self-consistency checks:** Verify lemmas against the stored lemma repository for contradictions

**Expected Impact:** If verifier F1-score improves from 85% to 95%, error propagation across 10 reasoning rounds drops from ~80% cumulative error probability to ~40%. This could unlock reliable reasoning on even harder problems.

#### 7.2.3 Optimizing Exploration-Verification Budget Allocation

**Research Question:** What is the optimal compute allocation across reasoning rounds, lemma verification, and solution revision?

**Why This Matters:** Section 5.4 reveals CMO2025 used 256-shot parallel search with 12 reasoning rounds and 24 revision rounds—substantially more than the standard 16-rollout, 8-round protocol. The paper does not analyze whether this allocation is optimal or how to adapt it per problem.

**Concrete Research Directions:**
- **Dynamic budget allocation:** Train a meta-policy to decide how many rollouts/rounds based on problem difficulty estimates
- **Early termination criteria:** Develop signals to stop exploration when additional rounds are unlikely to yield progress
- **Verification depth adaptation:** Allocate more verification rounds to lemmas that are more critical to the final proof (identified via lemma dependency graphs)
- **Cost-performance curves:** Systematically measure how performance scales with compute budget across problem difficulty tiers

**Expected Outcome:** A principled framework for "reasoning budget management" analogous to learning rate schedules in training—knowing when to spend compute and when to stop.

#### 7.2.4 Learning From Failed Reasoning Paths

**Research Question:** Can the system learn from lemmas that were generated but did not contribute to successful solutions?

**Why This Matters:** The OREAL-H framework backpropagates value from successful conclusions via lemma dependency graphs (Equation 5). However, it does not explicitly penalize reasoning paths that lead to dead ends. Learning from negative examples could improve efficiency by pruning unproductive patterns.

**Concrete Approach:** Modify the advantage estimation to include negative signals:
$$A_t = r_t + \gamma \max_{l' \in \mathcal{L}_{t+1}} v(l') - \max_{l \in \mathcal{L}_t} v(l) - \lambda \cdot \text{DeadEndPenalty}(t)$$

where $\text{DeadEndPenalty}(t)$ measures whether lemmas from round $t$ appear in any successful trajectory.

**Expected Impact:** Faster convergence during RL training and more efficient inference (fewer exploratory dead ends).

#### 7.2.5 Geometry and Spatial Reasoning Integration

**Research Question:** Can lemma memory be extended to geometry problems requiring diagrammatic reasoning?

**Why This Matters:** Section 5.1 explicitly excludes geometry from evaluation, representing ~25% of Olympiad content. This is the most significant gap in the system's coverage.

**Concrete Approaches:**
- **Hybrid representation:** Store geometric lemmas as both natural language statements and formal diagram annotations
- **Visual verification:** Use vision models to verify geometric constructions (e.g., checking angle relationships, parallel lines)
- **Formal geometry systems:** Integrate with geometry proof assistants like GeoGebra or GEX for deterministic verification

**Expected Challenge:** The translation cost from informal to formal geometry (identified in Introduction, paragraph 4 as a drawback of formal systems) must be overcome. A middle ground—semi-formal representations with visual grounding—may be necessary.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

Beyond mathematical competitions, Intern-S1-MO's architecture enables several practical applications:

#### 7.3.1 Automated Mathematical Research Assistance

**Use Case:** Assist mathematicians in exploring conjectures and discovering proofs.

**Why This Fits:** The lemma memory system mirrors how human mathematicians work—accumulating intermediate results, revisiting prior lemmas, and building proofs incrementally. The system's ability to produce partial progress (Section 3's prompt engineering explicitly encourages this) is valuable even when complete proofs are not found.

**Integration Guidance:**
- Use for **exploratory phases** of research where multiple reasoning paths should be tried
- Leverage the lemma repository as a **searchable knowledge base** of intermediate results
- Apply human review to high-confidence lemmas before incorporating into formal proofs

**When Not to Use:** For problems requiring creative reframing or "spark-of-insight" constructions (Section 5.2 identifies these as failure modes), the system may produce extensive but unproductive lemma chains.

#### 7.3.2 Educational Tutoring Systems

**Use Case:** Provide step-by-step mathematical tutoring with verified intermediate explanations.

**Why This Fits:** The system's structured output (lemmas with proofs, verification feedback) aligns with pedagogical needs. Students can see not just the answer but the verified reasoning path.

**Integration Guidance:**
- Use the **theorem verifier confidence scores** to identify steps that need additional explanation
- Leverage the **modification loop** to show how proofs are refined based on feedback
- Adapt the lemma format to student level (the prompts in Appendix A already support high-school-level explanations)

**When Not to Use:** For routine computational problems (basic algebra, arithmetic), single-pass models are more efficient. Reserve multi-round reasoning for problems where students benefit from seeing incremental proof construction.

#### 7.3.3 Formal Verification Pipeline

**Use Case:** Generate informal proofs that can be translated to formal verification systems.

**Why This Fits:** The lemma-based output structure maps naturally to formal proof assistants. Each lemma with its proof can be independently translated and verified in systems like Lean or Coq.

**Integration Guidance:**
- Use the **lemma extraction format** (Appendix A.2) as input to formal proof translators
- Apply the **process verifier** as a pre-check before formal translation (catches errors before costly formal verification)
- Store verified formal proofs in the lemma repository for reuse in related problems

**When Not to Use:** If the target domain lacks formal verification infrastructure, the overhead of lemma structuring may not be justified.

#### 7.3.4 High-Stakes Decision Support

**Use Case:** Support reasoning in domains where errors have significant consequences (e.g., regulatory compliance, safety analysis).

**Why This Fits:** The verification-heavy architecture (theorem verifier + process verifier + conjugate reward modeling) prioritizes correctness over speed. The modification loop ensures solutions are refined until verification passes.

**Integration Guidance:**
- Set **high verification thresholds** (e.g., require 4/4 verifier passes instead of 3/4)
- Use **human-in-the-loop review** for lemmas with confidence scores below a threshold
- Maintain an **audit trail** of all lemmas and verification outcomes for accountability

**When Not to Use:** For real-time decision-making (e.g., autonomous driving, trading), the multi-round latency is prohibitive. This approach suits deliberative rather than reactive scenarios.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

For practitioners considering adopting or extending this work, here is practical guidance on when and how to use Intern-S1-MO:

#### 7.4.1 When to Prefer This Approach

**Choose Intern-S1-MO when:**
- Problems require **sustained multi-step deduction** beyond single context windows
- **Verification is critical** and errors are costly (e.g., mathematical proofs, safety analysis)
- You have access to **reliable verifiers** for intermediate conclusions
- **Latency is secondary** to correctness (competition or research settings, not real-time applications)
- The domain supports **decomposable intermediate conclusions** (mathematics, formal logic, some scientific reasoning)

**Evidence:** The ablation study in **Table 2** shows the approach provides diminishing returns on easier problems (HMMT2025: +24.2 points from baseline) but substantial gains on harder problems (CNMO2025: +54.4 points from baseline).

#### 7.4.2 When to Use Alternatives

**Choose single-pass models when:**
- Problems are solvable within standard context windows (64k tokens)
- **Latency is critical** (real-time applications, interactive systems)
- The domain lacks **verifiable intermediate conclusions** (creative writing, open-ended analysis)
- **Compute budget is constrained** (Intern-S1-MO uses ~8× more tokens than single-pass)

**Choose formal proof systems when:**
- **Deterministic verification** is required (no tolerance for verifier error)
- The problem can be efficiently translated to formal logic
- You have existing formal proof infrastructure

**Evidence:** Section 2.1 notes formal systems have high translation costs and search overhead. Intern-S1-MO is preferable when informal reasoning with verification suffices.

#### 7.4.3 Implementation Checklist

For teams implementing this approach:

| Component | Minimum Requirement | Recommended |
|-----------|--------------------|-------------|
| Base LRM | 64k context support | 128k+ context |
| Verifier | 80%+ F1-score on domain | 90%+ F1-score with ensemble |
| Memory System | Simple lemma storage | Structured repository with dependency tracking |
| RL Framework | Outcome rewards only | Process-verified conjugate rewards |
| Inference Budget | 8 reasoning rounds | Adaptive based on problem difficulty |

**Critical Dependencies:**
- **OPV [34] or equivalent process verifier:** Essential for final solution evaluation
- **Lemma extraction prompts (Appendix A.2):** Required for consistent memory format
- **Conjugate reward implementation (Section 4.3.2):** Necessary for stable RL training

#### 7.4.4 Known Reproducibility Challenges

**Challenge 1: Verifier Quality**
The system's performance depends on verifier accuracy. Teams without access to OPV-level verifiers should:
- Invest in verifier development before deploying multi-round reasoning
- Use ensemble verification to compensate for individual verifier weaknesses
- Monitor lemma confidence scores and flag low-confidence accumulations

**Challenge 2: Training Data Curation**
Section 5.1 mentions "Art of Problem Solving (AoPS) and in-house datasets" but does not specify sizes. Teams should:
- Start with publicly available math competition datasets (AIME, IMO archives)
- Filter trajectories for well-formed lemma structures (Section 4.2)
- Discard problems with 0% or 100% pass rates during RL training (Appendix B.2)

**Challenge 3: Compute Requirements**
The CMO2025 evaluation used 256-shot parallel search with 12 rounds and 24 revision rounds. Teams should:
- Start with the standard 16-rollout, 8-round protocol for benchmarking
- Scale up compute only for high-stakes applications
- Monitor token consumption per problem to optimize budget allocation

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision: Towards Cumulative AI Reasoning

Intern-S1-MO points toward a broader vision: **AI systems that accumulate knowledge across tasks, not just within single problems**.

**Current State:** Lemmas are stored per-problem and discarded after completion.

**Future Direction:** Build a **cross-problem lemma repository** where verified lemmas from one problem can seed reasoning on related problems. This would enable:
- Transfer learning across mathematical domains (number theory lemmas aiding combinatorics problems)
- Community knowledge accumulation (shared lemma libraries across research groups)
- Lifelong learning (systems that improve as they solve more problems)

**Key Research Questions:**
- How to index and retrieve relevant lemmas from a large repository?
- How to handle lemma conflicts when new evidence contradicts stored lemmas?
- How to weight lemmas by recency, verification confidence, and domain relevance?

**Connection to AGI:** The Introduction positions reasoning as a milestone toward AGI (Introduction, paragraph 1). Cumulative knowledge accumulation—learning from past reasoning and building upon it—is a hallmark of human intelligence. Intern-S1-MO's lemma memory is a step in this direction, albeit confined to single problems. Extending it across tasks would be a significant advance toward more general reasoning capabilities.

### 7.6 Summary: What This Work Enables

Intern-S1-MO demonstrates that **architectural innovation can overcome hardware constraints** in reasoning systems. The key takeaways for the field are:

1. **Lemma-based memory extends effective reasoning depth** without requiring larger context windows
2. **Verification is integral, not optional**—unverified memory propagates errors
3. **Process-verified RL training stabilizes optimization** on long-horizon tasks
4. **Open methodology can compete with proprietary systems** when architecture is properly designed
5. **Real-world competition evaluation** establishes a new standard for mathematical AI assessment

The work does not solve all challenges—geometry reasoning, creative insight, and compute efficiency remain open. But it establishes a foundation upon which these challenges can be systematically addressed. For researchers and practitioners, the question is not whether to adopt this approach, but **how to extend it** to broader domains and more efficient implementations.
