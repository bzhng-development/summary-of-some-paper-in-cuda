## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the instability in reinforcement learning (RL) for large language models (LLMs) caused by the mismatch between sequence-level rewards and token-level optimization objectives, proposing a novel formulation that validates token-level objectives as a first-order approximation only when training–inference discrepancy and policy staleness are minimized. Through extensive experiments on a 30B Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) model consuming hundreds of thousands of GPU hours across math reasoning benchmarks (HMMT25, AIME25, AIME24), the authors demonstrate that techniques preserving this approximation—specifically importance sampling correction, clipping, and Routing Replay—are essential for stabilizing training. This work matters because it provides a principled theoretical basis for widely used stabilization practices and reveals that once training is stabilized, prolonged optimization yields comparable final performance regardless of cold-start initialization, shifting focus toward RL algorithm robustness over initialization specifics.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Mismatch: Sequence-Level Rewards vs. Token-Level Optimization

To understand the problem this paper tackles, we first need to recognize a fundamental tension in how reinforcement learning is applied to language models. When an LLM generates a response to a prompt, the quality of that response is typically evaluated as a **sequence-level reward**—a single scalar score assigned to the complete output. For example, in mathematical reasoning tasks, the model receives a reward of 1 if the final answer is correct and 0 otherwise. The reward cannot be meaningfully assigned to individual tokens mid-generation because correctness depends on the entire reasoning chain.

However, mainstream RL algorithms like REINFORCE and GRPO optimize at the **token level**. They compute gradients for each token position independently and update model parameters accordingly. This creates a conceptual mismatch:

$$
\text{Reward granularity (sequence)} \neq \text{Optimization granularity (token)}
$$

The paper identifies that this mismatch has been largely accepted in practice without rigorous theoretical justification. Practitioners observe that token-level optimization *works*, but nobody has clearly articulated **why** it works or **under what conditions** it remains valid. This gap becomes critical when training becomes unstable—without understanding the underlying mechanics, debugging failures becomes guesswork.

### Why This Problem Matters: Stability at Scale

The importance of this problem extends beyond theoretical curiosity. The paper emphasizes that **stable training is crucial for successfully scaling RL** (Section 1, footnote 1). Instability manifests in several ways:

- **Training collapse**: Model performance suddenly degrades after initially improving
- **Entropy collapse**: The policy becomes overly confident, reducing exploration and locking into suboptimal behaviors
- **Training-inference divergence**: The model behaves differently during training versus deployment, undermining the validity of learned policies

These failures carry enormous computational costs. The authors report experiments consuming **hundreds of thousands of GPU hours** on a 30B MoE model. When training collapses after thousands of gradient steps, all that compute is wasted. More importantly, instability prevents reliable scaling—if you cannot guarantee that adding more compute will yield better performance, you cannot systematically improve model capabilities.

There is also a practical deployment concern. The paper notes that training and inference typically use different engines (e.g., Megatron/FSDP for training vs. SGLang/vLLM for inference). These engines employ different computational kernels optimized for their respective purposes, which can produce **numerically different outputs** even with identical model weights. This **training-inference discrepancy** compounds the optimization mismatch problem.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

Before this work, the RL-for-LLMs community developed several stabilization techniques through empirical observation rather than theoretical derivation:

**1. Importance Sampling (IS) Correction**

Standard policy gradient methods use importance sampling to correct for the difference between the policy that generated the data (rollout policy) and the policy being optimized (target policy). However, existing implementations like GRPO and CISPO often **omit IS correction for training-inference discrepancy** (Appendix A). They assume the training and inference engines produce identical outputs, which the paper shows is a dangerous assumption—removing this correction causes "rapid training collapse and a sharp drop in entropy" (Section 4.3, Figure 1).

**2. Length Normalization**

Algorithms like GRPO normalize the token-level objective by response length ($\frac{1}{|y|}$). The paper demonstrates this **invalidates the first-order approximation** to the true sequence-level reward, leading to "suboptimal performance" despite maintaining stability (Section 4.3). This is a subtle but important finding: a technique can prevent collapse while still biasing the optimization away from the true objective.

**3. Clipping Mechanisms**

PPO-style clipping prevents aggressive policy updates by truncating the importance ratio. While widely adopted, prior work lacked clarity on **what clipping actually protects against**. This paper positions clipping as a mechanism to control **policy staleness**—the divergence between the rollout policy and the target policy during off-policy updates.

**4. Routing Replay for MoE Models**

Mixture-of-Experts models introduce additional complexity: the expert routing mechanism dynamically selects which parameters activate for each token. Recent work (Zheng et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2025) proposed Routing Replay to stabilize MoE RL training, but without explaining **why** it works or **when** different variants should be used. The paper notes conflicting findings— their on-policy results contradict Ma et al. (2025), possibly due to scale differences (180 vs. 1000+ gradient steps) or precision settings (BF16 vs. FP8 inference) (Section 4.3, footnote 5).

**5. Sequence-Level Optimization Alternatives**

Some studies proposed directly optimizing sequence-level objectives rather than token-level approximations (Zheng et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025a). While theoretically cleaner, these approaches face practical challenges: sequence likelihoods have "large numerical range and high variance," making direct optimization "intractable" (Section 2.2).

### How This Paper Positions Itself

This work distinguishes itself by providing a **unifying theoretical framework** that explains why disparate stabilization techniques work—and when they fail. Rather than proposing entirely new algorithms, the authors:

1. **Derive conditions for validity**: They show token-level objectives are valid as a **first-order approximation** to sequence-level objectives, but only when two conditions hold simultaneously:
   - Training-inference discrepancy is minimized
   - Policy staleness is controlled

2. **Reinterpret existing techniques**: Each stabilization method maps to preserving one or both conditions:
   - Importance sampling correction → addresses training-inference discrepancy
   - Clipping → controls policy staleness
   - Routing Replay → addresses both for MoE models

3. **Provide empirical recipes**: Through systematic ablation across on-policy and off-policy settings, they establish when each technique is necessary. For example, Routing Replay variant R2 works better under small off-policiness, while R3 becomes necessary under large off-policiness (Section 4.4).

4. **Shift focus from initialization to stability**: A notable finding is that once training is stabilized, "prolonged optimization consistently yields comparable final performance regardless of cold-start initialization" (Abstract). This suggests the community should invest more in RL algorithm robustness than in perfecting initial supervised fine-tuning.

The paper positions itself as a **foundational analysis** rather than a novel algorithm proposal. Its contribution is making implicit practitioner knowledge explicit and testable—transforming "this seems to work" into "this works because it preserves condition X, and here is the evidence."

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

This paper presents a theoretical formulation that explains when and why token-level reinforcement learning objectives can successfully optimize sequence-level rewards in large language models. The solution takes the shape of a mathematical proof showing that token-level optimization is valid as a first-order approximation, but only when two specific sources of error—training-inference numerical discrepancy and policy staleness from delayed updates—are simultaneously minimized.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The RL training system described in this paper consists of two separate computational engines that operate in an alternating loop. First, the **inference engine** (e.g., SGLang or vLLM) uses a rollout policy denoted as $\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ to sample complete response sequences $y$ for each input prompt $x$ from a prompt set $D$. Second, these sampled responses are transferred to the **training engine** (e.g., Megatron or FSDP), which holds the target policy $\pi_\theta$ that is being optimized via gradient updates. Third, a **reward model** assigns a single scalar reward $R(x, y)$ to each complete response based on correctness (e.g., 1 for correct math answers, 0 for incorrect). Fourth, the **optimization module** computes token-level gradients using a surrogate objective that approximates the true sequence-level reward, applying corrections for importance sampling, clipping, and—when using Mixture-of-Experts models—Routing Replay to fix which experts are activated. Finally, the updated target policy becomes the new rollout policy for the next iteration, and the cycle repeats. The critical insight is that the inference engine and training engine may produce numerically different outputs even with identical weights, and the rollout policy may become stale if multiple gradient updates are applied before resampling, both of which undermine the validity of the token-level approximation.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first establish the true objective (sequence-level expected reward) and explain why direct optimization is computationally intractable due to high variance in sequence likelihoods.
- We then derive the token-level surrogate objective and show mathematically how it emerges as a first-order approximation to the sequence-level objective.
- We decompose the approximation error into two distinct sources—training-inference discrepancy and policy staleness—and explain the infrastructure and algorithmic origins of each.
- We examine why Mixture-of-Experts models amplify both error sources through dynamic expert routing, requiring specialized handling.
- We detail the two Routing Replay variants (R2 and R3), explaining how each trades off bias against approximation validity under different off-policy conditions.
- We conclude with the MiniRL baseline algorithm, including all hyperparameters and implementation choices used in the empirical validation.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Paper type and core idea.** This is a theoretical analysis paper that provides a mathematical formulation explaining why token-level reinforcement learning objectives can optimize sequence-level rewards in LLMs, with the core idea being that the token-level objective is a first-order approximation to the sequence-level objective that remains valid only when training-inference discrepancy and policy staleness are jointly minimized (Abstract, Section 2.3).

**The true sequence-level objective.** The paper starts from the ground truth of what we actually want to maximize: the expected sequence-level reward, which is formally written as:

$$
J_{\text{seq}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x)} [R(x, y)]
$$

In plain language, this equation states that we want to find model parameters $\theta$ such that when we sample prompts $x$ from our dataset $D$ and generate responses $y$ from our policy $\pi_\theta$, the average reward $R(x, y)$ across all samples is maximized. The reward $R(x, y)$ is a single scalar assigned to the complete response—for example, 1 if a math problem is solved correctly and 0 otherwise (Section 4.2). This sequence-level formulation matches how we actually evaluate model quality, but it creates an optimization challenge because the gradient must account for the probability of entire sequences, not individual tokens.

**Importance sampling transformation for separate engines.** Because responses are sampled in the inference engine rather than the training engine, the paper applies an importance sampling transformation to rewrite the objective as:

$$
J_{\text{seq}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D, y \sim \mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \frac{\pi_\theta(y|x)}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y|x)} R(x, y) \right]
$$

This equation introduces critical notation distinctions that reflect the two-engine architecture. The symbol $\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ denotes the rollout policy in the inference engine that actually sampled the responses, while $\pi_\theta$ denotes the target policy in the training engine that we are optimizing (Section 2.2). The ratio $\frac{\pi_\theta(y|x)}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y|x)}$ is the sequence-level importance sampling weight, which corrects for the fact that we are evaluating a new policy using data sampled from an old policy. The paper deliberately uses different symbols ($\mu$ vs. $\pi$) because training and inference engines typically produce numerically different outputs even with identical model weights due to different computational kernels (Section 2.4).

**Why direct sequence-level optimization fails.** The gradient of the sequence-level objective can be written as:

$$
\nabla_\theta J_{\text{seq}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D, y \sim \mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \frac{\pi_\theta(y|x)}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y|x)} R(x, y) \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t}) \right]
$$

However, the paper states this gradient is "intractable to utilize due to the large numerical range and high variance of sequence likelihood" (Section 2.2). The problem is that $\pi_\theta(y|x)$ and $\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y|x)$ are products of many token probabilities, which can span many orders of magnitude for long sequences. This makes the importance weights extremely unstable—small changes in individual token probabilities compound multiplicatively across the sequence, leading to gradients with such high variance that training becomes unreliable. This is why practitioners resort to token-level objectives despite the theoretical mismatch.

**The token-level surrogate objective.** The paper proposes the following surrogate objective that operates at the token level rather than the sequence level:

$$
J_{\text{token}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D, y \sim \mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})} R(x, y) \right]
$$

The key difference from the sequence-level objective is that the importance sampling ratio is now computed per-token rather than per-sequence (Section 2.3). Each token $y_t$ receives its own importance weight $\frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}$, and these weights are summed across all tokens in the response. The gradient of this objective is:

$$
\nabla_\theta J_{\text{token}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D, y \sim \mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})} R(x, y) \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t}) \right]
$$

This is recognizable as the REINFORCE algorithm with token-level importance sampling correction (Section 2.3). The critical claim is that this token-level objective is not an arbitrary heuristic—it is mathematically justified as an approximation to the true sequence-level objective under specific conditions.

**First-order approximation derivation.** The paper's central theoretical contribution is showing that the token-level objective is a first-order approximation to the sequence-level objective. The derivation starts by assuming the target policy $\pi_\theta$ and rollout policy $\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ are close to each other, which allows us to write the token-level importance ratio as:

$$
\frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})} = 1 + \delta_t
$$

where $\delta_t$ is a small quantity representing the deviation from 1. The sequence-level importance ratio is the product of all token-level ratios:

$$
\frac{\pi_\theta(y|x)}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y|x)} = \prod_{t=1}^{|y|} (1 + \delta_t)
$$

Using a first-order Taylor expansion, this product can be approximated as:

$$
\prod_{t=1}^{|y|} (1 + \delta_t) \approx 1 + \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} \delta_t + O(\delta^2) \approx 1 + \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} \delta_t
$$

The rightmost step neglects second-order and higher-order terms like $\delta_i \delta_j$, which is valid only when all $\delta_t$ values are small (Section 2.3). Substituting this approximation into the sequence-level gradient and taking the derivative yields:

$$
\nabla_\theta J_{\text{seq}}(\theta) \approx \nabla_\theta J_{\text{token}}(\theta)
$$

This is why the paper claims Equation (3) (the token-level objective) is a first-order approximation to Equation (1) (the sequence-level objective). The practical implication is clear: when $\pi_\theta$ is close to $\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$, we can improve the true sequence-level reward by optimizing the token-level surrogate. However, when the policies diverge significantly, the approximation breaks down and training becomes unstable.

**Decomposing the approximation error.** The paper identifies that the gap between $\pi_\theta$ and $\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ comes from two distinct sources, which can be seen by decomposing the token-level importance weight as:

$$
\frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})} = \underbrace{\frac{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}}_{\text{training–inference discrepancy}} \times \underbrace{\frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}}_{\text{policy staleness}}
$$

This decomposition in Equation (5) is crucial because it separates infrastructure-level errors from algorithm-level errors (Section 2.4). The first term, **training-inference discrepancy**, captures numerical differences between the training engine's computation of $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ and the inference engine's computation of $\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$, even when both use the same model parameters $\theta_{\text{old}}$. The second term, **policy staleness**, captures the divergence between the current target policy $\pi_\theta$ and the rollout policy $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ due to gradient updates applied after sampling.

**Training-inference discrepancy: infrastructure origins.** The training-inference discrepancy arises from complex infrastructure factors that are "heavily tied to the underlying infrastructure" (Section 2.4). Training engines like Megatron and FSDP use computational kernels optimized for gradient computation and parameter synchronization, while inference engines like SGLang and vLLM use kernels optimized for throughput and low-latency generation. These different kernels can produce numerically inconsistent outputs given the same model input and weights. The problem is compounded because inference engines often disable batch-invariant kernels to maximize throughput, meaning the same input can receive variant outputs depending on batch composition (Section 2.4). For the experiments in this paper, the authors deliberately stress-test algorithmic correctness by using FP8 precision for inference and BF16 precision for training, which amplifies the training-inference discrepancy (Section 4.2). The paper monitors this discrepancy using the KL divergence between rollout policies in the inference and training engines:

$$
D_{\text{KL}}[\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}} \| \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}] = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D, y_t \sim \mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|x, y_{&lt;t})} \left[ \log \frac{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})} \right]
$$

Recent work cited in the paper shows that "instability or collapse in RL training is often accompanied by a sharp increase in the training-inference discrepancy" (Section 4.2), making this a critical diagnostic metric.

**Policy staleness: algorithmic origins.** Policy staleness arises from trade-offs made to improve training efficiency and computational utilization (Section 2.4). In a purely on-policy setting, the rollout policy equals the target policy ($\theta = \theta_{\text{old}}$), so there is no staleness. However, the inference stage in RL is bounded by generation length and cannot be accelerated by adding more compute. To leverage increased computational resources for faster convergence, practitioners split a large batch of sampled responses into mini-batches for multiple gradient updates. For example, if the global batch size is 4,096 responses and the mini-batch size is 1,024 responses, the system applies $N = 4$ gradient updates per global step (Section 4.4). The first mini-batch uses fresh policy parameters, but mini-batches consumed later exhibit greater policy staleness because the parameters have already been updated. In asynchronous RL frameworks, a single response can even be generated sequentially by multiple model versions, further introducing staleness. The clipping mechanism from PPO is positioned by this paper as a tool to "restrain policy staleness by preventing aggressive policy updates" (Abstract, Section 1).

**Mixture-of-Experts amplification.** Mixture-of-Experts models introduce an additional layer of complexity because they dynamically select and activate only a small subset of expert parameters for each token via an expert routing mechanism (Section 3.1). When incorporating expert routing into the importance weight decomposition, the equation becomes:

$$
\frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})} = \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t}, e^\pi_t)}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t}, e^\mu_{\text{old}, t})} = \underbrace{\frac{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t}, e^\pi_{\text{old}, t})}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t}, e^\mu_{\text{old}, t})}}_{\text{training–inference discrepancy}} \times \underbrace{\frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t}, e^\pi_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t}, e^\pi_{\text{old}, t})}}_{\text{policy staleness}}
$$

Here, $e^\pi$ and $e^\mu$ denote the routed experts in the training and inference engines respectively (Section 3.1). The challenge is that expert routing is now entangled with both error sources. Training-inference discrepancy can cause inconsistent routed experts ($e^\pi_{\text{old}, t}$ versus $e^\mu_{\text{old}, t}$) given the same model parameters and input, which amplifies output divergence. Policy staleness manifests not only in parameter changes ($\theta$ versus $\theta_{\text{old}}$) but also in shifts of routed experts ($e^\pi_t$ versus $e^\pi_{\text{old}, t}$), which can heavily alter the effective policy defined by activated parameters. This makes the first-order approximation significantly more likely to break down for MoE models than for dense models.

**Routing Replay: restoring approximation validity.** To address the MoE-specific challenge, the paper analyzes the Routing Replay approach, which stabilizes RL training by fixing the routed experts during policy optimization so the model can be optimized like a dense one (Section 3.2). The paper formalizes two concrete implementations with distinct trade-offs. **Vanilla Routing Replay (R2)** replays the routed experts determined by the rollout policy in the training engine ($e^\pi_{\text{old}, t}$) during gradient updates, which primarily mitigates the impact of expert routing on policy staleness (Section 3.2). The importance weight under R2 becomes:

$$
\frac{\pi^{R2}_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})} = \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t}, e^\pi_{\text{old}, t})}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t}, e^\mu_{\text{old}, t})}
$$

**Rollout Routing Replay (R3)** uniformly replays the routed experts determined by the rollout policy in the inference engine ($e^\mu_{\text{old}, t}$) within the training engine, which reduces training-inference discrepancy while also mitigating policy staleness (Section 3.2). The importance weight under R3 becomes:

$$
\frac{\pi^{R3}_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})} = \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t}, e^\mu_{\text{old}, t})}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t}, e^\mu_{\text{old}, t})}
$$

Both variants restore the validity of the first-order approximation by reducing one or both error sources, but they introduce a critical trade-off: they implicitly bias the target policy. The original target policy $\pi_\theta$ should use naturally-routed experts $e^\pi_t$, but Routing Replay constrains routed experts to be $e^\pi_{\text{old}, t}$ or $e^\mu_{\text{old}, t}$, creating a modified policy $\pi^{R2}_\theta$ or $\pi^{R3}_\theta$ that deviates from the original (Section 3.2).

**R2 versus R3: when to use each.** The key difference between R2 and R3 is how they alter the target policy across mini-batches, as shown in Table 1 (Section 3.2). For the first mini-batch, R2 does not alter the original target policy because $e^\pi_{\text{old}, t} = e^\pi_t$, while R3 always alters it because $e^\mu_{\text{old}, t} \neq e^\pi_t$. For non-first mini-batches, both variants alter the target policy. This leads to different performance depending on the degree of off-policiness. The experimental results show that "when off-policiness is small (gbs = 2 × mbs), R2 outperforms R3, while when off-policiness is large (gbs = 4 × mbs and gbs = 8 × mbs), R3 surpasses R2" (Section 4.4). Under high off-policiness, R2 fails to sustain stable training because policy staleness accumulates across mini-batches, while R3's consistent expert replay better preserves the first-order approximation despite introducing more bias. The authors hypothesize that "when off-policiness is small, the detrimental impact of R3's alteration to the target policy outweighs its benefit in preserving the validity of the first-order approximation, while under larger off-policiness, the opposite holds true" (Section 4.4).

**MiniRL: the minimalist baseline algorithm.** For empirical validation, the paper employs a minimalist baseline algorithm called MiniRL, which applies two modifications to the REINFORCE objective in Equation (3) (Section 4.1). First, it applies group-normalization to raw rewards as the advantage estimate for each response $y$:

$$
\hat{A}(x, y) = R(x, y) - \mathbb{E}_{y' \sim \mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|x)} [R(x, y')]
$$

This lowers the variance of raw rewards by centering them around the mean reward for each prompt. Second, it adopts the PPO clipping mechanism that prevents aggressive policy updates by stopping gradients for certain tokens, using $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ as the proximal policy to decide whether to clip based on the ratio $r_t = \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}$ (Section 4.1). The full MiniRL objective is:

$$
J_{\text{MiniRL}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D, y \sim \mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} M_t \, \text{sg} \left[ \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})} \right] \hat{A}(x, y) \log \pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t}) \right]
$$

where $\text{sg}$ denotes stop-gradient, and $M_t$ is a masking coefficient defined as:

$$
M_t = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } \hat{A}(x, y) > 0 \text{ and } r_t > 1 + \varepsilon_{\text{high}} \\ 0 & \text{if } \hat{A}(x, y) &lt; 0 \text{ and } r_t &lt; 1 - \varepsilon_{\text{low}} \\ 1 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
$$

This clipping stops gradients when the policy ratio exceeds thresholds, preventing the policy from changing too aggressively in a single update (Section 4.1).

**Key hyperparameters and experimental configuration.** The experiments use a 30B MoE model fine-tuned from Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base with FP8 inference and BF16 training to stress-test algorithmic correctness (Section 4.2). The prompt set contains 4,096 math problems with verified answers for RL training. Evaluation reports average accuracy over 32 sampled responses on HMMT25, AIME25, and AIME24 benchmarks, each consisting of 30 competition-level math problems (90 total). The maximum generation length is set to 32,768 tokens. For MiniRL, $\varepsilon_{\text{high}}$ is set to 0.27 and $\varepsilon_{\text{low}}$ is set to 0.2. The paper additionally applies Truncated Importance Sampling with a truncation threshold of 5 to the token-level IS weight (Section 4.2). For the synchronous RL framework, each global step samples a batch of $B$ prompts with $G$ responses per prompt, then splits responses into $N$ mini-batches for $N$ gradient updates. Across all runs, the mini-batch size is fixed at 1,024 responses ($B = 64$ and $G = 16$ for on-policy; varied $B$ for off-policy experiments). The off-policy experiments test global batch sizes of 2,048, 4,096, and 8,192, corresponding to $N = 2, 4, 8$ gradient updates per global step (Section 4.4). The cold-start initialization experiments use an early-experimental small Qwen3Next MoE model with global batch size 4,096, mini-batch size 2,048 ($B = 128, G = 16, N = 2$), and generation length of 65,536 tokens (Section 4.5). The total compute consumption across all experiments is estimated at 5 to 6 GPU hours per gradient step, amounting to hundreds of thousands of GPU hours overall (Section 4.2).

**Design choices and alternatives considered.** The paper deliberately chooses MiniRL as the baseline to maintain consistency with the surrogate token-level objective in Equation (3), which has been justified by the formulation in Section 2 (Section 4.1). Alternative algorithms like GRPO and CISPO are compared in Appendix A, but they differ in three key ways: they do not consider training-inference discrepancy in their original objectives, they employ length normalization which invalidates the first-order approximation, and CISPO does not clip token gradients which can result in unstable training (Appendix A). The paper also notes that while alternative clipping strategies exist (such as clipping a whole response based on sequence likelihood ratio), the current token-level clipping strategy "has worked decently" so further exploration is left for future work (Section 4.1, footnote 3). Similarly, exploring better advantage estimates beyond group-normalization may be helpful but falls outside the scope of this work. The choice to use a synchronous RL framework rather than asynchronous is deliberate to enable controlled experiments where the degree of off-policiness can be precisely manipulated (Section 4.2).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper's contributions fall into two categories: **fundamental theoretical innovations** that reframe how we understand RL for LLMs, and **empirical discoveries** that challenge prevailing assumptions in the field. Below, I distinguish between what is genuinely novel versus what validates or refines existing practices.

---

### 4.1 Fundamental Innovation: Token-Level Objectives as First-Order Approximations

**What is novel:** Prior work treated token-level RL objectives as pragmatic heuristics that "work in practice." This paper provides the **first rigorous mathematical justification** showing that token-level optimization is valid as a first-order approximation to sequence-level rewards—but only under specific, verifiable conditions.

**Why this differs from prior work:**

| Prior Assumption | This Paper's Insight |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| Token-level objectives are convenient approximations with no formal guarantee | Token-level objectives are mathematically derived as first-order Taylor expansions of sequence-level objectives |
| Stability techniques (clipping, IS correction) are empirical fixes | Each technique preserves a specific condition required for the approximation to hold |
| Training-inference discrepancy is a deployment concern | Training-inference discrepancy directly invalidates the optimization objective itself |

**Significance:** This transforms RL algorithm design from trial-and-error into a **principled engineering problem**. Instead of asking "does this technique improve performance?", researchers can now ask "does this technique preserve the validity of the first-order approximation?" This explains why some widely-used techniques (like length normalization in GRPO) maintain stability while yielding suboptimal performance—they prevent collapse but bias the objective away from the true sequence-level reward (Section 4.3, Figure 1).

**What remains incremental:** The mathematical derivation itself uses standard Taylor expansion techniques. The innovation lies not in the math but in **applying it to reveal why existing practices work or fail**.

---

### 4.2 Practical Innovation: Conditional Guidance for Routing Replay Variants

**What is novel:** While Routing Replay was proposed in concurrent work (Zheng et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2025), this paper provides the **first systematic analysis of when to use R2 versus R3** based on the degree of off-policiness.

**The key finding:** The choice between R2 and R3 is not universal—it depends on how many gradient updates are applied per global step:

- **Small off-policiness** (global batch = 2× mini-batch, $N=2$ updates): **R2 outperforms R3** because the bias introduced by R3's expert replay outweighs its benefits when policy staleness is minimal
- **Large off-policiness** (global batch = 8× mini-batch, $N=8$ updates): **R3 surpasses R2** because accumulated policy staleness across mini-batches makes R2's inconsistent expert routing untenable (Section 4.4, Figures 2–4)

**Why this matters:** This resolves apparent contradictions in the literature. Ma et al. (2025) reported R3's superiority, but their experiments used only 180 global steps with unspecified off-policiness. This paper's larger-scale experiments (1000+ gradient steps) reveal that **R2 fails under high off-policiness** while R3 maintains stability (Section 4.3, footnote 5). The discrepancy stems from experimental scale and precision settings (BF16 vs. FP8 inference), not fundamental algorithmic differences.

**Significance:** Practitioners now have **actionable guidance** rather than conflicting recommendations. For resource-constrained settings with minimal off-policy updates, R2 suffices. For large-scale training leveraging maximum compute parallelism, R3 becomes necessary. This prevents wasted compute on inappropriate stabilization techniques.

**What remains incremental:** Routing Replay itself was proposed in prior work. This paper's contribution is the **conditional analysis** that clarifies when each variant should be deployed.

---

### 4.3 Empirical Discovery: Cold-Start Initialization Becomes Irrelevant Under Stable Training

**What is novel:** The paper demonstrates that once RL training is stabilized, **prolonged optimization consistently yields comparable final performance regardless of cold-start initialization** (Section 4.5, Figure 5).

**The experiment:** Three cold-start models were distilled from different frontier models:
- Qwen3-Max-Thinking-Preview
- DeepSeek-R1-0528
- gpt-oss-120b (high mode)

Despite different initialization sources, all three converged to **similar benchmark scores** on AIME25 and AIME24 after sufficient RL training steps.

**Why this challenges prevailing assumptions:** The RL-for-LLMs community has invested heavily in perfecting cold-start initialization through supervised fine-tuning and distillation from stronger models. This finding suggests that **initialization differences vanish given prolonged stable RL training**. The bottleneck is not where you start—it's whether you can train stably long enough to reach the model's performance ceiling.

**Significance:** This shifts research priorities:

| Previous Focus | Recommended Focus |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Improving cold-start data quality | Improving RL algorithm stability |
| Distilling from stronger models | Extending stable training duration |
| Initialization-specific tuning | Universal stabilization techniques |

**Caveat:** This finding applies to models that can be stabilized. Unstable training still collapses regardless of initialization quality. The insight is that **stability is the prerequisite** that makes initialization differences moot.

**What remains incremental:** The observation that prolonged training improves performance is not new. The novelty is demonstrating that **initialization effects diminish systematically** once stability is achieved.

---

### 4.4 Methodological Contribution: Large-Scale Empirical Validation Across Training Regimes

**What is novel:** The paper conducts **systematic ablation across on-policy and off-policy settings** at a scale rarely seen in RL-for-LLMs research—hundreds of thousands of GPU hours on a 30B MoE model.

**Why this scale matters:** Many prior RL-for-LLMs papers validate on small models (7B or below) for limited training steps (often under 200 gradient steps). This paper's experiments extend to 1000+ gradient steps with explicit monitoring of:
- Training-inference KL divergence (detecting engine discrepancy)
- Token-level entropy (detecting policy collapse)
- Benchmark scores across three math reasoning datasets (Section 4.2)

**Key validation findings:**

1. **On-policy training:** Basic policy gradient with importance sampling correction achieves highest stability. Adding length normalization or removing IS correction degrades performance (Section 4.3, Figure 1).

2. **Off-policy training:** Both Routing Replay and clipping become **necessary** (not optional) for stability. Omitting either causes premature collapse (Section 4.4, Figures 2–4).

3. **Precision stress test:** Using FP8 inference with BF16 training deliberately amplifies training-inference discrepancy, providing a stricter test of algorithmic correctness than prior BF16-only studies.

**Significance:** This large-scale validation **distinguishes techniques that scale from those that only work at small scale**. For example, R3's superiority under high off-policiness only becomes apparent after sufficient training steps—small-scale experiments would miss this (Section 4.3, footnote 5).

**What remains incremental:** The experimental setup follows standard RL evaluation protocols. The innovation is the **systematic manipulation of off-policiness** and the diagnostic metrics tracked throughout training.

---

### 4.5 Synthesis: What Is Truly Novel Versus What Is Validated

| Contribution | Novelty Level | Evidence |
|-------------|--------------|----------|
| First-order approximation formulation | **Fundamental** | Mathematical derivation in Section 2.3 |
| Training-inference discrepancy as optimization concern | **Fundamental** | Equation (5) decomposition, Figure 1 collapse without IS |
| R2 vs. R3 conditional guidance | **Practical innovation** | Section 4.4 comparison across off-policiness levels |
| Cold-start irrelevance under stable training | **Empirical discovery** | Section 4.5, Figure 5 convergence |
| MiniRL baseline algorithm | **Incremental** | Combines existing techniques (REINFORCE + PPO clipping + IS) |
| Large-scale empirical validation | **Methodological** | Hundreds of thousands of GPU hours, 30B MoE model |

**The core insight unifying all contributions:** Stability in RL for LLMs is not achieved by adding more techniques—it is achieved by **preserving the validity of the first-order approximation** that justifies token-level optimization. Every effective stabilization technique (importance sampling, clipping, Routing Replay) maps to minimizing either training-inference discrepancy or policy staleness. Techniques that do not serve this purpose (like length normalization) may prevent collapse but bias the objective.

This reframing transforms RL algorithm design from **empirical tuning** into **constraint satisfaction**: identify what breaks the approximation, then apply the minimum technique necessary to restore it.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section walks through the empirical validation that supports the paper's theoretical claims. The experiments are designed not merely to show "what works" but to test **whether preserving the first-order approximation conditions correlates with training stability**. We examine the methodology, then analyze results across three experimental axes: on-policy training, off-policy training, and cold-start initialization variations.

---

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

#### Datasets and Benchmarks

The paper focuses on **mathematical reasoning** as the test domain, which provides clear binary rewards (correct/incorrect answers) without ambiguity. The experimental setup includes:

- **Training data**: 4,096 math problems with verified answers curated as the prompt set $D$ (Section 4.2)
- **Evaluation benchmarks**: Three competition-level math datasets:
  - **HMMT25**: 30 problems
  - **AIME25**: 30 problems
  - **AIME24**: 30 problems
  - **Total**: 90 problems across all benchmarks

For evaluation, the paper reports **average accuracy over 32 sampled responses** per problem, which reduces variance from stochastic generation and provides a more reliable estimate of model capability (Section 4.2).

#### Model and Infrastructure Configuration

The primary experiments use a **30B Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) model** fine-tuned from `Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base`. A critical design choice is the precision configuration:

- **Inference**: FP8 precision
- **Training**: BF16 precision

This deliberate mismatch creates a **stress test for algorithmic correctness** by amplifying the training-inference discrepancy (Section 4.2). The paper notes this is more stringent than prior work (e.g., Ma et al., 2025 used BF16 for both), which may explain conflicting findings about Routing Replay effectiveness (Section 4.3, footnote 5).

For the cold-start initialization experiments (Section 4.5), the authors use an **early-experimental small Qwen3Next MoE model** with a longer generation length of 65,536 tokens (vs. 32,768 for main experiments).

#### Metrics and Diagnostics

Beyond benchmark accuracy, the paper tracks four diagnostic metrics throughout training to detect instability early:

1. **Benchmark Score**: Average accuracy on HMMT25, AIME25, AIME24
2. **Training-inference KL Divergence**: 
   $$
   D_{\text{KL}}[\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}} \| \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}] = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D, y_t \sim \mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|x, y_{&lt;t})} \left[ \log \frac{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})} \right]
   $$
   This detects numerical divergence between engines (Section 4.2)
3. **Training Reward**: The sequence-level reward during training (binary 0/1 for math correctness)
4. **Token-level Entropy**: 
   $$
   H[\pi_\theta] \approx \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D, y_{&lt;t} \sim \mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ -\sum_{w \in V} \pi_\theta(w|x, y_{&lt;t}) \log \pi_\theta(w|x, y_{&lt;t}) \right]
   $$
   This detects policy collapse (entropy dropping indicates the model becomes overly confident) (Section 4.2)

A sharp increase in KL divergence or drop in entropy typically precedes training collapse, making these **early warning signals** for instability.

#### Baseline Algorithm: MiniRL

All experiments build on **MiniRL**, a minimalist baseline that applies two modifications to the REINFORCE objective (Section 4.1):

1. **Group-normalized advantages**: $\hat{A}(x, y) = R(x, y) - \mathbb{E}_{y' \sim \mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|x)} [R(x, y')]$
2. **PPO-style clipping** with thresholds $\varepsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.27$ and $\varepsilon_{\text{low}} = 0.2$

The paper additionally applies **Truncated Importance Sampling (TIS)** with a truncation threshold of 5 to prevent extreme importance weights (Section 4.2). Mini-batch size is fixed at **1,024 responses** across all experiments ($B = 64$ prompts, $G = 16$ responses per prompt for on-policy).

Compute cost is substantial: **5–6 GPU hours per gradient step**, with total experiments consuming **hundreds of thousands of GPU hours** (Section 4.2). This scale is necessary to observe long-term stability patterns that smaller experiments miss.

#### Experimental Regimes

The paper tests three distinct training regimes:

| Regime | Global Batch Size | Mini-batch Size | Gradient Updates (N) | Off-policiness |
|--------|------------------|-----------------|---------------------|----------------|
| On-policy | 1,024 | 1,024 | 1 | None ($\theta = \theta_{\text{old}}$) |
| Off-policy (low) | 2,048 | 1,024 | 2 | Small |
| Off-policy (medium) | 4,096 | 1,024 | 4 | Moderate |
| Off-policy (high) | 8,192 | 1,024 | 8 | Large |

This systematic manipulation of off-policiness allows the authors to test **when each stabilization technique becomes necessary** (Section 4.4).

---

### 5.2 On-Policy Training Results

On-policy training ($\theta = \theta_{\text{old}}$) provides the cleanest test of the first-order approximation because policy staleness is eliminated by definition. The paper compares six variants:

1. **MiniRL** (baseline with IS correction)
2. **MiniRL + length-normalization**
3. **MiniRL w/o train-infer-IS** (removes importance sampling for training-inference discrepancy)
4. **MiniRL + R3** (Rollout Routing Replay)
5. **MiniRL + R3 + length-normalization**
6. **MiniRL + R3 w/o train-infer-IS**

Note: R2 is inapplicable in on-policy settings because the first mini-batch would have no staleness to correct (Table 1, Section 3.2).

#### Quantitative Results (Figure 1)

After **1,200 gradient steps**, the results show clear separation:

| Method | Benchmark Score (Final) | Training Stability | Entropy Behavior |
|--------|------------------------|-------------------|------------------|
| MiniRL | ~0.75 (highest) | Stable | Maintains ~0.25–0.30 |
| MiniRL + length-norm | ~0.72 (suboptimal) | Stable | Maintains ~0.25 |
| MiniRL w/o train-infer-IS | **Collapse before 400 steps** | Unstable | Sharp drop to ~0.10 |
| MiniRL + R3 | ~0.73 | Stable | Maintains ~0.25 |
| MiniRL + R3 + length-norm | ~0.70 (degraded) | Stable | Maintains ~0.23 |
| MiniRL + R3 w/o train-infer-IS | **Collapse before 400 steps** | Unstable | Sharp drop |

Key observations from **Figure 1**:

- **MiniRL achieves the best performance** (~0.75 benchmark score), confirming that the basic policy gradient with IS correction preserves the first-order approximation most faithfully (Section 4.3)
- **Length normalization leads to suboptimal performance** (~0.72 vs. ~0.75) despite maintaining stability. This validates the theoretical claim that length normalization "invalidates the first-order approximation" and biases the objective (Section 4.3)
- **Removing training-inference IS correction causes rapid collapse** before 400 gradient steps, accompanied by a sharp entropy drop. This confirms IS weight is "an inherent component of the first-order approximation" (Section 4.3)
- **R3 provides no benefit in on-policy training** despite reducing training-inference KL divergence. The benchmark score is slightly lower (~0.73 vs. ~0.75), supporting the hypothesis that R3 "can alter the original target policy and introduce bias" when staleness is minimal (Section 4.3)

#### Training-Inference KL Divergence

The KL divergence metric reveals an important pattern: **R3 effectively reduces training-inference discrepancy** (the KL curve is lower for R3 variants in Figure 1), yet this does not translate to better performance in on-policy settings. This suggests that **reducing discrepancy alone is insufficient**—the technique must not introduce compensating biases.

#### Assessment

The on-policy experiments **convincingly support** the paper's core claim: techniques that preserve the first-order approximation (MiniRL with IS correction) outperform those that violate it (length normalization, no IS correction). The rapid collapse of variants without IS correction is particularly compelling evidence—this is not a subtle performance difference but a fundamental failure mode.

However, the R3 results reveal a **trade-off**: reducing training-inference discrepancy can introduce target policy bias. This nuance is critical for understanding when Routing Replay helps versus harms.

---

### 5.3 Off-Policy Training Results

Off-policy training introduces policy staleness by applying multiple gradient updates ($N > 1$) per global step. This is where stabilization techniques become essential. The paper tests three off-policiness levels with four methods:

1. **MiniRL (no clipping)**
2. **MiniRL + R2 (no clipping)**
3. **MiniRL + R2** (with clipping)
4. **MiniRL + R3** (with clipping)

#### Low Off-Policiness: Global Batch = 2× Mini-Batch (N=2)

**Figure 2** shows results up to **2,500 gradient steps**:

| Method | Peak Benchmark Score | Training Stability | Notes |
|--------|---------------------|-------------------|-------|
| MiniRL (no clipping) | **Collapse before 1,000 steps** | Unstable | KL divergence spikes |
| MiniRL + R2 (no clipping) | **Collapse before 1,500 steps** | Unstable | Clipping is necessary |
| MiniRL + R2 | ~0.78 (highest) | Stable | Best performer |
| MiniRL + R3 | ~0.76 | Stable | Slightly lower than R2 |

At low off-policiness, **R2 outperforms R3** (~0.78 vs. ~0.76), and both clipping and Routing Replay are necessary for stability. Methods without clipping collapse prematurely.

#### Medium Off-Policiness: Global Batch = 4× Mini-Batch (N=4)

**Figure 3** shows results up to **4,000 gradient steps**:

| Method | Peak Benchmark Score | Training Stability | Notes |
|--------|---------------------|-------------------|-------|
| MiniRL (no clipping) | **Collapse before 1,500 steps** | Unstable | — |
| MiniRL + R2 (no clipping) | **Collapse before 2,000 steps** | Unstable | — |
| MiniRL + R2 | ~0.77 | **Unstable after 2,500 steps** | R2 fails to sustain |
| MiniRL + R3 | ~0.78 (highest) | Stable | R3 surpasses R2 |

At medium off-policiness, the **crossover point emerges**: R2 can no longer sustain stable training beyond ~2,500 steps, while R3 maintains stability. R3's peak performance (~0.78) also exceeds R2's peak (~0.77).

#### High Off-Policiness: Global Batch = 8× Mini-Batch (N=8)

**Figure 4** shows results up to **5,000 gradient steps**:

| Method | Peak Benchmark Score | Training Stability | Notes |
|--------|---------------------|-------------------|-------|
| MiniRL + R2 (no clipping) | **Collapse before 2,000 steps** | Unstable | — |
| MiniRL + R2 | ~0.75 | **Unstable after 3,000 steps** | R2 fails earlier |
| MiniRL + R3 | ~0.77 (highest) | Stable | Only stable option |

At high off-policiness, **R2 fails to sustain stable training** entirely, collapsing after ~3,000 steps. R3 is the **only method that remains stable** throughout, achieving ~0.77 benchmark score.

#### Entropy and KL Divergence Patterns

Across all off-policy experiments (Figures 2–4), unstable methods show consistent warning signs:

- **Entropy drops sharply** before collapse (from ~0.35–0.40 to ~0.25 or lower)
- **KL divergence spikes** (from ~10⁻² to ~10⁻¹), indicating training-inference discrepancy explosion
- **Training reward plateaus or decreases** before benchmark scores drop

These diagnostics provide **actionable early warning signals** for practitioners monitoring RL training.

#### Why R2 vs. R3 Performance Flips

The paper provides a coherent explanation for the R2/R3 performance flip (Section 4.4):

- **R2** replays experts from the training engine rollout policy ($e^\pi_{\text{old}, t}$). For the **first mini-batch**, this equals the natural routing ($e^\pi_{\text{old}, t} = e^\pi_t$), so the target policy is unaltered (Table 1, Section 3.2). However, for **later mini-batches**, expert routing diverges as parameters update.
- **R3** replays experts from the inference engine rollout policy ($e^\mu_{\text{old}, t}$). This **always alters the target policy** because $e^\mu_{\text{old}, t} \neq e^\pi_t$, but it maintains consistency across all mini-batches.

The hypothesis: **When off-policiness is small**, R3's target policy bias outweighs its consistency benefit, so R2 wins. **When off-policiness is large**, accumulated staleness makes R2's inconsistent routing untenable, so R3's consistency becomes more valuable (Section 4.4).

#### Assessment

The off-policy experiments **strongly support** the paper's claims about when stabilization techniques are necessary:

1. **Both clipping and Routing Replay are necessary** (not optional) for off-policy training—omitting either causes collapse
2. **The R2/R3 choice is conditional** on off-policiness level, not universal
3. **Diagnostic metrics (KL, entropy) reliably predict collapse** before benchmark scores degrade

The systematic manipulation of off-policiness ($N = 2, 4, 8$) is particularly convincing because it reveals the **threshold behavior** where R2 fails and R3 becomes necessary. This level of granularity is rare in RL-for-LLMs research.

One limitation: the paper does not explore **asynchronous RL frameworks** where policy staleness can be even more severe (Section 2.4 mentions this as a source of staleness). The synchronous framework used here provides controlled experiments but may not capture all real-world deployment scenarios.

---

### 5.4 Cold-Start Initialization Results

The paper investigates whether **stable RL training can overcome initialization differences**—a question with significant practical implications for how the community allocates research effort.

#### Experimental Setup

Three cold-start models distilled from different frontier models:

1. **Qwen3-Max-Thinking-Preview**
2. **DeepSeek-R1-0528**
3. **gpt-oss-120b (high mode)**

Training configuration (Section 4.5):
- Model: Early-experimental small Qwen3Next MoE
- Global batch size: 4,096
- Mini-batch size: 2,048 ($N = 2$ gradient updates)
- Generation length: 65,536 tokens
- Recipe: MiniRL + R2

#### Quantitative Results (Figure 5)

After **600 gradient steps**, all three initializations converge to similar performance:

| Cold-Start Source | Final Benchmark Score (AIME25 & AIME24) | Response Length (Final) |
|------------------|----------------------------------------|------------------------|
| Qwen3-Max-Thinking-Preview | ~0.86 | ~31,000 tokens |
| DeepSeek-R1-0528 | ~0.86 | ~30,500 tokens |
| gpt-oss-120b (high) | ~0.86 | ~30,000 tokens |

The benchmark score curves are **nearly indistinguishable** after 400+ gradient steps, despite different starting points. Response lengths also converge to similar ranges (~30,000–31,000 tokens), indicating similar reasoning strategies emerge.

**Figure 10** (Appendix B) provides per-benchmark breakdowns showing the same convergence pattern on AIME25 and AIME24 individually.

#### Comparison to On-Policy/Off-Policy Results

The paper notes that comparing Figures 1–4 with Figure 5 reveals another pattern: **once training is stabilized, both on-policy and off-policy training achieve similar peak performance** (~0.75–0.78 on main experiments, ~0.86 on cold-start experiments with a different model). The training regime matters less than whether the regime is stable (Section 4.5).

#### Assessment

This finding **challenges prevailing assumptions** in the RL-for-LLMs community, which has invested heavily in perfecting cold-start initialization through supervised fine-tuning and distillation. The paper's evidence suggests:

- **Initialization differences diminish** given prolonged stable RL training
- **Stability is the prerequisite** that makes initialization quality moot
- Research focus should shift toward **RL algorithm robustness** rather than initialization specifics

However, important caveats apply:

1. This finding applies only to models that **can be stabilized**. Unstable training collapses regardless of initialization quality.
2. The experiments use **math reasoning tasks** with binary rewards. Results may differ for open-ended tasks with dense reward signals.
3. The cold-start experiments use a **different model** (Qwen3Next) than the main experiments (Qwen3-30B-A3B), so direct numerical comparison is not appropriate.

Despite these caveats, the convergence pattern is striking and warrants serious consideration for research prioritization.

---

### 5.5 Ablation Studies and Failure Cases

The paper includes several ablation studies that isolate the contribution of each stabilization technique:

#### Ablation 1: Importance Sampling Correction (Figure 1)

Removing training-inference IS correction causes **rapid collapse before 400 gradient steps**, with entropy dropping from ~0.30 to ~0.10. This is the most dramatic failure case in the paper, confirming IS correction is **non-negotiable** for the first-order approximation to hold.

#### Ablation 2: Length Normalization (Figure 1)

Adding length normalization maintains stability but reduces final benchmark score from ~0.75 to ~0.72. This demonstrates that **stability does not imply correctness**—a technique can prevent collapse while still biasing the objective away from the true sequence-level reward.

#### Ablation 3: Clipping Mechanism (Figures 2–4)

Removing clipping causes collapse in all off-policy settings, with timing dependent on off-policiness level:
- $N=2$: Collapse before 1,000 steps
- $N=4$: Collapse before 1,500 steps
- $N=8$: Collapse before 2,000 steps

Higher off-policiness accelerates collapse, confirming clipping's role in **controlling policy staleness**.

#### Ablation 4: Routing Replay Variants (Figures 2–4)

The R2 vs. R3 comparison is itself an ablation that reveals **conditional effectiveness**:
- R2 is sufficient (and superior) at $N=2$
- R3 becomes necessary at $N \geq 4$

This is a rare example of **threshold behavior** in RL stabilization—techniques are not universally good or bad but depend on training configuration.

#### Failure Mode: Training-Inference KL Spike

Across all unstable runs, the training-inference KL divergence spikes from ~10⁻² to ~10⁻¹ **before** benchmark scores degrade. This provides a **diagnostic signal** practitioners can monitor to detect impending collapse.

#### Robustness Check: Precision Stress Test

The FP8 inference / BF16 training configuration deliberately amplifies training-inference discrepancy. The fact that MiniRL with IS correction remains stable under this stress test (while variants without IS correction collapse) provides **stronger evidence** than BF16-only experiments would. The paper explicitly contrasts this with Ma et al. (2025), whose BF16-only setting may have masked the importance of IS correction (Section 4.3, footnote 5).

---

### 5.6 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

#### Strengths

1. **Scale and Duration**: Hundreds of thousands of GPU hours and 1,000+ gradient steps reveal long-term stability patterns that smaller experiments miss. The R2/R3 crossover only becomes apparent after sufficient training.

2. **Systematic Manipulation**: The controlled variation of off-policiness ($N = 1, 2, 4, 8$) isolates the effect of policy staleness, enabling causal claims about when techniques are necessary.

3. **Diagnostic Metrics**: Tracking KL divergence and entropy throughout training provides **mechanistic insight** into failure modes, not just final performance numbers.

4. **Stress Testing**: The FP8/BF16 precision mismatch is a deliberate stress test that strengthens confidence in algorithmic correctness claims.

5. **Cold-Start Comparison**: The three-way initialization comparison directly tests a widely-held assumption (initialization matters greatly) and provides evidence to the contrary.

#### Limitations

1. **Task Domain**: All experiments use math reasoning with binary rewards. Results may not generalize to open-ended generation, dialogue, or tasks with dense reward signals.

2. **Synchronous Framework**: The experiments use synchronous RL, which provides control but may not capture asynchronous deployment scenarios where policy staleness is more severe (Section 2.4).

3. **Single Model Family**: All experiments use Qwen-family MoE models. Dense models or other MoE architectures may exhibit different behavior.

4. **R2 On-Policy Inapplicability**: The paper notes R2 is inapplicable for on-policy training (Table 1), but does not explain why R3 was chosen over R2 for the cold-start experiments (which use $N=2$, where R2 should excel per Section 4.4).

5. **Compute Cost Transparency**: While the paper reports "5–6 GPU hours per gradient step," it does not specify the GPU type (A100, H100, etc.), making it difficult for others to estimate replication costs.

#### Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

**Yes, with high confidence.** The experimental design directly tests the paper's theoretical claims:

| Claim | Experimental Support | Confidence |
|-------|---------------------|------------|
| Token-level objective is first-order approximation | On-policy MiniRL outperforms biased variants (Fig. 1) | High |
| IS correction is necessary for approximation validity | Removing IS causes rapid collapse (Fig. 1) | High |
| Clipping controls policy staleness | No-clipping variants collapse in off-policy (Figs. 2–4) | High |
| R2 vs. R3 depends on off-policiness | Crossover at $N=4$ is clear (Figs. 2–4) | High |
| Stable training overcomes initialization differences | Three cold-starts converge (Fig. 5) | Moderate (single task domain) |

The most compelling evidence is the **ablation-driven collapse patterns**: when a technique that preserves the first-order approximation is removed, training fails predictably. This is stronger evidence than performance improvements alone, which could have alternative explanations.

The cold-start finding, while striking, has **moderate confidence** because it uses a different model and task configuration than the main experiments. Replication across model families and task domains would strengthen this claim.

---

### 5.7 Practical Takeaways from Experimental Results

For practitioners implementing RL for LLMs, the experiments yield actionable guidance:

1. **Always include training-inference IS correction**—omitting it causes rapid collapse regardless of other settings.

2. **Use length normalization cautiously**—it maintains stability but biases the objective, leading to suboptimal performance.

3. **For on-policy training**: MiniRL with IS correction is sufficient; Routing Replay provides no benefit and may introduce bias.

4. **For off-policy training**:
   - $N=2$ (low off-policiness): Use R2 with clipping
   - $N \geq 4$ (medium/high off-policiness): Use R3 with clipping
   - Never omit clipping in off-policy settings

5. **Monitor KL divergence and entropy** during training—spikes/drops precede collapse and provide early warning.

6. **Don't over-optimize cold-start initialization**—invest in RL stability instead, as initialization differences diminish with prolonged stable training.

These takeaways transform the paper from theoretical analysis into a **practical recipe book** for stable RL training at scale.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the assumptions, constraints, and unresolved questions in the paper's formulation and experimental validation. Understanding these limitations is essential for practitioners who wish to apply these insights beyond the specific conditions tested.

---

### 6.1 Theoretical Assumptions and Their Boundaries

**First-Order Approximation Requires Small Policy Deviations**

The core mathematical derivation hinges on the assumption that $\pi_\theta$ and $\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ are sufficiently close that second-order terms ($\delta_i \delta_j$) can be neglected (Section 2.3). This assumption breaks down when:

- Policy updates are too aggressive (large learning rates or insufficient clipping)
- Many gradient updates are applied between rollouts (high off-policiness)
- The model undergoes distributional shift during training

The paper acknowledges this implicitly by showing that **clipping becomes necessary** when off-policy updates are introduced (Section 4.4). However, the formulation does not provide a **quantitative threshold** for when the approximation fails—practitioners must discover this empirically through diagnostic metrics like KL divergence and entropy.

**Sequence-Level Reward Assumption**

The paper explicitly excludes value-based methods (e.g., PPO with a value model) from consideration:

> "We do not consider the value-based setting (e.g., PPO, Schulman et al. 2017), where policy optimization is steered by a value model that assigns scalar scores to each token in a response $y$. This is because we found it inherently difficult (if not impossible) to devise general and scalable approaches to obtaining reliable value models." (Section 2.1)

This is a significant limitation because:

- Many production RL systems for LLMs use actor-critic architectures with value models
- The formulation's insights about training-inference discrepancy and policy staleness may not directly transfer to value-based settings
- The claim that reliable value models are "inherently difficult" to obtain is asserted but not empirically demonstrated in this paper

**Binary Reward Setting**

All experiments use **binary rewards** (1 for correct math answers, 0 for incorrect) (Section 4.2). This simplifies the advantage estimation but limits generalizability:

- Dense reward signals (e.g., reward models that score response quality on a continuous scale) may behave differently
- The group-normalization advantage estimate $\hat{A}(x, y) = R(x, y) - \mathbb{E}_{y' \sim \mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|x)} [R(x, y')]$ becomes less meaningful when rewards are not binary
- Tasks with sparse rewards but long horizons (e.g., code generation with unit tests) may exhibit different stability patterns

---

### 6.2 Practical Trade-offs in Stabilization Techniques

**Routing Replay: Bias vs. Approximation Validity**

Section 3.2 explicitly identifies a fundamental trade-off in Routing Replay:

> "Alterating routed experts, while introducing bias into the optimization objective, also makes the first-order approximation—on which the altered token-level objective using $\pi^{R2}_\theta$ or $\pi^{R3}_\theta$ as the target policy relies—more likely to hold."

This creates a **stability-correctness tension**:

| Technique | Benefit | Cost |
|-----------|---------|------|
| R2 (Vanilla Routing Replay) | Preserves target policy in first mini-batch | Inconsistent expert routing across mini-batches under high off-policiness |
| R3 (Rollout Routing Replay) | Consistent expert replay across all mini-batches | Always alters target policy ($e^\mu_{\text{old}, t} \neq e^\pi_t$) |

The paper's recommendation (R2 for low off-policiness, R3 for high off-policiness) requires practitioners to **know their off-policiness level in advance** and switch techniques accordingly. This adds implementation complexity that the paper does not fully address.

**Length Normalization: Stability Without Correctness**

Section 4.3 reveals that length normalization maintains training stability but leads to **suboptimal performance** (~0.72 vs. ~0.75 benchmark score in Figure 1):

> "Adding length normalization leads to suboptimal performance, although training remains stable. This is as expected, since length normalization invalidates the first-order approximation to the true expected sequence-level reward, resulting in a biased token-level optimization objective."

This demonstrates that **stability does not imply correctness**—a technique can prevent collapse while still optimizing the wrong objective. Practitioners who prioritize benchmark performance over training smoothness should avoid length normalization, but the paper does not provide guidance on when this trade-off is acceptable.

**Clipping Thresholds: Untuned Hyperparameters**

The paper uses fixed clipping thresholds ($\varepsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.27$, $\varepsilon_{\text{low}} = 0.2$) without ablation or justification (Section 4.2). Footnote 3 in Section 4.1 acknowledges:

> "While there are alternative clipping strategies, such as clipping a whole response based on the ratio of sequence likelihood (Zheng et al., 2025), we found that the current clipping strategy has worked decently. Therefore, we leave the study of clipping or masking strategies for future work."

This leaves open questions:

- Are these thresholds optimal for all model sizes and task domains?
- Would adaptive clipping (adjusting thresholds based on training dynamics) improve performance?
- How sensitive is stability to small changes in these values?

**Advantage Estimation: Limited Exploration**

Similarly, the paper uses group-normalization for advantage estimation but notes:

> "Similarly, exploring better advantage estimates $\hat{A}(x, y)$ may also be helpful, but falls outside the scope of this work." (Section 4.1, footnote 3)

This is a notable gap because advantage estimation significantly affects policy gradient variance and convergence. The paper's insights about approximation validity apply to the objective structure, but **different advantage estimates may interact with stabilization techniques** in ways not explored here.

---

### 6.3 Empirical Scope and Generalizability

**Single Model Family**

All main experiments use **Qwen-family MoE models** (Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base and Qwen3Next). The paper does not test:

- Dense models (which lack expert routing, making Routing Replay inapplicable)
- MoE models from other architectures (which may have different routing mechanisms)
- Models of significantly different scales (the cold-start experiments use a "small Qwen3Next MoE model" but do not specify parameter count)

This limits confidence in whether the findings generalize beyond the Qwen architecture. In particular:

- **Routing Replay is MoE-specific**—dense models do not face the expert routing challenge
- The **training-inference discrepancy** may differ for models using different inference optimizations
- The **30B scale** may not reflect behavior at 7B or 70B+ scales

**Mathematical Reasoning Domain**

All experiments focus on **mathematical reasoning tasks** with verifiable answers (Section 4.2). This domain has specific characteristics:

- Binary rewards (correct/incorrect)
- Long generation lengths (up to 32,768 tokens, or 65,536 for cold-start experiments)
- Deterministic evaluation (ground truth answers exist)

The paper does not address whether the formulation applies to:

- **Open-ended generation** (e.g., creative writing, dialogue) where rewards are subjective
- **Multi-turn conversations** where rewards depend on conversation history
- **Tool-use tasks** where rewards depend on external system interactions
- **Code generation** where rewards come from unit test execution (which may be flaky or incomplete)

The cold-start finding (Section 4.5) that "initialization differences vanish given prolonged stable RL training" is particularly important to validate across domains, as different tasks may have different **optimization landscapes** that respond differently to initialization.

**Synchronous RL Framework**

All experiments use a **synchronous RL framework** where responses are sampled, then all gradient updates are applied before the next rollout (Section 4.2). However, Section 2.4 acknowledges:

> "In asynchronous RL frameworks, a single response can be generated sequentially by multiple model versions, which also introduces policy staleness."

The paper does not test asynchronous settings, which are common in large-scale production systems. This is a significant gap because:

- Asynchronous frameworks introduce **additional sources of policy staleness** beyond mini-batch splitting
- The **R2 vs. R3 trade-off** may shift in asynchronous settings where staleness is more severe
- The **clipping thresholds** tuned for synchronous training may not transfer

**Precision Stress Test: Artificial Amplification**

The paper deliberately uses **FP8 inference with BF16 training** to amplify training-inference discrepancy (Section 4.2). While this provides a rigorous stress test, it may not reflect typical deployment:

- Many production systems use the **same precision** for training and inference (e.g., BF16 for both)
- The **magnitude of discrepancy** in standard settings may be smaller, potentially reducing the importance of IS correction
- The paper's finding that removing IS correction causes "rapid training collapse" (Section 4.3) may be **exaggerated** in same-precision settings

Footnote 5 in Section 4.3 acknowledges this when contrasting with Ma et al. (2025):

> "Our FP8 inference setting imposes a more stringent stress test on algorithmic correctness, which cannot be adequately captured under their BF16 inference setting."

This suggests the paper's conclusions about IS correction necessity may be **upper bounds** on importance—practitioners using matched precision may observe less dramatic failure modes.

---

### 6.4 Computational and Scalability Constraints

**Prohibitive Compute Requirements**

The experiments consume **hundreds of thousands of GPU hours**, with an estimated **5–6 GPU hours per gradient step** (Section 4.2). This creates significant barriers:

| Aspect | Constraint |
|--------|------------|
| Replication cost | Most research groups cannot afford hundreds of thousands of GPU hours |
| Hyperparameter tuning | The paper does not ablate clipping thresholds, learning rates, or batch sizes due to cost |
| Domain expansion | Testing additional task domains (beyond math) would multiply compute requirements |
| Model scale variation | Testing multiple model sizes is computationally prohibitive |

The paper's findings are **empirically robust** precisely because of this scale, but the scale also limits how thoroughly the findings can be validated across conditions.

**Cold-Start Experiment Model Mismatch**

The cold-start initialization experiments (Section 4.5) use a **different model** (early-experimental small Qwen3Next MoE) than the main experiments (Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base). This introduces uncertainty:

- The convergence of cold-start initializations may be **model-specific**
- The Qwen3Next model uses a **generation length of 65,536 tokens** (vs. 32,768 for main experiments), which may affect stability
- The paper does not clarify whether Qwen3Next has the **same MoE architecture** as Qwen3-30B-A3B

This limits confidence in the generalizability of the cold-start finding. The claim that "initialization differences vanish given prolonged stable RL training" is striking but requires validation across model families.

**No Asynchronous or Distributed Training Analysis**

The paper's synchronous framework simplifies analysis but does not address:

- **Pipeline parallelism** effects on training-inference discrepancy
- **Data parallelism** synchronization overhead on policy staleness
- **Multi-node training** where network latency introduces additional staleness

Large-scale RL training typically involves all three, and the paper's stabilization recipes may require adjustment in these settings.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Future Directions

**Quantitative Thresholds for Approximation Validity**

The paper identifies that the first-order approximation holds when training-inference discrepancy and policy staleness are "minimized" (Abstract), but does not provide:

- **Numerical thresholds** for KL divergence that indicate safe vs. dangerous discrepancy levels
- **Maximum acceptable off-policiness** (how many gradient updates per rollout) before approximation breaks
- **Early stopping criteria** based on diagnostic metrics

Practitioners must currently discover these thresholds through trial and error, monitoring KL divergence and entropy for warning signs (Section 5.6 notes KL spikes precede collapse).

**Interaction Between Stabilization Techniques**

The paper tests combinations of techniques (IS correction + clipping + Routing Replay) but does not fully explore:

- Whether techniques are **additive** or have **diminishing returns**
- Whether certain techniques **interfere** with each other (e.g., does aggressive clipping reduce the benefit of Routing Replay?)
- The **minimum sufficient set** of techniques for a given training configuration

For example, if R3 already reduces training-inference discrepancy, does IS correction remain equally important? The on-policy experiments suggest yes (Section 4.3), but this interaction is not deeply analyzed.

**Long-Term Training Dynamics**

The longest experiments run for **~5,000 gradient steps** (Figure 4, high off-policiness). The paper claims that "prolonged optimization consistently yields comparable final performance" (Abstract), but:

- What constitutes "prolonged"? Is 5,000 steps sufficient, or do differences emerge at 50,000 steps?
- Do stabilization techniques that work at 5,000 steps **continue to work** at 50,000 steps?
- Is there a **performance ceiling** that RL training cannot exceed, regardless of stability?

The cold-start experiments run for only **600 gradient steps** (Figure 5), which may be insufficient to observe long-term divergence.

**Applicability to Non-MoE Models**

The paper's formulation applies to all LLMs, but the **empirical validation focuses on MoE models**. For dense models:

- Routing Replay is **inapplicable** (no expert routing)
- Training-inference discrepancy may be **smaller** (fewer kernel differences)
- Policy staleness remains relevant, but the **relative importance** of clipping vs. IS correction may shift

The paper does not provide dense model experiments to confirm whether the formulation's predictions hold.

**Value Model Integration**

The paper excludes value-based methods but acknowledges their prevalence. Open questions include:

- Can the **first-order approximation framework** extend to actor-critic settings?
- Does training-inference discrepancy affect **value model training** differently than policy training?
- Would a **value model trained with IS correction** improve stability?

These questions are explicitly left for future work but represent significant gaps in the paper's scope.

| Category | Limitation | Impact |
|----------|------------|--------|
| **Theoretical** | First-order approximation requires small policy deviations | Limits applicability to aggressive training regimes |

### 6.7 Fair Assessment

Despite these limitations, the paper makes **substantial contributions** within its scope:

1. **Theoretical clarity**: The first-order approximation formulation provides a principled lens for understanding RL stability, even if the quantitative boundaries remain unspecified.

2. **Empirical rigor**: The large-scale experiments (hundreds of thousands of GPU hours) provide confidence that findings are not artifacts of small-scale training.

3. **Actionable guidance**: The R2 vs. R3 conditional recommendation, while adding complexity, gives practitioners concrete decision criteria based on off-policiness level.

4. **Diagnostic metrics**: The KL divergence and entropy tracking provides early warning signals that are valuable regardless of model architecture or task domain.

The limitations primarily reflect **scope boundaries** rather than fundamental flaws. The paper explicitly acknowledges many of these constraints (e.g., excluding value-based methods, leaving clipping threshold tuning for future work). The key takeaway for practitioners is:

> **Apply these insights within the tested conditions** (MoE models, sequence-level rewards, synchronous training), and **validate empirically** when extending to new domains or architectures.

The paper provides a **foundation** for stable RL training, not a complete solution. Future work should address the open questions identified above, particularly around asynchronous training, dense models, and non-math task domains.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper's contributions extend beyond immediate algorithmic recommendations—they reshape how the research community conceptualizes, designs, and scales reinforcement learning for large language models. This section examines the broader impact on the field, identifies concrete research directions this work enables, outlines practical deployment scenarios, and provides actionable integration guidance for practitioners.

---

### 7.1 Landscape Impact: From Empirical Tuning to Principled Engineering

**Reframing RL Algorithm Design**

Prior to this work, RL-for-LLMs research operated largely through **empirical iteration**: practitioners observed that certain techniques (clipping, importance sampling, Routing Replay) improved stability, but lacked a unified explanation for why they worked or when they would fail. This paper transforms that paradigm by establishing a **constraint-satisfaction framework** for algorithm design:

$$
\text{Stable RL} \iff \text{First-order approximation validity preserved}
$$

Where approximation validity requires jointly minimizing:
1. **Training-inference discrepancy** (infrastructure-level error)
2. **Policy staleness** (algorithm-level error)

This reframing has three immediate consequences for the field:

| Previous Mindset | New Mindset Enabled by This Work |
|-----------------|----------------------------------|
| "Add more stabilization techniques until training doesn't collapse" | "Apply the minimum technique necessary to preserve approximation validity" |
| "Length normalization helps stability, so use it" | "Length normalization biases the objective—avoid unless stability is the only concern" (Section 4.3, Figure 1) |
| "Routing Replay is good for MoE models" | "R2 for low off-policiness, R3 for high off-policiness" (Section 4.4, Figures 2–4) |

**Shifting Research Priorities**

The cold-start initialization finding (Section 4.5, Figure 5) carries profound implications for how the community allocates research effort:

> "Once training is stabilized, prolonged optimization consistently yields comparable final performance regardless of cold-start initialization." (Abstract)

This suggests a **priority inversion** for the field:

| Previous Focus Area | Recommended Focus Area |
|--------------------|------------------------|
| Improving cold-start data quality through distillation | Improving RL algorithm stability and duration |
| Initialization-specific hyperparameter tuning | Universal stabilization techniques |
| Supervised fine-tuning recipes | Extended RL training infrastructure |

The logic is straightforward: if initialization differences vanish after ~400–600 gradient steps of stable training (Figure 5), then investing disproportionate effort in perfecting initialization yields **diminishing returns**. The bottleneck shifts from "where you start" to "how long you can train stably."

**Establishing Diagnostic Standards**

The paper introduces two diagnostic metrics that should become **standard monitoring tools** for RL training:

1. **Training-inference KL divergence**: 
   $$
   D_{\text{KL}}[\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}} \| \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}] = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D, y_t \sim \mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|x, y_{&lt;t})} \left[ \log \frac{\mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t|x, y_{&lt;t})} \right]
   $$
   A sharp increase from ~10⁻² to ~10⁻¹ precedes training collapse (Section 4.2, Figures 1–4).

2. **Token-level entropy**:
   $$
   H[\pi_\theta] \approx \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D, y_{&lt;t} \sim \mu_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|x)} \left[ -\sum_{w \in V} \pi_\theta(w|x, y_{&lt;t}) \log \pi_\theta(w|x, y_{&lt;t}) \right]
   $$
   A sharp drop indicates policy collapse and loss of exploration capacity (Section 4.2).

These metrics provide **early warning signals** that allow practitioners to intervene before benchmark scores degrade—transforming RL training from "wait and see if it collapses" to "monitor and prevent collapse."

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

This paper's formulation opens multiple concrete research avenues. Below, I categorize them by proximity to the current work and estimated impact.

#### 7.2.1 Immediate Extensions (High Confidence, Low Risk)

**1. Quantitative Thresholds for Approximation Validity**

The paper identifies that the first-order approximation holds when training-inference discrepancy and policy staleness are "minimized" (Abstract), but does not provide numerical thresholds. Follow-up work should establish:

- **KL divergence thresholds**: What maximum $D_{\text{KL}}$ value indicates safe training? Is 10⁻² a universal boundary, or does it vary by model scale?
- **Maximum off-policiness**: How many gradient updates per rollout ($N$) can a model tolerate before approximation breaks? The paper tests $N = 1, 2, 4, 8$—what about $N = 16, 32$?
- **Entropy floor**: What minimum entropy level indicates impending collapse? Can adaptive clipping prevent entropy from dropping below this threshold?

**Expected impact**: Practitioners could implement **automatic early stopping** or **adaptive hyperparameter adjustment** based on these thresholds, reducing wasted compute on doomed training runs.

**2. Clipping Threshold Ablation**

The paper uses fixed clipping thresholds ($\varepsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.27$, $\varepsilon_{\text{low}} = 0.2$) without ablation (Section 4.2, footnote 3). Follow-up work should investigate:

- Are these thresholds optimal for all model sizes (7B vs. 30B vs. 70B+)?
- Would **adaptive clipping** (adjusting thresholds based on observed KL divergence or entropy) improve performance?
- How sensitive is stability to small perturbations (e.g., $\varepsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.25$ vs. $0.30$)?

**Expected impact**: Adaptive clipping could extend stable training duration by dynamically responding to training dynamics rather than using static thresholds.

**3. Dense Model Validation**

All empirical validation uses **MoE models** (Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base and Qwen3Next). The formulation applies to dense models, but this remains untested. Follow-up work should:

- Replicate the on-policy/off-policy experiments on dense models (e.g., Llama-3-70B)
- Verify whether training-inference discrepancy has similar magnitude without expert routing
- Confirm whether IS correction remains equally critical without Routing Replay

**Expected impact**: Extends the paper's findings to the broader population of dense models, increasing generalizability.

#### 7.2.2 Medium-Term Extensions (Moderate Confidence, Medium Risk)

**4. Asynchronous RL Frameworks**

The paper uses a **synchronous RL framework** for controlled experiments (Section 4.2), but acknowledges that asynchronous frameworks introduce additional policy staleness:

> "In asynchronous RL frameworks, a single response can be generated sequentially by multiple model versions, which also introduces policy staleness." (Section 2.4)

Follow-up work should:

- Test whether the R2 vs. R3 trade-off shifts in asynchronous settings (where staleness is more severe)
- Determine if clipping thresholds need adjustment for asynchronous training
- Investigate whether **stale gradient correction** techniques from distributed optimization apply to RL staleness

**Expected impact**: Asynchronous training is common in production systems—validating these insights in that setting would significantly increase practical utility.

**5. Value-Based Method Integration**

The paper explicitly excludes value-based methods (e.g., PPO with a value model) from consideration (Section 2.1):

> "We do not consider the value-based setting... This is because we found it inherently difficult (if not impossible) to devise general and scalable approaches to obtaining reliable value models."

This leaves open whether the first-order approximation framework extends to actor-critic architectures. Follow-up work should:

- Derive an analogous approximation for value-based objectives
- Test whether training-inference discrepancy affects value model training differently than policy training
- Investigate whether **value models trained with IS correction** improve stability

**Expected impact**: Many production RL systems use actor-critic architectures—extending these insights would broaden applicability.

**6. Non-Math Task Domains**

All experiments use **mathematical reasoning with binary rewards** (Section 4.2). Follow-up work should validate whether the findings generalize to:

- **Open-ended generation** (creative writing, dialogue) with subjective rewards
- **Code generation** with unit test execution rewards (which may be flaky)
- **Multi-turn conversations** where rewards depend on conversation history
- **Tool-use tasks** where rewards depend on external system interactions

**Expected impact**: Confirms whether the formulation is domain-agnostic or specific to verifiable reasoning tasks.

#### 7.2.3 Long-Term Extensions (Lower Confidence, Higher Risk)

**7. Theoretical Bounds on Approximation Error**

The paper uses a first-order Taylor expansion but does not bound the neglected second-order terms ($O(\delta^2)$). Follow-up theoretical work could:

- Derive **explicit error bounds** for the approximation as a function of $\delta_t$
- Establish conditions under which second-order terms become non-negligible
- Connect approximation error to observable metrics (KL divergence, entropy)

**Expected impact**: Provides rigorous guarantees rather than empirical observations, strengthening the theoretical foundation.

**8. Alternative Advantage Estimation**

The paper uses group-normalization for advantage estimation but notes this is unexplored:

> "Exploring better advantage estimates $\hat{A}(x, y)$ may also be helpful, but falls outside the scope of this work." (Section 4.1, footnote 3)

Follow-up work could investigate:

- **Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE)** adapted for sequence-level rewards
- **Baseline subtraction** methods that reduce variance without biasing the objective
- Whether advantage estimation interacts with stabilization techniques (e.g., does better advantage estimation reduce the need for aggressive clipping?)

**Expected impact**: Could improve sample efficiency and reduce variance without compromising approximation validity.

**9. Automatic Technique Selection**

Given the conditional guidance (R2 for low off-policiness, R3 for high off-policiness), follow-up work could develop:

- **Automated detection** of off-policiness level during training
- **Dynamic switching** between R2 and R3 based on observed staleness
- **Meta-optimization** that selects stabilization techniques based on diagnostic metrics

**Expected impact**: Reduces practitioner burden by automating technique selection based on training dynamics.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The insights from this paper apply most directly to scenarios where **stable, large-scale RL training** is critical. Below, I outline specific use cases and their alignment with the paper's findings.

#### 7.3.1 High-Alignment Use Cases

**1. Reasoning Model Training (Math, Code, Science)**

The paper's experiments focus on mathematical reasoning, making this the **most directly applicable domain**:

- **Why it fits**: Binary rewards (correct/incorrect) match the experimental setup; long generation lengths (32,768–65,536 tokens) match the paper's configuration
- **Recommended recipe**: MiniRL + R2 for $N \leq 2$, MiniRL + R3 for $N \geq 4$, with IS correction always enabled
- **Expected benefit**: Stable training to convergence without collapse, enabling prolonged optimization that overcomes initialization differences

**2. MoE Model RL Fine-Tuning**

Any organization training **Mixture-of-Experts models** with RL should adopt these practices:

- **Why it fits**: Expert routing amplifies both training-inference discrepancy and policy staleness (Section 3.1), making Routing Replay essential
- **Recommended recipe**: R3 for large-scale training (high off-policiness), R2 for resource-constrained settings (low off-policiness)
- **Expected benefit**: Prevents expert routing inconsistency from undermining the first-order approximation

**3. Production RL Systems with Separate Training/Inference Engines**

Organizations using **different engines for training and inference** (e.g., Megatron for training, vLLM for inference) should prioritize these insights:

- **Why it fits**: Training-inference discrepancy is inherent in this architecture (Section 2.4)
- **Recommended recipe**: Always enable IS correction for training-inference discrepancy; monitor KL divergence as an early warning signal
- **Expected benefit**: Prevents numerical inconsistency from invalidating the optimization objective

#### 7.3.2 Moderate-Alignment Use Cases

**4. Dense Model RL Training**

Organizations training **dense models** (non-MoE) can apply subset of these insights:

- **Why it fits partially**: Training-inference discrepancy and policy staleness still apply, but Routing Replay is inapplicable
- **Recommended recipe**: MiniRL with IS correction and clipping; skip Routing Replay
- **Expected benefit**: Improved stability from IS correction and clipping, though magnitude may differ from MoE results

**5. Actor-Critic Systems (PPO with Value Model)**

Organizations using **value-based methods** can apply conceptual insights with caution:

- **Why it fits partially**: Policy staleness and training-inference discrepancy still affect the policy component
- **Recommended recipe**: Apply IS correction and clipping to policy updates; value model training requires separate validation
- **Expected benefit**: Improved policy stability, though value model may introduce additional failure modes

#### 7.3.3 Low-Alignment Use Cases

**6. Online RL with Continuous Interaction**

Systems that interleave **rollout and update steps continuously** (e.g., dialogue agents learning from user feedback):

- **Why it fits poorly**: Policy staleness dynamics differ significantly from batched RL; rewards may be dense and subjective
- **Recommended approach**: Treat this paper's findings as hypotheses to validate empirically, not prescriptive recipes
- **Expected benefit**: Conceptual framework for understanding instability, but specific techniques may require adaptation

**7. Multi-Agent RL Settings**

Scenarios where **multiple policies interact** (e.g., debate systems, negotiation agents):

- **Why it fits poorly**: Non-stationarity from other agents' learning introduces additional instability sources
- **Recommended approach**: Apply stabilization techniques but expect different threshold behavior
- **Expected benefit**: Diagnostic metrics (KL, entropy) remain useful, but technique effectiveness may vary

**Cost-saving strategies**:
- Use **BF16 for both training and inference** to reduce training-inference discrepancy (though this weakens the stress test)
- Start with **on-policy experiments** ($N = 1$) to validate baseline stability before scaling to off-policy
- Use **smaller models** (7B–13B) for initial validation, then scale to 30B+ for final confirmation

### 7.5 Summary: The Path Forward

This paper transforms RL-for-LLMs from an empirical craft into a **principled engineering discipline**. The key takeaways for the field are:

1. **Stability is the bottleneck, not initialization**. Once training is stabilized, prolonged optimization overcomes initialization differences (Section 4.5). Research effort should shift accordingly.

2. **Every stabilization technique serves a purpose**. IS correction addresses training-inference discrepancy; clipping controls policy staleness; Routing Replay handles MoE-specific routing instability. Use techniques intentionally, not reflexively.

3. **Diagnostic metrics enable proactive intervention**. KL divergence and entropy provide early warning signals before benchmark scores degrade. Monitor these continuously.

4. **Conditional guidance beats universal recipes**. R2 vs. R3 depends on off-policiness level; clipping necessity depends on whether training is on-policy or off-policy. Context matters.

5. **Scale reveals truth**. Many findings (R2/R3 crossover, cold-start convergence) only become apparent at 1,000+ gradient steps. Small-scale experiments may mislead.

For researchers, the open questions in Section 7.2 provide a **research agenda** for the next 1–3 years. For practitioners, the integration guidance in Section 7.4 provides a **deployment checklist** for stable RL training. For the field collectively, this work establishes a **foundation** upon which more sophisticated RL methods can be built—with clarity about what makes them stable and why.

The ultimate goal is not just stable training, but **reliable scaling**: the confidence that investing more compute in RL will yield predictable performance improvements. This paper takes a significant step toward that goal by explaining what breaks stability and how to prevent it. The path forward requires validating these insights across model families, task domains, and training frameworks—but the destination is now visible.
