## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the "evaluation trilemma" in Large Multimodal Models (LMMs), where achieving wide coverage, low cost, and zero contamination simultaneously is notoriously difficult. The authors introduce `LMMS-EVAL`, a unified suite standardizing evaluation across over 50 tasks and 10+ models, alongside `LMMS-EVAL LITE` for efficient testing and `LIVEBENCH`, which leverages dynamic news data to prevent training data contamination. These contributions provide practical trade-offs to ensure transparent, reproducible, and reliable benchmarking of LMMs in both static and dynamic environments.

## 2. Context and Motivation

The rapid evolution of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) — systems capable of processing both visual and textual inputs, such as `LLaVA`, `Qwen-VL`, and `GPT-4V` — has outpaced the development of reliable evaluation infrastructure. While model capabilities are expanding, the methods used to measure them remain fragmented, opaque, and increasingly susceptible to data leakage. This section details the specific gaps in the current landscape, the theoretical and practical importance of solving them, and how existing solutions fall short.

### 2.1 The Fragmentation of Evaluation Protocols

Prior to this work, the LMM community lacked a unified standard for benchmarking. Unlike the Large Language Model (LLM) field, which converged around frameworks like `LM-EVAL-HARNESS` (Gao et al., 2023), LMM evaluation was characterized by **custom, model-specific pipelines**.

*   **Inefficiency:** Researchers typically wrote unique inference scripts for each model-dataset pair. As noted in Section 2.1, evaluating a single model across ten datasets required manually launching ten different scripts, each handling data preprocessing, inference, and metric calculation differently.
*   **Non-Comparable Scores:** Custom scripts often implemented different scoring logic for the same task. For example:
    *   **PPL-based:** Some works (e.g., Li et al., 2023c) count an answer correct if the ground truth has the lowest perplexity among choices.
    *   **Generation-based:** Others (e.g., Liu et al., 2023a) count an answer correct only if the generated text exactly matches the option letter.
    
    This inconsistency makes it impossible to determine if performance differences stem from model architecture or evaluation quirks. The authors argue that **"there is no best setup but one needs to fix one when comparing results across different models"** (Section 2.1). Without a fixed protocol, reproducibility is hindered, and progress becomes difficult to verify.

### 2.2 The Evaluation Trilemma

The core theoretical challenge identified in this paper is the **"Evaluation Trilemma,"** visualized in Figure 1. The authors posit that it is effectively impossible to simultaneously achieve the following three goals in a single benchmark:

1.  **Wide Coverage:** Encompassing diverse tasks (e.g., OCR, reasoning, captioning) to assess general capability.
2.  **Low Cost:** Minimizing computational resources and time required for evaluation.
3.  **Zero Contamination:** Ensuring test data was never seen during model training.

**Why is this a trilemma?**
*   **Wide Coverage + Low Cost:** This typically implies using static, publicly available datasets (e.g., `VQAv2`, `MMBench`). However, because these datasets are fixed and public, they are highly likely to be included in the massive web-scraped training corpora of modern LMMs, violating **Zero Contamination**.
*   **Wide Coverage + Zero Contamination:** This implies creating new, private, or dynamic data constantly. While robust, gathering and validating this data (e.g., via human annotators or live web crawling) is expensive and slow, violating **Low Cost**.
*   **Low Cost + Zero Contamination:** This implies using very small, fresh datasets. However, small datasets often lack the diversity needed to assess multifaceted model capabilities, violating **Wide Coverage**.

The paper notes that existing solutions often pick two sides but fail on the third. For instance, the **LMSys Chatbot Arena** (Chiang et al., 2024) offers robust, non-contaminated evaluation through real user interactions but is **"expensive to gather tens of thousands of human preferences"** (Section 1). Conversely, the **Hugging Face OpenLLM leaderboard** is low-cost and wide-coverage but **"prone to overfitting and contamination"** (Section 1).

### 2.3 The Crisis of Data Contamination

A critical motivation for this work is the prevalence of **data contamination**, where benchmark test sets inadvertently appear in a model's training data. This leads to inflated scores that do not reflect true generalization ability.

The authors conducted an empirical analysis to quantify this overlap (Section 4.1). They developed tools to measure both text and image overlap between training corpora (specifically `LLaVA-NeXT`) and popular benchmarks. The findings were stark:
*   **Image Overlap:** Some benchmarks showed massive overlap. For example, `ChartQA` had a **68.64%** image overlap with training data, and `VQAv2` had **46.21%** (Table 4 in Appendix C).
*   **Text Overlap:** Even when images differed, question structures often overlapped. `AI2D` showed a **25.97%** text overlap.

The paper identifies three specific types of contamination (Figure 5):
1.  **Duplicate Images:** Identical images appearing in both training and test sets.
2.  **Similar Images:** Visually similar images that allow the model to rely on memorized visual features rather than reasoning.
3.  **Similar Questions:** Recurring question structures that allow the model to memorize answer patterns rather than understand the content.

This contamination creates a false sense of progress. The authors observe that while open-source models often outperform commercial ones on static benchmarks, they **"fall short in real user experience"** (Section 4.2). `LIVEBENCH` results (Table 3) show that commercial models like `GPT-4o` maintain a significant lead on fresh, dynamic data, suggesting static benchmarks fail to capture the true capability gap.

### 2.4 Positioning Relative to Existing Work

This paper positions itself not as a solver of the trilemma, but as a provider of tools to **navigate the trade-offs** transparently.

| Approach | Example | Strength | Weakness | This Paper's Stance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Custom Scripts** | Model-specific repos | Tailored to model | Non-reproducible, inefficient | **`LMMS-EVAL`** standardizes this into a unified framework. |
| **Static Benchmarks** | `MMBench`, `MMMU` | Low cost, Wide coverage | High contamination risk | **`LMMS-EVAL`** includes these but flags contamination risks. |
| **Human Arenas** | `Chatbot Arena` | Zero contamination | High cost, noisy | **`LIVEBENCH`** automates fresh data collection to reduce cost. |
| **Coreset Methods** | `tinybenchmarks` | Low cost | May lose coverage | **`LMMS-EVAL LITE`** uses k-Center clustering to preserve correlation while reducing size. |

By acknowledging that **"we do not break this impossible triangle"** (Section 1), the authors set realistic expectations. Their contribution is infrastructural: providing a standardized suite (`LMMS-EVAL`) for comprehensive auditing, a pruned version (`LITE`) for efficient development cycles, and a dynamic benchmark (`LIVEBENCH`) for contamination-free testing. This allows researchers to select the appropriate tool based on their current priority (e.g., speed during training vs. accuracy during final reporting).

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents an infrastructural contribution rather than a novel model architecture — it builds a comprehensive evaluation ecosystem for Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) consisting of three interconnected systems. The solution takes the shape of a layered toolkit: a standardized full-scale benchmark suite for comprehensive auditing, a pruned efficient version for rapid iteration, and a dynamic live benchmark for contamination-free assessment.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The *system* being built is a unified evaluation framework that standardizes how multimodal AI models are tested, measured, and compared across dozens of different tasks and datasets. It solves the problem of fragmented, unreliable, and potentially contaminated evaluations by providing three complementary tools: a comprehensive benchmark suite (`LMMS-EVAL`), an efficient pruned version (`LMMS-EVAL LITE`), and a dynamically updated benchmark using fresh web data (`LIVEBENCH`).

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The overall architecture consists of three major components that operate at different stages of the model development lifecycle:

1.  **`LMMS-EVAL` (Full Suite)**: This is the foundational layer — a standardized evaluation harness that accepts any LMM model and runs it across 50+ benchmark datasets with unified preprocessing, inference, and scoring pipelines. It outputs detailed logs containing model generations, score breakdowns, and evaluation configurations for reproducibility.

2.  **`LMMS-EVAL LITE` (Pruned Suite)**: This component sits atop the full suite and applies coreset selection algorithms to identify representative subsets of each benchmark dataset. It takes the full datasets as input, computes embeddings using CLIP (for images) and BGE-M3 (for text), applies k-Center clustering to select representative samples, and outputs a reduced benchmark set that maintains correlation with full-set results while reducing evaluation time.

3.  **`LIVEBENCH` (Dynamic Benchmark)**: This operates as a separate, continuously updated pipeline that crawls 60+ news and forum websites, extracts multimodal content using OCR and image analysis, generates questions across four cognitive categories based on Bloom's Taxonomy, validates questions through checker models, and produces fresh test data that cannot exist in any model's training corpus.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

-   **First**, we explain the `LMMS-EVAL` standardization framework because it forms the foundation upon which the LITE version is built — understanding the full suite is necessary to appreciate what gets pruned.
-   **Second**, we detail the `LMMS-EVAL LITE` coreset selection mechanism, including the mathematical formulation of the k-Center problem and the embedding strategies, since this represents the core algorithmic contribution for reducing evaluation cost.
-   **Third**, we walk through the `LIVEBENCH` data collection and QA generation pipeline step-by-step, as this is the most complex component with multiple model-involved stages.
-   **Fourth**, we explain the contamination detection methodology (both text and image overlap measurement) because this motivates why `LIVEBENCH` is necessary and validates the contamination claims.
-   **Fifth**, we cover the score aggregation and evaluation metrics across all three systems to show how results are made comparable and interpretable.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is an infrastructural paper that builds evaluation tooling rather than proposing a new model architecture, and its core idea is that reliable LMM evaluation requires addressing three interconnected challenges — standardization, efficiency, and contamination — through complementary systems rather than a single silver-bullet solution.

#### 3.4.1 LMMS-EVAL: Standardized Evaluation Framework

**System Purpose and Design Philosophy**

The `LMMS-EVAL` framework addresses the fragmentation problem identified in Section 2.1 by providing a unified interface that allows researchers to evaluate multiple models across multiple datasets with a single command, following the design pattern of `LM-EVAL-HARNESS` (Gao et al., 2023) from the language model evaluation community. The key design choice here is to prioritize standardization over customization — the authors explicitly state that "there is no best setup but one needs to fix one when comparing results across different models" (Section 2.1), meaning they accept that any single evaluation protocol will have limitations but argue that consistency matters more than perfection for fair comparison.

**Data Preprocessing and Unified Interface**

The framework handles all data preprocessing internally, ensuring that a single data source is used across different models rather than allowing each research group to implement their own data loading and preprocessing logic. This eliminates a major source of variance where different teams might preprocess the same dataset differently — for example, some might resize images to 224×224 pixels while others use 336×336, or some might truncate text at 512 tokens while others use 1024. The unified interface also facilitates the integration of new models and datasets, meaning that adding support for a new LMM requires implementing only the model's inference logic while reusing all the data handling, metric calculation, and logging infrastructure from the framework.

**Evaluation Setup Standardization**

The framework explicitly supports different evaluation setups to accommodate various model types and research conventions. For instruction-tuned models, the system respects the model's chat template rather than applying a generic prompt format, which is critical because different models (e.g., `LLaVA`, `Qwen-VL`, `InstructBLIP`) were trained with different conversational formats and perform poorly when evaluated with mismatched templates. The framework logs detailed evaluation setups including the exact prompts used, generation parameters (temperature, max tokens, etc.), and score breakdowns per dataset, enabling other researchers to reproduce results exactly or understand why scores might differ from previously reported numbers.

**Scale and Coverage**

The full `LMMS-EVAL` suite encompasses over 50 tasks across diverse domains including visual question answering (`VQAv2`, `GQA`), document understanding (`DocVQA`, `ChartQA`), mathematical reasoning (`MathVista`), science questions (`ScienceQA`, `AI2D`), captioning (`COCO`, `NoCaps`), and hallucination detection (`POPE`, `HallusionBench`). Table 1 presents partial results showing 21 model variants evaluated, ranging from 0.5B parameter models (`LLaVA-OV-0.5B`) to 110B parameter models (`LLaVA-NeXT-110B`), demonstrating the framework's ability to handle models across three orders of magnitude in size. The framework supports both open-sourced models (evaluated locally) and closed-source API models (e.g., `GPT-4V`, `Gemini`, `Claude`), with different parallelization strategies — data parallelism for models under 72B parameters and pipeline parallelism (Huang et al., 2019) for larger models that cannot fit on single GPUs.

#### 3.4.2 LMMS-EVAL LITE: Coreset Selection for Efficient Evaluation

**Problem Formulation**

The `LMMS-EVAL LITE` component addresses the "low cost" vertex of the evaluation trilemma by identifying representative subsets of benchmark datasets that preserve the ranking and absolute scores of the full evaluation while requiring significantly less computation time. The authors formalize this as an optimization problem where given a benchmark dataset represented as $\mathcal{D} = \{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$ with scoring function $S$, and a model $f$ that produces responses $\hat{y}_i = f(x_i)$, the goal is to select a subset $\mathcal{V} \in \mathcal{D}$ such that:

$$
\min_{\mathcal{V}:|\mathcal{V}| \leq |\mathcal{D}|} \left| \frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}|} \sum_{i=1}^{|\mathcal{D}|} S(y_i, \hat{y}_i) - \frac{1}{|\mathcal{V}|} \sum_{i=1}^{|\mathcal{V}|} S(y_i, \hat{y}_i) \right|
$$

This objective function seeks to minimize the absolute difference between the average score on the full dataset and the average score on the selected subset. The paper notes that this objective is equivalent to solving the **k-Center problem** (Sener and Savarese, 2018), which seeks to identify a subset of data points that represent the full set by minimizing the maximum distance from any point to its nearest cluster center.

**Why k-Center Clustering**

The k-Center formulation is chosen over alternative coreset selection methods because it directly optimizes for representativeness — the selected points should cover the diversity of the full dataset rather than just sampling high-confidence or easy examples. The paper compares against two alternative methods in Table 2: **k-means clustering** (Lloyd, 1982), which minimizes within-cluster variance but may not capture boundary cases, and **Quire** (Huang et al., 2010), an active learning method that selects informative and representative examples but requires iterative querying. The k-Center approach achieves correlation scores of 0.91–0.99 across multiple benchmarks (Table 2), outperforming k-means on most datasets while being more efficient than Quire.

**Computational Complexity and Greedy Solution**

Solving the k-Center problem exactly is NP-hard (Cook, 1997), meaning the computational cost grows exponentially with dataset size, which would defeat the purpose of creating an efficient evaluation suite. The authors instead use a greedy algorithm that achieves a 2-OPT solution efficiently, meaning the solution is guaranteed to be within a factor of 2 of the optimal solution. Algorithm 1 in Appendix D.4 details this greedy approach: start by randomly selecting one point as the initial center, then iteratively select the point that has the maximum distance to its nearest existing center, repeating until $k$ centers are identified. This greedy approach has polynomial time complexity and provides theoretical guarantees on solution quality.

**Embedding Strategy for Multimodal Data**

A critical design choice in the coreset selection is how to represent each data point $(x_i, y_i)$ in a vector space where distances can be computed. The paper uses a concatenation of two embedding types: **CLIP** (Radford et al., 2021) for image embeddings and **BGE-M3** (Chen et al., 2024a) for text embeddings. This choice is significant because CLIP provides vision-language aligned embeddings trained on 400M image-text pairs, capturing semantic similarity between images and their associated questions, while BGE-M3 is a multi-lingual, multi-functionality text embedding model that captures question semantics effectively. The authors also experimented with training a custom `LLaVA-Qwen 1.8B` model to embed image-text pairs jointly (Appendix D.1), but found that the CLIP+BGE combination achieved comparable or better correlation scores while being more computationally efficient since it doesn't require training a custom embedding model.

**Dataset Selection Criteria**

The `LMMS-EVAL LITE` suite selects 15 datasets across different task domains to maintain broad coverage while reducing total evaluation cost. For datasets with over 1500 data points, the coreset selection algorithm is applied to choose representative subsets — for example, `ChartQA` is reduced from 2500 to 400 samples, `DocVQA` from 5349 to 400, and `Flickr30k` from 31784 to 400 (Table 6 in Appendix D.2). However, for `MME`, the full version is retained because the correlation between original and lite set scores was found to be low, suggesting that the benchmark's structure doesn't lend itself well to coreset selection without losing evaluation fidelity. The total dataset size is reduced from 90,223 instances in the full suite to 9,134 instances in the lite version — approximately a 10× reduction in evaluation cost while maintaining score correlation above 0.87 for most datasets (Table 5 in Appendix D.1).

**Score Aggregation Strategy**

To provide an overall signal for model development, the authors design a score aggregation strategy that accounts for the fact that different benchmarks use different metrics (accuracy, CIDEr, ANLS, F1, etc.) with different scales. The aggregation process first normalizes scores from each dataset to a range of 0–100, then calculates the average across all datasets to produce a final aggregated score. Figure 3 shows that the aggregated scores before and after lite set pruning remain highly correlated across different models, validating that the pruned suite preserves relative model rankings. The paper explicitly notes that `LMMS-EVAL LITE` is "not designed to fully compare the performance of different model families" but rather serves as "a tool to provide useful and low-cost signals during model training and ablations" (Section 3), meaning it should be used for iterative development rather than final benchmark reporting.

#### 3.4.3 LIVEBENCH: Dynamic Contamination-Free Evaluation

**Motivation and Core Innovation**

The `LIVEBENCH` component addresses the "zero contamination" vertex of the evaluation trilemma by creating evaluation data that literally cannot exist in any model's training corpus because it is generated from news and forum content that was published after the model's training cutoff date. This is fundamentally different from static benchmarks where the test data exists in a fixed form that can be scraped and included in training data — as demonstrated in Section 4.1, many popular benchmarks show 20–68% overlap with training corpora. The core innovation is an automated pipeline that continuously collects fresh web content, extracts multimodal information, generates questions, validates them, and produces a monthly-updated benchmark.

**Data Collection Pipeline**

The data collection process begins by capturing screenshots of homepage sections from over 60 news outlets and forums, including `CNN`, `BBC`, `Bloomberg`, `WSJ`, `Reuters`, `Reddit`, and `Hacker News` (full list in Table 28 of Appendix E.1). These sources span multiple categories including general news, business, technology, sports, science, and international affairs to ensure diverse content coverage. The pipeline captures screenshots rather than scraping HTML directly because screenshots preserve the visual layout, images, and typography that models must interpret — this is critical for evaluating true multimodal understanding rather than just text extraction from HTML source.

**Information Extraction Process**

The information extraction consists of three sequential steps, all performed using `Claude-3.5-Sonnet` as the extraction model. First, the model performs **OCR (Optical Character Recognition)** to extract all text from the website screenshot, capturing headlines, article text, timestamps, and any other textual content visible in the image. Second, the model identifies significant images within the screenshot and extracts relevant details about these images, including the environment depicted, actions and expressions of individuals, and the relationship between images and corresponding text — this step ensures that visual information is not lost and can be used to generate image-grounded questions. Third, the model is asked to specify what makes the information "newsworthy," identifying temporal markers (e.g., "what occurred in September 2024") that differentiate this news from historical content, which is critical for ensuring the data is truly fresh and not just republished older content.

**QA Generation Based on Bloom's Taxonomy**

The extracted information is sent to a quiz model that generates questions and answers across four categories based on **Bloom's Taxonomy** (Bloom et al., 1956), a hierarchical framework for classifying educational learning objectives from basic recall to complex creation. The four categories are:

1.  **Concrete Recognition**: Questions requiring recall of facts and explanation of fundamental concepts from the news content, such as "What are the key points in this news story?" or "Identify the key details of the U.S. 10 Year Treasury Note provided in the image." This tests basic perception and information extraction capabilities.

2.  **Real-world Application**: Questions requiring application of knowledge to practical situations, such as "Please present this news in Arabic and output it in markdown format," "Organize all the news on this page in the form of an HTML table," or "Sort out the exchange rate data and plot them using the Julia language." This tests the model's ability to transform and apply extracted information.

3.  **Analytical Understanding**: Questions requiring dissection of content to understand relationships and deeper meanings, such as "What are the factors that led to this event?" or "How does this event relate to other current issues?" This tests reasoning and connection-making capabilities.

4.  **Divergent Thinking & Creation**: Questions requiring generation of new ideas and synthesis of concepts, such as "How could you create a new headline that captures the essence of the event differently?" or "If you were the reporter, how would you approach this story to provide a unique angle?" This tests creative and generative capabilities.

Table 9 in Appendix E.2 provides the full prompt used for QA generation, which instructs the model to create challenging, answerable, and checkable questions while avoiding political bias and focusing on understanding the image content rather than expressing opinions.

**QA Checker and Finalizer Pipeline**

To ensure question quality, the pipeline includes two additional model-based refinement stages. The **Checker model** is responsible for refining questions and answers, restructuring them to ensure questions are answerable, verifiable, and challenging, and verifying that each QA pair falls into the correct Bloom's Taxonomy category. If a QA pair does not meet requirements, the Checker modifies the question and forwards it to the Finalizer. The **Finalizer model** is responsible for reformatting questions to enhance readability for human users, converting dict/JSON-formatted criteria to natural language, ensuring scoring criteria are rational and facilitate accurate assessment, and verifying that full score equals 10 points and directly relates to the specific answer. Tables 11 and 12 in Appendix E.2 provide the exact prompts for these stages.

**QA Scoring and Selection**

A **Scorer model** evaluates each QA pair based on three criteria with a maximum total score of 10 points: **Authenticity** (5 points, measuring whether information can be directly obtained from the image or reasonably inferred), **Logical Coherence** (3 points, measuring whether the answer logically follows from the question and maintains consistency with image context), and **Clarity and Precision** (2 points, measuring whether the question and answer are clearly articulated). Table 10 in Appendix E.2 provides the scoring prompt with detailed rubrics for each score level. To balance data collection costs with evaluation efficiency, the pipeline collects approximately 500 questions each month and selects 100 to 300 for the final `LIVEBENCH` problem set based on those exceeding a certain score threshold, with manual review to remove inappropriate questions.

**Evaluation Metrics and Judge Models**

The evaluation adopts scoring criteria from `LLaVA-Wilder` (Li et al., 2024) and `Vibe-Eval` (Padlewski et al., 2024), using `GPT-4o` as the primary judge model that assigns scores from 1 to 10 based on ground-truth answers and the scoring criteria established during QA generation. The final report results are scaled to an accuracy metric ranging from 0 to 100 based on the assigned scores. Alternative judge models include `Claude-3.5-Sonnet` and `Gemini 1.5 Pro` for cross-validation. Table 14 in Appendix E.3 provides the judge prompt format, which requires JSON output with explanation and rating fields. Table 3 presents results from `LiveBench-2024-09`, showing that `GPT-4o` achieves 92.0 overall score, `Claude-3.5-sonnet` achieves 90.3, while open-source models like `LLaVA-OV-72B-Chat` achieve 75.0, demonstrating a significant capability gap that static benchmarks may not fully capture.

#### 3.4.4 Contamination Detection Methodology

**Text Overlap Detection**

To measure text overlap between training data and benchmarks, the authors use an **n-gram matching technique** similar to those employed by `GPT-4`, `PaLM`, and `LLaMA` for contamination detection. The standard approach uses 8–13 n-grams (Brown et al., 2020), but this paper consistently uses **8-grams** for simplicity. An n-gram is a contiguous sequence of $n$ tokens from a text — for example, the 8-gram "the quick brown fox jumps over the lazy" from a sentence. The pipeline excludes any n-gram appearing more than 10 times in the training data, labeling these as "meaningless n-grams" that represent common phrases rather than unique content. For each new n-gram candidate from benchmark data, the system calculates an overlap ratio against the set of meaningless n-grams and excludes candidates exceeding a predefined threshold, ensuring that only meaningful overlaps are counted toward contamination metrics.

**Image Overlap Detection**

Determining image overlap is more challenging than text overlap because images cannot be directly compared via string matching. While common practice involves computing image embeddings and calculating cosine similarity, selecting an appropriate threshold applicable to all datasets is difficult — a similarity threshold of 0.8 might work for one dataset but miss overlaps in another. Instead, the authors empirically find that using the pretrained **SEED-tokenizer** (Ge et al., 2023) leads to meaningful separation in detecting overlap. The SEED-tokenizer converts each image into a 1-D sequence of 32 tokens (discrete visual tokens similar to how text is tokenized into word pieces). An **8-gram lookup table** is then constructed from these image tokens to detect image contamination, where the occurrence of 8-gram overlap can be interpreted as approximately 1/4 of the image overlapping (since 8 out of 32 tokens represents 25% of the image representation).

**Contamination Results and Analysis**

Figure 4 presents contamination analysis across 20+ benchmarks, revealing that datasets with overlap proportions exceeding 20% include `ChartQA`, `VQAv2`, `COCO2014`, and `GQA`, all confirmed to have their training sets included in `LLaVA`'s training data. Table 4 in Appendix C provides detailed statistics: `ChartQA` shows 68.64% image overlap and 26.52% text overlap with `LLaVA-NeXT` training data, `VQAv2` shows 46.21% image overlap, `COCO2014` shows 46.05% image overlap, and `POPE` shows 42.20% image overlap (because it uses image sources from COCO). The analysis identifies three primary contamination types illustrated in Figure 5: **duplicate images** (completely identical images between training and benchmark), **similar images** (visually similar images that could lead to semantically similar questions), and **similar questions** (recurring question structures that advantage models even with different images).

#### 3.4.5 Design Choices and Trade-offs

**Why Three Separate Systems Rather Than One**

The authors explicitly acknowledge that they "do not break this impossible triangle" (Section 1), meaning no single system can achieve wide coverage, low cost, and zero contamination simultaneously. Instead of claiming to solve the trilemma, they provide three complementary tools that allow researchers to navigate the trade-offs transparently based on their current needs. During model development and ablation studies, `LMMS-EVAL LITE` provides fast feedback at 10× reduced cost. For comprehensive final evaluation and comparison with prior work, `LMMS-EVAL` full suite provides wide coverage across 50+ tasks. For contamination-free assessment of true generalization ability, `LIVEBENCH` provides fresh data that cannot exist in training corpora. This modular approach is more honest about limitations than claiming a single benchmark solves all problems.

**Why CLIP+BGE Embeddings for Coreset Selection**

The choice to use separate CLIP and BGE-M3 embeddings rather than training a custom multimodal embedding model reflects a cost-benefit analysis. Training a custom embedding model would require significant computational resources and careful validation to ensure the embeddings capture the right semantic similarities for benchmark representativeness. CLIP and BGE-M3 are both pretrained on massive datasets (CLIP on 400M image-text pairs, BGE-M3 on diverse text corpora) and provide strong off-the-shelf performance. Appendix D.1 shows that the custom `LLaVA-Qwen 1.8B` embedding achieved comparable correlation scores (0.94 vs 0.98 on `AI2D`) but required additional training compute, making CLIP+BGE the more efficient choice for a tool designed to reduce evaluation costs.

**Why 8-gram for Contamination Detection**

The consistent use of 8-grams for both text and image contamination detection (rather than the more common 13-grams for text) reflects a design choice prioritizing sensitivity over specificity. Shorter n-grams will detect more potential overlaps, including partial matches, which is appropriate for a contamination detection tool where false negatives (missing actual contamination) are more harmful than false positives (flagging non-problematic overlaps). The 8-gram threshold for images (8 out of 32 SEED tokens) corresponds to approximately 25% image overlap, which the authors consider meaningful enough to flag as potential contamination while not being so strict as to flag every image with minor similarities.

**Why Claude-3.5-Sonnet for Information Extraction**

The pipeline uses `Claude-3.5-Sonnet` for the information extraction stage rather than the models being evaluated because the extraction needs to be highly reliable — errors at this stage would propagate through the entire QA generation pipeline and compromise benchmark quality. Using a strong commercial model ensures high-quality extraction even though it adds cost, and this cost is amortized across all models that will be evaluated on the resulting benchmark. This is a deliberate separation of concerns: the benchmark creation pipeline can use expensive, high-quality models because it runs once per month, while the benchmark itself evaluates models efficiently.

**Why GPT-4o as Primary Judge Model**

The evaluation uses `GPT-4o` as the primary judge model due to its "popularity and high-throughput API" (Appendix E.3), though `Claude-3.5-Sonnet` and `Gemini 1.5 Pro` serve as alternatives for cross-validation. This choice acknowledges that judge models themselves have biases and limitations, so providing alternative judges allows researchers to assess whether results are robust to judge model selection. The paper does not claim that `GPT-4o` is the objectively best judge, but rather that it provides a consistent, accessible baseline that other researchers can replicate.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper does not propose a new model architecture but instead introduces infrastructural and methodological innovations that reshape how Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) are assessed. The contributions range from conceptual frameworks to automated pipelines and empirical measurements. Below, we distinguish between fundamental innovations that change the evaluation paradigm and incremental improvements that optimize existing processes.

### 4.1 Conceptual Innovation: The Evaluation Trilemma Framework

While trade-offs in benchmarking are generally understood, this work formalizes them into a specific **"Evaluation Trilemma"** for LMMs (Figure 1). Prior efforts often attempted to maximize all three vertices—**Wide Coverage**, **Low Cost**, and **Zero Contamination**—simultaneously, leading to benchmarks that were either too expensive (e.g., human arenas), too narrow (e.g., small fresh datasets), or compromised (e.g., static public datasets).

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** Existing frameworks like `LM-EVAL-HARNESS` (Gao et al., 2023) focus primarily on standardization (Coverage + Cost) but do not explicitly address the contamination vertex. Conversely, human-based arenas like `Chatbot Arena` (Chiang et al., 2024) address Contamination + Coverage but fail on Cost.
*   **Significance:** By explicitly stating that **"we do not break this impossible triangle"** (Section 1), the authors shift the community goalpost from finding a "perfect benchmark" to **managing trade-offs transparently**. This insight justifies the release of three separate tools (`LMMS-EVAL`, `LITE`, `LIVEBENCH`) rather than a single monolithic suite. It encourages researchers to select the tool that matches their development phase (e.g., using `LITE` for ablation studies and `LIVEBENCH` for final reporting), preventing the misuse of static benchmarks for claiming state-of-the-art generalization.

### 4.2 Methodological Innovation: Automated Dynamic Benchmark Construction

The `LIVEBENCH` pipeline represents a fundamental shift in how evaluation data is sourced. Traditionally, benchmarks are **static**: curated once by humans and fixed forever (e.g., `VQAv2`, `MMBench`). This static nature is the root cause of contamination. `LIVEBENCH` introduces a **dynamic, automated generation pipeline** that creates fresh evaluation data continuously from live web sources.

*   **Novelty:** Unlike human-driven dynamic evaluations (which are slow and expensive) or static scrapes (which are prone to leakage), this system uses a strong multimodal model (`Claude-3.5-Sonnet`) to parse live news screenshots into structured QA pairs (Section 3.4.3). This leverages the capability of modern LMMs to **bootstrap their own evaluation infrastructure**.
*   **Why It Works:** The innovation lies in the **temporal guarantee**. Because the data is sourced from news published *after* model training cutoffs (e.g., September 2024 news for models trained earlier), contamination is theoretically impossible by construction.
*   **Impact:** Table 3 shows that this method reveals capability gaps hidden by static benchmarks. For instance, while open-source models often compete closely with proprietary ones on static tasks, `LIVEBENCH` shows `GPT-4o` (92.0) leading `LLaVA-OV-72B-Chat` (75.0) by a significant margin. This suggests that static benchmarks may have saturated, and dynamic evaluation is necessary to differentiate top-tier models.

### 4.3 Algorithmic Innovation: Multimodal Coreset Selection for Evaluation

The `LMMS-EVAL LITE` suite demonstrates that benchmark efficiency can be drastically improved without sacrificing fidelity, challenging the assumption that comprehensive evaluation requires full datasets. The core innovation is applying **k-Center clustering** to **multimodal embeddings** (CLIP for images + BGE-M3 for text) to select representative subsets.

*   **Differentiation from Prior Work:** Prior coreset methods in NLP often rely on text-only embeddings or random sampling. This work validates that **multimodal semantic similarity** is sufficient to preserve evaluation signals.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The results in Table 2 and Table 5 (Appendix D.1) show correlation scores between **0.87 and 0.99** across diverse tasks (e.g., `AI2D`, `DocVQA`, `TextVQA`) despite reducing dataset sizes by approximately **90%** (from 90,223 to 9,134 instances).
*   **Significance:** This proves that evaluation cost can be reduced by an order of magnitude (Figure 2) while maintaining reliable relative rankings (Figure 3). This is critical for the research community, where running full evaluations on 50+ tasks can take days even on 8×A100 GPUs. It enables rapid iteration during model training, a capability previously unavailable for large-scale multimodal evaluation.

### 4.4 Empirical Innovation: Quantifiable Image Contamination via Tokenization

Perhaps the most critical empirical contribution is the development of a **quantitative metric for image contamination**. Prior work often assumed contamination existed but lacked robust tools to measure it, especially for visual data where exact string matching is impossible.

*   **Technical Distinction:** Common methods use cosine similarity on image embeddings, which requires arbitrary thresholds (e.g., is 0.85 similarity "contamination"?). This paper introduces a **discrete tokenization approach** using the `SEED-tokenizer` (Ge et al., 2023). By converting images into sequences of 32 tokens and checking for **8-gram overlaps**, the authors establish a concrete definition of overlap: an 8-gram match implies approximately **25% of the visual content** is identical (Section 3.4.4).
*   **Key Findings:** This method revealed severe contamination levels previously unquantified. For example, `ChartQA` showed **68.64% image overlap** and `VQAv2` showed **46.21% overlap** with `LLaVA-NeXT` training data (Table 4 in Appendix C).
*   **Implication:** These numbers validate the necessity of `LIVEBENCH`. If nearly half of a benchmark's images exist in the training set, performance metrics on that benchmark reflect **memorization** rather than **generalization**. This insight forces a re-evaluation of past claims based on static benchmarks like `VQAv2` or `COCO`, suggesting that reported scores may be significantly inflated.

### 4.5 Incremental Contribution: Unified Standardization at Scale

While less novel than the dynamic benchmarking or contamination metrics, the `LMMS-EVAL` suite provides a necessary **infrastructural baseline**. It adapts the `LM-EVAL-HARNESS` philosophy to the multimodal domain, supporting over **50 tasks** and **10+ models** (Table 1).

*   **Role:** This serves as the "control group" for the field. Without a standardized harness, comparing the efficiency gains of `LITE` or the contamination resistance of `LIVEBENCH` would be impossible.
*   **Limitation:** The authors acknowledge this is an incremental improvement over existing custom scripts (Section 2.1). Its value lies not in algorithmic novelty but in **reproducibility**. By logging detailed model outputs and score breakdowns, it allows future researchers to audit exactly how a score was derived, addressing the "black box" nature of many model release reports.

In summary, the paper's primary innovations are **conceptual** (accepting the trilemma), **methodological** (automating dynamic benchmark creation), and **empirical** (quantifying image contamination). These contributions collectively move the field from opaque, static evaluation toward transparent, efficient, and contamination-resistant benchmarking.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section walks through the experimental evidence supporting the paper's three core contributions: the standardized `LMMS-EVAL` suite, the efficient `LMMS-EVAL LITE` version, and the contamination-free `LIVEBENCH`. We examine not just what numbers were reported, but **what those numbers actually tell us** about model capabilities, evaluation reliability, and the validity of the trilemma framework.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

**Models Evaluated**

The experiments cover a comprehensive range of LMMs to ensure findings generalize across model families and scales. Table 1 presents results for **21 model variants** spanning:

*   **Parameter scales:** From 0.5B (`LLaVA-OV-0.5B`) to 110B (`LLaVA-NeXT-110B`) — covering two orders of magnitude.
*   **Model families:** `LLaVA` (multiple variants), `Qwen-VL`, `InstructBLIP`, `InternVL`, `Idefics2`, `XComposer`, and `LLaVA-OV`.
*   **Access types:** Both open-sourced models (evaluated locally) and closed-source API models (`GPT-4V`, `Gemini`, `Claude`).

For `LIVEBENCH` (Table 3), the evaluation expands to include commercial models like `GPT-4o` (92.0 overall), `Claude-3.5-sonnet` (90.3), and `Gemini-1.5-Pro` (84.5), enabling direct comparison between open-source and proprietary systems on fresh data.

**Hardware and Inference Configuration**

The evaluation setup is explicitly documented to ensure reproducibility (Section 3):

*   **GPUs:** Evaluations conducted on **8×A100 GPUs** with flash attention enabled.
*   **Parallelization:** Data parallelism by default for models under 72B parameters; pipeline parallelism (Huang et al., 2019) for larger models that cannot fit on single GPUs.
*   **Model weights:** Replicated across GPUs for data parallel evaluation.

This level of detail is critical — many prior evaluation reports omit hardware specifications, making it impossible to assess whether performance differences stem from model architecture or inference optimization.

**Metrics and Scoring**

Different benchmarks use different metrics, which complicates aggregation. The paper handles this by:

*   **Task-specific metrics:** Accuracy for multiple-choice tasks (`AI2D`, `ScienceQA`), CIDEr for captioning (`COCO`, `NoCaps`), ANLS for document VQA (`DocVQA`), F1 for hallucination detection (`POPE`), and exact match for text VQA (`TextVQA`).
*   **Normalization for aggregation:** For `LMMS-EVAL LITE`, scores from each dataset are normalized to a 0–100 range before averaging (Section 3.4.2), enabling a single aggregated score despite metric heterogeneity.
*   **Judge-based scoring for LIVEBENCH:** `GPT-4o` serves as the primary judge model, assigning scores from 1 to 10 based on ground-truth answers and predefined criteria (Appendix E.3). Final results are scaled to 0–100 accuracy metrics.

**Datasets Covered**

The full `LMMS-EVAL` suite encompasses **over 50 tasks** across diverse domains (Table 25 in Appendix F):

### 5.2 LMMS-EVAL Full Suite Results

**Model Performance Patterns**

Table 1 reveals several important patterns about model capabilities across tasks:

*   **Scale matters, but not uniformly:** The 110B `LLaVA-NeXT-110B` achieves 80.4 on `AI2D` compared to 54.8 for the 7B `LLaVA-1.5-7B` — a **25.6 point improvement**. However, on `MMMU`, the improvement is only **13.8 points** (49.1 vs. 35.3). This suggests that scaling benefits vary by task domain.

*   **Architecture matters more than scale for some tasks:** The 26B `InternVL-1.5` achieves 92.4 on `DocVQA`, outperforming the 110B `LLaVA-NeXT-110B` (85.7) by **6.7 points**. This indicates that architectural choices (e.g., vision encoder design, alignment strategy) can outweigh parameter count for document understanding tasks.

*   **Instruction tuning variants show mixed results:** `LLaVA-OV-72B(SI)` achieves 85.1 on `AI2D` compared to 85.6 for the non-SI version — a slight **0.5 point decrease**. However, on `MME`, the SI version achieves 2269.0 vs. 2261.0 for the non-SI version — a **8 point improvement**. This suggests instruction tuning benefits depend on the specific task.

**Reproducibility Verification**

The paper emphasizes that standardized evaluation enables verification of previously reported scores. For example, `LLaVA-1.5-7B` achieves 59.6 on `LLaVAW` in this evaluation (Table 1), which can be compared against the original paper's reported numbers. Discrepancies would indicate either evaluation protocol differences or implementation variations — the detailed logging in `LMMS-EVAL` allows researchers to trace exactly where differences arise.

**Limitation: Incomplete Model Coverage**

The authors acknowledge in Appendix F.1 that **"due to limited computational resources, we are only able to provide a holistic view of models from the LLaVA series."** This is an important caveat — while 21 model variants are evaluated, comprehensive coverage across all major model families (e.g., `Qwen-VL`, `InternVL`, `Idefics`) is not achieved for all 50+ datasets. This limits the ability to make definitive cross-family comparisons on the full suite.

### 5.3 LMMS-EVAL LITE Validation

**Correlation with Full Suite**

The core claim for `LMMS-EVAL LITE` is that it preserves evaluation fidelity while reducing cost. Table 2 and Table 5 (Appendix D.1) provide the empirical evidence:

| Dataset | Full Size | Lite Size | Correlation (CLIP+BGE) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |

All correlation scores exceed **0.87**, with most above **0.95**. This is strong evidence that the lite set preserves relative model rankings. The authors note that `SeedBench` has the lowest correlation (0.87), which may reflect the dataset's diversity — with 17,990 instances spanning multiple task types, a 700-instance subset may not capture all dimensions adequately.

**Comparison with Alternative Methods**

Table 2 compares the k-Center approach against two baselines:

*   **k-means clustering:** Achieves 0.79 on `Flickr30k` vs. 0.91 for k-Center — a **12 point gap**.
*   **Quire (active learning):** Achieves 0.97 on `Flickr30k` vs. 0.91 for k-Center — Quire performs slightly better but requires iterative querying, making it less efficient.

The k-Center method strikes a balance between correlation quality and computational efficiency, which aligns with the goal of reducing evaluation cost.

**Cost Reduction**

Figure 2 demonstrates the evaluation time savings. While exact numbers are not provided in the figure caption, the text states that `LMMS-EVAL LITE` achieves approximately a **10× reduction** in evaluation cost (from 90,223 to 9,134 instances). For researchers running evaluations daily during model development, this translates to hours instead of days per evaluation cycle.

**Score Aggregation Validation**

Figure 3 plots aggregated scores before and after lite set pruning across different models. The x-axis represents the weighted average percentage of scores across all datasets. The key observation is that **relative rankings are preserved** — models that perform well on the full suite also perform well on the lite suite. This validates the claim that `LMMS-EVAL LITE` can be used for iterative development without misleading conclusions about model improvements.

**Important Caveat**

The authors explicitly state that `LMMS-EVAL LITE` is **"not designed to fully compare the performance of different model families"** but rather serves as **"a tool to provide useful and low-cost signals during model training and ablations"** (Section 3). This is a critical limitation — researchers should not use lite set results for final paper claims or leaderboards, only for development iteration.

### 5.4 LIVEBENCH Results and Analysis

**Overall Performance Gap**

Table 3 presents `LiveBench-2024-09` results for 14 models across five cognitive categories. The most striking finding is the **capability gap between open-source and closed-source models**:

| Model | Overall | Recognition | Analysis | Thinking | Realworld |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| `GPT-4o` | **92.0** | 91.7 | 93.8 | 94.8 | 87.6 |
| `Claude-3.5-sonnet` | **90.3** | 94.6 | 93.4 | 95.3 | 85.8 |
| `GPT4o-mini` | **91.9** | 94.6 | 93.4 | 95.3 | 84.3 |
| `Qwen2-VL-72B` | **85.9** | 86.7 | 88.8 | 89.0 | 79.2 |
| `Gemini-1.5-Pro` | **84.5** | 85.4 | 83.8 | 88.6 | 80.1 |

| `LLaVA-1.5-7B` | **30.2** | 9.4 | 36.4 | 45.4 | 29.4 |

**Key observations:**

1.  **Top-tier models cluster together:** `GPT-4o`, `Claude-3.5-sonnet`, and `GPT4o-mini` all score above 90.0, suggesting a capability ceiling that open-source models have not yet reached.

2.  **Open-source models lag significantly:** The best open-source model (`Qwen2-VL-72B` at 85.9) trails `GPT-4o` by **6.1 points**. The `LLaVA-OV-72B-Chat` (75.0) trails by **17.0 points** — a substantial gap.

3.  **Recognition is the weakest area for open-source:** `LLaVA-OV-72B-Chat` scores 62.0 on Recognition vs. 91.7 for `GPT-4o` — a **29.7 point gap**. This suggests that basic visual perception and OCR capabilities remain a bottleneck for open-source models.

4.  **Analytical capabilities are more competitive:** On Analysis, `LLaVA-OV-72B-Chat` achieves 87.8 vs. 93.8 for `GPT-4o` — only a **6.0 point gap**. This suggests that once visual information is correctly extracted, open-source models can reason about it more competently.

**Why This Gap Matters**

The authors argue that **"open-sourced models are still far from achieving the level of GPT-4V"** and that **"the current superiority in benchmarks can be attributed to the simplicity, fixed nature, or potential contamination of the evaluated scenarios"** (Section 4.2.2). The `LIVEBENCH` results support this claim — on static benchmarks like `MME` or `MMBench`, open-source models often report competitive scores, but on fresh, dynamic data, the gap widens significantly.

**Category-Specific Insights**

The five cognitive categories reveal different model strengths:

*   **Concrete Recognition:** Tests basic OCR and information extraction. `Claude-3.5-sonnet` leads at 94.6, while `LLaVA-1.5-7B` scores only 9.4 — a **85.2 point gap**. This extreme difference suggests that older open-source models struggle fundamentally with reading text from images.

*   **Real-world Application:** Tests practical task completion (e.g., generating HTML tables, translating news). `LLaMA-3.2-V-11B-Instruct` performs surprisingly well at 74.7, outperforming `LLaVA-OV-72B-Chat` (66.6) despite being **6× smaller**. This suggests that instruction tuning quality may matter more than scale for application tasks.

*   **Divergent Thinking & Creation:** Tests creative synthesis. `Claude-3.5-sonnet` leads at 95.3, with most top models clustering above 88.0. The smaller gap here (compared to Recognition) suggests that creative capabilities are more evenly distributed across model families.

### 5.5 Contamination Analysis

**Quantitative Overlap Measurements**

Table 4 (Appendix C) provides the most concrete evidence for the contamination problem. The authors measured both image and text overlap between `LLaVA-NeXT` training data and 20+ benchmarks:

| Dataset | Image Overlap (%) | Text Overlap (%) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| `ChartQA` | **68.64** | 26.52 |
| `VQAv2` | **46.21** | 2.90 |
| `COCO2014` | **46.05** | 22.19 |
| `POPE` | **42.20** | 0.00 |
| `DocVQA` | 36.08 | 4.06 |
| `AI2D` | 6.09 | **25.97** |
| `MMBench` | 2.77 | 7.97 |
| `MME` | 1.60 | 1.39 |

**Critical findings:**

1.  **Some benchmarks are severely compromised:** `ChartQA` at 68.64% image overlap means that **more than two-thirds** of the test images exist in the training data. Performance on this benchmark reflects memorization rather than generalization.

2.  **Text overlap can be high even when image overlap is low:** `AI2D` has only 6.09% image overlap but 25.97% text overlap. This means that while images differ, question structures are similar enough that models can learn answer patterns without seeing identical images.

3.  **Some benchmarks remain relatively clean:** `MME` (1.60% image, 1.39% text) and `MMBench` (2.77% image, 7.97% text) show low overlap, suggesting they may still provide meaningful evaluation signals. However, these are exceptions rather than the rule.

**Three Types of Contamination**

Figure 5 illustrates three contamination types identified through qualitative analysis:

1.  **Duplicate Images:** Completely identical images between training and test sets. Example: Two identical images found in `ChartQA` and `MM-Vet`.

2.  **Similar Images:** Visually similar images that enable models to rely on memorized visual features. Example: Similar chart structures in `ChartQA` training and test data.

3.  **Similar Questions:** Recurring question structures that allow pattern matching. Example: Similar mathematical question formats in `MathVista` despite different images.

**Why Tokenization-Based Detection Matters**

The paper's use of **SEED-tokenizer** for image overlap detection (Section 3.4.4) is methodologically significant. Prior work typically used cosine similarity on image embeddings, which requires arbitrary thresholds (e.g., is 0.85 similarity "contamination"?). By tokenizing images into 32 discrete tokens and checking for 8-gram overlaps, the authors establish that an 8-gram match represents approximately **25% of the visual content** being identical. This provides a concrete, interpretable metric rather than an abstract similarity score.

### 5.6 Failure Cases and Limitations

**Case Study Analysis**

Appendix E.5 provides detailed failure case analyses (Tables 15–20) that reveal specific model weaknesses:

*   **Table 15 (`LLaVA-1.5-7B` on Concrete Recognition):** The model produced a list of timestamps that **"do not correspond to the actual timestamps in the image"** and headlines that are **"completely incorrect."** GPT-4o judge score: **0/10**. This demonstrates fundamental OCR and text extraction failures in older open-source models.

*   **Table 16 (`Qwen-VL-72B-Instruct` on Analytical Questions):** The model incorrectly stated that 'cosmic grapes' clumps have larger sizes and masses compared to 'CID-931 clumps', when the ground truth indicates the opposite. GPT-4o judge score: **1/10**. This shows that even large open-source models can fail at basic chart interpretation.

*   **Table 17 (`Qwen-VL-72B-Instruct` on Real-world Assistance):** The model identified the wrong volcano name (水良部島 instead of 口永良部島) and incorrect warning type. GPT-4o judge score: **0/10**. This reveals OCR errors propagating through the entire reasoning chain.

*   **Table 18 (`LLaVA-NeXT-OV-72B-Chat` on Concrete Recognition):** The model incorrectly matched tennis players and scores — stating "Karolina Muchova is leading against Anna Blinkova" when the correct match was "Muchova vs. Qinwen Zheng." GPT-4o judge score: **0/10**. This demonstrates hallucination when models cannot read small text on webpages.

*   **Table 19 (`LLaMA-3.2-Vision-11B-Instruct` on Concrete Recognition):** The model provided a detailed image description but **"fails to summarize the main points of the news article."** GPT-4o judge score: **0/10**. This shows that visual description capabilities do not guarantee comprehension of article content.

*   **Table 20 (`LLaVA-NeXT-OV-72B-Chat` on Analytical Question):** The model incorrectly listed Bitcoin closing prices (including an erroneous value of 62242, which is the current price, not a closing price) and computed an incorrect average (15556.8 vs. correct 61,754). GPT-4o judge score: **0/10**. This reveals arithmetic reasoning failures even when numbers are extracted.

**What Failure Cases Reveal**

These case studies support the paper's central argument that **static benchmarks mask true capability gaps**. On `LIVEBENCH`, failure modes are exposed that would not appear on curated static datasets:

1.  **OCR remains a bottleneck:** Multiple failures stem from incorrect text extraction from webpages, suggesting that vision encoders still struggle with real-world text rendering.

2.  **Hallucination under uncertainty:** When models cannot read text clearly, they tend to hallucinate plausible-sounding answers rather than acknowledging uncertainty.

3.  **Instruction following varies:** Some models (e.g., `LLaMA-3.2-Vision-11B-Instruct`) can describe images well but fail to follow specific instructions (e.g., "summarize main points"), indicating a disconnect between perception and task execution.

**Limitations in Experimental Design**

Several limitations should be noted:

1.  **Judge model bias:** `LIVEBENCH` uses `GPT-4o` as the primary judge model. While alternative judges (`Claude-3.5-Sonnet`, `Gemini 1.5 Pro`) are available, the paper does not report inter-judge agreement rates. If `GPT-4o` systematically favors certain answer styles, results may be biased.

2.  **Limited temporal coverage:** `LiveBench-2024-09` represents a single month of data. The paper claims continuous updates, but long-term stability of the benchmark (e.g., whether difficulty remains consistent across months) is not evaluated.

3.  **Training data access requirement:** The contamination analysis requires access to model training data, which **"most research does not open-source"** (Section 6). This limits the ability to verify contamination claims for closed-source models or models with undisclosed training corpora.

4.  **Incomplete cross-family comparison:** As noted in Appendix F.1, comprehensive evaluation across all 50+ datasets was only achieved for `LLaVA` series models due to computational constraints. This limits the generalizability of full-suite findings.

### 5.7 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

**Do Results Support the Trilemma Framework?**

The experimental evidence **partially supports** the trilemma framework but does not definitively prove it:

*   **Support:** The contamination analysis (Table 4) demonstrates that wide-coverage static benchmarks (`VQAv2`, `ChartQA`) have high contamination, validating the claim that Coverage + Cost compromises Contamination. The `LIVEBENCH` results (Table 3) show that fresh data reveals capability gaps hidden by static benchmarks, supporting the need for contamination-free evaluation.

*   **Limitation:** The paper does not experimentally demonstrate that achieving all three goals simultaneously is *impossible* — only that existing benchmarks fail on at least one dimension. A stronger proof would require attempting to construct a benchmark that achieves all three and documenting why it fails.

**Do Results Support LMMS-EVAL LITE's Efficiency Claims?**

The correlation results (Table 2, Table 5) **strongly support** the claim that lite sets preserve evaluation fidelity:

*   All correlation scores exceed 0.87, with most above 0.95.
*   The 10× reduction in instances (90,223 → 9,134) is empirically verified.
*   Figure 3 shows preserved relative rankings across models.

However, the caveat that lite sets should not be used for final cross-family comparisons is important — the experiments validate lite sets for *iterative development*, not *definitive benchmarking*.

**Do Results Support LIVEBENCH's Contamination-Free Claims?**

The contamination-free claim is **theoretically sound but empirically unverified**:

*   **Theoretical strength:** By sourcing data from news published after model training cutoffs, contamination is impossible by construction.
*   **Empirical gap:** The paper does not provide direct evidence that `LIVEBENCH` data is absent from training corpora (since training data for closed-source models is not accessible). The claim rests on temporal logic rather than empirical verification.

**Overall Assessment**

The experiments convincingly demonstrate that:

1.  **Standardization matters:** The detailed logging and unified interface enable reproducibility that was previously lacking.
2.  **Contamination is severe:** Quantitative overlap measurements provide concrete evidence that many popular benchmarks are compromised.
3.  **Capability gaps are hidden:** `LIVEBENCH` reveals performance differences that static benchmarks obscure.

However, some claims rely on **theoretical arguments** (e.g., the impossibility of breaking the trilemma) rather than exhaustive experimental proof. The paper's strength lies in providing practical tools for navigating trade-offs, not in definitively solving the underlying challenges.

**Conditions and Trade-offs**

Researchers should understand the following conditions when using these tools:

| Tool | Best Used For | Should NOT Be Used For |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| `LMMS-EVAL` (Full) | Final paper claims, comprehensive auditing | Rapid iteration during training (too slow) |
| `LMMS-EVAL LITE` | Ablation studies, development iteration | Final leaderboard submissions, cross-family comparisons |
| `LIVEBENCH` | Contamination-free assessment, real-world capability evaluation | Tasks requiring domain-specific expertise (e.g., medical imaging) |

Understanding these trade-offs ensures that evaluation results are interpreted appropriately and that claims about model capabilities are grounded in the right evidence.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This paper makes significant strides in addressing LMM evaluation challenges, but it is important to understand where the approach falls short, what assumptions underpin the methodology, and what scenarios remain unaddressed. The authors are refreshingly honest about these limitations — Section 6 explicitly acknowledges that **"we assume that the evaluation trilemma cannot be resolved"** rather than claiming to have solved it. This section walks through the key constraints, trade-offs, and open questions that researchers should consider when adopting these tools.

### 6.1 Fundamental Assumptions and Theoretical Limitations

**The Trilemma Is Accepted, Not Solved**

The most important limitation is conceptual: the paper does not prove that the evaluation trilemma is mathematically impossible to break — it assumes it and works within that constraint. As stated in Section 6:

> "Throughout our papers, we assume that the evaluation trilemma cannot be resolved. This suggests future work that goes deeper into finding a better trade-off among the sides of the trilemma or potentially overcoming it."

This is a reasonable pragmatic stance, but it means the three-tool approach (`LMMS-EVAL`, `LITE`, `LIVEBENCH`) is a **workaround** rather than a **solution**. Researchers hoping for a single benchmark that achieves wide coverage, low cost, and zero contamination will be disappointed. The paper shifts the burden to users to select the appropriate tool for their needs, which requires understanding the trade-offs — a cognitive load that some practitioners may find burdensome.

**Contamination Detection Requires Training Data Access**

The contamination analysis methodology has a critical dependency that limits its applicability. Section 6 states:

> "Additionally, we address the issue of data contamination using a relatively simple method that requires access to the training data, while most research does not open-source their data."

This is a significant constraint. The contamination percentages reported in Table 4 (Appendix C) — such as 68.64% image overlap for `ChartQA` — are only verifiable for models where training data is publicly available (e.g., `LLaVA-NeXT`). For closed-source models like `GPT-4o`, `Claude-3.5`, or `Gemini-1.5-Pro`, contamination levels **cannot be empirically verified** using this method. The paper's claim that `LIVEBENCH` is contamination-free rests on **temporal logic** (news published after training cutoffs) rather than empirical verification for these models.

This creates an asymmetry: open-source models can have their contamination measured and potentially penalized, while closed-source models benefit from the assumption of contamination without direct evidence. Future work would need contamination detection methods that operate **without training data access** — perhaps through influence functions or memorization tests — but this paper does not provide such tools.

### 6.2 Computational and Scalability Constraints

**Incomplete Cross-Family Evaluation on Full Suite**

Despite the claim of evaluating "10+ models," the comprehensive coverage is limited. Appendix F.1 explicitly acknowledges:

> "Due to limited computational resources, we are only able to provide a holistic view of models from the LLaVA series."

This means that while Table 1 shows 21 model variants, the **full 50+ task evaluation was not completed for all model families**. Models like `Qwen-VL`, `InternVL`, and `Idefics2` have partial results, but comprehensive cross-family comparisons on the full `LMMS-EVAL` suite are not available. This limits the ability to make definitive claims about relative model performance across architectures — researchers must rely on `LIVEBENCH` results (Table 3) for cross-family comparisons, which covers only 14 models on dynamic data.

**LMMS-EVAL LITE Has Known Correlation Failures**

The coreset selection method does not work uniformly across all benchmarks. Section 3 notes:

> "For MME (Fu et al., 2024), due to low correlation between the original and lite set scores, we retain the full version."

This reveals that the k-Center clustering approach **fails for certain dataset structures**. The `MME` benchmark likely has characteristics (e.g., highly balanced yes/no questions, specific cognitive skill distributions) that make representative sampling difficult. Table 5 (Appendix D.1) shows `SeedBench` has the lowest correlation at 0.87 — while acceptable, this is notably lower than the 0.98–0.99 correlations achieved on `DocVQA` and `TextVQA`. 

The implication is that researchers cannot blindly apply the lite set to all benchmarks — each dataset requires correlation validation before pruning. This adds overhead to extending `LMMS-EVAL LITE` to new benchmarks, partially undermining the efficiency gains.

**Embedding Choice Trade-offs**

The paper experimented with custom multimodal embeddings but chose off-the-shelf solutions for efficiency. Appendix D.1 reports:

> "For LLaVA embeddings, the last hidden states for all tokens were averaged into a single vector to serve as the feature vector for each data point. We report the correlation results for both embeddings in Table 5."

The custom `LLaVA-Qwen 1.8B` embedding achieved comparable correlation (0.94 on `AI2D`) to CLIP+BGE (0.98) but required additional training compute. The choice of CLIP+BGE prioritizes **efficiency over customization** — a reasonable trade-off for a tool designed to reduce costs, but it means the coreset selection may not capture task-specific semantic relationships as well as a purpose-trained embedding model would.

### 6.3 Data Quality and Coverage Limitations

**LIVEBENCH QA Quality vs. Human Curation**

The automated QA generation pipeline introduces quality trade-offs. Section 4.2.1 acknowledges:

> "It is important to note that the quality of our QA may still fall below that of human-curated answers, as we are aiming to build a dynamic evaluation pipeline that strikes a balance between cost and broad coverage."

This is a critical limitation. While `LIVEBENCH` achieves contamination resistance through automation, the **question quality, answer accuracy, and scoring criteria** are all generated by models (primarily `Claude-3.5-Sonnet` for extraction, `GPT-4o` for judging). Errors at any stage propagate through the pipeline:

- **Extraction errors** (misreading text from screenshots) lead to incorrect ground truths.
- **QA generation errors** produce unanswerable or ambiguous questions.
- **Judge model bias** affects scoring consistency.

Table 15–20 (Appendix E.5) show failure cases where models receive 0/10 scores, but the paper does not report how often the **ground truth itself may be incorrect**. Human validation is mentioned ("we also manually review the questions to remove any that are inappropriate"), but the scale and rigor of this validation are not quantified.

**Website Dependency and Stability**

`LIVEBENCH` relies on 60+ news and forum websites (Table 28, Appendix E.1), but this introduces fragility. The paper notes:

> "It should be noted that this is a initial set of candidate websites and there may be changes depending on the situations of these websites."

Websites can change their layout, implement anti-scraping measures, go offline, or alter their content policies — any of which could disrupt the data collection pipeline. Additionally, the **geographic and linguistic coverage** is skewed toward English-language sources (BBC, CNN, WSJ, Reuters) with limited non-English representation (e.g., Japan's Asahi Shimbun, China's Xinhua). This limits `LIVEBENCH`'s ability to evaluate multilingual models fairly — a `Qwen-VL` model optimized for Chinese content may be disadvantaged on an English-heavy benchmark.

**Domain Coverage Gaps**

The benchmark suite does not cover all important application domains. The Broader Impacts section (Appendix B) states:

> "Besides, automatic evaluations cannot replace expert human assessment in specialized fields such as medical imaging."

This acknowledges that `LMMS-EVAL` and `LIVEBENCH` are not suitable for **high-stakes domains** where expert validation is required. Medical imaging, legal document analysis, scientific figure interpretation, and other specialized tasks require domain-specific expertise that general news-based benchmarks cannot provide. Researchers working in these domains will need supplementary evaluation methods.

### 6.4 Evaluation Methodology Weaknesses

**Judge Model Bias and Lack of Inter-Judge Agreement**

`LIVEBENCH` uses `GPT-4o` as the primary judge model (Appendix E.3), with `Claude-3.5-Sonnet` and `Gemini 1.5 Pro` as alternatives. However, the paper does not report:

- **Inter-judge agreement rates**: How often do different judge models assign the same score to the same response?
- **Systematic bias analysis**: Does `GPT-4o` favor certain answer styles, lengths, or phrasings?
- **Self-preference bias**: Does `GPT-4o` score `GPT-4o` responses higher than other models' responses?

Table 3 shows `GPT-4o` achieving 92.0 overall — the highest score — but without inter-judge validation, it is unclear whether this reflects true capability or judge model preference. Recent research on LLM-as-a-judge has shown that judge models often exhibit self-preference bias, which could inflate `GPT-4o`'s reported performance.

**Temporal Coverage and Benchmark Stability**

`LiveBench-2024-09` represents a **single month** of data collection. The paper claims continuous updates but does not evaluate:

- **Difficulty consistency**: Are questions from different months equally challenging?
- **Topic distribution stability**: Does the benchmark maintain balanced coverage across domains over time?
- **Score comparability**: Can scores from September 2024 be directly compared to October 2024?

Without longitudinal analysis, researchers cannot determine whether score changes reflect model improvements or benchmark difficulty fluctuations. This limits `LIVEBENCH`'s utility for tracking progress over time.

**8-gram Sensitivity Trade-off**

The contamination detection uses 8-grams rather than the more common 13-grams (Section 4.1):

> "Typically, an 8∼13 n-grams range is used (Brown et al., 2020), but we consistently use 8 n-grams for simplicity."

This choice prioritizes **sensitivity over specificity** — shorter n-grams detect more potential overlaps, including partial matches. While appropriate for a contamination detection tool where false negatives are harmful, it may produce **false positives** that flag non-problematic overlaps. The paper does not report precision/recall metrics for the contamination detection, making it difficult to assess how many flagged overlaps represent actual training data leakage versus coincidental similarity.

### 6.5 Privacy and Ethical Considerations

**Web Crawling and Data Privacy**

The Broader Impacts section (Appendix B) acknowledges:

> "The construction of LIVEBENCH uses real-world data crawled from the web. It could potentially lead to concerns regarding data privacy."

While the paper states "The benchmarks we provide are meant for research purposes only and should be used with caution," this does not fully address:

- **Personal information in news content**: News articles may contain names, locations, or other identifying information about individuals.
- **Copyright concerns**: Screenshots of news websites may violate terms of service or copyright restrictions.
- **Consent**: Individuals depicted in news images did not consent to their images being used for AI evaluation.

These are not unique to this work — many AI benchmarks face similar issues — but they represent ongoing ethical challenges that the paper does not resolve.

**Political Content Handling**

The `LIVEBENCH` prompts explicitly instruct models to avoid political bias (Table 9, 11):

> "Please note that although the image may contain a lot of political content, try to avoid questions with any political bias when asking questions."

However, **news is inherently political**, and attempting to extract "neutral" questions from political content creates tension. The paper does not report how often questions were rejected due to political content or whether the filtering process introduces systematic biases (e.g., avoiding certain topics entirely). This remains an unresolved design challenge.

### 6.6 Summary of Trade-offs

The table below summarizes the key trade-offs researchers must navigate when using these tools:

| Tool | Strength | Limitation | Best Use Case |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **LMMS-EVAL (Full)** | Comprehensive coverage (50+ tasks), standardized protocol | High computational cost, contamination risk, incomplete cross-family results | Final paper claims, comprehensive auditing |
| **LMMS-EVAL LITE** | 10× cost reduction, high correlation (0.87–0.99) | Not for final comparisons, some benchmarks (MME) cannot be pruned | Development iteration, ablation studies |
| **LIVEBENCH** | Zero contamination (by construction), reveals hidden capability gaps | QA quality below human curation, judge model bias, English-heavy | Contamination-free assessment, real-world capability evaluation |

**Open Questions for Future Work**

Several important questions remain unanswered:

1. **Can the trilemma be broken?** The paper assumes impossibility but does not prove it. Could novel data synthesis or private evaluation protocols achieve all three goals?

2. **How to detect contamination without training data access?** Current methods require training corpora, which closed-source models do not provide.

3. **What is the long-term stability of LIVEBENCH?** Does difficulty remain consistent across months and years?

4. **How to reduce judge model bias?** Can ensemble judging or human validation improve scoring reliability?

5. **Can coreset selection be improved for problematic benchmarks?** Why does MME show low correlation, and can alternative methods preserve fidelity?

By acknowledging these limitations explicitly, the paper sets realistic expectations and provides a foundation for future work to address remaining challenges. Researchers adopting these tools should understand both their capabilities and constraints to use them appropriately.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper does more than release evaluation tools — it fundamentally reshapes how the LMM community thinks about benchmarking, what claims are credible, and what infrastructure the field needs to mature. This section explores the downstream consequences of this work, the research avenues it opens, and practical guidance for integrating these tools into your workflow.

### 7.1 Shifting the Evaluation Paradigm

**From "Perfect Benchmark" to "Transparent Trade-offs"**

Prior to this work, many evaluation efforts implicitly claimed to solve all evaluation challenges simultaneously. This paper's explicit acknowledgment that **"we do not break this impossible triangle"** (Section 1) represents a philosophical shift with practical consequences:

*   **Honesty over optimism:** Researchers are now encouraged to state which vertex of the trilemma their benchmark prioritizes rather than claiming comprehensive superiority. A paper claiming state-of-the-art performance should specify whether results come from `LMMS-EVAL` (coverage), `LITE` (efficiency), or `LIVEBENCH` (contamination-free).

*   **Tool selection becomes part of methodology:** Just as researchers specify learning rates and batch sizes, future papers should specify which evaluation suite was used and why. This enables readers to interpret results appropriately — a 5-point improvement on `LIVEBENCH` carries different weight than a 5-point improvement on `VQAv2` given the 46.21% contamination rate (Table 4, Appendix C).

*   **Contamination claims require evidence:** The quantitative contamination measurements (Section 4.1) set a new standard. Future model releases may need to report overlap statistics with common benchmarks, similar to how LLM papers now report training data cutoffs.

**Revealing Hidden Capability Gaps**

The `LIVEBENCH` results (Table 3) expose a critical insight: **static benchmarks may have saturated** for distinguishing top-tier models. The 17.0-point gap between `GPT-4o` (92.0) and `LLaVA-OV-72B-Chat` (75.0) on `LIVEBENCH` is substantially larger than gaps on many static benchmarks. This suggests:

*   **Open-source models face a generalization ceiling:** Strong performance on static benchmarks does not guarantee robust real-world capability. The failure cases in Tables 15–20 (Appendix E.5) show that OCR errors, hallucination, and instruction-following failures persist even in 72B-parameter open-source models.

*   **Commercial models maintain advantages in perception:** The 29.7-point gap on Concrete Recognition (91.7 vs. 62.0) indicates that basic visual perception remains a differentiator. This redirects research attention from purely scaling parameters to improving vision encoder quality and OCR robustness.

*   **Benchmark saturation signals need for dynamic evaluation:** When models achieve near-human performance on static tests, the field needs continuously refreshed benchmarks to maintain discrimination power. `LIVEBENCH` provides a template for this.

**Infrastructure Standardization as a Community Good**

The `LMMS-EVAL` framework adapts the `LM-EVAL-HARNESS` philosophy to multimodal models. This has ripple effects:

*   **Reproducibility becomes achievable:** Detailed logging of evaluation setups, prompts, and score breakdowns (Section 2.1) enables other researchers to verify claims or identify discrepancies. This addresses the "black box" evaluation reports common in model release papers.

*   **Cross-paper comparisons become possible:** When multiple research groups use the same evaluation harness, their results become directly comparable even if models were developed independently. This accelerates cumulative progress.

*   **New benchmarks integrate faster:** The unified interface means new dataset creators can plug into `LMMS-EVAL` rather than building custom evaluation scripts. This lowers the barrier to benchmark creation and encourages diversity in evaluation tasks.

### 7.2 Follow-up Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions in Section 6, but additional research avenues emerge from the methodology and findings:

**Contamination Detection Without Training Data Access**

Current contamination analysis requires access to model training corpora, which **"most research does not open-source"** (Section 6). This creates an asymmetry where open-source models can be audited but closed-source models cannot. Follow-up work could explore:

*   **Membership inference attacks:** Adapt techniques from privacy research to detect whether specific benchmark samples were in a model's training set without direct data access.

*   **Memorization tests:** Design probes that distinguish memorized answers from generalized reasoning, similar to approaches in LLM contamination detection (Shi et al., 2024).

*   **Influence function approximations:** Use gradient-based methods to estimate whether removing a benchmark sample from training would change model outputs, without needing the full training corpus.

**Improving Coreset Selection for Problematic Benchmarks**

The `MME` benchmark showed low correlation between full and lite sets, requiring retention of the full version (Section 3.4.2). This suggests k-Center clustering does not work uniformly. Research directions include:

*   **Task-aware clustering:** Incorporate task type metadata (e.g., yes/no questions vs. free-form answers) into the embedding space before clustering.

*   **Stratified sampling hybrids:** Combine k-Center with stratified sampling to ensure rare but important sample types are preserved.

*   **Benchmark-specific correlation thresholds:** Establish minimum correlation requirements per dataset type rather than applying uniform standards.

**Long-term Stability of Dynamic Benchmarks**

`LiveBench-2024-09` represents a single month of data. Longitudinal research is needed to understand:

*   **Difficulty consistency:** Are questions from different months equally challenging? A model scoring 85.0 in September should score similarly in October if capabilities are stable.

*   **Topic distribution drift:** Does the benchmark maintain balanced coverage across domains (science, business, sports, etc.) over time, or does news cycle variation introduce bias?

*   **Score comparability:** Can scores from different months be directly compared, or do they require normalization? This is critical for tracking model progress over time.

**Judge Model Bias Mitigation**

`LIVEBENCH` uses `GPT-4o` as the primary judge (Appendix E.3), but judge models exhibit known biases:

*   **Self-preference bias:** Does `GPT-4o` score its own responses higher? The 92.0 overall score for `GPT-4o` warrants scrutiny without inter-judge validation.

*   **Style preferences:** Do certain answer formats (bullet points vs. paragraphs, verbose vs. concise) receive systematically higher scores?

*   **Ensemble judging:** Could averaging scores from multiple judge models (`GPT-4o`, `Claude-3.5-Sonnet`, `Gemini-1.5-Pro`) reduce individual model bias?

**Domain-Specific Extensions**

The current suite focuses on general-purpose evaluation. Specialized domains need tailored benchmarks:

*   **Medical imaging:** Requires expert-validated ground truths and domain-specific error metrics (Appendix B acknowledges this gap).

*   **Scientific figure interpretation:** Needs benchmarks with expert-annotated reasoning chains, not just final answers.

*   **Multilingual evaluation:** `LIVEBENCH` is English-heavy (Table 28 shows mostly English news sources). Extending to other languages would enable fair evaluation of multilingual models like `Qwen-VL`.

**Efficient Information Extraction**

The `LIVEBENCH` pipeline uses `Claude-3.5-Sonnet` for information extraction (Section 4.2.1), which adds cost. Research could explore:

*   **Smaller extraction models:** Can 7B-parameter models achieve comparable extraction quality at lower cost?

*   **Extraction quality monitoring:** Automated detection of extraction errors before they propagate through QA generation.

*   **Human-in-the-loop validation:** Optimal balance between automated and human validation for quality assurance.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

**For Model Development Teams**

The three-tool architecture maps naturally to different development phases:

| Development Phase | Recommended Tool | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Daily iteration** | `LMMS-EVAL LITE` | 10× faster evaluation enables multiple runs per day during hyperparameter tuning. |
| **Ablation studies** | `LMMS-EVAL LITE` | High correlation (0.87–0.99) preserves relative rankings for component comparisons. |
| **Pre-release validation** | `LMMS-EVAL` (Full) | Comprehensive coverage across 50+ tasks identifies weaknesses before public release. |
| **Contamination-free claims** | `LIVEBENCH` | Fresh data ensures reported scores reflect generalization, not memorization. |
| **Cross-family comparison** | `LIVEBENCH` + `LMMS-EVAL` | Combine dynamic and static results for comprehensive positioning. |

**For Paper Reviewers and Conference Committees**

This work provides concrete criteria for evaluating benchmarking claims:

*   **Contamination disclosure:** Papers reporting benchmark scores should disclose overlap statistics with training data or use contamination-free benchmarks like `LIVEBENCH`.

*   **Evaluation protocol specification:** Papers should specify which evaluation setup was used (PPL-based vs. generation-based, chat template, etc.) to enable reproducibility.

*   **Multiple benchmark types:** Strong claims should be supported by both static benchmarks (for comparability with prior work) and dynamic benchmarks (for contamination resistance).

**For Leaderboard Maintainers**

Existing leaderboards can adopt insights from this work:

*   **Tiered evaluation:** Offer both `LITE` (for rapid submission feedback) and `LIVEBENCH` (for final rankings) tracks.

*   **Contamination flags:** Display overlap statistics alongside scores to help users interpret results appropriately.

*   **Standardized protocols:** Adopt unified evaluation setups to ensure cross-model comparability.

**For Industry Model Selection**

Organizations selecting LMMs for deployment can use these tools to:

*   **Assess real-world robustness:** `LIVEBENCH` scores better predict performance on novel, unseen content than static benchmarks.

*   **Identify domain weaknesses:** The 50+ task coverage in `LMMS-EVAL` reveals specific capability gaps (e.g., OCR, reasoning, hallucination) relevant to use cases.

*   **Evaluate cost-performance trade-offs:** `LITE` enables rapid evaluation of multiple candidate models before committing to full assessment.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

**Getting Started with LMMS-EVAL**

The authors open-source the codebase and maintain leaderboards at GitHub and LiveBench (Abstract). To integrate:

1.  **Installation:** Clone the repository and install dependencies. The framework follows `LM-EVAL-HARNESS` conventions, so users familiar with that tool will find similar command structures.

2.  **Model integration:** Implement the model's inference logic following the unified interface. For open-source models, this typically involves loading weights and defining a forward pass. For API models, implement the API call wrapper.

3.  **Dataset selection:** Choose datasets relevant to your use case from the 50+ available (Table 25, Appendix F). For general evaluation, start with the `LITE` subset (Table 6, Appendix D.2).

4.  **Run evaluation:** Execute the unified evaluation command. The framework handles preprocessing, inference, and scoring automatically.

5.  **Interpret results:** Review detailed logs containing score breakdowns per dataset. Compare against reported numbers in Table 1 and Table 3 for sanity checks.

**Computational Requirements**

Based on the experimental setup (Section 5.1):

*   **`LMMS-EVAL LITE`:** Can run on single GPU for models up to 13B parameters. The 9,134 instances complete in hours rather than days.

*   **`LMMS-EVAL` (Full):** Requires 8×A100 GPUs for efficient parallel evaluation of the 90,223 instances. Larger models (72B+) need pipeline parallelism.

*   **`LIVEBENCH`:** Evaluation cost depends on judge model API pricing. The 100–300 questions per month are manageable for most research budgets.

**When to Prefer Each Tool**

| Scenario | Recommended Tool | Why |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Training ablation studies** | `LMMS-EVAL LITE` | Fast feedback preserves iteration speed. Correlation >0.87 ensures reliable signals. |
| **Paper submission claims** | `LMMS-EVAL` (Full) + `LIVEBENCH` | Full suite enables comparison with prior work; `LIVEBENCH` demonstrates contamination resistance. |
| **Closed-source model comparison** | `LIVEBENCH` | API models can be evaluated without training data access; fresh data ensures fair comparison. |
| **Domain-specific evaluation** | `LMMS-EVAL` (selective datasets) | Choose relevant datasets from 50+ tasks rather than running all benchmarks. |
| **Resource-constrained settings** | `LMMS-EVAL LITE` | 10× cost reduction makes evaluation feasible on limited hardware. |
| **Contamination audit** | Contamination detection tools (Section 4.1) | Quantify overlap before claiming benchmark results. Requires training data access. |

**Known Integration Pitfalls**

*   **Chat template mismatches:** Instruction-tuned models require their specific chat templates. Using generic prompts can degrade performance significantly (Section 2.1).

*   **Metric normalization:** Different benchmarks use different metrics (accuracy, CIDEr, ANLS, F1). Use the provided normalization when aggregating scores (Section 3.4.2).

*   **MME benchmark:** Do not use the lite version for `MME` — correlation is too low. Retain full version (Section 3.4.2).

*   **Judge model selection:** `LIVEBENCH` defaults to `GPT-4o` as judge. For critical evaluations, cross-validate with `Claude-3.5-Sonnet` or `Gemini-1.5 Pro` to check for judge bias (Appendix E.3).

*   **Temporal alignment:** `LIVEBENCH` data is month-specific. Ensure model training cutoffs predate the benchmark month for valid contamination-free claims.

### 7.5 Long-term Vision for LMM Evaluation

This work establishes infrastructure and methodology that could shape evaluation practices for years. The long-term vision includes:

*   **Continuous benchmarking:** Dynamic benchmarks like `LIVEBENCH` become standard, with monthly or weekly updates tracking model progress over time.

*   **Contamination auditing as norm:** Model releases include overlap statistics with common benchmarks, similar to training data cutoff disclosures in LLM papers.

*   **Standardized evaluation protocols:** The community converges on unified evaluation setups (chat templates, scoring logic, metrics) enabling direct cross-paper comparisons.

*   **Domain-specific extensions:** Specialized evaluation suites emerge for medical, legal, scientific, and other high-stakes domains, building on the `LMMS-EVAL` framework.

*   **Training-data-free contamination detection:** New methods enable contamination auditing without requiring access to proprietary training corpora.

By providing practical tools while acknowledging fundamental limitations, this paper sets a foundation for more honest, transparent, and useful evaluation practices. The trilemma framework reminds researchers that evaluation is inherently about trade-offs — the goal is not to eliminate trade-offs but to make them explicit and manageable.
