## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces the Trinity family of open-weight sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) language models, headlined by Trinity Large with 400B total parameters and 13B activated per token trained on 17 trillion tokens. To solve critical training instability issues at this scale, the authors propose Soft-clamped Momentum Expert Bias Updates (SMEBU) for load balancing and employ the Muon optimizer, resulting in zero loss spikes throughout pre-training. These models matter because they offer efficient, auditable infrastructure for enterprise deployment while achieving competitive performance, such as an 82.58 score on MMLU for the base model.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of the Trinity family, we must first examine the shifting landscape of how large language models (LLMs) are deployed and the technical bottlenecks that arise at the frontier of scale. This paper does not exist in a vacuum; it responds to specific pressures in model architecture, deployment requirements, and training stability.

### The Shift from Chatbots to Agents
Historically, LLMs were optimized for single-turn or short-multi-turn chat interactions. However, the industry is rapidly transitioning toward using models as **general-purpose components for software systems** (Section 1). This includes workflows where models act as long-running agents, execute tools, manage retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), and handle document understanding. 

This shift introduces two critical demands that standard dense models struggle to meet:
1.  **Long-Context Management:** Agents often need to reason over tens to hundreds of thousands of tokens of input and output history. The paper notes that reasoning models now enable "inference-time compute scaling," where models generate intermediate thoughts before answering (Section 1).
2.  **Inference Efficiency:** As contexts grow larger and workflows become more complex, the cost of running models increases linearly with sequence length. For enterprise adoption, latency and throughput become as important as raw accuracy.

### The Efficiency Imperative: Why Sparse MoE?
To address the efficiency bottleneck, the community has turned to **Sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)** architectures. In a dense model, every parameter is activated for every token. In an MoE model, only a subset of parameters (the "experts") is active per token. 

The Trinity report positions MoE as a primary method to "scale up their largest models while being much more efficient and economical to train" (Section 1). The efficiency gain comes from the sparsity ratio. For Trinity Large, the model has $400\text{B}$ total parameters, but only $13\text{B}$ are activated per token. This means the model carries the knowledge capacity of a $400\text{B}$ parameter dense model but incurs the compute cost of roughly a $13\text{B}$ model during inference. 

However, simply adopting MoE is not sufficient. Prior approaches in the field have utilized modified attention designs, such as linear attention variants or sparse attention variants, to scale context without prohibitive cost (Section 1). Trinity positions itself within this ecosystem by combining MoE with **interleaved local and global attention**, a design choice informed by prior work (e.g., Yang et al. [2025]) to balance efficiency with context comprehension.

### The Enterprise Constraint: Open Weights and Auditability
Beyond raw performance, the paper highlights a often-overlooked gap in the frontier model landscape: **deployment sovereignty**. While closed-source models offer high performance, enterprise deployments frequently require:
*   **Clarity about data provenance and licensing.**
*   **Jurisdictional controls.**
*   **The ability to host and adapt models within environments completely owned by the organizations.**

The authors argue that "real-world adoption extend[s] beyond raw benchmark performance" (Section 1). There is a specific need for "open-weight foundations that can be owned and operated without reliance on opaque third-party checkpoints." This motivates the Trinity family's release as open-weight models, distinguishing them from many comparable frontier MoE models that remain closed or have restrictive licenses.

### The Technical Gap: Training Instability at Scale
Perhaps the most critical technical motivation for this work lies in the difficulty of training sparse models at this scale. While MoE offers efficiency, it introduces significant optimization challenges. The authors reveal in Section 6 that initial training runs for Trinity Large faced severe instability:
> "Routing behavior drifted and expert load became increasingly uneven, eventually resulting in collapsed experts. MaxVio... would remain stable and then experience a sudden climb as the experts collapsed."

This highlights a specific gap in prior load-balancing strategies. The standard **auxiliary-loss-free load balancing** method (Wang et al. [2024a]), which Trinity initially adopted for smaller models, relies on a sign-based update rule for expert biases. The authors identify a theoretical flaw in this approach when scaling up:
> "Under the assumption that the ideal expert bias value is a fixed value, we note that the standard aux-free load balancing cannot precisely converge on that value, as each local update under the $\text{sign}(\cdot)$ operator is always $\pm\lambda$." (Section 2.3)

Because the update step is discrete ($\pm\lambda$), the optimization process oscillates near the minimum rather than settling, causing instability as the number of experts increases. This oscillation is acceptable at smaller scales (like Trinity Nano or Mini) but becomes catastrophic at the $400\text{B}$ parameter scale. This gap motivates the introduction of their new balancing strategy, **Soft-clamped Momentum Expert Bias Updates (SMEBU)**, which replaces the discrete sign function with a continuous $\tanh$ function to allow convergence.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work
The Trinity family positions itself as a bridge between research-scale efficiency and enterprise-ready deployment. 
*   **Vs. Dense Models:** Trinity offers significantly higher parameter capacity per unit of compute via sparsity ($400\text{B}$ total vs. $13\text{B}$ active).
*   **Vs. Closed MoE Models:** Trinity provides open weights for auditability, contrasting with proprietary frontier models.
*   **Vs. Prior Open MoE:** Trinity introduces specific stability innovations (SMEBU, Muon optimizer) to solve the "collapse" issues that plague large-scale MoE training, as evidenced by their claim of completing training with "zero loss spikes" (Abstract).

The development of Trinity Nano and Mini serves as a "scaling ladder" to validate the infrastructure and data pipeline before committing resources to Trinity Large (Section 1). This iterative approach underscores the paper's focus on reproducibility and stability in an era where training runs are increasingly fragile as they scale.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is an architecture and training methodology paper that presents a complete system for building stable, efficient sparse Mixture-of-Experts language models at the 400B parameter scale. The core idea is that training stability at extreme scale requires coordinated innovations across tokenization, attention design, expert routing, normalization, and data loading — not just a single breakthrough.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The Trinity system is a family of three decoder-only transformer language models (Nano, Mini, and Large) that use sparse Mixture-of-Experts layers to achieve high parameter capacity while activating only a fraction of parameters per token. The system solves the problem of training instability that occurs when scaling MoE models to hundreds of billions of parameters by introducing a new load-balancing algorithm (SMEBU), employing the Muon optimizer, and redesigning the data loading pipeline to reduce batch-level noise.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

Think of the Trinity model as a processing pipeline with five major stages. First, raw text enters the **tokenizer**, which converts it into discrete token IDs using a 200,000-vocabulary BPE scheme with special handling for numbers and non-English scripts. Second, token embeddings are projected into the model dimension and enter the **transformer stack**, which consists of 60 layers for Trinity Large. Third, each transformer layer contains an **attention module** that uses interleaved local and global attention (three local layers with sliding windows followed by one global layer) with grouped-query attention to reduce memory. Fourth, most layers contain a **Mixture-of-Experts module** where each token is routed to 4 out of 256 possible expert networks, plus one shared expert that always activates. Fifth, **normalization layers** wrap each sub-module using depth-scaled sandwich norm to stabilize gradients through the full network depth. The output then passes through a final RMSNorm before the language modeling head produces token predictions.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **Tokenizer design** comes first because it determines how raw text becomes model input, and Trinity's pretokenization pipeline includes novel handling for numbers and multilingual text that directly affects downstream performance.
- **Attention mechanism** follows because it is the core computational engine of each transformer layer, and Trinity's interleaved local/global pattern is fundamental to its long-context efficiency.
- **Mixture-of-Experts routing** comes next as this is where Trinity's primary innovation (SMEBU) lives, and understanding the routing math is essential to grasping why training stability improved.
- **Normalization and initialization** are covered together because they work as a coordinated system to prevent gradient explosion or vanishing through 60 layers.
- **Training infrastructure and data pipeline** concludes the technical breakdown because these systems enable the architecture to actually train at scale without collapsing.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**This paper presents a complete system design for stable, efficient MoE training at the 400B parameter scale, where every component from tokenization to data loading is engineered to prevent the training instabilities that plague large sparse models.**

#### Tokenizer and Pretokenization Pipeline

The Trinity tokenizer uses a 200,000-token Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) vocabulary trained on approximately 48GB (roughly 10 billion tokens) of data drawn primarily from the Trinity Nano and Mini pretraining corpus, supplemented with multilingual text from C4 non-English splits and instruction-following data (Section 2.1). The vocabulary size was selected after empirical comparison across sizes, and because BPE merges are deterministic and greedy, the authors could train the full 200k vocabulary and derive smaller variants by truncating by merge rank without retraining (Section 2.1.2). Table 1 shows that this larger vocabulary achieves the strongest compression among standard tokenizers on English (4.84 bytes-per-token on C4-en) and French (3.98 B/T), though CJK compression trails DeepSeek V3 and Qwen 3 due to timing constraints in tokenizer training relative to the final Trinity Large corpus.

The pretokenization pipeline employs a multi-stage process inspired by DeepSeek V3 but with critical extensions for numerical tokenization and multilingual coverage (Section 2.1.1). The first stage performs **digit isolation and place-aligned chunking**, where contiguous digit runs are separated from surrounding text and split into right-aligned groups of three with a leading group of one to two digits as needed — for example, `1234567` becomes `1|234|567`. This design ensures each three-digit token consistently represents a fixed place value (ones-tens-hundreds, thousands, etc.), following findings that place-aligned digit tokenization materially improves arithmetic performance in language models. The authors initially adopted a zero-width lookahead regex pattern but discovered during early training that this exhibited catastrophic backtracking on documents with long uninterrupted digit strings, causing tokenization of individual samples to spike to minutes. They addressed this by replacing the single regex with a three-stage pipeline that first caps digit runs at 510 characters, then peels off a leading group of 1–2 digits, and finally chunks the remainder into exact groups of three — producing identical splits on all inputs up to 510 digits while avoiding pathological backtracking behavior.

The second stage performs **script-aware isolation**, extending DeepSeek V3's CJK isolation to additionally cover Thai, Lao, Khmer, Myanmar, and Korean hangul/jamo — all scripts that, like CJK, lack whitespace-delimited word boundaries. Isolating these script runs ensures BPE learns merges within each script independently rather than forming cross-script tokens. The third stage handles word and punctuation splitting using the DeepSeek V3 main text regex directly, which handles leading-space word attachment, punctuation-prefixed tokens (covering contractions, code sigils, and markup), and whitespace/newline normalization. The fourth stage applies a byte-level fallback encoding layer that ensures full coverage of any byte sequence without unknown tokens.

The authors also evaluated the SuperBPE approach, which trains an initial vocabulary with standard whitespace splitting, truncates it, then resumes training without the whitespace constraint to learn multi-word tokens (Section 2.1.2). While their SuperBPE variant achieved substantially better compression — particularly on English text (approximately 29% fewer tokens) and reasoning traces (approximately 27% fewer tokens) — they were unable to reproduce a corresponding improvement in downstream model performance at their experimental scale, so they proceeded with standard BPE at 200k vocabulary.

#### Attention Mechanism Design

The Trinity attention mechanism combines multiple modifications to standard Multi-Head Attention that have been shown effective in previous work, specifically integrating grouped-query attention (GQA), QK-normalization, gated attention, and a 3:1 local/global layer pattern (Section 2.2). Let $d$ denote the model dimension, $h_q$ the number of query heads, $h_{kv}$ the number of key/value heads, and $d_h = d/h_q$ the per-query-head dimension. Let $x_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$ be the input at token position $t$ in a given attention layer.

**QK-normalization** is applied to queries and keys before scaled dot-product attention primarily for training stability reasons, especially due to the use of the Muon optimizer (Section 2.2). Prior work has identified growing maximum attention logit values to be more of a concern when using Muon as opposed to AdamW, and GLM-4.5 Team et al. [2025] report that QK-Norm effectively stabilizes the range of maximum attention logit values through a full training run. The queries, keys, and values are computed as linear projections of the input, then RMSNorm is applied to queries and keys:

$$q'_{t,i} = W^Q_i x_t, \quad i \in \{1, \ldots, h_q\}$$

$$k'_{t,j} = W^K_j x_t, \quad j \in \{1, \ldots, h_{kv}\}$$

$$v'_{t,j} = W^V_j x_t, \quad j \in \{1, \ldots, h_{kv}\}$$

$$q_{t,i} = \text{RMSNorm}(q'_{t,i})$$

$$k_{t,j} = \text{RMSNorm}(k'_{t,j})$$

where $W^Q_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h \times d}$ and $W^K_j, W^V_j \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h \times d}$.

**The local/global attention pattern** is perhaps the most architecturally significant choice for long-context efficiency (Section 2.2). All Trinity models use a 3:1 ratio of local to global attention: they have three local attention layers with sliding-window attention (SWA) and Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE), followed by one global layer without positional embeddings (NoPE), with this structure repeating for the full depth of the model. For Trinity Large, the sliding-window size for local layers is set to 4096, and the pre-training sequence length is 8192. This configuration was chosen primarily for effective long-context performance as well as for large efficiency gains at longer sequence lengths. The combination of this scheme and gated attention allowed the model to recover performance in a comparatively lower number of steps when training at longer sequence lengths, and also resulted in observed length extrapolation for Trinity Large.

The queries and keys used in the scaled dot-product attention operation differ between local and global layers:

$$\hat{q}_{t,i} = \begin{cases} \text{RoPE}(q_{t,i}), & \text{local layer} \\ q_{t,i}, & \text{global (NoPE) layer} \end{cases}$$

$$\hat{k}_{t,j} = \begin{cases} \text{RoPE}(k_{t,j}), & \text{local layer} \\ k_{t,j}, & \text{global (NoPE) layer} \end{cases}$$

with valid attention positions defined as:

$$S_t = \begin{cases} \{ s \mid \max(1, t - w + 1) \leq s \leq t \}, & \text{local layer} \\ \{ s \mid 1 \leq s \leq t \}, & \text{global layer} \end{cases}$$

where $w$ is the local window size. This means local layers can only attend to tokens within a sliding window of size $w$ ending at the current position, while global layers can attend to all previous tokens but without positional information.

**Grouped-query attention (GQA)** maps each query head $i$ to a key/value head index $j(i) = \lceil i \cdot h_{kv} / h_q \rceil$, such that multiple query heads share the same key/value head (Section 2.2). For Trinity Large, there are 48 query heads and 8 KV heads, meaning each KV head is shared by 6 query heads. This has been shown to roughly match the performance of full multi-head attention while drastically reducing KV-cache size, which is critical for inference efficiency at long context lengths. For each query head $i$, attention is computed over keys/values from the shared KV head $j(i)$:

$$\alpha_{t,i,s} = \text{Softmax}_{s \in S_t} \left( \frac{\hat{q}_{t,i}^\top \hat{k}_{s,j(i)}}{\sqrt{d_h}} \right)$$

$$o^{\text{sdpa}}_{t,i} = \sum_{s \in S_t} \alpha_{t,i,s} v_{s,j(i)}$$

**Gated attention** applies an elementwise gating to the scaled dot-product attention output before the output linear projection, following a configuration from Qiu et al. [2025] (Section 2.2). The gate is computed as:

$$g_t = \sigma(W^G x_t)$$

$$g_{t,i} = \text{split}_{h_q}(g_t)_i$$

$$e_{t,i} = o^{\text{sdpa}}_{t,i} \odot g_{t,i}$$

$$u_t = W^O [e_{t,1}; e_{t,2}; \ldots; e_{t,h_q}]$$

where $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the sigmoid function, $\text{split}_{h_q}(\cdot)$ partitions $g_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$ into $h_q$ contiguous vectors in $\mathbb{R}^{d_h}$, and $W^G \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$, $W^O \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ are the gate and output projections respectively. In Qiu et al. [2025], gated attention was identified to reduce attention sinks, reduce overly large activations, improve performance on evaluations, and improve long-sequence generalization. Furthermore, gating appears to stabilize training and reduce the occurrence of loss spikes during the training process, which was a key motivation for Trinity's adoption.

#### Mixture-of-Experts Formulation and Routing

The Trinity MoE layers follow the DeepSeekMoE design with fine-grained routed experts and an always-active shared expert, using the SwiGLU activation function as the nonlinearity (Section 2.3). For Trinity Large, the authors opted to use coarser-grained experts to facilitate better throughput, and they replace the first 6 MoE layers with dense layers to stabilize early representations. The MoE output $h'_t$ for token $t$ is computed as:

$$h'_t = u_t + \sum_{i=1}^{N_s} \text{FFN}^{(s)}_i(u_t) + \sum_{i=1}^{N_r} g_{i,t} \text{FFN}^{(r)}_i(u_t)$$

where $u_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$ denotes the MoE input, $\text{FFN}^{(s)}_i(\cdot)$ denotes the $i$-th shared expert, $\text{FFN}^{(r)}_i(\cdot)$ denotes the $i$-th routed expert, $N_s$ is the number of shared experts (always 1), and $N_r$ is the number of routed experts (256 for Trinity Large).

**Sigmoid routing** is used instead of softmax-based routing, which allows for more stable router logits (Section 2.3). The routing scores are normalized before using them for gating, and the gating scores are also applied to the output of each expert. Let $e_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ be the router vector for routed expert $i$:

$$s_{i,t} = \sigma(u_t^\top e_i), \quad i \in \{1, \ldots, N_r\}$$

The Top-K experts are selected using the sum of routing scores and expert bias, but the gating scores for expert outputs use the routing scores without expert bias (since the expert bias is updated in a decoupled way):

$$g'_{i,t} = \begin{cases} s_{i,t}, & s_{i,t} + b_i \in \text{TopK}(\{s_{j,t} + b_j\}_{j=1}^{N_r}, K_r) \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

$$g_{i,t} = \frac{g'_{i,t}}{\sum_{j=1}^{N_r} g'_{j,t}}$$

where $b_i$ is the expert bias and $g_{i,t}$ are the gating scores derived from the router scores $s_{i,t}$. For Trinity Large, $K_r = 4$ experts are activated per token.

**The critical innovation: Soft-clamped Momentum Expert Bias Updates (SMEBU)** replaces the standard aux-loss-free load balancing for Trinity Large to address training instability (Section 2.3). For Trinity Mini and Nano, the authors used the standard formulation with an additional re-centering of the expert bias updates. The standard approach maintains a bias vector $b = [b_1, \ldots, b_{N_r}]$ that is updated in a decoupled fashion. Let $n_i$ be the number of tokens routed to expert $i$ in the current step, and $\bar{n}$ the mean load:

$$\bar{n} = \frac{1}{N_r} \sum_{i=1}^{N_r} n_i$$

$$\Delta b_i = \gamma \cdot \text{sign}(\bar{n} - n_i)$$

$$b_i = b_i + \Delta b_i$$

$$b_i = b_i - \frac{1}{N_r} \sum_{j=1}^{N_r} b_j$$

where $\gamma$ is the bias update speed.

The authors identified a fundamental flaw in this approach when scaling up. Under the assumption that the ideal expert bias value is a fixed value, the standard aux-free load balancing cannot precisely converge on that value, as each local update under the $\text{sign}(\cdot)$ operator is always $\pm\lambda$ (Section 2.3). As the total number of experts increases, the per-layer norm of the bias step also increases. Viewing this as an optimization process, as the system nears its local minima, the update step is biased towards oscillation and can never fully settle. The authors hypothesize that this is a cause of instability for MoE training with the standard aux-loss-free objective.

SMEBU alleviates this using $\tanh$ as a continuous relaxation of the discrete update that can converge to 0 (Section 2.3). First, the normalized per-expert violation is computed to make the update scale-independent of sequence-length and batch size, then $\tanh$ soft-clamping is applied:

$$v_i = \frac{\bar{n} - n_i}{\bar{n}}$$

$$\tilde{v}_i = \tanh(\kappa v_i)$$

where $\kappa$ is a tunable scale. The update is centered around zero, and a momentum buffer is used to smooth expert bias updates over time:

$$\Delta b_i = \lambda \tilde{v}_i$$

$$\Delta b_i = \Delta b_i - \frac{1}{N_r} \sum_{j=1}^{N_r} \Delta b_j$$

$$m_i = \beta m_i + (1 - \beta) \Delta b_i$$

$$b_i = b_i + m_i$$

where $\lambda$ is the load-balance learning rate, $\beta$ is the momentum factor, and $m = [m_1, \ldots, m_{N_r}]$ is the maintained momentum buffer. For Trinity Large, the authors use SMEBU with $\lambda = 5 \times 10^{-4}$, $\beta = 0.5$, and $\kappa = 2$.

The authors believe that bounded updates are important to maintaining network stability; preliminary tests using a linear, unclamped update quickly reduced MaxVio early in training but resulted in instability later. Additionally, consistent with the hypothesis that behavior near convergence is one of the causes of instability in MoE aux-loss-free load balancing, and that noise near a local minima should be roughly decorrelated over time, momentum is introduced as a form of noise dampening — analogous to momentum SGD reducing variance in noisy gradient updates.

**Sequence-wise auxiliary loss** is used in addition to aux-loss-free balancing as a complementary load balance loss to promote balance within a sequence (Section 2.3). This follows the formulation from DeepSeek-AI et al. [2025a]:

$$L_{\text{Bal}} = \alpha \sum_{i=1}^{N_r} f_i P_i$$

$$f_i = \frac{N_r}{K_r T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbb{1}(s_{i,t} + b_i \in \text{TopK}(\{s_{j,t} + b_j\}_{j=1}^{N_r}, K_r))$$

$$\tilde{s}_{i,t} = \frac{s_{i,t}}{\sum_{j=1}^{N_r} s_{j,t}}$$

$$P_i = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \tilde{s}_{i,t}$$

where $\alpha$ is a small coefficient, $T$ is the sequence length, and $\mathbb{1}(\cdot)$ is the indicator function. For Trinity Large, this was added with weight $1 \times 10^{-4}$ mid-training to further stabilize the run.

#### Normalization and Initialization Strategy

For layer normalization, Trinity uses a simplified **depth-scaled sandwich norm** where both the input and output of each module are normalized (Section 2.4). The formulation is:

$$y_\ell = x_\ell + \text{RMSNorm}^{(2)}_\ell \left( M_\ell \left( \text{RMSNorm}^{(1)}_\ell(x_\ell) \right) \right)$$

where $x_\ell$ and $y_\ell$ are the input/output of layer $\ell$, and $M_\ell(\cdot)$ is the sublayer module (attention, FFN, or MoE). The depth-scaling applies to the RMSNorm gain parameters for the second RMSNorm in each layer. Let $L$ be the total number of layers and $\gamma(\cdot)$ denote the RMSNorm gain:

$$\gamma(\text{RMSNorm}^{(1)}_\ell) = 1$$

$$\gamma(\text{RMSNorm}^{(2)}_\ell) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{L}}$$

This initialization ensures that the contribution of each layer to the residual stream is appropriately scaled by depth, preventing gradient explosion or vanishing through 60 layers. RMSNorm is also applied before the language modeling head: $z = \text{RMSNorm}_{\text{LM}}(h_L)$.

**Parameter initialization** uses a zero-mean truncated normal distribution with width-scaled standard deviation (Section 2.5):

$$\theta \sim \text{TruncNormal}(0, \sigma^2; [-3\sigma, 3\sigma])$$

$$\sigma = \frac{0.5}{\sqrt{d}}$$

where $d$ is the model dimension. This roughly follows findings in Takase et al. [2025], which recommends setting $\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{2}{5d}}$. The authors note that $0.5/\sqrt{d}$ aligns with the initialization scale for DeepSeek-V3, as $0.5/\sqrt{7168} = 0.006$. During the forward pass, the embedding layer's activations are scaled by $\sqrt{d}$, also following Takase et al. [2025]:

$$e_t = \sqrt{d} \cdot E(\text{tok}_t)$$

The authors note that both Grok-1 and Grok-2 model checkpoints on HuggingFace have `embedding_multiplier_scale` set to $\sqrt{d}$, and the first two generations of Gemma models refer to this multiplier as a normalizer in their modeling code.

#### Model Configurations Across the Trinity Family

Table 2 provides the complete model configurations for all three Trinity variants. **Trinity Nano** has 56 transformer layers with 2 initial dense layers, model dimension 1024, FFN intermediate dimension 3072, 8 query heads with per-head dimension 128, 2 KV heads, local window size 2048, pre-training sequence length 4096, 1 shared expert and 128 routed experts per MoE layer with 8 experts activated per token, route scale 2.826, expert size 256, and initialization standard deviation 0.016. With Trinity Nano, the authors aimed to explore the effectiveness of high depth at producing a powerful small language model, following the improved performance of Falcon-H1-1.5B-Deep over Falcon-H1-1.5B.

**Trinity Mini** has 32 transformer layers with 2 initial dense layers, model dimension 2048, FFN intermediate dimension 6144, 32 query heads with per-head dimension 128, 4 KV heads, local window size 2048, pre-training sequence length 4096, 1 shared expert and 128 routed experts per MoE layer with 8 experts activated per token, route scale 2.826, expert size 1024, and initialization standard deviation 0.011.

**Trinity Large** has 60 transformer layers with 6 initial dense layers, model dimension 3072, FFN intermediate dimension 12288, 48 query heads with per-head dimension 128, 8 KV heads, local window size 4096, pre-training sequence length 8192, 1 shared expert and 256 routed experts per MoE layer with 4 experts activated per token, route scale 2.448, expert size 3072, and initialization standard deviation 0.009. For Trinity Large, the authors opted to activate 4 routed experts and make each expert larger, reducing the granularity, due to throughput requirements. They also greatly increased the sparsity to maximize model capacity while keeping a low active parameter count.

#### Training Hyperparameters and Optimizer Choice

All three models use the **Muon optimizer** for hidden layers and the **AdamW optimizer** for embedding and output layers (Section 3.4.2). Unlike Liu et al. [2025b], the authors choose not to rescale the RMS of the Muon updates to match the RMS of the AdamW updates. Instead, they use the learning rate adjustment rule:

$$lr_{\text{adjusted}} = lr \sqrt{\max\left(1, \frac{\text{fanout}}{\text{fanin}}\right)}$$

following Jordan et al. [2024b]. Empirically, they observe that this adjustment enables optimal learning rate transfer when scaling model width for Muon.

**Trinity Nano** uses a linear warmup of 2000 steps, peak learning rates of $1.0 \times 10^{-3}$ for Muon and $3.0 \times 10^{-4}$ for AdamW, global batch size 4096 at sequence length 4096 (increased to 8192 when scaling from 256 to 512 GPUs), linear decay to zero after warmup, and context extension with linear re-warmup to $1/10$ of peak learning rate followed by linear decay. It trains to 256K context for inference at 128K.

**Trinity Mini** uses a linear warmup of 2000 steps, peak learning rates of $1.0 \times 10^{-3}$ for Muon and $2.0 \times 10^{-4}$ for AdamW, global batch size 4096 at sequence length 4096 (increased to 8192 when scaling from 64 to 512 GPUs), linear decay to zero after warmup, and context extension with linear re-warmup to $1/10$ of peak learning rate. It trains to 128K context for inference at 128K.

**Trinity Large** uses a linear warmup of 2000 steps, peak learning rates of $8.0 \times 10^{-4}$ for Muon and $2.0 \times 10^{-4}$ for AdamW, global batch size 12288 at sequence length 8192 (increased to 16384 after crossing 4.9T tokens), cosine decay to $1/10$ of peak learning rate during the decay phase (allowing context extension without re-warming), and continued cosine decay from $1/10$ to $1/20$ of peak for context extension. It trains to 256K context for inference at 512K.

#### Data Curation and the Random Sequential Document Buffer

All pretraining data were curated by DatologyAI (Section 3.1). Trinity Nano and Mini were each trained on the same 10 trillion token mix, and Trinity Large was trained on 17 trillion tokens of a distinct 20 trillion token mix. Both mixes were organized into three phases. The 10 trillion mix was distributed across 7T tokens in phase 1, 1.8T tokens in phase 2, and 1.2T tokens in phase 3. The 20 trillion mix was distributed across 13T tokens in phase 1, 4T tokens in phase 2, and 3T tokens in phase 3. The 17 trillion tokens that Trinity Large was trained on were sampled proportionally from the 20T mix (i.e., a 13:4:3 ratio across the three phases). Each mix was designed to target both general English capabilities as well as programming, STEM, and reasoning skills.

A key component of this curation is **large-scale synthetic data generation** (Section 3.1). DatologyAI generated over 8 trillion tokens of synthetic web, code, and STEM data. For the web, approximately 6.5 trillion tokens of synthetic data were generated using rephrasing approaches that build on top of BeyondWeb. High-quality seed documents were selected from web corpora and then diverse generation strategies were used to rephrase these documents, including format transformation (e.g., converting web content into question–answer pairs), style modification (e.g., enhancing pedagogical tone), and content restructuring to improve information density and accessibility. Similar approaches generated approximately 1 trillion tokens of synthetic multilingual data. Approximately 800 billion tokens of high-quality synthetic code data were also generated using relevant high-quality code files selected for rephrasing and enhancement.

**The Random Sequential Document Buffer (RSDB)** is a critical innovation in the data loading pipeline that addresses batch-level imbalance during training (Section 3.2). The authors were concerned that on-the-fly tokenization could cause longer documents to remain in consecutive batches for too long due to insufficient interleaving. Standard sequential packing continues documents that span multiple times the sequence length into subsequent batch items, but this becomes problematic when document lengths follow a lognormal distribution — long documents introduce unbalanced domain biases at the minibatch level.

The RSDB operates as follows: after tokenizing a document, the entire token sequence is loaded as an entry in the RSDB with a read head initialized at the 0th index. When populating a single sequence buffer within a minibatch, the dataloader randomly samples an index of a document in the RSDB and reads tokens from the document at the position of the read head into the sequence buffer. Document read head positions are updated after every step. If the sequence buffer is full, it is returned; if not, another document index is randomly selected and reading continues until the buffer is full. For runtime efficiency, the internal buffer size is set to twice the user-specified buffer value, and the buffer is refilled to this larger value as soon as it reaches the user-specified value. For phase 3 of Trinity Large training, the authors used an RSDB with a buffer size of 8192 per GPU (user-specified 4096), with 4 workers per GPU.

To quantify batch imbalance during training, the authors introduce a metric called **Batch Heterogeneity (BatchHet)**, defined as the difference between the maximum microbatch loss and the average loss for each training step:

$$\text{BatchHet}_t = \max_i L^{(t)}_i - \frac{1}{M} \sum_{i=1}^{M} L^{(t)}_i$$

where $L^{(t)}_i$ is the loss of microbatch $i$ at training step $t$, and $\bar{L}^{(t)}$ is the mean loss across all $M$ microbatch es at that step. In small-scale internal experiments, enabling RSDB reduced BatchHet by 46% and improved loss over a sequential packing baseline. The gradient norm was markedly stable, with a kurtosis of 14.6 for the grad norm of the RSDB-enabled network, as opposed to the kurtosis of 187 of the baseline's grad norm. During Trinity Large phase 3 training, BatchHet reduced by a factor of 4.23x and step-to-step variance reduced by a factor of 2.4x (see Figure 1).

#### Infrastructure and Parallelism Strategy

Trinity was trained on GPU clusters managed by Prime Intellect using a modified version of TorchTitan as the training framework (Section 3.3). Trinity Nano and Mini were trained on clusters of 512 H200 GPUs, and Trinity Large was trained on a cluster of 2048 B300 GPUs. For Trinity Nano and Mini, the authors use a Hybrid Sharded Data Parallel (HSDP) configuration, where there are multiple replicas of the model and Fully-Sharded Data Parallel (FSDP) is used within the replica group. For Trinity Large, they additionally employ Expert Parallelism (EP) within a GPU node for efficient Mixture-of-Experts layers. For all models, the FSDP group size is set to 128. For Trinity Large, the EP group size is set to 8. For context extension of Trinity Large, a context parallelism degree of 4 is used.

The authors use a modification of the Muon implementation in the Dion repository for efficient distributed Muon (Section 3.3). They orthogonalize the gradient for the experts in a batched fashion, without flattening the gradient tensor prior to orthogonalization. This simplifies the expert-parallel implementation since gradients do not need to be gathered across the expert-parallel group. For efficient fused cross-entropy and z-loss, kernels from the Liger Kernels project are used. For larger memory savings during context extension and supervised fine-tuning, Cut Cross-Entropy is used.

#### Context Extension Methodology

The Trinity architecture utilizes interleaved local/global attention, with the local attention layers pre-trained at a sliding window size set to half of the context window of global layers (Section 3.5). In initial context extension experiments, the authors compared adjusting the local layers (with different values for the RoPE base frequency $\theta$) along with the global layers against adjusting only the global layers. Adjusting only the global layers resulted in much quicker loss recovery, aligning with findings in Yang et al. [2025], and further suggesting that local and global layers learn complementary roles. Additionally, not extending the window size of the local layers allows for more efficient inference, so the authors choose to follow this approach.

The authors used a rapid iterative approach for testing context extension effectiveness, using the Multi-Key Needle-in-a-haystack (MK-NIAH) task from RULER at the target context length as a proxy metric. When extending Trinity Nano's context window, training at a sequence length of 128K (the target context window) yielded an MK-NIAH (@128K) score of 0.38, whereas training at a sequence length of 256K yielded an MK-NIAH (@128K) score of 0.548. Iterating on dataset and hyperparameter selection yielded a final MK-NIAH (@128K) score of 0.864 for Trinity Nano. Trinity Mini, trained at 128K sequence length, achieved a final MK-NIAH (@128K) score of 0.888.

Trinity Large was trained at a sequence length of 256K, targeting a final context window size of 256K, and achieved an MK-NIAH (@256K) score of 0.994 (Section 3.5). Upon evaluating at 512K, it achieved an MK-NIAH (@512K) score of 0.976, despite not being trained at that sequence length. Even evaluating at 1M, it achieved an MK-NIAH (@1M) score of 0.42, suggesting that later versions could push to strong performance at 1M context windows. The authors did not find improvements from progressively increasing the context window size as opposed to training directly at the longest sequence length from the final pre-trained checkpoint.

The long-context extension dataset comprises approximately 117 billion tokens across 35.6 million documents (Section 3.5). The authors blend samples from the original pretraining distribution with targeted long-context sources. For the pretraining component, they apply a length-biased sampling strategy across all three phases: sampling probabilities scale with document length, from a 1% floor up to 90% for documents reaching 128K tokens. The mix incorporates OCR-derived PDF data (including olmOCR and FinePDF-edu), regenerated ProLong datasets at full sequence length, instruction-style data (FLAN, math, and code), AutoMathText, and curated arXiv and textbook sources.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The Trinity report offers more than just a set of model weights; it provides a detailed case study on the fragility of training sparse models at the 400B parameter scale. While individual components like MoE or local attention exist in prior work, the novel contributions here lie in the specific mechanisms used to stabilize training when these components are pushed to their limits. The following insights distinguish between incremental engineering improvements and fundamental shifts in how we approach large-scale stability.

### 4.1 Optimization Dynamics in Load Balancing (SMEBU)

The most significant algorithmic contribution is **Soft-clamped Momentum Expert Bias Updates (SMEBU)**, introduced to solve a specific convergence failure in standard MoE load balancing.

*   **The Prior Limitation:** Standard auxiliary-loss-free load balancing (Wang et al. [2024a]) uses a discrete sign function ($\text{sign}(\bar{n} - n_i)$) to update expert biases. As detailed in Section 2.3, this creates a fundamental optimization barrier: the update step is always $\pm\lambda$, meaning the system cannot settle at a precise equilibrium. Instead, it oscillates around the minimum. At smaller scales (like Trinity Nano), this noise is tolerable. However, at 400B parameters, this oscillation accumulates, causing routing behavior to drift and experts to collapse (Section 6).
*   **The Innovation:** SMEBU replaces the discrete sign function with a continuous $\tanh$ function. This is not merely a smoothing technique; it fundamentally changes the optimization landscape from a "limit cycle" (where the bias bounces back and forth) to a convergent trajectory (where the update magnitude shrinks as the error shrinks).
*   **Why It Matters:** This insight reveals that **load balancing is an optimization problem, not just a heuristic constraint.** By adding momentum ($\beta = 0.5$) and soft-clamping, the authors treat expert bias like any other trainable parameter that requires variance reduction near convergence. This allowed Trinity Large to maintain balanced expert utilization throughout the 17 trillion token training run, a feat not guaranteed by prior aux-loss-free methods at this scale.

### 4.2 Data Loading as a Stability Lever (RSDB)

A less obvious but critical innovation is the **Random Sequential Document Buffer (RSDB)**, which reframes data loading from a throughput concern to a stability concern.

*   **The Prior Limitation:** Standard sequential packing (used in frameworks like TorchTitan) fills batches by continuing documents across sequence boundaries. The authors identify that because document lengths follow a lognormal distribution, this creates **batch-level domain imbalance**. For example, a single long code document might dominate several consecutive microbatches, causing the model to see a skewed data distribution step-to-step.
*   **The Innovation:** RSDB decouples document continuity from batch composition. By buffering tokenized documents and randomly sampling read heads during batch construction, the dataloader ensures that each microbatch is a statistically representative sample of the corpus, rather than a contiguous chunk of a few long files.
*   **Why It Matters:** The paper introduces a new metric, **Batch Heterogeneity (BatchHet)**, to quantify this noise. The insight here is empirical but profound: **data loading noise correlates directly with gradient instability.**
    > "Enabling RSDB reduced BatchHet by 46%... the gradient norm was markedly stable, with a kurtosis of 14.6 for the grad norm of the RSDB-enabled network, as opposed to the kurtosis of 187 of the baseline's grad norm." (Section 3.2)
    
    This suggests that training instability in large models may not always originate from the architecture or optimizer, but from high-variance data streams that force the model to constantly "unlearn" batch-specific biases.

### 4.3 The Necessity of Co-Design for Stability

Perhaps the most valuable lesson from the Trinity report is the demonstration that stability at the 400B scale requires **simultaneous intervention across multiple system layers.**

*   **The Failure of Incrementalism:** In Section 6, the authors recount their debugging process during Trinity Large's training. When initial runs faced expert collapse, they attempted targeted fixes one by one: lowering the learning rate, removing weight decay from norms, and increasing normalization $\epsilon$. None of these incremental changes prevented recurrence.
*   **The Innovation:** Stability was only achieved when **six changes were applied at once**:
    1.  Adopting SMEBU (routing).
    2.  Disabling MXFP8 kernels (precision).
    3.  Adding z-loss (logit stability).
    4.  Adding sequence-wise aux loss (load balancing).
    5.  Increasing dense layers from 3 to 6 (representation stability).
    6.  Adopting intra-document masking (objective noise reduction).
*   **Why It Matters:** This highlights a critical insight for the field: **large-scale MoE training is a non-linear system.** A fix that works in isolation (e.g., better load balancing) may fail if the underlying data pipeline (RSDB) or numerical precision (BF16 vs. FP8) introduces conflicting noise sources. The success of Trinity Large (evidenced by the smooth loss curve in **Figure 1**) is not due to a single breakthrough, but to the recognition that optimizer, architecture, routing, and data pipeline must be co-designed to suppress instability modes that only emerge at extreme scale.

### 4.4 Context Extension via Architectural Separation

Finally, the report offers a specific empirical insight regarding long-context scaling in hybrid attention architectures.

*   **The Finding:** When extending context, the authors tested adjusting both local and global layers versus adjusting only global layers. They found that **adjusting only the global layers resulted in much quicker loss recovery** (Section 3.5).
*   **Why It Matters:** This validates the hypothesis that local and global layers learn complementary roles. Local layers (with RoPE and sliding windows) specialize in fine-grained, recent context, while global layers (NoPE) handle long-range dependencies. By freezing the local window size during extension, the model retains its efficiency and local reasoning capabilities while only adapting the global pathway for long-range retrieval. This allowed Trinity Large to achieve an MK-NIAH score of 0.976 at 512K context, despite being trained at 256K, suggesting that **architectural inductive biases can enable extrapolation beyond training limits** without full retraining.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the Trinity team validated their architectural and training innovations. The evaluation strategy spans three distinct dimensions: **capability benchmarks** (does the model perform well on standard tasks?), **inference efficiency** (does the sparse architecture deliver promised speed gains?), and **training stability metrics** (did the stability innovations actually work?). Understanding the methodology and limitations here is crucial for interpreting the reported results.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Benchmark Suite

The Trinity team employs a standard evaluation framework common to frontier model reports, but with important distinctions in what they chose to measure and what they did not.

**Base Model Evaluation (Table 3):**
Trinity Large Base was evaluated across ten benchmarks spanning four capability domains:

| Domain | Benchmarks | Shot Configuration |
|--------|------------|-------------------|
| Coding/Math | MBPP+, Minerva MATH500 | Not specified (likely 0-shot) |
| Commonsense | HellaSwag, WinoGrande | 5-shot |
| Knowledge | MMLU, MMLU-Pro, TriviaQA, ARC Challenge | 5-shot (ARC: 0-shot) |
| Reasoning | BBH, GPQA Diamond | Few-shot / 5-shot |

The evaluation suite deliberately targets the capability domains emphasized during data curation: programming, STEM, and reasoning (Section 3.1). Notably, the team reports **base model** performance separately from **instruct-tuned** performance, which is methodologically sound — base model scores reflect pretraining quality without post-training confounds.

**Instruct Model Evaluation (Table 4):**
Trinity Large Preview (the post-trained variant) was evaluated on a narrower suite:
- MMLU: 87.21
- MMLU-Pro: 75.25
- GPQA Diamond: 63.32
- SimpleQA: 23.92
- AIME25: 24.36

**Critical Context:** The authors explicitly state in Section 4 that Trinity Large Preview is "best viewed as a preliminary release rather than a fully post-trained model" due to cluster time constraints. This is essential context — the instruct scores should not be compared directly to fully post-trained competitors without acknowledging this limitation.

**Inference Benchmarking (Figure 4):**
Throughput measurements were conducted using vLLM with all models quantized to FP8 on an 8×H200 node. This setup is industry-standard for inference benchmarking, though the paper does not report latency metrics (time-to-first-token, inter-token latency), only throughput.

**Training Stability Metrics:**
The team introduces two novel metrics to quantify training stability:

1. **MaxVio** (Maximum Violation): Measures relative worst-case expert overload:
   $$\text{MaxVio} = \frac{\max_i \text{Load}_i - \overline{\text{Load}}}{\overline{\text{Load}}}$$
   where $\overline{\text{Load}}$ is the mean expert load and $\text{Load}_i$ is the load on expert $i$ (Section 6).

2. **BatchHet** (Batch Heterogeneity): Quantifies batch-level data imbalance:
   $$\text{BatchHet}_t = \max_i L^{(t)}_i - \frac{1}{M} \sum_{i=1}^{M} L^{(t)}_i$$
   where $L^{(t)}_i$ is the loss of microbatch $i$ at step $t$ (Section 3.2).

These metrics are significant because they provide **quantitative proxies for instability** rather than relying solely on loss curve inspection.

The stability metrics provide quantitative evidence that the SMEBU and RSDB innovations worked:

- Small-scale experiments: BatchHet reduced by **46%**
- Gradient norm kurtosis: **14.6** (RSDB-enabled) vs **187** (baseline)

**Figure 1** shows the training loss graph for Trinity Large with no subsampling or smoothing. Before the six stability fixes were applied, MaxVio "would remain stable and then experience a sudden climb as the experts collapsed." After applying SMEBU and the other fixes, "MaxVio stopped diverging, expert utilization remained balanced throughout the run, and loss continued to smoothly converge to a lower value."

The Trinity Large design "with its extreme sparsity and interleaved local/global attention results in strong performance." This is a notable gap — readers cannot extract precise efficiency gains without digitizing the figure.

### 5.3 Critical Assessment: Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

The experimental evidence **partially supports** the paper's central claims, with important caveats.

#### Strong Support

1. **Training Stability Claim:** The combination of MaxVio metrics, BatchHet reductions, and the smooth loss curve in **Figure 1** provides convincing evidence that the stability innovations worked. The gradient norm kurtosis improvement (14.6 vs 187) is particularly compelling — this is a **12.8× reduction** in gradient instability.

2. **Context Extension Claim:** The MK-NIAH scores provide clear, quantitative evidence of successful context extension. The 0.976 score at 512K (trained at 256K) demonstrates genuine extrapolation capability, not just memorization of the training context length.

3. **Efficiency Claim:** The sparsity ratio (400B total / 13B active ≈ 30.8×) is clearly documented, and **Figure 4** visually supports the throughput advantage, though specific numbers are missing.

#### Weak or Missing Support

1. **No Controlled Ablations:** The most significant methodological limitation is explicitly acknowledged in Section 6:
   > "Because the fixes were introduced together to unblock training, we did not have time to run controlled ablations to attribute stabilization to any individual change."
   
   This means we cannot determine which of the six stability fixes (SMEBU, disabling MXFP8, z-loss, sequence-wise aux loss, increasing dense layers, intra-document masking) was most critical. SMEBU may be necessary but not sufficient — the other five changes may have been equally important.

2. **Limited Baseline Comparisons:** While **Figure 3** compares Trinity Large to other open-weight base models, the paper does not provide a detailed table with competitor scores. The claim of competitiveness with GLM 4.5 Base cannot be independently verified without access to GLM 4.5's published benchmark scores.

3. **Inference Throughput Missing Specifics:** **Figure 4** shows relative throughput but does not provide tokens/second numbers. Readers cannot calculate the exact efficiency gain from sparsity without extracting data from the figure.

4. **Post-Training Limitations:** The Trinity Large Preview instruct scores should be interpreted cautiously. The authors state this is a "preliminary release" with "relatively light post-training" (Section 4). Comparing these scores to fully post-trained competitors would be unfair without this context.

5. **Tokenizer CJK Gap:** Table 1 shows Trinity's tokenizer trails DeepSeek V3 and Qwen 3 on CJK compression (C4-zh: 1.38 C/T for Trinity vs 1.54 for DeepSeek R1 — note lower is better for C/T, so Trinity actually leads here; C4-ja: 1.39 vs 1.60; C4-ko: 1.26 vs 1.47). Wait — re-reading Table 1, Trinity actually **outperforms** on CJK C/T metrics. The paper attributes the gap to "training data timing constraint" but the numbers show Trinity at 1.38 C/T on Chinese vs DeepSeek R1 at 1.54 C/T. This is actually a strength, not a weakness. The paper states "CJK compression is competitive but trails DeepSeek V3 and Qwen 3" — this appears to be an error in the paper's self-assessment, as the table shows Trinity leading on C/T for all three CJK languages.

#### Mixed or Conditional Results

1. **SimpleQA Performance:** The SimpleQA score of 23.92 for Trinity Large Preview is relatively low compared to the MMLU score of 87.21. SimpleQA measures short-form factuality, and this gap suggests the model may struggle with precise factual recall despite strong general knowledge (MMLU). This is a notable weakness that the paper does not discuss.

2. **AIME25 Performance:** The AIME25 score of 24.36 is respectable but not frontier-leading. For a 400B parameter model, this suggests room for improvement in mathematical reasoning, particularly given the heavy math/code focus in the training data (8 trillion tokens of synthetic STEM data).

3. **Scale-Dependent Stability:** The SMEBU innovation was only necessary for Trinity Large — Trinity Nano and Mini used standard aux-loss-free load balancing without reported instability (Section 2.3). This suggests the stability benefits are **scale-conditional**: SMEBU matters primarily at the 400B parameter scale, not for smaller MoE models.

### 5.4 Failure Cases and Robustness Checks

The paper is notably transparent about failure modes, which is uncommon in technical reports:

**Documented Failures:**
1. **Initial Training Collapse:** Section 6 describes how early training runs experienced "collapsed experts" with MaxVio suddenly climbing. This is a candid acknowledgment of failure that many reports omit.

2. **MXFP8 Instability:** The team disabled MXFP8 kernels mid-training and fell back to BF16 due to stability concerns. This suggests that lower-precision training, while more efficient, was not viable for this scale without additional stabilization.

3. **Z-Loss Weight Sensitivity:** The z-loss weight had to be reduced from $1 \times 10^{-4}$ to $1 \times 10^{-6}$ because "larger values destabilized the network" (Section 6). This indicates the model is sensitive to regularization strength.

**Missing Robustness Checks:**
1. **No Seed Variability:** The paper does not report whether training was repeated with different random seeds to assess result variability.

2. **No Downstream Fine-Tuning:** Beyond the light post-training for Trinity Large Preview, the paper does not evaluate how well the base model fine-tunes on domain-specific tasks.

3. **No Long-Context Task Evaluation:** While MK-NIAH scores are reported, the paper does not evaluate actual long-context tasks (e.g., document QA, code repository understanding) at the extended context lengths.

|-------|------------------|---------------------|

| SMEBU improves load balancing | **Moderate** | No ablation, but MaxVio stabilized after adoption |
| RSDB reduces batch noise | **Strong** | Gradient kurtosis 14.6 vs 187, BatchHet reduced 46% (small-scale) |

The transparency about limitations (no ablations, preliminary post-training) strengthens the report's credibility even as it tempers some claims.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

No technical report is complete without a candid assessment of where the approach falls short. The Trinity family represents a significant engineering achievement, but the paper reveals several important limitations, trade-offs, and open questions that readers should understand before adopting or building upon this work. This section examines the assumptions underlying the approach, the scenarios not addressed, and the constraints that may limit broader applicability.

### 6.1 Lack of Controlled Ablations

The most significant methodological limitation is explicitly acknowledged in Section 6:

> "Because the fixes were introduced together to unblock training, we did not have time to run controlled ablations to attribute stabilization to any individual change."

This statement has profound implications for interpreting the paper's contributions. Six stability interventions were applied simultaneously:
1. SMEBU load balancing
2. Disabling MXFP8 kernels (falling back to BF16)
3. Adding z-loss at weight $1 \times 10^{-6}$
4. Adding sequence-wise auxiliary loss at weight $1 \times 10^{-4}$
5. Increasing dense layers from 3 to 6
6. Adopting intra-document masking

**Why this matters:** Without ablations, we cannot determine which interventions were necessary versus sufficient. SMEBU may be the critical innovation, or it may only work in conjunction with the other five changes. For practitioners attempting to replicate this work at smaller scales, this creates uncertainty: should they adopt all six changes, or can they selectively apply only SMEBU? The paper provides no guidance on this question.

**Trade-off:** The team prioritized completing the training run over scientific rigor. This is understandable given the computational cost of re-running a 400B parameter model, but it limits the paper's value as a reproducible research contribution. Readers must treat the stability claims as a **package deal** rather than isolatable innovations.

### 6.2 Scale-Conditional Benefits

A critical but under-emphasized limitation is that the primary innovations appear to be **scale-conditional**. The paper states in Section 2.3:

> "For Trinity Mini and Trinity Nano, we used the standard formulation of auxiliary-loss-free load balancing... For Trinity Large, we replace the per-step sign-based expert bias update with a tanh soft-clamped magnitude-aware update."

This reveals that SMEBU was only necessary for the 400B parameter model. Trinity Nano (6B total parameters) and Trinity Mini (26B total parameters) trained successfully with standard aux-loss-free load balancing without reported instability.

**Implications:**
- Practitioners training MoE models below ~50B parameters may not benefit from SMEBU's complexity.
- The innovation addresses a problem that only emerges at extreme scale, limiting its immediate applicability to the broader community.
- The paper does not identify a clear threshold where SMEBU becomes necessary — is it 100B? 200B? This remains an open question.

**Trade-off:** The engineering effort invested in SMEBU yields diminishing returns at smaller scales. Organizations without access to 2048-GPU clusters may find the standard aux-loss-free approach sufficient for their needs.

### 6.3 Preliminary Post-Training

The paper is transparent about the limitations of the instruct-tuned model. Section 4 states:

> "Because we allocated most of our cluster time to pretraining, we were constrained to a relatively light post-training phase. As a result, the model we report here, Trinity-Large-Preview, is best viewed as a preliminary release rather than a fully post-trained model."

**Evidence of this limitation:**
- The SimpleQA score of 23.92 (Table 4) is notably low compared to the MMLU score of 87.21. SimpleQA measures short-form factuality, and this gap suggests the model struggles with precise factual recall despite strong general knowledge.
- The AIME25 score of 24.36, while respectable, is not frontier-leading for a 400B parameter model, particularly given the 8 trillion tokens of synthetic STEM data in pretraining (Section 3.1).
- The paper does not report comparison to fully post-trained competitors, making it difficult to assess how much performance headroom remains.

**Trade-off:** The team prioritized pretraining quality over post-training refinement. This is a reasonable choice for a base model release, but readers should not expect Trinity Large Preview to compete with fully post-trained frontier models on instruction-following or reasoning benchmarks.

### 6.4 Tokenizer Timing Constraints

Section 2.1 reveals a significant constraint in the tokenizer development:

> "The larger multilingual corpus used for Trinity Large was not yet finalized at the time of tokenizer training; consequently, non-English languages are less well represented relative to their proportion in the final pretraining data."

**Consequences:**
- The tokenizer was trained on approximately 48GB (~10B tokens) drawn primarily from the Trinity Nano and Mini corpus, not the final Trinity Large corpus.
- While Table 1 shows competitive CJK compression (C4-zh: 1.38 C/T, C4-ja: 1.39 C/T, C4-ko: 1.26 C/T), the paper acknowledges this "trails DeepSeek V3 and Qwen 3" — though the table numbers actually show Trinity leading on C/T metrics, suggesting potential inconsistency in the paper's self-assessment.
- The 200k vocabulary was selected after empirical comparison, but the timing constraint means the vocabulary may not be optimal for the final 17 trillion token training mix.

**Trade-off:** The team prioritized schedule over tokenizer optimization. A tokenizer trained on the final corpus might have improved multilingual performance, but this would have delayed the training start date.

### 6.5 Precision and Efficiency Trade-offs

Section 6 reveals that the team had to disable MXFP8 kernels mid-training:

> "We disabled our MXFP8 kernels for linear layers and grouped GEMMs, falling back to BF16."

**Why this matters:**
- MXFP8 offers significant memory and compute efficiency gains over BF16.
- The decision to fall back to BF16 indicates that lower-precision training was not stable at this scale without additional (unidentified) stabilization.
- This reduces the overall training efficiency of the final run compared to what might be achievable with stable FP8 training.

**Open question:** The paper does not specify whether MXFP8 instability was due to the MoE architecture specifically, the Muon optimizer, or the interaction of both. This limits the ability of other practitioners to determine whether FP8 training is viable for their MoE setups.

### 6.6 Infrastructure and Hardware Dependencies

The training infrastructure requirements are substantial and may limit reproducibility:

| Model | GPUs | GPU Type | Parallelism |
|-------|------|----------|-------------|
| Trinity Nano | 512 | H200 | HSDP |
| Trinity Mini | 512 | H200 | HSDP |
| Trinity Large | 2048 | B300 | HSDP + EP + Context Parallelism |

**Constraints:**
- **B300 availability:** Trinity Large was trained on "brand new B300 systems" (Section 3.3), which are cutting-edge hardware not widely available to most research organizations.
- **Expert Parallelism complexity:** The EP group size of 8 for Trinity Large adds infrastructure complexity that may not be accessible to practitioners without specialized training frameworks.
- **PyTorch version sensitivity:** The team conducted "a binary search across nightly versions to find a package that worked well for the training setup" (Section 3.3), indicating fragility in the software stack.

**Trade-off:** The efficiency gains from MoE and advanced parallelism come at the cost of increased infrastructure complexity. Organizations without access to 2000+ GPU clusters or specialized training frameworks may find it difficult to replicate this work.

### 6.7 Synthetic Data Reliance

Section 3.1 documents extensive use of synthetic data:

> "DatologyAI generated over 8 trillion tokens of synthetic web, code, and STEM data."

This represents approximately 47% of the 17 trillion token Trinity Large training mix.

**Potential concerns:**
- **Model collapse risk:** Heavy reliance on synthetic data raises questions about whether future iterations might experience quality degradation if synthetic data generation pipelines are not continuously improved.
- **Quality verification:** The paper does not report human evaluation of synthetic data quality, relying instead on downstream benchmark performance as a proxy.
- **Reproducibility:** The synthetic data generation pipeline uses proprietary infrastructure (Ray and vLLM on Kubernetes), which may not be replicable by external researchers.

**Trade-off:** Synthetic data enabled rapid scaling of the training corpus, but introduces uncertainty about long-term scaling behavior. The paper does not address whether there are diminishing returns from synthetic data beyond certain thresholds.

### 6.8 Context Extension Limits

While the context extension results are strong, they reveal clear boundaries:

| Context Length | MK-NIAH Score |
|---------------|---------------|
| 256K (trained) | 0.994 |
| 512K (extrapolated) | 0.976 |
| 1M (extrapolated) | 0.42 |

**Observations:**
- Performance degrades significantly at 1M context (0.42 score), indicating the architecture does not extrapolate indefinitely.
- The paper does not evaluate actual long-context tasks (e.g., document QA, code repository understanding) at extended context lengths — only the MK-NIAH proxy metric is reported.
- Section 3.5 states "we did not find any improvements from progressively increasing the context window size as opposed to training directly at the longest sequence length," but this finding is limited to the 256K→512K range and may not hold at 1M+.

**Open question:** What architectural modifications would be needed to achieve strong performance at 1M+ context? The paper does not speculate on this, leaving it as an area for future work.

### 6.9 Missing Robustness Checks

Several standard robustness evaluations are absent from the paper:

1. **Seed variability:** The paper does not report whether training was repeated with different random seeds to assess result variability. Given the sensitivity to stability fixes, this is a notable gap.

2. **Downstream fine-tuning:** Beyond the light post-training for Trinity Large Preview, the paper does not evaluate how well the base model fine-tunes on domain-specific tasks. This limits understanding of the base model's adaptability.

3. **Long-context task evaluation:** MK-NIAH scores are reported, but actual long-context tasks (e.g., multi-document QA, full-codebase understanding) are not evaluated at extended context lengths.

4. **Inference latency:** Figure 4 reports throughput but does not provide latency metrics (time-to-first-token, inter-token latency), which are critical for real-world deployment decisions.

**Trade-off:** The team prioritized completing the training run and releasing base weights over comprehensive evaluation. This accelerates community access to the models but limits the paper's value as a complete performance characterization.

### 6.10 Competitive Positioning Gaps

While Section 5.1 claims Trinity Large Base "achieves competitive scores with GLM 4.5 Base," the paper does not provide:

- A direct numerical comparison table with GLM 4.5 Base scores.
- Statistical significance testing on benchmark differences.
- Hardware-normalized efficiency comparisons (e.g., tokens/second per dollar).

**Figure 3** visually positions Trinity Large against other open-weight base models, but readers cannot extract precise numerical comparisons without digitizing the figure.

**Implication:** The competitive claims cannot be independently verified without access to competitor benchmark scores. This limits the paper's utility for organizations making model selection decisions.

| Trade-off | Decision | Consequence |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| Scientific rigor vs. schedule | No ablations | Cannot isolate which stability fixes were critical |
| Pretraining vs. post-training | Light post-training | Instruct scores are preliminary, not frontier-competitive |
| Precision vs. stability | BF16 over MXFP8 | Reduced training efficiency for stability |

### 6.12 Open Questions for Future Work

The paper identifies two promising directions in Section 7, but several additional questions remain:

1. **What is the minimum scale where SMEBU becomes necessary?** Is there a parameter threshold (e.g., 100B, 200B) where standard aux-loss-free load balancing fails?

2. **Can MXFP8 be stabilized for MoE training?** What additional interventions would enable FP8 training without sacrificing stability?

3. **How does synthetic data quality scale?** Are there diminishing returns beyond certain synthetic data proportions, and how can quality be verified without downstream benchmarks?

4. **What architectural changes enable 1M+ context?** The 0.42 MK-NIAH score at 1M suggests significant room for improvement — what modifications would close this gap?

5. **How does Trinity fine-tune on domain-specific tasks?** The base model's adaptability remains untested beyond the light post-training reported.

These limitations do not diminish the technical achievement of the Trinity family, but they define the boundaries within which the results should be interpreted. Practitioners adopting this work should be aware that the stability innovations are scale-conditional, the post-training is preliminary, and the infrastructure requirements are substantial. The paper's greatest value may lie not in the specific model weights, but in the detailed documentation of failure modes and stability interventions that can guide future large-scale MoE training efforts.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The Trinity family of models represents more than a collection of model weights — it provides a detailed blueprint for stable, efficient sparse MoE training at the 400B parameter scale. This section examines how this work reshapes the field's understanding of large-scale training, what research directions it opens, and how practitioners should approach adoption.

### 7.1 Field-Level Implications: Shifting the Stability Paradigm

The Trinity report fundamentally changes how the community should think about training stability at extreme scale. Prior work often treated instability as an unavoidable cost of scaling, but Trinity demonstrates that **instability is a solvable engineering problem** requiring coordinated interventions across multiple system layers.

**The Co-Design Imperative**

Perhaps the most significant implication is the demonstration that stability cannot be achieved through isolated fixes. Section 6 documents how the team attempted incremental changes — lowering learning rates, adjusting normalization parameters, increasing $\epsilon$ values — none of which prevented expert collapse. Stability was only achieved when **six changes were applied simultaneously**:

1. SMEBU load balancing (routing)
2. Disabling MXFP8 kernels (precision)
3. Adding z-loss at $1 \times 10^{-6}$ (logit stability)
4. Adding sequence-wise auxiliary loss (load balancing)
5. Increasing dense layers from 3 to 6 (representation stability)
6. Adopting intra-document masking (objective noise reduction)

This reveals a critical insight: **large-scale MoE training is a non-linear system** where instability modes emerge from the interaction of multiple components, not from any single weakness. Future training efforts at this scale should anticipate needing similar coordinated interventions rather than expecting any single innovation to suffice.

**Load Balancing as Optimization, Not Heuristic**

The SMEBU innovation reframes expert load balancing from a heuristic constraint into a proper optimization problem. The key insight from Section 2.3 is that the standard $\text{sign}(\cdot)$ update rule cannot converge — it oscillates indefinitely around the minimum:

> "Under the assumption that the ideal expert bias value is a fixed value, we note that the standard aux-free load balancing cannot precisely converge on that value, as each local update under the $\text{sign}(\cdot)$ operator is always $\pm\lambda$."

By replacing this with a continuous $\tanh$ function and adding momentum, SMEBU allows the bias updates to shrink as the system approaches equilibrium. This insight should generalize to other MoE implementations — any load balancing strategy that relies on discrete updates may face similar convergence barriers at sufficient scale.

**Data Loading as a Stability Lever**

The RSDB innovation (Section 3.2) demonstrates that **data pipeline design directly impacts training stability**, not just throughput. The gradient norm kurtosis improvement from 187 to 14.6 (a 12.8× reduction) when enabling RSDB is striking evidence that batch-level data imbalance propagates through the optimization process.

This challenges the common assumption that data loading is purely an infrastructure concern. For large-scale training, the statistical properties of each minibatch matter as much as the architecture or optimizer. Future training frameworks should treat data loading as a first-class stability concern, not just a throughput optimization.

**Open-Weight MoE as an Enterprise Viable Path**

The successful training and release of a 400B parameter open-weight MoE model demonstrates that **enterprise deployment sovereignty is achievable at frontier scale**. Section 1 emphasizes that organizations require "clarity about data provenance, licensing, and jurisdictional controls" — needs that closed-source models cannot satisfy.

Trinity Large provides a concrete reference point for organizations evaluating whether to build or buy frontier capabilities. The 400B total / 13B active parameter ratio means inference costs are comparable to a 13B dense model while retaining knowledge capacity approaching much larger dense models. This efficiency profile makes self-hosted deployment economically viable for organizations that could not justify hosting a 400B dense model.

### 7.2 Research Directions Enabled

The Trinity report opens several concrete research directions that the community should pursue. Some are explicitly identified by the authors; others emerge from the gaps and limitations documented in the report.

**Direction 1: Scale Thresholds for Stability Innovations**

The paper reveals that SMEBU was only necessary for Trinity Large (400B), while Trinity Nano (6B) and Trinity Mini (26B) trained successfully with standard aux-loss-free load balancing (Section 2.3). This raises an important unanswered question: **at what parameter scale does SMEBU become necessary?**

Follow-up research should systematically evaluate load balancing strategies across scales:
- Train MoE models at 50B, 100B, 200B, and 400B total parameters
- Compare standard aux-loss-free vs. SMEBU at each scale
- Identify the threshold where oscillation-induced instability emerges

This would provide practical guidance for practitioners: organizations training below the threshold can use simpler load balancing, while those above it should adopt SMEBU or similar continuous update strategies.

**Direction 2: FP8 Stability for MoE Training**

Section 6 documents that the team had to disable MXFP8 kernels mid-training and fall back to BF16 due to stability concerns. This represents a significant efficiency loss — FP8 training can offer 2-4× memory and compute improvements over BF16.

Research questions include:
- Was the FP8 instability specific to the MoE architecture, the Muon optimizer, or their interaction?
- Would additional stabilization (e.g., higher z-loss weight, different gradient clipping) enable stable FP8 training?
- Can SMEBU's bounded updates help stabilize low-precision training by preventing extreme bias values?

Solving this would directly improve training efficiency for future MoE efforts. The 2048 B300 GPU cluster used for Trinity Large represents substantial computational cost — any efficiency gains would reduce barriers to replication.

**Direction 3: Synthetic Data Quality Scaling**

The Trinity Large training mix included over 8 trillion tokens of synthetic data (Section 3.1), representing approximately 47% of the 17 trillion token corpus. While downstream benchmarks show strong performance, the paper does not address whether there are **diminishing returns or quality degradation** from heavy synthetic data reliance.

Follow-up research should investigate:
- At what proportion does synthetic data quality begin to degrade model performance?
- Can synthetic data quality be verified without downstream benchmarks (e.g., through automated quality metrics)?
- How does synthetic data diversity affect model collapse risk in iterative training scenarios?

This is particularly important as the community scales toward 100+ trillion token training runs — synthetic data will likely be necessary, but its limits are not well understood.

**Direction 4: 1M+ Context Architectures**

Trinity Large achieved an MK-NIAH score of 0.976 at 512K context (trained at 256K), but performance dropped to 0.42 at 1M context (Section 3.5). This reveals a clear boundary — the architecture extrapolates well to 2× training length but degrades significantly at 4×.

Research directions include:
- What architectural modifications enable stable 1M+ context training?
- Does the 3:1 local/global attention ratio need adjustment for longer contexts?
- Would training directly at 512K or 1M (rather than extrapolating from 256K) close the performance gap?

The authors note they "did not find any improvements from progressively increasing the context window size" (Section 3.5), but this was only tested for the 256K→512K transition. Different strategies may be needed for 1M+.

**Direction 5: Post-Training at Scale**

Section 4 explicitly states that Trinity Large Preview is "best viewed as a preliminary release rather than a fully post-trained model" due to cluster time constraints. The gap between base model MMLU (82.58) and instruct model MMLU (87.21) shows improvement, but the SimpleQA score of 23.92 suggests room for growth in factual precision.

Research opportunities include:
- How does post-training data composition affect MoE models differently than dense models?
- Do expert routing patterns change during instruction tuning, and does this affect capability retention?
- What RLHF or DPO strategies work best for highly sparse models?

The open-weight release enables the community to explore these questions without needing to retrain from scratch.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

The Trinity family's design choices directly enable specific deployment scenarios. Understanding these use cases helps practitioners determine whether Trinity is appropriate for their needs.

**Long-Context Document Understanding**

The 256K training context (512K inference capability) makes Trinity Large suitable for applications requiring analysis of lengthy documents:

- **Legal document review:** Contracts, briefs, and case files often exceed 100K tokens. Trinity's MK-NIAH score of 0.994 at 256K suggests reliable retrieval across full documents.
- **Codebase understanding:** The ProLong dataset inclusion (Section 3.5) specifically targeted "whole-repository code concatenations" for cross-file code understanding.
- **Scientific literature analysis:** The OCR-derived PDF data (olmOCR, FinePDF-edu) prepares the model for processing academic papers with complex formatting.

The interleaved local/global attention design means inference costs scale sub-linearly with context length — local layers only attend within their 4096-token window, reducing KV-cache pressure compared to full global attention.

**Enterprise Self-Hosting**

The open-weight release directly addresses enterprise deployment requirements identified in Section 1:

- **Data sovereignty:** Organizations can host Trinity Large without sending data to third-party APIs, satisfying regulatory requirements for sensitive domains (healthcare, finance, legal).
- **Customization:** The base model can be fine-tuned on proprietary data without relying on closed-model fine-tuning APIs.
- **Auditability:** Full weight access enables security audits and compliance verification that closed models cannot provide.

The 13B active parameter count means inference hardware requirements are comparable to running a 13B dense model, making self-hosting economically feasible for organizations that could not justify 400B dense model infrastructure.

**Agentic Workflows**

Section 4 describes post-training that "lean[s] heavily into agentic coding supervision" using OpenCode harnesses to collect "full interaction traces (edits, tool calls, and test outcomes)." This prepares Trinity Large Preview for:

- **Multi-step coding tasks:** The model learns the edit-run-test loop, not just final completion.
- **Tool execution:** Training on tool call traces enables reliable API and function invocation.
- **Long-running agents:** The 512K context capability supports agents that maintain state across extended interactions.

The sparse MoE architecture is particularly well-suited for agentic workloads where latency matters — the 13B active parameter count keeps response times competitive while the 400B total capacity supports complex reasoning.

**Multilingual Deployment**

The 200K token vocabulary and multilingual training data (targeting Arabic, Mandarin, Japanese, Spanish, German, French, Italian, Portuguese, Indonesian, Russian, Vietnamese, Hindi, Korean, and Bengali per Section 3.1) enable deployment in non-English contexts. Table 1 shows competitive compression across languages:

| Language | Trinity C/T or B/T |
|----------|-------------------|
| Chinese (C4-zh) | 1.38 C/T |
| Japanese (C4-ja) | 1.39 C/T |
| Korean (C4-ko) | 1.26 C/T |
| French (C4-fr) | 3.98 B/T |

While the paper notes the tokenizer was trained before the final multilingual corpus was finalized (Section 2.1), the vocabulary size and script-aware isolation (covering Thai, Lao, Khmer, Myanmar, and Korean hangul/jamo) provide a foundation for multilingual applications.

### 7.4 Integration and Reproduction Guidance

Practitioners considering Trinity adoption or replication should understand when this approach is appropriate and what infrastructure is required.

**When to Prefer Trinity's Approach**

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| Training MoE <50B parameters | Standard aux-loss-free load balancing | SMEBU benefits are scale-conditional (Section 2.3) |
| Training MoE >100B parameters | Adopt SMEBU or similar continuous updates | Oscillation-induced instability likely at this scale |
| Limited GPU cluster (<500 GPUs) | Consider Trinity Mini or Nano | Trinity Large requires 2048 B300 GPUs (Section 3.3) |
| FP8 training required | Expect additional stabilization work | Trinity fell back to BF16 mid-training (Section 6) |
| Long-context (>128K) needed | Trinity's 3:1 local/global pattern | Proven to 512K with 0.976 MK-NIAH (Section 3.5) |
| Enterprise self-hosting | Trinity Large open weights | Auditability and data sovereignty (Section 1) |
| Maximum benchmark performance | Wait for fully post-trained version | Trinity Large Preview is preliminary (Section 4) |

**Infrastructure Requirements for Replication**

Attempting to replicate Trinity Large's training requires substantial infrastructure:

- **GPU cluster:** 2048 B300 GPUs (or equivalent compute)
- **Training framework:** Modified TorchTitan with Expert Parallelism support
- **Memory:** Sufficient for 400B parameter model with EP group size 8, FSDP group size 128
- **Storage:** High-performance NFS for fast checkpoint recovery (Section 3.3)
- **Monitoring:** Heartbeat monitoring with 3-minute timeout detection for stalled jobs

For organizations without this infrastructure, Trinity Mini (26B total, 3B active) or Trinity Nano (6B total, 1B active) provide accessible alternatives trained on 512 H200 GPUs.

**Key Configuration Decisions**

Practitioners adapting Trinity's approach should pay attention to these configuration choices:

1. **Dense layer count:** Trinity Large uses 6 initial dense layers (Table 2), increased from 3 mid-training for stability (Section 6). Do not skip this — early representation stability matters.

2. **Expert granularity:** Trinity Large activates 4 experts per token with 3072-dimension experts (coarser), while Nano/Mini activate 8 experts with smaller dimensions (finer). Choose based on throughput vs. capacity trade-offs.

3. **Local window size:** Set to half the global context window (4096 for 8192 pre-training context). Do not extend local windows during context extension — only adjust global layers (Section 3.5).

4. **RSDB buffer size:** Trinity Large used 8192 per GPU (user-specified 4096) with 4 workers. Smaller buffers may not adequately reduce BatchHet.

5. **Z-loss weight:** Start at $1 \times 10^{-6}$, not $1 \times 10^{-4}$. Larger values "destabilized the network" (Section 6).

**Reproduction Checklist**

For teams attempting to reproduce Trinity's stability results:

- [ ] Implement SMEBU with $\lambda = 5 \times 10^{-4}$, $\beta = 0.5$, $\kappa = 2$ (Section 2.3)
- [ ] Enable RSDB with buffer size ≥4096 per GPU
- [ ] Use BF16 precision (FP8 requires additional stabilization)
- [ ] Apply z-loss at weight $1 \times 10^{-6}$
- [ ] Add sequence-wise auxiliary loss at weight $1 \times 10^{-4}$
- [ ] Use 6 initial dense layers for models >100B parameters
- [ ] Enable intra-document masking for sequence packing
- [ ] Monitor MaxVio and BatchHet as stability metrics (Sections 3.2, 6)
- [ ] Plan for mid-training interventions — stability fixes may need to be applied after training begins

**Accessing the Models**

The Trinity model checkpoints are available at https://huggingface.co/arcee-ai (Abstract). Practitioners should:

1. Download the base model for fine-tuning on domain-specific tasks
2. Use Trinity Large Preview for initial instruction-following evaluation (with awareness of its preliminary status)
3. Consider Trinity Mini or Nano for resource-constrained deployment scenarios

The open-weight release enables the community to build upon this foundation without requiring 17 trillion token pretraining runs.

### 7.5 Conclusion: A Foundation for Stable Sparse Scaling

The Trinity family demonstrates that **stable, efficient MoE training at 400B parameters is achievable** through coordinated innovations across routing, data loading, precision, and architecture. The key takeaway for the field is not any single technique, but the recognition that large-scale training stability requires system-level co-design.

The open-weight release transforms this from a closed research achievement into a community resource. Practitioners can now:
- Deploy frontier-scale capabilities with data sovereignty
- Fine-tune on domain-specific tasks without API dependencies
- Build upon the stability innovations for future scaling efforts

The two directions identified in Section 7 — **greater sparsity** and **large-batch training** — represent the next frontiers. As the community adopts and extends Trinity's approach, the focus should shift from "can we train at this scale?" to "how efficiently can we train at this scale?" The Trinity report provides the stability foundation needed to pursue that question.
