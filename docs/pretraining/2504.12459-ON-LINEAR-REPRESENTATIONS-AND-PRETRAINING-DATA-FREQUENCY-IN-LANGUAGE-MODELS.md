## 1. Executive Summary

This paper establishes a strong causal link between pretraining data frequency and the emergence of linear representations in language models, demonstrating that factual relations form robust Linear Relational Embeddings (LREs) once subject-object co-occurrences exceed specific thresholds (e.g., ~2,000 times for OLMo-7B and GPT-J, ~4,400 for OLMo-1B). By leveraging this relationship, the authors train a regression model on LRE metrics that predicts term frequencies in unseen pretraining corpora with approximately 70% accuracy (within one order of magnitude), significantly outperforming baselines based solely on log probabilities. This work matters because it provides a novel interpretability tool to reverse-engineer properties of closed-data models and offers concrete guidance for controlling model behavior by manipulating training data frequencies.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Missing Link Between Data Statistics and Internal Geometry
The central problem this paper addresses is a fundamental gap in our understanding of **how** pretraining data shapes the internal representations of Language Models (LMs). While it is well-established that the *content* of pretraining data dictates model behavior—specifically, that models perform better on tasks involving high-frequency terms (Razeghi et al., 2022; Mallen et al., 2023)—the mechanistic pathway from "seeing a fact often" to "representing that fact internally" remains opaque.

Previous research has largely treated this as a black-box correlation: more data leads to better accuracy. However, this paper asks a deeper interpretability question: **What specific structural changes occur inside the model's activation space as a function of data frequency?** The authors hypothesize that the answer lies in the emergence of **linear representations**. Specifically, they investigate whether the frequency of subject-object co-occurrences (e.g., "Miles Davis" and "trumpet" appearing together) directly causes the model to encode that relationship as a simple linear transformation (an affine map) within its hidden states.

This distinction is critical. If linear representations are merely an artifact of model size or architecture, they tell us little about the data. But if they are strictly governed by data frequency, they become a measurable "fossil record" of the training corpus, allowing researchers to infer properties of unknown datasets by inspecting the model's weights.

### The Rise of Linear Representations in Interpretability
To understand the significance of this work, one must first grasp the current state of **interpretability research**. In recent years, the field has shifted towards the **Linear Representation Hypothesis**, which posits that LMs encode concepts and relationships as linear directions or subspaces within their high-dimensional activation vectors (Park et al., 2024; Elhage et al., 2021).

This hypothesis is powerful because linear structures are manipulable. If a concept like "gender" or a relation like "capital city" exists as a linear direction, researchers can perform **steering**: adding or subtracting vectors to control model outputs without retraining (Todd et al., 2024; Rimsky et al., 2024). A specific class of these structures, **Linear Relational Embeddings (LREs)**, was recently formalized by Hernandez et al. (2024). LREs approximate the model's computation for a factual relation (e.g., mapping a subject vector $s$ to an object vector $o$) using a simple affine transformation:

$$ o \approx Ws + b $$

Here, $W$ is a weight matrix and $b$ is a bias vector. Hernandez et al. demonstrated that for many relations, this linear approximation holds surprisingly well, despite the underlying transformer being highly non-linear.

### The Open Question: Why Do Some Relations Become Linear and Others Don't?
While the existence of LREs is established, a major theoretical gap remains: **Under what conditions do these linear structures form?**

Hernandez et al. (2024) and Chanin et al. (2024) observed massive variance in linearity across different relations. For instance, over 80% of entities in the "country-largest-city" relation exhibit strong linear structure, whereas less than 30% do so for "star-in-constellation." Prior work offered two competing explanations, neither fully satisfactory:
1.  **Task Difficulty:** Perhaps some relations are inherently harder to linearize.
2.  **Implicit Bias:** Jiang et al. (2024) provided theoretical evidence that gradient descent implicitly biases models toward linear solutions, but this theory does not explain why the bias succeeds for some concepts and fails for others in practice.

This paper positions itself directly against this uncertainty. The authors argue that the missing variable is **pretraining data frequency**. They draw a parallel to the era of static word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec), where Ethayarajh et al. (2019) showed that vector arithmetic (like `King - Man + Woman = Queen`) only works reliably for high-frequency terms. The authors hypothesize that a similar frequency threshold governs the emergence of linear structure in modern, contextual LMs.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Existing methods for analyzing the data-model relationship fall short in three key areas, which this paper aims to resolve:

*   **Reliance on Output Metrics Alone:** Most prior studies correlate data frequency with **downstream accuracy** (e.g., few-shot learning performance). While accurate, this is a behavioral metric that hides the internal mechanism. A model might get the right answer via memorization or complex non-linear reasoning, not necessarily via a clean linear representation. This paper distinguishes between *knowing* a fact (accuracy) and *representing* it linearly (causality/faithfulness metrics).
*   **Inability to Probe Closed Data:** Current techniques for inferring training data, such as **Membership Inference Attacks** (Shokri et al., 2017) or memorization checks (Carlini et al., 2023), typically aim to recover specific text snippets or determine if a document was seen. They do not provide a method to estimate **aggregate statistical properties** (like term frequencies) of a closed dataset using only the model's internal geometry.
*   **Static vs. Dynamic Analysis:** Previous work on linear representations often analyzes only final, fully-trained checkpoints. This makes it difficult to determine *when* linearity emerges. Does it form gradually? Does it require a specific "critical mass" of data exposure? Without tracking this over training time, it is impossible to establish the causal link between frequency counts and representation formation.

### Positioning and Contribution
This paper bridges the gap between **dataset statistics** and **mechanistic interpretability**. It moves beyond asking "Does the model know this fact?" to asking "Is the fact encoded linearly, and did seeing it $N$ times cause that encoding?"

By developing a novel tool for **batch-level token counting** (allowing precise frequency tracking across training checkpoints) and correlating these counts with LRE quality metrics (**Faithfulness** and **Causality**), the authors provide the first empirical evidence that linear representations are not just an architectural inevitability, but a direct consequence of data frequency thresholds.

Furthermore, the paper flips the traditional direction of analysis. Instead of using known data to explain model behavior, it proposes using the **presence of linear representations** to reverse-engineer unknown data properties. This positions the work as a foundational step toward **dataset inference**: the ability to audit closed-source models (like proprietary LLMs) to estimate the frequency of terms in their secret training corpora, a capability with significant implications for copyright, bias auditing, and data provenance.

## 3. Technical Approach

This empirical study constructs a measurement pipeline to correlate exact pretraining data frequencies with the geometric linearity of internal model representations, ultimately demonstrating that data statistics can be reverse-engineered from model weights. The system operates by simultaneously tracking token co-occurrences across training checkpoints and measuring the quality of Linear Relational Embeddings (LREs) at those same steps, creating a paired dataset of "exposure count" vs. "linear structure quality."

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system is a dual-track analysis framework that counts how often specific fact pairs (like "France" and "Paris") appear in training batches while simultaneously measuring how well a simple linear equation can predict the second term from the first inside the neural network. It solves the problem of opaque data-model relationships by replacing vague correlations with precise thresholds, showing exactly how many times a model must see a fact before it encodes that fact as a straight line in its internal memory.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The approach consists of three primary modules operating in parallel: the **Data Counter**, the **Representation Probe**, and the **Regression Analyzer**.
*   **The Data Counter (`Batch Search`)**: This component ingests raw pretraining corpora (tokenized into fixed-length sequences) and outputs exact co-occurrence counts for specific subject-object pairs at arbitrary training steps, ensuring the counts match exactly what the model saw during optimization.
*   **The Representation Probe (LRE Fitting)**: This module takes intermediate model checkpoints, extracts hidden state vectors for subjects and objects, and fits an affine transformation ($Ws+b$) to approximate the model's internal logic, outputting quality scores (Faithfulness and Causality).
*   **The Regression Analyzer**: This final component ingests the paired outputs (counts and quality scores) to train a predictor that estimates data frequency solely from the geometric properties of the linear representation, validating whether the "linear signal" generalizes across different models.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   First, we define the specific class of linear representations being measured (**Linear Relational Embeddings**) and the mathematical formulation used to approximate non-linear transformer computations with simple affine maps.
*   Second, we detail the novel **Batch Search** algorithm developed to count token co-occurrences with sequence-level precision, explaining why standard document-level counting is insufficient for this analysis.
*   Third, we explain the experimental setup, including the specific models (OLMo and GPT-J), the factual relation datasets, and the hyperparameter choices for fitting the linear transformations.
*   Fourth, we describe the two core metrics (**Faithfulness** and **Causality**) used to quantify whether a linear representation has successfully formed, distinguishing between mere prediction accuracy and structural linearity.
*   Finally, we outline the regression methodology used to invert this relationship, training a model to predict pretraining frequencies from LRE metrics to prove the signal's robustness across different architectures.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Defining the Target: Linear Relational Embeddings (LREs)
The core technical object of study is the **Linear Relational Embedding (LRE)**, a method proposed by Hernandez et al. (2024) to approximate the complex, non-linear computation of a transformer language model using a simple affine transformation.
*   In a standard transformer, mapping a subject token (e.g., "Miles Davis") to an object token (e.g., "trumpet") involves passing the subject vector through multiple layers of non-linear activations, attention heads, and feed-forward networks.
*   The LRE hypothesis posits that for certain factual relations, this entire complex path can be approximated by a single linear equation of the form $o \approx Ws + b$, where $s$ is the subject's hidden state vector, $o$ is the object's hidden state vector, $W$ is a transformation matrix, and $b$ is a bias vector.
*   To compute the parameters $W$ and $b$, the authors use a first-order Taylor Series approximation of the model's function $F(s, c)$, where $c$ represents the few-shot context.
*   The transformation matrix $W$ is estimated as the expected value of the Jacobian matrix of the model's output with respect to the subject input, averaged over $n=8$ examples from the relation:
    $$ W = \mathbb{E}_{s_i, c_i} \left[ \frac{\partial F}{\partial s} \bigg|_{(s_i, c_i)} \right] $$
*   The bias term $b$ is calculated to ensure the transformation passes through the average position of the data points:
    $$ b = \mathbb{E}_{s_i, c_i} \left[ F(s, c) - \frac{\partial F}{\partial s} s \bigg|_{(s_i, c_i)} \right] $$
*   In practice, the authors extract the subject representation $s$ from a middle layer of the model and the object representation $o$ from the final layer's last token position during a few-shot prompting setup.
*   A critical design choice in this work differs from the original Hernandez et al. implementation: instead of filtering the 8 examples to only include those the model predicts correctly, the authors include **all examples**, even those where the model fails.
*   This decision allows for a consistent comparison across different training checkpoints (including early stages where accuracy is low) and prevents the metric from conflating "knowing the fact" with "having a linear structure."
*   Additionally, while the original work selected a single scaling hyperparameter $\beta$ per model to adjust the slope of $W$, this paper optimizes a unique $\beta$ **per relation** to avoid disadvantaging specific relations that might require different scaling factors.

#### The Data Counting Engine: Batch Search
To establish a causal link between data frequency and representation formation, the authors require exact counts of how many times specific subject-object pairs co-occurred in the training data up to any given checkpoint.
*   Standard tools like "What's in My Big Data?" (WIMBD) provide counts over entire documents, but this introduces noise because language models are trained on fixed-length sequences that often split documents mid-sentence.
*   If a subject and object appear in the same document but in different training sequences (batches), the model never sees them together in a single context window, meaning no learning signal for that specific co-occurrence is generated at that step.
*   To solve this, the authors developed a custom tool called **Batch Search**, which operates directly on the tokenized batches used during pretraining.
*   The tool scans the approximately 2 trillion tokens of the Dolma dataset (used for OLMo) by processing the data as a stream of $(batch\_size, sequence\_length)$ arrays.
*   A co-occurrence is counted **only** if both the subject token and the object token appear within the same sequence instance in a batch.
*   This method ensures that the frequency counts perfectly mirror the actual exposure the model received during gradient updates, rather than an approximation based on document proximity.
*   The implementation uses Cython bindings for efficiency, allowing the search of 10,000 unique terms across the massive Dolma corpus in approximately one day using 900 CPUs.
*   For the GPT-J model, where exact batch reconstruction is not possible, the authors use WIMBD counts on the full Pile dataset as a close approximation, noting in Appendix D that the correlation between Batch Search and WIMBD counts is extremely high (slope of 0.94), though WIMBD slightly overestimates due to document-level aggregation.

#### Experimental Configuration and Models
The study evaluates this pipeline across three distinct language models to test the generality of the findings across scales and architectures.
*   The primary models are from the **OLMo** family (Groeneveld et al., 2024), specifically the **OLMo-7B (v0424)** and **OLMo-1B (v0724)** variants.
*   A key advantage of OLMo for this research is the availability of **intermediate checkpoints**, allowing the authors to measure LRE quality at eight distinct points during training: after seeing 41B, 104B, 209B, 419B, 628B, 838B, 1T, and 2T tokens.
*   The third model is **GPT-J (6B)** (Wang & Komatsuzaki, 2021), trained on The Pile dataset, which serves as an out-of-distribution test case for the regression experiments.
*   The factual relations used for analysis are drawn from a subset of the **RELATIONS dataset** (Hernandez et al., 2024), focusing on 25 specific factual categories such as `capital-city`, `person-mother`, and `company-CEO`.
*   The authors explicitly exclude the `landmark-on-continent` relation from the main analysis because 74% of its answers are "Antarctica," which would confound the extraction of a generalizable linear representation.
*   Across these 25 relations, the dataset contains 10,488 unique subject and object entities, providing a dense sampling of frequency distributions.
*   Hyperparameter sweeps for the LRE fitting process (detailed in Appendix C) explore the optimal layer for extracting the subject vector $s$, the scaling factor $\beta$, and the rank of the pseudoinverse matrix used for causality edits.
*   The authors select the layer for analysis based on maximizing the **Causality** metric rather than Faithfulness, arguing that Causality is a more robust indicator of true linear structure, especially in early checkpoints where models might trivially predict high-frequency tokens.

#### Quantifying Linearity: Faithfulness and Causality
To determine whether a linear representation has successfully formed for a given relation, the authors employ two distinct metrics that probe different aspects of the affine approximation $o \approx Ws + b$.
*   **Faithfulness** measures the consistency of the linear approximation: it calculates the proportion of times the LRE predicts the exact same object token as the original non-linear model.
    *   Mathematically, if the model predicts token $t_{model}$ and the LRE predicts $t_{LRE}$, Faithfulness is the accuracy of $t_{LRE} == t_{model}$.
    *   A high Faithfulness score indicates that the linear map is a valid proxy for the model's actual computation, but it can be artificially inflated if the model simply predicts a common token (e.g., "the") regardless of the input.
*   **Causality** measures the controllability of the representation: it tests whether editing the subject vector $s$ using the LRE direction forces the model to change its prediction to the target object of a *different* example from the same relation.
    *   For instance, if the model originally predicts "trumpet" for "Miles Davis," a causality edit attempts to shift the hidden state such that the model predicts "guitar" (the instrument played by Cat Stevens).
    *   This metric is preferred for the main analysis because it demonstrates that the linear direction actively encodes the relational logic, rather than just passively approximating a static output.
*   The authors define a "high quality" linear representation as one achieving a Causality score above **0.9**, indicating that the linear intervention successfully changes the model's behavior in the intended direction 90% of the time.
*   By tracking these metrics across the 8 OLMo checkpoints, the authors can observe the exact moment a relation transitions from non-linear to linear as the co-occurrence count increases.

#### Inverting the Signal: Regression for Frequency Prediction
The final technical contribution is a regression framework designed to prove that the strength of linear representations contains sufficient information to estimate pretraining data frequencies, even for unseen models.
*   The authors train a **Random Forest regression model** with 100 decision tree estimators to predict the log-frequency of terms (either object-only or subject-object co-occurrence) based on features extracted from the LRE analysis.
*   The input features for the regression include the **Faithfulness** score, the **Causality** score, the **Faith Probability** (log probability of the correct answer under the LRE), and **Hard Causality** (a binary variant of causality).
*   To prevent data leakage, the evaluation uses a **leave-one-relation-out** cross-validation strategy across 4 random seeds: the model is trained on 24 relations and tested on the held-out 25th relation.
*   This setup ensures that the regression learns the general mapping between "linearity strength" and "frequency" rather than memorizing specific facts about, for example, countries or musicians.
*   The target variable is the ground-truth count obtained via Batch Search (for OLMo) or WIMBD (for GPT-J), transformed into natural log space to handle the wide dynamic range of frequencies.
*   Performance is evaluated using **Within-Magnitude Accuracy**, which counts a prediction as correct if it falls within one order of magnitude (10x) of the true frequency (e.g., predicting 500 when the truth is 200 is a success).
*   Crucially, the authors test **cross-model generalization**: a regressor trained on OLMo-7B features is applied directly to features extracted from GPT-J (and vice versa), with the only adjustment being a scaling factor for the total number of tokens seen by each model.
*   This experiment tests the hypothesis that the relationship between data frequency and linear geometry is a universal property of transformer training, not an artifact of a specific dataset or architecture.
*   Feature importance analysis via permutation tests reveals that **Hard Causality** is the most predictive feature, contributing to a ~15% increase in accuracy, followed by Faithfulness metrics, confirming that the structural integrity of the linear map is the primary carrier of frequency information.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper moves beyond establishing correlations to revealing the mechanistic rules governing how data shapes model geometry. The following insights represent fundamental shifts in how we understand the relationship between pretraining statistics and internal representations, distinguishing between incremental improvements in measurement and genuine theoretical breakthroughs.

### 4.1 The Existence of a Universal "Linearity Threshold"
**Innovation Type:** Fundamental Theoretical Advance

Prior work established that frequency correlates with accuracy (Razeghi et al., 2022) and that linear structures exist in trained models (Hernandez et al., 2024). However, this paper discovers a **phase transition** phenomenon: linear representations do not emerge gradually or linearly with exposure. Instead, they appear abruptly once a specific co-occurrence threshold is crossed.

*   **What Makes It Different:** Previous studies treated the data-representation relationship as a continuous slope. This work identifies a **critical mass** requirement. As shown in **Figure 2**, relations with subject-object co-occurrences below a specific count exhibit near-zero causality (non-linear), while those just above the threshold jump to high causality (>0.9).
*   **The Specific Thresholds:** The paper quantifies these thresholds precisely, revealing a dependency on model scale:
    *   **OLMo-7B & GPT-J (6B):** A mean co-occurrence of approximately **2,000 times** is required.
    *   **OLMo-1B:** Requires roughly **4,400 times**, suggesting smaller models need double the exposure to form the same linear structure.
*   **Significance:** This finding implies that "knowing" a fact (accuracy) and "encoding" it linearly are distinct regimes. A model might achieve high few-shot accuracy on a low-frequency relation (e.g., "star-constellation" at ~21 co-occurrences) via non-linear memorization or heuristic reasoning, but it will **not** possess the clean, steerable linear direction associated with that fact until the threshold is met. This redefines the conditions under which interpretability tools like steering vectors are expected to work.

### 4.2 Time-Invariance of Representation Formation
**Innovation Type:** Counter-Intuitive Empirical Discovery

A common assumption in deep learning is that the *timing* of data exposure matters—that early training establishes foundational structures while later training refines them. This paper overturns that assumption for linear representations.

*   **What Makes It Different:** By leveraging OLMo's intermediate checkpoints (Section 3.3), the authors demonstrate that the formation of an LRE is **independent of the training step**. As illustrated by the scatterplots in **Figure 2**, a relation seen 2,000 times at step 10k (early training) exhibits the same high causality score as a relation seen 2,000 times at step 400k (late training).
*   **The Mechanism:** The data suggests that the model does not need to "wait" for other capabilities to mature before forming linear relations. Once the cumulative count of a specific subject-object pair hits the threshold, the linear structure forms immediately, regardless of the model's overall loss or general capability at that moment.
*   **Significance:** This simplifies the theoretical model of how LMs learn facts. It suggests that the emergence of linear geometry is a local property driven strictly by aggregate exposure counts, not a global property dependent on the model's developmental stage. This insight is crucial for curriculum design: to induce linear representations for specific concepts, one only needs to ensure the total frequency threshold is met, regardless of *when* in the training run those examples appear.

### 4.3 Reverse-Engineering Data Statistics via Geometry (Dataset Inference)
**Innovation Type:** New Capability / Methodological Inversion

Most interpretability research uses known data to explain model behavior. This paper innovates by **inverting the pipeline**: it uses the model's internal geometry to infer properties of the unknown training data.

*   **What Makes It Different:** Traditional data inference relies on **Membership Inference Attacks** (checking if specific text was memorized) or analyzing output probabilities. This approach is brittle and often fails to estimate aggregate statistics. In contrast, this work trains a regression model on LRE metrics (Causality, Faithfulness) to predict term frequencies.
*   **Performance Gain:** As detailed in **Table 1** and **Figure 3**, this geometric approach significantly outperforms baselines using only log-probabilities or task accuracy.
    *   For predicting object frequencies, the LRE-based model achieves **~70% within-magnitude accuracy**, whereas the log-probability baseline performs barely better than random guessing.
    *   Crucially, this signal **generalizes across models**. A regressor trained on OLMo-7B successfully predicts frequencies in GPT-J (a different architecture trained on a completely different dataset, The Pile) with **65% accuracy** (Table 1, "Eval. on GPT-J").
*   **Significance:** This provides the first practical tool for **auditing closed-source models**. If a proprietary model exhibits strong linear representations for a specific niche topic (e.g., legal code or medical terminology), we can now infer with reasonable confidence that this topic appeared frequently in its secret training corpus. This transforms linear representations from an interpretability curiosity into a forensic tool for data provenance and copyright investigation.

### 4.4 Decoupling Accuracy from Linearity
**Innovation Type:** Conceptual Clarification

The paper rigorously disentangles two metrics often conflated in prior literature: **task accuracy** and **representational linearity**.

*   **What Makes It Different:** While Section 4.3 notes a high correlation ($r=0.82$) between causality and frequency, the authors identify critical edge cases where the two diverge. Specifically, they highlight relations like `star-constellation-name`, which achieves **84% few-shot accuracy** despite having a low causality score (**44%**) and very low frequency (~21 co-occurrences).
*   **The Insight:** High accuracy does *not* imply a linear representation. Models can solve tasks via complex, non-linear circuits or shallow heuristics when data is scarce. Linear representations are a specific, efficient encoding strategy that the model only adopts when data abundance makes it statistically advantageous.
*   **Significance:** This distinction warns against using accuracy as a proxy for interpretability. Just because a model answers correctly doesn't mean its internal logic is simple or linear. This has profound implications for safety and steering: attempting to steer a model on a high-accuracy but low-linearity relation (like star constellations) using linear interventions is likely to fail, as the underlying mechanism is not linear.

### 4.5 Sequence-Level Counting vs. Document-Level Approximation
**Innovation Type:** Methodological Rigor

While less theoretical than the other insights, the development of the **Batch Search** tool (Section 3.2) represents a critical innovation in measurement precision that enables the above discoveries.

*   **What Makes It Different:** Prior counting methods (like WIMBD) operate at the document level. If "France" and "Paris" appear in the same 10-page document but 5 pages apart, they are counted as a co-occurrence. However, if the model's context window is 2k tokens, it never sees them together.
*   **The Correction:** The authors show in **Appendix D** (Figure 10) that document-level counting overestimates co-occurrences (slope of 0.94 compared to batch counts). More importantly, only **sequence-level** counting (within the exact batch array) correlates perfectly with the formation of LREs.
*   **Significance:** This proves that the "unit of learning" for linear relations is the **training sequence**, not the document. This precision was necessary to identify the sharp thresholds in Insight 4.1; without sequence-level counting, the noise from irrelevant document-level co-occurrences would have blurred the phase transition, potentially leading to the incorrect conclusion that the relationship is merely gradual.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence supporting the paper's core thesis: that linear representations are a direct function of data frequency. The authors employ a rigorous, multi-stage evaluation strategy that moves from establishing correlation to demonstrating causality via threshold detection, and finally to validating the utility of these findings through cross-model prediction tasks.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

The experimental design is built on a paired measurement framework. For every factual relation in the dataset, the authors generate two synchronized data streams:
1.  **Exposure Stream:** Exact counts of subject-object co-occurrences within training sequences, tracked across eight distinct checkpoints for OLMo models.
2.  **Geometry Stream:** Quality metrics (Faithfulness and Causality) of the Linear Relational Embedding (LRE) fitted to that relation at the corresponding checkpoint.

**Datasets and Models:**
The primary analysis utilizes the **OLMo** family (1B and 7B parameters) trained on the **Dolma** corpus (~2T tokens). The availability of intermediate checkpoints allows for a temporal analysis of representation formation. The study covers **25 factual relations** (e.g., `country-capital`, `person-mother`) comprising **10,488 unique entities**.
*   **Validation Model:** **GPT-J (6B)** trained on **The Pile** is used exclusively for cross-model generalization tests. Since exact batch reconstruction is impossible for GPT-J, the authors use document-level counts from the "What's in My Big Data?" (WIMBD) tool, noting a high correlation (slope 0.94) with sequence-level counts (Appendix D, Figure 10).

**Metrics:**
*   **Primary Metric:** **Causality**. This measures the proportion of times an LRE-based intervention successfully forces the model to predict a *different* target object from the same relation. It is preferred over Faithfulness because it proves the linear direction actively encodes the relational logic, rather than just passively approximating a static output.
*   **Secondary Metric:** **Faithfulness**. Measures whether the LRE predicts the same token as the original model.
*   **Prediction Metric:** **Within-Magnitude Accuracy**. Used for the regression task, a prediction is considered correct if it falls within one order of magnitude ($10\times$) of the ground truth frequency (e.g., predicting 500 when the truth is 200).

**Baselines:**
To prove that linear structure carries unique information about data frequency, the authors compare their LRE-based regression against:
1.  **LM-Only Baseline:** A regression using only log-probabilities of the correct answer and few-shot accuracy.
2.  **Mean Frequency Baseline:** Predicting the average frequency of the training set for every input.
3.  **Random Baseline:** Randomly sampling frequencies from the training distribution.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results: The Frequency-Linearity Correlation

The most striking result is the strength of the correlation between data exposure and geometric structure. As shown in **Figure 2**, the Pearson correlation coefficient ($r$) between **Log Subject-Object Co-occurrence** and **Causality** is **0.82** for both OLMo-7B and OLMo-1B.

This correlation is significantly stronger than those observed for individual term frequencies:
*   Subject Frequency vs. Causality: $r = 0.66$
*   Object Frequency vs. Causality: $r = 0.59$

This disparity confirms the authors' hypothesis that the *relational* context (the co-occurrence) is the driving factor for linear structure formation, not merely the familiarity with the individual entities.

#### The Phase Transition Thresholds
The data does not support a gradual, linear improvement. Instead, **Figure 2** reveals a sharp phase transition. The authors identify specific co-occurrence thresholds where the average Causality score jumps to **>0.9** (near-perfect linearity):
*   **OLMo-7B:** Threshold $\approx$ **1,998** co-occurrences.
*   **GPT-J (6B):** Threshold $\approx$ **1,097** co-occurrences.
*   **OLMo-1B:** Threshold $\approx$ **4,447** co-occurrences.

**Table in Figure 2** explicitly lists these values. The implication is clear: smaller models require roughly **2.2x more exposure** than larger models to form the same linear structure. Once a relation crosses this threshold, the Causality score remains high regardless of whether the count was accumulated early or late in training.

#### Time-Invariance of Formation
A critical subset of the results involves the temporal analysis of OLMo checkpoints. **Figure 2** plots data points from 8 different training stages (from 41B to 2T tokens).
*   **Observation:** Data points from early checkpoints (red dots, e.g., 41B tokens) that exceed the frequency threshold align perfectly with data points from final checkpoints (blue dots) that exceed the same threshold.
*   **Interpretation:** If a relation has been seen 2,000 times by step 10k, it exhibits high causality. If a different relation reaches 2,000 times only by step 400k, it exhibits the *same* high causality. The "age" of the model is irrelevant; only the cumulative count matters.

### 5.3 Decoupling Accuracy from Linearity

The paper provides compelling evidence that high task performance does not guarantee linear representation. In **Section 4.3**, the authors analyze the `star-constellation-name` relation:
*   **Few-Shot Accuracy:** **84%** (The model can correctly answer the question).
*   **Causality:** **44%** (The internal mechanism is not linear).
*   **Average Co-occurrence:** Only **~21** times.

This divergence supports the claim that models can solve low-frequency tasks via non-linear memorization or complex heuristics. Linear representations appear to be an optimization strategy adopted only when data abundance makes the linear approximation statistically efficient. Conversely, for high-frequency relations like `food-from-country`, causality often leads accuracy during training (65% causality vs. 42% accuracy at intermediate steps), suggesting the linear structure forms *before* the model can reliably execute the task in a few-shot setting.

### 5.4 Reverse-Engineering Data: Regression Results

The ultimate test of the theory is whether the geometric signal can be inverted to predict unknown data statistics. The authors train a Random Forest regressor to predict term frequencies using LRE features.

**Performance on Held-Out Relations (OLMo-7B):**
As shown in **Figure 3**, the LRE-based model significantly outperforms the LM-only baseline:
*   **Object Frequency Prediction:**
    *   **LRE Features:** **~70%** Within-Magnitude Accuracy.
    *   **LM-Only Features:** Barely above the Mean Baseline (~40-45%).
    *   **Mean Absolute Error (Log Space):** 2.1 (LRE) vs. 4.2 (LM-Only).
*   **Subject-Object Co-occurrence Prediction:**
    *   This task proves more difficult due to higher variance. The LRE model achieves marginal gains over the mean baseline, indicating that while the signal exists, predicting joint probabilities is harder than predicting individual term frequencies.

**Cross-Model Generalization (The "Closed Data" Test):**
The most significant result for practical application is presented in **Table 1**. The authors train the regressor on OLMo-7B and evaluate it on GPT-J (and vice versa) without retraining.
*   **Setup:** Train on OLMo (Dolma corpus) $\rightarrow$ Evaluate on GPT-J (The Pile corpus).
*   **Result (Object Frequency):** The LRE-based model achieves **65% ± 12%** accuracy.
*   **Comparison:** The LM-Only baseline drops to **42% ± 10%**, which is nearly indistinguishable from the Mean Frequency Baseline (41%).
*   **Significance:** This demonstrates that the mapping between "linear structure strength" and "data frequency" is a **universal property** of transformer training, independent of the specific dataset or model architecture. It validates the feasibility of auditing closed-source models.

**Feature Importance:**
Permutation tests (Appendix E, **Figure 12**) reveal that **Hard Causality** is the dominant feature, contributing a **~15%** increase in accuracy when included. Faithfulness metrics contribute an additional ~5%. Log-probabilities contribute negligibly to the generalization performance, confirming that the *geometry* of the representation, not the confidence of the output, carries the frequency signal.

### 5.5 Failure Cases and Limitations

While the results are robust, the paper explicitly identifies conditions where the method struggles:

1.  **Low-Frequency Outliers:** As shown in **Table 2**, the regression fails dramatically for relations with extremely low frequencies that nonetheless have high accuracy (e.g., `star-constellation-name`).
    *   *Example:* For the subject "Arcturus" and object "Boötes", the model predicts a frequency of **974,550**, while the ground truth is only **2,817** (a **346x error**).
    *   *Reason:* The model likely infers high frequency from the high accuracy/confidence of the output, failing to account for the non-linear mechanism used to solve the task.

2.  **Subject Sensitivity:** The regression can be sensitive to the specific subject chosen within a relation.
    *   *Example (Table 2):* For the `person-mother` relation, predicting the frequency of "Princess Diana" yields vastly different errors depending on whether the subject is "Prince William" (4.6x error) or "Prince Harry" (207x error). This suggests that local variations in how specific entities are represented can introduce noise into the frequency estimate.

3.  **Co-occurrence vs. Object Frequency:** The method is consistently better at predicting the frequency of the *object* term alone than the *subject-object pair*. The authors attribute this to the tighter clustering of object frequencies around the mean, making the regression task inherently harder for joint counts.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment

Do these experiments convincingly support the claims? **Yes, with high confidence.**

*   **Causal Link:** The use of intermediate checkpoints and the identification of sharp thresholds (Figure 2) move the argument beyond correlation. The fact that linearity appears immediately upon crossing a count threshold, regardless of training step, strongly implies a causal mechanism driven by exposure.
*   **Robustness:** The cross-model generalization (Table 1) is the strongest evidence. If the relationship were an artifact of the Dolma dataset or the OLMo architecture, the regressor would fail on GPT-J. The fact that it maintains **65% accuracy** across different models and datasets confirms the universality of the phenomenon.
*   **Precision:** The development of the **Batch Search** tool addresses a critical confounding variable in prior work (document-level vs. sequence-level counting). The correlation between batch counts and LRE quality is cleaner than document counts, validating the methodological rigor.

The primary limitation remains the inability to perfectly predict frequencies for low-count, high-accuracy relations (the "memorization" regime). However, the paper explicitly frames this not as a failure of the theory, but as evidence of the distinct mechanisms (linear vs. non-linear) at play. The experiments successfully demonstrate that **linear representations are a reliable forensic signal for high-frequency pretraining data**, providing a new, powerful tool for understanding and auditing language models.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper establishes a robust link between data frequency and linear representations, the approach relies on specific assumptions and faces distinct boundaries in its applicability. Understanding these limitations is crucial for correctly interpreting the results and avoiding over-generalization to scenarios the method cannot support.

### 6.1 The "Factual" Assumption and Co-occurrence Proxies
The entire methodology rests on the assumption that **subject-object co-occurrence within a training sequence** is an accurate proxy for the model learning a specific factual relation. This holds well for explicit factual statements (e.g., "Paris is the capital of France"), where the subject and object naturally appear in close proximity.

However, this assumption breaks down for **commonsense or implicit relations**. In Appendix G, the authors test the method on 8 commonsense relations (e.g., `fruit-outside-color`, `task-person-type`) and find a significantly weaker correlation ($r=0.42$) compared to factual relations ($r=0.82$).
*   **The Problem:** For a relation like "historians research history," the concept of a historian might be learned from thousands of contexts where the word "history" does not appear immediately adjacent to "historian." Conversely, the co-occurrence of "researching" and "historian" might not capture the full semantic definition of the role.
*   **Consequence:** The "Batch Search" tool counts exact token proximity, which may severely undercount the true semantic exposure for implicit concepts. Therefore, the frequency thresholds identified in this paper (~2k co-occurrences) **cannot be blindly applied** to non-factual domains. The authors explicitly caution against treating commonsense relations under the same lens as factual triplets.

### 6.2 The "Memorization Regime": High Accuracy, Low Linearity
A critical limitation of using linear representations as a forensic tool is the existence of a **non-linear memorization regime**. The paper identifies specific cases where models achieve high task accuracy despite extremely low data frequency, implying the use of complex, non-linear circuits rather than simple affine maps.

*   **Evidence:** The `star-constellation-name` relation serves as the primary counter-example. Despite an average co-occurrence of only **~21 times**, OLMo-7B achieves **84% few-shot accuracy**. However, its Causality score is only **44%**, indicating no robust linear structure.
*   **Impact on Prediction:** When the regression model attempts to predict frequencies for such relations, it fails catastrophically because it interprets the high performance (or specific activation patterns) as a signal of high frequency.
    *   **Table 2** shows a stark failure: for the star "Arcturus" and constellation "Boötes" (Ground Truth: 2,817), the model predicts a frequency of **974,550**—an error of **346x**.
*   **Takeaway:** The method is reliable only for relations that have crossed the linearity threshold. For rare facts that models have managed to memorize via non-linear mechanisms, the geometric signal becomes noisy and misleading, leading to massive overestimations of frequency.

### 6.3 Computational and Data Accessibility Constraints
The rigor of this study depends heavily on access to resources that are rarely available for modern proprietary models.

*   **Requirement for Exact Batches:** The discovery of sharp frequency thresholds was only possible because the authors could reconstruct the exact **tokenized batches** seen by OLMo during training. Standard document-level counting (like WIMBD) introduces noise that blurs the phase transition (Appendix D, Figure 10).
    *   **Trade-off:** For closed-source models (e.g., GPT-4, Claude), researchers cannot access the training batches. They must rely on document-level approximations or public datasets that *might* resemble the training data. While the cross-model generalization (OLMo $\to$ GPT-J) suggests the signal is robust, the precision of the frequency estimate will inevitably degrade without exact batch reconstruction.
*   **Computational Cost of Batch Search:** The custom `Batch Search` tool, while efficient relative to naive methods, still required **900 CPUs running for ~24 hours** to process the 2 trillion tokens of the Dolma dataset for just 10,000 terms.
    *   **Scalability:** Scaling this to search for millions of potential terms or to analyze multiple large-scale checkpoints simultaneously presents a significant computational barrier. This limits the technique's utility for broad, exhaustive audits of massive corpora.

### 6.4 Sensitivity to Entity Selection and Relation Variance
The regression model's predictions are not uniform across all entities within a relation, revealing sensitivity to specific subject choices.

*   **Entity Variance:** As shown in **Table 2**, predicting the frequency of "Princess Diana" yields vastly different errors depending on the subject used:
    *   Subject "Prince William" $\to$ Prediction error: **4.6x**.
    *   Subject "Prince Harry" $\to$ Prediction error: **207x**.
*   **Implication:** This suggests that the strength of the linear representation can vary significantly even within the same relation, likely due to imbalances in how specific entity pairs are represented in the data or how the model clusters their embeddings.
*   **Limitation:** To get a reliable frequency estimate for a term, one cannot rely on a single subject-object pair. The method requires averaging over multiple examples from the relation to smooth out these local variances, which may not always be feasible for niche topics with few known instances.

### 6.5 Correlation vs. Causation
Finally, while the paper presents strong evidence for a causal link (via the time-invariant threshold discovery), it explicitly acknowledges in **Appendix A** that it cannot make definitive **causal claims** in the mechanistic sense.

*   **The Counterfactual Gap:** Proving causality would require **counterfactual pretraining**: training two identical models where one sees a specific fact 2,000 times and the other sees it 1,999 times, then observing if the linear representation appears only in the former. The computational cost of such an experiment is prohibitive.
*   **Current Evidence:** The authors rely on the strong correlation across checkpoints and the sharpness of the threshold as proxies for causality. While compelling, this leaves open the possibility that unobserved confounding variables (e.g., the complexity of the surrounding context in which the fact appears) might also influence linearity formation alongside raw frequency.

### 6.6 Summary of Open Questions
These limitations point to several unresolved questions for future research:
1.  **Non-Linear Forensics:** Can we develop similar forensic tools to detect the *non-linear* memorization circuits used for low-frequency facts (like star constellations)?
2.  **Template Dependence:** The authors speculate that the *presentation* of facts (templates) has minimal impact, but the `Batch Search` tool now enables tracking exact positions. Does the syntactic diversity of co-occurrences affect the threshold, or is raw count the sole driver?
3.  **Tighter Bounds:** The current regression predicts frequencies within one order of magnitude ($10\times$). Can incorporating additional features (e.g., tokenizer statistics, layer-specific activations) tighten these bounds to provide more precise estimates for auditing purposes?

In summary, while the paper provides a powerful new lens for understanding model internals, its application as a precise auditing tool is currently bounded to **high-frequency, explicit factual relations** and requires careful handling of entity-specific variances and non-linear outliers.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This work fundamentally shifts the paradigm of mechanistic interpretability from a purely descriptive field—observing *what* structures exist—to a predictive and forensic one, where we can infer *why* they exist and *what* data created them. By establishing data frequency as the primary driver for the emergence of linear representations, the paper provides a unified theory that connects dataset statistics, model geometry, and behavioral capabilities. This section outlines how these findings reshape research trajectories, enable new auditing capabilities, and offer practical guidelines for model development.

### 7.1 Transforming the Interpretability Landscape: From Observation to Prediction
Prior to this work, the **Linear Representation Hypothesis** was largely treated as an architectural curiosity: linear structures exist, but their formation seemed stochastic or dependent on opaque optimization dynamics. This paper replaces that uncertainty with a **deterministic threshold model**.

*   **Predictive Interpretability:** Researchers can now predict *a priori* whether a specific concept will be linearly representable in a model simply by estimating its frequency in the training corpus. If a relation like `person-mother` appears 5,000 times, we can confidently expect a robust steering vector ($W, b$) to exist. If a relation like `star-constellation` appears only 20 times, we can predict that linear interventions will fail, regardless of the model's accuracy on the task.
*   **Unifying Accuracy and Geometry:** The findings resolve the tension between behavioral performance and internal structure. The paper demonstrates that high accuracy does not imply linearity; rather, linearity is a specific "efficiency mode" the model adopts only when data abundance makes the affine approximation statistically optimal. This forces the community to distinguish between **linear recall** (efficient, steerable, frequency-dependent) and **non-linear memorization** (complex, brittle, possible even at low frequencies).

### 7.2 New Frontiers in Research
The discovery of frequency-dependent linearity opens several critical avenues for future investigation:

#### A. Forensic Dataset Inference for Closed Models
The most immediate application is the development of **dataset inference tools** for proprietary models. Since the regression model trained on OLMo generalizes to GPT-J (Table 1), it suggests a universal mapping between geometry and frequency.
*   **Research Direction:** Future work can expand the set of "probe relations" to cover diverse domains (e.g., medical terminology, legal code, specific programming languages). By measuring the causality scores of these relations in a closed model (like Llama-3 or GPT-4), researchers could reconstruct a **frequency profile** of its hidden training data.
*   **Goal:** Detect copyright infringement or data contamination without accessing the training logs. If a model shows linear structures corresponding to a niche, copyrighted dataset appearing >2,000 times, it provides strong geometric evidence of inclusion.

#### B. The Mechanics of Non-Linear Memorization
The paper identifies a "blind spot": relations with high accuracy but low linearity (e.g., `star-constellation`).
*   **Research Direction:** If linear circuits are not responsible for these low-frequency facts, what is? This invites mechanistic studies into **non-linear memorization circuits**. Do these facts rely on specific attention heads acting as key-value lookups? Do they involve multi-layer induction heads that operate differently than the affine maps described here?
*   **Hypothesis:** Low-frequency facts may be stored in "sparse" subspaces or via discrete lookup mechanisms that do not generalize linearly, making them harder to steer but potentially more robust to interference.

#### C. Curriculum Learning and Data Mixing
The finding that linearity is **time-invariant** (Section 4.2) has profound implications for curriculum design.
*   **Research Direction:** Does the *distribution* of exposure matter? The paper shows that cumulative count is the driver, but future work could investigate if clustering examples (seeing "France-Paris" 2,000 times in one epoch) yields different geometric properties than spreading them out over the entire training run.
*   **Optimization:** Could we intentionally manipulate the training schedule to force linear representations for safety-critical concepts (e.g., "do not generate hate speech") by ensuring they cross the ~2k threshold early, thereby making them easier to steer via activation addition later?

#### D. Extending Beyond Factual Triplets
The current study focuses on explicit subject-relation-object triplets.
*   **Research Direction:** Can frequency thresholds be identified for **abstract concepts** (e.g., "sarcasm," "logical reasoning," "theory of mind")? While counting co-occurrences for abstract concepts is harder, the principles of **Batch Search** could be adapted to count semantic patterns or syntactic templates. If abstract reasoning capabilities also rely on linear subspaces, they too might have frequency thresholds.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

#### 1. Auditing and Compliance
Regulators and auditors can use LRE metrics as a **proxy for data provenance**.
*   **Use Case:** A company claims their model was not trained on private patient records. Auditors can construct a set of rare patient-name/diagnosis pairs. If the model exhibits high causality scores for these pairs (implying >2k co-occurrences), it contradicts the claim of non-exposure, providing geometric evidence of data leakage.

#### 2. Efficient Model Steering and Editing
Practitioners deploying models can use frequency estimates to decide **which steering methods to employ**.
*   **Guideline:**
    *   **High Frequency (>2k co-occurrences):** Use **Linear Steering** (e.g., Activation Addition, LRE interventions). These methods will be highly effective, computationally cheap, and stable because the underlying representation is linear.
    *   **Low Frequency (<2k co-occurrences):** Avoid linear steering. Instead, use **In-Context Learning (ICL)** or **Fine-Tuning**. Since the internal representation is likely non-linear and fragmented, adding a linear vector will fail to alter behavior reliably.

#### 3. Data Curation for Specific Capabilities
Model developers can optimize pretraining corpora to ensure specific capabilities are encoded linearly.
*   **Strategy:** If a model needs to robustly answer questions about a specific domain (e.g., local laws), developers should ensure that subject-object pairs in that domain appear together in the same context window at least **2,000–4,500 times** (depending on model size). This guarantees the formation of a linear circuit, making the knowledge not only accessible but also editable and interpretable.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

For researchers and engineers looking to integrate these findings, the following practical guidelines apply:

*   **When to Use This Method:**
    *   **Prefer LRE Analysis** when you need to understand the *structural* basis of a model's knowledge or when attempting to steer/edit model behavior. It is superior to log-probability analysis for predicting data frequency (70% vs. ~40% accuracy).
    *   **Prefer Batch Search** over document-level counting (like WIMBD) when precise correlation with model internals is required. Document-level counts introduce noise that obscures the sharp phase transition thresholds.

*   **Integration Steps:**
    1.  **Select Relations:** Choose factual relations relevant to your domain. Ensure they are explicit triplets (Subject-Relation-Object).
    2.  **Estimate Frequency:** If you have access to the data, use the released `Batch Search` tool to count sequence-level co-occurrences. If not, use the regression model provided by the authors to estimate frequency from the model's weights.
    3.  **Check Thresholds:** Compare your counts against the identified thresholds (~2k for 7B-class models, ~4.5k for 1B-class models).
    4.  **Select Intervention:**
        *   If **Count > Threshold**: Proceed with linear interventions (e.g., computing $W$ and $b$ via Jacobian estimation). Expect high causality.
        *   If **Count < Threshold**: Do not expect a single linear direction to control this behavior. Investigate non-linear circuits or rely on few-shot prompting.

*   **Tooling:** The authors have released two key tools to facilitate this work:
    *   **`freq`**: For computing LREs and running the regression analysis.
    *   **`batchsearch`**: For efficient, sequence-level token counting in large corpora.
    *   *Note:* These tools are essential for replicating the precise thresholds found in the paper; approximations using document-level counts will yield softer, less actionable correlations.

### 7.5 Conclusion
This paper transforms our understanding of language model internals from a black box of emergent behaviors into a system governed by quantifiable data laws. By proving that **frequency dictates geometry**, it provides a roadmap for controlling model behavior through data curation and offers a new forensic lens for auditing the opaque datasets behind modern AI. The era of "guessing" why a model knows what it knows is ending; we can now count the exposures that built its mind.
