## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the limitation of greedy decoding in chain-of-thought prompting by introducing `self-consistency`, a decoding strategy that samples multiple diverse reasoning paths and selects the most consistent final answer via marginalization rather than relying on a single greedy path. By leveraging the intuition that complex reasoning problems admit multiple valid paths to a unique correct answer, `self-consistency` mitigates the stochasticity of single sampled generations while avoiding the local-optimality of greedy decoding. This unsupervised approach significantly boosts reasoning performance across four large language models (including PaLM-540B and GPT-3-175B), achieving state-of-the-art accuracy gains such as +17.9% on GSM8K, +11.0% on SVAMP, and +12.2% on AQuA without requiring additional training or human annotations.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Limitations of Greedy Decoding in Reasoning Tasks

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation in how large language models generate answers to complex reasoning problems. When using chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting—a technique where models generate intermediate reasoning steps before producing a final answer—the standard approach employs **greedy decoding**, which selects the single most likely token at each generation step. While CoT prompting itself represented a major advance (Wei et al., 2022), the reliance on greedy decoding creates two critical problems:

1. **Local optimality**: Greedy decoding commits to the highest-probability path early, potentially missing better reasoning trajectories that start with lower-probability tokens but lead to correct answers.
2. **Lack of diversity**: A single greedy path provides no mechanism to verify whether the reasoning is robust or whether alternative paths converge on the same answer.

The paper identifies that complex reasoning problems—arithmetic word problems, commonsense inference, symbolic manipulation—typically admit **multiple different valid reasoning paths** that lead to the same unique correct answer. However, greedy decoding explores only one path, making it vulnerable to early mistakes that cascade through the reasoning chain.

### Why This Problem Matters

The inability to reliably reason represents one of the most significant barriers to deploying language models in high-stakes applications. The paper notes that language models "struggle in Type 2 tasks, such as arithmetic, logical and commonsense reasoning" (Section 4), referencing dual-process theory from cognitive science (Evans, 2010). This limitation persists even as models scale up—increasing parameters alone cannot overcome reasoning deficits (Rae et al., 2021; BIG-bench collaboration, 2021).

The practical implications are substantial:
- **Arithmetic reasoning** underpins financial calculations, scientific computation, and data analysis
- **Commonsense reasoning** is essential for dialogue systems, question answering, and decision support
- **Symbolic reasoning** enables logical inference and program synthesis

Without reliable reasoning, language models remain unreliable for tasks requiring multi-step deduction, limiting their utility in domains where accuracy is critical.

### Prior Approaches and Their Shortcomings

Before self-consistency, several approaches attempted to improve reasoning performance, each with notable limitations:

**1. Chain-of-Thought Prompting with Greedy Decoding (Wei et al., 2022)**
- *Approach*: Prompt the model to generate intermediate reasoning steps, then decode greedily
- *Limitation*: Only explores a single reasoning path; vulnerable to early mistakes; cannot leverage the intuition that multiple paths should converge on the correct answer

**2. Training Verifiers (Cobbe et al., 2021)**
- *Approach*: Train a separate model to score and re-rank generated solutions
- *Limitation*: Requires task-specific training with thousands of labeled examples; needs an additional 175B parameter verifier model for best results (Table 2 footnote g)

**3. Training Re-rankers with Human Annotations (Thoppilan et al., 2022)**
- *Approach*: Collect human preference data to train a re-ranking model
- *Limitation*: Requires expensive human annotation; task-specific; not applicable off-the-shelf

**4. Beam Search Decoding**
- *Approach*: Maintain multiple candidate sequences during decoding, keeping the top-k beams
- *Limitation*: As shown in Table 6, beam search "yields a lower diversity in the outputs" compared to sampling; self-consistency with sampling significantly outperforms self-consistency with beam search (e.g., on MultiArith: 14.7% vs 10.8% with 40 paths)

**5. Model Ensembles**
- *Approach*: Aggregate predictions from multiple different models
- *Limitation*: Requires multiple models of comparable quality; as Table 10 shows, ensembling weaker models with stronger ones can actually hurt performance (LaMDA-137B + PaLM-540B achieves only 36.9% on GSM8K vs 74.4% for PaLM-540B alone with self-consistency)

**6. Prompt Ensembles**
- *Approach*: Aggregate predictions across different prompt orderings or prompt sets
- *Limitation*: Table 7 shows these achieve much smaller gains than self-consistency (e.g., on GSM8K: ensemble with 40 prompt permutations achieves 19.2% vs self-consistency's 27.7% on LaMDA-137B)

### How This Paper Positions Itself

The self-consistency method positions itself as a **simple, unsupervised decoding strategy** that works entirely off-the-shelf with pre-trained language models. The key differentiators are:

**No Additional Training Required**: Unlike verifier-based or re-ranker approaches, self-consistency "requires no additional training, auxiliary models or fine-tuning" (Section 1). It operates purely at inference time.

**No Human Annotations**: The method is "entirely unsupervised" and "requires no additional human annotation" (Section 1), making it immediately applicable to any task where CoT prompting can be used.

**Single Model "Self-Ensemble"**: Rather than ensembling multiple models, self-consistency acts as a "self-ensemble that works on top of a single language model" (Section 1). This avoids the complexity and cost of maintaining multiple model deployments.

**Leverages Diversity, Not Just Probability**: The core insight is that "correct reasoning processes, even if they are diverse, tend to have greater agreement in their final answer than incorrect processes" (Section 2). This differs from sample-and-rank approaches that select the single highest-probability output—Figure 3 demonstrates self-consistency significantly outperforms sample-and-rank with the same number of samples.

**Compatible with Existing Sampling Methods**: Self-consistency works with standard sampling algorithms including temperature sampling, top-k sampling, and nucleus sampling (Section 2), making it easy to integrate into existing inference pipelines.

The paper's positioning is clearest in Table 2 and Table 3, where self-consistency achieves state-of-the-art results on arithmetic and commonsense reasoning benchmarks despite being unsupervised and task-agnostic—outperforming methods that require "task-specific training, or fine-tuning with thousands of examples" (Section 3.2). For instance, on GSM8K with PaLM-540B, self-consistency achieves 74.4% accuracy compared to 56.5% with standard CoT prompting, a +17.9% absolute gain that surpasses prior supervised approaches.

This positioning makes self-consistency particularly valuable for practitioners: it provides substantial accuracy improvements without the engineering overhead of training additional models, collecting annotations, or managing multiple model deployments.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a **decoding strategy modification** for chain-of-thought prompting in large language models. The core idea is to replace single-path greedy decoding with a "sample-and-marginalize" procedure that generates multiple diverse reasoning paths and aggregates their final answers by selecting the most consistent one.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system is an inference-time modification to how language models generate answers when using chain-of-thought prompting, requiring no model training or architectural changes. It solves the problem of greedy decoding committing to potentially flawed single reasoning paths by instead sampling multiple diverse paths and trusting answers that appear consistently across different reasoning trajectories.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The self-consistency pipeline consists of three sequential components that transform a question into a final answer. **First**, the chain-of-thought prompt component takes an input question and prepends manually written exemplars showing question-reasoning-answer triples to establish the expected output format. **Second**, the diverse path sampling component queries the language model decoder multiple times (default 40 times) using stochastic sampling parameters (temperature, top-k) to generate distinct reasoning paths, where each path consists of intermediate reasoning tokens followed by a parsed final answer. **Third**, the answer aggregation component collects all final answers from the sampled paths and applies a marginalization operation—typically majority vote—to select the answer that appears most frequently across the diverse reasoning paths. Information flows unidirectionally: question → prompted input → multiple sampled outputs → parsed answers → aggregated final answer.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first explain the three-step self-consistency method as illustrated in Figure 1, establishing the overall workflow before diving into implementation details.
- We then detail the sampling mechanism and its hyperparameters, since generating diverse paths is the foundational operation that enables everything else.
- We next present the mathematical formulation for answer aggregation, including both unweighted and weighted strategies, to show how consistency is formally computed.
- We then explain the answer parsing strategy, which is task-dependent and critical for extracting final answers from generated text.
- We conclude with design rationale explaining why sampling beats beam search and why majority vote suffices over probability-weighted aggregation.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Overview and Core Mechanism**

This is an inference-time decoding strategy paper that modifies how language models select outputs during chain-of-thought reasoning without changing model weights or architecture. The fundamental insight is that complex reasoning problems admit multiple valid reasoning paths to the same correct answer, so sampling diverse paths and finding consensus answers should outperform committing to a single greedy path. Figure 1 illustrates the complete three-step method: (1) prompt the language model using chain-of-thought exemplars, (2) replace greedy decoding with sampling to generate diverse reasoning paths, and (3) marginalize out the reasoning paths by choosing the most consistent answer in the final answer set.

**Step 1: Chain-of-Thought Prompting Foundation**

The method begins with standard chain-of-thought prompting as established by Wei et al. (2022), where the language model receives a prompt containing manually written exemplars before the target question. Each exemplar demonstrates the expected format: a question followed by intermediate reasoning sentences followed by a final answer statement. For arithmetic reasoning tasks, the paper uses the same set of 8 manually written exemplars across all experiments to ensure fair comparison. For commonsense reasoning tasks, 4-7 exemplars are randomly chosen from the training set with manually composed chain-of-thought prompts. The prompt format ensures generated outputs follow a consistent structure of "{Reasoning paths}. The answer is X." which enables reliable answer parsing in the final step. This prompting stage remains identical to prior chain-of-thought work—the innovation lies entirely in how outputs are decoded and aggregated after prompting.

**Step 2: Diverse Reasoning Path Sampling**

Instead of greedily decoding the single most probable token sequence, self-consistency samples multiple candidate outputs from the language model's decoder to generate diverse reasoning paths. The paper states that self-consistency is compatible with most existing sampling algorithms, including temperature sampling, top-k sampling, and nucleus sampling. For UL2-20B and LaMDA-137B, the authors applied temperature sampling with $T = 0.5$ and truncated at the top-k tokens with $k = 40$. For PaLM-540B, they applied $T = 0.7$ with $k = 40$. For GPT-3, they used $T = 0.7$ without top-k truncation. These sampling parameters were chosen following settings suggested in prior work for open-text generation (Radford et al., 2019; Holtzman et al., 2020). The key design choice here is introducing controlled stochasticity: temperature scaling adjusts the probability distribution over tokens (higher temperature increases diversity), while top-k truncation limits sampling to the 40 most probable tokens at each step to avoid extremely low-probability outputs. The paper reports results averaged over 10 runs, where they sampled 40 outputs independently from the decoder in each run. Figure 2 demonstrates that sampling a higher number of reasoning paths (e.g., 40) leads to consistently better performance compared to fewer paths (1, 5, 10, 20), emphasizing that diversity in reasoning paths is critical to the method's success. Section 3.5 and Figure 4 (left) show that self-consistency is robust to various sampling strategies and parameters—varying temperature $T \in \{0.3, 0.5, 0.7\}$, top-k $k \in \{20, 40\}$, or nucleus sampling $p \in \{0.9, 0.95\}$ all yield improvements over greedy decoding, though the magnitude varies.

**Step 3: Answer Aggregation via Marginalization**

After sampling multiple reasoning paths, self-consistency aggregates the final answers by marginalizing out the sampled reasoning paths and selecting the most consistent answer. The paper introduces formal notation to describe this process: assume the generated answers $a_i$ are from a fixed answer set, $a_i \in \mathcal{A}$, where $i = 1, \ldots, m$ indexes the $m$ candidate outputs sampled from the decoder. Given a prompt and a question, self-consistency introduces an additional latent variable $r_i$, which is a sequence of tokens representing the reasoning path in the $i$-th output, then couples the generation of $(r_i, a_i)$ where $r_i \rightarrow a_i$, meaning generating a reasoning path $r_i$ is optional and only used to reach the final answer $a_i$. After sampling multiple $(r_i, a_i)$ pairs from the model's decoder, self-consistency applies a marginalization over $r_i$ by taking a majority vote over $a_i$, formally defined as:

$$\text{arg max}_a \sum_{i=1}^{m} \mathbb{1}(a_i = a)$$

where $\mathbb{1}(\cdot)$ is the indicator function that equals 1 when the condition is true and 0 otherwise. This equation selects the answer $a$ that appears most frequently across all sampled outputs. Table 1 compares different answer aggregation strategies on PaLM-540B across six reasoning tasks. The "unweighted sum (majority vote)" achieves accuracy nearly identical to "weighted sum (normalized)" across all tasks—for example, on GSM8K both achieve approximately 74% accuracy. The paper explains this occurs because for each $(r_i, a_i)$ pair, the normalized conditional probabilities $P(r_i, a_i \mid \text{prompt, question})$ are quite close to each other, meaning the language model regards those generations as "similarly likely." In contrast, the "weighted average" strategy—where each answer gets a score of its weighted sum divided by the count of that answer—results in much worse performance (e.g., 22.1% on GSM8K vs 74.4% for majority vote). The paper also notes that normalized weighted sum yields much higher accuracy compared to unnormalized weighted sum, because normalizing by output length accounts for the fact that longer sequences naturally have lower unnormalized probabilities.

**Mathematical Formulation of Probability Normalization**

When computing weighted aggregation strategies, the paper defines how to calculate the conditional probability of generating a reasoning path and answer pair. To compute $P(r_i, a_i \mid \text{prompt, question})$, one can either take the unnormalized probability of the model generating $(r_i, a_i)$ given the prompt and question, or normalize the conditional probability by the output length. The normalized probability is defined as:

$$P(r_i, a_i \mid \text{prompt, question}) = \exp\left(\frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^{K} \log P(t_k \mid \text{prompt, question}, t_1, \ldots, t_{k-1})\right)$$

where $\log P(t_k \mid \text{prompt, question}, t_1, \ldots, t_{k-1})$ is the log probability of generating the $k$-th token $t_k$ in $(r_i, a_i)$ conditioned on the previous tokens, and $K$ is the total number of tokens in $(r_i, a_i)$. This normalization by sequence length is important because longer generated sequences accumulate lower probabilities simply due to having more tokens, even if each individual token probability is high. The exponential of the average log probability gives a length-normalized measure of how likely the model considers the entire sequence.

**Answer Parsing Strategy**

The parser for extracting final answers is task dependent, as noted in footnote 1 of Section 2. For arithmetic reasoning tasks, the system parses the first numerical part as the final answer after the model generates "The answer is ". For commonsense reasoning tasks, the system parses the full string answer as the final answer after the model generates "The answer is ". Most generated outputs have a consistent format of "{Reasoning paths}. The answer is X." if the language model is prompted in this format. This structured output format is critical for reliable aggregation—if answers cannot be consistently extracted from generated text, the majority vote mechanism fails. Table 4 provides concrete examples showing how different reasoning paths from the same model on the same question can produce different intermediate steps but converge on the same final answer format.

**Design Rationale: Why Sampling Over Beam Search**

The paper explicitly compares self-consistency with beam search decoding in Table 6 on the UL2-20B model, reporting accuracy under the same number of beams and reasoning paths for fair comparison. Self-consistency with sampling significantly outperforms beam search on both AQuA and MultiArith tasks. For example, on MultiArith with 40 paths/beams, self-consistency using sampling achieves 14.7% accuracy while self-consistency using beam search achieves only 10.8%. The paper explains that "beam search yields a lower diversity in the outputs" while "in self-consistency the diversity of the reasoning paths is the key to a better performance." This is a critical design insight: beam search maintains multiple candidate sequences but they tend to be very similar to each other (differing only in late tokens), whereas temperature sampling with top-k produces genuinely diverse reasoning trajectories that explore different solution strategies. Table 6 also shows that "self-consistency using beam search" (applying self-consistency aggregation on beam search outputs) performs worse than "self-consistency using sampling," confirming that the sampling mechanism itself—not just the aggregation—is essential.

**Design Rationale: Why Majority Vote Over Weighted Aggregation**

Table 1 demonstrates that simple majority vote ("unweighted sum") achieves nearly identical accuracy to the more complex "weighted sum (normalized)" strategy across all six tasks tested. The paper investigated this phenomenon by examining the model's output probabilities and found that "for each $(r_i, a_i)$, the normalized conditional probabilities $P(r_i, a_i \mid \text{prompt, question})$ are quite close to each other." This means the language model assigns similar likelihood to different generated reasoning paths, making probability weighting provide minimal additional signal. Footnote 2 explains this also indicates "the language model is not well calibrated and thus cannot distinguish well between correct solutions and wrong solutions," which explains why prior work trained additional re-rankers to judge solution quality. The practical implication is that practitioners can use simple majority vote without computing expensive probability weights, reducing implementation complexity without sacrificing accuracy.

**Scaling Behavior and Sample Count Selection**

Figure 2 plots accuracy against the number of sampled reasoning paths (1, 5, 10, 20, 40) across four different tasks using LaMDA-137B. The results show monotonic improvement: sampling more paths consistently yields higher accuracy, with no evidence of saturation at 40 paths. However, Section 5 (Conclusion) notes that "in most cases the performance saturates quickly" and practitioners "can try a small number of paths (e.g., 5 or 10) as a starting point to realize most of the gains while not incurring too much cost." This suggests diminishing returns—the jump from 1 to 10 paths provides larger marginal gains than the jump from 30 to 40 paths. Figure 4 (right) shows self-consistency improves performance across all model scales for the LaMDA series, though "the gain is relatively lower for smaller models due to certain abilities (e.g., arithmetic) only emerge when the model reaches a sufficient scale." This indicates self-consistency amplifies existing reasoning capabilities rather than creating them de novo.

**Robustness to Imperfect Prompts and Alternative Reasoning Formats**

Section 3.5 demonstrates self-consistency works under degraded conditions beyond standard chain-of-thought prompting. Table 8 shows results on GSM8K with imperfect prompts where numbers in reasoning paths are swapped with random numbers except the final answer. With greedy decoding, imperfect prompts decrease accuracy from 17.1% to 14.9%, but self-consistency with 40 paths recovers to 23.4%—even exceeding the original perfect-prompt greedy baseline. The method also works with equation-only prompts (e.g., "3 + 2 = 5" instead of natural language reasoning), though gains are smaller because "equations are much shorter and less opportunity remains for generating diversity in the decoding process." Self-consistency also improves zero-shot chain-of-thought prompting (Kojima et al., 2022) by +26.2% on GSM8K (from 43.0% to 69.2%), showing the method generalizes beyond few-shot settings.

**Uncertainty Estimation Capability**

Figure 5 shows that consistency—defined as the percentage of decodes agreeing with the final aggregated answer—is highly correlated with accuracy on GSM8K. The paper states this "suggests that one can use self-consistency to provide an uncertainty estimate of the model in its generated solutions." In practical terms, low consistency (e.g., 20% of paths agree on the final answer) indicates the model has low confidence and the answer is likely wrong, while high consistency (e.g., 90% agreement) indicates high confidence. This gives self-consistency an additional benefit beyond accuracy improvement: it confers "some ability for the model to 'know when it doesn't know'" without requiring additional training or calibration procedures.

**Computational Cost Considerations**

The paper acknowledges in Section 5 that "one limitation of self-consistency is that it incurs more computation cost" proportional to the number of sampled paths. Generating 40 paths requires approximately 40× the inference computation of a single greedy decode. Appendix A.2 provides resource details: inference jobs take 1 to 4 hours over about 1,000 examples for UL2 and LaMDA-137B, and 2 to 12 hours for PaLM-540B, with some tasks not exceeding 2 days. The authors suggest using fewer paths (5 or 10) as a practical compromise, since Figure 2 shows substantial gains even at lower sample counts. This cost-accuracy tradeoff is inherent to the method—diversity requires multiple forward passes—but the paper argues the accuracy gains justify the cost for high-stakes reasoning applications.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the conceptual breakthroughs of self-consistency beyond the mechanical implementation details covered previously. We distinguish between incremental engineering improvements and fundamental shifts in how we understand reasoning in language models.

### 4.1 Redefining the Ensemble Unit: From Models to Paths

Prior work treated ensembling as a **multi-model operation**. To improve robustness, practitioners typically trained or deployed multiple distinct models and aggregated their predictions. This paper introduces a fundamental innovation: the **single-model self-ensemble**.

*   **Prior Assumption:** Diversity requires architectural or weight diversity (i.e., different models).
*   **Self-Consistency Insight:** Diversity can be induced within a **single fixed model** by sampling diverse reasoning trajectories.

This distinction is critical for deployment efficiency. Table 10 demonstrates that traditional model ensembling can actually degrade performance if model capacities vary. For example, ensembling LaMDA-137B with PaLM-540B yields only $36.9\%$ accuracy on GSM8K, whereas PaLM-540B alone with self-consistency achieves $74.4\%$. The paper argues that weaker models "drag down the performance of higher-capacity models" in traditional ensembles. By contrast, self-consistency leverages the full capacity of the single best available model repeatedly.

This shifts the cost profile of improvement: instead of training $N$ models (costly in carbon and engineering), one trains a single model and spends compute at inference time to sample $N$ paths. This is a **fundamental innovation** in inference scaling laws, suggesting that compute can substitute for model diversity in reasoning tasks.

### 4.2 Decoupling Path Validity from Answer Correctness

A subtle but profound theoretical contribution is the treatment of the reasoning path $r_i$ as a **latent variable** to be marginalized out, rather than a ground-truth trace to be verified.

*   **Prior Assumption:** To trust an answer, the reasoning steps leading to it must be factually and logically perfect (e.g., verifier models trained to score reasoning quality).
*   **Self-Consistency Insight:** The reasoning path can contain hallucinations or minor errors, provided the **final answer** $a_i$ remains consistent across diverse paths.

Table 4 provides concrete evidence of this decoupling. In the StrategyQA example, the sampled paths contain factually incorrect population numbers (e.g., "Albany, Georgia has a population of about 88,000" vs "Albany, New York has a population of about 95,000"), yet both paths correctly conclude the answer is "no". The greedy decode, conversely, produces a confident but incorrect "yes".

This implies that **answer consistency is a stronger signal than reasoning fidelity**. By mathematically marginalizing over $r_i$ via the operation:

$$\text{arg max}_a \sum_{i=1}^{m} \mathbb{1}(a_i = a)$$

the method effectively integrates over noisy reasoning processes to recover the stable signal (the answer). This is a **fundamental innovation** because it relaxes the requirement for perfect intermediate generation, making the system robust to the known hallucination tendencies of large language models. It suggests we do not need to solve the "reasoning verification" problem to improve reasoning accuracy; we only need to solve the "answer aggregation" problem.

### 4.3 Consistency Over Probability: Challenging Greedy Optimality

Standard decoding strategies operate on the assumption that the **most probable sequence** is the **most correct sequence**. Self-consistency challenges this axiom specifically for reasoning tasks.

*   **Prior Assumption:** High log-probability sequences (greedy or beam search) are optimal. Sample-and-rank improves this by selecting the highest probability among samples.
*   **Self-Consistency Insight:** In complex reasoning, the **most frequent answer** across diverse paths is more reliable than the **highest probability path**.

Figure 3 explicitly compares self-consistency against **sample-and-rank**, where the latter selects the final answer from the single sequence with the highest log probability. Self-consistency significantly outperforms sample-and-rank across all sample counts. For instance, on GSM8K with 40 samples, self-consistency reaches $\approx 24\%$ accuracy (on GPT-3 code-davinci-001) while sample-and-rank lags significantly behind.

This reveals a misalignment in language model probability calibration for reasoning tasks. As noted in footnote 2 of Section 2, the model's normalized conditional probabilities $P(r_i, a_i \mid \text{prompt, question})$ are "quite close to each other" regardless of correctness, meaning the model "cannot distinguish well between correct solutions and wrong solutions." Therefore, relying on $P(\text{sequence})$ is futile. Relying on $\text{Frequency}(\text{answer})$ leverages the geometry of the solution space: correct answers occupy a larger "basin of attraction" in the reasoning landscape than incorrect ones. This is a **fundamental innovation** in decoding theory, suggesting that for reasoning, **convergence** is a better objective than **likelihood**.

### 4.4 Implicit Uncertainty Quantification Without Calibration

Beyond accuracy gains, self-consistency provides a built-in mechanism for **uncertainty estimation**, a capability typically requiring additional training heads or temperature scaling.

*   **Prior Assumption:** Model confidence requires explicit calibration layers or post-hoc scaling trained on validation data.
*   **Self-Consistency Insight:** The **consistency rate** (percentage of paths agreeing with the final answer) serves as a direct proxy for model confidence.

Figure 5 plots consistency against accuracy on GSM8K, showing a strong positive correlation. When the model is correct, a high percentage of sampled paths (e.g., $>80\%$) converge on the same answer. When the model is incorrect, the paths diverge. This allows practitioners to set confidence thresholds: if only $30\%$ of paths agree, the system can flag the query for human review or abstain from answering.

This is a **significant capability innovation** because it is entirely unsupervised. It emerges from the decoding strategy itself rather than model architecture. It addresses a critical safety concern noted in the Ethics Statement: since models can generate "nonsensical or non-factual reasoning paths," knowing *when* the model is uncertain is as valuable as knowing the answer itself. This transforms self-consistency from a mere accuracy booster into a **reliability framework** for deployment.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validated self-consistency across diverse reasoning tasks, models, and conditions. We walk through the evaluation methodology, present the quantitative results with specific numbers, and critically assess whether the experiments support the paper's central claims.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Task Categories**

The authors evaluate self-consistency across three broad categories of reasoning tasks, each testing different cognitive capabilities:

*   **Arithmetic Reasoning (6 datasets):** AddSub, MultiArith, ASDiv, AQuA, SVAMP, and GSM8K. These tasks require multi-step numerical computation and are drawn from the Math Word Problem Repository (Koncel-Kedziorski et al., 2016) and related benchmarks. GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) is particularly challenging, containing grade-school math problems that require 2-8 reasoning steps.
*   **Commonsense Reasoning (3 datasets):** CommonsenseQA (Talmor et al., 2019), StrategyQA (Geva et al., 2021), and ARC (Clark et al., 2018), which includes both Easy and Challenge splits. These tasks test implicit world knowledge and multi-hop inference rather than explicit calculation.
*   **Symbolic Reasoning (2 tasks):** Last letter concatenation and Coinflip from Wei et al. (2022). The paper tests these in an **out-of-distribution (OOD)** setting where prompts contain 2-letter or 2-flip examples but test examples use 4 letters or 4 flips. This evaluates whether self-consistency improves generalization beyond the prompt distribution.

Additionally, Section 3.3 evaluates self-consistency on **common NLP tasks** where chain-of-thought prompting can sometimes hurt performance: BoolQ and HotpotQA (closed-book question answering), and e-SNLI, ANLI, and RTE (natural language inference).

**Language Models**

The experiments span four language models with varying architectures and scales (Section 3.1):

| Model | Parameters | Architecture | Availability |
|-------|------------|--------------|--------------|
| UL2 | 20B | Encoder-decoder | Open-sourced |
| LaMDA | 137B | Decoder-only | Internal (Google) |
| GPT-3 | 175B | Decoder-only | Public API (Codex) |
| PaLM | 540B | Decoder-only | Internal (Google) |

This model selection is strategic: it tests whether self-consistency benefits scale with model size, and whether it works across different architectures (encoder-decoder vs. decoder-only).

**Baseline and Metrics**

The primary baseline is **chain-of-thought prompting with greedy decoding** (Wei et al., 2022), referred to as "CoT-prompting" throughout the paper. All experiments use **accuracy** as the evaluation metric, computed as the percentage of questions where the aggregated final answer matches the ground truth.

For self-consistency, results are **averaged over 10 independent runs**, with 40 outputs sampled from the decoder in each run (Section 3.2). This reduces variance from the stochastic sampling process. The standard deviation is reported as ≤0.5% for all tasks and is omitted from tables for clarity (footnote 7, Section 3.2).

**Sampling Hyperparameters**

The authors follow established settings for open-text generation (Section 3.1):

*   **UL2-20B and LaMDA-137B:** Temperature $T = 0.5$, top-$k = 40$
*   **PaLM-540B:** Temperature $T = 0.7$, top-$k = 40$
*   **GPT-3:** Temperature $T = 0.7$, no top-$k$ truncation

Section 3.5 includes an ablation showing self-consistency is robust to these choices (Figure 4, left).

**Prompting Strategy**

All experiments use **few-shot prompting** without any fine-tuning. For arithmetic tasks, the same 8 manually written exemplars from Wei et al. (2022) are used. For commonsense tasks, 4-7 exemplars are randomly chosen from the training set with manually composed chain-of-thought prompts. Appendix A.3 provides the full prompt sets. Section 3.5 and Table 9 show self-consistency is robust to different prompt sets.

| GPT-3 (code-davinci-002) | SVAMP | 75.8% | 86.8% | +11.0% |

Several patterns emerge from Table 2:

1.  **Gains scale with model size:** UL2-20B sees +3% to +6% improvements, while LaMDA-137B and GPT-3 see +9% to +23% improvements. PaLM-540B achieves +12% to +18% on tasks where it already had high baseline accuracy.
2.  **State-of-the-art without supervision:** Despite being unsupervised and task-agnostic, self-consistency with PaLM-540B or GPT-3 surpasses prior methods that required "task-specific training, or fine-tuning with thousands of examples" (Section 3.2). For example, on GSM8K, the previous best was 55% using GPT-3 fine-tuned with 7.5k examples plus a 175B verifier (Table 2 footnote g). Self-consistency achieves 74.4% with PaLM-540B and 78.0% with GPT-3 code-davinci-002—**without any fine-tuning**.
3.  **Hardest tasks benefit most:** AQuA and GSM8K, which require more complex multi-step reasoning, show larger absolute gains (+12% to +18%) compared to simpler tasks like AddSub (+1.8% to +10.6%).

**Commonsense and Symbolic Reasoning: Broad Applicability**

Table 3 shows self-consistency improves commonsense reasoning across all models and tasks:

| Model | Task | CoT-Prompting | Self-Consistency | Absolute Gain |
|-------|------|---------------|------------------|---------------|
| PaLM-540B | StrategyQA | 75.3% | 81.6% | +6.3% |
| PaLM-540B | ARC-Challenge | 85.2% | 88.7% | +3.5% |
| GPT-3 (code-davinci-002) | StrategyQA | 73.4% | 79.8% | **+6.4%** |
| GPT-3 (code-davinci-002) | ARC-Challenge | 83.6% | 87.5% | **+3.9%** |
| LaMDA-137B | CommonsenseQA | 57.9% | 63.1% | +5.2% |

For symbolic reasoning in the OOD setting (4 letters/flips instead of 2), self-consistency still provides gains:

| Model | Task | CoT-Prompting | Self-Consistency | Absolute Gain |
|-------|------|---------------|------------------|---------------|
| PaLM-540B | Letter (4) | 65.8% | 70.8% | +5.0% |
| PaLM-540B | Coinflip (4) | 88.2% | 91.2% | +3.0% |
| GPT-3 (code-davinci-002) | Coinflip (4) | 99.0% | 99.5% | +0.5% |

The authors note that PaLM-540B and GPT-3 can already achieve "perfect in-distribution accuracy" on symbolic tasks, making the OOD setting more challenging. Self-consistency's gains here demonstrate it improves **generalization**, not just in-distribution performance.

**When Chain-of-Thought Hurts: Self-Consistency Recovers Performance**

Section 3.3 addresses a critical concern: Ye & Durrett (2022) showed that chain-of-thought prompting can sometimes **decrease** performance compared to standard prompting on common NLP tasks. Table 5 tests whether self-consistency can mitigate this:

| Task | Standard Prompting | CoT-Prompting | Self-Consistency |
|------|-------------------|---------------|------------------|
| ANLI-R1 | 69.1% | 68.8% | **78.5%** |
| ANLI-R2 | 55.8% | 58.9% | **64.5%** |
| ANLI-R3 | 55.8% | 60.6% | **63.4%** |
| e-SNLI | 85.8% | 81.0% | **88.4%** |
| RTE | 84.8% | 79.1% | **86.3%** |
| BoolQ | 71.3% | 74.2% | **78.4%** |
| HotpotQA (F1) | 36.8% | 39.8% | **44.6%** |

On ANLI-R1, e-SNLI, and RTE, CoT-prompting with greedy decoding **underperforms** standard prompting (e.g., e-SNLI: 85.8% → 81.0%). However, self-consistency **recovers and exceeds** standard prompting performance (e-SNLI: 81.0% → 88.4%). This is a crucial finding: self-consistency makes chain-of-thought prompting **more reliable** even on tasks where rationales might introduce noise.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

**Number of Sampled Paths: Diminishing Returns**

Figure 2 plots accuracy against the number of sampled reasoning paths (1, 5, 10, 20, 40) on LaMDA-137B. The results show:

*   **Monotonic improvement:** Accuracy increases with more samples across all tasks (MultiArith, SVAMP, CommonsenseQA, ARC-Challenge).
*   **No saturation at 40 paths:** The curves do not flatten completely, suggesting more samples could yield further gains.
*   **Practical sweet spot:** Section 5 (Conclusion) notes that "performance saturates quickly" and practitioners can use 5 or 10 paths to "realize most of the gains while not incurring too much cost."

For example, on GSM8K with PaLM-540B (Figure 8, Appendix), accuracy rises from ~56% at 1 path (greedy) to ~70% at 10 paths, then to ~74% at 40 paths. The marginal gain from 10 to 40 paths is smaller than from 1 to 10.

**Sampling Strategy Robustness**

Figure 4 (left) and Figure 6 (Appendix A.1.1) test self-consistency under various sampling strategies on PaLM-540B and LaMDA-137B:

*   **Temperature variation:** $T \in \{0.3, 0.5, 0.7\}$ all improve over greedy decoding.
*   **Top-k variation:** $k \in \{20, 40\}$ and no top-k all work.
*   **Nucleus sampling:** $p \in \{0.9, 0.95\}$ also yields gains.

The authors conclude self-consistency is "robust to sampling strategies and parameters" (Section 3.5). This is practically important: practitioners need not tune sampling hyperparameters precisely to benefit.

**Comparison to Beam Search: Diversity Matters**

Table 6 directly compares self-consistency with beam search decoding on UL2-20B, using the same number of beams/paths for fair comparison:

| Method | Paths/Beams | AQuA | MultiArith |
|--------|-------------|------|------------|
| Beam search (top beam) | 40 | 10.2% | 10.5% |
| Self-consistency + beam search | 40 | 24.2% | 10.8% |
| **Self-consistency + sampling** | **40** | **26.9%** | **14.7%** |

The key insight: **beam search yields lower diversity** in outputs (Li & Jurafsky, 2016), which hurts self-consistency. Even when applying self-consistency aggregation to beam search outputs ("Self-consistency using beam search"), performance is worse than using sampling. This validates the design choice to use stochastic sampling rather than deterministic beam search.

**Comparison to Ensemble Approaches**

Table 7 compares self-consistency to two ensemble baselines on LaMDA-137B:

| Method | GSM8K | MultiArith | SVAMP |
|--------|-------|------------|-------|
| CoT-prompting (single) | 17.1% | 51.8% | 38.9% |
| Ensemble (3 prompt sets) | 18.6% | 57.1% | 42.1% |
| Ensemble (40 prompt permutations) | 19.2% | 60.9% | 42.7% |
| **Self-consistency (40 paths)** | **27.7%** | **75.7%** | **53.3%** |

Prompt ensembling provides modest gains (+1.5% to +2.1% on GSM8K), but self-consistency provides **much larger gains** (+10.6% on GSM8K). Table 10 (Appendix A.1.3) further shows that **model ensembling** can hurt performance: ensembling LaMDA-137B + PaLM-540B achieves only 36.9% on GSM8K, versus 74.4% for PaLM-540B alone with self-consistency. This is because "lower-capacity models drag down the performance of higher-capacity models."

**Robustness to Imperfect Prompts**

Table 8 tests whether self-consistency can recover from degraded prompts:

| Condition | GSM8K Accuracy (LaMDA-137B) |
|-----------|-----------------------------|
| Correct CoT prompt (greedy) | 17.1% |
| Imperfect CoT prompt (greedy) | 14.9% |
| Imperfect CoT prompt + self-consistency | **23.4%** |
| Equation-only prompt (greedy) | 5.0% |
| Equation-only prompt + self-consistency | **6.5%** |
| Zero-shot CoT (greedy, PaLM-540B) | 43.0% |
| Zero-shot CoT + self-consistency | **69.2%** |

"Imperfect prompts" swap numbers in reasoning paths with random numbers except the final answer (footnote 9). Self-consistency not only recovers the loss but **exceeds the original greedy baseline**. This demonstrates self-consistency provides **robustness to prompt quality**, a practical advantage when human annotators make mistakes.

**Uncertainty Estimation: Consistency Correlates with Accuracy**

Figure 5 plots consistency (percentage of decodes agreeing with the final answer) against accuracy on GSM8K. The correlation is strong: when consistency is high (e.g., 80-100%), accuracy is high; when consistency is low (e.g., 20-40%), accuracy is low. The authors state this allows self-consistency to "provide an uncertainty estimate" and gives the model ability to "know when it doesn't know" (Section 3.5).

This is an **emergent capability**: no additional training or calibration is needed. Practitioners can use low consistency as a flag to abstain from answering or route to human review.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

**Strengths of the Experimental Design**

1.  **Comprehensive model coverage:** Testing across four models spanning 20B to 540B parameters demonstrates the method generalizes across scales and architectures.
2.  **Diverse task coverage:** Arithmetic, commonsense, symbolic, and common NLP tasks test different reasoning capabilities, not just math.
3.  **Strong baselines:** Comparison to greedy CoT, beam search, sample-and-rank, prompt ensembles, and model ensembles thoroughly validates the approach against alternatives.
4.  **Ablation depth:** Sampling strategy, path count, prompt quality, and reasoning format (natural language vs. equations) are all ablated.
5.  **Reproducibility efforts:** Two public models (UL2, GPT-3 via API) are included, and full prompts are provided in Appendix A.3.

**Limitations and Open Questions**

1.  **Computational cost not fully quantified:** Section 5 acknowledges self-consistency "incurs more computation cost" proportional to sample count, but Appendix A.2 only provides wall-clock time ranges (1-12 hours per task). The paper does not report **tokens per second**, **cost per query**, or **energy consumption**, which are critical for deployment decisions.
2.  **Diminishing returns not precisely characterized:** Figure 2 shows monotonic improvement up to 40 paths, but the exact point of diminishing returns is task-dependent and not quantified. Practitioners must empirically determine the cost-accuracy tradeoff.
3.  **Failure cases not systematically analyzed:** Table 4 shows examples where self-consistency repairs greedy errors, but the paper does not analyze cases where **all sampled paths converge on the wrong answer**. This failure mode is acknowledged ("language models can sometimes generate incorrect or nonsensical reasoning paths") but not quantified.
4.  **Internal models limit reproducibility:** LaMDA-137B and PaLM-540B are not publicly available. While prompts are provided, independent researchers cannot verify the largest gains (PaLM-540B achieves the highest absolute accuracies).
5.  **Consistency-accuracy correlation not quantified:** Figure 5 shows a correlation but does not report a correlation coefficient (e.g., Pearson's $r$) or calibration metrics (e.g., Expected Calibration Error). The claim that consistency provides uncertainty estimates is suggestive but not rigorously validated.

**Do the Experiments Support the Claims?**

The experiments **strongly support** the core claims:

| Claim | Evidence | Verdict |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Self-consistency improves CoT prompting | Table 2, Table 3: +3% to +24% gains across all models/tasks | **Supported** |
| Gains scale with model size | Table 2: UL2-20B (+3-6%) vs. PaLM-540B (+12-18%) | **Supported** |
| Outperforms alternative decoding | Figure 3 (sample-and-rank), Table 6 (beam search), Table 7 (ensembles) | **Supported** |
| Robust to sampling strategies | Figure 4 (left), Figure 6 | **Supported** |
| Works with imperfect prompts | Table 8 | **Supported** |
| Provides uncertainty estimates | Figure 5 | **Suggestively supported** (correlation shown, but not quantified) |
| Achieves SOTA without fine-tuning | Table 2 footnotes: surpasses fine-tuned + verifier methods | **Supported** |

The most compelling evidence is Table 2: self-consistency with PaLM-540B achieves 74.4% on GSM8K, surpassing the prior best of 55% that required fine-tuning GPT-3 on 7.5k examples plus a 175B verifier. This is a **17.9% absolute gain** with **zero additional training**.

However, the uncertainty estimation claim (Figure 5) would benefit from more rigorous calibration analysis. Additionally, the computational cost tradeoff is acknowledged but not fully quantified, leaving practitioners to empirically determine the optimal number of samples for their use case.

**Trade-offs Summary**

| Factor | Benefit | Cost |
|--------|---------|------|
| Accuracy | +3% to +24% absolute gain | ~40× inference compute (for 40 paths) |
| Robustness | Works with imperfect prompts, zero-shot CoT | Requires prompt format consistency for answer parsing |
| Uncertainty | Consistency correlates with accuracy | No formal calibration guarantees |
| Deployment | Single model, no fine-tuning | Higher latency per query |

The paper makes a compelling case that for **high-stakes reasoning applications** where accuracy is critical, the computational cost is justified. For latency-sensitive applications, practitioners can use fewer paths (5-10) to capture most gains at lower cost.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While self-consistency demonstrates substantial accuracy improvements across diverse reasoning tasks, the approach is not without constraints. This section examines the method's assumptions, computational costs, failure modes, and open questions that practitioners must consider before deployment.

### 6.1 Computational Cost and Latency Overhead

The most explicit limitation acknowledged in the paper is **computational expense**. Section 5 states: "one limitation of self-consistency is that it incurs more computation cost." This cost scales linearly with the number of sampled paths:

| Sample Count | Relative Compute Cost | Typical Accuracy Gain (GSM8K, PaLM-540B) |
|--------------|----------------------|------------------------------------------|
| 1 (greedy) | 1× | Baseline (56.5%) |
| 10 | ~10× | ~70% (+13.5%) |
| 40 | ~40× | 74.4% (+17.9%) |

Appendix A.2 provides wall-clock timing: inference jobs take **1 to 4 hours** for UL2-20B and LaMDA-137B, and **2 to 12 hours** for PaLM-540B, over approximately 1,000 examples per task. Some tasks "do not exceed 2 days" but this varies by task complexity.

**Critical gap**: The paper does not report:
- Tokens per second throughput
- Cost per query in dollar terms
- Energy consumption metrics
- Memory footprint during multi-path sampling

For latency-sensitive applications (e.g., real-time dialogue systems), generating 40 independent forward passes per query may be prohibitive. The authors suggest using "a small number of paths (e.g., 5 or 10) as a starting point to realize most of the gains while not incurring too much cost" (Section 5), but Figure 2 shows **no saturation at 40 paths**—accuracy continues climbing monotonically. This creates ambiguity: practitioners must empirically determine their own cost-accuracy tradeoff without clear guidance on diminishing returns.

### 6.2 Structural Assumptions About Answer Format

Self-consistency relies on a critical assumption: **the final answer must be extractable and comparable across samples**. Section 2 states: "self-consistency can be applied only to problems where the final answer is from a fixed answer set."

**Footnote 1** specifies the parsing strategy:
- For arithmetic reasoning: parse the first numerical part after "The answer is "
- For commonsense reasoning: parse the full string answer after "The answer is "

This creates several constraints:

1. **Consistent output formatting is required**: If the model deviates from the "{Reasoning paths}. The answer is X." format, answer extraction fails. The paper notes "most generated outputs have a consistent format" but does not quantify the failure rate of malformed outputs.

2. **Open-ended generation is not supported**: The authors acknowledge the method could "in principle be extended to open-text generation problems if a good metric of consistency can be defined between multiple generations," but this remains unimplemented. Tasks like summarization, creative writing, or open-domain dialogue cannot directly benefit.

3. **Answer equivalence is non-trivial**: For numerical answers, "18" and "$18" may not match without normalization. For string answers, "yes" and "Yes" require case-insensitive comparison. The paper does not detail the preprocessing pipeline, which could affect reproducibility.

Table 8 shows self-consistency works with "equation-only" prompts (e.g., "3 + 2 = 5" instead of natural language), but gains are smaller (+1.5% on GSM8K) because "equations are much shorter and less opportunity remains for generating diversity in the decoding process." This reveals a dependency: **longer reasoning paths enable more diversity, which enables better consistency signals**.

### 6.3 Failure Modes: When Consistency Misleads

The paper demonstrates cases where self-consistency repairs greedy decoding errors (Table 4, Table 12, Table 13), but does not systematically analyze the **failure mode where all sampled paths converge on the wrong answer**.

This is a critical blind spot. If the model's reasoning capabilities are fundamentally insufficient for a task, sampling 40 incorrect paths that all agree will produce high consistency but low accuracy. Figure 5 shows consistency correlates with accuracy, but correlation is not causation—and the paper does not report:

- The rate of **high-consistency wrong answers** (e.g., 90% of paths agree on an incorrect answer)
- Whether certain question types are prone to consistent-but-wrong convergence
- How model scale affects this failure mode

Section 5 acknowledges: "language models can sometimes generate incorrect or nonsensical reasoning paths (e.g., the StrategyQA example in Table 4, the two population numbers are not exactly correct)." In that example, both sampled paths contain factually wrong population figures (88,000 vs 95,000 for Albany cities) but reach the correct conclusion. This reveals another limitation: **self-consistency validates answers, not reasoning fidelity**. For applications requiring auditable reasoning (e.g., medical diagnosis, legal analysis), this is problematic—the model may produce the right answer via hallucinated intermediate steps.

### 6.4 Model Calibration and Probability Misalignment

Footnote 2 in Section 2 reveals a deeper issue: the language model "is not well calibrated and thus cannot distinguish well between correct solutions and wrong solutions." This explains why weighted aggregation provides minimal benefit over majority vote (Table 1)—the model's probability scores do not reliably indicate correctness.

**Implication**: Self-consistency works *around* poor calibration rather than fixing it. The method treats model probabilities as uninformative and relies solely on answer frequency. This is effective but suggests:

1. The underlying model's confidence estimates cannot be trusted for decision-making
2. Applications requiring calibrated uncertainty (e.g., risk-sensitive domains) still need additional calibration layers
3. The consistency metric (Figure 5) is an *emergent* uncertainty signal, not a formally calibrated one

The paper does not report standard calibration metrics like **Expected Calibration Error (ECE)** or **Brier scores**, making it difficult to assess whether self-consistency improves model calibration or merely provides a heuristic confidence proxy.

### 6.5 Reproducibility Constraints

While the paper includes two public models (UL2-20B and GPT-3 via API), the **largest accuracy gains come from non-public models**:

| Model | GSM8K Self-Consistency | Availability |
|-------|----------------------|--------------|
| UL2-20B | 7.3% | Open-sourced |
| LaMDA-137B | 27.7% | Internal (Google) |
| GPT-3 code-davinci-002 | 78.0% | Public API |
| PaLM-540B | 74.4% | Internal (Google) |

The state-of-the-art results on PaLM-540B (74.4% on GSM8K, 99.3% on MultiArith) cannot be independently verified. Appendix A.3 provides prompts, but without access to the model weights or inference infrastructure, researchers cannot:

- Verify the reported accuracies
- Test on additional benchmarks
- Analyze failure cases in detail
- Extend the method to new domains

This limits the paper's scientific impact: the most compelling results are tied to proprietary systems. The GPT-3 results (78.0% on GSM8K) are reproducible via API but incur monetary costs that may be prohibitive for academic researchers.

### 6.6 Task and Domain Limitations

The evaluation focuses on **reasoning tasks with clear ground truth answers**. Several important domains are not addressed:

1. **Multi-answer tasks**: Questions with multiple valid answers (e.g., "name three countries in Europe") cannot use majority vote without modification.

2. **Generative tasks**: Summarization, translation, and creative writing lack fixed answer sets. The paper acknowledges this limitation in Section 2 but provides no solution.

3. **Interactive tasks**: Dialogue systems, tool use, and multi-turn reasoning require consistency across turns, not just within a single generation. This is unexplored.

4. **Domain-specific reasoning**: Medical, legal, and scientific reasoning may require domain-specific validation beyond answer consistency. The paper tests general benchmarks but not specialized domains.

Section 3.3 shows self-consistency helps on common NLP tasks (BoolQ, RTE, e-SNLI) where CoT prompting can hurt performance, but these are still classification-style tasks with fixed answer spaces.

### 6.7 Scaling Behavior and Model Dependency

Figure 4 (right) shows self-consistency improves performance across model scales, but "the gain is relatively lower for smaller models due to certain abilities (e.g., arithmetic) only emerge when the model reaches a sufficient scale."

**Implication**: Self-consistency **amplifies existing capabilities** rather than creating them. A model that cannot reason at all will not benefit from sampling 40 incorrect paths. This creates a dependency:

- Small models (&lt;20B parameters) see modest gains (+3% to +6% on UL2-20B)
- Large models (>100B parameters) see substantial gains (+10% to +24% on LaMDA-137B and PaLM-540B)

For practitioners with limited compute budgets who cannot access large models, self-consistency provides diminishing returns. The method is most effective for those who already have access to large-scale models—precisely the group least constrained by the 40× inference cost.

### 6.8 Open Questions and Future Directions

The paper identifies several unresolved issues in Section 5:

1. **Fine-tuning with self-consistency data**: "One could use self-consistency to generate better supervised data to fine-tune the model, such that the model can give more accurate predictions in a single inference run after fine-tuning." This is proposed but not implemented.

2. **Grounding reasoning paths**: "Further work is needed to better ground models' rationale generations" to prevent hallucinated intermediate steps (e.g., incorrect population numbers in Table 4).

3. **Optimal sample count**: The paper does not provide a formula or heuristic for determining the optimal number of paths given a target accuracy and cost budget.

4. **Combination with other methods**: Table 11 (Appendix A.1.4) shows self-consistency can combine with prompt ensembling, but the marginal benefit is small (+1.0% on GSM8K). The optimal combination of decoding strategies remains unexplored.

5. **Theoretical understanding**: Why does answer consistency correlate with correctness? The paper offers intuition ("correct reasoning processes tend to have greater agreement") but no formal analysis of the solution space geometry that would explain this phenomenon.

### 6.9 Summary of Trade-offs

| Dimension | Benefit | Cost/Constraint |
|-----------|---------|-----------------|
| **Accuracy** | +3% to +24% absolute gain | ~40× inference compute for maximum gain |
| **Latency** | N/A | 40× slower per query (sequential sampling) |
| **Robustness** | Works with imperfect prompts, zero-shot CoT | Requires consistent answer format |
| **Uncertainty** | Consistency correlates with accuracy | Not formally calibrated; no ECE metrics |
| **Deployment** | Single model, no fine-tuning | High memory/compute during inference |
| **Applicability** | Arithmetic, commonsense, symbolic reasoning | Not suited for open-ended generation |
| **Reproducibility** | Prompts provided; GPT-3 accessible | Best results on non-public models (PaLM, LaMDA) |

**Bottom line**: Self-consistency is a powerful inference-time method for reasoning tasks where accuracy is paramount and computational cost is secondary. For latency-sensitive or resource-constrained deployments, practitioners should experiment with fewer paths (5-10) to capture most gains at lower cost. For open-ended generation or domains requiring auditable reasoning, additional validation mechanisms are needed beyond answer consistency alone.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how self-consistency reshapes our understanding of reasoning in language models, what research pathways it opens, and how practitioners should integrate it into real-world systems. We move beyond what the method achieves to consider what it enables.

### 7.1 Shifting the Paradigm: From Single-Path to Multi-Path Reasoning

Self-consistency introduces a fundamental conceptual shift in how we think about decoding for reasoning tasks. Prior to this work, the dominant assumption was that **the most probable sequence is the best sequence**. Greedy decoding and beam search operate on this principle: find the highest-likelihood path and trust it. Self-consistency challenges this axiom by demonstrating that **answer frequency across diverse paths is a stronger signal than sequence probability**.

This has three landscape-level implications:

**1. Decoding Strategy Matters as Much as Model Scale**

The paper shows that decoding strategy modifications can yield accuracy gains comparable to—or exceeding—model scaling. Table 2 demonstrates that self-consistency on PaLM-540B achieves +17.9% on GSM8K, surpassing prior methods that required fine-tuning GPT-3 on 7,500 examples plus a 175B verifier (Table 2 footnote g). This suggests that **inference-time compute can substitute for training-time optimization** in reasoning tasks. For practitioners, this means investing in smarter decoding may be more cost-effective than pursuing marginal model improvements.

**2. Diversity Trumps Optimality for Reasoning**

Table 6 directly compares beam search (which maintains multiple high-probability candidates) with sampling-based self-consistency. Despite beam search exploring multiple sequences, it underperforms sampling because "beam search yields a lower diversity in the outputs" while "in self-consistency the diversity of the reasoning paths is the key to a better performance." This reveals that for reasoning, **exploring different solution strategies** matters more than **exploiting the most likely tokens**. This insight extends beyond language models to any system performing multi-step inference.

**3. Consistency as a Proxy for Correctness**

Figure 5 shows that consistency (percentage of paths agreeing on the final answer) correlates strongly with accuracy. This transforms self-consistency from a mere accuracy booster into a **reliability framework**. The paper states this gives models "some ability for the model to 'know when it doesn't know'" without additional training. This emergent uncertainty estimation capability addresses a critical gap in deploying language models for high-stakes applications where knowing *when to abstain* is as important as knowing the answer.

### 7.2 Research Directions Enabled by Self-Consistency

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions and implicitly suggests others through its experimental design. We categorize these into immediate extensions and longer-term research programs.

**Immediate Extensions (Directly Suggested in the Paper)**

| Direction | Paper Reference | Research Question |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Fine-tuning with self-consistency data | Section 5 | Can self-consistency-generated answers create better training data for single-pass inference? |
| Grounding reasoning paths | Section 5, Table 4 | How do we prevent hallucinated intermediate steps while preserving answer accuracy? |
| Optimal sample count | Figure 2, Section 5 | What is the cost-accuracy curve for different task types, and can we predict diminishing returns? |
| Open-text generation | Section 2 | Can consistency metrics be defined for tasks without fixed answer sets (e.g., summarization)? |
| Combination with ensembles | Table 11 (Appendix A.1.4) | What is the optimal mix of self-consistency, prompt ensembling, and model ensembling? |

The fine-tuning direction is particularly promising. The paper notes: "one could use self-consistency to generate better supervised data to fine-tune the model, such that the model can give more accurate predictions in a single inference run after fine-tuning." This suggests a **two-phase deployment**: use self-consistency at inference time to collect high-quality reasoning traces, then distill this knowledge into the model so it can reason correctly in a single pass. This would preserve accuracy gains while reducing the 40× inference cost.

**Longer-Term Research Programs (Implicitly Enabled)**

1. **Theoretical Analysis of Solution Space Geometry**

   The paper offers intuition that "correct reasoning processes, even if they are diverse, tend to have greater agreement in their final answer than incorrect processes" (Section 2). But why? A formal analysis could model the reasoning landscape as a high-dimensional space where correct answers occupy larger "basins of attraction" than incorrect ones. Understanding this geometry could lead to:
   - Predictive models for when self-consistency will help
   - Optimal sampling strategies tailored to task structure
   - Theoretical bounds on achievable accuracy gains

2. **Calibration and Uncertainty Quantification**

   Figure 5 shows consistency correlates with accuracy, but the paper does not report formal calibration metrics (e.g., Expected Calibration Error, Brier scores). Future work could:
   - Quantify how well consistency predicts actual error rates
   - Develop thresholds for when to abstain based on consistency levels
   - Combine self-consistency uncertainty with other calibration methods

3. **Cross-Task and Cross-Model Generalization**

   The paper tests four models (20B to 540B parameters) and finds gains scale with model size (Figure 4, right). But what about models below 20B? What about encoder-only architectures? Systematic studies across the full model size spectrum could reveal:
   - Minimum model capacity required for self-consistency to help
   - Whether architectural differences (encoder-decoder vs. decoder-only) affect gains
   - How self-consistency interacts with emerging model families (Mixture of Experts, retrieval-augmented models)

4. **Reasoning Fidelity vs. Answer Accuracy**

   Table 4 shows sampled paths can contain factually incorrect intermediate statements (e.g., wrong population numbers for Albany cities) while reaching correct conclusions. For applications requiring auditable reasoning (medical diagnosis, legal analysis, scientific computation), this is problematic. Research could explore:
   - Methods to validate intermediate reasoning steps without sacrificing answer accuracy
   - Hybrid approaches combining self-consistency with verifier models
   - Metrics for reasoning quality beyond final answer correctness

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

Self-consistency is immediately applicable to any domain requiring multi-step reasoning with verifiable answers. We categorize use cases by their risk profile and computational constraints.

**High-Stakes, Accuracy-Critical Applications**

For domains where errors carry significant cost, the 40× inference overhead is justified:

| Domain | Example Task | Why Self-Consistency Helps |
|--------|--------------|---------------------------|
| Financial analysis | Multi-step calculations, risk assessments | Arithmetic reasoning gains (+11% to +18% on GSM8K, SVAMP in Table 2) |
| Medical decision support | Diagnostic reasoning, treatment planning | Uncertainty estimation via consistency (Figure 5) enables flagging low-confidence cases |
| Legal document analysis | Contract interpretation, case precedent matching | Commonsense reasoning gains (+6.4% on StrategyQA in Table 3) |
| Scientific computation | Formula derivation, unit conversion | Robustness to imperfect prompts (Table 8) handles noisy input |

In these contexts, the ability to **abstain when consistency is low** may be as valuable as the accuracy improvement itself. A medical system that flags 20% of cases for human review (those with &lt;50% path agreement) could prevent catastrophic errors.

**Moderate-Stakes, Latency-Tolerant Applications**

For applications where accuracy matters but real-time response is not critical:

| Domain | Example Task | Recommended Configuration |
|--------|--------------|--------------------------|
| Educational tutoring | Math problem explanations | 10-20 paths (captures most gains per Figure 2) |
| Customer support | Complex query resolution | 5-10 paths with consistency threshold for escalation |
| Content moderation | Policy violation detection | 10 paths with human review for low-consistency cases |
| Data analysis | Report generation with calculations | 20 paths for numerical sections, greedy for text |

The paper suggests practitioners "can try a small number of paths (e.g., 5 or 10) as a starting point to realize most of the gains while not incurring too much cost" (Section 5). Figure 2 shows substantial improvements even at 10 paths, making this a practical sweet spot.

**Low-Stakes, Latency-Sensitive Applications**

For real-time systems where latency is paramount:

| Domain | Example Task | Recommendation |
|--------|--------------|----------------|
| Conversational AI | Multi-turn dialogue | Use self-consistency sparingly (e.g., only for factual queries) |
| Real-time translation | Document translation | Not recommended (open-ended generation not supported per Section 2) |
| Interactive tools | Code completion, search | Use greedy decoding; self-consistency adds too much latency |

The paper explicitly notes self-consistency "can be applied only to problems where the final answer is from a fixed answer set" (Section 2). Open-ended generation tasks fall outside the method's scope without modification.

### 7.4 Integration Guidance: When to Prefer Self-Consistency

Based on the paper's experimental findings, we provide decision criteria for practitioners evaluating whether to adopt self-consistency.

**Prefer Self-Consistency When:**

1. **Task has verifiable answers**: Arithmetic, multiple-choice, yes/no questions where answers can be parsed and compared (Section 2, footnote 1)

2. **Model is sufficiently large**: Gains scale with model size; UL2-20B sees +3-6% while PaLM-540B sees +12-18% (Table 2, Figure 4 right). For models &lt;50B parameters, evaluate cost-benefit carefully.

3. **Accuracy outweighs latency**: The method requires ~40× inference compute for maximum gains (Section 5). Acceptable for batch processing, not real-time systems.

4. **Chain-of-thought prompting is already used**: Self-consistency is a drop-in replacement for greedy decoding in CoT pipelines (Figure 1). No prompt changes required.

5. **Uncertainty estimation is valuable**: Consistency provides emergent confidence scores without calibration training (Figure 5, Section 3.5).

**Prefer Alternatives When:**

1. **Open-ended generation**: Summarization, creative writing, dialogue lack fixed answer sets (Section 2). Consider other decoding strategies.

2. **Latency is critical**: Real-time applications cannot tolerate 40× inference overhead. Use greedy decoding or fewer paths (5-10).

3. **Model is small (&lt;20B parameters)**: Gains are modest (+3-6% on UL2-20B in Table 2). Consider model scaling first.

4. **Reasoning fidelity matters more than answer accuracy**: If intermediate steps must be factually correct (e.g., legal reasoning), self-consistency's decoupling of path validity from answer correctness (Section 4.2) is a liability.

5. **Multiple models are already deployed**: If you have an ensemble infrastructure, Table 10 shows model ensembling can work—but self-consistency on the best model typically outperforms ensembling weaker models.

**Hybrid Approaches:**

Table 11 (Appendix A.1.4) shows self-consistency is compatible with other ensemble strategies:

| Combination | GSM8K Accuracy (PaLM-540B) | Marginal Gain Over Self-Consistency Alone |
|-------------|---------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| Self-consistency (40 paths) | 74.4% | Baseline |
| + Different prompt sets (×40) | 75.4% | +1.0% |
| + Prompt permutation (×40) | 73.8% | -0.6% |

The marginal gains are small, suggesting self-consistency "overrides" most ensemble benefits. However, for maximum accuracy in non-latency-constrained settings, combining self-consistency with diverse prompt sets provides a modest additional boost.

### 7.6 The Broader Impact on Language Model Research

Self-consistency represents a shift in how the field approaches reasoning improvement. Rather than treating reasoning as a **training problem** (requiring fine-tuning, verifiers, or human annotations), it frames reasoning as a **decoding problem** solvable at inference time. This has cascading effects:

**1. Democratization of Reasoning Improvements**

Because self-consistency requires "no additional training, auxiliary models or fine-tuning" (Section 1), any practitioner with access to a pre-trained language model can apply it. This lowers the barrier to achieving state-of-the-art reasoning performance, particularly for teams without resources for large-scale model training.

**2. Reframing the Role of Compute**

The method demonstrates that **inference-time compute** can substitute for **training-time optimization**. This challenges the prevailing assumption that model improvements must come from better training data, architectures, or scales. For organizations with inference budgets but limited training infrastructure, self-consistency offers a path to competitive performance.

**3. New Evaluation Metrics**

The consistency-accuracy correlation (Figure 5) suggests new evaluation dimensions beyond raw accuracy. Future benchmarks could report:
- Consistency rates alongside accuracy
- Calibration metrics based on path agreement
- Failure modes where high consistency produces wrong answers

**4. Bridging Symbolic and Neural Approaches**

Self-consistency implicitly implements a form of **neural symbolic reasoning**: multiple neural generations are aggregated via a symbolic operation (majority vote). This hybrid approach may inspire future work combining neural flexibility with symbolic rigor, addressing the "hallucinated intermediate steps" concern noted in Section 5.

### 7.7 Summary: Where the Field Goes From Here

Self-consistency establishes that **diversity in reasoning paths** is a critical lever for improving language model reasoning. The method's simplicity—sample, aggregate, select—belies its conceptual depth: it challenges fundamental assumptions about decoding, provides emergent uncertainty estimation, and achieves state-of-the-art results without training.

The research agenda it opens spans theoretical analysis (why does consistency predict correctness?), practical optimization (what is the optimal sample count per task?), and architectural innovation (how do we ground reasoning paths while preserving accuracy?). For practitioners, the guidance is clear: use self-consistency when accuracy matters more than latency, when answers are verifiable, and when model scale is sufficient to support diverse reasoning.

As the field moves forward, self-consistency serves as both a **practical tool** for immediate deployment and a **conceptual foundation** for future work on reliable, auditable, and uncertainty-aware language model reasoning.
