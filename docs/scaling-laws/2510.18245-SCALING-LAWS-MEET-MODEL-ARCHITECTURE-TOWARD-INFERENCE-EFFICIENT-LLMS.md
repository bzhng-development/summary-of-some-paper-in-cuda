## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the critical but underexplored trade-off between model accuracy and inference cost by introducing a conditional scaling law that augments the Chinchilla framework with architectural factors like hidden size, MLP-to-attention ratio, and Grouped-Query Attention (GQA). Validated by training over 200 models spanning 80M to 3B parameters and 8B to 100B training tokens, the proposed search framework identifies architectures that optimize both metrics simultaneously without increasing training budgets. The resulting models, such as Panda-3B and Surefire-3B, achieve up to 2.1% higher accuracy and 42% greater inference throughput compared to LLaMA-3.2 baselines, demonstrating a practical path toward deployment-efficient LLM design.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Inference Costs Are Ignored in Scaling Law Design

The fundamental gap this paper addresses is that **traditional scaling laws optimize for training performance while completely ignoring inference costs**. When researchers use scaling laws like Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., 2022) to determine optimal model architecture, they solve the following optimization problem:

$$\arg \min_{N,D} L(N, D) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \text{FLOPs}(N, D) = C$$

where $N$ is the number of parameters, $D$ is the number of training tokens, and $C$ is the compute budget. This formulation treats all architectures with the same parameter count as equivalent — but they are not.

> "A major limitation of existing scaling laws is their omission of inference costs, which constitute the dominant expense in deploying large models in real-world applications" (Section 1).

This matters because **inference dominates total cost of ownership**. Once a model is trained, it may be queried millions or billions of times in production. A model that is 40% slower at inference incurs 40% higher operational costs over its lifetime, even if training costs are identical. The paper identifies three specific architectural factors that influence inference efficiency but are absent from conventional scaling laws:

1. **Hidden size** (`d_model`): The dimension of the model's internal representations
2. **MLP-to-attention ratio** (`r_mlp/attn`): How parameters are allocated between feed-forward and attention layers
3. **Grouped-Query Attention (GQA)**: A technique that reduces key-value cache size by sharing heads across queries

### Why This Problem Matters: Both Practical and Theoretical Significance

**Real-world impact:** The deployment economics of LLMs have shifted dramatically. While training a 3B parameter model might cost tens of thousands of dollars once, serving that model continuously can cost orders of magnitude more over time. The paper notes that inference costs are "the dominant expense in deploying large models in real-world applications" (Section 1). For organizations running LLMs at scale, even modest throughput improvements translate to substantial cost savings or capacity increases.

**Theoretical significance:** From a research perspective, this work challenges an implicit assumption in the scaling law literature: that architecture is secondary to scale. The paper demonstrates that **architectural choices create a Pareto frontier** — you cannot simply maximize accuracy without considering efficiency, and vice versa. Figure 1 illustrates this clearly: contours of inference throughput and training loss over hidden size and MLP-to-attention ratio show that optimal points for each metric do not coincide. This means the "best" architecture depends on your specific efficiency-accuracy trade-off preference.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper positions itself against two categories of prior work, each with distinct shortcomings:

**1. Inference-Aware Scaling Laws (Sardana et al., 2023)**

This work attempted to incorporate inference costs into scaling laws by accounting for total FLOPs from both training and inference. However, the paper identifies a critical flaw:

> "Their formulation requires estimating the total number of tokens generated over a model's entire lifespan. Because inference is performed repeatedly during deployment, this assumption renders the proposed scaling law impractical for real-world use" (Section 1).

In practice, no one can reliably predict how many tokens a model will generate over its lifetime — this depends on adoption, usage patterns, and product decisions that are unknowable at training time. This makes the approach theoretically sound but operationally unusable.

**2. Architecture-Aware Scaling Laws (Bian et al., 2025)**

This work extended Chinchilla by incorporating architectural factors, but with three key limitations:

- **Only considers aspect ratio**: The study uses hidden size divided by number of layers as the sole architectural parameter. However, Figure 2 demonstrates that aspect ratio alone "fails to capture the full range of factors that influence inference efficiency." The figure shows Qwen2.5-1.5B outperforming Qwen3-0.6B in throughput despite having the same number of layers — the difference comes from hidden size, GQA, and MLP-to-attention ratio, not aspect ratio.

- **Depth variations hurt generalization**: The paper notes that "cutting layers tends to impair the model's generalization after fine-tuning" (Section 1, citing Petty et al., 2023). Varying layer count under fixed parameter budgets creates confounding effects that make scaling law predictions unreliable for downstream tasks.

- **No framework for GQA**: Grouped-Query Attention has become standard in modern models (LLaMA-3, Qwen, Gemma), but prior scaling laws do not incorporate it as a tunable parameter.

### How This Paper Positions Itself

The paper carves out a distinct niche by making three strategic design choices that differentiate it from prior work:

**Fixed layer count, variable internal architecture:** Instead of varying the number of layers (which affects depth and generalization), the paper fixes `n_layers` and studies how hidden size and MLP-to-attention ratio can be adjusted under a fixed parameter budget. This is motivated by observing that "recent open-weight models such as LLaMA, Qwen, Gemma, and Phi... despite having a comparable number of parameters, adopt markedly different architectural designs" (Section 1). Table 3 in Appendix C shows concrete examples: Qwen2.5-1.5B uses 28 layers with `d_model=1536`, while Qwen3-0.6B uses 28 layers with `d_model=1024` — same depth, different internal structure.

**Two-step conditional framework:** Rather than attempting to fit a single unified scaling law $L(d/\sqrt{N}, r, N, D)$ across all configurations (which the authors deem "unrealistic"), the paper proposes a reference-and-calibration approach:

1. First, obtain optimal loss $L_{opt}(N, D)$ from standard Chinchilla scaling as a reference point
2. Then, calibrate architectural variant loss relative to this reference using multiplicative or additive factors

This is expressed in Equation 3 (Section 3.3):

$$L(d/\sqrt{N}, r | N, D) = (a_0 + a_1 \log(\frac{d}{\sqrt{N}}) + a_2 \frac{\sqrt{N}}{d}) \cdot (b_0 + b_1 \log r + b_2 \frac{1}{r}) \cdot L_{opt}$$

The separable formulation assumes hidden size and MLP-to-attention ratio affect loss independently — an assumption the paper validates through ablation studies in Appendix J.

**Practical search framework over theoretical optimization:** Instead of deriving closed-form optimal architectures, the paper proposes Algorithm 1 (Section 3.4), which:
- Fits scaling laws on small models (80M–297M parameters)
- Solves a constrained optimization for target architectures (1B–3B parameters)
- Performs local search over GQA values with early stopping

This acknowledges that GQA does not exhibit a smooth continuous relationship with loss (Figure 24, Appendix I), making it unsuitable for gradient-based optimization but amenable to discrete search.

**Validation through large-scale training:** The paper does not stop at fitting scaling laws on small models. It trains 3B parameter models (Panda-3B, Surefire-3B) using architectures predicted by the scaling law, then evaluates them against LLaMA-3.2-3B on nine downstream benchmarks. Table 1 (Section 5) shows Panda-3B achieves 62.5% average accuracy vs. 61.9% for LLaMA-3.2-3B, while Figure 7 demonstrates 42% higher inference throughput. This end-to-end validation — from scaling law fitting to large-scale training to downstream evaluation — distinguishes the work from studies that only fit laws without verifying predictions at scale.

### Summary of Positioning

| Aspect | Prior Scaling Laws | This Paper |
|--------|-------------------|------------|
| **Optimization target** | Training loss only | Training loss + inference throughput |
| **Architectural parameters** | Parameters $N$, tokens $D$ | $N$, $D$, `d_model`, `r_mlp/attn`, GQA |
| **Inference cost modeling** | Ignored or requires lifetime token estimates | Measured directly on target hardware |
| **Layer count** | Often varied | Fixed (to isolate internal architecture effects) |
| **Validation** | Fit on small models | Fit on small, validate on 3B with downstream tasks |

This positioning allows the paper to offer actionable guidance for practitioners: given a parameter budget (e.g., 3B parameters) and token budget (e.g., 100B tokens), what specific architectural configuration maximizes inference efficiency while maintaining accuracy competitive with standard baselines?

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a methodology for discovering LLM architectures that simultaneously optimize inference efficiency and accuracy under fixed training budgets. The core idea is to extend traditional scaling laws with architectural calibration factors, then use these calibrated laws to search for optimal configurations before committing to expensive large-scale training.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The system being built is a **predictive framework that tells you what LLM architecture to train** given your parameter budget, token budget, and efficiency requirements. It solves the problem that traditional scaling laws only tell you how many parameters and tokens to use, but not how to structure those parameters internally for optimal inference performance.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The technical pipeline consists of four sequential components that transform architectural hypotheses into validated model designs. **First**, the training infrastructure generates over 200 small model variants (80M to 297M parameters) by systematically varying hidden size, MLP-to-attention ratio, and GQA while holding layer count fixed. **Second**, the conditional scaling law fitting module takes the training loss from these small models and learns calibration coefficients that capture how architectural choices deviate from the Chinchilla-optimal baseline. **Third**, the search framework uses these fitted coefficients to solve a constrained optimization problem that identifies architectures satisfying both accuracy thresholds and efficiency targets for larger model scales (1B to 3B parameters). **Fourth**, the validation pipeline trains the predicted architectures at scale and measures both downstream task accuracy and inference throughput on target hardware (A100/H200 GPUs with vLLM/SGLang). Information flows unidirectionally: small model training data feeds the scaling law, the scaling law feeds the optimizer, the optimizer outputs architecture specifications, and those specifications are validated through large-scale training and benchmarking.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the **architectural parameter space** because you must understand what variables are being tuned before understanding how they are optimized.
- We then explain the **conditional scaling law formulation** since this is the mathematical core that enables prediction across model scales.
- We next detail the **two-step calibration strategy** because it reveals why the authors chose separable multiplicative/additive forms over unified equations.
- We then walk through the **search and optimization framework** (Algorithm 1) to show how predictions become actionable architecture specifications.
- We conclude with the **validation methodology** to demonstrate how the paper verifies that small-model predictions hold at 3B scale.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Paper Type and Core Mechanism**

This is an empirical scaling law paper that introduces a conditional extension to the Chinchilla framework, where architectural factors are incorporated as multiplicative or additive calibration terms rather than as direct inputs to the base loss function. The fundamental insight is that architectural choices create systematic, predictable deviations from the Chinchilla-optimal loss, and these deviations can be modeled separately from the parameter-token scaling relationship.

**Architectural Parameter Space and Design Constraints**

The paper studies three architectural factors that influence both inference efficiency and training loss, while holding one factor constant to isolate effects. The **hidden size** (`d_model`) determines the dimension of internal representations and directly affects the size of query, key, value, and output projection matrices in attention layers. The **MLP-to-attention ratio** (`r_mlp/attn`) controls how non-embedding parameters are allocated between feed-forward layers and attention layers, where `r_mlp/attn = fsize / (4 × d_model² / n_heads)` approximately. The **Grouped-Query Attention factor** (GQA) specifies how many query heads share each key-value head, reducing KV cache size during inference. Critically, the **number of layers** (`n_layers`) is held fixed within each parameter scale to avoid confounding depth effects with internal architecture effects — for example, all 80M models use 12 layers, all 1B models use 16 layers, and all 3B models use 28 layers. This design choice is motivated by prior work showing that "cutting layers tends to impair the model's generalization after fine-tuning" (Section 1), and it allows the scaling law to focus on internal parameter allocation rather than depth.

The parameter ranges explored are exhaustive within practical bounds. The MLP-to-attention ratio spans from 0.10 to 12.6 across all model variants in Appendix D, though the paper notes that open-weight models typically fall between 0.5 and 5 (e.g., LLaMA-3.2-1B has `r = 4.80`, LLaMA-3.2-3B has `r = 1.5`, Qwen3-8B has `r = 4.67`). The normalized hidden size `d_model / √N_non-embed` ranges from 0.043 to 0.340, capturing both narrow-deep and wide-shallow configurations. GQA values tested include 1 (standard multi-head attention), 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, 8, 9, and 15, where higher values indicate more query-head sharing per KV head.

**The Base Scaling Law Reference Point**

Before incorporating architectural factors, the paper establishes a baseline using the standard Chinchilla scaling law formulation. The Chinchilla law expresses training loss as a power-law function of parameters and tokens:

$$L(N, D) = E + \frac{A}{N^\alpha} + \frac{B}{D^\beta}$$

where $L$ is the training loss, $N$ is the total number of parameters, $D$ is the number of training tokens, and $E$, $A$, $B$, $\alpha$, $\beta$ are learned coefficients. The term $E$ represents the irreducible loss floor, while the $A/N^\alpha$ and $B/D^\beta$ terms capture diminishing returns from increasing parameters and tokens respectively. The paper does not refit these Chinchilla coefficients from scratch; instead, it empirically searches over architecture variants at each scale to find the optimal loss $L_{opt}(N, D)$ for models with $N_{non-embed} < 1B$ parameters. This empirical $L_{opt}$ serves as the reference point against which architectural variants are calibrated, avoiding the need to fit the full Chinchilla law while still capturing the optimal loss achievable at each scale.

**The Conditional Scaling Law Formulation**

The paper's core contribution is a conditional scaling law that predicts the loss of architectural variants relative to the Chinchilla-optimal baseline. The multiplicative calibration form (Equation 3 in Section 3.3) is:

$$L(d/\sqrt{N}, r | N, D) = \left(a_0 + a_1 \log\left(\frac{d}{\sqrt{N}}\right) + a_2 \frac{\sqrt{N}}{d}\right) \cdot \left(b_0 + b_1 \log r + b_2 \frac{1}{r}\right) \cdot L_{opt}$$

In plain language, this equation states that the loss of any architectural configuration equals the optimal Chinchilla loss multiplied by two calibration factors: one for hidden size and one for MLP-to-attention ratio. The hidden size calibration factor $(a_0 + a_1 \log(d/\sqrt{N}) + a_2 \sqrt{N}/d)$ captures the U-shaped relationship between normalized hidden size and loss observed in Figure 4, where both excessively small and excessively large hidden sizes degrade performance. The MLP-to-attention calibration factor $(b_0 + b_1 \log r + b_2 / r)$ similarly captures the U-shaped relationship in Figure 5, where both attention-heavy and MLP-heavy allocations underperform relative to an interior optimum.

The paper also evaluates an additive calibration alternative:

$$L(d/\sqrt{N}, r | N, D) = \left(a_0 + a_1 \log\left(\frac{d}{\sqrt{N}}\right) + a_2 \frac{\sqrt{N}}{d}\right) + \left(b_1 \log r + b_2 \frac{1}{r}\right) + L_{opt}$$

Appendix J (Figure 25) shows that multiplicative and additive calibrations achieve nearly identical MSE and Spearman correlation on Task 3 (fit on 80M/145M/297M, evaluate on 1B), with both achieving MSE of 0.0001 and Spearman of 0.7451. This suggests the two-step reference-and-calibration framework is robust to the specific calibration form, as long as the separability assumption holds.

**Why Separable Calibration Instead of Unified Formulation**

The paper explicitly rejects fitting a single unified scaling law $L(d/\sqrt{N}, r, N, D)$ across all configurations, calling this approach "unrealistic" (Section 3.3). The separable calibration design is motivated by three considerations. First, the U-shaped relationships in Figures 4 and 5 are consistent across model sizes when architectural factors are held fixed, suggesting that hidden size and MLP ratio effects are approximately independent of scale. Second, Appendix J (Figure 26) tests a joint non-separable formulation $(a_0 + a_1 \log(dr/\sqrt{N}) + a_2/(dr/\sqrt{N})) \cdot L_{opt}$ and finds it performs significantly worse, with MSE increasing from 0.0001 to 0.0067 and Spearman dropping from 0.7451 to 0.2108. Third, the separable form enables analytical optimization — the paper solves $\partial L / \partial d_{model} = 0$ and $\partial L / \partial r = 0$ to find optimal configurations, which would be intractable with a fully joint formulation.

**Why GQA Is Handled via Local Search Rather Than Continuous Optimization**

Grouped-Query Attention is excluded from the continuous scaling law and instead optimized via discrete local search (Algorithm 1, Section 3.4). This design choice is driven by empirical observation: Figure 24 in Appendix I shows that the relationship between GQA and training loss is "highly fluctuating" rather than smooth, unlike the consistent U-shaped curves for hidden size and MLP ratio. The paper explains this fluctuation occurs because varying GQA while holding total heads fixed creates opposing effects — increasing query heads expands query projection dimensionality but reduces KV expressivity through increased sharing, while decreasing query heads has the opposite effect. These tradeoffs create a non-monotonic, non-smooth loss landscape that cannot be captured by the log-and-inverse functional form used for other factors. Additionally, the search space for GQA is small once $N_{non-embed}$, $d_{model}$, and $r_{mlp/attn}$ are fixed, since GQA must be a prime factor of the number of attention heads $n_{heads}$. The local search enumerates feasible GQA values with early stopping once performance falls below the GQA=4 baseline, making this approach computationally tractable.

**The Search and Optimization Framework (Algorithm 1)**

Algorithm 1 in Section 3.4 formalizes the process of identifying inference-efficient, accurate architectures. The algorithm takes as input the target model parameters $N$, training tokens $D$, target loss threshold $L_t$, and an inference efficiency function $I_N(\cdot)$. The first step trains smaller models to fit the Chinchilla scaling law if $L_{opt}(N, D)$ is unavailable, though the paper empirically searches for $L_{opt}$ rather than fitting Chinchilla coefficients directly. The second step solves the constrained optimization problem:

$$\arg\max_P I_N(P) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad L(P | N, D) \leq L_t$$

where $P$ represents the architectural configuration $(d_{model}, r_{mlp/attn})$, $I_N(P)$ is the inference throughput, and $L_t$ is the maximum allowable training loss (set to match LLaMA-3.2 baseline loss in the experiments). This formulation prioritizes efficiency while guaranteeing accuracy does not fall below the baseline. The third step performs local GQA search with early stopping to maximize inference efficiency, acknowledging that GQA affects throughput substantially (Figure 11) but cannot be optimized via gradient methods. The algorithm returns the final architecture specification $\{P, \text{GQA}\}$ ready for large-scale training.

**Training Configuration and Scale Progression**

The paper trains over 200 model variants across five parameter scales to fit and validate the scaling law. The parameter scales are 80M, 145M, 297M, 1B, and 3B non-embedding parameters, with layer counts of 12, 12, 12, 16, and 28 respectively (Appendix D, Table 4). All models are trained on $100 \times N_{non-embed}$ tokens, which is 5× the Chinchilla-optimal token count, to ensure convergence and reduce variance in loss measurements. Training hyperparameters (Appendix E, Table 5) include batch sizes of 256 for 80M/145M models and 512 for 297M/1B/3B models, maximum learning rates from 1.5e-3 (80M) down to 6.0e-4 (3B), AdamW optimizer with $\beta_1 = 0.9$ and $\beta_2 = 0.95$, weight decay of 0.1, gradient clipping at 1.0, cosine learning rate schedule with 500 warmup steps, and sequence length of 2048 tokens.

The scaling law fitting follows a progressive evaluation strategy to test extrapolation capability. Task 1 fits on 80M results and evaluates on 145M results. Task 2 fits on 80M and 145M results and evaluates on 297M results. Task 3 fits on 80M, 145M, and 297M results and evaluates on 1B results. Figure 6 shows that all three tasks achieve low MSE (0.0001 to 0.0002) and high Spearman correlation (0.7451 to 0.8909), validating that small-model fits predict larger-model behavior. However, Section 5.1 reveals an important nuance: fitting the 3B scaling law using only 1B data yields better predictions than fitting on 80M/145M/297M/1B data (Figure 8), with Spearman improving from 0.5000 to 1.0000. This suggests that when scaling up, it is "often sufficient, and sometimes preferable, to fit the law using models within a closer size range to the target, such as about one third of its scale" (Section 5.1).

**Inference Efficiency Measurement Protocol**

Inference throughput is measured using the vLLM framework (Kwon et al., 2023) by default, with SGLang (Zheng et al., 2023) used for cross-validation. The standard evaluation uses 4096 input tokens and 1024 output tokens, with throughput reported as tokens per second averaged over 5 repeated runs. All experiments run on NVIDIA Ampere A100 GPUs (40GB) unless otherwise specified, with additional validation on NVIDIA H200 GPUs in Appendix G. The paper measures throughput across batch sizes 16, 32, 64, and 128 to capture performance across different serving scenarios. Table 6 in Appendix H provides detailed throughput statistics: for example, Surefire-1B achieves 4800.05 tokens/s at batch size 128 on A100/vLLM compared to 3825.91 tokens/s for LLaMA-3.2-1B, representing a 25% improvement. On H200/SGLang, Surefire-3B achieves 4877.16 tokens/s at batch size 128 compared to 4183.09 tokens/s for LLaMA-3.2-3B, representing a 17% improvement.

**Downstream Evaluation Methodology**

Model accuracy is evaluated in the zero-shot setting using the lm-evaluation-harness framework on nine benchmarks: ARC-Easy, ARC-Challenge, LAMBADA, HellaSwag, OpenBookQA, PIQA, SciQ, WinoGrande, and CoQA (Section 4). The average accuracy across these nine tasks serves as the primary accuracy metric, with individual task results reported in Appendix L (Tables 9 and 10). For example, Panda-3B achieves 62.5% average accuracy compared to 61.9% for LLaMA-3.2-3B, with improvements on 7 of 9 tasks including SciQ (91.2% vs. 89.4%) and CoQA (63.4% vs. 63.3%). The paper also reports byte-level WikiText perplexity in Appendix H (Tables 7 and 8) as an additional accuracy proxy, where Panda-3B achieves 1.6454 perplexity compared to 1.6489 for LLaMA-3.2-3B.

**Learned Coefficients and Optimal Configurations**

The paper reports the specific learned coefficients for the multiplicative calibration at 1B scale (Section 5.1): $a_0 = 2.697$, $a_1 = 0.0974$, $a_2 = 0.0078$, $b_0 = 0.3870$, $b_1 = 0.0063$, and $b_2 = 0.0065$. Solving $\partial L / \partial d_{model} = 0$ and $\partial L / \partial r = 0$ with these coefficients yields optimal configurations of $d_{model}/\sqrt{N} = 0.08$ and $r = 1.032$ for 1B models. For 3B models fitted on 80M/145M/297M/1B data, the optimal configuration is $d_{model}/\sqrt{N} = 0.08$ and $r = 1.055$. When refitted using only 1B data for 3B prediction, the coefficients become $a_0 = 2.319$, $a_1 = 0.238$, $a_2 = 0.0176$, $b_0 = 0.5104$, $b_1 = 0.0051$, and $b_2 = 0.0062$, yielding $d_{model}/\sqrt{N} = 0.074$ and $r = 1.229$. These coefficients reveal that the MLP-to-attention ratio optimum is near 1.0, substantially lower than LLaMA-3.2's ratio of 4.80 at 1B scale and 3.0 at 3B scale, explaining why the Panda and Surefire models allocate more parameters to attention relative to MLP layers.

**Why This Approach Over Alternatives**

The paper's design choices reflect tradeoffs between predictive accuracy, computational tractability, and practical usability. The two-step calibration approach is chosen over unified scaling laws because it separates the well-understood parameter-token scaling from the less-understood architectural effects, enabling more stable fitting. Fixed layer counts are chosen over variable depth because depth affects generalization after fine-tuning in ways that confound pretraining loss predictions. Local GQA search is chosen over continuous optimization because the GQA-loss relationship is non-smooth and discrete. Progressive fitting is chosen over single-scale fitting because it tests extrapolation capability, though the paper acknowledges that near-scale fitting (1B to predict 3B) may outperform far-scale fitting (80M to predict 3B). These choices collectively enable the framework to produce actionable architecture specifications that are validated through large-scale training rather than remaining theoretical predictions.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the fundamental contributions that distinguish this work from prior scaling law research. We separate **incremental improvements** (refinements to existing methods) from **fundamental innovations** (new conceptual understanding that changes how we think about LLM design).

---

### 4.1 Fundamental Innovation: The Architectural Pareto Frontier

**What prior work assumed:** Traditional scaling laws treat architecture as secondary to scale. The implicit assumption is that once you fix parameter count $N$ and token count $D$, all architectures with those budgets are roughly equivalent in performance. Efficiency-accuracy trade-offs are assumed to be **zero-sum**: faster inference means lower accuracy, and vice versa.

**What this paper reveals:** There exists a **Pareto frontier in architectural space** where you can simultaneously improve both accuracy and inference throughput without increasing training budgets. This is not an incremental optimization — it fundamentally challenges how we think about model design.

Figure 1 provides the clearest evidence: the contours for inference throughput (left) and training loss (right) do not align. The LLaMA-3.2 architecture sits in a region that is **suboptimal on both dimensions**. By moving to the Panda/Surefire configurations, the paper achieves:
- **Up to 42% higher inference throughput** (Table 6, Appendix H)
- **Up to 2.1% higher downstream accuracy** (Table 1, Section 5)

**Why this is fundamental:** This discovery means that many open-weight models are leaving performance on the table not because of insufficient compute, but because of **systematic architectural misallocation**. The implication is that the field has been optimizing the wrong variables — focusing on scale while neglecting internal parameter distribution.

**Distinction from prior work:** Sardana et al. (2023) incorporated inference costs but required unknowable lifetime token estimates. Bian et al. (2025) incorporated architecture but only via aspect ratio. This paper is the first to demonstrate that **architectural tuning alone** (without more parameters or tokens) creates a Pareto-improving opportunity.

---

### 4.2 Fundamental Innovation: MLP-to-Attention Optimum Near 1.0

**What prior work assumed:** Open-weight models have been trending toward **MLP-heavy architectures**. LLaMA-3.2-1B uses `r_mlp/attn = 4.80`, LLaMA-3.2-3B uses `r = 3.0`, and Qwen3-8B uses `r = 4.67` (Section 5). The implicit design heuristic is that more MLP capacity improves representational power.

**What this paper reveals:** The optimal MLP-to-attention ratio is **near 1.0**, not 3–5. The learned coefficients in Section 5.1 yield optimal configurations of `r = 1.032` for 1B models and `r = 1.055` for 3B models (when fitted progressively), or `r = 1.229` (when fitted on 1B data alone).

**Why this is fundamental:** This finding suggests the field has been **systematically over-allocating parameters to MLP layers** at the expense of attention. The U-shaped loss curve in Figure 5 shows that both attention-heavy (`r < 0.5`) and MLP-heavy (`r > 5`) configurations underperform. The optimum lies in a narrow band around 1.0 that no major open model currently occupies.

**Practical significance:** Table 1 shows Panda-3B uses `r = 1.0` compared to LLaMA-3.2-3B's `r = 3.0` — a 3× shift in parameter allocation — yet achieves **lower training loss (2.619 vs. 2.625)** and **higher downstream accuracy (62.5% vs. 61.9%)**. This is not a marginal improvement; it indicates a **systematic design error** in existing model families.

**Distinction from prior work:** Bian et al. (2025) studied aspect ratio (hidden size / layers) but did not examine MLP-to-attention allocation. This paper is the first to identify and quantify the optimal ratio, providing actionable guidance that contradicts current industry practice.

---

### 4.3 Fundamental Innovation: Scale-Proximity Fitting Principle

**What prior work assumed:** Scaling law literature generally assumes that **more diverse training data improves prediction**. The standard approach is to fit laws on many small models and extrapolate to larger scales, under the assumption that the functional form is stable across scales.

**What this paper reveals:** Fitting the scaling law on models **closer in scale to the target** produces better predictions than fitting on a wider range of smaller models. Figure 8 (Section 5.1) shows that fitting on 1B data alone to predict 3B behavior achieves **Spearman correlation of 1.0000**, compared to **0.5000** when fitting on 80M/145M/297M/1B data.

**Why this is fundamental:** This challenges the assumption that scaling law coefficients are **scale-invariant**. The paper's authors note: "the law's coefficients shift with model size" (Section 5.1). This means that scaling laws fitted on very small models (80M–297M) may systematically mispredict behavior at larger scales (3B+), even when the functional form is correct.

**Practical significance:** For practitioners, this means you should **fit scaling laws on models within ~3× of your target scale**, not on the smallest models you can afford. The paper states: "when scaling up, it is often sufficient, and sometimes preferable, to fit the law using models within a closer size range to the target, such as about one third of its scale" (Section 5.1).

**Distinction from prior work:** Prior scaling law studies (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022) fit on wide ranges of model sizes and assume coefficient stability. This paper provides empirical evidence that **coefficient drift** occurs, requiring scale-proximate fitting for accurate large-scale prediction.

---

### 4.4 Incremental Innovation: GQA as Discrete Search Variable

**What prior work assumed:** Grouped-Query Attention is treated as a standard architectural choice, but scaling laws do not incorporate it as an optimizable parameter. Some works assume GQA effects are smooth and continuous.

**What this paper reveals:** GQA cannot be modeled via continuous scaling laws because the **loss-GQA relationship is highly fluctuating** (Figure 24, Appendix I). The paper explains this occurs because varying GQA creates opposing effects on query projection dimensionality and KV expressivity that do not trade off smoothly.

**Why this is incremental (not fundamental):** The insight that GQA should be searched discretely rather than optimized continuously is important for practical implementation, but it does not fundamentally change our understanding of scaling laws. It is a **methodological refinement** that acknowledges the limitations of continuous optimization for discrete architectural choices.

**Practical significance:** Algorithm 1 (Section 3.4) handles GQA via local search with early stopping, enumerating feasible values (GQA must be a prime factor of `n_heads`) rather than attempting gradient-based optimization. This makes the search framework **computationally tractable** while still capturing GQA's substantial impact on throughput (Figure 11 shows 2–3× throughput gains from GQA=1 to GQA=9).

**Distinction from prior work:** No prior scaling law work incorporates GQA as a tunable parameter. This paper provides the first framework for optimizing GQA alongside continuous architectural factors, even if the optimization method differs.

---

### 4.5 Fundamental Innovation: Separable Calibration Outperforms Joint Formulation

**What prior work assumed:** When incorporating multiple architectural factors into scaling laws, the natural approach is to fit a **unified function** $L(d/\sqrt{N}, r, N, D)$ that captures all interactions simultaneously.

**What this paper reveals:** A **separable calibration** approach — where hidden size and MLP-to-attention ratio affect loss independently — outperforms joint non-separable formulations. Appendix J (Figure 26) shows joint calibration increases MSE from 0.0001 to 0.0067 and drops Spearman from 0.7451 to 0.2108.

**Why this is fundamental:** This finding suggests that **architectural factors interact weakly** with each other relative to their individual effects on loss. The separable form is not just computationally convenient — it is **empirically more accurate**. This simplifies the scaling law fitting problem and enables analytical optimization (solving $\partial L / \partial d = 0$ and $\partial L / \partial r = 0$).

**Practical significance:** The separable formulation makes the search framework **tractable for practitioners**. A joint formulation would require exhaustive search over the $(d, r)$ space for each target scale, while the separable form allows closed-form optimal configuration derivation.

**Distinction from prior work:** Bian et al. (2025) used a unified formulation with aspect ratio as the sole architectural parameter. This paper demonstrates that **multi-factor separable calibration** is both more accurate and more practical than unified approaches.

---

### Summary: Incremental vs. Fundamental Contributions

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| Architectural Pareto Frontier | **Fundamental** | Challenges zero-sum efficiency-accuracy assumption |
| MLP-to-Attention Optimum ~1.0 | **Fundamental** | Contradicts industry practice (LLaMA uses 3–5) |
| Scale-Proximity Fitting | **Fundamental** | Reveals coefficient drift across model scales |
| GQA as Discrete Search | Incremental | Methodological refinement for optimization |
| Separable Calibration | **Fundamental** | More accurate than joint formulations, enables analytical optimization |

The three fundamental innovations collectively establish that **architecture matters as much as scale** for LLM performance, and that current open-weight models are systematically misconfigured. The incremental contributions provide the methodological tools to act on these insights.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the paper validates its conditional scaling law framework through systematic experimentation. We analyze the evaluation methodology, quantify the main results, assess whether the experiments support the paper's claims, and identify limitations revealed through ablation studies.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: Datasets, Metrics, and Baselines

**Training Data and Sampling Strategy**

The paper trains all models on data sampled from **Dolma-v1.7** (Soldaini et al., 2024), a corpus containing data from 15 different sources. Tokens are sampled with probability proportional to each source's contribution, ensuring the training distribution matches Dolma-v1.7's composition. This choice provides consistency with prior scaling law studies while using a publicly available dataset.

**Model Scales and Architecture Variants**

The experimental design spans five parameter scales with over 200 distinct architectures:

| Scale | Non-embedding Parameters | Layers | Variants Trained |
|-------|-------------------------|--------|------------------|
| 80M | 80M | 12 | ~57 variants |
| 145M | 145M | 12 | ~55 variants |
| 297M | 297M | 12 | ~62 variants |
| 1B | ~1B | 16 | ~17 variants |
| 3B | ~3B | 28 | ~5 variants |

Appendix D (Table 4) provides the complete architecture list. For scaling law fitting, the paper varies `d_model/√N_non-embed` from 0.043 to 0.340 and `r_mlp/attn` from 0.10 to 12.6, though it notes that open-weight models typically fall between 0.5 and 5.

**Training Configuration**

All models are trained on **100× N_non-embed tokens**, which is 5× the Chinchilla-optimal token count. This over-training ensures convergence and reduces variance in loss measurements. Key hyperparameters (Appendix E, Table 5):

- **Batch sizes**: 256 (80M/145M), 512 (297M/1B/3B)
- **Max learning rates**: 1.5e-3 (80M) down to 6.0e-4 (3B)
- **Optimizer**: AdamW with β₁ = 0.9, β₂ = 0.95
- **Weight decay**: 0.1
- **Sequence length**: 2048 tokens
- **LR schedule**: Cosine with 500 warmup steps

**Inference Efficiency Measurement**

Throughput is measured using **vLLM** (Kwon et al., 2023) by default, with **SGLang** (Zheng et al., 2023) for cross-validation. The protocol (Section 4):

- **Input length**: 4096 tokens
- **Output length**: 1024 tokens
- **Batch sizes**: 16, 32, 64, 128
- **Repetitions**: 5 runs averaged
- **Hardware**: NVIDIA A100 (40GB) and NVIDIA H200

This setup captures performance across different serving scenarios, from low-latency (small batch) to high-throughput (large batch) deployments.

**Downstream Accuracy Evaluation**

Models are evaluated in the **zero-shot setting** using lm-evaluation-harness on nine benchmarks (Section 4):

1. ARC-Easy
2. ARC-Challenge
3. LAMBADA
4. HellaSwag
5. OpenBookQA
6. PIQA
7. SciQ
8. WinoGrande
9. CoQA

The **average accuracy across all nine tasks** serves as the primary accuracy metric. The paper also reports byte-level WikiText perplexity in Appendix H (Tables 7 and 8) as an additional accuracy proxy.

**Baselines for Comparison**

The paper compares against **LLaMA-3.2-1B** and **LLaMA-3.2-3B** as the primary baselines. These are industry-standard open-weight models at the target scales. Additional comparisons in Appendix H (Tables 7 and 8) include:

- LLaMA-3.2-1B-HF and LLaMA-3.2-3B-HF (Hugging Face versions)
- OLMo-2-1B-HF (1B scale alternative)
- Qwen2.5-3B-HF (3B scale alternative)

**Scaling Law Fitting Protocol**

The paper uses a **progressive evaluation strategy** to test extrapolation capability (Section 4):

- **Task 1**: Fit on 80M, evaluate on 145M
- **Task 2**: Fit on 80M + 145M, evaluate on 297M
- **Task 3**: Fit on 80M + 145M + 297M, evaluate on 1B

Fitting uses the **Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm** for least-squares curve fitting. Performance is measured with **Mean Squared Error (MSE)** and **Spearman's rank correlation coefficient**.

**Claim 4: MLP-to-attention optimum is near 1.0, not 3–5**

**Support level: Moderate to Strong**

**Caveat**: This finding is based on a single extrapolation (1B→3B). The paper does not test other scale gaps (e.g., 297M→1B, 1B→7B), so the generalizability of the "one-third of scale" recommendation is uncertain.

**Limited Scale Range**

The paper explicitly acknowledges: "our evaluation does not extend to 7B models" (Section 7). All validated predictions stop at 3B parameters, leaving open whether the scaling law holds at larger scales where memory bandwidth and parallelism constraints may differ.

**Dense Models Only**

The ablation in Appendix J (Figure 25) shows that including extreme `r_mlp/attn` values (&lt;0.5 or >5) degrades Spearman correlation from 0.7451 to 0.3309. This suggests the scaling law is **fragile to architectural extremes**, requiring practitioners to constrain the search space based on prior knowledge.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the assumptions, constraints, and open questions that bound the applicability of the conditional scaling law framework. While the paper demonstrates strong results within its tested scope, several limitations warrant careful consideration before deploying these findings in production settings.

### 6.1 Core Assumptions and Their Validity

**Assumption 1: Architectural Effects Are Separable**

The conditional scaling law assumes that hidden size and MLP-to-attention ratio affect training loss independently, enabling the multiplicative calibration form:

$$L(d/\sqrt{N}, r | N, D) = f(d/\sqrt{N}) \cdot g(r) \cdot L_{opt}$$

While Appendix J (Figure 26) validates this empirically — showing joint non-separable formulations perform 67× worse on MSE — this remains an **unproven theoretical assumption**. The separability may break down at larger scales (7B+) or with different architectural components (e.g., rotary embeddings, SwiGLU activations) that were not tested. Practitioners should verify separability holds for their specific architecture family before applying the scaling law.

**Assumption 2: Layer Count Can Be Fixed Without Loss of Generality**

The paper fixes `n_layers` within each parameter scale (12 layers for 80M–297M, 16 for 1B, 28 for 3B) to isolate internal architecture effects. This choice is motivated by prior work showing "cutting layers tends to impair the model's generalization after fine-tuning" (Section 1, citing Petty et al., 2023). However, this **precludes discovering optimal depth configurations** for specific use cases. A model optimized for pre-training loss may not be optimal for fine-tuning, and the fixed-layer approach cannot capture depth-accuracy trade-offs that may emerge at different scales.

**Assumption 3: Small-Model Behavior Extrapolates to Larger Scales**

The entire framework relies on fitting scaling laws on 80M–297M models to predict 1B–3B behavior. While Figure 6 shows strong predictive performance (Spearman ≥ 0.7451), Figure 8 reveals a critical caveat: **prediction quality degrades with scale distance**. Fitting on 80M/145M/297M/1B to predict 3B achieves Spearman 0.5000, while fitting on 1B alone achieves Spearman 1.0000. This implies practitioners need access to models at ~1/3 of their target scale to fit the law reliably — a non-trivial requirement for organizations targeting 70B+ models, where even 20B fitting models require substantial compute.

**Assumption 4: Inference Efficiency Measurements Generalize Across Hardware**

Throughput is measured on NVIDIA A100 and H200 GPUs using vLLM and SGLang. While Appendix F and G show consistent gains across these combinations, the paper acknowledges: "inference efficiency $I_N(P)$ could, in principle, be expressed analytically, it depends heavily on hardware and inference configurations" (Section 5.1). This means the **optimal architecture for A100 may not be optimal for TPU, Inferentia, or edge devices** with different memory bandwidth characteristics. The framework requires re-measuring throughput on target hardware for each deployment scenario.

---

### 6.2 Scenarios and Problem Settings Not Addressed

**Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) Architectures**

> "Our analysis is restricted to dense models, and it remains unclear whether the results extend to Mixture of Experts (MoE) architectures" (Section 7).

This is a significant gap given the industry shift toward MoE models (e.g., Mixtral 8×7B, DeepSeek-V3). Appendix M (Figure 27) presents preliminary MoE inference results showing that larger hidden sizes and higher Active-Experts-to-Attention ratios improve throughput — consistent with dense model findings. However, **no MoE scaling law is established**, and the interaction between expert count, expert capacity, and attention allocation remains unexplored. The conditional scaling law cannot guide MoE architecture design without substantial extension.

**Post-Training and Fine-Tuning Behavior**

> "Our analysis is limited to pre-training, and it remains unclear how the results would change under post-training" (Section 7).

All downstream evaluations use **zero-shot settings** on nine benchmarks (Section 4). The paper does not test:
- Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) performance
- Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) stability
- Task-specific adaptation after pre-training

Given that depth affects "compositional generalization in transformer language models" (Petty et al., 2023, cited in Section 1), the fixed-layer approach may have different implications for fine-tuned models. An architecture optimized for pre-training loss may not transfer optimally to instruction-tuned deployments.

**Very Large Model Scales (7B+)**

> "Due to limitations in resources and time, our evaluation does not extend to 7B models" (Section 7).

This is not merely a gap in coverage — it is a **fundamental uncertainty about scaling law stability**. Memory bandwidth constraints, activation checkpointing overhead, and pipeline parallelism efficiency all change non-linearly with model size. The MLP-to-attention optimum near 1.0 may shift at 7B+ scales where attention computation becomes a larger fraction of total inference cost. Without validation beyond 3B, practitioners targeting 7B–70B models cannot confidently apply these findings.

**Long-Context Inference Scenarios**

The inference evaluation uses 4096 input tokens and 1024 output tokens (Section 4). This does not capture:
- **Extreme long-context scenarios** (32K–128K tokens) where KV cache size dominates memory costs
- **Streaming inference** where latency per token matters more than batch throughput
- **Prefill vs. decode phase trade-offs** that may favor different architectures

GQA's impact on KV cache size becomes more pronounced at longer contexts, potentially shifting the optimal GQA value. The local GQA search in Algorithm 1 may need re-tuning for long-context deployments.

---

### 6.3 Computational, Data, and Scalability Constraints

**Training Cost for Scaling Law Fitting**

The paper trains **over 200 model variants** across five parameter scales to fit the conditional scaling law (Section 4). While each individual model is small (80M–297M for fitting), the aggregate compute is substantial:

| Scale | Parameters | Tokens | Approx. FLOPs (6ND) |
|-------|-----------|--------|---------------------|
| 80M | 80M | 8B | ~3.8 × 10¹⁸ |
| 145M | 145M | 14.5B | ~1.3 × 10¹⁹ |
| 297M | 297M | 29.7B | ~5.3 × 10¹⁹ |
| 1B | 1B | 100B | ~6.0 × 10²⁰ |
| 3B | 3B | 100B | ~1.8 × 10²¹ |

All models are trained on **100× N_non-embed tokens** (5× Chinchilla-optimal) to ensure convergence (Section 4). For organizations without access to large-scale training infrastructure, fitting the scaling law from scratch may be **prohibitively expensive**. The paper does not provide pre-fit coefficients that could be reused, limiting accessibility.

**GQA Search Overhead**

Algorithm 1 performs local GQA search with early stopping, enumerating feasible values where "GQA must be a prime factor of the number of attention heads $n_{heads}$" (Section 3.4). While this keeps search tractable, it introduces:
- **Additional training runs** for each candidate GQA value during search
- **No guarantee of global optimum** — early stopping may miss better configurations
- **Scale-dependent search space** — larger models have more head count options, increasing search complexity

For the 3B models, the paper does not report how many GQA configurations were evaluated before settling on GQA=7 for Surefire-3B. This opacity makes it difficult to estimate search costs for new scales.

**Outlier Sensitivity Requires Prior Knowledge**

Appendix J (Figure 25) shows that including extreme `r_mlp/attn` values (&lt;0.5 or >5) degrades Spearman correlation from 0.7451 to 0.3309. This means practitioners must **constrain the search space based on prior knowledge** of reasonable MLP-to-attention ratios. However, this creates a circular dependency: if you already know the reasonable range, why fit a scaling law? The paper resolves this by noting open-weight models typically fall between 0.5 and 5 (Section 5), but this heuristic may not hold for novel architecture families.

**Token Budget Assumption**

All models are trained on 100B tokens at 1B and 3B scales (Table 1). This is **5× the Chinchilla-optimal token count** for these parameter sizes. While this ensures convergence, it may not reflect practical training budgets where organizations train closer to Chinchilla-optimal to minimize costs. The scaling law coefficients may shift if trained on fewer tokens, as the loss landscape changes with training progress.

---

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

**Unexplained Industry Practice Discrepancy**

The paper finds optimal `r_mlp/attn ≈ 1.0`, yet industry models use `r = 3.0–4.8` (LLaMA-3.2-1B: 4.80, LLaMA-3.2-3B: 3.0, Qwen3-8B: 4.67). The paper does not explain this discrepancy. Possible factors include:
- **Fine-tuning performance**: MLP-heavy models may adapt better to downstream tasks
- **Historical design choices**: Early LLaMA models established conventions that persisted
- **Unmeasured metrics**: Throughput and accuracy may not capture all deployment concerns (e.g., latency tail, memory footprint)

Without addressing this gap, practitioners may hesitate to adopt `r ≈ 1.0` configurations despite the paper's evidence.

**GQA Optimization Remains Heuristic**

The paper treats GQA via discrete local search because "the relationship between GQA and training loss is highly fluctuating" (Appendix I, Figure 24). While practical, this means:
- **No analytical optimum** can be derived for GQA
- **Search results may vary** across random seeds or training runs
- **No theoretical understanding** of why GQA affects loss non-smoothly

This contrasts with hidden size and MLP ratio, where the U-shaped curves enable closed-form optimization. The asymmetry in treatment suggests the framework is incomplete for full architectural optimization.

**Coefficient Drift Across Scales**

Figure 8 reveals that scaling law coefficients "shift with model size" (Section 5.1). The 1B-fitted coefficients for 3B prediction ($a_0 = 2.319$, $a_1 = 0.238$) differ substantially from the progressive fit coefficients ($a_0 = 2.697$, $a_1 = 0.0974$). This **coefficient drift** implies:
- No single set of coefficients works across all scales
- Practitioners must refit for each target scale range
- The "universal" scaling law is actually **scale-local**

This undermines the appeal of scaling laws as general predictive tools and increases the burden on practitioners.

**No Latency or Memory Footprint Optimization**

The framework optimizes for **throughput (tokens/s)** and **accuracy**, but does not consider:
- **Time-to-first-token (TTFT)** latency, critical for interactive applications
- **Peak memory usage**, which determines deployability on edge devices
- **Energy consumption**, increasingly important for sustainability

An architecture optimized for throughput may have higher latency or memory requirements, making it unsuitable for latency-sensitive or resource-constrained deployments.

**Limited Downstream Task Diversity**

The nine evaluation benchmarks focus on **reasoning and knowledge tasks** (ARC, HellaSwag, SciQ, etc.). The paper does not evaluate:
- **Code generation** performance, where MLP capacity may matter differently
- **Multilingual capabilities**, which may have different architectural requirements
- **Tool use and agent behaviors**, emerging use cases with distinct demands

The optimal architecture for benchmark accuracy may not be optimal for specialized applications.

### 6.5 Summary of Limitations

| Category | Limitation | Severity | Mitigation |
|----------|-----------|----------|------------|
| **Scale** | No validation beyond 3B parameters | High | Fit on 1B+ models for 7B+ targets |
| **Architecture** | Dense models only (no MoE) | High | Extend scaling law to MoE parameters |
| **Training** | Pre-training only (no fine-tuning) | Medium | Evaluate post-training performance |
| **Hardware** | A100/H200 only | Medium | Measure on target deployment hardware |
| **Optimization** | GQA via heuristic search | Medium | Develop smoother GQA-loss models |
| **Data** | Requires ~200 model variants to fit | High | Release pre-fit coefficients for reuse |
| **Metrics** | Throughput + accuracy only | Medium | Add latency, memory, energy metrics |
| **Tasks** | Nine benchmarks, no code/multilingual | Medium | Expand evaluation suite |

The paper's contributions are **substantial within its validated scope** (80M–3B, dense models, pre-training, throughput optimization). However, practitioners should treat the findings as **scale-local guidance** rather than universal laws, and verify key assumptions (separability, optimum ratios) for their specific deployment context before committing to large-scale training.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how the conditional scaling law framework reshapes LLM development practices, identifies concrete research opportunities it unlocks, and provides actionable guidance for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this approach.

---

### 7.1 How This Work Changes the Landscape

**From Scale-Only to Architecture-Aware Optimization**

Prior to this work, scaling law research operated under an implicit assumption: **once you fix parameter count $N$ and token count $D$, architectural choices are secondary**. The Chinchilla framework (Hoffmann et al., 2022) answers "how many parameters and tokens?" but not "how should those parameters be structured internally?" This paper fundamentally shifts that paradigm by demonstrating that **architectural tuning alone** — without increasing parameters or tokens — creates a Pareto-improving opportunity.

The evidence is unambiguous: Table 1 (Section 5) shows Panda-3B achieves **lower training loss (2.619 vs. 2.625)** and **higher downstream accuracy (62.5% vs. 61.9%)** than LLaMA-3.2-3B despite using the same parameter and token budgets. This is not an incremental optimization — it reveals that **existing open-weight models are systematically misconfigured**.

**The MLP-to-Attention Ratio Revelation**

Perhaps the most disruptive finding is that the optimal `r_mlp/attn` is **near 1.0**, not the 3.0–4.8 range used by LLaMA-3.2 and Qwen3. Section 5.1 reports learned coefficients yielding `r = 1.032` for 1B models and `r = 1.055` for 3B models. Figure 5 (Section 3.3) shows consistent U-shaped loss curves across all scales with minima near `r = 1.0`.

> "This suggests the field has been systematically over-allocating parameters to MLP layers at the expense of attention" (Section 4.2).

This finding contradicts industry practice and demands explanation. If `r ≈ 1.0` is truly optimal for pre-training, why do major model families use `r = 3–5`? Possible explanations the paper does not resolve:
- **Fine-tuning dynamics**: MLP-heavy models may adapt better to downstream tasks
- **Historical path dependence**: Early LLaMA designs established conventions that persisted
- **Unmeasured deployment metrics**: Latency, memory footprint, or energy efficiency may favor different configurations

Regardless of the explanation, this finding forces the community to **re-examine architectural heuristics** that have become standard practice.

**Scale-Proximity Fitting Principle**

Figure 8 (Section 5.1) reveals a counterintuitive result: fitting the scaling law on **1B data alone** to predict 3B behavior achieves **Spearman correlation of 1.0000**, compared to **0.5000** when fitting on 80M/145M/297M/1B data. This challenges the assumption that "more diverse training data improves scaling law prediction."

The practical implication is profound: **you need models at ~1/3 of your target scale to fit the law reliably**. For organizations targeting 70B models, this means fitting on 20B+ models — a non-trivial compute requirement. This finding reframes scaling law fitting from a "use the smallest models you can afford" problem to a "invest in near-scale fitting models" problem.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Opportunities

The paper explicitly acknowledges several gaps in Section 7 (Limitations and Future Work). We expand these into concrete research directions:

**1. Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) Scaling Laws**

> "Our analysis is restricted to dense models, and it remains unclear whether the results extend to Mixture of Experts (MoE) architectures" (Section 7).

Appendix M (Figure 27) shows preliminary MoE inference results: larger hidden sizes and higher Active-Experts-to-Attention ratios improve throughput, consistent with dense model findings. However, **no MoE scaling law is established**. Key open questions:

- How do expert count, expert capacity, and routing sparsity interact with the architectural factors studied here?
- Does the `r_mlp/attn ≈ 1.0` optimum hold when MLP layers are sparsely activated?
- How should GQA be optimized in MoE contexts where different experts may have different attention patterns?

This is the highest-priority extension given the industry shift toward MoE (Mixtral, DeepSeek-V3, Grok).

**2. Post-Training and Fine-Tuning Behavior**

> "Our analysis is limited to pre-training, and it remains unclear how the results would change under post-training" (Section 7).

All downstream evaluations use **zero-shot settings** on nine benchmarks (Section 4). The paper does not test:
- Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) performance on instruction-following tasks
- Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) stability
- Task-specific adaptation after pre-training

Given that Petty et al. (2023) — cited in Section 1 — shows depth affects "compositional generalization in transformer language models," the fixed-layer approach may have different implications for fine-tuned models. Research needed:

- Do architectures optimized for pre-training loss transfer optimally to instruction-tuned deployments?
- Does the `r_mlp/attn ≈ 1.0` optimum shift after fine-tuning?
- How does GQA affect fine-tuning convergence and final task performance?

**3. Larger Scale Validation (7B+)**

> "Due to limitations in resources and time, our evaluation does not extend to 7B models" (Section 7).

This is not merely a coverage gap — it is a **fundamental uncertainty about scaling law stability**. Memory bandwidth constraints, activation checkpointing overhead, and pipeline parallelism efficiency all change non-linearly with model size. Research priorities:

- Does the `r_mlp/attn ≈ 1.0` optimum hold at 7B, 13B, and 70B scales?
- Does the scale-proximity fitting principle (fit on ~1/3 of target scale) generalize?
- Do coefficient drift patterns observed in Figure 8 follow predictable trajectories?

**4. GQA Optimization Theory**

The paper treats GQA via discrete local search because "the relationship between GQA and training loss is highly fluctuating" (Appendix I, Figure 24). While practical, this leaves key questions unanswered:

- **Why** does GQA affect loss non-smoothly? The paper hypothesizes opposing effects on query projection dimensionality and KV expressivity, but provides no formal model.
- Can we develop a **predictive theory** for GQA-loss relationships that enables analytical optimization?
- Does the optimal GQA value depend on context length, batch size, or hardware characteristics?

**5. Hardware-Aware Architecture Search**

Section 5.1 acknowledges: "inference efficiency $I_N(P)$ could, in principle, be expressed analytically, it depends heavily on hardware and inference configurations." The current framework requires re-measuring throughput on target hardware for each deployment scenario. Research opportunities:

- Can we build **hardware performance models** that predict throughput from architectural parameters without empirical measurement?
- How do optimal architectures differ across A100, H200, TPU, Inferentia, and edge devices?
- Can we optimize for **latency** (time-to-first-token) rather than throughput for interactive applications?

**6. Extended Evaluation Suites**

The nine evaluation benchmarks focus on **reasoning and knowledge tasks** (ARC, HellaSwag, SciQ, etc.). The paper does not evaluate:

- **Code generation** performance, where MLP capacity may matter differently
- **Multilingual capabilities**, which may have different architectural requirements
- **Tool use and agent behaviors**, emerging use cases with distinct demands

Expanding the evaluation suite would test whether the architectural optima discovered here generalize across application domains.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

**When to Use This Framework**

The conditional scaling law framework is most valuable in the following scenarios:

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| **Training custom 1B–3B dense models** | **Strongly recommended** | Validated at these scales with strong results (Table 1) |
| **Inference cost dominates training cost** | **Strongly recommended** | Framework explicitly optimizes throughput (Section 3.4) |
| **Target scale ≥7B** | **Use with caution** | No validation beyond 3B; fit on ~1/3 target scale |
| **MoE architectures** | **Not applicable** | No MoE scaling law established (Section 7) |
| **Fine-tuning focused deployments** | **Use with caution** | Only pre-training validated; fine-tuning behavior unknown |
| **Latency-critical applications** | **Extend framework** | Optimizes throughput, not time-to-first-token |

**Integration with Existing Training Pipelines**

For organizations with existing training infrastructure, the framework integrates as follows:

1. **Small-model fitting phase**: Train 50–100 model variants at 80M–297M scale (or ~1/3 of target scale) varying `d_model` and `r_mlp/attn`. Use the hyperparameters in Appendix E (Table 5) as a starting point.

2. **Scaling law fitting**: Apply the multiplicative calibration form (Equation 3, Section 3.3) using the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm. Exclude outliers with `r_mlp/attn < 0.5` or `> 5` (Appendix J, Figure 25).

3. **Architecture search**: Solve the constrained optimization (Equation 4, Section 3.4) with target loss $L_t$ set to match your baseline model's loss. Perform local GQA search per Algorithm 1 (Section 3.4).

4. **Large-scale validation**: Train 1–2 models at target scale using predicted architectures before committing to full training runs.

**Expected Compute Requirements**

Based on the paper's experimental setup (Section 4):

- **Fitting phase**: ~200 model variants at 80M–297M scale, each trained on $100 \times N_{non-embed}$ tokens
- **Total fitting FLOPs**: Approximately $10^{20}$ FLOPs (dominated by 297M models)
- **Validation phase**: 1–3 models at target scale (1B–3B)

For organizations without access to this compute, the paper does not provide pre-fit coefficients that could be reused. This is a **significant barrier to adoption** that future work should address.

**Comparison to Alternatives**

| Approach | When to Prefer | Trade-offs |
|----------|---------------|------------|
| **Standard Chinchilla** | Training cost dominates; architecture is fixed | Ignores inference efficiency; may leave performance on the table |
| **This framework** | Inference cost dominates; custom architecture allowed | Requires ~200 small model training runs; validated only to 3B |
| **Sardana et al. (2023)** | Lifetime token generation is predictable | Requires unknowable deployment estimates; impractical for most use cases |
| **Bian et al. (2025)** | Only aspect ratio tuning needed | Does not capture GQA, MLP-to-attention effects |

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

**Reproducibility Checklist**

The paper provides strong reproducibility support:

- **Training framework**: Megatron-LM (Shoeybi et al., 2019) — publicly available
- **Inference framework**: vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023) and SGLang (Zheng et al., 2023) — publicly available
- **Evaluation harness**: lm-eval-harness (Gao et al., 2024a) — publicly available
- **Training data**: Dolma-v1.7 (Soldaini et al., 2024) — publicly available
- **Hyperparameters**: Appendix E (Table 5) provides complete training configuration
- **Architecture variants**: Appendix D (Table 4) lists all 200+ model configurations

**Gaps for Practitioners**:

- **Pre-fit coefficients**: The paper does not release fitted scaling law coefficients that could be reused without training 200+ models
- **GQA search code**: Algorithm 1 describes the approach but does not provide implementation details (e.g., early stopping threshold)
- **Throughput measurement scripts**: No code provided for reproducing Table 6 throughput measurements

**Integration Recommendations**

For practitioners adopting this framework:

1. **Start with scale-proximate fitting**: If targeting 3B models, fit on 1B variants rather than 80M–297M (Figure 8 shows Spearman 1.0000 vs. 0.5000).

2. **Constrain the search space**: Exclude `r_mlp/attn < 0.5` or `> 5` based on Appendix J findings — including outliers degrades Spearman from 0.7451 to 0.3309.

3. **Validate on target hardware**: Throughput measurements on A100 may not transfer to H200, TPU, or edge devices. Re-measure inference efficiency on your deployment hardware.

4. **Test fine-tuning behavior**: Before committing to production, fine-tune the predicted architecture on your target tasks to verify pre-training optima transfer.

5. **Consider latency metrics**: If your application is latency-sensitive (e.g., chatbots), measure time-to-first-token in addition to throughput — the framework optimizes for throughput only.

### 7.5 Summary: A New Paradigm for LLM Design

This work establishes that **architecture matters as much as scale** for LLM performance. The key takeaways for the field:

| Insight | Prior Belief | New Understanding |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Architectural tuning** | Secondary to scale | Creates Pareto improvements without more compute |
| **MLP-to-attention ratio** | Higher is better (3–5) | Optimum near 1.0 |
| **Scaling law fitting** | More scales = better prediction | Scale-proximate fitting outperforms far-scale fitting |
| **GQA optimization** | Continuous parameter | Discrete search variable due to fluctuating loss relationship |
| **Inference efficiency** | Post-training concern | Should be optimized during architecture design |

The conditional scaling law framework provides **actionable guidance** for practitioners training custom models at 1B–3B scales. However, significant open questions remain around MoE architectures, fine-tuning behavior, and larger scale validation. The field should treat this as a **foundational contribution** that opens new research directions rather than a final answer to architecture optimization.

For organizations considering adoption: if you are training dense models at 1B–3B scales and inference cost matters, this framework offers **validated, measurable improvements** (up to 42% throughput, 2.1% accuracy). If you are targeting larger scales or MoE architectures, use the principles but expect to extend the methodology for your specific context.
