## 1. Executive Summary

This study investigates whether Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) genuinely expands reasoning capabilities or merely sharpens precision within the base model's existing solution space, introducing the concept of "empirical support" to quantify accessible correct completions. Across diverse models (1.5B–14B parameters, including ProRL and Nemotron) and benchmarks (MATH500, AIME, Reasoning Gym), experiments reveal that while RLVR improves `pass@1`, it consistently yields negative Net Support Change Rates (−0.01 to −0.06) and near-zero Net Discovery Rates (≤0.04), indicating support shrinkage outweighs expansion. These findings expose an "invisible leash" where RLVR acts as a support-constrained optimizer that narrows answer-level entropy, suggesting future methods must seed probability mass into underrepresented regions to truly extend reasoning horizons.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Rise of RLVR and the Unanswered Question

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) has emerged as the dominant training paradigm for large reasoning models. Systems like DeepSeek-R1 and OpenAI-o1 achieve remarkable performance on mathematics and programming tasks by optimizing pretrained base models using automatically computed rewards — signals that can be verified without human judgment (e.g., whether a math answer equals the ground truth, or whether code passes test cases). This approach offers a scalable alternative to Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), which relies on expensive human preference labels and learned reward models for subjective alignment.

However, despite empirical success, a fundamental question remains actively debated within the research community: **Does RLVR genuinely expand a model's reasoning capabilities, or does it simply amplify high-reward outputs that the base model already knows?**

This distinction matters profoundly. If RLVR merely sharpens precision within existing knowledge, then its improvements reflect better exploitation of known solution patterns rather than true capability expansion. Conversely, if RLVR can discover reasoning strategies absent from the base model, it represents a mechanism for genuine capability growth. The paper frames this as the "invisible leash" hypothesis: RLVR may be fundamentally constrained by its initialization, unable to escape the base model's effective reasoning boundary regardless of training duration or compute budget.

### Why This Question Matters

The stakes extend beyond academic curiosity to practical deployment and research direction:

**Theoretical Significance.** Understanding RLVR's capacity for capability expansion informs how we think about model improvement. If RLVR cannot discover solutions outside the base model's support, then scaling RLVR alone — without architectural changes or explicit exploration mechanisms — hits a hard ceiling. This has implications for how we interpret performance gains: are we witnessing genuine reasoning breakthroughs, or merely more reliable retrieval of latent knowledge?

**Real-World Impact.** In high-stakes domains like mathematics, medicine, and code generation, diversity of solutions matters. A model that converges to a narrow set of correct answers may fail on edge cases that require alternative reasoning paths. Section 4.2 documents a paradoxical pattern: while RLVR models outperform base models at low sampling budgets (`pass@1`), base models often dominate at high budgets (`pass@8192`) due to broader solution coverage. For instance, on AIME2024, the base model achieves `pass@8192 = 93.3%` versus ProRL-1.5B's `83.3%` (Table 6, Appendix A). This suggests that RLVR's precision gains come at the cost of losing access to valid alternative solutions — a critical trade-off for applications requiring robustness.

**Research Direction.** If RLVR is inherently support-constrained, then future progress requires explicit exploration strategies that seed probability mass into underrepresented solution regions. The paper's conclusion emphasizes that breaking the "invisible leash" demands hybrid approaches beyond standard RLVR recipes.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

Existing work has examined RLVR through several lenses, each with notable gaps:

**Before/After Snapshots.** Most prior analyses compare base and RLVR models only at training endpoints, leaving unexplored how RLVR reshapes the model's effective reasoning support throughout training (Section 1). This static view cannot capture the temporal dynamics of support expansion versus shrinkage. Table 4 (Section 4.2) addresses this by tracking support metrics across training steps, revealing that Net Support Change Rate (NSCR) gradually decreases over time — indicating steady contraction of accessible solutions even as `pass@1` improves.

**Aggregate Metrics Without Mechanism.** Standard evaluation relies on `pass@k` as a proxy for reasoning capability. While useful, `pass@k` primarily captures solution retrieval rather than novel reasoning capacity (Wen et al., 2025, cited in Section 1). A model could improve `pass@1` by concentrating mass on known correct answers without discovering new reasoning patterns. This paper introduces **empirical support** as a finer-grained lens: the set of correct solutions a model can realistically discover under finite sampling, defined formally in Section 3.1 as `supp_ϵ(p) := {y ∈ C | p(y|x) > ϵ}` where `ϵ` is a threshold separating practically observable likelihood from statistically negligible probability.

**Domain-Specific Claims.** Some studies argue that RLVR's limitations appear only in specialized domains where base models were already well-trained, suggesting RLVR could substantially expand reasoning in other domains (Liu et al., 2025a, cited in Section 1). This paper tests that claim across mathematics, logical reasoning, factual QA, and code generation, finding the precision-diversity trade-off is **fundamental, not domain-specific** (Section 1, Claim 2). Table 1 shows consistent patterns across all evaluated models (1.5B–14B parameters) and domains, with Support Retention Rate (SRR) near 0.93–0.99 but Net Discovery Rate (NDR) ≤ 0.04.

**Entropy Interpretation.** Prior work documents entropy collapse during RLVR training (Cui et al., 2025, cited in Section 5.2), but often conflates token-level uncertainty with global exploration. This paper reveals a critical decoupling: RLVR sometimes **increases** token-level entropy (appearing more uncertain during generation) while simultaneously **reducing** answer-level entropy (converging to fewer final solutions). Section 5.2 terms this "local stochasticity without global exploration" — stepwise variability that ultimately collapses to a narrow set of answers. Table 5 shows ProRL-1.5B increases token-level entropy from 0.44 to 0.52 on average while decreasing answer-level entropy from 1.30 to 0.66.

### This Paper's Positioning

The paper positions itself as a **systematic empirical investigation** into RLVR's fundamental capabilities and limitations, distinct from prior work in three ways:

**1. Support-Centric Framework.** Rather than treating `pass@k` as the primary metric, the paper introduces empirical support dynamics with four categories (Figure 1):
- **Support Preservation:** Correct completions accessible to both base and RLVR models
- **Support Shrinkage:** Correct completions downweighted by RLVR below threshold `ϵ`
- **Support Expansion:** Correct completions newly upweighted by RLVR despite negligible base model mass
- **Out of Support:** Completions missed by both

This framework enables quantifying whether RLVR preserves, expands, or contracts the accessible solution space — a question prior before/after comparisons cannot answer.

**2. Multi-Scale, Multi-Domain Validation.** The study evaluates models from 1.5B to 14B parameters (ProRL, Nemotron, Skywork, Phi4-Reason, plus a vision-language model) across math benchmarks (MATH500, AIME, Minerva, OlympiadBench, AMC) and non-math tasks (SimpleQA, LiveBench, SciBench, Reasoning Gym, LiveCodeBench). This breadth tests whether observed patterns are artifacts of specific model families or training procedures. Table 1 aggregates results showing consistent negative NSCR values (−0.01 to −0.06) across all configurations.

**3. Theoretical Grounding.** Appendix C provides formal proofs establishing why RLVR is inherently support-bounded. Theorem C.1 proves that for on-policy gradient updates, `supp(π_θ(·|x)) ⊆ supp(q(·|x))` — RLVR cannot assign positive probability to any solution the base model assigns zero probability. Corollary C.2 extends this to asymptotic sampling: `lim sup_{k→∞} pass@k_{π_θ}(x) ≤ lim sup_{k→∞} pass@k_q(x)`. This theoretical foundation explains the empirical patterns: RLVR improves sampling efficiency within existing support but cannot discover truly novel reasoning patterns without explicit exploration mechanisms.

**4. Training Dynamics Analysis.** Unlike static before/after comparisons, Section 4.2 tracks support metrics throughout RLVR training (Table 4). Results show SRR remains consistently high while NSCR gradually decreases, indicating RLVR progressively narrows the accessible solution space even as single-sample accuracy improves. This temporal perspective reveals that support contraction is not a training artifact but an inherent consequence of the RLVR objective.

**5. SFT vs. RLVR Comparison.** Section 4.2 includes a controlled comparison between Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and DAPO (an RLVR variant) on identical base models, datasets, and hyperparameters. Table 3 shows SFT produces moderate support expansion with positive NSCR values, while DAPO produces sharply concentrated distributions with negative NSCR. This confirms that support-constrained behavior emerges from the RLVR objective itself, not from scaling, data mixture, or training procedure.

By combining empirical support metrics, multi-scale validation, theoretical proofs, and training dynamics analysis, this paper provides a comprehensive answer to the RLVR capability question: current RLVR practices act as **precision enhancers, not capability expanders**. The "invisible leash" is real — RLVR remains fundamentally constrained by its initialization, requiring future innovations to seed probability mass into underrepresented solution regions for genuine reasoning horizon extension.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is an empirical investigation paper that introduces a measurement framework to diagnose whether RLVR training expands reasoning capabilities or merely sharpens precision within existing knowledge, and the core idea is that by tracking which correct solutions remain accessible before and after RLVR training, we can distinguish genuine capability expansion from support-constrained optimization.

**Empirical Support Framework: Formalizing Solution Accessibility**

The paper begins by formalizing what it means for a model to "have access" to a correct solution, because without this definition we cannot measure whether RLVR expands or contracts the solution space. Let `$\mathcal{X}$` denote the space of natural language prompts (the questions we ask), and let `$\mathcal{Y}$` denote the space of token sequences (the reasoning traces or completions the model generates). For a fixed prompt `$x \in \mathcal{X}$`, the base model produces an output distribution `$q(y|x)$` over possible completions, while the RLVR-trained model produces a distribution `$\pi_\theta(y|x)$` parameterized by `$\theta$`. A verifiable reward function `$R(x, y) \in \{0, 1\}$` indicates whether completion `$y$` is correct for prompt `$x$`, where 1 means correct and 0 means incorrect. The set of all correct completions is denoted `$\mathcal{C} = \{y \in \mathcal{Y} \mid R(x, y) = 1\}$`.

The **effective support** of correct completions for any distribution `$p(y|x)$` is defined as `$\text{supp}(p) := \{y \in \mathcal{C} \mid p(y|x) > 0\}$`, meaning the set of correct completions that the model assigns any positive probability to. However, this definition is too strict for practical measurement because softmax layers in neural networks yield strictly positive probabilities across all tokens, making the nominal support span the entire space `$\mathcal{Y}$`. To address this, the paper introduces **empirical support relaxation** with a threshold `$\epsilon > 0$`, defined as `$\text{supp}_\epsilon(q) := \{y \in \mathcal{C} \mid q(y|x) > \epsilon\}$`. This threshold `$\epsilon$` separates completions with practically observable likelihood from those that are statistically negligible under finite sampling. The choice of `$\epsilon$` is crucial: it should correspond to the minimum probability required for a correct completion to appear within finite samples, and Appendix C.4 derives a principled estimate based on sampling confidence bounds (for example, with `$k = 8192$` samples and 95% confidence, `$\epsilon \approx 3.66 \times 10^{-4}$`).

**Four-Category Support Taxonomy: Preservation, Expansion, Shrinkage, Out of Support**

Using the empirical support definition, the paper categorizes what happens to each correct solution under RLVR training into four mutually exclusive categories, as illustrated conceptually in Figure 1. **Support Preservation** includes completions where both the base model and RLVR model assign probability above `$\epsilon$` (formally, `$q(y^*|x) > \epsilon$` and `$\pi_\theta(y^*|x) > \epsilon$`), meaning RLVR maintains access to solutions the base model already knew. **Support Shrinkage** includes correct completions that the base model could find but RLVR downweights below `$\epsilon$` (formally, `$q(y^*|x) > \epsilon$` but `$\pi_\theta(y^*|x) \leq \epsilon$`), meaning RLVR loses access to previously accessible solutions. **Support Expansion** includes completions that RLVR newly upweights above `$\epsilon$` despite negligible base model mass (formally, `$q(y^*|x) \leq \epsilon$` but `$\pi_\theta(y^*|x) > \epsilon$`), meaning RLVR discovers solutions the base model effectively could not find. **Out of Support** refers to completions missed by both models (both assign probability `$\leq \epsilon$`), representing solutions neither model can access.

Let `$P$`, `$E$`, `$S$`, and `$O$` denote the counts of correct completions in preservation, expansion, shrinkage, and out-of-support categories respectively. These counts form the foundation for all quantitative claims about RLVR behavior.

**Support Dynamics Metrics: Quantifying Preservation Versus Discovery**

The paper introduces four metrics derived from `$P$`, `$E$`, `$S$`, and `$O$` to quantify different aspects of RLVR's impact on solution accessibility. The **Support Retention Rate (SRR)** measures how well RLVR preserves the base model's accessible correct solutions, computed as `$\text{SRR} = \frac{P}{P + S}$`. SRR ranges from 0 to 1, where SRR = 1 means no shrinkage occurred (all base model solutions remain accessible), and lower values indicate more solutions were lost. The **Net Discovery Rate (NDR)** measures the fraction of RLVR's accessible solutions that represent genuine discoveries, computed as `$\text{NDR} = \frac{E}{P + E}$`. NDR ranges from 0 to 1, where NDR = 0 means no new solutions were found (all accessible solutions were already known to the base model), and NDR = 1 means all accessible solutions are discoveries (the base model had no access).

The **Support Dynamic Score (SDS)** provides a balanced measure combining retention and discovery using the harmonic mean, computed as `$\text{SDS} = \frac{2 \cdot \text{SRR} \cdot \text{NDR}}{\text{SRR} + \text{NDR}} = \frac{2PE}{P^2 + 2PE + ES}$`. SDS ranges from 0 to 1, and high SDS requires both good retention (high SRR) and meaningful expansion (high NDR), making it sensitive to imbalance between the two. The **Net Support Change Rate (NSCR)** captures the net expansion or shrinkage of empirical support, computed as `$\text{NSCR} = \frac{E - S}{P + E + S}$`. NSCR ranges from -1 to 1, where positive values indicate net expansion (more solutions gained than lost), negative values indicate net shrinkage (more solutions lost than gained), and values near zero indicate balanced changes.

These metrics enable distinguishing between different RLVR behaviors: support-constrained optimization (high SRR, low NDR), genuine capability expansion (high SRR, high NDR), inefficient redistribution (low SRR, low NDR), and aggressive exploration (low SRR, high NDR). Table 1 in Section 4.2 reports aggregate values across all evaluated models, showing SRR ≈ 0.93–0.99 but NDR ≤ 0.04 and NSCR ≈ -0.01 to -0.06, indicating predominant preservation with net shrinkage.

**RLVR Objective Function: The Optimization Target**

The paper studies various RLVR algorithms including PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), RLOO (Kool et al., 2019), GRPO (Guo et al., 2025), DAPO (Yu et al., 2025), and REINFORCE++ (Hu, 2025), all of which learn a new distribution `$\pi_\theta(y|x)$` to optimize variants of the following regularized objective:

$$
\max_\theta \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x), x \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ R(x, y) - \beta^{-1} \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y|x)}{q(y|x)} \right]
$$

where `$\mathcal{D}$` is the distribution of prompts, and `$\beta > 0$` is a hyperparameter controlling the strength of KL divergence regularization that penalizes deviation from the base model `$q$`. In plain language, this objective maximizes expected reward while constraining how far the trained policy can drift from the base model, with `$\beta$` determining the trade-off: larger `$\beta$` allows more deviation, smaller `$\beta$` enforces closer adherence to the base distribution.

The KL regularization term `$\log \frac{\pi_\theta(y|x)}{q(y|x)}$` is critical for understanding why RLVR exhibits support-bounded behavior. Appendix C.2 shows that the optimal solution to this objective takes the form `$\pi_\theta(y|x) \propto q(y|x) \cdot \exp(\beta R(x, y))$`, meaning the RLVR distribution is an exponentially tilted version of the base distribution that upweights high-reward completions but cannot assign positive probability to any completion that `$q$` assigns zero probability to. This mathematical structure explains why RLVR acts as a reweighting mechanism rather than a discovery mechanism: it can shift probability mass among solutions the base model already knows, but it cannot create probability mass where none existed.

**Sampling Protocol and Evaluation Configuration**

The experimental setup uses specific sampling budgets to ensure fair comparison between base and RLVR models while maintaining statistical power to detect support changes. For math reasoning tasks (MATH500, Minerva, OlympiadBench, AIME 2024, AIME 2025, AMC 2023), sampling budgets are `$k \in \{4096, 8192\}$`. For Reasoning Gym tasks, budgets are `$k \in \{1024, 2048, 4096, 8192, 16384\}$` to accommodate varying task difficulty. For other non-math datasets (SimpleQA, LiveBench, SciBench, LiveCodeBench), budgets are `$k \in \{1024, 2048\}$`. These budgets are chosen to ensure that any unreachable solution `$y^* \in \mathcal{C}$` remains below the empirical support threshold of the base model, preventing false positives in expansion detection.

All models use a sampling temperature of 0.6 and a top_p value of 0.95 during evaluation, with a maximum response length of 32768 tokens (Section B.1, Appendix B). These hyperparameters balance response diversity and quality while ensuring completions are long enough to contain full reasoning traces. The inference backend is vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023) for efficient batched generation across the large sampling budgets required for support measurement.

**Entropy Measurement: Token-Level Versus Answer-Level**

To understand the mechanism behind support shrinkage, the paper measures two distinct entropy metrics that capture uncertainty at different granularities. **Token-Level Entropy** measures local uncertainty at each generation step. Let `$V$` denote the vocabulary and `$y^{(i)} = (y^{(i)}_1, y^{(i)}_2, \ldots, y^{(i)}_{T^{(i)}})$` denote the `$i$`-th generated sequence of length `$T^{(i)}$` for `$1 \leq i \leq N$`. At each timestep `$t$`, the model outputs a probability distribution `$p^{(i)}_t(v)$` over vocabulary tokens `$v \in V$`. The entropy of this distribution is `$H^{(i)}_t = -\sum_{v \in V} p^{(i)}_t(v) \log p^{(i)}_t(v)$`. The average token-level entropy over all `$N$` sequences and their timesteps is computed as:

$$
\text{TokenEntropy} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \left( \frac{1}{T^{(i)}} \sum_{t=1}^{T^{(i)}} H^{(i)}_t \right)
$$

This captures the local uncertainty at each generation step, with higher values indicating more stochastic token selection.

**Answer-Level Entropy** measures global diversity over final outputs. Let `$\{o^{(1)}, \ldots, o^{(N)}\}$` denote the answers extracted from each generated sequence `$y^{(i)}$` (using NA for incomplete outputs), and let `$\{o^*_1, \ldots, o^*_M\}$` be the `$M$` unique answers. Let `$f_j$` be the frequency of answer `$o^*_j$`, with empirical probability `$p_j = \frac{f_j}{N}$`. Then:

$$
\text{AnswerEntropy} = -\sum_{j=1}^M p_j \log p_j
$$

This captures global diversity over output completions, with lower values indicating increased mode collapse (convergence to fewer distinct answers).

The critical insight from Section 5.2 is that these two entropy measures can diverge: RLVR sometimes **increases** token-level entropy (appearing more uncertain during generation) while simultaneously **reducing** answer-level entropy (converging to fewer final solutions). Table 5 shows ProRL-1.5B increases average token-level entropy from 0.44 to 0.52 while decreasing average answer-level entropy from 1.30 to 0.66. The paper terms this phenomenon "local stochasticity without global exploration": the model exhibits variability in token selection but ultimately collapses to a narrow set of answers. This decoupling explains why token-level entropy alone is insufficient for measuring exploratory behavior — stepwise uncertainty does not guarantee diversity in final solutions.

**Theoretical Foundations: Why RLVR Is Inherently Support-Bounded**

Appendix C provides formal proofs establishing that support-bounded behavior is inherent to RLVR, not an artifact of specific implementations. **Theorem C.1 (Support Preservation under RLVR)** states that for all `$x \in \mathcal{X}$`, `$\text{supp}(\pi_\theta(\cdot|x)) \subseteq \text{supp}(q(\cdot|x))$`. In particular, if `$q(y^*|x) = 0$` for some correct solution `$y^*$`, then RLVR cannot discover `$y^*$`. The proof proceeds by induction: RLVR initializes the policy to the base model (`$\pi_{\theta_0}(y|x) = q(y|x)$`), and all standard policy-gradient updates (REINFORCE, PPO, GRPO) take the form:

$$
\theta' = \theta + \eta \nabla_\theta \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x)} \left[ R(x, y) - \beta^{-1} \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y|x)}{q(y|x)} \right]
$$

where `$\eta$` is the learning rate. Since the outer expectation is over `$y \sim \pi_\theta$`, any `$y^* \in \mathcal{C}$` with `$\pi_\theta(y^*|x) = 0$` is never sampled and thus contributes no gradient component, meaning `$\pi_{\theta'}(y^*|x) = 0$` remains true after the update. By induction, none of the updates can introduce positive probability mass on any `$y^* \in \mathcal{C}$` for which `$q(y^*|x) = 0$`.

**Corollary C.2 (Asymptotic Sampling Upper Bound)** extends this to pass@k performance. Let `$\text{pass@k}_p(x)$` be the probability that at least one out of `$k$` samples `$y_i \sim p(\cdot|x)$` is correct. Under the conditions of Theorem C.1 and sampling independence:

$$
\limsup_{k \to \infty} \text{pass@k}_{\pi_\theta}(x) \leq \limsup_{k \to \infty} \text{pass@k}_q(x)
$$

This establishes a ceiling: no matter how many samples are drawn, the RLVR-trained model cannot exceed the base model's pass@k in the limit. This explains the paradoxical pattern in Section 4.2 where RLVR models outperform base models at low `$k$` (better precision) but base models dominate at high `$k$` (broader coverage).

**Theorem C.6 (Entropy Reduction and Precision-Coverage Trade-off)** proves that any RLVR update `$\pi_\theta$` satisfies `$H[\pi_\theta] \leq H[q]$`, with equality only if the reward is constant on the support of `$q$`. The proof uses the exponentially tilted distribution form `$\pi_\theta(y|x) \propto q(y|x) \exp(\beta R(x, y))$` and standard properties of KL divergence to show `$H[\pi_\theta] = H[q] - \text{KL}(\pi_\theta \| q) \leq H[q]$`. This establishes that entropy reduction is a structural property of RLVR optimization, not an implementation artifact.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This paper delivers several contributions that fundamentally reshape how we understand RLVR's capabilities and limitations. Below, I distinguish between what represents genuine innovation versus incremental refinement of existing ideas.

### 4.1 The "Invisible Leash" as a Fundamental Constraint (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Makes This Novel:** Prior work documented that RLVR improves `pass@1` while sometimes underperforming at high `pass@k` (Yue et al., 2025a; Liu et al., 2025a). However, these observations remained descriptive patterns without a unifying theoretical explanation. This paper provides the first formal characterization proving that RLVR is **inherently support-bounded** — it cannot assign positive probability to any solution the base model assigns zero probability to (Theorem C.1, Appendix C).

**Why This Matters:** This transforms the debate from "does RLVR expand capabilities?" to "under what conditions can RLVR escape its initialization?" The theoretical proof establishes a hard ceiling: no amount of training duration or compute budget allows RLVR to discover solutions outside the base model's effective reach. This has profound implications for research direction — scaling RLVR alone cannot突破 this barrier without explicit exploration mechanisms.

**Evidence:** Table 1 shows consistent negative NSCR values (−0.01 to −0.06) across all 1.5B–14B models and all domains (math, non-math, multimodal). The pattern holds regardless of model family (ProRL, Nemotron, Skywork, Phi4-Reason), confirming this is not an artifact of specific implementations but a structural property of the RLVR objective itself.

**Distinction from Prior Work:** Earlier analyses treated support limitation as an empirical observation. This paper proves it as a mathematical consequence of on-policy gradient updates, elevating the claim from correlation to causation.

### 4.2 Empirical Support Shrinkage Outweighs Expansion (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Makes This Novel:** The prevailing assumption in the RLVR literature is that training either preserves or expands the accessible solution space. This paper reveals the counterintuitive finding that RLVR systematically **loses access** to correct solutions the base model could previously find. Section 4.2 documents that ProRL-1.5B-v2 loses 175 completions while gaining only 48 (ratio ≈ 3.6:1), and similar patterns appear across all evaluated models.

**Why This Matters:** This challenges the interpretation of RLVR gains as pure improvement. When RLVR improves `pass@1`, it does so partly by concentrating probability mass on a narrower subset of correct answers — sometimes excluding valid alternatives. For applications requiring robustness (e.g., medical diagnosis, code generation with multiple valid implementations), this shrinkage represents a hidden cost not captured by standard accuracy metrics.

**Evidence:** Figure 4 illustrates specific Reasoning Gym tasks (Leg Counting, Family Relationships, Power Function) where base models maintain broader solution coverage at high sampling budgets while RLVR models plateau earlier. Table 6 (Appendix A) shows AIME2024 `pass@8192` drops from 93.3% (base) to 83.3% (ProRL) despite `pass@1` improvements.

**Distinction from Prior Work:** Previous before/after comparisons measured net performance changes but could not distinguish between preservation, expansion, and shrinkage. The four-category taxonomy (Section 3.2) enables this fine-grained diagnosis, revealing that apparent gains mask underlying losses.

### 4.3 Token-Level vs. Answer-Level Entropy Decoupling (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Makes This Novel:** Prior work documented entropy collapse during RLVR training (Cui et al., 2025) but treated entropy as a monolithic concept. This paper reveals a critical decoupling: RLVR can **increase** token-level entropy (appearing more uncertain during generation) while simultaneously **decreasing** answer-level entropy (converging to fewer final solutions). Section 5.2 terms this "local stochasticity without global exploration."

**Why This Matters:** This finding invalidates token-level entropy as a proxy for exploratory behavior. Researchers monitoring entropy during RLVR training might observe increasing token-level values and incorrectly conclude the model is exploring more diverse reasoning paths. In reality, these uncertain token sequences converge to the same narrow set of answers. This has direct implications for how we design exploration bonuses and diversity-promoting objectives.

**Evidence:** Table 5 shows ProRL-1.5B increases average token-level entropy from 0.44 to 0.52 while decreasing average answer-level entropy from 1.30 to 0.66. Skywork-OR1-7B exhibits lower token-level entropy than AceReason-7B yet higher answer-level entropy, demonstrating the measures are not correlated.

**Distinction from Prior Work:** Earlier entropy analyses focused on single metrics (typically token-level). This paper's dual-measurement approach reveals that local uncertainty does not predict global diversity — a distinction critical for interpreting RLVR training dynamics.

### 4.4 Precision-Diversity Trade-off as Inherent to RLVR Objective (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Makes This Novel:** Some prior work suggested RLVR's limitations appear only in specialized domains where base models were already well-trained (Liu et al., 2025a, cited in Section 1). This paper demonstrates the precision-diversity trade-off is **fundamental, not domain-specific**. The pattern appears consistently across mathematics, logical reasoning, factual QA, code generation, and visual reasoning (Table 1).

**Why This Matters:** This eliminates the hypothesis that RLVR could expand reasoning in "underexplored" domains while being constrained in mature ones. The trade-off arises from the RLVR objective structure itself (exponentially tilted distribution `$\pi_\theta(y|x) \propto q(y|x) \exp(\beta R(x, y))$`), meaning any domain with verifiable rewards will exhibit similar behavior.

**Evidence:** Table 1 shows NDR ≤ 0.04 across all domains, with math benchmarks particularly stagnant (NDR ≤ 0.01). Even the vision-language model (Kangheng-OVR-7B) exhibits SRR = 1.00, NDR = 0.00, NSCR = −0.00, confirming the pattern transcends modality.

**Distinction from Prior Work:** Previous claims about domain-specificity remained untested across diverse benchmarks. This paper's multi-domain evaluation (11+ benchmarks across 4 domains) provides comprehensive evidence that the trade-off is structural.

### 4.5 SFT vs. RLVR Objective Isolation (Incremental but Important)

**What Makes This Novel:** While not the paper's primary contribution, the controlled comparison between SFT and DAPO (Table 3) isolates the effect of the training objective from confounding variables (model architecture, data, hyperparameters). This confirms support shrinkage emerges from RLVR-style multiplicative updates rather than training procedure artifacts.

**Why This Matters:** This provides actionable guidance for practitioners: if support expansion is desired, SFT may be preferable to RLVR for certain use cases. It also validates that the "invisible leash" is not inevitable for all fine-tuning methods — only for RLVR's specific optimization structure.

**Evidence:** Table 3 shows SFT achieves positive NSCR on AIME25 (0.200) and Minerva (0.050), while DAPO achieves negative NSCR on the same benchmarks (0.118 and −0.120 respectively) under identical conditions.

**Distinction from Prior Work:** Earlier comparisons between SFT and RLVR lacked controlled experimental design, making it unclear whether differences arose from objectives or training configurations. This experiment holds all variables constant except the loss function.

**Summary of Innovation Levels:**

| Contribution | Innovation Level | Reasoning |
|-------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Invisible Leash theoretical proof | Fundamental | First formal proof of support-bounded behavior |
| Support shrinkage > expansion | Fundamental | Counterintuitive finding challenging RLVR improvement narrative |
| Entropy decoupling | Fundamental | Reveals measurement artifact in prior entropy analyses |
| Domain-independent trade-off | Fundamental | Eliminates domain-specificity hypothesis |
| SFT vs. RLVR isolation | Incremental | Confirms mechanism but builds on known objective differences |

These insights collectively establish that current RLVR practices function as **precision enhancers, not capability expanders**. The "invisible leash" is not a temporary limitation awaiting better hyperparameters — it is a structural constraint requiring explicit exploration mechanisms to overcome.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section walks through how the paper validates its claims about RLVR's "invisible leash." I will explain the experimental design choices, present the specific quantitative results, and critically assess whether the evidence supports the paper's conclusions. The goal is to help you understand not just what numbers were reported, but why those particular measurements matter for testing the support-bounded hypothesis.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: Designing Tests for Support Dynamics

The paper's experimental design is driven by a single question: **How do we measure whether RLVR expands or contracts the set of correct solutions a model can access?** This requires careful choices about models, datasets, sampling protocols, and metrics.

**Model Families and Scales.** The study evaluates models across three parameter scales (1.5B, 7B, 14B) and multiple families to test whether observed patterns are artifacts of specific architectures or training procedures. Table 1 aggregates results from:

- **ProRL-1.5B-v1 and v2**: Based on DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B, using GRPO enhanced with decoupled clipping, dynamic sampling, KL regularization, and periodic reference resets
- **Nemotron-1-7B and Nemotron-1-14B**: Based on DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B and 14B respectively
- **Skywork-OR1-7B**: Based on DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B
- **Phi4-Reason-Plus-14B**: Based on Phi4-Reason-14B
- **AceReason-Nemotron-7B and 14B**: Additional RLVR variants for cross-validation
- **Kangheng-OVR-7B**: A vision-language model to test multimodal settings
- **OLMo-2-0425-1B**: Additional small-scale coverage
- **DAPO-32B**: For entropy analysis comparison

This breadth is critical: if the "invisible leash" pattern appears consistently across all these configurations, it suggests a structural property of RLVR rather than an implementation artifact.

**Benchmark Coverage.** The paper evaluates across two broad categories to test domain-generality:

| Category | Benchmarks | Purpose |
|----------|------------|---------|
| **Math Reasoning** | MATH500, Minerva, OlympiadBench, AIME 2024, AIME 2025, AMC 2023 | Test domains with clear verifiable rewards and single correct answers |
| **Non-Math Reasoning** | SimpleQA (factuality), LiveBench (logic, coding, language), SciBench (scientific QA), Reasoning Gym (cognition, geometry, graph theory, games), LiveCodeBench (code generation) | Test whether patterns hold beyond mathematics |

For vision-language evaluation, MathVision and MathVista testmini sets are used (Appendix B.2).

**Sampling Budgets.** A critical design choice is the sampling budget `$k$` used to estimate empirical support. The paper uses different budgets for different domains to ensure statistical power while maintaining computational feasibility:

- **Math tasks**: `$k \in \{4096, 8192\}$`
- **Reasoning Gym**: `$k \in \{1024, 2048, 4096, 8192, 16384\}$` (wider range due to varying task difficulty)
- **Other non-math**: `$k \in \{1024, 2048\}$`

These budgets are chosen to ensure that any unreachable solution `$y^* \in \mathcal{C}$` remains below the empirical support threshold `$\epsilon$` of the base model. Appendix C.4 derives that with `$k = 8192$` and 95% confidence, `$\epsilon \approx 3.66 \times 10^{-4}$`. This means completions with probability below this threshold are statistically unlikely to appear in samples, justifying their classification as "out of support."

**Inference Configuration.** All models use consistent sampling parameters during evaluation (Appendix B.1):
- Temperature: `0.6`
- Top_p: `0.95`
- Maximum response length: `32768` tokens
- Inference backend: vLLM for efficient batched generation

These settings balance response diversity and quality while ensuring completions are long enough to contain full reasoning traces.

**Answer Processing Enhancements.** A notable methodological contribution is the enhanced answer extraction protocol for Reasoning Gym tasks (Appendix B.3). The paper identifies that base models struggle with format adherence compared to RLVR models trained on Reasoning Gym data. To ensure fair evaluation, a hierarchical extraction mechanism is implemented:

1. First attempt: Reasoning Gym's `extract_answer()` function for `<answer></answer>` tags
2. Second attempt: Search for `$\boxed{}$` formatting
3. Third attempt: Lighteval's math normalizer for fraction conversion (e.g., `a/b` to `$\frac{a}{b}$`)
4. Final fallback: Extract content following `</think>` tags

Task-specific modifications address format incompatibilities:
- **Dice tasks**: Convert ground truth fractions from `a/b` to LaTeX `$\frac{a}{b}$` for base model compatibility
- **Prime factorization**: Standardize multiplication symbols (`$\times$` to `×`), expand LaTeX exponentiation, remove equation restatements
- **Palindrome generation**: Remove excess whitespace to prevent string reversibility judgment failures
- **Advanced geometry**: Convert LaTeX expressions to three-decimal floating-point numbers
- **Boxnet**: Clean JSON format, convert single dictionaries to dictionary lists, filter illegal elements

These enhancements are crucial for valid comparison — without them, apparent support shrinkage could be attributed to format mismatches rather than genuine probability mass redistribution.

4. **SDS is uniformly low** (≤0.07): The harmonic mean of SRR and NDR reveals poor balance — high retention cannot compensate for near-zero discovery.

5. **Pattern holds across scales**: 1.5B, 7B, and 14B models all exhibit similar dynamics, suggesting this is not a scale-dependent artifact.

6. **Pattern holds across domains**: Math benchmarks show particularly stagnant NDR (≤0.01), while non-math domains fare only marginally better (NDR ≤ 0.04).

**Per-Benchmark Detail (Appendix A, Tables 6–12).** The aggregate patterns mask interesting per-benchmark variations. Table 6 (ProRL-1.5B-v1) reveals:

- **AIME2024**: `pass@8192` drops from 93.3% (base) to 83.3% (RLVR), with SRR = 0.893 and NSCR = −0.107. This is a concrete example of the paradox: RLVR improves `pass@1` but loses coverage at high sampling budgets.
- **Minerva**: `pass@8192` drops from 71.7% to 63.6%, with 22 correct completions lost to shrinkage and 0 gained through expansion.
- **SimpleQA**: SRR = 0.743 (lowest in the table), NSCR = −0.221, indicating substantial support contraction for factual QA.
- **LiveBench-L**: NDR = 0.167 (highest in the table), but this comes with SRR = 0.769 and NSCR = −0.067, showing expansion does not compensate for shrinkage.

**Pass@k Curves (Figures 2–4).** The paper visualizes support dynamics through `pass@k` curves across sampling budgets:

- **Figure 2 (Support Preservation)**: Tasks like Graph Coloring, Palindrome Generation, and Advanced Geometry show RLVR accelerating convergence toward near-perfect `pass@k` with large budgets, but base models eventually match or exceed RLVR performance at very high `$k$`.

- **Figure 3 (Support Expansion)**: Rare cases like Boxnet, Dice, and Arc 1D show genuine expansion where RLVR's `pass@k` curve exceeds the base model's even at high budgets. However, these are isolated exceptions — Section 4.3 notes these tasks exhibit modular structure where the base model already possesses subskills but fails to compose them correctly.

- **Figure 4 (Support Shrinkage)**: Tasks like Leg Counting, Family Relationships, and Power Function illustrate vivid shrinkage where base models maintain broader solution coverage at high `$k$` while RLVR models plateau earlier. For Power Function, the base model reaches `pass@1024 = 1.000` while ProRL plateaus at `0.901`.

**Training Dynamics (Table 4).** Unlike static before/after comparisons, Table 4 tracks support metrics throughout RLVR training on DeepSeek 1.5B across math benchmarks. Key observations:

- **SRR remains consistently high** throughout training (typically 0.88–1.000), confirming RLVR reliably preserves previously correct reasoning paths.
- **NSCR gradually decreases** over training steps, indicating steady contraction of accessible solutions. For AIME24, NSCR goes from 0.000 at step 0 to −0.120 at step 300.
- **NDR shows mild fluctuations** on harder datasets but generally trends toward zero. For Olympiad, NDR drops from 0.022 at step 30 to 0.009 at step 300.
- **Pass@256 shows non-monotonic behavior**: On AIME25, `pass@256` peaks at 73.33% (steps 60, 120, 150) then drops to 63.33% (step 180) before recovering slightly. This suggests RLVR reshapes rather than monotonically improves the reasoning distribution.

This temporal perspective is critical: support contraction is not a training artifact but an inherent consequence of the RLVR objective that accumulates over time.

### 5.3 Entropy Analysis: Mechanism Behind Support Shrinkage

Section 5 investigates the mechanism behind support shrinkage through entropy measurements. The key finding is the **decoupling between token-level and answer-level entropy**.

**Entropy Metrics (Table 5).** The paper measures entropy at `$k = 32$` sampling budget across math benchmarks:

| Model | Avg Token Entropy | Avg Answer Entropy | Avg Response Length |
|-------|------------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **DeepSeek-1.5B** (Base) | 0.44 | 1.30 | 10,422 |
| **ProRL-1.5B** (RLVR) | 0.52 ▲ | 0.66 ▼ | 6,479 |
| **DeepSeek-7B** (Base) | 0.37 | 0.75 | 7,745 |
| **AceReason-7B** (RLVR) | 0.23 ▼ | 0.50 ▼ | 6,995 |
| **Skywork-OR1-7B** (RLVR) | 0.16 ▼ | 0.54 ▼ | 10,096 |
| **DeepSeek-14B** (Base) | 0.33 | 0.54 | 6,755 |

Several patterns emerge:

1. **Token-level entropy varies**: ProRL-1.5B and DAPO-32B show **increased** token-level entropy (0.44→0.52 and 0.18→0.29 respectively), while AceReason and Skywork show **decreased** token-level entropy. This variability suggests token-level entropy is not a reliable indicator of RLVR's effect.

2. **Answer-level entropy consistently decreases**: Every RLVR model shows lower answer-level entropy than its base counterpart. ProRL-1.5B drops from 1.30 to 0.66 (49% reduction), AceReason-7B from 0.75 to 0.50 (33% reduction). This confirms RLVR converges to fewer distinct final answers.

3. **Response length generally decreases**: ProRL-1.5B reduces average response length from 10,422 to 6,479 tokens (38% reduction), suggesting RLVR produces more concise reasoning traces. However, Skywork-OR1-7B increases length (7,745→10,096), indicating this is not universal.

4. **Decoupling is real**: Skywork-OR1-7B has lower token-level entropy (0.16) than AceReason-7B (0.23) but higher answer-level entropy (0.54 vs. 0.50). This confirms the measures capture distinct phenomena.

**Interpretation.** The paper terms this "local stochasticity without global exploration": RLVR models may exhibit variability in token selection (higher token-level entropy) but ultimately collapse to a narrow set of answers (lower answer-level entropy). This explains why monitoring token-level entropy alone is insufficient for assessing exploratory behavior — stepwise uncertainty does not guarantee diversity in final solutions.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment: Do Experiments Support the Claims?

The experimental evidence strongly supports the paper's core claims, but several limitations warrant acknowledgment.

**Strengths of the Experimental Design:**

1. **Multi-scale, multi-domain validation**: The pattern holds across 1.5B–14B parameters, multiple model families, and diverse domains (math, logic, factual QA, code, vision). This breadth makes it difficult to attribute findings to specific implementation choices.

2. **Controlled SFT vs. RLVR comparison**: By holding all variables constant except the training objective, Table 3 isolates the RLVR objective as the source of support shrinkage. This is a rigorous ablation that strengthens causal claims.

3. **Temporal dynamics tracking**: Table 4 shows support contraction accumulates over training steps, ruling out the hypothesis that shrinkage is a transient training artifact.

4. **Dual entropy measurement**: The token-level vs. answer-level distinction reveals a measurement artifact in prior work — researchers monitoring only token-level entropy might incorrectly conclude RLVR increases exploration.

5. **Answer processing enhancements**: Appendix B.3's detailed format compatibility work addresses a potential confound: without these enhancements, apparent shrinkage could be attributed to format mismatches rather than genuine probability redistribution.

**Limitations and Open Questions:**

1. **Threshold `$\epsilon$` sensitivity**: The empirical support definition depends on threshold `$\epsilon$`, which is derived from sampling confidence bounds (Appendix C.4). While principled, different threshold choices could affect categorization counts. The paper does not report sensitivity analysis across `$\epsilon$` values.

2. **Finite sampling constraints**: Even with `$k = 8192$` samples, extremely low-probability completions may remain undetected. The theoretical upper bound (Corollary C.2) applies to infinite `$k$`, but practical evaluation is necessarily finite.

3. **Task selection bias**: Reasoning Gym tasks where expansion occurs (Dice, Arc 1D, Boxnet) are noted to have modular structure where the base model already possesses subskills. The paper does not systematically characterize which task properties predict expansion vs. shrinkage.

4. **KL regularization strength**: The `$\beta$` hyperparameter controlling KL regularization strength varies across RLVR implementations. The paper does not ablate `$\beta$` to test whether weaker regularization enables more expansion (at the cost of stability).

5. **Longer training horizons**: Table 4 tracks up to 300 training steps. It remains unclear whether support dynamics stabilize or continue contracting with extended training.

6. **Alternative exploration mechanisms**: The paper recommends explicit exploration strategies but does not evaluate any. This leaves open whether the "invisible leash" can be broken in practice.

**Robustness Checks Present:**

- **Multiple RLVR variants**: ProRL, Nemotron, Skywork, Phi4-Reason, AceReason, DAPO all show consistent patterns
- **Vision-language inclusion**: Kangheng-OVR-7B confirms the pattern transcends text-only modalities
- **Per-benchmark detail**: Appendix A Tables 6–12 provide granular statistics enabling independent verification
- **Training dynamics**: Table 4 shows patterns persist throughout training, not just at endpoints

**Verdict:** The experiments convincingly support the claim that current RLVR practices act as precision enhancers rather than capability expanders. The consistency across models, domains, and training stages, combined with the controlled SFT comparison and theoretical proofs, makes a compelling case that support-bounded behavior is structural to RLVR. However, the paper appropriately frames this as a characterization of **current** RLVR practices — future methods with explicit exploration mechanisms may escape the "invisible leash."

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this study provides a rigorous diagnostic framework for RLVR, both the methodology and the underlying RLVR phenomenon exhibit inherent constraints. Understanding these limitations is crucial for interpreting the results correctly and for designing future systems that might overcome the "invisible leash." Below, I break down the methodological dependencies, domain boundaries, inherent trade-offs, and unresolved questions.

### 6.1 Methodological Dependencies: The Role of Sampling Budgets

The empirical support framework relies fundamentally on the assumption that finite sampling budgets are sufficient to detect whether a solution is "accessible." This introduces a dependency on the choice of threshold $\epsilon$ and sampling count $k$.

*   **Threshold Sensitivity:** The definition of empirical support uses a threshold $\epsilon$ to separate observable solutions from statistically negligible ones (Section 3.1). Appendix C.4 derives a principled estimate for $\epsilon$ based on confidence bounds (e.g., for $k=8192$ and 95% confidence, $\epsilon \approx 3.66 \times 10^{-4}$). However, the paper does not provide a sensitivity analysis showing how support dynamics metrics (SRR, NDR, NSCR) shift if $\epsilon$ is varied. In practice, this means the reported "shrinkage" might be partially artifacts of the chosen budget. A solution with probability $3.0 \times 10^{-4}$ is counted as "lost" under $k=8192$ but might be "preserved" under $k=16384$.
*   **Finite Sampling Ceiling:** Corollary C.2 establishes an asymptotic upper bound on `pass@k` as $k \to \infty$, but practical evaluation is necessarily finite. Section 4.1 uses budgets up to $k=16384$ for Reasoning Gym but only $k \in \{1024, 2048\}$ for some non-math datasets. This discrepancy means support shrinkage might be overestimated in domains with lower sampling budgets, as rare correct solutions are less likely to be detected in the base model let alone the RLVR model.
*   **Independence Assumption:** The theoretical bounds in Appendix C assume sampling independence across draws. In reality, autoregressive generation might exhibit correlations between samples (e.g., getting stuck in similar reasoning loops) that violate this assumption, potentially making the theoretical ceiling tighter or looser than predicted.

### 6.2 Domain Boundaries: The Verifiability Requirement

The study's scope is constrained by the requirement for **verifiable rewards**, which limits the generalizability of findings to domains with clear ground truth.

*   **Verifiable Rewards Only:** The RLVR objective defined in Section 3.1 relies on a reward function $R(x, y) \in \{0, 1\}$ that is automatically computable. This works well for mathematics (exact answer matching), code (unit tests), and factual QA (string matching). However, it excludes open-ended domains like creative writing, dialogue generation, or complex strategic planning where rewards are subjective or sparse. Section C.3 acknowledges that entropy reduction might be desirable in precision tasks but harmful in open-ended ones, yet the empirical validation is restricted to the former.
*   **Format Sensitivity:** The results are sensitive to answer formatting. Appendix B.3 details extensive engineering required to normalize outputs (e.g., converting LaTeX fractions, handling JSON structures in Boxnet). While necessary for fair evaluation, this introduces a confound: some observed "support shrinkage" might stem from the RLVR model adhering to a specific format while the base model produces semantically correct but format-incompatible outputs. The paper mitigates this via hierarchical extraction, but residual format bias may remain.
*   **Task Structure Bias:** Section 4.3 notes that expansion occurs primarily in tasks with modular structure (e.g., Graph Coloring, Arc 1D) where the base model possesses subskills but fails to compose them. This suggests the "invisible leash" might be looser for compositional tasks and tighter for monolithic reasoning tasks, but the paper does not systematically categorize benchmarks by structural properties to quantify this effect.

### 6.3 Inherent Trade-offs: Precision vs. Diversity

The paper identifies several trade-offs that appear intrinsic to the current RLVR objective, implying that improving one metric may inherently degrade another.

*   **The Precision-Diversity Ceiling:** Theorem C.6 proves that any RLVR update satisfies $H[\pi_\theta] \leq H[q]$, meaning entropy reduction is a mathematical consequence of reward optimization, not a bug. This creates a hard trade-off: improving `pass@1` (precision) inherently narrows the solution space (diversity). Practitioners cannot expect to gain precision without paying a diversity tax unless the objective function itself is modified.
*   **Token-Level vs. Answer-Level Entropy:** Section 5.2 reveals that token-level entropy is a misleading proxy for exploration. Models like ProRL-1.5B increase token-level entropy (0.44 $\to$ 0.52) while decreasing answer-level entropy (1.30 $\to$ 0.66). This implies that monitoring standard entropy metrics during training could lead to false conclusions about exploratory behavior. The trade-off here is interpretability: token-level metrics are cheap to compute but unreliable, while answer-level metrics require expensive sampling and extraction.
*   **SFT vs. RLVR Objectives:** Table 3 shows that SFT can achieve positive Net Support Change Rate (NSCR) where RLVR achieves negative NSCR (e.g., Minerva NSCR = 0.050 for SFT vs. -0.120 for DAPO). This suggests a trade-off between optimization efficiency and support coverage. RLVR converges faster on high-reward modes (better `pass@1`), but SFT preserves broader coverage (better `pass@k` at high $k$). Choosing between them depends on whether the deployment scenario prioritizes single-shot accuracy or robustness across multiple samples.

### 6.4 Computational and Diagnostic Costs

Applying the empirical support framework itself incurs significant costs that may limit its adoption for routine model evaluation.

*   **Sampling Budget Requirements:** To reliably estimate support dynamics, the paper uses sampling budgets of $k \in \{4096, 8192\}$ for math tasks (Section 4.1). For a single benchmark like AIME2024 with 30 problems, this requires generating $\approx 245,760$ completions per model. For larger models (14B–32B), this represents substantial GPU-hours, making routine support diagnostics expensive compared to standard `pass@1` evaluation.
*   **Answer Processing Overhead:** Appendix B.3 describes a complex pipeline for answer extraction involving multiple fallback levels (tags, boxed, normalizers) and task-specific logic (JSON cleaning, LaTeX conversion). This engineering overhead is non-trivial and must be replicated for each new domain to ensure fair comparison, potentially limiting the framework's scalability to new benchmarks.
*   **Perplexity Evaluation Costs:** Table 2 uses external reasoning traces (DeepSeek-R1, Claude Sonnet 4) to compute perplexity. This requires accessing proprietary models or large external checkpoints, adding cost and complexity to the analysis pipeline.

### 6.5 Unresolved Questions and Future Directions

While the paper diagnoses the "invisible leash," it does not provide a complete solution, leaving several critical questions open for future research.

*   **Breaking the Leash:** Section 6 concludes that breaking the leash requires "explicit exploration strategies or off-policy mechanisms," but the paper does not evaluate any such methods. It remains an open question whether techniques like entropy regularization, curiosity-driven bonuses, or off-policy replay can practically expand support without destabilizing training.
*   **KL Regularization Strength:** The RLVR objective includes a KL penalty controlled by $\beta$ (Section 3.1). The paper does not ablate $\beta$ to determine if weaker regularization allows more support expansion at the cost of stability. It is possible that the "invisible leash" is partly a function of strong KL constraints rather than the RL algorithm itself.
*   **Long-Term Dynamics:** Table 4 tracks support metrics for up to 300 training steps. It is unclear whether support contraction continues indefinitely, stabilizes, or reverses with extended training. Some fluctuations in NDR and SDS suggest transient exploration (Section 4.2), but the long-term trajectory remains unknown.
*   **Generalization to Larger Scales:** The study covers models up to 32B parameters (DAPO-32B). While patterns are consistent across 1.5B–14B, it is possible that at much larger scales (e.g., 100B+), the base model's support is so vast that shrinkage becomes less consequential, or conversely, that optimization becomes even more aggressive. The scaling laws of support dynamics remain unexplored.

In summary, the "invisible leash" is a robust phenomenon under current RLVR practices, but its severity depends on sampling budgets, domain verifiability, and hyperparameter choices. The trade-off between precision and diversity is theoretically grounded, suggesting that future progress requires algorithmic innovations beyond standard on-policy RLVR.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper's findings fundamentally reframe how we understand RLVR's role in capability development. The "invisible leash" is not a temporary limitation awaiting better hyperparameters — it is a structural constraint arising from the RLVR objective itself. Below, I outline how this work changes the research landscape, what follow-up directions it enables, and practical guidance for practitioners.

### 7.1 Reshaping the RLVR Research Landscape

**From "Does RLVR Work?" to "How Can RLVR Escape Its Initialization?"**

Prior to this work, the dominant question in the RLVR literature was whether RLVR improves performance on reasoning benchmarks. The answer was clearly yes — models like DeepSeek-R1 and OpenAI-o1 demonstrated dramatic gains. This paper shifts the question to a more nuanced level: **what kind of improvement is occurring?**

The empirical support framework reveals that RLVR acts as a **precision enhancer, not a capability expander**. Table 1 shows Support Retention Rate (SRR) ≈ 0.93–0.99 but Net Discovery Rate (NDR) ≤ 0.04 across all models. This means RLVR reliably preserves known solutions but rarely discovers new ones. The research community must now distinguish between:

- **Exploitation gains**: Improving `pass@1` by concentrating mass on known high-reward solutions
- **Exploration gains**: Discovering reasoning patterns absent from the base model's support

This distinction matters for interpreting performance claims. A model achieving 90% `pass@1` through support concentration represents a different achievement than one reaching 90% through genuine capability expansion.

**Theoretical Grounding Changes How We Interpret Empirical Patterns**

Before this work, support limitation was an empirical observation. Theorem C.1 (Appendix C) proves it as a mathematical consequence of on-policy gradient updates:

$$
\text{supp}(\pi_\theta(\cdot|x)) \subseteq \text{supp}(q(\cdot|x))
$$

This elevates the claim from correlation to causation. Researchers can no longer attribute support shrinkage to specific implementations (ProRL vs. Nemotron vs. DAPO) — it is inherent to the RLVR objective structure. Corollary C.2 establishes an asymptotic ceiling:

$$
\limsup_{k \to \infty} \text{pass@k}_{\pi_\theta}(x) \leq \limsup_{k \to \infty} \text{pass@k}_q(x)
$$

This means no amount of training duration or compute budget allows RLVR to exceed the base model's coverage at high sampling budgets. Figure 4 illustrates this concretely: on Power Function, the base model reaches `pass@1024 = 1.000` while ProRL plateaus at `0.901`.

**Entropy Measurement Requires Dual Metrics**

Section 5.2 reveals that token-level entropy is a misleading proxy for exploration. ProRL-1.5B increases token-level entropy from 0.44 to 0.52 while decreasing answer-level entropy from 1.30 to 0.66 (Table 5). This "local stochasticity without global exploration" invalidates prior entropy analyses that monitored only token-level metrics.

Future RLVR research must adopt dual entropy measurement:
- **Token-level entropy**: Captures local uncertainty during generation
- **Answer-level entropy**: Captures global diversity of final solutions

Monitoring only token-level entropy could lead researchers to incorrectly conclude a model is exploring more diverse reasoning paths when it is actually converging to fewer distinct answers.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper's diagnosis points toward several concrete research directions for breaking the "invisible leash."

**1. Explicit Exploration Mechanisms**

Section 6 concludes that breaking the leash requires "explicit exploration strategies or off-policy mechanisms that allocate probability mass to underrepresented regions." Specific approaches include:

- **Entropy regularization bonuses**: Add explicit diversity penalties to the RLVR objective beyond KL regularization. The current objective (Section 3.1) is:

$$
\max_\theta \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x), x \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ R(x, y) - \beta^{-1} \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y|x)}{q(y|x)} \right]
$$

Future work could add answer-level entropy terms:

$$
\max_\theta \mathbb{E}[\cdots] + \lambda \cdot H[\text{AnswerEntropy}]
$$

- **Curiosity-driven bonuses**: Reward the model for generating solutions that differ from previously discovered modes, encouraging exploration of underrepresented solution regions.

- **Off-policy replay buffers**: Store diverse correct completions from the base model and periodically replay them during RLVR training to prevent support collapse.

**2. Hybrid Training Objectives**

Table 3 shows SFT achieves positive Net Support Change Rate (NSCR) where RLVR achieves negative NSCR (Minerva: 0.050 for SFT vs. −0.120 for DAPO). This suggests hybrid approaches:

- **SFT-first, RLVR-second**: Use SFT to expand support, then RLVR to sharpen precision on the expanded set.
- **Interleaved training**: Alternate between SFT phases (expansion) and RLVR phases (concentration) throughout training.
- **Multi-objective optimization**: Simultaneously optimize for reward maximization and support preservation, treating NSCR as a secondary objective.

**3. KL Regularization Strength Ablation**

The paper does not ablate the $\beta$ hyperparameter controlling KL regularization strength. Section C.2 shows the optimal policy takes the form:

$$
\pi_\theta(y|x) \propto q(y|x) \cdot \exp(\beta R(x, y))
$$

Larger $\beta$ allows more deviation from the base distribution. Future work should test whether weaker KL constraints enable more support expansion at the cost of training stability. The "invisible leash" may be partly a function of strong KL constraints rather than the RL algorithm itself.

**4. Task Structure Characterization**

Section 4.3 notes expansion occurs primarily in tasks with modular structure (Dice, Arc 1D, Boxnet) where the base model possesses subskills but fails to compose them. Future work should:

- Systematically categorize benchmarks by structural properties (compositional vs. monolithic)
- Identify which task properties predict expansion vs. shrinkage
- Develop task-adaptive RLVR strategies that apply stronger exploration bonuses to monolithic tasks

**5. Long-Term Training Dynamics**

Table 4 tracks support metrics for up to 300 training steps. It remains unclear whether support contraction continues indefinitely, stabilizes, or reverses with extended training. Future work should:

- Track support dynamics over 1000+ training steps
- Identify whether NDR fluctuations represent transient exploration or permanent gains
- Determine if there exists a "sweet spot" in training duration before support collapse becomes severe

**6. Scaling Laws of Support Dynamics**

The study covers models up to 32B parameters (DAPO-32B). While patterns are consistent across 1.5B–14B, scaling laws remain unexplored:

- Does support shrinkage become more or less severe at 100B+ scales?
- Do larger base models have sufficiently vast support that shrinkage becomes less consequential?
- How does the SRR/NDR trade-off scale with parameter count?

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Deployment Guidance

**When to Use RLVR vs. SFT**

Table 3 provides actionable guidance for practitioners choosing between training objectives:

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| **Single-shot accuracy critical** (e.g., production APIs with low latency budgets) | RLVR | Improves `pass@1` by concentrating mass on known high-reward solutions |
| **Robustness across multiple samples** (e.g., systems with retry mechanisms) | SFT or Hybrid | Preserves broader support, better `pass@k` at high $k$ |
| **Open-ended domains** (creative writing, dialogue, brainstorming) | Avoid standard RLVR | Entropy reduction harmful where diversity is valuable (Section C.3) |
| **Precision-critical domains** (math proofs, code with unit tests) | RLVR acceptable | Lower answer-level entropy reflects desirable convergence to correct solutions |
| **Edge case coverage important** (medical diagnosis, safety-critical systems) | SFT-first, RLVR-second | Expand support via SFT, then sharpen precision without losing rare correct solutions |

**Monitoring Support Dynamics During Training**

Practitioners implementing RLVR should adopt the empirical support framework for routine diagnostics:

1. **Sample at multiple budgets**: Evaluate at $k \in \{1, 32, 256, 8192\}$ to detect support shrinkage early
2. **Track both entropy metrics**: Monitor token-level AND answer-level entropy to detect "local stochasticity without global exploration"
3. **Compute NSCR periodically**: Net Support Change Rate provides a single metric capturing expansion vs. shrinkage balance
4. **Compare against base model**: Always retain base model samples for comparison — support dynamics are relative, not absolute

**Cost-Benefit Considerations**

Applying the empirical support framework incurs significant costs (Section 6.4):

- **Sampling budget**: $k = 8192$ for math tasks requires $\approx 245,760$ completions per benchmark (30 problems × 8192 samples)
- **Answer processing**: Hierarchical extraction with multiple fallback levels (Appendix B.3) requires non-trivial engineering per domain
- **Perplexity evaluation**: External reasoning traces (DeepSeek-R1, Claude Sonnet 4) add cost and complexity

For routine evaluation, practitioners may use reduced budgets ($k \in \{256, 1024\}$) with the understanding that extremely low-probability completions may remain undetected. The threshold $\epsilon$ scales with $k$ (Appendix C.4):

$$
\epsilon \approx \frac{-\log \zeta}{k}
$$

For $k = 1024$ and 95% confidence, $\epsilon \approx 2.93 \times 10^{-3}$ vs. $\epsilon \approx 3.66 \times 10^{-4}$ for $k = 8192$.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

**Reproducing Support Dynamics Analysis**

Researchers wishing to apply the empirical support framework should follow these steps:

1. **Define threshold $\epsilon$**: Use Appendix C.4's principled estimate based on sampling budget $k$ and confidence level $\zeta$. For $k = 4096$ and $\zeta = 0.05$:

$$
\epsilon \leq \frac{-\log(0.05)}{4096} \approx 7.31 \times 10^{-4}
$$

2. **Categorize completions**: For each correct completion $y^* \in \mathcal{C}$, determine:
   - **Preservation**: $q(y^*|x) > \epsilon$ AND $\pi_\theta(y^*|x) > \epsilon$
   - **Shrinkage**: $q(y^*|x) > \epsilon$ BUT $\pi_\theta(y^*|x) \leq \epsilon$
   - **Expansion**: $q(y^*|x) \leq \epsilon$ BUT $\pi_\theta(y^*|x) > \epsilon$
   - **Out of Support**: Both $\leq \epsilon$

3. **Compute metrics**: Calculate SRR, NDR, SDS, and NSCR using formulas from Section 3.2:

$$
\text{SRR} = \frac{P}{P + S}, \quad \text{NDR} = \frac{E}{P + E}, \quad \text{NSCR} = \frac{E - S}{P + E + S}
$$

4. **Track training dynamics**: Evaluate at multiple training checkpoints (not just endpoints) to capture temporal evolution of support.

**Integration with Existing RLVR Pipelines**

The empirical support framework can be integrated into existing RLVR training pipelines with minimal modification:

- **During training**: Periodically sample from both base and current policy to compute support metrics
- **As early stopping criterion**: Halt training when NSCR drops below a threshold (e.g., −0.10) indicating severe support collapse
- **For hyperparameter tuning**: Use NSCR alongside `pass@1` to select $\beta$ values that balance precision and diversity
- **For model selection**: Prefer models with higher SDS (Support Dynamic Score) when deployment requires robustness across multiple samples

**Limitations to Acknowledge in Practice**

When applying this framework, practitioners should acknowledge:

- **Threshold sensitivity**: Different $\epsilon$ choices affect categorization counts. Report $\epsilon$ values alongside metrics.
- **Domain specificity**: Results apply to verifiable reward domains (math, code, factual QA). Open-ended domains require adapted metrics.
- **Format compatibility**: Invest in answer processing pipelines (Appendix B.3) to avoid conflating format mismatches with genuine support shrinkage.
- **Finite sampling**: Even $k = 8192$ may miss extremely low-probability completions. Interpret "out of support" as "statistically undetectable" rather than "impossible."

### 7.5 Summary: A New Lens for RLVR Evaluation

This paper provides the field with a diagnostic framework that distinguishes between precision gains and capability expansion. The key takeaway for researchers and practitioners:

> **RLVR improves sampling efficiency within existing support but cannot discover solutions outside the base model's effective reach without explicit exploration mechanisms.**

Breaking the "invisible leash" requires algorithmic innovations beyond standard on-policy RLVR. Until such methods are developed, practitioners should:

1. **Set realistic expectations**: RLVR sharpens precision, does not expand reasoning horizons
2. **Monitor support dynamics**: Track SRR, NDR, and NSCR alongside `pass@k` metrics
3. **Consider hybrid approaches**: Combine SFT (expansion) with RLVR (concentration) for balanced outcomes
4. **Match method to deployment**: Use RLVR for single-shot accuracy, SFT for robustness across samples

The empirical support framework transforms how we evaluate reasoning models — from asking "how accurate is this model?" to "what solutions can this model access, and how did training change that accessibility?" This shift enables more informed decisions about training objectives, deployment strategies, and future research directions.
