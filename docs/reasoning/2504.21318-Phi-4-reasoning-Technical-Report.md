This paper introduces **Phi-4-reasoning** and **Phi-4-reasoning-plus**, a family of compact **14-billion parameter** models that achieve reasoning performance competitive with significantly larger state-of-the-art systems like **DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-70B** and **o1-mini** on complex benchmarks such as **AIME 2025** and **GPQA**. By combining supervised fine-tuning on **1.4 million** high-quality "teachable" prompts generated by **o3-mini** with a targeted phase of outcome-based reinforcement learning (**GRPO**) on **~6,000** math problems, the authors demonstrate that meticulous data curation and inference-time scaling can allow small models to master multi-step decomposition without requiring massive parameter counts. This work matters because it establishes a data-centric pathway for developing efficient, open-weight reasoning models that leverage extended chain-of-thought traces (up to **32K tokens**) to solve NP-hard problems, coding tasks, and scientific queries with accuracy previously reserved for much larger architectures.

**Reinforcement Learning (RL): GRPO and Reward Design**
Following SFT, the `Phi-4-reasoning-plus` variant is created using a short phase of outcome-based Reinforcement Learning to further boost performance, specifically on mathematical tasks.
*   **Algorithm**: The authors utilize Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), an algorithm that estimates advantages by comparing the rewards of a group of sampled responses rather than relying on a separate critic model.
*   **Training Scale**: The RL phase is remarkably data-efficient, training for only **90 steps** on a subsample of **64 problems per iteration** from a seed set of roughly **72,000** math problems, totaling only about **6,000** unique examples used for updates.
*   **Reward Function Design**: To avoid reward hacking, a rule-based reward function $R_{final}$ is designed to balance correctness, length, and formatting:
    $$ R_{final} = w_{acc} R_{acc\_scaled} + w_{rep} R_{rep} $$
    where $w_{acc} = 8/13$ and $w_{rep} = 1/13$.

This incentivizes the model to "think longer" (increase $L$) when it is struggling, as longer incorrect traces receive a less negative reward than short, confident errors.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

The evaluation of `Phi-4-reasoning` and `Phi-4-reasoning-plus` is designed not just to report accuracy scores, but to rigorously stress-test the models' ability to generalize reasoning skills to unseen domains and to expose the statistical fragility inherent in evaluating complex reasoning tasks. The authors move beyond single-run reporting, acknowledging that high-temperature sampling (necessary for reasoning exploration) introduces significant variance.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

**Datasets and Domains**
The evaluation suite is divided into two primary categories: **Reasoning-Specific** and **General-Purpose**.

*   **Reasoning Benchmarks:** The core assessment relies on challenging, often unsaturated benchmarks.
    *   **Mathematics:** `AIME 2025` (30 problems, contamination-free as it was released after training data finalization), `AIME 1983–2024` (949 problems), `HMMT Feb 2025`, and `Omni-MATH` (4,428 olympiad-level problems).
    *   **Science:** `GPQA Diamond` (198 graduate-level questions in biology, physics, and chemistry).
    *   **Coding:** `LiveCodeBench` (August 2024 – January 2025) and `Codeforces` (Elo rating based on 143 problems).
    *   **Algorithmic & Planning (Out-of-Domain):** To test generalization, the authors introduce tasks *not* explicitly targeted during training: `3SAT` (800 instances), `TSP` (960 instances), `BA-Calendar` (2,000 scheduling problems), `Maze` (1,500 spatial paths), and `SpatialMap` (1,500 relational queries).
*   **General-Purpose Benchmarks:** These ensure reasoning training does not degrade standard capabilities. They include `MMLU-Pro`, `IFEval` (instruction following), `FlenQA` (long-context reasoning), `ArenaHard` (human preference), `HumanEvalPlus`, `Kitab` (constrained retrieval), and `Toxigen` (safety).

**Baselines and Experimental Protocol**
The models are compared against a wide spectrum of competitors:
*   **Proprietary Frontier Models:** `o1`, `o3-mini` (high effort), `Claude-3.7-Sonnet` (thinking enabled), and `Gemini-2.5-Pro`.
*   **Open-Weight Reasoning Models:** `DeepSeek-R1` (671B MoE), `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-70B`, `QwQ-32B`, and `OpenThinker2-32B`.
*   **Base Model:** `Phi-4` (14B).

**Critical Statistical Rigor:**
A defining feature of this evaluation is the handling of **non-determinism**. Recognizing that reasoning models are typically sampled at high temperatures (0.6–0.8), the authors report results averaged over **50 independent runs** for `AIME 2025` and **64 runs** for `HMMT`, rather than the standard single run or average of 3–5. For other benchmarks, they use 5 runs. They explicitly report standard deviations to highlight the variance, noting that for `AIME 2025`, two separate runs of "average-of-5" can differ by **5–10 percentage points**, making single-run comparisons unreliable (Section 5.1.2).

### 5.2 Quantitative Results: Reasoning Capabilities

The results demonstrate that `Phi-4-reasoning` and its RL-enhanced variant `Phi-4-reasoning-plus` achieve performance competitive with models 5–50 times their size.

**Mathematical and Scientific Reasoning**
As shown in **Table 1** and **Figure 1**, the models exhibit massive gains over the base `Phi-4`:
*   **AIME 2025:** `Phi-4` scores a mere **12.9%**. `Phi-4-reasoning` jumps to **63.1%**, and `Phi-4-reasoning-plus` reaches **78.0%**. This surpasses `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-70B` (51.5%) and `o1-mini` (54.8%), approaching the full `DeepSeek-R1` (70.4%) and `o1` (71.4%).
*   **Omni-MATH:** The models improve from **31.9%** (Phi-4) to **76.6%** and **81.9%** respectively, outperforming `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-70B` (63.4%) and `o1-mini` (60.5%).
*   **GPQA Diamond:** Scores improve from **54.7%** to **67.1%** and **69.3%**, remaining competitive with `DeepSeek-R1` (73.0%) and `o1` (76.7%).

**Generalization to Unseen Domains (Algorithmic & Planning)**
Perhaps the most striking evidence of acquired "reasoning as a meta-skill" is the performance on tasks excluded from the RL training set (which was math-only). **Figure 8** details these gains:
*   **Calendar Planning (BA-Calendar):** Accuracy surges from **16.0%** (Phi-4) to **67.7%** (`Phi-4-reasoning`) and **65.6%** (`Phi-4-reasoning-plus`). This exceeds `DeepSeek-R1` (66.8%) and `o1` (62.0%).
*   **NP-Hard Problems:** On `3SAT`, accuracy rises from **17.1%** to **78.0%**. On `TSP` (Traveling Salesman Problem), it improves from **8.2%** to **37.5%** (`Phi-4-reasoning`) and **42.6%** (`Phi-4-reasoning-plus`).
*   **Spatial Reasoning:** On `Maze` solving, performance increases from **51.4%** to **78.0%** (`Phi-4-reasoning`).

These results confirm that the reasoning strategies learned during SFT and RL on math problems transfer effectively to logical constraint satisfaction and spatial navigation, domains not explicitly optimized during the RL phase.

**Coding Performance**
On `LiveCodeBench`, `Phi-4-reasoning` achieves **53.8%**, a significant lift from the base model, though slightly behind `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-70B` (57.5%). Notably, `Phi-4-reasoning-plus` sees a slight dip to **53.1%**, suggesting the math-focused RL did not benefit coding and may have slightly shifted the model's distribution away from code optimization.

### 5.3 Quantitative Results: General-Purpose and Safety

The transition to reasoning-focused training does not degrade general capabilities; in many cases, it enhances them. **Table 2** provides the evidence:
*   **Instruction Following (IFEval):** `Phi-4-reasoning-plus` scores **84.9%**, significantly outperforming `Phi-4` (62.3%) and even `GPT-4o` (81.8%).
*   **Long-Context Reasoning (FlenQA):** On the 3K-token subset, the model achieves **97.9%** accuracy, vastly superior to `Phi-4` (82.0%) and `GPT-4o` (90.8%). The authors attribute this to the model's training on long chain-of-thought traces, making it more robust to context dispersion (**Figure 13**).
*   **Human Preference (ArenaHard):** Scores improve from **68.1%** to **79.0%**, indicating that the structured "thinking" process leads to responses humans prefer.
*   **Safety (Toxigen):** While `Phi-4-reasoning-plus` improves toxic detection (86.7% vs 72.6%), it sees a drop in neutral detection (77.3% vs 90.0%), indicating a trade-off where the model becomes more sensitive but potentially over-cautious. `Phi-4-reasoning` (SFT only) offers the best balance (86.7% toxic, 84.7% neutral).

### 5.4 Critical Analysis: Trade-offs, Variance, and Limitations

**The Token Length vs. Accuracy Trade-off**
The paper explicitly analyzes the cost of reasoning in terms of compute (tokens). **Figure 11** and **Section 5.1.3** reveal distinct profiles:
*   `Phi-4-reasoning-plus` generates traces approximately **1.5× longer** than `Phi-4-reasoning` on average.
*   This extra compute yields diminishing returns outside of math. While `Phi-4-reasoning-plus` gains ~15% on `AIME 2025` over the SFT-only version, the gain on `BA-Calendar` is negligible (65.6% vs 67.7%), and on `TSP` it is modest.
*   This suggests the RL reward function successfully incentivized "thinking harder" for math (where verification is easy) but failed to generalize this efficiency to other domains, leading to verbose but not necessarily more accurate traces for non-math tasks.

**Statistical Variance and Reliability**
The authors' decision to run 50+ evaluations per benchmark exposes a critical issue in the field. **Figure 9** shows the kernel density estimation of accuracy on `AIME 2025`:
*   `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-70B` has a massive variance, ranging from **30% to 70%** across runs.
*   `o3-mini` ranges from **70% to 100%**.
*   `Phi-4-reasoning-plus` is relatively stable, largely intersecting with `o3-mini`'s distribution.
This variance implies that reported "SOTA" claims based on single runs are statistically fragile. The paper argues that robust evaluation must report standard deviations and use larger sample sizes, a practice often ignored in other technical reports.

**Failure Cases and Limitations**
Despite strong performance, specific weaknesses remain:
*   **Domain Gaps:** As shown in **Figure 16**, improvements in `GPQA` are driven by Physics; gains in **Biology** and **Chemistry** are marginal compared to baselines. Similarly, **Figure 15** indicates that **Discrete Mathematics** and **Geometry** remain harder for all models, including `Phi-4-reasoning`, compared to Algebra or Calculus.
*   **Context Window Constraints:** The model is trained for 32K tokens. While it can generate up to 64K at inference (via RoPE interpolation), the RL training clipped responses at ~31K. **Figure 7e** shows that as training progresses, the "clip ratio" (fraction of responses hitting the limit) increases, preventing the model from correcting itself if it hasn't found the solution by token 31,000. This caps the potential benefit of further inference-time scaling.
*   **Recall Degradation:** In the `Kitab` retrieval benchmark, the reasoning models show improved precision but degraded recall in the "no context" setting (**Table 2**), suggesting that the focus on rigorous step-by-step verification may make the model more hesitant to retrieve parametric knowledge unless explicitly grounded.

### 5.5 Conclusion on Experimental Validity

The experiments convincingly support the paper's central claim: **careful data curation and SFT can distill strong reasoning capabilities into small models, which can be further amplified by targeted RL.**

The evidence is strongest in the **transfer learning** results—the ability to solve `TSP` and `Calendar` planning problems despite never seeing them during RL training proves the model learned a generalizable reasoning algorithm, not just math heuristics. However, the results also highlight a conditional success: the benefits of RL are heavily skewed toward domains with verifiable answers (Math), while the token efficiency of these long traces remains suboptimal for open-ended or complex planning tasks. The rigorous statistical analysis (50+ runs) adds significant credibility to the findings, setting a new standard for how reasoning models should be evaluated.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While `Phi-4-reasoning` demonstrates that small models can achieve frontier-level reasoning through data-centric training, the approach is not without significant constraints. The performance gains come with specific trade-offs in computational efficiency, domain coverage, and architectural boundaries. Understanding these limitations is crucial for deploying these models effectively and for guiding future research.

### 6.1 Dependence on High-Quality Synthetic Data and Teacher Models

The success of `Phi-4-reasoning` is fundamentally anchored to the availability of a superior "teacher" model to generate the training signal.
*   **Teacher Reliance:** The supervised fine-tuning (SFT) dataset relies entirely on reasoning traces generated by `o3-mini` (Section 2.2). The quality of the student model is thus capped by the capabilities of this teacher. If `o3-mini` fails to solve a specific class of problems or exhibits a particular bias in its reasoning chain, `Phi-4-reasoning` will likely inherit these flaws. The paper does not explore how the model performs if the teacher provides noisy or sub-optimal reasoning traces, leaving the robustness of the distillation process to teacher quality an open question.
*   **Data Curation Bottleneck:** The methodology requires an intensive, multi-stage filtering process to identify "teachable" prompts—those where the base model fails but the teacher succeeds (Section 2.1). This involves heuristic difficulty estimation and LLM-based rubric evaluation. This process is computationally expensive and difficult to scale to new domains where verifiable ground truth or strong teacher signals are unavailable. Unlike pre-training on raw web data, this approach does not easily benefit from simply increasing data volume; it requires increasing data *quality*, which is a harder constraint to satisfy.

### 6.2 Domain Specialization and Generalization Gaps

The training strategy introduces a clear bias toward domains with verifiable, objective solutions, leading to uneven performance across different task types.
*   **Math-Centric RL Bias:** The Reinforcement Learning (RL) phase was conducted exclusively on **mathematical problems** (Section 4). While this yielded massive gains in math (e.g., +15% on AIME 2025 for `Phi-4-reasoning-plus`), the benefits did not uniformly transfer.
    *   **Coding Regression:** On `LiveCodeBench`, `Phi-4-reasoning-plus` (53.1%) actually performed slightly worse than the SFT-only `Phi-4-reasoning` (53.8%) (Table 1). This suggests that the math-focused reward function may have shifted the model's distribution in a way that slightly degraded its coding capabilities, or simply failed to reinforce them.
    *   **Scientific Disparities:** In scientific reasoning (`GPQA`), improvements were driven almost entirely by **Physics**. Performance gains in **Biology** and **Chemistry** were marginal compared to baselines (Figure 16). Similarly, within mathematics, the model struggles more with **Discrete Mathematics** and **Geometry** than with Algebra or Calculus (Figure 15). This indicates that the "reasoning" skill learned is not perfectly uniform; it is heavily optimized for the logical structures present in the training data (primarily physics-style and algebraic problems).
*   **Parametric Knowledge vs. Reasoning:** The focus on step-by-step derivation appears to come at the cost of direct knowledge retrieval. In the `Kitab` benchmark (Table 2), while precision improved, **recall degraded** in the "no context" setting (e.g., dropping from 8.2% to 4.9% for `Phi-4-reasoning`). The model seems to have learned to be hesitant to answer from parametric memory without explicit grounding, potentially over-applying its "verify before answering" heuristic to facts that should be retrieved directly.

### 6.3 Computational Costs and Inference Efficiency

The "inference-time scaling" that powers these models' success imposes a heavy tax on latency and compute resources.
*   **Token Overhead:** To achieve its performance, `Phi-4-reasoning-plus` generates reasoning traces that are approximately **1.5× longer** than `Phi-4-reasoning` and significantly longer than non-reasoning models (Section 5.1.3).
*   **Diminishing Returns:** The relationship between token count and accuracy is not linear. For tasks like `BA-Calendar` and `TSP`, `Phi-4-reasoning-plus` generates longer traces than the SFT version but achieves **negligible or no accuracy gains** (Figure 11). This indicates that the RL-induced "thinking more" behavior is not always efficient; the model sometimes expends extra compute without finding a better solution, particularly in domains where the reward signal (math correctness) was not present during RL training.
*   **Latency Implications:** For real-time applications, the requirement to generate thousands of additional tokens before producing a final answer creates a significant latency bottleneck. The model cannot provide a "quick answer"; it must always engage in the full reasoning cycle, which may be overkill for simple queries.

### 6.4 Architectural Constraints: The Context Window Ceiling

A hard limitation of the current approach is the model's context window, which acts as a ceiling on reasoning complexity.
*   **The 32K Token Barrier:** The model was trained with a maximum context length of **32K tokens** (Section 3). During RL training, responses exceeding ~31K tokens were clipped (Section 4.2).
*   **Truncation Risks:** As shown in **Figure 7e**, the "clip ratio" (the fraction of generations hitting the length limit) increases as training progresses. If a model fails to solve a complex problem within 31K tokens, it is forced to truncate its thought process, often preventing it from reaching the final `\boxed{}` answer tag. This results in a failed generation regardless of whether the model was on the right track.
*   **Inference vs. Training Mismatch:** While the authors note the model can be tested at up to 64K tokens via RoPE interpolation, it was not *trained* to reason effectively at those lengths. The RL reward function penalizes length beyond a certain point for correct answers, potentially discouraging the model from utilizing the full extended context even when it might be necessary for harder problems. This suggests that simply extending the context window at inference time is insufficient; the model needs to be explicitly trained to manage and reason over such long horizons.

### 6.5 Evaluation Fragility and Non-Determinism

The paper highlights a meta-limitation regarding how we assess reasoning models, which complicates the interpretation of all results presented.
*   **High Variance:** Reasoning models exhibit significant non-determinism. As detailed in **Section 5.1.2** and **Figure 9**, the accuracy of models on small benchmarks like `AIME 2025` can vary by **5–10 percentage points** between two sets of just 5 runs. For `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-70B`, the range across runs spans from 30% to 70%.
*   **Unreliable Comparisons:** This variance makes side-by-side comparisons based on single-run or low-sample evaluations statistically unreliable. While `Phi-4-reasoning` used 50+ runs to establish robustness, many competing models in the literature report only single scores. This makes it difficult to definitively claim superiority over models that have not been evaluated with the same statistical rigor.
*   **Safety Evaluation Gaps:** Current safety evaluation tools (LLM judges) are not optimized for long chain-of-thought traces. They may miss biases hidden deep in the reasoning block or falsely flag valid exploratory steps (e.g., considering a counterfactual) as harmful (Section 5.3). The true safety profile of models that "think" extensively before answering remains an open area of research.

### 6.6 Summary of Open Questions

The results of `Phi-4-reasoning` point to several critical questions for future work:
1.  **Can RL be effectively scaled to non-verifiable domains?** The current success relies on math problems with clear right/wrong answers. How can similar gains be achieved for open-ended tasks like creative writing or complex strategic planning where a rule-based reward function is impossible to define?
2.  **How do we break the context ceiling?** Will training with 64K or 128K contexts unlock further reasoning capabilities, or does the model hit a fundamental limit in its ability to maintain coherent logical threads over such lengths?
3.  **Can we decouple reasoning depth from token count?** Is it possible to train models that perform deeper logical operations per token, reducing the computational cost of reasoning without sacrificing accuracy?

In summary, `Phi-4-reasoning` proves that small models can reason, but it does so by leaning heavily on specific conditions: high-quality synthetic data, verifiable reward signals, and generous compute budgets for inference. Breaking free from these constraints remains the next frontier.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance
For practitioners considering integrating `Phi-4-reasoning` into their workflows, the following guidelines are derived from the paper's findings:

*   **When to Prefer This Model:**
    *   Choose `Phi-4-reasoning` (or `plus`) when **accuracy on complex, multi-step problems** is the primary metric and you are constrained by **hardware memory** (cannot host 70B+ models).
    *   It is particularly well-suited for **STEM education, mathematical verification, and algorithmic planning** tasks.
    *   Avoid using it for **low-latency, real-time chat** or simple factual retrieval tasks where the overhead of generating thousands of reasoning tokens is unjustified and the base `Phi-4` or other standard models would be more efficient.

*   **Inference Configuration:**
    *   **Temperature:** Use a temperature of **0.8** (as used in the paper's evaluations) to encourage the exploration necessary for effective reasoning. Lower temperatures may collapse the diversity of thought paths needed for hard problems.
    *   **Max Tokens:** Set the maximum generation length to at least **32,768 tokens**. For the hardest problems (e.g., AIME 2025), consider extending to **64K** via RoPE interpolation, though be aware the model was not explicitly RL-trained at this length.
    *   **System Prompt:** Strictly adhere to the **reasoning-specific system message** provided in Section 3.1. Removing or altering the instruction to use `` tags will likely degrade performance significantly, as the model relies on this structural cue to activate its reasoning mode.

*   **Evaluation Best Practices:**
    *   Do not rely on a single run for benchmarking. If evaluating on small datasets (like AIME with 30 questions), run **at least 5–10 independent generations** per prompt and report the **average and standard deviation**.
    *   For critical applications, consider implementing a **"Best-of-N"** strategy (generating multiple solutions and selecting the best via a verifier or majority vote), as Figure 12 shows a significant gap between average and best-of-5 performance.

In conclusion, `Phi-4-reasoning` demonstrates that the future of AI may not just be about building bigger models, but about building **smarter, more efficient ones** that know how to think. By shifting the focus to data quality and inference-time dynamics, this work provides a scalable path forward for making advanced reasoning accessible to all.
