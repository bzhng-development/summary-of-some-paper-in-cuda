## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces Optimization by PROmpting (OPRO), a framework that leverages large language models (LLMs) like PaLM 2-L and GPT-4 as derivative-free optimizers by iteratively generating new solutions based on natural language descriptions and previous solution-score trajectories. Applied to prompt optimization, OPRO discovers instructions that maximize task accuracy, outperforming human-designed prompts by up to 8% on GSM8K (achieving 80.2% vs. 71.8%) and by up to 50% on Big-Bench Hard tasks. This work matters because it enables optimization in settings lacking gradients or formal specifications, showing that LLMs can navigate solution spaces effectively by learning from optimization trajectories described in natural language.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of Optimization by PROmpting (OPRO), we must first examine the landscape of optimization problems and why standard solutions often fail in modern machine learning contexts, particularly when interacting with Large Language Models (LLMs) via APIs.

### The Gradient Barrier in Real-World Optimization

Optimization is the process of finding the best solution from a set of feasible alternatives, typically by maximizing or minimizing an objective function. In traditional machine learning, this is dominated by **derivative-based algorithms** like stochastic gradient descent (Kingma & Ba, 2015). These methods rely on calculating the gradient (the slope) of the loss landscape to determine which direction improves the solution.

However, a critical gap exists: **many real-world applications do not provide gradients.**
*   **Black-Box Access:** When using commercial LLMs (e.g., via API), users cannot access the model weights or internal states to compute gradients.
*   **Discrete Spaces:** Problems like finding the best text prompt involve discrete tokens, where the concept of a "slope" is undefined.
*   **Non-Differentiable Objectives:** Metrics like accuracy or human preference scores are not differentiable functions.

The paper notes that while derivative-free optimization algorithms exist (e.g., evolutionary algorithms, heuristic solvers), they typically require significant customization:
> "The optimization algorithm typically needs to be customized for an individual task to deal with the specific challenges posed by the decision space and the performance landscape" (Section 1).

This customization burden limits the accessibility of optimization for non-experts or rapidly changing tasks.

### The Specific Challenge: Prompt Optimization

The paper positions **prompt optimization** as a primary use case to demonstrate OPRO's capabilities. Prompt engineering is critical because LLM performance is highly sensitive to prompt formatting. The authors highlight a non-obvious difficulty in this space:
> "Semantically similar prompts may have drastically different performance" (Section 1).

For example, on GSM8K, the instruction "Let's think step by step." achieves 71.8% accuracy, while a semantically similar variant might perform significantly worse (Section 5.2.3). This sensitivity creates a rugged optimization landscape where small changes in text lead to large, unpredictable jumps in performance. Furthermore, the search space is vast and discrete, making exhaustive search impossible.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

Before OPRO, researchers attempted to solve prompt optimization through several distinct paradigms. Understanding these helps clarify what OPRO changes.

#### 1. Continuous Prompt Tuning
Methods like prompt tuning (Lester et al., 2021) optimize continuous vectors representing prompts.
*   **Limitation:** These require access to model weights to compute gradients, making them incompatible with API-only models (Section 6).

#### 2. Discrete Search and Reinforcement Learning
Approaches using gradient-guided search (Shin et al., 2020) or reinforcement learning (Deng et al., 2022) attempt to search the discrete token space.
*   **Limitation:** Like continuous tuning, many of these require model access or complex training setups that are not feasible for black-box API usage (Section 6).

#### 3. Edit-Based Optimization
Some works propose editing existing prompts using human-defined operations (e.g., swapping phrases) or language models (e.g., back-translation) (Xu et al., 2022; Prasad et al., 2022).
*   **Limitation:** These are constrained by the predefined editing rules and may not explore the space creatively enough.

#### 4. LLM-Generated Prompts (APE and APO)
Recent works leverage LLMs to generate or refine prompts:
*   **APE (Zhou et al., 2022b):** Generates initial instructions, selects the top performers, and asks the LLM to generate semantically similar variants.
*   **APO (Pryzant et al., 2023):** Instructs the LLM to produce text feedback on how to update an old instruction.
*   **Limitation:** The paper argues these methods do not fully leverage the **optimization trajectory**. APE focuses on semantic similarity to initial instructions, while APO relies on feedback for single-step edits. They do not explicitly provide the LLM with a history of multiple past solutions and their scores to identify patterns of improvement (Section 6).

#### 5. Evolutionary Approaches (EvoPrompt)
Concurrent work like EvoPrompt (Guo et al., 2023) uses meta-prompts asking the LLM to perform mutation and crossover on existing prompts.
*   **Limitation:** EvoPrompt typically operates on pairs of prompts (crossover) and may lack task-specific context. The paper notes that EvoPrompt "does not utilize exemplars for prompt optimization, thus it lacks the understanding of the task to optimize for" (Section 5.5).

### How OPRO Positions Itself

OPRO distinguishes itself by treating the LLM not just as a generator, but as an **optimizer engine** driven by natural language. Its positioning relies on three key differentiators relative to the prior work described above:

1.  **Natural Language Problem Description:** Instead of formal mathematical specifications, the optimization task is described in text. This allows users to define objectives and constraints without deriving update steps programmatically (Section 1).
2.  **Trajectory-Based Learning:** Unlike APE or APO, OPRO includes the **optimization trajectory** in the meta-prompt. This consists of previously generated solutions sorted by their scores.
    > "Different from recent work... each optimization step in our work generates new prompts that aim to increase the test accuracy based on a trajectory of previously generated prompts" (Section 1).
    This allows the LLM to implicitly learn patterns of what makes a solution "good" without explicit gradient signals.
3.  **Black-Box Compatibility:** OPRO requires only API access. It does not need model weights, gradients, or fine-tuning of the optimizer LLM itself (Section 6).

By framing optimization as a language task, OPRO bridges the gap between powerful derivative-free optimization needs and the accessibility of modern LLM APIs. It posits that LLMs can recognize optimization patterns (e.g., "if decreasing $w$ improved the score last time, try decreasing it again") purely from in-context examples of past successes and failures.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems paper that proposes a novel optimization framework; the core idea is to treat the optimization process itself as a language task, where an LLM reads a history of past solutions and their scores in natural language format, then generates new candidate solutions that improve upon them.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

OPRO builds a closed-loop optimization system where a large language model serves as the optimizer, reading a text description of the problem and a history of what has been tried before, then proposing new solutions. The system solves derivative-free optimization problems by converting the optimization trajectory into a natural language prompt, allowing the LLM to recognize patterns of improvement without requiring mathematical gradients or formal update rules.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The OPRO framework consists of four interconnected components that operate in a iterative loop. First, the **Meta-Prompt Constructor** assembles the optimization context by combining the problem description, optimization trajectory (past solution-score pairs), and meta-instructions into a single text prompt. Second, the **Optimizer LLM** receives this meta-prompt and generates multiple new candidate solutions in natural language or parameter format. Third, the **Objective Function Evaluator** (also called the scorer) assesses each new solution by computing its score—for prompt optimization, this means running the generated instruction on a training set and measuring accuracy. Fourth, the **Trajectory Manager** updates the optimization history by adding the new solution-score pairs, sorting them by score, and preparing the updated meta-prompt for the next iteration. Information flows sequentially: the meta-prompt feeds the optimizer, optimizer outputs feed the evaluator, evaluator outputs feed the trajectory manager, and the trajectory manager feeds back into the meta-prompt constructor for the next step.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the meta-prompt structure because it is the primary interface through which the LLM understands the optimization task, and every other component feeds information into this prompt.
- We then explain the solution generation mechanism, including how multiple candidates are sampled and why this improves stability.
- We describe the evaluation pipeline next, since understanding how solutions are scored is necessary to understand what the optimizer is trying to improve.
- We cover the optimization loop termination conditions and iteration dynamics, which determine when the process stops and how performance evolves over time.
- We conclude with the specific hyperparameter configurations discovered through ablation studies, as these reveal which design choices matter most for performance.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Core Optimization Loop Mechanics**

The OPRO framework operates as an iterative optimization procedure where each step $t$ produces a batch of new candidate solutions based on the history of all previous steps. At initialization (step $t=0$), the system starts with a small set of initial solutions—for prompt optimization, this might be an empty string or a simple instruction like "Let's solve the problem." with their corresponding accuracy scores computed on a training set. At each optimization step $t$, the meta-prompt is constructed by concatenating three components: (1) the optimization problem description in natural language, (2) the optimization trajectory containing up to 20 of the best solution-score pairs from steps $0$ through $t-1$, sorted in ascending order by score, and (3) meta-instructions that tell the optimizer LLM what format to use and what properties the new solutions should have. The optimizer LLM then generates $k$ new solutions, where the default value is $k=8$ solutions per step (Section 5.1). Each of these $k$ solutions is evaluated by the scorer LLM or objective function to obtain a score, and these new solution-score pairs are added to the trajectory for step $t+1$. The optimization terminates when either the LLM fails to propose solutions with better scores than existing ones, or a maximum number of steps is reached—the default is 200 steps for prompt optimization experiments (Section 5.2.1).

**Meta-Prompt Structure and Information Encoding**

The meta-prompt is the critical interface that translates the optimization problem into a format the LLM can process, and its design directly determines whether the LLM can recognize improvement patterns. The optimization problem description component specifies the objective function in natural language—for prompt optimization, this includes instructions like "generate a new instruction that achieves a higher accuracy" along with constraints such as "the instruction should be concise and generally applicable" (Section 2.2). The optimization trajectory component contains previously generated solutions paired with their scores, and critically, these pairs are sorted in ascending order by score so that the best solutions appear at the end of the prompt. This ordering exploits the recency bias observed in LLMs, where models are more likely to generate tokens similar to the end of the prompt (Zhao et al., 2021, as cited in Section 5.3). The paper tests this design choice in ablation studies, comparing ascending order (lowest to highest score) against descending order (highest to lowest) and random ordering, finding that ascending order achieves better final accuracies and converges faster (Figure 7a and 7b). The score values themselves are presented as integers from 0 to 100, which is equivalent to bucketizing accuracies into 100 buckets—ablation studies show that showing scores (even bucketized) helps the optimizer LLM understand quality differences better than showing only the ranking without scores (Figure 7c and 7d). For prompt optimization specifically, the meta-prompt also includes 3 randomly sampled training exemplars that demonstrate the task format and show where the generated instruction will be inserted (Figure 3). These exemplars are essential because they provide context about what the task looks like, but ablation studies reveal that more exemplars (e.g., 10) do not improve performance and may distract the optimizer from the optimization trajectory (Figure 7e and 7f).

**Solution Generation with Exploration-Exploitation Control**

The optimizer LLM generates new solutions through controlled sampling, where the temperature parameter governs the trade-off between exploring novel solutions and exploiting known good patterns. At temperature 0.0, the LLM greedily decodes the most likely tokens, which encourages exploitation but reduces diversity—the ablation studies show this causes the optimizer to get stuck generating the same instruction for tens of steps, resulting in flat optimization curves (Figure 10). At the default temperature of 1.0, the LLM balances exploration and exploitation effectively, generating diverse enough solutions to discover improvements while still building on patterns from high-scoring historical solutions. At higher temperatures (1.5 and 2.0), the LLM becomes too exploratory and often ignores the optimization trajectory entirely, causing the optimization curve to lose its steady upward trend (Figure 10). The system generates multiple solutions per step (default $k=8$) rather than a single solution, which serves a similar purpose to computing mini-batch gradients in stochastic gradient descent—it reduces variance and improves optimization stability (Section 2.3). Ablation studies compare generating 1, 2, 4, 8, or 16 instructions per step, finding that 8 instructions achieves the best overall performance when accounting for the total evaluation budget (Figure 8). This is because generating too few instructions (1 or 2) provides insufficient exploration per step, while generating too many (16) reduces the total number of optimization steps possible within a fixed evaluation budget, limiting how much trajectory information the LLM can accumulate.

**Evaluation Pipeline and Scorer Configuration**

The objective function evaluator, called the scorer LLM, assesses each generated solution by computing its performance on a held-out training set, and this scorer can be the same model or a different model from the optimizer LLM. For prompt optimization, the scorer receives the generated instruction concatenated with task exemplars at one of three positions: `Q_begin` (instruction before the question), `Q_end` (instruction after the question), or `A_begin` (instruction at the beginning of the answer) (Section 4.1). The choice of position depends on the scorer model type—pre-trained models like PaLM 2-L work best with `A_begin` formatting in a few-shot QA pattern, while instruction-tuned models like text-bison work best with `Q_begin` or `Q_end` without the QA pattern (Section 5.1). The scorer evaluates each instruction by running it on a subset of the training set—for GSM8K, this is 3.5% of the 7,473 training samples (approximately 261 examples), and for Big-Bench Hard, this is 20% of each task's examples (Section 4.1 and 5.2.1). The training accuracy on this subset serves as the optimization objective, and the paper demonstrates that optimizing on this small subset is sufficient to achieve high performance on the full test set. During evaluation of final instructions, the scorer uses temperature 0.0 (greedy decoding) to ensure deterministic outputs, while the optimizer uses temperature 1.0 during the optimization process (Section 5.1).

**Mathematical Optimization Case Studies**

Before applying OPRO to prompt optimization, the paper validates the framework on two classical mathematical optimization problems to demonstrate that LLMs can optimize without task-specific programming. For linear regression, the goal is to find coefficients $w$ and $b$ that minimize the objective function for $y = wx + b + \epsilon$, where $\epsilon$ is Gaussian noise (Section 3.1). The meta-prompt includes up to 20 of the best $(w, b)$ pairs from history with their objective values, sorted in ascending order, and asks the LLM to propose a new pair with a lower objective value (Figure 19 in Appendix C.1). The optimization starts from 5 randomly sampled $(w, b)$ pairs in the region $[10, 20] \times [10, 20]$, and the LLM generates up to 8 new pairs per step. Table 2 shows results across three settings: ground truth parameters within the starting region, "near outside" (within distance 10 of the region), and "far outside" (distance greater than 10). The results demonstrate that LLMs perform black-box optimization—they compare scores and propose descent directions without seeing the analytic form of the objective function. For the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP), the goal is to find the shortest route traversing $n$ nodes and returning to the start (Section 3.2). The meta-prompt includes previous traces (node orderings) with their total distances, and the LLM generates new traces that visit all nodes exactly once (Figure 20 in Appendix C.1). Table 3 shows that GPT-4 significantly outperforms other LLMs, reaching the global optimum about 4× faster on small problems ($n=10$), but performance degrades as $n$ increases—with $n=50$, all LLMs fail to find optimal solutions while the Farthest Insertion heuristic succeeds.

**Prompt Optimization Specific Configuration**

For the main application of prompt optimization, OPRO uses specific configurations tailored to natural language instruction search. The optimizer LLM generates instructions that are concatenated to task exemplars, and the position depends on the scorer model as described above (Section 4.1). The meta-prompt at each step contains the best 20 instructions found so far (not all instructions, to respect context length limits) along with 3 randomly picked training exemplars (Section 5.1). The optimization runs for up to 200 steps by default, but the paper notes that outstanding instructions can be found much earlier—for example, on GSM8K with PaLM 2-L-IT optimizer, the instruction "Let's do the math!" was found at step 6 with 78.2% training accuracy, nearly matching the best instruction "Take a deep breath and work on this problem step-by-step." found at step 107 with 80.2% accuracy (Section 5.2.1). This occurs because optimization curve leaps do not always correspond to discovering a single much-better instruction; instead, they can reflect qualitative improvement across all 8 instructions generated in a step, and the meta-prompt gradually filters out worse instructions over subsequent steps.

**Design Choices and Their Rationale**

Several non-obvious design choices in OPRO distinguish it from prior LLM-based optimization approaches and warrant explanation. First, OPRO provides the full optimization trajectory (multiple past solutions with scores) rather than just the single best solution or pairwise comparisons—this allows the LLM to identify patterns across multiple high-scoring solutions rather than just imitating one (Section 1 and 6). This contrasts with APE (Zhou et al., 2022b), which generates semantically similar variants of initial instructions, and APO (Pryzant et al., 2023), which produces feedback for editing single instructions. Second, OPRO does not require the new solutions to follow the same semantic meaning as previous ones—the LLM is only instructed to improve the score, allowing it to discover instructions with different semantic content that happen to work better (Section 1). Third, the system generates multiple solutions per step rather than one, which improves stability because LLM outputs can be sensitive to low-quality solutions in the early trajectory when the solution space is underexplored (Section 2.3). Fourth, the ascending score ordering in the trajectory exploits LLM recency bias—since models attend more to recent tokens, placing the best solutions at the end encourages the LLM to build upon them (Section 5.3). Fifth, OPRO separates the optimizer LLM from the scorer LLM, allowing different models to serve different roles—the optimizer needs creativity and pattern recognition, while the scorer needs accurate evaluation (Section 4.1). This separation enables configurations like using GPT-4 as the optimizer with PaLM 2-L as the scorer, or using PaLM 2-L to optimize its own prompts (Figure 4b).

**Comparison with Evolutionary Approaches (EvoPrompt)**

The paper directly compares OPRO to EvoPrompt (Guo et al., 2023), which uses genetic algorithm (GA) and differential evolution (DE) meta-prompts that explicitly instruct the LLM to perform mutation and crossover on pairs of existing prompts (Section 5.5). EvoPrompt's GA meta-prompt takes two prompts, instructs the LLM to cross them over, then mutates the result. The DE variant adds more detailed instructions, such as asking the LLM to identify differences between the two prompts before mutation. In contrast, OPRO leverages the full optimization trajectory (up to 20 past prompts) rather than just 2, and provides richer task information including training exemplars and accuracy scores. Figure 12 shows that on GSM8K, EvoPrompt actually degrades performance from the initial instructions, while OPRO steadily improves. The paper attributes this to EvoPrompt not utilizing exemplars, so it "lacks the understanding of the task to optimize for" and requires good-quality, task-specific initial prompts to work (Section 5.5). On BBH sports_understanding with task-specific initial instructions, EvoPrompt (DE) does find better prompts than the starting point, but the optimization curve is less stable than OPRO's, indicating that the trajectory-based approach helps the LLM identify more promising improvement directions.

**Termination Conditions and Convergence Behavior**

The optimization process terminates under one of two conditions: either the LLM fails to propose new solutions with better scores than existing ones, or the maximum number of optimization steps is reached (Section 2). In practice, for prompt optimization experiments, the default is to run 200 steps, but the optimization curves typically show convergence behavior where improvements become smaller in later steps (Figure 1, Figure 4, Figure 6). The curves also show decreasing variance among the accuracies of instructions generated at each step, indicating that the optimizer LLM generates distributionally better instructions as optimization progresses (Section 5.2.1). However, the paper notes that optimization can get stuck in certain scenarios—for mathematical optimization, this happens when the loss landscape is too bumpy for the LLM to propose correct descent directions, or when all past solutions in the trajectory share parameter values in quantitatively opposite directions from the global optimum (Appendix A). For prompt optimization, overfitting can occur where training accuracy exceeds test accuracy by 5%-20%, but the paper shows that validation accuracy trends alongside training accuracy, meaning higher training accuracy solutions still achieve higher test accuracy (Figure 11, Section 5.4).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the fundamental contributions that distinguish OPRO from prior optimization approaches. We distinguish between **incremental improvements** (refinements of existing methods) and **fundamental innovations** (new capabilities or conceptual shifts). The evidence for each claim is anchored to specific results in the paper.

---

### 4.1 Fundamental Innovation: Optimization Trajectory as In-Context Learning Signal

**What it is:** OPRO provides the LLM optimizer with the complete history of past solutions and their scores (the optimization trajectory), sorted by performance, rather than just the single best solution or pairwise comparisons.

**Why this differs from prior work:** Previous LLM-based prompt optimization methods operate on fundamentally different principles:
- **APE (Zhou et al., 2022b)** generates semantically similar variants of initial instructions, essentially searching around a fixed semantic region (Section 6).
- **APO (Pryzant et al., 2023)** produces text feedback for editing single instructions, focusing on incremental modification rather than pattern recognition across multiple solutions (Section 6).
- **EvoPrompt (Guo et al., 2023)** performs crossover and mutation on pairs of prompts, limiting the optimization context to exactly two parent solutions (Section 5.5).

OPRO instead provides up to 20 past solution-score pairs, allowing the LLM to implicitly learn optimization patterns like "when parameter $w$ decreased in the last three high-scoring solutions, the objective improved" without explicit gradient signals.

**Why this is significant:** This represents a conceptual shift from **editing-based optimization** to **pattern-based optimization**. The evidence shows this matters substantially:
- In the comparison with EvoPrompt (Figure 12), OPRO steadily improves on GSM8K while EvoPrompt actually degrades performance from initial instructions. The paper attributes this to OPRO's richer trajectory information enabling better direction identification.
- The ablation study on instruction ordering (Figure 7a, 7b) shows ascending order (best solutions at the end) converges faster and achieves higher final accuracy than descending or random order. This confirms the LLM is actively learning from the trajectory structure, not just copying the best solution.
- Removing score values entirely (showing only ranked instructions) reduces performance (Figure 7c, 7d), indicating the LLM uses magnitude information to gauge improvement potential, not just relative ranking.

**Classification:** This is a **fundamental innovation** because it enables a new optimization paradigm where the LLM learns optimization dynamics from in-context examples rather than following explicit update rules or editing operations.

---

### 4.2 Fundamental Innovation: LLMs as Task-Agnostic Derivative-Free Optimizers

**What it is:** The paper demonstrates that the same OPRO framework optimizes fundamentally different problem types—continuous mathematical functions (linear regression), discrete combinatorial problems (TSP), and natural language prompts—without task-specific modifications to the optimizer.

**Why this differs from prior work:** Traditional optimization requires algorithm customization:
> "The optimization algorithm typically needs to be customized for an individual task to deal with the specific challenges posed by the decision space and the performance landscape" (Section 1).

Derivative-free optimizers like evolutionary algorithms or specialized solvers (e.g., Gurobi for TSP) are designed for specific problem structures. Prior LLM optimization work focused exclusively on prompts.

**Why this is significant:** This demonstrates **generalization across optimization domains** through natural language abstraction:
- For linear regression (Table 2), GPT-4 finds the global optimum in 4.0 ± 1.5 steps when ground truth is within the starting region, exploring only 17.2 ± 5.1 unique $(w, b)$ pairs—far fewer than exhaustive search.
- For TSP with $n=10$ nodes (Table 3), GPT-4 reaches the global optimum in 9.6 ± 3.0 steps with 0.0% optimality gap, matching oracle solutions.
- The same meta-prompt structure (solution-score pairs + problem description + generation instructions) works for all three domains.

**Important limitation:** The paper is explicit that OPRO is **not designed to outperform specialized solvers** on large-scale mathematical problems (Section 3.2 Limitations). Performance degrades significantly as TSP problem size increases—at $n=50$, all LLMs fail to find optimal solutions while the Farthest Insertion heuristic succeeds. The innovation is demonstrating feasibility and generalization, not state-of-the-art mathematical optimization performance.

**Classification:** This is a **fundamental innovation** because it establishes LLMs as general-purpose optimizers that can handle diverse problem types through natural language abstraction, rather than requiring domain-specific algorithm design.

---

### 4.3 Key Insight: Semantic Equivalence Does Not Imply Performance Equivalence in Prompts

**What it is:** The paper reveals that prompts with nearly identical semantic meaning can have drastically different accuracies, and conversely, semantically different prompts can achieve similar or better performance.

**Evidence from the paper:**
- On GSM8K with PaLM 2-L scorer (Section 5.2.3):
  - "Let's think step by step." → 71.8% accuracy
  - "Let's solve the problem together." → 60.5% accuracy
  - "Let's work together to solve this problem step by step." → 49.4% accuracy
  
  The third instruction is essentially a semantic combination of the first two, yet performs worst.

- Table 1 shows top instructions from different optimizers have varied semantic content but comparable performance:
  - PaLM 2-L-IT: "Take a deep breath and work on this problem step-by-step." → 80.2%
  - PaLM 2-L: "Break this down." → 79.9%
  - GPT-3.5-turbo: "A little bit of arithmetic and a logical approach will help us quickly arrive at the solution to this problem." → 78.5%

**Why this is significant:** This finding has two important implications:

1. **Prompt optimization cannot rely on semantic similarity constraints.** Methods like APE that generate "semantically similar variants" of initial instructions (Section 6) may miss high-performing regions of the prompt space. OPRO's approach of only requiring score improvement (not semantic similarity) enables discovery of unexpectedly effective instructions.

2. **The mechanism by which prompts influence LLM behavior is not fully captured by semantic meaning.** Short, imperative phrases ("Break this down.") can match or exceed verbose, explanatory instructions. This suggests prompt effectiveness depends on factors beyond literal meaning—possibly token-level patterns, training data associations, or model-specific sensitivities.

**Classification:** This is a **key insight** rather than a fundamental innovation because it reveals important properties of the optimization landscape that OPRO exploits, rather than introducing a new capability. However, it fundamentally challenges assumptions underlying prior prompt optimization approaches.

---

### 4.4 Fundamental Innovation: Self-Optimization Without External Intervention

**What it is:** The paper demonstrates that an LLM can optimize its own prompts—using PaLM 2-L as both optimizer and scorer improves PaLM 2-L's performance on GSM8K from 34.0% (empty instruction) to 79.9% (optimized instruction "Break this down.") (Table 4, Figure 4b).

**Why this differs from prior work:** Previous prompt optimization methods typically assume:
- A separate optimizer model (often more capable than the scorer)
- Human-designed initial prompts
- External feedback signals

OPRO shows a model can iteratively improve its own instruction-following performance through self-generated optimization trajectories.

**Why this is significant:** This capability has implications for **autonomous model improvement**:
- Figure 4b shows the optimization curve for pre-trained PaLM 2-L optimizing itself, with clear upward trend from initial accuracies of 32.2% and 33.3% to final accuracy of 79.9%.
- The generated instructions follow a consistent style ("Here you go:", "Let's do it:") suitable for the pre-trained model's few-shot formatting, indicating the LLM understands its own output preferences (Section 5.2.1).
- This suggests models could potentially improve their task performance without human prompt engineering or access to more capable optimizer models.

**Important caveat:** The paper notes this works best when the prompt format matches the model type—pre-trained models perform better with few-shot `A_begin` formatting, while instruction-tuned models work with `Q_begin`/`Q_end` (Section 5.1). Self-optimization requires understanding these format constraints.

**Classification:** This is a **fundamental innovation** because it demonstrates a new capability—models improving their own performance through iterative self-prompting—that was not established in prior work.

---

### 4.5 Key Insight: Meta-Prompt Design Principles for LLM Optimization

**What it is:** The ablation studies (Section 5.3) reveal specific design choices that determine whether LLMs can effectively learn from optimization trajectories, providing actionable principles for future work.

**Critical design principles discovered:**

| Design Choice | Optimal Setting | Performance Impact |
|--------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Instruction ordering | Ascending (best at end) | Faster convergence, higher final accuracy (Figure 7a, 7b) |
| Score presentation | Integer bucketization (100 buckets) | Better than 20 buckets or no scores (Figure 7c, 7d) |
| Exemplars | 3 training examples | More (10) distracts from trajectory (Figure 7e, 7f) |
| Solutions per step | 8 instructions | Balances exploration with trajectory accumulation (Figure 8) |
| Temperature | 1.0 | Lower lacks exploration, higher ignores trajectory (Figure 10) |

**Why this is significant:** These principles explain **why OPRO works** and provide guidance for applying LLM optimization to new domains:
- The ascending order finding connects to established LLM behavior (recency bias from Zhao et al., 2021), but demonstrates its practical impact on optimization convergence.
- The temperature ablation (Figure 10) shows optimization fails at both extremes—temperature 0.0 causes the LLM to repeat the same instruction for tens of steps, while temperature 2.0 causes it to ignore the trajectory entirely. This establishes an operational range for LLM-based optimization.
- The exemplar finding reveals a trade-off: exemplars are critical for task understanding, but too many dominate the context and distract from the optimization trajectory.

**Classification:** This is a **key insight** rather than a fundamental innovation because it refines understanding of how to effectively deploy the core OPRO framework, rather than introducing new capabilities. However, these principles are essential for reproducibility and extension to new domains.

---

### Summary: Incremental vs. Fundamental Contributions

| Contribution | Classification | Significance |
|-------------|---------------|--------------|
| Trajectory-based optimization learning | Fundamental Innovation | New optimization paradigm using in-context pattern recognition |
| Task-agnostic optimizer across domains | Fundamental Innovation | Generalizes beyond prompt-specific optimization |
| Semantic-performance decoupling in prompts | Key Insight | Challenges assumptions in prior prompt optimization |
| Self-optimization capability | Fundamental Innovation | Enables autonomous model improvement |
| Meta-prompt design principles | Key Insight | Provides actionable guidance for deployment |

The fundamental innovations establish OPRO as a new approach to derivative-free optimization using LLMs, while the key insights explain the conditions under which this approach succeeds and reveal important properties of the optimization landscape.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section evaluates whether OPRO delivers on its core claims: that LLMs can serve as effective derivative-free optimizers across diverse problem domains, and that trajectory-based prompt optimization discovers instructions superior to human-designed baselines. We examine the experimental methodology, quantitative results, ablation studies, and limitations revealed through failure case analysis.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Datasets and Problem Domains**

The paper evaluates OPRO across three distinct optimization domains to test generalization:

1.  **Linear Regression (Continuous Optimization):** Synthetic problems with 50 data points where $y = w_{true}x + b_{true} + \epsilon$, with $\epsilon$ as standard Gaussian noise. The optimization searches for coefficients $(w, b)$ starting from 5 random pairs in $[10, 20] \times [10, 20]$. Three ground truth settings are tested: within the starting region, "near outside" (distance < 10 from region), and "far outside" (distance > 10) (Section 3.1).

2.  **Traveling Salesman Problem (Discrete Combinatorial Optimization):** TSP instances with $n \in \{10, 15, 20, 50\}$ nodes, with coordinates sampled from $[-100, 100]$. Five problem instances are generated per $n$ value. The optimality gap is computed against Gurobi solver oracle solutions (Section 3.2).

3.  **Prompt Optimization (Natural Language):**
    - **GSM8K:** 7,473 training samples, 1,319 test samples of grade school math word problems. Optimization uses 3.5% of training data (~261 examples) (Section 5.1, 5.2.1).
    - **Big-Bench Hard (BBH):** 23 tasks covering arithmetic, symbolic manipulation, and commonsense reasoning. Each task has up to 250 examples. Optimization uses 20% of examples per task (Section 5.1, 5.2.2).
    - **Transferability Benchmarks:** MultiArith and AQuA for evaluating GSM8K-optimized instructions on different math reasoning datasets (Section 5.1).

**Models and Configurations**

The experiments test multiple optimizer-scorer combinations (Section 5.1):

| Role | Models |
|------|--------|
| Optimizer LLM | PaLM 2-L (pre-trained), PaLM 2-L-IT (instruction-tuned), text-bison, gpt-3.5-turbo, gpt-4 |
| Scorer LLM | PaLM 2-L (pre-trained), text-bison (instruction-tuned) |

Key hyperparameters (Section 5.1, 5.3):
- **Temperature:** 1.0 for optimizer LLM (default), 0.0 for scorer LLM during final evaluation
- **Instructions per step:** 8 (default)
- **Trajectory size:** Best 20 instructions kept in meta-prompt
- **Exemplars:** 3 randomly sampled training examples per step
- **Maximum steps:** 200 for prompt optimization

**Baselines**

The paper compares against multiple baseline categories:

1.  **Human-Designed Prompts:**
    - "Let's think step by step." (Kojima et al., 2022)
    - "Let's work this out in a step by step way to be sure we have the right answer." (Zhou et al., 2022b)
    - Empty string

2.  **Mathematical Optimization Heuristics:**
    - Nearest Neighbor (NN) for TSP
    - Farthest Insertion (FI) for TSP
    - Gurobi solver for oracle solutions (Section 3.2)

3.  **LLM-Based Prompt Optimization:**
    - EvoPrompt (GA and DE versions) (Guo et al., 2023) (Section 5.5)
    - One-step instruction generation (50 instructions in single step without trajectory) (Section 5.3)

**Metrics**

- **Mathematical Optimization:** Number of steps to reach global optimum, number of unique $(w, b)$ pairs explored, optimality gap (%) for TSP (Table 2, Table 3)
- **Prompt Optimization:** Training accuracy (optimization objective), test accuracy (final evaluation), overall accuracy (training + test combined) (Table 7, Table 10, Table 14)
- **Transferability:** Test accuracy on unseen datasets (MultiArith, AQuA) (Table 6)

### 5.3 Ablation Studies

Section 5.3 presents comprehensive ablation studies using text-bison as scorer and PaLM 2-L as optimizer on GSM8K and BBH sports_understanding.

**Instruction Ordering in Trajectory**

|----------|---------------------|----------------------------------------|

Ascending order (lowest to highest score, best solutions at end) achieves better final accuracies and converges faster. The paper hypothesizes this exploits LLM recency bias—models attend more to tokens near the end of the prompt (Zhao et al., 2021, cited in Section 5.3).

| No scores (ranking only) | ~62% | ~75% |

Showing scores (even bucketized) helps the optimizer LLM understand quality differences better than showing only rankings. This confirms the LLM uses magnitude information, not just relative ordering (Section 5.3).

**Number of Exemplars**

### 5.5 Failure Cases and Limitations

Appendix A and Section 3.2 document several failure modes that reveal important limitations.

**Mathematical Optimization Limitations**

1.  **Hallucination of Calculated Values:** Optimizer LLMs sometimes output incorrect function values (e.g., "the function value at (5, 3) is 15" when the true value differs). This occurs because the LLM attempts calculation without external tools (Appendix A).

2.  **Generating Duplicate Solutions:** Despite instructions to "give me a new (w, b) pair that is different from all pairs above," the optimizer LLM does not 100% reliably follow this constraint, sometimes outputting self-contradictory responses (Appendix A).

3.  **Getting Stuck at Suboptimal Points:** In linear regression, optimization can get stuck when:
    - All in-context exemplars share the same $w$ or $b$ that differs from ground truth
    - Best previous solutions have parameters in quantitatively opposite directions from global optima (e.g., all $w$ values smaller than $w_{true}$ while all $b$ values larger than $b_{true}$)
    
    This is mitigated by sampling multiple solutions per step for more exploration (Appendix A).

4.  **Bumpy Loss Landscapes:** For the Rosenbrock function $f(x, y) = (a-x)^2 + b(y-x^2)^2$ with $a=20$, optimization often gets stuck around $(0, 0)$ instead of navigating the narrow valley to the global optimum at $(20, 400)$. The LLM sees initial improvement by decreasing both $x$ and $y$ to 0, but then cannot navigate the valley (Figure 13, Appendix A).

5.  **TSP Scalability:** At $n=50$ nodes, all LLMs fail to find optimal solutions while the Farthest Insertion heuristic succeeds. Context window limits make it hard to fit large-scale problem descriptions, and the optimization landscape becomes too complex for the LLM to propose correct directions (Section 3.2 Limitations).

**Prompt Optimization Limitations**

1.  **Overfitting:** As documented in Section 5.4, training accuracies exceed test accuracies by 5%-20% in many cases.

2.  **Error Case Utilization:** The optimizer LLM does not effectively use error cases from the training set to infer improvement directions. The paper tested including error cases in the meta-prompt rather than random sampling, but results were similar—error cases alone are "not informative enough for the optimizer LLM to grasp the cause of the wrong prediction" (Section 7).

3.  **Training Set Size Requirement:** Prompt optimization requires at least tens of training samples to avoid severe overfitting. The paper identifies reducing this requirement as future work (Section 7).

4.  **Model-Specific Instructions:** Optimized instructions are often model-specific. Table 1 shows different optimizers produce instructions of different styles (PaLM 2-L-IT and text-bison produce concise instructions; GPT models produce long, detailed instructions). An instruction optimized for one model may not transfer optimally to another.

### 5.6 Assessment: Do Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: LLMs can serve as derivative-free optimizers**

**Supported.** The linear regression (Table 2) and TSP (Table 3) experiments demonstrate that LLMs can optimize without gradients or formal update rules. GPT-4 finds global optima for small-scale problems with fewer evaluations than exhaustive search. However, the paper is explicit that OPRO is **not designed to outperform specialized solvers** on large-scale mathematical problems—the goal is demonstrating feasibility, not state-of-the-art mathematical optimization (Section 3.2 Limitations).

**Claim 2: OPRO outperforms human-designed prompts**

**Strongly supported.** On GSM8K, OPRO achieves 80.2% vs. 71.8% baseline (Table 4). On BBH, OPRO outperforms "Let's think step by step." by over 5% on 19/23 tasks with PaLM 2-L scorer (Section 5.2.2). The up to 50% improvement claim is supported by word_sorting (54.4% vs. 4.0%). These are substantial, statistically meaningful improvements across multiple models and tasks.

**Claim 3: Trajectory-based learning is superior to alternative approaches**

**Supported.** The comparison with EvoPrompt (Figure 12) shows OPRO steadily improves while EvoPrompt degrades on GSM8K. The one-step vs. iterative comparison (Section 5.3) shows iterative optimization with trajectory substantially outperforms single-step generation (78.2% vs. 64.4% on GSM8K). Ablation studies confirm trajectory design choices matter (ordering, scores, exemplars).

**Claim 4: Optimized prompts transfer across datasets**

**Supported.** Table 6 shows GSM8K-optimized instructions improve MultiArith accuracy from 85.7% to 95.3% (PaLM 2-L scorer). This demonstrates generalization beyond the optimization training set, though the paper notes transfer is evaluated on datasets of the same domain (math reasoning).

**Claim 5: Self-optimization is possible**

**Supported.** Figure 4(b) and Table 4 show pre-trained PaLM 2-L optimizing its own prompts from 34.0% to 79.9%. This is a novel capability not demonstrated in prior work.

**Conditions and Trade-offs**

The experiments reveal important conditions under which OPRO succeeds or struggles:

| Condition | Effect on Performance |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Problem scale | Small-scale problems (TSP n≤20, linear regression near starting region): strong performance. Large-scale (TSP n=50, high-dimensional regression): performance degrades |
| Loss landscape smoothness | Smooth landscapes: good convergence. Bumpy landscapes (Rosenbrock): gets stuck |
| Initial instruction quality | Better initial instructions accelerate early convergence, but final performance similar after enough steps (Figure 9) |
| Temperature setting | Too low (0.0-0.5): lacks exploration, gets stuck. Too high (1.5-2.0): ignores trajectory, unstable. Optimal: 1.0 |
| Training set size | Too small (< tens of samples): severe overfitting. Adequate (3.5% GSM8K, 20% BBH): good generalization |

**Unresolved Questions**

The paper identifies several open questions (Section 7):
- How to reduce sensitivity to initialization?
- How to better balance exploration with exploitation?
- How to incorporate richer feedback about error cases beyond aggregated accuracy?
- How to reduce the training set size needed for prompt optimization?

These limitations are acknowledged transparently, and the experimental evidence supports the paper's core claims within the stated scope.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While OPRO demonstrates that LLMs can serve as derivative-free optimizers, the paper is transparent about significant constraints that limit its applicability. Understanding these limitations is critical for practitioners deciding when to deploy OPRO versus traditional optimization methods. We organize these constraints into four categories: core assumptions, scalability boundaries, optimization landscape sensitivities, and data requirements.

### 6.1 Core Assumptions and Dependencies

OPRO relies on several non-trivial assumptions that constrain its deployment scenarios.

**Assumption 1: The LLM Can Recognize Optimization Patterns from In-Context Examples**

The entire framework depends on the optimizer LLM's ability to infer improvement directions from solution-score pairs presented in natural language. This assumption breaks down in several documented scenarios:

- **Hallucinated calculations:** The optimizer LLM sometimes outputs incorrect function values without external tools. Appendix A reports cases where the model states "the function value at (5, 3) is 15" despite the true value being different. This occurs because the LLM attempts calculation internally rather than triggering reliable external tools.
  
- **Duplicate solution generation:** Despite explicit meta-instructions to "give me a new (w, b) pair that is different from all pairs above," the optimizer does not 100% reliably follow this constraint (Appendix A). The paper notes outputs are "almost guaranteed to be different" only when the model explicitly compares the new pair against all old pairs in its reasoning.

**Assumption 2: Score Magnitudes Are Meaningful to the LLM**

The ablation studies (Figure 7c, 7d) reveal that removing score values entirely—showing only ranked instructions—reduces performance. This confirms the LLM uses magnitude information, not just relative ordering. However, this creates a dependency on the scorer LLM providing consistent, calibrated scores. If the scorer's evaluation is noisy or inconsistent across optimization steps, the optimizer may learn spurious patterns.

**Assumption 3: The Optimization Trajectory Fits Within Context Limits**

OPRO keeps only the best 20 solutions in the meta-prompt to respect context length limits (Section 5.1). This is a hard constraint: for problems requiring longer histories to identify patterns, or problems where the solution representation itself is large (e.g., TSP with many nodes), the trajectory may not fit. The paper explicitly states:
> "The length limit of the LLM context window makes it hard to fit large-scale optimization problem descriptions in the prompt" (Section 3.2 Limitations).

### 6.2 Scalability and Computational Constraints

The most significant limitation is that OPRO does not scale well to large optimization problems.

**Mathematical Optimization Scalability**

Table 3 documents dramatic performance degradation as TSP problem size increases:

| Nodes (n) | GPT-4 Optimality Gap | Farthest Insertion Heuristic Gap |
|-----------|---------------------|----------------------------------|
| 10 | 0.0% | 3.2% |
| 15 | 0.2% | 1.2% |
| 20 | 1.4% | 0.2% |
| 50 | 11.0% | 9.8% |

At $n=50$, all LLMs fail to find optimal solutions (0/5 successes for GPT-4), while the Farthest Insertion heuristic succeeds with a 9.8% gap. The paper is explicit about this limitation:
> "OPRO is designed for neither outperforming the state-of-the-art gradient-based optimization algorithms for continuous mathematical optimization, nor surpassing the performance of specialized solvers for classical combinatorial optimization problems" (Section 3.2 Limitations).

**Why scalability fails:** Two factors contribute:
1. **Context window limits:** Large TSP instances require listing many node coordinates, consuming context space needed for the optimization trajectory.
2. **Optimization landscape complexity:** As problem size increases, the search space grows factorially, and the LLM cannot propose correct descent directions from trajectory patterns alone.

**Computational Cost of Iterative Optimization**

OPRO requires multiple LLM calls per optimization step. The default configuration generates 8 instructions per step for up to 200 steps (Section 5.1), meaning up to 1,600 optimizer LLM calls plus scorer LLM evaluations for each generated instruction. For GSM8K with ~261 training examples per evaluation, this translates to approximately 417,600 scorer forward passes (1,600 × 261) just for optimization—not including final test evaluation.

The ablation study in Figure 8 shows that generating too many instructions per step (16) reduces the total number of optimization steps possible within a fixed evaluation budget, limiting trajectory accumulation. This reveals a fundamental **compute-quality trade-off**: more exploration per step reduces the depth of optimization history the LLM can learn from.

### 6.3 Optimization Landscape Sensitivities

OPRO performs well on smooth optimization landscapes but struggles when the landscape is complex.

**Bumpy Loss Landscapes**

Appendix A documents failure on the Rosenbrock function $f(x, y) = (a-x)^2 + b(y-x^2)^2$ with $a=20$. The global optimum is at $(20, 400)$, but optimization often gets stuck around $(0, 0)$. Figure 13 visualizes why: the landscape has a narrow valley between these points. The LLM sees initial improvement by decreasing both $x$ and $y$ to 0, but then cannot navigate the valley toward the true optimum.

This reveals a critical constraint: **OPRO works best when the optimization landscape allows the LLM to infer consistent descent directions from past solutions.** When the landscape requires navigating narrow valleys or has many local optima, the LLM's pattern recognition from trajectory examples is insufficient.

**Linear Regression: Distance from Starting Region**

Table 2 shows that all LLMs require more steps and explorations as ground truth parameters move farther from the starting region:

| Setting | GPT-4 Steps | GPT-4 Unique Pairs Explored |
|---------|-------------|----------------------------|
| Within region $[10, 20] \times [10, 20]$ | 4.0 ± 1.5 | 17.2 ± 5.1 |
| Far outside (distance > 10) | 50.4 ± 18.8 | 116.4 ± 32.7 |

When ground truth is far outside the starting region, the LLM must explore more broadly before finding descent directions. This is compounded by the failure mode where "all $w$ values [in trajectory] are smaller than $w_{true}$ while all $b$ values are larger than $b_{true}$"—the LLM cannot infer the correct direction when past solutions point in quantitatively opposite directions from the optimum (Appendix A).

**Temperature Sensitivity**

Figure 10 reveals that OPRO operates within a narrow temperature range:

| Temperature | Behavior |
|-------------|----------|
| 0.0–0.5 | Gets stuck repeating same instruction for tens of steps (flat curves) |
| 1.0 (default) | Balanced exploration-exploitation |
| 1.5–2.0 | Ignores trajectory, unstable optimization curves |

This sensitivity means practitioners cannot simply increase temperature to escape local optima—doing so causes the LLM to disregard the optimization trajectory entirely, breaking the core learning mechanism.

### 6.4 Data and Generalization Requirements

**Training Set Size Requirements**

Prompt optimization requires a training set to compute accuracy scores that guide optimization. The paper states:
> "Currently the training set at least contains tens of samples, so that the optimized prompt does not severely overfit to the training samples" (Section 7).

The experiments use 3.5% of GSM8K training data (~261 examples) and 20% of BBH task data. While the paper demonstrates this is sufficient for good generalization, it identifies reducing this requirement as future work. For tasks with very limited labeled data, OPRO may not be applicable without modification.

**Overfitting Magnitude**

Section 5.4 documents that training accuracies exceed test accuracies by 5%–20% in many cases:

| Task | Scorer | Training Acc | Test Acc | Gap |
|------|--------|--------------|----------|-----|
| causal_judgement | PaLM 2-L | 84.8% | 58.0% | 26.8% |
| sports_understanding | PaLM 2-L | 98.0% | 88.0% | 10.0% |
| boolean_expressions | text-bison | 98.0% | 87.0% | 11.0% |

While Figure 11 shows validation accuracy tracks training accuracy (meaning higher training accuracy solutions still achieve higher test accuracy), the absolute gap remains. The paper suggests setting aside a larger training set and using early stopping may help, but these are not part of the default configuration.

**Model-Specific Instructions**

Table 1 and Table 4 reveal that different optimizer LLMs produce instructions of different styles:
- **PaLM 2-L-IT and text-bison:** Concise instructions (e.g., "Break this down.", "Let's work through this problem step-by-step:")
- **GPT-3.5-turbo and GPT-4:** Long, detailed instructions (e.g., "A little bit of arithmetic and a logical approach will help us quickly arrive at the solution to this problem.")

This creates a **transferability constraint**: an instruction optimized for one model may not transfer optimally to another. Section 5.2.4 demonstrates transfer across datasets of the same domain (GSM8K → MultiArith/AQuA), but cross-model transfer is not evaluated. Practitioners should optimize instructions separately for each target model.

**Error Case Utilization**

A significant weakness is that the optimizer LLM does not effectively use error cases to infer improvement directions. Section 7 reports:
> "We tried including error cases in the meta-prompt rather than randomly sampling from the training set at each optimization step, but the results are similar, indicating that the error cases alone are not informative enough for the optimizer LLM to grasp the cause of the wrong prediction."

This means OPRO optimizes based on aggregated accuracy scores rather than learning from specific failure modes. For tasks where understanding why solutions fail is critical for improvement, this is a fundamental limitation.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Future Directions

The paper identifies several unresolved questions that constrain OPRO's current applicability (Section 7):

| Open Question | Why It Matters |
|--------------|----------------|
| How to reduce sensitivity to initialization? | Figure 9 shows weaker starting points require more steps to converge. For time-constrained optimization, this matters. |
| How to better balance exploration with exploitation? | Temperature tuning helps but is coarse-grained. More adaptive exploration strategies could improve convergence. |
| How to incorporate richer feedback about error cases? | Current optimization uses only aggregated accuracy. Per-instance error signals could enable more targeted improvements. |
| How to reduce training set size requirements? | Tasks with limited labeled data cannot use OPRO without modification. |
| How to safeguard against harmful generated content? | The Ethics Statement acknowledges LLMs may generate harmful information; safety mechanisms are future work. |

**Comparison to Specialized Solvers**

It is critical to emphasize that OPRO is not positioned as a replacement for specialized optimization algorithms. The paper states explicitly:
> "OPRO is designed for neither outperforming the state-of-the-art gradient-based optimization algorithms for continuous mathematical optimization, nor surpassing the performance of specialized solvers for classical combinatorial optimization problems such as TSP" (Section 3.2 Limitations).

The contribution is demonstrating **feasibility and generalization** across problem types through natural language abstraction, not achieving state-of-the-art optimization performance on any single domain.

### 6.6 Summary: When to Use OPRO

Based on the limitations documented above, OPRO is appropriate when:

| Condition | Recommendation |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Gradient access unavailable** (API-only models) | ✓ Suitable—OPRO is designed for black-box optimization |
| **Small-scale optimization problems** (TSP n≤20, low-dimensional regression) | ✓ Suitable—LLMs find global optima efficiently |
| **Prompt optimization with adequate training data** (tens to hundreds of examples) | ✓ Suitable—demonstrated strong performance on GSM8K, BBH |
| **Large-scale mathematical optimization** (TSP n≥50, high-dimensional regression) | ✗ Not suitable—specialized solvers outperform significantly |
| **Bumpy loss landscapes** (Rosenbrock-type functions) | ✗ Not suitable—LLMs get stuck at suboptimal points |
| **Very limited training data** (< tens of samples) | ✗ Not suitable—severe overfitting risk |
| **Cross-model prompt transfer required** | ⚠️ Caution—optimize separately for each target model |
| **Error-case learning critical** | ⚠️ Caution—OPRO uses aggregated accuracy, not per-instance errors |

The trade-off is clear: OPRO sacrifices optimization performance on large-scale or complex landscapes in exchange for **accessibility** (no gradients needed), **flexibility** (natural language problem descriptions), and **generalization** (same framework across problem types). For prompt optimization specifically, where gradients are unavailable and the search space is discrete, this trade-off is favorable—the 8% improvement on GSM8K and up to 50% on BBH tasks demonstrate practical value despite the limitations.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

OPRO establishes a new paradigm for derivative-free optimization that fundamentally shifts how we approach problems lacking gradient access. This section examines how the work changes the optimization landscape, what research directions it opens, where it can be practically deployed, and guidance for practitioners deciding when to adopt this approach.

### 7.1 Landscape Shift: From Formal Specifications to Natural Language Optimization

OPRO changes the optimization landscape in three consequential ways:

**1. Democratization of Optimization Through Natural Language Abstraction**

Prior to OPRO, derivative-free optimization required either:
- Specialized algorithm design customized to each problem type (Section 1)
- Access to model weights for gradient-based methods (Section 6)
- Complex training setups for reinforcement learning approaches (Section 6)

OPRO removes these barriers by framing optimization as a language task. The paper demonstrates that the **same meta-prompt structure** works across fundamentally different domains—continuous linear regression, discrete TSP, and natural language prompts—without task-specific modifications (Section 3, 4). This means practitioners can optimize problems by describing them in text rather than deriving mathematical update rules.

**2. LLMs as General-Purpose Optimizers, Not Just Generators**

Prior LLM-based optimization work treated models primarily as prompt generators (APE, APO) or evolutionary operators (EvoPrompt) (Section 6). OPRO establishes that LLMs can serve as **full optimization engines** that:
- Learn optimization dynamics from in-context trajectory examples
- Recognize patterns like "decreasing parameter $w$ improved scores in recent steps" without explicit gradient signals
- Balance exploration and exploitation through temperature tuning (Figure 10)

This shifts the conceptual role of LLMs from passive content generators to active optimization agents capable of iterative improvement.

**3. Self-Optimization Without External Intervention**

The demonstration that PaLM 2-L can optimize its own prompts from 34.0% to 79.9% accuracy (Table 4, Figure 4b) establishes a new capability: **autonomous model improvement**. Prior work typically assumed:
- A separate, more capable optimizer model
- Human-designed initial prompts
- External feedback signals

OPRO shows models can iteratively improve their own task performance through self-generated optimization trajectories. This has implications for reducing human labor in prompt engineering and enabling models to adapt to new tasks without external intervention.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions that define a research agenda for LLM-based optimization (Section 7):

**1. Richer Error Feedback Beyond Aggregated Accuracy**

Current OPRO optimization uses only aggregated accuracy scores to guide improvement. The paper reports:
> "We tried including error cases in the meta-prompt rather than randomly sampling from the training set at each optimization step, but the results are similar, indicating that the error cases alone are not informative enough for the optimizer LLM to grasp the cause of the wrong prediction" (Section 7).

This suggests follow-up work should explore:
- **Per-instance error signals**: Instead of only showing accuracy percentages, provide specific examples where instructions failed and why
- **Error categorization**: Classify errors by type (e.g., calculation mistakes, reasoning errors, format violations) to help the optimizer identify patterns
- **Natural language feedback**: Incorporate explicit feedback on generated solutions, as suggested in Section 6: "We consider incorporating explicit natural language feedback on generated solutions for later optimization steps as future work."

**2. Reducing Training Set Requirements**

OPRO currently requires "at least tens of samples" to avoid severe overfitting (Section 7). The experiments use 3.5% of GSM8K (~261 examples) and 20% of BBH task data (Section 5.1). Follow-up research should investigate:
- **Few-shot optimization**: Can OPRO work with single-digit training examples?
- **Synthetic data augmentation**: Can additional training samples be generated to reduce overfitting risk?
- **Cross-task transfer**: Can optimization trajectories from one task inform optimization on related tasks?

**3. Better Exploration-Exploitation Balance**

The temperature ablation (Figure 10) reveals OPRO operates within a narrow range—temperature 0.0-0.5 causes stagnation, while 1.5-2.0 causes trajectory ignoring. Follow-up work could explore:
- **Adaptive temperature scheduling**: Adjust temperature based on optimization progress (increase when stuck, decrease when converging)
- **Diversity-aware sampling**: Explicitly encourage diverse solutions rather than relying solely on temperature
- **Hybrid approaches**: Combine OPRO's trajectory learning with explicit exploration strategies from evolutionary algorithms

**4. Reducing Initialization Sensitivity**

Figure 9 shows weaker starting points require more steps to converge. The paper notes: "one direction for future work is to accelerate convergence from weaker starting points" (Section 5.3). Research directions include:
- **Better initialization strategies**: Systematic methods for generating high-quality starting instructions
- **Warm-start from related tasks**: Use optimization trajectories from similar problems to bootstrap new optimization
- **Meta-learning optimization patterns**: Train optimizer LLMs to recognize optimization dynamics more quickly

**5. Safety and Harmful Content Mitigation**

The Ethics Statement acknowledges: "There is a peril that LLMs may generate harmful information that poses safety risks; how to safeguard model behavior remains valuable future work" (Ethics Statement). As OPRO enables autonomous prompt generation, follow-up work must address:
- **Content filtering**: Detect and reject harmful generated instructions
- **Constraint enforcement**: Ensure optimized prompts adhere to safety guidelines
- **Auditability**: Track optimization trajectories to understand how harmful content might emerge

**6. Scaling to Larger Problems**

OPRO fails on TSP with $n=50$ nodes while heuristics succeed (Table 3). The paper attributes this to context window limits and landscape complexity (Section 3.2 Limitations). Follow-up research should explore:
- **Hierarchical optimization**: Break large problems into sub-problems that fit within context limits
- **Trajectory compression**: Summarize optimization history to fit more information in limited context
- **Hybrid solver integration**: Combine OPRO with specialized solvers for different optimization phases

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

Based on the experimental results, OPRO is well-suited for several practical applications:

**1. Automated Prompt Engineering for Production LLM Systems**

The 8% improvement on GSM8K (80.2% vs. 71.8%) and up to 50% improvement on BBH tasks (Table 4, Table 7) demonstrate practical value for:
- **Customer service chatbots**: Optimize instructions for specific domains without manual prompt engineering
- **Content generation pipelines**: Discover instructions that maximize quality metrics for specific use cases
- **API-based LLM deployments**: Optimize prompts when only API access is available (no model weights)

**2. Cross-Dataset Prompt Transfer**

Table 6 shows GSM8K-optimized instructions improve MultiArith accuracy from 85.7% to 95.3% and AQuA from 44.9% to 54.3% (PaLM 2-L scorer). This enables:
- **Domain adaptation**: Optimize prompts on one dataset, deploy on related tasks
- **Reduced evaluation cost**: Optimize on small training sets, deploy on larger test distributions
- **Multi-task systems**: Single optimized instruction serving multiple related tasks

**3. Model-Specific Prompt Optimization**

Table 1 reveals different optimizers produce different instruction styles (PaLM 2-L-IT: concise; GPT models: detailed). This suggests:
- **Per-model optimization**: Optimize separate instructions for each target model in multi-model deployments
- **Model selection guidance**: Use optimization results to identify which model responds best to certain instruction types
- **Instruction tuning feedback**: Use discovered high-performing instructions to inform instruction-tuning data

**4. Black-Box Optimization for Non-Differentiable Objectives**

The linear regression and TSP experiments (Section 3) demonstrate OPRO can optimize without gradients. Applications include:
- **Hyperparameter optimization**: Optimize hyperparameters when objective functions are non-differentiable
- **System configuration**: Optimize system settings where performance can only be measured empirically
- **A/B test optimization**: Iteratively improve variants based on measured performance without gradient signals

**5. Autonomous Model Improvement**

The self-optimization result (PaLM 2-L optimizing itself from 34.0% to 79.9%) enables:
- **Self-improving agents**: Models that iteratively refine their own instructions for specific tasks
- **Reduced human labor**: Minimize manual prompt engineering for routine tasks
- **Continuous adaptation**: Models that adapt instructions as task distributions shift

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

**When to Prefer OPRO Over Alternatives**

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| **API-only LLM access** (no model weights) | ✓ Use OPRO | Gradient-based methods require weight access (Section 6) |
| **Discrete optimization** (prompts, configurations) | ✓ Use OPRO | Gradients undefined for discrete spaces |
| **Small-scale mathematical optimization** (TSP $n \leq 20$) | ✓ Use OPRO | GPT-4 finds global optima efficiently (Table 3) |
| **Prompt optimization with ≥ tens of training samples** | ✓ Use OPRO | 3.5% GSM8K (~261 examples) sufficient for generalization (Section 5.1) |
| **Large-scale mathematical optimization** (TSP $n \geq 50$) | ✗ Use specialized solvers | OPRO fails at $n=50$; Farthest Insertion succeeds (Table 3) |
| **Bumpy loss landscapes** (Rosenbrock-type) | ✗ Use gradient-based or specialized methods | OPRO gets stuck at suboptimal points (Appendix A, Figure 13) |
| **Very limited training data** (< tens of samples) | ✗ Use alternative approaches | Severe overfitting risk (Section 7) |
| **Cross-model prompt transfer required** | ⚠️ Optimize separately per model | Instructions are model-specific (Table 1, Table 4) |
| **Error-case learning critical** | ⚠️ OPRO uses aggregated accuracy only | Error cases alone not informative enough (Section 7) |

**Key Hyperparameters for Reproduction**

Based on ablation studies (Section 5.3), practitioners should use:

| Hyperparameter | Recommended Value | Impact of Deviation |
|---------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Optimizer temperature** | 1.0 | Lower: stagnation; Higher: ignores trajectory (Figure 10) |
| **Instructions per step** | 8 | Fewer: insufficient exploration; More: fewer optimization steps (Figure 8) |
| **Trajectory size** | 20 best solutions | Limited by context window; ascending order critical (Figure 7a, 7b) |
| **Exemplars** | 3 training examples | Zero: poor task understanding; More: distracts from trajectory (Figure 7e, 7f) |
| **Score presentation** | Integer (0-100) | Removing scores reduces performance (Figure 7c, 7d) |
| **Maximum steps** | 200 (prompt optimization) | Outstanding instructions often found earlier (Step 6 vs. Step 107, Section 5.2.1) |

**Integration Checklist**

Before deploying OPRO:

1. **Verify training set size**: Ensure at least tens of samples to avoid severe overfitting (Section 7)
2. **Select appropriate scorer format**: Pre-trained models use `A_begin`; instruction-tuned models use `Q_begin`/`Q_end` (Section 5.1)
3. **Set up validation monitoring**: Track validation accuracy alongside training to detect overfitting (Figure 11)
4. **Plan for model-specific optimization**: Optimize separate instructions for each target model (Table 1)
5. **Budget for compute**: Default configuration requires up to 1,600 optimizer calls plus scorer evaluations (Section 6.2)
6. **Implement safety filters**: Monitor generated instructions for harmful content (Ethics Statement)

**Code and Resource Availability**

The paper provides:
- **Code repository**: https://github.com/google-deepmind/opro (Abstract)
- **Model access**: text-bison via Google Cloud Vertex AI; GPT models via OpenAI API (Reproducibility Statement)
- **Benchmarks**: GSM8K and Big-Bench Hard are public datasets (Section 5.1)

Practitioners can reproduce results using the provided code with API access to the specified models. The meta-prompt templates are documented in Appendix C (Figures 19-22).

### 7.5 Summary: The OPRO Research Program

OPRO establishes a foundation for **language-based optimization** that extends beyond prompt engineering. The core insight—that LLMs can learn optimization dynamics from in-context trajectory examples—opens a research program with three pillars:

1. **Better feedback signals**: Move from aggregated accuracy to rich, per-instance error information
2. **Broader applicability**: Extend to larger problems, fewer training samples, and more complex landscapes
3. **Safer deployment**: Ensure autonomous optimization produces beneficial, aligned outputs

The 8% GSM8K improvement and up to 50% BBH gains demonstrate practical value today. The open questions—reducing initialization sensitivity, incorporating error feedback, scaling to larger problems—define a clear agenda for making LLM-based optimization more powerful, efficient, and broadly applicable. For practitioners working with API-only models or discrete optimization problems, OPRO provides an immediately deployable framework that outperforms human-designed solutions while requiring only natural language problem descriptions.
