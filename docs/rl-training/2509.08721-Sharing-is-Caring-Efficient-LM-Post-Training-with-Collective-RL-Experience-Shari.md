## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces Swarm sAmpling Policy Optimization (SAPO), a fully decentralized reinforcement learning algorithm that enables heterogeneous compute nodes to share decoded rollouts without synchronizing policy weights, thereby eliminating the communication bottlenecks and high costs of traditional distributed RL post-training. In controlled experiments using a swarm of eight Qwen2.5 0.5B models on the ReasoningGYM dataset, SAPO achieved cumulative reward gains of up to 94% over a no-sharing baseline, with the balanced 4 local / 4 external rollout configuration yielding the strongest performance. By facilitating collaborative training across thousands of diverse hardware nodes, SAPO democratizes access to efficient LM post-training while allowing successful reasoning strategies to propagate rapidly through the network.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of this paper, we must first examine the current landscape of language model (LM) post-training and the specific infrastructure bottlenecks that limit its scalability. The paper positions **Swarm sAmpling Policy Optimization (SAPO)** as a solution to the high costs and technical fragility associated with scaling Reinforcement Learning (RL) for LMs.

### The Challenge of Scaling RL Post-Training
Recent advancements, such as **DeepSeek-R1-Zero**, have demonstrated that RL can significantly enhance a model's complex reasoning capabilities without relying on supervised fine-tuning (Section 1 of the paper). However, effectively utilizing RL for LMs requires significant parallelization to scale up inference during the training process. 

In traditional distributed RL systems, multiple compute nodes (e.g., GPUs) work together to train a single policy. To ensure stability, these systems typically require **policy weight synchronization**. This means that after every update step, all nodes must communicate their gradient updates or model parameters to ensure every copy of the model remains identical. The paper's Introduction highlights that this approach introduces non-trivial technical challenges:
*   **Latency:** Communication overhead slows down training steps.
*   **Memory:** Storing optimizer states and gradients across clusters consumes significant resources.
*   **Reliability:** Large clusters are prone to failures, requiring carefully engineered infrastructure to remain stable.
*   **Financial Cost:** Orchestrating large GPU clusters is expensive.

These constraints create a barrier to entry, limiting efficient RL post-training to well-funded organizations with access to homogeneous, high-bandwidth hardware clusters.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
The paper's **Related Work (Section 2)** categorizes existing solutions into two main streams, both of which have limitations that SAPO aims to address.

#### 1. Centralized Distributed RL
Systems like those described by **Mistral-AI et al. (2025)** or **Wu et al. (2025)** focus on distributed systems that orchestrate large GPU clusters. 
*   **Mechanism:** These systems keep policy weights synchronized during training.
*   **Shortcoming:** As noted in the Introduction, these approaches are expensive and introduce communication bottlenecks. They often require **homogeneous hardware** (e.g., all nodes must have the same GPU type and memory capacity) to function efficiently. If one node fails or lags, it can stall the entire cluster.

#### 2. Structured Multi-Agent Methods
Research into multi-agent methods has explored ideas like **debate**, **specialization (role-playing)**, and **self-improvement**.
*   **Mechanism:** Multiple LMs independently answer queries and refine responses through iterative dialogue (e.g., **Multi-Agent Debate**). Some systems use separate agents for generation, verification, and refinement (e.g., **MALT**).
*   **Shortcoming:** While effective for inference or specific fine-tuning tasks, these approaches are often **orchestrated**. They require structured collaboration where agents are assigned specific roles or must communicate in rigid formats. The paper notes that SAPO differs by not aiming to produce specialized nodes or orchestrated collaboration. Instead, it allows nodes to operate independently while indirectly benefiting from shared experiences.

### The Gap: Heterogeneity and Decentralization
A critical gap exists between single-agent RL fine-tuning and structured multi-agent frameworks. 
*   **Single-Agent RL:** Efficient for one model but lacks the exploration benefits of diverse perspectives.
*   **Structured Multi-Agent:** Offers diverse exploration but requires heavy coordination and often homogeneous infrastructure.

There is a need for a method that allows **heterogeneous compute nodes** to collaborate without the overhead of weight synchronization or rigid orchestration. The paper emphasizes that swarms are most often implemented on local or edge devices (e.g., MacBooks), which typically run **Small LMs (SLMs)** defined as models with fewer than `$10B$` parameters. Existing centralized RL systems are not optimized for this fragmented, heterogeneous environment where nodes may have different hardware capabilities, network latencies, or even different model architectures.

### SAPO's Positioning
SAPO positions itself as a **bridge** between single-agent RL fine-tuning and structured multi-agent frameworks. It introduces a decentralized paradigm where:
*   **No Weight Synchronization:** Nodes manage their own policy models. They do not share weights, gradients, or optimizer states.
*   **Experience Sharing:** Nodes share **decoded rollouts** (e.g., in plain text). A rollout consists of a question, the model's generated answers, and the ground-truth solution.
*   **Heterogeneity:** Because only text is shared, the framework is independent of model architecture and hardware. A node running a `$0.5B$` parameter model can learn from rollouts generated by a different `$0.5B$` model on entirely different hardware.

The paper argues that by sampling rollouts "shared" across the network, successful reasoning strategies (or "Aha moments") can propagate, bootstrapping the learning process without the communication costs of weight synchronization. This approach allows the system to behave like a multi-agent setup where diverse models and abundant data enhance exploration, but without the infrastructure fragility of conventional distributed RL methods.

### Why This Matters
The motivation extends beyond technical efficiency to **accessibility and democratization**. 
*   **Scalability:** By avoiding synchronization bottlenecks, the system can scale to thousands of nodes (as demonstrated in the open-source demo with Gensyn community members).
*   **Resource Efficiency:** It enables training on consumer-grade hardware rather than requiring dedicated GPU clusters.
*   **Robustness:** The asynchronous nature means nodes can operate in silos if desired, or join/leave the swarm without disrupting the entire network.

In summary, the paper addresses the high cost and fragility of scaling RL post-training by shifting the unit of collaboration from **model weights** to **experiences (rollouts)**. This design choice enables a decentralized, heterogeneous swarm of Small LMs to train collectively, offering a practical path towards augmenting reasoning capabilities without the prohibitive infrastructure requirements of traditional distributed RL.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
SAPO is a decentralized reinforcement learning system where independent compute nodes train their own language models while exchanging completed reasoning attempts (rollouts) as plain text rather than synchronizing model weights. The system solves the scalability bottleneck of distributed RL by replacing expensive parameter synchronization with lightweight experience sharing, allowing heterogeneous devices to collaboratively improve without requiring identical hardware or continuous network connectivity.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The SAPO architecture consists of five major components operating in a continuous loop. First, each **Node** maintains its own policy model and local dataset of verifiable reasoning tasks. Second, the **Rollout Generator** produces multiple candidate answers for each question assigned to that node. Third, the **Sharing Protocol** broadcasts decoded rollouts (question, answer candidates, ground-truth solution, and verification metadata) to the swarm without transmitting model weights. Fourth, the **Training Set Constructor** at each node samples a mixture of locally-generated rollouts and externally-sampled rollouts from the shared pool, applying filtering rules to remove uninformative samples. Fifth, the **Policy Updater** computes rewards using a local verifier and applies a policy gradient algorithm (such as GRPO) to update the node's model parameters independently. Information flows unidirectionally from generation through sharing to sampling, with no backward propagation of gradients between nodes.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
- We begin by defining the swarm topology and node structure, establishing what each participant contributes and maintains locally.
- We then explain rollout generation and the sharing protocol, detailing what data is exchanged and in what format.
- We describe training set construction, including the critical balance between local and external samples and the filtering mechanisms.
- We present the policy update mechanism with specific hyperparameters from the controlled experiments.
- We conclude with the mathematical formulation of SAPO, providing formal notation for all previously described components.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This paper presents a methodological contribution: a decentralized reinforcement learning algorithm that enables collaborative post-training of language models through experience sharing rather than weight synchronization. The core idea is that nodes in a network can accelerate learning by sampling rollouts generated by other nodes, allowing successful reasoning strategies to propagate without the communication overhead of traditional distributed training.

**The Swarm Topology and Node Structure**

The system operates on a decentralized network of `$N$` nodes, which the paper refers to as a swarm, where each node operates independently in discrete time steps `$t \in [T]$` with `$T > 0$`. Each node `$n$` maintains its own dataset `$\mathcal{D}_n$` consisting of question-answer pairs, formally defined as `$\mathcal{D}_n := \{(q, y_q) \mid q \in \mathcal{Q}_n\}$`, where `$\mathcal{Q}_n$` represents the set of questions assigned to node `$n$` and `$y_q \in \mathcal{Y}_n$` denotes the ground-truth solution for question `$q$`. A critical requirement is that all tasks in `$\mathcal{D}_n$` must be verifiable, meaning their answers can be efficiently and algorithmically checked for correctness without human intervention. Each node also maintains metadata `$\mathcal{M}_n$` that specifies how each task in the dataset can be verified, ensuring that other nodes in the swarm can validate rollouts even if they did not generate the original question. Each node `$n$` holds a model, represented in reinforcement learning terminology as a policy `$\pi_n$`, which maps an input question to generated answers. The paper focuses on Small Language Models (SLMs) with fewer than `$10B$` parameters, as swarms are typically implemented on local or edge devices that cannot accommodate larger models. Importantly, the framework does not require all nodes to use the same model architecture or parameter count, enabling true heterogeneity across the swarm.

**Rollout Generation and Sharing Protocol**

During each training round, node `$n$` subsamples a batch of questions `$\mathcal{B}_n \subseteq \mathcal{Q}_n$` from its local dataset. For each question `$q \in \mathcal{B}_n$`, the node generates `$L_n$` candidate answers, which collectively form the rollout for that question. The rollout is formally denoted as `$\mathcal{R}_n(q) := \{a_n^1(q), \ldots, a_n^{L_n}(q)\}$`, where each `$a_n^i(q)$` represents one generated completion. In the controlled experiments described in Section 4, each agent generates exactly 8 completions per question, meaning `$L_n = 8$` for all `$n \in [8]$`. After generation, each node selects a subset `$\mathcal{S}_n \subseteq \mathcal{B}_n$` of its questions to share with the swarm. The shared data packet for each question `$q \in \mathcal{S}_n$` is denoted as `$\mathcal{C}_n(q) := (q, y_q, \mathcal{R}_n(q), \mathcal{M}_n)$`, containing the question, ground-truth answer, all generated rollouts, and verification metadata. A crucial design choice is that rollouts are shared in decoded format, such as plain text, rather than as token IDs or logits specific to a particular model's vocabulary. This enables individuals in the swarm to re-encode and compute token-level values as if the rollout was generated by their own policy, regardless of how unlikely those tokens might be under their current model. This decoded sharing mechanism is what enables heterogeneity: a node running one model architecture can learn from rollouts generated by a completely different architecture because the shared data is modality-agnostic text rather than model-specific parameters.

**Training Set Construction with Local and External Sampling**

After sharing rollouts, each node constructs its training set `$\mathcal{T}^n$` by combining locally-generated samples with externally-sampled rollouts from the swarm. The training set is formally defined as the union of two components: `$I_n$` samples drawn from the node's own rollouts and `$J_n$` samples drawn from rollouts shared by other nodes in the swarm. The mathematical formulation is:

$$
\mathcal{T}^n = \underbrace{\left\{ I_n\text{-many samples from } \bigcup_{q \in \mathcal{B}_n} \mathcal{C}_n(q) \right\}}_{\text{self-rollouts}} \cup \underbrace{\left\{ J_n\text{-many samples from } \bigcup_{m \neq n, q \in \mathcal{S}_m} \mathcal{C}_m(q) \right\}}_{\text{external rollouts}}
$$

Individual nodes have full control over the sampling methodology used for choosing between locally-generated and swarm-sampled rollouts, which is an important mechanism for allowing individuals to tailor and filter experiences being shared with their policy model. In the controlled experiments discussed in Section 5, all nodes first discard rollouts with zero advantage before uniformly sampling from the remaining swarm rollouts. This filtering step is critical because rollouts with zero advantage provide no learning signal and would waste training capacity if included. The paper evaluates four configurations varying the ratio of local to external rollouts: 8 local / 0 external (baseline), 6 local / 2 external, 4 local / 4 external, and 2 local / 6 external. In all configurations, the total number of training samples per round is fixed at 8, ensuring comparability across setups. The baseline configuration with `$J_n = 0$` reduces node `$n$`'s training to standard RL fine-tuning without any experience sharing. SAPO gives agents the flexibility to subsample from a larger pool and remove uninformative zero-advantage samples, which the baseline cannot do because it only has access to its own generated rollouts.

**Policy Update Mechanism and Hyperparameters**

After constructing the training set `$\mathcal{T}^n$`, node `$n$` uses its local reward model `$\rho_n$` to compute rewards over all samples in `$\mathcal{T}^n$`. The node then updates its policy `$\pi_n$` using any policy gradient algorithm, such as Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) or Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). In the controlled experiments, the authors used GRPO to update each node's policy. Based on initial experiments and findings identified in DAPO (Yu et al., 2025), training was more efficient without the KL-divergence penalty, so its weight was set to zero. For clipping, the authors used asymmetric thresholds with `$\epsilon_{\text{low}} = 0.2$` (lower ratio bound) and `$\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.28$` (upper ratio bound), which differs from the symmetric clipping typically used in standard PPO. Training ran for 2000 rounds and used the Adam optimizer with default hyperparameters, including a learning rate of 0.001. For each node and task, the system used flexible, rule-based verifiers provided by the ReasoningGYM dataset. If the task-specific verifier was able to parse the correct answer from a completion, it assigned a reward of 1; otherwise, it assigned 0. Interestingly, the authors initially added a formatting reward but soon removed it because experience sharing in SAPO made it unnecessary: knowledge about the correct formatting expected by ReasoningGYM's verifiers spread throughout the swarm almost immediately without needing an explicit formatting reward signal. This observation demonstrates the propagation effect central to SAPO's design: once one node discovers the correct output format, that knowledge propagates through shared rollouts to all other nodes.

**Mathematical Formulation of the SAPO Algorithm**

The complete SAPO algorithm can be summarized mathematically as follows. For each node `$n \in [N]$`, given dataset `$\mathcal{D}_n$`, metadata `$\mathcal{M}_n$`, policy `$\pi_n$`, reward model `$\rho_n$`, policy update algorithm, number of local samples `$I_n$`, and number of external samples `$J_n$`, the algorithm proceeds through training rounds. In each round `$t$`, each node samples a batch of questions `$\mathcal{B}_n \leftarrow \text{SampleBatch}(\mathcal{Q}_n)$`. For each question `$q \in \mathcal{B}_n$`, the node generates rollouts `$\mathcal{R}_n(q) \leftarrow \{a_n^1(q), \ldots, a_n^{L_n}(q)\}$`. The node then selects a subset `$\mathcal{S}_n \leftarrow \text{SelectSubset}(\mathcal{B}_n)$` and communicates `$\{\mathcal{C}_n(q) \mid q \in \mathcal{S}_n\}$` to the swarm. The training set is assembled by sampling `$I_n$` samples from local rollouts and `$J_n$` samples from external rollouts: `$\mathcal{T}^n \leftarrow \text{SampleSelf}(\{\mathcal{C}_n(q) \mid q \in \mathcal{B}_n\}, I_n) \cup \text{SampleExternal}(\bigcup_{m \neq n} \{\mathcal{C}_m(q) \mid q \in \mathcal{S}_m\}, J_n)$`. Finally, the policy is updated via `$\pi_n \leftarrow \text{PolicyGradient}(\pi_n, \rho_n, \mathcal{T}^n)$`. This process can be fully decentralized and run in parallel across all nodes, with no requirement for synchronization barriers or weight averaging.

**Design Choices and Rationale**

The decision to share decoded rollouts rather than model weights addresses the fundamental bottleneck of traditional distributed RL. Weight synchronization requires all nodes to have compatible model architectures and sufficient bandwidth to transmit large parameter sets frequently. By contrast, sharing text rollouts requires orders of magnitude less bandwidth and imposes no architectural constraints. The decision to allow nodes full control over sampling methodology enables heterogeneity in learning strategies: some nodes might prioritize high-reward rollouts, while others might explore diverse reasoning paths. The filtering of zero-advantage rollouts before sampling is a critical optimization that prevents the shared pool from being contaminated with uninformative training data. The asymmetric clipping thresholds (`$\epsilon_{\text{low}} = 0.2$`, `$\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.28$`) reflect empirical tuning that allows larger positive updates while constraining negative updates more conservatively, which the authors found more stable for this collaborative setting. The removal of the KL-divergence penalty follows findings from DAPO that this constraint can limit exploration in RL post-training for reasoning tasks. The fixed total sample count ( `$I + J = 8$` ) across all configurations ensures that performance differences arise from the composition of training data rather than from varying training compute per round.

**Controlled Experiment Configuration Details**

The controlled experiments used a swarm of eight Qwen2.5 models, each with 0.5B parameters, implemented in PyTorch and running within Docker containers. Docker Compose scripts orchestrated the containers, enabling scalable deployment across multiple GPUs. Multi-GPU parallelism was managed with PyTorch's distributed package, assigning one GPU per agent and allowing NCCL to facilitate communication in the swarm. The ReasoningGYM dataset generated problems on demand in nine domains: base_conversion, basic_arithmetic, arc_1d, bf (Brainf*ck programs), propositional_logic, fraction_simplification, decimal_arithmetic, calendar_arithmetic, and binary_matrix. At each training round, every agent randomly sampled a set of specialties with replacement from this list and received one ReasoningGYM question per specialty. The authors emphasize that agents were generalists, receiving questions from all specialties with equal probability rather than specializing in specific domains. The backend used was GenRL, Gensyn's decentralized modular framework designed for scalable multi-agent multi-stage reinforcement learning, which integrates seamlessly with ReasoningGYM and offers out-of-the-box access to over 100 procedurally generated verifiable reasoning tasks.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the paper's most significant contributions, distinguishing between fundamental architectural innovations and empirical insights gained from the experiments. While the technical mechanics of SAPO were detailed in Section 3, the following points explain *why* these mechanics matter and what they reveal about collaborative reinforcement learning (RL) at scale.

### 1. Fundamental Innovation: Decoupling Policy Weights from Experience Sharing
The most profound architectural contribution of SAPO is the decoupling of **policy weight synchronization** from **collaborative learning**. Traditional distributed RL systems (discussed in Section 2) require nodes to synchronize gradients or model parameters to ensure consistency. This mandates homogeneous hardware and high-bandwidth connections, creating a bottleneck that scales poorly with network size.

SAPO inverts this paradigm by synchronizing **decoded rollouts** (plain text experiences) instead of weights.
*   **Why this is innovative:** It shifts the communication payload from gigabytes of floating-point parameters to kilobytes of text. This reduces bandwidth requirements by orders of magnitude.
*   **Significance:** This design enables **heterogeneity**. As noted in Section 3, nodes can run different model architectures or operate on different hardware (e.g., MacBooks vs. GPUs) because they only need to agree on the text format of the rollout, not the internal parameter structure. This transforms the training infrastructure from a rigid cluster into a flexible swarm, allowing participation from edge devices that were previously excluded from distributed RL training.

### 2. Empirical Insight: The Non-Monotonic "Sweet Spot" of Experience Sharing
A critical finding from the controlled experiments (Section 5) is that more experience sharing is not always better. The relationship between external rollouts and performance is non-monotonic, revealing a stability-exploration trade-off.

*   **The Optimal Balance:** The **4 local / 4 external** configuration achieved the highest cumulative reward, yielding a **94% improvement** over the baseline (no sharing). This is explicitly shown in **Figure 1**, where the 4/4 setup outperforms both the baseline and the higher-sharing configurations.
*   **The Instability Threshold:** When external reliance increased to **2 local / 6 external**, performance became oscillatory. **Figure 2** illustrates that this configuration shows strong oscillations as training progresses, unlike the smoother curves of the 4/4 setup.
*   **Mechanism of Failure:** The paper attributes this instability to two network effects:
    1.  **Quality Dilution:** When agents contribute too few local rollouts relative to what they consume, the shared pool's quality diminishes.
    2.  **Catastrophic Forgetting:** High-performing agents relying too heavily on external data may overwrite their specialized knowledge with lower-quality external samples, leading to "steep learning and forgetting behavior."

This insight is crucial for practitioners: SAPO is not a "set and forget" system. The ratio of `$I_n$` (local) to `$J_n$` (external) samples must be tuned to balance the benefits of diverse exploration against the risk of destabilizing the policy with noisy external data.

### 3. Emergent Behavior: Implicit Knowledge Propagation ("Aha Moments")
SAPO enables a form of implicit teaching where specific reasoning strategies or formatting conventions propagate through the swarm without explicit reward signals. This phenomenon is described in the paper as **"Aha moments"** spreading across the network.

*   **Evidence:** In **Section 4.3**, the authors note that they initially added a **formatting reward** to ensure models outputted answers in a way the verifier could parse. However, they "soon removed it."
*   **Why it works:** Because nodes share decoded rollouts that include the ground-truth solution and the generated answer, a node that discovers the correct format generates high-reward rollouts. When other nodes sample these rollouts, they re-encode the text and compute token-level values. Even if the format was unlikely under their current policy, the high reward signal associated with that text structure reinforces the behavior.
*   **Significance:** This reduces the engineering burden of reward modeling. In traditional RL, every constraint (like output format) usually requires a specific reward term. In SAPO, successful constraints discovered by *any* node can implicitly bootstrap the rest of the swarm, reducing the need for dense, hand-crafted reward functions.

### 4. Scalability Nuance: Model Capacity Dependence
The open-source demo results (Section 6) reveal that SAPO's benefits are not uniform across all model sizes. This is a critical nuance for deploying swarm training in real-world scenarios.

*   **The Finding:** As shown in **Figure 3**, **Qwen2.5 models with 0.5B parameters** showed statistically significant performance gains from swarm participation after approximately **175 normalized rounds**. In contrast, stronger models like **Qwen3 with 0.6B parameters** achieved similar performance in and out of the swarm.
*   **Interpretation:** The paper hypothesizes that SAPO's benefits are most pronounced for **mid-capacity models** that can actively "absorb" and propagate diverse rollouts. Larger or more capable models may already possess sufficient internal reasoning capacity that the marginal gain from external rollouts is negligible, or they may be less susceptible to the exploration benefits provided by the swarm.
*   **Implication:** This suggests SAPO is particularly well-suited for **Small Language Models (SLMs)** running on edge devices, aligning with the paper's focus on democratizing AI. It may be less cost-effective to deploy SAPO for large models unless the sampling strategies are improved to filter out low-quality rollouts, as noted in the discussion of the demo's uniform random sampling limitation.

### 5. Methodological Contribution: Filtering as a Control Mechanism
While not a new algorithm in itself, the paper highlights **filtering** as a critical control mechanism for decentralized RL. In the controlled experiments (Section 5), nodes discarded rollouts with **zero advantage** before sampling from the swarm.

*   **Why this matters:** In a decentralized network, nodes cannot trust the quality of incoming data. Without filtering, the shared pool would be contaminated with uninformative samples (rollouts that provide no gradient signal).
*   **Innovation:** By allowing individual nodes to apply local filtering rules (e.g., advantage thresholding) before incorporating external data, SAPO provides a defense against noise without requiring a central verifier. This contrasts with centralized RL where data quality is often managed globally. The paper notes in Section 6 that the open-source demo *lacked* this filtering (using uniform random sampling), which likely contributed to the overrepresentation of useless rollouts and the muted performance gains for larger models. This highlights filtering as a key lever for future optimization.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the empirical evidence supporting SAPO's claims. We analyze both the controlled experiments (Section 5 of the paper) and the large-scale open-source demo (Section 6), evaluating whether the results convincingly demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness, identifying conditions under which it succeeds or fails, and assessing the robustness of the findings.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The paper employs a two-tiered evaluation strategy: tightly controlled experiments for isolating variables, and a large-scale open-source demo for real-world validation.

#### Controlled Experiment Setup

The controlled experiments were designed to isolate the effect of experience sharing while holding all other variables constant.

- **Model Architecture:** Eight instances of Qwen2.5 with `$0.5B$` parameters each (Section 4).
- **Infrastructure:** Models ran within Docker containers, with one GPU per agent. PyTorch's distributed package managed multi-GPU parallelism, and NCCL facilitated swarm communication.
- **Training Duration:** `$2000$` rounds per configuration.
- **Optimizer:** Adam with default hyperparameters, including a learning rate of `$0.001$`.
- **Policy Update Algorithm:** GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) with KL-divergence penalty weight set to zero and asymmetric clipping thresholds (`$\epsilon_{\text{low}} = 0.2$`, `$\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.28$`).

#### Dataset: ReasoningGYM

All experiments used the ReasoningGYM dataset (Stojanovski et al., 2025), which generates verifiable reasoning problems on demand across nine domains:

| Specialty | Domain Type |
|-----------|-------------|
| `base_conversion` | Numeric |
| `basic_arithmetic` | Numeric |
| `arc_1d` | Abstract |
| `bf` (Brainf*ck) | Algorithmic |
| `propositional_logic` | Symbolic |
| `fraction_simplification` | Numeric |
| `decimal_arithmetic` | Numeric |
| `calendar_arithmetic` | Symbolic |
| `binary_matrix` | Abstract |

This selection ensures evaluation across symbolic, numeric, and abstract reasoning domains. At each training round, every agent randomly sampled specialties **with replacement** and received one question per specialty. Agents were **generalists**, receiving questions from all specialties with equal probability rather than specializing.

#### Reward Mechanism

The paper used rule-based verifiers provided by ReasoningGYM:
- **Correct answer parsed:** Reward = `$1$`
- **Incorrect or unparseable:** Reward = `$0$`
- **Partial credit:** Available for edge cases in specific verifiers (footnote 4, Section 4.3)

Notably, the authors initially added a **formatting reward** but removed it after observing that correct formatting spread through the swarm via experience sharing without explicit reward signals (Section 4.3).

#### Baselines and Configurations

The paper evaluates four configurations, varying the ratio of local (`$I_n$`) to external (`$J_n$`) rollouts while keeping the total fixed at `$8$` samples per round:

| Configuration | Local Rollouts | External Rollouts | Description |
|---------------|----------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Baseline | 8 | 0 | Standard RL fine-tuning (no sharing) |
| Config A | 6 | 2 | Low external sharing |
| Config B | 4 | 4 | Balanced sharing |
| Config C | 2 | 6 | High external sharing |

This design ensures that performance differences arise from **data composition** rather than varying training compute per round.

#### Open-Source Demo Setup

The large-scale demo involved thousands of Gensyn community members contributing training runs across diverse hardware and model configurations. Key characteristics:

- **Ephemeral Environment:** Nodes joined, left, or restarted unpredictably (Section 6, footnote 5).
- **Evaluation Protocol:** After each round, nodes submitted answers to a central "judge" that scored them using ReasoningGYM verifiers (pass@1 evaluation).
- **Sampling Strategy:** Unlike controlled experiments, the demo used **uniform random sampling without filtering**, which the authors acknowledge as a limitation.
- **Normalization:** Rounds were normalized based on total participation time per node due to the ephemeral nature of the swarm.

### 5.2 Controlled Experiment Results

The controlled experiments provide the strongest evidence for SAPO's effectiveness, with clear quantitative improvements over the baseline.

#### Cumulative Reward Gains

**Figure 1** displays reward trajectories for all eight agents across the four configurations. The paper reports the following cumulative rewards (total reward accumulated across all agents and rounds):

| Configuration | Cumulative Reward | Improvement vs. Baseline |
|---------------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| 8 local / 0 external (Baseline) | 561.79 | — |
| 6 local / 2 external | 854.43 | 52% |
| **4 local / 4 external** | **1093.31** | **94%** |
| 2 local / 6 external | 945.87 | 68% |

> "Overall, the 4 local / 4 external scheme delivers the strongest performance, with a 94% improvement over the baseline." (Section 5)

This **94% improvement** is the paper's headline result. It demonstrates that balanced experience sharing nearly doubles the learning efficiency compared to isolated training.

#### Reward Trajectory Analysis

**Figure 1** reveals several important patterns:

- **Peak Rewards:** Both the 4/4 and 2/6 configurations achieve the highest peak rewards, clearly outperforming the baseline.
- **Consistency:** The 4/4 configuration maintains high rewards more consistently across rounds, while the 2/6 configuration shows greater variance.
- **Baseline Performance:** The no-sharing baseline shows the lowest rewards throughout training, confirming that experience sharing provides measurable benefits.

#### Smoothed Average Rewards

**Figure 2** presents agent-averaged rewards smoothed with a moving average window of `$100$` samples. The paper explains this smoothing rationale:

> "Since the policy parameters change slower than individual training steps, the moving average effectively averages rewards as if the policy were frozen. As a result, the smoothed curve serves as a reasonable estimate of the expected average reward across tasks." (Section 5)

Key observations from **Figure 2**:

- The 4/4 configuration **consistently achieves higher expected average reward** than the baseline across nearly all training rounds.
- The 4/4 configuration outperforms the 6/2 configuration in nearly all rounds.
- The 4/4 configuration surpasses the 2/6 configuration for most rounds, though the difference is smaller than in other comparisons.
- **Confidence Intervals:** The figure includes confidence intervals given by the minimum and maximum across agents, showing that the 4/4 configuration's advantage holds across all eight agents, not just on average.

#### Instability in High-Sharing Configurations

A critical finding is that excessive reliance on external rollouts destabilizes learning. The 2/6 configuration shows **strong oscillations** as training progresses, unlike the smoother curves of the 4/4 setup. The paper attributes this to two network effects:

1. **Quality Dilution:** "When agents draw many rollouts from the swarm but collectively contribute too few, the quality of the shared pool diminishes." (Section 5)
2. **Catastrophic Forgetting:** "When high-performing agents overly rely on external rollouts, their progress can be adversely affected by the answers of worse-performing agents." (Section 5)

> "Taken together, these effects lead to steep learning and forgetting behavior that explains the oscillatory pattern." (Section 5)

The paper emphasizes that these oscillations reflect **meaningful large-scale training dynamics** rather than task-related randomness, since the moving average window smooths out task-level idiosyncrasy.

#### Filtering Mechanism Impact

An important methodological detail is that in the controlled experiments, all nodes **discarded rollouts with zero advantage** before sampling from the swarm. This filtering step is critical because:

- Rollouts with zero advantage provide no learning signal.
- The baseline cannot perform this filtering because it only has access to its own generated rollouts.
- SAPO gives agents the flexibility to subsample from a larger pool and remove uninformative samples.

This filtering likely contributes to the 4/4 configuration's stability compared to the 2/6 configuration, as it prevents low-quality external data from contaminating the training set.

### 5.3 Open-Source Demo Results

The large-scale demo provides real-world validation but also reveals important limitations and model-dependent effects.

#### Statistical Significance Threshold

**Figure 3** shows the performance comparison between swarm-trained and isolated models in the open-source demo. The figure includes regions marked in red where the adjusted p-value is greater than `$0.05$`, indicating non-significant differences.

> "After a certain number of rounds, in this case approximately 175, the performance per round of the models in the swarm significantly exceeds that of the model trained in isolation." (Figure 3 caption, Section 6)

This **175 normalized rounds** threshold indicates that swarm benefits accumulate over time and require sufficient exposure to external rollouts before becoming statistically detectable.

#### Model Capacity Dependence

A crucial finding from the demo is that SAPO's benefits are **model-dependent**:

| Model | Parameters | Swarm Benefit |
|-------|------------|---------------|
| Qwen2.5 | 0.5B | **Significant improvement** after ~175 rounds |
| Qwen3 | 0.6B | **Similar performance** in and out of swarm |

> "Interestingly, by contrast, stronger models such as Qwen3 with 0.6B parameters achieved similar performance in and out of the swarm, suggesting that SAPO's benefits are most pronounced for mid-capacity models that can actively 'absorb' and propagate diverse rollouts." (Section 6)

This finding has important implications:
- SAPO is particularly well-suited for **Small Language Models (SLMs)** running on edge devices.
- Larger or more capable models may not benefit as much, possibly because they already possess sufficient internal reasoning capacity.
- The marginal gain from external rollouts diminishes as model capacity increases.

#### Sampling Strategy Limitation

The authors acknowledge a significant limitation in the demo's methodology:

> "It is important to note that, in this demo, models selected rollouts from the swarm using straightforward uniform random sampling without any filtering. This caused rollouts without useful reward signals to be overrepresented in the swarm." (Section 6)

This contrasts with the controlled experiments, where zero-advantage rollouts were filtered before sampling. The authors hypothesize that **better sampling strategies** could enable more performant models to benefit from swarm participation. This is a critical insight for future deployments: filtering is not optional for optimal performance.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment

#### Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

The paper makes three primary claims, which we evaluate against the experimental evidence:

| Claim | Evidence | Assessment |
|-------|----------|------------|
| SAPO achieves up to 94% reward gains over baseline | Figure 1: 1093.31 vs. 561.79 cumulative reward | **Strongly Supported** — Clear quantitative evidence in controlled experiments |
| Balanced sharing (4/4) outperforms extreme configurations | Figure 2: 4/4 consistently exceeds 2/6 and 6/2 | **Supported** — Smoothed curves and confidence intervals confirm stability |
| Swarm training accelerates learning for SLMs | Figure 3: Qwen2.5 0.5B shows significant gains after 175 rounds | **Conditionally Supported** — Benefit is model-dependent; Qwen3 0.6B showed no significant gain |

#### Strengths of the Experimental Design

1. **Fixed Total Samples:** Keeping `$I + J = 8$` across all configurations ensures that performance differences arise from data composition rather than compute budget.
2. **Multiple Configurations:** Testing four ratios (8/0, 6/2, 4/4, 2/6) reveals the non-monotonic relationship between sharing and performance.
3. **Confidence Intervals:** Figure 2 includes min/max bounds across agents, showing that results hold across all nodes, not just on average.
4. **Two-Tier Evaluation:** Controlled experiments isolate variables, while the open-source demo validates real-world applicability.
5. **Diverse Task Domains:** Nine ReasoningGYM specialties ensure evaluation across symbolic, numeric, and abstract reasoning.

#### Limitations and Failure Cases

1. **Model Size Constraint:** All controlled experiments used `$0.5B$` parameter models. The paper does not test SAPO on models larger than `$0.6B$` in controlled settings, limiting generalizability to larger LMs.
2. **Demo Sampling Limitation:** The open-source demo lacked the filtering mechanism used in controlled experiments, likely contributing to muted performance gains for larger models.
3. **Oscillation in 2/6 Configuration:** The high-sharing configuration showed instability, indicating that SAPO requires careful tuning of the local/external ratio.
4. **Heterogeneity Not Fully Explored:** While the paper emphasizes heterogeneity as a key feature, the controlled experiments used **identical models** (eight Qwen2.5 0.5B instances). The open-source demo included diverse models but lacked controlled comparison.
5. **Short Training Horizon:** `$2000$` rounds may not capture long-term convergence behavior or potential degradation over extended training.

#### Robustness Checks

The paper includes several robustness checks:

- **Moving Average Smoothing:** Figure 2 uses a `$100$`-sample window to distinguish training dynamics from task-level randomness.
- **Agent-Level Variance:** Confidence intervals in Figure 2 show min/max across all eight agents.
- **Statistical Testing:** Figure 3 includes adjusted p-values to identify statistically significant differences.
- **Multiple Configurations:** Testing four sharing ratios reveals the optimal balance rather than assuming more sharing is always better.

However, some robustness checks are **missing**:

- No ablation on the filtering mechanism (comparing filtered vs. unfiltered sampling in controlled settings).
- No ablation on the number of nodes (all controlled experiments used exactly 8 nodes).
- No ablation on model architecture (all controlled experiments used Qwen2.5).
- No long-term stability analysis beyond 2000 rounds.

#### Trade-offs and Conditional Success

The experiments reveal that SAPO's success is **conditional** on several factors:

| Factor | Optimal Setting | Risk if Misconfigured |
|--------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Local/External Ratio | 4/4 (balanced) | 2/6 causes oscillations; 8/0 misses sharing benefits |
| Filtering | Discard zero-advantage rollouts | Unfiltered sampling overrepresents useless data |
| Model Capacity | Mid-capacity SLMs (e.g., 0.5B) | Larger models (e.g., 0.6B Qwen3) show diminished returns |
| Training Duration | >175 rounds for significance | Early rounds show no statistically significant difference |

> "On the other hand, since the 4 local / 4 external configuration outperforms the 2 local / 6 external case, we find that relying too heavily on external rollouts can actually hinder performance." (Section 5)

This trade-off is central to understanding SAPO: it is not a universal improvement but a **context-dependent optimization** that requires tuning the sharing ratio to the specific swarm composition and model capacity.

### 5.5 Summary of Experimental Findings

The experimental evidence convincingly supports SAPO's core claim that balanced experience sharing improves RL post-training efficiency for Small Language Models. The **94% cumulative reward improvement** in controlled experiments is substantial and well-documented. However, the benefits are **not universal**:

- **Model-dependent:** Larger models (Qwen3 0.6B) showed no significant swarm benefit in the demo.
- **Ratio-sensitive:** The 2/6 configuration demonstrated that excessive sharing causes instability.
- **Filtering-dependent:** The demo's lack of filtering likely limited performance gains.

These findings suggest that SAPO is best deployed with:
1. **Mid-capacity SLMs** (around `$0.5B$` parameters).
2. **Balanced sharing ratios** (approximately 50% local, 50% external).
3. **Advantage-based filtering** to remove uninformative rollouts.
4. **Sufficient training duration** (>175 rounds) for benefits to accumulate.

The paper's experiments are rigorous within their scope but leave open questions about scalability to larger models, long-term stability beyond 2000 rounds, and optimal configurations for highly heterogeneous swarms. These gaps represent promising directions for future research, as acknowledged in the paper's Conclusion and Future Directions section.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While SAPO demonstrates compelling improvements in controlled settings, the paper acknowledges several important limitations and trade-offs that constrain its applicability. This section examines the assumptions underlying the approach, scenarios it does not address, computational and scalability constraints, and open questions that remain unresolved.

### 6.1 Core Assumptions and Their Implications

**Verifiable Rewards Requirement**

SAPO fundamentally relies on tasks having **algorithmically verifiable answers**. Section 3.1 states:

> "Importantly, we require that tasks in $\mathcal{D}_n$ are verifiable (i.e. their answers can be efficiently and algorithmically checked for correctness)..."

This assumption enables the use of rule-based verifiers (like ReasoningGYM's) that assign binary rewards (1 for correct, 0 for incorrect). However, this constrains SAPO's applicability to domains where:
- Ground-truth solutions exist and can be programmatically checked
- Partial credit schemes are well-defined (footnote 4 in Section 4.3 mentions exceptions for edge cases)
- Human preference-based tasks (e.g., creative writing, subjective evaluation) cannot be directly supported without additional reward modeling infrastructure

This is a significant limitation compared to **RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback)**, which can optimize for subjective human preferences. SAPO aligns more closely with **RLVR (RL with Verifiable Rewards)** paradigms, limiting its use cases to reasoning, mathematics, code generation, and similar domains with objective correctness criteria.

**Compatible Rollout Modalities**

Section 3.1 footnote 1 acknowledges:

> "An example of a multimodal swarm is one in which some nodes only generate images while others only generate language. In practice, since nodes filter samples from the swarm locally, the assumption about modalities can be omitted and these rollouts in different modalities would simply be ignored when incompatible."

While the paper claims modality-agnosticism, this requires nodes to **locally filter incompatible rollouts**. In highly heterogeneous swarms (e.g., mixing text and image generators), a significant portion of shared data may be discarded, reducing sample efficiency. The controlled experiments did not test multimodal scenarios, leaving this claim unvalidated.

**Local Reward Model Availability**

Each node must maintain its own reward model or verifier ($\rho_n$ in Section 3.2). While ReasoningGYM provides "off-the-shelf" verifiers, deploying SAPO in novel domains requires:
- Developing task-specific verifiers for each node
- Ensuring verifier consistency across the swarm (otherwise, nodes may optimize for conflicting reward signals)

The paper does not address what happens when verifiers disagree on rollout quality—a plausible scenario in heterogeneous swarms with different verification standards.

### 6.2 Stability-Exploration Trade-off

**The Oscillation Problem**

A critical trade-off emerges between **exploration benefits** from external rollouts and **training stability**. Section 5 explicitly documents this:

> "On the other hand, since the 4 local / 4 external configuration outperforms the 2 local / 6 external case, we find that relying too heavily on external rollouts can actually hinder performance."

The **2 local / 6 external** configuration showed strong oscillations in Figure 2, which the paper attributes to two network effects:

1. **Quality Dilution**: "When agents draw many rollouts from the swarm but collectively contribute too few, the quality of the shared pool diminishes."
2. **Catastrophic Forgetting**: "When high-performing agents overly rely on external rollouts, their progress can be adversely affected by the answers of worse-performing agents."

This reveals a **tragedy of the commons** dynamic: if all nodes maximize external sampling (consuming more than they contribute), the shared pool degrades. The optimal 4/4 balance requires coordination or implicit equilibrium, but the paper does not propose mechanisms for nodes to dynamically adjust their $I_n$/$J_n$ ratios based on swarm conditions.

**No Adaptive Sampling Strategy**

The controlled experiments used **fixed ratios** (8/0, 6/2, 4/4, 2/6) throughout all 2000 rounds. Section 7 (Future Directions) acknowledges this limitation:

> "A promising direction is to develop meta-strategies for adaptively balancing local vs. shared rollouts, or for strategically filtering swarm samples."

This is a significant gap. In practice, the optimal ratio may change during training:
- Early rounds might benefit from more external rollouts (rapid knowledge propagation)
- Later rounds might require more local rollouts (policy stabilization)

Without adaptive mechanisms, practitioners must manually tune the ratio—a non-trivial hyperparameter that depends on swarm size, model capacity, and task difficulty.

### 6.3 Model Capacity Dependence

**Diminished Returns for Larger Models**

Section 6 presents a crucial limitation:

> "Interestingly, by contrast, stronger models such as Qwen3 with 0.6B parameters achieved similar performance in and out of the swarm, suggesting that SAPO's benefits are most pronounced for mid-capacity models that can actively 'absorb' and propagate diverse rollouts."

**Figure 3** shows statistically significant gains for Qwen2.5 0.5B models after ~175 rounds, but Qwen3 0.6B models showed **no significant swarm benefit**. This has important implications:

| Model | Parameters | Swarm Benefit | Implication |
|-------|------------|---------------|-------------|
| Qwen2.5 | 0.5B | Significant (p < 0.05 after 175 rounds) | SAPO effective |
| Qwen3 | 0.6B | Not significant | SAPO benefits diminish |

The paper hypothesizes that larger models may already possess sufficient internal reasoning capacity, making external rollouts less valuable. However, this limits SAPO's relevance as models continue to scale. If benefits disappear at 0.6B parameters, SAPO may not be suitable for **large language models (LLMs)** with billions of parameters—the very models where RL post-training is most expensive and most needed.

**Hypothesis About Sampling Strategy**

The authors offer a potential explanation in Section 6:

> "We hypothesize that, with better sampling strategies, more performant models could also benefit from participating in the swarm under SAPO."

The open-source demo used **uniform random sampling without filtering**, which "caused rollouts without useful reward signals to be overrepresented." This suggests the limitation may be methodological rather than fundamental. However, this remains unproven—the controlled experiments with filtering only tested 0.5B models, leaving the question unresolved for larger architectures.

### 6.4 Heterogeneity Gap

**Controlled Experiments Used Homogeneous Models**

Despite emphasizing heterogeneity as a core advantage, the controlled experiments in Section 4 used **eight identical Qwen2.5 0.5B models**:

> "We used a swarm of eight Qwen2.5 models (Qwen Team, 2024), each with 0.5B parameters..." (Section 4)

Section 7 (Future Directions) explicitly acknowledges this gap:

> "As discussed in §4.1 and §5, a natural next step is to evaluate SAPO under greater heterogeneity—for example, with specialized tasks or different base models."

The open-source demo included diverse models, but:
- No controlled comparison between heterogeneous and homogeneous swarms
- No analysis of how model architecture differences affect knowledge transfer
- No evaluation of cross-architecture learning (e.g., can a Llama-based node learn from Qwen-generated rollouts?)

This is a significant limitation because SAPO's primary value proposition—enabling collaboration across heterogeneous hardware and models—remains **empirically unvalidated** in controlled settings. The claim that "the framework is independent of model architecture" (Section 1) is theoretically sound but not experimentally demonstrated.

**Specialization Not Explored**

Section 4.1 notes that agents were **generalists**:

> "We emphasize that, in the experiments, agents are generalists; i.e., they received questions from all specialties with equal probability."

The paper does not test whether **specialized agents** (e.g., nodes focused on specific reasoning domains) could improve swarm performance through complementary expertise. Section 7 mentions this as future work, but it remains an open question whether specialization enhances or hinders collective learning in SAPO.

### 6.5 Scalability Constraints

**Swarm Size Not Ablated**

The controlled experiments used exactly **8 nodes** (Section 4). No ablation studied how performance scales with swarm size:

- Does adding more nodes improve sample efficiency linearly, sublinearly, or with diminishing returns?
- At what swarm size does communication overhead outweigh benefits?
- How does node churn (nodes joining/leaving) affect convergence?

The open-source demo involved "thousands of Gensyn community members" (Section 6), but this was an uncontrolled environment without systematic analysis of swarm size effects. Section 5.4 (Limitations) explicitly notes:

> "No ablation on the number of nodes (all controlled experiments used exactly 8 nodes)."

**Communication Overhead**

While SAPO avoids weight synchronization, it still incurs communication costs. Section 2 acknowledges:

> "Although communication with the swarm and re-encoding sampled rollouts introduces communication and computational overhead, as will be shown in §5, models trained with the swarm perform better with fewer rounds of training."

However, Section 5 does not quantify this overhead:
- No measurements of bandwidth consumption per round
- No analysis of latency impact on training throughput
- No comparison of wall-clock time between SAPO and baseline

The claim that overhead is "outweighed by collective gains" is supported by reward improvements but not by **efficiency metrics** (e.g., rewards per hour, compute cost per improvement). For practitioners deciding whether to deploy SAPO, this missing data makes cost-benefit analysis difficult.

**Ephemeral Node Handling**

Section 6 footnote 5 notes the open-source demo was an **ephemeral environment**:

> "The large-scale demo's swarm was an ephemeral environment where nodes came and went or occasionally stopped then restarted."

While SAPO's asynchronous design theoretically handles node churn, the paper does not analyze:
- How node dropout affects shared pool quality
- Whether frequent churn destabilizes learning
- What minimum node count is required for stable training

This is a practical concern for real-world deployments where nodes may have unreliable connectivity (e.g., consumer devices, edge computing).

### 6.6 Training Horizon and Long-term Stability

**Limited Training Duration**

Controlled experiments ran for **2000 rounds** (Section 4.2). Section 5.4 notes:

> "No long-term stability analysis beyond 2000 rounds."

This raises questions about:
- Does the 4/4 configuration remain optimal beyond 2000 rounds?
- Do oscillations in the 2/6 configuration eventually converge or worsen?
- Is there performance degradation from accumulated noise in external rollouts over extended training?

The **175-round threshold** for statistical significance (Figure 3) suggests benefits accumulate over time, but the paper does not show whether this trend continues indefinitely or plateaus.

**Forgetting Behavior**

Section 5 describes "steep learning and forgetting behavior" in high-sharing configurations. While attributed to external rollout reliance, the paper does not:
- Quantify forgetting rates (e.g., performance on early tasks after later training)
- Propose mitigation strategies beyond reducing external sampling
- Analyze whether forgetting is reversible or permanent

This is particularly concerning for **continual learning** scenarios where models must retain previously acquired capabilities.

### 6.7 Filtering Mechanism Dependencies

**Critical but Untested**

The controlled experiments filtered **zero-advantage rollouts** before sampling (Section 5), but the open-source demo used uniform random sampling without filtering (Section 6). The authors acknowledge this likely limited performance:

> "This caused rollouts without useful reward signals to be overrepresented in the swarm. We hypothesize that, with better sampling strategies, more performant models could also benefit from participating in the swarm under SAPO." (Section 6)

However, **no ablation compared filtered vs. unfiltered sampling** in controlled settings (Section 5.4). This leaves critical questions unanswered:
- How much does filtering contribute to the 94% improvement?
- What is the optimal advantage threshold for filtering?
- Does filtering introduce bias that limits exploration?

The filtering mechanism is presented as essential for stability, but its contribution relative to other factors (sharing ratio, model capacity) remains unquantified.

### 6.8 Summary of Limitations

| Limitation | Evidence | Severity |
|------------|----------|----------|
| Verifiable rewards required | Section 3.1 | High (restricts application domains) |
| Model capacity dependence | Section 6, Figure 3 | High (benefits diminish at 0.6B+) |
| Heterogeneity not validated | Section 4, Section 7 | Medium (core claim untested) |
| Stability-Exploration trade-off | Section 5, Figure 2 | Medium (requires careful tuning) |
| No adaptive sampling | Section 7 | Medium (manual tuning required) |
| Swarm size not ablated | Section 5.4 | Medium (scalability unclear) |
| Communication overhead unquantified | Section 2, Section 5 | Low-Medium (claimed but not measured) |
| Long-term stability unknown | Section 5.4 | Medium (2000 rounds may be insufficient) |
| Filtering mechanism untested | Section 5.4, Section 6 | Medium (critical for performance) |

The paper is transparent about these limitations, with Section 7 (Future Directions) explicitly calling out stability, heterogeneity, and sampling strategies as open questions. This honesty strengthens the work's credibility but also indicates that SAPO is a **promising foundation** rather than a complete solution. Practitioners should view SAPO as effective for **mid-capacity SLMs** (around 0.5B parameters) on **verifiable reasoning tasks** with **carefully tuned sharing ratios**, while recognizing that deployment at larger scales or with larger models requires additional research.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of Swarm sAmpling Policy Optimization (SAPO) represents more than a technical optimization; it proposes a fundamental shift in how we conceptualize distributed learning for language models. By decoupling collaboration from weight synchronization, SAPO opens new architectural possibilities for scaling reinforcement learning (RL) post-training. This section explores how this work changes the landscape of the field, outlines specific research avenues it enables, identifies practical use cases, and provides guidance for practitioners considering adoption.

### 7.1 Shifting the Paradigm of Distributed RL

SAPO challenges the prevailing assumption that effective distributed RL requires homogeneous hardware and synchronized policy weights. Traditional approaches, such as those described in Section 1 (e.g., Mistral-AI et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025), rely on tightly coupled clusters where latency and bandwidth dictate scalability. SAPO inverts this constraint by treating **experience** (decoded rollouts) as the unit of exchange rather than **parameters**.

This shift has three profound implications for the field:

*   **Democratization of Compute:** As noted in Section 1, swarms are often implemented on local or edge devices (e.g., MacBooks) running Small Language Models (SLMs) with fewer than `$10B$` parameters. By removing the need for high-bandwidth weight synchronization, SAPO allows fragmented, consumer-grade hardware to contribute to collective model improvement. This lowers the barrier to entry for RL post-training, moving it from centralized data centers to decentralized networks.
*   **Resilience to Churn:** In centralized systems, node failure can stall training or require complex checkpointing. SAPO's asynchronous design means nodes can operate in silos or join/leave the swarm without disrupting the global process. The open-source demo described in Section 6 involved thousands of community members with ephemeral connectivity, demonstrating that the system tolerates high churn rates that would cripple synchronous clusters.
*   **Emergent Knowledge Propagation:** The paper highlights that successful reasoning strategies ("Aha moments") propagate through the swarm without explicit reward signals for every constraint. Section 4.3 notes that an explicit **formatting reward** became unnecessary because correct formatting spread through shared rollouts. This suggests that decentralized swarms can self-organize around implicit standards, reducing the engineering burden of dense reward modeling.

### 7.2 Promising Avenues for Future Research

The paper explicitly outlines several directions for future work in Section 7, many of which address the limitations identified in Section 6. These avenues represent critical next steps for validating and extending SAPO's capabilities.

#### 1. Systematic Heterogeneity Studies
While SAPO is designed for heterogeneous networks, the controlled experiments in Section 4 used eight identical Qwen2.5 `$0.5B$` models. The paper acknowledges this gap:
> "A natural next step is to evaluate SAPO under greater heterogeneity—for example, with specialized tasks or different base models." (Section 7)

Future research must empirically validate whether a node running a `$0.5B$` model can effectively learn from rollouts generated by a `$1B$` model or a different architecture (e.g., Llama vs. Qwen). The open-source demo included diverse models, but lacked controlled comparison. Rigorous ablation studies are needed to determine if architectural mismatches introduce noise that outweighs the benefits of experience sharing.

#### 2. Adaptive Sampling and Stability Mechanisms
Section 5 demonstrated that excessive reliance on external rollouts (e.g., the 2 local / 6 external configuration) causes oscillations and forgetting. Section 7 proposes developing **meta-strategies** to mitigate this:
> "A promising direction is to develop meta-strategies for adaptively balancing local vs. shared rollouts, or for strategically filtering swarm samples." (Section 7)

Instead of fixed ratios (`$I_n$` local, `$J_n$` external), future algorithms could dynamically adjust sampling based on:
*   **Training Phase:** Early rounds might benefit from high external sampling (`$J_n \gg I_n$`) for rapid knowledge propagation, while later rounds shift to local sampling (`$I_n \gg J_n$`) for stabilization.
*   **Reward Variance:** Nodes could increase external sampling when local reward gradients vanish, using the swarm to escape local optima.
*   **Trust Scores:** In open networks, nodes might weight external rollouts based on the historical performance of the contributing peer.

#### 3. Multimodal Swarms
SAPO is agnostic to data modality, yet the paper focuses on language. Section 7 suggests intriguing multimodal applications:
> "In image-based (or any data with a notion of 'aesthetic') swarms, individual nodes can define local reward mechanisms that codify a personal sense of 'taste'..." (Section 7)

Footnote 6 in Section 7 describes a text-to-image swarm where some nodes reward aesthetics and others reward CLIPScore. This enables **collaborative style transfer** without sharing weights: a node optimizing for "aesthetic quality" can learn from rollouts generated by a node optimizing for "semantic alignment," potentially producing images that satisfy both criteria. This expands SAPO's applicability beyond reasoning tasks to generative art and design.

#### 4. Incentive Mechanisms for Non-Trained Policies
Section 3.1 notes that nodes do not necessarily need to partake in training; humans or other non-trained policies can serve as generators. Section 7 highlights the need for incentive structures:
> "...it would be interesting to explore the effect of unconventional, non-trained policies (e.g., humans) within the swarm when proper incentive mechanisms are put in place to encourage earnest contributions." (Section 7)

This opens the door to **Human-in-the-Loop RL** where human-generated rollouts are mixed with model-generated ones. However, this requires robust verification to prevent malicious or low-quality data from poisoning the swarm—a challenge distinct from the algorithmic verification used in ReasoningGYM.

#### 5. Hybrid Verification Strategies
Currently, SAPO relies on **verifiable rewards** (RLVR) where answers are programmatically checked (Section 4.3). To apply SAPO to subjective tasks (e.g., creative writing), future work must integrate **RLHF (RL from Human Feedback)** mechanisms. Section 7 suggests hybrid approaches:
> "Hybrid approaches that integrate SAPO with reward-guided sharing, RLHF, or generative verifiers (Zhang et al., 2025) may help resolve this." (Section 7)

Generative verifiers could score rollouts in domains where rule-based checks are impossible, though this introduces additional compute overhead that must be balanced against SAPO's efficiency gains.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

Based on the experimental results and architectural design, SAPO is best suited for specific scenarios where its strengths align with deployment constraints.

#### 1. Edge AI and Privacy-Preserving Fine-Tuning
Because SAPO shares decoded text rather than model weights or raw data, it offers a degree of privacy protection. Organizations can collaborate on model improvement without exposing proprietary model architectures or sensitive user data (only the generated reasoning traces are shared). This is ideal for:
*   **Healthcare or Finance:** Where data cannot leave local premises, but models can benefit from collective reasoning patterns.
*   **Mobile Devices:** SLMs running on phones can improve via swarm participation without draining battery on heavy weight synchronization traffic.

#### 2. Cost-Effective Reasoning Enhancement for SLMs
The controlled experiments showed a **94% cumulative reward gain** for `$0.5B$` parameter models (Section 5). For applications requiring reasoning capabilities (math, code, logic) on budget hardware, SAPO provides a high-return investment.
*   **Use Case:** Enhancing open-source SLMs (e.g., Qwen2.5 `$0.5B$`) for specific vertical tasks (e.g., legal clause analysis, code debugging) using a community swarm rather than expensive GPU clusters.
*   **Constraint:** Tasks must have **verifiable rewards** (Section 3.1). SAPO is not yet proven for subjective alignment tasks.

#### 3. Community-Driven Model Improvement
The open-source demo (Section 6) demonstrates a model where community members contribute compute to improve a shared model ecosystem.
*   **Use Case:** Open-source projects where users run local nodes to fine-tune a base model for their specific hardware constraints, contributing rollouts back to a central pool that benefits all participants.
*   **Benefit:** Reduces the central organization's compute costs while increasing model robustness across diverse hardware configurations.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For practitioners considering SAPO, the paper provides sufficient detail for reproduction but highlights critical configuration choices that determine success.

#### When to Prefer SAPO Over Alternatives
*   **Choose SAPO if:**
    *   You are training **Small Language Models (SLMs)** (`$<10B$` parameters). The paper found benefits for `$0.5B$` models but diminishing returns for `$0.6B$` models in the demo (Section 6, Figure 3).
    *   You have **heterogeneous hardware** (e.g., mix of GPUs, CPUs, consumer devices).
    *   Your tasks have **programmatic verifiers** (e.g., math, code, logic puzzles).
    *   Network reliability is low (asynchronous design tolerates churn).
*   **Choose Standard Distributed RL (e.g., PPO with weight sync) if:**
    *   You are training **Large Language Models (LLMs)** where weight synchronization infrastructure already exists.
    *   Your tasks require **subjective reward models** (RLHF) without reliable verifiers.
    *   You require strict convergence guarantees (SAPO showed oscillations in high-sharing configs, Section 5).

#### Critical Configuration Requirements
To replicate the **94% improvement** observed in controlled experiments, practitioners must adhere to specific settings identified in Section 5:

1.  **Balanced Sharing Ratio:** Do not maximize external sharing. The optimal configuration was **4 local / 4 external** rollouts. The 2 local / 6 external config caused instability (Figure 2).
    *   *Recommendation:* Start with `$I_n = J_n$` and monitor reward variance.
2.  **Advantage Filtering:** The controlled experiments filtered out rollouts with **zero advantage** before sampling from the swarm. The open-source demo lacked this and saw muted gains for larger models (Section 6).
    *   *Recommendation:* Implement local filtering to remove uninformative samples. Do not use uniform random sampling for external rollouts.
3.  **Verifier Consistency:** Ensure all nodes use compatible verification logic. While models can differ, the reward signal must be consistent, or nodes will optimize for conflicting objectives.
4.  **Training Duration:** Benefits are not immediate. Figure 3 shows statistical significance only after approximately **175 normalized rounds**.
    *   *Recommendation:* Commit to sufficient training rounds before evaluating swarm efficacy.

#### Tooling and Infrastructure
The paper utilized specific tools that facilitate reproduction:
*   **GenRL:** The decentralized backend used for experiments (Section 4.4). It supports peer-to-peer coordination and integrates with ReasoningGYM.
*   **ReasoningGYM:** The dataset providing procedurally generated, verifiable tasks (Section 4.1). It covers nine domains including `base_conversion`, `propositional_logic`, and `bf` (Brainf*ck).
*   **Policy Algorithm:** The experiments used **GRPO** with no KL-divergence penalty and asymmetric clipping (`$\epsilon_{\text{low}} = 0.2$`, `$\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.28$`) (Section 4.2). Deviating from these hyperparameters may require re-tuning the sharing ratio.

#### Integration Checklist
*   [ ] **Verify Task Verifiability:** Can your task be scored algorithmically (0 or 1)?
*   [ ] **Select Model Size:** Are you using an SLM (`$\approx 0.5B$`)? Larger models may need advanced filtering.
*   [ ] **Implement Filtering:** Can you compute advantage locally to filter external rollouts?
*   [ ] **Set Sharing Ratio:** Configure `$I_n$` and `$J_n$` to start at 50/50.
*   [ ] **Monitor Oscillations:** Watch for reward variance similar to Figure 2; if present, reduce `$J_n$`.

In summary, SAPO offers a viable path for decentralized, cost-effective post-training of small models, provided practitioners respect the stability constraints identified in the experiments. The work invites the community to explore the untapped potential of heterogeneous swarms, moving beyond centralized clusters toward collaborative, self-organizing AI ecosystems.
