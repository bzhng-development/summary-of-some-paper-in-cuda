## 1. Executive Summary
This paper introduces a high-quality "Multimodal Textbook" corpus constructed from 22,000 class hours (2.5 years) of instructional videos across six subjects, addressing the low knowledge density and loose image-text coherence found in existing web-crawled interleaved datasets like MMC4 and OBELICS. By employing an LLM-driven taxonomy and a multi-level extraction pipeline to organize 6.5 million keyframes with refined ASR and OCR text, the authors create a dataset that significantly improves Vision-Language Model (VLM) performance on reasoning-intensive benchmarks, yielding up to a +5.5% gain on MathVista after instruction tuning compared to baselines. The primary significance lies in demonstrating that video-centric, logically coherent interleaved data uniquely enhances a model's ability to leverage few-shot context and solve complex scientific and mathematical problems, a capability often overlooked when training on standard webpage-derived corpora.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Limitations of Current Multimodal Data
To understand the necessity of this work, we must first examine the current state of data used to train Vision-Language Models (VLMs). Historically, VLM pretraining has relied heavily on **image-text paired datasets** (e.g., LAION-5B, COYO-700M), where a single image is associated with a single caption. While effective for basic alignment tasks like image captioning, this format lacks the narrative complexity required for advanced reasoning. It treats images as isolated snapshots rather than parts of a evolving story or logical argument.

To address this, the community recently shifted toward **image-text interleaved corpora**, such as **MMC4** and **OBELICS**. These datasets are constructed by crawling webpages (e.g., from Common Crawl) and preserving the original order of text paragraphs and images as they appear on a site. This structure allows models to learn **in-context learning**—the ability to solve new tasks by looking at examples provided in the prompt—and handle multi-image inputs.

However, the paper identifies three critical structural flaws in these webpage-centric datasets that fundamentally limit their utility for teaching complex reasoning (illustrated in **Figure 1**):

1.  **Loose Text-Image Relations:** On the open web, images are often decorative, promotional, or tangential. A webpage about physics might include a diagram of a circuit, but the surrounding text could be an advertisement or a unrelated news sidebar. The paper notes that associations are often weak, with images serving as logos or ads rather than integral components of the explanation.
2.  **Lack of Logical Coherence in Image Sequences:** Webpages typically contain very few images, and when they do appear in sequence, the logical connection between them is often vague. For a model to learn complex visual reasoning (like the step-by-step derivation of a math proof), it needs to see a sequence of images where Image B logically follows Image A. Web crawls rarely provide this tight temporal or logical chaining.
3.  **Low Knowledge Density:** The internet is vast, but much of it is low-signal content (entertainment, news, opinions). Web-crawled datasets inevitably ingest this noise, resulting in corpora with low "knowledge density." They lack the concentrated, foundational knowledge found in educational materials, making it difficult for models to acquire deep expertise in subjects like mathematics or science.

### The Untapped Potential of Instructional Videos
The authors posit that the solution to these data quality issues lies not in better web crawling, but in a different modality entirely: **instructional videos**.

Platforms like YouTube host a massive repository of human knowledge in the form of online courses and tutorials (e.g., geometry lessons, physics demonstrations). These resources possess inherent qualities that webpages lack:
*   **Temporal Coherence:** Videos unfold over time. An instructor explains a concept while simultaneously updating a whiteboard or animation. This creates a natural, chronological link between visual frames and spoken explanations.
*   **High Knowledge Density:** Unlike entertainment videos, instructional content is explicitly designed to teach foundational subjects. The signal-to-noise ratio for educational content is significantly higher.
*   **Tight Image-Text Alignment:** In a tutorial, the visual content (slides, diagrams) is directly referenced by the audio narration. The "text" (speech) and "image" (frame) are synchronized by design.

Despite these advantages, the paper notes that instructional videos remain **underexplored** for VLM pretraining. Most prior video-language work focuses on action recognition or short clips, rather than extracting long-form, textbook-quality knowledge sequences.

### Positioning Relative to Prior Work
This paper positions itself as a bridge between the **structured quality of textbooks** and the **scalability of internet data**.

*   **Vs. Image-Text Pairs:** While pairs teach *what* an object is, the "Multimodal Textbook" teaches *how* concepts evolve. It moves beyond static alignment to dynamic reasoning.
*   **Vs. Web-Interleaved Data (MMC4/OBELICS):** The authors argue that their dataset is superior not just in volume, but in **logical integrity**. Where MMC4 offers a chaotic mix of web elements, the proposed dataset offers a curated, linear progression of knowledge.
    *   *Evidence of the gap:* In **Table 1**, the authors introduce a metric called **In-sample Image Similarity (InSI-SIM)** to quantify how related images are within a single sample. Their dataset achieves an average score of **0.686**, whereas OBELICS scores only **0.345** and MMC4 scores **0.319**. This quantitative gap proves that images in web datasets are semantically disjointed, while images in the video-derived textbook are tightly coupled.
*   **Vs. Text-Only "Textbooks":** Recent Large Language Model (LLM) research (e.g., the Phi series) has shown that training on high-quality "textbook" data yields better reasoning than training on massive amounts of noisy web text. This paper extends that philosophy to the **multimodal** domain, arguing that VLMs similarly need "textbook-level" visual-textual interleaving to reach their full potential.

### The Core Hypothesis
The driving hypothesis of this work is that **coherence matters more than sheer scale** for reasoning tasks. By constructing a dataset where images and text are chronologically synchronized and logically dependent (derived from 22,000 class hours of video), the authors aim to create a pretraining corpus that enables VLMs to:
1.  Acquire dense foundational knowledge in STEM subjects.
2.  Develop robust **interleaved context awareness**, allowing them to effectively use few-shot examples to solve problems—a capability that models trained on noisy web data often fail to demonstrate.

In essence, the paper argues that to build VLMs that can reason like humans, we must feed them data that reflects how humans actually learn: through structured, coherent, and explanatory narratives, not through disconnected web scraps.

## 3. Technical Approach

This section details the construction of the "Multimodal Textbook," a dataset engineered to transform raw, unstructured instructional videos into a high-density, logically coherent image-text interleaved corpus. The core idea is that by strictly enforcing temporal alignment and knowledge density through a multi-stage filtering pipeline, we can extract training signals that are superior to those found in chaotic web-crawled data.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system is an automated data curation pipeline that ingests massive collections of online educational videos and outputs a structured "textbook" where key visual frames are interleaved with refined spoken explanations and on-screen text. It solves the problem of noisy, disjointed multimodal data by replacing random web scraping with a taxonomy-driven collection strategy and a rigorous "coarse-to-fine" filtering process that ensures every image and text segment contributes to a unified logical narrative.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The architecture functions as a sequential funnel consisting of four primary modules:
1.  **Taxonomy-Driven Collector:** Uses a Large Language Model (LLM) to generate a hierarchical knowledge map (Subject $\to$ Course $\to$ Topic) and queries video APIs to gather a raw pool of 159,565 instructional videos.
2.  **Video-Level Processor:** Extracts audio, transcribes it to text (ASR), and employs an LLM to filter out non-instructional content and rewrite colloquial speech into formal textbook prose.
3.  **Clip-Level Segmenter:** Cuts long videos into short, semantically coherent clips (10–20 seconds) based on ASR timestamps and verifies that the visual content matches the spoken explanation using a vision-language model.
4.  **Keyframe & OCR Extractor:** Identifies visually distinct frames within clips using structural similarity metrics, extracts on-screen text and formulas (OCR), and assembles the final interleaved sequence of frames and text.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
To understand how raw video becomes a high-quality textbook, we will traverse the pipeline in chronological order of data processing:
*   **Knowledge Taxonomy & Collection:** We first explain how the system avoids random scraping by using an LLM to define exactly *what* to look for, ensuring broad subject coverage.
*   **Video-Level Filtering & Refinement:** We detail how the system cleans the audio track, removing noise and rewriting casual speech into high-quality text, which is critical for language modeling.
*   **Temporal Segmentation (Clip-Level):** We describe the mechanism for breaking hour-long lectures into manageable, logically tight chunks where vision and text are perfectly synchronized.
*   **Visual Extraction (Keyframe & OCR):** We analyze the algorithmic choice of using Structural Similarity (SSIM) over semantic models to capture subtle changes in diagrams and formulas, and how on-screen text is harvested.
*   **Final Assembly:** We conclude with how these disparate elements are stitched into the final interleaved format used for training.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Phase 1: LLM-Proposed Knowledge Taxonomy and Collection
The process begins not with data, but with a structured definition of knowledge, ensuring the dataset covers foundational subjects systematically rather than randomly.
*   The authors instruct an LLM (specifically **GPT-4o**, as noted in Supplementary Section 7.1) to generate a four-layer hierarchical taxonomy: **Subject $\to$ Course $\to$ Sub-course $\to$ Knowledge Point**.
*   This taxonomy spans **6 core subjects** (Mathematics, Physics, Chemistry, Earth Science, Engineering, Computer Science), **55 courses** (e.g., Algebra, Solid Geometry), and **3,915 specific knowledge points** (e.g., "definition of Irrational Numbers").
*   Each of the 3,915 knowledge points serves as a specific search query keyword to retrieve videos via the **YouTube Search API**.
*   The system retrieves the top **50 videos** for every knowledge point, resulting in an initial raw pool of **159,565 videos**.
*   To ensure quality at the source, the system performs metadata-level filtering where an LLM reviews the title, description, and comments of each video to exclude irrelevant, pornographic, or illegal content before download.

#### Phase 2: Video-Level Extraction and ASR Refinement
Once videos are collected, the pipeline separates the auditory channel from the visual channel to process and refine the textual knowledge contained in the instructor's speech.
*   The system uses **FFmpeg** to extract the audio track from each video file.
*   It employs **Whisper-large-v3** to perform Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR), transcribing the audio into raw text.
*   The authors observe that raw ASR from tutorials has high perplexity (PPL) due to colloquialisms, filler words ("um," "uh"), and fragmented sentences, which can degrade LLM training.
*   To correct this, they introduce a refinement step using **Qwen2-72B-Instruct** to rewrite the raw ASR transcripts.
*   The rewriting prompt instructs the model to improve fluency and coherence while strictly preserving the original semantics and technical accuracy.
*   Following transcription, a second round of video-level filtering occurs: an LLM scores each video's ASR based on three criteria: **Relevance** (does it match the target knowledge point?), **Knowledge Density** (are there too many filler phrases?), and **Transcription Quality** (is the text repetitive or erroneous?).
*   Videos failing these criteria are discarded, reducing the pool from 159k to **75,000 high-quality videos**, totaling **22,697 class hours**.

#### Phase 3: Clip-Level Segmentation and Visual-Text Alignment
Long videos (average 18 minutes) are too large and often contain topic shifts; the system must segment them into short, logically atomic units where the visual and textual modalities are tightly aligned.
*   The system merges fragmented ASR segments into semantically coherent paragraphs to establish complete thought units.
*   Using the timestamps associated with these merged ASR paragraphs, the system cuts the long video into short clips, typically lasting **10 to 20 seconds**.
*   This creates a preliminary sequence of pairs: $\langle \text{clip}_1, \text{asr}_1 \rangle, \langle \text{clip}_2, \text{asr}_2 \rangle, \dots, \langle \text{clip}_n, \text{asr}_n \rangle$.
*   However, some clips may contain visuals that do not match the audio (e.g., a shot of the instructor's face while the audio describes a diagram).
*   To filter these, the system employs **VideoLlama2-7B** to generate a detailed caption describing the visual content of each clip.
*   It then calculates the semantic similarity between the generated visual caption and the ASR text using the **gte-Qwen2-7B-instruct** text embedding model.
*   Clips with low similarity scores (indicating a mismatch between what is said and what is shown) are discarded as "uninformative."
*   Crucially, even if a clip's *visuals* are discarded, its *ASR text* is retained if it contains valuable knowledge, resulting in sequences where text may exist without corresponding images, preserving the narrative flow.

#### Phase 4: Keyframe Extraction via Structural Similarity
Within each valid video clip, multiple frames are often nearly identical; the system must select "keyframes" that represent significant visual changes without redundancy.
*   The authors explicitly reject using semantic models (like CLIP) for keyframe extraction because instructional videos often feature abstract diagrams or geometric proofs where semantic meaning changes subtly even if the global image embedding remains similar.
*   Instead, they adopt a **Structural Similarity Index (SSIM)** approach, which measures pixel-level structural changes ideal for detecting updates in equations or diagrams.
*   The extraction follows an iterative algorithm (detailed in **Algorithm 1** of the supplementary material):
    1.  Set the first frame of the clip as the `reference_frame`.
    2.  Iterate through subsequent frames $F_i$ and calculate $SSIM(\text{reference\_frame}, F_i)$.
    3.  If the SSIM score is above a threshold $T$ (indicating high similarity), skip the frame.
    4.  If the SSIM score drops below $T$ (indicating a significant visual change), mark $F_i$ as a **keyframe** and update `reference_frame` to $F_i$.
*   This method yields **6.5 million keyframes** from the 4 million video clips.
*   Ablation studies (Table 6) show this choice is critical: pixel-level methods extracted too many frames (18M, causing a 9% performance drop), while CLIP-based methods extracted too few (1.7M, missing critical steps), whereas SSIM provided the optimal balance.

#### Phase 5: OCR Extraction and Final Interleaving
The final step enriches the data by extracting text directly from the images (slides, blackboards) which often contains formulas and symbols not present in the spoken audio.
*   The system employs **InternVL2-40B**, a powerful Vision-Language Model, to perform Optical Character Recognition (OCR) on every selected keyframe.
*   This extracts on-screen text, mathematical symbols, and formulas, generating **500 million OCR tokens**.
*   A filtering step removes redundant OCR results; if the text extracted from a current keyframe is identical or highly similar to the previous one, it is discarded to prevent training on repetitive data.
*   Finally, the system constructs the "Multimodal Textbook" samples by chronologically interleaving the retained elements:
    $$ \{ \text{frame}^{k_1}_1, \text{frame}^{k_2}_1, \text{ocr}_1, \text{asr}_1, \text{asr}_2, \text{asr}_3, \text{frame}^{k_1}_4, \text{ocr}_4, \text{asr}_4, \dots \} $$
*   To optimize for training efficiency and context limits, multiple fragments from the same video are concatenated into single samples, capping at the model's maximum context length.
*   An explicit `End of Video` token is inserted between concatenated segments from different videos to prevent the model from learning false logical connections between unrelated topics.
*   The final dataset comprises **610,000 interleaved samples**, with an average of **10.7 images** and **1,297 text tokens** per sample, significantly denser than web-based counterparts.

#### Design Choices and Rationale
The technical decisions in this pipeline prioritize **logical coherence** and **signal-to-noise ratio** over sheer volume.
*   **Why Taxonomy-First?** Random crawling yields inconsistent quality; using an LLM-generated taxonomy ensures the dataset covers a balanced curriculum of foundational knowledge, mimicking a school syllabus rather than the chaotic structure of the web.
*   **Why ASR Rewriting?** Raw speech is disfluent. Training an LLM component on disfluent text degrades its language modeling capabilities. Rewriting ASR into formal prose aligns the data distribution with high-quality text corpora, improving the model's linguistic reasoning.
*   **Why SSIM for Keyframes?** Instructional content relies on incremental visual updates (e.g., adding one line to a geometry proof). Semantic models often miss these subtle changes, while pixel-difference methods are too sensitive to compression artifacts. SSIM offers a robust middle ground that detects meaningful structural updates in diagrams.
*   **Why Retain Text-Only Segments?** In a lecture, an instructor might speak for 10 seconds while the slide remains static. Discarding the audio during these static visual periods would lose valuable explanatory context. The pipeline preserves the audio continuity even when visual keyframes are sparse, maintaining the logical narrative.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The "Multimodal Textbook" represents a paradigm shift in how we conceive pretraining data for Vision-Language Models (VLMs). While prior work focused on scaling data volume through indiscriminate web crawling, this paper introduces a methodology centered on **logical coherence** and **pedagogical structure**. The following insights distinguish fundamental innovations from incremental improvements, explaining *why* these design choices yield superior reasoning capabilities.

### 4.1 The "Coherence Hypothesis": Temporal Logic as a Reasoning Scaffold
**Innovation Type:** Fundamental Conceptual Shift

Prior interleaved datasets (e.g., MMC4, OBELICS) operate on the assumption that *proximity* implies *relation*—if an image appears near text on a webpage, they are related. However, as noted in **Section 2**, web layouts often break this assumption with ads, sidebars, and disjointed image galleries.

This paper introduces the **Coherence Hypothesis**: For VLMs to learn complex reasoning, the training data must enforce a strict **temporal and logical dependency** between sequential images.
*   **The Mechanism:** By sourcing data from instructional videos, the dataset inherently encodes a causal chain. Image $B$ is not just "near" Image $A$; it is the *result* of a transformation applied to $A$ (e.g., adding a variable to an equation, drawing a new line in a geometry proof).
*   **Evidence of Impact:** This is quantified in **Table 1** via the **In-sample Image Similarity (InSI-SIM)** metric. While web datasets show a sharp decline in similarity as the number of images per sample increases (dropping ~10% from $L=4$ to $L=8$), the Multimodal Textbook maintains a stable, high similarity (~0.68). This stability proves that the model is seeing a continuous narrative, not a random collage.
*   **Significance:** This structural integrity directly translates to **in-context learning** capabilities. In the "Cheat Test" (**Table 4**), models trained on this coherent data achieved near-perfect scores (98.4% on MathVision) when the answer was hidden in the few-shot context, whereas baselines struggled (~69%). The model learned to *track* logical states across images, a capability absent in models trained on static or loosely connected web data.

### 4.2 Algorithmic Precision: SSIM Over Semantic Embeddings for Educational Content
**Innovation Type:** Non-Obvious Technical Design Choice

A common assumption in video processing is that semantic models (like CLIP) are superior for selecting representative frames because they understand "meaning." The authors challenge this assumption with a critical finding specific to educational content.

*   **The Problem:** Instructional videos often feature static backgrounds (whiteboards, slides) where the *semantic* context remains identical, but the *informational* content changes via small textual or geometric updates (e.g., changing $x=2$ to $x=3$). Semantic embeddings often fail to detect these subtle shifts, treating distinct steps in a derivation as identical.
*   **The Solution:** The pipeline utilizes **Structural Similarity Index (SSIM)**, a pixel-level structural metric, rather than semantic distance, to extract keyframes.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The ablation study in **Table 6** provides definitive proof of this design choice's necessity:
    *   **CLIP-based extraction** yielded only 1.7M keyframes, missing critical intermediate steps, resulting in a **6.5% drop** in accuracy.
    *   **Pixel-level difference (absdiff)** was too sensitive to noise, yielding 18M redundant frames and causing a **9% drop** in performance due to context dilution.
    *   **SSIM** struck the optimal balance (6.5M keyframes), capturing meaningful structural changes without noise.
*   **Significance:** This insight overturns the "semantic-first" dogma for specific domains. It demonstrates that for tasks requiring precise symbolic tracking (math, physics), **structural fidelity** is more valuable than high-level semantic clustering.

### 4.3 The "Textbook" Data Regime: Quality-Weighted Pretraining
**Innovation Type:** Strategic Data Curation Philosophy

Building on the "Textbooks are All You Need" philosophy from the LLM domain (e.g., Phi models), this work extends the concept to the **multimodal** realm. The innovation lies not just in filtering, but in the **active refinement** of modalities to match high-quality text corpora.

*   **ASR Refinement as a Critical Step:** Raw Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) from videos is colloquial, fragmented, and filled with filler words ("um," "uh"). The paper reveals that training directly on raw ASR significantly harms language modeling capabilities.
    *   **Data Point:** Raw ASR has a perplexity (PPL) of **16.86**, whereas refined ASR drops to **13.92** (**Table 6**).
    *   **Result:** Removing the LLM-based refinement step causes a **4.9% drop** in downstream benchmark accuracy. This proves that the *linguistic quality* of the interleaved text is just as critical as the visual alignment.
*   **OCR as a Knowledge Multiplier:** The pipeline treats On-Screen Text (OCR) not as metadata, but as a primary knowledge source. In STEM subjects, formulas and symbols often appear *only* visually. Integrating OCR tokens provides a **+2.3% accuracy gain**, particularly on math benchmarks.
*   **Significance:** This establishes a new standard for multimodal data hygiene. It argues that "raw" internet data is insufficient for high-level reasoning; data must be **pedagogically processed** (rewritten for fluency, enriched with OCR) to function as a true "textbook."

### 4.4 Emergent Interleaved Context Awareness
**Innovation Type:** New Model Capability

Perhaps the most surprising insight is the emergence of **robust interleaved context awareness** solely through pretraining on this specific data distribution.

*   **The Phenomenon:** Models trained on web-crawled data (MMC4/OBELICS) often ignore the visual context in few-shot prompts, relying instead on parametric memory or text-only cues. In contrast, the Multimodal Textbook-trained models actively attend to the provided image-text sequences.
*   **The "Cheat Test" Revelation:** As detailed in **Section 5.3**, when the test question itself was inserted into the few-shot context (a "cheat"), the Textbook-trained model immediately recognized the duplicate and answered correctly. Baselines failed to notice the identical context, especially as the sequence length increased (2-shot vs. 1-shot).
*   **Sensitivity to Order:** **Figure 3** shows that shuffling the image order in the Textbook dataset causes a severe performance drop, whereas shuffling images in web datasets has little effect. This confirms that the Textbook-trained models have learned a **sequential reasoning dependency**—they expect Image $B$ to follow Image $A$ logically.
*   **Significance:** This suggests that **in-context learning** in VLMs is not an inherent property of the architecture alone, but a skill acquired only when the pretraining data exhibits strong, coherent sequential patterns. The dataset effectively "teaches" the model how to read a multimodal story.

### Summary of Contributions

| Contribution | Prior Approach | This Work's Innovation | Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | : |
| **Data Source** | Web crawls (Common Crawl) | **Instructional Videos** (Taxonomy-driven) | High knowledge density; logical coherence. |
| **Image Selection** | Heuristic clustering / Semantic (CLIP) | **Structural Similarity (SSIM)** | Captures subtle diagram/equation updates; +6.5% acc. vs CLIP. |

These insights collectively argue that the future of VLM pretraining lies not in scaling the *quantity* of noisy web data, but in engineering the *quality* and *logical structure* of the data to mirror human pedagogical processes.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section rigorously evaluates whether the "Multimodal Textbook" corpus actually translates into superior model capabilities. The authors do not simply claim their data is "better"; they design experiments to test specific hypotheses: Does this data improve reasoning? Does it enable models to truly understand interleaved context? And which specific components of the data pipeline (ASR refinement, OCR, keyframe selection) are responsible for the gains?

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: Benchmarks and Baselines

To ensure a fair and comprehensive assessment, the authors employ a robust experimental setup comparing their dataset against standard baselines across diverse tasks.

**Baselines and Datasets:**
The study compares the **Textbook-6.5M** dataset (6.5M keyframes, 0.75B tokens) against two prominent web-crawled interleaved datasets:
*   **MMC4** (and its filtered variant `MMC4-core-ff`)
*   **OBELICS**

To isolate the effect of the data, the authors control for volume, sampling exactly **610,000 interleaved samples** from each dataset for pretraining. They utilize two distinct Vision-Language Model (VLM) architectures to prove generalizability:
1.  **LLaVA-1.5-7B:** A standard architecture typically trained on image-text pairs. Here, it undergoes *continual pretraining* on the interleaved data to test adaptability.
2.  **Idefics2-8B:** An advanced model natively supporting multi-image inputs. The authors test this in two modes: *training from scratch* (randomly initialized projector) and *continual pretraining* from an existing base.

**Benchmarks and Metrics:**
Performance is measured across seven benchmarks covering four distinct domains, using **accuracy** as the primary metric in few-shot settings (0-shot to 4-shot, and up to 8-shot for Idefics2):
*   **General VQA:** `OKVQA` (requires external knowledge) and `TextVQA` (requires reading text in images).
*   **Scientific Reasoning:** `ScienceQA-IMG` (multi-modal science questions).
*   **Mathematical Reasoning:** `MathVista`, `MathVision`, and `MathVerse` (complex geometry and algebra problems requiring step-by-step logic).

The evaluation follows the **RICES** strategy (Retrieval-based In-Context Example Selection), where few-shot examples are retrieved based on similarity to the test query, ensuring the model is tested on its ability to leverage relevant context.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results: Reasoning and Knowledge Gains

The experimental results strongly support the hypothesis that high-coherence, knowledge-dense data significantly outperforms chaotic web data, particularly in reasoning-intensive domains.

**Superiority in STEM Reasoning:**
As detailed in **Table 2**, models pretrained on the Multimodal Textbook achieve substantial gains on science and math benchmarks compared to those trained on MMC4 or OBELICS.
*   **ScienceQA:** The Textbook-trained LLaVA model achieves a **26.3%** accuracy in the 0-shot setting, vastly outperforming OBELICS (which scores effectively 0% or negligible values in 0-shot for this specific table configuration, though it reaches 16.4% in 4-shot). More critically, in the 4-shot setting, the Textbook model reaches **37.3%**, a massive improvement over MMC4's **11.6%**. This represents a **>20% absolute gain**, highlighting the dataset's ability to instill foundational scientific knowledge.
*   **MathVista:** On this complex math benchmark, the Textbook model achieves an average improvement of **+5.3%** over OBELICS and **+6.4%** over MMC4 across few-shot settings. Specifically, in the 4-shot setting, the Textbook model hits **29.2%**, whereas OBELICS lags at **27.6%**.
*   **MathVision & MathVerse:** Similar trends appear here. For `MathVision` (4-shot), the Textbook model scores **18.1%**, compared to **14.9%** for OBELICS.

**Generalization to Advanced Architectures:**
The benefits are not limited to the LLaVA architecture. **Table 3** shows that even the state-of-the-art **Idefics2-8B** benefits from this data.
*   When continually pretrained from the Idefics2 base, the Textbook dataset yields a **+1.4%** average improvement over OBELICS across five benchmarks.
*   Notably, on `MathVista`, the Textbook model reaches **29.7%** accuracy, surpassing OBELICS (27.6%) and MMC4 (27.8%).
*   When trained **from scratch**, the Textbook dataset enables Idefics2 to achieve **26.8%** on `TextVQA` and **26.1%** on `MathVista`, consistently beating the web-crawled alternatives.

**The "Context Awareness" Phenomenon:**
A subtle but crucial finding emerges in general VQA tasks (`OKVQA`, `TextVQA`). While the Textbook model starts slightly behind or comparable in the **0-shot** setting (e.g., lagging OBELICS by 2.8% on OKVQA 0-shot), it rapidly surpasses baselines as the number of shots increases.
*   In the **4-shot** setting for `OKVQA`, the Textbook model reaches **39.9%**, exceeding OBELICS (**37.5%**) by **2.4%**.
*   This trend suggests that while web data might provide broad world knowledge (helpful for 0-shot), the **coherent structure** of the Textbook data uniquely teaches the model *how to use* the provided few-shot examples to solve problems. The model learns to attend to the context rather than ignoring it.

### 5.3 Deep Dive: Proving Interleaved Context Awareness

The paper's most compelling claim is that their data teaches models to truly understand sequential, interleaved context. The authors verify this through two ingenious experimental designs: the "Cheat Test" and the "Order Shuffling" test.

**The "Cheat Test":**
To determine if models actually read the few-shot context, the authors designed a test where the **test question itself** is inserted as one of the few-shot examples (complete with the answer). A model with strong context awareness should recognize the duplicate and output the correct answer immediately.
*   **Results (Table 4):** The difference is stark. In the **1-shot Cheat** scenario on `MathVision`, the Textbook-trained model achieves **98.4%** accuracy, nearly perfect. In contrast, MMC4 and OBELICS score only **69.3%** and **66.5%**, respectively.
*   **Robustness to Length:** As the context becomes harder (2-shot Cheat, where the cheat example is mixed with a distractor), web-based models collapse. OBELICS drops from 66.5% to **39.9%** on `MathVision`. The Textbook model, however, remains robust at **70.7%**.
*   **Interpretation:** Models trained on web data appear to ignore the visual-textual context, relying on parametric memory. The Textbook model has learned to actively parse the input sequence, locate the relevant example, and extract the answer.

**Sensitivity to Image Order (Figure 3):**
If a dataset relies on logical sequences, shuffling the images should destroy its value. If the images are unrelated (as in web galleries), shuffling should matter little.
*   The authors shuffled image orders in 20%, 50%, and 100% of the training samples.
*   **MMC4:** Performance remains largely **unaffected** by shuffling, confirming that images in web datasets lack strong sequential dependency.
*   **Textbook:** Performance drops **significantly** as the shuffling ratio increases. This is a *positive* failure mode: it proves the model has learned that Image B logically follows Image A. Disrupting this causal chain degrades the model's ability to reason, validating that the dataset successfully encodes temporal logic.

**Post-Instruction Tuning Performance (Table 5):**
Does this pretraining benefit survive fine-tuning? The authors fine-tuned the pretrained models on the standard `LLaVA-665K` instruction dataset.
*   The Textbook-pretrained model achieved **28.7%** on `MathVista` after fine-tuning, a **+5.5%** gain over the baseline.
*   This is more than double the gain provided by OBELICS (+2.4%) and triple that of MMC4 (+1.6%). This confirms that the "textbook knowledge" is not overwritten during instruction tuning but serves as a stronger foundation for downstream task specialization.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies: Dissecting the Pipeline

The authors perform critical ablation studies (**Table 6**) to justify every step of their complex pipeline. These results reveal that "raw" video data is insufficient; specific processing steps are mandatory for high performance.

**1. The Necessity of ASR Refinement:**
*   **Experiment:** Training with raw Whisper transcriptions vs. LLM-refined text.
*   **Result:** Using raw ASR causes a **4.9% drop** in 1-shot accuracy (from 31.1% to 26.2%).
*   **Why?** Raw ASR has a high perplexity (**16.86**) due to colloquialisms and fragmentation. Refined ASR lowers this to **13.92**, aligning the data distribution with standard high-quality text corpora. This proves that *linguistic fluency* in the text modality is essential for the language component of the VLM to function correctly.

**2. The Value of OCR:**
*   **Experiment:** Training with vs. without Optical Character Recognition (formulas/symbols extracted from frames).
*   **Result:** Removing OCR leads to a **2.3% drop** in accuracy.
*   **Why?** In STEM videos, critical information (equations, variable definitions) often appears *only* on the slide, not in the spoken audio. OCR acts as a "knowledge multiplier," bridging the gap between what is said and what is shown.

**3. Keyframe Extraction Algorithm (SSIM vs. Others):**
*   **Experiment:** Comparing SSIM against pixel-level difference (`absdiff`) and semantic clustering (CLIP).
*   **Result:**
    *   **Pixel-level:** Extracts too many frames (18M), diluting the context with redundancy and causing a **9% performance drop**.
    *   **CLIP-based:** Extracts too few frames (1.7M), missing subtle steps in derivations (e.g., a single changed number in an equation), causing a **6.5% drop**.
    *   **SSIM:** The sweet spot (6.5M frames), capturing structural changes without noise.
*   **Takeaway:** This validates the paper's technical insight that **structural similarity** is superior to semantic similarity for educational content where small visual changes carry high informational weight.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment

**Do the experiments support the claims?**
Yes, convincingly. The paper moves beyond standard benchmark reporting to include mechanistic tests (Cheat Test, Shuffling) that directly probe the *nature* of the learning. The consistent outperformance on reasoning tasks (Math/Science) combined with the collapse of performance when logical order is broken provides strong evidence that the dataset teaches **sequential reasoning**, not just static recognition.

**Limitations and Trade-offs:**
*   **Domain Specificity:** The gains are most pronounced in STEM (Math/Science). While general VQA (`OKVQA`) improves in few-shot settings, the 0-shot performance sometimes lags behind web-scale datasets. This suggests a trade-off: the model becomes a better *reasoner* but may have slightly less broad *world knowledge* than models trained on the entire chaotic web.
*   **Computational Cost:** The pipeline is expensive. It requires running heavy models (Whisper, Qwen2-72B, InternVL2) on 22,000 hours of video. This is not a trivial process to replicate compared to simple web scraping.
*   **Language Bias:** The current dataset is English-centric (filtered via metadata and ASR). The effectiveness of this approach for non-Latin scripts or languages with different educational video cultures remains an open question.

**Conclusion of Analysis:**
The experimental analysis demonstrates that **data coherence is a force multiplier**. By curating a dataset that respects the logical flow of human instruction, the authors have unlocked a level of context awareness in VLMs that was previously absent. The ablation studies confirm that this is not magic; it is the result of deliberate engineering choices (ASR refinement, SSIM keyframing, OCR integration) that transform raw video into a true "multimodal textbook."

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the "Multimodal Textbook" demonstrates that coherent, high-density data significantly outperforms noisy web crawls for reasoning tasks, the approach is not without significant constraints. The gains in logical coherence come at the cost of domain breadth, computational complexity, and specific architectural assumptions. A critical analysis of the paper reveals several key limitations and trade-offs that define the boundaries of this work.

### 6.1 Domain Specificity: The STEM Bias
The most prominent limitation is the **narrow domain coverage** inherent to the data source. The dataset is explicitly constructed from instructional videos covering six foundational subjects: Mathematics, Physics, Chemistry, Earth Science, Engineering, and Computer Science (**Section 3.1**).

*   **The Trade-off:** By prioritizing "textbook-level" knowledge density, the dataset sacrifices the **broad world knowledge** found in web-crawled corpora like LAION or Common Crawl. Web data contains diverse concepts ranging from pop culture and history to obscure geography and social norms. The Multimodal Textbook, by design, excludes these domains.
*   **Evidence of Impact:** This bias is reflected in the experimental results. While the Textbook model dominates in STEM benchmarks (e.g., **+20%** on ScienceQA, **+5.3%** on MathVista), its advantage in general knowledge tasks is less pronounced. In **Table 2**, for the `OKVQA` benchmark (which requires external world knowledge beyond the image), the Textbook model actually **lags behind OBELICS by 2.8%** in the 0-shot setting. It only surpasses the baseline when provided with few-shot examples (2-shot and 4-shot), suggesting that while the model has learned *how to reason* using context, it possesses less *parametric knowledge* about the general world compared to models trained on the chaotic but diverse web.
*   **Implication:** This dataset is a specialized tool for building "expert" reasoners in STEM, not a universal replacement for general-purpose pretraining corpora. A production VLM would likely need a hybrid strategy: pretraining on broad web data for world knowledge, followed by continual pretraining on the Multimodal Textbook for reasoning alignment.

### 6.2 Computational and Engineering Overhead
The pipeline described in **Section 3** is computationally prohibitive compared to standard web scraping. The "quality over quantity" philosophy demands massive processing power, creating a high barrier to entry for replication.

*   **Heavy Model Dependency:** The curation process relies on running multiple large-scale models on 22,000 hours of video:
    *   **ASR Transcription:** `Whisper-large-v3` on 22k hours of audio.
    *   **Text Refinement:** `Qwen2-72B-Instruct` to rewrite colloquial speech into formal prose for millions of segments.
    *   **Visual Filtering:** `VideoLlama2-7B` to caption every 10–20 second clip.
    *   **OCR Extraction:** `InternVL2-40B` to extract text and formulas from 6.5 million keyframes.
*   **Cost vs. Benefit:** Unlike MMC4 or OBELICS, which primarily rely on heuristic filtering of already-downloaded HTML, this approach requires active generation and inference. The authors do not report the specific GPU-hours or financial cost, but the sheer scale of inference (e.g., running a 72B parameter model on hundreds of millions of text segments) suggests a cost orders of magnitude higher than standard dataset creation.
*   **Scalability Constraint:** Scaling this approach to cover *more* subjects (e.g., literature, art history, biology) would linearly increase this computational burden. The "taxonomy-driven" collection method also requires manual or LLM-guided curriculum design for each new subject, which is less scalable than the "crawl everything" approach of Common Crawl.

### 6.3 Architectural Assumptions: The "Understanding-Only" Gap
The current implementation and training strategy rely on a specific architectural assumption: that image tokens are used only for **context/understanding**, not for **generation**.

*   **Loss Computation:** As explicitly stated in **Section 9 (Limitations)** of the supplementary material, "During training, the loss is not computed for image tokens." The model learns to predict the next *text* token given previous text and images, but it does not learn to generate or reconstruct images.
*   **Missed Potential:** This limits the dataset's utility to **discriminative** or **text-generation** tasks (VQA, reasoning). It cannot currently be used to train **omni-modal** models that generate images or videos as part of their reasoning process (e.g., "draw the next step of the proof").
*   **Future Work:** The authors acknowledge this, noting that while the textbook *could* be used for generation tasks, doing so would require different training objectives and potentially different tokenization strategies for the visual modality. This remains an open question for future research.

### 6.4 Residual Noise and Quality Ceiling
Despite the rigorous multi-level filtering pipeline (Video $\to$ Clip $\to$ Keyframe), the authors admit in **Section 9** that the dataset is not perfect.

*   **Remaining Redundancy:** The manual evaluation in **Supplementary Section 7.2** notes that while only 7% of images were "highly similar," some redundancy persists. In instructional videos, instructors often pause or repeat visual states while speaking, which even SSIM-based filtering might not fully eliminate if the structural change is minimal but the time gap is significant.
*   **OCR Limitations:** The quality of the dataset is bottlenecked by the OCR engine (`InternVL2`). As noted in **Section 5.4**, low-quality OCR can introduce noise that degrades performance. Complex handwritten formulas on whiteboards or poor lighting in videos may still result in garbled text tokens, which could confuse the model during pretraining. The pipeline filters *redundant* OCR, but it cannot fully correct *erroneous* OCR without a ground-truth verification step, which is infeasible at this scale.

### 6.5 Language and Cultural Bias
The dataset is heavily skewed toward **English-language** content.

*   **Filtering Bias:** The collection pipeline explicitly filters out non-English videos at the metadata and ASR levels (**Section 3.2**). While this ensures linguistic consistency for English-centric VLMs, it excludes vast repositories of high-quality instructional content in other languages (e.g., Mandarin, Spanish, Hindi).
*   **Pedagogical Style:** The "textbook" structure reflects a specific style of Western/Global-North online education (slide-based, explicit verbal explanation). Educational styles in other cultures may rely more on demonstration, less on verbal narration, or different visual conventions. A model trained solely on this dataset might struggle to interpret instructional videos that do not follow this specific "lecture" format.

### 6.6 Summary of Trade-offs

| Feature | Benefit | Cost / Limitation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Source Data** | High logical coherence; dense STEM knowledge. | Narrow domain (STEM only); lacks general world knowledge. |
| **Curation Pipeline** | Removes noise; aligns text/image perfectly. | Extremely high computational cost; hard to scale to new domains. |
| **Training Objective** | Optimized for reasoning and VQA. | Cannot train image-generation capabilities (loss not on images). |
| **Language** | High-quality English prose. | Excludes non-English educational resources; cultural bias in teaching style. |
| **Keyframe Selection** | Captures subtle diagram changes (SSIM). | Still retains some redundancy; dependent on OCR quality for text-heavy frames. |

In conclusion, the "Multimodal Textbook" is a powerful but specialized instrument. It solves the problem of **reasoning coherence** in VLMs but introduces new challenges regarding **domain breadth** and **computational feasibility**. It represents a shift from "data scaling" to "data engineering," suggesting that future progress in VLMs will depend less on crawling more of the internet and more on surgically extracting and refining high-value educational narratives.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of the "Multimodal Textbook" marks a pivotal transition in Vision-Language Model (VLM) research: a shift from **data scaling** (collecting more raw web data) to **data engineering** (curating coherent, pedagogical narratives). By demonstrating that logical coherence and knowledge density are superior drivers for reasoning than sheer volume, this work fundamentally alters how the community should approach pretraining data construction. The following sections outline the broader landscape changes, specific avenues for future research, practical applications, and integration guidelines derived from this paradigm shift.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Data Landscape: From "Web Scrapes" to "Curricula"
Historically, progress in multimodal AI has been driven by the "Common Crawl" philosophy: scrape the entire web, apply basic filters, and scale up. This paper challenges that dogma by proving that **structure matters as much as scale**.

*   **The End of "Bag-of-Images":** Prior interleaved datasets treated images as independent entities loosely associated with text. The success of the Multimodal Textbook proves that VLMs require **temporal causality**—where Image $B$ is a logical evolution of Image $A$. Future dataset creation will likely move away from static webpage snapshots toward dynamic, time-aligned sources (videos, screen recordings, interactive simulations) that inherently possess this causal structure.
*   **Pedagogy as a Data Objective:** The use of an LLM-generated **knowledge taxonomy** (Section 3.1) to drive data collection introduces the concept of a "syllabus" for AI training. Instead of hoping relevant data appears in a crawl, future pipelines may actively target specific knowledge gaps (e.g., "collect 50 hours of content on *quantum entanglement*") to ensure balanced coverage, mimicking human curriculum design.
*   **Refinement Over Raw ingestion:** The significant performance drop observed when using raw ASR (Table 6) establishes that **linguistic hygiene** is critical. The field must move beyond using raw transcriptions or alt-text. The new standard involves an active "cleaning" layer where LLMs rewrite noisy input into formal, textbook-quality prose before it ever touches the VLM weights.

### 7.2 Avenues for Future Research
This work opens several concrete research directions that build directly upon its methodology and findings:

*   **Cross-Lingual and Cross-Cultural Textbooks:** The current dataset is English-centric (Section 6.5). A critical next step is applying this pipeline to non-English instructional videos (e.g., Mandarin, Hindi, Spanish). This would not only democratize access to high-quality reasoning models but also test whether the "lecture style" coherence found in Western education translates universally, or if different cultures require different interleaving strategies (e.g., more demonstration-heavy vs. text-heavy).
*   **Omni-Modal Generation:** As noted in the limitations (Section 9), the current training objective only computes loss on text tokens. Future work should explore training objectives that also compute loss on **image tokens**, enabling models to *generate* the next step in a derivation or draw the next frame in a physics simulation. The coherent nature of this dataset makes it the ideal candidate for training **diffusion-based VLMs** or autoregressive image generators that can "show their work."
*   **Dynamic Context Length Scaling:** The "Cheat Test" (Section 5.3) revealed that models trained on this data can effectively utilize long contexts. Future research should investigate the upper limits of this capability. Can a model trained on 22,000 hours of coherent video process an entire textbook chapter (hundreds of pages/images) in a single context window to solve complex, multi-step problems that require looking back 50 pages?
*   **Hybrid Pretraining Strategies:** Since the Textbook dataset lacks broad world knowledge (Section 6.1), research is needed to determine the optimal **mixing ratio** between "chaotic" web data (for breadth) and "coherent" textbook data (for depth). Does a "sandwich" approach (Web $\to$ Textbook $\to$ Instruction Tuning) outperform mixing them simultaneously? How much textbook data is needed to unlock reasoning capabilities without causing catastrophic forgetting of general knowledge?

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases
The specific strengths of models pretrained on the Multimodal Textbook—namely, step-by-step reasoning and robust context tracking—make them uniquely suited for high-stakes, knowledge-intensive domains:

*   **Automated STEM Tutoring Systems:** The most direct application is building AI tutors that can guide students through math and science problems. Unlike standard VLMs that might guess an answer, a Textbook-trained model can generate **chain-of-thought explanations** that mirror human pedagogy, referencing specific diagrams and explaining *why* a step follows another, much like the instructors in the training videos.
*   **Technical Documentation and Maintenance:** In engineering and IT, troubleshooting often requires following a logical sequence of diagrams (e.g., circuit schematics, assembly instructions). Models with strong **interleaved context awareness** can ingest a manual and guide a technician through a repair process, understanding that "Step 4" visually modifies the state shown in "Step 3."
*   **Scientific Literature Analysis:** Research papers often contain sequences of figures that tell a story of experimental progression. A model trained on coherent video narratives is better equipped to parse these sequences, understanding the logical flow from hypothesis to method to result, rather than treating each figure as an isolated captioning task.
*   **Legal and Compliance Review:** While not STEM, legal documents often involve sequential logic and evidence presentation. The ability to track state changes and logical dependencies across multiple pages/images could enhance models used for contract review or case law analysis.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance
For practitioners and researchers looking to adopt or extend this work, the following guidelines clarify when and how to utilize this approach:

*   **When to Prefer This Method:**
    *   **Choose the Textbook Approach if:** Your target application involves **complex reasoning**, **multi-step problem solving**, or **domain-specific expertise** (Math, Science, Engineering). If your use case requires the model to "show its work" or understand a sequence of visual changes, this data regime is superior to standard web crawls.
    *   **Stick to Web-Scale Data if:** Your application relies on **broad world knowledge**, pop culture recognition, or open-ended creative generation where strict logical coherence is less critical than diversity.
    *   **Hybrid Recommendation:** For most production systems, a **continual pretraining** strategy is recommended. Start with a base model pretrained on broad web data (for general knowledge), then perform continual pretraining on the Multimodal Textbook (or a domain-specific subset of it) to inject reasoning capabilities before final instruction tuning.

*   **Implementation Prerequisites:**
    *   **Compute Budget:** Replicating the curation pipeline is computationally expensive. Practitioners must account for the cost of running heavy inference models (Whisper, Qwen2-72B, InternVL2) on video data. It is not a "free" data source; the quality comes at a high processing cost.
    *   **Domain Adaptation:** If applying this to a new domain (e.g., Medical Imaging), one cannot simply swap the videos. You must first construct a **domain-specific taxonomy** (as done in Section 3.1) to guide the collection, ensuring the data covers the necessary foundational concepts systematically.
    *   **Keyframe Algorithm:** Do not default to CLIP-based frame selection for technical content. As proven in the ablation studies (Table 6), **SSIM (Structural Similarity)** is essential for capturing subtle changes in diagrams and equations. Using semantic clustering for technical data will likely result in missed information and degraded performance.

*   **Integration into Existing Pipelines:**
    *   The dataset is designed to be drop-in compatible with architectures supporting interleaved inputs (e.g., Idefics, Flamingo, LLaVA-NeXT).
    *   When fine-tuning, ensure the **prompt template** respects the interleaved nature of the data. Do not strip images to text-only; the model's reasoning capability is contingent on the joint processing of the visual sequence and the refined text.

In summary, the "Multimodal Textbook" provides a blueprint for the next generation of VLMs: models that do not just recognize the world, but **understand** it through the structured, logical lens of human education. By prioritizing coherence over chaos, this work paves the way for AI systems capable of genuine scientific reasoning and reliable expert assistance.
