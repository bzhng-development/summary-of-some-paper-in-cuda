This paper introduces **Magistral**, Mistral's first family of reasoning models, demonstrating that pure Reinforcement Learning (RL) using the **Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)** algorithm can boost the **AIME-24** pass@1 accuracy of **Mistral Medium 3** from 26.8% to **73.6%** without any distillation from prior reasoning models. The authors release **Magistral Small (24B)** under Apache 2.0, which combines supervised fine-tuning on teacher traces with further RL to achieve **70.7%** on AIME-24, while surprisingly showing that text-only RL training preserves and even enhances multimodal reasoning capabilities on benchmarks like **MMMU-Pro**. This work matters because it validates a scalable, ground-up RL infrastructure that eliminates the need for expensive "cold-start" reasoning data while maintaining broad capabilities in coding, multilingual tasks, and function calling.

## 2. Context and Motivation

The development of Large Language Models (LLMs) capable of complex reasoning represents a pivotal shift from standard chatbots to systems that can solve novel, multi-step problems. However, the path to achieving these capabilities has been dominated by a specific paradigm: **distillation**. Prior to this work, the prevailing assumption in the community, reinforced by seminal studies like DeepSeek-R1, was that to create a strong reasoning model, one must first distill (copy) long chains-of-thought from a superior "teacher" model into a "student" model via Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). Only after this "cold start" could Reinforcement Learning (RL) be effectively applied to refine performance.

This paper addresses a critical gap in that methodology: **Is distillation strictly necessary?** The authors question whether the reliance on pre-existing reasoning traces limits the scalability and independence of model development. If a model must learn reasoning by mimicking another, the field remains bottlenecked by the availability of high-quality teacher data. Magistral posits that a sufficiently robust RL infrastructure can train a model to reason from scratch, using only verifiable rewards (e.g., "is the math answer correct?") without ever seeing a human or AI-generated example of *how* to think.

### The Limitations of Prior Approaches

To understand the significance of Magistral, we must examine the shortcomings of the existing "Distillation + RL" recipe:

*   **Dependency on Teacher Quality:** Distillation requires a teacher model that is already smarter than the student. This creates a ceiling; the student struggles to surpass the teacher's reasoning depth unless the RL phase is exceptionally powerful.
*   **Data Scarcity and Cost:** Generating high-quality reasoning traces (long chains-of-thought) for diverse domains (math, code, science) is computationally expensive and data-intensive.
*   **The "Small Model" Ceiling:** Previous literature, specifically citing DeepSeek-AI et al. [2025], suggested that smaller models (e.g., ~24B parameters) fail to learn reasoning via pure RL. The consensus was that small models lack the inherent capacity to discover reasoning strategies without the "hint" provided by distillation. They would collapse or fail to converge if trained with RL alone.
*   **Modal Fragility:** There was a concern that optimizing a multimodal model (one that sees images and text) strictly on text-based reasoning tasks might degrade its vision capabilities, a phenomenon known as catastrophic forgetting.

### The Magistral Proposition: Pure RL from Scratch

Magistral positions itself as a direct challenge to these constraints. The authors argue that the failure of prior pure RL attempts was not due to algorithmic impossibility, but rather **infrastructure and training instability**. By building a ground-up RL stack, they aim to demonstrate three counter-intuitive findings:

1.  **RL Alone is Sufficient:** A base model (Mistral Medium 3) can be transformed into a top-tier reasoning model (Magistral Medium) using **only** reinforcement learning on verifiable tasks, achieving a nearly **50% boost** in AIME-24 accuracy without a single distillation step.
2.  **Small Models Can Learn via RL:** Contradicting prior claims, the paper shows that even smaller models (Magistral Small, 24B) can benefit significantly from pure RL, though the combination of SFT and RL yields the absolute best results for this size class.
3.  **Cross-Modal Generalization:** Training exclusively on text data does not harm multimodal abilities. Instead, the enhanced reasoning skills learned from text transfer "for free" to visual tasks, improving performance on benchmarks like MMMU-Pro.

### Why This Matters

The implications of this work extend beyond benchmark scores. Theoretically, it suggests that **reasoning is an emergent property of optimization against verifiable goals**, not a behavior that must be explicitly taught via imitation. Practically, it offers a more scalable path forward: if models can learn to reason by simply trying to solve problems and receiving binary feedback (correct/incorrect), the AI community can bypass the bottleneck of curating massive datasets of synthetic reasoning traces.

Furthermore, the paper introduces a novel approach to **multilingual reasoning**. Standard RL training often causes models to default to English for their internal "thought process," even when prompted in other languages. Magistral introduces a specific reward mechanism to force the model to reason in the user's language, addressing a significant usability gap in prior reasoning models that often exhibited unwanted code-switching.

In summary, Magistral does not just present a new model; it validates a new **training philosophy**. It shifts the focus from "how do we copy reasoning?" to "how do we engineer an environment where reasoning emerges?" This distinction is crucial for the future of autonomous AI agents that must learn to solve problems in domains where no human teacher exists.

Failure to meet these criteria results in an immediate reward of **0.0**, and the generation is not graded further. **Correctness** provides the primary learning signal. For math, a rule-based verifier extracts the content inside the last `\boxed{}` and compares it to the ground truth using SymPy to handle equivalent but syntactically different expressions (e.g., $2/4$ vs $0.5$). A correct match awards an additional **0.9**, bringing the total reward to **1.0**. For code, the system extracts the first code block and compiles/executes it against a randomly selected subset of 20 test cases (with a 4-second timeout per test). Passing all tests yields the full **0.9** bonus.

**Length Penalty** is applied to prevent the model from generating unnecessarily long chains-of-thought, which wastes compute and hits context limits. The penalty is "soft," meaning it gradually increases as the generation approaches the maximum limit. The reward function $R_{length}(y)$ is defined as:
$$ R_{length}(y) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } |y| \le l_{max} - l_{cache} \\ -0.1 \cdot \frac{|y| - l_{max} + l_{cache}}{l_{cache}} & \text{if } l_{max} - l_{cache} < |y| \le l_{max} \\ -0.1 & \text{if } |y| > l_{max} \end{cases} $$
Here, $l_{max}$ is the hard cutoff, and $l_{cache}$ defines a buffer zone where the penalty linearly interpolates from 0 to -0.1. During training, these thresholds were dynamically increased (e.g., from 16k to 32k tokens) to encourage the model to explore longer reasoning paths as its capabilities grew.

**Language Consistency** addresses a unique failure mode where models mix languages (e.g., thinking in English but answering in Spanish, or vice versa). To enforce monolingual reasoning, the authors translated 10% of the English training problems into French, Spanish, Italian, German, Chinese, and Russian. A fastText classifier evaluates the language of the problem, the thought trace, and the final answer. If all three components match, an additional **0.1** reward is granted. This simple incentive successfully forced the model to perform its internal reasoning in the user's language without degrading performance.

#### Asynchronous Infrastructure
The efficiency of Magistral relies on a novel **asynchronous training pipeline** designed to handle the extreme variance in generation lengths inherent to reasoning tasks. In a synchronous system, the trainer would have to wait for the slowest generation in a batch to finish before updating weights, leading to massive idle time since some math proofs take 5x longer than others.

Magistral decouples the **Generators** from the **Trainers**. Generators run continuously at maximum throughput, pulling prompts and producing completions using the latest available weights. As soon as a single completion finishes, it is sent to a Verifier. Once scored, the result is routed to a Trainer. Crucially, the Trainers do not wait for a specific batch of prompts to complete; instead, they accumulate finished sequences from the stream until a fixed batch size (e.g., 2048 sequences) is reached, at which point a gradient step is performed.

When the Trainers update the weights, the new parameters are broadcast to the Generators via **NCCL** (a high-speed GPU communication library) in under 5 seconds. A key design choice here is that **in-flight generations are not discarded**. If a Generator is in the middle of creating a long chain-of-thought when new weights arrive, it seamlessly switches to the new model for the subsequent tokens. While the Key-Value (KV) cache contains states computed by the old weights, the authors found that recomputing the cache was unnecessary for stability, likely due to the inherent off-policy correction capabilities of the GRPO loss function. This approach ensures that the GPU clusters remain fully utilized, and the model weights are updated as frequently as possible, keeping the training "on-policy" despite the asynchronicity.

To further optimize the Trainer side, the system uses a **greedy collation algorithm**. Since minibatches are formed from variable-length sequences, naive packing leads to significant padding (wasted compute). The system sorts incoming sequences by descending length and fits them into microbatches of fixed token sizes, reducing padding by **19%** and ensuring homogeneous workloads across data-parallel workers.

#### Data Curation and Difficulty Filtering
The success of pure RL depends heavily on the "Goldilocks" principle: the training data must be neither too easy (providing no learning signal) nor too hard (providing no positive examples). Magistral employs a rigorous **two-stage difficulty filtering** pipeline to curate its dataset.

In the first stage, the authors used **Mistral Large 2** to sample 16 solutions for each of the ~700k initial math problems. Problems that were never solved (too hard) or always solved (too easy) were discarded. However, relying solely on Mistral Large 2 risked discarding genuinely difficult problems that the model simply couldn't solve yet.

To address this, a second stage was introduced using a **stronger, RL-trained checkpoint** (an early version of Magistral). This model re-graded the entire dataset, sampling 16 responses per problem again. This second pass allowed the retention of challenging problems that the initial model failed but the stronger model could solve. Furthermore, the system filters out problems where the model's consensus answer contradicts the provided ground truth, flagging potential errors in the dataset itself. This process reduced the initial 699k math samples to a highly curated set of **38k** high-quality, verifiable problems. A similar process was applied to code data, resulting in **35k** problems with verified test cases, ensuring that every training step provides a meaningful gradient signal.



## 5. Experimental Analysis

To validate the efficacy of the Magistral training stack, the authors conducted a comprehensive evaluation across mathematics, coding, STEM, and multimodal domains. The experimental design is structured to answer two distinct questions: (1) Can pure Reinforcement Learning (RL) transform a base model into a top-tier reasoning engine without distillation? and (2) What is the optimal training paradigm for smaller models given a strong teacher?

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Baselines

The evaluation suite targets high-difficulty reasoning tasks where standard chatbots typically fail.
*   **Mathematics:** The primary benchmarks are **AIME'24** and **AIME'25** (American Invitational Mathematics Examination), which consist of extremely challenging competition problems. The authors also report on **MATH-500**, a subset of the standard MATH dataset.
*   **Coding:** Performance is measured on **LiveCodeBench** (versions v5 and v6), a contamination-free benchmark of competitive programming problems, and **Aider Polyglot**, which tests polyglot code generation.
*   **STEM & General Knowledge:** The **GPQA** (Graduate-Level Google-Proof Q&A) benchmark assesses scientific reasoning, while **Humanity's Last Exam** (text-only subset) provides a broad stress test across dozens of subjects.
*   **Multimodal:** To test cross-modal transfer, the models are evaluated on **MathVista**, **MMMU**, and **MMMU-Pro**, which require interpreting images to solve problems.

**Baselines and Metrics:**
The primary baseline for comparison is the work of **DeepSeek-AI et al. [2025]**, specifically the trajectory from DeepSeek-v3 to DeepSeek-R1-Zero (pure RL) and DeepSeek-R1 (SFT + RL). This allows for a direct comparison of "RL-only" vs. "Distillation + RL" strategies.
*   **Metrics:** For AIME, the paper reports both **pass@1** (accuracy of a single sample) and **maj@64** (accuracy via majority voting over 64 samples), the latter reducing variance in stochastic generation. For coding and other benchmarks, standard pass@1 accuracy is used.
*   **Inference Setup:** Evaluations use a temperature of **0.7** for math and GPQA, and **0.95** for coding. Crucially, the maximum token length is set to **40k** for AIME and LiveCodeBench to accommodate the long chains-of-thought generated by the model, and **32k** for others.

### 5.2 Magistral Medium: The Case for Pure RL

The most striking result of this paper is the performance of **Magistral Medium**, which was trained on **Mistral Medium 3** using *only* RL, with no cold-start data from other reasoning models.

**Table 2** presents the core quantitative findings. The pure RL pipeline yields a massive leap in capability:
*   **AIME'24:** Accuracy jumps from **26.8%** (Mistral Medium 3) to **73.6%** (Magistral Medium). This represents a **~47 percentage point increase**, or nearly a **50% relative boost** in performance.
*   **AIME'25:** The model improves from **21.2%** to **64.9%**, demonstrating that the gain generalizes to unseen, harder exam years.
*   **Coding:** On LiveCodeBench (v5), the score more than doubles, rising from **29.1%** to **59.4%**.
*   **STEM:** GPQA accuracy increases from **59.6%** to **70.8%**.

**Comparison with DeepSeek:**
When compared to the DeepSeek-R1-Zero experiment (their pure RL attempt), Magistral Medium significantly outperforms it on AIME'24 (**73.6%** vs. **71.0%**). While DeepSeek-R1 (which used distillation) achieves a slightly higher score of **79.8%**, Magistral Medium closes the gap remarkably well without ever seeing a single reasoning trace from a teacher. This suggests that the "ceiling" for pure RL is much higher than previously believed, provided the infrastructure and data curation are robust.

The authors also note that with **majority voting (maj@64)**, Magistral Medium reaches **90.0%** on AIME'24, indicating that the model possesses the latent knowledge to solve these problems consistently, even if its single-shot sampling occasionally misses the mark.

### 5.3 Magistral Small: Optimizing the Student Model

For the 24-billion parameter **Magistral Small**, the authors explore three training paradigms to determine the best path for smaller models:
1.  **SFT Only:** Fine-tuning Mistral Small 3 on reasoning traces generated by Magistral Medium.
2.  **RL Only:** Training Mistral Small 3 from scratch with pure RL.
3.  **SFT + RL:** Fine-tuning on traces first, then applying RL (the final Magistral Small).

**Table 3** reveals a nuanced picture that challenges prior literature:
*   **RL Works on Small Models:** Contrary to claims that small models cannot learn reasoning via pure RL, the "RL-only" variant achieves **65.8%** on AIME'24, nearly matching the "SFT-only" variant (**65.4%**). This contradicts the notion that distillation is a strict prerequisite for smaller architectures.
*   **The Synergy of SFT + RL:** The combination yields the best results. **Magistral Small (SFT + RL)** achieves **70.7%** on AIME'24 (pass@1) and **83.3%** (maj@64).
*   **Trade-offs:** Interestingly, while SFT+RL wins on math, the "RL-only" model actually outperforms the combined model on **MATH-500** (**95.4%** vs. **95.9%** is close, but RL-only is competitive) and shows distinct behaviors. However, on coding (LiveCodeBench v5), the SFT+RL model (**55.8%**) clearly surpasses both SFT-only (**52.2%**) and RL-only (**46.4%**).

This suggests that for smaller models, distillation provides a valuable "initialization" of reasoning patterns, but RL is still essential to push performance to the state-of-the-art. The "RL-only" path is viable but slightly less efficient than the hybrid approach for this specific model size.

### 5.4 Multilingual and Cross-Domain Generalization

A unique contribution of Magistral is its ability to reason in the user's language. **Table 4** reports performance on translated versions of AIME'24.
*   **Performance Drop:** The model performs slightly lower in non-English languages, ranging from **63.7%** (Chinese) to **69.3%** (Spanish), compared to **73.6%** in English.
*   **Analysis:** The authors attribute this **4–10% drop** not to a lack of reasoning ability, but to the constraint of forcing the *entire* chain-of-thought into the target language. Since the training data was predominantly English, the model has less practice reasoning in, say, Russian. However, the fact that it solves these problems at all in the target language is a significant usability win over models that default to English internally.

**Cross-Domain Generalization (Table 5):**
The authors tested whether training on one domain (math) helps another (code), and vice versa.
*   Training on **Math Only** improved Code performance on LiveCodeBench from **22.7%** (baseline) to **38.3%**.
*   Training on **Code Only** improved Math performance on AIME'24 from **32.2%** to **49.7%**.
This confirms that RL fosters a **general reasoning capability** rather than rote memorization of domain-specific tricks. The ability to "think step-by-step" learned in math transfers effectively to debugging code, and the logical structure learned in code aids mathematical proofs.

### 5.5 Ablation Studies and Failure Modes

The paper includes rigorous ablations to justify its design choices and honestly report what *didn't* work.

**Batch Size Dynamics (Figure 6):**
In asynchronous RL, the relationship between the number of concurrent sequences ($n_{async}$), the batch size ($n_{batch}$), and the minibatch size ($n_{minibatch}$) is critical.
*   **Finding:** Performance remains stable as long as $n_{batch} = n_{minibatch}$ and the ratio $n_{async}/n_{batch} \le 2$.
*   **Failure Case:** When the authors decreased $n_{minibatch}$ while keeping $n_{batch}$ large (i.e., performing multiple gradient steps on the same batch), performance degraded sharply. This suggests that doing multiple updates on a single batch of off-policy data introduces too much noise or bias, destabilizing the learning process.

**Advantage Normalization (Figure 7):**
The authors compared normalizing advantages within a minibatch vs. within a group (per prompt). Unlike some prior works that warned of bias, Magistral found **no significant difference** in final performance or generation length growth between these methods. They settled on minibatch normalization for simplicity.

**Unsuccessful Approaches (Section 7.4):**
*   **Proportional Rewards for Code:** The team experimented with giving partial credit for code that passes *some* tests (e.g., 50% reward for 50% pass rate) instead of a binary pass/fail.
    *   *Result:* While this allowed the model to learn faster initially (discarding 3x less data), the final performance on LiveCodeBench was **2% lower** than with binary rewards. The authors hypothesize that partial rewards provide a "false signal," reinforcing partially incorrect logic that happens to pass weak test cases.
*   **Entropy Bonus:** Adding an explicit entropy bonus term to the loss to encourage exploration proved unstable. On math data, it collapsed entropy; on mixed data, it caused entropy to explode. The authors found that simply relaxing the clipping upper bound ($\epsilon_{high}$) in the PPO/GRPO update was a far more stable and effective method for maintaining exploration.

### 5.6 The "Multimodal Free Lunch"

Perhaps the most surprising finding is presented in **Figure 10** and **Section 7.2**. Since Magistral was trained *exclusively* on text-based math and code problems, one might expect its vision capabilities to atrophy (catastrophic forgetting). Instead, the opposite occurred.
*   **MMMU-Pro:** Accuracy improved by **12%** (reaching **52.1%**).
*   **MMMU:** Accuracy improved by **5%** (reaching **70%**).
*   **MathVista:** Showed no regression.

The authors argue that the enhanced textual reasoning capabilities learned during RL transfer to visual tasks. When the model sees an image, its improved ability to decompose problems and verify steps allows it to solve the associated questions more effectively, even though the *vision encoder* itself was not updated. This implies that reasoning is a modality-agnostic skill that, once strengthened in text, lifts performance across the board.

### 5.7 Critical Assessment

The experiments in Magistral convincingly support the paper's central thesis: **Pure RL is a viable and powerful path to reasoning capabilities.**
*   **Strengths:** The sheer magnitude of the gain on AIME (from ~27% to ~74%) without distillation is undeniable evidence of the method's potency. The ablation studies are thorough, particularly the rejection of proportional rewards and the careful tuning of batch dynamics, which adds credibility to the results. The demonstration of multimodal improvement is a compelling "free lunch" argument.
*   **Limitations:** The multilingual results, while promising, show a tangible performance penalty (up to 10%) when forcing non-English reasoning, suggesting that true multilingual reasoning requires more than just a reward signal—it may need more diverse training data. Additionally, while "RL-only" works for the 24B model, the "SFT+RL" hybrid still wins, implying that distillation retains value for efficiency in smaller models, even if it isn't strictly necessary for convergence.

In summary, the experimental analysis validates Magistral not just as a new model, but as a proof-of-concept for a more scalable, independent, and robust approach to building reasoning AI.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Magistral demonstrates that pure Reinforcement Learning (RL) can unlock state-of-the-art reasoning capabilities, the approach is not without significant constraints, assumptions, and trade-offs. The success of the pipeline relies on specific conditions that may not hold in all domains, and the "ground-up" philosophy introduces computational costs that differ from distillation-based approaches.

### 6.1 Dependency on Verifiable Rewards
The most fundamental assumption of the Magistral framework is the existence of a **verifiable reward signal**. The entire training loop collapses without an automated, deterministic way to judge correctness.
*   **Scope Limitation:** The methodology is strictly limited to domains with ground-truth answers, such as mathematics (numerical solutions) and competitive programming (unit tests). As noted in **Section 4**, the authors explicitly filtered out "proof-based and multi-part problems" because they are difficult to verify with a rule-based system.
*   **The Open-Ended Gap:** This approach cannot currently be applied to open-ended reasoning tasks where correctness is subjective or requires human evaluation, such as creative writing, complex strategic planning without a simulator, or nuanced ethical reasoning. In these domains, the binary or proportional rewards used in Magistral (Section 2.2) cannot be computed, leaving a gap between "verifiable reasoning" and "general agentic behavior."
*   **Reward Hacking Risks:** While the authors mitigated this by filtering dataset errors (Section 4.1), the reliance on automated verifiers creates a risk of the model learning to "game" the verifier rather than solving the problem genuinely. For instance, in code generation, a model might learn to pass specific test cases via hard-coded shortcuts rather than implementing the general algorithm, a risk the authors acknowledged when rejecting proportional rewards for providing "false signals" (Section 7.4.1).

### 6.2 The Multilingual Performance Penalty
Although Magistral introduces a novel mechanism to enforce multilingual reasoning, the results reveal a clear trade-off between language consistency and peak performance.
*   **Quantifiable Degradation:** As shown in **Table 4**, forcing the model to reason in the user's language results in a **4.3% to 9.9% drop** in accuracy on AIME'24 compared to English. For Chinese, the score drops from **73.6%** to **63.7%**.
*   **Root Cause:** The authors attribute this to the scarcity of non-English reasoning data. While the model *can* reason in these languages due to the reward signal, it lacks the same density of high-difficulty training examples in French, Russian, or Chinese as it does in English. The reward function successfully prevents code-switching, but it cannot instantly conjure the latent reasoning capacity that comes from massive-scale exposure. This suggests that for truly equitable multilingual reasoning, the "pure RL" approach must be paired with a significantly more diverse, multilingual problem set, which was not fully realized in this iteration.

### 6.3 Computational Intensity and Infrastructure Complexity
The claim of avoiding "cold-start" data does not imply the process is computationally cheaper; rather, it shifts the cost from data curation to **online compute**.
*   **Online Sampling Cost:** Pure RL requires generating multiple samples (a "group") for every prompt at every training step to compute the group-relative advantage (Section 2.1). This is vastly more compute-intensive per gradient update than Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), where the data is static. The infrastructure described in **Section 3** requires a complex, asynchronous cluster of Generators, Trainers, and Verifiers running continuously.
*   **Infrastructure Barrier:** The reliance on a custom, highly optimized asynchronous pipeline (using NCCL for weight broadcasts and greedy collation to reduce padding) raises the barrier to entry. Reproducing these results requires not just the algorithm, but the specific distributed systems engineering to handle the "heterogeneous and hard to predict" workload of reasoning generations (Section 3). A standard synchronous RL setup would likely fail due to the extreme variance in completion times (up to 5x difference between shortest and longest sequences).
*   **Data Efficiency vs. Compute Efficiency:** While Magistral uses a small, curated dataset (38k math problems), it iterates over this data extensively with heavy sampling. The trade-off is **data efficiency** (learning from fewer unique problems) at the cost of **compute efficiency** (spending massive FLOPs on sampling and verification).

### 6.4 Small Model Constraints and the Value of Distillation
Despite the success of pure RL on Magistral Small, the ablation results in **Table 3** and **Section 5.3** reveal that distillation remains a valuable tool for smaller architectures.
*   **The Hybrid Advantage:** While "RL-only" on the 24B model achieved **65.8%** on AIME'24, the "SFT + RL" hybrid reached **70.7%**. This ~5 point gap indicates that for models with limited parameter counts, the "hint" provided by distillation accelerates convergence and leads to a higher final ceiling.
*   **Domain Specificity:** The gap is even more pronounced in coding. On LiveCodeBench (v5), the "RL-only" model scored **46.4%**, while the hybrid scored **55.8%**. This suggests that small models struggle to discover efficient coding patterns via pure exploration alone and benefit significantly from mimicking the structural patterns of a teacher before refining via RL. Thus, the claim that "distillation is unnecessary" is nuanced: it is *possible* to skip it, but *optimal* performance for smaller models still leverages it.

### 6.5 Unresolved Stability Challenges
The paper highlights several areas where the training dynamics remain fragile and require careful manual intervention.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The stability of the run depended heavily on dynamic tuning. For example, the upper clipping threshold ($\epsilon_{high}$) had to be manually adjusted between **0.26 and 0.28** throughout training to maintain stable entropy (Section 2.1). Similarly, the batch sizes and maximum completion lengths had to be scaled down or up in distinct stages to prevent memory overflow or stagnation (Section 5.2).
*   **Failure of Standard Regularization:** Common RL stabilization techniques, such as adding an entropy bonus or a KL divergence penalty, were found to be detrimental or unstable (Section 7.4.2 and 2.1). The authors removed the KL penalty entirely and found entropy bonuses caused divergence depending on the dataset mix. This implies that the "stable" configuration for large-scale reasoning RL is counter-intuitive and lacks the robust safety nets (like KL constraints) present in standard RLHF pipelines, making it harder to debug if the training collapses.

### 6.6 Open Questions on Scaling and Generalization
Finally, the paper leaves several critical questions unanswered regarding the long-term trajectory of this approach.
*   **The "Length" Ceiling:** The analysis in **Section 7.1** shows a strong correlation between output length and reward, following a logarithmic scaling law. It remains unclear if this trend continues indefinitely or if the model will eventually hit a "reasoning wall" where generating more tokens yields diminishing returns, requiring architectural changes rather than just more RL steps.
*   **Generalization to Non-Competitive Domains:** The training data is heavily skewed towards competitive programming and math Olympiads. It is an open question whether the reasoning strategies learned in these highly structured, rule-bound environments generalize effectively to messy, real-world scientific research or software engineering tasks where the "correctness" criteria are ill-defined and the feedback loop is slow.

In summary, Magistral proves that pure RL is a powerful engine for reasoning, but it is an engine that requires high-octane fuel (verifiable rewards), a custom-built chassis (asynchronous infrastructure), and careful tuning to avoid instability. It shifts the bottleneck from "finding a teacher" to "building a scalable verification and sampling loop," a trade-off that favors well-resourced labs with strong engineering capabilities.
