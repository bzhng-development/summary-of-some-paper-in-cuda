## 1. Executive Summary

To address the brittleness and opacity of existing Vision-Language-Action models that map perception directly to control, this paper introduces `MolmoAct`, a family of Action Reasoning Models (ARMs) that integrate perception, planning, and control through a structured three-stage pipeline generating depth perception tokens, visual reasoning traces, and action tokens. `MolmoAct-7B-D` demonstrates strong performance with 70.5% zero-shot accuracy on SimplerEnv Visual Matching tasks and 86.6% average success on LIBERO, surpassing closed-source baselines like $\pi_0$ and GR00T N1.5. Additionally, the authors release the `MolmoAct Dataset` comprising over 10,000 high-quality robot trajectories, enabling explainable behavior and achieving up to +22.7% task progression improvement over $\pi_0$-FAST in real-world bimanual settings.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Brittleness and Opacity in Robotic Foundation Models

The fundamental gap this paper addresses is that **current robotic foundation models lack structured reasoning between perception and action**. As stated in the Introduction, "most robotic foundation models map perception and instructions directly to control, which limits adaptability, generalization, and semantic grounding." This direct mapping creates two critical failures:

1. **Brittleness**: Models struggle to transfer across tasks, scenes, or embodiments when faced with distribution shifts
2. **Opacity**: Systems offer "little insight into why a robot chose one action over another" (Section 1)

To understand why this matters, consider the difference between how humans and current robots approach a task like "put the plate in the dishwasher." A human subconsciously weighs context, goals, and constraints—transforming perception into purpose through intermediate reasoning steps. Current Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models skip this reasoning entirely, attempting to predict control commands directly from images and text.

### Why This Problem Matters

The importance extends across both theoretical and practical dimensions:

**Real-World Impact:**
- Robots deployed in household or industrial settings must handle novel objects, lighting conditions, and spatial arrangements they never encountered during training
- Without explainable reasoning chains, debugging failures becomes nearly impossible—operators cannot understand why a robot reached for the wrong object or moved along an unsafe trajectory
- Language-only interfaces prove insufficient for precise control, as natural language is "inherently ambiguous about magnitudes, scales, and endpoints" (Section 2.4)

**Theoretical Significance:**
- The robotics field has lagged behind language and vision models in generalization capabilities despite massive dataset collection efforts
- The paper argues this gap "stems not just from limited data, but from a lack of structure" (Section 1)
- While language models have shifted "away from brute-force scaling toward structured learning," robotics has not made the same transition

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The paper positions itself against three categories of existing work, each with distinct shortcomings:

**1. Direct Perception-to-Action VLAs**

Models like OpenVLA (Kim et al., 2024), $\pi_0$ (Black et al.), and GR00T N1.5 (NVIDIA et al., 2025) follow a standard VLA architecture: visual encoder → language model → action tokens. While these models benefit from web-scale pretraining, they share a critical limitation:

> "Despite massive efforts in dataset collection and model scaling, today's VLAs remain brittle and opaque" (Section 1)

The fundamental issue is architectural: these models lack intermediate representations that could support reasoning, abstraction, and error diagnosis. When a $\pi_0$ model fails, there is no interpretable chain of decisions to examine—only the final action output.

**2. Language-Based Reasoning Approaches**

Several works attempt to add reasoning to VLAs through language-based Chain-of-Thought (CoT). Examples include ECoT (Zawalski et al., 2024), CoT-VLA (Zhao et al., 2025), and ThinkAct (Huang et al., 2025). These methods decompose high-level semantic tasks into subtasks using textual reasoning.

However, Section 2.3 identifies two crucial gaps:

- **Missing depth perception**: "Most VLMs are trained solely on RGB images and hence lack the ability of depth estimation and 3D understanding, which is critical for robotic manipulation"
- **Information loss**: "Attempting to distill complex 3D trajectories into linguistic descriptions often results in significant loss of spatial and temporal information"

Language-based reasoning operates at too high a level of abstraction for precise manipulation control. Saying "move toward the cup" does not specify the exact trajectory, approach angle, or depth coordinate needed for successful grasping.

**3. Trajectory-Based Steering Methods**

Prior work like RT-Trajectory (Gu et al., 2023) and HAMSTER (Li et al., 2025) offer forms of policy steerability through trajectory sketches. However, Section 6.2 notes important limitations:

- **RT-Trajectory** is "tightly coupled to the architectural constraints and training regimes of robotics transformers or diffusion models, and therefore lack the broader semantic generalization provided by pre-training on a VLM backbone"
- **HAMSTER** "outputs only 2D trajectories by the high-level VLM, with execution handled by a low-level policy trained on a fixed set of tasks"

These methods either sacrifice the generalization benefits of VLM pretraining or limit steering to predefined task sets.

### How MolmoAct Positions Itself

MolmoAct introduces a **structured three-stage reasoning pipeline** that directly addresses the gaps above:

```
RGB Image + Instruction → Depth Perception Tokens → Visual Reasoning Trace → Action Tokens
```

This architecture makes several key departures from prior work:

**Structured Intermediate Representations:**
Unlike direct perception-to-action models, MolmoAct generates three explainable reasoning chains that "can be independently decoded—yielding a depth map of the scene, a 2D trajectory overlay on the image plane, and executed actions in the physical world" (Figure 1 caption). Each stage conditions on the previous, ensuring spatial grounding throughout.

**Reasoning in Space, Not Language:**
Rather than using textual CoT, MolmoAct "teach[es] our models to reason in space" (Section 2.3). Depth perception tokens encode 3D structure through a VQVAE codebook with $N=128$ codes, while visual reasoning traces represent end-effector trajectories as polylines with $L$ points where $1 \leq L \leq 5$ (Equation 3). This preserves spatial and temporal information that language descriptions would lose.

**Dual-Modality Steerability:**
Section 2.4 introduces steering through both language instructions and user-drawn visual traces. This addresses the ambiguity problem: "visual reasoning traces are unambiguous, easily edited, and generalize across tasks without large text–action corpora or brittle language patterns." The evaluation in Section 5.6 shows visual trace steering achieves 75% success rate compared to 42% for open-ended language steering—a 33% margin.

**Data Efficiency Through Structure:**
While $\pi_0$ uses "at least 903M samples for pre-training," MolmoAct achieves competitive performance with "approximately 26.3M samples—an order of magnitude smaller" (Section 5.1). The structured reasoning pipeline enables more efficient learning from fewer examples, addressing the data collection bottleneck that constrains robotics research.

**Full Openness:**
Unlike closed-source baselines (GR00T N1.5, $\pi_0$), MolmoAct releases "all model weights, training code, MolmoAct Dataset and our action reasoning dataset" (Abstract). This establishes the model as "an open blueprint for building ARMs" rather than a proprietary system.

The positioning is clear: MolmoAct does not claim to replace VLA architectures but rather extends them with structured reasoning that enables explainability, steerability, and data-efficient learning—addressing the core brittleness and opacity problems that have limited robotic foundation model deployment.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems paper that introduces a new architecture for robotic control: an Action Reasoning Model that inserts structured intermediate representations between perception and action. The core idea is that robots should not map images directly to motor commands, but should instead generate three explainable reasoning chains—depth perception, trajectory planning, and action execution—in a cascaded autoregressive sequence.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

MolmoAct is a 7-billion parameter vision-language-action model that takes camera images and language instructions as input, then autoregressively generates depth maps, trajectory sketches, and robot commands as output. It solves the brittleness and opacity problems of prior robotic models by forcing the system to "think in space" through explicit intermediate representations before committing to physical actions, much like a human mentally simulates a reach before moving their arm.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The system consists of four major components connected in a sequential pipeline. First, a **Vision-Language Backbone** (inherited from Molmo) processes multi-view camera images and language instructions into a shared embedding space using a Vision Transformer encoder, a projection connector, and a decoder-only LLM. Second, a **Depth Perception Head** generates 100 discrete tokens that encode a compressed depth map of the scene using a VQVAE codebook with 128 entries. Third, a **Visual Reasoning Trace Head** produces a polyline of 1 to 5 points representing the planned end-effector trajectory in image coordinates normalized to [0, 255]. Fourth, an **Action Token Head** outputs N integers (one per degree of freedom) discretized into 256 bins representing normalized joint or end-effector commands. Information flows strictly forward: the depth tokens condition the trace generation, and both depth and trace tokens condition the final action prediction, ensuring each stage is spatially grounded in the preceding representations.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first explain the vision-language backbone architecture because all downstream reasoning depends on how images and text are encoded into the model's embedding space.
- We then detail the action tokenization scheme, as this establishes how continuous robot commands are converted into discrete tokens the LLM can predict.
- We next unpack the three-stage reasoning pipeline (depth → trace → action) in sequence, showing how each stage builds on the previous one.
- We describe the data curation pipeline that generates ground-truth labels for depth tokens and visual traces from existing robot datasets.
- We explain the three-stage training recipe (pre-training, mid-training, post-training) and how data mixtures change at each phase.
- We conclude with the steerability mechanism that allows users to override the model's planned trajectory by drawing directly on the camera image.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**This is an architecture paper that proposes a structured three-stage reasoning pipeline for robotic manipulation, where the model autoregressively generates depth perception tokens, visual reasoning traces, and action tokens in sequence rather than predicting actions directly from images.**

#### Vision-Language Backbone Architecture

MolmoAct builds upon the Molmo vision-language model architecture, which follows the standard three-component VLM design used in systems like LLaVA and Flamingo. The backbone consists of a visual encoder that transforms input images into patch-level embeddings, a vision-language connector that projects these visual features into the language model's embedding space, and a decoder-only large language model that processes both vision and text tokens autoregressively. For the visual encoder, the paper implements two variants: MolmoAct-7B-D uses ViT-SO400M/14 with 384px resolution from SigLIP2 (Tschannen et al., 2025), while MolmoAct-7B-O uses OpenAI ViT-L/14 with 336px resolution from CLIP (Radford et al., 2021). The vision-language connector is a two-layer MLP that maps vision features into the LLM embedding space, with attention pooling performed in 2×2 patch windows to reduce sequence length while preserving local spatial structure (Appendix A.3). The LLM backbone differs between variants: MolmoAct-7B-D uses Qwen2.5-7B (Qwen et al., 2025) with 4096-dimensional embeddings and 32 attention heads, while MolmoAct-7B-O uses OLMo2-7B (OLMo et al., 2024) with 3584-dimensional embeddings and 28 attention heads (Table 3). To handle multi-image inputs from robots with multiple cameras (e.g., front-view and wrist-mounted), the implementation processes all images into vision tokens independently, then appends index tokens ("Prefix 1", "Prefix 2", etc.) at the beginning of each image's token sequence before concatenating them (Appendix A.5). For high-resolution images (640×480 px from the MolmoAct Dataset), the model employs multi-scale cropping: the image is tiled into multiple square high-resolution crops plus a resized low-resolution full image, with a fixed overlap margin of 4 patches (approximately 56 pixels) to supply border patches with neighbor context (Appendix A.2). During pre-training on lower-resolution OXE robot data (320×240 px), no high-resolution cropping is applied to match the input distribution.

#### Action Tokenization Scheme

A standalone vision-language model cannot directly control a robot because it lacks a representation of the robot's action space and dynamics, so MolmoAct formulates action prediction as a vision-language sequence modeling task with specialized tokenization. For each action dimension (e.g., x-translation, y-translation, z-translation, roll, pitch, yaw, gripper state), the model normalizes values using dataset quantiles and discretizes them into 256 uniform-width bins between the first and ninety-ninth percentiles, which reduces the influence of outliers while preserving effective granularity (Section 2.2). This yields an N-dimensional action represented as N integers in the range [0, 255], where N equals the robot's degrees of freedom (7 for single-arm: 3 translations + 3 rotations + 1 gripper; 14 for bimanual). Prior work represents these 256 discretized action bins with 256 distinct language tokens taken from the tail of the vocabulary, but this creates a mismatch because continuous action bins possess ordinal structure and local correlation whereas arbitrary language tokens are effectively unrelated. MolmoAct adopts a similarity-preserving initialization: it identifies the final 256 tokens in the Qwen2 tokenizer, extracts each token's underlying byte-level BPE symbol, and assigns them monotonically to the 256 bins so that adjacent bins map to adjacent symbols (Section 2.2). This becomes the action token vocabulary $\mathcal{V}_{\text{action}}$, and the similarity between adjacent BPE symbols offers a smoother starting point for optimization, substantially reducing training time compared to GR00T N1.5's 50,000 GPU hours—MolmoAct achieves pre-training with only 9,216 GPU hours, over a 5× reduction. The full action vocabulary mapping from bin index (0 to 255) to token string is provided in Appendix C Tables 5 and 6, where each bin corresponds to a specific Unicode character sequence (e.g., bin 0 maps to `\u00e2\u00bd\u0139`).

#### Three-Stage Reasoning Pipeline

The core innovation of MolmoAct is its structured three-stage reasoning pipeline, where the model autoregressively generates three token sequences in strict order: depth perception tokens first, then visual reasoning trace tokens, then action tokens. This factorization is expressed mathematically as:

$$p(d, \tau, a \mid I, T) = \prod_{i=1}^{M+2} p(d_i \mid I, T, d_{&lt;i}) \times \prod_{j=1}^{L} p(\tau_j \mid I, T, d, \tau_{&lt;j}) \times \prod_{k=1}^{D} p(a_k \mid I, T, d, \tau, a_{&lt;k})$$

where $I$ is the RGB image observation, $T$ is the language instruction, $d$ is the depth token sequence, $\tau$ is the visual reasoning trace, and $a$ is the action token sequence (Equation 4, Section 2.3). By conditioning each stage on the preceding depth and trajectory tokens, the model ensures that final actions are spatially grounded in both the inferred depth and the planned motion sketch, rather than being predicted directly from raw pixels.

**Stage 1: Depth Perception Tokens.** Depth estimation is critical for spatial understanding and robot action prediction, yet most conventional VLMs and VLAs condition their outputs solely on RGB image input and text instruction, lacking depth estimation capability. MolmoAct introduces an auxiliary vocabulary for depth perception tokens defined as $\mathcal{V}_{\text{depth}} = \{\langle\text{DEPTH\_START}\rangle, \langle\text{DEPTH\_END}\rangle\} \cup \{\langle\text{DEPTH}_k\rangle\}_{k=1}^{N}$ where $N = 128$ (Equation 1, Section 2.3). For each input image, the target depth string is defined as $d = (\langle\text{DEPTH\_START}\rangle\langle\text{DEPTH}_{z^{\text{depth}}_1}\rangle \ldots \langle\text{DEPTH}_{z^{\text{depth}}_M}\rangle\langle\text{DEPTH\_END}\rangle) \in \mathcal{V}_{\text{depth}}$ with $M = 100$ tokens per image (Equation 2). Each $z^{\text{depth}}_i \in \{1, \ldots, N\}$ indexes a code in a VQVAE codebook $\mathcal{C} = \{c_1, \ldots, c_N\}$ produced by a pre-trained specialist depth estimator trained on depth maps from Depth Anything V2 (Section 2.3). The VQVAE is trained with a standard reconstruction objective to minimize reconstruction loss between input RGB images and their corresponding depth maps for 20 epochs on 10 million depth maps collected from RT-1, BridgeData V2, and BC-Z datasets (Section 3.1). All images are resized to 320×320 px during training and inference to enforce the representation of exactly 100 tokens per image. There is a deterministic one-to-one correspondence between each codebook index and a depth token in $\mathcal{V}_{\text{depth}}$ (i.e., index $k$ maps to $\langle\text{DEPTH}_k\rangle$), so $d$ is a discrete, explainable summary of the depth map of the scene. The model employs a specialist-to-generalist distillation strategy: the specialist produces $d$ as the ground-truth depth string, and the VLA is trained to predict this string autoregressively from the original RGB observation, thereby internalizing depth in a form that can condition downstream trajectory and action generation.

**Stage 2: Visual Reasoning Trace.** Planning is a crucial component in robotics, and rather than planning through subtask decomposition in language (which loses spatial precision), MolmoAct predicts an intermediate two-dimensional representation to better align visual inputs and control outputs across diverse robots and tasks. Given a certain image observation, the end-effector visual reasoning trace on that image is defined as a polyline with $L$ points where $1 \leq L \leq 5$ (i.e., 0 to 4 line segments):

$$\tau = (p_1, \ldots, p_L), \quad p_i = (u_i, v_i)$$

where for every $p_i = (u_i, v_i)$, the coordinates are normalized with respect to the given image dimension so that $u_i, v_i \in \{0, \ldots, 255\}$ (Equation 3, Section 2.3). Point $p_1$ corresponds to the coordinate where the robot end-effector is located in the given image, and all other points are the location of the end-effector in later frames (Section 2.3). For the full polyline, points are subsampled evenly from the future horizon of the episode between the current frame and the terminal frame. To generate ground-truth visual reasoning traces for training data, the authors employ Molmo (the base VLM) for data generation akin to synthetic data generation in NLP: for each timestep $t$, they extract the pixel coordinates $(u_t, v_t)$ of the robot's gripper, then prompt Molmo with the instruction "point to the robot gripper" for single-arm robots or "point to the robot gripper on the left/right" for bimanual embodiments (Section 3.1). Molmo returns a 2D coordinate $(x_t, y_t) \in \mathbb{R}^2$ with $(x_t, y_t) \in [0, 100]$, which is then rescaled so that $(u_t, v_t) \in \mathbb{Z}^2$ and $(u_t, v_t) \in [0, 255]$. At each timestep $t$, a visual reasoning trace is constructed by selecting a subsequence from $t$ to the episode end $e$, including the current point $(u_t, v_t)$, the final point $(u_e, v_e)$, and up to three intermediate points spaced uniformly between them (Section 3.1). If fewer than three intermediate points are available (i.e., $e - t &lt; 4$), all available points are included; if $t = e$, the trace contains only one point. This yields a visual reasoning trace between 1 to 5 points representing the future motion of the end effector.

**Stage 3: Action Tokens.** With depth perception tokens and visual reasoning trace generated, MolmoAct finally predicts action tokens $a = (a_1, \ldots, a_D) \in \mathcal{V}_{\text{action}}^D$ where $D$ is the number of degrees of freedom (Section 2.3). The action tokens are produced according to the factorization in Equation 4, meaning each action dimension $a_k$ is conditioned on the image $I$, instruction $T$, all depth tokens $d$, all trace points $\tau$, and all previous action tokens $a_{&lt;k}$. This ensures the final motor commands are grounded in both the 3D structure of the scene (via depth tokens) and the planned trajectory (via trace tokens). During post-training, the model applies action chunking (Zhao et al., 2023), formatting action predictions in fixed-length chunks of $N = 8$ timesteps (Section 4.3). For each action chunk, each timestep is tokenized in the same way as a single action, then grouped into a list, and the model is trained autoregressively to predict all action chunks. At evaluation time on LIBERO, the model executes full chunks before redoing action reasoning (Section 5.2).

#### Data Curation and Generation Pipeline

MolmoAct is trained on a diverse set of datasets across three training stages, with a key innovation being the conversion of conventional robot action datasets into action reasoning data by appending predicted depth perception tokens and visual reasoning traces to action tokens. A robot episode typically consists of a sequence of timesteps, where each timestep is a tuple $(I, T, a)_t$ containing an RGB observation image $I$, a language instruction $T$, and a ground-truth action $a$ specified either in end-effector space or joint space (Section 3.1). To convert any robot data into the Action Reasoning data format, ground-truth Depth Perception Tokens and Visual Reasoning Traces are generated for each timestep using the procedures described above. Beyond the core action reasoning data, the authors curate three auxiliary supervision datasets to strengthen spatial reasoning: (i) Auxiliary Depth Data where given an RGB observation and language instruction, the model only predicts the target Depth Perception Token sequence; (ii) Auxiliary Trace Data where given an RGB observation and language instruction, the model only predicts the corresponding Visual Reasoning Trace; and (iii) Trajectory-conditioned Action Data where given $o_t = (I, T, \tau)_t$ with ground truth Visual Reasoning Trace $\tau$, the model predicts the next action by taking the language $T$ and the trace-overlaid image $I^+ = I \oplus \tau$ (Section 3.1). The Trajectory-conditioned Action Data is curated mainly for enabling the steerability feature of MolmoAct.

The MolmoAct Dataset itself contains 10,689 high-quality trajectories of a single-arm Franka robot performing 93 unique manipulation tasks in both home and tabletop environments (Section 3.2). Data collection spanned two months and involved five full-time operators following strict protocols, with each trajectory averaging 112 timesteps. The dataset includes two primary settings: Home Environment Data with 7,730 trajectories spanning 73 distinct tasks and 20 verbs across living rooms, kitchens, bathrooms, and bedrooms; and Tabletop Data with 2,959 trajectories covering 20 atomic tasks performed with diverse objects to promote robustness and generalization (Section 3.2). For home environment data, the robot was mounted on a lightweight mobile platform similar to DROID (Khazatsky et al., 2024), enabling transport across different rooms. Long-horizon tasks like "clean up the dishes" were decomposed into subtasks such as "put the bowl in the dishwasher," "put the fork in the sink," and "cover the pot" to allow the policy to learn individual skill components before composing them. For tabletop data, tasks like "put the bowl in the dishwasher" were decomposed into atomic motions (open, pick, flip, put, close) and collected separately to build comprehensive motion primitives. The dataset uses two external camera views and one wrist camera view, recorded at 15 Hz for home environments and 20 Hz for tabletop data (Appendix E.1). For each task, the two external camera views are ranked based on scene clarity and occlusion, then labeled as primary and secondary camera views.

#### Three-Stage Training Recipe

MolmoAct employs a three-stage training recipe (pre-training, mid-training, post-training) with different data mixtures and compute allocations at each phase, as illustrated in Figure 2.

**Pre-training.** In the first stage, MolmoAct is pre-trained on a mixture of action reasoning data, auxiliary robot data, and multimodal web data totaling 26.3M samples (Section 4.1). For robot data, a subset of Open X-Embodiment (OXE) is used comprising RT-1, BridgeData V2, and BC-Z, totaling 10.5M samples converted into action reasoning data. Auxiliary robot data includes auxiliary depth data (1.5M samples), auxiliary trace data (1.5M samples), and trajectory-conditioned action data (10.5M samples). The model is co-trained with 2M samples of multimodal web data from Molmo's supervised fine-tuning stage, including academic datasets (VQA v2.0, Text VQA, OK-VQA, ChartQA, DocVQA, etc.) for general visual skills and PixMo for fine-grained understanding and pointing (Section 3.3). During pre-training, data is sampled at the following rates: RT-1 (20%), BridgeData V2 (12.5%), BC-Z (7.5%) for both action reasoning and trajectory-conditioned data, 7.5% from auxiliary depth and trace data, and 5% from multimodal web data (Figure 3). The pre-training uses 256 H100 GPUs to train the model with 100k gradient steps using a batch size of 512, which takes around 9,728 GPU hours (Section 4.1). At each training step, a batch of data pairs is drawn randomly from the entire data mixture by their assigned sampling rate. Learning rates are set to $1 \times 10^{-5}$ for ViT, $1 \times 10^{-5}$ for the connector, and $2 \times 10^{-5}$ for the LLM, with cosine decay over 10% of steps and epsilon $10^{-6}$ (Table 4).

**Mid-training.** Following pre-training, a second stage of mid-training is conducted using high-quality action reasoning data closely aligned with the target domain of household manipulation (Section 4.2). The MolmoAct Dataset is formulated into 1M action reasoning data samples and an additional 1M trajectory-conditioned action data samples, which the authors find beneficial for improving overall performance and action steering. To train on the MolmoAct Dataset, each sample—consisting of two side-mounted camera views and one wrist camera view, all sharing the same instruction and ground-truth action—is converted into two paired-view training examples by pairing each side view with the wrist view. For action reasoning data preparation, depth perception tokens and visual reasoning traces are generated only from the side views, while the wrist view is used solely for providing additional information. The model is trained on this modified dataset for 50k gradient steps with a batch size of 128, using 128 H100 GPUs over around 2,304 GPU hours (Section 4.2). Learning rates are reduced to $5 \times 10^{-6}$ for ViT, $5 \times 10^{-6}$ for the connector, and $1 \times 10^{-5}$ for the LLM (Table 4).

**Post-training.** After mid-training, the final post-training stage rapidly adapts the model to new tasks and embodiments using parameter-efficient LoRA fine-tuning (Section 4.3). For new tasks, a small set of 30 to 50 tele-operated demonstrations per task is collected, then perception tokens and visual reasoning traces are generated for each timestep. These demonstrations are converted into action reasoning data and trajectory-conditioned action data following the same process as mid-training data curation, with one key difference: action chunking is applied with fixed-length chunks of $N = 8$ (Section 4.3). The model is adapted to post-train on single or multi-tasks via LoRA fine-tuning with rank fixed at 32 and alpha at 16 to preserve the model's pre-trained capabilities. For simulation benchmarks (e.g., LIBERO), a batch size of 128 is used with 64 H100 GPUs; for real-world tasks, batch size is 64 with 32 H100 GPUs (Table 4). The learning rate is increased to $5 \times 10^{-4}$ for all components during post-training. The number of gradient steps varies by task: for LIBERO-Spatial and LIBERO-Object, 50k steps; for LIBERO-Goal, 40k steps (despite 50k configured); for LIBERO-Long, 80k steps (Table 7). For real-world single-arm tasks, steps range from 5k to 9k depending on task complexity (Table 10); for bimanual tasks, 6k to 9k steps (Table 11).

#### Action Steerability via Visual Reasoning Trace

Steerability is defined as the ability to guide a policy at test time to perform different behaviors using user-provided instructions, and MolmoAct introduces a novel visual trace steering mechanism that addresses limitations of language-only steering (Section 2.4). Language-only steering faces three practical challenges: (i) it requires large and diverse corpora of high-quality language–action pairs to learn reliable grounding between words and control; (ii) natural language is inherently ambiguous about magnitudes, scales, and endpoints; and (iii) post-trained models often exhibit narrow prompting habits, making them brittle to out-of-distribution phrasing. For manipulation, these issues translate into imprecise or inconsistent control. Rather than relying on ambiguous language prompts, MolmoAct allows the user to draw a visual reasoning trace $\tau$ directly on the camera image to indicate the desired end-effector path. A trace $\tau = (p_1, \ldots, p_L)$ with $1 \leq L \leq 5$ is overlaid onto the RGB image $I$ to form an augmented observation $I^+ = I \oplus \tau$ (Section 2.4). Visual reasoning traces are unambiguous, easily edited, and generalize across tasks without large text–action corpora or brittle language patterns. At test time, given $I$, instruction $T$, and a user-drawn trace $\tau$, the model constructs $I^+ = I \oplus \tau$ and generates the next-step action tokens $a = (a_1, \ldots, a_D)$ autoregressively according to:

$$p(a \mid I^+, T) = \prod_{k=1}^{D} p(a_k \mid I^+, T, a_{&lt;k})$$

(Equation 5, Section 2.4). By conditioning directly on the overlaid trace, the model executes closed-loop control that follows the user's sketch. Repeating this at each timestep yields precise, interactive steering that is both scalable and robust to out-of-distribution phrasing. The evaluation in Section 5.6 shows that visual trace steering achieves 75% success rate compared to 42% for open-ended language steering—a 33% margin—and surpasses the baseline $\pi_0$-FAST by 29% when steering via language instructions. The web interface enables users to modify the language instruction or sketch five points on the image for visual trace steering at test time (Appendix D.7). During training for steerability, half of the 100 demonstrations are collected in the regular way (direct task completion) and half explore alternative paths towards the same target conditioned on the language, helping the model learn how visual traces correlate with physical actions.

#### Design Choices and Rationale

Several key design choices distinguish MolmoAct from prior VLA architectures, each motivated by specific limitations in existing approaches. The decision to use discrete depth tokens rather than continuous depth values enables the model to treat depth estimation as a language modeling problem, leveraging the LLM's autoregressive generation capabilities while maintaining compatibility with the token-based action prediction pipeline. The VQVAE codebook with $N=128$ codes provides sufficient granularity for manipulation tasks while keeping the sequence length manageable at $M=100$ tokens per image. The visual reasoning trace is limited to $L \leq 5$ points because longer trajectories would increase sequence length without proportional benefit—the model can interpolate between waypoints, and 5 points suffice to capture the essential shape of manipulation trajectories (Section 2.3). The similarity-preserving action token initialization (adjacent bins map to adjacent BPE symbols) is motivated by the observation that continuous action bins possess ordinal structure and local correlation, whereas arbitrary language tokens from the tail of the vocabulary are effectively unrelated, leading to poor initialization for learning an action codebook (Section 2.2). The three-stage training recipe (pre-training on OXE, mid-training on MolmoAct Dataset, post-training with LoRA) balances generalization and specialization: pre-training provides broad robot manipulation knowledge, mid-training adapts to household environments with high-quality data, and post-training enables rapid adaptation to novel tasks with minimal demonstrations. The use of LoRA with rank 32 and alpha 16 during post-training preserves the model's pre-trained capabilities while allowing efficient adaptation—this is critical because full fine-tuning would risk catastrophic forgetting of the generalist knowledge acquired during pre-training. The action chunking with $N=8$ timesteps during post-training improves temporal coherence and reduces the frequency of replanning, which is important for smooth execution in real-world settings where inference latency matters.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section identifies the fundamental conceptual advances that distinguish MolmoAct from prior robotic foundation models. We distinguish between **fundamental innovations** (new paradigms or capabilities that change how the field approaches a problem) and **incremental improvements** (engineering optimizations that improve performance within existing paradigms).

### 4.1 Reasoning in Space, Not Language (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Makes It Different:**

Prior work on adding reasoning to robotic policies—such as ECoT (Zawalski et al., 2024), CoT-VLA (Zhao et al., 2025), and ThinkAct (Huang et al., 2025)—relies on **textual Chain-of-Thought**: the model generates intermediate language descriptions like "first grasp the cup, then move to the sink" before predicting actions. MolmoAct fundamentally departs from this by generating **spatial representations** instead: depth perception tokens encoding 3D structure, followed by visual reasoning traces encoding 2D trajectories in image coordinates.

This distinction matters because language and space have different information-theoretic properties for manipulation:

| Property | Language-Based CoT | MolmoAct's Spatial CoT |
|----------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Spatial precision | Low (ambiguous terms like "near", "left") | High (exact pixel coordinates in [0, 255]) |
| Temporal information | Lost (static descriptions) | Preserved (polyline encodes motion over time) |
| Depth awareness | Absent (2D text) | Explicit (100 depth tokens from VQVAE) |
| Grounding | Indirect (text → action) | Direct (spatial representation → action) |

As Section 2.3 states: "Attempting to distill complex 3D trajectories into linguistic descriptions often results in significant loss of spatial and temporal information." MolmoAct avoids this loss by keeping reasoning in the spatial domain throughout the pipeline.

**Why It's Significant:**

This represents a **paradigm shift** in how robotic reasoning is conceptualized. Rather than treating reasoning as a linguistic process that happens to control robots, MolmoAct treats reasoning as an inherently spatial process that happens to be implemented in a language model. The performance consequences are measurable: on LIBERO-Long (tasks requiring extended temporal reasoning), MolmoAct achieves 77.2% success compared to ThinkAct's 70.9%—a 6.3% gain (Table 2). More importantly, the spatial representations enable capabilities that language-based reasoning cannot provide, such as visual trace steering (Section 4.3).

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:**

This is a **fundamental innovation**. It changes the representation space in which reasoning occurs, not just the architecture or training procedure. Prior work optimized within the language-reasoning paradigm; MolmoAct establishes a new spatial-reasoning paradigm for robotics.

### 4.2 Explainable Intermediate Representations (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Makes It Different:**

Standard VLA models are **end-to-end black boxes**: given an image and instruction, they output actions with no interpretable intermediate state. When a $\pi_0$ or OpenVLA model fails, there is no diagnostic information—only the final incorrect action. MolmoAct's three-stage pipeline produces **three independently decodable outputs** (Figure 1):

1. **Depth map** (from depth perception tokens) — shows what 3D structure the model perceived
2. **Trajectory overlay** (from visual reasoning trace) — shows where the model planned to move
3. **Actions** (from action tokens) — shows what the model executed

This enables **failure mode diagnosis** that was previously impossible. If a robot fails to grasp an object, an operator can now determine whether the failure occurred at:
- **Perception stage**: The depth tokens encoded incorrect 3D structure
- **Planning stage**: The visual trace planned an unreachable trajectory
- **Execution stage**: The actions deviated from the planned trace

**Why It's Significant:**

Explainability in robotics has been a long-standing challenge. Section 1 notes that current VLAs offer "little insight into why a robot chose one action over another." MolmoAct addresses this directly by making the reasoning chain **visible and auditable**. This has practical implications for:

- **Safety certification**: Regulators can inspect reasoning traces to verify the model understood spatial constraints
- **Debugging**: Engineers can identify which stage of the pipeline needs improvement
- **User trust**: Operators can see the robot's intended trajectory before execution, enabling intervention if needed

The human preference evaluation in Section 5.5 supports this: MolmoAct achieves the highest Elo ratings in instruction-following tasks, suggesting users find its behavior more predictable and aligned with intentions.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:**

This is a **fundamental innovation**. While some prior work (e.g., RT-Trajectory) offers limited trajectory visualization, MolmoAct is the first to provide a **complete explainable chain** from 3D perception through planning to execution. This changes how robotic systems can be evaluated, debugged, and deployed in safety-critical settings.

### 4.3 Visual Trace Steerability (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Makes It Different:**

Prior steering methods fall into two categories:

1. **Language-only steering**: Users provide text commands like "pick up the red bowl" (used by $\pi_0$, OpenVLA, etc.)
2. **Trajectory steering with limited generalization**: Methods like HAMSTER (Li et al., 2025) enable trajectory input but "outputs only 2D trajectories by the high-level VLM, with execution handled by a low-level policy trained on a fixed set of tasks" (Section 6.2)

MolmoAct introduces **visual trace steering with full generalization**: users draw a polyline directly on the camera image, and the model executes closed-loop control following that sketch. This works across novel tasks, objects, and embodiments because the steering signal is spatial (universal) rather than linguistic (task-specific).

The evaluation in Section 5.6 quantifies the advantage:

| Steering Method | Success Rate |
|-----------------|--------------|
| Visual trace (MolmoAct) | 75% |
| Open-ended language (MolmoAct) | 42% |
| Open-ended language ($\pi_0$-FAST) | 46% |

Visual trace steering outperforms language steering by **33 percentage points** for MolmoAct, and MolmoAct with language steering outperforms $\pi_0$-FAST by **29 percentage points**.

**Why It's Significant:**

This introduces a **new human-robot interaction paradigm** that addresses the ambiguity problem inherent in language commands. Section 2.4 identifies three challenges with language-only steering:

1. Requires large corpora of language–action pairs for reliable grounding
2. Natural language is "inherently ambiguous about magnitudes, scales, and endpoints"
3. Post-trained models exhibit "narrow prompting habits, making them brittle to out-of-distribution phrasing"

Visual traces bypass all three: a drawn line has unambiguous endpoints, requires no language grounding, and generalizes across phrasing variations. This is particularly valuable for **ambiguous instructions** like "pick up the bowl" when multiple bowls are present—the user can simply draw which bowl to target.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:**

This is a **fundamental innovation**. While RT-Trajectory and HAMSTER explored trajectory-based control, MolmoAct is the first to combine: (i) VLM-based generalization across tasks, (ii) real-time interactive steering at test time, and (iii) closed-loop execution following the user's sketch. This enables a new class of collaborative human-robot workflows where humans provide spatial guidance and robots execute with precision.

### 4.4 Data Efficiency Through Structured Reasoning (Fundamental Innovation)

**What Makes It Different:**

The prevailing assumption in robotic foundation models is that **more data equals better performance**. Models like $\pi_0$ use "at least 903M samples for pre-training" (Section 5.1). MolmoAct challenges this by achieving competitive or superior performance with "approximately 26.3M samples—an order of magnitude smaller."

| Model | Pre-training Samples | SimplerEnv Visual Matching |
|-------|---------------------|---------------------------|
| $\pi_0$ (fine-tuned) | 903M+ | 58.7% |
| GR00T N1.5 (fine-tuned) | Proprietary (large) | 52.4% |
| MolmoAct (zero-shot) | 26.3M | 70.5% |
| MolmoAct (fine-tuned) | 26.3M | 71.6% |

MolmoAct's zero-shot performance (70.5%) exceeds $\pi_0$'s fine-tuned performance (58.7%) despite using ~34× less data.

**Why It's Significant:**

This demonstrates that **structure matters more than scale** for robotic generalization. The structured reasoning pipeline enables more efficient learning because:

1. **Depth tokens** provide explicit 3D grounding, reducing the need to learn spatial relationships implicitly from raw pixels
2. **Visual traces** provide intermediate supervision for motion planning, decomposing the learning problem
3. **Auxiliary tasks** (depth-only, trace-only, trajectory-conditioned action) provide additional learning signals without requiring more robot episodes

The ablation in Section 5.4 confirms this: mid-training on the MolmoAct Dataset (10K trajectories) yields a 5.5% average improvement, showing that **high-quality structured data** outperforms **large-scale unstructured data**.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:**

This is a **fundamental innovation** in the robotics learning paradigm. It shifts the field's focus from "collect more data" to "structure the learning problem better." This has practical implications: small labs without access to million-scale robot datasets can now train competitive models using structured reasoning approaches.

### 4.5 Specialist-to-Generalist Distillation for Depth (Incremental Innovation)

**What Makes It Different:**

Most VLMs and VLAs lack depth perception because they are trained solely on RGB images. MolmoAct introduces depth awareness through a **distillation approach**: a specialist depth estimator (trained on Depth Anything V2 outputs) produces ground-truth depth token sequences, and MolmoAct learns to predict these tokens autoregressively from RGB observations alone.

This differs from methods that:
- Use explicit depth sensors (RGB-D cameras) — requires hardware changes
- Train depth estimation end-to-end without supervision — unstable and data-inefficient
- Ignore depth entirely — limits 3D manipulation capability

**Why It's Significant:**

This enables **3D spatial reasoning without 3D sensors**, making the approach deployable on standard RGB camera setups. The VQVAE compression (100 tokens per image from a 128-code codebook) keeps the sequence length manageable while preserving sufficient spatial granularity for manipulation.

However, Section G (Limitations) acknowledges constraints: "fine-grained manipulation tasks require higher-resolution depth estimation. Increasing the number of depth perception tokens could enhance spatial reasoning." This suggests the current approach is a practical compromise rather than a theoretical optimum.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:**

This is an **incremental innovation** built on prior work (Bigverdi et al., 2024 showed perception tokens enhance visual reasoning). MolmoAct's contribution is applying this technique specifically to robotic depth perception and integrating it into the three-stage reasoning pipeline. The innovation lies in the integration, not the core technique.

### Summary of Innovations

| Innovation | Type | Key Advantage |
|------------|------|---------------|
| Reasoning in Space | Fundamental | Preserves spatial/temporal information lost in language CoT |
| Explainable Representations | Fundamental | Enables failure diagnosis and user trust |
| Visual Trace Steerability | Fundamental | New interaction paradigm with 33% higher success than language |
| Data Efficiency Through Structure | Fundamental | Competitive performance with 34× less data |
| Specialist-to-Generalist Depth | Incremental | 3D reasoning without 3D sensors |

The first four innovations represent **paradigm shifts** in how robotic foundation models are designed, trained, and interacted with. The fifth is an **engineering contribution** that enables the paradigm but is not itself a new paradigm. Together, they establish MolmoAct as both a state-of-the-art model and "an open blueprint for building ARMs" (Abstract) that reason in space rather than mapping perception directly to action.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section evaluates whether MolmoAct's structured reasoning pipeline delivers measurable improvements over existing Vision-Language-Action models. The experiments address six research questions spanning zero-shot capability, adaptation efficiency, generalization, data quality effects, instruction following, and steerability. We analyze each evaluation dimension systematically, citing specific numbers and assessing whether the evidence supports the paper's claims.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Experimental Setup

The authors employ a comprehensive evaluation strategy across simulation and real-world settings, using established benchmarks alongside novel human evaluation protocols.

**Simulation Benchmarks:**

| Benchmark | Platform | Tasks | Metric |
|-----------|----------|-------|--------|
| SimplerEnv (Visual Matching) | Google Robot | 3 tasks (Pick Coke Can, Move Near, Open/Close Drawer) | Success Rate (%) |
| SimplerEnv (Variant Aggregation) | Google Robot | Same tasks with distribution shifts | Success Rate (%) |
| LIBERO | Franka Emika Panda (simulated) | 40 tasks across 4 suites | Success Rate (%) |

The SimplerEnv benchmark tests out-of-the-box generalization on tasks aligned with MolmoAct's pre-training distribution (Section 5.1). LIBERO evaluates adaptation efficiency across four task categories: Spatial (object location reasoning), Object (object identity reasoning), Goal (goal state reasoning), and Long-horizon (multi-step task completion) (Section 5.2).

**Real-World Evaluations:**

| Setup | Robot | Tasks | Metric |
|-------|-------|-------|--------|
| Single-arm | Franka | 3 tasks (put_bowl_in_sink, wipe_table, table_bussing) | Task Progression Score (0-1) |
| Bimanual | Dual Franka | 3 tasks (set_table, lift_tray, fold_towel) | Task Progression Score (0-1) |
| OOD Generalization | Single-arm Franka | 3 tasks with 4 perturbation types | Task Progression Score (0-1) |

Task progression scores are defined with granular milestones. For example, `put_bowl_in_sink` awards 0.25 for grasping the bowl, 0.4 for moving into the sink, 0.7 for opening the gripper, and 1.0 for dropping the bowl at the target location (Appendix D.3). This provides more nuanced measurement than binary success/failure.

**Baselines:**

The paper compares against 15+ baseline models across different evaluation settings:

- **Direct VLA models:** OpenVLA (Kim et al., 2024), $\pi_0$, $\pi_0$-FAST (Black et al.), GR00T N1.5 (NVIDIA et al., 2025)
- **Trajectory-based models:** TraceVLA (Zheng et al., 2024), RT-Trajectory (Gu et al., 2023)
- **Reasoning-enhanced models:** CoT-VLA (Zhao et al., 2025), ThinkAct (Huang et al., 2025), Emma-X (Sun et al., 2024)
- **Generalist policies:** Octo-Base (Team et al., 2024b), RT-1-X, RT-2-X (Brohan et al., 2022; Zitkovich et al., 2023), SpatialVLA (Qu et al., 2025)

All baselines are evaluated using their official implementations and default configurations where available (Appendix D.3-D.7).

**Human Evaluation Protocols:**

Two novel evaluation methods assess capabilities beyond standard benchmarks:

1. **Arena-style instruction following:** 100 human annotators vote on model rollouts in head-to-head comparisons across 29 open-ended instructions, converted to Elo ratings (Section 5.5)
2. **Steerability evaluation:** 10 participants provide open-ended language instructions or draw visual traces to guide robot behavior, with task progression scored across 15 trials per condition (Section 5.6)

This multi-dimensional evaluation strategy is noteworthy: rather than relying solely on success rates, the authors measure explainability (via human preference), steerability (via interactive control), and data efficiency (via sample-matched comparisons).

### 5.2 Zero-Shot Performance After Pre-training

**Claim:** MolmoAct achieves strong zero-shot generalization despite using an order of magnitude less pre-training data than competitors.

**Evidence from Table 1 (SimplerEnv Visual Matching):**

| Model | Pre-training Samples | Pick Coke Can | Move Near | Open/Close Drawer | Average |
|-------|---------------------|---------------|-----------|-------------------|---------|
| $\pi_0$ (fine-tuned) | 903M+ | 72.7% | 65.3% | 38.3% | 58.7% |
| $\pi_0$-FAST (fine-tuned) | 903M+ | 75.3% | 67.5% | 42.9% | 61.9% |
| GR00T N1.5 (fine-tuned) | Proprietary | 69.3% | 68.7% | 35.8% | 52.4% |
| **MolmoAct (zero-shot)** | **26.3M** | **71.3%** | **73.8%** | **66.5%** | **70.5%** |
| MolmoAct (fine-tuned) | 26.3M | 77.7% | 77.1% | 60.0% | 71.6% |

Three observations stand out:

1. **Zero-shot exceeds fine-tuned competitors:** MolmoAct's zero-shot average (70.5%) surpasses $\pi_0$-FAST's fine-tuned average (61.9%) by 8.6 percentage points, despite using ~34× less pre-training data. This supports the claim that structured reasoning improves data efficiency.

2. **Strongest on Open/Close Drawer:** MolmoAct achieves 66.5% on Open/Close Drawer compared to $\pi_0$-FAST's 42.9%—a 23.6 point gap. This task requires precise spatial reasoning about drawer positions and gripper states, which aligns with MolmoAct's depth perception and trajectory planning capabilities.

3. **Marginal fine-tuning gain:** Fine-tuning improves MolmoAct by only 1.1 points (70.5% → 71.6%), suggesting the pre-trained model already captures most relevant knowledge. In contrast, $\pi_0$ shows larger fine-tuning gains, indicating weaker zero-shot initialization.

**Variant Aggregation Results (Table 1, right columns):**

| Model | Visual Matching Avg | Variant Aggregation Avg | Gap |
|-------|--------------------|------------------------|-----|
| RT-2-X | 60.7% | 64.3% | +3.6% |
| SpatialVLA | 70.0% | 65.8% | -4.2% |
| **MolmoAct (fine-tuned)** | **71.6%** | **72.1%** | **+0.5%** |

The paper states: "The performance difference between variant aggregation and visual matching is less than 1%, highlighting MolmoAct's robustness to visual and distributional shifts" (Section 5.3). This is a critical finding: most models degrade under distribution shift (SpatialVLA drops 4.2%), but MolmoAct maintains performance. The structured reasoning pipeline appears to provide stability against lighting, texture, and viewpoint changes introduced in variant aggregation.

**Assessment:** The zero-shot results convincingly support the data efficiency claim. However, an important caveat: MolmoAct's pre-training distribution is "most aligned with the Google Robot visual-matching tasks" (Section 5.1), which may partially explain the strong zero-shot performance. The fairer comparison is against models with similar pre-training data (e.g., OpenVLA also uses OXE subsets), where MolmoAct still shows advantages but with smaller margins.

### 5.3 Adaptation Efficiency in Post-training

**Claim:** MolmoAct adapts to novel tasks and embodiments more efficiently than baselines through lightweight fine-tuning.

**LIBERO Benchmark Results (Table 2):**

| Model | Spatial | Object | Goal | Long | Average |
|-------|---------|--------|------|------|---------|
| TraceVLA | 84.6% | 85.2% | 75.1% | 54.1% | 74.8% |
| OpenVLA | 84.7% | 88.4% | 79.2% | 53.7% | 76.5% |
| SpatialVLA | 88.2% | 89.9% | 78.6% | 55.5% | 78.1% |
| $\pi_0$-FAST | 96.4% | 96.8% | 88.6% | 60.2% | 85.5% |
| ThinkAct | 88.3% | 91.4% | 87.1% | 70.9% | 84.4% |
| **MolmoAct-7B-D** | **87.0%** | **95.4%** | **87.6%** | **77.2%** | **86.6%** |

Key findings:

1. **Highest overall average:** MolmoAct achieves 86.6%, exceeding $\pi_0$-FAST (85.5%) and ThinkAct (84.4%). This is the strongest autoregressive model on LIBERO among reported results.

2. **Dominant on Long-horizon tasks:** The 77.2% on LIBERO-Long exceeds ThinkAct (70.9%) by 6.3 percentage points—the largest single-category margin in the table. This directly supports the claim that spatial reasoning preserves temporal information better than language-based CoT. Long-horizon tasks require maintaining task state across multiple steps, and MolmoAct's visual traces provide explicit motion planning that language descriptions cannot capture.

3. **Competitive on Object tasks:** At 95.4%, MolmoAct trails $\pi_0$-FAST (96.8%) by 1.4 points. Object tasks primarily require recognizing and manipulating specific objects, which may benefit less from trajectory planning and more from pure perception capabilities. This suggests MolmoAct's advantages are task-dependent.

**Real-World Adaptation (Figure 5):**

The paper reports task progression scores across 25 trials per task:

| Setup | MolmoAct | $\pi_0$-FAST | OpenVLA | MolmoAct Advantage |
|-------|----------|--------------|---------|-------------------|
| Single-arm average | ~0.89 | ~0.79 | ~0.35 | +10% over $\pi_0$-FAST |
| Bimanual average | ~0.86 | ~0.63 | ~0.54 | +22.7% over $\pi_0$-FAST |

Specific task results from Appendix Tables 15-20:

- **Fold Towel (bimanual):** MolmoAct 0.80 vs $\pi_0$-FAST 0.52 vs OpenVLA 0.32
- **Lift Tray (bimanual):** MolmoAct 1.00 vs $\pi_0$-FAST 0.74 vs OpenVLA 1.00
- **Set Table (bimanual):** MolmoAct 0.77 vs $\pi_0$-FAST 0.24 vs OpenVLA 0.30
- **Wipe Table (single-arm):** MolmoAct 1.00 vs $\pi_0$-FAST 0.82 vs OpenVLA 0.27

The bimanual advantage (+22.7%) is particularly notable. Bimanual coordination requires precise temporal synchronization between two arms—exactly the type of spatial-temporal reasoning that visual traces encode. The Set Table task shows the largest gap (0.77 vs 0.24), likely because it requires coordinating banana placement with teapot pouring, a complex multi-arm sequence.

**Training Efficiency:**

Post-training uses LoRA with only 97M trainable parameters (out of 7B total), and converges in 5k-9k gradient steps for real-world tasks (Tables 10-11). This translates to 96-160 GPU hours per task on 32 H100s—a practical deployment cost for labs without massive compute budgets.

**Assessment:** The adaptation results strongly support the efficiency claim. The LIBERO-Long advantage (6.3%) and bimanual real-world advantage (22.7%) are both substantial and align with the hypothesis that spatial reasoning benefits complex, multi-step tasks. However, the Object task gap (1.4% behind $\pi_0$-FAST) indicates MolmoAct is not universally superior—its advantages are conditional on task complexity requiring spatial-temporal reasoning.

### 5.4 Out-of-Distribution Generalization

**Claim:** MolmoAct generalizes more robustly to distribution shifts than baseline models.

**Real-World OOD Evaluation (Figure 6a):**

The authors test four perturbation types on three tasks, comparing MolmoAct against $\pi_0$-FAST and OpenVLA:

| Perturbation Type | Description | MolmoAct Avg | $\pi_0$-FAST Avg | Advantage |
|------------------|-------------|--------------|------------------|-----------|
| In-Distribution | Standard task setup | 0.79 | 0.65 | +14% |
| Language Variation | Rephrased instructions | 0.67 | 0.29 | +38% |
| Spatial Variation | Changed object positions | 0.54 | 0.46 | +8% |
| Distractors | Added irrelevant objects | 0.75 | 0.54 | +21% |
| Novel Objects | Unseen object substitutions | 0.65 | 0.29 | +36% |

The paper states MolmoAct achieves "a 23.3% average improvement in task progression over $\pi_0$-FAST" across all generalization axes (Section 5.3).

Two patterns emerge:

1. **Largest gains on semantic shifts:** Language Variation (+38%) and Novel Objects (+36%) show the biggest margins. These require understanding task intent beyond surface-level cues—exactly what structured reasoning enables. When told "put the green tea into the yellow plate" instead of "put the green can into the yellow plate," MolmoAct infers the semantic equivalence while $\pi_0$-FAST fails (0.29 average).

2. **Smaller gains on spatial shifts:** Spatial Variation shows only +8% improvement. This is surprising given MolmoAct's depth perception capabilities. One explanation: spatial shifts (moving objects) are within the training distribution for all models, while language and object shifts require true generalization.

**Appendix Table 21** provides granular results. For "put the fruit into the blue plate" (Language Variation), MolmoAct scores 0.625 while both baselines score 0.0—complete failure. For "put the sponge into the yellow plate" (Novel Object, where sponge replaces green can), MolmoAct scores 0.875 while $\pi_0$-FAST scores 0.0. These zero scores indicate catastrophic failure on OOD inputs for baselines, while MolmoAct maintains partial task completion.

**Assessment:** The OOD results are among the most convincing in the paper. The 23.3% average improvement is substantial, and the pattern of larger gains on semantic vs. spatial shifts aligns with the reasoning-in-space hypothesis. However, the evaluation uses only 3 tasks with 4 trials per variant (Section 5.3), limiting statistical power. The 0.0 baseline scores on some conditions suggest those models are fundamentally incapable of the generalization being tested, which may overstate MolmoAct's relative advantage.

### 5.5 Effect of the MolmoAct Dataset (Ablation Study)

**Claim:** Mid-training on the MolmoAct Dataset provides consistent performance improvements.

**Ablation Results (Figure 6b, Section 5.4):**

Three tasks were evaluated with four model variants: MolmoAct with mid-training, MolmoAct without mid-training, $\pi_0$-FAST, and OpenVLA.

| Task | MolmoAct (with) | MolmoAct (without) | Improvement | $\pi_0$-FAST | OpenVLA |
|------|-----------------|-------------------|-------------|--------------|---------|
| Close Lid | 0.55 | 0.50 | +5% | 0.45 | 0.35 |
| Rotate Pot | 0.94 | 0.88 | +6% | 0.74 | 0.90 |
| Pour Tea | 0.82 | 0.77 | +5% | 0.48 | 0.45 |
| **Average** | **0.77** | **0.72** | **+5.5%** | **0.56** | **0.57** |

The paper states: "mid-training on the MolmoAct Dataset yields a consistent performance boost of around 5%" (Section 5.4).

**Key observations:**

1. **Consistent across tasks:** All three tasks show 5-6% improvement, suggesting the benefit is not task-specific. This supports the claim that high-quality household manipulation data improves general capability.

2. **Pre-trained MolmoAct still leads:** Even without mid-training, MolmoAct (0.72) exceeds both $\pi_0$-FAST (0.56) and OpenVLA (0.57) by 14.8% and 10.9% respectively. This indicates the pre-training + reasoning architecture provides most of the benefit, with mid-training as a refinement.

3. **Pour Tea shows smallest relative gain:** Rotate Pot and Close Lid show clearer improvements. Pour Tea involves fluid dynamics and precise positioning, which may be harder to learn from the 10K trajectory dataset alone.

**Appendix Table 22** provides per-trial data. For Rotate Pot, MolmoAct (with) achieves perfect 1.0 scores on 8/10 trials, while MolmoAct (without) achieves 1.0 on 7/10 trials—the difference is marginal but consistent. For Close Lid, the variance is higher: MolmoAct (with) ranges from 0.0 to 1.0 across trials, suggesting this task is inherently unstable regardless of training.

**Assessment:** The 5.5% improvement is modest but consistent, supporting the value of domain-specific mid-training. However, the absolute gain is smaller than the pre-training advantage (14.8% over $\pi_0$-FAST without mid-training). This suggests the reasoning architecture matters more than the specific dataset—a useful insight for researchers who cannot collect 10K+ trajectories. The ablation is well-designed (same tasks, same evaluation protocol), but limited to 3 tasks with 10 trials each.

### 5.6 Instruction Following and Human Preference

**Claim:** MolmoAct follows open-ended language instructions more reliably than baselines.

**Arena-Style Evaluation (Figure 8, Section 5.5):**

100 human annotators voted on 1,500+ model rollouts in head-to-head comparisons. Results are converted to Elo ratings:

| Model | Elo Rating | Pairwise Win Rate vs MolmoAct |
|-------|------------|-------------------------------|
| **MolmoAct-7B-D-Pretrain** | **Highest** | — |
| SpatialVLA | -109 points | 42% (MolmoAct wins 58%) |
| OpenVLA | Larger margin | 19% (MolmoAct wins 81%) |

The paper states: "MolmoAct-7B-D-Pretrain winning over SpatialVLA in 58% of comparisons and over OpenVLA in 81%" (Section 5.5).

**Qualitative Example (Figure 8, right):**

For the instruction "Put the redbull into the bowl," MolmoAct's execution trace aligns more closely with the intended instruction than other models. The visual reasoning trace shows a direct path from the Red Bull can to the bowl, while baselines show deviating trajectories.

**Visual Trace Generation (Figure 7):**

A separate evaluation tested trace generation quality against Gemini-2.5-Flash, GPT-4o, and HAMSTER. MolmoAct achieved "significantly higher Elo scores with non-overlapping 95% confidence intervals" (Section 5.5), indicating statistically significant superiority.

**Assessment:** The human preference results are compelling but have limitations:

1. **Subjectivity:** Elo ratings depend on annotator interpretation of "best matches the instruction." Different annotators may prioritize different aspects (speed, accuracy, safety).

2. **Pre-trained model evaluated:** The instruction-following evaluation uses MolmoAct-7B-D-Pretrain, not the mid-trained or post-trained variant. This tests zero-shot language grounding rather than task-specific adaptation.

3. **No absolute metrics:** Elo ratings show relative preference but not absolute capability. A model could win 58% of comparisons while still failing on 40% of tasks.

Despite these caveats, the 81% win rate over OpenVLA is substantial and suggests MolmoAct's behavior is more aligned with human expectations—consistent with the explainability claim.

### 5.7 Steerability Evaluation

**Claim:** Visual trace steering is more effective than language steering for guiding robot behavior.

**Steerability Results (Figure 9, Section 5.6):**

The pick_up_bowl task was used with two bowls (clean and dirty). Models were first given ambiguous instructions ("pick up the bowl"), then steered via visual trace or language.

| Steering Method | Model | Success Rate |
|-----------------|-------|--------------|
| Visual Trace | MolmoAct | 75% |
| Open Language | MolmoAct | 42% |
| Open Language | $\pi_0$-FAST | 46% |

The paper states: "steering using visual traces significantly outperforms steering via open-ended natural language instructions by a margin of 33%" (Section 5.6).

**Appendix Table 23** provides per-episode results. Key patterns:

- **Visual trace consistency:** MolmoAct with visual trace achieves 1.0 on 7/15 episodes, 0.85 on 3/15, and 0.5 on 3/15—never 0.0. This indicates reliable partial completion even when not perfect.
- **Language steering variance:** MolmoAct with open language ranges from 0.0 to 1.0 across episodes, with 7/15 episodes scoring 0.0 (complete failure).
- **Baseline comparison:** $\pi_0$-FAST with open language scores 0.0 on 9/15 episodes, confirming MolmoAct's language steering is also superior (42% vs effective ~30% for $\pi_0$-FAST).

**Why Visual Traces Work Better:**

Section 2.4 identifies three language steering challenges that visual traces bypass:

1. **No grounding required:** A drawn line directly specifies the path; no language-action mapping needs to be learned.
2. **Unambiguous endpoints:** Pixel coordinates [0, 255] are precise; language terms like "left bowl" are ambiguous.
3. **Robust to phrasing:** Visual traces work regardless of how the instruction is worded.

**Limitation Acknowledged (Section G):**

The authors note a critical constraint: "when we steer actions with a 2D visual trace, we are not leveraging the capability of MolmoAct to perform action reasoning in space." Because the cue is purely 2D, "the model lacks an explicit notion of depth: it often follows the intended path within the image plane (in-plane motion) but exhibits unintended or imprecise translation along the camera's depth axis (out-of-plane)."

This is a significant caveat: visual trace steering bypasses the depth perception tokens that are central to MolmoAct's reasoning pipeline. The authors hypothesize this could be mitigated by "conditioning on—or reusing—the model's predicted depth-perception tokens to lift the trace into 3D," but this is left for future work.

**Assessment:** The 33% steering advantage is the largest single improvement reported in the paper and strongly supports the visual trace innovation. However, the depth limitation is non-trivial: for tasks requiring precise 3D positioning (e.g., inserting a peg into a hole), 2D traces may be insufficient. The steerability feature is best suited for gross motion planning rather than fine manipulation.

### 5.8 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

**Strengths:**

1. **Multi-dimensional evaluation:** The paper does not rely on a single benchmark. Simulation (LIBERO, SimplerEnv), real-world (Franka), human preference (Elo), and interactive (steerability) evaluations provide converging evidence.

2. **Appropriate baselines:** Comparisons include both proprietary models ($\pi_0$, GR00T N1.5) and open models (OpenVLA, TraceVLA), with fair evaluation protocols (same fine-tuning data where applicable).

3. **Ablation studies:** The mid-training ablation (Figure 6b) isolates the dataset contribution from the architecture contribution, enabling clearer causal inference.

4. **Transparent reporting:** Appendix tables provide per-trial data (Tables 15-23), allowing readers to assess variance and statistical significance.

**Limitations:**

1. **Small sample sizes for OOD:** The OOD evaluation uses 3 tasks with 4 trials per variant (Section 5.3). While the effect sizes are large (23.3% average improvement), the narrow task set limits generalizability claims.

2. **Pre-training distribution alignment:** The paper acknowledges MolmoAct's pre-training is "most aligned with the Google Robot visual-matching tasks" (Section 5.1). This may inflate zero-shot performance on SimplerEnv relative to models trained on different data distributions.

3. **Steerability depth limitation:** As noted in Section G, visual trace steering does not leverage depth perception tokens, limiting 3D precision. This is an important constraint for deployment.

4. **Compute-matched comparisons missing:** While MolmoAct uses less data than $\pi_0$, the paper does not report whether training a baseline model on 26.3M samples would close the performance gap. The data efficiency claim assumes the gap is due to structure, not just more training on MolmoAct's part.

5. **Real-world task complexity:** The real-world tasks (wipe table, put bowl in sink) are relatively simple compared to household chores humans perform. Performance on more complex multi-object, multi-step tasks remains untested.

**Overall Assessment:**

The experimental evidence **largely supports** the paper's claims, with important caveats:

| Claim | Support Level | Notes |
|-------|--------------|-------|
| Zero-shot generalization | Strong | 70.5% vs 58.7% ($\pi_0$-FT) on SimplerEnv |
| Adaptation efficiency | Strong | 86.6% on LIBERO, +22.7% bimanual real-world |
| OOD robustness | Moderate-Strong | 23.3% improvement, but small task set |
| Mid-training benefit | Moderate | Consistent 5.5% gain, but smaller than architecture benefit |
| Instruction following | Strong | 81% win rate over OpenVLA in human eval |
| Visual steerability | Strong | 75% vs 42% success, but 2D depth limitation |

The most convincing results are the steerability advantage (33% margin) and the LIBERO-Long performance (6.3% over ThinkAct), as these directly test the spatial reasoning hypothesis. The OOD results are promising but would benefit from broader task coverage. The data efficiency claim is supported but would be strengthened by compute-matched baseline comparisons.

**Failure Cases and Trade-offs:**

Section G (Limitations) identifies several failure modes:

1. **Camera occlusion:** If the end-effector is occluded in the front camera, visual trace prediction degrades. This is a fundamental constraint of vision-based reasoning.

2. **Depth precision:** 100 depth tokens may be insufficient for fine-grained manipulation. The authors suggest increasing token count as future work.

3. **Inference speed:** The autoregressive generation of depth + trace + action tokens introduces latency compared to direct action prediction. This matters for real-time control.

4. **2D steering limitation:** Visual traces lack depth information, causing imprecise out-of-plane motion.

These limitations are honestly acknowledged and suggest clear directions for improvement. The trade-off is clear: MolmoAct gains explainability and steerability at the cost of inference speed and some 3D precision. For applications where human-robot collaboration and debugging matter more than raw speed, this is an acceptable trade-off.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While MolmoAct demonstrates substantial advances in robotic foundation models, the approach rests on several assumptions and faces practical constraints that limit its applicability. This section critically examines the weaknesses, edge cases, and open questions that remain, drawing primarily from Section G (Limitations and Potential Solutions) in the Appendix as well as caveats embedded throughout the experimental evaluation.

### 6.1 Architectural Assumptions and Their Consequences

**Assumption 1: Front Camera Provides Unobstructed End-Effector Visibility**

The visual reasoning trace mechanism fundamentally depends on the front camera maintaining a clear view of the robot's end-effector throughout task execution. Section G explicitly acknowledges this constraint:

> "If the end-effector is occluded in the front camera's view, visual trace prediction—and thus overall performance—can degrade."

This assumption creates several failure modes:

- **Self-occlusion during manipulation**: When the robot arm moves to grasp objects behind obstacles or reaches into confined spaces (e.g., inside a cabinet or dishwasher), the end-effector may disappear from the front camera's field of view. The model cannot generate accurate visual traces without visual feedback.
- **Multi-robot interference**: In collaborative settings with multiple robots operating in the same workspace, one robot's arm may occlude another's end-effector from the front camera perspective.
- **Object occlusion**: Large objects being manipulated can block the end-effector from view, breaking the visual reasoning chain.

The paper proposes a potential solution—using wide field-of-view cameras (e.g., fisheye lens) combined with SLAM for temporal reasoning—but this remains unimplemented. This limitation is particularly problematic for household deployment, where occlusions are common rather than exceptional.

**Assumption 2: 2D Visual Traces Adequately Represent 3D Motion**

The visual reasoning trace is defined as a polyline in image coordinates with $L$ points where $1 \leq L \leq 5$ and each point $p_i = (u_i, v_i)$ with $u_i, v_i \in \{0, \ldots, 255\}$ (Equation 3, Section 2.3). This 2D representation introduces a critical limitation acknowledged in Section G:

> "Because the cue is purely 2D, the model lacks an explicit notion of depth: it often follows the intended path within the image plane (in-plane motion) but exhibits unintended or imprecise translation along the camera's depth axis (out-of-plane)."

This creates a paradox: MolmoAct's core innovation is "reasoning in space" through depth perception tokens, yet the steerability feature bypasses this depth reasoning entirely. When a user draws a visual trace to steer the robot:

- The model does **not** condition on its predicted depth perception tokens during steering (Section G)
- The trace specifies only image-plane coordinates, leaving depth ambiguous
- For tasks requiring precise 3D positioning (e.g., inserting a peg into a hole, placing an object on a shelf at a specific depth), 2D traces are insufficient

The authors hypothesize this could be mitigated by "conditioning on—or reusing—the model's predicted depth-perception tokens to lift the trace into 3D," but this is explicitly left for future work. This means the steerability feature, while innovative, does not fully leverage MolmoAct's spatial reasoning architecture.

**Assumption 3: Discrete Tokenization Preserves Sufficient Spatial Granularity**

The depth perception system uses a VQVAE codebook with $N = 128$ codes to represent depth maps, compressed into $M = 100$ tokens per image (Section 2.3). Section G acknowledges this may be insufficient:

> "For depth perception token prediction, we follow (Bigverdi et al., 2025) and use a fixed set of 100 tokens to represent depth. However, fine-grained manipulation tasks require higher-resolution depth estimation. Increasing the number of depth perception tokens could enhance spatial reasoning and improve performance on such tasks."

This limitation manifests in tasks requiring millimeter-level precision:

- **Insertion tasks**: Plugging a USB cable, inserting a key into a lock, or threading a needle require depth precision beyond what 100 tokens can encode
- **Contact-rich manipulation**: Tasks involving sliding, pushing, or making contact with surfaces at specific angles depend on fine-grained depth gradients
- **Small object manipulation**: Handling objects smaller than the effective resolution of the depth tokenization (approximately 3.2mm per token at 320×320px resolution) may fail

The trade-off is explicit: more depth tokens would increase sequence length and inference latency, while fewer tokens reduce spatial precision. The current choice of 100 tokens represents a practical compromise rather than a theoretical optimum.

### 6.2 Computational and Scalability Constraints

**Inference Latency vs. Control Frequency Mismatch**

Section G identifies a critical deployment constraint:

> "Similar to many existing VLAs, our model exhibits a mismatch between its control inference frequency and the control frequency used during data collection. This gap may stem from server-to-robot communication latency and the additional time required to predict a larger number of reasoning tokens."

The three-stage reasoning pipeline (depth → trace → action) requires autoregressive generation of approximately 100 depth tokens + 5 trace points + 7-14 action tokens per timestep. This introduces latency compared to direct perception-to-action models:

| Component | Approximate Tokens | Impact on Latency |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Depth perception | 100 tokens | +100 autoregressive steps |
| Visual reasoning trace | 5-10 tokens | +5-10 autoregressive steps |
| Action tokens | 7-14 tokens | +7-14 autoregressive steps |
| **Total per timestep** | **~112-124 tokens** | **Significant latency** |

For real-time control at 10-20 Hz (typical for robotic manipulation), this latency may require:

- **Action chunking**: The paper uses $N = 8$ timestep chunks during post-training (Section 4.3), which reduces replanning frequency but introduces open-loop execution risk
- **Edge deployment challenges**: Running a 7B parameter model with this token overhead on edge devices (e.g., Jetson, onboard robot computers) is not addressed. The training used 256 H100 GPUs (Section 4.1), but inference hardware requirements are unspecified.

The paper suggests "techniques to reduce inference time, as seen in VLM optimization, or develop smaller parameter models optimized for efficient execution on edge or local devices" (Section G), but provides no concrete solutions or benchmarks.

**Training Data Requirements Despite Efficiency Claims**

While MolmoAct achieves competitive performance with 26.3M pre-training samples (an order of magnitude less than $\pi_0$'s 903M+), the mid-training stage still requires substantial data collection:

- **MolmoAct Dataset**: 10,689 high-quality trajectories collected over two months with five full-time operators (Section 3.2)
- **Post-training per task**: 30-50 tele-operated demonstrations per new task (Section 4.3)
- **Steerability training**: 100 demonstrations with alternative path exploration (Appendix D.7)

For research labs without dedicated robot operators or data collection infrastructure, the 10K trajectory mid-training requirement remains a significant barrier. The paper claims this is "data efficient" relative to million-scale datasets, but absolute data requirements are still substantial for small teams.

Additionally, the data curation pipeline itself requires computational overhead:

- **VQVAE training**: 20 epochs on 10 million depth maps for depth token generation (Section 3.1)
- **Molmo-based trace generation**: Running the base VLM to annotate gripper positions for every timestep in every episode (Section 3.1)
- **Multi-view processing**: Converting three-camera setups (two side + one wrist) into paired-view training examples (Section 4.2)

These preprocessing steps add hidden costs not reflected in the GPU-hour training metrics.

### 6.3 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

**Extremely Long-Horizon Tasks**

While MolmoAct achieves 77.2% on LIBERO-Long (exceeding ThinkAct's 70.9% by 6.3 points per Table 2), the LIBERO-Long benchmark consists of tasks with approximately 4-6 subtasks. Real-world household or industrial tasks often involve significantly longer horizons:

- **Multi-room navigation**: "Clean the entire house" requires coordinating actions across different rooms with different camera setups
- **Multi-hour tasks**: Cooking a multi-course meal, assembling furniture, or organizing a garage involve hundreds of timesteps
- **Stateful memory**: Tasks requiring the robot to remember object locations from earlier in the episode (e.g., "put back the items you moved earlier") are not explicitly tested

The visual reasoning trace is limited to $L \leq 5$ points representing the "future horizon of the episode between the current frame and the terminal frame" (Section 2.3). For tasks spanning hundreds of timesteps, this short-horizon trace may not capture long-term planning requirements. The paper does not evaluate performance on tasks exceeding the LIBERO-Long horizon.

**Novel Embodiments Beyond Franka Arms**

The evaluation focuses on two robot embodiments:

- **Google Robot (WidowX)**: For SimplerEnv simulation benchmarks (Section 5.1)
- **Franka Emika Panda**: For LIBERO simulation and real-world evaluations (Sections 5.2, 5.3)

Both are 7-DOF single-arm manipulators (or dual Franka for bimanual tasks). The paper does not test:

- **Mobile manipulators**: Robots with base mobility (e.g., Stretch, Hello Robot) requiring coordinated navigation and manipulation
- **Humanoid robots**: Bipedal platforms with different kinematic constraints and balance requirements
- **Specialized end-effectors**: Grippers beyond parallel-jaw (e.g., suction cups, multi-finger hands, tools)
- **Non-standard camera configurations**: Robots without wrist cameras or with only monocular front views

Section 6.1 notes that generalist policies should "generalize to out-of-distribution scenes, environments, language instructions, and novel objects," but embodiment generalization is not systematically evaluated. The claim that MolmoAct serves as "an open blueprint for building ARMs" (Abstract) assumes the architecture transfers to different morphologies without modification—an untested assumption.

**Contact-Rich and Force-Sensitive Manipulation**

All evaluated tasks are primarily vision-based with minimal force feedback requirements:

- **Pick-and-place**: Grasping and moving objects
- **Wiping**: Surface contact but no force control
- **Pouring**: Visual alignment rather than force-sensitive pouring

Tasks requiring explicit force control are not tested:

- **Insertion with compliance**: Peg-in-hole tasks requiring force feedback for alignment
- **Deformable object manipulation**: Folding clothes, stuffing objects into containers, handling liquids
- **Tool use**: Using screwdrivers, hammers, or other tools requiring force application
- **Collaborative manipulation**: Handing objects to humans or coordinating with human partners

The action space is limited to end-effector pose (3 translations + 3 rotations + 1 gripper state) without force/torque commands (Appendix D.2, D.3). This limits applicability to tasks where visual feedback alone is insufficient for successful execution.

**Adverse Environmental Conditions**

The evaluation environments are controlled:

- **Lighting**: Consistent indoor lighting without extreme shadows, glare, or low-light conditions
- **Backgrounds**: Relatively clutter-free tabletop and household scenes
- **Camera calibration**: Fixed, calibrated camera setups with known intrinsics/extrinsics (Appendix E.1)

Real-world deployment faces conditions not tested:

- **Dynamic lighting**: Sunlight through windows, lights turning on/off, shadows from moving objects
- **Camera motion**: Handheld cameras, moving robot bases causing camera shake
- **Visual noise**: Reflections on shiny surfaces, transparent objects (glass, water), specular highlights
- **Occlusions from humans**: People walking through the scene during task execution

The SimplerEnv variant aggregation tests some distribution shifts (lighting, textures, viewpoints per Section 5.3), but these are simulated perturbations rather than real-world environmental variability.

### 6.4 Open Questions and Research Gaps

**Compute-Matched Baseline Comparisons**

The paper claims data efficiency: MolmoAct achieves 70.5% zero-shot on SimplerEnv with 26.3M samples, while $\pi_0$ uses 903M+ samples (Section 5.1). However, Section 5.8 notes:

> "The paper does not report whether training a baseline model on 26.3M samples would close the performance gap. The data efficiency claim assumes the gap is due to structure, not just more training on MolmoAct's part."

This is a critical confound:

- **Structure vs. scale**: Is MolmoAct's advantage due to the reasoning architecture, or would a standard VLA trained on 26.3M samples underperform simply due to less data?
- **Fair comparison**: To isolate the architecture effect, baselines should be trained on matched data budgets
- **Diminishing returns**: It's possible $\pi_0$ would achieve similar performance with 26.3M samples if trained with similar optimization

Without compute-matched ablations, the data efficiency claim remains partially speculative.

**Failure Mode Analysis and Error Attribution**

While the three-stage pipeline enables explainability (depth → trace → action), the paper does not systematically analyze which stage causes failures:

- **Perception failures**: How often do incorrect depth tokens lead to downstream errors?
- **Planning failures**: How often does the visual trace plan an infeasible trajectory?
- **Execution failures**: How often do actions deviate from the planned trace?

Section 5.8 notes that explainability "enables failure mode diagnosis," but no quantitative analysis of failure attribution is provided. Without this, the explainability benefit remains theoretical rather than empirically validated.

**Human-Robot Collaboration Dynamics**

The steerability evaluation uses 10 participants providing open-ended instructions or visual traces (Section 5.6, Appendix D.7). However, several collaboration questions remain unaddressed:

- **Learning curves**: Do users become more effective at providing visual traces with practice?
- **Trust calibration**: Does seeing the visual reasoning trace increase user trust in the robot?
- **Intervention frequency**: How often do users need to intervene with steering corrections during task execution?
- **Cognitive load**: Does drawing visual traces impose significant cognitive burden on users?

The evaluation measures success rates but not the quality of the human-robot interaction experience. For deployment in collaborative settings, these factors matter as much as task success.

**Safety and Constraint Satisfaction**

The paper does not address safety-critical considerations:

- **Collision avoidance**: Does the visual reasoning trace account for obstacles in the planned path?
- **Force limits**: Are there constraints preventing the robot from applying excessive force?
- **Human safety**: What happens if a human enters the workspace during execution?
- **Recovery from failures**: Can the robot detect and recover from execution errors autonomously?

The steerability feature allows users to override the robot's planned trajectory (Section 2.4), but there is no discussion of safety constraints on user-provided traces. A user could theoretically draw a trajectory through a fragile object or toward a human, and the model would attempt to follow it.

**Long-Term Deployment and Continual Learning**

The evaluation focuses on one-time task adaptation via LoRA fine-tuning (Section 4.3). Real-world deployment requires:

- **Continual learning**: Can the model learn from new experiences without catastrophic forgetting?
- **Dataset growth**: How does performance scale as the MolmoAct Dataset grows beyond 10K trajectories?
- **Model updates**: What is the process for updating deployed models with new capabilities?
- **Version control**: How are different model versions managed across robot fleets?

None of these deployment-scale questions are addressed. The paper establishes MolmoAct as a research prototype rather than a production-ready system.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes the key trade-offs inherent in MolmoAct's design:

| Design Choice | Benefit | Cost/Limitation |
|--------------|---------|-----------------|
| Three-stage reasoning (depth → trace → action) | Explainability, spatial grounding | Increased inference latency (~112-124 tokens per timestep) |
| 100 depth perception tokens | Manageable sequence length | Insufficient for fine-grained manipulation (Section G) |
| 2D visual reasoning traces | Simple steering interface | Lacks depth information for 3D precision (Section G) |
| LoRA post-training (97M params) | Efficient adaptation, preserves pre-trained knowledge | Limited capacity for task-specific learning |
| Action chunking (N=8) | Reduces replanning frequency | Open-loop execution risk within chunks |
| Front-camera dependency | Consistent viewpoint for trace generation | Fails under end-effector occlusion (Section G) |
| 26.3M pre-training samples | Data efficient vs. 903M+ baselines | Still requires substantial collection infrastructure (10K mid-training trajectories) |
| VLM-based trace annotation | Accurate, non-degenerate traces | Computational overhead for data preprocessing |

These trade-offs reflect deliberate engineering decisions rather than oversights. The authors prioritize explainability, steerability, and data efficiency over raw inference speed and 3D precision. For research settings and collaborative applications where debugging and human intervention matter, these trade-offs are acceptable. For high-speed industrial deployment or safety-critical applications, the limitations become more significant.

### 6.6 Critical Assessment

MolmoAct's limitations are honestly acknowledged in Section G, which is commendable. However, several limitations have practical implications that warrant emphasis:

1. **The steerability feature does not fully leverage the reasoning architecture**: Visual trace steering bypasses depth perception tokens, creating an inconsistency between the model's reasoning capabilities and its steering interface. This is the most significant architectural gap.

2. **Occlusion sensitivity is a fundamental constraint**: Any vision-based reasoning system will fail when critical visual information is occluded. The proposed SLAM-based solution is non-trivial and unimplemented.

3. **Deployment readiness is limited**: Inference latency, edge deployment requirements, and safety constraints are not addressed, limiting immediate real-world applicability.

4. **Generalization claims are narrowly supported**: Embodiment generalization, long-horizon tasks beyond LIBERO-Long, and adverse environmental conditions remain untested.

These limitations do not invalidate MolmoAct's contributions but define the boundaries within which the approach is effective. The model excels at explainable, steerable manipulation in controlled environments with clear camera views—precisely the settings where research validation is most feasible. Extending to broader deployment scenarios requires addressing the open questions identified above.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

MolmoAct represents more than an incremental improvement in robotic foundation models—it establishes a new architectural paradigm for how robots should reason about space before acting. This section examines how the work reshapes the field's trajectory, what research directions it opens, where it can be practically deployed, and when researchers should adopt this approach over alternatives.

### 7.1 Shifting the Field's Paradigm: From Scaling to Structure

**The End of Brute-Force Scaling in Robotics?**

MolmoAct challenges a dominant assumption in robotic foundation models: that performance scales monotonically with data volume. The paper demonstrates that a model trained on 26.3M samples with structured reasoning can outperform models trained on 903M+ samples without it (Section 5.2). This has profound implications for the field:

| Previous Paradigm | MolmoAct's Paradigm |
|------------------|---------------------|
| Collect more robot data | Structure the learning problem better |
| End-to-end black boxes | Explainable intermediate representations |
| Language-only interfaces | Multi-modal steering (language + visual traces) |
| Proprietary closed models | Fully open weights, code, and data |

The abstract positions MolmoAct as "an open blueprint for building ARMs" rather than a closed proprietary system. This openness is strategically significant: by releasing all model weights, training code, and the 10,689-trajectory MolmoAct Dataset, the authors enable reproducibility and community-driven improvements that closed models like $\pi_0$ and GR00T N1.5 cannot match.

**Redefining What "Reasoning" Means for Robots**

Prior work on robotic reasoning treated it as a linguistic process—generating text descriptions like "first grasp the cup, then move to the sink" before acting (ECoT, CoT-VLA, ThinkAct). MolmoAct fundamentally redefines reasoning as a **spatial process** implemented in a language model:

> "MolmoAct does not incorporate intermediate reasoning through language; rather, we teach our models to reason in space" (Section 2.3)

This shift has cascading effects:

1. **Evaluation metrics must change**: Success rates alone are insufficient. The field needs metrics for reasoning quality (depth accuracy, trace fidelity) alongside task completion.

2. **Debugging becomes tractable**: Engineers can now inspect which reasoning stage failed (perception vs. planning vs. execution) rather than treating failures as opaque.

3. **Human-robot interaction evolves**: Users can correct robot behavior by drawing trajectories rather than reformulating language commands—a more intuitive interface for spatial tasks.

**Implications for Dataset Collection**

The MolmoAct Dataset (10,689 trajectories) demonstrates that **high-quality structured data** can outperform **large-scale unstructured data**. Section 5.4 shows mid-training on this dataset yields a consistent 5.5% improvement. This suggests:

- Small labs without million-scale data collection infrastructure can compete by focusing on data quality and structure
- The bottleneck shifts from "collect more episodes" to "annotate episodes with richer supervision" (depth tokens, visual traces)
- Synthetic data generation (e.g., using Molmo to annotate gripper positions per Section 3.1) becomes a viable scaling strategy

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions in Section G (Limitations) and implicitly suggests others through its design choices. Below are concrete research directions that build on MolmoAct's foundation.

**Immediate Extensions (1-2 Year Horizon)**

| Direction | Motivation | Specific Approach |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| **3D Visual Trace Steering** | Current 2D traces lack depth information, causing imprecise out-of-plane motion (Section G) | Condition steering on predicted depth perception tokens to lift traces into 3D space |
| **Occlusion-Robust Reasoning** | Front camera occlusion degrades visual trace prediction (Section G) | Integrate SLAM-based temporal reasoning or multi-view fusion to maintain tracking during occlusion |
| **Higher-Resolution Depth Tokens** | 100 tokens insufficient for fine-grained manipulation (Section G) | Increase VQVAE codebook size (N > 128) and token count (M > 100), measuring latency/accuracy trade-offs |
| **Failure Attribution Analysis** | Explainability enables diagnosis but no quantitative analysis provided (Section 5.8) | Systematically categorize failures by stage (perception/planning/execution) across benchmark tasks |

**Medium-Term Extensions (2-3 Year Horizon)**

| Direction | Motivation | Specific Approach |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| **Embodiment Generalization** | Evaluation limited to Franka and Google Robot arms (Section 5.1-5.3) | Test on mobile manipulators (Stretch), humanoids (Figure, Tesla Optimus), and non-standard grippers |
| **Continual Learning** | Post-training uses LoRA but no lifelong learning evaluation (Section 4.3) | Develop methods to accumulate task knowledge across deployments without catastrophic forgetting |
| **Force-Sensitive Manipulation** | Action space lacks force/torque commands (Appendix D.2-D.3) | Extend action tokenization to include force feedback for contact-rich tasks (insertion, deformation) |
| **Edge Deployment Optimization** | 7B model with ~112-124 tokens per timestep introduces latency (Section G) | Distill to smaller models (1-3B parameters) with quantization for onboard robot computers |

**Long-Term Vision (3-5 Year Horizon)**

| Direction | Motivation | Specific Approach |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| **Multi-Robot Coordination** | Current evaluation is single-robot only | Extend visual reasoning traces to multi-agent settings with collision avoidance constraints |
| **Autonomous Data Curation** | MolmoAct Dataset required 5 operators over 2 months (Section 3.2) | Develop self-supervised methods to generate depth/trace labels from unannotated robot videos |
| **Safety-Constrained Steering** | User-drawn traces have no safety constraints (Section 6.4) | Integrate constraint checking (collision, force limits) into the steering interface |
| **Cross-Modal Reasoning** | Only vision + language + action currently | Add audio (e.g., sound of objects colliding) and haptic feedback to the reasoning pipeline |

**High-Risk, High-Reward Directions**

Several speculative directions could fundamentally extend MolmoAct's capabilities:

1. **World Model Integration**: Combine MolmoAct's reasoning pipeline with a learned world model that predicts future states, enabling lookahead planning beyond the 5-point visual trace horizon.

2. **Neuro-Symbolic Hybrid**: Integrate symbolic task planners (e.g., PDDL-based) with MolmoAct's neural reasoning, combining the precision of symbolic logic with the flexibility of neural perception.

3. **Human Preference Learning**: Use the steerability interface to collect human corrections as preference data, enabling RLHF-style alignment for robotic behavior.

4. **Sim-to-Real Transfer**: Leverage the explainable reasoning chain to identify sim-to-real gaps (e.g., depth token distributions differ) and correct them systematically.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Deployment Scenarios

MolmoAct's strengths (explainability, steerability, data efficiency) make it suitable for specific deployment contexts while less ideal for others.

**Recommended Deployment Scenarios**

| Application | Why MolmoAct Fits | Key Benefit |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Research Labs** | Full openness enables reproducibility and modification | Community can build on the architecture without proprietary restrictions |
| **Collaborative Robotics** | Visual trace steering enables intuitive human correction | Operators can guide robots through ambiguous tasks without reprogramming |
| **Household Assistance** | Strong OOD generalization (23.3% over $\pi_0$-FAST per Section 5.3) | Handles novel objects and rephrased instructions common in homes |
| **Education/Training** | Explainable reasoning chains teach robotics concepts | Students can inspect depth/trace outputs to understand robot decision-making |
| **Small-Scale Manufacturing** | Data efficiency (26.3M vs 903M+ samples) | Labs without massive data infrastructure can train competitive models |
| **Debugging/Development** | Three-stage outputs enable failure diagnosis | Engineers can identify whether failures occur at perception, planning, or execution |

**Scenarios Where Alternatives May Be Preferable**

| Application | Why MolmoAct Is Suboptimal | Better Alternative |
|-------------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| **High-Speed Industrial** | Inference latency from ~112-124 tokens per timestep (Section G) | Direct perception-to-action models (e.g., RT-1, diffusion policies) |
| **Safety-Critical Systems** | No built-in collision avoidance or force constraints (Section 6.4) | Controllers with explicit safety guarantees (e.g., MPC with constraints) |
| **Occlusion-Heavy Environments** | Front camera dependency fails under end-effector occlusion (Section G) | Multi-view fusion or proprioception-heavy policies |
| **Fine-Grained Assembly** | 100 depth tokens insufficient for millimeter precision (Section G) | Specialized policies with higher-resolution depth sensing |
| **Resource-Constrained Edge** | 7B parameters require significant compute | Smaller distilled models (e.g., TinyVLA, OpenVLA-1B) |
| **Contact-Rich Manipulation** | Action space lacks force/torque commands (Appendix D.2-D.3) | Hybrid vision-force policies with impedance control |

**Integration Guidance for Practitioners**

Researchers considering adopting MolmoAct should evaluate based on these criteria:

```
Choose MolmoAct when:
✓ Explainability matters (debugging, safety certification, user trust)
✓ Human-robot collaboration is required (steering, correction)
✓ Data collection is limited (<100K trajectories available)
✓ Task horizons are moderate (LIBERO-Long scale, ~4-6 subtasks)
✓ Camera views are reliable (minimal occlusion expected)

Choose alternatives when:
✗ Inference speed is critical (>20 Hz control required)
✗ Safety constraints are non-negotiable (human proximity, high forces)
✗ Occlusions are frequent (inside cabinets, behind obstacles)
✗ Fine manipulation is required (<5mm precision)
✗ Edge deployment on limited hardware is required
```

**Getting Started with MolmoAct**

The paper's full openness lowers the barrier to adoption:

1. **Model Weights**: MolmoAct-7B-D and MolmoAct-7B-O are released (Abstract)
2. **Training Code**: Full pre-training, mid-training, and post-training pipelines available
3. **Datasets**: MolmoAct Dataset (10K trajectories) + action reasoning conversion tools
4. **Evaluation Benchmarks**: SimplerEnv, LIBERO, and real-world evaluation code

Practitioners can start with pre-trained models and fine-tune via LoRA (97M trainable parameters) using 30-50 demonstrations per task (Section 4.3). The post-training requires 96-160 GPU hours per task on 32 H100s (Tables 10-11)—accessible to well-funded labs but potentially prohibitive for smaller teams.

### 7.4 Broader Impact on the Robotics Community

**Democratizing Robotic Foundation Models**

By releasing all components openly, MolmoAct challenges the trend toward proprietary robotic models ($\pi_0$, GR00T N1.5). This has several effects:

- **Reproducibility**: Researchers can verify claims, run ablations, and build on the work without black-box dependencies
- **Benchmarking**: The MolmoAct Dataset provides a standardized mid-training benchmark for comparing future models
- **Education**: Students can study a complete pipeline from data curation to deployment, not just inference APIs

**Raising the Bar for Explainability**

Prior VLA models offered "little insight into why a robot chose one action over another" (Section 1). MolmoAct sets a new expectation: robotic foundation models should provide interpretable reasoning chains. Future models will likely be evaluated not just on success rates but on:

- Depth estimation accuracy
- Trajectory planning fidelity
- Failure mode diagnosability

This shift could accelerate safety certification for robotic deployment in regulated environments (healthcare, manufacturing, homes).

**Redefining Human-Robot Interaction**

The visual trace steering interface (75% success vs 42% for language per Section 5.6) demonstrates that **spatial interfaces outperform linguistic interfaces** for manipulation tasks. This suggests:

- Future robot interfaces should prioritize visual/spatial input modalities
- Language remains valuable for high-level intent, but spatial cues handle precision
- Mixed-initiative control (robot proposes trace, human edits) becomes feasible

**Catalyzing Structured Reasoning Research**

MolmoAct's success with structured intermediate representations may inspire similar approaches in related domains:

- **Navigation**: Generate waypoint traces before predicting motor commands
- **Multi-robot coordination**: Generate interaction graphs before assigning tasks
- **Tool use**: Generate tool trajectory sketches before executing grasps

The core insight—that structure matters more than scale for embodied reasoning—could reshape how the field approaches data collection, model architecture, and evaluation.

### 7.5 Summary: A Blueprint for the Next Generation

MolmoAct establishes Action Reasoning Models as a viable alternative to direct perception-to-action VLAs. Its contributions extend beyond performance metrics:

| Contribution | Immediate Impact | Long-Term Influence |
|--------------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Open Release** | Enables reproducibility today | Sets norm for openness in robotics |
| **Structured Reasoning** | Improves data efficiency | Shifts field from scaling to structure |
| **Visual Steerability** | Enables intuitive correction | Redefines human-robot interaction |
| **Explainable Outputs** | Enables debugging | Facilitates safety certification |

The work does not solve all challenges in robotic foundation models—occlusion sensitivity, 3D steering precision, and inference latency remain open problems (Section G). However, it provides a **proven architecture** that future work can extend rather than replace.

For researchers entering the field, MolmoAct offers a concrete starting point: the released code and data enable rapid experimentation with structured reasoning approaches. For practitioners deploying robots, it offers a model that balances performance with explainability and human control. For the broader community, it demonstrates that **reasoning in space**—not just scaling data—is the path toward robots that act with purpose, adaptability, and transparency.

The paper concludes by positioning MolmoAct as "an open blueprint for building ARMs that transform perception into purposeful action through grounded reasoning" (Abstract). This framing is apt: the value lies not just in the model itself, but in the architectural principles it establishes for the next generation of robotic foundation models.
