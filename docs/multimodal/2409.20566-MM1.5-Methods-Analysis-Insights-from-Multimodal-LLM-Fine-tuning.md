## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces **MM1.5**, a family of multimodal large language models (ranging from **1B to 30B** parameters, including dense and **Mixture-of-Experts** variants) that achieves state-of-the-art performance in text-rich image understanding, visual referring/grounding, and multi-image reasoning through a rigorously optimized data-centric training recipe. By systematically ablating three distinct stages—large-scale pre-training with a refined **50:10:40** ratio of image-caption, interleaved, and text-only data; high-resolution (**up to 4 Megapixels**) continual pre-training on **45M OCR samples**; and a balanced supervised fine-tuning mixture—the authors demonstrate that careful data curation allows small-scale models (e.g., **MM1.5-3B**) to outperform larger competitors like **Phi-3-Vision (4B)** and **MiniCPM-V2 (3B)** on benchmarks such as **DocVQA** (87.7% vs. 71.9%) and **RefCOCO** (85.6% vs. unavailable). This work matters because it proves that efficient, mobile-deployable models can match or exceed the capabilities of much larger systems in complex tasks like grounding and multi-image reasoning without requiring architectural breakthroughs, solely by optimizing data composition and resolution strategies.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: The "Data-Centric" Gap in Multimodal LLMs
While the architecture of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) has largely converged on a standard design—a vision encoder connected to a Large Language Model (LLM) via a projector—the path to high performance remains obscured by empirical uncertainty. The central problem this paper addresses is not *how* to build an MLLM architecturally, but *how to train it effectively* through data curation.

Despite the proliferation of open-source models like the LLaVA series, InternVL2, and Cambrian-1, the field lacks a systematic understanding of how specific data categories influence distinct capabilities. Most existing works treat the training data mixture as a monolithic block or rely on heuristics, failing to answer critical questions:
*   How does adding "text-rich" data (e.g., documents, charts) impact a model's ability to perform "visual grounding" (identifying coordinates)?
*   Does increasing the ratio of multi-image data degrade single-image performance?
*   What is the optimal balance between pre-training on image-text pairs versus pure text-only data for knowledge-intensive tasks?

The paper identifies a significant gap: while prior research (such as the predecessor MM1) explored pre-training strategies, there is limited exploration into the **inter-play between data categories during Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)** and the specific role of a **high-resolution continual pre-training stage**. Without this granular understanding, developers often resort to brute-force scaling or ad-hoc data mixing, leading to models that are either too large for edge deployment or deficient in specific skills like OCR or referring.

### Why This Matters: From Cloud Giants to Edge Agents
The motivation for solving this problem is twofold, spanning both theoretical efficiency and real-world applicability.

**1. Theoretical Significance: Decoupling Scale from Capability**
The prevailing assumption in deep learning is that performance scales monotonically with parameter count. MM1.5 challenges this by demonstrating that **data quality and composition can compensate for parameter scarcity**. By proving that a 3B parameter model can outperform 7B or even larger models on specific benchmarks (e.g., text-rich understanding), the paper provides a blueprint for efficient model development. It shifts the research focus from "building bigger models" to "engineering smarter data recipes," a crucial pivot as the cost of training massive models becomes prohibitive.

**2. Real-World Impact: Enabling On-Device Intelligence**
The push for **mobile and edge deployment** is a primary driver. Large proprietary models like GPT-4o or Gemini-1.5 require cloud infrastructure, introducing latency, privacy concerns, and connectivity dependencies.
*   **Mobile UI Automation:** To act as an autonomous agent on a smartphone (e.g., "Click the login button"), a model must understand screen layouts, read tiny text, and output precise coordinates. This requires **visual referring and grounding**, a capability often missing in small open-source models.
*   **Document Analysis:** Professionals need to extract data from high-resolution invoices, scientific papers, or infographics on the go. This demands **text-rich image understanding** at resolutions far exceeding the standard $378 \times 378$ pixels used in early MLLMs.
*   **Multi-Image Reasoning:** Real-world queries often involve context spread across multiple images (e.g., comparing two product photos or analyzing a sequence of events).

MM1.5 targets the **1B to 3B parameter regime**, explicitly aiming for models that can run locally on mobile devices while retaining the sophisticated reasoning capabilities previously reserved for cloud-based giants.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
To understand MM1.5's contribution, one must recognize where previous methods fall short:

*   **Architectural Saturation vs. Data Neglect:** Many recent open-source efforts focus on novel architectures (e.g., different vision encoders or connectors) but rely on generic or poorly balanced SFT data mixtures. For instance, models like **LLaVA-OneVision** and **Phi-3-Vision** show strong general performance but often lack native **visual referring and grounding** capabilities. They cannot natively output bounding boxes (e.g., `<x1, y1, x2, y2>`) to point to objects; instead, they rely on external prompting tricks like **Set-of-Mark (SoM)**, which requires pre-processing the image with marks—a cumbersome and non-end-to-end solution.
*   **The Resolution Bottleneck:** Early MLLMs typically resized all inputs to a fixed low resolution (e.g., $336 \times 336$ or $378 \times 378$). While some recent works introduced "any-resolution" approaches (splitting images into tiles), the impact of **resolution specifically during the continual pre-training phase** was under-explored. Without high-resolution exposure during pre-training, models struggle to recognize fine-grained text even if the architecture supports it later.
*   **Single-Image Bias:** The majority of existing MLLMs are trained predominantly on single-image datasets. Consequently, they fail at **multi-image reasoning** and **in-context learning** (learning from examples provided in the prompt) unless explicitly fine-tuned on such data, often at the expense of single-image performance.
*   **Lack of Specialized Variants:** General-purpose models often underperform on niche but critical domains like **mobile UI understanding** or **video analysis** because they are not exposed to domain-specific data distributions during training.

### Positioning of MM1.5
MM1.5 positions itself not as an architectural revolution, but as a **methodological refinement** built upon the solid foundation of its predecessor, **MM1**.

*   **Architecture:** It retains the exact architecture of MM1 (CLIP vision encoder, C-Abstractor connector, and decoder-only LLM). This deliberate choice isolates the variable of interest: **the training recipe**. By holding the architecture constant, the authors ensure that any performance gains are attributable solely to their data-centric strategies.
*   **The "Three-Stage" Recipe:** Unlike prior works that might jump straight from pre-training to SFT, MM1.5 introduces a rigorous **three-stage pipeline**:
    1.  **Large-Scale Pre-training:** Optimizing the ratio of image-caption, interleaved, and text-only data (shifting to a **50:10:40** split to boost knowledge).
    2.  **Continual Pre-training:** A novel intermediate stage using **45M high-resolution OCR samples** to specifically harden the model against text-rich inputs before fine-tuning.
    3.  **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT):** A meticulously ablated mixture of general, text-rich, referring/grounding, and multi-image data to balance competing capabilities.
*   **Capability Expansion:** MM1.5 explicitly integrates **visual referring and grounding** (outputting coordinates) and **multi-image reasoning** as core, native features, distinguishing it from competitors that treat these as afterthoughts or require external tools.
*   **Specialization:** It extends the generalist framework into specialized domains (**MM1.5-Video** and **MM1.5-UI**) to demonstrate the versatility of its data recipe, showing that a strong generalist foundation can be efficiently adapted to vertical tasks.

In summary, MM1.5 addresses the "black box" of MLLM training data. It argues that with the right data mixture, resolution strategy, and training stages, small models can achieve "large model" capabilities, bridging the gap between theoretical potential and practical, on-device utility.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper is an empirical study on training methodologies for Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs), arguing that performance gains in small-scale models (1B–3B parameters) are driven not by architectural novelty but by a rigorously optimized, three-stage data-centric training recipe. The core idea is that by systematically ablating and refining the composition of pre-training, continual pre-training, and supervised fine-tuning data—alongside dynamic high-resolution image processing strategies—one can instill complex capabilities like text-rich understanding, visual grounding, and multi-image reasoning into efficient, mobile-deployable models.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
The system being built is **MM1.5**, a family of multimodal AI models that can "see" images (including documents, charts, and UI screens) and "read" them to answer questions, locate objects with coordinates, or reason across multiple images simultaneously. It solves the problem of small models lacking specialized skills (like reading tiny text or pointing to specific image regions) by feeding them a carefully curated diet of data in three distinct phases, rather than trying to fix these issues with a larger or more complex neural network architecture.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The MM1.5 system follows a standard encoder-projector-decoder pipeline, identical to its predecessor MM1, consisting of three primary components:
1.  **Vision Encoder:** A CLIP-based model that converts input images (potentially split into high-resolution tiles) into a sequence of visual feature vectors.
2.  **Vision-Language Connector (C-Abstractor):** A lightweight module that compresses and projects the visual features from the encoder into the embedding space of the language model, effectively translating "image tokens" into "language tokens."
3.  **Decoder-Only LLM Backbone:** A large language model (ranging from 1B to 30B parameters, with optional Mixture-of-Experts variants) that processes the combined sequence of image tokens and text instructions to autoregressively generate text responses, including grounded coordinates.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
To fully understand how MM1.5 achieves its results, we will proceed in the following logical order:
*   **Dynamic High-Resolution Image Processing:** We first explain how the model handles images of arbitrary sizes and resolutions, as this preprocessing step dictates the quality of visual information fed into the network.
*   **The Three-Stage Training Pipeline:** We then detail the chronological training process, moving from large-scale pre-training to the novel high-resolution continual pre-training stage, and finally to the balanced supervised fine-tuning mixture.
*   **Data Composition and Ablation Logic:** We dissect the specific data categories (e.g., OCR, referring, multi-image) and the empirical ratios derived from ablation studies that form the "recipe" for each stage.
*   **Model Scaling and Variants:** We conclude by describing how this recipe is applied across different model scales (1B to 30B) and specialized variants (Video, UI, and MoE).

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Dynamic High-Resolution Image Processing
A critical enabler for MM1.5's text-rich understanding is its ability to process images at resolutions up to **4 Megapixels** (e.g., $2016 \times 2016$ for square images or $6048 \times 672$ for panoramic views), far exceeding the fixed low resolutions (e.g., $378 \times 378$) typical of earlier models. Instead of using a static grid that forces every image into a rigid $2 \times 2$ split (which wastes computation on small images or distorts aspect ratios), MM1.5 employs a **dynamic image splitting** strategy, often referred to as "AnyRes."

The mechanism for selecting the optimal grid configuration operates as follows:
*   Given an input image with height $h$ and width $w$, and a vision encoder with native resolution $r$ (specifically $672 \times 672$ in MM1.5), the system considers a set of candidate grids $G = \{(n_h, n_w) \in \mathbb{N} \mid n_{\min} \le n_h \cdot n_w \le n_{\max}\}$, where $n_h$ and $n_w$ are the number of rows and columns of sub-images.
*   The system selects the optimal grid $g^* = (n_h, n_w)$ that minimizes the amount of padding required after resizing the image to fit the grid, subject to the constraint that the grid must cover the image dimensions. Mathematically, this is expressed as:
    $$g^* = \arg \min_{(n_h, n_w) \in G} |n_h n_w r^2 - h_g w_g|$$
    where $h_g$ and $w_g$ are the height and width of the image after resizing the longer side to fit the candidate grid.
*   If no grid can cover the image without downscaling (i.e., the image is larger than the maximum grid capacity), the system chooses the grid that minimizes the resolution loss due to scaling the image down while still fully covering the longer side.
*   To ensure the model retains a global understanding of the image context alongside local details, MM1.5 always includes an **overview image** (the original image resized to the encoder resolution $r$) in addition to the split sub-images.
*   The position of this overview image in the token sequence is critical due to the autoregressive nature of the LLM; MM1.5 places the overview image **after** the sub-images, allowing the decoder's attention mask to let the overview tokens attend to all preceding sub-image tokens, thereby integrating global and local features effectively.
*   To help the model reconstruct the 2D spatial structure from the flattened sequence of sub-image tokens, MM1.5 uses an **index-based position indicator**, appending a tuple $(k, i, j)$ to each sub-image, where $k$ is the image index in a multi-image sample, and $i, j$ are the row and column indices of the sub-image.
*   During training, the grid configuration is fixed to $(n_{\min}, n_{\max}) = (4, 9)$, but during inference, these parameters can be increased (e.g., to $(4, 16)$) to support even higher effective resolutions without retraining, leveraging the model's learned ability to handle variable sequence lengths.

#### The Three-Stage Training Pipeline
MM1.5 deviates from the standard two-stage (Pre-training $\to$ SFT) paradigm by introducing an intermediate **Continual Pre-training** stage specifically designed to harden the model's text-rich capabilities before fine-tuning. The entire pipeline is executed with both the vision encoder and LLM backbone unfrozen to allow end-to-end adaptation.

**Stage 1: Large-Scale Pre-training**
*   The foundation of MM1.5 is built on a massive corpus comprising three distinct data types: image-caption pairs, interleaved image-text documents, and pure text-only data.
*   Crucially, the authors refined the data mixture ratio from the original MM1's **45:45:10** (image-caption : interleaved : text-only) to **50:10:40**.
*   This shift significantly downweights interleaved data (from 45% to 10%) and quadruples the proportion of high-quality text-only data (from 10% to 40%), a decision driven by ablation studies showing that enhanced language comprehension directly improves performance on knowledge-intensive benchmarks like MMMU.
*   The text-only data is sourced from a high-quality set (HQ-Text) curated to include diverse contexts in general knowledge, mathematics, and coding, strengthening the LLM's reasoning backbone before it ever sees an image.
*   This stage is trained for **200,000 steps** with a sequence length of **4096**, following the same learning rate schedule as MM1.

**Stage 2: High-Resolution Continual Pre-training**
*   This novel intermediate stage serves as a bridge between generic pre-training and task-specific fine-tuning, specifically targeting the domain of text-rich image understanding.
*   The model is initialized with the Stage 1 checkpoint and trained exclusively on **45 million OCR-centric samples** drawn from datasets like PDFA, IDL, RenderedText, and DocStruct-4M.
*   Unlike Stage 1, which may use lower resolutions for efficiency, this stage mandates a high-resolution input setting of **$1344 \times 1344$** (achieved via dynamic splitting) to ensure the model learns to resolve fine-grained text details.
*   The training runs for a maximum of **30,000 steps** using the AdaFactor optimizer with a peak learning rate of **$1 \times 10^{-5}$** and cosine decay.
*   Ablation results indicated that synthetic image captions (e.g., from LLaVA-Recap or ShareGPT4V) did not provide consistent improvements over the simpler OCR data in this specific stage, so the final recipe relies solely on the 45M OCR dataset.
*   The high resolution is non-negotiable here; experiments showed that continual pre-training at lower resolutions (e.g., $378 \times 378$) actually degraded performance compared to skipping this stage entirely, likely because the blurred text hindered the model's ability to learn meaningful OCR representations.

**Stage 3: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)**
*   The final stage adapts the model to follow instructions and balance multiple capabilities using a meticulously curated mixture of single-image, multi-image, and text-only data.
*   The final data mixture is composed of **80% single-image data**, **10% multi-image data**, and **10% text-only data**.
*   Within the single-image category (which constitutes the bulk of the training), the data is further subdivided by capability: **37.2% text-rich**, **22.5% referring & grounding**, **11.3% general**, **5.6% math**, **2.3% code**, and **1.1% science**.
*   These specific ratios were derived from extensive ablation studies where the authors measured the impact of upsampling or downsampling specific categories relative to a "general" baseline. For instance, the optimal ratio ($\alpha$) for science data was found to be **0.1**, for math **0.5**, for code **0.2**, and for referring/grounding **2.0** (to ensure robust grounding capabilities despite a slight trade-off in general scores).
*   The SFT stage is trained for **one epoch** (approximately **23,000 steps**) with a peak learning rate of **$2 \times 10^{-5}$**.
*   Dynamic image splitting is enabled during SFT but is restricted to samples containing fewer than three images to prevent excessive sequence lengths that could destabilize training or exceed memory limits.

#### Data Composition and Capability Integration
The success of MM1.5 relies on the precise integration of data categories that are often treated separately or ignored in other models.

*   **Visual Referring and Grounding:** Unlike models that require external prompting tricks to locate objects, MM1.5 is trained to natively output bounding box coordinates (e.g., `<x1, y1, x2, y2>`) as part of its text generation. The training data includes datasets like GRIT-Visual Genome and GRIT-RefCOCO, where the model learns to interpret input coordinates (points or boxes) and generate output coordinates, effectively turning the LLM into a spatial reasoner.
*   **Multi-Image Reasoning:** To support tasks involving multiple images (e.g., "Compare these two charts"), the SFT mixture includes **10% multi-image data** sourced from datasets like Birds-to-Words, Coinstruct, and NLVR2. This exposure allows the model to perform in-context learning and reason across disjoint visual contexts without needing a specialized architecture.
*   **Text-Rich Specialization:** The heavy weighting of text-rich data (37.2% of the single-image mix) combined with the high-resolution continual pre-training stage ensures the model excels at tasks like document VQA (DocVQA) and chart understanding (ChartQA), areas where standard MLLMs typically fail due to insufficient resolution or lack of OCR-specific training signals.

#### Model Scaling and Variants
The MM1.5 recipe is designed to be scalable and adaptable, supporting a range of model sizes and specialized architectures.

*   **Dense vs. Mixture-of-Experts (MoE):** The authors provide both dense models (where all parameters are active for every token) and MoE variants for the 1B and 3B scales. The MoE models utilize **64 experts** with a **top-2 gating** mechanism, meaning only two experts are activated per token, keeping the inference cost similar to the dense model while significantly increasing the total parameter count and capacity. A load balance loss of **0.01** and a router z-loss of **0.001** are applied to ensure stable training and even expert utilization.
*   **Scaling Behavior:** The training recipe demonstrates strong scaling properties from **1B to 30B parameters**. For example, the 30B dense model achieves a **MathVista** score of **55.6** and a **DocVQA** score of **91.4**, significantly outperforming the 3B variant, proving that the data recipe remains effective as the model backbone grows.
*   **MM1.5-Video:** This variant leverages the multi-image reasoning capabilities of the base model for video understanding. It operates by uniformly sampling **24 frames** from a video and feeding them as a multi-image input. Two versions are presented: a **training-free** version that directly applies the image-trained model to video frames, and an **SFT version** fine-tuned on video-specific datasets (ShareGPTVideo, VideoChat2, ActivityNet-QA) to improve temporal modeling. Notably, dynamic image splitting is disabled for video frames to manage token limits, with each frame represented by **144 tokens**.
*   **MM1.5-UI:** Tailored for mobile User Interface understanding, this variant is fine-tuned on the Ferret-UI dataset (801K samples), which includes tasks for widget captioning, tapability prediction, and screen grounding. The high-resolution capabilities of the base model are particularly critical here for recognizing small UI elements and text on smartphone screens.

By adhering to this rigorous, data-centric protocol—optimizing resolution, balancing data categories, and introducing a targeted continual pre-training phase—MM1.5 achieves a level of capability in small models that previously required much larger systems, effectively decoupling performance from parameter count.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The MM1.5 paper distinguishes itself not through architectural novelty, but through a series of counter-intuitive empirical findings that redefine how we approach data curation for Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs). While prior work often assumes that "more data is better" or that synthetic captions are universally superior, MM1.5 reveals that **data quality, resolution alignment, and specific mixture ratios** are the true levers for capability in small-scale models. Below are the four most significant innovations derived from their extensive ablation studies.

### 4.1 The "Resolution Mismatch" Penalty in Continual Pre-training
**The Innovation:** The discovery that high-resolution image understanding requires **high-resolution exposure during the *continual pre-training* stage**, not just during fine-tuning.
**Prior Work Assumption:** Most existing pipelines (e.g., standard LLaVA variants) perform large-scale pre-training at low resolutions (e.g., $336 \times 336$ or $378 \times 378$) for computational efficiency, assuming that switching to high-resolution inputs only during the final Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) stage is sufficient to teach the model OCR and fine-grained details.
**The MM1.5 Finding:** The authors demonstrate a critical "resolution mismatch" penalty. As shown in **Figure 9(a)**, performing continual pre-training on OCR data at low resolution ($378 \times 378$) actually **degrades** final performance compared to having no continual pre-training at all (58.28 vs. 58.8 MMBase score). The model fails to learn meaningful text representations because the input is too blurry to resolve characters, effectively teaching it noise.
**Significance:** This is a fundamental shift in training strategy. It proves that capability transfer is resolution-dependent; you cannot learn to read fine print if your "pre-school" education (pre-training) only ever shows you blurred text. By mandating a high-resolution ($1344 \times 1344$) continual pre-training stage specifically for the 45M OCR dataset, MM1.5 unlocks text-rich capabilities that remain inaccessible to models skipping this step, regardless of their SFT resolution.

### 4.2 The Primacy of Real OCR Data Over Synthetic Captions
**The Innovation:** The empirical evidence that **specialized, real-world OCR data outperforms high-quality synthetic captions** for boosting text-rich and knowledge capabilities in the continual pre-training phase.
**Prior Work Assumption:** Following trends in models like LLaVA-NeXT, there is a prevailing belief that "synthetic captions" generated by powerful teacher models (e.g., GPT-4V or large proprietary MLLMs) are the gold standard for pre-training data. The assumption is that rich, descriptive natural language provides better semantic grounding than simple text transcription.
**The MM1.5 Finding:** In **Figure 9(b)**, the authors ablate the impact of adding synthetic caption datasets (LLaVA-Recap, ShareGPT4V) to their OCR mixture. Surprisingly, adding these sophisticated captions **did not consistently improve** performance over the baseline of using *only* OCR data (60.26 vs. 59.48/59.0). In some configurations, the simpler OCR-only mixture performed best.
**Significance:** This challenges the "complexity bias" in current MLLM research. For the specific goal of enhancing text comprehension and knowledge retrieval, direct exposure to ground-truth text (OCR) appears more signal-dense and effective than indirect semantic descriptions. This insight offers a massive practical advantage: developers can achieve state-of-the-art text-rich performance using publicly available OCR datasets without needing expensive access to proprietary teacher models for caption generation.

### 4.3 The "Knowledge-Text" Trade-off: Optimizing the 50:10:40 Ratio
**The Innovation:** The strategic rebalancing of pre-training data to heavily favor **text-only data (40%)** at the expense of interleaved image-text documents (10%), reversing the standard ratios used in prior multimodal pre-training.
**Prior Work Assumption:** Multimodal models are typically trained with a heavy emphasis on image-text pairs (both caption and interleaved), under the assumption that multimodal alignment is the primary bottleneck. Text-only data is often treated as a minor auxiliary component (e.g., the original MM1 used a 45:45:10 ratio).
**The MM1.5 Finding:** Through ablation in **Section 3.4** and **Figure 10**, the authors found that increasing the text-only ratio from 10% to 40% (shifting to a **50:10:40** split of Image-Caption : Interleaved : Text-Only) significantly boosts performance on knowledge-heavy benchmarks like MMMU (+0.99 points) and general reasoning, with negligible loss in visual tasks.
**Significance:** This reveals that for small models (1B–3B), the limiting factor for complex reasoning is often **language comprehension and world knowledge**, not visual alignment. By "over-loading" the pre-training phase with high-quality text (HQ-Text), MM1.5 strengthens the LLM backbone's reasoning engine, which then transfers more effectively to multimodal tasks during fine-tuning. This suggests that the most efficient path to a smarter multimodal model might be to train it more like a powerful text-only LLM first.

### 4.4 Native Grounding as a Core Capability vs. An Afterthought
**The Innovation:** The integration of **visual referring and grounding** (inputting/outputting coordinates) as a native, first-class capability in small models, achieved through specific data mixing rather than architectural changes.
**Prior Work Assumption:** Visual grounding (pointing to objects with coordinates) is often considered a specialized skill requiring large models (13B+) or external prompting techniques like **Set-of-Mark (SoM)**, where the image is pre-processed with visual markers to help the model locate regions. Most small open-source models (e.g., Phi-3-Vision, early LLaVA) lack this ability entirely.
**The MM1.5 Finding:** By allocating a significant portion of the SFT mixture to referring and grounding data ($\alpha = 2.0$, meaning double the sampling rate relative to general data), MM1.5 instills this capability into models as small as **1B and 3B parameters**. As shown in **Table 7**, the MM1.5-3B model achieves **85.6%** on RefCOCO, vastly outperforming Phi-3-Vision (38.1%) and matching models several times its size.
**Significance:** This democratizes spatial reasoning. It proves that grounding is not an emergent property of scale but a learnable skill dependent on data density. By making this native, MM1.5 enables practical on-device applications (like UI automation where a model must click a specific button) without the latency and complexity of external prompting pipelines. It shifts grounding from a "party trick" of giant models to a standard feature of efficient edge AI.

### Summary of Impact
These insights collectively argue that the frontier of efficient MLLM development has moved from **architecture design** to **data engineering**. MM1.5 demonstrates that:
1.  **Resolution must match the task** at every training stage, not just inference.
2.  **Simplicity wins:** Real OCR data beats synthetic captions for text tasks.
3.  **Language is the bottleneck:** More text-only pre-training yields better multimodal reasoning.
4.  **Capabilities are configurable:** Specific skills like grounding can be "turned on" in tiny models via targeted data mixing.

These findings provide a reproducible blueprint for building high-performance, mobile-deployable AI that rivals cloud-based giants, fundamentally changing the cost-benefit analysis of MLLM development.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

The empirical evaluation of MM1.5 is designed to rigorously test the hypothesis that data-centric optimization can enable small-scale models (1B–3B parameters) to outperform larger competitors across diverse multimodal capabilities. The authors employ a comprehensive benchmarking strategy covering general understanding, text-rich reasoning, visual grounding, multi-image analysis, and specialized domains (video and UI).

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

**Benchmarks and Metrics**
The evaluation spans **35 distinct benchmarks**, categorized into five core capability groups. The authors utilize an internal fork of `lm-eval-harness` to ensure consistent zero-shot settings and greedy decoding across all models, unless specified otherwise.

*   **General Understanding:** Includes **MME** (perception/cognition sum), **SeedBench** (image), **POPE** (object hallucination), **LLaVA-Wild** (open-ended), **MM-Vet**, and **RealWorldQA**. Metrics range from normalized accuracy to GPT-assisted scoring.
*   **Text-Rich Understanding:** Focuses on OCR and document analysis using **WTQ** (WikiTableQuestions), **TabFact**, **OCRBench**, **ChartQA**, **TextVQA**, **DocVQA**, and **InfoVQA**. Metrics include exact match accuracy and ANLS (Average Normalized Levenshtein Similarity) for document tasks.
*   **Visual Referring & Grounding:** Evaluates the model's ability to interpret and generate coordinates using **RefCOCO/+/g**, **Flickr30k**, **LVIS-Ref**, and **Ferret-Bench**. The primary metric is Recall@1 with IoU > 0.5 or exact match accuracy for referring tasks.
*   **Knowledge (Math/Science/Code):** Tests reasoning on **AI2D** (science diagrams), **ScienceQA**, **MMMU** (college-level multi-discipline), and **MathVista**.
*   **Multi-Image & In-Context Learning (ICL):** Assesses reasoning across multiple images using **Q-Bench2**, **Mantis**, **NLVR2**, **MVBench** (video transfer), **BLINK**, and **MuirBench**. ICL is specifically measured via the **VL-ICL** benchmark.

**Baselines and Competitors**
MM1.5 is compared against a wide spectrum of State-of-the-Art (SOTA) models:
*   **Mobile/Edge Scale (1B–4B):** Phi-3-Vision (4B), MiniCPM-V2 (3B), InternVL2 (2B), LLaVA-OneVision (0.5B/7B), and TinyLLaVA.
*   **Mid-to-Large Scale (7B–34B):** LLaVA-NeXT (7B/34B), Cambrian-34B, Ferret-UI (13B), and Qwen-VL.
*   **Proprietary Giants:** GPT-4V, GPT-4o, and Gemini-1.5-Pro (scores sourced from OpenVLM Leaderboard).

**Experimental Controls**
To isolate the impact of the training recipe, the authors keep the architecture identical to MM1. For fair comparison, they note decoding differences: while MM1.5 uses greedy decoding, some competitors like MiniCPM-V2 and InternVL2 use beam search. For multiple-choice questions, MM1.5 outputs are not post-processed by external models (e.g., ChatGPT), preserving case sensitivity and punctuation to reflect raw model capability.

### 5.2 Core Quantitative Results

The results overwhelmingly support the claim that MM1.5's data recipe yields superior performance at small scales, often surpassing models with 2–4× more parameters.

#### Dominance at the 1B and 3B Scales
**Table 4** and **Table 5** provide the most direct evidence of MM1.5's efficiency.

*   **1B Parameter Regime:** The **MM1.5-1B** model establishes a new SOTA for its size class.
    *   On **ScienceQA**, it scores **82.1%**, significantly outperforming LLaVA-OneVision-0.5B (67.2%) and SPHINX-Tiny (21.5%).
    *   On **DocVQA** (document understanding), it achieves **81.0%**, compared to 70.0% for LLaVA-OneVision-0.5B.
    *   Remarkably, the **MM1.5-1B-MoE** variant pushes these numbers further, hitting **87.6%** on ScienceQA and **41.2%** on the challenging **MMMU** benchmark, rivaling much larger dense models.

*   **3B Parameter Regime:** The **MM1.5-3B** model demonstrates competitive parity with 4B+ models and superiority over other 3B variants.
    *   **Text-Rich Superiority:** On **DocVQA**, MM1.5-3B scores **87.7%**, crushing MiniCPM-V2 (71.9%) and beating Phi-3-Vision (83.3%). On **InfoVQA**, it reaches **58.5%**, far ahead of MiniCPM-V2 (37.6%) and Phi-3-Vision (49.0%).
    *   **Knowledge Reasoning:** It achieves **44.4%** on **MathVista**, outperforming MiniCPM-V2 (38.7%) and matching Phi-3-Vision (44.5%), despite Phi-3-Vision having 33% more parameters.
    *   **General Performance:** On **MME** (perception/cognition), it scores **1478.4/319.6**, which is competitive with InternVL2-2B (1864.3/70.9 sum) and Phi-3-Vision (1441.6/320.0).

#### Breakthrough in Visual Referring and Grounding
A standout contribution is the native grounding capability in small models, detailed in **Table 7**. Prior small models typically lack this ability or perform poorly.

*   **RefCOCO Performance:** MM1.5-3B achieves an average score of **85.6%** on RefCOCO. In stark contrast, **Phi-3-Vision** scores only **38.1%**, and **MiniCPM-V2** reports no results (indicating lack of capability).
*   **Comparison to Specialized Models:** MM1.5-3B (85.6%) outperforms **Ferret-7B** (82.5% avg) and approaches **Ferret-13B** (84.4% avg), despite being less than a quarter of the size of the latter.
*   **Coordinate Generation:** The model successfully generates bounding boxes (e.g., `<x1, y1, x2, y2>`) without needing Set-of-Mark (SoM) prompting, a technique required by GPT-4o for similar tasks.

#### Multi-Image Reasoning and In-Context Learning
**Table 9** and **Table 8** highlight the effectiveness of the interleaved pre-training and multi-image SFT data.

*   **Multi-Image Benchmarks:** On **NLVR2** (visual reasoning), MM1.5-3B scores **83.8%**, significantly beating Phi-3-Vision (53.6%) and InternVL2-2B (67.4%). On **MuirBench** (comprehensive multi-image), it reaches **44.3%**, more than doubling Phi-3-Vision's 38.0% and vastly exceeding InternVL2's 23.1%.
*   **In-Context Learning (ICL):** The **VL-ICL** benchmark results in **Table 8** show a massive gap. MM1.5-3B achieves an average score of **56.3%**, whereas Phi-3-Vision languishes at **19.5%**. Even against larger models like **Idefics-80B** (35.6%), the small MM1.5-30B variant scores an impressive **77.6%**, demonstrating that the pre-training recipe effectively instills few-shot learning capabilities.

#### Scaling to 30B and MoE Variants
The recipe scales effectively. **Table 5** shows that **MM1.5-30B** achieves **91.4%** on DocVQA and **67.3%** on InfoVQA, surpassing **Cambrian-34B** (75.5% and N/A respectively) and closing the gap with proprietary models like GPT-4V (88.4% on DocVQA).
*   **MoE Efficiency:** The **MM1.5-3B-MoE** model (with 64 experts) often outperforms the dense 7B variant. For instance, on **RefCOCO**, the 3B-MoE hits **86.2%** vs. the dense 7B's **87.2%**, while maintaining the inference cost of a 3B model. This validates the use of sparsity to enhance capacity without proportional compute increases.

### 5.3 Specialized Variants: Video and UI

The paper extends its analysis to domain-specific fine-tuning, proving the generalist foundation is adaptable.

**MM1.5-Video (Section 5.2, Tables 10 & 11)**
*   **Training-Free Capability:** Remarkably, the base image model (MM1.5-Video-3B training-free) achieves **72.8%** on **NExTQA** and **72.7%** on **IntentQA**, outperforming many dedicated 7B video models like SlowFast-LLaVA (64.2% and 60.1%). This confirms strong zero-shot transfer from multi-image reasoning to video frames.
*   **SFT Improvements:** After fine-tuning on video data, the 7B variant reaches **60.9%** on **ActivityNet-QA** (open-ended) and **86.6%** on **IntentQA**, establishing new SOTA results among open-source models using only public datasets. It trails slightly behind LLaVA-OneVision-7B on long-form benchmarks (e.g., VideoMME), which the authors attribute to LLaVA's use of re-annotated data and more input frames (32 vs. 24).

**MM1.5-UI (Section 6.2, Table 12)**
*   **Small Model Dominance:** The **MM1.5-UI-1B** model outperforms the **Ferret-UI-13B** model on nearly all elementary tasks. For iOS grounding (**Grd-i**), MM1.5-UI-1B scores **86.5%** vs. Ferret-UI-13B's **79.4%**.
*   **Task Hierarchy:** The results confirm that text-based UI tasks are hardest, followed by icons, then widgets. MM1.5-UI significantly narrows the gap between icon and widget tasks compared to prior work, validating the benefit of high-resolution dynamic splitting for small UI elements.
*   **Transfer Efficiency:** An ablation shows that starting from the full MM1.5 SFT checkpoint (vs. just pre-training) yields better UI performance after only 1 epoch of fine-tuning, proving the generalist data mixture provides a superior foundation for specialized domains.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Ablations and Trade-offs

The paper's claims are strongly supported by extensive ablation studies, though some trade-offs are explicitly acknowledged.

**Convincing Evidence:**
*   **Resolution is Critical:** **Figure 9(a)** provides definitive proof that low-resolution ($378 \times 378$) continual pre-training harms performance (58.28 score) compared to no continual pre-training (58.8). This validates the necessity of the high-resolution stage.
*   **Data Mixture Balance:** **Figure 6** and **Figure 8** clearly demonstrate the trade-offs. Increasing referring/grounding data ($\alpha=2.0$) boosts grounding scores dramatically but causes a slight dip in general/text-rich scores. The final "All Mixture" is shown to be the optimal Pareto frontier, balancing these competing objectives.
*   **Text-Only Pre-training:** **Figure 10** confirms that shifting the pre-training ratio to include 40% text-only data improves knowledge benchmarks (MMMU +0.99 points) with minimal visual degradation.

**Limitations and Conditional Results:**
*   **Synthetic Captions Ambiguity:** In **Figure 9(b)**, the benefit of synthetic captions (LLaVA-Recap, ShareGPT4V) over pure OCR data is inconclusive during continual pre-training. While the authors later show in **Appendix A.1** that *in-house* generated captions can help, the initial finding suggests that simply adding public synthetic data is not a silver bullet and may depend heavily on data quality and style.
*   **Multi-Image vs. Base Capabilities:** **Figure 7(right)** shows that increasing the multi-image data ratio ($w_{multi}$) improves multi-image benchmarks but degrades the base MMBase score. The chosen ratio of 0.1 is a compromise; users prioritizing exclusively single-image tasks might prefer a lower multi-image weight.
*   **Video Frame Limits:** The video variant is limited to **24 frames** due to token constraints, and dynamic splitting is disabled for video. This results in slightly lower performance on very long-form video benchmarks (e.g., VideoMME) compared to models optimized specifically for long contexts with more frames.
*   **Grounding Sensitivity:** **Table 2** reveals that grounding performance is highly sensitive to the minimum grid size ($n_{min}$) during inference. Changing $n_{min}$ from 4 to 1 causes a drastic drop in grounding scores (e.g., Refer&Ground avg drops from 74.0 to 24.5 in row 7), indicating that the coordinate conversion logic relies heavily on consistent grid assumptions.

### 5.5 Conclusion on Experimental Validity

The experimental analysis in MM1.5 is robust and convincing. By holding the architecture constant and systematically varying data composition, resolution, and training stages, the authors provide causal evidence for their design choices. The results definitively show that:
1.  **Small models can ground:** The RefCOCO scores (~85%) for 3B models are undeniable proof that grounding is a data problem, not a scale problem.
2.  **Resolution matters early:** The degradation caused by low-res continual pre-training is a critical warning for future practitioners.
3.  **Text strengthens vision:** The 50:10:40 pre-training split validates that strengthening the language backbone is essential for complex multimodal reasoning.

While trade-offs exist (particularly between specialization and generalization), the final "All Mixture" recipe demonstrably yields a model family that rivals or exceeds significantly larger competitors across the board, successfully achieving the paper's goal of efficient, high-performance multimodal AI.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While MM1.5 demonstrates that data-centric optimization can yield state-of-the-art performance in small-scale models, the approach is not without significant constraints, trade-offs, and unresolved challenges. The empirical success of the model relies on specific assumptions about data availability and computational resources that may not hold for all practitioners. Furthermore, the "balancing act" required to integrate diverse capabilities inevitably leads to performance compromises in specific domains.

### 6.1 The Computational Cost of High-Resolution Continual Pre-training
A central pillar of the MM1.5 recipe is the **high-resolution continual pre-training stage** (Stage 2), which the authors identify as crucial for text-rich understanding. However, this introduces a steep computational barrier.
*   **Resolution Dependency:** The ablation study in **Figure 9(a)** reveals a non-linear dependency on resolution. Training at $378 \times 378$ resolution actually *degraded* performance (58.28 MMBase score) compared to skipping the stage entirely (58.8). This implies that practitioners cannot simply reuse low-resolution pre-training pipelines to save costs; they *must* commit to high-resolution processing ($1344 \times 1344$) to see any benefit.
*   **Token Explosion:** Processing images at 4 Megapixels via dynamic splitting significantly increases the sequence length of image tokens. While the paper notes in **Section 3.5.1** that dynamic splitting is only slightly less efficient than static splitting on average (539k vs. 500k sub-images in a sample), the *peak* memory and compute requirements for handling large grids (up to 16 sub-images during inference) are substantial. This limits the batch size and necessitates powerful GPU clusters, potentially negating the "efficiency" gains of using a smaller model backbone during the training phase.

### 6.2 The "Pareto Frontier" of Capability Trade-offs
The paper explicitly acknowledges that optimizing for one capability often comes at the expense of another. The final "All Mixture" recipe represents a compromise rather than a global optimum for every single task.
*   **Grounding vs. General Knowledge:** As shown in **Figure 6(d)** and **Figure 8**, increasing the ratio of referring and grounding data ($\alpha = 2.0$) dramatically boosts grounding scores (e.g., RefCOCO) but causes a measurable drop in the **MMBase score** (the aggregate of general, text-rich, and knowledge capabilities). The authors accept a slight regression in general VQA and knowledge tasks to enable the grounding feature. For applications where grounding is irrelevant, this trade-off represents an inefficiency in the training data usage.
*   **Multi-Image vs. Single-Image Performance:** Similarly, **Figure 7(right)** illustrates that increasing the weight of multi-image data ($w_{multi}$) improves multi-image reasoning benchmarks but degrades single-image base capabilities. The chosen ratio of $w_{multi} = 0.1$ is a heuristic balance; a model trained with $w_{multi} = 0$ would likely perform better on standard single-image benchmarks like MME or POPE, while a model with $w_{multi} = 0.3$ would excel at multi-image tasks but struggle with basic visual question answering. This suggests that a single "generalist" model may never be optimal for highly specialized downstream tasks.

### 6.3 Sensitivity to Inference Hyperparameters
The robustness of MM1.5's grounding capability is surprisingly fragile with respect to inference-time configuration, specifically the dynamic image splitting parameters.
*   **Grid Configuration Sensitivity:** **Table 2** (Row 7) presents a critical failure mode: changing the minimum number of sub-images ($n_{min}$) from 4 to 1 *only during inference* causes the Refer & Ground average score to collapse from **74.0** to **24.5**.
*   **Coordinate Conversion Fragility:** The authors attribute this to the difficulty in converting local sub-image coordinates to global image coordinates when the grid structure changes unexpectedly. This implies that MM1.5 is not truly "resolution-agnostic" in a plug-and-play sense; deploying the model requires careful tuning of $(n_{min}, n_{max})$ to match the training distribution. If a user attempts to process a very high-resolution image by increasing $n_{max}$ without adjusting $n_{min}$ appropriately, or if the aspect ratio forces a grid configuration unseen during training, the model's spatial reasoning may fail catastrophically.

### 6.4 Data Dependencies and the Synthetic Caption Ambiguity
The success of MM1.5 relies heavily on the availability of specific, high-quality datasets that may not be universally accessible.
*   **Reliance on Proprietary/Specific OCR Data:** The continual pre-training stage utilizes 45M samples from specific datasets (PDFA, IDL, DocStruct-4M). While these are described, the exact curation and cleaning processes are critical. The finding in **Figure 9(b)** that public synthetic captions (LLaVA-Recap, ShareGPT4V) did *not* improve performance over pure OCR data suggests that simply throwing more data at the model is ineffective.
*   **The "In-House" Caveat:** In **Appendix A.1**, the authors reveal that *in-house* generated synthetic captions (using a self-trained 3B captioner) *did* show improvements when scaled to 7M samples. This creates a circular dependency: to replicate the best results, one arguably needs a strong pre-existing model to generate high-quality training data. This limits the reproducibility for researchers who do not have access to the specific "self-training" engine or the computational resources to generate 7M high-quality captions.
*   **Domain Gaps:** The model's performance is tied to the distribution of its training data. While it excels on documents and mobile UIs (which are well-represented in the training mix), the paper does not provide evidence of strong performance on highly specialized domains like medical imaging or satellite imagery, where the visual features and text layouts differ significantly from the web-scraped data used.

### 6.5 Video Understanding Constraints
The extension to video (**MM1.5-Video**) highlights the limitations of treating video as a sequence of static images without architectural modifications for temporal modeling.
*   **Frame Limitations:** Due to token limits, the model is restricted to sampling only **24 frames** per video, and dynamic image splitting is disabled for video inputs (**Section 5**). This fixed, low-frame approach struggles with long-form videos requiring dense temporal reasoning.
*   **Performance Gap on Long Context:** As noted in **Section 5.2**, MM1.5-Video lags behind **LLaVA-OneVision-7B** on long-form benchmarks like **VideoMME** and **EgoSchema**. The authors hypothesize this is due to LLaVA-OneVision's use of 32 frames and joint image-video training stages. This indicates that while the "multi-image as video" approach works for short clips or summary tasks, it hits a ceiling for complex, long-duration temporal reasoning that likely requires dedicated temporal encoders or memory mechanisms.

### 6.6 Open Questions
Several questions remain unanswered by the current study:
*   **Scalability of MoE beyond 3B:** While MoE variants are provided for 1B and 3B models, the paper does not explore MoE scaling at the 7B or 30B levels. It is unclear if the efficiency gains (inference cost vs. performance) hold linearly as the model size increases.
*   **Robustness to Adversarial Layouts:** The sensitivity of grounding to grid changes raises questions about robustness. How does the model perform on images with unusual aspect ratios that force extreme padding or non-standard grid splits not well-covered in the training set?
*   **Real-World Latency:** While the models are "mobile-friendly" in terms of parameter count, the paper does not report end-to-end latency on actual mobile hardware (e.g., iPhone or Android devices) when processing 4MP images with dynamic splitting. The computational overhead of the vision encoder and the long token sequences generated by high-resolution splitting could result in prohibitive latency for real-time interactive applications, despite the small LLM backbone.

In summary, MM1.5 offers a powerful blueprint for data-efficient training, but it shifts the bottleneck from **model architecture** to **data engineering and compute-intensive pre-training**. The approach assumes access to massive, high-quality OCR datasets and the compute to process them at high resolution, and it requires careful hyperparameter tuning at inference to maintain spatial consistency.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The release of MM1.5 marks a pivotal shift in the development of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs), moving the field's primary lever of innovation from **architectural complexity** to **data-centric engineering**. By demonstrating that a 3B parameter model can outperform 7B+ competitors on specialized tasks like visual grounding and document understanding, this work fundamentally alters the cost-benefit analysis for researchers and practitioners. The implications extend beyond mere benchmark scores, reshaping how we approach model efficiency, deployment, and future research trajectories.

### 7.1 Shifting the Landscape: The Democratization of Capabilities
Historically, advanced multimodal capabilities such as **visual referring and grounding** (outputting precise coordinates) or **high-resolution OCR** were considered emergent properties of massive scale, accessible only via proprietary giants like GPT-4o or large open-source models (13B+ parameters). MM1.5 dismantles this assumption.

*   **Capabilities are Configurable, Not Emergent:** The paper proves that skills like grounding are not magic that appears at 70B parameters; they are learnable behaviors instilled by specific data densities. By allocating just 22.5% of the SFT mixture to referring/grounding data, the authors "switched on" this capability in a 1B model. This suggests that future model development will focus less on scaling laws and more on **capability-specific data recipes**.
*   **The End of the "One-Size-Fits-All" Pre-training:** The success of the **50:10:40** pre-training ratio (heavily weighting text-only data) challenges the dogma that multimodal models must be trained predominantly on image-text pairs. It implies that for small models, the bottleneck is often **language reasoning**, not visual alignment. Future pre-training strategies will likely decouple language strengthening from visual alignment, treating them as distinct optimization problems rather than a single monolithic objective.
*   **Resolution as a First-Class Citizen:** The finding that low-resolution continual pre-training *harms* performance (Section 6.1) forces a reevaluation of training pipelines. The era of training at $336 \times 336$ and hoping fine-tuning fixes resolution issues is over. High-resolution processing must be integrated into the foundational stages of training, raising the compute floor for entry but ensuring higher capability ceilings for efficient models.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions
MM1.5 opens several critical avenues for future investigation, particularly in optimizing the trade-offs identified in the ablation studies.

*   **Dynamic Data Mixing and Curriculum Learning:** The static "All Mixture" used in MM1.5 represents a compromise. Future work could explore **dynamic curriculum learning**, where the data mixture shifts during training—e.g., starting with high text-only ratios to build reasoning, transitioning to high-resolution OCR for visual acuity, and finishing with grounding data for spatial precision. This could potentially mitigate the slight performance dips in general VQA observed when maximizing grounding data.
*   **Robust Coordinate Conversion Mechanisms:** The catastrophic drop in grounding performance when inference grid parameters ($n_{min}$) deviate from training settings (Table 2, Row 7) highlights a fragility in current spatial reasoning. Research into **resolution-invariant coordinate representations** (e.g., relative coordinates normalized to [0, 1] independent of grid splits, or learned spatial embeddings that adapt to variable tile counts) is urgently needed to make grounding robust across arbitrary resolutions.
*   **Efficient Synthetic Data Engines:** While public synthetic captions failed to boost performance, the authors' *in-house* self-trained captioner succeeded (Appendix A.1). This points to a rich area for research: developing **lightweight, open-source data generation engines** that can produce high-quality, domain-specific training data (like OCR or grounding pairs) without relying on black-box proprietary APIs. The goal is to democratize the "data flywheel" that MM1.5 leveraged internally.
*   **Temporal Architectures for Video:** The limitation of MM1.5-Video to 24 frames and its struggle with long-form temporal reasoning suggests that treating video as a bag of images has a hard ceiling. Future work will likely hybridize MM1.5's strong visual backbone with **dedicated temporal encoders** or **memory-augmented architectures** (e.g., recurrent states or sparse attention over time) to handle hour-long videos without exploding token counts.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases
The specific strengths of MM1.5—small size, high-resolution understanding, and native grounding—make it uniquely suited for several high-impact real-world applications.

*   **On-Device Mobile Agents (UI Automation):** The **MM1.5-UI** variant demonstrates the feasibility of running autonomous agents directly on smartphones. Unlike cloud-based models, a 3B model can run locally with low latency, interpreting screen contents, reading tiny text (e.g., "Settings," "Login"), and outputting precise tap coordinates `<x, y>` to execute user commands like "Book the cheapest flight" or "Turn on Night Shift." This enables privacy-preserving, offline-capable digital assistants.
*   **Industrial Document Processing:** For industries dealing with high-resolution invoices, scientific papers, or engineering diagrams, MM1.5 offers a deployable solution for **edge-based document analysis**. A warehouse robot or a field technician's tablet could instantly read serial numbers, extract table data from complex charts, or verify safety labels without needing an internet connection to query a large cloud model.
*   **Visual Search and Retail:** The native grounding capability allows for **fine-grained visual search**. A user could upload a photo of a room and ask, "Where is the red lamp?" and the model could return the bounding box, enabling AR overlays or direct purchase links. This is far more efficient than current solutions that require cropping or external object detectors.
*   **Educational Tools:** The strong performance on **ScienceQA** and **MathVista** at the 1B/3B scale makes MM1.5 ideal for educational apps that run on low-end tablets. It can solve geometry problems by interpreting diagrams, explain steps in reasoning, and even point to specific parts of a diagram ("The hypotenuse is here...") to guide student learning.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance
For practitioners looking to adopt or extend MM1.5, the following guidelines clarify when and how to utilize this approach:

*   **When to Prefer MM1.5:**
    *   **Constraint:** You have strict latency, memory, or privacy constraints requiring on-device inference (mobile, edge IoT, robotics).
    *   **Requirement:** Your application demands **visual grounding** (outputting coordinates) or **high-resolution text reading** (OCR).
    *   **Alternative:** If your use case is purely general image captioning on low-res images and you have abundant cloud GPU resources, larger models like LLaVA-NeXT-34B or proprietary APIs might still offer marginal gains in fluency, though MM1.5 is highly competitive.

*   **Critical Integration Steps:**
    *   **Match Inference Grids:** When deploying, ensure your inference configuration for dynamic splitting $(n_{min}, n_{max})$ aligns closely with the training distribution (e.g., $n_{min}=4$). Drastic deviations will break grounding capabilities. Do not treat the model as fully resolution-agnostic without testing coordinate stability.
    *   **Disable Splitting for Video:** If adapting MM1.5 for video, follow the paper's lead: disable dynamic splitting per frame to manage token budgets, and limit frame sampling (e.g., 24 frames) unless you implement additional temporal compression.
    *   **Data is King:** If fine-tuning for a new domain (e.g., medical imaging), do not rely solely on architectural tweaks. You must curate a **domain-specific SFT mixture** that mirrors the MM1.5 strategy: include grounding pairs if spatial localization is needed, and ensure high-resolution inputs during any continual pre-training phase.

*   **Reproducibility Note:** The authors utilized specific in-house datasets (PDFA, IDL, proprietary OCR mixes) and a self-trained captioner. To fully replicate the SOTA results, practitioners may need to substitute these with equivalent public high-resolution OCR datasets (e.g., DocVQA, ICDAR variants) and potentially invest in generating their own high-quality synthetic captions if public ones prove insufficient, as noted in the ambiguity of Figure 9(b).

In conclusion, MM1.5 serves as a definitive proof-of-concept that **data engineering is the new architecture**. It provides a reproducible blueprint for building efficient, capable, and specialized multimodal models, empowering the next generation of AI applications to run locally, reason deeply, and interact precisely with the visual world.
