## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces **OLMES** (Open Language Model Evaluation Standard), a fully documented and reproducible framework designed to resolve the critical lack of consistency in Large Language Model (LLM) evaluations, where varying choices in prompt formatting, few-shot examples, and probability normalization can cause performance scores on tasks like **ARC-CHALLENGE** to fluctuate by over **10%** across different references (Table 1). OLMES addresses this by standardizing key variables—such as using curated **5-shot** examples, specific **PMI** or **character-based** normalizations depending on the dataset, and dynamically selecting between **Multiple-Choice (MCF)** and **Cloze (CF)** formulations based on model strength—to enable meaningful comparisons across **15 diverse base models** ranging from **1B to 70B** parameters. By providing justified, open-source protocols for **10 popular benchmarks** including **MMLU**, **HellaSwag**, and **BoolQ**, OLMES ensures that claims about model capabilities are scientifically credible and directly comparable, eliminating the ambiguity that currently plagues leaderboards and research papers.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Reproducibility Crisis in LLM Benchmarking

The central problem this paper addresses is the **lack of a unified, documented standard for evaluating Large Language Models (LLMs)**, which has led to a crisis of reproducibility and comparability in AI research. While the field rapidly releases new models claiming state-of-the-art performance, these claims are often built on shaky foundations because the evaluation setups are inconsistent, under-specified, or arbitrarily chosen.

The core issue is that minor, often unreported details in how a task is presented to a model can drastically alter the resulting performance metric. The authors highlight that variations in:
*   **Prompt Formatting:** Simple changes like using "Question:" versus "Q:", or how answer labels are formatted (e.g., `A.` vs `(A)`), can shift scores.
*   **In-Context Examples:** The number of examples (0-shot vs. 25-shot) and whether they are randomly sampled or manually curated significantly impact results.
*   **Probability Normalization:** When scoring answers based on token probabilities, different methods to account for answer length (e.g., normalizing by character count vs. token count vs. Pointwise Mutual Information) yield different rankings.
*   **Task Formulation:** Whether the model is asked to predict a label (Multiple-Choice Formulation, **MCF**) or complete a sentence with the answer text (Cloze Formulation, **CF**) creates a massive divergence in scores, especially depending on the model's size and training stage.

The real-world impact of this ambiguity is severe. As shown in **Table 1**, the reported score for the same model (e.g., `Llama2-13B`) on the same dataset (`ARC-CHALLENGE`) varies wildly across different reputable sources (References 1 through 6). For instance, one reference might report a score of **49.4**, while another reports **67.6** for the exact same model. This discrepancy arises not from the model changing, but from the evaluation setup changing (e.g., one uses 0-shot CF, another uses 25-shot MCF). Consequently, researchers cannot reliably determine if a new model is genuinely an improvement over previous work, nor can they reproduce the results claimed in model cards or leaderboards. This undermines the scientific credibility of AI progress, as "state-of-the-art" becomes a moving target defined by evaluation cherry-picking rather than genuine capability.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches

Before OLMES, the community relied on several efforts to standardize evaluation, but these approaches fell short in critical ways:

1.  **Evaluation Frameworks with Underspecified Defaults:** Tools like the **Eleuther LM Evaluation Harness** and benchmarks like **HELM (Holistic Evaluation of Language Models)** provide the *infrastructure* to run evaluations and often support multiple configurations. However, they frequently present these configurations as open choices to the user without mandating a single, justified default. When defaults exist, the rationale behind specific choices (e.g., why use character normalization instead of PMI?) is often not rigorously documented or experimentally validated within the framework itself. This leads to researchers selecting different settings for different papers, perpetuating the inconsistency.
2.  **Leaderboards with Inconsistent Adoptions:** Leaderboards like the **Hugging Face Open LLM Leaderboard** enforce a specific setup for the models they rank, ensuring internal consistency. However, model developers often do not adopt these specific setups for their own official evaluations. Instead, they may use different prompt formats, shot counts, or normalization techniques in their technical reports (as evidenced by the discrepancies in **Table 1** between leaderboard scores and paper-reported scores). This creates a fragmented landscape where a model's "official" score differs from its "leaderboard" score, confusing the community about the model's true standing.
3.  **Lack of Justification for Design Choices:** Existing literature often adopts evaluation practices based on convention rather than empirical evidence. For example, the shift from Cloze Formulation (CF) to Multiple-Choice Formulation (MCF) happened gradually as models got stronger, but there was no standardized protocol for *when* to switch or how to compare models that straddle this capability gap. Similarly, the choice of normalization was often arbitrary or based on legacy code rather than a systematic study of which method yields the most accurate signal for specific datasets.

### Positioning OLMES Relative to Existing Work

OLMES positions itself not merely as another evaluation tool or leaderboard, but as a **completely documented, practical, and open standard** that resolves the "open choices" problem of prior work. Its distinct contributions relative to existing efforts are:

*   **From "Options" to "Standards":** Unlike frameworks that offer a menu of options, OLMES prescribes a single, justified configuration for every variable. It does not ask the user to choose between normalization schemes; it dictates that `ARC-CHALLENGE` must use **PMI normalization** while `BOOLQ` must use **no normalization**, backed by new experiments (Section 3.3) showing why these specific choices maximize signal accuracy.
*   **Bridging the Model Strength Gap:** A unique insight of OLMES is the necessity of supporting **both** MCF and CF formulations dynamically. Prior work often committed to one formulation. OLMES recognizes that smaller, early-training base models fail at MCF (scoring near random) but succeed at CF, while larger models excel at MCF but saturate on CF. By standardizing a protocol to evaluate using **both** and report the best result (Section 3.4), OLMES enables meaningful comparison across the entire spectrum of model sizes (1B to 70B parameters), a capability missing from standards that assume all models can handle natural language multiple-choice prompts.
*   **Rigorous Documentation of Rationale:** OLMES differentiates itself by explicitly documenting the *why* behind every decision. It combines a review of existing literature with new ablation studies (e.g., testing 4 different normalization methods across 15 models) to resolve open questions. For instance, it justifies the use of **manually curated 5-shot examples** over random sampling to ensure balanced label coverage and high quality, addressing the variance introduced by random few-shot selection (Section 3.2).
*   **Practicality for Development Cycles:** While benchmarks like HELM are comprehensive, they can be computationally expensive. OLMES is designed with **practicality** in mind for model developers iterating during training. It caps evaluation instances at 1,000 for large datasets and limits few-shot examples to 5, striking a balance between statistical significance and computational efficiency, making it feasible to run frequently during the model development lifecycle.

In essence, OLMES moves the field from a state of "evaluations are reproducible only if you guess the exact settings" to "evaluations are reproducible by definition because the standard leaves no ambiguity." It serves as the missing layer of consensus that allows leaderboards, papers, and model cards to speak the same quantitative language.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a prescriptive engineering standard rather than a novel algorithmic discovery, establishing a rigid protocol for transforming raw benchmark datasets into reproducible performance metrics for Large Language Models (LLMs). The core idea is that by fixing every degree of freedom in the evaluation pipeline—from prompt string construction to probability normalization—the community can eliminate the variance that currently obscures true model progress.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
OLMES is a strictly defined "recipe book" for evaluating language models that replaces arbitrary choices with documented, experimentally verified defaults for every step of the testing process. It solves the problem of incomparable scores by enforcing a single, unified path through the evaluation pipeline, ensuring that a score of "70%" means the exact same thing regardless of who runs the test or which model is being tested.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The OLMES system operates as a deterministic data processing pipeline with four sequential stages:
1.  **Data Ingestion & Sampling Module:** Takes raw benchmark datasets (e.g., MMLU, ARC) and filters them to a fixed subset (maximum 1,000 instances) using a specific random seed to ensure consistency.
2.  **Prompt Construction Engine:** Injects a manually curated set of 5 in-context examples and formats the target question using strict string templates (e.g., `Question: <text>\nAnswer:`), handling label tokenization quirks like prefix spaces.
3.  **Formulation & Inference Router:** Dynamically selects between two distinct task formats—**Multiple-Choice Formulation (MCF)** where the model predicts a label (A/B/C), and **Cloze Formulation (CF)** where the model completes the answer text—running both for every model to capture the strongest signal.
4.  **Scoring & Normalization Unit:** Calculates the final accuracy by applying dataset-specific probability normalization techniques (e.g., Pointwise Mutual Information or character-length normalization) to the raw token log-probabilities, then reports the maximum score achieved between the MCF and CF runs.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Instance Formatting and Tokenization:** We first explain how raw text is converted into prompts, detailing the specific string templates and the critical handling of tokenizer quirks (prefix spaces) that prevent scoring errors.
*   **Few-Shot Example Curation:** We detail the move away from random sampling to manually curated 5-shot examples, explaining how this stabilizes the label distribution and improves signal quality.
*   **Task Formulation Strategy (MCF vs. CF):** We analyze the dual-formulation approach, explaining why weaker models require "cloze" completion while stronger models need "multiple-choice" labels, and how OLMES bridges this gap.
*   **Probability Normalization Mechanics:** We provide the mathematical definitions for the four normalization schemes and justify the specific selection of `pmi`, `char`, or `none` for each of the 10 benchmark tasks.
*   **Aggregation and Reporting:** We conclude with the logic for selecting the final score and the specific averaging methods (macro vs. micro) used for complex datasets like MMLU.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Standardizing Instance Formatting and Tokenization
The foundation of OLMES is a rigid set of string templates that convert dataset fields into model inputs, designed to remove ambiguity in how questions and answers are presented. For most tasks, the system constructs the prompt using the prefix `Question: ` followed by the query text, and suffixed by `Answer: `, creating a natural instruction-free interface that clarifies the task without relying on the model's ability to follow complex commands. There are three specific exceptions to this rule to preserve dataset semantics: **PIQA** uses `Goal: ` as the prefix; **HellaSwag** (in MCF mode) uses `Choose the best continuation:`; and **WinoGrande** (in MCF mode) uses `Fill in the blank: `. When using the Cloze Formulation (CF) for HellaSwag and WinoGrande, these prefixes are entirely removed to approximate a pure language modeling task where the model simply predicts the next natural token sequence.

A critical, non-obvious design choice in OLMES addresses a common failure mode in tokenizers regarding answer labels. In Multiple-Choice Formulation (MCF), the model must predict a single token corresponding to the correct label (e.g., "A", "B", "C"). However, many tokenizers treat a letter at the start of a line differently than a letter following a space; for instance, the Llama tokenizer produces a distinct token `_A` for " A" but a different token `A` for "A" at the start of a string. To ensure the target label token in the prompt matches the token the model generates, OLMES mandates adding a prefix space before every answer label in the option list. The format is strictly `\n A. <choice_text>` rather than `\nA. <choice_text>`. This guarantees that when the model outputs the completion ` A`, it corresponds to the exact same token ID as the label presented in the context, preventing systematic scoring errors caused by tokenization mismatches.

#### Curated Few-Shot Example Selection
OLMES rejects the common practice of randomly sampling in-context examples, instead mandating the use of a fixed, manually curated set of exactly **5 shots** for every task. Random sampling introduces high variance because a random batch might accidentally contain skewed label distributions (e.g., 4 out of 5 examples having answer "A") or low-quality instances, which can artificially inflate or deflate model performance. The OLMES curation process involves manually selecting examples from the training set to ensure two properties: **label balance** (the 5 examples cover the full range of possible answer keys, e.g., A, B, C, D, E) and **topic diversity** (the examples are not all from the same semantic cluster). This fixed set of 5 examples is reused for every model evaluation, ensuring that differences in scores are due to model capabilities rather than the luck of the draw in example selection. The decision to cap examples at 5 is based on empirical evidence suggesting that increasing the count beyond 5 yields diminishing returns in performance while linearly increasing computational cost, making 5 the optimal trade-off for practical development cycles.

#### Dynamic Task Formulation: MCF vs. CF
The most significant architectural innovation in OLMES is the simultaneous support for two distinct task formulations, recognizing that a single format cannot fairly evaluate models across the entire spectrum of capability.
*   **Multiple-Choice Formulation (MCF):** The model is presented with the question and labeled options (A, B, C, D) and asked to predict the label. This is the natural format for humans and strong models but often results in near-random performance for smaller or early-training base models that have not yet learned to map questions to abstract labels.
*   **Cloze Formulation (CF):** The model is presented with the question and asked to complete the sentence with the actual answer text (e.g., "Answer: iron"). This leverages the model's core next-token prediction objective and often elicits correct knowledge from weaker models that fail at MCF.

OLMES dictates that evaluators must run **both** formulations for every model and dataset. The system then compares the accuracy from the MCF run against the accuracy from the CF run and reports the **maximum** of the two as the final score. This approach is justified by training dynamics observed in models like OLMo-7B, where early in training (e.g., first 400 billion tokens), the CF score provides a strong, non-random signal of learning while the MCF score remains flat at random chance. As the model matures, it acquires the ability to understand the MCF prompt, at which point the MCF score surpasses the CF score and provides a more accurate ceiling for performance. By taking the maximum, OLMES ensures that small models are not penalized for lacking "instruction following" skills, while large models are credited with their full capability to handle natural multiple-choice structures.

#### Probability Normalization Schemes
When using the Cloze Formulation (CF), simply comparing the raw log-probability of the answer string $P(a_i|q)$ is flawed because it inherently biases the model toward shorter answers. OLMES implements and prescribes specific normalization techniques to correct for this length bias, selecting the optimal method for each dataset based on extensive ablation studies across 15 models.

The paper defines four normalization strategies mathematically:
1.  **None:** Uses the raw log-probability.
    $$ \text{Score} = \ln(P(a_i|q)) $$
2.  **Token Normalization:** Divides the log-probability by the number of tokens in the answer $N_{tokens}(a_i)$.
    $$ \text{Score} = \frac{\ln(P(a_i|q))}{N_{tokens}(a_i)} $$
3.  **Character Normalization:** Divides the log-probability by the number of characters in the answer $N_{chars}(a_i)$.
    $$ \text{Score} = \frac{\ln(P(a_i|q))}{N_{chars}(a_i)} $$
    *Note: OLMES specifies including the leading space in the character count if present in the tokenization.*
4.  **PMI (Pointwise Mutual Information):** Normalizes by the unconditional probability of the answer string $P(a_i|u)$, where $u$ is a generic prompt like "Answer:". This measures how much more likely the answer is given the question compared to its prior likelihood.
    $$ \text{Score} = \ln\left(\frac{P(a_i|q)}{P(a_i|u)}\right) = \ln(P(a_i|q)) - \ln(P(a_i|u)) $$

OLMES assigns a specific normalization scheme to each of the 10 targeted tasks based on empirical "win rates" (how often a method produced the highest accuracy across models):
*   **PMI Normalization:** Prescribed for **ARC-CHALLENGE**, **CommonsenseQA**, and **OpenBookQA**. These datasets contain answer choices with unexpected or specific phrases (e.g., "Whirlpool bath" vs. "Bathtub") where the prior likelihood $P(a_i|u)$ varies significantly. PMI effectively cancels out this bias, allowing the model's reasoning capability to drive the score. While computationally more expensive (requiring an extra forward pass for the unconditional probability), the gain in signal accuracy is substantial.
*   **Character Normalization:** Prescribed for **ARC-EASY**, **HellaSwag**, **MMLU**, **PIQA**, and **Social IQa**. Experiments showed this method empirically outperforms others for these tasks and is computationally cheaper than PMI. It is also widely implemented in existing frameworks (like the Eleuther LM Harness as `acc_norm`), facilitating adoption.
*   **No Normalization:** Prescribed for **BoolQ** and **WinoGrande**. For BoolQ, the answers are strictly "yes" or "no" (single tokens), making length normalization unnecessary and potentially harmful (since "yes" has more characters than "no", character normalization would artificially penalize "yes"). For WinoGrande, the CF setup involves identical continuations with different prompts, rendering normalization irrelevant.

Table 3 in the paper demonstrates that the difference between the OLMES-prescribed normalization and the theoretical "oracle" best normalization for any given model is negligible (often $&lt;1\%$), validating these fixed choices as robust defaults.

#### Data Sampling and Aggregation Protocols
To ensure evaluations are computationally feasible for frequent use during model training, OLMES enforces strict sampling limits on dataset sizes. If a dataset split contains more than **1,500 instances**, OLMES samples exactly **1,000 instances** using a fixed random seed (`Random(1234).sample()` in Python). This cap aligns with practices in HELM and prevents the diminishing returns of evaluating on tens of thousands of examples, where the statistical noise from prompt formatting often dwarfs the gain from larger sample sizes. The system prioritizes the **test split** if labels are publicly available; otherwise, it falls back to the **validation split**.

For the **MMLU** dataset, which comprises 57 distinct subjects, OLMES specifies the use of **macro-averaging** rather than micro-averaging. Micro-averaging calculates accuracy over all 14,042 individual questions globally, which can be dominated by larger subjects. Macro-averaging calculates the accuracy for each of the 57 subjects independently and then averages those 57 scores. This approach ensures that niche fields (e.g., abstract algebra) contribute equally to the final score as broad fields (e.g., high school biology), providing a more representative measure of a model's general knowledge breadth.

#### Final Score Selection Logic
The final output of the OLMES pipeline for a given model and task is determined by a "best-of" logic. Let $Acc_{MCF}$ be the accuracy obtained using the Multiple-Choice Formulation and $Acc_{CF}$ be the accuracy obtained using the Cloze Formulation with the prescribed normalization. The reported OLMES score $S$ is:
$$ S = \max(Acc_{MCF}, Acc_{CF}) $$
This logic is explicitly marked in the results tables (e.g., Table 4) with a dagger symbol ($\dagger$) indicating when the MCF score was the winner. This mechanism ensures that the reported metric always reflects the model's peak capability on the task, regardless of whether that capability is best accessed via label prediction or text completion. For example, a small 1B parameter model might score 30% on MCF (random chance) but 50% on CF; OLMES reports 50%. Conversely, a 70B model might score 69% on CF but 93% on MCF; OLMES reports 93%. This prevents the unfair comparison that would arise if all models were forced into a single formulation that favors only one end of the size spectrum.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The OLMES standard introduces several fundamental shifts in how the community approaches LLM evaluation, moving beyond incremental tweaks to address systemic flaws in reproducibility and comparability. While prior work often treated evaluation settings as hyperparameters to be tuned or left as user choices, OLMES establishes them as fixed, justified constants. The following insights represent the core innovations that distinguish this work from existing frameworks like HELM or the Eleuther LM Evaluation Harness.

### 4.1 The Dynamic Formulation Protocol: Bridging the "Capability Gap"
**Innovation Type:** Fundamental Methodological Shift

Prior evaluation standards typically commit to a single task formulation—either Multiple-Choice (MCF) or Cloze (CF)—for a given benchmark. This binary choice creates a "capability gap" where evaluations are either unfair to weak models (which fail at MCF) or saturate too early for strong models (which max out on CF). For instance, as shown in **Figure 1**, a model like `OLMo-7B` scores near random chance on MMLU using MCF during early training stages, providing no useful gradient for development, while CF provides a clear learning signal. Conversely, for mature models like `Llama3-70B`, CF caps at ~69% accuracy while MCF reveals the true capability of ~93% (**Figure 2**).

OLMES innovates by institutionalizing a **dynamic dual-formulation protocol**. Instead of forcing a single format, it mandates running *both* MCF and CF for every model and reporting the maximum score ($S = \max(Acc_{MCF}, Acc_{CF})$).
*   **Why it matters:** This is the first standard to explicitly acknowledge that "task understanding" (knowing how to answer a multiple-choice question) is a distinct skill from "knowledge retrieval" (knowing the answer). By decoupling these, OLMES allows researchers to track knowledge acquisition in small models without penalizing them for lacking instruction-following capabilities, while simultaneously capturing the full reasoning potential of large models. This transforms evaluation from a pass/fail test based on format compatibility into a continuous, comparable metric across the entire 1B–70B parameter spectrum.

### 4.2 Empirically Grounded, Task-Specific Normalization
**Innovation Type:** Resolution of Open Scientific Questions

A major source of variance in prior literature is the arbitrary selection of probability normalization techniques for Cloze Formulation. Frameworks often expose `none`, `token`, `char`, and `pmi` as options without prescribing a default, leading to inconsistent adoptions (e.g., some papers use `char` for ARC, others use `pmi`). OLMES resolves this ambiguity not by convention, but through a systematic empirical study across 15 diverse models.

The key insight here is that **no single normalization scheme is optimal for all tasks**; the optimal choice depends on the semantic nature of the answer space.
*   **Differentiation:** Unlike prior works that might default to `char` normalization for simplicity, OLMES prescribes **PMI (Pointwise Mutual Information)** specifically for datasets with heterogeneous answer lengths and specific terminology (e.g., `ARC-CHALLENGE`, `OpenBookQA`), where answers like "Whirlpool bath" have vastly different priors than "Bathtub". Conversely, it mandates **no normalization** for `BoolQ` to avoid penalizing the token "yes" simply because it has more characters than "no".
*   **Significance:** As detailed in **Table 3**, the OLMES-prescribed scheme achieves performance within negligible margins (&lt;1% difference) of the theoretical "oracle" best normalization for each specific model-task pair. This demonstrates that OLMES successfully distills complex, model-dependent optimal strategies into a simple, static lookup table. This eliminates the need for researchers to perform costly ablation studies on normalization for every new model release, standardizing the "signal-to-noise" ratio across the field.

### 4.3 Deterministic Curation Over Stochastic Sampling
**Innovation Type:** Reduction of Evaluation Variance

Standard practice in LLM evaluation involves randomly sampling few-shot examples from a training set for each run. While statistically valid in theory, this introduces unnecessary variance: a model might score higher simply because its random 5-shot batch happened to have balanced labels or easier examples. OLMES replaces this stochasticity with **deterministic, manually curated few-shot sets**.

*   **The Innovation:** OLMES provides fixed sets of 5 examples for each of the 10 tasks, curated to ensure **label balance** (covering all possible answer keys A-E) and **topic diversity**. This moves evaluation from a probabilistic estimate ("how does the model perform on average over random shots?") to a deterministic benchmark ("how does the model perform on this specific, high-quality representative set?").
*   **Impact on Reproducibility:** As highlighted in the Appendix (**Table 5**), while minor variations in prompts yield stable results, the variance introduced by random shot selection can be significant enough to obscure small but meaningful improvements between model iterations. By fixing the shots, OLMES ensures that a 1% score increase is attributable to model improvements, not sampling luck. This is critical for model developers iterating on architecture or data mixtures, where detecting small signal gains is essential.

### 4.4 The "Open Standard" Paradigm: From Tool to Protocol
**Innovation Type:** Sociotechnical Advancement in AI Research

Perhaps the most profound contribution of OLMES is conceptual: it redefines the role of an evaluation framework. Existing tools (like the Eleuther Harness) are **libraries** that provide the *capability* to evaluate, leaving the *design* of the evaluation to the user. This flexibility is the root cause of the reproducibility crisis; two researchers using the same tool can produce incomparable results by selecting different defaults.

OLMES positions itself as a **protocol**, not just a tool.
*   **Distinctiveness:** It does not merely offer options; it removes them. Every degree of freedom—from the exact string template (`Question: ` vs `Q: `) to the handling of tokenizer prefix spaces (Section 3.1)—is fixed and documented.
*   **Significance:** This shifts the burden of justification from the individual researcher to the standard itself. Once the community adopts OLMES, a claim like "Model X achieves 79.3 on ARC-CHALLENGE" becomes a scientifically rigorous, reproducible fact, comparable across time and institutions. It transforms evaluation from a "configurable experiment" into a "standardized measurement," akin to using a calibrated scale in physics rather than building a new balance for every paper. This cultural shift is essential for restoring scientific credibility to AI progress claims.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

The authors validate OLMES through a comprehensive empirical study designed to resolve open questions about evaluation design choices and to demonstrate the standard's ability to produce stable, meaningful comparisons across a wide spectrum of model capabilities. Unlike typical papers that introduce a new model and benchmark it against others, this work treats the **evaluation protocol itself** as the variable under investigation, systematically testing how different configurations affect performance signals.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

**Model Selection:**
To ensure the standard is robust across the entire landscape of open-weight base models, the authors selected **15 diverse pretrained LLMs** ranging from **1B to 70B parameters**. This selection intentionally covers:
*   **Small/Early-Training Models:** e.g., `Pythia-1B`, `OLMo-1B`, `TinyLlama-1.1B`. These models are critical for testing whether the standard can elicit non-random signals from weak systems.
*   **Mid-Range Models:** e.g., `Llama2-7B`, `Mistral-7B`, `Falcon-7B`.
*   **Large/State-of-the-Art Models:** e.g., `Llama2-13B`, `Llama3-8B`, `Llama3-70B`. These test whether the standard avoids saturation and distinguishes top-tier performance.

The focus is strictly on **base models** (not instruction-tuned), as these are the primary subjects of development cycles where consistent evaluation signals are most needed before fine-tuning.

**Targeted Tasks:**
The experiments cover **10 popular Multiple-Choice Question Answering (MCQA) benchmarks** (Table 2), selected for their ubiquity in community leaderboards and papers:
*   **Science & Reasoning:** `ARC-CHALLENGE`, `ARC-EASY`, `OpenBookQA`.
*   **Commonsense:** `CommonsenseQA`, `HellaSwag`, `PIQA`, `Social IQa`, `WinoGrande`.
*   **Knowledge & Factuality:** `MMLU` (covering 57 subjects), `BoolQ`.

**Metrics and Scoring:**
The primary metric is **accuracy** (percentage of correct predictions). For Cloze Formulation (CF), accuracy is determined after applying the specific normalization scheme prescribed for that task. The final reported score for any model-task pair is the maximum of the MCF and CF accuracies:
$$ S_{final} = \max(Acc_{MCF}, Acc_{CF}^{norm}) $$

**Experimental Variables:**
The authors conducted ablation studies on four key dimensions that typically cause variance:
1.  **Task Formulation:** Comparing MCF vs. CF across all models.
2.  **Probability Normalization:** Testing `none`, `token`, `char`, and `pmi` schemes.
3.  **Few-Shot Selection:** Comparing curated vs. random examples.
4.  **Prompt Formatting:** Testing sensitivity to minor string variations.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

The experiments yield three critical findings that justify the OLMES design choices: the necessity of dynamic formulation, the task-specific optimality of normalization, and the stability of the final scores.

#### The "Cross-Over" Phenomenon in Task Formulation
The most striking result, illustrated in **Figure 2** and detailed in **Tables 6 and 7**, is the divergent performance trajectory of MCF and CF as model size increases.
*   **Weak Models (1B–3B):** These models perform near random chance on MCF but show significant capability on CF. For example, on `ARC-CHALLENGE`, `Pythia-1B` scores **24.1%** with MCF (random is 25%) but **31.4%** with CF. Similarly, `OLMo-1B` scores **25.3%** (MCF) vs. **38.6%** (CF). Without CF, these models would appear to have zero reasoning capability.
*   **Strong Models (8B+):** As models grow, MCF performance surpasses CF. For `Llama3-70B` on `ARC-CHALLENGE`, the MCF score is **93.7%**, while the CF score lags significantly at **69.0%**. The gap represents a **5x difference in error rate** (6.3% error for MCF vs. 31% error for CF).
*   **The Cross-Over Point:** **Figure 1** tracks `OLMo-7B` during training, showing that MCF remains flat at random levels until approximately **400 billion tokens** of training data, after which it rapidly overtakes CF. This confirms that MCF is a skill acquired late in training, while CF provides a useful signal throughout.

By adopting the `max(MCF, CF)` rule, OLMES captures the **31.4%** signal for `Pythia-1B` and the **93.7%** signal for `Llama3-70B`, whereas a rigid standard using only MCF would report ~24% and ~94% (missing the small model's capability), and one using only CF would report ~31% and ~69% (severely underestimating the large model).

#### Empirical Validation of Normalization Schemes
The authors tested four normalization methods across all 15 models to determine the optimal scheme for each dataset. **Table 3** summarizes the "win percentage" (how often a method was best) and the "diff oracle" (difference between the OLMES choice and the theoretical best possible choice for that specific model).

*   **PMI Dominance for Complex Answers:** For `ARC-CHALLENGE`, `CommonsenseQA`, and `OpenBookQA`, **PMI normalization** won **66.7%**, **53.3%**, and **100%** of the time, respectively. This supports the hypothesis that answers with varying semantic priors (e.g., "Whirlpool bath" vs. "Bathtub") require PMI to cancel out length and frequency bias.
*   **Character Normalization for Standard Tasks:** For `ARC-EASY`, `HellaSwag`, `PIQA`, `Social IQa`, and `MMLU`, **character normalization** was the dominant winner (e.g., **100%** win rate on `HellaSwag`, **86.7%** on `ARC-EASY`). It offers a computationally cheaper alternative to PMI with comparable accuracy for these datasets.
*   **No Normalization for Binary/Identical Tasks:** For `BoolQ` (Yes/No) and `WinoGrande` (identical continuations), **no normalization** was optimal (**46.7%** and **100%** win rates). The paper notes that applying character normalization to `BoolQ` can accidentally penalize "yes" (3 chars) vs "no" (2 chars), introducing artificial bias.

Crucially, the **"diff oracle"** column in **Table 3** shows that the difference between the OLMES-prescribed method and the perfect, model-specific oracle method is negligible, typically **&lt; 0.5%** and never exceeding **1.1%** (on `BoolQ`). This validates the use of a fixed, task-specific rule rather than requiring users to tune normalization per model.

#### Reproducible Leaderboard Results
**Table 4** presents the final OLMES scores for the 15 models. The results demonstrate a smooth, monotonic increase in performance with model size and architectural improvements, validating the standard's ability to rank models meaningfully:
*   **Scaling Law Verification:** `Pythia-1B` averages **49.0%**, while `Pythia-6.7B` averages **59.1%**. `Llama2-7B` averages **69.0%**, improving to **74.0%** for `Llama2-13B`.
*   **Architectural Gains:** `Llama3-8B` (**78.7%** average) significantly outperforms `Llama2-7B` (**69.0%**), and `Llama3-70B` reaches **88.4%**.
*   **Specific Task Breakdown:** On `MMLU`, scores range from **31.1%** (`Pythia-1B`) to **79.8%** (`Llama3-70B`). On `ARC-CHALLENGE`, the range is **31.4%** to **93.7%**.

These numbers resolve the inconsistencies shown in **Table 1**, where `Llama2-13B` was previously reported anywhere from **48.8%** to **67.6%** on `ARC-CHALLENGE` depending on the reference. Under OLMES, it has a single, reproducible score of **67.3%** (using MCF).

### 5.3 Robustness Checks and Ablation Studies

To ensure OLMES is not fragile to minor implementation details, the authors conducted robustness checks detailed in **Appendix A (Table 5)**. They evaluated five models (`Pythia-1B` to `Llama3-8B`) under three variant conditions:
1.  **Variant 1 (Prompt Wording):** Changing label separators (`.` to `)`), adding newlines, and changing "Answer" to "Correct answer".
2.  **Variant 2 (Few-Shot Examples):** Replacing 3 of the 5 curated examples with new, diverse examples.
3.  **Variant 3 (Combined):** Applying both changes.

**Results:**
*   **Stability:** The difference between the original OLMES score and the average of the variants (`diff` column in Table 5) is consistently **&lt; 1.0%** for most tasks. For example, on `ARC-EASY`, the maximum difference observed was **0.8%** (for `Gemma2-2B`). On `PIQA`, the difference was **0.0%** for `Llama2-7B` and `Gemma2-2B`.
*   **Statistical Significance:** The reported standard errors (`std err`) are generally around **1.2% – 2.2%**. The variations introduced by these minor changes fall well within the statistical noise of the evaluation itself.
*   **Conclusion:** This confirms that while the *exact* prompt matters for reproducibility (hence the strict standard), the *general principles* of OLMES are robust. A model that performs well under OLMES will not suddenly fail due to a minor formatting tweak, provided the tweak remains within natural language conventions.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

The experiments convincingly support the paper's central claims:

1.  **Claim:** *Current evaluation practices are inconsistent and lead to contradictory conclusions.*
    *   **Support:** **Table 1** and **Table 14** provide irrefutable evidence, showing >10% variance in reported scores for the same model across different papers/leaderboards. The OLMES results in **Table 4** collapse this variance into a single, justified number.

2.  **Claim:** *No single task formulation (MCF or CF) works for all models.*
    *   **Support:** **Figure 2** and **Tables 6/7** clearly visualize the "cross-over" dynamic. The data shows that relying solely on MCF would discard valid signals from 1B–3B models, while relying solely on CF would cap the measured potential of 70B models at ~70% accuracy instead of ~94%. The `max()` strategy is empirically necessary.

3.  **Claim:** *Normalization choices must be task-specific, not model-specific.*
    *   **Support:** **Table 3** demonstrates that while the *best* normalization varies by task (PMI for ARC, Char for HellaSwag, None for BoolQ), it does *not* vary significantly by model within a task. The low "diff oracle" scores prove that a static, task-level prescription is sufficient and practical.

**Limitations and Trade-offs:**
*   **Computational Overhead:** The requirement to run **both** MCF and CF doubles the inference cost per task compared to a single-formulation standard. Additionally, using **PMI normalization** requires an extra forward pass for the unconditional probability $P(a_i|u)$, further increasing compute for tasks like `ARC-CHALLENGE`. The authors acknowledge this but argue it is a necessary trade-off for accuracy.
*   **Scope:** The experiments are limited to **Multiple-Choice QA**. While the principles (formatting, few-shot curation) likely generalize, the specific findings on normalization and formulation do not directly apply to generative tasks (e.g., summarization, code generation) or Chain-of-Thought prompting, which the authors note as future work.
*   **Base Models Only:** The study focuses exclusively on base models. While this is the intended scope (evaluating pre-training progress), the behavior of instruction-tuned models under these specific formulations (especially the "cloze" format which might conflict with chat templates) is not explicitly tested, though the authors suggest CoT is less effective on base models.

In summary, the experimental analysis is rigorous and data-driven. It does not merely propose a standard; it stress-tests every component of that standard against a diverse model zoo, providing quantitative proof that the proposed choices (dynamic formulation, task-specific normalization, curated shots) are superior to the arbitrary conventions currently plaguing the field.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While OLMES provides a robust, empirically validated framework for standardizing base model evaluations, it is not a universal solution for all AI assessment needs. The standard makes specific design choices that prioritize reproducibility and signal clarity for pre-training checkpoints, inevitably introducing trade-offs in computational cost, scope, and applicability to advanced model types. Understanding these limitations is crucial for researchers deciding when to adopt OLMES and when to supplement it with other evaluation strategies.

### 6.1 Computational Overhead and Efficiency Trade-offs
The most immediate practical constraint of OLMES is its increased computational cost compared to single-configuration evaluations. This overhead arises from two primary design decisions intended to maximize accuracy:

*   **Dual-Formulation Inference:** To bridge the capability gap between small and large models, OLMES mandates running **both** Multiple-Choice Formulation (MCF) and Cloze Formulation (CF) for every task and reporting the maximum score ($S = \max(Acc_{MCF}, Acc_{CF})$).
    *   **Impact:** This effectively **doubles** the inference compute required per task compared to standards that commit to a single formulation. For a large-scale evaluation suite like MMLU (14,042 instances) or even the sampled 1,000-instance subsets, this represents a significant increase in GPU hours.
    *   **Justification vs. Cost:** The paper argues this is necessary because relying on only one formulation yields misleading results (near-random for weak models on MCF, saturated/low-ceiling for strong models on CF). However, for teams with extremely limited compute resources, this requirement may be prohibitive, forcing them to choose a single formulation and accept the associated blind spots.

*   **PMI Normalization Overhead:** For specific datasets (`ARC-CHALLENGE`, `CommonsenseQA`, `OpenBookQA`), OLMES prescribes **Pointwise Mutual Information (PMI)** normalization.
    *   **Mechanism:** PMI requires calculating the unconditional probability of the answer string, $P(a_i|u)$, typically by running a forward pass with a generic prompt like `"Answer: <answer>"`.
    *   **Impact:** This adds an **extra forward pass** for every answer choice in the affected tasks, further increasing latency and cost beyond the dual-formulation baseline.
    *   **Trade-off:** The authors explicitly acknowledge this cost but deem it essential for tasks where answer lengths and priors vary wildly (e.g., "Whirlpool bath" vs. "Bathtub"). They avoid PMI for other tasks (using cheaper character normalization) specifically to mitigate this burden, highlighting a conscious balance between statistical rigor and practical feasibility.

### 6.2 Scope Constraints: The Multiple-Choice Boundary
OLMES is explicitly scoped to **Multiple-Choice Question Answering (MCQA)** tasks. While this covers a vast portion of current benchmarking practices (science, commonsense, factual knowledge), it leaves significant gaps in evaluating modern LLM capabilities:

*   **Exclusion of Generative Tasks:** The standard does not address open-ended generative tasks such as summarization, creative writing, code generation, or mathematical proof derivation.
    *   **Missing Mechanisms:** Evaluating these tasks requires different metrics (e.g., ROUGE, BLEU, pass@k, or LLM-as-a-Judge) and different standardization challenges (e.g., how to extract an answer from a long chain of thought, how to handle varying output lengths). The prompt formatting and normalization rules defined in OLMES (Sections 3.1 and 3.3) are specific to selecting among fixed options and do not directly translate to free-form generation.
*   **Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting:** The paper notes that OLMES focuses on **base models**, for which Chain-of-Thought prompting is often ineffective or detrimental compared to instruction-tuned models.
    *   **Limitation:** Consequently, OLMES does not provide a standard for evaluating CoT reasoning. As the field moves towards models that rely heavily on intermediate reasoning steps to solve complex problems (like GSM8K or MATH), the "direct answer" approach of OLMES may underestimate the capabilities of models designed to reason step-by-step. The authors list extending OLMES principles to CoT as a key direction for future work.
*   **Multimodal Evaluation:** The current framework is text-only. It does not account for vision-language models (VLMs) or other multimodal architectures, which require standardized protocols for image encoding, cross-modal attention, and joint reasoning.

### 6.3 Assumptions About Model Behavior and Tokenization
The effectiveness of OLMES relies on several assumptions about the models being evaluated, which may not hold for all architectures or training regimes:

*   **Tokenizer Sensitivity:** A critical component of the OLMES prompt formatting (Section 3.1) is the addition of a **prefix space** before answer labels (e.g., `\n A.`) to ensure token alignment.
    *   **Assumption:** This assumes that the model's tokenizer distinguishes between a leading space and no leading space (which is true for byte-level BPE tokenizers like those used in Llama, GPT, and OLMo).
    *   **Edge Case:** For models using tokenization schemes that do not exhibit this sensitivity, or for models with highly idiosyncratic tokenizers that split common words unexpectedly, this specific fix might be unnecessary or insufficient. The standard assumes a "reasonable" tokenizer behavior common in current open-weight models.
*   **Base Model Focus:** OLMES is optimized for **base (pre-trained)** models.
    *   **Limitation:** While the principles of reproducible formatting apply broadly, the specific findings on formulation (MCF vs. CF) are derived from base model dynamics. Instruction-tuned models, which are aligned to follow prompts and often trained to reject "cloze" style completions in favor of structured responses, might behave differently. For instance, an instruction-tuned model might perform poorly on the raw CF formulation if it has been fine-tuned to always output a specific header or format, potentially making the `max(MCF, CF)` logic less effective or requiring adaptation for chat models.
*   **Robustness to Adversarial Perturbations:** The robustness checks in Appendix A (Table 5) demonstrate stability against *minor*, natural variations in prompt wording and few-shot selection.
    *   **Weakness:** The paper explicitly states it does **not** evaluate robustness against **adversarial setups**, such as using rare symbols as labels, intentionally skewed few-shot distributions, or unnatural prompt structures. A model that scores highly under OLMES is not guaranteed to be robust to these adversarial conditions. The standard measures "capability under best practices," not "robustness under attack."

### 6.4 Static Defaults vs. Dynamic Evolution
OLMES resolves current ambiguities by fixing defaults (e.g., 5-shot, specific normalization per task). However, this static nature presents a long-term challenge:

*   **Future-Proofing:** As models become significantly more capable (e.g., surpassing human performance on MCQA), the signal from current benchmarks may saturate entirely, rendering the specific choice between MCF and CF irrelevant if both hit 100%.
    *   **Open Question:** The standard does not prescribe *when* to retire a benchmark or how to dynamically adjust the difficulty scaling. While the principles (documentation, curation) remain valid, the specific hyperparameters (like the 5-shot limit) might need re-evaluation as context windows expand and models become more data-efficient.
*   **The "Oracle" Gap:** Although the "diff oracle" in Table 3 is small (&lt;1%), it is not zero. By enforcing a single normalization scheme per task, OLMES accepts a tiny amount of sub-optimality for specific model-task pairs to gain global consistency.
    *   **Trade-off:** This is a deliberate engineering trade-off: sacrificing the theoretical maximum score for a specific model to ensure that scores are comparable across *all* models. Researchers seeking the absolute peak performance metric for a single model might still find value in running full ablations, though OLMES argues this is inefficient for community-wide comparison.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

| Feature | Benefit | Trade-off / Limitation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Dual Formulation (MCF + CF)** | Captures signal from both weak (1B) and strong (70B) models; prevents false negatives. | **2x Inference Cost:** Doubles compute requirements per task. |
| **PMI Normalization** | Corrects for length/prior bias in complex answer spaces (e.g., ARC). | **Extra Forward Pass:** Requires computing unconditional probabilities, increasing latency. |
| **Fixed 5-Shot Curation** | Eliminates variance from random sampling; ensures label balance. | **Static Difficulty:** May not adapt to models that learn effectively from 0-shot or require >5 shots for complex reasoning. |
| **MCQA Focus** | Standardizes the most common benchmark type; high reproducibility. | **Narrow Scope:** Excludes generative, coding, math (CoT), and multimodal tasks. |
| **Base Model Optimization** | Ideal for tracking pre-training progress and checkpoint selection. | **Untuned for Chat:** May not reflect optimal evaluation strategies for instruction-tuned/RLHF models. |

In conclusion, OLMES is a highly specialized tool designed to solve the specific crisis of reproducibility in **base model MCQA evaluation**. It trades computational efficiency and broad task coverage for rigorous standardization and cross-model comparability. Researchers should adopt it as the gold standard for pre-training benchmarks while recognizing the need for complementary frameworks to evaluate generative capabilities, reasoning chains, and multimodal understanding.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of OLMES represents a paradigm shift from treating evaluation as a configurable experiment to treating it as a **standardized measurement protocol**. By resolving the ambiguity that has plagued LLM benchmarking, this work fundamentally alters how the community validates progress, selects models, and designs future research.

### 7.1 Transforming the Landscape: From "Configurable" to "Calibrated"
The most immediate impact of OLMES is the restoration of **scientific comparability** across the field. Prior to this standard, a claim like "Model X outperforms Model Y on ARC-CHALLENGE" was often unverifiable because the underlying evaluation setups (shots, normalization, formulation) differed between the two models' reports.
*   **Unifying the Fragmented Leaderboard:** OLMES collapses the variance seen in **Table 1**, where `Llama2-13B` scores ranged from **48.8%** to **67.6%** depending on the reference. By enforcing a single, justified protocol, OLMES ensures that future leaderboards and model cards report a single, reproducible number. This eliminates "benchmark cherry-picking," where developers might inadvertently (or intentionally) select evaluation settings that maximize their model's specific strengths.
*   **Democratizing Model Development:** By providing a documented standard that works for both **1B parameter** early-training checkpoints and **70B parameter** final models, OLMES lowers the barrier for rigorous evaluation. Small research teams no longer need to conduct expensive ablation studies on normalization or few-shot strategies to validate their models; they can adopt the OLMES defaults with confidence, knowing these choices are empirically optimized across a diverse model zoo.
*   **Shifting the Burden of Proof:** Previously, the burden was on the reader to decipher if two scores were comparable. With OLMES, the burden shifts to the evaluator to justify any *deviation* from the standard. If a paper reports a score using a non-OLMES setup (e.g., 0-shot random sampling with token normalization), the community can immediately flag it as non-comparable, raising the bar for scientific rigor.

### 7.2 Enabling New Research Avenues
OLMES does not merely stabilize current practices; it opens specific avenues for follow-up research that were previously obscured by noise:

*   **Fine-Grained Training Dynamics Analysis:** Because OLMES validates the use of **Cloze Formulation (CF)** for weak models and **Multiple-Choice Formulation (MCF)** for strong ones, researchers can now track the exact moment a model acquires "task understanding." As shown in **Figure 1**, the crossover point (where MCF surpasses CF) is a measurable metric of model maturity. Future work can investigate what architectural changes or data mixture shifts cause this crossover to happen earlier in training, providing a new objective for optimizing pre-training efficiency.
*   **Robustness vs. Capability Decoupling:** The robustness checks in **Appendix A (Table 5)** show that OLMES scores are stable under minor perturbations. This allows researchers to decouple **capability** (can the model solve the problem?) from **fragility** (does the model fail if the prompt changes slightly?). Future studies can use the OLMES baseline as a control to specifically measure adversarial robustness, knowing that the baseline capability signal is pure and unconfounded by formatting artifacts.
*   **Normalization as a Diagnostic Tool:** The finding that **PMI normalization** is critical for some tasks (ARC, OpenBookQA) but harmful or unnecessary for others (BoolQ) suggests that answer space semantics are a key variable in evaluation. Future research could explore using the *difference* between normalized and unnormalized scores as a diagnostic metric for model bias toward short answers or high-frequency phrases, turning a correction mechanism into an analytical tool.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases
For practitioners and industry developers, OLMES offers immediate utility in several critical workflows:

*   **Checkpoint Selection During Pre-training:** Training large models is computationally expensive. Developers often need to decide whether to continue training or stop early based on validation metrics. OLMES provides a reliable signal across all stages: using CF scores to monitor early progress (when MCF is random) and switching to MCF scores as the model matures. This prevents premature termination of promising models that haven't yet learned the MCF format.
*   **Model Selection for Downstream Tasks:** When choosing a base model for fine-tuning on a specific application (e.g., medical QA or legal reasoning), practitioners can rely on OLMES scores to compare models of different sizes and architectures fairly. The **macro-averaged MMLU** score prescribed by OLMES ensures that a model isn't selected just because it performs well on broad topics but fails on niche, critical domains.
*   **Integration into Evaluation Harnesses:** The paper explicitly designs OLMES to be compatible with existing infrastructure like the **Eleuther LM Evaluation Harness** and **HELM**. The practical application here is the release of OLMES as a predefined configuration within these tools. Instead of users manually setting `num_few_shot=5`, `normalization=char`, and `formulation=max(mcf, cf)`, they can invoke a single `olmes` flag, instantly standardizing their entire evaluation pipeline.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance
Adopting OLMES requires a shift in mindset from "tuning for max score" to "adhering to protocol." Here is when and how to prefer OLMES over alternative approaches:

*   **When to Use OLMES:**
    *   **Base Model Evaluation:** Always use OLMES when evaluating pretrained base models, especially when comparing models of varying sizes (e.g., 1B vs. 7B vs. 70B). The dynamic MCF/CF selection is essential here.
    *   **Paper Claims & Leaderboards:** Use OLMES when publishing performance claims to ensure they are reproducible and comparable to prior work. Citing "OLMES-compliant evaluation" serves as a shorthand for full methodological transparency.
    *   **Resource-Constrained Settings:** While OLMES doubles inference cost (MCF+CF), it eliminates the need for users to run their own ablation studies on shots and normalization. The fixed **5-shot curated** sets and **1,000-instance cap** make it more efficient overall than running extensive hyperparameter searches.

*   **When to Supplement or Deviate:**
    *   **Instruction-Tuned Models:** OLMES is optimized for base models. For instruction-tuned or chat models, the "cloze" formulation may conflict with the model's alignment training (which often expects specific chat templates). In these cases, adopt the OLMES formatting and few-shot curation principles but rely primarily on MCF or task-specific chat prompts.
    *   **Generative & Reasoning Tasks:** For tasks requiring Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning, code generation, or open-ended summarization, OLMES's MCQA-specific rules (like PMI normalization) do not apply. However, the **principles** of curated few-shot examples and strict prompt formatting should still be adopted to maintain consistency.
    *   **Adversarial Robustness Testing:** If the goal is to test model fragility, do *not* use the standard OLMES setup alone. Instead, use OLMES as the baseline and intentionally introduce the variations (random shots, altered labels) that OLMES guards against to measure the delta in performance.

In summary, OLMES transforms evaluation from a source of confusion into a foundational pillar of AI science. By providing a "calibrated scale" for LLMs, it enables the community to measure progress with precision, ensuring that the next generation of models is built on a foundation of reproducible, comparable, and scientifically sound evidence.
