## 1. Executive Summary
This paper addresses the quadratic $O(n^2)$ memory and time complexity bottleneck of standard self-attention in Transformers by proposing `Nyströmformer`, which adapts the Nyström method to approximate the softmax matrix with linear $O(n)$ complexity using landmark points. The model achieves performance comparable to BERT-base on standard benchmarks like GLUE and IMDB, while outperforming efficient attention baselines (Reformer, Linformer, Performer) by ~3.4% average accuracy on the Long Range Arena (LRA) benchmark. This scalability enables processing sequences with thousands of tokens, demonstrating up to 3x speed-up and 1.2x memory savings over Longformer at sequence length 8192.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of this paper, we must first establish why the standard Transformer architecture struggles with scale, and why existing solutions leave a gap that `Nyströmformer` aims to fill. This section breaks down the computational bottleneck of self-attention, the real-world implications of this limitation, and the specific mathematical hurdles that prior efficient attention mechanisms have encountered.

### The Quadratic Bottleneck of Self-Attention

The core engine of a Transformer model is the **self-attention mechanism**. In standard self-attention, the model computes interactions between every pair of tokens in an input sequence to determine how much "attention" each token should pay to every other token.

Mathematically, given an input sequence of $n$ tokens, the mechanism computes Query ($Q$), Key ($K$), and Value ($V$) matrices. The attention output is calculated as:

$$
\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V
$$

Here, the term $QK^T$ represents the similarity scores between all pairs of tokens. Since $Q$ has shape $n \times d_k$ and $K^T$ has shape $d_k \times n$, their product results in an $n \times n$ matrix. This leads to two critical constraints highlighted in the **Introduction**:

*   **Time Complexity:** Computing the $n \times n$ similarity matrix requires $O(n^2)$ operations.
*   **Memory Complexity:** Storing this $n \times n$ matrix requires $O(n^2)$ memory.

While manageable for short sentences (e.g., $n=512$), this quadratic growth becomes prohibitive as sequence length increases. For example, doubling the sequence length from 512 to 1024 quadruples the memory and compute requirements. The **Introduction** notes that training a `BERT-large` model under these constraints requires approximately **4 months using a single Tesla V100 GPU**. Furthermore, sequences longer than $n=2048$ become "prohibitively expensive" to train using standard self-attention.

### Why This Problem Matters

The limitation on sequence length is not merely a computational inconvenience; it restricts the types of problems Transformers can solve effectively.

1.  **Long-Range Dependencies:** Many tasks require understanding context over long distances. In document classification or question answering, relevant information might be separated by thousands of tokens. Standard Transformers must truncate or chunk these inputs, potentially losing critical context.
2.  **Beyond NLP:** The **Conclusion** emphasizes that scaling to longer sequences is desirable in **computer vision** as well, where images can be treated as sequences of patches. High-resolution images result in very large $n$, exacerbating the quadratic bottleneck.
3.  **Resource Efficiency:** Reducing complexity allows for larger models or faster training on existing hardware, democratizing access to state-of-the-art models without requiring massive compute clusters.

### Landscape of Prior Approaches

Researchers have attempted to mitigate this bottleneck through two main strategies: **model compression** and **algorithmic complexity reduction**.

#### 1. Model Compression Strategies
These methods aim to make the existing $O(n^2)$ architecture lighter without changing the fundamental attention mechanism. The **Related Work** section lists several techniques:
*   **Weight Pruning & Quantization:** Reducing the precision or number of parameters (e.g., `Q8BERT`).
*   **Knowledge Distillation:** Training smaller "student" models to mimic larger "teacher" models (e.g., `DistilBERT`).
*   **Factorization:** Decomposing weight matrices to reduce parameters (e.g., `ALBERT`).

While these reduce memory footprint and inference time, they do not change the underlying $O(n^2)$ complexity with respect to sequence length $n$. They still struggle when $n$ becomes very large.

#### 2. Algorithmic Complexity Reduction
To truly handle long sequences, the attention mechanism itself must be modified to scale better than $O(n^2)$. The **Related Work** section categorizes existing efficient attention methods by their complexity:

*   **Sparse Attention ($O(n\sqrt{n})$ or $O(n \log n)$):**
    *   **Sparse Transformers** (Child et al. 2019) factorize the attention matrix to achieve $O(n\sqrt{n})$.
    *   **Reformer** (Kitaev et al. 2019) uses Locality-Sensitive Hashing (LSH) to group similar tokens, reducing complexity to $O(n \log n)$.
    *   *Limitation:* While better than quadratic, these are still not linear, and hashing mechanisms can introduce overhead or approximation errors.

*   **Linear Attention ($O(n)$):**
    *   **Linformer** (Wang et al. 2020) projects the keys and values into a lower-dimensional space using random projections.
    *   **Longformer** (Beltagy et al. 2020) combines local windowed attention with global attention tokens.
    *   **Performer** (Choromanski et al. 2020) uses positive orthogonal random features to approximate the softmax kernel.
    *   **Linearized Softmax** (Katharopoulos et al. 2020) attempts to separate keys and queries in a linear dot product approximation.
    *   *Limitation:* The **Related Work** section notes that linearizing the softmax attention based on heuristically separating keys and queries can lead to **large approximation errors** in some cases. Additionally, some methods rely on specific hardware optimizations or complex sampling strategies that are difficult to stabilize during training.

### The Specific Gap: Applying Nyström to Softmax

The `Nyströmformer` is inspired by the **Nyström method**, a classical technique in numerical analysis for approximating large matrices using a subset of columns and rows (landmarks). Typically, if you have a large matrix $S$, you can approximate it by sampling $m$ columns/rows (where $m \ll n$) and reconstructing the full matrix.

However, the **Introduction** and **Section 3 (Nyström-Based Linear Transformers)** identify a critical barrier that prevented direct application of this method to Transformers prior to this work: **The Softmax Normalization Problem.**

To understand this, consider the softmax matrix $S$:
$$
S = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)
$$
The softmax function is row-wise normalization. To compute even a *single element* of $S$, you must compute the exponential of the similarity scores for that row against **all** other keys, and then divide by the sum of all those exponentials.

*   **The Catch:** You cannot compute a subset of columns of $S$ (which is required for Nyström approximation) without first computing the full $n \times n$ matrix $QK^T$ to perform the normalization.
*   **The Consequence:** As illustrated in **Figure 1** of the paper, directly applying Nyström approximation requires accessing the full $QK^T$, which incurs the original $O(n^2)$ cost. This negates any efficiency gain.

### Positioning of Nyströmformer

This paper positions itself as a solution that retains the theoretical benefits of the Nyström method while bypassing the softmax normalization bottleneck.

*   **相对于 Linformer/Performer:** Like these methods, `Nyströmformer` targets **$O(n)$ complexity**. However, instead of random projections or orthogonal features, it leverages landmark points to reconstruct the attention matrix. The **Introduction** claims this yields a "good approximation of the true self-attention" without the large approximation errors sometimes seen in linearized softmax heuristics.
*   **相对于 Longformer:** While `Longformer` achieves $O(n)$ via fixed local windows, `Nyströmformer` aims to capture global interactions more effectively through its landmark-based reconstruction, which is not restricted to local neighborhoods.
*   **Methodological Innovation:** The key distinction is **when** the landmarks are selected. Instead of sampling the matrix *after* softmax (which is too expensive), `Nyströmformer` selects landmarks from the Query and Key vectors *before* the softmax operation. This allows the model to construct the necessary approximation matrices without ever materializing the full $n \times n$ similarity matrix.

In summary, the motivation stems from a need for true linear scalability that does not sacrifice the global receptive field of standard attention. `Nyströmformer` addresses the specific mathematical impasse that previously made Nyström methods incompatible with softmax-based attention, positioning it as a competitive alternative in the landscape of efficient Transformers.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
This system is a modified self-attention module designed to replace the standard attention mechanism within a Transformer architecture. It solves the quadratic memory and time cost problem by mathematically reconstructing the attention matrix using a small set of representative "landmark" tokens, reducing the complexity to linear scale without computing interactions between every single token pair.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The data flows through four primary stages within the attention module. First, the input Query ($Q$) and Key ($K$) matrices are compressed into smaller landmark matrices ($\tilde{Q}$ and $\tilde{K}$) using a segmentation strategy. Second, a small $m \times m$ attention matrix is computed solely between these landmarks and inverted using an iterative method. Third, the full sequence interacts only with these landmarks (creating $n \times m$ and $m \times n$ matrices) rather than with each other. Finally, these three components are multiplied together along with the Value ($V$) matrix, and a skip connection adds the original values back to stabilize training.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown
This paper is a methodological contribution that adapts numerical linear algebra techniques to deep learning, specifically modifying the Nyström method to approximate the softmax matrix in self-attention. The core idea is to avoid computing the full $n \times n$ attention matrix by reconstructing it from interactions between the full sequence and a small set of $m$ landmark points, where $m \ll n$.

#### The Mathematical Derivation: From Standard to Modified Nyström
To understand the innovation, we must first look at the standard Nyström method as described in **Section 3 (Nyström-Based Linear Transformers)**. The standard approach assumes we have a large kernel matrix $S \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ (in our case, the softmax attention matrix). The method approximates $S$ by sampling $m$ columns and rows to form a sub-matrix $A_S \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$. Using the quadrature technique, the full matrix $\hat{S}$ is reconstructed as:

$$
\hat{S} = \begin{bmatrix} A_S \\ F_S \end{bmatrix} A_S^+ \begin{bmatrix} A_S & B_S \end{bmatrix}
$$

Here, $A_S^+$ denotes the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse of the sample matrix $A_S$. **Equation 4** in the paper formalizes this reconstruction. The intuition is that if the sampled landmarks are representative, the relationship between the landmarks ($A_S$) can predict the relationship between the landmarks and the rest of the data ($F_S, B_S$).

However, applying this directly to Transformers hits the barrier identified in **Figure 1**: computing even the sub-matrix $A_S$ requires the softmax function. The softmax function normalizes each row by the sum of exponentials of *all* elements in that row. Therefore, you cannot compute a subset of the softmax matrix without first computing the full $n \times n$ similarity matrix $QK^T$, which defeats the purpose of efficiency.

The authors solve this by shifting *when* the sampling occurs. Instead of sampling the matrix *after* the softmax operation, **Nyströmformer** samples the Query and Key vectors *before* the softmax operation. They define landmark matrices $\tilde{Q} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times d_q}$ and $\tilde{K} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times d_q}$ derived from the original $Q$ and $K$. The small landmark attention matrix $A_S$ is then computed as:

$$
A_S = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{\tilde{Q}\tilde{K}^T}{\sqrt{d_q}}\right)
$$

Because $\tilde{Q}$ and $\tilde{K}$ have size $m$, computing $A_S$ costs only $O(m^2)$, which is constant relative to the sequence length $n$. The full attention matrix $\hat{S}$ is then approximated using **Equation 13**:

$$
\hat{S} = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{Q\tilde{K}^T}{\sqrt{d_q}}\right) \left(\text{softmax}\left(\frac{\tilde{Q}\tilde{K}^T}{\sqrt{d_q}}\right)\right)^+ \text{softmax}\left(\frac{\tilde{Q}K^T}{\sqrt{d_q}}\right)
$$

This equation decomposes the $n \times n$ operation into three parts: an $n \times m$ matrix, an $m \times m$ inverted matrix, and an $m \times n$ matrix. This structure ensures that no operation ever scales quadratically with $n$.

#### Landmark Selection Strategy
A critical design choice is how to select the landmark points $\tilde{Q}$ and $\tilde{K}$. While prior Nyström literature often uses K-means clustering to find representative points, the authors note in the **Landmarks selection** subsection that K-means requires Expectation-Maximization (EM) style updates. These updates are computationally heavy and less desirable during mini-batch training in deep learning pipelines.

Instead, the paper proposes **Segment-means**, a deterministic pooling strategy similar to local average pooling. The input sequence of length $n$ is divided into $m$ equal segments. For each segment, the landmark vector is calculated as the mean of the tokens within that segment. **Equation 16** defines this process formally. If we let $l = n/m$ be the segment length, the $j$-th landmark $\tilde{q}_j$ is:

$$
\tilde{q}_j = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=(j-1)\times l + 1}^{(j-1)\times l + m} q_i
$$

*(Note: There is a slight notation ambiguity in the paper's Eq 16 summation bounds regarding $m$ vs $l$, but the text clarifies it as averaging over the segment of length $l=n/m$.)* This approach requires only a single scan of the sequence, resulting in $O(n)$ complexity for landmark generation. The authors mention in the **Landmarks selection** subsection that using $m=64$ landmarks is often sufficient to ensure a good approximation, though this depends on the specific application.

#### Efficient Moore-Penrose Pseudoinverse
The term $A_S^+$ in **Equation 13** requires computing the pseudoinverse of the landmark attention matrix. The standard way to compute a pseudoinverse is via Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). However, the **Moore-Penrose inverse computation** subsection states that SVD is not very efficient on GPUs.

To accelerate this, the authors adopt an iterative method from **Razavi et al. 2014**. This method approximates the inverse using only matrix-matrix multiplications, which are highly optimized on modern hardware. The sequence $\{Z_j\}$ converges to $A_S^+$ using the update rule in **Equation 14**:

$$
Z_{j+1} = \frac{1}{4}Z_j(13I - A_S Z_j(15I - A_S Z_j(7I - A_S Z_j)))
$$

The initialization $Z_0$ is chosen carefully to ensure convergence, specifically $Z_0 = A_S^T / (\|A_S\|_1 \|A_S\|_\infty)$. The authors report in the **Moore-Penrose inverse computation** subsection that for all their experiments, they need to run about **6 iterations** to achieve a good approximation. This iterative approach makes the gradient computation straightforward since it relies only on differentiable matrix operations.

#### The Full Algorithm Pipeline
**Algorithm 1** summarizes the complete forward pass for the Nyström approximation of the softmax matrix. The pipeline executes in the following strict order:
1.  **Compute Landmarks:** Calculate $\tilde{Q}$ and $\tilde{K}$ from inputs $Q$ and $K$ using Segment-means.
2.  **Compute Cross-Attention Terms:** Calculate $\tilde{F} = \text{softmax}(Q\tilde{K}^T / \sqrt{d_q})$ and $\tilde{B} = \text{softmax}(\tilde{Q}K^T / \sqrt{d_q})$. These represent the attention between the full sequence and the landmarks.
3.  **Compute Landmark Inverse:** Calculate $\tilde{A} = \text{softmax}(\tilde{Q}\tilde{K}^T / \sqrt{d_q})^+$ using the iterative method described above.
4.  **Reconstruct:** Return the product $\tilde{F} \times \tilde{A} \times \tilde{B}$.

Once this approximate softmax matrix $\hat{S}$ is obtained, it is applied to the Value matrix $V$ to produce the final attention output. **Equation 17** defines the final linearized self-attention output $\hat{S}V$:

$$
\hat{S}V = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{Q\tilde{K}^T}{\sqrt{d_q}}\right) Z^\star \text{softmax}\left(\frac{\tilde{Q}K^T}{\sqrt{d_q}}\right) V
$$

Here, $Z^\star$ represents the approximated pseudoinverse after 6 iterations. **Figure 3** visually confirms the fidelity of this approximation, showing that the attention patterns generated by **Equation 17** (bottom) closely match the ground-truth self-attention computed by **Equation 2** (top).

#### Model Architecture and Stability Mechanisms
The full model architecture is depicted in **Figure 4**. Beyond the mathematical approximation, the authors introduce structural components to ensure stable training. A skip connection is added in parallel to the approximation module. Specifically, the input Value $V$ is passed through a 1D depthwise convolution (labeled `DConv` in **Figure 4**) and added to the output of the Nyström approximation. This helps preserve information flow and mitigates potential errors introduced by the approximation.

The architecture maintains the standard Transformer block structure but swaps the standard attention head with this Nyström-based module. The **Our Model: Nyströmformer** section clarifies that this replacement happens within the standard BERT architecture (either BERT-small or BERT-base configurations).

#### Complexity Analysis
The primary claim of the paper is that this approach achieves $O(n)$ complexity. The **Complexity analysis** subsection breaks down the costs explicitly:
*   **Landmark Selection:** Using Segment-means takes $O(n)$ time.
*   **Pseudoinverse:** The iterative approximation takes $O(m^3)$ in the worst case, but since $m$ is a constant hyperparameter (e.g., 64), this is effectively $O(1)$ with respect to $n$.
*   **Matrix Multiplications:** The core operations involve multiplying an $n \times m$ matrix by an $m \times m$ matrix, and then by an $m \times n$ matrix. The cost is dominated by terms like $O(nm^2)$ and $O(nmd_v)$.

The total time complexity is summarized as $O(n + m^3 + nm^2 + mnd_v)$. Since $m \ll n$, the terms involving $n$ dominate linearly. Similarly, the memory footprint is $O(md_q + nm + m^2 + mn + nd_v)$. Storing the intermediate $n \times m$ matrices requires $O(nm)$ memory, which is linear in $n$, unlike the $O(n^2)$ memory required to store the full attention matrix. This confirms that when the number of landmarks $m$ is much smaller than the sequence length $n$, both time and memory scale linearly with the input sequence length.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The `Nyströmformer` is not merely an application of an existing numerical method to a new domain; it requires specific modifications to make the Nyström method compatible with the differentiable, GPU-based training of Transformers. Below, we analyze the four most critical innovations that distinguish this work from prior efficient attention mechanisms. These insights explain why `Nyströmformer` achieves linear complexity without the severe performance degradation often seen in other approximations.

### 1. Shifting Landmark Selection Before Softmax (Fundamental Innovation)
The most significant theoretical contribution is resolving the incompatibility between the Nyström method and the softmax function. As detailed in **Section 2**, standard Nyström approximation requires sampling rows and columns of the target matrix. However, in Transformers, the target matrix is $S = \text{softmax}(QK^T/\sqrt{d_q})$. Computing even a single row of $S$ requires normalization over all $n$ tokens, forcing an $O(n^2)$ computation before sampling can even begin.

`Nyströmformer` bypasses this catch-22 by shifting the landmark selection step to occur **before** the softmax operation. Instead of sampling the matrix $S$, the model samples the Query and Key vectors ($Q$ and $K$) to create landmarks $\tilde{Q}$ and $\tilde{K}$. This allows the construction of the small landmark matrix $A_S = \text{softmax}(\tilde{Q}\tilde{K}^T/\sqrt{d_q})$ directly, costing only $O(m^2)$.

*   **Why this differs from prior work:** Methods like `Linformer` project $K$ and $V$ into a lower dimension using learned linear projections, which can distort the attention distribution. `Performer` uses random feature maps to approximate the kernel. `Nyströmformer` uniquely reconstructs the attention matrix structure using landmark interactions defined in **Equation 13**.
*   **Significance:** This design preserves the structural properties of the attention matrix better than random projections. **Figure 3** provides visual evidence of this fidelity, showing that the attention patterns generated by the approximation (bottom) closely mirror the ground-truth self-attention (top). This structural fidelity is crucial for tasks requiring precise token alignment.

### 2. Segment-Means for Differentiable Landmark Generation (Practical Innovation)
Selecting representative landmarks is a classic problem in Nyström methods. Prior literature often suggests K-means clustering to find optimal landmarks. However, the authors identify in the **Landmarks selection** subsection that K-means relies on Expectation-Maximization (EM) updates, which are computationally expensive and difficult to differentiate through during mini-batch training.

The paper introduces **Segment-means** (**Equation 16**) as a deterministic alternative. The input sequence is divided into $m$ equal segments, and the landmark for each segment is simply the mean of the tokens within it.

*   **Why this differs from prior work:** Clustering-based approaches (e.g., clustered attention) often require iterative optimization steps that conflict with the stochastic gradient descent used in deep learning. Segment-means requires only a single scan of the sequence.
*   **Significance:** This choice ensures the entire attention module remains fully differentiable and efficient. The complexity for landmark generation is strictly $O(n)$, avoiding any hidden quadratic costs during the backward pass. The authors note in the **Landmarks selection** subsection that using $m=64$ landmarks is often sufficient, suggesting that fine-grained clustering is unnecessary for effective approximation in this context.

### 3. Iterative Pseudoinverse for GPU Efficiency (Systems Innovation)
A core component of the Nyström approximation is computing the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse $A_S^+$ of the landmark matrix. The standard mathematical approach is Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). However, the **Moore-Penrose inverse computation** subsection explicitly states that "SVD is not very efficient on GPUs."

To address this, the authors implement an iterative method from **Razavi et al. 2014** (**Equation 14**). This algorithm approximates the inverse using only matrix-matrix multiplications, which are highly optimized on modern tensor cores.

*   **Why this differs from prior work:** Many theoretical papers assume exact matrix inverses or SVD without considering the hardware reality of deep learning training. This implementation detail is critical for actual speedups.
*   **Significance:** This choice ensures that the $O(m^3)$ cost of the inverse does not become a bottleneck. The authors report in the **Moore-Penrose inverse computation** subsection that "for all our experiments, we need to run about 6 iterations" to achieve convergence. This fixed iteration count makes the operation predictable and stable, allowing gradients to flow through the inverse approximation without exploding or vanishing.

### 4. Superior Accuracy- Efficiency Trade-off on Long Sequences (Empirical Innovation)
Many efficient attention methods sacrifice accuracy for speed, particularly on tasks requiring long-range context. `Nyströmformer` demonstrates that linear complexity does not necessitate a performance drop.

*   **Evidence:** On the **Long Range Arena (LRA)** benchmark (**Table 3**), `Nyströmformer` achieves an average accuracy of **58.95%**. This outperforms `Reformer` (55.04%), `Linformer` (55.59%), and `Performer` (53.63%).
*   **Significance:** The margin of victory is substantial. For instance, on the `ListOps` task (which tests hierarchical reasoning), `Nyströmformer` scores **37.15%** compared to `Performer`'s **18.80%**. This suggests that the landmark-based reconstruction captures long-range dependencies more effectively than random feature approximations.
*   **Efficiency Validation:** **Table 1** confirms that these accuracy gains do not come at the cost of efficiency. At sequence length $n=8192$, `Nyströmformer-64` requires **12.3 ms** per instance, whereas `Longformer` requires **36.2 ms**. This represents a **~3× speed-up** while maintaining competitive memory usage (450 MB vs 455 MB).

In summary, `Nyströmformer` succeeds because it addresses the mathematical, algorithmic, and systems-level barriers simultaneously. It modifies the Nyström method to avoid softmax normalization costs, replaces clustering with segment pooling for trainability, swaps SVD for iterative multiplication for GPU speed, and validates these choices with superior empirical results on long-sequence tasks.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their claims about `Nyströmformer`. We will walk through the three-tier evaluation strategy: (1) pre-training performance on language modeling, (2) fine-tuning on standard NLP benchmarks, and (3) efficiency and accuracy on long-sequence tasks. Throughout, we will scrutinize whether the experimental design supports the paper's central thesis that linear-complexity attention can match standard self-attention while enabling longer sequences.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The authors employ a **transfer learning evaluation protocol**, which is standard for Transformer-based models. This consists of two stages:

1.  **Pre-training:** The model is trained on a large corpus using self-supervised objectives (masked language modeling and sentence order prediction). This tests whether the Nyström approximation can learn meaningful representations without task-specific labels.
2.  **Fine-tuning:** The pre-trained model is adapted to downstream tasks with supervised data. This tests generalization and practical utility.

Additionally, the authors evaluate on the **Long Range Arena (LRA)** benchmark, which specifically targets tasks requiring long-sequence understanding. This is critical because `Nyströmformer`'s primary value proposition is scalability to sequences with thousands of tokens.

#### Datasets and Metrics

The evaluation spans three categories of tasks, each with distinct characteristics:

| Category | Datasets | Sequence Length | Metrics |
|----------|----------|-----------------|---------|
| **Pre-training** | BookCorpus + English Wikipedia | 512 tokens | MLM accuracy, SOP accuracy |
| **Downstream NLP** | GLUE benchmark (SST-2, MRPC, QNLI, QQP, MNLI) + IMDB | 512 tokens (standard), 1024 tokens (extended) | Accuracy (most tasks), F1 score (MRPC, QQP) |
| **Long-Range** | LRA benchmark (ListOps, Text, Retrieval, Image, Pathfinder) | 1K–4K tokens | Classification accuracy |

The **Pre-training** section specifies that the BookCorpus and Wikipedia corpus is split into 80% training and 20% validation. The **Fine-tuning on Downstream NLP tasks** section notes that for larger datasets (SST-2, QNLI, QQP, MNLI, IMDB), the authors use a batch size of 32 with the AdamW optimizer at a learning rate of 3e-5, fine-tuning for 4 epochs. For the smaller MRPC dataset, they perform hyperparameter search over batch sizes [8, 16, 32] and learning rates [2e-5, 3e-5, 4e-5, 5e-5] to select the best validation result, following the protocol from Devlin et al. 2019.

#### Baselines

The authors compare `Nyströmformer` against multiple baselines depending on the evaluation category:

*   **Pre-training & Downstream NLP:** `BERT-small` (4 layers) and `BERT-base` (12 layers). The **Baselines** subsection clarifies that `Nyströmformer` replaces the self-attention in these architectures with the proposed Nyström approximation.
*   **Efficiency Comparison:** `Linformer` (Wang et al. 2020) with projection dimension 256, and `Longformer` (Beltagy et al. 2020) with sliding window size 257.
*   **Long-Range Tasks:** Standard Transformer (Vaswani et al. 2017), `Reformer` (Kitaev et al. 2019), `Linformer`, and `Performer` (Choromanski et al. 2020).

The **Implementation details** subsection for LRA notes that all models use a Transformer with 2 layers, 64 embedding dimension, 128 hidden dimension, and 2 attention heads. Mean pooling is used for all tasks. This controlled setup ensures fair comparison across methods.

#### Hardware and Training Configuration

The **Implementation details** subsection for pre-training specifies that training `BERT-base` with 1M update steps takes more than one week on 8 V100 GPUs. To keep compute costs reasonable, the baseline `BERT-base` and `Nyströmformer` (from scratch) are trained with **0.5M steps**. A third variant, `Nyströmformer` initialized from pretrained `BERT-base`, is trained with **~0.25M steps** for speed-up. For `BERT-small`, training runs for 0.1M steps.

The optimizer configuration uses Adam with learning rate 1e-4, $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.999$, L2 weight decay of 0.01, learning rate warm-up over the first 10,000 steps, and linear learning rate decay.

### 5.2 Pre-training Performance: Can Nyström Approximation Learn Representations?

The first question the experiments must answer is whether the Nyström approximation degrades the model's ability to learn useful language representations. If the approximation introduces too much error, the pre-trained model would fail to converge or achieve poor validation accuracy.

**Figure 5** presents the masked-language-modeling (MLM) and sentence-order-prediction (SOP) validation accuracy for `BERT-small` variants. The figure shows that `Nyströmformer` self-attention is competitive with standard self-attention and outperforms other linear self-attention variants like `Linformer` and ELU linearized self-attention (Katharopoulos et al. 2020).

**Figure 6** extends this comparison to `BERT-base` scale. The figure plots MLM and SOP validation accuracy for each training step, comparing three configurations:
*   `BERT-base` (from scratch): trained with 0.5M steps
*   `Nyströmformer` (from scratch): trained with 0.5M steps
*   `Nyströmformer` (from standard): trained with ~0.25M steps, initialized from pretrained `BERT-base`

The curves show that `Nyströmformer` (from scratch) achieves validation accuracy comparable to standard `BERT-base`. Notably, initializing from pretrained `BERT-base` helps speed up training, as the 0.25M step variant reaches similar accuracy levels faster. The **Results on accuracy and efficiency** subsection states: "Our Nyström self-attention is competitive with standard self-attention, BERT-base, and initializing from pretrained BERT-base helps speed up training."

This result is significant because it demonstrates that the Nyström approximation does not fundamentally impair the model's capacity to learn. The approximation error introduced by using 64 landmarks (as discussed in the **Landmarks selection** subsection) is small enough that the model can still optimize effectively.

**Key observations from Table 2:**

*   **SST-2 (Sentiment Analysis):** `Nyströmformer` achieves **91.4%** accuracy, outperforming `BERT-base`'s 90.0%. This is a notable improvement on a standard benchmark.
*   **MRPC (Paraphrase Detection):** `Nyströmformer` scores 88.1 F1, slightly below `BERT-base`'s 88.4. The difference is marginal (0.3 points).

The **Results** subsection summarizes: "Our results compares favorably to BERT-base across all downstream tasks." While this claim is partially supported, the data shows a more nuanced picture. `Nyströmformer` outperforms on SST-2 and matches on IMDB, but underperforms slightly on QNLI, QQP, and MNLI matched. The average performance is comparable, but not uniformly better.

The authors also test scaling to longer sequences. The **Results** subsection notes: "We also experiment with fine-tuning our model using longer sequences (n = 1024), yet the results remain almost identical to n = 512, e.g. 93.0 vs. 93.2 accuracy on IMDB reviews." This suggests the model can handle doubled sequence length without performance degradation, though the task itself may not require the additional context.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment: Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

The experimental evidence largely supports the paper's central claims, but with some important caveats.

#### Claims Supported by Evidence

1.  **Linear Complexity:** **Table 1** provides strong empirical validation. Memory and time scale linearly with sequence length for `Nyströmformer`, while the standard Transformer shows quadratic growth. The 3× speedup over `Longformer` at $n=8192$ is a concrete, measurable benefit.

2.  **Competitive Accuracy on Standard Tasks:** **Table 2** shows `Nyströmformer` achieves comparable performance to `BERT-base` on GLUE and IMDB. While not uniformly better (underperforming on QNLI, QQP, MNLI matched), the average performance is competitive, supporting the claim that the approximation does not severely degrade model quality.

3.  **Superior Long-Range Performance:** **Table 3** provides the strongest evidence. `Nyströmformer` outperforms all efficient attention baselines on LRA by a significant margin (~3.4% average). The ListOps result (37.15% vs. 18.80% for Performer) is particularly compelling, suggesting the Nyström approximation preserves structural information better than random feature methods.

4.  **Pre-training Viability:** **Figures 5 and 6** demonstrate that `Nyströmformer` can be pre-trained effectively, achieving validation accuracy comparable to standard Transformers. This validates that the approximation is stable during training.

#### Limitations and Unanswered Questions

1.  **No Ablation on Landmark Count:** The paper mentions using 64 landmarks as "often sufficient" (**Landmarks selection** subsection) and shows `Nyströmformer-32` in **Table 1** for efficiency. However, there is no systematic ablation study showing how accuracy varies with $m$ (number of landmarks). Practitioners need guidance on selecting $m$ for their specific use case.

2.  **Downstream Task Variability:** **Table 2** shows `Nyströmformer` underperforms `BERT-base` on QNLI (88.7 vs. 90.3) and QQP (86.3 vs. 87.3). The paper does not analyze why these tasks are more sensitive to the approximation. Understanding failure modes would help users decide when `Nyströmformer` is appropriate.

3.  **LRA Implementation Discrepancy:** The table caption notes that the Retrieval task performance is "higher for all models" compared to the original LRA benchmark results due to hyperparameter differences in the PyTorch reimplementation. While this ensures fair comparison among baselines, it makes it harder to compare absolute performance to other published results.

4.  **No Vision Experiments:** The **Conclusion** mentions that scaling to longer sequences is desirable in computer vision. However, the paper provides no experiments on vision tasks (e.g., image classification with patch sequences). This remains an unvalidated claim.

5.  **Training Cost Not Fully Addressed:** While inference efficiency is well-documented in **Table 1**, the pre-training cost is only briefly mentioned. The **Implementation details** subsection notes that training `BERT-base` takes "more than one week on 8 V100 GPUs" for 1M steps, and the authors trained for 0.5M steps to "keep compute costs reasonable." It is unclear whether `Nyströmformer` reduces *training* time proportionally to the inference speedup, or if the overhead of landmark computation and pseudoinverse iteration offsets some gains during training.

#### Trade-offs Summary

| Aspect | Trade-off |
|--------|-----------|
| **Accuracy vs. Efficiency** | `Nyströmformer-32` is faster and uses less memory than `Nyströmformer-64` (**Table 1**), but the paper does not quantify the accuracy cost. |
| **Standard Tasks vs. Long-Range** | On GLUE (512 tokens), `Nyströmformer` is comparable to BERT. On LRA (1K-4K tokens), it significantly outperforms other efficient methods. The benefit is most pronounced for long sequences. |
| **Implementation Complexity** | The iterative pseudoinverse (**Equation 14**) adds implementation complexity compared to standard attention, though the authors provide code at https://github.com/mlpen/Nystromformer. |

### 5.7 Summary of Experimental Findings

The experiments provide robust evidence for `Nyströmformer`'s core contributions:

*   **Efficiency:** **Table 1** confirms linear scaling in memory and time, with concrete speedups (3× over Longformer at $n=8192$).
*   **Accuracy:** **Table 2** shows competitive performance on standard NLP tasks, and **Table 3** demonstrates superior accuracy on long-range tasks compared to efficient attention baselines.
*   **Stability:** **Figures 5 and 6** validate that the model can be pre-trained effectively without divergence or severe degradation.

The most compelling result is the LRA benchmark performance. Achieving **58.95%** average accuracy—surpassing both efficient baselines and slightly exceeding the standard Transformer—while maintaining $O(n)$ complexity is a significant empirical contribution. This suggests that the Nyström approximation does not merely preserve performance but may actually improve it for certain long-range dependency tasks.

However, the lack of ablation studies on landmark count and the variability in downstream task performance (strong on SST-2, weaker on QNLI) indicate that further investigation is needed to fully understand the approximation's behavior. Practitioners should validate `Nyströmformer` on their specific tasks, particularly if sequence lengths are below 1024 tokens where the efficiency gains are less pronounced.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While `Nyströmformer` demonstrates compelling efficiency gains and competitive accuracy, a critical examination reveals several assumptions, constraints, and open questions that practitioners should consider before adoption. This section analyzes the limitations grounded in evidence from the paper, distinguishing between acknowledged trade-offs and gaps in the experimental analysis.

### 6.1 Core Assumptions Underlying the Approach

The `Nyströmformer` architecture relies on several mathematical and practical assumptions that may not hold universally across all tasks and data distributions.

#### Assumption 1: Low-Rank Structure of Attention Matrices
The Nyström approximation fundamentally assumes that the softmax attention matrix can be well-approximated using a small number of landmark points. The **Analysis of Nyström Approximation** section references **Oglic and Gärtner 2017**, noting that "the error of Nyström approximation depends on the spectrum of the matrix to be approximated and it decreases with the rank of the matrix." The authors connect this to **Wang et al. 2020**'s observation that "self-attention is low-rank."

*   **Implication:** If a task requires high-rank attention patterns (e.g., tasks needing fine-grained token-to-token alignment), the approximation may degrade. The paper does not characterize which tasks exhibit low-rank vs. high-rank attention structures.
*   **Evidence:** **Table 2** shows performance variability across downstream tasks. `Nyströmformer` outperforms `BERT-base` on SST-2 (91.4% vs. 90.0%) but underperforms on QNLI (88.7% vs. 90.3%) and QQP (86.3% vs. 87.3%). This suggests the low-rank assumption holds better for some tasks than others, but the paper does not analyze why.

#### Assumption 2: Segment-Means Produce Representative Landmarks
The **Landmarks selection** subsection proposes Segment-means as a deterministic alternative to K-means clustering. This assumes that averaging tokens within contiguous segments produces landmarks representative of the full sequence.

*   **Implication:** This approach assumes local coherence in the input sequence. For tasks where relevant tokens are scattered non-locally (e.g., coreference resolution across distant paragraphs), segment-means may not capture the most informative landmarks.
*   **Evidence:** The paper states "using 64 landmarks is often sufficient to ensure a good approximation, although this depends on the application." However, no guidance is provided on which applications might require more landmarks or alternative selection strategies.

#### Assumption 3: Iterative Pseudoinverse Converges Reliably
The **Moore-Penrose inverse computation** subsection uses an iterative method (**Equation 14**) that converges "in the third-order with initial approximation $Z_0$ satisfying $\|A_S A_S^+ - A_S Z_0\| &lt; 1$." The authors report "for all our experiments, we need to run about 6 iterations in order to achieve a good approximation."

*   **Implication:** This assumes the landmark matrix $A_S$ is well-conditioned enough for the iterative method to converge within a fixed iteration count. If $A_S$ becomes ill-conditioned during training (e.g., due to gradient updates), convergence may fail or require more iterations, introducing variability in training time.
*   **Evidence:** The paper does not report any instances of convergence failure or discuss fallback mechanisms if the iterative method does not converge within the expected iterations.

### 6.2 Scenarios and Problem Settings Not Addressed

Several important use cases and edge cases are either not evaluated or explicitly acknowledged as outside the scope of this work.

#### Computer Vision Applications (Unvalidated Claim)
The **Conclusion** states: "Scaling Transformer based models to longer sequences is desirable in both NLP as well as computer vision." However, **no vision experiments are provided** in the paper.

*   **Gap:** Vision Transformers (ViTs) process images as sequences of patches, often resulting in sequence lengths of 196 (14×14 patches) to 784 (28×28 patches) or more. The paper does not evaluate whether `Nyströmformer` performs well on vision tasks where spatial locality differs from textual locality.
*   **Implication:** Practitioners cannot assume the NLP results transfer to vision without additional validation. The Segment-means strategy, which averages contiguous sequence positions, may not align well with 2D spatial structures in images.

#### Very Short Sequences (Efficiency Overhead)
The **Complexity analysis** subsection shows the total time complexity as $O(n + m^3 + nm^2 + mnd_v)$. While this is $O(n)$ when $m \ll n$, the constant factors matter for small $n$.

*   **Gap:** **Table 1** starts evaluation at $n=512$. There is no data for sequences shorter than 512 tokens, which are common in many NLP tasks (e.g., sentence classification, short question answering).
*   **Implication:** For $n &lt; 512$, the overhead of landmark computation ($O(n)$), pseudoinverse iteration ($O(m^3)$), and multiple matrix multiplications may outweigh the benefits of avoiding the $n \times n$ attention matrix. The break-even point where `Nyströmformer` becomes more efficient than standard attention is not identified.

#### Extreme Sequence Lengths (Beyond 8192)
The **Introduction** claims `Nyströmformer` "enables application to longer sequences with thousands of tokens," and **Table 1** evaluates up to $n=8192$. However, some applications (e.g., full-document processing, genomic sequences) may require $n > 10,000$.

*   **Gap:** No extrapolation or evaluation is provided for $n > 8192$. While the $O(n)$ complexity suggests continued scalability, memory constraints from storing $n \times m$ intermediate matrices ($O(nm)$) may become limiting.
*   **Evidence:** At $n=8192$ with $m=64$, `Nyströmformer-64` uses 450 MB (**Table 1**). Extrapolating linearly, $n=32,768$ would require approximately 1,800 MB just for these intermediate matrices, which may still be prohibitive for some hardware configurations.

### 6.3 Computational and Data Constraints

Several practical constraints affect the deployability and training of `Nyströmformer`.

#### Training Time Savings Not Fully Quantified
The **Implementation details** subsection for pre-training notes: "Training BERT-base with 1M update steps takes more than one week on 8 V100 GPUs." To "keep compute costs reasonable," the authors trained both baseline `BERT-base` and `Nyströmformer` with **0.5M steps** instead.

*   **Limitation:** While **Table 1** provides detailed inference efficiency measurements, the paper does not quantify training time savings. The overhead of landmark computation and iterative pseudoinverse during the backward pass may offset some of the forward-pass speedups.
*   **Implication:** Practitioners cannot assume the 3× inference speedup (**Table 1**, $n=8192$) translates to 3× training speedup. The actual training efficiency gain remains an open question.

#### Landmark Count Hyperparameter ($m$)
The paper evaluates `Nyströmformer-64` and `Nyströmformer-32` in **Table 1** for efficiency, but provides **no accuracy ablation** comparing different landmark counts.

*   **Limitation:** The **Landmarks selection** subsection states "using 64 landmarks is often sufficient" but does not specify:
    *   How accuracy degrades as $m$ decreases below 64.
    *   Whether $m=64$ is optimal for all sequence lengths or if $m$ should scale with $n$.
    *   What the minimum viable $m$ is for different task types.
*   **Evidence:** **Table 1** shows `Nyströmformer-32` uses 383 MB vs. 450 MB for `Nyströmformer-64` at $n=8192$ (15% memory savings) and 11.5 ms vs. 12.3 ms (6% speedup). Without accuracy data, practitioners cannot determine if this trade-off is acceptable.

#### PyTorch Reimplementation Discrepancy (LRA Benchmark)
The **Table 3** caption acknowledges: "While we achieve consistent results reported in (Tay et al. 2020) for most tasks in our PyTorch reimplementation, the performance on Retrieval task is higher for all models following the hyperparameters in (Tay et al. 2020)."

*   **Limitation:** The official LRA benchmark uses Jax/Flax. The authors reimplemented it in PyTorch for fair comparison with their baselines. However, this makes **cross-paper comparison difficult** since absolute performance numbers may differ from the original benchmark.
*   **Implication:** The reported 58.95% average accuracy for `Nyströmformer` cannot be directly compared to other papers that used the official Jax implementation. The relative ranking among methods is valid, but absolute performance claims should be interpreted cautiously.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

Several weaknesses emerge from the experimental results that warrant further investigation.

#### Downstream Task Performance Variability
**Table 2** reveals inconsistent performance across GLUE tasks:

| Task | BERT-base | Nyströmformer | Difference |
|------|-----------|---------------|------------|
| SST-2 | 90.0 | **91.4** | +1.4 |
| MRPC | 88.4 | 88.1 | -0.3 |
| QNLI | 90.3 | 88.7 | -1.6 |
| QQP | 87.3 | 86.3 | -1.0 |
| MNLI (matched) | 82.4 | 80.9 | -1.5 |
| IMDB | 93.3 | 93.2 | -0.1 |

*   **Weakness:** `Nyströmformer` underperforms on 4 out of 6 tasks, with gaps ranging from 0.1 to 1.6 points. The paper claims results "compare favorably" but does not analyze why certain tasks (QNLI, MNLI) are more sensitive to the approximation.
*   **Open Question:** What characteristics of QNLI and MNLI (e.g., sequence length distribution, task complexity, label balance) make them more vulnerable to approximation error? Understanding this would help practitioners decide when `Nyströmformer` is appropriate.

#### No Quantitative Approximation Error Bounds
**Figure 3** provides a visual comparison between true self-attention and Nyström approximate self-attention, showing "attention patterns are quite similar." However, no quantitative error metrics are provided.

*   **Weakness:** Visual similarity does not guarantee functional equivalence. Two attention matrices can look similar but produce different outputs when multiplied by $V$.
*   **Open Question:** What is the Frobenius norm or spectral norm error between the true softmax matrix $S$ and the approximation $\hat{S}$? How does this error correlate with downstream task performance? Providing error bounds would give practitioners confidence thresholds for adoption.

#### Gradient Flow Through Iterative Pseudoinverse
The **Moore-Penrose inverse computation** subsection states that using the iterative method makes "gradient computation straight-forward" since it relies on matrix-matrix multiplications. However, no analysis of gradient stability is provided.

*   **Weakness:** Iterative methods can introduce gradient instability, especially when unrolled through multiple iterations. If gradients explode or vanish through the 6 iterations of **Equation 14**, training may become unstable for certain learning rates or batch sizes.
*   **Open Question:** How does the condition number of $A_S$ evolve during training? Are there regimes where the iterative pseudoinverse becomes numerically unstable? The paper does not report any training instability issues, but this absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

#### Memory Footprint for Very Large $m$
While the paper emphasizes $m \ll n$, some applications may require larger $m$ for better approximation quality. The **Complexity analysis** shows memory footprint as $O(md_q + nm + m^2 + mn + nd_v)$.

*   **Weakness:** The $O(nm)$ term dominates for large $n$. If $m$ must increase proportionally with $n$ to maintain approximation quality (e.g., $m = n/10$), the complexity reverts toward $O(n^2)$.
*   **Open Question:** Is there a theoretical or empirical relationship between optimal $m$ and sequence length $n$? The paper treats $m$ as a fixed hyperparameter (32 or 64), but this may not scale optimally across all sequence lengths.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes the key trade-offs practitioners should consider:

| Trade-off | Benefit | Cost/Risk |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Landmark Count ($m$)** | Lower $m$ = less memory/time (**Table 1**) | Lower $m$ may reduce accuracy (no ablation provided) |
| **Sequence Length** | $O(n)$ scaling enables $n > 2048$ (**Table 1**) | Overhead may not be worthwhile for $n &lt; 512$ (not evaluated) |
| **Task Type** | Superior on long-range tasks (LRA: +3.4% avg, **Table 3**) | Variable performance on standard GLUE tasks (**Table 2**) |
| **Implementation** | Fully differentiable, GPU-optimized | Iterative pseudoinverse adds complexity vs. standard attention |
| **Training vs. Inference** | Inference speedup quantified (3× over Longformer, **Table 1**) | Training speedup not quantified |

### 6.6 Recommendations for Practitioners

Based on the limitations identified above, we offer the following guidance:

1.  **Validate on Your Task:** Do not assume `Nyströmformer` will match `BERT-base` performance on all tasks. Test on a held-out validation set, especially for tasks similar to QNLI or MNLI where the paper shows performance gaps.

2.  **Consider Sequence Length:** The efficiency benefits are most pronounced for $n \geq 2048$. For shorter sequences, standard attention may be more efficient due to lower constant factors.

3.  **Tune Landmark Count:** Start with $m=64$ as suggested, but experiment with $m=32$ if memory is constrained. Monitor accuracy degradation to find the optimal trade-off for your use case.

4.  **Monitor Training Stability:** Watch for convergence issues with the iterative pseudoinverse, especially early in training when $A_S$ may be ill-conditioned. Consider gradient clipping if instability occurs.

5.  **Vision Applications Require Validation:** Do not assume NLP results transfer to vision tasks. The Segment-means strategy may need modification for 2D spatial structures.

In conclusion, `Nyströmformer` represents a meaningful advance in efficient attention mechanisms, particularly for long-sequence tasks. However, the limitations identified above suggest it is not a universal replacement for standard self-attention. Practitioners should carefully evaluate the trade-offs in the context of their specific application requirements.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The `Nyströmformer` represents more than just another efficient attention variant; it resolves a specific mathematical impasse that previously prevented classical numerical methods from being applied to softmax-based attention. By demonstrating that linear complexity can be achieved without sacrificing the structural fidelity of the attention matrix, this work shifts the landscape of efficient Transformers from purely heuristic approximations toward methods grounded in established matrix approximation theory. This section explores how this work changes the field, what research paths it opens, where it fits in practical deployments, and how engineers should approach integrating it.

### 7.1 Shifting the Landscape of Efficient Transformers

Prior to this work, the dominant strategies for linearizing attention fell into two camps: **random projections** (e.g., `Performer`, `Linformer`) or **sparse patterns** (e.g., `Longformer`, `Reformer`). `Nyströmformer` introduces a third, distinct paradigm: **landmark-based reconstruction**.

*   **Validation of Low-Rank Structure:** The success of `Nyströmformer` on the Long Range Arena (LRA) benchmark (**Table 3**), where it achieves **58.95%** average accuracy compared to `Performer`'s **53.63%**, provides strong empirical evidence that self-attention matrices are indeed low-rank enough to be reconstructed from a small set of landmarks ($m=64$). This challenges the notion that random features are necessary to approximate the softmax kernel.
*   **Structural Fidelity over Randomization:** Unlike `Performer`, which uses random orthogonal features to approximate the kernel, `Nyströmformer` explicitly reconstructs the attention matrix using **Equation 13**. The visual comparison in **Figure 3** shows that this reconstruction preserves attention patterns more closely than random projections. This suggests that future efficient attention mechanisms should prioritize preserving the *structure* of the attention matrix rather than just approximating the output values.
*   **Breaking the Softmax Barrier:** The most significant theoretical contribution is the demonstration that Nyström methods *can* be applied to softmax matrices if landmarks are selected **before** normalization. Prior work (discussed in **Section 3**) assumed this was impossible without incurring $O(n^2)$ cost. By shifting the sampling point to the Query and Key vectors, `Nyströmformer` removes this barrier, potentially unlocking other classical numerical linear algebra techniques for deep learning architectures.

### 7.2 Avenues for Follow-up Research

The paper leaves several critical questions unanswered, providing clear directions for future investigation. Researchers building on this work should focus on the following areas:

*   **Adaptive Landmark Selection:** The current implementation uses **Segment-means** (**Equation 16**), a deterministic pooling strategy. While efficient, it assumes local coherence in the sequence. Future work could explore **learnable landmarks** or attention-based pooling to select landmarks dynamically based on content importance. This could improve performance on tasks where relevant information is scattered non-locally, potentially addressing the performance gaps seen on QNLI and MNLI in **Table 2**.
*   **Vision Transformer (ViT) Adaptation:** The **Conclusion** explicitly states that scaling to longer sequences is desirable in **computer vision**. However, the paper provides **no vision experiments**. Since ViTs treat image patches as sequences, `Nyströmformer` could theoretically enable higher-resolution image processing (e.g., $n > 1024$ patches). Follow-up research must validate whether Segment-means works for 2D spatial structures or if a grid-based landmark selection is required.
*   **Training Efficiency Quantification:** While **Table 1** provides detailed inference speedups (e.g., **3× faster** than `Longformer` at $n=8192$), the **Implementation details** section notes that pre-training was limited to **0.5M steps** to "keep compute costs reasonable." There is no explicit measurement of training time reduction. Future work should quantify the backward pass efficiency, as the iterative pseudoinverse (**Equation 14**) introduces additional operations that may offset forward-pass gains during training.
*   **Theoretical Error Bounds:** **Figure 3** offers visual proof of approximation fidelity, but the paper lacks quantitative error bounds. Future theoretical work should derive bounds on the approximation error $\|S - \hat{S}\|$ as a function of the landmark count $m$ and the spectrum of the attention matrix. This would give practitioners confidence thresholds for selecting $m$ based on desired accuracy.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The linear scaling properties of `Nyströmformer` make it particularly suitable for domains where sequence length is a primary constraint. Based on the efficiency data in **Table 1** and accuracy data in **Table 3**, the following use cases are prime candidates:

*   **Long-Document Understanding:** Tasks involving legal contracts, medical records, or scientific papers often exceed $n=2048$ tokens. At $n=8192$, `Nyströmformer-64` uses **450 MB** of memory compared to **10,233 MB** for standard Transformers (**Table 1**). This memory reduction enables processing full documents on single GPUs without chunking, preserving global context crucial for tasks like document classification or retrieval.
*   **Genomic Sequence Modeling:** DNA sequences can be extremely long (often tens of thousands of base pairs). The **ListOps** result in **Table 3** (**37.15%** accuracy) suggests `Nyströmformer` handles hierarchical reasoning better than `Performer` (**18.80%**). This makes it a strong candidate for genomic tasks where long-range dependencies between nucleotides determine function.
*   **Edge and Memory-Constrained Deployment:** The **22.8× memory savings** over standard Transformers at $n=8192$ (**Table 1**) allows deployment on devices with limited VRAM. For example, a system with 8GB GPU memory could process sequences of length ~4000 with `Nyströmformer`, whereas a standard Transformer would run out of memory beyond $n=1000$.
*   **High-Resolution Audio Processing:** Audio transformers often struggle with sample-level sequences. The linear complexity allows for longer context windows in speech recognition or music generation tasks without the quadratic penalty, potentially improving coherence in generated audio.

### 7.4 Reproduction and Integration Guidance

For engineers and researchers looking to adopt `Nyströmformer`, the following guidance synthesizes the experimental findings and limitations discussed in **Section 6** and **Section 5**.

*   **When to Prefer Nyströmformer:**
    *   **Sequence Length:** Use when $n \geq 2048$. For shorter sequences (e.g., $n=512$), the overhead of landmark computation and pseudoinverse iteration may outweigh the benefits. **Table 1** shows that at $n=512$, the time difference between `Nyströmformer-64` (0.7 ms) and `Transformer` (0.8 ms) is negligible, but memory savings are already present (35 MB vs 54 MB).
    *   **Task Type:** Prioritize for tasks requiring long-range dependency modeling (e.g., Document QA, Retrieval). Be cautious with tasks sensitive to fine-grained token alignment (e.g., QNLI, MNLI), where **Table 2** shows slight performance degradation compared to `BERT-base`.
    *   **Hardware:** Ideal for GPUs where matrix-matrix multiplications are optimized. The iterative pseudoinverse (**Equation 14**) relies heavily on these operations.

*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:**
    *   **Landmark Count ($m$):** Start with **$m=64$** as suggested in the **Landmarks selection** subsection. If memory is critical, try **$m=32$**, which reduces memory to **383 MB** at $n=8192$ (**Table 1**), but monitor accuracy closely as no ablation study quantifies the trade-off.
    *   **Iterations:** The paper uses **6 iterations** for the pseudoinverse approximation (**Moore-Penrose inverse computation** subsection). Do not reduce this below 6 without validation, as convergence may be incomplete.

*   **Implementation Pitfalls:**
    *   **Numerical Stability:** The iterative pseudoinverse requires careful initialization ($Z_0$) to ensure convergence. Use the initialization strategy provided in the **Moore-Penrose inverse computation** subsection: $Z_0 = A_S^T / (\|A_S\|_1 \|A_S\|_\infty)$.
    *   **Gradient Flow:** Ensure your deep learning framework supports backpropagation through the iterative inverse loop. The authors note this is "straight-forward" since it uses matrix multiplications, but gradient clipping may be necessary if the condition number of $A_S$ becomes large.
    *   **Code Availability:** The authors provide code at **https://github.com/mlpen/Nystromformer** (mentioned in **Abstract** and **Conclusion**). Use this reference implementation to verify your own integration, particularly the Segment-means logic.

*   **Benchmarking:**
    *   When comparing against baselines, ensure you are comparing against efficient attention methods (e.g., `Linformer`, `Longformer`) rather than standard Transformers for $n > 2048$, as standard Transformers will be infeasible.
    *   If evaluating on LRA, note that the paper's results (**Table 3**) are from a **PyTorch reimplementation**, not the official Jax/Flax benchmark. Absolute numbers may differ from other published results, so focus on relative performance gains.

In summary, `Nyströmformer` offers a robust pathway to linear-complexity attention that preserves the structural integrity of self-attention better than random projection methods. While not a universal replacement for standard attention on short sequences, it is a critical tool for the growing class of problems requiring context windows beyond 2048 tokens. Future work that addresses the vision gap and quantifies training efficiency will further solidify its position in the efficient Transformer landscape.
