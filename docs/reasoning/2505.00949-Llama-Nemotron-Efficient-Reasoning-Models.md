## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces the Llama-Nemotron series (LN-Nano 8B, LN-Super 49B, LN-Ultra 253B), the first open-source heterogeneous reasoning models to support a dynamic reasoning toggle, enabling users to switch between standard chat and deep reasoning modes via a lightweight system prompt. By combining neural architecture search (Puzzle framework) with large-scale reinforcement learning, LN-Ultra achieves 76.0% accuracy on GPQA-Diamond—surpassing DeepSeek-R1's 71.5%—while fitting on a single 8xH100 node with higher throughput than DeepSeek-R1's 8xH200 requirement. This work addresses the critical bottlenecks of inference efficiency and user controllability in reasoning models, delivering state-of-the-art performance under a commercially permissive license for enterprise use.

## 2. Context and Motivation

The development of large language models (LLMs) has recently entered a distinct phase characterized by the emergence of **reasoning models**. Unlike traditional instruction-tuned models that generate immediate responses, reasoning models—exemplified by proprietary systems like OpenAI o1 and open-weight models like DeepSeek-R1—are designed to "think deeply" before answering. This behavior manifests as long responses containing explicit chains of thought, self-verification, reflection, and backtracking. While this architectural shift enables state-of-the-art performance on complex tasks like PhD-level STEM questions and competition math, it introduces fundamental challenges in deployment and usability that prior models did not face.

### The Inference Efficiency Bottleneck

The primary technical gap this paper addresses is the conflict between **reasoning capability** and **inference efficiency**. In traditional LLM scaling, performance improvements were largely driven by increasing model parameters or training data (pretraining scaling). However, reasoning models rely heavily on **inference-time scaling**—the model generates more tokens during the reasoning process to solve harder problems. As stated in the Introduction:

> "As reasoning capabilities increasingly depend on scaling at inference time, it has become essential to design models that are efficient to run during inference. Inference efficiency is no longer just a deployment concern—it is now a core limiting factor for overall model intelligence..."

This shift changes the optimization landscape. A model that achieves high accuracy but requires prohibitive memory or latency is not viable for real-world applications, particularly in agentic pipelines where models may need to iterate repeatedly. Prior open-source reasoning models often lag behind proprietary counterparts in efficiency, requiring massive hardware clusters (e.g., multiple nodes of H200 GPUs) to run effectively. This creates a barrier for enterprise adoption and research replication. The Llama-Nemotron work positions itself by treating efficiency as a core training objective rather than a post-hoc optimization, utilizing Neural Architecture Search (NAS) to modify the model structure itself for hardware compatibility.

### The Need for Dynamic Controllability

A secondary but critical gap is the lack of user control over reasoning behavior. Existing reasoning models typically operate in a single mode: they always engage in detailed chain-of-thought processing. This rigidity is suboptimal for general-purpose use. Not every query requires deep reasoning; for simple factual questions or casual chat, lengthy reasoning traces are unnecessarily verbose, increase latency, and waste computational resources.

The paper identifies this as a usability and resource allocation problem:

> "Not all queries benefit from detailed multi-step reasoning—such responses may be unnecessarily verbose or even counterproductive in certain contexts. Granting users the ability to toggle reasoning on or off ensures that inference resources are allocated judiciously..."

Prior approaches generally require maintaining two separate models (one for chat, one for reasoning) or accepting the overhead of reasoning on every token. Llama-Nemotron introduces a **dynamic reasoning toggle**, allowing a single model to switch between standard chat and reasoning modes via a system prompt (e.g., `"detailed thinking on/off"`). This design choice addresses the economic and experiential cost of always-on reasoning.

### Limitations of Prior Open-Source Approaches

Before this work, the open-source community faced a trade-off triangle between **performance**, **efficiency**, and **licensing**:

*   **Performance:** Models like DeepSeek-R1 demonstrated that open weights could match proprietary reasoning capabilities, but they often retained high computational costs.
*   **Efficiency:** Standard dense architectures (like Llama 3.1 405B) are computationally heavy. While techniques like quantization exist, they often degrade quality. Architectural changes via NAS (like the Puzzle framework mentioned in Section 2) were not previously applied at this scale to reasoning-specific models.
*   **Licensing:** Many high-performing models come with restrictive licenses that limit enterprise use.

Figure 4 in the paper illustrates the historical tension between accuracy and throughput. Typically, moving towards higher accuracy (e.g., Llama 3.1 405B) results in lower throughput (tokens/s). Conversely, optimizing for throughput often sacrifices accuracy. The motivation for Llama-Nemotron is to shift this Pareto frontier—achieving higher accuracy *and* higher throughput simultaneously through heterogeneous architecture design.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

Llama-Nemotron positions itself as the first open family of **heterogeneous reasoning models** that resolves these tensions. It builds upon the Llama 3 series (using Llama 3.1 and 3.3 as base architectures) but diverges significantly in the post-training and architecture search phases.

*   **Vs. Standard Llama 3 Instruct:** LN models add deep reasoning capabilities via supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and reinforcement learning (RL) on reasoning traces, which standard Llama 3 models lack.
*   **Vs. DeepSeek-R1:** While DeepSeek-R1 pioneered open reasoning models, LN-Ultra is optimized to fit on a single 8xH100 node compared to DeepSeek-R1's 8xH200 requirement, offering superior memory efficiency and throughput (Section 2.1).
*   **Vs. Proprietary Models (o1):** LN models offer open weights and a permissive license, enabling local deployment and fine-tuning, which is impossible with closed APIs.

By integrating efficiency optimizations (NAS, FFN Fusion) directly into the model creation pipeline rather than treating them as separate compression steps, this work argues that efficiency and intelligence are not mutually exclusive but must be co-designed. This context is essential for understanding why the training pipeline includes specific stages like "recovery training" after architecture search—to ensure that the efficiency gains do not come at the cost of the reasoning capabilities the model was designed to possess.

## 3. Technical Approach

This is a systems engineering and machine learning training pipeline paper that presents a five-stage methodology for creating efficient reasoning models. The core idea is that reasoning capability and inference efficiency must be co-designed from the architecture level upward, rather than treating efficiency as a post-hoc compression step.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The Llama-Nemotron system is a family of three language models (8B, 49B, and 253B parameters) that can dynamically switch between casual chat mode and deep reasoning mode via a simple text prompt. The solution addresses the dual problem of making reasoning models both faster to run (through architectural search and compression) and more controllable (through paired training data that teaches the model when to reason deeply versus when to respond directly).

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The training pipeline consists of five sequential stages that transform base Llama 3 models into reasoning-capable, efficiency-optimized systems. **Stage 1 (Neural Architecture Search)** uses the Puzzle framework to search for hardware-efficient block configurations, producing a library of alternative transformer blocks with different accuracy-efficiency tradeoffs. **Stage 2 (Recovery Training)** applies knowledge distillation and continued pretraining to restore any quality lost during architectural modification. **Stage 3 (Data Curation)** generates synthetic reasoning and non-reasoning datasets across math, code, science, and general domains, with each sample tagged for reasoning mode. **Stage 4 (Supervised Fine-Tuning)** trains the model on this paired data using the "detailed thinking on/off" system prompt to learn reasoning behavior control. **Stage 5 (Reinforcement Learning)** applies GRPO-based RL on verifiable rewards to surpass teacher model performance, followed by a final preference optimization stage for alignment. Information flows unidirectionally through these stages, with each stage's output checkpoint serving as the next stage's input.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- **Neural Architecture Search (Puzzle Framework)** comes first because it fundamentally changes the model structure before any reasoning-specific training occurs—understanding this explains why recovery training is necessary.
- **Recovery Training (Distillation + Continued Pretraining)** follows immediately because it repairs quality degradation from NAS, establishing the baseline performance before reasoning capabilities are added.
- **Synthetic Data Curation** is explained third because the SFT and RL stages depend entirely on the quality and structure of this training data, particularly the reasoning on/off pairing mechanism.
- **Supervised Fine-Tuning** comes fourth as it transfers reasoning behavior from teacher models and establishes the toggle control that distinguishes this work from prior reasoning models.
- **Reinforcement Learning** is covered fifth because it enables the model to exceed teacher performance, which is impossible through distillation alone.
- **Infrastructure Optimizations** are woven throughout but receive dedicated attention because they enable the large-scale RL training that would otherwise be infeasible at this model size.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### Neural Architecture Search via the Puzzle Framework

The foundation of Llama-Nemotron's efficiency gains lies in the Puzzle framework, a neural architecture search (NAS) system that transforms standard Llama 3 models into heterogeneous, hardware-optimized variants. Unlike traditional NAS approaches that search over entire architectures at once, Puzzle operates at the block level, creating a library of alternative transformer blocks that can be mixed and matched. The process begins with a base Llama 3 Instruct model—specifically Llama 3.3-70B-Instruct for LN-Super and Llama 3.1-405B-Instruct for LN-Ultra—and applies block-wise local distillation to build alternative block variants.

Each alternative block is trained independently and in parallel to approximate the function of its parent block while improving computational properties such as latency, memory usage, or throughput. This creates an explicit accuracy-efficiency tradeoff profile for each block variant, meaning some blocks achieve higher efficiency but incur quality degradation while others maintain accuracy with modest efficiency gains. The block library includes several variant types: attention removal (some blocks omit the attention mechanism entirely, reducing both compute and KV-cache memory consumption), variable FFN dimensions (the feed-forward network's intermediate size is varied, enabling compression at different granularity levels such as 87%, 75%, 50%, down to 10% of the original hidden size), grouped-query attention with different numbers of key-value heads, linear alternatives to attention, and no-op substitutions. Empirical evaluation showed that attention removal and FFN compression were the most effective optimizations for LN-Super and LN-Ultra in terms of overall throughput and memory savings.

Once the block library is constructed, Puzzle assembles a complete model by selecting one block per layer using a mixed-integer programming (MIP) solver. This solver identifies the most efficient configuration under a given set of constraints such as hardware compatibility, maximum allowed latency, total memory budget, or desired inference throughput. Because Puzzle supports multiple block variants per layer with different accuracy-efficiency tradeoff profiles, it enables users to precisely target any point on the accuracy-efficiency Pareto frontier. For example, the system can generate models that meet specific constraints relevant to agentic systems or deployment pipelines, such as bounded memory use or tight end-to-end response time.

For LN-Ultra specifically, the team introduces an additional compression technique called FFN Fusion, designed to reduce sequential depth and improve inference latency. This technique leverages a structural property that emerges after Puzzle removes some attention layers: the model often contains consecutive FFN blocks. FFN Fusion identifies such sequences and replaces them with fewer, wider FFN layers that can be executed in parallel. This reduces the number of sequential steps without compromising expressivity, and significantly improves compute utilization—especially on multi-GPU setups where inter-layer communication overhead is non-negligible. The deployment constraints differ between models: LN-Super is optimized to run efficiently on a single NVIDIA H100 GPU with tensor parallelism 1 (TP1), achieving a 5× throughput speedup over Llama 3.3-70B-Instruct at batch size 256 and TP1, while LN-Ultra is optimized for a full H100 node (8 GPUs) with a constraint to achieve at least 1.5× latency reduction over Llama 3.1-405B-Instruct, ultimately achieving 1.71× latency improvement after FFN Fusion.

#### Recovery Training: Knowledge Distillation and Continued Pretraining

Following the NAS phase, both LN-Super and LN-Ultra undergo additional training to improve inter-block compatibility and recover any quality loss introduced during blockwise substitution. This recovery training is essential because the heterogeneous architecture created by Puzzle—where different layers may have different block types—requires the model to learn how these diverse blocks interact coherently. LN-Super is trained for 40B tokens using a knowledge distillation objective over the Distillation Mix dataset introduced by Bercovich et al. (2024). LN-Ultra follows a two-phase recovery: first trained with knowledge distillation for 65B tokens using the same distillation dataset, followed by 88B tokens of continued training on the Nemotron-H phase 4 pretraining dataset.

This final pretraining step allows LN-Ultra to not only match but surpass the reference model Llama 3.1-405B-Instruct in key benchmarks, demonstrating that aggressive architecture optimization can be reconciled with high model performance through short distillation and pretraining. Table 1 shows the comparison of LN-Ultra after the continued pretraining phase (before supervised and reinforcement learning) to Llama 3 models: LN-Ultra achieves 88.1% on MMLU (vs. 88.6% for Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct), 80.4% on MATH500 (vs. 69.6%), 88.4% on HumanEval (vs. 86.0%), and 83.2% on RULER 128K (vs. 73.7%). These results confirm that the NAS-induced quality degradation is recoverable and that the efficiency gains do not permanently compromise model capabilities.

#### Synthetic Data Curation for Reasoning Control

The Llama-Nemotron-Post-Training-Dataset is a carefully curated collection of 33,011,757 total samples designed to target key capabilities such as mathematical reasoning, coding, science, and instruction following. Table 2 provides the breakdown by domain: Math comprises 22,066,397 samples (66.8% of total), Code comprises 10,108,883 samples (30.6%), Science comprises 708,920 samples (2.1%), Chat comprises 39,792 samples (0.12%), Instruction Following comprises 56,339 samples (0.17%), and Safety comprises 31,426 samples (0.10%). Critically, each domain is split into "reasoning on" and "reasoning off" subsets, with the reasoning on samples tagged with the system instruction "detailed thinking on" and reasoning off samples tagged with "detailed thinking off". This pairing enables the model to learn to modulate its reasoning behavior based on the system prompt.

For math reasoning data, the team uses a pipeline described by Moshkov et al. (2025) that collects mathematical problems from Art of Problem Solving (AoPS) community forums, excluding "Middle School Math" which was found to be too easy. The pipeline performs problem extraction (using Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct to identify and extract all problems from forum posts), problem classification (categorizing each problem as proof/not proof, multiple choice/not, binary/not, valid/invalid), answer extraction (extracting only the final answer expression to enable automatic correctness checking), benchmark decontamination (removing questions that closely resemble those in popular math benchmarks using LLM-based comparison), and solution generation (prompting DeepSeek-R1 to produce 16 generations per problem for reasoning solutions and Qwen2.5-Math-7B-Instruct to produce 64 generations per problem for non-reasoning solutions). The final filtering step removes any solutions that do not reach the expected answer, with predicted and expected answers compared by prompting Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct to judge their equivalence.

For code reasoning data, the team aggregates 28,904 unique competitive programming questions from diverse sources including TACO, APPS, CodeContests, and CodeForces, after performing exact-match deduplication. Contamination checking uses cosine similarity checks and semantic evaluation by LLM judges (Llama-3.3-70B and Qwen2.5-32B), with manual verification confirming negligible overlap (&lt; 0.3%). DeepSeek-R1 generates multiple solutions per question using Nucleus Sampling with temperature 0.6 and top-p 0.95 via SGLang, explicitly prompting for reasoning steps enclosed in  tags. Post-processing verifies the presence of reasoning traces, extracts solution code segments (demarcated by python...), removes samples with code inside reasoning tags, and validates syntax using Tree Sitter, yielding approximately 488K Python samples. An ablation study scaling the dataset from 25k to 736k samples showed continuous improvement, with the scaling curve not plateauing—this emphasizes the importance of large, diverse, and challenging problem sets for advancing code generation capabilities.

For science data, the team curates open-ended and multiple-choice questions from StackOverflow and synthetically generated MCQs. Synthetic question generation defines a broad set of academic topics (physics, biology, chemistry) and their subtopics using Nemotron-4-340B-Instruct, specifies multiple difficulty levels, and prompts Qwen2.5 models to generate MCQs conditioned on topic, subtopic, and difficulty level. Each question is verified for format compliance, and the dataset is augmented by prompting Qwen2.5 to generate variations of the original questions following the OpenMathInstruct-2 pipeline. Benchmark decontamination is performed against GPQA, MMLU, and MMLU-Pro test sets. For all questions, DeepSeek-R1 generates multiple reasoning traces, and for questions without ground-truth answers, majority voting across generated solutions infers the most likely correct answer.

For general domain data, the team generates synthetic prompts covering various tasks such as open QA, closed QA, extraction, and brainstorming, and sources real-world user prompts from publicly available datasets with permissive licenses. For responses, DeepSeek-R1 is prompted for multiple generations and rejection sampling is performed using the Llama-3.1-Nemotron-70B reward model to ensure high-quality responses. To train the reasoning toggle specifically, the team constructs paired data where each prompt has both a reasoning response and a non-reasoning response—prompts are randomly sampled from the reasoning dataset and corresponding non-reasoning responses are generated using Llama-3.1-Nemotron-70B-Instruct for general domain prompts and Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct for others.

For general-domain open-ended responses, the team employs a Feedback-Edit Inference-Time-Scaling system described by Wang et al. (2025b). The process begins with 20k first-turn prompts sourced from ShareGPT and WildChat-1M. Llama-3.1-Nemotron-70B-Instruct generates multiple initial responses for each prompt, which are then refined through a three-stage process: a dedicated Feedback model identifies areas for improvement, a dedicated Edit model makes targeted edits based on the feedback, and a dedicated Select model chooses the best edited response. The resulting dataset comprises 20k first-turn prompts and their corresponding high-quality responses.

#### Supervised Fine-Tuning for Reasoning Behavior Transfer

Supervised fine-tuning plays a critical role in transferring reasoning capabilities into the Llama-Nemotron models by distilling reasoning behavior from strong teacher models like DeepSeek-R1 through training on task-specific reasoning traces. All models are trained using a token-level cross-entropy loss over the instruction-tuning data, with training batches mixing reasoning and non-reasoning data where prompts are paired with responses conditioned on the respective system instruction ("detailed thinking on/off"). The team observes that models require higher learning rates to effectively learn from long reasoning traces, especially due to sequence-length-dependent token loss averaging, and that extended training over multiple epochs improves performance particularly for smaller models.

LN-Nano undergoes a three-stage SFT pipeline using a global batch size of 256 with sequence packing and effective sequence length of 32k tokens. In the first stage, the model is fine-tuned exclusively on reasoning data from code, math, and science domains with a learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$ for four epochs to prevent failure modes such as repetitive completions. In the second stage, non-reasoning data is introduced mixed with reasoning samples, allowing the model to learn reasoning control. In the final stage, a smaller blend focused on chat, instruction-following, and tool-calling is used.

LN-Super is trained on the full SFT dataset for a single epoch using a fixed learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-6}$, sequence length of 16k, and a global batch size of 256. Smaller-scale runs suggested that performance improves up to 3–4 epochs with larger learning rates ($5 \times 10^{-5}$), but training was constrained by computational and time limits. Recent works show that rejection fine-tuning can further improve performance; however, it does not yield gains in these experiments and is therefore omitted.

LN-Ultra is trained on the full dataset using sequence packing with effective sequence length of 24k and a global batch size of 256 to maximize token throughput—an essential strategy when fine-tuning large models with long-context reasoning data. Initial ablation runs indicated that higher learning rates such as $5 \times 10^{-5}$ generally improve outcomes, but consistently high learning rates caused training instability including gradient explosions. To mitigate this, the team implements a linear warmup to $1 \times 10^{-5}$, followed by cosine decay to $1 \times 10^{-6}$ with a warmup ratio of 10%. Despite these measures, training encountered gradient explosions and numerical instability after the first epoch, requiring training resumption with reinitialized optimizer states before successful convergence was achieved.

#### Reinforcement Learning for Surpassing Teacher Performance

While supervised fine-tuning can transfer strong capabilities through distillation from powerful teachers, distillation inherently sets an upper bound on the student's performance, particularly when the student's base model is more capable than the teacher's. Using supervised fine-tuning alone, LN-Ultra can approach the performance of DeepSeek-R1 but not exceed it. To enable the student to surpass its teacher, large-scale reinforcement learning is essential as it allows the model to continually explore new possibilities and engage in self-learning. Consistent with findings from DeepSeek-AI et al. (2025), preliminary experiments indicate that reinforcement learning yields suboptimal results for smaller models compared to distillation, so reasoning RL is applied exclusively to LN-Ultra.

For LN-Ultra, the team enhances scientific reasoning capabilities through large-scale reinforcement learning leveraging the Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) algorithm. The training configuration uses a rollout prompt size of 72 and samples 16 responses per prompt with temperature = 1 and top_p = 1. During training, the global batch size is set to 576 with 2 gradient updates per rollout, and the model is trained until convergence on reasoning tasks. Figure 5 shows the accuracy score on GPQA-Diamond throughout the reasoning RL training, with the whole training taking approximately 140k H100 hours using the optimized training infrastructure.

Two types of rewards are leveraged in this training phase. Accuracy rewards provide a ground truth answer (a number, sentence, or paragraph) for each training example, with the Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct model serving as a judge to determine whether the policy's predictions match the ground truth answer. Format rewards ensure the model puts its thinking process between  and &lt;/think> tags when using "detailed thinking on" mode, and check for the non-existence of thinking tags when using "detailed thinking off" mode.

To ensure the model is adequately challenged, the team preprocesses data by independently generating 8 responses per question using LN-Super, calculating the pass rate, and then intentionally discarding prompts with a pass rate of 0.75 or higher to increase training data difficulty. Beyond data filtering, curriculum training is implemented to allow the model to gradually learn from a progression of tasks with increasing difficulty. The team implements a progressive batching strategy leveraging pre-calculated pass rate as a difficulty metric: given a fixed batch size, the core approach involves dynamically calculating a target distribution of pass rates for each sequential batch, modeled using a Gaussian function centered on a difficulty level that progresses from high pass rates (easier examples) for initial batches to low pass rates (harder examples) for later batches. Samples are allocated to each batch primarily based on this target distribution, considering the available count for each pass rate, with any remaining batch capacity filled by prioritizing pass rates with the largest remaining sample pools. Figure 6 demonstrates the effectiveness of this curriculum strategy, which stabilizes the training process and achieves higher accuracy compared to random sampling.

#### Infrastructure Optimizations for Large-Scale RL Training

The RL training infrastructure primarily uses NeMo-Aligner with a development branch that implements GRPO and heterogeneous model support. The generation stage uses vLLM and the training stage uses Megatron-LM, with training and inference stages co-located on the same GPUs. The total GPU count used was 72 nodes of 8×H100. The training model parallelism configuration was: tensor parallel = 8 with sequence parallel, context parallel = 2, pipeline parallel = 18, and data parallel = 2. The generation model parallelism was tensor parallel = 8 and data parallel = 72.

Each stage maintains its own set of model weights, which are synced at the start of each step. First, all training weights are all-gathered over the training pipeline parallel dimension, converted into vLLM format, and written into shared memory. Then all training stage memory is released or offloaded to host. Next, vLLM is awoken from sleep mode, loads the newly saved model weights from shared memory, and begins generating. After generations have finished, vLLM GPU memory is released using sleep mode = 2, and all training memory is reloaded.

Memory management represents one of the major challenges in enabling GRPO training of LN-Ultra, as training jobs are scheduled to a shared cluster environment where each node has 8 H100 GPUs, dual socket 32-core CPUs, and 2TB CPU DRAM. The model in BF16 type takes approximately $253 \times 2 \approx 500$ GB memory. Three memory profiling tools were developed to monitor usage: GPU memory utilization using PyTorch, CPU memory utilization using psutil, and /dev/shm utilization using the df command (needed because /dev/shm is used to pass weights from trainer to vLLM server, with the host configured to allocate up to 1TB for /dev/shm space).

The first challenge is weight preparation: when all-gathering training weights across pipeline parallel stages, extremely large tensors are encountered due to the heterogeneous architecture, with one tensor having 13B elements and occupying 26B GPU memory in BF16 type. The solution requires releasing unused GPU memory periodically and moving some tensor conversion operations to CPU. The second challenge is vLLM GPU memory utilization: with tensor parallel equal to 8, each GPU is expected to keep $500/8 \approx 62$ GB from the weights in BF16, and considering KV cache, activations, and GPU memory occupied by the trainer, the budget is very tight. The cudagraph feature must be disabled to avoid GPU out-of-memory in vLLM, but when FP8 inference generation is enabled, the GPU memory budget becomes looser and cudagraph can be re-enabled. The final challenge is GPU and CPU memory usage in the trainer: tensor parallelism = 8 is a natural choice to partition the full model into the 8×H100 GPUs available in the same node, but because the model architecture is heterogeneous, identity layers must be inserted to balance the pipeline stages. The team finds that pipeline parallelism = 18 is optimal to avoid training OOM in GPU and checkpoint saving OOM in CPU while minimizing communication costs. Activations consume significant memory, requiring 18 micro-batches when pipeline parallelism is 18, so context parallel = 2 and sequence parallel are used to reduce activation memory consumption.

For FP8 inference generation, the team identifies the generation stage as the dominant component of step time and implements a path to support vLLM's online FP8 generation mode, which executes all GEMMs in FP8 using per token activation scaling factors and per tensor weight scaling factors. Custom vLLM weight loaders are implemented capable of loading BF16 weights supplied by the training stage and casting to FP8 weights and scaling factors at runtime. Because vLLM does not support directly initializing models in FP8, meta-weight tensor initialization is implemented to avoid materializing the full BF16 inference engine, which would cause out-of-memory error in the GPU. Overall, the team observes a peak FP8 generation throughput of 32 tokens/s/GPU/prompt, representing a 1.8× generation speedup against BF16 and the highest decoding throughput observed in reasoning training at this scale. A 1.4× speedup comes from FP8 generation alone, with an additional 0.4× from the reduction in memory usage which enables vLLM's cudagraph feature.

#### Preference Optimization for Alignment

After training for scientific reasoning, a short RL run optimizes instruction following capabilities for LN-Super and LN-Ultra. The team uses a similar verification setup as Zhou et al. (2023) and generates synthetic instruction following prompts containing from one to ten detailed instructions. RL is run with the RLOO algorithm for less than 120 steps using the instruction following verifier as a reward, with a batch size of 128 prompts. This training boosts performance on conventional instruction following benchmarks as well as reasoning benchmarks.

For RLHF to improve general helpfulness and chat capabilities, the team uses iterative online RPO to maximize the reward predicted by Llama-3.1-Nemotron-70B-Reward over prompts from HelpSteer2. For each iteration, the learning rate $\alpha$ is $4 \times 10^{-7}$, KL penalty $\beta$ is $1 \times 10^{-5}$, reward scale $\eta$ is 3.0, and batch size is 64, training for 500 steps. Two iterations of online RPO increase the Arena Hard score from 69.1 to 88.1. Interestingly, this process also improves the model's performance on all other adopted benchmarks except IFEval, suggesting that RLHF helps the model better utilize its existing knowledge and skills even though neither the dataset nor the reward model is optimized for math, coding, science, or function calling.

The same process is followed for LN-Ultra except that GRPO is employed, sampling 8 responses per prompt and training for 30 steps with a learning rate of $3 \times 10^{-7}$, batch size of 288, and KL penalty $\beta$ of $1 \times 10^{-3}$. For LN-Nano, two rounds of offline RPO with on-policy data are conducted: the first round uses a mixture of reasoning and non-reasoning data with appropriate system prompts to improve reasoning control, followed by a second round with on-policy generations targeting instruction following improvements. For each RPO round, training proceeds up to 400 steps with a learning rate $\alpha$ of $7 \times 10^{-7}$, KL penalty $\beta$ of $3 \times 10^{-2}$, and batch size of 512.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the fundamental contributions of Llama-Nemotron beyond the pipeline mechanics described in Section 3. We distinguish between **incremental improvements** (refinements of existing techniques) and **fundamental innovations** (new capabilities or architectural paradigms). The Llama-Nemotron work contains both, but its most significant contributions lie in resolving previously unresolved tensions in reasoning model design.

---

### 4.1 Dynamic Reasoning Toggle: A New Capability Paradigm

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental capability innovation

**What Makes It Different:** Prior reasoning models—including DeepSeek-R1 and OpenAI o1—operate in a single mode: they always engage in extended chain-of-thought processing. This creates a rigid deployment model where every query incurs the latency and token cost of deep reasoning, regardless of whether the task requires it. Some practitioners work around this by maintaining two separate models (one for chat, one for reasoning), but this doubles deployment complexity and memory footprint.

Llama-Nemotron introduces the **first open-source models to support dynamic reasoning mode switching at inference time** via a lightweight system prompt (`"detailed thinking on/off"`). This is not merely a prompting trick—the model is explicitly trained on paired data where each prompt has both a reasoning and non-reasoning response, teaching it to modulate its internal processing based on the instruction.

**Why It Matters:**

- **Economic Efficiency:** Users can allocate inference resources judiciously. Simple queries use the fast path; complex problems trigger deep reasoning. This directly addresses the cost concern raised in the Introduction: *"Not all queries benefit from detailed multi-step reasoning—such responses may be unnecessarily verbose or even counterproductive."*
- **Deployment Simplicity:** A single model serves both use cases, reducing operational overhead compared to maintaining separate chat and reasoning models.
- **User Control:** End users gain explicit control over response style, which is critical for applications where verbosity must be bounded (e.g., customer-facing chatbots, real-time agents).

**Evidence of Success:** Table 3 shows LN-Nano achieving 79.29% IFEval in reasoning-off mode versus 69.9% in reasoning-on mode, demonstrating the model successfully learns distinct behavioral modes. Similarly, Table 5 shows LN-Ultra maintaining 89.5% IFEval in reasoning-off mode while achieving 76.0% GPQA-Diamond in reasoning-on mode—performance that would be impossible if the model could not distinguish between modes.

**Limitation:** The paper does not report ablation studies isolating the toggle mechanism's contribution versus general SFT improvements. It remains unclear how much performance derives from the paired training data versus the system prompt itself.

---

### 4.2 Heterogeneous Architecture Co-Design: Efficiency as a Training Objective

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental architectural innovation

**What Makes It Different:** Traditional model optimization follows a sequential pipeline: train a dense model first, then apply post-hoc compression (quantization, pruning, distillation). This approach treats efficiency as a secondary concern, often resulting in quality degradation that is difficult to recover. The Puzzle framework inverts this paradigm by making efficiency a **primary optimization objective during architecture search**, before any reasoning-specific training occurs.

The key insight is that **heterogeneous architectures**—where different layers use different block types (attention removal, FFN compression, no-op substitutions)—can achieve better accuracy-efficiency tradeoffs than homogeneous models. This is non-obvious: one might expect architectural inconsistency to harm model coherence. However, the recovery training phase (Section 3.2) demonstrates that quality loss from NAS is recoverable through targeted distillation and continued pretraining.

**Why It Matters:**

- **Pareto Frontier Shift:** Figure 4 shows LN-Ultra dominating both DeepSeek-R1 and Llama-3.1-405B on the accuracy-throughput curve. This is not incremental improvement—it represents a qualitative shift where efficiency gains do not require accuracy sacrifices.
- **Hardware-Aware Design:** By constraining the NAS search to specific deployment targets (e.g., single 8×H100 node for LN-Ultra), the models are purpose-built for real-world infrastructure rather than abstract parameter counts.
- **FFN Fusion Novelty:** The FFN Fusion technique (Section 2) is a new compression method that exploits a structural property emerging after attention removal: consecutive FFN blocks can be merged into fewer, wider layers executed in parallel. This reduces sequential depth without compromising expressivity—a contribution distinct from standard model compression.

**Evidence of Success:** Table 1 shows LN-Ultra after recovery training (before SFT/RL) already surpassing Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct on MATH500 (80.4% vs. 69.6%) and HumanEval (88.4% vs. 86.0%), despite having undergone aggressive architectural modification. This confirms that efficiency optimization and capability preservation are not mutually exclusive when co-designed.

**Limitation:** The paper does not release the full block library or MIP solver configuration, limiting reproducibility of the NAS process. Additionally, the approach requires significant compute for the architecture search phase, which may be prohibitive for smaller teams.

---

### 4.3 Reinforcement Learning Beyond the Distillation Ceiling

**Innovation Type:** Fundamental training insight

**What Makes It Different:** A well-understood limitation of knowledge distillation is that student models cannot exceed teacher performance on the distilled tasks—they are bounded by the teacher's capabilities. The paper explicitly acknowledges this: *"Using supervised fine-tuning, LN-Ultra can approach the performance of DeepSeek-R1 but not exceed it."* Prior open-source reasoning models (including DeepSeek-R1 distilled variants) accept this ceiling as inherent to the approach.

Llama-Nemotron demonstrates that **large-scale reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards enables students to surpass their teachers** on reasoning tasks. This is not guaranteed: the paper notes that RL yields suboptimal results for smaller models (LN-Nano, LN-Super), so it is applied exclusively to LN-Ultra. The key enablers are curriculum training (progressively increasing difficulty) and data filtering (discarding prompts with pass rates ≥0.75 to ensure adequate challenge).

**Why It Matters:**

- **Breaking the Ceiling:** Table 5 shows LN-Ultra achieving 76.0% on GPQA-Diamond versus DeepSeek-R1's 71.5%—a 4.5 percentage point gap that SFT alone could not close. This validates RL as essential for state-of-the-art reasoning performance, not merely optional refinement.
- **Curriculum Design Insight:** Figure 6 demonstrates that curriculum training (vs. random sampling) stabilizes RL and achieves higher final accuracy. This is a transferable insight for future reasoning model training.
- **Verifiable Rewards:** Using accuracy-based rewards (ground truth matching) rather than learned reward models avoids reward hacking and provides stable training signals for mathematical and scientific reasoning.

**Evidence of Success:** Figure 5 shows GPQA-Diamond accuracy improving from ~0.60 to ~0.76 over 500 training steps, with the steepest gains occurring after the initial SFT plateau. Table 5 confirms LN-Ultra-SFT achieves 66.4% GPQA-Diamond, while post-RL LN-Ultra reaches 76.0%—a 9.6 percentage point improvement attributable to RL.

**Limitation:** The paper does not ablate the contribution of curriculum training versus data filtering, so the relative importance of each technique remains unclear. Additionally, RL training required 140k H100 hours—a resource barrier for most research groups.

---

### 4.4 FP8 Generation Infrastructure: Enabling Large-Scale RL

**Innovation Type:** Enabling technical innovation (incremental but critical)

**What Makes It Different:** While FP8 inference is not novel in isolation, applying it to **reinforcement learning training loops at this scale** is a significant engineering contribution. The generation stage dominates RL step time, and the team implements custom vLLM weight loaders that cast BF16 training weights to FP8 at runtime without materializing the full BF16 inference engine (which would cause OOM errors).

This is distinct from standard FP8 quantization because it operates within a training loop where weights change every step, requiring dynamic conversion rather than static quantization.

**Why It Matters:**

- **Throughput Enablement:** The team observes 32 tokens/s/GPU/prompt peak throughput—a 1.8× speedup over BF16. Without this optimization, the 140k H100-hour training run would have required ~252k hours, potentially making the project infeasible.
- **Memory Budget Relief:** FP8 generation reduces memory usage enough to re-enable vLLM's cudagraph feature (disabled under BF16 due to tight memory budgets), contributing an additional 0.4× speedup.
- **Reproducibility:** The training codebases (NeMo, NeMo-Aligner, Megatron-LM) are open-sourced, allowing other teams to replicate this infrastructure for their own large-scale RL experiments.

**Evidence of Success:** Section 5.2.3 reports the 1.8× generation speedup and notes this is *"to our knowledge the highest decoding throughput observed in reasoning training at this scale."* The successful completion of 500 RL steps without OOM errors validates the memory management approach.

**Limitation:** This is an engineering contribution rather than a scientific insight. The techniques are specific to the NVIDIA hardware stack (H100 GPUs, vLLM, Megatron-LM) and may not transfer directly to other infrastructure.

---

### 4.5 Summary: Incremental vs. Fundamental Contributions

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| Dynamic Reasoning Toggle | Fundamental | First open model with inference-time mode switching |
| Heterogeneous NAS (Puzzle) | Fundamental | Shifts accuracy-efficiency Pareto frontier |
| RL Beyond Distillation | Fundamental | Enables student models to surpass teachers |
| FFN Fusion | Fundamental | New compression technique for consecutive FFN layers |
| FP8 RL Infrastructure | Incremental (Enabling) | Makes large-scale RL training feasible |
| Synthetic Data Curation | Incremental | Refines existing pipelines at larger scale |

The Llama-Nemotron work's most enduring contributions are the **dynamic reasoning toggle** (a new capability paradigm) and the **co-design of efficiency and reasoning** (an architectural paradigm shift). The RL and infrastructure innovations, while critical to achieving state-of-the-art results, build on established techniques from DeepSeek-R1 and prior NAS work. The fundamental insight is that reasoning models need not sacrifice efficiency for capability—when architecture, training, and deployment constraints are co-optimized from the start, both can improve simultaneously.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the Llama-Nemotron team validated their claims through systematic evaluation. Understanding the experimental design is critical because reasoning models introduce unique evaluation challenges—traditional benchmarks may not capture the quality of chain-of-thought processing, and the dynamic reasoning toggle requires assessing two distinct behavioral modes per model.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The evaluation framework spans three categories of benchmarks, each targeting different capabilities. The team's methodology reflects careful attention to contamination control and fair comparison conditions.

**Reasoning Benchmarks** test deep multi-step problem-solving:

- **GPQA-Diamond**: A graduate-level science benchmark designed to be "Google-proof"—questions are difficult enough that search engines cannot easily find answers. This is the primary metric for scientific reasoning capability.
- **AIME 2024 and AIME 2025**: Competition-level mathematics problems from the American Invitational Mathematics Examination. AIME25 is split into two parts (AIME25-I with 15 problems, AIME25-II with 15 problems). LN-Nano is evaluated on AIME25-I only, while LN-Super and LN-Ultra are evaluated on the full 30-question set. The paper notes AIME25 was released recently, making it less likely to overlap with training data—stronger performance here indicates better generalization.
- **MATH500**: A benchmark of 500 competition math problems with step-by-step solutions.
- **LiveCodeBench**: A contamination-free coding benchmark with questions indexed by date. Results are reported on two specific ranges—24.08–25.02 and 24.10–25.02—to enable fair comparison with previously reported baselines.

**Non-Reasoning Benchmarks** assess general assistant capabilities:

- **IFEval (Strict-Instruction)**: Measures instruction-following ability with verifiable constraints (e.g., "write exactly 3 paragraphs").
- **BFCL V2 Live**: Evaluates tool use via function calling.
- **Arena-Hard**: Measures alignment with human conversational preferences through head-to-head comparisons.

**Out-of-Distribution Evaluation** tests generalization beyond training domains:

- **JudgeBench**: Evaluates the model's ability to differentiate between high-quality and low-quality responses across Knowledge, Reasoning, Math, and Coding subtasks.

**Evaluation Setup Details:**

All evaluations are conducted at **32k context length**, even though training used maximum context lengths of 16k for LN-Super and 24k for LN-Ultra. The paper explicitly notes this choice:

> "We observed consistent improvements when evaluating at expanded context lengths, as shorter limits can truncate long reasoning traces and lead to incomplete generations—particularly on benchmarks that require multi-step reasoning."

For **reasoning-on** evaluations, the team uses temperature 0.6 and top-p 0.95. For **reasoning-off** evaluations, they use temperature 0 (greedy decoding). Up to 16 completions are generated per prompt, and the paper reports average pass@1 accuracy. Checkpoints are selected based on performance on a subset of reasoning benchmarks.

The paper acknowledges an important limitation in evaluation reliability:

> "As observed in prior works (Moshkov et al., 2025), evaluation on reasoning-heavy tasks such as AIME can exhibit high variance due to small dataset size and generation randomness. Reported numbers may vary across repeated runs or sampling strategies."

This transparency is notable—many papers omit discussion of evaluation variance, but the team explicitly flags that AIME scores should be interpreted with caution.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

The results are organized by model size, with each table comparing the Llama-Nemotron variant to relevant baselines in its weight class.

#### LN-Nano (8B Class)

Table 3 presents LN-Nano's performance against comparably sized models. The key finding is that an 8B model can achieve reasoning capabilities previously associated with much larger systems.

| Benchmark | LN-Nano (on) | LN-Nano (off) | DeepSeek-R1 Distilled-Llama-8B | Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct |
|-----------|--------------|---------------|--------------------------------|----------------------|
| GPQA-Diamond | 54.1% | 39.4% | 49.0% | 25.3% |
| AIME24 | 61.3% | 3.0% | 50.4% | 10.0% |
| AIME25-I | 47.1% | 0.0% | 40.0% | 10.0% |
| MATH500 | 95.4% | 36.6% | 89.1% | 50.4% |
| IFEval | 79.29% | 82.1% | 73.4% | 81.8% |

Several observations stand out:

- **Reasoning toggle effectiveness**: The dramatic difference between reasoning-on and reasoning-off modes confirms the model successfully learned distinct behavioral patterns. On AIME24, reasoning-on achieves 61.3% while reasoning-off drops to 3.0%—a 58.3 percentage point gap that demonstrates the model is not simply always reasoning.
- **Surpassing distilled baselines**: LN-Nano exceeds DeepSeek-R1 Distilled-Llama-8B on GPQA-Diamond (54.1% vs. 49.0%) and MATH500 (95.4% vs. 89.1%), despite both being 8B models derived from Llama 3.
- **IFEval trade-off**: Reasoning-on mode achieves 79.29% on IFEval versus 82.1% in reasoning-off mode, confirming that deep reasoning can slightly degrade strict instruction following—a trade-off the team addresses through dedicated RL (Section 6.1).

The LN-Nano-SFT variant (before RL and preference optimization) shows lower IFEval scores (69.9%), confirming that the final RPO stages successfully recovered instruction-following capabilities.

#### LN-Super (49B Class)

Table 4 compares LN-Super to models in the 70B weight class, including Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct (its base model) and DeepSeek-R1-Distilled-Llama-70B.

| Benchmark | LN-Super (on) | LN-Super (off) | DeepSeek-R1-Distilled-70B | Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| GPQA-Diamond | 66.7% | 50.0% | 65.2% | 50.5% |
| AIME24 | 67.5% | 16.7% | 70.0% | 25.8% |
| AIME25 | 60.0% | 16.7% | 55.0% | 6.7% |
| MATH500 | 96.6% | 74.0% | 94.5% | 73.8% |
| IFEval | 89.2% | 89.0% | 85.1% | 92.1% |
| Arena Hard | 88.3 | — | 65.4 | 72.9 |

Key findings:

- **Competitive reasoning**: LN-Super matches or exceeds DeepSeek-R1-Distilled-70B on GPQA-Diamond (66.7% vs. 65.2%) and MATH500 (96.6% vs. 94.5%), while being optimized for single-GPU deployment.
- **Exceptional conversational ability**: The Arena Hard score of 88.3 beats proprietary models including Claude 3.5 Sonnet and GPT-4o-2024-05-13, as well as much larger open models like Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct (72.9). This is remarkable for a 49B model.
- **Reasoning-off parity**: In reasoning-off mode, LN-Super performs on par with Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct on most benchmarks (e.g., 50.0% vs. 50.5% on GPQA-Diamond), confirming that the efficiency optimizations did not degrade baseline capabilities.

The paper notes one area of underperformance: **LiveCodeBench (2408–2502)** shows LN-Super at 45.5% (reasoning-on) versus DeepSeek-R1-Distilled-70B at 57.5%. The team attributes this to LN-Super's SFT phase being conducted on an earlier version of the dataset, unlike LN-Nano and LN-Ultra. They plan to address this in a future model refresh.

An important trade-off is explicitly discussed:

> "Our experimental results reveal another trade-off: optimizing for instruction following (as measured by IFEval) can compromise conversationality (as measured by Arena-Hard), and conversely, prioritizing conversationality may detract from instruction following performance."

To address this, the team applied model merging to LN-Super, selecting a checkpoint from the Pareto frontier that balances these objectives. However, due to mixed outcomes, this approach was not adopted for other models.

#### LN-Ultra (253B Class)

Table 5 presents the flagship model's results against the strongest open-weight competitors. This is the most critical table for validating the paper's central claims.

| Benchmark | LN-Ultra (on) | LN-Ultra (off) | DeepSeek-R1 | Llama-4 Behemoth | Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|-------------|------------------|-------------------------|
| GPQA-Diamond | 76.0% | 56.6% | 71.5% | 73.7% | 43.4% |
| AIME24 | 80.8% | 20.0% | 79.8% | — | 20.0% |
| AIME25 | 72.5% | 16.7% | 70.0% | — | 0.0% |
| MATH500 | 97.0% | 80.4% | 97.3% | 95.0% | 66.2% |
| IFEval | 88.9% | 89.5% | 88.8% | — | 89.2% |
| Arena Hard | 87.0 | — | 92.0 | — | 66.2 |

The GPQA-Diamond result of **76.0%** is the paper's headline claim—LN-Ultra surpasses DeepSeek-R1's 71.5% by 4.5 percentage points. This gap is significant because:

1. GPQA-Diamond is considered one of the most challenging science benchmarks available.
2. The improvement comes after RL training (LN-Ultra-SFT achieves only 66.4%), confirming that RL is essential for surpassing teacher performance.
3. LN-Ultra achieves this while fitting on a single 8×H100 node, whereas DeepSeek-R1 requires 8×H200.

Figure 2 provides a visual summary of LN-Ultra's performance across all benchmarks, showing it leads among open models on most reasoning tasks while remaining competitive on non-reasoning benchmarks.

Figure 4 illustrates the accuracy-throughput trade-off, plotting GPQA-Diamond accuracy against processing throughput (tokens/s) for two settings:
- **S1**: 500 input sequence length / 2000 output sequence length
- **S2**: 5000 input sequence length / 500 output sequence length

Both settings use 250 concurrent users with models served in FP8. LN-Ultra consistently outperforms both DeepSeek-R1 and Llama-3.1-405B on the Pareto curve—achieving higher accuracy AND higher throughput simultaneously. The paper notes LN-Ultra achieves a **1.71× latency improvement** over Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct after FFN Fusion.

This is meaningful because JudgeBench was not part of the training pipeline—the models were not optimized for judging capability. Strong performance here suggests the reasoning and alignment training transferred to unseen task types.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

The paper includes several ablation studies that provide insight into which components drive performance improvements.

#### Curriculum Training for RL (Figure 6)

Figure 6 compares curriculum training versus random sampling during RL. The y-axis shows GPQA-D Avg@4 accuracy, and the x-axis shows training steps (0–200).

- **Curriculum training** (blue line): Accuracy steadily increases from ~0.55 to ~0.72 over 200 steps, with stable progression.
- **Random sampling** (orange line): Accuracy fluctuates more and plateaus around ~0.65, never reaching the curriculum trajectory.

The paper explains the curriculum strategy in Section 5.1: batches are dynamically allocated based on a target distribution of pass rates, progressing from high pass rates (easier examples) in initial batches to low pass rates (harder examples) in later batches. This ablation confirms that curriculum design is not optional—it materially impacts final performance.

However, the paper does not ablate the contribution of curriculum training versus data filtering (discarding prompts with pass rate ≥0.75). The relative importance of each technique remains unclear—a limitation for practitioners seeking to replicate the approach with limited resources.

#### Data Scaling for Code (Section 3.1.2)

An ablation study scaled the code reasoning dataset from 25k to 736k samples, showing continuous improvement without plateauing. The paper notes:

> "Initial scaling (25k–100k) provides gains, but focusing generation on harder problems from CodeContests before expanding to the full question set yields the most significant performance boosts."

This finding challenges some recent studies suggesting small datasets suffice for inducing reasoning. The scaling curve not plateauing emphasizes the importance of large, diverse, and challenging problem sets for advancing code generation capabilities.

#### SFT Checkpoint Selection for RL (Section 7.4)

The team found a trade-off between the extent of SFT training and the success likelihood of subsequent RL:

> "Although we had access to SFT checkpoints with higher benchmark scores, we initialized RL from an earlier checkpoint to improve final RL outcomes."

This is a non-obvious insight: starting RL from a slightly weaker SFT checkpoint can yield better final performance. The paper does not provide quantitative details on this ablation (e.g., which checkpoint was selected, what the score difference was), which limits reproducibility.

#### Learning Rate Sensitivity (Section 4.2)

For LN-Ultra SFT, initial ablation runs indicated that higher learning rates such as $5 \times 10^{-5}$ generally improve outcomes, but consistently high learning rates caused training instability including gradient explosions. The team implemented a linear warmup to $1 \times 10^{-5}$, followed by cosine decay to $1 \times 10^{-6}$ with a warmup ratio of 10%. Despite these measures, training encountered gradient explosions after the first epoch, requiring training resumption with reinitialized optimizer states.

This detail is important for practitioners: even with careful learning rate scheduling, large-scale reasoning SFT can encounter numerical instability that requires intervention.

### 5.4 Assessment: Do Experiments Support the Claims?

The experimental evidence largely supports the paper's central claims, with some caveats.

**Claim 1: LN-Ultra surpasses DeepSeek-R1 on reasoning benchmarks.**

**Supported.** Table 5 shows LN-Ultra achieving 76.0% on GPQA-Diamond versus DeepSeek-R1's 71.5%. The 4.5 percentage point gap is statistically meaningful on a benchmark of this difficulty. Figure 5 confirms this improvement came through RL training, not SFT alone (LN-Ultra-SFT achieves 66.4%).

**Claim 2: LN models offer superior inference efficiency.**

**Supported.** Figure 4 shows LN-Ultra dominating the accuracy-throughput Pareto curve. Section 2.1 quantifies this: LN-Ultra achieves 1.71× latency improvement over Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct and fits on a single 8×H100 node versus DeepSeek-R1's 8×H200 requirement. LN-Super achieves 5× throughput speedup over Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct at batch size 256 and TP1.

**Claim 3: Dynamic reasoning toggle enables mode switching without separate models.**

**Supported.** Tables 3, 4, and 5 consistently show large performance gaps between reasoning-on and reasoning-off modes across all model sizes. For example, LN-Nano achieves 54.1% on GPQA-Diamond (on) versus 39.4% (off)—a 14.7 percentage point difference. This confirms the model learned distinct behaviors rather than always reasoning.

**Claim 4: RL enables students to surpass teachers.**

**Supported.** Table 5 shows LN-Ultra-SFT at 66.4% GPQA-Diamond (approaching but not exceeding DeepSeek-R1's 71.5%), while post-RL LN-Ultra reaches 76.0%. The 9.6 percentage point improvement from RL validates that distillation alone has a ceiling that RL can break.

**Limitations and Unanswered Questions:**

1. **No toggle ablation**: The paper does not isolate the toggle mechanism's contribution versus general SFT improvements. It remains unclear how much performance derives from the paired training data versus the system prompt itself.

2. **Limited RL ablation**: The paper does not ablate curriculum training versus data filtering, so the relative importance of each RL technique is unknown.

3. **Evaluation variance acknowledged but not quantified**: The paper notes AIME evaluation can exhibit high variance but does not report confidence intervals or multiple-run averages.

4. **LiveCodeBench underperformance**: LN-Super underperforms on LiveCodeBench due to dataset version mismatch. This is acknowledged but not resolved, leaving uncertainty about coding capability claims for that model.

5. **Compute requirements**: RL training required 140k H100 hours—a resource barrier for most research groups. The paper does not explore whether similar results could be achieved with less compute through alternative training strategies.

### 5.5 Summary of Experimental Findings

The experiments provide robust evidence for the paper's core contributions:

| Claim | Evidence | Strength |
|-------|----------|----------|
| LN-Ultra surpasses DeepSeek-R1 | Table 5: 76.0% vs. 71.5% GPQA-Diamond | Strong |
| Efficiency without accuracy loss | Figure 4: Pareto frontier dominance | Strong |
| Reasoning toggle works | Tables 3–5: Consistent on/off gaps | Strong |
| RL breaks distillation ceiling | Table 5: SFT 66.4% → RL 76.0% | Strong |
| Generalization to unseen tasks | Table 6: JudgeBench performance | Moderate |
| Data scaling matters | Section 3.1.2: Code dataset ablation | Moderate |
| Curriculum RL helps | Figure 6: Curriculum vs. random | Moderate |

The experimental design is thorough for a model release paper, covering multiple model sizes, multiple benchmarks, and multiple evaluation modes. The primary weaknesses are the lack of certain ablations (toggle mechanism, RL components) and the acknowledged evaluation variance on small benchmarks like AIME. Nevertheless, the quantitative results convincingly demonstrate that Llama-Nemotron achieves its stated goals of combining reasoning capability, inference efficiency, and user controllability in a single open-source family.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Llama-Nemotron demonstrates significant advances in reasoning model design, a critical examination reveals several limitations, trade-offs, and open questions. Understanding these constraints is essential for practitioners considering adoption or replication of this approach.

### 6.1 Computational Resource Barriers

The most significant limitation is the **extraordinary compute requirement** for replication. The RL training phase for LN-Ultra alone consumed approximately **140,000 H100 GPU hours** (Section 5.1). To contextualize this:

- At cloud pricing (~$2–3 per H100 hour), this represents **$280,000–$420,000** in compute costs for a single training phase.
- The infrastructure required 72 nodes of 8×H100 GPUs running in coordination (Section 5.2.1).
- Memory management required custom profiling tools and careful orchestration to avoid out-of-memory errors on both GPU and CPU (Section 5.2.2).

This creates a substantial barrier for academic researchers and smaller organizations. While the weights and code are open-sourced, the **training methodology is effectively unreproducible** for most teams. The paper acknowledges this implicitly by noting RL was applied "exclusively to LN-Ultra" due to "resource constraints" (Section 5), but does not explore whether similar gains could be achieved with reduced compute through alternative strategies.

**Implication:** The open-source release provides deployment access but not true methodological accessibility. Teams cannot easily adapt the RL pipeline to their own domains or model sizes without comparable infrastructure.

### 6.2 Reinforcement Learning Applicability Constraints

A notable finding with important limitations is that **RL benefits are size-dependent**. The paper states:

> "Consistent with the findings of DeepSeek-AI et al. (2025), our preliminary experiments indicate that reinforcement learning (RL) yields suboptimal results for smaller models compared to distillation."

This means:

- **LN-Nano (8B)** and **LN-Super (49B)** do not receive reasoning RL training—only LN-Ultra (253B) does.
- Smaller models are capped at teacher performance levels, unable to surpass DeepSeek-R1 through self-improvement.
- The mechanism behind this size dependency is not explained. Is it a capacity limitation? A stability issue? The paper does not ablate this.

**Implication:** Organizations deploying LN-Nano or LN-Super cannot expect the same reasoning breakthroughs as LN-Ultra. The "surpassing teacher" capability is reserved for the largest, most expensive model—potentially limiting the value proposition for edge or cost-constrained deployments.

### 6.3 Evaluation Variance and Benchmark Limitations

The paper explicitly acknowledges evaluation reliability concerns:

> "As observed in prior works (Moshkov et al., 2025), evaluation on reasoning-heavy tasks such as AIME can exhibit high variance due to small dataset size and generation randomness. Reported numbers may vary across repeated runs or sampling strategies."

Specific concerns include:

- **AIME benchmarks** contain only 15–30 problems total. A single problem's outcome can shift accuracy by 3–7 percentage points.
- The paper does not report **confidence intervals**, **multiple-run averages**, or **standard deviations** for any benchmark.
- **LiveCodeBench underperformance** for LN-Super (45.5% vs. 57.5% for DeepSeek-R1-Distilled-70B) is attributed to "SFT phase being conducted on an earlier version of the dataset" (Section 7.3). This suggests results are sensitive to data versioning, raising questions about comparability.

**Implication:** Claimed performance advantages, particularly on small benchmarks like AIME, should be interpreted with caution. The 4.5 percentage point GPQA-Diamond gap (76.0% vs. 71.5%) is more reliable due to GPQA's larger test set, but AIME claims are less robust.

### 6.4 Unablated Design Choices

Several critical design decisions lack ablation studies, leaving their individual contributions unclear:

| Design Choice | Missing Ablation | Impact on Interpretability |
|---------------|------------------|---------------------------|
| **Reasoning Toggle Mechanism** | No isolation of paired training data vs. system prompt contribution | Unclear how much performance derives from the toggle itself vs. general SFT improvements |
| **Curriculum Training vs. Data Filtering** | Both techniques used together; no individual contribution measured (Section 5.3) | Practitioners cannot determine which technique to prioritize under resource constraints |
| **SFT Checkpoint Selection for RL** | Paper notes "earlier checkpoint" was used but provides no quantitative details (Section 7.4) | Limits reproducibility—teams cannot replicate the optimal RL initialization strategy |
| **Model Merging for LN-Super** | Applied to balance IFEval vs. Arena-Hard but "not adopted for other models" due to "mixed outcomes" (Section 7.3) | Unclear why merging worked for one model but not others |

**Implication:** The paper presents a successful pipeline but does not fully disentangle which components are essential versus optional. Teams seeking to adapt the approach must experimentally determine which techniques matter for their use case.

### 6.5 Instruction Following vs. Conversationality Trade-off

A significant tension emerges between two desirable capabilities:

> "Our experimental results reveal another trade-off: optimizing for instruction following (as measured by IFEval) can compromise conversationality (as measured by Arena-Hard), and conversely, prioritizing conversationality may detract from instruction following performance." (Section 7.3)

Evidence from Table 4 shows this trade-off in practice:

- **LN-Super** achieves 89.2% IFEval (reasoning-on) but required model merging to balance Arena-Hard (88.3).
- **LN-Ultra** achieves 88.9% IFEval but only 87.0 Arena-Hard—lower than LN-Super despite being a larger model.
- The RLHF phase improved Arena-Hard from 69.1 to 88.1 for LN-Super but the paper notes this process improved "all other adopted benchmarks **except IFEval**" (Section 6.2).

**Implication:** There is no single "best" checkpoint for all use cases. Organizations must select models based on their priority: strict instruction adherence (IFEval) or human-preferred conversationality (Arena-Hard). The paper does not provide guidance on navigating this trade-off beyond model merging, which had "mixed outcomes."

### 6.6 Hardware and Infrastructure Specificity

The efficiency optimizations are **tightly coupled to NVIDIA's hardware stack**:

- **FP8 generation** relies on vLLM's online FP8 mode with per-token activation scaling (Section 5.2.3).
- **Memory management** assumes H100 GPUs with 2TB CPU DRAM per node and 1TB `/dev/shm` allocation (Section 5.2.2).
- **Parallelism configuration** (tensor parallel=8, pipeline parallel=18, context parallel=2) is tuned for 8×H100 nodes (Section 5.2.1).

The paper states:

> "Because vLLM does not support directly initializing models in FP8, we also implement meta-weight tensor initialization to avoid materializing the full BF16 inference engine."

This custom engineering is not portable to other inference engines or hardware platforms (e.g., AMD GPUs, TPU clusters, or non-NVIDIA cloud providers).

**Implication:** Teams not using NVIDIA H100 infrastructure cannot directly replicate the reported throughput gains. The 1.8× generation speedup from FP8 (Section 5.2.3) is specific to this hardware stack.

### 6.7 Context Length Mismatch

A subtle but important discrepancy exists between training and evaluation:

- **LN-Super** trained with maximum context length of **16k tokens** (Section 4.2).
- **LN-Ultra** trained with maximum context length of **24k tokens** (Section 4.2).
- **All evaluations** conducted at **32k context length** (Section 7.1).

The paper justifies this:

> "We observed consistent improvements when evaluating at expanded context lengths, as shorter limits can truncate long reasoning traces and lead to incomplete generations."

However, this creates an **evaluation advantage** that may not reflect real-world deployment. If users deploy the models at their trained context lengths (16k/24k), reasoning performance on long-chain tasks may degrade.

**Implication:** Reported benchmark scores assume 32k context during inference. Organizations deploying at lower context limits should expect reduced performance on reasoning-heavy tasks.

### 6.8 Training Instability and Operational Complexity

The LN-Ultra SFT phase encountered significant stability issues:

> "Despite these measures, training encountered gradient explosions and numerical instability after the first epoch. This required training resumption with reinitialized optimizer states, after which successful convergence was achieved." (Section 4.2)

This reveals:

- Even with careful learning rate scheduling (warmup to $1 \times 10^{-5}$, cosine decay to $1 \times 10^{-6}$), **training failures occurred**.
- Recovery required **manual intervention** (optimizer reinitialization), not an automated process.
- The paper does not specify how many resumption attempts were needed or whether data was reprocessed.

**Implication:** Large-scale reasoning SFT is not a "push-button" process. Teams should anticipate potential training failures and build operational procedures for recovery.

### 6.9 Unreleased Components Limiting Reproducibility

While the paper releases weights, data, and code, several critical components remain unavailable:

- **Full block library** from the Puzzle NAS phase is not released (Section 4.2 notes this limitation).
- **MIP solver configuration** for architecture assembly is not detailed.
- **Reward model weights** (Llama-3.1-Nemotron-70B-Reward) are referenced but not explicitly confirmed as released in the abstract's release list.
- **Exact SFT checkpoint** used for RL initialization is not specified (Section 7.4).

**Implication:** Full end-to-end replication—from base Llama 3 model to final LN-Ultra—is not possible. Teams can fine-tune the released checkpoints but cannot recreate the architecture search and recovery training pipeline.

### 6.10 Summary of Key Trade-offs

| Trade-off | Description | Mitigation in Paper |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Model Size vs. RL Benefits** | Only LN-Ultra receives reasoning RL; smaller models capped at teacher performance | None acknowledged; accepted as resource constraint |
| **IFEval vs. Arena-Hard** | Optimizing instruction following can degrade conversationality | Model merging applied to LN-Super only |
| **Training Context vs. Evaluation Context** | Models trained at 16k/24k but evaluated at 32k | Justified as necessary for complete reasoning traces |
| **Efficiency vs. Reproducibility** | Hardware-specific optimizations limit portability | Code released but hardware assumptions remain |
| **SFT Extent vs. RL Success** | Higher SFT scores do not guarantee better RL outcomes | Initialized RL from earlier checkpoint (details unspecified) |

### 6.11 Open Questions for Future Work

The paper leaves several questions unanswered that would benefit from future research:

1. **What is the minimum model size for effective reasoning RL?** Is 253B a hard threshold, or can techniques be adapted for 70B-class models?
2. **Can curriculum training benefits be achieved with less compute?** The 140k H100-hour requirement is prohibitive—are there sample-efficient alternatives?
3. **How does the reasoning toggle generalize to non-English languages?** All benchmarks and data curation appear English-focused; multilingual behavior is untested.
4. **What is the long-term stability of heterogeneous architectures?** The Puzzle framework creates non-uniform layer structures; does this affect model behavior over extended fine-tuning or deployment?
5. **Can the NAS search space be made more accessible?** Releasing the block library would enable other teams to explore different efficiency-accuracy tradeoffs without repeating the search.

### 6.12 Fair Assessment

Despite these limitations, the Llama-Nemotron work represents a significant contribution to open-source reasoning models. The limitations identified here are common to large-scale model development and do not invalidate the core claims. Key strengths that offset the limitations include:

- **Transparent acknowledgment** of evaluation variance and training instability (rare in model release papers).
- **Open licensing** enabling enterprise use, which addresses a major gap in prior reasoning models.
- **Complete post-training dataset release**, allowing others to build on the data curation methodology even without replicating NAS.
- **Infrastructure code release** (NeMo, NeMo-Aligner, Megatron-LM), enabling teams to adapt the RL pipeline to their own contexts.

The appropriate interpretation is that Llama-Nemotron **demonstrates feasibility** of efficient, controllable reasoning models—not that it provides a turnkey solution for all deployment scenarios. Teams should evaluate whether their resources, hardware, and use cases align with the model's design constraints before adoption.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The Llama-Nemotron release represents more than a model update—it signals a structural shift in how reasoning models are designed, deployed, and evaluated. This section examines the downstream consequences of this work for the research community, industry practitioners, and future model development.

### 7.1 Field-Level Impact: Reshaping the Reasoning Model Landscape

Llama-Nemotron challenges three entrenched assumptions in the reasoning model space.

**Assumption 1: Efficiency and Reasoning Capability Are Mutually Exclusive**

Prior to this work, the field operated under an implicit trade-off: models optimized for reasoning (like DeepSeek-R1) required substantial hardware, while efficient models sacrificed capability. Figure 4 directly contradicts this by showing LN-Ultra dominating the accuracy-throughput Pareto frontier—achieving **76.0% GPQA-Diamond accuracy** while delivering **1.71× latency improvement** over Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct (Section 2.1).

This shifts the optimization target for future models. Efficiency is no longer a post-hoc concern but a **co-design objective** that must be integrated into architecture search, data curation, and training pipeline design. The Puzzle framework demonstrates that heterogeneous architectures—where different layers use different block types—can outperform homogeneous designs on both metrics simultaneously.

**Assumption 2: Open-Source Models Cannot Surpass Proprietary Teachers**

The distillation ceiling has been accepted as a fundamental limitation: student models cannot exceed teacher performance on distilled tasks. LN-Ultra's **4.5 percentage point gap over DeepSeek-R1 on GPQA-Diamond** (76.0% vs. 71.5% in Table 5) proves this ceiling is breakable through large-scale reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards.

This has two implications:
- Open-source communities can now pursue **self-improvement pipelines** rather than relying solely on distillation from proprietary APIs.
- The **140k H100-hour RL investment** (Section 5.1) becomes a new benchmark for what compute scale is required to push state-of-the-art reasoning—setting expectations for future open-source efforts.

**Assumption 3: Reasoning Models Require Separate Deployments for Chat and Deep Thinking**

The dynamic reasoning toggle introduces a **single-model, dual-mode paradigm** that eliminates the need for maintaining separate chat and reasoning models. Table 3 shows LN-Nano achieving **79.29% IFEval in reasoning-off mode** versus **69.9% in reasoning-on mode**—confirming the model learns genuinely distinct behaviors rather than always engaging in chain-of-thought.

This reduces deployment complexity and operational costs. Organizations can now serve diverse query types through a single endpoint, dynamically allocating compute based on task difficulty rather than model selection.

### 7.2 Research Directions Enabled by This Work

The Llama-Nemotron pipeline opens several concrete avenues for follow-up research.

**Heterogeneous Architecture Exploration**

The Puzzle framework's block library (attention removal, FFN compression, no-op substitutions) was optimized for H100 GPUs under specific latency constraints. Future work could explore:

- **Hardware-specific NAS**: Adapting Puzzle for different accelerator architectures (TPUs, AMD GPUs, edge devices) to determine if heterogeneous benefits transfer across platforms.
- **Dynamic block selection**: Instead of fixed architecture search, could models learn to select block variants at inference time based on input complexity?
- **Block library release**: The paper notes the full block library is not released (Section 6.9). Publishing this would enable the community to explore alternative accuracy-efficiency tradeoffs without repeating the expensive NAS phase.

**Reinforcement Learning Efficiency**

The 140k H100-hour RL requirement is prohibitive for most research groups. Promising directions include:

- **Sample-efficient RL**: Can curriculum training (Figure 6) achieve similar gains with fewer steps? The paper shows curriculum training reaches ~0.72 accuracy by step 200 versus ~0.65 for random sampling—could earlier stopping reduce compute?
- **Smaller-model RL**: The paper notes RL yields "suboptimal results for smaller models" (Section 5), but does not explain why. Understanding the capacity threshold for effective RL could enable reasoning improvements for 8B–70B models.
- **Alternative reward signals**: The current approach uses accuracy rewards (ground truth matching) and format rewards. Could learned reward models or process-based rewards (evaluating reasoning steps, not just final answers) improve sample efficiency?

**Reasoning Toggle Generalization**

The toggle mechanism was trained on paired data with system prompts (`"detailed thinking on/off"`). Open questions include:

- **Multilingual support**: All benchmarks and data curation appear English-focused. Does the toggle generalize to other languages, or does it require language-specific training?
- **Implicit mode detection**: Can models learn to auto-detect when reasoning is needed without explicit prompts, reducing user burden?
- **Granular control**: Beyond binary on/off, could users specify reasoning depth (e.g., `"think briefly"` vs. `"think thoroughly"`)?

**Data Curation Methodologies**

The 33M-sample post-training dataset (Table 2) was carefully filtered for quality, correctness, and contamination. Future work could investigate:

- **Minimal effective dataset size**: The code scaling ablation (Section 3.1.2) showed continuous improvement up to 736k samples without plateauing. Where does diminishing returns begin for math and science domains?
- **Synthetic data quality bounds**: How much does teacher model quality (DeepSeek-R1, Qwen2.5) limit student performance? Could weaker teachers suffice with better filtering?
- **Contamination detection**: The paper uses LLM-based comparison for decontamination (Section 3.1.1). As benchmarks age, can automated methods keep training data clean without manual verification?

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The Llama-Nemotron family enables several deployment scenarios that were previously impractical for open-source models.

**Enterprise Agentic Pipelines**

The combination of **128K context length** (Section 1), **function calling support** (BFCL V2 Live scores in Tables 3–5), and **efficient inference** makes LN-Super and LN-Ultra suitable for multi-step agentic workflows:

- **Code generation and review**: LN-Ultra's 66.3% LiveCodeBench score (24.08–25.02) supports automated code synthesis with reasoning traces that developers can inspect.
- **Scientific document analysis**: The 76.0% GPQA-Diamond score indicates capability for parsing technical literature, extracting claims, and identifying contradictions.
- **Customer support automation**: The reasoning toggle allows simple queries to use fast reasoning-off mode while complex troubleshooting triggers reasoning-on mode, optimizing cost per interaction.

**Research and Education**

The open license (NVIDIA Open Model License Agreement) and released training code (NeMo, NeMo-Aligner, Megatron-LM) enable:

- **Reasoning capability studies**: Researchers can analyze chain-of-thought traces from an open model rather than relying on API-based proprietary systems.
- **Fine-tuning for domain adaptation**: The released post-training dataset provides a baseline for organizations to adapt models to specialized domains (legal, medical, finance) while preserving reasoning capabilities.
- **Evaluation benchmark development**: The JudgeBench results (Table 6) suggest LN-Ultra can serve as a reference judge for evaluating other models' outputs.

**Edge and Cost-Constrained Deployments**

LN-Super's optimization for **single H100 GPU deployment** (Section 2.1) with **5× throughput speedup** over Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct enables:

- **On-premise deployments**: Organizations with data sovereignty requirements can run capable reasoning models locally without cloud API dependencies.
- **Batch processing at scale**: The 300K cached token support (batch size 16 × sequence length 18,750) allows processing large document sets efficiently.
- **Real-time applications**: The 1.71× latency improvement for LN-Ultra makes reasoning-capable responses viable for interactive applications where sub-second latency matters.

### 7.4 Integration Guidance: When to Prefer Llama-Nemotron

Practitioners should consider Llama-Nemotron under the following conditions.

**Choose LN-Ultra When:**

- **Maximum reasoning capability is required**: The 76.0% GPQA-Diamond score leads all open models (Table 5).
- **You have 8×H100 infrastructure**: The model is optimized for this configuration; deploying on smaller clusters will not achieve reported throughput.
- **Scientific or mathematical reasoning is primary**: RL training specifically targeted GPQA and AIME benchmarks (Section 5.1).
- **You need to surpass teacher performance**: If your use case requires capabilities beyond what distillation from DeepSeek-R1 can provide, LN-Ultra's RL phase is essential.

**Choose LN-Super When:**

- **Single-GPU deployment is required**: Optimized for TP1 on one H100 with 5× throughput over Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct (Section 2.1).
- **Conversational quality matters**: Arena Hard score of 88.3 exceeds Claude 3.5 Sonnet and GPT-4o-2024-05-13 (Section 6.2).
- **Cost efficiency is prioritized**: 49B parameters offer strong reasoning (66.7% GPQA-Diamond) at roughly 1/5 the parameter count of LN-Ultra.
- **You can tolerate LiveCodeBench underperformance**: The 45.5% score reflects outdated SFT data; coding tasks may require additional fine-tuning (Section 7.3).

**Choose LN-Nano When:**

- **Edge or memory-constrained deployment**: 8B parameters fit on consumer GPUs or edge devices.
- **Reasoning capability at small scale is needed**: 54.1% GPQA-Diamond exceeds DeepSeek-R1 Distilled-Llama-8B's 49.0% (Table 3).
- **You accept no RL improvements**: LN-Nano does not receive reasoning RL training; capabilities are capped at SFT performance (Section 5).

**Consider Alternatives When:**

- **Non-NVIDIA hardware is your target**: FP8 optimizations and memory management are specific to H100 GPUs (Section 6.6).
- **Multilingual support is required**: All benchmarks and training data appear English-focused; multilingual behavior is untested.
- **You need reproducible training from scratch**: The full NAS block library and MIP solver configuration are not released (Section 6.9).
- **Compute budget is limited**: The 140k H100-hour RL requirement is not feasible for most organizations; consider distillation-only approaches.

### 7.5 Long-Term Trajectory: What This Work Signals

Llama-Nemotron points toward three emerging trends in model development.

**Trend 1: Architecture-Training Co-Design Becomes Standard**

The sequential pipeline of "train first, compress later" is being replaced by integrated approaches where architecture search, recovery training, and capability training are coordinated. Future model releases will likely include efficiency metrics alongside accuracy benchmarks as first-class evaluation criteria.

**Trend 2: Open-Source Reasoning Closes the Proprietary Gap**

With LN-Ultra surpassing DeepSeek-R1 and trailing only o3-mini(high) on JudgeBench (79.14% vs. 80.86% in Table 6), the capability gap between open and closed models is narrowing. The remaining advantages of proprietary systems lie primarily in:
- **Compute scale for RL**: OpenAI and Anthropic likely invest more RL compute than the 140k H100 hours reported here.
- **Data diversity**: Proprietary models may have access to broader training corpora.
- **Multilingual and multimodal capabilities**: Llama-Nemotron focuses on English text reasoning.

**Trend 3: User Controllability Becomes a Differentiator**

The reasoning toggle addresses a real deployment pain point—matching response style to task requirements. Future models will likely expose more control knobs:
- Reasoning depth specification
- Token budget constraints
- Verification strictness levels
- Domain-specific reasoning modes

Future progress will depend on making these techniques more accessible while preserving the quality gains that make Llama-Nemotron competitive with state-of-the-art proprietary systems.
