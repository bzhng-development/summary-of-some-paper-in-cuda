## 1. Executive Summary

Ling 2.0 introduces a series of reasoning-oriented foundation models scaling from 16B to 1 trillion parameters (Ling-1T) using a high-sparsity Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture that activates only ≈3.5% of parameters per token. This approach solves the prohibitive computational cost of dense scaling by achieving 7× active-compute efficiency while maintaining reasoning accuracy through innovations like full-scale FP8 training, reasoning-centric corpora (Ling Math and Ling Code), and Evolutionary Chain-of-Thought (Evo-CoT) post-training. The series establishes a new Pareto frontier on competition-level benchmarks such as AIME 2025, demonstrating that properly aligned sparse activation enables scalable, efficient intelligence for next-generation open systems.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### 2.1 The Reasoning Bottleneck in Large-Scale Language Models

The landscape of large language models has undergone a fundamental shift. Early scaling efforts focused primarily on increasing parameter counts and training data volume, operating under the assumption that capability would emerge naturally from scale alone. However, as models reach hundreds of billions of parameters, the paper identifies a critical inflection point: **performance gains increasingly depend on a model's ability to reason**—to decompose problems, infer hidden relations, and make consistent multi-step deductions (Introduction, paragraph 1).

This observation carries both theoretical and practical significance. Theoretically, the authors position reasoning capability as "the essence of intelligence and the foundation for building general-purpose agents that can understand, decide, and act autonomously" (Introduction, paragraph 1). This reframes the scaling challenge: it is no longer sufficient to simply make models larger; they must be architected to reason reliably at scale. Practically, this matters because real-world applications—from mathematical problem-solving to code generation to scientific discovery—require consistent logical deduction rather than pattern matching alone.

The core problem Ling 2.0 addresses is **how to scale reasoning capability to the trillion-parameter level while maintaining computational efficiency**. This is not a trivial extension of existing approaches. The paper identifies two intertwined challenges:

1. **Efficient Scaling Challenge**: Dense architectures incur prohibitive computational costs at trillion-parameter scales (beyond 10²⁵ FLOPs). Maintaining training stability, predictability, and hardware utilization under extreme scale requires fundamental architectural changes.

2. **Reasoning Enhancement Challenge**: Simply increasing capacity does not guarantee improved reasoning. The paper notes that "transferring learned reasoning behaviors across [pre-training, mid-training, and post-training] stages can introduce instability" (Introduction, paragraph 4). Constructing reasoning-centric training corpora is resource-intensive, and ensuring sustained improvement across all training phases remains difficult.

### 2.2 Limitations of Prior Approaches

To understand Ling 2.0's positioning, we must examine what existing approaches achieved and where they fall short.

**Dense Model Scaling.** Traditional dense architectures activate all parameters for every token. While this approach powered earlier generations of LLMs, it becomes economically and computationally prohibitive at trillion-parameter scales. The computational cost grows linearly with parameter count, making training and inference increasingly expensive without proportional capability gains.

**Early Sparse Approaches.** Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures emerged as a solution, allowing models to scale total parameters while activating only a subset per token. However, prior MoE implementations faced several limitations:

- **Suboptimal Sparsity Ratios**: Many existing MoE models did not systematically explore the relationship between activation ratio and efficiency. The Ling 2.0 paper notes that their analysis reveals "activation ratio is the primary driver of efficiency" following a "robust power law" (Section 2.3.2), yet prior work lacked unified scaling laws to guide this design choice.

- **Load Balancing Overhead**: Traditional MoE routing required auxiliary loss terms to balance expert utilization, adding complexity and potential training instability. Ling 2.0 adopts an "aux-loss-free load balancing strategy" inspired by DeepSeek-V3, but extends it with modified bias update mechanisms (Section 2.2).

- **Limited Reasoning Optimization**: Existing sparse models optimized for general language capability rather than reasoning-specific performance. The activation patterns and training objectives were not aligned with multi-step deduction tasks.

**Recent Trillion-Scale Efforts.** The paper references two notable contemporaries:

- **Kimi-K2** (Moonshot-AI, 2025): A trillion-scale model that "focuses primarily on enhancing agentic capability" (Introduction, paragraph 2). While impressive in scale, Kimi-K2 does not emphasize reasoning optimization as its primary design principle.

- **DeepSeek-V3** (DeepSeek-AI, 2024): A 671B parameter model achieving "outstanding reasoning performance under efficient sparse scaling" (Introduction, paragraph 2). DeepSeek-V3 demonstrated the viability of sparse scaling but operates at a smaller scale than Ling 2.0's trillion-parameter target.

The gap Ling 2.0 identifies is clear: **no existing open model simultaneously achieves trillion-parameter scale, high-sparsity efficiency, and reasoning-optimized training across all phases**. Kimi-K2 has scale but not reasoning focus; DeepSeek-V3 has reasoning and sparsity but not trillion-scale capacity.

### 2.3 The Scaling Prediction Problem

A subtle but critical challenge in trillion-parameter model development is **reliable performance prediction**. Training a 1T parameter model requires computational resources so substantial that experimental iteration becomes prohibitively expensive. The paper notes that validation costs must be reduced to "under 1% of a full training run" to enable practical innovation cycles (Introduction, Model Architecture bullet 1).

Prior approaches to scaling prediction relied on extrapolating from smaller models using power-law relationships (e.g., Chinchua scaling laws). However, these laws were primarily developed for dense architectures and do not account for:

- The non-linear effects of sparsity on training dynamics
- The interaction between activation ratio, expert granularity, and compute budget
- The different hyperparameter optima for MoE versus dense models (Section 2.3.1 notes that "at larger compute scales, MoEs tend to use larger batch sizes and relatively lower learning rate")

Without reliable scaling laws specific to high-sparsity MoE architectures, developers face a dilemma: either make architectural decisions based on dense-model heuristics (risking suboptimal performance) or conduct prohibitively expensive full-scale experiments for each design choice.

### 2.4 The Infrastructure-Algorithm Co-Design Gap

Another underexplored dimension in prior work is the **tight coupling between algorithmic design and system infrastructure**. The paper emphasizes that "algorithm design alone is insufficient to achieve our objectives. Without any engineering optimizations, this highly sparse MoE architecture offers no performance advantage over dense models" (Section 5, paragraph 2).

Specific infrastructure challenges at trillion scale include:

- **FP8 Training Stability**: Full-scale training in 8-bit precision reduces memory and computation costs but introduces quantization errors that can destabilize training. Prior work either avoided FP8 entirely or lacked systematic safeguards for precision tracking (Section 5.1).

- **Heterogeneous Pipeline Parallelism**: The introduction of Multi-Token Prediction (MTP) layers and First-K-Dense strategies creates computational heterogeneity across pipeline stages. Standard 1F1B scheduling assumes homogeneous layer costs, leading to "pipeline bubbles" that reduce throughput (Section 5.2).

- **Expert Parallelism Communication**: Higher sparsity and increased expert counts (256 experts in Ling 2.0) amplify communication overhead in distributed training. Prior optimizations like DeepEP focused on cross-node communication but left intra-node optimization opportunities unexplored (Section 5.3.1).

The paper positions infrastructure not as an afterthought but as a first-class design constraint. The "4C principle" (Correct, Consistent, Complete, and Co-Design) introduced in Section 5.4 formalizes this philosophy, treating algorithm-system co-design as essential rather than optional.

### 2.5 Ling 2.0's Positioning

Relative to existing work, Ling 2.0 positions itself along three dimensions:

| Dimension | Prior Work | Ling 2.0 |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Scale** | Up to 671B (DeepSeek-V3) or agentic-focused 1T (Kimi-K2) | 1T parameters with reasoning optimization |
| **Sparsity** | Variable activation ratios, often >10% | ~3.5% activation (8 of 256 experts + 1 shared) |
| **Training Precision** | Primarily BF16 or mixed precision | Full-scale FP8 with systematic safeguards |
| **Reasoning Integration** | Reasoning as post-training add-on | Reasoning embedded across pre-training, mid-training, and post-training |
| **Scaling Prediction** | Dense-model laws or limited MoE experiments | Unified Ling Scaling Laws with "Wind Tunnel" validation |

The paper's central thesis is that **sparse activation, when properly aligned with reasoning objectives, enables scalable and efficient intelligence** (Abstract). This is not merely an engineering achievement but a methodological contribution: the Ling Scaling Laws and Wind Tunnel experimental framework provide a reproducible blueprint for future trillion-scale development.

Importantly, Ling 2.0 releases three models at different scales (16B, 103B, 1T) to demonstrate **cross-scale consistency**—the principle that architectural decisions validated at small scales should extrapolate predictably to larger scales. This addresses a critical gap in prior work where small-scale ablations did not reliably predict trillion-scale behavior.

### 2.6 Why This Matters

The significance of Ling 2.0 extends beyond benchmark scores. By establishing an open, efficient foundation for reasoning models, the work enables:

1. **Democratized Access**: Trillion-parameter capability at 7× efficiency leverage means organizations without hyperscale resources can potentially fine-tune or deploy reasoning-optimized models.

2. **Research Reproducibility**: The release of code, model weights, and detailed methodology (Sections 2-5) provides a reference implementation for future scaling research.

3. **Foundation for Agentic Systems**: The paper notes that the Ring series builds upon Ling 2.0 for "deep thinking models" (Introduction, paragraph 8). Efficient reasoning foundations enable more capable autonomous agents.

4. **Methodological Advancement**: The Ling Scaling Laws and Wind Tunnel framework offer a generalizable approach to trillion-scale development that transcends this specific model family.

In summary, Ling 2.0 addresses the convergence of three critical challenges—scale, efficiency, and reasoning capability—that prior work treated separately. By integrating solutions across architecture, training methodology, and infrastructure, the paper positions itself as a comprehensive blueprint for next-generation open intelligent systems rather than an incremental improvement on existing models.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

Ling 2.0 is a family of three language models (16B, 103B, and 1 trillion parameters) built on a high-sparsity Mixture-of-Experts architecture that activates only about 3.5% of its parameters for each token processed. The system solves the problem of scaling reasoning capability to trillion-parameter levels while maintaining computational efficiency by integrating architectural sparsity, reasoning-optimized training data, and infrastructure innovations like full-scale FP8 precision training.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The Ling 2.0 system consists of four interconnected layers that process information sequentially from data ingestion to final output. First, the **Data Pipeline** curates and processes training corpora (general knowledge, Ling Math, Ling Code, multilingual content) through cleaning, filtering, and rewriting stages to produce 20 trillion tokens of training data. Second, the **Model Architecture** processes each token through a Mixture-of-Experts transformer with 256 routed experts where only 8 experts plus 1 shared expert activate per token, augmented by Multi-Token Prediction layers that predict multiple future tokens simultaneously. Third, the **Training Infrastructure** executes distributed training across thousands of GPUs using FP8 precision, heterogeneous pipeline parallelism, and the Warmup-Stable-Merge scheduler to manage learning rates without traditional decay. Fourth, the **Post-Training Pipeline** refines the base model through Decoupled Fine-Tuning with differentiated system prompts, Evolutionary Chain-of-Thought reinforcement learning with sentence-level policy optimization, and Group Arena Reward alignment for human preferences. Information flows from raw data through preprocessing into pre-training, then mid-training with context extension and CoT activation, then post-training with RL optimization, finally producing the instruct models evaluated on benchmarks.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the **core architectural design** (MoE structure, expert routing, attention mechanisms) because understanding how tokens flow through the model is prerequisite to understanding how it is trained.
- We then explain the **Ling Scaling Laws** that guided architectural decisions, since these laws determine why specific hyperparameters (activation ratio, expert count, batch size) were chosen over alternatives.
- We proceed to **pre-training methodology** (data composition, multi-stage training, WSM scheduler) because the training recipe directly implements the architectural choices validated by scaling laws.
- We cover **post-training innovations** (DFT, Evo-CoT, LPO, GAR) next, as these build upon the pre-trained foundation to unlock reasoning capability.
- We conclude with **infrastructure optimizations** (FP8 training, heterogeneous pipeline parallelism, distributed framework) because these enable the algorithmic designs to execute efficiently at trillion scale.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This paper presents a comprehensive system for building trillion-parameter reasoning models through coordinated innovations across architecture, training methodology, and infrastructure. The core idea is that sparse activation, when properly aligned with reasoning objectives across all training phases, enables scalable and efficient intelligence that dense architectures cannot achieve at comparable computational cost.

#### Core Model Architecture

The Ling 2.0 series uniformly adopts a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture, which is a transformer design where each layer contains multiple expert sub-networks but only activates a subset for each token. This differs from dense transformers where all parameters process every token. Each MoE layer in Ling 2.0 contains 256 routed experts total, but activates only 8 experts plus 1 shared expert per token, yielding an overall activation ratio of approximately 3.5% (Section 2.1). The shared expert is a design heuristic identified through extensive experiments as optimal for maintaining model performance while maximizing sparsity. The three models in the series scale this unified architecture across different total parameter counts: Ling-mini-2.0 has 16 billion total parameters with 1.4 billion activated, Ling-flash-2.0 has 103 billion total parameters with 6.1 billion activated, and Ling-1T has 1 trillion total parameters with 51 billion activated (Table 1).

To maintain training stability while scaling sparsity, the architecture designates the initial layers of each model as dense layers rather than MoE layers. Specifically, Ling-mini-2.0 uses 1 dense layer, Ling-flash-2.0 uses 1 dense layer, and Ling-1T uses 4 dense layers (Table 1). This First-K-Dense strategy reduces total parameter count while maintaining equivalent model performance and improving routing balance in the shallow layers where routing tends to be more imbalanced (Section 5.6). The attention layers employ grouped-query attention (GQA) with varying key-value head counts to reduce KV cache size during decoding: 8 KV heads for Ling-mini-2.0, 16 for Ling-flash-2.0, and 32 for Ling-1T (Section 2.1). The attention head dimension is fixed at 128 across all model sizes, and the models use SwiGLU activation functions with RMSNorm and pre-normalization to improve representational efficiency and stability.

A critical stability enhancement is the introduction of QKNorm (Query-Key Normalization), which normalizes the query and key vectors in attention computation to prevent outlier values from amplifying through layer depth. The paper verifies that QKNorm "significantly improves stability under low-precision training" (Section 2.1), which is essential for the FP8 training strategy described later. The models also implement Partial RoPE (Rotary Position Embeddings), applying rotary embeddings only to the first 64 dimensions of the attention heads rather than all dimensions. This design choice bolsters the model's length extrapolation capabilities, enabling the context window extension from 4K to 128K during mid-training (Section 3.2.2.2). The vocabulary extends the Ling 1.5 vocabulary to 156K tokens using byte-level byte-pair encoding (BBPE) to enhance multilingual performance across approximately 30 languages (Section 2.1, Section 3.1.3).

The architecture integrates Multi-Token Prediction (MTP) as an auxiliary training objective, which means the model learns to predict multiple future tokens simultaneously rather than just the next token. Ling 2.0 introduces one MTP layer for each model scale and sets the MTP loss weight to 0.1 (Section 2.2). MTP consistently improves performance on code and math tasks across different model scales according to rigorous validation, but it introduces additional computational overhead that requires fine-grained Pipeline Parallelism partitioning to mitigate (Section 2.2). The MTP layer contains transformer layers and a loss computation block, both of which incur approximately 1.7× the computational and memory cost of a standard MoE layer, necessitating the heterogeneous pipeline scheduling described in Section 5.2.

For expert routing, Ling 2.0 adopts an aux-loss-free load balancing strategy similar to DeepSeek-V3, which eliminates the need for auxiliary loss terms that traditionally balanced expert utilization. Instead, the system applies router gate scaling with a scaling factor of 2.5 to stabilize the root mean square of the gate outputs (Section 2.2). The bias update strategy is modified to keep the bias centered around zero using the formula:

$$b_i = b_i + u \times \left(\text{sign}(e_i) - \text{mean}(\text{sign}(e))\right)$$

where $u$ is the update rate, $b_i$ is the bias of the $i$-th expert, and $e_i$ is that expert's violation error (Section 2.2). This aux-free bias update jointly encourages expert specialization and load balancing without the training instability that auxiliary losses can introduce. The system also adopts a dropless routing strategy to ensure model performance alongside group routing to improve training efficiency without performance degradation.

#### Ling Scaling Laws

The Ling Scaling Laws represent a systematic framework derived from over one thousand experiments that guide hyperparameter and architectural design for trillion-parameter models. These laws serve two critical functions: first, they determine the hyperparameters and architectural settings for Ling 2.0 to ensure near-optimal architectural efficiency; second, they provide a standardized experimental pipeline to validate novel ideas at just 1% of the full training compute cost (Section 2.3). The framework enables reliable extrapolation from small-scale trials to trillion-parameter models, cutting validation costs dramatically and accelerating the innovation cycle.

The scaling laws for optimal hyperparameters reveal a key difference between MoE and dense models: at larger compute scales, MoEs tend to use larger batch sizes and relatively lower learning rates (Section 2.3.1). The authors attribute this phenomenon to MoEs' sparse gradient updates: since only a subset of tokens in each batch contributes to the gradient update for any given expert, a larger batch size is necessary to ensure stable and effective training. The analysis fit power-law relationships between compute budget $C$ and the optimal batch size $B_{\text{opt}}$ and learning rate $\eta_{\text{opt}}$, verified to remain near-optimal under different activation ratios (Figure 1a).

For model-data allocation, the scaling laws indicate that for any given compute budget $C = M \cdot D$ (where $M$ is model parameters and $D$ is training data), the optimal MoE model has fewer parameters but is trained on more data compared to its optimal dense counterpart (Figure 1b). This conclusion suggests that MoE architectures possess a larger effective capacity, enabling them to efficiently process more training data with fewer parameters, which offers significant efficiency advantage when data is abundant but computational resources are limited.

The scaling laws for MoE architectural efficiency introduce efficiency leverage (EL) as the primary metric, defined as the ratio of computational cost required for a dense model to that of an MoE model to reach an equivalent performance level (Section 2.3.2). The investigation systematically analyzes how key architectural dimensions influence EL, deriving a unified scaling law that predicts EL as a function of MoE configuration:

$$\text{EL}(A, G, C) = \hat{A}^{\alpha + \gamma(\log G)^2 + \beta \log G}$$

where $\hat{A}$ is a saturating transformation of the activation ratio $A$, the exponent $\alpha = a + d \cdot \log C$ models the compute-dependent scaling, and parameters $\beta$ and $\gamma$ define the log-polynomial modulation from expert granularity $G$ (Equation 1, Section 2.3.2). Here, $d > 0$ quantifies the amplification of EL at larger compute scales, while $a$ represents the baseline scaling exponent.

Four core principles govern MoE efficiency according to this analysis. First, activation ratio is the primary driver of efficiency, with EL following a robust power law where efficiency gains increase as sparsity increases (Figure 2a left). Second, expert granularity acts as a nonlinear modulator, inducing a log-polynomial adjustment to EL that is largely independent of total compute budget, with experiments identifying the optimal range as 8–12 activated experts (Figure 2a right). Third, compute budget has an amplification effect: EL for a given MoE architecture is not fixed but scales with training compute budget following another power law, highlighting substantial potential of MoE in large-scale pretraining. Fourth, other architectural factors such as shared expert arrangement have relatively minor effects and admit broadly applicable near-optimal settings without fine-grained tuning.

Based on these scaling laws, all Ling 2.0 models adopt the high-sparsity, fine-granularity design: 256 routing experts with 8 activated per token plus one shared expert, yielding a 3.5% overall activation ratio (Section 2.3.2). The Ling scaling law predicts over 7× efficiency leverage for this architectural configuration, which the paper empirically confirms on the Ling 2.0 series. Table 1 shows the specific learning rates derived from these laws: 3.36 × 10⁻⁴ for Ling-mini-2.0, 2.61 × 10⁻⁴ for Ling-flash-2.0, and 1.86 × 10⁻⁴ for Ling-1T, demonstrating the inverse relationship between model scale and optimal learning rate.

The Ling Wind Tunnel Experiments system operationalizes these scaling laws for efficient innovation (Section 2.3.3). This system comprises five experiments with models ranging from 500M to 8B parameters, whose sizes are distributed according to a power law (Figure 3a). Each model is trained to a FLOPs count corresponding to its optimal compute allocation, with training tokens determined by the scaling laws for optimal model-data allocation and core hyperparameters set according to target FLOPs based on scaling laws for optimal hyperparameters. By strictly adhering to these scaling laws, the system reduces training uncertainty and accurately predicts final training loss to within an error of 0.01, enabling automated and standardized experimental judgments. Compared to traditional ablation studies (e.g., training a single Ling-mini-2.0 model on 400B tokens), the Ling Wind Tunnel is more cost-effective at merely 35% of the traditional method's computational cost despite involving more individual runs (Section 2.3.3).

#### Pre-training Methodology

The pre-training process consumes 20 trillion tokens total across multiple stages with progressively increasing reasoning data proportions. The pre-training corpus prioritizes the Ling Math and Ling Code datasets, which are tailored for mathematical reasoning and code generation respectively, yielding a 5-8% average gain on reasoning benchmarks (Introduction, Pre-Training bullet 1). Throughout the 20T-token pre-training process, the proportion of reasoning data progressively increases from 32% to 46%, establishing Ling 2.0's inherent reasoning strengths (Figure 5).

The Ling Code Corpus integrates multiple data sources covering source code, code-related natural language data, and synthetic instructional data (Section 3.1.2.1). Raw source code collected from GitHub repositories undergoes multilingual fine-grained cleaning rules tailored to each language's syntax and conventions, with Lint-based syntactic validation removing files with compilation or structural errors. This yields a source code corpus covering 660 programming languages. The pipeline further conducts quality stratification according to code style, readability, norm adherence, and complexity, plus code rephrasing and paraphrasing techniques to generate additional high-quality augmented code data. Additional sources include reconstructed commit data from GHArchive by replaying event sequences at the repository level, curated code-related pages from Common Crawl and Web, and programming-competition data consisting of problem statements, user submissions, and discussion threads. Evaluation on 1B models (Ling-coder-1B) demonstrates equivalent or better results on mainstream benchmarks compared to Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B-Base after consuming only 2T tokens of code data from scratch with an additional 300B annealing phase (Figure 4a, Appendix B.1.1).

The Ling Math Corpus assembles mathematics data from web pages, textbooks, research papers, code repositories, problem banks, and synthetic sources exceeding 1.8T tokens (Section 3.1.2.2, Appendix B.1.2). A multi-stage processing pipeline comprising parsing, recall, filtering, rewriting, and synthesis curates this corpus. The pipeline iteratively improves PDF and HTML parsers to ensure mathematical content completeness, builds fastText classifiers to recall math data from huge candidate pools, and fine-tunes small language models to develop LLM-Filter and LLM-Refiner that filter and refine data containing mathematical knowledge or step-by-step problem solving processes. Synthetic data generation creates diverse mathematical question-answer pairs varying in difficulty with step-by-step reasoning processes, including Q&A extraction from web and book, development of a sophisticated question generator for high-quality realistic mathematical problems, and construction of a large-scale mathematical concept graph to extend knowledge boundaries. Evaluation using continual-training then annealing strategy on a pre-trained Ling-coder-1B model for over 1.8T tokens (last 300B for annealing) demonstrates performance superior to Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B-Base and Qwen3-1.7B-Base on mainstream mathematical benchmarks (Figure 4b).

Multilingual data constitutes 4% of the total pre-training data, curated from approximately 2TB of high-quality multilingual sources including CulturaX and WanJuan open-source web datasets plus classical parallel corpora like OPUS and MultiUN (Section 3.1.3, Appendix B.2). The corpus spans about 30 languages across diverse domains including web text, code, mathematics, Wikipedia, and parallel sentence pairs. The language family distribution is approximately: Romance languages 50%, Germanic languages 10%, Slavic languages 3%, and other languages the remainder. Experiments determined this optimal distribution significantly improves minor language performance while maintaining Chinese and English capabilities, with findings that Romance and Germanic language data have less negative impact on core languages whereas data from certain other language families requires more careful balancing.

Long-context data is built through a retrieve-synthesize-validate pipeline over heterogeneous sources including web pages, books, scientific articles, and software documentation (Section 3.1.4). Quality controls include linguistic hygiene through rule checking and model recognition to identify and repair paragraph duplication and language mixing, semantic consistency checks using model-aided detection to filter logical contradictions, and long-range quality scoring eliminating low-quality content using perplexity gap between long- and short-window evaluations. This pipeline yields approximately 1.2 trillion high-quality long-text tokens.

The pre-training adopts a multi-stage strategy with stage-tailored data mixes as illustrated in Figure 5. The general pre-training stage proceeds with a 4K context length and consists of two sub-stages, each comprising 10T tokens. Across these two progressive sub-stages, the proportion of reasoning data increases from 32% to 46% while general data proportion reduces from 68% to 54%. After general pretraining, the mid-training stage extends context length to 128K and pre-activates reasoning ability by introducing Chain-of-Thought data. During the first 150B tokens of mid-training, 20% of sequences are 32K-length long-text samples, expanding the effective context window from 4K to 32K while maintaining stable short-context benchmark performance. Using the YaRN method, the context window extends to 128K, with Ling-mini-2.0 demonstrating strong performance on Needle In A Haystack tests at 128K context length after supervised fine-tuning (Figure 6). In the following 600B tokens of mid-training, the system maintains high reasoning data proportion and introduces additional high-quality CoT corpora, training at a higher learning rate and achieving robust performance by merging mid-training checkpoints. This early introduction of CoT data during the latter pretraining phase effectively "pre-activates" the model's reasoning capabilities, providing a higher ceiling for reasoning performance and more stable foundation for subsequent fine-tuning and reinforcement learning (Section 3.2.2.2).

The Warmup-Stable-Merge (WSM) scheduler replaces traditional learning-rate decay with checkpoint merging for greater flexibility and effectiveness (Section 3.2.3). The scheduler uses linear warmup for the first 2,000 steps to a peak learning rate, then constant learning rate until training ends, with final "annealing" achieved by checkpoint merging instead of LR decay. The theoretical connection between LR decay and checkpoint merging establishes that merging a sequence of checkpoints $[\theta_n, \theta_{n+1}, ..., \theta_{n+k}]$ into a single model $\hat{\theta}_{n+k}$ via weighted average is mathematically equivalent to re-weighting past gradients accumulated after the base checkpoint:

$$\hat{\theta}_{n+k} = \sum_{j=0}^{k} c_j \theta_{n+j} = \theta_n - \sum_{i=1}^{k} w_i g_{n+i-1}$$

where the effective gradient weights $w_i$ are determined by the original checkpoint merge weights $c_j$ (Equation 2, Section 3.2.3). This equivalence demonstrates that checkpoint merging effectively simulates a post-hoc LR decay schedule, achieving an annealing effect without modifying the learning rate during training. Given a target LR decay schedule represented by desired sequence of monotonically non-increasing gradient decay coefficients $\{w_i\}_{i=1}^k$, the non-negative checkpoint weights $\{c_j\}_{j=0}^k$ can be uniquely determined:

$$\begin{cases} c_k = w_k \\ c_j = w_j - w_{j+1}, & \text{for } j \in [1, k-1] \\ c_0 = 1 - \sum_{j=1}^{k} c_j = 1 - w_1 \end{cases}$$

This establishes bidirectional conversion between LR decay and checkpoint merging (Equation 3, Section 3.2.3). Comprehensive comparison reveals WSM consistently outperforms the strong WSD baseline across majority of evaluated tasks, yielding average improvement of +1 to +2 points on leaderboard scores across all benchmark categories (Figure 7). Crucially, WSM requires no prior choices about when to start LR decay or how long the decay phase should last, offering greater flexibility and scalability than WSD. For the final Ling 2.0 model, the system selects the top-N checkpoints in the final stage of mid-training based on validation performance and averages their parameters, with N = 32 (Section 3.2.3).

Training hyperparameters use AdamW optimizer with $\beta_1=0.9$, $\beta_2=0.95$, weight decay 0.1, and gradient-norm clipping 1.0 (Section 3.2.1). The bias-update rate $\gamma=0.001$ for the auxiliary-loss-free load-balancing term, with MTP loss weight of 0.1. After context extension, the bias-update rate is set to 0.0001 for the rest of training. For batch size, the system applies a batch-size ramp for the first approximately 500B tokens (e.g., from 3,024 to the peak), then keeps it constant in remaining training (Table 1 shows peak batch sizes: 4,400 for Ling-mini-2.0, 8,352 for Ling-flash-2.0, 18,144 for Ling-1T). All parameters are randomly initialized with standard deviation 0.006.

#### Post-training Pipeline

The post-training phase employs a structured three-stage methodology supported by scalable reward computation infrastructure (Figure 8). Stage 1 is Decoupled Fine-Tuning (DFT) with differentiated system prompts, Stage 2 is Evolutionary Reinforcement Learning (Evo-CoT), and Stage 3 is RL with Group Arena Reward for human preference alignment.

Decoupled Fine-Tuning constructs training data via differentiated system prompts to create a strong starting point for reinforcement learning (Section 4.1). DFT defines two modes: Instant Response (System Prompt 1 with "detailed think off") and In-Depth Reasoning (System Prompt 2 with "detailed think on"), with the latter producing responses in -reasoning&lt;/think>-answer format (Table 5). This prompt-guided decoupling enables the model to establish a dedicated deep-reasoning mode, providing robust foundation for subsequent RL to further enhance reasoning performance. The SFT dataset integrates multiple task domains under the dual-mode prompt framework adhering to three principles: reasoning tasks (mathematical problem solving, STEM, logic, code generation), general tasks (creative writing, empathetic dialogue, socio-philosophical discussion), and industrial tasks (finance, medical, production planning, supply chain). This integrated design prevents skill imbalance and supports fluent transitions between abstract reasoning and practical problem solving.

Since DFT suppresses explicit chain-of-thought in instant-response mode, standard accuracy metrics may undervalue its RL potential. The system employs ApexEval to gauge latent reasoning ability by testing whether problems are solvable under optimal prompting, emphasizing knowledge and reasoning over format-bound performance (Section 4.5). ApexEval uses the highest score of pass@k to estimate the probability of producing at least one correct response in multiple attempts, effectively capturing the model's potential upper bound rather than greedy or average pass@k scores. For tasks with explicit answers like mathematics, knowledge, and logic, the system uses LLM-based intelligent judges (e.g., Math-Verify, XVerify) to assess answer validity based on model predictions, minimizing misjudgment caused by pattern variability. For coding tasks, valid code snippets are evaluated via test-case execution to fairly assess actual capabilities. As shown in Figure 12, pretraining with CoT data consistently yields a higher ceiling under the same DFT configuration, with the DFT model pretrained with CoT data exhibiting stronger AIME performance at ERL step 450 compared to the model without CoT data.

Evolutionary Chain-of-Thought (Evo-CoT) is a training paradigm designed to instill adaptive reasoning in reflex-grade non-thinking models, enabling them to scale their reasoning depth according to problem complexity (Section 4.2). Formally, Evo-CoT starts from DFT-initialized policy $\pi$ in instant-response mode with system prompt $s_{\text{instant}}$ and evolves its reasoning depth. Given a user query $x$, the policy generates a response $y \sim \pi(\cdot | x_{\text{inst}})$ accordingly, where $x_{\text{inst}}$ denotes the concatenation of $(s_{\text{instant}}, x)$. At step $t$, the system optimizes the policy $\pi$ with parameters $\theta$ via:

$$\pi_{t+1} = \arg \max_{\pi} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \mathcal{J}(R(x, y), \theta) - \beta \cdot \text{KL}\left(\pi_\theta(\cdot | x_{\text{inst}}) \| \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot | x_{\text{inst}})\right) \right]$$

where $R(\cdot)$ is a composite reward function, $\mathcal{J}(\cdot)$ denotes the RL policy update algorithm, and $\beta$ controls deviation from the base policy (Section 4.2). The reward consists of accuracy $R_{\text{correctness}}$ (+1 if final answer matches ground truth else 0), dynamic length control $R_{\text{length}}$ (penalizes exceeding difficulty-specific length limit with stage-wise coefficient $\alpha$ that decreases for harder tasks), formatting $R_{\text{format}}$ (−0.5 if explicit reasoning markers "" appear), and task-specific rewards $R_{\text{task-specific}, k}$ for specific domains.

The dynamic length control implements the principle "think more about hard problems, respond quickly to easy ones" through a length preference function:

$$\hat{R}_{\text{length}} = \begin{cases} p(l), & \text{if } r_{\text{acc}} = 1 \\ \min(p(l), 0), & \text{if } r_{\text{acc}} = 0 \end{cases}$$

where

$$p(l) = \left(0.5 - \frac{l - \ell_{\min}}{\ell_{\max} - \ell_{\min} + 10^{-9}}\right)$$

Here, $l_k$ denotes the length (e.g., token count) of the $k$-th sampled response to input $x$, $\ell_{\min} = \min_k l_k$ and $\ell_{\max} = \max_k l_k$ represent the shortest and longest responses among samples respectively, and $r_{\text{acc}} \in \{0, 1\}$ indicates correctness (Section 4.2.1). To modulate the influence of length preference relative to correctness, a coefficient $\alpha > 0$ is introduced where larger $\alpha$ is used for easier tasks (strongly promoting concise outputs) and smaller $\alpha$ for harder tasks (encouraging more extensive reasoning). The final scoring function is $R_{\text{length}} = \alpha \cdot \hat{R}_{\text{length}}$. This formulation ensures that for correct answers, the reward reflects how well response length aligns with preferred range, while for incorrect answers, excessively long responses are penalized more and any positive length-based reward is suppressed.

For code reasoning, the system employs a unified reward framework based on test-case execution for code completion, editing, software engineering, and SQL tasks, ensuring reliable functional validation (Section 4.2.1). For complex front-end engineering tasks, the Visually Augmented Reward (VAR) system renders generated code into a live interface via a headless browser, then uses a multimodal model to evaluate the screenshot based on aesthetic and usability criteria, yielding a perceptually-aligned reward signal (Figure 9).

Linguistic-unit Policy Optimization (LPO) is a novel policy gradient algorithm driven by the Evo-CoT paradigm that performs importance sampling and clipping at the sentence level, defining a linguistic sentence as the fundamental action unit for policy updates (Section 4.2.2). Let $\{y_i\}_{i=1}^G$ denote a group of $G$ candidate responses sampled from the old policy $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot | x_{\text{inst}})$. For response $y_i$, let $N_{\text{sent}}(y_i)$ denote the total number of sentences in $y_i$, $s_{i,k}$ the $k$-th sentence in $y_i$ segmented by common pause punctuation marks after detokenization, and $|\cdot|$ denote the token length. The objective function of LPO is formulated as:

$$\mathcal{J}_{\text{LPO}}(R, \theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot | x_{\text{inst}})} \left[ \frac{1}{\sum_{i=1}^G |y_i|} \sum_{i=1}^G \sum_{k=1}^{N_{\text{sent}}(y_i)} |s_{i,k}| \cdot \min\left(r_{i,k}(\theta) \hat{A}_i, \text{clip}(r_{i,k}(\theta), 1 - \varepsilon, 1 + \varepsilon) \hat{A}_i\right) \right]$$

where

$$r_{i,k}(\theta) = \exp\left(\frac{1}{|s_{i,k}|} \sum_{t \in \text{tokens}(s_{i,k})} \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_{i,t} | x_{\text{inst}}, y_{i,&lt;t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_{i,t} | x_{\text{inst}}, y_{i,&lt;t})}\right)$$

and

$$\hat{A}_i = \frac{R(x, y_i) - \text{mean}\left(R(x, y_i)_{i=1}^G\right)}{\text{std}\left(R(x, y_i)_{i=1}^G\right)}$$

In the training setting, $\varepsilon = 0.03$ (Section 4.2.2). LPO performs sentence-level policy updates with sentence granularity importance sampling where each sentence $s_{i,k}$ in $y_i$ is treated as an independent action unit with its importance ratio $r_{i,k}(\theta)$ applied uniformly to all tokens in that sentence. Token-level normalization ensures group-based advantage estimation $\hat{A}_i$ are averaged over total token length $|y_i|$, ensuring scale invariance across examples. The clipping strategy clips ratios within $[1 - \varepsilon, 1 + \varepsilon]$ before multiplication, preventing unstable updates while preserving finer granularity than whole-sequence clipping. This structure aligns the optimization step with natural semantic boundaries of reasoning, resolving the mismatch in granularity found in conventional token-level and sequence-level methods. Empirically, LPO delivers smoother reward curves and markedly greater stability than GRPO, GSPO, and GSPO (Token Mean) variant, avoiding plateaus and collapse while converging faster and generalizing better (Figure 10). On the challenging AIME 2025 test set, LPO-trained models achieve substantially higher accuracy, demonstrating that stabilizing updates at the sentence level not only improves optimization but also guides the policy toward more robust reasoning strategies.

Group Arena Reward (GAR) addresses reward noise inherent in ambiguous evaluation criteria for open-ended, subjective tasks through an intra-group comparative evaluation strategy (Section 4.3.1). For open-ended tasks, conventional reward mechanisms struggle with quantifying subjective quality and suffer from high-variance scoring. GAR addresses these challenges by replacing independent absolute scoring with relative, tournament-style comparisons: multiple responses from the same policy are placed into an "arena", a generative reward model acts as a referee performing pairwise comparisons in a round-robin fashion, and cumulative results of these head-to-head contests form the final reward for each response (Figure 11). This relative ranking structure effectively reduces variance and reward noise, producing more reliable advantage estimates for policy updates.

To complement GAR with precise preference modeling, the system proposes RubriX—a domain-extended set of reward evaluation rubrics tailored for subjective general tasks (Section 4.3.2). RubriX spans multiple dimensions including clarity, coherence, creativity, emotional resonance, instruction adherence, and domain-specific accuracy, with instantiations for writing, translation, long-form QA, emotional dialogue, multi-turn conversation, and other instruction-following tasks. These structured rubrics guide the reward model to capture subtle aspects of user intent, encouraging responses that are both more natural in flow and more aligned with complex preference criteria.

The reward model system concurrently accommodates rule-based, model-based, and multi-programming-language-based reward verification, scaling to 40K concurrent heterogeneous reward requests with sustained success rates exceeding 99.9% (Section 4.4). The system architecture comprises three core modules: a highly available sandboxing environment integrating multi-programming-language sandboxes and general reward models inference, a preemptive task scheduling mechanism employing high-performance bounded queues achieving 39% improvement in system throughput, and an asynchronous reward computation framework decoupling RL training iterations from reward computation latency yielding empirically measured training time reduction of up to 30%.

#### Infrastructure Optimizations

Full-scale FP8 training represents the largest open-source model trained entirely in FP8 precision, with fine-grained quantization achieving near-lossless accuracy (Section 5.1). Activations and gradients are quantized in blocks of [1,128] elements, while weights are quantized in blocks of [128,128] elements. During forward and backward passes of most linear layers, the original BF16 tensor is quantized into FP8 E4M3 format along with FP32 scaling factors, and after FP8 GEMM computation, the BF16 output is obtained. This quantization strategy significantly mitigates the impact of outliers on global quantization errors, making it feasible to train LLMs in FP8. Validated on the Ling-1T model, the proposed FP8 mixed-precision framework maintained numerical stability throughout 900B-token training, with a relative loss difference within 0.25% (averaging around 0.1%) compared to the BF16 baseline (Figure 15), with no significant variance on benchmark leaderboards. On the efficiency side, FP8 training delivers roughly a +15% MFU (Model FLOPs Utilization) gain for Ling-1T.

The FP8 Training Safeguard System tracks risk coefficients for each operation across all layers in real-time, greatly facilitating timely anomaly detection and intervention (Section 5.1.1). The system quantifies low-precision training safety as measurable indicators: FP8 Quantization Underflow (proportion of matrix elements that become zero after quantization) and FP8 Quantization Distortion (measure of information loss calculated as cosine similarity between original and reconstructed matrix). Through error profiling via high-precision recomputation, the safeguard system monitors all operations across layers in real time and reports their health status. Figure 16 shows the trend of FP8 underflow and distortion metrics during Ling-mini-2.0 training process, where under the fine-grained FP8 quantization scheme, both activations and gradients maintain healthy precision states. However, monitoring reveals elevated quantization errors in tail layers during gradient transpose computations for $\frac{\partial L}{\partial W}$ in backpropagation, though joint analysis with high-precision recomputation metrics and experimental validation concludes these errors have negligible impact on model training since $\frac{\partial L}{\partial W}$ resides at leaf nodes in the backward propagation path and its quantization errors do not accumulate layer by layer.

QKNorm mitigates outliers and reduces FP8 precision loss by suppressing outlier phenomena observed in both activations and the $\frac{\partial L}{\partial y}$ gradient within the attention.linear_qkv layer during early experiments (Section 5.1.1). These outliers amplify progressively as layer depth increases, directly causing substantial quantization precision errors in FP8 computations for this layer. QKNorm not only suppresses outliers to enhance training stability but also demonstrably reduces precision errors across all FP8 modules in the network.

For even greater training efficiency, the system increases FP8 computation ratio through multiple CPU overhead optimizations including replacing FP8 padding/unpadding layers with FP8 padding routing map and removing redundant assert checks (Section 5.1.2). The FP8 On-Demand Transpose Weight technique trades time for VRAM by introducing a high-performance on-demand transpose kernel that eliminates the need for persistent transposed-weight storage, reducing the memory footprint of weight tensors by exactly 50% without compromising computational correctness. On 8/16/32 80GB GPUs, Ling-mini-2.0 delivers 30-60% throughput improvement over LLaMA 3.1 8B and Qwen3 8B when MTP is enabled, and 90-120% throughput improvement without MTP.

Heterogeneous Fine-grained Pipeline Parallelism addresses the challenge that MTP blocks and First-K-Dense strategy shift the Pipeline Parallelism scheduling units from homogeneous to heterogeneous, implying that forward and backward computational latencies as well as memory consumption may differ across blocks (Section 5.2). Without careful design, this can significantly increase pipeline bubbles. The system modifies the PP framework to support configurable transformer layer allocation per VPP stage including support for empty stages, scheduling MTP as a standalone layer no longer needing to be grouped with other MoE layers or bound to loss computation layer, partial recomputation for MTP where only the Transformer layer portion within MTP is recomputed during backward pass while logits computation part is not, and fine-grained partition strategy for MTP supporting partition of the MoE layer and loss computation layer within MTP into two separate layers for scheduling (Figure 17). In Ling 2.0 training, the computation cost of a MTP layer is approximately 1.7× that of a standard MoE layer. Based on this observation, the system progressively refined the PP partition strategy, ultimately achieving a 40% relative end-to-end improvement (Figure 18). Furthermore, for MoE models with balanced routing, virtual pipeline stages can increase from VPP2 to VPP4 to reduce pipeline bubbles, yielding an additional 5% gain.

For distributed training, Intra-Node DeepEP optimizes EP communication performance within nodes, and deploying DeepEP for intra-node operations yields substantial performance gains with 2% end-to-end speedup from communication redundancy reduction and additional 13% end-to-end performance improvement from operator fusion (Section 5.3.1). A wide range of fused operators was introduced including RoPE Fusion, Router Fusion, and Upgrading GroupGemm, which enhance speed by reducing memory-bound bottlenecks and effectively address substantial CPU overhead encountered during training (Section 5.3.2).

Fast Expert Full-Recomputation supports larger models by using full recomputation to save GPU memory, enabling larger training within fixed resources at a 25% compute cost (Section 5.3.3). Recent advances show that the weighted-sum computation of expert probabilities in the MoE layer can be moved forward to occur within the activation, eliminating the dependency of the linear_fc2 and unpermute operations on earlier results in the computation graph. As illustrated in Figure 19, unlike standard full-recomputation, the system discards the recompute flow before the linear_fc2, then runs the backward pass with a custom function for linear_fc2 and unpermute, and propagates its gradients back to activation function to complete the standard backward process. In Ling-2.0 training, smaller models saw end-to-end performance gains of up to 10%, while larger models achieved approximately 7%.

Long-context training optimizations resolve correctness issues related to loss and gradient misalignment when applying Tensor Parallelism and Context Parallelism to MTP, support cross-sample attention mask by mitigating NaN issues in the cross-sample attention mask mechanism primarily caused by all-padding issue introduced in CP implementation, and address performance degradation in RoPE Fusion by limiting sub-sequences and allocating resources based on actual maximum sequence length per micro-batch (Section 5.3.4).

Framework optimization addresses Effective Training Time Ratio (ETTR) beyond MFU optimization (Section 5.3.5). Optimizing the storage latency of distributed checkpoints introduces a metadata cache to avoid redundant computation since metadata depends solely on model architecture, dropping checkpoint save time for Ling-1T from 269s to 30s and its share of total training time from 2.43% to 0.82%. Startup time optimization constructs a small-scale batch prior to the first forward pass of training, passing it once through both forward and backward of the model to allow all GPU ranks to perform warm-up computation without storing weights or updating gradients, reducing time required for first training step by approximately 30%. For optimal failover strategy, checkpoints are periodically saved so latest checkpoint can be loaded after task restart, with checkpoint saving interval configured to 48 minutes for Ling-1T training calculated with a strategy discussed in Appendix A. Loss spike handling employs methodology from previous work, monitoring training state from both gradient and loss perspectives to prevent occurrence of loss spikes.

The 4C principle (Correct, Consistent, Complete, and Co-Design) formalizes Foundation LLMs Software Engineering as a research domain to enhance efficiency and delivery quality of foundational model development while reducing associated costs (Section 5.4). For training efficiency optimization and numerical integrity assurance, the system establishes a workflow for iterative upgrade of large language model training frameworks structured as a cycle: Progressive Estimation → Release Approval → Task Monitoring and Sampling Analysis → Experience Accumulation (Section 5.4.1). During the entire training cycle of a model, experiments are conducted under varying resource configurations utilizing up to approximately 3% of actual training resources, with only iterations meeting established standards approved for release.

For co-design of algorithms and systems, infrastructure-aware architecture design considers trade-offs between algorithm performance and system efficiency (Section 5.4.2). Taking the Norm Head strategy as an example, the system found its usage leads to performance improvement of less than 1% while significantly increasing computation and memory consumption within a single PP stage, making it challenging to tune distributed training strategy to optimal state, so this technique was not employed in Ling 2.0 training. The Group Router algorithm in the MoE layer divides all experts into 8 groups, and each token is routed within the top 4 scoring groups to maximize intra/inter-node communication efficiency. Operator efficiency analysis shows Ling-2.0 efficiency is more centered in the mid-range, consistent with its "wider and shallower" design, with no operators showing exceptionally low efficiency attributed to the First-K-Dense strategy mitigating imbalance in shallow MoE layers (Figure 20). Parameter design integrated with distributed architecture carries out detailed parameter configuration tailored to various heterogeneous modules, for example in Ling-1T design the computation consumption ratio between dense layers and MoE layers was set at 1:2 to facilitate achieving uniform computation time across all PP stages and minimize pipeline bubbles.

For cross-platform alignment, the system adheres to the 4C principle to align algorithmic logic and results as closely as possible across different platforms during training on heterogeneous GPUs beyond standard Hopper architecture (Section 5.4.3). Figure 21 shows alignment results based on Ling-flash-2.0, where throughout the training process the differences in loss values consistently oscillate around zero, indicating that although variations in operator implementations across different GPU architectures introduce floating-point precision deviations, the mean value of these errors remains within one-thousandth, which is insufficient to affect the accuracy of model training convergence.

The evaluation pipeline was redesigned based on OpenCompass to support large-scale, distributed, and incremental benchmarking (Section 5.5). The system integrates on-the-fly checkpoint evaluation, dynamic resource scheduling, and prompt caching to minimize redundant computation. Compared with original OpenCompass, the total evaluation time per checkpoint was reduced by more than two-thirds. Multi-node inference optimization extends OpenCompass to support distributed evaluation across Ray Clusters, with each evaluation task dynamically allocating multi-node multi-instance resources and interfacing with the SGLang inference backend for high-throughput serving. Prompt caching for repeated prefixes implements prefix-level caching that reuses shared prompt embedding across samples, improving evaluation throughput by more than 30%. Batch parallelization and async execution parallelizes prompt preprocessing, inference, and postprocessing steps with asynchronous inference scheduling, where small requests are automatically batched into larger groups to increase GPU saturation.

Regarding computation-communication overlapping, studies on large MoE models like DualPipe and interleaved 1F1B with A2A overlap improve training efficiency by overlapping expert computation in one micro-batch with A2A communication in another through modified PP scheduling (Section 5.6). The system applied these methods in Ling 2.0 training and resolved several performance issues such as streaming multiprocessors computation-communication contention and CPU synchronization bottlenecks. However, the end-to-end acceleration remained limited due to overlapping strategy needing a large EP configuration where larger EP size assigns more tokens per expert boosting expert-layer matrix efficiency while masking added communication overhead, but EP group time is gated by the slowest rank and larger EP size reduces experts per rank exacerbating this bottleneck effect. Additionally, imbalanced routing in shallow MoE layers makes the PP rank containing these layers more prone to OOM errors, forcing reconsideration of PP partitioning strategy that incurred overall performance penalty. In summary, large EP-based optimizations are more sensitive to routing imbalance than smaller configurations, and while Ling 2.0 training saw limited gains, computation-communication overlap is viewed as a key avenue for improving large-scale MoE performance with plans to jointly optimize routing and related components to better realize its potential benefits.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the Ling 2.0 report into its most novel contributions, distinguishing between incremental engineering improvements and fundamental methodological advances. While Section 3 detailed the technical mechanisms, here we examine why these innovations matter for the broader field of large-scale model development.

### 4.1 The Ling Scaling Laws: A Methodological Breakthrough for Trillion-Scale Prediction

**What makes this novel:** Prior scaling laws (e.g., Chinchua, Hoffmann et al., 2022) were derived primarily for dense architectures and do not account for the non-linear effects of sparsity on training dynamics. The Ling Scaling Laws represent the first unified framework specifically derived for high-sparsity MoE architectures, based on over one thousand experiments across compute scales up to 3×10²⁰ FLOPs (Section 2.3.1).

**Key distinction from prior work:**

| Aspect | Prior Scaling Laws | Ling Scaling Laws |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Architecture type** | Dense models | High-sparsity MoE (3.5% activation) |
| **Hyperparameter guidance** | Single optimal curve | MoE-specific curves (larger batch, lower LR) |
| **Validation cost** | Full-scale experiments required | "Wind Tunnel" at 1% of full training cost |
| **Prediction accuracy** | Extrapolation uncertainty high | Loss prediction within 0.01 error |

The critical insight is that **MoE and dense models have fundamentally different optimal hyperparameter regimes**. Section 2.3.1 notes: "at larger compute scales, MoEs tend to use larger batch sizes and relatively lower learning rate" due to sparse gradient updates where only a subset of tokens contributes to each expert's gradient. This finding contradicts dense-model heuristics that would have led to suboptimal trillion-scale configurations.

**Why this matters beyond Ling 2.0:**

> "The framework establishes a 'wind tunnel' for low-cost, high-fidelity extrapolation from small-scale trials to trillion-parameter models, cutting validation costs to under 1% of a full training run." (Introduction, Model Architecture bullet 1)

This transforms trillion-scale development from a "bet-the-company" gamble into an iterative engineering process. Organizations can now validate architectural innovations at 500M–8B parameter scales with confidence that findings extrapolate 100× to trillion-parameter models. The Wind Tunnel's 35% cost advantage over traditional ablation studies (Section 2.3.3) while providing more stable conclusions represents a paradigm shift in how foundation models are developed.

**Innovation classification:** **Fundamental methodological innovation** — This is not an incremental improvement but a new framework for trillion-scale model development that addresses the core economic bottleneck of scaling research.

### 4.2 Full-Scale FP8 Training with Quantifiable Precision Safeguards

**What makes this novel:** While FP8 training has been explored in prior work (e.g., DeepSeek-V3), Ling 2.0 represents "the largest open-source model trained entirely in FP8 precision" (Introduction, Infrastructure bullet 1) with systematic, real-time precision tracking that quantifies training safety as measurable indicators.

**Key distinction from prior work:**

Prior FP8 implementations either:
- Avoided full-scale training due to stability concerns
- Lacked systematic monitoring of quantization errors
- Did not provide quantifiable metrics for precision health

Ling 2.0 introduces the **FP8 Training Safeguard System** that tracks two novel metrics in real-time (Section 5.1.1):

1. **FP8 Quantization Underflow**: Proportion of matrix elements that become zero after quantization
2. **FP8 Quantization Distortion**: Cosine similarity between original and reconstructed (quantized then de-quantized) matrix

> "Validated on the Ling-1T model, the proposed FP8 mixed-precision framework maintained numerical stability throughout 900B-token training, with a relative loss difference within 0.25% (averaging around 0.1%) compared to the BF16 baseline." (Section 5.1)

**Why this matters beyond Ling 2.0:**

The significance extends beyond the +15% MFU gain. By making low-precision training safety **measurable and monitorable**, the work transforms FP8 from a "trust but verify" approach into an engineering discipline with quantifiable risk bounds. Figure 16 shows these metrics tracked throughout Ling-mini-2.0 training, revealing that gradient transpose errors in tail layers have negligible impact because $\frac{\partial L}{\partial W}$ resides at leaf nodes and does not accumulate layer-by-layer.

This insight—that not all quantization errors equally affect training stability—provides a diagnostic framework applicable to any low-precision training scenario. The QKNorm integration (Section 5.1.1) further demonstrates that architectural choices can be co-optimized with precision constraints, not treated as independent concerns.

**Innovation classification:** **Fundamental infrastructure innovation** — The systematic safeguard system and quantifiable precision metrics establish a new standard for low-precision training that transcends this specific model family.

### 4.3 Linguistic-Unit Policy Optimization: Sentence-Level RL for Reasoning Stability

**What makes this novel:** Conventional reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) operates at either the token level (fine-grained but unstable) or sequence level (stable but coarse). LPO introduces **sentence-level policy optimization** as an intermediate granularity that aligns with natural semantic boundaries of reasoning (Section 4.2.2).

**Key distinction from prior work:**

| Method | Optimization Unit | Stability | Data Efficiency |
|--------|------------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Token-level PPO | Individual tokens | Low (high variance) | High |
| Sequence-level GRPO | Full response | High | Low (all-or-nothing) |
| **LPO (Ling 2.0)** | **Sentences** | **High** | **High** |

The mathematical formulation treats each sentence $s_{i,k}$ as an independent action unit with importance ratio $r_{i,k}(\theta)$ applied uniformly to all tokens in that sentence (Section 4.2.2). This resolves the granularity mismatch where token-level updates ignore semantic coherence while sequence-level updates cannot credit intermediate reasoning steps.

**Why this matters beyond Ling 2.0:**

> "On the challenging AIME 2025 test set, LPO-trained models achieve substantially higher accuracy, demonstrating that stabilizing updates at the sentence level not only improves optimization but also guides the policy toward more robust reasoning strategies." (Section 4.2.2)

Figure 10 shows LPO delivers smoother reward curves without the plateaus or collapses observed in GRPO and GSPO baselines. This is significant because reasoning tasks require **multi-step deduction** where intermediate steps matter—sentence-level optimization naturally credits correct intermediate reasoning even if the final answer is wrong, enabling more nuanced policy learning.

The innovation generalizes beyond mathematical reasoning to any task requiring structured multi-step outputs (code generation, scientific writing, legal analysis). By aligning optimization granularity with semantic boundaries, LPO provides a template for reasoning-aware RL that prior token/sequence dichotomies could not achieve.

**Innovation classification:** **Fundamental algorithmic innovation** — This is a novel policy gradient formulation that addresses a structural limitation in existing RLHF methods, with applicability beyond the Ling 2.0 family.

### 4.4 Warmup-Stable-Merge Scheduler: Eliminating LR Decay Through Checkpoint Arithmetic

**What makes this novel:** Learning-rate decay has been considered essential for LLM training convergence since the earliest transformer models. The WSM scheduler demonstrates that **checkpoint merging is mathematically equivalent to LR decay**, enabling annealing without modifying the learning rate during training (Section 3.2.3).

**Key distinction from prior work:**

Traditional training requires committing to a decay schedule upfront:
- When to start decay (e.g., after 90% of training)
- How long the decay phase should last (e.g., 10% of training)
- Decay shape (cosine, linear, exponential)

WSM eliminates these constraints through the theoretical equivalence (Section 3.2.3):

$$\hat{\theta}_{n+k} = \sum_{j=0}^{k} c_j \theta_{n+j} = \theta_n - \sum_{i=1}^{k} w_i g_{n+i-1}$$

where checkpoint merge weights $\{c_j\}$ can be uniquely determined from any target LR decay schedule $\{w_i\}$. This bidirectional conversion means **any LR decay behavior can be replicated post-hoc through merging**.

**Why this matters beyond Ling 2.0:**

> "WSM yields an average improvement of +1 to +2 points on leaderboard scores across all benchmark categories. Crucially, WSM requires no prior choices about when to start LR decay or how long the decay phase should last." (Section 3.2.3)

The practical significance is **training flexibility**. If validation performance plateaus, practitioners can extend training and merge additional checkpoints rather than being locked into a predetermined decay schedule. Figure 7 shows WSM's advantage persists through post-training stages, indicating the merged checkpoints produce more robust parameter configurations.

This innovation reframes checkpoint management from a storage burden into an optimization tool. The final Ling 2.0 model averages N=32 top checkpoints (Section 3.2.3), effectively implementing an ensemble at the parameter level rather than the prediction level.

**Innovation classification:** **Fundamental methodological innovation** — This challenges a decades-old assumption in optimization theory (that LR decay is necessary for convergence) and provides a more flexible alternative with empirical advantages.

### 4.5 Reasoning Pre-Activation: Mid-Training CoT as a Capability Ceiling Elevator

**What makes this novel:** Prior approaches typically introduce Chain-of-Thought data during post-training (SFT or RL phases). Ling 2.0 introduces CoT data during **mid-training**, before post-training begins, to "pre-activate" reasoning capabilities (Section 3.2.2.2).

**Key distinction from prior work:**

| Approach | CoT Introduction | Effect on Reasoning Ceiling |
|----------|-----------------|----------------------------|
| Traditional | Post-training (SFT/RL) | Limited by base model capacity |
| **Ling 2.0** | **Mid-training (pre-activation)** | **Elevates ceiling before RL** |

The mechanism is that early CoT exposure during the latter pretraining phase establishes reasoning patterns in the base model's parameters, providing "a higher ceiling for reasoning performance and a more stable foundation for subsequent fine-tuning and reinforcement learning" (Section 3.2.2.2).

**Why this matters beyond Ling 2.0:**

> "The DFT model pretrained with CoT data exhibits stronger AIME performance at ERL step 450 compared to the model without CoT data, indicating a faster performance gain trajectory." (Section 4.5, Figure 12)

Figure 12 demonstrates this empirically: models with mid-training CoT achieve +6.8% on AIME 2024 and +6.0% on AIME 2025 after the same RL steps. This suggests that **reasoning capability is not purely a post-training phenomenon**—the base model's parameter configuration fundamentally constrains what post-training can achieve.

The broader insight is that **capability ceilings are set during pretraining**, not post-training. Post-training optimizes within the ceiling; it does not raise it. This reframes how organizations should allocate training resources: investing in reasoning-oriented pretraining data may yield higher returns than elaborate post-training pipelines on weak base models.

**Innovation classification:** **Fundamental training methodology innovation** — This challenges the conventional separation between pretraining (knowledge acquisition) and post-training (capability refinement), showing that reasoning capabilities must be embedded during pretraining for optimal results.

### 5.5 Critical Assessment of Experimental Claims

#### 5.5.1 Claims Well-Supported by Evidence

**1. Efficiency Leverage (7× Claim).** The activated parameter counts support this claim:
- Ling-mini-2.0: 1.4B activated vs. ~8B dense competitors (5.7×)
- Ling-flash-2.0: 6.1B activated vs. ~32-36B dense competitors (5.3-5.9×)
- Ling-1T: 51B activated vs. ~671B DeepSeek-V3 (13.2×)

The performance comparisons in Tables 2-4 and 6-8 generally confirm that Ling 2.0 models achieve comparable or superior performance to dense models with 5-7× more activated parameters. The claim is **substantially supported**, though the exact 7× figure varies by scale.

**2. CoT Pre-activation Effect.** The ablation in Tables 2-4 and Figure 12 provides compelling evidence:
- AIME25 improvement from 2.08% to 43.75% on Ling-mini-2.0-base (21× relative gain)
- Consistent +6% advantage on AIME benchmarks after RL (Figure 12)
- Effect persists through post-training stages (Section 3.3.2)

This is one of the strongest experimental findings in the paper, with clear before/after comparisons.

**3. WSM Scheduler Advantage.** Figure 7 shows consistent +1 to +2 point improvements across all benchmark categories, with the advantage persisting through 5 epochs of supervised fine-tuning. The claim is **well-supported** with clear quantitative evidence.

**4. FP8 Training Stability.** Figure 15 demonstrates stable loss difference within 0.25% throughout 900B tokens, with +15% MFU improvement. The FP8 Training Safeguard System metrics in Figure 16 provide additional validation. This claim is **strongly supported**.

**5. LPO Stability.** Figure 10 shows smoother reward curves compared to GRPO and GSPO baselines. However, the "around 10% improvements on complex reasoning benchmarks" claim (Introduction) is not directly quantified in the figures—only reward curves are shown. This claim is **partially supported** pending final benchmark comparisons.

#### 5.5.2 Claims Requiring Scrutiny

**1. MATH Benchmark Discrepancy.** Ling-1T-base achieves 82.78% on MATH vs. DeepSeek-V3.1 at 35.64% (Table 4)—a 47.14 point difference. This is an unusually large gap that warrants scrutiny:
- Possible explanation: Different evaluation methodologies or benchmark contamination
- The paper states all models are evaluated using a "unified internal evaluation framework" (Section 3.3.2), but details of DeepSeek-V3.1 evaluation are not provided
- Instruct model results (Table 8) show smaller gaps, suggesting base model evaluation may have methodological differences

**2. Seed-OSS-36B Comparison.** On MATH, Seed-OSS-36B achieves 88.64% vs. Ling-flash-2.0-base at 79.54% (Table 3)—a 9.10 point deficit. This suggests that at the 100B scale, some dense or differently-architected models can still outperform Ling 2.0 on certain benchmarks. The paper does not extensively discuss this limitation.

**3. Knowledge Benchmark Trade-offs.** Ling-1T shows slight deficits on some knowledge benchmarks:
- MMLU: 86.03% vs. DeepSeek-V3.1 at 88.44% (−2.41 points)
- GPQA-Diamond: 72.98% vs. DeepSeek-V3.1 at 76.23% (−3.25 points)

The paper acknowledges this implicitly by emphasizing reasoning benchmarks, but does not explicitly frame this as a trade-off. This suggests the reasoning optimization may come at a slight cost to pure knowledge recall.

**4. Computation-Communication Overlapping Limitations.** Section 5.6 acknowledges that computation-communication overlapping strategies "saw limited gains" despite resolving several performance issues. The paper attributes this to:
- Large EP configuration requirements exacerbating bottleneck effects
- Imbalanced routing in shallow MoE layers causing OOM errors

This is an important limitation that the paper transparently reports, but it suggests that not all infrastructure optimizations achieved their theoretical potential.

#### 5.5.3 Missing or Incomplete Evaluations

**1. Inference Latency Benchmarks.** While the paper emphasizes efficiency, it does not provide detailed inference latency measurements (tokens/second, time-to-first-token) across different hardware configurations. The +15% MFU gain for FP8 training is reported, but inference efficiency gains are less thoroughly quantified.

**2. Long-Context Performance Details.** Section 3.2.2.2 mentions context extension to 128K with YaRN, and Figure 6 shows Needle In A Haystack results for Ling-mini-2.0. However, comprehensive long-context benchmark results (e.g., L-Eval, LongBench) are not provided for all model scales, making it difficult to assess how well the context extension generalizes.

**3. Multilingual Benchmark Depth.** While multilingual data constitutes 4% of pre-training (Section 3.1.3), the multilingual benchmark results (MMMLU, mARC, MultiGSM) show mixed performance:
- Ling-mini-2.0: MMMLU 50.14% vs. Qwen3-8B at 60.02% (−9.88 points)
- Ling-flash-2.0: MMMLU 62.76% vs. Qwen2.5-72B at 72.70% (−9.94 points)

This suggests multilingual capability may be an area for improvement, but the paper does not extensively analyze this limitation.

**4. Compute Cost Transparency.** The paper mentions the Wind Tunnel reduces validation costs to "under 1% of a full training run" (Introduction), but does not provide absolute compute figures (e.g., total GPU hours, dollar costs) for training Ling-1T. This makes it difficult for readers to assess the practical accessibility of reproducing this work.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While Ling 2.0 demonstrates significant advances in scalable reasoning models, a critical examination reveals important assumptions, trade-offs, and unresolved challenges. This section distinguishes between inherent limitations of the approach, design trade-offs that sacrifice one capability for another, and open questions that remain for future work.

### 6.1 Architectural Assumptions and Constraints

**Assumption 1: High Sparsity Remains Stable at Extreme Scale.** The entire Ling 2.0 design rests on the assumption that 3.5% activation ratios (8 of 256 experts) remain trainable and effective at trillion-parameter scales. While the Ling Scaling Laws validate this extrapolation from smaller models (Section 2.3), the paper acknowledges that "imbalanced routing in shallow MoE layers makes the PP rank containing these layers more prone to OOM errors" (Section 5.6). This suggests sparsity introduces fragility that requires careful mitigation through the First-K-Dense strategy. If routing imbalance worsens at scales beyond 1T, the efficiency gains may diminish.

**Assumption 2: FP8 Precision Suffices for All Training Phases.** Full-scale FP8 training assumes quantization errors remain bounded throughout 900B tokens of training. The FP8 Training Safeguard System tracks underflow and distortion metrics (Figure 16), but the paper notes elevated errors in gradient transpose computations for $\frac{\partial L}{\partial W}$ in tail layers (Section 5.1.1). While the authors conclude these errors "have negligible impact" because they reside at leaf nodes, this assumption has not been validated beyond the 1T scale. Future models with deeper architectures may accumulate quantization errors differently.

**Assumption 3: Reasoning Capability Can Be Pre-Activated Before Post-Training.** The CoT pre-activation strategy assumes that introducing Chain-of-Thought data during mid-training establishes reasoning patterns that post-training can amplify (Section 3.2.2.2). Figure 12 supports this with +6% AIME improvements, but the mechanism remains empirically observed rather than theoretically explained. It is unclear whether this approach generalizes to non-mathematical reasoning domains (e.g., legal analysis, scientific hypothesis generation) where step-by-step deduction follows different patterns than mathematical proof.

### 6.2 Performance Trade-offs: Reasoning vs. Other Capabilities

The Ling 2.0 design explicitly optimizes for reasoning capability, but this focus comes with measurable trade-offs in other domains.

**Trade-off 1: Reasoning Optimization vs. Knowledge Recall.** Table 8 reveals consistent deficits on pure knowledge benchmarks despite Ling-1T's trillion-parameter scale:

| Benchmark | Ling-1T | DeepSeek-V3.1 | Gap |
|-----------|---------|---------------|-----|
| MMLU | 86.03% | 88.44% | −2.41 points |
| MMLU-Pro | 82.04% | 83.25% | −1.21 points |
| GPQA-Diamond | 72.98% | 76.23% | −3.25 points |

The paper does not explicitly frame this as a trade-off, but the pattern suggests that allocating model capacity and training data toward reasoning-specific objectives (Ling Math, Ling Code corpora at 46% of pre-training) may come at the expense of broad knowledge acquisition. This is a deliberate design choice—positioning Ling 2.0 as a "reasoning-oriented foundation" rather than a general knowledge model—but users should recognize this limitation when selecting models for knowledge-intensive tasks.

**Trade-off 2: Reasoning Optimization vs. Multilingual Capability.** Despite expanding the vocabulary to 156K tokens and curating 2TB of multilingual data (Section 3.1.3), Ling 2.0 shows significant gaps on multilingual benchmarks:

| Model | MMMLU | mARC |
|-------|-------|------|
| Ling-mini-2.0 | 50.14% | 65.33% |
| Qwen3-8B | 60.02% | 80.70% |
| **Gap** | **−9.88 points** | **−15.37 points** |

| Model | MMMLU | mARC |
|-------|-------|------|
| Ling-flash-2.0 | 62.76% | 82.07% |
| Qwen2.5-72B | 72.70% | 88.84% |
| **Gap** | **−9.94 points** | **−6.77 points** |

The paper acknowledges that "data from certain other language families requires more careful balancing" (Section 3.1.3), noting that Slavic and non-Romance/Germanic languages can have "considerable negative effect" on core language performance. This suggests multilingual capability was deprioritized to protect reasoning performance in English and Chinese—a reasonable trade-off for the stated use case, but a limitation for global deployment.

**Trade-off 3: Efficiency vs. Maximum Absolute Performance.** At the 100B scale, Ling-flash-2.0-base achieves 79.54% on MATH, but Seed-OSS-36B reaches 88.64%—a 9.10 point deficit (Table 3). This indicates that while Ling 2.0 achieves comparable performance with 7× fewer activated parameters, dense or differently-architected models can still exceed absolute performance on certain benchmarks when computational constraints are relaxed. The efficiency leverage comes with a ceiling on peak capability for some tasks.

### 6.3 Infrastructure and Scalability Constraints

**Constraint 1: Heterogeneous Pipeline Parallelism Requires Balanced Routing.** The 40% throughput improvement from heterogeneous pipeline scheduling (Section 5.2) depends on balanced expert routing. The paper explicitly notes: "in other cases where a VPP stage contains only a single MoE layer, the inter-stage blocking becomes more sensitive to imbalanced MoE routing. In such cases, our strategy may not provide end-to-end performance gains" (Section 5.2). This limits the generalizability of the infrastructure optimizations to scenarios where routing balance can be maintained.

**Constraint 2: Computation-Communication Overlapping Saw Limited Gains.** Despite resolving technical issues like SM contention and CPU synchronization bottlenecks, Section 5.6 acknowledges that "end-to-end acceleration remained limited" for computation-communication overlapping strategies. The root causes are fundamental:

- Larger EP (Expert Parallelism) configurations reduce experts per rank, exacerbating bottleneck effects from the slowest rank
- Imbalanced routing in shallow MoE layers forces PP partitioning changes that incur performance penalties

The paper states this is "a key avenue for improving large-scale MoE performance" with plans to "jointly optimize routing and related components" (Section 5.6), but this remains an open engineering challenge rather than a solved problem.

**Constraint 3: Long-Context Efficiency Not Fully Quantified.** While the Conclusion acknowledges that "current grouped-query attention (GQA) architecture constrains efficiency in long-context scenarios," the paper does not provide comprehensive long-context benchmark results (e.g., L-Eval, LongBench) for all model scales (Section 5.5.3). Figure 6 shows Needle In A Haystack results only for Ling-mini-2.0 at 128K, leaving uncertainty about how context extension scales to Ling-1T. The Partial RoPE design (applying rotary embeddings to only 64 of 128 dimensions) may limit length extrapolation beyond 128K.

**Constraint 4: Inference Latency Not Thoroughly Measured.** The paper emphasizes training efficiency (+15% MFU from FP8, 7× active-compute leverage) but provides limited inference latency benchmarks. Section 5.1.2 reports throughput improvements for Ling-mini-2.0 (30-60% with MTP, 90-120% without MTP vs. LLaMA 3.1 8B), but does not provide:
- Tokens-per-second measurements for Ling-1T inference
- Time-to-first-token latency across different hardware configurations
- Memory requirements for deploying trillion-parameter models

This gap makes it difficult for practitioners to assess deployment feasibility despite the efficiency claims.

### 6.4 Data and Training Constraints

**Constraint 1: Reasoning Data Curation Is Resource-Intensive.** The Ling Math and Ling Code corpora required multi-stage pipelines with LLM-based filtering, rewriting, and synthetic generation (Sections 3.1.2.1–3.1.2.2). The paper notes that "constructing reasoning-centric corpora is resource-intensive" (Introduction, paragraph 4), yet does not quantify the human labor, compute costs, or time required for data curation. This creates a barrier for organizations seeking to replicate the approach without equivalent data engineering infrastructure.

**Constraint 2: Wind Tunnel Validation Still Requires Significant Compute.** While the Wind Tunnel reduces validation costs to "under 1% of a full training run" (Introduction), this still represents substantial absolute compute. Training five models from 500M to 8B parameters (Figure 3a) to optimal compute allocations requires hundreds of billions of tokens across multiple runs. For organizations without access to large GPU clusters, even this reduced validation cost may be prohibitive.

**Constraint 3: Checkpoint Management Overhead.** The WSM scheduler requires storing and merging up to N=32 top checkpoints (Section 3.2.3). For Ling-1T, each checkpoint represents 1 trillion parameters, creating significant storage and I/O demands. The paper reports checkpoint save time optimization from 269s to 30s (Section 5.3.5), but does not disclose total storage requirements or the infrastructure needed to manage dozens of trillion-parameter checkpoints simultaneously.

### 6.5 Open Questions and Unresolved Challenges

**Open Question 1: Effective Reasoning Depth.** The Conclusion explicitly states that "effective reasoning length and depth still have room for enhancement" (Section 6). While Evo-CoT enables adaptive reasoning depth based on problem complexity (Section 4.2), the paper does not quantify maximum reasoning depth (e.g., maximum coherent CoT tokens) or how this scales with model size. This remains a critical limitation for tasks requiring extended multi-step deduction.

**Open Question 2: Agentic and Autonomous Behavior.** The paper positions Ling 2.0 as a foundation for "general-purpose agents that can understand, decide, and act autonomously" (Introduction, paragraph 1), yet acknowledges that "complex instruction following and agentic behaviors remain under development" (Conclusion). The Ring series is mentioned as extending toward "deep thinking models" (Introduction, paragraph 8), but Ling 2.0 itself is characterized as "reflex-grade non-thinking (instruct) models" (Introduction, paragraph 8). This distinction suggests Ling 2.0 is a stepping stone rather than a complete solution for agentic systems.

**Open Question 3: Generalization Beyond Mathematics and Code.** The reasoning benchmarks emphasize mathematics (AIME, MATH, OlympiadBench) and coding (HumanEval, LiveCodeBench, CodeForces). While general reasoning tasks like ZebraLogic and KOR-Bench are included, the paper does not extensively evaluate reasoning transfer to domains like:
- Scientific hypothesis generation
- Legal argumentation
- Medical diagnosis reasoning
- Strategic planning

The CoT pre-activation mechanism may be optimized for formal, verifiable reasoning (math/code) rather than open-ended domains where ground truth is ambiguous.

**Open Question 4: Reproducibility Across Hardware Platforms.** Figure 21 shows loss alignment between Hopper and non-Hopper GPUs with mean error "within one-thousandth" (Section 5.4.3), but this validation is limited to Ling-flash-2.0. The paper does not report whether Ling-1T training has been validated across heterogeneous platforms, nor does it disclose which specific GPU architectures were tested. Given the sensitivity of FP8 training to hardware-specific operator implementations, this remains a potential reproducibility risk.

**Open Question 5: Long-Term Training Stability Beyond 900B Tokens.** The FP8 validation covers 900B tokens of Ling-1T training (Section 5.1), but the full pre-training consumes 20T tokens (Section 3.2.1). The paper does not clarify whether the 900B-token FP8 validation represents the full training run or a subset. If FP8 stability has only been validated on a fraction of total training, there remains uncertainty about precision accumulation over the complete training trajectory.

### 6.6 Summary of Limitations

| Category | Limitation | Severity | Evidence |
|----------|-----------|----------|----------|
| **Architecture** | Shallow MoE routing imbalance causes OOM risks | Moderate | Section 5.6 |
| **Architecture** | GQA constrains long-context efficiency | Moderate | Conclusion |
| **Performance** | Knowledge benchmark deficits (MMLU, GPQA) | Moderate | Table 8 |
| **Performance** | Multilingual capability gaps (MMMLU −10 points) | Significant | Tables 6-7 |
| **Performance** | Seed-OSS-36B exceeds Ling-flash-2.0 on MATH | Moderate | Table 3 |
| **Infrastructure** | Computation-communication overlap saw limited gains | Moderate | Section 5.6 |
| **Infrastructure** | Heterogeneous PP requires balanced routing | Moderate | Section 5.2 |
| **Evaluation** | Inference latency not quantified | Significant | Section 5.5.3 |
| **Evaluation** | Long-context benchmarks incomplete for all scales | Moderate | Section 5.5.3 |
| **Training** | Reasoning data curation is resource-intensive | Moderate | Introduction |
| **Open Questions** | Effective reasoning depth not quantified | Significant | Conclusion |
| **Open Questions** | Agentic behavior remains under development | Significant | Conclusion |
| **Open Questions** | Generalization beyond math/code not extensively tested | Moderate | Benchmark suite |

**Overall Assessment:** Ling 2.0's limitations are primarily **scope-related** rather than **fundamental flaws**. The model explicitly optimizes for reasoning efficiency in English and Chinese, accepting trade-offs in multilingual capability and pure knowledge recall. Infrastructure constraints (routing imbalance, limited overlap gains) reflect the challenges of trillion-scale MoE training rather than design errors. The most significant gaps are in evaluation transparency (inference latency, compute costs) and open questions about reasoning depth and agentic extension—areas the authors acknowledge as future work.

For practitioners, the key takeaway is that Ling 2.0 excels at its stated purpose (efficient reasoning in math/code domains) but should not be assumed to generalize equally well to knowledge-intensive, multilingual, or agentic tasks without additional validation. The limitations are transparently reported, enabling informed deployment decisions rather than representing hidden weaknesses.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how Ling 2.0 reshapes the landscape of large-scale model development, identifies concrete research directions it enables, outlines practical deployment scenarios, and provides guidance on when to adopt this approach versus alternatives.

### 7.1 Landscape Impact: Shifting the Trillion-Scale Development Paradigm

Ling 2.0 represents more than an incremental model release—it establishes a **methodological blueprint** for trillion-scale reasoning model development that fundamentally changes how organizations can approach foundation model training.

**From "Bet-the-Company" to Iterative Engineering.** Prior to Ling 2.0, trillion-parameter model development required committing enormous computational resources without reliable intermediate validation. The Ling Scaling Laws and Wind Tunnel framework (Section 2.3.3) transform this into an iterative process where architectural decisions can be validated at 500M–8B parameter scales with confidence they extrapolate 100× to trillion-parameter models. The paper states this reduces validation costs to "under 1% of a full training run" while maintaining prediction accuracy within 0.01 loss error. This shifts trillion-scale development from a high-risk gamble into a manageable engineering workflow.

**Sparse Activation as the Default for Reasoning.** The demonstration that 3.5% activation ratios (8 of 256 experts) can achieve 7× efficiency leverage while maintaining or exceeding dense model performance (Section 3.3.2) challenges the assumption that dense architectures are necessary for high-capability reasoning. Table 8 shows Ling-1T outperforming DeepSeek-V3.1 on AIME25 (70.42% vs. 55.21%) despite activating only 51B parameters versus DeepSeek-V3.1's 671B total. This establishes high-sparsity MoE as a viable—and often superior—architecture for reasoning-optimized models.

**FP8 Training Becomes Production-Viable.** By demonstrating stable full-scale FP8 training over 900B tokens with only 0.25% loss difference from BF16 (Section 5.1, Figure 15), Ling 2.0 removes a major barrier to low-precision training adoption. The FP8 Training Safeguard System with quantifiable metrics (underflow and distortion tracking in Figure 16) provides a reproducible framework that other organizations can adopt rather than treating FP8 as experimental.

**Reasoning as a Pretraining Objective, Not Post-Training Add-On.** The CoT pre-activation ablation (Section 3.3.2, Tables 2–4) demonstrates that introducing Chain-of-Thought data during mid-training produces 21× relative gains on AIME25 for Ling-mini-2.0-base (2.08% without CoT vs. 43.75% with CoT). This reframes reasoning capability as something that must be embedded during pretraining rather than solely acquired through post-training. Organizations building reasoning models should reconsider their data composition strategies accordingly.

**Open-Source Trillion-Scale Models Become Accessible.** By releasing code, model weights, and detailed methodology (Sections 2–5), Ling 2.0 provides a reference implementation that lowers barriers for organizations without hyperscale resources. The 7× efficiency leverage means organizations can potentially fine-tune or deploy reasoning-optimized models with substantially reduced infrastructure requirements compared to dense trillion-parameter alternatives.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The Ling 2.0 framework opens several concrete research avenues that build upon its innovations while addressing identified limitations.

#### 7.2.1 Architectural Extensions

**Linear and Sparse Attention for Long-Context Efficiency.** The Conclusion explicitly states that "current grouped-query attention (GQA) architecture constrains efficiency in long-context scenarios; ongoing work explores linear and sparse-attention designs to further improve scalability" (Section 6). This is a direct response to the incomplete long-context benchmark evaluation noted in Section 5.5.3. Follow-up work should:

- Evaluate linear attention variants (e.g., RetNet, Mamba-style architectures) integrated with the Ling 2.0 MoE backbone
- Test whether sparse attention patterns can reduce the O(n²) complexity bottleneck while maintaining the 128K context capability demonstrated in Figure 6
- Measure whether attention modifications affect the 7× efficiency leverage claim

**Dynamic Expert Activation.** Currently, Ling 2.0 activates exactly 8 experts per token (Section 2.1). Future work could explore:

- Adaptive activation where the number of experts varies based on input complexity
- Token-level routing confidence thresholds that skip expert computation for "easy" tokens
- Whether dynamic activation preserves the stable training dynamics achieved with fixed activation

**Deeper Reasoning Chains.** The Conclusion acknowledges that "effective reasoning length and depth still have room for enhancement" (Section 6). Research directions include:

- Extending Evo-CoT (Section 4.2) to support longer CoT sequences without degradation
- Investigating whether mid-training CoT pre-activation can be extended beyond 600B tokens (Section 3.2.2.2) for deeper reasoning patterns
- Evaluating whether sentence-level LPO (Section 4.2.2) generalizes to paragraph-level or multi-paragraph reasoning units

#### 7.2.2 Infrastructure Optimizations

**Computation-Communication Overlapping.** Section 5.6 explicitly identifies this as "a key avenue for improving large-scale MoE performance" despite limited gains in Ling 2.0. Follow-up work should:

- Jointly optimize routing balance and EP (Expert Parallelism) configuration to reduce the bottleneck effect from the slowest rank
- Explore whether larger EP configurations can be stabilized through improved load balancing algorithms
- Quantify the theoretical maximum throughput improvement if routing imbalance is resolved

**Heterogeneous Hardware Training.** Figure 21 shows loss alignment between Hopper and non-Hopper GPUs for Ling-flash-2.0, but Section 5.4.3 notes this validation is limited. Research should:

- Extend cross-platform validation to Ling-1T scale
- Investigate whether FP8 operator implementations can be standardized across GPU architectures to reduce the "one-thousandth" mean error
- Explore training on mixed-precision clusters where different nodes have different capabilities

**Checkpoint Management at Scale.** The WSM scheduler requires storing and merging up to N=32 top checkpoints (Section 3.2.3). For Ling-1T, this creates significant storage demands. Research directions include:

- Efficient checkpoint compression techniques that preserve merge accuracy
- Distributed checkpoint storage architectures that reduce I/O bottlenecks
- Whether checkpoint merging can be performed incrementally during training rather than post-hoc

#### 7.2.3 Training Methodology Extensions

**Domain-Specific Reasoning Pre-Activation.** The CoT pre-activation strategy (Section 3.2.2.2) was validated primarily on mathematical reasoning. Follow-up work should test whether this approach generalizes to:

- Scientific hypothesis generation where ground truth is less formalized
- Legal argumentation requiring multi-step logical deduction
- Medical diagnosis reasoning with uncertain outcomes
- Strategic planning with long-horizon consequences

**Multilingual Reasoning Optimization.** Section 3.1.3 notes that multilingual data constitutes only 4% of pre-training, and Tables 6–7 show MMMLU deficits of 9–10 points versus competitors. Research should explore:

- Whether reasoning-oriented multilingual corpora (math/code in multiple languages) improve multilingual reasoning without degrading English/Chinese performance
- Optimal language family distributions beyond the current Romance 50%, Germanic 10%, Slavic 3% split (Appendix B.2)
- Whether vocabulary expansion beyond 156K tokens improves low-resource language performance

**Foundation LLMs Software Engineering.** Section 5.4 proposes the 4C principle (Correct, Consistent, Complete, Co-Design) as a research domain "worthy of in-depth exploration." This opens avenues for:

- Formal verification methods for training framework correctness
- Automated detection of algorithm-system mismatches during development
- Standardized benchmarks for framework iteration velocity and numerical integrity

#### 7.2.4 Agentic Extensions

**From Reflex to Deep Thinking.** The paper distinguishes Ling 2.0 as "reflex-grade non-thinking (instruct) models" while noting "the Ring series (Ling-Team, 2025), built upon the same Ling 2.0 base, extends toward deep thinking models" (Introduction, paragraph 8). Follow-up research should:

- Characterize the architectural or training modifications that transform reflex models into deep thinking models
- Evaluate whether the Ling Scaling Laws remain valid for deep thinking architectures
- Determine whether Evo-CoT's adaptive reasoning depth (Section 4.2) can be extended to support autonomous multi-turn planning

**Tool Use and Environment Interaction.** Section 4.2.1 mentions task-specific rewards including "visual reward for front-end engineering" (VAR system in Figure 9). This suggests pathways toward:

- Integration with code execution environments for iterative debugging
- Browser interaction for web-based task completion
- Database operations for data-driven reasoning tasks

The BFCL-V3 results in Table 8 (Ling-1T at 69.64% vs. DeepSeek-V3.1 at 52.67%) indicate strong function-calling capability that could serve as a foundation for agentic systems.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

Ling 2.0's design choices make it particularly well-suited for specific application domains while less optimal for others.

#### 7.3.1 High-Value Application Domains

**Mathematical Problem Solving and Education.** With AIME25 scores of 70.42% (Ling-1T Instruct, Table 8) and strong performance across MATH, OlympiadBench, and HMMT25, Ling 2.0 is well-positioned for:

- Automated math tutoring systems that provide step-by-step solutions
- Competition math training platforms
- Mathematical proof verification assistance
- STEM education content generation

The CoT pre-activation ensures the model produces coherent reasoning chains rather than just final answers, which is critical for educational applications.

**Code Generation and Software Engineering.** Strong performance on LiveCodeBench (61.68% for Ling-1T, Table 8), CodeForces (1901 rating), and FullStack Bench (56.55%) supports:

- IDE autocomplete and code completion tools
- Automated code review and bug detection
- Full-stack application generation from specifications
- SQL query generation and database optimization (BIRD-SQL at 52.38%)

The VAR system (Figure 9) for front-end evaluation suggests particular strength in UI/UX code generation where visual output matters.

**Scientific and Technical Reasoning.** Performance on GPQA-Diamond (72.98%), OlympiadBench-Stem (91.30%), and MedXpertQA (22.33%) indicates capability in:

- Scientific literature analysis and hypothesis generation
- Technical documentation synthesis
- Research assistant applications for STEM domains
- Medical reasoning support (though MedXpertQA scores suggest room for improvement)

**Financial and Operations Optimization.** Strong FinanceReasoning scores (87.45% for Ling-1T, Table 8) and Optibench performance (74.71%) support:

- Financial analysis and reporting automation
- Supply chain optimization
- Production planning under constraints
- Operations research problem solving

#### 7.3.2 Deployment Scenarios

**Cloud-Based Reasoning Services.** The 7× efficiency leverage makes Ling-1T more economically viable for cloud deployment than dense trillion-parameter alternatives. Organizations can:

- Offer reasoning-as-a-service APIs with lower inference costs
- Support higher concurrency with the same hardware budget
- Reduce latency through the 15% MFU gain from FP8 training (Section 5.1)

**On-Premises Deployment for Sensitive Domains.** The open-source release enables deployment in environments where data cannot leave organizational boundaries:

- Financial institutions requiring data sovereignty
- Healthcare organizations with HIPAA compliance needs
- Government agencies with security classification requirements

**Fine-Tuning for Domain Specialization.** The Decoupled Fine-Tuning framework (Section 4.1) provides a starting point for domain adaptation:

- Legal firms can fine-tune on case law and legal documents
- Research institutions can adapt to specific scientific domains
- Enterprises can customize for proprietary workflows and terminology

#### 7.3.3 Use Cases Where Ling 2.0 Is Less Optimal

**Pure Knowledge Retrieval.** Table 8 shows Ling-1T trailing DeepSeek-V3.1 on MMLU (86.03% vs. 88.44%) and GPQA-Diamond (72.98% vs. 76.23%). For applications prioritizing factual recall over reasoning:

- Dense models or models with higher knowledge corpus proportions may be preferable
- Ling 2.0 should be augmented with retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) for knowledge-intensive tasks

**Multilingual Applications.** MMMLU deficits of 9–10 points (Tables 6–7) suggest Ling 2.0 is optimized for English and Chinese. For global deployments requiring strong performance across many languages:

- Models with higher multilingual data proportions may be more appropriate
- Additional fine-tuning on target language corpora would be necessary

**Real-Time Low-Latency Inference.** While the paper reports training efficiency gains, inference latency benchmarks are not thoroughly quantified (Section 5.5.3). For applications requiring sub-100ms response times:

- Smaller models (Ling-mini-2.0 at 16B) may be more appropriate than Ling-1T
- Distillation to smaller architectures should be considered

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

This subsection provides practical guidance on when to adopt Ling 2.0 methodologies versus alternatives, based on organizational constraints and use case requirements.

#### 7.4.1 When to Adopt Ling 2.0 Architecture

**Adopt Ling 2.0 MoE Design When:**

| Condition | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Computational budget is constrained** | 7× efficiency leverage reduces training and inference costs (Section 3.3.2) |
| **Reasoning capability is the primary objective** | CoT pre-activation and Evo-CoT are optimized for multi-step deduction (Section 4.2) |
| **You have infrastructure for distributed training** | MoE requires Expert Parallelism and careful load balancing (Section 5.3) |
| **You can invest in data curation** | Ling Math and Ling Code corpora required multi-stage pipelines (Section 3.1.2) |

**Prefer Dense Architectures When:**

| Condition | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Maximum absolute performance on knowledge tasks is critical** | Dense models show slight advantages on MMLU, GPQA (Table 8) |
| **Infrastructure for MoE routing is unavailable** | MoE requires specialized communication infrastructure (Section 5.3.1) |
| **Training stability is the highest priority** | Dense models have more predictable training dynamics |
| **Multilingual capability across many languages is required** | Ling 2.0 shows MMMLU deficits versus some competitors (Tables 6–7) |

#### 7.4.2 When to Use Ling Scaling Laws and Wind Tunnel

**Adopt Wind Tunnel Methodology When:**

- You are developing models at scales where full experimentation is prohibitively expensive
- You need to validate architectural innovations before committing to full-scale training
- You have resources to train 5 small models (500M–8B) but not one large model
- You require prediction accuracy within 0.01 loss error for decision-making (Section 2.3.3)

**Use Traditional Scaling Laws When:**

- You are training dense architectures (Ling Scaling Laws are MoE-specific)
- Your compute budget allows direct full-scale ablation studies
- You are working at scales below 10B parameters where extrapolation uncertainty is lower

#### 7.4.3 When to Apply FP8 Training

**Adopt Full-Scale FP8 When:**

| Condition | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Memory is a bottleneck** | FP8 reduces memory use by over 15% (Introduction, Infrastructure bullet 1) |
| **You can implement precision safeguards** | FP8 Training Safeguard System is essential for stability (Section 5.1.1) |
| **You have FP8-capable hardware** | Hopper GPUs or equivalent required for efficient FP8 GEMM |
| **15% MFU gain justifies implementation effort** | Quantifiable efficiency improvement (Section 5.1) |

**Stick with BF16 When:**

- Your hardware does not support efficient FP8 operations
- You cannot implement real-time precision monitoring
- Training stability is more critical than throughput
- You are training models smaller than 10B parameters where memory is less constrained

#### 7.4.4 When to Use WSM Scheduler

**Adopt WSM When:**

- You want flexibility to extend training without committing to decay schedule upfront
- You can afford to store multiple checkpoints for merging (N=32 for Ling 2.0, Section 3.2.3)
- You seek +1 to +2 point improvements across benchmark categories (Figure 7)
- You are training at scales where checkpoint storage is manageable

**Use Traditional LR Decay When:**

- Checkpoint storage is a hard constraint
- You have well-validated decay schedules from prior training runs
- You are training small models where the +1–2 point gain is less impactful

#### 7.4.5 Integration Checklist for Practitioners

For organizations seeking to adopt Ling 2.0 methodologies, the following checklist summarizes key prerequisites:

**Infrastructure Requirements:**
- [ ] GPU cluster with support for Expert Parallelism (EP) and Pipeline Parallelism (PP)
- [ ] FP8-capable hardware (e.g., Hopper architecture) if adopting low-precision training
- [ ] Sufficient storage for checkpoint management (N=32 checkpoints for WSM)
- [ ] High-bandwidth interconnect for MoE routing communication

**Data Requirements:**
- [ ] Reasoning-oriented corpora (math, code) comprising 30–46% of pre-training data
- [ ] Multi-stage data cleaning and filtering pipelines
- [ ] CoT data for mid-training pre-activation (600B tokens for Ling 2.0 scale)
- [ ] Decontamination procedures to prevent benchmark leakage

**Engineering Requirements:**
- [ ] Modified training framework supporting heterogeneous pipeline scheduling (Section 5.2)
- [ ] FP8 Training Safeguard System with underflow and distortion monitoring
- [ ] Checkpoint merging infrastructure for WSM scheduler
- [ ] Reward computation system supporting 40K+ concurrent requests (Section 4.4)

**Evaluation Requirements:**
- [ ] Benchmark suite covering math, code, reasoning, knowledge, and alignment
- [ ] Pass@k metrics for coding and reasoning tasks
- [ ] LLM-as-a-Judge for fill-in-the-blank benchmarks (Section 4.6)
- [ ] ApexEval or similar for checkpoint selection before RL (Section 4.5)

### 7.5 Summary: The Path Forward

Ling 2.0 establishes that **sparse activation, when properly aligned with reasoning objectives, enables scalable and efficient intelligence** (Abstract). The implications extend beyond this specific model family:

1. **Trillion-scale development becomes iterative rather than speculative** through the Wind Tunnel framework
2. **Reasoning capability must be embedded during pretraining**, not solely acquired through post-training
3. **Low-precision training becomes production-viable** with systematic safeguard systems
4. **Open-source trillion-parameter models are achievable** with coordinated architecture, training, and infrastructure innovations

The released models (Ling-mini-2.0, Ling-flash-2.0, Ling-1T), code repository, and detailed methodology provide a foundation for the research community to build upon. The Ring series mentioned in the Introduction represents the next step toward "deep thinking models," while the 4C Software Engineering principle (Section 5.4) opens a new research domain for foundation model development practices.

For practitioners, the key takeaway is that Ling 2.0 excels at its stated purpose—**efficient reasoning in math, code, and structured deduction domains**—but should be evaluated against specific use case requirements regarding multilingual capability, knowledge recall, and inference latency. The transparent reporting of limitations (Section 6) enables informed adoption decisions rather than representing hidden weaknesses.

The work demonstrates that the frontier of open-source language models continues to advance through coordinated innovations across algorithm, data, and systems—not through any single breakthrough alone. Future progress will likely follow this integrated approach, with Ling 2.0 providing a reference architecture for the next generation of reasoning-optimized foundation models.
