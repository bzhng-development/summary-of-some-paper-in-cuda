## 1. Executive Summary
This work uncovers a fundamental bias in group-relative advantage estimation used in Reinforcement Learning from Verifier Rewards (RLVR) methods like GRPO, proving theoretically that it systematically underestimates advantages for hard prompts and overestimates them for easy prompts. To address this, the authors propose History-Aware Adaptive Difficulty Weighting (HA-DW), an adaptive reweighting scheme that consistently improves performance across five mathematical reasoning benchmarks, such as raising Qwen3-4B-Base average accuracy from 46.5% to 48.7% over standard GRPO. These results demonstrate that correcting biased advantage estimation is critical for robust RLVR training, enabling a better exploration-exploitation balance without the computational cost of increasing rollout counts.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this work, we must first establish the landscape of Reinforcement Learning from Verifier Rewards (RLVR) and the specific mechanical trade-offs involved in training reasoning-oriented Large Language Models (LLMs).

### The Rise of Group-Based RLVR
Following the success of models like DeepSeek-R1, RLVR has become a dominant paradigm for post-training LLMs on complex reasoning tasks (Introduction). In standard Reinforcement Learning (RL), an agent learns by maximizing rewards. A critical component of policy optimization algorithms (like PPO) is the **advantage function**, which measures how much better a specific action is compared to the average expected outcome.

Traditionally, estimating this advantage requires a **critic model**—a separate neural network trained to predict value estimates. However, training a critic alongside a large language model is computationally expensive and often unstable. To bypass this, **group-based methods** like GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) emerged (Shao et al., 2024). Instead of a critic, GRPO samples a group of $G$ responses (rollouts) for each prompt and uses the group's average reward as a baseline.

The group-relative advantage $\hat{A}_{t,i}$ for response $i$ at step $t$ is calculated as:

$$
\hat{A}_{t,i} = r_{t,i} - \hat{p}_t, \quad \text{where} \quad \hat{p}_t = \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^{G} r_{t,i}
$$

Here, $r_{t,i}$ is the binary reward (0 or 1) for the $i$-th response, and $\hat{p}_t$ is the empirical success rate within that group. This design is appealing because it eliminates the need for a learned critic, reducing memory overhead and simplifying the training pipeline.

### The Specific Problem: Statistical Bias in Small Groups
While group-based methods avoid the complexity of critics, they introduce a subtle statistical problem that this paper identifies as a fundamental gap. The core assumption of GRPO is that the group average $\hat{p}_t$ is a reliable estimator of the true expected reward $p_t$ (the probability the model solves the prompt correctly).

However, in practice, computational constraints limit the group size $G$ to small numbers (typically $G=8$). The paper argues that when $G$ is small, the estimator $\hat{p}_t$ is not just noisy—it is **systematically biased** relative to $p_t$, particularly when we account for how training algorithms handle "degenerate" groups.

In RLVR, if a group yields all correct responses ($R=G$) or all incorrect responses ($R=0$), the advantage becomes zero for all samples, providing no learning signal. These groups are often discarded or ignored (Section 2.2). The paper defines the **effective update regime** as the event $S := \{1 \le R \le G-1\}$.

The critical gap uncovered is that conditioning on this event $S$ distorts the expectation of the baseline. As stated in Section 2.2:

> "The group-relative advantage estimator is inherently biased relative to the true (expected) advantage... it systematically underestimates advantages for hard prompts and overestimates them for easy prompts."

This happens because for **hard prompts** (where true success rate $p_t < 0.5$), groups that survive the filtering condition $S$ are those that managed to get *at least one* correct answer by chance. This forces the empirical baseline $\hat{p}_t$ to be higher than the true $p_t$, thereby shrinking the calculated advantage for the correct responses. Conversely, for **easy prompts**, the surviving groups are those that made *at least one* mistake, forcing $\hat{p}_t$ lower than $p_t$, inflating the advantage.

### Why This Problem Matters
This bias is not merely a theoretical curiosity; it has direct consequences for model capability and training efficiency.

1.  **Imbalanced Exploration-Exploitation:** The bias causes the policy to "under-learn" from hard questions and "over-exploit" easy ones. Since reasoning capabilities are often bottlenecked by the hardest problems, suppressing their learning signal stalls progress on complex tasks.
2.  **Training Stability:** As illustrated in **Figure 1(b)**, the bias can be substantial even with moderate rollout counts (e.g., 8 vs. 128). Relying on biased signals can lead to unstable convergence or suboptimal policy plateaus.
3.  **Computational Cost of Naive Fixes:** A straightforward way to reduce this bias is to increase the group size $G$. However, generating multiple rollouts per prompt is computationally expensive. **Table 3** (Appendix E.2) shows that while increasing rollouts from 8 to 16 improves performance, it incurs significant memory and time costs, and eventually hits out-of-memory (OOM) limits (e.g., at 32 rollouts).

### Prior Approaches and Their Shortcomings
Several variants of GRPO have been proposed to improve stability and performance, but none directly address this underlying statistical bias:

*   **PPO (Schulman et al., 2017):** Uses a learned critic. While theoretically sound, it requires training an additional model, increasing complexity and memory usage.
*   **GRPO Variants (GSPO, DAPO, Dr.GRPO):** These methods introduce modifications like sequence-level clipping (GSPO), decoupled clipping (DAPO), or removing heuristic normalizations (Dr.GRPO) (Section 6). However, the paper notes that these variants still "adopt static prompt difficulty and suffer from insufficient exploration of model's capability" (Section 6). They tweak the optimization objective but retain the biased group-relative advantage estimator $\hat{A}_{t,i}$.
*   **Brute-Force Sampling:** Increasing the number of rollouts reduces variance and bias but scales linearly with compute cost, making it impractical for large-scale training (Appendix E.2).

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself as the first to provide a **theoretical characterization** of this specific bias in group-relative RLVR. Unlike prior work that focuses on optimization stability (e.g., clipping mechanisms) or sampling efficiency (e.g., dynamic batch sizes), this work targets the **estimator itself**.

The authors propose **History-Aware Adaptive Difficulty Weighting (HA-DW)**, which does not replace GRPO but acts as a plug-and-play correction module. As depicted in **Figure 3**, HA-DW introduces a "difficulty anchor" that aggregates historical reward trends across batches. This allows the algorithm to correct the advantage estimate based on long-term model capability rather than relying solely on the noisy, biased snapshot from the current small group.

By addressing the root cause—the biased advantage estimation—the paper aims to unlock performance gains without the computational penalty of increasing rollouts, bridging the gap between the efficiency of critic-free methods and the statistical robustness of larger-sample estimators.

## 3. Technical Approach

This section provides a complete, standalone explanation of the History-Aware Adaptive Difficulty Weighting (HA-DW) framework. We walk through every component, equation, and design decision so that a reader unfamiliar with the paper can understand exactly how the system works and why each choice was made.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

HA-DW is a plug-and-play correction module that sits on top of existing group-relative reinforcement learning algorithms like GRPO to fix biased advantage estimates. The solution works by maintaining a running memory of the model's capability across training batches and using this historical anchor to reweight advantage estimates—amplifying signals from hard prompts where the model tends to under-learn and suppressing signals from easy prompts where the model tends to over-exploit.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The HA-DW system consists of two collaborative phases that operate in sequence during each training step. **Phase 1 (Evolving Difficulty Anchor)** takes the current batch's accuracy observation and combines it with historical capability beliefs through a Kalman-style update to produce an updated capability estimate $C_t$ that serves as a difficulty reference point. **Phase 2 (Adaptive Reweighting)** takes this capability estimate, compares it against the current group's empirical success rate to compute a history-based difficulty measure, and then generates a multiplicative reweighting factor $\Phi_{t,i}$ that adjusts the advantage term in the policy optimization objective. The output flows into the standard GRPO-style loss function, where the reweighted advantages drive parameter updates. Information flows unidirectionally: batch rewards feed the capability tracker, the tracker feeds the difficulty calculator, and the difficulty calculator feeds the loss function.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We first explain the **evolving difficulty anchor** mechanism because it establishes the historical memory that all subsequent calculations depend on.
- We then detail the **adaptive reweighting formula** because it shows exactly how the difficulty anchor translates into concrete advantage corrections.
- We next present the **modified objective function** to show where HA-DW integrates into existing RL algorithms like GRPO, GSPO, and DAPO.
- We then cover the **theoretical guarantees** that prove HA-DW actually reduces bias under specified conditions.
- Finally, we discuss **hyperparameter choices and ablation results** to explain what values work in practice and why the dynamic approach outperforms fixed alternatives.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This paper presents an algorithmic correction method for reinforcement learning, specifically targeting the biased advantage estimation problem in group-relative policy optimization. The core idea is to introduce cross-batch historical information into the advantage computation so that the model can distinguish between prompts that are genuinely difficult versus prompts that merely appeared difficult due to small-sample noise.

**The Data Pipeline: What Happens First, Second, Third**

At each training step $t$, the system processes one batch of prompts through a fixed sequence of operations. First, the model generates $G$ responses (rollouts) for each prompt in the batch, where $G=8$ in all reported experiments (Table 8). Second, each response receives a binary reward $r_{t,i} \in \{0, 1\}$ from a verifier, and the system computes the batch-level accuracy $y_t$ as the proportion of correct responses. Third, the system updates the evolving capability anchor $C_t$ using a Kalman-style belief update that blends the new observation with historical information. Fourth, the system computes a history-based difficulty measure for each prompt by comparing its group success rate against the capability anchor. Fifth, the system generates a reweighting factor $\Phi_{t,i}$ for each response using an exponential function of the difficulty measure. Sixth, the reweighted advantages are plugged into the policy optimization objective, and gradients are computed to update the model parameters. This entire pipeline repeats for each training batch, with the capability anchor $C_t$ persisting across batches to accumulate long-term reward trends.

**Component 1: Evolving Difficulty Anchor (Historical Memory)**

The first innovation in HA-DW is the introduction of a cross-batch difficulty anchor that tracks the model's solving capability over time. The authors treat the model's capability $C_t$ as a latent belief state that evolves as training progresses. At training step $t$, the system observes the current batch's prompt accuracy, denoted $y_t$, which is computed as:

$$
y_t = \frac{K_t}{B_t}, \quad \text{where} \quad K_t = \sum_{i=1}^{B_t} r_{t,i}
$$

Here, $B_t$ denotes the total number of responses in batch $t$, and $K_t$ counts how many of those responses received a correct reward. This batch accuracy $y_t$ serves as the observation that updates the model's capability belief.

The capability update follows a Kalman-style recursive formula that blends the prior belief $C^-_t$ with the new observation $y_t$:

$$
C^+_t = (1 - \eta_t) C^-_t + \eta_t y_t, \quad \eta_t \in [0, 1]
$$

The forgetting factor $\eta_t$ controls how much weight the update gives to new information versus historical information. A larger $\eta_t$ means the anchor adapts quickly to recent performance, while a smaller $\eta_t$ preserves long-term trends and reduces noise. The key design choice here is that $\eta_t$ is not fixed—it is dynamically modulated based on model stability.

To compute the adaptive forgetting factor, the system first calculates the average capability belief over the previous $m$ batches:

$$
\bar{C}_t = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{j=1}^{m} C_{t-j}
$$

The paper does not explicitly state the value of $m$ in the main text, but this represents a sliding window of historical batches used to assess stability. The system then computes the standard deviation of capability beliefs over this window:

$$
\sigma_t = \sqrt{\frac{1}{m} \sum_{j=1}^{m} \left(C_{t-j} - \bar{C}_t\right)^2}
$$

The adaptive forgetting factor is then defined as:

$$
\eta_t = \eta \cdot \sigma_t
$$

where $\eta$ is a task-dependent hyperparameter that scales the influence of the standard deviation. The intuition behind this design is that during early training stages, when the model's capability is shifting rapidly, $\sigma_t$ will be large, leading to a larger $\eta_t$ that allows the anchor to track rapid changes. In later, more stable training stages, $\sigma_t$ decreases, leading to a smaller $\eta_t$ that preserves historical information and reduces sensitivity to batch-level noise.

Between consecutive training steps, the posterior belief $C^+_t$ becomes the prior belief for the next batch:

$$
C^+_t \rightarrow C^-_{t+1}
$$

This ensures that the capability anchor accumulates information across the entire training trajectory rather than resetting at each batch. The paper also mentions an alternative "hard update" variant in Appendix F, where the belief update simplifies to a direct average over the last $h$ batches:

$$
C^+_t = \frac{1}{h} \left(\sum_{j=1}^{h-1} y_{t-j} + y_t\right)
$$

This hard update variant ignores short-term oscillations but significantly simplifies the algorithm. However, the main experiments use the Kalman-style soft update described above.

**Component 2: History-Aware Adaptive Difficulty Weighting (Advantage Correction)**

The second innovation is the actual reweighting mechanism that uses the capability anchor to correct biased advantage estimates. The system first defines a history-based prompt difficulty measure:

$$
\text{diff}^{\text{his}}_t = \hat{p}_t - C^-_t
$$

Here, $\hat{p}_t$ is the empirical success rate within the current group (the same group baseline used in standard GRPO), and $C^-_t$ is the prior capability belief from the evolving anchor. This difference captures both the magnitude and direction of a prompt's difficulty relative to the model's current expected capability. If $\text{diff}^{\text{his}}_t$ is negative, the prompt performed worse than the model's historical capability suggests it should have, indicating a potentially hard prompt. If $\text{diff}^{\text{his}}_t$ is positive, the prompt performed better than expected, indicating a potentially easy prompt.

To determine the direction of the advantage adjustment, the system computes a direction indicator:

$$
D_{t,i} = -\text{sgn}(\hat{A}_{t,i}) \cdot \text{sgn}(\text{diff}^{\text{his}}_t)
$$

where $\text{sgn}(\cdot)$ denotes the sign function that returns $+1$ for positive inputs, $-1$ for negative inputs, and $0$ for zero. The term $\hat{A}_{t,i}$ is the original group-relative advantage for response $i$. This direction indicator ensures that the reweighting pushes in the correct direction: for hard prompts where advantages are underestimated, the system amplifies the advantage; for easy prompts where advantages are overestimated, the system suppresses the advantage.

The system then quantifies the magnitude of adjustment using the absolute history-based difficulty:

$$
M_t = \left|\text{diff}^{\text{his}}_t\right|
$$

This magnitude $M_t$ measures how far the prompt's performance deviates from the model's current capability belief. Larger deviations indicate more extreme prompts (either very hard or very easy relative to capability), which should receive stronger reweighting.

The history-aware reweighting factor is then defined as:

$$
\Phi_{t,i} = \lambda_{\text{scale}} \cdot \exp(D_{t,i} \cdot M_t)
$$

where $\lambda_{\text{scale}}$ is a scaling constant hyperparameter. The exponential form ensures smooth and multiplicative adjustment of advantage weights. When $D_{t,i} \cdot M_t$ is positive, the exponential term exceeds 1, amplifying the advantage. When $D_{t,i} \cdot M_t$ is negative, the exponential term is less than 1, suppressing the advantage. The $\lambda_{\text{scale}}$ parameter provides an additional degree of freedom to tune the overall magnitude of the correction.

**Component 3: Modified Policy Optimization Objective**

The HA-DW correction integrates directly into the group-relative policy optimization objective. The standard Group-PO objective is defined as:

$$
J_{\text{group}}(\theta) = \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^{G} \psi\left(\frac{\pi_\theta(y_{t,i} | x_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_{t,i} | x_t)}\right) \phi(\hat{A}_{t,i})
$$

where $\pi_\theta$ is the current policy, $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$ is the reference (behavior) policy, $\psi(\cdot)$ is a function applied to the importance sampling ratio (such as identity, clipping, or logarithmic transformation), and $\phi(\cdot)$ is a function applied to the advantage term. The HA-DW objective modifies this by inserting the reweighting factor:

$$
L_{\text{HA-DW}}(\theta) = \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^{G} \psi\left(\frac{\pi_\theta(y_{t,i} | x_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_{t,i} | x_t)}\right) \cdot \phi(\hat{A}_{t,i}) \cdot \Phi_{t,i}
$$

This modification is plug-and-play: it can be integrated into GRPO, GSPO, DAPO, or any other group-relative algorithm by simply multiplying the advantage term by $\Phi_{t,i}$. Appendix B provides detailed instantiations for each algorithm. For GRPO specifically, the objective becomes:

$$
J_{\text{GRPO+HA-DW}}(\theta) = \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^{G} \frac{1}{|o_{t,i}|} \sum_{\tau=1}^{|o_{t,i}|} \min\left(r_{t,i,\tau}(\theta) \hat{A}_{t,i,\tau} \cdot \Phi_{t,i}, \text{clip}(r_{t,i,\tau}(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \hat{A}_{t,i,\tau} \cdot \Phi_{t,i}\right)
$$

where $r_{t,i,\tau}(\theta)$ is the token-level importance sampling ratio and $\epsilon$ is the clipping hyperparameter (set to 0.2 for GRPO per Table 8). Similar modifications apply to GSPO and DAPO with their respective objective structures.

**Theoretical Guarantees: Why This Correction Works**

The paper provides formal theoretical analysis showing that HA-DW provably reduces advantage estimation bias under appropriate conditions. Lemma 1 (Baseline Rectification) establishes that there exists a scaling factor $c$ such that adjusting the empirical baseline $\hat{p}_t$ to $\tilde{p}_t = c \cdot \hat{p}_t$ can bring the expected estimate closer to the true expected reward $p_t$. Specifically, for any $\delta \in (0, 1)$, the lemma defines an error bound:

$$
\epsilon_\delta := \sqrt{\frac{1}{2G} \log\left(\frac{2}{\delta(1 - (1-\Delta)^G - \Delta^G)}\right)}
$$

where $\Delta \in (0, 1/2]$ defines the valid range for $p_t$. The lemma then defines feasible ranges for the scaling factor $c$ (denoted $c_{\text{low}}$ and $c_{\text{high}}$) such that choosing $c \in (c_{\text{low}}, c_{\text{high}})$ ensures the rectified baseline expectation falls within $\epsilon$ of the true $p_t$.

Theorem 3 builds on this result to show that HA-DW provably mitigates bias. The theorem states that if there exists a scaling factor $\lambda_{\text{scale}}$ in Equation (16) such that:

$$
\lambda_{\text{scale}} \in \left[\frac{1 + (1-c_{\text{high}})\hat{p}_t}{1-\hat{p}_t} \exp(D_{t,i} M_t), \frac{1 + (1-c_{\text{low}})\hat{p}_t}{1-\hat{p}_t} \exp(D_{t,i} M_t)\right] \cup \left(\frac{c_{\text{low}}}{\exp(D_{t,i} M_t)}, \frac{c_{\text{high}}}{\exp(D_{t,i} M_t)}\right)
$$

then the HA-DW algorithm satisfies:

$$
\left|\mathbb{E}[\hat{A}_{t,i} \cdot \Phi_{t,i} | S] - A_{t,i}\right| < \left|\mathbb{E}[\hat{A}_{t,i} | S] - A_{t,i}\right|
$$

This inequality shows that the reweighted advantage estimate is closer to the true advantage $A_{t,i}$ in expectation than the uncorrected estimate. The theorem provides principled guidance for selecting $\lambda_{\text{scale}}$ in practice, though the exact bounds depend on unknown quantities like $c_{\text{low}}$ and $c_{\text{high}}$.

**Hyperparameter Choices and Ablation Results**

The paper reports extensive ablation studies to determine optimal hyperparameter values. Table 7 shows performance across different $\lambda_{\text{scale}}$ values on Qwen3-4B-Base with GRPO+HA-DW. The results indicate that $\lambda_{\text{scale}} = 1.3$ achieves the best average performance (48.7% across five benchmarks), with $\lambda_{\text{scale}} = 1.5$ performing nearly as well (48.6%). Values below 1.0 or above 1.7 show degraded performance, suggesting that the correction needs to be substantial but not extreme.

Table 2 compares the dynamic capability anchor $C_t$ against fixed threshold alternatives (0.4, 0.5, 0.6). The dynamic $C_t$ achieves 48.7% average accuracy, outperforming all fixed thresholds (48.1%, 47.8%, 48.0% respectively). This ablation validates the design choice to use an evolving anchor rather than a static difficulty threshold. The fixed thresholds still improve over the baseline (46.5%), confirming that even partial bias correction helps, but the dynamic approach captures long-term reward signals more effectively.

Table 8 provides the full hyperparameter configuration used in experiments. Key settings include: learning rate $1 \times 10^{-6}$, training batch size 256, mini-batch size 16, micro-batch size 4, maximum prompt length 1024, maximum response length 4096, and rollout count $G=8$. The clipping hyperparameters vary by algorithm: GRPO uses 0.2, GSPO uses 0.0004 (clip-high) and 0.0003 (clip-low), and DAPO uses 0.28 (clip-high) and 0.2 (clip-low). All experiments run on a single node with 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs using the VeRL framework.

**Design Choices: Why This Approach Over Alternatives**

The authors chose the Kalman-style belief update for the capability anchor because it naturally balances responsiveness to new information with stability from historical data. A simple moving average would treat all past batches equally, potentially over-weighting outdated capability estimates from early training. A fixed threshold would ignore the model's actual learning trajectory. The adaptive forgetting factor $\eta_t = \eta \cdot \sigma_t$ allows the system to automatically adjust its sensitivity based on training dynamics without manual scheduling.

The exponential form of the reweighting factor $\Phi_{t,i} = \lambda_{\text{scale}} \cdot \exp(D_{t,i} \cdot M_t)$ was chosen to ensure smooth, multiplicative adjustments rather than hard thresholds. This avoids discontinuous gradients that could destabilize training. The exponential also naturally produces larger corrections for more extreme difficulty deviations, which aligns with the theoretical finding that bias increases as $p_t$ moves away from 0.5.

The plug-and-play integration design allows HA-DW to work with any group-relative algorithm without modifying their core mechanics. This is important because the bias problem is fundamental to the group-relative advantage estimator itself, not to any specific algorithm's clipping or normalization strategy. By correcting the advantage term directly, HA-DW addresses the root cause rather than symptoms.

**Limitations and Scope**

The paper explicitly notes that HA-DW is restricted to group-relative methods and does not address bias in other RL settings (Limitations section). The theoretical analysis assumes binary rewards, though Appendix D.5 extends the analysis to continuous bounded reward distributions (Beta and truncated Gaussian) and shows the bias phenomenon persists. The capability anchor $C_t$ aggregates information at the batch level, which may miss prompt-specific difficulty patterns. The optimal $\lambda_{\text{scale}}$ value may vary across tasks and model scales, requiring some tuning despite the theoretical guidance from Theorem 3.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section isolates the specific contributions that distinguish this work from prior reinforcement learning literature. We distinguish between **fundamental innovations** (which change our theoretical understanding or architectural assumptions) and **methodological improvements** (which optimize existing frameworks). The paper delivers one fundamental theoretical discovery and two significant methodological innovations that collectively address the efficiency-accuracy trade-off in RLVR.

### 1. Fundamental Discovery: Bias is Structural, Not Just Noisy
Prior work on group-relative policy optimization (such as GRPO and its variants) generally treated the group baseline $\hat{p}_t$ as an unbiased, albeit noisy, estimator of the true expected reward $p_t$. Existing theoretical analyses lacked a detailed characterization of how the estimator behaves under the specific constraints of RLVR training (Xiong et al., 2025; Tan et al., 2025).

This paper provides the first theoretical proof that the bias is **structural and systematic**, arising directly from the algorithm's handling of degenerate groups.
*   **The Mechanism:** As established in **Theorem 1 (Section 2.2)**, the bias is not random variance. It is directionally consistent: the estimator systematically underestimates advantages for hard prompts ($p_t < 0.5$) and overestimates them for easy prompts ($p_t > 0.5$).
*   **The Cause:** This occurs because training updates condition on the non-degenerate event $S := \{1 \le R \le G-1\}$. By discarding groups where all responses are correct or all are incorrect, the algorithm truncates the distribution of the baseline estimator. **Corollary 1** quantifies this, showing that for hard prompts with $p_t < 0.25$ and group size $G \in [2, 8]$, the probability of underestimation exceeds 78%.
*   **Significance:** This shifts the community's understanding of GRPO-style methods. It implies that simply increasing training steps or tweaking clipping ranges (as done in variants like DAPO or GSPO) cannot fully resolve the issue, because the signal itself is distorted. This is a **fundamental theoretical innovation** that necessitates a correction mechanism rather than just optimization tuning.

### 2. Methodological Innovation: Cross-Batch Historical Memory
Standard group-relative algorithms are inherently **intra-batch**; they compute advantages using only the $G$ rollouts generated for the current prompt, ignoring all other data. Variants like GSPO or DAPO modify how advantages are clipped or normalized but remain confined within the current batch's information horizon (Section 6).

HA-DW introduces a **cross-batch difficulty anchor** ($C_t$) that breaks this isolation.
*   **The Difference:** Instead of treating each batch as an independent statistical sample, HA-DW maintains a latent belief state of the model's capability that evolves over time. As described in **Section 3.1**, this anchor integrates long-term reward trends via a Kalman-style update.
*   **Why It Matters:** This allows the algorithm to distinguish between a prompt that is *intrinsically hard* versus a prompt that merely *appeared hard* due to small-sample noise in the current batch. A fixed difficulty threshold cannot adapt to the model's improving capability during training.
*   **Evidence:** **Table 2** provides a critical ablation study comparing the dynamic anchor $C_t$ against fixed thresholds (0.4, 0.5, 0.6). The dynamic approach achieves 48.7% average accuracy, outperforming the best fixed threshold (48.1% at 0.4). This confirms that **adaptive historical context** is superior to static priors, validating the design choice to propagate information across batches.

### 3. Practical Innovation: Decoupling Bias Reduction from Compute Scaling
In standard statistical estimation, the canonical method to reduce bias and variance in an empirical mean is to increase the sample size. In RLVR, this corresponds to increasing the number of rollouts $G$. However, this scales linearly with computational cost and memory usage.

HA-DW demonstrates that **algorithmic correction can substitute for computational scaling**.
*   **The Trade-off:** Typically, one must choose between the efficiency of small $G$ (fast training, high bias) and the accuracy of large $G$ (slow training, low bias). HA-DW disrupts this Pareto frontier.
*   **Evidence:** **Table 3 (Appendix E.2)** compares three settings on Qwen3-4B-Base:
    *   GRPO with $G=8$: 75.4% on MATH500.
    *   GRPO with $G=16$: 76.2% on MATH500.
    *   GRPO+HA-DW with $G=8$: **78.0%** on MATH500.
*   **Significance:** The corrected small-batch method ($G=8$ + HA-DW) not only matches but **surpasses** the uncorrected large-batch method ($G=16$). Furthermore, the paper notes that $G=32$ was impossible due to out-of-memory (OOM) errors. This makes HA-DW a **critical efficiency innovation**, enabling higher performance within fixed hardware constraints rather than requiring expensive cluster scaling.

### 4. Generalizability Across Optimizers
While the theoretical analysis focuses on the group-relative advantage estimator, the solution is designed to be optimizer-agnostic.
*   **The Insight:** The bias resides in the *advantage estimation step*, not the *policy update step*. Therefore, correcting the advantage should benefit any algorithm that relies on group-relative baselines.
*   **Evidence:** **Table 1** validates HA-DW across three distinct algorithms: GRPO, GSPO, and DAPO. In every case, integrating HA-DW yields consistent gains (e.g., DAPO improves from 46.8% to 49.5% average accuracy on Qwen3-4B-Base).
*   **Significance:** This confirms that the bias is a property of the **group-relative paradigm** itself, not a specific implementation flaw in GRPO. It suggests that HA-DW (or similar correction mechanisms) should be considered a standard component for future group-based RLVR systems, similar to how learning rate schedulers are standard in supervised fine-tuning.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the experimental methodology and results. We walk through exactly how the authors validated their claims, what benchmarks they used, and whether the evidence convincingly supports the theoretical discoveries from Section 2. The goal is to help you understand not just *what* numbers were reported, but *why* these particular experiments matter and what they reveal about HA-DW's effectiveness.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: Datasets, Models, and Metrics

**Benchmark Selection Rationale**

The authors evaluate on five mathematical reasoning benchmarks, each serving a distinct purpose in assessing model capability:

- **MATH500**: A 500-problem subset of the MATH dataset, representing standard mathematical reasoning tasks across algebra, geometry, and calculus. This serves as the primary benchmark for comparing methods.
- **AIME25**: American Invitational Mathematics Examination problems, which are significantly harder than standard MATH problems and require multi-step reasoning.
- **AMC23**: American Mathematics Competition problems, positioned between MATH and AIME in difficulty.
- **Minerva**: Scientific reasoning problems that test the model's ability to apply mathematical knowledge to scientific contexts.
- **OlympiadBench**: Olympiad-level problems representing the highest difficulty tier, designed to stress-test advanced reasoning capabilities.

This stratification is critical because the paper's central claim is that HA-DW improves performance specifically on **hard prompts** where bias is most severe. Using benchmarks with varying difficulty levels allows the authors to test whether the correction mechanism actually helps where it matters most.

**Model Selection**

Experiments span three models of different scales and families (Section 5, Appendix C):

- **Qwen3-4B-Base**: A 4-billion parameter base model from the Qwen family.
- **Qwen3-8B-Base**: An 8-billion parameter variant, testing scalability of the method.
- **LLaMA-3.2-3B-Instruct**: A 3-billion parameter instruct-tuned model from Meta, testing generalizability across model families.

This diversity is important because it demonstrates that the bias problem and its correction are not specific to one architecture or scale. If HA-DW only worked on Qwen models, it might suggest architecture-specific interactions rather than a fundamental correction of group-relative estimation.

**Training Setup**

All experiments follow a consistent training protocol (Table 8):

- **Framework**: VeRL (Sheng et al., 2024) for reinforcement learning training.
- **Hardware**: Single node with 8 × NVIDIA A100 GPUs.
- **Batch Configuration**: Training batch size 256, mini-batch size 16, micro-batch size 4.
- **Rollout Count**: $G=8$ responses per prompt for all main experiments.
- **Sequence Lengths**: Maximum prompt length 1024 tokens, maximum response length 4096 tokens.
- **Learning Rate**: $1 \times 10^{-6}$ with AdamW optimizer.
- **Training Epochs**: 3 epochs for GRPO and GSPO, 9 epochs for DAPO (reflecting DAPO's different convergence characteristics).
- **Clipping**: Algorithm-specific clipping hyperparameters (GRPO: 0.2, GSPO: 0.0004/0.0003, DAPO: 0.28/0.2).

The consistent rollout count of $G=8$ is particularly important because this is exactly the regime where Corollary 1 predicts substantial bias (probability of underestimation > 78% for hard prompts with $p_t < 0.25$). If the authors had used $G=32$ or $G=64$, the bias would be naturally reduced, making HA-DW's contribution less visible.

**Baseline Algorithms**

The paper evaluates HA-DW integrated into three representative group-relative algorithms (Section 5, Section 6):

- **GRPO**: The original group-relative policy optimization method.
- **GSPO**: Group Sequence Policy Optimization, which uses sequence-level importance ratios.
- **DAPO**: Decoupled Advantage Policy Optimization, which introduces decoupled clipping and dynamic sampling.

Testing across all three is essential because it isolates whether the improvement comes from HA-DW's correction mechanism or from interactions with specific algorithmic features. If HA-DW only helped GRPO, it might suggest compatibility issues rather than fundamental bias correction.

**Evaluation Metric**

The primary metric is **accuracy** (percentage of correctly solved problems) on each benchmark. For AIME25 and AMC23, the authors report **avg@16**, meaning they sample 16 responses per problem during evaluation and report the average accuracy. This reduces variance on small benchmark sets and provides more reliable comparisons (Appendix C).

### 5.3 Difficulty-Stratified Analysis: Testing the Core Hypothesis

**Performance by Difficulty Level (Figure 1c)**

The paper's central theoretical claim is that group-relative advantage estimation underestimates advantages for hard prompts and overestimates them for easy prompts. If HA-DW corrects this bias, we should see disproportionate improvements on hard problems.

Figure 1(c) presents exactly this analysis, stratifying MATH500 results by difficulty levels:
- **Easy (Level 1)**: Comparable performance between GRPO and GRPO+HA-DW
- **Mid (Levels 2-3)**: Comparable performance between GRPO and GRPO+HA-DW
- **Hard (Levels 4-5)**: GRPO+HA-DW outperforms GRPO by **3.4 percentage points**

This result is critical evidence supporting the theoretical analysis from Section 2.2. The fact that improvements concentrate on hard prompts directly validates Theorem 1's prediction that bias is most severe when $p_t < 0.5$. If HA-DW were simply adding noise or providing a generic regularization effect, we would expect uniform improvements across all difficulty levels.

**Why Easy Prompts Show Limited Improvement:**

For easy prompts where $p_t > 0.5$, the bias causes overestimation of advantages. HA-DW suppresses these inflated advantages, which prevents over-exploitation but doesn't necessarily improve accuracy—the model was already solving these problems correctly. The correction primarily prevents wasted learning signal on problems the model has already mastered.

**Why Hard Prompts Show Large Improvement:**

For hard prompts where $p_t < 0.5$, the bias causes underestimation of advantages. This means correct responses on hard problems receive weaker learning signals than they should, causing the model to "under-learn" from its rare successes. HA-DW amplifies these advantages, allowing the model to better capitalize on correct solutions to difficult problems. This directly addresses the exploration-exploitation imbalance identified in the Introduction.

### 5.4 Training Dynamics: How HA-DW Changes Learning Behavior

**Accuracy and Reward Trajectories (Figure 4)**

Figure 4 shows training dynamics for Qwen3-4B-Base and Qwen3-8B-Base across three metrics:

1. **Average Accuracy**: HA-DW variants converge to higher performance plateaus. The gap between HA-DW and baseline widens over training, suggesting the correction accumulates benefits over time rather than providing a one-time boost.

2. **Training Reward**: HA-DW achieves higher training rewards, indicating the model is successfully solving more problems during training. This is important because it shows HA-DW doesn't just improve evaluation performance through better generalization—it actually improves the learning process itself.

3. **Response Length**: HA-DW encourages longer reasoning chains. The paper notes this "greatly improving its reasoning abilities" (Section 5.1), citing prior work (Jin et al., 2024; DeepSeek-AI, 2025) that longer reasoning chains correlate with better performance on complex tasks.

**Why Response Length Matters:**

The increase in response length is not incidental—it reflects a fundamental change in how the model approaches problems. Standard GRPO, with its biased advantage estimation, may prematurely converge on short, shallow reasoning patterns that work for easy problems but fail on hard ones. By correcting the advantage signal for hard prompts, HA-DW incentivizes the model to invest more computation (longer chains of thought) on problems that require it.

This connects back to the theoretical analysis: when advantages for hard prompts are underestimated, the model receives weak gradients for correct solutions to difficult problems. This discourages exploration of complex reasoning strategies. HA-DW's correction restores appropriate gradient magnitudes, enabling the model to learn sophisticated reasoning patterns.

**Dynamic vs. Fixed Difficulty Threshold (Table 2)**

**Key Insights:**

1. **Any Correction Helps**: All fixed thresholds (0.4, 0.5, 0.6) improve over the baseline (46.5%). This confirms that even crude bias correction provides value, validating the core premise that bias exists and matters.

2. **Dynamic Outperforms Static**: The dynamic $C_t$ achieves 48.7% vs. 48.1% for the best fixed threshold (0.4). While this seems like a small difference (0.6 percentage points), it represents meaningful improvement in a saturated regime where all methods already incorporate some correction.

3. **Why Dynamic Matters**: Fixed thresholds cannot adapt to the model's improving capability during training. A prompt that is "hard" at the start of training may become "easy" after several epochs. The dynamic anchor tracks this evolution, ensuring the correction remains appropriate throughout training.

**Rollout Count Comparison (Table 3, Appendix E.2)**

**Why This Matters:**

Increasing rollouts from 8 to 16 doubles the computational cost of generating training data. Yet HA-DW achieves better results without this cost. The paper notes that $G=32$ was "out of memory" (Table 3 footnote), establishing a hard computational ceiling. HA-DW effectively pushes the performance frontier beyond what's achievable through brute-force sampling alone.

3. **Overcorrection**: Values above 1.7 show degraded performance, suggesting that excessive amplification introduces instability or distorts the learning signal in the opposite direction.

4. **Sensitivity**: The performance curve is relatively smooth around the optimum (1.3-1.5 all achieve >48.5%), indicating the method is not hypersensitive to this hyperparameter. This is important for practical deployment.

### 5.6 Empirical Verification of Bias (Appendix E.1, Figure 6)

**Methodology:**

The authors conducted a direct empirical test of the bias hypothesis:
1. Selected prompts from MATH and DAPO-Math-17k datasets.
2. Evaluated model performance at $G=8$ rollouts (standard training configuration).
3. Identified groups with exactly 1 correct response (for hard prompts) or 1 incorrect response (for easy prompts) at $G=8$.
4. Re-evaluated these same prompts at $G=128$ rollouts to estimate true difficulty.
5. Compared the $G=8$ classification against the $G=128$ ground truth.

**Results (Figure 6):**

- **Hard Prompts (Figure 6a)**: For groups with 1 correct response at $G=8$, 24 out of 50 groups (MATH) and 15 out of 50 groups (DAPO-Math-17k) had fewer than 16 correct responses at $G=128$. This confirms these prompts are genuinely hard, and the $G=8$ estimate underestimated their difficulty.

- **Easy Prompts (Figure 6b)**: For groups with 1 incorrect response at $G=8$, 12 out of 50 groups (MATH) and 21 out of 50 groups (DAPO-Math-17k) had fewer than 16 incorrect responses at $G=128$. This confirms these prompts are genuinely easy, and the $G=8$ estimate overestimated their difficulty.

**Significance:**

This empirical verification directly supports Theorem 1 and Corollary 1. The bias is not just a theoretical artifact—it manifests in actual training data. The fact that a substantial fraction of prompts (roughly 25-40%) exhibit this misclassification at $G=8$ explains why the correction produces meaningful performance gains.

### 5.7 Assessment: Do the Experiments Convincingly Support the Claims?

**Strengths of the Experimental Design:**

1. **Multiple Lines of Evidence**: The paper provides theoretical proofs (Section 2), main results (Table 1), difficulty-stratified analysis (Figure 1c), training dynamics (Figure 4), ablation studies (Tables 2, 3, 7), and empirical bias verification (Figure 6). This multi-faceted approach makes the conclusions robust.

2. **Cross-Algorithm Validation**: Testing HA-DW on GRPO, GSPO, and DAPO demonstrates the bias is fundamental to group-relative estimation, not specific to one algorithm's implementation.

3. **Cross-Model Validation**: Results across Qwen3-4B, Qwen3-8B, and LLaMA-3.2-3B show the method generalizes across scales and families.

4. **Computational Efficiency Demonstration**: Table 3's comparison of $G=8$+HA-DW vs. $G=16$ baseline directly addresses the practical question of whether algorithmic correction can substitute for compute scaling.

5. **Ablation Rigor**: The ablation studies isolate individual components (dynamic anchor, scaling factor, rollout count), allowing readers to understand which aspects drive performance.

**Limitations and Open Questions:**

1. **Domain Restriction**: All experiments are on mathematical reasoning benchmarks. The paper acknowledges this limitation (Limitations section), noting that HA-DW is restricted to group-relative methods and may not generalize to other RL settings. It remains unclear whether the bias manifests similarly in non-mathematical domains (e.g., code generation, dialogue).

2. **Reward Type**: The theoretical analysis assumes binary rewards (pass/fail), though Appendix D.5 extends to continuous bounded rewards. All experiments use binary verification, so the continuous reward case remains empirically untested.

3. **Hyperparameter Tuning**: While Table 7 shows $\lambda_{\text{scale}}$ sensitivity, the paper does not report how this value was selected (grid search, theoretical guidance, or heuristic). The optimal value may vary across tasks.

4. **Long-Term Training Effects**: Experiments run for 3-9 epochs (Table 8). It's unclear whether HA-DW's advantages persist, diminish, or grow over longer training horizons.

5. **Comparison to Critic-Based Methods**: The paper positions HA-DW as an alternative to critic-based methods (like PPO) but does not directly compare against them. A full evaluation would include PPO with a learned value function to establish the complete Pareto frontier.

**Overall Assessment:**

The experiments convincingly support the paper's core claims:
- The bias exists (empirically verified in Figure 6, theoretically proven in Section 2).
- HA-DW corrects the bias (difficulty-stratified results in Figure 1c).
- The correction improves performance (Table 1 across all models and algorithms).
- The improvement is computationally efficient (Table 3 showing $G=8$+HA-DW beats $G=16$ baseline).

The main limitation is domain specificity—mathematical reasoning with binary rewards. However, within this domain, the evidence is comprehensive and well-controlled.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

No method is without constraints, and HA-DW is no exception. This section critically examines the assumptions, boundary conditions, and practical trade-offs that define where HA-DW succeeds and where it may fall short. Understanding these limitations is essential for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this approach and for researchers identifying open problems.

### 6.1 Structural Assumptions: What HA-DW Requires to Work

**Assumption 1: Group-Relative Policy Optimization Framework**

The most fundamental limitation is explicitly stated in the Limitations section:

> "Our study primarily focuses on the issue of group-wise estimation bias, restricting the application of HA-DW to group-relative methods."

HA-DW corrects the *group-relative advantage estimator* specifically. It cannot be applied to:
- **Critic-based methods** like PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) that use learned value functions
- **Off-policy algorithms** that rely on importance sampling from replay buffers
- **Direct preference optimization** methods that don't compute advantages in the traditional RL sense

This is a meaningful constraint because it means HA-DW solves a problem within a specific paradigm rather than offering a universal bias correction for all RLVR settings.

**Assumption 2: Binary or Bounded Reward Signals**

The theoretical analysis in Section 2.2 assumes binary rewards ($r_{t,i} \in \{0, 1\}$), which matches the verifier-based setup common in mathematical reasoning. Appendix D.5 extends the analysis to continuous bounded rewards (Beta and truncated Gaussian distributions), showing the bias phenomenon persists theoretically. However, **all experiments use binary verification** (Section 5, Appendix C).

This leaves an empirical gap: we do not know how HA-DW performs with:
- Learned reward models that produce continuous scores
- Soft verifiers that return partial credit
- Multi-dimensional reward signals (e.g., correctness + style + safety)

The theoretical extension in Appendix D.5 is promising but untested empirically.

**Assumption 3: Non-Degenerate Group Sampling**

The theoretical analysis conditions on the event $S := \{1 \le R \le G-1\}$, excluding groups where all responses are correct or all are incorrect (Section 2.2). This is standard practice in GRPO-style algorithms because degenerate groups produce zero advantages. However, this conditioning is baked into the theory.

In practice, if a model becomes very capable on a subset of prompts, degenerate groups (all correct) become more frequent. HA-DW's correction mechanism does not apply to these groups since they produce no gradients anyway. This means **HA-DW cannot help the model learn from prompts it has already mastered**—it only corrects the learning signal for prompts in the "learning zone."

### 6.2 Scenarios and Edge Cases Not Addressed

**Domain Specificity: Mathematical Reasoning Only**

All five benchmarks (MATH500, AIME25, AMC23, Minerva, OlympiadBench) are mathematical reasoning tasks (Section 5, Appendix C). The paper does not evaluate HA-DW on:
- Code generation tasks
- Scientific reasoning outside mathematics
- Open-ended dialogue or creative tasks
- Multi-turn conversation settings

The Limitations section acknowledges this:

> "Nevertheless, estimation bias is pervasive, and future work will focus on extending this concept to a broader scope."

This is a significant gap because different domains may exhibit different bias characteristics. For example, code generation often uses test-case-based verification with partial credit, which differs from the binary pass/fail signals in math benchmarks.

**Prompt-Specific Difficulty Patterns**

The capability anchor $C_t$ aggregates information at the **batch level**, not the prompt level (Section 3.1). This means:
- The system tracks the model's overall capability trend
- It does not maintain per-prompt or per-topic difficulty histories

A prompt that is consistently hard for a specific model (e.g., geometry problems for a model weak in spatial reasoning) may not be distinguished from a prompt that is temporarily hard due to training dynamics. The paper does not explore whether prompt-specific difficulty tracking would yield additional gains.

**Long-Term Training Dynamics**

Experiments run for 3 epochs (GRPO, GSPO) or 9 epochs (DAPO) per Table 8. The paper does not report:
- Whether HA-DW's advantages persist over 50+ epochs
- Whether the correction becomes less necessary as the model converges
- Whether the capability anchor $C_t$ saturates or continues evolving meaningfully

Figure 4 shows training dynamics, but only over the reported training horizon. It remains an open question whether HA-DW provides sustained benefits or primarily accelerates early learning.

**No Direct Comparison to Critic-Based Methods**

The Introduction positions group-relative methods as alternatives to critic-based approaches like PPO to avoid training a separate value network. However, the paper **does not compare HA-DW+GRPO against PPO with a learned critic**.

This leaves an important question unanswered: Does correcting group-relative bias close the performance gap with critic-based methods, or does PPO still outperform even corrected GRPO? Without this comparison, we cannot fully assess whether HA-DW makes group-relative methods competitive with the traditional alternative.

### 6.3 Computational and Scalability Constraints

**Memory Overhead of Historical Tracking**

While HA-DW avoids the computational cost of increasing rollouts, it introduces its own overhead:
- The capability anchor $C_t$ must be stored and updated across batches
- The sliding window of $m$ previous batches (Section 3.1, Equation 9) requires memory proportional to $m$
- The adaptive forgetting factor computation requires calculating standard deviation over the window (Equation 10)

The paper does not quantify this overhead in terms of memory usage or latency. Given that $C_t$ is a scalar value and $m$ is likely small (the paper does not specify $m$'s value), this overhead is probably negligible compared to model forward passes. However, the lack of explicit measurement is a gap.

**Hardware Constraints Still Apply**

Table 3 (Appendix E.2) notes that $G=32$ rollouts was "out of memory." HA-DW improves performance at $G=8$, but it **does not eliminate hardware constraints**:
- Maximum response length is still capped at 4096 tokens (Table 8)
- Batch size is still limited to 256 (Table 8)
- GPU memory still bounds the maximum model scale and sequence length

HA-DW makes better use of fixed hardware but does not expand the hardware envelope itself.

**Hyperparameter Tuning Requirement**

Table 7 shows that $\lambda_{\text{scale}}$ requires tuning:
- $\lambda_{\text{scale}} = 0.5$ matches baseline performance (46.5%)
- $\lambda_{\text{scale}} = 1.3$ achieves peak performance (48.7%)
- $\lambda_{\text{scale}} = 2.0$ degrades to 47.3%

The paper does not explain how $\lambda_{\text{scale}} = 1.3$ was selected (grid search, theoretical guidance, or heuristic). Theorem 3 provides theoretical bounds for $\lambda_{\text{scale}}$, but these bounds depend on unknown quantities like $c_{\text{low}}$ and $c_{\text{high}}$, which depend on the true $p_t$ values.

This means practitioners cannot compute the optimal $\lambda_{\text{scale}}$ from first principles—they must tune it empirically for each task and model scale. This reduces the "plug-and-play" claim somewhat.

### 6.4 Theoretical Gaps and Open Questions

**Theoretical Bounds Depend on Unknown Quantities**

Theorem 3 provides conditions under which HA-DW provably reduces bias:

$$
\lambda_{\text{scale}} \in \left[\frac{1 + (1-c_{\text{high}})\hat{p}_t}{1-\hat{p}_t} \exp(D_{t,i} M_t), \ldots\right]
$$

However, $c_{\text{low}}$ and $c_{\text{high}}$ depend on the true expected reward $p_t$ (Lemma 1, Appendix D.4), which is unknown during training. This creates a gap between theory and practice:
- The theory tells us an optimal range exists
- The theory does not tell us how to find it without knowing $p_t$

This is not a flaw in the theory—it accurately reflects the inherent uncertainty in estimation problems. But it means the theoretical guidance cannot be directly operationalized without empirical tuning.

**Continuous Reward Case Untested**

Appendix D.5 extends the bias analysis to continuous bounded rewards (Beta and truncated Gaussian distributions), showing theoretically that bias persists. Corollary 4 and Corollary 5 provide explicit integral expressions for the bias under these distributions.

However, **no experiments validate this extension**. All reported results use binary rewards. This leaves open whether:
- The magnitude of bias differs for continuous vs. binary rewards
- HA-DW's correction mechanism works equally well for continuous rewards
- The optimal $\lambda_{\text{scale}}$ changes when rewards are continuous

**Hard Update Variant Trade-offs**

Appendix F describes a "hard update" variant of the capability anchor:

$$
C^+_t = \frac{1}{h} \left(\sum_{j=1}^{h-1} y_{t-j} + y_t\right)
$$

This simplifies the Kalman-style update but "ignores short-term oscillations in belief updates." The paper does not report experiments comparing the hard update vs. soft update variants, leaving practitioners without guidance on whether the simplification is worth the potential loss in tracking accuracy.

### 6.5 Summary of Trade-offs

| Aspect | Trade-off | Evidence |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| **Algorithm Scope** | Only works with group-relative methods, not critic-based RL | Limitations section |
| **Reward Type** | Theory extends to continuous rewards, but experiments only use binary | Appendix D.5 vs. Section 5 |
| **Domain** | Evaluated only on mathematical reasoning, not other tasks | Table 1 benchmarks |
| **Compute vs. Correction** | Reduces need for more rollouts, but adds hyperparameter tuning | Table 3 vs. Table 7 |
| **Memory** | Avoids critic model memory, but requires batch history tracking | Section 3.1 |
| **Theoretical Guidance** | Proves correction exists, but optimal parameters depend on unknown $p_t$ | Theorem 3, Lemma 1 |
| **Training Duration** | Shows gains over 3-9 epochs, long-term effects unknown | Table 8 epochs |

### 6.6 Fair Assessment

Despite these limitations, HA-DW represents a meaningful advance for its target domain. The constraints are primarily about **scope** (where it applies) rather than **effectiveness** (whether it works where applied). Within the domain of group-relative RLVR for mathematical reasoning:

- The bias correction is theoretically grounded (Section 2)
- The empirical improvements are consistent across models and algorithms (Table 1)
- The computational efficiency gain is demonstrated (Table 3)
- The ablation studies validate design choices (Tables 2, 7)

The open questions—continuous rewards, non-math domains, long-term training, critic-based comparisons—are natural directions for future work rather than flaws in the current contribution. Practitioners working on math reasoning with GRPO-style algorithms can adopt HA-DW with confidence, understanding that some hyperparameter tuning ($\lambda_{\text{scale}}$) will be required and that the method does not address bias outside the group-relative paradigm.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how the discovery of systematic bias in group-relative advantage estimation reshapes our understanding of RLVR training, what research pathways it opens, and how practitioners should think about adopting HA-DW in real-world settings. We move beyond what the paper demonstrates to what it enables.

### 7.1 Shifting the Field's Understanding of Group-Relative RL

**From "Noisy but Unbiased" to "Structurally Biased"**

Prior to this work, the prevailing assumption in the RLVR community was that group-relative advantage estimators like those in GRPO were unbiased but high-variance due to small sample sizes. This paper fundamentally changes that view by proving the bias is **structural and directional**, not merely stochastic noise.

This has three cascading implications:

1. **Optimization Tweaks Are Insufficient:** Variants like GSPO, DAPO, and Dr.GRPO focus on clipping mechanisms, normalization strategies, and sampling dynamics (Section 6). While these improve stability, they cannot eliminate the underlying bias because they operate on a distorted signal. Future algorithm design must address the estimator itself, not just how the estimator is consumed.

2. **Compute Scaling Has Diminishing Returns:** The canonical solution to estimation error—increasing sample size—hits hard hardware limits (Table 3 shows $G=32$ causes OOM). This work demonstrates that **algorithmic correction can outperform brute-force scaling** (GRPO+HA-DW at $G=8$ beats GRPO at $G=16$). This shifts the optimization frontier from "more compute" to "smarter estimation."

3. **Theoretical Scrutiny of Other Components:** If the advantage estimator has hidden bias, what about other RLVR components? This work invites similar theoretical analysis of:
   - Importance sampling ratio clipping thresholds
   - Batch-level normalization procedures
   - Reward shaping in verifier-based settings

**Repositioning Group-Relative Methods vs. Critic-Based Methods**

Group-relative methods emerged as lightweight alternatives to PPO, trading statistical rigor for computational efficiency. This work narrows that trade-off gap. By correcting the bias without adding a critic model, HA-DW brings group-relative methods closer to the statistical properties of critic-based approaches while retaining their memory efficiency.

However, an important open question remains: **Does HA-DW+GRPO match PPO with a well-trained critic?** The paper does not answer this (Section 6.2), but it establishes that group-relative methods are no longer inherently disadvantaged by bias—they now compete on other dimensions like convergence speed and stability.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The theoretical framework and empirical findings in this paper enable several concrete research pathways:

**1. Domain Generalization Beyond Mathematics**

All experiments use mathematical reasoning benchmarks with binary pass/fail verification (Section 5). Key questions for future work:

- Does the bias manifest similarly in **code generation** tasks, where rewards may come from test case execution?
- How does the bias behave in **dialogue or creative tasks** where rewards are learned models rather than verifiers?
- Can HA-DW's difficulty anchor adapt to **multi-dimensional rewards** (correctness + style + safety)?

The theoretical extension to continuous bounded rewards in Appendix D.5 suggests the bias persists, but empirical validation is needed.

**2. Prompt-Specific vs. Batch-Level Difficulty Tracking**

HA-DW tracks capability at the batch level ($C_t$ aggregates across all prompts in a batch). A natural extension is **per-prompt or per-topic difficulty anchors**:

- Maintain separate $C_t$ values for different problem types (e.g., algebra vs. geometry)
- Use retrieval mechanisms to match current prompts with historically similar prompts
- Explore whether prompt-specific tracking yields additional gains beyond batch-level aggregation

This would increase memory overhead but could better capture heterogeneous difficulty patterns across a dataset.

**3. Long-Horizon Training Dynamics**

Experiments run for 3–9 epochs (Table 8). Open questions include:

- Does HA-DW's advantage **persist, diminish, or grow** over 50+ epochs?
- Does the capability anchor $C_t$ **saturate** as the model converges, making correction less necessary?
- Are there **phase transitions** in training where bias correction becomes more or less critical?

Figure 4 shows training dynamics, but only over the reported horizon. Long-term studies would clarify whether HA-DW accelerates early learning or provides sustained benefits.

**4. Direct Comparison with Critic-Based Methods**

The paper positions group-relative methods as alternatives to PPO but does not compare HA-DW+GRPO against PPO with a learned value function. A comprehensive evaluation would:

- Match compute budgets across methods (critic training vs. additional rollouts)
- Compare convergence speed, final performance, and stability
- Identify regimes where each approach excels

This would establish the complete Pareto frontier for RLVR training methods.

**5. Combining HA-DW with Other GRPO Variants**

HA-DW is designed as a plug-and-play module (Section 3.2), but the paper only tests it with GRPO, GSPO, and DAPO. Future work could explore:

- Integration with **Dr.GRPO** (which removes heuristic normalizations)
- Combination with **dynamic sampling** strategies beyond DAPO's approach
- Stacking with **length-aware optimization** (e.g., LSPO from Appendix A)

Understanding how HA-DW interacts with other corrections could reveal synergistic or antagonistic effects.

**6. Theoretical Analysis of Other Estimation Components**

This paper's theoretical framework—conditioning on non-degenerate events, analyzing expectation-level and probability-level bias—could apply to:

- **Token-level advantage estimation** (vs. response-level)
- **Off-policy correction terms** in RLVR
- **Reward normalization** procedures across batches

Extending this analytical approach could uncover additional hidden biases in standard RLVR pipelines.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

**Immediate Applications**

HA-DW is ready for deployment in specific settings:

| Use Case | Suitability | Rationale |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| Math reasoning training with GRPO | **High** | Directly validated in this paper |
| Code generation with test-case verification | **Medium** | Binary rewards match assumptions, but untested empirically |
| Dialogue RL with learned reward models | **Low** | Continuous rewards untested; reward model noise may dominate |
| Multi-turn conversation training | **Low** | Group-relative methods less common in this setting |
| Resource-constrained environments | **High** | Avoids rollout scaling costs |

**Integration Workflow**

For practitioners adopting HA-DW:

1. **Start with $\lambda_{\text{scale}} \in [1.3, 1.5]$**: Table 7 shows this range achieves peak performance on Qwen3-4B-Base. Tune within this range for your specific model and task.

2. **Use the Kalman-style soft update by default**: Appendix F's hard update variant is simpler but untested. The soft update (Section 3.1) has empirical validation.

3. **Monitor capability anchor stability**: If $C_t$ oscillates wildly, consider increasing the sliding window $m$ (Equation 9) or reducing the base forgetting factor $\eta$.

4. **Expect 1–3 percentage point gains**: Table 1 shows consistent improvements in this range across models and algorithms. Larger gains may indicate other training issues.

**When to Prefer HA-DW Over Alternatives**

| Scenario | Recommended Approach | Reasoning |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Training math reasoning with GRPO | **HA-DW + GRPO** | Directly validated, minimal overhead |
| Compute-constrained (cannot increase $G$) | **HA-DW + GRPO** | Outperforms $G=16$ baseline at $G=8$ (Table 3) |
| Already using PPO with critic | **Keep PPO** | HA-DW only applies to group-relative methods |
| Continuous/learned rewards | **Caution** | Theoretically supported but untested empirically |
| Non-mathematical domains | **Experimental** | Bias likely exists but magnitude unknown |
| Long-horizon training (50+ epochs) | **Experimental** | Unknown if benefits persist or diminish |

### 7.4 Broader Impact on RLVR Research Practices

**Encouraging Theoretical Scrutiny of "Simple" Methods**

GRPO's appeal lies in its simplicity—no critic, straightforward advantage computation. This work demonstrates that **simplicity can hide subtle statistical issues**. The field should adopt a practice of:

- Formally analyzing estimators before widespread adoption
- Checking for conditioning-induced biases in truncated distributions
- Validating theoretical assumptions against empirical behavior

**Reframing the Efficiency-Accuracy Trade-off**

The standard view holds that efficiency (small $G$, no critic) comes at the cost of accuracy (biased/noisy estimates). HA-DW shows this trade-off is **not fundamental**—algorithmic corrections can recover accuracy without sacrificing efficiency. This encourages researchers to:

- Look for correction mechanisms before scaling compute
- Treat bias as a solvable estimation problem, not an inherent limitation
- Design algorithms with correction hooks built in from the start

**Opening the Door to Cross-Batch Information Flow**

Standard group-relative methods are strictly intra-batch—each prompt's advantage depends only on its own $G$ rollouts. HA-DW introduces **cross-batch information flow** through the capability anchor $C_t$. This suggests:

- Other cross-batch signals could improve training (e.g., gradient history, reward trends)
- Batch isolation may be unnecessarily restrictive in on-policy RL
- Memory-efficient historical tracking is a promising research direction

### 7.5 Summary: What This Work Enables

This paper does more than propose a new algorithm—it **reframes how we understand group-relative RLVR**. The key takeaways for the field are:

1. **Bias is structural, not incidental**: Group-relative advantage estimation has provable, directional bias that cannot be fixed by optimization tweaks alone.

2. **Correction beats scaling**: Algorithmic correction (HA-DW) can outperform computational scaling (increasing $G$) within fixed hardware constraints.

3. **Historical context matters**: Cross-batch information improves estimation quality, challenging the intra-batch isolation standard in group-relative methods.

4. **Theoretical analysis is practical**: The paper's proofs directly inform hyperparameter selection (Theorem 3 guides $\lambda_{\text{scale}}$) and explain empirical patterns (Figure 1c's difficulty-stratified results).

For practitioners, HA-DW offers a **low-overhead, high-impact modification** to existing GRPO pipelines. For researchers, it opens a **new analytical framework** for examining estimation bias in RLVR. The work's true legacy may be less about HA-DW itself and more about establishing that even "simple" RL components deserve rigorous theoretical scrutiny before becoming standard practice.
