## 1. Executive Summary

This survey addresses the foundational challenges in scaling Reinforcement Learning (RL) for Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) by systematically reviewing advances since the release of DeepSeek-R1 and OpenAI o1, which demonstrated RL's ability to transform LLMs into reasoning systems via RL with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR). It categorizes core components such as reward design and policy optimization algorithms (§ 3), while analyzing open problems like "Sharpening vs. Discovery" (§ 4.1) to guide future research toward Artificial SuperIntelligence (ASI). The paper matters because it identifies critical bottlenecks in computational resources, algorithm design, and infrastructure that must be resolved to enable the continued scaling of RL-driven reasoning capabilities across domains like mathematics, coding, and agentic tasks.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Paradigm Shift: From Alignment to Reasoning Capability

To understand why this survey is necessary, we must first recognize a fundamental shift in how Reinforcement Learning (RL) is being applied to language models. Historically, RL gained prominence in the LLM era as a **post-training strategy for human alignment** (Section 1). Methods like Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) were designed to make pre-trained models follow instructions and reflect human preferences—improving what researchers call the "3H" properties: helpfulness, honesty, and harmlessness (Section 1). These approaches treat RL as a way to shape model *behavior* to match human values, not to fundamentally expand what the model can *compute*.

However, a new trend has emerged since late 2024: **RL for Large Reasoning Models (LRMs)** aims not merely to align behavior but to **incentivize reasoning itself** (Section 1). This distinction is critical. Alignment ensures a model responds appropriately; reasoning enables it to solve problems it could not solve before. Two milestone systems demonstrate this shift:

- **OpenAI o1** (Jaech et al., 2024): Shows performance improves smoothly with both additional RL training (train-time compute) and more time spent "thinking" at inference (test-time compute) (Section 1).
- **DeepSeek-R1** (Guo et al., 2025a): Employs explicit, rule-based accuracy rewards for mathematics and compiler-based rewards for coding, demonstrating that large-scale RL can induce sophisticated reasoning behaviors even in base models prior to alignment stages (Section 1).

This shift reframes reasoning as a **capability that can be explicitly trained and scaled** (Section 1). LRMs allocate significant test-time compute to generate, evaluate, and revise intermediate chain-of-thought, and their performance rises as this compute budget increases. This dynamic introduces a **complementary path to capability gains**, orthogonal to data and parameter scaling during pre-training (Section 1, Figure 2).

### The Specific Gap: Foundational Challenges in Scaling RL for Reasoning

Despite these breakthroughs, the paper identifies a critical gap: **further scaling of RL for LRMs faces foundational challenges** that remain unresolved (Section 1). The survey explicitly names four constraint categories:

1. **Computational Resources**: Large-scale RL training requires massive GPU clusters and efficient infrastructure.
2. **Algorithm Design**: Current methods (e.g., PPO, GRPO) may not scale optimally for long-horizon reasoning tasks.
3. **Training Data**: Verifiable rewards require tasks with clear ground truth, limiting applicability to open-ended domains.
4. **Infrastructure**: Distributed training systems must handle asynchronous rollouts, reward computation, and policy updates at scale.

The paper argues that **"how and where RL for LRMs can be scaled to achieve high-level intelligence and generate real-world value remain unresolved issues"** (Section 1). This is not merely an engineering problem—it is a foundational research question. For instance, the survey discusses the **"Verifier's Law"** (Section 3.1.1), which posits that the ease of training AI systems to perform a task is proportional to the degree to which the task is verifiable. Tasks like mathematics and coding satisfy this law (clear ground truth, rapid automated verification), but open-ended question answering or creative writing do not. This creates a fundamental bottleneck: RLVR excels where verification is easy, but many high-value reasoning tasks lack reliable automated rewards.

### Why This Problem Matters: Real-World and Theoretical Significance

The importance of addressing these challenges operates on two levels:

**Real-World Impact**: RL-trained reasoning models are already being deployed across high-stakes domains. The survey catalogs applications in:
- **Mathematics and Science**: Competition-level problem solving (Section 5.1, Table 4)
- **Coding and Software Engineering**: From competitive programming to repository-level code generation (Section 6.1)
- **Agentic Tasks**: Search, tool use, GUI automation, and deep research (Section 6.2)
- **Medical Reasoning**: Clinical diagnosis, treatment planning, and radiology report generation (Section 6.6)
- **Robotics**: Vision-Language-Action models for manipulation tasks (Section 6.5)

The ability to scale RL for reasoning directly affects progress in these domains. For example, DeepSeek-R1 employs rule-based rewards for mathematics and unit-test pass rates for coding (Section 1). If RL training cannot scale efficiently, models will plateau before reaching expert-level performance in these areas.

**Theoretical Significance**: The emergence of RLVR reveals a **new scaling axis beyond pre-training alone** (Section 1). Traditional scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020; Aghajanyan et al., 2023) describe how performance improves with more data and parameters during pre-training. RL introduces a complementary dimension: performance can improve through **train-time RL optimization** and **test-time compute allocation** (Section 1). This has profound implications for the path toward Artificial SuperIntelligence (ASI). The survey explicitly states its goal is to "explore strategies to enhance the scalability of RL toward Artificial SuperIntelligence" (Abstract, Section 1). If RL can continually improve reasoning capabilities without requiring ever-larger pre-training runs, it offers a more sustainable path to advanced intelligence.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches and Surveys

Existing literature falls short in several ways, which this survey aims to address:

**Prior RL Surveys Focus on Classical RL or Alignment**: Several surveys cover RL algorithms broadly (Ghasemi et al., 2024; Huh and Mohapatra, 2023) or RL in computer vision (Wu et al., 2025h), but do not explicitly address application to LLMs (Section 2.3). Others focus on RL for alignment (Srivastava and Aggarwal, 2025), reviewing RLHF, RLAIF, and DPO, but remain "primarily focused on alignment rather than reasoning capabilities" (Section 2.3).

**Prior Reasoning Surveys Treat RL as One Element**: Surveys on reasoning LLMs (Chen et al., 2025n; Li et al., 2025y; Xia et al., 2024) highlight RL-based methods as useful tools but "treat them as only one element among a wide range of reasoning strategies" (Section 2.3). They do not offer a "systematic treatment of RL methods as a whole for LLMs" (Section 2.3).

**Missing Holistic Treatment**: The paper positions itself uniquely by placing **"RL at the center"** and providing "a systematic synthesis of its role throughout the LLM training lifecycle, including reward design, policy optimization, and sampling strategies" (Section 2.3). Unlike prior work oriented toward reasoning performance or reward design alone, this survey examines the full pipeline and identifies "new directions for scaling reinforcement learning in LRMs toward ASI, focusing on long-term interactions and evolution" (Section 2.3).

### Timing: Why This Survey Now?

The survey's timing is deliberate. It explicitly focuses on advances **"since the release of DeepSeek-R1"** (Abstract, Section 1), which occurred in early 2025. DeepSeek-R1 was the first open-source model to match o1's performance across benchmarks and employed a multi-stage training pipeline exploring "pure RL without supervised finetuning (i.e., Zero RL)" (Section 2.2). This release catalyzed a wave of replication and extension efforts (Table 1 lists over 30 open-source reasoning models released between January and September 2025). The field is evolving rapidly, with proprietary releases (GPT-5, Claude-3.7-Sonnet, Gemini 2.5) and open-source efforts (Qwen3, Skywork-OR1, Kimi K2) all adopting RL-based reasoning training (Section 2.2, Figure 4).

The paper argues that **"it is timely to revisit the development of this domain, reassess its trajectory, and explore strategies to enhance the scalability of RL toward Artificial SuperIntelligence"** (Abstract). Without a systematic review at this inflection point, researchers risk duplicating efforts, overlooking critical bottlenecks, or pursuing dead ends in algorithm design and infrastructure.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

This survey examines a system where large language models are trained using reinforcement learning to develop explicit reasoning capabilities, transforming them from instruction-following assistants into problem-solving agents that can work through complex tasks step-by-step. The solution addresses the fundamental challenge of scaling reasoning abilities by treating model outputs as actions in a sequential decision-making process, where verifiable rewards guide the model toward discovering effective reasoning strategies rather than merely memorizing correct answers.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The RL-for-LRMs pipeline consists of five interconnected components that operate in a continuous training loop. First, the **Environment** provides prompts or tasks drawn from a data distribution, serving as the initial state for each training episode. Second, the **Policy Model** (the language model being trained) generates sequences of tokens or reasoning steps in response to these prompts, with each token representing an action in the reinforcement learning framework. Third, the **Reward System** evaluates the generated outputs using either rule-based verifiers (for tasks with clear ground truth like mathematics) or learned reward models (for open-ended tasks), producing scalar feedback signals. Fourth, the **Policy Optimization Algorithm** (such as PPO, GRPO, or their variants) computes gradient updates based on the rewards and advantage estimates, adjusting the model parameters to increase the probability of high-reward outputs. Fifth, the **Sampling Strategy** module controls how training examples are selected and how many rollouts are generated per prompt, dynamically adapting based on difficulty estimates and learning progress to maximize training efficiency. Information flows cyclically: prompts enter the policy model, outputs are evaluated by the reward system, optimization algorithms process the feedback, and sampling strategies adjust future data collection based on observed learning signals.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the mathematical foundation of RL for LLMs, explaining how language generation maps to sequential decision-making and defining the core optimization objective that drives all subsequent algorithms.
- We then examine reward design in detail, as the reward signal determines what behaviors the model learns and represents the most critical design choice distinguishing reasoning-focused RL from alignment-focused RL.
- Next, we analyze policy optimization algorithms, comparing critic-based approaches like PPO against critic-free methods like GRPO, and explaining why the field has shifted toward simpler algorithms for reasoning tasks.
- We explore sampling strategies, which have emerged as equally important as algorithm choice for determining training efficiency and final model performance.
- Finally, we discuss regularization techniques and hyperparameter configurations that stabilize training at scale, as these practical details often determine whether large-scale RL succeeds or fails.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This survey paper synthesizes technical advances in applying reinforcement learning to large language models for reasoning tasks, with the core idea that reasoning capabilities can be explicitly trained through reward-maximization objectives rather than emerging solely from pre-training scale. The technical framework treats language generation as a sequential decision-making problem where the model learns to produce reasoning traces that lead to correct answers through trial-and-error optimization guided by verifiable feedback signals.

**Mathematical Foundation: RL as Sequential Decision-Making for Language**

The paper formalizes RL for LLMs using the Markov Decision Process framework, which provides a mathematical structure for modeling sequential decisions under uncertainty. An MDP is defined by a tuple $(S, A, P, R, \gamma)$ where $S$ represents the state space, $A$ represents the action space, $P$ represents transition dynamics, $R$ represents the reward function, and $\gamma \in [0, 1]$ represents a discount factor that determines how much future rewards are valued relative to immediate rewards. In the language model context, a prompt or task $x$ corresponds to the initial state or environment context, drawn from a data distribution $\mathcal{D}$. The policy $\pi_\theta$ represents the language model itself, parameterized by $\theta$, which generates a sequence of length $T$ denoted as $y = (y_1, ..., y_T)$ in response to the prompt. The state at step $t$ is defined as $s_t = (x, a_{1:t-1})$, meaning the state consists of the original prompt concatenated with all tokens generated so far. The action $a_t$ at each step can operate at different granularities: it may be an entire sequence $y$ at the sequence-level, a single token $a_t \in \mathcal{V}$ at the token-level where $\mathcal{V}$ is the vocabulary, or a segment $y^{(k)} = (y^{(k)}_1, ..., y^{(k)}_{T_k})$ at the step-level. The transition dynamics $P$ are typically deterministic in LLMs since $s_{t+1} = [s_t, a_t]$ where $[\cdot, \cdot]$ denotes string concatenation, and the trajectory terminates when the policy generates an end-of-sequence token. The reward $R(x, y)$ or $r_t$ is assigned based on the action granularity, with sequence-level rewards $R(x, y)$ given at trajectory end, token-level rewards $r_t = R(x, a_{1:t})$ given per token, or step-level rewards $r_k = R(x, y^{(1:k)})$ given per segment. The return $G$ represents the cumulative reward of the whole trajectory $y$ for prompt $x$, typically with $\gamma = 1$ for finite horizons, which reduces to the single scalar $R(x, y)$ with sequence-level reward or aggregates per-token and per-step rewards otherwise.

The learning objective maximizes the expected cumulative reward over the data distribution, formulated as:

$$\max_\theta J(\theta) := \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}, y \sim \pi_\theta(x)}[G]$$

This equation states that we seek policy parameters $\theta$ that maximize the expected return $G$ when prompts $x$ are sampled from distribution $\mathcal{D}$ and responses $y$ are sampled from the policy $\pi_\theta$ given $x$. In practice, the learned policy is regularized toward a reference policy $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ using KL-divergence constraints to stabilize training and maintain language quality, preventing the model from diverging too far from its initial capabilities while optimizing for reward.

**Policy Gradient Methods: The Core Optimization Mechanism**

The paper explains that RL policy optimization algorithms for LLMs are mostly first-order gradient-based algorithms due to the large number of parameters in LLMs, making second-order methods computationally intractable. The general formulation for LLM gradient calculation uses the Policy Gradient algorithm, which estimates the gradient of the expected return with respect to policy parameters. The policy gradient can be written as:

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}, y \sim \pi_\theta}\left[\sum_{t=1}^T \nabla_\theta \pi_\theta(y_t | y_{&lt;t}) Q_t\right]$$

This equation indicates that the gradient is computed as an expectation over prompts and generated sequences, where each token's contribution to the gradient is weighted by its estimated quality value $Q_t$. The policy gradient is justified by the intuition that an algorithm following this gradient should maximize the probability of better-than-average actions and minimize the probability of worse-than-average actions. This notion led to the introduction of the advantage function $A(s, a) = Q(s, a) - V(s)$, where $V(s)$ represents the value function (expected cumulative reward from state $s$) and $Q(s, a)$ represents the quality function (expected cumulative reward from state-action pair $(s, a)$). The advantage measures how much the current action improves upon the expected total reward compared to the existing policy, providing a baseline-reduced signal that reduces variance in gradient estimates.

For LLM training specifically, vanilla policy gradient algorithms often suffer from stability issues due to the high variance of gradient estimates and the sensitivity of language models to parameter changes. Instead, training is typically done with the PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) algorithm, which introduces a clipped surrogate objective to prevent destructive policy updates. For an algorithm with $N$ samples, the general PPO-style objective is formulated as:

$$J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\text{data}}\left[\frac{1}{Z} \sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{t=1}^{T_i} \min\left(w_{i,t}(\theta) \hat{A}_{i,t}, \text{clip}(w_{i,t}(\theta), 1 - \epsilon_{\text{low}}, 1 + \epsilon_{\text{high}}) \hat{A}_{i,t}\right)\right]$$

In this equation, $w_{i,t}(\theta)$ represents the importance ratio between the current policy and the behavior policy, $\hat{A}_{i,t}$ represents the advantage estimate (either token-wise or sequence-level), $T_i$ represents the number of tokens or responses per sample, $N$ represents the total number of samples under the given prompt, and $Z$ represents a normalization factor such as total tokens or group size. The clipping mechanism constrains the importance ratio to stay within $[1 - \epsilon_{\text{low}}, 1 + \epsilon_{\text{high}}]$, preventing the policy from changing too drastically in a single update step. Recent work has adopted decoupled clipping where $\epsilon_{\text{low}} = 0.2$ and $\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.28$, allowing the probabilities of unlikely but potentially useful tokens to increase more freely while still constraining downward changes.

**Reward Design: The Critical Signal Determining What Gets Learned**

The survey identifies reward design as the most critical component distinguishing reasoning-focused RL from alignment-focused RL, with four primary categories of reward mechanisms that serve different purposes and applicability domains. Verifiable rewards provide the foundation for RLVR (RL with Verifiable Rewards), using rule-based systems that offer scalable and reliable training signals especially in math and code tasks. Accuracy rewards require the policy to produce the final solution within a prescribed delimiter (commonly `\boxed{...}`) for tasks with deterministic outcomes like mathematics, where an automatic checker compares the output to ground truth. For coding tasks, unit tests or compilers provide the pass/fail signal. Format rewards impose structural constraints requiring the model to place its private chain-of-thought between `` and `</think>` tags and output the final answer in a separate field like `&lt;answer>...</answer>`, which improves reliable parsing and verification in large-scale RL. These rule-based rewards stand in contrast to outcome-based or process-based Reward Models trained on human preference rankings, as DeepSeek-V3 and DeepSeek-R1 demonstrate that learned reward models may suffer from reward hacking when scaled to large-scale RL settings, but rule-based rewards ensure greater reliability by making the system resistant to manipulation and exploitation.

The paper introduces "Verifier's Law," which posits that the ease of training AI systems to perform a task is proportional to the degree to which the task is verifiable, emphasizing that once a task can be equipped with robust automated feedback, it becomes amenable to rapid improvement via RL. Tasks such as mathematical problem solving and code generation satisfy the main criteria for efficient RL optimization: the existence of clear ground truth, the availability of rapid automated verification, the scalability of evaluating many candidate solutions, and a reward signal that is closely aligned with correctness. By contrast, tasks lacking fast or objective verification (such as open-ended question answering or free-form writing) remain challenging for outcome-based RL, as they rely on noisy learned reward models or subjective human feedback.

Generative rewards extend RL to subjective, non-verifiable domains by leveraging the generative capabilities of LRMs to produce structured critiques, rationales, and preferences rather than simple scalar scores. This approach addresses two key challenges: first, it improves the robustness of verification for verifiable tasks that are difficult to parse, and second, it enables the application of RL to subjective, non-verifiable domains where rule-based systems fail. Model-based verifiers serve as flexible, specification-based systems trained to semantically assess the equivalence between a model's free-form output and a reference answer, mitigating the brittleness of rule-based systems that often produce false negatives when a model generates a correct answer in an unexpected format. Assessment-based generative reward models enable RL for tasks where Verifier's Law does not hold, evolving from using powerful LLMs as zero-shot evaluators to sophisticated, co-evolving systems. Reasoning reward models represent a major advancement beyond simple preference prediction by training reward models to explicitly reason before rendering a judgment, formulating reward modeling as a reasoning task where the reward model produces detailed rationales before assigning a score or preference. Rubric-based rewards anchor the evaluation of subjective tasks in more consistent criteria by using natural language descriptions to capture nuanced evaluation criteria, employing an LLM to either generate or follow a checklist of principles to guide its assessment. Co-evolving systems move beyond a static policy-reward relationship toward dynamic systems where the generator and verifier improve together, either through self-rewarding where a single model generates its own training signals, or through co-optimization where the policy and a separate reward model are trained concurrently.

Dense rewards provide fine-grained credit assignment by supervising intermediate steps rather than only outcomes, which shortens the credit assignment horizon and often improves sample efficiency and optimization stability. Based on the action and reward granularity definitions, the paper formalizes sparse/outcome and dense rewards according to whether feedback is provided at the trajectory level, token level, step level, or turn level. Token-level rewards can be computed as log-likelihood ratios between the policy and the reference model, or obtained by training an outcome reward model and using specific parameterizations to derive token-wise signals. Step-level rewards fall into two classes: model-based approaches that use trained process reward models to provide step-level labels (though these are vulnerable to reward hacking and expensive to train online), and sampling-based approaches that use Monte Carlo sampling for online process reward estimation. Turn-level rewards evaluate each complete agent-environment interaction, such as a tool call and its result, providing feedback at the granularity of a single turn in multi-turn tasks, which serves as a bridge between process-based and outcome-based supervision and plays a central role in stabilizing and improving optimization in multi-turn agent RL.

Unsupervised rewards eliminate the human annotation bottleneck by using automatically generated, verifiable reward signals instead of ground-truth labels, enabling reward signal generation at the scale of computation and data rather than human labor. Model-specific rewards use an LLM's internal knowledge as the sole source of supervision, operating on the assumption that a high-performing model will generate consistent, confident, or evaluatively sound outputs. Rewards from output consistency posit that correct answers will form a dense, consistent cluster among multiple generated outputs, operationalized via clustering and majority voting. Rewards from internal confidence derive signals directly from the model's internal states, using confidence as a proxy for correctness based on cross-attention, negative entropy, or generation probabilities. Rewards from self-generated knowledge use the model's knowledge to create learning signals, either by acting as a judge in self-rewarding or as a problem proposer in self-instruction. Model-agnostic rewards derive signals from external, automated sources, including heuristic rewards that employ simple, predefined rules based on output properties such as length or format as proxies for quality, and data-centric rewards that derive signals from the structure of large, unlabeled corpora by reframing next-token prediction as an RL task.

**Policy Optimization Algorithms: From Critic-Based to Critic-Free**

The survey divides on-policy optimization algorithms in RL into two categories based on how the reward is generated for the gradient calculation process: critic-based algorithms that use a learned value function to estimate advantages, and critic-free algorithms that compute advantages directly from sampled rewards without a separate value model. Critic-based algorithms train a value model on a small subset of labeled data to provide scalable token-level value signals for unlabeled roll-out data, but the critic is required to run and update alongside the LLM, resulting in significant computational overhead that scales unfavorably for complex tasks. The PPO algorithm formulates its objective using a value-model-based advantage $\hat{A}_t$ and importance weights $w_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{&lt;t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t | x, y_{&lt;t})}$, where the advantage is typically constructed using Generalized Advantage Estimation with temporal difference error $\delta_t = r_t + \gamma V(y_{t+1}) - V(y_t)$ accumulated across time steps as $\hat{A}_{\text{GAE}, t} = \sum_{l=t}^T (\gamma \lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}$, where $\gamma$ is the discount factor and $\lambda$ is a parameter controlling the bias-variance tradeoff. Recent work has argued that the decay factor scales unfavorably for complex reasoning tasks that require long chain-of-thought, prompting proposals for Value-Calibrated PPO and VAPO with novel mechanisms for enhancing the robustness of the critic model under noisy reward signals.

Critic-free algorithms apply the same rule-based or model-generated response-level reward for all tokens in the response and demonstrate effectiveness across various tasks without requiring a separate critic model, significantly reducing computational requirements and simplifying training. The classic REINFORCE algorithm takes the entire sequence as a single action and considers the response task as a bandit, formulated as $J_{\text{REINFORCE}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}, \{y\} \sim \pi_{\text{old}}(\cdot|x)}[R(x, y) \nabla_\theta \log(\pi_\theta(y|x))]$, where $R(x, y)$ usually takes the form of $\pm 1$ for RLVR tasks, though this naive formulation suffers from severe instability issues due to high variance. ReMax introduced a variance reduction mechanism with a greedy baseline estimation, and RLOO further provides an unbiased baseline with more stable results. GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization) has emerged as one of the most popular critic-free approaches, formulated as:

$$J_{\text{GRPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot|x)}\left[\frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^G \frac{1}{|y_i|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \min\left(w_{i,t}(\theta) \hat{A}_{i,t}, \text{clip}(w_{i,t}(\theta), 1 - \epsilon, 1 + \epsilon) \hat{A}_{i,t}\right)\right]$$

where all tokens in $y_i$ share the same advantage $\hat{A}_i = \frac{R(x, y_i) - \text{mean}(\{R(x, y_i)\}_{i=1}^G)}{\text{std}(\{R(x, y_i)\}_{i=1}^G)}$, calculated by group-relative normalization as a better estimation than the binary rule-based reward. GRPO is a critic-free modification of PPO where instead of GAE provided by a critic, the entire sequence uses the same advantage estimate calculated by group-relative normalization, which effectively reduces variance from training signals and has been shown to speed up the training process compared to PPO and REINFORCE-style methods. Other recent approaches including DAPO, CISPO, Dr. GRPO, and LitePPO made further modifications to GRPO with careful tuning of sampling strategy, clipping threshold, and loss normalization to further enhance the stability of the RL training process. GSPO replaces the token-wise clipped importance sampling ratio with a sequence-level clipping, while VinePPO modifies PPO by replacing the learned critic with a Monte Carlo advantage estimation.

Off-policy optimization addresses the scenario where the policy being learned (the target policy) differs from the policy generating the data (the behavior policy), allowing an agent to learn about an optimal course of action without having to follow it during data collection. The core challenge in these methods is correcting for the distributional shift between the behavior policy and the target policy, often addressed using importance sampling with a weighted objective function $L_{\text{policy}}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}, y \sim \pi_b(y|x)}\left[\frac{\pi_\theta(y|x)}{\pi_b(y|x)} \cdot r(x, y)\right]$, where the fraction serves as the importance weight between the target policy $\pi_\theta$ and the behavior policy $\pi_b$. In practical large-scale model training, off-policy learning manifests in three forms: training-inference precision discrepancies where models are trained with high precision but deployed in lower precision, asynchronous experience replay mechanisms which enhance efficiency and stability by reusing past trajectories during learning, and broader off-policy optimization approaches including optimizer-level improvements, data-level offline learning, and hybrid methods that combine supervised fine-tuning with RL. Mix-policy methods represent a significant trend that combines the strengths of SFT and RL, either at the loss-level where SFT and RL objectives are combined directly in the loss function, or at the data level using expert data to structure the generation process itself where high-quality data serves as a prefix or anchor to guide the model's exploration.

**Regularization Objectives: Stabilizing Long-Horizon Training**

The survey emphasizes that ensuring stability and preventing catastrophic policy drift is paramount, particularly for long-horizon training, where techniques such as KL regularization and entropy regularization are widely employed. KL divergence regularization serves a highly controversial role, applied either between the current policy $\pi_\theta$ and the reference policy $\pi_{\text{ref}}$, or between the current policy $\pi_\theta$ and the old policy $\pi_{\text{old}}$, with the unified formulation $L_{\text{KL}} = \beta \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} KL(\pi_\theta(\cdot|y_t) || \pi_{\text{ref/old}}(\cdot|y_t))$. For the former case, this technique was initially introduced in RLHF to prevent the model from being destructively updated, with prior work arguing that incorporating a KL penalty is essential for maintaining stability and avoiding entropy collapse over thousands of training steps. However, in the context of RL for reasoning with LLMs, which is more challenging than standard RLHF, the necessity of this kind of KL regularization needs reconsideration, as many studies have identified that the policy is expected to explore freely during training and thus may diverge significantly from its initialization to discover new chain-of-thought structures, making the KL constraint an unnecessary restriction. A majority of recent works advocate for removing the KL penalty entirely to simplify implementation, reduce memory cost, and achieve more scalable GRPO. For the latter case, KL regularization can serve as a substitute for the clip form of the policy loss, with discussions of differences between forward KL, reverse KL, normalized KL, and other forms demonstrating potential across different RL training scales.

Entropy regularization actively controls policy entropy through the formulation $L_{\text{ent}} = -\alpha \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} H[\pi_\theta(\cdot|y_t)] = \alpha \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} \sum_{v=1}^{|\mathcal{V}|} \pi_\theta(y^v_t|y_t) \log \pi_\theta(y^v_t|y_t)$, where preserving policy entropy is widely considered critical in RL literature to maintain exploration capacity. However, in RL for LLMs, directly applying entropy regularization is neither common nor effective, with the use of an explicit entropy regularization term in the loss function remaining a point of contention. While some find it beneficial using either a standard coefficient or a targeted loss function, others argue against it, finding it can lead to instability or even training collapse, especially with sparse rewards. Many studies have shown the phenomenon of entropy collapse when no intervention is applied, which hinders effective policy exploration during training. To address this, some works dynamically adjust the coefficient of the entropy loss, others employ the clip-higher technique to involve more low-probability tokens in the policy update, some directly train on 20% high-entropy tokens, and others emphasize entropy through incorporating it into the advantage computation. Beyond techniques that explicitly maximize entropy, theoretical explanations identify the covariance between an action's output probability and its advantage as the entropy "driver", with Clip-Cov and KL-Cov proposed to regulate entropy by selectively constraining a small portion of tokens exhibiting exceptionally high covariance.

Length penalty addresses the tension between the validated effectiveness of long chain-of-thought reasoning on complex tasks and the higher inference costs incurred by longer reasoning traces. Many works seek to reduce the reasoning cost while retaining model performance, with approaches including controlling reasoning length by ensuring adherence to user-specified length constraints, designing relative-length regularization and accuracy-preservation constraints to the optimization objective, and applying adaptive length penalties conditioned on problem difficulty to preserve model ability.

**Sampling Strategy: The Underappreciated Lever for Training Efficiency**

The survey identifies sampling as a first-class lever in RL fine-tuning for reasoning LLMs, serving as an efficient and adaptive mechanism to maximize data utilization, reduce wasted computation, and enhance training effectiveness. Unlike static datasets, RL depends on actively curated rollouts where decisions about what and how to sample directly influence learning efficiency, stability, and the quality of acquired reasoning behaviors. Dynamic sampling adapts both the selection of prompts for rollout and the computational budget allocated to each, based on online learning signals such as success rate, advantage, uncertainty, or estimated difficulty, with the primary goal of concentrating computing on informative examples while avoiding saturated or unproductive ones. Efficiency-oriented sampling uses online-filtering to concentrate training on questions of medium difficulty to ensure training effectiveness and efficiency, with representative designs applying an online filter to drop out too-easy or too-difficult problems, over-sampling and filtering prompts whose rollouts are saturated (all-correct) or degenerate (all-wrong), then repeatedly sampling until each mini-batch contains prompts with non-zero advantage. Prioritized schemes allocate rollout budget toward under-mastered items by sampling proportional to failure rates as $p(i) \propto (1 - s_i)$ where $s_i$ is the success rate, while curriculum learning approaches operate at multiple scales with category-level selection using non-stationary bandits and easy-to-hard schedules with convergence guarantees for small models.

Exploration-oriented sampling aims for diversity using dynamic rollout, with some methods implementing entropy-guided rollout to ensure high uncertainty so that the model will call external tools, improving diversity, while others find that steps with high attention scores are related to reasoning behaviors and branch at these steps for better exploration. Structured sampling controls not only what is sampled but also the topology of reasoning traces, aligning generation, credit assignment, and compute reuse with the underlying structure of problem solving. Search-driven tree rollouts leverage Monte Carlo Tree Search for tree-format response generation using the classic phases of initialization, selection, expansion, and backpropagation, viewing a single inference as a tree rather than a single chain and assigning rewards at the node level to produce more dense and fine-grained process signals. Shared-prefix or segment-wise schemes design segmented or shared prefix sampling to improve generation efficiency, conducting additional sampling starting from previously generated prefix and implementing segment-wise tree sampling algorithms that alleviate the KV cache burden, reducing GPU hours for training and improving sampling efficiency.

Sampling hyperparameter adjustment emerges as the joint tuning of exploration (temperature, entropy targets, clipping), efficiency (staged length curricula), and sequence management (overlength filters, penalties, or inference-time extrapolation). For temperature, strategies vary significantly with some works proposing dynamic approaches such as staged temperature increases (for example, 1.40 → 1.45 → 1.50 for a 4B model, or 0.7 → 1.0 → 1.1 for a 7B model) to gradually expand trajectory diversity as training progresses, or using a scheduler to dynamically adjust temperature to maintain a stable entropy level. A more prescriptive approach recommends tuning the training temperature to keep the post-scaling entropy around a target of 0.3, which is found to strike an optimal balance, while other works simply advocate for a high, fixed temperature (such as 1.0 or 1.2) to encourage initial exploration while noting it is insufficient on its own to prevent long-term entropy decline. Nearly all works grapple with managing the length of generated responses to balance performance and cost, with the most prevalent strategy being staged context lengthening that involves starting RL with a short context window (such as 8k) before progressively increasing it to 16k, 24k, or 32k in later stages. The initial short-context stage is considered essential as it forces the model to learn more concise and token-efficient reasoning patterns. For handling responses that exceed the length budget, there is no consensus, with some works applying a soft, linear penalty as the response approaches the maximum length, others applying a tunable penalty directly in the reward function, and a more nuanced, stage-dependent strategy filtering (masking the loss of) overlong samples when the length budget is short (8k-16k) but penalizing them when the budget is large (32k), as filtering can become detrimental at very long contexts.

**Infrastructure and Implementation: Enabling Scale**

The survey catalogs open-source RL infrastructure that promotes development not only in algorithmic research but also in downstream applications, with modern RL infrastructure centering on flexible pipelines and communication layers that allocate resources between agent rollout and policy training, typically implemented as wrappers over mature distributed training frameworks and inference engines. Primary development frameworks rely heavily on mature training frameworks and inference engines designed for LLMs, with frameworks such as DeepSpeed, Megatron, and Fully Sharded Data Parallel optimized for both pre-training and post-training of LLMs, and inference engines like vLLM and SGLang tailored for efficient inference incorporating advanced schedulers and flash attention mechanisms. TRL focuses on trainer-centric post-training with SFT, PPO/GRPO, DPO, and a dedicated RewardTrainer, integrating vLLM for online methods but not natively targeting SGLang or TensorRT-LLM, with scaling delegated to accelerate which natively supports DDP, DeepSpeed ZeRO, and FSDP. OpenRLHF provides distributed implementations of PPO, GRPO, REINFORCE++ and RLOO, with runtime supporting both asynchronous pipeline RLHF and asynchronous agentic RL modes, integrating tightly with vLLM for high-throughput rollouts and organizing training around DeepSpeed ZeRO-3 with Auto Tensor Parallelism. Verl offers one of the broadest algorithm menus together with multi-turn training and tool use, with runtime centered on the HybridFlow controller supporting vLLM and SGLang for serving and providing both FSDP and Megatron-LM training backends. AReaL targets high-throughput RL for large reasoning models with a fully asynchronous design that decouples generation from training via interruptible rollout workers, a replay buffer, and a parallel reward service, empirically reporting up to 2.77× training speedups at matched or better final accuracy on math/code benchmarks and scaling near-linearly to 512 GPUs. Secondary development frameworks build upon primary frameworks and extend features to support agentic RL, multimodal RL, and multi-agent RL, with specialized variants commonly supporting asynchronous rollouts and training plus standardized environment interfaces.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This survey synthesizes several foundational contributions that distinguish it from prior work on RL and LLMs. Below, we identify the most novel insights, explaining what makes each different from existing literature and why it matters for the field's trajectory.

---

### 4.1 RLVR as a Paradigm Shift: From Alignment to Capability Training

**What Makes It Different:** Prior RL-for-LLM work centered on **alignment** — making models helpful, honest, and harmless through methods like RLHF and DPO (Section 2.3). These approaches treat RL as a way to shape *behavior* to match human preferences, not to expand what the model can *compute*. The RLVR paradigm introduced by DeepSeek-R1 and OpenAI o1 fundamentally reframes RL as a **capability training mechanism** that incentivizes reasoning itself (Section 1).

**Why It's Significant:** This is a **fundamental innovation**, not an incremental improvement. The survey documents that RLVR introduces a **new scaling axis orthogonal to pre-training** — performance can improve through train-time RL optimization and test-time compute allocation, independent of data and parameter scaling (Section 1, Figure 2). This has profound implications:

- **Sustainability**: If RL can continually improve reasoning without requiring ever-larger pre-training runs, it offers a more computationally sustainable path to advanced intelligence.
- **Verifiability as a bottleneck**: The "Verifier's Law" principle (Section 3.1.1) explains why RLVR excels for math and code (clear ground truth, rapid automated verification) but struggles for open-ended tasks. This identifies a concrete research frontier: developing reliable automated rewards for subjective domains.
- **Emergent behaviors**: DeepSeek-R1 demonstrated that pure RL training (without supervised fine-tuning) can induce sophisticated reasoning behaviors like reflection and self-correction in base models (Section 1, Section 4.3). This suggests reasoning may be more trainable than previously assumed.

**Evidence from the Survey:** Table 1 catalogs over 30 open-source reasoning models released between January and September 2025, nearly all adopting RLVR-style training. This rapid adoption indicates the field recognizes RLVR as a distinct paradigm, not merely a variant of RLHF.

---

### 4.2 The "Sharpening vs. Discovery" Debate: A Foundational Open Question

**What Makes It Different:** Prior surveys on reasoning LLMs treat RL as one tool among many, without interrogating *what RL actually does* to model capabilities (Section 2.3). This survey surfaces a **controversial, unresolved theoretical question**: Does RL merely sharpen latent capabilities already present in the base model, or does it discover genuinely new reasoning patterns?

**Why It's Significant:** This is a **fundamental theoretical contribution** with practical implications for how researchers design RL training pipelines:

- **If Sharpening dominates**: Then RL's value lies in efficiently eliciting pre-existing capabilities. Research should focus on better sampling, verification, and compute allocation to surface latent reasoning.
- **If Discovery dominates**: Then RL can create capabilities absent from the base model. Research should focus on extended training, exploration mechanisms, and avoiding premature convergence.

**Evidence from the Survey:** The paper presents contradictory findings that prevent consensus (Section 4.1):

| View | Supporting Evidence |
|------|---------------------|
| **Sharpening** | Limit-of-RLVR shows RL improves Pass@1 but underperforms base models at large-k Pass@K, suggesting RL narrows search space rather than expanding it (Yue et al., 2025b). Spurious rewards can shift Qwen models, implying RL surfaces pre-trained features rather than learning new ones (Shao et al., 2025). |
| **Discovery** | ProRL reports that prolonged, stabilized RL extends a base model's reasoning frontier, improving both Pass@1 and Pass@K (Liu et al., 2025). Yuan et al. (2025c) demonstrate LLMs can learn new skills through composition of existing capabilities in RL. |

**Why This Matters:** The survey does not resolve this debate — it **explicitly flags it as an open problem** requiring further investigation (Section 4.1). This is valuable because it prevents researchers from assuming consensus where none exists. The unified perspective offered (Section 4.1) suggests the debate should shift from "Sharpening *or* Discovery" to understanding *conditions under which each phenomenon dominates* — a more productive framing for future research.

---

### 4.3 Critic-Free Algorithms: GRPO as a Scalable Alternative to PPO

**What Makes It Different:** Traditional RLHF pipelines rely on PPO with a learned critic (value model) to estimate advantages (Section 3.2.2). This survey documents a **systematic shift toward critic-free algorithms** like GRPO for reasoning tasks, where sequence-level rewards are sufficient and token-level value estimation introduces unnecessary complexity (Section 3.2.3).

**Why It's Significant:** This is a **fundamental algorithmic innovation** with concrete efficiency gains:

- **Computational overhead eliminated**: Critic models must run and update alongside the target LLM, creating significant computational costs. GRPO removes this requirement entirely.
- **Reward hacking mitigation**: In rule-based environments where rewards are clearly defined, critic-free algorithms avoid reward hacking issues that may arise from ill-trained critic models (Section 3.2.3).
- **Variance reduction through group normalization**: GRPO calculates advantages using group-relative normalization across multiple rollouts for the same prompt, effectively reducing variance from training signals compared to binary rule-based rewards (Section 3.2.3, Equation 12).

**Evidence from the Survey:** Table 3 shows the algorithmic evolution from PPO (2017) to GRPO (2024) to numerous GRPO variants in 2025 (DAPO, CISPO, Dr. GRPO, GSPO, etc.). This proliferation indicates the field is actively refining critic-free approaches rather than reverting to critic-based methods.

**Incremental vs. Fundamental:** While GRPO variants (DAPO, CISPO, etc.) represent **incremental improvements** tuning sampling, clipping, and normalization, the shift from critic-based to critic-free is **fundamental** — it changes the information flow and computational requirements of the training pipeline.

---

### 4.4 Verifier's Law: A Principle Explaining RL's Domain Asymmetry

**What Makes It Different:** Prior work observes that RL works better for some tasks than others but lacks a unifying principle to explain *why*. This survey introduces **"Verifier's Law"** (Section 3.1.1) as a theoretical framework: *the ease of training AI systems to perform a task is proportional to the degree to which the task is verifiable.*

**Why It's Significant:** This is a **fundamental theoretical contribution** that:

- **Explains current success patterns**: Math and coding satisfy Verifier's Law (clear ground truth, rapid automated verification, scalable evaluation, reward aligned with correctness). Open-ended writing and creative tasks do not.
- **Identifies research frontiers**: Many open problems in Section 7 arise from the absence of dependable automated rewards. Verifier's Law directs attention to reward design as the critical bottleneck.
- **Guides task selection**: Researchers can use Verifier's Law to predict which domains are amenable to RLVR training versus which require alternative approaches (e.g., generative rewards, unsupervised signals).

**Evidence from the Survey:** Section 3.1.1 explicitly contrasts tasks satisfying Verifier's Law (mathematics, coding) with those that do not (open-ended QA, free-form writing), linking this distinction to the success of DeepSeek-R1 and limitations of current RLVR approaches. Section 3.1.2 on generative rewards directly addresses the gap: GenRMs extend RL to subjective domains where Verifier's Law does not hold.

**Why This Matters:** Verifier's Law is not merely descriptive — it is **prescriptive**. It tells researchers where to invest effort (building verifiers for new domains) and where to expect diminishing returns (applying RLVR to inherently unverifiable tasks without intermediate verification mechanisms).

---

### 4.5 Holistic Treatment: RL at the Center of the Reasoning Pipeline

**What Makes It Different:** As documented in Section 2.3, prior surveys fall into two camps: (1) general RL surveys that do not address LLMs specifically, or (2) reasoning LLM surveys that treat RL as one element among many. This survey **places RL at the center** and provides systematic synthesis of its role throughout the LLM training lifecycle (Section 2.3).

**Why It's Significant:** This is a **fundamental organizational innovation** that enables insights unavailable from narrower perspectives:

- **Cross-component interactions**: By examining reward design, policy optimization, and sampling together, the survey reveals how these components interact. For example, critic-free algorithms (Section 3.2.3) work well with verifiable rewards (Section 3.1.1) but may struggle with sparse, subjective signals requiring dense rewards (Section 3.1.3).
- **Identifies bottlenecks**: The holistic view exposes that computational resources, algorithm design, training data, and infrastructure are **interdependent constraints** (Section 1). Improving one without addressing others yields limited gains.
- **Surfaces controversies**: By covering the full pipeline, the survey can flag foundational debates (Sharpening vs. Discovery, RL vs. SFT, Process vs. Outcome rewards) that narrower surveys miss or gloss over (Section 4).

**Evidence from the Survey:** Figure 1 provides a visual taxonomy showing how foundational components, open problems, training resources, and applications interconnect. Section 4 explicitly surfaces "foundational and still controversial problems" rather than presenting false consensus.

**Why This Matters:** The holistic treatment enables **strategic research planning**. Researchers can identify which component is the binding constraint for their use case (e.g., reward design for open-ended tasks, infrastructure for large-scale training) rather than applying one-size-fits-all solutions.

---

| Contribution | Type | Significance |
|--------------|------|--------------|
| RLVR paradigm shift | **Fundamental** | New scaling axis beyond pre-training; capability-focused vs. alignment-focused RL |
| Sharpening vs. Discovery debate | **Fundamental** | Surfaces unresolved theoretical question with practical implications |

These contributions collectively position the survey as a **roadmap for future research** rather than merely a catalog of existing work. By surfacing open questions, theoretical principles, and interdependencies, the survey provides researchers with a framework for identifying high-impact directions in RL for reasoning models.

### 5.3 Assessment of Evidence Strength

The experimental evidence supporting the survey's claims varies in quality and consistency.

**Strong Evidence:**

1. **RLVR Effectiveness for Verifiable Tasks**: Multiple independent replications (Table 1 lists 30+ models) demonstrate consistent improvements on math and coding benchmarks when using rule-based rewards. The convergence on GRPO-style algorithms across organizations (DeepSeek, Qwen, Skywork, etc.) provides strong empirical validation.

2. **Critic-Free Algorithm Efficiency**: The widespread adoption of GRPO and variants (15+ models in Table 1) supports the claim that critic-free methods are computationally advantageous. AReaL's reported "2.77× training speedups" (Section 5.3) provides concrete efficiency evidence.

3. **Verifier's Law Validity**: The consistent success of RLVR on math/coding versus challenges in open-ended domains (Section 3.1.1, Section 6.6) supports the Verifier's Law principle. Medical tasks with verifiable components show stable training, while generation-oriented tasks "remain challenging" (Section 6.6).

**Moderate Evidence:**

1. **Sharpening vs. Discovery**: The survey presents contradictory findings (Section 4.1):
   - **Sharpening support**: Limit-of-RLVR shows RL "underperform[s] relative to base models when sampling broadly at large-k Pass@K" (Yue et al., 2025b)
   - **Discovery support**: ProRL reports "prolonged and stabilized RL can extend a base model's reasoning frontier, improving both Pass@1 and Pass@K" (Liu et al., 2025)
   
   This contradiction indicates the question remains empirically unresolved, depending on training duration, model architecture, and evaluation metrics.

2. **RL vs. SFT Generalization**: While Chu et al. (2025a) conclude "SFT memorizes, RL generalizes" (Section 4.2), the survey notes "RL is not a panacea" and "remains ineffective in cases of severe overfitting or abrupt distributional shifts" (Jin et al., 2025d). The evidence suggests RL generalizes better *under certain conditions* rather than universally.

3. **Model Prior Asymmetry**: Qwen models "show gains even under spurious rewards while Llama/OLMo models often do not" (Section 4.3). However, mid-training strategies can "narrow the performance gap with Qwen models" (Wang et al., 2025v), suggesting the asymmetry is addressable rather than fundamental.

**Weak or Limited Evidence:**

1. **Long-Horizon Agentic Tasks**: While many agentic RL frameworks are cataloged (Section 6.2), quantitative results are less consistent. The survey notes "scalability issues from high computational costs and long rollout times within environments" (Section 6.2 Takeaways), indicating empirical challenges remain.

2. **Multimodal RL**: Despite numerous multimodal RL papers cited (Section 6.3), the survey acknowledges "several challenges remain to be addressed: 1) Inconsistent reasoning and answering... 2) Long-chain exploration collapse... 3) Sensitivity to data quality" (Section 6.3). Quantitative improvements are less documented than in text-only domains.

3. **Robotics (VLA) RL**: The survey states "current VLA RL research remains primarily simulation-based" with "few works... deployed physical robots to collect real-world trajectories for RL" (Section 6.5). Sim2Real transfer claims (e.g., SimpleVLA-RL) lack extensive real-world validation.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

The surveyed literature includes several important ablation studies that inform best practices:

**KL Regularization Ablations:**
Section 3.2.5 reports that "a majority of other recent works advocate for removing the KL penalty entirely" citing works from An et al. (2025), Arora and Zanette (2025), Chen et al. (2025s), Cui et al. (2025a), and others. This represents a significant ablation finding: the standard RLHF practice of KL regularization may be unnecessary or harmful for reasoning-focused RL.

**Entropy Regularization Analysis:**
Cui et al. (2025b) provide "a theoretical explanation for the underlying mechanism of entropy dynamics, identifying the covariance between an action's output probability and its advantage as the entropy 'driver'" (Section 3.2.5). This led to Clip-Cov and KL-Cov methods that "regulate entropy by selectively constraining a small portion of tokens exhibiting exceptionally high covariance."

**Sampling Strategy Ablations:**
- **Dynamic Sampling**: DAPO "over-samples and filters prompts whose rollouts are saturated (all-correct) or degenerate (all-wrong), then repeatedly samples until each mini-batch contains prompts with non-zero advantage" (Section 3.3.1)
- **Length Curriculum**: "Staged context lengthening" starting at 8k and progressing to 16k, 24k, or 32k is "considered essential, as it forces the model to learn more concise and token-efficient reasoning patterns" (Section 3.3.2)
- **Temperature Tuning**: Some works recommend "tuning the training temperature to keep the post-scaling entropy around a target of 0.3" (Section 3.3.2)

**Reward Design Ablations:**
- **Process vs. Outcome**: Lightman et al. (2024) "clearly compare the two reward approaches: for mathematical reasoning, PRMs trained with process supervision are more stable and reliable, significantly outperforming those supervised solely by results" (Section 4.5)
- **Rule-based vs. Learned**: "DeepSeek-V3 and DeepSeek R1 demonstrate that RMs may suffer from reward hacking when scaled to large-scale RL settings, but by leveraging rule-based rewards wherever possible, we ensure greater reliability" (Section 3.1.1)

**Training Recipe Ablations:**
Liu et al. (2025a) present "a recent review with unified evaluation, incorporating common techniques into a single open-source framework to enable isolated and reproducible experiments" (Section 4.4). This work "demonstrates that a minimalist combination of methods can outperform GRPO and DAPO across multiple configurations," highlighting that complex recipes may not be necessary.

### 5.5 Failure Cases and Limitations

The survey explicitly documents several failure modes and limitations in the experimental literature:

**Reward Hacking:**
- "Rule-based RL is prone to reward hacking and the development of reasoning illusions" (Section 4.5)
- "Spurious or random reward signals can shift Qwen models, implying that RL often surfaces pre-trained reasoning features rather than learning entirely new ones" (Section 4.1)
- Unsupervised rewards "remain susceptible to reward hacking" (Section 3.1.4)

**Entropy Collapse:**
"Many studies have shown the phenomenon of entropy collapse when no intervention is applied, which hinders effective policy exploration during training" (Section 3.2.5). This represents a critical failure mode requiring explicit mitigation.

**Overthinking and Underthinking:**
"Inference-time scaling has improved the accuracy of LRMs on difficult tasks, but it also introduces systematic over-thinking (needlessly long reasoning chains for easy instances) and, under aggressive truncation, under-thinking (premature halting and reliance on brittle shortcuts)" (Section 7.4). This indicates RL-trained models may not optimally allocate compute.

**Distribution Shift Sensitivity:**
"RL training is highly sensitive to the initial data distribution; when SFT induces significant representation drift, the ability of RL to recover and generalize is limited" (Section 4.2). This limits RL's robustness to poor initialization.

**Evaluation Contamination:**
"There is still a lack of standardized, reproducible out-of-distribution benchmarks" and challenges in "distinguishing between genuine problem-solving ability and mere memorization of answers, while simultaneously avoiding data contamination" (Section 4.2). This undermines confidence in reported improvements.

**Infrastructure Limitations:**
"For multimodal generation, certain frameworks have been specifically developed for RL training of diffusion-based models... However, these approaches are beyond the scope of this paper" (Section 5.3), indicating infrastructure gaps for emerging architectures.

### 5.6 Conditional Results and Trade-offs

The survey reveals that many results are conditional on specific configurations:

**Model Architecture Dependencies:**
"Qwen-family models register significant gains even under random or spurious reward signals, whereas Llama and OLMo models often do not" (Section 4.3). This asymmetry suggests RL effectiveness depends on pretraining exposure to reasoning patterns.

**Training Duration Effects:**
The Sharpening vs. Discovery debate hinges on training duration: "Limit-of-RLVR" findings apply to shorter training runs, while "ProRL reports that sufficiently prolonged and stabilized RL can extend a base model's reasoning frontier" (Section 4.1).

**Task Verifiability:**
"RL in medical LLMs is well established for verifiable problems, where deterministic correctness allows for rule-based rewards and stable GRPO training. In contrast, generation-oriented tasks remain challenging" (Section 6.6). RL effectiveness is conditional on reward verifiability.

**Compute Budget Trade-offs:**
"Longer reasoning traces incur higher inference costs. To balance the reasoning budget and performance, many works seek to reduce the reasoning cost while retaining the model performance" (Section 3.2.5). There is an explicit accuracy-efficiency trade-off.

**SFT+RL Integration:**
"Best practices are converging towards unified or alternating hybrid paradigms that combine the strengths of both approaches" (Section 4.2). Pure RL or pure SFT are suboptimal compared to integrated approaches.

| Verifier's Law explains domain asymmetry | **Strong** | Consistent math/code success vs. open-ended challenges | Doesn't prescribe solutions for unverifiable tasks |
| Sharpening vs. Discovery | **Unresolved** | Contradictory Pass@K findings (Section 4.1) | Depends on training duration, metrics |
| Model prior asymmetry (Qwen vs. Llama) | **Moderate** | One-shot RLVR doubles MATH500 for Qwen | Addressable via mid-training |
| RL for agentic tasks | **Moderate** | Multiple frameworks in Section 6.2 | Scalability and cost challenges remain |
| RL for multimodal reasoning | **Moderate** | Vision-R1, VLM-R1 improvements | Challenges with hallucination, data quality |
| RL for robotics (VLA) | **Weak** | SimpleVLA-RL sim2real claims | Primarily simulation-based |

The experimental evidence strongly supports RLVR's effectiveness for verifiable tasks and the efficiency of critic-free algorithms. However, fundamental questions about what RL actually learns (Sharpening vs. Discovery), generalization boundaries, and applicability to open-ended domains remain empirically unresolved. The survey's value lies in surfacing these open questions rather than presenting false consensus.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This survey reveals that while RL for Large Reasoning Models has achieved remarkable progress, significant limitations and trade-offs constrain its current applicability and future scaling. We organize these limitations into four categories: foundational assumptions, unaddressed scenarios, resource constraints, and unresolved weaknesses.

### 6.1 Foundational Assumptions: What RLVR Requires to Work

The RLVR paradigm rests on several critical assumptions that limit its applicability:

**Assumption 1: Verifiable Ground Truth Exists**

The most fundamental constraint is **Verifier's Law** (Section 3.1.1): RLVR excels only when tasks have clear ground truth and rapid automated verification. The survey explicitly states:

> "Tasks such as mathematical problem solving and code generation are difficult to solve yet comparatively easy to verify, thereby satisfying the main criteria for efficient RL optimization... By contrast, tasks lacking fast or objective verification (e.g., open-ended question answering or free-form writing) remain challenging for outcome-based RL" (Section 3.1.1).

This creates a **domain asymmetry**: RLVR works reliably for mathematics and coding but struggles for creative writing, open-ended reasoning, or subjective evaluation tasks. Section 6.6 confirms this for medical tasks: "RL in medical LLMs is well established for verifiable problems, where deterministic correctness allows for rule-based rewards and stable GRPO training. In contrast, generation-oriented tasks remain challenging."

**Practical Implication**: Researchers cannot simply apply RLVR to arbitrary reasoning tasks. The absence of dependable automated rewards is a **fundamental bottleneck**, not an engineering problem solvable with more compute.

**Assumption 2: Base Model Contains Latent Reasoning Capabilities**

The "Sharpening vs. Discovery" debate (Section 4.1) reveals uncertainty about whether RL creates new capabilities or merely elicits pre-existing ones. Evidence suggests RL's effectiveness depends heavily on model priors:

- **Qwen-family models** "register significant gains even under random or spurious reward signals, whereas Llama and OLMo models often do not" (Section 4.3).
- **One-shot RLVR** can "more than double MATH500 accuracy for Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B" but this effect is not universal across architectures (Section 4.3).

**Practical Implication**: RL is not a universal capability amplifier. Its effectiveness depends on **pretraining exposure to reasoning patterns** (mathematical or code chain-of-thought). Models without such exposure may require extensive mid-training before RL becomes effective (Section 4.3 discusses "stable-then-decay" mid-training curricula to address this).

**Assumption 3: Sequence-Level Rewards Are Sufficient**

The shift toward critic-free algorithms like GRPO assumes that **sequence-level rewards provide adequate learning signals** for long-horizon reasoning (Section 3.2.3). However, this assumption breaks down in several scenarios:

- **Multi-turn agentic tasks** require turn-level or step-level feedback to stabilize training (Section 3.1.3 notes turn-level rewards "play a central role in stabilizing and improving optimization in multi-turn agent RL").
- **Sparse reward environments** (e.g., only final task completion is rewarded) can lead to entropy collapse without intervention (Section 3.2.5).

**Practical Implication**: Critic-free methods reduce computational overhead but may sacrifice learning signal granularity. For complex, multi-step tasks, dense rewards or process supervision may be necessary despite higher annotation costs.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios: Where RL for LRMs Falls Short

The survey identifies several problem settings where current RL approaches remain inadequate:

**Open-Ended Reasoning Without Verifiers**

Tasks without objective ground truth lack reliable reward signals. Section 3.1.2 discusses generative reward models (GenRMs) as a partial solution, but acknowledges limitations:

> "GenRMs are being adapted to provide more granular, process-level feedback to address the credit assignment problem in complex reasoning chains... In essence, generative rewards are proving indispensable for scaling RL to the full spectrum of tasks targeted by general-purpose LRMs" (Section 3.1.2).

However, GenRMs introduce their own problems: they can suffer from reward hacking, require their own training data, and add computational overhead that partially negates the efficiency gains of critic-free policy optimization.

**Long-Horizon Agentic Tasks**

While Section 6.2 catalogs numerous agentic RL frameworks, the takeaways explicitly note:

> "Agentic RL enables advanced behaviors but faces scalability issues from high computational costs and long rollout times within environments" (Section 6.2 Takeaways).

The survey documents frameworks for search agents, GUI agents, and deep research agents, but quantitative results are less consistent than for math/coding tasks. Section 5.2 notes that dynamic environments for agentic training exist (e.g., WebShaper, MicroThinker, ASearcher), but "scalability and verifiability" remain challenges.

**Real-World Robotics Deployment**

Section 6.5 on robotics tasks reveals a significant gap between simulation and deployment:

> "Current VLA RL research remains primarily simulation-based. While SimpleVLA-RL achieved real-world deployment through Sim2Real transfer, few works have yet deployed physical robots to collect real-world trajectories for RL" (Section 6.5).

Key challenges include "autonomous sampling on physical robots requiring multiple devices for efficiency, continuous manual resetting and annotation" (Section 6.5). This limits the practical impact of RL for robotics compared to text-based reasoning domains.

**Multimodal Reasoning Quality**

Despite numerous multimodal RL papers cited in Section 6.3, the survey acknowledges persistent challenges:

> "Several challenges remain to be addressed: 1) Inconsistent reasoning and answering: The thinking generated by the model fails to map to the final answer. 2) Long-chain exploration collapse: As the response length increases, the model becomes fragile and prone to generating hallucinations. 3) Sensitivity to data quality: RL sample selection is crucial, as low-quality training data may lead to suboptimal performance or even negative optimization" (Section 6.3).

These issues indicate that multimodal RL is less mature than text-only RL, with reliability concerns that limit deployment in high-stakes applications.

**Diffusion-Based Language Models**

Section 7.7 identifies RL for diffusion-based LLMs (DLLMs) as an emerging but unresolved direction:

> "A central challenge in applying RL to DLLMs lies in accurately and efficiently estimating log probabilities of sampled responses... ELBO involves a double expectation over diffusion timesteps and masked data, and typically demands extensive sampling to achieve accurate estimates; otherwise, it introduces high variance during preference optimization" (Section 7.7).

This represents a **architectural limitation**: current RL algorithms are designed for autoregressive models, and extending them to diffusion architectures requires fundamental algorithmic changes.

### 6.3 Resource Constraints: Computational, Data, and Infrastructure Bottlenecks

The survey identifies four interdependent constraint categories that limit scaling (Section 1):

**Computational Resources**

Large-scale RL training requires massive GPU clusters. The survey notes:

> "Further scaling of RL for LRMs introduces new constraints, not only in computational resources, but also in algorithm design, training data, and infrastructure" (Section 1).

While AReaL reports "up to 2.77× training speedups at matched or better final accuracy on math/code benchmarks and scales near-linearly to 512 GPUs" (Section 5.3), this still represents substantial infrastructure requirements. Smaller research groups may lack access to such resources, creating a **barrier to entry** for RL-for-LRM research.

**Training Data Quality and Scale**

Section 5.1 documents a shift from "scale-first" to "quality- and verifiability-first" data construction. However, this creates tension:

| Dataset Type | Scale | Verifiability | Limitation |
|--------------|-------|---------------|------------|
| LIMO (Math) | 800 samples | High | Too small for large-scale training |
| OpenMathReasoning | 5.5M samples | High | Requires extensive distillation |
| SWE-Gym (Code) | 2.4k samples | High | Limited task diversity |
| OpenCodeReasoning | 735k samples | Moderate | Distillation-dependent |

The survey notes that "data coverage has expanded beyond single domains (math/code/STEM) to include search, tool use, and agentic tasks" (Section 5.1 Takeaways), but high-quality, verifiable data for these newer domains remains scarce.

**Infrastructure Gaps**

Section 5.3 catalogs RL infrastructure frameworks but reveals fragmentation:

- **Primary frameworks** (TRL, OpenRLHF, Verl, AReaL, etc.) support different algorithm sets and backend combinations (vLLM vs. SGLang, DeepSpeed vs. Megatron vs. FSDP).
- **Secondary frameworks** for agentic, multimodal, and multi-agent RL are built on different primary frameworks, creating **compatibility issues**.
- **Diffusion model support** is explicitly noted as "beyond the scope of this paper" (Section 5.3), indicating infrastructure gaps for emerging architectures.

**Inference Cost vs. Performance Trade-off**

Section 3.2.5 discusses length penalties:

> "Recent successes of LRMs on complex tasks have validated the effectiveness of long-CoT reasoning. Yet longer reasoning traces incur higher inference costs. To balance the reasoning budget and performance, many works seek to reduce the reasoning cost while retaining the model performance" (Section 3.2.5).

This creates an **accuracy-efficiency trade-off**: models trained with RL may produce longer reasoning traces that improve accuracy but increase inference latency and cost. Section 7.4 identifies "teaching LRMs efficient reasoning" as a future direction, acknowledging this remains an open problem.

### 6.4 Unresolved Weaknesses and Open Questions

The survey surfaces several foundational weaknesses that remain unresolved:

**Entropy Collapse Without Intervention**

Section 3.2.5 explicitly documents this failure mode:

> "Many studies have shown the phenomenon of entropy collapse when no intervention is applied, which hinders effective policy exploration during training" (Section 3.2.5).

While techniques exist to mitigate this (dynamic entropy coefficients, clip-higher, training on high-entropy tokens), the survey notes that "the use of an explicit entropy regularization term in the loss function remains a point of contention" with some finding it beneficial and others finding it "can lead to instability or even training collapse, especially with sparse rewards."

**Reward Hacking and Reasoning Illusions**

Section 4.5 warns:

> "Outcome-only approaches may tacitly encourage unfaithful chain-of-thought, such as 'answer first, hallucinate later,' and reward speculation. Recent research indicates that state-of-the-art models also exhibit unfaithful reasoning and post-hoc rationalization in real-world scenarios" (Section 4.5).

Additionally, "rule-based RL is prone to reward hacking and the development of reasoning illusions" (Section 4.5). This undermines trust in RL-trained models for high-stakes applications where reasoning fidelity matters.

**Overthinking and Underthinking**

Section 7.4 identifies a compute allocation problem:

> "Inference-time scaling has improved the accuracy of LRMs on difficult tasks, but it also introduces systematic over-thinking (needlessly long reasoning chains for easy instances) and, under aggressive truncation, under-thinking (premature halting and reliance on brittle shortcuts)" (Section 7.4).

The survey states that "teaching LRMs to be resource-rational, to reason longer only when the marginal utility justifies it, remains a central, unsolved problem for RL in language reasoning."

**Distribution Shift Sensitivity**

Section 4.2 notes:

> "RL training is highly sensitive to the initial data distribution; when SFT induces significant representation drift, the ability of RL to recover and generalize is limited" (Section 4.2).

This limits RL's robustness to poor initialization and suggests that **SFT+RL integration** must be carefully designed rather than applied sequentially.

**Evaluation Contamination and Benchmark Limitations**

Section 4.2 identifies a critical methodological weakness:

> "There is still a lack of standardized, reproducible out-of-distribution benchmarks. Additionally, RL training is highly sensitive to the initial data distribution... There is still a lack of standardized, reproducible out-of-distribution benchmarks" (Section 4.2).

Without clean OOD evaluation, it is difficult to distinguish "genuine problem-solving ability and mere memorization of answers, while simultaneously avoiding data contamination" (Section 4.2). This undermines confidence in reported improvements.

**KL Regularization Controversy**

Section 3.2.5 reveals disagreement about a fundamental stabilization technique:

> "The role of KL divergence regularization is a highly controversial topic in this area... In the context of RL for reasoning with LLMs, which is more challenging than standard RLHF, the necessity of this kind of KL regularization needs to be reconsidered" (Section 3.2.5).

While "a majority of other recent works advocate for removing the KL penalty entirely," this remains unsettled. The choice affects training stability, memory costs, and exploration capacity.

**Model Architecture Dependencies**

The Qwen vs. Llama asymmetry (Section 4.3) reveals that RL effectiveness is **architecture-dependent**:

> "Qwen-family models register significant gains even under random or spurious reward signals, whereas Llama and OLMo models often do not. This divergence underscores the influence of model priors" (Section 4.3).

This suggests RL is not a plug-and-play solution—its effectiveness depends on pretraining choices that may not be controllable for all researchers.

### 6.5 Summary: Trade-offs and Design Choices

The survey reveals that RL for LRMs involves fundamental trade-offs rather than free lunches:

| Trade-off | Option A | Option B | Implication |
|-----------|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Reward Granularity** | Sequence-level (GRPO) | Token/Step-level (PPO+PRM) | Efficiency vs. credit assignment precision |
| **Regularization** | KL penalty removed | KL penalty applied | Exploration vs. stability |
| **Reasoning Length** | Long CoT | Length penalties | Accuracy vs. inference cost |
| **Training Paradigm** | Pure RL (Zero RL) | SFT+RL hybrid | Capability emergence vs. stability |
| **Verifier Type** | Rule-based | Generative/Learned | Reliability vs. domain coverage |
| **Sampling** | Static curriculum | Dynamic/adaptive | Simplicity vs. training efficiency |

**Key Takeaway**: The survey does not prescribe universal best practices because **optimal choices depend on the specific use case**. For verifiable math/coding tasks with ample compute, critic-free GRPO with rule-based rewards works well. For open-ended agentic tasks, dense rewards and hybrid SFT+RL may be necessary. For resource-constrained settings, removing KL regularization and using dynamic sampling can reduce costs but may sacrifice stability.

The survey's value lies in surfacing these trade-offs explicitly rather than presenting false consensus. Researchers must make informed choices based on their specific constraints: task verifiability, available compute, acceptable inference latency, and required generalization robustness.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This survey synthesizes a rapidly evolving field at an inflection point. The emergence of RLVR (RL with Verifiable Rewards) following DeepSeek-R1 and OpenAI o1 represents not merely an incremental improvement but a **fundamental reorientation** of how reinforcement learning is applied to language models. In this section, we articulate how this work changes the research landscape, identify concrete follow-up directions enabled by the survey's synthesis, outline practical applications across domains, and provide integration guidance for researchers seeking to apply these methods.

### 7.1 Landscape Transformation: From Alignment to Capability Scaling

The survey documents a paradigm shift with three transformative implications for the field:

**Implication 1: RL as a Primary Capability Engine, Not Just an Alignment Tool**

Prior to 2025, RL for LLMs was predominantly framed as an **alignment mechanism** (RLHF, DPO) designed to make pre-trained models safer and more helpful (Section 2.3). The RLVR paradigm reframes RL as a **capability training mechanism** that can explicitly develop reasoning skills absent from the base model (Section 1, Section 4.1). This has profound implications:

- **New Scaling Axis**: RL introduces a complementary path to capability gains orthogonal to data and parameter scaling during pre-training (Section 1, Figure 2). Performance can improve through train-time RL optimization and test-time compute allocation independently.
- **Sustainability**: If RL can continually improve reasoning without requiring ever-larger pre-training runs, it offers a more computationally sustainable path toward Artificial SuperIntelligence (ASI) (Abstract, Section 1).
- **Research Priority Shift**: The field's focus shifts from "how do we make models safer?" to "how do we make models smarter through reward maximization?"

**Implication 2: Verifiability as the Critical Bottleneck**

The survey introduces **Verifier's Law** (Section 3.1.1): *the ease of training AI systems to perform a task is proportional to the degree to which the task is verifiable.* This principle explains the domain asymmetry in RLVR success:

| Domain | Verifiability | RLVR Success | Reason |
|--------|---------------|--------------|--------|
| Mathematics | High (exact answers) | Strong | Clear ground truth, rapid automated checking |
| Coding | High (unit tests) | Strong | Compiler feedback, test pass rates |
| Medical QA | Moderate (guidelines) | Moderate | Some verifiable components, some subjective |
| Creative Writing | Low (aesthetic judgment) | Weak | No reliable automated rewards |

This creates a **research frontier**: developing reliable automated rewards for subjective domains is now the binding constraint on RLVR expansion. Section 3.1.2 on generative rewards and Section 3.1.4 on unsupervised rewards directly address this gap.

**Implication 3: Algorithmic Simplification Through Critic-Free Methods**

The survey documents a systematic shift from critic-based algorithms (PPO with learned value functions) to critic-free methods (GRPO and variants) for reasoning tasks (Section 3.2.3, Table 3). This has practical implications:

- **Reduced Computational Overhead**: Critic models must run and update alongside the target LLM, creating significant costs. GRPO removes this requirement entirely.
- **Reward Hacking Mitigation**: In rule-based environments, critic-free algorithms avoid reward hacking issues from ill-trained critics (Section 3.2.3).
- **Accessibility**: Simpler algorithms lower the barrier to entry for researchers without massive compute clusters.

Table 1 catalogs over 30 open-source reasoning models released between January and September 2025, with 15+ explicitly using GRPO. This rapid adoption indicates the field recognizes critic-free methods as the new default for reasoning-focused RL.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

Section 7 of the surveyed paper outlines nine concrete research directions. We expand on the most promising and actionable directions below:

**Direction 1: Continual RL for Lifelong Reasoning (§ 7.1)**

Current RLVR training is typically static: a model is trained on a fixed dataset and deployed. However, real-world reasoning requires **adaptation to evolving tasks and data distributions**. Key challenges include:

- **Stability-Plasticity Balance**: LLMs face "the fundamental challenge of balancing stability and plasticity during multi-stage RL training" (Section 7.1). Techniques from traditional continual RL (experience replay, policy reuse, reward shaping) need adaptation for LLMs.
- **Entangled Knowledge**: Unlike traditional RL where tasks are discretely defined, LLM knowledge is entangled, making modular policy organization difficult (Section 7.1).
- **Research Opportunity**: Develop CRL frameworks tailored to LRMs that enable lifelong learning without catastrophic forgetting.

**Direction 2: Memory-Based RL for Experience Reuse (§ 7.2)**

Current agentic RL designs memory mechanisms tailored to specific tasks with "limited generalization beyond it" (Section 7.2). Future work should transform agent memory from task-specific buffers into **structured, reusable experience repositories**:

- **Experience-Centric View**: Data generated from agent-environment interactions provides rich experiential traces that can be utilized effectively (Section 7.2).
- **Cross-Task Transfer**: Enable agents to "retrieve relevant strategies from past histories and adapt other agents' experiences to new task scenarios" (Section 7.2).
- **Research Opportunity**: Develop RL methods that automatically learn how to operate and manage memory, composing experiential knowledge across tasks.

**Direction 3: Model-Based RL with World Models (§ 7.3)**

A core challenge in RL is obtaining scalable and robust reward signals from the environment. Model-based RL with LLMs as world models offers a promising direction:

- **Generative World Models**: Recent advances show generative world models (including those enhanced by video pre-training) are "both practical and effective" (Section 7.3).
- **Integration Challenge**: "Seamlessly integrating world models with RL for LLM-based agents remains an open research problem" (Section 7.3).
- **Research Opportunity**: Develop methods for LLMs to construct world models that accurately capture environmental states and generate reliable rewards for language agents.

**Direction 4: Efficient Reasoning and Compute Allocation (§ 7.4)**

Inference-time scaling has improved accuracy but introduces systematic problems:

> "Inference-time scaling has improved the accuracy of LRMs on difficult tasks, but it also introduces systematic over-thinking (needlessly long reasoning chains for easy instances) and, under aggressive truncation, under-thinking (premature halting and reliance on brittle shortcuts)" (Section 7.4).

Key research questions:
- **Resource-Rational Reasoning**: Teach LRMs to "reason longer only when the marginal utility justifies it" (Section 7.4).
- **Adaptive Halting**: Develop compute-allocation policies that adapt reasoning depth to instance difficulty and epistemic uncertainty.
- **Research Opportunity**: Design reward functions and training curricula that explicitly optimize the cost-performance trade-off.

**Direction 5: Latent Space Reasoning (§ 7.5)**

Current CoT implementations rely on token-level sampling in discrete space, which "can act as a bottleneck as the lost of meaningful semantic information in continuous space" (Section 7.5). Latent Space Reasoning (LSR) offers an alternative:

- **Continuous Semantic Space**: LSR "operates reasoning in the continuous latent space of LLMs, facilitating more nuanced and fluid semantic reasoning" (Section 7.5).
- **Integration Challenge**: "Assessing the quality of continuous latent thought is more challenging than evaluating token-based thought" (Section 7.5), complicating reward provision.
- **Research Opportunity**: Develop RL methods compatible with latent space reasoning, including new reward mechanisms for continuous semantic evaluation.

**Direction 6: RL for Pre-Training (§ 7.6)**

Traditional pre-training relies on next-token prediction. Emerging research explores shifting RL earlier in the pipeline:

- **Reinforcement Pre-Training**: Reconcepualize next-token prediction as an RL problem with verifiable rewards derived from the corpus, reporting "consistent gains that increase with available compute" (Section 7.6).
- **RL-From-Scratch**: Open initiatives demonstrate "training language models from random initialization purely with RL, bootstrapping token-level rewards" (Section 7.6).
- **Research Opportunity**: Address the cost-effectiveness challenge: "how can RL-style pre-training be made cost-effective at scale?" (Section 7.6).

**Direction 7: RL for Diffusion-Based LLMs (§ 7.7)**

Diffusion Large Language Models (DLLMs) offer advantages in decoding efficiency and self-correction but pose RL challenges:

> "A central challenge in applying RL to DLLMs lies in accurately and efficiently estimating log probabilities of sampled responses... ELBO involves a double expectation over diffusion timesteps and masked data, and typically demands extensive sampling to achieve accurate estimates" (Section 7.7).

Key research questions:
- **Likelihood Estimation**: Develop efficient and accurate ELBO estimation methods for on-policy learning.
- **Trajectory Guidance**: Leverage RL to guide models toward optimal sampling traces through multiple feasible decoding trajectories.
- **Research Opportunity**: Design reward functions for intermediate denoising steps in diffusion models.

**Direction 8: RL for Scientific Discovery (§ 7.8)**

RL can improve LLM performance on reasoning-heavy scientific tasks, but result verification at scale remains challenging:

- **Wet Lab Bottleneck**: In biology and chemistry, verification "conventionally dependent on wet lab experimentation" (Section 7.8).
- **In Silico Simulations**: Perturbation response prediction at cellular level represents "a potential path forward" but lacks accuracy and generalizability (Section 7.8).
- **Research Opportunity**: Develop domain-specific models and RL environments that support rapid experimentation-feedback loops for scientific discovery.

**Direction 9: Architecture-Algorithm Co-Design (§ 7.9)**

Current RL pipelines assume fixed Transformer or MoE backbones. Making architecture a first-class action space in RL represents a high-impact challenge:

- **Reinforced MoE**: Enable models to "learn routing policies, expert activation, capacity allocation, or sparsity patterns during RL" (Section 7.9).
- **Hardware-Aware Objectives**: Optimize not only for task reward but also for latency, memory traffic, energy consumption, and activation budgets.
- **Research Opportunity**: Design robust multi-objective reward functions that avoid trivial solutions (e.g., all-expert sparsity) while enabling dynamic topology adaptation per prompt.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

The survey catalogs applications across six major domains (Section 6). We highlight the most mature and impactful use cases:

**Application 1: Competitive Programming and Code Generation (Section 6.1)**

- **Maturity**: High. RLVR excels here due to clear verifiers (unit tests, compilers).
- **Evidence**: Afterburner reports "raising Pass@1 from 47% to 62%" through execution feedback (Section 6.1). Over 10 code-focused RL models in Table 1.
- **Use Cases**: Algorithm competition training, automated code repair, repository-level code generation, Text-to-SQL.
- **Integration Guidance**: Use GRPO with rule-based accuracy rewards. Start with staged context lengthening (8k → 16k → 32k) to learn token-efficient patterns.

**Application 2: Agentic Tasks and Tool Use (Section 6.2)**

- **Maturity**: Moderate. Frameworks exist but scalability challenges remain.
- **Evidence**: Multiple frameworks (Search-R1, ToRL, ToolRL, WebAgent-R1) demonstrate feasibility, but "scalability issues from high computational costs and long rollout times" persist (Section 6.2 Takeaways).
- **Use Cases**: Web search agents, GUI automation, deep research assistants, multi-turn tool orchestration.
- **Integration Guidance**: Use turn-level rewards for multi-turn tasks. Employ asynchronous rollouts to reduce latency. Consider hybrid SFT+RL for stability.

**Application 3: Multimodal Reasoning (Section 6.3)**

- **Maturity**: Moderate. Vision-R1 and VLM-R1 show promise but challenges remain.
- **Evidence**: "Several challenges remain to be addressed: 1) Inconsistent reasoning and answering... 2) Long-chain exploration collapse... 3) Sensitivity to data quality" (Section 6.3).
- **Use Cases**: Visual question answering, video understanding, 3D spatial reasoning, chart-to-code generation.
- **Integration Guidance**: Use outcome-reward-driven optimization to eliminate need for costly step-wise supervision. Implement explicit multimodal-interleaved CoT to mitigate hallucinations.

**Application 4: Medical Reasoning (Section 6.6)**

- **Maturity**: Mixed. Verifiable tasks (MCQA, structured prediction) are mature; generation tasks remain challenging.
- **Evidence**: "RL in medical LLMs is well established for verifiable problems... In contrast, generation-oriented tasks remain challenging" (Section 6.6).
- **Use Cases**: Clinical diagnosis support, treatment planning, radiology report generation, medical visual grounding.
- **Integration Guidance**: For verifiable tasks, use rule-based rewards with GRPO. For generation tasks, use rubric-based rewards, curriculum transfer, or offline RL.

**Application 5: Robotics and Vision-Language-Action Models (Section 6.5)**

- **Maturity**: Low. Primarily simulation-based with limited real-world deployment.
- **Evidence**: "Current VLA RL research remains primarily simulation-based... few works have yet deployed physical robots to collect real-world trajectories for RL" (Section 6.5).
- **Use Cases**: Robotic manipulation, vision-and-language navigation, embodied task execution.
- **Integration Guidance**: Start with simulation training using binary success/failure rewards. Use Sim2Real transfer techniques. Expect significant sim-real gap requiring domain randomization.

**Application 6: Multi-Agent Systems (Section 6.4)**

- **Maturity**: Emerging. LLM-based MARL is an active frontier.
- **Evidence**: Frameworks like LLaMAC, CTRL, MAPoRL demonstrate collaboration mechanisms, but "key challenges remain in developing efficient collaboration and interaction mechanisms" (Section 6.4 Takeaways).
- **Use Cases**: Collaborative problem solving, distributed task execution, multi-agent debate for reasoning refinement.
- **Integration Guidance**: Use centralized training with decentralized execution (CTDE). Leverage LLMs for natural language-based value feedback and credit assignment.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

Based on the survey's synthesis, we provide practical guidance for researchers seeking to apply RL for LRMs:

**When to Use Critic-Free vs. Critic-Based Algorithms:**

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| Verifiable tasks (math, code) | **Critic-Free (GRPO)** | Sequence-level rewards sufficient; eliminates critic overhead (Section 3.2.3) |
| Sparse reward environments | **Critic-Based (PPO)** | Token-level value estimation improves credit assignment (Section 3.2.2) |
| Multi-turn agentic tasks | **Critic-Free with Turn-Level Rewards** | Balance between efficiency and granularity (Section 3.1.3) |
| Resource-constrained settings | **Critic-Free** | Lower memory and compute requirements (Section 3.2.3) |

**When to Use Rule-Based vs. Generative Rewards:**

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| Math, coding, STEM | **Rule-Based** | Clear ground truth, rapid verification, resistant to reward hacking (Section 3.1.1) |
| Creative writing, open QA | **Generative (GenRM)** | No objective ground truth; requires nuanced evaluation (Section 3.1.2) |
| Medical diagnosis | **Hybrid** | Verifiable components (MCQA) + subjective components (treatment planning) (Section 6.6) |
| Agentic tasks | **Hybrid** | Task completion (rule-based) + intermediate step quality (generative) (Section 6.2) |

**When to Remove vs. Apply KL Regularization:**

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| Reasoning-focused RL | **Remove KL** | Policy expected to explore freely; KL constraint unnecessary restriction (Section 3.2.5) |
| Alignment-focused RL | **Apply KL** | Prevent destructive updates; maintain stability over thousands of steps (Section 3.2.5) |
| Long-horizon training | **Remove or Dynamic** | Many recent works advocate removing KL entirely for scalable GRPO (Section 3.2.5) |
| Stability concerns | **Apply with Periodic Reset** | Update reference model to recent snapshot to reduce excessive constraint (Section 3.2.5) |

**Sampling Strategy Recommendations:**

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| Early training stage | **Dynamic Filtering** | Drop too-easy or too-difficult problems; focus on medium difficulty (Section 3.3.1) |
| Long-horizon reasoning | **Staged Context Lengthening** | Start at 8k, progress to 16k/24k/32k; forces token-efficient patterns (Section 3.3.2) |
| Entropy collapse risk | **Temperature Scheduling** | Staged increases (e.g., 0.7 → 1.0 → 1.1) or target entropy ~0.3 (Section 3.3.2) |
| Multi-turn tasks | **Tree-Based Sampling** | Shared-prefix sampling reduces KV cache burden; improves efficiency (Section 3.3.1) |

**Model Prior Considerations:**

| Base Model | Recommendation | Rationale |
|------------|---------------|-----------|
| Qwen-family | **Direct RLVR** | "Register significant gains even under random or spurious reward signals" (Section 4.3) |
| Llama/OLMo | **Mid-Training First** | Require "careful mid-training with annealed learning rates and high-quality math/code data injection" (Section 4.3) |
| Instruct-tuned | **Cautious RL** | "Entrenched formatting and obedience priors may interfere with reward shaping" (Section 4.3) |
| Base models | **Preferred Starting Point** | "Base models consistently outperform instruct-tuned models as RL starting points" (Section 4.3) |

**Infrastructure Selection:**

| Need | Framework | Rationale |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| General RLHF/RLVR | **OpenRLHF** | Distributed PPO/GRPO; vLLM integration; DeepSpeed ZeRO-3 (Section 5.3) |
| High-throughput RL | **AReaL** | Fully asynchronous design; "up to 2.77× training speedups" (Section 5.3) |

### 7.5 Critical Open Questions Requiring Community Attention

The survey surfaces several foundational debates that remain unresolved. We highlight the most critical:

**Question 1: Sharpening vs. Discovery (Section 4.1)**

> "The apparent dichotomy between Sharpening and Discovery may be reconciled through recent theoretical advances... The key distinction lies not in whether these methods can discover new capabilities, but rather in how they navigate the trade-off between exploration and exploitation" (Section 4.1).

**Research Needed**: Systematic studies varying training duration, model architecture, and evaluation metrics to identify conditions under which each phenomenon dominates.

**Question 2: RL vs. SFT Generalization Boundaries (Section 4.2)**

> "RL tends to achieve 'true generalization' on verifiable tasks and under substantial distribution shifts, but it is not a panacea... Best practices are converging towards unified or alternating hybrid paradigms" (Section 4.2).

**Research Needed**: Standardized, reproducible out-of-distribution benchmarks to distinguish genuine problem-solving from memorization while avoiding data contamination.

**Question 3: Optimal Regularization Strategies (Section 3.2.5)**

> "The role of KL divergence regularization is a highly controversial topic... A majority of other recent works advocate for removing the KL penalty entirely" (Section 3.2.5).

**Research Needed**: Controlled ablation studies across model scales and task types to establish when KL regularization helps vs. harms reasoning-focused RL.

**Question 4: Process vs. Outcome Rewards (Section 4.5)**

> "Outcome rewards provide 'scalable goal alignment with automated verification', while process rewards offer 'interpretable dense guidance.' Combining the two... may represent a promising future direction" (Section 4.5).

**Research Needed**: Comparative studies quantifying the trade-off between annotation cost (process rewards require step labels) and training stability/performance gains.

**Question 5: Architecture-Algorithm Co-Optimization (Section 7.9)**

> "Making architecture a first-class action space in RL represents an open and high-impact challenge for next-generation LLMs" (Section 7.9).

**Research Needed**: Methods for learning routing policies and expert activation during RL, with multi-objective rewards balancing task performance and hardware efficiency.

### 7.6 Path Toward Artificial SuperIntelligence

The survey explicitly positions RL scaling as a path toward ASI (Abstract, Section 1). We synthesize the key insights:

**Scaling Axes Beyond Pre-Training:**

The survey identifies three complementary scaling dimensions:

1. **Pre-Training Scale**: Traditional data and parameter scaling (Kaplan et al., 2020).
2. **Train-Time RL Optimization**: Performance improvement through extended RL training (Section 1).
3. **Test-Time Compute Allocation**: Performance improvement through increased "thinking" time at inference (Section 1).

**Critical Bottlenecks to Address:**

| Bottleneck | Current Status | Research Priority |
|------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Verifiable Rewards | Mature for math/code; immature for open-ended | **High** (Verifier's Law limitation) |
| Algorithm Efficiency | GRPO variants reduce overhead | **Medium** (Continued optimization needed) |
| Infrastructure | Multiple frameworks exist; fragmentation | **Medium** (Standardization needed) |
| Data Quality | Shift from scale-first to quality-first | **High** (Verifiable data scarce for new domains) |
| Evaluation | Lack of standardized OOD benchmarks | **High** (Contamination risks undermine claims) |

**Strategic Recommendations for ASI Path:**

1. **Prioritize Verifier Development**: Invest in automated verification mechanisms for new domains (science, medicine, law) to expand RLVR applicability.
2. **Develop Continual RL Methods**: Enable models to adapt to evolving tasks without catastrophic forgetting.
3. **Integrate Memory Mechanisms**: Transform agent memory into structured, reusable experience repositories for cross-task transfer.
4. **Optimize Compute Allocation**: Teach models to reason efficiently, allocating compute based on instance difficulty.
5. **Explore Architecture Co-Design**: Make model topology adaptable during RL, optimizing for both task performance and hardware efficiency.

### 7.7 Summary: A Roadmap for the Next Phase

This survey provides a roadmap for researchers seeking to advance RL for Large Reasoning Models. The key takeaways are:

- **RLVR is a Paradigm Shift**: Not merely an alignment tool but a capability training mechanism introducing a new scaling axis.
- **Verifier's Law Explains Domain Asymmetry**: Success depends on verifiability; expanding to subjective domains requires new reward mechanisms.
- **Critic-Free Algorithms Are the New Default**: GRPO and variants dominate recent releases due to efficiency and simplicity.
- **Foundational Questions Remain Open**: Sharpening vs. Discovery, RL vs. SFT generalization, and optimal regularization strategies require further investigation.
- **Applications Are Diverse but Uneven**: Math and coding are mature; agentic, multimodal, medical, and robotics applications are emerging but face challenges.
- **Infrastructure Is Fragmented**: Multiple frameworks exist but lack standardization; diffusion model support is notably absent.

The field stands at an inflection point. The release of DeepSeek-R1 catalyzed a wave of replication and extension (30+ open-source models in 9 months, Table 1), demonstrating that RLVR is not a one-off result but a reproducible paradigm. However, scaling toward ASI requires addressing fundamental bottlenecks in reward design, algorithm efficiency, and evaluation rigor.

**For Practitioners**: Start with GRPO and rule-based rewards for verifiable tasks. Use dynamic sampling and staged context lengthening. Remove KL regularization for reasoning-focused training. Select infrastructure based on your specific needs (OpenRLHF for general use, AReaL for high-throughput, Verl for agentic tasks).

**For Researchers**: Focus on the open questions surfaced in this survey. Develop verifiers for new domains. Investigate continual RL and memory-based methods. Explore architecture-algorithm co-design. Contribute to standardized evaluation benchmarks to enable reproducible progress.

The path forward is clear: RL for LRMs has moved from theoretical possibility to practical reality. The challenge now is to scale it responsibly, efficiently, and broadly enough to realize its potential for advancing artificial intelligence.
