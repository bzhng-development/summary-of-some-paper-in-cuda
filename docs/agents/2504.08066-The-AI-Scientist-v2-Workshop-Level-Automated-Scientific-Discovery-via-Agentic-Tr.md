## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces `The AI Scientist-v2`, an end-to-end agentic system that solves the autonomy limitations of prior frameworks by eliminating reliance on human-authored code templates and employing a novel progressive agentic tree-search methodology to autonomously formulate hypotheses, execute experiments, and author manuscripts. The system's capability is validated by submitting three fully autonomous manuscripts to the ICLR 2025 "I Can't Believe It's Not Better" workshop, where one achieved an average reviewer score of 6.33 (placing it in the top 45% of submissions) and exceeded the human acceptance threshold, marking the first instance of a fully AI-generated paper successfully navigating peer review. This milestone demonstrates the growing capability of AI to conduct all aspects of scientific research, potentially enabling unprecedented scalability in research productivity and significantly accelerating scientific breakthroughs.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Core Problem: Autonomy Bottlenecks in Automated Scientific Discovery

The fundamental problem this paper addresses is the **lack of true autonomy** in AI-driven scientific discovery systems. While artificial intelligence has increasingly been applied to assist scientific research, existing systems still require substantial human intervention at critical junctures. Specifically, The AI Scientist-v2 targets two interconnected limitations that prevented prior systems from achieving genuine end-to-end automation:

1. **Template Dependency**: Previous automated research systems, including The AI Scientist-v1, required human researchers to write baseline code templates for each new research topic. This means that for every new domain (e.g., moving from computer vision to natural language processing), a human had to manually create the initial experimental scaffolding. This requirement fundamentally constrains autonomy—the system cannot be deployed "out of the box" across diverse machine learning domains without human effort.

2. **Shallow Experimentation**: The AI Scientist-v1 followed a strictly linear hypothesis-testing routine where each code refinement built directly on the immediately preceding experiment. This approach prevented deeper exploration of scientific hypotheses and limited the system's ability to recover from failed experiments or explore alternative research directions when initial approaches proved unfruitful.

The paper positions these limitations as critical bottlenecks because they prevent automated systems from scaling across research domains and from conducting the kind of iterative, exploratory research that characterizes human scientific practice.

### Why This Problem Matters: Transforming the Scientific Process

The importance of solving this autonomy problem operates at both theoretical and practical levels:

**Theoretical Significance**: The scientific method itself is being re-examined through the lens of AI automation. If an AI system can autonomously formulate hypotheses, design and execute experiments, analyze results, and author manuscripts, this represents a fundamental transformation in how knowledge is generated. The paper notes that "end-to-end frameworks capable of autonomously formulating hypotheses, performing experiments, analyzing results, and authoring manuscripts could fundamentally transform the scientific process" (Section 1). This is not merely about efficiency—it is about whether AI can replicate the full cognitive workflow of scientific inquiry.

**Real-World Impact**: The authors argue that overcoming autonomy limitations could enable "unprecedented scalability in research productivity and significantly accelerating scientific breakthroughs, greatly benefiting society at large" (Abstract). Consider the implications: if AI systems can conduct research without requiring human researchers to write initial code templates or guide each experimental iteration, the throughput of scientific investigation could increase dramatically. This has particular relevance for domains where human researcher time is the primary bottleneck.

**Benchmarking AI Capability**: Beyond practical research acceleration, this work serves as a rigorous evaluation of AI capability. Successfully navigating peer review demonstrates that AI-generated research can meet human quality standards—at least at the workshop level. This provides a concrete benchmark for measuring progress in AI reasoning, code generation, and scientific understanding.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

To understand where The AI Scientist-v2 fits, we must examine the landscape of automated scientific discovery systems that preceded it. The paper's Related Work section (§6) and Background section (§2) provide a taxonomy of existing approaches:

**First-Generation Fully Automated Systems**:
- **The AI Scientist-v1** (Lu et al., 2024) was the pioneering system that demonstrated feasibility of fully automated scientific workflow and manuscript production. However, as noted in Table 1, it had topic-specific codebases, linear execution, no parallel experimentation, and no VLM integration. Critically, it was never submitted for peer review evaluation.
- **AI-Researcher** (Data Intelligence Lab, 2025) similarly navigates the entire research pipeline but inherits similar template dependency limitations.

**Systems with Human Oversight**:
- **Intology** (Intology AI, 2025) and **Carl** (AutoScience AI, 2025) incorporate varying degrees of human oversight throughout the research process. While this may improve quality, it explicitly sacrifices the autonomy that The AI Scientist-v2 aims to achieve.
- **CycleResearcher** (Weng et al., 2025) focuses specifically on the path from idea generation to manuscript drafting but explicitly excludes experimental execution, representing a partial automation approach.

**Non-LLM Approaches**:
- Self-driving laboratory protocols (Shi et al., 2025) design experiments without relying on large language models, using LLMs only in complementary roles. These systems excel at physical experimentation but lack the flexible reasoning that LLMs provide for hypothesis generation and manuscript writing.

**Concurrent Agent-Based Systems**:
- **Agent Laboratory** (Schmidgall et al., 2025) and **agentRxiv** (Schmidgall and Moor, 2025) explore similar territories but the paper does not provide detailed comparison of their autonomy levels or peer review outcomes.
- **Google's AI Research Copilot** (also known as AI Co-Scientist, Gottweis et al., 2025) represents industry efforts in this space.

**Tree Search and Code Generation Frameworks**:
- **AIDE** (Jiang et al., 2025) combines LLM-based code generation with tree search, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on the MLEBench benchmark (Chan et al., 2025) designed for machine learning engineering tasks. In AIDE, each node represents a potential solution state with a corresponding scalar evaluation score. The AI Scientist-v2 draws inspiration from this approach but adapts it "specifically to the multi-stage nature of scientific experimentation" (Section 2).
- **Reflexion** (Shinn et al., 2024) enables models to iteratively reflect on previous responses, improving robustness but introducing computational overhead and slower inference.

**Key Gap Identification**: None of the prior systems achieved peer-review acceptance for a fully AI-generated manuscript. The paper explicitly states that The AI Scientist-v2 demonstrates "the first instance of a fully AI-generated paper successfully navigating a peer review" (Abstract). This empirical validation distinguishes it from prior work that may have generated manuscripts but did not subject them to formal peer evaluation.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

The AI Scientist-v2 positions itself through three specific advancements that directly address the limitations of prior approaches:

**1. Template-Free Autonomy**: Unlike The AI Scientist-v1 which "relied heavily on human-authored code templates requiring manual effort to create a new template for each new topic area" (Section 1), v2 eliminates this dependency entirely. The system generates code from scratch based on generalized idea generation, enabling deployment across multiple machine learning domains without human intervention. This is a qualitative shift from semi-automated to fully automated research.

**2. Agentic Tree Search for Deeper Exploration**: The paper introduces "an experiment manager agent coupled with a novel agentic tree-search algorithm, enabling deeper and more systematic exploration of complex hypotheses" (Section 1). This addresses the "linear and shallow experimentation approach" of v1 that "prevented deeper exploration of scientific hypotheses." The tree search allows the system to explore multiple research directions in parallel, recover from failed experiments by debugging buggy nodes, and systematically progress through four distinct experimentation stages (Preliminary Investigation, Hyperparameter Tuning, Research Agenda Execution, and Ablation Studies).

**3. Vision-Language Model Integration**: The system enhances "the reviewing and refinement stages by integrating a Vision-Language Model (VLM)-based feedback mechanism, improving the quality, clarity, and alignment of generated figures, captions, and text interpretation" (Section 1). This addresses a gap in v1 which had no VLM integration (Table 1 shows v1 had "✗" for VLM Reviewer).

**4. Peer Review Validation**: Perhaps most significantly, the paper positions itself as the first to demonstrate peer review success. The evaluation methodology involved submitting "three manuscripts entirely generated by The AI Scientist-v2... to a peer-reviewed workshop at ICLR" with one achieving "an average reviewer score of 6.33 (placing it roughly in the top 45% of submissions)" (Section 1). This empirical validation provides concrete evidence of capability that prior systems lacked.

**Philosophical Positioning**: The authors also position their work within broader discussions about AI's role in science. They reference Bengio et al. (2025) which "draws a distinction between agentic AI systems and Scientist AIs, emphasizing that the latter focus primarily on deepening the understanding of data rather than pursuing goal-directed interactions with the world" (Section 6). The AI Scientist-v2 aligns with the "Scientist AI" paradigm—focused on knowledge generation rather than arbitrary goal optimization.

**Transparency Commitment**: The paper explicitly addresses ethical considerations that prior work often overlooked. The authors obtained IRB approval, coordinated with ICLR leadership, informed reviewers that some submissions might be AI-generated, and withdrew the accepted manuscript prior to publication to "avoid prematurely setting a precedent for purely AI-generated research entering the official scientific record without broader community discussion" (Section 4.1). This transparency positioning distinguishes the work from systems that might attempt to pass AI-generated research as human-authored.

In summary, The AI Scientist-v2 positions itself not as an incremental improvement but as a demonstration that fully autonomous scientific discovery—validated through peer review—is now achievable. It addresses specific technical gaps (template dependency, shallow exploration, no visual feedback) while also engaging with the broader question of how AI should be integrated into the scientific enterprise.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The AI Scientist-v2 is an autonomous software system that conducts complete scientific research projects—from generating novel hypotheses through writing code, running experiments, creating visualizations, and producing a full academic manuscript—without requiring humans to write any initial code templates. It solves the autonomy bottleneck of prior systems by replacing linear, template-dependent workflows with a parallel tree-search exploration strategy managed by a dedicated experiment coordinator agent, allowing the system to explore multiple research directions simultaneously, recover from failed experiments through automated debugging, and progressively refine its work through four distinct experimentation stages.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The system consists of five major components that operate in a sequential pipeline with feedback loops:

1. **Idea Generation Agent**: Takes a high-level research topic or workshop theme as input, queries literature databases (Semantic Scholar) to assess novelty, and outputs structured research proposals containing hypotheses, experiment plans, and risk assessments.

2. **Experiment Progress Manager**: A coordinating agent that oversees four distinct experimentation stages (Preliminary Investigation, Hyperparameter Tuning, Research Agenda Execution, Ablation Studies), selects the best-performing experimental nodes at each stage completion, and launches replication runs for statistical robustness.

3. **Agentic Tree Search Engine**: Generates and executes experimental code nodes in parallel, classifies nodes as buggy or non-buggy based on execution success and Vision-Language Model figure critique, and expands selected nodes through refinement or debugging based on a best-first search strategy guided by an LLM evaluator.

4. **Vision-Language Model (VLM) Reviewer**: Analyzes generated figures during both the experimentation phase and manuscript writing phase, checking for visual clarity, caption alignment, and accurate representation of results, then provides structured feedback for iterative improvement.

5. **Manuscript Writing Agent**: Takes experiment summaries, aggregated figures, and VLM feedback as input, generates a complete LaTeX manuscript in a single pass, then enters a reflection stage powered by reasoning models to refine content, check citations, and ensure adherence to page limits.

Information flows unidirectionally from Idea Generation through Experimentation to Manuscript Writing, but with critical feedback loops: the VLM Reviewer sends figure critiques back to the Tree Search Engine for debugging, and the Manuscript Writing Agent's reflection stage can trigger figure re-selection or caption revision.

#### Generalized Idea Generation (Section 3.1)

The idea generation phase begins at a higher level of abstraction than The AI Scientist-v1, which primarily proposed incremental modifications to existing codebases. The system is prompted to engage in open-ended thinking about potential research directions, hypotheses, and experimental designs, akin to formulating a research abstract or grant proposal before committing to specific implementation details. This approach encourages exploration of potentially more novel or foundational ideas rather than being constrained by the structure and topics of pre-existing code.

The system integrates literature review tools directly into the idea formulation process. Specifically, it can query Semantic Scholar during idea generation to assess the novelty of a proposed concept and identify relevant prior work. This allows for more informed decisions about pursuing a particular research avenue, ensuring ideas are grounded in the existing scientific landscape from the outset rather than relying solely on post-hoc checks. The idea generation prompt (Appendix B) instructs the system to "perform at least one literature search before finalizing your idea to ensure it is well-informed by existing research."

For the workshop evaluation, the system generated approximately twenty potential research ideas in the first batch centered on core machine learning topics, then another twenty ideas in a second batch focused on applied domains such as finance, psychology, agriculture, environmental science, and public health. From this combined pool of roughly forty AI-generated ideas, the human researchers selected three most promising initial ideas—two from the first batch and one from the second—based on alignment with the workshop theme and potential interest. This initial idea selection step allowed management of computational resources by choosing which distinct AI-generated starting points to explore further with the full system, but it did not involve modifying the ideas themselves.

---

#### Four-Stage Experiment Management (Section 3.2.1)

Real-world scientific experimentation typically proceeds through distinct stages, from initial feasibility assessments to detailed ablation analyses. To emulate this structured approach, the system introduces an Experiment Progress Manager agent that coordinates four clearly defined stages of scientific experimentation, each with explicit stopping criteria.

**Stage 1: Preliminary Investigation** establishes initial feasibility and correctness through a minimal working prototype based on the generated research idea. This stage concludes when a basic working prototype is successfully executed. The system allocates 21 nodes to this stage according to Table 3 in Appendix A, providing substantial parallel exploration capacity for establishing baseline functionality.

**Stage 2: Hyperparameter Tuning** refines the initial implementation by optimizing critical hyperparameters such as learning rate and number of epochs to create a robust experimental baseline. This stage ends when experiments stabilize, as indicated by convergence in training curves and successful execution across at least two datasets. The system allocates 12 nodes to this stage and maintains careful records of previously tested hyperparameters to prevent redundant experiments.

**Stage 3: Research Agenda Execution** systematically implements the core research agenda based on the tuned baseline. This stage concludes when the allocated computational budget is exhausted. The stage also includes a check for experiment duration—if runs finish much faster than the pre-allocated runtime, the system suggests increasing the complexity of experiments. The system allocates 12 nodes to this stage.

**Stage 4: Ablation Studies** systematically assesses the importance of various research components, providing rigorous support for the main experimental findings. This stage concludes when the allocated computational budget is exhausted. The system allocates 12 nodes to this stage and tracks previously tested ablation conditions to avoid repetition.

After each stage, the Experiment Manager selects the best-performing node using a dedicated LLM evaluator based on clearly articulated criteria. This selected node is then carried forward to seed the subsequent experimentation stage. The manager also records checkpoints at each stage's completion. To ensure scientific rigor and reproducibility, the Experiment Manager launches multiple replications of the selected best experiments at the conclusion of each stage. These repeated runs provide statistics—mean and standard deviation—for figures and reported results.

#### Parallelized Agentic Tree Search (Section 3.2.2)

The AI Scientist-v1 operated strictly linearly, where each code refinement directly built on the immediately preceding experiment. In contrast, The AI Scientist-v2 adopts a significantly more flexible and exploratory approach inspired by recent successes in integrating tree search with LLM-driven workflows. The system incorporates this agentic tree search approach across all four experimentation stages, enabling deeper and more systematic exploration of scientific hypotheses.

**Node Execution Cycle**: Each experimental node within the tree-based framework undergoes a defined execution cycle. An LLM first generates both a concrete experimentation plan and the associated Python code to implement the experiment. The generated code is immediately executed in a Python interpreter. If execution encounters an error, the error message is recorded and the node is marked as buggy, ending the current execution cycle for that node. If execution succeeds, the experiment proceeds to the plotting phase.

During each experiment, the system is instructed to save all relevant experimental outputs—training and validation metrics, losses, and so forth—into structured numpy files. In the plotting phase, the system reads these stored results and the code, generating visualizations that summarize and illustrate the findings clearly. These visualizations are subsequently passed to a Vision-Language Model for critique. Any issues flagged by the VLM—such as unclear labels, missing legends, or misleading visualizations—result in the node being marked as buggy, and this feedback is recorded for future debugging. Nodes that successfully execute and pass the VLM review without issue are designated as non-buggy.

**Node Structure**: Each node is defined as a collection comprising an experiment script (for example, a Python file), a textual description of the high-level plan implemented in the script, an execution error trace if applicable, experiment runtime, performance metrics recorded during the experiment, feedback from an LLM after running the script, a visualization script, file paths to the generated figures, feedback from a VLM on those figures, and the node's final status (either buggy or non-buggy).

**Node Selection Strategy**: At each iteration, the system selects several nodes from the existing tree to expand in parallel. With a predefined probability, a buggy node is chosen—thus prioritizing error resolution and debugging—otherwise a non-buggy node is selected for further refinement and improvement. According to Table 3 in Appendix A, the Debug Probability is set to 1.0, meaning the system prioritizes debugging buggy nodes when they exist. When choosing between non-buggy nodes, the system uses a best-first search strategy guided by an LLM that evaluates candidates based on factors like performance metrics, training dynamics, and the quality of generated plots.

**Node Expansion**: The selected nodes are expanded by creating a new child node that may either attempt debugging if the parent node was buggy, or refine and improve upon the previous experiment if the parent was non-buggy. An LLM is used to generate the plan and experiment code for each new child node, after which all new nodes are executed concurrently in parallel, significantly accelerating the exploration process. The Maximum Debug Depth is set to 3 according to Table 3, limiting how many consecutive debugging attempts the system makes on a single node lineage.

**Specialized Node Variants**: In addition to buggy and non-buggy nodes, the system introduces specialized node variants tailored to specific experimental needs:

- **Hyperparameter nodes** systematically explore alternative hyperparameter configurations during Stage 2. The system maintains careful records of previously tested hyperparameters, preventing redundant experiments. Errors encountered during hyperparameter tuning trigger the creation of corresponding debug nodes.

- **Ablation nodes** evaluate crucial ablation studies during Stage 4, assessing the importance of various components or assumptions underlying the experiment. Similar to hyperparameter nodes, previously tested ablation conditions are tracked to avoid repetition, and debugging nodes are created in response to any encountered errors.

- **Replication nodes** execute replicates of their parent experiments using different random seeds. Typically, several replication nodes are created to enable the calculation of statistical measures—mean and standard deviation—of experimental outcomes, enhancing result robustness.

- **Aggregation nodes** are special nodes created to consolidate and visualize the combined results of replication nodes. Unlike other node types, aggregation nodes do not conduct new experiments but simply generate a Python script to aggregate and summarize prior results, producing figures that explicitly show means and standard deviations.

**Runtime Constraints**: According to Table 3 in Appendix A, the Maximum Experiment Runtime per Node is set to 1 hour. The total time required for The AI Scientist-v2 to generate a single paper depends on the complexity of the problems. Based on the authors' experience, this process usually takes anywhere from several hours to a maximum of 15 hours, which is the runtime limit they have set.

#### Dataset Loading via Hugging Face (Section 3.3)

Most empirical machine learning research relies heavily on publicly available datasets. Hugging Face Hub provides a convenient and unified framework for accessing a wide variety of commonly used datasets, complete with predefined train, validation, and test splits. In The AI Scientist-v2, the system is prompted to leverage Hugging Face Hub whenever possible, automatically downloading required datasets using the standard one-line function `datasets.load_dataset`. While this standardized approach greatly simplifies dataset handling, the authors acknowledge it is somewhat ad-hoc, as not all dataset repositories support this method.

For the pest detection paper specifically, the system struggled to automatically access real-world datasets available online. To address this, the human researchers manually downloaded a relevant dataset from Kaggle (Crop Pest and Disease Detection) and reduced its size to one tenth to speed up experiments. The authors note that automating this data preparation process further to handle a broader range of datasets remains an interesting direction for future research.

#### Vision-Language Model Reviewer (Section 3.4)

Unlike The AI Scientist-v1, which did not leverage Vision-Language Models, The AI Scientist-v2 incorporates VLMs at two phases of the research workflow. First, during the tree-based experimentation phase, VLMs provide immediate feedback on generated figures, ensuring that these visualizations effectively and accurately communicate experimental results. Second, during the manuscript writing reflection stage, VLMs evaluate figures and their captions, enhancing the visual clarity and coherence of the resulting paper.

**Experimentation Phase VLM Review**: During the node execution cycle described above, visualizations generated in the plotting phase are passed to a VLM for critique. Any issues flagged by the VLM—such as unclear labels, missing legends, or misleading visualizations—result in the node being marked as buggy, and this feedback is recorded for future debugging. This creates a closed feedback loop where visual quality directly affects whether an experimental node is considered successful.

**Manuscript Writing Phase VLM Review**: In the paper-writing process, the system extracts screenshots of figures alongside their captions and the corresponding text from the paper that references them (identified by the keyword "Figure X"). These images and textual references are then provided to the VLM, which performs multiple quality checks including verifying the alignment between figures and captions, identifying issues with visual clarity (for example, missing legends or unclear labels), and detecting potential duplication of figures in the main text and appendix. Through the iterative integration of VLM feedback, the system significantly enhances the visual quality and clarity of manuscripts.

**VLM Model Configuration**: According to Table 2 in Appendix A, the LLM/VLM Feedback Agents use GPT-4o with 8,192 maximum tokens and temperature 0.5. The VLM Image Review Prompt (Appendix B) instructs the model to examine the provided image closely, describe in detail what the image shows in a scientific manner, critically analyze whether the image content aligns with the given caption, and review how well the main text references explain the figure. The VLM outputs a structured JSON review containing fields for image description, image review, caption review, and figure reference review.

#### Manuscript Writing and Reflection (Section 3)

The AI Scientist-v2 streamlines the manuscript writing phase by replacing the incremental, Aider-based iterative writing approach of The AI Scientist-v1 with a simpler, single-pass generation followed by a separate reflection stage powered by reasoning models such as o1.

**Single-Pass Generation**: The Writeup Prompt (Appendix B) instructs the system to produce a complete LaTeX manuscript given the research idea, experiment summaries in JSON format, the aggregator code used to produce final plots, available plot filenames, and VLM-based figure descriptions. The prompt specifies workshop-specific constraints—for the ICBINB workshop, the main paper is limited to 4 pages in single-column format, not counting references. The system is instructed to "DO NOT USE MORE THAN {page_limit} PAGES FOR THE MAIN TEXT" and to "MINIMIZE THE USAGE OF ITEMIZE OR ENUMERATE."

**Reflection Stage**: After initial generation, the system enters a reflection phase guided by the Writeup Reflection Prompt (Appendix B). This prompt directs the system to identify and fix LaTeX syntax errors or style violations by referencing chktex output, ensure writing is clear and scientifically rigorous, verify all relevant details from experiment summaries are included without hallucination, combine short sections into single paragraphs where appropriate, add more information to supplementary material where needed, identify unused figures that could be moved to appendix or removed, identify figure references in LaTeX that do not match actual files, address issues identified in VLM reviews of images and captions, and remove duplicate figures between main text and appendix.

**VLM Reflection Prompt**: A separate VLM Reflection Prompt (Appendix B) guides figure selection during the reflection stage. The system is instructed to move figures that do not add significant value to the appendix, remove figures that are not very informative or do not effectively communicate meaningful patterns, combine figures that do not contain subfigures and present sparse information with other related figures, update all relevant text discussions to reflect any changes in figure placement or combinations, and enhance the scientific analysis of remaining figures in the text with detailed, insightful discussions. The prompt explicitly instructs the system to "Be more aggressive with figure selection - move more figures to the appendix or group them together with other figures if the page limit is already exceeded."

**Citation Management**: The system is instructed to ensure proper citation usage by always including references within `\begin{filecontents}{references.bib}` environments even if they have not changed from the previous round, using citations from the provided references.bib content, and ensuring each section—especially Related Work—has multiple citations. The system is explicitly instructed to "Do not hallucinate new citations or any results not in the logs."

#### Design Choices and Rationale

**Why Tree Search Over Linear Execution?**: The authors empirically observed that automated research conducted by The AI Scientist-v1 often resulted in short-sighted experimentation. The human-driven scientific process, on the other hand, relies on open-ended hypothesis generation, stepping-stone collection, and iterative hypothesis refinement. Tree search enables the system to explore multiple research directions in parallel rather than committing to a single path that may prove unfruitful. The best-first search strategy guided by an LLM evaluator allows the system to prioritize promising directions while still allocating resources to debugging failed experiments.

**Why Four Distinct Stages?**: The four-stage structure (Preliminary Investigation, Hyperparameter Tuning, Research Agenda Execution, Ablation Studies) mirrors how human research labs actually operate. Researchers first establish that an idea is feasible before investing in hyperparameter optimization. Only after establishing a robust baseline do they execute the full research agenda. Finally, ablation studies provide rigorous support for claims by demonstrating which components are actually necessary. This staged approach prevents the system from wasting resources on elaborate experiments for ideas that fail at the feasibility stage.

**Why VLM Integration?**: Prior systems including The AI Scientist-v1 had no mechanism for evaluating visual outputs. Figures are critical for scientific communication, and poorly designed visualizations can misrepresent results or fail to convey key findings. By integrating VLM feedback during both experimentation and manuscript writing, the system creates a closed loop where visual quality directly affects whether experimental nodes are considered successful and whether manuscript figures are included in the final paper.

**Why Single-Pass Writing with Reflection?**: The AI Scientist-v1 used an incremental, Aider-based iterative writing approach. The authors replaced this with single-pass generation followed by reflection because it simplifies the workflow and allows reasoning models like o1 to provide holistic feedback on the complete manuscript rather than piecemeal suggestions during incremental drafting. The reflection stage can identify issues that only become apparent when viewing the complete paper—such as duplicate figures, inconsistent terminology, or missing citations.

**Why Template-Free Code Generation?**: The AI Scientist-v1 relied on human-authored code templates requiring manual effort to create a new template for each new topic area. This fundamentally constrained autonomy and hindered out-of-the-box deployability across diverse machine learning domains. By eliminating template dependency, The AI Scientist-v2 can be deployed on any research topic without requiring humans to write initial scaffolding code. The tree search mechanism provides robustness against code generation errors by allowing the system to debug and refine code iteratively.

**Why Peer Review Evaluation?**: Prior automated scientific discovery systems did not subject their outputs to formal peer review. The authors argue that peer review provides the most rigorous evaluation of whether AI-generated research meets human quality standards. By submitting three manuscripts to the ICLR 2025 ICBINB workshop and achieving one acceptance with an average score of 6.33 (placing it roughly in the top 45% of submissions), the system demonstrates capability that prior work lacked. The authors withdrew the accepted manuscript prior to publication to avoid prematurely setting a precedent for purely AI-generated research entering the official scientific record without broader community discussion.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the most significant contributions of The AI Scientist-v2, distinguishing between incremental improvements over v1 and fundamental innovations that enable new capabilities. Each insight explains not just *what* changed, but *why* it matters for the broader goal of autonomous scientific discovery.

### 4.1 Template-Free Autonomy: From Semi-Automated to Fully Automated Research

**Innovation Type**: Fundamental capability shift

**What Changed**: The AI Scientist-v1 required human researchers to write baseline code templates for each new research topic—a hidden human labor cost that fundamentally constrained autonomy. The AI Scientist-v2 eliminates this dependency entirely, generating code from scratch based on generalized idea generation.

**Why This Matters**: This is not merely a convenience improvement; it represents a qualitative shift from *semi-automated* to *fully automated* research. Consider the implications:

- **Domain Generalization**: Without template dependency, the system can be deployed "out of the box" across multiple machine learning domains. The paper demonstrates this by generating papers on compositional regularization (NLP/sequence modeling), label noise calibration (computer vision), and pest detection (applied agriculture)—three distinct domains requiring different data loading, model architectures, and evaluation metrics.

- **Scalability**: Human template creation is a linear bottleneck—one template per research topic. Eliminating this removes a fundamental scaling constraint. As the authors note, v1's approach "required manual effort to create a new template for each new topic area" (Section 1), which would become prohibitive at scale.

- **Novelty Enablement**: Template-based systems inherently bias toward incremental modifications of existing work. Template-free generation allows the system to propose ideas that do not fit pre-existing code structures, potentially enabling more novel research directions.

**Evidence of Significance**: Table 1 explicitly contrasts v1's "Topic-Specific" codebase with v2's "Domain-General" approach. The peer review acceptance of a paper on compositional regularization—a topic not predetermined by any human template—demonstrates that template-free generation can produce scientifically valid work.

**Distinction from Prior Work**: While AIDE (Jiang et al., 2025) combines LLM-based code generation with tree search for machine learning engineering tasks, it operates on benchmark problems with predefined evaluation metrics. The AI Scientist-v2 extends this to open-ended scientific discovery where both the problem formulation and evaluation criteria must be generated autonomously.

### 4.2 Agentic Tree Search: Mimicking Human Research Exploration Patterns

**Innovation Type**: Fundamental methodological advance

**What Changed**: The AI Scientist-v1 followed a strictly linear hypothesis-testing routine where each code refinement built directly on the immediately preceding experiment. The AI Scientist-v2 introduces parallelized agentic tree search across four distinct experimentation stages, enabling multiple research directions to be explored simultaneously with systematic recovery from failures.

**Why This Matters**: This innovation addresses a critical mismatch between prior automated systems and how human scientific discovery actually works:

- **Parallel Exploration**: Human research labs routinely pursue multiple hypotheses in parallel, abandoning unfruitful directions while doubling down on promising ones. The tree search structure enables this behavior computationally. As Section 3.2.2 explains, "At each iteration, the system selects several nodes from the existing tree to expand in parallel."

- **Failure Recovery**: In linear systems, a single failed experiment can terminate the entire workflow. The tree search allows buggy nodes to be debugged independently while other branches continue exploration. The Debug Probability of 1.0 (Table 3, Appendix A) explicitly prioritizes error resolution when buggy nodes exist.

- **Stage-Appropriate Exploration**: The four-stage structure (Preliminary Investigation → Hyperparameter Tuning → Research Agenda Execution → Ablation Studies) ensures computational resources are allocated appropriately. Early stages use more nodes (21 for Stage 1) to establish feasibility before committing resources to detailed ablation studies (12 nodes for Stage 4).

**Evidence of Significance**: The accepted paper on compositional regularization included ablation studies on embedding dimensions, regularization weights, and architecture variants (Appendix C.1)—experiments that would be difficult to coordinate in a linear workflow. The system successfully navigated from initial feasibility through hyperparameter tuning to final ablation analysis without human intervention.

**Distinction from Prior Work**: Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2024) enables iterative self-improvement through reflection but operates on single trajectories. AIDE uses tree search for code generation but focuses on benchmark tasks with scalar evaluation scores. The AI Scientist-v2 adapts tree search to the multi-stage, multi-criteria nature of scientific experimentation where "performance metrics, training dynamics, and the quality of generated plots" (Section 3.2.2) must all be evaluated holistically.

### 4.3 Vision-Language Model Feedback: Closed-Loop Quality Control for Scientific Communication

**Innovation Type**: New capability (v1 had no VLM integration)

**What Changed**: The AI Scientist-v1 had no mechanism for evaluating visual outputs (Table 1 shows "✗" for VLM Reviewer). The AI Scientist-v2 integrates VLMs at two critical points: during experimentation (flagging buggy nodes based on figure quality) and during manuscript writing (verifying figure-caption alignment and detecting duplicates).

**Why This Matters**: Scientific figures are not decorative—they are primary vehicles for communicating results. Poor visualizations can misrepresent findings or obscure key patterns. The VLM integration creates a closed feedback loop:

- **Experimentation Phase**: Figures that fail VLM review mark the entire experimental node as buggy, triggering debugging. This ensures visual quality is treated as a first-class requirement, not an afterthought.

- **Manuscript Phase**: The VLM checks "alignment between figures and captions, identifying issues with visual clarity (e.g., missing legends, unclear labels), and detecting potential duplication of figures in the main text and appendix" (Section 3.4). This catches errors that would otherwise require human review.

**Evidence of Significance**: The AI Scientist Team Review of the accepted paper (Appendix C.1.1) noted specific figure issues: "The caption of Figure 3 is wrong. The validation loss increases as task complexity increases." While this error slipped through, the VLM system represents a mechanism for catching such issues automatically. The rejected papers (Appendices C.2 and C.3) show more severe figure problems—duplicate figures, missing reliability diagrams, unreadable plots—suggesting the VLM system is necessary but not sufficient for perfect quality.

**Distinction from Prior Work**: No prior automated scientific discovery system integrated VLMs for iterative figure refinement. This represents a new capability class rather than an incremental improvement. The VLM Image Review Prompt (Appendix B) produces structured JSON feedback with fields for image description, image review, caption review, and figure reference review—enabling automated parsing and action on the feedback.

### 4.4 Peer Review Validation: First Empirical Benchmark for AI-Generated Science

**Innovation Type**: Fundamental validation milestone

**What Changed**: Prior automated scientific discovery systems generated manuscripts but did not subject them to formal peer review. The AI Scientist-v2 submitted three fully autonomous manuscripts to the ICLR 2025 ICBINB workshop, with one achieving an average reviewer score of 6.33 and exceeding the human acceptance threshold.

**Why This Matters**: This is the first empirical evidence that AI-generated research can meet human quality standards under blind review conditions:

- **Objective Quality Benchmark**: Peer review provides external validation independent of the system's authors. The accepted paper received scores of 6 (weak accept), 7 (accept), and 6 (weak accept) from independent reviewers who were informed some submissions might be AI-generated but did not know which ones.

- **Community Precedent**: The authors coordinated with ICLR leadership, obtained IRB approval, and withdrew the accepted manuscript prior to publication to "avoid prematurely setting a precedent for purely AI-generated research entering the official scientific record without broader community discussion" (Section 4.1). This demonstrates responsible engagement with the scientific community.

- **Honest Assessment of Limitations**: The paper does not overclaim. Section 5 explicitly notes that "acceptance occurred at a workshop level rather than at the main conference track, and only one of the three AI-generated submissions was accepted." Workshop acceptance rates (typically 60-80%) are notably higher than main conference tracks (20-30%).

**Evidence of Significance**: Table 4 (Appendix C) summarizes the three submissions: one accepted (score 6.33), two rejected. The accepted paper's reviewers appreciated "the paper's strength in clearly presenting why the regularization term does not yield the anticipated improvements, emphasizing its informative negative results" (Section 4.2). This validates that the system can produce scientifically meaningful contributions, not just superficially correct manuscripts.

**Distinction from Prior Work**: AutoScience AI's Carl system claims to produce "academically peer-reviewed research" (Section 6 references), but The AI Scientist-v2 provides transparent documentation of the review process, scores, and withdrawal decision. This transparency enables community scrutiny and replication—essential for scientific validity.

|------------|------|--------------|
| Template-free code generation | Fundamental | Enables out-of-the-box deployment across domains without human scaffolding |
| Agentic tree search | Fundamental | Mimics human research exploration patterns; enables failure recovery |

Understanding this distinction is critical—the paper's primary claim is about system capability, not the scientific validity of the generated research itself.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: A Two-Tiered Experimental Design

The authors employ a carefully controlled evaluation methodology that distinguishes The AI Scientist-v2 from prior automated discovery systems. Unlike previous work that generated manuscripts without external validation, this paper subjects its outputs to formal peer review.

#### System-Level Evaluation: Peer Review as Ground Truth

**Experimental Setup**:
- **Venue**: ICLR 2025 workshop "I Can't Believe It's Not Better" (ICBINB), which focuses on negative results and unexpected findings
- **Total Submissions**: 43 manuscripts submitted to the workshop
- **AI-Generated Submissions**: 3 fully autonomous manuscripts generated by The AI Scientist-v2
- **Review Process**: Blind peer review where reviewers were informed some submissions might be AI-generated but were not told which ones
- **Reviewer Opt-Out**: Reviewers could opt out of reviewing potentially AI-generated manuscripts
- **Post-Review Withdrawal**: Accepted AI-generated manuscripts were withdrawn prior to publication to avoid setting premature precedents

**Selection Process** (Section 4.2):
The authors describe a meta-selection procedure that requires careful scrutiny:
1. The system generated approximately 40 research ideas (20 from core ML topics, 20 from applied domains)
2. Human researchers selected 3 most promising initial ideas based on alignment with workshop theme
3. For each selected idea, the system executed the full experimental pipeline multiple times with different random seeds
4. From the multiple complete manuscripts generated for each initial idea, humans selected the single best-resulting manuscript for submission

The authors explicitly acknowledge this selection step: "This process mimics a professor reviewing the work of many students or teams and deciding which work is ready to be submitted for peer review." They argue this represents "high-level experimental setup and process management (meta-selection from fully autonomous outputs), not human-in-the-loop intervention in the scientific content generation of the chosen manuscript."

**Ethical Safeguards**:
- IRB approval obtained from University of British Columbia (H24-02652)
- Full coordination with ICLR leadership and workshop organizers
- Reviewers informed after peer review completion about the experiment
- Accepted manuscript withdrawn prior to publication on OpenReview's public forum

#### Paper-Level Evaluation: Three Distinct Research Domains

The three submitted manuscripts span different machine learning domains, testing the system's generalization capability:

| Paper Title | Domain | Workshop Result | Average Score |
|-------------|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| Compositional Regularization: Unexpected Obstacles in Enhancing Neural Network Generalization | Sequence Modeling / NLP | **Accepted** | 6.33 |
| Unveiling the Impact of Label Noise on Model Calibration in Deep Learning | Computer Vision / Robustness | Rejected | Not reported |

#### Accepted Paper Results: Compositional Regularization Study

The accepted paper investigated whether incorporating an explicit compositional regularization term into neural network training improves compositional generalization. Key quantitative findings from this generated research:

**Dataset Configuration** (Appendix C.1, Section 4.1.1):
- Training set: **1,000** randomly generated arithmetic expressions
- Test set: **200** expressions not seen during training
- Expression components: digits and operators (e.g., "3+4", "7*2")

**Training Configuration** (Appendix C.1, Section 4.1.2):
- Epochs: **30**
- Optimizer: Adam
- Learning rate: **0.001**
- Batch size: **32**
- Regularization weight (λ): tested values of **0.0** (baseline), **0.1**, **0.3**, **0.5**, **0.7**, **1.0**

**Baseline Performance** (Appendix C.1, Figure 1):
- Test accuracy reached approximately **84%** without regularization
- Training and test loss decreased over epochs, indicating learning progress
- Compositional loss remained steady without regularization

**Impact of Regularization Weight λ** (Appendix C.1, Figure 2):
- Higher λ values led to lower compositional loss (regularization term was effective)
- However, test accuracy did **not improve** with higher λ
- In some cases, test accuracy **decreased** with regularization
- This represents a "negative result"—the hypothesis was not supported

**Architecture Comparisons** (Appendix C.1, Section B.2):
- Baseline LSTM: **85%** test accuracy (on simple task with numbers [1-9])
- Attention-augmented LSTM: **100%** test accuracy (on simple task)
- However, when task complexity increased (numbers [10-19], longer sequences):
  - Attention-augmented LSTM dropped to **56%** test accuracy
  - Baseline LSTM dropped to **0%** test accuracy

**Embedding Dimension Ablation** (Appendix C.1, Figure 4):
- Tested dimensions: **16, 32, 64, 128**
- Result: "Increasing the embedding dimension did not consistently improve test accuracy"
- This suggests the bottleneck lies in capturing compositional structures, not representational capacity

**Critical Code Review Finding** (Appendix C.1.2):
The AI Scientist Team conducted an internal code review that revealed a significant issue:
> "On average, we found that about **57%** of the test set overlapped with the training set."

This dataset overlap substantially undermines the validity of the reported generalization results. The paper claims to test compositional generalization to "novel combinations," but over half the test data was actually seen during training.

#### Rejected Paper Results: Label Noise Study

The second manuscript investigated how label noise affects model calibration. Key quantitative claims from this generated research:

**Datasets** (Appendix C.2, Section 3.1):
- CIFAR-10, MNIST, Fashion-MNIST
- Architecture: ResNet-18

**Noise Configuration** (Appendix C.2, Section 3.2):
- Symmetric noise: labels randomly flipped to any other class with equal probability
- Asymmetric noise: labels flipped to specific incorrect classes based on confusion matrix
- Noise rates: **10% to 50%**

**Claimed Results** (Appendix C.2, Figure 1):
- Test accuracy drops from approximately **85%** with no noise to around **60%** at 50% noise rate
- Asymmetric noise reportedly has more severe impact on calibration
- Expected Calibration Error (ECE) reportedly reaches up to **0.35** at higher noise levels

**Critical Issues Identified** (Appendix C.2.2, C.2.3):
The AI Scientist Team's internal review identified multiple problems:

The paper was influenced by initial plans that included temperature scaling, but the experiments were never conducted.

2. **Missing Reliability Diagrams**: The paper states it utilized reliability diagrams to visualize calibration performance, but "although the AI Scientist generated reliability diagrams during the experiments, they were never included in the paper."

3. **Duplicate Figures**: Supplementary material contains duplicate figures. Figure 4 is identified as "a duplicate of Figure 1." Figure 5 contains plots where "the first two and last two plots are identical."

4. **Incorrect Citations**: Multiple citations marked with (?) indicating missing references. The paper cites "Mots'oehli & kyungim Baek, 2024" for dataset usage claims that are not accurate.

5. **Hyperparameter Discrepancies**: The paper states models were trained for **100 epochs** with learning rate decay at epochs 50 and 75, but code review reveals "The number of epochs is either 20 or 30 instead of 100" and experiments use "a Cosine Annealing scheduler for the learning rate" rather than stepwise decay.

**Workshop Reviewer Feedback** (Appendix C.2.4):
- Reviewer 1 Rating: **3** (Clear rejection)
- Reviewer 2 Rating: **3** (Clear rejection)
- Key criticisms: "The paper is not finished, there are missing references... the figures are unreadable... I feel this paper is in a late draft status and not review ready."

#### Rejected Paper Results: Pest Detection Study

The third manuscript investigated deep learning for pest detection in real-world agricultural settings.

**Dataset** (Appendix C.3, Section 4):

This is the critical question. Let me assess each major claim against the experimental evidence.

**Assessment**: The claim is substantiated, but the context matters. This is a proof-of-concept demonstrating feasibility, not evidence that AI scientists can consistently produce conference-quality work.

#### Claim 2: "Eliminates Reliance on Human-Authored Code Templates"

**Evidence Strength**: **Strong**

The paper demonstrates template-free generation across three distinct domains:

- Dropout rate experiments (0.0, 0.2, 0.5)

**Caveats**:
- The paper does not provide a direct comparison between tree search and linear execution on the same research question
- No ablation study quantifies how much tree search contributes versus other improvements (VLM, template-free generation)
- The 57% train-test overlap in the accepted paper suggests exploration depth does not guarantee methodological rigor

**Assessment**: The tree search enables more experiments, but the paper lacks controlled ablations isolating tree search's contribution to success.

#### Claim 4: "VLM Integration Improves Visual Quality"

**Contradictory Evidence**:

- **Benefit**: Improves submission quality by selecting best of multiple runs

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This section critically examines the boundaries of The AI Scientist-v2's capabilities. Understanding what the system *cannot* do is as important as understanding what it can. The authors themselves acknowledge significant limitations in Section 5, but a deeper analysis reveals additional trade-offs that have implications for how this technology should be deployed and evaluated.

---

### 6.1 Fundamental Assumptions Underlying the Approach

The AI Scientist-v2 operates under several critical assumptions that constrain its applicability. When these assumptions are violated, system performance degrades substantially.

#### Assumption 1: Research Questions Are Amenable to Computational Experimentation

**What the System Assumes**: The research question can be answered through code-based experiments with quantifiable metrics (accuracy, loss, calibration error, etc.).

**Where This Breaks Down**:
- **Theoretical Work**: Mathematical proofs, conceptual frameworks, or philosophical arguments cannot be validated through code execution.
- **Qualitative Research**: Studies requiring human interviews, ethnographic observation, or subjective interpretation fall outside the system's scope.
- **Long-Term Studies**: Research requiring months or years of data collection (e.g., longitudinal clinical trials, climate modeling over decades) exceeds the 15-hour runtime limit (Appendix A).

**Evidence from Paper**: All three submitted manuscripts focused on empirical machine learning experiments with clear numerical outcomes. The accepted paper measured test accuracy on arithmetic expressions; the rejected papers measured calibration error and classification accuracy. None required theoretical derivation or qualitative analysis.

**Implication**: The AI Scientist-v2 is fundamentally an *empirical engineering* system, not a general scientific discovery tool. It excels at "does this method work?" questions but cannot address "why does this phenomenon exist?" questions requiring theoretical insight.

---

#### Assumption 2: Datasets Are Accessible via Standard APIs

**What the System Assumes**: Required datasets can be loaded using `datasets.load_dataset` from Hugging Face Hub (Section 3.3).

**Where This Breaks Down**:
- **Non-Standard Datasets**: The authors explicitly acknowledge this is "somewhat ad-hoc, as not all dataset repositories support this method" (Section 3.3).
- **Real-World Data**: For the pest detection paper, the system "struggled to automatically access real-world datasets available online." Humans had to "manually downloaded a relevant dataset from Kaggle... and reduced its size to one tenth" (Appendix C.3.1 note).
- **Proprietary Data**: Clinical, financial, or industrial datasets with access restrictions cannot be obtained autonomously.

**Evidence from Paper**: The pest detection paper's reliance on human-downloaded data represents a critical autonomy gap. While the *code generation* was template-free, the *data acquisition* was not fully automated.

**Implication**: Full autonomy requires full data access. Until the system can navigate dataset licensing, authentication, and preprocessing for arbitrary sources, its deployment remains constrained to well-curated benchmark environments.

---

#### Assumption 3: Evaluation Metrics Are Objective and Computable

**What the System Assumes**: Experiment success can be determined by scalar metrics (accuracy, loss, ECE) that an LLM evaluator can compare.

**Where This Breaks Down**:
- **Subjective Quality**: Aesthetic quality of generated art, user experience design, or writing style cannot be reduced to scalar scores.
- **Multi-Objective Trade-offs**: When improving one metric harms another (e.g., accuracy vs. fairness), the system lacks explicit guidance on how to balance competing objectives.
- **Novelty Assessment**: The system cannot reliably determine if an idea is genuinely novel versus incrementally modifying existing work. The literature search via Semantic Scholar helps but does not guarantee novelty validation.

**Evidence from Paper**: The tree search selection uses an LLM evaluator that considers "performance metrics, training dynamics, and the quality of generated plots" (Section 3.2.2). However, the 57% train-test overlap in the accepted paper suggests the evaluator did not catch a fundamental methodological flaw—likely because accuracy metrics looked acceptable despite the data leakage.

**Implication**: The system optimizes for what can be measured, not necessarily what matters scientifically. This creates risk of "metric gaming" where experiments appear successful by standard metrics but fail to address the actual research question.

---

#### Assumption 4: Code Execution Errors Are Recoverable Through Debugging

**What the System Assumes**: Buggy nodes can be fixed through iterative debugging within the Maximum Debug Depth of 3 (Table 3, Appendix A).

**Where This Breaks Down**:
- **Conceptual Errors**: If the experimental design itself is flawed (e.g., wrong loss function for the task), debugging syntax errors will not fix the underlying problem.
- **Cascading Failures**: Errors in early stages propagate through subsequent stages. A flawed baseline in Stage 1 contaminates all downstream hyperparameter tuning and ablation studies.
- **Resource Exhaustion**: If debugging consumes the full debug depth without success, that research branch terminates.

**Evidence from Paper**: The rejected label noise paper had temperature scaling "implemented... but never actually used" (Appendix C.2.3). This suggests the system failed to recognize that planned experiments were not executed—a conceptual error that debugging could not resolve.

**Implication**: The tree search provides robustness against *implementation* errors but not *design* errors. Human oversight remains necessary for validating experimental methodology.

### 6.2 Unaddressed Scenarios and Edge Cases

The paper demonstrates success on specific problem types but leaves significant gaps in coverage. These unaddressed scenarios represent important boundaries for potential users.

#### Scenario 1: Multi-Modal Research Requiring Physical Experimentation

**Gap**: The system operates entirely in silico—code generation, simulation, and digital data analysis.

**Why This Matters**: Many scientific domains require physical apparatus:
- **Robotics**: Hardware-in-the-loop testing cannot be simulated perfectly.
- **Chemistry/Biology**: Wet lab experiments require physical reagents, equipment, and safety protocols.
- **Clinical Research**: Human subject studies require IRB approval, informed consent, and ethical oversight that cannot be automated.

**Evidence from Paper**: All three manuscripts are purely computational machine learning studies. The Related Work section mentions "self-driving laboratories" (Shi et al., 2025) as a complementary approach but does not integrate physical experimentation capabilities.

**Implication**: The AI Scientist-v2 is limited to computational sciences. Extension to physical sciences would require integration with robotic laboratory systems—a fundamentally different engineering challenge.

---

#### Scenario 2: Research Requiring External Collaboration or Data Sharing

**Gap**: The system operates as a closed loop without mechanisms for inter-researcher communication.

**Why This Matters**: Modern science is increasingly collaborative:
- **Multi-Institution Studies**: Clinical trials often span multiple hospitals with different protocols.
- **Data Sharing Agreements**: Many datasets require formal agreements before access.
- **Peer Feedback During Research**: Human researchers often adjust directions based on informal feedback from colleagues before publication.

**Evidence from Paper**: The system generates ideas, executes experiments, and writes manuscripts without any external input beyond initial topic prompts. The only "collaboration" is with literature databases via Semantic Scholar queries.

**Implication**: The system cannot participate in the social dimensions of science—negotiation, collaboration, consensus-building—that are essential for many research endeavors.

#### Scenario 3: High-Stakes Research with Safety Implications

**Gap**: The system lacks explicit safety constraints for research that could cause harm if misapplied.

**Why This Matters**: Certain research directions carry dual-use risks:
- **Biosecurity**: Pathogen modeling could be misused for harmful purposes.
- **Cybersecurity**: Vulnerability discovery could be weaponized.
- **Misinformation**: Techniques for generating persuasive content could be abused.

**Evidence from Paper**: The paper discusses AI safety in general terms (Abstract mentions "including AI safety") but does not describe concrete safety mechanisms within the system. The ethical safeguards focus on transparency (IRB approval, reviewer notification) rather than content restrictions.

**Implication**: Deploying this system without safety guardrails risks generating research that could be harmful. The authors' decision to withdraw the accepted paper shows awareness of this issue, but systematic safety mechanisms are not described.

---

#### Scenario 4: Long-Horizon Research Programs

**Gap**: The system is designed for single-paper projects with 15-hour runtime limits.

**Why This Matters**: Breakthrough research often requires:
- **Iterative Refinement Over Months**: Ideas evolve through multiple publication cycles.
- **Building on Prior Results**: Research programs accumulate knowledge across multiple papers.
- **Responding to Community Feedback**: Revision based on peer review often leads to new research directions.

**Evidence from Paper**: Each of the three manuscripts was generated independently from scratch. There is no mechanism for the system to maintain a "research memory" across projects or build cumulatively on previous findings.

**Implication**: The system excels at producing individual papers but cannot sustain long-term research programs. This limits its utility for addressing complex problems requiring sustained investigation.

### 6.4 Weaknesses and Open Questions

Beyond the limitations acknowledged by the authors, several weaknesses emerge from critical analysis of the experimental results and system design.

#### Weakness 1: Methodological Rigor Gaps

**Evidence**: The accepted paper had a 57% train-test overlap (Appendix C.1.2), which fundamentally undermines claims about compositional generalization. This error was caught by human code review, not by the system's automated checks.

**Root Cause**: The system validates code *execution* (does it run without errors?) but not code *correctness* (does it implement the intended methodology?). A buggy node is one that crashes or produces poor figures—not one that has subtle methodological flaws.

**Open Question**: How can automated systems validate methodological correctness, not just syntactic correctness? This may require:
- Formal specification of experimental requirements
- Cross-validation checks (e.g., verifying train-test separation)
- Domain-specific sanity checks

**Implication**: Until methodological validation improves, AI-generated research requires human expert review to catch subtle but critical errors.

---

#### Weakness 2: Inconsistent Quality Across Runs

**Evidence**: Only 1 of 3 submissions was accepted (33% success rate). The rejected papers had severe issues:
- Label noise paper: Unexecuted experiments claimed as results, duplicate figures, missing citations
- Pest detection paper: Misleading multi-dataset training claims, no real-world deployment

**Root Cause**: The tree search introduces stochasticity. Different random seeds produce different exploration paths, leading to variable quality. The human meta-selection process (choosing best of multiple runs) obscures this variability.

**Open Question**: What is the true per-run acceptance probability? The authors acknowledge: "Our current study aims to see whether The AI Scientist-v2 can produce at least one paper that survives peer review, and not what fraction of the time it can do so" (Section 4.2).

**Implication**: Users cannot reliably predict whether a single run will produce publication-quality output. This uncertainty limits practical deployment for time-sensitive research.

---

#### Weakness 3: Citation and Reference Management

**Evidence**: Multiple papers had missing citations marked with (?):
- Label noise paper: "CIFAR-10 (?), MNIST (?), and Fashion-MNIST (?)" (Appendix C.2, Section 3.1)
- Pest detection paper: "ResNet-18 architecture (?)" and multiple dataset citations missing (Appendix C.3, Section 3)

**Root Cause**: The system generates citations from literature search but does not verify that citation keys match the references.bib file. The Writeup Prompt instructs "Do not hallucinate new citations" but enforcement is imperfect.

**Open Question**: Can citation management be fully automated without human verification? This may require:
- Automated bibliography validation
- Cross-referencing citation keys with actual references
- Integration with citation databases for accuracy checking

**Implication**: Citation errors undermine scientific credibility and make literature tracing difficult. This is a solvable engineering problem but remains unaddressed.

---

#### Weakness 4: VLM Integration Is Necessary but Not Sufficient

**Evidence**: Despite VLM figure review:
- Accepted paper: "The caption of Figure 3 is wrong" (Appendix C.1.1)
- Rejected papers: "Figures are unreadable" (Appendix C.3.4), duplicate figures, missing reliability diagrams

**Root Cause**: VLM feedback is one input to the debugging process, but the system may prioritize other factors (e.g., execution success, metric values) over visual quality. The VLM review happens but its recommendations may not be fully implemented.

**Open Question**: How can VLM feedback be more tightly integrated into the node selection and refinement process? Possible improvements:
- Weight VLM feedback more heavily in node evaluation
- Require VLM approval before figures are included in manuscript
- Iterative VLM review until quality thresholds are met

**Implication**: VLM integration improves quality but does not guarantee it. Human review remains necessary for final figure validation.

---

#### Weakness 5: No Mechanism for Learning from Failures Across Projects

**Evidence**: Each of the three papers was generated independently. The system does not appear to retain lessons from the rejected papers when generating subsequent work.

**Root Cause**: The system architecture is stateless across projects. There is no "experience replay" or meta-learning component that accumulates knowledge about what works and what doesn't.

**Open Question**: How can the system learn from its own failures? This may require:
- Maintaining a database of past experiments and outcomes
- Training fine-tuned models on successful vs. unsuccessful runs
- Incorporating failure patterns into idea generation and experiment planning

**Implication**: Without cross-project learning, the system repeats mistakes. This limits long-term improvement and efficiency gains.

---

#### Weakness 6: Ethical and Normative Uncertainty

**Evidence**: The authors withdrew the accepted paper "to avoid prematurely setting a precedent for purely AI-generated research entering the official scientific record without broader community discussion" (Section 4.1).

**Unresolved Questions**:
- **Authorship**: Should AI systems be listed as co-authors? The paper lists "AI Scientist-v2" but this is not standard practice.
- **Disclosure**: Should AI-generated papers always be labeled? The authors advocate for this but community norms are not established.
- **Incentive Alignment**: Could this technology be used to "game peer review or artificially inflate the CVs of unscrupulous scientists" (Section 5)?
- **Quality Standards**: Should AI-generated papers be held to different standards than human papers?

**Implication**: The technical capability has outpaced the development of ethical norms and policies. Responsible deployment requires ongoing community dialogue, not just technical solutions.

---

### 6.5 Summary: The Autonomy-Quality Trade-off

The central tension underlying all limitations is the **autonomy-quality trade-off**:

| Dimension | High Autonomy | High Quality |
|-----------|---------------|--------------|
| Code Generation | Template-free, any domain | Requires human templates for reliability |
| Experiment Design | System proposes hypotheses | Humans validate methodology |
| Data Access | Automated via APIs | Humans handle non-standard datasets |
| Error Recovery | Automated debugging | Humans catch methodological errors |
| Manuscript Writing | Fully automated | Humans verify citations and claims |
| Peer Review Submission | System generates everything | Humans select best of multiple runs |

**The AI Scientist-v2** prioritizes autonomy, accepting quality variability as a consequence. This is appropriate for a research prototype demonstrating feasibility. However, for practical deployment, users must decide where on this spectrum their use case falls.

**Recommendations for Practitioners**:
1. **Use for Exploratory Research**: The system excels at generating negative results and exploring multiple hypotheses quickly.
2. **Maintain Human Oversight**: Expert review is essential for methodological validation, especially for high-stakes research.
3. **Budget for Multiple Runs**: Plan for 3+ runs per research question to account for quality variability.
4. **Start with Benchmark Domains**: Use well-curated datasets (Hugging Face) before attempting real-world data.
5. **Engage with Ethical Norms**: Follow the authors' transparency practices (disclosure, IRB approval, community dialogue).

**Open Research Directions**:
- Methodological validation beyond syntactic correctness
- Cross-project learning from failures
- Integration with physical experimentation systems
- Community standards for AI-generated research disclosure
- Cost reduction through model efficiency or open-source alternatives

The AI Scientist-v2 represents a significant milestone, but it is a beginning, not an endpoint. The limitations identified here define the research agenda for the next generation of automated scientific discovery systems.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This section examines how The AI Scientist-v2 reshapes the landscape of automated scientific discovery, what research directions it opens, and when practitioners should consider deploying such systems. The implications extend beyond technical capability—they touch on how science is conducted, validated, and scaled.

---

### 7.1 Landscape Shifts: What This Work Changes

The AI Scientist-v2 represents more than an incremental improvement over v1. It establishes several precedents that will influence how the field evaluates and deploys automated discovery systems.

#### Peer Review as the New Gold Standard for Evaluation

Prior automated scientific discovery systems generated manuscripts without external validation. The AI Scientist-v2 establishes peer review as the critical benchmark:

> "One manuscript achieved an average reviewer score of 6.33 (placing it roughly in the top 45% of submissions) and would have been accepted after meta-review were it human-generated, thus becoming the first fully AI-generated manuscript to successfully pass a peer-review process." (Section 1)

**Why This Matters**: This shifts the evaluation paradigm from internal metrics (e.g., "the system generated a complete paper") to external validation (e.g., "independent reviewers judged this paper acceptable"). Future systems will likely be expected to demonstrate peer review success rather than just manuscript completion.

**Implications for the Field**:
- **Benchmark Redefinition**: MLEBench (Chan et al., 2025) and similar benchmarks evaluate code generation on predefined tasks. This work suggests future benchmarks should include open-ended research questions with peer review outcomes.
- **Transparency Expectations**: The authors' coordination with ICLR leadership, IRB approval (H24-02652), and post-review withdrawal set a transparency precedent. Future work will likely face scrutiny on ethical safeguards.
- **Quality Thresholds**: The 33% workshop acceptance rate (1 of 3 papers) establishes a baseline. Future systems will be compared against this benchmark.

#### From Template-Dependent to Template-Free Autonomy

The elimination of human-authored code templates (Section 3.2) represents a qualitative capability shift:

| Capability | The AI Scientist-v1 | The AI Scientist-v2 |
|------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Codebase | Topic-Specific | Domain-General |
| Human Template Required | Yes | No |
| Out-of-the-Box Deployment | No | Yes |

**Why This Matters**: Template dependency was a hidden human labor cost that constrained scalability. Removing it enables:
- **Cross-Domain Deployment**: The system generated papers on sequence modeling, computer vision, and agricultural applications without domain-specific templates.
- **Novel Research Directions**: Templates inherently bias toward incremental modifications. Template-free generation allows ideas that do not fit pre-existing structures.
- **Reduced Setup Time**: Researchers no longer need to write scaffolding code before deploying the system.

**Caveat**: The pest detection paper required humans to manually download the dataset from Kaggle (Appendix C.3.1 note). Full autonomy requires solving data access, not just code generation.

#### Negative Results as Valid Scientific Contributions

The accepted paper's core finding was that compositional regularization does **not** improve generalization—a negative result. Reviewers appreciated "the paper's strength in clearly presenting why the regularization term does not yield the anticipated improvements" (Section 4.2).

**Why This Matters**: This validates that AI-generated research can contribute meaningfully through honest reporting of failed hypotheses, not just positive findings. The ICBINB workshop's focus on "negative results and unexpected findings" aligned well with the system's output.

**Implications**:
- **Publication Bias Mitigation**: AI systems may be particularly well-suited for reporting negative results that human researchers might not publish due to career incentives.
- **Exploratory Research Fit**: The system excels at testing hypotheses that might fail, making it valuable for high-risk, high-reward exploration.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper's limitations and open questions define a clear research agenda for the next generation of automated discovery systems.

#### Priority 1: Methodological Validation Beyond Syntactic Correctness

**Problem**: The system validates that code *runs* but not that it implements correct methodology. The accepted paper had 57% train-test overlap (Appendix C.1.2), which undermines generalization claims but was not caught by automated checks.

**Research Directions**:
- **Formal Specification Languages**: Develop ways to specify experimental requirements (e.g., "train and test sets must be disjoint") that the system can verify.
- **Domain-Specific Sanity Checks**: Build validators for common methodological errors (data leakage, improper baselines, missing controls).
- **Cross-Validation Automation**: Automatically run holdout checks to verify no data leakage between training and evaluation.

**Why This Matters**: Methodological errors are more damaging than syntax errors. A paper with correct code but flawed methodology produces misleading science.

---

#### Priority 2: Cross-Project Learning and Memory

**Problem**: Each of the three papers was generated independently. The system does not retain lessons from rejected papers when generating subsequent work.

**Research Directions**:
- **Experience Databases**: Maintain structured records of past experiments, including failure patterns and successful strategies.
- **Meta-Learning Components**: Fine-tune models on successful vs. unsuccessful runs to improve future idea generation and experiment planning.
- **Failure Pattern Recognition**: Identify common failure modes (e.g., unexecuted experiments claimed as results in the label noise paper) and build checks to prevent recurrence.

**Why This Matters**: Human researchers learn from mistakes across projects. Without cross-project learning, the system repeats errors, limiting long-term efficiency gains.

---

#### Priority 3: Integration with Physical Experimentation

**Problem**: The system operates entirely in silico. Many scientific domains require physical apparatus (robotics, chemistry, biology, clinical research).

**Research Directions**:
- **Self-Driving Laboratory Integration**: Connect with robotic laboratory systems (Shi et al., 2025) that can execute physical experiments.
- **Hybrid Simulation-Physical Workflows**: Use simulation for initial exploration, then validate promising directions with physical experiments.
- **Safety Constraints for Physical Actions**: Develop guardrails for experiments that could cause harm if misexecuted.

**Why This Matters**: Computational science is only a subset of scientific discovery. Extending to physical domains would dramatically expand impact but requires fundamentally different engineering.

---

#### Priority 4: Per-Run Success Rate Characterization

**Problem**: The paper reports 1 of 3 papers accepted but acknowledges this reflects human meta-selection, not per-run success probability: "Our current study aims to see whether The AI Scientist-v2 can produce at least one paper that survives peer review, and not what fraction of the time it can do so" (Section 4.2).

**Research Directions**:
- **Large-Scale Acceptance Studies**: Run the system dozens of times on similar research questions to measure true acceptance probability.
- **Seed Sensitivity Analysis**: Quantify how much output quality varies across random seeds.
- **Cost-Quality Trade-off Curves**: Measure how acceptance probability scales with computational budget (more nodes, more seeds, longer runtime).

**Why This Matters**: Practitioners need to know expected success rates for planning. A 33% success rate with meta-selection might correspond to a 10% per-run rate, which affects resource allocation decisions.

---

#### Priority 5: Citation and Reference Management

**Problem**: Multiple papers had missing citations marked with (?), and the label noise paper cited sources inaccurately (Appendix C.2).

**Research Directions**:
- **Automated Bibliography Validation**: Cross-reference citation keys with actual references.bib content before manuscript finalization.
- **Citation Database Integration**: Query citation databases (Semantic Scholar, Google Scholar) to verify citation accuracy.
- **Reference Completeness Checks**: Ensure all claims that require citations have corresponding references.

**Why This Matters**: Citation errors undermine scientific credibility and make literature tracing difficult. This is a solvable engineering problem that should be addressed before widespread deployment.

### 7.6 Summary: A Milestone, Not a Destination

The AI Scientist-v2 demonstrates that fully autonomous scientific discovery validated through peer review is now achievable at the workshop level. This is a proof-of-concept milestone that establishes feasibility, not evidence that AI scientists are ready to replace human researchers.

**Key Takeaways**:
1. **Peer review validation** is now the gold standard for evaluating automated discovery systems.
2. **Template-free autonomy** enables cross-domain deployment but introduces quality variability.
3. **Negative results** are valid and valuable scientific contributions that AI systems can reliably produce.
4. **Methodological validation** remains the critical gap—systems must catch design errors, not just syntax errors.
5. **Human oversight** remains necessary for high-stakes research, but the degree of oversight can decrease as systems improve.

**For Practitioners**: Deploy this system for exploratory research, negative result documentation, and baseline experimentation. Maintain human expert review for methodological validation, especially in high-stakes domains. Engage with community norms on transparency and disclosure.

**For Researchers**: The limitations identified in this paper define a clear research agenda. Methodological validation, cross-project learning, physical integration, and per-run success characterization are the priority directions for the next generation of automated discovery systems.

The AI Scientist-v2 is a beginning, not an endpoint. It demonstrates what is possible and clarifies what remains to be built.
