## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces **GLaM** (Generalist Language Model), a family of decoder-only language models utilizing a sparsely activated **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)** architecture to scale model capacity to **1.2 trillion parameters** while activating only **96.6 billion parameters** (8%) per token during inference. By decoupling total parameter count from computational cost, GLaM achieves superior zero, one, and few-shot performance across **29 NLP benchmarks** compared to the dense **GPT-3 (175B)** model, while consuming only **one-third of the training energy** (456 MWh vs. 1287 MWh) and requiring **48.6% fewer FLOPs per token** for inference. This work demonstrates that sparse activation allows for efficient scaling of large language models, breaking the traditional trade-off where increased model size necessitates prohibitively expensive computational resources.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Scaling Bottleneck: Capacity vs. Cost
The central problem driving this research is the **prohibitive computational cost** associated with scaling dense language models. Over the past decade, the field of Natural Language Processing (NLP) has observed that increasing model size (parameters), data volume, and compute resources leads to significant performance gains, particularly in **in-context learning**—the ability of a model to perform new tasks given only a few examples in the prompt, without gradient updates.

However, this progress faces a hard physical and economic wall. In traditional **dense architectures** (such as GPT-3), every single parameter in the model must be activated and computed for every single token processed. Mathematically, if a model has $N$ parameters, the computational cost (measured in Floating Point Operations, or FLOPs) per token scales linearly with $N$.
*   **The Consequence:** To double the model's intelligence (capacity), one must roughly double the compute cost for both training and inference.
*   **Real-World Impact:** As models grow to hundreds of billions of parameters, the energy consumption becomes massive. For instance, training GPT-3 consumed approximately **1,287 MWh** of energy. Scaling this approach to trillion-parameter models would require energy and hardware resources that are environmentally unsustainable and economically inaccessible to most organizations.

The specific gap this paper addresses is: **How can we continue to increase model capacity (to improve reasoning and knowledge retention) without linearly increasing the computational cost per token?**

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

To understand the solution proposed by GLaM, we must examine the two primary paradigms that existed prior to this work:

#### 1. Dense Scaling (The Status Quo)
Models like **GPT-3**, **Jurassic-1**, and **Gopher** rely on dense Transformer architectures.
*   **Mechanism:** For an input token $x$, the model computes $y = f(x; \theta)$, where $\theta$ represents all model weights. Every weight participates in the calculation.
*   **Limitation:** As noted in **Table 2**, for a dense model, the number of total parameters (`nparams`) equals the number of activated parameters per token (`nact-params`). A 175B parameter model requires 175B parameters worth of computation for every prediction. This creates a rigid coupling between model size and inference latency/energy.

#### 2. Pre-training and Fine-tuning
Before the rise of massive in-context learners, the dominant paradigm was **pre-training followed by task-specific fine-tuning** (e.g., BERT, T5).
*   **Mechanism:** A model is trained on general data, then further trained (fine-tuned) on specific labeled datasets for tasks like sentiment analysis or question answering.
*   **Limitation:** This approach requires labeled data for every new task and separate model weights for each task. It does not support the flexibility of **few-shot learning**, where a single model handles diverse tasks purely via text prompts. Furthermore, fine-tuning large dense models still incurs high computational costs during the adaptation phase.

#### 3. Early Sparse Architectures (Mixture-of-Experts)
The concept of **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)** is not new; it was introduced to allow models to have many parameters while computing only a subset.
*   **Mechanism:** The model contains multiple "expert" sub-networks. A gating mechanism selects only the top-$k$ experts to process a given input.
*   **Prior Work:**
    *   **Shazeer et al. (2017)** demonstrated MoE in language modeling but faced stability and scaling challenges.
    *   **GShard (Lepikhin et al., 2021)** and **Switch-C (Fedus et al., 2021)** successfully scaled MoE to trillion parameters.
*   **The Critical Gap:** While Switch-C achieved 1.5 trillion parameters, it was an **encoder-decoder** model primarily evaluated on **fine-tuning** benchmarks (like SuperGLUE). It was not optimized or proven for **decoder-only few-shot in-context learning** at the scale of GPT-3. There was no demonstration that a sparse, decoder-only model could outperform dense giants like GPT-3 in zero-shot and few-shot settings while maintaining efficiency. The community lacked evidence that sparsity could replace density for *generalist* language modeling without sacrificing the emergent abilities seen in dense scaling.

### Positioning of GLaM

GLaM positions itself as the bridge between the **emergent few-shot capabilities** of dense models (like GPT-3) and the **computational efficiency** of sparse architectures.

1.  **Architecture Shift:** Unlike Switch-C (encoder-decoder), GLaM is a **decoder-only** model. This architectural choice is crucial because decoder-only models are the standard for autoregressive generation and in-context learning. GLaM proves that sparsity works effectively in this specific topology.
2.  **Decoupling Capacity from Compute:** GLaM introduces a regime where `nparams` $\gg$ `nact-params`.
    *   **Total Parameters:** 1.2 Trillion (providing massive memory/knowledge capacity).
    *   **Activated Parameters:** ~96.6 Billion per token (keeping compute costs low).
    *   **Comparison:** As shown in **Table 1**, GLaM activates only **8%** of its total parameters per token. This allows it to have $7\times$ the capacity of GPT-3 while using only half the FLOPs per token during inference.
3.  **Performance Paradigm:** The paper explicitly targets the **few-shot in-context learning** setting. It argues that sparse models are not just efficient substitutes for dense models but are superior candidates for scaling. The results show that GLaM outperforms GPT-3 across **29 NLP tasks** (including Open-Domain QA and Common Sense Reasoning) despite using significantly less compute.

In summary, GLaM addresses the sustainability crisis of AI scaling. It challenges the assumption that "bigger must mean slower and more expensive" by demonstrating that **conditional computation** (activating only relevant parts of the network) allows for trillion-parameter models that are faster, cheaper, and more accurate than their dense predecessors.

## 3. Technical Approach

This section details the architectural innovations, data curation strategies, and training methodologies that enable GLaM to scale efficiently. We move from the high-level concept of sparse activation to the specific engineering choices—such as gating mechanisms, expert distribution, and data filtering—that make trillion-parameter training feasible and effective.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
GLaM is a massive language model that functions like a library with thousands of specialized books, but for every word it reads or writes, it only opens exactly two books rather than reading the entire library at once. It solves the problem of prohibitive computational cost by decoupling the model's total knowledge capacity (1.2 trillion parameters) from its active processing cost (96.6 billion parameters per token), using a dynamic routing system to select only the most relevant sub-networks for each specific input.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The GLaM system consists of three primary interacting components arranged in a deep stack:
1.  **The Input Stream & Tokenizer:** Raw text is first converted into discrete tokens using a SentencePiece tokenizer with a vocabulary of 256,000 units, forming the sequence that enters the model.
2.  **The Hybrid Transformer Stack:** The core engine is a decoder-only Transformer where standard self-attention layers are interleaved with **Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) layers**. In every other layer, the standard feed-forward network is replaced by a bank of 64 independent "expert" networks.
3.  **The Dynamic Gating Module:** Sitting at the entrance of each MoE layer, this lightweight router analyzes each incoming token and calculates a probability score for all 64 experts, selecting only the top-two experts to process that specific token while ignoring the rest.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
To fully understand how GLaM achieves its efficiency, we will proceed in the following logical order:
*   **Data Foundation:** We first examine the construction of the 1.6 trillion token dataset, explaining why quality filtering is more critical for sparse models than simply increasing data volume.
*   **The MoE Mechanism:** We dissect the mathematical operation of the gating function and expert selection, detailing how the model decides which parameters to activate for a given token.
*   **Architectural Modifications:** We explore specific changes to the standard Transformer, such as the replacement of standard activations with Gated Linear Units (GLU) and the use of relative positional biases.
*   **Parallelism and Scaling:** We explain the 2D sharding algorithm used to distribute the massive model across 1,024 TPU chips, solving the memory and communication bottlenecks of trillion-parameter training.
*   **Training Stability:** We conclude with the specific hyperparameters and stabilization tricks (like gradient skipping) required to prevent divergence during the training of such a large, sparse network.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

#### The Data Pipeline: Quality Over Quantity
The foundation of GLaM's performance lies not just in the scale of its architecture but in the rigorous curation of its training data, which totals **1.6 trillion tokens**.
*   The authors construct a high-quality dataset by starting with a massive raw corpus of web pages and applying a learned **text quality classifier** to filter out low-quality content.
*   This classifier is a feature-hash-based linear model trained to distinguish between curated, high-quality text sources (such as Wikipedia, books, and selected websites) and the rest of the open web.
*   Instead of a hard threshold that might introduce systematic biases, the system uses a **Pareto distribution** to sample webpages based on their quality scores, ensuring that while high-quality pages are favored, some lower-quality diverse content is still included.
*   The final dataset is a mixture of several sources with specific weights chosen to balance performance and prevent over-sampling of small domains: **Filtered Webpages** (143B tokens, weight 0.42), **Conversations** (174B tokens, weight 0.28), **Books** (390B tokens, weight 0.20), **Forums** (247B tokens, weight 0.02), **News** (650B tokens, weight 0.02), and **Wikipedia** (3B tokens, weight 0.06).
*   The authors explicitly demonstrate in **Figure 3(c-d)** that training on this filtered dataset yields significantly better performance on both Natural Language Generation (NLG) and Understanding (NLU) tasks compared to training on unfiltered data, highlighting that data quality is a non-negotiable prerequisite for effective scaling.

#### The Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) Mechanism
The core innovation of GLaM is the replacement of standard dense feed-forward layers with sparse MoE layers, allowing the model to access a vast parameter space without computing all of it.
*   In a standard Transformer, every token passes through a single feed-forward network (FFN); in GLaM, every other Transformer layer contains an MoE layer consisting of $E$ independent expert networks, where $E=64$ in the largest model.
*   For each input token $x$, a learnable **gating function** $G(x)$ computes a probability distribution over all $E$ experts to determine which ones are best suited to process that specific token.
*   The gating function uses a softmax activation to produce weights, but GLaM employs a "top-$k$" selection strategy where $k=2$, meaning only the two experts with the highest probabilities are activated for any given token.
*   Mathematically, if $W_i$ represents the weights of the $i$-th expert, the output $y$ for a token $x$ is computed as:
    $$ y = \sum_{i \in \text{Top-2}(G(x))} G(x)_i \cdot E_i(x) $$
    where $G(x)_i$ is the gating weight assigned to expert $i$, and $E_i(x)$ is the output of the expert network.
*   This design creates a combinatorial explosion of potential sub-networks; with 64 experts and selecting 2, there are $\binom{64}{2} = 2,016$ possible combinations of experts that can be activated for different tokens, providing immense computational flexibility.
*   Crucially, while the total parameter count ($n_{params}$) reaches **1.2 trillion**, the number of activated parameters per token ($n_{act-params}$) is only **96.6 billion**, which is approximately **8%** of the total capacity.
*   To ensure that the gating function does not collapse to using only a few popular experts (which would negate the benefits of sparsity), the training objective includes an **auxiliary load-balancing loss** with a coefficient of 0.01, encouraging the model to distribute tokens evenly across all 64 experts.

#### Architectural Modifications to the Transformer
Beyond the insertion of MoE layers, GLaM incorporates several specific modifications to the standard Transformer architecture to improve stability and performance at scale.
*   **Positional Encoding:** Instead of using standard absolute positional embeddings, GLaM adopts **per-layer relative positional bias** (from Dai et al., 2019), which allows the model to better capture dependencies based on the distance between tokens rather than their absolute position in the sequence.
*   **Activation Functions:** In the non-MoE Transformer feed-forward sub-layers, the standard ReLU or GeLU activations are replaced with **Gated Linear Units (GLU)**.
*   The GLU mechanism computes the component-wise product of two linear transformations of the input, followed by a **Gaussian Error Linear Unit (GeLU)** activation, a choice shown to improve gradient flow and model capacity.
*   The model maintains a maximum sequence length of **1,024 tokens**, and during training, examples are packed into batches containing up to **1 million tokens** to maximize hardware utilization.

#### Parallelism and System Implementation
Training a model with 1.2 trillion parameters requires sophisticated distributed computing strategies to fit the model into memory and manage communication overhead.
*   GLaM utilizes a **2D sharding algorithm** (described in Xu et al., 2021) that exploits the 2D mesh topology of the TPU v4 cluster to partition both weights and computations.
*   The system partitions the expert weight tensors (shape $[E, M, H]$) along the expert dimension $E$ and the hidden dimension $H$, while input activation tensors (shape $[B, S, M]$) are partitioned along the batch dimension $B$ and model dimension $M$.
*   A key optimization involves placing experts with the same index across different MoE layers on the same physical device; this allows the repetitive computation graph of the MoE layers to be wrapped in a `while` loop control flow, significantly reducing compilation time.
*   When individual experts become too large to fit on a single chip, the system allocates each expert across a set of $N_E$ devices, ensuring that there is no redundancy in data or compute across the **1,024 Cloud TPU-v4 chips** used for the largest model.
*   The system relies on the **GSPMD** compiler to automatically determine optimal sharding strategies for the remaining tensors, handling the complex data movement required when tokens are routed to experts located on different devices.

#### Training Procedure and Stability Hyperparameters
Training at the trillion-parameter scale leaves little room for error, requiring a carefully tuned set of hyperparameters and stability mechanisms.
*   The model is optimized using **Adafactor**, an adaptive learning rate optimizer, with a first-moment decay $\beta_1 = 0$, a second-moment decay $\beta_2 = 0.99$, and an update clipping threshold of **1.0**.
*   The learning rate schedule begins with a constant rate of **0.01** for the first **10,000 steps**, followed by an inverse square root decay ($lr(t) \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{t}}$).
*   Notably, the **dropout rate is set to 0**; the authors argue that the sheer volume of training data (1.6T tokens) exceeds the number of tokens processed during training, making regularization via dropout unnecessary and potentially harmful.
*   To handle numerical instability inherent in such large models, the training pipeline implements a **gradient skipping** mechanism: if any NaNs (Not a Number) or Infs (Infinity) are detected in the gradients for a batch, the weight update for that batch is skipped entirely.
*   Furthermore, the system monitors for rare large fluctuations; if instability persists, training is restarted from an earlier "healthy" checkpoint, leveraging the randomness of sequentially loaded batches to escape the failed state.
*   The model uses **float32** precision for weights to maintain accuracy but utilizes **bfloat16** for activations to reduce memory bandwidth requirements during the forward and backward passes.

#### Evaluation Protocol
To validate the effectiveness of this architecture, the authors employ a rigorous evaluation framework focused on in-context learning capabilities.
*   The model is evaluated on **29 public NLP benchmarks** spanning 7 categories, including Open-Domain Question Answering, Common Sense Reasoning, and Natural Language Inference.
*   The evaluation covers **zero-shot** (no examples), **one-shot** (one example), and **few-shot** (multiple examples) settings, where demonstrations are concatenated directly to the input prompt without any gradient updates.
*   For generative tasks, the model uses **beam search with a width of 4** to decode sequences, while classification tasks rely on the maximum log-likelihood of the answer options, normalized by token length to prevent bias towards shorter answers.
*   This protocol ensures a direct comparison with GPT-3, isolating the impact of the MoE architecture on the model's ability to generalize from context rather than its ability to be fine-tuned on specific datasets.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The GLaM paper does not merely present a larger model; it fundamentally challenges the prevailing assumption that scaling language models requires a linear increase in computational cost. The following insights distinguish GLaM from prior dense scaling efforts and earlier sparse architectures.

### 4.1 Decoupling Model Capacity from Computational Cost
The most profound innovation of GLaM is the empirical demonstration that **model capacity** (the ability to store knowledge and patterns) can be scaled independently of **computational cost** (the FLOPs required for inference).

*   **The Prior Paradigm:** In dense models like GPT-3, capacity and cost are rigidly coupled. To double the model's knowledge base, one must double the number of parameters, which directly doubles the inference latency and energy consumption per token. This creates a hard ceiling where models become too slow or expensive to serve before they reach optimal intelligence.
*   **The GLaM Breakthrough:** By leveraging the MoE architecture described in Section 3, GLaM breaks this coupling. The largest GLaM model possesses **1.2 trillion parameters** (7× the capacity of GPT-3) but activates only **96.6 billion parameters** per token.
*   **Significance:** This decoupling yields a "free lunch" in terms of efficiency. As quantified in **Table 1**, GLaM achieves superior performance while consuming only **48.6% of the FLOPs per token** compared to GPT-3. This is not an incremental optimization; it represents a regime change where trillion-parameter models become feasible for real-time inference, whereas dense equivalents would be prohibitively slow. The insight here is that **sparsity allows the model to be a "giant" in memory but a "dwarf" in computation.**

### 4.2 Superiority of Sparse Decoder-Only Models for In-Context Learning
While Mixture-of-Experts architectures existed prior to GLaM (e.g., Switch-C, GShard), their application was largely confined to encoder-decoder setups optimized for **fine-tuning** on specific downstream tasks.

*   **The Gap:** Previous trillion-parameter sparse models, such as Switch-C (1.5T parameters), were evaluated primarily on fine-tuning benchmarks like SuperGLUE. It remained an open question whether sparsity would degrade the **emergent abilities** seen in dense decoder-only models, specifically **few-shot in-context learning** (the ability to learn a task from a prompt without weight updates). Skeptics argued that the dynamic routing of MoE might disrupt the coherent context tracking required for few-shot generalization.
*   **The Innovation:** GLaM is the first work to successfully scale a **decoder-only** MoE architecture to the trillion-parameter regime and prove its dominance in few-shot settings.
*   **Evidence of Superiority:** As shown in **Figure 1** and **Table 1**, GLaM (64B/64E) outperforms GPT-3 across **29 NLP tasks** in zero, one, and few-shot settings. Notably, on challenging open-domain QA tasks like TriviaQA, GLaM's one-shot score (75.8%) exceeds GPT-3's 64-shot score (71.2%) despite using half the compute.
*   **Why It Matters:** This validates that sparsity is not just a compression technique but a **scaling accelerator** for generalist models. It proves that the "conditional computation" of MoE does not hinder—and actually enhances—the model's ability to generalize from context, closing the gap between sparse efficiency and dense capability.

### 4.3 Data Quality as a Critical Bottleneck for Sparse Scaling
A subtle but critical insight from this work is that **data quality becomes increasingly paramount as model sparsity increases**. While dense models can sometimes "average out" noise through massive parameter redundancy, sparse models rely on precise routing, making them more sensitive to input distribution shifts.

*   **The Finding:** The authors conducted a controlled ablation study (Section 6.2) comparing GLaM trained on their curated, high-quality filtered dataset versus an unfiltered dataset containing ~7 trillion tokens of raw web data.
*   **The Result:** Counter-intuitively, the model trained on the **smaller, filtered dataset** (1.6T tokens) significantly outperformed the model trained on the larger, unfiltered corpus. **Figure 3(c-d)** illustrates that the performance gap is particularly wide for **Natural Language Generation (NLG)** tasks.
*   **The Mechanism:** The authors hypothesize that NLG tasks require the generation of coherent, high-quality sequences. If the routing network (the gating function) learns from noisy, low-quality data, it may fail to select the appropriate experts for generating fluent text. In contrast, dense models might tolerate noise better due to their holistic activation.
*   **Significance:** This challenges the "data is all you need" quantity-focused narrative. For sparse architectures, **curating a high-signal dataset is more effective than simply increasing data volume**. This insight shifts the engineering focus from building larger clusters to building better data filters when scaling MoE models.

### 4.4 Closing the Bias Gap via Sparsity
Perhaps the most surprising social insight is that large, sparsely activated models may inherently exhibit **reduced stereotypical biases** compared to their dense counterparts, challenging the notion that larger models always amplify societal biases found in training data.

*   **The Observation:** On the **WinoGender** benchmark, which tests for gender bias in coreference resolution (e.g., associating "doctor" with "he" and "nurse" with "she"), GLaM achieved a new state-of-the-art score of **71.7%**.
*   **The Distinction:** Crucially, GLaM's performance was nearly identical for **stereotypical** examples (71.7%) and **anti-stereotypical** ("gotcha") examples (71.7%). In contrast, many dense models show a significant performance drop on anti-stereotypical cases, revealing a reliance on superficial statistical correlations (e.g., "men are doctors").
*   **The Hypothesis:** The authors suggest that because MoE models have vastly higher capacity (1.2T parameters) but process inputs through specialized sub-networks, they may rely less on broad, superficial statistical shortcuts and more on nuanced, context-specific features stored in different experts.
*   **Significance:** This suggests that **sparsity could be a pathway to more robust and fair AI**. If sparse activation forces the model to engage specific "expert" knowledge rather than relying on global bias trends, it may naturally mitigate some forms of representation bias without requiring explicit debiasing algorithms. This positions MoE not just as an efficiency tool, but as a potential architectural solution to alignment challenges.

### 4.5 Energy Efficiency as a Scaling Enabler
Finally, GLaM reframes energy consumption from an unavoidable side-effect of scaling to a solvable engineering constraint.

*   **The Metric:** Training the largest GLaM model consumed **456 MWh**, which is roughly **one-third** of the energy required to train GPT-3 (1,287 MWh). Even more strikingly, to reach comparable performance levels, GLaM required only **213 MWh** (1/6th of GPT-3's cost).
*   **The Implication:** This efficiency gain is not marginal; it is existential for the field. As noted in Section 6.4, continuing to scale dense models would soon hit environmental and economic limits that few organizations could sustain.
*   **Innovation:** By combining the MoE architecture with TPU-v4 hardware optimizations and the GSPMD software stack, GLaM demonstrates that **sustainable scaling is possible**. This shifts the narrative from "AI is becoming too expensive/dirty to scale" to "sparsity is the key to sustainable AGI development."

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section dissects the empirical evidence provided in the paper to validate GLaM's claims. We move beyond the headline numbers to examine *how* the evaluation was constructed, *where* the performance gains originate, and *under what conditions* the sparse architecture succeeds or struggles. The analysis focuses on the rigorous comparison against dense baselines, the scaling laws of sparsity, and the critical role of data curation.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: Ensuring an "Apples-to-Apples" Comparison

To convincingly argue that sparsity offers advantages over density, the authors designed an evaluation protocol that strictly isolates architectural differences from confounding variables like data contamination or task-specific tuning.

**Datasets and Task Categories**
The evaluation suite comprises **29 public NLP benchmarks**, carefully selected to match those used in the seminal GPT-3 paper (Brown et al., 2020), with specific exclusions to ensure fairness.
*   **Inclusions:** The suite covers 7 distinct categories:
    1.  **Open-Domain Question Answering:** TriviaQA, Natural Questions (NQS), Web Questions (WebQS).
    2.  **Cloze and Completion:** LAMBADA, HellaSwag, StoryCloze.
    3.  **Winograd-Style Tasks:** Winograd, WinoGrande.
    4.  **Common Sense Reasoning:** PIQA, ARC (Easy/Challenge), OpenBookQA.
    5.  **In-context Reading Comprehension:** DROP, CoQA, QuAC, SQuADv2, RACE.
    6.  **SuperGLUE:** A sticky benchmark including BoolQ, COPA, RTE, WiC, etc.
    7.  **Natural Language Inference:** ANLI (R1, R2, R3).
*   **Exclusions:** The authors explicitly excluded 7 synthetic tasks (arithmetic, word unscrambling) and 6 machine translation datasets. This ensures the comparison focuses on *generalist* language understanding and generation capabilities rather than specialized algorithmic skills or translation metrics where encoder-decoder models (like Switch-C) might have an inherent structural advantage.

**Metrics and Protocols**
The evaluation strictly adheres to **in-context learning** protocols, meaning no gradient updates (fine-tuning) are performed on the test tasks.
*   **Zero-shot:** The model receives the task description or query directly.
*   **One-shot/Few-shot:** The prompt includes 1 or $k$ random examples from the task's training set, separated by newlines.
*   **Scoring:**
    *   **Generative Tasks (NLG):** Performance is measured by **Exact Match (EM)** accuracy and **F1 score**. Decoding uses beam search with a width of 4.
    *   **Understanding Tasks (NLU):** For multiple-choice tasks, the model selects the option with the highest log-likelihood, normalized by the token length of the option to prevent bias toward shorter answers.
    *   **Normalization:** All scores (Accuracy and F1) are normalized to a 0–100 scale for averaging.

**Baselines and Controls**
The primary baseline is **GPT-3 (175B)**, the state-of-the-art dense decoder-only model at the time. To provide a broader context, the authors also reference results from **Gopher (280B)** and **Megatron-NLG (530B)**, though these are noted as significantly more computationally expensive.
*   **Internal Controls:** The paper trains a family of GLaM variants ranging from 130M to 1.2T parameters, including both **MoE** variants (e.g., `GLaM (8B/64E)`) and **Dense** variants (e.g., `GLaM (8B)`) with comparable *activated* parameter counts. This internal control group is crucial for disentangling the benefits of *scale* from the benefits of *sparsity*.

**Data Contamination Check**
A common critique of large language model evaluations is that test data may have leaked into the training corpus, artificially inflating scores. The authors conducted a rigorous overlap analysis (detailed in **Appendix D, Table 6**).
*   **Method:** They checked for $n$-gram collisions between the 1.6T token training set and the evaluation datasets.
*   **Result:** The percentage of "dirty" (overlapping) examples was found to be low and comparable to GPT-3's reported contamination rates. For instance, only **3.8%** of ANLI R1 and **1.84%** of DROP validation examples overlapped. This confirms that the performance gains are due to genuine generalization, not memorization of test sets.

### 5.2 Quantitative Results: Performance vs. Efficiency

The core claim of GLaM is that it achieves superior performance with drastically reduced computational cost. The data strongly supports this, revealing a consistent advantage across almost all metrics.

**Headline Performance: GLaM vs. GPT-3**
As summarized in **Table 1** and visualized in **Figure 1**, the largest GLaM model (`64B/64E`, 1.2T total parameters) outperforms GPT-3 (175B) across the board while using significantly less compute.

| Metric | GPT-3 (175B) | GLaM (1.2T) | Relative Improvement |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Avg Zero-shot Accuracy** | 56.9% | **62.7%** | **+10.2%** |
| **Avg One-shot Accuracy** | 61.6% | **65.5%** | **+6.3%** |
| **Avg Few-shot Accuracy** | 65.2% | **68.1%** | **+4.4%** |
| **Inference FLOPs/token** | 350 G | **180 G** | **-48.6%** |
| **Training Energy** | 1,287 MWh | **456 MWh** | **-64.6%** |

*   **Interpretation:** GLaM does not just match GPT-3; it surpasses it by double-digit percentages in zero-shot settings while consuming less than half the energy per token during inference. This validates the hypothesis that increased *total* capacity (1.2T vs 175B) improves reasoning, even if the *active* capacity per step is similar or slightly lower.

**Deep Dive: Open-Domain Question Answering**
The most striking gains appear in knowledge-intensive tasks, where model capacity is paramount. **Table 5** highlights results on **TriviaQA**, a challenging open-domain QA benchmark.
*   **GLaM One-shot:** Achieves **75.8%** accuracy on the dev set and **75.0%** on the test server.
*   **GPT-3 Comparison:** This exceeds GPT-3's one-shot score (68.0%) by **7.8 points** and even beats GPT-3's **64-shot** score (71.2%) by **3.8 points**.
*   **Significance:** GLaM accomplishes with a single example what requires 64 examples for GPT-3. Furthermore, it outperforms previous fine-tuned state-of-the-art models that utilized external knowledge graphs (e.g., KG-FiD at 69.8%), demonstrating that the sheer parameter count of the MoE architecture effectively internalizes world knowledge.

**Scaling Laws: MoE vs. Dense**
**Figure 3(a-b)** presents the scaling behavior of GLaM MoE models versus GLaM Dense models when plotted against **effective FLOPs per token** (determined by activated parameters, $n_{act-params}$).
*   **Small Scale:** At lower FLOP budgets (e.g., < 10 GFLOPs), MoE and Dense models perform similarly.
*   **Large Scale:** As the compute budget increases, the MoE curves diverge sharply upward, outperforming dense counterparts.
*   **Key Insight:** For a fixed computational budget, increasing the number of experts (and thus total parameters) yields better performance than simply making the dense layers wider. **Figure 6 (Appendix B)** reinforces this: for a fixed model size (1.7B activated params), increasing the number of experts from 1 to 256 consistently improves zero, one, and few-shot scores. This confirms that **sparsity provides a more efficient path to scaling performance than density.**

**Data Efficiency**
**Figure 4** illustrates learning curves against the number of training tokens processed.
*   GLaM MoE models reach the performance level of dense models using significantly fewer tokens.
*   Specifically, **GLaM (64B/64E)** trained on only **280B tokens** outperforms **GPT-3** (trained on ~300B tokens) in 4 out of 6 learning settings (Zero/One-shot NLU and One/Few-shot NLG).
*   This suggests that the specialized experts in the MoE architecture learn faster and more efficiently from data than the monolithic layers of a dense model.

### 5.3 Critical Ablation: The Necessity of Data Filtering

One of the most nuanced findings in the paper is the sensitivity of sparse models to data quality. Section 6.2 details an ablation study comparing GLaM trained on the curated **filtered dataset** (1.6T tokens) versus an **unfiltered dataset** (~7T tokens of raw web data).

*   **Result:** Contrary to the "more data is always better" heuristic, the model trained on the *smaller, filtered* dataset performed significantly better.
*   **Magnitude:** As shown in **Figure 3(c-d)**, the performance gap is substantial. For Natural Language Generation (NLG) tasks, the filtered model scores roughly **5-10 points higher** than the unfiltered variant across zero, one, and few-shot settings.
*   **Analysis:** The authors posit that NLG tasks require high-quality syntactic and semantic patterns. Noisy, low-quality web data confuses the gating mechanism, preventing experts from specializing effectively in high-quality generation.
*   **Conclusion:** For MoE architectures, **data quality is a stricter bottleneck than quantity**. Scaling sparse models requires rigorous curation; simply dumping more raw data onto a larger model can degrade performance.

### 5.4 Robustness, Bias, and Failure Modes

The paper goes beyond accuracy metrics to evaluate the social implications and robustness of the architecture.

**Bias Mitigation via Sparsity**
A surprising finding is that GLaM exhibits reduced stereotypical bias compared to dense models.
*   **WinoGender Benchmark:** This task tests coreference resolution with gendered pronouns in stereotypical (e.g., "The doctor... he") and anti-stereotypical (e.g., "The nurse... he") contexts.
*   **Performance:** GLaM achieves **71.7%** overall accuracy. Crucially, the split between stereotypical and anti-stereotypical examples is identical (**71.7% vs 71.7%**).
*   **Comparison:** Dense models typically show a "bias gap," performing well on stereotypical cases but failing on anti-stereotypical ones. GLaM's parity suggests that the high-capacity, specialized nature of MoE allows it to rely less on superficial statistical correlations (biases) and more on contextual reasoning.

**Toxicity Degeneration**
Using the **RealToxicityPrompts** dataset, the authors analyzed whether the model generates toxic continuations given toxic or non-toxic prompts.
*   **Findings (**Figure 5**):** The model's toxicity probability closely tracks the prompt's toxicity. While this indicates the model is highly responsive to context (a desired trait for few-shot learning), it also means it can be easily prompted into generating toxic content if the input is adversarial.
*   **Scale Effect:** Interestingly, toxicity levels remained relatively constant across model scales (from 1B to >100B), suggesting that simply scaling up via MoE does not inherently exacerbate toxicity degeneration, unlike some trends observed in dense scaling.

**Limitations and Trade-offs**
Despite the successes, the experiments reveal specific trade-offs:
1.  **Serving Cost vs. Training Cost:** While *training* and *inference FLOPs* are reduced, the **memory footprint** is massive. Storing 1.2 trillion parameters requires significantly more hardware memory (TPU HBM) than a 175B dense model. This increases the *capital cost* of serving (number of chips required to hold the model), even if the *operational cost* (energy per token) is lower. This is particularly challenging for low-traffic applications where the model cannot be fully utilized.
2.  **Stability Challenges:** The training process for trillion-parameter MoE models is fragile. The authors note the necessity of skipping batches with NaN/Inf gradients and restarting from checkpoints upon instability. This implies that training MoE models requires more sophisticated engineering oversight than dense models.
3.  **Task Variance:** While GLaM wins on average, it does not win every single task. For example, in **Table 11**, GPT-3 slightly edges out GLaM on **Winograd** (88.3% vs 87.2% in zero-shot) and **HellaSwag** (78.9% vs 76.6%). This suggests that for certain types of common-sense reasoning tasks that rely heavily on local context rather than global knowledge retrieval, the dense architecture may still hold a slight edge, or the specific hyperparameters for those tasks were not yet fully optimized for the MoE variant.

### 5.5 Assessment of Claims

Do the experiments support the paper's claims? **Yes, convincingly.**

*   **Claim:** *Sparse models can scale to trillion parameters with better efficiency.*
    *   **Evidence:** The direct measurement of 456 MWh training energy vs. 1,287 MWh for GPT-3, coupled with the 48.6% reduction in inference FLOPs, provides irrefutable quantitative proof.
*   **Claim:** *MoE models outperform dense models in few-shot learning.*
    *   **Evidence:** The consistent victory across 29 benchmarks, particularly the massive lead in TriviaQA and the superior scaling curves in Figure 3, validates this. The internal control (Dense vs. MoE GLaM variants) rules out data differences as the sole cause.
*   **Claim:** *Data quality is critical for sparse scaling.*
    *   **Evidence:** The ablation study in Section 6.2 clearly demonstrates that unfiltered data degrades performance, supporting the claim that sparsity amplifies the need for clean data.

The experiments are robust, utilizing standard benchmarks, controlling for contamination, and providing both aggregate and granular task-level analysis. The only caveat is the increased hardware memory requirement for serving, which is a practical constraint rather than a failure of the core hypothesis. Overall, the paper successfully establishes Mixture-of-Experts as a viable and superior alternative to dense scaling for future large language models.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While GLaM demonstrates that sparsity is a superior path for scaling model capacity and training efficiency, the paper explicitly acknowledges that this architecture introduces a distinct set of trade-offs. The benefits of reduced FLOPs and energy consumption do not come without costs in terms of hardware memory requirements, serving economics, and training stability. Understanding these limitations is crucial for determining where MoE architectures are appropriate and where dense models may still hold an advantage.

### 6.1 The Memory Wall: Decoupling Compute from Capacity
The most significant architectural trade-off identified in GLaM is the divergence between **computational cost** (FLOPs) and **memory footprint**.

*   **The Mechanism:** In a dense model like GPT-3, the memory required to store the model weights is directly proportional to the compute required to run it. If you can afford the compute to run the model, you inherently have the memory to store it.
*   **The GLaM Constraint:** GLaM breaks this coupling. While it activates only 96.6 billion parameters per token (low compute), it must **store all 1.2 trillion parameters** in high-bandwidth memory (HBM) across the device cluster to allow the gating function to access any expert at any time.
*   **Hardware Implication:** As noted in **Section 8 (Discussion)**, "the sparsely activated models consist of a higher number of parameters and thus require a larger number of devices."
    *   To serve GLaM, one must provision enough TPU v4 chips to hold the entire 1.2T parameter weight set, even if the workload is light.
    *   In contrast, a dense model with equivalent *active* compute (e.g., a ~100B parameter dense model) would fit on significantly fewer chips.
*   **The Economic Consequence:** This creates a high fixed cost for deployment. For applications with **low serving traffic**, the cost of idling the massive array of chips required to hold the model weights may outweigh the savings gained from lower energy consumption per token. The paper admits this "limits the resource accessibility," making GLaM less viable for small-scale deployments or edge devices compared to smaller, dense models.

### 6.2 Training Instability and Engineering Complexity
Training a trillion-parameter sparse model is empirically more fragile than training a dense counterpart. The paper details several "implementation tricks" in **Section 5.2** that were necessary to prevent the model from diverging, suggesting that MoE scaling is not yet a "plug-and-play" solution.

*   **Gradient Instability:** The authors report that NaNs (Not a Number) and Infs (Infinity) in gradients occur more frequently or catastrophically in MoE training. To mitigate this, they implemented a **gradient skipping** mechanism: "We skip weight updates for a batch if there are any NaNs or Infs in the gradients."
*   **Checkpoint Restarting:** In cases where instability persists despite skipping, the training pipeline must "restart from an early healthy checkpoint." The authors hypothesize that the randomness of re-loading sequential batches helps the model escape the specific state that caused the numerical explosion.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper notes, "There is little room for hyperparameter tuning" due to the extreme cost of training. This implies that the chosen hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate schedule, auxiliary loss coefficient of 0.01) are likely sub-optimal but represent the "safe" region where the model converges. A dense model of comparable *active* size might be more robust to hyperparameter variations, allowing for further optimization that was too risky to attempt with GLaM.

### 6.3 Data Quality as a Strict Bottleneck
While **Section 4.3** highlighted data quality as an insight, it also represents a limitation in terms of operational complexity.

*   **Sensitivity to Noise:** The ablation study in **Section 6.2** and **Figure 3(c-d)** reveals that MoE models are *more* sensitive to low-quality data than dense models. When trained on unfiltered web data (7T tokens), the GLaM variant performed significantly worse than when trained on the filtered dataset (1.6T tokens).
*   **The Limitation:** This suggests that scaling MoE models requires a sophisticated, upfront investment in data curation pipelines (classifiers, filtering heuristics) that dense models might tolerate less strictly. Organizations lacking the expertise or resources to build high-quality filtered corpora may find that scaling via sparsity yields diminishing returns or even performance degradation, whereas simply adding more raw data to a dense model might still provide marginal gains.

### 6.4 Task-Specific Performance Variance
Although GLaM outperforms GPT-3 on **average** across 29 tasks, it does not dominate every single benchmark. A granular look at **Table 11** reveals specific edge cases where the dense architecture retains an advantage.

*   **Winograd Schema Challenge:** In the zero-shot setting, GPT-3 achieves **88.3%** accuracy, while GLaM scores **87.2%**.
*   **HellaSwag:** GPT-3 scores **78.9%**, while GLaM scores **76.6%** (zero-shot).
*   **Analysis:** These tasks (Winograd and HellaSwag) rely heavily on fine-grained common sense reasoning and local context coherence rather than the retrieval of vast factual knowledge (which favors high-capacity MoE).
*   **Hypothesis:** The dynamic routing in MoE might occasionally disrupt the smooth flow of contextual information required for these specific types of reasoning, or the "specialization" of experts might lead to fragmentation where no single expert possesses the specific combination of features needed for these nuanced tasks. This indicates that sparsity is not a universal panacea; for certain reasoning-heavy, knowledge-light tasks, dense connectivity may still be superior.

### 6.5 Unaddressed Scenarios and Open Questions
The paper leaves several important scenarios unexplored, representing open questions for future research:

*   **Dynamic Batch Sizes and Latency:** The evaluation focuses on throughput (FLOPs/token) and energy. It does not deeply address **latency** under variable load. Because MoE requires all-to-all communication (routing tokens to specific experts on specific devices), the communication overhead can become a bottleneck if the batch size is small or if the load balancing is imperfect. The paper assumes large batch sizes (up to 1M tokens) which amortize this cost, but real-time interactive applications with low concurrency might suffer from higher latency than a dense model.
*   **Fine-Tuning Efficiency:** The paper explicitly focuses on **in-context learning** (zero/few-shot). It mentions in **Section 2** that Switch-C was evaluated on fine-tuning, but GLaM was not. It remains an open question whether the standard fine-tuning paradigm (updating all weights) is efficient or effective for a 1.2T parameter MoE model. Fine-tuning such a massive parameter set could reintroduce the computational costs that MoE seeks to avoid, or require specialized fine-tuning methods (like adapter layers) that were not tested here.
*   **Long-Context Scaling:** All experiments were conducted with a maximum sequence length of **1,024 tokens**. The interaction between MoE routing and very long contexts (e.g., 32k+ tokens) is untested. It is unclear if the "expert specialization" holds up over long dependencies or if the gating function becomes unstable when processing sequences significantly longer than those seen during pre-training.

### 6.6 Summary of Trade-offs

| Feature | Dense Models (e.g., GPT-3) | GLaM (MoE) | Trade-off Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Parameter Count** | Low (175B) | Massive (1.2T) | GLaM has higher knowledge capacity. |
| **Active Params/Token** | High (175B) | Low (96.6B) | GLaM is faster and more energy-efficient per token. |
| **Memory Footprint** | Moderate | Massive | GLaM requires significantly more hardware (GPUs/TPUs) to *host* the model, increasing capital cost. |
| **Training Stability** | Robust | Fragile | GLaM requires complex engineering (gradient skipping, restarts) to train successfully. |
| **Data Sensitivity** | Moderate | High | GLaM degrades significantly with unfiltered data; requires rigorous curation. |
| **Reasoning Tasks** | Strong | Variable | GLaM lags slightly on specific common-sense tasks (Winograd, HellaSwag). |

In conclusion, while GLaM successfully demonstrates that sparsity is the key to sustainable scaling of model capacity, it shifts the bottleneck from **compute/energy** to **memory/engineering complexity**. It is an architecture optimized for large-scale, high-throughput environments where the fixed cost of hosting the model can be amortized over massive usage, rather than a universal replacement for dense models in all settings.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of GLaM represents a paradigm shift in how the field approaches the scaling of artificial intelligence. By successfully decoupling model capacity from computational cost, this work moves the community away from the "brute force" era of dense scaling toward an era of **architectural efficiency**. The implications extend beyond mere energy savings; they redefine the economic feasibility of trillion-parameter models, suggest new avenues for mitigating bias, and open a frontier of research into conditional computation.

### 7.1 Reshaping the Scaling Landscape: From Dense to Sparse
Prior to GLaM, the dominant narrative—driven by the success of GPT-3 and the "Scaling Laws" of Kaplan et al. (2020)—was that performance gains were strictly a function of increasing dense parameters, data, and compute in lockstep. This created a trajectory where future models would quickly become environmentally unsustainable and economically exclusive to a handful of entities.

GLaM fundamentally alters this trajectory by demonstrating that **sparsity is a viable, and indeed superior, scaling strategy** for generalist language models.
*   **Breaking the Linear Cost Curve:** The paper proves that one can increase model capacity by an order of magnitude (from 175B to 1.2T) while *reducing* inference costs by nearly 50% and training energy by 65%. This breaks the linear relationship between intelligence and energy consumption.
*   **Democratization of Scale:** By lowering the operational cost (OpEx) of running massive models, MoE architectures make it feasible for more organizations to deploy state-of-the-art AI, provided they can manage the upfront capital cost (CapEx) of the required memory hardware.
*   **Validation of Decoder-Only Sparsity:** Before GLaM, trillion-parameter successes like Switch-C were confined to encoder-decoder architectures optimized for fine-tuning. GLaM validates that **decoder-only sparse models** can master **in-context learning**, the critical capability for flexible, prompt-based AI applications. This closes the loop, proving that sparsity works for the exact architecture used in modern chatbots and coding assistants.

### 7.2 Enabling Follow-Up Research Directions
GLaM opens several specific avenues for future investigation, moving the research focus from "how big can we go?" to "how smartly can we route?"

*   **Dynamic Expert Specialization and Interpretability:**
    With 64 experts per layer and combinatorial routing, GLaM effectively creates thousands of unique sub-networks. A rich area for future work is analyzing **what** these experts learn. Do specific experts specialize in syntax, factual retrieval, code generation, or reasoning? Understanding the emergent specialization could lead to **interpretable AI**, where we can trace a model's decision to a specific subset of experts, potentially allowing for targeted editing or debiasing without retraining the whole model.

*   **Advanced Load Balancing and Routing Algorithms:**
    The paper relies on a standard top-$k$ gating mechanism with an auxiliary loss to ensure load balancing. However, this is a relatively simple heuristic. Future research could explore:
    *   **Context-aware routing:** Gating functions that consider the entire sequence history rather than just the current token.
    *   **Differentiable architecture search:** Automatically learning the optimal number of experts ($E$) and the number of activated experts ($k$) per layer, rather than fixing them manually.
    *   **Drop-free routing:** Investigating if the routing mechanism itself provides enough regularization to eliminate the need for other stability tricks, or if new forms of dropout specific to MoE are needed.

*   **Hybrid Fine-Tuning Strategies:**
    GLaM focuses on in-context learning. A critical open question is how to efficiently **fine-tune** trillion-parameter MoE models. Standard fine-tuning (updating all 1.2T parameters) is prohibitively expensive. Future work will likely focus on **Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)** methods adapted for MoE, such as:
    *   Tuning only the gating networks while freezing experts.
    *   Adding lightweight adapter layers to specific experts.
    *   Selectively updating only the top-$k$ most relevant experts for a specific downstream task.

*   **Long-Context MoE Scaling:**
    GLaM was trained with a context window of 1,024 tokens. As applications demand windows of 32k, 100k, or more, the interaction between MoE routing and long-range dependencies needs exploration. Does expert specialization degrade over long sequences? Can MoE models maintain coherence better than dense models by dedicating specific experts to long-term memory retention?

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases
The unique efficiency profile of GLaM makes it particularly well-suited for specific high-value applications where dense models struggle with cost or latency.

*   **High-Throughput Generative Services:**
    For applications requiring massive scale, such as real-time translation for billions of users, large-scale content generation, or coding assistants (e.g., GitHub Copoleot style), the **48.6% reduction in inference FLOPs** translates directly to lower cloud bills and reduced latency. The ability to serve a 1.2T parameter model with the compute cost of a ~100B dense model is a game-changer for commercial viability.

*   **Knowledge-Intensive Reasoning:**
    The superior performance on **Open-Domain QA** (e.g., TriviaQA) suggests GLaM is ideal for applications requiring deep factual knowledge, such as medical diagnosis support, legal research, or complex scientific question answering. The massive parameter count allows the model to "memorize" vast amounts of world knowledge, while the sparse activation ensures it retrieves the right facts efficiently without processing irrelevant data.

*   **Sustainable AI Deployment:**
    For organizations with strict carbon footprint goals, GLaM offers a path to state-of-the-art performance with **1/3 the training energy** of comparable dense models. This is critical for government projects, academic institutions, and corporations aiming for "Green AI" certifications.

*   **Bias-Mitigated Decision Systems:**
    Given GLaM's demonstrated ability to close the performance gap between stereotypical and anti-stereotypical examples on the **WinoGender** benchmark, it is a strong candidate for sensitive applications like hiring screening, loan approval reasoning, or healthcare triage, where reliance on superficial statistical biases in dense models poses significant ethical risks.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance
For practitioners and researchers looking to adopt or build upon GLaM, the following guidance outlines when to prefer this approach and what constraints to anticipate.

**When to Prefer GLaM (MoE) over Dense Models:**
*   **Scenario A: High-Volume Inference.** If your application serves millions of requests per day, the reduced FLOPs per token will yield massive operational savings, justifying the higher hardware memory requirements.
*   **Scenario B: Knowledge-Retrieval Tasks.** If your use case involves answering complex questions or generating content requiring broad world knowledge, the 1.2T parameter capacity of GLaM will outperform dense models of similar compute budget.
*   **Scenario C: Sustainable Training.** If you have access to a large cluster (e.g., 1,000+ TPUs/GPUs) but are constrained by energy budgets or training time windows, MoE allows you to train a larger effective model faster.

**When to Stick with Dense Models:**
*   **Scenario A: Low-Traffic / Edge Deployment.** If you cannot amortize the cost of hosting a massive model across many requests, the high memory footprint (needing to store 1.2T params) makes MoE inefficient. A smaller dense model that fits on fewer chips is more cost-effective.
*   **Scenario B: Latency-Sensitive Interactive Apps with Small Batches.** MoE relies on batching to amortize the communication overhead of routing tokens to different devices. If your application requires single-request, ultra-low-latency responses (e.g., real-time voice interaction with <50ms budget), the all-to-all communication in MoE might introduce unacceptable jitter compared to a dense model.
*   **Scenario C: Limited Data Curation Resources.** As shown in **Section 6.2**, MoE models are highly sensitive to data quality. If you cannot invest in rigorous data filtering and cleaning pipelines, a dense model may be more robust to noisy data.

**Integration Considerations:**
*   **Hardware Requirements:** Implementing GLaM requires a distributed system capable of **2D sharding** (partitioning both model and data dimensions). You cannot simply load this model onto a single GPU. Frameworks like **GSPMD** (used in the paper) or modern equivalents in PyTorch (e.g., FSDP with custom MoE layers) are essential.
*   **Stability Monitoring:** When training or fine-tuning MoE models, implement **gradient skipping** and **checkpoint restarting** mechanisms from day one. Do not assume the stability of dense training; monitor for NaNs/Infs aggressively.
*   **Load Balancing:** Always include the **auxiliary load-balancing loss** during training. Without it, the gating function will likely collapse to using only a few experts, destroying the benefits of sparsity and leading to poor performance.

In summary, GLaM signals the end of the "dense-only" era for frontier models. While it introduces new engineering complexities regarding memory and stability, its promise of sustainable, high-capacity intelligence makes it the foundational architecture for the next generation of large language models.
