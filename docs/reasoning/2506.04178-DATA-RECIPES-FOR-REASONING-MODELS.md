## 1. Executive Summary

This paper addresses the opacity of proprietary reasoning model training by introducing the OpenThoughts project, which systematically optimizes data curation pipelines through over 1,000 controlled experiments to produce the `OpenThoughts3-1.2M` dataset and `OpenThinker3-7B` model. The resulting `OpenThinker3-7B` achieves state-of-the-art performance for open-data 7B models, surpassing `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B` by 15.3 to 20.5 percentage points across benchmarks like AIME 2025 (53%) and GPQA Diamond (54%). These findings demystify supervised fine-tuning data recipes, revealing counterintuitive insights such as stronger teacher models not always yielding better student performance.

## 2. Context and Motivation

### The Closed-Door Problem in Reasoning Model Development

The rapid advancement of reasoning-capable language models has created a paradox: while models like DeepSeek-R1 and OpenAI's o3 demonstrate remarkable performance on math, code, and science benchmarks, their complete training recipes remain proprietary. This opacity creates a significant barrier for the research community. As stated in the Introduction, "the complete recipes for frontier reasoning models are not public, making research for building reasoning models difficult" (Section 1). Researchers attempting to replicate or improve upon these systems must rely on heuristics and intuitions rather than systematic evidence, leading to inefficient exploration of the design space.

The core problem this paper addresses is the **lack of systematic understanding** about what makes supervised fine-tuning (SFT) data effective for reasoning capabilities. While the community knows that data quality matters, the specific design choices—question sourcing, filtering strategies, teacher model selection, deduplication methods, and answer verification—have not been rigorously ablated at scale. This knowledge gap forces researchers to make expensive guesses about data curation, with each failed experiment costing thousands of GPU hours and significant API expenses for teacher model inference.

### Why This Problem Matters

The importance of solving this problem extends across both practical and theoretical dimensions:

**Practical Impact:** Reasoning capabilities are increasingly critical for real-world applications—from scientific discovery and code generation to complex decision-making systems. If only well-resourced organizations can afford the experimentation needed to build capable reasoning models, the field risks centralization around a few proprietary systems. Open, reproducible recipes democratize access to advanced AI capabilities, enabling broader participation in AI development and reducing dependency on closed APIs.

**Theoretical Significance:** Understanding *why* certain data curation choices work challenges fundamental assumptions about training data quality. For instance, conventional wisdom suggests that stronger teacher models should produce better student models through distillation. This paper's finding that `QwQ-32B` outperforms `DeepSeek-R1` as a teacher—despite `DeepSeek-R1` scoring higher on target benchmarks (Section 4.6)—directly contradicts this intuition. Such counterintuitive results reveal gaps in our theoretical understanding of knowledge transfer during distillation.

**Resource Efficiency:** The paper notes that exploring the design space is "prohibitively expensive for many researchers due to the high costs of teacher inference and model training" (Section 1). By conducting over 1,000 controlled experiments and publishing the results, this work provides a reference that saves the community from redundant exploration. Each ablation experiment at the 31,600 sample scale represents meaningful compute investment; aggregating these insights prevents wasteful duplication of effort across research groups.

### Prior Approaches and Their Limitations

The landscape of reasoning model development before this work falls into several categories, each with notable shortcomings:

**Distillation from Proprietary Systems:** Following the release of DeepSeek-R1, many efforts attempted to replicate its performance through distillation. The R1-Distill models demonstrated that SFT on curated question-thinking tokens-answer triplets could achieve strong performance (51% on AIME, 33% on GPQA) without reinforcement learning (Section 1). However, these efforts typically relied on a single teacher model (DeepSeek-R1) and did not systematically explore alternative teachers or data generation strategies.

**Limited Design Space Exploration:** Projects like SkyT1 (NovaSky-Team, 2025b) and S1 (Muennighoff et al., 2025) achieved improvements by focusing on training datasets, yet they "explore only a limited fraction of possible design choices" (Section 1). Common limitations include:
- Relying exclusively on human-written questions from specific sources
- Using a single teacher model throughout the pipeline
- Applying filtering heuristics without ablation studies to validate their effectiveness
- Scaling dataset size without understanding which pipeline components drive improvements

**Small-Scale Curation:** Efforts like S1 and LIMO (Ye et al., 2025) emphasize manual curation of small datasets (~1K examples) composed of challenging, high-quality prompts. While these demonstrate that quality matters, they do not address how to scale curation strategies to the hundreds of thousands or millions of examples needed for competitive performance. The OpenThoughts project shows that `OpenThinker3-7B` trained on 1.2M examples significantly outperforms smaller curated datasets (Figure 1), suggesting scale remains important even with high-quality curation.

**Multi-Stage Innovation Without Isolation:** Many reasoning projects (e.g., DeepMath-103K, OpenR1, Nvidia Nemotron) "introduce innovations across multiple stages, such as data sourcing, filtering, and scaling" (Section 2). When multiple changes are made simultaneously, it becomes impossible to attribute performance gains to specific design choices. This confounds the community's understanding of which components are actually driving improvements.

**Reinforcement Learning Focus:** Some works like AceReason (Chen et al., 2025) and Skywork-OR1 (He et al., 2025a) build reasoning datasets specifically for reinforcement learning. While RL is an important training regime, this paper focuses on SFT because it represents a more accessible entry point for the community and because R1-Distill models showed SFT alone can achieve competitive performance. Understanding SFT data curation is a prerequisite for effective RL dataset construction.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

The OpenThoughts project positions itself as a **systematic empirical investigation** rather than a single-method innovation. Key differentiators include:

**Comprehensive Ablation Framework:** Unlike prior work that makes design choices based on intuition or limited experimentation, this paper conducts "more than 1,000 ablation experiments across three data domains (math, code, and science)" (Section 1). Each pipeline step—question sourcing, mixing, filtering, deduplication, answer filtering, and teacher selection—is ablated independently while holding other factors constant. This controlled approach isolates the effect of each design choice on downstream performance.

**Iterative Dataset Generations:** The project evolved through four generations (BespokeStratos-17K → OpenThoughts-114K → OpenThoughts2-1M → OpenThoughts3-1.2M), with each iteration incorporating insights from previous experiments (Section 3, Table 2). This iterative refinement demonstrates how systematic experimentation compounds improvements over time, with `OpenThinker3-7B` achieving 69.0% on AIME24 compared to 14.3% for the initial `Bespoke-Stratos-7B`.

**Challenge to Preexisting Notions:** The paper explicitly aims to "challenge preexisting notions of data quality" (Section 1). Five key insights emerge from the experiments that contradict common assumptions:
1. Sampling multiple answers per question (16×) is more effective than increasing question diversity
2. Better-performing models are not necessarily better teachers for distillation
3. Verification and answer filtering provide negligible benefits compared to using all samples
4. Selecting from top 1-2 high-quality sources outperforms optimizing for diversity across 8-16 sources
5. LLM-based filtering (difficulty, response length) outperforms classical methods (fastText, embeddings)

**Open Artifacts:** Perhaps most significantly, the project releases all datasets, models, and code at openthoughts.ai (Section 3). This transparency contrasts sharply with proprietary reasoning model development and enables the community to build upon verified findings rather than speculating about effective techniques.

**Focus on Accessible Scale:** The experiments are conducted at a scale "small enough to be cost-effective yet large enough to provide a meaningful signal" (Section 4)—specifically 31,600 samples per ablation, chosen as "a log-scale midpoint between 10K and 100K" (Section 4). This design enables researchers without massive compute budgets to replicate and extend the findings, lowering the barrier to entry for reasoning model research.

By positioning itself as a foundational empirical study rather than a single-technique improvement, this paper provides the reasoning model community with a validated reference for data curation decisions, reducing reliance on intuition and enabling more efficient future development.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

This paper presents a systematic engineering framework for building high-quality training datasets that teach language models to reason through complex problems in math, code, and science. The solution takes the form of a six-stage data curation pipeline that transforms raw question sources into verified question-answer pairs, with each stage optimized through controlled experiments measuring downstream model performance rather than relying on intuition about data quality.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The OpenThoughts3 pipeline operates as a sequential data transformation system with six major components connected in series. First, the **Question Sourcing** module collects raw questions from 62 different sources across math (21 sources), code (27 sources), and science (14 sources), producing an initial pool of candidate questions. Second, the **Question Mixing** module selects and combines questions from the top-performing sources, determining how many sources to blend together. Third, the **Question Filtering** module applies quality filters to retain only the highest-quality questions from the mixed pool, using methods like LLM-based difficulty scoring or response length analysis. Fourth, the **Deduplication and Multiple Answer Sampling** module removes duplicate questions while simultaneously querying a teacher model multiple times per question to generate diverse reasoning traces. Fifth, the **Answer Filtering** module optionally removes low-quality or incorrect answer traces before final dataset construction. Sixth, the **Teacher Model Selection** module determines which reasoning model generates the thinking tokens and answers that will train the student model. The final output feeds into a **Supervised Fine-Tuning** stage where Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct learns from the curated question-thinking tokens-answer triplets, with performance measured across eight reasoning benchmarks to validate each pipeline decision.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the experimental methodology that makes this systematic investigation possible, explaining how the authors isolate variables across 1,000+ ablation experiments at a controlled 31,600-sample scale.
- We then walk through each of the six pipeline stages in sequential order, detailing the specific techniques tested, the quantitative results from each ablation, and the design choices selected for the final OpenThoughts3-1.2M dataset.
- We explain the training and evaluation infrastructure, including hyperparameter configurations that vary by dataset scale and the benchmark suite used to measure downstream performance.
- We describe the scaling strategy that expands the validated pipeline from 31,600 samples to the full 1.2 million example dataset, including the domain mixture ratios.
- We conclude with the key counterintuitive findings that emerged from this systematic approach, highlighting where empirical results contradicted conventional wisdom about data curation.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This paper presents an empirical ablation study rather than a novel algorithm, where the core contribution is a validated methodology for optimizing supervised fine-tuning data recipes through controlled experimentation. The fundamental insight driving this approach is that data quality cannot be assessed in isolation—instead, the quality of a dataset must be measured by the downstream performance of models trained on it, which requires holding all training variables constant while varying only the data curation strategy.

**Experimental Methodology and Control Variables**

The authors establish a controlled experimental framework where each pipeline component is ablated independently while keeping all other factors constant. For every experiment, the pipeline generates exactly 31,600 data points, a number chosen as "a log-scale midpoint between 10K and 100K" since $\sqrt{10 \times 100} \approx 31.6$ (Section 4). This scale balances cost-effectiveness with statistical signal, allowing the team to run over 1,000 experiments without prohibitive compute expenses. Each dataset variant trains the same base model—Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct—using identical hyperparameters except where scale necessitates adjustment. The evaluation metric for selecting winning strategies is the average accuracy across eight reasoning benchmarks spanning math, code, and science domains, ensuring that optimizations do not overfit to a single task type.

The training hyperparameters vary by dataset scale according to Table 9 in Appendix D.2. For the ablation experiments at 31,600 samples (the "medium" scale), the configuration uses a learning rate of $4 \times 10^{-5}$, a batch size of 128, 5 epochs, and no sequence packing. For the final 1.2M dataset (the "large" scale), the learning rate increases to $8 \times 10^{-5}$, batch size increases to 512, epochs remain at 5, and sequence packing is enabled to improve computational efficiency. All training uses DeepSpeed v3 with a cosine learning rate schedule, a warmup ratio of 0.1, weight decay of 0, and the AdamW optimizer with betas $(0.9, 0.999)$.

**Stage 1: Question Sourcing**

The first pipeline stage addresses where questions originate, categorizing sources into three types: fully synthetic (LLM-generated from templates), semi-synthetic (LLM-transformed from existing text corpora), and non-synthetic (human-written from forums or competitions). The authors evaluate 62 distinct question sources: 27 for code, 21 for math, and 14 for science, with full details in Appendix R.1. For sources producing fewer than 31,600 questions, the authors repeat questions until reaching the target count, ensuring fair comparison across sources. All questions receive fresh responses from the teacher model (DeepSeek-R1 for initial experiments) even if pre-existing answers exist, maintaining consistency in answer generation quality.

Table 3 presents the ablation results for question sourcing. For code, StackExchange-CodeGolf achieves an average score of 38.8 with a code-specific average of 25.3, while OpenCodeReasoning scores 38.4 average with 27.5 on code benchmarks. For math, openmath-2-math leads with 38.1 average and 58.8 on math benchmarks, closely followed by NuminaMath-1.5 at 37.4 average and 58.5 on math benchmarks. For science, StackExchange-Physics achieves 34.3 average with 43.2 on science benchmarks, while OrganicChemistry-PDF reaches 34.0 average with 45.3 on science benchmarks. The gap between strongest and weakest sources is substantial—for code, the difference between top and bottom sources is 17.2 percentage points on average, demonstrating that question source selection fundamentally constrains downstream performance.

A critical finding from this stage is that simple synthetic methods perform comparably to complex manually curated pipelines. The authors note that "no clear pattern emerges across question generation strategies" (Section 4.1), meaning that sophisticated human curation does not guarantee superior results compared to programmatic generation approaches. This challenges the assumption that human-written questions are inherently superior for training reasoning models.

**Stage 2: Mixing Questions**

After identifying high-performing individual sources, the second stage investigates how to combine them. The intuitive hypothesis suggests that mixing many sources increases diversity and should improve generalization, but the experiments test whether this diversity comes at the cost of incorporating lower-quality questions. The mixing strategy selects the top-ranked $N$ datasets, randomly samples $\frac{31,600}{N}$ questions from each source, and concatenates them to form a 31,600-question dataset. The authors sweep $N \in \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16\}$ to measure the diversity-quality tradeoff.

Table 4 shows that mixing many sources degrades performance rather than improving it. For code, using only the top 1 source achieves 39.9 average, the top 2 sources achieve 41.3 average, but performance drops to 38.6 for top 4, 37.0 for top 8, and 36.4 for top 16 sources. Using two high-quality code sources instead of 16 strategies results in a 5% accuracy increase on average across all benchmarks. This pattern holds across all domains—math and science ablations in Tables 36 and 37 show identical trends where top 1 or 2 sources outperform mixing 8 or 16 sources.

The takeaway contradicts conventional data curation wisdom: "downstream performance benefits from increased quality of source data rather than diversity induced by mixing multiple question sources" (Section 4.2). The final pipeline uses OpenMath-2-Math as the sole math source, CodeGolf and OpenCodeReasoning as the two code sources, and StackExchange-Physics plus OrganicChemistry-PDFs as the two science sources.

**Stage 3: Question Filtering**

Since source datasets can contain millions of potential questions, the third stage selects a high-quality subset before expensive teacher annotation. The authors test multiple filtering strategies including fastText classifiers, difficulty scores from LLMs, embedding distance metrics, and response length filtering. For difficulty-based filtering, an LLM (GPT-4o-mini) assesses each question's difficulty on a 1-10 scale, and the pipeline retains the most difficult questions. For response length filtering, an LLM responds to each question directly, and the pipeline selects questions that elicit the longest responses, under the hypothesis that challenging questions provoke more extensive reasoning.

Table 5 presents filtering results. For math, Response Length Selection using GPT-4.1-mini achieves 41.9 average with 66.0 on math benchmarks, outperforming fastText methods which score 35.6 average. For code, Difficulty-based Selection achieves 43.0 average with 27.7 on code benchmarks, outperforming response length methods at 42.2 average. Across all domains, LLM-based filtering methods outperform classical filtering methods such as embedding-based and fastText filters. The improvement over random filtering baseline is 4% for math and 6% for code domains.

The authors test different LLMs for response length filtering and find that "using stronger models for response length filtering typically outperforms using weaker models" (Section 4.3). The final pipeline uses difficulty-based filtering with GPT-4o-mini for code questions, and response length filtering with GPT-4.1-mini for math and science questions.

**Stage 4: Deduplication and Multiple Answer Sampling**

The fourth stage addresses two complementary axes of data diversity: question diversity through deduplication and answer diversity through multiple teacher queries per question. The authors explore three deduplication strictness levels: no deduplication, exact match deduplication, and fuzzy deduplication using threshold-based string similarity measured by normalized Indel similarity:

$$\text{indelsim} = 100 \times \frac{\text{LCSlength}(s_1, s_2)}{\max(|s_1|, |s_2|)}$$

where $\text{LCSlength}(s_1, s_2)$ computes the longest common subsequence between strings $s_1$ and $s_2$. Questions with similarity above 75% are considered duplicates. Simultaneously, the authors explore three sampling levels: 1×, 4×, and 16× answers per question, trading higher answer diversity for lower question diversity.

Table 6 shows results for the science domain, with math and code in Appendix S.4. For code and science, various deduplication and sampling combinations yield similar results—the baseline of no deduplication with 1× answer performs only 0.7 points worse than exact deduplication with 16× answers for code. For math, exact deduplication with 4× answers performs best, but 16× answers is second-best. The authors adopt 16× sampling for all domains because it "provides better scalability" for the final 1.2M dataset construction.

The key insight is that "the benefits of question diversity may be limited for the reasoning datasets we measure performance on, at least when answer diversity increases" (Section 4.4). Sampling multiple answers per question effectively increases dataset size by at least 16× without requiring additional unique questions. The final pipeline uses 16× answers per question for all domains, exact deduplication for math and science, and no deduplication for code.

**Stage 5: Answer Filtering**

The fifth stage tests whether removing potentially incorrect or low-quality answer traces improves downstream performance. The intuition suggests that training on verified correct answers should outperform training on all answers, but the experiments challenge this assumption. To ensure fair comparison, the authors first generate 63,200 answers, apply each filtering strategy, then sample 31,600 question-answer pairs from the filtered dataset. The baseline uses all 63,200 samples without filtering, which is not compute-controlled but represents the maximum data utilization scenario.

Table 7 shows that for math datasets, the random filtering baseline outperformed all other filtering methods including GPT Verification, Shortest Answers Selection, and Majority Consensus Selection. For code, a fastText classifier was the best filtering method but performed similarly to no filtering. For science, keeping the top 8 longest answers was strongest but again showed negligible improvement over no filtering. Across all domains, "the no-filtering strategy (training on all samples without controlling compute) led to performance similar to that of all other methods of filtering" (Section 4.5).

This result suggests that "the benefits of answer filtering are not significant enough to justify reducing the number of samples in the dataset, regardless of the domain" (Section 4.5). The final pipeline skips answer filtering entirely, using all generated answers to maximize dataset size. This finding contradicts common practice in reasoning data pipelines where verification steps are standard.

**Stage 6: Teacher Model Selection**

The sixth stage investigates which teacher model produces the best distillation data, testing DeepSeek-R1, QwQ-32B, and Phi-4-Reasoning-Plus. All sampling hyperparameters remain constant across teacher models to isolate the teacher's effect. This experiment addresses whether stronger teacher models (by benchmark performance) produce better student models through distillation.

Table 8 shows that QwQ-32B outperforms all other teachers across all domains, yielding average accuracy improvements of 1.9% for code and 2.6% for math over DeepSeek-R1. This occurs "despite the fact that QwQ-32B scores lower on average when compared to DeepSeek-R1" (Section 4.6). For example, DeepSeek-R1 outperforms QwQ-32B by 9% on CodeElo, 8% on GPQA Diamond, and 23% on JEEBench, yet QwQ-32B produces better training data. Table 29 in Appendix K confirms DeepSeek-R1 is empirically the best model overall, making this result particularly counterintuitive.

The takeaway directly challenges distillation assumptions: "Models with better performance are not necessarily better teachers" (Section 1). The final pipeline uses QwQ-32B as the teacher model for all domains.

**Scaling to OpenThoughts3-1.2M**

After validating each pipeline stage at 31,600 samples, the authors scale the winning strategies to produce the full OpenThoughts3-1.2M dataset. Figure 3 demonstrates that "the scaling behavior improves as we successively stack the best choices from each stage in the pipeline," with each optimization shifting the scaling curve upward. The largest gains come from question source selection, question filtering strategies, and teacher model selection.

The final dataset contains 1.2 million datapoints with domain ratios of 850,000 math (70.8%), 250,000 code (20.8%), and 100,000 science (8.3%). These ratios follow the OpenThoughts2-1M mixture that "exhibited strong and balanced performance on par with the DeepSeek-R1-Distill models" (Section 5). To reach target sample counts, the pipeline works backwards—for math, this required increasing input questions to the filtering stage from 1M to 3M before applying the 16× sampling to reach 850,000 final examples.

Figure 4 illustrates the full construction pipeline: source datasets feed into question filtering, then deduplication (for math and science), then random sampling to target counts, then 16× answer generation, producing the final 1.2M dataset. The pipeline applies the highest-performing strategy at each stage from the ablation experiments, opting for more scalable choices when performance is equal.

**Evaluation Infrastructure and Held-Out Benchmarks**

The evaluation setup uses eight primary benchmarks to guide pipeline decisions: AIME24, AMC23, and MATH500 for math; CodeElo, CodeForces, and LiveCodeBench 05/23-05/24 for code; GPQA Diamond and JEEBench for science. All evaluations use Evalchemy with temperature 0.7, top_p 1.0, and max_new_tokens 32,768. To measure generalization without contaminating pipeline decisions, four benchmarks are held out until all experiments complete: AIME 2025, HMMT 02/25, Humanity's Last Exam (MCQ subset), and LiveCodeBench 06/24-01/25.

Decontamination removes training samples with high similarity to evaluation benchmarks using two methods. First, normalized Indel similarity above 75% indicates contamination. Second, 13-gram overlap between training and evaluation samples indicates contamination. The decontamination algorithm achieves 99.6% true negative rate on manually contaminated test data while discarding only 1.4% of noncontaminated samples (Appendix F, Figure 7).

**Key Design Choice Rationales**

Several design choices warrant explicit justification based on the ablation results. The 31,600 sample ablation scale was chosen over smaller scales (1K, 10K) because it provides sufficient statistical signal to distinguish between strategies while remaining cost-effective—each ablation dataset cost approximately $300 in API costs through the DeepSeek API (Appendix O). The decision to optimize for average performance across domains rather than per-domain optimization assumes cross-domain transfer exists, though the authors acknowledge "it's unclear whether such transfer persists once domains are mixed" (Section 6).

The choice to skip answer filtering despite conventional wisdom stems directly from empirical results showing no filtering strategy outperformed using all samples. This saves substantial compute that would otherwise verify 63,200 answers per experiment. The 16× sampling multiplier was selected over 4× despite similar performance because it enables greater dataset scale for the final 1.2M construction without requiring proportionally more unique questions.

The selection of QwQ-32B over DeepSeek-R1 as teacher represents the most significant counterintuitive finding. The authors hypothesize this may relate to the nature of reasoning traces rather than final answer correctness—QwQ-32B may produce more learnable thinking patterns even if its final answers are occasionally less accurate. This suggests distillation quality depends on trace characteristics beyond benchmark performance, an open question for future investigation.

**Training Configuration Details**

The final OpenThinker3-7B model trains on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct using the large hyperparameter set from Table 9: learning rate $8 \times 10^{-5}$, batch size 512, 5 epochs, with sequence packing enabled. Training requires 25,000 A100 GPU hours on 128 nodes with 4 GPUs each (512 GPUs total). The chat template uses  and </think> tokens to enclose reasoning traces, following the R1 template format rather than the SkyT1 template with <|begin_of_thought|> tokens, as Appendix D.4 shows comparable performance between templates.

Appendix D.5 investigates system prompt effects, finding that "enabling explicit reasoning consistently improves performance across mathematical and scientific reasoning tasks, with particularly dramatic improvements on AIME benchmarks (45.3% vs. 2.0% on AIME25)" when reasoning is enabled versus disabled. However, removing system prompts entirely sometimes yields better results (70.0% vs. 61.3% on AIME24), suggesting explicit instructions may occasionally constrain natural reasoning patterns.

**Limitations and Open Questions**

The technical approach has several acknowledged limitations. The pipeline optimizes for supervised fine-tuning only, not exploring reinforcement learning datasets which represent "a standard training regime for building reasoning models" (Section 6). The experiments do not investigate staged SFT or curriculum learning strategies that might further improve performance. The cross-domain transfer assumption underlying average-performance optimization remains unverified when domains are mixed at full scale.

An open question concerns scaling behavior as student performance approaches teacher capability: "How does this change as the student model's performance approaches that of the teacher?" (Section 6). The authors note it remains unclear whether models eventually plateau or achieve weak-to-strong generalization beyond teacher performance. Another open direction involves the interaction between question diversity and answer diversity across different model scales—the finding that limited question diversity matters less with high answer diversity may not hold for larger student models or different dataset sizes.

The safety analysis in Appendix L reveals a trade-off between reasoning capability and safety alignment. OpenThinker3-7B achieves a harmfulness rate of 55.5 on HarmBench compared to 14.5 for the base Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, indicating that "supervised fine-tuning on reasoning inadvertently degrades the pre-existing safety alignment" (Appendix L). This degradation occurs because OpenThinker3-7B was trained without explicit safety-tuning or alignment-focused data, highlighting the need for future work to incorporate safety datasets while preserving reasoning capabilities.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section distills the most significant conceptual contributions from the OpenThoughts project. Rather than cataloging every technical finding, we focus on insights that fundamentally shift how the community should think about reasoning model training data. These contributions range from methodological innovations (how to evaluate data quality) to empirical discoveries that contradict established assumptions (which teachers work best, whether filtering helps).

---

### 4.1 Methodological Innovation: Downstream Performance as the Only Valid Data Quality Metric

**What Prior Work Assumed:** Previous reasoning dataset efforts evaluated data quality through intrinsic metrics—question difficulty scores, answer correctness verification, embedding-based diversity measures, or human expert ratings. Projects like S1 and LIMO relied on manual curation of "challenging, high-quality prompts" (Section 2), assuming that expert judgment about question quality would translate to better model performance. Similarly, verification pipelines in OpenThoughts-114K used answer matching and unit tests to filter out "incorrect" reasoning traces, presuming that training on verified-correct answers would improve downstream capabilities.

**What This Work Demonstrates:** The OpenThoughts project establishes that **intrinsic data quality metrics do not reliably predict downstream model performance**. The only valid measure of a dataset's quality is the performance of models trained on it. This conclusion emerges from multiple converging findings:

- Questions from "simpler" synthetic sources (e.g., CodeGolf) outperformed questions from manually curated competition datasets on code benchmarks (Table 3)
- Answer filtering strategies that removed supposedly "incorrect" or "low-quality" traces performed no better than training on all samples (Table 7)
- The best teacher model for distillation (QwQ-32B) scored lower on target benchmarks than alternative teachers (Table 8, Table 29)

**Why This Is Significant:** This represents a **fundamental methodological shift** rather than an incremental improvement. Prior work treated data quality as an intrinsic property that could be assessed before training. This paper demonstrates that data quality is **relational**—it depends on the interaction between the data characteristics and the student model's learning dynamics. A question that appears "high quality" by human standards may not produce reasoning traces that a 7B model can effectively learn from.

The implication extends beyond reasoning models: any field using distillation or supervised fine-tuning should validate data curation choices through downstream performance ablations rather than relying on proxy metrics. This insight justifies the computational expense of running 1,000+ controlled experiments—without this empirical approach, the community would continue optimizing for the wrong objectives.

**Distinction:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in methodology, not an incremental technical improvement. It challenges the epistemological foundation of how data quality is assessed in machine learning.

---

### 4.2 Counterintuitive Discovery: Better-Performing Models Are Not Better Teachers for Distillation

**What Prior Work Assumed:** The distillation literature operates on an implicit assumption: stronger teacher models produce better student models. This assumption underlies the R1-Distill family, where DeepSeek-R1's strong benchmark performance motivated its use as the sole teacher. The logic is straightforward—if a teacher model achieves 76% on AIME24, its reasoning traces should contain more learnable signal than traces from a model achieving 69% on the same benchmark.

**What This Work Demonstrates:** Table 8 and Table 29 reveal a **direct contradiction** to this assumption. QwQ-32B outperforms DeepSeek-R1 as a teacher across all domains (average accuracy improvement of 1.9% for code, 2.6% for math), despite DeepSeek-R1 scoring higher on target benchmarks:

| Benchmark | DeepSeek-R1 | QwQ-32B | Gap |
|-----------|-------------|---------|-----|
| CodeElo | 53.7 | 44.3 | R1 +9.4% |
| GPQA Diamond | 73.7 | 65.0 | R1 +8.7% |
| JEEBench | 92.3 | 69.9 | R1 +22.4% |

Yet when used as a teacher for distillation, QwQ-32B produces student models that outperform those distilled from DeepSeek-R1.

**Why This Is Significant:** This finding **decouples teacher capability from teacher effectiveness**—a distinction that prior work treated as synonymous. The authors hypothesize (Section 4.6) that QwQ-32B may produce reasoning traces with characteristics that are more learnable for smaller models, even if its final answers are occasionally less accurate. Potential explanations include:

- **Trace structure:** QwQ-32B may generate more explicit, step-by-step reasoning that 7B models can better imitate
- **Error patterns:** DeepSeek-R1's errors may be more systematic or harder for students to recover from
- **Overthinking:** Stronger models may produce unnecessarily complex traces that confuse smaller students

This insight has immediate practical implications: researchers should not assume the strongest available model is the best teacher. Instead, teacher selection requires empirical validation through distillation experiments. It also opens theoretical questions about what makes reasoning traces "learnable" distinct from what makes answers "correct."

**Distinction:** This is a **fundamental empirical discovery** that challenges core assumptions in the distillation literature. It is not an incremental improvement but a conceptual correction to how the field thinks about knowledge transfer.

### 4.3 Paradigm Shift: Answer Diversity Trumps Question Diversity for Reasoning SFT

**What Prior Work Assumed:** Data curation best practices emphasize **question diversity**—collecting questions from many sources, domains, and difficulty levels to maximize coverage. Projects like Nemotron and OpenR1 aggregate questions from dozens of sources, assuming that exposure to diverse problem types improves generalization. The OpenThoughts2-1M dataset incorporated 26 question sources, following this diversity-first approach.

**What This Work Demonstrates:** Table 4, Table 35, Table 36, and Table 37 show that **mixing many question sources degrades performance**. Using only the top 1-2 sources outperforms mixing 8-16 sources by approximately 5% on average across benchmarks. Simultaneously, Table 6 and Appendix S.4 demonstrate that **sampling 16 answers per question** (with exact deduplication) performs as well as or better than annotating more unique questions once.

The final OpenThoughts3 pipeline uses only 5 question sources total (1 math, 2 code, 2 science) but generates 16 reasoning traces per question, achieving 1.2M examples from a much smaller question pool.

**Why This Is Significant:** This inverts the conventional diversity-quality tradeoff. Prior work assumed that question diversity was essential for generalization and that answer diversity was secondary. This paper demonstrates that for reasoning SFT:

1. **Answer diversity provides more learning signal** than question diversity—seeing 16 different reasoning approaches to the same problem teaches the model more about *how* to reason than seeing 16 different problems with single solutions
2. **High-quality sources are scarce**—mixing in lower-quality sources to increase diversity introduces noise that outweighs the benefits of broader coverage
3. **Scale through sampling is efficient**—generating multiple answers per question is more cost-effective than sourcing and filtering new unique questions

This insight directly enabled the OpenThoughts3-1.2M dataset construction. Without the 16× sampling strategy, reaching 1.2M examples would have required sourcing and filtering 1.2M unique questions instead of approximately 75,000 questions (Figure 4).

**Distinction:** This is a **fundamental paradigm shift** in data curation strategy. It changes how researchers should think about scaling reasoning datasets—not by collecting more questions, but by extracting more signal from each question.

---

### 4.4 Null Result with Practical Impact: Answer Filtering Provides Negligible Benefits

**What Prior Work Assumed:** Verification and answer filtering are standard components of reasoning data pipelines. OpenThoughts-114K used "answer matching and unit tests to verify math and code responses, filtering out reasoning traces with incorrect answers" (Section 3). The intuition is clear: training on incorrect answers should harm model performance, so removing them should help. Projects like SkyT1 and DeepMath-103K incorporate similar verification steps.

**What This Work Demonstrates:** Table 7, Table 44, Table 45, and Table 46 show that **no answer filtering strategy outperforms training on all samples**. For math, random filtering outperformed GPT Verification, Majority Consensus, and Shortest Answers Selection. For code, the best filtering method (fastText) performed within standard error of no filtering. For science, keeping the longest answers was strongest but showed negligible improvement.

The authors explicitly state: "the benefits of answer filtering are not significant enough to justify reducing the number of samples in the dataset, regardless of the domain" (Section 4.5).

**Why This Is Significant:** This null result has substantial **practical implications** for resource-constrained researchers:

- **Compute savings:** Verification requires additional LLM API calls or unit test execution for every generated answer. Skipping this step reduces annotation costs by approximately 50% (since verification typically requires generating 2× the target dataset size, then filtering down)
- **Simplified pipelines:** Removing the verification step reduces pipeline complexity and potential failure modes
- **Maximized data utilization:** Using all generated samples means no annotation compute is wasted on discarded examples

The theoretical implication is equally important: **incorrect reasoning traces may still contain learnable signal**. A model may benefit from seeing common error patterns and learning to avoid them, or the verification process may incorrectly discard valid alternative solution approaches. This challenges the assumption that only "correct" traces should be used for training.

**Distinction:** This is a **practically significant null result**. While not a positive discovery, it prevents the community from wasting resources on verification steps that do not improve performance. Null results are underreported in ML literature but are essential for efficient progress.

---

### 4.5 Open Artifacts at Competitive Scale: Democratizing Reasoning Model Development

**What Prior Work Provided:** Previous open reasoning efforts released either small datasets (S1 with ~1K examples, LIMO with similar scale) or models without full data recipes (DeepSeek-R1-Distill models released weights but not complete training data). Nemotron released large datasets but with less transparent pipeline documentation. No prior open project achieved competitive performance with frontier proprietary models while releasing all artifacts.

**What This Work Provides:** OpenThoughts3 releases:
- **1.2M verified reasoning examples** across math, code, and science
- **OpenThinker3-7B model weights** achieving state-of-the-art open-data performance
- **Complete pipeline code** for reproducing the data curation process
- **Full ablation results** from 1,000+ experiments documenting what does and does not work

Table 1 shows OpenThinker3-7B outperforms all open-data 7B and 8B models across domains, with average scores of 55.3% compared to 42.9% for DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B. This performance is achieved with fully open data and methods.

**Why This Is Significant:** This represents a **democratization milestone** for the reasoning model community. Prior to this work, researchers wanting to build competitive reasoning models faced a choice: use proprietary APIs (expensive, non-reproducible) or accept significantly worse performance with open methods. OpenThoughts3 closes this gap, enabling:

- **Reproducible research:** Other groups can build upon verified findings rather than guessing at effective techniques
- **Reduced barriers:** The 31,600-sample ablation scale costs approximately $300 in API fees per experiment (Appendix O), making replication feasible for university research groups
- **Foundation for RL:** While this work focuses on SFT, the curated dataset provides a starting point for reinforcement learning approaches that require high-quality initial policies

The release also establishes a **baseline for future open efforts**. Any new open reasoning dataset should demonstrate improvements over OpenThoughts3's validated pipeline, raising the standard for what constitutes a meaningful contribution.

**Distinction:** This is an **infrastructure and community contribution** rather than a technical innovation. However, its significance lies in enabling future research that would otherwise be impossible for resource-constrained groups. The open artifacts transform this paper from a standalone study into a platform for community progress.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Benchmark Suite and Domain Coverage**

The OpenThoughts project evaluates models across 12 distinct benchmarks spanning three reasoning domains, with 8 benchmarks used for pipeline optimization and 4 held out for final generalization assessment (Table 13, Appendix E). The benchmark selection reflects a deliberate balance between established standards and contamination-resistant evaluations:

| Domain | Primary Benchmarks (Pipeline) | Held-Out Benchmarks |
|--------|------------------------------|---------------------|
| Math | AIME24 (30 questions), AMC23 (40 questions), MATH500 (500 questions) | AIME25 (30 questions), HMMT 02/25 (30 questions) |
| Code | CodeElo (391 problems), CodeForces (453 problems), LiveCodeBench 05/23-05/24 (511 problems) | LiveCodeBench 06/24-01/25 (369 problems) |
| Science | GPQA Diamond (198 questions), JEEBench (515 questions) | HLE MCQ (512 questions) |

All evaluations use the Evalchemy framework with consistent generation parameters: temperature = 0.7, top_p = 1.0, and max_new_tokens = 32,768 (Appendix E). For benchmarks requiring multiple runs, the authors report mean accuracy with standard error of the mean (SEM) over 3-10 iterations depending on the benchmark. MATH500 uses single-pass evaluation on the full 500-sample set.

**Decontamination Protocol**

To prevent evaluation contamination from inflating results, the authors implement a two-method decontamination pipeline (Appendix F):

1. **Normalized Indel Similarity**: Training samples with ≥75% similarity to evaluation samples (computed via longest common subsequence) are removed
2. **13-Gram Overlap**: Training samples sharing any 13-gram with evaluation samples are removed

The decontamination algorithm achieves 99.6% true negative rate on manually contaminated test data while discarding only 1.4% of noncontaminated samples (Figure 7, Appendix F). This rigorous protocol is essential given that reasoning benchmarks like AIME have limited question pools that could easily leak into training corpora.

**Ablation Experimental Design**

The core methodological innovation is the controlled ablation framework. Each of the 1,000+ experiments follows identical protocols:

- **Dataset Size**: 31,600 samples per ablation (chosen as $\sqrt{10K \times 100K} \approx 31.6K$, a log-scale midpoint)
- **Base Model**: Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct for all experiments
- **Training Hyperparameters**: Learning rate $4 \times 10^{-5}$, batch size 128, 5 epochs, no sequence packing (Table 9, Appendix D.2)
- **Selection Metric**: Average accuracy across all 8 primary benchmarks

This design isolates the effect of each pipeline decision while controlling for training variance. Each ablation dataset cost approximately $300 in API costs through the DeepSeek API (Appendix O), making the full experimental campaign a substantial investment that justifies the confidence in the findings.

- At **100K samples**: OpenThoughts3 reaches ~55% on AIME24 vs. ~45% for AM-1.4M
- At **1M samples**: OpenThoughts3 achieves 69.0% on AIME24, saturating near the teacher model's performance

Notably, some benchmarks show saturation at 1M scale (AMC23, MATH500), while others continue improving (AIME25, LiveCodeBench 06/24-01/25). This suggests further scaling beyond 1.2M could yield additional gains on harder benchmarks.

**Pipeline Ablation Results**

Tables 3-8 present the ablation results for each pipeline stage. Key quantitative findings:

**Question Sourcing (Table 3)**: The gap between best and worst sources is substantial:
- Code: StackExchange-CodeGolf (38.8 avg) vs. bugdaryan/sql-create (21.6 avg) = **17.2 point gap**
- Math: openmath-2-math (38.1 avg) vs. Lap1official/Math (24.4 avg) = **13.7 point gap**
- Science: StackExchange-Physics (34.3 avg) vs. AdapterOcean/biology (21.9 avg) = **12.4 point gap**

**Question Mixing (Table 4)**: Counter to diversity intuition, fewer sources perform better:
- Top 2 Code Sources: 41.3 average
- Top 16 Code Sources: 36.4 average
- **Difference: 4.9 points (13.5% relative improvement)**

**Question Filtering (Table 5)**: LLM-based methods outperform classical filters:
- Code Difficulty-based: 43.0 average vs. FastText: 38.4-40.5 average = **2.5-4.6 point gain**
- Math Response Length (GPT-4.1-mini): 41.9 average vs. FastText: 35.6 average = **6.3 point gain**

**Deduplication and Sampling (Table 6)**: Multiple answers per question provides efficient scaling:
- Exact Dedup + 16× sampling: 36.2 average (science)
- No Dedup + 1× sampling: 35.5 average (science)
- **Difference: 0.7 points with 16× data efficiency**

**Answer Filtering (Table 7)**: The null result is clear:
- No Filtering: 41.9 average (math)
- Best Filtering (Random): 41.6 average (math)
- **Difference: 0.3 points (not statistically significant)**

**Teacher Model (Table 8)**: The counterintuitive finding:
- QwQ-32B: 44.2 average (code), 44.2 average (math)
- DeepSeek-R1: 42.3 average (code), 41.6 average (math)
- **Difference: 1.9-2.6 points despite DeepSeek-R1's superior benchmark performance**

### 5.3 Do the Experiments Support the Claims?

**Strengths of the Experimental Design**

The experiments provide **strong support** for the paper's main claims through several mechanisms:

1. **Controlled Ablations**: Each pipeline stage is tested independently with all other factors held constant. This isolates causal effects rather than correlational improvements. The 31,600-sample scale is large enough to detect meaningful differences (standard errors are typically 0.3-0.6 points in Tables 3-8) while small enough to run 1,000+ experiments.

2. **Held-Out Benchmark Validation**: The four held-out benchmarks (AIME25, HMMT, HLE, LiveCodeBench 06/24-01/25) were not used during pipeline optimization. OpenThinker3-7B's strong performance on these benchmarks (+15.3 to +20.5 points over DeepSeek-R1-Distill) demonstrates genuine generalization rather than benchmark overfitting.

3. **Scaling Curve Consistency**: Figure 1 and Figure 8 show OpenThoughts3 dominating across all dataset scales from 1K to 1M. If the improvements were due to chance or overfitting, we would expect inconsistent scaling behavior. Instead, the recipe shows monotonic improvement with scale.

4. **Multiple Domain Validation**: The pipeline was optimized across math, code, and science domains separately (Tables 32-49, Appendix S). The fact that similar patterns emerge across all three domains (e.g., top 1-2 sources outperform mixing, LLM filtering beats fastText) increases confidence that the findings are not domain-specific artifacts.

**Limitations and Conditional Findings**

However, some claims have **conditional support** or acknowledge limitations:

1. **Cross-Domain Transfer Assumption**: The pipeline optimizes for average performance across domains, assuming cross-domain transfer exists. Appendix H.5 (Table 24) tests this directly and finds that "out-of-domain gains do not persist when mixed with the in-domain dataset." When combining code and science data, the GPQA-D scores become identical (52.7%) regardless of whether the code dataset had high or low science transfer. This suggests the cross-domain transfer assumption may not hold at full mixture scale, though the final model still performs well.

2. **Model Scale Specificity**: All experiments use Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct as the base model. Appendix G.1 (Table 14) tests Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct and finds different scaling behavior—Llama shows larger improvements on AMC23 (+59.4 points vs. +33.3 for Qwen) but lower absolute performance. The paper acknowledges: "it's unclear whether such transfer persists once domains are mixed" and "how does this change as the student model's performance approaches that of the teacher?" (Section 6). The findings may not generalize to larger student models (32B+) or different architectures.

3. **SFT-Only Focus**: The experiments optimize for supervised fine-tuning only. Appendix H.1 (Table 15) shows that verification "hurts at the 7B model scale but helps at the 32B model scale." This suggests the "no answer filtering" finding may be scale-dependent. The paper explicitly states: "We did not explore datasets for reinforcement learning, a standard training regime for building reasoning models" (Section 6).

4. **Safety Trade-Off**: Appendix L (Table 30) reveals a significant limitation—OpenThinker3-7B achieves a HarmBench score of 55.5 compared to 14.5 for the base Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct. This 4.8× increase in harmfulness rate indicates that reasoning fine-tuning degrades safety alignment. The paper acknowledges this trade-off but does not propose mitigation strategies beyond noting that "future work on the OpenThoughts would benefit from incorporating safety-specific datasets."

5. **Generalization Deficits**: Appendix N (Figure 13) tests reasoning robustness using Alice in Wonderland problems—structure-preserving variations of simple problems. All distilled reasoning models, including OpenThinker3-7B, show "strong performance fluctuations" across variations despite identical problem structure. While reasoning models outperform conventional LLMs (Figure 14), they still exhibit generalization deficits compared to frontier models like o1-preview and o3-mini. This limits claims about the robustness of the reasoning capabilities.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

**Comprehensive Ablation Coverage**

The paper includes extensive ablation studies beyond the main pipeline stages:

- **Chat Template Ablation** (Appendix D.4, Table 11): R1 template (</think>) vs. SkyT1 template (<|begin_of_thought|>) shows comparable performance (32.7% vs. 31.3% on AIME24), justifying the simpler template choice.

- **System Prompt Ablation** (Appendix D.5, Table 12): Removing system prompts entirely achieves 70.0% on AIME24 vs. 61.3% with reasoning enabled, suggesting explicit instructions may sometimes constrain natural reasoning.

- **Sequence Packing** (Appendix D.3, Table 10): Packing shows mixed effects but no significant degradation, justifying its use for computational efficiency at large scales.

- **Reasoning Trace Compression** (Appendix H.3, Table 22): Removing self-reflection keywords reduces average trace length from 11.6K to 0.3K tokens but causes a **49.1% relative performance drop** across benchmarks. Filtering traces >2048 tokens causes a **33.3% drop**. This validates that long reasoning structures are essential.

- **Verification Impact by Scale** (Appendix H.1, Table 15): Verification helps at 32B scale (OpenThinker-32B: 64.5 vs. OpenThinker-32B-Unverified: 62.1) but hurts at 7B scale (OpenThinker-7B: 41.9 vs. OpenThinker-7B-Unverified: 45.0). This reveals an important scale-dependent interaction.

**Failure Cases and Edge Cases**

The paper documents several failure modes:

1. **Code Benchmark Saturation Drop**: Figure 12 (Appendix I.2) shows a slight performance drop on code benchmarks at 100K scale. Analysis reveals this occurs only in the "easy" category while medium and hard tasks continue improving—suggesting the model may be overfitting to simple patterns at intermediate scales.

2. **Phi-4-Reasoning-Plus Teacher Failure**: Table 8 and Table 29 show Phi-4-Reasoning-Plus performs poorly on code benchmarks (0.5% on CodeElo, 0.8% on LiveCodeBench). Appendix K explains this is because Phi-4 "often fails to produce code tags such as '```python' which Evalchemy uses for code extraction"—a formatting issue rather than capability limitation.

3. **Response Length Paradox**: Appendix I.3 (Table 27) shows incorrect responses are significantly longer than correct ones (e.g., 18,198 vs. 7,817 tokens on AIME24 for DeepSeek-R1-Distill). This explains why "shortest answer" selection often outperforms "longest answer" selection during inference, despite response length filtering being effective for question selection.

4. **Teacher Surpassing**: Table 1 shows OpenThinker3-7B (53.7% on GPQA-D) outperforms its teacher QwQ-32B (65.0% on GPQA-D per Table 29)—wait, this is actually lower. However, on JEEBench, OpenThinker3-7B achieves 72.4% vs. QwQ-32B's 69.9%, demonstrating one instance of weak-to-strong generalization (Appendix K).

### 5.5 Trade-Offs and Practical Considerations

**Compute Investment vs. Performance Gain**

The experimental campaign required substantial resources (Appendix O):
- **Annotation**: 22,000 H100 GPU hours for OpenThoughts3-1.2M with QwQ-32B
- **Training**: 25,000 A100 GPU hours for OpenThinker3-7B (512 GPUs × ~49 hours)
- **Per-Ablation Cost**: ~$300 in API costs per 31,600-sample dataset

For researchers without similar resources, the paper provides validated shortcuts: skip answer filtering, use top 1-2 question sources, apply LLM-based filtering, and sample 16 answers per question. These recommendations save significant compute without sacrificing performance.

**Safety vs. Capability Trade-Off**

Table 30 (Appendix L) quantifies the safety degradation:

| Model | HarmBench (↓) | XSTEST Over-Refusal (↓) |
|-------|---------------|------------------------|
| Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct | 14.5 | 4.4 |
| OpenThinker3-7B | 55.5 | 5.6 |

The 41-point increase in harmfulness rate is substantial and should be considered when deploying reasoning models in safety-critical applications. The paper does not propose mitigation but acknowledges this as an open direction.

**Scale vs. Saturation**

Figure 8 shows that some benchmarks saturate at 1M scale (AMC23, MATH500 reach ~90% and ~95% respectively) while others continue improving (AIME25, LiveCodeBench 06/24-01/25). This suggests:
- For easier benchmarks, 1M samples may be sufficient
- For harder benchmarks, further scaling beyond 1.2M could yield gains
- The optimal dataset size depends on target benchmark difficulty

**Domain Mixture Ratios**

The final dataset uses 70.8% math, 20.8% code, and 8.3% science (Section 5). This ratio follows OpenThoughts2-1M's successful mixture but is not ablated at the 1.2M scale. The paper acknowledges: "We chose this ratio following the OpenThoughts2-1M mixture" rather than through new optimization. Different application domains may benefit from adjusted ratios.

### 5.6 Overall Assessment

The experiments provide **convincing support** for the paper's core claims about data curation strategies, with appropriate caveats about scope and limitations:

**Well-Supported Claims:**
- ✓ Question source quality matters more than diversity (Table 4, Tables 35-37)
- ✓ LLM-based filtering outperforms classical methods (Table 5, Tables 38-40)
- ✓ Multiple answers per question provides efficient scaling (Table 6, Tables 41-43)
- ✓ Answer filtering provides negligible benefits (Table 7, Tables 44-46)
- ✓ QwQ-32B is a better teacher than DeepSeek-R1 for distillation (Table 8, Tables 47-49)

**Conditionally-Supported Claims:**
- ⚠ Cross-domain transfer persists at full mixture scale (Appendix H.5 suggests otherwise)
- ⚠ Findings generalize to larger student models (only tested at 7B and 32B)
- ⚠ SFT-optimized recipes apply to RL training (explicitly not tested)

**Unsupported/Contradicted Claims:**
- ✗ None of the main claims are contradicted by the data, though some have important boundary conditions

The held-out benchmark results, scaling curves, and comprehensive ablations provide strong empirical grounding. The primary limitations are scope (SFT-only, 7B-focused) rather than methodological weaknesses. For researchers building open reasoning models within the tested scope, the experimental findings provide actionable, validated guidance.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While the OpenThoughts project provides valuable empirical insights into reasoning model data curation, several important limitations and trade-offs constrain the generalizability and applicability of its findings. This section critically examines the assumptions underlying the approach, scenarios not addressed, resource constraints, and open questions that remain.

### 6.1 Methodological Assumptions

**Cross-Domain Transfer Assumption**

The pipeline optimizes for average performance across math, code, and science domains, implicitly assuming that improvements in one domain transfer to others. However, the paper's own experiments challenge this assumption. Appendix H.5 (Table 24) directly tests cross-domain transfer and finds that "out-of-domain gains do not persist when mixed with the in-domain dataset." When combining a code dataset with strong science performance alongside a strong science dataset, the GPQA-D scores become identical (52.7%) regardless of whether the code dataset had high or low science transfer. The authors acknowledge this uncertainty in Section 6: "it's unclear whether such transfer persists once domains are mixed."

This assumption has practical implications: researchers optimizing for specific domains (e.g., code-only applications) may not benefit from the multi-domain optimization strategy. The final domain mixture ratios (70.8% math, 20.8% code, 8.3% science) follow OpenThoughts2-1M "without new optimization" at the 1.2M scale (Section 5), suggesting this critical hyperparameter was not systematically ablated for the final dataset.

**Downstream Performance as Sole Quality Metric**

While using downstream model performance to evaluate data quality is methodologically sound, this approach has inherent limitations. The 31,600-sample ablation scale, while cost-effective, may not capture interactions that emerge at full 1.2M scale. Some strategies that perform well at small scale may not scale linearly—a concern the authors acknowledge when noting that "some benchmarks are saturating (AMC23, MATH500) at the largest scale, and others do not respond to scale (HLE)" (Appendix G).

Additionally, optimizing for average benchmark performance may miss important distributional effects. A strategy that improves average performance could degrade performance on specific subcategories (e.g., geometry vs. algebra in math), but the paper does not report fine-grained breakdowns beyond domain-level averages.

**SFT-Only Optimization**

The entire experimental framework focuses exclusively on supervised fine-tuning, yet the authors acknowledge that reinforcement learning represents "a standard training regime for building reasoning models" (Section 6). This is a significant scope limitation because:

1. **Verification may be scale-dependent**: Appendix H.1 (Table 15) shows verification "hurts at the 7B model scale but helps at the 32B model scale." OpenThinker-32B-Unverified scores 62.1% average vs. 64.5% for the verified version—a 2.4 point difference. This suggests the "no answer filtering" recommendation may not hold for larger models or RL training regimes.

2. **RL requires different data characteristics**: Reinforcement learning typically benefits from verifiable rewards and diverse exploration trajectories. The OpenThoughts pipeline's finding that answer verification provides negligible benefits for SFT may not apply to RL, where incorrect answers could provide misleading reward signals.

3. **No staged SFT or curriculum learning**: Section 6 explicitly states "we did not explore the use of staged SFT or curriculum learning to further improve performance." Prior work suggests that progressive difficulty curricula can improve reasoning capabilities, but this design space remains unexplored.

### 6.2 Model Scale and Architecture Constraints

**Base Model Specificity**

All 1,000+ ablation experiments use Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct as the base model. While Appendix G.1 (Table 14) tests Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, the results reveal architecture-dependent effects:

| Benchmark | Qwen Improvement | Llama Improvement |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| AMC23 | +33.3 points | +59.4 points |
| MATH500 | +18.4 points | +40.6 points |
| LiveCodeBench | +10.7 points | +31.3 points |

Llama shows larger relative improvements but lower absolute performance. The authors note that "Qwen models, which start from a stronger starting point, still perform better overall" but acknowledge uncertainty about generalization: "In the future, we would like to expand this to further consider stronger models such as the recent Qwen 3 series of models" (Appendix G.1).

This raises questions about whether the pipeline findings generalize to:
- **Larger student models (32B+)**: The verification scale-dependency suggests different optimal strategies at different scales
- **Different architectures**: Models with different attention mechanisms or reasoning inductive biases may respond differently to data curation choices
- **Models approaching teacher capability**: Section 6 poses the open question: "How does this change as the student model's performance approaches that of the teacher? It is an open question whether models eventually plateau or surpass the teacher, achieving weak-to-strong generalization."

**Teacher-Student Capability Gap**

The finding that QwQ-32B outperforms DeepSeek-R1 as a teacher despite lower benchmark performance (Section 4.6) is counterintuitive and not fully explained. The authors hypothesize that QwQ-32B may produce "more learnable thinking patterns" but do not provide mechanistic analysis of trace characteristics. This leaves open:

- What specific properties make reasoning traces more distillable?
- Does the optimal teacher change as student capability increases?
- Are there domains where DeepSeek-R1 would be the better teacher?

Table 29 (Appendix K) shows OpenThinker3-7B outperforms QwQ-32B on JEEBench (72.4% vs. 69.9%), demonstrating one instance of weak-to-strong generalization. However, on most benchmarks, the student remains below teacher performance, limiting claims about surpassing teacher capabilities.

### 6.3 Safety and Alignment Trade-offs

**Significant Safety Degradation**

Appendix L (Table 30) reveals a critical trade-off between reasoning capability and safety alignment:

| Model | HarmBench (↓) | XSTEST Over-Refusal (↓) |
|-------|---------------|------------------------|
| Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct | 14.5 | 4.4 |
| DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B | 30.5 | 2.4 |
| OpenThinker3-7B | 55.5 | 5.6 |

OpenThinker3-7B's harmfulness rate is **4.8× higher** than the base model. The authors acknowledge that "OpenThinker3-7B was trained without any explicit safety-tuning or alignment-focused data, which likely contributes to its degraded safety performance" (Appendix L). This represents a substantial deployment risk for real-world applications.

The paper identifies a "clear trade-off between reasoning capabilities and safety" but does not propose mitigation strategies beyond noting that "future work on the OpenThoughts would benefit from incorporating safety-specific datasets." For practitioners, this means:

- **Additional safety fine-tuning is required** before deployment in safety-critical applications
- **The compute cost of safety alignment** is not included in the reported training expenses
- **Safety-reasoning interactions** remain unexplored—safety tuning may degrade reasoning capabilities

**Over-Refusal Patterns**

The XSTEST over-refusal rate increases from 4.4 to 5.6, suggesting the model becomes slightly more likely to refuse benign requests that syntactically resemble unsafe prompts. While this increase is modest compared to the harmfulness increase, it indicates that reasoning fine-tuning affects the model's refusal behavior in complex ways that are not fully understood.

### 6.4 Computational and Resource Constraints

**Substantial Compute Requirements**

While the 31,600-sample ablation scale is designed to be "cost-effective" at approximately $300 in API costs per experiment (Appendix O), the full pipeline remains resource-intensive:

| Stage | Compute Requirement |
|-------|--------------------|
| Annotation (OpenThoughts3-1.2M) | 22,000 H100 GPU hours |
| Training (OpenThinker3-7B) | 25,000 A100 GPU hours (512 GPUs) |
| Per-Ablation Experiment | ~$300 API costs |
| Total Experimental Campaign | 1,000+ experiments × $300 = ~$300,000+ |

For university research groups or smaller organizations, replicating the full experimental campaign may be prohibitively expensive. The authors provide validated shortcuts (skip answer filtering, use top 1-2 sources, etc.), but researchers wanting to explore new design choices face significant barriers.

**Annotation Bottleneck**

The 16× sampling strategy, while efficient for scaling, requires generating 16 reasoning traces per question. For the 1.2M dataset, this means approximately 19.2M total traces generated. The annotation cost dominates the pipeline expenses, and the choice of teacher model significantly impacts this:

- QwQ-32B annotation required 22,000 H100 GPU hours on 16 GH200 nodes
- Using a larger teacher (e.g., DeepSeek-R1 671B) would increase costs substantially
- API-based annotation introduces dependency on external services and potential rate limiting

**Evaluation Costs**

Each evaluation run requires 32 GPU-hours on 16 GH200 nodes (Appendix O). With 1,000+ experiments and multiple benchmarks per experiment, evaluation represents a non-trivial portion of total compute. The held-out benchmarks were evaluated only once after pipeline completion to prevent contamination, but this means pipeline decisions were made without knowledge of final generalization performance.

### 6.5 Generalization and Robustness Limitations

**Alice in Wonderland Generalization Deficits**

Appendix N (Figure 13) tests reasoning robustness using structure-preserving problem variations. All distilled reasoning models, including OpenThinker3-7B, show "strong performance fluctuations" across variations despite identical problem structure:

- OpenThinker3-7B shows correct response rates varying from ~0.2 to ~0.6 across AIW Friends variations 1-6
- Larger 32B models show higher overall performance but similar fluctuation patterns
- Frontier models (o1-preview, o3-mini) show "much smaller fluctuations and higher overall correct response rates"

The authors conclude: "Distilled reasoning models, while strongly improving over standard LLMs, still suffer from generalization deficits." This limits claims about the robustness of reasoning capabilities—models may perform well on benchmark tasks but fail on structurally equivalent problems with different surface features.

**Benchmark Saturation and Ceiling Effects**

Figure 8 (Appendix G) shows that some benchmarks saturate at 1M scale:
- **AMC23**: Reaches ~93.5% at 1M, leaving limited room for improvement
- **MATH500**: Reaches ~90% at 1M, approaching saturation
- **AIME25**: Continues improving at 1M scale (53.3%), suggesting headroom remains

This saturation has implications:
- **Diminishing returns**: Further scaling beyond 1.2M may yield smaller gains on easier benchmarks
- **Benchmark selection bias**: Optimizing for benchmarks that haven't saturated may skew the pipeline toward harder problems at the expense of foundational skills
- **Evaluation limitations**: As models approach saturation, benchmark discrimination decreases, making it harder to distinguish between strategies

**Domain-Specific Limitations**

The science domain receives the least attention in the final dataset (8.3% of examples), and science benchmarks show different scaling behavior than math and code:

- Figure 3 shows "the final 'Teacher Model' curve not being the top science performer is a consequence of our design choice, where we select winning strategies based on average performance across domains"
- Science filtering strategies (Table 40) show smaller gaps between methods compared to math and code
- The OrganicChemistry-PDF pipeline (Section R.1.3) relies on copyrighted textbooks with "all rights reserved" licenses (Appendix Q), raising potential redistribution concerns

### 6.6 Data Licensing and Redistribution Concerns

**Mixed License Status**

Appendix Q reveals complex licensing across source datasets:
- **Open licenses**: Qwen-2.5-7B-Instruct (Apache 2.0), Open2Math (CC BY 4.0), StackExchange (CC BY-SA 4.0)
- **Restricted licenses**: Multiple organic chemistry textbooks have "all rights reserved"
- **Unclear licenses**: Introduction to Organic Chemistry has "no license" specified

The OpenThoughts3-1.2M dataset combines these sources, but the paper does not clarify:
- Whether the final dataset can be freely redistributed given mixed license status
- How copyrighted textbook content was processed (extraction vs. transformation)
- Whether commercial use is permitted given some NC (Non-Commercial) licensed sources

This ambiguity may limit adoption by organizations requiring clear licensing for production deployment.

### 6.7 Open Questions and Future Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several open directions in Section 6:

1. **Cross-domain transfer at scale**: "It's unclear whether such transfer persists once domains are mixed" at full 1.2M scale, despite Appendix H.5 suggesting it may not.

2. **Student-teacher capability convergence**: "How does this change as the student model's performance approaches that of the teacher?" The weak-to-strong generalization observed on JEEBench (72.4% vs. 69.9%) is a single data point, not a systematic finding.

3. **Question-answer diversity interaction**: "A key open question is how this interaction behaves across different domains, dataset sizes, and model scales." The 16× sampling finding may not hold for 32B+ student models or 10M+ example datasets.

4. **Safety-reasoning integration**: No experiments explore how to maintain safety alignment while improving reasoning capabilities. This represents a critical gap for real-world deployment.

5. **RL dataset construction**: The SFT-optimized pipeline may not transfer to RL training regimes, which require different data characteristics (verifiable rewards, exploration diversity).

6. **Compression and efficiency**: Appendix H.3 shows that removing self-reflection causes a 49.1% performance drop, but the paper notes "the ability to reduce overthinking post-hoc remains an active and highly relevant area of research." Efficient inference without capability loss remains unsolved.

### 6.8 Summary Assessment

| Limitation Category | Severity | Mitigation Status |
|--------------------|----------|-------------------|
| SFT-only focus | High | Acknowledged, not addressed |
| Safety degradation | High | Acknowledged, no mitigation proposed |
| Cross-domain transfer assumption | Medium | Partially tested, results ambiguous |
| Model scale specificity | Medium | Tested at 7B and 32B, larger scales unknown |
| Compute requirements | Medium | Shortcuts provided, but full replication expensive |
| Generalization deficits | Medium | Documented, no solution proposed |
| Licensing ambiguity | Low-Medium | Not addressed |
| Benchmark saturation | Low | Documented, suggests scaling headroom |

The limitations are substantial but appropriately acknowledged. The paper does not overclaim—findings are scoped to SFT at 7B scale with clear caveats about generalization. For researchers working within this scope, the findings provide actionable guidance. For those outside this scope (RL training, 70B+ models, safety-critical deployment), the results serve as a foundation requiring additional validation rather than prescriptive recommendations.

The most critical limitation is the safety-reasoning trade-off, which has immediate deployment implications. The second most significant is the SFT-only focus, as many state-of-the-art reasoning models use RL or hybrid approaches. Addressing these two limitations would substantially increase the practical utility of the OpenThoughts findings for the broader community.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The OpenThoughts project represents more than a dataset release—it establishes a validated methodology that reshapes how the research community approaches reasoning model development. This section examines how these findings change the field's trajectory, what research directions they enable, and practical guidance for practitioners deciding whether to adopt this approach.

### 7.1 Landscape Transformation: From Proprietary Black Boxes to Open, Validated Recipes

**Democratizing Access to Competitive Reasoning Models**

Prior to this work, researchers faced a stark choice: rely on proprietary APIs with opaque training methods and recurring costs, or accept significantly degraded performance with open methods. Table 1 demonstrates that OpenThinker3-7B closes this gap, achieving 55.3% average performance compared to 42.9% for DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B—a 12.4 percentage point improvement that makes open methods genuinely competitive.

This shift has three cascading effects on the research landscape:

1. **Reduced Barrier to Entry**: The validated 31,600-sample ablation scale costs approximately $300 in API fees per experiment (Appendix O). University research groups can now replicate and extend these findings without massive compute budgets, whereas prior exploration required "prohibitively expensive" teacher inference and training (Section 1).

2. **Reproducible Baselines**: The release of complete pipeline code, ablation results from 1,000+ experiments, and the OpenThoughts3-1.2M dataset establishes a reference point for future work. New open reasoning efforts can now demonstrate improvement over validated findings rather than comparing against undocumented proprietary systems.

3. **Accelerated Iteration**: By documenting what does *not* work (answer filtering, mixing many sources, using the strongest teacher), the paper prevents redundant exploration across research groups. Each avoided dead-end experiment saves thousands of GPU hours and API costs community-wide.

**Methodological Shift: Empirical Validation Over Intuition**

The paper's most significant conceptual contribution is establishing downstream model performance as the only valid metric for data quality (Section 4.1). This challenges the epistemological foundation of how data curation decisions are made:

- **Prior Paradigm**: Data quality assessed through intrinsic metrics (difficulty scores, verification accuracy, human expert ratings)
- **New Paradigm**: Data quality assessed through controlled ablation measuring downstream benchmark performance

This shift extends beyond reasoning models. Any field using distillation or supervised fine-tuning should validate data curation choices through downstream performance rather than proxy metrics. The OpenThoughts framework provides a template for this empirical approach that can be adapted to other domains like vision-language reasoning, legal reasoning (Appendix J), or scientific discovery.

**Challenging Distillation Assumptions**

The finding that QwQ-32B outperforms DeepSeek-R1 as a teacher despite lower benchmark performance (Table 8, Table 29) decouples teacher capability from teacher effectiveness. This discovery opens new theoretical questions:

- What characteristics make reasoning traces "learnable" distinct from making answers "correct"?
- Do different student model architectures require different teacher models?
- Is there an optimal teacher-student capability gap for distillation?

These questions were previously unexplored because the field assumed stronger teachers were universally better. The OpenThoughts findings suggest distillation quality depends on trace characteristics—potentially step-by-step explicitness, error pattern diversity, or reasoning structure—rather than final answer accuracy alone.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

**Immediate Follow-Up Opportunities**

The paper explicitly identifies six open directions in Section 6 that represent high-value research opportunities:

1. **Reinforcement Learning Dataset Construction**: The SFT-optimized pipeline provides a foundation for RL training, but RL requires different data characteristics (verifiable rewards, exploration diversity). Appendix H.1 (Table 15) shows verification "hurts at the 7B model scale but helps at the 32B model scale," suggesting RL datasets may need different filtering strategies. Researchers can build on the validated SFT pipeline while exploring RL-specific optimizations.

2. **Safety-Reasoning Integration**: Appendix L (Table 30) reveals a 4.8× increase in harmfulness rate (14.5 to 55.5 on HarmBench) from reasoning fine-tuning. No experiments explore maintaining safety alignment while improving reasoning. This represents a critical gap—future work should investigate safety-specific datasets, RLHF integration, or architectural modifications that preserve both capabilities.

3. **Cross-Domain Transfer at Scale**: Appendix H.5 (Table 24) finds that "out-of-domain gains do not persist when mixed with the in-domain dataset," yet the final pipeline optimizes for average cross-domain performance. Research should test whether domain-specific pipelines outperform mixed optimization for targeted applications, and whether transfer effects change at 1.2M scale versus 31,600 samples.

4. **Student-Teacher Capability Convergence**: Section 6 poses: "How does this change as the student model's performance approaches that of the teacher?" The single instance of weak-to-strong generalization on JEEBench (72.4% vs. 69.9% in Table 29) warrants systematic investigation. Does surpassing teacher performance require specific data characteristics, or does it emerge naturally at certain scales?

5. **Question-Answer Diversity Interaction Across Scales**: The 16× sampling finding may not hold for 32B+ student models or 10M+ example datasets. Research should test whether the answer diversity > question diversity principle persists at larger scales, or whether question diversity becomes more important as models approach saturation.

6. **Efficient Inference Without Capability Loss**: Appendix H.3 (Table 22) shows removing self-reflection causes a 49.1% performance drop, yet long reasoning traces incur substantial inference costs. Research into post-hoc compression, distillation of short traces from long ones, or architectural changes that reduce overthinking while maintaining performance represents a high-impact direction.

**Extended Research Opportunities**

Beyond the paper's explicit open questions, several additional directions emerge:

**Architecture-Specific Optimization**: All experiments use Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, with limited Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct testing (Appendix G.1). Different architectures may respond differently to data curation choices. Research should test whether the pipeline findings generalize to:
- Models with different attention mechanisms (e.g., Mixture of Experts)
- Models with reasoning-specific architectural modifications
- Multimodal models extending reasoning to vision-language tasks

**Domain-Specialized Pipelines**: The final pipeline optimizes for average performance across math, code, and science. Applications targeting specific domains (e.g., code generation for software engineering, math reasoning for education) may benefit from domain-specialized pipelines with adjusted:
- Domain mixture ratios (currently 70.8% math, 20.8% code, 8.3% science)
- Question sources weighted toward target domain
- Teacher models selected for domain-specific trace quality

**Curriculum Learning and Staged SFT**: Section 6 explicitly states "we did not explore the use of staged SFT or curriculum learning." Prior work suggests progressive difficulty curricula can improve reasoning capabilities. Research should investigate whether staging training from easy to hard questions, or from single-domain to mixed-domain, improves final performance.

**Verification at Different Scales**: The null result on answer filtering (Section 4.5) applies to 7B SFT. Appendix H.1 suggests verification helps at 32B scale. Systematic investigation across model scales (1B, 7B, 32B, 70B+) would clarify when verification becomes beneficial, informing resource allocation decisions for different project scales.

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Downstream Use Cases

**Research and Education**

The OpenThoughts artifacts enable several immediate applications:

- **University Research Programs**: Groups can fine-tune OpenThinker3-7B or replicate the pipeline for domain-specific research without API dependencies. The $300 per ablation cost makes this feasible for grant-funded academic projects.

- **Educational Tools**: The math-focused dataset (850,000 examples) provides a foundation for AI tutoring systems that show step-by-step reasoning. The open license status of most components (Appendix Q) facilitates educational deployment.

- **Benchmark Development**: The decontamination methodology (Appendix F) and held-out benchmark strategy provide templates for creating contamination-resistant evaluation suites for future reasoning models.

**Industry Applications**

Organizations can leverage these findings for:

- **Custom Reasoning Models**: Companies can fine-tune on OpenThoughts3-1.2M as a base, then continue training on proprietary domain data. The validated pipeline reduces risk compared to untested custom data curation.

- **Code Generation Systems**: The 250,000 code examples with verified reasoning traces support development of code generation and debugging tools. The finding that CodeGolf and OpenCodeReasoning are top sources (Table 3) guides additional data collection.

- **Scientific Assistant Systems**: The science dataset (100,000 examples from physics and organic chemistry sources) provides a starting point for scientific reasoning assistants, though licensing concerns around copyrighted textbooks (Appendix Q) require legal review.

**Foundation for Advanced Training**

The OpenThoughts3-1.2M dataset serves as a foundation for:

- **RL Training**: High-quality SFT initialization is critical for RL approaches like PPO or DPO. The OpenThoughts pipeline provides validated SFT data that can initialize RL training with strong reasoning capabilities.

- **Model Merging**: The open model weights enable merging with other specialized models (e.g., safety-tuned models, domain-specific models) to combine capabilities.

- **Distillation Chains**: OpenThinker3-7B can serve as a teacher for smaller models (1B-3B), extending reasoning capabilities to resource-constrained deployment environments.

**When to Use This Approach**

The OpenThoughts pipeline is well-suited for:

| Building open reasoning models | **Strongly Recommended** | Validated recipe achieves SOTA open-data performance (Table 1) |
| Resource-constrained projects | **Recommended** | $300 per ablation enables iterative optimization without massive budgets |
| SFT-focused development | **Strongly Recommended** | All findings optimized for supervised fine-tuning |
| Domain-specific applications | **Recommended with Modification** | Use pipeline but adjust domain mixture ratios and question sources |

**When to Consider Alternatives**

| Scenario | Alternative Approach | Rationale |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Safety-critical deployment | **Add safety fine-tuning** | OpenThinker3-7B has 55.5 HarmBench score vs. 14.5 for base model (Table 30) |
| Very large models (70B+) | **Validate at target scale** | Findings tested at 7B and 32B; may not generalize to larger scales |
| RL-only training | **Explore RL-specific data** | SFT-optimized pipeline may not match RL data requirements |
| Proprietary data available | **Hybrid approach** | Combine OpenThoughts pipeline with proprietary domain data |
| Low-latency inference required | **Investigate compression** | Long reasoning traces (11.6K average tokens) increase inference costs |

**Key Implementation Shortcuts**

For practitioners adopting this approach without running full ablations, the paper provides validated shortcuts that save substantial compute:

1. **Skip Answer Filtering**: No filtering strategy outperformed using all samples (Table 7). This saves ~50% of annotation compute that would verify 63,200 answers before filtering to 31,600.

2. **Use Top 1-2 Question Sources**: Mixing 8-16 sources degrades performance by ~5% compared to top 2 sources (Table 4). Focus curation effort on identifying the best sources rather than aggregating many.

3. **Apply LLM-Based Filtering**: Difficulty-based filtering (code) and response length filtering (math, science) outperform classical methods by 4-6 percentage points (Table 5). Use GPT-4o-mini for difficulty, GPT-4.1-mini for response length.

4. **Sample 16 Answers Per Question**: This provides efficient scaling without requiring proportionally more unique questions (Table 6). For 1.2M examples, source ~75,000 questions and generate 16 traces each (Figure 4).

5. **Use QwQ-32B as Teacher**: Despite lower benchmark performance, QwQ-32B produces better distillation data than DeepSeek-R1 (Table 8). This counterintuitive finding saves experimentation on teacher selection.

6. **Exact Deduplication for Math/Science, None for Code**: Math and science benefit from exact deduplication with 16× sampling; code performs best with no deduplication and 16× sampling (Section 4.4).

**Reproduction Checklist**

For researchers replicating this work:

- [ ] **Base Model**: Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Apache 2.0 license, Appendix Q)
- [ ] **Teacher Model**: QwQ-32B for answer generation
- [ ] **Ablation Scale**: 31,600 samples per experiment for cost-effective iteration
- [ ] **Training Hyperparameters**: LR 4e-5, batch size 128, 5 epochs, no packing (Table 9, medium scale)
- [ ] **Evaluation**: Use Evalchemy framework with temperature 0.7, max_new_tokens 32,768 (Appendix E)
- [ ] **Decontamination**: Apply 75% Indel similarity and 13-gram overlap filters (Appendix F)
- [ ] **Held-Out Benchmarks**: Reserve AIME25, HMMT, HLE, LiveCodeBench 06/24-01/25 for final evaluation only

The OpenThoughts pipeline integrates with common ML workflows:

- **LlamaFactory Training**: Pipeline uses LlamaFactory for training (Appendix D.1), widely adopted for fine-tuning

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision: Toward Open, Capable Reasoning Systems

The OpenThoughts project establishes a foundation for community-driven progress on open reasoning models. By providing validated recipes rather than single-method innovations, the work enables cumulative improvement—future efforts can build upon these findings rather than re-exploring the same design space.

The ultimate vision extends beyond the current 7B-scale SFT focus:

- **Scaling to Larger Models**: As 32B, 70B, and larger open models become available, the pipeline findings should be revalidated at these scales, potentially revealing new optimization opportunities.

- **Hybrid Training Regimes**: Combining SFT (validated by OpenThoughts) with RL (standard for frontier models) may achieve capabilities exceeding either approach alone.

- **Multimodal Reasoning**: Extending the data curation methodology to vision-language reasoning, where chain-of-thought traces include visual grounding, represents a natural expansion.

- **Specialized Reasoning Domains**: The pipeline can be adapted for legal reasoning (Appendix J demonstrates feasibility), medical reasoning, or scientific discovery, with domain-specific question sources and verification methods.

- **Efficient Reasoning**: Research into maintaining reasoning capabilities while reducing inference costs (addressing the 11.6K average token trace length) would enable broader deployment.

The OpenThoughts findings transform reasoning model development from an opaque, resource-intensive endeavor into a systematic, reproducible engineering process. By challenging assumptions, documenting null results, and releasing all artifacts, the project provides both immediate practical value and a foundation for long-term community progress toward open, capable reasoning systems.
