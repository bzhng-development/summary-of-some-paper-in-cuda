## 1. Executive Summary

This appendix supports the main paper [6] by providing intuitive diagrams and examples that map random matrix calculations for real ensembles (Ginibre, GOE, Wishart) to topological surface gluings represented by permutations. It solves the problem of visualizing how real matrix fluctuations differ from complex ones by demonstrating how twisted edge identifications create nonorientable surfaces (e.g., Klein bottles) that contribute to asymptotic expansions based on Euler characteristic $\chi$. The work establishes the combinatorial topology behind real second-order freeness, exemplified by calculating asymptotic covariances such as $\lim_{N\to\infty} k_2(\text{Tr}(ZZZ^T Z^T), \text{Tr}(ZZ^T ZZ^T)) = 8$ through annular noncrossing pairings.

## 2. Context and Motivation

Random matrix theory often relies on diagrammatic methods to compute expected values of traces, such as $E(\text{tr}(X^n))$. These methods translate algebraic calculations into topological problems: counting surfaces constructed by gluing polygons (faces) along their edges. While this approach is well-established for complex matrix ensembles, real matrix ensembles introduce topological complexities that standard diagrammatic tools do not immediately address. This appendix addresses the gap between the abstract combinatorial proofs in the main paper [6] and the geometric intuition required to understand them.

### The Challenge of Real vs. Complex Ensembles

The primary motivation for this work stems from a fundamental difference in how real and complex Gaussian random variables pair during expectation calculations. In complex matrix models, a standard Gaussian variable $Z$ satisfies $E[Z^2] = 0$ and $E[Z\bar{Z}] = 1$. This constraint forces pairings to always occur between a matrix $Z$ and its conjugate transpose $Z^*$. As noted in Section 2, this restriction ensures that edge identifications in the corresponding surface diagrams are always "untwisted," preserving the orientation of the surface. Consequently, complex matrix calculations typically involve only orientable surfaces (like spheres or tori).

Real matrix models do not share this restriction. For a real Gaussian variable $X$, both $E[X^2]$ and $E[X^2]$ are non-zero. This allows pairings between two $X$ terms or two $X^T$ terms. In the topological representation, pairing two terms with the same orientation creates a "twisted edge identification."
*   **Untwisted identification:** Occurs when pairing $X$ with $X^T$ (indices match directly).
*   **Twisted identification:** Occurs when pairing $X$ with $X$ or $X^T$ with $X^T$ (indices match in reverse order).

As illustrated in **Figure 3**, a twisted identification reverses the local orientation of the surface. When enough twisted edges are present, the resulting surface becomes **nonorientable** (e.g., a projective plane or Klein bottle). This topological distinction is not merely geometric; it directly impacts the asymptotic behavior of the matrices. The contribution of each surface to the expected value scales with $N^{\chi}$, where $\chi$ is the Euler characteristic. Nonorientable surfaces generally have lower Euler characteristics than their orientable counterparts, meaning they contribute to lower-order terms in the $1/N$ expansion. However, to fully characterize "second-order freeness" (fluctuations), one must account for all surfaces, including nonorientable ones.

### Limitations of Prior Combinatorial Approaches

Before this work, combinatorial map theory provided a robust framework for encoding surface gluings using permutations. Standard references such as [1], [4], and [10] describe how to represent a graph embedded on a surface using a pair of permutations $(\sigma, \alpha)$:
*   $\sigma$ encodes the cyclic ordering of edge-ends around vertices.
*   $\alpha$ encodes the pairing of edge-ends into edges.

However, as stated in Section 3, "the encoding used in [1,4,10] depends on the surface having an orientation." These standard definitions assume a consistent counterclockwise ordering around vertices, which breaks down when twisted edges reverse the local orientation. Without modification, these tools cannot rigorously encode the nonorientable surfaces generated by real matrix calculations.

Furthermore, while the main paper [6] establishes the theoretical results regarding real second-order freeness, the proofs involve intricate index constraints and summations that are difficult to visualize. Readers attempting to verify the results or extend them to new matrix models lack a diagrammatic reference to check their intuition against. There is a need for a concrete mapping between the algebraic constraints (indices of matrix entries) and the topological objects (faces, edges, vertices) that respects the nonorientable nature of real ensembles.

### Positioning and Contribution of This Appendix

This document positions itself as a pedagogical and technical companion to the main paper [6]. It does not introduce new asymptotic theorems but rather provides the "pictures which motivated the various proofs" (Introduction). Its specific contributions to the field's methodology include:

1.  **Extension to Nonorientable Surfaces:** The paper outlines how to encode unoriented face gluings using **premaps**. Instead of a single surface, the method constructs an **orientable covering space** (Section 3). For every face in the original nonorientable surface, two preimage faces (front and back) are created in the covering space. Twisted gluings in the original surface correspond to connections between the front and back in the cover. This allows the use of permutation algebra even when the underlying topology is nonorientable.
2.  **Visualization of Index Constraints:** Section 2 demonstrates how matrix index constraints (e.g., $X_{ij} X_{kl}$) translate directly into edge gluings. **Figure 1** shows the initial faces constructed from a trace expression, while **Figure 4** shows the resulting surface after applying the Wick formula to pair edges. This bridges the gap between the algebraic expression $E(\text{tr}_\gamma(\dots))$ and the topological quantity $\chi(\gamma, \pi)$.
3.  **Clarification of Asymptotic Terms:** By linking the Euler characteristic $\chi$ to the power of $N$, the appendix explains why certain diagrams vanish in the large $N$ limit. Section 7 clarifies that highest-order terms correspond to spheres (orientable, $\chi=2$), while real matrix fluctuations require counting annular noncrossing diagrams on both possible relative orientations of faces (Figure 17).

In summary, this work resolves the technical difficulty of applying permutation-based map enumeration to real random matrices. It provides the necessary visual and combinatorial machinery to distinguish between orientable and nonorientable contributions, ensuring that calculations of asymptotic freeness and fluctuations are topologically complete.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
This paper constructs a combinatorial translation engine that converts algebraic expectations of real random matrices into topological surface counts using permutation groups. It solves the problem of nonorientable surfaces arising from real matrix pairings by doubling the topological space into a "front" and "back" cover, allowing standard permutation algebra to track orientation reversals explicitly.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The technical system operates as a four-stage pipeline that transforms a matrix expectation into a topological sum.
*   **Input Stage (Trace Expression):** The system accepts an expected value of a product of traces, such as $E(\text{tr}(XY_1 \dots))$, where the cyclic structure of the trace defines the initial polygonal faces.
*   **Geometric Construction Stage (Face Gluing):** The system applies the Wick formula to pair matrix entries, translating algebraic constraints into physical edge identifications (gluings) that may be either untwisted or twisted.
*   **Combinatorial Encoding Stage (Permutation Premaps):** The system encodes the resulting surface into a set of permutations acting on a doubled set of indices (front and back faces) to handle nonorientability without losing algebraic consistency.
*   **Output Stage (Topological Expansion):** The system calculates the Euler characteristic $\chi$ from the permutations to determine the power of $N$ scaling each term, summing over all valid surface configurations to produce the final expected value.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Face and Edge Construction:** We first detail how trace cycles become polygons and matrix pairings become edge gluings, distinguishing the geometric effect of transposes.
*   **Permutation Encoding for Orientable Surfaces:** We explain the standard map encoding ($\sigma, \alpha$) to establish the baseline method that fails for real matrices.
*   **The Orientable Covering Space:** We introduce the core innovation of doubling the surface into front and back copies to restore orientability for permutation tracking.
*   **Vertex and Euler Characteristic Calculation:** We derive the specific permutation formulas used to count vertices and compute the scaling exponent $\chi$.
*   **Matrix Model Constraints:** We specify how different ensembles (Ginibre, GOE) restrict the allowed pairings $\pi$ in the summation.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown
This appendix functions as a combinatorial topology manual, providing the explicit machinery to convert random matrix integrals into surface enumerations. The core idea is that every term in the expansion of an expected trace corresponds to a specific way of gluing polygons together, and the value of that term is determined by the topology of the resulting surface. We begin by defining the input algebraic object and tracing its transformation through the geometric and combinatorial layers.

#### Input Algebra and Face Construction
The process starts with an expression involving the expected value of a product of traces of random matrices, typically formatted as $E(\text{tr}_\gamma(X^{(\epsilon(1))}Y_1, \dots, X^{(\epsilon(n))}Y_n))$. In this expression, $\gamma$ is a permutation in the symmetric group $S_n$ that dictates the cyclic ordering of the trace components. The paper constructs a geometric face for each cycle in the permutation $\gamma$. For example, if $\gamma = (1, 2, 3, 4, 5)(6, 7, 8)$, the system constructs two faces: a pentagon and a triangle, as illustrated in **Figure 1**. The edges of these faces represent the random matrix entries (specifically instances of $X$ or $X^T$), while the vertices represent the deterministic matrices $Y_k$. The direction of the edges is critical: an occurrence of $X$ is assigned a direction (e.g., counterclockwise), while an occurrence of $X^T$ reverses that direction relative to the face boundary. This directional assignment is visualized in **Figure 1** using arrows labeled $\iota^+_k$ and $\iota^-_k$, which track the row and column indices of the matrix entries.

#### Wick Pairing and Edge Identification
To compute the expected value, the system applies the Wick formula to the Gaussian random variables comprising the entries of $X$. The Wick formula dictates that the expectation of a product of Gaussian variables is the sum over all possible pairings of those variables. In the topological model, a pairing corresponds to gluing two edges together. The nature of this gluing depends on the transpose status of the paired matrices. If an edge from $X$ is paired with an edge from $X^T$, the indices match directly (row to row, column to column), resulting in an **untwisted edge identification**. **Figure 2** depicts this scenario, where the term $f_{\iota^+_5 \iota^-_5}$ from $X^T$ is constrained to equal $f_{\iota^+_8 \iota^-_8}$ from $X$, preserving the local orientation of the surface. Conversely, if an edge from $X$ is paired with another edge from $X$ (or $X^T$ with $X^T$), the indices match in reverse order, creating a **twisted edge identification**. **Figure 3** shows this twisted case, where pairing $f_{\iota^+_1 \iota^-_1}$ with $f_{\iota^+_8 \iota^-_8}$ forces a reversal of orientation. This distinction is the primary technical hurdle: twisted identifications create nonorientable surfaces (like Möbius strips or Klein bottles), which standard map permutations cannot encode directly.

#### Standard Permutation Encoding (The Orientable Baseline)
To understand the solution, one must first understand the standard encoding for orientable surfaces, described in **Section 3**. For an orientable surface, the topology is captured by two permutations acting on the set of edge-ends. The permutation $\sigma$ (for *sommet*, vertex) encodes the cyclic ordering of edge-ends around each vertex in a counterclockwise direction. The permutation $\alpha$ (for *arête*, edge) encodes the pairing of edge-ends into edges, consisting of transpositions swapping the two ends of each edge. The faces of the surface are then enumerated by the cycles of the permutation $\phi := \sigma^{-1}\alpha^{-1}$. **Example 3.1** demonstrates this with **Figure 6**, where $\sigma = (1, 2, 3, 4)(5, 6)$ and $\alpha = (1, 5)(2, 3)(4, 6)$ yield face cycles $(1, 6, 3)(2)(4, 5)$. This system works perfectly for complex matrices or orientable surfaces because the cyclic order around every vertex is consistent. However, when a twisted edge is introduced, the local orientation flips, making a single global cyclic order $\sigma$ impossible to define consistently.

#### The Orientable Covering Space Construction
To resolve the nonorientability issue, the paper introduces an **orientable covering space** in **Section 3**. This is the central technical innovation of the appendix. The system constructs a double cover of the original surface by creating two preimages for every point: a "front" and a "back." In permutation terms, for every face cycle in $\gamma$, the system creates two cycles: $\gamma_+$ representing the front faces and $\gamma_-$ representing the back faces. The back faces are assigned the opposite orientation, meaning $\gamma_-$ is effectively the inverse orientation of $\gamma_+$. **Figure 8** visualizes this covering space for the gluing in **Figure 4**, showing how the original nonorientable surface unfolds into a larger orientable structure. When an edge identification is untwisted, the system glues the front edge to the front edge and the back edge to the back edge. When an edge identification is twisted, the system glues the front edge to the back edge and vice versa. This ensures that within the covering space, all gluings preserve orientation, allowing standard permutation algebra to function correctly.

#### Permutation Premaps and Vertex Calculation
In this doubled space, the face information is encoded by the permutation $\gamma_+\gamma_-^{-1}$, which satisfies the definition of a **premap**. The hyperedges (matrix pairings) are encoded by a permutation $\pi$ acting on the signed indices $\pm[n]$. A cycle in $\pi$ may contain both positive and negative integers, representing a connection between the front and back of the covering space. **Example 3.3** and **Figure 9** illustrate a nonorientable hypermap where twisting is represented by sign changes in the cycle. To find the vertices of the original surface, the system must trace the boundaries of the faces and hyperedges in the covering space. The paper derives a specific formula for the vertex permutation in **Section 3**:
$$
\text{Vertex Permutation} = \gamma_-^{-1}\pi\gamma_+
$$
This formula accounts for the transition between front and back faces. Specifically, because the matrix $Y_k$ appears counterclockwise on the front but clockwise on the back, the system must conjugate by $\gamma_-^{-1}$ to align the orientations correctly. The traces read off the vertices correspond to the cycles of the inverse of this permutation, $\gamma_-^{-1}\pi\gamma_+$. **Example 3.4** applies this to the surface in **Figure 4**, calculating $\gamma_-^{-1}\pi\gamma_+ = (1, -3, 6, -5, -7)(7, 5, -6, 3, -1)\dots$. The cycles of this permutation tell us which $Y$ matrices meet at each vertex and whether they appear as $Y_k$ or $Y_k^T$ (indicated by the sign of the index).

#### Euler Characteristic and Asymptotic Scaling
Once the faces, edges, and vertices are encoded as permutations, the system calculates the Euler characteristic $\chi$ to determine the magnitude of the term in the large $N$ limit. The paper provides the explicit formula for the Euler characteristic in **Section 3**:
$$
\chi(\gamma, \pi) := \frac{\#(\gamma_+\gamma_-^{-1})}{2} + \frac{\#(\pi)}{2} + \frac{\#(\gamma_-^{-1}\pi^{-1}\gamma_+)}{2} - |I|
$$
Here, $\#(\cdot)$ denotes the number of cycles in the permutation, and $|I|$ is the number of edge-ends. The division by 2 accounts for the doubling in the covering space. In **Example 3.4**, this calculation yields $\chi = 2 + 4 + 2 - 8 = 0$, identifying the surface as a Klein bottle. The asymptotic value of the matrix expectation scales as $N^{\chi - 2\#(\gamma)}$, as shown in the expansion formula in **Section 4**. This means surfaces with higher Euler characteristics (like spheres with $\chi=2$) dominate the sum as $N \to \infty$, while nonorientable surfaces (like the Klein bottle with $\chi=0$) contribute to lower-order corrections. This formula is the bridge that allows topological classification to predict algebraic asymptotics.

#### Matrix Model Specifics and Pairing Constraints
The final layer of the technical approach involves constraining the sum over permutations $\pi$ based on the specific random matrix ensemble. **Section 4** details these rules. For **Ginibre matrices** (real Gaussian entries), the sum runs over all surface gluings consistent with the edge directions. A pairing $\pi$ must respect the directionality: an untransposed matrix edge must pair with a transposed one to be untwisted (a $\ast$-pairing), unless a twisted identification is explicitly allowed by the model. For **GOE (Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble)** matrices, the symmetry $T = T^T$ allows edges to be glued in either direction, meaning both twisted and untwisted identifications are permissible without penalty. **Example 4.2** illustrates this with $E(\text{tr}(T^2))$, where the two edges of the face can be glued untwisted (forming a sphere, $\chi=2$) or twisted (forming a projective plane, $\chi=1$). The total expectation is the sum of contributions from both topologies: $1 + N^{-1}$. For **Wishart matrices**, the constraints involve the product structure $X^T X$, which imposes specific adjacency requirements on the faces. The general expansion formula in **Section 4** unifies these models:
$$
E\left(\text{tr}_\gamma\left(X^{(\epsilon(1))}_{\lambda_1}Y_1, \dots\right)\right) = \sum_{\pi \in PM_c(\pm[n])} N^{\chi(\gamma, \delta_\epsilon \pi \delta_\epsilon) - 2\#(\gamma)} f_c(\pi) E\left(\text{tr}_{\gamma_-^{-1}\delta_\epsilon \pi^{-1}\delta_\epsilon \gamma_+/2}(Y_1, \dots)\right)
$$
This equation encapsulates the entire technical approach: it sums over valid premaps $\pi$, weights them by their topology $N^\chi$, and computes the resulting trace of $Y$ matrices using the vertex permutation derived from the covering space construction.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This appendix introduces several conceptual breakthroughs that distinguish it from prior combinatorial approaches to random matrix theory. While the Technical Approach section detailed the mechanics of the permutation encoding, this section identifies what makes these contributions fundamentally novel rather than incrementally improved.

### 4.1 Orientable Covering Space as a Computational Tool

**Prior Limitation:** Standard combinatorial map theory, as established in references [1], [4], and [10], requires surfaces to have a consistent global orientation. The permutation $\sigma$ encoding vertex orderings assumes a counterclockwise direction that remains well-defined everywhere. When twisted edge identifications appear (as they must for real matrices), this assumption breaks down, and the standard encoding becomes undefined.

**Innovation:** This paper introduces the **orientable covering space construction** (Section 3) not merely as a topological curiosity, but as a computational device. By doubling every face into front ($\gamma_+$) and back ($\gamma_-$) copies, the system restores orientability at the cost of working in a larger space. Twisted gluings in the original surface become front-to-back connections in the cover, which preserve orientation within the cover itself.

**Significance:** This is a **fundamental innovation** rather than an incremental improvement. It enables the application of permutation algebra to nonorientable surfaces for the first time in the random matrix context. Without this construction, one would need entirely different combinatorial machinery for real versus complex ensembles. The covering space approach unifies the treatment: the same permutation formulas work for both cases, with nonorientability encoded implicitly through sign changes in the cycles. This explains why the Euler characteristic formula in Section 3 divides cycle counts by 2—the covering space has twice the faces, edges, and vertices of the original surface.

**Why It Matters:** This innovation directly enables the calculation of real matrix fluctuations. As shown in Example 4.2, the GOE calculation $E(\text{tr}(T^2)) = 1 + N^{-1}$ requires summing contributions from both a sphere ($\chi=2$) and a projective plane ($\chi=1$). The covering space construction makes it possible to enumerate both topologies within a single permutation framework, rather than requiring separate combinatorial theories for orientable and nonorientable cases.

### 4.2 Relative Face Orientation as a Source of Real-Specific Fluctuations

**Prior Understanding:** In complex matrix ensembles, the relative orientation of faces in a diagram is fixed by the requirement that pairings occur between $Z$ and $Z^*$. There is only one valid relative orientation for any given pairing configuration. This simplifies the enumeration of contributing diagrams significantly.

**Innovation:** This paper identifies that **real matrix fluctuations require counting diagrams under both possible relative orientations of faces**. Section 7 explicitly states: "In the real case, we must consider whether a permutation is noncrossing on both possible relative orientations of the two faces." Figure 17 demonstrates this by flipping one face from Figure 11 over its horizontal axis, revealing that a pairing which appears crossed in one orientation becomes noncrossing in the other.

**Significance:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in understanding real second-order freeness. The asymptotic covariance calculation in Example 7.2 yields $\lim_{N\to\infty} k_2(\text{Tr}(ZZZ^T Z^T), \text{Tr}(ZZ^T ZZ^T)) = 8$, which comes from counting 4 contributing pairings in each of the two relative orientations. If one only considered a single orientation (as would suffice for complex matrices), the result would be 4, not 8. This doubling of contributions is a direct consequence of the real ensemble's ability to support twisted identifications.

**Why It Matters:** This insight explains why real matrix fluctuations differ quantitatively from complex ones, even when the leading-order moments coincide. The need to consider both orientations adds combinatorial complexity that was not present in prior work on complex second-order freeness [5]. It also clarifies why the asymptotic freeness conditions for real matrices are more restrictive: diagrams must be noncrossing under both orientations simultaneously to contribute to certain terms.

### 4.3 Spoke Diagram Structure for Centred Alternating Algebras

**Prior Understanding:** Freeness conditions typically require that centred terms from alternating algebras cannot connect to their neighbors within a trace. Prior work established this for first-order freeness (single-trace expectations vanish asymptotically), but the diagrammatic structure of second-order freeness (two-trace covariances) was less clear for real ensembles.

**Innovation:** Section 8 reveals that surviving diagrams for centred alternating algebras must adopt a **spoke arrangement** (Figure 18), where terms connect across the two faces rather than within a single face. The paper states: "the terms must be connected in a sort of spoke arrangement... In the real case, both relative orientations of the two faces must be considered." Each spoke contributes a factor of $E(\text{tr}(A_k B_l^{(\pm 1)})) - E(\text{tr}(A_k))E(\text{tr}(B_l^{(\pm 1)}))$, which is precisely the covariance of the individual terms.

**Significance:** This is a **fundamental innovation** in characterizing real second-order freeness. The spoke structure explains why certain covariance terms factorize while others do not. Example 8.1 demonstrates this by calculating $\lim_{N\to\infty} k_2(\text{Tr}(\mathring{A}_1 \mathring{A}_2 \mathring{A}_3), \text{Tr}(\mathring{B}_1 \mathring{B}_2 \mathring{B}_3)) = 2070$ as a product of contributions from individual spokes (2, 3, and 345 respectively). The multiplicative structure emerges from the topological constraint that each spoke must be a connected, noncrossing, untwisted diagram on its own.

**Why It Matters:** This insight provides a diagrammatic criterion for determining when real matrix ensembles exhibit second-order freeness. Unlike first-order freeness, where the criterion is simply that centred terms cannot connect to neighbors, second-order freeness requires the more subtle spoke structure. This explains why some real matrix ensembles are asymptotically free at first order but exhibit nontrivial correlations at second order.

### 4.4 Euler Characteristic as the Unified Scaling Exponent

**Prior Understanding:** Prior work on genus expansions recognized that the power of $N$ in asymptotic expansions relates to surface topology. However, the treatment was often case-specific, with different formulas for different matrix ensembles.

**Innovation:** This paper establishes a **unified Euler characteristic formula** (Section 3) that applies across all real matrix models:
$$
\chi(\gamma, \pi) := \frac{\#(\gamma_+\gamma_-^{-1})}{2} + \frac{\#(\pi)}{2} + \frac{\#(\gamma_-^{-1}\pi^{-1}\gamma_+)}{2} - |I|
$$
The expansion formula in Section 4 then uses this $\chi$ consistently: $N^{\chi(\gamma, \delta_\epsilon \pi \delta_\epsilon) - 2\#(\gamma)}$. This formula correctly handles orientable surfaces (spheres, tori), nonorientable surfaces (projective planes, Klein bottles), and disconnected surfaces within a single framework.

**Significance:** This is an **incremental improvement** built on the fundamental innovations above. The formula itself follows from standard topological principles (Euler's formula $V - E + F = \chi$), but its expression in terms of premap permutations is novel. The significance lies in its universality: the same formula applies to Ginibre, GOE, and Wishart matrices, with ensemble-specific constraints entering only through the allowed set of permutations $\pi$.

**Why It Matters:** This unified formula enables systematic comparison across matrix models. For instance, Example 3.4 calculates $\chi = 0$ for a Klein bottle, while Example 4.1 calculates $\chi = 2$ for a sphere. The difference of 2 in $\chi$ corresponds to a factor of $N^{-2}$ in the asymptotic expansion, explaining why the sphere contribution dominates. This provides a clear topological interpretation of the $1/N$ expansion hierarchy.

### 4.5 Distinguishing Fundamental from Incremental Contributions

| Contribution | Type | Reason |
|--------------|------|--------|
| Orientable covering space for nonorientable surfaces | **Fundamental** | Enables permutation algebra where none existed before for real matrices |
| Relative face orientation counting | **Fundamental** | Explains quantitative differences between real and complex fluctuations |
| Spoke diagram structure | **Fundamental** | Provides diagrammatic criterion for second-order freeness in real case |
| Unified Euler characteristic formula | **Incremental** | Applies known topological principles in new permutation framework |
| Matrix model pairing constraints | **Incremental** | Adapts existing Wick formula machinery to specific ensembles |

The three fundamental innovations (covering space, relative orientation, spoke structure) work together to enable the calculation of real second-order freeness. The covering space makes nonorientable surfaces computable, the relative orientation counting captures the full set of real-specific contributions, and the spoke structure identifies which diagrams survive under freeness conditions. The incremental contributions (Euler formula, pairing constraints) apply these innovations to specific calculations but do not themselves represent new theoretical capabilities.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This paper is a theoretical mathematical appendix; it does not contain empirical experiments with datasets, training loops, or statistical baselines in the machine learning sense. Instead, validation is performed through **worked examples** and **explicit calculations** that verify the consistency of the proposed topological framework against known algebraic results. The "evaluation" here consists of checking whether the topological rules (surface gluings, Euler characteristics) correctly predict the algebraic outcomes (expected values, asymptotic limits) for specific matrix models.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology: Worked Examples as Verification

The paper validates its theoretical claims by constructing specific matrix expressions and solving them using the proposed topological permutation method. The methodology follows a consistent pipeline across all examples:
*   **Input:** A specific expected value expression, such as $E(\text{tr}(T^2))$ for GOE matrices or a covariance of traces for Ginibre matrices.
*   **Topological Construction:** Faces are constructed based on the trace cycles (e.g., **Figure 11** for Ginibre traces). Edge pairings are determined by the Wick formula.
*   **Metric:** The primary metric is the **Euler characteristic** $\chi$, calculated via the permutation formula:
    $$
    \chi(\gamma, \pi) := \frac{\#(\gamma_+\gamma_-^{-1})}{2} + \frac{\#(\pi)}{2} + \frac{\#(\gamma_-^{-1}\pi^{-1}\gamma_+)}{2} - |I|
    $$
    This $\chi$ determines the scaling power of the matrix size $N$. The predicted term scales as $N^{\chi - 2\#(\gamma)}$.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** The results are compared against standard algebraic expectations (e.g., knowing that $E(\text{tr}(T^2))$ for GOE should yield specific integer values) or against the complex case (where twisted edges are forbidden).

This approach functions as a "sanity check" for the theory: if the topological counting yields the correct algebraic values for known cases, the framework is validated for use in proving the broader theorems in the main paper [6].

### 5.2 Quantitative Results and Specific Calculations

The paper provides several precise numerical results that demonstrate the framework's accuracy. These results highlight the contribution of different surface topologies (orientable vs. nonorientable) to the final value.

*   **GOE Matrix Expectation (Section 4, Example 4.2):**
    For the expression $E(\text{tr}(T^2))$ where $T$ is a Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble matrix, the theory predicts contributions from two distinct surfaces:
    1.  **Sphere (Untwisted):** Euler characteristic $\chi=2$.
    2.  **Projective Plane (Twisted):** Euler characteristic $\chi=1$.
    The calculated result is:
    $$
    E(\text{tr}(T^2)) = 1 + N^{-1}
    $$
    The leading term $1$ corresponds to the sphere ($N^{2-2}=N^0$), and the correction term $N^{-1}$ corresponds to the projective plane ($N^{1-2}=N^{-1}$). This explicitly quantifies the cost of nonorientability in the $1/N$ expansion.

*   **Ginibre Asymptotic Covariance (Section 7, Example 7.2):**
    The paper calculates the asymptotic covariance between two trace expressions:
    $$
    \lim_{N\to\infty} k_2\left(\text{Tr}(ZZZ^T Z^T), \text{Tr}(ZZ^T ZZ^T)\right) = 8
    $$
    This result is derived by counting valid pairings on the surfaces shown in **Figure 11** and **Figure 17**.
    *   **Figure 11 Orientation:** 4 contributing pairings (annular noncrossing $\ast$-pairings).
    *   **Figure 17 Orientation:** 4 contributing pairings (where the second face is flipped).
    *   **Total:** $4 + 4 = 8$.
    This number confirms the claim in **Section 7** that real matrix fluctuations require counting diagrams under **both possible relative orientations** of the faces. If only one orientation were considered (as in the complex case), the result would incorrectly be 4.

*   **Second-Order Freeness Product (Section 8, Example 8.1):**
    For a complex expression involving alternating algebras (Ginibre, GOE, Wishart), the paper calculates the asymptotic cumulant:
    $$
    \lim_{N\to\infty} k_2\left(\text{Tr}(\mathring{A}_1 \mathring{A}_2 \mathring{A}_3), \text{Tr}(\mathring{B}_1 \mathring{B}_2 \mathring{B}_3)\right) = 2070
    $$
    This value is factored into contributions from individual "spokes" (connections between the two faces):
    *   **Ginibre Spoke ($A_1, B_2^T$):** Contribution = 2.
    *   **GOE Spoke ($A_2, B_3^T$):** Contribution = $5 - 1 \cdot 2 = 3$.
    *   **Wishart Spoke ($A_3, B_1^T$):** Contribution = $429 - 2 \cdot 42 = 345$.
    *   **Product:** $2 \times 3 \times 345 = 2070$.
    This result validates the **spoke diagram structure** proposed in **Section 8**, demonstrating that the total covariance factorizes into the product of covariances for each connecting spoke.

### 5.3 Assessment of Claims Support

The worked examples convincingly support the paper's central claims regarding the relationship between topology and asymptotics.
*   **Topological Scaling:** The GOE example ($1 + N^{-1}$) provides direct evidence that nonorientable surfaces (projective plane) contribute to lower-order terms in the expansion, consistent with their lower Euler characteristic.
*   **Real vs. Complex Distinction:** The covariance calculation (Result = 8) strongly supports the claim that real ensembles differ from complex ones due to relative face orientations. The doubling of the count (4 vs. 8) is a precise quantitative verification of the topological intuition provided in **Figure 17**.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

While not termed "ablation studies," the paper performs a form of sensitivity analysis by comparing different matrix models and topological constraints.

*   **Twisted vs. Untwisted Edges (Section 2):** The paper implicitly ablates the effect of twisted edges by contrasting real and complex cases. In complex matrices, twisted edges are forbidden ($E[Z^2]=0$), restricting surfaces to orientable ones. In real matrices, twisted edges are allowed, introducing nonorientable surfaces. The GOE result ($1 + N^{-1}$) shows the quantitative impact of "ablating" the orientability constraint: the $N^{-1}$ term disappears if twisted edges are forbidden.
*   **Relative Orientation Sensitivity (Section 7):** The covariance calculation acts as a robustness check on the diagram counting rules. By showing that flipping a face (**Figure 17**) reveals *new* valid pairings that were crossed in the original orientation (**Figure 11**), the paper demonstrates that the counting rule is sensitive to orientation. Ignoring this sensitivity would lead to a 50% error in the asymptotic covariance (4 vs. 8).
*   **Disconnected vs. Connected Terms (Section 5):** The paper distinguishes between moments (sum over all surfaces) and cumulants (sum over connected surfaces). **Figure 13** shows a disconnected surface contributing to a moment, while **Figure 14** shows a connected surface contributing to a cumulant. This distinction is crucial for the freeness results in Section 8, where only connected spoke diagrams survive.

### 5.5 Limitations and Missing Empirical Validation

It is critical to note what is **missing** from this analysis:
*   **No Finite-$N$ Simulations:** The paper provides asymptotic limits ($\lim_{N\to\infty}$). It does not show how quickly the values converge for finite $N$ (e.g., $N=100$ or $N=1000$). A reader cannot assess the practical utility of these approximations for small matrix sizes.
*   **No Error Bounds:** There are no bounds provided on the remainder terms (e.g., $O(N^{-2})$). The analysis stops at identifying the leading and next-to-leading order terms.
*   **Reliance on Combinatorial Inspection:** The counts (e.g., "4 contributing pairings") are derived by inspection of diagrams. For larger matrices or more complex traces, this inspection becomes intractable without algorithmic assistance, which is not provided.

In summary, the "experiments" are rigorous mathematical verifications that confirm the internal consistency of the topological framework. They successfully demonstrate *why* real matrix fluctuations differ from complex ones (via twisted edges and relative orientations) but do not provide empirical evidence of convergence rates or numerical stability.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

This appendix provides powerful topological machinery for understanding real matrix ensembles, but the approach carries inherent constraints that limit its applicability and practical utility. Understanding these limitations is essential for readers attempting to extend the framework to new problems or apply it beyond the specific cases illustrated.

### 6.1 Dependence on Gaussian Structure

The entire topological framework rests on the **Wick formula**, which applies specifically to Gaussian random variables. Section 2 states: "We apply the Wick formula (see [4], p. 164) to the random variables. The entries of X are independent random variables, so for a given pairing, the indices on the paired random matrix entries must be equal."

This assumption creates several constraints:

*   **Gaussian-only applicability:** The pairing-based surface construction works because Gaussian expectations factor into sums over pairings. For non-Gaussian ensembles (e.g., matrices with heavy-tailed entries or bounded support), higher-order cumulants of individual entries would contribute, requiring hyperedges with more than two ends that are not fully addressed in the current framework.
*   **Independence requirement:** The Wick formula application assumes independent matrix entries. Correlated entry structures (beyond the transpose relationships explicitly modeled) would require modifications to the edge identification rules that are not discussed.
*   **Explicit acknowledgment:** The paper limits its examples to Ginibre, GOE, and Wishart matrices—all Gaussian-based ensembles. Section 4 states: "The expansion satisfied by all the matrix models in the main paper" refers specifically to these models, not general random matrix ensembles.

This is a **fundamental limitation** rather than a minor constraint. The topological interpretation of matrix calculations as surface gluings is intimately tied to the Gaussian pairing structure. Extending this to non-Gaussian cases would require rethinking the core correspondence between algebraic expectations and topological objects.

### 6.2 Asymptotic Focus Without Convergence Guarantees

The framework is designed for **large $N$ asymptotics**, but provides no quantitative bounds on convergence rates. Section 7 discusses "Genus Expansions and Asymptotics" and presents results as limits:

> $$
> \lim_{N\to\infty} k_2\left(\text{Tr}\left(ZZZ^T Z^T\right), \text{Tr}\left(ZZ^T ZZ^T\right)\right) = 8
> $$

However, the paper does not address:

*   **Finite-$N$ accuracy:** There is no discussion of how large $N$ must be for the asymptotic values to provide useful approximations. For practical applications with matrices of size $N=100$ or $N=1000$, the $O(N^{-1})$ and $O(N^{-2})$ corrections may still be significant.
*   **Error bounds:** The expansion formula in Section 4 includes terms weighted by $N^{\chi - 2\#(\gamma)}$, but no remainder bounds are provided. A reader cannot assess whether truncating the sum at a certain Euler characteristic yields a controlled approximation.
*   **Subleading topology:** While the paper identifies that nonorientable surfaces contribute to lower-order terms (e.g., the projective plane contribution $N^{-1}$ in Example 4.2), it does not systematically enumerate all surfaces at each order. For complex calculations, the number of contributing topologies grows rapidly, and the paper provides no guidance on when truncation is safe.

This limitation is particularly significant for **applied work**. Theoretical random matrix results often require substantial matrix sizes before asymptotic behavior dominates, and without convergence bounds, practitioners cannot determine whether the topological predictions are reliable for their specific problem scale.

### 6.3 Combinatorial Complexity and Scalability

The permutation-based encoding, while elegant, introduces **computational complexity** that scales poorly with problem size. Several factors contribute to this:

*   **Covering space doubling:** The orientable covering space construction requires working with signed indices $\pm[n]$ instead of $[n]$. Section 3 explains: "For each face of our original surface, we construct two preimage faces, which we can think of as the front and the back." This doubles the permutation domain, increasing the computational cost of cycle counting and permutation multiplication.
*   **Permutation enumeration:** The expansion formula sums over $\pi \in PM_c(\pm[n])$, the set of premaps on signed indices. The size of this set grows factorially with the number of edges. For a trace product with $n$ matrix occurrences, the number of pairings to consider is proportional to $(2n-1)!!$, making exhaustive enumeration intractable for large $n$.
*   **Manual inspection reliance:** The worked examples in Sections 4, 7, and 8 rely on **diagram inspection** rather than algorithmic enumeration. Example 7.2 states: "We may calculate the asymptotic covariance by counting the annular noncrossing $\ast$-pairings on both relative orientations of the faces, which we may do by inspection in this case." The phrase "in this case" implicitly acknowledges that inspection does not scale.

For the simple examples in this appendix (4-8 matrix occurrences per trace), manual counting is feasible. However, for realistic problems involving longer trace products or more matrices, the combinatorial explosion becomes prohibitive. The paper provides **no algorithmic guidance** for automated enumeration of valid pairings or efficient computation of the Euler characteristic from permutations.

### 6.4 Scope Restrictions: First and Second Order Only

The appendix focuses exclusively on **first- and second-order freeness**, as stated in Section 1: "those considered in first- and second-order freeness." This creates several gaps:

*   **Higher-order cumulants:** The framework does not address third-order or higher cumulants (e.g., $k_3(\text{tr}(A), \text{tr}(B), \text{tr}(C))$). While Section 5 mentions that "cumulants are sums over only connected surfaces," it does not provide the topological characterization for cumulants of three or more traces. The spoke diagram structure in Section 8 is specific to two-trace expressions.
*   **Multi-face topology:** Section 7 discusses "two-face maps" for covariances and mentions that these correspond to "annular noncrossing diagrams." However, the topological classification for three or more faces (which would be required for higher-order cumulants) is not developed. The classification theorem cited in Section 7 applies to "compact connected surfaces" but does not address surfaces with three or more boundary components.
*   **Main paper dependency:** The introduction explicitly states this is an **appendix** to [6]: "This document is intended as an appendix to [6]... in which we attempt to provide some intuition for how the topological constructions are represented in the permutations." Many technical details—particularly regarding the precise conditions for second-order freeness—are deferred to the main paper. Readers cannot fully understand the freeness results without consulting [6].

This scope restriction means the appendix provides **incomplete guidance** for researchers interested in higher-order fluctuations or more complex freeness structures. The topological machinery is presented as sufficient for second-order analysis but is not validated or extended beyond that regime.

### 6.5 Nonorientable Surface Enumeration Challenges

While the covering space construction enables encoding of nonorientable surfaces, it introduces **ambiguities and complexities** that are not fully resolved:

*   **Orientation sensitivity:** Section 7 states: "In the real case, we must consider whether a permutation is noncrossing on both possible relative orientations of the two faces." This requirement to check **both orientations** doubles the diagram enumeration workload compared to the complex case. Figure 17 demonstrates that flipping a face reveals different valid pairings, but the paper does not provide a systematic method for identifying when orientations yield distinct contributions versus redundant ones.
*   **Twisted edge identification tracking:** The distinction between twisted and untwisted edges is encoded through sign changes in the permutation cycles (Section 3: "paired integers of opposite signs represent a twisted one"). However, tracking which sign patterns correspond to which topologies requires careful bookkeeping. Example 3.4 calculates $\chi = 0$ for a Klein bottle, but the paper does not provide a general formula for predicting $\chi$ from the sign pattern without performing the full permutation calculation.
*   **Disconnected component handling:** Section 5 distinguishes between moments (all surfaces) and cumulants (connected surfaces only), but the criteria for determining connectivity in the covering space are not algorithmically specified. Figure 13 shows a disconnected surface, but determining connectivity for arbitrary permutations requires tracing through the cycle structure manually.

These challenges mean that while the framework is **theoretically complete** for nonorientable surfaces, it is **practically cumbersome** for all but the simplest cases. The elegance of the permutation encoding is offset by the complexity of interpreting the results topologically.

### 6.6 Summary of Trade-offs

| Aspect | Benefit | Cost |
|--------|---------|------|
| **Covering space construction** | Enables permutation algebra for nonorientable surfaces | Doubles the permutation domain ($\pm[n]$ vs $[n]$) |
| **Topological interpretation** | Provides geometric intuition for asymptotic scaling | Requires manual diagram inspection for enumeration |
| **Unified Euler formula** | Single formula applies across matrix models | No convergence bounds for finite $N$ |
| **Relative orientation counting** | Captures real-specific fluctuation contributions | Doubles the diagram counting workload |
| **Gaussian Wick pairing** | Clean correspondence between pairings and edges | Restricts applicability to Gaussian ensembles only |

The fundamental trade-off is between **theoretical completeness** and **practical tractability**. The framework successfully encodes all topological contributions to real matrix expectations, including nonorientable surfaces and orientation-sensitive pairings. However, extracting concrete results requires combinatorial work that scales poorly and provides no guarantees about approximation quality for finite matrix sizes.

For researchers seeking to apply this framework, the key limitation is that the appendix provides **proof intuition** rather than **computational tools**. The diagrams and examples illustrate why the main paper's theorems hold, but do not provide efficient methods for computing new results. Extending this work to practical applications would require developing algorithmic enumeration methods, convergence bounds, and potentially relaxing the Gaussian assumption—none of which are addressed in this document.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This appendix fundamentally alters how researchers approach random matrix calculations for real ensembles. By establishing a rigorous topological correspondence for nonorientable surfaces, it removes the barrier that previously forced real matrix theory to diverge from the well-trodden path of complex matrix analysis. The implications extend beyond mere calculation; they provide a new language for defining and verifying asymptotic freeness in real settings.

### 7.1 Transforming the Proof Landscape

Prior to this work, proofs involving real random matrices often required handling index constraints algebraically, which obscured the geometric intuition available in the complex case. This appendix shifts the paradigm from **algebraic index manipulation** to **topological surface enumeration**.

*   **Unification of Ensembles:** The orientable covering space construction (Section 3) allows real matrix models (Ginibre, GOE, Wishart) to be treated with the same permutation machinery used for complex models. Instead of developing separate combinatorial theories for orientable and nonorientable cases, researchers can now use a single framework where nonorientability is encoded via sign changes in the permutation cycles (e.g., $\gamma_-$ vs $\gamma_+$).
*   **Visual Verification of Asymptotics:** The connection between the Euler characteristic $\chi$ and the scaling power of $N$ (Section 4) provides a visual heuristic for determining the order of magnitude of any term. Instead of computing complex integrals, a researcher can draw the surface gluing (as in **Figure 4** or **Figure 11**) and calculate $\chi$ to know if a term vanishes as $N \to \infty$.
    $$
    \text{Term Magnitude} \propto N^{\chi(\gamma, \pi) - 2\#(\gamma)}
    $$
    This formula implies that any surface with $\chi < 2$ (for single traces) is subleading. This transforms proof verification into a topological counting problem.
*   **Rigorous Definition of Real Freeness:** Section 8 establishes that second-order freeness in real matrices is not just about vanishing cumulants, but about the specific **spoke diagram structure** (**Figure 18**). This provides a concrete criterion for verifying freeness: if a diagram cannot be drawn as a set of non-interacting spokes connecting two faces, the algebras are not asymptotically free at second order.

### 7.2 Pathways for Future Research

The framework established here opens several specific avenues for theoretical and computational development. These directions address the limitations identified in Section 6 while leveraging the core innovations of the covering space and permutation encoding.

#### 7.2.1 Algorithmic Enumeration of Premaps
Currently, the examples in this paper (e.g., **Example 7.2**) rely on manual inspection to count valid pairings. As the number of matrix occurrences $n$ grows, the number of permutations $\pi \in PM_c(\pm[n])$ grows factorially.
*   **Research Goal:** Develop algorithms to generate and filter premaps $\pi$ based on the constraints of specific matrix models (e.g., enforcing $\ast$-pairings for Ginibre matrices).
*   **Why It Matters:** Automating the calculation of $\chi(\gamma, \pi)$ would allow researchers to compute higher-order corrections (e.g., $O(N^{-2})$ terms) that are currently intractable by hand. This would extend the utility of the expansion formula in **Section 4** beyond the leading order.
*   **Technical Challenge:** The algorithm must efficiently check the "noncrossing" condition on **both** relative orientations of the faces (Section 7), as required for real matrices.

#### 7.2.2 Extension to Non-Gaussian Ensembles
The current framework relies on the Wick formula, which assumes Gaussian entries (Section 2). However, the paper briefly mentions hypermaps in **Section 3** (**Example 3.2**), where edges can have more than two ends.
*   **Research Goal:** Generalize the edge identification rules to accommodate higher-order cumulants of matrix entries. In a non-Gaussian ensemble, a single "hyperedge" might connect more than two face boundaries, corresponding to a non-zero cumulant $k_m(X_{ij}, \dots)$.
*   **Why It Matters:** This would broaden the applicability of the topological method beyond Ginibre/GOE/Wishart matrices to heavy-tailed or bounded random matrix ensembles, which are common in robust statistics and physics.
*   **Technical Challenge:** The Euler characteristic formula would need modification to account for hyperedges that do not correspond to simple pairwise gluings. The current formula assumes $\pi$ is a pairing (cycles of length 2).

#### 7.2.3 Higher-Order Cumulants and Multi-Face Topology
This appendix focuses on first- and second-order freeness (single and double traces). Section 5 notes that cumulants correspond to connected surfaces, but does not fully characterize surfaces with three or more boundary components.
*   **Research Goal:** Classify the topological structures corresponding to third-order cumulants $k_3(\text{tr}(A), \text{tr}(B), \text{tr}(C))$. These would correspond to surfaces with three boundary components (pairs of pants, etc.).
*   **Why It Matters:** Understanding higher-order fluctuations is crucial for characterizing the full distribution of linear spectral statistics, not just the mean and variance.
*   **Technical Challenge:** The "spoke diagram" logic in **Section 8** is specific to two faces. A new structural characterization is needed for $k \geq 3$ faces to determine which diagrams survive asymptotically.

#### 7.2.4 Numerical Verification of Asymptotics
Section 5 highlights the lack of finite-$N$ simulations.
*   **Research Goal:** Conduct Monte Carlo simulations to verify the convergence rates predicted by the Euler characteristic expansion. For example, verify that the error in the covariance approximation scales as $O(N^{-1})$ as predicted by the projective plane contribution in **Example 4.2**.
*   **Why It Matters:** This would provide empirical bounds on how large $N$ must be for the asymptotic freeness results to be practically useful in applications like signal processing or financial modeling.

### 7.3 Practical Integration and Reproducibility

For researchers intending to apply these methods to their own work, the following guidelines outline when and how to integrate this topological framework.

#### 7.3.1 When to Use This Method
*   **Real Matrix Ensembles:** Use this framework specifically when dealing with **real** random matrices (GOE, real Ginibre, real Wishart). For complex matrices, standard orientable map theory (references [1], [5]) is sufficient and simpler.
*   **Second-Order Analysis:** This method is essential when calculating **fluctuations** (covariances) rather than just expected values. First-order moments often depend only on planar (sphere) diagrams, but second-order terms require counting annular and nonorientable surfaces (**Section 7**).
*   **Freeness Verification:** Use the spoke diagram criterion (**Section 8**) to test if two algebras of real matrices are asymptotically free at second order. If you can construct a connected, noncrossing diagram that is *not* a spoke arrangement, the algebras are likely not free.

#### 7.3.2 Implementation Checklist
To reproduce the results or apply the method to a new expression:
1.  **Construct Faces:** Draw a polygon for each trace in the expression. Label edges with matrix indices and orientations (counterclockwise for $X$, clockwise for $X^T$) as shown in **Figure 1**.
2.  **Define Permutations:**
    *   Set $\gamma$ based on the trace cycles.
    *   Construct $\gamma_+$ and $\gamma_-$ for the covering space (Section 3).
3.  **Enumerate Pairings:** Generate valid pairings $\pi$ consistent with the matrix model (e.g., $\ast$-pairings for Ginibre).
4.  **Calculate Topology:** Compute the vertex permutation $\gamma_-^{-1}\pi\gamma_+$ and the Euler characteristic:
    $$
    \chi(\gamma, \pi) = \frac{\#(\gamma_+\gamma_-^{-1})}{2} + \frac{\#(\pi)}{2} + \frac{\#(\gamma_-^{-1}\pi^{-1}\gamma_+)}{2} - |I|
    $$
5.  **Check Orientations:** For covariances, repeat the counting with one face flipped (relative orientation change) as demonstrated in **Figure 17**. Sum the contributions from both orientations.
6.  **Sum Contributions:** Weight each term by $N^{\chi - 2\#(\gamma)}$ and sum to get the final expectation.

#### 7.3.3 Common Pitfalls
*   **Ignoring Twisted Edges:** Do not assume all pairings are untwisted. In real matrices, pairing $X$ with $X$ creates a twisted edge (**Figure 3**), which reduces $\chi$ and changes the surface topology.
*   **Single Orientation Counting:** Do not count diagrams in only one relative orientation. As shown in **Example 7.2**, ignoring the flipped orientation (**Figure 17**) leads to a 50% error in the asymptotic covariance (4 vs 8).
*   **Disconnected Cumulants:** When calculating cumulants, ensure the surface is connected (**Section 5**). Disconnected surfaces contribute to moments but cancel out in cumulants via the inclusion-exclusion principle (**Section 6**).
