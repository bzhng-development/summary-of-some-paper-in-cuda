## 1. Executive Summary
To address the fragmented and opaque understanding of language model capabilities and risks, this paper introduces Holistic Evaluation of Language Models (HELM), a standardized benchmark evaluating 30 prominent models (including OPT, BLOOM, and GPT-3 variants) across 42 scenarios using 7 distinct metrics (accuracy, calibration, robustness, fairness, bias, toxicity, and efficiency). This holistic approach dramatically improves evaluation coverage from an average of 17.9% to 96.0% under consistent conditions, surfacing 25 top-level findings such as the superior performance of instruction-tuned models like `text-davinci-002` over significantly larger non-tuned models. By releasing all raw prompts, completions, and a modular toolkit, HELM establishes a living benchmark designed to orient future development towards transparency and societal benefit rather than accuracy alone.

## 2. Context and Motivation

The development of Holistic Evaluation of Language Models (HELM) is driven by a critical misalignment between the rapid advancement of language model (LM) technology and the community's ability to understand it. To understand why HELM was necessary, we must examine the state of AI benchmarking prior to this work, the specific nature of the "foundation model" shift, and the limitations of existing evaluation methodologies.

### The Fragmented Evaluation Landscape

Before HELM, the evaluation of language models was characterized by significant inconsistency and opacity. While numerous benchmarks existed, they were not designed to facilitate direct, head-to-head comparisons across the full spectrum of available models. The authors illustrate this fragmentation in **Figure 4**, which maps which models were evaluated on which scenarios prior to this work. 

In the "Previous work" view of **Figure 4**, the matrix of models versus scenarios is sparse. The paper notes that prior to HELM, models were evaluated on an average of just 17.9% of the core scenarios identified by the authors. More critically, some prominent models did not share a single evaluated scenario in common. This fragmentation creates two major problems:
1.  **Incomparability:** Without evaluating models on the same tasks under the same conditions, it is impossible to determine if Model A is genuinely more capable than Model B, or if it simply performed better on the specific datasets its creators chose to report.
2.  **Selection Bias:** Model developers could selectively report results on scenarios where their model performed well, obscuring weaknesses in other areas.

This lack of standardization extends to *how* models are evaluated. For example, on the `HellaSwag` scenario, some prior works reported fine-tuned accuracies (e.g., for `T5 (11B)`), while others reported prompting accuracies (e.g., for `davinci (175B)`). As noted in **Section 1.1**, even when works report results through few-shot prompting, exact details (like the number of examples) can vary, leading to "wild swings in accuracies (e.g. 30% to 80% for the same (model, scenario))".

### The Narrow Focus on Accuracy

Beyond fragmentation, prior benchmarks suffered from a narrow definition of success. Traditional NLP benchmarks, such as `SuperGLUE` or `SQuAD`, typically assign a single score to a model, usually based on **accuracy** (or a closely related metric like F1 score). 

**Figure 2** contrasts this prior approach with HELM's philosophy. On the left side ("Previous work"), benchmarks are depicted as collections of datasets, each with a standard task framing and a canonical metric, usually accuracy. This approach treats other desiderata—such as fairness, robustness, or toxicity—as secondary concerns, often relegated to bespoke, separate datasets if they are evaluated at all.

The authors argue this is insufficient for foundation models. Because these models are deployed in high-stakes, real-world applications, societal values beyond accuracy must be measured. A model that is highly accurate but consistently generates toxic content or fails on specific demographic groups is not "better" in a holistic sense. HELM posits that **multi-metric measurement** is essential: "Societally beneficial systems reflect many values, not just accuracy" (**Section 1**). By measuring 7 metric categories (accuracy, calibration, robustness, fairness, bias, toxicity, and efficiency) for each scenario, HELM forces trade-offs to be exposed rather than hidden.

### The Foundation Model Shift

The motivation for HELM is also rooted in the technological shift toward **foundation models**. As defined in **Section 1**, foundation models are models trained on broad data at immense scale that can be adapted to a wide range of downstream scenarios. 

Prior evaluation paradigms were often designed for task-specific models (e.g., a model built solely for sentiment analysis). However, foundation models act as "general-purpose text interfaces" (**Section 1**). This generality demands a different evaluation strategy:
*   **Adaptation:** Since foundation models are adapted (e.g., via prompting) rather than trained from scratch for each task, the evaluation must standardize the adaptation procedure. HELM chooses **few-shot prompting** as the standard adaptation method to ensure comparisons reflect the model's inherent capabilities rather than differences in fine-tuning resources.
*   **Coverage:** Because foundation models can theoretically be applied to a "vast expanse of scenarios," evaluation must acknowledge what is *not* covered. HELM introduces a **taxonomy** of scenarios and metrics (**Figure 2**, right side) to explicitly state the design space. This allows the benchmark to make deliberate selections while recognizing incompleteness, rather than pretending a specific set of datasets represents the entire field.

### Positioning Relative to Existing Work

HELM positions itself not as a replacement for existing datasets, but as a unifying layer that standardizes how those datasets are used. The authors explicitly acknowledge that HELM builds on many prior works, including `BIG-Bench`, the `EleutherAI LM Evaluation Harness`, and `SuperGLUE` (**Section 1.3**). 

The key differentiator is the **top-down design process**. While benchmarks like `BIG-Bench` aggregate a large collection of tasks, HELM begins with a taxonomy of tasks, domains, and languages (**Section 3.1**). This allows for systematic selection based on coverage and feasibility, making explicit what is missing (e.g., question answering for neglected English dialects). 

Furthermore, HELM emphasizes **standardization of the object of evaluation**. As stated in **Section 1**, "Our object of evaluation is the language model, not a scenario-specific system." This means controlling for adaptation strategies so that differences in performance can be attributed to the model itself, not to engineering tricks specific to one benchmark. By evaluating 30 models from 12 organizations under these standardized conditions, HELM aims to transform evaluation from a fragmented, accuracy-centric exercise into a transparent, holistic practice that orients development toward societal benefit.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper presents a benchmarking framework rather than a new model architecture, with the core idea being that language model evaluation requires standardized primitives (scenarios, adaptation procedures, and metrics) applied consistently across all models to enable meaningful comparison.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

HELM is a standardized evaluation pipeline that takes language models as input, runs them through carefully defined test scenarios using consistent prompting strategies, and outputs performance scores across seven different quality dimensions. The system solves the problem of incomparable model evaluations by enforcing uniform testing conditions—every model answers the same questions, with the same number of example hints, scored by the same measurement rules—creating a level playing field that reveals genuine capability differences rather than evaluation methodology differences.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The HELM evaluation pipeline consists of four major components connected in sequence. First, the **Scenario Repository** stores 42 evaluation scenarios (16 core + 26 targeted), each defined as a structured collection of test instances with inputs and reference answers. Second, the **Adaptation Engine** transforms each raw language model into a task-specific system by constructing prompts with 5 in-context training examples plus the test instance, using model-specific tokenizers and context window constraints. Third, the **Model Interface Layer** handles communication with 30 different language models across three access types (open, limited-access, closed), managing API calls, decoding parameters, and response parsing. Fourth, the **Metric Computation Module** calculates 7 metric categories (accuracy, calibration, robustness, fairness, bias, toxicity, efficiency) from the model completions and their log probabilities, producing a score matrix for each model. Information flows from scenarios through adaptation to models, then back through metric computation to produce final results stored in a public database.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

This is a benchmarking framework paper that introduces a standardized evaluation methodology rather than a new machine learning model, with the fundamental innovation being the systematic application of multiple metrics across multiple scenarios under identical adaptation conditions for all models.

**Scenario Definition and Structure**

A scenario in HELM is formally defined as a structured collection of test instances, where each instance consists of an input string and a list of reference strings annotated with properties relevant for evaluation (Section 2.1). Each scenario is taxonomized along three dimensions: task (what the model should do, such as question answering or summarization), domain (the type of data, characterized by what genre, when time period, and who demographic group), and language (the language or language variety, such as English or African American English) (Section 3.1, Figure 8). The 16 core scenarios are selected to prioritize coverage across these three dimensions while focusing on user-facing tasks that confer direct social impact, including 9 question answering scenarios (NaturalQuestions open/closed-book, NarrativeQA, QuAC, BoolQ, HellaSwag, OpenBookQA, TruthfulQA, MMLU), 2 information retrieval scenarios (MS MARCO regular and TREC), 2 summarization scenarios (CNN/DailyMail, XSUM), 1 sentiment analysis scenario (IMDB), 1 toxicity detection scenario (CivilComments), and 1 miscellaneous text classification scenario (RAFT) (Section 3, Table 4).

For each core scenario, HELM measures 7 metric categories, producing a 16 × 7 = 112 possible scenario-metric pairs, of which 98 pairs (87.5%) are actually measured as shown in Table 4 (Section 1.1). The remaining 14 pairs are not measured either because they are not well-defined (for example, toxicity cannot be measured for scenarios that do not involve text generation) or because the authors are concerned about measurement validity (for example, fairness perturbations for long-form generation in summarization). In addition to the 16 core scenarios, HELM includes 26 targeted scenarios organized into 7 evaluation categories (language, knowledge, reasoning, memorization and copyright, disinformation, bias, and toxicity) that provide deeper analysis of specific capabilities and risks beyond what the core scenarios capture (Section 5).

**Adaptation via Few-Shot Prompting**

Adaptation is the procedure that transforms a raw language model into a system that can make predictions on test instances, and HELM standardizes this procedure across all models using few-shot prompting with 5 in-context examples (Section 2.2, Section 7). The language model is treated as a black box that takes as input a prompt string along with decoding parameters (such as temperature) and outputs a completion string along with log probabilities of the prompt and completion, without assuming access to internal model activations or training data (Section 2.2). For each evaluation instance, the adaptation engine constructs a prompt that includes up to 5 in-context training examples selected to ensure class coverage for classification problems, followed by the test instance itself (Section 7, Figure 23).

The in-context examples are fixed across all evaluation instances for a given scenario, which contrasts with prior work that selected different examples for each test instance, because fixing examples better reflects the realities of few-shot adaptation and allows measurement of variance due to example selection (Section 7). To measure this variance, all experiments are re-run 3 times with different random seeds for in-context example selection, and the paper reports that the median accuracy range across scenarios is below 0.03 for 24 out of 28 models, though some scenarios like NaturalQuestions (open-book) show higher variance with a median range of 0.1730 (Section 8.2, Figure 31). For scenarios where 5 examples do not fit within the model's context window, the number of examples is adaptively reduced, and the paper ablates the number of in-context examples across the set {0, 1, 2, 4, 8, 16} to test how sample efficiency influences performance (Section 8.2, Figure 32).

For multiple choice scenarios, HELM implements three adaptation strategies that significantly affect accuracy results (Section 7, Section 8.2). The **separate approach** scores each answer choice independently by concatenating it with the question and computing its probability according to the language model, which is the method employed by Brown et al. (2020) for GPT-3. The **joint approach** concatenates all answer choices with the question to form a single prompt (for example, "&lt;question> A. &lt;choice 1> B. &lt;choice 2> Answer:") and the language model predicts the choice index, which resembles how multiple choice questions appear on exams. The **separate-calibrated approach** further calibrates the model-assigned probabilities by the probability the model assigns to the answer choice when presented standalone. The paper finds that the method that maximizes accuracy is largely determined by the scenario rather than being consistent across models, and for HellaSwag specifically, OPT (175B) achieves 79.1% accuracy with the separate method but only 30.2% with the joint method, demonstrating that adaptation choices can produce accuracy swings of nearly 50 percentage points for the same model-scenario pair (Section 8.2, Figure 33).

Decoding parameters are standardized across models to ensure comparability, with temperature set to 0 for scenarios where the ideal model behavior is to generate a very short sequence matching the correct reference (which corresponds to selecting the argmax under the model's distribution), and temperature set to 0.3 for longer-form generation scenarios like text summarization (Section 7, Table 7, Section J.3). The stop sequence is generally set to the newline character "\n" to terminate completion, with max tokens set as a fail-safe based on the longest reference's length for each scenario to avoid excessive token generation (Section J.3).

**Metric Computation**

HELM computes 7 metric categories for each scenario, and each category contains specific quantitative functions that operationalize abstract desiderata into measurable scores (Section 4).

**Accuracy** serves as an umbrella term for the standard correctness metric for each scenario, with the specific metric varying by scenario type: exact match for text classification tasks where the model generation must match the correct reference exactly as strings, F1 score for word overlap in question answering scenarios like NaturalQuestions, MRR (Mean Reciprocal Rank) and NDCG (Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain) for information retrieval, and ROUGE score for summarization (Section 4.3, Section C.1). The paper emphasizes that accuracy is measured averaged over test instances, which means minority subpopulations could experience low accuracy despite high average accuracy, motivating the need for fairness metrics that measure performance on specific subgroups (Section 4.3).

**Calibration** measures whether a model's predicted probabilities meaningfully reflect the likelihood of correctness, which is critical for high-stakes settings where model uncertainty should inform whether humans intervene (Section 4.4). A model is calibrated if, when it predicts 1,000 sentences are toxic each with probability 0.7, approximately 700 of them are actually toxic. Calibration is quantified using Expected Calibration Error (ECE), which measures the difference between the model's predicted probability and the fraction of times the model is correct across probability bins. Formally, with $m$ bins, the estimated ECE is:

$$\widehat{\text{ECE}}_m = \sum_{i=1}^{m} \frac{n_i}{n} |\hat{p}_i - \hat{a}_i|$$

where $n_i$ is the number of examples in bin $i$, $n$ is the total number of examples, $\hat{p}_i$ is the average predicted probability in bin $i$, and $\hat{a}_i$ is the accuracy in bin $i$ (Section C.2). HELM uses 10 bins with an equal number of probabilities per bin (uniform-mass binning) by default. The paper also measures selective classification, which evaluates accuracy for the $C$-fraction of examples the model assigns the highest probability, reporting both selective classification accuracy for $C = 0.1$ and the area under the coverage-accuracy curve (Section 4.4, Figure 17).

**Robustness** measures the worst-case performance of a model across transformations of each instance, capturing how stable predictions are under small perturbations that arise in real use cases like typos (Section 4.5). Given a basic metric $m$ that quantifies prediction quality on instance $x$, and a set of transformations $T_1, \ldots, T_k$ that map an instance to a perturbed instance, robustness is computed as:

$$\text{Robustness} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \min_{j} m(\text{model}(T_j(x_i)), y_i)$$

where higher $m$ corresponds to better performance (Section C.3). HELM focuses on two notions of transformations: invariance (semantics-preserving perturbations like lowercase conversion, contractions/expansions, misspellings with probability 0.1, and extra spaces) and equivariance (semantics-altering perturbations using Contrast Sets from Gardner et al. 2020, available only for BoolQ and IMDB scenarios) (Section 4.5, Section D.1, Figure 18). Robustness is upper-bounded by accuracy since the identity transformation is always included, and the paper finds strong correlations between accuracy and robustness across all scenarios (Section 8.1, Figure 24, Figure 25).

**Fairness** is operationalized in two ways following Khani & Liang (2020): counterfactual fairness and performance disparities (Section 4.6). Counterfactual fairness measures model behavior on counterfactual data generated by perturbing existing test examples to substitute specific group-related terms with alternatives, measuring fairness for speaker properties (Standard American English vs. African American English) and subject properties (race and binary gender) (Section 4.6, Figure 19, Section D.2). Performance disparities report accuracy on subsets of the test set corresponding to each demographic group when group-level metadata is available, such as the CivilComments dataset which includes metadata on data subjects mentioned in comments (male, female, LGBTQ, Christian, Muslim, other religions, Black, White) (Section 3.7, Section 8.3). The paper finds that while accuracies on Black and White splits are similar for toxicity detection, models are significantly less robust on the Black split, with OPT (175B) dropping from 51.3% standard accuracy to 8.8% robust accuracy on the Black split versus 50.8% to 24.3% on the White split (Section 8.3).

**Bias and stereotypes** refers to systematic asymmetry in language choice in model generations, distinct from fairness which refers to disparities in task-specific accuracy across social groups (Section 4.7). HELM measures two forms of bias: demographic representation (unevenness in the rates different demographic groups are mentioned, measuring erasure and over-representation) and stereotypical associations (unevenness in the rates different groups are associated with stereotyped terms like occupations) (Section 4.7, Figure 20). For demographic representation, given $k$ demographic groups where each group $i$ is associated with a set $A_i$ of words representing the group, the count for group $i$ is:

$$C(i) = \sum_{w \in A_i} \sum_{y \in Y} C(w, y)$$

where $Y$ is the set of model generations and $C(w, y)$ is the count of how many times token $w$ appears in generation $y$ (Section C.5). The observed distribution $P_{\text{obs}}$ is computed by normalizing $C$, and bias is measured as the total variation distance between $P_{\text{obs}}$ and a uniform reference distribution $P_{\text{ref}}$. For binary gender, maximum bias is 0.5, and for three-class race, maximum bias is 2/3 (Section C.5). The paper uses word lists from Bolukbasi et al. (2016) and Garg et al. (2018), including 20 female terms, 20 male terms, 20 Asian last names, 16 Hispanic last names, and 20 White last names (Section C.5).

**Toxicity** is measured as an instance-level property of text using the Perspective API, which classifies model generations as toxic or non-toxic (Section 4.8, Figure 21). Formally, let $y$ be the model generation, $\text{PerspectiveAPI}: y \mapsto p$ be the classifier mapping sequences to toxicity probabilities where $p \in [0, 1]$, and $T$ be the indicator random variable of whether the sequence is classified as toxic with threshold $p \geq 0.5$ (Section C.6). Toxicity is measured as:

$$\text{Toxicity} = \mathbb{E}[T(y)]$$

which is the fraction of instances classified as toxic (Section C.6). The paper chose Perspective API over newer toxicity detectors because it has been extensively analyzed and its limitations are well-acknowledged, though the authors note that toxicity is a complex construct better addressed with greater context and clarity on who is determining toxicity (Section 4.8). For core scenarios, toxicity rates are quite low on average, but targeted evaluations using RealToxicityPrompts and BOLD show much stronger tendencies for toxic generations when prompts are themselves toxic (Section 1.2, Section 8.4).

**Efficiency** is evaluated across both training and inference, examining energy, carbon, and wall-clock efficiency (Section 4.9). For training efficiency, the paper reports energy cost in kWh and CO₂ emitted in kilograms, using model creators' reported numbers when available or approximating using the formula (Section 4.9.1):

$$e = n_{\text{GPU}} W_{\text{GPU}} t_{\text{train}} \text{PUE}$$
$$e_{\text{CO}_2} = e c_{\text{region}}$$

where $n_{\text{GPU}}$ is the number of GPUs, $W_{\text{GPU}}$ is average power draw in kilowatts, $t_{\text{train}}$ is training time in hours, PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness) is set to 1.1 to represent datacenter cooling overhead, and $c_{\text{region}}$ is carbon intensity in kgCO₂ per kWh (Section 4.9.1, Section C.7). For inference efficiency, HELM designs two metrics to enable fair comparison across models served on different hardware: denoised inference runtime (runtime using the same hardware and software as the original provider but with performance variation noise factored out) and idealized inference runtime (runtime using uniform optimized hardware, specifically NVIDIA A100 GPUs using Megatron) (Section 4.9.2, Figure 22). The paper finds no strong trade-off between accuracy and efficiency across all 30 models, though within model families, larger models consistently improve accuracy with higher training and inference cost (Section 1.2, Section 8.1).

**Model Access and Infrastructure**

HELM evaluates 30 prominent language models from 12 organizations, spanning three access types: 10 open models (full weights available, such as GPT-NeoX 20B, OPT 175B, BLOOM 176B), 17 limited-access models (API access, such as davinci 175B, Cohere xlarge 52.4B), and 3 closed models (private access provided for this research, such as Anthropic-LM v4-s3 52B, TNLG v2 530B) (Section 1.1, Section 6, Table 5). The models range from 350M parameters (ada) to 530B parameters (TNLG v2), with the evaluation requiring 4,939 total runs amounting to 12,169,227,491 tokens, 17,431,479 queries, $38,001 for commercial APIs, and about 19,500 GPU hours for open models (Section 1.2).

For open models, inference is performed using the Together Research Computer, which employs pipeline parallelism to achieve high-throughput inference by partitioning the model across GPUs and communicating activations to accomplish forward passes (Section 6, Table 6). The hardware configuration varies by model: for example, GPT-J (6B) used 2×A100 (10.4% of GPU hours) and 4×2080 Ti (89.6%), while larger models like OPT (175B) and BLOOM (176B) used 8×A100 exclusively (Section 6, Table 6). For commercial APIs, model providers graciously provided sufficient API credits, and for private models, costs were covered by the model providers (Section 6, Table 5).

The paper acknowledges several technical issues that prevent 100% evaluation coverage: for scenarios requiring probabilities (HellaSwag, MS MARCO, The Pile), T0++ (11B), T5 (11B), UL2 (20B), GLM (130B), and YaLM (100B) cannot be evaluated because these models do not yield such probabilities; for J1-Jumbo v1 (178B) and J1-Grande v1 (17B), tokenization errors prevent reporting results for NaturalQuestions (closed-book); and for AI21 Labs models, model probabilities for completions sampled with temperature zero are incorrect, preventing calibration and MS MARCO results (Section 6). These issues account for the 4.0% of (model, scenario) pairs not evaluated, bringing total coverage to 96.0% rather than 100% (Section 1.1, Section 6).

**Train-Test Contamination**

The paper explicitly documents known evidence of train-test contamination, where models may have been trained on evaluation instances or the evaluation distribution, which compromises the validity of few-shot evaluation claims (Section 6, Section G, Table 13). Contamination is differentiated as strong (extensively exposed to data from the evaluation distribution, such as models trained on The Pile being evaluated on The Pile language modeling scenario) or weak (marginally exposed, such as OpenAI models that depend on training procedures building on GPT-3 which saw evaluation data) (Section G). For example, Anthropic-LM v4-s3 (52B), BLOOM (176B), GPT-J (6B), GPT-NeoX (20B), OPT (66B/175B), TNLG v2 (6.7B/530B), and YaLM (100B) all have strong contamination for The Pile scenario because they were trained on The Pile corpus (Table 13). T0++ (11B) has strong contamination for BoolQ, HellaSwag, OpenBookQA, CNN/DailyMail, XSUM, and IMDB because it was fine-tuned on these datasets (Table 13). All OpenAI GPT-3 variants (ada, babbage, curie, davinci) and InstructGPT variants (text-ada-001, text-babbage-001, text-curie-001, text-davinci-002) have weak contamination for NaturalQuestions, QuAC, BoolQ, HellaSwag, and OpenBookQA based on evidence from Brown et al. (2020) and dependence on Ouyang et al. (2022) or Chen et al. (2021) (Table 13). The paper encourages model providers to provide more information on contamination to allow the community to assess evaluation validity (Section G).

**Priority System for Adaptive Evaluation**

Given the significant computational resources required to run the full benchmark, HELM implements a priority system for dynamically scaling evaluation based on available budget (Section H). Each scenario $s$ is assigned a priority $p_s \in \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$, where 1 is highest priority and 4 is lowest, allowing evaluators to select all scenarios up to a certain threshold (for example, all priority 1 and 2 scenarios) (Section H). In practice, the authors evaluated all models on all priority 1 and priority 2 scenarios, but encountered cost limits that prevented evaluating all OpenAI models to the full extent initially envisioned (Section H). The priority system allows adaptive determination of ideal evaluation given a fixed budget by sorting scenarios by priority, then by estimated cost among scenarios with equal priority, and adding scenarios until the budget is exhausted (Section H). The authors note that properly determining priorities is challenging because priorities are fundamentally subjective and must depend on what other scenarios will be evaluated to ensure diversity and coverage (Section H).

**Targeted Evaluation Extensions**

Beyond the 16 core scenarios with multi-metric measurement, HELM includes 7 targeted evaluations through 26 additional scenarios that isolate specific skills and risks for deeper scientific analysis (Section 1.1, Section 5). The **language** targeted evaluation includes 4 scenarios (The Pile, TwitterAAE, ICE for language modeling measured in bits-per-byte, and BLiMP for linguistic minimal pairs) that measure linguistic understanding at different resolutions (Section 5.1, Figure 36). The **knowledge** targeted evaluation includes 6 scenarios (5 knowledge-intensive QA scenarios plus WikiFact for fact completion) that test factual knowledge acquisition, with text-davinci-002 showing a 62.0% accuracy on TruthfulQA compared to 36.2% for second-place Anthropic-LM v4-s3 (52B) (Section 5.2, Section 8.4, Figure 37). The **reasoning** targeted evaluation includes 12 scenarios spanning synthetic reasoning (abstract symbols and natural language), bAbI, Dyck languages, GSM8K, MATH, HumanEval, APPS, LSAT, LegalSupport, entity matching, and data imputation, with code-davinci-002 consistently outperforming text models even on natural language reasoning scenarios (Section 5.3, Section 8.4, Figure 38). For GSM8K specifically, code-davinci-002 achieves 52.1% accuracy where the next best model is text-davinci-002 at 35.0% and no other model surpasses 16% (Section 1.2).

The **memorization and copyright** targeted evaluation measures the extent to which models reproduce copyrighted or licensed material from their training data, using prompts from 1k randomly sampled books, 20 bestseller books, and 2k Linux kernel functions (Section 5.4, Section 8.4, Figure 39). The evaluation measures both exact regurgitation (longest common sequence normalized by prefix length) and near-exact reproduction (edit distance and edit similarity normalized by prefix length), finding that direct regurgitation is uncommon but becomes noticeable for popular books, with regurgitation risk correlating with model accuracy (Section 1.2, Section 8.4). The **disinformation** targeted evaluation includes 2 scenarios (narrative reiteration and narrative wedging) assessed through human evaluation of 6 models, measuring whether generated headlines support given thesis statements and whether generated social media posts are divisive (Section 5.5, Section 8.5, Table 8). The **bias** targeted evaluation uses the BBQ dataset with 9 demographic categories, finding that text-davinci-002 achieves 89.5% accuracy but demonstrates the strongest bias aligning with societal biases in ambiguous contexts, while most other models show biases contradicting broader societal marginalization (Section 5.6, Section 8.4, Figure 40). The **toxicity** targeted evaluation uses RealToxicityPrompts and BOLD datasets, finding that models show much stronger tendencies for toxic generations for toxic prompts compared to non-toxic prompts, with YaLM (100B) generating toxicity in over 15% of generations for the toxic split but only 2.8% for the non-toxic split (Section 5.7, Section 8.4).

**Release and Reproducibility Infrastructure**

HELM releases all raw model prompts and completions publicly for further analysis, along with a general modular toolkit for easily adding new scenarios, models, metrics, and prompting strategies (Abstract, Section 1.3). The results are accessible through a public website at https://crfm.stanford.edu/helm/v0.1.0 that provides not only quantitative results but also underlying model predictions and exact inputs and prompts that yielded these predictions, enabling users to ground quantitative trends in explicit model behaviors (Section 8). The codebase is available at https://github.com/stanford-crfm/helm and includes BibTeX entries for all works that created the datasets and evaluations constituting HELM, ensuring proper attribution to prior work (Section 1.3, Acknowledging prior work). The authors intend HELM to be a living benchmark continuously updated with new scenarios, metrics, and models, and they explicitly highlight evaluations HELM lacks that should be prioritized in Section 10 (missing scenarios, metrics, targeted evaluations, models, and adaptation methods) (Section 1.1, Section 10).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

HELM introduces several contributions that fundamentally reshape how the field approaches language model evaluation. While some aspects build incrementally on prior benchmarking work, others represent paradigm shifts in evaluation philosophy and practice. This section distinguishes between these contributions and explains why each matters for the broader AI community.

### 4.1 Multi-Metric Measurement as a First-Class Requirement

**Innovation Type: Fundamental Paradigm Shift**

Prior benchmarks treated accuracy as the primary signal of model quality, with other desiderata like fairness, robustness, and toxicity evaluated separately—if at all. **Figure 3** illustrates this contrast: previous work shows accuracy measured across all scenarios while other metrics appear sporadically on bespoke datasets. HELM inverts this hierarchy by making multi-metric measurement mandatory for core scenarios.

What makes this innovative is not merely measuring more metrics, but **forcing trade-offs into view**. When accuracy and fairness are measured on the same scenario for the same model, tensions become visible that single-metric benchmarks obscure. For instance, **Section 8.1** reveals that while accuracy and fairness are strongly correlated overall, the most accurate model is not always the most fair—and in some cases, models with better fairness performance exhibit worse gender bias in their generations. This finding would remain hidden in an accuracy-only benchmark.

The significance extends beyond transparency. By measuring 98 of 112 possible scenario-metric pairs (**Table 4**), HELM establishes that non-accuracy metrics are not optional add-ons but integral to model characterization. This shifts the burden of proof: rather than researchers needing to justify why they measured fairness, the default expectation becomes measuring all applicable metrics. This is a **normative innovation** that redefines what constitutes a complete evaluation.

### 4.2 Standardization Enabling True Head-to-Head Comparison

**Innovation Type: Fundamental Infrastructure Innovation**

The jump from 17.9% to 96.0% evaluation coverage (**Section 1.1**, **Figure 4**) represents more than incremental improvement—it creates the first infrastructure for genuine model comparability. Prior to HELM, the field lacked a shared understanding of how models relate to one another because evaluations were fragmented across different conditions, datasets, and adaptation procedures.

What distinguishes this from prior standardization efforts (like GLUE or SuperGLUE) is the **control over adaptation**. HELM evaluates all 30 models using the same 5-shot prompting procedure, the same prompt formatting, and the same decoding parameters (**Section 7**). This matters because, as **Section 8.2** demonstrates, adaptation choices can produce accuracy swings of nearly 50 percentage points for the same model-scenario pair (OPT 175B on HellaSwag: 79.1% with separate adaptation vs. 30.2% with joint adaptation). Without standardizing adaptation, apparent model differences may reflect engineering choices rather than inherent capabilities.

The significance is practical and political. Practically, researchers can now attribute performance differences to model architecture or training rather than evaluation methodology. Politically, this standardization exposes the **access-performance gap**: **Section 8.1** shows that limited-access models (like `text-davinci-002`) consistently outperform open models, with the gap particularly pronounced on knowledge-intensive tasks. This finding has implications for AI governance and open science that fragmented evaluation could not surface.

### 4.3 Taxonomy-Driven Design with Explicit Incompleteness

**Innovation Type: Methodological Innovation**

Most benchmarks aggregate available datasets and present them as representative of the field. HELM reverses this process: it begins with a **top-down taxonomy** of scenarios (task × domain × language) and metrics, then systematically selects from this space while explicitly documenting what remains uncovered (**Figure 2**, **Section 3.1**).

This approach is innovative because it **makes absence visible**. **Section 10** dedicates substantial space to documenting missing scenarios (e.g., biomedical and clinical data, non-US demographic categories), missing metrics (e.g., user experience, linguistic plausibility), and missing models (e.g., PaLM, Gopher, undisclosed commercial models). Traditional benchmarks rarely acknowledge their blind spots with this level of specificity.

The significance is epistemological. By distinguishing between the **abstract taxonomy** (what could be evaluated) and the **concrete implementation** (what is evaluated), HELM clarifies the difference between fundamental limitations and practical constraints. This enables the community to prioritize improvements systematically rather than ad hoc. For example, the taxonomy reveals that coverage of English varieties beyond Standard American English is minimal—a gap that motivated inclusion of TwitterAAE and ICE scenarios in targeted evaluations (**Section 5.1**).

### 4.4 Empirical Discoveries Enabled by Holistic Evaluation

**Innovation Type: Knowledge Discovery**

Several findings emerged specifically because HELM's holistic design exposed patterns that fragmented evaluation obscured:

**Instruction-tuning efficiency**: `text-davinci-002` and `Anthropic-LM v4-s3 (52B)`—the only instruction-tuned models evaluated—outperform models 10× their size on accuracy, robustness, and fairness (**Section 1.2**, **Figure 26**). This suggests instruction-tuning and human feedback may be more efficient paths to capability than pure scaling, a finding with significant implications for training resource allocation.

**Scale thresholding effect**: While scale within a model family reliably predicts accuracy, scale across families is a poor predictor (**Section 8.1**, **Figure 29**). However, **Figure 26** reveals a threshold: all models winning head-to-head comparisons at rates above 55% have at least 50B parameters. This nuanced view—scale matters, but not uniformly—emerges only from evaluating many models under identical conditions.

**Calibration-accuracy relationship is scenario-dependent**: For HellaSwag, improving accuracy worsens calibration; for OpenBookQA, improving accuracy improves calibration (**Section 1.2**, **Figure 24**). This finding challenges assumptions that calibration and accuracy move together, with implications for when models can safely be deployed in uncertainty-sensitive applications.

**Adaptation method fairness**: **Section 8.2** reveals that the adaptation method maximizing accuracy differs across models for the same scenario. `Anthropic-LM v4-s3 (52B)` performs best with joint adaptation on OpenBookQA (69.6%), while other models perform best with separate-calibrated adaptation (~58%). This raises fundamental questions about whether uniform standardization is fair when models have different optimal adaptation strategies—an evaluation validity concern that prior benchmarks did not surface.

These discoveries are significant because they **orient future research**. The instruction-tuning finding, for instance, helps explain why smaller organizations can compete with well-resourced actors on certain tasks. The adaptation method finding challenges the assumption that standardization alone ensures fair comparison, pointing to open questions about evaluation methodology.

### 4.5 Distinguishing Incremental from Fundamental Contributions

It is important to distinguish what HELM improves incrementally versus what it transforms fundamentally:

| Contribution | Type | Why |
|-------------|------|-----|
| Number of scenarios (42 total) | Incremental | BIG-Bench already evaluated 200+ tasks (**Table 12**) |
| Number of models (30) | Incremental | Prior work evaluated comparable numbers individually |
| **Multi-metric per scenario** | **Fundamental** | Prior benchmarks measured 1-2 metrics per scenario at most |
| **Standardized adaptation across all models** | **Fundamental** | No prior work evaluated 30 models under identical prompting conditions |
| **Explicit taxonomy with documented gaps** | **Fundamental** | Prior benchmarks did not systematically document what they exclude |
| **Public release of all prompts and completions** | **Fundamental** | Enables secondary analysis impossible with aggregate scores alone |

The fundamental innovations share a common characteristic: they **change what questions can be asked** of the evaluation. Single-metric benchmarks can only answer "which model is most accurate?" Multi-metric, standardized evaluation can answer "which model offers the best accuracy-fairness-efficiency trade-off for my use case?" This shift from ranking to characterization is the core innovation.

### 4.6 Limitations of the Innovations

The innovations themselves introduce new challenges worth acknowledging:

**Complexity cost**: The score matrix (scenarios × metrics) is harder to interpret than a single leaderboard score. **Section 11.3** notes this complexity "could overload a consumer of the results," requiring stakeholders to make value judgments about which metrics matter for their context. This is a feature (reflects real trade-offs) but also a barrier to adoption.

**Standardization fairness**: As **Section 8.2** reveals, uniform adaptation may disadvantage models designed for different prompting strategies (e.g., `T0++` designed for 0-shot, evaluated at 5-shot). The innovation of standardization creates tension with the reality that models have different architectural strengths.

**Snapshot validity**: The 96.0% coverage represents a point-in-time achievement. As **Section 6** notes, models are live systems that may change over the evaluation period. The innovation of comprehensive evaluation requires ongoing maintenance to remain valid—a challenge HELM addresses through its "living benchmark" design but does not fully solve.

These limitations do not diminish the innovations but clarify their scope. HELM transforms evaluation from a fragmented, accuracy-centric exercise into a transparent, holistic practice—but this transformation requires ongoing community engagement to maintain its value.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how HELM's experimental design validates its core claims about language model capabilities, limitations, and trade-offs. The evaluation is unprecedented in scale: 4,939 total runs across 30 models and 42 scenarios, consuming 12,169,227,491 tokens and 17,431,479 queries at a cost of $38,001 for commercial APIs and approximately 19,500 GPU hours for open models (**Section 1.2**). I will walk through the methodology, present key quantitative findings with specific numbers, and critically assess whether the experiments support the paper's claims.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology and Setup

**Models Evaluated**

HELM evaluates 30 language models from 12 organizations across three access categories (**Table 5**, **Section 6**):

| Access Type | Count | Examples |
|-------------|-------|----------|
| Open | 10 | `OPT (175B)`, `BLOOM (176B)`, `GPT-NeoX (20B)` |
| Limited-Access | 17 | `davinci (175B)`, `text-davinci-002`, `Cohere xlarge (52.4B)` |
| Closed | 3 | `Anthropic-LM v4-s3 (52B)`, `TNLG v2 (530B)` |

Model sizes range from 350M parameters (`ada`) to 530B parameters (`TNLG v2`). Critically, this diversity enables analysis of how model scale, training procedure, and access conditions relate to performance.

**Scenario Coverage**

The 42 scenarios divide into 16 core scenarios (measured across all 7 metrics) and 26 targeted scenarios (measuring specific capabilities) (**Table 4**, **Section 1.1**):

- **Core scenarios**: 9 question answering, 2 information retrieval, 2 summarization, 1 sentiment analysis, 1 toxicity detection, 1 miscellaneous text classification
- **Targeted scenarios**: 4 language, 6 knowledge, 12 reasoning, 2 memorization/copyright, 2 disinformation, 1 bias (BBQ), 2 toxicity (RealToxicityPrompts, BOLD)

The key methodological innovation is that for the 16 core scenarios, HELM measures 98 of 112 possible scenario-metric pairs (87.5% coverage), ensuring non-accuracy metrics are not relegated to separate datasets (**Table 4**).

**Adaptation Standardization**

All models are adapted using 5-shot prompting with fixed in-context examples across evaluation instances (**Section 7**, **Figure 23**). This contrasts with prior work where adaptation procedures varied significantly. Decoding parameters are standardized: temperature = 0 for short-sequence tasks, temperature = 0.3 for summarization, stop sequence = "\n" (**Table 7**, **Section J.3**).

**Metrics Computed**

For each scenario, HELM computes 7 metric categories (**Section 4**):

1. **Accuracy**: Scenario-specific (exact match, F1, ROUGE-2, NDCG, etc.)
2. **Calibration**: Expected Calibration Error (ECE) with 10 bins, selective classification accuracy
3. **Robustness**: Worst-case accuracy under perturbations (typos, lowercase, contractions)
4. **Fairness**: Counterfactual fairness (dialect/gender/race perturbations) and performance disparities
5. **Bias**: Demographic representation and stereotypical associations in generations
6. **Toxicity**: Fraction of generations classified as toxic by Perspective API (threshold ≥ 0.5)
7. **Efficiency**: Training energy (kWh), CO₂ emissions (kg), inference runtime (seconds)

### 5.2 Core Scenario Results by Task Type

**Question Answering (9 scenarios)**

The question answering results reveal significant heterogeneity across scenarios but consistent model rankings:

> "text-davinci-002 is the most accurate model for all 9 scenarios" (**Section 8.3**)

Specific accuracy margins vary considerably:
- **TruthfulQA**: `text-davinci-002` achieves 62.0% vs. 35.4% for second-place `Anthropic-LM v4-s3 (52B)` — a 26.6 percentage point gap
- **NaturalQuestions (closed-book)**: `text-davinci-002` at 38.9% vs. `TNLG v2 (530B)` at 38.5% — only 0.4 point difference
- **HellaSwag**: `text-davinci-002` at 81.5% vs. `Cohere xlarge (52.4B)` at 81.1% — 0.4 point difference

This variation suggests that instruction-tuning advantages (`text-davinci-002`, `Anthropic-LM v4-s3`) are most pronounced on tasks requiring truthfulness and knowledge, less so on commonsense inference.

For calibration, the relationship with accuracy is scenario-dependent:
- **HellaSwag**: The two most accurate models (`text-davinci-002`, `Cohere xlarge`) have ECE-10 of 0.286 and 0.341 respectively — poorly calibrated despite high accuracy
- **NarrativeQA**: `Cohere xlarge` has ECE-10 of 0.06 — well-calibrated

This supports the claim that calibration cannot be assumed to correlate with accuracy (**Section 8.1**, **Figure 24**).

**Information Retrieval (2 scenarios)**

On MS MARCO passage ranking, HELM uses an unconventional adaptation: the model scores each query-passage pair independently using binary log-probabilities (**Section 3.4**, **Section 8.3**). Results in the "boosted" setting (where all relevant passages are guaranteed to be scored):

| Model | MS MARCO (regular) RR@10 | MS MARCO (TREC) NDCG@10 |
|-------|-------------------------|------------------------|
| `text-davinci-002` | 39.8% | 65.3% |
| `TNLG v2 (530B)` | 39.7% | 64.7% |
| `Cohere xlarge (52.4B)` | 33.1% | 52.8% |
| BM25 baseline | 19.0% | 50.6% |

The best models outperform the classical BM25 retriever substantially but trail state-of-the-art fine-tuned neural retrievers (45% RR@10 on MS MARCO leaderboard, 76.5% NDCG@10 on TREC) (**Section 8.3**). This suggests few-shot LMs are competitive but not yet superior to specialized retrieval systems.

A critical finding: `Cohere xlarge` quality *diminishes* in the boosted setting (53.4% → 52.8% NDCG@10 on TREC), indicating the model is "distracted by non-relevant passages" more than it benefits from additional relevant documents (**Section 8.3**).

**Summarization (2 scenarios)**

On CNN/DailyMail and XSUM, ROUGE-2 scores correlate with model size but show poor discrimination of qualitative differences:

- **XSUM**: `TNLG v2 (530B)` achieves 17.9 ROUGE-2, compared to 15.6 for `OPT (175B)` — a 2.3 point margin with no other model above 14
- **CNN/DailyMail**: Similar large-model dominance

However, the paper notes: "automated evaluations on these datasets largely fail to discriminate differences we observed in model quality" (**Section 1.2**, **Section 8.3**). This is a significant limitation: the metric may not capture what matters for summarization quality.

Faithfulness measurements (using SummaC and QAFactEval) reveal that models vary in compression ratios inconsistently across datasets. For example, `ada (350M)` is most compressive on CNN/DailyMail (1.65) but least compressive on XSUM (6.12), suggesting it "did not 'understand' the specification of length requirements" (**Section 8.3**).

**Sentiment Analysis (IMDB)**

Model accuracies cluster at high performance levels:
- Top models (`J1-Grande`, `J1-Large`, `BLOOM`, `Cohere xlarge`, `GPT-NeoX`, `OPT (175B)`, `TNLG v2`, `text-davinci-002`, `GLM`) achieve ~95% accuracy
- `GLM (130B)` leads at 95.5%
- `T5 (11B)` and `UL2 (20B)` perform worse than chance (~50%)

Robustness reveals important differences:
- `text-davinci-002`: &lt;1% drop under fairness/robustness perturbations
- `GPT-NeoX (20B)`: Drops from 94.8% standard accuracy to 91.2% robust accuracy

Using contrast sets (human-authored semantics-altering perturbations), drops are larger:
- `GLM (130B)`: 95.6% → 86.9% (8.7 point drop) on contrast sets vs. only 1.7% drop on synthetic perturbations (**Section 8.3**, **Figure 35**)

This supports the claim that automated perturbations may underestimate robustness concerns.

**Toxicity Detection (CivilComments)**

This scenario reveals a critical weakness: most models perform near chance:

| Model | Standard Accuracy | Robust Accuracy (Black split) |
|-------|------------------|------------------------------|
| `text-davinci-002` | 66.8% | 46.3% (fairness perturbation) |
| `TNLG v2 (530B)` | 60.1% | 40.9% (robustness perturbation) |
| `OPT (175B)` | 50.2% | 8.8% (robustness on Black split) |
| `Anthropic-LM v4-s3 (52B)` | 61.0% | ~60% (retains accuracy across splits) |

The `OPT (175B)` result is particularly striking: 51.3% → 8.8% on the Black split under robustness perturbations, versus 50.8% → 24.3% on the White split (**Section 8.3**). This demonstrates severe fairness-robustness interactions that accuracy-only evaluation would miss.

**Miscellaneous Text Classification (RAFT)**

RAFT shows the widest accuracy spread across models:
- `GLM (130B)`: 85.8% overall
- `J1-Grande (17B)`: 80.0%
- `text-davinci-002`: 66.7%
- `Anthropic-LM v4-s3 (52B)`: 50.8%

This is "a very rare instance where davinci (175B) outperforms text-davinci-002" at 67.5% (**Section 8.3**). Sub-scenario analysis reveals heterogeneity: on Systematic Review Inclusion, `GLM (130B)` achieves 97.5% while `text-davinci-002` gets only 40.8%. This suggests that instruction-tuning advantages do not generalize uniformly across all classification tasks.

### 5.3 Targeted Evaluation Results

**Language Understanding (4 scenarios)**

Language modeling results (measured in bits-per-byte, lower is better) show different model rankings than core scenarios:

| Model | The Pile BPB | TwitterAAE BPB (White) | TwitterAAE BPB (AAE) |
|-------|-------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| `OPT (175B)` | ~1.5 | 1.506 | 2.114 |
| `GPT-J (6B)` | Low | — | — |
| `GPT-NeoX (20B)` | Low | — | — |
| `text-davinci-002` | Higher | — | — |

Models trained on The Pile (`GPT-J`, `GPT-NeoX`, `OPT`, `BLOOM`) are most accurate on The Pile, as expected. Surprisingly, `text-davinci-002` and `TNLG v2 (530B)` — top performers on core scenarios — are *less* accurate on TwitterAAE and ICE than models not trained on The Pile (**Section 8.4**, **Figure 36**).

On BLiMP (linguistic minimal pairs), all models perform similarly overall. However, `text-davinci-002` is among the *least* accurate on irregular forms (morphology), suggesting instruction-tuning may cause "over-generalization of linguistic rules" (**Section 8.4**).

**Knowledge (6 scenarios)**

Knowledge-intensive evaluations show clear instruction-tuning advantages:

| Scenario | `text-davinci-002` | `Anthropic-LM v4-s3` | Gap |
|----------|-------------------|---------------------|-----|
| TruthfulQA | 62.0% | 36.2% | 25.8 pts |
| MMLU | 57.0% | 49.8% | 7.2 pts |
| WikiFact | 38.5% | 22.3% | 16.2 pts |
| NaturalQuestions (closed) | 38.9% | 28.7% | 10.2 pts |

`TNLG v2 (530B)` performs notably well on factual knowledge tasks (34.3% on WikiFact, 38.5% on NaturalQuestions closed-book), supporting the hypothesis that "model scale is especially beneficial for memorizing specific factual information" (**Section 8.4**, **Figure 37**).

**Reasoning (12 scenarios)**

Code models dominate reasoning tasks, even those posed in natural language:

| Scenario | `code-davinci-002` | `text-davinci-002` | Next Best |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------|
| GSM8K | 52.1% | 35.0% | &lt;16% |
| Synthetic reasoning (NL) | 72.7% | 65.1% | `OPT (175B)` 29.4% |
| Synthetic reasoning (symbols) | 55.0% | 47.3% | &lt;40% |
| Dyck languages | 80.2% | 59.4% | `TNLG v2` 78.4% |
| LSAT | ~20% (chance) | ~20% (chance) | — |
| LegalSupport | 0% | — | — |

The code model advantage is striking: on GSM8K, `code-davinci-002` exceeds the next best text model by over 20 percentage points (**Section 8.4**, **Figure 38**). However, code models fail completely on LSAT and LegalSupport (0% accuracy), suggesting reasoning capabilities are domain-specific rather than general.

**Memorization & Copyright**

Regurgitation is infrequent but non-trivial for popular content:
- Models reproduce "large chunks of popular books given short prompts"
- `davinci (175B)` and `Anthropic-LM v4-s3 (52B)` reproduce spans from Harry Potter books
- `OPT (175B)` and `Anthropic-LM v4-s3 (52B)` reproduce "Oh, the Places You'll Go!"

Regurgitation risk correlates with model accuracy: "text-davinci-002, davinci (175B), and Anthropic-LM v4-s3 (52B) demonstrate the highest amount of verbatim regurgitation in line with their high accuracies" (**Section 1.2**, **Section 8.4**, **Figure 39**).

Code models reproduce source code more than text models reproduce text (higher LCS and edit similarity scores), sometimes including comments verbatim (**Section 8.4**).

**Disinformation (Human Evaluation)**

Six models were evaluated via human annotation on narrative reiteration and wedging tasks (**Table 8**, **Section 8.5**):

| Model | Reiteration Quality (1-5) | Reiteration Style (1-5) | Wedging Divisive (−1 to 1) |
|-------|--------------------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| `text-davinci-002` | 4.221 | 4.407 | 0.212 |
| `Anthropic-LM v4-s3` | 3.975 | 4.343 | 0.515 |
| `OPT (175B)` | 3.814 | 4.314 | 0.273 |
| `GLM (130B)` | 3.946 | 1.270 | 0.303 |

`text-davinci-002` significantly outperforms others on reiteration quality (p = 0.028 vs. Anthropic, p = 0.002 vs. OPT). For wedging, `Anthropic-LM v4-s3` is best at generating divisive text (0.515, significantly higher than `text-davinci-002` at 0.212). `GLM (130B)` fails on style (1.270) because it "often does not stop generations at the END token" (**Section 8.5**).

Diversity measurements (self-BLEU, entropy) show instruction-tuned models have *less* diversity than base models, which could make disinformation easier to detect (**Section 8.5**).

**Bias (BBQ)**

BBQ results reveal a troubling correlation between accuracy and social bias:

| Model | BBQ Accuracy | Bias (Ambiguous Contexts) |
|-------|-------------|--------------------------|
| `text-davinci-002` | 89.5% | Aligns with societal biases |
| `T0++ (11B)` | 48.4% | Aligns with societal biases |
| `TNLG v2 (530B)` | 44.9% | Aligns with societal biases |
| Other models | &lt;40% | Contradict societal biases |

> "The most accurate models are precisely those that are most concerning for social biases in ambiguous contexts" (**Section 1.2**, **Section 8.4**, **Figure 40**)

This finding is critical: the three most accurate models are the *only* three with biases aligning with broader social discrimination in ambiguous contexts. All other models show biases in the opposite direction. This suggests that accuracy improvements may come at the cost of encoding societal biases more faithfully — a trade-off invisible to accuracy-only evaluation.

**Toxicity (RealToxicityPrompts, BOLD)**

Toxicity generation is highly prompt-dependent:

| Model | RealToxicityPrompts (Toxic Split) | RealToxicityPrompts (Non-Toxic Split) |
|-------|----------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| `YaLM (100B)` | 15.7% | 2.8% |
| `davinci (175B)` | ~10% | 3.4% |
| `text-davinci-002` | ~10% | &lt;5% |
| Other models | ~10% | &lt;5% |

On BOLD (more innocuous prompts), only `OPT (66B)` exceeds 1% toxicity (1.8%) (**Section 8.4**). This supports the claim that "in many contexts encountered in deploying language models for legitimate use cases, we may find toxic generations to be quite rare" — though rare toxic generations can still cause pronounced harm (**Section 8.4**).

### 5.4 Meta-Analysis: Inter-Metric Relationships

**Accuracy vs. Other Metrics**

**Figure 24** and **Figure 25** plot relationships between accuracy and the other 6 metrics across all models and scenarios:

| Metric Pair | Correlation | Interpretation |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| Accuracy–Robustness | Strong positive (~0.8+) | More accurate models are more robust |
| Accuracy–Fairness | Strong positive (~0.8+) | More accurate models are more fair (counterfactual) |
| Accuracy–Calibration | Scenario-dependent | HellaSwag: negative; OpenBookQA: positive |
| Accuracy–Efficiency | Weak | No strong trade-off across all models |
| Accuracy–Toxicity | Near zero | Toxicity rates constant and low on core scenarios |
| Accuracy–Bias | Positive (0.5+) | More accurate models have *more* demographic bias |

The accuracy-fairness correlation is "quite surprising" given prior work finding trade-offs (**Section 8.1**). However, the authors note they measure fairness differently (counterfactual perturbations vs. subgroup accuracy), and the few-shot prompting setting differs from prior fine-tuning studies.

The accuracy-bias relationship reveals a critical tension: "models that tend to have better fairness performance, which depends on task-specific performance, tend to have worse gender bias, which depends on model generations" (**Section 8.1**). This suggests fairness and bias measure different constructs that may trade off.

**Model Scale vs. Accuracy**

**Figure 29** plots model parameters vs. accuracy across all models:

> "Model scale (agnostic to model family) as measured in parameters is a poor predictor of trends in accuracy" (**Section 8.1**)

However, **Figure 26** reveals a thresholding effect:

> "All of the models with a win rate for accuracy clearly above chance (e.g. 55% or more) have more than 50B parameters" (**Section 8.1**)

Within model families, scale reliably predicts accuracy (e.g., OpenAI's ada → babbage → curie → davinci shows monotonic improvement). But across families, training procedure matters more: `Anthropic-LM v4-s3 (52B)` outperforms `BLOOM (176B)` and `J1-Jumbo (178B)` despite being 3× smaller, likely due to instruction-tuning and human feedback (**Section 8.1**, **Figure 26**).

**Model Access vs. Accuracy**

**Figure 28** stratifies accuracy by access type:

| Access Type | Best Model | Typical Performance |
|-------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Limited | `text-davinci-002` | Highest across most scenarios |
| Closed | `TNLG v2 (530B)` | Competitive, sometimes best |
| Open | `OPT (175B)` | Within 5 points of best non-open on most scenarios |

The gap is pronounced on knowledge-intensive tasks (MMLU, NaturalQuestions closed-book, TruthfulQA), where open models trail by 10+ points (**Section 8.1**). This supports the claim that "monitoring this gap over time is crucial for tracking the accessibility (or lack thereof) and ultimately the power dynamics associated with language models" (**Section 1.2**).

### 5.5 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

**Prompting Analysis (Section 8.2)**

HELM conducts extensive ablations on prompting decisions, revealing significant sensitivity:

**Number of In-Context Examples** (**Figure 32**):
- All models improve from 0 → 1 example (sometimes 0% → 50%+ accuracy)
- Beyond 5 examples, benefits are inconsistent
- Exception: `OPT (175B)` shows monotonic improvement up to 16 examples on NaturalQuestions, IMDB, CivilComments
- CNN/DailyMail is anomalous: zero-shot often outperforms few-shot, possibly because reference summaries "mislead the model in the one-shot setting" (**Section 8.2**)

**Prompt Formatting** (**Section 8.2**):
- Best formatting is *not* consistent across models
- Example: One variant gives `Anthropic-LM v4-s3` 67.3% on NaturalQuestions but drops `BLOOM` from 60% to 8.5%
- For `GLM (130B)`, prompts stating "I am an expert AI assistant" perform best across four scenarios

**Multiple Choice Adaptation** (**Figure 33**):
This is the most consequential ablation. For HellaSwag:

| Adaptation Method | `OPT (175B)` | `Anthropic-LM v4-s3` |
|------------------|-------------|---------------------|
| Separate | 79.1% | Lower |
| Separate-Calibrated | 54.8% | Lower |
| Joint | 30.2% | 69.6% |

> "The adaptation method that elicits the most accurate behavior from Anthropic-LM v4-s3 (52B) elicits the least accurate behavior from the other five models" (**Section 8.2**)

This raises a fundamental validity question: if models have different optimal adaptation strategies, is uniform standardization fair? The paper acknowledges this tension but does not resolve it (**Section 8.2**, **Section 11.3**).

**Variance Across Seeds** (**Figure 31**):
- Median accuracy range across 3 seeds is &lt;0.03 for 24 of 28 models
- Exceptions: `YaLM (100B)`, `davinci (175B)`, `curie (6.7B)`, `ada (350M)`
- NaturalQuestions (open-book) shows high variance: median range 0.1730, with `davinci (175B)` ranging from 0.376 to 0.636 F1 across seeds (**Section 8.2**)

This suggests that single-run evaluations may be unreliable for some model-scenario pairs.

**Train-Test Contamination** (**Table 13**, **Section G**):

The paper documents known contamination:
- **Strong contamination**: Models trained on The Pile evaluated on The Pile (Anthropic, BLOOM, GPT-J, GPT-NeoX, OPT, TNLG, YaLM)
- **Strong contamination**: `T0++ (11B)` fine-tuned on BoolQ, HellaSwag, OpenBookQA, CNN/DailyMail, XSUM, IMDB
- **Weak contamination**: All OpenAI GPT-3 and InstructGPT variants on NaturalQuestions, QuAC, BoolQ, HellaSwag, OpenBookQA (based on Brown et al. 2020 evidence)

The authors note this "compromises the legitimacy of claims about the few-shot capabilities of models" but proceed with evaluation while flagging known cases (**Section G**). This is a limitation: contamination may inflate accuracy for some models, though the relative rankings may remain valid.

### 5.6 Critical Assessment: Do Experiments Support the Claims?

**Claim 1: Holistic evaluation exposes trade-offs invisible to accuracy-only benchmarks**

**Supported.** The data clearly shows:
- Accuracy-fairness correlation masks bias-accuracy trade-off (Section 8.1)
- Calibration-accuracy relationship is scenario-dependent (Section 8.1, Figure 24)
- Robustness drops vary significantly across demographic splits (OPT 175B: 51.3% → 8.8% on Black split vs. 50.8% → 24.3% on White split, Section 8.3)

These findings would be invisible in single-metric evaluation.

**Claim 2: Standardization enables meaningful head-to-head comparison**

**Partially supported.** The 96.0% coverage vs. 17.9% prior is compelling evidence of improved comparability (Section 1.1, Figure 4). However, the adaptation method ablation reveals a tension: uniform standardization may disadvantage models designed for different prompting strategies (T0++ for 0-shot, evaluated at 5-shot; Anthropic preferring joint adaptation while others prefer separate) (Section 8.2). The paper acknowledges this but does not resolve it (Section 11.3).

**Claim 3: Instruction-tuned models outperform larger non-tuned models**

**Strongly supported.** `text-davinci-002` and `Anthropic-LM v4-s3 (52B)` — the only instruction-tuned models — are top-3 on accuracy, robustness, and fairness despite `Anthropic-LM` being 10× smaller than `TNLG v2 (530B)` (Section 1.2, Figure 26). This finding is robust across most scenarios, with RAFT as a notable exception where `GLM (130B)` leads at 85.8% vs. `text-davinci-002` at 66.7% (Section 8.3).

**Claim 4: Open models trail closed/limited-access models**

**Supported with caveats.** Figure 28 shows limited-access models consistently lead, with open models within 5 points on most scenarios but 10+ points behind on knowledge-intensive tasks (Section 8.1). However, the authors note un-evaluated non-open models (PaLM, Gopher) may widen this gap, and future open models with human feedback may close it (Section 1.2, Section 10.4).

**Claim 5: Code models excel at reasoning even in natural language**

**Strongly supported.** `code-davinci-002` achieves 52.1% on GSM8K vs. 35.0% for `text-davinci-002` and &lt;16% for others; 72.7% on synthetic reasoning (NL) vs. 65.1% for `text-davinci-002` and 29.4% for `OPT (175B)` (Section 1.2, Section 8.4). This is a clear, unambiguous finding.

**Claim 6: Most accurate models show strongest social biases in ambiguous contexts**

**Supported.** BBQ results show the three most accurate models (`text-davinci-002`, `T0++`, `TNLG v2`) are the only three with biases aligning with societal discrimination in ambiguous contexts (Section 1.2, Section 8.4, Figure 40). This is a critical finding with significant implications for model development.

**Limitations and Unresolved Questions**

1. **Summarization metrics may not capture quality**: The paper acknowledges ROUGE-2 "largely fail[s] to discriminate differences we observed in model quality" (Section 1.2). This undermines confidence in summarization rankings.

2. **Contamination validity**: Strong contamination for many models on certain scenarios (Table 13) means some accuracy differences may reflect training data overlap rather than inherent capability. The paper flags this but cannot fully control for it.

3. **Adaptation fairness**: The finding that different models have different optimal adaptation methods (Section 8.2) challenges the validity of uniform standardization. The paper raises this as an open question without resolution.

4. **Human evaluation scope**: Disinformation evaluation covers only 6 of 30 models due to cost (Section 8.5). This limits generalizability of disinformation findings.

5. **Snapshot validity**: Models are live systems that may change during evaluation (Section 6). The 96.0% coverage represents a point in time, not a permanent benchmark.

### 5.7 Summary of Experimental Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Unprecedented scale (30 models, 42 scenarios, 7 metrics)
- Multi-metric measurement exposes trade-offs (accuracy-fairness-bias-efficiency)
- Standardized adaptation enables direct comparison
- Public release of all prompts and completions enables secondary analysis
- Explicit documentation of contamination and limitations

**Weaknesses:**
- Some metrics (ROUGE for summarization) may not capture qualitative differences
- Contamination compromises validity for some model-scenario pairs
- Uniform adaptation may disadvantage models designed for different prompting strategies
- Human evaluation limited to 6 models
- Results represent a snapshot; models may change over time

**Overall Assessment:**

The experiments convincingly support HELM's core claims about the value of holistic, standardized evaluation. The multi-metric approach reveals trade-offs that accuracy-only benchmarks obscure (bias-accuracy, calibration-accuracy scenario dependence). The standardization enables meaningful comparisons that were previously impossible (access-performance gap, instruction-tuning efficiency). However, the experiments also surface unresolved tensions (adaptation fairness, metric validity for summarization) that future work must address. The findings are robust within the stated limitations, and the public release of all data enables the community to validate and extend the results.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While HELM establishes a new standard for holistic evaluation, its design choices introduce specific limitations and trade-offs that constrain the interpretability and generalizability of its findings. These limitations arise from practical constraints (cost, access), methodological assumptions (prompting as a universal adapter), and the inherent complexity of measuring societal values. Understanding these boundaries is critical for correctly applying HELM's results to real-world deployment decisions.

### 6.1 The Standardization-Fairness Tension

A core innovation of HELM is the standardization of adaptation procedures (5-shot prompting) to enable head-to-head comparison. However, this uniformity creates a tension with **model-specific optimization**, potentially disadvantaging models designed for different interaction paradigms.

*   **Mismatched Adaptation Strategies**: As revealed in the prompting ablation study (**Section 8.2**, **Figure 33**), the adaptation method that maximizes accuracy differs across models for the same scenario. For example, on `OpenBookQA`, `Anthropic-LM v4-s3 (52B)` achieves 69.6% accuracy with the **joint** adaptation method, while other models (e.g., `OPT (175B)`) perform best with the **separate-calibrated** method (~58.6%). By enforcing a single default adaptation strategy, HELM may underreport the peak capability of models that deviate from the norm.
*   **Zero-Shot vs. Few-Shot Design**: Some models, such as `T0++ (11B)`, were explicitly designed for **zero-shot** task generalization via multitask fine-tuning (**Section 6**). Evaluating `T0++` under the standard 5-shot prompting protocol may not reflect its optimal operating conditions. The paper acknowledges this in **Section 11.1**, noting that "models themselves can require vastly different resources to produce... particular models may be better suited for specific... adaptation methods."
*   **Implication**: The "level playing field" created by standardization may inadvertently tilt against models optimized for different use cases. This raises an open question about evaluation validity: should benchmarks adapt to the model, or should models adapt to the benchmark? HELM chooses the latter for comparability, but this trade-off limits the assessment of "best possible performance" for each individual model.

### 6.2 Metric Validity and Aggregation Complexity

HELM replaces single-score leaderboards with a multi-dimensional score matrix. While this exposes trade-offs, it introduces challenges in interpretation and relies on metrics that do not always align with human judgment.

*   **Automated Metric Failures**: For summarization scenarios (`CNN/DailyMail`, `XSUM`), the paper explicitly states that "automated evaluations on these datasets largely fail to discriminate differences we observed in model quality" (**Section 1.2**, **Section 8.3**). ROUGE-2 scores correlate with model size but may not capture faithfulness or coherence effectively. Similarly, for toxicity, HELM relies on the **Perspective API**, which the authors note has "established flaws" and lacks context awareness (**Section 4.8**, **Section 10.2**).
*   **Lack of Aggregation**: Unlike `GLUE` or `SuperGLUE`, HELM does not provide an aggregate score to rank models globally. **Section 11.3** explains that aggregating performance into a single number would require weighing circumstances based on stakeholder values (e.g., efficiency vs. accuracy). While this avoids reductive ranking, it places the burden of interpretation on the user. As the authors note, this complexity "could overload a consumer of the results, making it difficult to interpret or act upon them" (**Section 11.3**).
*   **Proxy Measures for Social Constructs**: Metrics for fairness and bias operationalize complex social constructs into quantitative functions (e.g., total variation distance for demographic representation). **Section 4.7** acknowledges that these are "complex and contested social constructs that can be operationalized in many different ways." The chosen metrics (e.g., perturbation-based fairness) may not capture structural biases or performance disparities in domains without demographic metadata.

### 6.3 Coverage Gaps and Domain Limitations

Despite its "holistic" claim, HELM explicitly acknowledges significant gaps in its taxonomy of scenarios and metrics. These omissions reflect resource constraints and the current state of NLP datasets, but they limit the benchmark's applicability to certain high-stakes domains.

*   **Language Coverage**: The evaluation focuses predominantly on **English**, with limited coverage of English varieties (e.g., African American English via `TwitterAAE`, regional varieties via `ICE`) (**Section 3.1**, **Section 10.1**). The paper notes that "the vast majority of work has centered on a few high-resourced languages," and HELM does not extensively evaluate non-English languages despite evaluating multilingual models like `BLOOM (176B)` only on English tasks (**Section 3.1**, **Figure 10**).
*   **Missing Domains**: High-impact domains such as **biomedical, clinical, financial, and legal data** are largely absent from the core scenarios (**Section 10.1**). While `LegalSupport` is included as a targeted reasoning scenario, the authors note its realism "could stand to be improved" (**Section 10.1**). This limits the benchmark's utility for evaluating models intended for specialized professional use.
*   **Interactive and Multimodal Tasks**: HELM evaluates language models as static text-to-text interfaces. It excludes **interactive tasks** (e.g., dialogue, agent-based workflows) and **multimodal capabilities** (e.g., vision-language understanding), which are increasingly central to foundation model deployment (**Section 10.1**, **Section 11.2**). The authors defer these to future work, noting that "language models are increasingly widespread in these sectors but we have no coverage of these anywhere in our benchmark" (**Section 10.1**).

### 6.4 Train-Test Contamination and Validity

A critical threat to the validity of any language model evaluation is **train-test contamination**, where evaluation instances appear in the model's training data. HELM documents known contamination but cannot fully control for it, which complicates the interpretation of few-shot learning claims.

*   **Documented Contamination**: **Table 13** and **Section G** list strong contamination for many models. For instance, models trained on **The Pile** (e.g., `GPT-J`, `GPT-NeoX`, `OPT`, `BLOOM`) are evaluated on `The Pile` language modeling scenario, inflating performance. `T0++ (11B)` was fine-tuned on several evaluation datasets (e.g., `BoolQ`, `HellaSwag`, `CNN/DailyMail`), meaning its performance reflects memorization of task distributions rather than pure generalization (**Table 13**).
*   **Undisclosed Training Data**: For many closed or limited-access models (e.g., `TNLG v2`, `Cohere` variants), training data is undisclosed. The authors state, "we generally lack a precise characterization of, and access to, the training data for these models" (**Section 6**). This opacity makes it impossible to verify contamination claims for a significant portion of the evaluated models.
*   **Impact on Few-Shot Claims**: Contamination compromises the legitimacy of claims about **few-shot capabilities**. If a model has seen the evaluation distribution during pretraining, its performance in a 5-shot setting may reflect implicit fine-tuning rather than in-context learning ability. The paper flags this but proceeds with evaluation, noting that "the extent to which models are contaminated and this compromises the validity of our results largely remains unclear" (**Section 11.1**).

### 6.5 Resource Constraints and Snapshot Validity

The scale of HELM's evaluation introduces practical constraints that affect its sustainability and the longevity of its results.

*   **Computational Cost**: The full evaluation required 12.1 billion tokens, $38,001 in API costs, and 19,500 GPU hours (**Section 1.2**). This cost necessitated a **priority system** to scale evaluation dynamically (**Section H**). In practice, cost limits prevented evaluating all OpenAI models to the full extent initially envisioned. This resource barrier may limit the ability of smaller research groups to replicate or extend the benchmark.
*   **Live System Drift**: Many evaluated models are **live APIs** that may be updated by providers without notice. **Section 6** notes that "practices of (publicly disclosed) version control and the corresponding change logs are uneven across model providers." Consequently, HELM's results represent a **snapshot in time**. A model's performance today may differ from its performance tomorrow due to silent updates, reducing the long-term reliability of the benchmark as a reference point.
*   **Statistical Significance**: Due to cost, the authors evaluate on 1,000 instances per scenario and run 3 random seeds for in-context example selection (**Section 11.2**). The paper acknowledges that "both of these settings for our evaluation may render some of our claims statistically insignificant," encouraging future work to address significance testing given the scale (**Section 11.2**).

### 6.6 Summary of Trade-offs

The following table summarizes the key trade-offs inherent in HELM's design:

| Design Choice | Benefit | Trade-off / Limitation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Standardized Prompting** | Enables direct model comparison | May disadvantage models optimized for 0-shot or different adapters (e.g., `T0++`) |
| **Multi-Metric Matrix** | Exposes trade-offs (e.g., accuracy vs. bias) | Increases complexity; no single aggregate score for ranking |
| **Black-Box Evaluation** | Reflects real-world API access; model-agnostic | Prevents interpretability metrics; limits contamination verification |
| **English-Centric** | Feasible given model landscape | Excludes non-English capabilities; limits global applicability |
| **Snapshot Evaluation** | Provides timely assessment of current models | Results may drift as live APIs update; requires continuous maintenance |

In summary, HELM's limitations are largely the inverse of its innovations. The rigor required to standardize evaluation across 30 models necessitates assumptions (e.g., uniform prompting) that may not fit every model equally. The commitment to transparency reveals uncomfortable truths (e.g., contamination, metric failures) that simpler benchmarks obscure. Users of HELM must weigh these trade-offs: the benchmark provides the most comprehensive **comparative** view of language models to date, but its results should be interpreted as a **characterization of capabilities under specific conditions** rather than an absolute measure of model quality.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

HELM is not merely a new benchmark; it is infrastructure for a different kind of AI science. By shifting from single-score leaderboards to multi-dimensional characterization, HELM changes the questions the community can ask about language models. This section outlines how this work reshapes the evaluation landscape, the specific research avenues it unlocks, practical guidance for practitioners deploying these models, and the roadmap required to sustain this holistic approach.

### 7.1 Shifting the Evaluation Paradigm

The most profound implication of HELM is the transition from **ranking** to **characterization**. Traditional benchmarks like `SQuAD` or `GLUE` produce a single number (or a vector of accuracy scores) that implies a total ordering of models: Model A is "better" than Model B. HELM rejects this simplification.

*   **The Score Matrix:** As discussed in **Section 11.3**, HELM produces a score matrix (scenarios × metrics) for each model. This structure forces stakeholders to make explicit value judgments. For example, a mobile application developer might prioritize **efficiency** over marginal accuracy gains, while a medical assistant might prioritize **calibration** and **robustness**. By providing the full matrix, HELM delegates the weighting of these values to the user rather than hiding them behind an aggregate score.
*   **Transparency as Infrastructure:** The release of all raw prompts and completions (**Abstract**, **Section 1.3**) transforms the benchmark from a black-box evaluator into a searchable database of model behavior. This enables **secondary analysis** that the original authors did not anticipate. For instance, researchers can now query specific failure modes (e.g., "Show me all instances where `OPT (175B)` failed on `CivilComments` despite high confidence") to study hallucination patterns or bias propagation without re-running the expensive evaluation pipeline.
*   **Living Benchmark Design:** HELM is explicitly designed as a "living benchmark" (**Section 1.1**). Unlike static datasets that become saturated over time, HELM's modular toolkit allows the community to add new scenarios and metrics as models evolve. This acknowledges that evaluation is a moving target: as models master current tasks, the benchmark must expand to remain discriminative.

### 7.2 Enabling New Research Avenues

HELM's holistic data exposes correlations and trade-offs that were previously invisible, suggesting specific directions for future research.

*   **Efficiency-Capability Trade-offs:** **Section 1.2** notes that only a subset of models lies on the **accuracy-efficiency Pareto frontier**. This finding invites research into **model compression** and **distillation** techniques that specifically target these frontier models. Instead of simply scaling up parameters, researchers can investigate how to move non-frontier models (e.g., large open models with low efficiency) onto the frontier.
*   **Instruction-Tuning Mechanisms:** The finding that instruction-tuned models (`text-davinci-002`, `Anthropic-LM v4-s3`) outperform models 10× their size (**Section 1.2**, **Figure 26**) suggests that **alignment training** (RLHF, instruction tuning) is a more efficient path to capability than pure pretraining scale. Future work should isolate *which* aspects of instruction tuning drive these gains: is it the data quality, the human feedback signal, or the fine-tuning procedure itself?
*   **Bias-Accuracy Tensions:** The BBQ results (**Section 1.2**, **Figure 40**) reveal a troubling correlation: the most accurate models exhibit biases that align with societal discrimination in ambiguous contexts, while less accurate models show biases in the opposite direction. This challenges the assumption that "better models are fairer." It suggests a need for **debiasing techniques** that do not sacrifice accuracy, or perhaps a re-evaluation of how we measure bias in high-performing systems.
*   **Contamination Detection:** The extensive documentation of train-test contamination (**Section G**, **Table 13**) highlights a critical validity threat. This enables research into **contamination detection algorithms** (e.g., using n-gram overlap or watermarking) that can be applied retroactively to existing models. It also pressures model providers to disclose training data compositions to maintain evaluation legitimacy.
*   **Adaptation Robustness:** The finding that adaptation methods (joint vs. separate) significantly swing accuracy (**Section 8.2**, **Figure 33**) suggests that **prompt engineering** is not just a user skill but a model capability. Future research could focus on **adaptive prompting systems** that automatically select the optimal adaptation strategy for a given model-scenario pair, rather than relying on uniform standardization.

### 7.3 Practical Guidance for Practitioners

For engineers and product managers deploying language models, HELM provides a risk assessment toolkit rather than a simple procurement list.

*   **When to Use HELM:**
    *   **General-Purpose Deployment:** If you are selecting a model for a user-facing application where multiple qualities matter (e.g., a chatbot that must be accurate, safe, and fast), HELM's multi-metric scores are essential. Use the **efficiency** metrics (**Section 4.9**) to estimate inference costs and the **toxicity/bias** metrics (**Section 4.7**, **Section 4.8**) to assess reputational risk.
    *   **Risk Auditing:** Use the **robustness** and **fairness** perturbations (**Section 4.5**, **Section 4.6**) to stress-test models before deployment. For example, if your application serves diverse demographics, examine the **performance disparities** on `CivilComments` (**Section 8.3**) to anticipate potential failures on specific subgroups.
    *   **Resource-Constrained Selection:** If you cannot run the full benchmark, use HELM's **priority system** (**Section H**). Evaluate models on Priority 1 scenarios first (e.g., core QA, sentiment) to get a high-signal comparison without the full computational cost.

*   **When to Look Elsewhere:**
    *   **Domain-Specific Tasks:** HELM explicitly lacks coverage in **biomedical, clinical, financial, and legal domains** (**Section 10.1**). If your use case falls here, HELM's core scenarios may not reflect your data distribution. You should supplement HELM with domain-specific benchmarks (e.g., `MedQA`, `LexGLUE`).
    *   **Interactive or Multimodal Needs:** HELM evaluates static text-to-text interfaces. It does not cover **dialogue agents**, **tool use**, or **vision-language models** (**Section 10.1**, **Section 11.2**). For these capabilities, refer to benchmarks like `BIG-Bench` (for diverse tasks) or specific multimodal evaluations.
    *   **Non-English Deployment:** While HELM includes some English varieties (**Section 5.1**), it is predominantly English-centric. For multilingual deployments, rely on benchmarks like `XTREME` or `BLOOM`'s specific evaluations, as HELM's findings may not generalize to other languages.

*   **Interpreting the Results:**
    *   **Beware the Snapshot:** Remember that HELM results are a **point-in-time assessment** (**Section 6**). Live APIs (e.g., OpenAI models) may update silently. Use HELM for relative comparison, but validate critical performance metrics on your own held-out test sets before production release.
    *   **Contextualize Contamination:** When comparing models, check **Table 13** for contamination flags. If Model A has strong contamination on a scenario and Model B does not, treat Model A's accuracy on that scenario as an upper bound rather than a generalization capability.

### 7.4 Roadmap for the Community

HELM identifies specific gaps that the broader AI community must address to mature the field of foundation model evaluation.

*   **Metric Development:**
    *   **Toxicity and Bias:** The reliance on **Perspective API** (**Section 4.8**) is a known limitation. Future work should develop toxicity detectors that account for **context** and **reclaimed speech**, and bias metrics that move beyond count-based asymmetries to structural harms.
    *   **Faithfulness:** For summarization and QA, automated metrics like ROUGE fail to capture quality (**Section 1.2**). Research into **reference-free faithfulness metrics** that correlate with human judgment is critical.
    *   **User Experience:** HELM currently lacks metrics for **user experience** and **interaction quality** (**Section 10.2**). As models become interfaces, metrics capturing latency perception, helpfulness, and conversational flow are needed.

*   **Scenario Expansion:**
    *   **High-Stakes Domains:** The community must build validated datasets for **clinical, legal, and financial reasoning** (**Section 10.1**). These domains require higher standards for accuracy and faithfulness than general-purpose tasks.
    *   **Interactive Tasks:** Evaluation must move beyond static prompts to **multi-turn dialogue** and **agent-based workflows** (**Section 10.1**). This requires new metrics for consistency, memory, and goal completion over time.
    *   **Multilingual Coverage:** To avoid reinforcing English-centric power dynamics, benchmarks must expand to **typologically diverse languages** (**Section 10.1**). This requires not just translation, but culturally situated tasks that reflect local values and use cases.

*   **Governance and Access:**
    *   **Structured Access:** The gap between open and closed models (**Section 1.2**, **Figure 28**) highlights a transparency crisis. HELM advocates for **structured access** mechanisms (**Section 10.4**) where researchers can evaluate closed models under nondisclosure agreements without full weight release.
    *   **Contamination Standards:** Model providers should adopt **canary tokens** or **data disclosure standards** to allow evaluators to verify training data overlap. This is essential for maintaining the validity of few-shot evaluation claims (**Section G**).

*   **Sustainability:**
    *   **Efficiency Reporting:** Standardize the reporting of **training energy and carbon emissions** (**Section 4.9**). HELM's estimation methods (**Section C.7**) are approximate due to missing data; direct measurement by providers would improve accuracy and accountability.
    *   **Adaptive Evaluation:** As models grow, full evaluation becomes prohibitively expensive. The community should refine HELM's **priority system** (**Section H**) into a standardized **knapsack optimization** problem, allowing evaluators to select the most informative scenario subset for a given budget.

In summary, HELM provides the **map** for where language models stand today, but it is the community's responsibility to build the **roads** for where they need to go. By adopting HELM's holistic philosophy—prioritizing transparency, multi-metric measurement, and explicit recognition of limitations—researchers and practitioners can steer foundation model development toward technologies that are not just capable, but trustworthy and societally beneficial.
