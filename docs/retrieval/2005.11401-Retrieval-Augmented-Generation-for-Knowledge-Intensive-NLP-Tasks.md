## 1. Executive Summary

This paper introduces Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), a general-purpose fine-tuning approach that combines a pre-trained BART seq2seq generator with a non-parametric dense vector index of Wikipedia accessed via a DPR retriever to address the inability of standard language models to precisely access, update, or verify factual knowledge. RAG models set the state of the art on three open-domain QA tasks (Natural Questions, WebQuestions, CuratedTrec) and outperform the 11 billion parameter T5-11B model despite having only 626 million trainable parameters, demonstrating that hybrid parametric and non-parametric memory is more efficient than scaling parameters alone. Furthermore, RAG generates more specific and factual language than parametric-only baselines on generation tasks like Jeopardy question generation and allows for knowledge updates via index hot-swapping without retraining.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the contribution of this paper, we must first establish the landscape of language models at the time of writing. The field was dominated by large pre-trained models that stored all their knowledge internally within their neural network weights. This section explains why that architecture creates specific bottlenecks, why existing solutions were insufficient, and how Retrieval-Augmented Generation (`RAG`) positions itself as a general-purpose bridge between internal knowledge and external facts.

### The Limitations of Parametric Memory
Standard pre-trained language models, such as `BART` or `T5`, rely on **parametric memory**. This means all factual knowledge, reasoning capabilities, and linguistic patterns are compressed into the model's parameters (weights) during training. While powerful, the Introduction identifies three critical downsides to this approach:

*   **Static Knowledge:** Once trained, the model's knowledge is fixed. Updating facts (e.g., "Who is the current President?") requires expensive re-training or fine-tuning, which risks **catastrophic forgetting** (losing previously learned knowledge).
*   **Hallucinations:** Because the model generates text based on probability distributions over its weights, it may produce plausible-sounding but factually incorrect statements. The authors note these models "may produce 'hallucinations'" (Introduction).
*   **Lack of Provenance:** When a parametric model makes a claim, it cannot cite its source. There is no way to inspect *where* the model retrieved a specific fact, making verification difficult.

These limitations are particularly damaging for **knowledge-intensive tasks**, which the paper defines as "tasks that humans could not reasonably be expected to perform without access to an external knowledge source" (Introduction). Examples include open-domain question answering or generating specific factual summaries.

### The Promise of Non-Parametric Memory
To address these issues, researchers began exploring **non-parametric memory**. This refers to external knowledge stores, such as a database or document index, that the model can query during inference. The theoretical benefit is clear:
*   **Updateability:** You can update the external index without changing the model weights.
*   **Interpretability:** You can inspect the retrieved documents to understand why the model generated a specific output.

However, prior to this work, integrating non-parametric memory into generation models remained an open research problem. The Introduction states that "Pre-trained models with a differentiable access mechanism to explicit non-parametric memory have so far been only investigated for extractive downstream tasks."

### Prior Approaches and Their Gaps
Before `RAG`, two main strands of research attempted to incorporate external knowledge, but both had significant limitations that this paper aims to resolve.

1.  **Extractive Retrieval Models (e.g., `REALM`, `ORQA`):**
    Models like `REALM` [20] and `ORQA` [31] combined masked language models with differentiable retrievers. They were successful in open-domain question answering but were restricted to **extractive tasks**.
    *   *How they worked:* The model retrieved documents and then selected a specific span of text (a substring) from those documents as the answer.
    *   *The Gap:* They could not generate free-form text. If the answer required synthesizing information from multiple sentences or rephrasing content, these models struggled. The authors note these approaches "have only explored open-domain extractive question answering" (Introduction).

2.  **Memory Networks trained from Scratch:**
    Earlier architectures like Memory Networks [64, 55] or stack-augmented networks [25] utilized external memory but were typically trained from scratch for specific tasks.
    *   *How they worked:* They learned both the memory access mechanism and the task logic simultaneously during task-specific training.
    *   *The Gap:* They did not leverage pre-trained knowledge. The Introduction highlights that in contrast, `RAG` explores a setting where "both parametric and non-parametric memory components are pre-trained and pre-loaded with extensive knowledge." This ensures the ability to access knowledge is present without additional training.

### Positioning RAG: A General-Purpose Hybrid
This paper positions `RAG` as the "workhorse of NLP" for knowledge-intensive tasks by extending hybrid memory to **sequence-to-sequence (`seq2seq`) generation**. Unlike prior work that focused solely on classification or extraction, `RAG` is designed for general generation.

The architecture combines two pre-trained components (Figure 1):
*   **Parametric Memory:** A pre-trained `seq2seq` transformer (`BART` [32]), responsible for generating fluent language.
*   **Non-Parametric Memory:** A dense vector index of Wikipedia, accessed via a pre-trained neural retriever (`DPR` [26]).

Crucially, `RAG` is not just a pipeline where retrieval happens before generation. It is trained **end-to-end**. The retriever and generator are jointly learned via a latent variable approach, where the retrieved document $z$ is treated as latent data marginalized during training (Section 2). This allows the model to learn *what* to retrieve to minimize generation error, rather than relying on fixed retrieval supervision.

By framing retrieval as a latent variable within a generative model, `RAG` aims to unify the flexibility of generative models with the accuracy of retrieval-based systems. The Discussion section reinforces this positioning, noting that the work "opens up new research directions on how parametric and non-parametric memories interact and how to most effectively combine them" (Section 6). This setup allows the model to handle tasks ranging from question answering to abstractive generation while maintaining the ability to update knowledge simply by swapping the document index (Section 4.5).

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
This system is a hybrid neural architecture that dynamically queries a searchable Wikipedia index before generating text, effectively giving the model a "open-book" test environment during inference. It solves the problem of factual inconsistency in language models by treating retrieved documents as latent variables that are optimized end-to-end, allowing the model to learn which documents are useful for generating the correct answer without explicit retrieval labels.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The architecture consists of three primary components connected in a sequential pipeline, as illustrated in Figure 1 of the paper.
*   **Query Encoder:** Takes the input text sequence $x$ (e.g., a question) and converts it into a dense vector representation $q(x)$.
*   **Document Index (Non-Parametric Memory):** Contains 21 million pre-computed vector representations of Wikipedia passages; it receives the query vector and returns the top-$K$ most similar document vectors $z$ using Maximum Inner Product Search (MIPS).
*   **Generator (Parametric Memory):** A sequence-to-sequence transformer (BART) that receives the original input $x$ concatenated with the retrieved text $z$ to generate the target sequence $y$ token by token.

The system operates in two distinct modes depending on how the retrieved documents are used during generation: **RAG-Sequence**, which selects one document to generate the entire answer, and **RAG-Token**, which can switch between different retrieved documents for each individual token of the answer.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Formal Probabilistic Model:** We first define the mathematical objective, explaining how the model marginalizes over retrieved documents to calculate the probability of the output.
*   **Retrieval Mechanism:** We detail the Dense Passage Retriever (DPR) component, including the encoder architecture and the similarity search process.
*   **Generation Mechanism:** We explain how the BART generator integrates retrieved text and the specific concatenation strategy used.
*   **Training Procedure:** We describe the end-to-end fine-tuning process, specifying which parameters are updated and how the loss function drives retrieval learning.
*   **Decoding Strategies:** We distinguish between the inference algorithms used for RAG-Sequence versus RAG-Token, including the "Thorough" and "Fast" decoding approximations.
*   **Index Configuration:** We specify the scale and construction of the non-parametric memory, including document chunking and indexing technology.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown
This is a methodological paper proposing a general-purpose fine-tuning recipe that combines pre-trained parametric memory (a seq2seq model) with non-parametric memory (a dense vector index) for language generation. The core technical innovation lies in treating the retrieved document as a latent variable $z$ that is marginalized out during training, enabling the retriever and generator to be optimized jointly without direct supervision on which document should be retrieved.

**The Data Pipeline and Information Flow**
The system processes an input query $x$ through a strict sequence of operations to produce an output $y$.
1.  **Encoding:** The input sequence $x$ is passed through a query encoder to produce a dense vector $q(x)$.
2.  **Retrieval:** The system performs a Maximum Inner Product Search (MIPS) against the document index to find the top-$K$ documents $z$ that maximize the similarity score $d(z)^\top q(x)$.
3.  **Conditioning:** The text content of the retrieved documents is concatenated with the original input $x$ to form an extended context sequence.
4.  **Generation:** The generator model processes this extended context to predict the probability distribution of the next token $y_i$, conditioned on previous tokens $y_{1:i-1}$.
5.  **Marginalization:** The final probability of the output sequence is calculated by summing (marginalizing) the probabilities across all $K$ retrieved documents, weighted by the retriever's confidence in each document.

**Probabilistic Formulation and Marginalization**
The paper defines two distinct formulations for calculating the probability of the target sequence $y$ given input $x$, differing in how they handle the latent document variable $z$.

The **RAG-Sequence Model** assumes that a single retrieved document is responsible for generating the entire target sequence. It treats the document $z$ as a latent variable that is marginalized once per sequence. The probability is approximated using a top-$K$ sum over the retrieved documents:
$$
p_{\text{RAG-Sequence}}(y|x) \approx \sum_{z \in \text{top-}k(p(\cdot|x))} p_\eta(z|x) p_\theta(y|x, z)
$$
Here, $p_\eta(z|x)$ is the retriever probability, and $p_\theta(y|x, z)$ is the generator probability of the full sequence $y$ given document $z$. This formulation expands to:
$$
\sum_{z \in \text{top-}k(p(\cdot|x))} p_\eta(z|x) \prod_{i}^{N} p_\theta(y_i|x, z, y_{1:i-1})
$$
In this equation, the same document $z$ is used for every token $i$ in the sequence of length $N$.

The **RAG-Token Model** allows the system to draw a different latent document for each target token, enabling the generator to synthesize information from multiple sources within a single answer. The marginalization happens at every token step:
$$
p_{\text{RAG-Token}}(y|x) \approx \prod_{i}^{N} \sum_{z \in \text{top-}k(p(\cdot|x))} p_\eta(z|x) p_\theta(y_i|x, z, y_{1:i-1})
$$
Notice that the summation over $z$ occurs inside the product over tokens $i$. This design choice allows the model to attend to Document A for the first part of the answer and Document B for the second part, which is particularly useful for complex queries requiring multi-hop reasoning or composite facts.

**Retrieval Component Specifications**
The retriever $p_\eta(z|x)$ is based on the Dense Passage Retriever (DPR) architecture, which follows a bi-encoder design.
*   **Encoders:** Both the document encoder and query encoder are based on `BERTBASE`. The document encoder produces a dense representation $d(z)$, and the query encoder produces $q(x)$.
*   **Similarity Score:** The probability of retrieving a document is proportional to the exponential of the inner product between the document and query vectors: $p_\eta(z|x) \propto \exp(d(z)^\top q(x))$.
*   **Initialization:** The retriever is initialized using a pre-trained bi-encoder from DPR that was originally trained to retrieve documents containing answers for TriviaQA and Natural Questions.
*   **Fixed Index:** To avoid the computational cost of periodically updating the document index during training (as done in REALM [20]), the paper keeps the document encoder fixed. Only the query encoder $BERT_q$ and the generator are fine-tuned.

**Generator Component Specifications**
The generator $p_\theta(y_i|x, z, y_{1:i-1})$ is implemented using `BART-large`, a pre-trained sequence-to-sequence transformer with 400 million parameters.
*   **Input Formatting:** To combine the input $x$ with retrieved content $z$, the system simply concatenates the text strings. For multiple retrieved documents, the paper implies they are handled via the marginalization process rather than concatenating all $K$ documents into a single massive context window (which would exceed transformer limits).
*   **Parametric Memory:** The BART parameters $\theta$ serve as the parametric memory, storing linguistic knowledge and reasoning capabilities that complement the factual knowledge in the index.

**Training Procedure and Optimization**
The model is trained end-to-end using stochastic gradient descent with the Adam optimizer.
*   **Objective:** The training objective is to minimize the negative marginal log-likelihood of the target sequence: $\sum_j -\log p(y_j|x_j)$.
*   **Latent Variable Learning:** Because the document $z$ is treated as latent, the model learns to retrieve relevant documents implicitly. If retrieving a specific document leads to a lower generation loss, the retriever parameters $\eta$ are updated to increase the probability of selecting that document for similar queries in the future.
*   **Parameter Updates:** During training, the document encoder (part of the index) is frozen. Only the query encoder (110M parameters) and the BART generator (406M parameters) are updated, totaling 626M trainable parameters. This is significantly fewer than the 11 billion parameters in comparable closed-book models like T5-11B.
*   **Retrieval Count:** During training, the system retrieves the top $k$ documents where $k \in \{5, 10\}$.

**Decoding Strategies at Inference**
At test time, the system must approximate $\arg \max_y p(y|x)$, but the two models require different decoding algorithms due to their marginalization structures.

For **RAG-Token**, decoding is straightforward because the likelihood breaks into conventional per-token probabilities. The system plugs the marginalized transition probability $p'_\theta(y_i|x, y_{1:i-1}) = \sum_z p_\eta(z|x)p_\theta(y_i|x, z, y_{1:i-1})$ into a standard beam search decoder.

For **RAG-Sequence**, the likelihood does not decompose per token, so a single beam search is insufficient. The paper proposes two methods:
*   **Thorough Decoding:** The system runs beam search independently for each retrieved document $z$. This yields a set of hypotheses $Y$. To estimate the true probability of a hypothesis $y$, the system runs an additional forward pass for any document $z$ where $y$ did not appear in the beam, multiplies the generator probability by the retriever probability $p_\eta(z|x)$, and sums across all documents.
*   **Fast Decoding:** To reduce computational cost, this method approximates that $p_\theta(y|x, z_i) \approx 0$ if $y$ was not generated during the beam search for document $z_i$. This avoids additional forward passes but relies on the assumption that unlikely hypotheses won't appear in the beams.
*   **Configuration:** For Open-domain QA, the paper uses Thorough Decoding with 50 retrieved documents for RAG-Sequence. For generation tasks like MS-MARCO, it uses Fast Decoding with 10 retrieved documents.

**Index Construction and Scale**
The non-parametric memory is constructed from a specific snapshot of Wikipedia to ensure reproducibility and fixed evaluation conditions.
*   **Source:** The December 2018 Wikipedia dump is used.
*   **Chunking:** Each article is split into disjoint 100-word chunks, resulting in a total of 21 million documents.
*   **Indexing Technology:** The document embeddings are stored in a FAISS index using a Hierarchical Navigable Small World (HNSW) approximation for sub-linear time retrieval.
*   **Memory Footprint:** The index consists of 21 million 768-dimensional vectors. While this originally required ~100 GB of CPU memory, the authors note that compressing the index using FAISS tools reduces the requirement to 36 GB.
*   **Hot-Swapping:** A key technical feature is that this index can be replaced at test time without retraining the model. The paper demonstrates this by swapping the 2018 index with a December 2016 index to update knowledge about world leaders, proving the decoupling of knowledge storage from model parameters.

**Design Choices and Trade-offs**
The authors make several specific engineering choices to balance performance and computational feasibility.
*   **Fixed Document Encoder:** Updating the document encoder would require rebuilding the 21M document index periodically during training. The authors found this unnecessary for strong performance, so they fixed it to save computation.
*   **Concatenation vs. Attention:** Instead of building complex cross-attention mechanisms between the generator and the retrieved vectors, the system concatenates raw text. This leverages BART's existing pre-training on text sequences without architectural modification.
*   **Top-K Approximation:** Summing over all 21 million documents is impossible. The system approximates the marginalization by summing only over the top-$K$ retrieved documents (e.g., $K=50$ at test time for QA). The paper notes in Section 4.5 that performance generally improves monotonically with more retrieved documents for RAG-Sequence, justifying the cost of larger $K$ at inference.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

While the architectural components of `RAG` (BART, DPR, FAISS) existed prior to this work, the paper's true contribution lies in how these components are combined to solve fundamental limitations of language modeling. The following insights distinguish `RAG` from prior retrieval-augmented models and explain why it achieves state-of-the-art performance with significantly fewer parameters.

### 4.1 Generalizing Latent Retrieval to Sequence-to-Sequence Generation
The most fundamental innovation in this paper is extending differentiable latent retrieval from **extractive** tasks to **generative** tasks. Prior models like `REALM` [20] and `ORQA` [31] successfully learned to retrieve documents without supervision, but they were restricted to extracting answer spans directly from the text.

*   **Why this matters:** By moving to a `seq2seq` generator, `RAG` unlocks the ability to synthesize information. The paper notes in Section 4.1 that "documents with clues about the answer but do not contain the answer verbatim can still contribute towards a correct answer being generated."
*   **Evidence:** This capability allows `RAG` to outperform extractive models even on extractive tasks. In Table 1, `RAG-Sequence` achieves 44.5 Exact Match (EM) on Natural Questions, surpassing the extractive `DPR` system (41.5 EM).
*   **The Insight:** Retrieval does not need to be perfect for generation to succeed. A generative model can bridge the gap between a retrieved clue and the final answer using its parametric memory, whereas an extractive model fails completely if the exact answer string is not present in the retrieved text.

### 4.2 Marginalization Strategies Enable Multi-Document Synthesis
The paper introduces two distinct probabilistic formulations—`RAG-Sequence` and `RAG-Token`—which are not merely implementation details but represent different reasoning capabilities. This distinction is a novel modeling contribution that allows users to trade off between consistency and synthesis.

*   **RAG-Sequence (Consistency):** By marginalizing over documents once per sequence, this model assumes a single source of truth for the entire answer. This is ideal for tasks requiring coherent, single-source grounding.
*   **RAG-Token (Synthesis):** By marginalizing at every token step, this model can attend to different documents for different parts of the answer.
*   **Why this matters:** This enables **multi-hop reasoning** within a single generation pass. The paper provides a compelling visualization in Figure 2 (Jeopardy generation). When generating the question "The Sun Also Rises is a novel by this author of A Farewell to Arms", the model dynamically shifts its posterior probability:
    *   Document 2 dominates when generating "The Sun Also Rises".
    *   Document 1 dominates when generating "A Farewell to Arms".
*   **The Insight:** `RAG-Token` effectively learns to "stitch together" facts from multiple sources without explicit supervision on which document contains which fact. This explains why `RAG-Token` outperforms `RAG-Sequence` on Jeopardy question generation (Table 2), a task requiring the combination of distinct factual elements.

### 4.3 Decoupling Knowledge Storage from Model Parameters
A critical practical innovation is the demonstration that knowledge can be updated via **index hot-swapping** without retraining the neural weights. This directly addresses the "Static Knowledge" limitation identified in Section 2.

*   **Mechanism:** Because the non-parametric memory (the Wikipedia index) is separate from the parametric memory (BART weights), replacing the index updates the model's world knowledge instantly.
*   **Evidence:** In Section 4.5, the authors swap the December 2018 index with a December 2016 index. When queried about world leaders who changed between these dates, accuracy drops significantly when using the mismatched index (e.g., 4% accuracy using the 2016 index for 2018 leaders).
*   **The Insight:** This proves that `RAG` relies on the index for factual specificity rather than memorizing facts in the weights. For deployed systems, this means factual updates (e.g., new medical guidelines, political changes) require only an index refresh rather than expensive fine-tuning or re-training, mitigating the risk of catastrophic forgetting.

### 4.4 Parameter Efficiency Challenges Scaling Laws
The results challenge the prevailing notion that performance gains in NLP require massive increases in parameter count. `RAG` achieves superior performance with a fraction of the parameters required by closed-book models.

*   **Comparison:** Appendix G notes that `RAG` has **626 million trainable parameters** (query encoder + generator). In contrast, the best performing closed-book model, `T5-11B`, has **11 billion parameters**.
*   **Performance:** Despite being ~17x smaller, `RAG-Sequence` outperforms `T5-11B` on Natural Questions (44.5 vs 34.5 EM in Table 1).
*   **The Insight:** For knowledge-intensive tasks, external memory is a more efficient storage mechanism than internal weights. Scaling model size alone yields diminishing returns compared to providing explicit access to a knowledge base. This suggests a shift in architectural design where model capacity is reserved for reasoning and language modeling, while facts are offloaded to non-parametric memory.

### 4.5 Learning Retrieval Without Retrieval Supervision
Finally, `RAG` demonstrates that a retriever can be optimized purely via **generation loss**, without any direct supervision signal indicating which document is "correct."

*   **Mechanism:** During training, the system minimizes the negative log-likelihood of the target sequence $-\log p(y|x)$. The gradient flows back through the marginalization to update the query encoder, teaching it to retrieve documents that lower the generation loss.
*   **Evidence:** Table 6 shows ablations where the retriever is frozen (not trained). `RAG-Token` on Natural Questions drops from 43.5 EM (trained) to 37.8 EM (frozen).
*   **The Insight:** The model learns a task-specific retrieval strategy implicitly. It does not simply retrieve documents that look similar to the query; it retrieves documents that are *useful for generating the answer*. This allows `RAG` to be applied to tasks where retrieval supervision (gold evidence documents) is unavailable, such as open-domain generation or fact verification (Section 4.4), widening its applicability compared to pipeline systems that require supervised retrievers.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validated `RAG`'s capabilities across diverse knowledge-intensive tasks. We will walk through the evaluation methodology, analyze the quantitative results with specific numbers, assess whether the experiments support the paper's claims, and examine ablation studies that reveal when and why `RAG` succeeds or struggles.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The authors designed experiments to test `RAG` across four distinct task categories, each probing different aspects of knowledge-intensive language understanding and generation.

**Datasets and Task Categories**

*   **Open-Domain Question Answering (Section 3.1):** Four standard benchmarks test factual retrieval and answer generation:
    *   `Natural Questions (NQ)` [29]: 79,169 training examples, 3,611 test examples (Appendix Table 7)
    *   `TriviaQA (TQA)` [24]: 78,786 training examples, 11,314 test examples
    *   `WebQuestions (WQ)` [3]: 3,418 training examples, 2,033 test examples
    *   `CuratedTrec (CT)` [2]: 635 training examples, 635 test examples
    *   *Metric:* Exact Match (EM) — the generated answer must exactly match a gold answer string.

*   **Abstractive Question Answering (Section 3.2):**
    *   `MS-MARCO NLG v2.1` [1]: 153,726 training examples, 101,093 test examples (hidden subset)
    *   *Key difference:* Unlike open-domain QA, this task provides gold passages during training, but `RAG` ignores them and retrieves from Wikipedia independently.
    *   *Metric:* BLEU-1 and Rouge-L for generation quality.

*   **Jeopardy Question Generation (Section 3.3):**
    *   `SearchQA` [10] splits: 100K train, 14K dev, 27K test examples
    *   *Task:* Generate a Jeopardy-style clue given an answer entity (e.g., answer = "Washington", generate = "It's the only U.S. state named for a U.S. president")
    *   *Metrics:* Q-BLEU-1 (entity-weighted BLEU), plus human evaluation for factuality and specificity.

*   **Fact Verification (Section 3.4):**
    *   `FEVER` [56]: 145,450 training examples, 10,000 test examples (3-way classification)
    *   *Task:* Classify claims as "supports", "refutes", or "not enough info" based on Wikipedia evidence.
    *   *Metric:* Label Accuracy. Crucially, `RAG` does **not** use supervision on which evidence documents are correct.

**Baselines for Comparison**

The authors compare `RAG` against three categories of prior systems:

*   **Closed-Book (Parametric Only):** `T5-11B` [52] with 11 billion parameters — tests whether external memory beats massive parameter scaling.
*   **Extractive Retrieval (Open-Book):** `REALM` [20] and `DPR` [26] — test whether generation beats extraction on QA tasks.
*   **Generative Baseline:** `BART-large` [32] with 400M parameters — tests whether retrieval augmentation improves a comparable generative model.

**Experimental Setup Details**

*   **Retrieval Count:** During training, $k \in \{5, 10\}$ documents are retrieved. At test time, $k$ is tuned on development data (Section 3).
*   **Decoding Configuration (Appendix A):**
    *   Open-domain QA: `RAG-Token` uses 15 retrieved documents; `RAG-Sequence` uses 50 retrieved documents with Thorough Decoding.
    *   Generation tasks (MS-MARCO, Jeopardy): Both models use 10 retrieved documents with Fast Decoding for `RAG-Sequence`.
*   **Training:** All models trained with Adam optimizer using mixed precision on 8 NVIDIA V100 GPUs (Appendix C).
*   **Index:** December 2018 Wikipedia dump, 21 million 100-word chunks, FAISS HNSW index (Section 3).

The results demonstrate that `RAG` achieves state-of-the-art performance on knowledge-intensive tasks while using far fewer parameters than closed-book alternatives.

### 5.3 Do the Experiments Support the Paper's Claims?

The experimental results provide strong evidence for the paper's core claims, though some claims are better supported than others.

**Claim 1: RAG achieves state-of-the-art on knowledge-intensive tasks.**

*   **Supported:** Table 1 shows `RAG` sets new records on NQ, WQ, and CT. The TQA result is state-of-the-art on the Wiki test set (68.0 EM).
*   **Caveat:** On TQA standard test set, `DPR` (57.9 EM) slightly edges out `RAG-Sequence` (56.8 EM), though both outperform closed-book models.

**Claim 2: RAG is more parameter-efficient than closed-book models.**

*   **Strongly Supported:** `RAG` (626M parameters) outperforms `T5-11B` (11B parameters) on NQ (44.5 vs 34.5 EM). This is a **17x parameter reduction** for better performance.
*   **Implication:** For knowledge-intensive tasks, external memory is more efficient than scaling parameters.

**Claim 3: RAG generates more factual and specific language.**

*   **Strongly Supported:** Human evaluation (Table 4) shows `RAG` is preferred for factuality by a **6:1 margin** (42.7% vs 7.1%). Diversity metrics (Table 5) confirm `RAG` produces less generic text.
*   **Evidence:** Table 3 shows concrete examples where `BART` hallucinates ("The middle ear is the part of the ear between the middle ear and the nose") while `RAG` generates factually correct responses.

**Claim 4: Knowledge can be updated via index hot-swapping.**

*   **Supported:** Section 4.5 demonstrates that swapping the 2018 index for a 2016 index changes model behavior appropriately:
    *   2016 index on 2016 leaders: **70% accuracy**
    *   2018 index on 2018 leaders: **68% accuracy**
    *   Mismatched indices: **12% and 4% accuracy** respectively
*   **Significance:** This proves factual knowledge resides in the index, not the model weights.

**Claim 5: Learned retrieval improves performance.**

*   **Supported:** Table 6 ablations show freezing the retriever drops NQ performance from **43.5 EM to 37.8 EM** for `RAG-Token`.
*   **Implication:** The model learns task-specific retrieval strategies, not just generic similarity matching.

### 5.4 Ablation Studies and Robustness Checks

The authors conduct several ablations to understand which components drive performance.

**Retrieval Mechanism Ablation (Table 6)**

| Model Variant | NQ (EM) | TQA (EM) | FEVER-3 Acc |
|---------------|---------|----------|-------------|
| RAG-Token (full) | 43.5 | 54.8 | 74.5 |
| RAG-Token-Frozen (retriever not trained) | 37.8 | 50.1 | 72.9 |
| RAG-Token-BM25 (word overlap retriever) | 29.7 | 41.5 | 75.1 |

*   **Learned retrieval matters:** Freezing the retriever drops NQ performance by **5.7 EM points**, confirming end-to-end training improves retrieval quality.
*   **Dense vs. Sparse:** `BM25` (word overlap) performs worse on QA tasks (29.7 vs 43.5 EM on NQ) but performs competitively on FEVER (75.1% vs 74.5%). The authors note FEVER claims are "heavily entity-centric and thus well-suited for word overlap-based retrieval" (Section 4.5).

**Number of Retrieved Documents (Figure 3)**

The authors test how performance scales with $K$ retrieved documents at test time:

*   **RAG-Sequence (NQ):** Performance **monotonically improves** from 10 to 50 documents (Figure 3, left). This justifies using 50 documents for QA at inference.
*   **RAG-Token (NQ):** Performance **peaks at 10 documents** and plateaus or slightly declines beyond that.
*   **MS-MARCO (Figure 3, right):** More documents increase Rouge-L for `RAG-Token` but decrease BLEU-1, indicating a trade-off between recall and precision in generation.

**Model Variant Comparison**

Across tasks, `RAG-Sequence` and `RAG-Token` show complementary strengths:

*   **QA Tasks:** `RAG-Sequence` generally performs better (Table 1: 44.5 vs 44.1 on NQ; 56.8 vs 55.2 on TQA).
*   **Generation Tasks:** `RAG-Token` generally performs better (Table 2: Jeopardy Q-BLEU 22.2 vs 21.4; Table 5: higher diversity for Sequence but Token preferred by humans in Section 4.3).
*   **Reasoning:** Section 4.3 explains `RAG-Token` excels at Jeopardy because questions "often contain two separate pieces of information" that can be drawn from different documents (Figure 2 visualization).

### 5.5 Limitations and Failure Cases

While the results are strong, the experiments reveal important limitations and conditional findings.

**Retrieval Collapse (Appendix H)**

In preliminary experiments on tasks like story generation, the retriever would "collapse" and learn to retrieve the same documents regardless of input. Once this happened:
*   The generator learned to ignore the retrieved documents.
*   `RAG` performed equivalently to `BART` without retrieval.
*   **Cause:** Tasks with "less-explicit requirement for factual knowledge" or longer target sequences provided less informative gradients for the retriever.

**Dependency on Retrieval Quality**

*   **FEVER Performance Gap:** `RAG` achieves 72.5% on FEVER-3, but state-of-the-art pipeline models reach 76.8% (Table 2). The **4.3% gap** reflects that `RAG` does not use retrieval supervision, while SotA models are trained with gold evidence labels.
*   **MS-MARCO Limitations:** The authors acknowledge "some MSMARCO questions cannot be answered using Wikipedia alone" (Section 3.2), which caps performance. `RAG` approaches SotA (44.2 Rouge-L vs 49.8* SotA) despite this constraint.

**Computational Trade-offs**

*   **Decoding Cost:** `RAG-Sequence` with Thorough Decoding requires running forward passes for each retrieved document separately. With 50 documents at test time, this is **50x more computation** than a single `BART` forward pass.
*   **Index Memory:** The 21M document index requires **36-100 GB of CPU memory** (Appendix C), which may be prohibitive for some deployments.

**Task-Specific Performance Variation**

*   `BM25` outperforms dense retrieval on FEVER (Table 6: 75.1% vs 74.5%), suggesting dense retrieval is not universally superior.
*   `RAG-Token` performance peaks at 10 documents while `RAG-Sequence` continues improving to 50 documents (Figure 3), indicating the two formulations have different scaling characteristics.

### 5.6 Summary Assessment

The experimental analysis provides convincing evidence for `RAG`'s core contributions:

1.  **Performance:** State-of-the-art results on 3 of 4 QA datasets, with competitive performance on generation and classification tasks.
2.  **Efficiency:** 17x fewer parameters than `T5-11B` while achieving better results on knowledge-intensive tasks.
3.  **Factual Grounding:** Human evaluation confirms `RAG` generates more factual and specific text than parametric-only baselines.
4.  **Updateability:** Index hot-swapping experiments demonstrate knowledge can be updated without retraining.

However, the experiments also reveal important constraints: retrieval collapse on low-knowledge tasks, computational overhead from marginalization, and performance gaps when retrieval supervision is available to competitors. These limitations define the boundary conditions for when `RAG` is the appropriate architectural choice.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While `RAG` demonstrates that hybrid memory architectures can outperform massive parametric models on knowledge-intensive tasks, this performance comes with specific engineering costs, architectural assumptions, and boundary conditions. Understanding these limitations is crucial for deciding when to deploy `RAG` versus a standard language model.

### 6.1 Computational and Infrastructure Overhead
The primary trade-off in `RAG` is between **accuracy** and **inference latency**. Unlike a standard `seq2seq` model that performs a single forward pass to generate text, `RAG` must perform retrieval and marginalization at every step.

*   **Inference Latency:** The decoding strategy significantly impacts speed. For `RAG-Sequence`, the "Thorough Decoding" method (Section 2.5) requires running a separate forward pass for each retrieved document to accurately marginalize probabilities. In the Open-domain QA experiments, the authors use **50 retrieved documents** (Appendix A). This implies up to **50 generator forward passes** per query, plus the retrieval time, compared to a single pass for `BART`. While "Fast Decoding" approximates this by ignoring hypotheses not found in the initial beam, it relies on the assumption that $p_\theta(y|x, z_i) \approx 0$ for ungenerated sequences, which may not always hold.
*   **Memory Footprint:** The non-parametric memory requires substantial RAM. The index consists of **21 million documents** represented as **768-dimensional vectors** (Appendix G). While the authors note that FAISS compression tools can reduce the CPU memory requirement from **~100 GB to 36 GB** (Appendix C), this still necessitates a server-grade environment. This contrasts with parametric models like `T5`, where the memory cost is purely the model weights (e.g., `T5-11B` requires significant GPU memory but no external CPU index).
*   **Training Complexity:** Although the document encoder is fixed to save computation (Section 2.4), training still requires managing the interaction between the retriever and generator. The authors note that updating the document encoder during training (as `REALM` does) was deemed "costly" and unnecessary for strong performance, but this design choice limits the system's ability to adapt the index representation to the specific downstream task.

### 6.2 Sensitivity to Retrieval Quality and Task Type
`RAG` assumes that relevant knowledge exists within the retrieved documents and that the retriever can find it. When these assumptions fail, performance degrades.

*   **Retrieval Collapse:** In Appendix H, the authors report a phenomenon called **retrieval collapse** on tasks like story generation. In these scenarios, the retriever learns to retrieve the same documents regardless of the input, causing the generator to ignore the retrieved context entirely. The authors hypothesize this occurs in tasks with a "less-explicit requirement for factual knowledge" or longer target sequences where gradients for the retriever become less informative. This suggests `RAG` is not universally beneficial for all generation tasks, particularly those relying on creativity rather than factuality.
*   **Dense vs. Sparse Retrieval:** While the dense neural retriever (`DPR`) outperforms word-overlap methods on QA tasks, it is not universally superior. In Table 6, **BM25** (a sparse, word-overlap retriever) actually outperforms the dense retriever on FEVER (**75.1%** vs **74.5%** accuracy). The authors explain that FEVER claims are "heavily entity-centric," making keyword matching highly effective. This indicates that the choice of retriever should be task-dependent; a dense vector index is not always the optimal non-parametric memory.
*   **Dependency on Index Coverage:** The system is bounded by the content of its index. In Section 3.2, the authors acknowledge that for MS-MARCO, "some questions cannot be answered using Wikipedia alone." In such cases, `RAG` must rely on its parametric memory, which may be less reliable. Similarly, if the answer exists in the index but the retriever fails to place it in the top-$K$ documents, the generator cannot access it. The "Answer Recall @ K" chart in Figure 3 (center) shows that recall increases with $K$, implying that strict latency constraints (limiting $K$) directly cap performance potential.

### 6.3 Knowledge Boundaries and Temporal Constraints
The "hot-swapping" capability (Section 4.5) allows knowledge updates, but the system still inherits the biases and limitations of the source data.

*   **Static Snapshots:** The experiments use a **December 2018 Wikipedia dump** (Section 3). While the index can be replaced, the model is only as current as the latest index provided. The hot-swapping experiment shows accuracy drops to **4%** when using a 2016 index for 2018 leaders (Section 4.5), proving the model does not memorize these facts. However, this also means the system cannot answer questions about events occurring after the index creation date without an explicit update procedure.
*   **Source Bias:** The Broader Impact section acknowledges that Wikipedia "will probably never be entirely factual and completely devoid of bias." Because `RAG` grounds its generation in this source, it inherits these biases. While this reduces hallucination compared to parametric models, it risks amplifying systemic biases present in the document index. The model provides provenance (the retrieved document), which helps interpretability, but does not solve the underlying truthfulness of the source material.
*   **Domain Specificity:** The current implementation uses a general Wikipedia index. For specialized domains (e.g., medical or legal), a generic index may lack the necessary granularity. While the architecture supports custom indices, building a high-quality dense index for a niche domain requires significant engineering effort (chunking, embedding, indexing) that is not needed for closed-book models.

### 6.4 Open Questions and Architectural Assumptions
The Discussion section (Section 6) highlights several areas where the current approach remains incomplete or exploratory.

*   **Joint Pre-training:** Currently, `RAG` combines components pre-trained separately (`BART` for generation, `DPR` for retrieval). The authors suggest it may be "fruitful to investigate if the two components can be jointly pre-trained from scratch." Separate pre-training might lead to suboptimal alignment between the retriever's representation space and the generator's attention mechanisms.
*   **Parametric vs. Non-Parametric Interaction:** The paper opens research directions on "how parametric and non-parametric memories interact." For instance, Figure 2 shows the model relying on parametric memory to complete book titles once triggered by retrieved documents. However, the precise conditions under which the model trusts its weights versus the retrieved text are not fully understood. In cases of conflict between internal knowledge and retrieved text, the model's behavior may be unpredictable without further study.
*   **Null Document Mechanism:** Appendix F describes experiments with a "Null document" mechanism to handle cases where no useful information is retrieved (similar to `REALM`). The authors found this did not improve performance and omitted it for simplicity. However, this leaves open the question of how the model behaves when retrieval is genuinely unhelpful; the ablation in Appendix H suggests the model might ignore retrieval entirely in such cases, effectively reverting to a standard language model without explicit signaling.

In summary, `RAG` trades computational efficiency and infrastructure complexity for factual accuracy and updateability. It excels when knowledge is explicit, retrievable, and contained within the index, but faces challenges in open-ended generation, tasks suited for sparse retrieval, or scenarios where the index coverage is incomplete.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

The introduction of `RAG` represents more than an incremental improvement in question answering performance—it signals a fundamental shift in how the NLP community approaches knowledge-intensive tasks. This section examines how this work reshapes the architectural landscape, identifies concrete research directions it opens, outlines practical deployment scenarios, and provides guidance on when to adopt retrieval-augmented approaches versus alternatives.

### 7.1 Shifting the Architectural Paradigm

Prior to this work, the dominant strategy for improving language model performance was **parameter scaling**. Models like `T5-11B` demonstrated that increasing parameter count yielded better results, leading to an arms race toward billion-parameter systems. `RAG` challenges this paradigm by demonstrating that **external memory can substitute for internal parameters** on knowledge-intensive tasks.

The evidence is striking: `RAG` achieves **44.5 EM on Natural Questions** with only **626 million trainable parameters**, while `T5-11B` achieves **34.5 EM** with **11 billion parameters** (Table 1, Appendix G). This **17x parameter reduction** for superior performance suggests that for factual tasks, non-parametric memory is a more efficient storage mechanism than neural weights.

> "Our work opens up new research directions on how parametric and non-parametric memories interact and how to most effectively combine them, showing promise in being applied to a wide variety of NLP tasks." (Section 6, Discussion)

This architectural shift has three cascading implications:

*   **Democratization of Performance:** Organizations without resources to train 11-billion-parameter models can achieve state-of-the-art results using `RAG`'s hybrid approach. The computational barrier to entry for knowledge-intensive NLP is lowered.
*   **Separation of Concerns:** Language modeling (fluency, reasoning) and knowledge storage (facts, entities) become separable concerns. The generator focuses on language quality while the index handles factual accuracy.
*   **General-Purpose Retrieval Architecture:** Unlike prior work that built task-specific retrieval pipelines (Section 5, Related Work), `RAG` demonstrates a **single architecture** applicable across QA, generation, and classification tasks. This unification reduces engineering complexity for multi-task systems.

### 7.2 Enabled Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions that subsequent research can address. These directions extend beyond the immediate contributions to shape the next generation of retrieval-augmented systems.

**Joint Pre-training of Retriever and Generator**

Currently, `RAG` combines components pre-trained separately: `BART` for generation and `DPR` for retrieval. The Discussion section notes:

> "In future work, it may be fruitful to investigate if the two components can be jointly pre-trained from scratch, either with a denoising objective similar to BART or some another objective." (Section 6)

This suggests a significant opportunity: a model where the retriever and generator are co-trained from initialization could develop better alignment between retrieval representations and generation attention mechanisms. The current approach may suffer from **representation mismatch**—the retriever optimizes for document similarity while the generator optimizes for sequence likelihood, with alignment only emerging during fine-tuning.

**Understanding Memory Interaction Dynamics**

Figure 2 provides a visualization of how `RAG-Token` shifts posterior probability across documents during generation, but the paper acknowledges limited understanding of when the model relies on parametric versus non-parametric memory:

> "This example shows how parametric and non-parametric memories work together—the non-parametric component helps to guide the generation, drawing out specific knowledge stored in the parametric memory." (Section 4.3)

Follow-up research should investigate:
*   **Conflict Resolution:** What happens when retrieved documents contradict the model's parametric knowledge? Does the model trust the index or its weights?
*   **Trigger Conditions:** Under what input conditions does the retriever activate versus remain dormant?
*   **Memory Boundaries:** Which types of knowledge are best stored parametrically (e.g., grammar, common sense) versus non-parametrically (e.g., entities, dates, statistics)?

**Preventing Retrieval Collapse**

Appendix H documents a critical failure mode: on tasks like story generation, the retriever can "collapse" and retrieve the same documents regardless of input, causing the generator to ignore retrieval entirely. The authors hypothesize this occurs with "less-explicit requirement for factual knowledge" or longer target sequences.

This observation enables research into:
*   **Regularization Techniques:** Methods to prevent the retriever from converging to a single document distribution.
*   **Task Suitability Metrics:** Criteria for determining whether a task benefits from retrieval augmentation before training begins.
*   **Gradient Flow Analysis:** Understanding why longer sequences provide less informative gradients for the retriever.

**Null Document and Abstention Mechanisms**

Appendix F describes experiments with a "Null document" mechanism to handle cases where no useful information is retrieved, similar to `REALM` [20]. The authors found this did not improve performance and omitted it for simplicity. However, this leaves open questions:

*   When should the model abstain from using retrieval entirely?
*   Can the model learn to signal low confidence when retrieval fails?
*   Would explicit null modeling improve performance on tasks where retrieval is often unhelpful (e.g., MS-MARCO questions not answerable from Wikipedia)?

**Domain-Specific and Multi-Modal Indices**

The current implementation uses a general Wikipedia index. The Broader Impact section suggests endowing `RAG` "with a medical index and asking it open-domain questions on that topic." This points to research on:

*   **Specialized Indices:** Building dense indices for legal, medical, or technical domains where Wikipedia lacks granularity.
*   **Multi-Modal Retrieval:** Extending the non-parametric memory to include images, tables, or structured databases alongside text.
*   **Dynamic Index Updates:** Automating the index refresh process for time-sensitive applications (e.g., news, stock prices).

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

The experimental results identify concrete scenarios where `RAG` provides measurable benefits over alternative approaches.

**Open-Domain Question Answering Systems**

With state-of-the-art results on Natural Questions, WebQuestions, and CuratedTrec (Table 1), `RAG` is immediately applicable to:
*   **Customer Support Chatbots:** Answering factual questions about products, policies, or procedures without maintaining explicit rule sets.
*   **Enterprise Search:** Allowing employees to query internal documentation using natural language, with retrieved documents providing provenance.
*   **Educational Tools:** Generating answers to student questions with citable sources, reducing hallucination risks.

**Fact Verification and Misinformation Detection**

The FEVER results (72.5% accuracy on 3-way classification, within 4.3% of supervised pipelines) suggest applicability to:
*   **News Verification:** Automatically checking claims against trusted sources before publication.
*   **Social Media Moderation:** Flagging potentially false claims with retrieved evidence for human review.
*   **Research Assistance:** Verifying factual claims in academic or technical writing.

**Knowledge-Intensive Content Generation**

The Jeopardy generation and MS-MARCO results demonstrate `RAG` produces more factual, specific, and diverse text than `BART` (Tables 2, 4, 5). Use cases include:
*   **Technical Documentation:** Generating product descriptions or API documentation grounded in source materials.
*   **Medical Summarization:** Creating patient-friendly explanations of medical conditions based on verified sources.
*   **Legal Brief Generation:** Drafting legal arguments with citable case law and statutes.

**Time-Sensitive Knowledge Applications**

The index hot-swapping experiment (Section 4.5) proves knowledge can be updated without retraining. This enables:
*   **News Summarization:** Updating the index with recent articles to answer questions about current events.
*   **Regulatory Compliance:** Refreshing indices when regulations change without retraining models.
*   **Product Catalogs:** Updating e-commerce systems with new products via index refresh rather than model fine-tuning.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

The authors have open-sourced `RAG` as part of the HuggingFace Transformers Library, making adoption straightforward. However, practitioners should consider the following guidance when deciding whether to use `RAG` versus alternatives.

**When to Prefer RAG Over Closed-Book Models**

| Scenario | Recommendation | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| Knowledge-intensive QA | **Use RAG** | Table 1 shows 10+ EM point improvement over `T5-11B` on NQ |
| Factual generation required | **Use RAG** | Table 4 shows 6:1 human preference for factuality over `BART` |
| Knowledge updates expected | **Use RAG** | Section 4.5 proves index hot-swapping works without retraining |
| Interpretability needed | **Use RAG** | Retrieved documents provide provenance for outputs |
| Limited compute budget | **Use RAG** | 626M parameters vs. 11B for comparable performance (Appendix G) |
| Creative/open-ended generation | **Use BART** | Appendix H shows retrieval collapse on story generation |
| Low-latency inference critical | **Use Closed-Book** | `RAG-Sequence` requires up to 50 forward passes (Appendix A) |
| Entity-centric classification | **Consider BM25** | Table 6 shows BM25 outperforms dense retrieval on FEVER |

**When to Use RAG-Sequence vs. RAG-Token**

The two formulations have complementary strengths identified through experiments:

*   **Use RAG-Sequence when:**
    *   Task requires coherent, single-source grounding (e.g., QA with short answers)
    *   Maximum accuracy on extractive-style tasks is needed (Table 1: 44.5 vs. 44.1 EM on NQ)
    *   Generation diversity is important (Table 5: 83.5% vs. 77.8% distinct tri-grams on MS-MARCO)
    *   Computational budget allows Thorough Decoding with 50 documents (Appendix A)

*   **Use RAG-Token when:**
    *   Task requires synthesizing information from multiple sources (e.g., Jeopardy questions)
    *   Multi-hop reasoning is needed within a single generation
    *   Lower latency is required (peaks at 10 documents vs. 50 for Sequence, Figure 3)
    *   Human-rated factuality and specificity are priorities (Section 4.3 human evaluation)

**Infrastructure Requirements**

Practitioners should plan for the following resource requirements based on Appendix C and G:

*   **CPU Memory:** 36-100 GB for the document index (compressible to 36 GB with FAISS tools)
*   **GPU Memory:** 8× 32GB NVIDIA V100 GPUs for training (inference possible on single GPU)
*   **Index Build Time:** Pre-computing 21M document embeddings requires significant one-time computation
*   **Retrieval Latency:** FAISS HNSW provides sub-linear time retrieval, but adds overhead per query

**Integration Checklist**

For teams adopting `RAG`, the following steps ensure successful deployment:

1.  **Index Selection:** Verify your knowledge domain is covered by the index (Wikipedia may not suffice for specialized domains, Section 3.2)
2.  **Retrieval Count Tuning:** Use development data to select optimal $K$ (Figure 3 shows different optima for Sequence vs. Token)
3.  **Decoding Strategy:** Choose Thorough Decoding for accuracy-critical tasks, Fast Decoding for latency-sensitive applications (Section 2.5)
4.  **Retriever Training:** Fine-tune the retriever end-to-end rather than freezing it (Table 6 shows 5.7 EM point drop on NQ when frozen)
5.  **Monitor for Collapse:** Track retrieval diversity during training to detect collapse early (Appendix H)

### 7.5 Long-Term Vision

The `RAG` architecture points toward a future where language models are **knowledge-agnostic**—the same generator can be applied to any domain by swapping the non-parametric index. This vision has profound implications:

*   **Modular AI Systems:** Organizations could maintain a single generator while operating multiple domain-specific indices (medical, legal, technical).
*   **Collaborative Knowledge Bases:** Communities could contribute to shared indices, improving model performance without model retraining.
*   **Verifiable AI:** Every generated claim could be traced to retrieved sources, enabling accountability and audit trails.

> "The fact that it is more strongly grounded in real factual knowledge (in this case Wikipedia) makes it 'hallucinate' less with generations that are more factual, and offers more control and interpretability." (Broader Impact)

However, realizing this vision requires addressing the limitations identified in Section 6: retrieval collapse prevention, better understanding of memory interactions, and efficient inference at scale. The `RAG` paper does not solve these challenges but provides the architectural foundation and empirical evidence to motivate their resolution.

In summary, `RAG` establishes retrieval-augmented generation as a viable alternative to pure parameter scaling for knowledge-intensive tasks. It demonstrates that hybrid architectures can achieve state-of-the-art performance with fewer parameters, enable knowledge updates without retraining, and produce more factual and interpretable outputs. The open-sourced implementation and clear experimental validation provide a foundation for both practical deployment and continued research into the interaction between parametric and non-parametric memory systems.
