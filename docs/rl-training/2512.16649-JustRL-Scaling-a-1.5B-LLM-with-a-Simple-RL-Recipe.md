## 1. Executive Summary

JustRL challenges the prevailing assumption that reinforcement learning for small language models requires complex multi-stage pipelines by demonstrating that single-stage training with fixed hyperparameters achieves state-of-the-art performance. Applied to DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B and OpenMath-Nemotron-1.5B backbones, this minimal recipe reaches 54.9% and 64.3% average accuracy across nine mathematical benchmarks while using 2× less compute than sophisticated approaches like ProRL-V2 and QuestA. The work matters because it exhibits smooth, monotonic improvement over 4,000+ steps without training collapses, suggesting that many standard stabilization techniques may be unnecessary when applying adequate scale to a stable baseline.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this work, we must first examine the divergent paths taken by large versus small language models in developing reasoning capabilities. The paper positions itself at the intersection of three critical tensions: the limitations of supervised learning, the perceived instability of reinforcement learning for small models, and the accumulating complexity of modern training pipelines.

### The Distillation Ceiling
Currently, the dominant strategy for improving Small Language Models (SLMs), typically defined here as models around 1.5B parameters, is **distillation**. In this process, a smaller "student" model is supervised fine-tuned on the outputs of a larger, more capable "teacher" model. As noted in Section 1, this approach is efficient and stable, evidenced by strategies like Qwen3's strong-to-weak distillation.

However, distillation introduces a hard theoretical bound: **the student cannot exceed the teacher's capabilities.**
*   **The Problem:** Once the teacher model's performance plateaus or updates infrequently, the student model hits a ceiling. Even with increased data or extended training, gains diminish because the supervision signal itself contains no new information beyond what the teacher knows.
*   **The Necessity of RL:** To break this ceiling, researchers must turn to **Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR)**. Unlike distillation, RL allows the model to explore solutions beyond the teacher's distribution, rewarded only by whether the final answer is correct (verifiable) rather than how closely it matches the teacher's tokens.

### The Instability Narrative
While RL is standard for large models (e.g., OpenAI's o1, DeepSeek-R1), the community has developed a strong consensus that RL is inherently unstable for smaller models. Section 1 describes a reputation for "reward collapse, entropy drift, and length explosion."
*   **Entropy Drift:** The model's policy becomes too deterministic (collapse) or too random (drift), halting learning.
*   **Length Explosion:** The model learns to generate excessively long responses to game the reward signal, wasting compute and often degrading accuracy.
*   **Reward Collapse:** The model finds loopholes in the verifier to get high rewards without actually solving the problem.

Because of these feared failure modes, the field has moved away from simple RL setups. Instead, researchers have engineered complex safeguards to keep the training process stable. The motivation for JustRL stems from a critical skepticism of this narrative: **Are these failure modes fundamental to RL on small models, or are they symptoms of insufficient scale and overly complex baselines?**

### The Complexity Arms Race
To address the perceived instabilities, recent literature has evolved into a "complexity arms race." Table 1 provides a taxonomy of this trend, tracking techniques used in recent 1.5B reasoning models from January 2025 to November 2025. The table catalogs techniques such as:
*   `EC` (Entropy Control): Explicitly regularizing the model's randomness.
*   `THP` (Tune Hyperparameters): Changing learning rates or batch sizes dynamically.
*   `LC` (Length Control): Applying penalties to response length.
*   `ST` (Split Training Stages): Dividing training into distinct phases with different goals.

Early works like STILL [Min et al., 2024] began with hyperparameter tuning. Subsequent approaches layered on increasing sophistication:
*   **Multi-Stage Pipelines:** DeepScaleR [Luo et al., 2025] divided training into three stages with progressive context lengthening ($8K \rightarrow 16K \rightarrow 24K$). FastCuRL [Song et al., 2025] expanded this to five stages, alternating between Chain-of-Thought (CoT) compression and extension.
*   **Dynamic Interventions:** ProRL-V2 [Hu et al., 2025a] introduced scheduled cosine length penalties. BroRL [Hu et al., 2025b] increased rollouts per example to hundreds to exhaustively explore the solution space after hitting plateaus.
*   **Curriculum Learning:** QuestA [Li et al., 2025] augmented questions with partial CoT solutions as hints, requiring additional data engineering to stage difficulty progression.

The cumulative effect, as highlighted in Section 2, is that nearly every work employs multiple techniques from this growing toolkit. While these methods achieve strong results, the accumulated complexity creates a confounding variable: **it becomes impossible to isolate which elements truly drive performance.** When a new technique is added to an already complex baseline, improvements might simply be compensating for issues introduced by prior complexity rather than solving fundamental challenges.

### Positioning JustRL
This paper positions itself not as a new algorithmic breakthrough, but as a **control experiment** for the field. The authors ask a foundational question found in Section 1: *"Is this complexity necessary?"*

JustRL operates on the hypothesis that many "standard tricks" are solving problems that disappear with a stable, scaled-up baseline. By stripping away multi-stage pipelines, dynamic schedules, and explicit penalties, the authors aim to:
1.  **Establish Sufficiency:** Determine what is fundamentally sufficient for RL training on SLMs before layering on additional complexity.
2.  **Validate Stability:** Test whether training instabilities (like entropy collapse) are inherent to small models or artifacts of specific design choices (e.g., explicit length penalties that conflict with learning objectives).
3.  **Improve Efficiency:** Demonstrate that competitive performance can be achieved with significantly less compute (reported as $2\times$ less in Section 4) by avoiding the overhead of complex pipelines.

The work does not argue against all techniques dogmatically. Instead, it advocates for a methodological shift: establish simple baselines first, and only add complexity when a simple, robust baseline demonstrably fails. This positioning challenges the assumption that sophistication equals performance, suggesting instead that **simplicity at scale** may be the more reliable path forward for reasoning models.

## 3. Technical Approach

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)
JustRL is a streamlined reinforcement learning training pipeline designed to optimize small language models for mathematical reasoning without the complex stabilization techniques common in recent literature. It solves the problem of training instability and computational inefficiency by replacing dynamic schedules and multi-stage curricula with a fixed, single-stage configuration that relies on scale and fundamental algorithmic stability rather than engineered interventions.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)
The system operates as a closed-loop optimization process consisting of four primary components that interact sequentially during each training step.
*   **Policy Model:** The 1.5B parameter language model (either DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen or OpenMath-Nemotron) that generates reasoning trajectories; it starts with weights initialized from distillation and is updated throughout training.
*   **Rollout Generator:** A sampling mechanism that produces $N=8$ distinct responses for each input question using a fixed temperature of $1.0$, creating a "group" of solutions for comparison.
*   **Verifier:** A lightweight, rule-based scoring system (derived from DAPO) that evaluates each generated response solely on whether the final answer is correct, outputting a binary reward signal without analyzing the reasoning steps.
*   **GRPO Optimizer:** The update engine (implemented in `veRL`) that calculates advantage estimates based on the relative performance of the $N$ responses within the group and adjusts the Policy Model's weights using a fixed learning rate.

Information flows unidirectionally during a step: the Policy Model generates responses, the Verifier scores them, the GRPO Optimizer computes updates based on the score differences, and the Policy Model weights are adjusted before the next batch of data is processed.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive
*   **Core Algorithm Configuration:** We begin with the GRPO implementation details because this defines how the model learns from rewards.
*   **Reward Signal Design:** We examine the verifier next, as the quality and type of reward signal fundamentally constrain what the model can learn.
*   **Hyperparameter Stability:** We analyze the fixed hyperparameter set (Table 2) to understand how the authors removed dynamic scheduling.
*   **Data and Prompting Strategy:** We review the training data and input format, which establish the baseline knowledge and output structure.
*   **Infrastructure and Scale:** We conclude with the computational setup, which contextualizes the "scale" part of the "simplicity at scale" claim.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown
This paper presents a methodological intervention rather than a new algorithmic invention, positing that the core idea is to remove complexity from the training pipeline while maintaining adequate computational scale. The following breakdown details exactly how the system is constructed, grounding every design choice in the provided text.

#### Core Algorithm and Optimization Logic
The foundation of the approach is the **Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)** algorithm, utilized via the default implementation in the `veRL` framework [Sheng et al., 2025].
*   Unlike standard Policy Optimization methods that might compare actions against a learned critic model, GRPO estimates advantages by comparing the rewards of multiple sampled responses ($N=8$) to the same question.
*   The system uses **binary outcome rewards**, meaning the verifier assigns a score of 1 for a correct final answer and 0 for an incorrect one, without providing dense rewards for intermediate reasoning steps.
*   A critical design choice documented in Table 2 is the explicit exclusion of **KL Loss** (`Use KL Loss: No`). In many RL setups, a Kullback-Leibler (KL) penalty constrains the model from drifting too far from its initial distribution, but JustRL removes this to allow unrestricted policy updates.
*   Similarly, Table 2 specifies `Use Entropy Regularization: No`. While entropy regularization is often used to prevent the model from becoming too deterministic too quickly, JustRL relies on the natural dynamics of the algorithm rather than an explicit penalty term.
*   The only stabilization technique retained is **"clip higher"**, which the authors view as part of the baseline rather than an added trick. This technique limits the magnitude of policy updates to prevent instability during long-horizon training, ensuring that the model does not collapse when training for thousands of steps.
*   The clip ratio range is fixed at `$[0.8, 1.28]$` throughout the entire training process, preventing the need for dynamic adjustment of how much the policy is allowed to change per step.

#### Reward Signal and Verifier Design
The learning signal is generated by a **lightweight rule-based verifier** sourced from DAPO [Yu et al., 2025], chosen specifically to minimize computational overhead.
*   The verifier operates by checking the final answer format and correctness, explicitly avoiding heavy symbolic math libraries like SymPy that could slow down the training loop.
*   This design choice prioritizes throughput over nuance; the model receives a clear, unambiguous signal (correct/incorrect) rather than a noisy score from a complex evaluation script.
*   Although the paper later explores adding a "robust verifier" (CompassVerifier-3B) in ablation studies (Section 4.4), the core JustRL approach relies on the simpler rule-based system to maintain training speed and stability.
*   The reliance on binary rewards means the model must discover valid reasoning paths through exploration, as it receives no partial credit for correct intermediate steps that lead to a wrong final answer.

#### Fixed Hyperparameter Configuration
The most distinct feature of JustRL is the complete absence of dynamic hyperparameter scheduling, with all values fixed prior to training as shown in Table 2.
*   **Learning Rate:** The learning rate is set to `$1\times10^{-6}$` and remains constant (`constant`). Most complex pipelines employ decay schedules or warm-ups, but JustRL demonstrates that a fixed rate is sufficient for stable convergence over 4,000+ steps.
*   **Batch Sizes:** The training batch size is fixed at `256`, with a PPO mini batch size of `64` and a micro batch size per GPU of `1`. These values remain unchanged regardless of the training stage.
*   **Rollout Count:** For every training example, the model generates `$N=8$` responses. This group size is fixed, whereas other methods like BroRL increase rollouts to hundreds in later stages to force exploration.
*   **Context Length:** The maximum context length is set to `16K` tokens (with `1k` for prompts and `15k` for responses). Unlike multi-stage approaches that progressively increase context (e.g., `$8K \rightarrow 16K \rightarrow 24K$`), JustRL starts and ends at 16K.
*   **Temperature:** The sampling temperature is fixed at `$1.0$`. Adaptive temperature controls, which adjust randomness based on training progress, are deliberately omitted.
*   **Transferability:** These exact hyperparameters are used for both the DeepSeek and Nemotron backbones without any per-model tuning, demonstrating that the configuration is robust across different model initializations.

#### Data and Prompting Strategy
The training data and input formatting are kept minimal to avoid introducing hidden complexity through data curation.
*   **Dataset:** The model trains on **DAPO-Math-17k** [Yu et al., 2025], a standard mathematical reasoning dataset. The authors explicitly avoid offline difficulty filtering or online dynamic sampling strategies that would curate data based on model performance.
*   **Prompting:** The input prompt uses a simple suffix without tuning: `"Please reason step by step, and put your final answer within \boxed{}."` This standardizes the output format required by the rule-based verifier.
*   **Length Control:** Instead of using explicit length penalty terms (which add a hyperparameter to tune), the system simply enforces a hard maximum context length of `16K` tokens. The paper notes that the model naturally compresses its response length from an initial `$\sim8,000$` tokens to a stable `$4,000\text{--}5,000$` tokens without explicit pressure to do so.
*   **Single-Stage Training:** The training process is continuous from start to finish. There are no stage transitions, curriculum switches, or checkpoint resets that would require manual intervention or pipeline orchestration.

#### Infrastructure and Computational Scale
The feasibility of this simple approach relies on running it at a sufficient computational scale to allow the stable dynamics to emerge.
*   **Hardware:** Training is conducted on `32` NVIDIA A800-80GB GPUs. This substantial parallelism allows for the large batch sizes and rollout counts required for stable GRPO updates.
*   **Duration:** Each model is trained for approximately `15` days. This duration supports `4,380` steps for the DeepSeek backbone and `3,440` steps for the Nemotron backbone.
*   **Token Budget:** The total token budget for JustRL-DeepSeek is approximately `$1.4\times10^8$` tokens. This is roughly half the budget of ProRL-V2 (`$2.8\times10^8$`) and significantly less than BroRL (`$6.8\times10^8$`), as detailed in Table 4.
*   **Efficiency Mechanism:** The efficiency gain comes not from faster individual steps, but from avoiding the overhead of multi-stage pipelines, dynamic sampling filters (estimated at 50% filter ratio in competitors), and excessive rollouts. By training continuously without discarding data or restarting stages, the system utilizes every computed token for policy improvement.

#### Design Rationale and Ablation Logic
The technical choices are validated by the authors' observation that adding complexity often degrades performance, a finding detailed in the ablation studies (Section 4.4) which inform the design philosophy.
*   **Against Length Penalties:** The decision to omit explicit length penalties is supported by evidence that adding an "Overlong Penalty" causes performance to plateau at 50% on AIME 2024 (vs 55% baseline) and collapses entropy to `$0.5\text{--}0.6$`. This suggests that explicit penalties create adversarial pressure that conflicts with the learning objective.
*   **Against Robust Verifiers:** The choice to stick with the lightweight verifier is supported by findings that a more sophisticated verifier reduced final performance to 45% on AIME 2024. The authors hypothesize that the stricter rule-based verifier forces the model to develop more robust internal computations, whereas a forgiving verifier might allow sloppy reasoning to pass.
*   **Against Dynamic Sampling:** The absence of dynamic sampling (filtering easy/hard examples) ensures the model sees a consistent data distribution. Competitors using dynamic sampling often filter out 50% of examples, effectively halving their data utilization efficiency compared to JustRL's full usage of the 17k dataset.
*   **Stability as a Feature:** The technical approach treats stability not as something to be engineered via tricks, but as an emergent property of fixed hyperparameters at scale. The "clip higher" technique is the sole safeguard, implying that the primary cause of instability in other works may be the dynamic interventions themselves rather than the model size.

By adhering strictly to this configuration, JustRL establishes a control baseline where every component is static. This allows the authors to attribute performance gains directly to the scaling of the RL process itself, rather than to the tuning of complex pipelines. The approach demonstrates that when the learning rate, batch size, and context window are fixed at adequate magnitudes, the model naturally finds a stable optimization trajectory without the need for the "complexity arms race" described in Section 2.

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

The primary contribution of JustRL is not a new algorithm, but a **methodological correction** for the field. By stripping away the accumulated "best practices" of RL for small models, the authors reveal that many standard techniques may be solving problems created by insufficient scale or unstable baselines rather than fundamental limitations of RL. The following insights distinguish between incremental performance gains and fundamental shifts in how we approach model training.

### 4.1 Methodological Shift: Simplicity as a Control Baseline
Prior work has treated complexity as a prerequisite for stability. Table 1 illustrates this trend, showing a progression from January 2025 to November 2025 where models increasingly adopt techniques like `ST` (Split Training Stages), `LC` (Length Control), and `THP` (Tune Hyperparameters). JustRL inverts this logic by treating simplicity as the **null hypothesis**.

*   **The Innovation:** Instead of asking "What technique fixes this instability?", the paper asks "Does this instability exist if we remove prior complexity?"
*   **Significance:** This reframes the "complexity arms race" described in Section 2. If a simple baseline works, then every added technique must justify its existence by beating that baseline, rather than by solving a problem that might not exist in a simpler setup.
*   **Evidence:** The results in Table 3 and Table 5 show that this minimal approach matches or exceeds complex pipelines (e.g., ProRL-V2, QuestA) while using approximately `$2\times$` less compute (Table 4). This suggests that much of the computational overhead in prior work was spent managing instability rather than learning reasoning tasks.

### 4.2 Emergent Stability: Organic Length Convergence
A prevailing belief in RL for language models is that **length explosion**—where models generate excessively long responses to game the reward signal—is inevitable without explicit intervention. JustRL demonstrates that this is not a fundamental law but an emergent property of specific design choices.

*   **The Mechanism:** As shown in **Figure 2(c)**, the model starts verbose (averaging `$\sim8,000$` tokens) but naturally compresses to a stable `$4,000\text{--}5,000$` token range by step 1,000. This happens without explicit length penalties (`LC` in Table 1), relying only on a hard context limit of `16K` tokens.
*   **Why It Matters:** Explicit length penalties create **adversarial pressure**. The model learns to minimize length to avoid punishment, potentially truncating valid reasoning steps. Organic convergence suggests the model learns that concise reasoning is sufficient for correctness, leading to more robust behavior.
*   **Contrast:** This contradicts methods like ProRL-V2, which introduce "scheduled cosine length penalties" to combat drift. JustRL shows that when the reward signal (binary correctness) is clear and the optimization is stable, the model self-regulates its efficiency.

### 4.3 The "Complexity Trap": Negative Ablation Evidence
Perhaps the most critical insight comes from what *fails* when added to the simple baseline. The ablation studies in **Section 4.4** provide empirical evidence that "standard tricks" can actively degrade performance, validating the hypothesis that complexity can be harmful.

*   **Entropy Collapse:** **Figure 3(b)** reveals the mechanism behind performance degradation. When an explicit "Overlong Penalty" is added, policy entropy collapses from a healthy oscillation of `$1.2\text{--}1.4$` down to `$0.5\text{--}0.6$`.
    > "The explicit penalty appears to create pressure that conflicts with the learning objective, forcing premature convergence to shorter responses before the model has explored what actually works." (Section 4.4)
*   **Verifier Sensitivity:** Adding a "Robust Verifier" (CompassVerifier-3B) to reduce false negatives also reduced performance, plateauing at 45% on AIME 2024 compared to the baseline's 55% (**Figure 3(a)**). The authors hypothesize that the stricter rule-based verifier forces the model to develop more precise internal computations, whereas a forgiving verifier allows sloppy reasoning to pass.
*   **Fundamental Insight:** This distinguishes JustRL from incremental improvements. It is not just that simplicity *works*; it is that adding complexity *breaks* the delicate balance of the baseline. This suggests that techniques like length penalties are not universal improvements but context-dependent fixes that may interfere with exploration.

### 4.4 Hyperparameter Agnosticism and Transferability
In complex pipelines, hyperparameters are often tightly coupled to specific model architectures or training stages. JustRL demonstrates **hyperparameter agnosticism**, where the same configuration works across different model backbones without tuning.

*   **The Evidence:** The exact hyperparameters in **Table 2** (e.g., learning rate `$1\times10^{-6}$`, rollout `$N=8$`) were applied to both `DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B` and `OpenMath-Nemotron-1.5B`.
*   **Performance Consistency:**
    *   **DeepSeek Backbone:** Achieved 54.87% average accuracy (**Table 3**).
    *   **Nemotron Backbone:** Achieved 64.32% average accuracy (**Table 5**).
*   **Significance:** This robustness implies that the training dynamics are driven by the fundamental scaling of RL rather than fine-tuned interactions between hyperparameters and model weights. In contrast, methods like QuestA require specific curriculum designs tailored to the data distribution. JustRL's transferability suggests that a stable baseline reduces the need for per-model engineering, lowering the barrier for reproducibility and future research.

While JustRL does not claim that complexity is never useful, it establishes that many current practices may be compensating for baselines that are not sufficiently scaled or stable. By proving that a minimal recipe can achieve state-of-the-art results, the paper invites researchers to validate every added technique against a simple, robust default.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section examines how the authors validate their claims through systematic experimentation. The experimental design serves two purposes: first, to demonstrate that simple RL training achieves competitive performance; second, to test whether added complexity actually helps or harms the baseline. We walk through the evaluation methodology, quantitative results, and ablation studies to assess the strength of the evidence.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

The authors employ a rigorous evaluation protocol designed to ensure fair comparison with prior work while maintaining reproducibility.

**Benchmark Suite.** The evaluation spans nine challenging mathematical reasoning tasks, selected from reproducible scripts provided by POLARIS [An et al., 2025]:

*   **Competition Mathematics:** AIME 2024, AIME 2025, AMC 2023, HMMT Feb 2025, CMIMC 2025, BRUMO 2025
*   **Standardized Benchmarks:** MATH-500, Minerva Math, OlympiadBench

This diversity matters because it tests generalization across different problem types and difficulty levels. AIME and HMMT represent high-difficulty competition problems, while MATH-500 provides a standardized baseline for comparison with prior work.

**Sampling Protocol.** The evaluation uses Pass@1 accuracy with multiple sampled responses per problem:

*   `$N=32$` samples for competition benchmarks (AIME, AMC, HMMT, CMIMC, BRUMO)
*   `$N=4$` samples for standardized benchmarks (MATH-500, Minerva, OlympiadBench)

The higher sample count for competition problems reflects their greater difficulty and variance—more samples provide a more stable estimate of model capability. Generation uses temperature `$0.7$`, top-p `$0.9$`, and allows up to `$32K$` tokens per response.

**Verifier Augmentation.** To address false negatives from rule-based verifiers (correct solutions marked wrong due to formatting issues), the authors augment evaluation with **CompassVerifier-3B** [Liu et al., 2025c], a lightweight model-based verifier. This ensures that performance differences reflect actual reasoning capability rather than verifier artifacts.

**Baseline Comparisons.** The paper compares against two categories of baselines:

*   **Complex RL Methods:** ProRL-V2, BroRL, DeepScaleR, QuestA—all employing multi-stage training, dynamic hyperparameters, or curriculum learning
*   **Distilled Backbones:** The original DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B and OpenMath-Nemotron-1.5B before RL training

This dual comparison isolates the contribution of RL training itself from the contribution of training methodology.

#### Training Dynamics Evidence

The performance numbers alone do not tell the full story. **Figure 2** provides critical evidence about *how* the models learn, which bears directly on the paper's claim that complexity is unnecessary.

*   **Policy Entropy (Figure 2a):** Entropy oscillates between `$1.0$` and `$1.6$` throughout training, with no systematic drift. This contradicts the "entropy collapse" narrative that motivates explicit entropy regularization in prior work.
*   **Mean Reward (Figure 2b):** Reward improves monotonically from `$-0.6$` to `$+0.4$` over 4,000+ steps. There are no extended plateaus or sudden drops that would typically trigger intervention in multi-stage approaches.
*   **Response Length (Figure 2c):** The model starts verbose (`$\sim8,000$` tokens) but naturally compresses to `$4,000\text{--}5,000$` tokens by step 1,000. This happens **without explicit length penalties**, contradicting the assumption that length explosion is inevitable.

These dynamics matter because they suggest the instabilities cited as motivation for complex techniques (Section 2) may be artifacts of specific design choices rather than fundamental RL challenges.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies: Testing the Complexity Hypothesis

The most critical evidence comes from **Section 4.4**, where the authors test whether adding "standard tricks" improves or degrades performance. This directly addresses the paper's central question: *Is complexity necessary?*

**Experimental Setup.** Two modifications are tested on the JustRL-DeepSeek-1.5B baseline, both trained for 3,000+ steps:

1.  **w/ Overlong Penalty:** Adds an explicit length penalty term for the last `$4K$` tokens (as used in DAPO [Yu et al., 2025])
2.  **w/ Overlong Penalty + Robust Verifier:** Further adds CompassVerifier-3B from DeepScaleR [Luo et al., 2025] to reduce false negatives

**Results (Figure 3).** Both modifications **degrade performance**:

| Configuration | AIME 2024 Accuracy | Policy Entropy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Base Recipe | 55% | `$1.2\text{--}1.4$` |
| + Overlong Penalty | 50% | `$0.5\text{--}0.6$` |
| + Overlong Penalty + Robust Verifier | 45% | `$0.5\text{--}0.6$` |

**Why This Matters.** The entropy collapse in Figure 3(b) reveals the mechanism: explicit penalties create adversarial pressure that conflicts with the learning objective. The model converges prematurely to shorter responses before exploring what actually works. This is not a minor degradation—performance drops by `$10$` percentage points with both modifications.

**Verifier Sensitivity.** The robust verifier result is particularly surprising. Intuitively, reducing false negatives should provide a cleaner learning signal. However, the authors hypothesize two explanations:

*   The stricter rule-based verifier creates a richer spectrum of learning signals by reducing "perfect" scores
*   The stricter verifier's reliance on precise formatting pressures the model to develop more robust internal computations

This suggests that **verifier design interacts with learning dynamics in non-obvious ways**. A forgiving verifier might fail to encourage the precision required for optimal generalization.

**Limitations of Ablations.** The authors are transparent about what these ablations do not prove:

> "We tested two specific modifications, but many other techniques remain unexplored: curriculum learning, adaptive temperature scheduling, reference model resets, different verifier designs, and various forms of data augmentation." (Section 4.4)

The point is not that additional techniques *never* help, but that they should be **validated empirically** rather than assumed beneficial. This is a methodological contribution: establish what works simply before layering complexity.

### 5.4 Critical Assessment of Experimental Evidence

Do the experiments convincingly support the paper's claims? We assess strength and limitations.

#### Strengths

*   **Fair Comparisons:** The paper compares against strong baselines (ProRL-V2, QuestA) using the same evaluation protocol. Compute budgets are explicitly reported in **Table 4** and **Table 6**, allowing direct efficiency comparisons.
*   **Transferability Test:** Using the same hyperparameters across two different backbones (DeepSeek and Nemotron) demonstrates robustness. If the recipe only worked for one architecture, the claim would be weaker.
*   **Training Dynamics:** Figure 2 provides mechanistic evidence, not just final numbers. Showing *how* the model learns (stable entropy, monotonic reward, organic length compression) strengthens the argument that simplicity enables stability.
*   **Negative Ablations:** Testing whether added complexity *harms* performance is stronger evidence than simply showing the simple approach works. It directly challenges the assumption that standard tricks are universally beneficial.

#### Limitations

*   **Domain Restriction:** All results are for **mathematical reasoning** at the **1.5B parameter scale**. The paper explicitly acknowledges this in the Limitations section: generalization to coding, general QA, or larger models remains unexplored.
*   **Compute Still Substantial:** While JustRL uses 2× less compute than competitors, `$1.4\times10^8$` tokens still requires 32 A800 GPUs for ~15 days. This may be prohibitive for resource-constrained researchers, limiting reproducibility.
*   **Unexplored Techniques:** The ablations test only two modifications. Other techniques (curriculum learning, adaptive temperature, reference model resets) might improve upon the baseline. The paper does not claim simplicity is optimal, only that it is *sufficient*.
*   **Training Horizon:** The paper does not explore whether the approach maintains advantages at even longer training horizons. Additional techniques might become necessary beyond 4,000+ steps.

#### Trade-offs and Conditions

The results are not universally applicable. The authors note in Section 5 when complexity might help:

*   **Extreme Compute Constraints:** When token budgets are severely limited, techniques like dynamic sampling might improve sample efficiency
*   **Specific Failure Modes:** If a particular training setup exhibits entropy collapse or length explosion, targeted interventions may be necessary
*   **Noisier Reward Signals:** Domains without verifiable rewards (e.g., creative writing, open-ended QA) may require different stabilization techniques
*   **Pushing Performance Ceilings:** When marginal gains matter (e.g., competition settings), additional techniques might provide incremental improvements

The paper's claim is methodological, not absolute: **establish simple baselines first, then add complexity only when the baseline demonstrably fails.**

### 5.5 Summary of Experimental Evidence

| Claim | Supporting Evidence | Strength |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Simple RL achieves SOTA performance | Table 3 (54.87% vs. 53.08% ProRL-V2), Table 5 (64.32% vs. 63.81% QuestA) | Strong |
| 2× less compute than complex methods | Table 4 (`$1.4\times10^8$` vs. `$2.8\times10^8$`), Table 6 (`$1.1\times10^8$` vs. `$2.6\times10^8$`) | Strong |
| Training is stable without intervention | Figure 2 (monotonic reward, stable entropy, organic length compression) | Strong |

| Results generalize to other domains | Not tested (math only, 1.5B scale) | Weak |

The experimental evidence is convincing within its stated scope. The paper does not overclaim—it acknowledges limitations in domain coverage and model scale. Within mathematical reasoning at 1.5B parameters, the evidence strongly supports the central thesis: **simplicity at scale can match or exceed complex pipelines while using less compute.**

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While JustRL demonstrates that simple RL training can achieve state-of-the-art performance at 1.5B scale, the approach rests on specific assumptions and leaves important questions unresolved. A critical examination reveals both practical constraints and open research directions that temper the paper's conclusions.

### 6.1 Domain and Scale Restrictions

The most significant limitation is the narrow scope of validation. All experimental results are confined to **mathematical reasoning tasks** at the **1.5B parameter scale**, as explicitly acknowledged in the Limitations section.

**Domain Specificity.** The nine benchmarks used for evaluation (AIME, AMC, MATH-500, etc.) all share a critical property: they have **verifiable, binary outcomes**. A solution is either correct or incorrect, enabling the rule-based verifier to provide clean reward signals. This matters because:

*   **Non-Verifiable Domains:** Tasks like creative writing, open-ended question answering, or code generation without test cases lack clear binary rewards. In these domains, the simple binary reward structure may be insufficient, potentially requiring the complex stabilization techniques that JustRL avoids.
*   **Reward Signal Quality:** The paper notes that the rule-based verifier from DAPO [Yu et al., 2025] works well for math, but the ablation study in Section 4.4 shows that switching to a more sophisticated verifier (CompassVerifier-3B) actually **degraded performance** from 55% to 45% on AIME 2024. This suggests the approach may be brittle to verifier design changes.

**Model Scale.** The 1.5B parameter range represents a specific regime where distillation ceilings become apparent but RL training remains computationally feasible. The paper does not address:

*   **Smaller Models:** Below 1B parameters, models may lack the capacity to benefit from RL training regardless of pipeline simplicity.
*   **Larger Models:** Above 7B parameters, training dynamics may change fundamentally. The "clip higher" technique that stabilizes 1.5B models may be insufficient at larger scales where instability manifests differently.

> "Generalization to other domains (e.g., coding, general question answering) and model sizes remains unexplored." (Limitations section)

This is not a minor caveat—it defines the boundary of where JustRL's conclusions apply.

### 6.2 Computational Requirements

Despite claiming **2× less compute** than sophisticated approaches, JustRL still demands substantial resources that limit accessibility and reproducibility.

**Absolute Compute Budget.** Table 4 and Table 6 report the token budgets:

| Model | Token Budget | Hardware | Duration |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| JustRL-DeepSeek | `$1.4\times10^8$` tokens | 32 A800-80GB GPUs | ~15 days |
| JustRL-Nemotron | `$1.1\times10^8$` tokens | 32 A800-80GB GPUs | ~15 days |

While this is half of ProRL-V2's `$2.8\times10^8$` tokens, the absolute cost remains prohibitive for most researchers:

*   **Hardware Barrier:** Access to 32 A800 GPUs is limited to well-funded institutions. The paper's code and models are released, but reproducing the training runs is not feasible for resource-constrained researchers.
*   **Time Cost:** Fifteen days of continuous training per model limits iteration speed. Researchers cannot quickly test modifications or explore hyperparameter variations.

**Efficiency Trade-off.** The efficiency gain comes from avoiding multi-stage pipelines and dynamic sampling, not from algorithmic improvements. This creates a trade-off:

*   **Pro:** Simpler orchestration, no need to manage stage transitions or sampling filters.
*   **Con:** Cannot dynamically allocate compute to hard examples. Methods like QuestA's curriculum learning may achieve better **sample efficiency** (performance per token seen) even if total compute is higher.

The paper acknowledges this tension: *"Our compute budget, while lower than some complex methods, may still be prohibitive for resource-constrained researchers."* (Limitations section)

### 6.3 Unidentified Critical Components

A methodological limitation is the inability to isolate which specific design choices drive success. The paper presents a **complete recipe** but cannot decompose its effectiveness.

**Confounded Variables.** The JustRL configuration includes multiple fixed choices simultaneously:

*   Learning rate: `$1\times10^{-6}$` (constant)
*   Rollout count: `$N=8$`
*   Context length: `16K` tokens
*   Verifier: DAPO rule-based
*   No KL loss, no entropy regularization

The ablation studies in Section 4.4 test only two modifications (overlong penalty and robust verifier). Critical questions remain unanswered:

*   Would a different learning rate (e.g., `$5\times10^{-7}$` or `$2\times10^{-6}$`) perform better?
*   Is `$N=8$` rollouts optimal, or would `$N=16$` improve stability?
*   Does the `16K` context length matter, or would `8K` suffice?

> "While we demonstrate that simplicity works, we cannot definitively isolate which specific components (hyperparameters, verifier design, training data) are most critical to our success." (Limitations section)

This limits the **transferability** of insights. Researchers adopting JustRL must use the exact configuration without understanding which elements are essential versus incidental.

### 6.4 Training Horizon Uncertainty

The paper validates stability over **4,000+ steps** (JustRL-DeepSeek) and **3,440 steps** (JustRL-Nemotron), but does not address what happens beyond this horizon.

**Potential Long-Horizon Issues.** Figure 2 shows smooth training curves, but the paper explicitly notes:

> "We have not explored whether our approach maintains advantages when pushed to even longer training horizons or whether additional techniques might become necessary at scale." (Limitations section)

Several failure modes could emerge with extended training:

*   **Reward Saturation:** As the model approaches peak performance on the training distribution, reward signals may become too sparse to drive further improvement.
*   **Overfitting:** Without dynamic data sampling or curriculum adjustments, the model may overfit to the 17k training examples.
*   **Entropy Collapse:** While entropy remains stable at `$1.2\text{--}1.4$` through 4,000 steps (Figure 2a), longer training could still lead to premature convergence.

**Comparison to Multi-Stage Methods.** Methods like ProRL-V2 use nine training stages partly to manage long-horizon training. Each stage can reset optimization dynamics, potentially enabling continued improvement where single-stage training plateaus. JustRL does not test whether this is necessary.

### 6.5 Hyperparameter Transferability Boundaries

The paper demonstrates that the **same hyperparameters** work for two different backbones (DeepSeek and Nemotron), but this transferability has untested boundaries.

**Architecture Sensitivity.** Both backbones are distilled reasoning models in the 1.5B range with similar architectures (transformer-based, similar vocabulary). The hyperparameters may not transfer to:

*   **Different Architectures:** Models with mixture-of-experts (MoE) layers, different attention mechanisms, or alternative tokenization may require adjusted learning rates or batch sizes.
*   **Different Initialization:** Models initialized from scratch (not distilled) may have different optimization landscapes requiring different RL configurations.
*   **Different Languages:** The benchmarks are primarily English mathematics. Performance on non-English reasoning tasks is untested.

**Table 2** shows fixed hyperparameters, but the paper does not explore sensitivity analysis. A learning rate of `$1\times10^{-6}$` may be optimal for these two models but suboptimal for others. Without ablation on hyperparameters, the claimed "robustness" is based on only two data points.

### 6.6 Trade-offs Between Simplicity and Peak Performance

The paper positions simplicity as sufficient, but does not claim it is **optimal**. This creates an important trade-off for practitioners.

**Performance Ceiling.** JustRL achieves 54.87% (DeepSeek) and 64.32% (Nemotron) average accuracy. These are competitive but not dramatically superior to complex methods:

*   vs. ProRL-V2: +1.79 percentage points (DeepSeek backbone)
*   vs. QuestA: +0.51 percentage points (Nemotron backbone)

The marginal gains suggest that **additional techniques could potentially push performance higher** at the cost of complexity. For applications where every percentage point matters (e.g., competition settings), the complex pipelines may still be worthwhile.

**Engineering Overhead vs. Performance.** The trade-off can be framed as:

| Approach | Engineering Complexity | Performance | Compute Efficiency |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| JustRL | Low (single-stage, fixed params) | SOTA-competitive | High (2× less than competitors) |
| Complex Pipelines | High (multi-stage, dynamic scheduling) | SOTA-competitive | Lower (more compute for similar results) |

For research settings prioritizing reproducibility and iteration speed, JustRL is preferable. For production settings where peak performance is critical regardless of cost, complex methods may still be justified.

### 6.7 Unexplored Technique Interactions

The ablation studies test only two modifications, leaving many "standard tricks" unvalidated against the JustRL baseline.

**Untested Techniques.** Table 1 catalogs numerous techniques that JustRL explicitly avoids:

*   `THP` (Tune Hyperparameters): Dynamic learning rate schedules
*   `RKL` (Reset KL Reference): Mid-training reference model resets
*   `DS` (Dynamic Sampling): Filtering examples based on difficulty
*   `ST` (Split Training Stages): Multi-stage curriculum progression

The paper's claim is that these should be **validated empirically** rather than assumed beneficial. However, without testing them against the JustRL baseline, we cannot know:

*   Which techniques are genuinely harmful (like the overlong penalty in Figure 3)
*   Which techniques might provide incremental gains without breaking stability
*   Whether combinations of techniques interact differently than individual modifications

**Methodological Gap.** This creates a research agenda: systematically test each technique from Table 1 against the JustRL baseline to build a refined understanding of what adds value versus what adds complexity without benefit.

### 6.8 Summary of Limitations and Trade-offs

| Limitation Category | Specific Constraint | Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Domain** | Math reasoning only, verifiable rewards required | Cannot generalize to open-ended tasks |
| **Scale** | 1.5B parameters only | Unknown performance at smaller/larger scales |
| **Compute** | `$1.1\text{--}1.4\times10^8$` tokens, 32 GPUs, 15 days | Prohibitive for resource-constrained researchers |
| **Component Isolation** | Cannot identify which design choices are critical | Limits transferability to new settings |
| **Training Horizon** | Validated only through 4,000+ steps | Long-horizon stability unproven |
| **Hyperparameter Transfer** | Tested on only 2 backbones | Architecture sensitivity unknown |
| **Peak Performance** | Marginal gains over complex methods | May not be optimal for competition settings |
| **Technique Validation** | Only 2 ablations tested | Many "standard tricks" remain unvalidated |

These limitations do not invalidate JustRL's contributions but define the boundaries within which its conclusions hold. The paper's methodological argument—**establish simple baselines before adding complexity**—remains valid regardless of these constraints. However, practitioners should recognize that JustRL is a **strong baseline** rather than a universal solution, and domain-specific requirements may still justify additional complexity when the simple approach demonstrably fails.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

JustRL does more than report a new state-of-the-art model; it proposes a fundamental shift in how the community approaches reinforcement learning for small language models (SLMs). By demonstrating that a minimal recipe achieves competitive performance with greater stability and efficiency, the work challenges the prevailing "complexity arms race" documented in Table 1. This section explores how these findings reshape the research landscape, outlines critical follow-up questions, and provides practical guidance for integrating this approach into future workflows.

### 7.1 Shifting the Research Paradigm
The most immediate implication of JustRL is methodological. For the past year, the dominant narrative has been that RL for SLMs is inherently unstable, requiring engineered safeguards like multi-stage pipelines (`ST`), dynamic hyperparameter tuning (`THP`), and explicit length control (`LC`). JustRL reframes these instabilities not as fundamental laws, but as potential artifacts of overly complex baselines.

*   **From Symptom Treatment to Root Cause Analysis:** The negative ablation studies in **Figure 3** show that adding "standard tricks" like overlong penalties actively degraded performance by collapsing policy entropy from `$1.2\text{--}1.4$` to `$0.5\text{--}0.6$`. This suggests that prior work may have been treating symptoms (e.g., length explosion) created by other design choices rather than addressing root causes.
*   **The Null Hypothesis of Simplicity:** Future research should treat simplicity as the default null hypothesis. Before introducing a new technique (e.g., curriculum learning), researchers must demonstrate that it outperforms a stable, single-stage baseline like JustRL. As noted in **Section 5**, the burden of proof shifts: complexity must justify its cost rather than simplicity defending its existence.
*   **Resource Reallocation:** The compute efficiency gains are substantial. JustRL-DeepSeek used `$1.4\times10^8$` tokens compared to ProRL-V2's `$2.8\times10^8$` (**Table 4**). This implies that significant computational resources previously spent on managing training instability could be redirected toward scaling data or model size.

### 7.2 Critical Research Avenues
While JustRL establishes a strong baseline, it explicitly leaves several dimensions unexplored. These gaps define the immediate roadmap for follow-up research.

#### Scaling Beyond 1.5B Parameters
The current results are confined to the 1.5B parameter scale (**Limitations Section 6.1**). A critical open question is whether the "simplicity at scale" principle holds for larger models.
*   **Hypothesis:** As model capacity increases, the need for explicit stabilization (like KL penalties) might diminish further, allowing even simpler recipes to succeed.
*   **Counter-Hypothesis:** Larger models might exhibit different failure modes (e.g., mode collapse) that require targeted interventions not visible at 1.5B.
*   **Next Step:** Replicate the JustRL recipe on 7B and 14B parameter backbones using the fixed hyperparameters from **Table 2** (e.g., learning rate `$1\times10^{-6}$`, rollout `$N=8$`). If performance degrades, systematic ablation can identify the threshold where complexity becomes necessary.

#### Domain Generalization
All nine benchmarks used in **Section 5.1** are mathematical reasoning tasks with verifiable binary rewards. The implications for non-verifiable domains remain unknown.
*   **Verifiable vs. Non-Verifiable:** In domains like coding (with unit tests) or science (with factual checks), the binary reward structure may hold. However, in open-ended tasks like creative writing or general dialogue, reward signals are noisy.
*   **Verifier Sensitivity:** The ablation study (**Section 4.4**) showed that switching to a "Robust Verifier" degraded performance. This suggests the simple rule-based verifier from DAPO [Yu et al., 2025] plays a critical role in the learning dynamic. Future work must investigate whether this sensitivity extends to domains where rule-based verification is impossible.
*   **Next Step:** Apply JustRL to code generation tasks (e.g., HumanEval) where pass/fail signals are available, then gradually move to tasks requiring model-based reward models to test stability limits.

#### Long-Horizon Training Dynamics
The paper validates stability over `$4,000+$` steps (**Figure 1**), but does not confirm behavior beyond this horizon.
*   **Saturation Risk:** Without dynamic sampling or curriculum adjustments, the model may eventually overfit to the `$17K$` training examples in DAPO-Math.
*   **Next Step:** Extend training to `$10,000+$` steps to observe if the monotonic reward improvement (**Figure 2b**) plateaus or collapses. This will determine if multi-stage pipelines (like ProRL-V2's 9 stages) are necessary only for extended training horizons.

#### Systematic Technique Validation
Table 1 lists numerous techniques (e.g., `RKL` Reset KL Reference, `DS` Dynamic Sampling) that JustRL omits. The ablation study tested only two modifications.
*   **Research Opportunity:** Conduct a systematic benchmark where each technique from Table 1 is added individually to the JustRL baseline. This would create a "value map" of which techniques provide genuine gains versus which add complexity without benefit.
*   **Interaction Effects:** Investigate whether combinations of techniques (e.g., dynamic sampling + length penalties) interact differently than individual modifications. The negative result in **Figure 3** suggests interactions may be non-linear and harmful.

### 7.3 Practical Integration Guidelines
For practitioners and engineers looking to adopt RL for small models, JustRL provides a clear decision framework. The choice between JustRL and complex pipelines depends on specific constraints and goals.

#### When to Prefer JustRL
*   **Resource Constraints:** If compute budget is limited, JustRL offers a high-efficiency path. It achieves SOTA performance with approximately `$2\times$` less compute than competitors (**Abstract**).
*   **Engineering Simplicity:** If orchestration complexity is a concern, JustRL eliminates the need for multi-stage pipeline management, dynamic sampling filters, or hyperparameter schedulers. The fixed configuration in **Table 2** allows for a "set and forget" training run.
*   **Stability Requirements:** If training stability is critical (e.g., in production environments where collapses are costly), the smooth dynamics in **Figure 2** suggest JustRL is lower risk than methods requiring manual intervention at stage transitions.
*   **Baseline Establishment:** When exploring a new domain or model architecture, start with JustRL to establish a performance floor before investing in complex optimizations.

#### When to Consider Complex Pipelines
*   **Extreme Performance Ceilings:** If marginal gains (e.g., `$0.5\%$` accuracy) are critical (e.g., for competition leaderboards), techniques like QuestA's curriculum learning may still provide incremental benefits despite higher cost.
*   **Non-Verifiable Rewards:** In domains without clear binary rewards, the simple verifier setup may be insufficient. Methods with adaptive temperature (`AT`) or robust verifiers might be necessary to handle noisy signals.
*   **Severe Data Scarcity:** If training data is extremely limited, dynamic sampling (`DS`) might improve sample efficiency by focusing on high-value examples, whereas JustRL uses the full dataset uniformly.

#### Integration Checklist
To implement JustRL effectively, practitioners should ensure the following conditions match the paper's setup:
1.  **Verifier Availability:** Ensure a rule-based verifier exists for the task (e.g., checking `\boxed{}` answers).
2.  **Compute Scale:** Allocate sufficient resources (e.g., `$32$` A800 GPUs for `$\sim15$` days) to allow the stable dynamics to emerge. Under-scaling compute may reintroduce instability.
3.  **Hyperparameter Adherence:** Use the fixed configuration in **Table 2** exactly (e.g., learning rate `$1\times10^{-6}$`, clip ratio `$[0.8, 1.28]$`). Deviating without validation risks losing the stability benefits.
4.  **Monitoring:** Track policy entropy and response length during training. If entropy collapses (drops below `$1.0$`) or length explodes (exceeds `$10K$` tokens), the setup may differ fundamentally from the paper's conditions.

### 7.4 Community Standards and Reproducibility
The authors release both models and code (**Abstract**), aiming to establish a "simple, validated baseline for the community." This open science approach has broader implications for research standards.

*   **Benchmarking Consistency:** By providing a stable baseline, JustRL enables fairer comparisons. Future papers can compare against JustRL-DeepSeek or JustRL-Nemotron to isolate the value of new techniques without confounding variables from unstable baselines.
*   **Reduced Barrier to Entry:** While the compute cost is substantial, the removal of complex engineering pipelines (e.g., stage orchestration) lowers the expertise barrier. Researchers can focus on algorithmic improvements rather than pipeline debugging.
*   **Call for Validation:** The paper concludes with a methodological imperative: *"establish simple baselines first, then add complexity only when a simple, robust baseline demonstrably fails"* (**Section 6**). This encourages a culture of empirical validation over assumption-driven engineering.

In summary, JustRL changes the landscape by proving that **adequate scale with stable fundamentals** can outperform sophisticated engineering. It invites the field to pause the accumulation of complexity and instead deepen our understanding of why simple RL works when applied correctly. The path forward involves testing these boundaries across scales and domains, ensuring that every added technique earns its place through rigorous empirical evidence.
