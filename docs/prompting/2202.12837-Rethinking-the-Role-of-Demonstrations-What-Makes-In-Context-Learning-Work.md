## 1. Executive Summary

This paper challenges the assumption that in-context learning requires correct input-label mappings by evaluating 12 language models, including GPT-3 (175B) and MetaICL (774M), across 26 classification and multi-choice datasets.

> The authors demonstrate that ground truth demonstrations are not required, as randomly replacing labels barely hurts performance by only 0–5% absolute across 12 models including GPT-3 (Abstract, Figure 1).

This matters because it reveals that large language models rely on pre-trained knowledge triggered by structural cues rather than acquiring new task semantics from demonstrations during test time.

## 2. Context and Motivation

To understand the significance of this work, we must first establish the operational landscape of large language models (LMs) and the specific mystery surrounding how they adapt to new tasks. This paper situates itself at the intersection of practical deployment constraints and theoretical understanding of model behavior.

### The Practical Problem: Scaling Adaptation Methods
Historically, adapting a pre-trained language model to a specific downstream task (e.g., sentiment analysis or question answering) relied on **finetuning**. In finetuning, the model's weights are updated via gradient descent using labeled task data. While effective for smaller models, the paper notes in Section 2 that this approach becomes "often impractical to finetune a very large model (e.g. ≥10B parameters)." As model sizes grew into the hundreds of billions (such as GPT-3), the computational cost and storage requirements for maintaining finetuned copies of every model for every task became prohibitive.

This constraint led to the rise of **in-context learning** (Brown et al., 2020), described in Section 1 as performing a new task "via inference alone by conditioning on a few input-label pairs (demonstrations)." Instead of updating weights, the user provides examples directly in the input prompt. While prior work has consistently shown that in-context learning outperforms **zero-shot inference** (making predictions without any examples) on a wide range of tasks (Zhao et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021), the mechanism remains opaque.

### The Knowledge Gap: How Does In-Context Learning Work?
The core problem this paper addresses is the lack of understanding regarding *why* in-context learning succeeds. Section 1 explicitly states: "there has been little understanding of how the model learns and which aspects of the demonstrations contribute to end task performance."

When a model sees a few examples in a prompt, standard intuition suggests it is learning the **input-label mapping**—the rule that connects a specific input $x$ to its correct label $y$. For instance, if shown "Great movie → Positive," the model should learn that positive sentiment maps to the label "Positive." However, this assumption had not been rigorously tested. The authors identify a critical gap: we do not know if the model is actually learning this mapping from the demonstrations, or if it is leveraging other signals present in the prompt structure.

### Limitations of Prior Approaches
Existing research prior to this paper generally fell into two categories, both of which left the fundamental mechanism unexamined:

*   **Optimization-Focused Work:** Much of the literature focused on *improving* performance rather than understanding it. Section 2 lists efforts to propose "better ways of formulating the problem," "better ways of choosing labeled examples," "meta-training with an explicit in-context learning objective," and "learning to follow instructions." While these methods enhance results, they operate under the assumption that the demonstration's content (specifically the correct labels) is vital.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** Some work attempted to explain the phenomenon theoretically. For instance, Xie et al. (2022) formalized in-context learning as Bayesian inference that uses demonstrations to recover latent concepts. Razeghi et al. (2022) showed performance correlates with term frequencies in pretraining data. However, Section 2 notes that "relatively less work has been done to understand why in-context learning works" from an empirical standpoint. There was no systematic dissection of the demonstration components to isolate their individual contributions.

### Why This Investigation Matters
The motivation for this study extends beyond academic curiosity; it has direct implications for how we deploy and trust these models:

1.  **Cost and Efficiency:** If ground truth labels are not strictly necessary, the cost of preparing demonstrations drops significantly. Users could potentially generate effective prompts using unlabeled data or synthetic labels, bypassing the need for expensive human annotation.
2.  **Model Capacity and Learning:** The results inform us about what the model learned during pretraining. If the model performs well without correct input-label mappings in the prompt, it suggests the model already possesses the task capability internally and the demonstrations merely "trigger" this knowledge. Section 6 discusses this implication, noting that if models do not rely on the correspondence provided in demonstrations, they may not be "learning new tasks at test time" in the strict sense.
3.  **Reliability and Risks:** Understanding the mechanism reveals limitations. If the model relies on pre-existing knowledge rather than learning from the prompt, in-context learning may fail on tasks where the input-label correspondence was not captured during pretraining. Section 6 warns that applying in-context learning to problems not present in pretraining data could be "potentially risky."

### Positioning of This Work
This paper positions itself as the "first that provides an empirical analysis that investigates why in-context learning achieves performance gains over zero-shot inference" (Section 2). Unlike prior work that tweaks hyperparameters or selects better examples, this study systematically ablates (removes or alters) specific components of the demonstrations to measure their impact.

The authors decompose the demonstration into four distinct aspects (Figure 7, Section 5):
1.  The input-label mapping (correctness of pairs).
2.  The distribution of the input text.
3.  The label space (the set of possible output words).
4.  The format (the structural pairing of inputs and labels).

By isolating these variables, the paper moves beyond asking "How do we make in-context learning better?" to ask "What information is the model actually extracting from the context?" This shift allows the authors to challenge the foundational assumption that correct labels are the primary driver of performance, setting the stage for the counter-intuitive findings presented in the Executive Summary.

## 3. Technical Approach

This paper is an empirical ablation study that systematically decomposes the components of in-context learning demonstrations to measure their individual contributions to model performance. The core idea is to treat demonstrations not as a monolithic input but as a composition of separable signals—input-label correctness, input distribution, label space, and structural format—and isolate each through controlled experimental variants.

### 3.1 Reader orientation (approachable technical breakdown)

The *system* being studied is a decoder-only large language model performing task inference by reading example input-output pairs in its context window without any weight updates. The problem it solves is understanding which parts of those example pairs actually matter for performance, and the solution shape is a series of controlled experiments where specific components of the demonstrations are systematically altered or removed while holding others constant.

### 3.2 Big-picture architecture (diagram in words)

The experimental pipeline consists of four major components arranged sequentially. First, the **Model Selection Module** chooses from 12 language model configurations spanning 774M to 175B parameters, including both standard pre-trained models and meta-trained variants. Second, the **Demonstration Construction Module** builds prompt sequences by sampling k training examples and applying one of several transformation rules (gold labels, random labels, out-of-distribution inputs, etc.). Third, the **Inference Engine** processes the constructed prompt using either direct probability estimation or noisy channel inference to produce predictions. Fourth, the **Evaluation Module** computes task-specific metrics (Macro-F1 for classification, Accuracy for multi-choice) averaged across datasets and random seeds. Information flows from model selection through demonstration construction into inference, with evaluation metrics feeding back to compare variant performance.

### 3.3 Roadmap for the deep dive

- We begin with the model inventory and inference methods because understanding what systems are being tested establishes the scope of claims.
- We then explain the demonstration construction pipeline since this is where the core experimental manipulation occurs.
- We detail the four ablation dimensions (input-label mapping, input distribution, label space, format) because these define the paper's analytical framework.
- We cover the evaluation protocol and hyperparameters to ensure reproducibility and proper interpretation of results.
- We conclude with the mathematical formulation of in-context learning to ground the intuition in precise notation.

### 3.4 Detailed, sentence-based technical breakdown

**Paper Type and Core Mechanism**

This is an empirical analysis paper that treats in-context learning as a black-box system and probes its behavior through controlled input perturbations rather than modifying model architecture or training objectives. The fundamental mechanism under investigation is how a language model transforms a sequence of demonstration examples plus a test query into a task prediction without any gradient-based learning.

**Model Inventory and Configuration**

The study evaluates 12 distinct model configurations derived from 6 base language models, each tested with two inference methods (Table 1). The models span three orders of magnitude in parameter count: GPT-2 Large and MetaICL at 774M parameters, GPT-J at 6B parameters, fairseq models at 6.7B and 13B parameters, and GPT-3 at 175B parameters. MetaICL is initialized from GPT-2 Large but undergoes additional meta-training on a collection of supervised datasets with an explicit in-context learning objective, meaning it is optimized to learn from demonstrations during inference rather than just during pretraining. The fairseq models are dense architectures from Artetxe et al. (2021), and GPT-3 is accessed via the Davinci API (base version, not the instruct-tuned version). All models are decoder-only, meaning they process text autoregressively from left to right, which is critical because it determines how demonstrations are positioned relative to the test input in the context window.

**Inference Methods: Direct vs. Channel**

Two inference methods are employed following Min et al. (2021a), and the choice between them affects how the model conditions on demonstration content. The **direct method** computes the probability of each candidate label $y$ given the test input $x$ and all demonstrations, then selects the label with maximum probability:

$$\hat{y} = \text{argmax}_{y \in C} P(y | x_1, y_1, ..., x_k, y_k, x)$$

where $C$ is the set of candidate labels, $(x_i, y_i)$ are the k demonstration pairs, and $x$ is the test input. The **channel method** (also called noisy channel inference) flips the conditioning by computing the probability of the input given each candidate label:

$$\hat{y} = \text{argmax}_{y \in C} P(x | y, x_1, y_1, ..., x_k, y_k)$$

This approach can be advantageous when the label space is small and well-defined, as it allows the model to evaluate how well each label explains the observed input. The paper notes that all methods described can be converted between direct and channel by swapping the roles of $x$ and $y$, maintaining experimental consistency across both inference paradigms.

**Demonstration Construction Pipeline**

The demonstration construction process follows a precise sequence that determines what information reaches the model. First, k training examples are sampled uniformly at random from the task's training data, where k defaults to 16 unless otherwise specified. Second, each example is formatted using a template that concatenates the input text with its label, with specific separators depending on the model family (space for GPT-2, newline for MetaICL/GPT-J/GPT-3, newline for fairseq models). Third, k demonstration pairs are concatenated together in sequence. Fourth, the test input is appended to the end of the demonstration sequence. Fifth, the model generates or scores candidate completions based on the full context. This pipeline is modified systematically across experimental conditions to isolate specific components of the demonstrations.

**Ablation Dimension 1: Input-Label Mapping Correctness**

The first ablation dimension tests whether the correctness of individual input-label pairings matters for performance. The baseline condition uses **demonstrations with gold labels**, where each input $x_i$ is paired with its true label $y_i$ from the training data. The experimental condition uses **demonstrations with random labels**, where each input $x_i$ is paired with a label $\tilde{y}_i$ sampled uniformly at random from the label space $C$, breaking the semantic correspondence between input and output. A more granular variant tests **demonstrations with a% correct labels**, where exactly $k \times a/100$ pairs use gold labels and $k \times (1 - a/100)$ pairs use incorrect labels sampled from $C - y_i$ (Algorithm 1 in Appendix B). This creates a continuum from 0% correct (all random) to 100% correct (all gold), allowing the authors to measure whether performance degrades gradually or remains stable as correctness decreases. The key insight is that if ground truth mappings matter, performance should drop proportionally with the percentage of incorrect labels; if they do not matter, performance should remain stable regardless of label correctness.

**Ablation Dimension 2: Input Text Distribution**

The second ablation dimension tests whether the input texts in demonstrations need to come from the same distribution as the test data. The experimental condition uses **OOD (out-of-distribution) demonstrations**, where the input texts $\{x_{i,rand}\}_{i=1}^k$ are sampled from an external corpus (CC-News) rather than the task's training data, while labels are still sampled from the task's label space $C$. The input texts are length-matched to test inputs to control for sequence length effects. This variant preserves the label space and format while removing the signal about what kinds of inputs the task involves. If input distribution matters, performance should drop significantly when demonstrations contain unrelated text (e.g., news articles for a sentiment analysis task); if it does not matter, performance should remain stable as long as the label space and format are preserved.

**Ablation Dimension 3: Label Space Specification**

The third ablation dimension tests whether the model needs to see the correct set of possible output labels in the demonstrations. The experimental condition uses **demonstrations with random English words**, where labels are sampled from a set of 61,569 English words (from pypi.org/project/english-words) rather than the task's label space $C$. The size of the random label set matches the size of $C$ (i.e., $|C_{rand}| = |C|$), controlling for the number of distinct labels. This variant preserves the input distribution and format while removing information about which specific words constitute valid outputs. For direct models that must generate labels, knowing the label space should be critical; for channel models that condition on labels, the label space may matter less since the model evaluates $P(x|y)$ rather than $P(y|x)$.

**Ablation Dimension 4: Structural Format**

The fourth ablation dimension tests whether the pairing structure itself (input followed by label, repeated k times) provides a useful signal independent of content. Two variants remove this format: **demonstrations with no labels** concatenate only the inputs $x_1, ..., x_k$ without any labels, and **demonstrations with labels only** concatenate only the labels $y_1, ..., y_k$ without any inputs. These variants are compared against the full format condition to measure how much the input-label pairing structure contributes to performance. The hypothesis is that the format signals to the model that it should complete the sequence by producing a label for the test input, essentially triggering a completion behavior learned during pretraining.

**Evaluation Protocol and Metrics**

The evaluation protocol uses 26 datasets spanning six task categories: sentiment analysis, paraphrase detection, natural language inference, hate speech detection, question answering, and sentence completion (Appendix A, Table 2). All datasets are low-resource with fewer than 10,000 training examples, ensuring that in-context learning is being tested in a regime where traditional finetuning would struggle. For classification tasks, performance is measured using Macro-F1, which is preferred over accuracy for imbalanced class distributions. For multi-choice tasks, performance is measured using Accuracy. Results are computed by averaging over 5 random seeds for demonstration sampling, then computing a macro-average across datasets. For the largest models (fairseq 13B and GPT-3), computational constraints limit evaluation to 6 datasets and 3 random seeds. The default number of demonstrations is k = 16, but ablations test k ∈ {4, 8, 16, 32} to measure how performance scales with demonstration count.

**Template Design and Formatting**

The paper uses minimal templates by default, which are simple concatenations of input and label with minimal instructional text (Appendix B, Table 3). For example, an MRPC (paraphrase detection) minimal template is: `sentence 1: [text1] [SEP] sentence 2: [text2] \n {equivalent|not_equivalent}`. Manual templates from prior work are also tested as a robustness check; these include more natural language framing like `The question is: [text2]. True or False? \n The answer is: {True|False}`. The finding that minimal templates perform comparably to manual templates suggests that the specific wording matters less than the structural format of input-label pairing.

**Mathematical Framework for Demonstration Variants**

The paper's experimental conditions can be formalized as different instantiations of the demonstration sequence $D$. For gold label demonstrations:

$$D_{gold} = [(x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2), ..., (x_k, y_k)]$$

For random label demonstrations:

$$D_{random} = [(x_1, \tilde{y}_1), (x_2, \tilde{y}_2), ..., (x_k, \tilde{y}_k)] \text{ where } \tilde{y}_i \sim \text{Uniform}(C)$$

For OOD demonstrations:

$$D_{OOD} = [(x_{1,rand}, \tilde{y}_1), (x_{2,rand}, \tilde{y}_2), ..., (x_{k,rand}, \tilde{y}_k)] \text{ where } x_{i,rand} \sim \text{External Corpus}$$

The prediction is then made by conditioning on the full sequence including the test input:

$$P(y | D, x) = P(y | x_1, y_1, ..., x_k, y_k, x)$$

This formulation makes explicit that the model's prediction depends on the joint distribution of all demonstration components, and the ablation study measures which components of $D$ carry the most predictive signal.

**Design Choices and Alternative Approaches**

The authors chose ablation over alternative methodologies for specific reasons. One alternative would be to analyze model attention patterns or internal representations, but this would require model access that is not available for GPT-3 and would not directly measure functional impact on performance. Another alternative would be to use synthetic tasks with controlled properties, but the authors prioritize natural language tasks to ensure findings generalize to real-world applications. The choice to use random labels rather than semantically incorrect but plausible labels (e.g., swapping "positive" and "negative" for sentiment) is deliberate: uniform random sampling ensures no systematic bias is introduced, and the finding that even completely random labels work suggests the model is not relying on label semantics at all. The decision to test both direct and channel inference methods ensures findings are not artifacts of a single inference paradigm.

**Reproducibility and Implementation Details**

All experiments are reproducible from the public code repository (github.com/Alrope123/rethinking-demonstrations). The HuggingFace Datasets library is used for data loading, with development sets used for evaluation following Ye et al. (2021). For GPT-3, the Davinci API is used with the assumption of 175B parameters following Gao et al. (2021) and Artetxe et al. (2021). MetaICL's meta-training datasets do not overlap with the evaluation datasets, preventing contamination. The random label sampling uses uniform distribution by default, but Appendix C.2 tests sampling from the true label distribution in the training data, which further reduces the performance gap between gold and random labels (from 1.9% to 1.3% for Channel MetaICL, from 5.0% to 3.5% for Channel GPT-J).

## 4. Key Insights and Innovations

This section synthesizes the empirical findings from Sections 4 and 5 to articulate the paper's fundamental contributions to our understanding of in-context learning. While the Technical Approach detailed *how* the experiments were constructed, here we explain *what the results imply* about model behavior. These insights challenge standard assumptions about machine learning and propose a new framework for interpreting prompt-based inference.

### 4.1 In-Context Learning is Retrieval, Not Task Learning

The most significant innovation of this work is the empirical demonstration that in-context learning does not function as "learning" in the traditional supervised sense. In standard supervised training, if you replace ground truth labels with random noise (0% correct labels), model performance collapses to random guessing because the gradient updates reinforce incorrect mappings. However, this paper shows that in-context learning behaves differently.

*   **The Finding:** As established in Section 4.1 and visualized in **Figure 3**, replacing gold labels with random labels results in a performance drop of only **0–5% absolute** across 12 models. Even more strikingly, **Figure 4** shows that using **0% correct labels** (all random) still preserves **92–100%** of the performance gains achieved by gold labels for models like MetaICL and GPT-J in multi-choice tasks.
*   **Why This Differs from Prior Work:** Prior literature (e.g., Brown et al., 2020) treated in-context learning as "few-shot learning," implying the model infers the task rule $y = f(x)$ from the examples. This paper provides evidence that the model does not infer $f$ from the prompt. Instead, the model relies on capabilities acquired during pretraining.
*   **Significance:** This reframes in-context learning as a **retrieval mechanism** rather than a learning mechanism. The demonstrations do not teach the model *how* to solve the task; they trigger the model to access a pre-existing capability that matches the demonstration's structure. As discussed in Section 6, this suggests the model "does not learn new tasks at test time" under a strict definition of learning (capturing input-label correspondence). This limits the scope of tasks solvable via in-context learning to those already implicitly represented in the pretraining distribution.

### 4.2 A Hierarchy of Prompt Signals

By decomposing the demonstration into four aspects (mapping, input distribution, label space, format), the paper establishes a hierarchy of importance that was previously undefined. This provides a theoretical framework for analyzing why some prompts work and others fail.

*   **The Finding:** Section 5 reveals that not all demonstration components are equal. **Figure 10** (Section 5.3) demonstrates that removing the **format** (the input-label pairing structure) reduces performance to near zero-shot levels, even if the input distribution and label space are preserved. Conversely, preserving the format allows the model to retain up to **95%** of performance gains even with random inputs and random labels (e.g., Direct MetaICL in classification).
*   **Why This Differs from Prior Work:** Previous optimization efforts focused heavily on selecting "better examples" (Liu et al., 2021) or engineering better natural language instructions. This work shows that the **structural format** is more critical than the semantic content of the labels.
*   **Significance:** This insight offers a practical design principle: **Structure > Content**. When constructing prompts, ensuring the model recognizes the *pattern* of input-output pairing is more important than ensuring the specific examples are semantically correct. This explains the brittleness of in-context learning noted in prior work (Lu et al., 2021); small changes to format may disrupt the retrieval signal more than changes to content.

### 4.3 Meta-Training Amplifies Shortcut Learning

The paper provides a novel analysis of how meta-training (training a model to learn from context) alters the model's reliance on demonstration components. This is critical for understanding the behavior of modern instruction-tuned models.

*   **The Finding:** Section 5.4 highlights that **MetaICL** (a meta-trained model) exhibits the observed trends more strongly than base models. For MetaICL, the ground truth input-label mapping matters **even less** (0.1–0.9% drop vs. larger drops for base models), and the format matters **even more**. The authors hypothesize that meta-training encourages the model to "exclusively exploit simpler aspects of the demonstrations," such as format, because they are easier to learn than complex input-label mappings.
*   **Why This Differs from Prior Work:** Meta-training is typically assumed to improve the model's ability to learn tasks from context. This paper suggests it may instead improve the model's ability to **ignore** the task definition in the context and rely on priors, effectively optimizing for "prompt following" rather than "task adaptation."
*   **Significance:** This warns against assuming that meta-trained models are more "capable" of learning novel tasks. They may be more efficient at recognizing prompt structures, but potentially less flexible in adopting truly new input-label correspondences that contradict their pretraining. This has implications for safety and alignment: if a model is optimized to follow format over content, it may be more susceptible to structural jailbreaks or less capable of correcting misconceptions via examples.

### 4.4 Redefining the Zero-Shot Baseline

A direct practical innovation arising from these insights is the proposal of a stronger zero-shot baseline that leverages unlabeled data.

*   **The Finding:** Section 6 discusses that since ground truth labels are not required, one can achieve nearly $k$-shot performance by pairing unlabeled training inputs with random labels. This creates a hybrid method that sits between zero-shot and few-shot learning.
*   **Why This Differs from Prior Work:** Standard zero-shot inference uses *no* task-specific data. Standard few-shot inference requires *labeled* task-specific data. This work identifies a middle ground: using *unlabeled* task-specific data with synthetic labels.
*   **Significance:** This lowers the barrier for deploying large models. Practitioners can improve performance over naive zero-shot inference without the cost of human annotation. As noted in Section 6, "our zero-shot baseline level is significantly higher than previously thought." This shifts the benchmark for future research; new methods must now beat this enhanced unlabeled baseline, not just the naive zero-shot baseline.

### 4.5 Limitations as Insights

Finally, the paper's limitations themselves provide insight into the boundaries of current model capabilities.

*   **The Finding:** The authors note in Section 6 that these findings apply to tasks where the input-label correspondence is likely captured during pretraining (e.g., sentiment analysis). They cite Rong (2021) to suggest synthetic tasks with limited inputs may actually use ground truth labels more. Additionally, extending this to **generation tasks** is non-trivial because removing input-output correspondence while maintaining output distribution is difficult for open-ended text.
*   **Significance:** This defines the **domain of applicability** for the paper's claims. In-context learning is not a universal learner; it is a **pattern matcher** for pre-existing knowledge. If a task requires a novel mapping not present in pretraining (e.g., mapping arbitrary symbols to meanings not seen before), in-context learning with random labels will likely fail. This clarifies that the "magic" of in-context learning is bounded by the scope of the pretraining data.

## 5. Experimental Analysis

This section provides a comprehensive examination of the experimental methodology and quantitative results that support the paper's central claims. We analyze the evaluation framework, present specific numerical findings from each ablation dimension, assess the strength of evidence, and identify boundary conditions where the findings do or do not hold.

### 5.1 Evaluation Methodology

**Dataset Selection and Characteristics**

The study evaluates on 26 datasets spanning six task categories (Table 2, Appendix A). This diverse selection ensures findings generalize beyond a single domain:

*   **Sentiment Analysis (2 datasets):** `financial_phrasebank` (1,811 train / 453 eval) and `poem_sentiment` (892 train / 105 eval)
*   **Paraphrase Detection (2 datasets):** `medical_questions_pairs` (2,438 train / 610 eval) and `glue-mrpc` (3,668 train / 408 eval)
*   **Natural Language Inference (5 datasets):** Including `glue-wnli`, `glue-rte`, `superglue-cb`, `sick`, and `climate_fever`
*   **Hate Speech Detection (6 datasets):** Including `hate_speech18`, `ethos` variants, and `tweet_eval` variants
*   **Question Answering (5 datasets):** Including `openbookqa`, `commonsense_qa`, `qasc`, `ai2_arc`, and `quarel`
*   **Sentence Completion (6 datasets):** Including `codah`, `superglue-copa`, `dream`, and `quartz` variants

All datasets are **low-resource** with fewer than 10,000 training examples, ensuring the evaluation targets the regime where in-context learning is most relevant. The authors use development sets for evaluation following Ye et al. (2021), and sample k training examples using 5 different random seeds to reduce variance.

**Metrics and Aggregation**

The paper employs task-appropriate metrics to ensure fair comparison:

*   **Macro-F1** for classification tasks (preferred over accuracy for imbalanced class distributions, as noted in footnote 3 of Section 3)
*   **Accuracy** for multi-choice tasks

Results are aggregated in two stages: first computing per-dataset averages over seeds, then computing a **macro-average over datasets**. This prevents large datasets from dominating the overall results. For the largest models (fairseq 13B and GPT-3), computational constraints limit evaluation to 6 datasets and 3 random seeds rather than the full 26 datasets and 5 seeds.

**Baseline Comparisons**

Three primary baselines anchor the experimental comparisons (Section 4.1):

1.  **No Demonstrations:** Zero-shot inference using $ \text{argmax}_{y \in C} P(y|x) $ without any labeled examples
2.  **Demonstrations with Gold Labels:** Standard in-context learning with k correct input-label pairs
3.  **Demonstrations with Random Labels:** Each input $ x_i $ paired with $ \tilde{y}_i $ sampled uniformly from the label space $ C $

The critical comparison is between baselines 2 and 3. If ground truth mappings matter, baseline 3 should significantly underperform baseline 2. If they do not matter, performance should be comparable.

**Default Hyperparameters**

Unless otherwise specified, experiments use **k = 16 demonstrations** (Section 3, "Other Details"). Examples are sampled uniformly from training data. Minimal templates are used by default (Appendix B, Table 3), though manual templates from prior work are tested as a robustness check in Section 4.2.

### 5.2 Main Quantitative Results

**Gold Labels vs. Random Labels (Section 4.1)**

The foundational finding is visualized in **Figure 1** (summary) and **Figure 3** (full model breakdown). The results are strikingly consistent:

> "Replacing gold labels with random labels only marginally hurts performance. The trend is consistent over nearly all models: models see performance drop in the range of **0–5% absolute**." (Section 4.1)

Specific breakdowns from **Figure 3** show:

*   **Classification tasks (Macro-F1):** Most models cluster between 35–55% for both gold and random labels, with gaps typically under 3 percentage points
*   **Multi-choice tasks (Accuracy):** Most models cluster between 45–65% for both conditions, with gaps typically under 2 percentage points

The paper notes that multi-choice tasks show **less impact** from random labels (1.7% average drop) compared to classification tasks (2.6% average drop). This suggests multi-choice tasks may have more constrained output spaces that are easier to infer from context alone.

**MetaICL Exception:** MetaICL shows particularly small drops of **0.1–0.9% absolute**, suggesting meta-training amplifies the model's ability to ignore input-label correctness (Section 4.1, discussed further in Section 5.4).

**Varying Percentage of Correct Labels (Section 4.2)**

**Figure 4** tests a continuum from 0% to 100% correct labels. The results challenge the assumption that more correct labels yield better performance:

*   **MetaICL (Classification):** 0% correct labels preserves **92%** of the improvements from gold labels
*   **MetaICL (Multi-choice):** 0% correct labels preserves **100%** of the improvements from gold labels
*   **GPT-J (Multi-choice):** 0% correct labels preserves **97%** of the improvements from gold labels
*   **GPT-J (Classification):** Shows more sensitivity, with nearly **10% drop** when always using incorrect labels, but still significantly better than no demonstrations

The key insight is that **always using incorrect labels significantly outperforms no demonstrations** across all conditions. This confirms that the mere presence of demonstrations (regardless of label correctness) provides substantial value.

**Varying Number of Demonstrations (Section 4.2)**

**Figure 5** ablates the number of demonstrations (k ∈ {4, 8, 16, 32}). Two findings emerge:

1.  **Small k is sufficient:** Using demonstrations significantly outperforms no demonstrations even with **k = 4**
2.  **Diminishing returns:** Performance does not increase much as k increases when **k ≥ 8**, for both gold and random labels. The gap between gold and random remains consistently small at **0.8–1.6%** across all k values (with one exception of 4.4% at k = 4 for classification, attributed to high variance)

This contrasts sharply with supervised training, where performance typically increases rapidly with more labeled data, especially at small k. The authors hypothesize that larger labeled data primarily helps supervise input-label correspondence, while other components (input distribution, label space, format) are recoverable from small data.

**Template Robustness (Section 4.2)**

**Figure 6** compares minimal templates (default) against manual templates from prior work. The finding that "replacing gold labels with random labels barely hurting performance" **holds with manual templates**. Notably, manual templates do not consistently outperform minimal templates, suggesting that specific wording matters less than the structural format of input-label pairing.

### 5.3 Ablation Studies: Decomposing Demonstration Components

Section 5 systematically isolates four aspects of demonstrations (Figure 7): input-label mapping, input distribution, label space, and format. Each ablation removes one component while preserving others.

**Impact of Input Distribution (Section 5.1, Figure 8)**

This ablation uses **OOD (out-of-distribution) demonstrations** where inputs are sampled from CC-News rather than task training data, while preserving label space and format.

Results show substantial performance drops when input distribution is mismatched:

*   **Channel MetaICL:** 3–16% absolute drop in both classification and multi-choice
*   **Direct GPT-J:** 3–16% absolute drop; in multi-choice, OOD inputs perform **worse than no demonstrations**
*   **Direct MetaICL:** Exception showing minimal drop, attributed to meta-training effects (Section 5.4)

The authors explain this occurs because "conditioning on the in-distribution text makes the task closer to language modeling, since the LM always conditioned on the in-distribution text during training." This suggests input distribution is a **critical signal** for in-context learning, more so than label correctness.

**Impact of Label Space (Section 5.2, Figure 9)**

This ablation uses **random English words** as labels (sampled from 61,569 words) instead of the task's label space $ C $, while preserving input distribution and format.

Results reveal a **divergence between direct and channel models**:

*   **Direct Models:** Performance gap of **5–16% absolute** between random labels (within label space) and random English words. This indicates direct models heavily rely on knowing the valid output vocabulary.
*   **Channel Models:** Only **0–2% drop** (sometimes even an increase). The authors hypothesize this is because channel models condition on labels ($ P(x|y) $) rather than generating them ($ P(y|x) $), so knowing the label space matters less.

This finding is nuanced: label space matters significantly for generation-based inference but minimally for scoring-based inference.

**Impact of Format (Section 5.3, Figure 10)**

This ablation removes the input-label pairing structure entirely, testing **demonstrations with no labels** (inputs only) and **demonstrations with labels only** (outputs only).

Results are definitive:

> "Removing the format is close to or worse than no demonstrations, indicating the importance of the format." (Section 5.3)

The format signal—simply showing the model that inputs and labels are paired in a repeating structure—triggers the completion behavior. More strikingly, **keeping the format alone preserves most performance gains**:

*   **Direct MetaICL (Classification):** Retains **95%** of in-context learning improvements by pairing random sentences with the label set (preserving format + label space, removing input distribution + mapping)
*   **Direct MetaICL (Multi-choice):** Retains **82%** of improvements under the same condition
*   **Channel MetaICL (Classification):** Retains **82%** of improvements by pairing training inputs with random English words (preserving format + input distribution, removing label space + mapping)
*   **Channel GPT-J (Classification):** Retains **87%** of improvements
*   **Channel MetaICL (Multi-choice):** Retains **86%** of improvements
*   **Channel GPT-J (Multi-choice):** Retains **75%** of improvements

This demonstrates that **format is the most critical component**, capable of recovering the majority of in-context learning benefits even when both input distribution and label space are partially compromised.

### 5.4 Meta-Training Effects (Section 5.4)

MetaICL receives special analysis because it is explicitly trained with an in-context learning objective. The patterns observed throughout the paper are **significantly more evident** with MetaICL:

*   Ground truth input-label mapping matters **even less** (0.1–0.9% drop vs. larger drops for base models)
*   Format matters **even more**
*   **Direct MetaICL:** Nearly zero influence from input-label mapping and input distribution
*   **Channel MetaICL:** Nearly zero influence from input-label mapping and output space

The authors hypothesize that "meta-training encourages the model to exclusively exploit simpler aspects of the demonstrations and to ignore others." The reasoning is that:
1.  Input-label mapping is harder to exploit
2.  Format is easier to exploit
3.  The space of text the model generates is easier to exploit than the space it conditions on

This suggests meta-training may optimize for **prompt following efficiency** rather than **task adaptation capability**, which has implications for how we interpret instruction-tuned model behavior.

### 5.5 Robustness Checks and Failure Cases

**Random Labels from True Distribution (Appendix C.2)**

The default experiments sample random labels uniformly from the label space. Appendix C.2 tests sampling from the **true label distribution** in training data (important for imbalanced datasets). Results show the gap further reduces:

*   **Channel MetaICL:** Gap reduces from **1.9% to 1.3%** absolute
*   **Channel GPT-J:** Gap reduces from **5.0% to 3.5%** absolute

This confirms the main finding is robust to label sampling strategy and may even be conservative under uniform sampling.

**Per-Dataset Breakdown (Appendix C.2, Figure 12)**

**Figure 12** shows performance gaps per dataset. The trend of small gaps is **consistent across most datasets**, but outliers exist:

*   **financial_phrasebank with GPT-J:** Up to **14% absolute** gap (the most extreme case noted in Section 6 Limitations)
*   **Several hate speech detection datasets:** Show larger than average gaps

The authors acknowledge this variability and note that "different datasets may behave differently." This is an important boundary condition: the findings hold at the macro level but may not apply uniformly to every task.

**Model Size Effects (Response to Reviewers)**

The reviewer response document notes that "absolute differences are similar across different model sizes (Figure 3), but since the large models have higher absolute performance, the **relative differences are larger with larger models**." This suggests the phenomenon scales with model capacity but does not fundamentally change.

**Constant Label Variant (Appendix C.3)**

An additional ablation tests **demonstrations with a constant label** (all labels replaced with "answer"). Results are consistently worse than random English labels. The authors attribute this to format disruption: constant labels may function as separators between examples rather than as labels, changing the structural signal.

**Test Input Repetition Variant (Appendix C.3)**

Another ablation replaces all demonstration inputs with the **test input** (each paired with a random label). This variant performs significantly worse than other methods. The authors hypothesize this changes the format because the repeated input functions as a separator rather than as varied demonstration content.

### 5.6 Assessment of Experimental Strength

**Strengths of the Evidence**

1.  **Scale and Diversity:** 12 models across 3 orders of magnitude (774M to 175B parameters) and 26 datasets across 6 task categories provides strong generalization evidence.
2.  **Systematic Ablation:** The four-component decomposition (mapping, distribution, label space, format) is comprehensive and isolates variables cleanly.
3.  **Multiple Inference Methods:** Testing both direct and channel inference ensures findings are not artifacts of a single paradigm.
4.  **Robustness Checks:** Template variations, label sampling strategies, and k-value ablations all support the main conclusions.
5.  **Quantitative Precision:** Specific percentage drops (0–5%, 3–16%, 5–16%) are reported consistently, allowing readers to assess effect sizes.

**Limitations and Boundary Conditions**

1.  **Task Scope:** Experiments are limited to **classification and multi-choice tasks**. Section 6 explicitly notes that extending to **generation tasks** is non-trivial because removing input-output correspondence while maintaining output distribution is difficult for open-ended text.
2.  **Dataset Outliers:** While most datasets show small gaps, some (like `financial_phrasebank`) show gaps up to 14%. The paper does not fully explain why certain tasks are more sensitive to label correctness.
3.  **GPT-3 Evaluation:** Due to computational constraints, GPT-3 is evaluated on only 6 datasets and 3 seeds rather than the full suite. Figure 11 provides comparable numbers across all models on 3 datasets each to address this.
4.  **Synthetic Tasks:** The authors cite Rong (2021) to note that synthetic tasks with limited inputs may use ground truth labels more. The findings apply primarily to natural language tasks where input-label correspondences are likely captured during pretraining.
5.  **Pretraining Dependency:** The reviewer response clarifies that findings hold "only when some notion of input-label correspondences are already captured during pre-training." For tasks with sparse pretraining coverage, random labels are "highly unlikely to work."

**Do the Experiments Support the Claims?**

The experiments **convincingly support** the paper's central claim that ground truth input-label mappings are not required for effective in-context learning on classification and multi-choice tasks. The evidence is strong because:

*   The 0–5% performance drop is consistent across 12 models
*   The finding holds across multiple ablation dimensions (varying k, varying % correct, varying templates)
*   Alternative explanations are ruled out through controlled comparisons (e.g., format ablation shows structure matters more than content)

However, the experiments **do not support** universal claims about all task types. The authors appropriately limit their conclusions to classification and multi-choice tasks and acknowledge that generation tasks and synthetic tasks may behave differently. This measured scope strengthens rather than weakens the paper's contributions.

## 6. Limitations and Trade-offs

While this paper provides compelling evidence that ground truth labels are not strictly necessary for in-context learning, several important limitations and trade-offs constrain the generalizability and practical application of these findings. Understanding these boundaries is critical for practitioners who might otherwise assume random labels work universally.

### 6.1 Task Type Constraints

The most significant limitation is the narrow scope of task types evaluated. All 26 datasets fall into **classification and multi-choice categories** (Table 2, Appendix A), where the output space is discrete and constrained. The authors explicitly acknowledge this boundary in Section 6:

> "Our experiments are limited to classification and multi-choice tasks. We hypothesize that ground truth output may not be necessary for in-context learning in the open-set tasks such as generation, but leave this to future work."

This limitation matters because generation tasks (e.g., summarization, translation, open-ended question answering) require the model to produce novel text sequences rather than select from a fixed label set. The paper notes in the reviewer response (lines 066–074) that extending the analysis to generation is "not very trivial because designing the demonstrations that do not keep the input-output correspondence but keep the output distribution is difficult." 

**Why this constraint exists:** In classification, you can replace "positive" with "negative" and still have a valid label from the same space. In generation, replacing a correct summary with a random sentence destroys both the input-output correspondence *and* the output distribution simultaneously. This means the paper's central finding—that label correctness matters less than format and distribution—may not transfer to the growing class of generative applications.

**Practical implication:** Practitioners should not assume random labels work for summarization, translation, or creative writing tasks. The retrieval mechanism identified in this paper likely depends on the model having seen similar input-label pairings during pretraining, which is more probable for common classification tasks than for open-ended generation.

### 6.2 Pretraining Dependency and Task Novelty

The findings rely on a critical assumption that is not always stated explicitly: **the input-label correspondence must already be captured during pretraining**. The reviewer response (lines 024–031) clarifies this boundary condition:

> "We believe that the findings will hold only when some notion of input-label correspondences are already captured during pre-training—for tasks whose input-label correspondences during pretraining is sparse, the demonstrations with random labels are highly unlikely to work."

This creates a fundamental trade-off:

| Scenario | Random Labels Work? | Reason |
|----------|---------------------|--------|
| Sentiment analysis (common in pretraining) | Yes | Model already knows "good" → "positive" |
| Novel symbol mapping (e.g., △ → "cat") | No | Never seen during pretraining |
| Domain-specific classification (e.g., medical diagnosis) | Uncertain | Depends on pretraining coverage |

**Why this matters:** The paper's claim that models "do not learn new tasks at test time" (Section 6) is actually a limitation, not just an insight. In-context learning cannot teach the model something it does not already know—it can only trigger retrieval of pre-existing knowledge. This contradicts the "few-shot learning" framing from Brown et al. (2020), which implied genuine task acquisition.

**Evidence from the paper:** The authors cite Rong (2021) in Section 6 to note that "synthetic tasks with more limited inputs may actually use the ground truth labels more." This suggests that when the input-label mapping is truly novel (not present in pretraining), the model cannot rely on priors and must actually learn from the demonstrations.

### 6.3 Dataset Variability and Outliers

While the macro-level analysis shows consistent 0–5% drops when using random labels, **individual datasets exhibit significant variability**. Figure 12 (Appendix C.2) reveals outlier cases where the gap is much larger:

> "In the most extreme case, nearly 14% absolute on the financial_phrasebank dataset with GPT-J." (Section 6 Limitations)

**Which datasets are most affected:** The paper identifies several categories with larger gaps:
- `financial_phrasebank` (up to 14% drop)
- Several hate speech detection datasets (`hate_speech18`, `ethos` variants)
- Some stance detection tasks (`tweet_eval-stance_*`)

**Why variability exists:** The authors do not provide a complete explanation, but several factors likely contribute:
1. **Label imbalance:** Financial sentiment may have skewed label distributions that uniform random sampling does not capture (though Appendix C.2 shows sampling from true distribution only reduces the gap marginally)
2. **Domain specificity:** Financial language may be less represented in pretraining data than general sentiment
3. **Task complexity:** Hate speech detection may require more nuanced input-label reasoning than simple sentiment

**Practical implication:** Practitioners cannot assume the 0–5% drop applies to their specific task. The paper recommends collecting labeled data and finetuning for problems not well-represented in pretraining (reviewer response, lines 037–041):

> "Applying in-context learning for problems that were not in the pretraining data would be potentially risky, and practitioners may want to collect the labeled data and finetune the model."

### 6.4 Meta-Training Trade-offs

The paper reveals an unexpected trade-off with meta-training (Section 5.4). MetaICL, which is explicitly trained to learn from context, shows the *smallest* dependence on ground truth labels (0.1–0.9% drop) but also shows the *strongest* reliance on format over content.

**The trade-off:** Meta-training optimizes for **prompt following efficiency** rather than **task adaptation capability**. The authors hypothesize:

> "Meta-training encourages the model to exclusively exploit simpler aspects of the demonstrations and to ignore others." (Section 5.4)

**Why this is concerning:**
- **Benefit:** Meta-trained models are more robust to label noise and require less careful prompt engineering
- **Cost:** They may be less capable of adopting truly novel input-label mappings that contradict pretraining priors
- **Safety implication:** If a model is optimized to follow format over content, it may be more susceptible to structural jailbreaks or less capable of correcting misconceptions via examples

**Evidence:** Direct MetaICL shows "nearly zero influence of the input-label mapping and the input distribution" (Section 5.4), suggesting it has learned to ignore semantic content in favor of structural cues. This efficiency gain comes at the cost of flexibility.

### 6.5 Computational and Evaluation Constraints

Several practical constraints limit the completeness of the analysis:

**GPT-3 evaluation scope:** Due to API costs and computational constraints, GPT-3 (175B) is evaluated on only **6 datasets and 3 random seeds** rather than the full 26 datasets and 5 seeds used for smaller models (Section 3, "Other Details"). Figure 11 provides comparable numbers across all models on 3 datasets each to partially address this, but the full GPT-3 results are less comprehensive.

**Why this matters:** GPT-3 is the most influential model in the in-context learning literature. If the findings do not hold uniformly at this scale, the paper's conclusions would be significantly weakened. The authors note in the reviewer response (lines 018–022) that "absolute differences are similar across different model sizes, but since the large models have higher absolute performance, the relative differences are larger with larger models."

**No training recommendations:** The reviewer response (lines 055–060) explicitly states:

> "Due to compute limitations, we were not be able to provide recommendations that are empirically supported. Future work may investigate aspects of language model pretraining that affect the findings, including the pretraining data and the objective."

This means practitioners cannot use this paper to guide *model training* decisions—only *inference* decisions. The findings describe what models do, not how to build better models.

### 6.6 Format Sensitivity and Brittleness

While the paper emphasizes that format is more important than label correctness, this creates a different kind of brittleness. Section 5.3 shows that removing the input-label pairing structure reduces performance to near zero-shot levels. Appendix C.3 reveals additional format sensitivities:

- **Constant labels** (all labels replaced with "answer") perform worse than random labels because they "change the format of the demonstrations, since they can be viewed as part of a separator between different demonstration examples"
- **Test input repetition** (all demonstration inputs replaced with the test input) performs significantly worse because "the input can be viewed as part of a separator between different demonstration examples"

**The trade-off:** The model is robust to *semantic* changes (wrong labels) but fragile to *structural* changes (format violations). This is counterintuitive—most practitioners would expect the opposite.

**Practical implication:** Prompt engineering effort should focus on maintaining consistent formatting rather than curating high-quality examples. However, this also means small formatting inconsistencies (e.g., varying separators, inconsistent newline usage) could degrade performance more than using incorrect labels.

### 6.7 Open Questions and Future Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several unresolved questions that limit the completeness of our understanding:

**Word ordering effects:** The reviewer response (lines 061–064) notes:

> "We think it is an important avenue for future work. For this paper, we did not include it in our scope due to requiring multiple times more experiments."

The order of demonstrations (Lu et al., 2021) and the order of tokens within demonstrations could affect results, but this was not systematically tested.

**Instruction-following connection:** Section 6 hypothesizes that findings extend to instruction-tuned models, citing Webson and Pavlick (2022) who showed "model performance does not degrade much with irrelevant or misleading instructions." However, the authors state:

> "We leave more analysis on instruction-following models for future work." (Section 6)

Given the industry shift toward instruction-tuned models (e.g., GPT-3.5, GPT-4, Claude), this is a significant gap.

**Chain-of-thought reasoning:** The paper notes (Section 6) that Madaan and Yazdanbakhsh (2022) conducted similar analysis with chain-of-thought prompting, finding that "using a random rationale in the demonstrations significantly degrades the performance, but other types of counterfactual rationales do not degrade the performance as much." This suggests the findings may not extend to reasoning tasks that require intermediate steps.

**How to improve truly novel task learning:** The paper identifies but does not solve the core limitation:

> "This leads to the research question of how to make progress in NLP problems that in-context learning does not solve: whether we need a better way of extracting the input-label mappings that are already stored in the LM, a better variant of the LM objective that learns a wider range of task semantics, or explicit supervision through fine-tuning on the labeled data." (Section 6)

This remains an open research direction with no proposed solution.

### 6.8 Summary of Trade-offs

Table 6 summarizes the key trade-offs identified in this analysis:

| Trade-off | Benefit | Cost | Evidence |
|-----------|---------|------|----------|
| Random vs. Gold Labels | Reduces annotation cost | 0–5% performance drop (up to 14% on outliers) | Figure 3, 12 |
| Meta-training | More robust to label noise | Less capable of novel task adaptation | Section 5.4 |
| Format focus | Simpler prompt engineering | Fragile to structural changes | Section 5.3, Appendix C.3 |
| Pretraining reliance | No test-time learning needed | Cannot solve novel tasks | Section 6, Reviewer Response |
| Classification tasks | Clean experimental control | Does not extend to generation | Section 6 |

**Final assessment:** The paper's findings are robust within their stated scope (classification and multi-choice tasks with pretraining coverage), but practitioners must carefully evaluate whether their specific use case falls within these boundaries. The trade-offs identified suggest that in-context learning is best viewed as a **retrieval and formatting mechanism** rather than a genuine learning mechanism—a distinction with significant implications for how we deploy and trust these systems.

## 7. Implications and Future Directions

This paper's findings extend far beyond the specific experimental results—they fundamentally reshape how we conceptualize in-context learning and open new research trajectories. This section synthesizes the broader implications for the NLP community, identifies concrete follow-up research directions, and provides actionable guidance for practitioners deciding when and how to apply these insights.

### 7.1 Reframing In-Context Learning: From Learning to Retrieval

The most profound implication of this work is the conceptual shift it demands. Prior to this paper, in-context learning was widely understood through the lens of **few-shot learning**—the model observes examples and infers a task rule. This paper provides empirical evidence that this framing is misleading.

**The New Framework:** In-context learning functions more accurately as **capability retrieval** than task acquisition. The demonstrations do not teach the model new input-label mappings; they trigger access to capabilities already encoded during pretraining. As Section 6 states:

> "If we take a strict definition of learning: capturing the input-label correspondence given in the training data, then our findings suggest that LMs do not learn new tasks at test time."

**Why This Matters:** This reframing has cascading implications:

1.  **Theoretical Understanding:** Models are not performing Bayesian inference over task rules (as Xie et al., 2022 proposed) in the way previously assumed. Instead, they are matching prompt structures to pretraining patterns.
2.  **Capability Boundaries:** In-context learning cannot solve tasks whose input-label correspondences were not captured during pretraining. This explains why some tasks respond well to prompting while others require finetuning.
3.  **Evaluation Standards:** Zero-shot baselines should be reconsidered. Section 6 notes that "our zero-shot baseline level is significantly higher than previously thought" because unlabeled data with random labels can achieve near few-shot performance.

**Connection to Prior Sections:** This builds directly on the findings in Section 4.1 (gold vs. random labels) and Section 5.4 (meta-training effects). The fact that MetaICL shows the smallest dependence on ground truth labels (0.1–0.9% drop) while showing the strongest reliance on format suggests meta-training optimizes for retrieval efficiency rather than adaptation flexibility.

### 7.2 Follow-Up Research Directions

The paper explicitly identifies several open questions that warrant immediate investigation. These directions span theoretical, empirical, and practical domains.

**7.2.1 Extension to Generation Tasks**

The most critical gap is the extension to **open-ended generation tasks** (summarization, translation, creative writing). Section 6 acknowledges:

> "Extending of our experiments to such tasks is not trivial, because it requires a variation of the output which has incorrect input-output correspondence while keeping the correct output distribution."

**Research Question:** Does the format-over-content principle hold when outputs are not constrained to a fixed label space?

**Why This Is Hard:** In classification, you can swap "positive" for "negative" and maintain a valid label. In generation, replacing a correct summary with random text destroys both correspondence and distribution simultaneously. Future work must design demonstration variants that preserve output distribution while breaking input-output mapping—a non-trivial experimental challenge.

**Potential Approach:** One direction is to use **semantic equivalents** (e.g., paraphrased summaries) as "incorrect" labels that maintain distribution properties. Another is to analyze **chain-of-thought prompting**, where Madaan and Yazdanbakhsh (2022) found random rationales degrade performance but counterfactual rationales do not.

**7.2.2 Instruction-Following Model Analysis**

The paper hypothesizes that findings extend to instruction-tuned models but leaves this unverified. Section 6 states:

> "We think the demonstrations and instructions largely have the same role to LMs... We leave more analysis on instruction-following models for future work."

**Research Question:** Do instruction-tuned models (GPT-3.5, GPT-4, Claude) show the same insensitivity to instruction correctness as base models show to label correctness?

**Why This Matters:** The industry has shifted heavily toward instruction-tuned models. If these models also rely on format over content, it has significant implications for:
- **Safety:** Models may follow harmful instructions if the format is correct
- **Alignment:** Correcting model behavior via examples may be less effective than assumed
- **Prompt Engineering:** Instruction wording may matter less than instruction structure

**Evidence Hint:** The paper cites Webson and Pavlick (2022), who showed "model performance does not degrade much with irrelevant or misleading instructions." This preliminary evidence supports the hypothesis but requires systematic validation.

**7.2.3 Pretraining Objective and Data Effects**

The reviewer response (lines 055–060) explicitly notes:

> "Due to compute limitations, we were not be able to provide recommendations that are empirically supported. Future work may investigate aspects of language model pretraining that affect the findings, including the pretraining data and the objective."

**Research Question:** Which pretraining conditions enable in-context learning with random labels? Is it corpus coverage, objective function, or model architecture?

**Why This Matters:** Understanding this would allow practitioners to predict *a priori* whether a task will work with random labels. Tasks with sparse pretraining coverage (e.g., domain-specific medical diagnosis) may require gold labels, while common tasks (sentiment analysis) may not.

**Potential Approach:** Train models with controlled pretraining variations (different corpora, different objectives) and measure their sensitivity to label correctness. This would establish causal relationships rather than correlations.

**7.2.4 Word Ordering and Demonstration Selection**

The reviewer response (lines 061–064) identifies another gap:

> "Word ordering matters? We think it is an important avenue for future work. For this paper, we did not include it in our scope due to requiring multiple times more experiments."

**Research Question:** Does the order of demonstrations, or the order of tokens within demonstrations, affect the format signal's strength?

**Why This Matters:** Prior work (Lu et al., 2021) showed prompt order sensitivity. If format is the primary signal, order effects may be explained as format consistency violations rather than semantic issues.

**7.2.5 Novel Task Learning Mechanisms**

Perhaps the most important open question is how to enable genuine task learning for tasks not covered in pretraining. Section 6 poses:

> "This leads to the research question of how to make progress in NLP problems that in-context learning does not solve: whether we need a better way of extracting the input-label mappings that are already stored in the LM, a better variant of the LM objective that learns a wider range of task semantics, or explicit supervision through fine-tuning on the labeled data."

**Research Question:** What training objectives or architectures enable models to learn truly novel input-label mappings from demonstrations?

**Potential Directions:**
- **Contrastive pretraining** that explicitly teaches input-label association
- **Architectural modifications** that separate retrieval from adaptation
- **Hybrid approaches** combining in-context learning with lightweight finetuning

### 7.3 Practical Applications and Use Cases

The findings enable several concrete applications that reduce costs and improve efficiency for practitioners.

**7.3.1 Low-Cost Prompt Engineering**

**Application:** Use unlabeled data with random labels instead of curated labeled examples.

**When to Apply:**
- Classification and multi-choice tasks with common label spaces (sentiment, NLI, QA)
- Tasks where the model has likely seen similar input-label patterns during pretraining
- Scenarios where 0–5% performance drop is acceptable for significant cost savings

**When to Avoid:**
- Domain-specific tasks with sparse pretraining coverage (e.g., `financial_phrasebank` showed up to 14% drop)
- Generation tasks (untested)
- Safety-critical applications where maximum accuracy is required

**Implementation Guidance:**
```
# Instead of this (requires labeled data):
demos = [(x1, "positive"), (x2, "negative"), (x3, "positive")]

# Try this (uses unlabeled data):
demos = [(x1, random_label()), (x2, random_label()), (x3, random_label())]
# where random_label() samples from {"positive", "negative"}
```

**Expected Outcome:** Based on Figure 3 and Figure 4, expect 92–100% of the performance gains from gold labels for most models on most classification tasks.

**7.3.2 Enhanced Zero-Shot Baselines**

**Application:** Establish stronger baselines for evaluating new prompting methods.

**Current Practice:** New prompting methods are compared against naive zero-shot (no examples).

**Recommended Practice:** Compare against **unlabeled few-shot** (k examples with random labels) as the baseline.

**Why:** Section 6 notes "our zero-shot baseline level is significantly higher than previously thought." If a new method cannot beat unlabeled few-shot, its contribution is limited.

**Implementation:** For any new prompting technique, report three baselines:
1.  Naive zero-shot (no examples)
2.  Unlabeled few-shot (k examples, random labels)
3.  Labeled few-shot (k examples, gold labels)

This establishes the full performance spectrum and clarifies whether improvements come from better labeling or better formatting.

**7.3.3 Format-First Prompt Design**

**Application:** Prioritize structural consistency over example quality in prompt engineering.

**Guidance:** Based on Figure 10, format preservation retains 75–95% of in-context learning gains. Invest effort in:
- Consistent separators between examples (newlines, special tokens)
- Consistent input-label pairing structure
- Consistent label space presentation

**De-prioritize:**
- Careful curation of individual example quality
- Semantic correctness of labels (for classification tasks)
- Natural language framing (minimal templates performed comparably to manual templates per Figure 6)

**Example:** For a sentiment analysis task, ensure all demonstrations follow the exact pattern:
```
[input text] \n [label]
[input text] \n [label]
[test input] \n
```
Rather than worrying whether each label is semantically correct.

**7.3.4 Meta-Training Decision Framework**

**Application:** Decide whether to use meta-trained models (like MetaICL) or base models.

**Trade-off Summary:**

| Use Meta-Trained Models When | Use Base Models When |
|------------------------------|----------------------|
| Label quality is uncertain | Task may be novel (not in pretraining) |
| Format consistency is high | Maximum adaptation flexibility needed |
| Cost of label annotation is high | Task requires genuine learning from examples |

**Evidence:** Section 5.4 shows MetaICL has 0.1–0.9% drop with random labels (highly robust) but "nearly zero influence of the input-label mapping and the input distribution." This makes MetaICL efficient but potentially inflexible for novel tasks.

### 7.4 Reproducibility and Integration Guidance

**7.4.1 When to Prefer Random Labels Over Gold Labels**

Based on the paper's findings, use the following decision framework:

**Prefer Random Labels If:**
1.  Task is classification or multi-choice (not generation)
2.  Label space is small and well-defined (≤10 classes)
3.  Task domain is common in pretraining data (sentiment, NLI, general QA)
4.  Performance tolerance allows 0–5% absolute drop
5.  Labeled data is expensive or unavailable

**Prefer Gold Labels If:**
1.  Task is domain-specific with sparse pretraining coverage
2.  Maximum accuracy is required (safety-critical applications)
3.  Dataset shows outlier behavior (check Figure 12 for similar tasks)
4.  Task involves novel input-label mappings not likely in pretraining

**7.4.2 Integration Checklist**

Before deploying in-context learning with random labels:

- [ ] Verify task type is classification or multi-choice
- [ ] Confirm label space matches between demonstrations and test inputs
- [ ] Ensure format consistency across all demonstrations
- [ ] Test on a small subset with gold labels to establish upper bound
- [ ] Compare against unlabeled few-shot baseline (not just zero-shot)
- [ ] Check for dataset-specific sensitivity (some tasks show 14% drops)

**7.4.3 Code and Data Availability**

The authors provide full reproducibility resources:
- **Code:** `github.com/Alrope123/rethinking-demonstrations`
- **Datasets:** 26 datasets via HuggingFace Datasets library (Lhoest et al., 2021)

### 7.5 Long-Term Field Impact

This work positions in-context learning research at an inflection point. The implications extend across multiple dimensions:

**Theoretical:** Researchers must develop new frameworks that explain in-context learning without assuming task rule inference. The Bayesian inference model (Xie et al., 2022) may need revision to account for format-driven retrieval.

**Methodological:** Future prompting research should control for format effects when claiming improvements from example selection or instruction design. Many reported gains may stem from format consistency rather than semantic quality.

**Practical:** The cost structure of deploying large models shifts. Annotation budgets can be reduced for many tasks, but practitioners must invest in understanding their task's pretraining coverage.

**Architectural:** Model designers may optimize pretraining objectives to maximize retrieval efficiency rather than adaptation flexibility, depending on target use cases.

**Ethical:** If models rely on pretraining priors rather than learning from context, they may perpetuate biases present in pretraining data even when demonstrations suggest otherwise. This warrants investigation into whether random-label prompting amplifies or mitigates bias.

**Final Perspective:** This paper does not diminish the value of in-context learning—it clarifies its mechanism. By understanding that demonstrations trigger retrieval rather than teach tasks, researchers can design better methods for both leveraging existing capabilities and extending them to truly novel problems. The path forward requires distinguishing between tasks the model already knows (where random labels suffice) and tasks it must learn (where gold labels or finetuning remain essential).
